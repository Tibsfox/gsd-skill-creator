# Cultural Sovereignty Policy — Heritage Skills Educational Pack

**Version:** 1.0 (Phase 1)
**Effective:** 2026-03-02

---

## Purpose

Indigenous communities hold inherent rights to their cultural knowledge, practices, and
heritage. These rights are not contingent on legal recognition by external parties, nor are
they diminished by the absence of formal documentation, academic consensus, or historical
accessibility. Article 31 of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous
Peoples (UNDRIP) recognizes the right of Indigenous peoples to maintain, control, protect,
and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural
expressions. This policy applies that recognition in concrete technical terms.

The Heritage Skills Educational Pack exists to educate — to help learners engage respectfully
with Appalachian, First Nations, and Inuit living skills traditions. Education requires access
to knowledge. Access to knowledge that belongs to a community requires the community's
authority over how that knowledge is shared. This pack cannot function as an educational
resource without the trust of the communities whose traditions it covers. That trust is
built through structural accountability, not through stated good intentions. This policy
describes the structural commitments that make community trust possible.

---

## Our Commitments

1. **Nation-specific attribution always.** Every piece of Indigenous content in this pack
   attributes to the specific nation or community whose tradition it represents. Generic
   language ("First Nations tradition", "Indigenous peoples", "Native American") is not
   acceptable as a primary attribution. Anishinaabe, Haudenosaunee, Cree, Dene, Innu, Metis,
   Inuit (with region: Inuvialuit, Kitikmeot, Kivalliq, Qikiqtaaluk, Nunavik, Nunatsiavut)
   — these are the attributions the pack uses.

2. **Level 4 sacred and ceremonial content is a structural hard block.** No content
   classified at Level 4 (SACRED_CEREMONIAL) is generated by this system under any
   circumstances. This is enforced at the code level through the CulturalSovereigntyWarden.
   There are no exceptions. There is no administrative override. There is no escalation path
   that results in Level 4 content being generated.

3. **OCAP compliance for all Indigenous data operations.** The Ownership, Control, Access,
   and Possession framework governs how First Nations knowledge is handled in this pack.
   The Heritage Book publication gate enforces OCAP Ownership and Access: community review
   must be documented before a Heritage Book containing First Nations or Inuit content can
   be exported.

4. **IQ alignment for all Inuit content.** Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit — the Inuit knowledge
   system and worldview — governs how Inuit content is presented, structured, and delivered.
   The pack's oral history interview methodology applies IQ principles: Inuuqatigiitsiarniq
   (caring for people and relationships), Tunnganarniq (openness and acceptance), Pilimmaksarniq
   (skills development through observation, mentoring, and practice), Aajiiqatigiinniq
   (consensus decision-making and collaborative problem-solving).

5. **CARE principles for all Indigenous content operations.** The CARE Principles (Collective
   Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics) govern how all Indigenous knowledge
   and data are used in this pack. Content use must benefit communities, not only external
   learners or researchers. Indigenous nations retain authority over their knowledge.
   Obligations of accountability are ongoing, not one-time.

6. **NISR alignment for Inuit research methodology.** The National Inuit Strategy on Research
   governs the oral history studio's consent protocols for Inuit interviews. NISR compliance
   means Inuit interview subjects retain control over how their knowledge is recorded, stored,
   and shared.

7. **UNDRIP Article 31 recognition.** This pack recognizes the right of Indigenous peoples to
   maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage and traditional knowledge
   as established in Article 31 of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous
   Peoples. Technical implementation of the 4-level classification system in this pack is a
   direct application of this recognition.

## Knowledge Classification System

All content in this pack is classified using a 4-level system. The classification level
determines what the system generates and how it presents content. Classification is applied
at the content interaction level by the CulturalSovereigntyWarden before any content is
delivered.

---

### Level 1: Publicly Shared

**Definition:** Knowledge and practices that communities have explicitly made available for
public education. The community has created educational materials, authorized documentation,
or otherwise indicated that this content is appropriate for general sharing.

**System behavior:** Full content is generated with nation-specific attribution.

**Examples:**
- Katajjaq (Inuit throat singing): the genre, two-singer format, interlocking breath patterns,
  and publicly documented rhythm patterns (Inuit, Nunavut and Nunavik)
- Haudenosaunee longhouse construction geometry and structural principles
- Anishinaabe birchbark canoe hull form and construction process
- Appalachian split-oak basket weaving patterns and techniques
- Inuinnait cold copper working (hammering and work hardening without forge or fire)
- IQ principles as educational framework (Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit overview)
- Frame drum construction technique (distinct from ceremonial use)

**Attribution requirement:** Every Level 1 content item must include the nation and, where
applicable, the region. "Inuit" alone is not sufficient when the practice is specific to a
regional group (e.g., Nunavik Inuit, Inuvialuit).

---

### Level 2: Contextually Shared

**Definition:** Knowledge that is appropriate for educational contexts but carries cultural
significance that warrants framing, contextual explanation, and referral to community sources
for specifics. The technique or general information may be shared; the deeper cultural
meanings belong to community explanation.

**System behavior:** Summary and contextual framing provided; system refers to community
sources for specific protocols, design meanings, or spiritual dimensions.

**Examples:**
- Haudenosaunee clan-associated design patterns in pottery (technique is Level 1; specific
  clan associations and meanings are contextually shared — the system frames this without
  claiming authority over specific meanings)
- Anishinaabe clan system and its governance function (publicly documented structure; specific
  clan protocols require community context)
- Banjo origin history — West African kora and ngoni lineage through African American enslaved
  communities (historically documented; the system presents with appropriate cultural framing)
- Drumming technique in general educational contexts (construction is Level 1; ceremonial
  use and song contexts are Level 3-4)
- IQ relational knowledge about animal relationships (the relational dimension is framed at
  Level 2 — the system acknowledges it without prescribing specific protocols)
- OCAP principles as educational framework in the context of community data governance

---

### Level 3: Community Restricted

**Definition:** Knowledge whose existence is publicly acknowledged but whose details and
protocols belong to the community and are not appropriate for external educational systems
to convey. The system acknowledges the existence of these practices and redirects to
community resources.

**System behavior:** Existence acknowledged. No content generated. User redirected to
community resources or nation-specific educational organizations.

**Examples:**
- First Nations ceremonial protocols of any kind (sweat lodge, naming ceremonies, winter
  ceremonies, healing ceremonies)
- Wampum belt treaty-reading protocols and interpretation methods
- Specific drumming ceremonial contexts and associated songs
- Potlatch ceremony specifics and associated protocols (Pacific Northwest Coast — Phase 2)
- Seasonal ceremony specifics: Green Corn Ceremony, sugar bush thanksgiving prayers,
  first-fish and first-caribou ceremonies
- Cree, Dene, or Anishinaabe trapping spiritual protocols (the relational and spiritual
  dimension of trapping relationships)

---

### Level 4: Sacred/Ceremonial — Absolute Protection

**Definition:** Sacred and ceremonial knowledge whose transmission is governed entirely by
community protocols, spiritual authority, and ceremonial contexts that this system cannot
and should not replicate.

**System behavior:** Hard block. No content generated. No referral target provided (the
absence of a referral target is deliberate and structural — there is no appropriate external
resource to which the system can send someone for this content).

The system will never generate this content. Not for academic research. Not for community
members who self-identify as belonging to the relevant nation. Not for any stated reason.
This is not a policy decision made at runtime — it is a structural property of the system.

**Why there are no exceptions:**

- No academic research exception: Academic framing does not change what is generated.
  The system cannot verify academic credentials. More importantly, even verified academic
  researchers do not have default access to sacred knowledge — that access is community-
  governed, not credential-governed.

- No credential override: The system evaluates content patterns, not requester identity.
  There is no mechanism to verify credentials. Even if there were, credentials from external
  institutions do not confer authority over Indigenous sacred knowledge.

- No community membership self-attestation: The system cannot verify community membership.
  A statement "I am a member of the Cree community" cannot be verified and does not override
  this protection. Legitimate access to sacred knowledge is mediated through community
  relationships and ceremonial structures, not through a text query to an AI system.

- No "I understand the risks" acknowledgment: Acknowledged risk does not change what is
  generated. The protection exists for the community's sovereignty over its knowledge, not
  for the requester's safety.

**Examples:**
- Sacred songs of any First Nations nation
- Inuit ceremonial songs and their contexts
- Angakkuq (Inuit shamanic practice) protocols, procedures, or specific knowledge
- Sacred oral narratives and creation stories of any tradition covered in this pack
- Sweat lodge ceremony specifics
- Any ceremony or ritual whose transmission requires initiation, ceremonial authority,
  or community sanction

## Frameworks We Follow

### OCAP — Ownership, Control, Access, Possession

Developed by the First Nations Information Governance Centre (FNIGC), the OCAP principles
are the standard for First Nations data governance in Canada.

- **Ownership:** A community or group owns information collectively in the same way that an
  individual owns their personal information.
- **Control:** First Nations people and their duly recognized authorities have control over
  all aspects of research and information management processes that affect them and their
  communities.
- **Access:** First Nations people must have access to information and data about themselves
  and their communities, regardless of where it is held.
- **Possession:** While ownership is a legal concept, possession is the mechanism through
  which ownership can be asserted and protected. Physical and digital custodianship of
  information supports community ownership.

How OCAP applies in this pack: The Heritage Book publication gate enforces OCAP Ownership
and Access principles. Before a Heritage Book containing First Nations or Inuit content can
be exported, the project's community review must be documented. This cannot be waived.

### IQ — Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit

Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit is the Inuit knowledge system, worldview, and way of life. It is
not simply a set of practices — it is the lens through which Inuit understanding of the world
is organized. The 8 IQ principles embedded in this pack's oral history and educational
methodology are:

1. Inuuqatigiitsiarniq (caring for people and relationships)
2. Tunnganarniq (openness and acceptance of others)
3. Pijitsirniq (serving others and community)
4. Aajiiqatigiinniq (consensus decision-making and collaborative problem-solving)
5. Pilimmaksarniq (skills and knowledge development through observation, mentoring, and practice)
6. Qanuqtuurniq (being innovative and resourceful)
7. Avatittinnik Kamatsiarniq (environmental stewardship and responsibility)
8. Kiinausuutigiittinnaarniq (self-reliance and responsibility for supporting family and community)

How IQ applies in this pack: Interview guides use IQ principles to structure the relationship-
building, preparation, and review phases of Inuit oral history interviews. The Interview
Simulator evaluates question appropriateness against IQ alignment. Pilimmaksarniq in particular
governs the educational philosophy: skills are developed through observation, mentoring, and
practice — not through one-time information delivery.

### CARE — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics

The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance were developed by the Global Indigenous
Data Alliance (GIDA) as a counterpart to the FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible,
Interoperable, Reusable) for open data.

- **Collective Benefit:** Data ecosystems shall be designed and function in ways that enable
  Indigenous peoples to derive benefit from the data. Use of Indigenous data must benefit
  Indigenous communities.
- **Authority to Control:** Indigenous peoples' rights and interests in Indigenous data must
  be recognized and their authority to control such data be empowered.
- **Responsibility:** Those working with Indigenous data have a responsibility to share how
  that data is used to support Indigenous peoples' self-determination and collective benefit.
- **Ethics:** Indigenous peoples' rights and wellbeing should be the primary concern at all
  stages of the data life cycle.

How CARE applies in this pack: All editorial decisions about what content is included at each
classification level apply the CARE framework. The question is not "can we include this?" but
"does including this benefit the community, respect their authority, and meet our ethical
obligations to them?"

### NISR — National Inuit Strategy on Research

The National Inuit Strategy on Research (NISR), developed by Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK),
is the framework governing how research involving Inuit knowledge, communities, and people
is conducted, governed, and used. NISR establishes that Inuit have the right to lead and
participate in research about Inuit, and that research methodologies must be appropriate to
Inuit ways of knowing.

How NISR applies in this pack: The oral history studio's NISR-compliant consent protocol
covers informed consent, knowledge holder control over recording and use, and the principle
that Inuit interview subjects retain the right to withdraw or modify their contribution. The
Interview Simulator evaluates question protocols for NISR compliance as part of its scoring.

### UNDRIP Article 31 — United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples

Article 31 of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples states:

"Indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect and develop their cultural
heritage, traditional knowledge and traditional cultural expressions, including human and
genetic resources, seeds, medicines, knowledge of the properties of fauna and flora, oral
traditions, literatures, designs, sports and traditional games and visual and performing arts.
They also have the right to maintain, control, protect and develop their intellectual property
over such cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions."

The 4-level classification system in this pack is a direct technical implementation of this
right. Level 4 (SACRED_CEREMONIAL) protection implements the "maintain, control, protect"
dimension. The OCAP publication gate implements the community's right to control how their
knowledge is used in documents that will be shared.

## Contribution Requirements

Before contributing any Indigenous content to this pack, the following requirements apply.
These are not suggestions — they are conditions for contribution.

### 1. Nation-Specific Attribution — Mandatory

Every piece of Indigenous content must attribute to the specific nation or community whose
tradition it represents. This attribution must appear in:

- The module's `nationAttribution` field
- Any Try Session step that references nation-specific practices
- The cultural configuration entry for the content

Pan-Indigenous language is not acceptable as a primary attribution:

| Not acceptable | Required instead |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "First Nations tradition" | "Anishinaabe tradition" or "Haudenosaunee tradition" |
| "Indigenous peoples believe" | "Cree oral tradition holds" or "Inuit (Nunavut) practice" |
| "Native American healers" | "Anishinaabe medicine practitioners" |
| "Aboriginal basket weaving" | "Haida basketry" (Phase 2) or specific nation |

### 2. Sovereignty Level Assessment — Required for All Contributions

Every piece of content contributed must include a sovereignty level assessment. The
contributor must determine which of the 4 levels applies and document their reasoning.
Content submitted without a sovereignty level assessment will be rejected.

### 3. Community Consultation for Level 2 and Above — Documentation Required

Any content assessed at Level 2 (CONTEXTUALLY_SHARED) or above requires documented
community consultation before submission. "Documented" means:

- Written acknowledgment from a representative of the relevant nation or community that
  the content and its framing are appropriate for inclusion in this educational pack
- The nature of the consultation (who was consulted, in what capacity, and what they
  reviewed)
- Date of consultation

Community consultation is not a bureaucratic formality. It is the mechanism by which the
community exercises authority over its knowledge. A contributor who cannot obtain documented
consultation should not contribute that content.

### 4. No Contribution of Level 3 or Level 4 Content

Level 3 (COMMUNITY_RESTRICTED) content cannot be contributed to this pack. The pack's
handling of Level 3 content is to acknowledge existence and redirect — it does not hold
Level 3 content.

Level 4 (SACRED_CEREMONIAL) content cannot be contributed to this pack under any
circumstances. If you believe you have received permission from a community to share Level 4
content in this system, that belief reflects a misunderstanding of what Level 4 means. Sacred
knowledge is transmitted through community relationships and ceremonial structures, not
through educational software.

### 5. Creator-First Links — Required for Canonical Works

Any content that references or draws on a canonical work (Foxfire volumes, First Nations or
Inuit authored books, academic works that involve Indigenous collaboration) must include the
creator-direct or creator-authorized purchase link. The canonical works catalog in this pack
maintains these links. Contributing content that references canonical works without providing
the appropriate purchase link will be flagged during review.

## Dispute and Correction Process

Any member of a community whose tradition is represented in this pack may request correction
or removal of content. This includes:

- A nation or community organization indicating that content is misattributed, inaccurate,
  or should be classified at a more restrictive level
- A knowledge keeper indicating that content attributed to their tradition has been presented
  without appropriate context or authority
- A community member indicating that content labeled Level 1 or Level 2 should be Level 3
  or Level 4

**Response commitment:**

Content that is the subject of a documented community request for correction or removal will
be suspended within 24 hours of receipt. "Suspended" means the content is removed from the
active pack pending review. Suspension does not require the dispute to be resolved first —
the community's request is sufficient to trigger suspension.

Review will be conducted by the project maintainers in consultation with the requesting
community representative. The outcome of the review is binding. If the community requests
permanent removal, the content is permanently removed. There is no appeal process that
overrides a community's decision about their own knowledge.

**How to submit a dispute or correction:**

File an issue at the project's GitHub repository. Use the issue title format:
"Cultural Sovereignty: [nature of concern] — [tradition/nation]"

Label the issue "cultural-sovereignty". Cultural sovereignty concerns are treated as the
highest-priority issues in this project. They are not categorized alongside feature requests
or bug reports.

## Contact

For cultural sovereignty concerns: open an issue at the project repository with the label
"cultural-sovereignty". These issues are reviewed as highest priority.

For urgent concerns (content that should be removed immediately): file an issue and include
"URGENT REMOVAL REQUEST" in the title. This triggers the 24-hour suspension commitment.

This policy is reviewed at each major release of the heritage-skills-pack. Community feedback
on this policy itself is welcome and will be incorporated into future versions.
