# M3: Native Plant Integration for PNW Gardens

> Comprehensive guide to integrating native plants into maritime Pacific Northwest gardens — species selection, habitat creation, pollinator support, and ecological design from Vancouver BC to Roseburg OR, west of the Cascades.

---

## Overview

Module M3 addresses the growing interest in native plant gardening across the maritime Pacific Northwest. As urban and suburban development fragments natural habitats, residential gardens become increasingly important refuges for native wildlife — from pollinators and songbirds to amphibians and beneficial insects. This module profiles over 30 native species suitable for home gardens, organized by structural layer (canopy, understory, groundcover, wildflower), and provides design frameworks for assembling ecologically functional plant communities.

The maritime PNW's wet-winter/dry-summer climate (USDA zones 7b–9a, Sunset zones 4–6) [GOV-01] shaped these plants over millennia. Species native to this region are pre-adapted to the region's acidic soils [GOV-04], summer drought stress [GOV-02], and cool wet winters [GOV-03]. Gardening with natives reduces irrigation demand, eliminates the need for most pesticides, and creates habitat that cannot be replicated by ornamental plantings alone [EXT-01].

**Primary sources:** EXT-01 (OSU native plant guide), GOV-06 (King County native plant resources), PRO-02 (Washington Native Plant Society), PRO-04 (Portland Audubon Backyard Habitat Certification), PRO-08 (Real Gardens Grow Natives).

**Supporting sources:** GOV-05 (Portland tree pests/diseases), EXT-05 (PNW pest management), PRO-01 (Tilth Alliance garden guide), PRO-03 (Pacific Horticulture), PRO-10 (Sky Nursery).

## 1. Ecological Function of Native Plants

### Why Native Plants Matter

Native plants are species that evolved within the maritime Pacific Northwest ecosystem over thousands of years, developing intricate relationships with local soils, climate patterns, pollinators, and wildlife [EXT-01]. These co-evolutionary relationships cannot be replicated by introduced ornamentals, no matter how attractive or well-adapted those imports may appear.

The maritime PNW's defining climate characteristic — cool, wet winters receiving 70–80% of annual precipitation followed by warm, dry summers with minimal rainfall [GOV-03, EXT-07] — created a unique selective pressure. Native species developed strategies for this pattern: deep root systems that access summer moisture, waxy or leathery leaves that resist desiccation, and dormancy periods aligned with seasonal drought [EXT-01]. Many introduced garden plants, adapted to summer rainfall climates, require supplemental irrigation throughout the dry season — a resource-intensive practice that natives largely avoid once established [GOV-06].

### Key Ecological Benefits

**Water conservation.** Established native plants require little to no supplemental irrigation after the first 1–2 years, relying on deep root systems and drought-avoidance strategies evolved over millennia in the PNW's dry-summer climate [EXT-01, GOV-06]. A native plant garden can reduce outdoor water use by 50–70% compared to conventional landscapes dominated by non-native ornamentals and turf grass [GOV-06].

**Pest and disease resistance.** Native plants co-evolved with local pathogens and herbivores, maintaining population-level resistance that reduces or eliminates the need for chemical intervention [EXT-05]. This resistance is population-wide, not dependent on single cultivar traits that can break down. For example, native sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*) is essentially pest-free in maritime PNW gardens, while many non-native fern species are susceptible to scale insects and fungal infections [GOV-05, EXT-01].

**Pollinator and wildlife support.** Native plants provide the specific nectar chemistry, pollen protein profiles, bloom timing, and flower morphology that co-evolved pollinators depend on [PRO-04, PRO-02]. Over 90% of native bees are specialists or strong preferrers of native pollen sources [PRO-04]. Native plants also provide host-plant relationships critical for butterfly and moth reproduction — relationships that non-native plants simply cannot fulfill.

**Soil health.** Native plants maintain the mycorrhizal networks that structure PNW forest soils [EXT-01]. Ectomycorrhizal fungi associated with native conifers and oaks create vast underground networks that facilitate nutrient transfer between plants, improve soil structure, and increase drought resilience. When native plants are removed and replaced with non-native landscapes, these fungal networks collapse [EXT-01, PRO-08].

**Carbon sequestration.** Deep-rooted native trees and shrubs sequester significant carbon in both biomass and soil. A single mature Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) can sequester over 48,000 pounds of CO₂ during its lifetime [GOV-06]. Native plant gardens with trees and shrubs store substantially more carbon than conventional turf-dominated landscapes.

### Gardens as Habitat Corridors

As urbanization fragments natural habitats across the maritime PNW, residential gardens become critical links in ecological connectivity [PRO-04, GOV-06]. The Backyard Habitat Certification Program in Portland — a collaboration between Portland Audubon and Columbia Land Trust — documents this principle: even small urban gardens planted with natives can provide stepping-stone habitat that allows pollinators, birds, and other wildlife to move through otherwise inhospitable urban landscapes [PRO-04].

A single yard with native plants may seem insignificant, but when multiplied across neighborhoods, these gardens create functional habitat corridors that support migration, gene flow, and population resilience for native species [PRO-04, PRO-02]. This is particularly important for species with limited dispersal ability — native bees that forage within a few hundred meters of their nest sites, or amphibians that require connected habitat between breeding and upland sites.

### Key Host Plant Relationships

Certain native trees and shrubs are disproportionately important as larval host plants for Lepidoptera (butterflies and moths), which in turn form the primary food base for breeding songbirds [PRO-04, PRO-02]. The most ecologically significant host plants in the maritime PNW include:

| Host Plant | Scientific Name | Lepidoptera Species Supported | Key Dependent Species |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| Oregon white oak | *Quercus garryana* | 150+ moth and butterfly species | California sister, propertius duskywing, grey hairstreak |
| Douglas-fir | *Pseudotsuga menziesii* | 100+ moth species | Pine white butterfly, Douglas-fir tussock moth |
| Willows (native spp.) | *Salix* spp. | 300+ moth and butterfly species | Western tiger swallowtail, mourning cloak, Lorquin's admiral |
| Bitter cherry | *Prunus emarginata* | 150+ moth and butterfly species | Western tiger swallowtail, pale swallowtail, spring azure |
| Red alder | *Alnus rubra* | 100+ moth species | Green comma, various geometrids |
| Bigleaf maple | *Acer macrophyllum* | 75+ moth species | Polyphemus moth, various noctuids |

These numbers reflect documented Lepidoptera-host associations across the native range of each species [PRO-04, PRO-02]. Oaks and willows stand out as keystone genera — removing them from a landscape eliminates food web support for hundreds of dependent insect species, with cascading effects through the entire food chain.

## 2. Canopy Trees

Canopy trees form the structural backbone of any native plant garden, providing shade, windbreak, wildlife habitat, and the upper story that defines the garden's ecological character. In the maritime PNW, native canopy trees range from towering conifers exceeding 200 feet to moderate-sized broadleaf species suitable for typical residential lots [EXT-01, GOV-06].

**Important design note:** Most native canopy trees are large at maturity. Select species appropriate for your lot size and consider ultimate height, canopy spread, and root zone. For small urban lots (under 5,000 sq ft), understory trees (Section 3) are often more appropriate than full canopy species [PRO-08, PRO-10].

### Douglas-fir — *Pseudotsuga menziesii*

**USDA zones:** 4–8 (maritime ecotype thrives in zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to light shade
**Moisture:** Moist to dry; extremely drought-tolerant once established
**Mature size:** 70–250 feet tall, 12–25 foot canopy spread [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate to fast — 12–24 inches per year in favorable conditions

**Ecological function:** The defining conifer of the maritime PNW, Douglas-fir is a keystone species supporting over 100 moth species as larval host, producing seed crops that feed crossbills, pine siskins, Douglas squirrels, and other wildlife [GOV-06, PRO-02]. Its furrowed bark hosts diverse lichen communities and provides nesting habitat for brown creepers and bats. Fallen needles create acidic duff that supports native groundcover species including salal and sword fern [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Suitable only for larger properties due to ultimate size. Young trees have an attractive pyramidal form and can serve as living Christmas trees for several years. Shallow, spreading root system — avoid planting within 15 feet of foundations or underground utilities [EXT-01]. Naturally self-prunes lower branches in forest conditions; in open gardens, lower limbs persist and may need management. Fire-resistant bark on mature specimens [GOV-06]. Tolerates PNW's summer drought without supplemental water once established (2–3 years) [EXT-01].

### Western Redcedar — *Thuja plicata*

**USDA zones:** 5–8 (performs well in zones 7b–9a in maritime PNW) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to moderate shade — one of the most shade-tolerant PNW conifers
**Moisture:** Moist to wet; prefers consistent moisture, tolerates seasonal flooding
**Mature size:** 50–200 feet tall, 15–25 foot canopy spread [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — 12–24 inches per year; faster in moist conditions

**Ecological function:** Western redcedar is a foundational species in PNW riparian and bottomland forests [GOV-06]. Its dense, scale-like foliage provides year-round cover for birds and small mammals. The fibrous bark sheds in strips used by nesting birds. Seeds feed pine siskins, juncos, and other finches. Old-growth redcedars develop cavities essential for nesting owls, woodpeckers, and Vaux's swifts [PRO-02]. Fallen redcedar logs persist for centuries as nurse logs, supporting the next generation of forest plants [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Thrives in the moist conditions that challenge many other conifers. Excellent choice for rain gardens, bioswales, and naturally wet areas of the garden [GOV-06]. The cultivar 'Green Giant' (*Thuja* × *plicata* hybrid) is commonly sold but is NOT the straight native species — insist on *Thuja plicata* species plants from a reputable native plant nursery [PRO-10, PRO-02]. Deer browse young foliage, so protect seedlings in areas with deer pressure. Aromatic foliage — culturally significant to Coast Salish and other Indigenous peoples of the PNW [GOV-06].

### Bigleaf Maple — *Acer macrophyllum*

**USDA zones:** 6–9 (well-adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 50–100 feet tall, 30–50 foot canopy spread [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — 24–36 inches per year in good conditions

**Ecological function:** The largest native maple in North America and a critical component of PNW lowland forests [GOV-06]. Bigleaf maple hosts over 75 moth species and supports dense communities of epiphytic mosses, ferns, and lichens on its bark — entire ecosystems growing on a single tree [PRO-02, EXT-01]. The abundant winged seeds (samaras) are eaten by evening grosbeaks, squirrels, and chipmunks. Spring flowers provide early nectar for native bees. Fallen leaves decompose rapidly, contributing significant nutrients to soil [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Magnificent shade tree for large properties, but its vigorous root system and massive canopy make it unsuitable for small lots or near structures [PRO-08]. Leaves are the largest of any maple — up to 12 inches across — creating heavy leaf fall in autumn that provides excellent mulch material [EXT-01]. Can be coppiced (cut to the base) and will resprout vigorously, a traditional management technique [PRO-02]. The dense canopy creates deep summer shade underneath — plant shade-tolerant natives like sword fern, inside-out flower, and wild ginger beneath [PRO-08].

### Oregon White Oak — *Quercus garryana*

**USDA zones:** 7–9 (optimal in zones 8a–9a in the maritime PNW) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun — requires open conditions, does not tolerate shade
**Moisture:** Dry to moderately moist; highly drought-tolerant; DOES NOT tolerate summer irrigation
**Mature size:** 40–80 feet tall, 40–60 foot canopy spread (broader than tall at maturity) [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow — 6–12 inches per year; very long-lived (500+ years)

**Ecological function:** Oregon white oak is the single most ecologically valuable native tree in the maritime PNW for supporting biodiversity [GOV-06, PRO-02]. It hosts over 150 moth and butterfly species — more than any other native tree in the region [PRO-04]. Acorns feed Steller's jays, western scrub-jays, woodpeckers, deer, and squirrels. The open, gnarled canopy structure supports diverse lichen communities and cavity-nesting birds. Oak savanna and oak woodland ecosystems — once covering over 1 million acres in the Willamette Valley — have declined by over 90%, making garden specimens critically important for conservation [GOV-06, PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** **Critical requirement — Oregon white oak will develop root rot and die if given regular summer irrigation** [EXT-01, GOV-06]. This is the most common cause of oak death in residential landscapes. If you have an existing oak, do NOT install irrigation within the drip line, and do NOT change the grade or compact soil over roots. Plant only drought-tolerant native companions under oaks: Roemer's fescue (*Festuca roemeri*), common camas (*Camassia quamash*), shooting star (*Dodecatheon hendersonii*), and snowberry (*Symphoricarpos albus*) [EXT-01, PRO-08]. Requires patience — this is a multi-generational tree. Plant acorns or small saplings and protect from deer browse. Full sun is non-negotiable; oaks suppressed by Douglas-fir shade will decline [PRO-02].

### Pacific Madrone — *Arbutus menziesii*

**USDA zones:** 7–9 (performs best in zones 8a–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun
**Moisture:** Dry; extremely drought-tolerant once established; intolerant of summer irrigation
**Mature size:** 30–80 feet tall, 20–40 foot canopy spread [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to moderate — 6–18 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Pacific madrone is an evergreen broadleaf tree with distinctive smooth, peeling red-orange bark and leathery leaves [GOV-06]. It produces clusters of white urn-shaped flowers in spring that are heavily visited by native bees, followed by bright red-orange berries in fall that are critical food for band-tailed pigeons, cedar waxwings, varied thrushes, and American robins [PRO-02, EXT-01]. The tree's persistent leaf litter creates a distinct micro-habitat, and its bark crevices host diverse arthropod communities.

**Garden notes:** Notoriously difficult to transplant — plant from seed or very small container stock (1-gallon or smaller) and disturb the root ball as little as possible [EXT-01, PRO-10]. Like Oregon white oak, madrone does not tolerate summer irrigation and will develop root diseases (primarily *Phytophthora*) if watered during the dry season [GOV-05, EXT-01]. Best sited on well-drained slopes, rocky soils, or areas that will not receive irrigation. The peeling bark and evergreen canopy provide year-round visual interest. A stunning specimen tree for the right site — but the right site is essential [PRO-08].

## 3. Understory Trees and Shrubs

The understory layer — small trees and shrubs growing beneath the canopy — is where most residential native plant gardening happens. These species are appropriately sized for typical lots, provide the most direct wildlife benefit per square foot, and offer multi-season visual interest through flowers, berries, bark, and fall color [EXT-01, PRO-08].

### Vine Maple — *Acer circinatum*

**USDA zones:** 6–9 (excellent across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; tolerates full sun with adequate moisture
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry
**Mature size:** 10–25 feet tall, 10–20 foot spread; multi-stemmed, arching habit [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — 12–18 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Vine maple is the signature understory tree of PNW forests, creating the characteristic layered canopy structure [GOV-06]. Its small winged seeds feed birds and small mammals. Spring flowers provide early nectar. The multi-stemmed habit creates dense cover for nesting songbirds — Swainson's thrush, Wilson's warbler, and Pacific-slope flycatcher commonly nest in vine maple thickets [PRO-02]. Fallen leaves decompose rapidly, enriching forest floor nutrient cycling [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Arguably the most versatile native tree for residential gardens [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Spectacular fall color — ranging from yellow through orange to brilliant crimson, depending on light exposure (more sun = more red) [EXT-01]. The naturally arching, multi-stemmed form creates graceful structure. Can be grown as a single-trunk small tree with selective pruning, or allowed to develop its natural shrubby, layered habit. Excellent under conifers or in woodland garden settings. Tolerates the deep shade beneath Douglas-fir and western redcedar where few other trees thrive [PRO-08]. Relatively pest-free in the PNW [GOV-05].

### Tall Oregon Grape — *Mahonia aquifolium* (syn. *Berberis aquifolium*)

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (reliable across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to full shade — remarkably adaptable
**Moisture:** Dry to moist; excellent drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 3–8 feet tall, 3–5 foot spread; upright, suckering habit [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — 6–12 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Oregon's state flower, tall Oregon grape provides multiple ecological services [GOV-06]. Its early spring blooms (February–April) are among the first significant nectar sources for emerging queen bumblebees and mason bees [PRO-04, PRO-02]. The blue-black berries ripen in late summer and are eaten by cedar waxwings, American robins, and other frugivorous birds. The holly-like evergreen foliage provides year-round cover. Extensive suckering root systems stabilize slopes and build soil [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** One of the easiest and most rewarding native shrubs for home gardens [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Bright yellow flower clusters in early spring, followed by grape-like clusters of dusty blue berries. Leaves often turn purple-bronze in winter, adding year-round interest. Extremely versatile in the landscape — equally at home in full sun dry borders or deep shade under conifers [EXT-01]. The closely related low Oregon grape (*Mahonia nervosa*) stays under 2 feet and makes an excellent groundcover [PRO-08]. Berries are tart but edible and traditionally used for jelly [EXT-01] — SC-MED: no medicinal claims; edibility noted per extension source only.

### Red-flowering Currant — *Ribes sanguineum*

**USDA zones:** 6–9 (excellent in zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; blooms best in sun
**Moisture:** Dry to moist; good drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 6–10 feet tall, 4–8 foot spread; upright, arching habit [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate to fast — 12–24 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Red-flowering currant is the single most important early-season hummingbird plant in the maritime PNW [PRO-02, PRO-04]. Its pink to deep red tubular flowers bloom in March–April, precisely timed with the northward migration of rufous hummingbirds. The relationship is so tightly co-evolved that rufous hummingbird migration timing tracks currant bloom phenology [PRO-02]. Flowers are also visited by early-season bumblebees, mason bees, and syrphid flies. Blue-black berries in summer provide food for birds, though they are not preferred [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** A must-have for any PNW native garden [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The drooping clusters of rose-pink to crimson flowers are showy and long-lasting (3–4 weeks). Several cultivars offer different flower colors: 'King Edward VII' (deep crimson), 'Elk River Red' (dark red), 'White Icicle' (white) [PRO-03, PRO-10]. Deciduous — drops leaves in late summer, a drought-adaptation strategy. Best in well-drained soil; avoid waterlogged conditions. Can be pruned hard after flowering to maintain shape. Pairs beautifully with tall Oregon grape, whose yellow flowers bloom at the same time [PRO-08].

### Pacific Wax Myrtle — *Morella californica* (syn. *Myrica californica*)

**USDA zones:** 7–10 (performs well in zones 8a–9a; marginal in 7b) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist to wet; moderate drought tolerance; tolerates seasonal flooding and salt spray
**Mature size:** 10–30 feet tall, 10–15 foot spread; dense, multi-stemmed [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — 18–36 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Pacific wax myrtle is a nitrogen-fixing evergreen shrub that enriches soil through its symbiotic relationship with *Frankia* bacteria — an actinomycete that forms root nodules converting atmospheric nitrogen into plant-available forms [EXT-01, GOV-06]. This makes it a pioneer species that improves degraded soils. The small waxy berries persist through winter and provide critical late-season food for tree swallows, yellow-rumped warblers, and other overwintering birds [PRO-02]. Dense evergreen foliage provides year-round cover and windbreak.

**Garden notes:** Excellent screening plant, windbreak, or informal hedge [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The aromatic foliage is attractive year-round. Fast growth makes it useful for quickly establishing structure in new landscapes. Tolerates coastal conditions including wind and salt spray — one of the best native choices for coastal PNW gardens [EXT-01]. Can be pruned to any shape or allowed to grow naturally. The nitrogen-fixing ability makes it particularly valuable for improving poor or compacted soils in new construction landscapes [GOV-06].

### Oceanspray — *Holodiscus discolor*

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (reliable across all maritime PNW zones) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Dry to moderately moist; excellent drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 6–15 feet tall, 6–10 foot spread; upright, arching habit [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — 12–18 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Oceanspray produces large, cascading panicles of creamy-white flowers in June–July that are visited by a wide diversity of native pollinators including bees, butterflies, beetles, and syrphid flies [PRO-02, GOV-06]. The dried flower clusters persist through winter, providing seed for finches and sparrows. Dense branching habit creates nesting cover for songbirds. Oceanspray is tolerant of disturbance and naturally colonizes forest edges, road cuts, and clearings, making it a resilient choice for challenging garden sites [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** The common name refers to the foam-like appearance of the massed white flower clusters, which resemble ocean spray [EXT-01]. Extremely tough and undemanding — thrives in the driest, most neglected corners of the garden [PRO-08]. The dried brown flower clusters persist through winter; some gardeners find this attractive in a naturalistic way, while others prefer to prune them off for neatness. Can be used as an informal hedge or screen. Pairs well with native roses and snowberry in mixed shrub borders [PRO-10].

### Red Elderberry — *Sambucus racemosa*

**USDA zones:** 4–8 (reliable across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; tolerates full shade
**Moisture:** Moist to wet; moderate drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 10–20 feet tall, 8–15 foot spread; fast-growing, multi-stemmed [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Very fast — 24–48 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Red elderberry is a powerhouse wildlife plant [GOV-06, PRO-02]. Its dome-shaped clusters of creamy-white flowers in April–May provide abundant nectar and pollen for native bees and other pollinators. The bright red berries that follow in June–July are among the most important native fruit sources for band-tailed pigeons, cedar waxwings, Swainson's thrushes, and over 40 other bird species [PRO-02]. The soft pithy stems are used as nest sites by cavity-nesting bees when dead [PRO-04]. Fast growth and tolerance of wet conditions make it valuable for riparian restoration.

**Garden notes:** The bright red berry clusters are visually striking and attract abundant birdlife [PRO-08]. **SC-MED note:** Raw red elderberry fruits and seeds contain cyanogenic glycosides and should not be consumed without proper preparation; this is well-documented in extension literature [EXT-01]. The closely related blue elderberry (*Sambucus cerulea*) produces edible berries and occurs in drier, sunnier habitats [EXT-01]. Red elderberry grows rapidly but can become leggy — prune up to one-third of oldest stems to the ground annually to maintain vigor and shape [PRO-10]. Excellent for wet areas, rain gardens, and stream buffers.

### Evergreen Huckleberry — *Vaccinium ovatum*

**USDA zones:** 7–9 (well-adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; tolerates full sun in cooler locations
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 3–8 feet tall, 3–6 foot spread; dense, compact, evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow — 3–8 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Evergreen huckleberry provides year-round cover and structure in the forest understory [GOV-06]. Its small pink bell-shaped flowers in spring are visited by bumblebees and other native pollinators. The blue-black berries are among the most palatable and nutritious native fruits, eaten by birds, bears, foxes, and other wildlife [PRO-02, EXT-01]. The glossy evergreen foliage supports overwintering insect populations that become food for kinglets, chickadees, and other insectivorous birds.

**Garden notes:** A slow-growing but supremely elegant native shrub [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The glossy, dark green foliage is attractive in all seasons and highly valued by the floral industry for cut greenery. Small but delicious berries ripen August–September — excellent fresh eating, pies, and preserves [EXT-01]. Prefers acidic soil (pH 4.5–5.5) — a natural fit for PNW soils without amendment [GOV-04]. Excellent foundation planting, understory filler, or low hedge. Pairs beautifully with sword fern and salal in shade gardens. Patience required — may take 3–5 years to establish before growth accelerates [PRO-10].

### Red Huckleberry — *Vaccinium parvifolium*

**USDA zones:** 6–8 (adapted to zones 7b–8b; can be marginal in 9a heat) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; does not tolerate hot full sun
**Moisture:** Moist; prefers consistent moisture, not drought-tolerant
**Mature size:** 4–12 feet tall, 3–6 foot spread; open, vase-shaped, deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to moderate — 4–10 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Red huckleberry is an iconic species of old-growth PNW forests, characteristically growing on nurse logs — decaying fallen trees and stumps [EXT-01, GOV-06]. The bright green angular stems are photosynthetic even after the tiny leaves drop in fall. Translucent red berries are eaten by grouse, thrushes, and small mammals. The delicate flowers are visited by native bees and hummingbirds. Red huckleberry's dependence on decaying wood makes it an indicator of forest health and structural complexity [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** Growing red huckleberry successfully requires mimicking its natural habitat — acidic, humus-rich soil with excellent drainage and consistent moisture [EXT-01, PRO-08]. The ideal planting site is on or adjacent to a decaying log or stump, where the naturally acidic, moisture-retentive wood provides perfect root conditions. Bright red berries are tart and flavorful — prized for cooking and traditionally important to Indigenous peoples of the PNW [EXT-01]. The architectural branching pattern and bright green stems provide winter interest even after leaf drop. Not a plant for dry, sunny sites — this is a true forest species [PRO-10].

### Nootka Rose — *Rosa nutkana*

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (reliable across all maritime PNW zones) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 3–10 feet tall, 3–8 foot spread; upright, thicket-forming [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — 12–24 inches per year; spreads by suckers

**Ecological function:** Nootka rose provides exceptional multi-season wildlife value [GOV-06, PRO-02]. Large single pink flowers (2–3 inches across) in May–June are visited by native bees, butterflies, and syrphid flies. The abundant rose hips persist through winter, providing critical food for American robins, cedar waxwings, and overwintering songbirds when other food sources are scarce [PRO-04]. Thorny thickets create protected nesting habitat for song sparrows, spotted towhees, and other ground and low-shrub nesters. Native rose species are larval hosts for the western tussock moth and other Lepidoptera [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** More restrained than the closely related clustered wild rose (*Rosa pisocarpa*), Nootka rose forms manageable clumps rather than aggressive thickets [PRO-08]. The large, fragrant single flowers are stunning in bloom. Rose hips are high in vitamin C and can be used for tea and jelly [EXT-01] — SC-MED: nutritional content per extension source. Excellent for hedgerows, property borders, and mixed shrub plantings. Remove suckers if spread is unwanted. The native aphids that may colonize new growth attract ladybugs and other beneficial predators — avoid treating with pesticides [PRO-04, EXT-05].

### Snowberry — *Symphoricarpos albus*

**USDA zones:** 3–9 (one of the hardiest PNW natives) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to full shade — extremely adaptable
**Moisture:** Dry to moist; excellent drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 3–6 feet tall, 3–6 foot spread; upright, suckering, deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — 12–24 inches per year; spreads by underground runners

**Ecological function:** Snowberry's small pink bell-shaped flowers are important nectar sources for hummingbirds and native bees in early summer [PRO-02, GOV-06]. Its distinctive white berries — which persist from fall well into winter — are eaten by grosbeaks, waxwings, and grouse. The suckering habit creates dense low thickets that provide cover for ground-nesting birds and small mammals. Snowberry is a larval host plant for the snowberry clearwing moth (*Hemaris diffinis*) and the vashti sphinx moth [PRO-04]. Tolerates poor soils and challenging conditions where other shrubs fail [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** The white berries are the defining ornamental feature — clusters of marble-sized white fruits that glow in autumn and winter gardens [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Extremely tough and adaptable to virtually any garden condition from dry sun to deep shade. Can be aggressive in rich, moist soils — best in drier or more challenging sites where it stays manageable [EXT-01]. One of the best native shrubs for planting beneath Oregon white oak, where summer irrigation must be avoided [PRO-08]. Excellent for erosion control on slopes. The berries are not palatable to humans but are non-toxic [EXT-01].

### Indian Plum — *Oemleria cerasiformis*

**USDA zones:** 6–9 (well-adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; tolerates full sun with adequate moisture
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 10–15 feet tall, 8–12 foot spread; multi-stemmed, upright, deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — 12–18 inches per year

**Ecological function:** Indian plum (also called osoberry) is the very first native shrub to bloom in the maritime PNW — its drooping clusters of greenish-white flowers open in February, weeks before any other native woody plant [PRO-02, GOV-06]. This makes it critically important for the earliest-emerging bumblebee queens and mason bees that have no other nectar source. The small blue-black fruits ripen in June and are eaten by cedar waxwings, robins, and thrushes. Indian plum is also one of the first natives to leaf out in late winter, providing early-season cover [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Plant Indian plum for its phenomenal early bloom — when the rest of the garden is still dormant in February, this shrub is already flowering and attracting pollinators [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The early leafout and bloom provide a psychological lift for the gardener as well. Fruits are edible but astringent and not highly palatable raw [EXT-01]. Dioecious — male and female flowers on separate plants; plant both for fruit production [PRO-02]. Graceful arching form makes it an attractive small tree or large shrub for woodland edges. Can become leggy in deep shade; benefits from occasional renewal pruning [PRO-10].

## 4. Groundcovers and Ferns

The ground layer provides the ecological floor of the native garden — stabilizing soil, retaining moisture, suppressing weeds, and creating habitat for ground-dwelling insects, amphibians, and small mammals. PNW native groundcovers and ferns are among the most visually satisfying and low-maintenance elements of the native garden [EXT-01, PRO-08].

### Sword Fern — *Polystichum munitum*

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (thrives across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; tolerates brief morning sun
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; good drought tolerance for a fern once established
**Mature size:** 2–4 feet tall, 3–5 foot spread; evergreen, fountain-like rosette [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — establishes slowly, then produces 3–5 new fronds annually

**Ecological function:** Sword fern is the dominant groundcover fern of the PNW lowland forest, creating the characteristic green floor beneath conifer canopy [GOV-06, EXT-01]. The dense frond canopy provides year-round cover for amphibians (Pacific giant salamander, red-legged frog, ensatina), ground-nesting birds (winter wren, varied thrush), and small mammals. Fallen fronds build acidic, moisture-retentive leaf litter. The root crowns host diverse invertebrate communities. In ecological terms, sword fern is a "nurse groundcover" — its litter creates conditions favorable for the germination of other native species [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** If you plant only one native species, make it sword fern [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Virtually indestructible in its preferred shade conditions, pest-free, deer-resistant, evergreen, and architecturally striking. A single mature plant with its symmetrical fountain of 3–4 foot fronds is as beautiful as any ornamental. Mass plantings under trees create an immediate PNW forest atmosphere [EXT-01]. Tolerates dry shade once established — one of very few groundcovers that thrives beneath Douglas-fir and western redcedar where summer rain is intercepted by the canopy [PRO-08]. Remove old fronds in late winter before new fiddleheads emerge for tidiest appearance [PRO-10].

### Kinnikinnick — *Arctostaphylos uva-ursi*

**USDA zones:** 2–9 (extremely hardy, performs well across all maritime PNW zones) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; best in full sun
**Moisture:** Dry to moderately moist; excellent drought tolerance; requires well-drained soil
**Mature size:** 6–12 inches tall, 3–6 foot spread; prostrate, mat-forming, evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to establish (first 1–2 years), then moderate lateral spread

**Ecological function:** Kinnikinnick (also called bearberry) creates dense evergreen mats that stabilize sandy and rocky soils on slopes, banks, and thin-soil sites [GOV-06]. Its small pink urn-shaped flowers in spring are important for early-season bumblebees and mason bees. The red berries persist through winter and are eaten by grouse, bears, foxes, and overwintering songbirds [PRO-02, EXT-01]. The mat-forming habit creates micro-habitat for ground beetles, spiders, and other beneficial invertebrates.

**Garden notes:** The premier native groundcover for dry, sunny slopes and banks [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Glossy evergreen leaves turn bronze-red in winter. Small pink flowers followed by bright red berries provide multi-season interest. Requires excellent drainage — will not tolerate heavy clay or wet conditions [EXT-01]. Excellent for rock gardens, retaining walls, and dry borders. Slow to establish but once spreading forms an impenetrable, weed-suppressing mat. Can be planted through landscape fabric on slopes for erosion control during establishment. Native to both sides of the Cascades and from sea level to subalpine — extremely site-adaptable in terms of climate [GOV-06].

### Coastal Strawberry — *Fragaria chiloensis*

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 6–10 inches tall, spreading indefinitely by runners; evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — aggressive runner production colonizes quickly

**Ecological function:** Coastal strawberry is a vigorous native groundcover that spreads by stolons (runners) to form dense mats [GOV-06]. White flowers in spring are visited by native bees and syrphid flies. The small red strawberries are eaten by birds, small mammals, and box turtles. The dense leafy mat provides cover for ground beetles and other beneficial predators. Coastal strawberry is one parent of the modern garden strawberry (*Fragaria × ananassa*) — PNW specimens were collected and crossed with Chilean strawberries to create the cultivated crop [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** An excellent lawn alternative for small areas — forms a dense, attractive evergreen mat 6–10 inches tall [PRO-08]. The glossy three-part leaves and white flowers are charming, and the small but flavorful strawberries are a garden bonus (they are smaller and less productive than cultivated varieties). Requires well-drained soil and will spread rapidly in ideal conditions — use edging or barriers if containment is needed [EXT-01]. The closely related woodland strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*) is more shade-tolerant and may be a better choice for understory conditions [PRO-10]. Also excellent between stepping stones, along pathways, and as a living mulch beneath shrubs.

### Maidenhair Fern — *Adiantum aleuticum* (syn. *A. pedatum* var. *aleuticum*)

**USDA zones:** 3–8 (performs best in zones 7b–8b; may stress in 9a summer heat) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full shade to partial shade; does not tolerate direct sun
**Moisture:** Moist to wet; requires consistent moisture; not drought-tolerant
**Mature size:** 12–24 inches tall, 12–18 inch spread; deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — slow to establish, then spreads gently by rhizomes

**Ecological function:** Western maidenhair fern is found in moist, shaded habitats — particularly near waterfalls, seeps, and stream banks where humidity is consistently high [EXT-01, GOV-06]. Its delicate fan-shaped fronds create unique micro-habitat at the soil surface. The species indicates high-quality moist forest conditions and contributes to nutrient cycling in riparian zones. In gardens, it supports the same moisture-loving invertebrate and amphibian communities as in the wild [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** One of the most graceful and beautiful native ferns, with fan-shaped fronds arranged in a semicircular spray on dark, wiry black stems [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The delicate appearance belies reasonable toughness — given consistent moisture and shade, maidenhair fern is long-lived and gradually spreading [EXT-01]. The key requirement is consistent moisture: plant near water features, in rain garden margins, or in naturally moist shade. Deciduous — fronds die back in late fall and emerge fresh in spring. Pair with wild ginger, piggyback plant, and stream violet for a lush riparian garden scene [PRO-08]. Protect from slugs during new frond emergence in spring [EXT-05].

### Salal — *Gaultheria shallon*

**USDA zones:** 7–9 (reliable across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full shade to full sun — one of the most adaptable PNW natives
**Moisture:** Dry to moist; excellent drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 2–6 feet tall (shorter in sun, taller in shade), spreading indefinitely by underground rhizomes; evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to establish (1–2 years), then moderate to fast spreading

**Ecological function:** Salal is a foundational understory species in PNW forests from the coast to the Cascade foothills [GOV-06, EXT-01]. The pink bell-shaped flowers in spring are heavily visited by bumblebees, mason bees, and other native pollinators. Dark purple-blue berries ripen in late summer and are a major food source for band-tailed pigeons, thrushes, waxwings, and bears [PRO-02]. The dense evergreen foliage provides year-round cover and nesting habitat. Salal's aggressive rhizome system makes it a powerful erosion control species and soil stabilizer on slopes [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** Salal is the quintessential PNW native groundcover — it grows almost everywhere, from deep forest shade to exposed coastal bluffs, from wet clay to dry sand [PRO-08, PRO-10]. In deep shade, it grows tall and leggy (4–6 feet); in full sun, it stays compact and dense (2–3 feet). The leathery leaves are the backbone of the PNW floral greens industry. Berries are edible and mildly sweet — traditionally dried into cakes by Indigenous peoples [EXT-01]. Can be aggressive in rich garden soil — best in naturalized areas, under trees, or on challenging sites where its vigor is an asset rather than a problem. Excellent deer resistance. Pairs with sword fern as the two most reliable shade groundcovers in the PNW [PRO-08].

### Inside-out Flower — *Vancouveria hexandra*

**USDA zones:** 5–8 (performs well in zones 7b–8b; may need afternoon shade in 9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full shade to partial shade; does not tolerate direct sun
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately moist; moderate drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 8–16 inches tall, spreading by rhizomes into patches; deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to moderate — gradual rhizomatous spread

**Ecological function:** Inside-out flower is a delicate woodland groundcover native to the moist forests of the PNW [GOV-06]. Its tiny white flowers — whose petals reflex backward, giving the "inside-out" appearance — bloom in May–June and are visited by small native bees and flies. The compound leaves create a lacy, textured groundcover that moderates soil temperature and moisture beneath forest canopy. It contributes to the diverse herbaceous layer that characterizes healthy PNW forest understory [EXT-01, PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** A gem for the shade garden — inside-out flower creates colonies of dainty, airy foliage that resembles a miniature columbine [PRO-08]. The tiny white flowers on wiry stems dance above the foliage in early summer. Spreads steadily by rhizomes but is never aggressive. Excellent companion for sword fern, wild ginger, and trillium in woodland gardens. Deciduous — disappears in winter, so interplant with evergreen companions [PRO-10]. One of the best native groundcovers for the area between shrubs in a shade garden where you want delicate texture rather than bold structure [PRO-08].

### Wild Ginger — *Asarum caudatum*

**USDA zones:** 5–8 (well-suited to zones 7b–8b; benefits from afternoon shade in 9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full shade to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist; prefers consistent moisture; moderate drought tolerance once deeply established
**Mature size:** 4–8 inches tall, spreading by rhizomes; evergreen to semi-evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow — gradual creeping spread via rhizomes

**Ecological function:** Wild ginger carpets the forest floor in moist PNW forests, its large heart-shaped leaves creating dense, overlapping cover at ground level [GOV-06, EXT-01]. The unusual brownish-purple flowers bloom at ground level in spring, pollinated by ground-dwelling beetles and flies rather than aerial pollinators. The dense leaf cover creates ideal micro-habitat for red-backed salamanders, Pacific giant salamanders, and other forest-floor amphibians. Fallen leaves decompose slowly, building rich humus [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** A beautiful, aromatic groundcover — the crushed leaves and rhizomes have a strong ginger scent, though the plant is unrelated to culinary ginger [EXT-01]. Heart-shaped, velvety dark green leaves overlap like shingles to create a dense, weed-suppressing carpet. The hidden ground-level flowers are a conversation piece when discovered. Excellent for filling in between sword fern, trillium, and other woodland plants. Requires consistent moisture — not suitable for dry shade conditions [PRO-08, PRO-10]. One of the few native groundcovers that truly thrives in full shade beneath dense canopy.

## 5. Wildflowers and Perennials

Native wildflowers and perennials bring seasonal color, support specialized pollinators, and connect gardens to the broader PNW landscape. Many of these species are adapted to specific light, moisture, and soil conditions — matching the species to the site is critical for success [EXT-01, PRO-08].

### Common Camas — *Camassia quamash*

**USDA zones:** 4–8 (excellent across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; best in full sun
**Moisture:** Wet to moist in winter/spring; tolerates dry conditions in summer dormancy
**Mature size:** 12–24 inches tall, clumping; deciduous bulb (goes dormant in summer) [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — bulbs multiply steadily, naturalizing over years

**Ecological function:** Common camas is one of the most culturally and ecologically important native plants in the PNW [GOV-06, EXT-01]. Its deep blue to violet flower spikes in April–May are major nectar sources for bumblebees, mason bees, syrphid flies, and butterflies. Historically, camas prairies covered thousands of acres in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, and coastal prairies — providing a nutritious starchy bulb that was a dietary staple for Indigenous peoples [EXT-01]. Today, remnant camas populations indicate intact prairie habitat, and garden plantings help preserve genetic diversity.

**Garden notes:** Plant camas bulbs in fall (September–November) at 3–4 inch depth, in groups of 10 or more for visual impact [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The blue flower spikes are stunning in mass — aim for drifts rather than scattered individuals. Camas thrives in seasonally wet meadow conditions: moist in winter/spring, dry in summer. This makes it ideal for rain garden margins, seasonally wet lawn areas, and bioswale plantings [GOV-06]. Goes completely dormant by July — overplant with later-emerging perennials to fill the gap. **Important:** Do not confuse with death camas (*Zigadenus venenosus*), which has white to cream flowers and is highly toxic — always purchase from reputable nurseries [EXT-01].

### Western Trillium — *Trillium ovatum*

**USDA zones:** 5–8 (adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full shade to partial shade; does not tolerate direct summer sun
**Moisture:** Moist; prefers consistent moisture
**Mature size:** 8–18 inches tall; deciduous perennial from rhizome [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Very slow — may take 5–7 years from seed to first flower

**Ecological function:** Western trillium is an indicator of mature, undisturbed forest understory [GOV-06, PRO-02]. The large three-petaled white flowers (fading to pink-purple with age) are pollinated by early-season bees and beetles. Seeds bear a fatty appendage (elaiosome) that attracts ants, which carry the seeds to their nests — a dispersal strategy called myrmecochory that creates scattered colonies [EXT-01]. Trillium populations are sensitive to habitat disturbance, deer browsing, and unauthorized collection, making garden cultivation a conservation contribution.

**Garden notes:** The iconic spring wildflower of PNW forests — a single white trillium flower above three broad leaves is unmistakable [PRO-08]. Plant in rich, moist, humusy soil beneath deciduous or mixed canopy. Patience is essential: plants from divisions may take 2–3 years to flower, and seed-grown plants take 5–7 years [EXT-01]. NEVER collect trilliums from the wild — purchase nursery-propagated plants only, and verify that the nursery propagates from cultivated stock [PRO-02, PRO-10]. Pairs beautifully with inside-out flower, bleeding heart, and ferns. Once established in a suitable site, trilliums are long-lived (decades) and gradually increase [PRO-08].

### Pacific Bleeding Heart — *Dicentra formosa*

**USDA zones:** 4–8 (reliable across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full shade; tolerates morning sun
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately moist; moderate drought tolerance (goes dormant in dry conditions)
**Mature size:** 12–18 inches tall, 12–18 inch spread; deciduous perennial [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — spreads by rhizomes and self-seeding

**Ecological function:** Pacific bleeding heart is a long-blooming native wildflower that provides nectar from April through July — one of the longest bloom seasons of any PNW native [PRO-02, GOV-06]. The pendant, heart-shaped pink flowers are adapted for long-tongued bumblebees, which are the primary pollinators. Like trillium, bleeding heart seeds have elaiosomes that facilitate ant-dispersed seed distribution. The fernlike foliage creates mid-layer structure in the woodland garden that benefits ground-nesting insects [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** One of the most reliable and rewarding native perennials for shade gardens [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The delicate pink flowers bloom profusely and persistently through spring and into summer. Finely divided, gray-green foliage is attractive even when not in bloom. Spreads moderately by rhizomes and self-seeding — forms lovely drifts in woodland settings but is easily managed [EXT-01]. Goes partially or fully dormant in late summer heat and drought, reemerging with fall rains. Combines beautifully with sword fern, trillium, and wild ginger. Virtually pest-free and deer-resistant [GOV-05, PRO-08].

### Yarrow — *Achillea millefolium* (native ecotypes)

**USDA zones:** 3–9 (extremely hardy across all maritime PNW zones) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun; tolerates very light shade
**Moisture:** Dry to moderately moist; excellent drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 12–36 inches tall, spreading by rhizomes; semi-evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — aggressive rhizomatous spread in ideal conditions

**Ecological function:** Native yarrow is one of the most important beneficial insect plants in the PNW [PRO-04, GOV-06]. The flat-topped white flower clusters (corymbs) attract an extraordinary diversity of pollinators and predatory insects: parasitoid wasps, syrphid flies, lacewings, lady beetles, tachinid flies, native bees, and butterflies [EXT-05, PRO-04]. This makes yarrow a premier "insectary plant" — one that recruits the natural enemies of garden pests. Native ecotypes have white flowers; the colored cultivars commonly sold in nurseries are derived from Eurasian yarrow species and lack the same ecological relationships [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** **Use native white-flowered ecotypes, not colored cultivars** — source from native plant nurseries to ensure local genotype [PRO-02, PRO-08]. The feathery, aromatic foliage is attractive even when not in bloom. Excellent for sunny meadow gardens, parking strips, and dry borders. Can be aggressive in rich, irrigated soil — best in leaner, drier conditions where it stays manageable [EXT-01]. Mow or shear in late summer after flowering to keep tidy and encourage a second flush. One of the best native plants for integrating with food gardens as a beneficial insect attractant along borders and pathways [PRO-04]. Tolerates poor soil, foot traffic, and mowing — sometimes used as a lawn alternative [PRO-08].

### Showy Milkweed — *Asclepias speciosa*

**USDA zones:** 3–9 (adapted across maritime PNW zones, though more common east of Cascades) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun; requires open, sunny conditions
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; tolerates seasonal drought
**Mature size:** 24–48 inches tall, spreading by underground rhizomes; deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate to fast — once established, spreads by vigorous underground rhizomes

**Ecological function:** Showy milkweed is the primary host plant for the western monarch butterfly (*Danaus plexippus*) in the PNW [PRO-02, PRO-04, GOV-06]. Monarch caterpillars feed exclusively on milkweed species — no milkweed means no monarchs. The fragrant pink-purple flower clusters (June–August) are also among the most productive nectar sources for native bees, butterflies, and hummingbird moths. The milky sap contains cardenolide toxins that make monarch caterpillars and adults unpalatable to predators — a classic co-evolutionary defense system [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** Planting milkweed is one of the single most impactful actions a PNW gardener can take for pollinator conservation [PRO-04, PRO-08]. The fragrant, showy flower clusters are beautiful in their own right. **Important:** Use only PNW-native milkweed species (*A. speciosa* or *A. cordifolia*), NOT tropical milkweed (*A. curassavica*), which can harbor the *Ophryocystis elektroscirrha* (OE) parasite that harms monarchs when grown year-round in mild climates [PRO-04]. Spreads vigorously by underground rhizomes — plant where spread is acceptable, or use root barriers [EXT-01]. Goes dormant in late summer and emerges late in spring (May) — mark location to avoid accidental disturbance. The seed pods and silky seeds provide fall and winter garden interest.

### Oregon Iris — *Iris tenax*

**USDA zones:** 7–9 (native to zones 7b–9a in western OR and WA) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; blooms best in sun
**Moisture:** Dry to moderately moist; good drought tolerance once established
**Mature size:** 8–16 inches tall, clumping; semi-evergreen to evergreen [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow to moderate — gradual clump expansion

**Ecological function:** Oregon iris (also called toughleaf iris) is native to open meadows, prairies, and woodland edges in the maritime PNW [GOV-06, EXT-01]. The flowers — ranging from deep blue-purple to lavender, yellow, and white — bloom in May–June and are visited by native bees, particularly bumblebees. The tough, fibrous leaves were traditionally used by Indigenous peoples for cordage [EXT-01]. Oregon iris is one of the few native wildflowers adapted to the dry, nutrient-poor soils of remnant prairies and oak savannas [PRO-02].

**Garden notes:** A delightful native wildflower for sunny, well-drained sites [PRO-08, PRO-10]. The flower color is remarkably variable — a planting of seed-grown Oregon iris will produce a range of blue, purple, lavender, and occasionally yellow or white flowers. Prefers lean soil with minimal fertilization; over-rich conditions produce leaves at the expense of flowers [EXT-01]. Excellent in rock gardens, meadow plantings, and dry borders. Pairs beautifully with camas, shooting star, and native bunch grasses in prairie-style plantings. Semi-evergreen foliage provides year-round presence [PRO-08]. Deer-resistant [PRO-10].

### Red Columbine — *Aquilegia formosa*

**USDA zones:** 4–8 (performs well across zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Partial shade to full sun; blooms well in light shade
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; moderate drought tolerance
**Mature size:** 18–36 inches tall, clumping; deciduous perennial [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Moderate — establishes quickly, short-lived perennial (3–5 years) that self-seeds freely

**Ecological function:** Red columbine is a premier hummingbird plant in the PNW, with red and yellow flowers perfectly shaped for hummingbird bill insertion [PRO-02, PRO-04, GOV-06]. The flowers bloom in May–July, coinciding with rufous hummingbird breeding season. Long-tongued bumblebees also access the nectar by probing between the spurs. The delicate, compound leaves are larval food for the columbine duskywing butterfly (*Erynnis lucilius*). Seeds are eaten by finches and sparrows [EXT-01].

**Garden notes:** The nodding red-and-yellow flowers are instantly recognizable and loved by gardeners and hummingbirds alike [PRO-08, PRO-10]. Red columbine is a short-lived perennial but maintains itself through prolific self-seeding — expect volunteer seedlings wherever conditions are favorable. The blue-green, deeply lobed foliage is attractive even when not in bloom. Excellent in woodland edges, partially shaded borders, and cottage garden settings mixed with native and non-native perennials [EXT-01]. May be affected by columbine leaf miner in some years — damage is cosmetic and does not harm the plant's vigor [GOV-05, EXT-05]. Hybridizes freely with non-native columbines, so plant away from garden hybrids if you want to maintain species integrity [PRO-02].

### Douglas Aster — *Symphyotrichum subspicatum* (syn. *Aster subspicatus*)

**USDA zones:** 5–9 (reliable across all maritime PNW zones) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade
**Moisture:** Moist to moderately dry; tolerates seasonal drought
**Mature size:** 18–36 inches tall, spreading by rhizomes; deciduous [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Fast — vigorous rhizomatous spread

**Ecological function:** Douglas aster is one of the most important late-season nectar sources in the PNW, blooming August–October when most other native flowers have finished [PRO-02, PRO-04, GOV-06]. The purple-blue daisy flowers attract migrating butterflies (painted ladies, monarchs), late-season native bees provisioning for winter, and syrphid flies. The seed heads provide fall and winter food for goldfinches and sparrows. As a late-blooming species, Douglas aster fills a critical nectar gap that few other PNW natives address [PRO-04].

**Garden notes:** Essential for extending native garden bloom into fall — few PNW natives flower as late as Douglas aster [PRO-08]. The blue-purple daisy flowers are profuse and long-lasting. Can be aggressive in rich, moist soil; combine with other vigorous growers like yarrow and goldenrod for a wildflower meadow effect, or contain with root barriers in formal borders [EXT-01]. Benefits from being cut back by half in June ("Chelsea chop") to reduce height and increase flower production [PRO-03, PRO-10]. Excellent companion for showy goldenrod (*Solidago canadensis*) — the classic purple-and-gold fall combination [PRO-08].

### Shooting Star — *Dodecatheon hendersonii*

**USDA zones:** 5–8 (adapted to zones 7b–9a) [GOV-01, EXT-01]
**Sun:** Full sun to partial shade; best in bright conditions with some afternoon shade
**Moisture:** Moist in winter/spring; tolerates dry summer dormancy
**Mature size:** 6–14 inches tall; deciduous bulb-like perennial (goes dormant in summer) [EXT-01]
**Growth rate:** Slow — forms clumps gradually, naturalizes over years

**Ecological function:** Shooting star is a spring ephemeral of PNW prairies and open woodlands [GOV-06, PRO-02]. The distinctive swept-back pink to magenta flowers are buzz-pollinated by bumblebees — the bee grasps the flower and vibrates its flight muscles at a specific frequency to release pollen from the tubular anthers. This specialized pollination mechanism means only bumblebees and a few other vibrating pollinators can effectively pollinate shooting stars [EXT-01]. Shooting star's presence indicates intact prairie or meadow habitat.

**Garden notes:** One of the most charismatic PNW native wildflowers — the swept-back petals and dart-like form are unmistakable [PRO-08]. Plant in groups of 5+ for visual impact. Like camas, shooting star goes completely dormant in summer, disappearing by late June. Excellent paired with camas and Oregon iris in seasonally wet meadow plantings [EXT-01]. Also performs well under Oregon white oak, where the no-irrigation regime matches its evolutionary preferences. Seeds require cold stratification for germination; easiest to establish from nursery-grown plants [PRO-10].

## 6. Plant Community Design

### Natural Associations

Native plants don't grow in isolation — they form communities shaped by light, moisture, soil, and centuries of ecological interaction [EXT-01]. The most successful native gardens mimic these natural associations rather than treating native species as individual ornamentals dropped into conventional landscape designs [PRO-08, GOV-06].

Understanding the natural plant communities of the maritime PNW allows gardeners to create plantings that are ecologically functional, visually harmonious, and inherently low-maintenance because the species are pre-adapted to grow together [EXT-01].

### Key Maritime PNW Plant Communities

**Conifer Forest Understory (shade, moist to moderately dry)**
This is the most common natural plant community west of the Cascades — the classic PNW forest floor beneath Douglas-fir and western redcedar canopy [EXT-01, GOV-06].

| Layer | Species | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Canopy | Douglas-fir, western redcedar | Structure, shade, wildlife habitat |
| Understory tree | Vine maple | Layered canopy, fall color, nesting habitat |
| Tall shrub | Red huckleberry, red elderberry | Fruit, cover, nurse-log specialist |
| Low shrub | Salal, evergreen huckleberry, low Oregon grape (*Mahonia nervosa*) | Evergreen groundcover, fruit, year-round cover |
| Groundcover | Sword fern, wild ginger, inside-out flower | Soil stabilization, amphibian habitat |
| Wildflower | Western trillium, Pacific bleeding heart | Spring color, ant-dispersed seed |

**Garden application:** This community is ideal for shaded lots beneath existing conifers or large deciduous trees. Start with sword fern and salal as the matrix, then add accent species [PRO-08].

**Oak Savanna / Prairie (full sun, dry)**
Oregon white oak ecosystems were once the dominant landscape of the Willamette Valley and parts of the Puget Sound lowlands — now reduced to less than 10% of their historic extent [GOV-06, PRO-02].

| Layer | Species | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Canopy | Oregon white oak | Keystone tree, Lepidoptera host (150+ species) |
| Shrub | Snowberry, Nootka rose, tall Oregon grape | Understory structure, fruit, nesting |
| Bunchgrass | Roemer's fescue (*Festuca roemeri*) | Matrix species, soil stabilization |
| Wildflower | Common camas, Oregon iris, shooting star | Spring bloom, pollinator support |
| Wildflower | Yarrow, Douglas aster | Summer/fall bloom, beneficial insects |

**Garden application:** For sunny, dry areas — especially where irrigation should be minimized. This community requires NO summer irrigation once established, making it ideal for water-conscious gardeners [PRO-08, EXT-01]. **Critical:** Do not irrigate within the drip line of Oregon white oaks.

**Riparian / Wetland Edge (sun to partial shade, wet to moist)**
Stream banks, pond edges, rain garden margins, and naturally wet areas [GOV-06, EXT-01].

| Layer | Species | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Canopy | Western redcedar, red alder (*Alnus rubra*) | Shade, nitrogen fixation (alder), nurse logs |
| Tall shrub | Red elderberry, Pacific wax myrtle | Fast cover, fruit, nitrogen fixation |
| Low shrub | Salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), twinberry (*Lonicera involucrata*) | Dense thicket, fruit, nesting |
| Fern | Maidenhair fern, lady fern (*Athyrium filix-femina*) | Moisture-loving groundcover |
| Wildflower | Common camas, Pacific bleeding heart, stream violet (*Viola glabella*) | Spring color, pollinator support |

**Garden application:** Rain gardens, bioswales, pond margins, and naturally wet areas. Many of these species tolerate seasonal flooding [GOV-06].

**Coastal Bluff / Dry Slope (full sun, dry, well-drained)**
Exposed sites with thin soil, wind, and summer drought [EXT-01, GOV-06].

| Layer | Species | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Shrub | Pacific wax myrtle, oceanspray, tall Oregon grape | Windbreak, erosion control |
| Low shrub | Kinnikinnick, salal (compact sun form) | Mat-forming groundcover |
| Groundcover | Coastal strawberry | Fast-spreading erosion control |
| Wildflower | Yarrow, Oregon iris, red columbine | Color, pollinator support |

**Garden application:** Slopes, retaining walls, parking strips, and any hot, dry, exposed site [PRO-08].

**Mixed Woodland Edge (partial shade, moderately moist)**
The transition zone between forest and open ground — one of the most species-rich habitats in the PNW [EXT-01, GOV-06].

| Layer | Species | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Small tree | Vine maple, bitter cherry, Indian plum | Structure, early bloom, fruit |
| Shrub | Red-flowering currant, oceanspray, Nootka rose | Seasonal bloom sequence, hummingbird nectar, hips |
| Low shrub | Tall Oregon grape, snowberry | Evergreen structure, winter fruit |
| Groundcover | Sword fern, coastal strawberry, inside-out flower | Textural matrix |
| Wildflower | Red columbine, bleeding heart, shooting star | Extended bloom color |

**Garden application:** The most versatile community for typical residential gardens with a mix of sun and shade. The woodland edge provides the greatest species diversity and visual complexity of any PNW plant community [PRO-08].

### Sourcing and Genetic Diversity

**Always source native plants from local genotype nurseries** [EXT-01, PRO-02, GOV-06]. Plants grown from locally collected seed are adapted to your specific climate, soil conditions, and the phenological timing of local pollinators. Native plant nurseries in the maritime PNW that specialize in local-genotype stock include (among others) those listed by the Washington Native Plant Society [PRO-02] and Portland Audubon's native plant resources [PRO-04].

**Why local genotype matters:**
- Bloom timing synchronized with local pollinator emergence [PRO-02]
- Cold hardiness and heat tolerance matched to local climate extremes [GOV-01]
- Disease resistance shaped by co-evolution with local pathogens [EXT-05]
- Genetic diversity supports population resilience under climate change [EXT-07, GOV-06]

**Avoid:** Big-box nursery "native" plants of unknown provenance, native species grown from non-local seed sources, and cultivars/hybrids of native species that may lack ecological function (e.g., double-flowered forms that don't produce accessible nectar) [PRO-02, PRO-08].

**Native plant sales:** Both WNPS [PRO-02] and Portland Audubon [PRO-04] organize annual native plant sales (typically spring and fall) featuring nursery-propagated, local-genotype stock. These events are excellent sources for hard-to-find species and provide expert guidance on species selection and planting.

## 7. Backyard Habitat Certification

### Portland Audubon / Columbia Land Trust Program

The Backyard Habitat Certification Program, a partnership between Portland Audubon and Columbia Land Trust, is the most established residential habitat certification program in the PNW [PRO-04]. Operating in the Portland-Vancouver metropolitan area, the program provides site assessments, plant recommendations, and recognition for homeowners who transform their yards into functional wildlife habitat.

**Certification Levels [PRO-04]:**

| Level | Requirements |
|-------|-------------|
| **Silver** | Minimum native plant coverage; removal of some invasive species; reduce pesticide use; provide water source |
| **Gold** | Higher percentage of native plants; significant invasive removal; eliminate most pesticides; structural habitat features (brush piles, snags, nesting boxes) |
| **Platinum** | Majority native plant coverage; near-complete invasive removal; pesticide-free management; multiple habitat features; naturalistic design principles throughout property |

**Program Features:**
- Free yard assessment by trained volunteer naturalists [PRO-04]
- Customized plant list based on your site conditions (sun, moisture, soil)
- Access to native plant sales and educational workshops
- Yard sign recognizing certification level
- Connection to a community of habitat gardeners

**Key Criteria:**
1. **Native plants:** Increasing coverage of native species appropriate to site conditions [PRO-04, PRO-08]
2. **Invasive species removal:** Systematic elimination of invasive plants (English ivy, Himalayan blackberry, English holly, etc.) — see Cross-Module Connections for invasive management [EXT-05]
3. **Pesticide reduction or elimination:** Moving toward organic and non-chemical pest management [PRO-04, EXT-05]
4. **Habitat features:** Water sources (birdbaths, shallow dishes), shelter (brush piles, dead wood, rock piles), nesting opportunities (birdhouses, bee blocks, unmown areas) [PRO-04]
5. **Stormwater management:** Reducing impervious surfaces, using rain gardens and permeable paths [GOV-06]

**Getting Started:**
The program is designed for gradual transformation — certification does not require converting an entire yard at once. Start by removing one invasive species, planting three native shrubs, and providing a water source. Build from there over multiple seasons [PRO-04]. The program's volunteer naturalists provide ongoing guidance and encouragement. Certification is available for single-family homes, multi-family properties, and community spaces within the Portland-Vancouver metro area.

### Washington Programs

**Washington Native Plant Society (WNPS)** promotes native plant gardening through education, field trips, and native plant sales across Washington state [PRO-02]. While WNPS does not offer a formal yard certification program, they provide:
- Chapter-based native plant sales (major events in spring and fall)
- Educational workshops on native landscaping
- Native plant identification resources and field guides
- Garden tours featuring exemplary native plant gardens
- Stewardship events (invasive removal, native planting at parks and natural areas)

**King County Dept. of Natural Resources and Parks** provides native plant resources including species lists, planting guides, and establishment protocols for residential and community landscapes in the Puget Sound region [GOV-06]. Their resources complement formal certification by providing the technical foundation for successful native planting.

**Tilth Alliance** (Seattle-based) offers workshops and resources on integrating native habitat with food gardening in the maritime PNW [PRO-01]. Their Maritime Northwest Garden Guide includes a section on native plant integration with food production.

**National Wildlife Federation Certified Wildlife Habitat** is available nationwide and recognizes gardens that provide food, water, cover, and places to raise young using native plants. This program is available to PNW gardeners outside the Portland metro area [PRO-04].

### Mason Bees

Mason bees (primarily *Osmia lignaria*, the blue orchard mason bee) are the most effective native spring pollinators in the maritime PNW [PRO-04, GOV-06]. A single mason bee can pollinate as many flowers as 60–100 honeybees due to their "messy" pollen-gathering behavior. Mason bees are solitary, non-aggressive, and readily nest in artificial bee blocks and tubes.

**Supporting mason bees in the native garden:**
- **Nectar and pollen sources:** Oregon grape, camas, red-flowering currant, kinnikinnick, salal — all bloom during mason bee active season (March–May) [PRO-04]
- **Nesting habitat:** Drill blocks (6-inch deep holes, 5/16 inch diameter in untreated wood), hollow reed tubes, or commercial mason bee houses oriented south to southeast [PRO-04]
- **Mud source:** Provide a small area of bare, moist clay soil within 50 feet of nesting sites — mason bees use mud to seal nest cells [PRO-04]
- **Pesticide-free zone:** Mason bees are extremely sensitive to insecticides; maintain a pesticide-free garden during their active season [PRO-04, EXT-05]
- **Overwintering protection:** In mild PNW winters, mason bee cocoons can be left in their tubes outdoors; in colder areas (zone 7b), consider moving tubes to an unheated garage from November to February [PRO-04]

### Hummingbird Plants

The rufous hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) and Anna's hummingbird (*Calypte anna*) are the two primary hummingbird species in the maritime PNW [PRO-02]. Rufous hummingbirds are migratory (present March–September), while Anna's hummingbirds are increasingly year-round residents in urban areas of the PNW.

**Key native hummingbird plants (bloom sequence):**

| Plant | Bloom Period | Flower Color | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Red-flowering currant | Mar–Apr | Pink to red | Primary spring migration fuel [PRO-02] |
| Red columbine | May–Jul | Red and yellow | Breeding season nectar [PRO-02] |
| Red elderberry | Apr–May | Cream (insect-pollinated, but hummingbirds visit) | Secondary nectar [PRO-02] |
| Snowberry | Jun–Jul | Pink | Small flowers, hummingbird-visited [PRO-04] |
| Orange honeysuckle (*Lonicera ciliosa*) | May–Jun | Orange | Tubular native vine, excellent hummingbird plant [PRO-02] |

**Garden design for hummingbirds:** Plant red-flowered natives in clusters (not scattered individuals) to create nectar-rich territories that hummingbirds will defend and return to throughout the season. Include perching branches (dead twigs, trellis) near flower clusters. Provide a shallow water feature with a dripper or mister — hummingbirds are attracted to moving water for bathing [PRO-04].

## 9. Native + Food Garden Integration

### The Complementary Garden

Native plants and food crops are not competing uses of garden space — they are complementary systems that enhance each other's productivity and health when thoughtfully integrated [PRO-01, PRO-04, PRO-08]. Native plants provide the ecological infrastructure (pollinator habitat, beneficial insect reservoir, soil biology) that supports productive food gardening without chemical inputs.

### Natives as Beneficial Insect Habitat

The most effective organic pest management strategy is maintaining resident populations of predatory and parasitoid insects that naturally suppress pest populations [EXT-05, PRO-04]. Native plants — especially yarrow, Douglas aster, and oceanspray — attract and sustain these beneficial insects throughout the season.

**Key insectary plantings for food garden borders:**

| Native Plant | Beneficial Insects Attracted | Garden Pest Targets |
|-------------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*) | Parasitoid wasps, lacewings, syrphid flies, lady beetles | Aphids, caterpillars, thrips, whiteflies [EXT-05, PRO-04] |
| Douglas aster (*S. subspicatum*) | Late-season beneficial insects (syrphid flies, parasitoids) | Late-season aphids, caterpillars [PRO-04] |
| Oceanspray (*Holodiscus discolor*) | Diverse predator community (beetles, flies, wasps) | Broad-spectrum pest suppression [GOV-06] |
| Native roses (*Rosa nutkana*, *R. pisocarpa*) | Syrphid flies, parasitoid wasps | Aphids (native rose aphids attract predators that also patrol crops) [EXT-05] |
| Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*) | Early-season bumblebees, mason bees | Pollination of early fruit crops (blueberries, fruit trees) [PRO-04] |

**Design principle:** Plant native insectary species within 30–50 feet of food crops — this is the effective foraging range for most beneficial insects [EXT-05]. A native hedge along one side of the vegetable garden, a yarrow-and-aster border along a path, or native shrubs at garden corners can provide effective pest suppression [PRO-04, PRO-08].

### Native Windbreaks and Hedgerows

Mixed native hedgerows provide wind protection, reduce moisture loss, moderate temperature extremes, and create linear habitat corridors through the garden landscape [PRO-01, GOV-06].

**Recommended hedgerow composition (layered for full function):**

| Layer | Species | Function |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Tall (back) | Pacific wax myrtle, red elderberry | Wind reduction, nitrogen fixation, fruit |
| Medium (middle) | Nootka rose, tall Oregon grape, snowberry | Thorny barrier, early nectar, overwintering cover |
| Low (front) | Kinnikinnick, coastal strawberry | Groundcover, bank stabilization |

This layered structure reduces wind speed by 50% for a distance of 10× the hedge height [PRO-01]. A 10-foot-tall hedgerow protects crops for 100 feet downwind — significant for maritime PNW gardens exposed to prevailing southwesterly winter winds and northerly summer winds.

### Rain Gardens and Bioswales

Native plants are the optimal choice for rain gardens — stormwater management features that capture roof and pavement runoff, filter pollutants, and infiltrate water into the soil [GOV-06]. Natives are adapted to the wet-winter/dry-summer cycle that rain gardens experience, and they require no fertilization that could leach into waterways.

**Native rain garden species by zone:**

| Zone | Condition | Recommended Species |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| Basin (wettest) | Standing water 24–48 hrs after rain | Common camas, slough sedge (*Carex obnupta*), red elderberry |
| Slope (transitional) | Intermittent moisture | Sword fern, salal, Pacific bleeding heart |
| Rim (driest) | Well-drained, dries between events | Tall Oregon grape, oceanspray, kinnikinnick |

Rain gardens positioned to capture runoff from food garden irrigation overflow create a closed water loop that maximizes water use efficiency [GOV-06]. The native plantings filter excess nutrients from garden soil runoff before it enters storm drains.

### Integration Design Patterns

**Pattern 1: Native border around food garden**
Ring the vegetable garden with a 4–6 foot border of yarrow, Oregon grape, and native roses. This provides beneficial insect habitat, pollinator support for food crops, and a beautiful frame for the productive garden [PRO-08, PRO-04].

**Pattern 2: Native hedgerow as garden room divider**
Use a mixed native hedgerow to separate garden "rooms" — creating a windbreak for tender crops while providing year-round habitat. Wax myrtle and tall Oregon grape make excellent evergreen dividers [PRO-01, PRO-08].

**Pattern 3: Native understory in fruit orchard**
Plant sword fern, wild ginger, and native groundcovers beneath fruit trees instead of turf. This supports beneficial insects, retains moisture, builds soil, and eliminates mowing [PRO-08, PRO-04].

**Pattern 4: Pollinator corridor connecting food garden to native area**
If native plantings and food gardens are in different parts of the yard, connect them with a flower-rich corridor (yarrow, aster, native grasses) that pollinators and beneficial insects can follow between habitats [PRO-04].

**Pattern 5: Edible-native matrix**
Interplant native huckleberries (edible fruit) with food-producing shrubs like blueberries (which share similar soil preferences — acidic, well-drained). Oregon grape and native strawberries can serve double duty as productive landscape plants and ecological contributors [PRO-08, EXT-01].

| **M1: Climate & Soil** | Native plant selection depends on USDA zone [GOV-01], soil pH [GOV-04], and moisture regime [GOV-03]. The wet-winter/dry-summer pattern (M1) is the fundamental selective force that shaped native plant adaptations (M3). Soil acidity (pH 5.0–6.5 typical of PNW soils) favors most native species without amendment [GOV-04, EXT-01]. | USDA zones, soil pH, microclimate, drought stress, cold-air drainage |

Common native plant pests and diseases are covered in M4, including management strategies compatible with habitat goals [GOV-05, EXT-05]. | Beneficial insects, IPM, insectary plants, pesticide-free management |
