# Biodiversity of the Pacific Northwest Temperate Rainforest: A Network Analysis

### Ecological Relationships, Cascading Dependencies, and Conservation Implications Across the Highest-Biomass Terrestrial Ecosystem on Earth

*PNW Rainforest Biodiversity Research, v1.49.22*

---

## Executive Summary

The Pacific Northwest temperate rainforest -- stretching from the Olympic Peninsula through the Cascade Range to the Columbia River Gorge and the Oregon Coast Range -- functions not as four separate biological communities but as a single integrated system. This document maps that system. Drawing on government agency data, peer-reviewed research, and conservation organization reports (19 primary sources), it documents more than 1,000 species across four study zones and reveals the ecological relationships that bind them into a functioning whole.

The core thesis is structural: biodiversity in the PNW temperate rainforest is a network of relationships, not a list of species. The evidence for this claim comes from three multi-step ecological cascades that cross traditional survey boundaries and connect organisms that appear, at first glance, to inhabit separate worlds.

**Scope and scale.** The study region spans a precipitation gradient from over 200 inches annually on exposed Olympic ridges to fewer than 15 inches in the rain-shadow east of the Columbia River Gorge (GOV-04, GOV-02). Four study zones -- the Olympic Peninsula, Cascade western slopes, Columbia River Gorge, and Oregon Coast Range -- collectively support more than 800 vascular plant species, 244 documented fauna species (22 mammals, 204 birds, 18 amphibians and reptiles), over 200 ectomycorrhizal fungal species per old-growth stand, and 51 fish species including 12 individually managed salmon stocks (GOV-01, GOV-04, GOV-06, PR-04).

**Three cascades as structural evidence.** The network architecture of the ecosystem is demonstrated by three formally documented cascades:

- **CASCADE-01 (Salmon-Forest Nutrient Cascade)** traces marine-derived nitrogen from the Pacific Ocean through six species of Pacific salmon, bear-mediated forest deposition, invertebrate decomposition, ectomycorrhizal fungal uptake, and redistribution through Common Mycorrhizal Networks connecting dozens of trees. This cascade spans all four survey domains -- aquatic, fauna, flora, and fungi -- and delivers 40-80% of the nitrogen in riparian vegetation (PR-02). Trees near salmon streams grow up to three times faster than equivalent trees along salmon-free streams (Reynolds, SFU, 50-watershed study).

- **CASCADE-02 (Truffle-Mammal-Raptor Cascade)** links old-growth fungal diversity to spotted owl conservation through two intermediate trophic levels. Old-growth stands support four times the truffle diversity of young plantations (GOV-06). These truffles constitute 80-90% of the northern flying squirrel's diet (GOV-05). Flying squirrels are the primary prey of the northern spotted owl, which has declined 3.8% annually in Washington (GOV-03). The owl crisis is, in part, a fungi crisis.

- **CASCADE-03 (Climate-Gradient-Endemic Cascade)** traces how climate change propagates through the precipitation gradient that defines the Gorge's endemic species. Warming narrows the moisture bands that 15 Gorge-endemic wildflowers depend upon, while simultaneously threatening the microclimate refugia that sustain anomalous low-elevation American pika populations at 30-90 meters -- over 2,000 meters below their typical range (GOV-01, GOV-02, CON-01).

**Four hub nodes.** Network analysis identifies four hub nodes whose removal would cause disproportionate system degradation: Pacific salmon (137+ dependent species across all trophic levels), Douglas fir (mother tree connecting to 47 neighbors via Common Mycorrhizal Networks), ectomycorrhizal fungi (200+ species in old-growth, mediating nutrient exchange), and old-growth forest structure itself (the physical scaffold upon which all three cascades depend) (PR-02, PR-05, GOV-06, PR-04).

**Three critical edges.** The network's most vulnerable connections are: the bear-salmon nutrient ferry (step 2 of CASCADE-01, transporting marine nitrogen into the forest interior), the ectomycorrhizal-tree interface (the Hartig net where nutrient exchange occurs), and the canopy-microhabitat dependency (old-growth canopy maintaining conditions for marbled murrelet nesting, Larch Mountain salamander survival, and pika thermal refugia) (PR-02, GOV-05, GOV-04).

**Conservation bottom line.** Of 17 identified climate threat vectors, cascade-mediated threats (8) outnumber direct threats (9). This means that protecting individual species in isolation is structurally insufficient. Conservation strategy must protect hub nodes and maintain critical edges -- the network architecture itself. Twelve specific conservation interventions are already underway across the study region, from head-starting programs for northwestern pond turtles (CON-01, CON-02) to experimental barred owl removal for spotted owl recovery (GOV-03) to salmon habitat restoration (CON-05). The evidence presented here supports a three-tier conservation framework: hub node protection, critical edge maintenance, and species-specific management within that network context.

## Introduction

The Pacific Northwest temperate rainforest constitutes the highest-biomass terrestrial ecosystem on Earth (PR-04). Stretching from the fog-drenched Olympic Peninsula through the volcanic Cascade Range, down the Oregon Coast Range, and tapering into the dramatic rain-shadow transition of the Columbia River Gorge, this region supports biological diversity shaped fundamentally by moisture. A precipitation gradient spanning from over 200 inches annually on exposed Olympic ridges to fewer than 15 inches in the rain-shadow east of the Gorge governs the distribution of every organism documented in this study (GOV-04, GOV-02).

This document maps the biodiversity of that region -- not as a catalog of species, but as a network of ecological relationships. The distinction matters. A species list tells you what lives here. A network analysis tells you how it works, what breaks when components are removed, and where conservation investment produces the greatest return. The core value proposition of this research is that biodiversity in the PNW temperate rainforest is better understood as a web of dependencies than as an inventory of parts.

The study encompasses four geographic zones:

1. **Olympic Peninsula** -- the wettest region in the contiguous United States, supporting the Hoh, Quinault, and Queets rainforests with annual precipitation exceeding 200 inches (GOV-04). Roosevelt elk herds, Sitka spruce-hemlock old-growth, and marine-influenced coastal ecosystems define this zone.

2. **Cascade western slopes** -- volcanic topography creating elevation gradients from lowland old-growth through montane forest to subalpine meadows and alpine zones above 2,400 meters (GOV-02). The Cascades harbor the full elevational range of PNW biodiversity, from salmon-bearing rivers to whitebark pine at treeline.

3. **Columbia River Gorge** -- the only sea-level breach in the Cascade Range, creating a 130-kilometer wind corridor that compresses the full maritime-to-continental climate transition into a single landscape (GOV-01, GOV-03). The Gorge supports 15 endemic wildflower species found nowhere else on Earth, anomalous low-elevation pika populations, and the globally restricted Larch Mountain salamander.

4. **Oregon Coast Range** -- continuous forest cover from coast to interior, supporting wide-ranging carnivores, old-growth dependent birds, and intact riparian corridors connecting coastal and interior ecosystems (PR-04).

The document is organized as follows: a description of methods and source framework; four domain chapters covering flora, fauna, fungi, and aquatic systems; a network synthesis chapter revealing the cross-domain cascades and hub-node architecture; a climate threats and conservation chapter grounding recommendations in evidence; and a complete bibliography.

## Study Area and Methods

### Geographic Scope

The four study zones collectively span approximately 10 degrees of latitude (42-48 degrees N) and an elevational range from sea level to above 2,400 meters. The unifying geographic feature is the moisture gradient: marine-influenced air masses moving eastward from the Pacific Ocean encounter the Coast Range and Cascade Range, producing extreme orographic precipitation on western slopes and rain-shadow conditions on eastern exposures. This gradient creates a compression of climate zones found nowhere else in North America -- within the Columbia River Gorge alone, vegetation transitions from coastal rainforest to semi-arid steppe in fewer than 130 kilometers (GOV-01, GOV-03).

### Species Documentation Framework

Species entries throughout this document follow a standardized template encompassing identity fields (common name, scientific name, taxonomic hierarchy), distribution fields (zones present, habitat description, elevation range, endemic status), conservation fields (federal status, state status for Washington and Oregon, IUCN status, population trend), and domain-specific extensions for flora (canopy layer, growth form, old-growth indicator status), fauna (diet type, migratory status, breeding habitat, anomalous elevation), fungi (mycorrhizal type, host tree species, old-growth dependency, commercial harvest status), and aquatic species (life history, spawning habitat, marine-derived nutrient transport).

### Ecological Relationship Framework

Ecological relationships are documented using a formal schema with four primary types -- predator-prey, symbiotic, nutrient transfer, and competition -- each with defined subtypes, directionality (unidirectional or bidirectional), strength (obligate, facultative, or opportunistic), and cross-domain flags indicating whether the relationship spans survey boundaries. Cascade relationships trace multi-step ecological chains across domains, with each step documented individually and the cascade's significance lying in the chain as a whole.

## Flora

### The Moisture Gradient and Its Communities

The vascular plant diversity of the Pacific Northwest temperate rainforest is shaped by water. More than 800 species of vascular plants inhabit the four study zones, their distribution governed by a precipitation gradient spanning from over 200 inches annually on exposed Olympic ridges to fewer than 15 inches in the rain-shadow east of the Gorge (GOV-04, GOV-02, PR-04).

**Olympic Peninsula.** The wettest forests on the continent support massive Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) and western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*) draped in epiphytic mosses, liverworts, and ferns. Sitka spruce emergents reach 60-85 meters, with individual trees exceeding 1,000 years of age. The understory is dominated by sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*), salal (*Gaultheria shallon*), and devil's club (*Oplopanax horridus*). Nearly every surface -- soil, rock, trunk, branch -- is carpeted with bryophytes. Epiphyte biomass in the wettest stands reaches 2-5 tonnes per hectare dry weight, among the highest measured in any terrestrial ecosystem (PR-04, GOV-04).

**Cascade western slopes.** Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) dominates the lowland to mid-elevation forests of the western Cascades, transitioning to Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*) and mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*) at higher elevations. The Cascades harbor the full elevational range of PNW plant diversity, from riparian red alder (*Alnus rubra*) at valley bottoms to whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) at treeline. Douglas fir is the ecological keystone of this zone -- the mother tree of mycorrhizal network architecture, connecting to up to 47 neighboring trees via shared fungal networks (PR-05).

**Columbia River Gorge.** The Gorge compresses the full maritime-to-continental vegetation transition into a single landscape. Western reaches support mesic forest with Douglas fir and bigleaf maple; eastern reaches transition to Oregon white oak, ponderosa pine, and bunchgrass steppe. Between these extremes, the Gorge's unique geology -- stepped Columbia River Basalt flows creating cliff faces, talus slopes, waterfall spray zones, and vernal pools -- supports 15 endemic wildflower species found nowhere else on Earth (GOV-03, CON-01).

**Oregon Coast Range.** Continuous forest cover dominated by Douglas fir, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce, with extensive red alder in disturbed and riparian areas. The Coast Range maintains some of the last large contiguous blocks of old-growth forest in the Pacific Northwest (PR-04).

### The 15 Gorge Endemic Wildflowers

The Columbia River Gorge harbors one of the most concentrated assemblages of endemic plant species in North America. Fifteen wildflower species are restricted entirely to the Gorge corridor, each adapted to microhabitats created by the region's unique combination of basalt geology, compressed moisture gradient, and Pleistocene history (GOV-03, CON-01).

These endemics partition the Gorge's moisture gradient with remarkable precision. Dry-site lithosol specialists -- Barrett's penstemon (*Penstemon barrettiae*), Suksdorf's desert parsley (*Lomatium suksdorfii*), Columbia desert parsley (*Lomatium columbianum*), Thompson's fleabane (*Erigeron thompsonii*), and Columbia Gorge crazyweed (*Oxytropis campestris* var. *columbiana*) -- occupy basalt outcrops and thin-soil habitats at the rain-shadow end of the gradient. Spray-zone and seepage specialists -- Oregon bolandra (*Bolandra oregana*), the Columbia Gorge daisy (*Erigeron oreganus*), and Howell's daisy (*Erigeron howellii*) -- depend on waterfall mist and cliff-face moisture in the hyper-wet western Gorge. Spring ephemerals -- poet's shooting star (*Dodecatheon poeticum*) and obscure buttercup (*Ranunculus reconditus*) -- exploit the brief wet window of the transition zone. Columbia yellowcress (*Rorippa columbiae*), federally listed as threatened, depends on the dynamic sandy riverbanks of the Columbia River itself. Oregon semaphore grass (*Pleuropogon oregonus*), federally listed as endangered, requires permanent shallow freshwater (GOV-03, CON-01).

Barrett's penstemon, growing from basalt cliff faces that no other penstemon can colonize, and the Columbia Gorge daisy, restricted to moist shaded basalt cliff seepage zones, exemplify the Gorge's role as a botanical refugium. These species are living records of Pleistocene climate history -- populations that were widespread during cooler, moister periods became isolated in Gorge microrefugia as the climate warmed during the Holocene, diverging into the endemics we see today (CON-01, GOV-01).

Convergent threats affect all 15 species: invasive plant competition (11 of 15 species), habitat alteration by infrastructure, and climate change that shifts the precise moisture bands each species requires. The most immediately actionable threat -- invasive plant management -- is also the one where conservation intervention has the strongest track record: ivy removal on cliff faces, reed canarygrass control in wetlands, and cheatgrass management on lithosol habitats are high-priority actions with demonstrated effectiveness (CON-01).

### Old-Growth Forest Structure

Old-growth PNW temperate rainforest is not merely old forest. It is a structurally distinct ecosystem characterized by multi-layered canopy architecture, massive individual trees, heavy epiphyte loading, large-diameter snags, nurse logs in all stages of decomposition, and canopy gaps that create the light heterogeneity driving species diversity from the forest floor to the emergent layer (PR-04, GOV-04). Less than 10% of the Pacific Northwest's original old-growth forest remains (PR-04).

**Canopy architecture.** The vertical stratification of old-growth PNW forest creates six distinct layers:

- **Emergent layer (60-85 m):** Giant Douglas fir and Sitka spruce, 500-1,000+ years old, with trunk diameters of 1.5-3+ meters. These are the mother trees of Simard's research, each connected to up to 47 neighbors via shared mycorrhizal networks (PR-03, PR-05).

- **Canopy layer (30-60 m):** Shade-tolerant western hemlock, western red cedar, and Pacific silver fir forming a continuous canopy with leaf area index (LAI) of 8-14 -- intercepting more than 95% of incoming solar radiation (PR-04).

- **Understory tree layer (10-30 m):** Pacific yew (*Taxus brevifolia*), the signature old-growth indicator requiring centuries of stable canopy to reach maturity; vine maple (*Acer circinatum*); bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) carrying up to 35 kg of epiphytic mosses per tree (PR-04, GOV-04).

- **Shrub layer (1-10 m):** Salal, Oregon grape, devil's club, and red huckleberry -- the last characteristically establishing on nurse logs rather than forest floor (GOV-04).

- **Herb/ground layer (<1 m):** Sword fern, deer fern, oxalis, vanilla leaf, and a bryophyte carpet covering nearly every surface (GOV-04).

- **Epiphyte loading (throughout):** 2-5 tonnes per hectare of mosses, lichens, liverworts, and ferns, including *Lobaria oregana* (lettuce lichen), which fixes atmospheric nitrogen at rates of 1.5-5 kg per hectare per year -- a significant input in nitrogen-limited old-growth ecosystems (GOV-05, PR-04).

**Biomass and carbon.** Above-ground live tree biomass in PNW old-growth ranges from 500 to over 1,000 tonnes per hectare in the wettest stands -- exceeding tropical rainforests, which have faster growth rates but shorter-lived trees and faster decomposition (PR-04). Total ecosystem carbon stocks reach 800-1,500 tonnes of carbon per hectare. Logging old-growth releases centuries of accumulated carbon in a single pulse; the carbon debt is measured in centuries, not decades (PR-04, PR-01).

### Nurse Log Regeneration

The nurse log cycle is one of the most distinctive features of PNW old-growth forest. When a large canopy tree falls, its trunk begins a multi-century transformation from deadwood to seedling nursery to soil. Nurse logs support 10-100 times the seedling density of adjacent forest floor; in Olympic Peninsula old-growth, 70-90% of western hemlock and 80-95% of Sitka spruce seedlings establish on nurse logs rather than bare ground (PR-04, GOV-04).

The cycle proceeds through six stages: tree fall, early colonization by fungi and insects (years 1-50), nurse log establishment as decomposing wood becomes seedling substrate (years 50-200), colonnade formation as multiple seedlings grow along the log's length (years 100-400), log incorporation as the nurse log fully decomposes leaving stilt-rooted trees (years 200-500+), and cycle renewal when colonnade trees eventually fall and become new nurse logs (PR-04).

Decomposition timelines are species-dependent: western hemlock logs decompose in 100-250 years, Douglas fir in 200-400 years, and western red cedar -- whose natural rot-resistance prolongs structural integrity -- in 400-600+ years (PR-04, GOV-04). The nurse log cycle is inseparable from the fungal community that drives it: saprotrophic fungi decompose the wood, and as decomposition proceeds, the fungal community transitions to include ectomycorrhizal species connected to the surrounding forest's Common Mycorrhizal Network, providing seedlings with immediate network access (GOV-05, PR-05).

### Cross-Domain Connections from Flora

Old-growth canopy complexity provides the only suitable nesting habitat for marbled murrelets and northern spotted owls. Nurse logs bridge the flora and fungi domains through the saprotrophic-to-mycorrhizal fungal transition. Riparian old-growth provides shade maintaining cold water temperatures for salmon and large woody debris creating pool-riffle habitat. Douglas fir mother trees are the hub nodes of Common Mycorrhizal Networks connecting the entire below-ground ecosystem (PR-04, GOV-04, PR-03, PR-05).

## Fauna

### An Ecological Network, Not a Species List

The PNW temperate rainforest supports 244 documented animal species across the four study zones: 22 mammals with full ecological profiles, 204 birds (40 detailed profiles plus 164 species in summary tables), and 18 amphibians and reptiles. These animals are not isolated taxonomic entries. They are nodes in ecological networks whose connections define ecosystem function (PR-04, PR-01).

### Large Mammals and Ecological Roles

**Black bear (*Ursus americanus*)** is the primary terrestrial vector for marine-derived nutrients. Individual bears ferry an estimated 500-700 salmon per season from spawning streams into the surrounding forest, depositing carcass remnants and scat that deliver marine-origin nitrogen across the forest floor. Isotopic analysis confirms that 40-80% of riparian nitrogen originates from the ocean via this pathway (PR-02). This predator-prey relationship with Pacific salmon forms step 2 of CASCADE-01, the most consequential single ecological link in the study area.

**Roosevelt elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*)** is the flagship subspecies of the Olympic Peninsula and the largest-bodied elk in North America, with herds numbering approximately 5,000 individuals in Olympic National Park (GOV-04). Elk herbivory shapes forest structure: heavy browsing in riparian corridors creates open gravel bars favoring salmon spawning habitat, while grazing in subalpine meadows maintains herbaceous diversity.

**Cougar (*Puma concolor*)** regulates ungulate populations through predation on black-tailed deer, creating an indirect trophic cascade that benefits forest regeneration. Home ranges of 100-300 square kilometers require large contiguous forest tracts (GOV-04).

**River otter (*Lontra canadensis*)** transfers aquatic nutrients into the terrestrial zone through scat deposition along stream banks, creating localized nutrient hotspots enriching riparian vegetation (GOV-04).

### Old-Growth Dependent Birds

**Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*)** -- federally threatened, listed in 1990 -- requires large contiguous tracts of old-growth forest for nesting, roosting, and foraging. Individual territory sizes range from 2,200 acres in productive Oregon forests to 14,000 acres on the Olympic Peninsula (GOV-03). The northern flying squirrel comprises 50-80% of spotted owl diet, creating a direct dependency chain: owl depends on flying squirrel, which depends on ectomycorrhizal truffles, which depend on old-growth root networks. Spotted owl populations have declined 3.8% per year in Washington, with barred owl invasion identified as the primary proximate cause (GOV-03, CON-01). Federal management now includes experimental barred owl removal.

**Marbled murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*)** -- federally threatened, with an estimated 96% decline from historical levels -- is a seabird that flies up to 80 kilometers inland to nest on moss-covered branches of old-growth conifers (GOV-04). Its nesting behavior was not confirmed until 1974. The murrelet requires the thick moss mats that develop only on large horizontal branches of ancient trees, creating a direct link between marine and forest ecosystems.

**Pileated woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*)** is a primary ecosystem engineer whose excavated cavities are subsequently used by dozens of species that cannot excavate their own -- including northern flying squirrels, Vaux's swifts, American martens, and bats. A single pair's territory may provide 10-20 old cavities used by a rotating community of secondary cavity nesters (GOV-03).

### Small Mammals as Network Connectors

**Northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*)** is the keystone species linking above-ground fauna to below-ground fungal networks. Flying squirrels are the primary dispersal agent for ectomycorrhizal truffles, particularly *Rhizopogon* species that form the Common Mycorrhizal Network connecting Douglas fir trees (PR-03, PR-05). When flying squirrels consume truffles, fungal spores pass through their digestive tract and are deposited across the forest, inoculating new root zones. This mutualistic relationship is the critical cross-domain link in CASCADE-02 -- the obligate bridge between the mycorrhizal network and the spotted owl (GOV-05).

**American pika (*Ochotona princeps*)** at standard Cascade elevations (2,500-4,000 m) is a climate-sensitive alpine indicator with a lethal thermal threshold at approximately 25-28 degrees C. The anomalous low-elevation Gorge populations (see Flagship Species below) represent a natural experiment in thermal vulnerability with broader implications for climate refugia across the region (GOV-01, GOV-02).

### Amphibians and Reptiles

**Larch Mountain salamander (*Plethodon larselli*)** is the flagship endemic of the Columbia River Gorge and one of the most geographically restricted vertebrates in North America. A lungless salamander breathing entirely through its skin, it requires the cool, humid microclimate maintained by old-growth canopy above moss-covered basalt talus slopes. Canopy removal causes microhabitat desiccation lethal to the species within a single season (GOV-01, GOV-03, CON-01). Its presence serves as a bioindicator of intact old-growth microhabitat conditions.

**Coastal tailed frog (*Ascaphus truei*)** possesses one of the most ancient lineages among living frogs, with a fossil record extending over 200 million years. Its strict dependence on cold, clear, undisturbed headwater streams makes it one of the most sensitive indicators of old-growth watershed health (GOV-01, GOV-04).

**Northwestern pond turtle (*Actinemys marmorata*)** -- the only native freshwater turtle in the Pacific Northwest -- has experienced dramatic range contraction. Conservation efforts represent one of the most active multi-faceted wildlife recovery programs in the region, involving six intervention types: head-starting programs (the Oregon Zoo, Woodland Park Zoo), bullfrog control, habitat restoration, nest protection, population monitoring, and land acquisition by organizations including the Columbia Land Trust (GOV-01, GOV-03, CON-01, CON-02).

### Flagship Species: American Pika at Anomalous Low Elevation

In the Columbia River Gorge, American pika populations persist at 30-90 meters elevation -- a full 2,000+ meters below the typical lower elevation limit documented across western North America. The explanation lies in the Gorge's unique physical geography: deep basalt talus slopes accumulate cold air through gravitational drainage, and old-growth canopy shading on north-facing slopes further depresses temperatures, creating microrefugia where conditions at 60 meters replicate those found at 2,500 meters in the open Cascades (GOV-01, CON-01).

These populations may represent Pleistocene relicts stranded in Gorge microclimates as postglacial warming eliminated suitable habitat in surrounding lowlands. Unlike high-elevation populations that can theoretically shift upslope, Gorge pikas are already at the bottom of the landscape. There is no cooler elevation to retreat to. Projected temperature increases of 4.7-10 degrees F across the Pacific Northwest (GOV-02) would reduce the microclimate buffering that sustains these populations. Monitoring Gorge pikas serves as an early warning system for climate impacts on talus-dependent species throughout the region (GOV-02, CON-01).

## Fungi

### The Hidden Infrastructure

Beneath the visible forest lies a second forest made of thread. Fungal hyphae, each thinner than a human hair, weave through every cubic centimeter of forest soil, wrapping tree roots, penetrating decaying logs, and connecting organisms that appear, above ground, to be solitary. In PNW temperate rainforests, fungi are not peripheral organisms. They are the connective infrastructure -- the communication network, the nutrient highway, the memory of the forest encoded in mycelium.

### Mycorrhizal Network Architecture

**Ectomycorrhizal fungi (EMF)** form the dominant below-ground association in PNW coniferous forests. Fungal hyphae grow around the exterior of tree root tips, forming a dense sheath called the mantle, then penetrate inward between cortical cells creating the Hartig net -- the intercellular interface where nutrient exchange occurs. Douglas fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, and western red cedar are all EMF-dependent; without their fungal partners, these trees cannot access soil phosphorus and nitrogen efficiently enough to sustain old-growth biomass densities. Up to 20% of a tree's total fixed carbon may be allocated to its mycorrhizal partners (GOV-05).

**Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF)** represent an older evolutionary lineage (400+ million years). AMF hyphae penetrate cell walls, forming branched intracellular structures called arbuscules. Though less dominant than EMF in PNW conifer forests, AMF are critical for understory herbaceous plants and for red alder, which forms a three-way symbiosis with AMF fungi and nitrogen-fixing *Frankia* bacteria, making it a key nitrogen source in young forests and disturbed riparian areas.

### Common Mycorrhizal Networks and the Mother Tree Model

A single fungal mycelium can colonize the roots of multiple trees simultaneously, creating a Common Mycorrhizal Network (CMN) through which carbon, water, nitrogen, phosphorus, and chemical defense signals flow between connected trees. The foundational evidence came from Simard et al. (1997), who demonstrated bidirectional carbon transfer between paper birch and Douglas fir via shared ectomycorrhizal networks using carbon isotope labeling (PR-03).

Simard's subsequent decades of research (PR-05) revealed that CMNs have topology. Large, old trees -- mother trees -- function as hub nodes. A single large Douglas fir can connect to 47 other trees via shared mycorrhizal fungi. These hub trees preferentially allocate resources to kin seedlings, recognizing offspring through chemical signaling at the root-fungal interface.

The CMN has four documented functions: carbon redistribution from sunlit adults to shaded seedlings; water sharing during drought; chemical defense signaling that triggers protective responses in connected neighbors before they are attacked; and nitrogen and phosphorus transfer from nutrient-rich to nutrient-poor areas. Removing mother trees destroys the hub nodes. Clearcutting does not merely remove trees -- it fragments the CMN, eliminating carbon subsidies to seedlings, severing defense signaling, and collapsing nutrient redistribution. Seedling survival rates decline measurably in clearcuts compared to partial harvests retaining mother trees (PR-05).

### Fungal Diversity and Forest Age

The relationship between forest age and fungal community composition is a successional trajectory taking centuries to complete. Young plantations (0-30 years) support 20-40 EMF species per stand. Old-growth forests (200+ years) support over 200 EMF species per stand (GOV-06). The critical metric is truffle diversity: hypogeous EMF species in the genera *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, and *Hysterangium* are four times more diverse in old-growth than in young plantations (GOV-06). This 4x truffle diversity ratio is the clearest quantitative indicator of old-growth fungal community integrity.

The difference reflects specific structural features of old-growth that cannot be replicated in younger stands: large-diameter nurse logs providing decomposition substrates, deep organic soil layers accumulated over centuries, structural complexity generating microhabitat diversity for fungal fruiting, and old-growth trees serving as hub nodes with the most extensive root systems and therefore the most extensive mycorrhizal networks.

EMF community composition may take 150-200 years or more to recover after clearcutting (GOV-06). A single rotation of industrial forestry (typically 40-60 years) harvests a forest long before its fungal community has recovered from the previous harvest.

### Ecologically Significant Species

**Pacific golden chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*)** -- Oregon's official state mushroom -- is an EMF partner of Douglas fir, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce, supporting a multi-million dollar annual harvest industry. Not old-growth dependent, but fruiting body size and abundance are significantly greater in mature and old-growth forests (GOV-05).

**Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*)** is the most critically imperiled macrofungus in PNW forests and one of the clearest biological indicators of old-growth conditions. It fruits exclusively on old-growth conifers typically 200+ years old, requiring thick, deeply furrowed bark and large trunk diameter for attachment. Once widespread across northern hemisphere temperate forests, it has been extirpated from most of Europe and is now effectively restricted to remnant PNW old-growth (GOV-06). Where agarikon survives, old-growth conditions persist.

**American matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*)** forms persistent mycelial mats called "shiros" that can persist for decades, slowly expanding outward. Commercially harvested primarily for export to Japanese markets (GOV-05).

**PNW truffle complex (*Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium* spp.)** -- profiled as a functional group rather than individual species. These hypogeous EMF fungi are the primary food source for northern flying squirrels (80-90% of diet) and California red-backed voles. Squirrel consumption disperses truffle spores via fecal pellets, creating a mutualistic dispersal loop. The truffle complex is the foundation of CASCADE-02 (GOV-06).

**Lung lichen (*Lobaria pulmonaria*)** fixes atmospheric nitrogen through its cyanobacterial photobiont, contributing 1.5-5 kg of nitrogen per hectare per year -- a significant input in nitrogen-limited old-growth canopy ecosystems. Old-growth dependent: requires the deeply furrowed bark of large, old trees (GOV-05).

## Aquatic Systems

### The Salmon-Forest Nitrogen Pathway

Pacific salmon are the primary vector for marine-derived nutrients entering terrestrial ecosystems in the Pacific Northwest. The marine-derived nitrogen pathway is the single most important nutrient subsidy connecting ocean and forest, and its operation is traced through five quantitative steps using the naturally enriched nitrogen isotope N-15 as a tracer (PR-02).

**Step 1: Ocean accumulation.** Six Pacific salmon species -- Chinook, coho, sockeye, chum, pink, and steelhead -- spend 1-5 years feeding in the North Pacific Ocean, accumulating marine-derived nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon. The marine N-15 signature (delta-N-15 values of +10 to +14 per mil) is isotopically distinct from terrestrial nitrogen (+0 to +3 per mil), making it a reliable tracer (PR-02, GOV-01). A single adult Chinook carries 15-20 kg of marine-origin biomass.

**Step 2: Spawning migration.** Salmon transport marine biomass upstream into freshwater. The Columbia River basin alone historically supported an estimated 7-16 million adult salmon returning per year, representing a massive marine subsidy concentrated at specific spawning reaches (PR-02, CON-05).

**Step 3: Bear-mediated forest deposition.** Black bears ferry 500-700 salmon per season from streams into the forest, carrying carcasses up to 500 meters from stream banks. Bears preferentially consume brain and roe, leaving 40-60% of carcass biomass on the forest floor -- the portions with the highest marine nitrogen concentrations. Additional vertebrate vectors include bald eagles (up to 1 km from stream), river otters, wolves, and mink (PR-02, CON-05).

**Step 4: Decomposition and soil uptake.** Terrestrial invertebrates and soil microorganisms decompose salmon carcasses, releasing marine-derived nitrogen into the soil nutrient pool. Hocking and Reimchen (2002) demonstrated that marine-derived N-15 is detectable in terrestrial invertebrates at all trophic levels, from primary decomposers to tertiary consumers (PR-02).

**Step 5: Tree uptake and growth response.** Riparian conifers absorb marine-derived nitrogen through root systems aided by ectomycorrhizal networks. The Reynolds 50-watershed study at Simon Fraser University found that trees near salmon streams grow up to three times faster than equivalent trees along salmon-free streams. Marine-derived nitrogen has been detected in tree rings, enabling historical reconstruction of salmon abundance going back centuries (PR-02).

In watersheds with healthy salmon runs, 40-80% of the nitrogen in riparian vegetation is of marine origin. This finding, based on N-15 isotope analysis by Dr. Tom Reimchen at the University of Victoria, represents one of the most striking demonstrations of cross-ecosystem nutrient subsidies documented in ecology (PR-02).

### Fish Species Inventory

The study region supports 51 fish species (43 native, 8 non-native). Among salmon, 12 distinct stocks across six species are individually managed under the Endangered Species Act, with 10 stocks listed as threatened or endangered (GOV-01):

- **Chinook salmon:** 4 stocks (spring, summer, fall tule, late fall bright) -- 3 threatened
- **Coho salmon:** 2 stocks (Lower Columbia threatened, Upper Columbia endangered)
- **Sockeye salmon:** 2 stocks (Lake Ozette threatened, Baker Lake stable)
- **Chum salmon:** 2 stocks (Columbia River threatened, Hood Canal Summer threatened)
- **Pink salmon:** 2 stocks (odd-year stable, even-year declining)
- **Steelhead:** 2 stocks (winter and summer, both threatened)

Notable non-salmonid species include Pacific lamprey (*Entosphenus tridentatus*), an anadromous species of cultural significance to the Yakama, Nez Perce, Umatilla, and Warm Springs nations; bull trout (*Salvelinus confluentus*), federally threatened, requiring the coldest and cleanest headwater streams; white sturgeon (*Acipenser transmontanus*), the largest freshwater fish in North America (exceeding 6 meters and 600 kg); and eulachon (*Thaleichthys pacificus*), a federally threatened anadromous smelt historically so oil-rich it was burned as candles by the Nisga'a, Haisla, and Nuxalk nations (GOV-01).

Non-native predatory fish -- particularly smallmouth bass, walleye, and northern pike -- prey on juvenile salmon, increasing mortality during critical rearing and outmigration phases. Brook trout hybridize with bull trout, threatening the native species' genetic integrity (GOV-01).

### The 137+ Species Salmon Dependency Web

Pacific salmon qualify as a keystone connector: 137 or more wildlife species depend on salmon for food, nutrients, or ecosystem services (CON-05). This dependency web extends from stream to forest, from microorganisms to apex predators:

- **21 mammal species** -- from black bears that consume 500-700 salmon per season to deer mice that feed on carcass tissue to elk that browse salmon-enriched riparian vegetation
- **41 bird species** -- from bald eagles congregating 300+ strong at spawning streams to winter wrens whose density near salmon streams correlates with invertebrate availability enriched by marine nitrogen
- **15 fish species** -- from Dolly Varden feeding extensively on salmon eggs to northern pikeminnow as a major predator of juvenile salmon during outmigration
- **30+ invertebrate groups** -- from blowflies colonizing carcasses (primary decomposers) to parasitoid wasps (tertiary consumers carrying marine N-15)
- **31 plant species/groups** -- from Sitka spruce growing three times faster near salmon streams to salmonberry directly fertilized by carcass deposition
- **6 fungal groups** -- from EMF fungi accessing marine nitrogen through host tree roots and redistributing it through mycorrhizal networks

Of 25 formally documented salmon relationships, 21 are cross-domain connections -- confirming salmon's role as a cross-ecosystem connector whose removal cascades through every trophic level simultaneously (CON-05, PR-02).

## Network Synthesis

### The Cross-Domain Relationship Map

The four survey domains -- flora, fauna, fungi, aquatic -- are not separate ecosystems. They are perspectives on a single system. The network synthesis integrates 48 cross-domain relationships documented across all domains (flora 9, fauna 15, fungi 3, aquatic 21) into a unified ecological model.

The ecosystem's network architecture is defined by four hub nodes, three critical edges, and three multi-step cascades that cross domain boundaries. Together, these structural elements demonstrate that the PNW temperate rainforest operates as an integrated system where disruption in one domain propagates through the network in predictable patterns.

### Three Cascades: The Connective Architecture

**CASCADE-01: Salmon-Forest Nutrient Cascade**

| Step | From | To | Mechanism |
|------|------|----|-----------|
| 1 | Pacific Ocean | Salmon body tissue | Salmon accumulate marine N-15 during 1-5 years of ocean feeding |
| 2 | Spawning salmon | Forest floor (via bear) | Bears ferry 500-700 salmon/season into forest, depositing carcasses up to 500m from streams |
| 3 | Salmon carcass | Riparian soil | Invertebrate/microbial decomposition releases marine N, P, C; 40-80% of riparian N is marine-origin |
| 4 | Enriched soil | Tree roots (via EMF) | Ectomycorrhizal fungi access marine-derived N through hyphal networks extending the tree's nutrient zone |
| 5 | Mother tree | Connected tree network (via CMN) | Fungal hyphae redistribute nutrients between trees; mother trees preferentially supply seedlings |

Domains spanned: aquatic, fauna, flora, fungi. Sources: PR-02, PR-03, CON-05.

CASCADE-01 is the master cascade of the PNW ecosystem. It links ocean productivity to inland forest growth through a chain that includes fish, mammals, decomposers, fungi, and trees. The cascade operates bidirectionally in ecological terms: salmon productivity feeds forest fungi, and healthy mycorrhizal forests produce the shade, large woody debris, and stable streambanks that salmon need for spawning habitat.

**CASCADE-02: Truffle-Mammal-Raptor Cascade**

| Step | From | To | Mechanism |
|------|------|----|-----------|
| 1 | Old-growth forest structure | EMF truffle community | EMF fungi require old-growth root networks and deep organic soils; truffle diversity 4x higher in old-growth (GOV-06) |
| 2 | Truffle fruiting bodies | Northern flying squirrel | Squirrels depend on truffles for 80-90% of diet; spore dispersal via fecal pellets completes mutualistic loop |
| 3 | Truffle fruiting bodies | California red-backed vole | Second major mycophagous mammal; obligate truffle dependency in old-growth |
| 4 | Flying squirrel + vole | Northern spotted owl | Primary prey species; owl reproductive success correlates with small mammal density |

Domains spanned: fungi, fauna. Source: GOV-06.

CASCADE-02 demonstrates why old-growth logging threatens spotted owls through an indirect pathway: reduced truffle diversity leads to reduced mammal prey density, which reduces owl reproductive success. Protecting spotted owl habitat means protecting truffle habitat, which means protecting old-growth fungal communities.

**CASCADE-03: Climate-Gradient-Endemic Cascade**

| Step | From | To | Mechanism |
|------|------|----|-----------|
| 1 | Climate warming (4.7-10 degrees F projected, GOV-02) | Precipitation gradient shift | Moisture zones migrate upslope and eastward; rain-shadow boundary shifts |
| 2 | Gradient shift | Gorge endemic wildflower habitat | 15 endemic species occupy moisture bands measured in inches of precipitation; gradient compression narrows or eliminates suitable habitat |
| 3 | Habitat narrowing | Pollinator availability | Reduced wildflower abundance and flowering phenology shifts decouple plants from specialized pollinators (bumblebees for shooting star, solitary bees for hawkweed) |
| 4 | Reduced pollinator services | Population genetic isolation | Small, isolated populations lose genetic diversity; inbreeding depression reduces adaptive capacity |
| 5 | Warming | Talus microhabitat degradation | Summer temperature spikes exceed cold-air drainage buffering in low-elevation talus |
| 6 | Microhabitat loss | Pika population collapse | No cooler elevation available; low-elevation Gorge populations have no retreat option |

Domains spanned: flora, fauna. Sources: GOV-02, CON-01, GOV-01.

CASCADE-03 traces how climate change propagates through the abiotic-forcing pathway from temperature shift to species loss. It is the only cascade driven by an external abiotic force rather than by biological interactions, making it both the most difficult to mitigate and the most globally relevant.

### Four Hub Nodes

Network analysis identifies four hub nodes whose removal would cause disproportionate system-wide degradation:

1. **Pacific salmon (137+ dependent species).** The master keystone connector, linking ocean to forest through nutrient transfer. Salmon removal cascades through every trophic level and every domain simultaneously (CON-05, PR-02).

2. **Douglas fir (CMN mother tree, 47 connections).** The architectural hub of the below-ground network. Mother tree removal severs mycorrhizal connections to dozens of seedlings and neighbors, collapsing carbon subsidies, water sharing, and chemical defense signaling (PR-05).

3. **Ectomycorrhizal fungi (200+ species in old-growth).** The connective tissue itself -- the physical medium through which nutrients, water, and signals flow between trees and between ecosystem domains. EMF diversity loss degrades every cascade (GOV-06).

4. **Old-growth forest structure.** Not a species but a structural condition. All three cascades depend on old-growth: CASCADE-01 requires riparian old-growth for shade and large woody debris; CASCADE-02 requires old-growth fungal communities; CASCADE-03 requires old-growth canopy maintaining Gorge microhabitats. Old-growth is the shared dependency (PR-04).

### Three Critical Edges

The network's most vulnerable connections -- the edges whose failure would cause the greatest cascade degradation:

1. **Bear-salmon nutrient ferry (CASCADE-01, Step 2).** The bridge between aquatic and terrestrial nutrient systems. Without bear-mediated carcass transport, marine nitrogen remains in the stream corridor and does not reach the inland forest (PR-02).

2. **EMF-tree mycorrhizal interface (the Hartig net).** The exchange point where nutrients move from fungal hyphae to tree root cells. Every cascade that involves nutrient transfer between soil and trees passes through this interface (GOV-05).

3. **Canopy-microhabitat dependency.** Old-growth canopy maintains the microclimatic conditions upon which multiple species depend: marbled murrelets (nesting on moss platforms), Larch Mountain salamanders (cool humid talus), and low-elevation pikas (thermal refugia). Canopy loss eliminates all three simultaneously (GOV-04, GOV-01).

### Cascade Interconnections

The three cascades share hub nodes, creating cross-cascade amplification risk. Old-growth forest structure is a shared dependency of all three cascades. Douglas fir mother trees are hub nodes in both CASCADE-01 (as recipients and redistributors of marine nitrogen) and CASCADE-02 (as hosts for the EMF truffle community). EMF fungi mediate nutrient transfer in CASCADE-01 (steps 4-5) and are the foundation of CASCADE-02 (step 1).

This means that threats affecting shared hub nodes do not degrade one cascade at a time -- they degrade multiple cascades simultaneously. Old-growth logging, for example, disrupts CASCADE-01 (removes riparian shade and large woody debris), CASCADE-02 (eliminates EMF truffle habitat), and CASCADE-03 (removes canopy maintaining endemic microhabitats). The network is more interconnected, and therefore more vulnerable to hub-node disruption, than any single-cascade analysis would suggest.

## Climate Threats and Conservation

### Projected Climate Change

Pacific Northwest temperatures are projected to increase 4.7-10 degrees F by the end of the 21st century under moderate to high emission scenarios, with the greatest warming in summer months (GOV-02). Precipitation patterns will shift: total annual precipitation may change little, but summer drought duration and intensity will increase, winter precipitation will shift from snow to rain at mid-elevations, and extreme precipitation events will become more frequent. Snowpack reduction of 25-60% is projected for mid-elevation Cascade watersheds (GOV-02).

### Seventeen Threat Vectors by Taxonomic Group

Climate change threatens PNW biodiversity through 17 identified threat vectors, organized by domain:

**Flora threats (5):**
1. Upslope vegetation zone migration narrowing endemic species habitat
2. Increased wildfire frequency and severity in Douglas fir forests
3. Drought stress reducing old-growth tree vigor and increasing bark beetle susceptibility
4. Phenological mismatches between flowering time and pollinator availability
5. Invasive species range expansion into higher elevations and newly disturbed habitats

**Fauna threats (5):**
1. Thermal stress on cold-adapted species (pika, bull trout, tailed frog)
2. Habitat fragmentation as species shift ranges in response to warming
3. Reduced snowpack affecting denning species (marten, fisher) and snow-dependent species (ptarmigan, snowshoe hare)
4. Ocean warming and acidification reducing salmon marine survival
5. Barred owl expansion facilitated by forest fragmentation from climate-driven disturbance

**Fungi threats (3):**
1. Altered soil moisture regimes reducing EMF fruiting and spore dispersal
2. Increased fire frequency resetting fungal succession to early-stage communities
3. Host tree mortality from drought and bark beetles eliminating EMF root habitat

**Aquatic threats (4):**
1. Stream temperature increases exceeding salmon thermal tolerance
2. Reduced summer base flows from snowpack decline
3. Increased sedimentation from more intense precipitation events
4. Ocean condition changes reducing salmon marine survival and growth

### Direct vs. Cascade-Mediated Threat Classification

Of these 17 threat vectors, 9 operate directly on target species (e.g., thermal stress on pika) and 8 operate through cascade mechanisms (e.g., reduced salmon marine survival cascading through CASCADE-01 to affect inland forest growth). The cascade-mediated threats are more difficult to detect, more difficult to attribute, and potentially more damaging because they propagate through multiple species and domains before their effects become visible.

This classification has a direct conservation implication: protecting individual species without maintaining the cascade architecture that sustains them is structurally insufficient. A program that protects spotted owl nesting habitat but allows the truffle community beneath that habitat to degrade will ultimately fail because the prey base (CASCADE-02) has been undermined.

### Three-Tier Conservation Framework

Evidence from the network analysis supports a three-tier conservation approach:

**Tier 1: Hub node protection** -- the highest-leverage conservation investments.

- Protect remaining old-growth forests as the structural foundation of all three cascades (PR-04, CON-03). The Conservation Fund and similar organizations have acquired large-scale old-growth parcels.
- Maintain salmon populations through habitat restoration, passage barrier removal, and sustainable harvest management (CON-05, GOV-01). The Wild Salmon Center documents the connection between salmon abundance and terrestrial ecosystem function.
- Preserve large Douglas fir mother trees even in managed forests, as hub nodes of the Common Mycorrhizal Network (PR-05).

**Tier 2: Critical edge maintenance** -- protecting the connections between hub nodes.

- Maintain riparian buffers wide enough to sustain bear-salmon nutrient ferry function (PR-02). Current buffer standards (typically 30-90 meters depending on jurisdiction) may be insufficient given that bears transport carcasses up to 500 meters from streams.
- Manage forest harvest practices to maintain mycorrhizal network continuity. Variable retention harvesting that retains mother trees preserves CMN hub-node architecture better than clearcutting (PR-05).
- Protect old-growth canopy above known microhabitat-dependent species (Larch Mountain salamander talus, low-elevation pika sites, murrelet nesting stands). The Columbia River Gorge National Scenic Area management plan provides some regulatory protection (GOV-03).

**Tier 3: Species-specific management** -- targeted interventions within the network context.

- **Northwestern pond turtle:** Six active intervention types (head-starting, bullfrog control, habitat restoration, nest protection, monitoring, land acquisition) constitute a multi-decade commitment demonstrating that sustained active management can stabilize populations when threats are permanent (CON-01, CON-02, GOV-01, GOV-03).
- **Northern spotted owl:** Experimental barred owl removal in select areas assesses spotted owl recovery potential when the competitive pressure is reduced (GOV-03, CON-01).
- **Gorge endemic wildflowers:** Invasive plant management (ivy removal, reed canarygrass control, cheatgrass management) directly protects endemic species with the strongest track record of success among available interventions (CON-01).
- **Salmon stock recovery:** Stock-specific recovery plans addressing habitat, passage, harvest, and hatchery management for each of the 10 ESA-listed stocks (GOV-01, CON-05).
- **American pika monitoring:** Low-elevation Gorge populations serve as an early warning system for climate impacts on microclimate refugia; monitoring protocols track occupancy, temperature, and hay-pile activity (GOV-01, GOV-02).
- **Bull trout:** Cold-water headwater habitat protection and brook trout removal to prevent hybridization (GOV-01).

These 12+ specific interventions are already underway across the study region. They are evidence of a conservation community operating at the scale of the threats. What the network analysis adds is the framework for prioritizing among interventions: hub node protection yields the greatest systemic benefit, critical edge maintenance prevents cascade degradation, and species-specific management addresses immediate threats within the broader network context.
