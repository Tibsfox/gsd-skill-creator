# Cascade Range Elevation Zones and Species Profile Template

> **Foundation document for Cascade Range Biodiversity research (Phase 610)**
>
> Defines the five elevation zones of the Cascade Range transect with boundaries, climate data, substrate geology, and characteristic species. Establishes the species profile template used by all downstream survey phases (611-614) and the network synthesis phase (617).
>
> **Organizing principle:** The elevation transect — a west-to-east, low-to-high cross-section through distinct ecological zones shaped by orographic precipitation, volcanic geology, and 30+ million years of tectonic history.

---

## Geological Context

The Cascade Range comprises two distinct geological provinces that directly shape elevation zone ecology:

### Old Cascades (Western Cascades)

- **Age:** 30-45 million years (Eocene-Miocene volcanism)
- **Character:** Deeply eroded, rounded ridgelines, deeply incised stream valleys
- **Elevation:** Generally 2,000-5,500 ft
- **Substrate:** Weathered andesite and basalt flows, volcanic tuffs, extensive clay-rich soils from prolonged weathering
- **Ecological significance:** Deep soils support the densest old-growth Douglas-fir forests; high-gradient streams provide critical salmonid spawning habitat
- **Source:** GOV-01 (USGS Cascade Range geological assessment)

### High Cascades

- **Age:** 3-5 million years (Pliocene-Holocene volcanism, ongoing)
- **Character:** Young volcanic peaks, glaciated summits, lava flows with minimal soil development
- **Elevation:** 5,000-14,411 ft (Mt. Rainier summit)
- **Substrate:** Young basalt and andesite, pumice deposits, glacial till, thin rocky soils
- **Ecological significance:** Porous volcanic substrate creates distinct hydrology — snowmelt percolates through lava rather than forming surface streams, emerging as springs at lower elevations
- **Source:** GOV-01, PR-01

## Elevation Zone Definitions

### Zone 1: Western Foothills

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | 500-2,000 ft (152-610 m) |
| **Mean annual temperature** | 50-54°F (10-12°C) |
| **Temperature range** | 32-95°F (0-35°C); frost days 30-60/yr |
| **Mean annual precipitation** | 40-60 in (1,016-1,524 mm) |
| **Precipitation type** | 85-95% rain, minimal snowfall |
| **Growing season** | 200-240 days |
| **Substrate** | Deep alluvial soils, Willamette Valley silts, weathered basalt colluvium, prairie remnant soils (Mollisols) |
| **Area (OR + WA estimate)** | ~4,200 sq mi |
| **Threat level** | **Highest** — most vulnerable to habitat conversion, urban expansion, and agricultural encroachment |

#### Ecological Character

The Western Foothills zone is a dynamic transition between the Willamette/Puget lowlands and the montane forest. This zone contains the last remnants of the historically vast Kalapuya and Molala camas prairies — fire-maintained oak savanna and wet prairie ecosystems that once covered hundreds of thousands of acres. Douglas-fir encroachment into oak savanna, suppressed by the cessation of Indigenous burning practices, is the dominant successional trajectory.

This zone supports the highest plant species diversity per unit area in the Cascade transect due to the convergence of prairie, oak woodland, riparian, and early-successional conifer communities.

#### Characteristic Species

**Flora:**
- Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) — savanna dominant, fire-dependent
- Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) — encroaching into oak habitats
- Bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) — riparian corridors and moist slopes
- Red alder (*Alnus rubra*) — nitrogen-fixing pioneer, stream corridors
- Sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*) — understory dominant
- Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) — shrub layer, dense thickets
- Common camas (*Camassia quamash*) — prairie remnant indicator
- Tall Oregon grape (*Berberis aquifolium*) — woodland understory
- Poison oak (*Toxicodendron diversilobum*) — oak savanna, south-facing slopes

**Fauna:**
- White-breasted nuthatch (*Sitta carolinensis*) — oak woodland specialist
- Acorn woodpecker (*Melanerpes formicivorus*) — oak savanna, range limit
- Western gray squirrel (*Sciurus griseus*) — state threatened (WA), oak-dependent
- Pacific giant salamander (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*) — cool streams
- Northern red-legged frog (*Rana aurora*) — wetlands, declining
- Western pond turtle (*Actinemys marmorata*) — state sensitive, prairie ponds

**Fungi:**
- Oregon white truffle (*Tuber oregonense*) — Douglas-fir association
- Golden chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*) — transitional forest

**Source:** GOV-02, GOV-04 (ODFW), GOV-05 (WDFW), PR-02, CON-01

### Zone 2: Montane Forest

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | 2,000-4,500 ft (610-1,372 m) |
| **Mean annual temperature** | 42-50°F (6-10°C) |
| **Temperature range** | 15-85°F (-9 to 29°C); frost days 90-150/yr |
| **Mean annual precipitation** | 60-120 in (1,524-3,048 mm) |
| **Precipitation type** | 60-80% rain, 20-40% snow; transient snow zone (rain-on-snow events) |
| **Growing season** | 150-200 days |
| **Substrate** | Deep volcanic soils (Andisols), weathered andesite and basalt, glacial till on north aspects, well-drained loams |
| **Area** | 3,905 sq mi (OR), 2,397 sq mi (WA) — **largest contiguous zone** |
| **Canopy height** | 180-300 ft in old-growth stands |

#### Ecological Character

The montane forest is the ecological heart of the Cascade Range — the zone of maximum biomass accumulation and structural complexity. Old-growth stands in this zone are among the most massive terrestrial ecosystems on Earth, with individual Douglas-fir and western red cedar exceeding 1,000 years of age and trunk diameters of 10+ ft. Living biomass in old-growth montane stands reaches 500-1,000 tonnes/ha, rivaling tropical rainforests.

The Western Cascades (Old Cascades) geology supports the deepest soils and most productive forest sites. High-gradient streams with large woody debris inputs from old-growth conifers create complex pool-riffle habitat critical for threatened salmonids.

The North Cascades segment of this zone harbors exceptional diversity: 1,630 documented vascular plant species, with 236,000 acres of old-growth forest remaining in the North Cascades National Park complex.

#### Characteristic Species

**Flora:**
- Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) — dominant canopy tree, 500-1,000+ yr lifespan
- Western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*) — shade-tolerant climax species, eventually replaces Douglas-fir
- Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) — moist sites, culturally significant
- Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*) — upper montane transition, north aspects
- Vine maple (*Acer circinatum*) — understory, gap-phase dynamics
- Vanilla leaf (*Achlys triphylla*) — old-growth understory indicator
- Oregon oxalis (*Oxalis oregana*) — deep shade ground cover
- Deer fern (*Blechnum spicant*) — moist ravines
- Western starflower (*Trientalis latifolia*) — ubiquitous herb layer

**Fauna:**
- Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — federally threatened, old-growth obligate
- Marbled murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — federally threatened, nests in old-growth canopy
- Roosevelt elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) — large herbivore, montane meadows
- American marten (*Martes americana*) — old-growth indicator
- Pileated woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*) — cavity excavator, large-diameter snags
- Coastal tailed frog (*Ascaphus truei*) — cold, fast streams, old-growth indicator
- Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) — anadromous, high-gradient spawning
- Coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*) — threatened, pool-dependent
- Pacific lamprey (*Entosphenus tridentatus*) — species of concern, cultural significance

**Fungi:**
- Chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*) — ectomycorrhizal, Douglas-fir associate, commercially harvested
- Matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*) — ectomycorrhizal, commercially harvested, culturally significant
- Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*) — old-growth dependent, rare, medicinal
- Lettuce lung lichen (*Lobaria oregana*) — old-growth canopy, nitrogen fixer
- Sulphur shelf (*Laetiporus conifericola*) — saprotrophic, conifer heartwood

**Source:** GOV-02, GOV-03 (USFWS), PR-01, PR-03, PR-04, CON-02

### Zone 3: Upper Montane / Subalpine

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | 4,500-6,500 ft (1,372-1,981 m) |
| **Mean annual temperature** | 34-42°F (1-6°C) |
| **Temperature range** | -10 to 75°F (-23 to 24°C); frost possible any month |
| **Mean annual precipitation** | 60-110 in (1,524-2,794 mm) |
| **Precipitation type** | 50-80% snow; persistent snowpack November-June |
| **Snow depth** | 8-20 ft maximum pack depth at typical sites |
| **Growing season** | 60-150 days |
| **Substrate** | Thin volcanic soils over basalt/andesite bedrock, glacial till and moraines, pumice deposits (especially OR east Cascades), cryoturbated soils at upper boundary |
| **Treeline** | ~6,000 ft (variable by aspect and latitude: 5,500 ft in North Cascades, 6,500 ft in southern OR) |

#### Ecological Character

The upper montane/subalpine zone is defined by the transition from closed-canopy forest to the open meadow-forest mosaics that characterize timberline. Trees at upper elevations grow in krummholz form — wind-flagged, stunted, and sculpted by ice crystal abrasion. Meadow-forest boundaries are dynamic, advancing during warm-dry periods and retreating during cool-wet periods, making this zone a sensitive indicator of climate change.

Subalpine meadows support extraordinary wildflower displays (July-August) that are keystone resources for native pollinators. Deep snowpack insulates soil biota through winter and provides extended moisture through the growing season via gradual melt.

Alaska yellow-cedar (*Callitropsis nootkatensis*) decline is an emerging conservation concern in this zone — warming winters reduce insulating snowpack, exposing shallow root systems to lethal freezing events.

#### Characteristic Species

**Flora:**
- Mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*) — dominant subalpine tree, heavy snow zones
- Subalpine fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*) — upper elevation limit, spire-shaped crown sheds snow
- Alaska yellow-cedar (*Callitropsis nootkatensis*) — declining due to reduced snowpack, USFWS threatened
- Noble fir (*Abies procera*) — OR Cascades endemic, mid-subalpine
- Whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) — federally threatened, keystone species, upper timberline
- Mountain huckleberry (*Vaccinium membranaceum*) — dominant shrub, critical food web species
- Beargrass (*Xerophyllum tenax*) — fire-dependent meadow species, culturally significant
- Avalanche lily (*Erythronium montanum*) — snowmelt indicator, early-season pollinator resource
- Red heather (*Phyllodoce empetriformis*) — subalpine heath, acidic soils
- Partridge foot (*Luetkea pectinata*) — subalpine meadow indicator

**Fauna:**
- Clark's nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — whitebark pine mutualist, seed dispersal
- Mountain goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) — cliff-dwelling, subalpine to alpine
- Hoary marmot (*Marmota caligata*) — talus slopes, colonial
- Sooty grouse (*Dendragapus fuliginosus*) — conifer-dependent, elevational migrant
- American pika (*Ochotona princeps*) — talus obligate, climate-sensitive indicator
- Cascade torrent salamander (*Rhyacotriton cascadae*) — cold headwater streams, range-restricted
- Gray-crowned rosy-finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) — cliff nester, snowfield forager

**Fungi:**
- King bolete (*Boletus edulis*) — subalpine fir association
- Morels (*Morchella* spp.) — post-fire subalpine meadows, commercially harvested
- Russula brevipes — ubiquitous ectomycorrhizal, subalpine conifer forests

**Source:** GOV-02, GOV-03, GOV-06 (NPS — North Cascades, Mt. Rainier), PR-05, PR-06, CON-01, CON-03

### Zone 4: Alpine

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | 6,500+ ft (1,981+ m) to summit (14,411 ft / 4,392 m at Mt. Rainier) |
| **Mean annual temperature** | 18-34°F (-8 to 1°C) |
| **Temperature range** | -40 to 65°F (-40 to 18°C); freeze-thaw cycles 200+ days/yr |
| **Mean annual precipitation** | 60-140 in (1,524-3,556 mm), nearly all snow |
| **Precipitation type** | 95-100% snow; glaciers persistent above ~7,500 ft |
| **Growing season** | 30-60 days (some microsites < 30 days) |
| **UV exposure** | Extreme — 25-50% higher UVB than sea level |
| **Substrate** | Exposed bedrock, volcanic rubble, glacial moraines, fellfield (rock-fragment soils), thin cryptogamic crusts, active periglacial features (patterned ground, solifluction lobes) |
| **Active glaciers** | ~700 glaciers in WA Cascades (largest glaciated area in contiguous US outside Alaska); ~35 in OR Cascades |

#### Ecological Character

The alpine zone lies above the climatic treeline where no upright trees survive. Vegetation is sparse, low-growing, and highly specialized for extreme UV radiation, desiccating winds, thin atmosphere, nutrient-poor substrate, and a growing season measured in weeks. Plants adopt cushion, mat, and rosette growth forms that minimize wind exposure and trap radiant heat near the ground surface.

Despite apparent barrenness, alpine ecosystems support surprising biodiversity in cryptic niches: wind-deposited insects on snowfields sustain rosy-finches and ice worms; lithophytic lichens colonize bare rock surfaces at elevations exceeding 10,000 ft; snow algae communities (*Chlamydomonas nivalis* — "watermelon snow") photosynthesize through snowpack.

Wildflower meadows in the 6,500-8,000 ft transition zone produce spectacular displays: lupine, paintbrush, avalanche lily, phlox, and aster carpet open slopes for 4-8 weeks following snowmelt, supporting dense pollinator communities.

The Cascade alpine zone is highly vulnerable to climate change — upward migration of treeline compresses available habitat, and species already at summit cannot migrate higher.

#### Characteristic Species

**Flora:**
- Spreading phlox (*Phlox diffusa*) — cushion plant, fellfield specialist
- Alpine lupine (*Lupinus lepidus*) — nitrogen fixer, primary colonizer of volcanic substrates
- Cascade aster (*Eucephalus ledophyllus*) — meadow-fellfield transition
- Davidson's penstemon (*Penstemon davidsonii*) — rock crevice specialist
- Cliff paintbrush (*Castilleja rupicola*) — hemiparasite, rocky outcrops
- Tolmie's saxifrage (*Micranthes tolmiei*) — snowmelt seeps, cushion form
- Elmera (*Elmera racemosa*) — Cascade endemic, rock ledges
- Snow willow (*Salix nivalis*) — prostrate shrub, < 2 inches tall
- Black alpine sedge (*Carex nigricans*) — snowbed communities

**Fauna:**
- White-tailed ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) — alpine obligate, camouflage specialist
- American pika (*Ochotona princeps*) — talus, extends from subalpine
- Gray-crowned rosy-finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) — forages on snowfield insects
- Ice worm (*Mesenchytraeus solifugus*) — glacier-obligate oligochaete, unique to PNW
- Cascade red fox (*Vulpes vulpes cascadensis*) — high-elevation subspecies, very rare
- Mountain goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) — extends from subalpine, cliff-dwelling

**Lichens and Cryptogams:**
- Map lichen (*Rhizocarpon geographicum*) — crustose, rock colonizer, used for lichenometry dating
- Snow algae (*Chlamydomonas nivalis*) — "watermelon snow," photoautotroph in snowpack
- Various crustose and foliose lichens on exposed rock — dominant primary producers above 9,000 ft

**Source:** GOV-06, GOV-02, PR-07, PR-08, CON-03

### Zone 5: Eastern Slopes (Rain Shadow)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | Variable — mirrors western zones in reverse: alpine (6,500+ ft) → subalpine (4,500-6,500 ft) → ponderosa (2,000-4,500 ft) → sagebrush steppe (< 2,000 ft) |
| **Mean annual temperature** | 40-52°F (4-11°C), varies by elevation |
| **Temperature range** | -20 to 105°F (-29 to 41°C); continental extremes, large diurnal swings (30-50°F daily) |
| **Mean annual precipitation** | < 10-25 in (< 254-635 mm); extreme rain shadow |
| **Precipitation type** | Mixed; drier than western equivalent at every elevation |
| **Growing season** | 100-180 days (limited by moisture, not temperature at lower elevations) |
| **Substrate** | Basalt flows and volcanic ash, pumice plains (especially OR east Cascades), granitic soils (WA North Cascades east), thin aridisols at lowest elevations |
| **Fire regime** | **Highest fire risk** — historically frequent, low-intensity surface fires (5-25 yr return interval in ponderosa zone); fire suppression has created dangerous fuel loads |

#### Ecological Character

The eastern slopes descend into the rain shadow of the Cascade crest, experiencing a dramatic precipitation gradient — from 60+ in/yr at the crest to < 10 in/yr at the steppe interface. This creates a compressed elevation zonation where moisture, not temperature, is the primary limiting factor.

The ponderosa pine zone (2,000-4,500 ft) is a fire-adapted ecosystem of open, park-like stands maintained by frequent low-intensity surface fires. A century of fire suppression has transformed these open woodlands into dense, ladder-fueled stands at extreme risk of stand-replacing crown fire — the dominant conservation crisis on the eastern Cascades.

The east-slope gradient sequence — Alpine → Subalpine fir/Engelmann spruce → Grand fir/Douglas-fir → Ponderosa pine → Sagebrush steppe — completes in as little as 15 horizontal miles from crest to valley floor, creating one of the steepest ecological gradients in North America.

#### Characteristic Species

**Flora:**
- Ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) — fire-adapted dominant, thick bark, open stands
- Grand fir (*Abies grandis*) — mid-elevation, encroaching into fire-suppressed ponderosa
- Engelmann spruce (*Picea engelmannii*) — east-slope subalpine, riparian corridors
- Western larch (*Larix occidentalis*) — deciduous conifer, fire-adapted, fire scar archives
- Bitterbrush (*Purshia tridentata*) — shrub steppe transition, wildlife browse
- Big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) — steppe dominant, aromatic shrub
- Bluebunch wheatgrass (*Pseudoroegneria spicata*) — native bunchgrass, steppe
- Balsamroot (*Balsamorhiza sagittata*) — spring ephemeral, east-slope meadows
- Bitterroot (*Lewisia rediviva*) — rocky outcrops, drought specialist

**Fauna:**
- Lewis's woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*) — ponderosa snag-dependent, declining
- White-headed woodpecker (*Dryobates albolarvatus*) — ponderosa pine specialist, near-threatened
- Flammulated owl (*Psiloscops flammeolus*) — old-growth ponderosa, insectivore
- Mule deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) — east-slope dominant ungulate
- American elk (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*) — Rocky Mountain subspecies
- Western rattlesnake (*Crotalus oreganus*) — basalt talus, dry slopes
- Pygmy short-horned lizard (*Phrynosoma douglasii*) — sagebrush steppe, cryptic

**Fungi:**
- Morels (*Morchella* spp.) — post-fire ponderosa, commercially harvested
- Western painted suillus (*Suillus lakei*) — Douglas-fir/ponderosa association
- Hawk's wing (*Sarcodon imbricatus*) — ectomycorrhizal, dry conifer forests

**Source:** GOV-02, GOV-04, GOV-05, GOV-07 (BLM), PR-09, PR-10, CON-04

## Cross-Zone Ecological Processes

### Orographic Precipitation Gradient

Prevailing Pacific westerlies force moist air upward over the Cascade crest. As air rises, it cools adiabatically, releasing moisture as precipitation on the western slopes. Descending east of the crest, the air warms and dries, creating one of the most dramatic rain shadows in North America: > 100 in/yr on western slopes to < 10 in/yr on eastern lowlands within 30 horizontal miles.

### Elevational Migration

Many species use multiple elevation zones seasonally:
- **Roosevelt elk:** Montane → Subalpine meadows (summer) → Western Foothills (winter)
- **Sooty grouse:** Montane ground (breeding) → Subalpine conifer canopy (winter) — reverse of typical elevational migration
- **Clark's nutcracker:** Alpine/Subalpine (seed harvest) → Montane (seed caching) — crosses 3 zones
- **Pacific salmon:** Ocean → Western Foothills → Montane streams (spawning) — crosses zones vertically via watersheds

### Fire Regime Gradient

| Zone | Historic Fire Return Interval | Fire Type | Current Status |
|------|-------------------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Western Foothills | 5-15 yr (Indigenous burning) | Surface, patchy | Suppressed; oak savanna declining |
| Montane Forest | 100-400 yr | Stand-replacing | Near-natural regime in wilderness |
| Subalpine | 200-500+ yr | Stand-replacing | Climate-driven increase |
| Alpine | N/A | N/A | N/A |
| Eastern Slopes | 5-25 yr | Surface, low-intensity | Suppressed; extreme fuel loads, megafire risk |

**Source:** GOV-02, PR-09, PR-10

### Marine-Derived Nutrient Transport

Anadromous salmonids (Chinook, coho, steelhead, sockeye, chum, pink) transport marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus from the Pacific Ocean into Cascade watersheds. Spawning carcasses fertilize riparian forests in Zones 1-2, with nutrient signatures detected in trees up to 500 m from spawning streams. This ocean-forest nutrient pipeline is a defining feature of Pacific Northwest montane ecology.

**Source:** PR-03, PR-04

## Species Profile Template

The species profile template defines a universal entry structure for all survey modules in the Cascade Range Biodiversity study. It is adapted from the Columbia Valley shared schemas (00-shared-schemas.md) with Cascade-specific extensions for elevation zone affinity, fire ecology, and volcanic substrate adaptation.

### Core Identity Fields

| Field | Type | Required | Description |
|-------|------|----------|-------------|
| common_name | string | required | Standard common name (English) |
| scientific_name | string | required | Binomial nomenclature (*Genus species*) or trinomial for subspecies |
| taxonomic_group | enum | required | One of: `vascular_plant`, `bryophyte`, `epiphyte`, `lichen`, `mammal`, `bird`, `amphibian`, `reptile`, `fish`, `invertebrate`, `ectomycorrhizal_fungus`, `arbuscular_mycorrhizal_fungus`, `saprotrophic_fungus`, `lichenized_fungus` |
| taxonomic_hierarchy | object | required | Nested: `kingdom`, `phylum`, `class`, `order`, `family`, `genus`, `species` |

### Elevation and Distribution Fields

| Field | Type | Required | Description |
|-------|------|----------|-------------|
| elevation_range | object | required | `min_ft` (integer), `max_ft` (integer), `min_m` (integer), `max_m` (integer) |
| zone_affinity | list[enum] | required | Primary zones where species occurs. One or more of: `western_foothills`, `montane_forest`, `upper_montane_subalpine`, `alpine`, `eastern_slopes` |
| aspect_preference | enum | optional | One of: `north`, `south`, `east`, `west`, `none`; relevant for species with strong aspect-dependent distribution |
| slope_position | enum | optional | One of: `ridge`, `mid_slope`, `valley_bottom`, `riparian`, `talus`, `cliff`, `meadow`, `none` |
| cascade_segment | list[enum] | optional | Regional presence. One or more of: `north_cascades_wa`, `central_cascades_wa`, `south_cascades_wa`, `north_cascades_or`, `central_cascades_or`, `south_cascades_or` |
| endemic_status | enum | required | One of: `none`, `cascade_endemic`, `pnw_endemic`, `regional_endemic` |

### Habitat Fields

| Field | Type | Required | Description |
|-------|------|----------|-------------|
| habitat_description | string | required | Free text describing primary habitat characteristics |

## Conventions for Downstream Survey Phases

### Zone Reference Format

When survey modules (Phases 611-614) reference elevation zones, use the short-form enum values defined in `zone_affinity`:

- `western_foothills` (Zone 1)
- `montane_forest` (Zone 2)
- `upper_montane_subalpine` (Zone 3)
- `alpine` (Zone 4)
- `eastern_slopes` (Zone 5)

### Elevation Reporting

- Always report elevations in **both feet and meters**: e.g., "4,500 ft (1,372 m)"
- Use the zone boundaries defined in this document as authoritative
- Species observed at zone boundaries should list both adjacent zones in `zone_affinity`

### Cross-Zone Species

Species that span 3+ zones should include all occupied zones in `zone_affinity` and use the `elevational_migrant` flag in `cross_module_flags` if they move seasonally between zones.

### GPS Coordinates

**Do not publish GPS coordinates for endangered or threatened species.** Use zone + habitat description instead. This applies to all species with `federal_status` of `endangered`, `threatened`, or `candidate`.

### Source Attribution

Every species profile must include at least one `primary_source` from the Source Index. Government agency data (`GOV-*`) and peer-reviewed research (`PR-*`) are preferred. Conservation organization data (`CON-*`) may supplement but should not be the sole source for species occurrence claims.
