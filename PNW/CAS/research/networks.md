# Ecological Networks — Cross-Module Synthesis

## Overview

The Cascade Range functions not as a stack of isolated elevation zones but as a single interconnected system. This module traces the ecological connections that bind the survey modules together: salmon carrying marine nutrients uphill through watersheds, mycorrhizal networks threading through forest soil across elevation bands, beavers engineering wetlands that sustain amphibian communities, and fire disturbance reshaping fungal diversity and driving forest regeneration.

Where the Columbia Valley study identified 3 cascades and 4 hub nodes, this study maps the Cascade Range's vertical network — connections that cross elevation zone boundaries as well as taxonomic module boundaries.

---

## Cross-Module Connections

### CASCADE-CAS-01: Salmon-Nitrogen Vertical Transport

**Pathway:** Ocean → River → Riparian Forest → Mycorrhizal Network → Upslope Trees

The marine-derived nutrient pathway operates across Zones 1-2 (Western Foothills through Montane):

1. **Marine origin:** Anadromous salmonids (Chinook, coho, steelhead, sockeye) accumulate marine-derived nitrogen (N-15 enriched) and phosphorus during 1-5 years of ocean feeding
2. **Upstream transport:** Salmon return to natal Cascade streams, carrying marine nutrients from sea level to spawning grounds at 500-3,500 ft elevation
3. **Carcass deposition:** Post-spawning mortality deposits carcasses in stream channels and riparian zones. Predators (black bear, bald eagle, river otter) and scavengers transport carcasses 50-500 m from streams into forest
4. **Soil enrichment:** Naiman et al. (2002) documented that 40-80% of riparian nitrogen in salmon-bearing PNW watersheds is marine in origin (PR-03). Helfield & Naiman (2001) showed Sitka spruce and red alder near salmon streams grow significantly faster than trees along non-salmon streams (PR-04)
5. **Mycorrhizal redistribution:** Marine-derived nitrogen taken up by riparian trees enters ectomycorrhizal networks (common mycorrhizal networks, CMN) and is redistributed to neighboring trees, potentially extending the marine nutrient signal beyond the immediate riparian zone

**Modules spanned:** Aquatic, Fauna, Flora, Fungi
**Hub nodes:** Pacific salmon (nutrient vector), Douglas-fir/red alder (uptake), EMF fungi (redistribution)
**Critical edge:** Salmon spawning access — dams, stream temperature, flow regime all threaten this cascade
**Threat vectors:** Climate warming (stream temperature rise), snowpack decline (summer flow reduction), habitat fragmentation (dam barriers — Klamath removal as positive counterexample)

**Source:** PR-03, PR-04, GOV-06

### CASCADE-CAS-02: Flying Squirrel-Truffle-Tree Mutualism

**Pathway:** Old-Growth Forest → Truffle Fruiting → Squirrel Mycophagy → Spore Dispersal → Seedling Mycorrhization

This cascade operates primarily in Zone 2 (Montane) with extensions into Zone 3 (Subalpine):

1. **Ectomycorrhizal fruiting:** Hypogeous (underground-fruiting) EMF species produce truffle fruiting bodies in forest soil, particularly near decaying down wood which serves as moisture-retaining substrate (PR-02)
2. **Squirrel excavation:** Northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) excavates and consumes truffles as a primary food source — truffle spores pass through the digestive tract intact
3. **Spore dispersal:** Fecal pellets deposited throughout the squirrel's home range inoculate new root systems with viable EMF spores, maintaining and extending the mycorrhizal network
4. **Seedling establishment:** Conifer seedlings (Douglas-fir, hemlock) depend on mycorrhizal colonization for establishment — seedlings without mycorrhizal partners show dramatically reduced survival and growth
5. **Spotted owl predation:** Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) preys on northern flying squirrel — linking this entire cascade to apex predator conservation

**Modules spanned:** Fungi, Fauna, Flora
**Hub nodes:** Northern flying squirrel (disperser), EMF truffle complex (fungi), old-growth forest structure (habitat)
**Critical edge:** Down wood availability — logging removes the decaying logs that serve as mycorrhizal refugia
**Threat vectors:** Old-growth loss (logging legacy), habitat fragmentation (squirrel population isolation)

**Source:** GOV-01, PR-01, PR-02, PR-03

### CASCADE-CAS-03: Beaver-Wetland-Amphibian Hydrology

**Pathway:** Beaver Dam Construction → Pond/Wetland Creation → Amphibian Habitat → Stream Temperature Buffering

This cascade operates in Zones 1-2 (Western Foothills through lower Montane):

1. **Dam construction:** American beaver (*Castor canadensis*) builds dams on low-to-moderate gradient streams, impounding water and creating pond-wetland complexes
2. **Habitat creation:** Beaver ponds create still-water habitat critical for Oregon spotted frog (*Rana pretiosa*) breeding, Pacific giant salamander larval development, and western toad (*Anaxyrus boreas*) reproduction
3. **Hydrological buffering:** Beaver dams slow stream velocity, raise water tables, increase groundwater recharge, and moderate downstream temperature fluctuations — critical during summer low-flow periods
4. **Riparian expansion:** Pond margins support expanded riparian vegetation (willow, alder, sedge meadows) that stabilizes banks, filters sediment, and provides terrestrial wildlife habitat
5. **Cold-water refugia:** In warming climate conditions, beaver-modified streams maintain lower temperatures than free-flowing reaches — potentially critical for salmonid persistence

**Modules spanned:** Fauna, Aquatic, Flora
**Hub nodes:** American beaver (ecosystem engineer), beaver ponds (habitat), Oregon spotted frog (indicator)
**Critical edge:** Beaver population recovery — Cascade Forest Conservancy actively reintroducing beavers (CON-01)
**Threat vectors:** Habitat fragmentation (prevents beaver colonization of new streams), development in foothills (removes beaver habitat)

**Source:** CON-01, GOV-08, GOV-09

### CASCADE-CAS-04: Fire-Disturbance-Fungi-Regeneration

**Pathway:** Fire Event → Canopy Opening → Soil Heating → Fungal Community Shift → Post-Fire Succession

This cascade operates differently on east slopes (Zone 5) vs. west slopes (Zones 1-2):

**East-slope (frequent fire):**
1. Low-intensity surface fire removes understory fuel and duff layer
2. Soil heating kills shallow-rooted EMF mycelia but deep mycorrhizal networks survive
3. Fire-following morels (*Morchella* spp.) fruit prolifically in first 1-2 years post-fire
4. Surviving ponderosa pine with intact mycorrhizal partners recover rapidly
5. Open canopy conditions favor shade-intolerant species (ponderosa, western larch)

**West-slope (infrequent, stand-replacing fire):**
1. High-severity fire kills canopy trees and surface organic layer
2. Most EMF mycelia killed; surviving fungi concentrated in deep soil and under remnant trees
3. Pioneer species (red alder — nitrogen-fixing) colonize first, establishing new mycorrhizal associations
4. Douglas-fir seedlings depend on mycorrhizal inoculation from surviving old-growth remnants or squirrel-dispersed spores
5. Full EMF community recovery takes decades to centuries — PR-04 (2023) documented detectable logging legacy in fungal communities 40+ years post-harvest

**Modules spanned:** Threats, Fungi, Flora
**Hub nodes:** Fire (disturbance agent), EMF community (regeneration driver), old-growth remnants (mycorrhizal refugia)
**Critical edge:** Mycorrhizal legacy persistence — if all old-growth patches are eliminated, regeneration pathway is broken
**Threat vectors:** Fire suppression (fuel buildup → catastrophic crown fire vs. historical low-severity), climate change (longer fire seasons)

**Source:** GOV-02, PR-04, PR-07

## Hub Node Analysis

| Hub Node | Type | Connections | Module | Cascades |
|----------|------|------------|--------|----------|
| Pacific salmon | Nutrient vector | 100+ dependent species (extrapolated from COL study: 137+) | Aquatic | CAS-01 |
| Douglas-fir | Foundation species | CMN mother tree, host to 100+ EMF species, canopy structure | Flora | CAS-01, CAS-02, CAS-04 |
| EMF fungi | Network infrastructure | Connects trees across root zones, stores/redistributes nutrients | Fungi | CAS-01, CAS-02, CAS-04 |
| Old-growth forest structure | Habitat architecture | Down wood refugia, canopy nesting, cavity habitat, microclimate | Flora/Fauna | CAS-02, CAS-04 |
| American beaver | Ecosystem engineer | Pond creation, hydrology modification, habitat expansion | Fauna | CAS-03 |
| Fire | Disturbance regime | Vegetation structure, species composition, nutrient cycling | Threats | CAS-04 |

**Key finding:** Douglas-fir participates in 3 of 4 cascades, confirming its role as the structural keystone of the montane Cascade ecosystem. Old-growth forest structure appears in 2 cascades — its loss would simultaneously disrupt truffle-squirrel-owl and fire-regeneration pathways.

## Elevation Zone Network Density

| Zone | Cascades Active | Hub Nodes Present | Network Density |
|------|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Western Foothills | CAS-01, CAS-03, CAS-04 | Salmon, beaver, fire | High (3 cascades, most threatened) |
| Montane | CAS-01, CAS-02, CAS-04 | All 6 hub nodes | **Highest** (all cascades overlap) |
| Subalpine | CAS-02 (partial) | Douglas-fir (upper limit), EMF | Moderate |
| Alpine | None directly | None | Low (primarily abiotic processes) |
| Eastern Slopes | CAS-04 | Fire, ponderosa pine | Moderate (fire-dominant) |

**Key finding:** The montane zone (2,000-4,500 ft) is the most densely networked elevation band — all 4 cascades operate here, all 6 hub nodes are present. This is also the zone with the greatest biomass, the most old-growth, and the richest EMF diversity. Protecting montane old-growth is the single highest-leverage conservation action for maintaining Cascade Range ecological networks.
