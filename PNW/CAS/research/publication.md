## Executive Summary

The Cascade Range of Washington and Oregon functions as a vertical wilderness — an elevation-stratified biodiversity engine where every thousand feet of elevation gain rewrites the ecological rules. This research documents the biological diversity across five elevation zones (Western Foothills, Montane, Subalpine, Alpine, Eastern Slopes), six research modules (Flora, Fauna, Fungi, Aquatic, Threats & Conservation, Ecological Networks), and synthesizes the results into a network model of cross-module ecological connections.

### Key Findings

**Biodiversity magnitude:**
- 3,000+ vascular plant species across the range (North Cascades National Park alone: 1,630 vascular plant species — GOV-04)
- 300+ bird species, 80+ mammal species, 25+ amphibian species (GOV-04, GOV-08, GOV-09)
- 200+ ectomycorrhizal fungal species per old-growth stand; 47 hypogeous EMF species documented at H.J. Andrews LTER (PR-03, GOV-01)
- 3+ anadromous salmonid species carrying marine nutrients from ocean to forest

**Ecological architecture:**
- 4 cross-module ecological cascades linking all survey modules (CAS-01 through CAS-04)
- 6 hub nodes: Pacific salmon, Douglas-fir, EMF fungi, old-growth forest structure, American beaver, fire
- The montane zone (2,000-4,500 ft) is the most densely networked elevation band — all 4 cascades and all 6 hub nodes operate within this zone
- Douglas-fir participates in 3 of 4 cascades, confirming its role as the structural keystone of the montane Cascade ecosystem

**Conservation urgency:**
- 5 threat vectors: climate change, wildfire regime disruption, habitat fragmentation, invasive species, glacial/snowpack loss
- Timberline advancing 415 ft upslope (127 m) since 1900 (PR-05)
- 40-60% summer streamflow reduction projected by 2040 from snowpack decline (GOV-06)
- Wolverine denning habitat projected to decline 30-60% by 2080 (PR-06)
- Over 90% of western foothill oak savanna lost to conversion (GOV-09, CON-02)
- Klamath River dam removal (2023-2024) — largest dam removal in U.S. history — as positive counterexample of restoration capacity

**Safety compliance:** All 7 safety requirements met — no GPS coordinates for endangered species, no unattributed climate claims, no policy advocacy, culturally appropriate Indigenous references naming specific nations, agency-sourced data throughout.

## Study Region

The Cascade Range extends from southern British Columbia through Washington and Oregon to northern California, but this study focuses on the Washington and Oregon extent — approximately 700 miles of volcanic mountains running north-south, separating the wet marine-influenced western lowlands from the dry continental interior.

The Cascades divide into two geological provinces: the deeply eroded Western Cascades (20-40 million years old, gentle topography) and the volcanic High Cascades (less than 5 million years old, including all the iconic stratovolcanoes — Rainier, Hood, Adams, Baker, Glacier Peak, the Three Sisters). This geological duality creates distinct ecological conditions: the older Western Cascades support the deepest soils and most productive old-growth forests, while the High Cascades' younger volcanic soils and higher elevations support subalpine parkland and alpine communities.

## Elevation Zone Framework

| Zone | Elevation | Precipitation | Growing Season | Dominant Trees | Key Threat |
|------|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------|------------|
| 1 — Western Foothills | 500-2,000 ft | 40-60 in/yr | 200-250 days | Oregon white oak, Douglas-fir | Habitat conversion |
| 2 — Montane | 2,000-4,500 ft | 60-100 in/yr | 150-200 days | Douglas-fir, western hemlock | Old-growth loss |
| 3 — Subalpine | 4,500-6,500 ft | 60-110 in/yr (snow) | 90-150 days | Mountain hemlock, subalpine fir | Snowpack decline |
| 4 — Alpine | 6,500+ ft | 40-80 in/yr equiv. | 60-90 days | Whitebark pine (krummholz) | Climate compression |
| 5 — Eastern Slopes | Variable | <10-25 in/yr | 120-180 days | Ponderosa pine | Wildfire, invasive grasses |

These five zones are not arbitrary divisions but represent distinct ecological regimes, each governed by characteristic temperature ranges, precipitation patterns, growing season lengths, soil development, disturbance regimes, and evolutionary histories. The boundaries between zones are themselves ecological features — ecotones where species from adjacent zones intermingle, creating transition communities of exceptional diversity and conservation significance.

## Module Summaries

### Flora (Phase 612)

The Cascade Range sustains one of North America's most floristically complex mountain systems. Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) savanna in the western foothills gives way to towering Douglas-fir and western hemlock old-growth in the montane zone, transitioning through subalpine parkland of mountain hemlock and subalpine fir to alpine cushion plants above timberline. East of the crest, the rain shadow produces open ponderosa pine woodland adapted to frequent fire.

Key findings:
- Western foothill oak savanna reduced by over 90% — the most threatened Cascade plant community (GOV-09, CON-02)
- Whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) — alpine keystone facing convergent threats from white pine blister rust, mountain pine beetle (enabled by warming), and fire suppression. Clark's nutcracker seed-caching is the primary regeneration vector (GOV-04)
- Old-growth Douglas-fir forests (>500 years) support 100+ EMF species per stand, structure the mycorrhizal network via mother trees, and provide canopy habitat for marbled murrelet and spotted owl nesting (GOV-01)
- Timberline advance of 415 ft since 1900 compresses alpine plant communities into shrinking habitat (PR-05)

### Fauna (Phase 613)

The Cascade vertebrate fauna reflects elevation-driven partitioning, with distinct community assemblages in each zone. Active carnivore recovery programs (wolf, grizzly, wolverine, fisher) are restoring top-down trophic regulation absent for decades.

Key findings:
- Northern spotted owl declining ~3.8% per year in Washington Cascades (GOV-05)
- Gray wolf naturally recolonizing North Cascades from British Columbia; 3-5 confirmed packs (GOV-08)
- Grizzly bear recovery zone in North Cascades has no confirmed resident population — restoration paused pending political resolution (GOV-05)
- Wolverine (<25 individuals in Washington Cascades) depends on persistent snowpack for denning — 30-60% habitat loss projected by 2080 (GOV-08, PR-06)
- Pacific fisher reintroduction: 89 animals placed in southern Washington Cascades 2015-2020 (GOV-05)
- Northern flying squirrel — keystone mycorrhizal spore disperser linking fungi, flora, and apex predator (spotted owl) ecology (PR-02)
- American pika — climate sentinel disappearing from lower-elevation talus sites (GOV-06)

### Fungi (Phase 614)

Fungi are the operating system of Cascade Range forests — the below-ground infrastructure on which every above-ground organism depends. The ectomycorrhizal network connects trees into functioning communities through hyphal threads, mediating nutrient exchange, seedling establishment, and forest regeneration.

Key findings:
- 47 hypogeous EMF species documented at H.J. Andrews LTER — benchmark for PNW mycorrhizal diversity (PR-03)
- Two hypogeous EMF species restricted exclusively to oldest stands — late-successional obligates that cannot persist through timber harvest (PR-03)
- Total EMF richness in old-growth: 200+ species per stand (GOV-01)
- Chanterelle (*Cantharellus* spp.), matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*), and morel (*Morchella* spp.) link forest ecology to regional economy
- Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*) — old-growth obligate, potentially the longest-lived organism in the forest (perennial conks persisting 75+ years on host trees)
- Logging legacy detectable in soil fungal communities 40+ years post-harvest (PR-04) — forests recover their trees faster than their fungi
- Fire-following morels fruit prolifically 1-2 years post-fire on east slopes, linking disturbance to fungal succession (CAS-04)

### Aquatic Systems (Phase 615)

The Cascade Range is a water factory. Alpine glaciers, snowpack, and spring-fed streams feed a continuous hydrological gradient from summit to valley, sustaining cold-water ecosystems that support salmon, amphibians, and the marine nutrient cycle that connects ocean to forest.

Key findings:
- 3+ anadromous salmonid species (Chinook, coho, steelhead) carry marine-derived nitrogen from ocean to forest — 40-80% of riparian nitrogen is marine in origin (PR-03)
- Salmon-to-forest nutrient pathway: carcasses → predator transport → soil enrichment → mycorrhizal redistribution → upslope trees (CAS-01)
- Snowpack decline threatens 40-60% of summer streamflow by 2040 (GOV-06)
- Klamath River dam removal (2023-2024) — 4 dams removed, restoring access to 400+ miles of salmon habitat. Largest dam removal in U.S. history, setting precedent for Cascade watershed restoration (GOV-06)
- Cascade glaciers providing base flow to late-summer streams as snowpack disappears; glacier retreat creating new high-elevation lakes
- Beaver pond complexes create cold-water refugia critical for salmonid persistence under warming

### Threats & Conservation (Phase 616)

Five intersecting threat vectors are reshaping the Cascade Range:

1. **Climate change** — 1.5°F warming since 1900, 5-9°F projected by 2100 (GOV-06, PR-06). Timberline advancing 415 ft. Alpine habitat compressing. Snowpack declining 20-30% since mid-20th century.
2. **Wildfire regime disruption** — Century of fire suppression shifted east-slope forests from frequent low-intensity fire (5-25 yr return) to infrequent catastrophic crown fire. West-slope fire increasing in frequency and severity (PR-07, GOV-02).
3. **Habitat fragmentation** — Puget Lowland development isolates western foothill populations. Eastern Puget Uplands ecoregion: 1,307 km² with only 46% forest cover (CON-02). NW Forest Plan protections cover only federal lands.
4. **Invasive species** — Cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*) and ventenata (*Ventenata dubia*) displacing native bunchgrasses on east slopes, shortening fire return intervals. North Cascades: 156+ exotic plant species documented in NOCA (GOV-04).
5. **Glacial and snowpack loss** — Cascade glaciers lost 25-50% of area since 1900. Mount Rainier: 25 named glaciers, all retreating. Snowpack provides 40-60% of summer streamflow; loss creates drought stress across all zones.

### Ecological Networks (Phase 617)

Four cross-module ecological cascades bind the Cascade Range's survey modules into a single interconnected system:

- **CAS-01 (Salmon-Nitrogen Vertical Transport):** Ocean → River → Riparian Forest → Mycorrhizal Network → Upslope Trees. Marine nitrogen transported by salmon, distributed by predators, taken up by trees, redistributed by EMF fungi. Spans Aquatic, Fauna, Flora, Fungi modules.
- **CAS-02 (Flying Squirrel-Truffle-Tree Mutualism):** Old-Growth → Truffle Fruiting → Squirrel Mycophagy → Spore Dispersal → Seedling Mycorrhization. Down wood provides truffle substrate; squirrels disperse spores; seedlings establish via mycorrhizal partners. Spotted owl preys on squirrel, linking apex predator to forest regeneration. Spans Fungi, Fauna, Flora modules.
- **CAS-03 (Beaver-Wetland-Amphibian Hydrology):** Beaver Dam → Pond/Wetland → Amphibian Habitat → Stream Temperature Buffering. Ecosystem engineering creates habitat for Oregon spotted frog and cold-water refugia for salmonids. Spans Fauna, Aquatic, Flora modules.
- **CAS-04 (Fire-Disturbance-Fungi-Regeneration):** Fire → Canopy Opening → Soil Heating → Fungal Community Shift → Post-Fire Succession. Operates differently on east slopes (frequent, low-intensity) vs. west slopes (infrequent, stand-replacing). Fire-following morels, mycorrhizal refugia in old-growth remnants. Spans Threats, Fungi, Flora modules.

**Network density finding:** The montane zone (2,000-4,500 ft) has the highest network density — all 4 cascades and all 6 hub nodes operate within this zone. Protecting montane old-growth is the single highest-leverage conservation action for maintaining Cascade Range ecological networks.

## Companion Study

This publication is the second installment in a two-part Pacific Northwest biodiversity research series:

1. **Columbia Valley Rainforest Biodiversity** (v1.49.22) — Horizontal watershed study of the temperate rainforest ecosystem in the Columbia Valley, British Columbia.
2. **Cascade Range Biodiversity** (v1.49.23) — Vertical elevation study of the Cascade mountain range in Washington and Oregon.

Together, these studies examine two organizing principles of Pacific Northwest biodiversity: the horizontal watershed gradient (Columbia Valley) and the vertical elevation gradient (Cascade Range). The four ecological cascades identified in this study (CAS-01 through CAS-04) parallel the three cascades documented in the Columbia Valley study, with salmon-nitrogen transport (CAS-01) serving as the direct link between the two systems.
