# Fauna Survey — Vertebrate Biodiversity by Elevation Band

> **Phase 613 — Cascade Range Biodiversity Research**
>
> Comprehensive survey of mammals, birds, amphibians, and reptiles across five elevation zones of the Cascade Range (Washington and Oregon). Organized by elevation transect with emphasis on carnivore recovery programs, keystone species profiles, and cross-module ecological connections.
>
> All species counts cite specific source_ids. Climate projections cite GOV-06, PR-05, or PR-06. Indigenous references name specific nations. No GPS coordinates for endangered species.

---

## Introduction

The Cascade Range vertebrate fauna reflects the same elevation-driven partitioning that organizes the flora and fungi. From black-tailed deer browsing oak savanna in the western foothills to white-tailed ptarmigan on alpine ridgelines, the vertical gradient compresses faunal communities that would span hundreds of miles of latitude into a few thousand feet of elevation change. This compression creates both extraordinary diversity and acute vulnerability — species adapted to narrow elevation bands have limited options when climate shifts their habitat upslope.

This survey documents vertebrate communities within each elevation zone, profiles keystone and indicator species, examines active carnivore recovery programs (wolf, grizzly, wolverine, fisher), and flags cross-module connections to flora (herbivory, seed dispersal, pollination), fungi (mycophagy, spore dispersal), aquatic systems (salmon predation, nutrient transport), and threats (habitat fragmentation, climate compression).

The Cascade Range supports approximately 300+ bird species, 80+ mammal species, 25+ amphibian species, and 20+ reptile species across its Washington and Oregon extent (GOV-04, GOV-08, GOV-09). Many of these are shared with the companion Columbia Valley study, but the Cascade Range's vertical structure creates distinct community assemblages not found in lowland watersheds.

## Zone 1: Western Foothills (500–2,000 ft)

### Mammals

The western foothills support the highest mammalian diversity of any Cascade zone due to proximity to lowland habitat, edge effects with agricultural lands, and relatively mild winters. Key species:

- **Columbian black-tailed deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*) — Primary large herbivore. Year-round resident in foothills, seasonal migrant to higher elevations. Browses oak woodland understory, maintaining savanna openings. Key prey for recovering wolf and cougar populations (GOV-08).

- **Roosevelt elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) — Larger-bodied western subspecies. Uses foothill meadows and forest edges for grazing. Herds of 20-50 animals maintain grassland openings through grazing pressure. Cultural significance to multiple Pacific Northwest nations including Yakama, Warm Springs, and Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde (GOV-09).

- **American beaver** (*Castor canadensis*) — Ecosystem engineer. Builds dams on low-gradient foothill streams creating pond-wetland complexes critical for amphibian breeding, stream temperature buffering, and riparian vegetation expansion. Cascade Forest Conservancy actively reintroducing beavers to Cascade watersheds (CON-01). **Cross-module flag:** Hub node in CASCADE-CAS-03 (beaver-wetland-amphibian cascade).

- **Black bear** (*Ursus americanus*) — Omnivore with significant ecological role as salmon carcass distributor. Bears transport salmon carcasses 50-500 m from streams into forest, depositing marine-derived nitrogen that fertilizes riparian trees (PR-03). **Cross-module flag:** Nutrient vector in CASCADE-CAS-01.

- **Coyote** (*Canis latrans*) — Mesopredator filling carnivore niche in absence of historical wolf populations. Range expanding into foothill-urban interface. Prey base includes small mammals, ground-nesting birds, and fruit.

### Birds

- **Bald eagle** (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) — Winter congregations along salmon-bearing foothill rivers represent one of the Cascade Range's most visible wildlife spectacles. Skagit River winter eagle concentrations exceed 500 individuals in peak years (GOV-08). Salmon carcass scavenger and predator — contributes to marine nutrient distribution away from streams. **Cross-module flag:** Nutrient vector in CASCADE-CAS-01.

- **Acorn woodpecker** (*Melanerpes formicivorus*) — Oak savanna obligate. Creates granary trees with thousands of individually stored acorns. Present in Oregon Cascade foothills where Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) communities persist. Indicator species for oak savanna habitat integrity (GOV-09).

- **Western screech-owl** (*Megascops kennicottii*) — Cavity nester in foothill deciduous and mixed forests. Depends on natural cavities or abandoned woodpecker holes in mature oaks and cottonwoods. Populations declining where foothill forests converted to development.

### Amphibians and Reptiles

- **Oregon spotted frog** (*Rana pretiosa*) — Federally Threatened. Once widespread in western Oregon and Washington lowland wetlands, now restricted to fewer than 50 known populations. Breeds in shallow, warm-water margins of beaver ponds and seasonal wetlands. Cascade foothill populations represent a significant fraction of remaining range (GOV-05). **Cross-module flag:** Indicator species in CASCADE-CAS-03.

- **Western pond turtle** (*Actinemys marmorata*) — State-listed species in both Washington and Oregon. Requires slow-moving or still-water foothill habitats with basking sites. Threatened by habitat loss, introduced bullfrogs, and nest predation by raccoons (GOV-08, GOV-09).

- **Northern red-legged frog** (*Rana aurora*) — Breeds in foothill wetlands and slow streams. Associated with older forests providing cool, moist terrestrial habitat between breeding seasons. Indicator of forest-wetland connectivity.

## Zone 2: Montane Forest (2,000–4,500 ft)

### Mammals

The montane zone hosts the greatest density of mammalian biomass in the Cascade Range. Old-growth forests provide multilayered habitat structure — canopy, subcanopy, understory, forest floor, and below-ground — supporting species at every level.

- **Northern flying squirrel** (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) — Obligate mycophage and keystone disperser of hypogeous ectomycorrhizal fungi (truffles). Excavates and consumes truffles as primary food source; spores pass through digestive tract intact and are deposited in fecal pellets throughout home range, inoculating new root systems with viable EMF spores (PR-02, GOV-01). Depends on old-growth forest structure — cavity nesting in large snags, aerial travel through continuous canopy. **Cross-module flag:** Hub node in CASCADE-CAS-02 (flying squirrel-truffle-tree mutualism). Primary prey of Northern spotted owl.

- **Northern spotted owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — Federally Threatened. Old-growth obligate requiring large-diameter trees for nesting (typically >30 in DBH) and continuous canopy for hunting. Home ranges of 2,000-14,000 acres in Washington Cascades, reflecting prey density and habitat quality (GOV-01, GOV-05). Population declining ~3.8% per year in Washington Cascades due to old-growth loss and barred owl competition. Links directly to CASCADE-CAS-02: spotted owl preys on flying squirrel, which disperses truffles, which inoculate seedlings.

- **Cougar** (*Puma concolor*) — Apex predator in montane forests. Solitary, territorial. Home ranges 50-400 km². Primary prey is black-tailed deer and elk. Top-down regulation of ungulate populations influences browse pressure on regenerating forests (GOV-08).

- **American marten** (*Martes americana*) — Montane-subalpine carnivore associated with complex forest structure — downed wood, canopy cover, and deep snow. Uses subnivean (under-snow) spaces created by downed logs for winter hunting of voles and squirrels. Indicator of forest structural complexity and connectivity (GOV-01).

- **Pileated woodpecker** (*Dryocopus pileatus*) — Though classified as a bird, functions as a mammalian-habitat engineer. Excavates large rectangular cavities in snags and large-diameter live trees that are subsequently used by martens, flying squirrels, Vaux's swifts, and other cavity-dependent species. Requires large snags (>20 in DBH) and mature forests with abundant carpenter ants (GOV-01).

### Birds

- **Northern goshawk** (*Accipiter gentilis*) — Forest interior raptor. Nests in mature and old-growth stands with closed canopy and open understory for hunting flights. Sensitive to forest fragmentation. Indicator species for landscape-level old-growth connectivity (GOV-01).

- **Varied thrush** (*Ixoreus naevius*) — Iconic Pacific Northwest forest bird. Breeds in dense, moist montane forests. Song is a single sustained tone that defines the acoustic environment of old-growth Cascade forests. Forages in leaf litter, connecting to detrital food webs.

- **Marbled murrelet** (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — Federally Threatened seabird that nests exclusively in old-growth trees, typically >200 years old, within 50 miles of coast. Montane Cascade forests within coastal influence zone (western slopes of southern Washington and northern Oregon Cascades) provide critical nesting habitat. One of the most unusual nesting strategies of any bird — a seabird requiring ancient forest (GOV-05).

### Amphibians

- **Pacific giant salamander** (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*) — Largest terrestrial salamander in North America (up to 13 in). Aquatic larvae in cold montane streams; adults in moist forest. Top predator in small stream food webs. Indicator of stream health and forest canopy integrity — requires cold water maintained by forest shade (GOV-08).

- **Coastal tailed frog** (*Ascaphus truei*) — Permanently stream-dwelling. Uniquely adapted to fast-flowing mountain streams with flattened body and tail-like copulatory organ (only North American frog with internal fertilization). Extremely sensitive to sedimentation and temperature changes. Old-growth indicator species (GOV-08).

## Zone 3: Subalpine (4,500–6,500 ft)

### Mammals

- **Hoary marmot** (*Marmota caligata*) — Colonial alpine/subalpine herbivore inhabiting talus slopes and subalpine meadows. Hibernates 7-8 months. Alarm whistles alert entire mountain community to predators. Burrows create microhabitat for plants and invertebrates. Populations potentially threatened by reduced snowpack (earlier emergence, predation mismatch) (GOV-04).

- **Cascade red fox** (*Vulpes vulpes cascadensis*) — Endemic Cascade Range subspecies. Subalpine and alpine specialist, genetically distinct from lowland red foxes. One of the rarest carnivores in the Pacific Northwest with estimated population of fewer than 500 individuals. Listed as a candidate for federal protection (GOV-08, PR-05). Range restricted to subalpine parkland and alpine meadows of the Washington and Oregon Cascades.

- **Wolverine** (*Gulo gulo*) — State Endangered in Washington. Requires persistent spring snowpack for denning (females dig natal dens in deep snow, typically >1.5 m depth, that must persist through May). Climate projections suggest 30-60% reduction in suitable wolverine denning habitat in Washington Cascades by 2080 (PR-06, GOV-06). Fewer than 25 individuals estimated in Washington Cascades. Recovery program monitoring via camera traps and DNA hair snares (GOV-08). **Cross-module flag:** Climate-threatened species profiled in Threats module.

- **Snowshoe hare** (*Lepus americanus*) — Keystone prey species in subalpine forests. Seasonal pelage change (brown to white) timed to historical snow cover patterns. Climate-driven snow reduction creating phenological mismatch — white hares on brown ground experience increased predation. Prey base for Canada lynx (historically present but now extirpated from Oregon, rare in Washington Cascades) (GOV-08).

### Birds

- **White-tailed ptarmigan** (*Lagopus leucura*) — Alpine specialist. Year-round resident above timberline. Complete seasonal plumage change (cryptic brown in summer, pure white in winter). Feeds on willow buds, alpine forbs, and insects. Only ptarmigan species in the contiguous western United States. Washington Cascades populations at southern range limit — vulnerable to upslope habitat compression under warming (GOV-04).

- **Clark's nutcracker** (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — Keystone seed disperser for whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*). Harvests and caches 30,000-100,000 whitebark pine seeds per year in thousands of individual cache sites across the subalpine landscape. Unretrieved caches germinate, making Clark's nutcracker the primary regeneration vector for whitebark pine (GOV-04). **Cross-module flag:** Links to Flora survey whitebark pine keystone profile.

- **Gray-crowned rosy-finch** (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) — Alpine specialist foraging on insects blown onto snowfields and glaciers. Nests in cliff crevices. One of North America's highest-elevation breeding passerines. Uses volcanic fumarole heat at some Cascade volcanic sites for winter survival.

### Amphibians

- **Cascades frog** (*Rana cascadae*) — Named for the Cascade Range. Breeds in subalpine lakes, ponds, and wet meadows at 2,500-7,500 ft elevation. Populations declining across range due to UV-B radiation (thin ozone at elevation), emerging fungal disease (chytrid — *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis*), and introduced trout predation on tadpoles (GOV-04, GOV-09). One of three Pacific Northwest amphibians listed as a Species of Concern.

## Zone 4: Alpine (6,500+ ft)

### Mammals

- **American pika** (*Ochotona princeps*) — Talus-dwelling lagomorph with extremely low thermal tolerance. Dies at body temperatures above 42°C and cannot survive ambient temperatures above 25.5°C for extended periods. Climate sentinel — populations disappearing from lower-elevation sites across the Great Basin but currently persisting in Cascade Range alpine talus due to relatively cool, moist conditions. However, USGS monitoring shows range contraction at lower elevation margins in Oregon Cascades (GOV-06). Pika haypiles (cached vegetation stored for winter consumption) support distinct microbial communities and seed germination.

- **Mountain goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) — Native to North Cascades, introduced to southern Washington Cascades (including Olympic Mountains, from which they were controversially relocated). Uses alpine cliff faces, snowfields, and meadows. Bands of 5-20 animals traverse steep terrain that excludes predators. Grazing on alpine vegetation can impact sensitive alpine plant communities, creating management tension between native ungulate and rare plant conservation (GOV-04).

### Birds

The alpine zone supports a limited but highly specialized avifauna. White-tailed ptarmigan (profiled in Zone 3), horned lark (*Eremophila alpestris*), and American pipit (*Anthus rubescens*) breed in alpine tundra. Raptors — golden eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*) and prairie falcon (*Falco mexicanus*) — hunt across alpine terrain but nest on cliff faces below the summit zone.

## Zone 5: Eastern Slopes (Rain Shadow)

### Mammals

- **Mule deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus hemionus*) — Eastern subspecies replacing black-tailed deer east of the crest. Adapted to open ponderosa pine woodland and shrub-steppe. Larger ears and different gait (stotting vs. running) reflect open-habitat predator detection strategy. Major seasonal migrant — summer in subalpine meadows, winter in low-elevation east-slope valleys (GOV-08).

- **Elk** (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*) — Rocky Mountain elk subspecies on eastern slopes (vs. Roosevelt elk on western slopes). Large herds in Yakima River drainage and central Oregon Cascades. Grazing maintains meadow openings in ponderosa pine forests. Hunted by Yakama Nation under treaty rights.

- **White-headed woodpecker** (*Dryobates albolarvatus*) — Ponderosa pine specialist. Only North American woodpecker with a white head. Depends on large-diameter ponderosa for nesting cavities and bark beetle prey. Fire suppression has degraded habitat by increasing stand density and reducing large ponderosa recruitment (GOV-01, GOV-09). Indicator species for east-slope ponderosa pine forest health.

### Birds

- **Lewis's woodpecker** (*Melanerpes lewis*) — Open-forest specialist using burned ponderosa stands and oak woodland edges on east slopes. Flycatching feeding strategy (unusual for woodpeckers) requires open canopy structure maintained by low-intensity fire. Listed as a Species of Concern in both Washington and Oregon. Population decline linked to fire suppression and snag removal (GOV-09).

- **Flammulated owl** (*Psiloscops flammeolus*) — Smallest owl in the Pacific Northwest. Exclusively insectivorous. Nests in ponderosa pine cavities in open, park-like stands maintained by frequent fire. East-slope Cascade forests represent core habitat. Migratory — winters in Mexico and Central America, breeding in Pacific Northwest ponderosa zone (GOV-01).

### Reptiles

- **Western rattlesnake** (*Crotalus oreganus*) — Only venomous snake in the Pacific Northwest. Found on dry east-slope habitats from talus slopes to ponderosa woodland. Den sites (hibernacula) used communally by dozens to hundreds of snakes are critical conservation features — destruction of a single den site can eliminate a local population (GOV-09).

- **Sagebrush lizard** (*Sceloporus graciosus*) — Dry east-slope shrub-steppe and open ponderosa specialist. Basking behavior on rocks and downed wood. Indicator of open, sun-exposed habitat structure.

## Carnivore Recovery Programs

### Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*)

The gray wolf was extirpated from the Cascade Range by the 1930s through systematic predator control programs. Recovery began naturally when wolves from the Northern Rocky Mountains population recolonized northeastern Washington in 2008. As of 2024, the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) documents approximately 40 confirmed packs statewide, with several packs established in the North Cascades (GOV-08).

**Cascade Range status:**
- North Cascades: 3-5 confirmed packs, naturally colonizing from British Columbia and northeastern Washington
- South Cascades (Washington): No confirmed packs as of latest survey
- Oregon Cascades: ODFW confirms wolf presence in eastern Oregon; western Oregon Cascades activity documented but no confirmed packs

**Ecological significance:** Wolf recovery restores top-down trophic regulation absent for nearly a century. Potential effects include:
- Reduced elk and deer browse pressure on riparian vegetation (trophic cascade)
- Mesopredator suppression (coyote population regulation)
- Carrion provisioning for scavenger communities (ravens, eagles, bears)

**Conflict and management:** Livestock depredation by Cascade packs remains low but politically significant. WDFW manages through lethal removal authority balanced against species recovery objectives.

**Source:** GOV-08

### Grizzly Bear (*Ursus arctos horribilis*)

The North Cascades Ecosystem (NCE) is one of six designated Grizzly Bear Recovery Zones in the contiguous United States. The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service and National Park Service completed a Draft Environmental Impact Statement for grizzly restoration in the NCE in 2017, but the process was paused and has not been finalized as of 2025 (GOV-05).

**Status:**
- Historical population: estimated 200-400 grizzlies in the North Cascades prior to Euro-American settlement
- Current population: possibly 0-5 individuals. No confirmed sighting in the U.S. North Cascades since 1996. Occasional tracks and unconfirmed reports
- The NCE is the only recovery zone with no confirmed resident grizzly population
- Contiguous habitat extends into British Columbia where a small population persists

**Recovery plan:** The proposed augmentation would translocate 25 grizzlies (from interior British Columbia or Montana) into the NCE over 5-10 years to establish a founding population. Process halted pending political resolution.

**Source:** GOV-04, GOV-05

### Wolverine (*Gulo gulo*)

Profiled in Zone 3 (Subalpine). The wolverine is the Cascade Range's most climate-vulnerable large carnivore. Recovery depends on persistent snowpack for denning — a resource directly threatened by warming projections (PR-06, GOV-06).

**Key recovery data:**
- Washington Cascades: estimated <25 individuals
- Denning habitat requires >1.5 m snow depth persisting through May
- Climate models project 30-60% loss of suitable denning habitat by 2080
- Monitoring: camera trap grids and DNA hair snares operated by WDFW and Cascades Carnivore Project

**Source:** GOV-08, PR-06

### Pacific Fisher (*Pekania pennanti*)

The fisher was listed as Federally Endangered (West Coast DPS) in 2020. Cascade Range populations are the subject of active reintroduction efforts.

**Recovery program:**
- Washington Cascades: 2015-2020 reintroduction placed 89 fishers from British Columbia source populations into the southern Washington Cascades (Gifford Pinchot National Forest area). National Park Service and WDFW collaborative project. Monitoring via GPS collars, camera traps, and track plates
- Oregon Cascades: Fisher presence detected in southwestern Oregon Cascades; connectivity between southern Oregon and Washington reintroduction populations is a conservation priority
- Habitat requirements: mature/old-growth forest with large trees for denning cavities, structural complexity, and connectivity. Fisher avoids open areas and young plantations

**Source:** GOV-05, GOV-08

## Keystone Species Profiles

### Pacific Salmon (Oncorhynchus spp.)

**Role:** Marine nutrient vector. Salmon connect Cascade Range forests to the Pacific Ocean through the upstream transport of marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus. Naiman et al. (2002) documented 40-80% of riparian nitrogen in salmon-bearing PNW watersheds is marine in origin (PR-03).

**Species in Cascade Range watersheds:**
- Chinook salmon (*O. tshawytscha*) — largest species, spawns in mainstem rivers
- Coho salmon (*O. kisutch*) — spawns in smaller tributaries, rears in beaver ponds
- Steelhead trout (*O. mykiss*) — anadromous rainbow trout, uses diverse stream types
- Sockeye salmon (*O. nerka*) — lake-rearing species, Baker Lake and Lake Wenatchee populations

**Cascade significance:** Salmon carcasses fertilize riparian forests. Predators (bear, eagle, otter, mink) distribute carcasses into forest. Marine nitrogen enters mycorrhizal networks and is redistributed to upslope trees. **Hub node in CASCADE-CAS-01.**

**Source:** PR-03, PR-04, GOV-06

### Northern Flying Squirrel (Glaucomys sabrinus)

**Role:** Mycorrhizal spore disperser. Primary food source is hypogeous ectomycorrhizal fungi (truffles). Spores pass through digestive tract intact and inoculate new root systems via fecal pellets. Without flying squirrel dispersal, truffle spore dispersal distances collapse from home-range scale (hectares) to below-ground diffusion (centimeters).

**Conservation dependency:** Old-growth forest structure — large-diameter snags for cavity nesting, continuous canopy for gliding, downed wood supporting truffle fruiting substrate.

**Hub node in CASCADE-CAS-02.** Prey of Northern spotted owl, linking mycorrhizal network maintenance to apex predator conservation.

**Source:** PR-02, GOV-01

### American Beaver (Castor canadensis)

**Role:** Ecosystem engineer. Dam construction creates pond-wetland complexes providing:
- Amphibian breeding habitat (Oregon spotted frog, Pacific giant salamander)
- Stream temperature buffering (critical under climate warming)
- Groundwater recharge
- Riparian vegetation expansion
- Cold-water refugia for salmonids

**Recovery:** Cascade Forest Conservancy beaver reintroduction program active (CON-01). Beavers being relocated to Cascade watersheds where dam-building activity restores degraded stream systems.

**Hub node in CASCADE-CAS-03.**

**Source:** CON-01, GOV-08, GOV-09

## Elevational Migration Patterns

Many Cascade vertebrates use elevation as a seasonal resource gradient:

| Species | Summer Elevation | Winter Elevation | Migration Driver |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Mule deer | Subalpine meadows (5,000-7,000 ft) | East-slope valleys (1,500-3,000 ft) | Snow depth |
| Roosevelt elk | Montane meadows (3,000-5,000 ft) | Western foothills (500-2,000 ft) | Forage availability |
| Mountain goat | Alpine (7,000+ ft) | Subalpine cliffs (4,500-6,000 ft) | Wind-cleared forage |
| Hermit thrush | Montane-subalpine (3,000-6,000 ft) | Migration to Mexico | Photoperiod + temperature |
| Dark-eyed junco | Subalpine (4,000-6,000 ft) | Foothills and lowlands (<2,000 ft) | Snow cover |
| American dipper | Montane streams (2,000-5,000 ft) | Lower-elevation streams (<2,000 ft) | Stream ice-up |

**Climate threat:** As warming compresses upper-elevation habitat and shifts phenology, species with narrow elevation-dependent migration timing face phenological mismatch. Snowshoe hare pelage change is the most documented example — white fur arriving before or persisting after snow cover, increasing predation vulnerability.

**Source:** GOV-04, GOV-08, PR-05
