# Fungi Survey: Mycological Biodiversity of the Cascade Range

> **Cascade Range Biodiversity Research (v1.49.23) — Phase 614**
>
> Survey module 4. Ectomycorrhizal, saprobic, and parasitic fungi across old-growth and managed Cascade Range forests. Uses source index from `01-source-index.md` and elevation zone schema from `00-elevation-zones.md`.

The Cascade Range is a mycological landscape as much as a geological one. From the deep volcanic soils of the Western Cascades lowlands to the pumice flats of the High Cascades crest, fungi mediate every significant nutrient exchange, tree establishment event, and decomposition cycle in these forests. They are not incidental inhabitants catalogued after the conifers and the elk. They are the operating system — the below-ground infrastructure on which every above-ground organism depends.

This document surveys four dimensions of Cascade Range fungal ecology. First, the ectomycorrhizal network architecture that connects trees into functioning communities through hyphal threads thinner than spider silk. Second, the commercially important species — chanterelle, matsutake, morel, and bolete — that link forest ecology to regional economy and cultural practice. Third, the agarikon profile: a single species whose biology compresses the entire old-growth conservation argument into one organism. Fourth, the logging legacy — the discovery that soil fungal communities carry detectable signatures of timber harvest four decades after the last chainsaw fell silent.

Throughout, quantitative data are attributed to specific sources in the Source Index (`01-source-index.md`). The emphasis is on relationships — fungi as connective tissue linking the Cascade Range's ecological modules into a functioning whole.

## Overview: Mycological Diversity of the Cascade Range

The Cascade Range supports one of the richest fungal floras in North America, a diversity driven by three factors that converge in these mountains more powerfully than in any other temperate mountain range on the continent.

**Precipitation gradient.** The Cascade crest divides one of the steepest moisture gradients in the world. Western slopes receive 60–120 inches of annual precipitation, sustaining the deep organic soils and year-round moisture that EMF communities require. Eastern slopes receive 15–30 inches, supporting dry-forest EMF assemblages adapted to ponderosa pine, western larch, and grand fir in well-drained volcanic pumice soils. The full transect — from hyper-wet western hemlock forests through the crest to dry eastern ponderosa woodland — spans the complete range of EMF community types found in Pacific Northwest coniferous forests (GOV-01, GOV-07).

**Host tree diversity.** The Cascade Range supports 15+ conifer species across its elevation and moisture gradients, each forming EMF partnerships with characteristic fungal associates. Douglas-fir dominates the low- and mid-elevation western slopes; western hemlock is the climax species below 3,000 ft; Pacific silver fir and mountain hemlock define the upper montane; noble fir occupies ridgetops and high slopes; grand fir and ponderosa pine dominate the eastern slopes; whitebark pine and subalpine fir mark treeline; western larch occupies transitional zones on the east side. Each host species supports a partially distinct EMF community, and the aggregate across all hosts produces the total EMF diversity figure — 200+ species per old-growth stand — that characterizes Cascade forests (GOV-01).

**Volcanic substrate diversity.** The Cascade Range is geologically young and volcanically active. Soils range from deep, clay-rich weathered volcanic deposits in the Old Cascades (western) to fresh pumice and ash deposits in the High Cascades (eastern). This substrate variation creates microhabitat diversity for soil fungi: different EMF species favor different soil textures, pH levels, and mineral compositions. Matsutake's affinity for pumice soils (Section 2.2) is one example; *Rhizopogon* truffle diversity in deep organic soils is another. The geological heterogeneity of the Cascades provides more fungal niches per unit area than geologically uniform mountain ranges (GOV-01, GOV-07).

**Functional guild diversity.** Cascade Range fungi span all major functional guilds: ectomycorrhizal mutualists, arbuscular mycorrhizal mutualists (in understory herbs and hardwoods), saprotrophic decomposers (white rot, brown rot, soft rot, litter decomposers), parasitic pathogens (root rot, stem decay, rust fungi), and lichenized fungi (nitrogen-fixing canopy lichens). This functional breadth means that fungi occupy every ecological role from primary nutrient acquisition to decomposition to pathogenesis to atmospheric nitrogen fixation. No other organism group in the Cascade Range spans this functional range.

**Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF).** While this document focuses primarily on ectomycorrhizal fungi — the dominant below-ground association in Cascade coniferous forests — arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) play a critical role in the Cascade understory and in hardwood-dominated zones. AMF form intracellular structures called arbuscules within plant root cells, providing phosphorus in exchange for carbon. In the Cascades, AMF are the primary mycorrhizal type for: understory herbs and ferns (sword fern, oxalis, vanilla leaf), deciduous hardwoods (red alder, bigleaf maple, vine maple), and meadow grasses and forbs in subalpine and alpine zones. Red alder (*Alnus rubra*) forms a particularly significant three-way symbiosis: AMF provide phosphorus access, nitrogen-fixing *Frankia* bacteria in root nodules fix atmospheric nitrogen, and the alder provides photosynthetic carbon to both partners. This alder-AMF-*Frankia* triad makes riparian alder stands major nitrogen input sites for Cascade forest ecosystems, linking AMF ecology to nutrient cycling in ways that complement but differ from the EMF-dominated conifer system (GOV-01).

**Research foundation.** The H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest LTER site, located in the Western Cascades of Oregon at the Blue River Ranger District of the Willamette National Forest, has been the epicenter of Pacific Northwest mycological research since the 1970s. Continuous ecological research at the Andrews since 1948 has produced the foundational datasets for Cascade EMF ecology: Luoma's 47-species hypogeous inventory (PR-03), Smith's *Piloderma*-down wood relationship (PR-01), Amaranthus's truffle-log proximity study (PR-02), and the 2023 logging legacy analysis (PR-04) were all conducted at or near the Andrews. The USFS PNW Research Station General Technical Reports (GOV-01) synthesize decades of Andrews-based research into the management-relevant frameworks cited throughout this document. The Cascade Range is, consequently, one of the best-studied fungal landscapes in the world — a distinction that makes the documented losses (logging legacy, old-growth species decline, agarikon contraction) all the more precisely quantified and difficult to dismiss.

The Andrews LTER occupies a representative position on the Cascade moisture and elevation gradient: 500–1,630 m elevation (1,640–5,350 ft), receiving 230 cm (90 inches) of annual precipitation, with stands ranging from 40-year-old plantations to 500+ year-old old-growth Douglas-fir/western hemlock. This range of conditions within a single research forest has enabled the controlled comparisons — old-growth vs. managed, intact vs. clearcut, down wood retained vs. removed — that underpin the quantitative claims in this document. No other temperate forest research station has produced a comparable depth of mycorrhizal data over a comparable timespan, and the Andrews datasets remain the primary reference against which Cascade Range fungal ecology is measured globally (GOV-01, PR-03).

**Scale of undocumented diversity.** Despite the Andrews LTER's intensive research history, the total fungal diversity of the Cascade Range remains substantially undocumented. Luoma's 47-species hypogeous inventory (PR-03) was conducted at a single site; extrapolation across the full latitudinal and elevational range of the Cascades would yield substantially higher species counts. Many Cascade fungi — particularly saprotrophic microfungi, endophytic fungi living within plant tissues, and soil-dwelling species that rarely produce visible fruiting bodies — have never been formally described. Modern eDNA metabarcoding studies (the methodology used in PR-04) consistently detect Operational Taxonomic Units (OTUs) that do not match any described species, suggesting that the named species represent a fraction of the total fungal diversity present in Cascade soils. The documented 200+ EMF species per old-growth stand (GOV-01) is best understood as a floor estimate — the actual number of fungal species in any functional guild at any given site likely exceeds what current taxonomy and sampling methods can capture.

## 1. Ectomycorrhizal Network Architecture

Ectomycorrhizal fungi (EMF) form the dominant below-ground symbiosis in Cascade Range coniferous forests. In these partnerships, fungal hyphae sheath tree root tips in a dense mantle, penetrating between cortical cells to form the Hartig net — an intercellular interface where the fundamental exchange occurs: soil-derived phosphorus and nitrogen flow from fungus to tree; photosynthetic carbon flows from tree to fungus. This is not a minor metabolic supplement. It is a structural dependency: without EMF partners, the dominant Cascade conifers — Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), noble fir (*Abies procera*), Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*) — cannot access sufficient soil nutrients to sustain the biomass densities that define these forests (GOV-01).

The USDA Forest Service Pacific Northwest Research Station has produced the foundational body of quantitative EMF research for the Cascade Range through a series of General Technical Reports and associated studies conducted primarily at the H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest Long-Term Ecological Research (LTER) site in the Western Cascades of Oregon. The following subsections draw on that body of work.

### 1.1 Andrews LTER Benchmark Study — Hypogeous EMF Diversity

The benchmark inventory for Cascade Range EMF diversity remains Luoma (1988), conducted at the H.J. Andrews LTER site in the middle Cascade Range. Luoma documented **47 species of hypogeous (below-ground fruiting) ectomycorrhizal fungi** in mixed-habitat areas spanning old-growth and younger forest stands (PR-03). This figure — 47 hypogeous species at a single research forest — established the Andrews as one of the most thoroughly inventoried mycorrhizal research sites in North America and provided the taxonomic baseline against which all subsequent Cascade EMF studies have been measured.

Two findings from Luoma's inventory carry particular weight for conservation:

1. **Two hypogeous EMF species were restricted exclusively to the oldest stands** — they were not detected in younger forest age classes at the Andrews site (PR-03). These late-successional obligates represent the fraction of EMF diversity that cannot persist through timber harvest rotations and cannot recolonize from adjacent young forests. Their presence is a diagnostic indicator of old-growth fungal community integrity.

2. The 47-species count represented only the hypogeous fraction — below-ground fruiters in the genera *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, *Leucogaster*, *Melanogaster*, and allies. The total EMF species richness including epigeous (above-ground fruiting) genera — *Russula*, *Cortinarius*, *Amanita*, *Suillus*, *Tricholoma*, *Inocybe*, *Lactarius*, and others — would be substantially higher. USFS PNW Research Station synthesis reports estimate total EMF richness in old-growth Cascade forests at **over 200 species per stand** (GOV-01), consistent with broader Pacific Northwest old-growth EMF inventories.

### 1.2 Quantitative EMF Data from USFS PNW Research Station

The following quantitative data points are drawn from USFS PNW Research Station publications (GOV-01: Gen. Tech. Rep. PNW-GTR-371, PNW-GTR-431, PNW-GTR-576) and associated peer-reviewed studies conducted in the Cascade Range. Each datum is attributed to its specific source.

**1. Old-growth EMF species richness exceeds 200 species per stand** in Cascade Range Douglas-fir/western hemlock forests, compared to 20–40 species in young plantations (0–30 years post-harvest). The ratio represents a 5–10x reduction in EMF species richness following clearcutting (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

**2. Hypogeous (truffle-producing) EMF diversity is approximately 4x higher in old-growth than in young plantations.** This truffle diversity ratio is the single most ecologically consequential metric for Cascade fungi, because truffle-producing EMF are the food base for mycophagous mammals (northern flying squirrel, red-backed vole) that in turn sustain the northern spotted owl (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

**3. Up to 20% of a host tree's total fixed carbon is allocated to its ectomycorrhizal partners.** This carbon subsidy, measured through isotope labeling studies, represents the energetic cost that Cascade conifers pay for mycorrhizal services — a cost that shapes forest carbon budgets and is invisible in above-ground biomass measurements (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-431).

**4. Forty-seven hypogeous EMF species documented at the Andrews LTER site** across mixed-habitat stands in the middle Cascade Range, with two species restricted to oldest stands (PR-03, cited in GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

**5. EMF community recovery following clearcutting requires an estimated 150–200 years** to reach old-growth composition in Cascade Range forests. Standard timber rotation lengths (40–60 years) harvest the forest before EMF communities have recovered from the previous rotation, creating a progressive loss of below-ground diversity across successive harvest cycles (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

**6. *Piloderma fallax* occurrence increases with increasing cover of down wood in decay class V** (the most advanced stage of decomposition) in both old-growth and young Douglas-fir stands in the West Cascades of Oregon. This EMF species, which forms bright yellow mycelial mats on and within heavily decayed logs, is an indicator of the down wood habitat quality that sustains EMF diversity (PR-01, supported by GOV-01: PNW-GTR-371).

**7. Down wood increases the probability of truffle occurrence** in Cascade Range forests, with truffle fruiting bodies found disproportionately within one meter of decaying logs. Amaranthus et al. (1994) documented that decaying logs function as "moisture reservoirs," maintaining soil moisture conditions that sustain active ectomycorrhizae during the seasonal summer drought that characterizes Cascade Range forests (PR-02, supported by GOV-01: PNW-GTR-371).

**8. Late-successional EMF specialists in the genera *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, and *Rhizopogon* (notably *R. vinicolor*) are absent or extremely rare in plantation forests** across the Cascade Range. These species require the specific soil chemistry, organic horizon depth, moisture regimes, and root network complexity of old-growth stands. Their absence from managed forests is structural — they cannot recolonize from spore rain alone — and represents a functional deficit that limits the mycorrhizal services available to regenerating trees (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

**9. Mycelial mat-forming EMF species occupy 15–40% of the forest floor area in productive old-growth Douglas-fir stands** in the Western Cascades. These mats, composed primarily of *Piloderma*, *Hysterangium*, and *Gautieria* species, create localized zones of enhanced mineral weathering, nutrient cycling, and water retention in the upper soil horizons (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-431).

**10. Truffle biomass production in old-growth Cascade forests averages 5–10 kg dry weight per hectare per year**, with production concentrated in autumn (September–November) and spring (April–May). This biomass production is the caloric foundation of the mycophagous mammal food web (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

### 1.3 Down Wood as Mycorrhizal Refugia

The relationship between coarse woody debris and ectomycorrhizal fungi is one of the best-documented ecological interactions in Cascade Range forest ecology, established through two complementary studies at the Andrews LTER.

**Smith et al. (2000)** examined EMF communities in relation to down wood in West Cascades Douglas-fir forests (PR-01). Their central finding — that *Piloderma fallax* occurrence increased with increasing cover of heavily decayed (class V) down wood — held in both old-growth and young stands. This is a significant result because it demonstrates that down wood benefits EMF diversity across the successional gradient, not only in old-growth. Even in young plantations, retained down wood from the previous rotation provides substrate and moisture refugia that sustain EMF species which would otherwise be lost.

*Piloderma fallax* is not merely a convenient indicator species. It is a prolific mat-forming EMF that creates visible bright yellow mycelial networks through the humus layer and within decayed wood. Its mats modify soil chemistry locally, increasing phosphorus availability and acidifying the surrounding substrate. Where *Piloderma* mats occur, other EMF species are also more abundant — the mats create microhabitat conditions (stable moisture, modified pH, concentrated nutrient exchange) that function as mycorrhizal hotspots on the forest floor (PR-01).

**Amaranthus et al. (1994)** provided the complementary mechanism: down wood functions as a moisture reservoir (PR-02). In the Western Cascades, where summers are dry (July–September precipitation drops to less than 5% of annual totals), soil moisture becomes the limiting factor for mycorrhizal activity. Decaying logs — particularly large-diameter logs from old-growth trees — absorb and retain water through winter and spring rains, then release it slowly through the summer dry season. Truffle fruiting bodies were found disproportionately within one meter of decaying logs, and the probability of truffle occurrence increased with proximity to large down wood (PR-02).

The ecological implication is direct: removing down wood — through slash burning after harvest, biomass removal for energy production, or simply through harvesting forests before they produce large-diameter trees that eventually fall — removes the moisture refugia that sustain EMF activity through the Cascade summer drought. The truffles fruiting near decaying logs are not merely growing where conditions happen to be favorable. They are dependent on those conditions. The log is not a coincidental neighbor; it is infrastructure.

### 1.4 Role in Forest Regeneration and Seedling Establishment

EMF fungi are not passive beneficiaries of established forests. They are active agents in forest regeneration — and in the Cascade Range, seedling establishment is functionally impossible without them.

When a Cascade Douglas-fir or western hemlock seedling germinates on the forest floor, it arrives without mycorrhizal partners. Its roots are bare. Within its first growing season, the seedling must establish ectomycorrhizal associations to access soil phosphorus and nitrogen at rates sufficient for survival. In old-growth forests, this colonization happens quickly: the existing mycelial network — the Common Mycorrhizal Network (CMN) connecting mature trees through shared EMF fungi — extends to the new seedling and draws it into the network. The seedling receives not only mycorrhizal nutrient access but also direct carbon subsidies from connected mature trees, particularly from large "mother trees" that function as hub nodes in the CMN architecture (PR-03, GOV-01).

In clearcuts, this network is severed. The seedling germinates into soil where the CMN has been fragmented or destroyed. EMF colonization must come from residual spores in the soil or from spore dispersal by mycophagous mammals — a slower, less reliable process. The seedling establishes partnerships with early-successional EMF generalists (*Thelephora terrestris*, *Cenococcum geophilum*) rather than the late-successional specialists (*Rhizopogon vinicolor*, *Gautieria* spp.) that characterize old-growth communities. These pioneer EMF provide basic nutrient access but lack the extensive mycelial networks and carbon-sharing capacity of mature CMN connections (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

The practical consequence is measurable: seedling survival and growth rates are lower in clearcuts than in partial harvests that retain mother trees and their associated CMN (GOV-01). This is not merely an EMF diversity concern — it is a silvicultural performance issue. The argument for retaining old-growth structure is not only ecological preservation; it is practical forest regeneration. The fungi are doing work that foresters cannot replicate through fertilization, irrigation, or nursery inoculation at operational scales.

### 1.5 EMF Community Variation by Elevation Zone

Ectomycorrhizal fungal community composition in the Cascade Range is not uniform across elevations. The elevation transect defined in `00-elevation-zones.md` maps to distinct EMF assemblages shaped by temperature, precipitation, host tree composition, and soil development.

**Zone 1: Western Foothills (500–2,000 ft).** The lowest Cascade elevations support EMF communities transitioning from valley-bottom hardwood associations to the conifer-dominated assemblages that characterize the montane zone. Where Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) persists, EMF associates include *Pisolithus arhizus* and *Scleroderma* species adapted to the dry, fire-maintained savanna conditions. As Douglas-fir encroaches (due to fire suppression), the EMF community shifts toward the conifer-associated genera — *Rhizopogon*, *Russula*, *Suillus* — that dominate higher elevations. This zone represents a mycological ecotone: the border between two EMF regimes, currently in flux as fire exclusion transforms the host tree community (GOV-01, GOV-07).

**Zone 2: Montane Forest (2,000–4,500 ft).** The core Cascade EMF zone. Douglas-fir and western hemlock dominate the canopy, and the EMF community reaches its maximum diversity in old-growth stands at these elevations. Luoma's 47-species hypogeous inventory (PR-03) was conducted in this zone at the Andrews LTER. Smith's *Piloderma* study (PR-01) and Amaranthus's down wood study (PR-02) were both set in montane forests. The deep, well-developed soils of Zone 2 — with thick organic horizons accumulated over centuries of continuous litter input — provide the substrate complexity that supports the full spectrum of EMF functional groups: mat-formers (*Piloderma*, *Hysterangium*), truffle-producers (*Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*), large-fruiting epigeous species (*Russula*, *Cortinarius*, *Amanita*, *Tricholoma*), and late-successional specialists restricted to old-growth. This is where the 200+ species per stand figure applies (GOV-01).

**Zone 3: Upper Montane / Subalpine (4,500–6,500 ft).** As Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*) and mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*) replace Douglas-fir and western hemlock, the EMF community shifts correspondingly. EMF species richness declines with elevation — shorter growing seasons, lower soil temperatures, and thinner soils limit both host tree productivity and fungal community development. However, the subalpine zone supports specialized EMF species not found lower, including cold-adapted *Cortinarius* and *Inocybe* species associated with mountain hemlock and subalpine fir. Truffle diversity is lower than in Zone 2, with reduced food availability for mycophagous mammals — a factor contributing to lower northern flying squirrel densities at high elevations (GOV-01, GOV-04).

**Zone 4: Alpine / Treeline (above 6,500 ft).** Above treeline, EMF associations occur only with scattered krummholz trees — stunted, wind-shaped subalpine fir and whitebark pine at the upper limit of tree growth. EMF diversity is minimal. The dominant soil fungi at alpine elevations are saprotrophic decomposers of alpine meadow vegetation and ericoid mycorrhizal fungi associated with heather (*Cassiope*, *Phyllodoce*) and huckleberry (*Vaccinium*) species. This zone is mycologically sparse compared to the montane forests below.

**East-side contrast.** The eastern Cascades — in the rain shadow of the crest — support a fundamentally different EMF community adapted to dry-forest conditions. Ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) and western larch (*Larix occidentalis*) dominate the canopy, and their EMF associates reflect the drier, warmer, fire-frequent conditions: *Suillus* species with strong pine specificity, drought-tolerant *Cenococcum geophilum* as a persistent soil colonizer, and *Rhizopogon* species adapted to mineral soils with thin organic horizons. Truffle diversity is lower on the east side than the west, reflecting thinner organic soils and more limited moisture — but the east side supports specialized EMF species not found in the wet western forests, including *Suillus pungens* (associated with ponderosa pine) and larch-specific *Suillus* species. The east-side EMF community has co-evolved with frequent surface fire (return intervals of 5–25 years in ponderosa pine stands), and these species tolerate periodic soil heating that would eliminate the moisture-dependent EMF species of the western slopes (GOV-01, GOV-02, PR-07).

The elevation gradient in EMF diversity reinforces a key conservation point: **the highest EMF diversity — and the greatest concentration of old-growth-dependent species — occurs in Zone 2 montane forests**, which are also the zone most heavily affected by a century of industrial timber harvest. The elevation band where EMF diversity peaks is the same elevation band where logging has been most intensive (GOV-01).

### 1.6 Common Mycorrhizal Network Architecture in Cascade Forests

The Common Mycorrhizal Network (CMN) is not an abstraction. In Cascade old-growth forests, it is a physical structure with measurable properties: connectivity, extent, directionality of resource flow, and response to disturbance.

**Hub-node topology.** CMN architecture in Cascade Douglas-fir forests follows a hub-node pattern. Large old-growth trees — with extensive root systems spanning 15–30 meters laterally and penetrating 2–3 meters vertically — function as network hubs. A single hub tree may connect to 30–50 neighboring trees through shared EMF species (GOV-01). The hub tree's root network is colonized by multiple EMF species simultaneously, each extending its mycelium to different neighboring trees. The result is a multi-species, multi-host network where carbon, water, nitrogen, and chemical signals can flow between any connected pair of trees — but preferentially flow through the hub.

**Resource flow direction.** Resource flow in the CMN is not random. It follows concentration gradients (nutrients flow from high to low concentration) and is modulated by the hub tree's physiological state. During summer drought, hub trees with access to deep water redistribute moisture through the network to shallower-rooted neighbors. During autumn carbon surplus (when photosynthesis exceeds respiration demands), hub trees export carbon through the network to shaded seedlings on the forest floor. During insect attack, damaged trees send chemical alarm signals through the CMN that trigger defensive compound production in undamaged neighbors — a below-ground early warning system (GOV-01).

**Network resilience and fragility.** The hub-node architecture gives the CMN both resilience and vulnerability. Resilience: the loss of a single peripheral tree does not significantly degrade network function, because the hub tree maintains connections to all remaining neighbors. Vulnerability: the loss of a hub tree — through windthrow, harvest, or disease — collapses network connectivity for all trees that depended on that hub. In old-growth forests, where hub trees are spaced 30–50 meters apart, the loss of one hub creates a connectivity gap that adjacent hubs may partially compensate for. In clearcuts, all hubs are removed simultaneously, and the entire network collapses to isolated fragments (GOV-01, PR-04).

**Network recovery timeline.** After clearcutting, CMN recovery proceeds through stages: individual seedling-fungus pairs form first (years 1–5), then bilateral connections between neighboring seedlings develop (years 5–20), then multi-node local networks emerge (years 20–80), and finally — if the stand is left undisturbed — hub-node architecture re-establishes as dominant trees emerge and accumulate extensive root networks (years 100–200+). The 40-year logging legacy documented by PR-04 represents a snapshot at the multi-node local network stage — functional but structurally simplified compared to old-growth CMN architecture. Full hub-node recovery requires trees large enough to function as hubs, which in Cascade Douglas-fir forests means trunk diameters exceeding 1 meter — a size class that takes 150+ years to reach (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

## 2. Commercially Important Species

Four genera of edible wild mushrooms are commercially harvested in Cascade Range forests: chanterelles (*Cantharellus*), matsutake (*Tricholoma*), morels (*Morchella*), and boletes (*Boletus* and allies). Together, they support a regional harvest economy estimated at tens of millions of dollars annually, employing seasonal pickers who range from recreational foragers to full-time commercial harvesters operating under varying permit regimes across National Forest districts (GOV-01).

### 2.1 Pacific Golden Chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*)

The Pacific golden chanterelle became Oregon's official state mushroom in 1999 — the first state mushroom designation in the United States. This recognition reflects both the species' cultural significance and its ecological ubiquity in Oregon's Cascade forests.

**Taxonomy and identification.** *Cantharellus formosus* was described as a species distinct from the European *C. cibarius* in 1997 by Danell et al., based on morphological and molecular differences. The Pacific golden chanterelle produces a golden-yellow to deep egg-yolk orange fruiting body with a funnel-shaped cap (2–14 cm diameter), false gills (forked ridges rather than true blade-like gills), and a solid, fleshy stipe. It has a distinctive fruity-apricot aroma. Spore print is pale yellow to cream.

**Mycorrhizal ecology.** *C. formosus* is an ectomycorrhizal fungus forming obligate partnerships with coniferous hosts, principally Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and to a lesser extent Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) and noble fir (*Abies procera*). Its mycelium colonizes tree root tips and extends into the surrounding soil, accessing phosphorus and water in exchange for photosynthetic carbon from the host tree. Unlike many EMF species, chanterelles are not old-growth obligates — they fruit successfully in second-growth forests of 40+ years, provided that host trees and appropriate soil conditions are present. However, fruiting body size and abundance are greater in mature and old-growth stands where deeper organic soil horizons and more extensive mycelial networks support larger colonies (GOV-01).

**Fruiting phenology in the Cascade Range.** Chanterelle fruiting in the Oregon Cascades is driven by the onset of autumn rains following summer drought. The primary season extends from late September through November, with peak harvest typically in October. Fruiting requires: (1) soil moisture recharge from sustained rainfall (typically >2 inches over 1–2 weeks), (2) soil temperatures above 45°F (7°C), and (3) intact host tree canopy providing the carbon subsidy that fuels fruiting body production. A warm, wet October is a banner chanterelle year; an early hard frost or delayed rains suppress fruiting.

Elevation affects phenology: fruiting begins earlier at higher elevations (Zone 2: montane, 2,000–4,500 ft) where autumn rains arrive first and temperatures cool sooner, and progresses downslope through the season. By late November, fruiting shifts to the lowest elevations (Zone 1: western foothills) where soils remain warm longest.

**Commercial harvest.** The Pacific golden chanterelle is the most commercially harvested wild mushroom in the Pacific Northwest. Oregon alone produces an estimated 500,000+ pounds of chanterelles annually in productive years, with significant year-to-year variation driven by weather. Commercial pickers work under varying permit regimes: some National Forest districts (Willamette, Mt. Hood, Umpqua) require commercial harvest permits; others restrict harvest to personal-use quantities; enforcement is inconsistent across jurisdictions.

Harvest pressure is a management concern but not a species-level conservation threat. Chanterelle mycelium persists in soil regardless of fruiting body removal — picking mushrooms is analogous to picking fruit, not cutting the tree. However, aggressive raking of forest duff to locate buttons (immature fruiting bodies) damages the organic soil layer and can reduce subsequent fruiting. The primary ecological concern is not chanterelle depletion but the broader impacts of commercial picker activity: soil compaction from foot traffic, trail formation in sensitive areas, and disturbance to wildlife during autumn activity periods (GOV-01).

**Habitat and microsite preferences.** Within Cascade forests, chanterelles exhibit predictable microsite preferences that experienced pickers learn to read. They favor: (1) slopes with moderate drainage — not ridgetops (too dry) and not valley bottoms (too wet); (2) areas with intact moss and duff layer over mineral soil; (3) canopy gaps and edges where light penetration is slightly higher than deep shade; (4) proximity to Douglas-fir rather than hemlock — though both hosts support chanterelles, fruiting density is typically higher near Douglas-fir. On the western Cascades slopes, the best chanterelle habitat often occurs at 1,500–3,500 ft elevation on north-facing slopes with 60–80% canopy closure — warm enough for prolonged autumn fruiting, moist enough from orographic precipitation, and structurally complex enough from mixed canopy composition (GOV-01).

**Distinguishing from look-alikes.** The Pacific golden chanterelle has several look-alike species in Cascade forests. The false chanterelle (*Hygrophoropsis aurantiaca*) resembles *C. formosus* in color but has true blade-like gills (not forked ridges), thinner flesh, and grows on decaying wood rather than from soil. The jack-o'-lantern mushroom (*Omphalotus olearius*) is a toxic look-alike that grows in clusters on wood and has true gills; it is rare in the Cascades but known. Experienced pickers distinguish chanterelles by the false-gill structure (blunt, forked, decurrent ridges), solid flesh, fruity aroma, and growth habit (solitary or scattered in soil, not clustered on wood) (GOV-01).

**Cross-module relationships.** Chanterelle fruiting bodies are consumed by California red-backed voles (*Myodes californicus*), banana slugs (*Ariolimax columbianus*), black-tailed deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*), and Roosevelt elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*), connecting chanterelle ecology to the fauna survey (→ Fauna Phase 613). As an EMF partner of Cascade conifers, chanterelle mycelium contributes to the mycorrhizal network architecture that sustains Douglas-fir and western hemlock establishment (→ Flora Phase 612).

### 2.2 American Matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*)

The American matsutake is the Cascade Range's most culturally significant and commercially valuable wild mushroom on a per-pound basis. Its dense, firm flesh and distinctive spicy-cinnamon aroma make it one of the most prized edible fungi globally — particularly in Japan, where the closely related *Tricholoma matsutake* commands prices exceeding $1,000 per kilogram for prime specimens.

**Taxonomy.** The American matsutake was long considered conspecific with the Japanese *T. matsutake* but is now classified as *Tricholoma murrillianum*, a distinct North American species described by Ovrebo and Smith (1979) and confirmed by molecular phylogenetic analysis. It is distinguished from its Japanese relative by subtle morphological differences (slightly broader cap, paler coloration) and its association with North American host trees.

**Mycorrhizal ecology.** *T. murrillianum* is an ectomycorrhizal fungus forming partnerships with Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and various pines including lodgepole pine (*Pinus contorta*) and shore pine (*P. contorta* var. *contorta*). Matsutake is not old-growth dependent — it fruits in forests of various ages — but it exhibits strong substrate specificity: it favors well-drained, mineral-rich, sandy or pumice soils with thin organic horizons, often on slopes with sparse understory. In the Cascade Range, this means matsutake is particularly abundant in the pumice soils of the eastern Cascade slopes and in the well-drained volcanic soils along the Cascade crest.

A defining feature of matsutake ecology is the **shiro** — a dense, perennial mycelial mat that forms in the soil around the root zone of host trees. Shiros expand radially at 2–5 cm per year and can persist for decades, representing years of accumulated fungal growth. A productive shiro is the matsutake equivalent of a mature tree: it cannot be replaced quickly if destroyed. Soil disturbance from road building, site preparation for replanting, or heavy equipment operation in logging units destroys shiros permanently (GOV-01).

**Fruiting phenology — latitudinal gradient.** Matsutake fruiting in the Cascade Range follows a striking latitudinal pattern, tracking the southward progression of autumn cooling:

| Latitude | Region | Typical Fruiting Season | Peak |
|----------|--------|------------------------|------|
| 49–54°N | Northern British Columbia | August | Mid-August |
| 47–49°N | Washington Cascades | Late August – September | September |
| 44–47°N | Oregon Cascades | September – October | Late September – early October |
| 42–44°N | Siskiyou Mountains / Southern Oregon | October – November | Late October – November |

This phenology gradient means that a commercial matsutake picker can follow the season southward from BC through Washington and Oregon over a 3–4 month period, and many do — the matsutake harvest supports a migratory workforce that moves with the fruiting front (GOV-01).

**Cultural and economic significance.** The American matsutake is commercially harvested primarily for export to Japanese markets, where it substitutes for the increasingly scarce Japanese *T. matsutake*. Wholesale prices for premium American matsutake (grade 1: cap closed, firm, aromatic) range from $20–80/lb depending on supply and Japanese market demand. In productive years, the Oregon Cascades alone produce hundreds of thousands of pounds. The harvest supports a diverse picking workforce including Southeast Asian immigrant communities (particularly Lao and Cambodian), Indigenous pickers, and Anglo-American recreational and commercial foragers (GOV-01, CON-01).

**Conservation status.** Federal status: none. State status OR/WA: none. Trend: stable. The primary management concern is sustainable harvest — commercial pickers who excavate immature buttons before cap expansion and spore dispersal reduce reproductive output. Some National Forest districts have implemented minimum size regulations and restricted harvest areas. Shiro destruction from forest management activities is a greater long-term threat than overharvesting of fruiting bodies (GOV-01).

**Identification and grading.** Matsutake grading is critical to commercial value and follows a system derived from Japanese market standards:

| Grade | Description | Typical Price ($/lb) | Characteristics |
|-------|------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Grade 1 (tsubomi) | Button, cap fully closed | $40–80 | Veil intact, firm, maximum aroma |
| Grade 2 (hirakake) | Cap partially open | $20–40 | Veil breaking, good aroma, firm flesh |
| Grade 3 (hiraki) | Cap fully open | $8–20 | Gills exposed, spores dispersing, softer |
| Wormy/damaged | Insect damage | $2–5 | Larvae tunnels, partial decay |

The price differential between grades incentivizes harvesting buttons (Grade 1) before the cap opens and spores disperse. This creates tension between commercial value and reproductive ecology: the most valuable specimens are those harvested before they have reproduced. Sustainable matsutake management requires either accepting lower per-pound value (harvesting Grade 2–3) or ensuring that enough Grade 1 buttons are left unharvested to maintain spore production (GOV-01).

**Matsutake camps.** During the autumn fruiting season, commercial matsutake pickers establish temporary camps in Cascade Range National Forests near productive picking areas. These camps — ranging from single tents to semi-permanent encampments of 20–50 people — have become a management issue for Forest Service districts. Camp impacts include fire risk, sanitation, waste accumulation, and social conflicts between picker groups competing for productive shiros. Some National Forest districts have designated camping areas and imposed time limits; others have struggled to manage the seasonal influx. The matsutake camp phenomenon illustrates how wild mushroom ecology creates human social structures — communities of practice that form, migrate, and dissolve in rhythm with fungal fruiting phenology (CON-01).

### 2.3 Morels (*Morchella* spp.)

Morels occupy a unique ecological niche in the Cascade Range: they are the fire-following fungi. While other commercially important species — chanterelle, matsutake, bolete — fruit in intact forest, morels fruit most prolifically in the spring following wildfire. This fire-associated phenology makes the morel harvest a direct ecological and economic response to the Cascade Range's fire regime.

**Taxonomy.** Cascade Range morels include multiple species in the genus *Morchella*, with taxonomy still being resolved through molecular phylogenetics. Key species include *Morchella sextelata* (the black morel, common in post-fire sites), *Morchella frustrata*, and *Morchella snyderi* (a Douglas-fir associated "natural" morel). The classic morel identification — a hollow, honeycomb-capped mushroom with pitted and ridged surface — applies across species, though field identification to species level is challenging.

**Ecology — fire association.** The Cascade morel story is inseparable from fire. In the spring following a forest fire — typically April through June, as snowmelt recedes upslope — burned areas can produce extraordinary flushes of black morels (*M. sextelata* and allies). The mechanism is not fully resolved, but the leading hypothesis involves fire's effects on soil chemistry and mycorrhizal networks: fire kills surface organic material, releases nutrients in ash, eliminates competing fungi, and disrupts existing mycorrhizal associations. For morel species with saprobic or semi-saprobic nutritional strategies, this creates a window of reduced competition and abundant substrate. For mycorrhizal morel species, the death of host trees may trigger a survival response — massive spore production before the fungal network dies (GOV-01).

The commercial significance is direct: after large Cascade Range wildfires, commercial morel permits are issued by affected National Forest districts, and thousands of pickers converge on burn areas. The 2020 Labor Day fires in the Oregon Cascades, which burned over 400,000 acres, produced a substantial morel harvest in spring 2021 across the Santiam, McKenzie, and North Umpqua drainages (GOV-01).

**Fruiting phenology.** Unlike the autumn-fruiting chanterelle and matsutake, morels are spring mushrooms:
- **Post-fire morels:** April–June following autumn/summer fires, tracking snowmelt up the elevation gradient. Peak at 2,000–4,500 ft elevation in May.
- **Natural (non-fire) morels:** April–May in unburned forests, associated with Douglas-fir, cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), and disturbed riparian areas. Production is lower and less predictable than post-fire flushes.
- **Burn-site productivity declines** after 1–2 years post-fire. The morel flush is an ephemeral event — a pulse of reproduction tied to the ecological disruption of fire, not a sustainable annual harvest.

**Commercial harvest.** Morels command wholesale prices of $15–40/lb fresh, with significant variation by species, condition, and market timing. The post-fire harvest is the primary commercial opportunity; non-fire morel production is too diffuse and unpredictable for organized commercial picking. Dried morels retain value well and are a significant value-added product — dried morels can command $100–200/lb retail.

**Identification safety.** Morels are among the more safely identified wild mushrooms due to their distinctive honeycomb cap and hollow interior. However, the false morel (*Gyromitra esculenta* and allies) — a brain-like, irregularly lobed mushroom that fruits in similar habitats and seasons — contains the toxin gyromitrin and can cause serious poisoning. False morels are found in Cascade Range forests and are sometimes confused with true morels by inexperienced foragers. The distinguishing characteristic is internal structure: true morels are completely hollow from cap to stem base; false morels have solid, chambered interior tissue. This identification difference has practical significance: in the rush of a productive post-fire morel season, when large volumes of mushrooms are being picked quickly, misidentification risk increases (GOV-01).

**Post-fire landscape ecology.** The spatial distribution of morel fruiting within a burn perimeter is not uniform. Morels fruit most abundantly in zones of moderate burn severity — where surface litter and understory were consumed but the soil was not sterilized to depth. The mosaic of burn severities within a typical Cascade wildfire creates a corresponding mosaic of morel productivity: hot spots of abundant fruiting interspersed with areas of no production (where fire was too severe or too light). Experienced morel pickers read the burn mosaic — identifying the moderate-severity zones by bark char height, remaining needle color on snags, and soil ash depth — and concentrate harvest effort in these zones. This ecological reading of fire severity mirrors the scientific assessment that moderate-severity fire creates the optimal conditions for both morel fruiting and EMF community recovery (GOV-01, GOV-02).

**Cross-module relationships.** The morel's fire association connects directly to the threats module (→ Threats Phase 616). As fire regime changes under climate warming increase both the frequency and severity of Cascade Range wildfires, the post-fire morel resource is affected in complex ways: more fires mean more burn sites and more potential morel habitat, but higher-severity fires that sterilize soil to greater depth may reduce morel production compared to moderate-severity burns that leave intact soil biology below the surface (GOV-01, GOV-02).

### 2.4 Boletes (*Boletus*, *Suillus*, *Leccinum* spp.)

Boletes — the "pore mushrooms" characterized by a sponge-like pore surface beneath the cap rather than gills — are the fourth major group of commercially harvested edible fungi in the Cascade Range. The term encompasses multiple genera with distinct ecologies.

**Key Cascade Range genera:**

- ***Boletus edulis*** **complex (king bolete, porcini):** The most commercially valuable bolete, forming ectomycorrhizal associations with spruce (*Picea*), Douglas-fir, and hemlock. Fruits in late summer through autumn (August–October) in Cascade montane forests, often at edges of trails and forest openings. Dense, nutty-flavored flesh commands $15–30/lb fresh.

- ***Suillus*** **spp. (slippery jacks):** A large genus of EMF boletes with strong host specificity, particularly to pine (*Pinus*) and larch (*Larix*). *Suillus lakei* associates with Douglas-fir; *Suillus brevipes* with lodgepole pine. Suillus species are more abundant than *Boletus* in the Cascade Range and are widely harvested for personal use, though their slimy cap surface limits commercial demand.

- ***Leccinum*** **spp. (scaber stalks):** EMF associates of birch (*Betula*), aspen (*Populus*), and conifers. *Leccinum manzanitae* and *Leccinum scabrum* are the common Cascade species. Harvested recreationally but not a major commercial species.

**Ecological role.** All Cascade boletes are ectomycorrhizal, contributing to the EMF network architecture described in Section 1. Their large fruiting bodies (king boletes can exceed 2 kg) represent substantial nutrient and carbon transfers from below-ground mycelial networks to above-ground food webs. Bolete fruiting bodies are consumed by deer, elk, squirrels, and invertebrates, linking bolete ecology to the fauna module (→ Fauna Phase 613). Bolete mycelium contributes to the aggregate EMF species richness — over 200 species per old-growth stand — that sustains Cascade forest nutrient cycling (GOV-01).

### 2.5 Harvest Economy Summary

The combined wild mushroom harvest from Cascade Range National Forests represents a significant component of the regional rural economy. The following table summarizes the four major commercial species by economic and ecological metrics:

| Species | Peak Season | Elevation Zone | Price Range ($/lb fresh) | Old-Growth Dependent | Primary Markets |
|---------|------------|---------------|-------------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Chanterelle (*C. formosus*) | Oct–Nov | Zones 1–2 | $5–15 wholesale | No (enhanced by old-growth) | Domestic restaurants, farmers markets |
| Matsutake (*T. murrillianum*) | Aug–Nov (latitudinal) | Zone 2 crest/east slope | $20–80 wholesale | No (shiro-dependent) | Japanese export, domestic specialty |
| Morel (*Morchella* spp.) | Apr–Jun | Zone 2 burn sites | $15–40 wholesale | No (fire-dependent) | Domestic restaurants, dried specialty |
| King Bolete (*B. edulis* complex) | Aug–Oct | Zone 2 | $15–30 wholesale | No (enhanced by old-growth) | Domestic restaurants, dried specialty |

Several features of this economy merit attention:

**Seasonal employment.** Wild mushroom harvest provides seasonal income to thousands of pickers in Oregon and Washington, with the harvest workforce peaking in October–November during the chanterelle and matsutake overlap. For some rural communities in the Cascades foothills — particularly in areas where timber employment has declined — mushroom harvest has partially replaced logging as a forest-dependent income source. The economic transition from extracting wood to harvesting fungi represents a shift from capital-intensive, infrastructure-heavy, ecosystem-destructive resource extraction to low-impact, renewable harvesting that is compatible with — and in fact dependent on — intact forest ecosystems (GOV-01, CON-01).

**Cultural diversity.** The Cascade mushroom harvest workforce is remarkably diverse. Southeast Asian immigrant communities — particularly Lao, Cambodian, and Vietnamese — developed commercial picking operations in the Oregon Cascades beginning in the 1980s, building on traditional foraging knowledge from their home countries. Indigenous pickers from regional tribes maintain long-standing relationships with forest foods. Anglo-American recreational foragers contribute to a growing culinary culture around wild mushrooms. This cultural diversity in the harvest workforce is itself a product of the forest's ecological productivity — a diverse ecosystem supporting diverse human communities (CON-01).

**Non-timber forest products (NTFPs).** Wild mushrooms are the highest-value non-timber forest product in the Cascade Range, and their economic contribution strengthens the case for forest management approaches that maintain mushroom habitat — meaning older forests, intact canopy, retained down wood, and minimal soil disturbance. The economic argument for mushroom-compatible forestry aligns with the ecological argument for old-growth and mature forest retention, creating a rare convergence of economic and conservation interests (GOV-01).

## 3. Agarikon — Old-Growth Obligate Profile

### 3.1 Taxonomy and Identification

Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*) is the sole extant species in its genus — a taxonomic singularity that reflects both its antiquity and its ecological specialization. Formerly classified as *Fomitopsis officinalis*, it was separated into the monotypic genus *Laricifomes* based on molecular phylogenetic analysis that confirmed its distinct evolutionary lineage within the order Polyporales.

The fruiting body is a perennial polypore conk — a woody, shelf-like or pendant structure that grows from the trunk of its host tree. Unlike annual mushrooms that emerge, sporulate, and decay within days or weeks, an agarikon conk grows incrementally for years, often decades, adding a new pore layer each growing season. Mature specimens can reach 30–50 cm in length, 20–30 cm in width, and weigh several kilograms. The exterior is chalky white to gray, often cracked and weathered; the interior is fibrous and bitter. The shape is typically elongated-conical, hanging vertically from the host trunk like a beehive — a silhouette distinctive enough to identify at distance in old-growth stands.

### 3.2 Host Trees and Habitat Requirements

Agarikon inhabits exclusively large old-growth conifers. Its primary hosts are:

- **Western larch (*Larix occidentalis*):** The historical primary host and the source of the genus name *Laricifomes*. Larch is the only deciduous conifer in the Cascade Range, and large old-growth larch — particularly in the eastern Cascades and transitional dry forests — has been the classic agarikon habitat. Old-growth larch trees with deeply furrowed, thick bark provide both the physical substrate for conk attachment and the heartwood decay conditions that sustain the fungus.

- **Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*):** The dominant host in the western Cascades, where larch is absent. Old-growth Douglas-fir with trunk diameters exceeding 1 meter and deeply fissured bark supports agarikon. The fungus colonizes heartwood through wounds, broken branch stubs, or natural bark fissures.

- **Other conifers:** Occasionally found on old-growth noble fir (*Abies procera*), grand fir (*Abies grandis*), and ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*), but these associations are rare and secondary.

The critical habitat requirement is old-growth tree structure. Agarikon cannot colonize young plantation trees for three structural reasons:

1. **Bark architecture.** The deeply furrowed, thick bark of old-growth conifers provides protected microsites for initial hyphal establishment and moisture retention. Young trees with smooth, thin bark lack these attachment points and desiccate too rapidly at the bark surface for hyphal colonization.

2. **Heartwood access.** Agarikon is a heartwood decay fungus — it requires access to the dead, lignin-rich core of a large tree. Young trees have thin sapwood and minimal heartwood development. The trunk diameter and internal structure needed to sustain an agarikon colony take 150–200+ years to develop.

3. **Longevity requirement.** Because agarikon conks grow for decades on a single host, the host tree must persist long enough for the fungus to complete its reproductive cycle. Trees harvested on 40–60 year rotations are removed before an agarikon colony could establish, mature, and sporulate effectively.

### 3.3 Ecological Roles — Parasite and Saprotroph

Agarikon exhibits a dual nutritional strategy that spans its host tree's lifetime:

**Parasitic phase.** On living host trees, agarikon functions as a brown rot fungus, enzymatically degrading cellulose in the heartwood while leaving the lignin matrix largely intact. This creates the characteristic cubical brown rot pattern — blocky, crumbly, brown-colored wood that breaks apart along geometric fracture planes. The parasitic degradation of heartwood weakens the structural core of the host tree, eventually contributing to tree failure in windstorms or creating cavity habitat used by woodpeckers, owls, and small mammals. In this phase, agarikon is a slow-acting parasite — it does not kill its host directly, but it degrades structural integrity over decades.

**Saprotrophic phase.** After the host tree dies — from windthrow, fire, or the cumulative effects of heartwood decay — agarikon continues to decompose the dead wood. The conk persists on the standing dead snag or fallen trunk, shifting from parasitic to saprotrophic nutrition. This phase can continue for years after host death, with the conk producing spores from its perennial pore surface each growing season.

This dual life history means that agarikon connects two ecological functions: it is both a creator of wildlife habitat in living trees (through heartwood excavation that produces cavities) and a decomposer that recycles nutrients from dead wood back into the forest soil. Both functions are mediated by old-growth forest structure — only large, old trees provide the substrate for either role (GOV-01, CON-01).

### 3.4 Conservation Status and Range Contraction

Agarikon is one of the most conservation-imperiled macrofungi in the Northern Hemisphere:

- **Federal status (U.S.):** Species of concern — recognized as imperiled by multiple agencies but not formally listed under the Endangered Species Act.
- **State status (Washington):** Candidate species.
- **State status (Oregon):** Sensitive species.
- **Trend:** Declining.

The species' global range has contracted dramatically. Agarikon was once found across the Northern Hemisphere's temperate forests — from Scandinavia through Russia to the Pacific coast of North America. It has been **extirpated from most of Europe** due to centuries of old-growth logging. In North America, it persists in remnant old-growth stands in the Pacific Northwest, with the Cascade Range representing one of its last significant habitats (GOV-01, CON-01).

The conservation logic is straightforward and irreversible:

1. Agarikon requires old-growth trees to persist.
2. Old-growth trees take 150–200+ years to develop the structural characteristics (bark depth, heartwood volume, trunk diameter) that agarikon needs.
3. Managed second-growth forests harvested on 40–60 year rotations never reach this structural stage.
4. Therefore, **agarikon cannot persist in managed forest landscapes.** Its survival is entirely contingent on the preservation of existing old-growth stands.

No captive propagation program exists. No artificial substrate replicates old-growth tree structure. No reintroduction technique has been developed. Where agarikon disappears, it does not return on human timescales. Its presence in a Cascade forest stand is diagnostic confirmation that the stand has not been logged — and its absence from a stand is often the quietest indicator that old-growth structure has been irrecoverably simplified (CON-01).

### 3.5 Historical Medicinal Use

Agarikon has a documented history of medicinal use spanning at least two millennia:

- **Indigenous peoples** of the Pacific Northwest used agarikon preparations to treat respiratory infections, coughs, and wounds, as recorded in ethnobotanical surveys. This Indigenous knowledge predates European contact and represents centuries of empirical pharmacological observation.
- **Classical Mediterranean** sources document *Agaricum* (the classical name) as a medicinal preparation used by Greek and Roman physicians. Dioscorides described its use in treating consumption (tuberculosis-like symptoms).
- **Modern research** has identified bioactive compounds in agarikon extracts with antiviral properties. Mycologist Paul Stamets has conducted preliminary screening showing activity against influenza, poxviruses, and other viral pathogens. This work is noted as ongoing research — it has produced screening data and patent applications, not peer-reviewed clinical trials. The medicinal potential is genuine and warrants further study, but it remains investigational (CON-01).

The medicinal dimension adds urgency to conservation: a species that may harbor pharmaceutically significant compounds is disappearing along with its old-growth habitat before its biochemistry has been fully characterized. This is not speculative — it is the documented pattern of tropical rainforest pharmacology applied to temperate old-growth forests.

### 3.6 Agarikon as Indicator Species

Beyond its intrinsic conservation value, agarikon functions as a biological indicator whose presence or absence communicates information about forest condition that no other single measurement provides.

**Presence indicates:** The stand has not been clearcut or heavily logged within the past 200+ years. Old-growth structural characteristics — large trunk diameter, deep bark fissures, heartwood development, canopy continuity — are intact. The stand has maintained the stable, humid microclimate conditions that agarikon requires. Host trees have been present and undisturbed long enough for the slow-growing fungus to colonize, establish, and produce perennial conks.

**Absence does not necessarily indicate:** Logging. Agarikon is naturally rare even in old-growth — it does not colonize every suitable host tree, and its spore dispersal range is limited. Absence from a particular old-growth stand may reflect natural rarity rather than disturbance history. However, absence from a region where agarikon was historically documented — combined with evidence of past logging — is diagnostic of habitat degradation.

**Survey utility:** Agarikon conks are visible from ground level and persist for years, making them practical survey indicators that do not require soil sampling, molecular analysis, or specialized equipment. A trained surveyor walking through an old-growth stand can detect agarikon conks on host tree trunks and use their presence to confirm old-growth integrity. This survey simplicity — combined with the species' strict old-growth dependency — makes agarikon one of the most cost-effective biological indicators available for Cascade Range forest assessment (GOV-01, CON-01).

**Cascade Range distribution.** Within the Cascade Range, agarikon is most likely to be found in:
- Western Cascades old-growth Douglas-fir stands at Zone 2 elevations (2,000–4,500 ft)
- Eastern Cascades old-growth western larch stands in the transitional dry forest zone
- Wilderness areas and late-successional reserves within National Forest boundaries
- North Cascades National Park and associated wilderness where old-growth has never been harvested

The species is effectively absent from managed timber stands, second-growth forests, and areas within the timber harvest base regardless of current forest age. If a Cascade forest was clearcut at any point in the past 200 years, agarikon will not be present (CON-01).

## 4. Logging Legacy Effects on Soil Fungal Communities

### 4.1 The 2023 Logging Legacy Study

The most consequential recent addition to Cascade Range fungal ecology is the 2023 study published in *Frontiers in Forests and Global Change* documenting **detectable signatures in soil fungal communities four decades after logging ceased** in Western Cascades forests (PR-04).

This study compared soil fungal community composition — using modern environmental DNA (eDNA) metabarcoding techniques — across three treatment histories in the Western Cascades of Oregon:

1. **Old-growth stands:** Never logged. Continuous forest cover for 200+ years.
2. **Selectively logged stands:** Partial harvest 40+ years prior. Some canopy trees retained.
3. **Clearcut stands:** Complete harvest 40+ years prior. Full canopy removal followed by replanting or natural regeneration.

The central finding: **soil fungal communities in clearcut and selectively logged stands remained compositionally distinct from old-growth communities four decades after logging ceased** (PR-04). The forests had regrown. The canopy had closed. The stands looked, from above, like functional forests. But the soil fungi had not recovered. The community composition — which species were present, their relative abundances, and their functional group assignments — carried a detectable logging signature that persisted across human generations.

### 4.2 Mechanisms of Persistent Community Alteration

The logging legacy effect is not a single mechanism but a cascade of interacting disturbances:

**Soil disruption.** Logging operations — falling, yarding, road construction — compact and disturb forest soil, destroying mycelial networks in the top 10–20 cm where most EMF activity occurs. Heavy equipment compresses soil pores, reducing the gas exchange and water infiltration that EMF hyphae require. This physical disruption kills active mycelium directly and degrades habitat quality for recolonization.

**Canopy removal.** Clearcutting eliminates the photosynthetic carbon source that sustains ectomycorrhizal fungi. EMF receive 10–20% of their host tree's fixed carbon (GOV-01). When the host trees are removed, this carbon subsidy ceases immediately. EMF species with limited carbon reserves — late-successional specialists in *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, and some *Rhizopogon* — die within months to years. Early-successional EMF generalists (*Thelephora*, *Cenococcum*) persist in soil as resistant propagules and recolonize seedling roots, but they represent a simplified community with reduced functional diversity.

**Down wood removal.** Slash burning or biomass removal eliminates the moisture refugia (Section 1.3) that sustain EMF activity through summer drought. Decaying logs from the previous stand are consumed by fire or removed, eliminating the substrate that supports *Piloderma fallax* and the moisture reservoirs that sustain truffle fruiting near log edges (PR-01, PR-02).

**Network fragmentation.** The Common Mycorrhizal Network that connected old-growth trees is severed. CMN architecture requires hub nodes — large trees with extensive root systems connected to dozens of neighbors through shared EMF. Clearcutting removes all hub nodes simultaneously, collapsing the network into disconnected fragments. Regenerating seedlings form new mycorrhizal partnerships, but these are simple, bilateral connections — individual seedling-to-fungus pairs, not the complex multi-node network that characterizes old-growth (GOV-01).

**Altered soil chemistry.** Slash burning changes soil pH, nutrient availability, and organic matter content. Ash deposition raises pH temporarily, favoring bacterial decomposers over fungi. The loss of continuous litter input from the previous canopy stops the organic horizon accumulation that took centuries to build. These chemical changes favor a different fungal community — one adapted to disturbed, mineral-rich, organic-poor conditions — rather than the deep-organic, acidic conditions that support old-growth EMF assemblages (PR-04).

### 4.3 Clearcut vs. Selectively Logged vs. Old-Growth

The 2023 study (PR-04) revealed a gradient of impact:

**Old-growth stands** supported the most diverse and functionally complex soil fungal communities, dominated by late-successional EMF species and characterized by high proportions of truffle-producing hypogeous fungi. These communities represent the reference condition — the endpoint of undisturbed fungal succession that takes centuries to develop.

**Selectively logged stands** (40+ years post-harvest) showed intermediate community composition. Some old-growth EMF species persisted, particularly in microsites near retained trees where mycelial networks and down wood were preserved. However, the overall community was shifted toward early- and mid-successional species compared to old-growth. The retained trees functioned as partial CMN anchors, maintaining some network connectivity that facilitated EMF persistence — but the network was degraded, with fewer hub nodes and reduced connectivity.

**Clearcut stands** (40+ years post-harvest) showed the greatest divergence from old-growth. Despite four decades of forest regrowth, the soil fungal community remained dominated by early- and mid-successional species. Late-successional EMF specialists — the *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, and *Rhizopogon vinicolor* species that define old-growth communities — were absent or present at trace abundances only. The community had not converged toward old-growth composition in 40 years and showed no trajectory of convergence (PR-04).

The implication is stark: **the logging legacy is not a temporary disturbance that time heals.** It is a persistent alteration of soil community structure that outlasts the visible recovery of the forest. A 40-year-old clearcut regrowth forest that looks like a forest from the air is not functioning like a forest below ground. Its soil fungal community remembers what happened to it — and that memory has consequences.

### 4.4 Implications for Forest Regeneration

The logging legacy connects directly to the forest regeneration function described in Section 1.4. Seedlings establishing in clearcut regrowth forests face a double disadvantage:

1. **Reduced EMF partner availability.** The late-successional EMF species that provide the most effective mycorrhizal services — extensive mycelial networks, efficient nutrient scavenging, drought resistance — are absent or rare. Seedlings establish partnerships with generalist EMF species that provide basic function but cannot replicate the performance of old-growth specialists.

2. **No CMN support.** Without large hub trees and their associated mycelial networks, seedlings receive no carbon subsidies, no water sharing during drought, and no chemical defense signaling from the below-ground network. Each seedling is nutritionally isolated — a condition that does not occur on old-growth forest floors where the CMN integrates seedlings into the forest's resource-sharing system within their first growing season.

These two deficits — reduced EMF diversity and absent CMN support — translate into measurable differences in seedling survival and growth. In a landscape where Douglas-fir and western hemlock establishment depends on mycorrhizal partnerships, the quality of those partnerships matters. The logging legacy degrades partnership quality for generations after the original harvest (PR-04, GOV-01).

### 4.5 Connection to Douglas-fir/Hemlock Establishment

The practical forestry implication is direct: **Cascade Range forest regeneration is constrained by soil fungal community condition**, not only by seed availability, light, or moisture.

Douglas-fir seedlings planted in clearcut units form mycorrhizal partnerships with whatever EMF species are present in the soil — typically early-successional generalists. These partnerships support survival and growth, but at rates below what the same seedling would achieve with old-growth EMF specialists connected to an intact CMN. The result is slower height growth, higher mortality during summer drought, and reduced resilience to the periodic stress events (drought years, insect outbreaks, frost events) that Douglas-fir seedlings must survive during their first decade.

Western hemlock, the climax species of Cascade forests below 3,000 ft, is even more dependent on mycorrhizal networks for establishment. Hemlock seedlings are extremely shade-tolerant — they germinate and survive under closed canopy where light levels are 1–5% of full sun. This shade tolerance is possible because hemlock seedlings receive carbon subsidies from connected mature trees via the CMN. In clearcuts or in regrowth forests where the CMN has been degraded by logging legacy effects, hemlock establishment is impaired. The species that is supposed to succeed Douglas-fir in the natural successional sequence cannot effectively colonize forests where the below-ground network that supports its shade-adapted establishment strategy has been disrupted (GOV-01, PR-04).

This is the deepest implication of the logging legacy: it does not merely reduce fungal species counts. It alters the successional trajectory of the forest itself. A clearcut stand that regenerates with impoverished EMF communities may remain stuck in an arrested successional state — dominated by Douglas-fir and early-successional species — because the fungal infrastructure required for hemlock establishment is degraded. The forest above ground is waiting for an below-ground ecosystem that may take 150–200 years to recover (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576).

### 4.6 Management Implications

The logging legacy research (PR-04) has direct implications for Cascade Range forest management:

**Selective harvest outperforms clearcutting for EMF conservation.** The gradient from clearcut (most divergent from old-growth) to selectively logged (intermediate) to old-growth (reference) demonstrates that retaining canopy trees during harvest preserves partial CMN connectivity and sustains some late-successional EMF species. Selective harvest is not a substitute for old-growth protection — the selectively logged stands still diverged from old-growth after 40 years — but it produces measurably better outcomes for soil fungal communities than clearcutting (PR-04).

**Down wood retention is a low-cost, high-impact practice.** The Smith (PR-01) and Amaranthus (PR-02) studies demonstrate that retaining down wood — particularly large-diameter, heavily decayed logs — provides EMF refugia in managed stands. This is a silvicultural recommendation that costs nothing: instead of burning slash or removing biomass, leave it. The down wood will sustain *Piloderma* mats, provide truffle moisture reservoirs, and maintain EMF diversity through the post-harvest transitional period (PR-01, PR-02, GOV-01).

**Green tree retention provides CMN anchors.** Retaining scattered old-growth trees ("legacy trees") during harvest operations preserves CMN hub nodes that maintain network connectivity for regenerating seedlings. Even a few retained hub trees per hectare can dramatically improve seedling mycorrhizal colonization and survival compared to complete clearcutting. The Northwest Forest Plan's retention requirements — while designed primarily for wildlife habitat — have the secondary benefit of preserving CMN infrastructure in harvest units (GOV-01).

**Rotation length matters.** The 150–200 year EMF recovery timeline (GOV-01: PNW-GTR-576) means that standard 40–60 year rotation lengths harvest forests before their soil fungal communities have recovered from the previous harvest. Each successive rotation starts from a more impoverished EMF baseline. Extending rotation lengths — or transitioning to uneven-age management with continuous canopy cover — allows EMF communities more time to develop toward old-growth composition. The economic case for longer rotations is strengthened by the mushroom harvest economy (Section 2.5), which depends on mature and old-growth forest conditions (GOV-01).

**Monitoring soil fungal communities should supplement above-ground surveys.** The central message of the logging legacy research is that above-ground forest appearance does not predict below-ground fungal community condition. A closed-canopy 40-year-old plantation may appear "recovered" while its soil fungi remain compositionally impoverished. Effective forest management requires monitoring what cannot be seen from above ground — and modern eDNA metabarcoding techniques (the methodology used in PR-04) make soil fungal community assessment increasingly practical and affordable (PR-04).

## 5. Cross-Module Relationships

The fungi survey connects to every other module in the Cascade Range Biodiversity research. Fungi are the connective tissue — the below-ground network that links trees to trees, trees to mammals, mammals to raptors, fire to regeneration, and logging history to future forest composition.

### 5.1 Flying Squirrel Truffle Dispersal (→ Fauna Phase 613)

The truffle–mammal–raptor cascade is the most consequential cross-module relationship mediated by Cascade Range fungi.

**The chain:** Old-growth EMF communities produce hypogeous (below-ground) truffle fruiting bodies → Northern flying squirrels (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) and California red-backed voles (*Myodes californicus*) locate truffles by scent and excavate them → Truffle spores pass through mammalian digestive tracts intact and are deposited in fecal pellets at locations distant from the parent mycelium → Spore dispersal inoculates new microsites with EMF, expanding the mycorrhizal network → Flying squirrels and red-backed voles, sustained by truffle nutrition, serve as primary prey for the northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*).

**Quantitative context:** Truffles constitute 80–90% of the northern flying squirrel's diet in old-growth Cascade forests. Truffle diversity is 4x higher in old-growth than in young plantations (GOV-01). When old-growth is logged, truffle diversity collapses, flying squirrel populations decline due to food scarcity, and spotted owl reproductive success degrades due to reduced prey density. The owl's endangered status is mediated, in significant part, through fungal diversity.

**Dispersal mechanism:** Mycophagous mammals are the primary dispersal agents for hypogeous EMF species. Truffles, unlike epigeous mushrooms, cannot disperse spores via wind — they fruit below ground. Their evolutionary strategy is aromatic: they produce volatile organic compounds that attract mammals, which excavate, consume, and transport the spores. This mutualistic dispersal loop means that mammal population health directly affects EMF network expansion and recovery. Where flying squirrel populations decline — due to habitat loss, predation, or food scarcity — truffle spore dispersal declines, limiting EMF colonization of new sites and slowing forest regeneration.

### 5.2 Tree-Fungi Mycorrhizal Mutualism (→ Flora Phase 612)

Every dominant canopy conifer in the Cascade Range — Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Pacific silver fir, noble fir, mountain hemlock, Sitka spruce — is an EMF-dependent species. The tree–fungi mutualism is not an optional ecological interaction; it is a structural dependency without which these species cannot sustain their characteristic biomass densities.

**Carbon for nutrients:** Trees provide 10–20% of fixed carbon to EMF partners (GOV-01). EMF provide soil phosphorus, nitrogen, water, and trace elements to trees. This exchange occurs at the Hartig net — the intercellular interface between fungal hyphae and tree root cortical cells.

**Network architecture:** Large old-growth trees function as hub nodes in the Common Mycorrhizal Network, connecting to dozens of neighboring trees through shared EMF and channeling carbon, water, and defense signals across the network. This hub-node architecture is documented in the Flora Phase 612 through the lens of tree structure; here in the Fungi Phase 614, it is documented through the lens of the EMF species that create and maintain those connections.

**Regeneration dependency:** Seedling establishment on the Cascade forest floor depends on rapid EMF colonization (Section 1.4). The Flora module documents the trees; the Fungi module documents the below-ground infrastructure that makes those trees possible.

### 5.3 Fire-Fungi Regeneration Dynamics (→ Threats Phase 616)

Fire is both destroyer and creator in Cascade Range fungal ecology.

**Destruction:** High-severity fire sterilizes the upper soil horizons, killing active EMF mycelium and destroying the organic horizon that supports fungal communities. In severely burned areas, EMF recolonization must proceed from deep soil propagules, wind-dispersed spores, or animal-dispersed truffle spores from unburned refugia. Recovery is slow — measured in decades, not years.

**Creation:** Moderate-severity fire creates the conditions for morel fruiting (Section 2.3), opens canopy gaps that favor early-successional EMF communities, and produces the charred and partially decomposed wood that becomes substrate for specialized saprotrophic fungi. The post-fire fungal community is compositionally distinct from pre-fire communities — dominated by fire-adapted species including *Morchella*, *Pyronema*, and *Anthracobia* — and represents the initial stage of a successional sequence that, if undisturbed, will progress toward old-growth EMF communities over centuries.

**Climate change interaction:** As Cascade Range fire regimes shift under climate warming toward larger, more frequent, and more severe fires (→ Threats Phase 616), the balance between fire's destructive and creative effects on fungal communities may tip. More frequent reburns prevent EMF community recovery between fire events. Higher severity fires sterilize soil to greater depth, eliminating the deep propagule bank that anchors recovery. The logging legacy effect (Section 4) and the fire regime shift compound each other: stands already impoverished by logging are more vulnerable to fire, and fire in impoverished stands delays EMF recovery further.

**Historical fire regime and EMF adaptation.** Cascade Range forests evolved with fire. The pre-European fire regime varied by elevation and aspect: low-frequency, high-severity stand-replacing fires in the wet Western Cascades (return intervals of 200–500 years in hemlock/fir forests), and high-frequency, low-severity surface fires in the dry Eastern Cascades (return intervals of 5–25 years in ponderosa pine forests) (GOV-02, PR-07). EMF communities in each zone evolved with their respective fire regimes. Western Cascades EMF species are adapted to rare but catastrophic disturbance — they persist through deep soil propagules and recover slowly over centuries between fire events. Eastern Cascades EMF species are adapted to frequent surface fire — they tolerate periodic soil heating and canopy thinning, and their host trees (ponderosa pine, western larch) are fire-resistant species.

**Fire exclusion paradox.** A century of fire suppression has altered both fire regimes and, consequently, the EMF communities they shaped. In the Eastern Cascades, fire exclusion has allowed shade-tolerant species (grand fir, Douglas-fir) to encroach into formerly open ponderosa pine stands. These encroaching trees bring their own EMF associates, shifting the below-ground community away from the fire-adapted, drought-tolerant EMF species associated with ponderosa pine. The irony is that fire exclusion — intended to protect forests — has inadvertently degraded the fire-adapted EMF communities that sustained the original forest type. When the inevitable stand-replacing fire occurs in these unnaturally dense stands (as it increasingly does), it burns with severity that exceeds the EMF community's evolved tolerance, causing deeper and more persistent soil fungal community disruption than the historical low-severity fires that the system was adapted to handle (GOV-02, PR-07).

**Post-fire EMF recovery trajectory.** After moderate-severity fire in Cascade forests, EMF recovery follows a predictable sequence: (1) resistant propagules in deep soil germinate and colonize surviving or regenerating tree seedlings (months); (2) early-successional EMF generalists (*Rhizopogon* section *Amylopogon*, *Thelephora*) dominate the initial post-fire community (years 1–10); (3) mid-successional species appear as the forest canopy develops and soil organic horizons rebuild (years 10–50); (4) late-successional specialists gradually return, if source populations exist within dispersal range (years 50–200+). The timeline for step 4 — the return of old-growth EMF specialists — is the critical bottleneck. If the fire returns before these species have re-established, recovery resets to step 1, and the site may never recover old-growth fungal diversity under a regime of increasingly frequent fires (GOV-01, PR-04).

### 5.4 Down Wood Habitat Dependency (→ Flora Phase 612, Old-Growth Structure)

Down wood — fallen logs, particularly large-diameter logs from old-growth trees — is the physical habitat that connects fungal ecology to forest structure. The relationship is documented quantitatively in Section 1.3 (Amaranthus 1994, Smith 2000) and operates through three mechanisms:

1. **Moisture refugia:** Decaying logs retain water through summer drought, sustaining EMF activity when surrounding soil desiccates (PR-02).
2. **Substrate for mat-forming EMF:** *Piloderma fallax* and other mat-forming species colonize heavily decayed (class V) wood, creating mycorrhizal hotspots (PR-01).
3. **Seedling establishment sites:** Nurse logs — decomposing trunks colonized by saprotrophic fungi — provide the elevated, nutrient-rich, well-drained substrate where hemlock and spruce seedlings establish their first mycorrhizal partnerships.

The Flora module documents down wood as structural habitat; the Fungi module documents down wood as mycorrhizal infrastructure. They are the same logs viewed from different ecological perspectives — and both perspectives confirm that large-diameter down wood is irreplaceable on timescales shorter than the centuries required to grow and fell old-growth trees.

## 6. Saprotrophic Fungi and Decomposition

Beyond the ectomycorrhizal network, Cascade Range forests host a diverse community of saprotrophic (decomposer) fungi that recycle organic matter — fallen trees, needle litter, animal remains, and fecal material — back into the nutrient pool that sustains forest productivity.

### 6.1 Wood Decay Guilds

Saprotrophic fungi in Cascade forests organize into functional guilds based on their enzymatic capabilities:

**White rot fungi** decompose both cellulose and lignin — the complete wood polymer suite. They produce ligninase enzymes that break the complex aromatic bonds in lignin, a capability that no other organism group possesses at scale. White rot leaves behind a pale, fibrous, spongy residue. Key Cascade genera: *Trametes* (turkey tail), *Fomitopsis*, *Ganoderma*, *Phellinus*. White rot is the dominant decay mode in moist Western Cascades forests where precipitation sustains year-round fungal activity.

**Brown rot fungi** decompose cellulose while leaving the lignin matrix largely intact, producing the characteristic cubical, brown, crumbly residue. Brown rot is less common than white rot in the Cascades but ecologically important because the residual lignin-rich material ("brown cubical rot") persists in soil for centuries, contributing to long-term carbon sequestration. Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*) is a brown rot specialist (Section 3). Key Cascade genera: *Laetiporus* (chicken of the woods), *Fomitopsis pinicola* (red-belted conk), *Postia*.

**Soft rot fungi** decompose cellulose in waterlogged wood, operating in conditions too wet for white or brown rot. They are important in Cascade riparian areas where fallen logs lie in streams and saturated floodplain soils. Soft rot contributes to the formation of large woody debris in aquatic habitats — a cross-module connection to aquatic ecosystem structure.

### 6.2 Lichenized Fungi and Canopy Nitrogen Fixation

Lichens — composite organisms consisting of a fungal partner (mycobiont) housing photosynthetic algae or cyanobacteria — occupy a distinct functional niche in Cascade Range forests. While technically classified as fungi, their ecological function crosses guild boundaries.

**Nitrogen-fixing cyanolichens.** *Lobaria pulmonaria* (lung lichen) and related cyanolichens (*Lobaria oregana*, *Pseudocyphellaria* spp.) are the primary biological nitrogen fixers in Cascade old-growth canopies. These large foliose lichens grow on the bark of old-growth conifers and bigleaf maples, harboring cyanobacterial photobionts (*Nostoc* cells) that convert atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into biologically available ammonia. In old-growth Cascade forests where atmospheric nitrogen deposition is low, cyanolichens can be the dominant source of new nitrogen input to the canopy ecosystem. This nitrogen enters the forest nutrient cycle when lichen fragments fall to the ground or are washed off branches by rain, enriching the soil beneath old trees (GOV-01).

*Lobaria* is old-growth dependent. It requires the deeply furrowed bark of large, old trees for stable attachment and the high canopy humidity of closed-canopy old-growth for sustained metabolic activity. Young plantation trees with smooth bark and open, sun-exposed canopies cannot support *Lobaria* colonies. The species is also sensitive to air quality degradation, making it a dual indicator of both old-growth forest structure and atmospheric purity. Its decline in managed forest landscapes represents a loss of both nitrogen fixation capacity and air quality monitoring capability (GOV-01).

### 6.3 Mycoheterotrophs — Plants That Parasitize Fungi

A small but ecologically significant group of Cascade Range plants has reversed the mycorrhizal mutualism entirely. Mycoheterotrophic plants — including coralroot orchids (*Corallorhiza maculata*, *C. striata*, *C. mertensiana*), Indian pipe (*Monotropa uniflora*), and pinesap (*Monotropa hypopitys*) — lack chlorophyll and cannot photosynthesize. Instead, they tap into the CMN by forming mycorrhizal associations with EMF fungi and extracting carbon that the fungi received from photosynthetic host trees. These plants are, in effect, parasites of the mycorrhizal network — they take carbon without contributing any in return.

Mycoheterotrophs are indicators of intact EMF networks. Their presence in a forest stand confirms that an active CMN exists with sufficient carbon flow to support non-photosynthetic organisms. Indian pipe, with its ghostly white stems emerging from deep forest shade, is a visible proof of concept for the invisible CMN: its existence demonstrates that carbon is flowing from trees through fungi to other organisms through the below-ground network. In clearcut or degraded forests where CMN connectivity is broken, mycoheterotrophs are absent — they cannot survive without the network they parasitize (GOV-01).

### 6.4 Litter Decomposers

The annual needle fall in Cascade Douglas-fir/hemlock forests produces a continuous rain of organic material — an estimated 3,000–5,000 kg of litter per hectare per year in productive stands. This litter is decomposed primarily by saprotrophic fungi in genera including *Mycena*, *Marasmius*, *Collybia*, and *Clitocybe*. Litter decomposition is the process that converts dead plant material into the humus and mineral nutrients that sustain living trees — a closed-loop nutrient cycle mediated almost entirely by fungi and soil bacteria.

The rate of litter decomposition varies with elevation and moisture: Western Cascades montane forests (Zone 2) decompose litter faster than subalpine forests (Zone 3) due to warmer temperatures and more sustained moisture. This decomposition rate gradient influences soil organic horizon depth, nutrient availability, and ultimately the EMF community composition that the soil can support — connecting saprotrophic and ectomycorrhizal ecology through shared soil habitat.

### 6.5 Parasitic Fungi

Parasitic fungi — species that derive nutrition from living hosts at the host's expense — occupy a distinct ecological niche in Cascade Range forests. While less species-rich than ectomycorrhizal or saprotrophic guilds, parasitic fungi exert disproportionate influence on forest structure, tree mortality patterns, and habitat creation.

**Root rot pathogens.** *Armillaria ostoyae* (honey mushroom) is the most ecologically significant parasitic fungus in Cascade Range forests. It attacks living tree roots, spreading through direct root-to-root contact and through rhizomorphs — dark, cord-like hyphal aggregations that can extend through soil for meters between infected trees. *Armillaria* kills trees from the roots up, creating mortality centers — expanding patches of dead and dying trees — that become habitat gaps in the forest canopy. These gaps provide the light conditions for shade-intolerant species to regenerate and create snag habitat for cavity-nesting wildlife. *Armillaria* infection centers are not random forest damage; they are a natural disturbance process that generates structural diversity in otherwise closed-canopy forests (GOV-01).

**Stem decay fungi.** Beyond agarikon (Section 3), several parasitic polypore fungi attack living Cascade conifers. *Phellinus pini* (red ring rot) creates heartwood decay columns in living Douglas-fir and hemlock, weakening stems and creating cavities used by woodpeckers, flying squirrels, and owls. *Fomitopsis pinicola* (red-belted conk) straddles the parasite-saprotroph boundary, attacking weakened trees and continuing decomposition after host death. These stem decay fungi are integral to the creation of wildlife habitat in Cascade forests — without them, the standing dead trees (snags) and cavity-bearing live trees that support dozens of vertebrate species would not exist in their current abundance (GOV-01).

**Rust fungi.** *Cronartium ribicola* (white pine blister rust) is a non-native parasitic rust fungus that has devastated western white pine (*Pinus monticola*) and whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) populations across the Cascade Range. This introduced pathogen alternates between pine hosts and *Ribes* (currant/gooseberry) hosts, causing canker formation on pine branches and trunks that eventually girdle and kill the tree. The decline of whitebark pine at subalpine elevations has cascading effects on Clark's nutcracker seed dispersal, grizzly bear food availability, and watershed hydrology — connecting a parasitic fungus to fauna and aquatic modules through trophic and functional chains (GOV-01, GOV-04).

### 6.6 The Fungal Nutrient Cycle — Integration

The three fungal guilds — ectomycorrhizal, saprotrophic, and parasitic — are not independent. They form an integrated nutrient cycle that sustains Cascade Range forest productivity:

1. **Living trees** allocate 10–20% of photosynthetic carbon to **EMF partners** (GOV-01), receiving phosphorus, nitrogen, and water in return. The EMF network connects trees into a resource-sharing community.

2. **Parasitic fungi** selectively kill trees and create standing dead wood (snags) and fallen logs. This mortality is not waste — it is the process that converts living biomass into the substrate that saprotrophic fungi will decompose.

3. **Saprotrophic fungi** decompose dead wood and litter, converting complex organic polymers (cellulose, lignin, hemicellulose) into simple mineral nutrients (nitrate, phosphate, potassium) that re-enter the soil nutrient pool.

4. **EMF fungi** access these mineralized nutrients from soil and transfer them to living tree roots, completing the cycle: tree → EMF → tree growth → parasitic mortality → saprotrophic decomposition → soil nutrients → EMF uptake → tree.

This cycle operates continuously in old-growth forests. Each guild depends on the others: EMF depend on living trees for carbon; saprotrophs depend on dead trees for substrate; parasites depend on living trees for hosts; and living trees depend on EMF for nutrients and on saprotrophic decomposition for soil fertility. Disrupting any link — by removing trees, eliminating down wood, or degrading EMF communities — breaks the cycle and reduces forest productivity.

The logging legacy (Section 4) is, at its core, a disruption of this cycle. Clearcutting simultaneously removes the EMF carbon source (living trees), the saprotrophic substrate (down wood, if burned), and the parasitic hosts (all trees). The cycle must rebuild from scratch — a process that, as documented, takes 150–200 years to reach old-growth functionality (GOV-01, PR-04).

**Carbon flow quantification.** The scale of carbon flowing through this fungal cycle in Cascade old-growth forests is ecologically significant. Consider a productive old-growth Douglas-fir stand:
- Gross primary productivity: approximately 15–20 metric tons of carbon per hectare per year
- EMF carbon allocation (10–20% of host photosynthate): 1.5–4.0 metric tons C/ha/year transferred to fungal partners
- Saprotrophic decomposition of coarse woody debris: 0.5–2.0 metric tons C/ha/year processed by wood decay fungi
- Litter decomposition: 1.5–2.5 metric tons C/ha/year processed by litter fungi
- Total fungal carbon throughput: approximately 3.5–8.5 metric tons C/ha/year

This means that in a mature Cascade forest, fungi process roughly 25–50% of all carbon flowing through the ecosystem — a proportion that underscores their metabolic centrality. They are not peripheral organisms attached to the edges of the carbon cycle; they are the carbon cycle's primary intermediaries, connecting photosynthesis to decomposition through the mycorrhizal and saprotrophic pathways that recirculate carbon between living and dead organic matter (GOV-01).

**Nutrient stoichiometry.** The fungal nutrient cycle also processes nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium at rates that control forest productivity. In nitrogen-limited Cascade forests (most of the western slopes), the primary nitrogen pathways are: (1) atmospheric fixation by cyanolichens (*Lobaria*) in old-growth canopies, (2) decomposition of organic nitrogen in litter and wood by saprotrophic fungi, and (3) EMF uptake of mineralized nitrogen from soil and transfer to host tree roots. All three pathways are fungal-mediated. Phosphorus cycling follows a similar pattern: saprotrophic fungi mineralize organic phosphorus from decomposing material, EMF hyphae scavenge inorganic phosphorus from soil mineral particles (accessing microsites too small for root hairs), and the Hartig net transfers phosphorus to the host tree. In both cases, the fungal network functions as the forest's nutrient distribution system — concentrating diffuse soil nutrients and delivering them to the organisms (trees) that drive ecosystem productivity (GOV-01).

## 7. Conservation Synthesis

The evidence assembled in this document converges on a single conservation argument with multiple lines of support:

**Old-growth forests are irreplaceable mycological habitat.** This claim rests on quantitative evidence: 200+ EMF species per old-growth stand vs. 20–40 in young plantations (GOV-01); 4x truffle diversity in old-growth vs. plantations (GOV-01); two EMF species restricted exclusively to oldest stands at the Andrews LTER (PR-03); agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*) unable to persist in any managed forest (CON-01); EMF community recovery requiring 150–200 years after clearcutting (GOV-01); and detectable logging legacy in soil fungal communities four decades post-harvest (PR-04).

**Below-ground biodiversity is invisible but consequential.** The logging legacy research (PR-04) demonstrates that above-ground forest appearance does not predict below-ground ecological function. Forests that look recovered are not. This invisibility means that conventional forest surveys — which assess canopy cover, tree diameter, stem density, and wildlife sightings — systematically miss the most persistent damage from timber harvest. Soil fungal community assessment must be incorporated into forest monitoring to reveal the true ecological condition of managed landscapes.

**Cascade Range fungi connect every other biodiversity module.** Through EMF networks, fungi sustain the trees (→ Flora Phase 612). Through truffle production, fungi feed the mammals that feed the raptors (→ Fauna Phase 613). Through decomposition, fungi recycle the nutrients that maintain forest productivity. Through fire response, fungi shape post-disturbance recovery trajectories (→ Threats Phase 616). Through down wood dependency, fungi require the structural legacy of old-growth forests. No other organism group in the Cascade Range occupies as many ecological roles simultaneously. Protecting Cascade fungi means protecting the infrastructure on which all other modules depend.

**The economic and ecological arguments align.** The commercial mushroom harvest (Section 2.5) depends on the same forest conditions — mature canopy, intact soil, retained down wood, minimal disturbance — that sustain EMF diversity and ecosystem function. This convergence is unusual in natural resource management, where economic and ecological interests often conflict. In the Cascade Range, the most economically productive mushroom forests are the most ecologically intact forests. Protecting fungi is protecting livelihoods.

**Time is the limiting resource.** The central constraint in Cascade Range fungal conservation is time. EMF communities take centuries to develop old-growth composition. Agarikon conks grow for decades on century-old trees. Shiro mats expand centimeters per year. CMN hub-node architecture requires trees large enough to function as network centers — a size class reached only after 150+ years of undisturbed growth. None of these timescales is compatible with standard timber rotation lengths. Every old-growth stand that is logged represents centuries of accumulated fungal community structure that cannot be recovered on any timescale relevant to current management decisions. The fungi are patient. The question is whether the management framework can afford to be.

**Northwest Forest Plan context.** The Northwest Forest Plan (1994), the federal management framework governing National Forest lands in the range of the northern spotted owl, provides structural protection for old-growth forests through Late-Successional Reserves (LSRs), Riparian Reserves, and Adaptive Management Areas. These designations, while designed primarily for spotted owl and marbled murrelet habitat, inadvertently protect the most significant Cascade Range fungal habitats. LSRs preserve old-growth EMF communities; Riparian Reserves protect the down wood and moisture conditions that sustain truffle production; Adaptive Management Areas provide opportunities for research on fungal community response to variable retention harvest. The Plan's requirement for green tree retention in harvest units — leaving scattered old-growth trees as legacy structure — also preserves CMN hub nodes that maintain below-ground network connectivity for regenerating seedlings. However, the Plan was designed around wildlife habitat needs, not soil fungal community conservation. Its protections for fungi are incidental, not targeted, and may not adequately address the specific management needs identified in this survey — particularly the 150–200 year EMF recovery timeline that exceeds the Plan's 100-year planning horizon (GOV-01, GOV-05).

**Climate change amplification.** Each of the conservation challenges documented in this survey — old-growth habitat loss, logging legacy, fire regime disruption, agarikon range contraction — is amplified by climate change. Warming temperatures extend the summer drought period, stressing moisture-dependent EMF communities. Reduced snowpack at mid-elevations shifts the moisture regime that sustains Zone 2 montane forests — the core EMF diversity zone. More frequent and severe wildfires destroy the old-growth structure that agarikon and late-successional EMF require. Changing precipitation patterns may shift the fruiting phenology of commercial species, disrupting the harvest economy that provides economic incentive for forest retention. Climate change does not create new threats to Cascade Range fungi — it accelerates and compounds every existing threat simultaneously (PR-04, GOV-06).

**The compound threat.** The most concerning scenario for Cascade Range fungal conservation is the compound interaction of logging legacy, fire regime change, and climate warming acting simultaneously on the same landscape. A clearcut stand with impoverished EMF communities (logging legacy) that burns at high severity (climate-amplified wildfire) during an extended drought (climate warming) faces triple disruption: the EMF community that was already degraded is further depleted by fire, in conditions that slow recovery. The convergence of these three pressures on the same Cascade landscapes — mid-elevation western slopes that have been both heavily logged and are increasingly fire-prone — represents a potential threshold beyond which EMF community recovery may not be possible within any ecologically meaningful timeframe. This compound threat is not hypothetical: the 2020 Labor Day fires burned through previously logged and previously unlogged stands in the Oregon Cascades, providing a natural experiment in compound disturbance whose soil fungal consequences are now being studied (PR-04, GOV-01, GOV-02).
