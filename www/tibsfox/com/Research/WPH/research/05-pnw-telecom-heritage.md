# PNW Telecom Heritage

> **Domain:** Regional Telecommunications History
> **Module:** 5 -- Pacific Northwest Bell, Rural Co-ops, and the Wires That Built the Territory
> **Through-line:** *The Pacific Northwest got telephone service later than the East Coast, across harder terrain, through denser forest, and for more dispersed populations. The engineering decisions forced by PNW geography -- long rural loops, microwave relay across the Cascades, submarine cable landings on storm-battered coastline -- created a telecommunications culture distinct from any other region. When Pacific Northwest Bell became US West became Qwest became CenturyLink became Lumen, the copper stayed in the ground. The fiber followed the same rights-of-way. The terrain hasn't changed.*

## 1. The Bell System in the Pacific Northwest

Telephone service reached the Pacific Northwest in the 1880s, approximately a decade after Bell's patent. The first telephone exchange in Seattle opened in 1883, serving a small number of subscribers connected by iron wire strung on wooden poles [1]. Portland received its first exchange in 1884, and Tacoma in 1883.

### Early Challenges

The PNW presented unique engineering challenges for telephone infrastructure:

- **Terrain:** The Cascade Range divides the region into wet western and dry eastern halves. Building trunk lines across the Cascades required long-span aerial cable through mountain passes or buried cable through valleys [2].
- **Forest density:** Western Oregon and Washington are some of the most heavily forested terrain in North America. Right-of-way clearing for telephone lines competed with the timber industry for access to the same corridors.
- **Distance:** Population centers are spread across vast distances. Seattle to Spokane is 280 miles. Portland to Boise is 430 miles. Rural subscribers could be 20-30 miles from the nearest central office.
- **Weather:** Ice storms, windstorms, and heavy wet snow regularly damaged aerial cable. The Columbus Day Storm of 1962 destroyed thousands of pole miles across Oregon and Washington [3].
- **Seismic risk:** The Cascadia Subduction Zone poses catastrophic risk to buried and aerial infrastructure throughout the region.

## 2. Pacific Northwest Bell

Pacific Northwest Bell (PNB) was the Bell Operating Company serving Oregon, Washington, and northern Idaho from 1961 to 1984 [4]. Before 1961, the territory was served by Pacific Telephone and Telegraph (PT&T), which also covered California and Nevada. The creation of PNB as a separate company reflected the distinct operational character of the Northwest territory.

### Scale of Operations

At the time of divestiture (1984), Pacific Northwest Bell served approximately:
- 3.6 million access lines
- ~2,000 central offices and remote switching units
- ~85,000 route miles of cable (aerial, buried, and underground)
- Approximately 12,000 employees [5]

### Central Office Equipment

PNB's central offices reflected the full evolution of switching technology:

```
PNW BELL SWITCHING EQUIPMENT TIMELINE
================================================================

  1880s-1920s:  Manual cordboard exchanges
                (rural offices, small towns)

  1920s-1950s:  Strowger step-by-step
                (medium towns, first automatic service)

  1940s-1970s:  Western Electric No. 5 Crossbar
                (Seattle, Portland, major cities)
                #1 Crossbar (large metro offices)

  1965-1975:    #1ESS electronic switching
                (Seattle downtown, Portland, Tacoma)

  1976-1984:    #5ESS digital switching
                (new installations, #1ESS replacements)
                Northern Telecom DMS-100
                (competitive procurement post-divestiture)

  Rural:        GTE Automatic Electric step-by-step
                Small co-op exchanges (50-500 lines)
                Party lines persisted into the 1980s
```

### Party Lines

Party line service -- where multiple households shared a single telephone line -- persisted in rural PNW well into the 1970s and 1980s [6]. A 4-party line meant four families shared one copper pair, each assigned a different ring pattern (e.g., two short rings, one long ring). Any party could pick up and listen to any other party's conversation. Conversion to private line service required running new copper pairs -- expensive in terrain where homes might be miles apart.

## 3. Rural Telephone Cooperatives

The PNW has a rich tradition of rural telephone cooperatives -- locally owned, community-governed telephone companies serving areas that the Bell System and GTE found uneconomic to serve [7].

### Notable PNW Cooperatives

| Cooperative | Location | Lines (approx.) | Founded |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|---------|
| Hood Canal Telephone Co-op | Mason County, WA | ~3,000 | 1953 |
| Tenino Telephone Co. | Thurston County, WA | ~2,500 | 1905 |
| Whidbey Telephone Co. | Island County, WA | ~12,000 | 1908 |
| Scio Mutual Telephone Assoc. | Linn County, OR | ~1,500 | 1901 |
| Roome Telephone Co. | Wheeler County, OR | ~300 | 1950s |
| TDS Telecom (various) | Multiple rural WA/OR | ~50,000 | Various |

### The REA and Rural Telephony

The Rural Electrification Administration (REA), established in 1935 and expanded to include telephone service in 1949, provided low-interest loans to rural telephone cooperatives [8]. REA funding was critical for PNW rural telephone development -- without it, many communities in eastern Oregon, rural Washington, and Idaho would have remained without service into the 1960s or later.

REA-funded construction in the PNW typically consisted of:
- Buried cable (where terrain permitted) or aerial cable on wooden poles
- Small central offices with step-by-step or small electronic switches
- Long subscriber loops (up to 30 miles in eastern Oregon)
- Carrier systems (SLC-96, later SLC-5) to multiplex subscribers on limited cable pairs

```
RURAL PNW TELEPHONE INFRASTRUCTURE
================================================================

  Central Office (small town)
    │
    ├── Subscriber loop (copper pair)
    │   └── 5-30 miles to farmhouse
    │       (loaded every 6,000 ft with H88 coils)
    │
    ├── SLC-96 Remote Terminal
    │   └── 2-10 miles from CO, serves 96 lines
    │       (multiplexed on 5 T1 circuits)
    │
    ├── Party line
    │   └── 2-8 subscribers sharing one pair
    │       (ring pattern identification)
    │
    └── Trunk to toll center
        └── Microwave relay or T-carrier to nearest
            Class 4 toll switch (often 50+ miles)

  Challenge: line loss on long loops
    Copper pair at 26 AWG: ~1.0 dB/mile at 1 kHz
    20-mile loop: ~20 dB insertion loss
    Loading coils reduce to ~0.4 dB/mile (loaded)
    But loading coils kill DSL (bandwidth limited to ~3.5 kHz)
```

## 4. The Cascades Backbone

Connecting western Washington and Oregon to their eastern halves required crossing the Cascade Range -- a formidable barrier for telecommunications infrastructure [9].

### Microwave Relay

Before fiber optics, long-haul telecommunications across the Cascades relied on microwave relay towers. AT&T Long Lines and Pacific Northwest Bell built chains of microwave towers across the major passes:

```
CASCADE MICROWAVE RELAY ROUTES
================================================================

  Northern Route (WA):
    Seattle → Snoqualmie Pass → Ellensburg → Vantage → Spokane
    (I-90 corridor, ~30-mile tower spacing)

  Central Route (WA):
    Tacoma → Stampede Pass → Yakima → Tri-Cities
    (Northern Pacific Railroad corridor)

  Southern Route (OR):
    Portland → Government Camp → Madras → Bend → Burns
    (US-26 / US-97 corridor)

  Columbia Gorge Route:
    Portland → Hood River → The Dalles → Pendleton
    (I-84 corridor, river-level path avoids summit)

  Tower specifications (typical TD-2 system):
    Frequency: 3.7-4.2 GHz (C-band)
    Capacity: 600 voice channels per radio
    Antenna: 10-ft parabolic dish
    Power: ~1W transmit
    Path length: 25-35 miles between towers
    Tower height: 60-200 ft (terrain-dependent)
    Fade margin: 40 dB (for PNW rain/fog)
```

### Fiber Optic Replacement

Beginning in the 1980s, fiber optic cable replaced microwave for backbone routes. The fiber typically followed railroad rights-of-way through the mountain passes -- the same routes the railroads had surveyed in the 19th century [10].

Key PNW fiber routes:
- **I-90 corridor:** Seattle to Spokane (Williams Communications, now Zayo)
- **I-5 corridor:** Seattle to Portland to Sacramento (multiple carriers)
- **I-84 corridor:** Portland to Boise through the Columbia Gorge
- **US-97 corridor:** Bend to Klamath Falls, connecting to California backbones

## 5. Submarine Cable Landings

The Oregon coast is a critical landing point for transpacific submarine fiber optic cables connecting North America to Asia [11].

### Pacific City and Nedonna Beach

```
PNW SUBMARINE CABLE LANDINGS
================================================================

  Pacific City, OR (Tillamook County):
    - PC-1 (Pacific Crossing-1): 21,000 km, Japan-US
      Capacity: 640 Gbps (original), upgraded
      Landed: 2000
      Route: Ajigaura, Japan → Pacific City, OR

  Nedonna Beach / Rockaway Beach, OR:
    - Multiple cable landings (specific systems classified
      by some operators for security)

  Bandon, OR:
    - Cable landing station (Southern Oregon coast)

  Hillsboro, OR (inland):
    - Major carrier hotel and interconnection point
    - Terminates multiple submarine cable systems
    - Connected to coast via buried fiber

  Significance:
    - PNW submarine cable landings carry a significant
      portion of US-Asia internet traffic
    - Redundancy: multiple landing points reduce
      single-point-of-failure risk
    - Security: cable landing stations are designated
      critical infrastructure by DHS
```

> **SAFETY WARNING:** Submarine cable routes and landing station locations are sensitive critical infrastructure. Detailed coordinates, security arrangements, and cable system capacities should not be published in ways that could aid sabotage. This module discusses only publicly available information from cable operator filings [12].

## 6. The Divestiture and US West

On January 1, 1984, the Bell System divestiture took effect. Pacific Northwest Bell became part of US West, one of seven Regional Bell Operating Companies [13].

### US West Territory

US West served 14 states: Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Iowa, Minnesota, Montana, Nebraska, New Mexico, North Dakota, Oregon, South Dakota, Utah, Washington, and Wyoming -- the largest geographic territory of any RBOC but among the lowest population density.

```
US WEST -- POST-DIVESTITURE STRUCTURE
================================================================

  US West, Inc. (holding company)
    │
    ├── US West Communications
    │   (local telephone service, access lines)
    │   └── Pacific Northwest Bell → becomes division
    │       (Oregon, Washington, Idaho operations)
    │
    ├── US West Marketing Resources (Yellow Pages)
    │
    └── US West NewVector (cellular, later MediaOne)

  PNW impact of divestiture:
    - Local service quality initially maintained
    - Long-distance now competitive (AT&T, MCI, Sprint)
    - Equipment purchases open to competition
      (no longer Western Electric monopoly)
    - Innovation investment varies by state commission
```

### US West and DSL

US West was an early deployer of DSL technology in the PNW, beginning ADSL trials in the mid-1990s [14]. However, the company's vast rural territory meant DSL was available primarily in metropolitan areas -- the copper loop lengths in rural PNW exceeded DSL's distance limitations (typically 18,000 feet maximum for ADSL).

## 7. Qwest and the Fiber Promise

In 2000, US West was acquired by Qwest Communications, a fiber-optic long-haul carrier founded by Philip Anschutz [15]. Qwest had built a 25,000-mile fiber network along railroad rights-of-way (primarily Union Pacific). The merger combined US West's local access network with Qwest's long-haul fiber.

### Qwest Fiber Build

Qwest deployed fiber-to-the-node (FTTN) and, in limited areas, fiber-to-the-premises (FTTP) in PNW markets:

```
QWEST/CENTURYLINK FIBER DEPLOYMENT IN PNW
================================================================

  FTTN (Fiber to the Node):
    - Fiber to neighborhood cabinets (DSLAM)
    - VDSL2 from cabinet to home (short copper loop)
    - Speeds: 25-100 Mbps (distance-dependent)
    - Deployed: major suburban areas of Seattle, Portland

  FTTP (Fiber to the Premises):
    - Fiber directly to the home/business
    - GPON (Gigabit Passive Optical Network)
    - Speeds: 1 Gbps symmetric
    - Deployed: selected new developments, business districts

  Coverage gaps:
    - Rural eastern WA/OR: DSL only (6-15 Mbps)
    - Many areas: no upgrade from original copper
    - Mountain communities: satellite or fixed wireless only
```

Qwest's financial difficulties (the company disclosed accounting fraud in 2002, resulting in SEC enforcement actions) limited investment in PNW infrastructure during a critical period when cable companies (Comcast) were deploying DOCSIS broadband [16].

## 8. CenturyLink to Lumen

CenturyTel acquired Qwest in 2011, becoming CenturyLink. In 2020, CenturyLink rebranded as Lumen Technologies, refocusing on enterprise fiber and edge computing while the consumer business continued under the CenturyLink name [17].

### PNW Service Today

As of 2025, Lumen/CenturyLink serves approximately 2.5 million access lines across the PNW, with service quality varying dramatically between urban fiber areas and rural copper areas [18]:

- **Urban (Seattle, Portland):** Fiber available in many neighborhoods. 1 Gbps symmetric.
- **Suburban:** Mixed FTTN/VDSL2 and copper DSL. 25-100 Mbps.
- **Rural:** Copper DSL, often 1.5-10 Mbps. Some areas limited to 768 kbps.
- **Remote:** No wireline service. Dependent on satellite (Starlink) or fixed wireless.

### The Copper Retirement Question

Lumen has sought regulatory approval to retire copper plant in areas where fiber or wireless alternatives exist. The FCC's copper retirement rules (47 CFR 51.332) require 180-day notice and alternative service availability [19]. In the PNW, copper retirement is contentious because:

- Many rural customers have no alternative
- POTS reliability (self-powered, works in outages) has no VoIP equivalent
- Copper rights-of-way, once abandoned, may not be recoverable for future fiber builds

## 9. PNW Cellular Deployment

### Early Cellular in the PNW

Cellular service launched in the PNW in 1984, with the first AMPS networks deployed in Seattle and Portland [20]:

- **Wire side (Block A):** US West NewVector (later became part of Verizon Wireless through AirTouch/PrimeCo lineage)
- **Non-wire side (Block B):** McCaw Cellular Communications (Seattle-based, founded by Craig McCaw)

Craig McCaw, who grew up in the Seattle area, built McCaw Cellular into the largest cellular carrier in the US before selling to AT&T in 1994 for $11.5 billion [21]. McCaw's insight was that cellular spectrum licenses, acquired at auction or through FCC lotteries, represented the most valuable real estate in the electromagnetic spectrum.

### PNW Cellular Coverage Challenges

```
PNW CELLULAR COVERAGE CHALLENGES
================================================================

  Terrain:
    - Cascade Range: 6,000-14,000 ft peaks block signal
    - Olympic Mountains: deep valleys, no line-of-sight
    - Columbia Gorge: canyon walls cause severe multipath
    - Dense forest canopy: 10-20 dB attenuation at 700 MHz

  Population distribution:
    - I-5 corridor (Seattle-Tacoma-Olympia-Portland): dense
    - I-90 corridor (Seattle-Ellensburg-Spokane): moderate
    - Eastern OR/WA: very sparse (1-5 people/sq mi)
    - Olympic Peninsula: sparse, extreme terrain

  Solutions:
    - 700 MHz (Band 12/13/71): best propagation through trees
    - FirstNet (Band 14): public safety, rural priority
    - Microwave backhaul: cell sites without fiber
    - Satellite backhaul: most remote sites
    - COW (Cell on Wheels): temporary for events, disasters
```

### The Seattle Kingdome

The Seattle Kingdome (1976-2000), the region's major indoor venue before its demolition, was one of the earliest indoor cellular deployment sites in the PNW. The concrete dome structure blocked cellular signals, requiring a distributed antenna system (DAS) inside the venue -- a precursor to the modern in-building cellular systems now standard in stadiums, airports, and office buildings [22].

## 10. The Rural Broadband Challenge

The PNW's rural broadband gap is one of the most significant telecommunications challenges in the region [23].

### Federal Broadband Programs

| Program | Agency | PNW Impact |
|---------|--------|------------|
| USDA ReConnect | USDA | Grants for rural fiber, WA/OR recipients |
| BEAD Program | NTIA | $42.45B national, WA allocation ~$1.2B |
| E-Rate | FCC/USAC | School and library connectivity |
| Lifeline | FCC | Subsidized service for low-income |
| ACP (ended 2024) | FCC | Broadband discount, served ~500K PNW households |

### Washington State Broadband Office

Washington established the State Broadband Office in 2019 to coordinate deployment, mapping, and policy [24]. Key initiatives:

- **Broadband mapping:** Detailed coverage maps at address level
- **Dig Once:** Requiring broadband conduit in state highway projects
- **Public-private partnerships:** Facilitating carrier investment in underserved areas

### Oregon Broadband Office

Oregon's Broadband Office focuses on similar goals, with particular attention to tribal lands, rural eastern Oregon, and coastal communities [25].

```
PNW BROADBAND COVERAGE ESTIMATE (2024)
================================================================

  Seattle metro:     95%+ at 100 Mbps (fiber + cable)
  Portland metro:    93%+ at 100 Mbps (fiber + cable)
  Suburban WA/OR:    80-90% at 25 Mbps
  Rural western:     50-70% at 25 Mbps
  Rural eastern:     30-50% at 25 Mbps
  Tribal lands:      20-40% at 25 Mbps
  Remote/mountain:   10-30% at 25 Mbps

  FCC definition of broadband: 100/20 Mbps (updated 2024)
  Previous definition: 25/3 Mbps (2015-2024)

  Gap: approximately 300,000-500,000 PNW households
  lack access to FCC-defined broadband service
```

## 11. Community Networks and Municipal Fiber

The PNW has a strong tradition of community-owned telecommunications networks, driven by frustration with incumbent carrier investment levels [26].

### Notable PNW Community Networks

```
PNW COMMUNITY AND MUNICIPAL NETWORKS
================================================================

  Tacoma (Click! Network):
    - City-owned fiber + cable TV + internet
    - Launched 1997 by Tacoma Public Utilities
    - Serves ~40,000 homes
    - Gigabit fiber available

  Grant County PUD (WA):
    - Public utility district-owned fiber network
    - Covers rural Grant County (Ephrata, Moses Lake)
    - Wholesale fiber to ISPs
    - One of the earliest PUD broadband deployments

  Mason County PUD No. 3 (WA):
    - Fiber-to-the-premises in rural Mason County
    - Serves areas Lumen/CenturyLink would not upgrade

  Sandy, OR (SandyNet):
    - City-owned fiber-to-the-premises
    - Population ~12,000
    - Gigabit service, competitive pricing
    - Funded by revenue bonds

  Ashland, OR (AFN):
    - Ashland Fiber Network
    - City-owned, launched 1999
    - One of the first municipal fiber networks in Oregon

  Whidbey Telecom (WA):
    - Cooperative, not municipal
    - Fiber-to-the-premises across much of Whidbey Island
    - Gigabit service in rural/semi-rural territory
```

### Legal Environment

Washington state law (RCW 54.16.330) allows public utility districts to provide wholesale telecommunications services. Some PUDs have interpreted this to include retail broadband, leading to legal and regulatory debate [27]. Oregon has fewer restrictions on municipal broadband, and several cities have built their own networks.
