Mission Package

Alexander Graham Bell's first telephone was a transducer, a wire, and another transducer. Every telephone system since — from Strowger step-by-step switches to 5G massive MIMO beamforming — is an elaboration on the same problem: how do you get a human voice from here to there, reliably, in real time? The engineering has changed beyond recognition. The question has not changed at all. The Weekly Phone traces that question from 1876 to the mesh networks being built today in the Pacific Northwest, where people with radios are building infrastructure that no corporation controls and no earthquake can take down all at once.

The Through-Line

The telephone network is a 150-year engineering conversation about the same problem: getting a human voice from here to there. Bell's carbon microphone converted sound to electricity. Strowger's step-by-step switch replaced the human operator with relay logic. Shannon proved that a noisy channel has a maximum capacity, and every generation since has been an attempt to approach that limit more closely. The Pacific Northwest built its telephone infrastructure across some of the hardest terrain in North America — mountain passes, dense forest, storm-battered coastline — and the engineering decisions forced by that geography still shape the region's connectivity today. Mesh communications is the latest answer to the oldest question: when the infrastructure fails, how do people with radios find each other? They build the network themselves, one node at a time, the same way the first telephone operators plugged patch cords into jacks — connecting people who wanted to talk.
