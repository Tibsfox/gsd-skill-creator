>
> This module defines HOW construction knowledge gets presented to each audience tier. ## Part 1: The 5-Tier Audience Depth System

The BCM audience system maps to Dimension 5 (Audience Depth — AD) of the parameter schema [00-parameter-schema.md]. Each level represents a distinct reader with distinct needs, and the boundary between levels is defined by three questions:

1. **What does this person need to KNOW?** (knowledge boundary)
2. **What does this person need to DO?** (action boundary)
3. **What must this person NOT do without professional help?** (safety boundary)

### Level Definitions and Boundaries

| Level | Reader | Knowledge Goal | Action Goal | Safety Boundary |
|-------|--------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| L1 — Homeowner | No construction background | Understand what systems exist, recognize problems, make informed hiring decisions | Observe, maintain, hire wisely | All repairs beyond basic maintenance require professionals |
| L2 — Skilled DIY | Tools, some experience | Know what is legally and safely within DIY scope, execute permitted projects correctly | Basic repairs, cosmetic improvements, permitted projects with inspection | Stop at license boundaries (electrical panel, gas, structural, sewer main) |
| L3 — Trade Student | Apprenticeship or CC program | Master theory, code basis, and calculation methods for chosen trade | Learn, practice under supervision, prepare for journeyman exams | Work only under licensed supervision until journeyman certification |
| L4 — Journeyman/Contractor | Licensed, runs projects | Code compliance at amendment level, estimating, inspection prep, business management | Design installations, manage crews, coordinate inspections, bid work | PE/SE stamp boundaries; scope-of-license limits |
| L5 — Engineer/Architect | PE, SE, or registered architect | Design theory, calculation procedures, code analysis including commentary and alternative compliance | Design systems, stamp drawings, develop curriculum, prepare for PE/SE exams | Professional liability; standard of care obligations |

### The Vocabulary Gradient

The same concept expressed at each level — demonstrated with "seismic load path":

| Level | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| L1 | "During an earthquake, your house needs a continuous chain of connections from the roof all the way down to the foundation. If any link in that chain is missing, that part of the house can pull apart." |
| L2 | "Seismic forces transfer through your building's structure from roof to foundation. The load path includes roof-to-wall connections (hurricane ties), wall-to-floor connections (hold-downs), and sill-to-foundation connections (anchor bolts). Each connection must be present and properly installed." |
| L3 | "The seismic load path is the continuous chain of structural elements and connections that transfers lateral forces from the point of application (typically the roof diaphragm) through the vertical lateral force-resisting system (shear walls, braced frames, or moment frames) to the foundation. Each connection is sized per ASCE 7-22 Section 12.1.3, with load combinations per Section 2.3 [CODE-04]. The IRC prescriptive path (Section R602.10) provides pre-engineered shear wall solutions for conventional light-frame construction [CODE-01]." |
| L4 | "Load path verification per ASCE 7-22 Section 12.1.3 at each connection: roof diaphragm to collector, collector to shear wall, shear wall to foundation. Oregon OSSC 2025 Section 1613 [STD-01] adopts ASCE 7-22 seismic provisions directly. For prescriptive residential, IRC R602.10 shear wall tables remain the primary path. Inspector will verify hold-down anchor bolt placement, nailing schedule on sheathing, and strap connections at each level transition. Document with photos at each stage." |
| L5 | "Complete load path analysis per ASCE 7-22 Chapter 12 [CODE-04]. Seismic base shear V = Cs*W, where Cs = SDS/(R/Ie) per Eq. 12.8-2, subject to upper/lower bounds of Eqs. 12.8-3 through 12.8-6. For the PNW Cascadia Subduction Zone, SDS values range from 0.70g to 1.20g depending on site class (ASCE 7-22 Chapter 11). Vertical distribution per Eq. 12.8-12, diaphragm design forces per Section 12.10. ASCE 41-17 Tier 1/Tier 2 evaluation required for existing buildings per Portland Ch. 24.85 [STD-16]. Oregon OSSC 2025 adopts IBC 2024 seismic provisions [STD-01]; Washington remains on IBC 2021 until May 2027 [GOV-02]." |

### Content Transformation Rules

When adapting content from one level to another, apply these rules:

| Rule | L1 | L2 | L3 | L4 | L5 |
|------|----|----|----|----|-----|
| Code sections cited | Never bare; plain-language only | By name with summary | Full section analysis | Amendment-level comparison | Commentary and intent |
| Calculations shown | Never | Result only, with context | Full worked examples | Design shortcuts with basis | Full derivations |
| Jargon handling | Defined at every use | Defined at first use | Used freely | Professional standard | Engineering convention |
| Cost data format | Ranges with disclaimers | Material + tool lists | Cost awareness | Unit cost tables | Life-cycle cost analysis |
| Safety callout density | High (BLOCK at every threshold) | Moderate-high | Moderate | Moderate-low | Low (professional judgment) |
| PNW specificity | "In our area..." | Climate zone + code state | OR/WA amendment detail | Amendment comparison tables | Design parameter tables |

## Part 2: ABET Engineering Accreditation Alignment

BCM L5 content is structured to support ABET-accredited engineering programs. This section maps BCM modules to ABET EAC Criteria 2025-2026 requirements [EDU-01, PRO-01].

> **Note:**
> This framework presents ABET alignment as a curriculum mapping tool. BCM is a knowledge resource, not an accredited program. Individual institutions bear responsibility for achieving and maintaining ABET accreditation through their own assessment processes.

### ABET EAC Student Outcomes (2025-2026)

ABET defines seven Student Outcomes (SOs) that all engineering graduates must attain [EDU-01]:

| SO | Description | BCM Module Coverage | Primary Assessment |
|----|------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| SO 1 | Identify, formulate, and solve complex engineering problems by applying principles of engineering, science, and mathematics | M1-ST (structural analysis), M2-EL (circuit analysis), M3-PM (fluid mechanics), M4-BE (heat transfer) | Calculation procedures, design examples |
| SO 2 | Apply engineering design to produce solutions that meet specified needs with consideration of public health, safety, and welfare, as well as global, cultural, social, environmental, and economic factors | M1-ST (seismic design), M4-BE (energy code compliance), M5-CS (code application) | Design projects, code compliance analysis |
| SO 3 | Communicate effectively with a range of audiences | M6-ED (the 5-tier system itself demonstrates this outcome) | Multi-audience writing assignments |
| SO 4 | Recognize ethical and professional responsibilities in engineering situations and make informed judgments considering global, social, economic impacts | M5-CS (code ethics, professional liability), M6-ED (professional responsibility) | Case studies, ethics analysis |
| SO 5 | Function effectively on a team whose members together provide leadership, create a collaborative and inclusive environment, establish goals, plan tasks, and meet objectives | All modules (project-based learning integration) | Group design projects |
| SO 6 | Develop and conduct appropriate experimentation, analyze and interpret data, and use engineering judgment to draw conclusions | M1-ST (materials testing), M4-BE (energy modeling), M3-PM (flow testing) | Laboratory exercises, field measurements |
| SO 7 | Acquire and apply new knowledge as needed, using appropriate learning strategies | M5-CS (code cycle tracking), M6-ED (continuing education) | Self-directed learning portfolios |

### Architectural Engineering Program Criteria

ABET EAC criteria for Architectural Engineering specify four curriculum areas [EDU-01]. BCM module coverage:

| ABET Curriculum Area | Required Level | BCM Module | Content Depth |
|---------------------|---------------|------------|---------------|
| Building structures | Synthesis (design) OR Application | M1-ST | Full structural analysis and design procedures including seismic |
| Building mechanical systems | Synthesis (design) OR Application | M3-PM, M4-BE | HVAC load calculations, energy analysis, plumbing system design |
| Building electrical systems | Comprehension minimum | M2-EL | Service calculations, power distribution, lighting design |
| Construction/construction management | Comprehension minimum | M5-CS, M6-ED | Scheduling, estimating, delivery methods, safety |

**ABET proficiency requirements:** Programs must demonstrate synthesis (design) in at least one area, application in at least one, and comprehension in the remaining two [EDU-01].

**ABET prerequisite requirements:** Mathematics through differential equations, probability and statistics, calculus-based physics, and chemistry [EDU-01].

### ABET Outcomes-Based Assessment Model

The ABET continuous improvement cycle maps directly to BCM's structure [EDU-01]:

```
Program Educational Objectives (PEOs)
    ↓ (broad, 3-5 year post-graduation goals)
Student Outcomes (SOs 1-7)
    ↓ (specific, measurable competencies at graduation)
Curriculum Mapping
    ↓ (where each SO is Introduced → Reinforced → Mastered)
Performance Indicators
    ↓ (measurable behaviors demonstrating attainment)
Assessment Methods
    ↓ (exams, projects, portfolios, rubrics)
Continuous Improvement
    ↓ (data-driven curriculum adjustments)
```

### Course 1: Construction Materials (3 credits lecture + 1 credit lab)

**Course Description:** Properties, behavior, testing, and selection of construction materials including wood, steel, concrete, masonry, and composites. Emphasis on PNW species and conditions. Laboratory testing of material properties per ASTM standards.

**Prerequisites:** Physics I (calculus-based), Chemistry I, Introduction to Engineering

**ABET Student Outcomes:** SO 1 (apply), SO 6 (reinforce)

**Learning Objectives:** Upon completion, students will be able to:
1. Identify mechanical, thermal, and durability properties of primary structural materials [SO 1]
2. Perform standard material tests (compression, tension, flexure) and interpret results [SO 6]
3. Select appropriate materials for PNW conditions including seismic, moisture, and marine exposure [SO 1, SO 2]
4. Explain degradation mechanisms (corrosion, decay, freeze-thaw, UV) and mitigation strategies [SO 1]
5. Compare material sustainability profiles including embodied carbon and lifecycle impacts [SO 2, SO 4]
6. Reference material design values from NDS, AISC, and ACI standards [SO 1, SO 7]

**Weekly Topics:**

| Week | Topic | BCM Reference | Lab Activity |
|------|-------|--------------|-------------|
| 1 | Material properties fundamentals: stress, strain, elastic modulus, Poisson's ratio | [M1-ST:Materials Science] | Lab safety orientation; measurement tools |
| 2 | Wood I: Species, grading, design values. Douglas fir in PNW construction | [M1-ST:Wood Systems], STD-19 | Wood species identification; moisture content testing |
| 3 | Wood II: Connection design, fastener capacity, engineered wood products (glulam, LVL, I-joists) | [M1-ST:Wood Connections], STD-19 | Tension/compression testing of wood specimens |
| 4 | Steel I: Structural steel properties, grades, shapes. AISC design values | [M1-ST:Steel Systems], STD-20 | Tensile testing of steel coupons |
| 5 | Steel II: Connection types, welding vs. bolting, fatigue, corrosion in marine environments | [M1-ST:Steel Connections], STD-20 | Bolt shear testing; weld inspection |
| 6 | Concrete I: Mix design, water-cement ratio, admixtures, curing. PNW aggregate sources | [M1-ST:Concrete Systems] | Cylinder casting; slump testing |
| 7 | Concrete II: Reinforcement, development length, durability, sulfate resistance | [M1-ST:Concrete Design] | **Midterm exam** |
| 8 | Concrete III: Testing — compressive strength (28-day), split tensile, flexural | [M1-ST:Concrete Testing] | 28-day cylinder break; flexural beam test |
| 9 | Masonry: CMU, brick, mortar types, grout, reinforced vs. unreinforced masonry | [M1-ST:Masonry Systems] | Mortar cube testing; prism construction |
| 10 | Composites and emerging materials: FRP, CLT, mass timber, recycled materials | [M1-ST:Emerging Materials] | FRP coupon testing |
| 11 | Thermal and moisture properties: R-value, permeability, hygroscopic behavior | [M4-BE:Thermal Properties] | Thermal conductivity measurement |
| 12 | Material degradation: corrosion, rot, pest damage, fire performance. PNW exposure conditions | [M1-ST:Durability], [M4-BE:Moisture] | Accelerated weathering comparison |
| 13 | Material selection methodology: performance, cost, availability, sustainability | [M1-ST:Material Selection] | Material selection project presentations |
| 14 | PNW-specific materials: regional species, local aggregate, seismic detailing materials | [M1-ST:PNW Materials] | Field trip to materials supplier or construction site |
| 15 | Course integration and review | All M1-ST sections | **Final lab practical** |

### Course 2: Structural Analysis (3 credits)

**Course Description:** Analysis of determinate and indeterminate structures under gravity, wind, and seismic loading. Methods include equilibrium, virtual work, slope-deflection, moment distribution, and matrix methods. PNW seismic design integration throughout.

**Prerequisites:** Statics, Mechanics of Materials, Calculus III (multivariable), Linear Algebra

**ABET Student Outcomes:** SO 1 (master), SO 2 (reinforce)

**Learning Objectives:** Upon completion, students will be able to:
1. Analyze determinate beams, frames, and trusses for internal forces, shear, and moment diagrams [SO 1]
2. Apply virtual work methods to calculate deflections [SO 1]
3. Analyze indeterminate structures using force method and displacement methods [SO 1]
4. Perform lateral load analysis for wind and seismic forces per ASCE 7-22 [SO 1, SO 2]
5. Use matrix structural analysis for computer-based solutions [SO 1]
6. Integrate PNW seismic design parameters into structural analysis [SO 2]

## Part 4: The Complete Homeowner's Guide Framework (L1)

This is the flagship L1 deliverable. It structures the complete guide that a PNW homeowner needs to understand, maintain, and make informed decisions about their home. Every section enforces the safety boundary: homeowners observe, maintain, and hire wisely — they do not perform structural, electrical, gas, or major plumbing work themselves.

> **BLOCK — Scope of This Guide**
>
> This guide helps you understand your home and make informed decisions. It does NOT provide instructions for performing work that requires licensed professionals or permits. When the guide says "call a professional," that is a safety boundary, not a suggestion.
>
> **Required professionals by system:**
> - **Structural:** Licensed Structural Engineer (SE) for assessment; licensed general contractor for repairs
> - **Electrical:** Licensed electrician for all panel, circuit, and wiring work
> - **Gas:** Licensed plumber or HVAC technician for all gas line and gas appliance work
> - **Plumbing:** Licensed plumber for sewer, water main, and water heater work
> - **HVAC:** Licensed HVAC technician for system installation and major repairs

### Guide Structure

The Complete Homeowner's Guide is organized into seven sections, each following the L1 template from `00-content-templates.md`:

---

#### Section 1: Understanding Your Home's Systems

**Purpose:** Give homeowners a mental model of what their house is made of and how the parts interact.

**Content framework:**

**1.1 Structural System — The Bones**
- What it is: foundation, framing (walls, floors, roof), connections between them
- Analogy: "Your home's structure is its skeleton. The foundation is the feet, the walls are the legs and spine, the roof is the skull. They all connect together, and if one part weakens, the others are affected."
- Where to find key components: foundation (crawlspace or basement), framing (attic access), connections (visible in unfinished areas)
- Cross-reference: [M1-ST:L1 content]

> **PNW Regional Note:**
> PNW homes sit in the Cascadia Subduction Zone — one of the most significant seismic hazards in North America. Homes built before 1974 in Oregon were not required to be bolted to their foundations. Homes built before 1993 were built before modern seismic code requirements were added to Oregon's building code [STD-16, GOV-05].
>
> *Applies to: Both OR and WA*

**1.2 Electrical System — The Nervous System**
- What it is: service entrance (where power enters), main panel (circuit breaker box), branch circuits (wiring to outlets, lights, appliances)
- Analogy: "Your electrical panel is like the fuse box in a car — it controls and protects every electrical circuit in your house."
- Where to find key components: meter outside, panel (garage, basement, or utility area), GFCI outlets (kitchen, bathroom, exterior)
- What NOT to touch: the main panel, any wiring, the meter base
- Cross-reference: [M2-EL:L1 content]

> **BLOCK — Licensed Professional Required**
>
> All electrical work beyond changing a light bulb or resetting a tripped breaker requires a licensed electrician. This includes adding outlets, replacing switches, and any work inside the electrical panel.
>
> **Required professional:** Licensed Electrician
> **Why:** Electrical shock, fire, and death are real risks from improper electrical work
> **Code basis:** NEC 2023, OESC 2023 [STD-03], WA NEC adoption [GOV-08]

**1.3 Plumbing System — The Circulatory System**
- What it is: water supply (clean water in), drain-waste-vent or DWV (used water out), water heater, fixtures
- Analogy: "Your plumbing has two completely separate pipe systems — one brings clean water in under pressure, the other drains used water out by gravity. They should never, ever cross."
- Where to find key components: main shutoff valve (near meter or where pipe enters house), water heater, cleanouts
- The one thing every homeowner must know: where the main water shutoff valve is and how to operate it
- Cross-reference: [M3-PM:L1 content]

**1.4 HVAC System — The Lungs**
- What it is: heating (furnace, heat pump, boiler), cooling (AC, heat pump), ventilation (fresh air), distribution (ducts, registers, returns)
- Analogy: "Your HVAC system breathes for your house — bringing in fresh air, heating or cooling it, and distributing it to every room."
- Where to find key components: furnace/heat pump (utility area), air filter (return air grille or furnace), thermostat, outdoor condenser unit
- The one thing every homeowner should do: change the air filter regularly (every 1-3 months in PNW, more during wildfire season)
- Cross-reference: [M3-PM:L1 content], [M4-BE:L1 content]

**1.5 Building Envelope — The Skin**
- What it is: everything that separates inside from outside — roof, walls, windows, doors, foundation waterproofing
- Analogy: "Your building envelope is your home's raincoat. In the PNW, it works harder than almost anywhere in the country."
- Where to find key components: attic insulation (visible from attic access), wall insulation (check at outlets on exterior walls), weatherstripping (doors and windows)
- Cross-reference: [M4-BE:L1 content]

> **PNW Regional Note:**
> PNW homes receive 36-60 inches of rain annually depending on location. The marine climate (Climate Zone 4C for western OR/WA) means persistent moisture, moderate temperatures, and rare hard freezes. Your envelope's primary job is keeping water out while letting moisture vapor escape. Gutters and downspouts are structural protection, not cosmetic features.
>
> *Applies to: Both OR and WA*

**1.6 How Systems Interact**
- Structure supports envelope; envelope protects structure
- Plumbing and electrical penetrate the envelope — each penetration is a potential leak point
- HVAC performance depends entirely on envelope quality — a new furnace in a poorly insulated house wastes energy and money
- Seismic events stress all systems simultaneously

**5.2 Moisture Management Assessment**

**"Is My Crawlspace Properly Managed?"**

Key observations for homeowners:
1. **Ground cover:** Is there a vapor barrier (plastic sheeting) covering the dirt floor? It should cover 100% of the ground with seams overlapped and sealed.
2. **Ventilation:** Are there foundation vents, and are they open/unblocked? (OR and WA both require crawlspace ventilation or conditioned crawlspace design per IRC [CODE-01].)
3. **Standing water:** Any pooling water after rain? This requires professional assessment.
4. **Wood condition:** Any visible rot, fungal growth (white or brown), or insect damage on floor joists or sill plates?
5. **Smell:** Musty or earthy smell indicates excessive moisture and likely mold.

> **GATE — Verify Before Proceeding**
>
> Before entering a crawlspace:
> - [ ] Wear appropriate PPE: N95 respirator, gloves, long sleeves, eye protection
> - [ ] Have a flashlight and phone
> - [ ] Tell someone you are going into the crawlspace
> - [ ] Do NOT enter if standing water is present (electrical shock risk from submerged wiring)
>
> **If standing water is present:** Call a licensed contractor. Do not enter.

## Part 9: Safety Gate Integration

> **SC-ADV (GATE):** All educational content in this module presents engineering principles and code requirements as technical knowledge. BCM does not advocate for specific legislative positions, code changes, or regulatory outcomes. Content describes what codes require and why, without taking positions on whether requirements should be more or less stringent.

> **SC-PER (GATE):** The homeowner guide framework (Part 4) explicitly delineates professional requirements at every decision point. BLOCK callouts are placed at every threshold where professional involvement is required. No content encourages unpermitted work or work beyond the reader's qualification level.

> **DIY Boundaries (GATE):** Clear delineation of professional vs. DIY is enforced at every audience level:
> - **L1:** Observe, maintain, hire wisely. All repairs beyond basic maintenance require professionals.
> - **L2:** Permitted DIY within legal scope. Stop at license boundaries. Never electrical panel, gas, structural, or sewer main.
> - **L3:** Work under licensed supervision only. Apprenticeship requirements are legal requirements.
> - **L4:** Work within scope of license. PE/SE stamp requirements clearly identified.
> - **L5:** Professional liability and stamp requirements explicitly stated.

Every BCM document must pass this checklist before final status:

| Check | Requirement | Pass Criteria |
