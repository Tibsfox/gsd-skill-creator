## 1. Democratic Fan Governance

Westercon's governance model is one of the purest expressions of democratic self-rule in voluntary community organizations. Every aspect of the convention -- from where it will be held to how large the font on name badges must be -- is governed by bylaws that are amended at annual business meetings open to every attending member [1][3].

This is not governance by committee or governance by executive. It is governance by parliament -- a direct democratic process where any member may speak, propose amendments, and vote. The rules of engagement are Robert's Rules of Order, the same parliamentary procedure used by the US Congress, but applied to questions like whether the convention should permit Australian bids and what minimum badge font size constitutes legibility [1][3].

The democratic ethos is fundamental to Westercon's identity. Science fiction fandom has always been a community of people who believe they can build better systems. The Westercon Business Meeting is where that belief is enacted -- where fans govern themselves according to rules they wrote, debated, amended, and ratified [1][3].

## 2. The LASFS Service Mark

The name "Westercon" is a registered service mark of the Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society, Inc. (LASFS). This simple legal fact underpins the entire governance structure [2][3].

LASFS retains three specific responsibilities:

1. **Archiving bylaws and business meeting minutes.** LASFS is the institutional memory of Westercon's governance. When individual conventions end and their committees dissolve, the bylaws and minutes survive at LASFS [2][3].

2. **Providing a prototype site-selection ballot** to each administering Westercon. This ensures procedural consistency across years, even as the organizing committee changes [3].

3. **Acting as the ultimate failsafe.** If a Westercon committee declares itself unable to fulfill its duties, the LASFS Board of Directors determines alternate arrangements. LASFS is the safety net of last resort [1][3].

The service mark relationship means that no individual or group can claim ownership of Westercon. The convention belongs to the community through LASFS. This is fan governance encoded in trademark law -- a creative use of legal infrastructure to protect community assets [2][3].

## 3. The Bylaws System

The Westercon Bylaws are the constitution of the convention. They are not guidelines or suggestions. They are binding rules that each annual convention is required to enforce and publish [1][3].

The bylaws cover:
- Geographic eligibility for convention sites
- Site selection voting procedures
- Bid requirements (officers, legal entity, hotel, non-profit status)
- Membership classes (attending, supporting, child, at minimum)
- Badge specifications (24-point bold type minimum for pre-registered names)
- Business Meeting procedures
- Failsafe provisions for committee failure
- Amendment process (two-year ratification cycle)
- Relationship to LASFS

The bylaws are published in at least one progress report and in the program book of each Westercon. This ensures that every member can access the rules that govern the convention [1][3].

## 4. Key Bylaw Provisions

### 4.1 Geographic Eligibility

Sites must be located on the North American continent west of the 104th meridian west, or in the state of Hawaii. This boundary runs through western Nebraska, Kansas, Oklahoma, and Texas, encompassing the entire western third of the continent [1][3].

The 1998 Australia amendment -- proposed by Down Under Fan Fund delegate Terry Frost -- adds an escape clause: Australian sites are permitted if either Australia or the United States annexes the other. When fans at the 2003 Business Meeting attempted to repeal this provision, the motion failed. The clause remains in the bylaws as a monument to fannish whimsy encoded in parliamentary procedure [1][3].

### 4.2 Badge Font Size

Pre-registered member names must appear on name badges in no less than 24-point bold type. This provision is in the actual bylaws. It is not a guideline. It is a constitutional requirement. In any other organization, badge font size would be an operational detail. In Westercon, it is law [3].

### 4.3 Membership Classes

Each Westercon must offer at minimum supporting and attending memberships. Supporting members can vote in site selection (participating in governance) without physically attending the convention. This ensures that geographic distance does not disenfranchise engaged community members [1][3].

### 4.4 Program Book Publication

The Westercon Bylaws must be published in at least one progress report and in the program book. This is the transparency requirement -- the convention's constitution must be publicly available to every member [3].

## 5. The Business Meeting

The Business Meeting is the legislative body of Westercon. Open to all attending members, it is typically held on the second day of the convention and follows Robert's Rules of Order [1][3].

At the Business Meeting, members may:
- Propose bylaw amendments (which require first passage and then ratification at the following year's meeting)
- Hear reports from the standing committees
- Vote on pending amendments that received first passage the previous year
- Raise questions and concerns about convention operations
- Participate in site selection announcements

The Business Meeting is where the democratic rubber meets the parliamentary road. It is simultaneously earnest (real decisions are made, real bylaws are amended) and performative (Robert's Rules provide a framework for structured debate that fans, as a community, genuinely enjoy) [1][3].

## 6. Site Selection Mechanics

The site selection process is the most consequential act of Westercon governance. It determines where the convention will be held two years hence [1][3].

The process:
1. **Bids are submitted** by groups meeting the minimum requirements (Chair, Treasurer, organizing instrument, hotel letter, non-profit evidence).
2. **Bids campaign** at the current Westercon through room parties, flyers, and presentations.
3. **Members vote** using a preferential ballot. "None of the Above" is always an option.
4. **Results are counted** using an elimination system: if no bid has a majority, the lowest-performing bid is eliminated and its votes redistributed.
5. **Results are announced** at the Business Meeting, typically on the third day.

The system is designed to produce consensus. The preferential ballot ensures that the winning bid has broad support, not just a plurality. "None of the Above" provides a safety valve: if no bid is acceptable, the community can reject all options and trigger the failsafe [1][3].

## 7. The Failsafe Provisions

Section 3.16 of the Westercon Bylaws addresses the scenario where site selection fails -- where no bid wins, or where no bids are filed at all [1][3].

The cascade:
1. If no bid wins site selection, the Business Meeting may select a site by three-fourths supermajority vote.
2. If the Business Meeting cannot reach a three-fourths consensus, LASFS selects the site.
3. If a selected convention committee subsequently declares itself unable to fulfill its duties, the LASFS Board of Directors determines alternate arrangements.

This three-tier failsafe ensures that Westercon can survive any single point of failure. Even if no one bids, the Business Meeting can intervene. Even if the Business Meeting deadlocks, LASFS can step in. Even if the selected committee collapses, LASFS provides the safety net [1][3].

The failsafe was designed for rare emergencies. In the 2020s, it became the standard operating procedure. When the failsafe is invoked more often than normal site selection, the system is signaling that its fundamental assumptions -- a reliable supply of volunteer committees -- no longer hold [1][3].

## 8. Kevin Standlee and Governance Stewardship

Kevin Standlee led the committee that redrafted the Westercon Bylaws in the 1990s and is effectively the primary author of the current version. His contributions to Westercon governance include [1][3]:

- Drafting the modern bylaws framework
- Maintaining the westercon.org website as the authoritative public record
- Chairing Westercon 74 in Tonopah, Nevada (2022) -- one of the last independently organized Westercons
- Serving as the institutional memory for governance procedures across decades
- Stewarding the site selection process through increasingly difficult bid cycles

Standlee's role illustrates both the strength and vulnerability of volunteer-dependent institutions. His expertise is irreplaceable. His availability is not guaranteed. When governance stewardship depends on the energy of individual volunteers, the institution is one retirement away from a knowledge gap [1][3].

## 9. Relationship to WSFS and Worldcon

Westercon and Worldcon share governance DNA. Both use:
- Rotating-host, bid-and-vote site selection
- Business meetings open to all members
- Robert's Rules of Order
- Preferential balloting
- Supporting memberships that enable remote governance participation

Westercon is often described as the "West Coast regional" to Worldcon's "global" event. This is accurate but incomplete. Westercon is also a governance laboratory -- a smaller-scale system where procedural innovations can be tested before being proposed at the larger Worldcon level [1][5][6].

The two institutions have directly overlapped at several points in history. Combined Westercon/Worldcon events in the 1950s demonstrated the interoperability of their governance models. WSFS (the World Science Fiction Society) and the Westercon bylaws share structural assumptions about how fan communities should govern themselves [1][5][6].

## 10. The Amendment Process

Bylaw amendments follow a two-year ratification cycle [1][3]:

1. **Year 1:** An amendment is proposed at the Business Meeting. If it receives majority support, it passes as a "first passage" motion.
2. **Year 2:** The amendment is presented at the following year's Business Meeting for ratification. A majority vote ratifies it into the bylaws.

This two-year cycle provides stability. No single year's Business Meeting can make permanent changes to the bylaws. Every amendment must survive the scrutiny of two separate groups of members, separated by a year of reflection and debate [3].

The two-year cycle is also the mechanism governing the proposed retirement. The bylaws repeal motion received first passage at Westercon 77 (July 2025). It must be ratified at Westercon 78 (July 2026). If ratified, the bylaws -- and with them, the formal institution of Westercon -- wind down after Westercon 80 (2028) [3][9].

## 11. Governance as Living System

Westercon's governance is not a static document. It is a living system -- amended, interpreted, and applied by changing groups of volunteers across decades. The bylaws have been modified dozens of times. The Business Meeting has adapted its procedures. The relationship between LASFS and the annual conventions has evolved [1][3].

What makes this governance model remarkable is not its elegance but its persistence. For nearly eight decades, a community of volunteers has governed itself through democratic procedures, maintained institutional continuity through a non-profit parent organization, and sustained a convention that serves an entire continent. The fact that this system may soon vote itself out of existence does not diminish the achievement. It may, in fact, be the governance model's final proof of concept: a system that can choose its own orderly retirement is a system that works [1][3].
