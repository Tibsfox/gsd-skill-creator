## 1. Not Failure

The most important thing to understand about Westercon's proposed retirement is that it is not a failure. It is the deliberate choice of a community that has spent 77 years governing itself democratically and now faces the question of whether to continue or to end with the same dignity with which it began [1][9].

Westercon taught the West Coast how to organize conventions. It incubated furry fandom. It sustained a democratic governance tradition that influenced every regional convention in the western United States. The question it faces is not "was it worth it?" -- the answer to that is self-evident in the 200+ conventions its cultural DNA helped create. The question is "can an institution end well?" [1][9].

This module documents the factors driving the decline and the mechanics of the proposed retirement, with the respect that a 77-year-old institution deserves.

---

## 2. The COVID Disruption

The COVID-19 pandemic did not cause Westercon's decline. But it accelerated it with devastating efficiency [1][9].

Westercon 73, originally scheduled for Seattle in July 2020, was postponed to 2021. Then it went virtual. Then the organizing committee disbanded entirely in April 2021, unable to sustain the organizational burden through a second year of uncertainty. LASFS assumed official Westercon functions and combined them with Loscon 47 in November 2021 [1][9].

Westercon 74 was similarly postponed and disrupted. The cascading postponements and cancellations broke the continuity chain that the traveling model depends on. Each annual Westercon draws organizational momentum from the previous year's event: volunteers are recruited, bids are promoted, institutional knowledge is transferred informally. When two consecutive years are disrupted, the chain snaps [1][9].

For a fixed-venue convention like Norwescon, COVID was survivable. The organizational infrastructure -- the hotel relationship, the volunteer corps, the programming committee -- was rooted in place. It could hibernate and then resume. For a traveling convention, the infrastructure was not rooted anywhere. When the traveling stopped, the infrastructure dissolved [1][10].

## 3. The Bid Pipeline Collapse

The post-COVID years revealed that the pipeline of willing host committees had dried up. The pattern is stark:

**Westercon 75 (2023):** The convention chair cancelled the event due to "a cascade of circumstances." LASFS and Loscon assumed Westercon duties, hosting a combined event. The cancellation was not from lack of interest among attendees but from organizational collapse at the committee level [1][9].

**Westercon 76 (2024):** No eligible bids were filed by the site selection deadline. A Utah group received 55 of 58 preference votes -- overwhelming community support -- but was technically ineligible because it lacked the required non-profit documentation. The Business Meeting selected them anyway under the Section 3.16 failsafe provision, waiving the eligibility requirements. This was governance adapting to emergency [1][3][9].

**Westercon 77 (2025):** No bids were filed at all. The Westercon 75 Business Meeting awarded the convention to Kevin Standlee and Lisa Hayes as placeholder organizers. It was eventually transferred to BayCon 2025, held in Santa Clara as a co-hosted event rather than an independent Westercon [1][3][9].

**Westercons 78 and 79:** Both awarded to BayCon in Santa Clara. At this point, Westercon had effectively ceased to exist as an independent convention. It survived only by being absorbed into an existing event [1][3][9].

The failsafe provisions in the bylaws were designed for rare emergencies. When the failsafe becomes the standard operating procedure, the system is signaling that its fundamental assumptions no longer hold [1][3].

## 4. The Volunteer Question

The root cause of the bid pipeline collapse is the volunteer question. Westercon's traveling model requires a new volunteer corps every year -- a group of people willing to invest two or more years of unpaid labor organizing a major convention from near-zero [1].

The volunteer pool contracted for several reasons:

**Aging community.** The core of Westercon's volunteer base is the generation that built West Coast fandom in the 1970s and 1980s. As this generation ages, the physical and emotional demands of convention organizing become harder to sustain [1][9].

**Competition for volunteer labor.** Other conventions, fan organizations, and community activities compete for the same pool of dedicated fans. A volunteer who commits to running a Westercon has limited capacity for other projects [1].

**Insufficient recruitment of younger fans.** The pipeline from attendee to volunteer to organizer did not replenish itself at the rate needed to sustain annual convention production. Younger fans had more options for community engagement -- online communities, local gaming groups, media conventions -- and fewer incentives to invest in the demanding labor of convention organizing [1][9].

**Burnout.** Convention organizing is exhausting. For a traveling convention, the exhaustion is compounded by the fact that last year's burned-out organizers have no institutional obligation to help this year's team. There is no organizational continuity to distribute the burden [1].

## 5. Competition and Alternatives

The landscape of fan gathering changed fundamentally between 1948 and 2025 [1]:

**Internet-mediated community.** The social function that conventions once uniquely served -- connecting geographically dispersed fans -- is now served by online communities, social media, Discord servers, and virtual events. The question "how do I find other people who like what I like?" no longer requires traveling to a hotel on July 4th weekend [1].

**Media conventions.** San Diego Comic-Con, Emerald City Comic Con, and their counterparts offer spectacle, celebrity access, and mainstream cultural cachet that literary SF conventions cannot match. These events compete for the same fans' attention, time, and money [1].

**Fixed-venue regional conventions.** Conventions like Norwescon, OryCon, BayCon, and Loscon offer the same community experience as Westercon -- literary programming, room parties, art shows -- without the organizational overhead of annual bootstrapping. Why bid for a Westercon when your local convention already provides what you need? [1][10].

**Economic pressures.** Hotel costs, travel expenses, and registration fees have risen faster than the discretionary budgets of the fans who attend. A four-day convention in a city you don't live in is an expensive proposition. For younger fans -- the demographic needed to replenish the volunteer pipeline -- the economics are especially challenging [1].

## 6. The Attendance Trajectory

Precise attendance data for Westercon is incomplete, but the trajectory is discernible:

| Era | Pattern |
|-----|---------|
| 1948-1960s | Growth from 77 attendees; establishing the format |
| 1970s-1980s | Expansion alongside the SF fandom boom |
| 1990s-2000s | Stable but not growing; peak of organizational capacity |
| 2010s | Moderate attendance (700-950 range for well-organized events) |
| 2020s | COVID disruption; merged events; declining independent attendance |

The numbers tell a story of an institution that served its purpose brilliantly during the era it was built for and then faced an environment that no longer required what it offered. This is not decline in the pejorative sense. It is the natural evolution of any institution whose founding conditions have changed [1].

## 7. The Allen-Deneroff Assessment

Kayla Allen and Linda Deneroff, longtime Westercon administrators with deep institutional knowledge, provided the assessment that catalyzed the retirement discussion. Their statement was direct: "Westercon simply doesn't have enough interest anymore, and rather than just have it fizzle out completely, we should try to organize an orderly shutdown by repealing the Bylaws and handing the convention's charter back to LASFS" [9].

This assessment deserves careful reading. It does not say Westercon failed. It does not say fandom has declined. It says that *Westercon* -- the specific institution, with its specific organizational model -- no longer has sufficient community interest to sustain itself. The communities it spawned thrive. The convention itself does not [9].

The proposal for "an orderly shutdown" rather than letting the convention "fizzle out completely" reflects a governing philosophy: if an institution must end, let it end with intention rather than entropy. Let the community make the choice, through the democratic governance process it has sustained for eight decades. Let the final act be a vote, not a default [9].

## 8. The Bylaws Repeal Motion

The mechanism for Westercon's retirement is the repeal of its own bylaws -- the same document that has governed the convention since its formalization. The motion follows the standard amendment process [1][3][9]:

1. **First passage:** The bylaws repeal motion was submitted to the Westercon 77 Business Meeting at BayCon 2025 in Santa Clara. It received first passage on July 6, 2025.
2. **Ratification required:** Under the two-year amendment cycle, the motion must be ratified at Westercon 78 (BayCon 2026, July 3-6, 2026).
3. **If ratified:** The bylaws are repealed. The "Westercon" service mark reverts fully to LASFS.

The use of the standard amendment process for the convention's own dissolution is fitting. Westercon lived by Robert's Rules, and it would die by Robert's Rules. The democratic process that governed the convention for 77 years would govern its ending [1][3][9].

---

## 9. The Ratification Timeline

If the bylaws repeal is ratified at Westercon 78 in July 2026, the consequences unfold as follows [1][3][9]:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| July 6, 2025 | First passage of bylaws repeal at Westercon 77 |
| July 3-6, 2026 | Ratification vote at Westercon 78 (BayCon 2026) |
| 2027 | Westercon 79 held (already selected), no site selection or business meeting |
| 2028 | Westercon 80 held (already selected), final convention under current bylaws |
| Post-2028 | "Westercon" name reverts to LASFS disposition |

Westercons 79 and 80 would still be held because they have already been selected. But they would not conduct site selection or hold a business meeting. They would be final performances -- the convention running out its existing commitments without creating new ones [1][3][9].

After Westercon 80, the name would belong entirely to LASFS. What LASFS chooses to do with it -- preserve it, license it, retire it permanently -- would be a matter for the LASFS Board of Directors [1][3].

## 10. The Lifecycle Model

Westercon's trajectory maps to a standard institutional lifecycle:

```
1948-1960s: FOUNDING
  Innovation, establishing the model, early growth
  |
1960s-1990s: GROWTH & MATURITY
  Peak organizational capacity, cultural radiation
  |
2000s-2010s: PLATEAU
  Stable but not growing, structural vulnerabilities emerging
  |
2020s: DECLINE
  COVID disruption, bid failures, volunteer exhaustion
  |
2025-2028: SUNSET (proposed)
  Orderly retirement through democratic process
```

This lifecycle is not unique to conventions. Technology platforms, community organizations, businesses, and governments all follow similar arcs. What is unique to Westercon is the conscious choice to manage the sunset phase through the same democratic governance that managed the growth phase. The institution is choosing its own ending [1][9].

## 11. What It Means to End Well

Westercon's proposed retirement raises a question that applies far beyond science fiction fandom: what does it mean for an institution to end well?

The answer, as embodied by the Westercon retirement process, involves several principles [9]:

**Acknowledge reality.** Allen and Deneroff's assessment was honest: the interest is no longer there. Pretending otherwise would be denial, not loyalty.

**Use the institution's own tools.** The retirement is processed through the same bylaws, business meetings, and amendment procedures that governed the convention's 77-year operation. The process validates the governance model.

**Honor existing commitments.** Westercons 79 and 80 will be held because they were already selected. The convention does not abandon its obligations.

**Preserve the legacy.** Returning the service mark to LASFS ensures that the name and the institutional memory are preserved by the oldest continuously operating SF club in the world.

**Let the community decide.** The retirement is a vote, not a decree. If the community at Westercon 78 decides not to ratify the repeal, Westercon continues. The democratic process is respected to the end.

Not everything that was great needs to continue. The furry fandom that was born in a Westercon hotel room runs 200+ conventions worldwide. The governance DNA of the Westercon Business Meeting lives in every fan-run convention that uses Robert's Rules. The organizational capacity that Westercon built across the western United States sustains dozens of active conventions. The seed has grown. The tree may rest [1][9].
