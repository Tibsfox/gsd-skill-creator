## 1. The Complete Story

WCN and NWC together tell the complete story of West Coast SF convention culture. They are not competitors or alternatives. They are complementary architectures -- two solutions to the same problem (sustaining fan community), each optimized for different constraints [1][10].

Westercon (WCN) is the original. Founded in 1948, it chose the traveling model: a different city every year, democratic site selection, rotating volunteer committees. This gave it geographic reach across the entire western half of the continent but denied it the organizational stability that comes from putting down roots [1].

Norwescon (NWC) is the descendant. Founded in 1978, it chose the fixed-venue model: one hotel, one city, one volunteer organization. This gave it institutional depth -- 30+ years of venue knowledge, volunteer continuity, programming refinement -- but limited its geographic reach to the Pacific Northwest [10].

Together, they represent the complete design space for community infrastructure: distributed for reach, centralized for stability. The question is not which is better. The question is what each reveals about the conditions under which communities thrive [1][10].

## 2. The Fixed Node and the Traveling Signal

In networking terms, Norwescon is a server and Westercon is a packet [10][1].

Norwescon is a fixed node in the network. It has a stable address (DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport, 18740 International Blvd, SeaTac WA 98188). It is always available at the same location. Clients (attendees) know where to find it. It accumulates state (institutional memory, volunteer experience, hotel relationships) over time. It serves the local network (Pacific Northwest fandom) with high reliability [10].

Westercon is a packet -- a unit of information that travels the network, carrying fandom culture from node to node. Its address changes every year. It adapts its payload (convention programming, local flavor, GoH selection) to each destination while maintaining the protocol (bylaws, traditions, governance model). It does not accumulate state; it distributes state [1].

When the packet stops traveling, the information it carried does not disappear. It has already been delivered -- to the conventions it spawned, the communities it connected, the traditions it established. Westercon's retirement does not delete the data. It retires the transport mechanism [1].

## 3. Convention as Distributed System

Westercon's architecture maps directly to distributed systems concepts:

| Distributed System Concept | Westercon Equivalent |
|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Protocol | Bylaws |
| Node | Annual convention instance |
| Service discovery | Site selection / bid process |
| State management | None (stateless by design) |
| Failover | Section 3.16 (LASFS failsafe) |
| Certificate authority | LASFS (service mark holder) |
| Node bootstrapping | Host committee formation |
| Network partition | COVID disruption (2020-2021) |
| Node exhaustion | Volunteer pipeline collapse |
| Graceful shutdown | Bylaws repeal motion |

The parallel is not metaphorical. Westercon literally operates as a distributed system: independent nodes (conventions) executing a shared protocol (bylaws) with a central authority (LASFS) providing identity and failover. The system's current failure mode -- insufficient nodes to maintain the network -- is exactly what happens when a distributed system's node pool drops below the minimum viable density [1][3].

## 4. Convention as Rosetta Stone

Westercon functions as a Rosetta Stone -- a translation layer between city-specific fandoms [1][5].

A fan from Portland and a fan from Phoenix speak different local dialects. Portland fandom has its own conventions (OryCon), its own clubs, its own gathering places. Phoenix fandom has its own. When both fandoms send representatives to a Westercon, the convention translates between them. It provides a shared vocabulary (the traditions), a shared grammar (the bylaws), and a shared context (the convention experience) [1].

This translation function is what the Rosetta Stone concept means in the Research series: a system that preserves the core signal while adapting to different contexts. Westercon's core signal -- democratic governance, volunteer labor, literary focus, community building -- remained constant across cities, decades, and host committees. The local adaptation -- this city's hotels, this city's fandom, this city's July weather -- varied without breaking the protocol [1].

The Rosetta Stone connection works at multiple levels:

- **Between fandoms:** Portland fandom ↔ Westercon protocol ↔ Phoenix fandom
- **Between eras:** 1950s fandom ↔ Westercon traditions ↔ 2020s fandom
- **Between institutions:** LASFS governance ↔ Westercon bylaws ↔ regional convention governance
- **Between communities:** SF literary fandom ↔ Westercon room parties ↔ furry fandom

## 5. The Lifecycle as Research Subject

Westercon's trajectory -- founding, growth, maturity, decline, proposed sunset -- is a case study in institutional lifecycle that connects to multiple domains in the Research series [1][9]:

**Technology lifecycles.** Every technology platform follows a similar arc: innovation, adoption, maturity, decline, replacement. The question "when should a system be retired?" applies equally to Westercon and to any software platform that has been superseded by better-adapted alternatives [1].

**Community lifecycles.** Communities form around shared needs, grow as they attract members, stabilize when they reach carrying capacity, and decline when the needs change or better alternatives emerge. Westercon's lifecycle tracks this pattern precisely [1][9].

**Governance lifecycles.** Democratic governance systems face specific lifecycle challenges: the energy required to sustain participatory democracy, the difficulty of succession planning in volunteer organizations, the tension between institutional memory and fresh perspectives [1][3].

**Cultural lifecycles.** Cultural movements are born, flourish, influence other movements, and eventually become part of the background. Westercon is no longer a movement; it is part of the background. Its influence is everywhere, but its institutional form may no longer be needed [1].

## 6. The Mesh Networking Parallel

Westercon's architecture parallels a mesh network in specific ways [1][3]:

- Each node (convention) maintains the protocol (bylaws) independently
- Nodes rely on sufficient density to maintain the network
- When node density drops below threshold, the mesh fragments
- The protocol survives even when the network fails (bylaws persist at LASFS)

The mesh networking parallel extends to the question of resilience. A mesh network is resilient against individual node failure but vulnerable to systemic reduction in node count. Westercon survived individual committee collapses (the failsafe handled them) but could not survive the systemic reduction in volunteer committees willing to host [1][3].

Compare this to the BRC (Burning Man) regional burn network, where each regional burn is a node in a distributed community. Both Westercon and the regional burn network operate as distributed systems where each node bootstraps from shared principles. The difference is that the regional burn network is growing (new regionals added regularly) while Westercon's network is contracting (fewer cities willing to host) [1].

## 7. The Trust Network

Westercon's community operates on a web of trust that mirrors the trust models explored elsewhere in the Research series [1]:

**Who vouches for the bid committee?** Site selection is fundamentally a trust question. When fans vote for a bid, they are expressing trust that the bid committee can deliver a convention. The committee's track record, the community members who support it, and the quality of its presentation all contribute to trust assessment [1][3].

**Who runs the room parties?** The room party ecosystem is a self-organized trust network. Parties are hosted by groups that have earned reputations over years of convention attendance. The furry parties at Westercon in the 1980s grew because Merlino and O'Riley had earned trust within the broader SF fandom community [1][7][8].

**Who maintains the protocol?** The trust in Westercon's governance system rests on specific individuals -- Kevin Standlee, the LASFS board, longtime Business Meeting participants -- whose sustained engagement provides the continuity that the rotating structure otherwise lacks [1][3].

This is trust maintained by face-to-face interaction, not credentials. It is the same trust model that sustains Burning Man camps, open source projects, and any community where reputation is earned through participation rather than certification [1].

## 8. The Packet-Switched Convention

The metaphor that best captures Westercon's architecture: it is a packet-switched convention.

In a circuit-switched network, a dedicated connection is maintained between endpoints. In a packet-switched network, data is broken into packets that travel independently through the network, reassembled at the destination. The network is stateless; the protocol handles routing [1].

Westercon is packet-switched:
- Each convention is a packet -- a self-contained unit of fandom culture
- The packet is routed to a new destination (city) each year
- The protocol (bylaws) handles addressing (site selection) and error correction (failsafe)
- No persistent connection is maintained between sender and receiver
- The packet adapts its payload (programming, local flavor) to each destination

Norwescon is circuit-switched:
- A dedicated connection exists between the convention and its venue
- The connection is maintained year-round (hotel relationship, volunteer corps)
- State is accumulated and preserved
- The circuit provides reliable, predictable service

Both models have their strengths. Packet-switched networks are resilient and flexible. Circuit-switched networks are efficient and reliable. The internet replaced the telephone network because packet-switching scaled better. Westercon is being replaced by fixed-venue conventions because circuit-switching sustains community better when node density drops below threshold [1][10].
