## 1. The Seed Convention

Westercon's most profound legacy is not what it was but what it spawned. Over nearly eight decades, the convention served as an incubator for communities, institutions, and traditions that grew far beyond its own scope. It is the seed convention -- the original from which dozens of descendant institutions drew their organizational DNA [1][5].

The mechanism was the traveling model itself. By visiting a different city each year, Westercon deposited organizational capacity wherever it went. Fans who helped run a Westercon in Portland gained the experience to start their own local convention. Fans who attended a Westercon in Sacramento brought ideas home to their own communities. The convention was a pollinator, carrying the pollen of organized fandom from flower to flower [1].

This is why Westercon's cultural impact cannot be measured by its own attendance numbers. Its impact is measured by the conventions it inspired, the communities it connected, and the traditions it established. By that measure, Westercon is one of the most influential science fiction conventions ever held [1][5].

## 2. The Birth of Furry Fandom

The most significant and most thoroughly documented cultural contribution of Westercon is its role as the birthplace of organized furry fandom. This is not hyperbole. The documented historical record traces the origin of furry fandom as a self-identified community directly to Westercon room parties in the mid-1980s [7][8][11].

The key figures were Mark Merlino (known in fandom as Sy Sable) and Rod O'Riley (Vinson Mink). Both were co-founders of the Cartoon/Fantasy Organization (C/FO), a Los Angeles-based club that had held its first meeting at the LASFS clubhouse in 1977. The C/FO was dedicated to Japanese animation and anthropomorphic art -- the crossroads where anime fandom and furry fandom would eventually diverge into separate communities [7][8].

Merlino and O'Riley recognized that there was a constituency within science fiction fandom for anthropomorphic animal characters -- not just as a literary trope but as a creative and social identity. The question was where to find each other. The answer was Westercon's room party culture [7][8][11].

## 3. The Prancing Skiltaire

In July 1985, at Westercon 38 in Sacramento, Merlino and O'Riley hosted a room party in Sheldon Linker and Toni Poper's room at the Red Lion Inn. The party was called "The Prancing Skiltaire" -- named after Merlino's house, which was itself named for a mink-like alien species Merlino had created, referencing the Prancing Pony Inn from *The Lord of the Rings* [7][8][11].

The party screened a fan-compiled version of *Animalympics* -- an animated film about anthropomorphic animal athletes competing in the Olympics. Attendees were people who shared an interest in anthropomorphic characters: artists, writers, costumers, and fans who saw themselves in animal characters or found creative expression through them [7][8][11].

This was the first openly publicized funny-animal fan party at a science fiction convention. The significance is not that anthropomorphic art was new -- it was as old as storytelling itself. The significance is that a self-identified *community* coalesced. People who had thought they were alone in their interest discovered that they were not. The room party was the discovery event [7][8][11].

## 4. From Room Party to Convention

The timeline from Westercon room party to independent convention is one of the most documented examples of subcultural genesis in fandom history:

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1985 | Westercon 38, Sacramento | "The Prancing Skiltaire" -- first publicized furry fan party at an SF convention |
| 1986 | Westercon 39, San Diego | First party explicitly called a "furry party" |
| 1987 | Westercon, Oakland | "Das FurryParty" -- annual party had grown significantly |
| 1987 | BayCon, San Jose | BayCon Furry Party attracted proposals for furry fanzines and a dedicated convention |
| 1989 | ConFurence Zero, Costa Mesa | World's first dedicated furry convention, 65 attendees |
| 2024 | Global | 200+ furry conventions worldwide, largest (MFF) draws 14,000+ |

Mark Merlino passed away on February 20, 2024, after battling cancer. The community he helped birth in a Sacramento hotel room had grown into a global phenomenon. From 77 people at the first Westercon in 1948 to 14,000+ at a single furry convention in 2024 -- the seed crystal and the lattice [7][8][11][12].

The Westercon-to-furry-fandom pipeline illustrates a general principle: conventions are incubators. The room party format -- informal, self-organized, low-barrier -- provides exactly the conditions for new communities to emerge. Westercon's room party culture was not designed to create furry fandom. It was designed to create the conditions where *any* community could coalesce. Furry fandom is what grew [7][8].

## 5. The Other Change of Hobbit

In 1977, attendees who ran a portable bookstore at Westercon went on to found The Other Change of Hobbit -- a science fiction bookstore in Berkeley, California that operated until 2016. It was one of the most beloved specialty bookstores in the United States, a gathering place for Bay Area fandom, and a direct descendant of Westercon's dealers room tradition [1][5].

The portable bookstore model -- fans bringing books to sell at conventions -- was common in mid-century fandom. What made The Other Change of Hobbit different was the leap from temporary venue to permanent institution. The fans who ran the bookstore at Westercon realized that the demand for curated science fiction literature existed year-round, not just during a four-day convention. The convention was the proof of concept; the bookstore was the production deployment [1].

## 6. Convention Governance Template

Westercon's governance model -- rotating host, bid-and-vote site selection, business meeting, LASFS failsafe -- served as a template for numerous regional conventions across the western United States. Conventions that adopted elements of the Westercon governance model include regional cons that followed its democratic, volunteer-run, community-governed approach [1][3].

The specific innovations that propagated:
- **Preferential ballot for site selection** -- ensuring broad consensus rather than plurality rule
- **Supporting membership** -- enabling governance participation without physical attendance
- **Published bylaws** -- transparency as a constitutional requirement
- **Business meeting open to all members** -- direct democracy rather than representative governance
- **Two-year amendment cycle** -- stability through deliberation

These are not obvious choices. Many conventions are governed by a board of directors, a fixed organizing committee, or a commercial entity. Westercon's fully democratic model -- where any attending member can propose and vote on bylaw amendments -- is unusual, and its influence on West Coast convention culture is significant [1][3].

## 7. Traditions That Traveled

Westercon's traveling nature meant that its traditions traveled too. Elements of convention culture that originated or were popularized at Westercon include:

- **The masquerade:** Costume contests became a standard feature of fan conventions, and Westercon was among the conventions that established the format.
- **The regency dance:** A structured social dance form that became a staple at many fan conventions.
- **Room party culture:** The informal, self-organized evening social events that are now ubiquitous at fan conventions.
- **The art show and dealers room:** The dual marketplace -- visual art for viewing/purchase and books/merchandise for sale -- that defines the commercial layer of fan conventions.
- **Ice cream social:** Introduced at Westercon by Mike Glyer in 1978, borrowed from Loscon. A deliberately low-key social event.
- **First Night Icebreaker:** The informal meet-the-guests event on the first evening, setting the tone for the entire convention [1].

Each of these traditions was carried from city to city by Westercon's traveling model, adopted by local fan communities, and integrated into their own conventions. The traveling convention was a tradition-distribution network [1].

## 8. The C/FO and Anime Fandom

The Cartoon/Fantasy Organization (C/FO), whose members were central to the early furry parties at Westercon, also played a role in the founding of North American anime fandom. The C/FO held its first meeting at the LASFS clubhouse in 1977 -- the same year that The Other Change of Hobbit was founded by Westercon bookstore operators [7][8].

The C/FO screened Japanese animation (anime) at a time when it was largely unknown in North America. Members of the C/FO attended Westercon and brought their interests with them. The cross-pollination between anime fandom, anthropomorphic art fandom, and science fiction fandom all happened within the Westercon ecosystem [7][8].

This is the incubator effect at work. Westercon did not set out to create furry fandom or anime fandom. It created the *conditions* -- room parties, social spaces, a traveling circuit connecting creative communities -- and let the communities self-organize [7][8].

## 9. Guests of Honor as Thesis Statements

Westercon's Guest of Honor tradition served as the convention's annual thesis statement. GoHs were chosen to represent the best of the western region's creative community -- authors, artists, scientists, and fan leaders. The GoH selection told the community what Westercon thought mattered in a given year [1][5].

The GoH tradition reinforced Westercon's literary identity. While media conventions celebrated actors and television personalities, Westercon celebrated writers, illustrators, and scientists. This was a deliberate positioning that distinguished fan-run conventions from commercial events [1][5].

Over seven decades, the GoH roster tells the story of West Coast speculative fiction: which authors were ascendant, which art forms were valued, which scientific questions captured the community's imagination. The roster is a cultural archive encoded in invitation [1][5].

## 10. The Cultural Radiation Map

Westercon's cultural contributions radiate outward in a pattern that resembles a root system more than a family tree:

```
WESTERCON (1948)
|
+-- Organizational capacity building
|   +-- Norwescon (1978, PNW)
|   +-- Orycon (1979, Portland)
|   +-- BayCon (1985, Bay Area)
|   +-- Loscon (1975, Los Angeles)
|   +-- Other regional conventions
|
+-- Furry fandom (1985-1989)
|   +-- ConFurence (1989, first dedicated convention)
|   +-- 200+ conventions worldwide (2024)
|   +-- Midwest FurFest (14,000+ attendees)
|
+-- Institutional models
|   +-- Democratic governance template
|   +-- Rotating-host model
|   +-- Room party tradition
|
+-- Cultural artifacts
|   +-- The Other Change of Hobbit (1977-2016)
|   +-- Regency dance tradition
|   +-- GoH tradition as community curation
```

The roots go deep. The branches go wide. And the convention that planted the seed may not need to continue for the forest to thrive [1][5][7][8].
