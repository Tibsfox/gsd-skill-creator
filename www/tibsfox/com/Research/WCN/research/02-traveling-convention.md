## 1. The Defining Feature

If you had to describe Westercon in a single sentence, it would be this: a science fiction convention that moves to a different city every year. This is not a logistical detail. It is the fundamental architectural decision that shapes everything else about the convention -- its governance, its culture, its strengths, and ultimately its vulnerabilities [1][3].

Unlike Norwescon (at the DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport since 1994), or Dragon Con (in Atlanta since 1987), or San Diego Comic-Con (in San Diego since 1970), Westercon has no home. Its home is the protocol -- the bylaws, the traditions, the shared understanding of what a Westercon is and how it should be run. The convention is a traveling instance of that protocol, instantiated in a new city every July [1][3].

This is a powerful model. It gives Westercon geographic reach that no fixed-venue convention can match. A fan in Honolulu and a fan in Boise can both attend Westercon without it being a "Seattle convention" or an "LA convention." But it also means that every year, the convention must be rebuilt from near-zero by a new team of volunteers in a new city with a new hotel [1].

## 2. How Site Selection Works

Westercon's site selection process is modeled on the World Science Fiction Convention's own bidding system. It is democratic, procedural, and -- in the way of all fan governance -- intensely participatory [1][3].

The basic mechanics:

- **Timeline:** Sites are selected two years in advance. At Westercon N, members vote on where Westercon N+2 should be held.
- **Eligibility to vote:** Attending and supporting members of the current Westercon may vote. Supporting memberships allow fans who cannot physically attend to participate in governance.
- **Geographic restriction:** Sites must be on the North American continent west of the 104th meridian west, or in the state of Hawaii.
- **The Australia clause:** A 1998 amendment, proposed by Down Under Fan Fund delegate Terry Frost, permits Australian sites -- but only if either Australia or the United States annexes the other. An attempt to repeal this provision at the 2003 Business Meeting failed. Fandom takes its jokes seriously [1][3].
- **Voting:** Preferential ballot. If no bid receives a majority on the first count, the lowest-performing bid is eliminated and its votes redistributed. "None of the Above" is always an option [1][3].

The two-year pipeline creates organizational runway. A bid that wins site selection in 2024 has until 2026 to organize the convention. This sounds like ample time, but for an all-volunteer organization building a major event from scratch, two years passes quickly [1][3].

## 3. The Bid Process

To bid for Westercon, a group must present:

- **At least two people:** A Chair and a Treasurer. These are the minimum named officers required to demonstrate organizational viability.
- **An organizing instrument:** Typically articles of incorporation or a similar document establishing the bid as a legal entity.
- **A hotel letter of intent:** Evidence that a suitable venue has been identified and is willing to host the convention.
- **Non-profit status evidence:** For US organizations, documentation of 501(c)(3) or similar status [1][3].

Bids are presented at the current Westercon. Bid committees typically run promotional room parties, distribute flyers, and campaign for member votes. The process is simultaneously democratic (anyone can bid) and demanding (the requirements filter out unserious proposals) [1][3].

The failsafe provision (Section 3.16) addresses the scenario where no bid wins. If site selection produces no winner, the Business Meeting may select a site by three-fourths vote. If the Meeting cannot decide, LASFS determines alternate arrangements. This failsafe has been invoked with increasing frequency in recent years -- a symptom of the declining volunteer pipeline [1][3].

## 4. Cities Across the Decades

Westercon's host cities span the western tier of North America:

| Decade | Representative Cities |
|--------|----------------------|
| 1940s-1950s | Los Angeles, San Francisco, Seattle |
| 1960s-1970s | Portland, San Diego, Phoenix, Oakland |
| 1980s-1990s | Sacramento, San Jose, Honolulu, Boise |
| 2000s-2010s | Seattle, Salt Lake City, Las Vegas, San Jose |
| 2020s | Tonopah (NV), Salt Lake City, Santa Clara (co-hosted with BayCon) |

The geographic range is striking. The convention has visited desert cities and rainforest cities, Pacific islands and mountain towns. Each location brings its own community, its own logistics, its own weather (July in Phoenix is a very different experience from July in Portland), and its own interpretation of the Westercon format [1][5].

The contraction of host cities in the 2020s is visible in the table. The last independently-hosted Westercons were small affairs. The recent ones have been co-hosted with existing conventions (BayCon) rather than standing on their own. The map of willing hosts has shrunk [1][3].

## 5. Advantages of the Traveling Model

The traveling model confers genuine benefits that fixed-venue conventions cannot replicate:

**Geographic accessibility.** Over the decades, Westercon has been within driving distance of fans across the entire western United States. No single fixed venue could serve Boise, Honolulu, and San Diego equally [1].

**Capacity building.** Each host city's fan community gains the experience of organizing a major convention. This builds skills, contacts, and infrastructure that benefit local fandom long after Westercon moves on. Norwescon itself was founded by fans who wanted to build the organizational experience needed for a Worldcon bid -- the same capacity-building logic that Westercon enables at a larger geographic scale [1][10].

**Cultural pollination.** The convention carries traditions, contacts, and ideas from city to city. A programming innovation introduced in Portland might be adopted by the next year's committee in San Jose. A room party tradition from Los Angeles might take root in Seattle. The convention is a vector for cultural transmission [1].

**Fresh perspectives.** A new host committee brings new ideas. They are not bound by "the way we've always done it" because they have not always done it. Each Westercon is an interpretation, not a repetition [1].

## 6. The Cost of Movement

The disadvantages of the traveling model are the mirror image of its advantages:

**No institutional memory.** The team that ran last year's Westercon in San Jose has no organizational relationship to the team running next year's in Portland. Lessons learned do not automatically transfer. Mistakes are repeated. Best practices are rediscovered from scratch [1][3].

**No venue relationship.** Fixed-venue conventions like Norwescon develop 30-year relationships with their hotels. The hotel management knows the convention's needs, the convention knows the hotel's layout, and both sides benefit from the continuity. Westercon must negotiate a new hotel relationship every year [1][10].

**Volunteer pipeline fragility.** The model requires a continuous supply of fan communities willing and able to organize a major convention from near-zero every year. When the supply contracts -- as it has in the 2020s -- the model breaks down [1][3].

**No accumulation.** A fixed-venue convention accumulates: staff experience, vendor relationships, programming refinements, attendee expectations. A traveling convention disperses. Each year is a fresh start, which is exhilarating when volunteers are plentiful and exhausting when they are not [1].

**Inconsistent quality.** The quality of a Westercon depends entirely on the competence and resources of that year's host committee. A well-organized committee in a good venue produces a memorable convention. A struggling committee in a suboptimal venue produces an experience that can damage the brand for years [1].

## 7. The July 4th Weekend Anchor

The July 4th weekend timing is the one constant in Westercon's rotating schedule. Established in the 1950s, this timing provides a fixed point in the calendar even as the geographic location changes [1][5].

The timing has practical advantages: the long weekend guarantees that working fans can attend without taking vacation. It has cultural resonance: Independence Day for the independent-minded. And it has competitive implications: any other convention scheduled for July 4th weekend in the western United States is, by definition, competing with Westercon for the same pool of fans [1].

In recent years, as Westercon has been co-hosted with BayCon, the July 4th timing has been maintained -- but the convention's independent identity has blurred. When Westercon is a track within BayCon rather than a standalone event, the July 4th anchor becomes less distinctive [1][4].

## 8. Fixed vs. Traveling: The Norwescon Contrast

The most instructive comparison is between Westercon and its descendant, Norwescon.

| Dimension | Westercon (Traveling) | Norwescon (Fixed) |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Venue | Different city every year | DoubleTree SeaTac since 1994 |
| Institutional memory | Resets annually | Accumulates over decades |
| Geographic reach | Continental | Pacific Northwest |
| Volunteer pipeline | Must recruit anew each year | Stable volunteer corps |
| Hotel relationship | Negotiated from scratch | 30+ year partnership |
| COVID resilience | Broke continuity chain | Survived with virtual year |
| Cultural consistency | Varies by host | Consistent identity |
| Longevity | 77+ years (facing retirement) | 48 years (going strong) |

Norwescon was founded in 1978 by Pacific Northwest fans who wanted the organizational experience that comes from running conventions. The model they were drawing from was the regional convention tradition that Westercon had established thirty years earlier. But Norwescon made a different architectural choice: instead of traveling, it put down roots. And that choice -- to invest in a fixed location rather than geographic breadth -- has given it the organizational stability that Westercon's traveling model increasingly lacks [1][10].

## 9. The Distributed System Analogy

In systems architecture terms, Westercon is a distributed system with no persistent state. Each node (annual convention) bootstraps from the shared protocol (bylaws) and whatever cached state the organizers carry in their heads [1][3].

The protocol provides:
- **Consensus rules:** Site selection voting, business meeting procedures
- **Identity:** The "Westercon" service mark, held by LASFS
- **Failsafe:** Section 3.16, routing to LASFS when no bid succeeds
- **Continuity:** Bylaws published in every progress report and program book

What the protocol cannot provide:
- **Institutional memory:** Each node must reconstruct operational knowledge
- **Relationship state:** Hotel contracts, vendor relationships, volunteer databases
- **Quality assurance:** No mechanism for enforcing quality standards across nodes

This makes Westercon resilient in one sense -- no single point of failure can kill the protocol -- but fragile in another. When the supply of nodes (willing host committees) drops below the replacement rate, the system fails not through catastrophic collapse but through gradual inability to instantiate new nodes [1][3].

Compare this to a mesh network, where each node maintains the protocol but relies on a sufficient density of nodes to maintain the network. Below a threshold, the mesh fragments. Westercon's node density dropped below threshold in the 2020s [1].
