## 1. Founding

In September 1948, seventy-seven science fiction fans gathered in Los Angeles for a one-day event that would become one of the longest-running traditions in speculative fiction fandom. The idea came from Walter J. "Doc" Daugherty of the Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society (LASFS), who proposed a regional convention for West Coast fans who could not afford the cross-country trek to Worldcon -- the World Science Fiction Convention that had been running since 1939 in various East Coast and Midwestern cities [1][2][5].

E. Everett Evans introduced the formal motion to create the event and chaired the first gathering. Daugherty chaired the second. The original name was the "West Coast Scienti-Fantasy Conference" -- a name that captures both the era's terminology and the breadth of the enterprise. It was not just science fiction. It was science *and* fantasy, united under a banner that acknowledged both traditions [1][5][6].

What makes this founding significant is its motivation. Westercon was not founded to be prestigious. It was not founded to attract celebrities or generate revenue. It was founded because fans in Los Angeles, San Francisco, Portland, and Seattle wanted to see each other, and the existing infrastructure -- a single annual Worldcon, often thousands of miles away -- did not serve them. Westercon was an act of accessibility. It brought fandom to the fans [1][5].

## 2. The LASFS Connection

The Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society is the organizational bedrock of Westercon. Founded on October 27, 1934, LASFS is the oldest continuously operating science fiction club in the world. Its motto is "De Profundis Ad Astra" (From the Depths to the Stars), and its unofficial Rule 0 is "Death will not release you" -- a joke that contains a truth about the persistence of fan communities [2][6].

LASFS provided Westercon with three things that no other organization could:

1. **Institutional continuity.** LASFS has been operating since 1934. When individual Westercon committees form and dissolve year after year, LASFS remains. It is the constant in a rotating equation [2][6].

2. **The service mark.** The name "Westercon" is a registered service mark of LASFS. This means that while any group of fans can organize and run a Westercon, the name itself belongs to the parent organization. This is the legal foundation that prevents the convention from being hijacked, abandoned, or commercialized without community consent [1][3].

3. **The failsafe.** Under Section 3.16 of the Westercon Bylaws, if no bid wins site selection and the Business Meeting cannot select a site, LASFS determines alternate arrangements. LASFS is the safety net -- the organizational guarantee that even if every volunteer committee fails, the institution itself survives [1][3].

This relationship between LASFS and Westercon is a model of how permanent organizations can support temporary ones. LASFS does not run Westercon. It provides the infrastructure that allows others to run Westercon. The distinction matters. LASFS is the protocol layer; each annual convention is the application layer [2][3].

## 3. Early Years and the July 4th Tradition

By the early 1950s, Westercon had evolved from a one-day gathering into a weekend-long convention, and it had settled into the timing that would define it for decades: the American Independence Day holiday weekend [1][5].

The July 4th timing was pragmatic and symbolic. Pragmatically, the long weekend guaranteed that working fans could attend without burning vacation days. Symbolically, it associated Westercon with independence -- appropriate for a convention built by and for people who defined themselves as independent thinkers. Science fiction fandom in the mid-20th century was a community of self-described outsiders, people who read things most of their neighbors did not read and imagined things most of their neighbors could not imagine. A holiday celebrating independence resonated [1].

The transition from one-day event to weekend convention also marked a shift in ambition. The first Westercon was a gathering. By the mid-1950s, Westercon was a *convention* -- with programming tracks, an art show, a dealers room, social events, and the organizational complexity that comes with hosting hundreds of people for multiple days [1][5].

## 4. Combined Worldcon Events

Several early Westercons were held jointly with Worldcon, reflecting the intertwined nature of the two institutions. The 1954 event featured John W. Campbell as the Worldcon Guest of Honor and Jack Williamson as Westercon's. Combined events also occurred in 1958 and at other points in the mid-century [1][5][6].

These combined events served multiple purposes. They reduced the travel burden on fans who would otherwise need to attend two separate conventions. They demonstrated Westercon's connection to the global SF community. And they gave West Coast fans a stake in the Worldcon tradition without requiring them to travel east [1][5].

The combined events also revealed the tension inherent in Westercon's relationship with Worldcon. When Westercon was combined with Worldcon, it risked being overshadowed -- reduced to a regional sideshow at a global event. When it was independent, it risked being seen as merely local. This tension between regional identity and global connection would persist throughout Westercon's history [1][5][6].

## 5. Growth Decades

Through the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, Westercon grew alongside the broader expansion of science fiction fandom. The convention's rotating-host model meant that different cities and fan communities took turns building organizing capacity. A group that hosted Westercon in Portland developed skills, contacts, and organizational infrastructure that benefited Portland fandom long after the convention moved on. The same was true in San Diego, Phoenix, Seattle, and every other host city [1][5].

The growth was not just numerical. Westercon's programming expanded from the literary core -- panel discussions about books, writing workshops, author readings -- to encompass art shows, masquerades, gaming, filk music (fan-written songs, often to borrowed tunes), regency dances, room parties, and the full ecosystem of activities that characterize modern fan conventions [1].

The Guest of Honor tradition became central to the convention's identity. Westercon GoHs were drawn from the western region's creative community -- authors, artists, scientists, and fan leaders who represented the best of West Coast speculative fiction. The GoH roster was the convention's annual thesis statement: this is what matters to us [1][5].

By the 1980s, Westercon had reached maturity. It was an established institution with a defined governance structure, a proven organizational model, and a community that spanned the western half of the continent. It was also about to incubate something entirely unexpected [1][7].

## 6. The Traveling Route

Westercon's geographic reach spans the western United States, western Canada, and Hawaii -- essentially anywhere west of the 104th meridian. Over nearly eight decades, the convention has visited cities including:

| State/Province | Cities |
|----------------|--------|
| California | Los Angeles, San Francisco, San Jose, San Diego, Sacramento, Santa Clara, Oakland |
| Washington | Seattle, SeaTac |
| Oregon | Portland |
| Arizona | Phoenix |
| Nevada | Las Vegas, Tonopah |
| Utah | Salt Lake City, Layton |
| Idaho | Boise |
| Hawaii | Honolulu |
| British Columbia | Vancouver |

Each city brought its own flavor to the convention. A Westercon in Honolulu was a different experience from one in Boise, which was different from one in San Diego. The convention was not just traveling -- it was translating. Each stop was an interpretation of the shared protocol (the bylaws, the traditions, the GoH format) through the lens of a local community [1][5].

The geographic breadth was both Westercon's strength and, eventually, part of its challenge. A convention that could be anywhere had to recruit volunteers everywhere. When the volunteer pipeline in any given city dried up, that city stopped bidding. The map of potential hosts gradually contracted [1][3].

## 7. Attendance Through the Decades

Westercon was never a massive convention. It was a community gathering, sized to sustain meaningful human interaction rather than spectacle.

| Year | Number | Location | Attendance |
|------|--------|----------|------------|
| 1948 | Westercon 1 | Los Angeles, CA | 77 |
| 2003 | Westercon 56 | SeaTac, WA | -- |
| 2011 | Westercon 64 | San Jose, CA | 693/826 |
| 2012 | Westercon 65 | Seattle, WA | 865/950 |
| 2019 | Westercon 72 | Layton, UT | ~750/835 |
| 2020 | Westercon 73 | Seattle, WA | Cancelled (COVID) |
| 2022 | Westercon 74 | Tonopah, NV | Small |
| 2024 | Westercon 76 | Salt Lake City | -- |
| 2025 | Westercon 77 | Santa Clara, CA | (w/ BayCon) |

The dual numbers (693/826) reflect the distinction between attending memberships (people who showed up) and total memberships (including supporting members who voted in site selection but did not attend). This distinction matters in fan governance: supporting members have a financial stake and a vote but do not occupy hotel rooms or consume programming [1][5].

## 8. The Convention That Kept Going

Westercon's longevity is remarkable by any standard. Seventy-seven years of continuous operation (with the COVID interruption) makes it one of the oldest science fiction conventions in the world -- predated only by Worldcon itself (since 1939) and Philcon (since 1936, though with significant gaps) [1][5].

The convention that was created as an accessible alternative to Worldcon -- a way for West Coast fans to gather without crossing the continent -- became an institution in its own right. It developed its own governance, its own traditions, its own cultural identity. It incubated entirely new forms of creative community. It outlasted the hotels that hosted it, the publishers whose authors it celebrated, and many of the literary genres it was built to serve [1].

The convention that started with 77 fans in a single room in Los Angeles in 1948 is now facing the question of whether 77 years is enough. The story of that question is told in [Module 05: The Question of When to Stop](05-decline-retirement.md).
