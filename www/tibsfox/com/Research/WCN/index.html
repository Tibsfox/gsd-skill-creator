Westercon

Since 1948 — the traveling convention that built West Coast fandom
Overview History Traveling Governance Legacy Decline Connections Verification Mission Pack
In 1948, seventy-seven fans in Los Angeles planted a seed that grew for nearly eight decades. Westercon carried science fiction fandom from city to city, teaching the West Coast how to organize, how to govern itself democratically, and how to build communities around the things that matter. Along the way, a room party in Sacramento gave birth to an entire subculture. Now, facing the question every institution eventually meets — whether to continue or to end with dignity — Westercon’s story becomes a case study in how communities are born, how they thrive, and how they choose to close the book.

Since 1948

History

Founded by “Doc” Daugherty and the LASFS as a regional alternative for West Coast fans who couldn’t travel to Worldcon. Seventy-seven years of rotating gatherings, from 77 attendees to a continent-spanning institution.

A Different City Every Year

Traveling Model

The bidding process, site selection, host committees. How a convention with no permanent address maintained continuity through protocol alone — and why that model eventually strained past its limits.

LASFS as founding organization, bylaws, the Board of Directors, site selection voting, the relationship to WSFS and Worldcon. Democratic fan governance encoded in parliamentary procedure — including a clause about annexing Australia.

The Conventions It Built

Cultural Legacy

The seed convention that taught the West Coast how to run itself. The 1985 room party that birthed furry fandom. The bookstore, the governance template, the regency dance. Every regional con carries Westercon’s DNA.

Attendance decline, volunteer exhaustion, COVID disruption, and the 2025 first-passage vote to repeal the bylaws. Not failure — the deliberate choice to end with dignity after 77 years of service.
