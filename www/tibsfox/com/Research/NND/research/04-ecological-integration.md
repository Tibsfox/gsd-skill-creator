## 1. The Randy Johnson Principle

On March 24, 2001, during a spring training game, Randy Johnson threw a fastball that struck a mourning dove that had entered the flight path. The bird was instantly vaporized. The incident became famous because it looked improbable -- a bird flying into a pitched baseball in real time.

It was not improbable. It was guaranteed.

A 95-mph fastball covers the 60.5-foot distance from pitcher's mound to plate in approximately 400 milliseconds. A mourning dove's neurological reaction time -- the minimum time from visual detection of a threat to initiation of evasive movement -- is longer than 400 ms. The bird had no chance of evasion not because it failed to react, but because the physics made reaction irrelevant. The problem was not the bird's performance. The problem was the geometry of the encounter.

**Scaled to the highway:**

A truck at 70 mph covers approximately 103 feet per second. A deer at the shoulder of the interstate detects the approaching headlights at perhaps 200 feet -- less than 2 seconds of reaction time. The deer's startle-to-sprint initiation takes 0.5 to 1.0 seconds. The driver's perception-reaction time -- from visual detection of the deer to foot reaching brake -- is a minimum of 1.5 seconds at highway alertness levels.

Neither organism can solve this problem. The geometry makes avoidance impossible before neurology engages. No deer-crossing sign, no reflector, no whistle on the truck, no wildlife camera, no public education campaign changes the physics. The only intervention that works is structural: eliminate the possibility of trajectories intersecting.

The enclosed corridor separates the trajectories permanently.

## 2. Wildlife-Vehicle Collision Statistics

**National scope:**

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Annual vehicle-large animal crashes | 1-2 million | Pew Charitable Trusts |
| Human deaths from deer-vehicle collisions | 200-440/year | Conover 2019 / State Farm |
| Human injuries from deer-vehicle collisions | 26,000-59,000/year | Conover 2019 |
| Property damage annually | $8-10 billion | Pew / IIHS |
| State Farm animal collision claims (Jul 2024-Jun 2025) | 1.7 million | Insurance Information Institute |
| Average cost per wildlife-vehicle collision | $6,126 | FHWA |
| Average cost per moose collision | $30,769-$44,546 | FHWA / signedsweet.com |

**Undercount factor:** Wildlife experts estimate 5-10 animal carcasses exist for every reported crash (Born Free USA / FHWA research). The 1-2 million annual figure represents insured, reported incidents. The actual number of wildlife killed by vehicles is likely 5-10 million per year.

**Rural state concentration:** In rural states such as Wyoming, wildlife-vehicle crashes represent almost 20% of all reported collisions (Pew Charitable Trusts). The interstate system carries the highest-speed, highest-volume traffic through corridors that bisect major wildlife habitat -- creating disproportionate collision concentration at the worst intersection of speed and wildlife density.

## 3. Endangered Species Road Mortality

**FHWA documented cases:**
The Federal Highway Administration identified 21 federally listed Threatened or Endangered species for which road mortality is a recognized major threat. Specific cases:

- **Hawaiian goose (Nene):** Listed as Threatened; vehicle collisions on low-traffic roads at Kilauea are a documented mortality source
- **Desert tortoise:** Road mortality on highways crossing Mojave Desert habitat; fragmentation prevents population recovery
- **San Joaquin kit fox:** Vehicle mortality in the San Joaquin Valley; roads fragment the population
- **California tiger salamander:** Road crossing mortality during breeding migrations; genetic isolation of populations by highway barriers

**Documented high-profile cases (2024):**
- **Grizzly bear 399:** The oldest known reproducing female grizzly in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem, known by researchers for decades, killed by a vehicle near Grand Teton National Park in October 2024. Her death prompted national media coverage. The population cannot replace her reproductive contribution on any human-relevant timescale.
- **Red wolves in North Carolina:** Four red wolves killed by vehicles on US-64 in eastern North Carolina over a 10-month period, including a breeding male. His orphaned pups subsequently died. The total US wild red wolf population is fewer than 20 individuals. Four vehicle deaths represent a catastrophic fraction of population viability.

**The recovery ceiling:** Species for which road mortality is a significant threat cannot fully recover under the Endangered Species Act as long as the highway system bisects their habitat at current traffic speeds and volumes. Protective regulations, captive breeding, and habitat preservation are all undermined by collision mortality. Structural corridor enclosure removes this ceiling.

## 4. Habitat Fragmentation at Continental Scale

The interstate system is not simply dangerous to wildlife on the road surface. It severs every major wildlife corridor in North America.

**The fragmentation mechanism:**
- Animals that need to migrate seasonally -- elk, mule deer, pronghorn, black bears, grizzly bears -- use corridors established over thousands of years
- These corridors follow terrain, water, and vegetation gradients that predate human infrastructure by millennia
- The interstate system bisects these corridors at right angles in hundreds of locations
- Animals cannot safely cross, populations become isolated, genetic exchange decreases, local extinction risk increases

**The 2008 FHWA Verdict:**
The FHWA Wildlife-Vehicle Collision Reduction Study (FHWA-HRT-08-034, 2008) reviewed 34 mitigation methods and concluded "there are no simple solutions." Wildlife crossing structures (overpasses, underpasses, culverts) work, but the cost is $4 million per overpass -- and there are currently only dozens of dedicated wildlife crossings along 48,000 miles of interstate.

**Point solution vs. corridor solution:**
The existing wildlife crossing paradigm installs crossings at proven high-mortality locations. This is a point solution to a linear problem. If an elk herd's historic migration corridor crosses I-90 at mile marker 52 but the crossing is at mile marker 55, the gap still kills animals. Corridor-length enclosure eliminates the entire length as a mortality source -- not just the points where crossings exist.

**Species that require corridor-length connectivity:**
- Pronghorn antelope (longest land migration in the western hemisphere, >150 miles annual)
- Elk herds (seasonal movements between summer and winter range)
- Grizzly bears (home range 200-1,500 square miles; road avoidance behavior creates de facto range reduction)
- Mountain lions (young males need to disperse 100+ miles to establish territories)
- Gray wolves (pack territory expansion requires safe corridor crossing)

## 5. The Enclosed Corridor as Continental Wildlife Crossing

The enclosed corridor does not install crossings. It removes the corridor as a barrier entirely.

**How it works:**
- Wildlife moves on the surface. The corridor is below the surface level, or the canopy is above at sufficient height that wildlife does not encounter the roadway.
- For the rural canopy typology: the road passes under a structure; wildlife walks over or around the structure's perimeter
- Wildlife cannot enter the enclosed corridor -- the enclosure that excludes rain, ice, and UV also excludes deer, elk, bears, and birds

**The surface returned:**
In the rural canopy typology, the land directly above the enclosed roadway is returned to usable surface. This surface can be:
- Restored native vegetation (prairie grass on plains, pine forest in mountain corridors)
- Managed as a wildlife movement corridor
- Used for utility siting, fiber optic conduit, or other infrastructure that benefits from linear access
- Leased to adjacent landowners for agricultural use

The Snoqualmie Pass wildlife overpass demonstrates the principle at point scale: a vegetated bridge over the interstate that animals use freely. The enclosed corridor applies this principle at linear scale -- for the entire length of the system.

## 6. Snoqualmie Pass: The Pacific Northwest Model

**The existing crossing:**
The Snoqualmie Pass wildlife overpass on I-90 in Washington State was completed in 2024 after decades of advocacy by wildlife biologists, tribal nations, and conservation organizations. It is one of the largest wildlife crossings in North America.

**Documented use:**
Within the first year of operation, remote cameras documented elk, deer, black bears, cougars, and dozens of smaller species using the crossing. The crossing reconnects Yakama Nation ancestral territory with Snoqualmie tribal territory via a corridor that the highway had severed.

**Tribal nation involvement:**
The Snoqualmie Tribe and Yakama Nation were central to the advocacy, design, and implementation of the Snoqualmie Pass wildlife crossing. Their knowledge of historical migration patterns, sacred sites, and ecological relationships in the corridor was incorporated into the crossing location and design. The tribal nations are named specifically because the ecological relationship is specific -- the Snoqualmie people's ancestral connection to this corridor is not generic "Indigenous connection to nature" but a documented multi-generational relationship with specific species, sites, and seasonal cycles.

**Scaling up:**
The enclosed corridor extends the Snoqualmie Pass model from a single overpass to the full I-90 corridor. Animals that currently must find the single crossing can move along the entire length of the enclosed section. The crossing becomes the rule, not the exception.

## 7. Tribal Nation Coordination in Corridor Ecology

Any enclosed corridor passing through treaty-protected territory requires specific tribal nation coordination. The interstate system crosses dozens of sovereign tribal territories, each with specific treaty rights, wildlife management authority, and cultural relationships with corridor species.

**Specific treaty relationships relevant to PNW I-90 and I-5:**

- **Yakama Nation:** Reserved fishing, hunting, and gathering rights in ceded territories under the Treaty of 1855. I-90 passes through the Yakama's ancestral territory. Corridor design affecting salmon habitat (Columbia River tributaries adjacent to I-90) requires Yakama consultation.
- **Snoqualmie Tribe:** Federally recognized 1999; ancestral territory centered on the Snoqualmie Valley through which I-90 passes. Wildlife management cooperation with WDFW for Snoqualmie Pass crossing.
- **Nez Perce Tribe:** Treaty rights to hunt, fish, and gather in ceded territories across Idaho and Washington. I-90 crosses through this territory in Idaho.
- **Lummi Nation:** Marine territory adjacent to I-5 in northwestern Washington; orca and salmon recovery coordination.

**The corridor authority model:** Regional corridor authorities (see Module 05) must include tribal nation representation with meaningful decision-making power over sections of corridor passing through treaty territory. This is not consultation -- it is governance.

## 8. Bird Strike Physics and Avian Species Protection

**The mourning dove principle, applied to raptors:**
Hawks, eagles, and falcons hunt along highway corridors because roads create clear sight lines and edge habitat. A red-tailed hawk hunting a mouse at the shoulder of I-5 is in the flight path of vehicles traveling at 70 mph. The closing speed physics are identical to Randy Johnson's fastball: the bird cannot process and respond to the threat vector before contact.

**Documented bird strike data:**
- FHWA does not systematically collect bird strike data for highways (unlike the FAA's mandatory wildlife strike reporting for airports)
- Studies of specific highway sections show bird mortality rates of 10-30 birds per kilometer per year (European roadway ecology literature)
- Raptors (protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act and in some cases the Endangered Species Act) are disproportionately represented due to hunting-along-corridor behavior

**The bald eagle case:** Bald eagles feed on fish and waterfowl. I-5 and I-90 corridors follow river valleys where eagles hunt. Vehicle strikes on eagles are documented annually in Washington and Oregon. Eagles, as apex predators with low reproductive rates (typically one eaglet per nest per year), are highly sensitive to adult mortality.

**Enclosed corridor avian protection:**
- Birds cannot enter an enclosed corridor
- Raptor hunting habitat shifts away from the road edge to natural hunting terrain
- Migratory bird corridors over the road surface are unaffected (birds fly over the canopy structure, not through it)
- Songbird mortality from vehicle strikes (the most common category in terms of numbers) is eliminated for the full corridor length
