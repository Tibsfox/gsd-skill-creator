## Songs of Faith and Devotion

*Songs of Faith and Devotion* (1993) debuted at #1 in both the US and UK -- DM's commercial peak. The album incorporated live drums, gospel choirs, and distorted guitars alongside electronics. "I Feel You" opens with a guitar riff that could belong to a rock band, but the production marks it as unmistakably DM.

Recorded in Madrid at a villa converted into a studio, the sessions were tense. Flood returned as producer, but the dynamic had shifted. The band was at the height of its powers and the edge of its endurance.

"Walking in My Shoes" combined orchestral strings with electronic beats. "Condemnation" featured a gospel choir. "In Your Room" built from whisper to howl. The album was the most emotionally raw thing DM had made -- and the most costly to produce, in human terms.

## The Devotional Tour

The Devotional Tour (1993-1994) was documented in Anton Corbijn's *Devotional* film. 159 shows across 14 months. The staging was theatrical -- smoke, dramatic lighting, Corbijn's visual direction transforming arenas into electronic cathedrals.

The intensity was unsustainable. The tour is remembered as both the band's greatest live achievement and the period that nearly ended them. The physical and psychological toll was enormous. What audiences saw was transcendent. What happened offstage was survival.

## Wilder's Departure

Alan Wilder announced his departure on June 1, 1995, via a statement on his website. His reasons were characteristically precise: insufficient creative recognition, frustration with the band's working methods, and personal exhaustion. The statement was not angry -- it was an engineer's assessment that the system was no longer functional.

Wilder's departure removed DM's most meticulous sound designer. His Recoil project demonstrated what he could do with complete creative control -- sophisticated, dark electronic music that critics admired but that lacked DM's pop accessibility. The separation clarified both parties: DM needed Wilder's discipline; Wilder needed DM's platform.

## Ultra and Bare Survival

*Ultra* (1997) was produced by Tim Simenon (Bomb the Bass). The album was the first without Wilder, and it sounded different -- cleaner, less layered, more dependent on Gore's songwriting and Gahan's voice to carry the weight. "Barrel of a Gun" was aggressive. "It's No Good" was seductive. "Home" was devastating.

The album reached #5 in the US, #1 in the UK. DM had survived. Not intact -- they would never sound like the Wilder-era band again -- but alive, functional, and still capable of making music that mattered.

The late 1990s were about proving viability. DM could continue as a trio. The sound would be different. The ambition remained.
