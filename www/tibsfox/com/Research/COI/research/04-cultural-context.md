## Overview

Coil's work is inseparable from its cultural matrix. To study them purely as musicians -- even as "occult musicians" -- is to miss at least half the picture. They were embedded in and shaped by a specific set of cultural circumstances: London's gay subculture of the 1980s and early 1990s, the devastating early years of the AIDS crisis, the post-punk/post-industrial underground, the alternative cinema of Derek Jarman, and the broader network of independent artists who operated outside mainstream commercial structures.

This module situates Coil within these overlapping cultural contexts, tracing the connections between their social world and their creative output. The core argument is that Coil's most characteristic qualities -- their fusion of the esoteric and the carnal, their commitment to transgression as a form of honesty, their oscillation between confrontation and tenderness -- were not abstract aesthetic choices but direct responses to the specific pressures and possibilities of their cultural moment.

The AIDS crisis, in particular, was not a background concern but a defining experience. When your friends and lovers are dying of a new and poorly understood disease, when your government's response ranges from indifference to active hostility, when the very fact of your sexuality becomes associated with death -- these conditions produce art of a particular urgency and depth. Coil's work from 1985 onward cannot be understood without this context.

## AIDS Crisis and Queer Identity

### London's Gay Subculture in the 1980s

Balance and Christopherson were active participants in London's gay nightlife and subculture during the period when AIDS transformed that community from a site of liberation and pleasure into a community under siege. Their experience was not that of distant observers commenting on a crisis but of people embedded in a community that was being devastated, who responded with art that was simultaneously personal testimony, political resistance, and magickal practice. The London gay scene of the early 1980s -- concentrated in areas like Soho, Earl's Court, and Vauxhall -- was a world of clubs, bars, bathhouses, and informal networks that provided both social space and creative stimulus.

This was a community that had fought for visibility and acceptance through the 1970s, building on the partial decriminalization of homosexuality in England and Wales (1967) and the subsequent cultural shifts of the liberation movement. By the early 1980s, London's gay scene was vibrant, politically engaged, and culturally productive -- a world that included not just nightlife but theater, film, literature, visual art, and music.

The arrival of AIDS devastated this community. The first UK cases were identified in 1981-1982, and by the mid-1980s the epidemic was killing young men in numbers that overwhelmed the community's capacity to grieve. The government's response under Margaret Thatcher was widely perceived as indifferent at best -- Section 28 of the Local Government Act (1988), which prohibited local authorities from "promoting homosexuality," was experienced by the gay community as an act of deliberate cruelty at the height of a health crisis.

Balance and Christopherson experienced this devastation firsthand. Their friends were dying. Their community was under attack from both a virus and a hostile political establishment. These circumstances shaped their art in ways that went beyond thematic content: the urgency, the refusal to compromise, the insistence on confronting taboo subjects directly -- these qualities were not abstract aesthetic positions but survival strategies forged in crisis.

### The Tainted Love Single

The cultural significance of Coil's AIDS-related work cannot be overstated. In a period when mainstream culture was largely silent or openly hostile, and when the British government was actively suppressing public discussion of homosexuality, Coil's recordings constituted acts of cultural resistance that combined artistic ambition with political urgency.

Coil's 1985 cover of "Tainted Love" / "Panic" is one of the most significant cultural artifacts of the early AIDS era. Released as a charity single with proceeds donated to the Terrence Higgins Trust -- the UK's leading HIV/AIDS charity, founded in 1982 and named after one of the first people to die of AIDS-related illness in the UK -- it is widely cited as the first music release dedicated to AIDS awareness and charity.

The cover radically inverted the meaning of Soft Cell's 1981 pop hit. Where Marc Almond sang of romantic betrayal and emotional contamination, Balance and Christopherson transformed "Tainted Love" into a literal statement about physical contamination -- "tainted love" as infected love, love that carried death within it. The deliberate inversion was a political act: taking a song that the mainstream pop audience knew and loved and forcing it to confront the reality that the same mainstream was ignoring.

The music video, directed by Christopherson, contained multiple subliminal references to Aleister Crowley and the Law of Thelema, layering esoteric content within a charity release -- a characteristic Coil strategy of operating on multiple levels simultaneously.

The video's significance extended beyond the music world: it was purchased by the Museum of Modern Art in New York, becoming the first music video to enter MoMA's permanent collection. This institutional recognition validated both the artistic quality of the work and its cultural importance as a document of the AIDS crisis.

### Horse Rotorvator and the Weight of Loss

*Horse Rotorvator* (1986) -- frequently cited as Coil's masterwork -- was partly shaped by the AIDS-related deaths of friends and the broader atmosphere of grief and anger that pervaded the gay community in the mid-1980s. The album's themes of death, transformation, and the violence that society inflicts on those it designates as other were not abstract philosophical explorations but responses to lived experience.

The album's intensity -- its combination of beauty and horror, tenderness and brutality -- reflects the emotional reality of living within a community under existential threat. The title itself, referring to a machine that tears up the ground, suggested both destruction and the possibility of new growth from the broken earth.

### The Gay Man's Guide to Safer Sex

In 1992, Coil composed the soundtrack for *The Gay Man's Guide to Safer Sex*, a video that was simultaneously a practical harm-reduction resource and an uncompromising celebration of male homosexuality. Created during the pre-antiretroviral therapy period when AIDS remained effectively a death sentence, the video and its soundtrack occupied a radical position: refusing to allow the association between gay sexuality and death to produce shame or silence, instead insisting on both safety and joy.

This was not a comfortable or easy position. The *Guide* was explicit, unapologetic, and designed to be used -- a functional resource produced within a community that was learning, through terrible necessity, how to survive while remaining true to itself. Coil's soundtrack for this project was an act of solidarity, service, and defiance.

### Sexuality as Creative and Philosophical Material

For Coil, sexuality was not a private matter that happened to influence their art; it was a core creative and philosophical domain. Their queerness was inseparable from their occult practice (the integration of sexual and spiritual energies drew from both Western Tantric traditions and Crowley's sex magick), their sonic methodology (the physical, bodily dimension of sound experience), and their cultural positioning (as artists who refused to separate their identity from their work).

This integration was itself a political act in a cultural context where homosexuality was subject to legal restriction (Section 28), social stigma, and the additional burden of association with a lethal epidemic. To produce art that was openly, unapologetically queer while also being intellectually serious and aesthetically ambitious was to challenge multiple forms of marginalization simultaneously.

## Derek Jarman Collaborations

### The Angelic Conversation (1985)

Coil's first documented live performance (August 4, 1983) took place at the Magenta Club during a screening of films by Cerith Wyn Evans and Derek Jarman. This event established a creative connection between Coil and Jarman that would span a decade and produce some of the most significant work in both their catalogs.

*The Angelic Conversation* (1985) was a meditation on homosexual desire, featuring Shakespeare's sonnets read by Judi Dench over a visual backdrop of slow-motion male bodies and natural landscapes. Coil composed the full instrumental soundtrack, creating a sonic environment of drone, bells, echoed percussion, and layered textures that provided both emotional foundation and temporal structure for Jarman's images.

Jarman described the film as "a dream world, a world of magic and ritual" -- language that could equally describe Coil's sonic methodology. The collaboration was natural: both Jarman and Coil were queer artists working with esoteric themes, both were committed to formal experimentation, and both understood their work as political without being reducible to political messaging.

The film is now recognized as one of the key works of queer cinema and a landmark in the artistic treatment of homosexual love. Coil's soundtrack -- delicate, luminous, and deeply felt -- demonstrated a range of emotional expression that went far beyond the confrontational industrial of their early recordings.

### Blue (1993)

*Blue* was Jarman's final major work -- 79 minutes of unchanging International Klein Blue filling the screen, accompanied by a soundtrack in which Jarman described his experience of living and dying with AIDS. Coil contributed music to this profoundly stripped-down work, one of the most radical and moving films ever made.

Coil's contribution was conceived as "a representation of the ghost-like memory of the gay clubs at the end of the 70s/beginning of the 80s, where people had sex with strangers in backdoors and caught the HIV virus." This description is remarkable in its honesty: it acknowledges the reality of the sexual behavior that facilitated HIV transmission without judgment, and simultaneously evokes the lost world of pre-AIDS gay culture with a combination of nostalgia and grief.

*Blue* premiered at the Venice Film Festival in 1993. Jarman died of AIDS-related illness on February 19, 1994, less than a year later. Coil's contribution to his final work was both a creative collaboration and an act of accompaniment -- being present, through music, at the threshold between life and death.

### The Hellraiser Rejected Score

In 1985-1986, Coil composed a score for Clive Barker's horror film *Hellraiser* (1987). The score was ultimately rejected, reportedly because the music was deemed "too terrifying" -- an anecdote that, even if apocryphal or exaggerated, speaks to the visceral power of Coil's sonic work during this period.

The rejected material was later released in various forms on compilation, becoming a valued part of Coil's catalog. The *Hellraiser* connection is significant for several reasons: it demonstrated Coil's potential for commercial film work, it connected them to the horror genre (whose exploration of transgression and the body paralleled their own concerns), and it provided a concrete example of their work being considered too extreme for mainstream consumption -- a position that both limited their audience and defined their artistic identity.

### Jarman as Kindred Spirit

The Coil-Jarman relationship was more than a series of professional collaborations. It represented a genuine meeting of artistic and philosophical kindred spirits:

| Shared Quality | Jarman | Coil |
|---------------|--------|------|
| Queerness as creative material | Central to all work from *Sebastiane* onward | Integrated into esoteric practice and sonic methodology |
| Formal experimentation | Super-8, garden films, *Blue* | Drone, glitch, cut-up, altered states |
| Esoteric interests | Alchemy, John Dee, ritual | Thelema, chaos magick, shamanic practice |
| AIDS as defining experience | Documented his own illness in art | Produced first AIDS charity single; soundtrack for *Blue* |
| English outsider tradition | Part of the "hidden reverse" of English culture | Central figures in the post-industrial underground |

## The Post-Industrial Scene

### England's Hidden Reverse

David Keenan's book *England's Hidden Reverse* (SAF Publishing, 2003) -- the primary published biographical and critical text on Coil -- positions them alongside Current 93 and Nurse With Wound as the three pillars of what he terms the "hidden reverse" of English music: a tradition of experimental, esoteric, and transgressive music-making that runs beneath and against the mainstream of English popular music.

This framing is illuminating: it connects Coil not just to their immediate peers but to a broader English tradition of visionary outsiders -- from William Blake through Austin Osman Spare to the present -- who have used art to explore territories that polite culture refuses to acknowledge.

### Current 93 and David Tibet

Current 93, the project of David Tibet, was one of Coil's closest creative allies. The two projects shared personnel, performed together, and released music on each other's labels. Tibet's progression from noise and industrial toward apocalyptic folk music paralleled (though did not mirror) Coil's own evolution, and the mutual influence between the two projects enriched both.

The Current 93 connection was also significant in esoteric terms: Tibet's engagement with Christian mysticism, Aleister Crowley's writings, and the work of the 17th-century scholar Thomas Browne provided a different angle of approach to some of the same traditions that Coil explored, creating a productive dialogue between alternative modes of esoteric practice.

### Nurse With Wound and Steve Stapleton

Steve Stapleton's Nurse With Wound -- the third pillar of Keenan's "hidden reverse" -- represented the most abstract and sonically experimental extreme of the post-industrial underground. Nurse With Wound's surrealist-influenced approach to sound collage and improvisation provided a context in which Coil's own experimental tendencies could be understood and appreciated.

The three projects -- Coil, Current 93, and Nurse With Wound -- collectively defined a space within English experimental music that was distinct from both the academic avant-garde and the commercial industrial of Ministry or Nine Inch Nails. Their mutual support and cross-pollination created an ecosystem that sustained itself through decades of commercial marginality.

### Nine Inch Nails and Trent Reznor

Coil's relationship with Nine Inch Nails and Trent Reznor was significant for both projects:

- **Christopherson directed NIN videos:** "Pinion," "Wish," "Help Me I Am In Hell," "Happiness In Slavery," and the unreleased *Broken* short film.
- **Coil remixed NIN:** Material from *The Downward Spiral* was remixed by Coil, resulting in recordings later released as *Recoiled* (2014) through Cold Spring Records.
- **Reznor attempted to sign Coil:** In the 1990s, Reznor tried to bring Coil to his Nothing Records imprint. Recordings took place at Nothing Studios in New Orleans.
- **How to Destroy Angels:** Reznor named his side project (with his wife Mariqueen Maandig) after Coil's debut EP, with Christopherson's approval. This naming was a deliberate act of homage and acknowledged lineage.

The Coil-NIN connection demonstrated the underground-to-mainstream influence chain that operated throughout the 1990s alternative music landscape. Reznor, as perhaps the most commercially successful industrial musician, served as a conduit through which Coil's innovations reached a vastly larger audience -- even if most of that audience never encountered Coil's work directly.

### The Some Bizzare Connection

Some Bizzare Records, the label founded by Stevo Pearce, released Coil's first two proper albums (*Scatology* and *Horse Rotorvator*). Some Bizzare's roster -- which also included Soft Cell, Einsturzende Neubauten, Cabaret Voltaire, and others -- positioned Coil within a European network of experimental and transgressive music that bridged the gap between the avant-garde and the commercial underground.

The Some Bizzare connection also established a link to the synth-pop world through Soft Cell and Marc Almond -- a connection that became visible in Almond's guest contributions to Coil recordings and in the "Tainted Love" cover itself, which transformed a Soft Cell hit into an AIDS awareness single.

## The Film and Cinema Connection

Coil's relationship to cinema extended beyond the Jarman collaborations. Their engagement with film -- as soundtrack composers, as subjects of Christopherson's video direction, and as participants in a broader culture of experimental and transgressive cinema -- represented a significant dimension of their cultural positioning.

**Clive Barker and Hellraiser.** The rejected *Hellraiser* score (see Derek Jarman Collaborations above) connected Coil to the horror genre, whose exploration of bodily transformation, transgression, and the boundary between pleasure and pain resonated with their own thematic concerns. The anecdote that the score was deemed "too terrifying" for a horror film became part of Coil's mythology -- evidence that their work operated at an intensity level that even professional purveyors of horror found excessive.

**Christopherson's video direction.** Beyond the NIN videos, Christopherson directed and produced visual material throughout his career. His training at Hipgnosis and his understanding of the relationship between visual and sonic communication informed every aspect of Coil's visual presentation, from album art to stage design to the videos themselves.

**Cerith Wyn Evans.** The Welsh artist Cerith Wyn Evans -- at whose film screening Coil's first live performance took place -- represented another connection to experimental visual culture. Wyn Evans' practice, which spans film, installation, and text-based work, shared Coil's interest in the boundary between different artistic media and in the use of light, sound, and space as interrelated creative materials.

**The broader tradition.** Coil's engagement with cinema placed them within a broader tradition of musicians who have worked seriously with film: from Ennio Morricone and Bernard Herrmann through the early electronic film scores of Louis and Bebe Barron (*Forbidden Planet*) to the contemporary practice of artists like Mica Levi and Jonny Greenwood. Coil's contributions to this tradition -- particularly the Jarman soundtracks -- have been increasingly recognized as significant works of film music in their own right.

## Geography and Relocation

### London: Urban Hedonism and Creative Friction

Coil's London period (from their formation through the mid-1990s) was characterized by immersion in the city's cultural life: the gay scene, the club world, the post-industrial underground, the film and art communities. London provided both creative stimulus and the social context that informed major works -- the AIDS crisis, the Jarman collaborations, the connections to Current 93, Nurse With Wound, and Nine Inch Nails.

The city's energy -- its friction, density, and capacity for both pleasure and suffering -- informed the "solar" period of Coil's work: aggressive, outward-directed, engaged with the social world.

### Weston-super-Mare: Hermetic Rural Practice

The move from London to Weston-super-Mare in the late 1990s represented a fundamental shift in Coil's relationship to their environment. The Victorian seaside town -- quiet, faded, far from the intensity of London -- provided a setting for the "moon musick" era: introspective, nocturnal, contemplative.

The relocation was partly practical (London's cost and pace were unsustainable), partly therapeutic (an attempt to create a more stable environment as Balance's struggles with alcohol dependence intensified), and partly philosophical (a deliberate withdrawal from the social world into a more hermetic mode of creative practice).

The shift from urban to rural, from social to hermetic, from extroverted to introverted mapped onto the solar/moon musick transition and onto the broader alchemical progression from the outward-directed work of confrontation and dissolution to the inward-directed work of refinement and contemplation.

## The Alternative / Underground Network

The underground culture that sustained Coil was not a single scene but an overlapping network of scenes, each with its own norms, aesthetics, and distribution channels. Understanding this network is essential to understanding how an artist could produce significant, influential work while remaining almost entirely invisible to the mainstream.

### Cassette Culture and Independent Distribution

Coil emerged from and participated in the cassette culture of the early 1980s -- a decentralized network of independent musicians, small labels, and mail-order distributors that operated entirely outside the mainstream music industry. This network, which had grown from the DIY ethos of punk, provided the infrastructure for artists whose work was too experimental or transgressive for commercial release.

Threshold House, Coil's own label, was an extension of this independent ethos: a mechanism for releasing work without the compromises that major-label distribution required. The label's name -- evoking the liminal, the threshold between states -- was itself a statement of creative philosophy.

### The Zine and Small Press World

Balance's fanzine *Stabmental* connected Coil's origins to the small press and zine culture that provided the intellectual infrastructure of the post-punk underground. Zines served as forums for critical discourse, artistic manifestos, and community building in an era before the internet provided these functions -- and they operated with a directness and rawness that commercial publications could not match.

### Live Performance and the Club Scene

Coil's relationship to live performance was distinctive: after only four performances in 1983, they did not play live again until December 1999 -- a sixteen-year hiatus that made them one of the most reclusive significant acts in experimental music. When they returned to live performance, it was in a transformed mode: ritualistic staging, the signature "fluffy suits" (full-body costumes that obscured individual identity), and dramatically slowed re-interpretations of earlier material.

The return to live performance coincided with Thighpaulsandra's question ("Why doesn't Coil play live?") and produced approximately 50 concerts between 1999 and 2004, including the Royal Festival Hall performances (2000) and appearances at major festivals. Four live albums (*Live One* through *Live Four*) were released in 2003. The final concert was at the 2004 Dublin Electronic Music Festival.

The Royal Festival Hall concerts were particularly significant culturally: a venue associated with classical music and prestige programming hosting a group that had emerged from the most confrontational wing of the post-industrial underground. The programming decision represented a form of institutional recognition that validated Coil's artistic seriousness while the performances themselves -- ritualistic, costumed, sonically extreme -- refused to conform to the venue's usual expectations. The tension between institutional prestige and radical artistic practice was itself a statement about where Coil stood in the cultural landscape.

## Coil in the Broader Musical Landscape

Coil occupied a unique position in the broader musical landscape of the 1980s-2000s:

| Context | Coil's Position | Significance |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Industrial music | Post-TG, more esoteric and experimental than mainstream industrial | Deepened industrial's engagement with occult and philosophical content |
| Electronic music | Parallel to but separate from techno, house, ambient mainstreams | Introduced ritual and esoteric dimensions to electronic music |
| Experimental/avant-garde | More emotionally direct than academic experimentalism | Bridged the gap between emotional intensity and formal experimentation |
| Dark ambient / neofolk | Precursors and influences | Shaped the aesthetic and philosophical vocabulary of these later genres |
| Queer music | Pre-dated the concept of "queer music" as a category | Demonstrated integration of queer identity with experimental practice |

What this mapping reveals is that Coil occupied a genuinely unique position -- not merely at the intersection of multiple scenes but in a territory that none of those scenes, individually, could accommodate. They were too experimental for the industrial mainstream, too emotional for academic experimentalism, too queer for the heteronormative rock underground, too intellectual for the club scene, and too committed to aesthetic ambiguity for any movement that demanded clear allegiance. This irreducibility -- the refusal to fit neatly into any available category -- was both the source of their marginal commercial position and the guarantor of their artistic significance. Categories are containers; Coil was the thing that broke the container.

## Coil and the Visual Arts

Beyond their music, Coil's engagement with the visual arts was a significant dimension of their cultural practice:

**Album art and design.** Christopherson's background in Hipgnosis -- one of the most important design collectives in popular music history -- informed Coil's approach to visual presentation. Every release was designed with the same care and intentionality as the music itself, creating a unified aesthetic experience that extended from the sonic to the visual domain.

**Video work.** Christopherson's career as a music video director -- particularly his NIN work -- positioned him at the intersection of experimental and commercial visual culture. His videos for Coil itself, though less widely seen, demonstrated the same visual intelligence applied to the project's own material.

**Performance design.** The late-period "fluffy suits" -- full-body costumes worn during live performances -- were a visual statement that obscured individual identity and foregrounded the collective, ritualistic character of the performance. The suits were simultaneously absurd and threatening, playful and otherworldly, in keeping with Coil's refusal to settle into any single register.

**Derek Jarman connection.** The Jarman collaborations (see above) placed Coil within the tradition of experimental British cinema, connecting their sonic practice to a visual art tradition that included Super-8 filmmaking, garden art, painting, and the radical aesthetics of queer self-representation.

## The Economy of Scarcity

Coil's relationship to commercial culture was complex and revealing. On one hand, they operated largely outside the mainstream music industry, releasing through their own label and small independent outlets. On the other hand, they engaged with commercial structures when it served their purposes -- the Some Bizzare releases, the NIN connection, the "Tainted Love" charity single.

The limited-edition strategy -- exemplified by *Astral Disaster*'s initial pressing of 99 copies -- created an economy of scarcity that gave each release the character of a rare and precious object. This strategy was philosophically consistent with the treatment of albums as ritual artifacts (see Module 3), but it also created practical barriers to access that the posthumous reissue program has since removed.

The collector culture that grew up around Coil's releases reflected both the genuine quality and rarity of the material and the quasi-religious devotion that the project inspired in its most dedicated listeners. Original pressings of key releases commanded premium prices on the secondhand market, creating an economic dimension to Coil fandom that was both a testament to the work's value and a barrier to wider engagement.

## The Question of Accessibility

Coil occupied an uncomfortable position regarding accessibility throughout their career. The work demanded engagement, refused easy categorization, and rewarded repeated, attentive listening in ways that casual consumption could not provide. This was not accidental -- the entire philosophical framework behind the project (see Module 3) was predicated on the idea that transformative art required active participation from the listener.

Yet the limited availability of releases, the density of the esoteric references, and the challenging nature of the sonic material all served to restrict the audience in ways that were not always intentional or desirable. The post-2010 reissue program and critical reassessment have partially addressed this tension, making the work physically available while the growing body of critical and scholarly commentary provides interpretive frameworks for new listeners.

The fundamental tension remains, however: Coil's work is designed to resist easy consumption, and any attempt to make it more "accessible" in the conventional sense risks diluting the very qualities that make it significant.

## Coil and the Politics of Refusal

Throughout their career, Coil practiced a consistent politics of refusal -- a stance that was as much cultural as it was artistic:

**Refusal of genre.** By continuously evolving their sonic methodology and operating under multiple aliases, Coil refused to be pinned to any single genre identity. They were never "just" an industrial band, an electronic act, a dark ambient project, or a drone outfit -- though they were all of these things at various moments.

**Refusal of celebrity.** The sixteen-year hiatus from live performance, the limited editions, the retreat from London to Weston-super-Mare -- all represented a refusal to participate in the celebrity economy of popular music. Coil were not interested in being famous; they were interested in making work that operated on consciousness.

**Refusal of compromise.** The *Hellraiser* rejected score, the departure from PTV, the insistence on self-releasing through Threshold House -- all demonstrated a willingness to sacrifice commercial opportunity in order to maintain creative control. This was not mere stubbornness but a principled position rooted in the belief that compromised art could not function as effective magick.

**Refusal of explanation.** While Balance and Christopherson gave interviews and made statements about their work, they consistently refused to provide comprehensive explanations of their esoteric methodology or creative process. The work was designed to operate on the listener directly, without the mediation of artist commentary -- a stance that respected the listener's capacity for direct engagement while maintaining the mystery that made the work effective.

These refusals, taken together, constituted a cultural position that was itself political: a demonstration that it was possible to create significant art outside the mainstream commercial system, on one's own terms, without seeking permission or validation from established institutions. In an era of increasing corporate control of cultural production, Coil's example remains instructive and inspiring.
