## Overview

Coil's engagement with occult and esoteric traditions was neither decorative nor sensational. It was the structural foundation of their creative method -- the philosophical operating system on which their sonic and conceptual work ran. To treat Coil's occultism as aesthetic accessory is to misunderstand the project fundamentally; equally, to treat it uncritically as genuine magickal practice without examining its intellectual and cultural dimensions is to forfeit analysis for credulity.

This module takes a rigorous approach: documenting the specific esoteric traditions that informed Coil's work, tracing the connections between philosophical frameworks and creative decisions, and distinguishing Coil's approach from the more systematized occultism of their former collaborators in Psychic TV and Thee Temple ov Psychick Youth. The goal is neither advocacy nor dismissal but accurate mapping of how these traditions functioned within Coil's creative ecology.

What emerges is a picture of remarkable philosophical sophistication. Balance and Christopherson drew from at least seven distinct esoteric traditions -- Thelema, the work of Austin Osman Spare, chaos magick, Enochian systems, grimoire traditions, Qabalah, and shamanic practice -- synthesizing them into a personal methodology that resisted codification. Their approach was protean by design: the refusal to systematize was itself a philosophical commitment, rooted in Spare's Neither-Neither doctrine and in the chaos magick principle that belief itself is a tool rather than a fixed position.

## Primary Esoteric Influences

### Aleister Crowley and Thelema

Crowley's magickal system pervaded Coil's work from the earliest recordings through to the final sessions. The influence operated on multiple levels:

**Textual foundation.** Balance's 1983 manifesto *The Price of Existence Is Eternal Warfare* drew its title and central thesis directly from Chapter 3 of Crowley's *The Book of Lies* (1913). This foundational text established the Thelemic framework for everything Coil would produce: the concept of True Will (the authentic purpose of the individual, discovered through disciplined self-knowledge), the Law of Thelema ("Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law"), and the understanding of creative work as a form of magickal operation.

**Symbolic vocabulary.** The "Tainted Love" music video (1985) contained multiple subliminal references to Crowley and the Law of Thelema, embedding esoteric symbolism within a charity release that was simultaneously Coil's most publicly visible work. This layering -- esoteric content within accessible presentation -- was characteristic of Coil's approach throughout their career.

**Practical methodology.** Crowley's emphasis on the disciplined practice of magickal technique -- the keeping of a magickal diary, the systematic exploration of consciousness states, the use of ritual as a technology for producing specific mental and spiritual effects -- provided a framework for Coil's approach to both studio practice and live performance.

**Solar/Lunar symbolism.** Balance's division of Coil's output into "solar" and "moon musick" periods drew directly from the Thelemic understanding of solar and lunar currents -- active/receptive, conscious/unconscious, Will/Imagination -- that Crowley elaborated throughout his writings.

However, Coil's relationship to Crowley was not one of simple discipleship. They absorbed and applied Thelemic concepts selectively, combining them with influences from other traditions in ways that would have been heterodox by the standards of any formal Thelemic organization. Coil were magickal practitioners, not Thelemites in the organizational sense.

### Austin Osman Spare and the Neither-Neither Doctrine

If Crowley provided the structural framework, Austin Osman Spare (1886-1956) provided the operational philosophy. Spare's influence on Coil was arguably even more fundamental than Crowley's, shaping not just their theoretical commitments but their actual creative methodology.

**The Neither-Neither Doctrine.** Spare's philosophical core -- the state of total vacuity of the conscious mind, related to Buddhist sunyata and tantric concepts of emptiness -- became central to both Coil's creative philosophy and their sonic methodology. The Neither-Neither represents a state beyond binary opposition: neither this nor that, neither affirming nor denying, but occupying a third position that transcends the duality entirely.

Applied to music, the Neither-Neither doctrine produced the "Sidereal Sound" concept (see Module 2): sound that exists between conventional categories, that refuses to resolve into familiar patterns, that occupies the liminal space where transformation becomes possible.

**Sigil technique.** Spare's method of creating sigils -- condensing a desire or intention into a visual symbol, then "forgetting" the original intent to allow the sigil to operate on the unconscious -- informed Coil's approach to both visual art and sonic composition. Albums, tracks, and visual materials were sometimes conceived as sigils: encoded intentions designed to operate on the listener's unconscious mind.

**Drew McDowall's testimony.** McDowall has credited Spare as "the subtextual influence on his entire body of work," providing independent corroboration of Spare's central importance to the creative methodology of those who worked within Coil's orbit.

**The Zos Kia name.** Balance and Christopherson's pre-Coil project took its name directly from Spare's terminology: Zos (the body considered as a whole) and Kia (the atmospheric "I," the magical self). This naming choice demonstrates that Spare's influence was present from the earliest stage of their collaboration, predating the formal establishment of Coil.

### Chaos Magick

The chaos magick movement, codified in 1978 by Peter Carroll (*Liber Null*) and Ray Sherwin (*The Book of Results*), rejected the elaborate hierarchies and fixed belief systems of Victorian-era magical orders (the Golden Dawn, the A.'.A.'., the various Rosicrucian bodies) in favor of a pragmatic, results-oriented approach that treated belief itself as a tool to be adopted and discarded as circumstances required.

Coil absorbed chaos magick's core principles:

| Principle | Chaos Magick Formulation | Coil's Application |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Belief as tool | Any belief system can be used for magickal purposes; none is inherently "true" | Drew from multiple traditions simultaneously without committing to any single system |
| Results orientation | Magick is evaluated by outcomes, not by adherence to tradition | Judged recordings by their effect on consciousness, not by technical orthodoxy |
| DIY ethos | Anyone can practice magick; no initiation or credentials required | Self-taught esoteric practice; no formal organizational affiliation |
| Paradigm shifting | The ability to adopt and discard belief systems at will | Moved fluidly between Thelemic, shamanic, Enochian, and other frameworks |
| Anti-dogmatism | No fixed rules except "nothing is true, everything is permitted" | Rejected TOPY's systematization in favor of protean experimentation |

However, Coil's relationship to chaos magick was more complex than simple adoption. Where Carroll and Sherwin sought to create a stripped-down, efficient methodology free of cultural baggage, Coil reveled in the cultural richness of diverse esoteric traditions. They took chaos magick's permission to draw from any source but rejected its tendency toward minimalism and demystification.

### Enochian Magick and John Dee

The Enochian system of angelic communication, developed by the Elizabethan polymath John Dee (1527-1608) and his scryer Edward Kelley in the 1580s, provided Coil with both practical content and symbolic resonance:

**The Hieroglyphic Monad.** The artwork for *Time Machines* (1998) featured John Dee's Hieroglyphic Monad inverted. Dee's *Monas Hieroglyphica* (1564) presented a glyph composed of the seven symbols of the alchemical planetary elements, intended to represent the unity of all existence in a single visual form. The inversion on *Time Machines*' artwork suggested a complementary or reversed relationship to Dee's unifying project -- perhaps the fragmentation of unified experience (through hallucinogenic compounds) as a mirror to Dee's attempt to unify fragmented knowledge.

**Angelic systems.** The debut EP *How to Destroy Angels* (1984) took its title from a reference within the Enochian system -- the concept of angelic entities whose function is destruction and dissolution, a necessary complement to creation within the magickal worldview. This title established from the outset Coil's engagement with the destructive, shadow aspects of esoteric tradition -- not merely the elevating and illuminating.

**Dee as archetype.** John Dee himself -- the scholar-magus who combined rigorous intellectual inquiry with visionary practice, who served as advisor to Elizabeth I while conducting experiments in angelic communication -- embodied a synthesis of rational and non-rational modes of knowing that resonated with Coil's own practice of combining technical sophistication with esoteric intent.

### The Goetia and Grimoire Traditions

The grimoire tradition -- practical manuals of ceremonial magick, primarily concerned with the evocation and command of spiritual entities -- was referenced across multiple Coil works. The *Goetia* (the first section of the *Lesser Key of Solomon*) was the primary reference:

Originally excavated by S. Liddell MacGregor Mathers (co-founder of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn) and published in a new edition by Aleister Crowley in 1904, the *Goetia* catalogs 72 demons with their attributes, sigils, and modes of evocation. These entities are associated with earthly desires -- wealth, knowledge, love, power -- and the magician's task is to summon and command them through precise ritual procedure.

Coil engaged with the grimoire tradition not as literal demon-summoners but as artists who recognized in these texts a systematic mapping of human desires, fears, and psychological forces. The grimoire's catalogue of entities provided a vocabulary for naming and addressing aspects of experience that resist ordinary language -- precisely the kind of material that Coil sought to work with through sound.

### The Qabalah

The Qabalah -- the Jewish mystical tradition of scriptural interpretation and cosmological mapping, adopted and adapted by Western occultists from the Renaissance onward -- provided Coil with a comprehensive symbolic system for organizing and relating concepts across multiple domains. The Tree of Life, with its ten sephiroth (divine emanations) and twenty-two connecting paths, offered a structural framework for understanding relationships between sounds, symbols, numbers, and states of consciousness.

Coil's engagement with Qabalah was syncretic rather than orthodox, drawing from both the traditional Jewish source material and the Hermetic Qabalah as developed by the Golden Dawn and Crowley. The system's capacity for mapping correspondences between apparently disparate phenomena -- colors, sounds, planets, letters, psychological states -- aligned naturally with Coil's practice of creating music that operated on multiple levels simultaneously.

### Shamanic Practice

The shamanic tradition -- broadly defined as the use of altered states of consciousness, often induced by drumming, fasting, or entheogenic substances, to access non-ordinary realities for purposes of healing, divination, or spiritual development -- was the most ancient of Coil's influences and perhaps the most deeply integrated into their practice.

Coil's use of altered states in the creative process (see Module 2), their conception of the musician as intermediary between ordinary and non-ordinary realities, and their understanding of sound itself as a technology for inducing trance states all drew from shamanic precedent. The *Time Machines* project -- drone compositions designed to induce "temporal slips" in the listener -- was essentially a technological update of the shaman's drumming practice: sustained, repetitive sonic stimulation designed to shift consciousness from its ordinary mode of operation.

## Magick as Creative Method

### The Album as Ritual Object

Coil consistently treated their releases as more than mere commercial products -- an approach that distinguished them from contemporaries who used occult imagery purely for aesthetic effect. Physical albums were sometimes packaged with:

- Sigil-like autographs drawn by hand
- Blood stains (actual or symbolic)
- Art objects and inserts with ritual significance
- Hand-finished elements unique to individual copies
- Numbered limited editions with Qabalistic significance

These additions transformed the physical record into "something akin to occult artifacts" -- objects charged with intentionality that extended the sonic content into the material realm. The practice drew from the sympathetic magick tradition, in which physical objects serve as vessels for magickal intent.

*Astral Disaster* (1999) was initially released in an edition of 99 copies -- a number chosen for its Qabalistic significance (the numerical value of the Hebrew word *Amen*). The extreme limitation was not merely a marketing strategy but a deliberate restriction that gave each copy the character of a unique ritual object.

### Sigils and Sonic Sigils

Spare's sigil technique -- condensing a desire or intention into a visual symbol, charging it through focused concentration, and then "forgetting" the conscious intent to allow it to operate through the unconscious -- was extended by Coil from the visual to the sonic domain.

If a visual sigil encodes intention in a geometric form that bypasses rational interpretation to operate on the unconscious mind, a "sonic sigil" would encode intention in a sonic form that achieves the same bypassing effect through the auditory channel. Certain Coil tracks can be understood as operating in precisely this way: their complex, layered, often disorienting sonic structures resist rational parsing while producing measurable psychological and physiological effects in listeners.

### Psychoactive Sound Design

The concept of music designed to produce specific alterations in consciousness reached its most explicit formulation in *Time Machines* (1998). The four tracks, each named after a hallucinogenic compound, were created with the stated intention of producing drug-like effects through purely sonic means.

Balance described the tracks as having been "tested and retested" for their apparent narcotic potency -- a formulation that treats the music not as art to be evaluated aesthetically but as technology to be evaluated functionally. Whether or not the tracks reliably produce the claimed effects in listeners, the intention and methodology represent a rigorous application of the magickal principle that sound can directly affect consciousness.

### Temporal Magick

Coil's interest in time as a medium for magickal operation manifested in several ways:

1. **Calendar-aligned recording.** The Moon's Milk sessions were timed to solstices and equinoxes, treating the astronomical calendar as a magickal timing system.

2. **Temporal distortion through sound.** *Time Machines* explicitly aimed to produce "temporal slips" -- subjective distortions of time perception -- in listeners.

3. **Solar/lunar periodicity.** The division of the entire discography into "solar" and "moon musick" periods imposed a temporal-cosmological framework on the body of work.

4. **Deceleration as transformation.** The late-period practice of performing earlier songs at drastically reduced tempos represented time itself as a medium to be manipulated for transformative effect.

## Distinguishing Coil from TOPY

### The Three Magickal Rifts

The PopMatts analysis identifies three fundamental rifts between Coil's esoteric methodology and that of Psychic TV / Thee Temple ov Psychick Youth:

**Rift 1: Relationship to chaos magick's legacy.** TOPY adopted Carroll and Sherwin's chaos magick framework but institutionalized it into an organization with membership structures, regular meetings, and prescribed ritual formats. Coil took chaos magick's anarchic, anti-hierarchical spirit and applied it consistently -- including against chaos magick's own tendency to become codified.

**Rift 2: Systematized vs. intuitive practice.** TOPY sought replicable results through disciplined sigil technique: regular workings at prescribed times, standardized methods, documented outcomes. The assumption was that magickal effects could be reliably produced by following specific procedures. Coil rejected this assumption, pursuing "protean, ad hoc experimentation" that varied its methods continuously and refused to settle into any fixed routine.

**Rift 3: Group discipline vs. individual exploration.** TOPY was a collective enterprise with a charismatic leader (P-Orridge), organizational hierarchy, and expectations of conformity to group practice. Coil was a private partnership that invited collaborators but never sought members in the TOPY sense. There was no "joining Coil" -- only being invited into the creative space by its custodians.

### Systematized vs. Protean Practice

The distinction between TOPY's systematized approach and Coil's protean methodology reflects a genuine philosophical debate within the Western esoteric tradition. The debate can be traced back to the split between the Golden Dawn's elaborate ceremonialism and Spare's stripped-down, individual-centered practice in the early 20th century.

Coil's position -- that effective magickal practice requires continuous adaptation, improvisation, and the willingness to discard methods that have become habitual -- aligns more closely with Spare's approach and with the Zen Buddhist concept of "beginner's mind" than with the ceremonial tradition's emphasis on precise repetition of established forms.

### The Individual vs. The Collective

Coil's decision to operate as a private creative partnership rather than a collective with organizational structure had profound implications for their esoteric practice. Without the social pressure to conform to group norms, Balance and Christopherson were free to follow their magickal interests wherever they led -- including into territories that would have been difficult to navigate within an organizational context.

This individualism came at a cost: the lack of a community of practice meant that their esoteric development was largely self-directed, without the corrective feedback that a group can provide. But it also produced a body of work that is remarkably coherent in its philosophical commitments, undiluted by the compromises that collective decision-making inevitably requires.

## The Alchemy of Transmutation

### Alchemical Principles in Coil's Practice

The alchemical metaphor is perhaps the most comprehensive framework for understanding Coil's work. Alchemy -- the art and science of transmutation, of transforming base material into refined states through disciplined experimental process -- maps onto Coil's practice at every level:

- **Nigredo** (blackening): The confrontational, destructive phase of the early work -- *How to Destroy Angels*, *Scatology* -- corresponds to the alchemical stage of dissolution, in which base material is broken down to its elemental components.

- **Albedo** (whitening): The purification and clarification of the middle period, particularly the *Angelic Conversation* soundtrack and the more structured compositions of *Horse Rotorvator*, corresponds to the stage of washing and refinement.

- **Citrinitas** (yellowing): The solar illumination of *Gold Is the Metal with the Broadest Shoulders* and *Love's Secret Domain* -- the conscious engagement with cultural energy (acid house, dance culture) -- corresponds to the stage of awakening and solar consciousness.

- **Rubedo** (reddening): The final stage of the Great Work, the production of the Philosopher's Stone, may be mapped onto the moon musick period -- not in the sense that Coil achieved the magnum opus, but in the sense that their late work represented the most mature and integrated expression of all the preceding stages.

### The Philosopher's Stone as Creative Goal

*Scatology* (1985) explicitly engaged alchemical themes -- the title itself refers to the study of excrement, connecting to the alchemical principle that the Stone can be found in the most despised and overlooked material ("in stercore invenitur" -- "it is found in filth"). The album's exploration of transmutation themes -- turning base sonic material into refined art, extracting meaning from taboo subjects -- was a direct enactment of the alchemical project.

## Drug Use and Magickal Practice

The relationship between psychoactive substance use and esoteric practice in Coil's work requires careful treatment. This was not casual recreational drug use that happened to inform art; it was a deliberate incorporation of pharmacological consciousness alteration into a broader framework of magickal practice.

The philosophical precedent for this approach is well-established in multiple esoteric traditions:

- **Shamanic entheogens:** The use of psychoactive plants and fungi in shamanic traditions worldwide for purposes of healing, divination, and spiritual development.
- **Crowley's experiments:** Aleister Crowley's documented experimentation with cannabis, mescaline, ether, and other substances as part of his magickal practice.
- **The psychedelic tradition:** The mid-20th century research of Aldous Huxley, Timothy Leary, and others into the consciousness-expanding properties of psychedelics.

*Love's Secret Domain* (LSD) and *Time Machines* (tracks named after specific hallucinogenic compounds) made the connection between drug experience and creative practice explicit. The claimed goal was not merely to create art influenced by drug states but to create art that could replicate aspects of drug states through purely sonic means -- an ambitious technological project with deep roots in both esoteric tradition and avant-garde art practice.

It should be noted that Balance's struggles with alcohol dependence were well-documented and caused significant personal difficulty. The distinction between controlled, intentional use of altered states as a magickal tool and uncontrolled substance dependence was a tension that ran through the later years of the project.

## The Sacred and the Profane

Coil's esoteric practice deliberately collapsed the boundary between sacred and profane -- a collapse that was itself a magickal act. Their work combined:

- Explicit sexuality with ritual solemnity
- Scatological imagery with alchemical aspiration
- Pop culture references with deep esoteric learning
- Commercial release formats with ritual object design
- Dance music structures with consciousness-altering intent

This refusal to separate the sacred from the profane drew from the Tantric tradition (in which sexual and bodily energies are harnessed for spiritual purposes), from Crowley's insistence that "every act is a magickal act," and from the broader Western esoteric understanding that the division between spirit and matter is itself the fundamental illusion to be overcome.

> "Without wishing to sound pompous, we want to make sacred music. I don't mean something that assumes Messianic proportions or delusions, but I think all good music should be an attempt to change people's mind-sets." -- John Balance, The Wire, 1995

This statement encapsulates Coil's position: music can be sacred without being pious, transformative without being pretentious, magickal without being mystified. The esoteric frameworks documented in this module were not a costume worn over conventional music but the operating system that determined what the music was, how it was made, and what it was intended to do.

## The Evolution of Esoteric Practice

Coil's engagement with esoteric traditions was not static but evolved significantly over the project's two decades:

**Early period (1982-1986):** Heavily Thelemic, drawing on Crowley's vocabulary and conceptual framework. The manifesto, the "Tainted Love" subliminal references, and the *How to Destroy Angels* naming all reflect direct Crowleyan influence. The practice was more declarative than subtle -- announcing esoteric intent through titles, imagery, and packaging.

**Middle period (1987-1991):** Broadening engagement with chaos magick, Spare, and shamanic traditions. The acid house turn of *Love's Secret Domain* introduced the pharmacological dimension more explicitly. The practice became more integrated -- less a framework applied to music and more a methodology inseparable from the creative process.

**Late period (1999-2004):** The most deeply integrated phase, in which esoteric practice and creative practice became indistinguishable. The calendar-aligned recording sessions, the improvisational methods rooted in the Neither-Neither doctrine, the treatment of live performance as ceremony -- all reflected a maturity of practice in which the philosophical framework and the creative output were one and the same thing.

This evolution tracked the broader shift from solar to moon musick: from extroverted declaration of esoteric intent to introverted enactment of esoteric practice. The early works told you they were magickal; the late works simply were.

## Evaluating the Claims

Any rigorous treatment of Coil's esoteric practice must acknowledge the fundamental question: did it work? Did the sigils function? Did *Time Machines* produce temporal slips? Did the calendar-aligned recording sessions yield qualitatively different results?

These questions are ultimately unanswerable in the terms in which they are posed, because the esoteric framework deliberately operates outside the parameters of conventional empirical verification. What can be assessed is the functional relationship between the esoteric practice and the creative output: did the philosophical commitments produce better art than they would have without them?

The evidence suggests yes -- not because the magick "worked" in any literal sense, but because the esoteric framework provided:

1. **Intentionality.** Every creative decision was made within a framework that demanded conscious purpose. Nothing was arbitrary or merely conventional.
2. **Continuity.** The philosophical commitments provided a through-line that sustained creative evolution over two decades without loss of coherence.
3. **Permission.** The esoteric framework authorized experiments that conventional creative practice would not have attempted -- calendar-aligned recording, psychoactive sound design, the treatment of albums as ritual objects.
4. **Depth.** The layering of esoteric meaning within the sonic material created a richness and resonance that rewards repeated engagement in ways that purely aesthetic or formal decisions cannot.

Whether one believes in the literal efficacy of magickal practice or not, the functional value of the esoteric framework to Coil's creative process is demonstrable through the quality and coherence of the output it produced.
