## Overview

Coil's discography is one of the most complex and challenging to navigate in experimental music. Over twenty-two years of active recording (1982-2004), supplemented by an extensive posthumous release program continuing through the present day, the project produced well over 40 distinct releases across studio albums, EPs, singles, side-project albums, soundtracks, live recordings, compilations, and archival material. The catalogue is further complicated by multiple aliases, limited editions, contested posthumous releases, and the overlap between side projects and the "main" Coil identity.

This module provides a comprehensive survey organized by era, following the trajectory that Balance himself identified: the "solar" period of outward-directed, aggressive work giving way to the "moon musick" period of introspective, nocturnal electronics. Within this framework, each release is documented with its date, label, significance, and relationship to the broader arc of Coil's evolution.

The discography is not merely a catalogue but a document of continuous transformation -- each release marking a shift in sonic methodology, philosophical orientation, or personal circumstance that altered the project's trajectory.

## Early Period: 1984-1986

### How to Destroy Angels (1984)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | EP / 12" |
| Label | L.A.Y.L.A.H. Antirecords |
| Dedication | Mars (the planet of war) |
| Key Instruments | Iron and steel percussion, early samplers |
| Era | Solar |

Coil's debut release established the project's identity with immediate force. The title, drawn from the Enochian magickal system, announced the esoteric framework that would underpin all subsequent work. The use of iron and steel instruments -- physical, metallic, ritualistic -- created a sound that was both primordial and technologically mediated.

The EP was a statement of intent: Coil would operate in the territory between industrial noise and ceremonial sound, between technological innovation and ancient practice. The dedication to Mars (not merely as a planet but as an archetypal force of war, conflict, and transformative destruction) established the "solar" orientation of the early work.

### Scatology (1985)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Some Bizzare / Force & Form |
| Themes | Alchemy, transmutation, the base and the refined |
| Key Instruments | Fairlight CMI IIx/III, DX7, Emulator, PPG Wave |
| Era | Solar |

The first full-length album took its title from the study of excrement -- a deliberately provocative choice that was also a precise alchemical reference. The alchemists understood that the Philosopher's Stone could be found in the most despised material: "in stercore invenitur" (it is found in filth). *Scatology* enacted this principle sonically, extracting beauty and meaning from base, confrontational material.

The album demonstrated a quantum leap in sonic sophistication from the debut EP. The Fairlight CMI, one of the most capable digital instruments of its era, enabled complex sampling and sequencing that placed Coil at the technical forefront of electronic music. Christopherson's experience with early sampler development meant these instruments were exploited far beyond their preset capabilities.

### The Angelic Conversation Soundtrack (1985)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | Soundtrack |
| Director | Derek Jarman |
| Character | Drone, bells, echoed percussion, orchestral elements |
| Era | Solar (transitional) |

The soundtrack for Derek Jarman's meditation on homosexual desire marked Coil's first significant departure from the confrontational industrial of their early work. The music -- delicate, layered, luminous -- demonstrated an emotional and timbral range that expanded the project's identity considerably.

The *Angelic Conversation* soundtrack is significant as the earliest evidence that Coil's "solar" period contained the seeds of the "moon musick" to come: the drone elements, the contemplative atmosphere, and the prioritization of mood over confrontation all foreshadowed the late-period aesthetic.

### Tainted Love / Panic (1985)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | 12" single |
| Label | Some Bizzare |
| Significance | First music release for AIDS charity (Terrence Higgins Trust) |
| Cultural Impact | Video purchased by MoMA (first music video in permanent collection) |
| Era | Solar |

The cover of Soft Cell's pop hit, transformed into an AIDS awareness single, was Coil's most publicly visible release and one of the most culturally significant recordings in their catalogue. The deliberate inversion of meaning -- from romantic heartbreak to literal physical contamination -- demonstrated Coil's capacity for operating simultaneously on multiple levels: pop song, political statement, esoteric ritual, and charity release.

### Horse Rotorvator (1986)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Some Bizzare / Force & Form |
| Themes | Death, AIDS crisis, transformation, violence |
| Key Instruments | Fairlight CMI, mixed acoustic/electronic |
| Era | Solar |

Often cited as Coil's masterwork, *Horse Rotorvator* represented the peak of the early-period aesthetic. The album combined industrial textures, orchestral elements, and sophisticated sampling with a compositional maturity that elevated the material beyond provocation into genuine art.

The album was partly informed by the AIDS-related deaths of friends, and its themes of death, transformation, and the violence that society inflicts on the marginal reflected the lived experience of Balance and Christopherson during the mid-1980s. Tracks like "Ostia (The Death of Pasolini)" -- a meditation on the murder of the filmmaker Pier Paolo Pasolini -- connected personal grief to broader cultural and political violence.

## Middle Period: 1987-1991

### Gold Is the Metal with the Broadest Shoulders (1987)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House (first release on own label) |
| Character | Demos, outtakes, transitional material |
| Era | Solar |

*Gold Is the Metal* held a unique position in the catalogue. Described in its own liner notes as "a completely separate package -- a stopgap and a breathing space -- the space between two twins," it was a collection of demos and outtakes that nonetheless contained substantial material. Its significance extends beyond its content: it launched Threshold House, Coil's own label, establishing the independent infrastructure that would serve the project for the remainder of its existence.

The album's title referenced alchemy directly -- gold as the ultimate goal of the alchemical process, the metal that resists corruption and symbolizes spiritual perfection. The self-description as "the space between two twins" evoked the liminal, between-state quality that characterized Coil's philosophical stance.

### Love's Secret Domain (1991)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Themes | Acid house, altered states, the LSD acronym |
| Key Instruments | Serge modular, advanced sampling, drum machines |
| Significance | Deliberate engagement with dance music culture |
| Era | Solar (terminal) |

*Love's Secret Domain* -- whose acronym, LSD, was entirely deliberate -- represented Coil's conscious engagement with acid house culture. The album absorbed the energy, rhythmic drive, and chemical associations of dance music while transforming them through Coil's esoteric methodology.

This was the final major release of the "solar" period and represented a boundary: the outward-directed, culturally engaged energy of Coil's first decade was here at its most expansive, reaching into dance culture and electronic music in ways that pushed the project toward new territory. The Serge modular synthesizer appeared for the first time on a Coil recording, introducing the instrument that would define the moon musick era.

## Side Projects and Aliases: 1985-1998

Coil operated under multiple aliases, each representing a distinct sonic territory. These were not mere pseudonyms but genuinely separate creative identities, each exploring dimensions of the sonic space that the "main" Coil identity could not accommodate.

### Sickness of Snakes (1985)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Release | *Nightmare Culture* |
| Collaboration | With Boyd Rice (NON) |
| Character | Harsh industrial, power electronics |

The earliest side-project release, a collaboration with Boyd Rice under the name Sickness of Snakes, produced *Nightmare Culture* -- a work rooted in the power electronics tradition that represented the most aggressive extreme of Coil's sonic range.

### ELpH: Worship the Glitch (1995)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Character | Glitch, electronic experimentation, digital error as material |
| Significance | Anticipated the "glitch" genre by several years |

The ELpH alias represented Coil at their most abstract and process-oriented. *Worship the Glitch* explored the sonic possibilities of digital error, feedback, and system malfunction as compositional material -- treating technological failure not as a problem to be solved but as a creative resource to be exploited.

This approach anticipated by several years the emergence of "glitch" as a recognized electronic music genre (associated with artists like Oval, Farmers Manual, and Pan Sonic). Coil's version was more visceral and less academic than much subsequent glitch music, grounded in the physical reality of malfunctioning equipment rather than in theoretical discourse about digital aesthetics.

### Black Light District (1996)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Release | *A Thousand Lights in a Darkened Room* |
| Character | Dark atmospheric electronics |
| Significance | Bridge between industrial roots and emerging drone interest |

The Black Light District alias produced darker, more atmospheric work that occupied the middle ground between Coil's industrial heritage and their emerging interest in drone and ambient forms. *A Thousand Lights in a Darkened Room* was a nocturnal record that prefigured the moon musick aesthetic without fully adopting it.

### Time Machines (1998)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Eskaton |
| Character | Pure drone, modular synthesis, psychoactive sound design |
| Key Contributor | Drew McDowall (original demo) |
| Artwork | John Dee's Hieroglyphic Monad, inverted |

*Time Machines* was perhaps the most radical single work in Coil's catalogue -- four extended drone pieces named after hallucinogenic compounds (telepathine, DOET, 5-MeO-DMT, psilocybin), composed entirely with modular synthesizers. The stated purpose was to produce "temporal slips" -- subjective alterations of time perception -- in the listener through purely sonic means.

Drew McDowall created the original demo, inspired by hypnotic states in Tibetan music. The album used filters and oscillators fine-tuned over extended sessions, with the results "tested and retested" for apparent psychoactive potency. The artwork's use of John Dee's Hieroglyphic Monad, inverted, connected the technological practice of modular synthesis to the Renaissance tradition of unifying all knowledge in a single symbol.

*Time Machines* is widely credited with opening floodgates in the drone and experimental electronic music world. Its influence on subsequent artists working with sustained tones, psychoacoustic effects, and consciousness-altering sound design has been immense.

## Late Period / Moon Musick: 1998-2004

### Moon's Milk Series (1998)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | Seasonal EPs |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Key Feature | Recorded on solstices and equinoxes |
| Key Instrument | Electric viola (William Breeze) |

The Moon's Milk series -- EPs timed to the turning points of the astronomical calendar -- marked the formal beginning of the moon musick era. The practice of calendar-aligned recording reflected Coil's magickal methodology: aligning creative work with cosmic cycles to amplify its effectiveness.

The addition of William Breeze's electric viola introduced sustained, bowed textures that became a defining element of the late-period sound -- organic timbres that complemented the electronic textures of the Serge and Synton modular systems.

### Astral Disaster (1999)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Prescription (initial limited edition of 99 copies) |
| Character | Modular synthesis, studio experimentation |
| Significance | First major Thighpaulsandra collaboration |

Initially released in an edition of 99 copies (a Qabalistically significant number), *Astral Disaster* marked the beginning of Thighpaulsandra's central role in the group and introduced the Serge modular synthesizer as a primary compositional tool. The extreme limitation of the initial pressing transformed each copy into a quasi-unique object -- consistent with Coil's treatment of releases as ritual artifacts.

### Musick to Play in the Dark Vol. 1 (1999)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Chalice |
| Character | Nocturnal electronics, drone, slow tempos |
| Key Instruments | Serge/Synton modular, Nord Modular |
| Era | Moon musick (definitive) |

The definitive statement of the moon musick aesthetic. *Musick to Play in the Dark Vol. 1* established the template for Coil's final creative period: long-form compositions built on slowly evolving synthesizer textures, treated vocals, and an atmosphere of contemplative darkness. The music was designed for nocturnal listening -- an explicit inversion of the extroverted, daylight energy of the solar period.

The album is now widely regarded as one of the finest achievements in experimental electronic music, its reputation growing steadily in the decades since its release.

### Musick to Play in the Dark Vol. 2 (2000)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Chalice |
| Character | Continuation and deepening of Vol. 1's approach |

The sequel expanded and deepened the nocturnal aesthetic of Vol. 1, further developing the possibilities of the Serge and Synton modular systems and exploring even more contemplative sonic territories.

### Constant Shallowness Leads to Evil (2000)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Eskaton |
| Character | Deep noise, harsh drone |

A more confrontational work within the moon musick period, *Constant Shallowness Leads to Evil* explored the harsher end of the drone spectrum -- demonstrating that the contemplative turn of the late period had not eliminated Coil's capacity for sonic aggression.

### The Remote Viewer (2002)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Character | Semi-structured ambient, return to compositional form |
| Key Instruments | Kurzweil K2000/K2600, analog synthesis |

A return to more structured composition within the moon musick framework, *The Remote Viewer* balanced the free-form exploration of the preceding releases with a greater degree of compositional architecture. The title referenced parapsychological research into remote viewing -- the claimed ability to perceive distant locations through extra-sensory means -- connecting the album to Coil's ongoing engagement with consciousness research.

### ANS (2003)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Significance | One of few Western groups to use the Russian ANS photoelectronic synthesizer |
| Location | Recorded at the Theremin Center, Moscow |

Coil's experimentation with the ANS photoelectronic synthesizer -- an instrument that converts graphical images drawn on glass plates into sound -- produced one of their most unique recordings. The album represented the intersection of visual and sonic domains, using an instrument that bypassed conventional musical interfaces entirely to generate sound from graphic input.

### Black Antlers (2004)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Character | Live-derived, final Balance sessions |
| Significance | Last studio recordings with both core members |

*Black Antlers* was derived from live recordings and represents the final studio material featuring both Balance and Christopherson. The album captures Coil at the end of their active period -- a document of what the project had become through twenty years of continuous evolution.

## Posthumous Releases: 2005-Present

### The Ape of Naples (2005)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Format | LP |
| Label | Threshold House |
| Completed by | Peter Christopherson |
| Significance | Memorial to Balance; Christopherson's final Coil statement |

Completed by Christopherson after Balance's death, *The Ape of Naples* served as both a posthumous album and a memorial -- Christopherson's farewell to his creative partner and to the project they had built together. The album included reworked versions of earlier material alongside new compositions, creating a retrospective frame that honored the scope of the collaboration.

### The New Backwards (2008 / 2022)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Label | Cold Spring (2008) / Infinite Fog (2022 extended edition) |
| Material | Disputed *Backwards* sessions |
| Significance | Extended edition provided definitive version |

The *Backwards*/*New Backwards* material became one of the most complex chapters in the posthumous release story. Different versions, overseen by different collaborators, appeared through different labels, creating a contested archival landscape. Infinite Fog's 2022 extended edition aimed to provide a definitive presentation of the material.

### Backwards (2015 / 2026)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Label | Cold Spring Records |
| Master | Danny Hyde |
| Significance | Alternative mastering by key collaborator; 2026 10-year anniversary edition |

Danny Hyde's mastering of the *Backwards* material, released through Cold Spring Records, represented an alternative presentation of the same source recordings that had produced *The New Backwards*. The existence of two distinct versions of essentially the same material, each with different mastering, editing, and sequencing choices, illustrated the complexity of the posthumous archival situation.

## Live Recordings

After a sixteen-year hiatus from live performance (1983-1999), Coil returned to the stage in December 1999 and performed approximately 50 concerts through 2004:

| Milestone | Date | Venue | Significance |
|-----------|------|-------|-------------|
| First live performance | August 4, 1983 | Magenta Club | Derek Jarman film screening |
| Final early performance | 1983 | -- | Only 4 performances total |
| Return to live performance | December 14, 1999 | Volksbuehne, Berlin | 18-minute ELpH set |
| Royal Festival Hall | 2000 | London | Two concerts; prestige venue |
| *Live One* through *Live Four* | 2003 | Various | Four live albums released |
| Final concert | 2004 | Dublin Electronic Music Festival | Last performance |

The live recordings document a dramatically different Coil from the studio project. Earlier songs were deliberately slowed and transformed -- "Ostia," "Slur," and "Teenage Lightning" became extended, meditative pieces that bore little surface resemblance to their recorded forms. The signature "fluffy suits" (full-body costumes) obscured individual identity, foregrounding the sound over the performers' physical presence.

## Remix Work

Coil's remix practice connected them to a broader network of experimental and electronic artists:

| Artist | Work Remixed | Significance |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Nine Inch Nails | *The Downward Spiral* material | Later released as *Recoiled* (2014) |
| Various | Compilation appearances | Expanded sonic range demonstrations |

The NIN remix work was particularly significant: Coil's reinterpretation of Trent Reznor's material demonstrated the radical difference between industrial-influenced rock and Coil's genuinely experimental approach to the same sonic territory.

## The Solar-Moon Arc

Balance's own framework for understanding Coil's output -- the division into "solar" and "moon musick" periods -- provides the most coherent organizing principle for the discography:

**Solar trajectory (1984-1991):** Progressive expansion from the ritualistic industrial of the debut through the sophisticated art-music of *Horse Rotorvator* to the acid house absorption of *Love's Secret Domain*. The arc is centrifugal -- reaching outward, engaging with the world, absorbing cultural energy.

**Transition (1992-1998):** The side projects serve as laboratories for exploring territories beyond the solar framework. *ELpH* deconstructs digital materiality. *Black Light District* explores nocturnal atmospherics. *Time Machines* discovers the pure drone that will define the moon musick era.

**Moon musick trajectory (1999-2004):** Progressive deepening from the nocturnal electronics of *Musick to Play in the Dark* through the unique explorations of *ANS* to the final live-derived sessions of *Black Antlers*. The arc is centripetal -- turning inward, withdrawing from the world, refining toward essence.

The posthumous releases complicate this framework by presenting material from various periods in new configurations, but Balance's solar/moon distinction remains the most useful guide to the intended trajectory of the work.
