# Sesame Street -- The Classroom

> **Domain:** Educational Television & Developmental Psychology
> **Module:** 3 -- Research-based pedagogy at scale
> **Through-line:** *Sesame Street did not invent the idea that television could teach. It invented the idea that television teaching should be tested, measured, iterated, and held accountable to evidence -- and then it proved the principle at a scale that no one has matched in fifty-five years. The show's architecture is not entertainment with educational content added. It is educational content with entertainment as the delivery mechanism. The distinction matters because it determines what gets cut when the budget shrinks.*

## 1. The Problem Statement: 1966

In the mid-1960s, American children's television was widely criticized as a "vast wasteland" -- a phrase originally used by FCC Chairman Newton Minow in 1961 to describe television generally, but increasingly applied to children's programming specifically. The landscape was dominated by cartoons designed to sell toys, with little educational value and no systematic research behind content decisions [1].

Simultaneously, the data was clear: by the late 1960s, 97% of American households owned at least one television set, and children watched an average of 27 hours per week. Television had more contact hours with preschool-age children than any institution except the family. The medium was reaching children whether anyone planned to teach them or not [2].

The question Joan Ganz Cooney asked in 1966 was not "Should television teach children?" but "Can it be proven to work?" The difference between those questions is the difference between advocacy and engineering [1].

---

## 2. Joan Ganz Cooney and Lloyd Morrisett

Joan Ganz Cooney was a documentary producer at Channel 13 (WNDT, now WNET) in New York. In 1966, she attended a dinner party at the home of Lewis Freedman (then head of programming at Channel 13). Among the guests was Lloyd Morrisett, vice president of the Carnegie Corporation. The conversation turned to whether television could be used deliberately to prepare preschool children for school. Morrisett, whose own young daughter had been observed staring transfixed at a television test pattern, was interested in the medium's attentional power [1].

Morrisett commissioned Cooney to produce a feasibility study. The result, delivered in 1967, was a 55-page document titled "The Potential Uses of Television in Preschool Education" -- a rigorous analysis of existing research, international models, and production economics. The study concluded that a research-based educational television program was feasible but would require sustained funding, academic partnership, and a production model fundamentally different from existing children's programming [1].

The Carnegie Corporation and the Ford Foundation provided initial funding. The United States government contributed through the Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB) and the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare. Total initial capitalization exceeded $8 million -- unprecedented for a children's television project [2].

## 3. The Children's Television Workshop

The Children's Television Workshop (CTW) was formally established in 1968 as a not-for-profit production company. It was designed from the outset as an unusual hybrid: equal parts television production company, educational research laboratory, and curriculum design studio. The organizational structure reflected the show's founding premise -- that educational television required the integration of expertise from domains that traditionally did not interact [1].

CTW assembled:

- **Television producers** (Jon Stone, Joe Raposo, Dave Connell) with experience in commercial and documentary production
- **Developmental psychologists** (Gerald Lesser, Edward Palmer) with expertise in how children learn
- **Educators** specializing in preschool curriculum
- **Puppeteers** (Jim Henson and his team)
- **Writers** who could translate educational objectives into narrative entertainment

The organization's name was later changed to **Sesame Workshop** (2000), reflecting its expanded mission beyond a single television show. As of 2026, Sesame Workshop operates as an independent nonprofit organization with a portfolio including television, digital media, international co-productions, and community engagement programs [3].

## 4. Gerald Lesser and the Curriculum Architecture

Gerald S. Lesser, a developmental psychologist at Harvard University, designed the show's curriculum framework. Lesser's approach was structured and systematic: before any segment was written, a curriculum document specified exactly what cognitive, literacy, numeracy, or social-emotional skill each segment was intended to teach. The curriculum was organized into domains:

### Curriculum Domains (Original 1969 Framework)

| Domain | Content Areas | Target Skills |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Cognitive | Letters, numbers, sorting, classification, relational concepts | Pre-literacy, pre-numeracy, logical reasoning |
| Language | Vocabulary, sentence structure, storytelling | Oral language development, comprehension |
| Social-Emotional | Cooperation, self-regulation, empathy, diversity | Social skills, emotional intelligence |
| Physical Environment | Nature, urban environment, community | Environmental awareness, spatial reasoning |

The critical innovation was the **curriculum-before-content** principle: the educational objectives were specified *first*, and then writers, producers, and performers were tasked with creating entertaining content that served those objectives. This is the reverse of the commercial model, in which entertainment content is created first and educational value, if any, is incidental [4].

Lesser's framework was reviewed and updated annually. Each season's curriculum was designed in collaboration with educators, tested against current developmental research, and adapted to reflect evolving understanding of how preschool children learn. This iterative process continued for every season of the show's five-decade run [4].

## 5. The Formative Research Model

Sesame Street was the first preschool educational television program to base its content and production decisions on laboratory and formative research. The model, developed primarily by Edward Palmer (CTW's Director of Research), operated through a multi-stage process [5]:

### Stage 1: Curriculum Specification

Educational objectives for each season defined by developmental psychologists and educators, informed by the latest peer-reviewed research in child development.

### Stage 2: Content Development

Writers and producers create segments (songs, sketches, animated shorts, Muppet interactions) designed to deliver specific curriculum objectives.

### Stage 3: Formative Testing

Before broadcast, segments were tested with groups of preschool children (typically drawn from Head Start programs). Researchers measured:

- **Attention**: How long did children look at the screen during each segment?
- **Comprehension**: Could children demonstrate understanding of the educational content after viewing?
- **Engagement**: Did children react emotionally (laughing, singing along, pointing)?
- **Distraction resistance**: How well did segments hold attention compared to competing stimuli?

### Stage 4: Revision

Segments that failed formative tests were revised or replaced. Segments that succeeded were refined and scheduled.

### Stage 5: Summative Evaluation

After broadcast, the Educational Testing Service (ETS) and later other research organizations conducted large-scale studies measuring the show's educational impact on viewing populations compared to control groups [5].

### The Critical Discovery

During formative testing of the pilot episodes, researchers made a discovery that shaped the show's entire architecture. Test audiences of Head Start children were observed to be:

- **Transfixed** during Muppet segments
- **Significantly less attentive** during human-only live-action segments

This observation directly shaped the show's format. Muppet characters were integrated into street scenes (a decision that was initially controversial among the educational advisors) because the research demonstrated that children's attention -- and therefore learning -- was maximized when Muppets were present. The Muppets were not decoration. They were the *delivery mechanism* for the educational content [5].

> **Related:** See [Jim Henson -- The Architect](01-jim-henson-the-architect.md) for the camera-intimacy innovation that made Muppet characters compelling on screen. The Head Start data confirmed experimentally what Henson had designed intuitively: television puppets, properly executed, command attention more effectively than human performers for preschool audiences.

## 6. Henson and the Muppet Integration

Jim Henson was invited to contribute Muppet characters to Sesame Street in 1968. In a decision that would have enormous financial consequences decades later, Henson waived his performance fee for the first season in exchange for retaining ownership of all Muppet characters created for the program. This meant that Big Bird, Oscar the Grouch, Cookie Monster, Ernie, Bert, Grover, and all other Sesame Street Muppets remained Henson's intellectual property even as they became the most recognizable characters in children's television [1].

### Key Sesame Street Muppet Characters

| Character | Performer | Introduced | Educational Role |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Big Bird | Caroll Spinney | 1969 | Child surrogate; asks the questions children ask; 8'2" full-body puppet |
| Oscar the Grouch | Caroll Spinney | 1969 | Emotional regulation; dealing with negative feelings; accepting diversity |
| Cookie Monster | Frank Oz | 1969 | Impulse control; humor; self-awareness ("Me want cookie!") |
| Bert | Frank Oz | 1969 | Rule-following; patience; the straight man in Ernie-Bert dynamic |
| Ernie | Jim Henson | 1969 | Curiosity; playfulness; creative thinking; bath-time songs |
| Grover | Frank Oz | 1970 | Helpfulness; physical comedy; "near and far" concepts |
| The Count | Jerry Nelson | 1972 | Numbers; counting; mathematical enthusiasm |
| Elmo | Kevin Clash | 1984 (prominent from 1985) | Younger child surrogate; third-person speech; warmth |
| Abby Cadabby | Leslie Carrara-Rudolph | 2006 | Fairy-tale newcomer; learning school readiness skills |

The Sesame Street Muppets were purchased by Sesame Workshop in 2000 for an undisclosed sum, separating them permanently from the classic Muppet characters that Disney later acquired. This means that Kermit the Frog (Disney) and Big Bird (Sesame Workshop) are now owned by different companies despite being created by the same production team [6].

## 7. Premiere and Early Reception

Sesame Street premiered on November 10, 1969, on the National Educational Television (NET) network, which became the Public Broadcasting Service (PBS) in 1970. The show was broadcast on approximately 67.6% of American televisions in its first week, earning a 3.3 Nielsen rating (approximately 1.9 million households). By its second week, it was reaching nearly 2 million homes [2].

The set was modeled on a Harlem street corner -- deliberately urban, racially diverse, and working-class. Producer Jon Stone cast a diverse ensemble of human actors, with white actors as the minority in the cast. The urban setting was a conscious choice to make the show relatable to the low-income, minority children it was designed to serve [1].

The show's name came from "Open Sesame" -- the magical phrase from Ali Baba and the Forty Thieves, conveying the idea of a place where exciting discoveries happen. Over fifty alternate names were considered and rejected before "Sesame Street" was chosen [2].

### Initial Critical Response

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive. The show received extensive coverage in major newspapers and magazines. However, there were dissenters:

- Some educators worried that the show's fast-paced, commercial-style format would shorten children's attention spans
- The Mississippi State Commission for Educational Television voted in May 1970 not to air Sesame Street because of its "highly integrated cast" -- a ban that lasted less than a month after public backlash forced reversal [7]
- A small number of educational theorists questioned whether television could ever substitute for human interaction in early learning

## 8. Educational Impact: Documented Outcomes

Sesame Street's educational impact has been the subject of over 1,000 published research studies -- more than any other television program in history [8].

### Key Research Findings

| Metric | Finding | Source |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Grade-level enrollment | Children exposed to Sesame Street were 14% more likely to be at the correct grade level in middle/high school | Wellesley College / University of Maryland (Levine & Kearney), 2016 [9] |
| 1970 summative evaluation | Significant positive educational impact confirmed for regular viewers vs. non-viewers | Educational Testing Service (ETS), 1970 [5] |
| Daily viewership (1979) | 9 million US children under age 6 watching daily | Sesame Street 10th anniversary production data [2] |
| Preschooler reach (1996) | 95% of all American preschoolers had watched by age 3 | Published survey (Sesame Workshop) [8] |
| Global reach (2019) | Broadcast in 120+ countries; near-universal brand awareness globally | Sesame Workshop 50th anniversary data [3] |
| Emmy Awards (by 2006) | Most Emmy Awards of any children's show; 12 consecutive Outstanding Children's Series | Television Academy records [10] |
| Research body (2001) | Over 1,000 research studies published | Fisch & Truglio, *"G" is for Growing* (2001) [8] |

### The Levine-Kearney Study (2016)

The most comprehensive modern study of Sesame Street's long-term educational impact was published by Phillip Levine (Wellesley College) and Melissa Kearney (University of Maryland) in 2016. Using variation in television signal coverage across counties during the show's early years as a natural experiment, they found that children in areas with better access to Sesame Street were approximately 14% more likely to be enrolled at the appropriate grade level during their school years. The effects were largest for boys, Black children, and children in economically disadvantaged areas -- precisely the populations the show was designed to serve [9].

### The ETS Summative Evaluations

The Educational Testing Service conducted summative evaluations after Sesame Street's first and second seasons (1970, 1971). These studies, using pre- and post-test designs with control groups, found statistically significant gains in the tested domains (letters, numbers, body parts, sorting, classification) for children who watched the show regularly compared to those who did not. The gains were largest for children from disadvantaged backgrounds [5].

## 9. The Equity Mission

Sesame Street was explicitly designed to close the education gap between low-income, minority children and their middle-class, white peers. This was not a secondary consideration or a marketing message -- it was the founding purpose [1].

### Design Choices Serving Equity

- **The urban set**: Modeled on a Harlem block; relatable to inner-city children who were the primary target audience
- **Racial diversity**: The cast was deliberately diverse from the first episode, with white actors as the minority
- **Spanish-language integration**: Spanish vocabulary and bilingual characters (notably Luis and Maria) were included from early seasons, decades before "diversity" became a corporate mandate
- **Muppet characters as equalizers**: Every child, regardless of background, could identify with Big Bird (a child learning to navigate the world) or Cookie Monster (someone struggling with impulse control)
- **Free broadcast**: The show aired on public television, available without cable subscription to any household with a television and an antenna

### The Mississippi Ban

In May 1970, the Mississippi State Commission for Educational Television voted not to air Sesame Street because of its "highly integrated cast of children." The commission was reported to have said that Mississippi was "not yet ready" for such programming. The decision generated immediate national media coverage and public backlash. Within weeks, the commission reversed its ban. The incident documented, in real time, the tension between the show's equity mission and the racial politics of the American South [7].

### Measured Equity Impact

The Levine-Kearney (2016) study specifically examined whether Sesame Street's benefits were equitably distributed. Their findings confirmed that the greatest educational gains accrued to the populations the show was designed to serve: boys, Black children, and children from economically disadvantaged households. The show's equity mission was not merely aspirational -- it was empirically validated [9].

## 10. Global Reach and International Adaptations

Sesame Street has been adapted for broadcast in over 120 countries, with approximately 140 international versions produced as of 2019. These are not simple translations -- each international co-production adapts the show's format, characters, and curriculum to local cultural contexts, languages, and educational priorities [3].

### International Adaptation Model

| Element | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| Curriculum | Adapted to local educational standards and developmental priorities |
| Characters | Local Muppet characters created alongside familiar international characters |
| Set design | Adapted to reflect local architecture and community settings |
| Language | Produced in local language(s); bilingual elements where culturally appropriate |
| Cultural content | Music, food, customs, and social norms reflect the local context |
| Research | Local formative research conducted to validate content effectiveness |

### Notable International Versions

| Version | Country | Launched | Distinctive Feature |
|---------|---------|----------|-------------------|
| *Sesamstrasse* | Germany | 1973 | One of the first international versions |
| *Plaza Sesamo* | Mexico/Latin America | 1972 | Spanish-language; Pan-American audience |
| *1, Rue Sesame* | France | 1978 | French cultural adaptation |
| *Sesame Park* | Canada | 1996 | Bilingual English/French |
| *Alam Simsim* | Egypt | 2000 | Arabic-language; gender equity curriculum |
| *Takalani Sesame* | South Africa | 2000 | 11 official languages; HIV awareness |
| *Galli Galli Sim Sim* | India | 2006 | Hindi; girls' education focus |
| *Ahlan Simsim* | Middle East/North Africa | 2020 | Arabic; refugee children programming |

The international adaptation model demonstrates that Sesame Street's architecture -- research-based curriculum, formative testing, Muppet delivery mechanism -- is culturally portable. The content changes; the methodology is constant [3].

## 11. Platform Evolution: PBS to HBO to Netflix

### The PBS Era (1969-2016)

For its first 47 years, Sesame Street aired new episodes exclusively on PBS, available free over-the-air to all American households. The show was funded through a combination of CPB grants, foundation support, corporate underwriting, and Sesame Workshop's licensing revenue from character merchandise [2].

### The HBO Migration (2016-2022)

In 2015, Sesame Workshop announced a deal with HBO under which new episodes would premiere on HBO (and its streaming service HBO Go/HBO Max) with PBS receiving the episodes nine months later. The decision was driven by financial necessity: declining public funding and changing viewing habits had reduced the economic sustainability of the PBS-first model. The deal was controversial -- critics argued it created a paywall between the show's educational content and the low-income children it was designed to serve. Defenders noted that PBS retained access to all episodes and that the HBO funding enabled increased production [11].

### The Netflix Deal (2025)

In 2025, Sesame Workshop signed a deal with Netflix to stream new episodes on the same day as PBS broadcast. This represented a shift from the HBO model's delayed PBS access back toward simultaneous availability, though through a subscription streaming service rather than free-to-air broadcast [11].
