# The Muppets -- The Characters

> **Domain:** Character Design & Cultural History
> **Module:** 2 -- From Sam and Friends to the Disney era
> **Through-line:** *The Muppets are not a property. They are a language -- a way of encoding emotional complexity into foam and fabric and performing it through a television camera so that a child and an adult sitting on the same couch can both understand something true, each at their own level. That the language survived its creator is proof that Henson built it to last.*

## 1. The Name and the Concept

Jim Henson created the term "Muppet" in the mid-1950s. He initially described it as a portmanteau of "marionette" and "puppet," though he later gave varying and sometimes contradictory accounts of its origin. In at least one interview, he suggested it was simply a word he liked the sound of. Regardless of etymology, the term became the brand name for a specific style of puppet character: fabric-covered, operated from below by a performer whose hand controls the mouth, with a soft, expressive face designed for television close-ups rather than stage distance [1].

The conceptual distinction between Muppets and other puppets is not taxonomic -- it is architectural. A Muppet is designed for a camera. Its proportions, materials, and expressive range are optimized for the intimate close-up rather than the distant wide shot. This is what separates Henson's work from the broader puppet tradition and what makes the "Muppet" designation meaningful as a creative standard rather than merely a trademark [2].

## 2. Sam and Friends: The First Generation

*Sam and Friends* aired on WRC-TV Washington from 1955 to 1961, producing approximately 86 episodes (each five minutes long). The cast was small and abstract -- the characters did not represent specific animals or types. Sam was a bald, round-faced humanoid. Kermit was an unnamed lizard-creature. Other characters included Yorick (a skull-like figure used in lip-sync comedy), Harry the Hipster, and Mushmellon [1].

The show's format was simple: Henson and Jane Nebel performed puppet lip-syncs to popular songs and comedy recordings, interspersed with original sketches. The comedy style was anarchic and often violent in a cartoonish way (characters eating each other, exploding, being destroyed and reconstituted). This energy, more Looney Tunes than Howdy Doody, established the Muppet comedic sensibility: broadly physical, gleefully absurd, but never cruel [1].

## 3. Kermit the Frog: Evolution of an Icon

Kermit's development from abstract creature to identifiable frog to global cultural icon spans the full arc of Henson's career.

### Phase 1: The Abstract Creature (1955-1968)

On *Sam and Friends*, Kermit was not a frog. He was a simple puppet with no specific species identity -- a round head with ping-pong ball eyes and a flat mouth, covered in green fabric from Henson's mother's old coat. The design was remarkable for its minimalism: two eyes, a mouth, and a performer's arm. The character's personality was defined entirely by Henson's performance [1].

### Phase 2: The Frog Identity (1968-1976)

As Henson's television appearances expanded in the late 1960s, Kermit gradually acquired frog-like attributes: a collar was added (suggesting a frog's wider throat), the color was standardized to a specific green, and the character began to be described as a frog in scripts and interviews. Don Sahlin built the definitive Kermit puppet that established the design standard still in use today [1].

### Phase 3: The Star (1976-1990)

On *The Muppet Show*, Kermit was the central character -- the harried stage manager trying to hold a chaotic variety show together. This role -- the calm center in a storm of absurdity -- perfectly suited Henson's performance style: understated, warm, occasionally exasperated, fundamentally optimistic. Kermit became the emotional anchor of the Muppet ensemble, the character through whom the audience experienced the chaos [3].

*The Muppet Movie* (1979) deepened Kermit's characterization with "Rainbow Connection," a song about the gap between dreams and reality that became Henson's signature piece. The song, written by Paul Williams and Kenneth Ascher, was nominated for the Academy Award for Best Original Song and was preserved in the Library of Congress National Recording Registry in 2020 as "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant" [4].

### Phase 4: After Henson (1990-present)

After Henson's death, Kermit was performed by Steve Whitmire (1990-2017) and subsequently by Matt Vogel (2017-present). The character's personality has remained consistent across performers, a testament to both Henson's original design and the Muppet tradition of treating characters as entities independent of any single performer [5].

## 4. The Commercial Years

During the 1960s, the Muppets appeared in hundreds of television commercials, primarily for regional brands. The most famous were the Wilkins Coffee commercials produced for the Washington, D.C. market, featuring the characters Wilkins (a cheerful Muppet who loved the coffee) and Wontkins (a grumpy Muppet who didn't). The ten-second spots typically featured Wilkins inflicting slapstick violence on Wontkins for rejecting the coffee -- a format that was enormously popular and widely imitated [1].

Rowlf the Dog achieved broader visibility through a regular role on *The Jimmy Dean Show* (ABC, 1963-1966). As the first Muppet with a weekly national television appearance, Rowlf demonstrated that puppet characters could function as full ensemble members alongside human performers -- interacting naturally, delivering punchlines, and developing a recognizable personality over time. Rowlf's success was a proof of concept for the variety-show format that would become *The Muppet Show* [1].

Other notable appearances during this period included guest spots on *The Ed Sullivan Show* (multiple appearances, 1966-1971), *The Jack Paar Show*, and *The Steve Allen Show*. These appearances built national awareness of the Muppets as a brand associated with sophisticated, adult-friendly humor rather than exclusively children's content [2].

## 5. The Muppet Show: Rejected, Greenlit, Beloved

### The Network Rejections

By the mid-1970s, Henson had a proven track record with Sesame Street (since 1969) and a growing roster of television specials. He wanted to produce a primetime variety show starring the Muppets. All three American broadcast networks (ABC, CBS, NBC) rejected the concept, judging that a puppet-based show could not sustain an adult audience in primetime [1].

### The Lew Grade Deal

British producer Lew Grade, head of Associated Television (ATV), offered Henson the opportunity to produce the show in the UK. Grade's ITC Entertainment would distribute internationally. The deal gave Henson creative freedom and access to ATV's Elstree Studios in Borehamwood, Hertfordshire. The show would be produced for the international syndication market, bypassing the American networks entirely [1].

### Production and Premiere

*The Muppet Show* premiered in the UK on September 5, 1976 and was syndicated in the US beginning in September 1976. It ran for five seasons (120 episodes, 1976-1981). The format was a variety show within a variety show: each episode featured a celebrity guest star performing musical numbers and sketches alongside the Muppet ensemble, framed by a backstage narrative in which Kermit (as stage manager) attempted to maintain order amid chaos [3].

### Global Audience

By its third season, *The Muppet Show* reached a weekly worldwide audience of 235 million viewers across more than 100 countries, making it one of the most-watched television programs in the world at that time [1]. The show's guest stars ranged from Rudolf Nureyev to Alice Cooper to Steve Martin, reflecting Henson's insistence that the show appeal to adults as well as children.

The Muppet Show's cast of characters, with their original performers, constitutes one of the most recognized character ensembles in entertainment history.

### The Frank Oz Catalog

Frank Oz performed more principal Muppet characters than any other performer. His range -- from Miss Piggy's bombastic femininity to Cookie Monster's childlike hunger to Bert's pedantic irritability to Fozzie's desperate need to be funny -- demonstrated that Muppet performance was a genuine acting discipline, not merely puppetry. Oz's characters were typically the bold, assertive counterpoints to Henson's gentler leads [6].

## 8. Post-Henson Transition

Henson's death in 1990 left the company and the characters in a state of profound uncertainty. The performers who had worked alongside Henson for decades faced the challenge of continuing characters whose souls had been defined by specific creative partnerships. Steve Whitmire assumed the role of Kermit, a responsibility he held for 27 years (1990-2017) before being replaced by Matt Vogel in a transition that generated significant public discussion about character stewardship and performer succession [5].

The company's creative output continued but changed in character. Brian Henson directed two well-regarded Muppet films (*The Muppet Christmas Carol*, *Muppet Treasure Island*) that maintained the tone and quality of the Henson era. However, subsequent projects -- including the television film *It's a Very Merry Muppet Christmas Movie* (2002) and various direct-to-video productions -- were more uneven [5].

The fundamental challenge was existential: could characters designed as expressions of specific performer-writer-director relationships survive the loss of the founding relationship? The answer, demonstrated across three decades, is "yes, but differently." The Muppets retained their core appeal -- warmth, anarchic humor, musical energy -- but the specific creative tension that had defined the Henson-Oz era was irreplaceable [1].

## 9. The Disney Acquisition

The Walt Disney Company purchased the classic Muppet characters in February 2004 for a reported $75 million. The acquisition included the Muppet Show characters, Bear in the Big Blue House, and related properties. It explicitly excluded the Sesame Street Muppets (owned by Sesame Workshop since 2000) and the Fraggle Rock characters, Dark Crystal, Labyrinth, and Farscape (retained by the Jim Henson Company) [1].

### IP Split Summary

| Entity | Properties | Year of Separation |
|--------|------------|-------------------|
| The Walt Disney Company | Classic Muppet characters (Kermit, Miss Piggy, Fozzie, Gonzo, etc.); Muppet Show library; Bear in the Big Blue House | 2004 |
| Sesame Workshop | All Sesame Street Muppet characters (Big Bird, Elmo, Cookie Monster, Oscar, Grover, etc.) | 2000 |
| The Jim Henson Company | Fraggle Rock; The Dark Crystal; Labyrinth; Farscape; Creature Shop; Jim Henson Company production library | Retained continuously |

This three-way split meant that characters created by the same team, in the same workshops, using the same materials and techniques, now belonged to three separate corporate entities with different creative priorities and brand strategies. Cookie Monster and Kermit the Frog, both performed by the same era of puppeteers in the same buildings, cannot appear together on screen without a licensing agreement between Disney and Sesame Workshop [8].

## 10. The Modern Muppet Era

Under Disney ownership, the Muppets have been deployed across multiple formats:

- **Film**: *The Muppets* (2011) and *Muppets Most Wanted* (2014)
- **Television**: *The Muppets* (ABC, 2015-2016), a mockumentary-style series
- **Streaming**: *Muppets Now* (Disney+, 2020), an unscripted variety format; *The Muppets Mayhem* (Disney+, 2023), following the Electric Mayhem band
- **Special**: *The Muppets* (2024/2025 Disney+ specials)

The 2011 film, written by Jason Segel and Nicholas Stoller, was the most successful creative reinterpretation, earning $88.6 million domestically and winning the Academy Award for Best Original Song. It succeeded by treating the Muppets' cultural absence as a story element, making the characters' return to relevance the emotional core of the narrative [7].
