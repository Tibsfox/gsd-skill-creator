# Architecture, Buildings & Social Mission

> **Domain:** Urban Architecture, Social Services & Community Infrastructure
> **Module:** 5 -- Building Inventory, Rehabilitation, Affordable Housing, Social Services, Current Scale, and Verification
> **Through-line:** *The 1971 preservation initiative protected not just buildings but social character -- one of the first preservation movements in American history to insist that saving the structure without saving the community within it was no preservation at all.* The Market's architecture is its social mission made physical. The narrow arcade forces eye contact between vendor and customer. The steep hillside creates vertical layers where different economies coexist. The affordable housing above the shops means that residents wake up, walk downstairs, and buy their groceries from someone they know by name. None of this is accidental. All of it was nearly lost.

## 1. Building Inventory

The Pike Place Market Historic District comprises a complex of interconnected buildings constructed between 1907 and 2017, cascading down a steep bluff from Pike Place at the top to Western Avenue at the bottom. The building inventory is not a collection of discrete structures but an architectural system -- buildings connected by ramps, stairways, covered walkways, and shared walls in a configuration that evolved organically over more than a century [1].

| Building | Built | Current Function | Notes |
|----------|-------|-----------------|-------|
| Main Arcade | 1907 | Primary daystall row; farmer and craft vendor stalls | Original covered arcade by Frank Goodwin |
| Triangle Market | 1908 | Commercial retail | Adjacent to Main Arcade |
| Athenian Inn (building) | 1909 | Restaurant/bar | Second-oldest continuously operating Market restaurant |
| Sanitary Market | 1911 | Butchers, delicatessens, bakeries | Named for modern (1911) sanitary standards |
| Corner Market | 1912 | Commercial retail; Three Girls Bakery | Major commercial building |
| Economy Market | 1900s | Specialty retail; DeLaurenti | Connects to Post Alley |
| Post Alley | Continuous | Cobblestone alley; restaurants, shops, Gum Wall | Parallel to Pike Place |
| DownUnder Levels | Various | Antiques, comics, specialty shops | Multi-level spaces below main arcade |
| Municipal Market Bldg | Burned 1974 | Surface lot for 43 years; MarketFront site | Lost to fire |
| MarketFront | 2017 | Mixed use; vendors, housing, public space | First major Historic District expansion in 30+ years |
| Overlook Walk | 2024 | Pedestrian connection | Links Victor Steinbrueck Park to waterfront |

## 2. The Main Arcade

The Main Arcade is the Market's spine -- the covered walkway where the original daystalls were established in 1907 and where farmers and craftspeople continue to sell today. Frank Goodwin's first structure was a simple covered sidewalk providing rain shelter for vendors. Over the following years, the arcade was expanded, enclosed, and connected to adjacent buildings to create the continuous covered commercial corridor that visitors experience today [2].

### Architectural Character

The arcade's architecture is utilitarian, not decorative. Exposed beams, concrete floors, and minimal ornamentation create a space that is entirely focused on the transaction between vendor and customer. The narrow width of the arcade -- wide enough for two streams of pedestrian traffic with vendor stalls on both sides -- forces physical proximity between shoppers and sellers. This is not a design flaw; it is the design's primary virtue. The narrow arcade creates the density of human interaction that makes the Market feel alive.

The arcade's north-south orientation runs roughly parallel to the waterfront, capturing natural light from the west (Elliott Bay side) in the afternoon. During the winter months, when Seattle's light is low and angled, the arcade's covered structure provides shelter while still admitting enough light to display produce and flowers effectively.

### The Daystall Layout

Daystall positions within the arcade are assigned by the PDA according to the rules established by the Hildt/Licata-Hildt Agreement. Farmer stalls are concentrated in the sections closest to the main entrance, reflecting the Market's founding identity as a produce market. Craft vendor stalls are distributed in other sections. The specific assignment rotates, but the general pattern -- food nearest the entrance, crafts further in -- has been stable for decades.

## 3. The Supporting Structures

The buildings surrounding the Main Arcade each serve distinct functions within the Market's commercial ecosystem:

### Sanitary Market (1911)

The Sanitary Market was named to advertise its compliance with early-20th-century hygiene standards -- enclosed spaces, plumbing, and surfaces that could be cleaned. It was built to house butchers, delicatessens, and bakeries -- food categories that required more controlled environments than the open arcade. The name, which sounds oddly clinical to modern ears, was a genuine selling point in an era when food safety in public markets was a serious concern [3].

### Corner Market (1912)

The Corner Market is one of the Market's major commercial buildings, located at the intersection of Pike Place and Pike Street. Three Girls Bakery was among its first tenants. The building's corner location gives it street frontage on two sides, making it one of the Market's most visible structures and a natural anchor for pedestrian traffic moving between Pike Place and the adjacent streets.

### Economy Market

The Economy Market connects the main arcade area to Post Alley and houses DeLaurenti Food & Wine among other tenants. Its name, like the Sanitary Market's, reflects early-20th-century commercial values -- "economy" meaning affordability and value, a marketing message directed at the working-class consumers who were the Market's primary customers.

## 4. Post Alley and the Vertical Market

Post Alley runs parallel to Pike Place on the Market's eastern side, creating a secondary commercial corridor that is narrower, quieter, and more intimate than the main arcade. The alley's cobblestone surface, brick walls, and lack of vehicular traffic create a pedestrian environment that feels distinctly different from the arcade's commercial energy [4].

### The Vertical Dimension

The Market's most distinctive architectural feature is its vertical organization. Built on a steep bluff, the Market contains multiple levels stacked between Pike Place at the top and Western Avenue at the bottom. These levels are connected by ramps, stairways, and elevators that create a three-dimensional commercial labyrinth.

  +-----------------------------------------+
  |  DownUnder Level 1                       |
  |  Specialty shops, vintage clothing       |
  +-----------------------------------------+
              |

  +-----------------------------------------+

  |  Antiques, collectibles, small cafes     |

  ELLIOTT BAY WATERFRONT

This vertical structure means that the Market contains significantly more commercial space than its nine-acre footprint would suggest on a flat site. The hillside effectively multiplies the usable area by creating multiple levels, each with its own character and tenant mix.

## 5. The 1974 Fire and the Municipal Market

On October 23, 1974, a fire destroyed the Municipal Market Building -- one of the Market's original structures. The site remained a surface parking lot for 43 years, a gap in the Market's physical fabric that interrupted the continuity of the arcade and severed the direct connection between the Market's upper levels and the waterfront below [5].

The fire occurred just one year after the PDA's establishment, during the early phase of the rehabilitation program. The loss of the Municipal Market Building was a setback for the preservation effort, but it also created the site that would eventually become MarketFront -- the Market's most significant physical expansion since the founding era.

## 6. MarketFront: The 2017 Expansion

MarketFront, which opened in 2017, was built on the site of the destroyed Municipal Market Building. It is the first major expansion of the Pike Place Market Historic District in over 30 years and represents a carefully designed integration of new construction into a historic context [6].

### Design and Function

MarketFront includes:

- **Vendor spaces:** New daystall positions and permanent shop spaces
- **Affordable housing:** 40 units of low-income housing, continuing the Market's residential tradition
- **Public plaza:** Open space with views of Elliott Bay
- **Parking:** Below-grade parking serving the Market district
- **Pedestrian connections:** Improved access between the upper Market and the waterfront

### The Capital Campaign

The MarketFront development was funded in part through a capital campaign involving over 7,000 donors ("Piggybackers") contributing to a $9 million fundraising effort. The campaign's success demonstrated continuing public investment in the Market's future -- echoing the 1971 ballot initiative's demonstration of public commitment to preservation [7].

### Historic District Integration

MarketFront's design was subject to Historical Commission review, ensuring that the new construction was compatible with the Market's historic character. The challenge was to create a building that was visibly contemporary -- not a fake-historic pastiche -- while respecting the scale, materials, and spatial relationships of the surrounding historic structures.

## 7. Overlook Walk: The 2024 Connection

The Overlook Walk, completed in 2024, is a pedestrian connection that links Victor Steinbrueck Park at the Market's northern end to the Seattle waterfront below. The connection completes a long-planned integration of the Market with the city's broader waterfront redevelopment [8].

The Overlook Walk's completion has practical implications for the Market's circulation patterns. Visitors can now walk from the waterfront through MarketFront, up through the Market's DownUnder levels and main arcade, out to Victor Steinbrueck Park, and back down to the waterfront via the Overlook Walk -- a complete loop that connects the Market to the city's waterfront infrastructure.

## 8. Affordable Housing

The Market contains eight buildings with residential units housing 450+ residents, with emphasis on low-income and senior housing. This residential component is not an afterthought or an add-on -- it is a core element of the Market's identity and one of the values explicitly protected by the 1971 preservation initiative [9].

### The Social Character Mandate

The 1971 initiative's inclusion of "social character" as a protected value was extraordinary for its time. Most preservation ordinances in the early 1970s protected physical structures -- buildings, facades, spatial relationships. The Pike Place initiative went further, asserting that the community living within and around the Market was as much a part of its character as the buildings themselves.

This meant that the preservation framework protected not just the right of buildings to stand but the right of low-income residents to live in them. It was an early articulation of a principle that would later become central to preservation policy nationally: that displacement of existing communities undermines the purpose of preserving the physical structures they inhabit.

### Housing Stock

The Market's residential units include studio apartments, one-bedroom units, and shared facilities. Rents are maintained below market rate through the PDA's management of the residential buildings. Residents include seniors, low-income workers, and individuals who depend on the Market's proximity to social services, transit, and daily commercial needs.

The 40 affordable housing units added in the 2017 MarketFront expansion represent the most recent addition to the Market's residential stock and demonstrate continuing commitment to the housing mandate.

## 9. The Five Social Service Programs

The Pike Place Market Foundation funds and operates five social service programs within or adjacent to the Historic District. These programs serve the Market's residential community, its vendor community, and the broader low-income population of downtown Seattle [10].

### 1. Pike Market Medical Clinic

The clinic provides free and low-cost healthcare within the Historic District. Located within the Market complex, it serves residents, vendors, and community members who may not have access to healthcare through employment or insurance. The clinic's integration into the Market means that patients can access medical care as part of their daily routine -- not as a separate trip to a separate institution.

### 2. Pike Market Senior Center

The Senior Center provides services and community programming for elderly residents of the Market and surrounding neighborhoods. Programming includes meals, social activities, health screening, and referral services. The center serves as a social hub for the Market's older residents, many of whom live alone in the Market's affordable housing units.

### 3. Downtown Food Bank

The Downtown Food Bank provides food access for low-income community members. Its location within the Market district creates an irony that is not lost on anyone: a food bank operating inside a food market, serving people who cannot afford to buy the produce displayed in the stalls above. The juxtaposition is honest rather than hypocritical -- it acknowledges that proximity to food and access to food are not the same thing.

### 4. Pike Market Childcare and Preschool

On-site childcare for residents and workers addresses one of the most significant barriers to economic participation for low-income parents. A parent who can drop their child at childcare within the Market and then work at a stall or shop in the same building has a fundamentally different economic equation than a parent who must find and pay for childcare across the city.

### 5. Market Foundation Community Resource Center (The Market Commons)

The Market Commons provides wrap-around social support -- connecting individuals to housing assistance, employment services, mental health resources, and other community services. It functions as a navigator within the social services system, helping people who need multiple forms of support access them efficiently.

## 10. The Market Foundation

The Pike Place Market Foundation, established in 1982, is the nonprofit organization that raises funds for the Market's social service programs. Its funding mechanisms include:

- **Rachel the Pig:** approximately $20,000 per year in visitor donations
- **Piggybackers campaign:** $9 million capital campaign for MarketFront (7,000+ donors)
- **Annual fundraising events and direct donations**
- **Grants from government and private foundations**

The Foundation operates independently from the PDA, which manages the Market's commercial operations. This separation ensures that social service funding does not compete directly with commercial operations for institutional attention -- each organization has its own mandate, board, and funding streams [11].

## 11. Governance Structure

The Market's governance is distributed across seven organizations, each with distinct responsibilities:

| Entity | Role | Established |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Pike Place Market PDA | Not-for-profit public corporation; owns and manages operations | 1973 |
| Historical Commission | 12-member citizen body; reviews all design and use changes | 1971 |
| Pike Place Market Foundation | Nonprofit; raises funds for social services | 1982 |
| Pike Place Merchants Association | Member organization; legal, accounting, insurance services; publishes Market News | 1973 |
| Daystall Tenants Association (DTA) | Represents daystall vendor interests; meets on Desimone Bridge | Late 1980s |
| United Farmers Coalition (UFC) | Represents daystall food vendors (produce, flower, processed food) | 1998 |
| Pike Market Performers' Guild | Represents 60+ permitted buskers | 2001 |

This multi-organization structure creates a system of distributed authority that prevents any single entity from dominating Market governance. The PDA manages day-to-day operations, but the Historical Commission can block changes, the Foundation operates social services independently, the Merchants Association represents permanent tenants, and the DTA and UFC represent temporary vendors. Each organization has its own constituency and its own voice in Market governance.

## 13. The 2008 Seismic Levy

In 2008, Seattle voters approved a levy of $71-73 million for seismic upgrades and infrastructure modernization of Market buildings. The levy addressed a serious structural concern: the Market's buildings, most constructed before modern seismic codes, were vulnerable to earthquake damage in a region with significant seismic risk (the Cascadia Subduction Zone and the Seattle Fault) [13].

The levy's approval demonstrated continuing public commitment to the Market's long-term survival. Like the 1971 ballot initiative, it required citizens to vote directly for the expenditure of public funds on Market preservation -- and like the 1971 initiative, it passed.

## 14. The Synthesis: 1907-2026

The arc of Pike Place Market's history from 1907 to 2026 is a story about what cities choose to value. At every decision point -- the founding, the internment, the demolition proposal, the preservation vote, the rehabilitation, the expansion -- Seattle was forced to choose between competing visions of what the Market should be.

In 1907, the choice was between the middlemen's monopoly and the farmers' direct-sale model. The farmers won, and the Market was born.

From 1907 to 1941, the choice was between acknowledging the Japanese-American community's economic centrality and continuing to discriminate against them in stall assignments. The city chose discrimination -- and when the federal government offered a more radical option in 1942, Seattle did not resist. The community that built the Market was destroyed.

In 1963, the choice was between the modernizers' vision of a new, efficient, automobile-friendly downtown and the preservationists' vision of a messy, old, human-scaled commercial ecology. The city's leadership chose demolition. The citizens overruled them.

In 1973, the choice was between private property rights and public institutional governance. The PDA charter chose governance -- creating a purpose-bound institution to protect the Market's public function from private market pressures.

In 2008, the choice was between seismic risk and the cost of structural improvement. The citizens chose investment.

In 2017, the choice was between a parking lot and a new Market building. MarketFront filled the gap left by the 1974 fire, reconnecting the Market to the waterfront and adding 40 units of affordable housing.

In 2024, the Overlook Walk completed the physical connection between the Market and the waterfront -- closing a loop that had been open since the Market's founding on the bluff above the water.

At each of these points, the easier choice was available. Let the middlemen profit. Let the internees disappear. Let the developers build. Let the buildings age. Let the parking lot remain. At each point, enough people chose the harder option to keep the Market alive.

The Market's 117-year survival is not inevitable. It is the accumulated result of specific choices made by specific people at specific moments -- choices that could have gone the other way and nearly did.
