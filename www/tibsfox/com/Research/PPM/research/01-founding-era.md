# Founding Era & Political Origins (1907-1930s)

> **Domain:** Urban Public Market History
> **Module:** 1 -- Political Origins, Opening Day, and Early Physical Expansion
> **Through-line:** *Eight farmers brought their wagons to an empty bluff above Elliott Bay, and ten thousand people showed up before noon.* The Market did not emerge from urban planning or civic investment -- it was an act of political defiance against a price-gouging system, enabled by a dormant ordinance and a crowd that voted with its feet. Everything that followed -- the buildings, the culture, the preservation battles -- grew from that single August morning when direct commerce proved it could outcompete the middlemen.

## 1. The Produce Middlemen Crisis

Before Pike Place Market existed, Seattle-area farmers sold their produce at a three-block open-air area known as The Lots, located at Sixth Avenue and King Street. Most goods from The Lots were then channeled through commercial wholesale houses on Western Avenue, collectively known as Produce Row. The system was built entirely around intermediaries. Farmers sold on consignment, receiving a percentage of the final sale price set by the wholesaler. At worst, they broke even or earned nothing at all [1].

The economics were extractive by design. Wholesale commission merchants controlled the pricing pipeline between the farm and the consumer. A farmer growing onions in the White River Valley might spend the entire growing season tending a crop, transport it to Seattle by wagon, deliver it to a commission house, and receive a fraction of its retail value -- or, in some cases, nothing, if the commission house judged the lot unsaleable after fees.

The crisis reached public visibility when onion prices surged from ten cents per pound at the farm gate to one dollar per pound at the retail counter -- a 900% markup that traveled entirely through the middlemen's pockets. This was not an anomaly. It was the system working as designed. The markup on other staples -- potatoes, cabbages, eggs, berries -- followed similar patterns, though the onion price became the rallying symbol because the disparity was so stark and so easily communicated [2].

### The Scale of the Problem

By the early 1900s, Seattle's population was growing rapidly. The 1900 census counted approximately 80,671 residents; by 1910 it would reach 237,194 -- nearly tripling in a single decade. The Klondike Gold Rush (1897-1899) had positioned Seattle as the primary outfitting port for Alaska, and the economic boom that followed brought waves of immigration from across the Pacific, Scandinavia, Southern and Eastern Europe, and the American South. Every one of these new residents needed to eat, and every dollar they spent on food passed through Produce Row [3].

The commission merchants had no incentive to reform. Their margins depended on the information asymmetry between farm and market. Farmers did not know what consumers would pay. Consumers did not know what farmers received. The middlemen occupied the gap and extracted rent from both sides.

The system was not merely unfair -- it was structurally entrenched. Commission merchants controlled warehouse space, transportation access, and commercial relationships with retail grocers. A farmer who attempted to bypass Produce Row and sell directly to a retail grocer would find that the grocer already had an exclusive arrangement with a commission house. The wholesale system functioned as a cartel, not through formal agreement but through the accumulated infrastructure of intermediation. Breaking it required not just a new selling location but a new economic model -- and that model was the public market.

## 2. The Political Maneuver

City Councilman Thomas Plummer Revelle identified a dormant 1896 city ordinance that authorized the designation of public market land -- a legal mechanism that had been written into law but never activated. Revelle's insight was not legislative; it was archaeological. He did not need to write new law. He needed to enforce old law that the commission merchants had no reason to fear because nobody remembered it existed [4].

On August 5, 1907, Ordinance 16636 was passed by the Seattle City Council, designating a stretch of the newly planked Pike Place bluff -- on the western edge of downtown, overlooking the industrial waterfront and Elliott Bay -- as a public market where farmers could sell directly to consumers without intermediaries.

### The Ordinance's Language

Ordinance 16636 is notable for what it does and does not say. It designates the physical space for a public market. It does not create an institution to manage it, fund it, or protect it. It does not establish rules for vendor selection, stall assignment, or quality control. It simply creates the legal permission for farmers to sell directly to consumers in a designated public space. This minimalist approach -- creating opportunity rather than infrastructure -- would prove to be both the Market's greatest strength (allowing organic growth and adaptation) and its greatest vulnerability (leaving the Market without institutional protection when threats materialized).

### Why This Location

The Pike Place site was not prime commercial real estate in 1907. It was a steep bluff with a recently planked street surface, overlooking the working waterfront below. The land dropped off sharply to the west toward Western Avenue and the piers. This geography -- which would later become the Market's most distinctive architectural feature, with buildings cascading down the hillside in multiple levels -- was initially a liability. The site was chosen because it was available, because it was within walking distance of residential neighborhoods, and because Revelle could secure City Council approval for a location that nobody else particularly wanted [5].

The adjacency to the waterfront meant that fresh fish could travel from the boats on Elliott Bay to the market stalls in minutes. This unplanned proximity would become one of the Market's defining characteristics -- the fishmonger tradition that persists to this day.

## 3. Opening Day: August 17, 1907

The historical record on opening day is both vivid and contested in its details. Most sources agree on the essential facts: between six and twelve farmers brought their wagons to the designated stretch of Pike Place on the morning of Saturday, August 17, 1907. A crowd estimated at nearly ten thousand people descended on the site. Every farmer sold out before noon [6].

The disparity between the sources -- "six" farmers in some accounts, "twelve" in others, "eight" in the most commonly cited version -- reflects the informal nature of the Market's founding. There was no registration system, no formal count, and no official photograph of the first morning. What we know comes from newspaper accounts published in the days following and from retrospective institutional histories. The imprecision of the founding record is itself a historical datum: the Market was not launched by a corporation with a marketing department. It was started by a small group of farmers who showed up with wagons.

The crowd was not expected at that scale. The city had designated the space and published notice, but there was no marketing campaign, no organized publicity beyond newspaper coverage and word of mouth. The turnout was a spontaneous market correction -- ten thousand people choosing direct purchase over the Produce Row markup, casting an economic vote with their feet.

### What Was Sold

The opening day offerings were seasonal summer produce: vegetables, fruits, and eggs. The farmers were truck farmers -- small-scale producers from the Duwamish, White River, and Green River valleys south of Seattle, along with farms on Bainbridge Island and Vashon Island accessible by the Mosquito Fleet steamers. The prices were dramatically lower than Produce Row retail, which was the entire point [7].

### The Second Saturday

One week later, on August 24, 1907, seventy vendor stalls were operating -- a tenfold increase from opening day. The demand signal was unambiguous. By the end of 1907, the Market was operating daily, and Frank Goodwin had begun his first covered structure [8].

## 4. Frank Goodwin and Private Enterprise

Frank Goodwin is one of the most important and most complicated figures in the Market's founding story. As owner of the Leland Hotel adjacent to the new market site, Goodwin immediately recognized the commercial opportunity created by Revelle's ordinance. He covered the sidewalk near his hotel to give farmers shelter from the rain -- a practical necessity in Seattle -- and over the following decade, his Public Market and Department Store Company acquired most of the surrounding property [9].

Goodwin constructed or expanded the Corner Market, Economy Market, Sanitary Public Market, and Main Market buildings. He was not a public servant. He was a real estate developer who recognized that the presence of a public market dramatically increased the value of surrounding property, and who moved faster than any civic body to capitalize on that dynamic.

### The Public-Private Tension

This created a tension that would define the Market for the next century. The Market's *function* was public -- a civic space for direct commerce, mandated by city ordinance. But the Market's *physical infrastructure* was largely private -- built and owned by Goodwin and his successors for profit. Farmers operated under a public mandate but within private buildings. The daystall rents they paid went to Goodwin, not the city.

This dual nature -- public mission, private landlord -- would become the central issue of the 1960s preservation crisis. The buildings could be sold, redeveloped, or demolished by their private owners at any time. The public function had no structural guarantee beyond the goodwill of the property owners and the political will of the City Council.

## 5. The First Buildings

The physical Market grew rapidly between 1907 and 1922, transforming from an open-air line of wagons into a complex of interconnected buildings cascading down the hillside.

| Date | Building | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Nov 30, 1907 | First Covered Arcade | Frank Goodwin's initial structure; rain shelter for vendors |
| 1908 | Triangle Market | Adjacent to Main Arcade; Outlook Hotel constructed nearby |
| 1909 | Athenian Inn opens | Originally a bakery; becomes second-oldest continuously operating Market restaurant |
| 1911 | Sanitary Market | Named for its modern (for the era) sanitary standards; housed butchers, delicatessens, bakeries |
| 1912 | Corner Market | Major commercial building; Three Girls Bakery among the first tenants |
| 1921 | City Fish | City-operated fish stall created to counter high fish prices |
| 1922 | Market reaches 11 buildings | Nine-acre footprint along the waterfront established |

The naming convention of these early buildings tells a social story. The "Sanitary Market" was named to distinguish it from the open-air stalls -- it was enclosed, had plumbing, and met the hygiene standards of the era. The name was a selling point, not a given. Sanitation in urban food markets was a serious public health concern in early-20th-century America, and the Sanitary Market's construction was a statement that this was not merely a collection of wagons but a permanent institution [10].

### Construction Materials and Methods

The buildings of this era were constructed primarily in brick and timber-frame -- the standard construction methods of early-20th-century Seattle. The Great Fire of 1889 had prompted a citywide shift from wood-frame to masonry construction, and the Market's buildings reflected this mandate. The brick walls, concrete floors, and timber roof structures that visitors see today are largely original, repaired and reinforced during the 1975-1984 rehabilitation and the 2008 seismic upgrade but fundamentally unchanged in their material character.

The construction quality varies significantly between buildings because each was built by a different owner at a different time with a different budget. Goodwin's earliest structures were functional and expedient -- designed to generate rental income as quickly as possible. Later buildings, constructed as the Market proved its economic viability, were more substantial. This variation in construction quality created challenges for the rehabilitation program decades later, as each building required a customized structural assessment and repair approach.

### Vertical Architecture

The steep hillside that made the site initially undesirable became its most distinctive architectural feature. As buildings were added, they stepped down the bluff in terraces, creating the multi-level structure that visitors encounter today. The Main Arcade sits at the Pike Place street level. Below it, accessible by ramps and stairways, lie the DownUnder levels -- a labyrinth of shops, antique dealers, and small restaurants that occupies the space between the arcade and Western Avenue below.

This vertical layering was not planned as a coherent design. It emerged from the topography and the economics of incremental construction on a hillside. Each new building was built to meet the grade where it stood. The result is an organic, almost geological structure -- strata of commerce laid down decade by decade on a bluff above the water.

## 6. Early Vendor Diversity

The Market's vendor community was international from opening day -- a reflection of Seattle's position as a Pacific Rim port city with immigration streams from multiple continents. The diversity was not curated or intentional. It emerged from the simple mechanics of the daystall system: anyone who could grow or make something and pay the stall fee could sell at the Market. This structural openness attracted vendors from communities that were excluded from other commercial channels.

From its earliest days, the Market was a polyglot commercial space reflecting Seattle's immigration patterns. The Puget Sound region in the early 1900s was receiving immigrants from Japan, China, the Philippines, Scandinavia, Italy, Greece, and Eastern Europe, along with internal migrants from across the American West [11].

Japanese immigrants -- Issei, meaning first-generation emigrants from Japan -- were among the very first to sell produce at the Market on opening day. Their presence was not incidental; Japanese-American farming families had established themselves throughout the fertile valleys south of Seattle and recognized the Market as a direct route to consumers that bypassed the discriminatory commission system on Produce Row. (Module 2 covers the Japanese-American community in detail.)

Italian immigrants brought the delicatessen and specialty food traditions that DeLaurenti would later institutionalize. Greek immigrants established some of the earliest restaurant-counter operations. Filipino vendors arrived in increasing numbers through the 1920s and 1930s. The Market became, in effect, a commercial United Nations -- not by design, but because direct-sale commerce rewards anyone who can grow or make something and show up with it.

### Women Entrepreneurs

Three Girls Bakery, founded in 1912, is notable not only for its longevity (it continues to operate) but for its founding story: it is believed to be one of the first businesses in Seattle started by women. The "three girls" were three young women who opened a bakery counter in the newly completed Corner Market building. Their presence was not unusual for the Market -- the daystall system and the low barriers to entry made the Market more accessible to women entrepreneurs than conventional commercial real estate, which typically required capital and credit relationships that excluded women in the early 20th century [12].

## 7. The Sanitary Market and Food Safety

The opening of the Sanitary Market in 1911 represented a turning point in the Market's evolution from open-air wagons to permanent commercial institution. The name "Sanitary Market" was not ironic -- it was aspirational and promotional. The building was designed to meet the food safety standards of the Progressive Era, which was transforming American attitudes toward public health, food handling, and commercial hygiene [10].

### Progressive Era Food Reform

The passage of the Pure Food and Drug Act in 1906 -- just one year before the Market's founding -- reflected a national reckoning with food safety that directly influenced the Market's physical development. Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* (1906) had exposed the horrific conditions in Chicago's meatpacking industry, and public demand for sanitary food-handling spaces was intense.

The Sanitary Market responded to this demand by offering enclosed, plumbed commercial space for butchers, delicatessens, and bakeries -- food categories that required more controlled environments than the open arcade could provide. The building had concrete floors that could be hosed down, refrigeration for perishable goods, and pest-control measures that were advanced for the era. Its very name was a competitive differentiator: *come buy your meat here, because we're clean*.

### The Split Between Indoor and Outdoor Commerce

The Sanitary Market's construction created a functional split in the Market's commercial organization that persists today. Produce -- fruits, vegetables, and flowers -- remained in the open arcade, where the daystall system allowed seasonal rotation and where the visual display of fresh goods attracted foot traffic. Meat, dairy, and prepared foods moved into the enclosed buildings, where temperature control and sanitation requirements could be met.

This indoor/outdoor split is architecturally visible throughout the Market. The arcade is open to the air on one side, creating a semi-outdoor space that works well for produce but would be unacceptable for raw meat. The permanent shops are fully enclosed, with walls, doors, and HVAC systems. The two types of space serve different commercial functions and different food safety requirements, and their coexistence within the same nine-acre district is part of what makes the Market's commercial ecosystem uniquely complex.

## 8. The Mosquito Fleet and Regional Draw

The Mosquito Fleet -- the network of small private steamers that served Puget Sound islands and communities before the establishment of Washington State Ferries -- brought shoppers to the Market from across the region. Colman Dock, the primary ferry landing, was within walking distance of Pike Place. Residents of Bainbridge Island, Vashon Island, and communities along the Kitsap Peninsula could board a steamer in the morning, shop at the Market, and return home the same day [13].

This regional connectivity meant that the Market was never purely a neighborhood institution. It drew from a catchment area spanning the entire central Puget Sound -- a geography that would have required hours of travel by road but was efficiently served by water transit. The Mosquito Fleet created a market radius that automobiles would not replicate for decades.

## 9. Growth Timeline: 1907-1922

The Market's physical expansion followed a clear pattern: demand pulled supply. Each new building was a response to vendor demand that exceeded available stall space, which in turn was driven by consumer traffic that exceeded the capacity of existing structures.

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1907 | Market opens; first covered arcade | Public market principle established |
| 1908 | Triangle Market, Outlook Hotel | First expansion beyond original arcade |
| 1909 | Athenian Inn opens | Restaurant/hospitality function added |
| 1910 | Census: Seattle population 237,194 | Tripled from 1900; demand base exploding |
| 1911 | Sanitary Market opens | Enclosed, plumbed retail space; butchers and bakeries |
| 1912 | Corner Market opens; Three Girls Bakery | Major commercial addition; women entrepreneurs |
| 1914-1918 | WWI era | Japanese-American farmers reach ~70% of stalls |
| 1921 | City Fish stall created | Municipal intervention against fish price gouging |
| 1922 | 11 buildings, 9 acres | Market reaches its approximate modern footprint |

By 1922, the Market had achieved the physical scale that would define it for the next half-century. The nine-acre footprint, the multi-level hillside architecture, the mix of permanent shops and rotating daystalls -- all the essential elements were in place. What would change was the composition of the vendor community, the political context, and the existential threats that would nearly destroy it.

## 10. The Daystall System

The daystall system is the Market's most distinctive operational mechanism and one of its most important. A daystall is a temporary selling space -- typically a table or counter section within the arcade -- that is assigned to a vendor on a daily or seasonal basis rather than through a permanent lease. The system allows farmers and craftspeople to sell at the Market without the capital commitment of a permanent shop lease [14].

### How Daystalls Work

Farmers and craft vendors apply for daystall permits from the Market governing body (originally the city, later the PDA). Permitted vendors are assigned stall positions through a rotation system that considers seniority, product category, and -- historically -- more troubling criteria including race.

The daystall system creates a constantly shifting retail landscape. A stall that holds strawberries in June may hold pumpkins in October and handmade pottery in January. This rotation is what gives the Market its distinctive seasonal character and its sense of discovery -- the same physical walk through the arcade yields different offerings on different days and in different seasons.

### Seniority and the Waiting List

The daystall system includes a seniority component that rewards long-term participation. Vendors who sell at the Market consistently over multiple seasons accumulate seniority credits that give them preferential access to premium stall positions. New vendors start at the back of the queue and work their way forward over time. This seniority system creates a tension between rewarding loyalty (which stabilizes the vendor community) and maintaining access for newcomers (which keeps the Market dynamic). The balance is imperfect but deliberate -- the system is designed to provide stability for established vendors while maintaining a path of entry for new ones.

### Economic Accessibility

The daystall system's most important feature is its low barrier to entry. A farmer with a truck and a permit can sell at Pike Place Market without signing a commercial lease, hiring employees, or investing in a storefront. This accessibility is what made the Market viable for immigrant communities, women entrepreneurs, and small-scale producers who would have been excluded from conventional retail channels.

The system also creates natural quality pressure. Because daystall assignments can be revoked and because consumers can see and compare offerings side by side, vendors who offer poor quality or overcharge are quickly disciplined by the market -- not by regulation, but by the crowd walking past their stall to the next one.

## 11. Political Tensions: Public Function vs. Private Profit

The founding tension between the Market's public function and its private real estate ownership shaped its political history from the beginning. Revelle's ordinance created the *right* to a public market but did not create the *institution* to sustain it. Goodwin's buildings created the *physical space* for that market but were private commercial investments subject to the owner's financial interests.

Throughout the 1910s and 1920s, this tension manifested in disputes over stall rents, building maintenance, and vendor access. The city could designate public market space but could not compel a private landlord to maintain it, improve it, or keep rents affordable. Goodwin's interests were aligned with the Market's success only insofar as success increased his property values and rental income.

This structural flaw -- public purpose dependent on private goodwill -- would remain latent for decades, unnoticed as long as the Market thrived economically. It would become the central crisis when the Market's economic fortunes declined and the private owners saw greater value in demolition and redevelopment than in continued operation.

## 12. The Public Market Center Sign

The iconic Public Market Center neon sign and clock, which today serves as one of the most photographed landmarks in Seattle, was installed in 1927. The sign, with its red neon lettering and clock face, marked the Market's main entrance at Pike Place and became the visual symbol that distinguished the Market from the surrounding commercial district. The sign has been maintained, repaired, and preserved through every subsequent era -- including the preservation crisis -- and now stands as both a functioning wayfinding element and a heritage artifact [15].

The sign's installation in 1927 represents a moment of institutional self-awareness: the Market was no longer just a collection of vendor stalls but a recognized urban institution with a brand identity. The decision to invest in a permanent, illuminated sign indicated that the Market's operators understood its commercial value extended beyond the individual transactions taking place inside.

### Architectural Signage as Urban Identity

The Public Market Center sign belongs to a category of commercial signage that has become inseparable from the urban identity of its host city. Like the Hollywood sign in Los Angeles or the Welcome to Fabulous Las Vegas sign in Nevada, the Pike Place Market sign transcends its original commercial function and becomes a civic symbol. The difference is that the Pike Place sign still marks a functioning institution -- it is not a relic of a bygone era but a sign that points to something real and operating.

The sign's preservation during the 1960s-1970s battle was never questioned -- it was so clearly part of the Market's identity that even the demolition proponents did not suggest removing it. Its continued operation today, with its original neon tubes periodically replaced and its clock mechanism maintained, represents one of the longest-running acts of civic sign preservation in the Pacific Northwest.

## 13. The Economics of Direct Sale

The Market's founding proposition -- eliminating the middleman markup between farm and consumer -- had implications that extended beyond simple price reduction. Direct-sale markets create a different economic relationship than intermediated retail, and understanding this difference is essential to understanding why the Market has persisted for 117 years while the wholesale commission system it opposed has largely disappeared [16].

### Price Transparency

In a direct-sale market, the price is set by the vendor in full view of competing vendors and informed consumers. There is no hidden markup, no commission structure, and no information asymmetry between buyer and seller about the cost of goods. A consumer who thinks the strawberries at one stall are overpriced can walk fifteen feet to the next stall and compare. This real-time competitive pressure keeps prices honest without any regulatory intervention.

### Quality Accountability

When a farmer sells directly to a consumer, there is no intermediary to blame for quality problems. If the lettuce wilts or the peaches are mealy, the consumer knows exactly who sold it and can avoid that vendor next time -- or simply tell them. This direct accountability creates quality pressure that operates at the speed of conversation, not the speed of corporate complaint resolution.

### Seasonal Honesty

Direct-sale markets cannot fake seasonality. A farmer who grows strawberries can sell strawberries when they ripen and not before. There are no out-of-season imports, no cold-storage aging, and no supply-chain gymnastics to create the illusion of year-round availability. The Market's offerings change with the seasons because that is what the land produces. This forced seasonality, which might seem like a limitation, is actually a strength -- it creates the constantly changing retail experience that keeps customers returning.

> **Related:** The economics of direct-sale agriculture connect to food system analysis in the [AGR project](../AGR/index.html) and the garden-to-table patterns in [GDN](../GDN/index.html). The transparency principles parallel the open data themes in [AWF](../AWF/index.html).

### Social Capital

Perhaps most importantly, direct-sale markets create social capital that intermediated retail cannot. A consumer who buys from the same farmer every Saturday for ten years has a relationship -- they know the farmer's name, the farm's location, the family's story. This social bond creates customer loyalty that is more durable than brand loyalty and more resistant to price competition. The Market's most successful vendors are not necessarily the cheapest; they are the ones with the deepest customer relationships.

## 14. The Market's First Decline

The Market's golden age -- roughly 1910 to 1940 -- was not a period of uninterrupted growth. The Great Depression hit Seattle hard, reducing consumer purchasing power and stressing the already thin margins of small-scale farmers. Some vendors went under. Others survived by cutting prices to the point where they were barely subsistence farming [15].

But the Depression did not destroy the Market because the Market's fundamental proposition -- cheap food sold directly -- became more valuable during economic hardship, not less. Consumers who could no longer afford Produce Row prices came to Pike Place. The Depression, paradoxically, reinforced the Market's relevance.

The true first decline came not from economics but from demographics. The Japanese-American community, which by 1941 constituted approximately 75-80% of all Market vendor permits, was forcibly removed from the West Coast beginning in early 1942. The number of farmer-seller licenses dropped from 515 in 1939 to 196 in 1943 -- a collapse of more than 60% in four years. The Market did not recover its pre-war vendor density for decades, and in a meaningful sense it never recovered the specific community that had been its economic backbone.

The story of that community and its destruction is the subject of Module 2.

## 15. The Market in Context: 1907 Seattle

To understand the Market's founding, it helps to understand the city it was born into. Seattle in 1907 was barely two decades past the Great Fire of 1889, which had destroyed 25 blocks of the central business district and prompted a complete rebuilding in brick and stone. The city had been an outfitting point for the Klondike Gold Rush (1897-1899), which brought explosive population growth, new wealth, and a frontier mentality that prized practical solutions over institutional formality [17].

### Infrastructure

Seattle's infrastructure in 1907 was a work in progress. The Denny Regrade -- the massive earth-moving project that would literally flatten hills to make building sites -- was underway. The city's electric trolley system was expanding rapidly. Running water and sewage systems were recent additions. The Pike Place bluff was accessible by the newly planked street surface, but the industrial waterfront below was a working harbor, not a recreational amenity.

### Population and Demographics

The 1910 census would count 237,194 residents -- nearly triple the 1900 count. This growth rate meant that Seattle was adding population faster than it could build housing, infrastructure, or commercial facilities. The Market's success on opening day was partly a function of this compressed demand: a rapidly growing population with limited commercial options found, in the Market, a direct and efficient way to feed itself.

### The Temperance Context

Washington state would adopt prohibition in 1916 -- four years before national Prohibition. The temperance movement was a significant social force in early-1900s Seattle, and it influenced the Market's character. Some of the Market's early establishments -- like the Virginia Inn, which operated as a cardroom during Prohibition -- adapted their commercial identities to navigate the changing regulatory environment. The Market's mixed-use character, combining food, hospitality, and retail, gave its businesses the flexibility to survive regulatory disruptions that would have destroyed more specialized enterprises.
