# Preservation Movement & Governance

> **Domain:** Urban Preservation History & Civic Governance
> **Module:** 3 -- The Demolition Threat, Friends of the Market, Initiative 1, and the PDA Charter
> **Through-line:** *In 1963, Seattle's civic leadership proposed replacing Pike Place Market with a parking garage, office towers, and a hotel. Eight years later, the citizens voted 73,369 to 53,264 to save it.* The preservation of Pike Place Market was not a sentimental act. It was a political battle fought by a coalition of architects, artists, activists, and ordinary Seattleites against the full weight of the city's development establishment. It was one of the first grassroots urban preservation victories in American history, and its model -- citizen initiative over institutional authority -- became a template for preservation movements nationwide.

## 1. The Demolition Threat

By the early 1960s, Pike Place Market was in physical decline. The post-internment loss of Japanese-American vendors had reduced the Market's commercial density. Buildings that had been constructed between 1907 and 1922 -- most under Frank Goodwin's private ownership -- were aging, and deferred maintenance had accumulated for decades. The Market looked shabby, outdated, and increasingly out of place in a downtown Seattle that was modernizing rapidly [1].

The decline was not solely physical. Seattle's self-image was changing. The 1962 World's Fair (Century 21 Exposition) had positioned Seattle as a forward-looking, technologically progressive city. The Space Needle, built for the fair, symbolized a city that valued the future over the past. In this context, Pike Place Market -- with its century-old buildings, its mud-splattered farmers, and its maze of narrow stairways -- looked like an embarrassment rather than an asset.

The Market's remaining vendor community was older, smaller, and less politically connected than it had been before the war. The institutional voice that might have defended the Market -- the Japanese-American farming community that had constituted its majority -- had been destroyed twenty years earlier. The void they left was not just commercial but political.

## 2. The Pike Plaza Proposal

In 1963, consultant Donald Monson prepared a modernization plan for Seattle's downtown that recommended the demolition of Pike Place Market and its replacement with Pike Plaza -- a large-scale urban renewal project featuring office towers, apartments, a hotel, and a 3,000-car parking garage. The plan was enthusiastically supported by the Mayor, the major media outlets, and most civic leaders [2].

The Pike Plaza proposal was not unusual for its era. American cities in the 1960s were demolishing historic neighborhoods at an extraordinary rate under the banner of urban renewal. Federal funding through the Housing and Urban Development Act incentivized cities to clear "blighted" areas and replace them with modern development. Across the country, neighborhoods that we would now recognize as irreplaceable -- the West End in Boston, Penn Station in New York, the Pruitt-Igoe complex in St. Louis -- were being destroyed with federal money and local enthusiasm.

### What Would Have Been Lost

The Pike Plaza proposal would have demolished approximately nine acres of historic structures, including:

- The Main Arcade and North Arcade (primary daystall rows)
- The Corner Market, Sanitary Market, and Economy Market buildings
- Post Alley and its associated commercial spaces
- The DownUnder levels beneath the main arcade
- Multiple residential buildings housing low-income tenants
- The entire daystall system and the vendor community it supported

The replacement -- office towers, a hotel, and a 3,000-car parking garage -- would have been indistinguishable from any other mid-century urban renewal project. The specific thing that made Pike Place Market irreplaceable -- its organic, multi-level, multi-generational, human-scaled commercial ecology -- was precisely what the developers considered blight.

## 3. Victor Steinbrueck

Victor Steinbrueck was a University of Washington architecture professor who had co-designed the Space Needle for the 1962 World's Fair. He was not a preservationist by training or by temperament -- he was a practicing architect who understood how buildings work and who recognized that the Market's apparent disorder was actually a sophisticated urban ecology that could not be replicated by design [3].

Steinbrueck's opposition to the Pike Plaza proposal was grounded in architectural analysis, not nostalgia. He argued that the Market's value lay not in any individual building but in the system of relationships between buildings, vendors, customers, and the hillside topography -- a system that had evolved over fifty years and could not be reproduced by demolishing it and starting over.

### The Sketches

Steinbrueck was also a gifted draftsman. His pen-and-ink sketches of the Market -- capturing its visual character with an architect's precision and an artist's eye -- became powerful advocacy tools. Two collections, *Seattle Cityscape* (1962) and *Market Sketchbook* (1968), documented the Market's visual identity in a format that was accessible to the general public. The sketches made the abstract argument for preservation concrete and emotional: *this is what you would lose* [4].

### The Meeting at Lowell's

In 1964, Steinbrueck convened a meeting at Lowell's Cafe, a restaurant within the Market, to discuss the demolition threat. This meeting became the founding of Friends of the Market (FOM), the organization that would lead the seven-year preservation campaign. The choice of venue was deliberate -- the advocates met in the place they were trying to save [5].

## 4. Friends of the Market

Friends of the Market was formed as a spin-off of Allied Arts of Seattle, an existing civic advocacy organization. Its founding members included Steinbrueck, artist Mark Tobey (an internationally recognized painter who had long used the Market as subject matter), and a coalition of architects, artists, writers, small business owners, and neighborhood residents [6].

FOM's organizational model was grassroots rather than institutional. It relied on volunteer labor, donated expertise, public meetings, petition drives, and media engagement rather than corporate fundraising or political party infrastructure. This was both a strategic choice and a necessity -- the city's political and business establishment was overwhelmingly in favor of demolition, so FOM could not work through existing institutional channels.

### The Opposition

The forces aligned against preservation were formidable:

- **The Mayor's office** -- actively promoting the Pike Plaza development
- **The Seattle Times and Seattle Post-Intelligencer** -- both editorially supporting demolition
- **The downtown business community** -- viewing the Market as a barrier to modernization
- **Federal urban renewal funding** -- creating financial incentives for demolition
- **The Central Association of Seattle** -- the downtown business lobby

Against this, FOM had: an architecture professor, an artist, a group of volunteers, and an argument about the value of old, inconvenient, human-scaled places in a city that was trying to be modern.

## 5. The Seven-Year Campaign

The preservation campaign lasted from 1964 to 1971 -- seven years of sustained advocacy against well-funded opposition. The campaign employed multiple strategies:

### Public Education

FOM organized public meetings, published newsletters, and created visual materials (including Steinbrueck's sketches) that communicated the Market's value to citizens who might never have considered it threatened. The campaign reframed the Market from "outdated commercial district" to "irreplaceable civic asset" -- a shift in perception that required changing how Seattleites thought about their own city.

### Legal and Regulatory Advocacy

FOM worked with sympathetic city council members and planning staff to challenge the Pike Plaza proposal through the regulatory process. They argued that the environmental impact assessment was inadequate, that the displacement of low-income residents and small businesses had not been properly addressed, and that the urban renewal funding mechanisms were being misapplied.

### Coalition Building

The preservation campaign drew support from an unusual coalition: architecture professionals who understood the Market's design value, artists who found inspiration in its visual character, small business owners who would lose their livelihoods, low-income residents who would lose their housing, neighborhood activists concerned about the character of downtown Seattle, and ordinary citizens who simply valued the Market as a place they used and enjoyed [7].

### Mark Tobey's Contribution

Mark Tobey, whose paintings of the Market had brought international attention to its visual character, lent his considerable reputation to the preservation cause. Tobey's involvement elevated the campaign from a local planning dispute to a matter of cultural significance -- if an internationally recognized artist considered the Market worth painting, perhaps it was also worth saving.

## 6. The City Council Vote of 1969

On August 4, 1969, despite years of public advocacy and growing opposition to the demolition plan, the Seattle City Council voted to proceed with the Pike Plaza urban renewal project. The vote was a devastating setback for Friends of the Market, which had spent five years building public support for preservation [8].

The Council's decision reflected the political reality of the time: the development interests were better funded, better organized, and better connected to the political establishment than the preservationists. The institutional power structure of Seattle -- the Mayor, the newspapers, the downtown business community -- had lined up behind demolition, and the City Council followed.

But the Council vote was not the end. It was the catalyst for FOM's most ambitious strategy: bypassing the City Council entirely through a citizen initiative.

## 7. Initiative 1: The 1971 Ballot

Friends of the Market sponsored Initiative 1 on the November 2, 1971 Seattle ballot. The initiative would create a seven-acre Pike Place Market Historical District and establish a Historical Commission with authority to approve any alteration or demolition within the district. It would, in effect, give the citizens of Seattle direct authority over the Market's future, overriding the City Council's 1969 vote [9].

### The Petition Drive

Washington state law required a substantial number of signatures to place a citizen initiative on the ballot. FOM collected 25,000 petition signatures in three weeks -- a remarkable grassroots mobilization that demonstrated the depth of public support for preservation.

### The City's Counter-Proposal

The City Council, recognizing that Initiative 1 had substantial public support, placed its own counter-proposal on the same ballot. The city's proposal would have created a smaller preservation district with weaker protections and more flexibility for development -- essentially, a compromise that would have preserved some of the Market while allowing significant demolition and redevelopment.

### The Vote

| Measure | Votes | Outcome |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Initiative 1 (full preservation) | 73,369 | **Passed** |
| City counter-proposal | 53,264 | Failed |
| Margin | 20,105 votes | 3-to-2 in favor of full preservation |

The citizens of Seattle chose the strongest preservation option by a decisive margin. The vote was not close. It was a clear mandate for full preservation over partial compromise [10].

### Significance

The Initiative 1 victory was significant beyond Seattle for several reasons:

- It demonstrated that citizen initiative could override institutional resistance to preservation
- It prioritized a grassroots coalition over established business and political interests
- It preserved not just buildings but a functioning commercial ecosystem and social community
- It set a precedent for preservation movements in other American cities

The New York Times published an editorial in May 1974 acknowledging the "epic ten-year battle" and its significance in the national urban preservation landscape [11].

## 8. The Historical District

Following the passage of Initiative 1, the Pike Place Market Historical District was formally established. The district boundaries encompassed approximately seven acres (later expanded to nine with the 2017 MarketFront addition) of the Market's core properties.

The Historical Commission, created by the initiative, was established as a 12-member citizen volunteer body appointed by the Mayor. Its authority includes review and approval of all design changes, use changes, demolition requests, and new construction within the district. No significant alteration to any Market building can proceed without Historical Commission review [12].

### What the District Protects

The Historical District designation protects several categories of Market character:

- **Physical structures** -- buildings, arcades, walkways, stairs, and the hillside topography
- **Use patterns** -- the daystall system, farmer stalls, craft vendor spaces, and the mix of commercial and residential uses
- **Social character** -- the 1971 initiative explicitly included social character as a protected value, not just physical buildings
- **View corridors** -- visual connections between the Market and Elliott Bay

The inclusion of "social character" was unusual for a preservation ordinance in 1971 and reflected the preservation movement's understanding that the Market was not merely a collection of buildings but a functioning community. Saving the buildings without saving the community within them would have been a hollow victory.

## 9. The PDA Charter

In 1973, two years after the Initiative 1 victory, the City of Seattle created the Pike Place Market Preservation and Development Authority (PDA) as a not-for-profit public corporation to own and manage Market buildings. The PDA was a novel institutional form -- not a city department, not a private company, but a public corporation with a specific charter mandate [13].

### Charter Requirements

The PDA charter requires the authority to:

1. **Preserve, rehabilitate, and protect** the physical structures of the Market
2. **Increase farm and food retailing opportunities** -- maintaining the Market's original function as a direct-sale agricultural market
3. **Incubate and support small and marginal businesses** -- ensuring the Market remains accessible to vendors who could not afford conventional commercial rents
4. **Provide services for low-income people** -- including affordable housing, healthcare, and social services

These four mandates reflect the lessons of the Market's history. The preservation mandate addresses the physical neglect that invited demolition. The farm-and-food mandate addresses the founding purpose. The small-business mandate addresses the economic accessibility that made the Market viable for immigrant communities. And the low-income services mandate addresses the social community that the 1971 initiative explicitly sought to protect.

### Governance Innovation

The PDA model was innovative in several respects. It created a purpose-bound institution -- the PDA exists to serve the Market's charter mandates, not to maximize revenue or serve political interests. It separated Market governance from city politics -- the PDA operates independently from the City Council, which had voted in 1969 to demolish the Market. And it created a permanent institutional home for the Market's public function -- solving the founding-era problem of public purpose dependent on private property owners.

## 10. The Role of Artists and Writers

The Pike Place Market preservation campaign was unusual in the prominence of artists and writers among its leaders. Victor Steinbrueck was an architect, but his advocacy took the form of artistic documentation -- sketches, drawings, and publications that communicated the Market's value in visual terms. Mark Tobey was a painter whose international reputation lent cultural authority to the preservation cause. This artistic leadership shaped the campaign's character and its legacy [16].

### The Art of Documentation

Steinbrueck's sketches served a dual purpose. As architectural documentation, they recorded the Market's physical character with professional precision -- proportions, materials, spatial relationships, sight lines. As advocacy tools, they communicated the emotional and aesthetic experience of the Market in a way that technical arguments could not. A planning document arguing that the Market had "urban design merit" might convince a professional. A sketch showing a flower seller's stall with afternoon light on the arcade walls could convince anyone.

### The Cultural Argument

The preservation campaign's artistic dimension elevated the debate beyond a planning dispute. It was not merely a question of whether to demolish some old buildings or not. It was a question of whether Seattle valued the kind of creative, improvisational, human-scaled environment that artists and writers found inspiring -- or whether it preferred the predictable, efficient, controlled environment of modern development.

This cultural framing was strategically important. The Market's opponents could argue (and did) that the Market was economically inefficient, physically deteriorating, and standing in the way of progress. They could not easily argue that the Market was culturally worthless -- not when internationally recognized artists were publicly declaring it the most visually rich environment in the city.

### Literary Resonance

The Market has appeared in numerous literary works about Seattle, from novels to memoirs to poetry. Its dense, layered, sensory environment -- smells of fish and flowers, sounds of vendors calling, visual complexity of stacked levels and narrow passages -- provides the kind of rich material that writers seek. This literary presence reinforced the cultural argument for preservation: places that inspire art are places worth keeping.

## 11. Rehabilitation: 1975-1984

Following the PDA's establishment, a major rehabilitation program was undertaken between 1975 and 1984. The program addressed decades of deferred maintenance across 24 historic properties, using a combination of Urban Renewal funding, HUD grants, and the PDA's own revenues [14].

### The Rehabilitation Challenge

Rehabilitating the Market's buildings was architecturally and logistically complex. The buildings had been constructed incrementally over decades by different owners, using different construction methods, on a steep hillside. They were interconnected in ways that made it impossible to close one building without affecting adjacent structures. And the rehabilitation had to be conducted while the Market continued to operate -- vendors could not simply relocate for years of construction.

### Key Milestones

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1975 | Rehabilitation begins | First systematic repair of Market buildings since original construction |
| 1982 | Market Foundation established | Nonprofit arm for social service funding |
| 1982 | Victor Steinbrueck Park opens | Originally "Market Park"; renamed for the preservation leader |
| 1984 | Rehabilitation substantially complete | 24 historic properties restored |
| 2008 | Seattle voters approve $71-73 million levy | Seismic upgrades and infrastructure modernization |
| 2017 | MarketFront expansion opens | First major Historic District expansion in 30+ years |
| 2024 | Overlook Walk completed | Pedestrian connection to Seattle waterfront |

## 12. Post-Preservation Governance

The Market's governance structure as it operates today is a multi-layered system of organizations, each with distinct responsibilities:

| Entity | Role | Established |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Pike Place Market PDA | Not-for-profit public corporation; owns and manages day-to-day operations | 1973 |
| Historical Commission | 12-member citizen body; reviews all design and use changes in the 9-acre district | 1971 |
| Pike Place Market Foundation | Nonprofit; raises funds for social service programs; operates The Market Commons | 1982 |
| Pike Place Merchants Association | Incorporated member organization; legal, accounting, and health insurance services | 1973 |
| Daystall Tenants Association (DTA) | Represents daystall vendor interests; meets on Desimone Bridge | Late 1980s |
| United Farmers Coalition (UFC) | Represents daystall food vendors (produce, flower, processed food) | 1998 |
| Pike Market Performers' Guild | Represents 60+ permitted buskers | 2001 |

This multi-organization structure reflects the Market's complexity. No single entity could effectively manage all the Market's functions -- real estate, preservation review, social services, vendor relations, and performance arts -- without either becoming too large and bureaucratic or losing focus on its core mission. The distributed governance model allows specialization while maintaining coordination through the PDA's central role.

### The 1983 Hildt Agreement

The balance between farmer vendors and craft vendors in the daystall system -- a perennial source of tension -- was formalized in the 1983 Hildt Agreement, later revised as the 1999 Licata-Hildt Agreement. These agreements established allocation ratios and procedures for daystall assignments that balanced the Market's agricultural heritage with its evolving craft and artisan community [15].

## 13. The 2008 Levy and Ongoing Investment

The preservation framework established in 1971-1973 created the institutional structure, but maintaining the Market requires continuous investment. The most significant post-rehabilitation investment came in 2008, when Seattle voters approved a levy of $71-73 million for seismic upgrades and infrastructure modernization [16].

### Seismic Vulnerability

The Market's buildings, most constructed between 1907 and 1922, were built before modern seismic codes existed. Seattle sits within a seismically active region -- the Cascadia Subduction Zone is capable of producing magnitude 9+ earthquakes, and the Seattle Fault runs directly through the metropolitan area. A major earthquake could have devastated the Market's unreinforced masonry buildings, potentially causing structural collapse and loss of life.

The 2008 levy addressed this vulnerability through systematic structural reinforcement of Market buildings. The work was complex -- each building is different, each shares walls with adjacent structures, and the work had to be conducted while the Market continued to operate.

### Democratic Continuity

The 2008 levy vote maintained the pattern established in 1971: direct citizen approval of public investment in the Market's survival. Seattle voters were asked, for the second time in the Market's history, to spend public money to preserve a place they valued. And for the second time, they said yes. This pattern of democratic investment -- citizens repeatedly choosing to fund the Market's maintenance and improvement -- is itself a form of preservation. The Market survives not because it was saved once, but because the city keeps choosing to save it.

## 14. The Preservation Philosophy

The Pike Place Market preservation campaign developed an approach to historic preservation that differed significantly from the mainstream practice of the 1960s and early 1970s, and its innovations have influenced preservation philosophy nationally [17].

### Beyond Facades

Most historic preservation in the 1960s focused on building facades -- the visible exterior surfaces that gave a streetscape its historical character. The Pike Place initiative went further, protecting not just the buildings' appearance but their function, their social character, and their economic accessibility. A facade-preservation approach would have allowed the Market's buildings to be gutted and converted to high-end retail or office space while maintaining their external appearance. The 1971 initiative explicitly prevented this by protecting use patterns and social character.

### The Living Building Standard

The preservation framework effectively created what modern preservation theory would call a "living building" standard -- the Market was to be preserved not as a museum or a heritage display but as a functioning institution. Buildings would be maintained and rehabilitated for continued use, not frozen in a particular historical state. The Market was allowed -- indeed, expected -- to evolve, add new vendors, renovate spaces, and adapt to changing conditions. What it could not do was change its fundamental character: direct commerce, small-scale vendors, low-income accessibility, and mixed-use occupancy.

### The Community Preservation Precedent

By including "social character" in the preservation mandate, the 1971 initiative established a precedent for community preservation that was ahead of its time. The contemporary gentrification debate -- in which historic buildings are preserved but their communities are displaced by rising property values -- was anticipated and partially addressed by the Pike Place preservation framework. The PDA's mandate to maintain affordable housing and small-business accessibility was a structural attempt to prevent exactly the kind of preservation-without-community that would become common in other cities in subsequent decades.

## 15. National Significance

The Pike Place Market preservation campaign is recognized in the historic preservation literature as one of the most significant grassroots preservation victories in American history. Its significance derives from several factors:

**Chronological precedent.** The campaign began in 1964 and succeeded in 1971, contemporaneous with the early years of the national historic preservation movement. The National Historic Preservation Act was passed in 1966; the Pike Place campaign predates many of the institutional mechanisms that later preservation movements could draw upon.

**Citizen initiative model.** The use of a direct citizen ballot initiative to override City Council approval of demolition established a model that preservation advocates in other cities could study and adapt.

**Broad preservation scope.** The 1971 initiative protected not just physical buildings but social character, use patterns, and community services -- a breadth of preservation intent that was unusual for its time and influenced subsequent preservation philosophy.

**Successful institutional design.** The PDA model demonstrated that a purpose-bound public corporation could effectively manage a complex historic district without either becoming a rigid bureaucracy or succumbing to development pressure.
