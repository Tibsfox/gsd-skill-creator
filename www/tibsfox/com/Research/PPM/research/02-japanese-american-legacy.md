## 1. From Opening Day to Economic Backbone

Japanese immigrants -- Issei, meaning first-generation emigrants from Japan -- were among the very first to sell produce at Pike Place Market when it opened on August 17, 1907. This was not coincidence. Japanese-American farming families had established themselves throughout the Puget Sound region's fertile valleys precisely because agriculture was one of the few economic paths open to them. Restrictive covenants, professional licensing barriers, and outright discrimination in urban employment pushed many Issei toward farming -- and farming, in turn, brought them to the Market [1].

Over the following three decades, Japanese-American farmers became the Market's economic foundation. They cultivated rich farmland throughout the White River Valley, the Green River Valley, and on the east side of Lake Washington in what is now Bellevue. Their produce -- vegetables, fruits, berries, and flowers -- was transported to the Market by truck and wagon, following the same direct-sale model that Revelle's ordinance had established.

The numbers tell a clear story of economic dominance through agricultural skill and relentless labor:

| Period | Japanese-American Share of Market Stalls | Context |
|--------|------------------------------------------|---------|
| 1907 | Present from opening day | Among the first vendors |
| World War I era | ~70% of Market stalls | Largest single vendor group |
| 1930s peak | 75%+ of vendor permits | Over 600 total permits issued |
| 1939 | Majority of 515 farmer-seller licenses | Pre-war peak year |
| December 7, 1941 | ~75-80% of all Market stalls | On the day of the Pearl Harbor attack |

This arc -- from opening day presence to overwhelming economic dominance in thirty years -- represents one of the most significant entrepreneurial achievements of any immigrant community in Pacific Northwest history. It was accomplished despite systematic discrimination in stall assignments, housing, land ownership (the 1921 Alien Land Law prohibited "aliens ineligible for citizenship" from owning agricultural land in Washington), and social acceptance [2].

## 2. The Farming Communities

Japanese-American farmers in the Puget Sound region were not a homogeneous group. They came from different prefectures in Japan, settled in different valleys, and specialized in different crops. What united them was the Market as their primary commercial venue.

### The White River Valley

The White River Valley (now the cities of Auburn, Kent, and Renton) was one of the most productive agricultural areas in western Washington. Japanese-American families farmed lettuce, celery, peas, beans, strawberries, and other vegetables on the valley's rich alluvial soils. The valley's proximity to Seattle -- roughly 20 miles south -- made daily produce transport to the Market practical [3].

### The Green River Valley

Adjacent to and partially overlapping with the White River Valley, the Green River Valley supported similar truck farming operations. Japanese-American farming families here often specialized in root vegetables, cabbages, and the hearty produce that thrived in the Pacific Northwest's cool, wet growing season.

### Bellevue and the Eastside

Before Bellevue became a suburb, it was farmland. Japanese-American families established strawberry farms on the east side of Lake Washington, transporting their harvest across the lake by ferry and then to the Market. The Bellevue farming community was particularly close-knit, with mutual aid societies and cooperative purchasing arrangements for seed and equipment [4].

### Bainbridge Island

Japanese-American farmers on Bainbridge Island, connected to Seattle by the Mosquito Fleet ferry service, grew strawberries and other berries for the Market. The Bainbridge Island Japanese-American community is significant in the internment history because they were among the first on the West Coast to be forcibly removed -- shipped out on March 30, 1942, just 39 days after Executive Order 9066 was signed [5].

## 3. Discriminatory Stall Assignments

Despite their numerical dominance and economic centrality, Japanese-American farmers faced systematic discrimination in the Market's daily stall lottery system. Stalls were designated by race, with preferential positions -- the most visible, most accessible, highest-traffic spots -- assigned to white farmers even when Japanese-American farmers outnumbered them two to one or more [6].

This was not informal bias. It was administered policy. The stall assignment system explicitly categorized vendors by race and allocated the premium locations to white vendors first. Japanese-American farmers were relegated to odd-numbered stalls in the back of the Market -- less visible, less accessible, and lower in foot traffic.

### Petitions and Refusals

The Japanese farming community did not accept this discrimination silently. They petitioned the Seattle City Council at least four times during the 1910s to change the discriminatory stall assignment practices. Each time they were ignored. The City Council's refusal to act was not passive neglect -- it was an active choice to maintain a racial hierarchy within a public institution [7].

By the 1920s, the pattern was entrenched. Japanese-American farmers produced the majority of the Market's goods, drew the majority of its customers, and paid the majority of its stall fees -- while receiving the worst stall positions and the least institutional support. The system extracted their labor and excluded them from its benefits.

### Documentation of Discrimination

The evidence for discriminatory stall assignment practices comes from multiple sources: Seattle Municipal Archives records documenting the stall allocation system, petitions filed by Japanese-American farmers with the City Council (preserved in the city's archival record series), and oral histories collected by the Densho project from surviving community members and their descendants. The documentation is clear and specific: this was not informal bias or social exclusion. It was administered racial segregation within a public institution, executed through a formal stall-assignment system and maintained despite repeated protests from the affected community.

### The Broader Context

The stall discrimination at Pike Place Market did not exist in isolation. It was part of the systematic anti-Asian discrimination that defined Pacific Northwest politics in the early 20th century. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, the Gentlemen's Agreement of 1907 restricting Japanese immigration, the Washington Alien Land Law of 1921, and a pervasive social hostility toward Asian communities all formed the context within which Market farmers operated daily.

The Market was, in this sense, a microcosm of the broader contradiction: an institution founded on the principle of direct, fair commerce that simultaneously enforced racial hierarchy in its own operations.

## 4. Peak: The 1930s Market

The Depression-era Market was not just a commercial space for Japanese-American farmers -- it was a social institution. Farming families who traveled to the Market together spent the entire day there, arriving before dawn to set up their stalls and departing after dark. Children grew up at the Market, helping with stall work after school and on weekends. The Market was a place where Japanese-American families from different valleys met, exchanged news, arranged marriages, and maintained the social bonds that held the community together [8].

The 1930s represented both the peak of Japanese-American economic presence at the Market and the growing shadows that would culminate in their forced removal. By the mid-1930s, the Market was issuing over 600 vendor permits, with the majority held by Japanese-American farmers [8].

The Great Depression, counterintuitively, reinforced the Market's importance. As consumer purchasing power declined, the Market's direct-sale model -- eliminating the middleman markup that had motivated its founding -- became more essential, not less. Japanese-American farmers, already operating on thin margins and accustomed to maximizing yield from small plots, were better positioned than most to weather the economic downturn.

The 1930s Market was a vibrant, multilingual, overwhelmingly Japanese-American commercial space. The sights, sounds, and smells of the arcade were defined by Japanese farming families calling out prices, arranging displays of produce with the care and precision that characterized Japanese agricultural aesthetics, and conducting business in a mixture of Japanese and English.

By 1939, farmer-seller licenses totaled 515 -- the pre-war peak. The Market was not merely a place where Japanese-American farmers happened to sell. It was, functionally, a Japanese-American commercial institution operating within a nominally public framework.

## 5. Pearl Harbor and the Immediate Aftermath

The Market on December 6, 1941 was a functioning Japanese-American commercial institution. The arcade was filled with vendors who had been selling there for years, in many cases for decades. The produce was the same late-fall Pacific Northwest offering it had been every November: hardy greens, root vegetables, stored apples, dried goods. The customers were the same working-class Seattleites who depended on the Market for affordable food. Nothing about the Market on December 6 suggested that it was about to be gutted.

On December 7, 1941 -- the day of the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor -- Japanese-American farmers occupied approximately four-fifths of all Market stalls. The next day, everything changed.

The immediate aftermath of Pearl Harbor brought a wave of anti-Japanese hysteria across the West Coast. Japanese-American businesses were vandalized. Bank accounts were frozen. Community leaders were arrested by the FBI within hours, often on the basis of nothing more than their prominence in Japanese-American civic organizations. In Seattle, the Japanese-American community faced the sudden transformation from economic backbone to suspected enemy [9].

At the Market, the effect was immediate and devastating. Japanese-American farmers who had sold at Pike Place for decades -- some for the full 34 years since opening day -- faced hostility from customers and fellow vendors. The FBI raids targeted community leaders, removing the organizational capacity of the Japanese-American farming community precisely when they needed it most.

## 6. Executive Order 9066

On February 19, 1942, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, authorizing the Secretary of War to designate military areas from which "any or all persons may be excluded." The order did not mention Japanese Americans by name. It did not need to. Its sole practical application was the forced removal of approximately 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry -- roughly two-thirds of them American citizens -- from the West Coast exclusion zone [10].

Three weeks later, on March 11, 1942, Executive Order 9095 froze all "alien property and assets," preventing most Japanese Americans from selling their businesses, liquidating their assets, or even moving their belongings to storage. Families who had built farming operations over decades were forced to abandon them. Crops in the ground rotted. Equipment was left to rust. Land that could not be sold was often seized or purchased at predatory prices by neighbors and speculators [11].

> **SAFETY NOTE:** The internment of Japanese Americans during World War II is one of the most well-documented civil rights violations in American history. Not a single person of Japanese ancestry was ever charged with or convicted of espionage or sabotage against the United States. The Commission on Wartime Relocation and Internment of Civilians, established by Congress in 1980, concluded that the internment was motivated by "race prejudice, war hysteria, and a failure of political leadership." In 1988, President Ronald Reagan signed the Civil Liberties Act, formally apologizing and authorizing reparation payments of $20,000 to each surviving internee.

## 7. The Collapse in Numbers

The statistical record of the Market before and after internment is stark:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Japanese-American share of Market stalls, 1941 | ~75-80% | Pike Place Market PDA / Market Foundation |
| Farmer-seller licenses, 1939 (pre-war peak) | 515 | Seattle Municipal Archives |
| Farmer-seller licenses, 1943 (post-internment) | 196 | Seattle Municipal Archives |
| Decline in farmer-seller licenses, 1939-1943 | 62% | Calculated from above |
| Japanese Americans who never returned to Seattle | More than one-third | Densho, various oral histories |
| Japanese Americans charged with espionage | Zero | Commission on Wartime Relocation, 1983 |

The drop from 515 to 196 farmer-seller licenses in four years was not a decline. It was an amputation. The Market lost the majority of its vendor base in a single administrative action, and the stalls that had been filled by Japanese-American farmers stood empty. There was no ready replacement community with the agricultural skill, the established supply chains, and the Market relationships to fill the gap [12].

## 8. Minidoka and the Camps

Most Japanese Americans removed from the Seattle area were transported to the Minidoka Relocation Center in Jerome County, Idaho -- a hastily constructed camp in the high desert, surrounded by barbed wire and guard towers. Minidoka held approximately 9,397 internees at its peak, drawn primarily from the Pacific Northwest [13].

The conditions at Minidoka -- as at all the relocation centers -- were harsh. Families were housed in tar-paper barracks, with minimal privacy, inadequate heating, and communal latrines. The landscape was the antithesis of the lush Puget Sound valleys these families had farmed: dry, flat, windy, and hostile. Many of the older Issei, who had spent decades building farming operations in the wet, temperate Pacific Northwest, found the desert conditions physically and psychologically devastating.

### Life in the Camps

Despite the conditions, internees at Minidoka created community structures within the camp. Schools were established. A camp newspaper, the Minidoka Irrigator, was published. Gardens were planted in the desert soil -- a direct continuation of the agricultural skills that had made these families the backbone of Pike Place Market. Some internees volunteered for military service, forming the core of the 442nd Regimental Combat Team, which became the most decorated unit in U.S. Army history for its size and length of service.

The agricultural knowledge that Japanese-American families carried into the camps also served them there. They improved the barren camp grounds, grew vegetables to supplement the institutional meals, and maintained the farming identity that had defined their lives in the Puget Sound region. This continuity of agricultural practice -- from the fertile valleys of Washington to the desert of Idaho -- is one of the most poignant aspects of the internment story.

### The Return

When the camps closed in 1945, Japanese-American families who returned to Seattle found their farms gone -- sold, repossessed, or abandoned. Their Market stall positions had been filled by other vendors. Their social networks had been shattered. Many never returned to farming. Some never returned to Seattle at all.

More than one-third of the Japanese Americans removed from the Seattle area did not come back. Those who did return faced the task of rebuilding from nothing -- no land, no equipment, no savings, no credit, and a community that had, at best, failed to defend them and, at worst, actively participated in their dispossession.

## 9. The Human Cost

Statistics describe the scale of the internment. They do not describe the experience. Families who had lived in the Puget Sound region for decades -- who had children in American schools, who had businesses in the Market, who had mortgages and car payments and library cards -- were given days to dispose of everything they had accumulated. They could bring only what they could carry [14].

### What Was Lost

The inventory of losses is impossible to complete. The tangible items -- farms, equipment, inventory, vehicles, household goods, savings accounts frozen by Executive Order 9095 -- can be partially enumerated. The intangible losses -- professional reputations, customer relationships built over years, community networks, children's educational continuity, the psychological security of knowing where you belong -- cannot be quantified.

Many families sold their possessions at exploitative prices to opportunistic buyers who knew the sellers had no choice and no time. Farm equipment worth hundreds of dollars was sold for pennies. Vehicles were abandoned. Household furnishings were left in houses that would be rented to strangers. Some families gave their businesses to trusted non-Japanese friends who promised to maintain them until the families returned. Some of those promises were kept. Many were not.

### The Children

The Nisei -- American-born children of Japanese immigrants -- were American citizens. They held American birth certificates, attended American schools, pledged allegiance to the American flag. They were removed from their homes and sent to camps in the desert because of their ancestry, not their citizenship, their actions, or their beliefs.

Many Nisei who were removed as children from the Puget Sound area carried the experience for the rest of their lives. The Densho project -- which means "to transmit, to pass down" in Japanese -- was created in part to record the oral histories of surviving internees before their memories were lost. These testimonies document not just the facts of internment but its psychological texture: the shame, the confusion, the anger, the decades of silence that many internees observed before the redress movement of the 1970s and 1980s made it possible to speak publicly about what had happened.

### The Dog-Tag Generation

Internees were issued identification tags -- essentially, dog tags -- that reduced their identities to numbers. These tags, and the camp identification photographs that accompanied them, are now archived at Densho and other repositories as primary documents of the internment. The bureaucratic precision of the identification system -- numbering and photographing every man, woman, and child for efficient processing into camps -- is one of the most chilling aspects of the documentary record.

## 10. The Market After Internment

The post-war Market was a diminished institution. The vendor density never recovered its pre-war levels. The spaces that had been filled by Japanese-American farmers were partially replaced by other communities -- Filipino vendors, Italian merchants, and eventually Hmong flower farmers (see Section 11) -- but the scale and character of the pre-war Market was irretrievably lost.

The demographic collapse of the vendor community contributed directly to the Market's physical decline in the 1950s and early 1960s. With fewer vendors generating less revenue, building maintenance deferred, and the Market began to look shabby and outdated. This physical decline, in turn, provided the justification for the 1963 demolition proposal that would trigger the preservation crisis documented in Module 3.

The causal chain is direct: internment gutted the vendor community, the gutted vendor community could not sustain the physical infrastructure, the declining infrastructure invited demolition proposals. The 1960s preservation battle was, at its root, a downstream consequence of Executive Order 9066.

## 11. Legacy and Acknowledgment

The Pike Place Market's engagement with its Japanese-American history has evolved significantly over the decades, from silence to acknowledgment to active commemoration.

### The Song of the Earth

*The Song of the Earth*, a five-panel mural at the Market entrance, was commissioned as a lasting tribute to the Japanese-American community's central role in the Market's history. The mural acknowledges what the Market lost and what was done to the people who built it [14].

### Day of Remembrance

The Pike Place Market Foundation observes an annual Day of Remembrance on February 19 -- the anniversary of Executive Order 9066's signing. The observance includes educational programming, community events, and public acknowledgment of the harm done. The Foundation's statement for the 2025 observance included:

> "Not a single Issei or Nisei was ever charged or convicted of espionage, yet thousands of families suffered the cost of our country's racial exclusion and paranoia." -- Pike Place Market Foundation, Day of Remembrance, 2025 [15]

### Institutional Limitations

Acknowledgment is not repair. The Market Foundation's commemorative work is genuine and important, but it cannot restore the farms, the family businesses, the community networks, or the decades of economic participation that were destroyed. The Japanese-American community that existed at Pike Place Market in 1941 is gone -- not declined, not reduced, but destroyed by an act of government.

The Market's honest engagement with this history -- neither minimizing the harm nor claiming that acknowledgment constitutes resolution -- is one of its more admirable institutional qualities. Many American institutions with comparable histories have not managed even this.

## 12. The Hmong Flower Farmers

In 1982, the Pike Place Market partnered with King County to create the Indochinese Farm Project, a structured program supporting Hmong refugees from Laos, Cambodia, and Thailand who had arrived in the Pacific Northwest in the late 1970s and early 1980s. The project helped Hmong families re-establish farming practices adapted to the Puget Sound climate and provided pathways into the Market's daystall system [16].

Today, the largest community among the Market's flower vendors is of Hmong descent. Hmong flower farmers are a defining visual element of the Market experience -- their stalls, arranged with extraordinary care and color, line the arcade during the growing season.

### The Visual Character of Hmong Flower Stalls

The Hmong flower stalls are among the most visually striking elements of the Market experience. Hmong flower farmers arrange their displays with a precision and aesthetic sensibility rooted in their cultural traditions -- bright colors grouped by hue, bouquets assembled in graduated sizes, seasonal blooms rotated with the calendar. The stalls create a visual corridor of color that draws visitors through the arcade, serving a commercial function (attracting foot traffic) and an aesthetic function (defining the Market's visual character) simultaneously.

The flower trade also connects to the Market's agricultural identity in a way that is sometimes overlooked. Flowers are agriculture -- they require planting, cultivation, harvesting, and transport just like food crops. The Hmong flower farmers are farmers in the same sense that the Japanese-American truck farmers were farmers: they grow living things in soil and bring them to the Market to sell directly to consumers. The product is ornamental rather than nutritional, but the economic model is identical.

### Continuity and Difference

The Hmong flower farmers' presence at the Market echoes and does not replicate the Japanese-American farming community's history. Both are immigrant/refugee communities who found in agriculture and the Market's daystall system a path to economic participation. Both brought horticultural knowledge from their home countries and adapted it to Pacific Northwest conditions. Both faced cultural and linguistic barriers in the broader economy.

But the differences matter. The Hmong community arrived as refugees from war, not as economic immigrants. Their farming traditions -- rooted in the highlands of Laos and Thailand -- were adapted to very different climatic conditions than the Pacific Northwest's. And they arrived into a Market that had institutional memory of what happens when an immigrant community is destroyed -- a memory that, one hopes, provides some structural protection that was absent for the Japanese-American community in 1942.

## 13. The Alien Land Laws

The Japanese-American farming community at Pike Place Market operated under legal constraints that are essential to understanding both their achievements and their vulnerability. The Washington Alien Land Law of 1921 prohibited "aliens ineligible for citizenship" from owning or leasing agricultural land. Because Japanese immigrants (Issei) were barred from naturalization by the Naturalization Act of 1790 (which restricted citizenship to "free white persons"), the Alien Land Law effectively prohibited the first-generation Japanese community from owning the farms they worked [17].

### Legal Workarounds

Japanese-American families developed legal strategies to work within these constraints. Some purchased land in the names of their American-born children (Nisei), who were citizens by birth under the Fourteenth Amendment. Others arranged informal land-use agreements with sympathetic landowners. These workarounds were legally precarious and socially exhausting -- requiring constant navigation of a system designed to exclude them.

The Alien Land Law meant that even at the peak of their economic dominance at the Market, Japanese-American farmers did not have secure tenure on the land they farmed. They were the Market's majority vendor community operating on borrowed land, subject to legal eviction at any time. This structural insecurity made the community uniquely vulnerable to the mass removal that came in 1942 -- they could not sell land they did not legally own, and the workaround arrangements they had constructed did not survive their forced absence.

### Federal Context

The Alien Land Laws were not unique to Washington. California passed its version in 1913, and multiple other western states followed. The laws were part of a coordinated legal framework of Asian exclusion that included the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), the Gentlemen's Agreement (1907), the Immigration Act of 1924 (which banned Japanese immigration entirely), and the Naturalization Act's racial restrictions. The Market's Japanese-American farming community existed within this web of legal hostility -- succeeding economically despite it, but never protected from it.

## 14. The Produce That Built the Market

Understanding the specific agricultural contributions of Japanese-American farmers helps explain their economic centrality. These were not commodity growers producing generic crops -- they were skilled truck farmers specializing in high-value produce that required intensive cultivation, precise timing, and detailed knowledge of local growing conditions [18].

### Crop Specializations

- **Strawberries:** Particularly from Bainbridge Island and the Bellevue area; a high-value, labor-intensive crop that required hand-picking and same-day transport to market
- **Lettuce and greens:** Year-round staples of the Pacific Northwest growing season; Japanese farming techniques emphasized succession planting for continuous harvest
- **Root vegetables:** Daikon radish, burdock root (gobo), and turnips alongside conventional western root crops
- **Beans and peas:** Well-suited to the cool, wet Puget Sound spring
- **Berries:** Raspberries, blackberries, and blueberries from valley farms
- **Flowers:** Cut flowers for the Market stalls; a tradition that the Hmong flower farmers would later continue

### Agricultural Techniques

Japanese-American farmers brought intensive cultivation techniques from Japan that were well-suited to the small farms available in the Puget Sound region. These techniques emphasized high yield per acre rather than mechanized efficiency per labor hour -- an approach that produced more food per unit of land but required more human attention. The result was produce of exceptional quality and variety, grown on farms that were often too small to be viable under American extensive-farming practices.

This intensive approach was not just a cultural preference. It was an adaptation to the constraints of the Alien Land Laws and the limited capital available to immigrant families. Unable to accumulate large landholdings, Japanese-American farmers maximized the productivity of small plots -- turning a legal constraint into an agricultural advantage.

## 15. Princess Angeline and Indigenous Presence

Any complete account of Pike Place Market's location must acknowledge the Indigenous presence that preceded all subsequent settlement. The Market sits on the traditional territories of the Duwamish people, and Princess Angeline (Kikisoblu), the eldest daughter of Chief Si'ahl (for whom Seattle is named), lived in a small cabin near the waterfront below what is now the Market until her death in 1896 [19].

Angeline's presence on the waterfront -- selling baskets and clams to passersby -- represents a form of direct-sale commerce that predates the Market by decades. The irony is structural: the Market was founded in 1907 on land from which the Duwamish people had been largely displaced, using a direct-sale model that Indigenous people had practiced on the same waterfront for centuries.

This acknowledgment is not incidental to the Market's history. It is foundational. The land the Market sits on, the waterfront it overlooks, and the agricultural traditions it hosts all exist within a context of Indigenous displacement that preceded and enabled European-American settlement of the Puget Sound region.

> **SAFETY NOTE:** Indigenous individuals and communities should be identified by their specific nation (e.g., Duwamish) rather than by generic terms. Princess Angeline was Duwamish. The traditional territories acknowledged here are those of the Duwamish people specifically.

## 16. Lessons for Community Resilience

The Japanese-American experience at Pike Place Market offers several lessons for understanding community resilience and institutional fragility:

**Economic centrality does not confer political protection.** Japanese-American farmers were the Market's majority -- they could have shut it down by simply not showing up. This economic power provided zero protection when the political decision was made to remove them. Market share is not political power.

**Institutional memory is fragile.** The Market operated for decades after internment without substantial public acknowledgment of what had happened. It took the preservation movement of the 1960s, and the broader civil rights consciousness it reflected, to begin the process of honest historical accounting.

**Repair requires structural change, not just remembrance.** The PDA's charter, created in 1973, includes explicit mandates to support small and marginal businesses and to serve low-income populations. These provisions are, in part, a structural response to the lesson of 1942: that a public institution's commitment to its community must be embedded in governance, not dependent on goodwill.

**Direct-sale markets are inherently democratic but not inherently just.** The Market's founding principle -- eliminate the middleman, connect producer to consumer -- is economically democratic. But the stall assignment system demonstrated that even democratic commercial structures can be administered in discriminatory ways. Structure is not sufficient; administration matters.

**Community documentation is a form of protection.** The Densho project, the Market Foundation's Day of Remembrance, and the Song of the Earth mural all serve a documentary function -- they create a public record that makes forgetting harder. The Japanese-American community's history at the Market was nearly lost to institutional silence. The documentary efforts of the past three decades have reconstructed that history from oral testimonies, municipal archives, and community records. This reconstruction is itself a lesson: if the history had been documented in real time, with the care and specificity it deserved, the reconstruction would not have been necessary. Document while the witnesses are alive. Document with names, dates, and places. Document because the alternative is silence, and silence enables repetition.

**The wound does not heal by acknowledgment alone.** The Market Foundation's commemorative work is genuine and necessary. But acknowledgment is the beginning of repair, not its completion. The farms are still gone. The families that built the Market's golden age are still dispersed. The community that would exist today if Executive Order 9066 had never been signed -- the fourth- and fifth-generation Japanese-American farming families, the businesses, the social networks -- is a counterfactual that no amount of commemoration can make real. Honest engagement with this history requires accepting that some losses are permanent.
