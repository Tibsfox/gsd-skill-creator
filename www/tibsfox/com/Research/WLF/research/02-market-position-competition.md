## 1. The Seattle-Tacoma Radio Market

Seattle-Tacoma is the 14th-largest radio market in the United States by population, encompassing approximately 4 million people in a metropolitan area that stretches from Everett in the north to Olympia in the south, and from the Puget Sound shoreline east to the Cascade foothills. The market is measured by Nielsen Audio using Portable People Meter (PPM) methodology, which provides monthly ratings data based on passive listening detection [1][2].

The market is dominated by non-commercial public radio. KUOW (94.9 FM, NPR) consistently leads the market with a 6+ share that frequently exceeds 9.0 -- a dominance that is unusual among major American radio markets and reflects the Seattle audience's educational demographics and progressive cultural orientation. Any commercial station that achieves a 5.0+ share in Seattle is performing exceptionally well.

The commercial landscape is controlled by four ownership groups:

- **Audacy:** KKWF (country), KISW (active rock), KNDD (alternative), KSWD/Emma 94.1 (hot AC), KHTP/Hot 103.7 (rhythmic AC)
- **iHeartMedia:** Multiple stations across formats
- **Hubbard Broadcasting:** KPNW/The Bull (country), KQMV, KRWM
- **Lotus Communications:** KPLZ/Hank FM (classic country)

The market structure means KKWF competes not just against other country stations but against a broad array of well-programmed rock, alternative, and adult contemporary stations for advertising dollars and listener attention. Country music has never been the dominant format in Seattle the way it is in Nashville, Dallas, or Houston. It is a format that has had to earn its audience in a market that identifies culturally with rock, indie, and public radio.

## 2. Nielsen Audio PPM Methodology

All ratings data in this module is derived from Nielsen Audio's Portable People Meter system, which replaced diary-based measurement in major markets in the mid-2000s. PPM uses a pager-sized device worn by panelists that passively detects encoded audio from participating stations. The system provides monthly data with demographic breakdowns including 6+, 18-34, 25-54, and other age/gender splits [1].

PPM methodology has specific characteristics that affect how country radio ratings should be interpreted:

- **Passive detection** captures all listening, including background exposure in vehicles, workplaces, and retail environments -- favoring stations with broad, consistent reach.
- **Monthly measurement** creates volatility that diary ratings (which averaged across a quarter) tended to smooth out.
- **Panel size** in Seattle is typically 2,500--3,500 respondents, meaning small shifts in panelist behavior can move shares significantly.

Research Director, Inc. (RDI) publishes monthly analysis of PPM results for major markets, including Seattle-Tacoma. Their reports provide context beyond raw share numbers, including demographic trends, cume (cumulative audience) figures, and competitive dynamics.

## 3. KKWF Ratings Trajectory

KKWF's ratings history since the 2005 country launch follows a pattern of gradual growth punctuated by dramatic shifts tied to competitive events:

| Period | 6+ Share | Market Rank | Context |
|--------|----------|-------------|---------|
| 2006--2016 | 2.0--3.5 | Mid-pack | Dual-station format with KMPS; KKWF the #2 country |
| Nov 2017 (pre-KMPS flip) | 2.9 | Mid-pack | KMPS held 3.8 share |
| Dec 2017--Feb 2023 | 3.5--5.5 | Top 5 | Sole country station (KMPS gone, Bull not yet threat) |
| Jan 2024 | 7.1--7.2 | #2 overall | KPNW AAA at 0.8; KKWF absorbing entire format |
| Feb 2024 | 7.2--7.5 | #2 overall | All-time highs; approaching KUOW for market leadership |
| Apr 2025 | ~3--4 | #3 (18-34) | The Bull relaunched; Hank FM entered; share fragmented |
| Aug 2025 | Lowest in 1yr | #11 (18-34) | Sharp decline in younger demo; Bull gaining |
| Nov 2025 | 3.5 | Mid-pack | Partial recovery; streaming share 0.8 |

The trajectory reveals a clear pattern: KKWF's ratings are inversely correlated with the number of competing country stations in the market. When it was one of two country stations (2005--2017), it averaged a 2.5--3.5 share. When it was the sole country station (2018--2023), it grew to 5.0+. When it had the format entirely to itself after KPNW abandoned country (2023--early 2024), it surged to all-time highs above 7.0. When three country stations returned (April 2024 onward), the share fragmented back to the 3.0--4.0 range.

This pattern has significant implications for understanding country radio's audience size in Seattle. The all-time high of 7.2--7.5 represents something close to the total addressable market for country radio in the Seattle metro area. When that audience is split among three stations, each station gets roughly one-third -- which is almost exactly what the 2025 data shows.

## 4. Inflection Point One: The KMPS Elimination

On December 4, 2017, Entercom completed its merger with CBS Radio and acquired KMPS 94.1, Seattle's forty-year country incumbent. One industry estimate pegged KMPS annual revenue at $8--10 million, outbilling KKWF by an estimated 2:1 margin. Entercom now owned both of Seattle's country stations [2][3].

The standard playbook in radio mergers is to eliminate format overlap: if you own two country stations in the same market, you flip one to a different format and consolidate the country audience on a single signal. Entercom chose to flip KMPS, not KKWF. On December 4, 2017, KMPS became "94.1 The Sound," a soft adult contemporary station, launching with Lionel Richie's "Hello" [3].

The decision to keep KKWF rather than KMPS was not obvious. KMPS had the stronger brand, the larger audience, and the longer heritage. But KKWF had lower operating costs, a younger demographic profile, and a programming team (led by Drew Bland) that Entercom believed could capitalize on a monopoly position. The bet was that KKWF could absorb KMPS's audience without inheriting its cost structure.

The monopoly lasted approximately 90 minutes. Hubbard Broadcasting, watching the KMPS flip in real time, immediately flipped its rock station KVRQ (96.9, then moved to 98.9) to "Country 98.9," which became "98.9 The Bull" (initially KNUC, later KPNW). Hubbard had pre-positioned the country format, waiting for exactly this scenario. The 90-minute monopoly became a duopoly by the end of the afternoon [3].

Despite the rapid competitive response, KKWF benefited enormously from the KMPS elimination. The former KMPS audience had to choose between KKWF and a brand-new competitor with no established identity. Most chose The Wolf. KKWF's ratings climbed from the 2.9 range into the 4.0--5.0 range within months.

## 5. Inflection Point Two: The Accidental Monopoly

On February 14, 2023 -- Valentine's Day -- Hubbard Broadcasting flipped The Bull to adult alternative as KPNW "98.9 The Sound" (not to be confused with the former KMPS "94.1 The Sound," which had itself been renamed to "Emma 94.1"). The AAA (adult album alternative) format was intended to fill a gap in Seattle's rock landscape, positioned between KNDD's alternative and KZOK's classic rock [2][4].

The AAA experiment was a commercial failure. KPNW never exceeded a 1.0 share in any Nielsen survey period, making it one of the weakest-performing stations in the market. The format attracted neither the rock audience it targeted nor the country audience it abandoned.

Meanwhile, KKWF surged. With The Bull gone and no other country competition, The Wolf achieved something that no previous iteration of 100.7 had accomplished: it became one of the top two stations in the entire Seattle market. The January 2024 6+ share of 7.1--7.2 placed KKWF second only to KUOW. The February 2024 share of 7.2--7.5 was the highest rating any country station had achieved in Seattle history -- higher than KMPS in its best years, higher than The Wolf and The Bull had ever managed combined.

The data proved a thesis that the industry had debated for decades: Seattle's country audience was substantial, but it was being split among multiple stations. When a single station captured the entire format audience, the combined share was competitive with the market's strongest stations. Country was not a niche format in Seattle; it was an underserved format.

## 6. Inflection Point Three: Re-Fragmentation Day

April 1, 2024, is the most extraordinary single day in Seattle country radio history. In the span of hours, the market went from one country station to three [2][4].

First, Hubbard reversed course and relaunched The Bull on KPNW 98.9, bringing back former morning host Cory "Fitz" Fitzner to anchor the relaunch. The AAA experiment had lasted barely thirteen months. The return to country was an implicit admission that the format flip had been a mistake -- one that cost Hubbard millions in lost revenue and audience goodwill.

Hours later, Lotus Communications flipped Hot AC station KPLZ (Star 101.5) to classic country as "101.5 Hank FM." The Hank FM format targets an older country audience with a playlist centered on heritage artists, positioning itself as the country equivalent of a classic rock station. The format was imported from Lotus's playbook in other markets.

In a single day, Seattle's country radio landscape went from monopoly to the most competitive configuration in the market's history. Three country stations, each owned by a different company, each with a distinct positioning:

- **KKWF 100.7 The Wolf** (Audacy): Mainstream contemporary country
- **KPNW 98.9 The Bull** (Hubbard): Contemporary country with personality-driven morning
- **KPLZ 101.5 Hank FM** (Lotus): Classic and heritage country

The immediate effect on KKWF was predictable: the share dropped from the 7.0+ range to the 3.0--4.0 range. The total country listening in Seattle remained roughly constant, but the audience was now divided among three competitors. KKWF retained market leadership within the format, but it was no longer approaching overall market leadership.

## 9. Station Profile: KPLZ 101.5 Hank FM

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Frequency | 101.5 MHz |
| Call Sign | KPLZ |
| Brand | 101.5 Hank FM |
| Owner | Lotus Communications |
| Format | Classic Country |
| History | Flipped from Hot AC (Star 101.5) on April 1, 2024 |

Hank FM occupies a distinct competitive position by targeting the classic country audience -- listeners who prefer George Strait, Garth Brooks, Alan Jackson, and the Hank Williams legacy artists from which the format takes its name. This positioning avoids direct competition with KKWF and The Bull for the contemporary country listener, instead seeking to capture an underserved segment of the broader country audience.

The classic country format is a niche play. Its audience skews older and smaller than contemporary country, but it is also less expensive to program (no need for new music research, concert promotions, or artist relationships) and its audience is loyal. Whether the Seattle market is large enough to sustain three country stations -- including one classic country outlet -- is the defining question for Hank FM's long-term viability.

Lotus Communications is a privately held company based in Los Angeles with approximately 30 stations in western markets. Its presence in Seattle is modest, and Hank FM represents an opportunistic entry into the country format race rather than a strategic commitment to the market.

## 10. The Dominant Non-Country Stations

KKWF does not compete solely against other country stations. In the advertising marketplace, it competes against every station in the market for share of commercial revenue. Understanding the dominant non-country players provides context for KKWF's competitive position:

| Station | Format | Owner | Typical 6+ Share |
|---------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| KUOW 94.9 | NPR / Public | UW | 9.0+ |
| KZOK 102.5 | Classic Rock | iHeart | 4.0--5.0 |
| KNDD 107.7 | Alternative | Audacy | 3.0--4.0 |
| KISW 99.9 | Active Rock | Audacy | 3.0--4.0 |
| KRWM 106.9 | Hot AC | Hubbard | 3.0--4.0 |

The KUOW dominance is particularly significant. Public radio's strength in Seattle means the total available commercial audience is smaller than in markets where public radio is weaker. Every commercial station is competing for a listener pool that has already allocated its most educated, highest-income segment to non-commercial radio.

Two of the strongest commercial stations in the market -- KNDD and KISW -- are Audacy siblings of KKWF. This cluster relationship means KKWF's success is evaluated not in isolation but as part of a portfolio. A ratings decline at KKWF that coincides with strength at KISW or KNDD may be tolerable from Audacy's portfolio perspective, even if it represents a competitive loss within the country format.

## 11. Demographic Analysis

KKWF's demographic performance reveals the challenges of country radio in a market like Seattle:

**25-54 (primary advertising demo):** KKWF's strongest demographic, where the station typically ranks in the top 5 among commercial stations. Country music's core audience of 30--55-year-old adults aligns well with this advertising sweet spot, and KKWF has maintained consistent performance here even during periods of format competition.

**18-34 (youth demo):** KKWF's most vulnerable demographic. The August 2025 data showed the station at #11 in the 18-34 ranking, its lowest position in a year. Country music nationally is gaining young listeners through streaming and genre crossovers (Morgan Wallen, Jelly Roll), but translating streaming popularity into terrestrial radio listening among 18-34s in a market like Seattle -- where alternative, hip-hop, and indie formats compete aggressively for young ears -- remains challenging.

**6+ (total audience):** The broadest measure, where KKWF's performance fluctuates most dramatically based on competitive conditions. The 7.0+ shares of early 2024 were a 6+ phenomenon driven by having the format to itself; the 3.5 share of November 2025 reflects the three-station split.

## 12. The Format Monopoly Paradox

The KKWF ratings data illuminates a paradox that affects country radio across the industry: the format performs best as a monopoly, but the economics of radio ownership make monopolies unstable.

When KKWF was Seattle's sole country station, it captured a 7.0+ share -- a rating that placed it among the most successful commercial stations in the market. That success immediately attracted competitors. Hubbard and Lotus both saw the same data and concluded that the country audience was large enough to split. The re-fragmentation was a rational competitive response to KKWF's monopoly-era success.

But the re-fragmentation reduced each station's individual share to a level (3.0--4.0) that may not be commercially sustainable for all three. At some point, one or more of the three stations will likely exit the format, consolidating the audience again -- which will raise shares, which will attract new competitors, and the cycle will repeat.

This is not unique to Seattle. Markets across the country experience cycles of country station consolidation and fragmentation. The underlying dynamic is that the country music audience is large enough to create a tempting prize but not quite large enough (in most markets) to support three stations profitably. Two country stations typically coexist; three is unstable.

For KKWF, the strategic implication is clear: long-term ratings stability depends less on programming quality than on competitive dynamics that the station cannot control. The Wolf can be the best-programmed country station in Seattle and still see its ratings fluctuate by 100% based solely on how many competitors enter or exit the format.

## 13. Signal Coverage and Geographic Competition

The competitive dynamics among Seattle's three country stations are shaped by their transmitter locations and signal coverage patterns. All three stations broadcast from sites that provide broad coverage of the Seattle-Tacoma metro, but the specifics differ:

**KKWF 100.7 (68,000 watts, Tiger Mountain):** The strongest signal among the three country stations. Tiger Mountain's elevation and central position in the Cascade foothills provide line-of-sight coverage to the entire Puget Sound basin. The signal reaches south to Olympia, north past Everett, and west across Puget Sound to the Kitsap Peninsula. This coverage footprint encompasses Joint Base Lewis-McChord, Naval Station Everett, and the majority of the region's military installations -- a geographic advantage that amplifies the station's military-focused community engagement.

**KPNW 98.9 (63,900 watts, Tiger Mountain):** Nearly identical coverage to KKWF, as both stations transmit from the same mountain. The 4,100-watt difference in ERP is negligible in practical coverage terms. The competitive differentiation between KKWF and The Bull cannot come from signal advantage -- it must come from programming, personality, and community connection.

**KPLZ 101.5 (Lotus):** Coverage data for the post-flip Hank FM configuration varies, but the station's transmitter site and power provide comparable metro coverage. The competitive question for Hank FM is less about signal reach than about whether the classic country audience within that reach is large enough to sustain a viable advertising business.

The signal parity among the three stations means that no listener in the metro area is forced to choose one station based on reception quality. The choice is entirely about content preference. This is the purest form of format competition: identical geographic reach, differentiated only by programming.

## 14. Revenue Implications of Three-Station Competition

The advertising revenue implications of the three-station country market are significant and not yet fully resolved. Radio advertising revenue in a given format is roughly proportional to the format's share of the total audience. When that share is concentrated in one station, that station captures all of the format's advertising revenue. When the share is split among three stations, each station receives approximately one-third of the format revenue.

The critical question is whether total format revenue increases when there are more stations. Industry evidence is mixed. In some markets, multiple country stations have expanded the total country listening, as each station's promotional activity and community engagement attracts new listeners to the format. In other markets, multiple stations simply divide a fixed-size audience among more competitors, reducing per-station revenue without growing the total.

Seattle's April 2024 re-fragmentation provides a natural experiment. If the total country listening share (all three stations combined) in 2025 exceeds the monopoly-era KKWF share of 7.0+, then the multi-station market has grown the format. If the combined share is similar to or less than 7.0, then the stations are simply dividing a fixed audience. Early data suggests the combined share is comparable to the monopoly-era level, implying that the primary effect of three-station competition is revenue division rather than market expansion.

For KKWF specifically, the revenue impact of going from a 7.0 share to a 3.5 share is approximately a 50% reduction in advertising rate card pricing -- a loss that Audacy's post-bankruptcy financial reality can ill afford. The station's community engagement and CMA-winning morning show provide some pricing power above what a 3.5 share would normally command, but the competitive mathematics are unfavorable.
