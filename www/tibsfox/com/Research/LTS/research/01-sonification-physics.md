## 1. Sound in Space: The Medium Problem
Sound requires a medium. Most of space is near-vacuum. However, galaxy clusters are not vacuums -- the Perseus cluster contains hot plasma exceeding 50 million degrees (CXC, 2003). Jets from the central SMBH blow cavities ("bubbles") acting as pistons generating pressure waves detected by Chandra as X-ray brightness ripples.

## 2. The Perseus Calculation

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Discovery | 2003, British astronomers, Chandra data |
| Wave interval | ~1 cycle per 300 million million seconds |
| True pitch | ~57 octaves below B-flat above middle C |
| Frequency scaling (2022) | 57-58 octaves upward; 144-288 quadrillion x original |
| Cluster distance | ~240 million light-years |
| Cluster span | ~11 million light-years |

The 2022 sonification used radar-like radial scan technique.

## 3. LIGO and Gravitational Wave Acoustics
LIGO is categorically different: actual spacetime vibration measurement. GW150914 (Sep 14, 2015): two ~30 Msun BHs merging. The chirp rose to approximately middle C before stopping at merger. Only modest frequency scaling needed because the inspiral sweeps through the audio range naturally.

## 4. Real Waves vs. Data Translation
Critical distinction: Perseus produces real pressure waves in plasma. LIGO detects real spacetime oscillations. Most CXC sonifications are data-mapping translations -- not "sound in space" but telescope data converted to audio using defined conventions.
