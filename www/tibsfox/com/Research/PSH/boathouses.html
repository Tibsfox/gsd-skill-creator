Module A: Boathouses & Maritime Culture

Between Edmonds and Mukilteo, a chain of boathouses once defined shoreline life on Possession Sound. From the 1920s through the 1960s, these family-run operations rented small fishing boats to workers and families, supported a thriving salmon derby culture, and anchored communities that straddled the bluff, the railroad, and the tide. By 2001, every one had closed. This module documents the boathouses, the boats they built, and the way of life they sustained.

1. Losvar’s Mukilteo Boathouse

Mukilteo waterfront, between lighthouse and ferry dock · c. 1910–1971

Founded by Paul Losvar, a Norwegian immigrant who took his surname from his hometown as he passed through Ellis Island. His son George Losvar married Anna Christiansen, the lighthouse keeper’s daughter, in 1910. Three generations operated the boatyard for sixty years. Art Losvar (George’s son, d. 2019) joined his father in the business.

The Losvars built, rented, and stored boats. Their signature product was the “Mukilteo Boat” (also called the “Muk Boat”) — a clinker-built design with high bows, round bottoms, and low rear ends to accommodate outboard engines. An Eddie Bauer advertisement once claimed: “There has never been a casualty in a Mukilteo boat.” Eddie Bauer himself was among the Losvars’ most loyal customers.

They never sold used rental boats — when a boat’s useful life ended, they burned it on the beach rather than let a sub-standard vessel carry their name on the water.

— recounted in Seattle Times, 1990

The boathouse was torn down in 1971 to build the Losvar Condominiums. The last boat built by the family hangs in the lobby of Ivar’s restaurant in Mukilteo.

Sources: Seattle Times, 1990 · Mukilteo Beacon · Arthur Losvar Obituary

2. McConnell’s Boathouse

Mukilteo waterfront, near ferry dock · c. 1925–1999

Victor McConnell arrived in Mukilteo around 1900 and built a facility on Front Street for servicing boats and motors (constructed 1925, expanded 1941). George McConnell came in the 1940s, purchased Victor’s facility, and operated it with his wife for nearly 50 years. He raised three children there — “all fishermen.”

The smell of small boats, salt water, big timber beams, and a unique musty smell.

— remembered by former patrons, Seattle Times, 2002

At time of sale in 1993, McConnell’s owned about 20 boats for rent or charter and stored approximately 50 boats. The boathouse stood until 1999, when it was replaced by a hotel. George McConnell later lived in a waterfront condo built on the site of another Mukilteo boathouse.

Sources: Seattle Times, 1993 · Seattle Times, 2002

3. Norma Beach Boathouse

14725 Norma Beach Road, Edmonds · c. 1920s–present

Named for Norma Ganzina, an Italian immigrant who partnered with the original boathouse owner in 1929, then took over as sole owner and operated it successfully until 1939. After selling, Norma bought land at Lake Stickney, where she pursued various ventures — a tavern, a swim club, a par-3 golf course, and a banquet hall. The beach, the creek (Norma Creek, flowing from Lake Serene to Puget Sound), and the road are all named after her.

The boathouse still operates today — fishermen have been coming for 75+ years. Located at the same address as Sunset Bay Wharf / Beach Camp at Sunset Bay, described as the oldest continuously operating commercial establishment in Snohomish County (over 100 years), now functioning as a marine education center and summer camp.

Norma Beach is a residential neighborhood in north Edmonds, roughly at the midpoint of the Edmonds-to-Mukilteo shoreline, south of Picnic Point and north of Browns Bay/Meadowdale.

Sources: Mukilteo Beacon, 1996 · Marinas.com · Sunset Bay Wharf History

4. Haines Wharf / Meadowdale Marine

16111 76th Place West, Edmonds (Browns Bay) · 1920s–2001

The main building was a large barn barged across Puget Sound from Irondale (near Port Townsend) and placed on site in the 1920s. A wood boathouse was added in 1920; a metal boathouse was built in 1976. Herbert F. Haines acquired the facility in 1939 and created Herb Haines Sport Fishing — a full-service operation that rented kicker boats and fishing motorboats, sold fishing goods and snacks.

At peak, it stored 150 sixteen-foot plywood rental boats lowered to the water by an elevator-like device. It was the only wharf between Mukilteo and Seattle. A U.S. Coast Guard patrol boat operated from beneath the structure during World War II.

There were many, many times when all 150 of those boats would be out, with a line of cars a mile long.

— recounted in PugetSoundScape, 2013

The family sold in 1976, repurchased in 1981, and operated as Meadowdale Marine (shifted to boat storage, 200-boat capacity) until 2001. The wood boathouse collapsed in 2011 from heavy winds. The metal structure remains but is inaccessible. Redevelopment proposals in 2006 were rejected due to regulations prohibiting new waterfront structures.

Sources: PugetSoundScape · My Edmonds News · HeraldNet · Seattle Times

5. Andy’s Boat House

Brackett’s Landing, Edmonds · 1948–1977

Operated by Howard Anderson Jr. The Anderson family purchased land at Brackett’s Landing in 1945 and established a wooden boat-building operation (keelboats, sailboats, cruisers, fishing vessels). Andy’s rented 16-foot rowboats for $2.50 a day, sold bait and gear, stored private boats, and featured a small beach railway for launching boats regardless of tidal conditions. The operation had some of the earliest all-fiberglass kicker boats.

Howard Anderson Jr. sold the 47-year family property to the City of Edmonds in 1992 for parkland.

Source: Daily Journal of Commerce

6. Other Boathouses Along the Shoreline

Ole’s Boat House (Brackett’s Landing, 1930s) — Operated on what is now Brackett’s Landing Park, predating the Anderson/Andy’s operation at the same location. DJC

Chauncey’s Boathouse (Edmonds waterfront) — Described as “Chauncey’s old boathouse — once a place where prize fighters trained.” Purchased by the Port of Edmonds and torn down as part of marina development. HistoryLink

A 2002 Seattle Times retrospective names Lloyd’s, Golden Tides, Meadowdale, and Sunset Beach among the nostalgic boathouse names of Puget Sound, likely along or near this stretch of coast.

7. Kicker Boats — The Standard Rental Vessel

A “kicker boat” was a small (typically 14–16 foot) rental fishing boat used at Puget Sound boathouses from roughly the 1920s through the 1960s. The term derives from “kicker” — slang for a small outboard motor used for trolling.

CharacteristicDetail
Size14–16 feet (Losvar earliest: clinker-built 14-foot; later 16 and 18-foot models)
ConstructionPacific Northwest timber; Losvar design: high bows, round bottoms, low stern. Haines Wharf: 16-foot plywood.
PowerOriginally rowed; later fitted with small outboard “kicker” motors for slow trolling
PriceLosvar boats: under $200 (mid-1930s) to $500 (1951). Andy’s rentals: $2.50/day
SafetyEddie Bauer ad: “There has never been a casualty in a Mukilteo boat.”

Scale of the Boathouse Era

In 1955, there were 4,000 boats for rent on Puget Sound across more than 150 boathouses and resorts. In the 1950s, there were eight or nine times as many rental boats as privately owned boats on the Sound. In 1951, sport fishermen caught more than 130,000 chinook salmon in Puget Sound. Fishing derbies, beginning in the 1930s, “made heroes of a man or woman or child with a hand line, spoon, or bamboo pole and rented rowboat.”

Sources: Seattle Times, 2002 · Center for Wooden Boats · Seattle Times, 1990

8. Fish Processing Along the Shoreline

While a “Franklin family smokehouse” is referenced in local oral tradition, no corroborating documentation has been located in digitized sources across HistoryLink, Mukilteo Historical Society, Edmonds Historical Museum, Snohomish County records, or Seattle Times archives. What is documented:

  • John Lund (homesteaded Meadowdale Beach, 1878–1909): His family “operated fish seines during the salmon runs, and cleaned, split, and brined about 20 barrels of fish per season.” The preserved salmon was shipped east as a delicacy. Children helped make nets.
  • Mukilteo had “the Puget Sound region’s first cannery” in the 1880s.
  • Forage fish species present along this shoreline include chinook salmon (primary sport fish), surf smelt, Pacific herring, and sand lance (confirmed by Snohomish County MRC surveys).

Unresolved: Fish Scale Insulation

A local tradition describes actual fish scales used as building insulation in shoreline homes. No corroborating documentation exists in any published source. The term “fish scale” in architecture universally refers to a decorative shingle pattern (wood, not fish). If fish processing waste was packed into walls of structures along this shore, the practice is preserved only in oral history. The Edmonds Historical Museum (32,000+ items) and Mukilteo Historical Society are the most likely repositories for documentation.

Sources: Alderwood Heritage, Part 1 · Part 2

9. The Character of Shoreline Life

Access Between Bluff and Beach

The BNSF railroad (originally Great Northern Railway, tracks laid 1893, service began 1910 with eight daily stops) runs along the shoreline, creating a physical barrier between bluff-top communities and the beach. At Meadowdale Beach, access required walking down Lund’s Gulch (425 feet of elevation loss over about 1.25 miles) and passing through a narrow concrete culvert under the railroad — the only opening for both people and salmon. A NOAA restoration project has since widened this crossing.

At Haines Wharf, boats were lowered to the water by an elevator-like device. Andy’s Boat House had a beach railway for launching regardless of tide. In Mukilteo, families lived “in one of three houses on the beach between the lighthouse and the seawall.”

Daily Life Before and During the Boathouse Era

Pre-1910 — Water was the highway

All transportation was by water. John Lund “rowed out to steamers passing on the bay to move his products to market.” The “mosquito fleet” of small steamships linked communities. Families fished (20+ barrels brined per season), raised chickens and turkeys, planted orchards, and sold products to logging camps and Seattle restaurants.

1910s — The railroad era

Three mail trains and six passenger trains daily stopped at Meadowdale. The school served children from Picnic Point, Browns Bay logging camp, and Norma Beach. A devastating 1913 storm “tore away all the bulkheads, woodshed, boathouse, and chicken house” at Mukilteo beach homes.

1920s–1940s — Summer homes and growth

Population exploded from 100 families (1910) to 799 (1925) to over 2,100 (1945), including Seattle families with summer homes — such as George H. Bartell Sr., founder of Bartell Drugs. W.D. Cleaveland operated the first Meadowdale store (1905) and served as postmaster.

1930s–1960s — The golden age

Workers with “leathery faces and dirty fingernails” arrived in steady streams. All 150 boats at Haines Wharf would be out at once, with a mile-long line of cars. The smell was “a mixture of fish and drying boat cushions.” Fishing derbies were major social events. During WWII, a U.S. Coast Guard patrol boat operated from beneath Haines Wharf.

Sources: Alderwood Heritage, Part 1 · Part 2 · Seattle Times, 2002 · Mukilteo Historical Society · DJC · NOAA

10. The Decline of the Boathouse Era

By the mid-1960s, the boathouse era ended due to converging forces:

FactorDetail
Private boat ownershipFiberglass manufacturing and regional affluence made private boats affordable
Waterfront land valuesRising property values made boathouse operations uneconomical
1974 Boldt DecisionFederal court ruling restricted non-treaty fishing, reducing sport fishing opportunity
Chinook crashSalmon populations declined sharply; fishing closed from southern Mukilteo through Edmonds in early 1990s
RegulationsGovernment regulations increased operational costs; new overwater structures now prohibited
Cultural shiftTelevised sporting events and changing recreation patterns reduced demand

Closure Timeline

  • 1971: Losvar’s demolished for condominiums
  • 1992: Andy’s property sold to City of Edmonds for parkland
  • 1999: McConnell’s torn down, replaced by hotel
  • 2001: Haines Wharf / Meadowdale Marine ceased operations
  • 2011: Haines Wharf wood boathouse collapsed in heavy winds

The Meadowdale Country Club (built on the Lund homestead) failed in the 1960s when the access road washed out in mudslides. Vandals took over; the clubhouse burned in 1970. Snohomish County acquired the land in 1971. Regulations now prohibit new overwater structures, effectively preventing any revival of the boathouse model.

Sources: Seattle Times, 2002 · CWB Blog
