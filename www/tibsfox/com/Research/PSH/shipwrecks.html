1. Shipwreck Point & Possession Lane

The beach at the bottom of the bluffs near Possession Lane — a private road in the Windandtide neighborhood (also called “Wind and Tide”), an unincorporated area of Snohomish County between Edmonds and Mukilteo near Picnic Point — is known as Shipwreck Point, also called Franzen Beach. This was a ship-breaking and burning ground operated by the Franzen family from the Depression era through the early 1960s.

At least 26 sizeable ships were towed here and burned for scrap iron during this period. The most prominent surviving vessel is the MV Pacific Queen. Multiple other hull remnants are visible in satellite imagery — Google Earth shows at least five skeletal vessel remains on the beach, though only the Pacific Queen still resembles a ship. The other burned hulls are fragmentary remains of mostly worn-out sailing vessels towed from Seattle’s docks.

Possession Lane runs from the blufftop down toward the Puget Sound shoreline. The area has a history of geological instability — a slide shut down Possession Lane for the first half of 1997. At least 14 homes have been built along or near it over the past several decades by developer Jake Begis. The road descends steep bluffs to the BNSF railroad corridor and the beach below.

The property is private, with the current owners stating the family owns “all of the land, the tidelands, and the ships.” The beach is accessible by kayak at high tide, but beach walking from Picnic Point Park is discouraged. The Pacific Queen hull is visible from Picnic Point Park at a distance.

2. MV Pacific Queen — USS Weight (ARS-35)

Identification

The Puget Sound Maritime Historical Society (PSMHS) definitively identified the Pacific Queen hull as the former USS Weight (ARS-35), originally designated HMS Plymouth Salvor (BARS-7). The key identifying feature: it was the sole ARS vessel constructed with twin screws (dual propellers), which ruled out other candidates like USS Anchor (ARS-13). Earlier misidentifications as USS Anchor or as an Army tug have been debunked by PSMHS researchers.

The 1957 Sinking

On September 17, 1957, MV Pacific Queen sank at Tacoma’s Old Town Dock in approximately 30 feet of water following a gasoline explosion and fire originating under the afterdeck. Unknown to insurers, the vessel’s fuel capacity had been secretly enlarged from 3,000 to 8,000 gallons to supply fishing vessels. One crew member was killed in the conflagration. Insurance litigation proceeded through 1962.

Salvage & Beaching (1958–1959)

During 1958, salvors Dave Updike and Jim Vallentyne raised the wreck, floated it, and towed it to Lake Union in Seattle for removal of heavy internal items and the steel superstructure down to the main deck. At the Coast Guard’s and Seattle Fire Department’s requests, the lightened hull was towed from Lake Union and beached north of Picnic Point at Franzen Beach.

Karl Elder (grandson of Arvid Franzen, the final owner) reported he was 11 years old at the time and watched the tug labor for hours to ground the wreck, working around tidal changes, creating a large propwash depression in the beach. Arvid Franzen “blew the hole in the port side to anchor the Queen, paying $1 for a receipt.”

— Karl Elder, via PSMHS Inside Passage blog (2015)

The hull was never burned because it had been pushed too close to the hillside, and Franzen did not want to set the woods on fire. The wooden hull remains visible year-round, sitting high on the beach. Twin propeller struts are visible, confirming the twin-screw identification.

3. The Franzen Family

Origins

Arvid Franzen was born in Christchurch, New Zealand in 1880, of Anglo-Scandinavian descent. He left on a sailing ship at age fourteen, eventually reaching Alaska, where he always told people he was from Australia. His great-grandfather was Frans Michael Franzen, a Lutheran Bishop, with a statue erected in Finland.

Hilda Franzen was born in Nome, Alaska in 1905. She was Inupiat (Northern Eskimo) who grew up on King Island in Alaska.

Arvid and Hilda came to Shipwreck Point in 1930 with a daughter named Vi. Three more daughters were born there: Sonja (born 1937), Patricia (born 1941), and Jean.

The Homestead

The Franzens settled on land below the Windandtide neighborhood near Picnic Point, originally owned by the Merrill and Ring Lumber Company. They initially lived in a tent (visible in a 1930s photograph in the MHS newsletter).

Depression-Era Resourcefulness

  • First room built from a dismantled pump house, carried from Everett on a flatbed truck to Picnic Point and towed by water on log “floats”
  • Kitchen table, clawfoot tub, and stairs/stair railing salvaged from a ferry
  • Chicken coop was the wheelhouse of a tugboat (the tugboat Active, visible in a 1945 photograph)
  • Rugged one-lane road built down to the railroad tracks near the house in 1947
  • The house did not have electricity until 1992 when generators were installed
  • Propane tanks to fuel the generators were wheeled across the tracks on handcarts
  • When Arvid worked a job in Everett, he commuted by train, which would stop near his house

Family Legacy

Sonja Franzen married Roy Main in 1956, after both graduated from Everett High. They did not live at the beach house permanently until 1993. They have three children: Dale, Steven, and Lisa. Their son Steven described seeing killer whales (orcas) swimming along crab trap lines and sea lions scrambling from predatory orcas.

As of the Winter 2011 MHS newsletter, Roy and Sonja had decided to sell the family property and move away from the beach to travel. The ship and surrounding land and tidelands are all privately owned, with active no-trespassing enforcement by the current owners.

MHS Newsline Winter 2011 (primary — John & Ann Collier, interview with Sonja Franzen Main)

4. Ship-Breaking Operations

Arvid Franzen operated the salvage operation from the beginning of the Depression until the early 1960s. Ships — mostly worn-out sailing vessels taking up valuable dock space at Seattle — were towed to the beach to be burned so the iron could be salvaged.

5. Other Shipwrecks & Maritime Incidents

Schooner Equator — Robert Louis Stevenson Connection

The schooner Equator, built in 1888 by Matthew Turner in San Francisco for the South Seas copra trade, was famously chartered by Robert Louis Stevenson in 1889 for a voyage from Honolulu to the Gilbert Islands. It was placed on the Jetty Island breakwater the week of August 15, 1956. In 1967, local dentist Eldon Schalka led a rescue effort. It was the first artifact in Snohomish County named to the National Historic Registry. It now sits under a protective structure at the Port of Everett’s boat launch, though it has suffered significant deterioration.

HeraldNet

Jetty Island WWII Barracks Boat Breakwater

Ships were intentionally sunk along the northern shore of Jetty Island at the mouth of the Snohomish River in the 1930s to form a breakwater. After WWII, identical barracks boats from Lakes Washington and Union were purchased and towed north to join the breakwater. The cabins were dismantled and moved to Kayak Point, where they served as lodging for decades. The remaining hulls are heavily deteriorated, with barnacles, mussels, and even trees growing from them.

Live in Everett

The Hideaway — Marina Beach, Edmonds

A wooden boat named Hideaway, owned by Brick Engstrom, ran aground on rocks at the Marina Beach dog park in Edmonds on Halloween night (~2016–2017). Engstrom was traveling from Port Townsend to Tacoma and had anchored; he believed a ferry wake caused his anchor to slip. The boat was taking on water and listing; he jumped overboard and waded in. His dog Gio was injured and required surgery. The city of Edmonds worked with the Washington State DNR Derelict Vessel Removal Program (90% cost reimbursement) to remove it.

My Edmonds News

Derelict Sailboat — Lighthouse Park, Mukilteo (2015)

A 25-foot sailboat previously moored at the Port of Everett was cut loose and drifted south down the Snohomish River channel in April 2015. It washed up at Lighthouse Park near the Mukilteo ferry terminal. Recovery teams discovered structural damage; the boat flooded immediately when placed back in water. Towed to the Port of Edmonds for dry dock storage; DNR covered 90% of removal costs.

HeraldNet

Ghost Ship Mary B (December 2025)

A 100-foot commercial fishing vessel named Mary B broke free of its mooring at Sanko Fisheries Pier south of the Highway 2 bridge near Everett and drifted through Possession Sound on December 11, 2025, narrowly missing the ferry Tokitae and the Mukilteo terminal structures. Identified and towed away within days.

Mukilteo Beacon

Possession Sound Capsizing (May 2025)

On May 21, 2025, a 26-foot vessel carrying four people capsized northeast of Mukilteo near Howarth Park while shrimping. One man was rescued by passing boaters. Three others were lost; the boat was found upside down at a depth of 165 feet. Two bodies were recovered on May 31. One person remained missing.

Snohomish River Derelict Vessel Removal

The Snohomish MRC (Marine Resources Committee) has worked with Washington State DNR since 2018 to remove derelict vessels from the Snohomish River estuary and Everett nearshore: 5 boats removed in 2018, 3 in early 2019, 4 in 2020. One notable vessel, The Confusion, was removed in 2021 at a cost of $60,400.

6. WWII Maritime Operations — Mukilteo & Possession Sound

Mukilteo Ammunition Dock

The U.S. government acquired the former Crown Lumber Company site and constructed a large ammunition-loading complex. Workers numbered about 600 at peak, transferring ammunition, bombs, shells, and nitroglycerin from rail cars to ships via a huge pier projecting into Possession Sound (now the ferry terminal site). After WWII, the site became a “tank farm” (aviation fuel depot) during the Korean War.

Tulalip Reservation Ammunition Depot

A 2,000-acre section of the Tulalip Reservation served as a federal ammunition depot during WWII. Ammunition was stored there and brought by train to Mukilteo’s docks for loading into ships, “including Russian” vessels (per Leon L. Stock, quoted in the MHS newsletter).

Coast Guard picket boats navigated around the ammunition dock and ships loading explosives at Mukilteo during WWII. Naval Air Station Whidbey Island operated across Possession Sound at Oak Harbor to support and refuel Navy patrol planes defending Puget Sound.

— MHS Newsline Winter 2011; Leon L. Stock

Lumber Industry Context

Mukilteo had an extensive lumber milling industry along its waterfront. The Crown Lumber Company (which had acquired Mukilteo Lumber Company in 1909 for $800,000) operated a major mill. Crown closed in 1930, and the former site became the ammunition dock. A mill also once operated at the site that would become Franzen Beach/Shipwreck Point, before the Franzens settled there.

7. Correcting the Record

Several persistent misidentifications circulate in blog posts and casual sources. The PSMHS research definitively resolves these:

“Built in the 1800s” Built 1942–1943 American Car & Foundry Co., Wilmington, DE; Navy records
