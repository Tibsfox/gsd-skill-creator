Module G: The Lighthouses

Paired treaties, paired lights across the water. On January 22, 1855, the Treaty of Point Elliott was signed at Mukilteo. Four days later, on January 26, the Treaty of Point No Point was signed on the opposite shore. Half a century later, two lighthouses rose to face each other across the same water — Point No Point first lit in 1880, Mukilteo in 1906. The treaties divided the land; the lights united the passage.

1. Mukilteo Light Station

In 1902, the U.S. Lighthouse Board determined that a light and fog signal were needed at Point Elliott, citing navigating hazards including “strong, deceiving tides,” the importance to Everett shipping, and expanding maritime commerce. The Board noted the light would benefit “vessels entering the harbor of Everett, Wash., but to vessels going up Possession Sound and Saratoga Passage and by way of Deception Pass to points north.” Congress appropriated $22,000 on January 9, 1903. Total construction cost: $27,000.

Construction began in 1905 and took eight months. The light was first lit on the evening of March 1, 1906.

Carl W. Leick, Architect

Carl W. Leick, a renowned Lighthouse Service architect, designed the station. Leick’s same design was also used for the second Ediz Hook Lighthouse and Oregon’s Cape Arago Lighthouse. He reportedly promised to “build ’em stout, and make ’em last.”

The result was an octagonal wood-frame tower, 38 feet tall, with a focal height of 33 feet. Built of fir wood — unusual for the era, when most lighthouses (including nearby Lime Kiln and Alki Point) were built of concrete or brick. Victorian in style, with “delicate brackets,” “embellished entryways, steep-pitched roofs with ornate gables, and windows marked with decorative flourishes.”

The Original Station Complex

The station complex included the combination lighthouse/fog signal building, two large two-story keeper’s dwellings, an oil house, workshop, coal room, and a windmill built over a well that pumped fresh water to a 1,000-gallon tank — also supplying the town of Mukilteo.

Pre-Lighthouse History

The Snohomish people maintained a year-round village in the Mukilteo area for at least 600 years before European contact. Artifacts uncovered during waterfront construction in the 2010s were carbon-dated to approximately 1,000 years before present. The name “Mukilteo” derives from Lushootseed (bak’waltiw’), most commonly interpreted as “good camping ground.”

Captain George Vancouver landed May 31, 1792, naming the point “Rose Point” for the wild pink roses growing there. Lt. Charles Wilkes later renamed it “Point Elliott” during the U.S. Exploring Expedition (1838–42).

Treaty of Point Elliott (January 22, 1855)

The treaty was signed at Muckl-te-oh / Point Elliott, now Mukilteo. Washington Territorial Governor Isaac Stevens presided. More than 2,000 Native Americans gathered from throughout the Northern Puget Sound region. Eighty-two tribal representatives signed, including Chief Seattle (Duwamish/Suquamish), Chief Patkanim (Snoqualmie/Snohomish), Chief Goliah (Skagit), and Chief Chow-its-hoot (Lummi). Approximately 22 named tribal groups were represented.

The translation chain ran from English to Chinook Jargon (by Benjamin F. Shaw), then to various Lushootseed dialects (by John Taylor, a Snohomish Indian). Chinook Jargon had roughly 50 words. Tribal leaders drew the letter “X” as their signature.

Tribes ceded millions of acres in exchange for protected reservation lands, hospital and school facilities, and preserved rights to “hunt, gather, and fish in the ancestral lands” (Article 5). The treaty established the Port Madison, Tulalip, Swinomish, and Lummi reservations — but notably, no reservations were designated for the Duwamish, Snohomish, Snoqualmie, or Skagit peoples. Ratified by the U.S. Senate March 8, 1859; proclaimed by President James Buchanan April 11, 1859. A copy of the treaty is displayed at the lighthouse. The Boldt Decision (1974, upheld 1979) later affirmed the treaty-protected fishing rights.

2. The Christiansen-Losvar Connection

Peter N. Christiansen — born Peder Nicolaij Kristiansen on December 3, 1858, in Stavanger, Norway — was the first and longest-serving keeper of the Mukilteo Light Station, serving 19 years from 1906 to 1925.

He went to sea at age 14, spent 11 years in the merchant marine, and immigrated to the United States in 1882 from Glasgow, Scotland. He served approximately 10 years in the U.S. Navy before joining the Lighthouse Service in 1894. He served at Turn Point Lighthouse on Stuart Island in the San Juan Islands — 4 years as assistant keeper, 8 years as head keeper — before his assignment to Mukilteo, which was considered “a reward for his long record of faithful services.”

Physical Description (1923 Passport)

5’5” tall, blue eyes, brown hair, “sailor with girl” tattoo on left forearm.

Christiansen married Theodine Tonnessen on January 12, 1888, in Oslo, Norway. Their children: Anna (b. 1888, Oslo), Charles (b. August 1894 at Turn Point), Perry (b. February 1, 1896), and Clara (b. 1902).

The Turn Point Rescue (1897)

On February 16, 1897, at Turn Point, Christiansen and fellow keeper Durgan rescued the crew of the tug Enterprise after it ran aground. They subdued an intoxicated sailor brandishing a knife and rescued additional crew from a barge. The Lighthouse Board commended their “sterling courage and fidelity to duty.”

The Losvar Connection

Anna Christiansen married George Losvar on October 23, 1910, connecting the lighthouse’s first keeper family to the Losvar boathouse family along the Mukilteo waterfront. Anna and George had children: Theodine (b. 1918), Arthur (b. 1925), Albert (b. 1928), Paul (b. 1930), and Alden (died around age 12).

Theodine Christiansen (Peter’s wife) served as Assistant Keeper in 1918. After Peter’s death in 1925, she served as head keeper until replaced by Edward Brooks.

Peter Christiansen’s Death

On September 4, 1925, Christiansen rescued a drifting boy near the lighthouse. One month later, on October 5, 1925, he died shortly after helping unload a shipment of coal at the station — likely a heart attack. He was 66. Buried at Evergreen Cemetery in Everett on October 9, 1925.

3. Keeper Histories

4. Operational History

The Fresnel Lens

The light has been through three eras. The original revolving fourth-order Fresnel lens (Barbier & Benard, Paris) used a kerosene lamp and was visible 10+ miles. In 1927, electricity was installed and the revolving lens was replaced by a fixed fourth-order Fresnel lens manufactured in 1852 by L. Sautter & Cie of Paris. This 1852 lens remains in use today — the only active Fresnel lens in Washington State.

In 1960, the Coast Guard planned to replace the Fresnel lens with a modern airport-style beacon. Local community protest successfully preserved it. Current characteristic: white flash every 5 seconds, range of 12 nautical miles.

Two Fresnel lenses are now present at the station: the active 1852 fixed fourth-order, and a multi-bull’s-eyed fourth-order of uncertain origin (possibly the Desdemona Sands lens, acquired by the Mukilteo Historical Society for display).

The Fog Signal

1906

Daboll trumpet: compressed air passing across a vibrating reed, blasted through a large brass trumpet extending through the wall into Possession Sound. Blasts at 16-second intervals; audible 6–8 miles. In its first four months (March 1 – June 30, 1906), the signal operated for 28 hours.

1979

Automated fog signal installed simultaneously with light automation.

1981

Remote fog sensor installed. Initially malfunctioned on clear nights — the sensor was detecting the white-painted seawall as fog. Problem solved by painting the seawall black.

Ownership Transfers

1939

U.S. Lighthouse Service merged into the U.S. Coast Guard; Coast Guard assumed responsibility.

1979

Light automated under keeper Russell E. Medley.

1991

Coast Guard leased the lighthouse to the City of Mukilteo; public access granted.

2001

Full ownership transferred to the city. Conditions: nonprofit center for maritime history interpretation; maintained as Coast Guard aid to navigation; maintained per the National Historic Preservation Act of 1966.

2003

Governor Gary Locke formally deeded the 18-acre state park property to the city. Officially renamed “Mukilteo Lighthouse Park.”

WWII

The station’s light was shut off during wartime blackout periods imposed along the West Coast. This was the only period of darkness in the lighthouse’s history.

5. Architecture & Preservation

Officially classified as “Late 19th and 20th Century Revivals: Colonial Revival/Classical Revival” per the NRHP nomination. Carl W. Leick’s design featured the octagonal tower with decorative brackets, the attached fog signal building, and keeper’s houses with ornate gables. Wood-frame construction in fir is distinctive — most contemporary West Coast lighthouses used concrete or brick.

National Register of Historic Places

Listed October 21, 1977 (NRHP No. 77001360). Also listed on the Washington Heritage Register (No. EO 01) on the same date. In 2008, a successful renomination expanded coverage to all Light Station buildings and grounds (the original nomination covered only the lighthouse itself).

Cultural Significance

“The lighthouse is the icon of the city” (president, Mukilteo Historical Society). It is one of the most visited lighthouses in Washington State due to easy access, adjacent to the Washington State Ferries Mukilteo-Clinton terminal and the commuter train station.

Welcome Poles (2025)

Two 10-foot red-cedar Welcome Poles by Tulalip artists Joe Gobin and James Madison were unveiled during MukFest 2025 — one representing Tulalip Peoples, the other a modern “Blackfish” (orca) narrative. The art recognizes Mukilteo’s “10,000-year human history” and the ancestral lands of the Tulalip Peoples. Gobin had been working to bring Native artwork to Mukilteo for over 30 years.

6. Point No Point Lighthouse

First lit January 1, 1880 — the oldest lighthouse on Puget Sound proper. Located on the northern tip of the Kitsap Peninsula at Hansville, Washington, at the entrance to Puget Sound from Admiralty Inlet.

The Name

Named by the Wilkes Expedition in 1841. The name reflects a navigational illusion: when approaching from the north, the point appears prominent and clearly defined, but as a vessel draws closer, the point seems to flatten out and nearly disappear. A low, sandy spit that barely rises above the high tide line — a point that was “no point.” This deceptive quality, combined with the point’s navigational importance at the transition between Admiralty Inlet and Puget Sound, is precisely why a lighthouse was built there.

Treaty of Point No Point (January 26, 1855)

Signed just four days after the Treaty of Point Elliott at Mukilteo. Part of Territorial Governor Isaac Stevens’s rapid treaty-making campaign. The signatory tribes:

  • S’Klallam (Nux-Sklai-Yum) — territory along the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the northern Kitsap Peninsula
  • Skokomish (Twana) — homeland was Hood Canal and the southern Olympic Peninsula lowlands
  • Chimakum (Tsemakum) — a small tribe around Port Townsend and the northeastern Olympic Peninsula, already diminished by 1855 and largely absorbed within a generation

The treaty ceded lands encompassing the northern Kitsap Peninsula, the eastern shore of the Olympic Peninsula along Hood Canal, and lands along the Strait of Juan de Fuca. In exchange: reservation lands (most notably the Skokomish Reservation at the head of Hood Canal), cash payments, and retained fishing, hunting, and gathering rights. The Treaty of Point Elliott (east side) and the Treaty of Point No Point (west side) are geographic mirrors — one signed at Mukilteo looking west, the other at Point No Point looking east.

The Keeper’s Quarters

The keeper’s quarters have been converted into a vacation rental operated by the US Lighthouse Society. Visitors can stay overnight in the historic dwelling — one of a small number of lighthouse keeper’s quarters in the US that offer overnight stays. The rental generates revenue supporting ongoing preservation. The US Lighthouse Society has been the primary steward under a lease agreement, investing in restoration, interpretation, and public access.

The Park

Point No Point County Park (Kitsap County Parks) encompasses the lighthouse grounds and surrounding beach. Harbor seals haul out on the nearby beach and rocks. Bald eagles nest in the area. The point sits on the Pacific Flyway, excellent for observing migratory shorebirds, waterfowl, and raptors. Orca and gray whales are occasionally spotted during seasonal movements.

7. Paired Lights Across the Water

On clear days, Point No Point Lighthouse is directly visible from the eastern shore along the Edmonds, Picnic Point, and Mukilteo waterfront — the low white lighthouse buildings seen across approximately 7–8 miles of open water. The two lights face each other, one on the Kitsap Peninsula, one on the Snohomish County mainland.

Vessels transiting between Seattle/Everett and the Strait of Juan de Fuca passed through a corridor where both Point No Point and Mukilteo Lighthouse were visible simultaneously. A northbound vessel leaving Everett/Possession Sound would have Mukilteo Light to starboard (east) and then Point No Point Light to port (west) as it entered Admiralty Inlet. The two lights functioned as lateral markers for the main north-south shipping channel through central Puget Sound.

The Mosquito Fleet

During the era of the Puget Sound Mosquito Fleet (roughly 1880s–1930s), dozens of small passenger and freight steamers crisscrossed these waters daily. These vessels — serving communities on both sides of the Sound including Edmonds, Mukilteo, Kingston, Hansville, and the San Juan Islands — relied on Point No Point and the other Puget Sound lights for safe navigation, particularly during fog, rain, and winter darkness.

Geographic Symmetry

The placement of these two lighthouses creates a remarkable symmetry. The Treaty of Point No Point (1855, west side) and the Treaty of Point Elliott at Mukilteo (1855, east side) mirror the same pattern in political geography. Both shores witnessed the same historical forces — Indigenous treaty-making, maritime commerce, lighthouse construction — playing out in parallel across the water.

Today, Washington State Ferries’ Kingston-Edmonds route crosses this same stretch of water. Ferry passengers pass within a few miles of Point No Point and can clearly see the lighthouse from the ferry deck — a direct, living connection between the two shores.
