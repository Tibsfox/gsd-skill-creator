Module I: Coastal Defense & Forts

Puget Sound’s coastal defense system — from the 1885 Endicott Board through the WWII-era radar batteries — represents one of the most complete and best-preserved harbor fortification networks in the United States. The forts never fired a hostile shot, yet they shaped the military geography, industrial economy, and community character of the entire Possession Sound corridor.

1. The Endicott System

In 1885, Secretary of War William C. Endicott convened a board to evaluate the state of American coastal defenses. The conclusion was stark: the nation’s fortifications were “dangerously obsolete,” still relying on Civil War–era masonry forts that could not withstand the rifled naval guns of modern steel warships. The Endicott Board recommended a $127 million national modernization program — the largest peacetime military construction effort in American history to that point.

For Puget Sound, the strategic calculus was clear. Admiralty Inlet is the sole deep-water passage from the Strait of Juan de Fuca into the Sound. Behind that chokepoint lay the Puget Sound Navy Yard at Bremerton (established 1891), the cities of Seattle, Tacoma, and Everett, extensive timber and shipping industries, and the railroad termini of the Northern Pacific and Great Northern railways. Losing control of Puget Sound would have been a catastrophic military and economic blow.

The Endicott-era solution was elegant: concentrate massive firepower at the chokepoint. Three forts — Casey, Worden, and Flagler — were built between 1897 and 1905 to create interlocking fields of fire across Admiralty Inlet. The total system cost approximately $7.5 million in 1900s dollars. By 1905, Fort Worden was fully staffed with four Coast Artillery companies and the system was declared complete and operational.

The Taft Board (1905) subsequently added searchlights, electrification, and improved optical fire control. The Coast Artillery Corps was established as a separate Army branch in 1907, and the 14th Coast Artillery Regiment was organized at Fort Worden on July 1, 1924, becoming the Regular Army component of the Harbor Defenses of Puget Sound.

2. Triangle of Fire

The Triangle of Fire — sometimes called the “Triangle of Death” in period sources — was the operational concept linking three Endicott-era forts into a single interlocking defense system across Admiralty Inlet. Any hostile fleet attempting to enter Puget Sound would face crossfire from three directions simultaneously.

Fort Casey (Whidbey Island, 1897)

Established on Admiralty Head at the western entrance to Admiralty Inlet. Named after Brigadier General Thomas Lincoln Casey, U.S. Army Chief of Engineers who oversaw completion of the Washington Monument. Fort Casey formed the western point of the Triangle.

The Buffington-Crozier disappearing carriages allowed guns to rise above the parapet to fire, then recoil below it for reloading — protecting crews from return fire. Two restored 10-inch guns (brought from Fort Wint in the Philippines) are on display today, among the few remaining Endicott-era disappearing carriages in the nation. Fort Casey is now Fort Casey State Park, with the restored Admiralty Head Lighthouse (1903) serving as an interpretive center.

Fort Worden (Port Townsend, 1897–1902)

Located at Point Wilson on the Quimper Peninsula. Named after Rear Admiral John L. Worden, commander of the USS Monitor. Fort Worden was the most heavily armed of the three and served as headquarters for the Harbor Defenses of Puget Sound.

Battery Kinzie mounted the most powerful weapons in the entire Triangle: two 12-inch guns on barbette carriages with a range of approximately 8–9 miles. Fort Worden also controlled the submarine mine field across Admiralty Inlet — electrically-controlled contact mines that could be detonated from the fort’s mine casemate. Now Fort Worden Historical State Park and conference center, famously used as a filming location for An Officer and a Gentleman (1982).

Fort Flagler (Marrowstone Island, 1897)

Established at Marrowstone Point, the southern point of the Triangle. Named after Brigadier General Daniel W. Flagler, Chief of Ordnance. Fort Flagler served as the “backstop” — any ship that survived the initial crossfire from Worden and Casey would face Flagler’s guns.

Fort Flagler’s most distinctive capability was Battery Calwell: four 12-inch mortars that lobbed 700-pound shells in high parabolic arcs designed to strike the thin deck armor of warships. Battery L of the 14th Coast Artillery operated a joint Army-Navy hydro-acoustic ranging station at Fort Flagler. Now Fort Flagler State Park.

How the System Worked

A hostile fleet approaching from the Strait of Juan de Fuca would first come under fire from Fort Worden and Fort Casey as it entered the inlet. It would be caught in crossfire from all three forts through the narrowest section. The submarine mine field was designed to slow or stop vessels in the kill zone where all three forts’ guns could concentrate fire. Searchlights could illuminate the channel at night. Fire-control stations used triangulation between the forts to calculate range and bearing. The three forts were linked and operated as if they were a single installation.

Obsolescence

The Triangle was never tested in combat. Against pre-dreadnought battleships of the 1900–1917 era, it would have been a serious obstacle. Against WWI-era dreadnoughts with 14–16 inch guns, the forts would have been outranged. By WWII, fixed coastal fortifications were considered largely obsolete against modern naval forces employing carrier aviation, long-range gunfire, and amphibious assault. The Endicott-era guns were scrapped for metal during wartime drives. All three forts were deactivated between 1946 and 1953; the Harbor Defenses of Puget Sound were officially deactivated in 1953.

3. Fort Ebey & Battery 248

Fort Ebey was established in 1942 on Partridge Point on the west coast of Whidbey Island, fundamentally different from the Endicott-era forts. Construction of Battery 248 began on May 18, 1942, was completed February 1, 1944, and transferred to the Coast Artillery for operational use on March 22, 1944.

The fort was named for Colonel Isaac Neff Ebey (1818–1857), the first permanent white settler on Whidbey Island. On August 11, 1857, a raiding party of North Coast Indians — likely Kake Tlingit, not Haida as traditionally stated — beheaded Colonel Ebey at his home near Ebey’s Landing. The raiders were retaliating for the killing of 27 tribal members, including a chief, by the USS Massachusetts the previous year at the Battle of Port Gamble. The raiding party was reportedly led by a woman warrior.

Why It Was Built

The Triangle of Fire defended Admiralty Inlet, but after Pearl Harbor the military recognized a critical gap: an enemy approaching from the open Pacific through the Strait of Juan de Fuca could bypass the inlet defenses entirely. Fort Ebey addressed the western approach threat. While the Endicott forts faced inward toward Admiralty Inlet, Fort Ebey and Camp Hayden faced outward toward the Pacific.

The SCR-296A radar was a decisive technological leap over the Endicott era. It determined range and azimuth of enemy ships and transmitted data directly to electro-mechanical gun data computers, replacing purely optical systems and enabling target acquisition in complete darkness, fog, or rain. The battery incorporated extensive camouflage to hide from aerial observation — a WWII-specific concern absent from the 1890s designs.

Gunnery Record

Battery 248 was never fired against an enemy target. However, in 1944, Battery C of the 14th Coast Artillery won an international gunnery competition by scoring 22 hits in 18 rounds on a target towed at 14,000 yards simulating a destroyer — an exceptional achievement for coastal artillery.

Fort Ebey State Park

Declared surplus in 1947 and manned until 1948. Washington State Parks purchased 204 acres for $57,500 in 1965; the park opened June 13, 1981. Now 651 acres with 25 miles of trails, 3 miles of Strait of Juan de Fuca shoreline, and 50 campsites. The concrete gun emplacements and underground rooms of Battery 248 are open for visitor exploration. Fort Ebey lies within Ebey’s Landing National Historical Reserve, the first and only National Historical Reserve in the United States (established 1978), preserving 19,333 acres of continuously cultivated prairie farmland and settlement history dating to the 1850s.

4. Camp Hayden

Camp Hayden (later Fort Hayden) was established in 1940 at Striped Peak/Tongue Point in Clallam County, west of Port Angeles. It was the most powerful WWII addition to the Puget Sound defense network, mounting weapons that dwarfed everything in the Endicott-era arsenal.

Battery 131 — The 16-Inch Guns

Two 16-inch guns in reinforced concrete casemates. Each gun fired a projectile weighing one ton to a range of nearly 28 miles. The plotting and switchboard room had 12–15 foot thick walls and a 16-foot roof covered with 3 feet of earth. Construction cost: $1,557,500. Supported by 11 fire control structures positioned along the Strait of Juan de Fuca.

Battery 249

Two 6-inch rapid-fire guns, range 15 miles, firing 105-pound armor-piercing shells at 5 rounds per minute. Cost: $350,000. Supported by 9 fire control structures. Approximately 150 personnel were housed in temporary barracks along Salt Creek.

Camp Hayden protected the entrance to the Sound, Victoria BC harbor, and Canada’s Esquimalt naval base. It was redesignated Fort Hayden on April 17, 1944, and abandoned in 1948. The site is now Clallam County’s Salt Creek Recreation Area.

5. Fort Ward & Fort Whitman

Fort Ward — Protecting Bremerton

Fort Ward (1900–1934) occupied the south end of Bainbridge Island, built specifically to protect the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard at Bremerton — the only West Coast facility equipped to dry-dock battleships and aircraft carriers when Pearl Harbor was attacked. During WWII, Bremerton repaired 344 fighting ships operating five dry docks 24 hours a day, including five of the eight battleships bombed at Pearl Harbor.

Fort Ward’s primary weapon was the controlled mine field in Rich Passage — mines connected via waterproof electrical cables to individual firing boards on shore-based control panels. Approximately 22,000 tons of concrete were used for gun emplacements alone. Four batteries protected the mine operators:

Battery Nash gunners once scored 8 of 10 hits on floating targets, beating all surrounding forts. Admiralty Inlet itself was too deep and its currents too swift for reliable mine deployment — which is precisely why the Triangle of Fire’s gun batteries had to be so powerful. Fort Ward is now a Bainbridge Island city park.

Fort Whitman — The Back Door

Fort Whitman (1900–1947) occupied Goat Island in Skagit Bay near LaConner. Named for Dr. Marcus Whitman (killed November 29, 1847). It guarded Deception Pass and Saratoga Pass — the “back entrance” to Puget Sound via the east side of Whidbey Island.

Battery Harrison mounted four 6-inch guns on three mounts with associated rooms and tunnels. Fort Whitman also maintained a remotely-fired mine field across the back channel, with a mine/engineer wharf, mine casemate, battery engineer station, radio room, and searchlight shelter. Battery C of the 14th Coast Artillery was rushed to Fort Whitman on December 20, 1941 — the emergency reactivation after Pearl Harbor — before transferring to Fort Ebey in 1943. The guns were “pretty much obsolete” before WWI, but Goat Island continued to serve through WWII. Described as “hiding in plain sight,” the remains are still accessible.

Submarine Mine Fields

The mine defense network was a critical complement to the gun batteries:

  • Rich Passage — Fort Ward’s electrically controlled mine field, detonated by remote control from onshore firing stations
  • Admiralty Inlet — Too deep, currents too swift for reliable deployment; the gun batteries had to compensate
  • Deception Pass — Fort Whitman’s remotely-fired mine field across the back channel
  • Naval Magazine Indian Island — Assembled mines and manufactured giant anti-submarine nets; at peak, 350 military and 200 civilian personnel working around the clock

6. Paine Field Military History

Paine Field (KPAE) has deep military roots that directly shaped the industrial character of the Mukilteo-Everett corridor. The field was named after Lieutenant Topliff Olin Paine, a pioneer Army aviator from Everett killed in a training accident at Rockwell Field, San Diego, in 1922. Snohomish County purchased 640 acres near Mukilteo in 1936 for a county airport.

WWII (1941–1947)

After Pearl Harbor, the Army Air Corps took over Paine Field as a military installation. P-38 Lightning and P-40 Warhawk fighters were stationed for Pacific Northwest air defense — tasked with intercepting any Japanese aircraft that might reach the coast. B-17 Flying Fortress and B-25 Mitchell bombers operated for patrol and training. The 361st Fighter Group trained at Paine Field before deploying to Europe. Runways were lengthened and hardened, hangars and support facilities built — establishing the infrastructure that would later attract Boeing.

Cold War (1951–1966)

During the Korean War, the Air Force reactivated Paine Field for Air Defense Command interceptor squadrons. The 325th Fighter-Interceptor Squadron flew F-86 Sabres, then F-102 Delta Daggers and F-106 Delta Darts — dedicated to defending the Pacific Northwest (and specifically Boeing and the Hanford nuclear facilities) from Soviet bomber attack. Paine Field was part of the SAGE (Semi-Automatic Ground Environment) air defense network.

The Boeing Transition

In 1966, the Air Force departed as interceptor missions consolidated elsewhere. Boeing selected the former military site for its new 747 manufacturing plant — the largest building by volume in the world. The existing military runway infrastructure was a key factor: the 747 needed a long runway for test flights. The Boeing Everett Factory opened in 1968; the first 747 rolled out in February 1969. Without military investment in the 1940s–1960s, Paine Field might never have had the infrastructure to attract the world’s largest aircraft factory.

Today Paine Field serves triple duty: Boeing production and test flights, general aviation, and commercial airline service (since 2019). The Flying Heritage & Combat Armor Museum (founded by Paul Allen) preserves WWII-era fighters and bombers — a direct connection to the field’s military heritage.

7. The Japanese Threat

The fear of Japanese attack on the Pacific Coast was not hypothetical. Japanese I-class submarines operated extensively along the Washington and Oregon coasts in 1942, bringing the war within striking distance of Puget Sound’s entrance.

I-25: Operations in Pacific Northwest Waters

June 20, 1942

I-25 torpedoed the Canadian freighter SS Fort Camosun approximately 9 nautical miles off the Washington coast. The ship was carrying zinc, lead, and plywood for the war effort and was towed into Puget Sound for repairs.

June 21, 1942

I-25 shelled Fort Stevens, Oregon at the mouth of the Columbia River. Commander Akiji Tagami fired 17 shells at Battery Russell. Fort Stevens did not return fire — Commander Carl Doney determined the submarine was out of range and did not want to reveal gun positions. This was the only time a military base in the contiguous United States was attacked by Axis powers during WWII.

September 9 & 29, 1942

I-25 launched a Yokosuka E14Y floatplane piloted by Warrant Officer Nobuo Fujita to drop incendiary bombs over Oregon forests near Brookings and Port Orford — the only time the mainland U.S. was bombed by enemy aircraft during the war. Wet conditions prevented major fires.

October 4–5, 1942

I-25 torpedoed the tanker SS Camden (76,000 barrels of gasoline; towed toward Puget Sound but destroyed by fire) and sank the tanker SS Larry Doheny (66,000 barrels; four crew killed). I-25 was eventually sunk September 3, 1943, near the New Hebrides; all 100 crew perished.

Balloon Bombs

Between November 1944 and April 1945, Japan launched approximately 9,300 Fu-Go balloon bombs. About 300 were found or observed in the U.S., Canada, and Mexico. 28 incendiary balloons landed in Washington State.

The most strategically significant incident occurred on March 10, 1945, when a balloon struck a Bonneville Power Administration high-tension transmission line near Toppenish, Washington, causing a short circuit that cut power to the Manhattan Project’s Hanford plutonium production facility. Backup systems restored power, but it took three days for Hanford to reach full capacity. This was the closest the balloon bombs came to causing major strategic damage.

On May 5, 1945, at Bly, Oregon, six civilians were killed discovering a balloon bomb — the only fatal enemy action casualties in the continental U.S. during WWII. A strict censorship campaign suppressed news coverage; the Office of Censorship stated media cooperation had “baffled the Japanese” who could not confirm their bombs were reaching America.

Fort Lawton — The POW Incident

Fort Lawton (1900) on Magnolia Bluff in Seattle served as a prisoner of war camp during WWII, holding primarily Italian POWs. On the night of August 14–15, 1944, Italian POW Guglielmo Olivotto was found lynched. The subsequent Fort Lawton court-martial was the largest Army court-martial of WWII: 43 African American soldiers were tried; 28 were convicted. Decades later, author Jack Hamann’s research (On American Soil, 2005) revealed coerced confessions, suppressed evidence, and racial bias. In 2007–2008, the U.S. Army formally overturned the convictions and granted honorable discharges. Fort Lawton became Discovery Park in 1972; the Daybreak Star Indian Cultural Center (opened 1977) was established after a 1970 occupation asserting treaty rights.

The Guns Never Fired

None of the Puget Sound coastal defense guns — at any fort — were ever fired against an enemy target. The Triangle of Fire forts never fired a hostile shot. Fort Ebey’s Battery 248 was never fired in combat. The guns’ deterrent value, and the fact that Japan never attempted to force the Strait of Juan de Fuca, may have been their greatest success.
