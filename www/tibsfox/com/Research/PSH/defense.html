Module H: Defense & Aerospace

The Possession Sound corridor sits at the intersection of three major defense and aerospace installations that together create one of the densest concentrations of military-industrial economic activity on the West Coast outside of San Diego. This module traces the Boeing factory, the naval presence, the shipwright heritage that preceded both, and the avionics innovations that emerged from the same workforce culture.

1. Boeing Everett & Paine Field

The World’s Largest Building

Groundbreaking for the Boeing Everett factory occurred in June 1966 on a former WWII-era military airfield with runways long enough for widebody aircraft and large tracts of undeveloped land. The first building was completed in 1967 for 747 production. The facility has been expanded multiple times — for the 767 (1978), 777 (1992), and 787 (2004–2007) — and today covers 98.3 acres under one roof: 4,280,000 square feet of floor area and 472 million cubic feet of volume. At peak employment, the complex supported approximately 30,000–35,000 workers; current estimates place the workforce at 20,000–25,000 following the 787 consolidation to North Charleston, SC in 2021.

Mukilteo as Boeing Bedroom Community

Mukilteo is essentially a Boeing bedroom community. Before 1966, south Snohomish County was largely rural and semi-rural, with small towns and beach communities strung along the bluffs above Possession Sound. The construction of the Everett factory triggered explosive suburban development between 1967 and 1980. Housing developments filled the formerly wooded areas between the beach bluffs and I-5. Schools, shopping centers, and infrastructure were built to serve the Boeing workforce. The population of Mukilteo went from approximately 1,800 in 1960 to over 20,000 by 2000.

Boeing Boulevard (officially Mukilteo Speedway / SR 525) became the primary route between the factory and the residential areas of Mukilteo, Lynnwood, and points south. The bluff-top neighborhoods above Picnic Point, Meadowdale, and Norma Beach — overlooking Possession Sound — are heavily populated by Boeing workers and retirees. Edmonds drew Boeing managers and engineers attracted to its waterfront character and ferry access to Seattle.

Paine Field Aviation Landmarks

Paine Field hosts a cluster of aviation heritage institutions: the Future of Flight Aviation Center & Boeing Tour (approximately 300,000 annual visitors), the Historic Flight Foundation (flyable vintage aircraft, founded by John T. Sessions), the Museum of Flight Restoration Center (a satellite of Seattle’s Museum of Flight), and formerly the Flying Heritage & Combat Armor Museum (founded by Paul Allen). Alaska Airlines began commercial passenger service from a new terminal in March 2019, later joined by United Airlines — a controversial development adding jet noise to communities already experiencing Growler overflights from NAS Whidbey.

2. Naval Station Everett

Naval Station Everett was commissioned on December 21, 1994, a product of the Navy’s “Strategic Homeporting” initiative of the 1980s under Secretary John Lehman, intended to disperse the Pacific Fleet beyond San Diego and Bremerton. The station occupies approximately 102 acres on the Everett waterfront at Port Gardner Bay, on land formerly occupied by industrial and Weyerhaeuser timber operations. It serves as home port for a Carrier Strike Group — including the aircraft carrier USS Nimitz (CVN-68) — and typically hosts 5–7 surface combatants.

Defense Contractor Ecosystem

NSE’s establishment created a significant defense contracting ecosystem in south Snohomish County. Ship maintenance and repair is handled by Vigor Everett (multi-year Navy contracts), BAE Systems Ship Repair, and numerous small marine service companies (diving, hull cleaning, marine electrical, valve repair). Electronics and IT support comes from Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (RTX), L3Harris, SAIC, Leidos, and General Dynamics IT. Many of these contractors maintain offices in the Lynnwood/Bothell/Everett corridor along the I-5/I-405 interchange rather than on the waterfront itself.

NAS Whidbey Island

NAS Whidbey Island (established September 21, 1942) at Oak Harbor is home to all Navy EA-18G Growler electronic warfare aircraft — approximately 17 squadrons under Electronic Attack Wing Pacific — plus maritime patrol squadrons and search and rescue. The base employs approximately 10,000 military personnel and 2,500 civilians, generating an estimated $1.2–1.5 billion annually in regional economic impact. A significant portion of the civilian workforce commutes via the Mukilteo-Clinton ferry, one of the busiest routes in the Washington State Ferries system at approximately 4 million passengers per year. Contractors supporting Growler systems — Northrop Grumman, Boeing, Raytheon — maintain offices in the Lynnwood/Bothell corridor.

3. Puget Sound Naval Shipyard — Bremerton

130 Years of Continuous Operation

Puget Sound Naval Shipyard was established on September 16, 1891, when the Navy Department purchased 190 acres of waterfront land on the south shore of Sinclair Inlet at Point Turner, Bremerton. The site was championed by Lieutenant Ambrose Barkley Wyckoff and Washington territorial delegate Watson C. Squire, selected for its deep-water access, protected harbor, and strategic distance from the open Pacific. William Bremer had platted the townsite of Bremerton specifically anticipating the Navy yard. The first drydock was completed in 1896; the yard serviced vessels from the Spanish-American War and the Great White Fleet before employing approximately 6,000 workers during World War I.

World War II: Peak of 32,500 Workers

PSNS’s defining era came during World War II. Employment surged from roughly 4,500 pre-war to a peak of approximately 32,500 civilian workers in 1944–1945 — making it the largest employer in the state of Washington, surpassing even Boeing. The yard expanded from 190 to over 520 acres. While it built approximately 38 new vessels (destroyers, destroyer escorts, escort carriers), its primary wartime mission was repair and overhaul: the yard handled approximately 260 ships per year at peak.

The most ambitious projects were the Pearl Harbor battleships. USS West Virginia (BB-48), sunk at Pearl Harbor, was raised, towed to PSNS, and essentially rebuilt from the waterline up, returning to the fleet in 1944. USS California (BB-44) and USS Tennessee (BB-43) received the same treatment — complete rebuilds and modernizations that returned three shattered battleships to the Pacific war.

Women comprised 20–25% of the wartime workforce — roughly 6,000–8,000 women working as welders, electricians, crane operators, sheet metal workers, and painters. Bremerton’s pre-war population of 15,000 was overwhelmed by the influx; the greater area swelled to 75,000–80,000, producing one of the worst housing crises of any defense community in the country. Federal housing projects (Sinclair Park, Westpark, Eastpark) helped but were insufficient. Workers lived in trailer parks, converted garages, rented rooms, and cars. “Hot-bedding” — shift workers sharing the same bed in rotation — was common.

The Ferry Commuters: 5,000–8,000 Daily

The housing crisis is the primary reason so many workers commuted from across the Sound. An estimated 5,000–8,000 workers per day crossed Puget Sound by ferry to reach PSNS during the war peak. The Black Ball Line coordinated shift-change ferry runs aligned to the yard’s three shifts (7:00 AM, 3:30 PM, midnight). Workers from Edmonds, Mukilteo, Everett, and Marysville used the Edmonds-Kingston crossing — approximately 30 minutes on the water, then 35–45 minutes by road to the shipyard gate. Total one-way commute: roughly 1 hour 15 minutes to 1 hour 30 minutes.

This ferry commuter culture became one of the most distinctive patterns of the Puget Sound region. Multi-generational ferry commuting developed in Edmonds, Meadowdale, Norma Beach, Picnic Point, and Mukilteo — grandfather, father, son, and increasingly mother and daughter working at the yard. The 30-minute crossing became social time: card games, reading, sleeping after swing shift, and a ferry cabin cohort as familiar as the shop floor cohort. This pattern directly paralleled the Boeing commuter culture that emerged from the same communities after 1967.

Nuclear Era and Submarine Recycling

During the Cold War, PSNS became one of only four U.S. naval shipyards capable of nuclear work. It performed its first nuclear submarine overhaul on USS Sargo (SSN-583) beginning in 1963, and became the primary facility for the Ship-Submarine Recycling Program — decommissioning and disposing of reactor compartments. Over 125 nuclear submarines have been recycled at PSNS, more than any other facility in the world. Today the yard has six drydocks (the most of any U.S. naval shipyard), employs approximately 16,000–17,000 civilians, and remains the largest industrial employer in the state of Washington.

The Apprenticeship Pipeline

PSNS has operated one of the oldest and most respected apprenticeship programs in the United States since 1901, when it began with six original apprentices. The four-year program alternates classroom instruction (partnered with Olympic College since 1950) and hands-on dock work, training in 26 trades including shipwright, shipfitter, welder, machinist, rigger, painter, electrician, and toolmaker. Graduates receive journey-level certifications from both the Navy and Department of Labor plus an Associate in Technical Arts degree. The program has graduated 9,617 mechanics since its founding — approximately 200 per year in recent classes. As one program director noted, “Most of the civilian management at the shipyard went through the program.”

The trades skills at PSNS and Boeing are substantially overlapping: welding, sheet metal, electrical, machining, assembly. Workers historically moved between the two employers depending on hiring cycles and layoffs. South Snohomish County was the geographic sweet spot — Edmonds and Mukilteo are roughly equidistant in commute time from PSNS (via Edmonds-Kingston ferry) and Boeing Everett (20 minutes north on I-5). The apprenticeship pipeline fed both. “Dad worked at the shipyard, Uncle Joe worked at Boeing” is a common pattern in local oral histories.

4. Shipwright Heritage — Wood to Steel

The Puget Sound Wooden Boatbuilding Tradition

The Puget Sound region developed a distinctive wooden boatbuilding tradition rooted in the abundance of old-growth timber — Douglas fir for structural strength, Western red cedar for planking, Sitka spruce for spars, Port Orford cedar, and white oak for frames — and the demands of the commercial fishing industry. The “Puget Sound boat,” a broad category encompassing gillnetters, purse seiners, trollers, and workboats, became a recognized type in Pacific Northwest maritime culture. Clinker-built and carvel-planked small craft, typically 16–32 feet. Flat-bottomed or V-bottom salmon skiffs essential for the gillnet fishery. Double-ended hull forms influenced by Scandinavian traditions, reflecting the Norwegian, Swedish, and Finnish immigration to the region in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

By 1910, Scandinavians were the largest ethnic group in Washington, constituting over 20% of the foreign-born population. Norwegians numbered 7,191 in Seattle alone. They settled in Ballard because it reminded them of home — fjords, maritime economy, fishing. Sivert Sagstad learned boatbuilding from his father in Norway, emigrated at 25, and within six months had a yard operating in Ballard building halibut schooners. His employees “were from Norway, Denmark, or Finland, and trained as ship carpenters, fine joiners, hull men, riggers, and machinists.” UNESCO recognized the Nordic clinker boat tradition as Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2021.

The Tools and the Trade

A wood shipwright was a master craftsman who encompassed the entire process: lofting (transferring hull lines from drawings to full-size patterns), selecting and shaping timber, framing the skeleton, planking, caulking seams for watertight integrity, and finishing. The shipwright read the grain of the wood the way a steel worker reads blueprints — intuiting structural loads and knowing where a plank would want to bend.

The toolkit included the adze (both foot adze and hand adze, for gouging and shaping large timbers, including carving the stem), the broadaxe (for rough-shaping frames), caulking mallets and irons, planes, chisels, augers, and drawknives, the half-model and lofting tools for hull design. These were hand tools passed between generations — often literally inherited.

Caulking was a specialized sub-trade. Keeping water on the outside of the “tremendous number of wooden hulls that once crowded Puget Sound was the sole responsibility of an army of caulkers.” The process: cotton wedged deep between planks, then oakum (oiled hemp fiber) folded and hammered into the seam with a caulking iron and mallet, then the seam painted and topped with cement. Caulkers had their own union and worked in gangs, moving from yard to yard as jobs and seasons demanded.

Where They Worked

The major clusters of wooden boatbuilding on Puget Sound included Lake Union, Seattle (between 1919 and 1927, six legendary yards opened: Blanchard, Grandy, Jensen, Lake Union Drydock, Prothero, and Vic Franck’s); Ballard / Salmon Bay (Norwegian builders lined the waterway — Sagstad, Heggem, Strand, Nilson, Johnson, Berg, and the Pacific Fishermen cooperative since 1946); Gig Harbor (the Croatian Skansie family built over 100 purse seiners); Poulsbo (Ronald Young built his distinctive “Poulsbo Boats” from 1933 to 1965); and Port Townsend (the Shipwrights Co-Op and the Northwest School of Wooden Boatbuilding, both established 1981). Naval architect Ed Monk Sr. (1914–1973, over 3,000 designs) epitomized the regional style: red cedar planking over bent-oak frames, functionality over flashiness, deep-V hulls for Puget Sound conditions.

The Prothero Line: Wales Since the 1600s

Training was overwhelmingly through apprenticeship, often family-based. In the British naval tradition, shipwright apprenticeship was “generally only by family connections” — access to the trade was hereditary. The Prothero family exemplifies the full arc: they started building ships in Wales in the late 1600s, their great-grandfather immigrated to America in 1870 and opened a boat shop on Lake Union, and both Frank and Bob Prothero were legendary shipwrights who had learned the trade from their father. Frank built upwards of 250 boats; Bob built approximately 250 more. Together the Protheros represent centuries of unbroken craft transmission from the Welsh shipyards to the waters of Puget Sound.

The Transition: When Steel Became Dominant

The wood-to-steel transition was gradual, but World War II was the decisive inflection point. In 1894, the steel-hulled whaleback SS City of Everett was launched — early steel work on the Sound. By 1918, Seattle shipyards delivered 57 steel ships and 17 wooden ones in WWI; steel was already dominant for large vessels. In the 1920s, Lake Washington Shipyards converted from wood to steel construction. In the 1930s they built the iconic streamlined steel ferry Kalakala. Then WWII made the transition complete — Todd Shipyards on Harbor Island employed 17,000 workers building 45 steel destroyers. Welding replaced riveting as the primary joining method, dramatically speeding production.

The post-war decade saw a final golden era of wooden boatbuilding on Lake Union (late 1940s through the late 1950s), but by the 1960s fiberglass was competing with wood for pleasure boats and steel was universal for commercial and military vessels. The Puget Sound fishing fleet transitioned from wood to steel and aluminum primarily between 1960 and 1990. Aluminum became the preferred material for new gillnetters (lighter, corrosion-resistant in saltwater, lower maintenance); steel was used for larger seiners and combination vessels.

What Carried Over

The fundamental understanding of hull form, structural loading, watertight integrity, and craftsmanship transferred directly from wood to steel. A man who grew up watching his father fair a wooden plank to a curve understood — at a bone-deep level — what it meant to fair a steel plate. The eye for form, the understanding of how water moves against a hull, the instinct for structural integrity: these are material-independent. Welding replaced caulking as the watertight sealing method. Steel plate replaced planking. Plasma torches and welding rigs replaced adzes and caulking mallets. But the core knowledge — how loads distribute through a vessel, how to read a set of lines, the concept of the ship as a system — carried directly over.

The pattern of grandfather working wood and father or son working steel was widespread across the maritime communities of Puget Sound because of precise historical timing: the wooden boat economy thrived from the 1880s through the 1930s while WWII created massive demand for steel ship workers from the 1940s forward. The PSNS apprenticeship was the bridge — it took young men and women with maritime intuition and trained them in steel trades. The Grandy family exemplifies the sequence: Lewis Lee Grandy opened a boatyard in 1903, sons Earl and Bill continued in the 1920s, and the “legions of union shipwrights” who “served their apprenticeships with the Grandys” carried that knowledge into the broader workforce, including into the steel yards.

1981: The Year of Preservation

The founding of the Northwest School of Wooden Boatbuilding (1981), the Center for Wooden Boats (1976), and the Port Townsend Shipwrights Co-Op (1981) all happened in the same narrow window — the moment when the last generation of trained wooden boatbuilders was aging out and the knowledge faced extinction. Bob Prothero co-founded the school specifically because “boatbuilding skills that could not be found in textbooks” were about to die with his generation. There was pride in the steel work — skilled, well-paid, essential to the nation’s defense. But there was also a profound sense of loss that something irreplaceable was disappearing. The wooden boat revival movement represents families and communities trying to hold onto that thread. The Port Townsend Shipwrights Co-Op today supports 350 wooden boat workers; the Northwest School has graduated over 1,800 students.

5. Honeywell & the Ground Proximity Warning System

Don Bateman and “PULL UP”

The most compelling aerospace innovation story connecting the Puget Sound corridor to global aviation safety is the Ground Proximity Warning System (GPWS), invented by Don Bateman at Sundstrand Data Control in Redmond, Washington. Sundstrand developed flight data recorders and then created the system that warns pilots “PULL UP, PULL UP” and “TERRAIN, TERRAIN” when an aircraft is on a collision course with the ground. GPWS is credited with virtually eliminating Controlled Flight Into Terrain (CFIT) accidents — historically one of the leading causes of fatal airline crashes. The FAA mandated GPWS for all turbine-powered commercial aircraft in 1974.

The corporate lineage that brought this technology into the south Snohomish County aerospace ecosystem traces through one of the most complex merger chains in industrial history: Sundstrand Data Control (Redmond, WA) was acquired by AlliedSignal in 1993; AlliedSignal merged with Honeywell in 1999, taking the Honeywell name. The Redmond site also traces heritage through the Sperry Flight Systems and Bendix Corporation lineages. Today Honeywell Aerospace operates from the Redmond facility (and a second Washington site near Camas/Vancouver), producing flight management systems, cockpit displays, the Primus Epic avionics suite, IntuVue weather radar, and the Enhanced GPWS (EGPWS).

Korry Electronics — Everett

Korry Electronics, based in Everett, makes cockpit switches, indicators, and display panels supplied to Boeing and military platforms. Acquired by Esterline Technologies, then by the TransDigm Group, Korry represents the smaller but critical tier of aerospace suppliers that cluster around Paine Field — machine shops, avionics repair stations, composite fabricators, and surface treatment facilities that form the dense supply chain serving the world’s largest aircraft factory.

Workforce Character

The corridor creates a specific socioeconomic character for the Edmonds-to-Everett shoreline. The bluff-top neighborhoods are populated by a mix of Boeing engineers and machinists, Navy civilian employees, defense contractor employees, and retirees from all three sectors — a predominantly technical workforce (engineers, technicians, skilled trades) that gives these communities relatively high household incomes and a distinctive professional identity. Transportation patterns are defined by commuter flows: south on I-5/I-405 to Seattle/Bellevue tech jobs, north to Boeing/Everett, and west via ferry to Whidbey Island. SR 525 (Mukilteo Speedway / Boeing Freeway) is the primary arterial connecting the ferry terminal, Boeing, and I-5.

Tensions and Vulnerabilities

The concentration of military and commercial aviation creates layered noise impacts: Growler transits from NAS Whidbey, Boeing test and delivery flights from Paine Field, and commercial airline operations. Communities like Mukilteo and Picnic Point simultaneously depend on and resist the aerospace presence. And the corridor is heavily dependent on a small number of very large employers — Boeing production slowdowns (787 issues, 737 MAX crisis, the pandemic), changes to NAS Whidbey operations, and Navy force structure decisions all send significant ripple effects through these communities.

The Trades Thread

The heritage story running through this corridor connects pre-industrial maritime culture to modern industrial employment. A resident might have a grandfather who built wooden fishing boats at a Ballard shipyard, a father who welded steel at PSNS during WWII, and a son who machined jet engine parts at Boeing. The materials and employers change but the fundamental skills remain constant — working with your hands, precision metalwork and woodwork, understanding how things are built. “Shipyard family” and “Boeing family” are identity markers in south Snohomish County, and many families span both.
