Module L: Community Life & Culture

The Possession Sound corridor’s identity is not defined by city boundaries but by a continuous shoreline experience — from the Edmonds ferry terminal north through Meadowdale, Picnic Point, and into Mukilteo. Two school districts, seven high schools, and a year-round calendar of community festivals weave a cultural fabric that spans the seven miles between the Kingston ferry and the Mukilteo lighthouse.

1. Festivals & Community Events

Taste of Edmonds

Founded in 1987 by the Edmonds Chamber of Commerce, the Taste of Edmonds is the signature food-and-community festival of the corridor’s southern anchor. Held over three days in mid-August at the Civic Center Playfield (6th Avenue and Bell Street), the event draws an estimated 15,000–25,000 visitors across the weekend. Entry to the festival grounds is free — an accessibility decision that has driven high foot traffic since the event’s founding.

The format has evolved from a straightforward restaurant-tasting event in the late 1980s into a full weekend festival with a main concert stage, craft beer and wine garden, 50–100+ craft vendors, and a dedicated kids’ zone. Fifteen to twenty-five local restaurants serve small-plate tastings, showcasing Edmonds’ dining corridor along 5th Avenue and Main Street. The event is widely considered the single largest annual economic event for the downtown business district, functioning as a gateway tourism event that introduces visitors to Edmonds who return throughout the year.

Edmonds Arts Festival

Running since approximately 1957, the Edmonds Arts Festival is one of the oldest juried art festivals in Washington State. Held at the Frances Anderson Center in mid-June, it features fine art, crafts, student art, and community art activities. The festival predates most other Edmonds community events and helped establish the city’s identity as an arts community — a reputation later reinforced by the Edmonds Center for the Arts, housed in the former 1930s-era Edmonds High School building.

MukFest / Mukilteo Lighthouse Festival

MukFest bookends the late summer on the Possession Sound coastline, held on Labor Day weekend (early September) directly on the Mukilteo waterfront at the lighthouse grounds. Where the Taste of Edmonds leans into Edmonds’ identity as a dining and arts destination, MukFest reflects Mukilteo’s character as a waterfront heritage community, anchored by its 1906 Mukilteo Light Station and featuring what is reputedly the largest fireworks display in Snohomish County. Together, these two festivals define the cultural bookends of the corridor — a resident of Meadowdale or Picnic Point might attend both.

Year-Round Community Calendar

Edmonds deliberately cultivates a year-round event calendar that keeps its walkable downtown vibrant: the Farmers Market (Saturdays, May–October), the 4th of July celebration with fireworks over the water from Brackett’s Landing, the Classic Car Show (September), monthly art walks, and a December tree-lighting ceremony. This calendar is not accidental — the Chamber of Commerce and city maintain a rhythm of community gathering that reinforces Edmonds’ identity as a small town with genuine cultural density.

2. Edmonds Underwater Park

Established in 1970, the Edmonds Underwater Park (formally the Bruce Underwater Park, honoring local diver Bruce Higgins) is considered the first marine sanctuary and underwater park on the U.S. Pacific Coast. Located at Brackett’s Landing immediately south of the ferry terminal, the park covers approximately 27 acres of subtidal habitat as a designated marine preserve.

The park is a major draw for scuba divers and marine education programs, and its establishment predates the broader marine sanctuary movement in the United States by several years. Its location at the symbolic heart of Edmonds’ waterfront — adjacent to the Kingston ferry terminal and within walking distance of the historic downtown — ties marine conservation directly into the community’s daily life. The underwater park is one of the features that distinguishes Edmonds from typical suburban Puget Sound cities: a genuine underwater wilderness within sight of the town’s main street.

3. Schools of the Corridor

Two school districts divide the Possession Sound corridor, and their boundaries, histories, and alumni networks map directly onto the shoreline communities this heritage study documents.

Edmonds School District No. 15 (est. 1884)

Founded in a feed barn owned by settler George Brackett in downtown Edmonds — predating Washington statehood by five years. The district serves approximately 21,700 students across Edmonds, Lynnwood, Mountlake Terrace, Woodway, and Brier. Its five high schools span a remarkable demographic range, from Woodway (one of the wealthiest communities per capita in Washington, population 1,318, no commercial zoning, 2-acre minimum lots) to the diverse Highway 99 corridor.

  • Edmonds-Woodway HS (EWHS) — formed in 1990 by merging Edmonds HS (est. 1909, the first high school in south Snohomish County) and Woodway HS (est. 1970). Only IB school in the district. Enrollment: 1,578.
  • Mountlake Terrace HS (MTHS) — opened 1960. Nationally recognized jazz program. Enrollment: 1,385.
  • Meadowdale HS — opened 1963. Traditional rival of EWHS. Enrollment: 1,535.
  • Lynnwood HS — est. 1970; current award-winning building opened 2009. Enrollment: 1,375.
  • Scriber Lake HS — alternative program.

Mukilteo School District No. 6 (est. 1878)

The sixth-oldest school district in Washington. Serves approximately 15,270 students across Mukilteo, portions of south Everett, and — critically for this study — Picnic Point. Had no high school until 1970, when Mariner opened to handle the Boeing population boom; students previously attended Everett HS or Cascade HS.

  • Kamiak HS — opened 1993. Named for Chief Kamiakin of the Yakama. First and only high school within Mukilteo city limits. Enrollment: 2,382 (largest in the corridor).
  • Mariner HS — opened 1970. Named for the Mariner neighborhood of south Everett. Enrollment: 2,100.

Everett School District

  • Cascade HS — opened 1961 at 801 East Casino Road. Serves south Everett and the diverse Casino Road corridor. Enrollment: 1,651.

5. Jazz Corridor

The Edmonds School District contains one of the most concentrated pockets of competitive high school jazz in the United States. Two adjacent suburban high schools — Edmonds-Woodway and Mountlake Terrace — have combined for 14 appearances at Essentially Ellington, the premier high school jazz competition held at Jazz at Lincoln Center in New York City.

SchoolEssentially Ellington AppearancesYearsOther Honors
MTHS 9 2000, 2002, 2005, 2008, 2011, 2012, 2017, 2018, 2020 3rd place (2005, 2011); Dynamics vocal jazz: 3x Lionel Hampton winner
EWHS 5 2003, 2007, 2010, 2013, 2017 Montreux Jazz Festival; North Sea Jazz Festival; 2018 Lionel Hampton Sweepstakes; 2019 Mello-Aires: DownBeat Award

This concentration is unusual nationally. Essentially Ellington selects only 15 bands per year from across the country, and having two schools from the same suburban district repeatedly appear — sometimes in the same year (2017) — speaks to a jazz culture that runs deeper than individual program leadership. EWHS ensembles have performed at the Montreux Jazz Festival and North Sea Jazz Festival; MTHS has toured Europe. The schools share the Edmonds School District Stadium and the district’s only full theater (at MTHS), creating cross-pollination between programs. The EWHS Mello-Aires vocal jazz ensemble won DownBeat Magazine’s Student Music Award for Best Large Vocal Jazz Ensemble in 2019.

6. The Boeing Boom’s Schools

The Boeing 747 program, announced in 1966, transformed south Snohomish County from semi-rural communities into a dense suburban corridor almost overnight. The Everett factory — the world’s largest building by volume — was constructed on 780 acres adjacent to Paine Field between 1967 and 1969, employing approximately 30,000 workers at peak. The population boom it triggered directly created or transformed the corridor’s schools:

  • Cascade HS (1961) — opened five years before Boeing broke ground, but was positioned to absorb the earliest wave. Built for $3 million; Everett annexed its location on February 6, 1962.
  • Meadowdale HS (1963) — already in place when the boom hit. Enrollment surged as Boeing workers’ families filled the Martha Lake, Meadowdale, and Larch Way neighborhoods.
  • Mariner HS (1970) — opened specifically because of the Boeing boom. The Mukilteo School District needed a second comprehensive high school to handle the population explosion in south Everett. Named for the Mariner neighborhood, itself named for the maritime and aerospace culture of the area.
  • Woodway HS (1970) — opened the same year as Mariner to handle growth on the Edmonds side. Merged with Edmonds HS in 1990 to form Edmonds-Woodway.
  • Lynnwood HS (1970) — also opened in the boom year. Its original campus near Alderwood Mall was eventually demolished; the current award-winning building opened in 2009.
  • Kamiak HS (1993) — the second wave. Opened to relieve overcrowding at Mariner, funded by $66.7M and $89.5M levies, built for $32M.

The “Boeing Bust” of 1970–71 (“Will the last person leaving Seattle please turn out the lights?”) hit south Snohomish County less severely than Seattle proper, because the Everett plant retained more 747 production workers than the Renton plant. But the schools born of the boom carried the aerospace imprint for decades: strong shop and technical programs feeding directly into Boeing apprenticeships, alumni rolls dominated by engineers and machinists, and a community identity forged in the shadow of the world’s largest building.

The Casino Road corridor surrounding Cascade HS has since undergone a demographic transformation from predominantly white working-class Boeing families to one of the most diverse communities in Snohomish County — 59% minority enrollment, 60% economically disadvantaged, with significant populations from Mexico, Central America, Africa, and Russia. One in four residents is foreign-born. Schools, churches, and community organizations now form a tight mutual-support network in a neighborhood experiencing rising rents faster than incomes.
