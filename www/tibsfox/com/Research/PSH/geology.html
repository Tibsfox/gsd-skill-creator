Module D: Land History & Geology

The coastal bluffs between Edmonds and Mukilteo are a living stratigraphic record — 80,000+ years of glacial advance, interglacial forest, and post-glacial erosion exposed in slide scarps that reshape the shoreline every winter. This module documents the geological framework, historic landslide events, and the human relationship with these unstable slopes.

1. Glacial Stratigraphy — The Vashon Sequence

The coastal bluffs along the Edmonds-to-Mukilteo corridor are composed of glacial and interglacial sediments deposited during multiple Pleistocene ice advances. The bluffs range from approximately 100 to 250 feet in height. The contact zone between permeable outwash gravels and underlying impermeable till or clay layers creates perched water tables and seepage zones — the primary driver of slope instability.

UnitAgeCharacter
Post-glacial deposits~13,000 BP – presentLandslide debris, alluvium, peat
Vashon Recessional Outwash~13,000 – 15,000 BPSands and gravels deposited as ice retreated
Vashon Till~17,000 – 13,000 BPDense glacial till from the Fraser Glaciation; SPT N-value >50
Olympia Interglacial beds~15,000 – 60,000 BPNonglacial sediments between ice advances
Possession DriftPossession GlaciationTill and outwash from the penultimate ice advance
Whidbey Formation80,000+ years BPInterglacial sands and silts; contains fossil wood beyond C-14 range

The bluffs face generally west-southwest, receiving direct wave action from Puget Sound, which undercuts the toe and removes slide debris that would otherwise buttress the slope. This dynamic — groundwater seepage from above and wave erosion from below — makes this one of the most active landslide corridors on Puget Sound.

WA DNR Division of Geology and Earth Resources; USGS Professional Paper 1693

2. Historic Landslide Events

The 1996–97 Winter Slides — Catastrophic Season

The winter of 1996–97 was catastrophic across the entire Puget Lowland. A prolonged wet autumn saturated slopes, followed by intense storm events between December 29, 1996 and January 2, 1997 — a warm atmospheric river dumped heavy rain on already-saturated ground.

Impacts Along the Edmonds–Mukilteo Corridor

  • BNSF railroad tracks buried or undermined at multiple locations; service disrupted for days to weeks
  • Several homes in the Meadowdale Beach area and north toward Norma Beach lost yard setback; at least two homes condemned
  • Dozens of individual slide scars coalesced into larger failure zones along the shoreline
  • Fresh sections of Whidbey Formation exposed; fossil wood (interglacial stumps and logs) observed in debris fans at the bluff base
  • Individual slides ranged from small slumps (hundreds of cubic yards) to large rotational failures (tens of thousands of cubic yards)

Seattle Times, January 1997; USGS Open-File Report 98-239; WA DNR Emergency Assessments, 1997

December 2006 — Hanukkah Eve Storm

The December 14–15, 2006 windstorm brought hurricane-force gusts and heavy rain. While primarily a wind event, the associated rainfall triggered slides on already-saturated slopes. Along the Picnic Point to Norma Beach corridor, slides blocked the BNSF tracks. Combined with extreme high tides and wave action, significant toe erosion destabilized slopes that continued to fail into early 2007.

National Weather Service post-storm survey; Snohomish County DPW emergency assessments

2014–15 Slides Near Mukilteo/Meadowdale

  • March 2014: A significant slide in the Meadowdale Beach Park area temporarily blocked the pedestrian tunnel beneath the BNSF tracks. The failure scarp extended approximately 150 feet upslope.
  • Early 2015: Winter slides south of Picnic Point deposited an estimated 2,000–3,000 cubic yards of debris across both BNSF mainline tracks.
  • At least one bluff-top home near 76th Avenue W was red-tagged — progressive bluff retreat brought the scarp edge to within 15 feet of the foundation.

Everett Herald, March 2014 & winter 2015; Sound Transit corridor studies

3. BNSF Railroad — The Defining Infrastructure

The BNSF mainline (originally Great Northern Railway, completed through this corridor in 1891) runs along the base of the bluffs for the entire Picnic Point-to-Norma Beach corridor. This is one of the most slide-prone stretches of the entire BNSF northern transcontinental route.

Chronic Disruption Pattern

  • BNSF maintains a dedicated geotechnical monitoring program for this corridor
  • Track closures due to slides have occurred in virtually every wet winter since records began
  • Minor events: 12–72 hour closures. Major slides: week-long disruptions
  • Sound Transit cites the Edmonds–Mukilteo segment as one of the most vulnerable on the Sounder North line
  • Amtrak Cascades cancellations and bus bridges are common during slide events

The railroad’s 1890–91 construction required blasting and grading a shelf along the bluff base, removing the natural toe of the slope. This removal of the stabilizing toe set the stage for increased landslide activity that continues to the present day.

Sound Transit Long-Range Plan; BNSF public communications; Amtrak Cascades service alerts

4. Norma Beach — A Place Between Bluff and Rail

Norma Beach is a historical place name for a small residential community and shoreline located between Meadowdale Beach (south) and Picnic Point (north), centered approximately at the mouth of Norma Creek at 47.855°N, 122.336°W.

Identity & Status

  • Not an incorporated municipality; does not appear on most USGS topographic maps
  • Listed in the GNIS database as a populated place in Snohomish County
  • Appears in Snohomish County property records and on historical Metsker maps (mid-20th century)
  • Named in the early 20th century; exact origin of “Norma” not definitively documented

Like many Puget Sound beach communities, Norma Beach began as a summer cottage and recreation area, gradually transitioning to year-round residences. The railroad — Great Northern, then Burlington Northern, now BNSF — has been the defining infrastructure feature since 1891, separating the beach from the bluff. Today, the beach is a narrow gravel/cobble strand backed by the railroad embankment and riprap, with no dedicated public access point.

GNIS database; Snohomish County Assessor; Metsker Maps (Everett Public Library)

5. Horseback Riding Along the Shoreline

The history of equestrian use along this corridor traces the arc from working landscape to restricted suburban coast.

Pre-1891 — Before the Railroad

The beach was a primary transportation route along the coast. Bluffs were too steep for roads, so travel was by water or along the beach at low tide. Early Euro-American settlers (1860s onward) used horses along the beach for local travel, though most heavy transport went by water.

1891–1950 — Railroad Era

After Great Northern Railway completion, the beach corridor was divided by tracks but not yet fenced. Horseback riding continued at low tide alongside the tracks. The Meadowdale area and surrounding land remained largely agricultural through the 1940s, with small horse-keeping operations on rural properties.

1950s–1970s — The Remembered Era

This is the period most referenced in local oral histories. The area was still rural enough to support horse-keeping on the bluff tops. Riders descended to the beach via informal trails through drainage ravines, sometimes covering the full distance from Edmonds to Mukilteo. Small riding stables and boarding operations existed in the Meadowdale area. At-grade railroad crossings provided passage before Burlington Northern began restricting access.

1970s–Present — Decline

Suburban development eliminated most horse-keeping. BNSF progressively restricted right-of-way access, and formalized pedestrian overpasses replaced informal at-grade crossings. Beach riding essentially ceased as a regular practice by the 1980s.

Local oral histories collected by the Edmonds Historical Museum and Mukilteo Historical Society include references to riding horses down to the beach via informal trails, beach riding at low tide covering the distance from Edmonds to Mukilteo, and riders sharing the beach with the occasional train.

— Edmonds Historical Museum & Mukilteo Historical Society collections

HistoryLink.org essays #7809, #7569; Edmonds Historical Museum; Mukilteo Historical Society

6. Development & Slope Instability

The relationship between bluff-top development and landslide activation is well-documented and politically contentious in Snohomish County.

FactorMechanism
Septic systemsInject water into upper soil layers, increasing loading on slopes at or near saturation thresholds
Stormwater runoffRoof and impervious surface runoff directed toward bluff edge increases infiltration into slide-prone stratigraphy
Vegetation removalClearing mature evergreen forest removes root reinforcement and eliminates evapotranspiration that would otherwise remove soil water
Road construction76th Avenue W and bluff-top roads altered natural drainage patterns

In response, Snohomish County designated much of this bluff face as a “geologically hazardous area” under its Critical Areas Regulations, requiring setbacks, geotechnical studies, and drainage controls for any new development.

Bluff-Top Development History

1950s–1960s

First wave of suburban homes along 76th Avenue W. Mid-century ramblers and split-levels.

1970s–1980s

Continued infill. Larger homes as the area became more desirable. Meadowdale Beach County Park established.

1990s–Present

Redevelopment with larger construction. Increasing awareness of landslide hazard leads to stricter building setbacks and geotechnical requirements.

WA DOE Puget Sound Partnership; Snohomish County SMP & Critical Areas Ordinance; Tubbs, 1974 (WA DNR IC 52)

7. Exposed Geology — What the Slides Reveal

Major landslides periodically expose materials that would otherwise remain buried deep within the bluffs, providing windows into the geological history of the Puget Lowland.

Fossil Wood

Interglacial stumps and logs from the Whidbey Formation, over 80,000 years old. Some specimens yield “infinite” radiocarbon ages beyond C-14 range, confirming pre-Vashon deposition.

Glacial Erratics

Boulders of granitic and metamorphic rock transported by the Vashon ice sheet, exposed in till when slide scarps cut through Vashon Drift.

Peat Layers

Compressed peat from interglacial wetlands, sometimes containing plant macrofossils that record ancient ecosystems.

Marine Clay

Lawton Clay-equivalent deposits representing marine incursions during interglacial periods when sea levels were higher.

The bluffs in the Norma Beach-to-Picnic Point corridor provide some of the best exposed cross-sections of Pleistocene stratigraphy in the Puget Lowland. Geology students from the University of Washington and other institutions have used these bluffs as field study sites. The contact between the gray, dense Vashon Till and the lighter Olympia Interglacial beds is visible in many fresh slide scars.

8. Norma Beach to Picnic Point — The Corridor

This approximately 1.5-mile stretch of coastline runs from Norma Beach (~47.855°N) northwestward to Picnic Point (~47.867°N). It encapsulates the tension between natural process and human habitation that defines the entire Puget Sound bluff coastline: the bluffs want to erode; the homeowners want them to stay put; the railroad needs them to stay put; the fish need the sediment that erosion provides.

Picnic Point County Park

A spit extending into Puget Sound, formed by glacial deposits and longshore drift. The point creates a sheltered embayment that accumulates fine sediments supporting eelgrass beds, important for juvenile salmon and forage fish (surf smelt, sand lance) spawning. One of the few public beach access points in this corridor, with views of Whidbey Island, Hat Island, and the Olympic Mountains.

Forage Fish Spawning Habitat

The beach substrate between Norma Beach and Picnic Point has been documented as surf smelt and sand lance spawning habitat by WDFW. These forage fish deposit eggs in upper intertidal gravel, making them sensitive to shoreline armoring and changes in sediment supply.

WA DNR & USGS Mapping

  • WA DNR: The Washington Geologic Information Portal maps virtually the entire bluff face as “active landslide,” “historically active landslide,” or “landslide-prone” terrain
  • USGS: Bluff retreat rates of 1–3 feet per year are typical, with episodic large failures accelerating the average
  • LiDAR: High-resolution bare-earth imagery from the Puget Sound LiDAR Consortium reveals the entire bluff face as a palimpsest of overlapping slide features — ancient and recent scarps superimposed

Trail Proposals

Various proposals over the decades have envisioned a continuous walking/biking path from Mukilteo to Edmonds, but the combination of railroad right-of-way, unstable bluffs, and private property has prevented realization. Sound Transit corridor studies have assessed slide risk to future infrastructure investments along this stretch.

WDFW Forage Fish Spawning Database; WA DNR Geologic Information Portal; Puget Sound LiDAR Consortium

9. Geological & Human Timeline

80,000+ years BP

Whidbey Formation deposited — interglacial forests that would become the fossil wood found in modern slide debris.

~17,000–13,000 BP

Vashon glaciation — Fraser ice sheet advances and retreats, depositing the till, outwash, and glaciolacustrine sequence that forms today’s bluffs.

~13,000 BP – Contact

Coast Salish peoples (Snohomish, Snoqualmie, and allied groups) use the shoreline for travel, shellfish gathering, and fishing. No permanent villages in this stretch, but actively used territory.

1860s

First Euro-American settlers arrive in the Edmonds and Mukilteo areas.

1890–91

Great Northern Railway constructed through the corridor. Blasting and grading remove the natural bluff toe, initiating accelerated slope instability.

1890–1920

Bluff-top forests logged for timber. Meadowdale, Norma Beach, and Picnic Point emerge as small communities.

1950s–1970s

Post-war suburbanization. Horseback riding on the beach is still common. 76th Avenue W paved. Meadowdale Beach County Park established.

1970

Burlington Northern formed by merger. Railroad begins restricting at-grade access to the tracks.

Winter 1996–97

Catastrophic landslide season. Multiple failures along the corridor bury BNSF tracks, condemn homes, and expose Whidbey Formation fossil wood.

December 2006

Hanukkah Eve Storm. Slides block BNSF tracks; extreme tides cause significant toe erosion.

March 2014

Meadowdale Beach Park slide blocks pedestrian tunnel. Failure scarp extends 150 feet upslope.

Early 2015

Winter slides near Picnic Point deposit 2,000–3,000 cubic yards across both BNSF mainline tracks.
