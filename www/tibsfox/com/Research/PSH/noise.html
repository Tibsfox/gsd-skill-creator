Module J: The Sound Landscape

The Possession Sound corridor exists within overlapping noise envelopes from military aviation, commercial aviation, Boeing flight test operations, heavy rail, and rotary-wing aircraft. No single agency evaluates the cumulative burden. This module documents the acoustic geography of a region where competing legitimate interests — military readiness, economic identity, historic preservation, and community health — remain unresolved.

1. OLF Coupeville & the Growler

Outlying Landing Field Coupeville was built in 1943 during World War II as a satellite field for NAS Whidbey Island (Ault Field, commissioned September 21, 1942). The Navy needed a dedicated outlying field because Ault Field was increasingly congested with operational squadrons — carrier landing practice requires repetitive circuits that tie up a runway for extended periods. OLF sits on the central prairie of Whidbey Island near the town of Coupeville, the Island County seat, on flat, open agricultural terrain topographically similar to a carrier deck approach: flat, unobstructed, with water on approach paths.

The field is intentionally austere: a single runway of approximately 6,000 feet (oriented roughly 14/32), a small operations building, arresting gear for simulated carrier arrested landings, runway lighting for night operations, and a Fresnel Lens Optical Landing System (FLOLS) — the same “meatball” visual landing aid used on aircraft carriers. No hangars, no permanent squadron facilities. Its sole purpose is to replicate carrier deck conditions for Field Carrier Landing Practice (FCLP).

Field Carrier Landing Practice

FCLP is mandatory shore-based training that every Navy carrier pilot must complete before being qualified to land on an aircraft carrier. Aircraft fly a racetrack-shaped pattern at approximately 800–1,200 feet AGL, descending on a 3.5-degree glide slope at 130–140 knots in full carrier landing configuration — gear down, flaps down, hook down. Upon touchdown, the pilot immediately applies full military power and climbs away, simulating a missed wire on the carrier deck. A single FCLP session involves 6–10+ successive touch-and-goes with a circuit interval of approximately 60–90 seconds. Multiple aircraft may be in the pattern simultaneously.

The 2018 Environmental Impact Statement documented plans for approximately 35,100 to 36,400 operations per year at OLF Coupeville under the preferred alternative (an “operation” equals one takeoff or one landing; a touch-and-go counts as two operations). Peak days can see 100+ operations. Night FCLP — the most controversial — can run until 10:30–11:00 PM, generating the most community complaints because ambient noise is lowest at night and sleep disruption is severe.

The Growler Inflection

The community coexisted — uncomfortably but functionally — with Navy operations for decades. The transition from the EA-6B Prowler to the EA-18G Growler beginning around 2008–2009 fundamentally changed the equation. NAS Whidbey Island is the sole home of all Navy EA-18G Growler squadrons (approximately 82+ aircraft as of the 2018 EIS, with plans to grow to approximately 118). The Growler is approximately 10–15 dB louder than the Prowler it replaced — and because decibels are logarithmic, this represents roughly a 3–4x increase in perceived loudness. It was not a gradual increase but a step-function jump in noise that broke the prior equilibrium.

For context: sustained exposure above 85 dB causes hearing damage; a normal conversation is approximately 60 dB. A Growler at full power on approach is roughly equivalent to standing next to a rock concert speaker stack. Community health reports include hearing damage (temporary and permanent threshold shifts), chronic sleep deprivation from night FCLP, cardiovascular stress, PTSD-like symptoms (hypervigilance, startle responses, anxiety), and children’s developmental impacts including reading delays and concentration problems. The WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines (2018) identify aircraft noise as a significant public health concern above 45 dB Lden for long-term health effects.

Coupeville schools have documented classroom interruptions where teachers must stop teaching during passes because students cannot hear instruction — pauses of 15–30+ seconds per pass, with passes occurring every 60–90 seconds during FCLP blocks. This can mean half of instructional time is lost during active FCLP periods.

2. Sound Over Water

Sound travels farther and with less attenuation over water than over land. Over land, terrain features, vegetation, and ground absorption reduce sound levels with distance. Over water, the surface is smooth and reflective — sound waves bounce off the water surface rather than being absorbed. At sea level over calm water, sound can travel with 6–10 dB less attenuation compared to equivalent distance over land with vegetation and terrain. This means Growler noise from OLF Coupeville carries efficiently across Admiralty Inlet, Saratoga Passage, and Penn Cove to affect communities far from the field.

Weather Amplification

Temperature inversions are common in the Puget Sound region, especially in fall and winter. When warm air overlies cool air (surface-based inversion), sound waves that would normally radiate upward are refracted back down to the surface, creating a “sound duct” that can carry noise 2–5x farther than normal. This is why some days the Growlers sound dramatically louder in distant communities. Fog and low clouds similarly trap sound energy near the surface. The persistent marine layer in the Puget Sound region frequently creates conditions for enhanced sound propagation.

Downwind propagation significantly increases noise levels at distant receptors. Prevailing winds in the region (often from the south/southwest) push sound from OLF toward communities to the north and northeast. Communities on the eastern shore of Possession Sound — including Mukilteo, Edmonds, Picnic Point, and Meadowdale — report hearing Growler noise from operations at both Ault Field and OLF Coupeville. Sound from Ault Field operations radiates across Possession Sound and Saratoga Passage. Residents in these communities describe hearing Growlers as a low rumble or roar that penetrates structures, distinct from the higher-frequency whine of commercial aircraft at Paine Field.

The key acoustic difference: commercial aircraft noise is relatively steady-state and predictable. Growler FCLP noise is impulsive, repetitive, and at much higher peak levels — the pattern of repeated high-energy passes every 60–90 seconds for hours is qualitatively different from a commercial aircraft passing overhead once.

3. Ebey’s Landing Conflict

OLF Coupeville sits within the boundaries of Ebey’s Landing National Historical Reserve — one of the most striking land-use contradictions in the National Park System. Ebey’s Landing NHR was established by Congress in 1978 (Public Law 95-625) to preserve the historic agricultural landscape and cultural heritage of central Whidbey Island, specifically the unbroken pattern of land use from the 1850s Donation Land Claims through the present. The Reserve covers approximately 17,572 acres (over 27 square miles) of central Whidbey Island.

Unlike a traditional national park, the Reserve is a living landscape that includes private land, the town of Coupeville (one of the oldest towns in Washington), state parks, county land, and federal land — including OLF Coupeville. The NPS manages the Reserve in partnership with the Trust Board of Ebey’s Landing and Island County. It protects historic farmsteads (some dating to the 1850s), the agricultural prairie, bluffs, forests, and shoreline along Admiralty Inlet and Penn Cove.

The Prior Claim Paradox

OLF Coupeville predates the Reserve designation (1943 vs. 1978), so the Navy has a prior claim. When Congress created the Reserve, OLF was already present, and the legislation did not exclude it. However, the character of operations has changed dramatically. In 1978, FCLP was conducted by A-6 Intruders and EA-6B Prowlers — loud, but not at Growler levels. The Growler transition represented a qualitative change that the 1978 Congress could not have anticipated. The NPS’s mission to preserve the Reserve’s historic character is directly undermined by jet noise that makes the agricultural landscape — the very thing the Reserve exists to protect — unpleasant or unhealthy to inhabit.

Legal Challenge and Court Ruling

The Sound Defense Alliance filed suit challenging the adequacy of the 2018 EIS under NEPA. In 2020, Judge Richard Jones (U.S. District Court, Western District of Washington) ruled that the Navy’s EIS was inadequate — specifically that the Navy failed to adequately analyze impacts on children’s learning and on Ebey’s Landing National Historical Reserve. The court did not order a halt to operations but required a supplemental EIS to address these deficiencies. The National Trust for Historic Preservation has at times listed Ebey’s Landing as one of America’s most endangered historic places, citing military noise.

Critics of the original EIS argued it underestimated noise impacts by using Day-Night Average Sound Level (DNL) metrics which average noise over 24 hours — a metric that obscures the impact of intermittent, extremely loud events like FCLP passes. The EIS used modeling rather than extensive real-world measurement, which community groups challenged. The Navy acknowledged that noise impacts would be “significant” for communities near both Ault Field and OLF Coupeville, documenting that approximately 75,000+ people in the region experience significant noise exposure (DNL 65+ dB).

The Sound Defense Alliance and Citizens of Ebey’s Reserve (COER) continue to advocate for meaningful noise reduction, honest measurement, real-time noise monitoring, limits on night operations, and exploration of alternative FCLP sites.

4. Paine Field Cumulative Noise

The critical insight that individual noise source analysis misses: Mukilteo and Picnic Point experience a cumulative noise burden from multiple simultaneous and overlapping sources. Within a single hour, a resident can experience a Boeing 777X test flight departure, a BNSF coal train with horn blasts, a Chinook helicopter transit, and a Growler flyover. Each individually might be “within standards” — together they create a relentless noise environment.

Boeing Test Flights

Boeing has been the dominant presence at Paine Field since the 747 factory opened in 1967. Boeing conducts 5–20+ test and delivery flights per week depending on production rates. Test flights involve full-power takeoffs, steep climbs, and pattern work that generates significantly more noise than routine airline operations. The 777X flight test program (first flight January 25, 2020; certification ongoing) has been a significant noise source — the GE9X engine, among the largest commercial turbofans ever built, produces substantial low-frequency noise during takeoff thrust. Active programs include the 787 Dreamliner, 767 freighter variants, KC-46A Pegasus military tanker, and the 777X. Flight test operations are not bound by the voluntary noise restrictions that apply to commercial carriers.

CH-47 Chinook Helicopters

Boeing Defense, Space & Security uses Paine Field as a flight test and delivery hub for CH-47F and the newer CH-47F Block II Chinooks. Washington Army National Guard and active duty Chinooks from Joint Base Lewis-McChord also transit through the corridor on training routes that pass over Mukilteo and Picnic Point. The CH-47’s tandem-rotor design produces a characteristic low-frequency beating (typically 15–40 Hz fundamental) caused by overlapping rotor disc interference patterns. This low-frequency sound propagates much farther than higher-frequency noise and penetrates home insulation in ways that standard noise barriers cannot address. Residents report windows rattling, pets becoming agitated, and the sensation of feeling the helicopter before hearing it. Standard A-weighted noise measurements (dBA) undercount low-frequency impacts, meaning Chinooks may register as “quieter” on paper than they feel subjectively.

Commercial Operations (Since 2019)

Paine Field opened a commercial passenger terminal on March 4, 2019, after a decades-long political battle. The terminal operates with a cap of approximately 24 daily commercial flights (12 departures, 12 arrivals) by Alaska Airlines and United Airlines. Departures to the south overfly Mukilteo directly; arrivals from the south pass over Picnic Point at 1,500–2,500 feet AGL. Aircraft types — primarily Boeing 737 and Airbus A320 family — produce 75–85 dBA at ground level during low-altitude overflights. The real controversy was never just about 24 flights: opponents argued it was a foot-in-the-door strategy, that 24 flights would become 48, then unlimited.

Growler Transits

EA-18G Growlers transit through the Paine Field area airspace on training routes, particularly when conducting FCLP at Ault Field and OLF Coupeville approximately 30 miles north. Even at transit altitude over south Snohomish County, Growler passes can reach 80–95 dBA. Residents describe Growler transits as the most intense single-event noise — a searing roar that stops conversations and triggers startle responses.

5. BNSF Railroad

The BNSF Railway mainline runs along the Mukilteo waterfront, literally feet from homes in some areas and directly through the commercial district. Approximately 50+ trains per day transit through Mukilteo:

  • Unit coal trains (1.0–1.5 miles long, heavy, slow) — decreased somewhat as coal exports declined but remain significant
  • Intermodal container trains (double-stack, fast)
  • Oil trains (unit trains of tank cars — a separate safety controversy)
  • Amtrak Cascades and Sounder commuter trains

Train noise includes locomotive horn blasts at grade crossings (95–110 dBA at 50 feet), wheel squeal on curves, locomotive engine noise, coupling impacts, and continuous low-frequency rumble from heavy freight. Mukilteo has multiple at-grade crossings along the waterfront, each requiring horn blasts. The Mukilteo Multimodal Project (ferry terminal relocation and associated road/rail improvements) has been partly motivated by the desire to reduce at-grade crossing conflicts and associated horn noise.

6. The Regulatory Gap

The regulatory framework for noise in the Possession Sound corridor is fragmented across agencies with no integrating authority:

Noise SourceRegulatory AuthorityFramework
Commercial aviationFAAPart 150 Noise Compatibility (but no Part 150 study completed for Paine Field)
Boeing test flightsFAA (nominal)Exempt from commercial noise restrictions as defense/test operations
Military aviationU.S. Navy / DoDNEPA environmental review; operations self-regulated
RailroadFRAFederal Railroad Administration horn requirements at grade crossings
Helicopter (military/defense)DoD / FAAMilitary/defense contractor rules; largely exempt from local ordinances

No single agency evaluates the cumulative burden. This regulatory gap is a persistent community frustration. The WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines (2018) emphasize that cumulative exposure from multiple sources produces health impacts — cardiovascular stress, sleep disruption, cognitive impairment in children — that exceed what any single-source analysis would predict.

What Is Missing

  • No Part 150 study for Paine Field — unlike Sea-Tac, which has a comprehensive FAA Part 150 Noise Compatibility Program with sound insulation programs for affected homes, Paine Field has not completed a full Part 150 study. Without Part 150, there is no federal framework for noise mitigation funding (home insulation, land use compatibility) for Paine Field.
  • No cumulative noise modeling that combines aviation, rail, and military sources into a single assessment
  • No health impact study specific to Mukilteo/Picnic Point populations
  • No sound insulation program for homes under flight paths (Sea-Tac has insulated thousands of homes; Paine Field has insulated none)
  • No continuous noise monitoring network with public real-time data (Sea-Tac has this; Paine Field does not at comparable scale)

Mukilteo city government has been the most active local voice, passing resolutions opposing commercial expansion and advocating for noise mitigation, but a city cannot regulate federal airspace or override county airport authority. The Snohomish County Airport Commission and Paine Field administration remain caught between pro-commercial and anti-noise factions. The Puget Sound Regional Council has addressed aviation noise in regional planning documents but has no regulatory authority.
