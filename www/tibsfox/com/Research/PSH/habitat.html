Module F: Habitat Preservation & Recovery

Approximately 90% of the historic estuarine habitat in the Snohomish River delta and surrounding Possession Sound shoreline has been lost to diking, filling, and industrial development since European settlement. The scale of this loss — among the highest of any estuary system in Puget Sound — frames every restoration effort documented in this module.

1. Historic Loss of Estuarine Habitat

The Snohomish River estuary was once one of the largest and most productive estuarine systems in Puget Sound, encompassing thousands of acres of tidal marsh, mudflat, and distributary channels that provided critical rearing habitat for juvenile Chinook salmon, forage fish, and migratory shorebirds. Today, approximately 90% of that historic estuarine habitat has been eliminated through a century and a half of diking, draining, filling, and conversion to agricultural and industrial use.

The transformation began in the 1860s with early settlers diking marshlands for farming. By the early 20th century, the Weyerhaeuser timber operation at Mill A on the Everett waterfront, the Great Northern Railway corridor, and expanding port facilities had converted vast tidal areas to impervious industrial surfaces. The City of Everett's sewage and industrial discharges further degraded the remaining nearshore habitat through the mid-20th century, reducing the estuary's ecological function to a fraction of its historic capacity.

This loss is not merely historical. The consequences are measured today in depressed salmon returns, reduced forage fish spawning substrate, declining eelgrass meadows, and the endangered status of Southern Resident killer whales that depend on the Possession Sound prey base. Every restoration project in this module operates against the backdrop of this 90% deficit — even the most ambitious projects recover only a small percentage of what was lost.

2. Major Restoration Projects

Four landmark restoration projects define the modern effort to recover estuarine and nearshore habitat in the Possession Sound watershed. Together they represent over 1,300 acres of restored or protected tidal and riparian habitat and more than $100 million in public investment.

Qwuloolt Estuary Restoration — $20.5M

The Qwuloolt Estuary Restoration Project, led by the Tulalip Tribes, is the largest estuarine restoration effort in the Snohomish River basin. The project breached a mile-long dike along the lower Snohomish River in 2015, reconnecting approximately 350 acres of historic floodplain and tidal marsh to the river system. The $20.5 million project was funded through a partnership of federal, state, tribal, and local sources including NOAA, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, and the Salmon Recovery Funding Board.

The restored site provides juvenile Chinook salmon rearing habitat in the critical estuary transition zone where smolts acclimate from freshwater to saltwater. Monitoring data from the Tulalip Tribes' Natural Resources Department has documented rapid colonization by native salt marsh vegetation, increased use by juvenile salmonids, and the return of migratory shorebird species. The project demonstrates that large-scale estuarine restoration is both feasible and ecologically effective in the heavily modified Snohomish delta system.

The name Qwuloolt comes from the Lushootseed language of the Tulalip Tribes, reflecting the project's cultural significance as a reconnection to ancestral lands and traditional ecological knowledge.

Port Susan Marine Stewardship Area — 523 Acres

Port Susan, the tidal embayment northeast of Camano Island, was designated a Marine Stewardship Area (MSA) encompassing 523 acres of protected tidal and estuarine habitat. The designation recognizes Port Susan as one of the most ecologically intact estuarine systems remaining in Puget Sound, supporting commercial shellfish harvest, eelgrass meadows, and extensive mudflat habitat for migratory shorebirds along the Pacific Flyway.

The Stillaguamish River delta at the head of Port Susan is a focal area for salmon recovery, with ongoing restoration of diked agricultural lands to tidal influence. Port Susan's proximity to Possession Sound makes it a critical reference site for understanding what the Snohomish estuary once looked like before 90% of its tidal habitat was lost.

Spencer Island — 414 Acres

Spencer Island, a 414-acre site at the confluence of the Snohomish and Steamboat Slough channels, is jointly managed by Snohomish County and the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife. The island includes both managed wetland units and areas restored to full tidal exchange, providing a mosaic of habitat types from freshwater marsh to salt-influenced mudflat.

The site serves as both wildlife habitat and public access for birding and environmental education. Spencer Island's location at the heart of the Snohomish River delta makes it a keystone site for the broader estuarine restoration strategy, connecting upstream habitat improvements to the Possession Sound nearshore.

Blue Heron Slough — 353 Acres

The Blue Heron Slough Restoration Project restored 353 acres of tidal estuarine habitat on the former Biringer Farm site along the lower Snohomish River. Completed in 2019, the project removed dikes and fill material to restore tidal channels and salt marsh, creating new habitat for juvenile Chinook salmon, shorebirds, and estuarine-dependent species.

The project was a mitigation effort associated with the Port of Everett's Riverside Business Park development, demonstrating how regulatory frameworks (Section 404 Clean Water Act permits) can drive meaningful habitat restoration when applied to sites with high ecological potential.

3. Southern Resident Killer Whales & the Possession Triangle

The Southern Resident killer whale (SRKW) population, listed as endangered under the Endangered Species Act since 2005, is among the most critically threatened marine mammal populations in the world. As of the most recent census, the population numbers fewer than 75 individuals divided among three family groups: J pod, K pod, and L pod. Of these, J pod is the group most frequently observed in the waters of Possession Sound.

Marine biologists and whale researchers have identified a foraging pattern informally known as the “Possession Triangle” — a recurrent travel and hunting route that J pod follows through the waters bounded by Whidbey Island, Camano Island, and the Snohomish County mainland from Mukilteo to Everett. This triangle encompasses Possession Sound proper, Saratoga Passage, and Port Susan, creating a circuit through some of the most productive nearshore waters in the central Puget Sound region.

The ecological logic of the Possession Triangle is tied to prey availability. Chinook salmon — the primary prey species for SRKWs, constituting approximately 80% of their diet — pass through these waters in substantial numbers during summer and fall runs to the Snohomish, Stillaguamish, and Skagit River systems. The convergence of multiple river plumes, tidal mixing zones, and nearshore structure creates foraging conditions that concentrate prey in predictable locations along the triangle route.

Threats Within the Triangle

  • Prey depletion: Depressed Chinook salmon returns across Puget Sound reduce the available prey base. The 90% loss of estuarine rearing habitat in the Snohomish delta directly affects juvenile salmon survival and adult returns.
  • Vessel noise and disturbance: Shipping traffic through Possession Sound, including container vessels, tankers, and the Mukilteo–Clinton ferry route, generates underwater noise that interferes with SRKW echolocation and foraging efficiency.
  • Contaminants: SRKWs carry some of the highest PCB and PBDE body burdens measured in any marine mammal population. Contaminated prey from the 11 cleanup sites in Everett harbor contributes to bioaccumulation through the food chain.
  • Reduced population viability: With fewer than 75 individuals, the population faces genetic bottleneck risks, low reproductive rates, and vulnerability to disease and environmental stochasticity.
The Possession Triangle is not an officially designated management area but an observed behavioral pattern. Its recognition by researchers underscores the importance of Possession Sound as foraging habitat for the most at-risk SRKW family group.

4. Forage Fish & Eelgrass

Forage fish — primarily surf smelt (Hypomesus pretiosus) and Pacific sand lance (Ammodytes personatus) — are the ecological linchpins of the Possession Sound nearshore food web. These small, schooling fish convert planktonic energy into prey biomass accessible to salmon, seabirds, and marine mammals. Their spawning behavior ties them directly to shoreline habitat quality.

Spawning Habitat Requirements

Surf smelt spawn on mixed sand-gravel beaches in the upper intertidal zone, typically from July through September along the Possession Sound shoreline. Pacific sand lance spawn lower on the beach, depositing eggs in clean, well-sorted sand substrates from November through February. Both species require specific grain-size distributions, wave exposure, and beach slope conditions that are easily degraded by shoreline armoring, dredging, and stormwater-driven sediment contamination.

The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) maintains a forage fish spawning habitat survey that documents confirmed and suspected spawning beaches throughout Puget Sound. Several beaches along the Possession Sound shoreline between Mukilteo and Everett are documented surf smelt and sand lance spawning sites, making shoreline habitat protection in this corridor a direct forage fish conservation action.

Eelgrass Beds

Native eelgrass (Zostera marina) meadows in Possession Sound provide essential ecosystem services: nursery habitat for juvenile fish and invertebrates, wave attenuation that reduces shoreline erosion, sediment stabilization, carbon sequestration, and water quality improvement through nutrient uptake. Eelgrass beds are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth per unit area, rivaling tropical rainforests in primary productivity.

The Washington Department of Natural Resources (DNR) conducts the Submerged Vegetation Monitoring Program (SVMP), which tracks eelgrass extent and condition throughout Puget Sound. In the Possession Sound survey region, eelgrass beds are documented along the Mukilteo shoreline, around Hat Island, and in the shallows of Port Gardner Bay. These beds face ongoing threats from dredging, anchor scarring, overwater structures that shade the seafloor, and declining water clarity from stormwater turbidity.

The interconnection between forage fish, eelgrass, and salmon creates a cascade of ecological dependencies. Eelgrass beds shelter juvenile forage fish; forage fish feed juvenile and adult salmon; salmon feed Southern Resident killer whales. Degradation at any link in this chain propagates upward to the apex predator — a dynamic that makes nearshore habitat protection in Possession Sound simultaneously a forage fish, salmon, and SRKW conservation action.

5. Contamination & Cleanup

Everett harbor and the lower Snohomish River estuary contain 11 active cleanup sites under the oversight of the Washington Department of Ecology's Toxics Cleanup Program. These sites represent a legacy of over a century of industrial activity on the Everett waterfront, including lumber milling, pulp and paper manufacturing, shipbuilding, metal fabrication, and petroleum storage. The contaminants of concern include polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), heavy metals (arsenic, lead, mercury, zinc), dioxins/furans, and petroleum hydrocarbons.

Weyerhaeuser Mill A — $200–300M Estimated Cleanup

The former Weyerhaeuser Mill A site on the Everett waterfront is the largest and most complex contaminated site in Possession Sound. The mill operated from 1900 to 1992, processing timber and manufacturing wood products on a site that extends over approximately 68 acres of upland and 40 acres of adjacent waterway sediments in the Snohomish River estuary. Decades of operations deposited dioxins, furans, wood waste (including submerged log debris fields), and heavy metals into the nearshore sediments.

The estimated cleanup cost of $200–300 million reflects the scale of contamination and the complexity of remediating both upland soils and in-water sediments while maintaining navigation in the active waterway. The cleanup is proceeding under a consent decree between Weyerhaeuser (now Weyerhaeuser Company, a REIT) and the Washington Department of Ecology, with remedial investigation and feasibility study phases ongoing. The site's redevelopment potential is significant — the Port of Everett and City of Everett have identified Mill A as a transformational waterfront revitalization opportunity, contingent on successful environmental remediation.

The cleanup sites collectively represent both a contamination legacy and a restoration opportunity. As sediment remediation proceeds, the potential to restore nearshore habitat function in areas that have been ecologically dead for decades becomes real. The intersection of cleanup and habitat restoration — removing contamination while simultaneously rebuilding tidal marsh, eelgrass beds, and forage fish spawning substrate — is the most promising pathway to meaningful ecological recovery in the Everett harbor area.

Contaminants in Everett harbor sediments bioaccumulate through the food chain: from sediment to benthic invertebrates, to forage fish, to salmon, to Southern Resident killer whales. The 11 cleanup sites are not isolated environmental problems — they are direct contributors to the body burden carried by an endangered species.
