Module K: Roads & Rails

The transportation corridors of south Snohomish County shaped its communities as profoundly as any natural feature. Highway 99, the Interurban Railway, and the named crossroads that served as gathering points for dispersed homesteaders defined where people lived, worked, and stopped for a drink — legal or otherwise.

1. Pacific Highway (Highway 99)

The Pacific Highway was championed by “good roads advocates” in the early 1910s and added to the state highway system in 1913. The original route followed the Bothell–Everett Highway (now SR 527) along North Creek — an inland route, not the current Highway 99 alignment. The “North Trunk Road” from Seattle to the area east of Edmonds was completed in August 1912, initially paved with bricks. A 6.5-mile brick-paved section between Lake Forest Park and Bothell was contracted in 1913 for $84,450.

By 1924, the Pacific Highway was complete as a continuously paved road between Seattle and Everett. In 1927, it received its federal designation as U.S. Highway 99. The 14-mile stretch of 20-foot-wide pavement between the King County line and Everett city limits opened October 9, 1927 — the road opened “sometime very early on Sunday morning” without fanfare.

From the King/Snohomish county line, the highway ran north three miles to Seattle Heights (centered around 212th Street SW), continued through what is now Lynnwood, past Keeler’s Korner (164th Street SW), up over Gunnysack Hill (approximately 148th–170th Street SW), through Esperance (228th Street SW area), and on toward Everett. In 1947, Aurora Avenue received the Blue Star Memorial Highway designation honoring WWII veterans.

The Aurora Bridge (“George Washington Memorial Bridge”) opened February 22, 1932, creating the western route through Aurora Avenue. By September 1931, a four-lane highway extended north from the bridge to Everett. Before I-5, Highway 99 was the main north–south link along the entire West Coast and “became a major revenue source for cities and towns along the route.”

The I-5 Transition

The 19.7-mile section of I-5 between north Seattle and Everett opened February 3, 1965. I-5 was built east of Aurora Avenue / Highway 99, effectively creating a bypass that shifted major through-traffic away from the Highway 99 commercial strip. Many roadside businesses declined. By 1968, I-5 was complete and US 99 was decommissioned, becoming State Route 99. The corridor that was once the main highway became a secondary arterial — motels, gas stations, and roadside businesses that served through-traffic lost their customer base.

Modern Revitalization

The Edmonds Highway 99 Gateway Revitalization, completed August 2023, replaced the center two-way left-turn lane with landscaped raised medians and mid-block left-turn pockets along the entire corridor through Edmonds. A WSDOT SR 99 Pre-Design Study (2024–2026) covers 6.6 miles from 212th Street SW to Airport Road. The safety record is stark: 63 fatal or serious injury collisions between 2019–2023, over 60 pedestrians and cyclists hit, 8 pedestrian fatalities. Full rebuild cost estimate: $660–780 million, plus $310–370 million for right-of-way acquisition.

2. Gunnysack Hill

Gunnysack Hill is the long slope on Highway 99 located approximately between 148th Street SW and 170th Street SW in what is now Lynnwood / unincorporated Snohomish County. The High Point feed store at the top of the hill was said to be “the highest point between Seattle and Everett.” The name is colloquial, not an official geographic designation, but persists in community usage nearly 100 years later — there is even a “Gunny Sack Hill” AA meeting group at the Lynnwood Alano Club.

Origin of the Name — Two Accounts

Account 1: Ole Bloss and the Feed Sacks

A man named Ole Bloss, who had a farm at the bottom of the hill on the west side, supplied stranded motorists with empty feed sacks to wrap around their tires for traction when icy conditions caused early automobiles to skid. The hill eventually became known as Gunnysack Hill. This is the most commonly cited origin and connects to the broader practice of using burlap sacks on tires — similar to how they were used on horse hooves on steep grades.

Account 2: Ola R. Bloss and the “Gunny Sack Ranch”

In the 1920s–1930s, an ex-logger from Michigan named Ola R. Bloss owned land behind the Owl Tavern. His shack was covered with tar paper, “with gunnysacks for chinking wherever needed.” He raised rabbits and “used gunnysacks as curtains around the pens.” His principal crop was corn, “which he sold by the gunnysack-full.” A sign reading “Gunny Sack Ranch” was a local monument for years. Early truckers laboring up the graveled hill may have named the long slope after this sign.

Source: With Angels to the Rear by Delmar H. Caryl

Both accounts likely reference the same person (Ole/Ola Bloss) with slightly different emphases. The name arose from a combination of the ubiquitous gunnysacks on the Bloss property and the practical use of burlap sacks for tire traction on the steep, unpaved hill. Before paving (pre-1927), the graveled road surface made the hill particularly treacherous — early milk trucks “would chug up the hill very slowly,” and children could sled down Highway 99 in winter.

3. Prohibition Roadhouses

Highway 99 became an escape route for speakeasy operators fleeing Seattle law enforcement. From the county line north to Seattle Heights, “roadhouses, tourist cabins, gas stations and an occasional cafe soon dotted the landscape.” A constellation of Prohibition-era establishments thrived along the corridor from 1927 through the mid-1930s:

Blakewood Inn

Located at Seattle Heights on the southeast corner. An infamous dance hall and bar where “gin was conveniently made.” Attracted gangsters. Destroyed by suspicious fire on May 20, 1935.

Jungle Temple No. 2

Located where Ranch 99 now sits. A musical epicenter that hosted Oscar Holden, known as the “Patriarch of Seattle Jazz.” Holden arrived in Seattle in 1919 and became a foundational figure in the city’s jazz scene, performing at venues along Jackson Street and beyond. His presence at the Jungle Temple placed world-class jazz on a rural highway in south Snohomish County.

The Ranch

One-half mile south of Seattle Heights on the west side, owned by Doc Hamilton, a Seattle speakeasy operator. Offered gambling and illegal drinking.

Woody’s Tavern

Formerly Carl Melby’s Echo Lake Tavern. Ran Canadian whiskey from British Columbia via boats to Richmond Beach. Had a false floor and trap door for concealing alcohol.

Other notable establishments included The Olympic Tavern (a block west on 220th Street SW, closed after a 1927 New Year’s Eve raid, later serving as a hospital, hunting lodge, brothel, dog training business, and church youth center), Harvey’s Lounge (still operating with its neon rabbit sign), The Maryland (Bavarian-style, later demolished), Merry Max Dine and Dance (near 225th Street SW), and Golden Slipper (near the county line).

Roadside Americana

The highway also spawned legitimate roadside landmarks. The Igloo (opened 1940) featured twin metal-clad hemisphere structures with ice-blue neon; carhops wore “short flared skirts, white jacket, and white cowboy boots” and served on trays hooked to half-open car windows. Twin Teepees Restaurant (opened March 13, 1937) had two concrete teepee structures fabricated in California and transported by flatbed truck — demolished July 31, 2001. Peter Canlis Restaurant (opened 1950), designed by architect Roland Terry, still operates as one of Seattle’s finest dining establishments.

4. The Seattle–Everett Interurban Railway (1910–1939)

Fred Sander launched construction circa 1900 but completed only six miles of track between Ballard and Hall’s Lake in six years. Stone & Webster purchased Sander’s line in 1909 and organized the Seattle–Everett Traction Company. Service began April 30, 1910.

From downtown Seattle near today’s Westlake Center (5th and Pike), the line ran north to Fremont via Westlake Avenue, up Phinney and Greenwood avenues, then followed a route later paralleled by Highway 99. Key Snohomish County stations included Ballinger, Seattle Heights, Halls Lake, Beverly Park, Esperance, Alderwood, Martha Lake, and Forest Park. The northern terminus was Colby Avenue and Pacific Avenue in Everett — the “Interurban and Stage Depot.”

The Interurban did not pass through Meadowdale. The closest stops were Martha Lake or Alderwood Manor — for Meadowdale residents, the interurban “was too far away for most to use.” Meadowdale had its own rail connection through the Seattle & Montana Railway (later Great Northern), which established a stop near 75th Avenue and North Meadowdale Road by 1905, seeing “three mail trains and six passenger trains a day” by 1907.

The Final Run

As growth expanded after WWI, commuter and mercantile traffic switched to cars and trucks. Highway 99 (completed 1927) drew ridership away. The railway, by then owned by Puget Sound Power & Light Company, made its final run on February 20, 1939. “People came out to see the last run of it and the 50-Piece Veterans of Foreign Wars Band gave it a farewell salute.”

Today the old right-of-way is the Interurban Trail, 16+ miles of paved path through Snohomish County. Heritage Park in Lynnwood preserves Interurban Car 55 (a 1909 wooden electric trolley) and the Wickers Building (Transportation Museum), originally located along the rail line.

5. Keeler’s Corner

At the intersection of 196th Street SW and 68th Avenue West, Carl and Gladys Keeler built the first “mom-and-pop” grocery store and gas station between Seattle and Everett in 1927. Families stopped to “picnic, gas up, buy groceries, play croquet, rest and even camp.” The store sat at the crossroads of a major east–west route (connecting Edmonds to Bothell) and a north–south county road, making it a key waypoint for the bluff-top and waterfront communities of Picnic Point, Meadowdale, and Norma Beach.

Keeler’s Corner was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1982. The 1920s-era gas pumps still stand. The gas station closed in the mid-1960s when the last family members retired, a casualty of I-5 drawing traffic away from the local road network. The building currently operates as an antique shop.

The crossroads pattern was common in pre-suburban south Snohomish County: a single commercial establishment at an intersection served as retail hub, post office, social gathering point, and news exchange for the surrounding farming community. Keeler’s Corner sat west of both Highway 99 and the Interurban line, serving the communities that the main corridors missed. Other named crossroads in the area — Alderwood Manor, Martha Lake, Esperance, Perrinville — followed the same pattern, with family names attaching to whatever crossroads store served the surrounding homesteads.

6. Alderwood Manor

The Puget Mill Company owned approximately one-quarter of south Snohomish County — logged-off land carrying a tax burden. In 1917, they created the planned community of Alderwood Manor on 7,000 acres of cutover land, surveyed and platted into 5- to 10-acre “ranchettes.” The company built roads and provided water and electricity. The area was previously known as Forest Park, a stop on the Interurban line.

The Free Poultry College

A 30-acre Demonstration Farm was established as a marketing tool, billed as a “Free Poultry College.” The large incubator/hatching facility could produce 55,000 chicks every 21 days. Potential settlers arrived via the Interurban Railway and were shown “how they could make a living raising chickens on the 5 acres they were considering purchasing.” The farm attracted settlers from communities “as far away as New York.”

By the early 1920s, Alderwood Manor was the second-largest egg-producing area in the United States. At peak, egg prices ran about $1 per dozen. But local historian Ray Pennock noted in 1999 that the “primary commodity actually transported was chicken manure, not eggs or meat” — a symbiotic relationship developed with fruit growers in the region.

Decline

The Great Depression collapsed egg prices from $1/dozen to $0.10/dozen. Many families sold their chicken ranches. Puget Mill closed the Demonstration Farm in the late 1930s and divided the land into one-acre ranchettes. The Interurban ceased operations in 1939. Lynnwood incorporated in 1959. An incorporation vote for Alderwood Manor itself failed in the 1960s, and the area was annexed into Lynnwood, completing the rural-to-suburban transformation.

7. Mosher Flag Station

Mosher, located between Picnic Point and Norma Beach on the shore of Puget Sound, was a flag station for the Great Northern Railway with a post office established June 23, 1892 (discontinued February 28, 1901). It also had a dock for steamboats of the Mosquito Fleet traveling Puget Sound.

In the era before roads, communities like Picnic Point, Norma Beach, and Meadowdale were geographically isolated by steep bluffs, dense forest, and lack of roads. Early residents “traveled along muddy dirt paths, trails created by Native Americans, logging roads, and abandoned tracks from logging companies.” Upper and Lower Meadowdale were “nearly separate entities” due to poor transportation links. In the earliest days, “boats and canoes had to be used for travel, mail deliveries, and freight, even between Meadowdale and Edmonds.” Seattle was “a half-day boat ride away.”

The Great Northern Railway, built into the area in the 1890s, provided the first reliable link between these waterfront communities and the cities of Seattle and Everett. Mosher’s flag station and dock represented the critical connection point where bluff-bottom communities met the outside world — a role that would later shift inland as Highway 99 and the automobile transformed the region’s transportation geography.
