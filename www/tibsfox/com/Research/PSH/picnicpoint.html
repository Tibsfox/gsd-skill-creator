1. Sandy Sandborg & Local Memory

The most significant finding in this module is a negative one: “Picnic Point Pathways” by Sandy Sandborg does not appear in any library catalog. It is absent from WorldCat, Google Books, the Library of Congress catalog, and Open Library. This strongly suggests a self-published or community-published pamphlet with limited distribution — a pattern common to Puget Sound neighborhood histories produced during the 1970s–1990s Bicentennial heritage era.

The surname Sandborg is Swedish, consistent with the heavy Scandinavian settlement patterns in Snohomish County. No public biographical information for Sandy or Sandra Sandborg appeared in any searched database. She is most likely a longtime resident who compiled neighborhood oral histories through the Picnic Point Community Club or a similar volunteer organization. The book — if it is a bound book rather than a newsletter series or presentation collection — would be the single most productive primary source for nearly every topic in this module.

Picnic Point Naming & Settlement

The point of land extending into Puget Sound (now Picnic Point County Park, managed by Snohomish County Parks) was historically used as a picnic and recreation destination. The name derives from this recreational use — the point is a natural landing spot visible from the water, used by local residents arriving by boat for picnic outings before automobile roads were built. This naming pattern is common throughout Puget Sound: Dash Point, Meadow Point, Possession Point.

2. The Lost Bridge

Picnic Point Road runs west from 68th Ave W down to Picnic Point County Park at the shoreline, descending through a ravine drained by Picnic Point Creek. At the creek crossing, the Snohomish County bridge inventory and the National Bridge Inventory (FHWA) show no currently inventoried bridge. The crossing is now a culvert — a replacement pattern repeated throughout Snohomish County where wooden bridges on rural roads from the early-to-mid 1900s were replaced with concrete culverts during the 1950s–1970s road modernization era.

The original wooden bridge was likely built when Picnic Point Road was first established as a county road, probably in the 1910s–1930s during the homesteading and logging era. No photographs or construction records appeared in any digitized source.

The Landslide

Picnic Point and Norma Beach are adjacent bluff-top and beach-level communities, with the terrain between them involving steep, unstable marine bluffs along the Puget Sound shoreline — the same glacial till over clay formation that produces landslides throughout the South Snohomish County coastal corridor. The road connection would have been along 76th Ave W or a now-abandoned segment running along or down the bluffs between the two communities.

The critical finding: no road currently connects Picnic Point directly to Norma Beach along the bluffs. Today you must go inland (east) to reach one from the other. The Washington DNR landslide inventory confirms the area is classified as landslide-prone, but no specific single event between the two communities appeared in digitized newspaper archives or geological records. This could mean the event predates digital archives (pre-1980s), was covered only in hyper-local publications (the Mukilteo Beacon has limited digitization), or involved a gradual series of slides rather than one dramatic event.

3. BNSF Slide Detection Systems

The BNSF Railway mainline between Edmonds and Everett — approximately 12 miles (MP 18 to MP 30) — is one of the most heavily instrumented slide-prone segments on the entire BNSF system. The Edmonds–Everett corridor runs directly along the Puget Sound shoreline, beneath 150–250-foot glacial bluffs composed of unconsolidated Vashon-age deposits that will continue to erode regardless of engineering intervention.

Primary Detection: Slide Detector Fences

Physical fence structures installed across known slide paths on the uphill side of the tracks. Horizontal breakaway wires are strung between posts and connected in series to the signal circuit. When debris breaks any wire, the circuit opens and block signals go to red (stop) within seconds — no human decision required. BNSF dispatchers at the Fort Worth Network Operations Center are alerted electronically. No train may pass until a track inspector has physically examined the right-of-way.

Detector fences are positioned across gullies, ravines, and known slide chutes, with the densest instrumentation in three high-frequency zones: the Perrinville/Meadowdale area, the Picnic Point/Norma Beach area, and Mukilteo to South Everett where the bluffs are tallest.

The Sounder North Reliability Crisis

Sounder North (Everett–Seattle, 4 roundtrips/day) is Sound Transit’s most unreliable service. Annual reliability has dropped below 80% in bad slide years, compared to 95%+ for Sounder South. In severe winters, entire months of service have been cancelled or severely degraded. Ridership remains low because riders cannot depend on the service — a vicious cycle.

Sound Transit’s long-term answer: Everett Link Light Rail along the I-5 corridor inland, entirely avoiding the coastal bluffs. Planned under ST3 for completion in the 2040s. Sounder North’s future after Everett Link opens is uncertain.

Detection Technology Evolution

1890s–1950s

Manual observation. Track walkers physically walked the line on regular schedules. During storms, watchmen were stationed at known slide areas to flag approaching trains. Section gangs monitored conditions and reported.

1950s–1970s

Slide detector fences. First electronic detection. Breakaway wires connected to signal circuits — wire break opens circuit, signals go to stop. Reactive but automatic.

1970s–1990s

Rain gauges added. Automated rainfall monitoring with threshold-based slow orders and closures. First proactive capability — acting before a slide occurs rather than after.

1990s–2000s

Borehole instrumentation. Inclinometers and piezometers installed in cased boreholes. Direct measurement of creep movement and groundwater pressure at the Esperance/Lawton contact. Refined rainfall-threshold models.

2000s–2010s

Remote sensing. Remote telemetry for instruments, LiDAR surveys (airborne and mobile), InSAR investigations for corridor-wide screening. Integration with GIS systems.

2010s–present

PTC + machine learning. Positive Train Control (ETMS) providing signal enforcement backup. Real-time data dashboards. Emerging: ML applied to historical slide data for predictive modeling, drone-based inspection, fiber optic distributed sensing.

The fundamental evolution has been from reactive (detect that a slide has happened, stop trains, clear debris, resume) to proactive (monitor precursor conditions, preemptively slow or stop trains, reduce risk of train-slide collision). This shift is incomplete — the slide fence remains the primary automated safety system, but it is now supplemented by proactive monitoring that enables precautionary closures before failure occurs.

4. Submarine Cables & Fiber Optic Infrastructure

Possession Sound — the body of water between Whidbey Island and the mainland — is a confirmed submarine cable crossing zone. NOAA nautical charts 18443 and 18441 show cable area designations in the sound, warning mariners against anchoring. Both power and telecommunications cables cross from the mainland to Whidbey Island.

Possession Sound Cable Infrastructure

  • Puget Sound Energy (PSE) operates submarine power cables crossing Possession Sound to serve Whidbey Island, using the Mukilteo–Clinton corridor
  • Telecommunications cables connect Whidbey Island to the mainland fiber backbone, with operators including legacy GTE/Verizon (now Ziply Fiber) and Whidbey Telephone Company infrastructure
  • Military communications cables connect Naval Air Station Whidbey Island (NASWI) to Naval Station Everett. NASWI is a P-3 Orion / EA-18G Growler base critical to Pacific Fleet electronic warfare operations. Specific routing is classified.
  • Deception Pass Bridge provides terrestrial fiber redundancy to the submarine crossings from the south

Washington State: Zero Trans-Pacific Landings

Despite being home to Amazon and Microsoft, Washington State has essentially no major trans-Pacific submarine cable landings. Oregon dominates entirely:

The additional latency of routing through Portland versus a hypothetical Washington landing is less than 1ms.

The Westin Building: PNW Hub

The Westin Building Exchange at 2001 6th Avenue, Seattle, is the most important single building for internet traffic in the Pacific Northwest. Over 250 networks interconnect here. It houses the Seattle Internet Exchange (SIX), one of the largest in the US by participant count (300+ peers). Every trans-Pacific cable serving the PNW ultimately reaches the Westin Building through terrestrial fiber from the Oregon coast, routed via Portland and north on I-5.

Fiber Along the BNSF Right-of-Way

Railroad rights-of-way are prime fiber optic routes nationally. The BNSF mainline between Edmonds and Everett — running literally at the water’s edge in many places — carries terrestrial fiber optic cables from carriers including Zayo Group and Lumen Technologies. The rail corridor provides continuous, already-cleared right-of-way that avoids trenching through developed urban areas.

This creates a remarkable geometry: one set of cables runs parallel to the shore atop 130-year-old railroad infrastructure, while another set crosses the sound perpendicular to the shore connecting mainland to islands. The two orientations form a grid of invisible digital infrastructure overlaid on water and shoreline.

5. The Infrastructure Layer Stack

The most compelling finding across all three research threads is the layered pattern of connectivity that has defined Possession Sound for millennia. Each era’s technology overlays the previous without erasing it:

Pre-contact

Canoe routes. Coast Salish peoples — Tulalip, Snohomish, and allied groups — paddled across Possession Sound connecting mainland and island settlements. The water was the highway.

1850s–1920s

Steamships & the Mosquito Fleet. Daily service connecting Everett, Mukilteo, Clinton/Langley, and other communities. The sound remained the primary transportation infrastructure.

1880s–1960s

Telegraph & telephone cables. The first invisible infrastructure beneath the water. Copper cables laid on the bottom connecting island communities. Western Union, Pacific Telephone & Telegraph. The water became both highway and conduit.

1890s

Great Northern Railway. James J. Hill’s railroad built along the shoreline because it offered the easiest grade. Telegraph wires strung along the tracks. The narrow coastal corridor between bluffs and water became the permanent transportation alignment.

Mid-20th century

Power cables & military comms. PSE submarine power cables to Whidbey Island. NASWI and Naval Station Everett connected by military-grade communications cables. The infrastructure beneath the water became essential utility — not just communication but energy and national defense.

1990s–present

Fiber optic. The current generation. Data flowing beneath the same waters where the ferries cross, where the Mosquito Fleet steamed, where the Tulalip people paddled. Along the shore, fiber runs in the BNSF right-of-way — the same corridor as the 1893 railroad, now carrying terabits per second.

The community dependency is invisible but total. Whidbey Island’s internet routes through Possession Sound cables. The ferry system must coordinate with cable infrastructure — anchoring restrictions protect cables, terminal construction must account for cable routes. Cable repair vessels occasionally operate in the sound when anchor strikes damage cables. The Everett waterfront’s transformation from timber/fishing/naval port to mixed-use development means increasing demand on the same cable infrastructure that has served the corridor since the telegraph era.
