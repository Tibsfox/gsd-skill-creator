Module B: Industrial Shoreline

The Edmonds–Mukilteo shoreline is a layered industrial landscape. Petroleum storage, gravel extraction, lumber transport, and a transcontinental railroad compressed a century of extractive industry into a narrow strip of coast, leaving environmental legacies that shape land use, ecology, and community politics to this day.

1. Standard Oil at Point Wells (1911)

Establishment

Standard Oil Company of California (SOCAL) established a bulk fuel storage and distribution terminal at Point Wells in 1911, the same year the Standard Oil Trust was broken up under the Sherman Antitrust Act. Point Wells sits on the Puget Sound shoreline at the southern tip of unincorporated Snohomish County, just north of the King/Snohomish county line and south of Meadowdale Beach Park. The site offered three critical assets: deep-water access for tanker ships, a flat bench of shoreline suitable for tank farms, and proximity to the Great Northern Railway for overland distribution.

Scale of Operations

At peak operation, the facility contained dozens of above-ground storage tanks occupying approximately 61 acres along the shoreline. A substantial marine terminal with pier and wharf infrastructure allowed ocean-going tankers to offload refined petroleum products arriving from refineries at Cherry Point and Anacortes. Products stored and distributed included gasoline, aviation fuel, diesel, heating oil, marine bunker fuel, and kerosene. This was a distribution terminal, not a refinery. The facility served as a critical fuel supply point during WWII for Pacific Northwest military operations, with peak activity spanning the 1920s through the 1970s.

Ownership Succession

SOCAL became Chevron Corporation through the 1984 merger with Gulf Oil. The facility has been largely decommissioned as an active petroleum terminal, with Chevron reducing operations since the early 2000s. Some limited fuel storage and marine operations persisted into the 2010s, but the site is now characterized primarily by its redevelopment potential and environmental cleanup obligations.

Environmental Legacy

Point Wells is listed in the Washington Department of Ecology’s Toxics Cleanup Program database as Chevron Point Wells Terminal. Decades of petroleum storage resulted in contamination including:

Historical spills and seepage affected the intertidal zone, and the Washington State Department of Health has issued shellfish harvesting advisories for areas near Point Wells. Remediation is ongoing under Ecology oversight, complicated by the unresolved question of future land use.

The Point Wells Redevelopment Controversy

One of the most contentious land-use battles in Snohomish County has played out over Point Wells for more than 15 years. BSRE (Point Wells LP) has proposed a large-scale mixed-use development called Point Wells Urban Center with 2,800–3,000+ residential units, retail, and office space on the former 61-acre tank farm.

The jurisdictional problem is acute: Point Wells is in unincorporated Snohomish County, but road access runs only through the City of Shoreline (King County) via Richmond Beach Road and through the Town of Woodway (population ~1,300) via a narrow, steep road. Neither municipality wants the traffic burden. The City of Edmonds has expressed interest in annexing the site.

Environmental and safety concerns compound the jurisdictional conflict: the bluffs above Point Wells are classified as landslide-prone, the low-lying site faces tsunami and seismic risk, there is only a single evacuation route, and residential development requires more stringent cleanup standards than industrial use. The site is adjacent to critical nearshore habitat for juvenile Chinook salmon migration, and forage fish spawn on nearby beaches. Multiple legal challenges have been filed by Woodway, environmental groups, and neighboring jurisdictions. As of 2025, the development has not proceeded to construction.

2. Puget Sound Sand & Gravel

Operations Along the Corridor

The Puget Sound Sand & Gravel Company (later acquired by Lone Star Industries) was the most significant aggregate operator in the region. Pacific Sand & Gravel and Glacier Sand & Gravel Company also operated in the broader Puget Lowland, exploiting glacial geology that made the entire corridor rich in construction aggregate.

Gravel extraction operations concentrated along the high bluffs between Meadowdale and Picnic Point, where bluffs rise 100–200+ feet above the shoreline. These bluffs are composed of Vashon-age glacial deposits (approximately 15,000–17,000 years ago):

Glacial UnitCharacterCommercial Value
Vashon TillDense, compacted diamicton (clay, silt, sand, gravel, cobbles, boulders)Fill and road base
Advance OutwashWell-sorted sand and gravel from meltwater streamsPrimary target — concrete aggregate, road base
Esperance SandNotable sand unit in glacial stratigraphyConstruction sand
Pre-Vashon interglacialOlder deposits in lower bluffsVariable

Timeframe

Active operations ran primarily from the 1900s through the 1960s/1970s, with peak extraction during the post-WWII construction boom when enormous quantities of aggregate were needed for I-5 construction, housing development, and commercial construction. Environmental regulations — the Shoreline Management Act of 1971 and the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act — combined with increasing residential development pressure and growing awareness of bluff instability to curtail shoreline-area gravel mining.

Bluff Destabilization

The removal of material from the bluff face and toe directly contributed to landslide hazards that persist to this day. Four mechanisms drove the destabilization:

  • Toe removal: Mining at the base of bluffs removed the buttressing material that supports upper slopes — one of the most destabilizing interventions possible.
  • Slope steepening: Excavation created artificially steepened slopes exceeding the angle of repose for unconsolidated glacial materials.
  • Altered drainage: Mining disrupted natural groundwater flow patterns. Perched water tables on impermeable layers (clay lenses, till) are a primary slope instability driver in this geology.
  • Vegetation removal: Stripping of vegetation removed root reinforcement and transpiration that helps manage soil moisture.

The Meadowdale–Picnic Point corridor is now one of the most landslide-prone areas in Snohomish County, mapped extensively by the Washington State Department of Natural Resources. Recurring slides periodically close or threaten sections of the BNSF Railway tracks. The legacy is intertwined with ecological processes: natural bluff erosion is a critical source of sediment for Puget Sound beaches (“feeder bluffs”), and shoreline armoring to protect the railroad has interrupted this sediment supply, degrading beach habitat for forage fish spawning and juvenile salmon migration.

Current Status

Many former extraction sites have been revegetated and are now residential neighborhoods, parks, or undeveloped steep slopes. Some former pit areas remain visible as anomalously steep or bowl-shaped terrain features on the bluff faces. The legacy of slope instability continues to affect land use and building permits.

3. Pope & Talbot and the Lumber Industry

Company History

Pope & Talbot, Inc. was founded in 1849 in San Francisco by Andrew Jackson Pope and Frederic Talbot. Their Pacific Northwest operation, Puget Mill Company, established its first major mill at Port Gamble, Washington on the Kitsap Peninsula in 1853 — one of the longest continuously operating sawmills in North America, running for 142 years until closure in 1995. The business model was direct: cut timber in the Pacific Northwest, ship lumber by sea to San Francisco and Pacific markets.

Connection to the Edmonds–Mukilteo Corridor

Pope & Talbot’s direct operations were on the Kitsap Peninsula (Port Gamble, Port Ludlow) and in Oregon, not on the eastern Puget Sound shoreline. However, the broader lumber and pulp industry was intensely active along the corridor:

  • Everett: The Weyerhaeuser Company (headquartered in Everett from 1900) operated massive mills on the Everett waterfront along Possession Sound and Port Gardner Bay.
  • Everett Pulp & Paper / Scott Paper: A major pulp mill operated on the Everett waterfront, with effluent discharged directly into Puget Sound.
  • Crown Lumber, Mukilteo: Lumber loading operations connected to the Great Northern Railway, with timber from inland forests transported to shoreline loading points.
  • Japanese Gulch: The gulch between Mukilteo and south Everett hosted various industrial operations including a charcoal and wood products kiln.

Pope & Talbot’s log booms and shipping were a constant presence in central Puget Sound waters, and the company likely sourced timber from Snohomish County forests transported via railroad through the corridor, even with their mills across the Sound.

Later History & Environmental Legacy

Pope & Talbot operated for over 150 years before filing for bankruptcy in 2008. Pope Resources, a successor entity managing timberlands, was acquired by Rayonier in 2020. Port Gamble is now being redeveloped as a mixed-use historic village. The Everett waterfront lumber and pulp operations left significant contamination, particularly in Port Gardner Bay and along the Snohomish River estuary. Log storage in the water deposited bark and wood debris on the seafloor, creating anoxic conditions. Multiple Ecology cleanup sites exist in Everett.

4. Great Northern Railway & Industrial Corridor

The Railroad Arrives (1893)

James J. Hill’s Great Northern Railway reached Puget Sound in 1893, arriving at Everett and extending south along the shoreline to Seattle. The route from Everett south runs directly along the Puget Sound shoreline through the Edmonds–Mukilteo corridor — one of the most scenic and most geologically hazardous rail corridors in North America.

Corporate succession: Great Northern Railway → Burlington Northern (1970 merger with Northern Pacific, CB&Q, SP&S) → BNSF Railway (1995 merger) → now owned by Berkshire Hathaway.

The Railroad as Industrial Artifact

The rail corridor from Everett through Mukilteo, Picnic Point, Meadowdale, and Edmonds is itself one of the most significant industrial features on this shoreline. The rail line runs on fill and armored embankments along the beach, fundamentally altering shoreline morphology. Ongoing conflicts include:

  • Landslide closures: Slides from unstable bluffs periodically close the tracks. BNSF conducts ongoing slope monitoring and stabilization, spending enormous sums on the corridor.
  • Oil train traffic: After the Bakken shale boom (2010s), oil trains through this corridor increased dramatically. The 2013 Lac-Mégantic rail disaster in Quebec heightened safety concerns in Edmonds, Mukilteo, and other shoreline communities.
  • Ecological disruption: The armored railroad embankment disrupts the connection between feeder bluffs and beaches, starving beaches of sediment. This harms forage fish spawning habitat and the nearshore migration corridor for juvenile salmon.
  • Sound Transit Sounder North: Commuter rail service runs on BNSF tracks through this corridor, adding passenger service conflicts to freight operations.

5. Industrial Timeline

1849

Pope & Talbot founded in San Francisco. Puget Mill Company established for Pacific Northwest logging.

1853

Port Gamble mill opens on the Kitsap Peninsula — visible across Puget Sound from the Edmonds–Mukilteo shoreline.

1893

Great Northern Railway reaches Puget Sound at Everett, extending south along the shoreline to Seattle. Industrial access along the corridor begins.

1900s

Weyerhaeuser headquarters established in Everett. Gravel extraction operations begin along Meadowdale–Picnic Point bluffs. Crown Lumber active at Mukilteo.

1911

Standard Oil of California establishes the Point Wells bulk fuel terminal — 61 acres of tank farms on the shoreline.

1941–1945

Point Wells serves as a critical fuel supply point for Pacific Northwest military operations during WWII.

1945–1960s

Post-war construction boom. Peak gravel extraction for I-5 highway construction, housing, and commercial development.

1970

Great Northern merges into Burlington Northern. Railroad remains the industrial spine of the corridor.

1971

Washington Shoreline Management Act passed. Environmental regulations begin curtailing shoreline gravel mining.

1984

SOCAL merges with Gulf Oil, becoming Chevron Corporation. Point Wells passes to Chevron through corporate succession.

1995

Port Gamble mill closes after 142 years. BNSF Railway formed through Burlington Northern–Santa Fe merger.

2000s–present

Post-industrial era: contamination cleanup at Point Wells, Point Wells redevelopment controversy begins, BNSF oil train safety concerns, ecological restoration efforts compete for the same narrow shoreline strip.
