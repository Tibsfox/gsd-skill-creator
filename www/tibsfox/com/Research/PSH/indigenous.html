Module E: Indigenous Sovereignty

The Tulalip Tribes, Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, Swinomish, Suquamish, and allied Coast Salish nations are sovereign peoples with living governments, active treaty rights, and ongoing cultural traditions. This module presents their history with respect for that sovereignty. All claims are sourced to published academic, governmental, or tribal publications. Sacred site locations, restricted cultural knowledge, and unpublished oral traditions are not included.

1. Pre-Contact: 14,000 Years of Habitation

The Puget Sound lowland emerged from beneath the Cordilleran Ice Sheet approximately 16,000–14,000 years ago. As glaciers retreated, the landscape became habitable. Coast Salish habitation of the Salish Sea has been continuous since its formation — archaeological evidence from the broader Puget Sound region documents human presence beginning roughly 10,000–13,000 years ago. The Bear Creek site near Redmond is dated to approximately 10,000 years before present (Ames and Maschner, Peoples of the Northwest Coast, 1999).

The Marpole culture phase (named for a site at the mouth of the Fraser River in Vancouver, c. 2,500 years ago to European contact) is defined by ground slate tools, large-scale salmon processing, social stratification evident in burial practices, long-distance trade in obsidian and dentalium, and the earliest Coast Salish-style art. Marpole culture artifacts are found on both sides of the international border — at sites in the Fraser delta, the Gulf Islands and San Juan Islands, and the northern Puget Sound — direct archaeological evidence for the cultural continuum the border later divided (Matson and Coupland, The Prehistory of the Northwest Coast, 1995).

The Possession Sound shoreline has been occupied for at least several thousand years based on shell midden deposits. Shell middens — accumulated deposits of discarded shellfish remains mixed with cultural materials — can be meters thick and represent centuries or millennia of continuous habitation. Specific midden site locations are protected under Washington State law (RCW 27.53) and are not published for heritage protection reasons (Washington State DAHP records).

2. The Snohomish People (sduhubsh)

The Edmonds-to-Mukilteo shoreline lies within the ancestral territory of the Snohomish people (Lushootseed: sduhubsh, meaning “the lowland people” or related to a style of portaging). They spoke Southern Lushootseed (x̧ʷəlšucid), a Central Salish language shared with the Snoqualmie, Skykomish, Duwamish, Puyallup, and Nisqually. The definitive linguistic works are Thom Hess, Dictionary of Puget Salish (University of Washington Press, 1976) and Bates, Hess, and Hilbert, Lushootseed Dictionary (University of Washington Press, 1994).

Snohomish territory centered on the Snohomish River drainage and the eastern shore of Possession Sound, extending from approximately the mouth of the Stillaguamish River south to present-day Edmonds, including portions of Whidbey and Camano Islands, Hat Island, and the lower Snohomish River to the forks near present-day Monroe. Territorial boundaries were permeable, defined by kinship networks and marriage alliances rather than fixed borders (Suttles, Handbook of North American Indians, Vol. 7, 1990; Tweddell, Indian Claims Commission testimony, Docket 125, 1953).

Population Before and After Epidemics

Pre-contact population estimates vary significantly. James Mooney (1928) estimated approximately 1,200 Snohomish. Colin Tweddell (1953) estimated 1,500–2,000 across the territory. But Robert Boyd, in The Coming of the Spirit of Pestilence (University of Washington Press, 1999), documents devastating epidemics in the 1770s–1830s — likely smallpox in the 1770s and 1801, the “intermittent fever” of the 1830s, and measles/smallpox in 1847–48 — and demonstrates that contact-era population counts already reflected 50–90% decline from pre-epidemic levels. At the Treaty of Point Elliott (1855), the combined Snohomish, Snoqualmie, and allied bands numbered approximately 2,500 total — but this was post-epidemic. A reasonable inference from the published literature is that the pre-epidemic Snohomish population could have been 3,000–5,000 or more, though this is necessarily speculative.

Social Organization

Snohomish society followed the Central Coast Salish pattern: extended families occupied cedar-plank longhouses (40–100+ feet long, sheltering 20–80 people), led by a house chief (si?ab, “high-born person”). Society was stratified into three classes: si?ab (hereditary leaders who controlled resources and hosted potlatches), tu?əx̧ʷ (free common people), and stutcas (enslaved war captives or their descendants). The potlatch (sɡʷiɡʷi in Lushootseed) was the central ceremonial and legal institution — births, deaths, marriages, and territorial rights were witnessed and validated at potlatches (Suttles, 1990; Miller, The Problem of Justice, 2001).

3. Village Sites Along This Shoreline

Hibulb (hibəlb) — The principal winter village of the Snohomish, located near present-day Tulalip/Marysville on the Snohomish River delta. Hibulb was a fortified village with a cedar palisade — one of the few documented fortified settlements in Puget Sound. The fortification indicates both the wealth worth protecting and the threat of raids from northern peoples (Haida, Tlingit, and Lekwiltok Kwakwaka'wakw). The village contained multiple longhouses and a major potlatch house, and was the seat of Snohomish leadership. A 1974 preliminary excavation by archaeologist John Lyle Mattson yielded 930 artifacts. Interpretive signs were dedicated June 5, 2013 (HistoryLink Essay 10766; Hibulb Cultural Center).

Priest Point Village (ch'tl'aq's) — Located on the southeastern shoreline of the Tulalip Reservation, this site had three unfortified longhouses. In 2001, human remains were discovered during bulkhead construction. Excavation of the shell midden yielded mammal, fish, and bird bones plus stone tools including abraders, adze blades, drills, projectile points, and scrapers. The remains were carefully documented and reburied (Hibulb Cultural Center).

Mukilteo (bak'waltiw') — The Snohomish had a year-round village in the Mukilteo area for at least 600 years before European contact. Artifacts uncovered during waterfront construction in the 2010s were carbon-dated to 1,000 years before present. The Lushootseed name's meaning is disputed — “good camping ground” or “narrow passage” (Waterman, Puget Sound Geography, c. 1920/2001).

The Edmonds Marsh — Originally approximately 100 acres (now 22.5 acres), the marsh was an important summer gathering site for Coast Salish peoples: a fishing village and clam digging site, confirmed by a 1986 map drawn by Snoqualmie Elders Earnest Barr and Ed Davis from Tulalip tribal archives. Bark and cattails were harvested from the marsh; fishing, berry gathering, inter-tribal trade, and ceremonies sustained communities through the seasons (Edmonds Historical Museum; Sierra Club, “Edmonds Marsh Through Time”).

4. Seasonal Life on Possession Sound

The Snohomish followed a seasonal pattern tightly adapted to the resource calendar of Puget Sound and the Cascade foothills:

Winter (November–February)

Families gathered in permanent winter villages like Hibulb. The season of spirit dancing, potlatches, storytelling, and craft production. Stored foods — dried salmon, dried berries, rendered oils — sustained the population. Cedar bark processing and tool-making.

Spring (March–May)

Dispersal from winter villages. Herring spawning along the shoreline — eggs on kelp and eelgrass collected and eaten fresh or dried. Root digging: camas in prairies, wapato in river floodplains. Nettle harvesting for cordage.

Summer (June–September)

The main salmon fishing season. Families established fishing camps along the Snohomish River at falls, weirs, and reef-net locations. Chinook ran June–August. Berry picking intensified: salmonberries (June), thimbleberries (July), huckleberries in mountain camps (August–September). Shellfish harvesting along Possession Sound beaches year-round but intensified in summer.

Fall (September–November)

Late salmon runs (coho, chum) taken and preserved. The critical period for smoking and drying fish for winter stores. Waterfowl hunting intensified as migratory birds arrived. Preparation to return to winter villages.

Western red cedar (Thuja plicata) was the foundational material of all material culture: longhouses, canoes, baskets, clothing, cordage, mats, bentwood boxes. Cedar was sacred — prayers were offered before harvesting (Stewart, Cedar: Tree of Life to the Northwest Coast Indians, 1984; Gunther, Ethnobotany of Western Washington, 1945/1973).

The Snohomish River as Lifeblood

Formed by the confluence of the Skykomish and Snoqualmie rivers near present-day Monroe, the Snohomish flows approximately 20 miles west to Possession Sound at Everett. It was the primary canoe highway, a trade corridor connecting saltwater and interior peoples over the Cascades, and host to massive salmon runs in all five Pacific species. Its vast estuary (dramatically altered by railroad construction, port development, and Weyerhaeuser operations in the 20th century) supported enormous biological productivity: wapato marshes, waterfowl habitat, and shellfish beds (Suttles, 1990; Kruckeberg, The Natural History of Puget Sound Country, 1991).

Trade Networks

Puget Sound peoples participated in extensive regional trade. Dentalium shells from the west coast of Vancouver Island served as currency across the entire Pacific Northwest. Obsidian from Oregon volcanic sources (Glass Buttes, Newberry Volcano) found at Puget Sound sites proves long-distance trade connections. Dried salmon, mountain goat wool, cedar products, camas, and wapato moved through networks following river corridors and cross-Sound canoe routes. The Snohomish River connected to the Snoqualmie and Skykomish valleys, providing access over the Cascades via Snoqualmie Pass and Stevens Pass to the Columbia Plateau (Suttles, 1990; Ames and Maschner, 1999).

5. Contact & Treaties

Chief Patkanim of the Snoqualmie/Snohomish

Chief Patkanim (also Pat-ka-nam) was described by early settlers as “the most powerful chief on the Sound.” He controlled territory on the Snohomish River system with influence across the Snoqualmie, Snohomish, and allied groups. His relationship with Americans was pragmatic and strategic: initially feared by settlers, he shifted to alliance during the Indian War of 1855–56, leading his warriors alongside American forces. Most controversially, he reportedly delivered the heads of enemy combatants for bounties — an act that horrified both settlers and other tribal leaders. His shifting alliances made him distrusted even as he was used (Harmon, Indians in the Making, 1998; Richards, Isaac I. Stevens: Young Man in a Hurry, 1979).

The Treaty of Point Elliott (January 22, 1855)

“A treaty is not a grant of rights to the Indians, but a grant of rights from them.”
— United States v. Winans (1905), reaffirmed in the Boldt and Rafeedie decisions

Signed at Mukilteo (bak'waltiw'), directly on the shores of Possession Sound. Approximately 2,300 Indigenous people gathered; 82 headmen signed a treaty prepared beforehand and read in an unfamiliar language. Chief Seattle (Si'ahl, c. 1786–1866), Patkanim, and leaders from numerous Puget Sound tribes were present. The treaty was not ratified until 1859.

The Tulalip Reservation was formally established by Executive Order of President Ulysses S. Grant on December 23, 1873, encompassing approximately 22,000 acres in western Snohomish County, bounded by I-5 to the east, the Snohomish River to the south, Fire Trail Road to the north, and the waters of Puget Sound (Possession Sound) to the west (Treaty of Point Elliott, 1855, in Kappler, Indian Affairs: Laws and Treaties, Vol. 2; Tulalip Tribes; GOIA).

Forced Consolidation

The treaty assigned the Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, Stillaguamish, and allied bands to a single reservation — forcing distinct peoples with their own territories, leadership, and identities onto what was essentially Snohomish territory. Many refused or resisted. Many Snoqualmie and Stillaguamish people never moved to Tulalip, remaining in their traditional territories in defiance of federal authorities. The Stillaguamish eventually achieved their own federal recognition in 1976; the Snoqualmie in 1999 (Harmon, 1998).

Chief Seattle's Speech

Chief Seattle (Si'ahl) was present at Mukilteo as a representative of the Duwamish and Suquamish. The famous “speech of Chief Seattle” is one of the most debated documents in American environmental history. Dr. Henry Smith published a version in the Seattle Sunday Star on October 29, 1887 — over 30 years after it was allegedly delivered. The most famous version, beginning “How can you buy or sell the sky?”, was written by Ted Perry for a 1972 film and is not historically authentic. Scholars agree Seattle was an important orator but the exact words cannot be recovered (Furtwangler, Answering Chief Seattle, 1997; Buerge, Chief Seattle and the Town That Took His Name, 2017).

6. Boarding Schools & Assimilation

This section documents a painful history. The boarding school era caused deep, multigenerational harm to Indigenous families and communities. It is included because it is documented and because understanding this history is essential to understanding the present.
ABSOLUTE

The Tulalip Indian Training School was established in 1857 by Father E.C. Chirouse and the Oblates of Mary Immaculate, making it one of the first Indian boarding schools in the Pacific Northwest — predating the better-known Carlisle Indian Industrial School (1879) by over two decades.

The school operated under the policy of assimilation through education. The explicit goal was to eradicate Indigenous languages, cultural practices, and identity. Children were:

  • Removed from their families, sometimes forcibly
  • Forbidden to speak Lushootseed or any Indigenous language
  • Required to wear Euro-American clothing and given Christian names
  • Subjected to corporal punishment for cultural practices
  • Indoctrinated in Christianity

The school transitioned from Catholic to Bureau of Indian Affairs control in the late 19th century. It is documented in the Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Investigative Report (U.S. Department of the Interior, Volume 1, May 2022), which identified Tulalip as one institution in a nationwide system that operated at least 408 schools across 37 states and territories.

The Loss of Language

The boarding school era and assimilation policies resulted in catastrophic language loss. By the mid-20th century, Lushootseed was critically endangered, with fluent speakers numbering in the dozens. The potlatch was formally banned in Canada from 1884 to 1951; while not formally banned in the United States, it was actively discouraged by Indian agents. Spirit dancing, traditional medicine, and traditional governance were all suppressed (Harmon, 1998; Ficken, “After the Treaties,” Pacific Northwest Quarterly, 1989).

Resistance and Preservation

Despite systematic suppression, Coast Salish peoples maintained cultural knowledge: ceremonies continued underground, oral tradition was preserved by elders, and the foundational documentation work of linguist Thom Hess with Vi Hilbert (1918–2008, Upper Skagit elder and renowned Lushootseed scholar) preserved stories, grammar, and vocabulary that are now the bedrock of revitalization programs (Hess and Hilbert, multiple publications; Hilbert, Haboo: Native American Stories from Puget Sound, 1985).

7. Dispossession: Settlement, Railroad, Industry

The Donation Land Claim Act (1850)

The Oregon Donation Land Claim Act of 1850 allowed white settlers to claim up to 320 acres of “public land” — except the land was not public. It was occupied by Indigenous peoples who had not ceded it. The Act preceded any treaties in the Puget Sound region. Settlers claimed and fenced land that tribes were still actively living on. The Act created a legal framework for dispossession that the treaties of 1854–55 retroactively attempted to formalize (White, Land Use, Environment, and Social Change, 1980).

Railroad and Industrial Impact

The Great Northern Railway reached Everett in 1893. Railroad tracks along the shoreline cut off tribal access to beaches and fishing sites. The Weyerhaeuser Company established massive lumber operations in Everett beginning in 1900, and the Snohomish River estuary was dramatically altered by mill operations. The destruction of old-growth western red cedar forests eliminated the source of the most important material in Coast Salish culture. Logging debris choked rivers, log drives scoured streambeds, erosion smothered spawning gravel, and splash dams destroyed river ecosystems (Taylor, Making Salmon, 1999; Rajala, Clearcutting the Pacific Rain Forest, 1998).

What Was Lost

The transformation of the Possession Sound shoreline from tribal territory to settler landscape involved the loss of: unrestricted access to fishing sites, shellfish beaches, and plant gathering areas; old-growth forests that provided cedar, medicinal plants, and game habitat; wetlands and estuaries (diked, filled, channelized); clean water; the cultural landscape of place names, spiritual sites, and ancestral connections; and political sovereignty — from self-governing peoples to wards of the federal government on a fraction of their territory (Harmon, 1998; Thrush, Native Seattle, 2007).

Northern Raiding and the Fortified Village

The palisade at Hibulb reflects a significant pre-contact reality: raiding by northern peoples — Haida from Haida Gwaii, Tlingit from Southeast Alaska, and Lekwiltok from northern Vancouver Island — who traveled hundreds of miles south through the Inside Passage into Puget Sound to capture slaves and plunder wealth. The most documented incident in this area was the murder and beheading of Colonel Isaac Ebey on Whidbey Island on August 11, 1857, by Kake Tlingit raiders — directly across Possession Sound (Donald, Aboriginal Slavery on the Northwest Coast, 1997; HistoryLink Essay 21277).

8. Legal Sovereignty: The Boldt & Rafeedie Decisions

The Fish Wars (1960s–1970s)

Before the Boldt Decision, tribal fishermen were routinely arrested by state game wardens for exercising treaty fishing rights. The “Fish Wars” involved fish-ins (civil disobedience modeled on civil rights sit-ins), confrontations between tribal fishermen and state enforcement, and violence against tribal fishermen — boats rammed, nets cut, fishermen beaten. Billy Frank Jr. (Nisqually, 1931–2014) was arrested over 50 times for “illegal” fishing at Frank's Landing on the Nisqually River. He was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2015 (Wilkinson, Messages from Frank's Landing, 2000).

The Boldt Decision (1974)

United States v. Washington (384 F. Supp. 312, W.D. Wash. 1974): Judge George Hugo Boldt ruled that treaty tribes were entitled to 50% of the harvestable fish passing through their usual and accustomed fishing grounds. The decision reaffirmed that treaty fishing rights were reserved rights — the tribes retained rights they had always had, ceding everything else. The Supreme Court affirmed in 1979. The Tulalip Tribes' U&A is vast, extending from South Puget Sound to the San Juan Islands and up to the Canadian border. Possession Sound falls within the U&A of at least two treaty tribes — Tulalip and Suquamish — because salmon swim through these marine waters before entering the Snohomish River (Cohen, Treaties on Trial, 1986; NWIFC).

The Rafeedie Decision (1994)

Judge Edward Rafeedie ruled that treaty language reserved shellfish harvest rights to tribes, allowing harvest of up to 50% of the harvestable surplus from all usual and accustomed places, including on private tidelands, except those “staked or cultivated” by citizens. The Supreme Court declined review in 1999. Fifteen western Washington tribes are covered, including Tulalip, Swinomish, Suquamish, Muckleshoot, Lummi, Jamestown S'Klallam, Lower Elwha Klallam, Makah, Nisqually, Nooksack, Port Gamble S'Klallam, Puyallup, Skokomish, Squaxin Island, and Upper Skagit (NWIFC; Puget Sound Institute).

Post-Boldt: Co-Management

After the Boldt Decision, tribal and state co-management became the legal framework for salmon management in Puget Sound. The Tulalip Tribes developed sophisticated fisheries management programs including hatchery operations, habitat restoration, and harvest monitoring. The Northwest Indian Fisheries Commission, led by Billy Frank Jr. from 1974 until his death in 2014, became the coordinating body for tribal fisheries management. Tribal fishing boats operate in Possession Sound today as a visible exercise of treaty rights.

9. Cross-Border Coast Salish: One Cultural World

The Oregon Treaty of 1846 established the 49th parallel as the boundary between British and American territories. This line cut directly through Coast Salish territory, separating peoples who had been a single cultural world for millennia. As anthropologist Bruce Granville Miller has documented, virtually every Coast Salish community near the border has kinship ties on the other side. These were the normal state of affairs for a people whose social world predated the border by thousands of years (Miller, “The 'Really Real' Border and the Divided Salish Community,” BC Studies 112, 1996/97).

Languages Across the Border

Lushootseed (Puget Sound) and Halkomelem (Fraser River valley) are related but not mutually intelligible — roughly comparable to the difference between English and Dutch. The Northern Straits languages (Lummi, Samish, Saanich) occupy an intermediate position. Multilingualism was common: many individuals spoke two or three Salish languages plus Chinook Jargon, the trade pidgin of the Northwest Coast (Hess, 1995; Galloway, A Grammar of Upriver Halkomelem, 1993).

The Jay Treaty (1794)

Article III of the Jay Treaty stated that Indigenous peoples could freely cross the border. In practice, this right is recognized more robustly on the US side (the Immigration and Nationality Act allows persons with at least 50% blood quantum of a race indigenous to Canada to enter freely) than on the Canadian side. The leading US case is Akins v. Saxbe (1974). Contemporary Coast Salish peoples report ongoing difficulties with border crossing: members attending funerals or ceremonies on the other side face questioning, delays, or denial of entry (Boos, McLawsen, and Fathali, Seattle Journal for Social Justice 12(2), 2014).

The Salish Sea

Washington State approved the name “Salish Sea” in 2009, British Columbia in 2010, and the US Board on Geographic Names in 2014 — formally recognizing the cultural and ecological unity of Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Strait of Georgia as one interconnected system. The name was proposed in 1988 by marine ecologist Bert Webber of Western Washington University, deliberately chosen to honor the Coast Salish peoples whose territories surround the entire waterway (Webber, 2008; Washington State Board on Geographic Names).

10. Living Nations Today

Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources

Under Executive Director Jason Gobin, the Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources Department operates extensive programs focused on the Snohomish and Stillaguamish River Basins. The Qwuloolt Estuary Restoration Project is the flagship effort — one of the largest estuary restorations in Puget Sound. On August 28, 2015, the North Ebey Slough levee was breached, allowing tidal waters to flow into the estuary for the first time in over a century. Cost: $20.5 million. By 2021, marine species had returned; 350+ bird species were observed; harbor seals, sea lions, beavers, otter, and deer populated the site (qwuloolt.org; HistoryLink Essay 21379).

Other programs include the Pilchuck River Dam Removal (completed summer 2020, restoring 37+ miles of salmon habitat, with 320 logs flown via helicopter to create log jams), the Meadowdale Beach Park and Estuary Restoration (a partnership that removed 128 linear feet of railroad embankment and restored a historic 1.3-acre estuary), and salmon hatchery operations across the basin.

Swinomish Climate and Shellfish Innovation

The Swinomish Indian Tribal Community has produced some of the most recognized tribal climate adaptation work in the nation. Their climate modeling predicts that optimal Chinook salmon habitat will reduce from 16.5% to 0.3% under a 2.2°C temperature increase. The Swinomish are constructing the first known modern clam garden — an ancient Indigenous mariculture technique (4,000+ year history) revived as a climate adaptation strategy. They are also restoring Olympia oysters (only approximately 5% of original populations remain) and successfully argued that treaty fishing rights require healthy eelgrass ecosystems, winning against the Army Corps (Swinomish Climate Change Initiative; Civil Eats, 2022; Encyclopedia of Puget Sound).

Hibulb Cultural Center

Opened August 20, 2011, the Hibulb Cultural Center and Natural History Preserve in Tulalip includes approximately 23,000 square feet of exhibit and programming space with a permanent longhouse-inspired exhibit hall. Its mission is to revive and preserve the history, culture, and traditions of the Tulalip Tribes and the tribes consolidated at Tulalip. The Center offers cultural workshops in weaving, carving, and language; educational programs; and natural history interpretation of the adjacent wetland preserve (hibulbculturalcenter.org).

Lushootseed Language Revitalization

The Tulalip Lushootseed language program is one of the most significant Coast Salish revitalization efforts, offering language classes, immersion programs, and learning materials built on Vi Hilbert's foundational documentation. Complementary programs operate at Muckleshoot, Puyallup, and other tribal communities (Tulalip Lushootseed Department).

Tribal Canoe Journeys

The Tribal Canoe Journeys began in 1989 with the Paddle to Seattle during Washington's centennial, organized by Emmett Oliver (Quinault). Participants carve and paddle traditional cedar canoes across open water following ancestral routes. The journeys explicitly include both US tribes and Canadian First Nations — Squamish, Musqueam, Tsleil-Waututh, and Sto:lo canoe families paddle alongside Tulalip, Lummi, and Swinomish families. When Tulalip hosts, canoe families arrive directly in Tulalip Bay on Possession Sound. The journeys are ceremonies, not recreation: each stop requires protocols of landing, songs, dances, and hosting that are traditional practices revitalized through the journey (NWIFC; Burke Museum).

Welcome Poles at Mukilteo (2025)

In 2025, Welcome Poles were installed at Mukilteo Lighthouse Park — near the site of the Treaty of Point Elliott signing. Carved by Joe Gobin and James Madison, both Tulalip Tribes artists, the poles represent an act of cultural reclamation: placing Coast Salish art at the treaty site, marking the tribal presence and history at a place now primarily known as a ferry terminal (City of Mukilteo and Tulalip Tribes, 2025).
