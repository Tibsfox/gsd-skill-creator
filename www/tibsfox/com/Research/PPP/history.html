1. Introduction: Three Layers of History at the Shoreline

The shoreline at Picnic Point carries at least three distinct historical strata, each visible to anyone who walks the beach at low tide. The first is Indigenous — a relationship between the Tulalip Tribes and this stretch of Puget Sound shore that predates European settlement by millennia, formalized in treaty in 1855, and sustained through ongoing participation in fisheries management and nearshore restoration today. The second is industrial — the arrival of the Great Northern Railway in the early twentieth century, which armored the coast with a seawall and created the persistent ecological barrier that still separates the bluff from the intertidal zone. The third is maritime — the Franzen family ship-breaking operation and the beached hull of the MV Pacific Queen, a vessel whose biography spans World War II, the Alaska fisheries, a fatal explosion, and a final resting place on the gravel shore north of the park.

This module documents each of these layers with attention to what can be authenticated from published sources, what requires further primary research, and where the boundaries of responsible scholarship demand restraint. The MV Pacific Queen receives the most detailed treatment because the documentary record is richest; the Tulalip cultural relationship to this shoreline receives the most careful framing because the documentary record available to outside researchers is necessarily incomplete without tribal consultation.

2. Tulalip Tribes and the Pre-Railroad Shoreline

2.1 Ancestral Territory

The shoreline at Picnic Point falls within the ancestral territory of the Tulalip Tribes. The Tulalip reservation is located to the north, near Marysville, Washington, approximately 15 miles from Picnic Point along the Puget Sound coast. The Tulalip Tribes are a federally recognized tribal government and a successor organization to the tribes and bands who signed the Point Elliott Treaty of 1855 (Tulalip Tribes, "About Us," tulaliptribes-nsn.gov).

Prior to European settlement and the construction of the railroad corridor, the eastern Puget Sound shoreline between present-day Edmonds and Everett would have been an uninterrupted stretch of coastal habitat — mixed gravel and sand beaches backed by forested bluffs, with multiple creek outlets providing freshwater inputs and salmon habitat. This type of shoreline, with its combination of forage fish spawning substrate, shellfish beds, and nearshore eelgrass, is well documented in the archaeological and ethnographic literature as having been central to Coast Salish subsistence and cultural life (Suttles, 1990, "Northwest Coast," Handbook of North American Indians, Vol. 7, Smithsonian Institution).

2.2 What the Published Record Establishes

Several facts about the Tulalip Tribes' relationship to this broader shoreline corridor are documented in publicly available sources:

  • Treaty rights: The Point Elliott Treaty of 1855, signed at Mukilteo on January 22, 1855, ceded the surrounding territory to the United States while retaining for the signatory tribes “the right of taking fish at usual and accustomed grounds and stations” and “the privilege of hunting and gathering roots and berries on open and unclaimed lands” (Point Elliott Treaty, Article V, 12 Stat. 927). The Tulalip Tribes are among the successors to the treaty signatories.
  • Active restoration partnership: The Tulalip Tribes are active partners in Puget Sound salmon recovery and nearshore restoration. This is evidenced by the installation of monitoring cameras at the Meadowdale Beach estuary restoration site, located approximately 1.5 miles south of Picnic Point, as part of the broader NOAA/Snohomish County restoration program (Snohomish County Surface Water Management, Meadowdale Beach Park Restoration Project, 2021).
  • Natural resources department: The Tulalip Tribes maintain a Natural Resources Department that participates in fisheries co-management throughout Puget Sound under the framework established by the United States v. Washington (Boldt Decision, 1974), which affirmed treaty fishing rights and established the tribes as co-managers of the fisheries resource (384 F. Supp. 312, W.D. Wash. 1974).

2.3 What the Published Record Does Not Establish

Research Gap: Primary Source Research Needed

This module does not make specific claims about Tulalip cultural practices, place names, village sites, or spiritual relationships to the Picnic Point shoreline specifically. Such knowledge may exist within Tulalip oral history, tribal archives, or unpublished cultural resource surveys, but it is not available in the published sources accessible to this research project.

Additional primary source research on the specific Tulalip cultural and historical relationship to the Picnic Point shoreline is needed. No specific Indigenous knowledge claims are made here without proper tribal consultation and attribution.

Researchers seeking to understand this dimension of Picnic Point's history should contact the Tulalip Tribes' Cultural Resources Department and the Hibulb Cultural Center (Tulalip, WA), which maintains exhibits, archives, and educational programs related to Tulalip history and culture.

This restraint is not an omission — it is a methodological boundary. The history of outside researchers extracting, misrepresenting, or decontextualizing Indigenous knowledge is well documented in the ethnographic literature. This project follows the principle that the Tulalip Tribes are the appropriate authorities on their own history and cultural relationship to this place.

3. The Point Elliott Treaty and Its Consequences

3.1 Treaty Signing at Mukilteo

The Point Elliott Treaty was signed on January 22, 1855, at the community of Mukilteo, approximately 4 miles north of Picnic Point along the Puget Sound shoreline. The treaty was negotiated by Isaac Stevens, the first governor of Washington Territory, as part of a series of treaties with tribes throughout the territory. Signatories included leaders of the Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, and other tribes and bands whose descendants are today organized as the Tulalip Tribes (Point Elliott Treaty, 12 Stat. 927, ratified March 8, 1859).

The treaty ceded a vast territory encompassing much of present-day Snohomish, King, and Skagit counties. In exchange, the signatory tribes received a reservation (the present-day Tulalip Reservation), payments, and the retention of off-reservation fishing, hunting, and gathering rights at “usual and accustomed grounds and stations” (Treaty Article V).

3.2 The Boldt Decision and Modern Co-Management

The practical meaning of those retained treaty rights was contested for over a century until Federal Judge George Hugo Boldt issued his landmark ruling in United States v. Washington (384 F. Supp. 312, W.D. Wash. 1974). The Boldt Decision affirmed that the treaty tribes were entitled to an “equal share” of the harvestable fish at their usual and accustomed fishing grounds, and that the tribes had standing as co-managers of the fisheries resource. The decision was upheld by the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals and affirmed by the U.S. Supreme Court in Washington v. Washington State Commercial Passenger Fishing Vessel Association (443 U.S. 658, 1979).

This legal framework is directly relevant to the Picnic Point shoreline because it established the treaty tribes — including the Tulalip Tribes — as co-equal partners in managing the salmon, shellfish, and other marine resources of Puget Sound. Every contemporary restoration project along this coast, including the Meadowdale Beach estuary restoration adjacent to Picnic Point, operates within the co-management framework that the Boldt Decision created.

3.3 Treaty Context for the Shoreline Corridor

The Picnic Point shoreline sits within a corridor that has been continuously significant for fisheries. Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha), listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act by NOAA Fisheries in 1999 (64 FR 14308), migrate through the nearshore waters adjacent to Picnic Point. Surf smelt (Hypomesus pretiosus) and Pacific sand lance (Ammodytes personatus) spawn on the beach substrates documented in Module A of this study. These forage fish species are foundational to the marine food web that supports the Chinook runs that the treaty tribes co-manage.

The Tulalip Tribes' participation in monitoring at the nearby Meadowdale Beach restoration site reflects the ongoing exercise of treaty-secured interests in the health of this shoreline corridor (Snohomish County Surface Water Management, 2021). The relationship is not historical in the sense of being past — it is historical in the sense of being continuous.

4. The Great Northern Railway and the Armored Coast

4.1 James J. Hill and the Northern Transcon

The railroad that today dominates the Picnic Point shoreline began as the Great Northern Railway, built by James J. Hill to connect St. Paul, Minnesota, to Puget Sound. The Great Northern completed its transcontinental mainline to Seattle in 1893, making it the northernmost transcontinental railroad in the United States. The route along the eastern shore of Puget Sound, threading between the bluffs and the tidelands from Everett south through Mukilteo and Edmonds, was a critical segment of what became known as the Northern Transcon — a term still used by BNSF Railway for this corridor (BNSF Railway, "Our Network," bnsf.com).

4.2 Reconstruction: 1907–1908

The original Great Northern alignment along the Puget Sound shoreline was reconstructed in 1907–1908. This reconstruction included the construction of a seawall along substantial portions of the route to protect the tracks from high tides, storm waves, and the relentless erosion of the glacially derived bluff sediments that characterize this coast (Schwartz et al., 1991, "Net Shore-Drift in Puget Sound," Washington Division of Geology and Earth Resources). The seawall transformed the relationship between the railroad corridor and the intertidal zone, creating a hard boundary where a gradual transition had existed before.

4.3 The Railroad as Ecological Barrier

The ecological consequences of the railroad corridor along the Puget Sound shoreline are well documented in the nearshore restoration literature. The BNSF mainline and its associated seawall, riprap, and fill created a persistent barrier between the upland bluff habitat and the intertidal zone along virtually the entire eastern shore of Puget Sound between Everett and Seattle (Clancy et al., 2009, "Management Measures for Addressing Shoreline Armoring Impacts," NOAA Technical Memorandum).

At Picnic Point, the railroad's effects include:

  • Sediment supply interruption: The seawall blocks the natural delivery of sediment from the bluff face to the beach, disrupting the drift cell dynamics that maintain spawning substrate for forage fish. The geomorphic processes affected are documented in Module C of this study and in the broader literature on Puget Sound net shore-drift (Johannessen and MacLennan, 2007, "Beaches and Bluffs of Puget Sound," Puget Sound Nearshore Partnership Technical Report 2007-04).
  • Creek passage constriction: Picnic Point Creek passes under the railroad through a culvert before reaching the beach. While the creek at Picnic Point has not been the subject of the same barrier-removal work as the Meadowdale Beach estuary restoration (where a much larger culvert replacement was completed in 2021–2023 at a cost of approximately $30 million, per Snohomish County DCNR), the constriction pattern is the same.
  • Access separation: The railroad physically separates the park's upland facilities (parking, picnic shelters, restrooms) from the beach, requiring the pedestrian overpass that is one of the park's defining features. This separation is not merely logistical — it reflects the degree to which the railroad corridor has restructured the human relationship to the shoreline.

4.4 Modern Traffic Volume

The BNSF Northern Transcon corridor through the Picnic Point area carries approximately 30 trains per day, including freight, Amtrak Cascades passenger service, and Sound Transit Sounder commuter rail (BNSF Railway; Washington State Department of Transportation, Amtrak Cascades service data). The corridor remains one of the most heavily used rail routes in the Pacific Northwest, and its presence continues to shape every aspect of shoreline access, habitat connectivity, and restoration planning along this coast.

4.5 A Century of Consequences

The 1907–1908 reconstruction is now over a century old. Its consequences — interrupted sediment supply, constricted creek passages, armored shorelines, and severed habitat connectivity — are the baseline conditions against which all modern restoration work at Picnic Point and its neighboring parks is measured. The Meadowdale Beach estuary restoration, the largest nearshore restoration project in Snohomish County history, was fundamentally an effort to undo one specific consequence of the railroad: the constriction of Lunds Gulch Creek where it passed under the BNSF tracks (Snohomish County Surface Water Management, 2021). The railroad did not merely pass through this landscape — it reorganized it.

5. Shipbreaking at Franzen Beach: The Burning Grounds

5.1 The Franzen Family Operation

The stretch of shoreline immediately north of Picnic Point Park, known as Franzen Beach or Shipwreck Point, was the site of a ship-breaking operation run by the Franzen family from the 1920s onward. The business model was straightforward: obsolete or damaged vessels were towed to the beach, stripped of their metal superstructure and fittings (which had salvage value), and the remaining wooden hulls were burned. The site is sometimes referred to as “the burning grounds” in local maritime history (Puget Sound Maritime Historical Society [PSMHS], Inside Passage blog, "Picnic Point Mystery Revisited," August 2015).

Approximately 26 ships were burned at the Franzen Beach location over the decades of the operation's existence (PSMHS, 2015). The site functioned as an informal but commercially significant component of Puget Sound's maritime recycling infrastructure — a place where vessels that had outlived their usefulness were reduced to their recoverable materials and their unrecoverable remains were left to the tides.

5.2 Historical Context: Ship-Breaking on Puget Sound

Ship-breaking was a common practice on Puget Sound throughout the first half of the twentieth century. The Sound's shipbuilding industry — concentrated at yards in Seattle, Tacoma, Bremerton, and smaller facilities throughout the region — produced a steady supply of vessels that eventually aged out of service. Wooden-hulled vessels in particular had limited service lives, and the economics of the era made beaching and burning a practical disposal method. The Franzen Beach operation was one of several such sites around Puget Sound, though few operated for as long or processed as many vessels (PSMHS, 2015).

The remnants of this industry are scattered along the Sound's shoreline. Ribs and planking from burned hulls can still be found at various locations, though most have been reduced by decades of tidal action to little more than timber fragments in the substrate. At Franzen Beach, however, one vessel survived largely intact — the MV Pacific Queen, whose wooden hull proved too massive to burn completely and too uneconomic to remove.

5.3 The Site Today

Franzen Beach is private property, posted no-trespassing. The property is not part of Picnic Point Park and is not accessible through the park. The hull of the MV Pacific Queen is visible from the park and from the beach during low-tide walks northward from the park, but visitors should observe the posted property boundaries. Additional records on the property and its history are held by the Mukilteo Historical Society (PSMHS, 2015).

6. MV Pacific Queen: A Vessel Biography

The MV Pacific Queen is the centerpiece artifact of the Picnic Point shoreline's maritime history. Her story spans the full arc of twentieth-century Puget Sound maritime life: wartime construction, civilian conversion, the Alaska fisheries, catastrophic loss, salvage, and final disposition at the burning grounds. What follows is the documented chronology of the vessel, drawn primarily from the Puget Sound Maritime Historical Society's research (PSMHS, Inside Passage blog, "Picnic Point Mystery Revisited," August 2015).

6.1 Wartime Construction

The vessel that would become the MV Pacific Queen was originally laid down as an Auxiliary Rescue and Salvage vessel (ARS class) during World War II. She was built by Colberg Boat Works of Stockton, California, a yard that produced multiple vessels for the war effort. Her official number was 257731 (PSMHS, 2015).

The vessel was initially laid down under the Lend-Lease program for Great Britain. When that order was cancelled, construction continued for use by the U.S. Navy in the Pacific Theatre. The ARS class was designed for rescue and salvage operations — recovering damaged vessels, clearing harbors, and supporting fleet logistics in forward areas. These were workhorse ships, built for durability rather than speed, with heavy frames and robust construction suited to the punishing work of towing and salvage in open ocean conditions (PSMHS, 2015).

6.2 Civilian Conversion: 1947–1949

After the war, the vessel was one of thousands of surplus military ships that entered the civilian market. Between 1947 and 1949, she was rebuilt by Puget Sound Boat Building of Tacoma, Washington, and converted from a military salvage vessel into a refrigerated cargo vessel. The conversion was substantial — the vessel was reconfigured with refrigerated holds suitable for the fish-packing trade, transforming her from a warship into a commercial fishing industry support vessel (PSMHS, 2015).

This type of conversion was common in the postwar years. The U.S. Maritime Commission and the Navy disposed of hundreds of surplus vessels through sale to civilian operators, and Puget Sound's shipyards — which had expanded enormously during the war — found a postwar market in converting these hulls for commercial use. The conversion of a rescue and salvage vessel into a fish packer was a pragmatic adaptation: the same robust construction that made the ARS class suitable for ocean salvage work also made it suitable for the demanding conditions of the Alaska fisheries.

6.3 Bristol Bay Service

Following her conversion, the vessel was acquired by Pacific Queen Fisheries of Tacoma and operated as a fish packer and processor in the Bristol Bay fishery in Alaska (PSMHS, 2015). Bristol Bay, located at the northeastern end of the Alaska Peninsula, is one of the world's most productive sockeye salmon fisheries. The fishery operates on an intense seasonal cycle — the sockeye run typically peaks in late June and July — and requires a fleet of support vessels to receive, refrigerate, and transport the catch from the fishing grounds to processing facilities.

As a fish packer, the Pacific Queen would have operated as a floating collection and refrigeration platform, anchoring in Bristol Bay during the run and receiving fish from the smaller boats that worked the fishing grounds. The vessel's 173-foot length and her refrigerated hold capacity made her well suited to this role (PSMHS, 2015; vessel length measured by Karl Elder, grandson of Arvid Franzen).

6.4 The Sinking: September 17, 1957

On September 17, 1957, the MV Pacific Queen was destroyed by a gasoline explosion and fire while moored at the Old Town Dock in Tacoma, Washington. One crew member was killed in the explosion (PSMHS, 2015).

The details of the explosion — its cause, the circumstances of the fatality, and the extent of the damage to the dock and surrounding vessels — are recorded in contemporary accounts held by the PSMHS and in Tacoma-area newspaper archives. The explosion was severe enough to sink the vessel at the dock, but not so destructive as to break up the hull entirely. This fact would prove consequential: had the hull been more severely damaged, it would not have been worth salvaging, and the vessel's story would have ended at the bottom of Commencement Bay.

6.5 Salvage and the Journey to Franzen Beach: 1958–1959

The sunken hull of the Pacific Queen was raised in 1958. She was towed to Lake Union in Seattle, where her metal superstructure was stripped for salvage. The wooden hull — still largely intact despite the explosion and sinking — was then towed north to Franzen Beach, the Franzen family's ship-breaking site north of Picnic Point, where it was beached sometime between 1958 and 1959 (PSMHS, 2015).

Arvid Franzen was the final owner of the Pacific Queen. His grandson, Karl Elder, subsequently measured the hull at 173 feet in length (PSMHS, 2015). The vessel was beached at Franzen Beach with the apparent intention of burning the wooden hull, consistent with the Franzen operation's standard practice. However, the hull was never fully consumed — whether because of its massive construction, changing economics, or shifting regulations around open burning is not documented in the available sources.

6.6 The Hull Today

The wooden hull of the MV Pacific Queen remains on private property at Franzen Beach, posted no-trespassing. The hull is visible from Picnic Point Park and from the beach during low-tide walks northward along the shoreline. Over six decades of tidal exposure, the hull has weathered but retains its basic form — a testament to the heavy construction of the original ARS-class vessel (PSMHS, 2015).

The hull is not a designated historic site. It sits on private property and is not maintained, interpreted, or protected by any government agency. Its survival is essentially accidental — the result of a wooden hull that was too robust to burn completely, on a shoreline where no one had reason to remove it. Yet it has become, through that accidental persistence, one of the most recognizable landmarks of the Picnic Point shoreline and a tangible connection to the overlapping histories of wartime shipbuilding, the Alaska fisheries, and Puget Sound's ship-breaking industry.

9. Park Development and the Pedestrian Overpass

9.1 County Acquisition

Picnic Point Park is owned and operated by Snohomish County Department of Conservation and Natural Resources (DCNR). The county acquired the property for park purposes, establishing one of the few public shoreline access points along the railroad-dominated coast between Edmonds and Mukilteo (Snohomish County DCNR, park records).

The park occupies a site where the bluff is relatively accessible and a creek corridor — Picnic Point Creek — provides a natural break in the bluff face. These topographic conditions, combined with the availability of the land, made the site suitable for park development despite the fundamental challenge of the railroad corridor separating the upland from the beach.

9.2 The Overpass as Symbol and Infrastructure

The pedestrian overpass spanning the BNSF mainline is the defining infrastructure of Picnic Point Park. Without it, the beach would be inaccessible from the parking lot except by an at-grade railroad crossing — a configuration that would be unsafe given the approximately 30 trains per day that traverse the corridor (BNSF Railway; WSDOT). The overpass resolves this safety problem, but it also makes visible the degree to which the railroad has reorganized the landscape. Every visitor crosses the overpass, and in doing so physically experiences the separation between the community on the bluff and the shoreline below.

The overpass is not merely a crossing — it is a vantage point. From its span, visitors can see north toward Shipwreck Point and the location of the Pacific Queen hull, south toward Meadowdale Beach, west across Puget Sound toward Whidbey Island and the Olympic Mountains, and down to the beach where forage fish spawn on the gravel substrate. This combination of views makes the overpass one of the most information-dense observation points on the Snohomish County shoreline.

9.3 The Park in Regional Context

Picnic Point Park sits in a shoreline corridor that includes several other public access points, each shaped by the same railroad constraint:

  • Meadowdale Beach Park (1.5 miles south): Site of the major estuary restoration project completed 2021–2023, accessed via a 1.25-mile trail through Lunds Gulch. Also requires crossing the BNSF mainline, now via a new trail underpass constructed as part of the restoration project (Snohomish County DCNR).
  • Mukilteo Lighthouse Park (4 miles north): At the Mukilteo ferry terminal, where the railroad also runs along the shoreline. The lighthouse, built in 1906, is a National Register of Historic Places site (National Park Service, NRHP).
  • Edmonds waterfront (3 miles south): Another point where the railroad, ferry terminal, and public shoreline access converge.

In each case, the Great Northern Railway's original alignment — now BNSF — is the constant infrastructure that shapes access, habitat connectivity, and the character of the shoreline experience. Picnic Point Park is not an isolated site but one node in a corridor-wide pattern of negotiated coexistence between rail infrastructure, public recreation, and nearshore ecology.

10. Convergence: Three Histories at One Shoreline

10.1 The Layered Landscape

What makes the Picnic Point shoreline historically distinctive is not any single narrative but the convergence of three. The Tulalip Tribes' relationship to this coast is the oldest and the most continuous — predating European contact, surviving the treaty era, reaffirmed by the Boldt Decision, and actively expressed today through co-management of the fisheries and participation in restoration projects like Meadowdale Beach. The railroad is the most physically dominant — a century-old infrastructure that literally armored the coast and separated the upland from the intertidal zone. The Pacific Queen is the most narratively vivid — a single vessel whose biography connects World War II shipbuilding, postwar maritime economy, the Alaska fisheries, and the regional ship-breaking industry.

10.2 What the Landscape Preserves

Each of these histories has left physical evidence at the shoreline:

  • Treaty-era continuity: The Tulalip Tribes continue to exercise treaty-secured rights in these waters and participate as co-managers and restoration partners. Their monitoring cameras at Meadowdale Beach are contemporary evidence of a relationship to this shoreline that the treaty formalized but did not create.
  • Railroad infrastructure: The seawall, the tracks, the culvert under the railroad, and the pedestrian overpass are all physical artifacts of the 1907–1908 reconstruction and its successive modifications. They are also the primary constraint on every ecological restoration effort along the corridor.
  • Maritime remains: The hull of the Pacific Queen is the most visible of the approximately 26 vessels beached and burned at Franzen Beach. Its persistence as a recognizable hull form, six decades after beaching, is an artifact of the heavy wartime construction that characterized the ARS class.

10.3 Unresolved Questions

This module has documented what the available published sources establish. Several important questions remain unresolved and would benefit from further research:

  • What specific Tulalip place names, if any, are associated with the Picnic Point shoreline? The Hibulb Cultural Center (Tulalip, WA) would be the appropriate starting point for this inquiry.
  • What are the environmental consequences of the approximately 26 ship-burnings at Franzen Beach? Ship-breaking sites of this era are known to leave persistent contamination (heavy metals, petroleum residues, anti-fouling compounds) in shoreline sediments. No published environmental assessment of the Franzen Beach site was identified in this research.
  • What is the structural condition of the Pacific Queen hull, and what is its likely trajectory of deterioration? The hull sits in the intertidal zone and is subject to ongoing tidal, biological, and mechanical weathering. No engineering assessment of the hull's condition was identified.
  • What additional documentary records exist in the Mukilteo Historical Society's holdings regarding the Franzen operation and the vessels processed at the site?
