1. Introduction: Restoration Context at Picnic Point

Picnic Point Park sits within one of the most heavily modified stretches of the eastern Puget Sound shoreline. The BNSF Railway mainline, constructed in the late nineteenth century along the base of coastal bluffs between Edmonds and Everett, fundamentally altered the relationship between upland forests, creek outlets, and nearshore habitat. Armored railroad berms replaced natural beach transitions. Culverts replaced open stream mouths. The drift cells that once moved sediment freely along shore were interrupted by riprap and seawalls.

Against this backdrop of industrial-era modification, the restoration work at Picnic Point Park represents one thread in a broader regional effort to recover ecological function along Puget Sound's urbanized nearshore. The park's restoration trajectory is shaped by three overlapping programs: the Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project (HFP), a countywide urban forestry initiative launched in 2019; the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds (OFF), a conservation-oriented angling organization that adopted Picnic Point as a focal site beginning in 2023; and the lessons drawn from the nearby Meadowdale Beach Park estuary restoration, completed in 2022, which demonstrated what large-scale habitat recovery looks like when federal, state, local, tribal, and private partners align around a single salmon-bearing creek mouth.

This module inventories restoration activities at and near Picnic Point Park from 2019 through 2026, documents the shellfish harvest closure that signals ongoing water quality challenges, and examines Meadowdale Beach as a comparative model for what comprehensive nearshore restoration can achieve. All numerical values, event dates, and ecological claims are attributed to named sources. Where data gaps exist, they are noted explicitly.

2. The Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project

2.1 Origins and Model

The Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project was launched in 2019 as a partnership between Snohomish County and Forterra, a regional land conservation organization headquartered in Seattle (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra). The program was modeled on Forterra's Green City Partnership, an urban forestry framework that was, by 2019, active in 14 Puget Sound communities (Forterra, Green City Partnership program documentation). The Green City Partnership model emphasizes community-based stewardship of urban and suburban forested lands, combining professional ecological assessment with volunteer labor for invasive species removal, native planting, and long-term site monitoring.

Snohomish County dedicated $130,000 to the Healthy Forest Project in its launch year (Snohomish County DCNR, 2019 budget allocation). This initial investment funded staff coordination, ecological site assessments, volunteer event logistics, plant materials, and partnership development with Forterra. The funding model reflected a philosophy common to Puget Sound urban forestry programs: that public investment in restoration infrastructure—professional staff, volunteer training, plant procurement—could be leveraged through community labor to achieve restoration outcomes at scales that professional crews alone could not sustain.

2.2 Phase 1 Pilot: 10 Sites, 1,000 Acres

The HFP's initial phase identified 10 pilot sites across Snohomish County, comprising approximately 1,000 acres of county-managed forest and parkland (Snohomish County DCNR, Healthy Forest Project 20-Year Plan, 2022). Site selection prioritized parcels adjacent to salmon-bearing streams or key water bodies, reflecting the program's dual mandate: forest health and aquatic habitat protection. Picnic Point Park was designated as one of these 10 pilot sites (Snohomish County DCNR).

The 10-site pilot structure served multiple functions. It created a geographically distributed network of restoration sites that could demonstrate replicable methods across different forest types and land-use contexts. It generated baseline ecological data against which future restoration progress could be measured. And it established community entry points—places where volunteers could engage in hands-on restoration work close to their neighborhoods.

2.3 Primary Restoration Activities

The HFP's restoration activities at its pilot sites, including Picnic Point Park, center on four core practices (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra):

Activity Description Target Species / Conditions
Invasive plant removal Manual cutting, pulling, and in some cases targeted herbicide application to eliminate non-native species that suppress native forest regeneration Himalayan blackberry (Rubus armeniacus), English ivy (Hedera helix), Scotch broom (Cytisus scoparius), and other regionally significant invasives
Native plant installation Planting locally sourced native trees, shrubs, groundcovers, and herbaceous species in areas cleared of invasives Conifer and deciduous species appropriate to site conditions (moisture, elevation, aspect, soil type)

These four activities—removal, planting, mulching, and watering—constitute a standard restoration cycle used across Puget Sound urban forestry programs. The cycle reflects ecological reality: invasive removal without replanting creates bare soil that invasives rapidly recolonize; planting without mulching and watering results in high mortality during the region's increasingly warm and dry summers. Each activity depends on the others.

2.4 Governance Transition: 2023

In 2023, the HFP underwent a governance transition. Program direction shifted from a co-managed Forterra-county model to one primarily led by Snohomish County staff, while Forterra was retained as a partner (Snohomish County DCNR, 2023). This transition reflected the program's maturation: county staff had developed sufficient in-house expertise—in site assessment, volunteer management, and restoration planning—to take a lead role. The shift also aligned with a broader pattern observed across Green City Partnership communities, where local governments gradually internalize the capacity that Forterra initially provides.

The governance change did not alter the program's field-level activities. Invasive removal, native planting, mulching, and watering continued at pilot sites. What changed was the locus of planning authority and event coordination, which moved from a shared Forterra-county model to a county-led structure with Forterra in an advisory and support role.

2.5 The 20-Year Plan

In 2022, the HFP released its 20-Year Plan, a strategic document outlining restoration goals, site prioritization, and scaling strategies for extending the program beyond its initial 10-site pilot (Snohomish County DCNR, Healthy Forest Project 20-Year Plan, 2022). The 20-Year Plan represents the county's long-term commitment to urban forest health and positions the HFP as a permanent element of county land management rather than a time-limited grant-funded initiative.

The plan's significance for Picnic Point Park is that it provides institutional continuity: the park's restoration work is not dependent on a single funding cycle or partnership agreement but is embedded within a multi-decade county program with dedicated staff positions and recurring budget allocations.

4. Picnic Point as an HFP Pilot Site

Picnic Point Park's selection as one of the HFP's 10 Phase 1 pilot sites reflects the park's ecological profile: it contains a salmon-bearing creek (Picnic Point Creek) that flows through a forested riparian corridor before crossing beneath the BNSF railroad to discharge into Puget Sound (Snohomish County DCNR). The park's upland forest, creek corridor, and shoreline represent a compressed transect from ridgetop conifer canopy through riparian deciduous forest to intertidal beach—a gradient that, when ecologically intact, supports nutrient cycling, bank stability, sediment filtration, stream temperature regulation, and habitat connectivity for fish, birds, and invertebrates.

The primary restoration challenge at Picnic Point, as at other HFP pilot sites, is invasive plant dominance in the forest understory and riparian zone. Himalayan blackberry (Rubus armeniacus) and English ivy (Hedera helix) are the two most significant invasive species at the site (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra). Himalayan blackberry forms dense, thorny thickets that exclude native shrubs, suppress conifer regeneration, and reduce structural diversity in the forest understory. English ivy climbs native trees, adding weight that increases windthrow risk, shading out canopy foliage, and eventually killing host trees.

Restoration at Picnic Point follows the standard HFP cycle: invasive removal in the fall and winter when plants are dormant or recently cut material is less likely to reroot; native planting in the late fall through early spring when soil moisture is high and transplant stress is minimized; mulching immediately after planting; and supplemental watering through the first several dry seasons. The creek-mouth area, where Picnic Point Creek meets the beach, has received particular attention from the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds partnership, as described in Section 5.

5. Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds Partnership (2023–2026)

The Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds (OFF) is a conservation-oriented fly fishing club based in Edmonds, Washington. Beginning in 2023, OFF adopted Picnic Point Park as a focal site for community restoration work, organizing volunteer events in coordination with Snohomish County and the Healthy Forest Project (Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds). Over the period from 2023 through 2026, OFF organized a total of 9 community restoration events at the park, drawing hundreds of participants across all events (Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds).

The partnership between OFF and Snohomish County exemplifies a model increasingly common in Puget Sound restoration: organized community groups—often with specific ecological interests such as fisheries conservation—partner with county land managers to provide sustained volunteer labor at specific sites. The arrangement benefits both parties. The county gains a reliable, motivated volunteer workforce with a vested interest in the site's ecological health. The community group gains structured access to public land for restoration work that advances its conservation mission.

5.1 Conservation Focus: Creek-Mouth Habitat

OFF's restoration work at Picnic Point has emphasized the creek-mouth zone where Picnic Point Creek discharges to the beach. This focus reflects the organization's fisheries orientation: the creek mouth is the transition point between freshwater and marine environments, a zone of particular importance for juvenile salmon and trout moving between rearing habitat in the creek and forage habitat in Puget Sound's nearshore. Improving vegetation cover, reducing invasive plant dominance, and installing native species at the creek mouth directly supports the habitat conditions that salmonids depend on during this critical life-stage transition.

5.2 Volunteer Demographics

OFF reports a volunteer age range of 3 to 83 years across its Picnic Point events (Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds). This breadth reflects intentional outreach to families, retirees, and young people, not just the club's active fly fishing membership. Multi-generational participation serves both practical and social functions: it expands the labor pool available for physically demanding tasks like blackberry removal, and it builds community connection to the park that sustains long-term stewardship beyond any single event.

6.1 The March 2025 Shoreline Planting in Detail

The March 1, 2025 event is the best-documented single restoration action at Picnic Point Park. Forty volunteers installed 89 native plant species at the shoreline and creek-mouth area (Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds). The species count—89—refers to individual native plants installed, representing a mix of trees, shrubs, and groundcovers selected for the site's specific conditions: salt-influenced air, seasonal flooding at the creek mouth, well-drained beach substrates, and exposure to southwesterly winds off Puget Sound.

The event's description as "creek-mouth habitat improvement" indicates that planting locations were concentrated at the interface where Picnic Point Creek meets the beach—the zone most directly relevant to salmonid habitat. Native vegetation at creek mouths provides shade (reducing water temperature at the freshwater-saltwater transition), root-bound bank stabilization (reducing erosion and sedimentation), leaf litter input (supporting the invertebrate food web), and overhead cover (reducing predation risk for juvenile fish).

6.2 The April 2026 City Nature Challenge Bioblitz

The April 25, 2026 event at Picnic Point Park is part of the City Nature Challenge (CNC), an annual global biodiversity documentation initiative co-led by the California Academy of Sciences and the Natural History Museums of Los Angeles County (California Academy of Sciences; Natural History Museums of LA County). The 2026 event marks the CNC's 10th year (California Academy of Sciences). In the most recently completed CNC cycle, participants globally recorded more than 3 million observations and identified more than 73,000 species (California Academy of Sciences).

The bioblitz format—intensive, time-bounded biodiversity surveying by community participants using standardized digital tools—generates baseline species occurrence data that can be compared across years. For Picnic Point Park, the 2026 bioblitz will produce the first systematic, publicly accessible biodiversity inventory of the park, documenting plant, animal, and fungal species present across the site's upland forest, riparian corridor, and beach habitats. This data will also serve as a post-restoration benchmark: species documented in 2026 can be compared against future surveys to assess whether restoration activities are measurably increasing biodiversity at the site.

Helena Puche, Conservation Chair of the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds, framed the bioblitz in terms of restoration monitoring: the event provides "living records of life on Earth" and an opportunity to "evaluate the park's health" (Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds, April 2026). This language positions the bioblitz not merely as a public engagement activity but as a data-collection instrument with direct relevance to restoration assessment.

7. Community Stewardship and Forest Stewards

A structural element of the Healthy Forest Project is the Forest Steward program. The HFP recruits community members to serve as Forest Stewards—trained volunteer leaders who organize and lead local restoration events at specific pilot sites (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra, Green City Partnership model). Forest Stewards represent the HFP's mechanism for distributing leadership beyond county staff: rather than relying on paid professionals to organize every event, the program develops community members who can independently plan, schedule, and lead volunteer work parties at their adopted sites.

The Forest Steward model has several implications for long-term restoration success:

  • Continuity: Forest Stewards who live near their sites provide year-round awareness of site conditions—invasive regrowth, vandalism, irrigation failures, storm damage—that periodic staff visits might miss.
  • Scalability: Each trained Forest Steward multiplies the program's capacity to host events without proportional increases in staff time.
  • Community investment: Stewards develop personal connections to their sites that sustain engagement over years and sometimes decades, outlasting any single program cycle.
  • Knowledge transfer: Stewards train new volunteers in species identification, tool use, and restoration techniques, creating a self-reinforcing learning loop within the community.

Specific data on the number of active Forest Stewards at Picnic Point Park, or their individual contributions, is not available in current public reporting. This is noted as a research gap.

8. Shellfish Closure Analysis

GATE Public health notice: Shellfish harvesting at Picnic Point is subject to a year-round closure. Confirm current closure status with WDFW and DOH before any foraging activity.

8.1 Closure Status

The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) and the Washington State Department of Health (DOH) maintain a year-round shellfish harvest closure at the Picnic Point shoreline (WDFW; DOH). This closure prohibits the recreational and commercial harvest of all shellfish species—clams, mussels, oysters—from the affected beach area.

8.2 Closure Rationale

Shellfish closures in Puget Sound are typically triggered by water quality conditions that render shellfish unsafe for human consumption. Shellfish are filter feeders: they concentrate bacteria, viruses, biotoxins, and chemical contaminants from surrounding water in their tissues at levels that can exceed safe consumption thresholds even when ambient water concentrations appear low. The most common triggers for year-round (as opposed to seasonal or biotoxin-related) closures in Puget Sound are elevated fecal coliform bacteria levels, which indicate contamination from human or animal waste sources (DOH, Shellfish Program).

At Picnic Point, the probable driver of the year-round closure is stormwater runoff and associated water quality degradation (WDFW; DOH). The park's shoreline receives surface water inputs from Picnic Point Creek and from the broader developed landscape upslope—residential neighborhoods, roads, and impervious surfaces that generate stormwater carrying bacteria, nutrients, heavy metals, petroleum compounds, and other contaminants into the creek system and ultimately into the nearshore marine environment.

8.3 Data Gap: Site-Specific Contaminant Data

A significant research gap exists regarding the specific contaminants driving the shellfish closure at Picnic Point. While the general mechanism—stormwater-delivered contamination—is well established for Puget Sound nearshore closures, site-specific contaminant data for the Picnic Point shoreline are absent from current public reporting (research gap identified). The following questions remain unanswered:

  • What are the measured fecal coliform levels in Picnic Point Creek and the adjacent nearshore during wet-weather and dry-weather conditions?
  • What are the specific point and nonpoint pollution sources contributing to the closure—failing septic systems, pet waste, agricultural runoff, wildlife, or stormwater infrastructure deficiencies?
  • Has the closure zone been re-evaluated in light of upstream development changes or stormwater management improvements?
  • Are there heavy metal, PAH (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon), or other chemical contaminants in shellfish tissue at the site, beyond the bacterial indicators that typically trigger closures?

Without site-specific water quality and shellfish tissue data, it is not possible to determine whether the closure reflects a persistent, worsening, or potentially improving contamination trajectory. This data gap limits the ability to connect restoration activities—such as riparian buffer improvement and stormwater management—to measurable water quality outcomes at the shellfish harvest level.

8.4 Connection to Restoration

The shellfish closure is directly relevant to the restoration work documented in this module. Riparian buffer restoration along Picnic Point Creek—the kind of work that both the HFP and OFF are conducting—can reduce bacterial and sediment loading to the creek by filtering surface runoff through vegetated soil before it reaches the stream channel. Native riparian vegetation increases infiltration, reduces erosion, and provides biological uptake of nutrients. Over multi-decade timescales, comprehensive riparian restoration can measurably improve downstream water quality.

However, the relationship between upstream restoration and downstream shellfish closure status is complex and indirect. Shellfish closures are managed by DOH based on water quality monitoring data that must meet federal standards (National Shellfish Sanitation Program) before closures can be lifted. Even substantial improvements in riparian condition may not be sufficient to achieve shellfish-safe water quality if other contamination sources—such as failing septic systems or stormwater infrastructure—remain unaddressed. The absence of site-specific monitoring data (noted above) makes it impossible to evaluate whether restoration at Picnic Point is currently contributing to water quality improvement at a rate that might eventually support closure reassessment.

9. Meadowdale Beach Park Estuary Restoration: Comparative Model

Meadowdale Beach Park is located approximately 1.5 miles south of Picnic Point Park along the same stretch of Puget Sound shoreline. Both parks share fundamental geographic and infrastructural characteristics: they sit at the mouths of salmon-bearing creeks, they are separated from their upland access points by the BNSF Railway mainline, and they occupy narrow strips of beach between railroad embankment and tidal water. Meadowdale's completed estuary restoration project provides the most directly relevant comparative model for understanding what large-scale habitat recovery looks like at a site with these shared constraints.

9.2 The Culvert-to-Bridge Transformation

The central engineering achievement of the Meadowdale restoration was the replacement of a 6-foot concrete culvert—the structure through which Lunds Gulch Creek passed beneath the BNSF railroad tracks—with a 100-foot bridge opening (Snohomish County). The original culvert constrained the creek to a narrow, high-velocity channel that was impassable for most fish species and prevented tidal exchange between Puget Sound and the estuary on the inland side of the tracks. The new 100-foot bridge opening allows the creek to spread across a natural floodplain, restores tidal influence to the estuary, and provides unobstructed fish passage for salmon and trout migrating between the creek and Puget Sound.

The scale difference—6 feet to 100 feet—captures the magnitude of the ecological change. A 6-foot culvert treats a creek as a plumbing problem to be routed under an obstacle. A 100-foot bridge treats the creek as an ecosystem that needs space to function. The restored 1.3 acres of estuary habitat downstream of the bridge represent the physical space that this functional change created (Snohomish County).

9.3 Species Outcomes

Four salmonid species directly benefit from the Meadowdale restoration: Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha), listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act by NOAA Fisheries; Chum salmon (Oncorhynchus keta); Coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch); and Cutthroat trout (Oncorhynchus clarkii) (Snohomish County; NOAA Fisheries). All four species use the Lunds Gulch Creek system for spawning, rearing, or migration, and all four were impeded or blocked by the original 6-foot culvert.

The inclusion of ESA-listed Chinook salmon among the benefited species is significant for several reasons. Chinook recovery is a federal obligation under the Endangered Species Act. Chinook are the primary prey species of the Southern Resident Orca population (also ESA-listed as endangered), linking Meadowdale's creek restoration to marine ecosystem recovery at the apex predator level. And Chinook recovery is a central goal of the Puget Sound Salmon Recovery Plan, the regional framework that coordinates and prioritizes habitat restoration investments across the Sound's 16 watersheds (NOAA Fisheries; Puget Sound Partnership).

10. Engineering Innovation: Railroad Crossings and Salmon Passage

The Meadowdale restoration is described as the first Puget Sound project to widen a stream crossing under operating railroad tracks (Snohomish County). This distinction is technically significant: BNSF's mainline between Seattle and Everett is one of the busiest freight and passenger rail corridors on the West Coast, carrying Amtrak Cascades passenger service, BNSF freight trains, and Sound Transit commuter rail. Any construction affecting the trackbed must be engineered and sequenced to maintain train operations throughout—a constraint that dramatically increases project complexity and cost.

BNSF Railway participated as a collaborative partner in the project (Snohomish County). This collaboration was essential: the railroad owns the trackbed and right-of-way, and no modification to the stream crossing could proceed without BNSF's engineering review, operational coordination, and legal agreement. The successful BNSF partnership at Meadowdale established a precedent for future railroad-crossing restoration projects along the Puget Sound rail corridor.

U.S. Senator Maria Cantwell characterized the project in terms of this precedent: "This project is a blueprint for how federal, local, and private partners can work together to restore shore habitat along rail lines while keeping trains running" (U.S. Senator Maria Cantwell). The statement frames Meadowdale not merely as a single-site restoration but as a replicable model for addressing the dozens of undersized culverts that impede fish passage along the BNSF corridor throughout the Puget Sound region.

10.1 Relevance to Picnic Point

The Meadowdale precedent is directly relevant to Picnic Point Park, where Picnic Point Creek also passes beneath the BNSF mainline via a culvert. While the current module does not contain engineering assessments of the Picnic Point culvert's fish passage capacity, the Meadowdale model demonstrates that culvert replacement with wider bridge openings is technically feasible under operating railroad conditions, and that the funding and partnership frameworks necessary for such projects exist and have been successfully assembled in the immediate geographic vicinity.

Whether a Meadowdale-scale intervention is warranted at Picnic Point depends on ecological assessments that fall outside this module's scope: the current fish passage condition of the Picnic Point culvert, the upstream habitat capacity of Picnic Point Creek, and the cost-benefit analysis of culvert replacement relative to other restoration investments in the Snohomish County salmon recovery portfolio. These are noted as research gaps.

12. Tulalip Tribes: Monitoring and Co-Management

GATE Indigenous knowledge boundary: All Tulalip-attributed content in this section is drawn from published sources. Tribal consultation was not conducted for this research document.

The Tulalip Tribes installed monitoring cameras at the Meadowdale Beach Park restoration site as part of the project's 10-year post-completion estuary monitoring program (Snohomish County; Tulalip Tribes). These cameras provide fish monitoring data—documenting species presence, run timing, and behavior at the restored creek-mouth crossing—that contributes to the long-term assessment of restoration effectiveness.

The Tulalip Tribes' involvement in Meadowdale monitoring reflects the tribes' broader role in salmon recovery and natural resource co-management in the Snohomish River watershed and adjacent marine waters. Under the Boldt Decision (1974) and subsequent case law, western Washington treaty tribes hold co-management authority over fisheries resources. This legal framework means that the Tulalip Tribes are not merely stakeholders or advisors in restoration projects that affect salmon habitat—they are co-managers with legal standing and technical capacity.

12.1 Research Gap: Tulalip Engagement at Picnic Point

The extent of Tulalip Tribes involvement in restoration planning, monitoring, or stewardship specifically at Picnic Point Park is not documented in the sources available for this research module. This constitutes a research gap. Picnic Point lies within the Tulalip Tribes' traditional territory and within the geographic scope of their treaty-reserved resource rights. Any comprehensive restoration planning for the site—particularly work affecting Picnic Point Creek's fish passage or nearshore habitat—would appropriately involve tribal consultation and, potentially, tribal co-management of monitoring programs similar to those established at Meadowdale.

This gap is noted without presumption: the absence of documentation does not mean the absence of engagement. It means that this research module, relying on publicly available sources, cannot characterize the nature or extent of tribal involvement at Picnic Point.

13. Invasive Species Pressure and Removal Strategies

The two primary invasive plant species targeted by HFP and OFF restoration work at Picnic Point Park are Himalayan blackberry (Rubus armeniacus) and English ivy (Hedera helix) (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra). Both species are classified as Class C noxious weeds by the Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board, indicating that they are widespread in the state and that control is recommended but not required by law (Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board).

13.1 Himalayan Blackberry

Himalayan blackberry is the dominant invasive plant in western Washington lowland forests, riparian corridors, and disturbed areas (Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board). The species was introduced to the Pacific Northwest in the late nineteenth century as a cultivated berry crop and has since colonized virtually every disturbed site below 500 meters elevation in the Puget Sound lowlands. At Picnic Point, blackberry thickets occupy understory and edge habitat, particularly along the riparian corridor and at forest openings.

Removal strategies for Himalayan blackberry at HFP sites include manual cutting of canes during the dormant season (November through February), excavation of root crowns where feasible, and in some cases targeted herbicide application to cut stumps to prevent regrowth (Snohomish County DCNR; Forterra). Blackberry's vigorous vegetative reproduction from root fragments and its persistent seed bank mean that removal is not a one-time event but a multi-year commitment requiring 3 to 5 or more annual retreatments to exhaust the root system and deplete the seed bank.

13.2 English Ivy

English ivy poses a different but equally significant threat to forest health at Picnic Point. Unlike blackberry, which dominates edge and disturbed habitats, ivy invades intact forest interiors, climbing mature trees and forming dense ground mats that exclude native seedlings (Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board). On trees, ivy adds weight that increases windthrow risk, harbors rats and other pest species, and eventually shades out the tree's own canopy, leading to decline and death. On the ground, ivy mats suppress the native shrub and herbaceous layer that provides food and habitat for birds, insects, and small mammals.

Ivy removal involves cutting vines at the base of infested trees (a technique called "lifesaving"—severing the vine's connection to its root system so that the aerial portion dies in place) and hand-pulling ground mats. As with blackberry, ivy removal requires sustained effort over multiple years because root fragments left in the soil can regenerate and recolonize treated areas.

13.3 Other Invasive Species

While Himalayan blackberry and English ivy are the two species most frequently cited in HFP documentation for Picnic Point, other regionally significant invasive plants are likely present at the site. Scotch broom (Cytisus scoparius), herb Robert (Geranium robertianum), and various invasive grasses are common co-occurring species in Puget Sound lowland forests (Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board). A comprehensive invasive species inventory for Picnic Point Park has not been published in the sources available for this module. The April 2026 City Nature Challenge bioblitz (see Section 6) may provide the first systematic documentation of invasive species presence and distribution at the site.

14. Native Plant Installation and Riparian Recovery

Native plant installation is the constructive counterpart to invasive removal: after non-native species are cleared, native plants are installed to occupy the opened space before invasives can recolonize. The HFP and OFF have conducted native planting at Picnic Point Park as part of their restoration cycles, with the March 2025 shoreline planting (89 native species installed; Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds) providing the most specific documented instance.

14.1 Species Selection Principles

Native plant selection for Puget Sound restoration sites follows principles established by regional nurseries, conservation districts, and programs such as the Green City Partnership (Forterra). Key selection criteria include:

  • Provenance: Plants grown from seed or cuttings collected within the local seed zone (Puget Sound lowlands, typically within 50 miles and at similar elevation) to ensure genetic adaptation to local climate and soil conditions.
  • Site matching: Species selected for compatibility with specific site conditions—moisture regime, light availability, soil type, salt exposure, and aspect. Creek-mouth plantings at Picnic Point require salt-tolerant species that can withstand periodic tidal influence.
  • Structural diversity: A mix of canopy trees, understory trees, shrubs, and groundcovers that recreates the multi-layered forest structure of native Puget Sound lowland forests.
  • Ecological function: Species that provide specific habitat functions—stream shading (overhanging trees), bank stabilization (deep-rooted shrubs), food resources (berry-producing species), and nesting habitat (dense-branching evergreens).

14.3 Planting Survival and Establishment

Restoration planting success is measured by survival rates in the years following installation. In Puget Sound lowland restoration projects, typical first-year survival rates for properly installed native plants range from 60% to 85%, with higher survival observed in riparian sites with reliable moisture and lower survival on well-drained upland slopes during dry summers (Forterra; Snohomish Conservation District). The HFP's emphasis on mulching and supplemental watering addresses the primary cause of planting mortality in the region: summer drought stress during the 2- to 4-month dry season (approximately late June through mid-October).

Survival data specific to Picnic Point Park plantings have not been published. The September 2025 fall cleanup event organized by OFF (see Section 6) includes assessment of spring planting survival as one of its activities, suggesting that at least informal survival monitoring is being conducted. Formal, published survival monitoring data would strengthen the restoration record for the site.
