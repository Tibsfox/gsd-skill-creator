1. Glacial History of the Puget Lowland

The geology of Picnic Point Park is inseparable from the history of continental glaciation. The bluffs, beaches, and sediments that define this shoreline were deposited, compressed, and sculpted by the advance and retreat of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet during the late Pleistocene. Understanding the park's coastal processes begins with this glacial legacy.

1.1 The Fraser Glaciation

The most recent major glaciation to affect the Puget Lowland was the Fraser Glaciation, which lasted from approximately 25,000 to 10,000 years before present (BP). Within this broader glacial episode, the Vashon Stade (approximately 18,000–13,000 BP) represents the period of maximum ice advance into the lowland, according to chronologies established by the U.S. Geological Survey (Booth, 1994; Booth et al., 2003). During this interval, the Puget Lobe of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet advanced southward from British Columbia, filling the Puget Lowland with ice that reached thicknesses estimated at approximately 1,000 meters (3,300 feet) over the modern Seattle area (Thorson, 1980; USGS).

This enormous mass of ice fundamentally shaped the landscape. The weight of the glacier depressed the underlying crust, carved the deep north-south trending troughs that now form Puget Sound's basins, and deposited vast quantities of sediment in distinct stratigraphic layers. The Puget Sound shoreline between Seattle and Everett, including the reach at Picnic Point, is characterized by bluffs composed of these glacially derived, unconsolidated deposits (WDFW, Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources).

1.3 Isostatic Rebound and Marine Incursion

As the Vashon ice retreated northward between approximately 15,000 and 13,000 years BP, the depressed crust had not yet rebounded to its pre-glacial elevation. This allowed marine waters to flood into the lowland, depositing the Everson glaciomarine drift—a distinctive unit of fossiliferous clay and silt that records the temporary marine incursion into the isostatically depressed Puget Lowland (Easterbrook, 1986; USGS). As the crust slowly rebounded over the following millennia, the shoreline retreated to approximately its modern position, leaving these marine deposits stranded at elevations well above current sea level.

This sequence—glacial advance, maximum ice cover, retreat, marine flooding, and isostatic recovery—created the complex, layered bluffs that are now exposed along the Puget Sound shore. At Picnic Point, the coastal bluffs display these layers as a geological cross-section, with each unit possessing different engineering properties, permeability, and erosion characteristics.

2. Bluff Stratigraphy: The Geological Record Exposed

The coastal bluffs of the Seattle-to-Everett corridor, including those at and near Picnic Point, typically expose a characteristic stratigraphic sequence of unconsolidated glacial deposits. These are not bedrock cliffs but rather stacked layers of loose to moderately consolidated sediment, each with distinct physical properties that govern how the bluff erodes and how water moves through it (USGS, Booth et al., 2003; WDFW, Marine Shoreline Design Guidelines).

2.2 Hydrological Significance of Layering

The alternation of permeable and impermeable layers within the bluff creates a perched water table system that is central to understanding bluff erosion. Precipitation infiltrates through the relatively porous Vashon till cap and percolates downward through the highly permeable advance outwash sands. When this groundwater reaches the underlying low-permeability lacustrine clay layer, it is forced laterally toward the bluff face, emerging as seeps and springs at the contact between the two units (Tubbs, 1974; USGS).

These seepage zones are visible on many Puget Sound bluff faces as lines of wet, darker sediment, often colonized by mosses and ferns. They are also the loci of a distinctive erosion mechanism: the saturated outwash sands lose cohesion and ravel outward, undercutting the denser till above. Eventually, the unsupported till fails in large block slides or rotational slumps. This process, described in detail by the USGS in landslide hazard mapping for the Seattle-Everett corridor, is the primary mechanism of bluff retreat along this reach of Puget Sound (Baum et al., 2000; USGS).

Erosion cycle: Precipitation infiltrates till → percolates through outwash sands → perches on lacustrine clay → emerges as springs at bluff face → outwash ravels and pipes → till is undercut → block failure delivers sediment to beach. This natural cycle is the fundamental sediment supply mechanism for Puget Sound beaches (Shipman, 2004, WDFW).

2.3 Bluff Height and Exposure

Bluff heights along the Picnic Point reach of shoreline are representative of the broader Seattle-to-Everett corridor. The park's upland area sits at approximately 24 meters (79 feet) above sea level, as recorded by Snohomish County geographic data. Bluff faces in this reach commonly expose heights of 15 to 30 meters (50 to 100 feet) of unconsolidated material, according to USGS topographic mapping. The full stratigraphic sequence may or may not be visible at any given point, depending on local erosion history, vegetation cover, and the geometry of the bluff face.

4. Drift Cell Dynamics

Puget Sound's beaches are not continuous, uniform features. They are organized into discrete sediment transport units called drift cells (also termed "littoral cells" or "sediment compartments")—semi-closed systems in which sediment eroded from one location is transported along the shore by wave-driven currents and deposited at another. Drift cells are the fundamental organizing unit of Puget Sound's nearshore geomorphology, and understanding them is essential for interpreting the ecological and geological conditions at Picnic Point (Johannessen and MacLennan, 2007, WDFW/USGS; Shipman, 2004, WDFW).

4.1 Anatomy of a Drift Cell

A typical Puget Sound drift cell consists of three functional zones, as described in the WDFW/USGS Puget Sound nearshore technical literature:

Zone Process Geomorphic Expression Ecological Role
Divergence Zone (source) Bluff erosion supplies sediment; net transport initiates in both directions or one direction Eroding bluffs, narrow or absent upper beach, exposed till or outwash at wave base Limited spawning habitat due to coarse or insufficient substrate
Transport Zone (pathway) Longshore drift carries sediment downdrift via wave-driven currents Continuous beach, sediment sorting by grain size along transport path Transitional habitat quality; substrate composition changes downdrift
Accretion Zone (sink) Net sediment deposition; drift energy dissipates Spits, cuspate forelands, broad upper beaches, bars Preferred sand lance and surf smelt spawning habitat (WDFW)

Source: Johannessen and MacLennan (2007); Shipman (2004), WDFW; Finlayson (2006), USGS.

4.3 The Importance of Drift Cell Integrity

The drift cell concept has profound implications for shoreline management and nearshore ecology. Because drift cells are semi-closed systems, any interruption of the sediment supply at the source (eroding bluffs) or the transport pathway (longshore drift) will reduce sediment delivery to the accretion zone at the downdrift end. The consequences of such interruptions are well-documented in the WDFW and USGS nearshore literature:

  • Reduced beach width: Less sediment arriving at downdrift beaches causes them to narrow and coarsen, reducing the area of suitable spawning substrate for forage fish (Johannessen and MacLennan, 2007).
  • Lowered beach elevation: A starved beach sits lower in the tidal frame, altering the relationship between tide level and spawning habitat (Shipman, 2004, WDFW).
  • Increased wave energy on adjacent shores: Narrower beaches dissipate less wave energy, transferring erosion pressure to adjacent, unprotected shorelines (USACE, 2002).
  • Loss of accretionary landforms: Spits and cuspate forelands that depend on continuous sediment supply may erode or fail to maintain their form, eliminating the geomorphic features that create preferred forage fish spawning habitat (WDFW, Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources).
Critical ecological linkage (CF-14): Bluff erosion supplies sediment to the divergence zone of the drift cell. Longshore drift transports this material to the accretion zone (spits, broad beaches) at the downdrift end. These accretionary features provide the fine-grained, well-sorted upper intertidal substrate that Pacific sand lance (Ammodytes personatus) and surf smelt (Hypomesus pretiosus) preferentially select for spawning, according to WDFW forage fish spawning survey data. The integrity of the entire drift cell—from source to sink—is therefore essential to maintaining forage fish spawning habitat.

5. Sediment Budget Analysis

A sediment budget is a quantitative accounting of sediment inputs, outputs, and storage within a defined coastal compartment. For Puget Sound shorelines, sediment budgets have been estimated at the drift cell scale by several researchers working with USGS and WDFW data. The budget framework helps identify whether a given reach of shoreline is gaining, losing, or in equilibrium with respect to its sediment supply.

5.3 Budget Implications at Picnic Point

The sediment budget at Picnic Point is affected by two principal anthropogenic modifications, both documented in the WDFW and USGS nearshore literature:

  1. BNSF seawall: The railroad seawall (see Section 6) intercepts sediment that would otherwise be delivered from the eroding bluff face directly to the beach on the seaward side. Bluff erosion continues on the landward side of the tracks, but this material is trapped behind the rail corridor rather than reaching the intertidal zone. This represents a reduction in the primary sediment input to the local beach system (Shipman, 2004, WDFW).
  2. Shoreline armoring (regional): Armoring of bluffs by adjacent residential and commercial property owners throughout the Seattle-to-Everett corridor reduces the total sediment supply available to the drift cell system. The WDFW Puget Sound Nearshore Ecosystem Restoration Project (PSNERP) has estimated that a substantial percentage of the central Puget Sound eastern shoreline has been modified by armoring (Johannessen and MacLennan, 2007). Each armored bluff that ceases to erode removes a sediment source from the regional budget.

6. BNSF Railroad and Seawall Effects

6.1 Historical Context

The railroad corridor that now separates Picnic Point Park's upland facilities from its beach was originally constructed as the Great Northern Railway, part of James J. Hill's transcontinental route. The current alignment along the Puget Sound shoreline between Seattle and Everett was reconstructed with a protective seawall in 1907–1908, establishing the configuration that persists today as the BNSF Northern Transcon mainline (BNSF Railway).

The corridor currently carries approximately 30 trains per day, including freight, Amtrak Cascades passenger service, and Sound Transit Sounder commuter trains (BNSF Railway, Northern Transcon operational data). This level of traffic makes the corridor one of the most active rail lines on the West Coast and underscores the economic significance that constrains modification of the seawall and track alignment.

6.2 Geomorphic Effects of the Seawall

The BNSF seawall's effects on coastal processes are well-characterized in the WDFW nearshore literature. Shipman (2004) and the WDFW Marine Shoreline Design Guidelines describe the general mechanisms by which linear shore-parallel structures modify sediment transport:

Effect Mechanism Consequence
Sediment interception Seawall physically blocks eroded bluff material from reaching the seaward beach Reduced sediment input to local beach and downdrift drift cell
Wave reflection Hard vertical or near-vertical surface reflects incident wave energy rather than dissipating it Increased turbulence at seawall toe; potential scour of fronting beach
Creek mouth constriction Every stream crossing is channelized through a culvert or bridge beneath the tracks Altered sediment dynamics at creek mouths; reduced estuarine function; impaired fish passage at some crossings
Habitat truncation Seawall eliminates the natural transition zone between bluff toe and upper beach Loss of backshore habitat (large wood accumulation, wrack line, supratidal vegetation)
Placement loss The physical footprint of the seawall and riprap occupies former beach area Direct reduction of beach width at the structure location (Shipman, 2004, WDFW)

Sources: Shipman (2004), WDFW Marine Shoreline Design Guidelines; WDFW Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources; Johannessen and MacLennan (2007).

6.3 Access Infrastructure

Because the BNSF mainline separates the upland from the beach along nearly the entire Seattle-to-Everett corridor, public beach access requires grade-separated crossings. King and Snohomish County governments have constructed pedestrian overpasses at several locations, including Picnic Point, to provide safe beach access across the active rail corridor (Snohomish County DCNR). The Picnic Point pedestrian overpass serves as both a practical crossing and a defining feature of the park experience, requiring visitors to climb over the rail corridor before descending to the beach.

The Meadowdale Beach Park pedestrian overpass, located approximately 1.5 miles to the south, provides a comparative case. At Meadowdale, the overpass crosses both the BNSF corridor and Lunds Gulch Creek, where a recent estuary restoration project removed fish passage barriers at the rail crossing (Snohomish County Surface Water Management). These crossings illustrate the negotiated relationship between railroad operations, public access, and ecological restoration that characterizes this stretch of coast.

6.4 The Corridor as a Geological Boundary

From a geological perspective, the BNSF corridor functions as an artificial divide in the sediment transport system. On the landward side, bluff erosion processes continue as they have for millennia—groundwater seepage, raveling, and block failure deliver sediment to the base of the bluff. But this sediment is now trapped behind the railroad grade rather than reaching the beach. On the seaward side, the beach receives sediment only from longshore drift (originating at other, unarmored bluffs along the same drift cell) and from minor creek inputs channeled through the rail corridor's culverts.

This artificial separation of the bluff from its beach is, in the terminology of the WDFW nearshore program, a disconnection of the sediment source from the sediment sink—a disruption of the drift cell at its most fundamental level.

7. Landslide Hazards Along the BNSF Corridor

7.1 USGS Landslide Mapping

The USGS has mapped landslide locations along the BNSF corridor between Seattle and Everett, documenting numerous landslide events along this reach. The mapping reveals that landslides are a persistent and recurring hazard along the corridor, concentrated at locations where the stratigraphic conditions described in Section 2 create susceptible bluff geometries (Baum et al., 2000; Coe et al., 2004, USGS).

Particularly significant clusters of landslide activity were documented during the winters of 1996–1997, when above-normal precipitation saturated bluff sediments and triggered numerous failures along the corridor (USGS). These events disrupted rail service and provided dramatic demonstrations of the same bluff erosion processes that, under natural conditions, supply sediment to Puget Sound beaches.

7.3 Landslide-Sediment Supply Relationship

It is worth emphasizing that from a geological perspective, landslides along the Puget Sound bluffs are not exclusively hazardous events. They are also the natural sediment delivery mechanism upon which the entire nearshore ecosystem depends. The WDFW Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources explicitly states that natural bluff erosion, including landslide events, is essential for maintaining beaches and associated nearshore habitats.

This creates a fundamental tension in shoreline management: the same process that threatens railroad infrastructure and upland development is the process that feeds the beaches, supplies the drift cells, and creates the spawning habitat for forage fish. Armoring the bluffs to prevent slides protects infrastructure but starves the beach. Allowing natural erosion maintains the sediment supply but threatens built assets. The challenge of managing this tension is a central theme of the WDFW and Snohomish County nearshore programs.

8. The Geological Foundation of Forage Fish Habitat

The connection between geology and ecology at Picnic Point is nowhere more direct than in the relationship between sediment supply and forage fish spawning habitat. This section traces the geological thread from glacial deposits through drift cell dynamics to the beach substrate that Pacific sand lance and surf smelt require for reproduction.

8.2 The Geological Supply Chain

The substrate that forage fish require is not an independent resource. It is the end product of a geological supply chain that begins with glacial deposits and ends with sorted, reworked sediment on the beach:

Geological supply chain for forage fish spawning substrate:
  1. Source material: Glacially derived unconsolidated deposits (till, outwash, lacustrine sediments) left by the Vashon Stade of the Fraser Glaciation, approximately 15,000–13,000 years before present (Booth, 1994; USGS)
  2. Erosion and delivery: Natural bluff erosion (block failure, raveling, seepage-driven slumps) delivers a heterogeneous mix of grain sizes to the bluff toe and beach (WDFW, Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources)
  3. Longshore transport: Wave-driven currents sort and transport this material through the drift cell, progressively separating grain sizes as sediment moves downdrift (Johannessen and MacLennan, 2007)
  4. Accretion and sorting: At the downdrift end of the cell, sediment accumulates in spits, cuspate forelands, and broad beach platforms. The wave sorting process concentrates the sand and fine gravel fraction that forage fish prefer in the upper intertidal zone (WDFW; Shipman, 2004)
  5. Spawning: Sand lance preferentially spawn at these accretionary features, depositing eggs in the upper intertidal sand where incubation conditions (moisture, temperature, oxygen) are maintained by tidal wetting and draining (WDFW; Penttila, 2007)

8.3 Disruption Points

Any interruption of this supply chain reduces the quantity and quality of spawning substrate available at the accretion zone. The WDFW nearshore program has identified the following modifications as having the greatest impact on the geological supply of forage fish spawning habitat:

Modification Supply Chain Step Affected Habitat Consequence
Bluff armoring (bulkheads, riprap) Step 2 (erosion and delivery) Eliminates sediment input from armored bluff; starves downdrift beach
BNSF seawall Step 2 (erosion and delivery) Intercepts sediment between bluff and beach; traps material behind rail grade
Groins and jetties Step 3 (longshore transport) Traps sediment updrift; starves beach downdrift of structure
Beach nourishment with incompatible material Step 4 (accretion and sorting) Alters grain size distribution; may introduce material unsuitable for spawning
Vegetation removal on bluffs Steps 1–2 (source and delivery) Accelerates erosion beyond natural rates; may cause mass wasting that buries existing beach

Sources: WDFW Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources; Johannessen and MacLennan (2007); Shipman (2004), WDFW.

8.4 Implications for Picnic Point

At Picnic Point, the BNSF seawall represents the most significant local disruption of the geological supply chain. The seawall intercepts bluff-derived sediment at Step 2, preventing it from reaching the beach directly. However, the beach at Picnic Point does receive sediment from longshore transport (originating at unarmored bluffs elsewhere in the drift cell) and from Picnic Point Creek, whose outflow reaches the beach through the rail corridor. The net effect on spawning substrate availability depends on the balance of these inputs against the losses imposed by the seawall—a balance that the WDFW and Snohomish County MRC forage fish surveys help to monitor over time.

9. Synthesis: Geology as Ecological Infrastructure

The geological story of Picnic Point Park is, at its root, a story about how a glacier created the raw material for an ecosystem. The Vashon Stade of the Fraser Glaciation deposited the unconsolidated till, outwash, and lacustrine sediments that compose the bluffs. Natural erosion of these bluffs is the primary source of beach sediment along the Puget Sound shore (WDFW, Puget Sound Nearshore Technical Resources). Drift cells organize this sediment into a transport system that delivers it from eroding bluffs to accretionary beaches. And at those accretionary beaches, the sorted sand and gravel provide the spawning substrate upon which Pacific sand lance and surf smelt depend—species that in turn support Chinook salmon and, ultimately, the endangered Southern Resident Orca population (NOAA Fisheries).

This geological supply chain is interrupted at Picnic Point by the BNSF seawall, which has separated the bluff from the beach for over a century. The seawall protects critical rail infrastructure that carries approximately 30 trains per day (BNSF Railway), but it also removes one of the primary sediment inputs to the local beach system. Regional shoreline armoring by residential and commercial property owners further reduces the total sediment budget of the drift cell.

Understanding these geological processes and their disruption is essential context for evaluating the park's restoration trajectory, the ecological monitoring conducted by the Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee, and the broader challenge of recovering Puget Sound's nearshore. The bluffs at Picnic Point are not merely scenic features. They are the sediment factories upon which the entire nearshore food web ultimately depends.
