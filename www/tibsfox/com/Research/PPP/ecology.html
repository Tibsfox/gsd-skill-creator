1. Ecological Overview

Picnic Point Park, administered by Snohomish County Parks and Recreation at 7231 Picnic Point Road, Edmonds, WA 98036, occupies a narrow but ecologically significant transect from bluff top (elevation 79 ft) through a steep, forested ravine to a mixed sand-and-gravel intertidal beach on Puget Sound. The park sits within the broader Whidbey Basin sub-basin of Puget Sound, a region whose nearshore habitats have been the focus of sustained scientific attention since the establishment of the Puget Sound Partnership in 2007 (Puget Sound Partnership, Action Agenda, 2022).

Three interconnected habitat zones define the park's ecology:

  • The intertidal beach — a mixed sand-and-gravel substrate extending from the BNSF railroad riprap to the low-tide line, serving as documented or probable spawning habitat for at least two species of forage fish.
  • Picnic Point Creek — a small perennial stream that winds through native shoreline vegetation before discharging into Puget Sound, creating a creek-mouth estuary that concentrates nutrients, freshwater mixing, and potential salmonid rearing habitat.
  • The upland bluff and ravine forest — a steep, glacially derived slope supporting second-growth conifer and deciduous canopy, providing perching and foraging habitat for raptors, passerines, and woodpeckers.

These three zones are compressed into a horizontal distance of less than a quarter mile, creating ecological gradients that are unusually steep for an accessible public park. The compression also means that disturbances in any one zone — armoring of the shoreline, stormwater discharge from the upland watershed, or vegetation clearing on the bluff — propagate rapidly into the others.

The ecological significance of Picnic Point extends well beyond its modest acreage. As one of the diminishing number of unarmored or partially armored beaches along the eastern shore of central Puget Sound, it represents the type of nearshore habitat that WDFW, NOAA, and the Puget Sound Partnership have identified as critical for the recovery of forage fish populations, ESA-listed Chinook salmon, and the endangered Southern Resident killer whale population that depends on them (NOAA Fisheries, Recovery Plan for Southern Resident Killer Whales, 2008; revised 2021).

2. Intertidal Beach and Substrate Characteristics

2.1 Beach Morphology

The Picnic Point beach is a mixed sand-and-gravel intertidal surface typical of the glacially derived shorelines of central Puget Sound. Unlike the fine-grained mudflats found in sheltered embayments or the cobble-and-boulder beaches that develop at the bases of actively eroding bluffs, the Picnic Point substrate occupies the intermediate grain-size range that WDFW has documented as essential for forage fish spawning (WDFW, Forage Fish Management Plan, 1998).

The beach profile grades from coarser material near the railroad riprap at the upper beach (cobble and large gravel) to finer mixed sand-and-gravel in the mid-to-upper intertidal zone where forage fish spawning is most likely to occur. Below the mean lower low water line, the substrate transitions to finer sand and, farther offshore, to the muddy-sand bottom characteristic of the central Puget Sound basin.

2.2 Tidal Regime

Puget Sound experiences a mixed semidiurnal tidal regime, with two high tides and two low tides of unequal height each lunar day. At the nearest NOAA reference station (Mukilteo, Station #9447659), the mean tidal range is approximately 7.7 ft (2.35 m) and the diurnal range is approximately 11.3 ft (3.44 m) (NOAA, Tidal Datums, Mukilteo station). Extreme low tides, which expose the broadest extent of the intertidal beach and the subtidal eelgrass zone, occur during summer morning hours and winter afternoon hours.

The tidal range is ecologically significant because it determines the vertical extent of the spawning zone for surf smelt and Pacific sand lance. Both species deposit eggs in the upper intertidal zone, typically between the mean higher high water line and the ordinary high water line — a vertical band of approximately 3–5 ft that is alternately submerged and exposed with each tidal cycle (WDFW, Forage Fish Spawning Habitat Assessment, Penttila 2007).

2.3 Sediment Transport and Drift Cells

The Picnic Point beach lies within the Puget Sound littoral drift system, a network of independent sediment transport cells driven by wave energy, longshore currents, and sediment supply from eroding bluffs. WDFW and the Washington Department of Ecology have mapped the drift cell structure of Puget Sound's shoreline, identifying feeder bluffs (sediment sources), transport zones, and accretion shoreforms (depositional endpoints) (Johannessen and MacLennan, Beaches and Bluffs of Puget Sound, Ecology Publication 07-06-064, 2007).

The shoreline between Edmonds and Mukilteo functions as a net northward-drifting sediment transport corridor, with eroding glacial bluffs supplying sand and gravel to the littoral system. Armoring of bluffs and construction of shoreline structures (including the BNSF railroad grade, which runs along the upper beach for this entire reach) interrupts sediment supply, reducing the volume of spawning-grade substrate delivered to downstream beaches (Shipman et al., Puget Sound Shorelines and the Impacts of Armoring, USGS Scientific Investigations Report 2010-5254).

Picnic Point itself is a subtle accretionary feature — a slight protrusion of the shoreline that historically accumulated sediment from drift cell transport. The point geometry creates a degree of wave sheltering on its northern side, which may contribute to the retention of finer-grained spawning substrate in the upper intertidal zone. This geometry is also why forage fish spawning surveys have targeted the site: accretionary features and the distal ends of drift cells are preferentially used by Pacific sand lance for egg deposition (WDFW, Penttila 2007).

3. Forage Fish Species and Spawning Biology

Three species of forage fish are central to the nearshore ecology of Picnic Point and the broader Puget Sound food web. Two of them — surf smelt and Pacific sand lance — spawn directly on intertidal beaches with the substrate type found at Picnic Point. The third, Pacific herring, spawns on submerged vegetation in the shallow subtidal zone and is included here for food web context.

3.1 Surf Smelt (Hypomesus pretiosus)

Surf smelt are small, silvery osmerids that grow to approximately 9 inches (23 cm) in length. Adults are olive-green on the dorsal surface with a distinctive silver and yellow lateral band (WDFW, Forage Fish Biology and Management). They are found throughout the coastal waters of the northeastern Pacific, from Alaska to central California, and are one of the most broadly distributed forage fish species in Puget Sound.

Surf smelt spawn in the upper intertidal zone on mixed sand-and-gravel beaches. Females deposit adhesive eggs directly onto the beach substrate, where the eggs adhere to sand grains and develop in the surf zone. The spawning substrate requirements are specific: the grain size must be coarse enough to provide interstitial spaces for water circulation around the eggs but fine enough to anchor them against wave action. WDFW characterizes suitable substrate as mixed sand and gravel in the 1–7 mm grain-size range (WDFW, Forage Fish Management Plan, 1998).

Spawning seasonality varies among Puget Sound populations and has been the subject of WDFW survey work since the 1930s. Three spawning timing patterns have been documented across Puget Sound (Penttila, Marine Forage Fishes in Puget Sound, WDFW Technical Report FPT 97-01, 1997):

  • Summer spawning: May through August
  • Fall-winter spawning: September through March
  • Year-round spawning: documented at some sites where summer and winter populations overlap

The specific spawning timing at Picnic Point beach is determined by the results of the Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee (MRC) survey program, which has been collecting monthly samples since 2011 (see Section 4 below).

3.2 Pacific Sand Lance (Ammodytes hexapterus)

Pacific sand lance are slender, elongate fish that grow to approximately 8 inches (20 cm) in length. Adults are gray to green on the dorsal surface, with silver sides and a deeply forked caudal fin. Their common name derives from the species' habit of burrowing into sandy substrates during rest periods and when avoiding predators (WDFW, Forage Fish Biology).

Sand lance spawn in the upper intertidal zone on sand-and-gravel beaches, with spawning concentrated in the winter months from November through February. Unlike surf smelt, which may spawn across multiple seasons, sand lance in Puget Sound are exclusively winter spawners. Eggs are deposited on the beach surface and develop over an approximately four-week incubation period, during which they are vulnerable to desiccation, predation, and disturbance from foot traffic, vehicles, or debris (Penttila, Marine Forage Fishes in Puget Sound, WDFW, 1997).

Sand lance show a marked preference for spawning at the distal ends of drift cells — accretionary shoreforms where longshore transport deposits fine-grained sediment. Picnic Point's slight protrusion and its position within the local drift cell make it a plausible sand lance spawning site based on geomorphic criteria alone (WDFW, Penttila 2007).

"Pacific sand lance eggs are deposited in the upper intertidal zone on mixed sand and gravel beaches. Eggs are adhesive and adhere to sand grains in the top 1–2 inches of substrate. Incubation requires approximately 4 weeks, during which eggs must remain moist but not continuously submerged."
— WDFW, Forage Fish Spawning Habitat Assessment Protocol, Penttila 2007

3.3 Pacific Herring (Clupea harengus pallasi)

Pacific herring are the largest of the three principal Puget Sound forage fish species, growing to approximately 9 inches (23 cm). They are schooling fish that feed on phytoplankton and zooplankton in the water column and are a primary prey species for Chinook salmon, harbor seals, seabirds, and other predators throughout Puget Sound (WDFW, Puget Sound Herring Stock Assessment).

Unlike surf smelt and sand lance, herring do not spawn on beach surfaces. Instead, they deposit eggs on eelgrass (Zostera marina) and marine algae in the shallow subtidal zone. The largest Puget Sound herring stock spawns at Cherry Point in the northern Sound (Bellingham area), though smaller stocks use spawning grounds throughout the central and southern Sound (WDFW, Herring Stock Status).

While herring are not beach spawners, they are included in this assessment because they are a critical component of the nearshore food web at Picnic Point. Herring schools moving through the waters offshore of the park are prey for the same predators — Chinook salmon, harbor seals, bald eagles, and marine birds — that use the park's beach and nearshore habitats.

4. Forage Fish Spawning Survey Program

4.1 Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee (MRC)

The Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee (MRC) has conducted monthly forage fish spawning surveys at beaches throughout the county since 2011, accumulating more than 700 individual samples as of 2025. These surveys follow standardized WDFW protocols and are part of a statewide volunteer monitoring network coordinated by WDFW's forage fish research program (Snohomish County MRC, Forage Fish Monitoring Program Annual Reports, 2011–2025).

4.2 WDFW Survey Protocol

The survey methodology, developed by WDFW biologist Dan Penttila and refined over decades of application, is designed to detect the presence of forage fish eggs in beach substrate with minimal disturbance to the spawning habitat. The protocol proceeds as follows (WDFW, Methods for Sampling Surf Smelt and Sand Lance Spawn):

  1. Transect establishment: A 100-foot (30.5 m) transect is established along the upper intertidal zone, oriented parallel to the waterline at the elevation band where spawning is most likely to occur (typically between mean higher high water and ordinary high water).
  2. Sample collection: Surveyors collect 1.5 to 2 liters of sand from the top 1–2 inches (2.5–5 cm) of substrate along the length of the transect, pooling material from multiple points to create a composite sample.
  3. Field sieving: The composite sample is sieved through a nested series of three mesh screens:
    • 4 mm mesh — retains large gravel and shell fragments
    • 2 mm mesh — retains coarse sand and large organic debris
    • 0.5 mm mesh — retains forage fish eggs, which are approximately 0.7–1.0 mm in diameter
  4. Egg identification: Material retained on the 0.5 mm screen is examined under magnification. Forage fish eggs are identified to species based on size, adhesion characteristics, and developmental stage. Surf smelt eggs are approximately 0.9–1.0 mm in diameter and strongly adhesive; sand lance eggs are slightly smaller (0.7–0.8 mm) and less adhesive (WDFW, Penttila 2007).
  5. Data recording: Presence or absence of eggs is recorded along with species identification, developmental stage, substrate condition, weather, and tidal stage. Data are submitted to WDFW for inclusion in the statewide forage fish spawning database.

4.3 Survey Frequency and Coverage

MRC surveys are conducted monthly throughout the year, with the survey schedule designed to capture both summer-spawning surf smelt and winter-spawning sand lance. The monthly sampling frequency means that short-duration spawning events (those lasting less than 2–3 weeks) may not be detected in every month, introducing a known negative bias into the dataset. A negative result (no eggs found) does not confirm the absence of spawning — it may reflect timing mismatch between the survey and the spawning event (WDFW, Interpreting Forage Fish Survey Results).

Over more than 700 samples collected since 2011, the MRC program has built a dataset of sufficient temporal depth to characterize spawning periodicity, identify trends in spawning intensity, and detect long-term changes in habitat suitability at surveyed beaches across Snohomish County (Snohomish County MRC, annual program reports).

"A single negative survey result does not confirm the absence of forage fish spawning. Spawning may be episodic, and monthly sampling provides a minimum detection frequency. Multiple years of consistent data are needed to characterize a beach's spawning status."
— WDFW, Forage Fish Survey Interpretation Guidelines

5. Shellfish Harvest Closure

The Washington Department of Health (DOH) maintains a year-round closure on the harvest of clams, mussels, and oysters at Picnic Point County Park beach. The DOH classification states that "clams, oysters, and mussels from this beach are not fit for human consumption at any time" (Washington Department of Health, Shellfish Safety Information, Picnic Point County Park listing).

5.1 Closure Context

Shellfish harvest closures in Puget Sound are typically driven by one or more of the following factors (Washington Department of Health, Shellfish Program Annual Report):

  • Fecal coliform contamination from failing septic systems, pet waste, wildlife, or stormwater conveyance
  • Chemical contaminants including heavy metals, petroleum hydrocarbons, or industrial compounds
  • Biotoxins (paralytic shellfish poisoning, domoic acid) produced by harmful algal blooms
  • Pollution from stormwater runoff in urbanized watersheds

The Picnic Point closure likely reflects water quality impairment associated with stormwater runoff from the urbanized upland watershed. The census-designated place of Picnic Point (population 8,809 per the 2010 U.S. Census) is a residential community with impervious surface coverage typical of suburban Puget Sound lowlands. Stormwater from roads, driveways, and roofs is conveyed to the Sound through surface drainage and Picnic Point Creek, carrying bacterial loads, nutrients, and chemical contaminants that impair shellfish growing conditions.

5.2 Research Gap: Specific Contaminant Profile

The specific contaminant profile driving the Picnic Point shellfish closure is a documented research gap. DOH closure notices identify the beach as unsuitable for shellfish harvest but do not publish detailed water quality monitoring data for this specific site. Determining whether the closure is driven primarily by bacterial contamination (fecal coliforms), chemical pollutants, or a combination would require targeted water quality sampling of Picnic Point Creek and the nearshore receiving waters — work that could be incorporated into Snohomish County's Surface Water Management monitoring program but has not been publicly documented to date.

6. Picnic Point Creek and Riparian Corridor

6.1 Creek Description

Picnic Point Creek is a small perennial stream that descends through the forested ravine within the park before discharging into Puget Sound at the beach. The creek winds through native shoreline vegetation including red alder (Alnus rubra), western red cedar (Thuja plicata), bigleaf maple (Acer macrophyllum), and sword fern (Polystichum munitum) — species characteristic of the Western Hemlock vegetation zone that dominates the Puget Sound lowlands (Franklin and Dyrness, Natural Vegetation of Oregon and Washington, USFS General Technical Report PNW-8, 1973).

Trail reports from the Washington Trails Association (WTA) describe Picnic Point Creek as a small stream that is wadeable during most of the year, suggesting a baseflow regime driven by shallow groundwater inputs and seasonal precipitation rather than snowmelt or large watershed storage (WTA, Picnic Point Park trail reports). Flow likely peaks during the October–March wet season and reaches minimum baseflow during the July–September dry season, following the pattern typical of small Puget Sound lowland streams.

6.2 Creek-Mouth Habitat

The creek mouth — the point where Picnic Point Creek discharges across the beach into Puget Sound — creates a localized mixing zone of fresh and salt water that concentrates ecological activity. Creek-mouth habitats in Puget Sound are recognized by WDFW and NOAA as disproportionately important for juvenile salmonid rearing, providing (NOAA Fisheries, Puget Sound Nearshore Ecosystem Restoration Project, 2006):

  • Freshwater refuge: Juvenile salmon transitioning from freshwater to saltwater (smolts) use creek-mouth mixing zones to acclimatize gradually to marine salinity.
  • Nutrient concentration: Freshwater inputs carry dissolved organic matter, invertebrate drift, and terrestrial detritus that support prey production for juvenile fish.
  • Thermal refuge: During summer, creek water is typically cooler than nearshore marine water, providing thermal relief for heat-sensitive species.
  • Structural complexity: Accumulated large woody debris at creek mouths creates cover from predators and breaks current, reducing energetic costs for small fish holding in the area.

The creek-mouth area at Picnic Point is the primary focus of restoration work conducted by the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds (OFF) and the Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project (HFP) since 2023. Restoration activities have concentrated on removing invasive vegetation and replanting native riparian species to stabilize the creek banks, improve shade cover, and enhance the structural and biological quality of the creek-mouth habitat (OFF, restoration event documentation, 2023–2026).

6.3 Fish Passage and Salmonid Use

The fish passage status of Picnic Point Creek and its potential contribution to Puget Sound salmon ecology represent a documented research gap. Several questions remain unresolved:

  • Passage barriers: Whether the creek contains culverts, grade changes, or other structures that block upstream fish migration has not been publicly documented in available Snohomish County or WDFW databases.
  • Salmonid presence: Whether juvenile or adult salmon (Chinook, coho, chum, or pink salmon, or steelhead/cutthroat trout) currently use Picnic Point Creek for spawning, rearing, or migration has not been confirmed through published fish surveys.
  • Habitat capacity: The stream's flow regime, gradient, substrate, and riparian condition have not been formally assessed for their capacity to support salmonid life stages.

Small streams of this type throughout the Puget Sound lowlands historically supported populations of coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch), chum salmon (O. keta), and coastal cutthroat trout (O. clarkii clarkii). Many have been degraded by urbanization, and some have been disconnected from the marine environment by culverts or other infrastructure. The BNSF railroad grade, which crosses the lower reach of many small Puget Sound streams including those near Picnic Point, is a known barrier to fish passage at numerous sites along the rail corridor (WDFW, Fish Passage Barrier Database).

"Small nearshore streams throughout Puget Sound have been systematically undervalued for their contribution to salmonid productivity. Many support resident and anadromous populations that collectively represent significant habitat capacity."
— Beamer et al., Nearshore and Delta Habitat of the Skagit River, WDFW/Skagit River System Cooperative, 2005

7. Marine Food Web: Forage Fish to Southern Resident Orcas

The ecological significance of Picnic Point's intertidal beach is best understood through the marine food web that connects forage fish spawning habitat to the survival of endangered apex predators. This food web traces a chain of trophic dependence from primary producers (phytoplankton and zooplankton) through forage fish to ESA-listed Chinook salmon and, ultimately, to the critically endangered Southern Resident killer whale population.

7.1 Trophic Level 1–2: Primary Production and Forage Fish

Forage fish — surf smelt, Pacific sand lance, and Pacific herring — occupy a pivotal position in the Puget Sound marine food web. They feed directly on phytoplankton and zooplankton, converting primary production into energy-dense prey biomass available to higher trophic levels. Their schooling behavior and high reproductive output make them efficient energy transfer agents, and their intertidal spawning habit links the productivity of the beach zone to the productivity of the entire nearshore ecosystem (WDFW, Forage Fish Management Plan, 1998).

The loss of forage fish spawning habitat — through shoreline armoring, substrate degradation, or disturbance — removes the reproductive foundation of this energy pathway. WDFW has established a "no net loss" policy for forage fish spawning habitat in recognition of its irreplaceable role in the marine food web (WDFW, Hydraulic Code Rules, WAC 220-660).

7.2 Trophic Level 3: Chinook Salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha)

Puget Sound Chinook salmon were listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act (ESA) in 1999 (64 FR 14308, March 24, 1999; NOAA Fisheries). Chinook are the largest Pacific salmon species, and adults returning to Puget Sound rivers to spawn are the primary prey of Southern Resident killer whales during the summer and fall months.

Juvenile Chinook salmon depend heavily on the nearshore zone during their outmigration from natal rivers to the open ocean. During this critical life stage, which may last weeks to months, juveniles feed on the same prey resources concentrated in nearshore habitats: small crustaceans, larval fish, and — significantly — larval and juvenile forage fish. The productivity of nearshore spawning beaches like Picnic Point directly supports the prey base available to outmigrating juvenile Chinook (Beamish and Mahnken, A Critical Size and Period Hypothesis to Explain Natural Regulation of Salmon Abundance, Progress in Oceanography 57: 147–168, 2003).

7.3 Trophic Level 4: Southern Resident Killer Whales (Orcinus orca)

The Southern Resident killer whale (SRKW) population is listed as endangered under the ESA (70 FR 69903, November 18, 2005; NOAA Fisheries). As of the most recent census, the population numbers 73 individuals in three family groups (J, K, and L pods) (Center for Whale Research, annual census, 2024). The population has declined from a high of 98 individuals in 1995, and its recovery is constrained by three primary threats identified by NOAA: prey availability, vessel disturbance, and contaminant accumulation (NOAA Fisheries, Recovery Plan for Southern Resident Killer Whales, 2008; revised 2021).

Chinook salmon constitute approximately 80% of the SRKW diet during summer months when the whales are present in the inland waters of Puget Sound and the Salish Sea (Hanson et al., Species and Stock Identification of Prey Consumed by Endangered Southern Resident Killer Whales, Endangered Species Research 11: 69–82, 2010; NOAA Fisheries). The whales' dependence on Chinook means that factors limiting Chinook abundance — including degradation of nearshore rearing habitat and loss of the forage fish prey base — cascade through the food web to constrain SRKW recovery.

This trophic chain — from phytoplankton to orca — illustrates why WDFW, NOAA, and the Puget Sound Partnership treat forage fish spawning beaches as critical infrastructure for marine ecosystem recovery. The loss of any spawning beach reduces the total reproductive capacity of the forage fish population, which reduces prey availability for juvenile Chinook, which reduces the abundance of adult Chinook available to Southern Resident killer whales. The chain is direct, well-documented, and the basis for regulatory protections applied to beaches like Picnic Point (NOAA Fisheries, Recovery Plan for Southern Resident Killer Whales, 2008).

8. Bird Community

The convergence of intertidal beach, creek-mouth estuary, nearshore marine waters, and upland forest at Picnic Point creates habitat conditions that support a diverse avian community spanning multiple foraging guilds. The following species accounts are based on documented observations, expected occurrence based on habitat suitability and geographic range, and published range data from WDFW and the Cornell Lab of Ornithology's Birds of the World database.

8.1 Raptors and Fish-Eating Birds

Bald Eagle (Haliaeetus leucocephalus)

Bald eagles are documented residents at Picnic Point, using shoreline trees for perching and hunting over the intertidal zone and nearshore waters. Eagles at this site feed primarily on fish (including forage fish and any available salmon) and are known to scavenge carrion on the beach. The species was removed from the federal ESA threatened list in 2007 but remains protected under the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (16 U.S.C. 668–668d) and the Migratory Bird Treaty Act (WDFW, Bald Eagle Status Report; U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service). In compliance with SC-END safety rules, no specific nest or perch locations are reported here; observations are described at the park scale only.

Belted Kingfisher (Megaceryle alcyon)

Belted kingfishers are expected at Picnic Point based on the presence of suitable foraging habitat at the creek mouth and along the shoreline. Kingfishers hunt by diving from elevated perches into shallow water to capture small fish and aquatic invertebrates. The creek-mouth area and any exposed pilings or overhanging branches along the beach provide the combination of clear water and elevated perches that kingfishers require (Cornell Lab of Ornithology, Birds of the World).

8.2 Wading and Shorebird Species

Great Blue Heron (Ardea herodias)

Great blue herons are expected foragers in the Picnic Point intertidal zone, where they wade in shallow water to hunt fish, crabs, and invertebrates. The species is common throughout the Puget Sound lowlands and is regularly observed at beaches with mixed sand-and-gravel substrate and shallow nearshore waters. Great blue herons are designated as a Priority Species by WDFW, and their communal nesting colonies (rookeries) are tracked through WDFW's Priority Habitats and Species program (WDFW, Priority Habitats and Species List, 2023).

Black Oystercatcher (Haematopus bachmani)

Black oystercatchers are a species of high conservation concern that uses rocky and cobble intertidal habitats along the Pacific coast. Their occurrence at Picnic Point is considered possible but would depend on the presence of sufficient rocky substrate and their primary invertebrate prey (mussels, limpets, chitons). The species is a WDFW Priority Species and is monitored as an indicator of rocky intertidal habitat quality (WDFW, Priority Habitats and Species List, 2023).

Dunlin (Calidris alpina)

Dunlin are seasonal migratory shorebirds that use mudflat and upper beach habitats during their spring and fall migrations along the Pacific Flyway, and some overwinter in Puget Sound. They are among the most abundant shorebirds in the region during migration and winter months, foraging in flocks on intertidal invertebrates (Wahl et al., Birds of Washington, Oregon State University Press, 2005). The mixed-substrate upper beach at Picnic Point provides suitable dunlin foraging habitat during lower tidal stages.

8.3 Marine and Diving Birds

Surf Scoter (Melanitta perspicillata)

Surf scoters are seasonal visitors to the nearshore marine waters off Picnic Point, typically present from October through April. These diving ducks forage on benthic invertebrates (mussels, clams, crustaceans) in the shallow subtidal zone. Puget Sound supports significant wintering populations of surf scoters, and the species is designated as a WDFW Priority Species due to long-term population declines documented through the Puget Sound Seabird Survey and other monitoring programs (Puget Sound Seabird Survey, Seattle Audubon Society; WDFW, Priority Habitats and Species List, 2023).

Common Loon (Gavia immer)

Common loons are seasonal visitors to the marine waters off Picnic Point, present primarily from October through May during their non-breeding season. Loons breed on freshwater lakes in northern latitudes and winter in coastal marine waters, where they dive to capture fish. Puget Sound is an important wintering area for common loons, which can be observed from shore at sites like Picnic Point during calm conditions (Wahl et al., Birds of Washington, 2005).

8.5 Bird Community Summary

The Picnic Point bird community reflects the site's position at the intersection of three habitat types: marine nearshore, freshwater creek, and upland forest. At least 8 species are documented or expected as regular users of the park's habitats (bald eagle, great blue heron, surf scoter, black oystercatcher, dunlin, common loon, belted kingfisher, and glaucous-winged gull), with the total species list likely exceeding 30 when seasonal migrants, forest passerines, and occasional visitors are included. The site's value to birds is directly tied to the health of the intertidal food web: beach-spawning forage fish support eagles and kingfishers, benthic invertebrates support scoters and oystercatchers, and the creek-mouth mixing zone concentrates prey resources for herons and shorebirds.

9. Vegetation and Riparian Ecology

9.1 Upland Forest

The bluff and ravine at Picnic Point support second-growth forest within the Western Hemlock (Tsuga heterophylla) vegetation zone, the dominant lowland forest type of the Puget Sound basin (Franklin and Dyrness, Natural Vegetation of Oregon and Washington, USFS, 1973). The canopy includes:

  • Conifers: Western red cedar (Thuja plicata), Douglas-fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii), western hemlock (Tsuga heterophylla)
  • Deciduous: Red alder (Alnus rubra), bigleaf maple (Acer macrophyllum)
  • Understory: Sword fern (Polystichum munitum), salal (Gaultheria shallon), Oregon grape (Mahonia nervosa)

Red alder is a nitrogen-fixing pioneer species that dominates disturbed sites and stream corridors throughout the Puget Sound lowlands. Its presence along Picnic Point Creek indicates a riparian canopy that is contributing nitrogen to the stream system through leaf litter and root-zone fixation, supporting the aquatic invertebrate community that forms the base of the creek's food web.

9.2 Riparian Corridor

The riparian corridor along Picnic Point Creek provides critical ecological functions including streambank stabilization, shade cover (which moderates water temperature for aquatic organisms), leaf litter inputs that fuel aquatic food webs, and large woody debris recruitment that creates instream habitat complexity (Naiman and Bilby, River Ecology and Management, Springer, 1998).

Restoration work by the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds and the Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project has targeted invasive species removal and native replanting in the riparian zone since 2023, focusing on the creek-mouth area where native vegetation cover is most critical for maintaining habitat quality. The specific invasive species targeted and the native species planted in restoration efforts are documented in the restoration module of this study (OFF/HFP, restoration event records, 2023–2026).

9.3 Beach Vegetation and Wrack

The upper intertidal zone at Picnic Point supports a narrow band of salt-tolerant vegetation and accumulates wrack (decomposing seaweed and marine debris) at the high-tide line. Wrack deposits provide habitat for invertebrates (amphipods, isopods, beach hoppers) that are important prey for shorebirds and contribute organic matter to the beach ecosystem. The wrack line also provides moisture retention and physical shelter for forage fish eggs deposited in the upper intertidal zone (Sobocinski et al., Wrack and Macroalgae as Habitat for Fauna on Puget Sound Beaches, Marine Ecology Progress Series 388: 45–55, 2009).

10. Marine Mammals

The nearshore marine waters off Picnic Point are used by several marine mammal species, all of which are protected under the Marine Mammal Protection Act (16 U.S.C. 1361–1423).

10.1 Harbor Seal (Phoca vitulina)

Harbor seals are the most commonly observed marine mammal in Puget Sound and are expected in the waters off Picnic Point. They haul out on rocks, logs, and beaches throughout the Sound and forage on a diet that includes forage fish (herring, smelt, sand lance), salmon, and invertebrates. Harbor seal populations in Puget Sound have recovered to near-historical levels following passage of the Marine Mammal Protection Act in 1972, and the current Puget Sound population is estimated at approximately 14,000 individuals (Jeffries et al., NOAA/WDFW, Trends and Status of Harbor Seals in Washington State, 2003).

Harbor seals are significant predators of forage fish and represent an important link between the beach-spawning forage fish community and the broader marine food web. Their presence at Picnic Point is an indicator of productive nearshore foraging habitat.

10.2 Southern Resident Killer Whales

As discussed in Section 7.3, Southern Resident killer whales (73 individuals as of the 2024 Center for Whale Research census) transit the central Puget Sound during their seasonal movements and are known to forage in the inland waters between Edmonds and Mukilteo. While orcas do not use the intertidal beach directly, the food web connection through Chinook salmon makes the health of nearshore forage fish spawning habitat at sites like Picnic Point a factor in SRKW prey availability (NOAA Fisheries, Recovery Plan for Southern Resident Killer Whales, 2008).

10.3 Other Marine Mammals

Occasional observations of California sea lions (Zalophus californianus), Steller sea lions (Eumetopias jubatus), and harbor porpoises (Phocoena phocoena) are expected in the nearshore waters of central Puget Sound based on NOAA marine mammal survey data. Gray whales (Eschrichtius robustus) have been observed feeding in the shallow waters near Mukilteo and Edmonds during their northward spring migration, though such observations are infrequent (NOAA Fisheries, Marine Mammal Stock Assessment Reports).

11. Indigenous Ecological Context

The shoreline at Picnic Point lies within the traditional territory of the Tulalip Tribes, a federally recognized tribal nation whose ancestral lands encompass the eastern shore of Puget Sound from the Snohomish River drainage northward. The Tulalip Tribes are the successors in interest to the Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, and other tribes and bands that were signatories to the Treaty of Point Elliott (1855), which reserved fishing, hunting, and gathering rights throughout the ceded territory (Tulalip Tribes, official website; Treaty of Point Elliott, 12 Stat. 927, 1855).

The ecological knowledge held by the Tulalip Tribes regarding the nearshore habitats, fish populations, and seasonal resource use patterns of the Picnic Point shoreline represents an important dimension of understanding that is not fully captured in the Western scientific literature reviewed in this module. The Tulalip Tribes maintain active natural resources management programs, including fisheries management, habitat restoration, and environmental monitoring, and have published research relevant to Puget Sound nearshore ecology through their Natural Resources Department (Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources Department).

Research gap: This module does not include Indigenous ecological knowledge claims that have not been published or authorized for public use by the Tulalip Tribes. Any future expansion of this research to incorporate Tulalip perspectives would require direct consultation with the Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources Department and appropriate permissions for the use of traditional knowledge. The absence of this knowledge from the present module should be understood as a limitation of scope, not a statement about its relevance or value.

13. Ecological Synthesis

Picnic Point Park occupies a position of disproportionate ecological importance relative to its modest size. The park's mixed sand-and-gravel beach provides the specific substrate type required for surf smelt and Pacific sand lance spawning — a habitat type that has been progressively lost along the urbanized eastern shore of Puget Sound as shoreline armoring, railroad construction, and development have altered the natural sediment supply and beach profile. The Snohomish County MRC's sustained monthly monitoring program, with more than 700 samples collected since 2011 following standardized WDFW protocols, represents one of the most robust forage fish survey datasets in the county.

The food web connections traced in this module — from phytoplankton through forage fish to ESA-threatened Chinook salmon and ESA-endangered Southern Resident killer whales (73 individuals, 2024 Center for Whale Research census) — demonstrate that the health of this single beach contributes, in aggregate with other spawning sites, to the prey base that constrains the recovery of Puget Sound's most imperiled marine species.

Picnic Point Creek, though small and incompletely documented, adds a freshwater dimension to the site's ecology by creating a creek-mouth mixing zone with the characteristics that NOAA identifies as important for juvenile salmonid rearing. The riparian restoration work conducted by the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds and the Snohomish County Healthy Forest Project since 2023 represents an active investment in the ecological function of this corridor.

The bird community — at least 8 documented or expected species spanning raptors, wading birds, diving ducks, shorebirds, and passerines — reflects the habitat diversity created by the convergence of marine, freshwater, and forest environments within the park's boundaries. Several of these species (bald eagle, surf scoter, black oystercatcher, great blue heron) carry WDFW Priority Species designations that add formal conservation weight to the site.

The research gaps identified in this module — particularly the shellfish contaminant profile, Picnic Point Creek fish passage status, eelgrass distribution, and the absence of Tulalip Tribes ecological knowledge — define the boundaries of current understanding and point toward the work needed to complete the ecological picture. These gaps do not diminish the significance of what is known; they frame the questions that remain.
