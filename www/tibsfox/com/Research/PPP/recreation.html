1. Overview

Picnic Point Park functions simultaneously as a neighborhood beach park, a tide-dependent hiking corridor, and a working field station for two distinct community science programs. This module documents the practical dimensions of visiting the park — access constraints, facilities, tide windows, and beach hiking routes — alongside the protocols that transform casual visitors into contributors to regional ecological monitoring. The two roles are not incidental to each other: the same tide table that determines whether a family can walk south to Meadowdale Beach also determines whether a forage fish surveyor can collect sediment samples from the upper intertidal zone, and the same iNaturalist observation submitted by a dog walker may become the data point that influences a land management decision by Snohomish County.

Picnic Point sits at 7231 Picnic Point Road, Edmonds, WA 98036, administered by the Snohomish County Department of Conservation and Natural Resources (DCNR), Parks and Recreation Division. The park occupies a bluff-top lot with parking and a pedestrian overpass that descends to the beach across the BNSF Railway mainline — a physical separation that defines nearly every aspect of access, from the time required to reach the water to the ADA constraints that limit who can use the beach independently (Snohomish County DCNR).

Unlike urban parks with paved trail networks, Picnic Point has no established trails within its boundaries, per Washington Trails Association (WTA) documentation. What it has instead is the beach itself — a tide-dependent surface that extends approximately 1.5 miles south to Meadowdale Beach Park and approximately 5 miles north to Point Elliott at Mukilteo, with the wreck site of the MV Pacific Queen at Shipwreck Point approximately 1 mile north. The accessibility of these routes changes with every tidal cycle, making the NOAA tide prediction tables for Mukilteo (Station #9447659) as essential a planning tool as any trail map.

2. Park Facilities and Access

Picnic Point Park's facilities are modest in comparison to the engineered trail systems and interpretive centers found at larger Snohomish County parks. What the park provides instead is direct, largely unmediated access to a working shoreline — a beach that is simultaneously a recreational surface, a forage fish spawning ground documented by the Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee, and an intertidal corridor connecting two larger park systems. The facilities that exist are oriented toward picnicking, beach access, and basic sanitation rather than interpretive programming or extended-stay recreation.

The park's physical layout is dictated by the BNSF Railway mainline, which separates the parking area and park entrance (on the upland side of the tracks) from the beach, picnic areas, and restrooms (on the waterside). This division is not merely topographic — it creates a functional bottleneck through the pedestrian overpass that shapes visitor flow, limits capacity, and constrains ADA accessibility in ways that affect who can use the park and how.

2.1 Waterside Amenities

Below the overpass, the park provides picnic tables, open grassy areas suitable for informal gathering, and restroom facilities. The picnic areas are situated between the base of the overpass and the upper beach, providing views west across Puget Sound to Whidbey Island and the Olympic Mountains. Large driftwood logs accumulate north of the Picnic Point Creek mouth, providing natural seating and windbreak along the upper beach (Snohomish County DCNR).

Dogs are permitted on the beach but must be leashed at all times, per Snohomish County park regulations. The beach is not a designated off-leash area. Leash enforcement is relevant to community science activities because unleashed dogs can disturb shorebirds, marine mammals hauled out on the beach, and the upper intertidal substrate where forage fish eggs may be incubating (Snohomish County DCNR; WDFW, Priority Habitats and Species List, 2023).

3. Parking and Capacity

The park's parking lot is small and fills quickly on weekends and warm weekday afternoons, creating a practical capacity constraint that is more binding than any formal visitor limit. Snohomish County does not publish a specific vehicle capacity for the lot, but regular visitors report that arriving after mid-morning on summer weekends frequently means finding no available spaces (Snohomish County DCNR).

The parking limitation has a notable consequence for accessibility planning: Meadowdale Beach Park, located approximately 1.5 miles south along the shoreline, directs visitors who cannot navigate its own steep trail to Picnic Point as an alternative with more accessible parking and beach access. This referral relationship means that Picnic Point's small lot absorbs overflow demand from visitors who need accessible parking options — a dynamic that compounds the existing capacity constraint on high-use days (Snohomish County DCNR).

4. Pedestrian Overpass and ADA Constraints

The pedestrian overpass spanning the BNSF Railway mainline is the sole authorized route between the parking area and the beach. The overpass is a large, elevated structure that carries pedestrians over the active rail corridor — the same mainline that carries BNSF freight traffic and Amtrak Cascades passenger service between Seattle and Vancouver, BC. Crossing the tracks at grade is prohibited and dangerous; the overpass exists specifically to provide safe passage over an active rail line that sees substantial daily train traffic (BNSF Railway; Snohomish County DCNR).

The overpass is not fully ADA-accessible without assistance. While the structure includes ramps rather than stairs on at least portions of its approach, the grade, length, and surface conditions present challenges for visitors using wheelchairs, walkers, or other mobility devices. Visitors with mobility limitations should plan for the overpass crossing and may need assistance to navigate it safely (Snohomish County DCNR).

This ADA constraint is significant because Picnic Point is explicitly referenced by Snohomish County as an accessible alternative to Meadowdale Beach Park, whose trail descends approximately 500 feet through a steep forested ravine to the beach. The relative accessibility comparison is accurate — Picnic Point's overpass is substantially less demanding than Meadowdale's ravine trail — but the overpass itself is not barrier-free, and the distinction matters for visitors who were directed to Picnic Point on the assumption of full accessibility (Snohomish County DCNR).

5. Beach Character and Conditions

The beach at Picnic Point is a mixed rocky and gravel surface — not a sandy swimming beach in the conventional sense. The substrate consists of glacially derived sand, gravel, and cobble typical of central Puget Sound shorelines, with the grain-size distribution that WDFW has documented as suitable for forage fish spawning in the upper intertidal zone (WDFW, Forage Fish Management Plan, 1998). Walking on the beach surface requires sturdy footwear; the uneven gravel and occasional cobble make barefoot walking uncomfortable and inadvisable.

Large driftwood logs accumulate along the upper beach, particularly north of the Picnic Point Creek mouth. These logs are a natural feature of Puget Sound beaches, delivered by longshore drift and winter storm events. They provide informal seating, windbreak, and habitat for beach invertebrates, but they also represent a safety consideration: driftwood logs can roll unexpectedly on incoming tides or when waves undermine their resting position. WDFW and Washington State Parks recommend never sitting on the water side of a driftwood log or climbing on unstable log accumulations (Washington State Parks).

The water temperature in central Puget Sound ranges from approximately 45°F (7°C) in winter to approximately 55°F (13°C) in summer (NOAA, Mukilteo Station #9447659). These temperatures are cold by any swimming standard; immersion without a wetsuit carries the risk of cold-water shock and hypothermia. The park is not staffed with lifeguards. Wading is common in summer; extended swimming is uncommon and carries real risk (NOAA).

6. Views, Orientation, and Sunset Viewing

Picnic Point's westward-facing beach provides one of the more expansive viewsheds available from an accessible public park along the eastern shore of central Puget Sound. The view extends across the Sound to Whidbey Island, the largest island in Washington State, which forms the western boundary of the visible horizon from the beach. Behind Whidbey, on clear days, the Olympic Mountains rise along the western skyline, with the peaks of the Olympic Range visible from Mount Constance in the south through The Brothers to points north (USGS; Washington State Geographic Names).

Glimpses of the Cascade Range are available from the bluff-top parking area, where the elevated vantage point provides sight lines to the northeast and east. Mount Baker (10,781 ft, USGS) may be visible on clear days from the bluff, though the primary viewshed from the beach itself is oriented westward across the Sound.

The westward orientation makes Picnic Point a popular sunset viewing location, particularly during the extended daylight of Pacific Northwest summers. The park closes at dusk, and this closure is enforced at the actual onset of dusk rather than at a fixed clock time. Visitor reports confirm that the park does not close at a posted 5 PM; rather, the closure follows the natural light cycle, which in midsummer (late June) extends past 9:30 PM PDT and in midwinter (late December) arrives by approximately 4:30 PM PST (Snohomish County DCNR; U.S. Naval Observatory, sunrise/sunset tables for Everett, WA).

7. Tide Tables and Planning for Beach Access

The tidal regime at Picnic Point governs every aspect of beach recreation, from whether a family can spread a blanket on dry gravel to whether a forage fish surveyor can access the upper intertidal sampling zone to whether a hiker can walk to Meadowdale Beach without being trapped by rising water. Understanding the tides is not optional for visitors who intend to use the beach beyond the narrow strip of dry gravel above the ordinary high water line.

7.1 Tidal Regime

Puget Sound experiences a mixed semidiurnal tidal regime, producing two high tides and two low tides of unequal height within each lunar day (approximately 24 hours and 50 minutes). The nearest NOAA reference tide station to Picnic Point is Mukilteo (Station #9447659), which provides the following tidal datums (NOAA, Tidal Datums, Mukilteo Station):

7.2 How to Read Tide Predictions

NOAA publishes daily tide predictions for the Mukilteo station through its Tides and Currents website (tidesandcurrents.noaa.gov). Predictions are given as the time and height (in feet above MLLW) of each high and low tide. For beach recreation and hiking at Picnic Point, the critical parameters are:

  • Low-tide height: Tides predicted below +2.0 ft MLLW provide good beach walking conditions. Tides below 0.0 ft MLLW (negative tides) expose the broadest intertidal surface and are ideal for the longer beach hikes north toward Mukilteo.
  • Low-tide timing: The time of the lowest daily tide determines the window for beach access. For the longer routes (Meadowdale southbound, Mukilteo northbound), the low-tide window must be broad enough to allow the round trip before the tide returns.
  • Tidal range on that day: The difference between the day's high and low tides determines how rapidly the beach narrows on the incoming (flood) tide. Large ranges (12+ ft) mean faster water rise and less margin for error.

Tide predictions are freely available from NOAA at tidesandcurrents.noaa.gov and from mobile applications that source NOAA data. The Washington Trails Association recommends checking tide predictions before any beach walk and building a time buffer of at least one hour before the predicted incoming tide reaches the route's narrowest points (WTA).

7.3 Seasonal Tide Patterns

The timing of low tides in Puget Sound follows a seasonal pattern that is important for planning both recreation and community science fieldwork. In summer months (June through August), the lowest tides of the day tend to occur in the early morning hours, typically between 5:00 AM and 9:00 AM. In winter months (November through February), the lowest tides shift to afternoon hours, typically between 12:00 PM and 4:00 PM. Spring and fall represent transitional periods. This pattern is driven by the interaction of lunar tidal constituents and is consistent from year to year (NOAA, Tidal Datums).

The practical consequence for visitors is that the best beach walking conditions in summer require early-morning arrivals, while winter low tides are more conveniently timed for afternoon visits. For the April 25, 2026 City Nature Challenge bioblitz, the relevant low tides will fall during the morning-to-midday transition period; participants should check NOAA predictions for the specific date and plan arrival times accordingly.

8. Tide-Dependent Beach Hiking Routes

The Washington Trails Association documents that no established hiking trails exist within Picnic Point Park itself. What WTA and experienced local visitors recognize, however, is that the intertidal beach functions as a tide-dependent hiking corridor that connects Picnic Point to adjacent shoreline destinations in both directions. These routes are not trails in the conventional sense — they are exposed beach surfaces that exist only at low tide and that can become impassable or dangerous when the tide returns (WTA).

Three routes are documented below, ranging from a moderate 1.5-mile walk to a committed 5-mile traverse. All three require tide planning, and two of them involve stretches where the beach narrows against bluffs or armored shoreline at higher tides. The WTA issues a standing advisory: do not get stuck on the beach by an incoming tide, particularly on the longer northward route (WTA).

The walk south from Picnic Point to Meadowdale Beach Park follows the intertidal beach along the base of glacially derived bluffs and the BNSF railroad grade. The route is passable at low tide but becomes difficult or impassable at high tide, when the water reaches the armored shoreline and bluff base in several locations, leaving no dry passage. Meadowdale Beach Park is the site of the recently completed Meadowdale Beach Park and Estuary Restoration Project, a major Snohomish County capital project that replaced the former culvert under the BNSF mainline with a bridge and boardwalk to restore tidal and fish passage to Lund's Gulch Creek (Snohomish County DCNR).

This is the most commonly attempted beach walk from Picnic Point and is suitable for visitors with moderate fitness and sturdy footwear, provided they plan around the tide. The walk is entirely on mixed gravel and cobble — no sand, no paved surfaces. The round trip requires a tide window of approximately 2.5 to 3 hours centered on low tide, depending on walking speed and time spent at Meadowdale (WTA).

The walk north from Picnic Point leads to Shipwreck Point, named for the remains of the MV Pacific Queen, a former Bristol Bay cannery tender that was beached at Franzen Beach in 1957 and has gradually deteriorated into the shoreline over the subsequent decades (Puget Sound Maritime Historical Society; see Module D: History & Cultural Context for the full vessel chronology). The wreck is visible from the beach at low tide as a partially collapsed wooden hull structure.

Private property is posted no-trespassing at this location. Visitors should observe the wreck from the public beach and intertidal zone and must not enter posted private property. The upland property at Franzen Beach is privately owned and the no-trespassing signage is clearly posted (Snohomish County DCNR).

The full northbound walk from Picnic Point to Point Elliott at Mukilteo is a 5-mile beach traverse that represents a significant commitment in terms of both physical effort and tide planning. This is not a casual walk: the route passes through several locations where the beach narrows against armored shoreline and bluff base at higher tides, and the total distance makes a round trip of 10 miles on mixed gravel — a demanding day even for experienced coastal hikers (WTA).

Point Elliott at Mukilteo is the historic location where the Treaty of Point Elliott was signed on January 22, 1855, between territorial governor Isaac Stevens and leaders of the Duwamish, Suquamish, Snoqualmie, Snohomish, Lummi, Skagit, Swinomish, and other tribes and bands, including the ancestors of the present-day Tulalip Tribes. The treaty signing site is commemorated at Mukilteo Lighthouse Park (Treaty of Point Elliott, 12 Stat. 927, 1855; Tulalip Tribes).

The WTA advisory for this route is emphatic: plan for tide reversal. A hiker who departs Picnic Point at the start of a falling tide and walks north for 2.5 hours will arrive at Mukilteo near low tide, but the return walk will coincide with the rising tide. If the tidal range on that day is large (12+ ft), the beach may narrow significantly during the return trip. Experienced coastal hikers plan this route as a one-way walk with a vehicle shuttle or transit connection at Mukilteo, rather than attempting the full 10-mile round trip on a single tide window (WTA).

9. Safety and Hazards

Beach recreation at Picnic Point involves hazards that differ from those of inland parks and trail systems. The following safety considerations are sourced from NOAA, WDFW, Washington State Parks, and the Washington Trails Association.

9.1 Tidal Entrapment

The primary hazard for beach hikers is tidal entrapment: being caught between rising water and an impassable bluff, armored shoreline, or railroad grade with no upland escape route. This hazard is most acute on the longer northbound route toward Mukilteo, where several stretches of shoreline offer no exit to high ground between access points separated by a mile or more. The WTA advisory is direct: do not get stuck on the beach by an incoming tide (WTA).

Mitigation requires checking NOAA tide predictions before departure, timing the walk to center on low tide, and building a buffer of at least one hour before the predicted flood tide reaches the route's narrowest sections. Carrying a waterproof tide chart or a mobile application that sources NOAA predictions is recommended.

9.2 Cold Water

Puget Sound water temperatures at the Picnic Point latitude range from approximately 45°F (7°C) in winter to approximately 55°F (13°C) in summer (NOAA, Mukilteo Station #9447659). These temperatures are sufficient to cause cold-water shock (sudden involuntary gasping upon immersion) and hypothermia within minutes to tens of minutes depending on body mass, clothing, and water temperature. The National Water Safety Congress classifies water below 60°F (15.5°C) as dangerous for unprotected immersion. The park has no lifeguards (National Water Safety Congress; NOAA).

9.3 Driftwood

Large driftwood logs on Puget Sound beaches can weigh several tons and may roll unpredictably when lifted by wave action or undermined by rising water. Washington State Parks and WDFW advise against sitting on the seaward side of driftwood logs, climbing on log accumulations, or playing in areas where logs are floating in the surf zone. Fatalities from rolling driftwood logs occur on Pacific Northwest beaches, though they are uncommon (Washington State Parks).

9.4 Railroad Proximity

The BNSF Railway mainline runs immediately adjacent to the upper beach. Trains operate on this line frequently, and their approach can be difficult to hear over wind and wave noise, particularly for visitors wearing headphones or earbuds. Crossing the tracks at any point other than the designated overpass is prohibited, dangerous, and illegal (BNSF Railway).

9.5 Shellfish Safety

A year-round shellfish harvest closure is in effect for the Picnic Point area, as posted by the Washington Department of Health in coordination with WDFW. Visitors must not harvest or consume shellfish from this beach. The closure is driven by water quality and contamination concerns and is enforceable regardless of season (Washington Department of Health, Shellfish Safety Information).

10. Community Science: iNaturalist and the City Nature Challenge

Community science — sometimes called citizen science — provides a mechanism for park visitors to contribute directly to the ecological monitoring and land management decisions that shape the future of Picnic Point. Two complementary programs operate at the park: the iNaturalist platform, which accepts observations year-round from any visitor, and the Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee's forage fish survey program, which follows a structured monthly sampling protocol developed by WDFW. This section addresses iNaturalist; the MRC forage fish protocol is detailed in Section 11.

10.1 What Is iNaturalist?

iNaturalist is a biodiversity observation platform jointly managed by the California Academy of Sciences and the National Geographic Society. It allows anyone with a smartphone or camera to submit geotagged, time-stamped photographic observations of plants, animals, fungi, and other organisms to a global database. As of 2025, iNaturalist hosts more than 200 million observations from more than 3 million users worldwide (iNaturalist; California Academy of Sciences).

Observations submitted to iNaturalist are reviewed by a global community of naturalists, taxonomists, and subject-matter experts who assist with species identification. When two or more independent identifiers agree on a species determination, the observation achieves "Research Grade" status and is automatically exported to the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF), where it becomes available to researchers, government agencies, and conservation organizations worldwide (iNaturalist; GBIF).

Snohomish County DCNR has indicated that data from iNaturalist observations feeds into government land management decisions, making each Research Grade observation a potential input to the management of parks, natural areas, and wildlife habitat at the county level (Snohomish County DCNR).

10.2 How to Participate: Step-by-Step Guide for Visitors

The following instructions describe how a visitor to Picnic Point Park can participate in iNaturalist-based community science. No prior scientific training is required. The entire process can be completed with a smartphone.

Step 1: Download the iNaturalist App

The iNaturalist app is available free of charge from the Apple App Store (iOS) and Google Play Store (Android). Search for "iNaturalist" and install the app published by the California Academy of Sciences. Create a free account using an email address or existing Google/Apple account (iNaturalist).

Step 2: Enable Location Services

When prompted, allow the iNaturalist app to access your device's location. Geotagging is essential: it records where the observation was made and allows the data to be associated with Picnic Point Park specifically. Without location data, the observation has substantially reduced scientific value (iNaturalist).

Step 3: Observe and Photograph

When you encounter an organism at Picnic Point — a bird, a plant, a fungus on a driftwood log, an intertidal invertebrate, a seaweed, or anything else alive — photograph it using the iNaturalist app's built-in camera or your phone's native camera. Multiple photos from different angles improve the likelihood of accurate identification. For plants, include photos of leaves, flowers (if present), bark, and overall growth form. For birds, any identifiable photo is valuable, even at a distance (iNaturalist).

Step 4: Submit the Observation

In the iNaturalist app, tap "Observe" and either take a new photo or select a photo from your camera roll. The app will automatically suggest a species identification using its computer vision model. You may accept the suggestion, modify it, or leave the identification blank for the community to determine. Add any notes about the observation (behavior, habitat, context) and submit. The observation is now in the global database (iNaturalist).

Step 5: Engage with the Community

After submission, other iNaturalist users may comment on your observation, suggest different identifications, or ask for additional photos. Responding to these interactions improves the accuracy of the dataset. When two or more identifiers agree on the species, the observation achieves Research Grade and is automatically shared with GBIF and government agencies (iNaturalist; GBIF).

10.3 What Counts as an Observation

iNaturalist observations are not limited to direct sightings of live organisms. The following types of evidence are valid submissions, and all are relevant to the Picnic Point shoreline environment:

  • Direct sightings: Photographs of living plants, animals, fungi, lichens, algae, or other organisms
  • Tracks and sign: Animal tracks in sand or mud, including shorebird tracks, mammal tracks, and invertebrate trails
  • Shells and exoskeletons: Mollusk shells, crab exoskeletons, barnacle plates, and other hard-part remains
  • Indirect evidence: Feathers, scat, nests (do not disturb), owl pellets, feeding sign on vegetation, and burrows
  • Sound recordings: Bird songs and calls can be submitted as audio observations and identified by the birding community

For the April 25, 2026 City Nature Challenge bioblitz at Picnic Point, all of these observation types are valid and encouraged. The bioblitz is specifically designed to capture the broadest possible snapshot of biodiversity at the site on a single day, and indirect evidence such as tracks, shells, and feeding sign contributes meaningfully to that goal (California Academy of Sciences; Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds).

10.4 The City Nature Challenge

The City Nature Challenge is an annual worldwide community science event co-led by the California Academy of Sciences and the Natural History Museums of Los Angeles County. The 2026 event marks the 10th year of the challenge. During the observation window (typically a four-day period in late April), participants in cities around the world submit as many iNaturalist observations as possible, generating a global snapshot of urban and peri-urban biodiversity. The identification period follows, during which the global naturalist community works to identify the submitted observations to species level (California Academy of Sciences; Natural History Museums of Los Angeles County).

The scale of participation is substantial. In the most recent completed global event, participants submitted more than 3 million observations and identified more than 73,000 species worldwide (California Academy of Sciences). The 2026 Picnic Point bioblitz, organized by the Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds, is part of this global event.

11. Community Science: MRC Forage Fish Survey Protocol

The Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee (MRC) operates a long-running forage fish spawning survey program that represents one of the most sustained community science efforts on the eastern shore of central Puget Sound. Since 2011, trained MRC volunteers have collected more than 700 intertidal beach sediment samples along the Snohomish County shoreline, following protocols developed and maintained by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW). The data from these surveys are provided directly to WDFW for evaluation of restoration project effectiveness and for maintaining the statewide forage fish spawning habitat inventory (Snohomish County MRC; WDFW).

Unlike iNaturalist, which accepts observations from any visitor at any time, the MRC forage fish survey program is a structured, protocol-driven effort conducted by trained volunteers on a regular monthly schedule. The protocol described below is included for educational purposes and to provide visitors with an understanding of the scientific work being conducted on the beaches they walk. Visitors who wish to participate in forage fish surveys should contact the Snohomish County MRC, which provides training and coordinates survey teams.

11.1 Purpose of the Survey

The forage fish survey program is designed to detect the presence of forage fish eggs — primarily surf smelt (Hypomesus pretiosus) and Pacific sand lance (Ammodytes personatus) eggs — in the upper intertidal zone of Snohomish County beaches. The presence of eggs confirms that a beach is being used for spawning, which in turn triggers WDFW regulatory protections and influences land-use decisions that affect shoreline development and restoration (WDFW, Forage Fish Management Plan, 1998; Snohomish County MRC).

11.2 Sampling Protocol: The WDFW Standard Method

The following protocol summary describes the WDFW-standard method used by Snohomish County MRC volunteers. All procedural details are sourced from WDFW forage fish survey protocols and Snohomish County MRC documentation.

11.2.1 Transect Definition

Each sampling event targets a defined 100-foot transect in the upper intertidal zone of the beach. The transect is oriented parallel to the shoreline at the tidal elevation where forage fish spawning is most likely to occur — typically between the mean higher high water line and the ordinary high water line, corresponding to the zone that is wetted by wave action during spawning tides but exposed during lower tides (WDFW).

11.2.2 Sample Collection

The surveyor collects 1.5 to 2 liters of sand and gravel from the top 1 to 2 inches of the beach surface along the 100-foot transect. The sample is collected by scraping the surface layer into a container using a trowel or scoop. The shallow collection depth targets the zone where forage fish eggs are deposited and adhere to substrate particles (WDFW; Snohomish County MRC).

11.2.3 Sieving

The collected sample is processed through a nested series of three mesh sieves to separate material by grain size and concentrate any forage fish eggs present in the sample:

SieveMesh SizePurpose
First (coarsest)4 mmRemoves large gravel, shell fragments, and debris that are too large to contain or conceal forage fish eggs
Second (intermediate)2 mmRetains medium-grain material; removes sand fine enough to pass through while retaining particles in the size range where eggs may be attached
Third (finest)0.5 mmCaptures forage fish eggs and fine-grain material; eggs are typically 0.5–1.5 mm in diameter and are retained on this mesh

Surf smelt eggs are approximately 1.0–1.2 mm in diameter; Pacific sand lance eggs are approximately 0.7–1.0 mm in diameter. Both species' eggs are adhesive and tend to adhere to sand grains, making them visible as small, round, translucent-to-amber spheres attached to substrate particles in the sieve residue (WDFW, Forage Fish Identification Guide).

11.2.4 Egg Detection and Recording

After sieving, the material retained on the 0.5 mm mesh is examined visually, typically under a hand lens or low-power magnifier in the field, and later under a dissecting microscope in the laboratory. Surveyors record the presence or absence of eggs, the estimated number of eggs, the species (if identifiable from egg morphology), and the developmental stage of the eggs if visible. All data are recorded on standardized WDFW data sheets and submitted to both the Snohomish County MRC and WDFW (WDFW; Snohomish County MRC).

11.3 Survey Frequency and Coverage

The Snohomish County MRC conducts forage fish surveys on a monthly schedule, sampling designated transects along the county shoreline throughout the year. The monthly frequency captures seasonal variation in spawning activity: surf smelt in Puget Sound may spawn year-round with peak activity varying by beach, while Pacific sand lance spawn primarily from November through February (WDFW, Penttila 2007). The monthly cycle ensures that neither species' spawning window is missed.

Since the program's inception in 2011, MRC volunteers have collected more than 700 samples along the Snohomish County shoreline. This sustained data collection effort represents one of the most robust community science datasets for forage fish spawning habitat in the Puget Sound region. The data have been used by WDFW to evaluate the effectiveness of shoreline restoration projects and to maintain the statewide inventory of documented forage fish spawning beaches (Snohomish County MRC; WDFW).

11.4 How to Get Involved

The MRC forage fish survey program is volunteer-based but requires training before participation. Volunteers learn the sampling protocol, egg identification techniques, data recording procedures, and quality assurance standards through training sessions coordinated by the Snohomish County MRC. Interested visitors should contact the Snohomish County Marine Resources Committee through the Snohomish County DCNR website to inquire about training opportunities and upcoming survey dates (Snohomish County MRC).

12. Indigenous Context: Tulalip Tribes

The shoreline at Picnic Point lies within the traditional territory of the Tulalip Tribes, a federally recognized tribal nation whose reservation occupies the northern shoreline of Possession Sound and Port Gardner Bay, approximately 10 miles north of Picnic Point. The Tulalip Tribes are the successors in interest to the Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, and other tribes and bands that were signatories to the Treaty of Point Elliott, signed on January 22, 1855, at the point now known as Point Elliott in Mukilteo — the same Point Elliott that marks the northern terminus of the 5-mile beach walk described in Section 8.3 (Treaty of Point Elliott, 12 Stat. 927, 1855; Tulalip Tribes).

The Tulalip Tribes maintain active natural resources management programs, including fisheries management, shellfish management, habitat restoration, and environmental monitoring throughout their usual and accustomed fishing areas, which encompass the central Puget Sound shoreline including the Picnic Point reach. The shoreline resources documented in this module — forage fish, shellfish, intertidal habitats, and the creek ecosystem — were historically and remain presently significant to the Tulalip Tribes' resource management interests (Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources Department).

The recreational and community science activities described in this module occur on the ancestral and ceded lands of the Tulalip Tribes. Visitors participating in community science activities at Picnic Point contribute data that may be relevant to the Tulalip Tribes' resource management programs. Any future expansion of community science partnerships at the site should include consultation with the Tulalip Tribes Natural Resources Department regarding data sharing, cultural resource considerations, and the integration of Indigenous ecological knowledge into monitoring program design.

Research limitation: This module does not include Indigenous ecological knowledge claims that have not been published or authorized for public use by the Tulalip Tribes. The absence of this knowledge should be understood as a limitation of the present research scope, not a statement about its relevance or value.

13. Synthesis: Where Recreation Meets Monitoring

Picnic Point Park occupies an unusual position in the landscape of Puget Sound public parks. It is simultaneously one of the most accessible shoreline parks in Snohomish County — a place where families picnic, dogs wade, and sunset watchers linger — and an active field station for two community science programs that generate data used by state and county agencies to manage nearshore habitat. The park's modest facilities (a small parking lot, a pedestrian overpass, picnic areas, restrooms, and the beach itself) belie the ecological and scientific significance of what happens on its shoreline.

The access constraints documented in this module — the parking lot that fills on summer weekends, the overpass that is not fully ADA-accessible, the tide windows that govern beach hiking, the dusk closure that follows the natural light cycle rather than a fixed clock — are not obstacles to be engineered away but parameters that shape a particular kind of visitor experience. The park demands that visitors plan around natural cycles (tides, daylight) and shared resources (parking, the single overpass), and this demand creates a pace of engagement that is more compatible with careful observation than with high-throughput recreation.

The community science programs at Picnic Point translate this observational engagement into scientific data. The iNaturalist platform, accessible to any visitor with a smartphone, converts casual encounters with beach organisms into geotagged, time-stamped, expert-reviewed biodiversity records that feed into global databases and local land management decisions. The City Nature Challenge bioblitz, with more than 3 million observations and 73,000 species identified in its most recent global iteration, represents a scale of data collection that no professional survey team could replicate alone (California Academy of Sciences).

The MRC forage fish survey program represents a different model of community science: structured, protocol-driven, and longitudinal. With more than 700 samples collected since 2011 following standardized WDFW methods, the MRC dataset provides the kind of sustained temporal coverage that is essential for detecting trends in forage fish spawning activity and evaluating the effectiveness of shoreline restoration projects. The 100-foot transect, the 1.5-to-2-liter sample, the three nested sieves (4 mm, 2 mm, 0.5 mm) — these procedural details are not bureaucratic overhead but the quality controls that make the data usable by WDFW for regulatory and management purposes (Snohomish County MRC; WDFW).

The three tide-dependent beach hiking routes documented in this module — 1.5 miles south to Meadowdale Beach Park, 1 mile north to Shipwreck Point, and 5 miles north to Point Elliott at Mukilteo — situate Picnic Point within a larger shoreline corridor that connects multiple park systems, historical sites, and ecological zones. These routes exist only at low tide, and their ephemeral nature reinforces the central lesson of this park: the shoreline is not a fixed stage for human activity but a dynamic surface governed by astronomical forces that visitors must accommodate rather than override.

The convergence of recreation, access, and community science at Picnic Point creates a feedback loop in which the act of visiting the park can contribute to its long-term ecological management. A sunset watcher who photographs an unfamiliar seaweed on iNaturalist, a dog walker who notices shorebird tracks in the sand and submits the observation, a family that times its beach walk to the tide table — each of these interactions generates data or develops the kind of tidal literacy that supports both safe recreation and effective ecological monitoring. The park's value lies not in its facilities but in the quality of attention it demands and rewards.
