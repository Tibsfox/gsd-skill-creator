Systems administration is ultimately a human discipline. The tools change every few years, but the practices — on-call rotations, incident response, knowledge management, mentorship — have deep roots. With LOPSA’s dissolution in 2025, preserving and transmitting this professional knowledge becomes the responsibility of every practicing sysadmin.

On-Call Best Practices

On-call is the defining feature of systems administration work. Done well, it provides fast incident response with sustainable human cost. Done poorly, it burns out teams and degrades reliability.

  • Rotation structure: Primary + secondary, weekly rotation, follow-the-sun for global teams
  • Escalation paths: Clear escalation from primary to secondary to management to vendor
  • Runbooks: Every alert links to a runbook with diagnostic steps and remediation actions
  • Compensation: On-call time must be compensated — either financially or with time off
  • Load balancing: Track pages per person per rotation; redistribute if imbalanced
  • Rest after incidents: Extended incidents should trigger rest periods (follow the sun or next-day off)

Google’s SRE book recommends that on-call engineers handle no more than 2 events per 12-hour shift on average. More than that indicates systemic reliability problems, not personnel problems.

ITIL Change Management

Change management controls the lifecycle of infrastructure changes to minimize risk. The ITIL framework defines:

Change TypeApprovalExampleRisk
StandardPre-approvedPatching, adding a user, scalingLow
NormalCAB (Change Advisory Board)New service deployment, network changeMedium
EmergencyExpedited (post-hoc CAB review)Security patch for active exploitHigh

Modern organizations implement change management through CI/CD pipelines and GitOps: a pull request is the change request, code review is the CAB, and merge-to-main triggers the deployment. This automates approval workflows while preserving the audit trail.

Documentation & Knowledge Base

Documentation is the institutional memory of the team. Without it, knowledge walks out the door when people leave. Essential documentation types:

  • Runbooks: Step-by-step procedures for common operational tasks and incident response
  • Architecture diagrams: Network topology, service dependencies, data flow
  • Decision records (ADRs): Why a particular technology or architecture was chosen
  • Post-incident reviews: What happened, why, what was learned, what changed
  • Onboarding guides: What a new team member needs to know in their first week

Tools: Confluence (enterprise), Notion (modern), MkDocs/Docusaurus (docs-as-code), wiki.js (open source). The docs-as-code approach (Markdown in Git) is increasingly preferred because it keeps documentation in the same review workflow as code.

MitigationRestore service ASAP (rollback, failover, scale)Mitigate first, investigate later
CommunicationStatus page updates, stakeholder notificationsSilence breeds fear; over-communicate
ResolutionRoot cause fix deployed and verifiedVerified in production, not just staging

Blameless Postmortems

The blameless postmortem (formalized by Google SRE) focuses on system and process failures, not individual blame. The goal is learning, not punishment.

Postmortem template:

  • Summary: What happened, when, impact scope
  • Timeline: Minute-by-minute from detection to resolution
  • Root cause: Why the failure occurred (technical and organizational)
  • What went well: Detection speed, team coordination, tooling that helped
  • What went wrong: Delays, tool gaps, communication failures
  • Action items: Specific, assigned, time-bound improvements

For deeper methodology on root cause investigation, see RCA: Software Engineering & SRE.

Toil Elimination

Google SRE defines toil as manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of enduring value, and scaling linearly with service growth. SRE teams should spend no more than 50% of their time on toil; the rest goes to engineering work that reduces future toil.

Toil elimination ROI calculation: if a task takes 10 minutes, happens 5 times per week, and automation takes 8 hours to build, the breakeven is ~10 weeks. Beyond breakeven, every week returns 50 minutes. The xkcd “Is It Worth the Time?” chart remains the canonical reference for this calculation.

Career Paths

The sysadmin career has evolved from a single role into a family of specializations:

Entry Junior Linux Administrator — system setup, user management, basic troubleshooting, ticket resolution
Mid Systems Administrator / DevOps Engineer — automation, CI/CD, infrastructure as code, monitoring
Senior Senior SRE / Staff Engineer — reliability architecture, SLO design, incident management, mentorship
Principal Platform Engineer / Principal SRE — internal developer platform design, organization-wide infrastructure strategy
Leadership Director of Infrastructure / VP Engineering — team building, budget, vendor management, organizational reliability culture

Certifications that carry weight: RHCSA/RHCE (Red Hat), CKA/CKAD/CKS (Kubernetes), AWS SAA/SAP (cloud), CompTIA Linux+ (entry). Experience consistently outweighs certifications for hiring, but certifications validate baseline knowledge.

LOPSA/SAGE Ethics Code

The LOPSA Code of Ethics (adapted from SAGE) established professional standards that remain relevant despite the organization’s dissolution:

  • Professionalism: Maintain and improve technical skills continuously
  • Personal integrity: Be honest about capabilities and limitations
  • Privacy: Respect the privacy of users; access data only as required for duty
  • Laws and policies: Operate within legal and organizational policy boundaries
  • Communication: Share knowledge, educate users, communicate risks clearly
  • System integrity: Maintain system security and availability as a primary duty
  • Education: Contribute to the profession through teaching and mentorship

The most critical ethical obligation for a sysadmin is not abusing privileged access. Root access to production systems means access to all data, all communications, and all credentials. This trust must be earned and maintained through consistent professional behavior.

Mentorship & Teaching

With LOPSA gone, mentorship falls to individual practitioners and informal communities. Effective mentorship practices: pair on-call rotations (senior + junior), incident debriefs as teaching moments, code review of infrastructure changes, brown-bag sessions on new technologies, and conference talk encouragement. The best sysadmins are also teachers.
