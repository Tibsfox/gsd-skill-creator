Data is the reason systems exist. Storage and filesystem decisions are among the most consequential a sysadmin makes — they affect performance, reliability, and recoverability for years after the initial choice. Getting backups wrong is an extinction-level event for any organization.

ZFS provides the strongest data integrity guarantees with copy-on-write, built-in RAID (RAIDZ1/2/3), deduplication, compression, and send/receive for replication. Its main limitation on Linux is the CDDL license incompatibility with GPL, requiring out-of-tree kernel modules via OpenZFS.

RAID is not a backup. RAID protects against disk failure, not against accidental deletion, ransomware, corruption, or site loss. RAID and backups serve orthogonal purposes.

LVM (Logical Volume Management)

LVM adds a flexible abstraction layer between physical disks and filesystems. Physical Volumes (PVs) are grouped into Volume Groups (VGs), which are carved into Logical Volumes (LVs). This enables online resizing, snapshots, thin provisioning, and migration across physical disks without downtime.

# Create a thin-provisioned LV with snapshot support
pvcreate /dev/sdb1
vgcreate data_vg /dev/sdb1
lvcreate -L 100G --thinpool thin_pool data_vg
lvcreate -V 50G --thin -n app_data data_vg/thin_pool

Backup Strategies

The 3-2-1 rule is the minimum viable backup strategy:

3 copies of data (1 primary + 2 backups)

2 different storage media types

1 copy offsite (geographic separation)

Backup types: Full (complete copy, slow, large), Incremental (changes since last backup, fast, small, requires chain for restore), Differential (changes since last full, medium, requires only last full for restore). Modern tools: restic (dedup, encrypted, multi-backend), BorgBackup (dedup, compressed), rsync (efficient delta transfer), Veeam (enterprise VM backup).

Test your restores. An untested backup is not a backup. Schedule regular restore drills.

Disaster Recovery

Two metrics define DR requirements:

RTO (Recovery Time Objective) = maximum acceptable downtime
RPO (Recovery Point Objective) = maximum acceptable data loss (time)

A 4-hour RTO with 1-hour RPO means the system must be restored within 4 hours, losing at most 1 hour of data. These metrics drive architecture decisions: synchronous replication for RPO=0, warm standby for low RTO, cold backups for cost-optimized high-RTO scenarios.
