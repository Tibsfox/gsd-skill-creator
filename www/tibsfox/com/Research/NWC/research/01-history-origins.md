## 1. Founding

In 1978, a group of Pacific Northwest science fiction fans had a practical problem to solve. They wanted to bid for the 1981 World Science Fiction Convention (Worldcon) to be held in Seattle, but hosting a Worldcon requires organizational experience that can only be gained by running conventions. The solution was to create their own regional convention -- not as an end in itself, but as a training exercise [1][2].

The first Norwescon was held in what is now the city of SeaTac, Washington. Theodore Sturgeon -- author of *More Than Human*, originator of Sturgeon's Law ("ninety percent of everything is crap"), and one of the foundational voices of American science fiction -- served as the inaugural Guest of Honor. The convention drew just over 400 attendees [1][2].

Simultaneously with the convention itself, the founders created the Northwest Science Fiction Society (NWSFS), a 501(c)(3) charitable organization designed to build and sustain the volunteer base needed to run conventions. NWSFS would become Norwescon's permanent parent body, providing legal and organizational infrastructure that outlasted any individual volunteer or leadership team [1][2].

### 1.1 The Name

The name "Norwescon" carried historical weight that the founders chose deliberately. It was also the name of the 8th World Science Fiction Convention, held in Portland, Oregon, in 1950. By naming their new regional convention after a historic PNW Worldcon, the founders were connecting their effort to the deep tradition of organized science fiction fandom in the Pacific Northwest -- a lineage stretching back three decades before their first meeting [1].

## 2. The Worldcon Connection

The 1981 Seattle Worldcon bid that prompted Norwescon's creation sits within a larger context. Worldcons had been held since 1939, when the first World Science Fiction Convention took place in New York City alongside the World's Fair. The Worldcon is where the Hugo Awards are presented, where the World Science Fiction Society conducts its business, and where the global SF community gathers annually [1][2].

Regional conventions like Norwescon serve as the local infrastructure for this global network. They develop volunteers, nurture communities, provide year-round gathering spaces, and build the organizational competence that a traveling event like Worldcon cannot maintain on its own. The relationship is symbiotic: Worldcons provide prestige and global connection; regional conventions provide continuity and community [1].

What makes Norwescon's founding story remarkable is that the practice run outlasted the thing it was practicing for. Whether or not the 1981 bid succeeded, Norwescon kept running. The convention that was built as scaffolding became the permanent structure [1].

## 3. Venue Migrations

Norwescon's venue history is a map of growth, crisis, and eventual stability:

| Years | Venue | Conventions | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------------|-------|
| 1978 | Unincorporated SeaTac area | NWC 1 | 400+ attendees; Theodore Sturgeon GoH |
| 1979--1984 | Hyatt Hotel, SeaTac | NWC II--VII | Site of the 1961 Worldcon |
| 1985--1986 | Red Lion Inn, SeaTac | NWC VIII--IX | Growth pressures forced the move |
| 1987 | Hyatt Hotel (return) | "Alternacon" | Emergency relocation under different name |
| 1988--1992 | Sheraton, Tacoma | NWC X--XIV* | Five-year run in a different city |
| 1993 | Bellevue area | One year | Brief stop |
| 1994--present | DoubleTree SeaTac | NWC XV--48* | Continuous except 2020--2021 |

Each move tells a story. The initial Hyatt years were the convention's childhood -- finding its voice, building its audience. The Red Lion move was growth: the Hyatt could no longer contain the event. The emergency Alternacon return was crisis management. The Tacoma years were an experiment in relocating to a different part of the metro area. The brief Bellevue stop was a transition. And the 1994 arrival at the DoubleTree (originally the Red Lion under a new brand) was homecoming -- a return to the SeaTac corridor where it all began [1].

## 4. The Alternacon Interlude

In 1987, circumstances forced Norwescon to return temporarily to the Hyatt Hotel after two years at the Red Lion. To distinguish this emergency-relocated event from the regular convention series, it was held under the name "Alternacon." The name acknowledged that this was not business as usual while maintaining continuity for the community [1].

The Alternacon episode had a lasting effect on the convention's numbering. Norwescon 13 was skipped in the sequence, but since the numbering had already skipped a year for the Alternacon, the year count eventually resynced with the convention number. This kind of numbering irregularity is common in long-running convention series -- it is the organizational equivalent of a fossil record, preserving evidence of past disruptions in the sequence itself [1].

## 5. Growth and Identity

Through the 1980s and 1990s, Norwescon established its identity as the Pacific Northwest's premier literary science fiction convention. This was a deliberate positioning. As media conventions, comic cons, and gaming expos emerged and grew, Norwescon maintained its focus on books, writing, science, and ideas [1][2].

The convention's Guest of Honor tradition was central to this identity. Writer GoHs were authors -- not actors, not television personalities, not YouTube celebrities. Science GoHs were working researchers. Artist GoHs were illustrators and visual artists. The GoH roster was the convention's annual thesis statement: this is what we think matters [1][2].

By the time attendance regularly exceeded 3,000, Norwescon had developed its characteristic format: four days of programming spanning literature, science, art, gaming, costuming, and community events, all powered by an entirely volunteer workforce. The convention's character -- serious about ideas but joyful in execution, literary in focus but broad in programming, organized by professionals but staffed by volunteers -- was firmly established [1][2].

## 6. Finding Home at the DoubleTree

The 1994 return to the SeaTac corridor -- now at the DoubleTree Hotel (the former Red Lion site, rebranded) -- gave Norwescon the venue stability it needed. Over three decades later, the convention remains at the same hotel, now the DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport at 18740 International Blvd, SeaTac, WA 98188 [1][3].

The DoubleTree's advantages for a convention of Norwescon's size are practical: multiple ballrooms, sufficient conference rooms for 30+ simultaneous programming tracks, a party wing (Wing 5B), proximity to SeaTac Airport (light rail accessible), and a management relationship built over thirty years of annual occupation. The hotel knows how to host Norwescon because it has been hosting Norwescon for longer than some of its staff have been alive [1][3].

The venue stability also created cultural continuity. Veterans know which hallways lead to which ballrooms, which elevators are fastest to the party floor, where the quiet corners are for decompression. The physical space became part of the convention's institutional memory [3].

## 7. The COVID Disruption

Norwescon 43, scheduled for 2020, was cancelled due to the COVID-19 pandemic. It was the first cancellation in the convention's 42-year history -- a streak that had survived venue changes, organizational transitions, and the evolution of fandom itself [1].

The convention pivoted to a virtual format for 2021, holding Norwescon 43 online with Writer GoH Jacqueline Carey, Artist GoH Sana Takeda, and Science GoH Dr. Susan Langley. The virtual event maintained the convention's continuity -- the numbering sequence was not broken, the GoH tradition was honored, and the community had a gathering point even if it was not a physical one [1].

In-person conventions resumed in 2022. The return tested the volunteer infrastructure that had sustained Norwescon for over four decades. Could an all-volunteer organization survive a two-year hiatus? The answer was yes -- the same organizational principles that had kept the convention running through venue changes and leadership transitions proved adaptable enough to survive a global pandemic [1].

## 8. Norwescon 48

Norwescon 48 runs April 2--5, 2026, at the DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport. Forty-eight years of continuous operation (with the COVID interruption) makes it one of the longest-running regional science fiction conventions in the United States [1][3].

The 2026 guest roster reflects the convention's breadth: Writer GoH Ursula Vernon / T. Kingfisher, Artist GoH Geneva Bowers, Science GoH Summer Ash, Spotlight Publisher Orbit Books (represented by executive editor Bradley Englert), and Special Guest Dutch Bihary. The Philip K. Dick Award ceremony on Friday evening anchors the literary programming [3].

The convention that started as practice for a Worldcon bid has outlasted the bid itself, the hotels that first housed it, many of the publishers whose authors it celebrated, and even some of the literary genres it was built to serve. Forty-eight years. Same region, same mission, same community. The practice was the thing all along [1].

## 9. Numbering and Continuity

A note for the record: convention numbering in long-running series is its own archaeology. Norwescon's numbering reflects its history -- the Alternacon skip, the Norwescon 13 omission, the COVID cancellation that didn't break the sequence because the virtual year preserved it. Other long-running conventions have similar fossils in their numbering. It is the organizational equivalent of tree rings: you can read the history in the sequence [1].
