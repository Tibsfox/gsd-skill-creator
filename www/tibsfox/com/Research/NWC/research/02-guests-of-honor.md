## 1. The GoH Tradition

The Guest of Honor system is one of the defining features of literary science fiction conventions, and Norwescon has practiced it since its first year. Each convention invites guests across multiple categories -- typically a Writer GoH, Artist GoH, Science GoH, and one or more special guests or spotlight categories. The invitation is not a booking. It is a statement of values [1][2].

Selecting Guests of Honor is one of the most consequential decisions a convention committee makes each year. The choices signal what the convention thinks is important, whose work deserves attention, and what direction the community should be looking. A convention that invites only established names is saying something different from one that elevates emerging voices. A convention that maintains a Science GoH category is making a claim about the relationship between science fiction and actual science [1].

At Norwescon, the GoH tradition has evolved over 48 years from a single guest at the first convention to a multi-category roster that reflects the convention's broadening scope. The categories themselves tell a story: Writer, Artist, Science, Publisher, Special Guest. Each category is a pillar of the community the convention serves [1][2].

## 2. Founding Guest: Theodore Sturgeon

The choice of Theodore Sturgeon as the first Norwescon Guest of Honor in 1978 was a statement of intent. Sturgeon (1918--1985) was one of the foundational voices of American science fiction -- author of *More Than Human* (1953), creator of Sturgeon's Law ("ninety percent of everything is crap"), and a writer whose work pushed science fiction toward emotional depth and literary ambition at a time when the genre was often dismissed as pulp entertainment [1][4].

By inviting Sturgeon, the first Norwescon declared that this convention would take science fiction seriously as literature. Not as escapist entertainment, not as a commercial product category, but as a literary tradition with important things to say about the human condition. That positioning -- literary SF as the convention's center of gravity -- has persisted for 48 years [1].

### 3.2 Scientists and Artists

Norwescon's Science GoH tradition ensures that working researchers are present and accessible. Past Science GoHs have included professionals from astrophysics, biology, computer science, and other fields, reflecting the convention's commitment to real science alongside speculative fiction [1][2].

Artist GoHs have included illustrators, painters, and visual artists working in the science fiction and fantasy genre -- the people who create the covers, the concept art, and the visual imagination that defines how readers picture the worlds they read about [1][2].

## 4. Norwescon 48: Writer GoH -- Ursula Vernon

**Ursula Vernon**, also known by her horror pen name **T. Kingfisher**, is an author and illustrator of extraordinary range. Her body of work spans children's picture books, middle-grade novels, adult horror, epic webcomics, and award-winning short fiction. She holds a degree in anthropology from Macalester College and is based in New Mexico [3][5].

### 4.1 Major Awards

| Year | Award | Work |
|------|-------|------|
| 2012 | Hugo Award for Best Graphic Story | *Digger* (webcomic) |
| 2013 | Mythopoeic Award | *Digger* |
| 2015 | Nebula Award for Best Short Story | "Jackalope Wives" |
| 2016 | Mythopoeic Award | *Castle Hangnail* |
| 2017 | Hugo Award for Best Novelette | "The Tomato Thief" |
| 2021 | Hugo Award for Best Short Story | "Metal Like Blood in the Dark" |
| 2021 | Andre Norton Nebula + Lodestar + Dragon + Locus + Mythopoeic | *A Wizard's Guide to Defensive Baking* |

### 4.2 Range

Vernon's work defies easy categorization. Her children's series *Dragonbreath* (Sequoyah Award winner) and *Hamster Princess* (Texas Bluebonnet nominee, Amelia Bloomer List) coexist with her T. Kingfisher horror novels, which explore dread with the same attention to craft that her children's books bring to wonder. Her 10-year webcomic *Digger* -- the story of a pragmatic wombat who accidentally tunnels into a world of gods and prophecies -- won the Hugo and the Mythopoeic and demonstrated that sequential art could sustain epic storytelling [3][5].

The range is not a contradiction. It is the same creative intelligence applied to different audiences and different emotional registers. Vernon writes what she wants to write, and what she wants to write spans the full spectrum from delight to terror [5].

## 5. Norwescon 48: Artist GoH -- Geneva Bowers

**Geneva Bowers** is a Hugo Award-winning illustrator whose work centers on atmosphere and magical realism. Inspired by growing up in Appalachia, her art captures a sense of calm and nostalgia, bridging nature and speculative imagination. Her illustrations have appeared on book covers, in galleries, and across the SFF community [3].

Bowers' selection as Artist GoH reflects Norwescon's appreciation for visual storytelling that goes beyond commercial illustration -- art that creates mood, evokes place, and bridges the real and the imagined [3].

## 6. Norwescon 48: Science GoH -- Summer Ash

**Summer Ash** is an astrophysicist, rocket scientist, and science communicator whose career spans engineering, research, and public outreach [3]:

- **Education:** Bachelor's in mechanical engineering from Stanford; master's in space studies from the International Space University
- **Engineering:** Aerospace engineer on the X-34 Program at Orbital Sciences Corporation
- **Research:** Graduate research at Cambridge's Cavendish Laboratory on radio galaxy evolution and active galactic nuclei effects
- **Communication:** Former "In-House Astrophysicist" for *The Rachel Maddow Show* (2009--2016). Published in *The Atlantic*, *Smithsonian*, *Scientific American*, *Slate*, and *Nautilus Magazine*

Ash represents the convention's commitment to real science. She is not a science fiction writer speculating about space; she is a scientist who has worked on actual spacecraft and studied actual galaxies. Her presence at the convention bridges the gap between the speculative and the empirical -- exactly the kind of translation that Norwescon's programming has always valued [3].

## 7. Norwescon 48: Spotlight Publisher -- Orbit Books

**Orbit Books** is a division of Hachette Book Group comprising the Orbit, Redhook, Orbit Works, and Run For It imprints. Launched in 2007, Orbit has become one of the most important SFF publishers in the English-speaking world. Represented at Norwescon 48 by executive editor **Bradley Englert** [3].

Orbit's roster reads like a who's-who of modern science fiction and fantasy: James S. A. Corey (*The Expanse*), N. K. Jemisin (*Broken Earth* trilogy), Ann Leckie (*Ancillary Justice*), Kim Stanley Robinson (*The Ministry for the Future*), Andrzej Sapkowski (*The Witcher*), Joe Abercrombie (*First Law*), and Brent Weeks (*Lightbringer*). The Run For It imprint, Orbit's new horror line, signals the publisher's expansion into adjacent genres [3].

The Spotlight Publisher category is relatively new in Norwescon's GoH structure. It recognizes that the publishing industry is part of the creative ecosystem that conventions celebrate -- not just authors, but the editorial, design, and business infrastructure that brings books to readers [3].

## 8. Norwescon 48: Special Guest -- Dutch Bihary

**Dutch Bihary** is a face and body art specialist, international instructor, SFX makeup and prosthetics artist. His credentials span multiple creative disciplines [3]:

- Featured on Season 1 of *Skin Wars* (Game Show Network body-painting competition)
- Returned as celebrity mentor on *Skin Wars: Fresh Paint*
- Expertise includes tattoo work, comic book illustration, face/body painting, practical SFX for TV/film, large-scale murals, 3D chromadepth attractions, and prop sculpting
- Teaches at institutions including MBS Academy (Korea) and Stan Winston Studio

Bihary's selection connects the convention's costuming and art traditions to professional SFX and body art -- the craft side of visual transformation that convention masquerades celebrate at the amateur level [3].

## 9. The Philip K. Dick Award

The Philip K. Dick Award was established in 1983 to recognize distinguished science fiction published as a paperback original in the United States. The award is particularly significant because it honors work that might otherwise be overlooked -- paperback originals, by definition, lack the marketing push and review attention that hardcover releases receive [3][6].

Since its inception, the PKD Award ceremony has been held at Norwescon, making it one of the convention's signature literary events and one of the reasons Norwescon maintains its literary identity. Hosting a major genre award ceremony is both a privilege and a responsibility -- it places the convention at the center of the field's literary conversation [3][6].
