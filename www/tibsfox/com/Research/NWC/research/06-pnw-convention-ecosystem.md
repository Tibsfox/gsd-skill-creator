## 1. Convention Country

The Pacific Northwest is convention country. Within a four-hour drive of Seattle, a fan can attend literary SF conventions, comic cons, gaming expos, anime conventions, furry conventions, and media events of every scale from 200-person intimate gatherings to 100,000-person spectacles. The density of convention culture in the PNW is remarkable even by the standards of a country that invented the convention format [1][2].

This density is not accidental. It reflects a specific set of cultural conditions: geographic isolation from the entertainment industry centers of Los Angeles and New York, a tradition of self-organized creative community, a climate that drives people indoors for nine months of the year, and a technology industry that produces a population with disposable income and a high tolerance for elaborate organizational infrastructure [1].

## 2. The Literary/Media Divide

The most important taxonomic distinction in convention culture is between **literary conventions** and **media conventions**. The distinction is fundamental, not superficial, and confusing the two is like confusing a library with a shopping mall [1][2]:

### 2.1 Literary Conventions

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Focus** | Books, writing, science, ideas |
| **Programming** | Panel-based, discussion-oriented |
| **Guests** | Authors, scientists, editors, artists |
| **Attendance** | Hundreds to low thousands |
| **Culture** | Participatory -- attendees contribute |
| **Revenue model** | Membership fees; volunteer-run |
| **Examples** | Norwescon, Orycon, Foolscap, Radcon |

### 2.2 Media Conventions

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Focus** | Visual media, gaming, commercial entertainment |
| **Programming** | Celebrity panels, corporate presentations |
| **Guests** | Actors, directors, franchise representatives |
| **Attendance** | Tens of thousands to 100,000+ |
| **Culture** | Consumer-oriented -- attendees purchase experiences |
| **Revenue model** | Ticket sales, vendor fees, sponsorships; professional staff |
| **Examples** | ECCC, PAX, San Diego Comic-Con |

### 2.3 Why the Distinction Matters

Both models are valid. They serve different audiences and different needs. But assuming Norwescon is "like Comic Con, but smaller" misses the point entirely. Norwescon is not a smaller version of ECCC any more than a book club is a smaller version of a bookstore. They are different things that happen to share a venue category [1].

## 3. The PNW Convention Landscape

| Convention | Location | Type | Scale | Character |
|-----------|----------|------|-------|-----------|
| **Norwescon** | SeaTac, WA | Literary SF/F | ~3,000 | The big one. 48 years. Writing, science, literary tradition [1] |
| **Emerald City Comic Con** | Seattle, WA | Media/Comic | 90,000+ | Massive commercial convention. Corporate booths, celebrities [2] |
| **PAX West** | Seattle, WA | Gaming | 70,000+ | Penny Arcade Expo. Video games, tabletop, indie [2] |
| **Orycon** | Portland, OR | Literary SF/F | ~800 | Portland's literary con. Smaller, intimate, strong writing focus [2] |
| **Radcon** | Tri-Cities, WA | Literary SF/F | ~1,500 | Eastern Washington con. Regional character, community-focused [2] |
| **Rustycon** | Tacoma, WA | Literary SF/F | ~500 | Smaller literary con. January timing fills a calendar gap [2] |
| **Foolscap** | Redmond, WA | Literary (small press) | 100--200 | Ultra-intimate discussion con. Single-track programming [2] |
| **Sakura-Con** | Seattle, WA | Anime | 25,000+ | PNW's largest anime convention [2] |
| **Furlandia** | Portland, OR | Furry | ~1,500 | PNW furry convention. Creative community focused [2] |

### 3.1 Calendar Spread

The conventions are distributed across the calendar, which is not accidental. Convention organizers coordinate informally to avoid date conflicts, particularly among literary cons that draw from the same attendee pool. Norwescon occupies Easter weekend in late March/early April. Orycon takes November. Radcon runs in February. Rustycon fills January. The calendar is a negotiated resource [1][2].

## 4. Norwescon in the Ecosystem

Norwescon occupies a specific ecological niche: the **largest literary SF convention in the Pacific Northwest**. It is big enough to attract major Guests of Honor and host the Philip K. Dick Award ceremony, but small enough to maintain the participatory culture that defines literary fandom. It is the convention where a first-time attendee can meet a Hugo Award-winning author in a hallway conversation, not through a paid meet-and-greet [1].

This niche is stable because the conventions around it occupy different niches. ECCC serves the media audience. PAX serves gamers. Orycon serves Portland fans who want something more intimate. Radcon serves Eastern Washington. Foolscap serves readers who want single-track depth. Each convention survives because it serves a need that the others do not [1][2].

## 5. Emerald City Comic Con

**ECCC** is Seattle's massive media convention -- part of the ReedPop network that includes New York Comic Con and C2E2. Attendance exceeds 90,000. The event fills the Washington State Convention Center with corporate booths, celebrity photo opportunities, artist alleys, and the spectacle-level cosplay that defines modern comic convention culture [2].

ECCC and Norwescon exist in the same city but serve almost entirely different audiences. The overlap is smaller than outsiders would expect. An ECCC attendee who has never heard of Norwescon is common. A Norwescon regular who avoids ECCC is equally common. They are adjacent communities with different values, different scales, and different purposes [2].

## 6. PAX

**PAX West** (Penny Arcade Expo) brings 70,000+ gaming enthusiasts to Seattle each September. PAX covers video games, tabletop games, indie games, and gaming culture with a mix of publisher showcases, indie exhibitions, tournaments, and panel programming [2].

PAX's relationship to Norwescon is tangential: both include gaming in their programming, but PAX is a gaming convention that includes panels, while Norwescon is a literary convention that includes gaming. The gaming track at Norwescon is a community activity; at PAX, gaming is the entire point [2].

## 7. The Literary Cons

### 7.1 Orycon (Portland)

Portland's primary literary SF convention, held annually in November. Smaller than Norwescon (approximately 800 attendees), Orycon offers a more intimate experience -- shorter hallways, fewer simultaneous panels, closer access to guests. Oregon-based authors are well-represented, connecting to the state's strong literary tradition (Le Guin, Cherryh, Brin) [2].

### 7.2 Radcon (Tri-Cities)

Eastern Washington's literary con, held in February in the Tri-Cities area (Richland, Kennewick, Pasco). Radcon has a distinct regional character that reflects the Inland Northwest -- different geography, different demographics, different cultural touchstones than the I-5 corridor conventions [2].

### 7.3 Rustycon (Tacoma)

A smaller literary convention held in January, Rustycon fills a calendar gap and provides a gathering point during the convention-light winter months. Its smaller scale means tighter community bonds and more casual programming [2].

### 7.4 Foolscap (Redmond)

The smallest and most focused of the PNW literary cons, Foolscap (100--200 attendees) offers single-track programming with a strong emphasis on small press, independent publishing, and deep literary discussion. Foolscap is the convention equivalent of a seminar rather than a lecture hall [2].

## 8. Worldcon and the Global Hierarchy

Science fiction convention fandom operates within a global hierarchy [1][3]:

| Level | Event | Hugo Awards | Attendance | Frequency |
|-------|-------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| **World** | Worldcon (World Science Fiction Convention) | Yes | 5,000--10,000 | Annual, rotating cities globally |
| **National/Major** | Westercon, NASFiC, regional hubs | No | 1,000--3,000 | Annual, rotating regionally |
| **Regional** | Norwescon, Orycon, Boskone, Arisia | No | 500--3,000 | Annual, fixed location |
| **Local** | Foolscap, local clubs | No | 50--500 | Varies |

### 8.1 Norwescon's Place

Norwescon sits at the **Regional** tier -- the largest and most established regional convention in the PNW. Its role in the ecosystem is to provide year-round community infrastructure that a traveling event like Worldcon cannot. Worldcon visits a city once every decade or two; Norwescon is there every spring [1].

The original 1978 founding of Norwescon as practice for a 1981 Seattle Worldcon bid embodies this relationship. Regional conventions develop the volunteer base, nurture the community, and provide continuity. Worldcon provides prestige, global connection, and the Hugo Awards. The relationship is symbiotic [1].

## 9. Why the PNW

The concentration of convention culture in the Pacific Northwest is not random. Several factors converge [1][2]:

### 9.1 Geographic Isolation

The PNW is far from New York (publishing) and Los Angeles (entertainment). This distance forced the creative community to build its own infrastructure rather than plugging into existing industry networks. Conventions became that infrastructure -- the gathering spaces for a community too spread out for regular meetups but too passionate to go without connection [1].

### 9.2 The Seattle Sound Parallel

The same cultural conditions that produced the Seattle music scene -- isolation, rain, a tradition of DIY culture, enough creative density to sustain a scene but not enough industry attention to commodify it -- produced the convention culture. Green River formed in the early 1980s; Norwescon had been running for several years by then. The scenes were parallel, not identical, but the cultural water table that fed both was the same PNW isolation [1].

### 9.3 Climate

Nine months of rain is a powerful incentive for indoor community. The convention season in the PNW spans the wettest months -- January (Rustycon), February (Radcon), March/April (Norwescon), November (Orycon). The calendar follows the weather [1][2].

### 9.4 Technology Industry

The Puget Sound technology sector produces a population with characteristics favorable to convention culture: high educational attainment, disposable income, comfort with complex organizational systems, and affinity for science fiction as a genre. The Microsoft, Amazon, and Boeing employee bases have been well-represented in PNW fandom for decades [1].

## 10. The Convention as Bioregion

The PNW convention ecosystem can be understood as a bioregion -- a geographic area defined by shared cultural species (conventions) occupying distinct niches:

- **Apex predator:** ECCC (largest, most resources, broadest reach)
- **Keystone species:** Norwescon (central to the literary ecosystem, without which the smaller cons would lose their anchor)
- **Specialist species:** Orycon, Radcon, Rustycon, Foolscap (adapted to specific niches)
- **Invasive/introduced:** PAX (originally Boston-based, established PNW presence)
- **Symbiotic:** All literary cons share attendees, volunteers, and cultural DNA

The ecosystem is healthy because its niches are well-differentiated. Competitions for the same niche would be destructive; complementary niches create mutual support [1][2].
