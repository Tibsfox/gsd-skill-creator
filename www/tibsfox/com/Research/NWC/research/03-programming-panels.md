## 1. What Convention Programming Is

A convention panel is not a lecture. It is not a presentation. It is a moderated conversation -- typically 50 minutes, with 3 to 5 panelists and audience participation -- on a topic the community has decided matters enough to discuss in public. The panel proposal process is open: anyone can suggest a topic. The programming committee curates, schedules, and assigns panelists. The result is a four-day grid of hundreds of simultaneous conversations spanning dozens of topics [1][2].

The programming grid is, in effect, a map of what fandom cares about in a given year. Tracks that grow reflect emerging interests. Tracks that persist reflect enduring ones. The shift from purely literary programming in early conventions to the multi-track format of modern Norwescon reflects the broadening of the SF community itself -- from a literary club to a creative ecosystem [1].

### 1.1 How Panels Work

A typical panel at Norwescon consists of:

- **Moderator:** Guides discussion, manages time, ensures all panelists are heard, facilitates audience questions
- **Panelists (2--4):** Authors, scientists, artists, fans, or professionals with relevant expertise or perspective
- **Audience:** Active participants, not passive observers. Q&A is expected and encouraged

Panels are not rehearsed. They are live, unrepeatable conversations. The best panels produce insights that no individual panelist would have reached alone -- the emergent property of smart people thinking together in real time [1].

## 3. The Writing Workshop

Norwescon's writing workshop is one of its signature programs and among the most respected convention-based workshops in the Pacific Northwest. It follows the **Milford method** -- named after the Milford Science Fiction Writers' Conference founded in 1956 by Damon Knight, James Blish, and Judith Merril [1][2].

### 3.1 How It Works

- Participants submit manuscripts in advance
- All participants read all submitted work before the workshop
- In session, each manuscript receives structured critique from every participant
- Professional authors serve as workshop leaders
- Application and acceptance are required -- the workshop maintains quality standards

### 3.2 Why It Matters

The Milford method treats writing as a craft that can be taught, practiced, and improved through disciplined peer review. It is not a gentle encouragement circle. It is a working critique process designed to identify what is not working and why. Many professional SFF careers have been launched or advanced through convention workshop programs modeled on this format [1].

The workshop requires significant commitment from participants -- reading multiple manuscripts, preparing thoughtful critique, accepting critique of one's own work -- and this investment is what makes it effective. The convention provides the space and the format; the participants provide the labor [1][2].

## 4. Science Programming

Norwescon's science programming is real science presented by real scientists. The Science GoH tradition ensures that at least one working researcher is present and accessible for the full weekend, but science panels also feature other scientists, engineers, and technical professionals from the PNW community [2].

### 4.1 Sample Topics (Norwescon 48)

From the published schedule [2]:

- "Artificial Intelligence and Economics" (Technology track)
- "Space Propulsion Systems -- Real and Imagined" (SF)
- "L4 Space Habitat Society" (Space)
- "Best Mad Scientist Plans" (SF/GoH/Streaming)
- Biology panels covering ecology, evolution, and bioethics

### 4.2 Science at a Literary Convention

The presence of real science at a fiction convention is not incidental. Science fiction, at its best, is a dialogue between the actual and the possible. Having working scientists discuss real research alongside fiction writers speculating about imaginary futures creates the exact kind of cross-pollination that Norwescon's programming is designed to produce [2].

Summer Ash (Science GoH 2026) embodies this: a rocket scientist who has worked on actual spacecraft, studied actual galaxies at Cambridge, and communicated science on national television. She is not speculating. She is reporting from the field [2].

## 6. The Masquerade

The masquerade is Norwescon's costume competition -- a tradition older than the word "cosplay." Categories typically range from novice to master, with judging criteria that include craftsmanship (construction quality, material choice, technique), presentation (stage presence, character embodiment), and creativity (originality, interpretation) [2].

Winners often receive passes to the following year's convention, creating a self-renewing cycle: the community's best costumers return, raising the bar for the next generation. The masquerade is both competition and celebration -- an exhibition of what fans can create when they apply the same imaginative energy to physical craft that authors apply to prose [2].

## 7. Gaming

Norwescon's gaming program encompasses board games, card games, tabletop role-playing games, and special competitive events. The 2026 highlight is a **CATAN US National Championship Qualifier** -- 24 seats, with the winner qualifying for the national championship [2].

The gaming track operates on a different rhythm from panel programming. Games run longer (hours rather than 50 minutes), require more physical space, and create their own social dynamics. The gaming room is one of the convention's late-night anchors -- games often run past midnight [2].

**Artemis: Cosmos** -- a spaceship bridge simulator -- operates from noon to 1 AM Thursday through Saturday (Sunday to 5 PM), offering a collaborative experience where groups of attendees crew a simulated starship together [2].

## 8. The Speculative Film Festival

The Speculative Film Festival showcases international short films in one-hour programming blocks throughout the convention. The focus is speculative fiction in cinematic form -- science fiction, fantasy, horror, and experimental work that explores the same themes the convention's literary programming addresses [2].

### 9.2 Concerts and Filk

Concerts run throughout the weekend, featuring both scheduled performers and filk circles. Filk -- the folk music of science fiction fandom -- is a tradition older than many attendees, with songs about spaceships, dragons, programming bugs, and the experience of being a fan. Filk is participatory: anyone can join a song circle [2].

## 10. Workshops and Hands-On

Beyond the writing workshop, Norwescon 48 offers practical hands-on workshops [2]:

- **"How to Pattern the Weird Stuff"** -- cosplay patterning workshop (10-person limit)
- **Regency Era Dance Workshop** -- learn period dance
- **Qigong movement meditation class** -- body awareness and movement
- **Artists Workshop** -- visual art critique and instruction

These workshops represent the convention's commitment to making, not just discussing. The shift from passive consumption to active creation is built into the programming structure [2].

## 11. The Volunteer-Run Model

Every person who makes Norwescon's programming happen is a volunteer:

- **Panel proposals:** Submitted by community members, curated by volunteer programming staff
- **Panelists:** Invited based on expertise; not compensated
- **Moderators:** Trained and assigned by volunteers
- **Room management:** Volunteer ops staff handle setup, A/V, timing
- **Schedule compilation:** Assembled by volunteer programmers using digital tools

The programming grid -- hundreds of events across four days in dozens of simultaneous rooms -- is the work product of volunteers who do this because they care about the community, not because they are being paid. This model, inherited from the earliest science fiction conventions, remains one of fandom's most distinctive organizational features [1].

## 12. Streaming and Access

Events on the Streaming track are live-streamed free via Twitch and YouTube. No membership required. This extends the convention's reach beyond its physical boundaries -- someone in Portland or Vancouver or Tokyo can watch Norwescon panels in real time without purchasing a membership or traveling to SeaTac [2].

The streaming program represents a significant evolution in convention culture. Historically, conventions were radically local -- you had to be there. Streaming creates a secondary audience that can participate remotely, potentially attracting new attendees for future years while serving fans who cannot travel [2].
