## 1. The Convention Takeover

When Norwescon is in session, the convention is the hotel. This is not a figure of speech. The event fills multiple ballrooms, conference rooms, meeting rooms, hospitality suites, common areas, and significant portions of the guest room inventory. Attendees in costume walk through lobbies. Panels run in rooms whose weekday function is corporate meetings. The bar fills with people discussing quantum mechanics, alien linguistics, and the correct way to build a lightsaber [1][2].

This total occupation is characteristic of hotel-based literary conventions and distinguishes them from conventions held in dedicated convention centers. A convention center is a neutral container -- it could hold anything. A hotel that has been taken over by Norwescon is a different place than the hotel that exists the other 361 days of the year. The transformation is the point [1].

## 2. The DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport

**Address:** 18740 International Blvd, SeaTac, WA 98188
**Phone:** 206-246-8600
**Convention home since:** 1994 (continuous, excluding 2020--2021)

The DoubleTree has been Norwescon's venue for over thirty years. The hotel knows how to host this event because it has been hosting this event since before many current attendees were born. That institutional knowledge -- on both sides -- creates a working relationship that a new venue would take years to develop [1][2].

### 2.1 Venue Advantages

- **Scale:** Multiple ballrooms and dozens of conference rooms accommodate 30+ simultaneous programming tracks
- **Sleeping rooms:** Attendees can stay where the convention happens -- no commuting between hotel and venue
- **Location:** Adjacent to SeaTac Airport; accessible by light rail, car, shuttle, and rideshare
- **Infrastructure:** A/V equipment, catering, security, and facilities management provided by the hotel
- **Parking:** Available on-site

### 2.2 Room Block

The convention negotiates a block of rooms at a group rate each year. For Norwescon 48, the room block is **closed** -- rooms may still be available at standard hotel rates. Nearby alternatives (no convention arrangement) include: Hilton Garden Inn Seattle Airport (next door), La Quinta Inn & Suites (across the street), Coast Gateway Hotel, and Days Inn by Wyndham SeaTac Airport [2].

## 3. The Atrium as Social Hub

The DoubleTree's atrium serves as the convention's central social space -- the place where planned encounters and chance meetings intersect. Between panels, between meals, between events, people congregate in the atrium. It is the convention's town square [1].

The atrium's function is emergent, not programmed. No one schedules "stand in the atrium and talk." But the architecture creates the opportunity: an open space visible from multiple levels, with seating, sight lines, and foot traffic that naturally concentrates social interaction. Some of the convention's most important conversations happen here -- not in panel rooms, not at the bar, but in the space between [1].

## 4. The Party Wing

Room parties are concentrated in **Wing 5B** (the party wing), with specific rules governing noise, timing, and conduct. The designation of a party wing is both practical and cultural [2]:

**Practical:** It concentrates noise-generating activities away from sleeping rooms, manages hotel relations, and provides a clear boundary between party space and quiet space.

**Cultural:** It creates a known destination. Attendees who want to find parties know where to go. Attendees who want to avoid them know where not to be. The spatial clarity reduces friction for everyone.

### 4.1 Party Rules

Party hosts must review and follow published party rules before hosting. Loud parties are permitted only in the party wing. Noise, conduct, and cleanup standards are enforced. The rules exist because Norwescon shares the hotel with non-convention guests and because the convention's relationship with the venue depends on responsible behavior [2].

## 5. Room Parties as Social Infrastructure

Room parties at science fiction conventions are not merely social events. They are **social infrastructure** -- self-organized gathering spaces that serve specific community functions [1]:

- **Publisher/editor parties:** Industry networking, author outreach, community building around a press or imprint
- **Fandom parties:** Groups organized around specific interests (a TV show, a book series, a gaming system) create temporary gathering spaces
- **Regional parties:** Fans from other cities or conventions host hospitality suites to build connections across the broader fandom network
- **Bid parties:** Groups bidding to host future conventions (especially Worldcon bids) use room parties for campaigning and community outreach

Each party is a self-organized cell within the larger convention organism. No central authority assigns parties or controls their content. The convention provides the wing; the community fills it. This is the same organizational principle as Burning Man's theme camps -- the event provides infrastructure; participants provide content [1].

## 6. The Con Suite

The con suite (convention suite) is the convention's hospitality room -- a space where attendees can find snacks, drinks, and casual social interaction throughout the day and into the evening. It is free to all badge-holders and serves as a decompression space, a refueling stop, and a place to meet people without the structure of a panel or the intensity of a party [1].

The con suite is typically managed by a dedicated team of volunteers who keep it stocked, clean, and welcoming. It is one of the convention's essential support functions -- the physical infrastructure of community care [1].

## 7. Late-Night Programming

Norwescon's programming does not stop when the sun goes down. Late-night events include [2]:

- **Themed dances:** First Contact Galactic Gala (Thursday), Black Light Rave (Friday), Goblin Ball (Saturday)
- **Concerts:** Multiple acts performing throughout the weekend
- **Film screenings:** Speculative Film Festival blocks running into the evening
- **Gaming:** Board games, RPGs, and the Artemis bridge simulator running until 1 AM
- **Panels:** After-hours panels on topics too edgy, too niche, or too fun for daytime programming
- **Room parties:** The party wing activates in the evening hours

The convention recognizes that some of its most important interactions happen after midnight. The people who are still awake at 2 AM, arguing about the physics of FTL travel or teaching each other card games or singing filk songs in the stairwell, are building the relationships that bring them back next year [2].

## 8. Physical Space Shaping Social Interaction

The relationship between architecture and social behavior at a convention is not accidental. The DoubleTree's layout creates specific interaction patterns [1]:

### 8.1 Elevators

Elevator rides at a convention are involuntary social encounters. You share a small space with strangers who share your interests. Conversations start. Connections form. The elevator is the convention's most efficient networking tool -- three floors of enforced proximity with someone wearing a badge and (probably) a costume [1].

### 8.2 Hallways

The hallways between panel rooms are transition spaces where attendees make decisions: which panel next, whether to grab food, who to seek out. These liminal zones are where chance encounters happen -- seeing someone you recognize from last year, spotting a costume you want to compliment, overhearing a conversation you want to join [1].

### 8.3 The Bar

The hotel bar becomes the convention's informal meeting room. Authors, editors, fans, and professionals mingle in a setting that dissolves the formal hierarchies of panel-and-audience. Some of the field's most important business conversations have happened at convention hotel bars [1].

### 8.4 Quiet Spaces

The Oasis lounge (Room 7204) provides a radically inclusive, low-sensory safer space for decompression. The existence of designated quiet spaces acknowledges that conventions can be overwhelming -- sensory overload, social fatigue, introversion needs -- and that caring for attendees means providing spaces to retreat as well as spaces to engage [2].

## 9. Logistics and Access

### 9.1 Transportation

The DoubleTree's location makes it accessible by multiple modes [2]:

| Mode | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Air** | SeaTac Airport is adjacent -- literally next door |
| **Light Rail** | Link Light Rail connects to downtown Seattle and beyond |
| **Car** | Ample parking available; detailed directions on convention website |
| **Hotel Shuttle** | Provided by the DoubleTree |
| **Transit** | King County Metro bus service |
| **Rideshare** | Uber/Lyft service the area |

### 9.2 Mobile Technology

The **Guidebook** mobile app (iOS and Android) provides schedule, maps, notifications, and custom schedule building. The schedule is also available as downloadable ePub and PDF for offline use [2].

## 10. The Cashless Hotel

The DoubleTree has gone cashless -- credit and debit cards only. No cash transactions at the hotel. This is worth knowing before arrival, particularly for attendees accustomed to cash economies at conventions (dealers room, tip jars, etc.) [2].
