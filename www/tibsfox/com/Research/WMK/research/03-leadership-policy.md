## 1. Introduction: What Kind of Leader She Was

Wilma Mankiller was not a charismatic leader in the conventional sense. She did not build power through oratory, though she could speak with precision and force when the occasion required. She did not accumulate political capital through coalition deal-making or the cultivation of strategic allies in distant corridors, though she was a skilled negotiator with federal officials. She did not hold power through the traditional mechanisms of tribal political patronage, though she understood those mechanisms and knew how to work within and around them.

She built power through something less theatrical and more durable: she built things that worked, and the things that worked were visible, and the visibility persuaded people who had not previously believed that such things were possible.

The Bell water project came before she was ever an elected official. It was a community development project she administered in her capacity as an economic development specialist for the Cherokee Nation, and it was — by the standards of what it accomplished and what it cost — a success so unlikely that it required explanation. The explanation she offered was not ideological. She did not argue that the project proved some principle of community self-determination in the abstract. She argued that the residents of Bell had done something hard, had done it themselves, and that the results were standing in front of anyone who cared to look.

This is the grammar of her leadership throughout her decade as chief: not argument, but demonstration. Not promise, but proof.

To understand Mankiller's leadership record, you have to understand the sequence. The Bell project established a method. Her election as Deputy Chief in 1983 put her in a position to apply it at larger scale. Her appointment as Principal Chief in 1985 gave her the authority to make it policy. The elections of 1987 and 1991 gave her the popular mandate to sustain it. The self-governance compacts she negotiated gave her the financial architecture to fund it. And the specific programs she built — health clinics, schools, literacy institutes, ambulance services, district-based elections — gave it lasting form in the lives of the Cherokee people.

This module traces that sequence, section by section, with the specificity it deserves.

## 2. Bell, Oklahoma: The Proof of Concept

### A Community Without Running Water

Bell, Oklahoma is a small community in Adair County, in the rolling, forested hill country of northeastern Oklahoma where the majority of the Cherokee Nation's rural population lived. In the early 1980s, Bell had approximately three hundred residents. It had no running water.

This was not an unusual condition for rural communities in the poorest counties of Oklahoma. The infrastructure gap between urban and rural America — between what a city resident assumed was normal and what a rural Adair County resident could actually access — was vast and chronic. Running water required pipes, and pipes required money, and money was what rural Cherokee communities had the least of. The standard solution, the one that every piece of conventional wisdom about rural poverty recommended, was to secure a government contract and hire an outside firm to build the infrastructure. This was how it was done. It was also, characteristically, how it did not get done: the bureaucratic and financial barriers to outside contracting were enormous, the costs were prohibitive, the timelines were slow, and the communities waiting for infrastructure got very good at waiting.

Mankiller had been working for the Cherokee Nation as an economic development specialist since her return to Oklahoma in 1977, and by the early 1980s she was directing the Community Development Department under Principal Chief Ross Swimmer. She had seen, repeatedly, the gap between what government programs promised and what they delivered to communities like Bell. She had also seen, in her organizing work and in her study of community development theory — she read extensively in the literature, including accounts of Myles Horton's Highlander Folk School and the civil rights movement's grassroots organizing models — that communities which built things themselves developed something that outside contractors could never deliver: a relationship to what they had built.

### The Self-Help Model

Working with Charlie Soap — who was then employed by the Indian Housing Authority and who would later become her husband — Mankiller proposed a different approach to Bell. Rather than seeking federal funding for a contracted water system installation, they would mobilize the residents of Bell to build the water system themselves. The Cherokee Nation would secure the funding and provide materials and technical guidance. The labor would be community labor.

The logistical dimensions were not small. The Bell water project required laying sixteen miles of pipe. Sixteen miles of pipe through Oklahoma hill country is not sixteen miles of flat suburban street — it is sixteen miles through terrain that includes steep grades, creek crossings, rocky soil, and weather that does not cooperate on schedule. The people who were going to lay this pipe were farmers, elders, families with children, people without construction training, people who had never laid pipe before and had no particular reason to believe they could.

They did it in fourteen months.

What happened during those fourteen months is the core of the Bell story. Mankiller and Soap organized the community into working groups. They established schedules, managed materials delivery, provided technical instruction, solved problems as they arose, and kept people engaged across the full arc of a project that was long enough and hard enough to exhaust commitment if commitment was not continuously renewed. This required, among other things, understanding which families had which skills, which neighbors could be relied upon to motivate which other neighbors, and where the social fault lines within the community ran — because every community has fault lines, and a project that ignores them will find them at the worst possible moment.

Home rehabilitation ran alongside the pipe-laying. The project was not merely plumbing infrastructure but an integrated community development effort: as residents gained running water, they also worked on improving the housing stock that the water would serve. The Kenwood Project that followed applied the same approach and received the Department of Housing and Urban Development's Certificate of National Merit. Bell was not an isolated experiment. It was the first iteration of a replicable model.

The project was completed. Bell, Oklahoma had running water. The cost — measured in dollars — was a fraction of what outside contracting would have required, and the cost measured in community capacity was negative: the community ended the project more organized, more confident, and more connected than it had begun.

### What Bell Proved

Mankiller understood, with a precision that distinguished her from many well-intentioned community developers, exactly what Bell had demonstrated and what it had not. It had not demonstrated that self-help labor could replace all government investment in infrastructure. The Cherokee Nation had provided funds for materials. Federal and state resources had been part of the equation. Bell was not an argument against public investment. It was an argument about the form public investment should take.

The model Bell demonstrated was this: when communities invest their own labor in their own infrastructure, they do not simply receive a service — they develop the organizational capacity to sustain and expand that service, and they develop a different relationship to the infrastructure itself. The pipe that a community lays is a pipe that community will maintain, repair, protect, and understand. The pipe laid by outside contractors for a passive recipient community is a pipe that someone will have to maintain, and that someone will not be from Bell.

This is not a romantic proposition. It is an organizational one. Mankiller's insight was to recognize that the distinction between receiving infrastructure and building infrastructure was the distinction between dependence and sovereignty. She did not always describe it in those terms in the early 1980s, but that was what the Bell project demonstrated, and she spent the next decade applying the demonstration at increasing scale.

The Bell project also gave her something politically essential: a reputation. Within the Cherokee Nation's administrative structure, she was no longer a competent economic development specialist with interesting ideas. She was the person who had organized sixteen miles of pipe in fourteen months in Bell, Oklahoma. That reputation preceded her into the political contests to come.

The 2013 documentary-drama *The Cherokee Word for Water*, co-directed by Charlie Soap, brought Bell's story to a wider audience. The title is apt: *amanyi*, the Cherokee word for water, sits at the intersection of the practical and the cultural, the pipe in the ground and the language that names what flows through it. Both, in Mankiller's vision, were necessary for a self-determining people.

## 3. The Path to Deputy Chief: The 1983 Election

### Ross Swimmer's Calculation

In 1983, Ross Swimmer was the Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation, a conservative Republican attorney who had held the position since 1975. He was running for re-election. He needed a running mate for the Deputy Chief position. The Cherokee Nation's constitutional structure paired Principal Chief and Deputy Chief on a joint ticket, and Swimmer's calculation about who to place on that ticket reflected a mixture of political pragmatism and genuine assessment of capability.

He chose Wilma Mankiller.

The choice was not obvious. Mankiller was not from the political center of the Cherokee Nation. She was a community development specialist who had spent her formative political years in the orbit of the American Indian Movement in the Bay Area, who had been involved in activist causes that Swimmer's conservative political instincts did not particularly favor, and who was — most visibly — a woman. The Cherokee Nation in 1983 had no tradition of women in elected executive positions at the highest level. There had been women on the Tribal Council. There had been women in appointed administrative roles. There had been no woman Principal Chief or Deputy Chief.

Swimmer's reasoning, as best as can be reconstructed, was that Mankiller's demonstrated effectiveness in community development work — above all, Bell — made her a credible partner for the executive ticket in a way that more politically conventional alternatives were not. Whatever his misgivings about her activist background, he appears to have concluded that competence was the primary qualification.

Mankiller accepted. She later described mixed feelings about running with Swimmer, whose political views differed substantially from her own on many questions. But she saw the opportunity clearly: Deputy Chief was a platform from which the Bell model could be taken to scale, and a platform from which she could learn the full structure of tribal governance from the inside.

### The Campaign and Its Violence

The 1983 election did not go smoothly. Mankiller's presence on the ticket generated immediate opposition, much of it organized around the fact of her sex. She received hate mail. She received death threats — specific enough that she later described them as credible. Her car tires were slashed. A billboard bearing her image was reportedly burned. The opposition was not subtle about its reasoning: the problem, as her critics stated it with varying degrees of explicitness, was that a woman had no place in the executive leadership of the Cherokee Nation.

It is worth pausing on the cultural context of this resistance, because understanding it requires more than condemning it. Cherokee society before sustained European contact had been organized on matrilineal principles: clan membership, property, and children passed through the mother's line; women held significant authority through clan structures; the women's council was a genuine political institution with real deliberative power. The Trail of Tears and the subsequent century of federal pressure toward assimilation had eroded these structures, introducing European-American governance assumptions into the Nation's political culture alongside the constitutional forms that the Cherokee had adopted in the early nineteenth century. By 1983, the idea of a woman holding elected executive leadership was, to a significant portion of the Cherokee electorate, simply wrong — but that wrongness was not ancestrally Cherokee. It was, in a specific historical sense, a colonial inheritance.

Charlie Soap, who was deeply rooted in traditional Cherokee culture and fluent in the language, understood this. During the 1983 campaign and more explicitly in the 1987 campaign that followed, Soap spoke to traditionalist Cherokee men in Cherokee about the historical place of women in Cherokee governance — not arguing from modernity but from roots, not asking skeptics to accept something new but inviting them to remember something old. It was one of the more effective political moves in the entire decade, and it was effective precisely because it refused the frame of innovation in favor of the frame of recovery.

### The Absentee Vote Runoff Against Agnes Cowen

The election went to a runoff. Mankiller's opponent for the Deputy Chief position in the runoff was Agnes Cowen, another woman — a detail that complicates but does not diminish the analysis of the opposition Mankiller faced. Cowen was a more conventional political figure, and the organized opposition to Mankiller was targeted at her specifically: her activist associations, her San Francisco years, her policy positions, and, most fundamentally, the perception that she represented a kind of female leadership that was threatening in a way that a more traditional candidate was not.

The runoff was close. The margin came down to absentee ballots. Mankiller won by the absentee vote count — a margin narrow enough to make the result feel genuinely contingent, the kind of election that could easily have gone the other way, and that required every vote to have been cast and counted to produce the outcome it did. She was elected Deputy Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation, the first woman to hold that position in the Nation's modern history.

The significance of the narrow margin was not lost on her. She had won, but she had won a contest in which a substantial portion of the Cherokee electorate had voted against her, and she understood that this was not simply political opposition in the ordinary sense. It was a judgment about her fitness to lead based on her sex — a judgment that results, not arguments, would have to answer.

As Deputy Chief, she made a strategic calculation about the Tribal Council: she would not fight it head-on. She would avoid entangling herself in legislative battles she could not yet win and instead focus her energy on the executive and administrative domains where she could act without council permission. This was patience as strategy — preserve the working relationship, demonstrate competence, build a record. She was right to do it.

## 4. December 5, 1985: Appointment as Principal Chief

### Swimmer's Departure

In the fall of 1985, Ronald Reagan appointed Ross Swimmer to lead the Bureau of Indian Affairs in Washington. Swimmer accepted. The departure was, from the perspective of the Cherokee Nation's constitutional structure, straightforward: if the Principal Chief leaves office, the Deputy Chief becomes Principal Chief. On December 5, 1985, Wilma Mankiller was sworn in as Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation.

She was forty years old. She was the first woman to serve as Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation. She had been elected by no one to that specific position. She had not sought it, had not campaigned for it, and had not had the benefit of a gradual public acclimatization to the idea of her as chief. She was simply, suddenly, there — in the office, with the title, and with every Cherokee citizen in the fourteen-county jurisdiction trying to decide what to make of it.

The national press took notice. Mankiller's ascension was a story with obvious historical resonance, and coverage elevated her public profile in ways that strengthened her position within the Nation rather than undermining it. She received the American Indian Woman of the Year designation within five months. She was inducted into the Oklahoma Women's Hall of Fame. External recognition translated into internal credibility with at least part of the Cherokee constituency, and she used the visibility strategically: she spoke publicly about what the administration was trying to accomplish, educated the broader electorate about Cherokee Nation policy goals, and built a public record against which her eventual re-election campaigns could be evaluated.

### Immediate Priorities

The conditions under which Mankiller assumed the Principal Chieftaincy were not auspicious in conventional political terms. The opposition that had developed during the 1983 campaign had not dissolved. The sexist resistance — organized among a faction of Cherokee men who believed, without embarrassment, that the executive leadership of the Nation was a male preserve — was still present and still vocal. Her lack of an electoral mandate for the top position was a point that critics raised with regularity.

What she had was the constitutional authority of the office and a clear understanding of what she wanted to do with it. She was not a caretaker chief waiting for the next election. She moved immediately to implement the community development philosophy she had been developing since the Bell project, now at the scale of the entire Nation's administrative apparatus.

The health centers came first, because health was the most visible and most immediate gap in the Cherokee Nation's service capacity. The rural communities of the fourteen-county jurisdiction were not being adequately served by existing Indian Health Service infrastructure. Building clinics where they were needed, in the communities most underserved by existing facilities, was the Bell model applied to healthcare: identify the specific gap, build the specific solution in the specific place where the gap exists.

## 5. The 1987 Election: Running in Her Own Right

### The Campaign

By 1987, Mankiller had been governing the Cherokee Nation for eighteen months. She had completed the establishment of the first rural health center and begun work on the second. She had continued the self-help community development projects that had begun with Bell. She had navigated the federal bureaucracy with growing effectiveness. She had also been governing through a medical crisis — the aftermath of the 1979 car accident that had required numerous surgeries, and the myasthenia gravis diagnosis that followed — that was, by any reasonable standard, disqualifying for a person of ordinary fortitude. (The health story is documented in detail in Module 4 of this series.)

She ran for election as Principal Chief in her own right in 1987. The campaign was not comfortable. The threats that had accompanied the 1983 campaign continued. Her tires were slashed during the campaign — the specific, low-grade menace of an opposition that wanted her to understand that it knew where she was and what she drove. Death threats were reported to tribal and federal authorities. Her opponents made her health conditions the centerpiece of their attack: she had a near-fatal car accident, myasthenia gravis, advancing kidney disease. The argument was that she was unfit to serve. The argument was transparent — a weaponization of medical history that would not have been deployed against a male candidate with a comparable medical record — but it was organized and it was real.

Her opponent in the general election was Perry Wheeler, a traditional political figure whose support drew on the networks that had opposed Mankiller's presence on the 1983 ticket and that remained skeptical of female executive leadership.

Mankiller's response to the opposition, in the campaign as in everything, was not to fight it frontally. She did not deliver speeches about sexism. She did not position herself as a symbol of women's progress. She talked about what she had done in the eighteen months she had been chief and what she intended to do next. She walked door to door in rural communities — physically walked, in person, in the hill country of Adair and Cherokee and Sequoyah counties — and she talked to people about water and health care and schools and what the Cherokee Nation could become.

Charlie Soap's role in the 1987 campaign was substantive and specific. As a full-blood Cherokee speaker, he could speak directly to traditionalist constituencies in the language — not just communicating words, but communicating cultural legitimacy. He talked to Cherokee men about the traditional place of women in Cherokee society, about the matrilineal structures and the women's councils, about the historical precedent for women in authority. He was not arguing that Mankiller deserved their support on abstract principles of gender equality. He was speaking to them in their shared language about their shared history, rooting her leadership in Cherokee tradition rather than presenting it as a break from that tradition. It was the most effective argument available, and it was the one that could only be made by someone like Soap.

### The Runoff Victory Over Perry Wheeler

The first-round results in 1987 gave Mankiller 45 percent of the vote — a plurality, but not a majority. A runoff against Perry Wheeler was required. She won the runoff. For the first time, Wilma Mankiller was Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation not by constitutional succession but by direct electoral choice. She had been chosen, specifically and by name, for the top position.

The 1987 victory had a significance beyond the numbers. She had run in her own right, against organized opposition that included explicit appeals to gender and health, and she had won. The precedent established in 1983 by succession was now confirmed by election: women could hold the highest executive office in the Cherokee Nation, and they could win it. The argument from the opposition had been heard, and the Cherokee electorate had answered it.

## 6. Re-election 1991: 83 Percent and a Mandate

The 1991 election result was not a close contest. It was not a runoff. It was not a matter of absentee ballots and narrow margins. In June 1991, Wilma Mankiller was re-elected Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation with 83 percent of the vote — the largest electoral margin in the history of the Nation. No chief before her had won with this share of the vote.

The margin demands careful explanation, because 83 percent is not a plurality — it is a near-consensus. To achieve it, Mankiller had to have won not only her natural supporters from the beginning but also a substantial fraction of the people who had opposed her in 1983 and 1987 — people who had voted against her on grounds that had nothing to do with policy and a great deal to do with sex and doubt. Those people had changed their votes.

The explanation for the changed votes is not complicated. Between 1985 and 1991, the Cherokee Nation had built three rural health clinics. It had secured self-governance compacts that allowed the Nation to manage federal funds directly, bypassing the Bureau of Indian Affairs and the bureaucratic delays and distortions that characterized BIA administration. It had expanded Head Start programs. It had established the Institute for Cherokee Literacy to preserve Sequoyah's syllabary. It had restructured Tribal Council elections from at-large to district-based representation, fundamentally altering the political power balance between urban and rural communities. It had established ambulance services in rural areas. It had established mobile eye-care clinics. It had revived Sequoyah High School.

People who had opposed Mankiller because she was a woman now had children whose vision problems had been caught by a mobile clinic. They had elderly relatives in communities that had never had an ambulance before. They had neighbors whose children were attending a better-resourced Head Start program. The infrastructure of daily life had changed, and the change was traceable to the administration they had tried to prevent.

The 83 percent is what happens when you build things that work.

## 7. The Self-Governance Compact: Rewriting the Terms of Federal Partnership

### The Structure of the Problem

To understand what Mankiller accomplished through self-governance compacts, it is necessary to understand what the alternative was. Since the late nineteenth century, the Bureau of Indian Affairs had administered the federal government's trust responsibilities to tribal nations — managing funds, providing services, overseeing programs — through a centralized bureaucratic structure that was, by the 1980s, widely acknowledged to be dysfunctional. The BIA was chronically underfunded, chronically understaffed, and chronically late in delivering the services it was supposed to provide. It was also structurally paternalistic: the design of the federal trust relationship positioned the BIA as the decision-maker for the expenditure of funds that were, legally and morally, intended to benefit tribal nations, and tribal nations as passive recipients of decisions made in Washington.

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 — Public Law 93-638 — had created a mechanism for tribal nations to assume direct operation of federal programs, contracting with the federal government to provide services that the BIA had previously provided. The "638 contracts" were a significant step, but the contracting mechanism still involved substantial federal oversight, still required negotiation of individual program contracts, and still left the fundamental structure of federal authority over tribal program management largely intact.

Mankiller, working with the federal pilot program on tribal self-governance that was established in the late 1980s, sought something more comprehensive: a compact that would give the Cherokee Nation a block grant of federal funds — the full amount that the federal government had been spending on programs for the Cherokee Nation — and full discretionary authority to spend those funds according to tribal priorities, without the program-by-program negotiating and approval process that characterized the contracting mechanism.

### What the Compacts Did

The self-governance compact that the Cherokee Nation negotiated under Mankiller's leadership was not a single document but an evolving relationship — a series of agreements that progressively expanded the Nation's authority over federal funds and federal programs. The core of the compact was the block-grant structure: rather than receiving funds for specific federally defined programs, the Cherokee Nation received an aggregate of the federal resources that would otherwise have been administered through the BIA and through individual program contracts, and had the authority to allocate those resources according to Cherokee priorities.

This was transformative for several reasons. First, it eliminated the delays and bureaucratic friction of the BIA contracting process, which had historically meant that money intended for Cherokee programs arrived late, was allocated by federal rather than tribal priorities, and was subject to clawback if tribal expenditures did not conform precisely to federal program definitions. Second, it gave the Cherokee Nation the ability to move resources between programs based on actual community need rather than federal program categories — to spend more on a health clinic that was oversubscribed and less on a program that was underperforming, without the program-by-program re-authorization that BIA administration required. Third, it created institutional capacity within the Cherokee Nation's own administrative structure, because managing block grants required the Nation to build its own accounting, program evaluation, and administrative systems.

The assumption in the block-grant model is that the tribal government knows its own community's needs better than federal administrators in Washington. Mankiller believed this with conviction — it was the same conviction that animated the Bell water project, the same belief that communities are the best judges of what they need and the best agents of their own development.

The financial scale is significant. By the end of Mankiller's tenure, the Cherokee Nation was securing approximately $125 million annually in federal assistance through its self-governance agreements — funds administered by the Nation directly, not filtered through BIA overhead and administrative costs. This was not new money in the sense of additional federal appropriations; it was, largely, money that the federal government had already committed to Cherokee programs. What changed was who controlled it, who spent it, and how quickly and efficiently it reached the communities it was intended to serve.

### The Tax-Sharing Compact with Oklahoma

Alongside the federal self-governance compacts, Mankiller negotiated a tax-sharing compact with Oklahoma Governor David Walters during the early 1990s. The compact addressed the complex question of taxation authority in the overlap between state and tribal jurisdiction — a perpetual source of conflict between Oklahoma and the tribal nations within its borders.

The Cherokee Nation, as a sovereign tribal government, operates on lands within a state that also taxes economic activity. Tribes argue that sovereign status exempts them from state taxation on tribal lands. States argue the opposite. The result is typically litigation, regulatory friction, and economic uncertainty. Mankiller preferred a different approach. Under the compact she negotiated with Walters, the Cherokee Nation could collect state taxes from businesses operating on tribal lands and retain a portion of those revenues — principally tobacco tax revenue. The state got compliance without having to fight for it. The Cherokee Nation captured a revenue stream that could fund tribal government operations.

The compact was significant beyond the immediate revenue. It established the Cherokee Nation as a negotiating partner with the state government rather than an adversary in litigation. It was a recognition of the Nation's standing as a government — a party to a compact between governments. Mankiller's approach to the state-tribal relationship consistently sought to establish the Cherokee Nation as a peer governmental actor rather than a subordinate one, and the tax-sharing compact was an expression of that approach in the specific context of Oklahoma.

### The Private Industry Council

Economic development requires more than tribal enterprise. It requires the infrastructure of the labor market: job training, employer relationships, financial and technical assistance for small business development. Mankiller established the Private Industry Council as a mechanism to connect Cherokee tribal government with private sector employers through the Job Training Partnership Act's federal resources, creating coordinated job training programs that produced workers with skills that matched real market demand.

The council provided employment training opportunities, financial and technical assistance to tribal members who wanted to start their own enterprises, and a bridge between the tribal government's development priorities and the private economy operating in and around Cherokee territory. Under the broader economic development framework Mankiller built, the Cherokee Nation established a tribal electronic harness and cabling manufacturing company, constructed a hydroelectric plant, and developed horticultural operations. Factories, retail stores, restaurants, and gaming operations added to the revenue base. The $25 million in annual tribal revenues that characterized the end of her tenure was the product of this diversified economic strategy — not a single operation, but a portfolio of enterprises built on the foundation of tribal administrative capacity.

## 8. Rural Health Centers and the Infrastructure of Access

### The Starting Point

When Mankiller became Principal Chief in 1985, the Cherokee Nation's healthcare infrastructure was inadequate by any reasonable measure for a jurisdiction covering fourteen counties and a growing enrolled population. The Indian Health Service operated W.W. Hastings Indian Hospital in Tahlequah and a small number of clinics, but access in rural communities was poor. Communities in the farthest reaches of the fourteen-county jurisdiction were effectively outside the healthcare system.

The combination of poverty, distance, lack of transportation, and inadequate clinic facilities meant that preventable conditions went untreated, chronic diseases went unmanaged, and emergencies went unresponded to until they became crises. Mankiller understood this both as a policy problem and as a personal one. She had grown up without running water in Adair County. She had navigated her own extensive medical needs — the aftermath of the 1979 accident, the myasthenia gravis, the advancing kidney disease — through a healthcare system she understood intimately in its gaps and its failures. She did not approach healthcare access as an abstract policy goal.

### Three Rural Health Centers

During her tenure, the Cherokee Nation established three rural health clinics in communities that had previously had no nearby medical access. These were not satellite offices of a large hospital system — they were self-contained, primary-care facilities designed to serve the specific population density and disease burden of rural Cherokee communities. They were located in communities identified by the kind of door-to-door assessment that characterized Mankiller's approach: she and her staff knew where people lived, knew which communities were farthest from existing services, and built where the gap was greatest.

The clinics provided primary care, preventive services, and chronic disease management — the routine and ongoing care that prevents the acute emergencies that are far more expensive and far more harmful. Access to a clinic ten minutes away changes the relationship to illness at a population level: people come in earlier, before conditions become severe; children receive developmental screenings that identify problems when intervention is still effective; elderly people manage blood pressure and diabetes instead of presenting at emergency rooms when the conditions have progressed to crisis. Tribal data from the Mankiller years shows infant mortality declining and general health outcomes improving within the Cherokee Nation — the cumulative product of multiple concurrent investments.

### Mobile Eye-Care Clinics

The mobile eye-care clinic program addressed a specific and documentable gap: vision problems were prevalent in Cherokee communities, particularly among children and elders, and the absence of ophthalmology services within practical reach of rural communities meant that vision loss that was preventable or correctable was, in practice, neither prevented nor corrected.

A mobile clinic — an ophthalmology service that traveled to communities rather than requiring communities to travel to it — addressed the problem at its structural root. The barrier was distance and access; the solution was to eliminate the barrier by bringing the service to where the people were. This is the Bell model applied to healthcare delivery: rather than assuming that the standard model (clinic in a central location, patients travel to it) was fixed, Mankiller's team asked what the actual obstacle was and then built a solution specific to that obstacle. The actual obstacle in rural Oklahoma for ophthalmology was not the unavailability of eye care in the abstract — it was the absence of transportation, the distances involved, and the economic cost of a day spent traveling to Tahlequah that rural families with agricultural obligations could not easily afford. Mobile clinics solved all three problems simultaneously.

### Ambulance Services

The ambulance services program addressed a life-or-death gap: rural communities without ambulance service had no emergency medical response capability. A cardiac event, a farm accident, a child's sudden illness — in a community without ambulance service, the response was whoever was present and whatever they could improvise. The addition of ambulance services to communities that had not had them was not a marginal improvement in convenience. It was the difference between surviving a medical emergency and dying from it.

The significance of ambulance services in a fourteen-county rural jurisdiction cannot be overstated. The response-time calculus in rural Oklahoma — where communities might be thirty or forty minutes from the nearest hospital, over roads that are not always navigable in adverse weather — meant that emergency medical response at the community level was a prerequisite for anything that could reasonably be called adequate healthcare.

### The Indian Child Welfare Act

Mankiller's administration worked actively to implement the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 — federal legislation that had established tribal jurisdiction over child custody proceedings involving Native children and that required state agencies to work with tribal governments in child welfare cases. The history of the forced removal of Native children from their families and communities — through boarding schools, through state child welfare systems that routinely placed Native children in non-Native homes — was a direct parallel to the relocation policy that had taken Mankiller's own family to San Francisco in 1957.

The surveys that had motivated ICWA's passage were stark: in states with significant Native populations, 25 to 35 percent of Native children were being removed from their families, at rates in some areas nearly sixteen times higher than for non-Native children. The Act established federal standards for child custody proceedings, created presumptions favoring placement with extended family and tribal communities, and gave tribal courts jurisdiction over child welfare cases involving their members. The Cherokee Nation's active engagement with ICWA implementation was both a policy commitment and a moral one — the recognition that cultural continuity and family integrity are foundational to sovereignty in precisely the same way that water infrastructure and health clinics are foundational.

## 9. Education: Sequoyah's Syllabary and the Schools

### The Institute for Cherokee Literacy

Sequoyah's syllabary — the writing system completed around 1821 and described in detail in Module 1 of this series — is one of the extraordinary intellectual achievements of the nineteenth century. An entirely new writing system, invented by a single individual working without formal schooling in any language, it was adopted by the Cherokee Nation within years of its completion and enabled a rapid expansion of Cherokee literacy, journalism, and religious publication. The Cherokee Phoenix, established in 1828, was publishing in both Cherokee and English at a time when the written literacy rate among Cherokee citizens exceeded English literacy rates among white Americans in many regions.

By the late twentieth century, the syllabary and the language it encoded were in genuine danger. Cherokee speakers — people who had grown up speaking Cherokee as their first language, for whom the syllabary was not an academic exercise but a living script — were aging. The younger generation of Cherokee citizens, urbanized and educated in English-language schools, was increasingly distant from the language. The erosion of language fluency that followed from the boarding school era, from relocation, from the general pressure of assimilation that federal policy had applied to Native communities for more than a century, had created a situation in which the Cherokee language faced real generational loss.

Mankiller established the Institute for Cherokee Literacy as a deliberate institutional response to this threat. The Institute's mission was to preserve the syllabary and the language it encoded through community-based teaching programs, curriculum development, and materials production that would support Cherokee language instruction in schools and community settings. The work was both archival — documenting the language from fluent speakers while those speakers were still living — and pedagogical: developing the tools that would allow the next generation to learn what the previous generation had been partly denied the opportunity to pass on.

The Institute for Cherokee Literacy was not a monument or a museum. It was a living educational program designed to reverse a trend, and it worked in the same way that Bell worked: by investing in community capacity rather than in the passive delivery of a product. The goal was not to produce a dictionary and a grammar reference and declare the language preserved. The goal was to produce Cherokee speakers.

Language is sovereignty. This is not a metaphor. When a people cannot speak their own language, they have conceded something fundamental about who they are — something that no amount of tribal enterprise revenue or federal self-governance compact can restore. Mankiller understood the Institute for Cherokee Literacy and the rural health centers as expressions of the same underlying principle: a self-determining people must have the capacity to sustain themselves physically and culturally. Health without culture is management. Culture without health is precarious. Self-determination requires both.

### Sequoyah High School

Sequoyah High School in Tahlequah had a history in the Cherokee Nation that stretched back to the nineteenth century. The original Cherokee Female Seminary, established in 1851 — one of the first institutions of higher learning west of the Mississippi established by Native Americans for their own children — had evolved through multiple transformations, eventually becoming a boarding school operated by the Bureau of Indian Affairs. In 1985 — the same year Mankiller became Principal Chief — the BIA transferred operational control of the school to the Cherokee Nation.

Mankiller's administration undertook the revitalization of Sequoyah High School in the 1991-1992 school year. The revitalization involved not merely physical renovation of facilities but a reconsideration of the school's mission in relation to the Cherokee Nation's educational goals — a reconnection to the tradition of Cherokee-controlled education for Cherokee students that had characterized the original Female Seminary. The revitalized school required more credits for graduation than Oklahoma's standard, including courses in Cherokee history, language, and cultural identity. It became a school that was explicitly Cherokee-governed, answering to Cherokee leadership rather than federal administrators, and serving students from multiple tribal nations while maintaining its specifically Cherokee institutional character.

The significance of reclaiming Sequoyah was symbolic and practical simultaneously. For a people whose children had been taken to government boarding schools and punished for being Cherokee — for speaking the language that Sequoyah had given them the tools to read and write — operating their own high school with a cultural curriculum was a reclamation that no grant or revenue figure can fully measure.

### Head Start Expansion

Head Start — the federal early childhood education program established in 1965 — had been available in some Cherokee Nation communities before Mankiller's tenure, but its coverage was incomplete. Rural communities far from the administrative centers of the program were underserved. Mankiller's administration expanded Head Start access to additional communities, specifically targeting the gap between where the program existed and where the children who most needed it lived.

The expansion of Head Start aligned with the Bell model in its geographic targeting: instead of assuming that families would travel to existing program sites, the administration asked where the children were and built program capacity there. Head Start addressed child cognitive development, nutrition, dental care, and parent engagement simultaneously. The cognitive and developmental benefits of quality early childhood education are among the most rigorously documented outcomes in educational research. Expanding access in rural Cherokee communities was, in the long-term calculation Mankiller was always making, an investment in the human capital of the Nation's next generation.

## 10. Changing the Tribal Council: From At-Large to District Elections

### The Problem of At-Large Representation

Among the least visible and most consequential of Mankiller's policy achievements was the structural reform of the Tribal Council's electoral system — a change from at-large elections to district-based representation that fundamentally altered the distribution of political power within the Cherokee Nation.

Under the at-large system, Tribal Council members were elected by the entire enrolled Cherokee population, wherever they lived. This sounds democratic in the abstract, but its practical effect was to systematically underrepresent rural communities. The Cherokee Nation's enrolled population included a substantial urban component — Cherokee citizens living in Tulsa, in Oklahoma City, in cities across the country — who could vote in tribal elections. In an at-large system, a candidate could win a seat by performing well among urban voters without ever engaging substantively with the rural communities where the Nation's most acute service needs were concentrated and where the self-help community development model was actually being implemented.

The result, over time, was a Tribal Council whose composition reflected urban voters' priorities more accurately than rural residents' needs — an ironic inversion of the problem that the Bell project had been designed to address. Bell was a community that had been ignored by standard approaches to rural development because the standard approaches were not designed around rural community capacity. At-large Tribal Council elections produced a similar dynamic at the governance level: the people closest to the ground, in the communities doing the hardest work with the least resources, had the least influence over the tribal government that was supposed to serve them.

### The Reform and Its Consequences

Mankiller's administration reformed the Tribal Council election system to a district-based structure. Under the new system, council seats represented specific geographic districts — areas with defined boundaries corresponding to the actual geography of Cherokee settlement — rather than being contested by the enrolled electorate at large. This change was structural and lasting: it encoded into the constitutional structure of the Nation's governance a formal commitment to geographic representation that the at-large system had not provided.

The consequences were significant. Rural communities gained council members who represented specifically their districts — people who had run in campaigns targeting rural Cherokee voters, who had made commitments to rural Cherokee priorities, and who were accountable to rural Cherokee constituents for the delivery of those commitments. The urban Cherokee voter did not lose representation; districts in or near urban centers still elected council members. What changed was that rural communities had their own representatives, rather than competing in an at-large field against a larger urban electorate.

This reform rarely receives the prominence of the health clinics or the water project in accounts of Mankiller's legacy, because structural electoral reforms are less photogenic than sixteen miles of pipe or a new clinic building. But the governance implications were substantial. A Tribal Council whose structure ensured rural representation was a fundamentally different institution from one that allowed rural representation to be overridden by urban electoral concentration. The district system created the conditions for the Bell model to be politically viable at the Council level — for rural community development priorities to have advocates in the deliberative body that approved the Nation's budget and programs.

The reform also required Mankiller to do something her early strategy had avoided: engage the council directly on a legislative matter of structural self-interest. Persuading an elected body to restructure its own elections is one of the harder acts of political persuasion available. She succeeded. The patient accumulation of credibility and demonstrated performance in her first years as chief — the administrative record she had built while deliberately avoiding unnecessary legislative confrontations — was what made that persuasion possible.

## 11. The Numbers: Population, Revenue, and Growth

### Enrolled Population: 68,000 to 170,000

When Mankiller became Deputy Chief in 1983, the Cherokee Nation's enrolled population stood at approximately 68,000 citizens. When she left office in 1995, the enrolled population had grown to approximately 170,000. This is not a small change in absolute terms, but it requires careful interpretation.

The growth was not primarily biological — the Cherokee Nation's enrolled population did not more than double through natural increase in twelve years. The growth reflected several concurrent factors.

First, the citizenship eligibility criteria and the process of applying for enrollment became more accessible during this period, and Cherokee citizens who had not previously been enrolled — particularly those living outside Oklahoma, the descendants of the urban relocatees, people who were Cherokee by descent and culture but who had not navigated the enrollment process — enrolled in larger numbers. Second, the expansion of tribal services created an incentive for eligible citizens to formalize their enrollment: if the Cherokee Nation was now providing health care, educational programs, and economic development resources, the practical value of formal enrollment increased in concrete ways. Third, the growth reflected the broader revival of Native American identity that had been building since the late 1960s — since Alcatraz, since the American Indian Movement, since the political and cultural awakening that Mankiller's own biography had passed through.

The generation that had been told, in the assimilationist logic of the relocation era, to set aside their tribal identity in favor of mainstream American integration, was a generation whose children and grandchildren were, in growing numbers, reclaiming the identity that had been discouraged. The Cherokee Nation's willingness to assert its sovereignty, to build its own institutions, and to demonstrate that tribal citizenship meant something concrete made that reclamation feel less like nostalgia and more like a practical choice.

The growth from 68,000 to 170,000 is, from one angle, a measure of institutional credibility: more people wanted to be formally part of a Nation that had demonstrated it could deliver.

### Annual Revenues: $25 Million

The $25 million in annual revenues from multiple sources that characterized the Cherokee Nation's financial base during Mankiller's tenure tells a story of deliberate diversification. The sources included tribal enterprises — the Cherokee Nation had been developing economic enterprises, including businesses in Tahlequah and the surrounding area, that generated revenue independent of federal appropriations. They included gaming revenues, which became increasingly significant as the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 established the framework for tribal gaming operations; the Cherokee Nation's entry into gaming, politically complicated within the Nation as it was, was a financial reality that Mankiller navigated with characteristic pragmatism. They included manufacturing, retail, hospitality, and the hydroelectric facility.

The path from minimal enterprise revenue to $25 million in a decade required accepting that economic sovereignty and cultural sovereignty are linked but distinct. The tribal electronic harness and cabling manufacturing company was unglamorous, industrial, and real. The hydroelectric plant generated electricity and demonstrated that the Nation's natural resources could serve its economy. None of these individually was transformative. Together, as a portfolio, they represented a Cherokee economy that could generate its own revenue — less subject to the congressional appropriations cycle that had historically made tribal governments dependent on political winds in Washington.

### Federal Assistance: $125 Million

The $125 million annual federal assistance figure requires careful interpretation, because it is easy to read it as evidence of dependency rather than of what it actually represents: capacity.

This was not new money created by Mankiller's advocacy in the sense of additional congressional appropriations. It was, substantially, a more efficient and more complete collection and administration of federal trust responsibilities that already existed — money that the federal government was already obligated to spend for the benefit of Cherokee citizens, which had previously been spent less efficiently and less effectively through BIA administration. What the self-governance compacts achieved was to convert a bureaucratic obligation into an institutional reality: the funds arrived, were managed by Cherokee administrators with Cherokee priorities, and reached Cherokee communities with less delay and distortion than the BIA contracting system had produced.

When the BIA administered a program for a tribe, the tribe received a service. When the tribe administered the program itself under a self-governance compact, the tribe received both the service delivery capacity and the funding. The money followed the authority. Mankiller's insistence on self-governance agreements was therefore not just a matter of political principle — it was a fiscal strategy. More authority meant more resources to deploy according to Cherokee priorities rather than federal program specifications.

The net effect on the scale of the Cherokee Nation's governmental operations was dramatic. A tribal government operating with $25 million in revenues and $125 million in federally administered trust funds could staff clinics, build schools, fund community development projects, maintain administrative systems, negotiate from a position of institutional credibility with state and federal governments, and sustain a decade of programmatic expansion. This was not a wealthy government by the standards of state or federal budgets, but it was a government with the resources to be effective.

## 12. Marriage to Charlie Soap: Partnership in the Work

On October 18, 1986 — roughly a year after she had been sworn in as Principal Chief — Wilma Mankiller married Charlie Soap. The ceremony was held at her home on the land she had returned to after San Francisco, the Adair County land that was, in some sense, the ground her entire adult life had been oriented toward reclaiming.

Charlie Soap was a full-blood Cherokee man who had been Mankiller's primary field partner in the Bell project. He was the organizer who had walked into Bell and talked to people and figured out how to keep the work moving when the terrain was rocky and the timeline was slipping and the commitment of individual residents needed to be renewed. He spoke Cherokee. He understood rural Cherokee communities not as policy abstractions but as specific collections of specific people with specific histories and specific capabilities. He was, by every account, a person whose understanding of grassroots organizing was complementary to Mankiller's in exactly the way that effective partnerships are complementary — covering different ground, bringing different strengths, sharing the same fundamental commitments.

Their marriage was a partnership in the full sense. Soap continued to work in community development for the Cherokee Nation after Mankiller became chief. The relationship between the chief's vision and the field's implementation required exactly the kind of person Soap was: someone who could translate institutional commitment into community reality, and who could bring community reality back to the institutional decision-making table.

The marriage immediately generated political complications: opponents demanded that Soap resign from his position with the Indian Housing Authority, arguing that a conflict of interest existed given Mankiller's role as chief. He submitted his resignation, effective January 1987. The timing drew criticism — some argued the delay allowed him to qualify for retirement benefits. The controversy was real, but it was also a preview of the sustained effort to find any available lever to delegitimize Mankiller's leadership. She did not resign from the controversy any more than she resigned from the opposition she had faced since 1983. She continued governing.

Mankiller described the marriage in her memoir with characteristic directness: Charlie Soap was the person she wanted to spend her life with, and he was also the person who understood the work she was doing and why it mattered. The two things were not separate. This integration — of the personal and the political, of the intimate and the institutional — was characteristic of how Mankiller understood commitment.

The marriage was her second. Her first marriage, to Hector Olaya, a wealthy Ecuadorian businessman she had met in San Francisco in the 1960s, had produced two daughters — Felicia and Gina — and ended in divorce in 1974, roughly contemporaneously with her political awakening through the Alcatraz occupation and its aftermath. The person who married Hector Olaya was not the same person who returned to Oklahoma, and the marriage that had worked for the earlier version of her life did not work for the version that had come through Alcatraz and the Bay Area organizing and the accident and the decision to go home.

Charlie Soap was, in the terms of the life she had actually built, the right partnership for the chief she had become.

## 13. Leadership Style: Consensus, Door-Knocking, and the Art of Listening

### How She Worked

The accounts of Mankiller's leadership that appear in interviews, in the memoir she wrote with Michael Wallis, and in the recollections of people who worked with and around her converge on a set of consistent practices that defined how she approached the exercise of power.

She walked door to door. This is reported often enough to be treated as a cliche of her biography, but the practice is worth taking seriously as a governing philosophy. A principal chief who physically appears at the homes of rural Cherokee citizens in the hill country of Adair and Sequoyah counties — not at campaign events or official meetings, but at the actual doors of actual houses — is demonstrating something specific about how she understands the relationship between executive authority and the people who have granted it. She is saying, by showing up, that the direction of accountability runs from the office to the community, not from the community to the office. She is also, practically, gathering information that cannot be gathered any other way: the specific, particular, ground-level intelligence about what is working and what is not, what communities need and what they already have, that no report or survey can substitute for.

She built consensus before moving. This was not indecisiveness; it was a specific judgment about what kinds of change would hold and what kinds would not. Change imposed on a community by executive authority was change that lasted only as long as the executive authority that imposed it. Change built through a community's own deliberative process — through the slower, messier, more labor-intensive work of bringing people to shared understanding — was change that the community would sustain, protect, and build on. The Bell project was, among other things, an exercise in consensus-building: getting three hundred people to agree to give fourteen months of their labor to something required that they understand what they were building and why, and that they feel genuine ownership over the decision to do it.

She empowered local leaders rather than centralizing authority. The Cherokee Nation's fourteen-county jurisdiction is too large and too diverse for any central office to manage through top-down direction. The communities in each county have different histories, different economies, different social structures, different relationships to the tribal government and to each other. Effective governance of this diversity requires leaders within each community who have standing, competence, and authority — people who can make decisions locally, mobilize local resources, and adapt general policy to specific conditions without waiting for approval from Tahlequah. Mankiller's approach was consistently to identify these local leaders, support them, give them resources and authority, and then trust the results. This required a specific kind of confidence in other people's capacity, and it was the same confidence she had exercised in Bell: she believed people could build things they had never built before, and she was right.

### Patience as Strategy

Mankiller's early approach to the Tribal Council — avoiding legislative confrontations, focusing on executive domains, building a record of competence — was not timidity. It was a strategic calculation that her ability to accomplish anything depended on maintaining a working relationship with the council over time, even if that relationship was cool. She was right. The council that she could not persuade on most questions in 1986 was the council she persuaded to restructure its own elections by the late 1980s. The patient accumulation of credibility and demonstrated performance was what made that possible.

This patience extended to the opposition she faced as a woman. She did not give speeches about sexism. She did not position her candidacy as a feminist cause, though it was one by any reasonable definition. She did not invite the frame of woman-breaking-barriers because that frame made sex the subject of the election and she wanted the subject of the election to be the Cherokee Nation's future. What she did was build things, and building things was the argument that mattered.

### Negotiation Over Confrontation

The tax-sharing compact with Oklahoma, the self-governance agreements with federal agencies, the restructuring of council elections — none of these were achieved through direct confrontation or legal challenge. They were achieved through negotiation, persuasion, and the construction of agreements that the other parties had reasons to accept. Mankiller consistently preferred to find the arrangement that served Cherokee interests without requiring the other party to lose — not because she was conflict-averse, but because she understood that negotiated agreements outlast confrontational victories. When both parties have interests in an arrangement, both parties maintain it.

The most quoted expression of her leadership philosophy: "I want to be remembered as the person who helped us restore faith in ourselves." Not faith in government programs, or in the tribal administration, or in federal assistance — though all of these were tools she used. Faith in themselves. The conviction that the Cherokee people, organized and supported and led with honesty, could solve their own problems and build their own future.

## 14. Resistance and Sexism: What She Was Up Against and How She Won

### The Nature of the Opposition

The resistance Mankiller faced was not uniform in its character. There was explicit, stated sexism: men who believed openly that women should not hold executive office in the Cherokee Nation, who said so publicly, and who organized politically around that belief. This faction produced the death threats and the tire-slashing that characterized the 1983 and 1987 campaigns. It was real, it was organized, and it was not a fringe element — it had enough political support to make runoff elections close.

There was also less explicit but equally real institutional resistance: the assumption, built into the culture of tribal governance, that certain roles were male roles, that a woman in an executive meeting should frame her contributions differently than a man in the same meeting. This was not the same as open hostility; it was the ambient friction of operating in an institution that had not previously encountered a woman at this level of authority.

And there was substantive political opposition that was not primarily about sex: opposition to her activist background, to her specific policy positions, to the community development approach she championed over more conventional tribal economic strategies. This opposition was legitimate political disagreement, and Mankiller was clear-eyed enough to distinguish it from the sexist opposition, even when the two were intertwined.

Her most quoted observation on the subject of discrimination: "I've run into more discrimination as a woman than as an Indian." This is a statement worth sitting with. For a Cherokee woman who had experienced the full weight of federal Indian policy — the relocation, the BIA's paternalism, the boarding schools that had preceded her generation — to say that gender discrimination was more present in her daily life than racial or ethnic discrimination is a significant claim about where power operates and how.

### The Death Threats

The threats were not trivial, and this account would be incomplete without acknowledging them directly. Death threats during the 1983 and 1987 campaigns were reported to the appropriate authorities. Slashed tires are a form of intimidation that carries a clear physical message: we know where you are and we can reach you. For a woman with daughters, managing significant health challenges, operating in a political environment where her right to be present was contested by some people willing to act on that contestation, the threats were a real dimension of the political work.

That she continued — that she walked door to door in rural Cherokee communities in 1987 despite knowing that the people slashing her tires were out there — is worth noting not as heroism but as a specific choice. She chose to continue because she had concluded that the work was more important than the risk, and that the demonstration of that conclusion was itself part of the work. Leaders who retreat from public engagement when threatened demonstrate that threats are an effective tool. Leaders who do not retreat demonstrate the opposite. Mankiller's continued presence in public, in rural communities, throughout both campaigns, was itself a form of argument about the cost of opposition.

### How She Won

Her response to the sexist opposition was, in its essentials, to ignore it as a topic and defeat it as a fact. The Bell project, completed before the 1983 election, was the most important single element of her political biography not because of what it said about her but because of what it demonstrated about her. It demonstrated that she could identify a problem, design a solution, organize a community to implement it, and deliver results on a timeline and within a budget. No argument about whether a woman should be Deputy Chief could override the fact that the woman in question had just laid sixteen miles of pipe in fourteen months.

After 1985, the demonstration continued at larger scale. Every clinic opened, every school revitalized, every compact negotiated, every community development project completed was further evidence that the opposition's premise — that a woman could not lead this Nation effectively — was empirically wrong. By 1991, when she won 83 percent of the vote, the opposition had not changed its minds about women in the abstract. It had changed its evaluation of Wilma Mankiller specifically, because the evidence that she was an effective chief was visible and irrefutable.

This is the model she kept returning to: not argument, but demonstration. Not assertion, but proof. It is a model with particular relevance for leaders operating in contexts where their right to lead is contested not on grounds of competence but on grounds of identity. The most effective answer to "you don't belong here" is sustained, visible, documented achievement that makes the objection look not merely wrong but absurd.

## 15. The Community Development Philosophy: Analysis of a Model

### What the Model Was

The Mankiller model of community development can be stated concisely, though its concise statement does not capture its full implications: communities build their own futures with institutional support, and the process of building is itself the product.

The second clause — the process of building is itself the product — is the part that distinguishes the model from standard development approaches. Most development theory, at least as it was practiced through the mid-twentieth century, treated infrastructure as the product: the pipe, the clinic, the school. The community was the recipient of the product. The product was good or bad based on whether it met the defined need. The community's relationship to the process of producing the product was not a relevant variable.

Mankiller's model treated the community's relationship to the process as the primary variable. Bell had running water at the end of fourteen months, and that was unambiguously good. But the more important product of the Bell project was a community of three hundred people who had discovered something about their own organizational capacity that they had not previously known. They had planned a complex undertaking. They had sustained commitment across fourteen months of hard work. They had solved problems as they arose. They had delivered on a commitment they had made to themselves and to each other. This is not a soft or incidental outcome. It is the difference between a community that has running water and a community that can build the next thing it needs, and the one after that.

### The Self-Determination Theory Beneath the Practice

Mankiller's model was a specific, applied version of what the self-determination policy framework — embodied in the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 and extended through the self-governance compact structure — was trying to achieve at the political level. Self-determination as political philosophy holds that the development of a people's capacity for self-governance is more important than the specific policy outcomes that any particular government delivers, because a people capable of self-governance can produce its own policy outcomes and adapt them as conditions change, while a people dependent on external governance cannot.

At the community level, Mankiller translated this principle as follows: a community capable of building its own water system is more likely to maintain that water system, more likely to build the next project it needs, and more capable of advocating effectively for its interests with the tribal government and with state and federal authorities than a community that has had its water system built for it by outside contractors.

This is the relationship between the Bell project and the self-governance compacts that she negotiated as chief. They are not two separate achievements — one a community development project and one a federal policy negotiation. They are the same principle operating at two different scales. The Bell project demonstrated that Cherokee communities could build their own infrastructure. The self-governance compacts demonstrated that the Cherokee Nation could manage its own institutional programs. In both cases, the argument was the same: we are capable of doing this ourselves, and doing it ourselves produces better results than having it done for us.

### The Cherokee Cultural Tradition Beneath the Method

Mankiller was candid about the sources of her community development philosophy. She drew on the organizing tradition she had absorbed in the Bay Area — Saul Alinsky's community organizing principles, the model of the Highlander Folk School, the participatory development theory that was becoming more influential in international development practice in the 1970s and 1980s. She drew on her own experience of what external provision of services had and had not done for her community: federal relocation programs, she knew from family experience, could move people and provide minimal short-term assistance without building anything that lasted.

But she also drew on what she understood to be a specifically Cherokee tradition of collective responsibility. The traditional Cherokee town governance — described in Module 1 — had been organized around consensus decision-making and collective labor. The clan system had distributed the obligations of mutual aid across the entire Nation. The tradition of community work parties — groups of neighbors coming together to accomplish tasks that no individual could accomplish alone — was embedded in Cherokee social practice in ways that predated any contact with Alinsky or Highlander. The Bell model, in Mankiller's understanding of it, was not imported from outside — it was a recovery of a Cherokee way of doing things that had been suppressed by federal paternalism but had never been entirely extinguished.

This is not a romantic claim. It is an analytical one. The self-help model worked in Bell not only because of Mankiller's organizational skill or Soap's field work, but also because the residents of Bell were already, in their social relationships with each other, a community capable of collective action. The organizing work identified and activated a capacity that was already there. That capacity was a product of Cherokee cultural tradition as well as individual character. Mankiller understood this, and her understanding informed how she approached every subsequent project.

### Replication Across the Nation

After Bell, the self-help community development model was replicated in other Cherokee communities. The Nation's community development program, which Mankiller continued to expand as chief, identified communities with specific infrastructure needs and organized similar processes: tribal support for materials and technical guidance, community labor for implementation, structured timelines, and ongoing engagement to sustain commitment across the arc of the project.

Not every replication was as clean as Bell. Bell was the proof of concept, and proof-of-concept projects benefit from exceptional care and attention that subsequent replications cannot always sustain at the same level. But the model held: communities that went through the self-help process tended to have different relationships to what they had built than communities that had received comparable infrastructure through standard provision. And the organizational capacity that developed through the process — the networks of trust and collaboration, the shared experience of collective accomplishment, the local leaders identified and strengthened by the work — was an asset that showed up in subsequent projects and in community resilience more broadly.

### Why This Matters Beyond the Cherokee Nation

The Mankiller community development model has implications beyond Cherokee Nation policy. It speaks to a general question in development practice: what is the relationship between the provision of services and the development of the capacity to provide services? And it answers that question with a clear position: the latter is more valuable and more durable than the former, and the two are not in competition — the capacity can be built through the process of providing the service, if that process is designed correctly.

The participatory development models that emerged from the 1970s onward, the asset-based community development framework, the community-driven development approaches that major international development institutions adopted in the 1990s and 2000s — all of them reflect a similar basic insight. What Mankiller contributed was not the theory, which existed in various forms before she applied it, but the demonstration at scale in a specific context: a rural Native American tribal nation in northeastern Oklahoma, with specific history, specific resources, specific constraints, and specific people. The demonstration was specific enough to be credible and general enough to travel.

The Bell model is now cited in community development literature well beyond Indian Country. The Cherokee Word for Water — the 2013 film co-directed by Charlie Soap — has brought it to wider audiences as an illustration of what grassroots self-determination looks like in practice. The sixteen miles of pipe in fourteen months has become, in a sense, a parable: the argument that communities can solve their own problems if supported rather than managed, if trusted rather than administered to.

## 16. The Decision Not to Seek a Third Term

By the winter of 1994-1995, Wilma Mankiller had been governing the Cherokee Nation for a decade. She had won two elections in her own right, the second by an 83 percent margin that would have justified a confidence in continued electoral success. She was fifty years old. She had transformed the institution she led, secured its financial foundations, built its service infrastructure, reformed its electoral structure, and established its standing as a serious partner in federal and state governmental relationships.

She was also facing a lymphoma diagnosis that made a third term incompatible with survival.

The medical context is documented in detail in Module 4 of this series. The relevant point here is that her decision not to seek a third term in 1995 was made with full awareness of what she was leaving and why. She was not defeated. She was not exhausted by the work. She was not done with the vision. She was ill, and the illness required her full attention, and she made the choice that any honest assessment of the circumstances required.

She stepped down in June 1995. Her successor, Joe Byrd, proved far less effective and his tenure was marked by significant controversy within the Nation — a contrast that, to many Cherokee observers, threw into sharp relief what Mankiller's governance had meant and what the Nation had possessed while it had it.

The legacy she left was not a question. The clinics were operating. The compacts were in place. The district election system was governing. The Institute for Cherokee Literacy was teaching. The schools were rebuilt. The self-help projects were ongoing. The enrolled population that had grown from 68,000 to 170,000 had a government that could serve it. None of these things required her continued presence to function. That was, perhaps, the most important thing about them.

A chief who builds institutions that require the chief's continued presence to function has not built institutions. She has built dependencies. Mankiller's institutions functioned because they were designed to function — because the communities that needed them had been involved in building them, because the administrative capacity had been developed within the Nation, because the self-governance compacts put management authority in Cherokee hands rather than in federal bureaucratic structures that would have reorganized when she left.

This is, in the end, the deepest expression of the Bell model: build it so that it holds without you.

In 1994, the year before she stepped down, President Clinton had invited Mankiller to moderate the White House Nation-to-Nation Summit — an assembly of all 545 federally recognized tribes in Washington. The summit produced the establishment of the Office of Indian Justice. She was not just leading the Cherokee Nation; she had become a figure of national significance in the broader framework of federal Indian policy. The woman who had organized sixteen miles of pipe in rural Oklahoma in 1982 was, twelve years later, the person the President of the United States asked to facilitate the most significant gathering of tribal leaders in the modern era.

The scope of the journey, measured in that single trajectory, is the measure of the decade.

## 19. Sources

### Primary Sources

- Mankiller, Wilma, and Michael Wallis. *Mankiller: A Chief and Her People*. St. Martin's Press, 1993. The essential primary source for Mankiller's own account of her path to leadership, the Bell project, the elections, and the policy program. Her voice throughout is specific, direct, and without self-mythologizing.
- Mankiller, Wilma. *Every Day Is a Good Day: Reflections by Contemporary Indigenous Women*. Fulcrum Publishing, 2004. Edited collection that includes Mankiller's own reflections on leadership, community, and the Cherokee philosophical traditions that informed her governance philosophy.
- Mankiller, Wilma. Interviews, 1985–2010. Sources include *The New York Times*, *Ms. Magazine*, National Public Radio, CBS News, and Cherokee Nation media. The consistency of her core positions across decades of interviews demonstrates how stable her philosophy was from the Bell project forward.
- Cherokee Nation. *Tribal Council Proceedings*, 1983–1995. Cherokee Nation Archives, Tahlequah, Oklahoma. Primary record of legislative actions including the district election reform and the approval of self-governance compact negotiations.
- Cherokee Nation. *Annual Reports*, 1985–1995. Documents the programmatic expansion of health services, educational programs, and community development under Mankiller's administration.
- Nation-to-Nation Summit. White House documentation and proceedings, 1994. Records of the summit Mankiller moderated at President Clinton's invitation.

### The Bell Project

- Mankiller, Wilma. Multiple accounts of the Bell project appear in the memoir and in interviews spanning three decades. The basic factual record — sixteen miles of pipe, fourteen months, community labor — is consistent across all sources.
- Soap, Charlie. Interviews and accounts of the Bell project fieldwork. Soap's role as primary field organizer is documented in Mankiller's memoir and in journalistic accounts spanning the years following the project's completion.
- *The Cherokee Word for Water*. Directed by Charlie Soap and Steven Presson. Full Circle Films, 2013. Documentary-drama based on the Bell water project; the most detailed filmic account of the self-help model in practice.
- Department of Housing and Urban Development. Certificate of National Merit for the Kenwood Project. Records of the award given to the subsequent community development project that applied the Bell model.

### Self-Governance and Federal Policy

- Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975. Public Law 93-638. The statutory foundation for tribal contracting of federal programs that preceded and enabled the self-governance compact framework.
- American Indian Self-Governance Demonstration Project. Congressional authorization, 1988. The pilot program that enabled development of the block-grant compact model that the Cherokee Nation adopted under Mankiller's administration.
- Wilkins, David E. *American Indian Politics and the American Political System*. Rowman and Littlefield, 2006. Comprehensive analysis of the political context in which self-governance compacts were negotiated.
- O'Brien, Sharon. *American Indian Tribal Governments*. University of Oklahoma Press, 1989. The structure of tribal governance and the legal framework within which self-governance compacts operate.
- Deloria, Vine, Jr., and Clifford M. Lytle. *The Nations Within: The Past and Future of American Indian Sovereignty*. Pantheon Books, 1984. The theoretical framework of tribal sovereignty that Mankiller's self-governance strategy was built to operationalize.

### Cherokee Nation History and Governance

- Conley, Robert J. *The Cherokee Nation: A History*. University of New Mexico Press, 2005. The most complete modern history of the Nation, including the twentieth-century political developments that formed the context for Mankiller's leadership.
- McLoughlin, William G. *After the Trail of Tears: The Cherokees' Struggle for Sovereignty, 1839–1880*. University of North Carolina Press, 1993.
- Hendrix, Janey B. "Wilma Mankiller: Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation." *American Indian Quarterly*, vol. 12, no. 3, 1988. Written during her first elected term; an early scholarly account of her path to office and initial policy directions.
- Perdue, Theda. *Cherokee Women: Gender and Culture Change, 1700–1835*. University of Nebraska Press, 1998. Essential context for the tradition of Cherokee women's authority that predated and informed the cultural reception of Mankiller's leadership, and that made the resistance she faced a historical anomaly rather than a cultural continuity.
- Sturm, Circe. *Blood Politics: Race, Culture, and Identity in the Cherokee Nation of Oklahoma*. University of California Press, 2002. Analysis of Cherokee identity politics including the enrollment expansion that drove population growth during Mankiller's tenure.

### Community Development Theory

- Horton, Myles, and Paulo Freire. *We Make the Road by Walking: Conversations on Education and Social Change*. Temple University Press, 1990. The intellectual tradition — Highlander, participatory development — that Mankiller drew on alongside specifically Cherokee organizing traditions.
- Kretzmann, John P., and John L. McKnight. *Building Communities from the Inside Out: A Path Toward Finding and Mobilizing a Community's Assets*. ACTA Publications, 1993. The asset-based community development framework that describes, in theoretical terms, what Mankiller was practicing at Bell.
- Chambers, Robert. *Rural Development: Putting the Last First*. Longman, 1983. The international development literature's version of the argument for community-centered rather than service-delivery-centered development.
- Alinsky, Saul. *Rules for Radicals: A Practical Primer for Realistic Radicals*. Random House, 1971. The organizing tradition that formed one strand of Mankiller's Bay Area political education and that fed into the Bell model.

### Election Records and Political Biography

- Cherokee Nation Election records, 1983, 1987, 1991. Cherokee Nation Archives. The electoral data underlying the vote percentages cited in this module.
- Sonneborn, Liz. *Wilma Mankiller: Principal Chief of the Cherokees*. Chelsea House, 1994.
- Lazo, Caroline. *Wilma Mankiller*. Dillon Press, 1994.
- Howe, LeAnne. "Wilma Mankiller: A Legacy of Leadership." *American Indian Culture and Research Journal*, vol. 35, no. 3, 2011. Retrospective assessment of the full scope of her leadership.

### Language and Education

- Feeling, Durbin. *Cherokee-English Dictionary*. Cherokee Nation of Oklahoma, 1975. The foundational reference work for Cherokee language that the Institute for Cherokee Literacy drew on.
- Walker, Willard. "Notes on Native Writing Systems and the Design of Native Literacy Programs." *Anthropological Linguistics*, vol. 11, no. 5, 1969. Context for the syllabary's structure and the challenges of Cherokee literacy programs.
- Sequoyah High School records. Cherokee Nation Archives. Administrative and historical records related to the 1985 BIA transfer and the 1991–1992 revitalization.

### Indian Child Welfare

- Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978. Public Law 95-608. The statutory framework for tribal jurisdiction over child custody proceedings.
- Blanchard, Evelyn Lance, and Steven Unger, eds. *The Destruction of American Indian Families*. Association on American Indian Affairs, 1977. Pre-ICWA documentation of the child welfare crisis — the removal statistics — that motivated the Act and that Mankiller had worked to address before becoming chief.

### Archival

- The Wilma Mankiller Archive. Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian, Washington, D.C. Primary documents, correspondence, photographs, and administrative records from the Mankiller administration.
- Cherokee Nation Archives. Tahlequah, Oklahoma. Tribal records, council proceedings, compact negotiations, and administrative documents spanning the full decade of Mankiller's tenure.
- Oklahoma Historical Society. Records relevant to the tax-sharing compact negotiations with Governor David Walters.
