# Wilma Mankiller: A Decade of Leadership and the Architecture of Cherokee Sovereignty

## Chapter Three — What She Did and How She Did It

---

*"We've had daunting problems in many critical areas, but I believe in the old Cherokee injunction to 'be of a good mind.' Today it's called positive thinking."*
— Wilma Mankiller

---

The decade between 1985 and 1995 was the most consequential period in Cherokee Nation governance since the forced removal of the 1830s — not because of catastrophe, but because of construction. Wilma Mankiller, sworn in as Principal Chief on December 5, 1985, inherited a nation of roughly 68,000 enrolled citizens, a fragile governmental infrastructure, and deep skepticism from a Tribal Council that had little interest in being led by a woman. By the time she chose not to seek a third full term in 1995, the Cherokee Nation had grown to 170,000 citizens, generated approximately $25 million in annual revenues from its own enterprises, and secured $125 million in annual federal assistance for education, health, housing, and employment. These numbers are real. Understanding how they happened — the philosophy behind them, the specific programs that drove them, the resistance that had to be overcome — is the point of this chapter.

---

## Part One: The Road to December 5, 1985

### The Bell Project — Where It All Started

To understand Mankiller as Principal Chief, you have to understand Bell, Oklahoma. Bell is a small community in the rolling, heavily forested hill country of eastern Oklahoma — in the heart of the Cherokee homelands that survived the Trail of Tears. When Mankiller first began working with Bell's residents in the early 1980s, the community had no indoor plumbing. Families drew water from wells or hauled it. Children grew up without running water as a fact of life. The landscape was green and the land was rich, but the infrastructure — the bones of daily modern existence — was absent.

Mankiller had returned to Oklahoma in 1977 after years of activism in California, taken a job with the Cherokee Nation as a grant writer and economic stimulus coordinator, and worked her way up to directing the Community Development Department under Principal Chief Ross Swimmer. She was not yet Deputy Chief. She was a grant writer who believed in something specific: that communities could build their own transformations, and that the act of building was itself part of the transformation.

The Bell water project was the proof of concept. Working alongside Charlie Soap, a full-blood Cherokee who then worked for the Indian Housing Authority, Mankiller organized the residents of Bell to lay 16 miles of water pipe across rough terrain in 14 months. The Cherokee Nation provided equipment and technical expertise. The residents of Bell provided the labor — voluntarily, on their own time, working alongside each other on a project that would bring their community into the twentieth century. They also built and rehabilitated homes as part of the same coordinated effort.

The number matters: 16 miles of pipe. That is not a symbolic gesture. That is a serious construction project executed by a community that had been told, implicitly or explicitly, that it lacked the capacity to transform its own circumstances. The 14-month timeline matters too — not because it was fast, but because it was sustained. Sustained collective work requires trust, coordination, shared purpose, and leadership that shows up. Mankiller showed up. Soap showed up. And Bell's residents showed up, morning after morning, for over a year.

What made the Bell model work was its specific inversion of the normal assistance relationship. Federal and tribal program assistance typically moves in one direction: the government designs an intervention, hires professionals to execute it, and delivers the outcome to the community as a recipient. The community's role is to accept what is built on their behalf. Bell reversed this entirely. The community's labor was not supplementary — it was central. The residents of Bell were not beneficiaries of a government water program; they were the builders of their own water system. The distinction is not merely philosophical. It has practical consequences: a community that builds its own infrastructure understands how it works, is invested in maintaining it, and develops the organizational capacity and social trust that the act of building together generates. A community that receives infrastructure from outside does not necessarily develop any of these things.

This was the lesson Mankiller drew from Bell and applied systematically across subsequent projects. The approach demanded a kind of leadership that is harder and slower than the standard program delivery model. You have to go where the community is. You have to listen before you plan. You have to organize people who have been told, explicitly and implicitly, that they cannot do this — and you have to do it through accumulated trust rather than institutional authority. Mankiller and Soap did exactly this, walking through Bell's community meeting this reality: household by household, conversation by conversation, until the organizational momentum was sufficient to sustain fourteen months of collective labor.

The result was clean water in Bell. But the secondary result was more important: Bell became the template. The model proved that communities could do this. It was not charity delivered from above. It was self-help executed in community — what Mankiller would later describe as allowing citizens to identify their problems and gain control of the challenges they faced. The Kenwood Project, a subsequent community development initiative, received the Department of Housing and Urban Development's Certificate of National Merit for applying the same principles. Bell's success rippled outward, inspiring similar self-help projects across the Cherokee Nation and across Indian Country more broadly — a reverberating proof of concept that the community development philosophy could scale.

The 2013 documentary-drama *The Cherokee Word for Water*, co-directed by Charlie Soap himself, brought this story to a wider audience. The title references the Cherokee language — *amanyi*, water, carrying weight in a language whose syllabary was created by a man who never learned to read any other script. The word and the project sit together as twin expressions of what Mankiller was building: not just infrastructure, but the renewed conviction that Cherokee people, in their own language and on their own terms, could solve what needed solving.

The Bell project did something else: it introduced Wilma Mankiller and Charlie Soap to each other in the fullest sense. They would marry in October 1986.

### The 1983 Election — Deputy Chief with Ross Swimmer

The political partnership between Wilma Mankiller and Ross Swimmer is one of the more interesting configurations in Native American political history. Swimmer was a Republican. Mankiller was a Democrat. Swimmer believed in tribal economic development through business enterprise. Mankiller believed in rural community improvement through self-help programs. They agreed on tribal sovereignty and on the goal of building Cherokee Nation capacity. On most policy specifics, they were pulling in different directions.

Yet in 1983, Swimmer asked Mankiller to run as his Deputy Chief. The invitation was recognition of her real work — the Community Development Department she led had secured millions in grant funding and proven a model that worked. Swimmer understood what he was getting: a capable, credible running mate with a constituency in the rural Cherokee communities that his business-focused platform didn't naturally reach.

What Mankiller did not fully anticipate was the ferocity of the opposition she would face as a woman seeking elected office within the Cherokee Nation. Cherokee society had historically been organized matrilineally — clan membership, property, and children passed through the mother's line. Women had traditionally held significant authority through clan systems and women's councils. But centuries of interaction with European-American governance models, the disruption of the Trail of Tears, and the long period of federal oversight had reshuffled the deck. By 1983, the idea of a woman holding elected leadership was, to a significant portion of the Cherokee electorate, simply wrong.

The opposition was not polite. Death threats arrived. Her tires were slashed. A billboard bearing her image was burned. These were not the actions of people who disagreed about policy — they were the actions of people trying to communicate that she had no legitimate place in the contest.

She ran anyway. The ticket won. The final victory came through absentee ballot counts in a runoff against Agnes Cowen. Mankiller became the first elected woman to serve as Deputy Chief of the Cherokee Nation — a historic first that arrived in the same package as vandalism and threats.

As Deputy Chief, she quickly understood that the Tribal Council had little interest in accommodating her. Some members viewed her as a political adversary; others simply didn't take her seriously as a result of her gender. She made a strategic decision early: she would not fight the council head-on. She would avoid entangling herself in legislative battles she could not yet win, and instead focus her energy on the executive and administrative domains where she could act without council permission. It was a patience calculation — preserve the relationship, demonstrate competence, build a record.

The historical irony here deserves notice — and Mankiller named it explicitly in her autobiography and in interviews throughout her career. Cherokee society before sustained European contact was organized on matrilineal principles. Women held significant authority through clan structures; the women's council was a genuine political institution. The Trail of Tears and the subsequent century of federal pressure toward assimilation had eroded these structures. The Cherokee Nation that Mankiller entered in the late 1970s carried both the memory of matrilineal authority — alive in the culture, present in the language — and the layered weight of a governance tradition that had absorbed enough of the surrounding patriarchal model to make a woman's leadership genuinely controversial. She was not simply fighting an external prejudice imported from the dominant culture; she was navigating a complex internal tension between older Cherokee tradition and more recently acquired norms. The resistance came from within the nation and was real. So was the tradition that supported her. Charlie Soap, speaking Cherokee to traditionalist men about the historical place of women in Cherokee governance, was activating that older memory — arguing not from modernity but from roots.

### December 5, 1985 — The Succession

When Ross Swimmer accepted the position of Assistant Secretary of Indian Affairs under President Ronald Reagan in 1985, the succession was automatic: the Deputy Chief becomes Principal Chief. On December 5, 1985, Wilma Mankiller was sworn in as Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation. She was the first woman to lead a major Native American tribal nation.

The media attention was immediate and significant. Mankiller's ascension was a story with obvious historical resonance, and national press coverage elevated her profile in ways that strengthened her position within the nation rather than undermining it. She received the American Indian Woman of the Year designation within five months. She was inducted into the Oklahoma Women's Hall of Fame. External recognition translated into internal credibility with at least part of the Cherokee constituency.

But the Tribal Council had not changed. Mankiller maintained the same strategic caution she had practiced as Deputy Chief: keep the executive-legislative friction low, demonstrate what competent administration looks like, educate voters directly through the press about what the administration was trying to accomplish. She focused on housing and health services — areas where she could show tangible results to rural Cherokee families who lived with inadequate infrastructure.

Charlie Soap, her collaborator from the Bell project, had become part of her life in a different way. They married in October 1986. The marriage immediately generated political complications: opponents demanded that Soap resign from his position with the Indian Housing Authority, arguing that a conflict of interest existed. He submitted his resignation, effective January 1987. The timing drew criticism — some said the delay allowed him to qualify for retirement benefits. The controversy was real, but it was also a preview of the sustained effort to find any available lever to delegitimize Mankiller's leadership.

## Part Two: The 1987 Election — Winning in Her Own Right

Her opponents in 1987 made her health the centerpiece of their campaign against her. Mankiller had survived a near-fatal head-on collision in 1979 that required seventeen surgeries and left her with lasting physical effects. She had subsequently developed myasthenia gravis, a neuromuscular disorder. By 1987, she was also managing kidney disease. Her opponents argued that her medical history made her unfit to serve as Principal Chief.

It was a transparent argument — fitness to serve is a legitimate question, but the weaponization of a woman's health problems to end her political career is a different enterprise. Mankiller responded by running anyway, and by using her press access to educate voters rather than to fight her opponents.

The first-round results in 1987 gave Mankiller 45 percent of the vote — a plurality, but not a majority. A runoff against Perry Wheeler was required. She won the runoff. For the first time, Wilma Mankiller was Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation not by succession but by election.

Charlie Soap's role in the 1987 campaign was substantive and specific. As a full-blood Cherokee speaker, he could speak directly to traditionalist constituencies in the language — not just communicating words, but communicating cultural legitimacy. He talked to Cherokee men about the traditional place of women in Cherokee society, about the matrilineal structures and the women's councils, about the historical precedent for women in authority. He defused the gender issue not by arguing that it didn't exist, but by rooting Mankiller's leadership in Cherokee tradition rather than positioning it as a break from that tradition. It worked. The combination of Mankiller's administrative record and Soap's cultural advocacy carried the runoff.

## Part Three: The Policy Record — What She Actually Built

The decade of Mankiller's leadership produced a specific, documentable set of policy achievements. This section covers them in detail.

### Health Care Infrastructure

One of Mankiller's clearest priorities was building health infrastructure in rural Cherokee communities. The Cherokee Nation under her administration opened three rural health centers in areas that previously had no clinic access within reasonable distance. For a nation whose territory spans fourteen counties in northeastern Oklahoma — an area larger than some eastern states — rural health access had been a chronic failure.

The mobile eye-care clinic program addressed a specific gap: many rural Cherokee residents, particularly the elderly and the poor, had no practical access to vision care. Fixed clinics in population centers didn't serve people who couldn't travel to them. A mobile clinic that came to the community eliminated that barrier. The same logic applied to the ambulance service expansion — in a region of rural roads and isolated homesteads, emergency response time is a matter of life and death. Mankiller understood that health policy without the infrastructure to deliver services is just paper.

During her 1990 kidney disease crisis, Mankiller received a transplant from her brother Don and returned to her duties within weeks. The experience reinforced rather than diminished her commitment to expanded health services. Her personal experience with the health care system — as a Cherokee woman who had navigated it repeatedly through crisis — gave her advocacy a grounded specificity that abstractions about policy cannot provide.

Tribal data from the Mankiller years shows infant mortality declining and general health outcomes improving within the Cherokee Nation, though these metrics reflect the cumulative investment across multiple programs rather than any single initiative.

### Head Start — Early Childhood as Foundation

The Head Start program expansion under Mankiller represented an understanding that community development begins at the beginning. Head Start — the federal early childhood education program providing comprehensive services to low-income children — had long operated in tribal communities, with grants awarded directly to tribes. Mankiller's administration expanded the program's reach within Cherokee territory, recognizing early childhood education as foundational infrastructure in the same way that running water is foundational infrastructure.

The investment in Head Start was part of a broader philosophy: you cannot have sustainable economic development in a community where children are not prepared to learn, where families are not stable, where the baseline conditions for education don't exist. Head Start addressed child cognitive development, nutrition, dental care, and parent engagement simultaneously. Expanding it was not a soft choice — it was a strategic one.

### The Institute for Cherokee Literacy

Language is sovereignty. This is not a metaphor. When the Cherokee syllabary was created by Sequoyah in the early 1820s — one of the few times in recorded history that a person created an entire writing system — the Cherokee people achieved a written literacy rate that within years exceeded English literacy rates among white Americans. The Cherokee Phoenix, the first Native American newspaper, published in both Cherokee and English beginning in 1828. The syllabary was not only a linguistic tool; it was a declaration of intellectual independence.

By the twentieth century, Cherokee language use had contracted severely under the pressures of forced assimilation, boarding school policies that punished children for speaking indigenous languages, and the steady erosion of community transmission. Mankiller understood that preserving the language was not a cultural luxury — it was an act of political survival. A people who cannot speak their own language are a people who have conceded something fundamental about who they are.

The Institute for Cherokee Literacy, which Mankiller helped establish and expand, was the institutional response: a dedicated center for language preservation, teaching, and documentation. The goal was to maintain the capacity for Cherokee literacy — reading and writing in the syllabary — and to transmit that capacity to future generations. In the longer arc, the Institute was part of the same project as the rural health centers and the water lines: building the institutional infrastructure of a self-determining nation.

### Sequoyah High School

The history of Sequoyah High School tracks the history of federal-tribal relations with uncomfortable fidelity. Founded in 1871 as the Cherokee Orphan Asylum to support children left destitute by the Civil War, the institution evolved and was renamed in honor of the syllabary's creator, eventually becoming a boarding school operated by the Bureau of Indian Affairs. In 1985 — the same year Mankiller became Principal Chief — the BIA transferred operational control of the school to the Cherokee Nation.

The revival of Sequoyah High School, which Mankiller's administration completed during the 1991-1992 school year, was the fulfillment of the institutional transfer's promise. A school that had been a federal facility became a tribal institution: operated by the Cherokee Nation, oriented toward Cherokee culture and identity, designed to serve Cherokee students from across the territory and beyond. The curriculum requires more credits for graduation than Oklahoma's standard — including courses in Cherokee history, language, and cultural identity. The school is now home to students from multiple tribal nations, but it is explicitly a Cherokee-governed institution, answering to Cherokee leadership rather than federal administrators.

The significance of reclaiming Sequoyah was symbolic and practical simultaneously. For a people whose children had been taken to government boarding schools and punished for being Cherokee, operating their own high school with a cultural curriculum was a reclamation that no grant or revenue figure can fully capture.

### Restructuring Tribal Council Elections

The change from at-large to district-based Tribal Council elections was one of Mankiller's most consequential governance reforms, and one of the least visible to observers outside the nation. In the at-large system, council candidates ran territory-wide. In a nation where the enrolled population was concentrated in urban centers and larger towns, this meant that rural Cherokee communities — often the most traditional, the most linguistically intact, the most economically marginal — were systematically underrepresented. Urban population mass determined outcomes.

Mankiller persuaded the council to restructure the election system so that council members came from newly created geographic districts, ensuring that each region of Cherokee territory had direct representation. This was redistricting in service of rural equity. It was also a structural change that would outlast her administration — the district system would continue providing rural communities a political voice regardless of who held the principal chief's seat.

The reform required Mankiller to do something her early strategy had avoided: engage the council on a legislative matter. The fact that she succeeded in persuading the council to restructure its own elections suggests that her patient approach — demonstrating administrative competence, building credibility, avoiding unnecessary confrontations — had generated enough goodwill, or at least enough respect, to make this kind of persuasion possible.

### Tax-Sharing Compact with Oklahoma

The Cherokee Nation, as a sovereign tribal government, operates on lands within the state of Oklahoma — a state that also taxes economic activity. The relationship between tribal sovereignty and state taxation is a chronic source of tension in federal Indian law. Tribes argue that their sovereign status exempts them from state taxation on tribal lands. States argue the opposite. The result is typically litigation, regulatory friction, and economic uncertainty.

Mankiller negotiated a different approach: a tax-sharing compact with Oklahoma Governor David Walters. Under the compact, the Cherokee Nation could collect state taxes from businesses operating on tribal lands and retain a portion of those revenues. This was a pragmatic resolution to a structural conflict — the state got revenue compliance without having to fight for it, and the Cherokee Nation captured a revenue stream that could fund tribal government operations and services.

The compact was significant beyond the immediate revenue. It established the Cherokee Nation as a negotiating partner with the state government rather than an adversary in litigation. It created a precedent for collaborative rather than adversarial resolution of sovereignty questions. And it modeled the kind of practical diplomacy that Mankiller consistently preferred to constitutional confrontation.

### Self-Governance Agreements with the Federal Government

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 had established a framework for tribal governments to contract with federal agencies to administer programs that would otherwise be run by the Bureau of Indian Affairs. The "638 contracts" — named after the law's public law number — allowed tribes to employ their own people to run their own programs, with federal funding following the work.

Mankiller pushed further. Under her administration, the Cherokee Nation participated in self-governance pilot programs that went beyond individual program contracts to encompass block grant arrangements: lump-sum federal allocations that the tribe could allocate according to its own priorities and needs, without the layer-by-layer oversight that individual program contracts required. This was a fundamental shift in the relationship between the Cherokee Nation and the federal government.

In the contract-by-contract model, the BIA retained substantial oversight authority over how each program was administered. In the block grant model, the Cherokee Nation received the funding and made its own decisions about deployment. The assumption in the block grant model is that the tribal government knows its own community's needs better than federal administrators in Washington. Mankiller believed this with conviction — it was the same conviction that animated the Bell water project, the same belief that communities are the best judges of what they need and the best agents of their own development.

The self-governance agreements secured under Mankiller's leadership reduced BIA oversight of Cherokee programs and transferred administrative authority to tribal hands. This was self-determination as an institutional practice, not just a political aspiration. When Mankiller left office, the Cherokee Nation was managing its own programs with its own staff using federal block grant funding — a fundamentally different relationship with the federal government than had existed a decade earlier.

### Private Industry Council

Economic development requires more than tribal enterprise. It requires the infrastructure of the labor market: job training, employer relationships, financial and technical assistance for small business development. Mankiller founded the Private Industry Council as a mechanism to connect Cherokee tribal government with private sector employers, creating coordinated job training programs that produced workers with skills that matched real market demand.

The council established employment training opportunities, provided financial and technical assistance to tribal members who wanted to start their own enterprises, and served as a bridge between the tribal government's development priorities and the private economy operating in and around Cherokee territory. The approach reflected Mankiller's understanding that tribal sovereignty exists in an economic context that neither begins nor ends at tribal boundaries, and that building Cherokee prosperity required engaging that larger context strategically rather than ignoring it or resisting it.

Under the economic development framework Mankiller built, the Cherokee Nation established a tribal electronic harness and cabling manufacturing company, constructed a hydroelectric plant, and developed horticultural operations. Factories, retail stores, restaurants, and bingo operations added to the revenue base. The $25 million in annual tribal revenues that characterized the end of her tenure was the product of this diversified economic strategy — not a single operation, but a portfolio of enterprises built on the foundation of tribal capacity.

### Indian Child Welfare Act

Mankiller's advocacy for the Indian Child Welfare Act predated her principal chiefship but reflected the same convictions that animated her governance. During her years in California, she had worked alongside attorneys and advocacy staff on legislation to prevent Native children from being removed from their cultural and family contexts and placed with non-Native families. The surveys that motivated ICWA's passage in 1978 were stark: in states with significant Native populations, 25 to 35 percent of Native children were being removed from their families, at a per capita rate in some areas nearly sixteen times higher than for non-Native children.

The Indian Child Welfare Act, signed by President Jimmy Carter in November 1978, established federal standards for child custody proceedings involving Native children, created presumptions favoring placement with extended family and tribal communities, and gave tribal courts jurisdiction over child welfare cases involving their members. It was the legal expression of a principle Mankiller had worked toward in community: that cultural continuity and family integrity are foundational to Native sovereignty, and that the systematic removal of children from their communities is a form of cultural destruction with the same effect, if not the intent, as more visible forms of elimination.

As Principal Chief, Mankiller supported ICWA's implementation and enforcement as an ongoing priority — part of the larger project of maintaining Cherokee cultural integrity alongside the institutional development that was her administration's most visible work.

## Part Four: The Numbers and What They Mean

The transformation of the Cherokee Nation during Mankiller's decade can be traced through a set of quantitative markers, each of which tells a story that the numbers alone cannot fully contain.

**Population: 68,000 to 170,000.** This growth reflects multiple forces. Tribal enrollment expanded as the Cherokee Nation extended services that made enrollment meaningful, attracting citizens who had been enrolled but inactive to re-engage. Descendant populations who had previously had no reason to formally connect with the nation found reasons to do so as the nation built real infrastructure and genuine capacity. The population growth was not demographic in the traditional sense — it was a measure of reconnection, of people choosing to identify formally with a nation that was demonstrating it could serve them. A nation that can offer health clinics, education, job training, and cultural programs is a nation that people want to belong to.

The enrollment growth also reflected a broader phenomenon in American Indian identity: the post-1970s recovery of Native identity in urban populations who had been relocated from tribal lands by federal policy in the 1950s and 1960s. The Indian Relocation Act of 1956 — the same policy that displaced Mankiller's family from Oklahoma to San Francisco — had moved tens of thousands of Native Americans into cities, ostensibly for economic opportunity, effectively for assimilation. Many of those relocated families lost their active connection to their tribal governments over a generation. As tribal nations began rebuilding capacity in the 1970s and 1980s, some of those urban descendants began reconnecting. The Cherokee Nation under Mankiller offered them something concrete to connect to.

**Annual revenues: $25 million.** This figure reflects the diversified enterprise portfolio — manufacturing, retail, hospitality, gaming — that Mankiller's economic development strategy produced. In 1985, the Cherokee Nation had limited enterprise revenue. The $25 million reached by 1995 represented not just income but institutional proof of concept: tribal sovereignty can be economically viable without dependence on federal programs alone.

The path from minimal enterprise revenue to $25 million in a decade required accepting that economic sovereignty and cultural sovereignty are linked but distinct. The Cherokee Nation built a tribal electronic harness and cabling manufacturing company — unglamorous, industrial, and real. It built a hydroelectric plant, generating electricity and demonstrating that the nation's natural resources could serve its economy. It ran horticultural operations, retail stores, restaurants, and bingo operations. None of these individually was transformative. Together, as a portfolio, they represented a Cherokee economy that could generate its own revenue — less subject to the congressional appropriations cycle that had historically made tribal governments dependent on political winds in Washington.

**Federal assistance: $125 million.** This number requires careful interpretation. The growth in federal assistance was not a sign of increased dependency — it was a sign of increased Cherokee Nation capacity to compete for and administer federal programs. Self-governance agreements that transferred program administration from BIA to tribal control brought the funding associated with those programs along with the responsibility. The Cherokee Nation was receiving more federal dollars because it was doing more work — running its own health programs, its own education programs, its own housing programs — rather than having those programs run by federal administrators on the tribe's behalf. The $125 million was the price tag of self-determination made operational.

The distinction matters in federal budget terms as well. When the BIA administered a program for a tribe, the tribe received a service. When the tribe administered the program itself under a self-governance compact, the tribe received both the service delivery capacity and the funding. The money followed the authority. Mankiller's insistence on self-governance agreements was therefore not just a matter of political principle — it was a fiscal strategy. More authority meant more resources to deploy according to Cherokee priorities rather than federal program specifications.

Together, these numbers describe a nation that tripled in active membership, grew its own revenue base significantly, and substantially increased its capacity to deliver services to its citizens — all within a decade. The management philosophy behind these outcomes deserves its own examination.

## Part Five: The Management Philosophy

Mankiller did not write a management textbook. But her approach to leadership was coherent, consistent, and replicable — which is why it is worth examining carefully.

**Community-first, not government-first.** The Bell water project established the template: the community identifies the problem, the community does the work, the government provides the technical support and resources. This is the opposite of the bureaucratic model, in which the government identifies the problem, designs a program, hires contractors, and delivers a service to passive recipients. The community-first model assumes that people are the agents of their own development, not the objects of government programs. It assumes that local knowledge is real and valuable. It assumes that the act of working together on a shared problem builds the social capital and collective confidence that make subsequent problems tractable.

Mankiller walked this principle out literally. She walked door-to-door in rural Cherokee communities, listening. She did not hold office hours in Tahlequah and wait for constituents to bring their problems to her. She went where the people were, in the terrain they inhabited, and heard what they had to say. This is not a management technique — it is a disposition toward the people you serve, a refusal to allow the distance that institutional authority creates to separate the leader from the led.

**Patience as strategy.** Mankiller's early approach to the Tribal Council — avoiding legislative confrontations, focusing on executive domains, building a record of competence — was not timidity. It was a strategic calculation that her ability to accomplish anything depended on maintaining a working relationship with the council over time, even if that relationship was cold. She was right. The council that she couldn't persuade on most questions in 1986 was the council she persuaded to restructure its own elections by the late 1980s. The patient accumulation of credibility and demonstrated performance was what made that possible.

**Identity and self-determination as inseparable.** For Mankiller, the Institute for Cherokee Literacy and the rural health centers were expressions of the same underlying principle: a self-determining people must have the capacity to sustain themselves physically and culturally. Health without culture is management. Culture without health is precarious. Self-determination requires both, simultaneously. This is why her administration built infrastructure for bodies and infrastructure for language at the same time — not because she had unlimited resources, but because she understood that you cannot build a sovereign nation out of only one of these components.

**Negotiation over confrontation.** The tax-sharing compact with Oklahoma, the self-governance agreements with federal agencies, the restructuring of council elections — none of these were achieved through direct confrontation or legal challenge. They were achieved through negotiation, persuasion, and the construction of agreements that the other parties had reasons to accept. Mankiller consistently preferred to find the arrangement that served Cherokee interests without requiring the other party to lose — not because she was conflict-averse, but because she understood that negotiated agreements outlast confrontational victories. When both parties have interests in an arrangement, both parties maintain it.

**Using opposition as data.** The sustained resistance Mankiller faced — from opponents who questioned her health, from council members who discounted her because of her gender, from Cherokee traditionalists who were skeptical of female leadership — she treated as information about the constituencies she needed to reach, not as obstacles to be defeated. Charlie Soap's campaign work in 1987 was the most visible example: instead of fighting the traditionalist constituency that opposed female leadership, she sent her husband to speak to them in Cherokee about the traditional place of women in Cherokee culture. The opposition was real. The response was to engage it on its own terms rather than dismissing it.

**The personal is institutional.** Mankiller's own medical crises — the car accident, the myasthenia gravis, the kidney disease, the transplant — are sometimes treated as background to her political career, as obstacles she overcame to do her real work. But her repeated confrontations with health system failures and the gaps in medical infrastructure available to Cherokee citizens in rural Oklahoma directly shaped her policy priorities. When she built rural health clinics, she was not implementing an abstract policy preference. She was building what she knew from experience was missing. The personal and the institutional were not separate domains for her — they informed each other continuously.

## Part Six: The Opposition — What She Faced and How She Overcame It

The resistance Mankiller faced as a woman seeking and holding power within the Cherokee Nation was real, sustained, and took multiple forms. Understanding what she overcame matters — not to frame her as a victim, which she would have rejected entirely, but to understand the conditions under which she accomplished what she accomplished.

The death threats during the 1983 campaign were the most extreme manifestation. Slashed tires. A burned billboard. These were messages intended to communicate that her candidacy was not acceptable and that the people sending the messages were willing to act on that view. She absorbed these messages and continued running.

The Tribal Council's early hostility was a different form of opposition — institutional rather than violent, but structurally significant. A principal chief who cannot work with the legislative body governs on paper only. Mankiller's strategic decision to avoid unnecessary confrontations was a direct response to this opposition — not capitulation, but adaptation.

The opposition during the 1987 campaign — focused on her health — attempted to use legitimate information (she did have serious health conditions) to make an illegitimate argument (that she was therefore unfit to serve). The argument was illegitimate not because her health was irrelevant but because the same scrutiny was not applied to male candidates with comparable health histories, and because the specific medical conditions being weaponized were ones she had already managed while doing the job effectively.

Mankiller addressed the sexism within the nation directly in her autobiography and in interviews. Her most quoted observation on the topic: "I've run into more discrimination as a woman than as an Indian." This is a statement worth sitting with. For a Cherokee woman who had experienced the full history of federal Indian policy — the relocation, the BIA's paternalism, the boarding schools that had preceded her generation — to say that gender discrimination was more present in her daily life than racial or ethnic discrimination is a significant claim about where power operates and how.

Her strategy for overcoming gender-based opposition combined several elements. Charlie Soap's role was specific and effective: speaking directly to traditionalist men in Cherokee, grounding Mankiller's leadership in cultural precedent rather than asking skeptics to accept a novel concept. Her public emphasis on results — the health clinics, the water systems, the employment programs — provided a record that was harder to dismiss than abstract arguments about leadership capacity. Her re-election in 1991 with 83 percent of the vote was not just a political victory; it was the electorate's definitive statement that her performance as Principal Chief had answered the question of her fitness to lead.

The 83 percent figure from 1991 is the clearest evidence of what she had built: not just programs and institutions, but trust. In a democratic system, 83 percent of the vote in a re-election is the public's most direct available expression of confidence in leadership. The Cherokee Nation gave her that number after watching her govern for six years. Whatever had been said about her fitness, her gender, or her health, the voters had a different assessment.

## Part Seven: Charlie Soap — The Partner

Any serious account of Wilma Mankiller's leadership must include Charlie Soap as more than a biographical footnote. Their partnership preceded their marriage and shaped both the Bell project and the entire community development philosophy that defined her administration.

Soap was a full-blood Cherokee, fluent in the language, deeply embedded in traditional Cherokee culture. His background in the Indian Housing Authority had given him experience with community-based development work — the practical knowledge of how to organize labor, coordinate resources, and sustain collective effort over the months that a project like Bell requires. When Mankiller was directing the Community Development Department and building the Bell water system, Soap was on the ground with her, doing the work.

Their marriage in October 1986 merged the personal and professional in a way that both had been building toward for years. Soap continued to be Mankiller's most effective partner in reaching constituencies that were skeptical of her leadership. His cultural credibility with traditional Cherokee men — who were most likely to resist the concept of a woman as principal chief — was something Mankiller herself could not replicate. He didn't argue that she deserved their support. He spoke to them about Cherokee tradition and the historical role of women in Cherokee governance, in their shared language, and let the tradition make the argument.

The calls for Soap's resignation from the Indian Housing Authority after their marriage were a politically motivated attempt to create a conflict of interest narrative. He resigned effective January 1987. The timing controversy — whether the delay allowed him to qualify for retirement benefits — generated noise without generating substance. Soap remained Mankiller's partner in the project of Cherokee Nation development, regardless of his formal employment status.

Their collaboration was the model in miniature of what Mankiller was trying to build at scale: two people with complementary skills and shared convictions, working toward the same goals, distributing the work according to who could do each piece most effectively.

## Part Eight: The 1991 Re-election — The Verdict

The 1991 election result — 83 percent — was the Cherokee electorate's definitive verdict on Mankiller's first elected term. It was, by any measure, the largest margin in Cherokee Nation election history. In an era when American politics was trending toward close, contested, divisive elections, Mankiller won nearly five of every six votes cast.

The 83 percent tells a story about what six years of competent, community-grounded, results-focused governance can produce in terms of public confidence. The health clinics were built. The water systems were extended. The Head Start programs were running. The Private Industry Council was operating. The self-governance agreements were in place. The Tribal Council elections had been restructured. Sequoyah High School had been revived. The tax compact had been negotiated. The numbers — population, revenue, federal assistance — had all moved dramatically in the right direction.

But 83 percent is not just a performance evaluation. It is a statement about identity. The Cherokee Nation in 1991 was telling itself who it was through the act of voting. It was saying that the community-first model of development was Cherokee. That health infrastructure in rural communities was Cherokee. That language preservation was Cherokee. That a woman could lead. That self-determination was not just a political aspiration but an operational reality. The vote was the electorate's self-portrait.

Mankiller's second term extended the work of her first: the revival of Sequoyah High School, the mentorship partnership with the American Association of University Women, the documentation of fraudulent organizations claiming Cherokee heritage, the enrollment policies designed to maintain the integrity of tribal citizenship. The second term also produced her autobiography, *Mankiller: A Chief and Her People* (1993), co-written with Michael Wallis, which became a national bestseller and brought her story — and the Cherokee Nation's story — to an audience far beyond Indian Country.

In 1994, President Clinton invited Mankiller to moderate the Nation-to-Nation Summit, an assembly of all 545 federally recognized tribes in Washington. The summit produced the establishment of the Office of Indian Justice. Mankiller was not just leading the Cherokee Nation; she had become a figure of national significance within the broader framework of federal Indian policy.

## Part Nine: The Architecture of Self-Determination

Looking back across the decade, what Mankiller built was not simply a collection of programs. She built the institutional architecture of a self-determining nation.

Self-determination is a political term that can mean many things. In Mankiller's administration, it meant something specific: the Cherokee Nation having the capacity — financial, administrative, technical, cultural — to govern itself, provide for its citizens, and manage its relationship with federal and state governments as a sovereign rather than as a dependent. Self-determination without capacity is aspiration. Capacity without self-determination is management. Mankiller built both simultaneously.

The federal self-governance agreements transferred administrative authority to the tribe. The tax-sharing compact established a negotiating relationship with the state. The rural health centers built health delivery capacity inside the nation. The Institute for Cherokee Literacy maintained the cultural capacity that self-determination requires if it is to mean more than administrative autonomy. The Private Industry Council built economic capacity. The district-based council elections built democratic capacity — ensuring that the governance structure represented the full geography of the nation, not just its population centers.

Each of these pieces was necessary. None was sufficient alone. A nation with administrative capacity but no cultural foundation is not self-determining; it is self-administering, which is different. A nation with cultural vitality but no health infrastructure loses its elders and its knowledge-holders to preventable illness. A nation with health care and cultural preservation but no economic base remains permanently dependent on federal appropriations, which can be cut.

Mankiller understood these interdependencies. The decade of her leadership was not a series of disconnected policy achievements — it was a systematic effort to build the interlocking capacities that self-determination requires. The Bell water project, in retrospect, was not just about water. It was the proof that the method worked. Everything that followed was an application of the same insight: communities, given the right support and the right leadership, can build what they need. The role of the tribal government was to provide that support and that leadership, and then to get out of the way.

In her own words: "I want to be remembered as the person who helped us restore faith in ourselves."

Faith in themselves. Not faith in government programs, or in the tribal administration, or in federal assistance — though all of these were tools she used. Faith in themselves. The conviction that the Cherokee people, organized and supported and led with honesty, could solve their own problems and build their own future.

That is what the Bell water project demonstrated. That is what the 83 percent vindicated. That is what the decade proved.

What Mankiller built was durable precisely because it was rooted in this conviction rather than in any particular program or administration. Programs can be defunded. Administrations change. But a community that has learned to build its own water system carries that knowledge forward. A nation that has negotiated its own self-governance compacts understands from experience that the negotiation is possible. A people who have seen their own language preserved in a dedicated institution know that preservation is not an accident — it is work that can be organized and sustained. The institutional architecture Mankiller constructed was designed to outlast her, and it did. The Cherokee Nation she handed to her successor in 1995 was not the same nation she had inherited in 1985. It was larger, wealthier, more self-governing, more confident, and more firmly grounded in both its cultural identity and its capacity for self-determination. The decade proved something that had been in doubt: that a tribal nation, well-led, could build toward sovereignty that was operational rather than merely aspirational. Mankiller did not just lead the Cherokee Nation. She demonstrated what Indigenous leadership, at its best, could accomplish.
