## 1. The Category Error That Distorts Everything

There is a mistake built into how most Americans think about Native American policy, and it corrupts every subsequent step in the analysis. The mistake is categorical. It is the assumption that the relationship between the United States government and tribal nations is a relationship between a majority society and a minority group — that the question of Native American rights is a question about civil rights, about equal access, about inclusion in the institutions that the dominant culture built.

This is wrong. And understanding why it is wrong is the beginning of understanding what Wilma Mankiller spent her career fighting for.

The Cherokee Nation is not a minority group. It is a government. It is a sovereign political entity that predates the United States Constitution by centuries, that entered into treaties with the United States as one government enters into treaties with another, and that retains — in law, in history, and in lived practice — the inherent right to govern itself. The question that frames Cherokee politics is not "how do we get included?" It is "how do we govern ourselves?" Those are different questions. They have different answers. They require different frameworks.

When Wilma Mankiller stood before the United States Congress and testified about the needs of the Cherokee Nation, she was not a minority-group representative seeking better treatment from a majority government. She was the elected head of a sovereign nation, participating in a government-to-government relationship that had been recognized in law since the founding era of the American republic. The distinction is not symbolic. It is the difference between receiving services and negotiating compacts. It is the difference between being administered and self-governing. It is the difference between a ward and a nation.

Everything else in this module follows from that distinction.

## 2. What Tribal Sovereignty Actually Means

Sovereignty, in the political science sense, is the authority to govern — to make and enforce laws, to manage territory, to establish relationships with other political entities. Sovereignty is not granted from above; it inheres in the existence of a polity, a people organized as a political community with the capacity and will to govern themselves.

The Cherokee Nation's sovereignty derives from its origins as a self-governing people — the Ani-Yunwiya, the Principal People — whose political institutions predate any contact with European civilization. When the United States entered into treaties with the Cherokee Nation beginning in 1785 with the Treaty of Hopewell, it was not creating Cherokee sovereignty. It was recognizing and engaging with sovereignty that already existed. The treaties are not documents through which the United States gave the Cherokee Nation its political status. They are documents through which two sovereign entities negotiated the terms of their relationship.

This is a foundational point that Mankiller returned to throughout her career, in every congressional testimony, every public speech, every interview: sovereignty is not something Washington can grant, because sovereignty is not Washington's to grant. The Cherokee Nation's right to govern itself flows from the Cherokee people's existence as a people, not from any decision made in Washington.

In practical terms, tribal sovereignty today means several things simultaneously. It means the right to establish and operate a tribal government — including legislative, executive, and judicial branches. It means the right to determine tribal citizenship, to decide who is and is not a citizen of the Cherokee Nation according to criteria the Nation establishes. It means the right to tax within the Nation's jurisdiction. It means the right to operate courts and enforce tribal law. It means the right to negotiate directly with the federal government and with state governments as a co-equal sovereign entity. It means treaty rights — the specific, legally binding commitments the United States made in exchange for Cherokee land cessions — that the federal government is constitutionally obligated to honor.

What tribal sovereignty does not mean — and this is where the federal government has, over two centuries, done the most damage — is absolute or uncontested authority. The relationship between tribal sovereignty and American federalism is, in Chief Justice John Marshall's phrase, "domestic dependency." Tribal nations are sovereign, but they exist within the territory of the United States and within a legal framework that the United States has, for most of its history, manipulated to its own advantage. The story of federal Indian policy is, in large part, the story of the United States alternately recognizing and contracting tribal sovereignty as its own interests shifted. The history of the Cherokee Nation's political survival is the story of a people holding onto the principle of their own sovereignty through every contraction, every betrayal, and every attempted erasure.

The government-to-government relationship is the legal expression of this principle. When the federal government deals with the Cherokee Nation, it is not administering a program for a minority population. It is conducting foreign — or at least inter-sovereign — relations with a nation that has its own government, its own laws, and its own claim to political existence. Federal Indian policy at its best acknowledges this. At its worst — and there have been long periods of worst — it treats tribal nations as administrative units to be managed, as problems to be solved, as populations to be assimilated or eliminated.

Mankiller understood this clearly and said it repeatedly: the language of Indian affairs, when it slips from "government-to-government" into "federal trust responsibility for dependent populations," signals a conceptual regression that has policy consequences. Every time the BIA made a unilateral decision about Cherokee resources, it was not merely being inefficient. It was denying, in practice, a sovereignty that existed in law.

## 3. The Legal Architecture: From Chief Justice Marshall to the Modern Era

The legal history of tribal sovereignty in the United States is built on a paradox: the foundational Supreme Court decisions that define the current framework were written by the same Chief Justice whose court simultaneously enabled the legal mechanisms through which the Cherokee were removed from their homeland. John Marshall's trilogy of Indian law cases — Johnson v. M'Intosh (1823), Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831), and Worcester v. Georgia (1832) — remain the bedrock of federal Indian law nearly two centuries after they were decided. They are, simultaneously, a recognition of tribal sovereignty and a qualification of it that set the terms for every subsequent contraction.

**Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831)** is the case that most directly confronted the question of what the Cherokee Nation was, politically. Georgia had extended its laws over Cherokee territory — effectively asserting that the Cherokee were subject to Georgia's jurisdiction rather than their own — and the Cherokee Nation sued the state directly in the Supreme Court. The threshold question was whether the Cherokee Nation had standing to sue as a foreign state.

Marshall's answer was no, but the reasoning was specific and consequential. The Cherokee Nation was not, in the legal sense applicable to Supreme Court jurisdiction, a foreign state. It was, Marshall wrote, a "domestic dependent nation" — a formulation that has shaped federal Indian law ever since. "Their relation to the United States resembles that of a ward to his guardian," Marshall wrote, in language that would be weaponized for the next century to justify every paternalistic policy the federal government imposed on tribal nations.

What is often missed in the "domestic dependent nation" framing is what Marshall was simultaneously conceding: the Cherokee Nation was a nation. Dependent, yes. Domestic, yes. But a nation. It had a distinct political identity. It was not merely a group of individuals with certain rights. It was a collective political entity with the capacity to have legal relationships with other governments. The ward-guardian metaphor was meant to explain why federal courts had jurisdiction over federal-Indian relations, not to deny that those relations were inter-governmental in character.

**Worcester v. Georgia (1832)** is, in some respects, a more important case — though its importance was immediately and famously negated by President Andrew Jackson's refusal to enforce it. Samuel Worcester was a missionary who had remained in Cherokee territory after Georgia passed a law requiring non-Native residents to obtain state licenses. He was arrested and convicted. His case gave Marshall the opportunity to write what is, in many ways, the strongest statement of tribal sovereignty in American constitutional law.

"The Cherokee nation," Marshall wrote, "is a distinct community, occupying its own territory, with boundaries accurately described, in which the laws of Georgia can have no force, and which the citizens of Georgia have no right to enter, but with the assent of the Cherokees themselves, or in conformity with treaties, and with the acts of congress."

This is a sweeping affirmation of Cherokee self-governance. Cherokee territory was not subject to Georgia law. Cherokee political authority was not subordinate to Georgia political authority. The federal government's treaty relationships with the Cherokee Nation were the operative legal framework, not Georgia's statutes.

Jackson's response — reportedly "Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it" — is the founding moment of what might be called the enforcement gap in federal Indian law: the law says one thing, and the political branches do another, and tribal nations are left with legal rights that the federal government lacks the will to vindicate. The Removal itself followed Worcester. The Supreme Court had affirmed Cherokee sovereignty; the executive branch proceeded to remove the Cherokee at bayonet point.

The trust relationship doctrine that emerged from this era is double-edged. On one hand, the federal government has a legally cognizable duty to act in the best interests of tribal nations — to protect tribal lands and resources held in trust, to ensure that federal programs serve tribal needs, to prevent state governments from infringing on tribal jurisdiction. On the other hand, the trust relationship has been routinely invoked to justify federal control over tribal affairs on the theory that the trustee knows better than the beneficiary how the beneficiary's interests should be served. The trust relationship, at its worst, is paternalism dressed up in fiduciary language.

The **Indian Reorganization Act of 1934** (IRA) — sometimes called the Wheeler-Howard Act — was the first major legislative departure from the allotment era that had been destroying tribal landholdings since the Dawes Act of 1887. The IRA ended allotment, restored some tribal lands, authorized tribal governments to organize under tribal constitutions, and created a revolving credit fund for tribal economic development. Its architect, Commissioner of Indian Affairs John Collier, was genuinely committed to preserving tribal cultures — a significant departure from the explicit assimilationism that had characterized federal policy for half a century.

The IRA's limitations are real. It required tribes to organize under constitutions that the federal government reviewed and approved — a significant intrusion on tribal sovereignty dressed as support for it. It preserved the BIA's administrative role in tribal affairs. And many tribes, for various reasons, voted against it. The Oklahoma tribes, including the Cherokee, were initially excluded from the IRA entirely, subject instead to the Oklahoma Indian Welfare Act of 1936, which applied IRA-like provisions with additional restrictions.

But the IRA established a conceptual precedent of enormous importance: it conceded that tribal governments should exist and should have authority over tribal affairs. After decades of policy aimed at eliminating tribal governments entirely, this was a significant shift, however incomplete.

The **termination era** that followed the IRA represents perhaps the most aggressive federal assault on tribal sovereignty since removal. House Concurrent Resolution 108, passed in August 1953, declared it the policy of Congress to terminate the federal government's special relationship with tribal nations — to "free" Native Americans from federal supervision by eliminating their tribal status and subjecting them to state law, exactly as ordinary American citizens were subject to state law.

The logic of termination was a peculiar blend of fiscal conservatism, Cold War liberalism, and assimilationism. Fiscal conservatives objected to the ongoing cost of the trust relationship. Cold War liberals argued, with genuine if misguided good faith, that treating Native Americans differently from other Americans was a form of racial discrimination inconsistent with American democratic ideals — a category error that confused the civil-rights framework, applicable to individual citizens, with the sovereignty framework, applicable to nations. Assimilationists simply wanted the "Indian problem" to go away.

The practical consequences were catastrophic. Approximately 100 tribes were terminated between 1953 and 1964. They lost federal protection for their lands, lost access to federal health and education programs, and lost the legal status that supported their governments. Termination-era policies including PL 280 — which transferred civil and criminal jurisdiction over tribal lands to certain states — further eroded tribal self-governance. The Menominee in Wisconsin, the Klamath in Oregon, the Western Oregon tribes: communities that had survived a century of assault were, in the space of a few years, legally dismantled by congressional resolution.

The Cherokee Nation, already operating in a complicated legal status under Oklahoma Indian law, was not formally terminated — but the termination era's assimilationist logic pervaded federal policy and shaped the environment in which the BIA administered the programs that touched Cherokee lives. It is the context in which the federal Indian Relocation Act of 1956 was passed, the policy that moved the Mankiller family from Adair County to San Francisco. Relocation was termination's complement: if you couldn't eliminate tribal governments directly, you could dismantle tribal communities by moving their members to cities, cutting the threads of culture, language, and land relationship that sustained collective identity.

The Indian Relocation Act sent Charley Mankiller and his family to San Francisco in 1957. It planted the seed of Wilma Mankiller's political education. The policy intended to destroy community; it produced an organizer.

## 4. The Pendulum: Reorganization, Termination, and the Recovery of Self-Determination

Federal Indian policy has, through American history, oscillated between two poles. At one pole is the assimilationist position: the goal of federal policy should be to eliminate tribal distinctiveness, to absorb Native Americans into the general American population, and to extinguish the special legal status that separates tribal nations from other American communities. At the other pole is the sovereignty position: the goal of federal policy should be to recognize and support tribal self-governance, to honor treaty obligations, and to create conditions in which tribal nations can develop according to their own cultural and political priorities.

The removal era, the allotment era, and the termination era all represent peaks of assimilationist policy. The IRA, the self-determination era, and the tribal self-governance movement all represent recoveries toward the sovereignty pole. The oscillation is not random — it tracks with broader shifts in American political culture, with the priorities of specific administrations and congresses, and with the capacity of tribal nations to organize effective political resistance to policies that threaten their existence.

What is remarkable about the self-determination movement that emerged in the late 1960s and crystallized in the 1970s is that it successfully reframed the terms of the debate in ways that have largely held for fifty years. The shift from termination to self-determination was not merely a policy change. It was a conceptual revolution — a redefinition of what the federal-tribal relationship was for, and of what counted as a good outcome for that relationship.

The termination era had defined success as assimilation: a tribal nation that no longer needed a special federal relationship was a success, because its members had been fully incorporated into American society. The self-determination era redefined success as the thriving of tribal nations as nations: a tribal nation that could govern itself effectively, manage its own resources, deliver its own services, and determine its own future according to its own values was a success. These are incompatible definitions, and the shift from one to the other required a generation of political struggle.

## 5. Nixon's Turn: The 1970 Special Message and Its Consequences

The document that inaugurated the self-determination era in federal Indian policy is, by any reasonable measure, one of the most important presidential messages on domestic policy in American history. It is also among the least remembered, given who sent it.

On July 8, 1970, President Richard Nixon delivered to Congress a Special Message on Indian Affairs that explicitly and emphatically repudiated the termination policy. "The time has come to break decisively with the past," Nixon wrote, "and to create the conditions for a new era in which the Indian future is determined by Indian acts and Indian decisions."

Nixon's message is worth reading carefully, because it makes the conceptual shift with unusual precision. Termination, he argued, was wrong not because it was ineffective but because it was conceptually incoherent. The federal government's treaty obligations to tribal nations were not a form of racial discrimination to be ended in the name of equality. They were legal commitments made to sovereign nations in exchange for land cessions, and they were as binding as any other international agreement. To terminate the federal relationship in the name of equal treatment was to use the language of rights to destroy the legal infrastructure that protected those rights. "The special relationship between Indians and the Federal Government," Nixon wrote, "is the result instead of solemn obligations which have been entered into by the United States Government."

The message called for the rejection of both termination and what Nixon called "excessive dependence on the Federal Government" — the paternalistic BIA model that had substituted federal judgment for tribal judgment in the administration of tribal affairs. The path Nixon proposed ran between these two failures: a relationship in which tribal nations were genuinely self-governing, in which they managed federal programs and resources according to their own priorities, but in which the federal government honored its trust obligations by providing the resources that treaty relationships specified.

Nixon's credibility on Indian policy is complicated by the rest of his record, and the message represented a coalition of interests that included Native American activists, certain congressional conservatives who liked the idea of less federal bureaucracy, and civil rights advocates who supported self-determination as an application of the broader principle that communities should control their own institutions. But the practical consequences of the 1970 message were real. It authorized legislative development that produced the key statutes of the self-determination era.

The Indian Education Act of 1972 gave tribal governments a role in the administration of federal education programs in tribal communities. The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 — the central legislative achievement of the era — gave tribal governments the authority to contract with the federal government to manage federal programs directly. The American Indian Religious Freedom Act of 1978 formally recognized the rights of Native Americans to practice their traditional religions — rights that had been actively suppressed by federal policy for decades.

Taken together, these statutes represented a legislative repudiation of a century of assimilationist policy and the creation of a new legal framework in which tribal self-governance was not merely tolerated but affirmatively supported.

## 6. PL 93-638 and the Architecture of Self-Determination

Public Law 93-638, the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975, is the statute that changed, more than any other piece of legislation since the IRA, the day-to-day reality of what tribal sovereignty means in practice.

Before PL 93-638, the BIA administered federal programs for tribal communities directly. BIA employees designed the programs, managed the funds, hired the staff, and made the operational decisions. Tribal input was solicited in some cases and ignored in others. The programs were designed according to BIA priorities, which were not necessarily tribal priorities, and they were administered according to BIA procedures, which were not necessarily efficient, effective, or culturally appropriate for the communities they supposedly served.

PL 93-638 established a new mechanism: the 638 contract, through which a tribal nation could contract with the federal government to operate a federal program that had previously been administered by the BIA or the Indian Health Service. The tribe would receive the federal funding that had previously gone to the BIA to administer the program, and the tribe would administer it directly, according to its own priorities and through its own administrative structures.

The practical implications were enormous. A tribe that contracted to operate its own health clinic under a 638 contract could hire providers from within or familiar to the community, design clinic hours and procedures that fit community patterns, incorporate traditional healing practices, and deploy resources according to the community's assessment of its own needs — rather than according to a BIA template designed for the statistical average of all Indian communities, which matched the specific needs of any particular community poorly.

The 638 contract mechanism was, however, still constrained by federal oversight requirements, by the requirement to negotiate specific contracts for specific programs, and by a procurement framework designed for federal contracting rather than tribal governance. The self-governance compact — developed through the Self-Governance Demonstration Project that began in 1988 — was the next step.

Under self-governance compacts, a tribal nation negotiates a single, comprehensive agreement with the federal government covering an entire portfolio of programs. Instead of separate contracts for each program, the tribe receives a block grant — the "Tribal Share" of the relevant federal funding stream — and has broad discretion over how to allocate and spend it. The federal oversight role is substantially reduced; the tribe's accountability is to its own citizens rather than to federal program managers.

This is the mechanism through which Mankiller's administration transformed the Cherokee Nation's relationship with the federal government. The self-governance compact replaced a thicket of individual program contracts with a unified government-to-government agreement. It moved the Cherokee Nation from being a contractor for federal programs to being a sovereign nation managing its own affairs with federal resources that the United States was legally obligated to provide.

The distinction is not administrative. It is philosophical. A contractor executes someone else's plan with someone else's money and is accountable to someone else's standards. A sovereign government implements its own plan with resources it controls, accountable to its own citizens. PL 93-638 and the self-governance compact framework made the latter possible in a way that nothing had since the pre-removal era.

## 7. Mankiller's Specific Contributions to the Framework

Wilma Mankiller did not invent tribal self-determination. The concept is older than the United States. She did not write PL 93-638. She did not argue Cherokee Nation v. Georgia. What she did was something more concrete and, in its way, more consequential: she proved that the theory worked, at scale, in a real government, under real fiscal and political pressures. And then she documented how she had done it, and shared those documents with every other tribe that wanted to try.

When Mankiller took office as Principal Chief in 1985, the Cherokee Nation was a government that had existed for decades in a condition of managed dependency. The BIA administered the programs. The funding flowed through federal channels and arrived, often late, often redirected, often inadequate, at the communities that needed it. The Cherokee Nation government had staff and buildings and a budget, but its operational scope was limited by the extent to which the BIA was willing to share control.

Mankiller's first task was not negotiating compacts. It was demonstrating, through the Bell water project and through the early community development work she had done before becoming Deputy Chief, that Cherokee communities, given control over their own resources, could deliver results that the BIA had never delivered. The evidence base for self-governance came first. The negotiating leverage came from that evidence.

The self-governance compacts Mankiller negotiated with the federal government beginning in 1988 through the Self-Governance Demonstration Project moved the Cherokee Nation from 638 contractor status to direct self-governance. The administrative consequences were immediate: BIA overhead costs, which had consumed a significant fraction of every federal dollar nominally designated for Cherokee programs, were eliminated. The money flowed directly to Cherokee-operated programs. The tribe's own administrative staff, who understood the community, replaced BIA officials who often did not.

Mankiller's congressional testimony during this period is among the most precise political analysis in the history of federal Indian policy. Speaking before Senate committees on Indian affairs, she made the same argument in multiple registers: the current system — BIA administration of tribal programs — was expensive, ineffective, and constitutionally incoherent. It was expensive because federal bureaucracy added cost without adding value. It was ineffective because programs designed in Washington did not fit communities in Oklahoma. It was constitutionally incoherent because the United States had, in its treaties and in its Supreme Court decisions, recognized Cherokee sovereignty, and administering tribal programs as though the tribe were a federal administrative unit denied that sovereignty in practice.

Her philosophy of sovereignty was neither abstract nor ideological in the polemical sense. It was operational. Sovereignty, she argued, is not a status to be aspired to. It is a practice to be engaged in. A community that lays its own water pipe is exercising sovereignty. A clinic that hires its own providers and designs its own care model is exercising sovereignty. A school that teaches Cherokee history in the Cherokee language is exercising sovereignty. Sovereignty is not a document or a decision. It is something that communities do.

This is why the Bell water project was not merely a development success story. It was a demonstration of a theory of governance. And this is why the self-governance compact was not merely an administrative reform. It was the creation of legal infrastructure for a practice that communities had already proven they could engage in.

Mankiller's genius — if that word is not too large — was in the movement from demonstration to policy. She took what had been proven at Bell, scaled it through the Cherokee Nation's government, and then negotiated the federal agreements that made it replicable. The model she created was not Cherokee-specific. It was a model for any tribal nation that wanted to move from managed dependency to genuine self-governance, and she made it available.

By the mid-1990s, the Cherokee Nation had compacts covering health care, education, housing, community development, and economic development. The administrative infrastructure the Nation had built to manage these compacts — the tribal management systems, the accounting frameworks, the government-to-government negotiating relationships with agencies from the BIA to the Department of Housing and Urban Development to the Public Health Service — was documented and shared. Other tribes sent staff to Tahlequah to learn how it worked. Mankiller's staff traveled to other reservations to explain it. The model diffused.

She also reshaped what was expected of tribal governments in their relationships with federal and state governments. Before Mankiller, the default assumption in many federal agencies was that tribal governments were administrative units to be managed, that tribal leaders were advocates for minority constituencies rather than heads of government. Mankiller insisted, consistently and publicly, on the government-to-government framing. She met with federal officials as a head of government. She testified before Congress as a head of government. She negotiated compacts as a head of government. Over time, this insistence changed the behavioral norms: the expectation in federal Indian affairs shifted, at least partially, toward treating tribes as the sovereign entities they were.

## 8. Bell, Oklahoma: Sovereignty in Pipe and Trench

In 1981, the community of Bell, Oklahoma, had no running water. This was not an oversight. It was the end result of a long chain of decisions — federal, state, and local — in which the people of Bell were consistently treated as a population to be administered rather than a community with the capacity to solve its own problems.

Bell was a small, poor Cherokee community in Adair County, about twenty miles from Tahlequah. Its residents had been asking for running water for years. Applications had been filed, studies had been commissioned, federal programs had been identified, and nothing had been built. The gap between the application and the actual pipe in the ground was the gap between the bureaucratic management of a dependent population and the exercise of sovereignty by a self-governing community.

Mankiller, then working as a community development director for the Cherokee Nation rather than as its chief, approached the problem differently. She did not file another application. She organized the community to build its own water system. The project would lay sixteen miles of water pipe across Adair County terrain — hills, creek crossings, rocky ground — using volunteer labor provided by Bell residents themselves.

The Bell project was not straightforward. It required persuading a skeptical community that the work was worth doing and that it could be done. It required convincing the Cherokee Nation's leadership to provide administrative and financial support. It required navigating federal and state regulatory requirements for water system construction. And it required recruiting, organizing, and sustaining volunteer labor through months of physical work — digging trenches by hand in weather that ranged from summer heat to winter cold, hauling pipe sections, learning to make water-tight joints in field conditions.

What the Bell project produced was not only water. It produced a community that had built something together. The people of Bell had dug the trenches, laid the pipe, and turned the valves. The water system was theirs in a way that a system built by contractors and transferred to a management committee could never be. They knew where every junction was. They knew which sections had given trouble. They understood the system from the inside because they had built it from the outside.

This is sovereignty at its most fundamental level: not the formal legal status recognized by treaties and Supreme Court decisions, but the actual capacity of a community to identify its own needs, mobilize its own resources, build the things it requires, and take responsibility for maintaining them. The legal framework matters — without the sovereign status that gave the Cherokee Nation the authority to organize the project and manage the federal funds that partially supported it, Bell might never have gotten its water system. But the legal framework is only the container. The content is the community laying the pipe.

Mankiller later wrote that Bell changed how she understood what development meant. Development, she concluded, was not something done to communities by external agents with resources and expertise. Development was what happened when a community exercised its own capacity — when it looked at a problem, assembled the resources it had, organized the labor that was available, and built the solution. External resources could support that process. External expertise could inform it. But the animating force was internal. And when it was internal, the community owned the result in the deepest possible sense.

This insight — that the practice of sovereignty is not the declaration of independence but the laying of pipe — became the operating philosophy of the Cherokee Nation under Mankiller's leadership. Every self-governance compact she negotiated was, in structural terms, the Bell project at scale: the Cherokee Nation taking direct responsibility for its own infrastructure, its own services, its own future, rather than waiting for the BIA to get around to it.

The parallel to infrastructure projects in the twenty-first century is not accidental. When a community takes on the work of building its own broadband network rather than waiting for a telecommunications company to decide the economics justify it, when a library district assembles its own computing infrastructure rather than paying indefinitely for cloud services it cannot afford to scale, it is enacting the same principle Bell enacted in 1981: communities that build their own infrastructure own their own future. The Open Compute Node project — a container-based edge computing installation built and maintained by and for communities that lack reliable access to large-scale computation — is, in this respect, a technological instantiation of a governance philosophy that Mankiller articulated and demonstrated forty years earlier.

The through-line is not metaphorical. It is structural. Communities need infrastructure. Infrastructure built by others on others' terms does not belong to the community in any meaningful sense. Infrastructure built by and for the community — even if it is materially supported by external resources — creates a different relationship between the community and the systems it depends on. Bell's water system was Cherokee. An Open Compute Node deployed in a tribal library, administered by tribal staff, painted by schoolchildren, and maintained by community members who understand how it works, is tribal in exactly the same way. The technology differs. The principle is identical.

## 9. The Cherokee Nation Today: What the Foundation Holds

The Cherokee Nation in 2026 is the largest tribal nation in the United States by enrolled citizenship, with more than 450,000 enrolled citizens. It operates a government with a budget measured in the hundreds of millions of dollars annually, providing services in health, education, housing, economic development, cultural preservation, and social services across northeastern Oklahoma and to Cherokee citizens throughout the country.

The Cherokee Nation Health Service operates fourteen health facilities across northeastern Oklahoma, including full-service hospitals, community health centers, and behavioral health facilities. It serves approximately 200,000 patients annually. The health system employs thousands of Cherokee citizens and has built a level of care that the BIA-administered Indian Health Service, chronically underfunded and bureaucratically constrained, was never able to achieve in the region.

The Cherokee Nation's educational infrastructure includes a school system that incorporates Cherokee language instruction, cultural programming, and academic preparation. The Cherokee Language Department works to document and revitalize a language that was nearly extinguished by a century of federal boarding school policy. The Sequoyah Schools, named for the inventor of the Cherokee syllabary, provide Cherokee-centered education to students from across northeastern Oklahoma.

Cherokee Nation Businesses, the Nation's economic development arm, generated more than $2 billion in annual revenue in recent years, operating across sectors including manufacturing, government contracting, hospitality, health care, and technology services. The Nation's economic diversification strategy, which traces to the community development philosophy that Mankiller advanced in the 1980s, has created thousands of jobs and a tax base that funds government operations independent of the federal funding streams that dominate the finances of many tribal nations.

The jurisdictional authority of the Cherokee Nation was dramatically expanded — and dramatically clarified — by the Supreme Court's 2020 decision in McGirt v. Oklahoma, which held that the Muscogee (Creek) Nation's reservation, established by treaty in the nineteenth century, had never been disestablished by Congress and remained Indian Country subject to tribal and federal rather than state jurisdiction. The McGirt decision, extended in subsequent rulings to the Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole nations, restored reservation status to most of northeastern Oklahoma — roughly 19 million acres, including much of the city of Tulsa.

The implications of McGirt for tribal governance are still unfolding. The decision did not create new tribal authority; it recognized authority that had existed in law but been denied in practice for more than a century. But the recognition has practical consequences: major crimes committed by Native Americans in Indian Country must now be prosecuted in tribal or federal court rather than state court, a shift that has required rapid expansion of tribal justice systems and that has produced intense political conflict with the state of Oklahoma. What McGirt illustrates is that the framework Mankiller helped build — a Cherokee Nation with robust governmental institutions, professional staff, and the administrative capacity to manage complex programs — made it possible for the Nation to absorb the jurisdictional expansion. A nation without that institutional capacity could not have done so.

The degree to which the present-day Cherokee Nation's institutional strength traces to Mankiller is not easily quantified, but the connection is direct. The self-governance compacts she negotiated established the administrative infrastructure that subsequent leadership built upon. The community development philosophy she advanced shaped the Nation's approach to economic development and social services. The standards she established for what Cherokee government could accomplish — and for how it should present itself in its relationships with the federal government, with state governments, and with the public — created a benchmark that her successors inherited and, in most cases, have worked to meet.

## 10. The Broader Movement: 700 Compacts and Counting

The self-governance model the Cherokee Nation helped pioneer under Mankiller's leadership has, in the forty years since the Self-Governance Demonstration Project began, been adopted by tribal nations across the country at a scale that makes it the de facto framework for federal-tribal relations in the areas it covers.

As of 2024, more than 700 tribal nations participate in self-governance arrangements under the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act and the Tribal Self-Governance Act. These compact-holding nations collectively administer billions of dollars in federal program funding annually, operating health systems, schools, housing programs, social services, and economic development programs that previously were managed — poorly, expensively, and paternalistically — by federal agencies.

The Indian Health Service self-governance program is among the most significant. Approximately one-third of all IHS-funded health care is now delivered under tribal self-governance compacts. The research consistently shows that tribal-administered health programs outperform federally administered programs on measures of cost-effectiveness, community responsiveness, and health outcomes. This is not surprising. Programs designed and operated by people who understand the community they serve, who are accountable to that community's political process, and who have incentives to innovate rather than to comply with federal templates perform better than programs designed by distant bureaucracies for a statistical average.

The spread of tribal broadband initiatives is among the most important contemporary expressions of the self-determination framework. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the catastrophic consequences of inadequate broadband infrastructure in tribal communities — health care, education, economic participation, and civic life all moved online, and tribal communities without reliable connectivity were excluded from all of it. The federal response, including the Emergency Connectivity Fund and subsequent broadband infrastructure programs, made significant resources available. But the differentiating factor between tribal nations that successfully deployed broadband infrastructure and those that did not was institutional capacity — the capacity to identify appropriate technologies, navigate federal procurement, hire technical staff, manage construction projects, and operate networks sustainably.

That institutional capacity is, in the communities that have it, a product of the self-determination era's investment in tribal governance capacity. The connection is direct: a tribal government that has been managing complex federal programs under self-governance compacts for thirty years has the administrative infrastructure — the accounting systems, the procurement processes, the management talent — to take on a broadband deployment project. A tribal government that has been operated as a BIA field office does not.

Water rights battles continue to represent one of the most critical sovereignty struggles in Indian Country. The Navajo Nation's case for access to Colorado River water — argued before the Supreme Court in 2023 as Arizona v. Navajo Nation — is the latest iteration of a principle as old as the treaties themselves: when the United States promised tribal nations a homeland, it implicitly promised the water necessary to make that homeland viable. The aridification of the American West under climate change makes this not an abstract legal question but an immediate question of physical survival.

Sacred site protection — the Bears Ears case, the Oak Flat case, the continuing struggle to prevent development of sites central to tribal religious practice — represents a sovereignty claim that the current legal framework inadequately supports. The American Indian Religious Freedom Act of 1978 declared a federal policy of protecting Indigenous religious freedom but provided no enforcement mechanism. Decades of litigation have produced inconsistent results. The sacred site cases illustrate the gap between formal legal recognition of tribal rights and the practical protection of those rights — the same gap that Worcester v. Georgia illustrated in 1832, before Andrew Jackson declined to enforce it.

The ongoing struggle for tribal sovereignty is not a relic of the nineteenth century. It is a contemporary political reality in which every court decision, every congressional appropriation, every executive order, every state statute that touches Indian Country either advances or contracts the framework of self-determination. The 700 self-governance compacts are a measure of how far the movement has come. The McGirt decision and its contested aftermath are a measure of how far it has yet to go.

## 11. The Through-Line: Infrastructure as the Practice of Sovereignty

Wilma Mankiller, in the last years of her life, was asked repeatedly to summarize what she had learned. She returned, in different formulations, to the same insight: that sovereignty is not a legal status to be claimed but a practice to be engaged in, and that the practice begins with the most concrete and physical expressions of community capacity.

A community that controls its water supply controls its future in a way that a community waiting for an external agency to provide water does not. A community that operates its own clinic, staffed by providers who know the community and are accountable to its members, has a different relationship to its own health than a community that depends on a federal health bureaucracy. A community that teaches its own children in its own language has a different relationship to its own culture than a community whose children are educated in institutions designed to eliminate that culture.

This is not a romantic vision of self-sufficiency. Mankiller was not arguing that communities should isolate themselves from external resources or that they should refuse federal funding that was legally owed to them. She was arguing that the difference between receiving a service and building a system is the difference between dependency and sovereignty — and that sovereignty, exercised through the building of systems, is not only politically preferable but practically more effective.

The grandmother in Bell, Oklahoma, turning on a faucet for the first time in 1982, was not experiencing the delivery of a government service. She was experiencing the fruit of her community's own labor, mediated through a government — the Cherokee Nation — that her community had elected and that was accountable to her. The water was Cherokee water, delivered through a Cherokee system, built by Cherokee hands. The political philosophy encoded in that moment of water running from a tap is as sophisticated as anything Chief Justice Marshall wrote.

The thread from Bell to the Open Compute Node is real. A container-sized computing installation deployed at a tribal library or a rural school, painted by the children who will use it, administered by the community that owns it, connected to networks whose architecture the community controls — this is the Bell project in a different medium. The technology has changed dramatically. The principle has not. Communities should build and own the infrastructure they depend on. Infrastructure built by communities for communities creates a different relationship between people and technology than infrastructure built by distant corporations and leased to communities on terms they did not negotiate.

Mankiller would have understood this immediately, not as a technological argument but as a sovereignty argument. The question she would have asked is not "does it work?" but "who owns it?" The answer to the second question determines the long-term value of the answer to the first.

The self-determination movement she helped lead has produced, in fifty years, the most significant expansion of effective tribal sovereignty since the treaty era. Seven hundred compacts. Tribal health systems that serve more than a million patients annually. Schools that teach in Indigenous languages that were nearly extinguished by federal policy. Economic enterprises that employ tens of thousands and generate billions. Governments with the institutional capacity to absorb a Supreme Court decision restoring nineteen million acres of Indian Country and to govern that land.

None of this happened automatically. It happened because specific people made specific decisions under specific constraints and built things that worked. Wilma Mankiller was among the most consequential of those people. The framework she operated within — the legal architecture of treaty rights and tribal sovereignty, the legislative framework of the self-determination era, the institutional framework of the self-governance compact — was built by many hands over many years. But within that framework, she made choices that changed what was possible.

She chose to treat the Bell project as a proof-of-concept rather than a one-time success. She chose to use the proof-of-concept as negotiating leverage for self-governance compacts. She chose to document and share the model rather than protect it as a competitive advantage. She chose to insist, in every forum available to her, on the government-to-government framing — on the principle that the Cherokee Nation was a nation and would be treated as one. These choices compounded over time into a movement.

The final measure of a political leader is not the awards they received or the office they held. It is whether the things they built held. The institutions the Cherokee Nation built under Mankiller's leadership have held — through political transitions, legal battles, economic cycles, and the extraordinary challenge of McGirt. They have held because they were built on a principle that was true before Mankiller was born and will be true long after the last person who knew her has died: that communities have the right and the capacity to govern themselves, and that the exercise of that capacity, concrete and daily and unglamorous as it usually is, is the most fundamental expression of human freedom.

The grandmother in Bell, turning on the tap. The library with its own computing infrastructure, accessible to students who had never imagined that kind of access. The clinic in Tahlequah, staffed by Cherokee providers, treating Cherokee patients, accountable to the Cherokee Nation. The school in Adair County, teaching the syllabary that Sequoyah invented so that his people's words would not be lost.

This is what sovereignty looks like when it is working. Not a declaration. Not a court decision. Not a congressional resolution. A tap. A clinic. A school. A library with enough compute to run the models that will help the next generation understand the world.
