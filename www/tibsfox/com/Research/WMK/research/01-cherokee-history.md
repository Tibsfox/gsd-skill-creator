# Cherokee Nation: History, Sovereignty, and the World Wilma Mankiller Was Born Into

**Research Module 01 — WMK Series**
*Cherokee Nation History and the Trail of Tears as essential context for understanding Wilma Mankiller's life and leadership*

## Preface

To understand Wilma Mankiller — why she became who she became, what she was fighting against, what she was fighting for — you must first understand the compressed weight of history she carried. Her father's ancestors walked the Trail of Tears. Her own family was relocated from Oklahoma to San Francisco under a federal policy that echoed that removal by a century. Every project she built, every negotiation she won, every federal compact she signed was an act of recovery from a specific sequence of historical events. This module traces that sequence: from the original Cherokee homeland to Mankiller's inauguration as Principal Chief in 1985.

This is not a survey of Native American history in the abstract. It is the specific, documented history of one people — the Cherokee — from their pre-contact civilization through the legislative, judicial, and military assaults that nearly destroyed them, and through their remarkable recovery. Every section connects directly to the world Mankiller inherited.

## Part I: The Ani-Yunwiya — The Principal People

### Origins and Territory

The Cherokee called themselves *Ani-Yunwiya* — the Principal People. Their homeland at the time of first sustained European contact encompassed approximately 135,000 square miles across the southern Appalachians and surrounding lowlands. This territory included parts of what are now eight states: North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Tennessee, Alabama, Virginia, West Virginia, and Kentucky. The geographic heart was the mountain country of the southern Appalachians, a land of steep ridges, deep river valleys, and dense hardwood forest — the most biologically diverse region in North America east of the Mississippi.

The Cherokee were one of the Five Civilized Tribes, a designation applied by European settlers to Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Creek (Muscogee), and Seminole peoples who had developed complex agricultural societies, trade networks, and governance systems that European observers recognized as analogous to their own political institutions. The term itself reflects the colonial gaze — the implication that civilization was European in character — but the underlying observation was accurate: the Cherokee nation, by the early nineteenth century, had a written language, a newspaper, a constitution, a bicameral legislature, a system of courts, and a settled agricultural economy.

### Language: The Cherokee Tongue

Cherokee belongs to the Iroquoian language family, making it a linguistic relative of the languages spoken by the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy) to the north — a relationship that reflects a much older migration history reaching back thousands of years. The Cherokee language, known as *Tsalagi Gawonihisdi* in the language itself, is polysynthetic: words are built from many meaningful units combined into single complex forms, encoding in one word what English might require a full sentence to express.

Cherokee verbs carry extraordinary informational density. A single verb form specifies not only the action but the number and nature of objects, the relationship between actor and acted-upon, the degree of certainty of the speaker, and the temporal aspect of the action. The language has approximately 17,000 distinct verb forms. This grammatical complexity is not a curiosity — it reflects a cognitive and cultural orientation to the world in which relationship, agency, and context are encoded at the most fundamental level of communication.

At the time of European contact, perhaps 22,000 Cherokee speakers lived in roughly 60 towns scattered through the Appalachian region. The language had no writing system. Knowledge was transmitted through oral tradition, ceremony, and direct instruction. The creation of a writing system — which would happen in 1821 — was one of the most extraordinary intellectual events in recorded history, and it happened because a man named Sequoyah paid attention to a problem.

### Governance: The Town as Sovereign Unit

Traditional Cherokee governance was not centralized in a European sense. Political authority resided primarily at the level of the town (*talwa*), and each town had considerable autonomy. Towns were organized around a central council house — typically a large, circular building capable of holding the entire population for collective decision-making. Leadership was dual in structure: a White (peace) organization managed civil affairs, agriculture, trade, and diplomacy; a Red (war) organization managed military affairs and was activated in times of conflict.

The principal peace chief (Uku) held ceremonial authority and presided over council deliberations. Decision-making proceeded by consensus. A chief who could not persuade would not be obeyed — coercive authority was structurally weak, and this was by design. The Cherokee political tradition held that no person could compel another's action without their consent. This was not a failure of governance; it was a philosophical commitment to collective sovereignty.

Towns belonged to clans that crosscut geography: a Cherokee person identified by clan affiliation — Wolf, Bird, Deer, Blue, Long Hair, Wild Potato, or Paint — and this clan membership determined kinship obligations, marriage rules (one could not marry within one's clan), and channels of support across the wider nation. A traveler arriving at a distant town who was unknown could declare their clan and receive the hospitality and protection of all clan relatives in that community. The clan system created a social infrastructure that bound the widely dispersed nation together without requiring centralized political authority.

Over the course of the eighteenth century, as Cherokee territory shrank under pressure from European settlement and the nation faced existential threats that required coordinated response, governance became more centralized. By the early nineteenth century, a national council with representation from the towns had assumed greater authority. The town remained important, but the nation as a unit was increasingly capable of acting as a unified political body. This evolution set the stage for the constitutional period.

### Economy, Agriculture, and Landscape Management

The Cherokee were sophisticated managers of the Appalachian landscape. The *Three Sisters* agriculture — corn, beans, and squash grown together in a mutually beneficial polyculture — formed the agricultural base. Corn was the caloric staple; beans fixed nitrogen and provided protein; squash covered the ground, retaining moisture and suppressing weeds. This system sustained dense populations without depleting soil fertility.

The Cherokee also maintained large orchards of introduced European fruit trees by the late eighteenth century, managed extensive river fisheries, and hunted deer, turkey, bear, and other game across wide territories. River systems were central to Cherokee life: the Tennessee, Hiawassee, Chattahoochee, Etowah, Coosa, Little Tennessee, and Tuckasegee rivers ran through Cherokee territory and provided transportation routes, protein sources, and the ecological context for a culture deeply rooted in moving water.

Fire management was systematic. Regular burning of forest understory maintained open parklike woodlands that supported game populations and made forest travel possible. The Appalachian landscape that European settlers encountered was not wilderness in the sense of untouched nature — it was a managed landscape shaped by thousands of years of deliberate human activity.

## Part II: Contact, Conflict, and the Compression of Territory

### The Long Eighteenth Century

The story of Cherokee-European relations from the late seventeenth century through the early nineteenth is one of progressive territorial loss under constant pressure from multiple directions. English colonists from Virginia and Carolina, French from Louisiana and Canada, Spanish from Florida — the Cherokee navigated competing European powers, at times playing them against each other, at times allied with one against another, always attempting to maintain the autonomy of the nation.

The Yamasee War of 1715–1717, the French and Indian War (1754–1763), the Anglo-Cherokee War (1758–1761), and the Cherokee's largely British-allied role in the American Revolutionary War each brought further territorial concessions and demographic losses. The Revolutionary War was particularly devastating: Cherokee towns allied with the British were attacked by colonial militias in 1776 in a coordinated series of raids that destroyed dozens of towns, burned crops, scattered populations, and killed hundreds. The political consequence was a series of treaties in which the Cherokee ceded substantial portions of their homeland in exchange for peace.

Between 1721 and 1819, the Cherokee signed at least 25 treaties with European and then American governments. Each treaty ceded territory. The cumulative effect was staggering: from the 135,000 square miles of the original homeland, Cherokee territory contracted to a core in northwestern Georgia, northeastern Alabama, southeastern Tennessee, and western North Carolina — perhaps 15,000 square miles at its smallest continuous extent before removal.

### The Transformation of Cherokee Society

Despite — or because of — this pressure, the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries saw remarkable transformations in Cherokee society. The Cherokee adopted elements of European agricultural and commercial practice: cattle and hogs supplemented traditional hunting; cotton gin technology was introduced; spinning and weaving became common in Cherokee homes; ferry crossings and trading posts operated as commercial enterprises along major routes through the territory.

These changes were uneven in their distribution. A Cherokee aristocracy emerged — primarily among mixed-descent families who had access to capital and whose English-language literacy gave them advantages in trade and diplomacy. Enslaved African Americans were held by a relatively small number of wealthy Cherokee families, imported as a practice from the surrounding plantation economy. This is a complicated part of Cherokee history, one that the Cherokee Nation itself has grappled with in recent decades through debates over citizenship rights for Cherokee freedmen.

For most Cherokee, the early nineteenth century meant adaptation under duress: trying to hold a homeland against encroachment while enough of the traditional economic and social fabric survived to sustain community life. The development of written Cherokee — which gave the nation a tool of cultural preservation and political organization of extraordinary power — happened in this context.

## Part III: Sequoyah and the Cherokee Syllabary

### The Man

George Gist, known in Cherokee as Sequoyah, was born around 1770 to a Cherokee mother named Wut-teh and a European father — probably a fur trader named Nathaniel Gist or possibly a soldier — who played no role in his life. Sequoyah grew up speaking only Cherokee. He was a silversmith, a hunter, and later a soldier who served with the Cherokee regiment under Andrew Jackson in the Creek War of 1813–1814. He was literate in neither English nor any other European language.

What Sequoyah observed, reportedly over many years, was the power that writing gave to Europeans: the ability to send knowledge across distances without a messenger, to preserve agreements in a form that could not be misremembered, to communicate with people not present. The Cherokee had a phrase for writing — *talking leaves* — because marks on paper seemed to the Cherokee observers to speak. Sequoyah determined to give the Cherokee their own talking leaves.

### The Intellectual Achievement

What makes Sequoyah's achievement singular in recorded history is this: he is the only individual known to have independently created a complete writing system for a language, without building on a pre-existing writing system, without literacy in any other written language, working alone over roughly a decade of sustained effort.

Other writing systems — Sumerian cuneiform, Egyptian hieroglyphics, Chinese characters, the Phoenician alphabet and all its descendants including the Latin letters on this page — were either invented by groups working over generations, or borrowed and adapted from pre-existing systems. Sequoyah did not borrow. He observed the fact of European writing, understood its function, and invented a completely original system to achieve the same result for Cherokee.

His first attempt was a logographic system — one symbol per word — which proved unworkable. He then recognized that Cherokee words could be broken into syllables, and that a manageable set of symbols for syllables could represent the entire language. The final system, completed around 1821, contains 85 characters (later reduced to 84 in the standard version), each representing a distinct syllable of the Cherokee language. It is a syllabary, not an alphabet.

The syllabary is notable for its rapid learnability. Because Cherokee is phonetically consistent — it does not have the complex spelling rules of English, where the same letters combine differently in different words — a speaker of Cherokee could learn to read and write in the syllabary in a matter of days or weeks. No other writing system in history has been adopted so quickly by so many people: within five years of the syllabary's introduction in 1821, it is estimated that the majority of adult Cherokee were literate in their own language. Literacy rates in Cherokee exceeded contemporary literacy rates in the United States.

The syllabary also served as a tool of cultural preservation. Cherokee medicine knowledge, prayers, ceremonial instructions, and historical accounts that had previously existed only in oral form were written down by their holders. Private correspondence flourished. Community newspapers began circulating before the founding of the official Cherokee Phoenix. Sequoyah had given his people a technology of memory at the precise moment that memory was under maximum threat.

Sequoyah received no formal payment or recognition from the Cherokee Nation during his lifetime proportionate to what he had given. He died around 1843 in Mexico, searching for a lost band of Cherokee who had reportedly migrated there decades earlier. He remains one of the most consequential individuals in the intellectual history of any civilization.

### The Syllabary's Relevance to Mankiller

Wilma Mankiller, as Principal Chief, made Cherokee language preservation a central priority. She funded the Institute for Cherokee Literacy and supported language immersion programs. The syllabary Sequoyah created was the tool available for this work. When Mankiller fought to reverse the language's decline — by 1985, the number of fluent Cherokee speakers had fallen precipitously from nineteenth-century levels — she was fighting to keep alive the intellectual achievement that had enabled Cherokee cultural survival during the century of dispossession.

## Part IV: The Cherokee Phoenix and the Constitutional Period

### The Cherokee Constitution of 1827

The constitutional period culminated in 1827 with the adoption of a formal written constitution modeled in significant ways on the United States Constitution. The Cherokee Constitution established three branches of government: an executive (Principal Chief and Assistant Principal Chief), a bicameral legislature (the National Committee and the National Council), and a judicial branch. The capital was established at New Echota in present-day Georgia.

The constitution declared that the Cherokee Nation constituted a sovereign nation with full authority over its territory and members. It explicitly asserted that no part of Cherokee territory could be ceded without the consent of the Cherokee people in council — a provision written in direct response to ongoing American pressure for land cessions. The constitution also, controversially, restricted political participation to Cherokee men and prohibited the children of enslaved African Americans from inheriting citizenship — provisions that reflected the contemporary political context and that have had lasting implications for Cherokee Nation citizenship debates.

The Cherokee Constitution was a remarkable document: a political assertion of sovereignty in European legal form, using the tools of the colonizer to resist the colonizer. It was also, in retrospect, a provocation. By constituting themselves formally as a sovereign nation with a capital and a government, the Cherokee were directly challenging Georgia's claim to jurisdiction over Cherokee territory — and Georgia's desire for the land itself.

### The Cherokee Phoenix

On February 21, 1828, the first issue of the *Cherokee Phoenix* was published at New Echota. It was the first Native American newspaper in United States history. The editor was Elias Boudinot (Cherokee name: Buck Watie), a mixed-heritage Cherokee who had been educated at the Foreign Mission School in Cornwall, Connecticut. The paper was printed in both English and Cherokee, with columns side by side in the two languages using the Sequoyan syllabary for the Cherokee text.

The *Phoenix* covered tribal government affairs, published laws of the Cherokee Nation, reported on events in the United States and internationally, and engaged directly with the growing debate over Indian removal. It was, by any standard of its era, a sophisticated publication — intellectually serious, politically engaged, editorially independent. Its existence was proof of what the Cherokee had built: a literate, constitutionally governed nation with its own free press.

The paper's significance extended beyond information. It was a political instrument. The *Phoenix* carried the Cherokee case against removal to American public opinion at a time when the removal debate was being conducted in Congress and in newspapers across the eastern United States. It documented Cherokee arguments, reprinted speeches from congressional debates, and made the Cherokee perspective available to any American who could read.

The *Cherokee Phoenix* was suppressed by Georgia authorities in 1835, near the end of the constitutional period, as the removal crisis came to its terminal phase. Its press was seized and destroyed. The silencing of the *Phoenix* was part of the systematic dismantling of Cherokee civil society that preceded forced removal.

## Part V: The Legal and Legislative Battle — 1828–1832

### The Context: Georgia's Escalation

The election of Andrew Jackson to the presidency in 1828 fundamentally changed the political calculus for the Cherokee. Jackson was a known advocate of Indian removal — he had made his military reputation fighting against Creek and Cherokee resistance in the South, and he was openly sympathetic to the demands of Georgia and other southern states for access to Native land. With a sympathetic president, Georgia's state government moved aggressively.

In 1828 and 1829, Georgia passed a series of laws that extended state jurisdiction over Cherokee territory, nullified all Cherokee laws, prohibited Cherokee from testifying in court against white citizens, and — critically — declared all lands, gold mines, and improvements in Cherokee territory to be property of Georgia. This last provision was prompted by the discovery of gold in the Cherokee territory near Dahlonega, Georgia, in 1828 — a gold rush that brought thousands of prospectors onto Cherokee land and created immediate pressure for removal that transcended even the background desire for agricultural land.

The Cherokee Nation's response was to sue in federal court.

### Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831)

In 1831, the Cherokee Nation brought suit in the United States Supreme Court to prevent Georgia from executing its laws against the Cherokee people. The case presented a foundational question: did the Supreme Court have jurisdiction to hear a case brought by the Cherokee Nation as a foreign nation?

Chief Justice John Marshall, writing for the Court, declined jurisdiction — but his reasoning was careful and consequential. Marshall held that the Cherokee were not a foreign nation in the sense contemplated by Article III of the Constitution, which extends federal judicial power to cases between states and foreign nations. Instead, Marshall wrote, the Cherokee and other tribes were "domestic dependent nations" — a category he essentially invented. The tribes "occupy a territory to which we assert a title independent of their will, which must take effect in point of possession when their right of possession ceases." Their relationship to the United States, Marshall wrote, "resembles that of a ward to his guardian."

The ward-guardian framing was paternalistic and would be used to justify federal control over tribal affairs for the next century and a half. But Marshall also wrote that the Cherokee had an "unquestionable" right to the lands they occupied, that they were "a distinct political society, separated from others, capable of managing its own affairs and governing itself," and that Georgia's laws were probably unconstitutional — though the Court could not reach that question without jurisdiction.

Marshall had turned down the case on a technicality while signaling that the Cherokee had a strong claim. The Cherokee's attorneys immediately looked for a vehicle to bring the merits before the Court.

### Worcester v. Georgia (1832)

The vehicle appeared in the case of Samuel Worcester, a missionary living in Cherokee territory who had refused to sign an oath of allegiance to Georgia required by state law. Worcester was arrested, tried by a Georgia court, and sentenced to four years of hard labor. Worcester's attorneys brought his case to the Supreme Court.

In Worcester v. Georgia (1832), Marshall wrote one of the most sweeping opinions in the history of federal Indian law. He held that the Cherokee Nation was a distinct community occupying its own territory, with boundaries accurately described in federal treaties, in which the laws of Georgia had no force. The relationship between the United States and the Cherokee was governed by federal treaties and federal law; states had no jurisdiction over tribal affairs within tribal territory. Georgia's laws purporting to extend state authority over the Cherokee were unconstitutional and void.

The opinion was, on paper, a complete legal vindication of Cherokee sovereignty. In practice, it was ineffective.

President Jackson reportedly responded — the quotation may be apocryphal but captures the political reality — "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it." Jackson's administration took no action to enforce the ruling. Worcester remained in a Georgia prison (Georgia eventually released him after he agreed not to pursue further legal action). Georgia continued to enforce its laws against the Cherokee. The federal government continued to negotiate removal. The Supreme Court had declared the law; the executive branch declined to implement it.

The Worcester decision established the foundational legal principle of tribal sovereignty that would not be fully implemented until the self-governance era that Mankiller later fought to advance. In 1831 and 1832, it was a decision without a sheriff.

## Part VI: The Indian Removal Act and Its Implementation

### The Indian Removal Act of 1830

On May 28, 1830, President Jackson signed the Indian Removal Act into law. The act authorized the President to negotiate with Native American nations for their removal from their eastern homelands to land west of the Mississippi River. It did not explicitly authorize forced removal — the act's text required that removal be negotiated and voluntary. In practice, this distinction was eroded from the beginning.

The passage of the act was contested. Congressman Davy Crockett of Tennessee, a Jackson ally on most matters, broke with the president and voted against it. Senator Theodore Frelinghuysen of New Jersey delivered a six-hour speech against the bill, arguing that the Cherokee had legal title to their lands and that removal violated both federal treaties and natural law. Newspapers across the North published editorials opposing the policy. Ralph Waldo Emerson wrote a letter of protest to President Jackson. Missionary societies organized petition campaigns.

The opposition failed. The act passed the Senate 28–19 and the House 102–97.

The Indian Removal Act set in motion the removal of the Five Civilized Tribes from the Southeast over the following decade. The Choctaw were removed first, in 1831–1833, under conditions of considerable hardship. The Creek were removed in 1836, many in chains after resisting. The Chickasaw negotiated more favorable terms and were removed in 1837–1838. The Seminole fought three wars of resistance in Florida, and a portion never surrendered. And the Cherokee — who had won in the Supreme Court and had the most developed institutions of any tribal nation — fought the longest and suffered most visibly.

### The Treaty of New Echota: Fraudulent Foundation of Removal

The legal justification for Cherokee removal rested on the Treaty of New Echota, signed on December 29, 1835. The treaty ceded all remaining Cherokee territory east of the Mississippi in exchange for $5 million, land in Indian Territory, and various other provisions. The problem — universally acknowledged at the time — was that the treaty was signed by a small faction of the Cherokee Nation that had no authority to cede national lands.

Principal Chief John Ross, the elected leader of the Cherokee Nation, did not sign. The vast majority of the Cherokee people opposed removal. A petition signed by 15,665 Cherokee — nearly the entire adult population — was presented to Congress protesting the treaty. Chief Ross argued before Congress and before the Supreme Court that the treaty was fraudulent, that the signers had no authority, that the Cherokee Constitution explicitly prohibited any individual or faction from ceding national territory.

The Senate ratified the Treaty of New Echota by a single vote — 31–15 — in May 1836. The ratification gave the United States government two years to complete Cherokee removal.

The fraudulent character of the treaty's origins was noted not only by the Cherokee but by military officers involved in the subsequent removal. General John Wool, ordered to begin preparations for removal, wrote to the Secretary of War that "the whole scene since I have been in this country has been nothing but a heart-rending one... If I could, and I could not do them a greater kindness, I would remove every Indian tomorrow beyond the reach of the white men." He resigned his command rather than continue. His replacement was General Winfield Scott.

## Part VII: Nunna Daul Tsuny — The Trail Where They Cried

### Preparations and Delay

The two-year removal period specified in the Treaty of New Echota expired in May 1838. The vast majority of the approximately 16,000 to 17,000 Eastern Cherokee remained in their homeland. Some had fled to the remote mountains of North Carolina, where they would eventually form the Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians. Most had simply refused to leave, believing — correctly — that their removal violated the law.

In May and June 1838, General Winfield Scott deployed approximately 7,000 Army troops and state militia to begin forcible removal. Soldiers moved through Cherokee communities, rounding up families from their homes, often giving no warning and no time to gather belongings. The operations were conducted with deliberate speed to minimize resistance: families were separated during roundups; personal property — livestock, furniture, farming equipment, stored food — was left behind and quickly seized by neighboring whites who followed behind the soldiers.

The Cherokee were concentrated in a network of stockade camps — some historians call them internment camps — established across the region. Conditions in the camps were catastrophic from the beginning. Disease spread rapidly in the overcrowded enclosures. Sanitation was inadequate. Food supplies were insufficient. The summer of 1838 was exceptionally hot and dry. Hundreds died in the camps before the primary march began.

Principal Chief John Ross, still attempting to control the process on behalf of his people, negotiated with Scott to allow the Cherokee to manage their own removal under Cherokee supervision. Scott agreed, partly to deflect responsibility, partly because Cherokee-managed removal promised to be more orderly. The primary march was delayed until fall 1838 to escape the summer heat.

### The March: Fall 1838 – Spring 1839

The main Cherokee detachments began their overland journey in October and November 1838. The route varied by detachment — some traveled entirely overland, others combined river transport with overland sections — but the general direction was northwest from Georgia and Tennessee toward Indian Territory in what is now eastern Oklahoma. The total distance was approximately 1,000 miles.

The winter of 1838–1839 was severe, one of the coldest on record. The Cherokee marched through snow, ice, and freezing rain with inadequate clothing and shelter. Food supplies were frequently delayed or diverted. Detachments contracted cholera, dysentery, whooping cough, and typhus. The elderly and the very young died at particularly high rates. Families watched their children, parents, and grandparents die along the road and had to bury them in unfamiliar ground, far from any ancestral landscape, and march on.

Estimates of the death toll vary among historians and have been a subject of ongoing scholarly debate. The Cherokee Nation's own accounts and the figures compiled by researchers using military records, missionary reports, and detachment accounts suggest that approximately 4,000 Cherokee died during removal — in the camps, on the march, or shortly after arrival in Indian Territory — out of a population of perhaps 16,000 to 17,000. This represents a mortality rate of approximately 25 percent of the entire nation.

The Cherokee called the removal *Nunna daul Tsuny* — "the trail where they cried." English speakers rendered it as the Trail of Tears.

Among those who died on the march was Quatie, the wife of Principal Chief John Ross. She gave her blanket to a sick child and died of exposure. Ross buried her along the route and continued leading his people.

The Eastern Cherokee who had fled into the mountains of North Carolina survived by remaining out of reach of the removal forces. Their descendants are the Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians, who maintain a reservation — the Qualla Boundary — in the Smoky Mountains to this day. This community represents the portion of the Cherokee people who never left their Appalachian homeland.

### The Mankiller Family Connection

Wilma Mankiller's paternal ancestors were among the Cherokee who walked the Trail of Tears. Her father's family was from Adair County in eastern Oklahoma — Indian Territory — where Cherokee families who survived the removal had settled in the late 1830s and 1840s. The displacement of 1838–1839 was not a distant historical fact for the Mankiller family. It was the reason they were in Oklahoma rather than Tennessee or Georgia; it was the foundational event of their American experience.

This connection was not abstract for Mankiller. In her memoir and in public speeches, she returned repeatedly to the Trail of Tears as the original rupture — the wound from which the Cherokee Nation had been recovering ever since. Everything that came after — the allotment era, the dissolution of tribal government, the relocation programs — repeated in different forms the central dynamic of the Trail of Tears: federal power deployed to break the connection between the Cherokee people and their land, their government, and their future.

## Part VIII: Rebuilding in Indian Territory

### Arrival and Reconstitution

The Cherokee who survived the Trail of Tears arrived in Indian Territory — present-day eastern Oklahoma — in a state of physical exhaustion and political crisis. The several thousand Cherokee who had earlier accepted removal and moved west under the terms of earlier treaties (the Old Settlers or Western Cherokee) had established their own government in the territory. The arriving Eastern Cherokee, led by John Ross, overwhelmed this earlier population in numbers. The question of who governed whom — the larger Eastern faction or the earlier-established Western government — erupted into violence.

From 1839 to 1846, the Cherokee Nation experienced a period of factional conflict that included assassinations, reprisals, and civil war-level violence. Major Ridge, John Ridge (his son), and Elias Boudinot — the primary signers of the fraudulent Treaty of New Echota — were killed by members of the Ross faction shortly after the main detachments arrived in Indian Territory. (The Cherokee Constitution included a provision imposing the death penalty on any Cherokee who signed away tribal lands without authority.) The violence escalated until federal intervention produced a new unified treaty in 1846.

Despite the factional conflict, the Cherokee demonstrated extraordinary capacity for institutional recovery. By 1839, a new Cherokee Constitution had been adopted, merging the Eastern and Western Cherokee governments into a unified nation with its capital at Tahlequah, in present-day northeastern Oklahoma. Schools were established. The first public school system west of the Mississippi was organized by the Cherokee Nation, with 18 schools operating by the 1840s and two institutions of higher education — Cherokee Male Seminary and Cherokee Female Seminary — opened in 1851.

The *Cherokee Phoenix* was revived as the *Cherokee Advocate*. The syllabary and bilingual print culture that Sequoyah had made possible continued to sustain community literacy and cultural continuity. The Cherokee Nation negotiated trade relationships, established farming operations, and rebuilt an economy in the new territory.

This period of recovery — the three decades between removal and the Civil War — demonstrated something that would be central to Mankiller's governing philosophy 150 years later: Cherokee people, given the minimum conditions of self-governance and territorial security, were capable of building and sustaining complex institutions without federal supervision or direction. The capacity was never the question. The question was always whether the federal government would allow it.

### The Civil War and Its Aftermath

The American Civil War devastated the Cherokee Nation as badly as any white community in the South. The Cherokee were internally divided: Principal Chief John Ross, a slaveholder himself despite his lifelong fight for Cherokee sovereignty, attempted neutrality and then allied with the Union. Stand Watie, his longtime political opponent and the last Confederate general to surrender in the Civil War (June 23, 1865), led a Confederate Cherokee regiment. The division produced a Cherokee civil war within the larger national conflict: Cherokee fought Cherokee, towns were burned, families were destroyed.

By the war's end in 1865, an estimated one-third of the Cherokee population had died — from combat, disease, and the displacement of tens of thousands of refugees fleeing the violence. Much of the infrastructure built since removal — schools, farms, mills, government buildings — lay in ruins.

The federal government, despite the Union victory, treated the Cherokee and other Five Civilized Tribes as conquered enemies, requiring new treaties in 1866 that imposed severe penalties. The treaties required the Cherokee to cede the western portion of their Indian Territory lands (to be settled by other removed tribes from elsewhere), to grant citizenship to formerly enslaved people held by Cherokee, and to accept railroad rights-of-way through their territory. The railroad concessions would prove particularly consequential: the railroads brought tens of thousands of white settlers into what had been a protected Indigenous territory.

## Part IX: Allotment and Dissolution — 1887–1907

### The Dawes Act of 1887

The General Allotment Act of 1887, known as the Dawes Act for its sponsor Senator Henry Dawes of Massachusetts, represents one of the most destructive federal Indian policies in American history. The policy was presented by its advocates as civilizing reform: by converting communal tribal landholdings to individual allotments, reformers argued, Native Americans would develop the individualist work ethic and property consciousness necessary for assimilation into American society. The actual effect was the systematic transfer of approximately 90 million acres of tribal land to non-Native ownership within 50 years.

The mechanism was straightforward. Each tribal member would receive an individual allotment — 160 acres for heads of households, smaller parcels for others. Land not allotted after the entire tribal roll had been assigned individual parcels was declared "surplus" and opened to non-Indian homesteading. The tribal government's legal authority over the territory would be dissolved; allottees would become subject to state and federal law as individuals rather than members of a sovereign nation.

The Five Civilized Tribes were initially excluded from the Dawes Act, due partly to the strength of their treaty rights and partly to the organized political resistance of their governments. This exemption lasted until 1893, when Congress passed legislation creating the Dawes Commission specifically to negotiate allotment with the Five Tribes. When the tribes refused to negotiate, Congress passed the Curtis Act of 1898, which imposed allotment without their consent.

### The Curtis Act of 1898

The Curtis Act was a direct legislative assault on Five Tribes sovereignty. It dissolved tribal courts and governments, voided tribal laws, required the compilation of official tribal rolls (the Dawes Rolls) as the basis for allotment, and made individual allotments mandatory regardless of tribal refusal. The act effectively ended the political independence the Cherokee had maintained in Indian Territory for 60 years.

The Dawes Rolls — the official enrollment of tribal members conducted from 1898 to 1914 — determined who received allotments and who was recognized as a tribal member. The enrollment process was imperfect, politically contested, and shaped by federal assumptions about Cherokee identity. Full-blood Cherokee who did not speak English and who lived in traditional communities were often difficult to enroll; mixed-heritage families with connections to the federal apparatus were better positioned. The Dawes Rolls became the definitive legal document of Cherokee identity for the next century, and their imperfections have generated ongoing disputes about tribal citizenship ever since.

### The Burke Act of 1906

The Burke Act of 1906 amended the allotment framework in ways that further restricted tribal sovereignty and allottee rights. The act extended the trust period — during which allotted land was held in trust by the federal government and could not be sold — while simultaneously allowing the Secretary of the Interior to declare individual allottees "competent" to manage their own affairs, at which point their land became immediately taxable and alienable.

In practice, the declaration of competency was used systematically to strip land from Native allottees who did not understand the implications or who were targeted by unscrupulous speculators. Oil and gas discoveries across Indian Territory — including on allotted Cherokee land in what is now northeastern Oklahoma — created enormous pressure from land companies, attorneys, and individual speculators to acquire allotted lands by any available means. Court-appointed guardians for minor allottees defrauded their wards. Fraud was pervasive, documented, and largely ignored by federal authorities.

The Mankiller family's allotment in Adair County was in this context: land held under the allotment framework, subject to the full weight of the post-Curtis Act legal regime, economically marginal and politically precarious.

### Oklahoma Statehood — 1907

On November 16, 1907, Oklahoma was admitted to the Union as the 46th state. Statehood incorporated Indian Territory — which had been the legislatively protected homeland of the Five Civilized Tribes — into a state government dominated by white settlers and oil interests. The Cherokee Nation, whose government had already been effectively dissolved by the Curtis Act, ceased to exist as a functioning political entity for most practical purposes.

Oklahoma statehood stripped the Cherokee of the remaining formal protections that the Indian Territory framework had provided. Cherokee people became Oklahoma citizens subject to state law. The territory that the federal government had promised to the Cherokee "as long as the grass grows and the rivers run" in the removal treaties of the 1830s had been absorbed into a state before those same people's grandchildren had fully grown old.

The impact on Cherokee identity was complex. Many Cherokee assimilated — their children attended Oklahoma public schools, took English names, entered the mainstream economy, and identified primarily as Oklahomans. Others maintained Cherokee language and culture in rural communities of eastern Oklahoma, in the rugged hill country around Tahlequah and Stilwell and Muskogee, with varying degrees of connection to the formal structures of tribal government that had been dissolved.

Wilma Mankiller was born into this Oklahoma — a state where Cherokee people had lived for 70 years since the Trail of Tears, where the institutions that had once made Cherokee self-governance a reality had been legislated out of existence, and where Cherokee identity was maintained primarily through family, community, and the persistence of the syllabary and language in older generations.

## Part X: Termination, Relocation, and the Mankiller Family's Second Removal

### Federal Termination Policy — 1953

The mid-twentieth century brought another wave of assimilationist federal policy, this time under the rubric of "termination." House Concurrent Resolution 108, passed in 1953, declared it the policy of the United States to terminate federal recognition of tribal governments and to make Native Americans fully subject to state law and federal civil rights law as individuals, without special tribal status. The policy was motivated by Cold War anti-communism (communal land ownership seemed suspiciously socialist to some legislators), fiscal conservatism, and the enduring assimilationist impulse.

Between 1953 and 1968, more than 100 tribes were terminated — their federal recognition ended, their reservations dissolved, their tribal assets liquidated. The results were consistently catastrophic: poverty, loss of health care, loss of educational services, loss of land. The Menominee of Wisconsin, terminated in 1954, experienced such severe deterioration in community welfare that their case became a symbol of termination's failure and drove the eventual reversal of the policy.

The Cherokee Nation — whose formal government had already been dissolved by the Curtis Act — was in an ambiguous position during the termination era. The office of Principal Chief still existed, but Congress had given the President of the United States the authority to appoint the Principal Chief, effectively making the Cherokee government an arm of federal Indian administration rather than an expression of Cherokee self-determination. The appointed chiefs of this era were not subject to Cherokee electoral accountability.

### The Indian Relocation Act of 1956

The Indian Relocation Act of 1956 — and the relocation programs that preceded and surrounded it, including the 1952 Voluntary Relocation Program — represented a different mechanism of assimilation than termination. Where termination dissolved tribal governments, relocation moved individual Native people off reservations and allotments into urban areas, providing one-way bus tickets, a few weeks of job placement assistance, and minimal support. The explicit goal was to move Native Americans into the mainstream economy; the implicit goal was to break the connection between Native people and their communities.

Between 1952 and 1972, an estimated 100,000 Native Americans were relocated from their home communities to cities including Chicago, Los Angeles, Denver, Dallas, San Jose, and San Francisco. Some returned home when the relocation promised failed to materialize. Others stayed and built new urban communities, including the community organizations that would eventually fuel the Red Power movement of the late 1960s and early 1970s.

In 1956, the Mankiller family — Charley Mankiller, his wife Irene Sitton Mankiller, and their eleven children, including ten-year-old Wilma — was relocated under this program from their allotment in Adair County, Oklahoma, to San Francisco. They moved from a rural Cherokee community with no running water or electricity to the housing projects of Hunter's Point in one of America's most expensive cities.

Wilma Mankiller described the relocation in her memoir *Mankiller: A Chief and Her People* (1993) as a second Trail of Tears — a term that was not hyperbolic for her but analytical. The federal government had forced her ancestors from their ancestral homeland in the 1830s. Now, a century later, the federal government had moved her family from their Oklahoma homeland to California with promises of economic opportunity that did not materialize and no plan for what happened if the urban experiment failed. The mechanisms differed; the structure was the same: Cherokee people displaced from their land and community by federal power, with their futures managed by bureaucratic decision-makers in Washington who knew nothing about their lives.

### Asgaya-dihi: The Etymology of a Name

The surname Mankiller requires explicit attention because it has been subject to misunderstanding and because its correct meaning is central to understanding Mankiller family identity.

*Asgaya-dihi* is an old Cherokee military title. The word breaks down in Cherokee as: *asgaya* (man, male person) and *dihi* (a suffix denoting a skilled practitioner, one who is capable or accomplished in a specific domain). The literal rendering is something like "skillful man" or "capable fighter" — more specifically, a man who had achieved recognized excellence in warfare. It was a rank of honor, a title earned through demonstrated military capability and leadership.

The title was conferred on a warrior who had proven himself in battle and had demonstrated the qualities — courage, tactical judgment, care for his warriors — that earned collective trust. It was not a description of violence for its own sake. In the hierarchical military structure of Cherokee society, Asgaya-dihi was a rank equivalent to what European military traditions would call a captain or major — a field commander of recognized competence.

The title Mankiller was borne by several noted Cherokee leaders and warriors in the eighteenth century, including a prominent chief known in historical records as the Mankiller of Tellico, who was active in the 1750s and 1760s. The surname passed through Wilma Mankiller's paternal line as a hereditary surname — a common practice after Cherokee contact with European naming conventions — but its military-honorific origin remained.

Wilma Mankiller spoke frequently about her surname's meaning because she encountered regular surprise and occasionally discomfort at the name. She used these encounters as pedagogical opportunities: the name is not what it appears to be in English, and the gap between its appearance and its meaning is itself a lesson about the danger of interpreting Cherokee culture through English-language assumptions. A title of honor, filtered through the lens of another language, looks like a threat. This perceptual distortion, she would note, was an apt metaphor for much of what Cherokee history looked like when filtered through federal government assumptions.

## Part XI: The Modern Reconstitution and the Path to Self-Governance

### The Red Power Movement and Urban Indian Identity

The relocation policy that brought Wilma Mankiller's family to San Francisco also concentrated tens of thousands of Native Americans in cities across the country, creating conditions for a new kind of pan-Indian political organization. The American Indian Movement (AIM), founded in Minneapolis in 1968, combined urban Indian experience with traditional cultural values and channeled them into direct-action politics. The National Indian Youth Council, founded in 1961, was already pushing a more assertive politics of tribal sovereignty.

The occupation of Alcatraz Island, which began on November 20, 1969, and lasted until June 11, 1971, became the defining event of this movement for Wilma Mankiller. A group of Native American activists, acting under the defunct Sioux treaty of 1868 (which granted Indians the right to claim unused federal land), occupied the abandoned federal prison on Alcatraz Island in San Francisco Bay. They demanded that the island be converted into a Native American cultural center and university.

The occupation ultimately failed in its specific demands — the activists were removed without achieving the center — but it succeeded in something larger. It demonstrated that Native Americans could organize, sustain a sustained political action, attract national media attention, and articulate a sovereignty-based critique of federal Indian policy that resonated far beyond Indian communities. For Mankiller, then in her early twenties and living in the Bay Area, Alcatraz was a political education. She raised funds, gathered supplies, and understood for the first time that the struggle of her people was ongoing, collective, and potentially winnable.

### The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975

The political pressure generated by the Red Power movement, combined with growing congressional recognition that termination had been a policy failure, produced the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 — the most significant shift in federal Indian policy since the New Deal. The act allowed tribal governments to contract directly with the federal government to administer programs — health, education, social services — that had previously been administered by the Bureau of Indian Affairs.

Self-determination contracting meant that a tribal government could take over the operation of a BIA school or Indian Health Service clinic, run it according to tribal priorities and community knowledge, and receive the federal funding that would otherwise have gone to the federal agency. The theory was that tribal governments, closer to their communities and accountable to their members, would run programs more effectively than distant federal agencies. In practice, where self-determination was implemented with adequate resources and genuine federal commitment, this proved correct.

The 1975 act was the legislative foundation for everything Mankiller would build as Principal Chief. When she negotiated the first tribal self-governance agreements allowing the Cherokee Nation to manage its own federal funding directly — bypassing BIA intermediaries entirely — she was working in the framework the 1975 act had created. The Cherokee Nation's ability to open health clinics, expand educational programs, and build community infrastructure under Mankiller depended on the self-determination framework that the Red Power movement's political pressure had helped produce.

### Cherokee Nation Government's Modern Reconstitution

The Cherokee Nation's formal government had been moribund since the Curtis Act of 1898 and Oklahoma statehood in 1907. The Principal Chief's office had been maintained as a federal appointment, but the elected tribal council and the full apparatus of constitutional governance had ceased to function. The Cherokee people in Oklahoma maintained community life through churches, schools, the language, and cultural practices — but they did not have a functioning elected government.

The reconstitution of the Cherokee Nation's elected government occurred gradually in the 1970s. W.W. Keeler, who served as the federally appointed Principal Chief from 1949 to 1971, worked to reestablish governance structures. In 1971, Congress passed legislation allowing the Five Civilized Tribes to elect their own principal chiefs for the first time since statehood. The Cherokee held elections. A new constitutional convention was convened in 1975 — the same year as the Self-Determination Act — and a new Cherokee Constitution was ratified in 1976.

The 1976 Cherokee Constitution reestablished the Tribal Council as an elected body, defined citizenship criteria, and created the governmental structure that Mankiller would work within. When she began her career at the Cherokee Nation in 1977 — as a volunteer organizer and then as an economic stimulus coordinator — the institution she joined was only a year old in its reconstituted form. The government she would eventually lead was still building the basic infrastructure of self-governance.

### The World Mankiller Entered in 1977

When Wilma Mankiller returned to Oklahoma in 1977 as a single mother of two daughters, driving from San Francisco with what she could fit in her car, she arrived in a world shaped by every layer of history traced in this module.

The Cherokee people she came home to were survivors of the Trail of Tears, of allotment, of the dissolution of tribal government, of the termination era, and of the relocation programs that had scattered families like her own across American cities. They were also survivors of recovery: they had rebuilt institutions three times — after the Trail of Tears, after the Civil War, and after Oklahoma statehood — and they were in the early stages of building again.

The constitutional government was two years old. The Self-Determination Act had just created new possibilities. Federal policy had shifted, in theory, from termination to self-determination. But the shift was incomplete, the resources inadequate, and the legacy of 150 years of dispossession — in land, in institutional capacity, in community health, in economic infrastructure — was not resolved by a change in federal policy language.

Rural Cherokee communities in eastern Oklahoma faced poverty rates among the highest in the state. Educational attainment lagged. Health outcomes — life expectancy, infant mortality, rates of diabetes and heart disease — reflected the combined effects of poverty, inadequate health infrastructure, and the physical and psychological stresses of historical trauma. The Cherokee syllabary, which Sequoyah had invented and which had once made the Cherokee the most literate people in North America, was now maintained by a diminishing number of fluent elders.

Into this world, Mankiller brought what she had learned in San Francisco: that communities could organize, that self-help projects could succeed where government programs had failed, that Cherokee people had not lost the capacity for collective action that had sustained them through every previous crisis. The Bell water project — in which she organized a community of Cherokee residents to lay 16 miles of water pipe with their own labor — was her proof of concept. It replicated, in microcosm, the model by which the Cherokee Nation had always survived: not waiting for external rescue, but building the infrastructure of its own future with its own hands.

## Conclusion: History as Load-Bearing Structure

The history traced in this module is not background to Wilma Mankiller's leadership. It is the load-bearing structure of that leadership. Every decision she made as Principal Chief was made in conversation with this history.

When she fought to expand Cherokee health clinics, she was responding to the 150 years in which Cherokee health had been undermined by displacement, poverty, and inadequate federal services. When she negotiated self-governance compacts that gave the Cherokee Nation direct control over federal funding, she was recovering the sovereignty that the Curtis Act and Oklahoma statehood had dissolved. When she funded the Institute for Cherokee Literacy, she was protecting what Sequoyah had built — the writing system that had preserved Cherokee culture through every assault on it. When she refused to acknowledge any inherent tension between Cherokee tradition and Cherokee modernization, she was drawing on the precedent of the constitutional period, when the Cherokee Nation had demonstrated that a people could build universities and a free press and a bicameral legislature while remaining distinctly Cherokee.

The Trail of Tears killed approximately a quarter of the Cherokee people in a forced march from their homeland. The Curtis Act dissolved the government they had rebuilt. The relocation program took Mankiller's own family from Oklahoma to San Francisco in a displacement that echoed the original removal. Each of these events narrowed the space in which Cherokee self-determination could operate. Mankiller's decade as Principal Chief was dedicated to widening that space — recovering, in practice, the sovereignty that the Cherokee Constitution of 1827 and the Worcester v. Georgia decision of 1832 had declared in theory.

She was building forward from a specific history. You cannot understand what she built without understanding what preceded it.
