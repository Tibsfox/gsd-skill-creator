November 18, 1945 — April 6, 2010

Wilma Pearl Mankiller

First Woman Elected Principal Chief of the Cherokee Nation
Cherokee Nation · Adair County, Oklahoma
This research is dedicated to her memory on the anniversary of her passing

"The most fulfilled people are the ones who get up every morning and stand for something larger than themselves. They are the people who care about others, who will extend a helping hand to someone in need or will speak up about an injustice when they see it."

— Wilma Mankiller

Wilma Mankiller was born at Hastings Indian Hospital in Tahlequah, Oklahoma, the sixth of eleven children. Her father, Charley Mankiller, was full-blood Cherokee. Her paternal ancestors walked the Trail of Tears from Tennessee to Indian Territory in the 1830s. The surname Mankiller is an old Cherokee military title — Asgaya-dihi — a rank of honor, not a description of violence.

At eleven, her family was relocated to San Francisco under the federal government's Indian Relocation Act of 1956 — a program designed to move Native Americans from reservations into cities, ostensibly for economic opportunity, effectively for cultural assimilation. The family went from a rural allotment in Adair County with no running water to the housing projects of Hunters Point. It was a displacement within a displacement, echoing the Trail of Tears a century later.

The Making of a Leader

In San Francisco, Mankiller found activism. The Occupation of Alcatraz in 1969 — when Native American activists reclaimed the abandoned federal prison island for 19 months — became her political education. She raised funds, gathered blankets and food, and learned organizing. She worked with the Pit River Tribe on land and compensation claims. She founded the East Oakland Native American Youth Center.

In 1977, she returned to Oklahoma as a single mother of two daughters, Felicia and Gina, with no job and no home. She lived in her car by a stream while searching for work. She found it at the Cherokee Nation as an economic stimulus coordinator — and from that starting point, she built a career that would reshape tribal governance in America.

Bell, Oklahoma

Her signature early project was in Bell, a small community in eastern Oklahoma where families had no running water. Rather than hiring outside contractors, Mankiller organized the residents themselves to lay 16 miles of water pipe over 14 months. The residents donated their own labor. The project delivered clean water — but more importantly, it proved a model: communities building their own infrastructure, owning their own transformation.

This is the pattern she would replicate across the Cherokee Nation for the next decade: self-determination through self-work, sovereignty through service.

Principal Chief

  • 1983 Elected Deputy Principal Chief — first woman to hold the position. Won the runoff by absentee vote.
  • 1985 Sworn in as Principal Chief on December 5 when Chief Ross Swimmer left to lead the Bureau of Indian Affairs. First woman to lead a major Native American tribal nation.
  • 1987 Elected Principal Chief in her own right with a majority in the runoff.
  • 1991 Re-elected with 83% of the vote — the largest margin in Cherokee Nation election history.
  • 1995 Did not seek a third full term due to a lymphoma diagnosis.

What She Built

Under Mankiller's leadership, the Cherokee Nation opened three rural health centers, expanded Head Start early childhood programs, established mobile eye-care clinics and ambulance services, created adult education and job training programs, and negotiated the first tribal self-governance agreements that allowed the Cherokee Nation to manage its own federal funds directly rather than through Bureau of Indian Affairs intermediaries.

She changed the Tribal Council election system from at-large voting — which favored urban Cherokee — to district-based representation, ensuring rural communities had a voice. She revived Sequoyah High School. She created the Institute for Cherokee Literacy to preserve the Cherokee syllabary. She negotiated a tax-sharing compact with the state of Oklahoma.

She did all of this while surviving a near-fatal car accident that required 17 operations, myasthenia gravis, two kidney transplants (from her brother Don in 1990 and her niece Virlee in 1998), lymphoma, and breast cancer.

Recognition

In January 1998, President Bill Clinton awarded Wilma Mankiller the Presidential Medal of Freedom — the highest civilian honor in the United States. She was inducted into the National Women's Hall of Fame in 1993. She received 14 honorary doctorates, including from Yale, Dartmouth, and Smith College. In 2022, her likeness appeared on the United States quarter-dollar coin as part of the American Women Quarters Program, alongside Maya Angelou, Sally Ride, Adelina Otero-Warren, and Anna May Wong.

"I want to be remembered as the person who helped us restore faith in ourselves."

— Wilma Mankiller
