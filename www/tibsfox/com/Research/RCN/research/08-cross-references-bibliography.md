# Cross-References and Bibliography

## Introduction

The RCN (Raccoons) project does not exist in isolation. Raccoons are embedded in ecological, cultural, and management contexts that connect to nearly every other cluster in the PNW Research Series. This document maps those connections, establishing the knowledge nodes that link raccoon research to the broader network, and provides a comprehensive bibliography organized by topic.

---

## Cross-References to Other Research Projects

### MAM --- Mammals

The MAM project provides the broader mammalian context for raccoon biology. Key intersections:

- **Carnivore evolution:** Raccoons as members of Carnivora, with connections to the broader carnivore phylogeny covered in MAM.
- **PNW mammal community:** Raccoon interactions with other PNW mammals (coyotes, black bears, river otters, bobcats, cougars, beavers, Douglas squirrels) are ecological relationships documented across both projects.
- **Mesocarnivore ecology:** Raccoons as the archetypal PNW mesocarnivore, occupying the trophic level between large predators and small mammals. The mesocarnivore release hypothesis (elevated mesocarnivore populations in the absence of large predators) applies directly to urban raccoon ecology.
- **Sensory biology:** The raccoon's tactile specialization can be contextualized against the sensory adaptations of other PNW mammals (echolocation in bats, olfaction in bears, vibrissae in river otters).
- **Winter ecology:** Raccoon torpor and winter survival strategies compared with true hibernation in ground squirrels and bears, and winter-active strategies in weasels and otters.

### ECO --- Ecology

The ECO project provides the ecosystem-level framework within which raccoon ecology operates:

- **Riparian ecology:** Raccoons as riparian obligates, dependent on the health of stream and river corridors that are a central focus of ECO research.
- **Marine-terrestrial nutrient transfer:** The salmon-raccoon-forest nutrient pathway is a specific instance of the marine-derived nutrient cycles documented in ECO.
- **Trophic cascades:** Raccoon predation effects on bird, turtle, and invertebrate populations as examples of mesocarnivore-driven trophic dynamics.
- **Urban ecology:** The raccoon as an indicator species for urban ecosystem health and a model organism for studying wildlife adaptation to anthropogenic landscapes.
- **Invasive species ecology:** Himalayan blackberry as a raccoon food subsidy; raccoons as invasive species in Japan and Europe, with lessons for invasion biology principles in ECO.
- **Climate change ecology:** Raccoon range expansion, phenological shifts, and altered population dynamics as case studies in climate-driven ecological change.

### AVI --- Avian Research

Raccoon-avian interactions are among the most ecologically significant and conservation-relevant aspects of raccoon ecology:

- **Nest predation:** Raccoons as major nest predators of ground-nesting birds, cavity nesters, and colonial waterbirds documented in AVI.
- **Marbled Murrelet:** Raccoon predation on this ESA-threatened seabird, with the interaction amplified by old-growth forest fragmentation --- a key conservation nexus linking RCN and AVI.
- **Band-tailed Pigeon:** Raccoon predation as a contributing factor to population decline of this PNW-characteristic species.
- **Waterfowl:** Raccoon predation on duck and goose nests, particularly in managed wetland habitats.
- **Raptors as raccoon predators:** Great horned owls as kit predators; the raccoon-owl predator-prey relationship as documented in AVI raptor research.
- **Nest box management:** Raccoon predation as a design consideration for nest box programs (bluebirds, wood ducks, kestrels) covered in AVI.

### FWL --- Fowl

The FWL project addresses domestic poultry, and raccoons are directly relevant:

- **Primary predator [FWL-06]:** Raccoons are the number-one chicken predator in many regions, making raccoon-proofing a central concern of FWL coop design and husbandry research. FWL-06 (Husbandry Guide) devotes dedicated sections to raccoon-specific predator defense, including hardware cloth specifications, latch designs, and PNW predator density data.
- **Comprehensive predator defense [FWL guides/predator-defense-pnw]:** The FWL Predator Defense Guide profiles raccoons as the #1 PNW poultry predator, detailing attack patterns, hardware cloth requirements, two-step latch specifications, and the nightly lockup routine. Directly references RCN-02 (intelligence), RCN-04 (urban density data), and RCN deep-dives/raccoon-proofing.
- **Latch and closure design [FWL-06]:** Raccoon dexterity as the benchmark for predator-proof latch specifications. If a raccoon can open it, the design fails. FWL-06 specifies two-step latches, carabiner clips, and padlocks as raccoon-resistant closure types.
- **Nocturnal predation patterns [RCN-02, RCN-04]:** Raccoon activity patterns informing FWL recommendations for when coops should be secured. Automatic coop doors closing at dusk are recommended throughout FWL-06.
- **Predator deterrence:** Effectiveness of lighting, fencing, guardian animals, and other deterrents against raccoon predation of poultry. See also the PNW-specific hawk and raccoon defense sections in FWL-06 and FWL-08.

### SPS --- Species Pairings

The SPS project examines ecological relationships between paired species:

- **Raccoon-coyote:** Predator-prey and competitive interactions; mesocarnivore release dynamics when coyotes are absent.
- **Raccoon-salmon:** Consumer-resource relationship with marine-derived nutrient transport implications.
- **Raccoon-beaver:** Commensal relationship where beaver pond engineering creates raccoon foraging habitat.
- **Raccoon-Western Pond Turtle:** Predator-prey relationship with direct conservation management implications.
- **Raccoon-Marbled Murrelet:** Predator-prey relationship amplified by habitat fragmentation.
- **Raccoon-Baylisascaris:** Host-parasite coevolution with implications for other species and public health.

### AWF --- Wildlife

The AWF project covers wildlife management and conservation:

- **Wildlife damage management:** Raccoon conflict resolution, exclusion techniques, and the ineffectiveness of relocation.
- **Disease surveillance:** CDV, Baylisascaris, leptospirosis monitoring in raccoon populations. See also [RCN deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease] for detailed disease profiles, latrine cleanup protocols, and PNW-specific rabies risk assessment.
- **Furbearer management:** Harvest regulations, population monitoring, and the economics of raccoon trapping.
- **Wildlife rehabilitation:** Raccoon kit rehabilitation protocols, release criteria, and outcomes. See also [RCN deep-dives/baby-raccoons] for the complete orphan response guide including PNW rehabilitator contacts and the 24-hour rule.
- **Human-wildlife coexistence:** The raccoon as a model for urban wildlife coexistence research and public education.

### GDN --- Garden

The GDN project addresses garden ecology, and raccoons are frequent garden visitors:

- **Garden pest:** Raccoon damage to garden crops (corn, tomatoes, berries, melons) and lawns (grub digging).
- **Compost ecology:** Raccoons as compost bin raiders and the design implications for garden composting systems.
- **Beneficial roles:** Raccoon consumption of garden pest insects (grubs, slugs) as a limited positive contribution.
- **Garden design for coexistence:** Fencing, netting, and plant selection strategies that reduce raccoon conflict in garden settings.

### Additional Cross-References

- **INF (Infrastructure):** Raccoon impacts on electrical systems, plumbing, and building structures; urban infrastructure as raccoon habitat.
- **ENR (Energy):** Raccoon-caused power outages; wildlife-infrastructure conflict at electrical substations and distribution systems.
- **CRE (Creative):** Raccoon imagery in PNW art, photography, and design; the raccoon as muse and cultural symbol.
- **MUS (Music):** Raccoons in song and musical storytelling, particularly in folk and country traditions.
- **BRC (Broadcasting):** Wildlife documentary coverage of raccoons; the "nature cam" phenomenon (raccoon den cameras and urban wildlife streaming).
- **SCI (Science):** Raccoon neuroscience (Herculano-Houzel), behavioral ecology research methodology, citizen science raccoon monitoring.
- **AI (AI & Computation):** Raccoon problem-solving as a benchmark for artificial intelligence research; animal cognition studies informing AI design.

## Knowledge Nodes

The following knowledge nodes represent conceptual intersections where raccoon research contributes to or draws from the broader research network:

### Node: Mesocarnivore Ecology
- **Projects:** RCN, MAM, ECO, AVI, SPS
- **Concept:** The ecological role of mid-sized predators in structuring communities, with raccoons as the exemplar PNW mesocarnivore.
- **Key questions:** How does mesocarnivore density affect prey communities? What happens when top predator removal causes mesocarnivore release? How do urban subsidies alter mesocarnivore population dynamics?

### Node: Marine-Terrestrial Nutrient Transfer
- **Projects:** RCN, ECO, SPS
- **Concept:** The movement of marine-derived nutrients into terrestrial ecosystems via salmon and their consumers, with raccoons as one vector.
- **Key questions:** What fraction of marine-derived nutrient transport is attributable to raccoons vs. bears vs. eagles? How does raccoon behavior (carcass dragging distance, selective feeding) affect the spatial distribution of nutrient deposition?

### Node: Urban Wildlife Adaptation
- **Projects:** RCN, ECO, MAM, AWF, INF
- **Concept:** The behavioral, demographic, and ecological changes that occur when wildlife colonizes urban environments.
- **Key questions:** Is urban adaptation reversible? Are urban raccoon populations genetically differentiating from rural populations? What role does social learning play in the spread of urban foraging innovations?

### Node: Nest Predation and Conservation
- **Projects:** RCN, AVI, SPS, AWF
- **Concept:** The impact of raccoon nest predation on bird and turtle populations, particularly species of conservation concern.
- **Key questions:** At what raccoon density does nest predation become unsustainable for prey populations? Can habitat management (reducing edge effects, maintaining corridor connectivity) reduce raccoon access to sensitive nesting areas?

### Node: Invasive Species Ecology
- **Projects:** RCN, ECO
- **Concept:** Raccoons as invasive species in Japan, Europe, and the Caribbean, and the lessons these invasions provide for invasion biology.
- **Key questions:** What traits predict invasion success? How does the absence of co-evolved predators and parasites affect invasive raccoon population dynamics? Can lessons from North American raccoon management inform management in invaded ranges?

### Node: Animal Cognition
- **Projects:** RCN, SCI, AI
- **Concept:** Raccoon intelligence as a case study in non-primate cognitive evolution, with implications for understanding the neural basis of behavioral flexibility.
- **Key questions:** What is the relationship between cortical neuron count and behavioral flexibility? How do raccoons compare with corvids, primates, and cetaceans on standardized cognitive tests? Can raccoon problem-solving strategies inform artificial intelligence approaches to novel problem domains?

### Node: Human-Wildlife Conflict Resolution
- **Projects:** RCN, AWF, FWL, GDN, INF
- **Concept:** Practical strategies for reducing negative interactions between raccoons and human activities, from poultry protection to building exclusion.
- **Key questions:** What exclusion methods are most cost-effective? Why does relocation fail? How does public perception of raccoons affect willingness to implement coexistence strategies?

### Key Texts

The following works are recommended as comprehensive references for raccoon biology and ecology:

**Zeveloff, S.I. (2002). *Raccoons: A Natural History*. Smithsonian Institution Press.**
The most comprehensive single-volume treatment of raccoon biology, covering taxonomy, evolution, behavior, ecology, and cultural history. Essential reading for anyone engaged in raccoon research.

**Gehrt, S.D., Riley, S.P.D., and Cypher, B.L. (eds.) (2010). *Urban Carnivores: Ecology, Conflict, and Conservation*. Johns Hopkins University Press.**
The definitive reference for urban mesocarnivore ecology, with substantial raccoon-specific content and a broader framework for understanding wildlife urbanization.

**Herculano-Houzel, S. (2016). *The Human Advantage: A New Understanding of How Our Brain Became Remarkable*. MIT Press.**
While not raccoon-specific, this work provides the theoretical framework for understanding the significance of the raccoon's cortical neuron count and its implications for cognitive capacity.

**Kazacos, K.R. (2016). "*Baylisascaris* larva migrans." USGS Circular 1412.**
The authoritative reference on raccoon roundworm biology, epidemiology, and public health significance.

## Closing Note

The raccoon is a connector. In ecology, it connects marine and terrestrial nutrient cycles, aquatic and arboreal habitats, natural and urban landscapes. In the research network, it connects projects spanning mammalogy, ecology, ornithology, agriculture, infrastructure, and culture. In the world beyond research, it connects the wild and the domestic, the ancient and the modern, the forest and the city.

This bibliography and cross-reference document is not exhaustive. The raccoon literature is vast and growing, reflecting the species' relevance to ecological research, wildlife management, public health, urban planning, and cultural studies. The references provided here are a foundation --- a starting point for deeper investigation of any of the themes explored in this project.
