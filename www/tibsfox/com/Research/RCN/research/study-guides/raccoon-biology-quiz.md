## How to Use This Guide

This is a self-assessment study guide. Read each question carefully, commit to an answer, and then check the detailed explanation below. The explanations are designed to teach, not just to grade --- each one connects to the broader biology, ecology, and cultural history covered in the RCN project documents.

**Scoring:**
- 22-25 correct: Procyonid expert. You know raccoons at a professional level.
- 18-21 correct: Advanced naturalist. Strong foundation with a few gaps to fill.
- 13-17 correct: Intermediate. You know the basics; the details will surprise you.
- 8-12 correct: Beginner. Welcome --- you are about to learn a lot.
- Below 8: Start with [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy] and read through the series.

## Answer Key

### Section A: True or False

**1. FALSE.** Raccoons are NOT members of Ursidae. They belong to the family Procyonidae. The confusion is historical --- Linnaeus originally classified the raccoon as *Ursus lotor* ("washing bear") in 1758, placing it with the bears. The raccoon was later moved to its own genus *Procyon* and family Procyonidae. Molecular phylogenetics places Procyonidae as the sister group to Mustelidae (weasels, otters, badgers) within the superfamily Musteloidea --- closer to weasels than to bears.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Taxonomic Position]*

**2. FALSE (mostly).** The translation is close but not quite right. *Procyon* comes from Greek *pro* (before) + *kyon* (dog), meaning "before the dog" --- a reference to the star Procyon in Canis Minor, which rises before Sirius (the Dog Star). *Lotor* is Latin for "washer." So the full name means "the washer that comes before the dog," a reference to the star and the food-handling behavior, not "washing dog." The distinction matters because the genus name has nothing to do with the animal's relationship to dogs --- it is an astronomical reference.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Genus Procyon]*

**3. TRUE.** Like all placental mammals, raccoons use the XX/XY sex determination system. Females are XX, males are XY. This is worth noting because not all vertebrates use this system --- birds, for instance, use ZW/ZZ (where females are the heterogametic sex). See [FWL study-guides/genetics-study-guide] for a comparison with the avian ZW system.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy]*

**4. FALSE.** This is one of the most persistent myths in mammalogy. The behavior called "washing" (*lotor* literally means washer) is actually a tactile exploration behavior. Raccoons manipulate food items in water because wetting their forepaws dramatically increases tactile sensitivity --- the thin, specialized skin of the forepaw transmits more sensory information when wet. Captive raccoons perform the same manipulation behavior even without water. The behavior is about *feeling* the food, not cleaning it. In the wild, raccoons frequently eat food that is already in water (crayfish, frogs, mussels) and equally frequently eat food that is dry (berries, bird eggs, garbage) without seeking water first.

*See: [RCN-02 Intelligence & Behavior --- Tactile Perception]; [deep-dives/hands-and-intelligence]*

**5. FALSE.** Raccoons do not hibernate. They enter a state of **torpor** during extended cold periods --- metabolic rate decreases, activity drops dramatically, and they may remain in their dens for days or weeks during severe winter weather. But they do not undergo true hibernation as bears, ground squirrels, and bats do. Their body temperature drops only a few degrees (unlike true hibernators, whose temperature may drop to near-freezing), and they arouse periodically to forage on mild nights throughout the winter.

*See: [RCN-06 PNW Ecology]*

**6. TRUE.** Herculano-Houzel et al. (2015) counted approximately 438 million cortical neurons in the raccoon brain. This is comparable to some primate species and significantly exceeds the domestic cat (approximately 250 million cortical neurons) and the dog (approximately 530 million, but in a much larger brain). The raccoon's cortical neuron density --- neurons per gram of brain tissue --- is among the highest measured in any carnivore. This is especially impressive given the raccoon's relatively small brain size (approximately 39-44 grams).

*See: [RCN-02 Intelligence & Behavior --- Neural Architecture]*

**7. FALSE.** This is a common misconception. Raccoon-variant rabies is essentially **absent from the Pacific Northwest**. The raccoon rabies variant is endemic to the eastern United States (primarily the Eastern Seaboard from Florida to Maine) but has not established in Washington, Oregon, or British Columbia. The primary rabies vectors in the PNW are bats. Raccoons in the PNW can theoretically contract rabies from bats, but confirmed cases are extremely rare. The more significant raccoon-associated health concerns in the PNW are **Baylisascaris procyonis** (raccoon roundworm), canine distemper virus, and leptospirosis.

*See: [deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease]*

**8. TRUE.** Raccoons are native exclusively to North America (ranging from southern Canada through Central America). They did not historically occur in Europe, Asia, or anywhere outside the Americas. However, raccoons were introduced to Europe (particularly Germany, where a breeding population was established in 1934 from a deliberate release and a 1945 escape from a fur farm) and Japan (introduced as pets following a popular 1977 anime series). These introduced populations are now significant invasive species in multiple European and Asian countries.

*See: [RCN-07 Cultural History]*

**9. FALSE.** Raccoon kits are born with their eyes closed and their ears folded. The eyes open at approximately 18-23 days of age. The mask pattern is present at birth as a faint shadow on the fur and becomes more defined as the kit grows. Kits are born furred but helpless (altricial), weighing approximately 60-75 grams (about the weight of a tennis ball). They are entirely dependent on the mother for warmth, feeding, and protection for the first several weeks.

*See: [RCN-05 Lifecycle & Reproduction --- Kit Development]; [deep-dives/baby-raccoons]*

**10. TRUE.** Raccoons possess an unusual anatomical adaptation: their hind ankles can rotate to face backward, allowing the hind feet to grip the tree trunk while the animal descends headfirst. Most mammals (including cats, which frequently get "stuck" in trees) cannot do this and must descend tail-first or jump. The raccoon's ability to descend headfirst is facilitated by hypermobile ankle joints and strong, curved claws that maintain grip in the reversed position. This adaptation reflects the raccoon's long evolutionary history as an arboreal and semi-arboreal animal.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Skeletal System]*

### Section B: Multiple Choice

**11. (c) 22.** Goldman (1950) described 22 subspecies of *Procyon lotor* across North and Central America, from *P. l. hirtus* in the northern Great Plains to *P. l. pumilus* in Panama. Subspecies are distinguished primarily by body size, skull proportions, and pelage color. Northern subspecies tend to be larger (following Bergmann's rule) with thicker, paler fur. The PNW subspecies is *P. l. pacificus*.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Subspecies Diversity]*

**12. (d) Procyonidae.** The family Procyonidae includes raccoons, coatis, kinkajous, olingos, and ringtails. It is a small New World carnivore family that originated in North America 25-30 million years ago and diversified primarily in Central and South America after the formation of the Isthmus of Panama.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Family Procyonidae]*

**13. (c) 438 million.** This remarkable neuron count, published by Herculano-Houzel et al. in 2015, places the raccoon in the same cortical neuron range as some primates. For perspective: a cat has ~250 million, a golden retriever has ~530 million (in a brain 3x larger), and a capuchin monkey has ~1.14 billion. The raccoon achieves its high count through exceptional neuronal packing density.

*See: [RCN-02 Intelligence & Behavior]*

**14. (b) 63-65 days.** Raccoon gestation is remarkably consistent at 63-65 days. Breeding in the PNW typically occurs in February-March, with kits born in April-May. The gestation period is similar to that of domestic dogs (63 days) and cats (65 days), which is expected given their shared membership in the order Carnivora.

*See: [RCN-05 Lifecycle & Reproduction]*

**15. (c) True omnivore.** Raccoons are among the most thoroughly omnivorous mammals in North America. Their diet includes invertebrates (crayfish, insects, worms, mussels), vertebrates (fish, frogs, bird eggs, small rodents), fruits, nuts, seeds, corn, and in urban settings, virtually any human food waste. Their dentition reflects this --- the carnassial teeth are modified for crushing rather than shearing, and the molars have broad, flat surfaces suited for grinding plant material. Seasonal diet studies show their food intake shifts dramatically through the year, from protein-heavy in spring to carbohydrate/fruit-heavy in fall.

*See: [RCN-03 Diet & Foraging]*

**16. (c) 50-150.** Urban raccoon densities can reach 50-150 individuals per square kilometer, compared to 5-15 per km2 in rural or forested habitats. The highest densities are found in cities with mature tree canopy (for denning), water features, and abundant anthropogenic food. Toronto, which has one of the best-studied urban raccoon populations, estimated densities exceeding 100/km2 in some neighborhoods.

*See: [RCN-04 Urban Adaptation]; [deep-dives/toronto-raccoon-wars]*

**17. (c) Enhancing tactile perception by wetting the forepaws.** Water increases the responsiveness of the mechanoreceptors in the raccoon's forepaw skin, amplifying tactile information. The forepaw skin is thin, hairless, and densely innervated, with a disproportionately large somatosensory cortex representation (similar to how the human hand has a large cortical representation). When wet, the pliable skin transmits pressure and texture information more effectively. Captive raccoons deprived of water still manipulate food items with the same motion patterns, confirming that the behavior is fundamentally tactile, not hygienic.

*See: [RCN-02 Intelligence & Behavior]; [deep-dives/hands-and-intelligence]*

**18. (c) Red panda.** The red panda (*Ailurus fulgens*) was once classified as a procyonid based on morphological similarities, but molecular phylogenetic studies have conclusively placed it in its own family, Ailuridae. The red panda's superficial resemblance to procyonids (masked face, ringed tail, arboreal habits) is a case of convergent evolution, not close kinship. Coatis, kinkajous, and olingos are all genuine procyonids.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Family Procyonidae]*

**19. (b) 5.** Raccoons have five toes on all four feet. The forepaws are remarkably hand-like, with long, flexible digits that can be spread wide and operated somewhat independently --- though raccoons lack a truly opposable thumb. The hind feet are plantigrade (the heel contacts the ground when walking, like humans and bears), contributing to the distinctive waddling gait. Raccoon tracks in mud are unmistakable: the front paw print resembles a tiny human handprint with five spread fingers.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Appendicular Skeleton]*

**20. (b) Raccoons could learn complex lock mechanisms and remember solutions after long intervals.** Herbert Burnham Davis's 1907 study, published in the *American Journal of Psychology*, was one of the earliest systematic investigations of raccoon cognition. Davis presented raccoons with a series of increasingly complex mechanical locks and latches. The raccoons learned to open them through a combination of trial-and-error and what appeared to be insight, and they remembered the solutions for months without practice --- in some cases, over a year. This study was groundbreaking for its time and established the raccoon as a subject of serious cognitive research.

*See: [RCN-02 Intelligence & Behavior --- Early Research]*

### Section C: Short Answer

**21.** Any two of: coati (*Nasua* spp.), kinkajou (*Potos flavus*), olingo (*Bassaricyon* spp.), ringtail (*Bassariscus astutus*), cacomistle (*Bassariscus sumichrasti*), crab-eating raccoon (*Procyon cancrivorus*), Cozumel raccoon (*Procyon pygmaeus*).

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Family Procyonidae]*

**22.** *Baylisascaris procyonis* (raccoon roundworm). The eggs of this nematode are shed in raccoon feces and accumulate in latrines (communal defecation sites). The eggs are extraordinarily resistant to environmental degradation and remain infectious in soil for years. If ingested by a human --- particularly a young child who contacts contaminated soil and then touches their mouth --- the larvae migrate through body tissues (visceral larva migrans) and can enter the central nervous system (neural larva migrans), causing severe, potentially fatal neurological damage. There is no reliable treatment once CNS involvement occurs. This is why raccoon latrines should never be disturbed without proper protective equipment and decontamination procedures.

*See: [deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease]*

**23.** Raccoons are frequent subjects of 911 and non-emergency police calls in urban areas. People call because raccoons are in their attic, on their porch, fighting with their cat, knocking over their garbage, swimming in their pool, or behaving in ways that seem threatening or abnormal. In many cities, these calls constitute a significant fraction of animal-related emergency calls. The "CAHOOTS connection" refers to the fact that many of these calls --- like many human mental health crisis calls that programs like CAHOOTS (Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets, Eugene, OR) handle --- do not actually require an armed police response. They need a wildlife professional or simply reassurance that the raccoon will leave on its own. This parallel illustrates how both human and wildlife "nuisance" calls often reflect a mismatch between the response system and the actual nature of the problem.

*See: [RCN-04 Urban Adaptation]*

**24.** Typical litter size in the PNW is 3-5 kits (average 3-4). Kits open their eyes at approximately 18-23 days of age. They begin exploring outside the den at 8-10 weeks and are weaned by 12-16 weeks, though they continue to follow their mother and learn foraging skills through their first fall and sometimes into their first winter.

*See: [RCN-05 Lifecycle & Reproduction --- Kit Development]; [deep-dives/baby-raccoons]*

**25.** The front paw print resembles a small human handprint --- five long, separated digits radiating from a small central palm pad. The overall shape is wider than it is long, and the fingers are thin and splayed. The hind paw print is distinctly different: it is longer than it is wide, with the five toes arranged more parallel to each other, and a prominent elongated heel pad that gives the print a shape more like a miniature human footprint. In a walking track, the hind print typically registers ahead of (and slightly overlapping) the front print on the same side, because the hind leg swings forward past the front leg with each stride.

*See: [RCN-01 Biology & Taxonomy --- Locomotion]; [try-sessions/track-identification]*
