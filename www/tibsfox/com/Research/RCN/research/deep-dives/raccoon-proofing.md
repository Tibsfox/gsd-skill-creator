# Raccoon-Proofing Your Property --- A Practical Guide

If you live in the Pacific Northwest, you live with raccoons. This is not a conditional statement. Whether you are in a downtown Seattle apartment, a Mukilteo rambler, a Snohomish hobby farm, or a cabin on the Olympic Peninsula, raccoons are part of your ecological neighborhood. The subspecies *Procyon lotor pacificus* ranges from British Columbia to northern California, west of the Cascades, and population densities in PNW urban and suburban areas reach 30-150 individuals per square kilometer [RCN-04]. You are surrounded.

This is not inherently a problem. Raccoons are intelligent, ecologically important, and --- from a respectful distance --- genuinely fascinating animals. The problem arises at the intersection of raccoon behavior and human property: garbage, chicken coops, pet food, bird feeders, gardens, and attics. Raccoons are generalist foragers equipped with 438 million cortical neurons and forepaws that function as precision instruments [RCN-02]. They are persistent, dexterous, social learners who remember solutions for years. They are, in short, the most formidable property-access adversary that most homeowners will ever face.

This guide covers the major conflict points and the evidence-based solutions for each. The emphasis is on what *works* --- not what the internet suggests, not what seems like it should work, but what has been demonstrated to actually exclude or deter raccoons in real-world conditions.

## Garbage: The First Line of Defense

Garbage is the primary attractant for urban and suburban raccoons. A standard residential garbage can, set at the curb on collection night, is a beacon --- raccoons can detect food waste by scent from considerable distances and have learned, through generations of urban living, that garbage cans represent reliable, concentrated caloric resources.

### What Does Not Work

**Bungee cords.** The most common DIY recommendation and one of the least effective. A standard bungee cord stretched over a garbage can lid can be defeated by a raccoon in under 30 seconds. The technique is simple: hook the fingers of one forepaw under the cord, stretch it upward, and push the lid with the other forepaw or with the body. Some raccoons simply pull the bungee cord sideways off the lid. Others tip the entire can, causing the lid to separate. Bungee cords may deter a naive juvenile raccoon for a single night. They will not deter an experienced adult.

**Heavy objects on the lid.** A brick or cinder block on a garbage can lid adds weight but not security. Raccoons can push or roll objects off lids with their forepaws, or they can tip the entire can, causing the weight to slide off. A raccoon weighing 10-15 kg can leverage its body weight against a can to topple it even with additional weight on top.

**Ammonia or mothballs in the garbage.** Chemical deterrents placed in or near garbage cans have minimal lasting effect. Raccoons have excellent olfaction and can distinguish food scent from deterrent scent. The aversive effect of ammonia or naphthalene fumes is temporary --- raccoons habituate to unpleasant odors if food reward is consistently present. Additionally, mothballs (naphthalene or paradichlorobenzene) are toxic and should not be distributed in the environment where they may be ingested by children, pets, or non-target wildlife.

### What Works

**Carabiner clips on lid handles.** A spring-loaded carabiner clip through the lid handle and the can handle requires a specific two-step manipulation (squeeze the gate and pull the clip) that raccoons consistently fail to perform. The carabiner works because it requires simultaneous opposing forces --- squeezing inward while pulling outward --- in a configuration that raccoon forepaw anatomy does not easily accommodate. Cost: approximately $3-5 per clip. Effectiveness: very high.

**Locking garbage enclosures.** A bear box, a wooden enclosure with a latching door, or a commercial garbage can enclosure that fully contains the can and secures with a human-operable but raccoon-resistant latch (padlock, combination lock, or two-step mechanism) provides the highest level of protection. This is the standard solution in areas where bear-raccoon-garbage conflict is persistent.

**Storing garbage in a secure structure.** The simplest solution is to keep garbage in a garage, shed, or other enclosed structure and set it out only on collection morning, not the night before. Raccoons are nocturnal; garbage set out at dawn rather than dusk avoids the peak raccoon foraging window entirely.

**Municipal raccoon-resistant bins.** Some PNW municipalities offer upgraded garbage bins with improved latch mechanisms. Toronto's experience [RCN deep-dive: Toronto Raccoon Wars] demonstrates that no bin design is 100% raccoon-proof, but modern gravity-lock and twist-lock designs reduce successful raids significantly.

## Chicken Coops: The Critical Intersection [FWL-06]

Raccoons are the number-one nocturnal predator of backyard chickens in the Pacific Northwest. A raccoon that gains access to a chicken coop will kill multiple birds in a single night, often more than it can eat. The killing behavior is not malicious --- it is predatory instinct triggered by the confined, panicking birds --- but the result is devastating. A single unsecured night can end a flock.

### Hardware Cloth, Not Chicken Wire

This is the most important single piece of advice in chicken coop construction: **use 1/2-inch welded hardware cloth, not chicken wire.** Chicken wire (hexagonal poultry netting) keeps chickens in. It does not keep raccoons out. The openings in standard chicken wire are large enough for a raccoon to reach through, grasp a sleeping bird, and pull it against the wire. Raccoons have been documented killing chickens through chicken wire by reaching through and grabbing whatever they can --- a wing, a leg, a head. The bird is killed or mortally injured without the raccoon ever entering the coop.

Half-inch welded hardware cloth has openings too small for a raccoon's hand to pass through. It is more expensive than chicken wire ($30-50 per roll vs. $15-25) and harder to work with (it requires tin snips and gloves, as the cut wire ends are sharp), but it is the minimum standard for predator exclusion in any area with raccoon populations --- which, in the PNW, is everywhere.

Cover all windows, ventilation openings, and run walls with hardware cloth. Secure it with screws and washers, not staples --- raccoons can pull out staples.

### Raccoon-Proof Latches

A raccoon can open any latch that requires a single action: hook-and-eye latches, simple slide bolts, lift latches, and basic spring-loaded latches are all defeatable. The criterion for a raccoon-proof latch is that it must require **two simultaneous, different actions** to open. Examples:

- **Carabiner clip through a hasp:** Requires squeezing and pulling simultaneously.
- **Padlock:** Requires a key. Raccoons do not have keys.
- **Two-step latches:** A slide bolt with a secondary pin or clip that must be removed before the bolt can slide. The raccoon can learn to slide the bolt, but it cannot simultaneously remove the pin and slide the bolt.
- **Spring-loaded snap hooks with a screw gate:** The carabiner's more secure cousin, requiring the gate to be screwed open.

The general principle: if you can open the latch with one hand while wearing oven mitts, a raccoon can open it. If you need two hands, each performing a different action, you are probably safe.

### Buried Apron or Trench

Raccoons will dig under a coop or run wall to gain access. The solution is a buried hardware cloth apron: extend the hardware cloth from the base of the wall outward in an L-shape, buried 2-4 inches underground, extending 18-24 inches from the wall. A raccoon digging at the base of the wall encounters the buried apron and cannot tunnel past it. Cover the apron with soil, gravel, or pavers.

### Automatic Coop Doors

An automatic coop door that closes at dusk and opens at dawn is one of the best investments a chicken keeper can make. Raccoons are nocturnal --- virtually all raccoon predation on chickens occurs between dusk and dawn. A door that closes reliably at sunset eliminates the risk of forgetting to lock up, which is the most common proximate cause of raccoon predation losses. Modern automatic doors are light-sensor-activated, battery or solar powered, and cost $100-300 depending on features.

### Covered Runs

In the PNW, covered runs serve double duty: rain protection and aerial predator exclusion [FWL-06]. A solid or hardware-cloth roof over the run prevents raccoons from climbing over the walls and dropping in from above. Raccoons are excellent climbers and will scale a six-foot fence easily.

## Pet Food: The Easiest Fix

Outdoor pet food --- dog bowls, cat bowls, feral cat feeding stations --- is a high-value, zero-effort food source for raccoons. A bowl of kibble left on a patio overnight is an invitation, and raccoons that discover a reliable pet food source will return nightly, bring their kits, and eventually consider your property a core foraging site.

The fix is simple: **bring pet food bowls inside at dusk.** Feed outdoor pets during the day and remove all food before sunset. If you feed feral cats, use timed feeding (put food out at a specific time, remove it after 30 minutes) rather than leaving food available 24/7. Every night that pet food is available outdoors is a night that raccoons are being trained to visit your property.

## Bird Feeders: Engineering the Standoff

Bird feeders attract raccoons for two reasons: the seed itself (particularly sunflower seed and suet) and the rodents (mice and rats) that accumulate beneath feeders, which raccoons also eat.

### Weight-Activated Baffles

The most effective bird feeder defense is a weight-activated system: a feeder mechanism that closes when weight exceeding a bird's (approximately 50-100g threshold) is applied to the perch. Squirrel-proof feeders that use this mechanism also exclude raccoons, as a raccoon's weight (5-15 kg) far exceeds the activation threshold. Brands like Brome (Squirrel Buster series) use this approach effectively.

### Pole Baffles

A cone-shaped or cylinder-shaped baffle mounted on the feeder pole, below the feeder, prevents raccoons from climbing past it. The baffle must be at least 18 inches in diameter and mounted at least 4 feet above the ground. The pole must be at least 10 feet from any tree, fence, or structure that a raccoon could use as a launch point --- raccoons can leap 4-5 feet horizontally from a stable platform.

### Timing

Bring feeders in at dusk if raccoon visits are persistent. Raccoons are nocturnal; birds are diurnal. Removing feeders at sunset and replacing them at sunrise eliminates the temporal overlap.

## Gardens: Selective Protection

Raccoons damage gardens in two ways: eating produce (corn, tomatoes, berries, grapes, melons) and digging for grubs (leaving characteristic small, cone-shaped holes in lawns and beds).

### Electric Fence

A single strand of electric fence wire, positioned 6-8 inches above ground level around a garden bed, is remarkably effective against raccoons. The raccoon's sensitive, unpadded forepaws make it exceptionally responsive to electric shock --- the tactile sensitivity that makes those hands such effective foraging tools also makes them vulnerable to even mild electric stimulation. A fence charger rated for garden use (typically 0.1-0.25 joules, available for $30-60 at farm supply stores) provides an aversive stimulus that is unpleasant but not harmful. The raccoon touches the wire, receives the shock through its exquisitely sensitive forepaw pads, and retreats. After one or two encounters, most raccoons avoid the garden.

The wire must be low --- raccoons approach at ground level, and a wire at standard livestock fence height (36-48 inches) may be passed under. The 6-8 inch height targets the raccoon's natural approach posture.

### Netting

Heavy-duty bird netting over corn patches and berry bushes provides physical exclusion. The netting must be secured at the edges --- raccoons will crawl under loose netting. Netting is less effective than electric fence but requires no power source.

### Grub Control

If raccoons are digging up your lawn, they are eating grubs (typically Japanese beetle larvae, European chafer larvae, or crane fly larvae). The raccoons are the symptom; the grubs are the cause. Treating the lawn with beneficial nematodes (*Heterorhabditis bacteriophora*) or milky spore (*Paenibacillus popilliae*) reduces the grub population, removing the food attractant. This is a long-term solution that takes weeks to months to show results but addresses the root cause.

## Attics: Exclusion, Not Eviction

Raccoons in attics are a common PNW problem, particularly in older homes with accessible soffit vents, deteriorated fascia boards, or uncapped chimneys. A female raccoon seeking a natal den will tear through roofing material, soffit, and vent covers to access attic space.

### One-Way Exclusion Doors

The humane, effective approach to attic raccoons is a one-way exclusion door: a device installed over the raccoon's entry point that allows the animal to exit but not re-enter. The raccoon leaves to forage at night, passes through the one-way door, and cannot return. After 3-5 nights (to ensure all animals have exited, including any kits old enough to travel), the one-way door is removed and the entry point permanently sealed.

**Critical timing:** Never seal an attic entry point between March and August without confirming that no kits are present inside. A mother raccoon sealed out of an attic containing her kits will cause extraordinary damage tearing through the structure to reach them. She will not stop. If kits are present, wait until they are mobile enough (8-10 weeks) to follow the mother through the one-way door, or contact a licensed wildlife control operator who can manually remove the kits and reunite them with the mother outside.

### Trim Tree Branches

Raccoons access roofs via overhanging tree branches. Trim all branches to at least 6 feet from the roofline. This eliminates the most common access route and forces raccoons to climb the building's exterior, which is more exposed and less appealing.

### Seal Entry Points

After exclusion, permanently seal all potential entry points with heavy-gauge hardware cloth, sheet metal, or commercial wildlife exclusion products. Raccoons can tear through lightweight materials (window screen, thin wood, foam insulation). The repair must be robust enough to resist a 15-kg animal with powerful jaws and dexterous hands that will test every edge and seam.

## Why Relocation Does Not Work

Many homeowners, faced with persistent raccoon problems, request that the offending animal be trapped and relocated to a rural area. This is intuitive, humane-sounding, and almost entirely ineffective. The data is stark:

**WDFW (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife) data and multiple peer-reviewed studies indicate that relocated raccoons experience approximately 50-97% mortality within the first year of relocation.** The causes include:

- **Territorial conflict:** The release site is already occupied by resident raccoons who defend their territory against the newcomer. The relocated animal, unfamiliar with local food sources, den sites, and social dynamics, is at a severe disadvantage.
- **Disorientation:** Raccoons have strong spatial memory and homing instinct. Relocated animals frequently attempt to return to their capture site, traveling long distances through unfamiliar territory where they face traffic, predators, and starvation.
- **Disease transmission:** Relocating raccoons moves potential disease vectors (canine distemper, *Baylisascaris procyonis*, rabies) from one area to another, potentially introducing pathogens to naive populations.
- **Stress:** The combination of trapping, transport, and release in an unfamiliar environment causes physiological stress that compromises immune function and survival.

Furthermore, relocating a raccoon from your property does not solve the problem. If your property provides food and shelter, another raccoon will occupy the vacated territory within days to weeks. The habitat is the attractant, not the individual animal. Removing one raccoon without modifying the habitat is like bailing water without fixing the leak.

**Washington State law (WAC 220-440-060) restricts the relocation of trapped wildlife.** Trapped raccoons must be released on the property where they were captured or euthanized. This regulation reflects the biological reality that relocation is ineffective and potentially harmful.

## Humane Deterrents: Layered Defense

No single deterrent is reliably effective against raccoons in isolation. Effective deterrence uses a layered approach combining multiple stimuli:

### Motion-Activated Lights

Motion-activated floodlights or spotlight systems startle raccoons and disrupt their foraging. Effectiveness is moderate and temporary --- raccoons habituate to predictable light patterns within days to weeks. Random or intermittent activation patterns (e.g., a light that activates for variable durations) delay habituation.

### Motion-Activated Sprinklers

More effective than lights alone, because the water stimulus is physically aversive. Products like the Orbit Yard Enforcer combine a motion sensor with a sprinkler head that delivers a sudden burst of water when triggered. Effectiveness is moderate to high for initial encounters, with gradual habituation over weeks.

### Ammonia-Soaked Rags

Ammonia is aversive to raccoons' sensitive noses. Rags soaked in ammonia and placed near entry points, den sites, or known travel routes can deter initial visits. Effectiveness degrades rapidly as the ammonia evaporates (within 24-48 hours) and as raccoons habituate. Ammonia must be refreshed frequently and is best used as a supplementary deterrent alongside physical exclusion, not as a primary defense.

### Talk Radio

This one sounds absurd, but it has an evidence base. A radio left on a talk station (human voices, varying volume and cadence) near a raccoon entry point or den site creates unpredictable auditory stimulation that raccoons find unsettling. The effect is temporary --- raccoons habituate within days to weeks --- but it can be useful as a short-term deterrent while physical exclusion measures are being installed.

### What Does Not Work

**Ultrasonic deterrent devices** have no demonstrated effectiveness against raccoons in controlled studies. Raccoons hear well across a broad frequency range, but there is no evidence that ultrasonic sound produces a reliable aversive response.

**Predator urine** (coyote, fox) has minimal and temporary effect. Urban raccoons coexist with coyotes in many PNW areas and do not show sustained avoidance of predator scent cues in the absence of actual predator threat.

## The PNW-Specific Reality: Year-Round Raccoons

In northern parts of the raccoon's range (Minnesota, Wisconsin, Ontario), raccoons undergo a period of winter torpor --- not true hibernation, but a prolonged reduction in activity during the coldest months. During torpor, raccoons den for weeks at a time, living off accumulated fat reserves, and the frequency of human-raccoon conflict drops dramatically.

PNW raccoons do not truly torpor. The mild, wet winters west of the Cascades (typical lows 25-40 degrees F) allow year-round activity. PNW raccoons forage throughout winter, maintaining access to anthropogenic food sources (garbage, pet food, compost) that are available regardless of season. This means that raccoon-proofing in the PNW is not a seasonal concern. There is no "off season." The raccoons are active in January, active in July, and active every month in between.

This year-round activity, combined with the PNW's high urban raccoon densities, means that PNW homeowners must treat raccoon-proofing as permanent infrastructure rather than a temporary response to a seasonal problem. The carabiner on the garbage can, the hardware cloth on the coop, the trimmed tree branches, the sealed soffit vents --- these are not seasonal installations. They are features of PNW home maintenance, as permanent and as necessary as the rain gutter.
