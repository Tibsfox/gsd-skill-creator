# The Health Risks --- Raccoon-Associated Diseases

Raccoons carry diseases. This is not a reason to fear them, any more than the fact that your dog carries bacteria is a reason to fear your dog. But it is a reason to understand what the actual risks are, how they are transmitted, and what practical steps reduce exposure. The internet is full of raccoon disease information that ranges from accurate to hysterical, and the hysterical end tends to dominate search results. This document aims for something different: honest, evidence-based information about the diseases associated with raccoons in the Pacific Northwest, presented with enough clinical detail to be useful and enough context to be proportionate.

The short version: raccoons in the PNW are not the disease vectors that panicked online commentary might suggest. The single genuinely serious zoonotic risk is *Baylisascaris procyonis*, the raccoon roundworm, and even that risk is manageable with basic awareness. Rabies is largely a non-issue in PNW raccoon populations (the raccoon rabies variant has not established west of the Appalachians). Canine distemper is devastating to raccoons but irrelevant to humans. Leptospirosis is real but uncommon. The practical message is: respect raccoon latrines, do not handle raccoons, and call wildlife professionals when something seems wrong. Beyond that, coexistence is straightforward.

## Baylisascaris procyonis: The Raccoon Roundworm

### What It Is

*Baylisascaris procyonis* is a large ascarid nematode (roundworm) that lives in the small intestine of raccoons. Adult worms are substantial --- females can reach 20-22 centimeters (roughly 8 inches) in length, and a single raccoon may harbor dozens to hundreds of adult worms. The worms are host-adapted to raccoons: adult raccoons tolerate heavy worm loads with minimal clinical signs, maintaining a chronic infection that sheds eggs into the environment continuously.

And "continuously" means continuously. A single infected raccoon can shed millions of *Baylisascaris* eggs per day in its feces. The eggs are deposited in raccoon latrines --- communal defecation sites that raccoons use repeatedly, often located on flat elevated surfaces (tree limbs, rock outcrops, fallen logs, rooftops, decks, woodpiles, the base of large trees). The eggs accumulate in enormous numbers at active latrines.

### The Egg Problem

Here is where *Baylisascaris* becomes a serious concern. The eggs are:

**Extremely durable.** *Baylisascaris* eggs have a thick, proteinaceous shell that resists desiccation, chemical disinfection, and environmental degradation. Eggs deposited in soil can remain viable for **years** --- estimates range from 2 to 5+ years under favorable conditions. Standard household disinfectants (bleach, ammonia, alcohol) do not kill *Baylisascaris* eggs. Formalin, which kills most parasite eggs, requires prolonged exposure to inactivate *Baylisascaris*. The only reliable environmental decontamination is **heat**: boiling water, steam, or fire.

**Not immediately infectious.** Freshly deposited *Baylisascaris* eggs are not infective. They require an embryonation period of 2-4 weeks in the environment before they develop to the infective larval stage within the egg. This means that fresh raccoon feces are less dangerous than old, weathered feces at an established latrine. The danger increases with time.

**Small and invisible.** The eggs are approximately 60-80 micrometers in diameter --- invisible to the naked eye. You cannot see them. You cannot tell by looking at soil, wood, or a deck surface whether *Baylisascaris* eggs are present. Contaminated surfaces look identical to clean surfaces.

### How Infection Occurs

Humans (and other animals) become infected by accidentally ingesting embryonated *Baylisascaris* eggs. This happens through:

- **Direct contact with contaminated soil or surfaces.** Children playing in soil near a raccoon latrine, gardening in contaminated soil, or handling objects (firewood, outdoor toys, garden tools) that have been in contact with latrine material.
- **Geophagia (soil eating).** Young children who put soil or dirty hands in their mouths are at highest risk. This is the primary exposure pathway for the most serious human cases.
- **Contaminated water.** Drinking or swimming in water contaminated with raccoon feces.

*Baylisascaris* is **not** transmitted by touching raccoons, by breathing near raccoons, or by being in the same outdoor space as raccoons. The transmission pathway is strictly fecal-oral: you must ingest the eggs.

### What Happens: Neural Larva Migrans

When a non-raccoon host (human, dog, bird, rodent) ingests *Baylisascaris* eggs, the larvae hatch in the intestine but cannot complete their life cycle. Instead, the larvae migrate through the body tissues --- a condition called **larva migrans**. The larvae are large (as parasitic larvae go) and aggressive migrators, and they have a tropism for the central nervous system. When larvae reach the brain, the condition is called **neural larva migrans (NLM)**, and the results can be severe:

- **Eosinophilic meningoencephalitis:** Inflammation of the brain and meninges, driven by the immune response to the migrating larvae
- **Neurological damage:** Depending on the number of larvae and their location in the brain, NLM can cause cognitive impairment, loss of coordination, blindness, seizures, coma, and in the most severe cases, death
- **Ocular larva migrans:** Larvae migrating to the eye can cause vision loss

It is essential to contextualize these outcomes. **Clinical NLM from *Baylisascaris* in humans is rare.** Fewer than 30 confirmed cases have been documented in the medical literature as of 2025, most in young children (under age 3) with histories of geophagia in areas with heavy raccoon latrine contamination. The rarity reflects both the specific conditions required for infection (ingestion of a significant number of embryonated eggs) and the difficulty of diagnosis (NLM is not easily distinguished from other causes of eosinophilic meningoencephalitis without specific antibody testing).

Rare does not mean trivial. The cases that have occurred have been devastating, with most resulting in severe, permanent neurological damage. The rarity of the disease is partly because most people do not eat soil from raccoon latrines. The severity of the disease when it does occur is why raccoon latrines deserve serious respect.

### Raccoon Latrine Identification

A raccoon latrine is a communal defecation site. Raccoons are habitual about latrine use, returning to the same sites repeatedly and adding fresh feces on top of older deposits. Latrines are typically found on:

- **Flat elevated surfaces:** large horizontal tree limbs, the tops of fallen logs, flat rocks, stumps
- **Rooftops, decks, and porches:** especially near den entry points (attics, chimneys)
- **At the base of large trees:** particularly old conifers and deciduous trees with rough bark
- **Woodpiles and lumber stacks**
- **Attics and crawl spaces** where raccoons den

Raccoon feces resemble small dog feces --- cylindrical, dark, typically containing visible undigested material (seeds, insect parts, berry fragments). Fresh feces are dark and moist; older deposits become dry and may turn gray-white. An active latrine will show feces of varying ages, from fresh to desiccated.

### Latrine Cleanup Protocol

If you identify a raccoon latrine on your property, cleanup should be taken seriously. The goal is to remove the fecal material and the contaminated soil or substrate, and to decontaminate the surface.

1. **Wear protective equipment.** Disposable gloves (nitrile or latex), an N95 respirator mask (to prevent inhalation of egg-containing dust), and disposable shoe covers or old shoes that can be discarded. Do not wear clothing you value.

2. **Moisten the area.** Lightly mist the latrine area with water to reduce dust. **Do not sweep or vacuum dry raccoon feces.** Sweeping and vacuuming aerosolize the eggs, creating an inhalation risk and spreading contamination. This is the single most important don't in latrine cleanup.

3. **Remove fecal material.** Using a disposable scoop or shovel, remove all fecal material and the top 1-2 inches of underlying soil or substrate (if on soil). Place the material in a heavy-duty plastic bag, double-bag it, and dispose of it in the garbage. Do not compost it.

4. **Decontaminate the surface.** Pour boiling water over the area. Boiling water is the only readily available household treatment that kills *Baylisascaris* eggs. Multiple applications are recommended. For hard surfaces (decks, patios), a propane torch or steam cleaner is more thorough.

5. **On contaminated soil:** Remove and replace the top 2-4 inches of soil. If full soil removal is impractical, repeated boiling water applications over several weeks can reduce egg viability, but complete decontamination of soil is extremely difficult.

6. **Dispose of gloves, shoe covers, and cleaning materials.** Wash hands thoroughly with soap and hot water. Launder clothing in hot water.

7. **Prevent re-establishment.** Raccoons return to former latrines. Block access if possible (seal entry points to attics, remove latrine substrate). Chemical deterrents are generally ineffective.

## Canine Distemper Virus (CDV)

### What It Is

Canine distemper is a viral disease (family Paramyxoviridae) that is devastating to raccoons and is one of the most common infectious causes of raccoon mortality. CDV in raccoons produces a range of clinical signs including:

- Respiratory distress (nasal discharge, coughing, labored breathing)
- Ocular discharge (crusted, weeping eyes)
- Neurological signs: disorientation, circling, staggering gait, seizures, head tilt, loss of fear of humans
- Lethargy, emaciation, rough/unkempt coat

A raccoon with advanced distemper may stagger in circles, walk into objects, approach humans without fear, or lie in the open during daylight. These behaviors look alarming and are sometimes mistaken for rabies.

### Human Risk

**None.** Canine distemper virus does not infect humans. CDV is species-specific to canids, procyonids, mustelids, and some other carnivore families. There is no documented case of human CDV infection.

### Domestic Animal Risk

**Dogs:** CDV is the same virus that causes canine distemper in dogs. Unvaccinated dogs can contract CDV from raccoon exposure (direct contact, shared environment). The core DAPP/DHPP canine vaccine provides effective protection. If your dog is current on vaccinations, CDV from raccoons is not a meaningful risk.

**Cats:** Cats are not susceptible to CDV.

### What Distemper Means for Raccoons

CDV epidemics sweep through raccoon populations periodically, particularly in dense urban populations where transmission is facilitated by social contact, shared latrines, and shared den sites [RCN-04, RCN-05]. Mortality in infected raccoons is high --- estimated at 50-80% in some outbreaks. Survivors may carry permanent neurological damage.

Distemper is one of the primary natural population regulators for raccoons, along with automobile mortality and, in some areas, hunting and trapping. The periodic die-offs caused by CDV epidemics temporarily reduce local raccoon densities, followed by rapid recovery as surviving raccoons breed and juvenile dispersal fills vacant territories.

## Rabies

### The PNW Situation

This is probably the disease people worry about most, so let us be clear about the actual risk.

**The raccoon rabies variant (RRV) has not established in the Pacific Northwest.** The raccoon-specific strain of rabies virus is an eastern phenomenon. It circulates in raccoon populations from the eastern seaboard through the Appalachian region, with the western edge of the enzootic zone roughly following the Appalachian mountain chain. Despite decades of westward expansion pressure, the raccoon rabies variant has not crossed the Appalachians into the Mississippi River basin or beyond. There is no established raccoon rabies in Washington, Oregon, or any state west of the Mississippi.

This does not mean that PNW raccoons are zero-risk for rabies. Other rabies variants circulate in the western US --- bat rabies (multiple variants), skunk rabies (in some regions), and fox rabies. A raccoon can theoretically contract rabies from a bat bite. But the probability of encountering a rabid raccoon in the PNW is extremely low --- orders of magnitude lower than in the eastern US.

### What Rabies Looks Like in Raccoons

Rabies in raccoons can present as:

- **Furious rabies:** Aggression, unprovoked attacks, disorientation, loss of fear
- **Dumb/paralytic rabies:** Lethargy, weakness, progressive paralysis, drooling, inability to swallow

These symptoms overlap significantly with canine distemper, and distinguishing the two diseases from behavioral observation alone is unreliable. This is why any raccoon displaying abnormal neurological behavior should be treated with caution regardless of the statistical likelihood of rabies.

### Practical Guidance

- **Do not handle raccoons.** This applies regardless of disease concerns, but especially for any animal showing neurological signs.
- **If bitten or scratched by a raccoon:** Wash the wound immediately and thoroughly with soap and warm water for at least 5 minutes. Contact your healthcare provider. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) for rabies is available and essentially 100% effective when administered promptly. Your healthcare provider will consult with the local health department to assess rabies risk based on the circumstances of the exposure.
- **Report abnormally behaving raccoons** to your local animal control or the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) / Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife (ODFW). They can assess the situation, capture the animal if warranted, and submit it for rabies testing if there has been human or domestic animal contact.

## Leptospirosis

### What It Is

Leptospirosis is a bacterial disease caused by spirochete bacteria of the genus *Leptospira*. Raccoons are among many wildlife species that can carry *Leptospira* in their kidneys and shed the bacteria in urine. The bacteria survive in warm, moist environments --- standing water, puddles, streams contaminated with urine --- and can enter the human body through mucous membranes (eyes, nose, mouth) or through broken skin.

### Risk Level

Leptospirosis from raccoon exposure is uncommon but real. The PNW's wet climate creates favorable conditions for *Leptospira* survival in the environment. Risk is highest for people who have contact with raccoon urine, urine-contaminated water, or urine-contaminated soil --- for example, cleanup of attic spaces where raccoons have denned, or work in areas with standing water near raccoon activity.

### Symptoms

Human leptospirosis ranges from mild (flu-like symptoms: fever, headache, muscle aches, lasting a few days) to severe (Weil's disease: jaundice, kidney failure, hemorrhage, meningitis). Most cases are mild and self-limiting. Severe disease is rare but serious.

### Prevention

- Wear gloves when cleaning areas of raccoon activity
- Avoid contact with standing water in areas of known raccoon activity
- Wear protective equipment during attic or crawl space cleanup after raccoon denning
- Ensure domestic dogs are vaccinated against leptospirosis (available as part of combination canine vaccines)

## Giardia

*Giardia duodenalis* (also *G. lamblia*) is a protozoal parasite that raccoons can carry and shed in feces. Giardia causes giardiasis in humans --- a gastrointestinal illness characterized by diarrhea, cramping, bloating, nausea, and fatigue. The disease is generally self-limiting but unpleasant, lasting 1-3 weeks.

Raccoons are one of many wildlife species (along with beavers, deer, and numerous others) that contribute Giardia to waterways. The risk of raccoon-specific Giardia transmission to humans is low compared to waterborne transmission from other sources. Standard water treatment (filtration, UV, boiling) eliminates Giardia cysts. The primary risk context is untreated water from streams or lakes in areas with raccoon activity.

## What to Do If You Find a Sick Raccoon

A raccoon that is visibly ill, injured, or behaving abnormally --- staggering, circling, lying in the open during daylight, approaching humans without fear, showing nasal or ocular discharge, or appearing emaciated --- needs professional attention. Here is what to do and what not to do.

### Do Not

- **Do not touch the animal.** Even a sick raccoon can bite, and the diseases that cause neurological symptoms (distemper, rabies) are precisely the diseases that make an animal unpredictable.
- **Do not attempt to feed or water the animal.** You cannot treat a sick raccoon with home care. Offering food to a distempered raccoon may prolong suffering without addressing the disease.
- **Do not attempt to trap the animal** unless you have experience with wildlife trapping and appropriate equipment.
- **Do not approach raccoon kits that appear to be alone** without first waiting 24 hours (see [RCN deep-dives/baby-raccoons]).

### Do

- **Observe from a safe distance.** Note the animal's location, behavior, and visible condition.
- **Contact local wildlife authorities:**
  - **Washington:** WDFW (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife) hotline: (360) 902-2515
  - **Oregon:** ODFW (Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife): (503) 947-6000
  - **Animal control:** Your city or county animal control can respond to wildlife calls
- **Contact wildlife rehabilitation if the animal appears injured rather than diseased:**
  - **PAWS Wildlife Center** (Lynnwood, WA): (425) 412-4040
  - **Portland Audubon Wildlife Care Center** (Portland, OR): (503) 292-0304
  - **West Sound Wildlife Shelter** (Bainbridge Island, WA): (206) 855-9057
  - **Sarvey Wildlife Care Center** (Arlington, WA): (360) 435-4817
- **If there has been human or domestic animal contact:** Contact your healthcare provider and/or veterinarian immediately. Report the contact to local animal control.

## The Key Message

Raccoons are not inherently dangerous. They are wild animals that carry a suite of parasites and diseases adapted to their species, as all wild animals do. The vast majority of human-raccoon interactions --- seeing them in the yard, hearing them on the roof, watching them raid the compost --- involve no disease risk whatsoever.

The risks that do exist are specific and manageable:

1. **Raccoon latrines deserve serious respect.** Identify them, clean them properly, and prevent re-establishment. Keep young children away from latrine sites. This is the single most important health precaution related to raccoons.

2. **Do not handle raccoons.** Not because they are disease-ridden, but because they are wild animals capable of biting and scratching, and because the diseases of concern (rabies, leptospirosis) require direct contact for transmission.

3. **Keep dogs vaccinated.** DAPP/DHPP (distemper) and leptospirosis vaccinations protect dogs from the two diseases most likely to be transmitted from raccoons.

4. **Call professionals for sick animals.** Wildlife rehabilitators and animal control officers have the training, equipment, and legal authority to handle sick wildlife safely.

Beyond these precautions, raccoons are neighbors --- intelligent, adaptable, ecologically important neighbors who have lived in the PNW for far longer than humans have. Understanding their disease ecology is part of understanding the animal, and understanding the animal is part of living alongside it responsibly.
