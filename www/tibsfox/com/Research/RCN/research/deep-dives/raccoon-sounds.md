# Charf Charf --- The 200+ Vocalizations of *Procyon lotor*

*For Chairo Charf Charf --- whose name is a sound, and whose sound is a greeting.*

---

## A Language in the Dark

Stand in a PNW backyard at two in the morning on a warm August night. Turn off the porch light. Wait.

The sounds will come. First, a rustle in the rhododendrons --- the characteristic heavy-footed shuffle of an animal that is confident in the dark and unconcerned about stealth. Then, a sound that is not a bark and not a growl and not a purr but somehow all three at once: a rapid, rolling trill that rises and falls like a small motor warming up, a sound that carries through the night with surprising clarity. *Charf-charf-charf-charf.* Another animal answers from the fence line, the same sound pitched slightly higher, slightly faster. Then a third voice joins from the cedar tree, a lower register, slower cadence, and for a moment the backyard is a conference of small, masked mammals conducting business in a language that humans can hear but cannot quite decode.

Raccoons are among the most vocal non-primate, non-cetacean mammals on Earth. Researchers have documented over 200 distinct vocalizations, a repertoire that rivals some primates and dwarfs most carnivores. Dogs, by comparison, produce roughly 15-20 distinct vocal types. Cats produce 20-30. Raccoons produce ten times that range, and they use it. They use it in the den, in the tree, at the latrine, at the garbage can, between mother and kit, between coalition partners, between rivals, between strangers. They use it to say "I am here," "I am angry," "I am your mother," "I am hungry," "I am afraid," "come here," "stay away," and a hundred things for which human language has no direct translation.

This document catalogs the major vocal categories of *Procyon lotor*, describes their acoustic characteristics and social contexts, and explains what you are hearing when the sounds begin outside your window in the dark.

## The Vocal Repertoire: A Catalog

### Purring

**Acoustic description:** A low, continuous, rhythmic vibration, typically in the 25-50 Hz range, produced during exhalation and sometimes during inhalation. The sound is felt as much as heard at close range --- a vibration transmitted through the raccoon's body that is detectable by touch when holding the animal or by sound when close to the den.

**Social context:** Purring is the sound of contentment and connection. It occurs most frequently in three contexts:

1. **Nursing.** A mother raccoon purrs while nursing her kits, and the kits purr simultaneously. The resulting den soundscape is a low, continuous vibration that sounds like a small, uneven engine --- multiple overlapping purrs at slightly different frequencies creating a complex harmonic texture. The purring appears to function as a bonding signal and may stimulate milk let-down in the mother, analogous to the rumored (and debated) role of purring in feline nursing.

2. **Grooming.** Adult raccoons groom each other (allogrooming) particularly within female kin groups and male coalitions. Grooming sessions are accompanied by mutual purring that signals relaxation and social trust. The sound during grooming is softer and more irregular than nursing purring --- intermittent bursts that coincide with particularly thorough grooming of sensitive areas behind the ears or along the belly.

3. **Resting in physical contact.** Raccoons denning together, particularly during cool weather when thermal contact is beneficial, produce a collective purr that wildlife rehabilitators describe as one of the most surprisingly soothing sounds in wildlife care. A den containing a mother and four kits in the purring stage sounds, from outside the den opening, like a distant lawnmower heard through several walls --- a low, steady, organic hum.

**Sonogram characteristics:** Purring shows as a broad-spectrum, low-frequency band on a sonogram, typically 25-100 Hz with harmonic overtones extending to 200-300 Hz. The fundamental frequency is remarkably stable within an individual but varies between individuals, suggesting that purring carries identity information.

### Chittering and Chattering

**Acoustic description:** A rapid series of short, staccato vocalizations --- *chit-chit-chit-chit* --- produced with the mouth partially open, the jaw moving rapidly. The sound has been variously described as clicking, chattering, twittering, or a fast mechanical tapping. Frequency is typically in the 2-6 kHz range, well within human hearing, and carries effectively over moderate distances (20-50 meters in open air).

**Social context:** Chittering is the raccoon's all-purpose social vocalization. It serves multiple functions depending on intensity, speed, and context:

- **Contact call.** A moderate-speed, moderate-volume chitter is a "here I am" signal used by raccoons moving through shared territory. Two raccoons approaching each other on a fence line will often exchange chittering before visual contact is established, essentially announcing their identity and intent before the encounter. The chittering in this context has an almost conversational quality --- call and response, each animal's chitter overlapping slightly with the other's, the volume staying steady and low.

- **Curiosity/investigation.** A faster, higher-pitched chitter accompanies investigation of novel objects, new food sources, or unfamiliar situations. Wildlife rehabilitators note that raccoons encountering a new enrichment item --- a puzzle feeder, a novel toy, an unfamiliar food --- will chitter excitedly while manipulating it, the vocalization apparently reflecting arousal and engagement rather than distress.

- **Mild agitation.** A louder, faster, more insistent chitter indicates rising frustration or anxiety. A raccoon defeated by a raccoon-proof garbage lid may escalate from investigative chittering to agitated chattering over the course of several minutes, the sound growing louder and faster as patience erodes.

**Sonogram characteristics:** Chittering appears on a sonogram as a rapid series of discrete, evenly spaced frequency bursts, each lasting 50-150 milliseconds, with sharp onset and offset. The regularity of the bursts distinguishes chittering from the more variable sounds of growling or whimpering.

### The "Charf" Trill

**Acoustic description:** A distinctive rolling vocalization that combines elements of purring, chittering, and trilling into a sound that is uniquely raccoon. The charf trill is produced with the mouth closed or nearly closed, the sound resonating through the nasal passages and throat. It has a rolling, vibrating quality --- *charf-charf-charf* --- that rises and falls in pitch over 0.5-2 seconds, the repetitions blending into a continuous, modulated stream.

The frequency range is broad --- 500 Hz to 4 kHz --- with strong harmonic structure that gives the sound a rich, almost musical quality. It is louder than purring but softer than chittering, and it carries through the night air with a warmth that is immediately recognizable once you have heard it.

**Social context:** The charf trill is the sound of recognition and affiliation. It is the raccoon's greeting, produced between individuals who know each other and are approaching with friendly intent.

- **Mother-kit bonding.** The charf trill is the primary vocalization used by mothers approaching their kits and by kits recognizing their mother's return to the den. A mother returning from a foraging trip will begin the charf trill while still approaching the den, and the kits answer with their own version --- higher-pitched, less steady, sometimes dissolving into excited chittering as the mother arrives. This mother-kit duet is one of the most frequently heard raccoon vocalizations in residential areas during the spring and summer kit-rearing season.

- **Coalition greetings.** Male raccoons in established coalitions [RCN-02] greet each other with the charf trill when reuniting after a separation. The vocalization accompanies nose-to-nose contact and sometimes mutual grooming. The sound in this context is lower-pitched and more abbreviated than the mother-kit version --- a brief, rolling acknowledgment rather than a sustained exchange.

- **Familiar adults.** Unrelated adults that share overlapping home ranges and have established a pattern of mutual tolerance may exchange charf trills during encounters. The trill in this context appears to signal "I know you, I am not a threat," reducing the likelihood of escalation to aggressive vocalizations.

**Why "Charf Charf":** The name Chairo Charf Charf is rooted in this vocalization. The trill is the signature sound of raccoon social life --- the sound of connection, of recognition, of the small, masked animals acknowledging each other in the dark. It is the sound that says *you are familiar, you are family, you are welcome here.* Of all the 200+ vocalizations in the raccoon repertoire, the charf trill is the one that most clearly communicates something that humans can recognize as warmth.

**Sonogram characteristics:** The charf trill appears as a series of closely spaced, harmonically rich bursts with smooth frequency modulation --- each "charf" shows as a rising-falling arc of frequency centered around 1-3 kHz, with overtones extending to 6-8 kHz. The modulation gives the sonogram a characteristic wavy appearance that distinguishes it from the flat-line regularity of chittering or the broad-band noise of growling.

### Growling and Hissing

**Acoustic description:** Growling is a low, sustained, rough-textured vocalization produced with the mouth open, the lips often retracted to expose the canine teeth. Frequency is low (100-500 Hz) with abundant harmonic noise that gives the sound a harsh, rasping quality. Hissing accompanies or alternates with growling --- a sharp, sibilant exhalation produced through the open mouth with the teeth bared, higher in frequency (2-8 kHz), shorter in duration.

**Social context:** These are the unambiguous signals of aggression and warning. A growling raccoon is communicating a clear message: *I am prepared to fight, and you should reconsider your approach.*

Growling occurs in several contexts:

- **Territorial defense.** A raccoon defending a food source, den site, or mate from a rival will growl continuously, the sound intensifying as the intruder approaches. The escalation sequence typically proceeds: low growl → louder growl → growl with hissing → growl with snarling (exposing teeth more fully) → physical contact (biting, clawing). At any point in this sequence, the intruder can de-escalate by retreating.

- **Human or predator encounters.** A cornered raccoon, a mother defending kits, or a raccoon surprised at close range will growl and hiss as a defensive warning. The sound is accompanied by characteristic body language: arched back, bristled fur, direct stare, sometimes standing on the hind legs to appear larger. The vocalization is directed at the perceived threat and ceases when the threat withdraws.

- **Intra-specific conflict.** Male raccoons during the breeding season engage in aggressive encounters that feature prolonged bouts of mutual growling, often escalating to physical combat. These nighttime confrontations produce sounds that homeowners frequently describe as "terrifying" --- sustained, overlapping growls punctuated by screams and snarls that carry through open windows with alarming clarity.

**A specific behavioral note:** Raccoons growl at humans more often than most people realize, because the encounters typically occur in conditions (darkness, surprise, confined spaces) where the human may not clearly see the animal but can clearly hear it. The sound of a raccoon growling from inside a dumpster, beneath a deck, or within a wall void is distinctive and unmistakable --- a wet, rumbling snarl that announces the presence of an animal that is neither afraid nor inclined to cooperate.

### Screaming and Screeching

**Acoustic description:** The highest-intensity vocalizations in the raccoon repertoire. Screams are high-pitched (3-10 kHz), loud (audible at 200+ meters), sustained (0.5-3 seconds per scream), and acoustically harsh, with broad-spectrum noise and irregular harmonic structure. They have been described as "sounding like a woman screaming," "like a small child in distress," and "like two cats fighting, but worse." None of these descriptions are entirely wrong.

**Social context:** Screaming occurs in contexts of extreme arousal:

- **Mating.** The breeding season (January-March in the PNW) is the peak screaming period. Males fighting over females produce extended bouts of screaming and snarling that can wake neighborhoods. The female herself may scream during copulation, which is prolonged (up to an hour) and sometimes involves aggressive behavior from the male. Mating-season screaming is the raccoon vocalization most commonly reported to police as a possible domestic disturbance.

- **Territorial fights.** Intense, escalated conflicts between males --- particularly during the breeding season --- produce screams that reflect extreme aggressive arousal. These fights can result in serious injuries and the screaming reflects genuine physical conflict, not merely posturing.

- **Fear/distress.** Raccoons in extreme fear --- trapped, cornered by a predator (dog, coyote), or in physical pain --- produce distress screams that are higher-pitched and more sustained than aggressive screams. The distress scream functions as both an alarm call (alerting nearby conspecifics to danger) and a startle stimulus (potentially deterring the predator).

**PNW timing:** In the Puget Sound lowlands, raccoon screaming peaks in February and March, coinciding with breeding season. Homeowners report being awakened by "something being murdered in the backyard" --- an accurate description of what it sounds like, if not what is actually happening. The screaming resolves when the breeding encounter concludes, typically leaving behind silence and (sometimes) a few tufts of fur on the lawn.

### Whimpering and Cooing

**Acoustic description:** Whimpering is a high, thin, rhythmic vocalization --- *eee-eee-eee* --- produced by kits and occasionally by adults in submissive or distressed states. Frequency is typically 2-6 kHz, with a plaintive, insistent quality that is difficult to ignore. Cooing is a softer, lower-pitched version --- *ooh-ooh-ooh* --- produced by mothers in the den, directed at kits. The coo is warm, rhythmic, and intimate, audible only at close range.

**Social context:**

- **Kit distress.** A separated kit --- lost, cold, or hungry --- produces whimpers that are directional (the mother can localize the sound) and persistent (the kit will whimper continuously until reunited with the mother or exhausted). This is the sound that generates the most phone calls to wildlife rehabilitation centers: a whimpering kit inside a chimney, wall void, or under a deck sounds like a human infant in distress, and the resemblance is close enough to trigger genuine alarm in listeners.

- **Kit solicitation.** Kits approaching the mother for nursing or food produce a softer whimper that grades into chirping --- a lower-urgency solicitation that communicates "I am here, I am hungry" without the desperation of the distress whimper.

- **Maternal cooing.** The mother's coo is the acoustic complement to the charf trill --- a sound of proximity, reassurance, and care. It is produced while nursing, while grooming kits, and while leading kits on early foraging excursions. The coo functions as a continuous contact call: *I am here, stay close, everything is safe.* Wildlife rehabilitators raising orphaned kits sometimes use recordings of maternal cooing to calm distressed kits, with moderate success.

### Bark

**Acoustic description:** A single, sharp, high-volume vocalization --- *wuf* or *huh* --- lasting 0.1-0.3 seconds, with a broad frequency range (500 Hz - 8 kHz). The bark is loud, abrupt, and carries well.

**Social context:** Barking is the rarest of the major raccoon vocalizations and functions as an alarm call. A raccoon that detects a sudden threat --- the approach of a dog, the appearance of a coyote, a loud unexpected sound --- may produce one or two barks before fleeing or freezing. The bark alerts nearby conspecifics (particularly kits) to the presence of danger. Kits that hear a maternal bark freeze immediately, a response that is likely innate rather than learned.

The bark is so rarely heard by humans that many raccoon observers never encounter it. It is most commonly documented in research settings where raccoons are subjected to controlled startle stimuli, and in anecdotal reports from wildlife professionals who encounter raccoons in unusual circumstances.

## Identifying Raccoon Sounds at Night

For PNW residents who hear sounds in their yard after dark, the following guide may help identify whether the source is a raccoon or something else:

**Raccoon vs. cat fight:** Raccoon fights involve deeper growling, more sustained screaming, and a "wet" quality to the snarling that reflects the larger oral cavity and heavier jaw structure. Cat fights are higher-pitched, sharper, and include the characteristic feline yowl --- a long, modulated howl that raccoons do not produce.

**Raccoon vs. owl:** Great horned owls (*Bubo virginianus*) produce a deep, resonant hooting --- *hoo-hoo-hoooo hoo-hoo* --- that is rhythmic and musical. Barred owls (*Strix varia*) produce the classic "who-cooks-for-you" call. Neither sounds like raccoon vocalizations. However, barred owls also produce a remarkable repertoire of screams, cackles, and shrieks during territorial disputes that can be confused with raccoon sounds. The owl sounds tend to be more musical and more clearly aerial in origin.

**Raccoon vs. opossum:** Opossums (*Didelphis virginiana*) are relatively quiet. When confronted, they hiss --- a dry, sharp exhalation that is shorter, sharper, and drier than a raccoon's hiss. Opossums do not chitter, trill, or growl with the sustained intensity of raccoons.

**Raccoon vs. coyote:** Coyotes (*Canis latrans*) yip, howl, and bark --- sounds that are distinctly canid and unlikely to be confused with raccoon vocalizations by anyone who has heard both. PNW coyotes are increasingly common in urban areas [MAM], and their nighttime vocalizations often occur in the same hours as raccoon activity.

**The "what was THAT?" test:** If you hear a sound in your backyard at night that you cannot identify, and it sounds like it was produced by an animal approximately the size of a medium cat, and the sound is somewhere on the spectrum between purring, chattering, and snarling, and it is accompanied by the sound of something being manipulated, opened, or knocked over --- it is almost certainly a raccoon. The manipulation sounds are the giveaway. Raccoons do not just vocalize. They vocalize *while doing things with their hands*, and the combination of vocal and mechanical sounds is as diagnostic as the vocalizations themselves.

## The Social Soundscape

The full picture of raccoon vocal communication emerges not from individual sounds in isolation but from the social contexts in which they are combined. A typical nighttime raccoon encounter in a PNW backyard might produce the following acoustic sequence:

1. **Chittering** from the approaching animal --- the "I'm here" announcement.
2. **Charf trill** from a second animal already present --- the "I know you" greeting.
3. **Mutual chittering** as the two animals approach each other --- social negotiation.
4. **Purring** if the animals settle into contact (grooming, resting side by side).
5. **Escalation to growling** if a third, unfamiliar animal approaches.
6. **Screaming** if the encounter escalates to physical conflict.
7. **Silence** as the conflict resolves (one animal retreats).
8. **Chittering** resuming as the remaining animals return to their previous activity.

This sequence --- greeting, negotiation, escalation, resolution, return to baseline --- is recognizable to anyone who has listened to raccoon activity over multiple nights. It is a social soundscape produced by animals that are communicating constantly, modulating their vocalizations in response to changing social circumstances, and maintaining a web of acoustic relationships that connects every raccoon in the neighborhood to every other.

The 200+ vocalizations are not a list. They are a language --- not in the human sense of symbolic reference and recursive grammar, but in the functional sense of a communicative system that conveys identity, intent, emotional state, and social context with enough specificity that the animals can coordinate their behavior in the dark, among strangers and family alike, across the full range of situations that a nocturnal, social, urban omnivore encounters.

*Charf charf.* You are familiar. You are welcome. I know you.
