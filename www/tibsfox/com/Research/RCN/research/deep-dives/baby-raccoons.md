# Found a Baby Raccoon? --- What to Do (and What Not to Do)

It is late April in the Pacific Northwest. You are cleaning up the yard, or walking the dog, or taking the garbage out at dusk, and there it is: a small, fuzzy shape huddled under a shrub, or wobbling across the sidewalk, or sitting on your porch step and looking up at you with dark eyes in a face that already has the mask. A baby raccoon. A kit. Possibly the single most disarmingly cute wild animal you will ever encounter. Its fur is soft and slightly spiky, like a bottle brush that has been left out in the rain. Its tiny hands --- and they are clearly hands, even at this size --- grip the edge of the step or the bark of a branch with an intensity that seems disproportionate to a body that weighs less than a pound. It makes a sound: a high, chattering whimper that is equal parts distress call and the kind of noise that makes every mammalian parenting instinct in your brain fire simultaneously.

You want to help it. Of course you do. You are a decent person and this is a baby animal and it seems to be alone and something has clearly gone wrong. The question is: what does "help" actually look like?

This document answers that question. The short version: in most cases, the best help you can give a baby raccoon is to leave it alone, wait, and call a professional. The long version --- including how to distinguish a genuinely orphaned kit from one whose mother is ten minutes away, what to do if the kit is truly in trouble, and why keeping it yourself is a terrible idea no matter how cute it is --- follows.

---

## Is It Really Orphaned?

This is the first and most important question, and the answer is "probably not." Raccoon mothers are devoted, resourceful parents [RCN-05]. A mother raccoon with kits does not abandon them casually. But she does leave them --- sometimes for hours at a time --- while she forages, relocates den sites, or investigates potential threats. A kit that appears to be alone may simply be waiting for a mother who is nearby and will return.

### The 24-Hour Rule

Wildlife rehabilitators across the PNW use a consistent guideline: **wait 24 hours before intervening** with an apparently alone but otherwise healthy-looking baby raccoon. This is not negligence. It is evidence-based practice rooted in decades of experience with the species.

Raccoon mothers frequently move their kits from one den to another [RCN-05]. A litter of 3-5 kits is moved one or two at a time, carried by the scruff of the neck. During a den relocation, individual kits may be left at the old den, at the new den, or in transit for hours while the mother shuttles siblings back and forth. A kit found alone on the ground in spring may be mid-transit --- temporarily set down while the mother goes back for a sibling.

Raccoon mothers also leave young kits in the den while foraging. Kits under 8-10 weeks of age are not yet coordinated enough to follow the mother, so she leaves them in a secure location (tree hollow, attic space, beneath a deck) and returns to nurse. If a kit has wandered from the den while the mother is away, it may be sitting in the open looking distressed simply because it is young, uncoordinated, and temporarily lost.

**The waiting protocol:**

1. Note the exact location where you found the kit.
2. If it is in immediate danger (in the road, in a puddle, being approached by a dog or cat), move it gently to the nearest sheltered location within a few feet of where you found it. Use gloves or a towel. Place it under a shrub, in a cardboard box tipped on its side, or at the base of a tree. The mother will find it by sound and scent.
3. Retreat to a distance of at least 50 feet. Do not hover. A mother raccoon will not approach her kit if she senses a human presence.
4. Wait. Check back at dusk (raccoons are most active from dusk to dawn). If the mother has returned and collected the kit, the problem is solved.
5. If the kit is still in the same location after 24 hours, it may genuinely be orphaned. Proceed to the next section.

### Signs That Intervention Is Needed Now

The 24-hour rule has exceptions. Some situations call for immediate action:

**Visible injury.** A kit with an obvious wound, broken limb, bleeding, or deformity needs immediate veterinary or rehabilitative care. Do not wait 24 hours for an injured animal.

**Cold body temperature.** A kit that feels cold to the touch (check by gently touching the belly through a cloth) is hypothermic and may not survive 24 hours of waiting. Healthy kits maintain body temperature through metabolic heat and huddling with siblings; a cold, isolated kit has been alone too long or is ill.

**Flies, maggots, or parasites.** Visible fly eggs (small white clusters, often on ears, eyes, or wounds) or maggots indicate an animal that has been debilitated for some time. This is an emergency.

**Continuous, uninterrupted crying.** Kits vocalize when their mother leaves --- this is normal [RCN deep-dives/raccoon-sounds]. But a kit that has been crying continuously for many hours without pause, particularly during daylight hours when the mother should be resting nearby, may be genuinely separated.

**Dead mother nearby.** If you find a dead adult raccoon (often roadkill) and a kit or kits nearby, the kits are orphaned. Collect them carefully and contact a rehabilitator immediately.

**Eyes still closed, found in the open.** Kits' eyes open at approximately 2-3 weeks of age [RCN-05]. A kit with closed eyes should be in a den, not out in the open. If you find a closed-eye kit outside a den, something has gone wrong --- the kit has fallen from a tree den, been displaced by a predator, or been separated during a den move. This kit needs help.

## What Not to Do

The impulse to rescue is powerful, and it leads well-meaning people into mistakes that make the situation worse. Here is what not to do.

### Do Not Feed It Cow's Milk

This is the number-one mistake people make with orphaned raccoon kits, and it can be fatal. **Cow's milk causes severe diarrhea in raccoon kits.** Raccoon milk has a composition that is substantially different from cow's milk --- higher fat, different protein ratios, different sugar content. Cow's milk overwhelms the kit's immature digestive system, producing watery diarrhea that leads to rapid dehydration. In a kit that is already stressed, dehydrated, and cold, the additional fluid loss can kill within hours.

Goat's milk is sometimes suggested as an alternative. It is marginally less harmful than cow's milk but still inappropriate. Do not feed any milk you have in your refrigerator to a raccoon kit.

If a rehabilitator directs you to provide feeding while transporting a kit, they will specify a wildlife-specific formula (typically Esbilac puppy milk replacer or Fox Valley Day One formula). Do not attempt to formulate your own substitute.

### Do Not Feed It Anything Unless Directed by a Rehabilitator

Beyond the milk issue, feeding a hypothermic or dehydrated kit before warming it can cause aspiration (inhaling fluid into the lungs) or digestive shutdown. The first priority for any distressed kit is warmth, not food. A rehabilitator will direct you on whether and what to feed, based on the kit's condition and age.

### Do Not Attempt to Raise It Yourself

This one is hard, because baby raccoons are powerfully, almost weaponizedly cute, and the fantasy of raising a pet raccoon has a persistent cultural appeal. But raising a raccoon yourself is:

**Illegal in most states.** Washington state law (WAC 220-450) prohibits keeping raccoons as pets without a wildlife rehabilitation permit. Oregon has similar restrictions (ORS 497.298, OAR 635-044). Violation can result in fines and seizure of the animal. These laws exist for good reasons.

**Bad for the raccoon.** A hand-raised raccoon that is not properly reared for release becomes a raccoon that cannot be a raccoon. It lacks the foraging skills, social behaviors, predator awareness, and territorial knowledge that a mother-reared kit acquires during 6-12 months of maternal tutelage [RCN-02, RCN-03]. It may be habituated to humans, which makes it dangerous (a 15-pound adult raccoon that has no fear of humans and expects food from them is a serious liability) and makes it unreleasable by rehabilitation facilities.

**Difficult in ways that are not obvious at first.** The trajectory is predictable and well-documented by wildlife rehabilitators who deal with the aftermath: the kit is adorable for the first 3-4 months. It is playful, affectionate, curious, and bonds tightly with its human caretaker. Then it hits approximately 5-6 months of age, and the personality changes. The playful curiosity becomes destructive exploration --- the raccoon opens every cabinet, empties every container, shreds every cushion, disassembles every object within reach. The affectionate biting of infancy becomes the firm, sometimes painful biting of a maturing carnivore. The bond with the human caretaker remains, but it is expressed through behaviors that are appropriate between raccoons (face-nuzzling, play-fighting, food competition) and less appropriate with humans. Male raccoons approaching sexual maturity (10-12 months) often become aggressive, territorial, and unpredictable.

By the time the raccoon is a year old, the person who rescued the adorable kit is living with a 15-pound animal that can open the refrigerator, destroy a couch, bite hard enough to require stitches, and cannot be released because it does not know how to be wild. This is the scenario that wildlife rehabilitators see repeatedly, and it is why "do not raise it yourself" is not a suggestion. It is a plea.

### Do Not Handle Without Protection

Even very young raccoon kits can scratch, and their claws are sharp. Older kits can bite. Handle with leather gloves or a towel wrapped around the kit. This protects both you and the animal --- a panicking kit that scratches you will be more stressed, not less.

## What To Do: Getting the Kit to a Professional

If you have determined that the kit is genuinely orphaned (mother confirmed dead, kit has been alone for 24+ hours, or kit shows signs of injury or distress that require immediate intervention), the next step is getting it to a licensed wildlife rehabilitator.

### Temporary Stabilization

While you are arranging transport to a rehabilitator:

1. **Warmth first.** Place the kit in a small, dark, quiet container --- a cardboard box or plastic tote with ventilation holes, lined with a soft cloth (an old t-shirt or towel). Place a heat source in the container: a hot water bottle wrapped in a towel (not touching the kit directly), a microwaveable heating pad, or a sock filled with uncooked rice and microwaved for 1-2 minutes. Position the heat source on one side of the container so the kit can move toward or away from the warmth. The target temperature in the container is approximately 85-90 degrees F. Hypothermic kits may take 30-60 minutes to warm.

2. **Dark and quiet.** Cover the box with a towel. Place it in a quiet room away from children, pets, television, and household traffic. A stressed, hypothermic kit needs minimal stimulation, not comfort or entertainment. Resist the urge to check on it every five minutes. Leave it alone.

3. **No food or water unless directed.** A rehabilitator may instruct you to offer fluids (usually warmed Pedialyte, not water, not milk) via syringe or dropper. Do not attempt this without guidance --- aspirating fluid into the lungs is a real risk, especially with very young kits.

4. **Do not attempt to reunite the kit with other raccoons.** An orphaned kit placed near an unrelated female may be attacked. Raccoon mothers are protective of their own kits and may view an unfamiliar kit as a threat or a competitor.

- Whether you have seen a dead adult raccoon nearby

The rehabilitator will advise you on next steps. In most cases, they will ask you to bring the kit to the facility. Some facilities have volunteer transporters who can pick up animals.

## The Rehabilitation Process

What happens after a kit reaches a wildlife rehabilitation facility is a carefully managed process designed to produce a raccoon that can survive in the wild.

### Assessment and Medical Care

Incoming kits are examined by a veterinarian or trained rehabilitator. They are weighed, checked for injuries and parasites, and assessed for dehydration and hypothermia. Dehydrated kits receive subcutaneous fluids. Injured kits receive appropriate medical treatment. Very young kits (eyes closed, under 3 weeks) require round-the-clock bottle feeding on a 2-3 hour schedule.

### Feeding and Growth

Kits are fed a commercial wildlife milk replacer (typically Esbilac or Fox Valley formulas) via syringe or bottle until they are old enough to eat solid food (approximately 7-9 weeks). Weaning is gradual: softened dry food is introduced alongside formula, and the kit transitions to a diet that approximates the wild omnivore diet --- fruits, vegetables, insects, fish, eggs, and commercial wildlife feed.

### Socialization --- With Raccoons, Not Humans

This is the critical distinction between rehabilitation and pet-keeping. Rehabilitators deliberately minimize human contact with kits beyond what is necessary for feeding and medical care. The goal is to prevent human habituation and to encourage species-appropriate social development.

Orphaned kits are raised in groups whenever possible. A single kit is placed with other orphaned kits of similar age, forming a surrogate sibling group. The kits play, wrestle, groom, and establish social hierarchies with each other --- the same social development that would occur in a wild litter under maternal care [RCN-02, RCN-05]. Group-raised kits develop normally; solitary kits raised with only human contact often develop behavioral abnormalities that compromise their release potential.

As kits grow, they are moved to larger outdoor enclosures that simulate natural habitat: climbing structures, water features for foraging practice, natural substrates for digging, and den boxes for sleeping. Human contact is progressively reduced.

### Pre-Release and Release

Kits approaching release age (typically 16-20 weeks, or approximately 4-5 months) are assessed for:
- Weight and physical condition
- Ability to forage for natural foods
- Appropriate fear of humans and domestic animals
- Social competence with conspecifics
- Climbing, swimming, and other motor skills

Kits that pass assessment are released in suitable habitat --- typically rural or semi-rural areas with water access, denning sites, and adequate food resources. Release sites are selected to avoid conflicts with existing territorial raccoons and to minimize proximity to high-traffic roads.

The first few weeks post-release are the most dangerous. Released kits must establish territories, find food sources, avoid predators, and navigate an unfamiliar landscape. Survival rates for rehabilitated raccoons vary by study but are generally estimated at 50-70% through the first year --- comparable to survival rates for wild-born kits, which face the same challenges with the advantage of maternal guidance and the disadvantage of no veterinary care.

## Why Not a Pet: The Honest Assessment

The question comes up every spring, in every wildlife rehabilitation center, from well-meaning people holding baby raccoons: "Can I just keep it?"

The honest answer involves more than legality and difficulty. It involves what the raccoon experiences.

A raccoon raised as a pet is a social animal deprived of its own kind. Raccoons in the wild live in complex social groups: female kin networks, male coalitions, mother-kit bonds that last months [RCN-02, RCN-05]. A pet raccoon has a human family that loves it, but it does not have raccoon companions who speak its language, groom its face, sleep in a pile with it, and understand the 200+ vocalizations that constitute its communicative world [RCN deep-dives/raccoon-sounds]. It is, in a sense, permanently lonely --- fluent in a language that no one in the household speaks.

Add to this the practical realities: raccoons cannot be house-trained reliably. They establish latrines [RCN-03] and use them consistently, but a raccoon's definition of "appropriate latrine location" may not align with yours. They are destructive --- not maliciously, but because their entire sensory world is built on reaching into things, turning things over, pulling things apart, and finding out what is inside [RCN deep-dives/hands-and-intelligence]. A raccoon does not trash your kitchen out of spite. It trashes your kitchen because every cabinet is a puzzle, every container is a challenge, and every closed door is an invitation.

And then there is the maturation issue. A 4-month-old kit is charming. A 12-month-old raccoon is a different animal --- literally, hormonally, behaviorally. The personality shift that occurs as raccoons reach sexual maturity is not a defect or a failure of socialization. It is normal raccoon development. A juvenile raccoon that was cuddly and compliant becomes an adult raccoon that is independent, assertive, and capable of inflicting serious bites when frustrated or frightened. This transition catches many would-be pet owners off guard, and it is the point at which many "pet" raccoons are surrendered to rehabilitation centers --- older, human-habituated, often unable to be released, and sometimes euthanized because no placement exists.

The kindest thing you can do for a baby raccoon is get it to someone who knows how to give it back its wildness. That is not a sacrifice. It is a gift.

## The Emotional Reality

This document has been practical and clinical, as it should be. But it would be dishonest to pretend that finding a baby raccoon is an unemotional experience. Baby raccoons are, by any reasonable standard, among the most appealing baby animals on the planet. The oversized eyes, the already-visible mask, the tiny hands that grip your finger with surprising strength, the soft chuffing vocalizations that sound like a small, broken motor --- these features trigger caregiving responses that evolution spent millions of years wiring into the mammalian brain. You are not weak for wanting to help. You are not foolish for feeling a surge of protective tenderness. You are a mammal, responding to a baby mammal's distress signals, and your instincts are working exactly as they should.

The adult part of the response --- the part that distinguishes helping from harming --- is calling a professional, following their guidance, and accepting that the best outcome for the kit is one where it never sees you again. It goes to a rehabilitation center, it grows up with other raccoons, it learns to forage and climb and navigate the world, and one autumn day it is released into a PNW forest or riparian corridor, and it walks into the trees and does not look back.

That is a happy ending. It does not look like the happy ending you might have imagined when you first saw the kit on your porch step. But it is the one the raccoon needs.
