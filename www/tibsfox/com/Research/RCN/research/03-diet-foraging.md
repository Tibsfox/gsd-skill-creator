# Generalist Supreme --- Diet, Foraging, and the Omnivore's Advantage

## Introduction

If intelligence is the engine that drives the raccoon's success, diet breadth is the fuel. *Procyon lotor* is one of the most thoroughly omnivorous mammals in North America --- an animal that eats crayfish and corn, salmon and salal berries, bird eggs and garbage, Dungeness crab and cat food, with equal facility and apparent enthusiasm. The raccoon diet is not merely broad; it is strategically broad, shifting across seasons, habitats, and food availability in ways that maximize caloric intake while minimizing risk.

This dietary flexibility is enabled by the biological toolkit described in the previous documents: the generalized omnivore dentition, the hypersensitive forepaws, the intelligence to solve foraging problems, and the memory to retain solutions. Together, these attributes produce a forager of extraordinary competence --- an animal that can extract calories from nearly any environment, from pristine old-growth forest to the dumpster behind a fast-food restaurant.

## The Omnivore's Toolkit

### Dentition and Digestion

The raccoon's 40 teeth, with their broadened carnassials and bunodont molars, can process an enormous range of food types. The canines retain enough shearing capacity to dispatch small vertebrate prey. The premolars and molars crush invertebrate exoskeletons, crack nut shells, and grind plant material. The jaw musculature is powerful enough to open bivalve mollusks and break crab carapaces.

The digestive system is relatively unspecialized --- a short gut typical of carnivores, without the elaborate fermentation chambers of herbivores. This means raccoons extract less energy from fibrous plant material than specialized herbivores would, but they compensate by selecting the most energy-dense plant foods (fruits, nuts, grains) and supplementing heavily with animal protein. The digestive tract's transit time is rapid, which is advantageous for an animal that may need to process potentially contaminated food from garbage and other anthropogenic sources before pathogens can establish.

### The Hands as Foraging Instruments

Every aspect of raccoon hand morphology serves foraging. The five long, flexible digits can grip, probe, pry, tear, and manipulate with a dexterity that no other North American carnivore approaches. Raccoons can turn doorknobs, unscrew jar lids, open latches, and untie simple knots. In natural foraging contexts, these hands allow raccoons to:

- Overturn rocks in streams to expose crayfish and insect larvae
- Probe into narrow crevices where prey items shelter
- Peel bark to access wood-boring insects
- Open bivalve shells by inserting digits into the gap and prying
- Extract eggs from nests, including nests in narrow tree cavities
- Harvest fruits and nuts with selective precision

The forepaw's tactile sensitivity, enhanced by water immersion, allows raccoons to identify food items by touch alone, foraging effectively in turbid water, in darkness, and in confined spaces where vision is useless.

## Seasonal Diet Shifts

The raccoon diet follows a pronounced seasonal cycle driven by food availability, nutritional requirements, and the imperative to accumulate fat reserves before winter.

### Spring (March-May)

Emerging from winter inactivity with depleted fat reserves, spring raccoons are hungry and opportunistic. The spring diet emphasizes:

- **Invertebrates:** Earthworms, beetle larvae, and other soil invertebrates become available as soil temperatures rise. These are high-protein, relatively easy to obtain, and critical for rebuilding body condition after winter.
- **Emerging vegetation:** Young shoots, buds, and early greens provide vitamins and moisture.
- **Eggs and nestlings:** Spring coincides with the nesting season for many bird species, and raccoons are significant nest predators. Ground-nesting birds, cavity-nesting birds, and species that nest on or near water are particularly vulnerable.
- **Amphibians:** Breeding frogs, salamanders, and their egg masses are consumed where available.
- **Carrion:** Winter-killed animals and the remains of other predators' meals are scavenged.

Spring is a critical period for lactating females, who face the highest energetic demands of the year. A nursing female's caloric requirements may be 2-3 times those of a non-reproductive individual.

### Summer (June-August)

Summer represents the period of greatest dietary diversity:

- **Fruits and berries:** As they ripen sequentially through the summer, fruits become a major dietary component. In the PNW, this includes salmonberries (June), thimbleberries (June-July), blueberries and huckleberries (July-August), blackberries (August-September), and salal berries (August-September).
- **Insects:** Grasshoppers, beetles, crickets, and other insects are abundant and heavily consumed.
- **Crayfish:** A staple food item in areas with suitable aquatic habitat. Raccoons are among the most effective crayfish predators in North American freshwater systems.
- **Fish:** Raccoons capture fish opportunistically, particularly in shallow water, drying pools, and fish spawning areas. They are not efficient open-water fishers but excel at capturing fish in confined or shallow situations.
- **Corn:** In agricultural areas, ripening corn is a major food source and the source of significant agricultural conflict. Raccoons can devastate cornfields, consuming the ears and knocking down stalks.
- **Garden produce:** Tomatoes, melons, grapes, strawberries, and sweet corn attract raccoons to home gardens.

### Autumn (September-November)

Autumn is the season of hyperphagia --- the period of intense feeding that builds the fat reserves needed for winter survival. Caloric intake may increase 50-100% above summer levels:

- **Hard mast:** Acorns, walnuts, hazelnuts, and other hard mast crops are consumed in enormous quantities. In years of good mast production, raccoon populations tend to be healthier and survival rates higher the following winter.
- **Soft mast:** Late-ripening fruits, including wild grapes, persimmons (in eastern populations), and PNW species like Oregon grape.
- **Grains:** In agricultural landscapes, harvested and unharvested grain fields provide concentrated caloric resources.
- **Salmon carcasses:** In the PNW, this is one of the most distinctive and ecologically important components of the raccoon diet. More on this below.
- **Continued invertebrate and small vertebrate consumption:** Though diminishing as temperatures drop and prey availability decreases.

### Winter (December-February)

Winter diet is constrained by food availability and the animal's reduced activity:

- **Stored fat:** The primary energy source during periods of extended inactivity.
- **Cached food:** Raccoons do cache food items, though less systematically than squirrels or jays. Spatial memory for cache locations is well-documented.
- **Opportunistic feeding:** On milder winter days, raccoons may emerge to forage on whatever is available --- garbage, bird feeders, pet food, persistent fruits, and carrion.
- **Garbage and anthropogenic food:** Urban raccoons may remain relatively active throughout winter, sustained by the year-round availability of human food waste.

## PNW-Specific Diet Items

The Pacific Northwest offers a distinctive suite of food resources that shape raccoon ecology in the region.

### Salmon Carcasses

The annual return of Pacific salmon (Chinook, coho, chum, pink, and sockeye) to PNW rivers and streams provides a massive pulse of marine-derived protein and fat. Spawned-out salmon die in enormous numbers, and their carcasses litter stream banks and shallow waters from September through December.

Raccoons are enthusiastic consumers of salmon carcasses. They feed on the energy-rich brain, eyes, and abdominal fat deposits preferentially, often leaving the bulk of the carcass. This selective feeding pattern is similar to that of bears and is an efficient strategy for maximizing caloric return per unit of foraging effort.

The ecological significance of raccoon salmon consumption extends beyond the raccoons themselves. By dragging carcasses away from streams and feeding in the riparian zone, raccoons (along with bears, eagles, and other carcass consumers) transport marine-derived nutrients --- nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon --- into the terrestrial forest ecosystem. Isotope studies have traced these marine-derived nutrients through soil, vegetation, and invertebrate communities in riparian forests, demonstrating that salmon-consuming wildlife plays a measurable role in forest nutrient cycling.

In the PNW, a raccoon feeding on salmon carcasses is participating in one of the most celebrated nutrient transport pathways in ecology: the ocean-to-forest pipeline that connects marine productivity to terrestrial ecosystem health.

### Intertidal Resources

PNW raccoons inhabiting coastal areas and Puget Sound shorelines exploit intertidal food resources that are unavailable to raccoons in most of the species' continental range:

- **Dungeness crab** (*Metacarcinus magister*): Raccoons forage for crabs at low tide, cracking the carapace with their powerful jaws and extracting the meat with their dexterous forepaws.
- **Razor clams** (*Siliqua patula*): On sandy beaches, raccoons dig for razor clams, though they are less efficient at this than dedicated clam-digging shorebirds.
- **Mussels** (*Mytilus trossulus* and *M. californianus*): Mussels are pried from rocks and opened using forepaw manipulation.
- **Shore crabs** (*Hemigrapsus* spp. and *Pachygrapsus crassipes*): Small crabs found under intertidal rocks are consumed readily.
- **Barnacles:** Raccoons will scrape and consume barnacles from intertidal rocks, though these are not a preferred food.
- **Intertidal fish:** Sculpins, gunnels, and other small fish trapped in tide pools are captured.

This intertidal foraging behavior connects PNW raccoons to the marine food web in ways that inland populations cannot access, and it represents a distinctive ecological role that likely contributed to the differentiation of the *P. l. pacificus* subspecies.

### PNW Berries and Fruits

The PNW berry calendar is a raccoon's paradise:

- **Salmonberries** (*Rubus spectabilis*): June-July, orange to red, soft and sweet. One of the first berries to ripen.
- **Thimbleberries** (*Rubus parviflorus*): June-August, red, delicate, high sugar content.
- **Trailing blackberries** (*Rubus ursinus*): The native blackberry, smaller and sweeter than the invasive Himalayan blackberry. July-August.
- **Himalayan blackberries** (*Rubus armeniacus*): The dominant invasive, producing enormous quantities of fruit from August through October. Raccoons consume them heavily, and the invasive's abundance has likely increased food availability for PNW raccoon populations.
- **Salal berries** (*Gaultheria shallon*): August-September, dark purple, mildly sweet. A significant food source in forested areas.
- **Huckleberries** (*Vaccinium* spp.): Multiple species ripen from July through September at varying elevations.
- **Oregon grape** (*Mahonia aquifolium*): Tart berries available in late summer and fall.
- **Pacific crabapples** (*Malus fusca*): Small, tart fruits available in fall, consumed after frost softens them.

The sequential ripening of PNW berry species provides raccoons with fruit resources across a 4-5 month window, contributing to the hyperphagia period that builds winter fat reserves.

## Tactile Foraging: The Dousing Behavior

The raccoon's signature foraging behavior --- immersing the forepaws in water while manipulating food items --- deserves detailed examination in the foraging context.

### Mechanism

When a raccoon dousing food, the thin, glabrous skin of the palms absorbs water rapidly. The stratum corneum (the outermost keratinized layer) softens and swells, becoming more pliable and reducing the distance between external stimuli and the underlying Meissner's corpuscles and Merkel cells (the primary mechanoreceptors responsible for fine touch). The result is a measurable increase in tactile sensitivity.

This enhanced sensitivity allows the raccoon to:

1. **Distinguish food from debris:** In turbid water or darkness, raccoons can identify and separate food items from substrate material by touch alone.
2. **Assess food quality:** Texture, firmness, and surface characteristics provide information about ripeness, freshness, and the presence of parasites or damage.
3. **Detect and remove inedible components:** Shell fragments, bones, and other hard elements can be identified and discarded.
4. **Locate hidden prey:** Crayfish, insect larvae, and small fish hiding under rocks or in substrate can be detected by their movement against the raccoon's sensitized palms.

### Ecological Context

In natural settings, dousing behavior is most commonly observed during aquatic foraging --- searching under rocks in streams, probing stream banks, and exploring pond edges. Raccoons foraging on land often skip the dousing step, particularly when dealing with familiar food items. The behavior is most prominent when raccoons encounter novel food items or forage in aquatic environments where visibility is poor.

The dousing behavior is innate but refined through experience. Kits begin dousing food at approximately 8-10 weeks of age, and the precision and efficiency of the behavior improve throughout the first year of life as the kit's tactile processing matures and its mental library of food-item textures expands.

## Impact on Other Species

### Nest Predation

Raccoons are among the most significant nest predators in North American ecosystems. Their climbing ability, nocturnal habits, dexterous forepaws, and intelligence make them effective at locating and accessing nests that are protected against less capable predators.

Species particularly affected by raccoon nest predation include:

- **Ground-nesting birds:** Shorebirds, waterfowl, grouse, and sparrows nesting on or near the ground are highly vulnerable.
- **Cavity-nesting birds:** Raccoons can reach into nest cavities that exclude larger predators, taking eggs and nestlings from woodpecker holes, bluebird boxes, and other cavity sites.
- **Turtles:** Raccoon predation on turtle nests is a major conservation concern for multiple species. In some populations of painted turtles, snapping turtles, and endangered species like the western pond turtle, raccoon predation accounts for 80-90% of nest loss.
- **Colonial waterbirds:** Herons, egrets, and terns nesting in colonies are subject to raccoon predation when colonies are accessible from the mainland.
- **Marbled Murrelet:** In the PNW, this federally threatened seabird nests on large horizontal branches in old-growth forest. Raccoons are documented predators of murrelet nests, and because murrelets lay only a single egg per nesting attempt, each depredated nest represents a complete reproductive failure.

The impact of raccoon nest predation is amplified by the species' high population densities in human-modified landscapes. Artificially elevated raccoon populations in suburban and agricultural areas can create "predator traps" where bird reproductive success is suppressed below replacement levels.

### Crayfish and Aquatic Invertebrates

Raccoons are significant predators of crayfish in freshwater systems. In some streams and ponds, raccoon predation is the primary source of crayfish mortality. The impact varies by system, but in small streams with high raccoon densities, crayfish populations can be substantially reduced.

In the PNW, this has potential implications for the invasive signal crayfish (*Pacifastacus leniusculus*) as well as native crayfish species. The interaction between raccoon predation and crayfish community composition is an area that warrants further research.

## Caching Behavior

Raccoons cache food, though they are not as systematic about it as squirrels, jays, or other dedicated caching species. Food items are buried in shallow excavations, stuffed into tree cavities, or wedged into crevices. Raccoons rely on spatial memory to relocate caches, and their memory for cache locations is reliable over periods of days to weeks.

Caching is most common during autumn, when food abundance exceeds immediate consumption capacity. Cached items include nuts, fruits, and occasionally animal prey. The perishability of many cached items limits the utility of long-term caching for raccoons compared to species that cache durable items like acorns and pine seeds.

The spatial memory demands of caching may have been one of the selective pressures driving the evolution of the raccoon's impressive cognitive abilities. An animal that can remember the locations of dozens of food caches, the productivity of multiple foraging patches, and the ripening schedules of multiple fruit species needs a substantial spatial memory system --- exactly the kind of cognitive machinery that raccoon brains appear to possess.

## Agricultural Impacts

### The Corn Problem

Raccoon damage to agricultural crops, particularly corn, constitutes a significant economic issue in many parts of North America. Raccoon depredation of corn is concentrated in the "milk stage" of kernel development, when the ears are high in sugar and moisture. Damage is distinctive: raccoons pull down stalks, husk the ears partially, and eat the kernels from the cob, often consuming only the upper portion of each ear before moving to the next.

Estimates of annual raccoon damage to U.S. corn crops range from $50 million to several hundred million dollars, depending on the methodology and year. In some fields adjacent to woodlands or riparian corridors (which provide daytime cover and den sites), raccoon damage can exceed 50% of the crop.

### Orchard and Vineyard Damage

Raccoons also damage fruit orchards and vineyards, consuming ripe fruit and breaking branches during foraging. Cherries, grapes, peaches, and plums are particularly targeted. The damage per animal is modest, but in areas with high raccoon densities, aggregate losses can be substantial.

### Poultry Predation

Raccoons are the number-one predator of domestic poultry in many areas of the United States --- a fact of direct relevance to the FWL (Fowl) research cluster. Raccoon predation on chickens, ducks, and other poultry is characterized by:

- **Reaching through wire mesh:** Raccoon forepaws can reach through standard chicken wire and hardware cloth with openings larger than 1 inch, grasping and pulling birds against the wire.
- **Opening latches:** Simple slide bolts, hook-and-eye closures, and even some keyed locks can be opened by raccoons. Raccoon-proofing a chicken coop requires either combination locks, padlocks, or multi-step latching mechanisms.
- **Digging:** Raccoons will dig under coop walls and fencing if other entry points are unavailable.
- **Persistent attacks:** Raccoons that gain access to a coop may kill multiple birds in a single event, consuming only portions of each --- a behavior called "surplus killing" that is common in predators that encounter artificially concentrated prey.

The raccoon-poultry conflict illustrates the broader tension between human food production systems and raccoon foraging intelligence. Any food resource that is accessible to raccoon hands and raccoon problem-solving will eventually be accessed.

## Urban Foraging

### The Garbage Specialist

Urban raccoons have access to the most concentrated and reliable food source in the species' evolutionary history: human garbage. A single residential garbage can may contain more calories than a raccoon could obtain in a night of natural foraging. Commercial dumpsters behind restaurants, grocery stores, and cafeterias represent an even more extravagant bounty.

The result is predictable: urban raccoon populations are denser, better-nourished, and more reproductively successful than rural populations. Urban raccoons are also fatter, which makes them more likely to survive winter and enter the breeding season in good condition.

### Pet Food and Bird Feeders

Outdoor pet food is one of the most common attractants for urban raccoons. Cat food, in particular, is highly palatable to raccoons --- it is protein-rich, fat-rich, and conveniently served in accessible dishes at ground level. The "cat food war" is a familiar scenario to anyone who has fed outdoor cats: the cat food disappears at an alarming rate, the cat is mysteriously hungry, and eventually a trail camera reveals a procession of raccoons visiting the feeding station nightly.

Bird feeders attract raccoons both directly (seed and suet are consumed) and indirectly (spilled seed attracts rodents, which attract raccoons). Raccoons are capable of defeating most "squirrel-proof" bird feeder designs, which are engineered for the weight and climbing abilities of squirrels rather than the greater weight and manual dexterity of raccoons.

### Compost Bins

Home compost bins containing food scraps are powerful raccoon attractants. Raccoons can open most compost bin lids, climb into open bins, and excavate buried food scraps. The combination of food availability and the raccoon's willingness to consume partially decomposed material makes compost management a significant aspect of urban raccoon coexistence.

### The Learning Cascade

Urban foraging opportunities create a positive feedback loop. Raccoons that learn to exploit anthropogenic food sources gain a nutritional advantage. Their higher survival and reproductive rates increase local population density. Higher density increases the probability that new foraging innovations (how to open a particular dumpster lid, how to access a particular pet door) will be discovered by at least one individual and then transmitted through the population via social learning.

The result is an arms race between human attempts to secure food waste and raccoon ingenuity in defeating those attempts. To date, the raccoons are generally winning.

## Conclusion

The raccoon diet is a masterwork of generalist adaptation. The species can extract nutrition from virtually any organic material, adjust its foraging strategy to match seasonal and local food availability, and learn new foraging techniques throughout its life. This dietary flexibility is not merely a convenience --- it is the foundation of the raccoon's ecological success across the broadest habitat range of any North American carnivore.

In the PNW, the raccoon's omnivory connects it to ecosystems from the intertidal zone to the forest canopy, from salmon-bearing streams to suburban garbage cans. Understanding what raccoons eat, and how they obtain it, is essential for understanding their ecological role, their economic impacts, and the dynamics of human-raccoon coexistence.

The generalist supreme does not specialize in any single food source. It specializes in the absence of specialization --- and that, paradoxically, is its greatest specialization of all.
