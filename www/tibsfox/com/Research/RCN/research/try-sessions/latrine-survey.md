# Try Session: Safe Raccoon Latrine Survey

*A safety-first field exercise in identifying, mapping, and (if on your property) cleaning raccoon communal defecation sites*

---

## Why This Matters

Raccoon latrines are the single most important public health concern associated with raccoons in the Pacific Northwest. Not rabies (the raccoon rabies variant is absent from the PNW). Not bites (raccoon bites are rare and almost always provoked). The danger is in the feces, and specifically in what lives inside it.

*Baylisascaris procyonis* --- raccoon roundworm --- is a nematode parasite carried by an estimated 70-80% of raccoons in the PNW [RCN deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease]. Adult raccoons tolerate the worms without significant illness. But the eggs shed in raccoon feces are a different matter entirely. These microscopic eggs are extraordinarily durable: they remain viable in soil for **years**, they resist freezing, they resist most chemical disinfectants, and they survive environmental conditions that would destroy most other parasites. If ingested by a human --- typically a young child who contacts contaminated soil and then touches their mouth --- the larvae hatch, migrate through body tissues (visceral larva migrans), and can enter the central nervous system (neural larva migrans), causing severe, potentially fatal neurological damage. There is no reliable treatment once CNS involvement occurs.

The eggs accumulate at latrines --- the communal defecation sites that raccoons return to repeatedly, night after night, month after month. A single latrine can contain millions of eggs. Knowing where latrines are located on your property and in your neighborhood protects children, pets, and you. This try session teaches you how to find them, identify them, map them, and --- if they are on your own property --- clean them up safely.

**This session is safety-first by design.** Every instruction prioritizes your health. Follow the protocols exactly.

## Part 1: What a Latrine Looks Like

### The Defining Feature: Flat, Elevated Surfaces

Raccoons do not defecate randomly. They select specific sites and return to them consistently, creating accumulations of feces (and therefore Baylisascaris eggs) that build up over time. The site selection follows a consistent pattern: raccoons prefer **flat, elevated surfaces** for communal defecation.

**Common latrine surfaces:**

- **Tree stumps.** Large, flat-topped stumps (12+ inches in diameter) are classic latrine sites. The flat top catches droppings and the height provides the slight elevation raccoons prefer.
- **Fallen logs.** Particularly large, flat-topped logs in wooded or landscaped areas. Raccoons will walk along the log and defecate at the widest, flattest point.
- **Large flat rocks.** Boulders, rock walls, and decorative stone features in gardens and landscaping.
- **Rooftops.** Flat or low-slope roofs on garages, sheds, outbuildings, and occasionally houses. This is a frequently missed latrine location because homeowners do not routinely inspect their roofs.
- **Decks and porches.** The flat surface of a deck, particularly unused back decks, covered porches, and elevated walkways.
- **Woodpile tops.** The flat top surface of a stacked woodpile.
- **Bases of trees.** At the base of large trees, particularly conifers with dense root flares that create a flat, slightly elevated platform at ground level.
- **Attic spaces.** If raccoons are denning in an attic, they will establish a latrine inside the attic, typically in a corner or along a wall. This is a serious contamination concern and requires professional remediation.
- **Crawl spaces.** Similar to attics --- if raccoons access a crawl space, latrines will form.

### What the Feces Look Like

Raccoon feces are distinguishable from other common PNW wildlife scat:

- **Shape:** Tubular, similar to a small dog's stool. Typically 2-3 inches long and 1/2 to 3/4 inch in diameter.
- **Color:** Dark brown to black when fresh, fading to gray as it ages and weathers.
- **Contents:** Often contains visible, undigested material --- seed fragments, berry seeds, insect exoskeleton pieces, shell fragments, and sometimes feather or hair. This visible content reflects the raccoon's omnivorous diet [RCN-03] and distinguishes it from cat feces (which are typically uniform in texture and buried).
- **Accumulation pattern:** The defining characteristic of a latrine is **repeated deposition in the same spot**. A single raccoon dropping could be from any passing animal. A pile of droppings in various stages of freshness (some fresh, some dried, some partially decomposed) on a flat surface is a latrine. Multiple raccoons may use the same latrine, creating substantial accumulations.

### Distinguishing From Other Animal Scat

| Animal | Scat Description | Key Differences |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Raccoon** | Tubular, 2-3", dark, visible seeds/shells, accumulated on flat surfaces | Repeated same-spot deposition, visible food fragments |
| **Domestic cat** | Tubular, 2-3", usually buried, uniform texture | Typically buried in soil or litter, no visible seeds |
| **Domestic dog** | Variable size, usually on ground, no pattern | Random deposition, no site fidelity |
| **Opossum** | Similar to raccoon but slightly smaller, less consistent site use | Less reliable latrine behavior, scat may be more scattered |
| **Coyote** | Rope-like, often with hair/bone fragments, deposited on trails | Typically on trails or prominent objects (marking behavior), contains fur/bone |
| **Skunk** | Small, variable, often with insect parts | Usually on ground, no elevated surface preference |

If you are uncertain whether a scat accumulation is raccoon, look for the combination of: flat/elevated surface + repeated deposition + visible food fragments (especially berry seeds). That combination is diagnostic.

## Part 2: Survey Protocol

### Rule One: Visual Only

**Do not touch raccoon feces. Do not disturb a latrine. Do not kick it, poke it, step on it, or get within arm's reach unless you are actively cleaning your own property (see Part 3).**

Baylisascaris eggs become infective 2-4 weeks after being deposited, once they embryonate in the environment. Fresh feces may or may not contain infective eggs, but weathered feces almost certainly do. The eggs are microscopic. You cannot see them. You cannot smell them. They are in the feces, in the soil underneath the feces, in the material the feces has been sitting on, and potentially in the soil for inches around the latrine where rain has washed them. Treat every latrine as if it is actively contaminated with infective Baylisascaris eggs, because it almost certainly is.

### Equipment for Survey

- **Disposable nitrile gloves.** Wear them even though you are not touching anything. If you trip, lean on a surface, or instinctively reach out for balance near a latrine, gloves prevent direct skin contact.
- **Garbage bags.** If you are surveying your own property and decide to proceed to cleanup, you will need heavy-duty bags for contaminated material (see Part 3).
- **Notebook or phone for recording.** Note location, surface type, estimated age (fresh vs. old), approximate volume, and take a photograph.
- **Closed-toe shoes.** Not sandals. Not barefoot. If you step near or on a contaminated surface, shoes protect your feet from contaminated soil.

### Survey Areas

Walk your property systematically, checking every flat, elevated surface:

**Ground level:**
- [ ] Tree stumps
- [ ] Fallen logs
- [ ] Decorative boulders and rock features
- [ ] Woodpile tops
- [ ] Base of large trees (check root flares)
- [ ] Garden bed borders and raised bed edges
- [ ] Retaining wall tops
- [ ] Fence rail tops (especially at corners and junctions)

**Elevated structures:**
- [ ] Deck surfaces (including the edges and corners of raised decks)
- [ ] Porch surfaces
- [ ] Garage roof (use binoculars from the ground or check from a ladder if safe)
- [ ] Shed roof
- [ ] Playhouse or play structure platforms
- [ ] Hot tub cover (closed hot tubs with flat covers are prime latrine sites)
- [ ] Firewood rack tops

**Enclosed spaces (inspect from outside if possible):**
- [ ] Attic (check for signs of raccoon entry: torn soffit vents, chewed fascia)
- [ ] Crawl space (check access points; do not enter without PPE if contamination is suspected)
- [ ] Under decks (if accessible, inspect with a flashlight from outside)

### Recording Your Findings

For each latrine identified, record:

```
Location: ___________
Surface type: (stump / log / rock / deck / roof / other) ___________
Approximate size: (area of contamination in square feet) ___________
Estimated age: (fresh / recent / old / mixed ages) ___________
Proximity to play areas: ___________
Proximity to garden/food production: ___________
Photo: (yes/no, filename) ___________
Notes: ___________
```

## Part 3: Cleanup Protocol (Your Own Property Only)

If you find a raccoon latrine on your own property, especially near areas where children play, where food is grown, or where pets have access, cleanup is warranted. This section describes the safe cleanup procedure.

**If the latrine is in an attic, crawl space, or other enclosed area:** Hire a professional wildlife remediation company. Enclosed-space latrine cleanup involves confined-space safety, respiratory protection, and contaminated-material disposal that is beyond the scope of DIY work. This is not an area to save money.

**If the latrine is on an outdoor surface you own (stump, deck, rock, log):**

### Personal Protective Equipment

- **Disposable nitrile or rubber gloves.** Double-glove (two pairs).
- **N95 or P100 respirator mask.** Baylisascaris eggs can become airborne in dust. Do not use a cloth mask or surgical mask --- they do not provide adequate particulate filtration.
- **Disposable coveralls or old clothes** that you will dispose of after cleanup (bag and trash, do not wash in your household laundry).
- **Rubber boots or disposable shoe covers.** Anything that contacts the contaminated area should be decontaminated or disposed of.
- **Eye protection.** Safety glasses or goggles.

### The Critical Step: Heat

**The single most important thing to know about Baylisascaris cleanup is that heat kills the eggs and chemical disinfectants do NOT.**

Bleach does not kill Baylisascaris eggs. Lysol does not kill Baylisascaris eggs. Ammonia does not kill Baylisascaris eggs. Pine-Sol, hydrogen peroxide, alcohol --- none of them. The eggs are protected by a multi-layered chitinous shell that is essentially impervious to chemical disinfection at practical concentrations.

What kills them is **heat**. Specifically, temperatures above 160 degrees F sustained for several minutes. The practical application: **boiling water.**

### Cleanup Steps

1. **Boil a large pot of water.** You will need several gallons. A stock pot or canning pot works. Bring to a rolling boil.

2. **Pour boiling water slowly and thoroughly over the entire latrine area.** Cover every surface that has visible fecal material, and extend 6-12 inches beyond the visible contamination. Allow the water to soak and run off. The goal is to heat the contaminated surface and the top layer of soil/material to a temperature that kills the eggs.

3. **Wait 5-10 minutes.** Allow the heat to penetrate.

4. **Remove contaminated material.** Using a shovel, scraper, or disposable scoop, remove all visible fecal material and the top 1-2 inches of substrate (soil, wood surface, leaf litter) beneath and around the latrine. Place everything in heavy-duty garbage bags.

5. **Pour a second application of boiling water** over the cleaned surface.

6. **For porous surfaces (wood stumps, logs, unsealed decks):** The eggs may have penetrated into the grain of the wood. Consider removing and disposing of the stump or log entirely. For decks, a propane torch or heat gun can be used to heat the wood surface after cleaning (use extreme caution with fire --- wet the surrounding area first).

7. **Seal the garbage bags.** Double-bag. Dispose of as regular household trash. Baylisascaris is not classified as a biohazard requiring special disposal for residential-scale cleanup.

8. **Decontaminate your equipment.** Wash shovels and scrapers with boiling water. Dispose of gloves, coveralls, and shoe covers in the trash.

9. **Remove your PPE in the correct order:** Gloves (peel off inside-out), coveralls, shoe covers, eye protection, mask. Wash hands thoroughly with soap and hot water.

### Preventing Re-Establishment

Raccoons are creatures of habit. A cleaned latrine will be re-colonized within days unless you modify the site:

- **Remove the surface.** Grind the stump, remove the log, remove the rock. No flat surface, no latrine.
- **Make the surface inaccessible.** Cover the area with hardware cloth secured to the ground. Raccoons will not defecate on wire mesh.
- **Install a motion-activated deterrent.** A motion-activated sprinkler (Orbit Yard Enforcer, $50-70) placed near the former latrine site deters raccoons from returning. The startling burst of water does not harm the animal but interrupts the behavior pattern.
- **Remove attractants.** If the latrine is near a food source (garbage, pet food, garden), securing those attractants reduces raccoon traffic through the area [RCN deep-dives/raccoon-proofing].

## Part 4: Mapping

If you survey a larger area (a neighborhood, a park edge, a school grounds, a community garden), the latrine locations form a map of raccoon activity intensity. This map has practical value:

### What the Map Tells You

- **Where raccoons are most active.** High-density latrine areas indicate high-use zones --- probably near den sites, foraging concentrations, or travel route intersections.
- **Where children are at risk.** A latrine on a playground stump, near a school garden, or on a community park structure is a public health issue. Report it to your local health department or parks department.
- **Where raccoon-proofing efforts should focus.** If latrines cluster near certain structures, those structures are high-priority targets for raccoon exclusion.

### How to Map

A simple sketch map is sufficient for a personal survey. For a more systematic approach:

1. **Mark each latrine location** on a satellite image of your area (Google Maps, Apple Maps, or a printed aerial photo).
2. **Note the surface type** at each location.
3. **Rate the activity level:** Active (fresh feces present), Recent (dried but identifiable feces), Historical (decomposed feces, staining on surface).
4. **Look for patterns:** Are latrines concentrated along a particular route (fence line, creek bank, tree line)? Do they cluster near certain structures or food sources?

### Reporting

If you find a raccoon latrine in a public area --- a park, a school, a community garden, a public playground --- report it:

- **Washington:** Contact your local health department (county health jurisdiction). King County Public Health, Snohomish Health District, Tacoma-Pierce County Health Department all handle environmental health complaints.
- **Oregon:** Contact your county health department or the Oregon Health Authority Environmental Health section.
- **Parks and playgrounds:** Contact the managing agency directly (city parks department, school district facilities management).

Public health departments take Baylisascaris reports seriously. A confirmed latrine in a public area, particularly near a playground or school, will typically result in a professional cleanup response.

## Part 5: What You Will Learn

This exercise develops skills that are, frankly, not glamorous. You are walking around looking at animal feces. There is no charismatic megafauna, no beautiful plumage, no mesmerizing underwater foraging behavior [RCN try-sessions/night-observation]. What there is, instead, is something more foundational: the ability to read an animal's presence through the signs it leaves behind, and the knowledge to protect yourself and others from a genuine, if uncommon, health risk.

### Field Identification

You will train your eye to distinguish raccoon scat from the feces of other animals by shape, content, deposition pattern, and site selection. This is a core naturalist skill: reading signs. Trackers and field biologists spend years developing the ability to identify an animal from its scat, and this exercise is a focused entry into that discipline.

### Risk Assessment

You will learn to evaluate a real environmental health risk with a calibrated response. Baylisascaris is serious but rare. Latrine contamination is real but manageable. The appropriate response is not panic (most people who live near raccoons will never be affected) and not dismissal (the consequences of actual infection are devastating). The appropriate response is informed awareness: know where the latrines are, keep children away, clean your own property, and report public hazards.

### Spatial Ecology

If you map latrine locations, you are doing spatial ecology --- the study of how organisms use space. The latrine map reflects raccoon movement patterns, territory use, and resource distribution. Combined with trail camera data [RCN try-sessions/build-a-trail-cam-station] and night observation records [RCN try-sessions/night-observation], the latrine survey contributes to a multi-layered picture of raccoon ecology in your specific location.

## Safety Summary

Before beginning your survey, review these rules:

1. **Do not touch feces, contaminated soil, or latrine surfaces without PPE.**
2. **Wear disposable gloves even for visual-only surveys.**
3. **Wear an N95/P100 mask during cleanup.**
4. **Use boiling water, not chemical disinfectants, to kill Baylisascaris eggs.**
5. **Do not enter attics or crawl spaces with suspected latrines without professional guidance.**
6. **Keep children and pets away from identified latrine sites until cleanup is complete.**
7. **Wash hands thoroughly after any outdoor survey, even if you did not touch anything.**
8. **Report public-area latrines to your local health department.**
9. **Prevent re-establishment after cleanup by modifying or removing the latrine surface.**
