# Try Session: Reading Raccoon Signs

*A field guide to identifying raccoon presence without seeing the animal*

---

## Why Track and Sign?

You may never see a raccoon in your neighborhood, even if a dozen live within a block of your house. They are nocturnal, they are wary of humans when they have reason to be, and they spend their daylight hours in den sites that are often invisible from ground level --- tree cavities, attic spaces, storm drains, dense brush piles. But raccoons are not ghosts. They leave evidence of their presence everywhere they go: in the mud, on the walls of buildings, in the pattern of damage to a garden, in the contents of their latrines. Learning to read this evidence is a foundational skill of field naturalism, and it is deeply satisfying --- you are reconstructing an animal's behavior from the traces it left behind, like a detective reading a crime scene.

This try session teaches you to identify raccoon tracks, scat, damage patterns, and other signs in the field. It includes safety information (raccoon latrines are a genuine health hazard), a guide to setting up affordable trail cameras for confirmation, and connections to citizen science platforms for logging your findings.

## Part 1: Tracks

### The Front Paw

The raccoon front paw print is one of the most distinctive and recognizable animal tracks in North America. It looks like a small human handprint. Five long, thin, separated digits radiate from a central palm pad, with each toe tipped by a short, non-retractable claw. The overall shape is wider than it is long --- approximately 5-7 cm (2-2.75 inches) wide and 5-6 cm (2-2.5 inches) long in an adult. The fingers are remarkably well-defined in good substrate (mud, wet sand, soft soil, fresh snow), and you can often see the individual finger pads.

**Key features:**
- Five distinct, separated toes
- Toes are long and thin (much longer relative to palm size than any other common North American mammal except opossum)
- Small, rounded palm pad
- Short claw marks ahead of each toe (claws do not retract)
- Overall shape: wider than long, hand-like

### The Hind Paw

The hind paw print is dramatically different from the front. It is elongated --- more like a miniature human footprint than a handprint. The five toes are arranged more parallel to each other (less splayed than the front), and a long, narrow heel pad extends behind the toes, giving the print an overall length of 7-10 cm (2.75-4 inches). This is because raccoons walk plantigrade on their hind feet --- the entire sole, from toe to heel, contacts the ground, just as in humans and bears.

**Key features:**
- Five toes, less splayed than front
- Long heel pad extending well behind the toes
- Overall shape: longer than wide, foot-like
- Claw marks present ahead of each toe
- Significantly larger than the front print

### The Walking Pattern

When a raccoon walks at its normal pace, it produces a distinctive track pattern. The hind foot registers ahead of and slightly to the inside of the front foot on the same side, because the hind leg swings forward past the front leg during each stride. This produces paired prints where the larger hind print sits slightly ahead of and overlapping the smaller front print. The stride length (distance between paired prints on the same side) is typically 30-45 cm (12-18 inches) in an adult walking at a normal pace.

**Track pattern at a glance:**
```
Right side:        Left side:

  [Hind R]           [Hind L]
[Front R]          [Front L]

                      [Hind R]
                   [Front R]

  [Hind L]
[Front L]
```

The paired prints, with the large hind print ahead of the small hand-like front print, are highly diagnostic. Few other animals produce this specific pattern. Opossum tracks can look somewhat similar (they also have five-toed, hand-like front prints), but opossum hind prints show a widely divergent, thumb-like hallux (big toe) that points sideways --- raccoons have no opposable digit.

### Where to Find Tracks

**Mud along water edges.** Creek banks, pond margins, and muddy trail edges after rain are the best track substrates. Raccoons forage extensively along water, and soft mud preserves excellent detail.

**Snow.** Fresh, shallow snow (2-5 cm deep) records raccoon tracks beautifully. Look along fence lines, stream corridors, and building perimeters. Deep snow obscures the toe detail.

**Dusty surfaces.** Fine dust on a paved surface --- parking lots, sidewalks, porches --- can hold surprisingly clear raccoon prints. Look for them in the morning before foot traffic disturbs them.

**Your own muddy garden.** After a rain, check soft garden soil for tracks. If raccoons are raiding your garden (and they are, if you grow corn, tomatoes, or grapes), their tracks will be everywhere.

### Practice Exercise

Find a location with suitable substrate (mud, wet sand, or fresh snow) within 50 meters of water or a known raccoon activity area. Visit in the morning and look for tracks. For each set of tracks you find, record:

1. Front or hind? (Identify by shape)
2. Size (measure or estimate width and length)
3. How many individuals? (Different sizes = different animals)
4. Direction of travel
5. What was the animal doing? (Straight-line travel = commuting; meandering with stops = foraging)

## Part 2: Scat (Droppings)

### Identification

Raccoon scat is typically tubular, 5-8 cm (2-3 inches) long and 1.5-2 cm (0.5-0.75 inch) in diameter. It is often broken into segments. The color and contents vary dramatically with diet:

- **Spring/summer:** Often dark, may contain visible insect exoskeletons (shiny wing fragments), crayfish shell pieces, or berry seeds.
- **Fall:** Frequently contains visible seeds, fruit skins, or corn kernels. May be lighter in color due to high fruit content.
- **Urban:** Can contain almost anything --- food packaging fragments, foil bits, and other non-food items are not unusual.

The most useful diagnostic feature is not the scat itself but its **location**.

### Latrines

Raccoons are one of the few North American mammals that defecate communally in specific, repeatedly used locations called **latrines**. A raccoon latrine is a site where one or more raccoons return repeatedly to deposit scat, building up accumulations that can become quite large over weeks and months.

**Typical latrine locations:**
- Flat, elevated surfaces: the tops of fallen logs, large flat rocks, tree stumps, and flat boulders
- Horizontal branches of large trees (look up --- raccoon latrines can be 10+ meters up in a tree)
- Rooftops, especially flat portions near access points (chimneys, roof vents)
- At the base of trees, particularly den trees
- Raised decks and porches
- Woodpiles

The preference for flat, elevated surfaces is consistent across habitats. If you find a concentration of scat on a log, rock, or rooftop, and the scat contains seeds, shell fragments, or other evidence of an omnivorous diet, it is almost certainly a raccoon latrine.

### CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING: Baylisascaris

**Do NOT disturb, touch, or attempt to clean a raccoon latrine without proper protection.** Raccoon latrines are the primary environmental reservoir for *Baylisascaris procyonis* eggs --- the raccoon roundworm. This parasite is a serious public health concern.

Key facts:
- *Baylisascaris* eggs are shed in raccoon feces and become infectious after 2-4 weeks of environmental exposure.
- The eggs are extraordinarily durable. They can survive in soil for **years**, resisting desiccation, freezing, and most chemical disinfectants.
- If ingested by a human (typically via hand-to-mouth contact with contaminated soil), the larvae migrate through body tissues. If they reach the central nervous system (neural larva migrans), they cause severe, irreversible neurological damage. There is no reliable post-infection treatment.
- Children are at highest risk because of their tendency to play in soil and put their hands in their mouths.
- Prevalence in PNW raccoons varies by study, but infection rates of 60-80% have been reported in some populations.

**If you find a raccoon latrine on your property:**
- Do not allow children or pets near it.
- Contact your local health department for cleanup guidance.
- Professional cleanup involves removing all contaminated material (including underlying soil), sealing it in bags, and disposing of it. The site should be treated with heat (boiling water or flame) --- chemical disinfectants are largely ineffective against *Baylisascaris* eggs.

**In the field:** Observe latrines from a distance. Do not step on or near them. Do not touch the scat. If you accidentally contact it, wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water.

## Part 3: Damage Signs

Raccoons leave distinctive evidence of their presence through the damage they cause to gardens, structures, and other features of the human environment. Learning to recognize these patterns lets you identify raccoon activity even when no tracks or scat are visible.

### Rolled-Back Sod

If you find patches of lawn where the turf has been peeled back or rolled up in strips, exposing the soil beneath, raccoons are the most likely culprit. They do this to access grubs (beetle larvae, especially Japanese beetle and European chafer grubs) that live in the root zone of grass. The raccoon inserts its forepaws under the sod edge, locates grubs by touch, and peels the turf back like a carpet. The resulting damage looks like someone has taken a spatula to your lawn. Skunks also dig for grubs but produce small, conical holes rather than rolled-back strips.

### Torn Garbage

Raccoons are methodical garbage raiders. Look for:
- Bags pulled from bins (raccoons can unlatch many "secure" container lids)
- Bags torn open with long claw-and-tooth rips (not the small punctures of rats)
- Contents sorted --- raccoons selectively remove food items and leave non-food packaging scattered
- Garbage spread over a wide area (raccoons often carry items away from the bin to eat)
- Repeated raids on the same night of the week (raccoons learn garbage collection schedules and raid bins the night before pickup, when bins are fullest)

### Pried-Open Containers

Raccoons are extraordinary problem-solvers, and their dexterous forepaws can manipulate latches, clasps, and lids that defeat most other animals. Evidence of raccoon entry includes:
- Pet food containers with lids removed or pried off
- Bird feeders dismounted from their poles or hooks
- Compost bins with lids pushed aside
- Garden cloches or row covers pulled up at the corners
- Screened vents or soffit panels pulled away from buildings (attic access)

### Muddy Handprints

One of the most charming and diagnostic signs of raccoon activity: muddy five-fingered handprints on walls, downspouts, window frames, and other vertical surfaces. Raccoons that have been foraging in mud or wet soil leave clear prints as they climb structures. Look for them on:
- Downspout surfaces (raccoons climb downspouts to access rooftops)
- Foundation walls near garden-level windows
- Fence posts and gate frames
- Siding near known entry points (soffit gaps, attic vents)

These prints are unmistakable --- no other urban animal leaves five-fingered handprints on vertical surfaces.

### Garden Damage

Raccoons in the garden produce specific damage patterns:
- **Corn:** Stalks pulled down, husks peeled back, ears partially eaten with tooth marks. Raccoons typically wait until corn is at peak sweetness (milk stage) and destroy multiple ears per night.
- **Tomatoes:** Fruit pulled from vines, often with a single bite taken and the rest discarded. Raccoons are frustratingly wasteful with tomatoes.
- **Grapes:** Vine damage, clusters partially stripped, crushed fruit on the ground.
- **Watermelon/squash:** Holes punched in the rind, flesh scooped out with forepaws.
- **Bird feeders:** Feeders pulled down, bent, disassembled, or emptied entirely.
- **Koi ponds/water gardens:** Fish disappearing overnight, muddy prints around the pond edge, water lilies disturbed.

## Part 4: Other Signs

### Den Sites

Raccoons use a variety of den sites and often maintain multiple dens within their home range, rotating among them. Evidence of den occupancy includes:

- **Tree cavities:** Look for worn, smooth bark around cavity entrances, and for claw marks on the trunk below the cavity (ascending/descending marks).
- **Attics and crawl spaces:** Sounds at night (heavy walking, chittering, scratching), staining around entry points, displaced insulation visible from below, and the distinctive ammonia smell of accumulated urine.
- **Storm drains:** Tracks entering and exiting drain openings, scat near drain entrances.
- **Brush piles and ground dens:** Worn paths leading to concealed entrances, hair caught on branches.

### Claw Marks on Trees

Raccoons leave distinctive claw marks on trees they climb regularly. The marks appear as sets of five parallel scratches (four close together from the main toes, one offset from the "thumb" toe) running vertically on the bark. On smooth-barked trees (beech, maple), these marks can persist for months. On den trees, the accumulated claw marks and worn bark around the cavity entrance are obvious.

### Hair and Fur

Raccoon guard hairs are banded --- each hair shows alternating light and dark bands when examined closely. Tufts of raccoon fur caught on fences, building edges, or branches near dens are identifiable by this banding pattern. The underfur is dense, soft, and grayish.

## Part 5: Trail Cameras

If you want to confirm raccoon activity at a specific location --- a suspected den tree, a latrine, a garden raiding site --- a trail camera is the most effective and least disruptive method.

### Affordable Setup

Basic trail cameras suitable for raccoon monitoring cost $40-80 and are available from outdoor retailers and online. Key features to look for:

- **Infrared (no-glow or low-glow) flash:** Invisible or nearly invisible to animals, does not disturb their behavior. Avoid cameras with visible white flash.
- **Motion trigger speed:** Under 1 second. Raccoons move quickly and a slow trigger will capture only the tail end of the animal.
- **Night image quality:** Look for reviews specifically commenting on nighttime image clarity. Raccoons are almost entirely nocturnal, so daytime image quality is irrelevant for this purpose.
- **Battery life:** Most run on AA batteries and last 2-6 months depending on trigger frequency.
- **Weatherproofing:** Essential in the PNW. Look for IP65 or higher weather rating.

### Placement Tips

- Mount the camera 30-50 cm (12-20 inches) above ground level for track-level shots, or at 1-1.5 meters (3-5 feet) for body-level identification shots.
- Aim the camera along a suspected travel route, not directly at it --- this gives the motion sensor more time to detect the approaching animal and reduces the number of empty triggering events.
- Avoid pointing the camera due south or west where direct sunlight can cause false triggers.
- Check and refresh weekly. Review the images and adjust aim if the raccoons are passing outside the frame.
- Secure the camera with a cable lock or strapping tape to prevent theft (this is a real concern in urban areas).

### What the Camera Reveals

A week of trail camera footage at a productive location will teach you more about raccoon behavior than months of casual observation. You will likely discover:

- Raccoons visit specific locations at remarkably consistent times each night
- Multiple individuals may use the same travel route at different times
- Family groups (mother + kits) move in coordinated formation
- The same individual may visit a site multiple times per night
- Other species share the raccoon's travel routes: opossums, cats, skunks, deer, and occasionally coyotes

## Part 6: Putting It Together --- A Field Exercise

Choose a location near your home where you suspect raccoon activity (near water, near garbage storage, near a garden, or near a known den site). Conduct a systematic sign survey using this checklist:

### Survey Checklist

| Sign Type | Found? | Location | Notes |
|-----------|--------|----------|-------|
| Tracks (front paw) | | | Size, substrate |
| Tracks (hind paw) | | | Size, stride measurement |
| Track pattern (walking pairs) | | | Direction of travel |
| Scat/latrine | | | Location type, contents visible |
| Rolled-back sod | | | Area affected, grub presence |
| Garbage damage | | | Container type, entry method |
| Muddy handprints | | | Surface type, height |
| Garden damage | | | Crop affected, damage pattern |
| Claw marks on trees | | | Tree species, height, cavity nearby? |
| Hair/fur on fence/branch | | | Banded? Color? |
| Den site evidence | | | Type, signs of active use |

### Interpreting Your Survey

If you found **3 or more sign types** at a single location, raccoons are actively using that area and likely visiting nightly. Consider deploying a trail camera for confirmation and to determine the number of individuals.

If you found **tracks only**, raccoons are passing through but may not be resident. Follow the track direction to find the destination (food source, den, water).

If you found **a latrine**, raccoons have been using this area for at least several weeks. The latrine is a reliable indicator of nearby denning or regular travel routes. Observe from a safe distance only.

If you found **damage patterns but no tracks**, check again after rain when substrates are soft. Alternatively, deploy a trail camera.

## Part 7: Contributing Your Findings

### iNaturalist

Log confirmed raccoon signs on iNaturalist (inaturalist.org). Photos of clear tracks, distinctive damage, or camera trap images all qualify as observations. Tag them as *Procyon lotor* and add notes describing the sign type. Research-grade observations contribute to distribution databases used by wildlife managers and researchers.

### Your Own Records

A field notebook with dated, located observations accumulates into a valuable long-term record. Over months and years, patterns emerge: seasonal shifts in activity areas, the establishment and abandonment of latrines, the appearance of new family groups in spring. This is the kind of longitudinal data that is difficult for researchers to collect and genuinely useful for understanding urban wildlife dynamics.
