# Bandit of the Bioregion --- Raccoons in Pacific Northwest Ecosystems

## Introduction

The Pacific Northwest is defined by water. Rain falling on ancient forests, rivers cutting through volcanic ranges to the sea, fog banking against coastal headlands, snow accumulating on stratovolcanoes and feeding glacial meltwater into salmon streams. It is a landscape of staggering biological productivity, from the marine upwelling zones off the continental shelf to the old-growth forests that once carpeted the lowlands in a continuous canopy from northern California to southeast Alaska.

In this landscape, *Procyon lotor pacificus* --- the Pacific raccoon --- occupies an ecological position of quiet significance. Neither apex predator nor keystone species in the classic sense, the PNW raccoon is nevertheless woven into the region's ecological fabric in ways that connect marine and terrestrial systems, influence the reproductive success of threatened species, and contribute to the nutrient cycles that sustain forest productivity. Understanding the raccoon's role in PNW ecosystems requires understanding the ecosystems themselves --- the riparian corridors, the salmon runs, the intertidal zones, and the old-growth forests that together constitute the bioregion's ecological identity.

---

## The Pacific Raccoon: *Procyon lotor pacificus*

### Subspecific Characters

The Pacific raccoon is distinguished from other *P. lotor* subspecies by a combination of morphological traits that reflect adaptation to the wet, temperate climate of the coastal Pacific Northwest:

- **Pelage:** Darker than eastern and central subspecies, with a deep grizzled brown to nearly black dorsal coloration. The underfur is exceptionally dense, providing insulation and water resistance in a climate where rainfall exceeds 100 cm annually across much of the range and can exceed 400 cm in the Olympic rain forests.
- **Body size:** Slightly larger on average than the nominate subspecies (*P. l. lotor*), consistent with Bergmann's rule (animals in cooler climates tend toward larger body size) and the high-quality food resources available in PNW ecosystems, particularly salmon.
- **Skull morphology:** Broader skull with more robust zygomatic arches, reflecting powerful jaw musculature used for crushing crab carapaces, mussel shells, and other hard-shelled prey.

### Range

*P. l. pacificus* ranges from southwestern British Columbia through western Washington and Oregon to northwestern California, primarily west of the Cascade Range crest. East of the Cascades, populations transition into other subspecies (*P. l. excelsus* in the interior Northwest, *P. l. hirtus* in the northern Great Plains). The Cascade Range serves as a partial biogeographic barrier, though genetic exchange occurs through mountain passes and river valleys.

Within this range, raccoons are found from sea level to approximately 2,000 meters elevation, though they are most abundant below 500 meters in the lowland and foothill zones. The upper elevation limit corresponds roughly to the transition from mixed conifer/deciduous forest to subalpine fir and mountain hemlock zones, where den site availability, food resources, and winter severity all decline.

## Habitat Use

### Riparian Corridors: The Primary Habitat

Raccoons are fundamentally riparian animals. Across their entire range, population density is highest along streams, rivers, lake shores, and wetland margins. In the PNW, riparian corridors are the backbone of raccoon distribution, providing:

- **Aquatic foraging habitat:** Streams, rivers, and lake edges support crayfish, fish, amphibians, and aquatic invertebrates that form a major component of the raccoon diet.
- **Terrestrial foraging habitat:** Riparian zones support diverse and productive plant communities, including berry-producing shrubs (salmonberry, thimbleberry, elderberry), nut-producing trees (Oregon white oak in the southern range, hazelnut), and abundant invertebrate fauna.
- **Den sites:** Large riparian trees --- particularly black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*), and western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) --- develop the large-diameter trunks and natural cavities that raccoons prefer for denning. Old-growth riparian trees may contain multiple usable cavities per tree.
- **Travel corridors:** Riparian corridors provide connected pathways through otherwise fragmented landscapes, facilitating dispersal and gene flow between populations.

In the PNW, the health of riparian corridors is the single best predictor of raccoon population health. Where riparian buffers are intact, raccoon populations are stable and ecologically integrated. Where riparian vegetation has been cleared for agriculture or development, raccoon populations either decline or shift to anthropogenic habitats (farms, suburbs), with corresponding changes in diet, behavior, and ecological impact.

### Old-Growth and Mature Forest

PNW old-growth forests provide high-quality raccoon habitat, though at lower densities than riparian zones. Key features of old-growth habitat include:

- **Large-diameter trees with cavities:** Old-growth western red cedar, Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), and Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) develop cavities through heartwood rot, woodpecker excavation, and branch loss that provide secure den sites.
- **Complex understory:** The multi-layered structure of old-growth forests supports diverse invertebrate communities and berry-producing shrubs.
- **Coarse woody debris:** Fallen logs and root wads provide shelter, foraging substrate (invertebrates under bark), and den sites.
- **Low disturbance:** The relative stability of old-growth forests provides consistent habitat conditions year after year.

The loss of old-growth forest across the PNW has reduced the availability of large-cavity den trees, potentially pushing raccoon populations toward second-growth forests (which lack suitable cavity trees for decades after harvest) and human structures. This shift from natural to anthropogenic denning may be one of the mechanisms driving raccoon urbanization in the region.

### Wetlands

Freshwater and estuarine wetlands support some of the highest raccoon densities in natural PNW habitats:

- **Puget Sound estuaries:** Where rivers meet saltwater, estuarine marshes provide crabs, fish, shellfish, and waterfowl nesting areas that support productive raccoon foraging.
- **Freshwater marshes:** Cattail and bulrush marshes provide amphibian prey, nesting birds, and invertebrate food sources.
- **Beaver ponds:** Beaver-created wetlands (*Castor canadensis*) are particularly valuable raccoon foraging habitat, concentrating fish, amphibians, and invertebrates in accessible shallow-water environments. The mutualistic relationship between beaver engineering and raccoon foraging illustrates the ecological connectivity of PNW wildlife communities.

### Agricultural Edges

Agricultural landscapes adjacent to forest or riparian habitat support high raccoon densities due to the combination of abundant food (crops, livestock feed, poultry) and nearby natural cover. In the Willamette Valley, Skagit Valley, and other PNW agricultural regions, raccoons exploit the edge between farmed and natural landscapes with characteristic efficiency.

## The Salmon Connection

### Raccoons as Salmon Consumers

The annual return of Pacific salmon to PNW rivers is one of the great ecological events of the Northern Hemisphere. Five species of Pacific salmon (Chinook, coho, chum, pink, and sockeye) complete their anadromous life cycles by returning to natal streams to spawn and die. The biomass of spawning and post-spawning salmon in PNW waterways is enormous: a single productive stream may receive thousands of individual fish, each weighing 2-15 kg, over a period of weeks to months.

Raccoons are consistent consumers of salmon carcasses along PNW streams. While they are less prominent in the popular narrative of salmon consumption than bears (which receive the majority of media and scientific attention), raccoons play a measurable role in the salmon carcass ecosystem:

- **Selective feeding:** Like bears, raccoons feed selectively on the most energy-dense tissues of salmon carcasses --- brain, eyes, gonads (roe and milt), and abdominal fat deposits. This selective feeding maximizes caloric return per unit of handling time and leaves substantial carcass biomass for other scavengers (eagles, gulls, crows, invertebrates, soil microorganisms).
- **Carcass dispersal:** Raccoons drag salmon carcasses from stream banks into the riparian zone for consumption. This lateral transport of carcass material moves marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon, and micronutrients) from the aquatic zone into the terrestrial forest.
- **Temporal coverage:** Raccoons are nocturnal, while bears are primarily diurnal carcass consumers. This temporal partitioning means that salmon carcasses are being processed and dispersed across both day and night cycles, increasing the efficiency and extent of nutrient transfer.

### Marine-Derived Nutrients

The ecological significance of salmon carcass dispersal extends far beyond the animals doing the eating. Stable isotope studies (using nitrogen-15, a marker of marine origin) have traced marine-derived nutrients from salmon through soil, riparian vegetation, invertebrate communities, and even upslope forest trees at distances of 100+ meters from streams.

The pathway is elegant: salmon accumulate marine nutrients during their ocean growth phase. They carry those nutrients upstream to spawning grounds. They die. Their carcasses are consumed and dispersed by bears, raccoons, eagles, and other scavengers. The nutrients enter the terrestrial food web through feces, urine, abandoned carcass fragments, and direct absorption by soil and vegetation.

In this system, raccoons are not merely consumers; they are vectors of ecosystem fertilization. A raccoon dragging a salmon carcass 30 meters into the forest and eating the brain is, from an ecosystem perspective, delivering a package of marine nitrogen and phosphorus to the forest floor. The trees that grow taller near salmon streams, the shrubs that fruit more prolifically, the invertebrate communities that support songbird populations --- all of these are connected, through salmon, through the animals that eat salmon, to the ocean.

## Intertidal Foraging

### A Unique PNW Behavior

PNW raccoons inhabiting coastal areas, Puget Sound shorelines, and the shores of the San Juan Islands engage in intertidal foraging behavior that has no parallel in inland raccoon populations. This behavior connects raccoons directly to the marine food web and represents a distinctive ecological role of the Pacific raccoon.

### Tidal Cycle Foraging

Raccoon intertidal foraging is tightly synchronized with tidal cycles. Activity peaks during low tides, particularly nighttime low tides that coincide with the raccoon's normal activity period. Spring tides (the extreme low tides that occur around new and full moons) expose the greatest area of intertidal substrate and produce the most intensive raccoon foraging activity.

### Intertidal Prey

PNW raccoons consume a diverse array of intertidal organisms:

- **Shore crabs** (*Hemigrapsus oregonensis*, *H. nudus*, *Pachygrapsus crassipes*): Small crabs found under intertidal rocks are the most commonly consumed intertidal prey. Raccoons flip rocks and capture crabs with their forepaws, using their broad molars to crush the carapace.
- **Dungeness crab** (*Metacarcinus magister*): Larger crabs encountered in shallow subtidal and low intertidal zones. Processing Dungeness crab requires more effort (stronger carapace) but yields substantially more food per item.
- **Mussels** (*Mytilus trossulus*, *M. californianus*): Raccoons pry mussels from rock surfaces and open them by inserting their digits between the valves and forcing them apart, or by cracking the shell with their teeth.
- **Clams:** Various species of intertidal and shallow subtidal clams are dug from sand and mud substrates.
- **Chitons, limpets, and snails:** Smaller gastropods and polyplacophorans are consumed opportunistically.
- **Intertidal fish:** Sculpins, gunnels, pricklebacks, and other small fish trapped in tide pools or under rocks are captured.
- **Barnacles:** Scraped from rocks and consumed, though these are low-ranked food items due to their small size and high handling cost.

### Ecological Impact on Intertidal Communities

Raccoon foraging in intertidal zones has the potential to influence community structure, particularly in areas with high raccoon density. Selective removal of predatory crabs can indirectly affect the entire intertidal community through trophic cascades. In areas where raccoons are the dominant nocturnal intertidal predator, their foraging may shape the size distribution, density, and behavior of crab populations and, through them, the composition of the intertidal community.

This is an area that has received relatively little research attention compared to the well-studied impacts of other intertidal predators (shorebirds, sea stars, sea otters). The ecological impact of raccoon intertidal foraging in Puget Sound and along the outer coast of Washington and Oregon represents a significant gap in our understanding of PNW intertidal ecology.

## Competitive Interactions

### River Otters

Raccoons and North American river otters (*Lontra canadensis*) share aquatic foraging habitat throughout the PNW. Both species consume fish, crayfish, and other aquatic prey. However, the competitive overlap is limited by:

- **Foraging depth and method:** Otters are fully aquatic foragers, diving and pursuing prey in open water. Raccoons forage primarily from the water's edge, using tactile methods in shallow water. The two species exploit different depth zones of the same aquatic habitat.
- **Diet differences:** Otters are more specialized fish predators; raccoons are more generalized, with a larger plant and terrestrial invertebrate component in their diet.
- **Activity patterns:** Both species are primarily nocturnal, but otters are more flexible in their activity timing, often foraging during the day.

Where both species occur, they appear to coexist with relatively low competitive intensity, partitioning resources along depth, diet, and habitat dimensions.

### Black Bears

In the PNW, raccoons and black bears (*Ursus americanus*) share several important food resources: salmon carcasses, berries (particularly huckleberries, salal, and blackberries), and insect larvae. The competitive relationship is asymmetric --- bears are dominant and can displace raccoons from concentrated food sources. Raccoons mitigate this competition through:

- **Temporal partitioning:** Bears are primarily diurnal; raccoons are nocturnal. At salmon streams, this means bears forage on carcasses during the day and raccoons forage at night, reducing direct competitive encounters.
- **Spatial partitioning:** Raccoons tend to use smaller streams and more fragmented habitat patches, while bears focus on larger streams with the most concentrated salmon runs.
- **Diet breadth:** Raccoons exploit a broader range of food items than bears, reducing their dependence on shared resources.

### Coyotes

The relationship between raccoons and coyotes (*Canis latrans*) is primarily predator-prey rather than competitive, though there is dietary overlap in the omnivorous components of both species' diets. Coyote population dynamics influence raccoon populations through predation pressure, and the recent expansion of coyotes into PNW urban areas has introduced a new top-down control on urban raccoon populations that was previously absent.

In a broader ecological context, the raccoon-coyote-cat interaction is significant: in areas where coyotes are abundant, they suppress both raccoon and feral cat populations, indirectly benefiting ground-nesting birds and other small prey species that both raccoons and cats consume. Where coyotes are absent (as in many urban cores), raccoon and cat populations tend to be higher, with corresponding increases in nest predation pressure.

## Impact on Native Species

### Marbled Murrelet [AVI]

The Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) is a small, cryptic seabird listed as threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. It nests on large, horizontal branches in old-growth and mature coniferous forests, typically within 80 km of the coast. Unlike most seabirds, which nest in colonies, murrelets nest as widely dispersed single pairs, making their nests difficult to find and protect.

Raccoons are documented predators of murrelet nests. Because murrelets lay only a single egg per nesting attempt, each depredated nest represents a complete reproductive failure for that pair for the year. In fragmented old-growth landscapes, where edge effects increase raccoon access to nest trees that were previously deep within contiguous forest, raccoon nest predation may be a significant factor limiting murrelet reproductive success.

The interaction between old-growth forest fragmentation, increased raccoon density in fragmented landscapes, and murrelet nest predation illustrates the cascading effects of habitat modification on sensitive species. The raccoon is not the root cause of murrelet decline (that distinction belongs to old-growth logging), but it is a proximate agent of reproductive failure that is amplified by habitat fragmentation.

### Western Pond Turtle

The Western Pond Turtle (*Actinemys marmorata*) is the only native freshwater turtle in western Washington and Oregon, and it is listed as endangered in Washington State. Raccoon predation on turtle nests is a major conservation concern: in some monitored populations, raccoons destroy 70-90% of nests within days of egg laying. Female pond turtles lay 3-11 eggs per nest and may only nest once per year, making nest predation a severe demographic bottleneck.

Conservation efforts for the western pond turtle in Washington include nest protection (wire mesh cages placed over nests) and headstarting programs (collecting eggs, incubating them in captivity, and raising hatchlings to a size where they are less vulnerable to predation before releasing them). Both strategies represent direct responses to the raccoon predation problem.

### Band-tailed Pigeon

The Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*) is a native PNW bird whose populations have declined significantly from historical levels. Band-tailed pigeons nest in trees and lay only 1 egg per clutch, making them highly sensitive to nest predation. Raccoons are documented predators of band-tailed pigeon nests, though the population-level significance of this predation is less well quantified than for murrelets or pond turtles.

### Ground-Nesting Birds [AVI, FWL-08]

Multiple species of ground-nesting birds in the PNW face raccoon nest predation, including:
- Spotted towhees, song sparrows, and other ground-nesting passerines
- Ruffed grouse and Sooty Grouse (formerly "blue grouse")
- Various shorebird species nesting along rivers and lakeshores
- Waterfowl (mallards, wood ducks, and other species with ground or near-ground nests)

In landscapes where raccoon populations are artificially elevated by human food subsidies, the predation pressure on ground-nesting birds can exceed the threshold for population sustainability, creating "ecological traps" where birds continue to nest in areas where reproductive success is chronically insufficient.

## Winter Ecology

### Not Quite Hibernation

PNW raccoons do not hibernate. The region's relatively mild winters allow raccoons to remain active year-round, though activity levels decrease during the coldest and wettest periods (typically December-February). PNW raccoons experience a less severe winter bottleneck than their counterparts in the northern Great Plains or Great Lakes regions, where weeks of sub-zero temperatures and deep snow force extended periods of inactivity.

During cold snaps, PNW raccoons may enter brief periods of torpor --- a state of reduced activity and slightly depressed body temperature that conserves energy without the deep metabolic depression of true hibernation. Communal denning during cold periods (multiple individuals sharing a den for thermoregulatory benefit) occurs in the PNW, though less frequently than in colder regions.

### Fat Reserves

Despite the milder PNW climate, fat reserves remain critical for winter survival. Raccoons that fail to accumulate sufficient fat during the autumn hyperphagia period are at risk of starvation during extended cold or wet periods when foraging is difficult. The autumn salmon runs, berry crops, and nut masts are the primary drivers of fat accumulation in PNW raccoons, and years of poor salmon returns or mast failure can increase winter mortality.

## Climate Change Implications

### Range Shifts

Climate change is expected to affect PNW raccoon populations through several mechanisms:

- **Northward expansion:** Warming temperatures are facilitating the northward expansion of raccoon range into areas of British Columbia and southeast Alaska that were previously unsuitable. This expansion brings raccoons into contact with species and ecosystems that have no evolutionary history of raccoon predation.
- **Elevation shifts:** Warming may allow raccoons to occupy higher-elevation habitats than they currently use, potentially bringing them into contact with species in subalpine and alpine zones.
- **Range compression of competitors:** If climate change differentially affects raccoon competitors (bears, otters, coyotes), the competitive landscape could shift in ways that alter raccoon population dynamics and ecological impact.

### Phenological Shifts

Warmer springs may advance the raccoon breeding season, allowing earlier kit birth and a longer development period before winter. This could increase first-year survival rates and potentially increase population growth rates. Conversely, mismatches between raccoon breeding timing and the availability of spring food resources (if food phenology shifts at a different rate than raccoon reproductive phenology) could reduce reproductive success.

### Drought and Water Availability

Projected increases in summer drought severity in the PNW could reduce the availability of aquatic foraging habitat, concentrate raccoon populations around remaining water sources, and increase competitive interactions and disease transmission. In a drier PNW, the raccoon's dependence on riparian corridors could become a vulnerability rather than merely a preference.

## Conservation Status

### Global Status

*Procyon lotor* is classified as Least Concern by the IUCN, reflecting its enormous range, high population density, and demonstrated ability to thrive in human-modified landscapes. The species is in no danger of extinction at the global or continental scale.

### Subspecific Vulnerability

The species-level security of *P. lotor* masks the vulnerability of isolated populations. The extinction of the Barbados raccoon (*P. l. gloveralleni*) and the endangered status of the Bahamas raccoon (*P. l. maynardi*) demonstrate that island and other isolated populations of even highly adaptable species can be lost. While *P. l. pacificus* is not in any conservation danger --- it is abundant and widespread across its range --- the principle applies: a species' global security does not protect its constituent populations from local threats.

### Management

In Washington State, raccoons are classified as a furbearer with managed harvest seasons. The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) manages raccoon populations through harvest regulations, damage permits for agricultural conflict, and public education about coexistence. In Oregon, raccoons are similarly classified and managed by the Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife (ODFW).

The primary conservation challenge involving PNW raccoons is not the conservation of raccoons themselves but the management of raccoon impacts on species of conservation concern --- murrelets, pond turtles, and other species whose populations are negatively affected by raccoon predation. This creates an unusual management dynamic in which a species of least concern (the raccoon) must be managed to protect species of high conservation concern (its prey), requiring a nuanced approach that recognizes the raccoon's legitimate ecological role while mitigating its impacts on vulnerable species in degraded landscapes.

## Conclusion

The Pacific raccoon is a citizen of every PNW ecosystem from the intertidal zone to the montane forest. It eats salmon and delivers marine nutrients to forest soil. It raids murrelet nests and digs up turtle eggs. It forages in beaver ponds and shelters in old-growth tree cavities. It is neither hero nor villain in the PNW ecological narrative but a participant --- a generalist threading its way through a web of relationships that connects the ocean to the mountains, the forest floor to the canopy, the wild to the urban.

Understanding the raccoon's place in PNW ecosystems requires holding two truths simultaneously: that raccoons are native, ecologically integrated members of the biotic community, and that human landscape modification has amplified raccoon populations and impacts in ways that threaten other native species. Both truths are valid. Both must inform management. The raccoon does not need our help to thrive. But the species it affects may need our help to persist alongside it.
