# Cross-References and Bibliography

## Connections Within the Research Series

The FAD (First Aid and Health Studies) research project exists at the intersection of nearly every other research project in the PNW Research Series. Health literacy is not a standalone topic — it is a substrate that underlies every human activity, from building furniture to attending conventions to climbing mountains. A person who knows first aid brings that knowledge to every environment they enter. A person who does not know first aid carries that deficit everywhere as well.

This document maps the connections between FAD and the other research projects, provides the bibliographic foundation for the entire series, and catalogues the resources — certification programs, organizations, and reference texts — that support continued learning beyond what this series can provide.

## Cross-References to Other Research Projects

### FSR — First Responders

The connection between FAD and FSR is the most direct and substantial in the entire research series. FAD is the civilian complement to FSR — the same emergencies, seen from the other side.

FSR examines the professional response system: who responds, how they are trained, what equipment they carry, what institutional structures support or fail them, and what happens when the system encounters emergencies it was not designed for (the mental health gap of FSR-08, the responder wellness crisis of FSR-09). FAD examines the civilian response: what happens before the professionals arrive, what a bystander can do, what knowledge every person should carry.

Key connections:
- FAD-01 (First Aid Foundations) establishes the assess-call-care framework that defines bystander action before the FSR-described professional response arrives — the gap between calling 911 and the apparatus pulling up
- FAD-02 (Wound Care) describes the civilian execution of hemorrhage control techniques developed in military medicine and trained through the Stop the Bleed program referenced in FSR
- FAD-04 (Medical Emergencies) describes civilian recognition and initial management of the cardiac arrests, strokes, and medical crises that EMS (FSR-03) is dispatched to treat — bystander CPR before the paramedic arrives
- FAD-06 (Mental Health Basics) is the explicit companion to FSR-08 (Mental Health Crisis Response) — the civilian side of the gap that FSR-08 documents from the responder side, including the 988 Lifeline and crisis intervention basics
- FAD-09 (First Aid Kits, Event/Go Bag) describes civilian trauma kits that parallel the medical equipment described in FSR-07 (Equipment and Apparatus), scaled for bystander capability
- FAD-10 (PNW Health Hazards, Cascadia earthquake injuries) connects to the FSR case studies on Cascadia earthquake response — the mass casualty scenario where professional response capacity is overwhelmed and civilian first aid becomes primary care
- The entire FAD series supports the FSR-10 (PNW Departments) theme that community preparedness reduces the burden on professional response systems

### RCN — Raccoon and Wildlife Studies

The intersection of FAD and RCN runs through animal encounters and zoonotic disease.

Key connections:
- FAD-05 (Environmental Emergencies) covers animal bites, rabies assessment, and tick-borne disease — the health consequences of wildlife encounters documented in the RCN project
- FAD-10 (PNW Health Hazards) addresses tick-borne diseases carried by Ixodes pacificus, the western black-legged tick, which parasitizes the wildlife species studied in RCN
- Baylisascaris procyonis (raccoon roundworm) — documented in the RCN project as a zoonotic risk — can cause severe neurological disease in humans and is a PNW-specific health hazard relevant to FAD-10's regional focus
- Wildlife encounter first aid (bite wound management, rabies post-exposure prophylaxis decision-making) bridges the ecological knowledge of RCN with the medical response knowledge of FAD

### FWL — Fowl and Poultry Studies

The FWL connection is indirect but meaningful: poultry health basics serve as an analog for understanding zoonotic disease, animal-to-human disease transmission, and the health practices required when working closely with animals.

Key connections:
- Avian influenza (documented in FWL) is a zoonotic disease with pandemic potential — the vaccination and preventive health principles in FAD-08 apply to understanding both human and animal disease prevention
- Biosecurity practices for poultry flocks parallel the personal protective equipment and hygiene practices described in FAD-01 — the principles are the same whether protecting yourself from animal pathogens or human ones
- Basic wound care for animal injuries (pecking wounds, scratches) follows the same principles described in FAD-02

### WDC — Woodcraft

The WDC connection is one of the most practical in the series. Workshop injuries are a predictable consequence of working with sharp tools, powerful machinery, and resistant materials, and the first aid for those injuries is directly addressed throughout FAD.

Key connections:
- FAD-02 (Wound Care) addresses the deep lacerations, puncture wounds, and avulsions characteristic of workshop injuries — table saw contact, chisel slips, router accidents, lathe catches
- FAD-03 (Musculoskeletal Injuries) covers the fractures and crush injuries from workshop accidents — kickback, dropped materials, falls from ladders during projects
- FAD-08 (Preventive Health, Hearing Protection) documents noise-induced hearing loss from power tools — table saws, planers, routers, and miter saws all exceed 100 dB, and hearing protection is a workshop-specific preventive health requirement
- FAD-09 (First Aid Kits, Workshop Kit) is designed specifically for the WDC environment — emphasis on wound closure (Steri-Strips, butterfly closures), hemostatic agents, eye wash, and burn treatment
- WDC-08 (Construction Framing) and the building construction knowledge it documents connect to understanding structural injury patterns — how buildings fail and injure occupants, relevant to FAD-10's earthquake coverage

### FFA — Fursuit and Fabric Arts

The FFA connection addresses the specific health challenges of costume construction and performance — a niche that standard first aid resources do not cover.

Key connections:
- FAD-05 (Environmental Emergencies, Heat Illness) is directly relevant to fursuit performance — heat exhaustion and heat stroke are documented hazards of performing in full-body insulated costumes in warm environments, and the recognition and treatment protocols apply without modification
- FAD-07 (Nutrition and Wellness, Hydration) addresses the fluid and electrolyte management critical for performers in thermally challenging costumes
- FAD-08 (Preventive Health, Ergonomics) covers the repetitive strain, neck and back issues, and hand/wrist problems that affect makers who spend extended hours in static positions doing detail work — sewing, sculpting, painting, hot-glue application
- FAD-09 (First Aid Kits, Convention Kit) is designed for the convention environment where FFA community members gather — blister care, hydration, sleep management, hygiene, and costume repair
- The heat management section of FAD-05 provides the physiological basis for the "handler" and buddy system practices common in the fursuit performance community

### PNT — Painting and Fine Art

The PNT connection addresses the occupational health hazards of artistic practice.

Key connections:
- FAD-08 (Preventive Health, Ergonomics) applies to the sustained postures and repetitive movements of painting, drawing, and sculpture — neck strain from looking down at a work surface, shoulder tension from brush work, hand fatigue from fine motor tasks
- Solvent safety is a PNT-specific health concern: turpentine, mineral spirits, and other solvents used in oil painting are volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that cause respiratory irritation, central nervous system effects (headache, dizziness, confusion), and liver/kidney damage with chronic exposure. Adequate ventilation, use of low-VOC alternatives where possible, and nitrile gloves when handling solvents are the preventive measures
- FAD-02 (Wound Care) covers the minor injuries common in art practice — utility knife cuts, palette knife slips, burns from encaustic or glass-blowing

## Key Texts

The following texts are recommended for readers who wish to deepen their knowledge beyond the scope of this research series. They are listed in approximate order of accessibility, from general-audience references to more specialized resources.

### General First Aid

**American Red Cross First Aid/CPR/AED Participant's Manual.** American Red Cross, current edition. The standard reference for the most widely taken first aid course in the United States. Clear, well-illustrated, and designed for non-medical readers. Updated regularly to reflect current American Heart Association and ILCOR guidelines.

**First Aid Manual (DK/St. John Ambulance/St. Andrew's First Aid/British Red Cross).** Dorling Kindersley, current edition. The standard British first aid reference. Comprehensive, heavily illustrated, and in some areas more detailed than the American Red Cross manual. Useful for a comparative perspective on first aid protocols, which vary in some specifics between American and European/Commonwealth guidelines.

### Wilderness Medicine

**Wilderness Medicine: Beyond First Aid** by William W. Forgey, MD. Globe Pequot Press, current edition. A concise, field-oriented reference for backcountry medical care. More accessible than Auerbach (below) and more practical for the lay reader who wants a trail-ready reference.

**Auerbach's Wilderness Medicine** edited by Paul S. Auerbach, MD, et al. Elsevier, current edition. The definitive reference text in wilderness medicine, used in the training of wilderness medicine physicians. Two volumes, over 2,500 pages. Not a casual read, but the authoritative source on environmental emergencies, expedition medicine, and austere-environment healthcare. The academic foundation underlying NOLS and SOLO wilderness medicine curricula.

**NOLS Wilderness Medicine** by Tod Schimelpfenig. Stackpole Books, current edition. The textbook for the NOLS Wilderness First Aid and Wilderness First Responder courses. Practical, scenario-based, and written for outdoor educators and trip leaders.

### Austere and Resource-Limited Medicine

**The Survival Medicine Handbook** by Joseph Alton, MD and Amy Alton, ARNP. Doom and Bloom LLC, current edition. Written for situations where professional medical care is unavailable — grid-down scenarios, remote locations, prolonged disasters. Practical, clearly written, and covers a broader range of medical conditions than standard first aid references. Relevant to the Cascadia earthquake scenario described in FAD-10.

**Where There Is No Doctor** by David Werner. Hesperian Health Guides, current edition (available free online at hesperian.org). Originally written for community health workers in developing countries, this book covers basic healthcare in resource-limited settings. It has been in continuous publication since 1977 and has been translated into more than 80 languages. Its plainspoken, practical approach to health education is a model for health literacy resources including this series.

### Mental Health

**Mental Health First Aid USA Manual.** National Council for Mental Wellbeing, current edition. The textbook for the Mental Health First Aid certification course described in FAD-06.

**Saving Lives: Why the Media's Portrayal of Nursing Puts Us All at Risk** by Sandy Summers and Harry Jacobs Summers. This is not a first aid text, but its discussion of how media representations shape public understanding of healthcare is relevant to the health literacy mission of this entire series.

### Nutrition and Wellness

**How Not to Die** by Michael Greger, MD. Flatiron Books, 2015. Evidence-based review of the dietary and lifestyle factors associated with the fifteen leading causes of death in the United States. Well-sourced (the bibliography alone is extensive) and written for a general audience.

**Why We Sleep** by Matthew Walker, PhD. Scribner, 2017. The definitive popular-science book on sleep research. Covers the physiology, neuroscience, and public health implications of sleep and sleep deprivation. Relevant to FAD-07's sleep section.

**In Defense of Food** by Michael Pollan. Penguin, 2008. The book that distilled nutrition science into seven words: "Eat food. Not too much. Mostly plants." While the FAD series takes a more detailed approach, Pollan's philosophical framework for thinking about food — distinguishing between "food" (things your grandmother would recognize) and "edible food-like substances" (processed products engineered for shelf life and palatability) — aligns with the health literacy goals of FAD-07.

### Regional and PNW-Specific

**Cascadia's Fault** by Jerry Thompson. Counterpoint, 2011. A journalist's investigation of the Cascadia Subduction Zone, the science behind the seismic risk, and the preparedness gaps in Pacific Northwest communities. Provides the narrative and scientific context for the earthquake preparedness discussion in FAD-10.

**The Big Ones** by Dr. Lucy Jones. Doubleday, 2018. A seismologist's examination of how natural disasters have shaped civilizations. Includes discussion of the Cascadia scenario and the systemic failures that turn natural events into human catastrophes. Relevant to FAD-10's treatment of earthquake injuries and infrastructure failure.

**Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest** by Steve Trudell and Joe Ammirati. Timber Press, current edition. The most authoritative field guide for PNW mushroom identification. Essential for anyone who forages in the PNW, and the reference standard for the mushroom poisoning prevention discussion in FAD-10. Does not substitute for expert mentorship but is the best print resource available.

**All That the Rain Promises and More** by David Arora. Ten Speed Press, 1991. A more accessible mushroom identification guide with excellent photographs and a sense of humor. The pocket-sized format makes it a practical field companion. Covers key look-alike dangers relevant to FAD-10's death cap warning.

## Certification Resources

### Emergency First Aid and CPR

- **American Red Cross** — redcross.org — First Aid/CPR/AED certification, available nationwide, blended learning (online + in-person skills session), $80-100, valid 2 years
- **American Heart Association** — heart.org — Heartsaver First Aid CPR AED, $60-80, valid 2 years; BLS (healthcare provider) also available
- **National Safety Council** — nsc.org — First aid and CPR courses, primarily workplace-focused

### Wilderness Medicine

- **NOLS (National Outdoor Leadership School)** — nols.edu — Wilderness First Aid (WFA, 16 hours, $300-400), Wilderness First Responder (WFR, 70-80 hours, $700-900), Wilderness EMT
- **SOLO (Stonehearth Open Learning Opportunities)** — soloschools.com — WFA, WFR, and WEMT courses, similar to NOLS in scope and recognition
- **Remote Medical International** — remotemedical.com — Wilderness medicine courses with a focus on industrial and expeditionary settings

### Hemorrhage Control

- **Stop the Bleed** — stopthebleed.org — Free, 1-hour course teaching hemorrhage control (direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application). Developed by the American College of Surgeons. Available through hospitals, fire departments, community organizations, and military installations nationwide.

### Mental Health

- **Mental Health First Aid** — mentalhealthfirstaid.org — 8-hour certification course (ALGEE action plan). Available for adults and youth populations. Offered through community organizations, employers, and educational institutions.
- **QPR Institute** — qprinstitute.com — Question, Persuade, Refer suicide prevention training. Available online, 1-2 hours.

### Specialized

- **Pediatric first aid** — offered by American Red Cross and other providers, specifically for childcare providers and parents
- **Pet first aid** — offered by American Red Cross, PetTech, and others, for pet owners and animal professionals

## Online Resources and Databases

The following online resources provide current, evidence-based health information that supplements the static references above:

**CDC (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention)** — cdc.gov — The primary U.S. public health agency. Vaccine schedules, screening guidelines, disease information, outbreak tracking, and travel health advisories. The authoritative source for U.S. public health recommendations.

**UpToDate** — uptodate.com — Evidence-based clinical decision support used by physicians worldwide. Some content is freely accessible; full access requires a subscription. The most current synthesis of medical evidence on virtually any topic. When this series cites current medical guidelines, UpToDate is frequently the underlying source.

**MedlinePlus** — medlineplus.gov — The National Library of Medicine's consumer health information site. Written for a lay audience, reviewed by medical professionals, and available in English and Spanish. An excellent starting point for understanding any medical condition, medication, or procedure.

**PubMed** — pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov — The U.S. National Library of Medicine's database of biomedical literature. Contains over 35 million citations from peer-reviewed journals. For readers who want to go to the primary literature on any topic in this series — wound healing biology, hypothermia physiology, vaccine efficacy, suicide intervention effectiveness — PubMed is where the evidence lives.

**Hesperian Health Guides** — hesperian.org — Free, downloadable health education resources designed for resource-limited settings. "Where There Is No Doctor" and related titles are available in full at no cost. These resources represent the gold standard for accessible health literacy and are a philosophical influence on this entire series.

## The Continuum of Care

One of the most important concepts that emerges from mapping the connections across the FAD series and its related research projects is the continuum of care — the idea that health is not a binary state (healthy/sick) but a spectrum, and that every point on that spectrum has appropriate interventions.

At one end: prevention. Vaccinations, screenings, nutrition, exercise, sleep, hearing protection, ergonomics (FAD-07, FAD-08). These are investments in a future where the emergency never happens.

In the middle: recognition and initial response. Recognizing the signs of a heart attack, a stroke, a mental health crisis, an environmental emergency. Performing the initial assessment. Calling for help. Providing bystander care (FAD-01 through FAD-06, FAD-10).

At the other end: professional response and definitive care. The EMS system, the emergency department, the operating room, the intensive care unit (documented in FSR). The professional infrastructure that takes over when bystander care reaches its limits.

FAD exists in the first two-thirds of this continuum. It does not replace the professional third. But the first two-thirds — the prevention and the initial response — determine whether the person reaches the professional third alive and in a condition where professional care can succeed.

The bystander who performs CPR for eight minutes maintains blood flow to the brain until the paramedic can defibrillate. Without those eight minutes, the defibrillator arrives too late. The parent who recognizes anaphylaxis and administers epinephrine maintains an airway until the ambulance arrives. Without that recognition and that injection, the ambulance finds a patient in cardiac arrest rather than a patient who is breathing. The hiker who layers correctly and carries emergency shelter never becomes a hypothermia patient at all — the rescue that was never needed because prevention worked.

This continuum — from prevention through bystander response through professional care — is the architecture of community health. FAD is about strengthening the links that ordinary people control: the prevention link and the initial response link. When those links are strong, the entire chain holds.

## College Connection — Universal

The FAD research project connects to every department in the College structure because health literacy is a universal competency:

**Science** — Anatomy, physiology, pharmacology, immunology, toxicology, environmental health, and the biological foundations of every topic in the series.

**Psychology** — Crisis intervention, mental health recognition, stress response, behavioral aspects of health-seeking and prevention.

**Mind-Body** — The stress response, emotional regulation, breathwork, the body-mind connection in both emergency response and daily wellness.

**Physical Education** — Exercise science, injury prevention, outdoor preparedness, and the physical conditioning that reduces injury risk.

**Nature Studies** — Wildlife encounters, environmental hazards, plant and fungal identification, ecological context for PNW-specific hazards.

**Home Economics** — Household safety, kit building, food safety, preventive maintenance of the home environment, emergency preparedness.

**Learning** — Procedural memory, protocol-based decision-making, certification as structured learning, the pedagogy of hands-on skills training.

**Geography** — Regional hazard mapping, Cascadia Subduction Zone, volcanic hazard zones, wildfire risk geography, marine hazard zones.

**Mathematics** — Dosage calculation (medications, body weight ratios), probability and risk assessment, epidemiological reasoning, the statistics of screening test performance (sensitivity, specificity, predictive value).

Health literacy does not belong to a single discipline. It belongs to all of them. The person who understands the science of wound healing, the psychology of crisis intervention, the geography of regional hazards, and the practical skills of first aid is not a medical professional. They are a competent, prepared, literate human being.

That is the standard this series aspires to.

## Closing Note

This research series — eleven documents, covering the spectrum from basic wound care to regional earthquake preparedness — does not replace professional medical education. It does not replace certification courses. It does not replace the judgment of a physician, a paramedic, or a nurse.

What it does is close a gap. The gap between knowing nothing and knowing enough. The gap between being a bystander who freezes and a bystander who acts. The gap between a person who waits for someone else to help and a person who is the help.

The professional responders are coming. They are brave, skilled, and committed. But they are minutes away, and the emergencies described in this series operate on a timeline of seconds.

In those seconds, the person who matters is the person who is there. The person who read the documents. The person who took the course. The person who built the kit. The person who asked the hard question. The person who showed up and did something.

Be that person.
