# Fuel and Rest — Nutrition and Wellness Basics

## This Is Not a Diet Book

Let that be clear from the start. This document is not going to tell you what to eat for breakfast. It is not going to calculate your macros. It is not going to rank superfoods, demonize carbohydrates, or sell you a thirty-day cleanse that will "transform your relationship with food."

This is a health literacy resource. It exists because the gap between what nutrition science actually says and what the average person understands about nutrition is enormous, filled with marketing, fad diets, influencer nonsense, and outright misinformation. The result is a population that simultaneously spends billions of dollars on supplements, diet programs, and wellness products while suffering epidemic rates of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and nutritional deficiency.

The science of nutrition is complex. But the practical application — the stuff that actually matters for a person trying to be reasonably healthy without devoting their life to it — is surprisingly simple. Simple enough to fit on a single plate, in fact. That is where we are headed. But first, the foundations.

## Macronutrients — The Big Three

Your body needs energy to function. That energy comes from three macronutrients: protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Each serves a different primary purpose, and all three are necessary.

### Protein

Protein is the building material. Every cell in your body contains protein. Your muscles, your organs, your immune system, your enzymes, your hormones — all built from amino acid chains assembled from dietary protein. When you exercise and create microscopic damage to muscle fibers, protein provides the raw material for repair and growth. When you are injured, protein drives wound healing. When you are sick, protein fuels the immune response.

**How much do you need?** The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight per day for a sedentary adult. For a 150-pound (68 kg) person, that is about 54 grams. However, growing evidence suggests this is the minimum to prevent deficiency, not the amount for optimal health. Active individuals, older adults (who need more protein to maintain muscle mass), and people recovering from injury or illness may benefit from 1.2 to 2.0 grams per kilogram.

**Where to get it:** Meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas), soy products (tofu, tempeh, edamame), nuts, seeds, and whole grains all contribute protein. Animal sources provide all essential amino acids in a single food. Plant sources can provide all essential amino acids when consumed in reasonable variety throughout the day — the old "complementary proteins at every meal" rule has been debunked; your body maintains an amino acid pool that is replenished across the day.

### Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the primary energy source for your body, and the preferred fuel for your brain. Your brain consumes approximately 20 percent of your daily energy, almost exclusively from glucose derived from carbohydrates. When people on extremely low-carb diets report brain fog, difficulty concentrating, and irritability, this is why — the brain is running on a suboptimal fuel mix.

Not all carbohydrates are equivalent. The distinction that matters:

**Whole grains** (brown rice, oats, quinoa, whole wheat, barley) contain the fiber, vitamins, and minerals of the intact grain. They are digested slowly, providing sustained energy without the blood sugar spike-and-crash cycle.

**Refined grains** (white bread, white rice, most pasta, pastries) have had the fiber and much of the nutritional content stripped away during processing. They are digested rapidly, produce a sharp blood sugar spike followed by a crash, and provide calories without much else.

**Fiber** is a carbohydrate that your body cannot digest — and that is precisely why it is valuable. Soluble fiber (oats, beans, apples) forms a gel in the digestive tract that slows nutrient absorption and feeds beneficial gut bacteria. Insoluble fiber (whole wheat, vegetables, nuts) adds bulk that promotes regular bowel movements. Most Americans consume about 15 grams of fiber per day. The recommended amount is 25-30 grams. The gut microbiome — the community of trillions of bacteria in your intestines that influences everything from immune function to mental health — thrives on fiber and starves without it.

**Fruits and vegetables** are carbohydrates. They are also the most nutrient-dense foods available — packed with vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, and fiber for relatively few calories. The recommendation to eat more fruits and vegetables is the single most universal, least controversial piece of nutrition advice in existence. Every dietary guideline, from every country, from every health organization, says the same thing: eat more plants.

### Fat

Fat has been unfairly demonized for decades. The low-fat craze of the 1980s and 1990s — driven by a flawed interpretation of the link between dietary fat and heart disease — led to an explosion of low-fat products that replaced fat with sugar. The result was worse health outcomes, not better.

Fat is essential. Your brain is approximately 60 percent fat by dry weight. Every cell membrane in your body is made of fat. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require dietary fat for absorption. Hormones, including testosterone and estrogen, are synthesized from cholesterol, which is a fat. Fat provides insulation, protects organs, and is the most energy-dense macronutrient (9 calories per gram, versus 4 for protein and carbohydrates).

The type of fat matters more than the amount:

**Unsaturated fats** (olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, fatty fish) are associated with reduced cardiovascular risk, reduced inflammation, and improved lipid profiles. Omega-3 fatty acids — found in salmon, mackerel, sardines, flaxseed, chia seeds, and walnuts — have anti-inflammatory properties and are essential for brain function. Most Americans do not consume enough omega-3s.

**Saturated fats** (butter, cheese, red meat, coconut oil) are more controversial. The simplistic "saturated fat causes heart disease" narrative has been revised by more recent evidence, which suggests the relationship is more nuanced and depends on the overall dietary context and the specific saturated fatty acids involved. Current guidance suggests limiting but not eliminating saturated fat, replacing some with unsaturated fat, and focusing on overall dietary pattern rather than individual nutrients.

**Trans fats** (partially hydrogenated oils, found in some margarine, fried foods, and processed baked goods) are genuinely harmful. They raise LDL cholesterol, lower HDL cholesterol, promote inflammation, and increase cardiovascular risk. The FDA has largely banned artificial trans fats from the food supply, but they persist in some products. Check ingredient lists for "partially hydrogenated" anything and avoid it.

## Micronutrients — The Ones Most People Lack

A balanced diet should provide all necessary vitamins and minerals. In practice, several micronutrient deficiencies are common enough to mention:

**Vitamin D.** The "sunshine vitamin" is synthesized in the skin through UV-B exposure. In the Pacific Northwest, where the sun angle is too low for effective vitamin D synthesis from roughly October through March, deficiency is endemic. An estimated 40-50% of adults in the PNW have insufficient vitamin D levels. Symptoms of deficiency include fatigue, bone pain, muscle weakness, and depression. Food sources (fatty fish, fortified milk, egg yolks) rarely provide enough. Supplementation (1,000-2,000 IU daily for most adults, potentially more based on blood levels) is reasonable for PNW residents, especially in winter. A blood test (25-hydroxyvitamin D) can establish your baseline.

**Iron.** Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide. It is particularly prevalent in menstruating women, pregnant women, vegetarians, and endurance athletes. Symptoms: fatigue, weakness, pale skin, shortness of breath, cold hands and feet, brittle nails, restless legs. Red meat, poultry, fish, beans, lentils, spinach, and fortified cereals are sources. Plant-based iron (non-heme) is less bioavailable than animal-based iron (heme), but absorption is enhanced by consuming vitamin C alongside iron-rich foods (lentils with tomato sauce, spinach salad with lemon dressing).

**B12.** Vitamin B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products. Vegetarians and especially vegans are at risk of deficiency unless they supplement or consume B12-fortified foods (nutritional yeast, fortified plant milks, fortified cereals). Deficiency causes fatigue, weakness, neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, balance problems), and a specific type of anemia (megaloblastic).

**Magnesium.** Involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including muscle and nerve function, blood sugar regulation, and blood pressure control. Many Americans consume below the recommended amount. Sources: nuts (almonds, cashews), seeds (pumpkin), legumes, whole grains, dark chocolate, leafy greens. Symptoms of deficiency: muscle cramps, fatigue, irritability, poor sleep.

## Hydration — Simpler Than You Think

The "8 glasses of 8 ounces per day" rule (8x8) is one of the most widely repeated nutrition claims, and it has essentially no scientific basis. It appears to trace to a 1945 National Research Council recommendation that was taken out of context — the original statement noted that most of this fluid would come from food.

The reality: **drink when you are thirsty.** Your body has an exquisitely sensitive thirst mechanism that evolved over millions of years to regulate fluid balance. In most circumstances, for most people, thirst is a reliable guide.

Increase your intake when active (especially in heat), when ill (fever increases fluid loss), when at altitude (dry air and increased respiration increase loss), and when consuming alcohol or caffeine (both are mild diuretics, though the fluid in coffee and tea still contributes to net hydration).

**The urine test.** Your urine should be pale yellow — the color of straw or light lemonade. If it is clear, you may be overhydrating (yes, this is possible, and in extreme cases — water intoxication/hyponatremia — it can be dangerous, particularly during endurance events). If it is dark yellow or amber, you need to drink more. Some vitamins (particularly B vitamins) turn urine bright yellow regardless of hydration status — if you take a B-complex supplement and your urine is neon yellow, that is the riboflavin, not dehydration.

Water is the ideal hydration source. Sports drinks (electrolyte beverages) are beneficial during prolonged vigorous exercise (over an hour) or in heat, when electrolyte losses through sweat become significant. For most daily hydration needs, water is sufficient and calorie-free.

## The Plate Model — Nutrition Made Visual

Calorie counting works for some people. For most, it is tedious, anxiety-producing, and unsustainable. The plate model is an alternative that provides portion guidance without requiring a food scale or a tracking app.

At each meal, look at your plate and aim for:

- **One half vegetables and fruits.** The more colorful and varied, the better. Different colors indicate different phytonutrient profiles.
- **One quarter protein.** A palm-sized portion of meat, fish, poultry, tofu, beans, or eggs.
- **One quarter whole grains or starchy vegetables.** Brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat bread, sweet potato, corn.

Add a small amount of healthy fat (olive oil on the salad, avocado on the side, nuts sprinkled on top) and a glass of water.

This is the USDA MyPlate model, and while no single visualization captures the full complexity of nutrition science, it is a functional heuristic that, if followed consistently, would dramatically improve the diet of most Americans.

## Sleep — The Undervalued Foundation

Sleep is not optional. It is not a luxury. It is a biological necessity that, when neglected, degrades every system in the body. The research is unambiguous: insufficient sleep increases the risk of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety, impaired immune function, reduced cognitive performance, accidents, and early death.

**How much?** Seven to nine hours for adults. Teenagers need eight to ten. Children need more. The people who genuinely need less than seven hours are so rare as to be statistically insignificant — if you think you are one of them, you are almost certainly wrong. You are adapted to sleep deprivation, not immune to its effects.

### Circadian Rhythm

Your body runs on an internal clock — the circadian rhythm — that is synchronized to the light-dark cycle by a cluster of neurons in the hypothalamus called the suprachiasmatic nucleus. This clock regulates not just sleep-wake cycles but hormone release, body temperature, digestion, immune function, and gene expression.

**Blue light** from screens (phones, tablets, computers, TVs) activates melanopsin receptors in the eye that signal "daytime" to the suprachiasmatic nucleus, suppressing melatonin production and delaying sleep onset. The practical advice: reduce screen exposure in the hour or two before bed, use night mode / blue light filters, or wear blue-blocking glasses. The evidence for these interventions is moderate — the benefit exists but is not enormous. The more impactful intervention is consistent sleep timing.

### Sleep Hygiene

The single most important sleep habit is **consistency.** Go to bed at the same time and wake up at the same time, every day, including weekends. The circadian rhythm is a rhythm — it depends on regularity. Sleeping in on weekends produces "social jet lag" that disrupts the rhythm as effectively as flying across time zones.

Other evidence-based sleep hygiene practices:

- Keep the bedroom cool (65-68 degrees F is optimal for most people)
- Keep it dark (blackout curtains, eye mask)
- Keep it quiet (earplugs, white noise machine)
- Reserve the bed for sleep and intimacy only (not working, not watching TV, not scrolling your phone)
- Avoid caffeine after noon (caffeine's half-life is 5-6 hours, meaning half of your afternoon coffee is still circulating at bedtime)
- Avoid alcohol before bed (alcohol is a sedative but disrupts sleep architecture, particularly REM sleep — you may fall asleep faster but sleep worse)
- If you cannot fall asleep within twenty minutes, get up, do something quiet in dim light, and return to bed when you feel sleepy (lying in bed frustrated trains your brain to associate the bed with frustration)

### The PNW Darkness Advantage

The Pacific Northwest's long winter nights — fourteen to sixteen hours of darkness from November through February — are, from a sleep perspective, a gift. The human circadian system evolved under natural light-dark cycles, and long dark periods promote melatonin production, deeper sleep, and a slower biological pace that industrialized society has largely eliminated. The cultural impulse to resist the darkness (bright lights, late schedules, fighting the night with screens) works against the biology.

Permission to sleep. When the dark comes at 4:30 PM and the rain is drumming on the roof, the biologically appropriate response is to wind down, dim the lights, and go to bed early. This is not laziness. It is alignment with a circadian reality that the PNW has in abundance.

## Exercise — The Single Best Medicine

If exercise were a pill, it would be the most prescribed medication in history. Regular physical activity reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, multiple cancers, depression, anxiety, dementia, osteoporosis, falls, and all-cause mortality. It improves sleep, cognitive function, mood, energy levels, metabolic health, and immune function. There is no pharmaceutical intervention that provides this breadth of benefit with this few side effects.

**The minimum effective dose:** 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming, hiking, dancing) or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity activity (running, fast cycling, competitive sports), plus strength training two or more days per week.

**The best exercise is the one you will actually do.** This is not a platitude. It is the most important sentence in exercise science. A theoretically optimal training program that you abandon after two weeks is inferior to a daily walk that you maintain for thirty years. Consistency trumps intensity. Adherence trumps optimization.

Walking counts. Walking is a legitimate form of exercise. Thirty minutes of brisk walking per day meets the moderate-intensity guideline. Walking requires no equipment, no gym membership, no special clothing, and no recovery time. In the PNW, walking means trails, waterfront paths, neighborhoods with hills, and forest parks. It is available to almost everyone and it is free.

Strength training does not require a gym. Bodyweight exercises (push-ups, squats, lunges, planks), resistance bands, or a modest set of dumbbells at home provide sufficient stimulus. The goal is to load your muscles against resistance at least twice per week to maintain muscle mass, bone density, and metabolic rate — all of which decline with age if not actively maintained.

## Stress Management — Breaking the Cortisol Cycle

Cortisol is the body's primary stress hormone, released by the adrenal glands in response to perceived threats. In acute situations (a near-miss in traffic, a tight deadline, a startling noise), cortisol mobilizes energy, sharpens focus, and prepares the body for action. This is adaptive and useful.

The problem is chronic stress. When the cortisol response is activated continuously — by work pressure, financial anxiety, relationship conflict, chronic pain, or the ambient stress of modern life — the system that was designed for sprints is forced to run a marathon. Chronic elevated cortisol suppresses immune function, impairs memory and learning (cortisol damages the hippocampus), promotes abdominal fat storage, raises blood pressure, disrupts sleep, and contributes to anxiety and depression.

Breaking the cortisol cycle:

**Breathing techniques.** Box breathing (inhale for four counts, hold for four, exhale for four, hold for four) activates the parasympathetic nervous system — the "rest and digest" branch that counterbalances the sympathetic "fight or flight" system. Four to five minutes of box breathing can measurably reduce cortisol levels. It works immediately, it requires no equipment, and it can be done anywhere.

**Physical activity.** Exercise is the intended endpoint of the stress response. Cortisol mobilizes energy for physical action. Completing that action — walking, running, cycling, lifting — signals to the body that the threat has been addressed and the stress response can stand down. This is why a twenty-minute walk after a stressful meeting feels so restorative — it is completing the stress cycle that the body initiated.

**Nature exposure.** The Japanese practice of shinrin-yoku (forest bathing) has been validated by research: spending time in forests reduces cortisol, lowers blood pressure, reduces heart rate, and improves mood. The PNW is, by this measure, a natural pharmacy. Forests reduce cortisol measurably, and the PNW has more accessible forest per capita than almost any metropolitan region in the country.

**Social connection.** Positive social interaction triggers oxytocin release, which directly counteracts cortisol. Isolation — even self-imposed — raises cortisol and impairs well-being. Conversation, laughter, physical touch, shared meals, and community participation are not luxuries. They are biological needs.

**The walk-and-talk.** Combining physical activity, nature exposure, and social connection into a single intervention: walking with a friend in a natural setting. This stacks three evidence-based stress-reduction strategies into one activity and is remarkably effective. The PNW, with its parks, trails, and waterfront paths, makes this accessible year-round with appropriate rain gear.

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Science** — Nutrition and physiology. The biochemistry of macronutrient metabolism (glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, beta-oxidation of fatty acids, amino acid transamination), the endocrinology of appetite regulation (ghrelin, leptin, insulin), and the physiology of sleep (NREM stages, REM sleep, glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste during sleep). The science is deep and fascinating, and it directly informs every practical recommendation in this document.

**Physical Education** — Exercise prescription and programming. The principles of progressive overload, specificity, recovery, and periodization. The physiology of cardiovascular adaptation (increased stroke volume, reduced resting heart rate, improved capillary density) and muscular adaptation (hypertrophy, neural recruitment, connective tissue strengthening) to regular exercise.

**Mind-Body** — Stress, sleep, and the body-mind connection. The bidirectional relationship between physical health and mental health: exercise improves mood; sleep deprivation impairs mood; chronic stress causes physical disease; physical disease causes psychological distress. The practices in this document — breathing techniques, nature exposure, consistent sleep, regular movement — are mind-body practices grounded in physiology.

## The Unglamorous Truth

There is no secret. There is no hack. There is no superfood, supplement, or program that will shortcut the basic requirements of a human body: adequate protein, enough vegetables, whole grains, healthy fats, sufficient water, seven-plus hours of sleep, regular movement, human connection, time in nature, and space to breathe.

It is unglamorous. It does not sell books. It does not trend on social media. It does not require an expensive subscription or a branded product.

It works.
