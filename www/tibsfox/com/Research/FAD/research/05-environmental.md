# Nature Bites Back — Environmental Emergencies

## The Outdoors Does Not Care About You

The Pacific Northwest is one of the most beautiful landscapes on the planet. Old-growth forests where the canopy is so thick the light goes green. Volcanic peaks wrapped in glaciers that feed rivers so clear you can count the rocks on the bottom. A coastline of sea stacks and tide pools and beaches that stretch until they disappear into fog. Desert canyons east of the Cascades. Alpine meadows that bloom in July with a density of wildflowers that takes your breath away.

The Pacific Northwest will also kill you.

Not out of malice. Out of indifference. Nature does not care whether you are prepared. It does not care that you are wearing cotton. It does not care that your phone has no signal. It does not care that you thought the hike would only take three hours. The environment presents conditions, and those conditions are survivable or they are not, and the difference is almost always preparation and knowledge.

This document covers the environmental emergencies most relevant to the PNW and the outdoors more broadly — hypothermia, heat illness, drowning, lightning, animal encounters, and the wildfire smoke that has become a seasonal reality. Some of these are fast (lightning, drowning) and some are slow (hypothermia, altitude sickness). All of them are manageable if you know what to look for and what to do.

## Hypothermia — The PNW's Number One Outdoor Killer

Hypothermia occurs when the body loses heat faster than it can produce it, and core body temperature drops below 95 degrees Fahrenheit (35 degrees Celsius). It is the Pacific Northwest's most underrated lethal hazard, and it kills more hikers in this region than any other environmental factor.

The reason is the PNW weather pattern: it does not need to be cold. Rain plus wind at fifty degrees Fahrenheit will produce hypothermia in a person wearing wet cotton faster than still air at twenty degrees will produce it in a person wearing appropriate layers. The combination of moisture and wind strips heat from the body through evaporation and convection at rates that are difficult to appreciate until you are experiencing them.

This is why the phrase "cotton kills" is an axiom in outdoor education. Cotton absorbs water like a sponge, loses all insulating value when wet, and clings to the skin, maximizing evaporative heat loss. A cotton t-shirt under a rain jacket is a hypothermia vector on any PNW trail from October through May — and sometimes in June and September as well.

### The Stages of Hypothermia

**Mild hypothermia (95-90 degrees F / 35-32 degrees C).** The person is shivering — possibly violently. Shivering is the body's involuntary mechanism for generating heat through muscle contraction. They are alert but may have impaired coordination, fumbling with zippers and buckles. Their speech may be slurred. They are cold and they know they are cold.

**Moderate hypothermia (90-82 degrees F / 32-28 degrees C).** Shivering slows or stops. This is a critical and counterintuitive sign: the cessation of shivering in a cold person is not improvement. It means the body has exhausted its ability to generate heat and is losing the fight. The person is confused, drowsy, and may make poor decisions (removing clothing, wandering off trail). Muscular rigidity replaces the fluidity of shivering. Speech is difficult to understand. They may not recognize that they are hypothermic.

**Severe hypothermia (below 82 degrees F / 28 degrees C).** The person is unconscious or nearly so. Breathing is shallow and slow. Pulse is faint and may be difficult to detect. The skin is cold and may appear blue or gray. The person appears dead. They may, in fact, be alive — the saying in wilderness medicine is "no one is dead until they are warm and dead." Hypothermic patients have been successfully resuscitated after prolonged cardiac arrest because the cold slows metabolic demand and extends the window for brain survival.

### Treatment

**Mild hypothermia:** Remove wet clothing. Replace with dry insulating layers. Move to shelter (tent, car, building) out of wind and rain. Give warm, sweet drinks — hot chocolate, sweetened tea, warm Gatorade. The sugar provides fuel for metabolic heat production and the warm liquid provides a small direct heat transfer. Do NOT give alcohol — it dilates blood vessels near the skin surface, increasing heat loss. Have them eat high-calorie food if they can. Activity and movement are appropriate if the person is alert and coordinated enough to walk safely.

**Moderate hypothermia:** Everything above, plus: handle the person gently. A moderately hypothermic heart is irritable and can fibrillate (go into a lethal rhythm) if jostled or moved roughly. Do not let the person walk — carry or transport them if possible. Apply external heat sources to the core — warm water bottles, chemical heat packs, or another person's body heat applied to the chest, armpits, and groin (areas of high blood flow near the surface). Insulate from the ground (a sleeping pad, a layer of branches, a foam pad — the ground conducts heat away from the body rapidly). Do NOT rub the extremities — cold blood from the arms and legs returning suddenly to the core can trigger cardiac arrest.

**Severe hypothermia:** Call 911 or activate a wilderness rescue. Handle the person extremely gently. Insulate and prevent further heat loss. If the person is not breathing and has no detectable pulse (check for a full sixty seconds — a severely hypothermic heart may be beating at a rate too slow to easily detect), begin CPR. In a wilderness setting, this creates a terrible calculus: CPR in a hypothermic patient must continue until the person is rewarmed and still has no cardiac activity, or until you are physically unable to continue. Rewarming requires hospital-level resources (warm IV fluids, warm lavage, in extreme cases cardiopulmonary bypass).

### Prevention

- Layer system: moisture-wicking base layer (merino wool or synthetic), insulating mid-layer (fleece, puffy jacket), waterproof and windproof outer layer (Gore-Tex or equivalent). No cotton anywhere in the system.
- Carry extra insulation. A lightweight puffy jacket and a rain shell weigh under a pound and can save your life.
- Eat and drink. Your body needs fuel to generate heat. Snack frequently on high-calorie foods during cold, wet outings.
- Know the weather forecast before you go. The PNW can shift from clear to rain and wind in hours.
- Turn back. If conditions deteriorate and you are getting cold and wet, retreat is the wise choice. The mountain will be there next weekend.

## Heat Exhaustion vs. Heat Stroke

Heat illness exists on a spectrum, and the distinction between heat exhaustion and heat stroke is the distinction between "sit down and drink water" and "call 911 or this person may die."

### Heat Exhaustion

The body is overheating but still compensating. The person is sweating heavily (this is important — sweating means the thermoregulatory system is still functioning), and their skin is cool and clammy. They feel nauseous, dizzy, weak, and have a headache. They may have muscle cramps.

Treatment: Move to shade or a cool environment. Lie down with legs elevated. Remove excess clothing. Cool the skin with wet cloths or fanning. Give cool water or electrolyte drinks in small sips. Most people with heat exhaustion recover within an hour with rest and rehydration.

### Heat Stroke

The body's thermoregulatory system has failed. Core temperature exceeds 104 degrees F (40 degrees C). The hallmark sign: **the person is NOT sweating.** The skin is hot, dry, and flushed. This is a true medical emergency with a high mortality rate if untreated.

Additional signs: confusion, altered consciousness, seizures, rapid breathing, rapid heart rate, headache, nausea, loss of consciousness.

Treatment: **Call 911 immediately.** Cool the person aggressively — immerse in cold water if available (the fastest method), apply ice packs to the neck, armpits, and groin, spray with water and fan, anything that pulls heat out of the body. Do not wait for the ambulance to start cooling. Every minute at elevated core temperature causes organ damage. Remove excess clothing. Do not give fluids if the person is confused or unconscious (aspiration risk). Monitor breathing and be ready for CPR.

The transition from heat exhaustion to heat stroke can be rapid. If someone with heat exhaustion stops sweating, becomes confused, or loses consciousness, the situation has escalated and 911 is the immediate action.

## Drowning — Reach, Throw, Row, Go

Drowning is the process of respiratory impairment from submersion or immersion in liquid. It is the third leading cause of unintentional injury death worldwide. In the Pacific Northwest, drowning occurs in rivers (especially during spring snowmelt, when water volume and speed increase dramatically), lakes, the ocean, and backyard pools.

The first rule of water rescue is brutal in its clarity: **do not become a second victim.** A drowning person is in a state of panic and will, without malice or intent, climb on top of a rescuer, pushing them under. Untrained rescuers attempting direct contact rescue in the water account for a significant number of drowning deaths each year. Lifeguards train extensively in approach techniques that prevent the victim from grabbing them. You have not had this training.

The rescue priority order:

**Reach.** If the person is close to shore or poolside, extend something for them to grab — a branch, a paddle, a towel, a pole, your arm while you are firmly braced on solid ground. Do not let them pull you in.

**Throw.** If they are too far to reach, throw something that floats — a life ring, a cooler, a sealed water jug, a rope with a flotation device attached. Aim past them and pull back so the line drags across them.

**Row.** If you have a boat, kayak, canoe, or paddleboard, use it to reach the person. Approach carefully and have them hold the stern or a thrown rope rather than climbing into the boat (which can capsize it).

**Go.** Enter the water only as a last resort, only if you are a strong swimmer, and only with a flotation device that you can push toward the victim while maintaining distance. This is the highest-risk option.

After rescue, if the person is not breathing, begin CPR immediately. Drowning victims benefit significantly from rescue breathing in addition to compressions (unlike cardiac arrest, where the primary problem is the heart, drowning's primary problem is the lungs). If you are trained in full CPR (compressions plus breaths), use it. If not, hands-only compressions are still better than nothing.

## Lightning Safety — The 30/30 Rule

Lightning kills approximately 20 people per year in the United States and injures hundreds more. The PNW thunderstorm season is shorter than in the Midwest and Southeast, but mountain thunderstorms in the Cascades and eastern Oregon and Washington can develop rapidly and be intense.

**The 30/30 rule:** If the time between seeing a flash and hearing the thunder is 30 seconds or less, the storm is within six miles and you should seek shelter immediately. After the last thunder, wait 30 minutes before resuming outdoor activity.

Safe shelter means a substantial building or a hard-topped vehicle with the windows closed. Open shelters (picnic pavilions, bus stops), tents, and trees are NOT safe.

If caught in the open: get to low ground, crouch low (feet together, hands on knees, head tucked), and stay away from tall isolated objects (trees, poles, ridgelines). Spread out if you are in a group — lightning striking a group standing close together can injure or kill multiple people through ground current.

If someone is struck by lightning, they are safe to touch (they do not carry a charge). Call 911. Begin CPR if they are not breathing — lightning strike victims have a high survival rate with immediate resuscitation because the cardiac arrest is often a transient event that responds to defibrillation and CPR.

## Altitude Sickness

Altitude sickness is less of a PNW issue than a mountain west issue, but anyone climbing the Cascades volcanoes (Rainier at 14,411 feet, Adams at 12,281, Baker at 10,781, Hood at 11,249) should be aware of it.

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) typically begins above 8,000 feet and is caused by reduced oxygen partial pressure at altitude. Symptoms: headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, and disturbed sleep. It feels like a hangover.

Treatment: stop ascending. If symptoms do not resolve with rest, descend. The only reliable cure for altitude sickness is descent. Acetazolamide (Diamox) can prevent and treat mild AMS but requires a prescription and advance planning.

Severe forms — High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE) and High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) — are life-threatening and require immediate descent. HAPE presents with breathlessness at rest, cough, and pink or bloody sputum. HACE presents with severe headache, confusion, ataxia (inability to walk in a straight line), and altered consciousness. Both can kill within hours without descent.

## Animal Bites

The PNW has relatively few dangerous animal encounters compared to regions with large populations of venomous snakes, alligators, or large predators that frequently interact with humans. But bites happen.

**Domestic animal bites (dogs, cats):** Wash the wound thoroughly with soap and running water for at least five minutes. Cat bites are particularly dangerous because the narrow, deep puncture wounds from feline teeth are difficult to clean and have a high infection rate. Any cat bite that breaks the skin should be evaluated by a doctor. Dog bites with significant tissue damage should be evaluated for possible closure, tetanus, and antibiotics.

**Rabies assessment:** Any bite from a wild mammal (bat, raccoon, skunk, fox, coyote) or an unvaccinated domestic animal should trigger rabies evaluation. Rabies is nearly 100 percent fatal once symptoms develop, but post-exposure prophylaxis (a series of four shots over fourteen days) is 100 percent effective if started before symptoms appear. If you are bitten by a bat — or even if you wake up in a room with a bat and cannot confirm you were not bitten — seek medical evaluation for rabies prophylaxis. Bat bites can be so small as to be invisible.

**Tetanus:** Any animal bite, and any wound contaminated with dirt, soil, or feces, is a potential tetanus exposure. If your last tetanus booster was more than five years ago, get a booster. If you have never been vaccinated against tetanus, or your vaccination status is unknown, you need both the tetanus immunoglobulin and the vaccine.

## Insect Stings and Tick Removal

**Bee and wasp stings:** If a stinger is visible, remove it by scraping with a flat edge (credit card, fingernail) rather than squeezing with tweezers (which can compress the venom sac and inject more venom). Apply ice for pain and swelling. Watch for signs of anaphylaxis (see FAD-04) — anyone with a known severe allergy should carry an epinephrine auto-injector at all times.

Most bee stings produce only local pain, redness, and swelling that resolves within hours to days. Multiple stings (more than ten in an adult) can produce a toxic reaction even without a true allergy, due to the sheer volume of venom. Seek medical attention for multiple stings.

**Tick removal:** Ticks in the Pacific Northwest — particularly the western black-legged tick (Ixodes pacificus) — can transmit Lyme disease, anaplasmosis, and other infections. The risk of transmission increases with the duration of attachment, so prompt removal is important.

Correct removal technique:

1. Use fine-tipped tweezers (not blunt household tweezers, which can crush the tick's body).
2. Grasp the tick as close to the skin surface as possible — you want to grip the tick's head/mouthparts, not its body.
3. Pull straight out with steady, even pressure. Do not twist, jerk, or rock the tick. Slow, steady traction.
4. If the mouthparts break off and remain in the skin, try to remove them with tweezers. If you cannot remove them easily, leave them alone — the body will expel them.
5. Clean the bite area with rubbing alcohol or soap and water.
6. **Save the tick.** Place it in a sealed bag or container with a damp paper towel. Label it with the date and body location of the bite. If symptoms develop in the following weeks, having the tick identified can inform treatment decisions.

Do NOT use a hot match, petroleum jelly, nail polish, or essential oils to try to make the tick "back out." These folk remedies do not work and may cause the tick to regurgitate its gut contents — including any pathogens — into the wound.

Watch for symptoms over the following thirty days: expanding red rash (the classic "bull's-eye" rash of Lyme disease is seen in 70-80% of Lyme cases, but not all), fever, headache, fatigue, joint pain. If any of these develop, see a doctor and mention the tick bite.

## Snake Bites in the Pacific Northwest

The PNW has only one venomous snake species: the **western rattlesnake** (Crotalus oreganus), found primarily east of the Cascades in dry, rocky habitats. Western Washington, western Oregon, and the coastal areas are essentially free of venomous snakes.

If bitten by a rattlesnake:

1. Move away from the snake to prevent a second bite.
2. Call 911.
3. Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing from the bitten extremity — swelling will be significant.
4. Immobilize the affected limb and keep it at or below heart level.
5. Do NOT apply ice, a tourniquet, or suction. Do NOT cut the wound. Do NOT try to capture or kill the snake (take a photo from a safe distance if possible for identification).
6. Transport to a hospital with antivenom capability.

Most rattlesnake bites in the PNW are "dry bites" (no venom injected) or involve a sublethal dose of venom. Deaths from rattlesnake bites in the United States are rare — approximately five per year out of roughly 7,000 to 8,000 bites. But the tissue damage from envenomation can be severe, and antivenom administered within a few hours dramatically reduces complications.

## Wildfire Smoke — The New PNW Season

The Pacific Northwest now has a fifth season: smoke. From roughly August through October, wildfire smoke from fires burning in Oregon, Washington, California, British Columbia, and occasionally as far away as Siberia can blanket the region in a haze that ranges from hazy-but-tolerable to apocalyptic-orange-sky dangerous.

**AQI (Air Quality Index) categories:**
- **0-50 (Green, Good):** No precautions needed.
- **51-100 (Yellow, Moderate):** Unusually sensitive individuals may experience symptoms.
- **101-150 (Orange, Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups):** Children, elderly, and people with respiratory conditions should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
- **151-200 (Red, Unhealthy):** Everyone should reduce prolonged outdoor exertion. Sensitive groups should avoid outdoor activity.
- **201-300 (Purple, Very Unhealthy):** Everyone should avoid prolonged outdoor exertion. Stay indoors with windows closed.
- **301-500 (Maroon, Hazardous):** Everyone should avoid all outdoor activity. This is a health emergency.

**Monitoring:** PurpleAir sensors provide hyperlocal, real-time AQI data. The EPA's AirNow.gov provides official readings. During smoke events, conditions can vary dramatically within a few miles depending on terrain and wind patterns.

**N95 masks:** NIOSH-approved N95 respirators filter the fine particulate matter (PM2.5) that makes wildfire smoke dangerous. Surgical masks and cloth masks do NOT provide meaningful protection against PM2.5. The N95 must fit snugly to the face with no gaps around the edges — if you can feel air leaking around the mask, it is not protecting you.

**Clean rooms:** Seal one room in your home as a clean air space. Close all windows and doors, seal gaps with wet towels or tape, and run a HEPA air purifier. A portable HEPA filter rated for the room's square footage can reduce indoor PM2.5 by 80% or more.

**Who is most vulnerable:** Children (smaller airways, higher breathing rates relative to body size, more time outdoors), elderly persons, people with asthma, COPD, or other chronic respiratory conditions, pregnant persons, and outdoor workers who cannot avoid exposure.

**Science** — Thermoregulation: how the body maintains core temperature through vasodilation, vasoconstriction, sweating, and shivering. The physics of heat transfer (conduction, convection, radiation, evaporation) and how each pathway is exploited by environmental conditions. The toxicology of wildfire smoke (PM2.5 particles penetrate deep into the lungs, triggering inflammatory responses and exacerbating cardiovascular disease).

Understanding animal behavior (why a bear charges, why a rattlesnake strikes, why a tick waits at a specific height on a grass blade) informs prevention. The ecology of wildfire — fire as a natural and necessary process versus fire as an escalating hazard in a changing climate — provides context for the smoke seasons that are reshaping PNW life.

The clothing system, the equipment list, the fitness baseline, the trip planning checklist, the turnaround time — all are elements of outdoor preparedness that physical education can teach before the trip, so that first aid is never needed during it. Prevention is the best medicine, and in the backcountry, it is often the only medicine available in time.

## Respect the Indifference

The outdoors is not hostile. It is indifferent. That distinction matters because hostility implies intent, and the temptation to assign intent to nature leads to two equally dangerous errors: overconfidence ("nature wouldn't do that to me") and helplessness ("there's nothing I can do").

The reality is that environmental hazards are predictable, preparable, and in most cases survivable — if you know what you are dealing with. Hypothermia is a physics problem. Heat stroke is a physiology problem. Drowning is a fluid dynamics problem. Lightning is an electrical problem. Each has a solution set that is well understood and well documented.

The person who checks the weather, packs appropriate layers, carries the ten essentials, monitors the AQI, knows tick removal, and turns back when conditions exceed their preparation is not timid. They are competent. And competence, in the Pacific Northwest backcountry, is the difference between a great story and a search and rescue operation.
