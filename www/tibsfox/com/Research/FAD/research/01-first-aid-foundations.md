# The First 60 Seconds — First Aid Foundations

## The Moment Before You Move

Someone collapses at the grocery store. A cyclist goes down at an intersection. Your coworker clutches their chest and sits down hard in their office chair. A child falls off playground equipment and does not get up.

In these moments, something happens inside you that is as old as the species. Your heart rate spikes. Your vision narrows. Your hands shake. Every instinct says do something — but the first thing you need to do is the hardest thing you will ever do in an emergency: stop.

Stop and think. Because what you do in the next sixty seconds matters more than anything that happens in the next sixty minutes, and doing the wrong thing first can make everything that follows worse.

This is not about being a hero. This is not about performing CPR like you saw on television. This is about being the person who shows up, makes good decisions under pressure, and keeps everyone — including yourself — alive until the professionals arrive. That person does not need a medical degree. They need a framework. A checklist. A set of priorities that work every single time, in every single situation, whether the emergency is a paper cut or a cardiac arrest.

This document is that framework.

## Scene Safety — Look Up, Look Down, Look Around

The single most important rule in first aid is one that feels wrong: take care of yourself first.

Not because you are more important than the person who is hurt. Because if you become a second victim, you have doubled the problem and halved the help. Every paramedic, every firefighter, every emergency physician learns this on day one: scene safety comes before patient care. Always. Without exception.

What does scene safety look like in practice? It is a three-part scan that takes five seconds and should become automatic:

**Look up.** Is there anything overhead that could fall on you? Power lines? Unstable structures? A tree branch? At a vehicle accident, are there elevated hazards — utility poles compromised by the collision, a vehicle on an embankment that could shift? In a building, are there signs of structural compromise?

**Look down.** What is on the ground? Broken glass? Spilled fluids that could be hazardous? Blood? In a workshop, sawdust covering a tool blade? On a trail, unstable footing that could send you off an edge? At a roadside emergency, are you standing in a traffic lane?

**Look around.** What is the broader environment? Is there traffic? Are there aggressive animals? Is there a fire, chemical spill, or gas leak? Are there other people who might be threatening? What is the mechanism of injury — and could whatever caused this injury cause it again, to you?

This is not paranoia. This is pattern recognition. The scenario that traps the most would-be rescuers is the one where the hazard that injured the first person is still active. Carbon monoxide does not care that you are trying to be helpful — it will poison you exactly as efficiently as it poisoned the person you are trying to reach. Downed power lines do not discriminate between victims and rescuers. A roadway where someone has been hit by a car is a roadway where you can be hit by a car.

If the scene is not safe, do not enter. Call 911 and describe the hazard. Professional responders have equipment, training, and procedures for operating in hazardous environments. You do not. Dying alongside the person you were trying to help is not courage. It is a tragedy multiplied by two.

If the scene is safe — or if you can make it safe, by turning off a car engine, moving away from a roadway, opening windows in a stuffy room — then you proceed.

## Personal Protective Equipment — The Basics

In a professional medical setting, PPE is an entire topic unto itself: gowns, gloves, masks, face shields, eye protection, respirators, each selected for the specific type of exposure anticipated. In a first aid context, the bar is simpler but still important.

**Gloves.** If you are going to touch someone who is bleeding, put on gloves first. Nitrile gloves are the standard — they are latex-free (avoiding allergic reactions), durable enough for most first aid tasks, and cheap enough to keep in every kit. If you do not have gloves, use a plastic bag, a piece of clean cloth, or even the injured person's own clothing as a barrier between your hands and their blood.

Why does this matter? Bloodborne pathogens. Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, and HIV can be transmitted through contact between infected blood and broken skin — a hangnail, a paper cut, cracked knuckles in winter. The risk from a single exposure is statistically low, but it is not zero, and the consequences of transmission are life-altering. Wearing gloves eliminates the risk almost entirely.

**Face shield or pocket mask.** If you are trained in rescue breathing (full CPR, not hands-only), a pocket mask or face shield provides a barrier during mouth-to-mouth ventilation. These are small, flat, and inexpensive. Most keychain first aid kits include one.

That said — and this is important — if you are not comfortable giving rescue breaths, or you do not have a barrier device, hands-only CPR (chest compressions without rescue breaths) is effective for adults in sudden cardiac arrest. The American Heart Association has been recommending hands-only CPR for bystanders since 2008. Compressions alone are dramatically better than doing nothing. Do not let the absence of a face shield stop you from performing CPR.

## Consent — Yes, You Need to Ask

Here is a scenario that surprises people: you see someone sitting on a park bench, clearly in pain, holding their arm at an odd angle. You approach, ready to help. You reach for their arm.

They pull away. "Don't touch me."

What do you do? You stop. That is what you do. A conscious adult has the right to refuse medical care, including first aid from a bystander. This is not a technicality — it is a fundamental principle of medical ethics and, in most jurisdictions, the law.

The rules of consent in first aid are straightforward:

**Conscious adults must give consent.** Before you touch someone, tell them who you are, what you think is wrong, and what you want to do. "My name is Maria, I'm trained in first aid. It looks like you've hurt your arm. Can I help you?" If they say yes, proceed. If they say no, respect it. You can still call 911 on their behalf, and you can stay nearby in case they change their mind or their condition deteriorates.

**Unconscious persons have implied consent.** If someone is unresponsive, the law assumes they would want help if they could ask for it. This is implied consent, and it applies in every U.S. state and most other jurisdictions. You do not need to wait for someone to wake up and give you permission to perform CPR on them.

**Minors follow parental consent — with a critical exception.** If a child is injured and a parent or guardian is present, get their permission before providing care. But if a minor is in a life-threatening situation and no parent is available, implied consent applies. You do not stand by while a child chokes because their parent is not there to sign a permission slip. The life-threatening exception overrides the parental consent requirement.

**People with altered mental status.** Someone who is confused, disoriented, intoxicated, or otherwise unable to make informed decisions is generally treated under implied consent. This is a judgment call — if someone is mildly intoxicated and says "I'm fine, leave me alone," that is different from someone who is incoherent and bleeding from the head. When in doubt, err on the side of providing care and calling 911.

The consent conversation takes ten seconds. It protects the person you are helping, it protects you legally, and it establishes a human connection that makes everything that follows easier. The person who says "I'm trained, here's what I see, can I help?" is far more reassuring than the person who just grabs them.

## The Assessment — Check, Call, Care

You have confirmed the scene is safe. You have gloves on. You have consent, or the person is unconscious and you are operating under implied consent. Now what?

The assessment follows a priority order that has been refined over decades of emergency medicine. The order exists because some problems will kill you in minutes, others in hours, and others not at all — and you need to address them in that order.

### Check Responsiveness

Approach the person and tap their shoulder firmly — not gently, firmly. Shout: "Are you okay? Can you hear me?" Use their name if you know it. If they respond — open their eyes, moan, move, speak — they are conscious and breathing, and you can begin gathering information about what happened and what hurts.

If they do not respond, they are unresponsive. This is the fork in the road where everything accelerates.

### Call 911

If the person is unresponsive, call 911 immediately. If there is someone else present, point at them — physically point, make eye contact — and say: "You, in the blue shirt. Call 911. Tell them someone is unresponsive at [location]. Come back and tell me when you've done it."

The pointing matters. In a crowd, a general shout of "someone call 911" often results in everyone assuming someone else is doing it. This is the bystander effect, and it is well documented in psychology research. Assigning the task to a specific person defeats it.

If you are alone, call 911 yourself. Put the phone on speaker and place it on the ground next to you. The dispatcher will guide you through what comes next, and they are trained to do so while you have your hands full.

When you call 911, be ready to provide:
- Your location (address, intersection, landmark — be specific)
- What happened (if you know)
- How many people are injured
- The person's condition (conscious? breathing? bleeding?)
- Your phone number (in case you get disconnected)

The dispatcher may give you instructions. Follow them. They have protocols for exactly this situation, and they are relaying medical direction from physicians who designed those protocols to be executed by untrained bystanders. The 911 dispatcher is your lifeline to professional medical guidance until the ambulance arrives.

### Check Breathing

With the person on their back, look at their chest. Watch for rise and fall. Listen for breath sounds. Feel for air on your cheek if you are close. Do this for no more than five to ten seconds.

If they are breathing normally, they are alive and their heart is beating. Place them in the recovery position (covered below) and monitor until help arrives.

If they are not breathing, or if they are only gasping (agonal breathing — irregular, infrequent, labored gasps that sound like snoring or gulping, occurring in the first minutes of cardiac arrest), they need CPR. Agonal breathing is not breathing. It is the body's last reflexive effort. If you are unsure whether someone is breathing normally, treat them as if they are not.

### Check for Severe Bleeding

While assessing breathing, do a rapid visual scan for severe bleeding. You are looking for blood pooling on the ground, blood soaking through clothing, or blood spurting (arterial bleeding — bright red, pulsatile). If you find severe bleeding, it needs to be controlled immediately — this may take priority over CPR in some situations, because a person who bleeds out in three minutes will not benefit from chest compressions.

Severe bleeding control is covered in detail in FAD Document 02 ("Bleeding, Burns, and Bandages"). The short version: apply direct pressure with whatever you have. Push hard. Do not let go.

## Good Samaritan Laws — Why You Are Protected

Every single U.S. state and the District of Columbia has a Good Samaritan law. The details vary by state, but the core principle is consistent: if you provide emergency care in good faith, without expectation of compensation, and your actions are reasonable for someone with your level of training, you are protected from civil liability.

This means that if you perform CPR and crack someone's ribs — which happens frequently, because effective chest compressions require significant force — you cannot be sued for the broken ribs. If you apply a tourniquet and the person later has complications, you are protected. If you move someone because the car they are in is on fire and they later turn out to have a spinal injury, you are protected — because the immediate threat of fire justified the risk of movement.

What Good Samaritan laws do not protect is gross negligence, reckless behavior, or care that exceeds your training. If you attempt to perform surgery in a parking lot, you are not protected. If you are intoxicated while providing care, your protection may be diminished. If you refuse to stop providing care when the person regains consciousness and asks you to stop, you may be outside the law's protection.

The practical takeaway: if you act in good faith, within the scope of what a reasonable person with your training would do, and you do not charge for your help, the law is on your side. Fear of liability should never stop you from helping someone who is dying. The legal risk of helping is vanishingly small. The moral and human cost of not helping, when you could have, is immeasurable.

Some states have gone further. Several now have "duty to call" laws that require bystanders to at least call 911 when they witness an emergency, even if they do not provide physical care. Vermont has the broadest duty-to-rescue law in the country, requiring reasonable assistance when someone is in grave physical harm, provided the rescuer can help without danger to themselves.

## The Action Plan — Assess, Call, Care

Everything above reduces to three words: assess, call, care.

**Assess.** Is the scene safe? What happened? Is the person responsive? Are they breathing? Are they bleeding?

**Call.** 911. Always. Even if you think the situation is minor, even if the person says they are fine. Let the professionals make that determination. The cost of an unnecessary 911 call is zero. The cost of a necessary call that was not made can be a life.

**Care.** Provide the care you are trained to provide, within the scope of your abilities, until professional help arrives. This might be CPR. It might be direct pressure on a wound. It might be nothing more than staying with someone, keeping them calm, and telling them help is coming. That last one — just being there — is more valuable than most people realize. A person who is injured or ill and alone is terrified. A person who is injured or ill with someone calm and reassuring beside them has a measurably better outcome. Stress hormones affect healing. Panic affects breathing. Reassurance is a medical intervention, even if it does not feel like one.

## When NOT to Move Someone

The default rule is: do not move an injured person unless there is an immediate threat to their life in their current location.

The reason is spinal injury. If someone has fallen, been in a vehicle accident, been struck by something, or has any mechanism of injury that could have damaged their spine, moving them incorrectly can convert a survivable spinal fracture into a permanent spinal cord injury. The difference between "broken vertebra" and "paralyzed for life" can be a single careless lift.

When do you move someone despite this risk?

- The building is on fire or filling with smoke
- They are in water and at risk of drowning
- They are in a traffic lane and vehicles cannot be diverted
- There is an imminent structural collapse
- There is an active hazardous materials exposure

If you must move someone with a potential spinal injury, support the head and neck, keep the spine in alignment as much as possible, and drag rather than lift — pulling from behind the shoulders with the head cradled between the forearms is better than trying to carry someone. This is imperfect. The entire situation is imperfect. You are making a judgment call between two risks — the risk of spinal damage from movement versus the risk of death from the environmental hazard — and choosing the lesser harm.

If the person is in no immediate danger, leave them where they are. Keep them still. Tell them not to move. If they want to get up, calmly explain that it is safer to stay still until the paramedics arrive and can check them properly. Most people will listen if you explain why.

## The Recovery Position

The recovery position is for someone who is unconscious but breathing. Its purpose is simple: keep the airway open and prevent choking on vomit or secretions.

An unconscious person lying flat on their back is at risk of airway obstruction. The tongue can fall back and block the throat. If they vomit — which is common in many types of medical emergencies — the vomit has nowhere to go and they aspirate it into their lungs. Aspiration pneumonia is a serious and sometimes fatal complication of an otherwise survivable event.

The recovery position prevents both problems by placing the person on their side with their airway angled downward so fluids drain out rather than in.

To place someone in the recovery position:

1. Kneel beside the person. Place the arm nearest to you at a right angle to their body, elbow bent, palm up.
2. Take their far arm and bring it across their chest, holding the back of their hand against their near cheek.
3. With your other hand, grab their far knee and pull it up so their foot is flat on the ground.
4. Pull the bent knee toward you, rolling them onto their side. Their head should end up resting on their hand.
5. Adjust the top leg so the hip and knee are at right angles, stabilizing the position.
6. Tilt their head back slightly to keep the airway open. Make sure their mouth is angled downward.

Monitor their breathing continuously. If they stop breathing, roll them onto their back and begin CPR.

One exception: if you suspect a spinal injury, do not use the recovery position unless you absolutely must to clear their airway (they are vomiting and you cannot suction it). In that case, log-roll them with as many helpers as possible, keeping the head, neck, and spine aligned.

## Certification Paths — Getting Trained

Reading about first aid is a start. Training is better. The difference between someone who has read about CPR and someone who has practiced it on a mannequin, with an instructor watching and correcting their hand placement and compression depth, is the difference between theoretical knowledge and functional skill. Under stress, you will default to what you have practiced, not what you have read.

Three main certification paths are available in the United States:

### American Red Cross First Aid/CPR/AED

**Cost:** $80-100
**Duration:** One day (approximately 6-8 hours), or blended learning (online + in-person skills session)
**What you learn:** First aid for injuries and illness, adult/child/infant CPR, AED use, choking relief
**Certification valid:** Two years
**Best for:** General population, workplace requirements, parents, coaches, teachers

The Red Cross certification is the most widely recognized in the United States and the one most often required by employers. The course is practical, hands-on, and designed for people with no medical background. You will practice CPR on mannequins, use a training AED, and work through scenarios. The online component covers knowledge; the in-person component covers skills.

### American Heart Association Heartsaver

**Cost:** $60-80
**Duration:** Approximately 4 hours (classroom or blended)
**What you learn:** Adult/child/infant CPR, AED use, choking relief, first aid basics
**Certification valid:** Two years
**Best for:** Similar population as Red Cross; some healthcare-adjacent workplaces prefer AHA certification

The AHA Heartsaver course is slightly shorter than the Red Cross equivalent and focuses more tightly on CPR and cardiac emergencies. The AHA writes the guidelines that define CPR technique (updated every five years based on the latest research), so their training reflects the most current science directly. The BLS (Basic Life Support) course — the AHA's healthcare provider version — is required for medical professionals, but Heartsaver is designed for the general public.

### NOLS Wilderness First Aid (WFA)

**Cost:** $300-400
**Duration:** Two days (16 hours), typically a weekend course
**What you learn:** Patient assessment in wilderness settings, improvised splinting and wound care, environmental emergencies (hypothermia, heat illness, altitude sickness), evacuation decision-making, medical emergencies when help is hours or days away
**Certification valid:** Two years
**Best for:** Hikers, backpackers, climbers, kayakers, outdoor trip leaders, anyone who spends time more than an hour from a hospital

The NOLS (National Outdoor Leadership School) Wilderness First Aid course is a different animal from the Red Cross and AHA courses. It trains you to manage injuries and illness in environments where 911 is not an option — or where the ambulance is a four-hour helicopter ride away. The course teaches improvisation (splinting with trekking poles and duct tape), extended patient monitoring, and the critical decision of when to evacuate versus when to treat in the field.

For Pacific Northwest residents who hike, ski, paddle, or climb, WFA is arguably the most valuable certification on this list. The Cascades, the Olympics, and the coast are beautiful but remote, and cell service is unreliable in many areas where people recreate. A twisted ankle at Colchuck Lake is a very different problem than a twisted ankle in a parking lot.

### Beyond These Three

**Stop the Bleed** is a free, one-hour course developed by the American College of Surgeons that teaches hemorrhage control — direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquet application. It was designed in response to mass casualty events but is applicable to any severe bleeding emergency. Many hospitals, fire departments, and community organizations offer it at no cost.

**Mental Health First Aid** is an eight-hour certification course that teaches recognition of mental health and substance use challenges. Covered in detail in FAD Document 06.

**Pediatric first aid** courses are offered by the Red Cross and other organizations specifically for childcare providers, and cover choking, allergic reactions, febrile seizures, and other child-specific emergencies.

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Science** — The anatomy basics underlying first aid: airway anatomy, circulatory system, the mechanics of breathing. Understanding why CPR works (manual cardiac compression maintains minimal blood flow to the brain and vital organs) makes the technique more than a rote procedure.

**Mind-Body** — The stress response during emergencies. When you encounter a crisis, your sympathetic nervous system activates: adrenaline surges, heart rate increases, fine motor skills degrade, auditory exclusion occurs (you stop hearing peripheral sounds), and time perception distorts. Understanding this response helps you manage it. The shaking hands and pounding heart are not signs of failure — they are signs that your body is preparing for action. Training teaches you to channel that arousal rather than be overwhelmed by it.

**Learning** — Emergency protocols as procedural memory. The reason certification courses have you practice the same sequence repeatedly is that procedural memory — the memory of how to do something — is stored differently than declarative memory (the memory of facts). Under extreme stress, declarative memory degrades significantly, but procedural memory is more resilient. You may not be able to recite the steps of CPR from a textbook while someone is dying in front of you. But if you have practiced those steps fifty times on a mannequin, your hands will know what to do even when your conscious mind is overwhelmed.

## The Person Who Shows Up

The survival rate for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest in the United States is approximately 10 percent. When a bystander performs CPR before the ambulance arrives, that number doubles or triples. When a bystander uses an AED (automated external defibrillator) within the first few minutes, survival rates can exceed 70 percent.

These are not abstract statistics. They are the difference between a parent who comes home from the grocery store and a parent who does not. A coworker who returns to their desk on Monday and a coworker whose desk sits empty. A child who gets back on the playground and a child who does not.

You do not need to be a doctor. You do not need to be a paramedic. You need to be present, willing, and minimally prepared. The framework in this document — scene safety, PPE, consent, check-call-care, recovery position — is enough to make you the most important person in someone's worst moment.

The professionals are coming. The ambulance is on its way. But the ambulance takes eight minutes on average, and in those eight minutes, the person who is there is you.

Be ready.
