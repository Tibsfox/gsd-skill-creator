# Study Guide: OTC Medications Everyone Should Understand

## The Medicine Cabinet Is a Pharmacy

There are eight medications that belong in every home, every first aid kit, and every medicine cabinet. You probably already own most of them. You have probably taken most of them. And if you are like most people, you have probably taken them without fully understanding what they do, how they work, who should not take them, and how much is too much.

This is a gap in health literacy that kills people. Acetaminophen is the leading cause of acute liver failure in the United States --- not from intentional overdose, but from people who do not realize that the cold medicine they are taking already contains acetaminophen and then take additional Tylenol on top of it. Ibuprofen taken daily by someone with kidney disease can accelerate kidney failure. Aspirin given to a child with a viral infection can trigger Reye's syndrome, a rare but potentially fatal condition.

These are not exotic risks. These are everyday medications used by hundreds of millions of people, and the risks are entirely manageable if you understand what you are taking. The golden rule is the simplest medical advice in this entire research series: READ THE LABEL. Every time. Even if you have taken the medication a hundred times before. The label contains the dose, the maximum daily amount, the warnings, the interactions, and the active ingredient. Everything you need to use the medication safely is on the label. The problem is that most people do not read it.

This study guide covers the eight medications you are most likely to encounter in a first aid or household context. For each one: what it does, how it works, the correct dose, the maximum safe dose, the critical interactions, and who should not take it.

## 1. Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)

### What It Does

Ibuprofen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID). It reduces pain, inflammation, and fever. It is the go-to medication for headaches, menstrual cramps, minor musculoskeletal injuries (sprains, strains, muscle soreness), dental pain, and fever.

### How It Works

Ibuprofen inhibits cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, specifically COX-1 and COX-2. These enzymes produce prostaglandins --- chemical messengers that promote inflammation, pain signaling, and fever. By blocking prostaglandin production, ibuprofen reduces all three.

The COX-1 inhibition is also why ibuprofen has side effects: COX-1 prostaglandins protect the stomach lining and support kidney function. Blocking them reduces that protection.

### Dose

**Adults and children 12+:** 200-400mg every 4-6 hours as needed.
**Maximum OTC dose:** 1200mg per day (three 400mg doses or six 200mg tablets).
**Prescription doses** can go higher (up to 3200mg/day), but only under physician supervision.
**Take with food** to reduce stomach irritation.

### Who Should NOT Take It

- **People with kidney disease** --- ibuprofen reduces blood flow to the kidneys, which can worsen kidney function or trigger acute kidney injury in vulnerable individuals.
- **People on blood thinners** (warfarin, apixaban, rivarelbaban) --- ibuprofen has mild antiplatelet effects and increases bleeding risk when combined with anticoagulants.
- **People with active stomach ulcers or GI bleeding** --- the loss of COX-1 protective prostaglandins makes the stomach lining vulnerable.
- **Third trimester of pregnancy** --- NSAIDs can cause premature closure of the ductus arteriosus in the fetus.
- **People with aspirin-sensitive asthma** --- cross-reactivity can trigger severe bronchospasm.

### Key Interactions

- Other NSAIDs (naproxen, aspirin) --- do not stack. Combining NSAIDs increases all side effects without meaningfully increasing benefit.
- ACE inhibitors and ARBs (blood pressure medications) --- ibuprofen can reduce their effectiveness and worsen kidney function.
- Alcohol --- increases stomach irritation and bleeding risk.

## 2. Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

### What It Does

Acetaminophen reduces pain and fever. It does NOT reduce inflammation. This is the critical difference from ibuprofen --- for a sprained ankle or a sore muscle, ibuprofen is the better choice because the problem is inflammation. For a headache or a fever where inflammation is not the issue, acetaminophen works well and has a different (and in some ways safer) side effect profile.

### How It Works

The mechanism of acetaminophen is, surprisingly, not fully understood. It appears to modulate pain perception centrally (in the brain and spinal cord) rather than peripherally (at the site of injury). It may inhibit a variant of the COX enzyme in the central nervous system, and it appears to interact with the endocannabinoid and serotonergic systems. Despite being one of the most widely used drugs in the world, its precise mechanism remains an active area of research.

### Dose

**Adults:** 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours as needed.
**Maximum OTC dose:** 3000mg per day (some sources say 4000mg, but the FDA and most guidelines have moved to 3000mg as the recommended maximum for safety).
**Children:** Dose by weight, not age. Follow pediatric dosing charts on the package or consult a pharmacist.

### Who Should NOT Take It

- **People with liver disease** --- the liver metabolizes acetaminophen, and a compromised liver is at dramatically higher risk for toxicity.
- **People who drink alcohol regularly** (three or more drinks per day) --- alcohol induces the liver enzymes that convert acetaminophen into its toxic metabolite (NAPQI), lowering the threshold for liver damage.

### The Liver Danger

Acetaminophen toxicity is the leading cause of acute liver failure in the United States. The mechanism: the liver converts a small percentage of each acetaminophen dose into NAPQI, a toxic metabolite. Normally, glutathione neutralizes NAPQI before it causes harm. When the dose exceeds the liver's glutathione supply, NAPQI accumulates and destroys liver cells.

The danger is compounding. Many combination medications contain acetaminophen without prominently advertising it: NyQuil, DayQuil, Percocet, Vicodin, Excedrin, and dozens of cold, flu, and pain formulations. A person who takes Tylenol for a headache and NyQuil for a cold may be taking double the safe dose without realizing it.

**THE RULE:** Before taking acetaminophen, check every other medication you are taking for acetaminophen content. Read the active ingredients list. The word "acetaminophen" or "APAP" (its abbreviation) must not appear in more than one product you are taking simultaneously.

## 3. Aspirin

### What It Does

Aspirin reduces pain, fever, and inflammation (it is an NSAID, like ibuprofen). But aspirin has a unique additional property that earns it a specific, labeled place in the first aid kit: it irreversibly inhibits platelet aggregation. This is the cardiac use.

### The Cardiac Use

During a suspected heart attack (chest pain, jaw pain, left arm pain, shortness of breath, crushing pressure), **chewing 325mg of aspirin** (one regular-strength tablet, or four 81mg baby aspirin) is a bystander intervention that can save a life. The aspirin inhibits platelets from clumping onto the clot that is blocking the coronary artery, slowing the growth of the blockage and potentially maintaining some blood flow to the heart muscle until the ambulance arrives and definitive treatment (catheterization, stent, or thrombolytics) can be delivered.

**Chewed, not swallowed whole.** Chewing breaks the tablet into fragments that are absorbed through the oral mucosa and stomach lining much faster than a whole tablet dissolving in the stomach. In a heart attack, minutes matter. Chew the aspirin.

### Dose

**Pain/fever:** 325-650mg every 4-6 hours. Maximum 4000mg per day.
**Cardiac event:** 325mg chewed once at the time of suspected heart attack, while calling 911.
**Daily low-dose for cardiovascular prevention:** 81mg (baby aspirin). This is a physician-directed regimen and should not be started without medical advice --- the bleeding risk must be weighed against the cardiovascular benefit.

### Who Should NOT Take It

- **Children and teenagers** --- aspirin in children with viral infections (influenza, chickenpox) is associated with Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition causing liver and brain swelling. Use acetaminophen or ibuprofen for children instead.
- **People with aspirin allergy** --- can cause anaphylaxis or severe bronchospasm.
- **People with active bleeding disorders** or who are on anticoagulant therapy.
- **People with active stomach ulcers** --- aspirin irritates the gastric mucosa and inhibits platelet function, both of which worsen GI bleeding.

### Expiration Matters

Aspirin is one of the few OTC medications where expiration significantly affects efficacy. Aspirin degrades into acetic acid (vinegar) and salicylic acid over time. Expired aspirin loses its antiplatelet effect --- the one property that makes it life-saving in a cardiac event. If your aspirin smells like vinegar, replace it. Check the expiration date in your first aid kit quarterly. See the [Build a Home First Aid Kit](../try-sessions/build-home-first-aid-kit.md) try session for maintenance schedules.

## 4. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)

### What It Does

Diphenhydramine is a first-generation antihistamine. It blocks H1 histamine receptors, reducing the effects of histamine release during allergic reactions: itching, hives, nasal congestion, watery eyes, swelling, and skin rash. It also causes significant drowsiness (it crosses the blood-brain barrier readily), which is why it was repurposed as a sleep aid (marketed as ZzzQuil, Unisom SleepGels, and numerous store brands).

### Dose

**Adults and children 12+:** 25-50mg every 4-6 hours as needed.
**Maximum:** 300mg per day.
**Onset:** 15-30 minutes.
**Duration:** 4-6 hours.

### When to Use It

- Allergic reactions: hives, itching, insect stings, mild food allergies, contact dermatitis
- Hay fever symptoms (though second-generation antihistamines like cetirizine or loratadine are preferred for daily allergy management because they cause less drowsiness)
- Motion sickness (it has antiemetic properties)
- Sleep aid (use only occasionally --- tolerance develops rapidly)

### When It Is NOT Enough

Diphenhydramine is a second-line treatment for allergic reactions. For anaphylaxis --- throat swelling, difficulty breathing, widespread hives with hypotension, dizziness or loss of consciousness --- **epinephrine (EpiPen) is the first-line treatment.** Diphenhydramine alone will not stop anaphylaxis. Epinephrine reverses bronchospasm and hypotension. Diphenhydramine reduces the histamine-mediated symptoms after epinephrine has stabilized the patient.

If someone is having an anaphylactic reaction and you do not have epinephrine, call 911 immediately and give diphenhydramine while waiting --- it will help, but it is not sufficient by itself.

### Side Effects

- **Drowsiness** --- significant. Do not drive or operate machinery.
- **Dry mouth, dry eyes** --- anticholinergic effects.
- **Urinary retention** --- particularly in older men with prostate enlargement.
- **Confusion in elderly** --- anticholinergic effects can worsen cognitive function in older adults. The Beers Criteria (a list of medications potentially inappropriate for older adults) includes diphenhydramine.

## 5. Loperamide (Imodium)

### What It Does

Loperamide is an anti-diarrheal. It slows intestinal motility (the contractions that move food and fluid through the gut), allowing more water to be absorbed from the stool and reducing the frequency and urgency of bowel movements.

### Dose

**Adults:** 4mg initially (two capsules or tablets), then 2mg after each subsequent loose stool. Maximum 8mg per day for OTC use (16mg under physician direction).
**Do not use for more than 2 days** without medical advice.

### When to Use It

- Acute non-specific diarrhea (traveler's diarrhea, viral gastroenteritis, food intolerance)
- Situations where diarrhea is functionally incapacitating (long car trips, events, work situations)

### When NOT to Use It

- **Bloody diarrhea** --- blood in the stool suggests bacterial infection (Salmonella, Shigella, E. coli, C. difficile). Slowing gut motility in bacterial infection can worsen the illness by keeping the bacteria and their toxins in the intestine longer.
- **High fever with diarrhea** --- same concern. Fever suggests infection, and infection-caused diarrhea should be evaluated rather than suppressed.
- **Children under 2** --- not recommended.

### Important Note

Loperamide in massive overdose can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias. This is not a risk at recommended doses, but it became a concern when some individuals began taking massive doses (50-100+ tablets) in an attempt to self-treat opioid withdrawal. At recommended doses, loperamide is safe and effective.

### Hydration Is More Important

Diarrhea kills through dehydration, not through the diarrhea itself. Oral rehydration --- water, broth, oral rehydration salts (Pedialyte, or a homemade solution of 1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt) --- is more important than loperamide. Replace fluids first, medicate second.

## 6. Antacids (Tums, Pepto-Bismol)

### What They Do

**Calcium carbonate (Tums):** Neutralizes stomach acid directly. Provides rapid relief from heartburn, acid indigestion, and sour stomach. Also provides supplemental calcium.

**Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol):** Coats the stomach lining, reduces inflammation, and has mild antimicrobial properties. Effective for heartburn, nausea, indigestion, and diarrhea. The pink color is iconic and the taste is distinctive.

### Dose

**Tums:** 1-2 tablets chewed as needed. Maximum varies by formulation --- check label.
**Pepto-Bismol:** 2 tablets or 30mL (2 tablespoons) every 30-60 minutes as needed. Maximum 8 doses per day.

### Interactions and Warnings

- **Pepto-Bismol contains salicylate** (chemically related to aspirin). Do not use in children or teenagers with viral illness (Reye's syndrome risk). Do not combine with aspirin or other salicylates.
- **Pepto-Bismol turns the tongue and stool black.** This is harmless (bismuth + sulfur in saliva and gut = black bismuth sulfide). But it can be alarming if you do not expect it, and it can be confused with GI bleeding (which also produces dark stool). If you have taken Pepto-Bismol recently, the black stool is the Pepto, not blood.
- **Tums can interfere with absorption** of certain antibiotics (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones) and other medications. Take two hours apart from other medications.

## 7. Hydrocortisone Cream (1%)

### What It Does

Hydrocortisone is a mild topical corticosteroid. It reduces itching, redness, and inflammation from insect bites, poison ivy/oak/sumac, contact dermatitis, eczema flares, and minor skin irritations. The 1% concentration is the strongest available without a prescription.

### How to Use

Apply a thin layer to the affected area two to four times daily. Rub in gently. Do not use on open wounds, broken skin, or the face (unless directed by a physician --- facial skin absorbs corticosteroids at a higher rate, and prolonged use can thin the skin).

### Duration

**Do not use for more than 7 days** without medical advice. Prolonged use of topical corticosteroids can thin the skin, cause stretch marks, and suppress local immune function. A week of OTC hydrocortisone is safe. Months of unsupervised use is not.

### When It Is NOT Enough

If a rash is spreading despite treatment, is accompanied by fever, involves large blisters, or is in the eyes, mouth, or genital area, see a doctor. Severe poison ivy/oak reactions may require oral corticosteroids (prednisone), which require a prescription.

## 8. Triple Antibiotic Ointment (Neosporin)

### What It Does

Triple antibiotic ointment contains three topical antibiotics --- bacitracin, neomycin, and polymyxin B --- that together provide broad-spectrum coverage against the bacteria most commonly responsible for wound infections (Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus species, and common skin flora).

### How to Use

After cleaning a minor wound (running water, one minute --- see the [Wound Care Practice try session](../try-sessions/wound-care-practice.md)), apply a thin layer of ointment to the wound bed. Cover with a bandage. Reapply with each bandage change (daily or when wet).

### Does It Actually Work?

The evidence is mixed but leans favorable. Multiple studies show that topical antibiotic ointment reduces minor wound infection rates compared to no treatment. However, the petroleum base of the ointment (which keeps the wound moist) may account for much of the benefit --- plain petroleum jelly (Vaseline) has been shown in some studies to be nearly as effective at preventing wound infection as triple antibiotic ointment.

The practical answer: it probably helps, it is unlikely to harm (with the allergy exception below), and it keeps the wound moist, which is beneficial regardless. Use it.

### Allergy Concern

**Neomycin allergy** is one of the most common contact allergies, affecting an estimated 1-6% of the population. Signs: redness, itching, and rash around the wound that is getting worse, not better, with ointment application. If this occurs, switch to plain bacitracin (which does not contain neomycin) or plain petroleum jelly.

## The Golden Rule: READ THE LABEL

Every medication in this guide has a label. The label contains:

- **Active ingredient(s)** --- what the drug actually is, not the brand name. Learn to look here first.
- **Purpose** --- what it treats.
- **Uses** --- approved indications.
- **Warnings** --- who should not take it, interactions, when to stop.
- **Directions** --- dose and frequency.
- **Other information** --- storage conditions.
- **Inactive ingredients** --- relevant for people with allergies to dyes, fillers, or preservatives.

Read the label every time. Even if you have taken the medication before. Formulations change. Your health conditions change. Other medications you take change. The label is always current. Your memory of the label from two years ago may not be.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Pharmacology]: Every medication in this guide operates through a specific biochemical mechanism --- enzyme inhibition, receptor antagonism, acid neutralization, antimicrobial activity. Understanding the mechanism explains the effects, the side effects, and the interactions. COX inhibition explains why ibuprofen helps inflammation and hurts kidneys. Histamine receptor blockade explains why diphenhydramine stops itching and causes drowsiness. Pharmacology is the science of how chemicals interact with biological systems, and these eight medications are an accessible entry point.
- **Home Economics** --- [Household Health]: A properly stocked medicine cabinet is a household management skill. Knowing which medications to keep, how to store them (cool, dry, out of reach of children), when to replace expired stock, and how to use them safely is no different from knowing how to maintain a smoke detector or a fire extinguisher. It is preparedness infrastructure.
