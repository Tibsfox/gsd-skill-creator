# Study Guide: Reading Vital Signs

## The Five Numbers That Tell You Everything

Vital signs are the body's dashboard. Just as a car's gauges --- speedometer, fuel gauge, temperature, oil pressure --- tell you the state of the engine without opening the hood, vital signs tell you the state of a person's body without a physical exam, without lab tests, without imaging. Five measurements, taken in under two minutes, that reveal whether the heart is working, whether the lungs are working, whether the brain is getting blood, and whether the body is fighting an infection, losing blood, or overheating.

Emergency physicians, paramedics, nurses, and first aiders all use the same vital signs, in the same order, for the same reasons. The numbers have not changed in decades because the physiology has not changed. A pulse rate of 120 meant the same thing in 1950 as it does today: something is wrong, and the heart is compensating.

This study guide teaches you the five vital signs, what the numbers mean, how to measure each one, and when the numbers should worry you. It also covers the AVPU scale --- a rapid assessment of neurological status that takes five seconds and tells you whether the brain is functioning normally. At the end, there is a fifteen-question quiz with answers so you can test yourself.

You do not need a medical degree to take vital signs. You need a watch with a second hand (or a phone timer), a thermometer, and your own two hands. Everything else is observation.

## Vital Sign 1: Pulse (Heart Rate)

### What It Is

The pulse is the wave of pressure that travels through the arteries each time the heart contracts. Every beat pushes blood forward, and that push is palpable --- you can feel it with your fingertips at several points on the body where arteries run close to the surface.

### Normal Range

**Adults:** 60-100 beats per minute (BPM) at rest.
**Children (1-10 years):** 70-120 BPM.
**Infants (under 1 year):** 100-160 BPM.

**Athletes and highly conditioned individuals** may have resting heart rates in the 40s-50s. This is normal for them --- a well-trained heart pumps more blood per beat (higher stroke volume) and therefore needs fewer beats per minute.

### How to Measure

**Radial pulse (wrist):** The most common measurement site. Place your index and middle fingers (never your thumb --- your thumb has its own pulse and will confuse the count) on the inside of the wrist, just below the base of the thumb. You are pressing the radial artery against the radius bone. Press lightly until you feel the rhythmic pulsation.

**Carotid pulse (neck):** Place your index and middle fingers on the side of the neck, in the groove between the trachea (windpipe) and the sternocleidomastoid muscle (the large neck muscle). Press gently. The carotid pulse is stronger than the radial pulse and easier to find in low blood pressure situations. Check one side at a time --- never press both carotid arteries simultaneously (this can reduce blood flow to the brain and theoretically trigger a vagal response).

**Counting:** Count the beats for fifteen seconds using a clock or timer. Multiply by four. This gives you beats per minute. For a more accurate count (especially if the rhythm seems irregular), count for thirty seconds and multiply by two, or count a full sixty seconds.

### What It Means

**Fast (tachycardia, above 100 BPM in adults):** The heart is compensating for something. Common causes: anxiety, pain, fever (heart rate increases approximately 10 BPM for each degree Fahrenheit of fever above normal), dehydration, blood loss, shock, stimulant use (caffeine, amphetamines), hyperthyroidism, or cardiac arrhythmia. A fast pulse in an injured person who is pale and sweating suggests shock (blood loss or fluid loss) until proven otherwise.

**Slow (bradycardia, below 60 BPM in adults):** May be normal in athletes. Abnormal causes: heart block, medication effects (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers), hypothermia, hypothyroidism. A slow pulse in an unconscious person is a concerning sign that the heart's conduction system may be failing.

**Irregular:** A pulse that is not rhythmic --- beats that come at uneven intervals, or that include occasional paused or extra beats. Some irregularity is normal (respiratory sinus arrhythmia --- heart rate varies slightly with breathing). Persistent irregularity, especially in someone with chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath, may indicate atrial fibrillation or other arrhythmias that warrant medical evaluation.

**Weak or thready:** A pulse that is difficult to feel, faint, and fast. This is a classic sign of shock --- the blood pressure is dropping and the peripheral arteries are constricting to maintain blood flow to vital organs. A weak, fast pulse in a trauma patient is an emergency.

**Bounding:** A pulse that is strong, full, and easily felt. Can be normal during exercise. At rest, may indicate fever, anxiety, or early fluid overload.

## Vital Sign 2: Breathing (Respiratory Rate)

### What It Is

Respiratory rate is the number of breaths per minute. One breath = one inhalation plus one exhalation. One complete rise and fall of the chest.

### Normal Range

**Adults:** 12-20 breaths per minute at rest.
**Children:** 20-30 breaths per minute (varies by age).
**Infants:** 30-60 breaths per minute.

### How to Measure

Watch the chest. Count each rise (inhalation) for thirty seconds and multiply by two. The key: do NOT tell the person you are counting their breathing. If they know, they will consciously control it, and you will get an inaccurate number. The standard technique is to count respirations while pretending to still be taking the pulse --- keep your fingers on their wrist, but watch their chest instead of looking at your watch.

### What It Means

**Fast (tachypnea, above 20 in adults):** The body needs more oxygen or is trying to blow off more carbon dioxide. Causes: anxiety, pain, fever, asthma, pneumonia, heart failure, blood loss (the body hyperventilates to compensate), metabolic acidosis (diabetic ketoacidosis causes deep, rapid breathing called Kussmaul respirations). A respiratory rate above 24 in an adult is always abnormal.

**Slow (bradypnea, below 12 in adults):** Not enough respiratory drive. Causes: opioid overdose (the most important one to recognize --- opioids suppress the brainstem's respiratory center), head injury, extreme hypothermia, sedative overdose. A respiratory rate below 8 in an adult is an emergency.

**Labored:** Breathing that requires visible effort --- use of accessory muscles (neck muscles pulling upward with each breath), nasal flaring, retractions (skin pulling in between the ribs or above the collarbone with each breath). Labored breathing indicates the body is working hard to move air and something is obstructing or restricting that movement.

**Shallow:** Breaths that are rapid but not moving much air. The chest barely rises. This is ineffective breathing --- the person may be getting enough breaths per minute but not enough volume per breath. Can indicate rib fractures (pain limits expansion), pneumonia, or fatigue in a person who has been breathing hard for an extended period.

**Gasping (agonal breathing):** Irregular, infrequent, labored gasps that occur in the first minutes of cardiac arrest. Agonal breathing is NOT breathing. It sounds like snoring, gurgling, or gulping and occurs perhaps once every ten to thirty seconds. A person with agonal breathing needs CPR, not the recovery position. See [FAD-01: First Aid Foundations](../01-first-aid-foundations.md).

**Cheyne-Stokes:** A distinctive cycling pattern --- breathing gradually increases in depth and rate, then gradually decreases, then stops entirely for a period (apnea), then the cycle repeats. This pattern indicates serious neurological or cardiac dysfunction and is sometimes seen in dying patients.

## Vital Sign 3: Blood Pressure

### What It Is

Blood pressure is the force that blood exerts against the walls of the arteries. It is expressed as two numbers: systolic (the pressure during a heartbeat, when the heart is contracting) over diastolic (the pressure between heartbeats, when the heart is relaxing).

### Normal Range

**Normal:** Systolic below 120 AND diastolic below 80 (written as "120/80" and spoken as "120 over 80").
**Elevated:** Systolic 120-129 AND diastolic below 80.
**Hypertension Stage 1:** Systolic 130-139 OR diastolic 80-89.
**Hypertension Stage 2:** Systolic 140 or higher OR diastolic 90 or higher.
**Hypertensive crisis:** Systolic above 180 AND/OR diastolic above 120. This is a medical emergency. Call 911.
**Hypotension (low):** Systolic below 90 OR diastolic below 60. May be normal for some individuals. In an injured or ill person, hypotension indicates shock.

### How to Measure

Blood pressure requires a sphygmomanometer (blood pressure cuff) --- either manual (with a stethoscope) or automatic (digital). Automatic home blood pressure monitors are available for $30-50 at any pharmacy and are accurate enough for monitoring.

**For first aiders without a blood pressure cuff:** You cannot measure blood pressure numerically, but you can estimate circulatory status using the pulse. A strong, easily palpable radial pulse generally correlates with a systolic pressure of at least 80 mmHg. A palpable carotid pulse but absent radial pulse suggests systolic pressure between 60-80 mmHg (significant hypotension). An absent carotid pulse means cardiac arrest.

### What It Means

**High blood pressure** damages blood vessel walls over time, contributing to heart attack, stroke, kidney damage, and vision loss. It is called the "silent killer" because most people with hypertension have no symptoms until a catastrophic event occurs. Sustained blood pressure above 180/120 can cause acute organ damage (hypertensive emergency) --- headache, chest pain, vision changes, confusion, and nosebleeds are warning signs.

**Low blood pressure** in a trauma patient means blood loss. Low blood pressure in a medical patient may mean dehydration, sepsis, cardiac failure, or anaphylaxis. Low blood pressure with a fast pulse is the hallmark of shock.

## Vital Sign 4: Temperature

### What It Is

Core body temperature, reflecting the balance between heat production (metabolism) and heat loss (radiation, convection, evaporation, conduction).

### Normal Range

**Normal:** 98.6 degrees F (37 degrees C). Individual variation is normal --- some people run slightly cooler or warmer. Normal range extends from approximately 97 degrees F to 99 degrees F.
**Fever:** 100.4 degrees F (38 degrees C) or above.
**High fever:** 103 degrees F (39.4 degrees C) or above --- warrants medical evaluation.
**Dangerous fever:** 104 degrees F (40 degrees C) or above --- seek emergency care.
**Hypothermia:** Below 95 degrees F (35 degrees C) --- the body is losing heat faster than it can produce it. This is an emergency.

### How to Measure

**Oral thermometer:** Most common for adults. Place under the tongue, close mouth, wait for the beep. Avoid eating or drinking for fifteen minutes before measurement (hot or cold liquids affect the reading).

**Temporal artery (forehead):** Quick and non-invasive. Swipe across the forehead. Less accurate than oral but useful for screening, especially in children.

**Axillary (armpit):** Place the thermometer in the armpit and hold the arm down. Reads approximately 1 degree F lower than oral temperature. Useful for children who will not tolerate oral measurement.

### What It Means

**Fever** indicates the immune system is fighting an infection. Fever itself is not the disease --- it is the body's defense mechanism. Moderate fever (up to 102 degrees F in adults) does not generally require treatment unless the person is uncomfortable. Acetaminophen and ibuprofen reduce fever and improve comfort. See the [Medication Safety Study Guide](medication-safety-reference.md).

**When fever is dangerous:** Fever above 103 degrees F, fever with stiff neck (meningitis concern), fever with confusion (CNS infection or sepsis), fever with rash (possible serious infection), fever in immunocompromised individuals, fever in infants under three months (always seek emergency evaluation).

**Hypothermia** indicates environmental cold exposure exceeding the body's ability to compensate. Mild hypothermia (90-95 degrees F): shivering, confusion, fumbling hands. Moderate hypothermia (82-90 degrees F): shivering stops (a dangerous sign --- the body has exhausted its shivering capacity), severe confusion, sleepiness. Severe hypothermia (below 82 degrees F): unconsciousness, cardiac arrhythmias, death. See [FAD-05: Environmental Emergencies](../05-environmental.md).

## Vital Sign 5: Skin

### What It Is

Skin assessment is the vital sign you take with your eyes and hands. Skin color, temperature, and moisture reflect circulatory status, oxygenation, and body temperature in real time.

### Color

**Pink (normal):** Adequate blood flow and oxygenation. In darker-skinned individuals, assess skin color at the nail beds, lips, and mucous membranes (inside the mouth and lower eyelids), where perfusion changes are visible regardless of skin tone.

**Pale:** Blood is being diverted away from the skin. Causes: shock (blood loss, dehydration), fear/anxiety (sympathetic nervous system vasoconstriction), cold exposure. Pale + fast pulse + sweating = shock until proven otherwise.

**Blue/gray (cyanosis):** Inadequate oxygenation. The blood is not carrying enough oxygen. Causes: respiratory failure, choking, cardiac arrest, severe asthma, pulmonary embolism. Cyanosis of the lips and fingertips is a sign that the person is not getting enough oxygen and needs immediate intervention.

**Flushed (red):** Causes: fever (the body is radiating heat through dilated skin blood vessels), heat exposure, exertion, allergic reaction, sunburn, carbon monoxide poisoning (classically described as "cherry red" skin, though this is often a late finding).

**Yellow (jaundice):** Liver dysfunction. Bilirubin, a breakdown product of hemoglobin, accumulates when the liver cannot process it. Visible in the whites of the eyes (sclera) before it is visible in the skin. Not a first aid emergency, but a sign of significant medical illness.

### Temperature

Touch the skin with the back of your hand (more sensitive to temperature than the palm).

**Warm:** Normal, or fever, or heat exposure.
**Cool:** Peripheral vasoconstriction --- the body is diverting blood to the core. Shock, cold exposure, or anxiety.
**Hot:** Fever, heat stroke, or sunburn.
**Cold:** Significant hypothermia or severe shock.

### Moisture

**Dry:** Normal.
**Sweaty (diaphoresis):** Exertion, anxiety, pain, fever breaking, or shock. Cool and sweaty is the classic shock presentation.
**Clammy:** Cool and moist. Shock. This is a dangerous sign that the body is losing the fight to maintain blood pressure and perfusion.

## The AVPU Scale: Mental Status in Five Seconds

AVPU is a rapid neurological assessment that tells you how well the brain is functioning.

**A --- Alert:** The person is awake, oriented, and responds appropriately. They know who they are, where they are, and approximately what time/day it is. This is normal.

**V --- Responds to Voice:** The person is not spontaneously alert but responds when you speak to them --- opens eyes, turns toward you, answers questions (even if confused). This is a decreased level of consciousness.

**P --- Responds to Pain:** The person does not respond to voice but reacts to painful stimulation. In first aid, a firm pinch to the trapezius muscle (the muscle between the neck and shoulder) is the standard stimulus. If they grimace, pull away, or moan, they are responsive to pain. This indicates significant neurological depression.

**U --- Unresponsive:** No response to voice or pain. The person is unconscious. This is the level that triggers CPR assessment (check breathing) and the recovery position (if breathing).

AVPU does not replace a full neurological exam. But it takes five seconds, requires no equipment, and communicates the person's neurological status in a single letter that any responder can understand. "The patient is V" tells the arriving paramedic exactly what they need to know about brain function.

## Practice: Take Your Own Vitals Right Now

1. **Pulse:** Find your radial pulse. Count for fifteen seconds. Multiply by four. Write it down.
2. **Breathing:** Count your breaths for thirty seconds. Multiply by two. Write it down.
3. **Temperature:** Use a thermometer if you have one. Write it down.
4. **Skin:** Look at your nail beds. Feel the skin on your forearm with the back of your other hand. Note color, temperature, moisture.
5. **AVPU:** You are reading this document. You are A.

Congratulations. You just took a complete set of vital signs. These are YOUR baseline numbers. They are useful to know, because if you ever need to assess someone else, you will have your own normal to compare against.

## Quiz: Test Your Knowledge

**1.** What is the normal resting heart rate range for an adult?
**2.** You find a person with a pulse of 130, pale skin, and sweating. What condition does this suggest?
**3.** Why should you not use your thumb to take a pulse?
**4.** What is the normal respiratory rate for an adult?
**5.** What is agonal breathing, and does it count as breathing for CPR decision-making?
**6.** What blood pressure reading constitutes a hypertensive crisis?
**7.** A trauma patient has a palpable carotid pulse but no radial pulse. What does this suggest about their blood pressure?
**8.** At what temperature (Fahrenheit) is a person considered hypothermic?
**9.** What does cyanosis indicate?
**10.** Where should you assess skin color in a person with dark skin?
**11.** What does "V" stand for in the AVPU scale?
**12.** A person's skin is cool and clammy. What condition does this classic presentation suggest?
**13.** Why does heart rate increase with fever?
**14.** You are counting a person's respiratory rate. Why should you not tell them what you are doing?
**15.** A person who was agitated and confused after a fall is now only responding to a firm shoulder pinch. What is their AVPU level, and is their condition improving or deteriorating?

### Answers

**1.** 60-100 beats per minute.
**2.** Shock (compensated --- the fast heart rate and vasoconstriction are the body's attempt to maintain blood pressure).
**3.** Your thumb has its own pulse (from the princeps pollicis artery) which can be confused with the patient's pulse.
**4.** 12-20 breaths per minute.
**5.** Agonal breathing is irregular, infrequent gasping that occurs in early cardiac arrest. It does NOT count as breathing. A person with agonal breathing needs CPR.
**6.** Systolic above 180 and/or diastolic above 120.
**7.** Systolic blood pressure is approximately 60-80 mmHg (significant hypotension). The body has enough pressure to perfuse the carotid arteries but not the radial arteries.
**8.** Below 95 degrees F (35 degrees C).
**9.** Inadequate oxygenation (hypoxia). The blood is not carrying sufficient oxygen.
**10.** Nail beds, lips, and mucous membranes (inside the mouth, lower eyelids).
**11.** Responds to Voice.
**12.** Shock.
**13.** The increased metabolic rate of fever increases oxygen demand, and the heart beats faster to deliver more oxygenated blood. Approximately 10 BPM increase per degree F above normal.
**14.** Conscious awareness of breathing causes people to alter their breathing pattern. Counting without their knowledge gives an accurate spontaneous rate.
**15.** AVPU level: P (responds to Pain). This is deterioration --- they went from V (agitated and confused = responsive to voice) to P. Deteriorating level of consciousness after a head injury is a neurological emergency.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Physiology]: Every vital sign is a window into a physiological system. Pulse reflects cardiac function and autonomic nervous system state. Respiratory rate reflects pulmonary function and metabolic demand. Blood pressure reflects cardiovascular integrity and fluid volume. Temperature reflects thermoregulation and immune activity. Skin reflects peripheral perfusion and oxygenation. Understanding the physiology behind the numbers transforms vital sign assessment from rote measurement to clinical reasoning.
- **Learning** --- [Clinical Observation]: Taking vital signs is a skill that improves with practice. The first time you try to find a radial pulse, it may take thirty seconds. After twenty repetitions, you will find it in two seconds. This is perceptual learning --- your fingers become attuned to the subtle sensation, and the signal emerges from the noise. Counting respiratory rate without being noticed is an observational skill that develops the same way.
