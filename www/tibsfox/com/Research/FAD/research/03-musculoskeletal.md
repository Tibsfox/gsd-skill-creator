# Bones, Muscles, and Joints — Musculoskeletal Injuries

## The Frame That Holds Everything Together

The human skeleton is an engineering marvel — 206 bones connected by ligaments, moved by muscles, cushioned by cartilage, and coordinated by a nervous system that lets you thread a needle or swing a sledgehammer with equal precision. It is strong enough to support hundreds of pounds of load, flexible enough to absorb shock and permit a full range of motion, and capable of repairing itself after injury in ways that no manufactured material can match.

It is also, unfortunately, breakable.

Musculoskeletal injuries — fractures, sprains, strains, dislocations — are among the most common reasons for emergency room visits in the United States. The National Safety Council reports millions of medically consulted injuries per year involving bones, muscles, and joints. They happen in car accidents and on hiking trails, in workshops and on playgrounds, on ice-covered parking lots and in gym classes. They range from the trivial (a mildly sprained ankle that hurts for a week) to the catastrophic (a spinal fracture that changes a life forever).

The first aid for musculoskeletal injuries follows a core principle: stabilize the injury and prevent further damage until professional care is available. You are not setting bones. You are not reducing dislocations. You are not diagnosing fractures. You are keeping things from getting worse.

## Fractures — When Bones Break

A fracture is any break in the continuity of a bone, from a hairline crack to a complete shattering. They are caused by force that exceeds the bone's structural tolerance — a fall, a blow, a twist, a compression. The force required depends on the bone's density (which decreases with age, making fractures more common in the elderly), the direction and speed of the impact, and the bone's geometry.

### Signs and Symptoms

Not all fractures are obvious. A displaced fracture — where the bone ends have shifted out of alignment — may produce visible deformity, an arm bent where it should not be, a leg shortened and rotated. But a non-displaced fracture or a hairline fracture may show none of the dramatic signs. Here is what to look for:

**Deformity.** The limb looks wrong — angulated, shortened, rotated compared to the other side. This is the most reliable sign that a bone is broken, and it means the fracture is displaced.

**Swelling.** Broken bones bleed internally (bone marrow is a blood-producing organ), and the bleeding causes rapid, significant swelling at the fracture site. A femur fracture can bleed a liter or more into the thigh.

**Pain on movement.** The person cannot move the injured area, or movement causes severe pain that is out of proportion to what you would expect from a bruise or sprain. Pain at the fracture site that is reproducible with gentle palpation (touching) is called point tenderness and is a reliable indicator of fracture.

**Crepitus.** A grinding or crackling sensation felt (or sometimes heard) when the fractured ends move against each other. Do not deliberately try to produce this — if you feel it during your assessment, note it and stop moving the area.

**Loss of function.** The person cannot use the injured extremity normally. They cannot bear weight on a fractured leg, cannot grip with a hand that has a fractured metacarpal, cannot lift the arm with a fractured shoulder.

**Bruising.** Ecchymosis (bruising) develops over hours as blood from the fracture site tracks through tissue to the skin surface. It may not be present initially.

### Open vs. Closed Fractures

A **closed fracture** is a break where the skin is intact. The bone is broken inside but has not pierced through the skin surface.

An **open fracture** (compound fracture) is one where the bone has broken through the skin, or where a wound communicates with the fracture site. Open fractures are a surgical emergency due to the risk of osteomyelitis (bone infection). If you see bone protruding through skin, or a wound at a fracture site with bone visible in the wound, cover the wound with a moist sterile dressing, stabilize the limb, and call 911. Do not push the bone back in. Do not pour anything into the wound.

### Splinting

Splinting immobilizes the fracture to prevent further injury during transport. The principles are straightforward:

**Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture.** A forearm fracture (radius or ulna) requires immobilization of both the wrist and the elbow. A tibia fracture requires immobilization of the knee and the ankle. The reason: any movement of an adjacent joint transmits force through the fracture site.

**Pad the splint.** A rigid splint (SAM splint, board, magazine, tightly rolled clothing) applied directly to skin is painful and can cause pressure injuries. Pad between the splint and the limb with soft material — a towel, clothing, gauze.

**Splint in the position found.** This is critical. Do not try to straighten a fractured limb. Do not try to realign the bone. Attempted reduction (realignment) by someone without specific training and appropriate sedation can damage blood vessels, nerves, and soft tissue. Splint the limb in whatever position it is in when you find it. If the arm is angulated, splint it angulated. If the leg is bent, splint it bent.

**Check CSM — Circulation, Sensation, Movement — before and after splinting.** This is the mnemonic that ensures your splint is not causing more harm than the injury.

- **Circulation:** Check the pulse distal to (below) the injury and splint. For an arm injury, check the radial pulse at the wrist. For a leg injury, check the pedal pulse on top of the foot. Also check capillary refill: press on a fingernail or toenail until it blanches (turns white), then release. Color should return within two seconds. If it does not, the circulation is compromised.
- **Sensation:** Can the person feel you touching their fingers or toes beyond the splint? Numbness or tingling indicates nerve compression.
- **Movement:** Can they wiggle their fingers or toes? Loss of movement may indicate nerve damage or circulatory compromise.

If CSM is impaired after splinting, the splint is too tight. Loosen it and recheck. If CSM was impaired before splinting, note it — the injury itself may be compressing a vessel or nerve, and this information matters to the emergency room team.

**Improvised splints.** You do not need a SAM splint from a first aid kit to splint a fracture. A forearm can be splinted with a folded magazine and a bandana. A finger can be buddy-taped to the adjacent finger. A leg can be splinted with a sleeping pad and duct tape. The person's own body can serve as a splint — an injured arm can be immobilized against the chest with a sling, a fractured leg can be splinted to the other leg.

**Do NOT try to realign the fracture.** This point bears repeating because the temptation is strong, especially when the limb looks dramatically deformed. In the field, without imaging, without pain management, without the ability to manage vascular complications, attempted reduction is more likely to cause additional injury than to help. Splint it as you find it. Let the emergency room handle the rest.

## Sprains vs. Strains — Know the Difference

These two terms are used interchangeably in casual conversation, but they are different injuries to different tissues.

A **sprain** is an injury to a ligament — the tough, fibrous connective tissue that connects bone to bone at a joint. Sprains occur when a joint is forced beyond its normal range of motion. The classic example is the ankle inversion sprain: you step on an uneven surface, your foot rolls inward, and the ligaments on the outside of the ankle stretch or tear.

Sprains are graded by severity:

- **Grade I:** Ligament fibers are stretched but not torn. Mild pain, mild swelling, the joint feels stable.
- **Grade II:** Partial tear. Moderate pain, moderate swelling, some joint laxity (looseness). Bruising is common.
- **Grade III:** Complete tear. Severe pain initially (though it may paradoxically hurt less than a Grade II once the ligament is completely disrupted and no longer sending partial stretch signals), significant swelling, the joint is unstable and may feel like it "gives way."

A **strain** is an injury to a muscle or tendon (the tissue that connects muscle to bone). Strains occur from overuse, overstretching, or sudden forceful contraction. A pulled hamstring, a strained lower back, a torn rotator cuff — these are strains.

In the field, the distinction between sprain and strain rarely changes your first aid approach. Both benefit from the same initial management protocol.

## PRICE — Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation

The original RICE protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) has been updated with the addition of Protection as the first step. Some sources now use POLICE (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation), reflecting current sports medicine thinking that early gentle movement may promote healing better than complete immobilization. For first aid purposes, PRICE covers the bases.

**Protection.** Prevent further injury. Remove the person from the activity that caused the injury. If it is a leg, do not let them walk on it until the severity is assessed. If it is a wrist, stop the activity.

**Rest.** The injured structure needs time to begin the healing process. For the first 48 to 72 hours, the injured area should be rested. This does not mean permanent immobilization — after the initial period, gentle range-of-motion exercises (as tolerated) actually promote healing.

**Ice.** Apply cold for twenty minutes on, then at least twenty minutes off, repeating as needed. Cold reduces blood flow to the injured area, limiting swelling and providing pain relief. Do not apply ice directly to skin — use a thin cloth barrier to prevent cold injury (frostbite from an ice pack is an ironic but real possibility). Frozen vegetables in a bag conform to body contours better than rigid ice packs.

The twenty-minute limit matters. Extended cold application can cause tissue damage and paradoxically increase inflammation. Set a timer.

**Compression.** An elastic bandage (ACE wrap) applied snugly — but not tightly — around the injured area helps control swelling. Wrap from distal to proximal (from the toes toward the knee for an ankle injury) to help fluid move back toward the heart. Check circulation beyond the bandage frequently (can they feel their toes? are the toes warm and pink?).

**Elevation.** Keep the injured area above the level of the heart when possible. For an ankle sprain, this means lying down with the foot propped on pillows. Elevation uses gravity to reduce swelling by promoting venous and lymphatic drainage.

## Sling Application

A sling immobilizes an injured arm, wrist, or shoulder by supporting the weight of the arm and holding it against the body. The triangular bandage — a square of cloth folded into a triangle — is the classic sling material, and it is included in most first aid kits.

Application of a triangular bandage arm sling:

1. Place the triangular bandage under the injured arm with the point toward the elbow and the long edge along the uninjured side.
2. Bring the lower end of the bandage up and over the injured arm to the neck on the injured side.
3. The upper end should already be at the neck on the uninjured side.
4. Tie the two ends together at the side of the neck (not at the back of the neck, where the knot will press against the spine and become uncomfortable quickly). A square knot works best.
5. Fold the point at the elbow forward and pin or tuck it to secure the elbow.
6. Adjust so that the hand is elevated slightly above the elbow (this promotes drainage and reduces swelling in the hand and fingers).
7. Check CSM — can they feel their fingers? Can they move them? Are the fingers warm and pink?

If you do not have a triangular bandage, a sling can be improvised from a shirt (pin the hem to the chest of the shirt with the arm inside), a belt, a scarf, or even by tucking the hand into a buttoned jacket.

For a clavicle (collarbone) fracture — a common injury from falls onto an outstretched hand or direct shoulder impacts — a sling alone may be sufficient. The person will naturally hold the arm close to their body because any movement of the shoulder is painful. Support this position with the sling and transport for evaluation.

## Dislocations

A dislocation occurs when the bones at a joint are forced out of their normal alignment. The shoulder is the most commonly dislocated large joint (the ball-and-socket design trades stability for range of motion), followed by the finger, patella (kneecap), and elbow.

**Do NOT attempt to reduce (relocate) a dislocation.** This is a firm rule for first aid providers. Reduction of a dislocation requires imaging to rule out associated fractures, sedation or local anesthesia for pain management, and specific technique knowledge for the joint involved. Attempting to "pop it back in" without these can fracture bone, tear blood vessels, damage nerves, or trap soft tissue in the joint.

First aid for a dislocation:

1. **Splint in the position found.** A dislocated shoulder with the arm held away from the body? Splint it there, supporting the arm with pillows or padding. A dislocated finger bent at an angle? Splint it at that angle.
2. **Apply ice** to reduce swelling and pain.
3. **Monitor CSM** distal to the dislocation. A dislocated joint can compress or stretch blood vessels and nerves. If the hand is pale, blue, numb, or pulseless below a dislocated shoulder, this is a limb-threatening emergency — transport urgently.
4. **Transport** for medical evaluation and reduction.

The person with a dislocation will be in significant pain and will resist any movement of the joint. Do not force movement. Calm reassurance, gentle immobilization, and rapid transport are the correct approach.

## Back Injuries and Spinal Precautions

Back injuries occupy a special place in first aid because the consequences of mismanagement are catastrophic. A fractured vertebra is a bone injury. A spinal cord injury is a neurological catastrophe that can result in permanent paralysis. The difference between the two often depends on whether the fractured vertebra moves and compresses or severs the spinal cord — and that movement can be caused by improper handling.

### When to Suspect Spinal Injury

The mechanism of injury is your primary guide. Suspect spinal injury if:

- **Fall from a significant height** — generally defined as greater than three times the person's height, or any fall onto the head
- **Diving injury** — a classic mechanism for cervical spine fracture
- **Vehicle accident** — especially ejection from the vehicle, rollover, or high-speed impact
- **Bicycle or motorcycle crash** with significant force
- **Significant head trauma** — if there was enough force to cause a head injury, there may have been enough force to injure the cervical spine
- **Axial loading** — a heavy object falling on the head or shoulders
- **Any trauma in a person who is unconscious** and cannot tell you what happened or what hurts

Also suspect spinal injury if the person reports:

- Neck or back pain, especially midline (over the spine)
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in the extremities
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Priapism (in males — an indicator of spinal cord injury)

### What to Do

**Tell the person not to move.** If they are conscious and cooperative, the most important intervention is verbal: "Please don't move your head or neck. Stay exactly as you are. Help is coming." Most cooperative patients will hold still if they understand why it matters.

**Manual cervical stabilization.** If you are trained and the person is supine (on their back), kneel at the top of their head and place your hands on either side of the head, holding it in a neutral position (eyes facing straight up, not rotated or tilted). Hold this position until EMS arrives and applies a cervical collar and backboard.

**Log roll.** If you must turn a person with a suspected spinal injury (for example, to clear the airway of an unconscious person who is vomiting), use the log roll technique. This requires a minimum of three people: one at the head directing the roll and maintaining cervical alignment, one at the shoulders and torso, and one at the hips and legs. On the count of the person at the head, all three roll the patient as a unit, keeping the spine in alignment.

**Do not remove helmets** from motorcycle or bicycle accident victims unless you must access the airway and cannot do so with the helmet in place. Helmet removal requires two people and specific technique to avoid cervical spine movement.

## Workshop Injuries — The WDC Connection

The woodcraft and workshop environment presents specific musculoskeletal injury patterns that deserve special attention, connecting this document to the WDC (Woodcraft) research project.

**Table saw kickback.** A table saw can throw a piece of wood back at the operator at speeds exceeding 100 mph. The most common injuries are to the hands (contact with the blade during or after kickback), the abdomen and chest (struck by the ejected workpiece), and the face. Fractures from high-velocity wood impacts are not uncommon. A riving knife, proper blade guard, and push sticks are the preventive measures — but when prevention fails, the first aid is splinting, wound care, and transport.

**Chisel slips.** A sharp chisel driven by a mallet that slips off the workpiece can inflict deep lacerations and puncture wounds to the non-dominant hand. The tendons of the hand run close to the surface, and even a relatively minor-looking cut can sever a tendon, resulting in loss of function to a finger. Any deep laceration on the hand that is followed by inability to fully flex or extend a finger requires surgical evaluation.

**Falls from ladders.** Workshop and home improvement projects frequently involve ladders, and falls from ladders are a leading cause of traumatic injury in the home environment. The classic pattern is a lateral fall from a stepladder, landing on an outstretched arm, resulting in fractures of the wrist (Colles fracture), forearm, or proximal humerus, and potentially head or spinal injury depending on the height and landing surface.

The first aid for all of these is the same: assess the injury, splint as found, control any bleeding, monitor for signs of shock if the injury is severe, and transport for evaluation.

## When to Call 911 for Musculoskeletal Injuries

Most musculoskeletal injuries do not require an ambulance — they require a car ride to the emergency room or urgent care. But some situations demand 911:

- **Open fracture** — bone visible or protruding through skin
- **Suspected spinal injury** — any mechanism or symptoms that suggest spinal involvement
- **Loss of circulation or sensation** below the injury that does not improve with repositioning
- **Obvious deformity of a major bone** (femur, pelvis) — femur fractures can cause life-threatening internal bleeding
- **Multiple injuries** — the person has more than one significant injury
- **The person cannot be safely moved** — they are in a remote location, on a slope, or the injury prevents any positioning change
- **Signs of shock** — pale, cool, clammy skin, rapid pulse, confusion, agitation — which can indicate significant internal bleeding from a fracture

For most sprains, isolated limb fractures in stable patients, and minor dislocations (fingers), urgent care or emergency room evaluation by private vehicle is appropriate. Apply first aid, immobilize, and drive carefully.

## College Connection

This document connects to two departments within the College structure:

**Science** — Skeletal anatomy and the biomechanics of injury. Understanding the structure of bone (cortical bone surrounding trabecular bone, periosteum covering the surface, marrow cavity inside), the types of joints (ball-and-socket, hinge, pivot, saddle, gliding), and the soft tissue structures (ligaments, tendons, cartilage, bursae) that hold everything together provides the foundation for understanding why injuries occur, why they present the way they do, and why the first aid interventions work. A spiral fracture pattern tells you the injury mechanism was rotational. An avulsion fracture tells you the ligament was strong enough to pull bone away rather than tearing itself. The anatomy is the story.

**Physical Education** — Injury prevention. Proper warm-up, appropriate progression in training load, correct form in lifting and movement, adequate rest and recovery, and sport-specific protective equipment all reduce the risk of musculoskeletal injury. The best first aid is the injury that never happens. Conditioning programs that emphasize joint stability (proprioceptive training for ankles, rotator cuff strengthening for shoulders, core stability for the spine) are effective injury prevention strategies supported by strong evidence.

## The Body Will Heal

The human body's capacity for repair is remarkable. Bones knit. Ligaments scar. Muscles regenerate. A fracture that is properly aligned and immobilized will, in most cases, heal to full strength within six to twelve weeks. The skeleton you have at sixty is not the skeleton you were born with — every bone in your body has been remodeled multiple times throughout your life, old bone dissolved by osteoclasts and new bone laid down by osteoblasts in a continuous cycle of maintenance.

Your job in the first minutes and hours after a musculoskeletal injury is to give the body's repair systems the best possible starting conditions: prevent further damage, control pain and swelling, maintain circulation, and get the person to a professional who can provide definitive care.

The body will do the rest. It has been doing it for a very long time.
