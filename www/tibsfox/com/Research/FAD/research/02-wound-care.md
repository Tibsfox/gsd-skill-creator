# Bleeding, Burns, and Bandages — Wound Care

## Blood Has a Way of Getting Your Attention

There is something primal about the sight of blood. It bypasses rational thought and triggers an immediate emotional response — panic, revulsion, urgency, sometimes all three at once. This response is useful in evolutionary terms: it motivates action. But in first aid terms, it can be misleading. A small scalp laceration can produce a shocking amount of blood while posing no real danger. A deep puncture wound in the abdomen can bleed almost invisibly into the body cavity while the person dies from the inside out.

Wound care is about learning to see past the emotional response and make clinical assessments. How much blood is actually being lost? Where is it coming from? Is it stopping on its own or getting worse? Does this need a bandage, a trip to urgent care, or an ambulance?

This document covers the spectrum — from the kitchen cut that needs a band-aid to the arterial hemorrhage that needs a tourniquet. The techniques range from things you will use weekly to things you may use once in your life. All of them are worth knowing.

## Minor Wounds — Clean, Protect, Cover

The vast majority of wounds you will deal with in a lifetime are minor: cuts, scrapes, abrasions, small punctures. They hurt, they bleed a little, and they heal on their own if you keep them clean. The goal of first aid for minor wounds is simple — prevent infection and create conditions for healing.

### Cleaning the Wound

**Use clean running water.** Hold the wound under a faucet and let the water flow over and through it for at least a minute. The mechanical action of the water removes dirt, debris, and bacteria far more effectively than any antiseptic. If you are outdoors, use your water bottle. If you have a wound irrigation syringe (included in some first aid kits), use it to create a gentle pressure stream.

**Do NOT use hydrogen peroxide.** This is one of the most persistent myths in wound care. Hydrogen peroxide does kill bacteria — but it also kills fibroblasts, the cells that build new tissue and close wounds. Studies published in the Journal of Wound Care, the Annals of Emergency Medicine, and multiple other peer-reviewed sources have consistently shown that hydrogen peroxide impairs wound healing. It damages healthy tissue, delays closure, and may actually increase infection risk by destroying the cells that form the body's natural barrier.

The bubbling that people associate with "it's working" is the enzyme catalase in your tissue breaking down the peroxide. It is not "cleaning" the wound. It is a chemical reaction that is harming the very cells you need to heal.

**Do NOT use rubbing alcohol on open wounds.** Same principle — tissue damage outweighs antimicrobial benefit, and the pain is significant.

**Soap is fine for the skin around the wound** but should not be scrubbed directly into the wound bed. Mild soap and water on the surrounding skin helps remove contamination.

**Antibiotic ointment** (bacitracin, Neosporin, or generic triple antibiotic) applied in a thin layer after cleaning provides a protective antimicrobial barrier and keeps the wound moist, which promotes faster healing. Some people are allergic to neomycin (one of the components in triple antibiotic ointment) — if redness and itching develop around a wound being treated with antibiotic ointment, switch to plain bacitracin or petroleum jelly.

### Bandaging

Cover the wound with an adhesive bandage or gauze pad secured with medical tape. The purpose of the bandage is threefold: protect from further contamination, absorb any ongoing drainage, and provide a reminder not to pick at or disturb the wound.

Change the bandage daily, or whenever it becomes wet, dirty, or soaked through. Each bandage change is an opportunity to inspect the wound for signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaks extending from the wound, or fever. These signs warrant a visit to a doctor.

### When Stitches Are Needed

Not every wound needs stitches (sutures), but some wounds will not heal properly without them. The general guidelines for seeking medical care for wound closure:

- **The wound edges do not stay together on their own.** If the cut gapes open when you release pressure, it probably needs closure.
- **The wound is deeper than a quarter inch.** Deep cuts that penetrate through the full thickness of the skin into the fat or muscle below need professional closure.
- **The wound is on the face, hands, feet, or over a joint.** These areas require precise closure for cosmetic and functional reasons. A poorly healed laceration on a finger can limit range of motion permanently.
- **The wound will not stop bleeding after fifteen minutes of steady direct pressure.** Persistent bleeding suggests a larger vessel is involved and may require ligation (tying off) in addition to wound closure.
- **The wound was caused by an animal bite, a dirty or rusty object, or contains embedded material** that you cannot remove with irrigation.
- **You are not current on tetanus vaccination.** Tetanus boosters are recommended every ten years, or after five years for dirty or deep wounds. If you cannot remember your last tetanus shot, the wound is an opportunity to get one.

The time window for sutures is generally six to eight hours for most body locations, and up to twenty-four hours for facial wounds (which have excellent blood supply and resist infection). After the closure window passes, the wound may need to heal by secondary intention (from the bottom up, without closure), which is slower and leaves a larger scar but avoids the risk of trapping infection inside a closed wound.

Wound closure alternatives include Steri-Strips (adhesive strips that hold wound edges together), skin glue (cyanoacrylate — essentially medical superglue), and staples. Your healthcare provider will choose the appropriate method based on wound location, depth, and tension.

## Severe Bleeding — When Minutes Matter

Severe bleeding — hemorrhage — kills faster than almost any other traumatic injury. A person can bleed to death from a severed femoral artery in as little as three minutes. An amputated limb or a deep wound to the neck, armpit, or groin can produce blood loss so rapid that it overwhelms the body's ability to compensate before any ambulance could possibly arrive.

This is why the Stop the Bleed campaign exists. This is why the techniques in this section are taught to soldiers, to police officers, and increasingly to ordinary civilians. Because in the time it takes for the ambulance to arrive, the person with the bleeding wound is either going to have that bleeding controlled by someone on scene, or they are going to die.

### Direct Pressure

Direct pressure is the first and most effective intervention for severe bleeding. The technique is simple in description and difficult in execution, because it requires you to push hard on a wound that is producing a lot of blood, and to keep pushing even when your hands are slippery and the gauze is soaked and every instinct tells you to peek and see if it is working.

Here is how to do it:

1. Put on gloves if available.
2. Place gauze, a clean cloth, a shirt, a towel — whatever you have — directly over the wound.
3. Press down with the heel of your hand. Hard. You are trying to compress the bleeding vessel against the bone beneath it. If the wound is on a limb, you should be pressing hard enough that it would be uncomfortable for a conscious person.
4. Do not lift the pressure to check. When the first layer of gauze soaks through, add more on top. Do not remove the blood-soaked gauze — it is forming a clot, and removing it restarts the process.
5. Maintain pressure for at least fifteen minutes by the clock.

For most wounds, direct pressure will control bleeding. It is the standard of care, it works, and it requires no equipment beyond something to press with and the willingness to press hard.

### Wound Packing

For deep wounds — a stab wound, a gunshot wound, a wound cavity that a gauze pad cannot reach the bottom of — direct pressure on the surface is not enough. The bleeding vessel is deep in the wound, and surface pressure does not reach it.

This is where wound packing comes in. It is the technique that saves lives in the Stop the Bleed curriculum, and it is the technique that military medics have been using in combat for decades.

Wound packing means taking gauze and pushing it into the wound cavity — deep into it, all the way to the bottom — and then packing more on top until the entire cavity is filled with gauze. The gauze provides a matrix for clot formation and allows you to apply pressure directly to the source of bleeding, not just the surface.

The technique:

1. If using hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze or similar — these contain agents that accelerate clotting), use it. If not, plain gauze works.
2. Take the end of the gauze and push it into the wound with your finger. Push it to the deepest point of the wound.
3. Continue feeding gauze in, packing it tightly. Think of filling a pothole with gravel — you want the entire space filled, firmly, with no air gaps.
4. Once the wound is packed, apply direct pressure on top with your hand.
5. Hold pressure.

This is uncomfortable to do. Pushing gauze into a wound feels wrong. The person, if conscious, will likely scream. Do it anyway. A packed wound with pressure controls bleeding. An unpacked wound with surface-only pressure often does not.

### Tourniquet

The tourniquet is the last-resort tool for extremity bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure and wound packing. For decades, tourniquets were considered dangerous — to be used only when amputation was already inevitable. This was wrong, and the military experience in Iraq and Afghanistan proved it wrong. Properly applied tourniquets save lives with a very low rate of complications, even when left in place for several hours.

The standard civilian tourniquet is the CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) or the SOF-T (Special Operations Forces Tourniquet). Both are commercially available for $25-30 and should be in any serious trauma kit.

Application:

1. Place the tourniquet **high and tight** on the limb — as high up on the arm or leg as possible, ideally on the upper arm or upper thigh. Not over a joint.
2. Pull the strap tight and secure it.
3. Turn the windlass (the twist rod) until the bleeding stops. This will take multiple turns and it will be extremely painful for the person.
4. Secure the windlass so it cannot unwind.
5. **Write the time** on the tourniquet or on the person's forehead with a marker. "TQ" and the time applied. Paramedics and surgeons need to know how long the tourniquet has been on.
6. Do not remove the tourniquet. Do not loosen it. Once on, it stays on until a medical professional removes it in a setting where they can manage the consequences.

An improvised tourniquet — a belt, a strip of cloth, a strap — is better than nothing but significantly less effective than a purpose-built device. If you must improvise, use material at least one and a half inches wide (narrow cords and wires cause tissue damage without effectively occluding blood flow) and a rigid object (a stick, a pen, a tool handle) as a windlass.

## Burns — The Degree System

Burns are classified by depth, and the classification determines everything about treatment.

### First-Degree Burns

**What they look like:** Red, painful, dry. No blisters. Think sunburn.

**What is happening:** Only the epidermis (outermost skin layer) is damaged. The skin is intact and will heal completely in three to five days with no scarring.

**Treatment:** Cool running water for ten to twenty minutes (the sooner the better — cooling within twenty minutes of the burn significantly reduces tissue damage). Over-the-counter pain relief (ibuprofen or acetaminophen). Aloe vera or a moisturizing lotion. No bandage needed unless for comfort.

### Second-Degree Burns

**What they look like:** Red, very painful, wet or weeping. Blisters. The blisters may be intact or ruptured.

**What is happening:** Both the epidermis and part of the dermis (the deeper skin layer) are damaged. These burns are painful because nerve endings in the dermis are exposed but not destroyed.

**Treatment:** Cool running water for ten to twenty minutes. Do NOT pop blisters — they are sterile biological bandages that protect the wound bed. If blisters have already ruptured, clean gently with water and apply antibiotic ointment and a non-stick bandage. Cover with a loose bandage. Seek medical attention for second-degree burns larger than three inches across, or on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or over joints.

### Third-Degree Burns

**What they look like:** White, waxy, leathery, or charred. Stiff. Painless at the center (nerve endings are destroyed), though the edges where the burn transitions to second-degree will be extremely painful.

**What is happening:** Full-thickness destruction of the epidermis and dermis. The tissue is dead and will not regenerate. Third-degree burns require skin grafting.

**Treatment:** Call 911. Do not apply water, ointments, or anything else to the burn. Cover loosely with a clean, dry, non-stick dressing or sheet. Monitor for shock (pale, cool, clammy skin, rapid pulse, confusion). Treat for shock by laying the person flat and keeping them warm (burns impair temperature regulation). Do not remove burned clothing that is stuck to the skin.

### Burns That Require Emergency Room Evaluation

Regardless of degree, seek emergency medical care for burns that:

- Are on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or perineum
- Wrap around (circumferential) a limb, finger, or toe — swelling from circumferential burns can cut off circulation
- Cover more than ten percent of body surface area (for reference, the person's palm including fingers is roughly one percent of their body surface)
- Are third-degree, regardless of size
- Are caused by chemicals, electricity, or inhalation (smoke or steam)
- Occur in children, elderly persons, or people with chronic medical conditions

### What NOT to Put on Burns

- **NOT ice.** Ice causes vasoconstriction and can cause frostbite on already-damaged tissue, converting a second-degree burn to third-degree.
- **NOT butter, oil, or grease.** These trap heat in the tissue, extending the burn.
- **NOT toothpaste.** A persistent folk remedy with no evidence of benefit and a meaningful risk of infection from the non-sterile chemicals.
- **NOT egg whites, honey, or any other kitchen remedy** unless you are following a specific protocol under medical supervision. (Manuka honey is used in some wound care protocols, but this is a medical-grade product applied in clinical settings, not something you scoop from a jar.)

Cool running water. That is the treatment. For ten to twenty minutes. It is simple, it is free, and it is backed by robust clinical evidence.

## Nosebleeds

Nosebleeds are common and rarely dangerous, but they are frequently managed incorrectly because the instinctive head position is exactly wrong.

**Lean FORWARD, not back.** Tilting the head back does not stop the bleeding — it redirects the blood down the throat, which causes nausea, vomiting, and aspiration risk, and makes it impossible to tell whether the bleeding has actually stopped.

The correct technique:

1. Sit down and lean slightly forward.
2. Pinch the soft part of the nose (below the bony bridge) firmly between your thumb and index finger.
3. Hold the pinch for a full ten minutes by the clock. Do not release to check. Ten minutes.
4. Breathe through your mouth.
5. After ten minutes, release. If bleeding has stopped, avoid blowing your nose, bending over, or straining for several hours.
6. If bleeding has not stopped after twenty minutes of continuous pinching, seek medical attention. Persistent nosebleeds may require cauterization or packing by a medical professional.

Nosebleeds that are caused by trauma to the face (a punch, a fall, a sports impact) should be evaluated by a doctor, especially if the nose is deformed or the person has clear fluid draining from the nose (possible cerebrospinal fluid leak indicating a skull base fracture — this is a medical emergency).

## Eye Injuries

The eye is one of the most delicate and exposed organs in the body, and eye injuries range from trivial to vision-threatening.

**Chemical splash:** Flush the eye immediately with clean water for at least fifteen to twenty minutes. Hold the eyelid open and let water run from the inside corner (near the nose) outward, so contaminated water flows away from the other eye. Remove contact lenses if present. Seek emergency medical care — bring the chemical container or label if possible.

**Foreign object (dust, metal shaving, wood chip):** If the object is on the surface of the white of the eye or inner eyelid, try flushing with water first. If that does not dislodge it, you can try to gently remove it with the corner of a clean, damp cloth. Never use dry cotton (fibers will stick to the eye).

**Embedded object (something stuck in the eye):** Do NOT attempt to remove it. Do not press on the eye. Do not rub. Stabilize the object if possible (a paper cup taped over the eye can protect a protruding object). Cover both eyes (yes, both — if one eye moves, the other moves with it, and movement of the injured eye can worsen the injury). Seek emergency medical care immediately.

**Blunt trauma (punched, hit by a ball, airbag):** Apply a cold compress gently — do not press. Watch for changes in vision, blood in the white of the eye (subconjunctival hemorrhage — usually harmless but alarming), or persistent pain. Seek evaluation if vision changes or pain does not improve.

For workshop environments — and this connects directly to the WDC research project — safety glasses should be worn as a matter of routine. A wood chip from a lathe, a metal fragment from a grinder, or a broken drill bit moves fast enough to penetrate the eye before you can blink. Safety glasses are a dollar. An eye is irreplaceable.

## Signs of Infection — When to See a Doctor

Every wound, no matter how well managed initially, carries a risk of infection. The risk increases with wound contamination (dirt, debris, bite wounds), wound depth, the presence of foreign material, and compromised immune function.

Watch for these signs in the days following a wound:

- **Increasing redness** spreading outward from the wound edges
- **Warmth** around the wound that was not present initially
- **Swelling** that is getting worse rather than better
- **Pus** — yellow, green, or cloudy drainage (clear or slightly pink drainage is normal in the first day or two)
- **Red streaks** extending from the wound toward the heart (this indicates the infection is spreading into the lymphatic system — seek care immediately)
- **Fever** — any temperature over 100.4F (38C) in the context of a wound should be evaluated
- **Increased pain** rather than decreasing pain over time

An infected wound needs medical evaluation and often antibiotics. Do not try to "wait it out" with a wound that shows signs of infection. Cellulitis (spreading skin infection) can progress rapidly, and in worst cases, untreated wound infections can lead to sepsis, which kills approximately 270,000 people per year in the United States.

## College Connection

This document connects to two departments within the College structure:

**Science** — Wound healing biology. The four phases of wound healing (hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, remodeling) are a cascade of cellular events that take weeks to months to complete. Understanding why wounds heal — clot formation, white blood cell migration, fibroblast proliferation, collagen deposition, angiogenesis — explains why interventions like keeping wounds moist (promotes cell migration), avoiding hydrogen peroxide (damages fibroblasts), and watching for infection (inflammation gone wrong) actually matter. The science is not abstract; it directly informs the practical decisions.

**Learning** — Procedural skills. Wound packing, tourniquet application, and burn management are hands-on skills that require practice. The Stop the Bleed course, available free in many communities, provides the hands-on training that turns this document from information into capability. Reading about wound packing is not the same as packing a simulated wound with your fingers while an instructor watches. The procedural memory formed during training is what you will access under stress.

## The Reality of Blood

Blood is frightening because it represents something going wrong with the body in a visible, visceral way. But blood is also a manageable problem. The techniques in this document — direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquets, burn cooling, nosebleed management — are simple to learn, require minimal equipment, and are effective.

The Stop the Bleed campaign estimates that 20 percent of people who die from traumatic injury could have survived if bleeding had been controlled before the ambulance arrived. One in five. These are people who bled to death in the presence of bystanders who did not know how to help.

You now know how to help. The rest is practice, preparation, and the willingness to push hard when it counts.
