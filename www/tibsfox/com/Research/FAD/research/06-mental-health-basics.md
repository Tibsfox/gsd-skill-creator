# The Invisible Emergency — Mental Health First Aid

## The Companion Document

This document is the civilian companion to FSR Document 08 ("The Gap — Mental Health Crisis Response"). Where FSR-08 examines mental health crisis from the perspective of the first responder who is dispatched to the scene — the officer, the paramedic, the firefighter sent because the system has no one else to send — this document examines it from the other side. From the perspective of the friend, the family member, the coworker, the stranger at the bus stop who notices something is wrong.

The gap that FSR-08 describes — the canyon between what people in mental health crisis need and what the emergency response system is built to provide — does not exist only at the institutional level. It exists at the personal level, in the space between noticing that someone is struggling and knowing what to do about it. Most people recognize that a person clutching their chest needs CPR. Far fewer recognize that a person withdrawing from their life, giving away their possessions, and expressing hopelessness needs intervention just as urgently.

Mental health emergencies kill people. Suicide is the second leading cause of death for Americans aged 10 to 34, and the twelfth leading cause of death overall. Over 49,000 people died by suicide in the United States in 2022. Each of those deaths was preceded by a crisis period during which intervention was possible, and in many cases, someone nearby noticed something was wrong but did not know what to do, or was afraid that doing something would make it worse, or assumed that someone else — someone more qualified — would step in.

This document is for the person who notices.

## Recognizing Crisis — What It Looks Like From the Outside

A mental health crisis does not always look like what you expect. The dramatic portrayal — screaming, raving, obviously disordered behavior in a public space — represents one end of a spectrum. Much of mental health crisis is quiet. It looks like withdrawal. It looks like someone who used to laugh but does not anymore. It looks like absences from work, declining hygiene, weight loss or gain that does not track to any explained change in diet.

Some patterns that may indicate a person is in crisis or approaching one:

**Withdrawal.** The person pulls away from friends, family, activities, and routines they previously valued. They stop answering texts, decline invitations, miss events. Isolation is both a symptom of and a risk factor for worsening mental health. The person may frame it as "needing space" or being "busy," and sometimes that is true. But sustained withdrawal, especially when accompanied by other signs, warrants attention and gentle inquiry.

**Agitation or irritability.** A person whose baseline temperament is calm but who has become snappish, easily frustrated, or disproportionately angry may be struggling. Irritability is an underrecognized symptom of depression, particularly in men. The cultural script for male depression is not sadness — it is anger, recklessness, substance use, and withdrawal.

**Paranoia or hypervigilance.** A person who has become suspicious, who sees threats that others do not perceive, who is convinced that people are watching them, plotting against them, or controlling their environment. This may indicate psychotic features of depression, schizophrenia spectrum conditions, severe anxiety, or substance-induced paranoia. The content of the paranoia is not the point — the change in the person's perception of reality is.

**Self-harm talk or behavior.** Statements like "everyone would be better off without me," "I can't do this anymore," "what's the point," or "I won't be around much longer" are not attention-seeking. They are warning signs. Direct self-harm — cutting, burning, head-banging — is a coping mechanism for emotional pain that indicates the person's internal resources are overwhelmed. Self-harm and suicidal ideation are related but distinct — not everyone who self-harms is suicidal, but self-harm does increase suicide risk.

**Sudden calm after prolonged distress.** This is the pattern that experienced clinicians watch for and that laypeople almost always miss. A person who has been depressed, agitated, or in crisis for weeks suddenly becomes calm, peaceful, and seems "better." They may give away possessions, settle accounts, contact people they have not spoken to in years, or express unusual serenity. This sudden calm can indicate that the person has made a decision to end their life and feels relief that the struggle is almost over. It is one of the most dangerous and most misread patterns in suicide risk.

**Increased substance use.** Alcohol, drugs, or medication misuse that is escalating in frequency or quantity. Self-medication is an attempt to manage unbearable emotional states. The substance use creates its own problems (impaired judgment, increased impulsivity, physiological depression of mood), creating a downward spiral.

**Psychomotor changes.** Either significant slowing (moving slowly, speaking slowly, taking a long time to respond to questions) or significant agitation (pacing, inability to sit still, restlessness). These are clinical features of major depression and other conditions.

None of these patterns in isolation necessarily indicates a crisis. People have bad weeks. People withdraw when they are tired. People get irritable when they are stressed. The signal is in the combination, the duration, and the change from baseline. You are not diagnosing. You are noticing.

## Suicide Intervention — The Conversation That Saves Lives

### Ask Directly

If you are concerned that someone may be thinking about suicide, ask them. Directly. Clearly. Without euphemism.

"Are you thinking about killing yourself?"

Not "you're not thinking about doing anything stupid, are you?" Not "you don't want to hurt yourself, do you?" These hedge-the-question phrasings invite a denial. A direct question invites a direct answer.

The fear that asking about suicide will "plant the idea" — that a person who was not suicidal will become suicidal because you mentioned it — is one of the most harmful myths in mental health. It has been studied extensively, in multiple populations, across multiple decades of research. The evidence is clear: asking about suicide does not increase suicidal ideation or behavior. What it does is open a door. It communicates that you see their pain, that you are not afraid of it, and that they can talk about it with you.

Many people who are suicidal have been waiting — desperately — for someone to ask. The question is not an intrusion. It is a lifeline.

### Listen Without Judgment

If the person says yes — if they disclose that they are having suicidal thoughts — the most important thing you do next is listen. Not fix. Not argue. Not minimize. Not rush to solutions. Listen.

"Tell me about that."
"How long have you been feeling this way?"
"What's been going on?"

Reflect what they say. "It sounds like you've been carrying this for a long time." Validate their pain. "I can hear how much you're hurting." Do not say "it's not that bad" or "other people have it worse" or "you have so much to live for." These statements, however well-intentioned, communicate that you are not hearing them. The person needs to feel heard before they can begin to consider alternatives.

Do not make promises you cannot keep. "I promise everything will be okay" is a promise you do not have the power to make. "I'm here and I'm not going anywhere right now" is a promise you can make and keep.

### Be There

Physical presence matters. A person in suicidal crisis is frequently alone — emotionally if not physically. Staying with them, being in the room, being on the phone, being present reduces the immediate risk. Suicidal crises are often time-limited — the most acute phase of a suicidal urge may pass within minutes to hours if the person can be kept safe during that window.

Remove means if possible. If there are firearms in the home, ask if they can be temporarily stored elsewhere (with a friend, at a gun shop, in a gun safe that someone else has the combination to). If there are medications that could be used for overdose, secure them. Reducing access to lethal means during a crisis period is one of the most evidence-based suicide prevention strategies available.

### Help Connect

The person in crisis needs professional help. Your role is not to be their therapist — it is to be the bridge between their crisis and professional care. Help them connect to resources:

**988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline.** Call or text 988. Available 24/7, staffed by trained crisis counselors. This replaced the old 1-800-273-8255 number in 2022, though the old number still routes to 988. The 988 system is expanding and is intended to become the mental health equivalent of 911 — the number you call when the emergency is psychological rather than physical. FSR-08 discusses the infrastructure challenges and funding gaps in the 988 system.

**Crisis Text Line.** Text HOME to 741741. For people who are not able or willing to make a phone call — younger individuals, people in environments where a phone call would be overheard, people who communicate more comfortably in writing.

**Local crisis services.** Most counties have a crisis line and mobile crisis teams that can respond in person. In King County, Washington, the crisis line is 866-427-4747. In Snohomish County, it is 800-584-3578. These numbers should be in your phone before you need them.

**911.** If the person is in immediate danger — they have a weapon, they have taken pills, they are at a location from which they could jump — call 911. This will bring a law enforcement response in most jurisdictions, which is the gap that FSR-08 describes in detail. It is not ideal, but when the immediate risk is death, the priority is preventing death, and the systemic reform work continues on a different timeline.

## Panic Attacks — Not Dangerous, Absolutely Terrifying

A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear and physical symptoms that peaks within minutes. The person experiencing it may be convinced they are having a heart attack, losing their mind, or dying. They are not — panic attacks are not medically dangerous — but the experience is so physically overwhelming that this reassurance can be difficult to believe in the moment.

Symptoms of a panic attack: racing heart, pounding chest, shortness of breath (often hyperventilation), chest tightness or pain, dizziness, tingling or numbness in hands and face, sweating, trembling, nausea, a feeling of unreality (derealization) or detachment from oneself (depersonalization), and an overwhelming sense that something catastrophic is about to happen.

### How to Help

**Stay calm yourself.** Your calmness is contagious. If you are agitated, the person's agitation will escalate.

**Use a calm, slow voice.** Speak slowly, evenly, and at a low pitch. Not patronizing — steady.

**Use their name.** "Sarah, I'm right here. This is a panic attack. It feels terrible but it's not dangerous and it will pass."

**Encourage slow breathing.** "Try to breathe with me. In through your nose for four counts. Hold for four. Out through your mouth for four. In... two... three... four..." Box breathing (4-4-4-4) is the standard tactical breathing technique used by military, law enforcement, and emergency services personnel to manage physiological stress responses, and it works for panic attacks too.

**Validate their experience.** "I know this feels terrifying. That's real. And it will pass." Do NOT say "just calm down," "there's nothing to be afraid of," or "you're being ridiculous." The person is experiencing real physiological symptoms — their body is in fight-or-flight mode. Telling them to "just calm down" is like telling a person having an asthma attack to "just breathe."

**Ground them in the present.** Grounding techniques help pull the person out of the spiral. The 5-4-3-2-1 technique: "Tell me five things you can see. Four things you can touch. Three things you can hear. Two things you can smell. One thing you can taste." This forces the brain to engage with sensory input from the present environment rather than the internal alarm loop.

**Do not restrain or confine.** A person in a panic attack may need to move — pace, shift position, go outside. Let them. Feeling trapped often worsens panic.

Most panic attacks resolve within ten to twenty minutes. If the person has never had a panic attack before, or if their symptoms are different from their usual pattern, encourage medical evaluation to rule out cardiac or other causes.

## De-Escalation — When Someone Is Agitated

Sometimes the person in crisis is not sad or anxious — they are angry. Agitated. Possibly threatening. De-escalation is the art of reducing the emotional temperature of an encounter through communication rather than force.

De-escalation is what FSR-08 describes as the skill that first responders are often not adequately trained in. It is equally important for civilians who may encounter agitated individuals in workplaces, public spaces, or family settings.

**Speak slowly.** Fast speech conveys urgency, and urgency escalates agitation. Slow your speech rate to about half your normal pace.

**Use their name.** Names are humanizing. A person who is agitated and dehumanizing you (seeing you as a threat, an authority figure, an obstacle) is harder to maintain that narrative against when you call them by name — and when they are using yours.

**Lower your voice.** Not to a whisper, but to a calm, even tone. Raised voices trigger escalation. Speaking quietly often causes the other person to lower their volume to match.

**Give choices, not commands.** "Would you like to sit down or step outside?" instead of "Sit down." "Can I get you some water or would you prefer to take a walk?" instead of "You need to calm down." Choices preserve the person's sense of agency, which is often what is being threatened in the situation that caused the agitation.

**Maintain an exit path — for both of you.** Never position yourself between an agitated person and the door. If they feel trapped, the situation will escalate. If you feel trapped, you have lost a critical safety option. Keep furniture or distance between you, stand at an angle rather than squarely facing them (a bladed stance is less confrontational), and keep your hands visible and relaxed.

**Acknowledge their feelings.** "I can see you're really upset." "It sounds like you've been treated unfairly and that's making you angry." Acknowledgment is not agreement — you are not validating the threat, you are validating the emotion beneath it.

**Set limits calmly.** "I want to help you, and I need you to lower your voice so we can talk." "I hear you, and I'm not able to continue this conversation if you're threatening me."

**Know when to disengage.** If the person is escalating despite your efforts, if they produce a weapon, if they become physically aggressive, or if you feel unsafe — leave. Remove yourself from the situation and call 911. You are not a hostage negotiator. Your safety is not negotiable.

## Mental Health First Aid Certification

Mental Health First Aid (MHFA) is a standardized, evidence-based training program that teaches participants to recognize the signs of mental health and substance use challenges and to provide initial help. It is the mental health equivalent of physical first aid — not treatment, but initial response.

The course is eight hours (typically one day) and teaches the ALGEE action plan:

**A — Assess** for risk of suicide or harm.
**L — Listen** nonjudgmentally.
**G — Give** reassurance and information.
**E — Encourage** appropriate professional help.
**E — Encourage** self-help and other support strategies.

The course covers depression, anxiety, trauma, psychosis, and substance use. It includes practice scenarios and role-playing. It is available through community organizations, employers, faith communities, and public health agencies. Many employers now offer it as workplace training.

Mental Health First Aid does not make you a therapist. It makes you a more competent bystander — someone who can recognize a crisis, respond appropriately, and help connect a person in need with professional resources. In the same way that a CPR certification does not make you a cardiologist but makes you the difference between life and death when someone's heart stops, MHFA certification makes you the difference between connection and isolation when someone's mental health collapses.

## Caring for the Caregiver — When Helping Hurts You

There is a cost to caring. If you are regularly supporting someone in mental health crisis — a family member with chronic mental illness, a friend with recurring suicidal ideation, a coworker who relies on you as their sole emotional support — you are at risk for compassion fatigue and vicarious trauma.

**Compassion fatigue** is the gradual erosion of your capacity for empathy through sustained emotional expenditure. You feel drained, numb, irritable, or resentful. You find yourself avoiding the person you are supposed to be helping, or going through the motions without emotional engagement. You may feel guilty about your own feelings — "I should want to help, what's wrong with me?"

**Vicarious trauma** (also called secondary traumatic stress) is the traumatic stress response that develops from absorbing the emotional content of someone else's traumatic experiences. You may develop intrusive thoughts, nightmares, hypervigilance, or emotional numbness related to the other person's experiences, even though you did not directly experience the events yourself.

Both are occupational hazards for first responders, therapists, nurses, social workers, and other helping professionals (FSR-09 covers this in the context of responder wellness). But they also affect informal caregivers — the spouses, parents, friends, and coworkers who carry someone else's pain because they care and because no one has told them that this carrying has a cost.

Signs that caregiving is affecting your own mental health:

- Emotional exhaustion that does not resolve with rest
- Dreading contact with the person you are caring for
- Feeling guilty about setting boundaries
- Neglecting your own health, relationships, or responsibilities
- Difficulty sleeping, changes in appetite, increased substance use
- Feeling like you are responsible for the person's survival
- Hopelessness that mirrors the person's hopelessness

What to do:

**Set boundaries.** You can care about someone without being available to them twenty-four hours a day. "I love you and I can't be your only support. Let's find additional help together." This is not abandonment. This is sustainability.

**Get your own support.** Therapy is not just for people in crisis. A therapist, a support group for caregivers (NAMI — the National Alliance on Mental Illness — offers family support groups), or even a trusted friend who is not involved in the caregiving situation can provide space for you to process your own feelings.

**Maintain your own life.** Exercise, social connections, hobbies, sleep, nutrition — the basics of wellness (FAD-07) are not optional extras for caregivers. They are the foundation that makes caregiving possible without self-destruction.

**Accept that you cannot fix someone else.** This is the hardest lesson. You can be present, you can provide resources, you can listen, you can love them. You cannot make them choose to get help, take their medication, stop using substances, or want to live. That agency belongs to them, and accepting that you do not have control over another person's choices is both painful and essential.

## The Connection to FSR

Every scenario described in this document — the suicidal friend, the agitated stranger, the person having a panic attack — is a scenario that first responders face every day, often multiple times per shift, without adequate training, without adequate support, and without a system designed for the task. FSR-08 describes this gap from the institutional side: the wrong tools, the wrong training, the wrong system. This document describes it from the civilian side: the right instinct, the need for knowledge, the desire to help.

The two perspectives converge on the same conclusion: we need better infrastructure for mental health crisis response, AND we need better-prepared individuals at every level of the response chain — from the friend who notices something is wrong, to the coworker who asks the hard question, to the 988 counselor on the phone, to the mobile crisis team that responds in person, to the first responder who arrives when the system has no one else to send.

Mental health first aid is not a substitute for professional care. It is the bridge to professional care. It is the sixty seconds between "something is wrong" and "help is on the way." It is the conversation that opens the door.

Have the conversation.

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Psychology** — Crisis intervention theory. The psychological models of suicide (Joiner's interpersonal theory — thwarted belonging, perceived burdensomeness, and acquired capability), the stages of crisis (pre-crisis, acute crisis, resolution or escalation), and the evidence base for lethal means restriction. Understanding why people reach crisis points informs how we respond to them.

**Mind-Body** — Emotional regulation. The neurophysiology of the stress response (amygdala activation, cortisol and adrenaline release, prefrontal cortex suppression) explains why a person in crisis cannot "just think rationally." It also explains why de-escalation techniques work — they target the same physiological systems through different inputs (calm voice, slow breathing, grounding in sensory experience).

**Science** — The neuroscience of stress. Chronic stress physically remodels the brain — shrinking the hippocampus (memory and emotional regulation), enlarging the amygdala (threat detection), and weakening prefrontal connections (executive function and impulse control). This is not metaphor. It is measurable on brain imaging. Understanding the physical reality of mental health conditions — that depression is not weakness, that anxiety is not cowardice, that psychosis is not moral failure — is the foundation of destigmatization, and destigmatization is the foundation of help-seeking.
