# An Ounce of Prevention — Staying Healthy

## The Math of Prevention

Benjamin Franklin's "an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure" was, characteristically, an understatement. In modern healthcare economics, the ratio is more dramatic. A flu vaccine costs $40. A hospitalization for influenza complications averages $15,000 to $30,000 in the United States. A screening colonoscopy costs $2,000 to $3,000 out of pocket (free with most insurance as a preventive benefit). Treatment for colorectal cancer averages $150,000 to $300,000 and may not succeed. A dental cleaning costs $100 to $200 twice a year. A root canal costs $700 to $1,500, and a dental implant to replace a tooth lost to decay runs $3,000 to $5,000.

The math is unambiguous. Prevention is cheaper, less painful, more effective, and more dignified than treatment. And yet Americans are, by and large, terrible at it. We skip screenings, postpone vaccinations, ignore warning signs, and wait until the acute crisis — the heart attack, the cancer diagnosis, the tooth that cracks — before engaging with the healthcare system.

The reasons are structural (cost, access, time, a healthcare system built around acute treatment rather than prevention), cultural (the American ethos of toughness and self-reliance that frames medical care as something you seek only when you "really" need it), and psychological (the optimism bias that convinces each of us that the bad thing will happen to someone else).

This document is an argument for prevention, a practical guide to what that looks like across a lifespan, and an invitation to treat your body's maintenance schedule with at least the seriousness you give to your car's oil change interval.

## Vaccinations — Training Your Immune System

A vaccine works by presenting your immune system with a harmless version of a pathogen — a weakened virus, an inactivated virus, a piece of viral protein, or the genetic instructions for making that protein — so that your immune system can learn to recognize it. Think of it as showing your body a mugshot of a criminal before the criminal shows up. When the actual pathogen arrives, your immune system already knows what it looks like, and it can mount a rapid, targeted response instead of scrambling to identify an unknown threat.

The development of vaccines is one of the greatest achievements in the history of medicine. Smallpox killed an estimated 300 million people in the twentieth century before vaccination eradicated it from the planet in 1980. Polio paralyzed tens of thousands of children annually in the United States before the Salk and Sabin vaccines reduced cases to near zero. Measles, which can cause encephalitis, pneumonia, and death, was declared eliminated from the United States in 2000 thanks to the MMR vaccine — though it has resurged in areas with declining vaccination rates.

### The Adult Vaccination Schedule

Many adults believe vaccines are a childhood thing and that they are "done" with vaccinations. This is incorrect. Several vaccines require boosters throughout adulthood, and new vaccines have been developed for conditions that primarily affect adults.

**Influenza (flu) — annually.** The flu vaccine is reformulated each year to match the circulating strains predicted by global surveillance. It reduces the risk of flu illness by 40-60% in years when the match is good, and it significantly reduces the risk of severe illness, hospitalization, and death even in years when the match is imperfect. Everyone over six months of age should receive a flu vaccine annually. The ideal timing is September or October, before flu season peaks.

**Tdap/Td — every 10 years.** Tetanus, diphtheria, and pertussis (whooping cough) protection wanes over time. Adults should receive a Td (tetanus and diphtheria) booster every ten years, with at least one dose being the Tdap formulation (which includes pertussis) if they have not previously received it as an adult. Anyone in close contact with infants should ensure their pertussis vaccination is current — whooping cough is mild in adults but can be fatal in newborns.

**Shingles (Shingrix) — age 50+.** Shingles is a reactivation of the chickenpox virus (varicella-zoster) that has been dormant in your nerve tissue since childhood. It produces a painful, blistering rash along a nerve pathway and can cause postherpetic neuralgia — chronic pain lasting months or years. Shingrix is a two-dose vaccine that is over 90% effective at preventing shingles and is recommended for all adults over 50, regardless of whether they remember having chickenpox (most adults over 50 carry the virus).

**COVID-19 — per current CDC guidance.** The COVID vaccination landscape evolves as the virus mutates and new formulations are developed. The current recommendation is an updated annual vaccine, similar to the flu shot model. Check the CDC website or consult your healthcare provider for the most current guidance.

**Pneumococcal (PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23) — age 65+ or with specific risk factors.** Pneumococcal disease causes pneumonia, meningitis, and bloodstream infections, particularly in older adults and those with chronic conditions.

**HPV (Gardasil 9) — through age 26, and up to 45 based on clinical judgment.** Human papillomavirus causes cervical, throat, anal, and penile cancers. The vaccine is most effective when given before exposure (ideally ages 11-12), but catch-up vaccination is recommended through age 26 and available through 45.

**Hepatitis B — if not previously vaccinated.** A three-dose series (or a newer two-dose series) that prevents a virus that causes chronic liver disease and liver cancer.

## Screenings by Age — Finding Problems Early

Screening means looking for disease before symptoms appear. The value of screening depends on the disease — it must be common enough, serious enough, and treatable enough at the early stage to justify the cost and inconvenience of looking for it. The following screenings have strong evidence supporting their use.

### Blood Pressure — Annually from Age 18

Hypertension (high blood pressure) has no symptoms in most people until it causes a stroke, heart attack, kidney failure, or other catastrophic event. It is called the "silent killer" for this reason, and it is the single most common modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease. Normal is below 120/80. Elevated is 120-129/less than 80. Stage 1 hypertension is 130-139/80-89. Stage 2 is 140+/90+.

Blood pressure screening takes two minutes at any pharmacy, clinic, or with a home monitor ($30-50 for a reliable automatic cuff). There is no reason to not know your blood pressure.

### Cholesterol (Lipid Panel) — Every 4-6 Years from Age 20

A lipid panel measures total cholesterol, LDL (the "bad" cholesterol that deposits in artery walls), HDL (the "good" cholesterol that helps remove deposits), and triglycerides. Elevated LDL and low HDL are risk factors for atherosclerosis and cardiovascular disease. A simple blood draw, typically fasting. More frequent screening if results are abnormal or if you have other cardiovascular risk factors (family history, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, obesity).

### Colorectal Cancer Screening — Starting at Age 45

Colorectal cancer is the third most common cancer in the United States and the second leading cause of cancer death. It is also one of the most preventable through screening, because it typically develops from polyps that can be identified and removed before they become cancerous.

Options include colonoscopy (every 10 years if normal), stool DNA test (Cologuard, every 3 years), fecal immunochemical test (FIT, annually), and others. Colonoscopy is considered the gold standard because it is both diagnostic (it finds the polyps) and therapeutic (it removes them in the same procedure). The preparation is unpleasant (the bowel-clearing liquid is the part everyone dreads), and the procedure itself is done under sedation and is typically painless. The screening age was lowered from 50 to 45 in 2021 due to rising rates of colorectal cancer in younger adults.

### Mammogram — Starting at Age 40

Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women (excluding skin cancer). Mammography detects tumors that are too small to feel on physical examination, enabling earlier treatment when outcomes are best. Current USPSTF guidance recommends biennial screening mammography for women aged 40 to 74. Some organizations (American Cancer Society, American College of Radiology) recommend annual screening starting at 40. Discuss with your healthcare provider based on your personal and family risk.

### Cervical Cancer Screening (Pap Smear/HPV Test) — Starting at Age 21

The Pap smear detects precancerous changes in cervical cells, and HPV testing identifies the virus that causes most cervical cancers. Screening has reduced cervical cancer mortality dramatically since its introduction. Current guidelines: Pap smear every 3 years for ages 21-29, and Pap smear with HPV co-testing every 5 years (or Pap alone every 3 years) for ages 30-65.

### Skin Examination — Annually if Outdoor Exposure

Skin cancer is the most common cancer in the United States. Most skin cancers are highly curable when caught early. An annual full-body skin examination by a dermatologist is recommended for individuals with significant sun or outdoor exposure, a history of sunburns, fair skin, a family history of skin cancer, or numerous moles. Monthly self-examination for new or changing moles (the ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving) catches changes between annual visits.

### Dental Examination — Every 6 Months

Twice-yearly dental exams and cleanings are not vanity care. They are preventive health. Dental disease affects overall health: periodontitis (gum disease) is linked to increased cardiovascular risk, diabetes complications, respiratory disease, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. The oral-systemic connection is well established and growing.

### Vision Examination — Every 2 Years (Annually After 60)

Comprehensive eye exams screen not only for refractive errors (nearsightedness, farsightedness, astigmatism) but also for glaucoma, macular degeneration, cataracts, and diabetic retinopathy. Glaucoma, in particular, causes irreversible vision loss and has no symptoms in early stages — it can only be detected by examination.

## Dental Health — The Unappreciated Essential

Dental health deserves its own section because it is simultaneously one of the most impactful and most neglected areas of preventive health.

**Floss daily.** This is not optional. This is not a suggestion your dentist makes because they enjoy nagging you. The spaces between your teeth harbor bacteria that your toothbrush cannot reach. These bacteria form biofilm (plaque) that mineralizes into tarite (calculus) if not disrupted daily. The bacteria produce acids that erode enamel (cavities) and toxins that inflame the gums (gingivitis, progressing to periodontitis). Periodontitis destroys the bone that holds your teeth in place and is the leading cause of tooth loss in adults.

**The bleeding gums myth.** Many people stop flossing because their gums bleed when they do it. This is exactly backwards. The gums bleed BECAUSE you have not been flossing. The bleeding is a sign of inflammation (gingivitis) caused by bacterial accumulation. If you start flossing daily after a period of not flossing, your gums will bleed for a week or two and then stop as the inflammation resolves. The bleeding is the reason to floss more, not less.

**Brush twice a day.** Fluoride toothpaste, two minutes, covering all surfaces. Electric toothbrushes are more effective than manual brushes for most people (the timer helps, and the oscillating action is better at disrupting plaque). Replace the brush head every three months.

## Hearing Protection — The Workshop Connection

This section connects directly to the WDC (Woodcraft) research project. Noise-induced hearing loss is permanent, irreversible, and entirely preventable.

The threshold for noise-induced damage is 85 decibels (dB) over prolonged exposure. For context:

- Normal conversation: 60-70 dB
- Table saw: 95-105 dB
- Planer: 95-105 dB
- Router: 95-110 dB
- Chainsaw: 105-120 dB
- Hammer drill: 100-110 dB
- Miter saw: 100-110 dB

At 100 dB, damage can occur in as little as fifteen minutes. At 110 dB, in as little as two minutes. Workshop tools routinely exceed these levels.

Hearing protection should be worn **every single time** you operate power tools. Options include foam earplugs (NRR 25-33, cheap, disposable, effective), over-ear earmuffs (NRR 22-30, more comfortable for extended use), and electronic earmuffs (NRR 22-25, amplify normal sounds while blocking impulse noise — ideal for workshops where you need to hear conversation but not tool noise).

The insidious nature of noise-induced hearing loss is that it is gradual. You do not notice the damage until it has accumulated to the point of functional impairment — difficulty understanding speech in noisy environments, tinnitus (ringing in the ears), needing to increase the TV volume. By then, the damage is done and cannot be undone. Hearing aids can compensate but do not restore the lost acuity.

Wearing hearing protection is like wearing a seatbelt. It is mildly inconvenient, you will probably be fine without it on any single occasion, and the one time you need it and do not have it, the consequences are permanent.

## Ergonomics — Preventing the Slow Injuries

Many injuries are not sudden. They accumulate over months and years of sustained poor positioning, repetitive motion, and unaddressed strain. Ergonomics is the science of fitting the work environment to the worker.

### The Desk Worker

The modern office worker spends an average of six to eight hours per day seated at a desk, often with poor posture, a poorly adjusted monitor, and inadequate breaks. The result: neck pain, back pain, shoulder tension, carpal tunnel syndrome, eye strain, and a gradually deteriorating posture that becomes its own source of chronic pain.

Setup: Monitor at eye level (the top of the screen should be at or slightly below eye level), at arm's length distance. Feet flat on the floor or on a footrest. Thighs parallel to the floor. Elbows at approximately 90 degrees with forearms parallel to the desk. Chair supporting the lumbar curve. This does not require expensive equipment — a stack of books under a monitor and a rolled towel behind the lower back can make a significant difference.

**The 20-20-20 rule for eyes.** Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This relaxes the ciliary muscles that focus the lens for near work and reduces eye strain. Set a timer if you need to — most people underestimate how long they go without looking up.

**Movement breaks.** Stand up and move for at least one to two minutes every thirty to sixty minutes. Walk to the water fountain. Stretch. Do a lap around the office. Prolonged sitting is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease and all-cause mortality, even in people who exercise regularly. The body is not designed to be static for hours at a time.

### The Crafter and Maker

The fursuit makers, cosplay builders, and detail crafters in the FFA (Fursuit and Fabric Arts) research project community spend hours in sustained positions that place significant strain on the neck, back, shoulders, and hands. Sewing machine work, hand-stitching, sculpting, painting, and hot-glue construction all involve forward head posture, rounded shoulders, and repetitive fine motor movements.

Recommendations: frequent breaks (every thirty to forty-five minutes), stretching of the neck (gentle lateral flexion, chin tucks), shoulders (doorway stretches, shoulder rolls), and hands (finger extensions, wrist circles). Adequate lighting that does not require you to lean forward to see. A work surface at the correct height. And the willingness to stop when you feel strain rather than pushing through to finish a piece.

### The Standing Worker

Standing all day is not inherently better than sitting all day. Prolonged standing causes its own problems: varicose veins, lower back pain, foot pain, and fatigue. The optimal pattern is alternation — sit some, stand some, move some. Standing desks with the option to alternate between sitting and standing address this. Anti-fatigue mats, supportive footwear, and regular shifting of weight help for occupations that require sustained standing.

## When to See a Doctor — The "Probably Nothing" That's Something

The human tendency to minimize symptoms — "it's probably nothing," "I'll see if it goes away," "I don't want to bother the doctor" — kills people. Not frequently, but predictably, and almost always in situations where earlier evaluation would have changed the outcome.

See a doctor if you notice:

**Unexplained weight loss.** Losing more than 5% of your body weight over six to twelve months without trying is a red flag. It can indicate thyroid dysfunction, diabetes, cancer, depression, or other conditions that warrant evaluation.

**Persistent pain.** Pain that does not resolve with rest and over-the-counter remedies within two to four weeks, or pain that is getting worse rather than better, should be evaluated. Pain is a signal. Persistent or worsening pain is a signal that something is wrong beyond what self-care can address.

**Changes in moles.** Any mole that changes in size, shape, color, texture, or starts bleeding should be evaluated by a dermatologist. The ABCDE criteria apply: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter greater than 6mm, Evolving. Melanoma caught early is almost always curable. Melanoma caught late can be fatal.

**Blood where it should not be.** Blood in the stool (red or black/tarry), blood in the urine, coughing up blood, or vomiting blood. Any of these requires evaluation. Many causes are benign (hemorrhoids, urinary tract infection), but some are not, and you cannot tell the difference without a doctor.

**Persistent changes in bowel or bladder habits.** Constipation or diarrhea that persists for more than two weeks, new urinary frequency or difficulty, or any unexplained change from your normal pattern.

**A lump or mass that is new, growing, or hard.** Anywhere on your body. Most lumps are benign (cysts, lipomas, enlarged lymph nodes from infection). Some are not. The only way to know is evaluation.

**Persistent fatigue that is not explained by lifestyle.** If you are sleeping seven to nine hours, eating adequately, exercising, managing stress, and still dragging through every day, something may be off — thyroid, iron, B12, depression, sleep apnea, or other conditions that are common and treatable.

The threshold for seeking medical evaluation should be low. A false alarm costs you a copay and an afternoon. A missed diagnosis costs you far more.

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Science** — Immunology (how vaccines prime the adaptive immune system through memory B cells and T cells), preventive medicine (the epidemiological evidence base for screening recommendations, the concepts of sensitivity, specificity, and predictive value), and the biology of noise-induced hearing loss (cochlear hair cell damage from sustained acoustic energy).

**Home Economics** — Household health management. Maintaining vaccination records, scheduling screenings, stocking a medicine cabinet, choosing appropriate over-the-counter medications, and creating a home environment that supports health (clean air, adequate lighting, ergonomic workspace).

**Mind-Body** — Proactive health as a psychological orientation. The shift from reactive healthcare (wait until sick, then seek treatment) to proactive healthcare (maintain the body, prevent disease, catch problems early) is a mindset change. It requires treating your future self as someone worth investing in, and overcoming the present bias that makes prevention feel less urgent than treatment.

## The Ounce

Every screening on this list takes less than a day. Every vaccination takes less than an hour. Flossing takes two minutes. Hearing protection takes five seconds to put on. A blood pressure check takes two minutes.

The pound of cure — the emergency room visit, the surgery, the chemotherapy, the hearing aid, the dental implant — takes weeks, months, or years, costs thousands or hundreds of thousands of dollars, and sometimes does not work.

An ounce. Invest the ounce.
