# Building Kits — First Aid for Every Situation

## The Kit You Have Is the Kit That Matters

There is a saying in emergency preparedness: the best kit is the one you have with you. A $500 trauma kit locked in your garage closet is less useful than a $20 collection of bandages and antibiotic ointment in your glove compartment when your kid falls off a bike three miles from home. A beautifully organized hiking kit left on the kitchen counter because you were in a hurry does exactly nothing on the trail.

Kits are not talismans. They do not protect you by existing. They protect you by being present, accessible, stocked, and — critically — by being accompanied by the knowledge of how to use their contents. A tourniquet in the hands of someone who has taken a Stop the Bleed course saves lives. A tourniquet in the hands of someone who does not know how to apply it is an expensive piece of nylon.

This document describes six kits optimized for different environments and use cases. Each kit is opinionated — it includes what matters and skips what does not. Each includes an estimated cost, a rationale for every item, and the specific scenarios where each item earns its place. Some items appear in every kit because some needs are universal. Others are specific to the environment.

Build the kit that matches your life. Then learn how to use it.

## The Home Kit — $30-50

The home first aid kit covers the injuries and illnesses most common in daily life: cuts, scrapes, minor burns, headaches, allergic reactions, sprains, and fever. It should be stored in an accessible location that every member of the household knows about — not buried in the back of a closet under a pile of towels.

### Contents

**Wound care:**
- Adhesive bandages (assorted sizes, including butterfly closures) — the everyday staple. Get a box of 100 and replenish as you use them.
- Gauze pads (4x4 inch, individually wrapped, at least 10) — for wounds too large for a bandage, and for applying pressure to bleeding.
- Gauze roll (one to two rolls) — for wrapping, securing pads, and wound packing if needed.
- Medical tape (1-inch roll, cloth or paper) — for securing gauze. Paper tape is gentler on skin. Cloth tape holds better.
- Triple antibiotic ointment (bacitracin/neomycin/polymyxin) — one tube. For minor wounds after cleaning. Apply a thin layer.

**Tools:**
- Tweezers (pointed tip) — for splinters, ticks, and removing foreign material from wounds. Spring-loaded, stainless steel, sharp enough to actually grip.
- Scissors (small, blunt-tipped) — for cutting tape, gauze, and clothing away from wounds.
- Disposable gloves (nitrile, at least 4 pairs) — for any wound care involving blood or body fluids. Nitrile because latex allergies are common.

**Cold/Heat:**
- Instant cold pack (at least 2) — squeeze to activate, no refrigeration needed. For sprains, strains, bumps, and reducing swelling. Does not replace ice for sustained cooling but is immediately available.

**Monitoring:**
- Digital thermometer — oral or temporal. Fever is an important clinical sign for dozens of conditions. A thermometer costs $8 and removes guesswork.

**Medications:**
- Ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin) — anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic. The most versatile over-the-counter medication. For pain, fever, and swelling. Not for people with stomach ulcers, kidney disease, or aspirin allergy.
- Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — analgesic and antipyretic. For pain and fever when ibuprofen is contraindicated or insufficient. Can be alternated with ibuprofen for enhanced pain control. Not for people with liver disease or heavy alcohol use.
- Aspirin (325mg tablets) — specifically for suspected heart attack (chewed, not swallowed — see FAD-04). Also useful as an analgesic.
- Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — antihistamine. For allergic reactions (hives, itching, mild allergic swelling), insect stings, and as a sleep aid in emergencies. Causes drowsiness — this is a feature, not a bug, when someone is itching at 2 AM.

**Reference:**
- First aid guide — a pocket-sized reference card or booklet. The American Red Cross publishes a concise first aid quick-reference that fits in any kit. In an emergency, you may not remember every step. Having a reference is not a sign of incompetence — it is a sign of preparation.

### Total Cost: $30-50

Most of these items are available at any pharmacy. Pre-assembled kits from the Red Cross, Adventure Medical Kits, and other manufacturers are available at similar price points but often contain items you do not need and lack items you do. Building your own kit ensures you know exactly what is in it and where everything is — familiarity that matters when your hands are shaking.

## The Car Kit — $40-60

The car kit contains everything in the home kit plus items specific to roadside and travel emergencies. Your car is both a common location for injuries (minor accidents, roadside assistance situations) and a primary transport vehicle for injured people heading to medical care. The kit lives in the car permanently.

### Additional Contents (Beyond Home Kit)

**Trauma and immobilization:**
- Emergency blanket (mylar/space blanket) — weighs nothing, takes up no space, and prevents hypothermia in a person who is injured, wet, or stranded. Also useful as a ground sheet, rain shelter, or signaling device.
- Triangular bandage (at least 2) — for arm slings, wound binding, tourniquet improvisation, or as a head wrap. The most versatile single item in any first aid kit.
- SAM splint (36-inch) — a moldable foam-and-aluminum splint that can be shaped to immobilize any extremity. Lighter and more packable than rigid splints. One SAM splint can splint a wrist, a forearm, an ankle, or a finger depending on how you fold it.

**Visibility and access:**
- Flashlight (LED, with fresh batteries) — for nighttime roadside situations, for looking into wound cavities, for signaling. A headlamp is even better because it frees both hands.
- Reflective vest (high-visibility yellow or orange) — for roadside situations where you are outside the vehicle near traffic. A $5 item that prevents you from being hit by a car while helping someone who was hit by a car.
- Seat belt cutter / window breaker — a $10-15 tool that can be the difference between extraction and entrapment. The window breaker is a spring-loaded point that shatters tempered glass (side windows). The seat belt cutter is a recessed blade that slices webbing without risk of cutting the occupant. Keep it within reach of the driver, not in the trunk.

### Total Cost: $40-60 (including home kit contents)

The car kit should be checked every six months. Medications expire, batteries die, cold packs can leak, and items get used and not replaced. Put a reminder in your calendar.

## The Hiking Kit — $50-80

The hiking kit optimizes for weight and the specific hazards of trail and backcountry environments. In the backcountry, the nearest ambulance may be hours away, and self-reliance is not a philosophy — it is a physical reality.

### Additional Contents (Beyond Home Kit)

**Blister care:**
- Moleskin (precut or sheet) — blisters are the number one foot problem on trail. Moleskin applied at the first sign of a hot spot (before the blister forms) prevents the blister entirely. Cut a donut shape to surround the hot spot without pressing on it.
- Blister pads (hydrocolloid bandages) — for blisters that have already formed. These provide cushioning, protect the blister, and promote healing while you continue hiking.

**Hydration and nutrition:**
- Electrolyte packets (at least 4) — for replenishing sodium, potassium, and other electrolytes lost through sweat during extended exertion. Dissolve in water. More effective than water alone for preventing hyponatremia during long, hot hikes.

**Water safety:**
- Water purification tablets (iodine or chlorine dioxide) — backup water treatment for when your filter breaks or you run out of treated water. Lightweight, compact, effective. Chlorine dioxide (Aquamira, Katadyn Micropur) is more palatable than iodine.

**Signaling and navigation:**
- Emergency whistle — three blasts is the universal distress signal. A whistle carries farther than a voice, requires less energy, and works when you are too exhausted or injured to shout. Every member of the party should carry one.

**Environmental protection:**
- Sunscreen (small tube, SPF 30+) — high altitude, open ridgelines, snow reflection, and PNW summer sun at 46 degrees latitude all produce significant UV exposure.
- Insect repellent (small bottle, DEET or picaridin) — mosquitoes in the Cascades from June through September, plus tick prevention in brushy terrain.

**Tick and insect care:**
- Tick removal kit — fine-tipped tweezers (if not already in the base kit, ensure the hiking kit tweezers are the pointed, fine-tipped variety suitable for tick removal), small sealable bag (for saving the tick), alcohol wipe.

**Emergency shelter:**
- Emergency bivy (lightweight, compact) — a step up from the mylar blanket for sustained shelter. If you are injured, lost, or benighted, an emergency bivy provides wind and rain protection and retains body heat. Weighs 3-5 ounces and can save your life during an unplanned overnight.

### Total Cost: $50-80

Weight matters on trail. The hiking kit should be as light as possible while still being complete. Repackage items from bulk containers into smaller volumes. Ditch the cardboard boxes. Use ziplock bags. Every ounce adds up over miles.

## The Workshop Kit — $30-40

This kit connects directly to the WDC (Woodcraft) research project. Workshop injuries have specific characteristics: deep lacerations from sharp tools, crush injuries from clamps and presses, splinter and foreign body wounds, eye injuries from flying debris, and burns from friction, hot tools, and chemicals. The workshop kit emphasizes wound closure, hemostasis, and eye care.

### Contents

**Advanced wound closure:**
- Butterfly closures (assorted sizes) — for closing clean lacerations with edges that can be approximated. Apply perpendicular to the wound after cleaning and drying.
- Steri-Strips (1/4 inch and 1/2 inch) — adhesive wound closure strips. More precise than butterfly closures. Can close a clean laceration well enough to avoid an emergency room visit for simple stitches. Clean the wound, dry the surrounding skin, apply strips at regular intervals across the wound, pulling the edges together.

**Hemostasis (bleeding control):**
- Hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox gauze) — gauze impregnated with agents that accelerate clotting. For deep wounds that are bleeding heavily and not responding to direct pressure alone. Pack the wound and apply pressure (see FAD-02). A single package can be the difference between controlling a table saw laceration at home and bleeding through a towel on the way to the ER.
- Blood clotting powder (QuikClot powder or Celox granules) — pour into the wound cavity, then apply pressure. For wounds that are too irregular or shallow for gauze packing but too deep or bloody for surface pressure alone.

**Eye care:**
- Eye wash (sterile saline, 4 oz bottle or single-use vials) — for flushing sawdust, metal shavings, wood chips, and chemical splashes from the eye. Immediate flushing is the standard of care for eye exposures. Having eye wash within arm's reach of the workbench is not optional.

**Burns:**
- Burn gel (water-based, lidocaine-free or with lidocaine for pain relief) — for friction burns, hot tool contact burns, and chemical burns after decontamination. Provides cooling and a protective barrier. Not a substitute for running water (see FAD-02) but useful for continued care after the initial cooling.

**Splinting:**
- Finger splints (aluminum, assorted sizes) — for finger fractures and jammed fingers. Bend to shape, secure with tape. Workshop injuries to the hand and fingers are common, and a finger splint allows continued function while awaiting evaluation.

**Standard items:**
- Disposable gloves (nitrile, 4+ pairs)
- Gauze pads, gauze roll, medical tape
- Adhesive bandages (assorted)
- Tweezers (for splinters — a daily occurrence in woodshops)
- Ibuprofen, acetaminophen

### Total Cost: $30-40

**Kit placement:** Wall-mounted, near the primary workbench, not in a drawer or a cabinet that requires opening with bloody hands. Visible, accessible, marked. Consider a bright red or orange container that stands out against the shop environment.

## The Convention Kit — $20-30

This kit connects to the FFA (Fursuit and Fabric Arts) research project and is designed for multi-day conventions, fan events, trade shows, and other gatherings where you are on your feet for extended hours in crowded, often poorly ventilated spaces, sleeping irregularly, and exposing yourself to every circulating pathogen in a large group.

### Contents

**Foot and blister care:**
- Moleskin (sheet, for custom cutting) — conventions mean hours of walking on concrete floors, often in costumes or unfamiliar shoes. Hot spots develop fast.
- Toe pads and cushions — for preventing blister formation between toes, especially in costume shoes or boots that fit differently than everyday footwear.
- Adhesive bandages (assorted, including large) — for the inevitable blisters that form despite prevention.

**Hydration and energy:**
- Electrolyte packets (at least 6) — convention halls are warm, dehydrating environments. Fursuit performers in particular lose significant fluid through sweating. Electrolyte packets in water bottles throughout the day maintain hydration better than water alone.

**Pain and sleep:**
- Ibuprofen — for sore feet, headaches, and the general physical toll of multi-day events.
- Melatonin (1-3mg tablets) — convention sleep is terrible. Shared hotel rooms, irregular schedules, excitement, and screen time all conspire against sleep. Melatonin is a mild, non-habit-forming sleep aid that helps reset circadian timing. Not a knockout pill — take it thirty minutes before your target bedtime and pair it with reduced light.

**Hygiene:**
- Hand sanitizer (travel size, at least 60% alcohol) — conventions are petri dishes. Thousands of people touching the same surfaces, shaking hands, sharing close quarters. Hand sanitizer between hand-washings reduces pathogen transmission.
- Lip balm — convention halls have dry, recirculated air. Cracked lips are painful and can become infected.
- Deodorant (travel size) — because you are in close proximity to other humans for extended periods, and conventions are warm, and this is a kindness to everyone around you.

**Repair and emergency:**
- Safety pins (assorted sizes, at least 10) — for costume repairs, securing bandages, pinning badges, emergency clothing fixes. The most versatile tool at a convention.
- Small sewing kit (needle, thread in basic colors, small scissors) — for costume repairs, loose buttons, split seams. A basic kit costs $3 and can save a costume.
- Hot glue sticks (3-4 mini sticks) — assuming access to a hot glue gun (many convention cosplay repair stations have them). For prop and costume repairs.

### Total Cost: $20-30

The convention kit fits in a small ziplock bag in your convention bag. It is not a first aid kit in the trauma sense — it is a comfort and resilience kit that prevents minor problems from becoming major ones and keeps you functional through a multi-day event.

For fursuit performers specifically: hydration, electrolytes, and heat management are not comfort measures — they are safety measures. Heat exhaustion inside a fursuit is a real and documented hazard. The convention kit should be paired with the heat illness knowledge from FAD-05 and a buddy system where someone monitors the suiter for signs of overheating.

## The Event / Go Bag Kit — $60-100

This kit connects to the FSR (First Responders) research project and is designed for people who attend large events, work in security or event staffing, or simply want to be prepared for mass casualty or high-threat scenarios. It is trauma-focused — designed to address the injuries most likely to kill in the first minutes before EMS arrives: hemorrhage, airway compromise, and tension pneumothorax.

This kit requires training to use effectively. A Stop the Bleed course (free, widely available) covers tourniquet application, wound packing, and direct pressure. A Tactical Emergency Casualty Care (TECC) or similar course covers the chest seal and NPA. Do not carry equipment you have not been trained on.

### Contents

**Hemorrhage control:**
- Tourniquet (CAT — Combat Application Tourniquet, or SOF-T) — for life-threatening extremity bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure. See FAD-02 for application technique. Carry the real thing, not a knockoff — counterfeit tourniquets have failed under tension, with fatal consequences. Purchase from a verified medical supply vendor.
- Israeli bandage (Emergency Bandage, 6-inch) — a pressure dressing with a built-in pressure applicator. Wraps around a limb or torso, provides compression, and secures itself. More effective than improvised pressure dressings.
- Wound packing gauze (hemostatic — QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox) — for deep wounds that require packing. A single roll, vacuum-sealed.

**Airway:**
- Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA, 28 French is the most common adult size) — a flexible rubber tube inserted through the nostril into the nasopharynx to maintain an open airway in an unconscious person who is breathing but whose airway is at risk of obstruction. Requires training. Lubricate with the water-based lubricant included in the package and insert gently.

**Chest trauma:**
- Chest seal (vented — HyFin Vent or SAM Chest Seal) — for open chest wounds (sucking chest wounds) caused by penetrating trauma. The seal prevents air from entering the chest cavity through the wound, which can cause a tension pneumothorax (collapsed lung with pressure that compromises the heart). Vented seals allow air to escape but not enter. Apply over the wound after wiping the area dry. Requires training to recognize the injury pattern and apply correctly.

**Thermal protection:**
- Emergency blanket (mylar) — for shock prevention and warmth.

**PPE:**
- Nitrile gloves (at least 4 pairs) — you will be handling blood.

### Total Cost: $60-100

This is a serious kit for serious scenarios. It is not the kit you reach for when someone scrapes their knee. It is the kit you reach for when someone is bleeding from a wound that is going to kill them in three minutes if you do not act. Get the training. Carry the kit. Hope you never need it.

## Kit Maintenance — The Forgotten Step

A kit that was assembled two years ago and never checked since is a kit full of expired medications, dead batteries, dried-out antibiotic ointment, and possibly leaked cold packs that have corroded everything around them.

Schedule kit checks every six months (an easy mnemonic: when you change your clocks, check your kits). During each check:

- Replace expired medications
- Replace used items
- Test batteries in flashlights
- Verify integrity of sealed packages (gauze, sterile pads, chest seals)
- Replace any item that has been opened, partially used, or has damaged packaging
- Verify that the kit is in its designated location and accessible

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Home Economics** — Household preparedness. Building and maintaining first aid kits is a core household management skill, alongside fire extinguisher placement, emergency supply storage, and disaster preparedness. The home kit is the minimum standard for any household.

**Science** — Pharmacology basics. Understanding what ibuprofen does (COX-2 inhibition reducing prostaglandin synthesis, which reduces inflammation, pain, and fever), why antibiotic ointment prevents infection (bacteriostatic and bactericidal action against common skin flora), and how hemostatic agents work (kaolin or chitosan-based products that activate the clotting cascade) elevates kit use from "follow the instructions" to "understand why."

**Learning** — Kit assembly as a planning exercise. Building a first aid kit is an exercise in risk assessment (what injuries are most likely in this environment?), resource allocation (what fits in the available space and budget?), and prioritization (what saves lives versus what provides comfort?). These are transferable planning skills that apply to any preparation task.

## Build It, Know It, Carry It

A first aid kit is three things: the container, the contents, and the competence to use them. Without the third element, the first two are supplies, not a kit.

Take a course. Practice the skills. Check the kit. Then carry it with you — in your home, in your car, on the trail, in the shop, at the convention, at the event.

The kit you have is the kit that matters. Build it for your life.
