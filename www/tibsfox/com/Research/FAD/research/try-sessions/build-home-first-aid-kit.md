# DIY Project: Build a Home First Aid Kit

## The Kit That Lives Where You Live

Every household needs a first aid kit. Not the dusty plastic box your parents bought in 1998 that has three dried-out bandages and a tube of ointment that expired during the Obama administration. A real kit. A kit you built yourself, item by item, knowing what every piece does and why it earned its place. A kit that lives in a specific spot that every person in your household can find in the dark, because emergencies do not wait for good lighting.

This try session walks you through building a complete home first aid kit from scratch. Every item is explained --- not just what it is, but why it is there and how to use it. By the end, you will have a kit that handles everything from a toddler's scraped knee to a serious laceration, assembled for thirty to fifty dollars with one upgrade tier that adds trauma capability for another forty to fifty dollars.

The total time is about an hour at a pharmacy or online, plus fifteen minutes to organize and store. After that, you maintain it quarterly --- ten minutes, four times a year. That is the full commitment. An hour to build, forty minutes a year to maintain, and the knowledge that when someone in your household is bleeding, burned, feverish, or in pain, you are ready.

## The Container

Start with the container. This matters more than people think. A good container keeps everything organized, protected from moisture, and visible at a glance so you can find what you need without dumping the entire kit on the floor.

**Best option: a waterproof plastic box with a latching lid.** A clear or translucent container lets you see the contents without opening it. A tackle box with divided compartments works beautifully --- the small compartments organize medications, the larger ones hold gauze and bandages. A dry bag works if you need something more portable. A gallon ziplock bag is a minimum viable container.

**Not a shoebox.** Not a paper bag. Not a drawer full of loose supplies. The point of a kit is that everything is together, portable, and ready.

**Where to store it:** Kitchen or bathroom --- the two places where most household injuries occur. Not under the sink (moisture and pipe leaks). Not on a high shelf that requires a step stool (you may need this while dizzy or holding a bleeding child). At waist or chest height, accessible to every adult in the household, and in a location that every household member knows. Say it out loud to everyone who lives with you: "The first aid kit is in the hall closet, second shelf, left side." If they cannot repeat the location back to you, you have not communicated it.

Mark the container. A red cross made from tape, a label that says FIRST AID, a strip of red duct tape --- anything that makes it instantly identifiable if someone else is looking for it in a panic.

## Wound Care Supplies

These are the items you will use most often. Cuts, scrapes, and minor wounds are the bread and butter of home first aid.

### Adhesive Bandages --- Assorted Sizes

**What:** A box of 50-100 adhesive bandages in assorted sizes. Small (3/4 inch) for fingertip cuts. Standard (1 inch) for most minor wounds. Large (2 inch) for bigger scrapes. Knuckle and fingertip shapes for the joints that move and make standard bandages peel off.

**Why they are there:** Adhesive bandages protect minor wounds from dirt and friction, keep antibiotic ointment in contact with the wound, and provide a moist healing environment that promotes faster closure. They are the single most-used item in any first aid kit.

**How to use:** Clean the wound first (running water, one minute minimum). Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment. Peel the bandage backing and center the pad over the wound. Press the adhesive wings firmly to dry skin. Change daily or whenever the bandage gets wet.

**Cost:** $4-6 for a box of 100.

### Butterfly Closures (Wound Closure Strips)

**What:** Small adhesive strips with a narrow center section, designed to pull wound edges together.

**Why they are there:** A cut that is too deep or too wide for a regular bandage but not deep enough for stitches can often be closed with butterfly closures or Steri-Strips. They hold the wound edges in alignment so the body can bridge the gap with new tissue. This reduces scarring and speeds healing. They are the at-home alternative to emergency room sutures for many lacerations.

**How to use:** Clean and dry the wound. Apply one strip perpendicular to the wound, pulling the edges together gently but firmly. Add strips every quarter inch along the wound length. Do not overlap them onto the wound itself --- they go across it. Apply a standard bandage or gauze pad over the top. Leave in place for five to seven days unless they peel off on their own.

**Cost:** $3-5 for a package of 10-20.

### Gauze Pads (4x4 inch, Sterile)

**What:** Individually wrapped, sterile cotton gauze squares. Get at least ten.

**Why they are there:** Gauze pads serve two critical functions. First, they are absorbent pads for wounds too large for a bandage --- place over the wound and secure with tape. Second, and more importantly, they are the tool for applying direct pressure to stop bleeding. A stack of gauze pads pressed firmly against a bleeding wound for five to ten continuous minutes without lifting is the primary technique for controlling most hemorrhage. This is the technique that saves lives.

**How to use:** For bandaging: place sterile side against wound, secure with medical tape on all four edges. For pressure: stack multiple pads over the wound, press hard with your palm, and do not lift to check for at least five minutes. If the first set soaks through, add more on top --- do not remove the blood-soaked pads, because removing them disrupts the clot forming underneath.

**Cost:** $3-5 for a package of 25.

### Gauze Roll

**What:** One or two rolls of conforming gauze bandage (2 or 3 inch width).

**Why it is there:** Gauze rolls wrap. They secure gauze pads in place around limbs, fingers, and heads without tape. They conform to body contours. They provide light compression. For a head wound, an arm wound, a hand wound, or any wound in a location where tape alone will not hold a pad in place, gauze rolls are essential.

**How to use:** Place the gauze pad over the wound, then wrap the roll around the limb or body part, overlapping each layer by half. Secure the end with tape or by tucking it under. Wrap firmly but not so tight that you cut off circulation --- you should be able to slip a finger under the wrap.

**Cost:** $2-4 for two rolls.

### Medical Tape

**What:** One roll of cloth tape (strong, for secure holds) and one roll of paper tape (gentle, for sensitive skin). One-inch width for both.

**Why it is there:** Tape secures gauze pads, holds butterfly closures in place, and attaches splints. Cloth tape (the white fabric tape) holds firmly even on sweaty or hairy skin. Paper tape (the beige, thin tape) is gentler --- use it on children, elderly skin, or anyone with sensitive skin that tears easily.

**How to use:** Tear or cut to length. For securing gauze pads, tape on at least two sides, ideally all four. Never wrap tape circumferentially around a limb tight enough to restrict blood flow --- always leave a gap or apply loosely enough that a finger fits underneath.

**Cost:** $3-5 for both rolls.

### Triple Antibiotic Ointment

**What:** One tube of triple antibiotic (bacitracin, neomycin, polymyxin B) --- generic is identical to Neosporin and costs a third as much.

**Why it is there:** Applied to clean wounds, triple antibiotic ointment provides a barrier against bacterial infection and keeps the wound moist, which research consistently shows promotes faster healing. Wounds that are kept moist heal faster and with less scarring than wounds that are allowed to dry out and scab.

**How to use:** After cleaning the wound, apply a thin layer with a clean finger or cotton swab. Thin layer. You are not frosting a cake. Reapply when you change the bandage. If redness or itching develops around the wound (not in it), you may have a neomycin allergy --- switch to plain bacitracin or plain petroleum jelly.

**Cost:** $3-5 for a tube.

## Tools

### Tweezers

**What:** One pair of pointed-tip, stainless steel tweezers. Spring-loaded. Sharp enough to actually grip.

**Why they are there:** Splinters and ticks. A splinter left in the skin invites infection. A tick left attached continues to transmit pathogens --- the longer it feeds, the higher the risk of Lyme disease and other tick-borne illness. Tweezers are the extraction tool.

**How to use:** For splinters: grasp the exposed end and pull in the direction the splinter entered. If the end is not visible, sterilize a needle (flame or alcohol), gently open the skin over the end of the splinter, then grasp with tweezers. For ticks: grasp the tick as close to the skin surface as possible (you want the mouthparts, not the body), pull upward with steady, even pressure. Do not twist, do not jerk, do not squeeze the body. Clean the bite with soap and water. Save the tick in a sealed bag with the date --- if symptoms develop later, the tick can be identified and tested.

**Cost:** $3-5.

### Scissors

**What:** Small, blunt-tipped bandage scissors.

**Why they are there:** Cutting gauze, tape, bandages, and clothing away from wounds. Blunt tips prevent accidentally stabbing the patient when cutting near skin.

**Cost:** $3-5.

### Nitrile Gloves

**What:** At least four pairs (two pairs for two separate incidents, or two pairs because one might tear). Nitrile, not latex --- latex allergies are common enough that nitrile is the universal standard.

**Why they are there:** Bloodborne pathogen protection. When you touch someone else's blood --- or your own blood contacts broken skin --- gloves are the barrier. Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, and HIV are transmissible through blood-to-broken-skin contact. The risk is low per exposure but the consequences are life-altering. Gloves reduce the risk to approximately zero.

**How to use:** Put them on before touching anyone who is bleeding. To remove, pinch the cuff of one glove and peel it off inside-out, balling it in your gloved hand. Then slide a finger under the cuff of the second glove and peel it off over the first, containing all blood-contaminated surfaces inside. Dispose of the glove ball. Wash your hands.

**Cost:** $5-8 for a box of 50 (you will have gloves for years).

## Thermal and Assessment Tools

### Instant Cold Pack

**What:** One or two chemical cold packs that activate by squeezing. No refrigeration needed.

**Why it is there:** Cold reduces swelling and pain in sprains, strains, bumps, bruises, and insect stings. In the first twenty minutes after a musculoskeletal injury, cold application (part of the RICE protocol --- Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) limits the inflammatory response.

**How to use:** Squeeze or strike the pack to activate the chemical reaction. Wrap in a thin cloth or paper towel before applying to skin (direct application can cause frostbite). Apply for fifteen to twenty minutes, then remove for at least twenty minutes before reapplying. Never apply directly to bare skin.

**Cost:** $3-5 for a two-pack.

### Digital Thermometer

**What:** One digital oral thermometer with disposable probe covers.

**Why it is there:** Fever assessment. Normal body temperature is approximately 98.6 degrees F (37 degrees C), with individual variation. Fever is generally defined as 100.4 degrees F (38 degrees C) or above. In children, fever management and monitoring is a core parenting skill. In adults, persistent fever above 103 degrees F (39.4 degrees C), fever with stiff neck, confusion, or rash, or fever in an immunocompromised person warrants medical evaluation.

**How to use:** Place under the tongue, close mouth, wait for the beep. Record the temperature and the time. For children too young for oral thermometry, axillary (armpit) or temporal artery (forehead swipe) thermometers are alternatives, with the understanding that they are slightly less accurate.

**Cost:** $5-10.

## Medications

Every medication in this kit is over-the-counter and available without a prescription. For each one: know what it does, know the dose, and know who should NOT take it.

### Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)

Anti-inflammatory, pain reliever, and fever reducer. 200-400mg every six hours as needed. Maximum 1200mg per day for OTC use. Do NOT take if you have kidney disease, active stomach ulcers, or are on blood thinners. Do NOT give to infants under six months without a pediatrician's guidance. Take with food to protect the stomach.

### Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

Pain reliever and fever reducer --- but NOT anti-inflammatory. 500-1000mg every six hours. Maximum 3000mg per day. The liver metabolizes acetaminophen, and exceeding the maximum dose causes liver damage that can be fatal. Do NOT combine with alcohol. Do NOT double up with combination medications that already contain acetaminophen (many cold medicines, NyQuil, Percocet).

### Aspirin

Pain reliever, fever reducer, and antiplatelet agent. The cardiac use is what earns aspirin a specific place in the kit: 325mg of aspirin chewed (not swallowed whole --- chewing speeds absorption) is the bystander intervention for a suspected heart attack. Aspirin inhibits platelet aggregation, which can slow or reduce the clot blocking the coronary artery. This is a life-saving intervention that buys time until the ambulance arrives. Do NOT give aspirin to children or teenagers (risk of Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition). Do NOT take if you have an aspirin allergy or active bleeding disorder.

### Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)

Antihistamine for allergic reactions, hives, insect stings, and itching. 25-50mg every six hours. Causes significant drowsiness --- this is a feature when someone is miserable from itching and needs to sleep, and a hazard when they need to drive or operate machinery. For severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis --- throat swelling, difficulty breathing, widespread hives, dizziness), diphenhydramine is a second-line treatment. Epinephrine (EpiPen) is first-line for anaphylaxis --- diphenhydramine alone will not stop anaphylaxis. But for mild-to-moderate allergic reactions, it is the right tool.

**Cost for all four medications:** $12-20 total for store-brand bottles.

## The Upgrade Tier --- Trauma Capability ($40-50 additional)

These three items elevate your kit from everyday first aid to trauma-ready. Each one addresses a specific scenario where the standard supplies are insufficient and the ambulance is minutes away.

### SAM Splint ($8-12)

A flexible, moldable aluminum-and-foam splint that can be shaped to immobilize any limb fracture, finger fracture, or joint injury. Weighs almost nothing. Rolls up for storage. Curves into a C-shape to become rigid enough to splint a forearm, a wrist, an ankle, or a finger. The SAM splint is standard equipment in ambulances, wilderness kits, and military medic bags because of its versatility. One splint, any fracture.

### Israeli Bandage ($8-12)

Also called an Emergency Bandage. A six-inch wide sterile dressing with a built-in pressure applicator and elastic wrap. Designed by the Israeli Defense Forces for battlefield wound treatment. You open the package, place the pad on the wound, wrap the elastic bandage around the limb or torso using the built-in pressure bar to apply compression, and secure with the closure clip. It applies more effective pressure than improvised bandages and is faster to apply. For any wound that needs compression --- deep lacerations, avulsions, penetrating wounds --- this is the best single device in the kit.

### Combat Application Tourniquet (CAT) ($25-30)

This is the item that generates the most debate and the one that matters most in the worst-case scenario. A tourniquet is a band that cinches around a limb and compresses the artery against the bone, stopping all blood flow below the application point. It is the intervention of last resort for life-threatening extremity bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure.

**Why you MIGHT need it:** A deep laceration to the inner thigh or upper arm that hits a major artery (femoral or brachial) can cause death from blood loss in three to five minutes. Direct pressure may not be sufficient. A tourniquet applied correctly stops the bleeding immediately.

Buy a genuine CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) or SOF-T (Special Operations Forces Tactical Tourniquet) from a verified medical supply vendor. Do NOT buy from random Amazon sellers --- counterfeit tourniquets have failed under tension in documented cases, with fatal outcomes. North American Rescue is the manufacturer of the CAT. That is where your tourniquet should originate.

Learn to use it. Take a Stop the Bleed course (free, available at hospitals and fire departments --- see [FSR try-session: Stop the Bleed](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md)). Practice applying it to your own thigh until you can do it in under thirty seconds.

## The Refresh Schedule

A kit is not a one-time project. It is an ongoing system.

**Quarterly (every 3 months):** Open the kit. Check expiration dates on all medications. Replace anything expired. Restock anything you have used since the last check. Verify that sealed packages (gauze, cold packs) are intact. Confirm the kit is in its designated location.

**Mnemonic:** Check your kit when the seasons change. Spring, summer, fall, winter --- four checks per year. Write the next check date on a piece of tape on the lid of the kit.

**Medications expire.** Expired ibuprofen and acetaminophen lose potency gradually but do not become dangerous. Expired aspirin degrades into acetic acid (vinegar smell) and loses its antiplatelet effect --- the one medication where expiration genuinely matters for the cardiac use case. Replace aspirin on schedule.

**Cold packs can leak.** If the inner ammonium nitrate bag has ruptured, the cold pack is useless and the chemicals can corrode other kit contents. Replace any cold pack with a damaged or swollen package.

**Antibiotic ointment dries out.** Once the tube is opened and used, the ointment at the tip begins to dry. If the ointment is hard, crumbly, or discolored, replace the tube.

## The Final Step: Tell Everyone

The kit is useless if no one can find it when you are not home. Walk every household member to the kit location. Show them the contents. Show the teenagers, especially --- they babysit, they host friends, they are the most likely to encounter a peer's injury and the least likely to have been shown where the supplies are.

Put the kit location in your family's emergency plan. If you do not have a family emergency plan, building this kit is a good excuse to start one (see [FSR try-session: Family Emergency Plan](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/family-emergency-plan.md)).

The best first aid kit in the world is the one that is there, stocked, and known. You just built it. Now make sure everyone knows where it lives.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Home Economics** --- [Household Safety]: Building and maintaining a first aid kit is a core household management skill. Knowing where the kit is, what it contains, and when to restock it is no different from knowing where the fire extinguisher is or when to change the smoke detector batteries. This is the infrastructure of a prepared home.
- **Science** --- [Pharmacology Basics]: Every medication in the kit has a mechanism of action --- ibuprofen inhibits COX enzymes to reduce prostaglandin synthesis, acetaminophen modulates pain perception through central nervous system pathways, aspirin irreversibly acetylates platelet cyclooxygenase. Understanding the mechanism transforms pill-taking from habit to informed decision.
- **Learning** --- [Risk Assessment]: Building a kit is an exercise in anticipating what could go wrong and preparing proportionate responses. What injuries are most likely in your household? What does each item address? This is applied risk analysis.
