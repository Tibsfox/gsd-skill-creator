# Try Session: Practice the Recovery Position

## The Single Most Useful Thing You Can Do

Of all the first aid skills a person can learn, the recovery position may be the single most broadly applicable. It requires no equipment, no certification, no supplies, and no physical strength beyond the ability to roll another person onto their side. It takes ten seconds to perform and ten minutes to learn. And it addresses the scenario that kills more unconscious people than any other single cause: aspiration.

An unconscious person lying flat on their back is in danger. The tongue relaxes and falls backward, partially or fully blocking the airway. If the person vomits --- which is common in alcohol intoxication, drug overdose, head injury, seizure, and many medical emergencies --- the vomit pools in the back of the throat and enters the lungs. Aspiration pneumonia is a serious and frequently fatal complication of an otherwise survivable event. People survive the overdose but die from the vomit. People survive the seizure but die from the airway obstruction.

The recovery position solves both problems. It uses gravity. The person lies on their side with their head angled slightly downward so that fluids drain out of the mouth rather than into the lungs. The tongue falls forward rather than backward. The airway stays open. Breathing continues.

This is the skill you use when someone is unconscious but breathing. It is the complement to CPR, which is the skill you use when someone is unconscious and NOT breathing. Together, these two skills cover the two most critical bystander scenarios. The [FSR try-session on hands-only CPR](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md) covers the other half. This session covers this half.

Today you will practice on a willing partner --- a family member, a friend, a housemate. You will take turns. Ten minutes total. By the end, your hands will know the sequence, and if you ever find someone unconscious and breathing at a party, on a trail, in a parking lot, or in your own home, you will not have to think. You will just do it.

## Before You Start: The Assessment

The recovery position is not the first thing you do. It is the fourth or fifth.

**Step 1: Scene safety.** Is the area safe for you to approach? Is there traffic, fire, electrical hazard, or an active threat? If the scene is not safe, do not enter. Call 911 and describe the hazard.

**Step 2: Check responsiveness.** Kneel beside the person. Tap their shoulders firmly. Shout: "Are you okay? Can you hear me?" Shout twice. Tap hard --- not a polite touch, a firm shoulder shake. If they respond in any way --- open eyes, moan, move, speak --- they are conscious and the recovery position is not needed yet. Stay with them, talk to them, call for help if needed.

**Step 3: Call 911.** If they do not respond, call 911 immediately. If someone else is present, point at them and say: "You --- call 911. Tell them someone is unconscious at [your location]." If you are alone, put your phone on speaker, call 911, and set it on the ground next to you.

**Step 4: Check breathing.** Look at their chest. Watch for the rise and fall of breathing. Listen for breath sounds. Feel for air on your cheek. Do this for no more than ten seconds.

- **If they are breathing normally:** Recovery position. This is where this try session picks up.
- **If they are NOT breathing, or only gasping:** CPR. See [hands-only CPR](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md).

The fork in the road is breathing. Breathing means recovery position. Not breathing means CPR. Every bystander should know both paths.

## The Technique: Step by Step

Find your practice partner. You need a carpeted floor, a yoga mat, or grass --- somewhere soft enough to be comfortable lying down. The partner who goes first lies flat on their back, arms at their sides, legs straight. The other partner kneels beside them.

Decide which side to roll toward. In most situations, it does not matter. If the person has an obvious injury on one side, roll them onto the uninjured side. For practice, pick the side where you are kneeling --- you will roll them toward you.

### Step 1: Position the Near Arm

Take the arm closest to you --- the arm on the side you are kneeling on --- and place it at a right angle to the body. The elbow is bent, the palm faces up. This arm acts as a stabilizer and prevents the person from rolling face-down once they are on their side.

**Practice cue:** The near arm makes an L shape on the ground, palm up, like they are waving at the ceiling.

### Step 2: Fold the Far Arm Across the Chest

Reach across and take the person's far hand --- the hand on the side away from you. Bring it across their chest and hold the back of their hand against their near cheek. The cheek closest to you. Hold it there --- your hand keeps their hand in position against their face.

**Why:** When they roll onto their side, their own hand will cushion their head and keep their face angled slightly downward. This is elegant engineering using the person's own body.

**Practice cue:** Their far hand is now pressed against their near cheek, held there by your hand.

### Step 3: Bend the Far Knee

With your free hand, reach across to the person's far leg --- the leg on the side away from you. Grab behind the knee and pull it up so the foot is flat on the ground. The knee is now bent, foot planted.

**Why:** The bent knee acts as a lever. When you pull it toward you, the entire body rotates. It also acts as a kickstand once they are on their side, preventing them from rolling onto their face.

**Practice cue:** The far knee is up, foot flat on the ground, like they are about to do a sit-up.

### Step 4: Roll Toward You

Now the roll. Keep one hand holding their hand against their cheek. With your other hand on their bent knee, pull the knee toward you smoothly. The body will roll onto its side as a unit. Guide the roll --- do not yank. Smooth, controlled, toward you.

They should end up on their side facing you, with:
- Their bottom arm (the L-shaped one) out in front of them, preventing them from rolling further forward
- Their top hand under their cheek, cushioning their head
- Their top knee bent at a right angle, acting as a kickstand

**Practice cue:** One smooth motion. Knee toward you, body follows. They end up in a stable side-lying position.

### Step 5: Open the Airway

Tilt their head back slightly --- gently extend the neck --- to make sure the airway is open. The chin should lift slightly. Check that their mouth is angled downward, so any fluids drain out rather than pooling in the throat.

### Step 6: Monitor

Stay with them. Watch their breathing. A hand on their upper back lets you feel the rise and fall of their ribcage. If breathing stops, roll them onto their back and begin CPR.

Check their breathing every minute. The recovery position is a holding pattern --- you are keeping the airway safe while waiting for the ambulance to arrive.

## Practice Drill: Take Turns

**Round 1:** Partner A lies flat on their back. Partner B performs the full sequence: near arm at right angle, far hand to cheek, bend far knee, roll toward you, tilt head, check breathing. Time it. The first time will take sixty to ninety seconds. That is fine.

**Round 2:** Switch roles. Partner B lies down. Partner A performs the sequence.

**Round 3:** Both partners repeat, aiming for thirty seconds. Speed comes from familiarity, not rushing. The steps are sequential and each one sets up the next.

**Round 4:** Practice from both sides. Kneel on the opposite side and roll the other direction. In a real scenario, you may not have a choice about which side to approach from.

**Total time:** Ten minutes for all four rounds.

## Why It Works: The Physics and Anatomy

The recovery position exploits three physical principles:

**Gravity clears the airway.** In a supine (face-up) position, gravity pulls the tongue backward and pools fluids in the throat. In a lateral (side-lying) position, gravity pulls the tongue forward and drains fluids out of the mouth. The simple act of rotating the body ninety degrees reverses the direction that gravity acts on the airway contents.

**The bent knee prevents prone rolling.** Without the kickstand leg, a person rolled onto their side might continue rolling onto their face, which creates a new airway obstruction problem. The bent knee stops the rotation at exactly the right point.

**The hand-under-cheek cushions and positions the head.** The head needs to be slightly extended (chin up) to keep the airway open, and slightly angled down to allow drainage. The person's own hand under their cheek achieves both naturally.

## When NOT to Use the Recovery Position

The recovery position is not universal. There are situations where it is the wrong move:

**Suspected spinal injury.** If the person fell from a height, was in a vehicle accident, was struck by an object, or has any mechanism of injury that could have damaged the spine --- do NOT roll them unless their airway is actively compromised (they are vomiting and you cannot clear it any other way). Moving someone with a spinal fracture risks converting a broken vertebra into a severed spinal cord. If you MUST roll them to protect the airway, use a log-roll technique with as many helpers as possible, keeping the head, neck, and spine aligned as a unit.

**Not breathing.** If the person is not breathing or is only gasping (agonal breathing --- irregular, infrequent gasps), they need CPR, not the recovery position. Roll them onto their back on a firm surface and begin chest compressions. See [FSR try-session: hands-only CPR](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md).

**Active seizure.** During a seizure, do not try to restrain the person or put anything in their mouth. Clear the area of objects they could hit. Let the seizure run its course. After the seizure ends (the postictal phase), if they are unconscious and breathing, THEN place them in the recovery position. The postictal phase is when aspiration risk is highest, because the person is unconscious and may vomit.

**Cardiac arrest.** If there is no pulse and no breathing, they need CPR and an AED, not the recovery position. The recovery position is for people whose hearts are beating. If the heart stops, the protocol changes entirely.

## Real-World Scenarios Where This Matters

**Alcohol intoxication.** Someone at a party drinks too much and passes out. The number one risk is vomiting and aspirating. Recovery position. Do NOT leave them alone. Do NOT "let them sleep it off" on their back. The difference between sleeping it off safely and dying in their own vomit is the position of their body.

**Drug overdose.** Opioid overdose causes respiratory depression. If the person is still breathing (even slowly), recovery position while waiting for EMS. If you have naloxone (Narcan), administer it, then place them in recovery position --- they may vomit as the naloxone takes effect.

**Postictal state after seizure.** A person who has had a seizure is typically unconscious and confused for minutes to hours afterward. Recovery position protects their airway during this vulnerable period.

**Fainting (syncope).** A person who faints and does not immediately regain consciousness should be placed in the recovery position while you assess for underlying causes.

**Found unresponsive.** You find someone on the ground --- in a park, a restroom, a parking lot. They are unconscious but breathing. Recovery position, call 911, monitor. You may not know why they are unconscious, and you do not need to. The airway management is the same regardless of cause.

## The Ten-Minute Investment

You just spent ten minutes learning and practicing a skill that has been keeping unconscious people alive since it was formalized in the 1950s and adopted as standard first aid protocol worldwide. The technique has not changed substantially in decades because it does not need to. Gravity has not changed. Airway anatomy has not changed. The physics of aspiration prevention has not changed.

What changes is you. Ten minutes ago, you might have found an unconscious person and panicked, or done nothing, or done the wrong thing. Now you know: near arm L-shape, far hand to cheek, bend far knee, roll toward you, tilt head, monitor breathing.

Your hands know the sequence. That is what matters. When the stress response hits and your conscious mind goes blank, your hands will remember. That is what practice does. That is why this was a try session and not just a document to read.

Practice it again in a month. Practice it with a different partner. Show it to your kids --- children as young as ten can learn this. The more people who know it, the safer everyone around them becomes.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Anatomy]: Airway anatomy explains everything about why this position works. The tongue is a muscular organ attached at the hyoid bone. In a supine unconscious person, the base of the tongue falls posteriorly against the pharyngeal wall, occluding the airway. Lateral positioning lets gravity pull the tongue forward and clear. The epiglottis, the valleculae, the piriform sinuses --- these are the structures that determine whether fluids drain out or pool in.
- **Physical Education** --- [Body Mechanics]: Rolling another person requires technique, not strength. The bent-knee lever converts a heavy, awkward lift into a smooth, controlled rotation. Understanding leverage and body positioning makes the skill accessible to anyone regardless of size difference between rescuer and patient.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Calm Under Pressure]: Finding someone unconscious triggers the stress response --- racing heart, shaking hands, tunnel vision. Having a practiced, sequential skill to execute gives the mind something concrete to do instead of freezing. The recovery position is as much a tool for managing your own stress as it is for managing the patient's airway.
