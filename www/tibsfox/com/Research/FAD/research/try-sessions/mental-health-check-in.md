# Try Session: The Monthly Mental Health Check-In

## You Cannot Fix What You Do Not Notice

Physical health has built-in warning systems. A fever announces itself. A broken bone makes its presence known immediately. A rash is visible. Pain is unmistakable. Your body has millions of years of evolved alarm systems designed to force you to notice when something is physically wrong.

Mental health has no such alarms. Depression does not announce itself. It seeps in. Anxiety escalates gradually. Burnout accumulates in increments so small that by the time you notice, you have been running on fumes for months. The human mind has a remarkable and dangerous capacity to normalize its own deterioration. You stop sleeping well, and within a week that becomes your new normal. You stop enjoying things, and within a month you have forgotten that you used to enjoy them. You withdraw from people, and the isolation feels like preference rather than symptom.

This is why monitoring matters. Not because you are sick, but because you are human, and being human means your mental health fluctuates in response to stress, seasons, life events, relationships, sleep, nutrition, substances, and a hundred other variables. A monthly check-in is a structured pause to notice what is actually happening inside your head, without the distortion of normalization.

This is not therapy. This is not diagnosis. This is a mirror. You look into it once a month, honestly, and you see what is there.

The entire exercise takes fifteen to twenty minutes. Sit with a journal, a notes app, or a piece of paper. Answer eight questions honestly. Score yourself. Act on the results if needed. That is it. Fifteen minutes a month to maintain the organ that runs your entire life.

## The Check-In: Eight Questions

Choose the same time each month. The first of the month works. A recurring calendar event works. Whatever makes it a habit rather than an intention. Sit somewhere quiet. Put your phone in airplane mode. This is not multitasking material.

For each question, do not answer with the version of yourself you want to project. Answer with the version of yourself that exists right now.

### 1. Sleep

*Am I getting seven to nine hours of sleep most nights? Do I wake feeling rested?*

This is the foundation question because sleep affects literally everything else. Insufficient sleep impairs mood regulation, increases anxiety, degrades memory and decision-making, weakens immune function, raises cortisol, increases appetite (particularly for high-calorie foods), and reduces pain tolerance. Chronic sleep deprivation is a risk factor for depression, and depression is a cause of sleep disruption --- the two create a feedback loop that spirals downward.

**What to note:** Average hours per night. Trouble falling asleep? Trouble staying asleep? Waking early and unable to return to sleep (a classic depression pattern)? Using substances (alcohol, cannabis, sleep aids) to fall asleep? How many hours between putting down your phone and attempting to sleep?

**Green:** 7-9 hours, waking rested, no sleep aids.
**Yellow:** 5-7 hours, or waking tired despite adequate time in bed.
**Red:** Under 5 hours, or significant insomnia that is affecting your daily function.

### 2. Energy

*How is my energy level? Normal, low, or running on empty?*

Energy is the output of sleep, nutrition, exercise, and psychological state combined. Low energy despite adequate sleep and nutrition suggests depression, thyroid dysfunction, anemia, or chronic stress. Low energy with poor sleep and poor nutrition has an obvious fix (see [FAD-07: Nutrition and Wellness](../07-nutrition-wellness.md)). Sustained fatigue that does not resolve with rest is a medical evaluation flag.

**What to note:** Can you get through the day without caffeine as a crutch? Are you dragging in the afternoon? Do you have energy for activities you used to enjoy?

**Green:** Normal energy, can function and engage.
**Yellow:** Noticeably lower than your baseline. Dragging through days.
**Red:** Exhausted regardless of rest. Cannot sustain basic daily activities.

### 3. Mood

*Is my mood mostly stable, mostly down, mostly anxious, or swinging unpredictably?*

Mood fluctuation is normal. Humans are not meant to be happy every day. Sadness after a loss, anxiety before an exam, irritability during a stressful week --- these are appropriate emotional responses, not pathology. The question is whether the mood state persists beyond the triggering event, or whether there is no identifiable trigger at all.

**What to note:** Predominant mood over the past month. Duration of low or anxious periods. Mood that feels disconnected from circumstances (feeling terrible when things are going well, or feeling nothing when things are going badly). Irritability that is disproportionate to the trigger. Numbness or flatness --- the absence of both positive and negative emotion, which is not calm but depression's quietest form.

**Green:** Mostly stable, with normal fluctuations tied to events.
**Yellow:** Persistent low mood, anxiety, or irritability lasting more than two weeks.
**Red:** Hopelessness, pervasive sadness, inability to experience pleasure (anhedonia), or mood swings that are affecting relationships and function.

### 4. Social Connection

*Am I talking to people I care about? Am I isolating?*

Humans are social animals. Isolation --- chosen or circumstantial --- is both a symptom of depression and a cause of its worsening. The feedback loop is vicious: depression makes social interaction feel exhausting, so you withdraw, and the withdrawal deepens the depression.

This question is not about having a busy social calendar. Introverts recharge alone, and that is healthy. The question is whether your current level of social contact meets your own needs. Are you seeing or talking to the people who matter to you? Or have you been declining invitations, not returning calls, and telling yourself you will reach out next week for the fourth consecutive week?

**What to note:** Last time you had a meaningful conversation with a friend (not a coworker about work). Last time you saw someone socially. Are you actively avoiding contact? Are you feeling lonely?

**Green:** Maintaining connections that feel adequate for your needs.
**Yellow:** Withdrawing more than usual. Declining invitations. Feeling isolated but not reaching out.
**Red:** Complete isolation. No meaningful social contact in weeks. Feeling that no one would notice or care if you disappeared.

### 5. Appetite

*Is my appetite normal? Am I stress-eating? Am I not eating?*

Appetite disruption runs in both directions. Stress and depression can suppress appetite entirely (food is unappetizing, meals are skipped, weight drops) or trigger stress-eating (compulsive consumption of comfort foods, particularly sugar and carbohydrates, eating past fullness, eating for emotional regulation rather than hunger). Either direction, sustained for more than a few weeks, is a data point.

**What to note:** Are you eating regular meals? Are you eating more or less than usual? Has your weight changed noticeably? Are you using food to manage emotions?

**Green:** Eating normally for you.
**Yellow:** Noticeable change in either direction lasting more than two weeks.
**Red:** Significant weight change (more than 5% of body weight in a month), inability to eat, or eating that feels out of control.

### 6. Substances

*Alcohol, caffeine, cannabis, nicotine, others --- am I using more than usual?*

Substance use escalation is one of the most reliable early indicators of mental health strain. It is also the one people are most reluctant to be honest about with themselves. The question is not whether you use substances --- many people do, in varying amounts, across a spectrum from harmless to destructive. The question is whether the amount or frequency has increased, and whether the increase is serving an emotional regulation function.

**What to note:** How many drinks per week? More than a month ago? Using any substance specifically to manage anxiety, insomnia, or emotional pain? Are you thinking about the substance when you are not using it? Are you defending your use to yourself ("I deserve this, it's been a hard week")?

**Green:** Stable pattern, not using to self-medicate.
**Yellow:** Noticeable increase in frequency or quantity. Using specifically to manage mood or sleep.
**Red:** Daily use to cope. Unable to stop or reduce. Using is causing problems (missed work, relationship conflict, health consequences) but continuing anyway.

### 7. Joy

*When did I last do something purely for fun? When did I last laugh?*

This question sounds trivial. It is not. Anhedonia --- the inability to experience pleasure from activities that used to be enjoyable --- is one of the cardinal symptoms of major depression. It is also one of the first to appear and one of the last to resolve. The person who used to love cooking and now just heats up whatever is fastest. The person who used to read for hours and now stares at their phone scrolling nothing. The person who used to laugh easily and now cannot remember the last time they did.

**What to note:** Last time you did something you genuinely enjoyed. Last time you laughed, not politely but genuinely. Do your hobbies feel like obligations? Do you feel like nothing sounds appealing?

**Green:** Regular enjoyment of activities, hobbies, and social time.
**Yellow:** Less enjoyment than usual. Things feel flat. Going through the motions.
**Red:** Nothing feels enjoyable. Cannot remember the last time something made you genuinely happy.

### 8. Stress

*What is the biggest source of stress in my life right now? Is it manageable?*

Everyone has stress. The question is not whether stress exists but whether the current stress load exceeds your coping capacity. Manageable stress (a busy period at work with a known end date, planning a move, studying for an exam) is uncomfortable but finite. Unmanageable stress (financial crisis with no solution in sight, a toxic work environment with no exit, a caregiving burden that is consuming your life, a relationship that is damaging you) is the kind that grinds you down over months and years.

**What to note:** Name the biggest stressor. When did it start? Is there an end in sight? Do you have a plan to address it? Have you told anyone about it? Is the stress bleeding into sleep, appetite, mood, and relationships?

**Green:** Stress is present but manageable. You have coping strategies and they are working.
**Yellow:** Stress is high and your coping strategies are straining. You are compensating (less sleep, more substances, withdrawing).
**Red:** Stress feels unmanageable. You cannot see a way through. It is affecting multiple areas of your life and you feel trapped.

## Scoring: Count Your Yellows and Reds

**Zero to two yellow flags, no red flags:** Normal fluctuation. Human life is not a flatline of wellness. Keep doing what you are doing. Check in again next month.

**Three to four yellow flags:** Something is shifting. You are not in crisis, but you are drifting away from your baseline. This is the intervention sweet spot --- small adjustments now (better sleep habits, more social contact, less alcohol, a few walks outside) can prevent the drift from becoming a slide. Consider talking to someone --- a friend, a counselor, a primary care doctor. Not because you are broken, but because you are paying attention.

**Five or more yellow flags, or any red flags:** Please reach out. This is not a sign of weakness. This is a sign that your system is overloaded and you need support. Options:

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline** --- Call or text 988. Available 24/7. Trained crisis counselors. This is not only for suicidal thoughts --- it is for anyone in emotional crisis.
- **Crisis Text Line** --- Text HOME to 741741. For people who prefer texting to calling.
- **A therapist** --- Psychology Today's therapist finder (psychologytoday.com) lets you search by location, insurance, specialty, and availability.
- **Your primary care doctor** --- They can screen for depression, anxiety, thyroid issues, and other treatable conditions. They can prescribe medication if appropriate. They can refer to specialists.
- **A trusted friend or family member** --- Sometimes the most important thing is just saying it out loud to someone who cares.

## Tracking Over Time

The real power of this check-in is the trend, not any single snapshot. One bad month is a data point. Three bad months in a row is a pattern. A month where everything was yellow followed by a month where you adjusted sleep and exercise and everything went back to green is evidence that the adjustments worked.

Keep your check-ins. A simple table works:

| Month | Sleep | Energy | Mood | Social | Appetite | Substances | Joy | Stress |
|-------|-------|--------|------|--------|----------|------------|-----|--------|
| Apr | G | Y | G | G | G | G | G | Y |
| May | | | | | | | | |

Over twelve months, you will have a personal mental health weather report. You will see your patterns. Maybe you always dip in February (seasonal affective disorder is common in the PNW, where winter daylight is eight and a half hours). Maybe you escalate substance use during work crunch periods. Maybe your social connection drops when a specific relationship is strained. Patterns give you leverage. They let you intervene before the slide.

## What This Is Not

This check-in is **not a diagnostic tool.** It does not tell you whether you have depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, or any other clinical condition. Diagnosis requires a trained clinician doing a structured assessment.

This check-in is **not a substitute for professional help.** If you score red on multiple items, the check-in's job is to get you to notice that and to reach out, not to treat you.

This check-in is **awareness.** That is all. And awareness is the prerequisite for everything else. You cannot seek help for a problem you have not noticed. You cannot adjust your habits in response to a pattern you have not tracked. You cannot tell a therapist what has changed if you never established your baseline.

Notice first. Then act.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Psychology** --- [Self-Assessment]: This check-in is a simplified version of screening tools used in clinical psychology (PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety). The eight domains map roughly to the symptom clusters that clinicians assess. The scoring is simplified for self-use, but the underlying principle --- structured self-observation against a baseline --- is sound psychological practice.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Emotional Regulation]: Recognizing your emotional state is the first step in regulating it. The check-in cultivates interoceptive awareness --- the ability to notice and accurately label internal states. People who are better at identifying their emotions (a skill called emotional granularity) manage stress more effectively and have better mental health outcomes.
- **Learning** --- [Reflective Practice]: Monthly self-assessment is reflective practice applied to personal wellness. The reflect-evaluate-adjust cycle is the same metacognitive process that drives skill improvement in any domain. You are getting better at knowing yourself, which is arguably the most important skill there is.
