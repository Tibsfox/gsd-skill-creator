# Try Session: Practice Wound Care on Yourself

## Your Next Kitchen Cut Is a Training Opportunity

You are going to get a minor cut. It is inevitable. You will slice a finger while chopping onions, scrape a knuckle while reaching behind the dryer, nick your shin on the corner of the coffee table, or peel a strip of skin off your palm with a vegetable peeler. These injuries are small, they are universal, and they are free training.

Most people's wound care protocol is something like: swear, stick the finger in their mouth, rummage for a bandage, slap it on, and hope for the best. Some add a splash of hydrogen peroxide because their mother told them to. A few skip the bandage entirely and let it bleed into their shirt.

This try session replaces that chaos with a protocol. A real protocol --- the same sequence that emergency room nurses follow for minor wound management, adapted for your kitchen counter. You will practice it the next time you get a minor cut or scrape, and by the third or fourth time, the protocol will be automatic. Your hands will know what to do before your brain finishes processing the surprise.

That is the point. Automatic protocols are the foundation of emergency response at every level, from the paramedic running a cardiac arrest to you managing a sliced fingertip. The protocol exists so that you do not have to make decisions under stress. The decisions have already been made. You just execute them.

When the cut that is not minor happens --- a deep laceration, a heavy bleed, a wound on someone else --- you will have practiced the foundation so many times that escalating to more advanced care is an extension of what you already know, not a terrifying leap into the unknown.

## The Protocol: Seven Steps

Print this. Put it on the refrigerator. Tape it inside the medicine cabinet. Keep it visible until you have used it enough times that you do not need it anymore.

### Step 1: Wash Your Hands (20 Seconds)

Before you touch the wound, wash your hands with soap and warm water for at least twenty seconds. Yes, your finger is bleeding. Yes, it seems counterintuitive to stand at the sink washing your hands while you are dripping blood. Do it anyway.

Your hands are the dirtiest tools you own. They have been touching doorknobs, phones, raw food, countertops, and a hundred other surfaces colonized with bacteria. Introducing those bacteria into an open wound is how minor cuts become infected wounds, and how infected wounds become cellulitis, abscess, or worse.

Twenty seconds is longer than you think. Sing the "Happy Birthday" song twice. Lather between your fingers, under your nails, and on the backs of your hands.

If the bleeding is severe --- pumping, soaking through a cloth in seconds --- skip this step and go directly to pressure (Step 2). But for the vast majority of minor wounds, the twenty seconds at the sink is time well spent.

### Step 2: Apply Pressure if Bleeding (5-10 Minutes)

Grab a clean gauze pad from your [first aid kit](build-home-first-aid-kit.md), a clean washcloth, or a clean paper towel. Press it firmly against the wound. Hold it there.

The key word is HOLD. Do not peek. Do not lift the gauze to see if it has stopped. Every time you lift the pressure, you disrupt the clot that is forming underneath. The clotting cascade --- the series of chemical reactions that transforms liquid blood into a solid plug --- takes time. Platelets must aggregate. Fibrin strands must form. The body is building a dam, and every time you lift the gauze to check, you are pulling bricks out of the dam before it is finished.

**For minor cuts:** Two to five minutes of continuous pressure is usually sufficient.

**For deeper cuts or cuts in highly vascular areas** (scalp, face, fingertips): Five to ten minutes. Set a timer. Do not guess. Time distorts under stress, and what feels like five minutes may be ninety seconds.

**If it soaks through:** Add more gauze on top. Do not remove the blood-soaked layer --- the clot is forming against it. Stack and maintain pressure.

**If it does not stop after fifteen minutes of continuous, firm pressure:** This wound needs professional evaluation. Keep pressure on and go to urgent care or the emergency room.

### Step 3: Clean the Wound

Once bleeding is controlled, clean the wound. This is where most people go wrong, because the most popular wound-cleaning products are the worst ones.

**Use clean running water.** Hold the wound under a faucet and let water flow over and through it for at least one minute. The mechanical flushing action of water removes dirt, debris, and bacteria more effectively than any chemical antiseptic. If the wound has visible debris (gravel, dirt, wood splinters), gently remove what you can with clean tweezers.

**Do NOT use hydrogen peroxide.** This is the myth that will not die. Hydrogen peroxide does kill bacteria --- but it also kills fibroblasts, the cells that build new tissue and close wounds. Multiple peer-reviewed studies published in the Journal of Wound Care, Annals of Emergency Medicine, and Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery have consistently demonstrated that hydrogen peroxide impairs wound healing, delays closure, and damages healthy tissue. The bubbling you see is catalase in your own cells breaking down the peroxide. It is a chemical reaction that is destroying the very cells you need to heal. Stop using it on wounds. Forever.

**Do NOT use rubbing alcohol on open wounds.** Same tissue damage, plus significant pain for no clinical benefit.

**Soap is fine** for the skin around the wound. Gentle soap and water on the intact skin surrounding the wound helps remove surface contamination. Do not scrub soap directly into the wound bed.

### Step 4: Assess the Wound

Now look at the wound with clean, controlled eyes. This is the decision point. Ask three questions:

**How deep is it?** Can you see fat (yellow, globular tissue below the skin)? Can you see muscle (red, fibrous)? Can you see bone (white, hard)? If yes to any of these, this wound needs professional care --- likely stitches, possibly exploration to check for tendon or nerve damage.

**Do the edges close on their own?** Gently press the skin on either side of the wound together. If the edges come together neatly and stay together, the wound can be closed with butterfly closures or Steri-Strips at home. If the edges gape apart, or if the wound is jagged with flaps of skin, professional closure (stitches or staples) will produce a better outcome.

**Where is it?** Wounds over joints (knuckles, elbows, knees) are harder to manage at home because movement constantly reopens them. Wounds on the face should generally be evaluated for stitches because of cosmetic concerns. Wounds on the hands should be evaluated if there is any loss of finger movement or sensation, which could indicate tendon or nerve injury.

For most minor kitchen cuts and household scrapes, the answer to all three questions is: not deep, edges close, and on a surface that is not over a joint. These wounds do well with home care.

### Step 5: Close the Wound (If Needed)

If the wound edges need help staying together, use butterfly closures or Steri-Strips.

**Dry the skin** around the wound first --- adhesive does not stick to wet skin. Pat gently with a clean gauze pad.

**Apply the first strip** across the wound, perpendicular to the cut, pulling the edges gently together. Not overlapping onto the wound --- the strip crosses over the wound, with adhesive on the intact skin on each side.

**Add additional strips** every quarter inch along the wound length. Each strip pulls the edges into alignment for that section.

**Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment** between the strips, on the wound itself. Then cover the entire area with a non-stick pad or adhesive bandage.

Leave the strips in place for five to seven days. They will peel off on their own as the skin underneath heals. Do not pull them off early.

### Step 6: Bandage

For wounds that do not need closure strips, the standard bandage protocol is:

1. Apply a thin layer of triple antibiotic ointment to the wound bed. Thin layer --- a translucent smear, not a glob.
2. Cover with a non-stick pad (for larger wounds) or an appropriately sized adhesive bandage (for smaller ones).
3. Secure the pad with medical tape on all four sides if using gauze, or press the adhesive wings firmly onto dry skin if using a bandage.

**Change the bandage daily** or whenever it gets wet. Each time: remove the old bandage, check the wound, clean gently with water if needed, reapply ointment, and apply a fresh bandage. Moist wound healing (with ointment and a bandage) is consistently better than dry wound healing (exposed to air) in terms of speed, pain, and scarring. The old advice to "let it air out" is wrong. Cover it. Keep it moist.

### Step 7: Watch for Infection

For the next seven to ten days, monitor the wound at each bandage change. You are looking for five signs of infection:

**Increasing redness.** Some redness around a wound is normal inflammatory response. Redness that is expanding --- especially red streaks radiating outward from the wound along the direction of blood vessels --- is a sign of spreading infection (cellulitis or lymphangitis). The red streak pattern is an urgent sign. See a doctor the same day.

**Warmth.** An infected wound feels warmer than the surrounding skin. Compare by touching the wound area and then touching nearby uninjured skin with the back of your hand.

**Swelling.** Some swelling is normal. Increasing swelling over days, especially with tenderness, suggests infection.

**Pus.** Clear or slightly yellow drainage from a wound is normal (serous fluid). Thick, opaque, white, yellow, or green drainage with an odor is pus --- the hallmark of bacterial infection.

**Fever.** A wound infection that has progressed to systemic infection causes fever (temperature above 100.4 degrees F or 38 degrees C). Fever with a wound means the infection has moved beyond the wound into the bloodstream. Seek medical care promptly.

**The rule:** If one sign is present, watch closely and clean more frequently. If two or more signs are present, see a doctor. If red streaks or fever are present, see a doctor today.

## When to See a Doctor

Some wounds exceed home first aid capability. Go to urgent care or the emergency room for:

- **Deep wounds** that expose fat, muscle, tendon, or bone
- **Wounds that will not stop bleeding** after fifteen minutes of continuous pressure
- **Wounds on the face** (cosmetic closure consideration)
- **Wounds over joints** that gape with movement
- **Puncture wounds** from rusty or dirty objects (tetanus concern --- if your last tetanus booster was more than five years ago, you may need a booster)
- **Animal bites** --- all animal bites should be evaluated for rabies risk and infection risk, regardless of how minor they appear
- **Human bites** --- human mouth bacteria cause aggressive wound infections
- **Signs of infection** (see above)
- **Any wound in a person with diabetes, immunosuppression, or poor circulation** --- these conditions impair healing and increase infection risk

## Building the Automatic Response

The first time you use this protocol, it will feel slow and deliberate. You will check the list. You will second-guess whether you cleaned long enough. You will wonder if you need butterfly closures or just a bandage. This is normal.

By the third or fourth minor wound, you will not need the list. Your hands will wash automatically. You will grab gauze and apply pressure without thinking. You will clean under running water by reflex. You will assess depth, edges, and location in a glance. You will bandage efficiently and change it on schedule.

This is procedural memory. It is the same mechanism by which a pianist plays scales without thinking, a driver shifts gears without looking, and a paramedic starts an IV in a moving ambulance. The protocol becomes part of you through repetition. And when the wound that is not minor happens --- a deep cut during a woodworking project, a bleeding injury on someone else, an accident on the trail --- the foundation is already in place. You scale up from a base of competence rather than starting from zero.

Every kitchen cut is a rehearsal for the real thing. Do not waste them.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Microbiology and Wound Healing]: The four phases of wound healing --- hemostasis (clot formation), inflammation (immune cell recruitment), proliferation (new tissue growth by fibroblasts), and remodeling (collagen reorganization over months) --- explain why every step of this protocol matters. Pressure supports hemostasis. Cleaning reduces bacterial load before inflammation begins. Moist bandaging optimizes the environment for proliferation.
- **Home Economics** --- [Self-Care]: Managing your own minor injuries competently is a basic self-sufficiency skill. Knowing when you can handle it at home and when you need professional care is the judgment that turns a cut finger from a trip to the ER into a five-minute first aid exercise.
- **Learning** --- [Procedural Memory]: This try session is explicitly designed to build procedural memory through repeated real-world practice. Each minor wound is a repetition of the protocol, encoding the sequence deeper into motor memory. Under stress, procedural memory persists when declarative memory fails.
