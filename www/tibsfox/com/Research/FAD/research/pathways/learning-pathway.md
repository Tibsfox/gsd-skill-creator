# FAD Learning Pathway: From Bystander to First Responder-Ready

A structured learning journey through first aid and health literacy --- the knowledge that transforms an ordinary person into someone who can keep people alive, manage everyday health challenges, and make informed decisions about their own body. You do not need a medical degree. You do not need to be brave or strong or unflappable. You need to be willing to learn, because the person bleeding on the kitchen floor does not care about your GPA. They care that you showed up and you know what to do.

This pathway organizes all FAD research documents, try-sessions, and study-guides into a coherent sequence from basic awareness through community preparedness and formal certification. Each level is self-contained --- completing Level 1 alone could save a life. Each level builds on the previous one. The total journey from bystander to certified first responder-ready takes weeks to months, but the first hour is the most important hour.

**Total estimated time:** 30-50 hours across all five levels (some levels require in-person training)
**Prerequisites:** None. Being a human who lives in a body and shares space with other humans is the only prerequisite.
**College of Knowledge connections:** Science, Mind-Body, Physical Education, Home Economics, Psychology, Learning, Nature Studies

**Important note:** This pathway covers knowledge and orientation. Some skills (CPR, Stop the Bleed, wound care under stress) benefit enormously from in-person practice. The try-sessions prepare you for training and reinforce it afterward, but they complement formal courses --- they do not replace them.

## Level 1 --- Aware (1 hour)

*"If something happens, I want to know what to do in the first sixty seconds."*

Level 1 covers the absolute essentials. The framework for any emergency. The one physical skill that keeps unconscious people alive. And the project that turns knowledge into tangible preparedness. One hour of reading and practice, and you are more prepared than 90% of the population.

### Reading

1. **[01-first-aid-foundations.md](../01-first-aid-foundations.md)** --- The foundation of everything. Scene safety, PPE, consent, the assess-call-care framework, when not to move someone, Good Samaritan laws, and the recovery position. This is the document that gives you the mental model for responding to any emergency. Every other document in the series builds on this one. (~20 min)

### Try-Sessions

2. **[try-sessions/learn-recovery-position.md](../try-sessions/learn-recovery-position.md)** --- Practice the recovery position with a partner. Ten minutes of hands-on practice. The single most useful physical skill for protecting an unconscious breathing person. You will practice from both sides, take turns, and build the muscle memory that fires under stress. (~15 min)

3. **[try-sessions/build-home-first-aid-kit.md](../try-sessions/build-home-first-aid-kit.md)** --- Build a complete home first aid kit for $30-50. Every item explained: what it does, why it is there, and how to use it. The kit is useless without the knowledge; the knowledge is less useful without the kit. Build both. (~25 min reading, 1 hour shopping/assembling)

### What You'll Learn
- The assess-call-care framework that applies to every emergency
- Scene safety: how to protect yourself before helping others
- Consent: why and how to ask
- When to call 911 and how to communicate effectively with dispatch
- The recovery position for unconscious breathing patients
- How to build and stock a first aid kit

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Assess a scene for safety before approaching
- Check a person for responsiveness and breathing
- Call 911 with the information dispatchers need
- Place an unconscious breathing person in the recovery position
- Locate and use the supplies in your home first aid kit

### College Connections
- **Science:** Basic airway anatomy (tongue position, aspiration risk)
- **Mind-Body:** Managing the stress response during emergencies
- **Home Economics:** Household preparedness and kit building
- **Learning:** Procedural memory through physical practice

## Level 2 --- Basic (1 day)

*"I want to handle the injuries I am most likely to encounter."*

Level 2 moves from the general framework to specific skills. Wound care is the most common first aid task. Vital signs tell you how the body is doing. Medical emergencies are the scenarios that kill people fastest. One full day of reading and practice covers the ground that matters most.

### Reading

4. **[02-wound-care.md](../02-wound-care.md)** --- The complete wound care document: minor wounds, deep lacerations, bleeding control, direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquets, burns (thermal, chemical, electrical), nosebleeds, and eye injuries. From band-aids to battlefield. (~30 min)

### Try-Session

5. **[try-sessions/wound-care-practice.md](../try-sessions/wound-care-practice.md)** --- Use your next minor cut or scrape to practice the seven-step wound care protocol: wash hands, apply pressure, clean wound, assess, close if needed, bandage, and monitor for infection. Each kitchen cut is a training opportunity. (~15 min reading, ongoing practice)

### Study Guide

6. **[study-guides/vital-signs-reference.md](../study-guides/vital-signs-reference.md)** --- The five vital signs: pulse, breathing, blood pressure, temperature, and skin. How to measure each one, what the numbers mean, and when to worry. Includes the AVPU scale for rapid neurological assessment and a 15-question self-quiz. Take your own vitals right now as you read. (~30 min)

### Reading

7. **[04-medical-emergencies.md](../04-medical-emergencies.md)** --- Heart attack, stroke, seizure, diabetic emergency, anaphylaxis, choking, poisoning, and opioid overdose. The time-critical emergencies where bystander action in the first minutes determines whether the person lives or dies. Includes the FAST mnemonic for stroke and the cardiac aspirin protocol. (~30 min)

### Cross-Reference: FSR

8. **[FSR try-session: hands-only CPR](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md)** --- Hands-only CPR: the skill that doubles or triples cardiac arrest survival. Call 911, push hard and fast on the center of the chest. Practice on a pillow. Then take an in-person CPR class. (~30 min reading + practice)

### What You'll Learn
- The full wound care spectrum from minor cuts to hemorrhage control
- How to take and interpret vital signs
- Recognition patterns for heart attack, stroke, seizure, anaphylaxis, and choking
- The cardiac aspirin protocol
- Hands-only CPR technique and rationale

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Clean, assess, close, and bandage a wound using proper technique
- Take a pulse, count respiratory rate, and assess skin signs
- Recognize a heart attack and administer aspirin
- Recognize a stroke using FAST and call 911 within seconds
- Perform the Heimlich maneuver on a choking adult
- Perform hands-only CPR on a cardiac arrest patient

### College Connections
- **Science:** Wound healing biology, cardiovascular physiology, pharmacology of aspirin
- **Learning:** Protocol-based decision-making (FAST, check-call-care)
- **Physical Education:** CPR as a physical skill requiring endurance and correct form

## Level 3 --- Prepared (1 week)

*"I want to be ready for more situations and take better care of myself."*

Level 3 broadens from emergency response to comprehensive health preparedness. Musculoskeletal injuries, environmental hazards, medication safety, mental health awareness, and the PNW-specific risks that define life in this region. This level also introduces the mental health check-in --- because caring for yourself is the foundation for caring for others.

### Study Guide

9. **[study-guides/medication-safety-reference.md](../study-guides/medication-safety-reference.md)** --- The eight OTC medications everyone should understand: ibuprofen, acetaminophen, aspirin, diphenhydramine, loperamide, antacids, hydrocortisone, and triple antibiotic. Dose, max daily, interactions, and who should NOT take each one. The golden rule: read the label, every time. (~25 min)

### Reading

10. **[03-musculoskeletal.md](../03-musculoskeletal.md)** --- Fractures, sprains, strains, dislocations, and splinting. RICE protocol. When to call 911 versus when to drive to the ER. Workshop-specific injury patterns for the woodworker and maker. (~25 min)

### Try-Session

11. **[try-sessions/mental-health-check-in.md](../try-sessions/mental-health-check-in.md)** --- The monthly mental health check-in. Eight questions about sleep, energy, mood, social connection, appetite, substances, joy, and stress. Fifteen minutes of honest self-assessment that can catch a downward trend before it becomes a crisis. Start this month and track over time. (~20 min first session, 15 min monthly thereafter)

### Reading

12. **[05-environmental.md](../05-environmental.md)** --- Hypothermia, heat illness, drowning, lightning, animal bites, tick-borne disease, wildfire smoke, and AQI monitoring. The environmental hazards of Pacific Northwest life, from trail to backyard. (~30 min)

13. **[10-pnw-health-hazards.md](../10-pnw-health-hazards.md)** --- PNW-specific hazards: Cascadia earthquake injuries, death cap mushrooms, shellfish toxin, volcanic ashfall and lahars, marine hazards. The risks unique to this region and the first aid responses specific to them. (~25 min)

### What You'll Learn
- Safe medication use and critical drug interactions
- Musculoskeletal injury assessment and splinting
- Mental health self-monitoring and when to seek help
- Environmental emergency recognition and treatment
- PNW-specific hazards and their first aid management

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Choose the correct OTC medication for a situation and use it safely
- Splint a suspected fracture and apply RICE for a sprain
- Conduct a monthly mental health self-assessment
- Recognize and treat hypothermia, heat illness, and smoke exposure
- Identify PNW-specific risks (death cap mushrooms, earthquake injuries, lahar evacuation)

### College Connections
- **Science:** Pharmacology, thermoregulation, toxicology
- **Psychology:** Self-assessment, emotional awareness
- **Mind-Body:** Stress management, emotional regulation
- **Nature Studies:** Wildlife encounters, environmental hazards, regional ecology
- **Geography:** Cascadia Subduction Zone, volcanic hazard zones, wildfire geography

## Level 4 --- Capable (1 month)

*"I want to handle serious emergencies and build lasting wellness habits."*

Level 4 integrates emergency skills with wellness foundations. CPR certification, Stop the Bleed training, mental health literacy, nutrition and wellness habits, and the go bag that prepares you for the disaster that is not a question of if. This level requires in-person training for the certification skills.

### In-Person Training

14. **Complete a hands-only CPR course.** The Red Cross and American Heart Association offer courses for $60-100, typically 4-8 hours. The [FSR hands-only CPR try-session](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md) prepared you. Now practice on mannequins with instructor feedback.

15. **Complete a Stop the Bleed course.** Free, 1-2 hours, available at hospitals and fire departments (stopthebleed.org). The [FSR Stop the Bleed try-session](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md) introduced the concepts. Now practice tourniquet application and wound packing hands-on.

### Reading

16. **[06-mental-health-basics.md](../06-mental-health-basics.md)** --- Mental health first aid for others. Recognizing crisis, suicide intervention (ask directly), panic attacks, de-escalation, the ALGEE action plan, and caring for the caregiver. The companion document to [FSR-08 mental health crisis response](../../FSR/research/08-mental-health-crisis.md). (~30 min)

17. **[07-nutrition-wellness.md](../07-nutrition-wellness.md)** --- Sleep, nutrition, exercise, hydration, and stress management. The foundational inputs that prevent most of the emergencies described in the rest of the series. Unglamorous, evidence-based, and effective. (~25 min)

### Cross-Reference: FSR

18. **[FSR try-session: Build a Go Bag](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md)** --- The 72-hour kit you grab when you need to leave your home in a hurry. Earthquake, wildfire, flood, extended power outage. Includes trauma supplies that complement your first aid training. (~2-3 hours to build)

### What You'll Learn
- CPR technique with mannequin practice and instructor correction
- Tourniquet application and wound packing under realistic conditions
- Mental health crisis intervention: the conversation that saves lives
- Nutrition, sleep, and exercise as preventive medicine
- Go bag contents and disaster preparedness principles

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Perform CPR with certification-level competence
- Apply a tourniquet in under 30 seconds
- Have the direct conversation with someone who may be suicidal
- Make evidence-based dietary and lifestyle choices
- Evacuate your home in under 5 minutes with essential supplies

### College Connections
- **Physical Education:** CPR as physical performance, exercise prescription
- **Psychology:** Crisis intervention, suicide intervention theory
- **Mind-Body:** Stress management, the cortisol cycle, breathwork
- **Home Economics:** Nutrition planning, household disaster preparedness

## Level 5 --- Certified (Ongoing)

*"I want formal training, recognized credentials, and the ability to serve my community."*

Level 5 is the bridge between civilian first aider and trained volunteer. Formal certification courses provide the structured training, supervised practice, and credentials that enable deeper community involvement. This level has no end date --- certification requires renewal, skills require maintenance, and the learning never stops.

### Certifications

19. **American Red Cross First Aid/CPR/AED.** ~$80, one day. The most widely recognized first aid certification in the United States. Covers adult/child/infant CPR, AED use, choking, and comprehensive first aid. Valid for two years. This is the standard. Get it.

20. **Stop the Bleed Course.** Free, 1-2 hours. If you have not taken it in Level 4, take it now. If you have, consider becoming a Stop the Bleed instructor --- the course trains instructors as well as participants.

21. **Mental Health First Aid (MHFA).** 8 hours, typically one day. The ALGEE action plan for mental health crisis. Offered through community organizations, employers, and public health agencies. This is the certification referenced throughout FAD-06 --- the structured training that makes you a competent mental health bystander.

22. **Consider CERT.** The Community Emergency Response Team (CERT) program is a free, 20-hour training offered by local fire departments and emergency management agencies. It teaches disaster preparedness, light search and rescue, fire suppression, triage, and team organization. See the [FSR CERT overview](../../FSR/research/try-sessions/neighborhood-cert-overview.md) for details.

### Reading

23. **[08-preventive-health.md](../08-preventive-health.md)** --- Vaccinations, health screenings, dental care, hearing protection, vision care, and the proactive health maintenance schedule. The ounce of prevention that is worth the pound of cure. (~25 min)

24. **[09-first-aid-kits.md](../09-first-aid-kits.md)** --- The full kit document: home, car, hiking, workshop, convention, and trauma kits. Six environment-specific kits with complete item lists, costs, and rationale. Build the kits that match your life. (~25 min)

25. **[11-cross-references-bibliography.md](../11-cross-references-bibliography.md)** --- The complete cross-reference map connecting FAD to the broader research series, plus the bibliography of recommended texts, certification resources, PNW-specific resources, and online databases. Your continued learning roadmap. (~20 min)

### Advanced Options

26. **NOLS Wilderness First Aid.** $300-400, 2 days. For hikers, backpackers, paddlers, climbers, and anyone who spends time more than an hour from a hospital. The PNW-specific value of this course is enormous --- the Cascades, the Olympics, and the coast are beautiful but remote.

27. **NOLS Wilderness First Responder (WFR).** $700-900, 9-10 days. The gold standard for backcountry medical training. Extended patient assessment, improvised treatment, evacuation decision-making. For outdoor trip leaders, guides, and serious recreationists.

### What You'll Learn
- Certification-level first aid, CPR, and AED operation
- Mental health first aid: the ALGEE framework in practice
- Community emergency response and volunteer coordination
- Preventive health maintenance for lifelong wellness
- Wilderness medicine for backcountry preparedness

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Respond to any common emergency with trained, certified competence
- Serve as a community resource during disasters (CERT)
- Recognize mental health crisis and provide initial intervention (MHFA)
- Maintain your own health proactively through screenings, vaccines, and lifestyle
- Manage injuries and illness in wilderness settings hours from a hospital

### College Connections
- **Science:** Advanced anatomy, physiology, pharmacology, immunology
- **Physical Education:** Wilderness fitness, outdoor preparedness, WFR physical demands
- **Psychology:** MHFA certification, crisis intervention practice
- **Learning:** Structured curriculum, certification renewal, lifelong skill maintenance
- **Nature Studies:** Wilderness medicine, environmental hazard management

## The Pathway Is Not Linear

This document presents five levels in sequence because that is the most useful structure for someone starting from zero. But real learning is not linear. You will loop back. You will reread FAD-01 after taking a CPR class and see things you missed. You will practice the recovery position with your kids after building the first aid kit. You will reference the medication guide every time you open a new bottle.

The levels are also not gates. You do not need to complete Level 2 before starting Level 3. If mental health is your urgent concern, jump to the mental health check-in and FAD-06 now. If you are going hiking next weekend, jump to FAD-05 and FAD-10 now. The pathway is a suggested route, not a required sequence.

What matters is forward motion. Any document read, any skill practiced, any kit built, any course taken moves you from bystander toward capable. And capable is all anyone needs to be.

The ambulance is eight minutes away. In those eight minutes, you are the help.

Start with Level 1. One hour. Today.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Biology, Chemistry, Physiology]: The scientific foundation runs through every level: anatomy in Level 1, wound healing biology in Level 2, pharmacology in Level 3, cardiovascular physiology in Level 4, immunology in Level 5.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Stress Response, Emotional Regulation]: From managing your own panic in Level 1 to mental health self-assessment in Level 3 to crisis intervention in Level 4, the mind-body thread is continuous.
- **Home Economics** --- [Household Preparedness]: Kit building in Level 1, medication management in Level 3, go bags in Level 4. The prepared household is the foundation of the prepared community.
- **Physical Education** --- [Skills, Fitness, Outdoor Preparedness]: CPR technique, wilderness fitness, and the physical demands of sustained emergency response.
- **Psychology** --- [Self-Assessment, Crisis Intervention]: From the monthly check-in through suicide intervention to MHFA certification, psychological literacy grows with each level.
- **Learning** --- [Procedural Memory, Certification, Lifelong Learning]: Every level builds skills through practice, and Level 5 formalizes that learning through certification and renewal.
- **Nature Studies** --- [Environmental Hazards, Wildlife, Regional Ecology]: PNW-specific risks in Level 3, wilderness medicine in Level 5. Knowing the land is knowing the risks.
