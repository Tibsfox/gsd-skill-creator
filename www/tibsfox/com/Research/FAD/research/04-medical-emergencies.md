# When the Body Fails — Medical Emergencies

## The Emergencies That Come Without Warning

Trauma — a car accident, a fall, a cut — gives you a visible cause and a visible injury. You can see what happened. Medical emergencies are different. They come from inside, often without warning, and the person who was fine a moment ago is suddenly, terrifyingly, not fine.

A coworker goes pale and grabs their chest. A dinner companion's face sags on one side. A stranger at the park begins convulsing. Your grandmother calls and says she feels "funny" and cannot describe how. A child at a birthday party clutches their throat and cannot speak.

These moments are disorienting because they lack the narrative of trauma. Nothing hit this person. Nothing fell on them. The threat is invisible, internal, and often life-threatening on a timeline measured in minutes. Heart attacks kill heart muscle in real time. Strokes destroy brain tissue at a rate of roughly 1.9 million neurons per minute. Anaphylaxis can close an airway completely in under ten minutes. A choking victim who cannot breathe has perhaps four minutes before brain damage begins.

The good news: for each of these emergencies, there are specific, learnable, executable interventions that a bystander can perform that dramatically improve outcomes. The bad news: the window for those interventions is small, and it closes fast.

This document is about recognizing what is happening and acting within that window.

## Heart Attack — The Chest That Tells You Something Is Wrong

A heart attack (myocardial infarction) occurs when blood flow to a section of the heart muscle is blocked, usually by a blood clot forming at the site of a ruptured atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery. The blocked section of heart muscle begins to die. The amount of muscle that dies — and therefore the severity of the heart attack and the likelihood of survival — depends on which artery is blocked, where the blockage is, and how quickly blood flow is restored.

Time is muscle. Every minute the artery remains blocked, more heart tissue dies. This is why the hospital treatment for heart attack (percutaneous coronary intervention — threading a catheter to the blockage and inflating a balloon to reopen the artery) is measured against a door-to-balloon time goal of ninety minutes. But that clock starts at the hospital door. The real clock starts when the heart attack begins, and the minutes between symptom onset and hospital arrival are minutes of heart muscle dying.

### Classic Signs

- **Chest pain or pressure** — often described as squeezing, tightness, heaviness, or "an elephant sitting on my chest." The pain is typically central (behind the sternum) and may radiate. It does not change with breathing or position.
- **Radiation to the left arm, jaw, neck, or back** — the pain may extend to the left arm (sometimes both arms), the jaw, the neck, or between the shoulder blades.
- **Shortness of breath** — difficulty breathing that may occur with or without chest pain.
- **Nausea or vomiting** — sometimes the dominant symptom, particularly in women.
- **Cold sweats** — diaphoresis (profuse sweating) that is out of proportion to activity or temperature.
- **Lightheadedness or dizziness.**
- **A sense of doom** — patients frequently describe an overwhelming feeling that something terrible is happening. This is not anxiety. It is a physiological signal that the body is in crisis.

### Atypical Presentation — Especially in Women

Women, elderly individuals, and people with diabetes are more likely to present with atypical heart attack symptoms. This matters because atypical presentation leads to delayed recognition, delayed treatment, and worse outcomes.

In women, the most common heart attack symptoms may include:

- **Unusual fatigue** — sometimes for days before the event, a fatigue that is not explained by activity or sleep deprivation
- **Back pain** — especially upper back, between the shoulder blades
- **Nausea, vomiting, or indigestion** — sometimes mistaken for a stomach problem
- **Shortness of breath without chest pain**
- **Jaw pain** — sometimes the only pain symptom
- **Dizziness or lightheadedness**

The classic "clutching the chest" presentation that everyone recognizes from movies is more common in men. Women are more likely to describe their symptoms as pressure, tightness, or discomfort rather than pain, and are more likely to have symptoms that mimic other conditions. The result: women take longer to seek care for heart attacks, are more likely to be misdiagnosed, and have higher in-hospital mortality rates.

### What to Do

1. **Call 911 immediately.** Do not drive to the hospital. Do not wait to see if the symptoms improve. Do not let the person talk you out of calling because they think it is "probably nothing." Call 911.
2. **Have the person sit or lie in a comfortable position.** Sitting upright or semi-reclined (propped up on pillows) often eases breathing.
3. **Give aspirin.** If the person is not allergic to aspirin and has no history of aspirin sensitivity, give them one regular aspirin (325mg) and tell them to chew it, not swallow it whole. Chewing gets the aspirin into the bloodstream faster. Aspirin inhibits platelet aggregation — it makes the blood less sticky and can help prevent the clot from getting larger. This is a medical intervention that bystanders can perform that has a measurable impact on heart attack survival.
4. **Be ready for CPR.** Heart attacks can cause cardiac arrest — the heart stops beating effectively. If the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing normally, begin CPR immediately. If an AED (automated external defibrillator) is available, use it. AEDs are designed for bystander use: turn it on, follow the voice prompts, attach the pads, and let the machine analyze and decide whether to deliver a shock.
5. **Do not give them water.** If they need surgery or other interventions at the hospital, having fluid in the stomach increases aspiration risk during sedation.

## Stroke — FAST and the Race Against Neurons

A stroke occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is interrupted. There are two types:

**Ischemic stroke** (approximately 87% of all strokes) — a blood clot blocks an artery supplying the brain. This is the type that can be treated with clot-busting drugs (tPA — tissue plasminogen activator) or mechanical thrombectomy (physically removing the clot with a catheter). The treatment window for tPA is generally 4.5 hours from symptom onset. The treatment window for thrombectomy may extend to 24 hours in some cases.

**Hemorrhagic stroke** (approximately 13%) — a blood vessel in the brain ruptures, bleeding into brain tissue. Treatment is surgical and the urgency is extreme.

In both cases, brain tissue is dying. The phrase "time is brain" is not a slogan — it is neurology. Every minute without treatment, approximately 1.9 million neurons, 14 billion synapses, and 7.5 miles of myelinated nerve fibers are destroyed.

### FAST

The FAST mnemonic is the gold standard for bystander stroke recognition:

**Face drooping.** Ask the person to smile. Does one side of the face droop or feel numb? Is the smile uneven?

**Arm weakness.** Ask the person to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward? Can they not raise one arm at all?

**Speech slurred.** Ask the person to repeat a simple sentence ("The sky is blue"). Is their speech slurred, garbled, or incomprehensible? Can they speak at all?

**Time to call 911.** If any of these signs are present, call 911 immediately.

### The Critical Detail: Note the Time

When you call 911 for a suspected stroke, the dispatcher will ask when the symptoms started. This is not a casual question. The answer determines whether the person is eligible for clot-busting treatment. If you witnessed the onset — you were talking to them and they suddenly could not speak — note the exact time. If you found them with symptoms already present, the "last known well" time (the last time someone can confirm they were normal) becomes the reference point.

Write the time down. Tell the paramedics. Tell the ER staff. This single piece of information can determine whether the patient receives treatment that saves brain tissue or is outside the window and can only be managed supportively.

### Other Stroke Signs

Beyond FAST, additional signs of stroke include:

- Sudden severe headache with no known cause (especially suggestive of hemorrhagic stroke)
- Sudden confusion or difficulty understanding speech
- Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
- Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, or loss of balance
- Sudden numbness or weakness in the face, arm, or leg, especially on one side of the body

"Sudden" is the key word. Strokes do not develop gradually over days. They hit like a switch being thrown.

## Seizures — When the Brain Misfires

A seizure is an episode of abnormal, excessive electrical activity in the brain. Seizures have many causes: epilepsy, high fever (febrile seizures in children), head trauma, low blood sugar, drug or alcohol withdrawal, eclampsia in pregnancy, stroke, brain tumor, infection, and others.

A generalized tonic-clonic seizure (formerly called a grand mal seizure) is the type most likely to be witnessed by a bystander. It involves:

- **Tonic phase:** The person becomes rigid, may cry out (this is air being forced through the vocal cords by the tonic contraction of chest muscles — it is not a conscious cry), and falls to the ground. They may stop breathing briefly.
- **Clonic phase:** Rhythmic jerking of the extremities, jaw clenching, sometimes loss of bladder or bowel control, drooling or frothing at the mouth.
- **Postictal phase:** The seizure ends and the person is confused, drowsy, sometimes combative, and may have a headache. This phase can last minutes to hours.

### What to Do

1. **Protect the person from injury.** Clear the area around them of anything hard or sharp. If they are near a staircase, furniture edges, or traffic, move hazards away from them if possible, or guide them away from the hazard if you can do so safely.
2. **Cushion their head.** Place something soft (a folded jacket, a towel, your hands) under their head to prevent it from striking the ground repeatedly.
3. **Do NOT restrain the person.** Do not hold their arms or legs down. Do not sit on them. Restraining a person during a seizure risks injury to them and to you, and it does not shorten the seizure.
4. **Do NOT put anything in their mouth.** This is one of the most persistent myths in first aid. The belief that a seizing person will "swallow their tongue" is anatomically impossible — the tongue is attached. Putting a wallet, a spoon, a stick, or your fingers in a seizing person's mouth risks breaking their teeth, lacerating their gums, and getting your finger bitten off. Jaw muscles during a tonic-clonic seizure can exert tremendous force.
5. **Time the seizure.** Look at a clock or start a timer. The duration of the seizure is critical information for medical providers. Most seizures last one to three minutes. A seizure lasting longer than five minutes (status epilepticus) is a medical emergency requiring immediate medication.
6. **Turn them on their side after the seizure ends.** Once the jerking stops and the person is in the postictal phase, place them in the recovery position to keep the airway clear.
7. **Stay with them.** The postictal phase can be frightening and disorienting. Speak calmly, tell them where they are, what happened, and that they are safe. Do not offer food or water until they are fully alert and can swallow safely.

### When to Call 911 for a Seizure

- The seizure lasts longer than five minutes
- The person does not regain consciousness after the seizure
- A second seizure occurs without full recovery from the first
- The person is injured during the seizure
- The person has no known history of seizures (this is their first)
- The seizure occurs in water
- The person is pregnant
- The person has diabetes (seizure may indicate severe hypoglycemia)
- You are unsure

If you know the person has epilepsy, has a seizure action plan, and the seizure follows their typical pattern and duration, calling 911 may not be necessary — follow their plan. But when in doubt, call.

## Diabetic Emergencies

Diabetes affects how the body regulates blood sugar (glucose). Both dangerously low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) and dangerously high blood sugar (hyperglycemia) can cause altered consciousness, and distinguishing between them in the field can be challenging.

### Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia)

Caused by too much insulin, missed meals, excessive exercise, or alcohol consumption in a person with diabetes. Onset is rapid — minutes to hours.

Signs: confusion, shakiness, sweating (often profuse), pale skin, rapid heartbeat, irritability, difficulty speaking, stumbling (may appear intoxicated), hunger, anxiety, seizures, loss of consciousness.

**What to do if the person is conscious and can swallow:** Give sugar. Glucose tablets (preferred), fruit juice, regular soda (not diet), candy, honey, or sugar packets. The goal is 15-20 grams of fast-acting sugar. After fifteen minutes, reassess. If they are not improving, give more sugar and call 911.

**What to do if the person is unconscious:** Call 911. Do NOT attempt to give food or liquid to an unconscious person — aspiration risk. Place in recovery position. If the person has a glucagon emergency kit and you have been trained to use it, administer it (glucagon injection or nasal spray). If they do not have one, wait for EMS.

### High Blood Sugar (Hyperglycemia/Diabetic Ketoacidosis)

Caused by insufficient insulin, illness, infection, or missed diabetes medication. Onset is gradual — hours to days.

Signs: excessive thirst, frequent urination, fruity or acetone-smelling breath (this is distinctive — the smell of ketones), nausea or vomiting, abdominal pain, confusion, deep and rapid breathing (Kussmaul breathing — the body trying to blow off acid), altered consciousness.

**What to do:** Call 911. This is a medical emergency requiring IV fluids and insulin in a hospital setting. There is no effective bystander intervention for severe hyperglycemia beyond calling for help and monitoring the person.

### The Practical Rule

If you encounter a person with known diabetes who is altered (confused, unresponsive, behaving abnormally) and you are unsure whether the problem is high or low blood sugar: **if they are conscious and can swallow, give sugar.** The reasoning: if their sugar is low, the sugar will help. If their sugar is high, a small amount of additional sugar will not significantly worsen the situation in the short term — they are already hyperglycemic, and the small additional glucose load is inconsequential compared to the harm of untreated hypoglycemia. Call 911 regardless.

## Severe Allergic Reaction — Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is a severe, systemic allergic reaction that can progress to airway closure, cardiovascular collapse, and death within minutes. Common triggers include foods (peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs), insect stings (bees, wasps, hornets), medications (penicillin, NSAIDs, anesthetics), and latex.

Signs of anaphylaxis (multiple body systems are typically involved):

- **Skin:** Hives, flushing, swelling (especially face, lips, tongue, throat), itching
- **Respiratory:** Difficulty breathing, wheezing, stridor (high-pitched breathing sound indicating upper airway narrowing), throat tightness, hoarseness
- **Cardiovascular:** Rapid pulse, low blood pressure, dizziness, fainting
- **Gastrointestinal:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps
- **Neurological:** Anxiety, confusion, sense of doom

### Epinephrine — The Life-Saving Drug

Epinephrine (adrenaline) is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis. It works by constricting blood vessels (raising blood pressure), relaxing airway smooth muscle (opening the airways), and reducing the immune response. It works fast and it saves lives.

Epinephrine auto-injectors (EpiPen, Auvi-Q, generic versions) are prescribed to people with known severe allergies. Increasingly, they are also available in public settings (schools, airports, restaurants) and some states allow pharmacists to dispense them without a prescription.

How to use an epinephrine auto-injector:

1. Remove the safety cap.
2. Hold the injector in your fist with the tip (orange end on an EpiPen) against the outer thigh — the large muscle of the mid-thigh.
3. Push firmly until you hear or feel the click of the needle deploying.
4. Hold for ten seconds.
5. Remove and note the time of injection.
6. **Call 911 even after administering epinephrine.** The effects of epinephrine are temporary (fifteen to twenty minutes), and anaphylaxis can rebound. The person needs hospital monitoring for at least four to six hours after an anaphylactic reaction, even if they feel completely better after the epi.

The injection goes through clothing. You do not need to remove pants. The thigh is the preferred site because it is a large muscle with good blood supply and is accessible even through jeans.

If the person does not improve within five to fifteen minutes, a second dose can be given. Many people with severe allergies carry two auto-injectors for this reason.

## Choking — The Silent Emergency

Choking occurs when a foreign object — food, a small toy, a piece of candy — lodges in the airway and obstructs breathing. It is called the "silent emergency" because a person with a completely obstructed airway cannot speak, cannot cough, and cannot call for help. They may clutch their throat (the universal choking sign), their face may turn red and then blue, and they will look panicked.

### Conscious Adult — Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich Maneuver)

If the person can cough forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing. A strong cough generates more airway pressure than any external maneuver and may dislodge the object on its own. Stand by and be ready to intervene if the cough becomes weak or ineffective.

If the person cannot cough, cannot speak, and cannot breathe:

1. Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist.
2. Make a fist with one hand and place it thumb-side against the abdomen, just above the navel and well below the breastbone.
3. Grab your fist with your other hand.
4. Deliver firm, upward thrusts — inward and upward, as if you are trying to lift them off the ground with your fist. Each thrust should be a distinct, forceful movement.
5. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious.

For pregnant persons or individuals too large to reach around, deliver chest thrusts instead: same position behind the person, but place your fist on the center of the breastbone (sternum) and thrust straight backward.

### Unconscious Choking Victim

If a choking victim becomes unconscious:

1. Lower them to the ground on their back.
2. Call 911 (or direct someone to call).
3. Begin CPR. Open the airway (head tilt-chin lift) and look in the mouth before each set of rescue breaths. If you can see the object, remove it with a finger sweep. If you cannot see it, do not blindly sweep — you may push it deeper.
4. The chest compressions of CPR can generate enough pressure to dislodge the obstruction. Continue the CPR cycle (thirty compressions, two breaths for adults) until the object is expelled and the person begins breathing, or until EMS arrives.

### Choking in Infants (Under 1 Year)

Infant choking uses a different technique due to the infant's size and anatomy:

1. Place the infant face-down on your forearm, supporting the head and jaw with your hand. Rest your forearm on your thigh for support.
2. Deliver five firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your other hand.
3. Turn the infant face-up on your other forearm. Give five chest thrusts using two fingers on the center of the breastbone, just below the nipple line.
4. Alternate back blows and chest thrusts until the object is expelled or the infant becomes unconscious.
5. If the infant becomes unconscious, begin infant CPR.

## Poisoning

Poisoning can occur through ingestion, inhalation, skin absorption, or injection. The range of substances is vast — household cleaners, medications, plants, carbon monoxide, industrial chemicals, illicit drugs.

**The first call is to Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222.** This number works anywhere in the United States, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, and connects to a certified poison specialist. They will tell you exactly what to do based on the substance, the amount, and the person's age and condition.

Do NOT induce vomiting unless Poison Control specifically tells you to. Many caustic substances (drain cleaner, bleach, battery acid) cause additional damage on the way back up. Syrup of ipecac, once a medicine cabinet staple, is no longer recommended by any major medical organization for home use.

If the person is unconscious, having seizures, or not breathing, call 911 first, then Poison Control.

Bring the substance container, the remaining pills, or any packaging to the phone and then to the hospital. Knowing exactly what was ingested — the active ingredient, the concentration, the amount — dramatically changes the treatment approach.

## Opioid Overdose — Naloxone Saves Lives

The fentanyl crisis has made opioid overdose a public health emergency that touches every community. Fentanyl is fifty to a hundred times more potent than morphine, and it is now found in counterfeit pills, heroin, cocaine, and methamphetamine, often without the user's knowledge. Overdose deaths in the United States exceeded 100,000 per year starting in 2021, driven primarily by synthetic opioids.

Signs of opioid overdose: pinpoint pupils, slow or absent breathing, blue or gray skin (especially lips and fingertips), unresponsiveness, gurgling or snoring sounds (partial airway obstruction), limpness.

**Naloxone (Narcan)** is an opioid antagonist — it binds to the same receptors as opioids and reverses their effects, including respiratory depression. It works in two to three minutes and can bring a person back from the edge of respiratory arrest.

Naloxone nasal spray (Narcan) is available without a prescription in all fifty states. Many pharmacies provide it free or at low cost. Community organizations, harm reduction programs, and some fire departments distribute it at no charge.

How to use naloxone nasal spray:

1. Call 911.
2. Lay the person on their back.
3. Tilt their head back and insert the nozzle into one nostril.
4. Press the plunger firmly to deliver the full dose.
5. If no improvement in two to three minutes, administer a second dose in the other nostril.
6. Place the person in the recovery position after administering naloxone (they may vomit as they regain consciousness).
7. Stay with the person until EMS arrives. Naloxone wears off in thirty to ninety minutes, and the opioid may still be in their system. They can re-enter overdose after naloxone wears off.

There is no harm in giving naloxone to someone who is not experiencing an opioid overdose. If you are unsure whether the person has taken opioids but they are unresponsive and not breathing adequately, give it. The worst that happens is nothing. The best that happens is you save a life.

Carrying naloxone is not an endorsement of drug use. It is a recognition that people die from opioid overdose, that death is preventable with a cheap and safe medication, and that bystanders are usually the first people on scene. This is health literacy, not politics.

## College Connection

This document connects to three departments within the College structure:

**Science** — Cardiovascular and neurological physiology. Understanding how the heart works as a pump, how atherosclerosis develops, how a clot forms, and why oxygen deprivation kills heart muscle and brain tissue provides the "why" behind the urgency. Similarly, understanding the pharmacology of epinephrine (how it works on alpha and beta receptors), aspirin (platelet aggregation inhibition), and naloxone (competitive opioid receptor antagonism) transforms these interventions from rote memorized actions to understood mechanisms.

**Mind-Body** — The emergency response under medical crisis. Medical emergencies produce extreme stress in both the patient and the bystander. The fear of "doing something wrong" paralyzes many bystanders into doing nothing — which is always worse. Understanding the stress response (tunnel vision, time distortion, loss of fine motor skills) and having practiced the interventions enough to rely on procedural memory rather than conscious recall are the antidotes to paralysis.

**Learning** — Emergency protocols as decision trees. Check-call-care. FAST for stroke. The sugar rule for diabetes. These are algorithms — structured decision-making processes that work even when the decision-maker is frightened, tired, or overwhelmed. The value of protocols is that they do the thinking for you when thinking is hardest.

## When the Body Fails, the Bystander Decides

The common thread in every medical emergency described in this document is the asymmetry between the speed of the emergency and the speed of the professional response. Heart attacks kill muscle in minutes. Strokes destroy brain in minutes. Anaphylaxis closes airways in minutes. The ambulance arrives in eight to twelve minutes on average, longer in rural areas.

In that gap, the bystander decides.

Call or wait? Aspirin or nothing? Epi-pen or hope the swelling stops? Abdominal thrusts or back slaps on the back? Narcan or fear of overstepping?

Every one of these decisions has a correct answer, and every one of those correct answers is available to a person with basic training. You do not need to diagnose the type of stroke or the blocked coronary artery. You need to recognize the pattern, call 911, and do the one or two things that buy time until the ambulance arrives.

That is enough. It is more than enough. It is the difference between life and death in a statistically meaningful number of cases every year.

Learn the patterns. Practice the responses. Carry the tools. Be the bystander who acts.
