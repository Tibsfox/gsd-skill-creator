# Rain, Smoke, and Shake — PNW-Specific Health Hazards

## A Beautiful Place With Teeth

The Pacific Northwest markets itself on beauty. The travel brochures show green forests, snow-capped volcanoes, dramatic coastlines, and latte-sipping urbanites in fleece vests. What the brochures do not show is the medical reality: this region has a specific hazard profile that differs meaningfully from the rest of the country, and the people who live here — especially those who recreate outdoors — face risks that are either unique to the PNW or dramatically amplified by its geography, climate, and geology.

Hypothermia kills hikers here at temperatures that would be considered pleasant in other contexts. Wildfire smoke — a seasonal reality that did not exist at this scale twenty years ago — blankets the region for weeks at a time, degrading air quality to levels that would trigger emergency declarations in other countries. The Cascadia Subduction Zone, a 600-mile fault off the coast, is capable of producing a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and tsunami that would be the worst natural disaster in American history. Mushrooms that look edible kill people every year. Ticks carry diseases that were once considered an East Coast problem.

This document maps the PNW-specific health hazards — the ones that visitors underestimate, transplants are unaware of, and even lifelong residents sometimes overlook. It builds on the environmental emergencies covered in FAD-05 with regional specificity and connects to the FSR case studies on Cascadia earthquake preparedness.

## Hypothermia Revisited — Rain and Wind at 50 Degrees

FAD-05 covers hypothermia in general terms. This section addresses the PNW-specific presentation: hypothermia in conditions that do not feel dangerous.

The classic hypothermia scenario — blizzard, sub-zero temperatures, extreme exposure — is dramatic and recognizable. The PNW hypothermia scenario is subtle and lethal: a day hike in the Cascades in October. The forecast says 52 degrees and overcast. You are wearing jeans and a cotton hoodie because it is not that cold, right? You start the hike warm and comfortable. At the trailhead, it is dry. Two miles up, the clouds descend. It begins to rain — not hard, just a steady PNW drizzle. The wind picks up as you gain elevation. Your cotton hoodie soaks through within twenty minutes. Your jeans, now wet, cling to your legs. The combination of wet fabric against your skin, wind moving across that wet fabric, and the evaporative cooling effect begins pulling heat from your body faster than your metabolism can replace it.

Within an hour, you are shivering. Within two hours, your coordination is degrading — you stumble, your fingers fumble with your pack buckles. Within three hours, if conditions have not changed and you have not found shelter or dry clothing, you are in moderate hypothermia. Your judgment is impaired (you may decide to keep going rather than turn back, because the summit is "so close"). Your shivering may slow or stop. Your friends think you are getting better because you stopped shivering. You are getting worse.

This scenario kills people in the PNW every year. Not in January. In September, October, March, April, May. The months when the weather is moderate at the trailhead and life-threatening at elevation.

### Cotton Kills — The PNW Axiom

The phrase "cotton kills" is taught in every outdoor education course in the PNW, and it is not hyperbole. Cotton absorbs up to 27 times its weight in water. When wet, it provides zero insulation — it actively conducts heat away from the body. It takes a very long time to dry, especially in the humid PNW environment. And it clings to the skin, maximizing the surface area of cold, wet contact.

The alternative is the layering system:

**Base layer:** Merino wool or synthetic (polyester, nylon). These fabrics wick moisture away from the skin, dry quickly, and retain some insulating value when wet. Merino wool has the additional advantage of being naturally antimicrobial (it does not develop odor as quickly as synthetics). A lightweight merino base layer top and bottom costs $40-80 per piece and lasts years.

**Mid layer:** Fleece (polyester fleece, Polartec), puffy jacket (down or synthetic insulation). This is the insulation layer — it traps warm air close to your body. Synthetic insulation (PrimaLoft, Climashield) maintains loft when wet, which makes it preferable to down for PNW conditions where rain is likely. Down is lighter and more compressible but loses insulation when wet unless treated with a hydrophobic coating.

**Outer layer:** Waterproof and windproof shell (Gore-Tex, eVent, or other waterproof breathable membrane). This layer keeps rain and wind out. It does not need to be insulating — that is the mid layer's job. A good rain jacket is the single most important piece of outdoor gear in the PNW. Not a "water-resistant" windbreaker. A properly waterproof, seam-sealed, hooded shell.

No cotton. Anywhere. In the system. Not the underwear, not the socks, not the t-shirt under the base layer. All of it synthetic or wool.

## Wildfire Smoke — August Through October

The Pacific Northwest's fire season has intensified dramatically since the early 2000s, driven by a century of fire suppression (which allowed fuel to accumulate), drought conditions exacerbated by climate change, and development in the wildland-urban interface. What was once an occasional inconvenience has become a seasonal certainty.

In 2020, the Labor Day fires in Oregon burned over a million acres in a single week, producing smoke so dense that the sun was invisible in Portland for days and the sky turned an apocalyptic orange. AQI readings exceeded 500 — beyond the scale's normal range. In 2017 and 2018, smoke from British Columbia fires blanketed Puget Sound for weeks. In 2024, fires in eastern Washington and Oregon produced prolonged smoke events that became, for many residents, simply "what August is now."

### Health Impacts of Wildfire Smoke

Wildfire smoke is a complex mixture of gases and fine particles. The primary health concern is PM2.5 — particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers in diameter. These particles are small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs, cross into the bloodstream, and trigger systemic inflammation.

**Short-term effects:** Coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath, eye irritation, throat irritation, headache, increased asthma attacks, exacerbation of COPD and other chronic respiratory conditions, increased cardiovascular events (the inflammation triggered by PM2.5 can destabilize arterial plaques and trigger heart attacks and strokes).

**Long-term effects (with repeated seasonal exposure):** Emerging research suggests that chronic exposure to wildfire smoke may contribute to reduced lung function over time, increased cardiovascular disease risk, and potential neurodegenerative effects. This research is relatively new — the sustained, population-level wildfire smoke exposure the PNW is experiencing is historically unprecedented, and long-term health outcomes are still being studied.

### Protection Strategies

**Monitor AQI.** PurpleAir (purpleair.com) provides real-time, hyperlocal readings from community sensors. During smoke events, AQI can vary dramatically over short distances depending on terrain, wind patterns, and building sealing. The sensor a mile from your house may read differently from the conditions outside your front door, but it provides a far better baseline than the official EPA monitors, which are spaced miles apart.

**N95 respirators.** A NIOSH-approved N95 respirator is the minimum effective protection against PM2.5. The "N95" designation means it filters at least 95% of airborne particles. Key requirements: it must seal to the face. Air gaps around the nose, cheeks, or chin render the filtration useless because unfiltered air flows through the gaps. Cloth masks and surgical masks do NOT provide meaningful PM2.5 protection.

N95 fit varies by face shape. Try multiple brands and models. The 3M 8210 is the classic disposable N95. The 3M Aura 9205+ has a better seal for many face shapes. KN95 masks (the Chinese standard) are generally similar in filtration performance but are not NIOSH-certified, and quality varies by manufacturer.

People with beards cannot achieve an adequate N95 seal. A powered air-purifying respirator (PAPR) is the alternative for bearded individuals who need respiratory protection, but these cost $200+ and are impractical for most civilian use.

**Create a clean room.** Designate one room in your home as a clean air space during smoke events. Close all windows and doors. Seal gaps under doors with wet towels or draft stoppers. Seal any obvious air leaks around window frames with tape. Run a HEPA air purifier rated for at least the room's square footage. A Coway Airmega, Blueair Blue Pure, or Levoit Core 400S — any quality HEPA purifier in the $100-200 range — will reduce indoor PM2.5 by 80% or more in a sealed room.

If you do not have a HEPA purifier, a box fan with a 20x20 MERV-13 furnace filter taped to the intake side (the "Corsi-Rosenthal box") is a surprisingly effective DIY alternative that costs under $30. During the 2020 smoke events, this hack was widely circulated by public health officials in Oregon and Washington.

**Reduce exertion outdoors.** Physical activity increases breathing rate, which increases particulate intake. During unhealthy AQI days (>150), avoid outdoor exercise. Move workouts indoors or skip them. The health cost of exercising in heavy smoke outweighs the health benefit of the exercise.

## Cascadia Earthquake — The Injuries That Follow

The Cascadia Subduction Zone is a 600-mile megathrust fault running from Cape Mendocino, California, to Vancouver Island, British Columbia. It last ruptured on January 26, 1700, producing a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and a tsunami that reached Japan. It will rupture again. The geologic record shows that it ruptures at intervals ranging from roughly 200 to 600 years, with an average of about 240 years. We are at 326 years and counting. The probability of a magnitude 8.0+ event in the next 50 years is estimated at 10-15%.

This section does not cover earthquake preparedness broadly — that is a topic for infrastructure and emergency management research. This section covers the specific injury patterns associated with major earthquakes and the first aid implications.

### Glass Lacerations

The number one earthquake injury in developed countries is not from building collapse. It is from broken glass. Windows shatter, mirrors fall, glass tabletops break, glass shelves collapse. People in bed when the earthquake strikes roll out of bed in darkness, step on broken glass, and sustain deep lacerations to the feet.

**Prevention:** Keep a pair of hard-soled shoes and a flashlight next to your bed. Not under the bed — next to it, within arm's reach. In a nightstand drawer. Tied to the bedframe. When the shaking stops and you need to move through a dark house full of broken glass, shoes on your feet are the intervention.

**First aid:** Treat glass lacerations as any other wound (FAD-02) — clean, control bleeding, bandage. Glass fragments may be embedded in the wound. If you can see and safely remove superficial fragments, do so. Deep or large embedded fragments should be left in place and stabilized (build up padding around them so they are not pushed deeper) until medical care is available.

### Crush Injuries

Building collapse, falling furniture, and structural elements trap limbs and bodies. Crush injuries present a unique and dangerous first aid challenge.

When a limb is crushed under a heavy object for a prolonged period (generally more than one hour), the compressed muscle tissue begins to die. This dead tissue releases potassium, myoglobin, and other intracellular contents into the surrounding area. As long as the object remains on the limb, these toxins are trapped in the compressed area.

When the weight is removed and blood flow is restored, these toxins flood into the systemic circulation. The resulting condition — **crush syndrome** (traumatic rhabdomyolysis) — can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia from hyperkalemia (excess potassium stopping the heart), kidney failure from myoglobin clogging the renal tubules, and systemic shock.

The critical first aid principle: **if a person has been crushed for more than fifteen minutes, do not remove the compression without medical personnel present.** This is counterintuitive — every instinct says to free them. But removing the compression without simultaneous IV fluid resuscitation and cardiac monitoring can kill the person you are trying to save. Call for medical help, keep the person calm, give fluids by mouth if they are conscious and can swallow, and wait for paramedics who can manage the reperfusion.

If the compression has been present for less than fifteen minutes, or if there is an immediately life-threatening reason to remove it (fire, further collapse), removal is appropriate. Apply a tourniquet above the crush site before or immediately after removal if the limb was crushed for any significant period.

### Infrastructure Failure

A Cascadia earthquake will not be a single event. It will be a cascade (the name is appropriate) of secondary effects: fires from ruptured gas lines, flooding from dam failures, bridge collapses cutting off communities, hospital damage reducing medical capacity, power grid failure eliminating the systems that modern medicine depends on.

In this environment, first aid is not a five-minute bridge to professional care. It may be the only care available for hours or days. The skills in this entire FAD series — wound care, splinting, burn treatment, hypothermia management, water purification, mental health support — become the primary healthcare system for anyone who is not within walking distance of a still-functioning hospital.

## Tick-Borne Disease — The Increasing Threat

The western black-legged tick (Ixodes pacificus) is the primary vector for Lyme disease in the Pacific Northwest. Historically, Lyme incidence in Washington and Oregon has been far lower than in the northeastern United States, where the related Ixodes scapularis tick transmits Borrelia burgdorferi at much higher rates. But the PNW is not zero-risk, and the risk is increasing.

Research from public health agencies indicates that the percentage of Ixodes pacificus nymphs infected with Borrelia burgdorferi in the PNW ranges from 1-5%, compared to 15-30% for Ixodes scapularis in endemic northeastern areas. The lower infection rate means lower per-bite risk, but the ticks are common in brushy, forested environments — exactly where PNW residents hike, camp, and work.

Additionally, Ixodes pacificus can transmit Anaplasma phagocytophilum (anaplasmosis), Babesia species (babesiosis), and potentially Borrelia miyamotoi (relapsing fever).

Tick prevention:
- Wear long pants tucked into socks in brushy terrain
- Treat clothing with permethrin (a persistent insecticide that kills ticks on contact — it remains effective through multiple washes)
- Apply DEET or picaridin to exposed skin
- Perform thorough tick checks after outdoor activity — check the scalp, behind ears, armpits, groin, behind knees, and waistband
- Shower within two hours of outdoor activity (ticks that have not yet attached can be washed off)

Tick removal technique is covered in FAD-05. The key points: fine-tipped tweezers, grasp near the skin, pull straight out with steady pressure, save the tick, and watch for symptoms over the following month.

## Mushroom Poisoning — The Silent Killer

The Pacific Northwest is a mycological paradise — hundreds of edible, medicinal, and ecologically fascinating fungal species flourish in the region's wet, temperate forests. It is also a region where people die from mushroom poisoning every year.

The most dangerous species is the **death cap** (Amanita phalloides). Originally from Europe, it was introduced to the Pacific Northwest and now grows in association with imported ornamental trees (oaks, lindens) in urban and suburban areas. It fruits in the fall, has a pleasant taste, and resembles several edible species — particularly straw mushrooms and paddy straw mushrooms, which are commonly eaten in Asian cuisines. This resemblance has led to multiple poisoning clusters in immigrant communities.

The death cap's toxin — amatoxin — is insidious. Initial symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) appear six to twelve hours after ingestion and then temporarily improve. The patient feels better. This "improvement" is an illusion: amatoxin is destroying liver cells during this period. By the time liver failure becomes apparent (jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy), the damage is often irreversible. Without a liver transplant, mortality from Amanita phalloides poisoning exceeds 50%.

Other dangerous PNW mushrooms include:

- **Destroying angel** (Amanita ocreata, Amanita smithiana) — related to the death cap, similar toxin, similar presentation, similar lethality
- **Galerina marginata** (funeral bell) — grows on decaying wood, contains amatoxins, can be confused with edible species
- **Conocybe filaris** — a small lawn mushroom containing amatoxins, sometimes mistaken for magic mushrooms or edible species

The first aid for suspected mushroom poisoning is immediate:

1. **Call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222.**
2. If possible, save a sample of the mushroom (or take a photo) for identification.
3. Transport to an emergency room. Do NOT wait for symptoms — by the time amatoxin symptoms appear, significant liver damage may already be occurring.
4. If the person is vomiting, prevent aspiration (recovery position).

The prevention is absolute: **never eat a wild mushroom without expert identification.** Not identification from an app. Not identification from a field guide used by someone who learned foraging last week. Identification by an experienced mycologist or an expert-level forager with years of field experience and specific knowledge of PNW species. The consequences of a misidentification are not a stomach ache. They are a liver transplant or a funeral.

## Marine Hazards

The Pacific Northwest coastline presents specific hazards beyond drowning (covered in FAD-05):

**Jellyfish stings.** Moon jellies (Aurelia aurita) and lion's mane jellies (Cyanea capillata) are the most common PNW species. Stings range from mild irritation to significant pain. Treatment: rinse with vinegar (acetic acid) to deactivate remaining nematocysts, then apply heat (hot water immersion at 110-113 degrees F for twenty minutes). Do NOT rinse with fresh water (which can trigger unfired nematocysts) and do NOT apply urine (a folk remedy with no evidence and a reasonable infection risk).

**Sea urchin spines.** The purple sea urchin (Strongylocentrotus purpuratus) is common in tide pools. Stepping on one drives brittle spines deep into the foot, where they fragment and are difficult to remove. Soak in hot water (which softens the spines and eases removal), remove visible spines with tweezers, and see a doctor if spines are deeply embedded or if signs of infection develop.

**Paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP).** Red tide — blooms of toxin-producing algae — can concentrate in filter-feeding shellfish (clams, mussels, oysters) at levels that cause paralytic shellfish poisoning in humans. Symptoms begin within minutes to hours of ingestion: tingling of the lips and tongue, progressing to numbness of the face and extremities, difficulty swallowing, muscle weakness, and in severe cases respiratory paralysis.

There is no antidote for PSP. Treatment is supportive (mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure occurs). Prevention is the only reliable strategy: check the WDFW (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife) shellfish safety hotline (1-800-562-5632) or website before clamming or harvesting any shellfish. Closure notices are posted when toxin levels exceed safe limits. Do not harvest shellfish from closed beaches. Do not assume that cooking destroys the toxin — it does not. PSP toxin is heat-stable.

## Volcanic Hazards

The Cascades Range volcanoes — Rainier, Baker, Hood, St. Helens, Adams, Glacier Peak — are active. Mount St. Helens last erupted in 2004-2008 (dome-building, not explosive). The risk is low in any given year but the consequences of volcanic activity range from regional inconvenience (ashfall) to catastrophic (lahar).

**Lahar.** A lahar is a fast-moving flow of volcanic debris and water — essentially a river of hot mud, rocks, and everything it picks up along the way. Mount Rainier is considered the most dangerous volcano in the Cascades specifically because of lahar risk: its glaciers contain enough ice to generate lahars that could reach the Puyallup Valley communities of Orting, Sumner, and Puyallup within thirty to forty-five minutes of initiation. USGS lahar detection systems are in place and the community has evacuation plans, but the time window is extremely short.

First aid implications: lahar injuries are crush injuries, thermal burns, and drowning. The medical response will be overwhelmed. Self-evacuation to high ground is the primary survival strategy — know your lahar zone (published by USGS and local emergency management) and your evacuation route.

**Ashfall.** Volcanic ash is not soft like wood ash. It is pulverized rock — abrasive, silica-rich glass particles that damage lungs, scratch corneas, contaminate water, and disable machinery. Health effects include respiratory irritation, eye irritation, and exacerbation of asthma and other respiratory conditions. Heavy ashfall (more than a few millimeters) can collapse roofs.

Protection during ashfall: stay indoors with windows and doors sealed. Wear an N95 mask if you must go outside. Wear goggles (not just glasses — ash gets around frames). Limit driving (ash clogs air filters and reduces visibility). Do not wash ash off roofs with water (wet ash is extremely heavy and can overload structures).

## Living Here Means Knowing Here

Every region has its hazards. The Midwest has tornadoes. The Southeast has hurricanes. The Southwest has extreme heat. The PNW has rain-driven hypothermia, wildfire smoke, subduction zone earthquakes, and a fungal diversity that includes some of the deadliest organisms on the continent.

None of these hazards are reasons not to live here. All of them are reasons to be informed. The person who keeps shoes by the bed, checks the AQI before going outside in August, wears wool instead of cotton on trail, and never eats a mushroom they cannot identify with certainty is not anxious. They are adapted.

The PNW rewards preparation. It punishes ignorance. Know the territory.
