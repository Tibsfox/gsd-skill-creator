## 1. Board Enforcement Authority

The Washington State Board of Accountancy derives its enforcement authority from RCW 18.04.295 and WAC 4-30-142. The Board may take action against both individual CPA licensees and CPA firms.

### 1.1 Who Is Subject to Board Jurisdiction

| Party | Basis |
|-------|-------|
| Washington CPA licensees | RCW 18.04; WAC 4-30 |
| CPA firms licensed in Washington | RCW 18.04.195 |
| Nonlicensee firm owners | WAC 4-30-110 |
| Out-of-state CPAs exercising practice privilege | RCW 18.04.350 |
| Out-of-state firms operating in Washington | RCW 18.04.195 |
| Unlicensed individuals using protected titles | RCW 18.04.345 |

### 1.2 Board Composition and Authority

The nine-member Board (six CPAs, three public members) is appointed by the governor. The Board functions as an administrative adjudicatory body — it can conduct hearings, receive evidence, make findings of fact, and issue final orders. Board orders are enforceable as civil judgments.

## 2. Disciplinary Sanctions Available

The Board may impose any combination of the following sanctions under RCW 18.04.295 and WAC 4-30-142:

| Sanction | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Civil fine** | Up to $30,000 per violation, plus investigative and legal costs |
| **License revocation** | Permanent or indefinite removal of license |
| **License suspension** | Temporary removal for a specified period |
| **Refusal to issue/renew** | Board declines to issue initial license or renew existing license |
| **Conditions precedent to renewal** | Remedial requirements before renewal is granted |
| **Remedial sanctions** | Required CPE, supervision, practice restrictions |
| **Full restitution** | Payment to injured parties of amounts lost due to misconduct |
| **Prohibition (nonlicensees)** | Prohibiting non-CPA firm owners from holding ownership interests in licensed firms |

### 2.1 The $30,000 Fine Maximum

The $30,000 ceiling applies **per violation** — not per investigation. A single enforcement action involving multiple violations can result in multiple fines. Additionally, the Board may impose reimbursement of its own investigative and legal costs, which can substantially increase the total financial exposure.

### 2.2 Aggravating and Mitigating Factors

The Board considers several factors in determining sanction level:
- Whether the violation involved dishonesty or fraud (aggravating)
- The harm caused to clients or the public (aggravating)
- Prior disciplinary history (aggravating)
- Cooperation with Board investigation (mitigating)
- Voluntary remediation before investigation conclusion (mitigating)
- Good character history (mitigating)

## 3. Prohibited Acts Catalog (WAC 4-30-142)

WAC 4-30-142 defines specific conduct that constitutes grounds for disciplinary action. The catalog is organized by type:

### 3.1 Credential Violations

- **Fraud or deceit in obtaining a license** — including false statements on license applications, fabricated CPE documentation, or misrepresented experience
- **Failure to disclose required information** to the Board
- **Use of protected titles** by unlicensed persons (CPA, PA, LPA, etc.)
- **Inactive licensee practicing public accounting** without reinstatement

### 3.2 Professional Service Violations

- **Dishonesty, fraud, or negligence in professional services** — the broadest category, encompassing audit failures, fraudulent tax returns, and negligent financial statements
- **Failure to comply with professional standards** — violations of GAAS, GAAP, or WAC 4-30 practice rules
- **Offering services when not qualified** — practicing in areas beyond the CPA's competence without proper supervision or disclosure
- **Making misleading representations** about qualifications, experience, or the nature of services being performed
- **Negligent tax preparation** — material errors in federal or state tax returns through lack of competence or care
- **Breach of fiduciary duties** to clients

### 3.3 Investigation and Compliance Violations

- **Failure to cooperate with Board investigations** — refusing to provide records, failing to respond to inquiries, or obstructing investigation
- **Concealing another's violation** — knowing about a colleague's violation and not reporting it
- **Failure to timely notify** the Board of triggering events (see Section 4)

### 3.4 Financial and Operational Violations

- **Failure to file required reports or returns** for clients within the practitioner's responsibility
- **Misappropriation of client funds**
- **Failure to maintain records** required by Board rules

## 4. Mandatory Notification Requirements

Washington CPAs must notify the Board within 30 days of:

| Event | Notification Required |
|-------|----------------------|
| Criminal conviction (any felony; misdemeanor involving dishonesty) | Yes — within 30 days |
| Disciplinary action taken by another professional licensing body | Yes — within 30 days |
| Disciplinary action taken by another state's CPA board | Yes — within 30 days |
| Malpractice judgment or settlement (above threshold) | Yes |
| Entry of a judicial sanction order | Yes |

### 4.1 Why Notification Matters

The mandatory notification requirement exists because the Board's jurisdiction is Washington-specific. A CPA disciplined in Oregon may still hold a valid Washington license if they don't notify the Board. The notification requirement closes this gap — Washington's Board can then evaluate whether the out-of-state action warrants action under Washington rules.

Failure to notify is itself a prohibited act under WAC 4-30-142, adding a second violation on top of whatever precipitated the external action.

## 5. The Consumer Complaint Process

### 5.1 Who Can File

Any person may file a complaint with the WBOA — clients, other CPAs, employers, or members of the public.

### 5.2 How to File

Complaints are filed through the Board's online complaint system at [https://acb.wa.gov](https://acb.wa.gov). The complaint should include:
- The name and license number of the CPA or firm (if known)
- A description of the alleged conduct
- Supporting documentation (engagement letters, tax returns, correspondence)
- The approximate dates of the alleged conduct

### 5.3 Investigation Outcomes

After receiving a complaint, the Board:
1. Screens for jurisdictional authority and completeness
2. Opens a formal investigation if warranted
3. Notifies the licensee and requests a response
4. Reviews evidence from both parties
5. Determines whether probable cause exists for disciplinary action
6. Proceeds to hearing or settlement if probable cause found

The Board publishes its investigation statistics and publishes all final disciplinary orders on its website. Disciplinary orders are permanently searchable through the license/discipline search tool.

## 6. Title Protection Enforcement

The Board actively monitors for unauthorized use of protected accounting titles. Under RCW 18.04.345:
- Unauthorized use of "CPA" or similar titles is a **misdemeanor**
- The Board may refer cases to county prosecutors for criminal prosecution
- The Board may also seek injunctive relief in superior court
- Each instance of unauthorized title use can constitute a separate violation

### 6.1 Common Title Violations

- Marketing materials describing an unlicensed bookkeeper as a "CPA" or "accountant"
- Websites claiming "CPA services" when no licensed CPA is on staff
- A lapsed licensee continuing to use the CPA designation after license expiration
- A retired licensee using the CPA title without the required "inactive" designation

## 7. Investigation Process

The Board's investigation process follows Washington administrative procedure:

```
1. COMPLAINT RECEIVED
   ↓
2. SCREENING (Board staff)
   → Jurisdictional? Within statute of limitations?
   → Sufficient allegations to warrant investigation?
   ↓
3. INVESTIGATION (Board investigators)
   → Request response from licensee
   → Gather records, work papers, correspondence
   → Interview witnesses
   ↓
4. STAFF REVIEW
   → Recommend: dismiss, informal resolution, or formal action
   ↓
5. BOARD REVIEW
   → Review staff recommendation
   → May issue Statement of Charges (formal complaint)
   ↓
6. SETTLEMENT OR HEARING
   → Consent Agreement (negotiated resolution)
   → Administrative hearing before Board
   ↓
7. FINAL ORDER
   → Published on WBOA website
   → Permanently searchable in license database
```

## 8. Case Examples and Patterns

Based on publicly published Board orders, common patterns in enforcement actions include:

**Most frequent violations (by type):**
1. Negligent tax preparation (errors, missed deadlines, unreported income)
2. Failure to maintain required CPE
3. Failure to cooperate with Board investigations
4. Unauthorized use of CPA title (unlicensed practitioners)
5. Misappropriation of client funds or trust account violations

**Typical sanction ranges:**
- CPE deficiency (first offense): Conditions on renewal; fine $1,000–$5,000
- Negligent tax preparation: License suspension 30–180 days; fine $5,000–$15,000; restitution
- Fraud: License revocation; fine up to $30,000; restitution; referral for criminal prosecution
- Unauthorized title use: Cease-and-desist order; fine $2,000–$10,000; criminal referral
