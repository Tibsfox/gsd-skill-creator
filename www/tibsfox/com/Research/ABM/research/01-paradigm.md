# The Paradigm: From Deficit to Difference

## Origins of the Neurodiversity Concept

The term "neurodiversity" was coined by Australian sociologist **Judy Singer** in her 1998 honors thesis, drawing on the disability rights movement's social model framework. Singer argued that neurological differences should be understood as natural human variation -- analogous to biodiversity. The concept positioned diversity of neurological function as intrinsically valuable.

The concept rapidly expanded beyond autism to encompass ADHD, dyslexia, dyscalculia, dyspraxia, Tourette syndrome, and other neurological conditions. Crucially, the neurodiversity paradigm does not deny genuine challenges -- it locates them in the *mismatch* between neurotype and environmental demands.

## Medical Model vs. Social Model

- **Medical model:** Frames neurodivergent conditions as disorders requiring treatment and normalization
- **Social model:** Argues that disability arises from the interaction between individual characteristics and an environment not designed for them
- **Contemporary position:** "Both/and" -- supporting genuine needs while rejecting the premise that neurological difference constitutes inherent deficit

## Diagnostic Evolution: DSM-III to DSM-5

The diagnostic landscape has undergone significant revision:

- **DSM-5 (2013):** Merged Asperger's syndrome into the broader Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) category
- **ADHD:** Diagnostic criteria expanded to include adult presentations
- **Dyslexia:** Increasingly framed as a "learning difference" rather than a disability

| Condition | Traditional Framing | Neurodiversity Reframe |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Autism | Social communication disorder | Different social cognition; pattern/system strength |
| ADHD | Attention and impulse disorder | Variable attention; hyperfocus and divergent thinking |
| Dyslexia | Reading disability | Explorative, visual-spatial, connective cognitive style |
| Giftedness | Exceptional intelligence | Often co-occurs with neurodivergence (2e) |
| Savant Syndrome | Exceptional ability despite disability | Local hyperconnectivity producing domain-specific excellence |

## The Shift Still Underway

Assessment tools were historically designed to measure what individuals *could not* do. The shift toward strength-based assessment -- measuring what individuals do well and what environments they need -- represents a paradigm transformation still underway in most institutional settings.
