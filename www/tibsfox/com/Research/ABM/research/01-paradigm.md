# The Paradigm: From Deficit to Difference

## Origins of the Neurodiversity Concept

The term "neurodiversity" was coined by Australian sociologist **Judy Singer**
in her 1998 honors thesis at the University of Technology, Sydney, drawing on
the disability rights movement's social model framework. Singer -- herself
autistic, with an autistic daughter and a mother she believed was also on the
spectrum -- argued that neurological differences should be understood as natural
human variation, analogous to biodiversity in ecological systems. The concept
positioned diversity of neurological function as intrinsically valuable rather
than pathological.

Singer's work did not emerge in isolation. The late 1990s saw the rise of
autistic self-advocacy communities online, particularly through the Independent
Living on the Autistic Spectrum (InLv) mailing list, where autistic adults were
articulating their experiences outside the clinical framework for the first time
at scale. Jim Sinclair's 1993 essay "Don't Mourn for Us," presented at the
International Conference on Autism in Toronto, is widely regarded as a
foundational text of the neurodiversity movement, predating Singer's coinage by
five years.

The concept rapidly expanded beyond autism to encompass ADHD, dyslexia,
dyscalculia, dyspraxia, Tourette syndrome, and other neurological conditions.
Crucially, the neurodiversity paradigm does not deny genuine challenges -- it
locates them in the *mismatch* between neurotype and environmental demands
rather than in the individual alone.

## Medical Model vs. Social Model

The tension between these two frameworks structures the entire field:

- **Medical model:** Frames neurodivergent conditions as disorders requiring treatment and normalization. The individual is the locus of the "problem." Interventions aim to reduce symptoms and approximate neurotypical functioning.
- **Social model:** Argues that disability arises from the interaction between individual characteristics and an environment not designed for them. The environment is the locus of the "problem." Interventions aim to modify contexts rather than people.
- **Contemporary position:** "Both/and" -- supporting genuine needs while rejecting the premise that neurological difference constitutes inherent deficit. Nick Walker (2021) articulates this as the "neurodiversity paradigm" distinct from the "pathology paradigm."

The medical model has historically dominated institutional practice. The DSM
(Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders) is organized around
deficit: each entry catalogs what the individual cannot do relative to a
normative baseline. The social model, by contrast, asks what the environment
fails to provide. Most contemporary researchers and advocates work within a
synthesis -- acknowledging that some neurodivergent traits cause genuine
distress independent of environment (e.g., sensory pain, executive dysfunction),
while insisting that much of the "disability" attributed to neurodivergence is
environmentally constructed.

## The Language Debate

Language itself is contested terrain in neurodiversity discourse:

- **Identity-first language** ("autistic person") is preferred by most autistic self-advocates, following the convention of other identity categories ("deaf person," "blind person")
- **Person-first language** ("person with autism") is preferred by some parents and clinicians, emphasizing the person before the condition
- **Neurodivergent/neurotypical:** Terms introduced by Kassiane Asasumasu to describe individuals whose neurology differs from or conforms to dominant societal norms
- **Neurodiversity:** Describes the *population-level* fact of neurological variation, not any individual condition. Saying someone "has neurodiversity" is a category error -- like saying someone "has biodiversity"

The language debate is not merely semantic. It reflects fundamentally different
ontological commitments about the relationship between neurology and identity.
Research by Kenny et al. (2016) in the journal *Autism* surveyed 3,470 people
and found that autistic adults overwhelmingly preferred identity-first language,
while parents and professionals were more divided.

## Diagnostic Evolution: DSM-III to DSM-5

The diagnostic landscape has undergone significant revision across four decades:

- **DSM-III (1980):** Introduced "Infantile Autism" as a distinct category, separating it from childhood schizophrenia for the first time
- **DSM-IV (1994):** Created the subcategories of Autistic Disorder, Asperger's Disorder, and PDD-NOS, establishing the concept of a spectrum but fragmenting it into discrete labels
- **DSM-5 (2013):** Merged all subcategories into the unified Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) category with severity levels. Introduced Social Communication Disorder as a separate diagnosis
- **ADHD evolution:** Diagnostic criteria expanded across editions to include adult presentations, inattentive-predominant type (previously overlooked), and recognition of female presentation patterns
- **Dyslexia:** Increasingly framed as a "learning difference" rather than a disability, with the British Dyslexia Association and similar organizations leading the reframe

| Condition | Traditional Framing | Neurodiversity Reframe |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Autism | Social communication disorder | Different social cognition; pattern/system strength |
| ADHD | Attention and impulse disorder | Variable attention; hyperfocus and divergent thinking |
| Dyslexia | Reading disability | Explorative, visual-spatial, connective cognitive style |
| Dyscalculia | Mathematics disability | Different quantitative reasoning; qualitative strength |
| Dyspraxia | Motor coordination disorder | Different motor planning; often strong verbal reasoning |
| Tourette syndrome | Tic disorder | Heightened neural sensitivity; creative association |
| Giftedness | Exceptional intelligence | Often co-occurs with neurodivergence (2e) |
| Savant Syndrome | Exceptional ability despite disability | Local hyperconnectivity producing domain-specific excellence |

## The Shift Still Underway

Assessment tools were historically designed to measure what individuals *could
not* do. The shift toward strength-based assessment -- measuring what
individuals do well and what environments they need -- represents a paradigm
transformation still underway in most institutional settings.

Key figures advancing this shift include:

- **Simon Baron-Cohen** (Cambridge): Developed the "systemizing-empathizing" theory of autism, reframing autistic cognition as a different balance rather than a deficit
- **Thomas Armstrong:** Author of *Neurodiversity: Discovering the Extraordinary Gifts of Autism, ADHD, Dyslexia, and Other Brain Differences* (2010), popularizing strengths-based neurodiversity in education
- **Steve Silberman:** Author of *NeuroTribes* (2015), the Pulitzer-nominated history that brought the neurodiversity paradigm to mainstream awareness
- **Devon Price:** Researcher and author of *Unmasking Autism* (2022), documenting the costs of neurodivergent masking

## Prevalence and Demographics

Neurodivergent conditions are common -- far more common than institutional
responses suggest:

- **Autism:** CDC estimates 1 in 36 children in the US (2020 data),
up from 1 in 150 in 2000. The increase reflects expanded diagnostic
criteria and better identification, not an "epidemic."
- **ADHD:** Estimated 5-7% of children, 2.5-4% of adults globally
(Faraone et al., 2021). Adult ADHD was essentially unrecognized
before the 1990s.
- **Dyslexia:** Estimated 5-17% of the population depending on
diagnostic threshold and definition.
- **Dyscalculia:** Estimated 3-7% of the population.
- **Tourette syndrome:** Estimated 0.3-1% of the population.

Gender disparities in identification are significant. Autism is diagnosed
at a 3-4:1 male-to-female ratio, but research increasingly suggests the
true ratio is closer to 2:1 or even 1.5:1 -- the gap reflects diagnostic
tools calibrated on male presentations and the higher masking rates
documented in women and girls (Loomes et al., 2017). Similar patterns
hold for ADHD, where the inattentive presentation more common in women
was historically underrecognized.

## Contested Boundaries

The neurodiversity paradigm is not without critique. Key tensions include:

1. **Inclusion boundaries:** Which conditions qualify as
"neurodivergent"? Schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and personality
disorders are included by some frameworks and excluded by others. There
is no consensus.
2. **Support needs erasure:** Critics argue that framing
neurodivergence as "difference" can minimize the support needs of
individuals with high-support-needs autism or severe ADHD. Autistic
self-advocate Amy Sequenzia has written extensively about how the
neurodiversity movement sometimes sidelines non-speaking autistic people.
3. **Romanticization risk:** The emphasis on neurodivergent
"superpowers" can create a new hierarchy where neurodivergent individuals
are valued only when they demonstrate exceptional ability.
4. **Cultural variation:** The neurodiversity paradigm emerged from
English-speaking Western contexts. Cross-cultural applicability remains
underexplored.
5. **Self-diagnosis debate:** Many neurodivergent adults, particularly
those from marginalized communities, cannot access formal diagnosis due
to cost, provider scarcity, or diagnostic bias. The community is divided
on whether self-identification should be recognized alongside clinical
diagnosis.

These tensions are productive rather than destructive -- they indicate a
paradigm mature enough to sustain internal critique while continuing to
advance.

## Study Topics

1. Read Judy Singer's original 1998 thesis abstract and Jim Sinclair's "Don't Mourn for Us" (1993). How do their framings differ?
2. Compare a DSM-5 diagnostic description of ASD with an autistic self-advocate's description of the same traits. What language choices differ?
3. Investigate the "double empathy problem" (Milton, 2012): the idea that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional, not one-sided.
4. Research one non-Western cultural framework for understanding cognitive difference (e.g., the Japanese concept of *tojisha-kenkyu*, first-person research by disabled people).

## DIY Exercises

1. **Language audit:** Take any clinical description of a neurodivergent condition and rewrite it using strength-based language. Note what information is gained and lost in translation.
2. **Environmental mismatch journal:** For one week, note moments where your environment (lighting, noise, social expectations, task structure) does not match your needs. Calculate the cumulative cognitive cost.
3. **Assessment comparison:** Find a standardized cognitive assessment (many sample items are publicly available). Analyze what it measures and what it does not. Who performs well on this assessment? Who does not? What does that tell you about the assessment?
4. **Timeline construction:** Build a timeline of the neurodiversity movement from Sinclair (1993) through Singer (1998) to the present. Identify the key publications, legal milestones, and organizational founding dates.
