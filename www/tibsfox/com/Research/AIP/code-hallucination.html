Code Agents and LLM Hallucination

Deep Research Module 4 of 5 — April 2026 arXiv Discoveries
Compiled: April 8, 2026 | Artemis II Research Series

The intersection of LLM-powered agents, scientific publishing, and hallucination represents one of the most consequential research areas in AI safety. As LLMs are increasingly deployed as autonomous publishing assistants, code generation tools, and research agents, the question of what they fabricate—and how we detect it—has moved from academic curiosity to urgent operational concern. This module examines seven key research threads spanning prosocial persuasion, neurosymbolic fact-checking, reference hallucinations, citation fabrication, scholarly visualization, visual reasoning, and affective image understanding.

1. Prosocial Persuasion: LLMs Outperforming Humans at Scale

The paper "When Large Language Models are More Persuasive Than Incentivized Humans, and Why" (arXiv:2505.09662) provides the first rigorous experimental evidence that LLMs can outperform financially motivated human persuaders in real-time conversational settings. The study, involving 39 researchers across multiple institutions, has immediate implications for both beneficial applications (public health messaging, education) and adversarial ones (disinformation, social engineering).

1.1 Experimental Design

The researchers employed a carefully controlled design where quiz participants (incentivized for accuracy) were paired with either human persuaders (incentivized for successful persuasion) or LLM persuaders (Claude 3.5 Sonnet in the primary study, DeepSeek v3 in the replication). Critically, persuasion attempts were categorized as either truthful (guiding toward the correct answer) or deceptive (guiding toward an incorrect answer), enabling analysis of LLM persuasion in both benign and adversarial modes.

Several nuances are critical:

  • Context dependence: LLM persuasive superiority varies by model and truthfulness condition. Claude dominated both conditions; DeepSeek showed clear advantages only in deceptive contexts.
  • Temporal decay: Unlike human persuaders whose effectiveness remains stable over repeated interactions, LLM persuasiveness diminishes with exposure. Recipients learn to recognize and discount LLM rhetorical patterns.
  • Mechanism: Linguistic analysis reveals that LLMs express higher conviction levels than human persuaders, with more assertive framing and fewer hedging expressions. This higher conviction is effective initially but may contribute to faster trust erosion over time.
"LLMs' persuasive superiority is context-dependent: it depends on whether the persuasion attempt is truthful or deceptive, on the LLM model, and wanes over repeated interactions, unlike human persuasiveness."

1.3 Implications for AI-Assisted Communication

The persuasion findings have immediate implications for AI-assisted communication in professional settings. When LLMs draft emails, proposals, or presentations, they apply the same high-conviction rhetorical style that makes them persuasive in experimental settings. This means that AI-drafted communications may be more persuasive than their authors intend, potentially committing organizations to positions expressed more strongly than the underlying evidence warrants. A product manager using an LLM to draft a feature proposal may find that the AI's version reads as a definitive recommendation when a tentative suggestion was intended. The conviction-accuracy decoupling means the AI's assertive framing does not reflect greater certainty about the proposal's merits—it reflects the model's default rhetorical style.

For agent systems that communicate with humans (customer support agents, writing assistants, educational tutors), calibrated expressiveness is as important as calibrated confidence. An agent that expresses appropriate uncertainty when uncertain and appropriate confidence when confident provides a more trustworthy interaction than one that is uniformly assertive. The persuasion research suggests that achieving this calibration requires explicit training for rhetorical calibration, not just factual calibration, as the default behavior of frontier LLMs is to express maximum conviction regardless of certainty.

1.4 Broader Meta-Analytic Context

A Nature Scientific Reports meta-analysis found no significant overall difference in persuasive performance between LLMs and humans (g = 0.02, p = .530) when averaging across diverse settings. This apparent contradiction with the primary study highlights the importance of context: LLMs may not be uniformly more persuasive, but they are more persuasive in specific high-stakes conversational settings where their ability to express unwavering conviction is advantageous.

2. Logical Soundness Problems in Neurosymbolic Fact-Checking

The position paper "Logical Soundness is not a Reliable Criterion for Neurosymbolic Fact-Checking with LLMs" (arXiv:2604.04177) by Chan, Gaizauskas, and Zhao challenges a widespread assumption in the fact-checking community: that formal logic provides a reliable foundation for automated claim verification.

2.1 The Core Argument

Neurosymbolic fact-checking systems typically work in two stages: (1) an LLM translates natural language claims into logical formulae, and (2) a formal verifier checks whether the formulae are logically sound—that is, whether the conclusions follow validly from premises verified to be true. The authors argue that this approach structurally fails to detect misleading claims because of systematic divergences between what is logically sound and what humans typically infer and accept.

2.2 Typology of Failures

Drawing on cognitive science and pragmatics research, the authors present a typology of cases where logically sound conclusions systematically elicit human inferences that are unsupported by the premises. Consider the claim "Country X increased defense spending by 300%." This may be logically sound (verified against budget data), yet misleading if the base amount was trivially small. A formal verifier confirms the mathematical truth; a human reader infers military escalation. The gap between logical validity and pragmatic implication is where misleading claims hide.

Categories of failure identified include:

  • Scalar implicature violations: Logically true statements that conversationally imply something false.
  • Base rate neglect: Percentage claims that are technically correct but misleading due to small absolute numbers.
  • Selective framing: True statements that create false impressions through omission of relevant context.
  • Causal implication from correlation: Logically valid statistical claims that pragmatically imply causation.

2.3 Detailed Failure Case Analysis

The typology of failures deserves concrete illustration to understand its practical impact. Consider the claim "Drug X reduced mortality by 50% in clinical trials." A neurosymbolic fact-checker would translate this to a logical formula, verify it against trial data, and confirm it as logically sound. But the claim could be deeply misleading if the absolute risk reduction was from 0.02% to 0.01%—a technically correct 50% relative reduction that, in absolute terms, represents a trivially small effect. A human reader, encountering "reduced mortality by 50%," would infer a large, clinically significant effect. The gap between the logical truth and the pragmatic inference is where misinformation operates.

Another illustrative case involves selective framing through omission. The claim "Country X's GDP grew 3% last year" may be logically sound when verified against economic data, but misleading if the population grew 4% over the same period—meaning per-capita GDP actually declined. A formal verifier checks only what is stated, not what is omitted, and this asymmetry is systematically exploitable by actors producing technically accurate but contextually misleading content.

The causal implication category is especially relevant for scientific fact-checking. Statistical claims like "Countries with higher chocolate consumption have more Nobel laureates per capita" are logically verifiable correlations that pragmatically imply causation. Formal logic correctly identifies these as correlational, but the natural language framing leads most readers to infer a causal relationship. The gap between formal and pragmatic interpretation is the vulnerability that current neurosymbolic systems cannot close.

2.4 The Proposed Inversion

Rather than treating LLMs' tendency toward human-like reasoning as a bug to be corrected through formalization, the authors propose leveraging it as a feature:

"We advocate for leveraging the human-like reasoning tendencies of LLMs as a feature rather than a bug, and using these models to validate the outputs of formal components against potentially misleading conclusions."

In this inverted architecture, the formal verifier establishes logical validity, and then an LLM-based "pragmatic auditor" evaluates whether the logically valid conclusion would mislead a typical human reader. This hybrid approach exploits the complementary strengths of formal and neural reasoning rather than treating them as competitors.

3. Reference Hallucination Detection in Commercial LLMs

The paper "Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations in Commercial LLMs and Deep Research Agents" (arXiv:2604.03173) by Rao, Wong, and Callison-Burch provides the first large-scale empirical analysis of citation reliability across commercial AI systems, with a clear path to mitigation.

3.1 Scale of Analysis

The study examined over 221,000 URLs across two benchmarks:

  • DRBench: 53,090 URLs from 10 models and agents
  • ExpertQA: 168,021 URLs from 3 models across 32 academic fields

The Wayback Machine was used as the ground truth source for URL existence verification, distinguishing between truly hallucinated URLs (no record of ever existing) and stale URLs (previously valid but now broken).

3.3 Deep Research Agents vs. Search-Augmented LLMs

A critical finding is that deep research agents—systems that perform multi-step web searches and synthesize information from multiple sources—generate substantially more citations per query than standard search-augmented LLMs. However, they hallucinate URLs at higher rates. The increased citation density creates an illusion of thoroughness while actually introducing more fabricated references into the output.

3.4 The urlhealth Correction Tool

The researchers developed and released urlhealth, an open-source tool for classifying citation status (valid, stale, hallucinated). When integrated as a post-processing step, urlhealth reduced non-resolving citations by 6-79x to under 1%. The wide range in improvement reflects differences in models' tool-use competence—some models effectively use urlhealth to find replacement URLs, while others struggle with the correction workflow.

4. BibTeX Citation Hallucinations in Scientific Publishing Agents

The companion paper "BibTeX Citation Hallucinations in Scientific Publishing Agents: Evaluation and Mitigation" (arXiv:2604.03159) by Rao and Callison-Burch provides a complementary analysis focusing on the structural metadata of citations rather than URL validity.

4.1 Benchmark Construction

The researchers constructed a benchmark of 931 papers across four scientific domains and three citation popularity tiers (popular, low-citation, and recent post-cutoff). Three frontier models were evaluated—GPT-5, Claude Sonnet-4.6, and Gemini-3 Flash—generating approximately 23,000 field-level observations across nine BibTeX fields.

4.2 The Accuracy Paradox

The headline finding reveals a deceptive gap between field-level and entry-level accuracy:

  • Field-level accuracy: 83.6% of individual BibTeX fields are correct.
  • Entry-level accuracy: Only 50.9% of complete BibTeX entries are fully correct.

This means that while most individual fields look plausible, nearly half of all generated citations contain at least one error. The implication is severe: a paper with 50 citations might have 25 with at least one incorrect field, yet the errors are distributed in a way that makes them hard to catch by spot-checking individual fields.

4.3 Error Taxonomy

A six-way error taxonomy categorizes the types of field-level errors:

4.4 Popularity-Dependent Degradation

Accuracy drops 27.7 percentage points when moving from popular to recent papers. This finding exposes a critical vulnerability: the papers most likely to need citation (recent work in active research areas) are exactly the papers where LLM citation quality is worst. Models show "heavy reliance on parametric memory even when search is available," suggesting they often generate citations from training data rather than performing fresh retrieval.

4.5 Mitigation: clibib

The clibib tool—an open-source deterministic BibTeX retrieval system—provides a two-stage mitigation: search for the correct entry in academic databases, then revise any fields that differ from the authoritative record. This integration improved accuracy to 91.5% with only a 0.8% regression rate (cases where the correction introduced new errors).

4.6 The Systemic Risk to Scientific Publishing

The combined findings of the reference hallucination and BibTeX citation papers paint a concerning picture for scientific publishing. As AI writing assistants become standard tools in academic work, the volume of citation errors entering the scholarly record is likely to increase substantially. Unlike human citation errors, which tend to be random and uncorrelated, AI citation errors are systematic: they cluster in specific fields (DOI, volume, pages), affect specific categories of papers (recent, niche), and are generated by the same few models used by millions of researchers.

This systematicity creates a new kind of scholarly infrastructure risk. If 50% of AI-generated BibTeX entries contain at least one error, and AI tools are used to generate citations in even 20% of published papers, the cumulative effect on the scholarly record could be significant. Incorrect DOIs create dead links in citation chains. Wrong page numbers make papers unfindable through standard reference lookups. Fabricated volume numbers corrupt bibliometric analysis. And hallucinated URLs (3-13% of citations) create phantom references that future researchers may waste time trying to locate.

The mitigation tools (urlhealth, clibib) demonstrate that the problem is technically solvable, but only if they are integrated into the publishing workflow. The challenge is adoption: individual researchers have little incentive to run additional verification tools, and journals have limited capacity to verify the tens of thousands of citations in each issue. The most promising path is integration into the submission and review pipeline itself, where citation verification becomes an automated pre-review step analogous to plagiarism detection.

The finding that models rely heavily on parametric memory even when search is available also has implications for AI tool design. Rather than giving models the option to search or generate from memory, citation tools should enforce retrieval-first workflows where the model cannot generate a citation without first querying an authoritative database. This architectural constraint trades some generation speed for dramatically improved accuracy, a worthwhile tradeoff for scientific publishing where accuracy is paramount.

5. GENFIG1: Visual Summaries of Scholarly Work

The automated generation of visual summaries for academic papers—the "Figure 1" that provides a graphical abstract of the work—represents an emerging application of multimodal AI to scientific publishing. While a specific paper titled "GENFIG1" was not identified in the April 2026 arXiv corpus, the related research landscape is active and growing.

5.1 FigGen: Text to Scientific Figure Generation

FigGen (arXiv:2306.00800) established the foundation for scientific figure generation from text descriptions, using diffusion models adapted for the structured visual language of academic figures. The system generates charts, diagrams, and schematic illustrations from natural language descriptions, with particular strength in generating bar charts, line plots, and flow diagrams that follow academic visual conventions.

5.2 The Scholarly Infrastructure Challenge

A Learned Publishing analysis from 2026 examines how generative AI is remaking scholarly infrastructure more broadly, noting that review articles, visual summaries, and meta-analyses are increasingly produced with AI assistance. The key concern is quality control: automatically generated visual summaries may accurately represent most aspects of a paper while subtly misrepresenting key nuances, and these errors are harder to catch in visual form than in text.

5.3 Generative Visual Reasoning

Gen-ViRe (arXiv:2511.13853) provides a benchmark for evaluating whether generative models can reason about visual content rather than merely producing plausible-looking images. This capability is essential for generating accurate scholarly figures: the system must understand the logical relationships between data, claims, and visual representations, not just produce aesthetically acceptable graphics.

6. Vero: An Open RL Recipe for Visual Reasoning

Vero (arXiv:2604.04917) by Sarch, Cai, Wang, Wu, Chen, and Liu introduces a fully open family of vision-language models trained with reinforcement learning that matches or exceeds existing open-weight models on diverse visual reasoning tasks. The work addresses a critical gap: most high-performing visual reasoning models are closed-source, preventing the research community from understanding what makes them work.

6.1 Scaling RL for Visual Reasoning

The core contribution is demonstrating how to scale RL training for visual reasoning effectively. The researchers constructed Vero-600K, a 600,000-sample dataset aggregated from 59 existing datasets spanning six broad task categories. Rather than using a single reward function, they designed task-routed rewards that handle heterogeneous answer formats—multiple choice, numerical, free-form text, and spatial coordinates—under a unified training framework.

6.2 The Diversity Thesis

The most important finding from systematic ablations is that different task categories elicit qualitatively distinct reasoning patterns that transfer poorly in isolation:

"Broad data coverage is the primary driver of strong RL scaling, suggesting that task diversity, not task depth, determines the ceiling of visual reasoning capability."

This finding has implications beyond visual reasoning: it suggests that the common practice of deep specialization through focused training may be less effective than broad exposure across diverse reasoning tasks. Models trained deeply on one category (e.g., chart understanding) improve substantially on that category but show minimal transfer to others (e.g., spatial reasoning).

6.4 Open Release

All data, code, and models are released publicly, enabling the research community to reproduce and build upon the results. This stands in contrast to the trend toward closed-source visual reasoning models and represents a deliberate effort to democratize frontier capabilities.

6.5 Implications for Code Agent Visual Understanding

Vero's visual reasoning capabilities have direct implications for code agents that must understand visual content. Modern software development increasingly involves visual artifacts: UI mockups, architecture diagrams, data flow charts, dashboard screenshots, and error visualizations. Code agents that can reason about these visual artifacts—"implement the layout shown in this mockup," "debug the issue visible in this error screenshot," "optimize the query plan shown in this diagram"—would be significantly more capable than text-only agents.

The diversity thesis from Vero suggests that training code agents on diverse visual reasoning tasks (not just code-related images) would produce better visual understanding than narrow training on software-specific visuals alone. A code agent trained on charts, scientific figures, spatial layouts, and natural images would develop more robust visual reasoning capabilities that transfer to the software domain, even though these training domains are superficially unrelated. This counterintuitive finding has implications for how organizations invest in training data for their internal tools: broader visual diversity may matter more than domain-specific depth.

The open release of Vero-600K and VeroEval also provides a foundation for evaluating code agents' visual capabilities. Current code agent benchmarks (SWE-bench, HumanEval, MBPP) are entirely text-based. A multimodal code agent benchmark that includes visual inputs—mockup-to-code, screenshot-to-debug, diagram-to-implementation—would fill a significant gap in the evaluation landscape and drive progress on capabilities that text-only benchmarks cannot measure.

7. AICA-Bench: VLM Affective Image Captioning Benchmark

AICA-Bench (arXiv:2604.05898) by She, Yao, Chen, Yu, Gao, and Jin addresses a notably underexplored dimension of vision-language model evaluation: affective understanding. The benchmark, accepted at Findings of ACL 2026, holistically examines VLMs' capabilities in analyzing the emotional and affective content of images.

7.1 Beyond Descriptive Captioning

Standard image captioning benchmarks evaluate whether models can describe what is visible in an image: objects, actions, spatial relationships. AICA-Bench evaluates a different and arguably more challenging capability: whether models can describe how an image feels. This requires understanding visual rhetoric (how composition choices evoke emotions), cultural visual conventions (what a sunset "means" beyond being a meteorological event), and the interaction between content and form.

7.2 Evaluation Dimensions

The benchmark evaluates VLMs across multiple dimensions of affective understanding:

  • Emotion recognition: Can the model identify the primary emotional tone of an image?
  • Affective reasoning: Can the model explain why an image evokes a particular emotional response?
  • Sentiment consistency: Are the model's affective assessments consistent across similar images?
  • Cultural sensitivity: Does the model recognize that affective interpretations vary across cultural contexts?

7.3 Implications for Agent Systems

Affective image understanding is increasingly important for agent systems operating in domains like social media moderation, mental health monitoring, creative assistance, and customer experience analysis. An agent that can accurately describe the factual content of an image but misreads its emotional register will produce responses that feel tone-deaf or inappropriate. AICA-Bench provides the first standardized evaluation framework for this capability.

8. Cross-Cutting Analysis: The Epistemology of Machine-Generated Knowledge

The seven research threads in this module collectively address a question that has moved from philosophical speculation to urgent practical concern: how much can we trust knowledge generated or curated by AI systems? The answer emerging from this research is nuanced—neither the optimistic "AI will revolutionize research" nor the pessimistic "AI output is unreliable"—but rather a differentiated assessment that depends critically on the type of knowledge, the verification infrastructure available, and the human oversight in the loop.

8.1 The Conviction-Accuracy Decoupling

The persuasion research reveals a deeply troubling pattern: LLMs express higher conviction than humans regardless of whether they are correct. Claude 3.5 Sonnet's superior persuasiveness in both truthful and deceptive conditions suggests that the model's conviction is not calibrated to its accuracy—it is equally assertive when right and when wrong. This conviction-accuracy decoupling is the hallucination problem expressed in its most socially consequential form.

The BibTeX citation paper provides quantitative evidence for the same pattern in a different domain. Models generate citations with 83.6% field-level accuracy—high enough to appear authoritative—but only 50.9% entry-level accuracy, meaning nearly half of all citations contain at least one error. The surface-level plausibility creates a false sense of reliability that is arguably more dangerous than obviously wrong output, because it passes casual inspection while corrupting the scholarly record.

The neurosymbolic fact-checking critique adds another dimension: even when LLM output is logically valid, it can be pragmatically misleading. Statements that are technically true but contextually deceptive represent a form of hallucination that existing detection methods cannot catch, because the content passes both factual verification (it is true) and logical verification (it follows from premises). Only pragmatic verification—checking whether the statement would mislead a typical reader—can identify this failure mode.

8.2 The Detection-Correction Pipeline

A encouraging finding across multiple papers is that hallucination detection and correction are tractable problems when approached systematically. The urlhealth tool reduces non-resolving citations by 6-79x. The clibib tool improves BibTeX accuracy from 50.9% to 91.5% full-entry correctness. These are not marginal improvements but transformative ones, suggesting that the right post-processing pipeline can convert unreliable LLM output into publishable quality.

The key insight is that detection and correction operate on different principles than generation. Generation is a creative act that draws on the model's parametric memory and tends toward plausible rather than accurate outputs. Detection is a verification act that can leverage external databases, URL resolution, and cross-referencing—deterministic tools that complement the model's generative capabilities. Correction combines both: using the model's generation capability to produce alternatives, constrained by deterministic verification to ensure accuracy.

This suggests a general architecture for reliable AI-assisted knowledge production: generate with the LLM, verify with deterministic tools, correct any discrepancies using the LLM constrained by verification results. The two-stage integration approach from the BibTeX paper (search then revision, achieving 91.5% accuracy with 0.8% regression) instantiates this pattern and demonstrates its effectiveness.

8.3 The Popularity Bias in Parametric Knowledge

The BibTeX paper's finding that accuracy drops 27.7 percentage points from popular to recent papers exposes a structural bias in LLM-generated knowledge: models are more reliable about popular topics than niche or recent ones. This is an expected consequence of training on internet text, where popular topics have more coverage and more redundant sources, but it has insidious implications for research applications.

Researchers are most likely to need citation assistance for recent papers (which they may not have memorized) or niche papers (which are harder to find). These are exactly the categories where LLM citation quality is worst. The finding that models show "heavy reliance on parametric memory even when search is available" suggests that models default to what they "remember" from training rather than performing genuine retrieval, even when retrieval tools are explicitly provided. This is a form of cognitive laziness—or, in more technical terms, a failure of the model to appropriately weight the reliability of its parametric memory versus external retrieval results.

The implication for agent system design is clear: for knowledge-sensitive tasks, agent architectures should force retrieval before generation, rather than allowing the model to generate first and retrieve only when uncertain. The clibib integration demonstrates this pattern: deterministic retrieval establishes ground truth, and the model's role is limited to bridging any gaps between retrieval results and the desired output format.

8.4 Visual Reasoning as Hallucination Countermeasure

Vero's open RL recipe for visual reasoning provides an interesting counterpoint to the textual hallucination findings. Visual reasoning tasks have a natural verification mechanism: the answer can often be checked against the image itself. A model that claims a bar chart shows value X can be verified by reading the chart. This built-in verifiability may explain why RL training is particularly effective for visual reasoning—the reward signal is clear and unambiguous, unlike textual tasks where "correctness" is often subjective or requires external knowledge.

The diversity thesis from Vero (broad data coverage drives RL scaling better than deep specialization) also has implications for hallucination reduction. Models trained deeply on one domain may overfit to domain-specific patterns that don't generalize, producing confident but wrong answers on out-of-distribution inputs. Broadly trained models, by contrast, develop more conservative reasoning strategies that generalize better, even if they sacrifice peak performance on any single domain. This suggests that hallucination-resistant models may require deliberately diverse training rather than domain-specific optimization.

8.5 The Affective Hallucination Blind Spot

AICA-Bench's focus on affective image understanding reveals an entirely underexplored category of hallucination: emotional and affective mischaracterization. Current hallucination detection methods focus on factual claims (Is this citation real? Does this code compile? Is this statistic correct?) but have no framework for detecting affective errors (Does this image evoke the emotion the model claims? Is the model's characterization of the image's emotional tone consistent with human perception?).

Affective hallucinations are particularly concerning in applications like mental health monitoring (where misreading emotional content could trigger inappropriate interventions), social media moderation (where emotional tone determines whether content is harmful), and creative assistance (where emotional resonance is the primary quality criterion). The absence of standardized evaluation frameworks for affective accuracy means that current deployed systems may have significant blind spots in emotional understanding that go entirely undetected by existing quality metrics.

8.6 Toward Reliable Machine-Generated Knowledge

Synthesizing across all seven research threads, a practical framework for reliable machine-generated knowledge emerges:

  • Layer 1: Generation with awareness. Train models to express calibrated uncertainty (BAS) and to prefer retrieval over parametric memory for factual claims.
  • Layer 2: Deterministic verification. Apply post-generation tools (urlhealth, clibib, symbolic validators) to check factual claims against authoritative sources.
  • Layer 3: Pragmatic auditing. Use LLMs themselves (as proposed in the neurosymbolic critique) to evaluate whether technically correct output could mislead human readers.
  • Layer 4: Human oversight. Maintain human review for high-stakes outputs, guided by model confidence (high-BAS outputs can be trusted; low-BAS outputs require review).
  • Layer 5: Longitudinal monitoring. Track hallucination rates over time to detect drift, with particular attention to the popularity bias that causes accuracy to vary across topics.

No single layer is sufficient, but the combination provides defense-in-depth against the full spectrum of hallucination types identified in this module: factual fabrication (caught by Layer 2), pragmatic misleading (caught by Layer 3), overconfident errors (caught by Layer 1 + 4), and affective mischaracterization (requiring Layer 4 + 5 until automated affective evaluation matures).

Synthesis: The Hallucination-Reliability Frontier

The papers in this module converge on a central tension in the deployment of LLM-powered agents: these systems are simultaneously more capable and more unreliable than previous generations of AI tools.

  • Persuasion and trust: LLMs can be more persuasive than humans, but this capability cuts both ways—high-conviction wrong answers are the hallucination failure mode in its most dangerous form.
  • Logic vs. pragmatics: The neurosymbolic fact-checking critique reveals that logical validity is necessary but not sufficient for trustworthy verification, because human reasoning operates on pragmatic rather than formal logical principles.
  • Citation reliability: Both reference hallucination papers demonstrate that citation errors are systematic, predictable, and correctable—but only if detection tools are integrated into the publishing workflow.
  • Open science as antidote: Vero's open RL recipe demonstrates that transparency about training methods enables the community to identify and address failure modes that remain hidden in closed-source systems.
  • Affective gaps: AICA-Bench highlights that current evaluation frameworks miss entire dimensions of understanding, suggesting that hallucination in emotional and affective domains is an underexplored failure mode.
