# The PNW I-5 Corridor — Helium Refinement and Distribution Potential

## Geographic Context

The Interstate 5 corridor from Bellingham, WA to Medford, OR passes through one of North America's most concentrated technology and manufacturing regions. This corridor connects the Silicon Forest (Portland/Hillsboro semiconductor cluster) with the Puget Sound technology hub and provides direct access to West Coast ports for Pacific Rim trade.

## Why the I-5 Corridor for Helium

### 1. Electricity Cost Advantage
PNW industrial electricity rates of $0.06–$0.08/kWh — among the lowest in the nation — make energy-intensive operations like helium liquefaction (-269°C) significantly more economical here than in the American Southwest or Gulf Coast.

### 2. Port Access
The Port of Tacoma / Northwest Seaport Alliance (NWSA) provides containerized shipping to East Asian semiconductor hubs:
- **15–25 days** to Kaohsiung (TSMC), Busan (Samsung/SK Hynix), and Tokyo
- Well within the **35–48 day** liquid helium holding window for ISO containers
- Alternative to congested LA/Long Beach ports

### 3. Existing Distribution Infrastructure
The PNW already has an established industrial gas distribution network (see: PNW Distribution Network research). What it lacks is upstream capacity — local purification and liquefaction. The distribution "last mile" is solved; the gap is in production and processing.

### 4. Customer Proximity
Semiconductor fabrication, research universities, and technology companies along the corridor create local demand:
- **Hillsboro, OR:** Intel D1X fab complex
- **Corvallis, OR:** Oregon State University, HP
- **Portland, OR:** OHSU research, PSU engineering
- **Seattle/Puget Sound:** Technology cluster, quantum computing research
- **Richland, WA:** PNNL cryogenic research

### 5. SMR Synergy
The PNW is a center for Small Modular Reactor (SMR) development. Some SMR designs use helium as a primary coolant in closed-loop systems. This creates a potential synergy: helium purification infrastructure serves both the semiconductor supply chain and emerging nuclear energy applications.

## The Co-operative Model

A cooperative business structure fits the helium corridor for several reasons:

1. **Shared infrastructure costs** — Cryogenic liquefaction equipment is expensive but serves all members
2. **Distributed risk** — No single operator bears the full capital burden
3. **Local benefit** — Co-op profits stay in the region rather than flowing to distant corporate parents
4. **Multiple stakeholders** — Gas suppliers, fab operators, research institutions, and transportation providers all benefit from participation
5. **Resilience** — A co-op with many members is harder to disrupt than a single corporate operation

### How It Could Work

```
MEMBER NODES                          SHARED HUB                      CUSTOMERS
  Local gas suppliers     ──────►    Purification (shared)    ──────►  Fabs
  Small-scale extractors             Liquefaction (shared)             Labs
  Industrial byproduct               Quality lab (shared)              Universities
  Fab recycling recovery             Storage (shared)                  Export
```

Each member contributes crude helium (from whatever source — purchased, extracted, or recovered) and receives purified product at cost plus a modest processing fee. The hub is owned collectively.

## The Opportunity

The PNW I-5 corridor is not going to become a primary helium extraction region — the geology doesn't support it. But it has everything needed to become a **value-added processing and distribution zone**:

- Buy crude helium from US primary producers (Colorado, Minnesota, Arizona, New Mexico)
- Purify to Grade-A (99.999%) or Research Grade (99.9999%) using cheap PNW electricity
- Distribute locally to Silicon Forest fabs and research institutions
- Export via West Coast ports to East Asian semiconductor hubs

The value is in the processing, not the extraction. The corridor adds purity, speed, and reliability to raw material sourced from elsewhere. This is the "craft brewery" model applied to industrial gas: small scale, high quality, local, responsive, and trusted.
