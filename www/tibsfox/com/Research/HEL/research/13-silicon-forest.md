# The Silicon Forest -- Portland Metro Semiconductor Ecosystem

## Origins: Tektronix and the Seed

The Silicon Forest traces to a single company: **Tektronix**, founded in Portland, Oregon on November 21, 1946 by C. Howard Vollum and Melvin J. "Jack" Murdock. Vollum, a radar engineer who served in the Army Signal Corps during World War II, built the company around a superior oscilloscope design -- the Model 511 -- that offered triggered sweep and higher bandwidth than any competitor. By the 1960s, Tektronix was the world's dominant oscilloscope manufacturer, with more than 10,000 employees in Beaverton, Oregon and a global reputation for precision engineering.

Tektronix's significance extends far beyond its own products. The company trained a generation of engineers in precision analog and digital electronics, clean manufacturing processes, and the test-and-measurement discipline that underpins semiconductor development. When Intel, Hewlett-Packard, and other technology companies looked for West Coast locations outside California in the 1970s, the Tektronix-trained talent pool was a decisive factor. The Silicon Forest is, in a meaningful sense, Tektronix's legacy -- the ecosystem that grew from the engineering culture Vollum and Murdock established.

## Intel in Oregon: The Decision That Shaped Everything

In 1974, Intel Corporation -- then a 6-year-old company with approximately 3,000 employees, headquartered in Santa Clara, California -- chose Aloha, Oregon (an unincorporated community west of Portland) as the site for its first fabrication facility outside California. The site became known as the Jones Farm campus.

The decision was driven by a specific combination of factors:

1. **Cheap, available land.** The Tualatin Valley in Washington County was still largely agricultural in 1974. Industrial-zoned land was available at a fraction of Santa Clara costs.

2. **Clean water.** Semiconductor fabrication requires enormous volumes of high-quality source water for ultrapure water (UPW) production. The Tualatin Valley's watershed, fed by the Coast Range, provided reliable, low-mineral-content water.

3. **Reliable, inexpensive power.** The Bonneville Power Administration's Columbia River hydroelectric system delivered industrial electricity at rates well below California's even in the 1970s. This advantage has persisted for fifty years and remains in effect (see Document 07 for current rate comparisons).

4. **Tektronix-trained workforce.** Intel needed engineers familiar with precision manufacturing, electronic test, and clean processes. Tektronix had been training exactly these engineers for twenty-five years.

5. **No state sales tax.** Oregon has no state sales tax, reducing the cost of procuring the hundreds of millions of dollars in capital equipment that fab construction requires.

6. **Quality of life.** Portland in 1974 offered an attractive living environment for engineers relocating from California -- lower housing costs, less congestion, natural amenities -- that helped with recruiting.

Intel's Oregon presence grew steadily from the Jones Farm campus through a series of expansions:

| Facility | Year | Investment | Significance |
|----------|------|------------|-------------|
| Jones Farm / Aloha campus | 1974 | Initial (~$10M) | First Oregon fab; F20 complex evolved here |
| Ronler Acres campus (Hillsboro) | 1992 | $1B+ initial | Intel's largest site globally; R&D + manufacturing |
| D1C fab | ~1999 | Included in Ronler Acres | 200mm development fab |
| D1D fab | ~2003 | ~$2B | 300mm development fab |
| D1X fab, Mod 1 | 2013 | ~$3B | First phase of current leading-edge development fab |
| D1X fab, Mod 2 | 2015 | ~$3B | Expansion of D1X |
| D1X fab, Mod 3 | ~2021 | ~$3B | Latest D1X expansion |
| Cumulative Oregon investment | 1974-2026 | **$52+ billion** | Total including all facilities, equipment, infrastructure |

The D1X fab complex is where Intel develops its leading-edge manufacturing processes. Intel 4 (formerly 7nm), Intel 3, and Intel 20A/18A (incorporating Gate All Around and backside power delivery) were all pioneered at D1X before being transferred to high-volume manufacturing sites in Chandler (Arizona), Leixlip (Ireland), and Kiryat Gat (Israel).

This development role has a specific implication for helium: Oregon's helium needs are disproportionately weighted toward the highest purity specifications. Process development requires 6N (99.9999%) research-grade helium for experiments where even parts-per-million contaminants can confound results. High-volume manufacturing can often use 5N (99.999%) industrial-grade helium. A regional processing hub capable of producing 6N helium (Document 18 for hub design specifications) would serve Intel's most demanding and highest-value need.

### CHIPS Act Impact on Intel Oregon

Intel received up to $8.5 billion in CHIPS Act preliminary funding (announced March 2024), the largest single award under the Act. While the primary target of this funding is Intel's Ohio mega-fab complex (Fabs 52 and 62 in New Albany/Licking County), Intel's Oregon operations benefit in several ways:

- Process technology developed at D1X is the foundation for Ohio production
- Oregon workforce training programs receive CHIPS Act workforce development funding
- Oregon's supply chain infrastructure (including gas supply) supports the broader Intel manufacturing network
- Oregon-specific state incentives under SB 4 ($190 million) complement federal funding

Intel's Oregon workforce peaked at approximately 22,000-23,000 employees (pre-2024 layoffs; the company announced significant workforce reductions in 2024 as part of a cost restructuring, with Oregon headcount reduced by an estimated 10-15%). Even at reduced levels, Intel remains the largest single-site employer in Oregon.

## The Semiconductor Cluster: Beyond Intel

Intel anchors the Silicon Forest, but the ecosystem extends well beyond a single company. The cluster has developed depth across the semiconductor value chain: design, manufacturing, equipment, materials, EDA tools, and testing.

TriQuint Semiconductor, which merged with RFMD to form Qorvo in 2015, had its headquarters in Hillsboro and operated GaAs (gallium arsenide) fab lines -- specialty semiconductor manufacturing that also consumes helium for leak detection and thermal management. The TriQuint-to-Qorvo lineage illustrates how the Silicon Forest continuously generates and absorbs companies.

### Equipment and Materials Suppliers

The presence of leading-edge fabs attracts the companies that supply them. This clustering effect is self-reinforcing: equipment companies locate near their customers for service and support, which makes the region more attractive to additional fabs, which attracts more equipment companies.

| Category | Companies with Portland-Area Operations |
|----------|----------------------------------------|

| **Facilities** | JE Dunn, Andersen Construction |

Air Liquide, Linde, and Air Products each maintain significant presence in the Hillsboro area, providing pipeline delivery of bulk gases (nitrogen, oxygen, argon, hydrogen) to Intel's fab complex. Helium, as a cryogenic liquid requiring specialized transport, is delivered by tanker truck from distant sources rather than by pipeline. This is the supply chain gap that a regional helium hub would address.

Lam Research's Tualatin, Oregon operation is particularly relevant. Lam is the world's second-largest semiconductor equipment company (after Applied Materials) and manufactures the etch and deposition tools that consume helium for wafer backside cooling. Lam's demo and service operations in Tualatin use helium; their proximity to a regional helium source would reduce their own supply chain risk and provide a local customer for a PNW helium cooperative.

Oregon State University's College of Engineering is the most significant talent pipeline. OSU graduates approximately 1,500 engineers per year, a substantial fraction of whom enter the semiconductor industry. The university's Advanced Technology and Manufacturing Institute (ATAMI) and the Oregon Process Innovation Center (OPIC) conduct research directly relevant to semiconductor process development.

OIT's Wilsonville campus deserves special mention. The Oregon Institute of Technology operates a cleanroom training facility that teaches hands-on semiconductor manufacturing processes -- photolithography, etching, deposition, metrology -- at a two-year degree level. This is the kind of technician training program that the CHIPS Act workforce provisions specifically target and fund. A cryogenic gas handling module added to OIT's curriculum would directly serve the workforce needs of a regional helium cooperative (Document 09 for NSF ATE funding opportunities).

## Employment Impact

The Portland metro semiconductor industry supports an estimated 35,000-40,000 direct jobs across fabrication, design, equipment, materials, EDA, and related services, with Intel representing approximately half. The Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA) estimates a multiplier of 3.5-4.5x for semiconductor direct employment, meaning the ecosystem's total economic footprint including indirect and induced employment approaches 120,000-160,000 jobs in the Portland metropolitan area.

| Category | Estimated Direct Jobs | Key Employers |
|----------|----------------------|---------------|
| Fabrication + process development | 18,000-22,000 | Intel (dominant), Microchip, Qorvo |
| Equipment + materials | 5,000-8,000 | Lam Research, Applied Materials, ASML, KLA, BASF |
| EDA + design services | 3,000-5,000 | Siemens EDA, Synopsys, Cadence, Lattice |
| Test + measurement | 2,000-3,000 | Tektronix, Keysight (partial) |
| Construction + facilities | 2,000-4,000 | Hoffman Construction, specialty contractors |
| Industrial gases + chemicals | 500-1,000 | Air Liquide, Linde, Air Products |

Average semiconductor industry compensation in the Portland metro is approximately $110,000-$130,000 per year (all positions; engineering roles average $130,000-$160,000), significantly above the regional median household income of approximately $82,000. This wage premium makes semiconductor employment a significant driver of regional prosperity and tax revenue.

## The Helium Connection -- Specific and Quantified

Intel's D1X fab complex is the largest helium consumer in the Pacific Northwest. The specific consumption points, cross-referenced to Document 08 for detailed semiconductor helium use cases:

**1. EUV lithography purge gas.** Intel's D1X houses multiple ASML EUV scanners for process development. Each scanner consumes approximately 1,000-2,000 standard cubic feet of helium per day during operation. The exact scanner count at D1X is not publicly disclosed, but leading-edge process development at the Intel 3 and Intel 20A nodes requires intensive EUV use. Estimated monthly EUV helium consumption at D1X: 200,000-500,000 SCF.

**2. Wafer backside cooling.** D1X contains hundreds of etch chambers and ion implanters, each using helium for thermal management through the electrostatic chuck backside cooling interface. Estimated monthly cooling helium consumption: 100,000-200,000 SCF.

**3. Leak detection.** Continuous vacuum system qualification across all process tools. Consumption variable but ongoing.

**4. Carrier gas.** CVD and ALD precursor delivery. Consumption distributed across dozens of deposition tools.

**Total estimated D1X helium consumption: 400,000-800,000 SCF per month**, or approximately 300,000-600,000 liters of liquid helium per year when accounting for gas-liquid conversion (1 liter of liquid helium produces approximately 748 liters of gas at STP, so 400,000-800,000 SCF/month equates to approximately 500-1,100 liters of liquid helium per month).

At March 2026 crisis pricing of $50-$100 per liter (liquid helium), D1X's annual helium spend approaches $300,000-$1.3 million at the low end and potentially higher depending on spot pricing. At normalized pricing ($15-$25 per liter), annual spend is $90,000-$330,000. These figures cover only D1X; Intel's older Oregon fabs (D1D, Aloha campus) add additional consumption.

At crisis pricing, the PNW helium market is a $40-$80 million annual expenditure. At normalized pricing, it is $11-$21 million. A regional cooperative capturing 20-30% of this market at a processing margin of $5-$10 per liter generates $550,000-$3.2 million in annual revenue -- enough to sustain hub operations and repay capital investment (see Document 23 for the full financial model).

## The Opportunity -- What to Build and Why

The Silicon Forest has everything except upstream helium infrastructure.

| Present | Missing |
|---------|---------|
| World-class fabs (Intel D1X -- $52B+ cumulative investment) | Local helium purification (nearest purification: out of state) |
| Complete equipment ecosystem (ASML, Applied Materials, Lam, KLA) | Closed-loop helium recycling (no installed base at Oregon fabs) |
| Deep academic pipeline (OSU, PSU, OIT, UO) | Regional helium storage/buffer (no strategic inventory) |
| Mature distribution network (Airgas, Linde, Air Products, Central Welding) | R&D-grade (6N) gas blending capability |
| Active CHIPS Act funding (federal + Oregon SB 4) | Supply chain redundancy (100% out-of-region sourcing) |

The actionable steps, in priority order:

**1. Deploy recycling at Intel D1X.** This is the single highest-ROI action available. A $3.5M closed-loop recycling system at D1X, recovering 90% of helium from EUV, etch, and implant exhaust, reduces external helium purchases by approximately $20M-$40M per year at crisis pricing (see Document 09 for the ROI calculation). Intel's capital planning process should treat this as emergency procurement. Document 21 provides the technical specification; Document 15 identifies vendors with current lead times.

**2. Establish a regional purification hub.** A membrane + PSA system producing 5N and 6N helium from crude feedstock, sited in the Portland-Hillsboro corridor or the Centralia-Chehalis zone (Document 07 for siting analysis). Capital cost: $4-$6M for a complete hub with quality lab and fill station (Document 23 for the financial model). Revenue from serving 20-30% of regional demand: $550K-$3.2M annually.

**3. Form a helium cooperative under RCW 23.86 or Oregon equivalent.** Five or more members -- gas distributors, smaller fabs, research institutions -- pooling capital for the shared hub (Document 19 for the co-op playbook, Document 14 for Washington cooperative law). One-member-one-vote governance prevents monopoly control.

**4. Apply for CHIPS Act supply chain resilience funding.** The gas supply layer is explicitly eligible (Document 08 for CHIPS Act analysis). A cooperative application that demonstrates domestic helium sourcing, regional customer base, workforce development (OIT cryogenic technician training), and supply chain redundancy is well-positioned.

**5. Develop Pacific Rim export capability.** Once the hub is producing, establish containerized liquid helium export through NWSA (Tacoma) to Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan (Document 10 for the Ring of Fire trading network, Document 07 for port access and transit times). This transforms a regional supplier into a node in a resilient Pacific Rim network.

The Silicon Forest was built by one decision -- Intel choosing Aloha in 1974. Fifty-two years later, the ecosystem that decision created is the reason a regional helium infrastructure makes sense here and not somewhere else. The customer base exists. The distribution network exists. The funding pathways exist. The gap is upstream, and the gap is fillable.
