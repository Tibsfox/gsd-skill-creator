# Regulatory and Legal Landscape

## Purpose

This document maps the complete regulatory terrain for establishing helium production, processing, distribution, and export operations in the Pacific Northwest. Every permit, statute, regulation, and filing requirement identified here has been traced to its enabling legislation or administrative code. The intent is to provide a regulatory roadmap that a business formation attorney, a CHIPS Act grant writer, or a cooperative organizing committee can use as a working reference. Regulatory compliance is not a barrier to entry in this industry -- it is a well-marked path.

## Federal Helium Regulation

### Extraction Permits -- Federal and Tribal Lands

Helium extraction on federal lands requires permits from the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) under the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. 181 et seq.) and its amendments. The relevant permit structure:

| Permit/Authorization | Statute/Regulation | Issuing Agency | Timeline | Cost |
|---------------------|-------------------|----------------|----------|------|
| Application for Permit to Drill (APD) | 43 CFR 3162 | BLM State Office | 30-180 days (varies by state) | Filing fee + bond |
| Helium lease or unit agreement | 30 U.S.C. 181-287; 43 CFR 3100 | BLM | Competitive or noncompetitive lease | Rental + royalty |
| Surface use plan | 43 CFR 3162.3-1 | BLM | Concurrent with APD | Included in APD |
| Cultural resource survey (NHPA Section 106) | 54 U.S.C. 306108 | BLM + SHPO | 30-120 days | $5,000-$25,000 (consultant) |
| Endangered Species Act consultation | 16 U.S.C. 1536(a)(2) | USFWS | 30-135 days (informal/formal) | Variable |

**Important clarification for PNW operations:** The I-5 corridor hub and distribution operations described in Documents 07 and 18 do not involve extraction on federal lands. The PNW is a processing and distribution zone, not an extraction zone (Document 16 confirms no helium-bearing geology in western Washington or Oregon). Federal extraction permits are relevant for upstream crude helium suppliers in Colorado, Wyoming, Minnesota, New Mexico, and other producing regions who would supply crude helium to the PNW hub.

### Extraction Permits -- Private Lands

On private lands (where the landowner also holds mineral rights), no federal drilling permit is required. However, the following federal requirements still apply:

| Requirement | Authority | Detail |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| Clean Air Act compliance | 42 U.S.C. 7401 et seq. | New Source Review (NSR) for processing facilities emitting criteria pollutants; Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) or Nonattainment NSR depending on area classification |
| Underground Injection Control (UIC) | 42 U.S.C. 300h (Safe Drinking Water Act) | Required if injection wells are involved (waste disposal, enhanced recovery) |
| NPDES discharge permit | 33 U.S.C. 1342 (Clean Water Act) | Required for any water discharge to surface waters |
| SPCC plan | 40 CFR 112 | Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure plan if oil storage exceeds 1,320 gallons aggregate |
| State drilling permit | Varies by state | Standard drilling permit from state oil & gas commission (applies even on private land) |

### Helium Stewardship Act of 2013 (P.L. 113-40)

This Act amended the original Helium Act of 1925 (50 U.S.C. 167) and replaced the 1996 Helium Privatization Act (P.L. 104-273). Its provisions are now largely historical -- the Federal Helium Reserve's commercial role has ended -- but its legacy shapes the current market structure.

**Key provisions and their current status:**

| Provision | Original Intent | Current Status (March 2026) |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Extended Reserve operations through 9/30/2021 | Orderly wind-down of commercial sales | Complete; BLM ceased commercial sales FY2021 |
| Phase A: Auction-based disposal | Market pricing for Reserve helium | Complete; final auction FY2018 |
| Phase B: Federal users only | Preserve supply for NASA, DOD, DOE | In effect; residual crude helium allocated to federal agencies |
| Phase C: Final disposition | Transfer of remaining assets | In progress; Bush Dome and pipeline disposition ongoing |
| National Academy of Sciences study | Assessment of supply fundamentals | Complete; NRC/NAS reports published 2010, 2016 |
| Priority access for federal agencies | NASA, DOE, DOD guaranteed supply | In effect, but diminishing relevance as Reserve depletes |

**Practical implication:** The market is now fully private. There is no federal helium to buy, no BLM auction to participate in, and no administered price to undercut private operations. This is favorable for new entrants: the competitive distortion created by below-market federal helium is gone. Private helium operations now compete on a level playing field (see Document 12 for the history of how federal pricing distorted the market).

## International Trade Regulations

### Export Controls -- Helium-4

Helium-4 (standard helium, the commercially traded isotope) is **not a controlled substance** under US export regulations.

| Regulatory Regime | Status for He-4 | Authority |
|------------------|----------------|-----------|
| ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) | **Not controlled.** He-4 is not on the US Munitions List (22 CFR 121) | State Department / DDTC |
| EAR (Export Administration Regulations) | **Not on the Commerce Control List** (15 CFR Part 774, Supplement 1) | Commerce Department / BIS |
| Export license | **Not required** for most destinations | -- |
| OFAC sanctions | **Cannot export to comprehensively sanctioned countries** (Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Crimea/Donetsk/Luhansk regions, as of March 2026) | Treasury Department / OFAC |

Helium-4 is classified under **HTS 2804.29.0010** (Rare gases: helium) for customs purposes. Standard customs declarations (Shipper's Export Declaration / Electronic Export Information via AES) are required for exports valued above $2,500 per shipment (15 CFR Part 30).

**No quotas, no licenses, no technology transfer restrictions apply to He-4 export.** A PNW cooperative exporting liquid helium to Taiwan, South Korea, or Japan faces no export control barriers beyond standard customs documentation and the OFAC sanctions screen (none of these destination countries are sanctioned).

### Export Controls -- Helium-3

**Important exception:** Helium-3 is treated differently from He-4 due to its nuclear applications.

| Regulatory Regime | Status for He-3 | Authority |
|------------------|----------------|-----------|
| DOE Isotope Program control | **Allocation-controlled.** All US He-3 supply is allocated through ORNL Isotope Program | DOE Order 474.2 |
| NRC licensing | May require license depending on end use (neutron detection research with nuclear materials) | 10 CFR Part 30 |
| Export controls | **Subject to DOE Part 810** (10 CFR Part 810) if related to nuclear technology transfer | DOE/NNSA |
| EAR | Listed under **ECCN 1C233** (enriched lithium and He-3 for nuclear applications) | Commerce/BIS |

He-3 export for quantum computing applications (dilution refrigerators) is generally permitted but requires end-use documentation. Export for neutron detection to foreign government agencies may require additional DOE/NNSA review. See Document 11 for He-3 supply and applications.

### Customs and Trade Documentation

For containerized liquid helium export through NWSA (Tacoma):

| Requirement | Detail | Reference |
|------------|--------|-----------|
| Electronic Export Information (EEI) | Filed via AES (Automated Export System) for shipments >$2,500 | 15 CFR Part 30 |
| Commercial invoice | Standard commercial documentation | CBP requirements |
| Bill of lading | Ocean carrier document | Standard maritime |
| Certificate of analysis | Purity certification (5N or 6N) | Customer requirement |
| Dangerous goods declaration | Required for Class 2.2 cryogenic liquids | IMDG Code |
| Container inspection certificate | Required for ISO cryogenic containers | IMO T75 |

No bilateral helium-specific trade restrictions exist between the US and major Pacific Rim trading partners (Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, Australia, Singapore). General trade agreements (US-Korea FTA, US-Japan Trade Agreement) apply.

## Shipping Regulations for Cryogenic Gases

### Domestic Transport (DOT)

Liquid helium is classified as a **Division 2.2 non-flammable, non-toxic gas** under 49 CFR 173.115. This is the least restrictive hazmat classification -- helium is not flammable, not toxic, not corrosive, and not radioactive. The primary hazard is asphyxiation in confined spaces (displaces oxygen) and cryogenic burns from contact with the -269 degree Celsius liquid.

| Requirement | Regulation | Detail |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| UN number | 49 CFR 172.101 | **UN 1963** (Helium, refrigerated liquid) |
| Hazard class | 49 CFR 172.101 | **2.2** (non-flammable gas) |
| Packaging | 49 CFR 173.320 | DOT-specification or UN-rated cryogenic containers (dewars) |
| Shipping papers | 49 CFR 172 Subpart C | Hazmat shipping paper with proper shipping name, UN number, hazard class |
| Placards | 49 CFR 172 Subpart F | Green "Non-Flammable Gas" placard (Class 2.2) |
| Driver certification | 49 CFR 177.816 | CDL with hazmat endorsement (H endorsement) |
| Emergency response | 49 CFR 172.602 | ERG Guide 122 (Gases - Non-flammable, including cryogenic) |
| Quantity limitations | 49 CFR 173.320 | None for highway or rail transport of cryogenic liquids in specification containers |

For tube trailer transport of compressed helium gas (not liquid):
- **UN 1046** (Helium, compressed)
- Same Class 2.2 classification
- Tube trailers must meet DOT 3AAX or DOT 3T specification (49 CFR 178 Subpart C)
- Operating pressure typically 2,400-3,600 PSI

**Practical note for PNW operations:** Crude helium transport from extraction nodes to the processing hub (Document 06 for virtual plant architecture) would use tube trailers on I-5 and connecting highways. Finished liquid helium delivery to customers would use cryogenic tanker trucks. Both are routine operations that existing regional industrial gas distributors (Central Welding, Airgas, Linde) perform daily. No special routing permits, escorts, or route restrictions apply to helium transport.

### Maritime Transport (International)

Liquid helium international shipping is governed by the International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code, implemented in the US through 49 CFR 176.

| Requirement | Standard/Regulation | Detail |
|------------|---------------------|--------|
| IMDG classification | IMDG Code, Class 2.2 | Same as domestic: non-flammable, non-toxic |
| Portable tank instruction | **T75** | Required for refrigerated liquefied gases |
| Container standard | **ISO 1496-3** (thermal containers) | 20-foot or 40-foot intermodal containers with vacuum insulation |
| Typical container | Proprietary 40-foot ISO dewar | ~40,000-45,000 liters capacity; multi-layer vacuum insulation (MLI) |
| Boil-off rate | Design specification | ~0.3-0.5% per day (well-designed containers) |
| Holding time | Design specification | **35-48 days** before pressure relief venting begins |
| Vessel stowage | IMDG Code, Chapter 7.1 | "On deck or under deck" -- few restrictions for Class 2.2 |
| Documentation | IMDG Code, Chapter 5.4 | Dangerous Goods Transport Document |

**Container logistics:** Major helium suppliers (Linde, Air Liquide, Air Products) own fleets of proprietary 40-foot ISO cryogenic containers. These containers are purpose-built for liquid helium and are not commercially available for purchase at small scale. A PNW cooperative seeking export capability has two options: (a) lease containers from a major supplier (typical rates: $15,000-$25,000 per month per container) or (b) commission custom containers from manufacturers such as Chart Industries or CIMC Enric (Zhuhai, China). Custom containers require 9-18 months lead time and cost approximately $500,000-$1,000,000 each.

For initial export operations, leasing is the practical choice. As volume grows, owned containers become economic (breakeven versus leasing at approximately 12-15 round trips per year per container).

### Air Transport

Liquid helium can be shipped by air under IATA Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR), Class 2.2. Air transport is economically viable only for small quantities (laboratory samples, emergency supply) due to high per-kilogram air freight costs. For the bulk quantities relevant to this research series (thousands of liters per shipment), ocean transport through NWSA is the appropriate mode.

## Environmental Regulations for New Facilities

### Federal NEPA Review

New helium processing facilities on federal land require review under the National Environmental Policy Act (42 U.S.C. 4321 et seq.). The level of review depends on the scale and location:

| Level | Trigger | Timeline | Cost |
|-------|---------|----------|------|
| Categorical Exclusion (CE) | Small facilities with no significant impact, within previously assessed areas | 1-3 months | Minimal |
| Environmental Assessment (EA) | Most new industrial facilities; results in Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI) or triggers EIS | 6-18 months | $50,000-$200,000 |
| Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) | Significant environmental impact identified; large-scale facilities, sensitive areas | 18-36 months | $500,000-$2,000,000 |

A helium purification hub on private industrial-zoned land in Washington or Oregon would not trigger federal NEPA review unless it involves federal funding (which CHIPS Act grants would), federal permits (which are unlikely for a processing-only facility), or effects on federal resources. If CHIPS Act funding is sought, the Commerce Department's environmental review process applies, which is typically an EA-level analysis.

### State Environmental Review

**Washington -- SEPA (State Environmental Policy Act, RCW 43.21C)**

All non-exempt projects in Washington require SEPA review. The process:

| Step | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Environmental checklist | Applicant completes SEPA checklist (WAC 197-11-960) |
| Threshold determination | Lead agency (usually county or city) issues Determination of Nonsignificance (DNS) or Determination of Significance (DS) |
| DNS | Project proceeds with no further review |
| DS | Environmental Impact Statement required (rare for industrial facilities on zoned land) |
| Timeline | DNS: 30-60 days. DS/EIS: 6-18 months |
| Public comment | 14-30 day comment period for DNS; longer for DS |

**Oregon -- Land Use Planning**

Oregon's land use system (ORS Chapter 197) is among the most structured in the nation. Industrial facilities must be sited within designated Urban Growth Boundaries (UGBs) on industrially zoned land. The permitting process:

| Step | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Land use compatibility | Confirm site is zoned for industrial use (typically I-1, I-2, or M-1 zones) |
| Site design review | County or city review of building plans, landscaping, traffic |
| DEQ permits | Oregon DEQ air emissions (OAR 340-200 through 340-268) and wastewater discharge |
| Building permit | Standard commercial/industrial building permit |
| Timeline | 3-12 months depending on jurisdiction |

### Air Quality Permits

| Jurisdiction | Regulation | Requirement |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Federal (EPA) | Clean Air Act, 42 U.S.C. 7401 | New Source Review (NSR) for facilities with potential to emit above major source thresholds |
| Washington | WAC 173-400 (General), WAC 173-460 (Toxic air pollutants) | Notice of Construction (NOC) required before building new emission sources; Order of Approval from Ecology or regional clean air agency |
| Oregon | OAR 340-210 (Stationary source permits) | Air Contaminant Discharge Permit (ACDP) for new sources |

**Helium-specific note:** Helium processing (PSA, membrane separation, cryogenic liquefaction) has minimal air emissions. The primary emission sources are backup generators (diesel), HVAC systems, and any natural gas combustion for process heat. A helium processing facility is unlikely to exceed major source thresholds (100 tons/year of any criteria pollutant for PSD; 10 tons/year of any single HAP for NESHAP). It would likely qualify as a minor source requiring only a basic air permit.

### Pressure Vessel and Safety

| Jurisdiction | Regulation | Requirement |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Washington | WAC 296-104 (Boiler and pressure vessel safety) | Registration and inspection of pressure vessels by L&I |
| Oregon | OAR 437-002 (Oregon OSHA) | Compressed gas handling per OSHA 29 CFR 1910.101 and CGA (Compressed Gas Association) standards |
| Federal | OSHA 29 CFR 1910.101-105 | Compressed gas storage, handling, and use; references CGA pamphlets |
| ASME BPVC | Industry standard | All pressure vessels must meet ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, Section VIII |

## Cooperative Formation in Washington State

Washington cooperatives are formed under **RCW 23.86** (Cooperative Associations Act). This statute provides a proven, well-defined framework for multi-member industrial cooperatives with democratic governance.

### Incorporation Requirements

| Requirement | RCW Section | Detail |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| Incorporators | 23.86.030 | **Five or more persons** (individuals, partnerships, or corporations) |
| Articles of incorporation | 23.86.030 | Must state: name, purpose, principal office, term (or perpetual), number of directors (min 3), incorporator names and addresses |
| Name requirement | 23.86.030(a) | Must contain the word **"cooperative"** or **"co-op"** |
| Filing | 23.86.030 | Filed with Washington Secretary of State |
| Filing fee | SOS schedule | Approximately $180 (check current schedule at sos.wa.gov) |
| Registered agent | 23.86.035 | Required in Washington |

### Governance

| Feature | RCW Section | Detail |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Voting | 23.86.160 | **One member, one vote** regardless of capital contribution |
| Directors | 23.86.070 | Minimum 3; elected by members |
| Officers | 23.86.090 | President, secretary, and treasurer required |
| Meetings | 23.86.100 | Annual meeting required; special meetings per bylaws |
| Quorum | 23.86.100 | As specified in bylaws (typically 10-25% of members) |
| Amendments | 23.86.130 | Articles may be amended by 2/3 vote of members present at a meeting with proper notice |

### Financial Structure

| Feature | RCW Section | Detail |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Membership certificates | 23.86.030 | May issue membership certificates or shares |
| Capital | 23.86.150 | May accept member capital contributions; par or no-par value |
| Patronage dividends | 23.86.155 | Net savings distributed to members based on patronage (usage), not capital |
| Reserves | 23.86.155 | Bylaws may require reserve fund allocations before patronage distribution |
| Non-member business | 23.86.160 | May conduct business with non-members (important for serving non-member customers at market rates) |
| Tax treatment | IRC 1381-1388 | Cooperative taxation: income distributed as patronage dividends is deductible; undistributed income taxed at corporate rates |

### Alternative: RCW 24.06 (Nonprofit Mutual Corporations)

For a cooperative structure focused on shared services rather than patronage-based distribution, **RCW 24.06** (Nonprofit Miscellaneous and Mutual Corporations Act) may be more appropriate. This statute is used for mutual water associations, mutual power companies, and similar shared-infrastructure organizations.

Key differences from RCW 23.86:
- No patronage dividend requirement (services provided at cost)
- Nonprofit tax treatment (potentially more favorable for CHIPS Act grant applications)
- Dissolution provisions distribute assets to members in proportion to membership rights
- May be more natural fit for a "shared processing hub" model where members pay processing fees rather than receiving patronage dividends

An attorney advising on cooperative formation should evaluate both statutes. Document 19 provides a step-by-step formation playbook; Document 22 surveys cooperative precedents in industrial gas, agriculture, and energy.

### Oregon Cooperative Options

Oregon cooperatives may be formed under **ORS Chapter 62** (Cooperative Corporations). The structure is substantially similar to Washington's:

| Feature | ORS Section | Detail |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Incorporators | 62.105 | **Three or more persons** |
| One member, one vote | 62.265 | Democratic governance |
| Patronage dividends | 62.355 | Net margins distributed on patronage basis |
| Filing | 62.105 | Secretary of State, Business Division |

For a cooperative spanning both states (members in Washington and Oregon), incorporation in one state with registration as a foreign corporation in the other is standard practice.

## CHIPS Act Supply Chain Compliance

Under the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 (P.L. 117-167), Section 9902 and the Commerce Department's implementing rules (15 CFR Part 231, published February 2024), supply chain applicants must demonstrate compliance with specific requirements.

### Eligibility Criteria

| Requirement | Citation | Detail | How a Helium Co-op Meets It |
|------------|----------|--------|---------------------------|
| Domestic production capacity | 9902(a)(1) | Must support semiconductor manufacturing supply chains | Helium purification/distribution directly supports fab operations (Document 08) |
| Financial viability | 9902(a)(2)(E) | Cost-sharing or matching investment required | Cooperative member contributions + SBA loans (Document 09) |
| Workforce development | 9902(a)(2)(C) | Training plan and community investment | Cryogenic technician training via OIT/PCC partnership |
| National security guardrails | 9902(a)(5)(C) | Cannot expand manufacturing capacity in countries of concern for 10 years | N/A for PNW-only operations |
| Environmental justice | Commerce guidance | Climate and community impact assessment | Standard SEPA/NEPA review addresses this |
| Clawback provisions | 9902(a)(5)(A) | Return funds if commitments not met within specified timeline | Standard compliance monitoring |

### Application Process

The CHIPS Program Office at NIST manages the application process. As of March 2026:

| Step | Detail | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| Pre-application consultation | Contact chipsgrants@nist.gov to discuss eligibility | 2-4 weeks |
| Statement of Interest | Brief description of proposed project | Optional but recommended |
| Full application | Detailed financial plan, workforce plan, environmental assessment, security compliance | 60-90 days to prepare |
| Commerce review | Technical, financial, and policy review | 90-180 days |
| Due diligence | Verification of claims, site visits | 30-60 days |
| Award notification | Preliminary Memorandum of Terms (PMT) | After review |
| Disbursement | Milestone-based fund release | Per agreement |

The total timeline from initial contact to first fund disbursement is typically 9-18 months. Given that a modular helium hub can be operational in 12-18 months (Document 09), the CHIPS Act application process and facility construction can proceed in parallel.

## Regulatory Summary -- What Stands Between You and Operations

For a PNW helium purification and distribution cooperative:

| Regulatory Step | Estimated Timeline | Estimated Cost | Blocking? |
|----------------|-------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Cooperative incorporation (WA RCW 23.86) | 2-4 weeks | $500-$2,000 (filing + attorney) | No |
| SEPA environmental checklist | 1-2 months | $1,000-$5,000 | No |
| Air quality Notice of Construction (WA) | 2-4 months | $5,000-$15,000 (consultant + fees) | No |
| Building permit (industrial zone) | 2-6 months | $10,000-$50,000 (depending on scope) | No |
| Pressure vessel registration (WA L&I) | 1-2 months | $500-$2,000 | No |
| DOT hazmat registration | 1 month | $300-$600 per year (registration fee) | No |
| CHIPS Act application | 9-18 months | $50,000-$100,000 (consultant + preparation) | No (can run in parallel) |
| **Total serial timeline** | **6-12 months** | **$67,000-$175,000** | **No showstoppers** |

Compare this to the timeline for building a semiconductor fab: 38-48 months of construction plus 9-12 months of tooling, at a cost of $10-$20 billion, with NEPA EIS review, wetlands mitigation, water rights acquisition, and transmission line construction. A helium processing facility is three orders of magnitude simpler and faster to permit than the fabs it serves.

## The Key Takeaway

The regulatory landscape for helium production and distribution is well-established, well-documented, and navigable. There are no exotic permits, no novel regulatory frameworks, and no unusual barriers to entry. The required permits -- air quality, environmental review, pressure vessel, hazmat transport, business formation -- are standard industrial permits that thousands of facilities obtain every year.

The cooperative formation process in Washington is particularly straightforward: five people, Articles of Incorporation filed with the Secretary of State, one-member-one-vote governance, and a filing fee of approximately $180. The entire cooperative can be legally formed in less time than it takes to order a PSA unit from Chart Industries.

The CHIPS Act provides an explicit funding pathway for the gas supply layer of the semiconductor stack. The Commerce Department has published guidance confirming eligibility. The application process is documented and the program office is staffed.

The law is not the obstacle. The permits are not the obstacle. The regulatory landscape is a paved road -- all someone has to do is drive on it.

The obstacle has always been the false sense of abundance created by cheap Federal Reserve helium and Qatari imports (Document 12 for the history). That illusion ended on March 2, 2026 (Document 05). The road is open. The question is who drives first.
