# Regulatory and Legal Landscape

## Federal Helium Regulation

### Extraction Permits
Helium extraction on federal lands requires permits from the **Bureau of Land Management (BLM)** under the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920 (30 U.S.C. 181 et seq.). Operators need:

| Permit | Agency | Requirement |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Application for Permit to Drill (APD) | BLM | Required for federal/tribal lands |
| Clean Air Act compliance | EPA (42 U.S.C. 7401) | Emissions permits |
| Underground Injection Control (UIC) | EPA (Safe Drinking Water Act) | If injection wells are involved |
| State drilling permit | State oil & gas commission | Standard drilling permit (varies by state) |

On private lands, federal drilling permits are not required, but EPA air quality and water discharge permits still apply.

### Helium Stewardship Act of 2013 (P.L. 113-40)
This act amended the original Helium Act of 1925 (50 U.S.C. 167) and replaced the 1996 Privatization Act:

- Extended Federal Helium Reserve operations through September 30, 2021
- Established **auction-based pricing** replacing below-market administered prices
- Created a phased disposal timeline: Phase A (auctions), Phase B (federal users only), Phase C (final disposition)
- Required BLM to maintain storage and pipeline during transition
- Directed a National Academy of Sciences study on supply fundamentals
- Preserved priority access for federal agencies (NASA, DOE, DOD)

The Reserve's commercial sales ended in FY2021. The market is now fully private.

## International Trade

### Export Controls
Helium (He-4) is **not subject to ITAR** (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) and is **not on the Commerce Control List** under Export Administration Regulations (15 CFR 730–774). It is classified under **HTS 2804.29** (rare gases) for customs.

| Rule | Status for He-4 |
|------|----------------|
| ITAR | Not controlled |
| EAR/CCL | Not listed |
| Export license | Not required for most countries |
| OFAC sanctions | Cannot sell to sanctioned countries (North Korea, Iran, etc.) |

**Important exception:** Helium-3 is controlled as a **nuclear material** relevant to neutron detection and fusion research, with stricter DOE oversight through the Isotope Program.

### Customs
Standard customs declarations required for import/export. No quotas on helium. Major producing nations (Qatar, Algeria, Russia, US) have no bilateral helium-specific trade restrictions, though general sanctions regimes apply.

## Shipping Regulations for Cryogenic Gases

### Domestic (DOT)
Liquid helium is a **Division 2.2 non-flammable gas** under 49 CFR 173.115:
- Shipped in DOT-specification or UN-rated cryogenic containers (dewars)
- Hazmat shipping papers required (49 CFR 172)
- **UN Number:** UN 1963 (Helium, refrigerated liquid)
- Proper shipping name and placards required

### Maritime (International)
- **IMDG Code:** Class 2.2
- Cryogenic containers must meet **IMO portable tank standards** (T75 instruction for refrigerated liquefied gases)
- **ISO containers:** Liquid helium transport uses ISO 20-foot or 40-foot intermodal containers meeting ISO 1496-3 (thermal containers)
- Major suppliers use proprietary 40-foot ISO dewars (~11,000 gallons)
- Holding time: 35–48 days before boil-off venting begins

### Air Transport
- IATA Dangerous Goods Regulations Class 2.2
- Permitted on cargo aircraft with proper packaging

## Environmental Regulations for New Wells

| Requirement | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| NEPA review | Required on federal land (42 U.S.C. 4321). Typically Environmental Assessment (EA); EIS if significant impacts found |
| State equivalent | SEPA in Washington applies on state/private land |
| Clean Water Act §404 | If wetlands affected |
| NPDES permit | For any water discharge |
| Air quality | New Source Review under Clean Air Act for processing facilities |

## Washington and Oregon State Regulations

Neither Washington nor Oregon is a helium-producing region, so neither has helium-specific extraction regulations. Applicable regulations cover storage, distribution, and workplace safety:

### Washington
| Regulation | Agency | Scope |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| WAC 173-400 | Dept of Ecology | Air quality for processing facilities |
| WAC 296-104 | L&I | Pressure vessel safety |
| SEPA | Dept of Ecology | Environmental review for new facilities |

### Oregon
| Regulation | Agency | Scope |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| OAR 340-200 through 340-268 | DEQ | Air emissions |
| Oregon OSHA | DCBS | Compressed gas handling |

## Co-operative Formation in Washington

Washington cooperatives are formed under **RCW 23.86** (Cooperative Associations Act):

| Requirement | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Incorporators | Five or more persons (RCW 23.86.030) |
| Filing | Articles of incorporation with Secretary of State |
| Name | Must contain "cooperative" or "co-op" |
| Directors | Minimum 3 |
| Governance | **One member, one vote** (RCW 23.86.160) |
| Distribution | Patronage dividends (standard method) |
| Reporting | Annual report filing required |

For industrial cooperatives, also consider **RCW 24.06** (Nonprofit Miscellaneous and Mutual Corporations) if the structure is mutual-benefit rather than patronage-based.

## CHIPS Act Supply Chain Compliance

Under the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 (P.L. 117-167), Section 9902 and Commerce Department rules (15 CFR Part 231), a supply chain applicant must demonstrate:

| Requirement | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Domestic production | Capacity supporting semiconductor manufacturing supply chains |
| Financial viability | Cost-sharing / matching investment required |
| Workforce development | Plan for training and community investment |
| National security guardrails | Cannot expand manufacturing in countries of concern (China, Russia, Iran, North Korea) for 10 years |
| Environmental justice | Climate and community impact considerations per Commerce guidance |

Helium is critical to semiconductor fabrication — a domestic helium supplier qualifies under **supply chain resilience** provisions. This is not speculative: the Act explicitly includes the chemical and gas layers of the semiconductor stack.

## Key Takeaway

The regulatory landscape for helium production and distribution is well-established and navigable. There are no unusual barriers to entry — standard industrial gas regulations, standard environmental review, standard shipping rules. The co-operative formation process in Washington is straightforward (RCW 23.86, five people, one-member-one-vote). And CHIPS Act funding explicitly covers the gas supply chain that a helium corridor would serve.

The law is not the obstacle. The obstacle has always been the false sense of abundance created by cheap Federal Reserve helium and Qatari imports. That illusion ended on March 2, 2026.
