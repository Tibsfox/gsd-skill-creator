# Co-op Formation Playbook — From First Conversation to First Helium

## What This Document Is

This is the step-by-step legal and operational playbook for forming a helium cooperative in Washington State. It covers incorporation, governance design, capital structure, funding sources, and the specific sequence of actions from "five people in a room" to "operating helium purification hub." It is written so that a business attorney can review the legal structure, a banker can evaluate the financial framework, and the founding members can follow it as a checklist.

For cooperative precedents that validate this model, see Document 22. For the financial projections, see Document 23. For the physical facility this co-op will build and operate, see Document 18.

## Legal Foundation

**Washington State: RCW 23.86** (Cooperative Associations Act)

Washington's cooperative statute is mature, well-tested, and straightforward. Cooperatives formed under RCW 23.86 have operated in Washington for over a century across agriculture, utilities, retail, finance, and housing. The statute provides clear rules for formation, governance, membership, and dissolution.

**Alternative structure: RCW 24.06** (Nonprofit Miscellaneous and Mutual Corporations). If the co-op's structure is mutual-benefit rather than patronage-based (for example, if members share processing capacity at cost rather than distributing patronage dividends based on volume), RCW 24.06 may be more appropriate. Discuss with your attorney. This playbook assumes RCW 23.86 as the primary framework.

**Oregon alternative:** If the hub is sited in Oregon, cooperatives are formed under **ORS Chapter 62** (Cooperative Corporations). The structure is similar to Washington's -- one member one vote, patronage dividends, democratic governance. Oregon's filing is through the Secretary of State, Corporation Division. The playbook steps below apply with minor procedural differences.

## Step-by-Step Formation Sequence

### Step 1: Assemble Founding Members (Month 1)

**Minimum required: five persons** (RCW 23.86.030). "Persons" includes individuals and organizations (corporations, LLCs, partnerships). This means a co-op can be founded by three individuals and two companies, for example.

**Who should be in the room:**

| Role | Why They Matter | Where to Find Them |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| Gas distribution professional | Understands industrial gas logistics, safety, regulatory compliance | Contact Central Welding (Smokey Point, WA), Industrial Source (Salem, OR), or regional gas distributors |
| Fab operations or facilities manager | Understands customer requirements, purity specifications, delivery logistics | Intel Hillsboro facilities team, OHSU research administration, OSU cryogenics lab |
| Cryogenic or process engineer | Can evaluate equipment, design process flows, manage commissioning | PNNL industry liaison office, UW Clean Energy Institute, OSU Chemical Engineering department |
| Business/finance professional | Can structure the co-op finances, manage grant applications, negotiate supply contracts | PNW cooperative development centers (see Step 2 below) |
| Community member with relevant expertise | Provides governance perspective, community connection, political support | REI, PCC, or BECU board/governance alumni who understand cooperative principles |

**The founding group does not need to include all stakeholder classes.** The five founders incorporate the co-op and elect the initial board. Additional members join after incorporation. The founding group's primary job is to get the legal entity created and the governance framework established.

**First meeting agenda:**
1. Agree on the co-op's purpose (helium purification and distribution for PNW semiconductor and research customers)
2. Agree on the target scale (small hub, ~100 L/day liquid output per Document 18)
3. Agree on initial capital contribution expectations ($50K-$500K per founding member, depending on class)
4. Assign tasks: one person leads articles drafting, one leads bylaws, one leads funding research, one leads equipment/technical evaluation, one leads member recruitment
5. Set a target date for filing articles (6-8 weeks from this meeting)

### Step 2: Engage Professional Support (Month 1-2)

**Co-op development organizations** (engage immediately -- many provide free or subsidized services):

- **Northwest Cooperative Development Center (NWCDC)** -- Olympia, WA. Provides technical assistance for cooperative formation including business planning, bylaws review, and governance design. Funded in part by USDA. Website: nwcdc.coop
- **National Cooperative Business Association CLUSA (NCBA CLUSA)** -- Washington, DC. Maintains bylaws templates, governance guides, and a national directory of cooperative attorneys. Website: ncba.coop
- **Cooperative Development Foundation** -- Provides educational resources and referrals
- **WA State Dept of Commerce** -- Can connect you to the Innovation Cluster Accelerator Program (ICAP) for funding, and to cooperative development resources

**Co-op attorney** (engage within 2 weeks of the founding meeting):

Washington has several law firms experienced in cooperative formation. Key firms and attorneys to contact:

- **Pacifica Law Group** (Seattle) -- Municipal and cooperative governance experience
- **Stoel Rives** (Seattle/Portland) -- Business formation including cooperatives
- **Schwabe Williamson & Wyatt** (Portland/Seattle) -- Agricultural and cooperative law

Ask the NWCDC for their current referral list. You want an attorney who has formed at least one cooperative under RCW 23.86 (or ORS 62 for Oregon).

**Budget for legal formation:** $5,000-$15,000 for articles, bylaws, and initial filings. This is a one-time cost.

### Step 3: Draft Articles of Incorporation (Month 1-2)

The Articles of Incorporation are the co-op's charter document, filed with the Secretary of State. They must include:

| Element | Content | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Name | Must contain "cooperative" or "co-op" (RCW 23.86.030). Example: "Pacific Helium Cooperative" or "Cascadia Gas Co-op" or "PNW Helium Cooperative" | Check name availability with WA SOS before finalizing: sos.wa.gov |
| Purpose | Helium purification, liquefaction, distribution, recycling services, and related activities for the benefit of members | Broad purpose language allows future expansion without amending articles |
| Duration | Perpetual | Standard |
| Registered office and agent | Must be a Washington State address and a person or entity authorized to receive legal process | Can be any founding member's address initially, or use a registered agent service ($100-$200/year) |
| Number and classes of membership | Define member classes (see Step 4 below) | Classes should be defined in articles, with detailed rights specified in bylaws |
| Initial board of directors | Minimum 3 directors. List names and addresses. | Board members must be members of the co-op (RCW 23.86.090) |
| Capital structure | Describe membership shares/units and any preferred shares | Keep articles language flexible; put details in bylaws |

**Filing:** Submit to Washington Secretary of State, Corporations Division. Online filing available at sos.wa.gov. Filing fee: approximately $180 (check current schedule). Processing time: 1-2 business days for online filing.

### Step 4: Design Governance and Draft Bylaws (Month 2-3)

The bylaws are the operating manual for the co-op. They are more detailed than the articles and can be amended by the membership (typically by supermajority vote) without re-filing with the state.

#### Membership Classes

A mixed-stakeholder cooperative serving multiple roles in the helium supply chain should use multiple membership classes. Each class has the same voting weight per member (one member, one vote per RCW 23.86.160) but may have different capital contribution requirements and patronage dividend formulas.

**Recommended class structure:**

| Class | Members | Capital Contribution | Patronage Basis | Board Seats |
|-------|---------|---------------------|----------------|------------|
| **Producer** | Crude helium suppliers, extraction node operators | $50,000-$250,000 | Volume of crude helium supplied to the co-op | 1-2 seats |
| **Processor** | Hub operators, equipment investors, technical partners | $100,000-$500,000 | Processing services provided, equipment contributed | 1-2 seats |
| **Consumer** | Semiconductor fabs, research labs, universities, hospitals buying helium | $25,000-$100,000 | Volume of purified helium purchased from the co-op | 2-3 seats |
| **Associate** | Supporters, future members, community partners | $1,000-$10,000 | None (no patronage dividends) | 0 seats (non-voting or advisory) |

**Decision tree for class structure:**
- If the co-op has mostly consumer members (fabs and labs buying helium): weight board representation toward consumers. Their needs drive product specifications and delivery requirements.
- If the co-op has strong producer participation (crude suppliers as members): balance producer and consumer representation. Producer-consumer tension on pricing is healthy and should be managed through governance, not suppressed.
- If the co-op is primarily a processing cooperative (members share the hub): processor class is dominant. This is closest to the agricultural processing cooperative model (see Document 22).

#### Key Bylaws Provisions

**Board composition:** Specify the number of directors per class. A 5-7 member board with class-balanced representation is recommended for a startup co-op. Larger boards (9-11) can be adopted as membership grows.

**Term limits:** 2-year terms with a maximum of 3 consecutive terms (6 years). Stagger terms so that not all directors are replaced simultaneously. Term limits prevent board capture by long-serving insiders (see common pitfalls below).

**Decision thresholds:**

| Decision Type | Threshold | Examples |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Routine operations | Board majority (>50%) | Approve budgets, hire staff, approve supply contracts under $100K |
| Major capital expenditure | Board supermajority (2/3) | Equipment purchases over $100K, facility lease, new debt |
| Amend bylaws | Membership supermajority (2/3) | Any bylaws change |
| Major strategic change | Membership supermajority (2/3) | Add new member class, change patronage formula, enter new line of business |
| Dissolution or conversion to for-profit | Membership unanimous or near-unanimous (80-90%) | Dissolve the co-op, convert to LLC or corporation |

**Professional management:** The bylaws should require the board to hire a General Manager for day-to-day operations. The board sets policy; the GM executes. This is critical. Cooperatives where board members manage operations directly suffer from micromanagement, decision paralysis, and volunteer burnout. Budget $100,000-$140,000/year for a competent GM (see Document 23 staffing model).

**Patronage dividend formula:** Net surplus (revenue minus expenses minus reserves) is allocated to members based on their volume of business with the co-op:
- Producers: proportion of crude helium supplied (measured in SCF)
- Consumers: proportion of purified helium purchased (measured in liters or SCF)
- Processors: proportion of processing services provided (measured in hours or throughput)

Patronage dividends can be paid in cash, equity credits (retained by the co-op as member equity), or a combination. Standard practice: 20-50% cash, 50-80% equity credits. The equity credits are redeemed (paid out in cash) on a revolving schedule (oldest credits redeemed first, typically 5-10 year rotation).

**Conflict of interest policy:** Any director or officer with a financial interest in a transaction before the board must disclose the interest and recuse themselves from the vote. This is especially important in a mixed-stakeholder co-op where a producer member might be both supplying crude helium and voting on the supply contract terms.

**Member removal:** Members can be removed for cause (failure to meet capital obligations, breach of membership agreement, conduct harmful to the co-op) by board supermajority vote, with right of appeal to the membership. Membership shares are redeemed at book value upon removal.

### Step 5: File and Establish the Legal Entity (Month 3)

Execute these actions in sequence within 2-3 weeks:

1. **File Articles of Incorporation** with WA Secretary of State. Online: sos.wa.gov. Fee: ~$180. Processing: 1-2 business days.

2. **Obtain EIN** from the IRS. File Form SS-4 online at irs.gov. Immediate issuance. Free.

3. **Register for WA Business License.** File with WA Department of Revenue. Online: dor.wa.gov. Include any applicable state tax registrations (B&O tax).

4. **Appoint registered agent** (if not a founding member at a WA address).

5. **Open co-op bank account.** Recommended: BECU (a credit union -- a co-op serving a co-op), or a community bank with cooperative experience. The EIN and filed articles are required to open the account.

6. **Adopt bylaws** at the first official board meeting. Record the adoption in meeting minutes. Have the attorney review final bylaws before adoption.

7. **Issue membership shares** to founding members. Record capital contributions. Issue membership certificates.

### Step 6: Initial Capital Call and Funding Applications (Month 3-6)

#### Member Capital

Each founding member contributes their agreed capital per the membership class schedule:

| Class | Suggested Initial Contribution | What It Funds |
|-------|-------------------------------|--------------|
| Producer | $50,000-$250,000 | Extraction node equipment share, working capital |
| Processor | $100,000-$500,000 | Hub equipment share, building improvements |
| Consumer | $25,000-$100,000 | Membership equity plus advance purchase credits |
| Associate | $1,000-$10,000 | General fund, future conversion to full membership |

**Target founding member equity: $1,000,000** (5 founding members averaging $200,000 each, per the Document 23 funding model). This provides the co-op's equity base for grant matching and loan applications.

#### Grant Applications (Submit Month 4-6)

**CHIPS Act supply chain grants** -- The primary federal funding opportunity. The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 (P.L. 117-167) explicitly includes the chemical and gas layers of the semiconductor supply chain. A domestic helium cooperative supplying semiconductor fabs qualifies under supply chain resilience provisions. See Document 8 for CHIPS Act details and Document 14 for compliance requirements.

Application process:
1. Monitor CHIPS Program Office announcements (chips.gov) for supply chain funding notices
2. Prepare a pre-application concept paper (5-10 pages): describe the co-op, the supply chain gap, the proposed hub, the jobs created, the semiconductor customers served
3. Submit full application when funding notice opens. Required: business plan, financial projections (use Document 23), letters of support from semiconductor customers, community benefit plan, environmental justice analysis

Target: $1,500,000-$3,000,000 grant. Typical cost-share requirement: 1:1 or better (co-op matches grant with member equity + debt).

**WA Innovation Cluster Accelerator Program (ICAP)** -- State grant for multi-business collaboration solving shared industrial bottlenecks. A helium co-op with multiple member companies directly fits ICAP's model.

Contact: WA Department of Commerce, Innovation Division. Website: commerce.wa.gov

Target: $250,000-$750,000 grant.

**USDA Rural Business-Cooperative Service** -- If the hub is sited in a rural area (Centralia, Tumwater, or another non-metro location per Document 18 site candidates):
- Rural Business Development Grants (RBDG): up to $500,000 for planning, technical assistance, and training
- Business & Industry (B&I) Guaranteed Loans: USDA guarantees up to 80% of a bank loan, reducing the bank's risk and the co-op's interest rate
- Intermediary Relending Program: low-interest loans through intermediary lenders

Contact: USDA Rural Development, WA State Office (Olympia). Website: rd.usda.gov

**SBA loans** -- The Small Business Administration 7(a) loan program supports cooperatives. Maximum loan: $5,000,000. Terms: 10-25 years. Rate: negotiated with lender, SBA-guaranteed. The co-op's business plan and financial projections (Document 23) form the core of the SBA loan application.

Contact: SBA Seattle District Office or any SBA-approved lender.

**Bank financing (equipment-secured)** -- Standard commercial bank loan secured by the processing equipment (membrane system, PSA, liquefier). Typical terms: 7-10 year amortization, 5-7% interest, 70-80% loan-to-value. The equipment serves as collateral. Most commercial banks in the PNW have industrial equipment lending programs.

#### Funding Sequencing

| Month | Action | Expected Result |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| 3 | Founding members contribute initial equity ($1M total) | Co-op bank account funded |
| 3-4 | Submit SBA loan application through SBA-approved lender | Pre-approval within 30-60 days |
| 4-5 | Submit CHIPS Act concept paper / pre-application | Feedback from CHIPS Program Office |
| 4-5 | Submit WA ICAP application | Decision within 60-90 days |
| 5-6 | Submit USDA RBDG application (if rural site) | Decision within 90-120 days |
| 6-8 | Submit full CHIPS Act application (when funding notice opens) | Award decision: 3-6 months after submission |
| 6-8 | Close bank loan (equipment-secured) | Funds available for equipment orders |

**Risk: grant funding does not materialize.** The financial model in Document 23 shows that the co-op is viable under Scenario B (normalized pricing) even without CHIPS Act funding, though margins are tighter and payback extends from 2 years to approximately 4 years. If grants are not awarded, the co-op can proceed with member equity + debt financing at a smaller initial scale (gas-phase operations only, deferring liquefaction). Grants accelerate the timeline and reduce risk; they are not existential.

## Financial Structure and Tax Treatment

### Patronage Dividends -- The Co-op's Primary Distribution Mechanism

Net surplus (revenue minus expenses minus reserves) is allocated to members based on their volume of business. This is fundamentally different from corporate dividends (allocated by share ownership) or partnership distributions (allocated by ownership percentage). In a co-op, you earn based on participation, not investment.

**Example:** If the co-op has $280,000 in net surplus (per Document 23, Scenario B base case) and Consumer Member A purchased 20% of all helium sold, Member A receives 20% of the consumer class allocation of the surplus.

### Subchapter T Tax Treatment

Cooperatives are taxed under **Subchapter T** of the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. sections 1381-1388). Key provisions:

| Rule | Effect |
|------|--------|
| Patronage dividends are deductible by the co-op | Net income distributed as patronage is not taxed at the co-op level |
| Members report patronage income on their individual/corporate returns | Single taxation, not double taxation (unlike C corporations) |
| The co-op pays tax only on retained earnings not distributed as patronage | Incentivizes distribution to members |
| Written notices of allocation count as distributed if at least 20% is paid in cash | Equity credits (retained by co-op) qualify as deductible patronage if 20%+ cash is paid |

**Practical implication:** A well-managed co-op can eliminate virtually all federal income tax at the entity level by distributing surplus as patronage dividends. The tax burden passes to members at their individual rates. This is a significant structural advantage over a C corporation. Consult a CPA experienced with Subchapter T before the first fiscal year end.

### Reserves

The co-op should establish mandatory reserves before distributing patronage:

| Reserve | Target | Source | Purpose |
|---------|--------|--------|---------|
| Operating reserve | 3-6 months of operating expenses ($465K-$1.3M) | Retained surplus | Cash flow buffer for seasonal variation, unexpected expenses |
| Equipment replacement reserve | Annual contribution of 2-5% of equipment value | Retained surplus | Fund major maintenance, adsorbent bed replacement, compressor overhauls |
| Expansion reserve | 5-10% of annual surplus | Retained surplus | Fund capacity additions, new equipment, additional hub locations |

Reserves should be funded before patronage dividends are calculated. The bylaws should specify minimum reserve levels and contribution rates. Under-capitalization is the most common cause of cooperative failure (see common pitfalls below).

## Governance Best Practices

| Practice | Why It Matters | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Term limits for directors | Prevents board capture by long-serving insiders | 2-year terms, 3-term maximum, staggered rotation |
| Professional management | Board sets policy, hired GM runs operations | Budget $100K-$140K/year for GM. Include in bylaws. |
| Annual member meeting | Transparency builds trust. Required by RCW 23.86. | Fixed date each year. Include financial report, election, strategic discussion. |
| Independent financial audit | External verification of financial statements | Annual audit by CPA firm not connected to any board member. Budget $10K-$20K/year. |
| Member education program | New members oriented on cooperative principles, governance, and operations | Develop orientation package. Include cooperative history, bylaws summary, rights and responsibilities. |
| Patronage based on use | Rewards active participation, not passive investment | Track member transactions monthly. Calculate patronage quarterly or annually. |
| Capital retention schedule | Revolving fund: oldest equity credits redeemed first, funding new investment | 5-10 year revolving period. Ensures ongoing capitalization regardless of member turnover. |
| Board committees | Focus expertise on key areas | Recommended: Finance/Audit, Operations/Technical, Membership/Governance |

## Common Pitfalls and Prevention

| Pitfall | Description | Prevention |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Free-rider problem** | Members who contribute little but benefit from co-op services | Patronage dividends tied to actual usage. Minimum annual purchase/supply commitments per class. |
| **Horizon problem** | Long-tenured members resist new investment because they will not benefit from long-term returns | Rolling capital retention ensures ongoing investment. Redemption schedule means members eventually receive their equity back. |
| **Board capture** | Small group of insiders controls the board indefinitely | Term limits, class-balanced representation, annual elections, supermajority for major decisions |
| **Demutualization pressure** | External investors or insiders push to convert co-op to for-profit to capture equity value | Bylaws make conversion extremely difficult (80-90% membership vote). Include anti-demutualization clause. |
| **Under-capitalization** | Co-op operates on thin reserves, vulnerable to any disruption | Mandatory reserve contributions BEFORE patronage dividends. Minimum equity requirements per member. Regular financial review by board finance committee. |
| **Founder syndrome** | Founding members resist governance transition as co-op grows | Term limits from day one. Professional management from day one. Culture of shared governance, not personal ownership. |
| **Member apathy** | Members stop participating in governance, creating a democratic deficit | Active member education, accessible meeting times, meaningful agenda items, publish election results and financial summaries |

## Precedents and Models

No industrial gas cooperative exists. But the structural templates are proven across sectors with identical challenges. See Document 22 for the full analysis. Summary:

| Model | What It Proves | Direct Application |

| **Rural Electric Cooperatives** (832 in US) | Federal patient capital + member commitment bootstraps distributed infrastructure | CHIPS Act funding plays the REA role |

## First Actions -- What to Do This Week

1. **Find four other people** who understand helium and care about this. They do not all need to be technical experts. One engineer, one business person, one potential customer, and two people who get things done.

2. **Contact the Northwest Cooperative Development Center** (nwcdc.coop) and ask for their cooperative formation assistance program. This is free or subsidized.

3. **Contact a co-op attorney** in Washington. Ask for a 30-minute consultation on forming under RCW 23.86. Budget $500-$1,000 for this initial consultation.

4. **Download bylaws templates** from NCBA CLUSA (ncba.coop) and customize for a multi-stakeholder industrial cooperative.

5. **Read Document 23** (Financial Model) and Document 18 (Hub Design) to understand the capital requirements and physical facility that the co-op will build.

6. **Draft a one-page concept paper** describing the co-op, the supply chain problem it solves, and the customers it will serve. This becomes the foundation for CHIPS Act and ICAP grant applications.

The legal path is not the obstacle. Five people, one filing, one vote each. The cooperative structure is designed for exactly this situation: shared infrastructure too expensive for any one participant but essential for all. The obstacle is finding the five people and making the first phone call.
