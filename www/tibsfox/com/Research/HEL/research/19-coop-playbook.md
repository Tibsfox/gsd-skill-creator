# Co-op Formation Playbook — Step by Step

## Legal Foundation

Washington State: **RCW 23.86** (Cooperative Associations Act)

### Requirements to Incorporate

| Requirement | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| Incorporators | **Five or more persons** (RCW 23.86.030) |
| Name | Must contain "cooperative" or "co-op" |
| Filing | Articles of Incorporation with WA Secretary of State |
| Directors | Minimum 3 |
| Registered agent | Required in Washington |
| Annual report | Required filing |
| Filing fee | ~$180 (check current SOS schedule) |

### Governance

**One member, one vote** (RCW 23.86.160) — regardless of investment size. This is the fundamental difference from a corporation. A member who invested $50K has the same vote as one who invested $500K.

## Step-by-Step Formation

### Step 1: Assemble Founding Members (Month 1)
Identify five or more individuals or organizations willing to incorporate. For a helium co-op, natural founders include:

- Gas distribution professionals (from existing suppliers)
- Fab operations/facilities managers (from Intel, labs)
- Cryogenic engineers (from PNNL, universities)
- Business/finance professionals
- Community members with relevant expertise

### Step 2: Draft Articles of Incorporation (Month 1–2)
Required content:
- Name (e.g., "Pacific Helium Cooperative" or "Cascadia Gas Co-op")
- Purpose (helium purification, distribution, and related services)
- Duration (perpetual)
- Registered office and agent
- Number and classes of membership
- Initial board of directors (minimum 3)

### Step 3: Draft Bylaws (Month 2–3)
Bylaws define governance in detail:

**Membership classes** (recommended for mixed-stakeholder co-op):

| Class | Members | Rights |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Producer** | Crude helium suppliers, extraction operators | Vote on sourcing, pricing, supply agreements |
| **Processor** | Hub operators, equipment owners | Vote on processing operations, capital expenditure |
| **Consumer** | Fabs, labs, universities buying helium | Vote on product specs, delivery terms, pricing |
| **Associate** | Supporters, future members | Non-voting, pathway to full membership |

**Key bylaws provisions:**
- Board composition: balanced representation across member classes
- Patronage dividend formula: based on volume of business transacted through the co-op
- Capital contribution requirements per class
- Decision thresholds: simple majority for operations, supermajority (2/3) for major capital, unanimous for dissolution
- Professional management: board hires a general manager for day-to-day operations
- Conflict of interest policy
- Member removal process

### Step 4: File with Secretary of State (Month 3)
- Submit Articles of Incorporation
- Obtain EIN from IRS (Form SS-4)
- Register for WA Business License
- Open co-op bank account

### Step 5: Initial Capital Call (Month 3–4)
Each founding member contributes agreed capital:

| Class | Suggested Initial Contribution | What It Funds |
|-------|-------------------------------|--------------|
| Producer | $50K–$250K | Extraction node equipment |
| Processor | $100K–$500K | Hub equipment share |
| Consumer | $25K–$100K | Membership equity + advance purchase credits |

### Step 6: Apply for Grants and Loans (Month 4–6)
- **CHIPS Act supply chain** — federal grant application
- **WA ICAP** — Innovation Cluster Accelerator Program
- **USDA Rural Business programs** — if hub is in rural area
- **SBA loans** — Small Business Administration
- **Bank financing** — secured by equipment

### Step 7: Begin Operations (Month 12–24)
See Hub Design document (18) for construction timeline.

## Financial Structure

### Patronage Dividends
The co-op's primary distribution mechanism. Net surplus (revenue minus expenses minus reserves) is allocated to members based on their volume of business:

- Producers: proportion of crude helium supplied
- Consumers: proportion of purified helium purchased
- Processors: proportion of processing services provided

Patronage dividends can be paid in cash, equity credits, or a combination.

### Reserves
The co-op should maintain:
- **Operating reserve:** 3–6 months of expenses
- **Equipment replacement reserve:** annual contribution toward major maintenance and replacement
- **Expansion reserve:** for adding nodes, capacity, or new services

### Tax Treatment
Cooperatives are taxed under **Subchapter T** of the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. §1381–1388). Patronage dividends paid to members are deductible by the co-op, avoiding double taxation. The co-op pays tax only on retained earnings not distributed as patronage.

## Governance Best Practices (From Co-op History)

| Practice | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| **Term limits for directors** | Prevents board capture by long-serving insiders |
| **Professional management** | Board sets policy, hired GM runs operations — prevents micromanagement |
| **Annual member meetings** | Transparency builds trust; required by RCW 23.86 |
| **Independent audit** | Annual financial audit by external CPA |
| **Member education** | New members oriented on co-op principles, governance, and operations |
| **Patronage based on use** | Rewards active participation, not passive investment |
| **Capital retention schedule** | Revolving fund — oldest equity redeemed first, funding new investment |

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|
| **Free-rider problem** | Patronage dividends based on actual usage — you get out what you put in |
| **Horizon problem** | Rolling capital retention ensures ongoing investment regardless of member age |
| **Board capture** | Term limits, class-balanced representation, supermajority for major decisions |
| **Demutualization pressure** | Bylaws should make conversion to for-profit extremely difficult (supermajority or unanimous) |
| **Under-capitalization** | Mandatory reserve contributions before patronage dividends are paid |

## Precedents and Models

No industrial gas co-op exists. But the structural templates are proven:

| Model | What It Proves | Applicable Lesson |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Rural Electric Cooperatives** (832 in US) | Federal lending + member commitment bootstraps distributed infrastructure | CHIPS Act funding can play the REA role |
| **Agricultural processing co-ops** (CHS, Land O'Lakes) | Commodity producers can collectively own capital-intensive processing | Helium producers can co-own a purification hub |
| **Mondragon Corporation** (80,000 employees, €12B) | Cooperatives can operate heavy industry at scale | Internal financing + education pipeline + inter-cooperation |
| **PNW co-op culture** (REI, PCC, BECU) | The region has deep cooperative appetite | Political and consumer receptivity is established |

## First Actions

1. **Find four other people** who understand helium and care about this
2. **Talk to a co-op attorney** in Washington (WA has several firms specializing in cooperative law)
3. **Draft articles and bylaws** — templates available from NCBA CLUSA (National Cooperative Business Association)
4. **File with Secretary of State** — $180, one week
5. **Open a bank account** — BECU or another credit union (co-op serving co-op)
6. **Apply for CHIPS Act supply chain grant** — this is the REA moment

The legal path is not the obstacle. Five people, one filing, one vote each. The obstacle is finding the five people and making the first phone call.
