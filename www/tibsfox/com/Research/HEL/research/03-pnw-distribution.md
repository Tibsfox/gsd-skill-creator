# PNW Commercial Helium Distribution Network

## Overview

The Pacific Northwest Interstate 5 corridor — stretching approximately 600 miles from Bellingham, Washington to Medford, Oregon — supports an established network of industrial gas suppliers providing helium in purities ranging from balloon-grade (99.5%) to research-grade (99.9999%) and in quantities from single cylinders to bulk liquid deliveries. This distribution infrastructure represents the "last mile" of the helium supply chain: the companies, facilities, delivery routes, safety certifications, and customer relationships that connect upstream production to end-use consumers. Understanding this network in detail is essential for evaluating any helium supply chain improvement in the region, because the gap in the PNW is not distribution — it is upstream production and purification.

The Pacific Northwest's helium demand is driven by several sectors: semiconductor fabrication (notably the growing cluster of fabs and advanced manufacturing in the Portland-Hillsboro metro area), aerospace manufacturing (Boeing's Everett and Renton facilities), medical imaging (MRI installations across dozens of hospitals), university and federal research laboratories (University of Washington, Oregon State University, PNNL), and general industrial applications (welding, leak detection, fiber optic manufacturing). Total regional demand is not publicly reported by individual distributors, but industry estimates place the combined Washington-Oregon market at approximately 15-25 million cubic feet (MMcf) per year, representing roughly 0.5-1.0% of U.S. domestic consumption.

## Primary Suppliers

### Central Welding Supply

Central Welding Supply is the largest regional independent industrial gas distributor in the Pacific Northwest, family-owned and operated since its founding in 1974 in Bellevue, Washington. The company has grown to over 30 locations across Washington, Oregon, and Alaska, making it a dominant regional player in specialty gas distribution.

Their flagship facility — a 10,000 square-foot fill plant in Smokey Point, Washington (Arlington area, approximately 50 miles north of Seattle on I-5) — serves as the central hub for high-purity and specialty gas distribution throughout the region. This facility performs cylinder filling, gas blending, and quality testing. It is the largest non-national-chain fill plant in the Pacific Northwest and handles a significant share of regional helium cylinder distribution.

- **Seattle area:** 5401 4th Avenue South, Seattle (SODO district — near Port of Seattle)
- **Portland area:** 2313 NE Martin Luther King Jr Blvd, Portland
- **Smokey Point fill plant:** Arlington, WA — primary processing and distribution hub
- **Coverage:** Full Washington state, western and central Oregon, Alaska (Anchorage and Fairbanks)
- **Capabilities:** High-purity gas distribution, specialty gas mixtures (helium-neon, helium-argon, helium-hydrogen calibration standards), cylinder and bulk delivery, equipment rental and sales
- **Strategic significance:** As an independent distributor, Central Welding has the operational flexibility to adopt new supply sources more rapidly than national chains whose purchasing decisions are made at corporate headquarters. If a regional helium production facility were established, Central Welding's existing logistics network and customer base would be a natural integration point.

### Airgas (Air Liquide subsidiary)

Airgas, acquired by Air Liquide in 2016 for $13.4 billion, is the largest U.S. distributor of industrial, medical, and specialty gases. Air Liquide is the world's second-largest industrial gas company by revenue (after Linde), with 2024 revenues of approximately 27.1 billion euros. The combined entity brings global sourcing capability and deep technical expertise to the PNW market.

- **Seattle area:** 4401 Airport Way South, Seattle (Georgetown district)
- **Portland area:** 7232 SE 82nd Avenue, Portland
- **Spokane:** 4230 East Trent Avenue
- **Eugene:** Regional service point
- **Capabilities:** Full range of helium products from balloon-grade through research-grade. High-pressure gas cylinders, liquid helium dewars, bulk liquid delivery via tanker. Specialty capabilities include isotope supply — notably Helium-3 (He-3), a rare isotope critical for quantum computing dilution refrigerators and neutron detectors, priced at approximately $2,000 per liter.
- **Parent company investment signal:** In 2024, Air Liquide invested $60 million in a new specialty gas facility in Chandler, Arizona, specifically to supply ultra-high purity hydrogen, helium, nitrogen, and carbon dioxide to semiconductor fabrication plants in the Phoenix metro area. This investment demonstrates two things: first, the parent company's active commitment to the semiconductor supply chain, and second, the scale of capital that major industrial gas companies are deploying to secure fab-grade helium supply. A similar investment targeting the Portland-Hillsboro semiconductor corridor is a logical extension if regional helium sourcing becomes available.
- **Strategic significance:** As the distribution arm of Air Liquide, Airgas connects the PNW market to one of the world's largest helium sourcing networks. However, this also means Airgas sources helium according to Air Liquide's global optimization — not regional priorities. PNW customers are supplied from wherever Air Liquide's global network offers the lowest delivered cost, which currently means out-of-region sources. A regional production facility would need to be cost-competitive with Air Liquide's global sourcing to capture this channel.

### Industrial Source

Industrial Source is an independent distributor focused specifically on the western Oregon I-5 corridor, with seven locations providing dense coverage from Portland to Medford. Founded in Portland, the company has built its business on serving the manufacturing, fabrication, and construction sectors of Oregon's Willamette Valley and southern Oregon.

- **Portland:** Primary location and administrative headquarters
- **Salem:** Serving the mid-Willamette Valley manufacturing base
- **Eugene/Springfield:** Serving the southern Willamette Valley, including University of Oregon research demand
- **Medford:** Southernmost location, serving the Rogue Valley and providing coverage to the California border
- **Additional locations:** Three more sites across the corridor
- **Strategic significance:** Industrial Source's seven-location Oregon network is already optimized for I-5 corridor logistics. Their delivery routes, customer relationships, and territorial knowledge represent a spoke network that any regional supply chain would want to integrate with rather than replicate. The company's independence (not owned by a multinational) gives it the same flexibility as Central Welding to adopt new regional supply sources.

### OXARC (Meritus Gas Partners)

OXARC is a member of Meritus Gas Partners, a network of independent gas distributors formed in 2018 with backing from private equity firm One Rock Capital Partners. The Meritus model allows local distributors to maintain their brand identity, customer relationships, and operational independence while gaining the purchasing power and shared services of a larger network. OXARC has operated in the inland Pacific Northwest since 1928, making it one of the oldest industrial gas distributors in the region.

- **Spokane:** 4003 East Broadway Avenue — serving eastern Washington, northern Idaho, and western Montana
- **Gresham/Portland:** 19310 NE San Rafael Street — extending coverage into the Portland metro area
- **Coverage:** Critically, OXARC extends the regional helium distribution network east along the I-90 corridor to Spokane and beyond. This is significant because most other PNW distributors concentrate on the I-5 corridor. OXARC provides the east-west link.
- **Strategic significance:** If helium-bearing formations in eastern Washington, Idaho, Montana, or Wyoming were developed, OXARC's eastern Washington logistics network would be among the first to handle inbound crude helium. Their Spokane location is approximately 280 miles from the Montana border and 350 miles from the Riley Ridge helium facility in Wyoming — the closest major production source to the PNW.

### Linde Welding Gas & Equipment

Linde plc is the world's largest industrial gas company by revenue (2024 revenue approximately $33 billion), formed from the 2018 merger of Linde AG (Germany, founded 1879) and Praxair (U.S., founded 1907). Linde's PNW presence is modest compared to its global footprint but provides access to the company's helium sourcing and technical capabilities.

- **Seattle:** 944 West Leary Way (Ballard district)
- **Capabilities:** Industrial supply, helium services, equipment
- **Strategic significance:** Linde operates several of the world's largest helium liquefaction plants and has long-term supply agreements with major production sources including Qatar's RasGas (now QatarEnergy LNG). The March 2026 Qatar force majeure has directly impacted Linde's global supply chain, potentially creating openness to new regional sourcing arrangements that would not have been considered when Qatari supply was flowing freely. Linde is also a leading manufacturer of helium liquefaction equipment — their standard product line includes liquefiers rated from 30 to 2,500 liters per hour, relevant to the hub facility described in [Document 06: Virtual Helium Plant](06-virtual-plant.md).

**Key distances along I-5:**
- Bellingham to Medford: ~600 miles (approximately 10 hours by truck)
- Seattle to Portland: ~175 miles (approximately 3 hours)
- Portland to Eugene: ~110 miles (approximately 2 hours)
- Seattle to Spokane (I-90): ~280 miles (approximately 4 hours)

A tube trailer carrying 180,000 standard cubic feet of compressed helium gas can serve the entire I-5 corridor in a single delivery run. Liquid helium tankers (typically 11,000-gallon capacity, approximately 40,000 liters) can similarly cover the full corridor, though liquid helium's unavoidable boil-off rate (approximately 0.5-1.0% per day, depending on dewar quality) means that transit time affects delivered quantity.

## Supply Chain Context (March 2026)

As of late March 2026, the PNW helium distribution network is operating under significant stress. The Qatar force majeure declared on March 2, 2026 — described in detail in [Document 05: Market Crisis](05-market-crisis.md) — has removed approximately one-third of global helium supply from the market. The compounding effects are specific and measurable:

### Current sourcing for PNW distributors

PNW distributors have historically sourced helium from three primary origins, all located far from the region:

1. **Federal Helium Reserve (Amarillo, TX) — now privatized:** For decades, the Bureau of Land Management's Federal Helium Reserve in Amarillo, Texas operated as the world's largest helium storage and distribution facility, supplying refined helium to government users and, through private operators, to the commercial market. In 2024, the Reserve was fully privatized under the Helium Stewardship Act of 2013, with remaining assets transferred to Messer and other private operators. The strategic buffer function — the ability to release stored helium during supply disruptions — was eliminated. The full implications of that decision became apparent in March 2026.

2. **Riley Ridge (LaBarge, Wyoming):** ExxonMobil's Shute Creek gas processing plant near LaBarge, Wyoming processes natural gas from the Riley Ridge formation, which contains approximately 0.5% helium alongside CO2 and methane. This single facility produces an estimated 25-30% of remaining U.S. helium supply — and by extension, approximately 15-20% of post-Qatar global supply. It is approximately 800 miles from Seattle by road. Riley Ridge is currently operating at or near capacity, with no ability to rapidly scale output. Its role as the dominant remaining U.S. source makes it a critical single point of failure — a risk the industry has not adequately addressed.

3. **Various smaller U.S. producers:** A growing number of primary helium producers — notably Desert Mountain Energy (New Mexico), Blue Star Helium (Colorado), North American Helium (Saskatchewan, with U.S. expansion), and Pulsar Helium (Minnesota) — are adding incremental supply. These producers are important for long-term diversification but are not yet operating at the scale needed to replace Qatari volumes. Their current combined output is likely in the range of 5-10% of U.S. consumption. See [Document 04: Global Production](04-global-production.md) for detailed profiles.

### The 2026 pricing environment

The supply disruption has driven dramatic price increases. PNW distributors are experiencing:

- **Spot market helium gas:** $450-$3,000+ per thousand cubic feet (Mcf), compared to pre-crisis prices of approximately $150-$300/Mcf
- **Liquid helium:** $50-$100+ per liter, compared to pre-crisis prices of approximately $15-$30/liter
- **Allocation restrictions:** Several major suppliers have implemented allocation programs, limiting existing customers to 70-80% of their historical purchase volumes
- **Extended lead times:** Delivery schedules that were previously 1-2 weeks have extended to 4-8 weeks for some products
- **Contract renegotiation:** Long-term contract holders are partially insulated, but contracts with price escalation clauses are being triggered, and several contracts are up for renewal in 2026-2027 at significantly higher rates

### Who is most affected in the PNW

- **Intel Ronler Acres (Hillsboro, OR):** The largest semiconductor fabrication complex in the Pacific Northwest, using helium for lithography gas, wafer cooling, and clean room atmosphere control. Intel has invested in closed-loop recycling at some facilities but remains a net purchaser of significant volumes.
- **Boeing (Everett and Renton, WA):** Helium is used in leak detection testing of pressurized aircraft assemblies, in specialized welding of titanium and aluminum components, and in wind tunnel testing. Boeing's supply contracts provide some buffer, but allocation restrictions affect production planning.
- **Regional hospitals and medical centers:** MRI systems using superconducting magnets require periodic helium refills (typically 500-1,500 liters per year per system, depending on the magnet design and recondensing technology). There are approximately 200+ MRI installations across Washington and Oregon. Newer MRI systems from Siemens (MAGNETOM Free.Max) and Philips use sealed helium systems that dramatically reduce consumption, but the installed base of older systems remains helium-dependent.
- **University research:** The University of Washington's physics and chemistry departments, Oregon State University's research programs, and smaller institutions depend on liquid helium for cryogenic experiments, NMR spectroscopy, and materials research. Research helium is typically purchased in small quantities at premium prices, and allocation restrictions can force delays or cancellation of experiments.

## Observations and Strategic Implications

1. **The I-5 corridor is already served** by a mature, competitive distribution network. Five major distributors operate across more than 40 locations. The physical infrastructure for last-mile delivery — trucks, tube trailers, cylinder fill plants, customer relationships, safety certifications, delivery routes — exists and functions well. Any new entrant focused on distribution would face entrenched competition and low margins.

2. **Smokey Point is the critical regional node.** Central Welding's fill plant is the largest independent cylinder processing facility in the region. It is the point where bulk helium is converted into distributed cylinder deliveries. Understanding its throughput capacity and sourcing is key to understanding regional supply dynamics.

3. **East-west coverage via OXARC and Airgas (Spokane)** extends the network along I-90 to eastern Washington. This corridor connects the PNW distribution network toward the nearest major production sources (Riley Ridge, Wyoming; Montana prospects; Saskatchewan operations). The east-west link is strategically important for any inbound crude helium logistics.

4. **Seven Oregon locations (Industrial Source) provide dense southern corridor coverage.** The Willamette Valley manufacturing base and Portland semiconductor corridor are well-served. This network is optimized for regional delivery and would integrate naturally with a regional supply source.

5. **No regional purification or liquefaction capacity exists.** This is the fundamental gap. All PNW helium distributors purchase finished product — purified gas or liquid helium — from out-of-region sources and transport it 800-2,500 miles. The region has no crude helium processing, no PSA purification facility, no liquefaction plant, and no bulk storage capability. Every unit of helium consumed in the PNW was produced, purified, and (if liquid) liquefied elsewhere.

6. **The gap is upstream, not downstream.** The distribution infrastructure is the strongest part of the regional supply chain. The missing links are production (extraction from geological sources), processing (purification to Grade-A or higher), and liquefaction (conversion to liquid for bulk transport and storage). These upstream capabilities are precisely what the Virtual Helium Plant architecture addresses (see [Document 06](06-virtual-plant.md)).

7. **The PNW has a structural advantage for liquefaction.** Hydroelectric power from the Columbia Basin provides industrial electricity at $0.04-0.06/kWh — among the lowest rates in the country. Since liquefaction is the most energy-intensive step in helium production (8-15 kWh per liter), low electricity costs provide a meaningful cost advantage over facilities in regions with higher power costs. This advantage is quantifiable: a liquefier producing 100 liters per hour consuming 1 MW would have annual electricity costs of approximately $350,000-$525,000 in the PNW versus $700,000-$1,050,000 in regions with $0.08-$0.12/kWh industrial rates.

8. **Existing distributors are natural partners, not competitors.** A regional helium production facility would sell to — not compete with — the existing distribution network. Central Welding, Airgas, Industrial Source, OXARC, and Linde all need a reliable helium supply. A facility that provides purified or liquefied helium at competitive prices, with shorter supply lines and faster delivery, would be welcomed by a distribution network currently suffering from allocation restrictions and price volatility. The integration point is clear: the regional production facility provides the upstream supply that the existing downstream network distributes.
