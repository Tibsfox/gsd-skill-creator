# Crude Helium Sourcing & Transport Logistics

## The Supply Chain Spine

The corridor model requires crude helium (50–70% purity) transported from US extraction sites to a PNW purification hub. This document maps the specific routes, vehicles, costs, and logistics.

## Source Regions

| Region | Distance to Portland | Distance to Seattle | Key Producers |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------|-|
| Colorado (Galactica project) | ~1,250 mi | ~1,350 mi | Blue Star Helium |
| Minnesota (Topaz project) | ~1,750 mi | ~1,650 mi | Pulsar Helium |
| Arizona (Holbrook Basin) | ~1,350 mi | ~1,450 mi | Altura Energy |
| New Mexico | ~1,400 mi | ~1,500 mi | Desert Mountain Energy |
| Saskatchewan | ~1,200 mi | ~1,100 mi | North American Helium |
| Wyoming (Riley Ridge) | ~800 mi | ~900 mi | ExxonMobil (existing) |

**Closest sources:** Wyoming (~800 mi) and Saskatchewan (~1,100 mi). Colorado and Arizona are mid-range. Minnesota is farthest.

## Transport Vehicles

### High-Pressure Tube Trailers (Primary Method)
Standard for crude and purified gaseous helium transport:
- **Capacity:** 180,000–250,000 SCF per trailer (~5,000–7,000 Nm³)
- **Pressure:** 2,400–3,600 psig (DOT 3AAX or 3T spec tubes)
- **Tractor-trailer combination:** Standard Class 8 truck
- **Hazmat classification:** DOT Division 2.2 (non-flammable gas)
- **Speed:** Highway speed, ~500 mi/day with single driver, ~800 mi/day team drivers
- **Cost to own/lease:** $150K–$250K per trailer

### ISO Containers (For Liquid Helium)
Used for long-distance and international transport:
- **Capacity:** ~11,000 gallons (~41,000 liters) liquid helium per 40-ft ISO container
- **Holding time:** 35–48 days before boil-off venting
- **Intermodal:** Truck, rail, or ship-compatible
- **Cost:** $300K–$500K per container (super-insulated vacuum-jacketed)

### Rail
- **Intermodal rail:** ISO containers on flatcars. BNSF and Union Pacific serve PNW from all source regions.
- **Transit time:** 3–5 days from Colorado/Wyoming to Portland/Seattle
- **Cost advantage:** ~40–60% cheaper per ton-mile than truck for distances >500 miles
- **Disadvantage:** Less flexible scheduling, terminal-to-terminal (requires truck for first/last mile)

## Route Analysis

### Wyoming → PNW (Shortest, Most Practical)
```
Riley Ridge, WY → I-80 W → I-84 W → Portland, OR
~800 miles, ~14 hours driving
Or: I-15 N → I-90 W → Seattle, WA
~900 miles, ~16 hours driving
```
- **Truck cost estimate:** $3,500–$5,000 per load (fuel + driver + equipment amortization)
- **Per-MCF transport cost:** ~$0.015–$0.025/SCF (at 200,000 SCF/trailer)
- **Frequency for 10-node co-op:** ~2–4 loads/month

### Colorado → PNW
```
Las Animas County, CO → I-25 N → I-80 W → I-84 W → Portland
~1,250 miles, ~20 hours driving
```
- **Truck cost:** $5,000–$7,500 per load
- **Per-MCF:** ~$0.025–$0.035/SCF

### Saskatchewan → PNW
```
Swift Current, SK → I-15 S (Montana) → I-90 W → Seattle
~1,100 miles, ~18 hours driving
```
- **Additional consideration:** Cross-border customs (US-Canada). Helium is not controlled but requires customs declaration.
- **Truck cost:** $5,000–$7,000 per load (plus customs brokerage ~$200–$500)

## Cost Model: Monthly Hub Feed

Assuming a co-op hub processing 500,000 SCF/month of crude helium (enough to produce ~350,000 SCF of Grade-A at 70% recovery):

| Source | Loads/Month | Transport Cost/Month | Crude He Cost/Month | Total Feed Cost |
|--------|------------|---------------------|--------------------|-|
| Wyoming | 2–3 | $7K–$15K | $50K–$100K | $57K–$115K |
| Colorado | 2–3 | $10K–$22K | $50K–$100K | $60K–$122K |
| Saskatchewan | 2–3 | $10K–$21K | $50K–$100K | $60K–$121K |

**Crude helium purchase price** is the dominant cost — transport adds 10–20% on top.

## Carrier Requirements

Carriers transporting helium need:
- **DOT Hazmat registration** (Division 2.2 non-flammable gas)
- **CDL with hazmat endorsement** (driver)
- **DOT-specification equipment** (3AAX or 3T tube trailers)
- **Shipping papers** per 49 CFR 172 (UN 1046 for compressed, UN 1963 for liquid)
- **Placards:** Green diamond (non-flammable gas)

Existing hazmat carriers on these routes include specialty gas haulers and general hazmat freight companies. The routes are well-traveled for industrial gases — this is not pioneering logistics.

## Rail Alternative

For regular, scheduled deliveries, rail is significantly cheaper:

| Route | Rail Transit | Truck Transit | Rail Cost Advantage |
|-------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Wyoming → Portland | 3–4 days | 14 hours | ~50% cheaper per unit |
| Colorado → Portland | 4–5 days | 20 hours | ~55% cheaper per unit |
| Saskatchewan → Seattle | 3–4 days | 18 hours | ~45% cheaper per unit |

**Trade-off:** Rail is slower and less flexible but substantially cheaper for regular bulk shipments. A mature co-op would likely use rail for baseline supply and truck for surge/emergency deliveries.

BNSF serves all source regions and has intermodal terminals in Seattle/Tacoma and Portland. Union Pacific also connects through Portland.

## First Steps

1. **Contact crude helium producers** (Blue Star, Pulsar, North American Helium, Desert Mountain) for pricing and minimum order quantities
2. **Get freight quotes** from hazmat carriers for tube trailer loads on specific routes
3. **Evaluate rail intermodal** through BNSF and UP for regular scheduled service
4. **Negotiate supply agreements** before building the hub — secure feed stock first

The transport logistics are solvable with standard industrial gas infrastructure. Nothing here requires invention — it requires execution.
