## ROI Analysis at March 2026 Crisis Prices

### For Helium Consumers (Fabs and Labs)

Closed-loop helium recycling at current crisis pricing:

| Scenario | Monthly External Cost | System Cost | Monthly After Recycling | Payback |
|----------|----------------------|-------------|------------------------|---------|
| Large fab, no recycling | ~$500K/month | — | $500K/month | — |
| Large fab + 90% recycling | — | $3.5M | ~$50K/month | **<8 months** |
| Medium fab, no recycling | ~$200K/month | — | $200K/month | — |
| Medium fab + 90% recycling | — | $2M | ~$20K/month | **<12 months** |

At pre-crisis pricing, these same systems had 3–5 year payback periods. The crisis has compressed the ROI to under a year.

### For Helium Producers (Extraction Nodes)

At crisis spot prices ($450–$3,000 per Mcf):

| Feed Gas Concentration | Unit Cost | IRR Estimate |
|-----------------------|-----------|-------------|
| >0.75% | $50K–$250K | **>50–100%** |
| 0.3–0.75% | $100K–$250K | **>25–50%** |
| <0.3% | Not economical | — |

The key insight: at $50K–$250K per unit, even modest production generates returns that justify the investment almost immediately at crisis prices.

## The "Ten Units vs. One Plant" Math

| Approach | Investment | Time to Revenue | Risk |
|----------|-----------|----------------|------|
| One traditional plant | $500M | 3–5 years | Concentrated in one location |
| Ten modular units | $2.5M total | 6 months | Distributed across ten sites |

The modular approach trades peak efficiency for speed and resilience. Ten small units produce less total helium than one large plant — but they start producing **now**, distribute risk across locations, and can scale linearly by adding more units.

## The Economic Window

The crisis creates a specific window of opportunity:

**Window opens:** March 2, 2026 (Qatar force majeure)
**Peak opportunity:** Now through end of 2026 (crisis pricing + active government funding)
**Window narrows:** When Qatar resumes OR large-scale alternative plants complete construction (3–5 years)

### What Happens After the Window?

Operations established during the crisis remain viable at normalized prices because:
1. Capital is already deployed and amortizing
2. Recycling economics are permanently favorable (just with longer payback)
3. Customer relationships are established
4. The structural argument for distributed supply doesn't change with price
5. Semiconductor demand continues to grow regardless of helium pricing

The crisis accelerates the economics. The fundamentals sustain them.
