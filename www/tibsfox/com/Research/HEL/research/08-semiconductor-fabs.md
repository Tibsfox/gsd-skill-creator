# Semiconductor Fabrication — Requirements, Funding, and the Gas Supply Layer

## What a Fab Needs

A modern leading-edge semiconductor fabrication facility costs approximately $10–$20 billion and requires extreme precision in every aspect of its design and operation.

### Infrastructure Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Water | Massive volumes for ultrapure water (UPW) systems — cooling, cleaning, chemical dilution |
| Power | Reliable, high-capacity grid connection; fabs are enormous electricity consumers |
| Gases | Ultra-high purity He, H2, CO2, N2, Ar, and dozens of specialty process gases |
| Vibration isolation | Sites must be away from airports, rail lines, and fault lines — lithography operates at atomic scales |
| Cleanroom | ISO Class 1 cleanrooms with multi-level sub-fab for piping, wiring, and chemical delivery |
| Tooling | Approximately 1,200 multimillion-dollar precision tools per fab |

### Timeline
- **Design and construction:** 3–5 years in the US (38-month average vs. 20 months in Taiwan)
- **Tooling and ramping:** Additional 9–12 months to install and qualify production equipment
- **Gas infrastructure alone:** Air Liquide recently invested $60 million in Phoenix just for gas supply — and that's for an existing fab, not a new one

## CHIPS and Science Act (Active as of March 2026)

The CHIPS Act represents the largest US government investment in semiconductor manufacturing since the industry's origin.

| Funding Stream | Amount | Status |
|---------------|--------|--------|
| Manufacturing grants | $33B+ allocated | Active — application cycles ongoing |
| Loans | $7B | Active |
| R&D and workforce | Additional billions | NSF programs running through 2027 |
| Texas Semiconductor Innovation Fund | $948M appropriated | Active (since June 2025) |

### Critical Detail: Gas Supply Is Explicitly Funded

The CHIPS Act includes specific funding for the "chemical and gas" layers of the semiconductor stack. This means projects that strengthen the supply of helium, hydrogen, nitrogen, and specialty gases to US fabs are eligible for federal funding. The Act recognizes that you cannot build chip independence without gas independence.

## The Manufacturing Ecosystem

A fab cannot operate in isolation. It requires a surrounding "cluster" of suppliers for:

- **Process chemicals:** Acids, solvents, photoresists, etchants
- **Specialty gases:** Ultra-high purity mixtures for specific process steps
- **Materials:** Silicon wafers, photomasks, target materials for deposition
- **Equipment maintenance:** Highly specialized technicians for lithography, etch, and deposition tools
- **Waste handling:** Chemical waste treatment, recycling, and disposal

Companies like MGC Pure Chemicals America and Sunlit Chemical are building new plants in Texas and Arizona to provide these materials domestically. The cluster effect means that as fabs concentrate in a region, the supporting supply chain follows.

## Helium's Role in Semiconductor Fabrication

Helium serves several critical functions in chip manufacturing:

1. **Wafer cooling:** Helium's thermal conductivity (the highest of any gas) makes it ideal for backside wafer cooling during plasma etch and ion implantation
2. **Leak detection:** Helium's small atomic size allows it to penetrate the tiniest leaks in vacuum systems — essential for maintaining process integrity
3. **Carrier gas:** Used to transport process chemicals in the gas phase
4. **Lithography:** EUV (extreme ultraviolet) lithography systems use helium-purged environments to prevent light absorption
5. **Cryogenic cooling:** Specialized cooling for superconducting components and quantum computing research

A single large fab can consume thousands of liters of liquid helium per month. At crisis prices of $50–$100 per liter, this represents a significant and growing cost pressure.

## Closed-Loop Helium Recycling

Modern helium recovery systems can capture 90–95% of helium that would otherwise be vented during fab operations:

- **Technology:** Cryocoolers recapture boil-off gas and re-liquefy it on-site
- **System cost:** $1.5M–$3.5M for a mid-to-large scale fab
- **ROI at crisis prices:** Under 12 months
- **Impact:** Reduces external helium purchases to a small "top-off" fraction

Every major fab should have closed-loop recycling. The economics have been favorable for years; at crisis prices, the case is overwhelming. The technology exists — the barrier is implementation, not invention.

## The Gap

The PNW has fabs, research institutions, and a distribution network. What it does not have is **local helium purification and recycling infrastructure**. All helium currently arrives from out-of-region sources through national distributors. A regional processing capability — even at modest scale — would reduce supply chain risk, lower costs through avoided transportation, and position the corridor for growth as semiconductor fabrication continues to expand domestically.
