# Strategic Material Cooperative Precedents

## The Question

"Has a cooperative model been used for strategic industrial materials before?"

The answer is: **not for industrial gas specifically, but the structural templates are proven across multiple sectors that share the same challenges — distributed production, shared infrastructure, essential commodities, and monopoly incumbents.**

## Rural Electric Cooperatives: The Primary Template

### Scale
- **832 electric distribution cooperatives** in the US
- **63 generation-and-transmission cooperatives**
- Serve ~42 million people across 56% of US landmass
- Deliver ~11% of US electricity

### Origin Story
Private utilities refused to wire low-density rural areas — per-mile costs were too high relative to revenue. The **Rural Electrification Act of 1936** created federal low-interest lending through the REA. Members paid nominal connection fees and committed to minimum usage. Cooperatives didn't need market-rate returns — only debt service plus operations.

### Why This Matters for Helium
The helium market has the same structural problem: **an oligopoly that serves existing customers but won't invest in distributed infrastructure.** Linde, Air Liquide, Air Products, and Nippon Sanso control ~80% of global merchant gas. They build large plants, not small distributed nodes.

**The CHIPS Act can play the REA role** — federal patient capital bootstrapping cooperative infrastructure where private capital won't go.

## Agricultural Processing Cooperatives

### Examples
- **Land O'Lakes** (dairy, founded 1921)
- **CHS Inc.** (grain/energy, founded 1929) — **$47B+ annual revenue**
- **Ocean Spray** (cranberry, 1930)
- **Blue Diamond** (almonds, 1910)

### Model
Farmers collectively own the processing infrastructure no single farm could afford — creameries, grain elevators, cold storage, distribution. Members deliver product and receive a base price plus **patronage dividends** based on volume.

### Lesson for Helium
**Commodity producers can collectively own capital-intensive processing infrastructure and compete against vertically integrated corporations.** Crude helium producers can co-own a purification hub the same way dairy farmers co-own a creamery.

## Telecommunications Cooperatives

~30–40 rural telecom cooperatives in the US successfully transitioned from copper to fiber broadband, often ahead of commercial carriers (GVTC Communications in Texas, Midwest Energy & Communications in Michigan). Funded through USDA RUS loans, internal reserves, and member assessments.

**Lesson:** Cooperatives can execute **major technology transitions** when members see long-term value. Directly applicable to building new gas processing infrastructure.

## Nordic Energy Cooperatives

Denmark has ~700 wind power cooperatives — community-owned turbines dating to the 1970s oil crisis. Swedish district heating cooperatives serve entire municipalities.

**Key design feature:** Danish law requires **local ownership** — members must live near the turbines. This builds political support for permitting and ensures the community bears both the costs and benefits.

**Lesson:** **Geographic proximity of members to infrastructure** strengthens governance and political viability. A PNW helium co-op should draw members from the I-5 corridor.

## Water Cooperatives and Irrigation Districts

~10,000+ mutual water companies in California alone. Western US irrigation districts (e.g., Salt River Project, founded 1903) are effectively water cooperatives sharing canal infrastructure, pumping stations, and reservoir costs.

**Lesson:** **Shared pipeline/distribution infrastructure** is a proven cooperative model for essential industrial inputs. Helium distribution along a corridor is structurally identical.

## Mondragon Corporation (Basque Country, Spain)

The most sophisticated cooperative model in the world:
- **80,000+ employees**, ~€12B revenue
- **95+ cooperatives** spanning manufacturing, finance, retail, education
- Founded 1956 by José María Arizmendiarrieta

### Key Structural Features

| Feature | How It Works | Helium Application |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Caja Laboral** (cooperative bank) | Internal bank provides patient capital | Co-op builds internal financing capability over time |
| **Pay ratio cap** (originally 3:1, now ~6:1) | Limits executive extraction | Keeps management costs aligned with member interests |
| **Inter-cooperation** | Co-ops cross-subsidize during downturns | Multiple hub locations support each other |
| **Education pipeline** | Mondragon University feeds talent | Partner with Sno-Isle TECH, OSU, PSU |

**Mondragon proves cooperatives can operate heavy industry at scale.** They make automotive components, industrial equipment, and appliances. The internal bank is the critical piece — it aligns capital allocation with member interests rather than external shareholders.

## The Industrial Gas Gap

**No significant industrial gas cooperative exists globally.**

Why:
- Extreme capital intensity ($50–200M+ for air separation units)
- Technical complexity (cryogenic engineering)
- Existing lock-in (10–25 year supply contracts with oligopoly)
- No historical "refusal to serve" moment like rural electrification

**But:** The March 2026 crisis IS the "refusal to serve" moment. The oligopoly cannot guarantee supply. The market structure has failed. This is exactly the condition that created rural electric cooperatives in 1936 — incumbents that either couldn't or wouldn't serve the need.

The fact that no industrial gas co-op exists is a **market gap, not proof of impossibility.** The technology is mature (not proprietary), the customer need is acute, and modular equipment means capital requirements have fallen to co-op scale ($2M–$15M, not $200M).

## PNW Cooperative Culture

The Pacific Northwest has the **deepest cooperative culture in the US** outside the Upper Midwest:

| Organization | Type | Scale |
|-------------|------|-------|
| **REI** (Seattle, 1938) | Consumer retail | 23M+ members, $3.8B revenue |
| **PCC Community Markets** (Seattle, 1953) | Food | Largest community-owned food co-op in US, 15 stores |
| **BECU** (Tukwila) | Credit union | 1.4M+ members |
| **Group Health** (Seattle, 1947–2017) | Healthcare | Proved co-op model works for complex services |
| Multiple rural electric co-ops | Utility | Serve rural WA and OR |

Political and consumer appetite for cooperative models is well-established in this region. A helium co-op in the PNW would be culturally native, not exotic.

## The Three Templates That Apply

| Template | What It Solves | From |
|----------|---------------|------|
| **REA model** | Federal patient capital + member commitment bootstraps infrastructure | Rural Electric Cooperatives |
| **Agricultural processing** | Commodity producers collectively own capital-intensive processing | CHS, Land O'Lakes |
| **Mondragon's internal bank** | Patient capital aligned with member interests, not external shareholders | Mondragon Corporation |

Combine all three: federal CHIPS Act funding (REA model) + member-owned hub (agricultural model) + internal cooperative financing as the operation matures (Mondragon model). The structure works. It just hasn't been applied to industrial gas yet.
