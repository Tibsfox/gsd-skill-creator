# Strategic Material Cooperative Precedents

## The Central Question

Can a cooperative structure work for an industrial gas supply chain?

No industrial gas cooperative exists anywhere in the world. That absence raises a legitimate question: is this a market gap waiting to be filled, or is there a structural reason cooperatives have not entered this space?

This document examines every relevant cooperative precedent to answer that question with evidence. The answer: the structural templates are proven across sectors that share the same challenges -- distributed production, shared infrastructure, essential commodities, and monopoly incumbents. The industrial gas sector has no unique characteristic that prevents cooperative entry. What it has lacked is a triggering event severe enough to mobilize collective action. The March 2026 helium crisis (Document 5) is that event.

## Rural Electric Cooperatives: The Primary Template

### The Parallel

In the 1930s, private electric utilities refused to wire rural America. The per-mile cost of running lines to dispersed farms was too high relative to the revenue those farms could generate. The utilities were not acting irrationally -- they were maximizing shareholder returns. But the result was that 90% of rural homes had no electricity in 1935 while urban homes had been electrified for decades.

In 2026, the industrial gas oligopoly -- Linde, Air Liquide, Air Products, and Nippon Sanso, controlling approximately 80% of global merchant gas -- serves existing large customers but will not invest in the kind of distributed, modular infrastructure that the helium market needs. They build centralized mega-plants ($200M-$500M+), not networks of small processing nodes ($2M-$15M). Their business model optimizes for concentration and scale. The result: when Qatar's supply disappeared overnight on March 2, 2026 (Document 5), the concentrated supply chain failed catastrophically. The oligopoly could not guarantee supply -- exactly the condition that created rural electric cooperatives in 1936.

### Scale and Duration

Rural electric cooperatives are not a historical curiosity. They are a massive, ongoing economic institution:

- **832 electric distribution cooperatives** operating in 2025
- **63 generation-and-transmission cooperatives** providing wholesale power
- Serving approximately **42 million people** across **56% of the US landmass**
- Delivering approximately **11% of total US electricity**
- Combined revenue exceeding **$50 billion annually**
- Average member tenure: decades (cooperatives rarely lose members)

These numbers demonstrate that cooperative infrastructure, once built, persists and grows. The fear that a helium co-op would be a temporary crisis response is contradicted by 90 years of rural electric cooperative history.

### The Funding Mechanism

The **Rural Electrification Act of 1936** created the Rural Electrification Administration (REA), which provided federal low-interest loans to cooperatives. The REA did not build or operate infrastructure -- it provided patient capital that private lenders would not. Members paid nominal connection fees ($5-$10, equivalent to roughly $100-$200 today) and committed to minimum usage. Cooperatives did not need market-rate returns on capital -- only enough revenue to cover debt service, operations, and modest reserves.

**The CHIPS Act can play the REA role for helium.** The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 (P.L. 117-167) explicitly funds the "chemical and gas layers" of the semiconductor supply chain (Document 14). A helium cooperative providing domestic purification and distribution to semiconductor manufacturers is a textbook CHIPS Act supply chain project. The parallels are precise: federal patient capital bootstrapping cooperative infrastructure in a market where private capital will not go because the return profile does not match corporate expectations.

| REA (1936) | CHIPS Act (2022) | Parallel |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Problem: Private utilities won't wire rural areas | Problem: Gas oligopoly won't build distributed helium infrastructure | Concentrated industry refuses to serve unmet need |
| Solution: Federal low-interest loans to co-ops | Solution: Federal grants for semiconductor supply chain | Patient public capital where private capital won't go |
| Members: Farmers, rural residents | Members: Fabs, labs, universities, gas professionals | Users of the essential commodity |
| Result: 832 co-ops, 42M people served, 90 years | Result: TBD | Proven model, untested in this sector |

## Agricultural Processing Cooperatives: The Operational Template

### The Model

Agricultural cooperatives solved a problem identical to the helium hub model (Document 18): individual commodity producers cannot afford the capital-intensive processing infrastructure needed to convert raw material into a marketable product. Dairy farmers cannot individually build creameries. Grain farmers cannot individually build elevators and export terminals. Almond growers cannot individually build processing and marketing operations. So they collectively own these facilities.

### Scale and Longevity

| Cooperative | Founded | 2025 Revenue | Members | What They Process |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| CHS Inc. | 1929 | $47B+ | 75,000+ | Grain, energy, food |
| Land O'Lakes | 1921 | $18B+ | 1,700+ | Dairy products |
| Ocean Spray | 1930 | $2B+ | 700+ | Cranberries |
| Blue Diamond | 1910 | $1.8B+ | 3,000+ | Almonds |
| Dairy Farmers of America | 1998 | $21B+ | 11,000+ | Dairy products |

CHS Inc. at $47 billion in annual revenue is larger than most Fortune 500 companies. These are not niche operations. They are dominant players in their respective commodity markets.

### The Patronage Mechanism

Agricultural co-ops distribute surplus through **patronage dividends** -- returns proportional to each member's volume of business transacted through the cooperative. A farmer who delivers 10,000 bushels receives proportionally more than one who delivers 1,000. This aligns incentives precisely: the more you use the co-op, the more you benefit. Tax treatment under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. 1381-1388) allows co-ops to deduct patronage dividends, avoiding double taxation.

**For a helium co-op:** Producers receive patronage dividends proportional to crude helium supplied. Consumers receive dividends proportional to purified helium purchased. The hub processes material from all members and distributes surplus based on participation. This is structurally identical to a dairy co-op processing milk from multiple farms and distributing returns based on volume delivered.

### The Capital Contribution Model

Agricultural co-ops typically require members to contribute equity upon joining, with the amount varying by member class and expected usage. CHS requires per-unit capital retains; Land O'Lakes requires capital contributions tied to production volume. These contributions fund infrastructure expansion and provide working capital.

**For a helium co-op (from Document 19's playbook):**

| Member Class | Contribution Range | What It Funds |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Producer (crude helium supplier) | $50K-$250K | Extraction node equipment share |
| Processor (hub operator) | $100K-$500K | Hub equipment and facility |
| Consumer (fab, lab, university) | $25K-$100K | Membership equity + advance purchase credits |

These contribution levels are modest relative to the annual helium expenditure of target members. A university spending $180,000/year on helium (Document 21, Scenario 1) can justify a $50K membership investment that reduces annual costs by $147,000.

## Telecommunications Cooperatives: The Technology Transition Template

Approximately 30-40 rural telecommunications cooperatives in the US successfully transitioned from copper telephone infrastructure to fiber broadband, often ahead of commercial carriers. Examples include GVTC Communications (New Braunfels, TX), Midwest Energy & Communications (Cassopolis, MI), and Paul Bunyan Communications (Bemidji, MN).

### Why This Matters

These cooperatives executed major technology transitions -- replacing their entire physical infrastructure -- while maintaining service continuity and financial stability. They funded transitions through USDA Rural Utilities Service (RUS) loans, internal reserves, and modest member assessments ($50-$200 per member). Many now offer gigabit broadband in areas where commercial carriers provide only DSL or cellular.

**The lesson for helium:** Building a new helium purification and distribution hub (Document 18) is a technology deployment comparable in complexity and capital intensity to a fiber broadband buildout. Telecom cooperatives prove that co-ops can execute capital-intensive infrastructure projects with long construction timelines (12-24 months for a helium hub, per Document 18, vs. 12-36 months for a fiber network) when members understand the long-term value proposition.

The critical success factor identified across telecom co-op transitions: **member education and communication.** Co-ops that invested in explaining the technology, timeline, and expected benefits to members before and during construction maintained higher member satisfaction and lower attrition. Document 19's playbook includes member education as a governance best practice for exactly this reason.

## Nordic Energy Cooperatives: The Community Ownership Template

### Danish Wind Cooperatives

Denmark has approximately 700 wind power cooperatives, community-owned turbines dating to the 1970s oil crisis. At their peak, these cooperatives owned approximately 80% of Denmark's wind capacity. The model has since shifted toward larger offshore installations with institutional investors, but the onshore cooperative model remains active and has been replicated across Scandinavia and Germany.

**Critical design feature:** Danish law requires local ownership -- cooperative members must live within a specified distance of the turbines (typically 4.5 km for new installations under the Danish Renewable Energy Act). This is not merely a cultural preference; it is a governance mechanism that ensures the community bearing the externalities (visual impact, noise, construction disruption) also receives the economic benefits.

**Application to helium:** A PNW helium cooperative should draw members from the I-5 corridor (Document 7). Geographic proximity of members to the hub (Document 18) strengthens governance because members can physically inspect operations, attend meetings without significant travel, and have direct relationships with hub staff. It also builds political support for permitting -- a community that owns and benefits from an industrial facility is far more supportive than one that merely hosts it (see Document 24 on community engagement).

### Swedish District Heating Cooperatives

Swedish municipalities operate district heating cooperatives that serve entire communities from centralized heat plants. The model is relevant because district heating, like helium processing, involves a capital-intensive central facility distributing a commodity through infrastructure to dispersed customers. The governance structure -- municipal or cooperative ownership, cost-of-service pricing, long-term infrastructure investment -- maps directly to the helium hub model.

## Water Cooperatives and Irrigation Districts: The Infrastructure Template

Approximately 10,000 mutual water companies operate in California alone. Western US irrigation districts -- including the Salt River Project (founded 1903, serving the Phoenix metropolitan area with both water and electricity), the Imperial Irrigation District, and dozens of others -- are effectively water cooperatives that share canal infrastructure, pumping stations, and reservoir costs.

**The structural parallel is precise:** An irrigation district collects water from a source (river, reservoir), processes it (filtration, treatment), and distributes it through shared infrastructure (canals, pipes) to member users who pay based on volume consumed. A helium cooperative collects crude helium from sources (extraction nodes or purchased from producers per Document 17), processes it (purification, liquefaction at the hub per Document 18), and distributes it through shared infrastructure (cylinder delivery, tube trailer) to member users who pay based on volume consumed.

Irrigation districts have operated continuously for over a century. They manage capital-intensive shared infrastructure, handle variable supply conditions (drought, flood), and balance competing demands from multiple user classes. These are exactly the operational challenges a helium cooperative would face.

## Mondragon Corporation: The Industrial Complexity Template

The most sophisticated cooperative model in the world demonstrates that cooperatives can operate heavy industry at global scale.

### Scale

- **80,000+ employees** across 95+ cooperatives
- **~12 billion euros annual revenue**
- Operations spanning manufacturing, finance, retail, and education
- Founded 1956 by Jose Maria Arizmendiarrieta, a Catholic priest in the Basque Country

### Key Structural Features

| Feature | How It Works | Helium Co-op Application |
|---------|-------------|-------------------------|
| Caja Laboral (cooperative bank) | Internal bank provides patient capital aligned with member interests, not external shareholders | Co-op builds internal financing capability as it matures; reinvests surplus into expansion (Document 23 models this) |
| Pay ratio cap (originally 3:1, now ~6:1) | Limits executive compensation relative to lowest-paid worker | Keeps management costs aligned with member interests; prevents executive extraction |
| Inter-cooperation | Cooperatives cross-subsidize each other during downturns | Multiple hub locations (if co-op expands) support each other through market fluctuations |
| Education pipeline | Mondragon University trains members and feeds talent into cooperatives | Partner with Sno-Isle TECH, community colleges, OSU, PSU for cryogenic and gas handling training |
| Democratic governance | One member, one vote regardless of capital contribution | Matches WA RCW 23.86.160 requirements (Document 19) |

**The Caja Laboral (internal bank) is the most important lesson from Mondragon.** Cooperatives that depend entirely on external financing are vulnerable to lender priorities that may conflict with member interests. Mondragon's internal bank aligns capital allocation with cooperative values. A helium co-op should plan for eventual internal financing capability -- using retained surplus from Document 23's financial model to fund expansion without external debt.

### Mondragon's Resilience

During the 2008-2012 Spanish economic crisis, Mondragon cooperatives that faced severe revenue declines did not lay off workers. Instead, they redeployed workers across cooperatives (inter-cooperation), reduced hours temporarily, and accepted temporary pay reductions approved by democratic vote. The cooperative structure's flexibility during crisis is directly relevant to Document 23's Scenario C (worst case): a helium co-op facing market collapse has governance tools (member capital calls, deferred patronage dividends, cost sharing) that a corporation does not.

## The Industrial Gas Gap

### Why No Industrial Gas Cooperative Exists

Four factors have prevented cooperative entry into industrial gas:

1. **Extreme capital intensity.** Traditional air separation units cost $50-$200M+. This exceeds cooperative capital formation capacity. **But:** Modular helium processing equipment (Document 15) has brought the capital requirement down to $2M-$15M -- well within cooperative scale.

2. **Technical complexity.** Cryogenic engineering requires specialized expertise. **But:** The equipment is commercially available from multiple vendors (Document 15). Cryomech, Chart Industries, and Guild Associates sell catalog products, not proprietary black boxes. Operating these systems requires trained technicians, not PhD engineers.

3. **Existing lock-in.** Gas customers are bound by 10-25 year supply contracts with oligopoly suppliers. **But:** Crisis-driven force majeure events (like Qatar's on March 2, 2026) void or suspend existing contracts, creating a window for new suppliers. Customers whose contracts were broken by the crisis have no obligation to return to the same supplier.

4. **No historical "refusal to serve" moment.** Until 2026, the oligopoly served the market adequately (if expensively). There was no rural-electrification-style gap. **But:** The March 2026 crisis IS the refusal-to-serve moment. The oligopoly cannot guarantee supply. Document 12 documents five shortage cycles in 20 years, each worse than the last. The market structure has failed.

### The Modular Equipment Revolution

The single most important change that makes a helium cooperative viable in 2026 is the availability of modular, skid-mounted processing equipment at cooperative-affordable scale (Document 15):

| Equipment | Cost Range | What It Replaced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Chart TITAN PSA skid | $150K-$3M | Custom-built air separation plants ($50M+) |
| Evonik SEPURAN membrane modules | $100K-$400K | Proprietary cryogenic distillation columns |
| Cryomech reliquefier | $250K-$600K | Large-scale helium liquefaction plants ($5M+) |

The total hub cost of $2.7M-$7.3M (Document 18) is comparable to what agricultural cooperatives spend on processing facilities. CHS, Land O'Lakes, and Blue Diamond were all founded with capital contributions far smaller than their current operations -- they grew into scale. A helium cooperative follows the same trajectory.

## PNW Cooperative Culture

The Pacific Northwest is not a random location for a cooperative venture. It has the deepest cooperative culture in the United States outside the Upper Midwest.

| Organization | Type | Founded | Scale | Relevance |
|-------------|------|---------|-------|-----------|
| REI | Consumer retail | 1938 (Seattle) | 23M+ members, $3.8B revenue | Proves cooperative model at massive consumer scale |
| PCC Community Markets | Food | 1953 (Seattle) | Largest community-owned food co-op in US, 15 stores | Proves complex operational co-op works in PNW |
| BECU | Credit union | 1935 (Tukwila) | 1.4M+ members, $28B+ assets | Financial cooperative at scale; potential banking partner |
| Group Health Cooperative | Healthcare | 1947-2017 (Seattle) | Served 600K+ at peak | Proved co-op model works for technically complex services |
| Puget Sound Energy (predecessor) | Utility | Various | Multiple rural electric co-ops | Infrastructure co-ops already operate in the corridor |

**Political and consumer appetite for cooperative models is well-established in this region.** Washington's RCW 23.86 (Cooperative Associations Act) provides a clear, tested legal framework (Document 19). BECU could serve as the co-op's banking partner (co-op serving co-op). REI's 23 million members demonstrate that PNW consumers actively choose cooperative options when available.

A helium cooperative in the PNW would be culturally native, not exotic. This is not a minor advantage. Cooperative ventures in regions without cooperative culture face higher barriers to member recruitment, political support, and community acceptance.

## Decision Framework: Choosing the Right Cooperative Model

Based on the precedent analysis, three models apply to a helium cooperative. The recommended approach combines elements of all three:

| Model | What It Solves | Risk It Creates | When to Apply |
|-------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| REA/CHIPS Act (federal patient capital) | Overcomes initial capital barrier | Government funding timelines and compliance requirements | Phase 1: Formation and hub construction |
| Agricultural processing (member-owned infrastructure) | Aligns incentives between producers and consumers | Free-rider problem if some members under-contribute | Phase 1-2: Ongoing operations |
| Mondragon internal bank (self-financing) | Reduces dependence on external capital | Requires sustained surplus before internal financing is viable | Phase 3+: Expansion and maturation |

**Recommended sequence:**
1. **Year 1-2:** REA model. Use CHIPS Act grants and SBA loans (Document 23 funding mix) to build the hub. Member equity contributions provide matching funds.
2. **Year 2-5:** Agricultural model. Operate the hub as a processing cooperative. Distribute patronage dividends based on usage. Build reserves.
3. **Year 5+:** Mondragon model. Use accumulated reserves as internal capital for expansion. Reduce external debt. Fund new nodes or second hub from internal sources.

## Risk and Mitigation

| Risk | Precedent Evidence | Mitigation |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| Free-rider problem (members benefit without contributing proportionally) | Agricultural co-ops solved with patronage-based dividends | Tie all returns to transaction volume (Document 19 bylaws) |
| Board capture by founding members | REI, credit unions use term limits and class representation | Mandatory term limits, multi-class board, supermajority for major decisions |
| Demutualization pressure (converting to for-profit) | Group Health sold to Kaiser in 2017 under financial pressure | Bylaws requiring unanimous or supermajority vote for conversion; strong reserve policy |
| Insufficient member commitment | Telecom co-ops lost members who preferred commercial alternatives | Geographic focus (I-5 corridor), clear value proposition, crisis-driven urgency |
| Government funding delays or denials | Rural broadband co-ops face multi-year USDA timelines | Apply to multiple sources simultaneously (CHIPS, ICAP, SBA per Document 23); size initial operation to survive without government funding |

## The Three Templates Combined

Combine all three: federal CHIPS Act funding (REA model) + member-owned hub (agricultural model) + internal cooperative financing as the operation matures (Mondragon model). The structure works. It has been proven across electricity, agriculture, telecommunications, energy, water, and complex industrial manufacturing.

The fact that it has not been applied to industrial gas is a market gap -- the same kind of gap that existed in rural electricity in 1935, in agricultural processing in 1910, and in community broadband in 2000. In each case, the cooperative model filled the gap when incumbents would not. The March 2026 helium crisis has created exactly that condition.

The question is not whether the cooperative model can work for helium. The precedents prove it can. The question is whether five people in the I-5 corridor are willing to file the paperwork (Document 19) and make the first phone call.
