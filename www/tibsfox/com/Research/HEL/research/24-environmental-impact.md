# Environmental Impact Assessment

## Purpose and Scope

This document serves as the environmental foundation for a helium processing cooperative proposing to build a purification and liquefaction hub in the I-5 corridor (Document 18). It is designed to answer every environmental question that a permit reviewer, community member, or co-op board member would ask -- with data, regulatory citations, and honest assessments of impact.

The core finding: helium processing is one of the cleanest industrial operations that can be sited in an industrial zone. Helium is inert, non-toxic, non-flammable, and non-reactive. It produces no emissions, contaminates no water, and generates no hazardous waste. The primary environmental footprint is electricity consumption -- and in the Pacific Northwest, that electricity is predominantly hydroelectric, giving a PNW helium hub roughly one-third the carbon footprint of an equivalent facility in Texas or the Midwest.

This is not marketing language. It is a consequence of chemistry and geography. The evidence follows.

## Regulatory Framework

### Federal Requirements

A helium processing hub (purification, liquefaction, and distribution of purchased crude helium) is a **processing-only facility** -- it does not extract helium from the ground. This distinction matters because extraction triggers drilling permits, mineral lease requirements, and potentially NEPA review on federal land. Processing does not.

**National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), 42 U.S.C. 4321-4347:** NEPA applies to "major federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment." A private helium processing facility on private industrial-zoned land does not constitute a federal action. NEPA review would be required only if the project receives direct federal funding (e.g., CHIPS Act grant) or requires a federal permit. If CHIPS Act funding is received, the Commerce Department may require a NEPA categorical exclusion (CE) or Environmental Assessment (EA). Given the minimal environmental impact profile, a CE is the most likely outcome.

**Clean Air Act (CAA), 42 U.S.C. 7401-7671q:** The CAA regulates air emissions from stationary sources. Helium processing produces no regulated air emissions. No combustion occurs. No volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are released. No hazardous air pollutants (HAPs) are emitted. The facility would not trigger New Source Review (NSR) under CAA Section 165 (42 U.S.C. 7475) because it does not emit criteria pollutants above de minimis thresholds. The sole potential emission source is an emergency diesel generator -- if installed, this would require a minor source permit from the relevant air quality authority (NWCAA or PSCAA in Washington; DEQ in Oregon).

**Clean Water Act (CWA), 33 U.S.C. 1251-1387:** CWA Section 402 requires an NPDES permit for point-source discharges to navigable waters. A helium hub uses water only for closed-loop cooling and domestic purposes. There is no process water discharge. Cooling tower blowdown (periodic discharge of concentrated cooling water) may require an NPDES industrial stormwater permit if discharged to a storm drain, but most facilities recirculate cooling water and dispose of blowdown through the municipal sewer system under a pretreatment permit. CWA Section 404 (wetland fill) is not applicable for industrial-zoned sites with no wetland features.

**Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), 42 U.S.C. 6901-6992k:** RCRA regulates hazardous waste generation, storage, and disposal. Helium processing generates no RCRA-listed hazardous waste (40 CFR 261.31-261.33). Waste streams -- spent PSA adsorbent, membrane modules, compressor oil -- are non-hazardous solid waste or recyclable materials. The facility would be classified as a Conditionally Exempt Small Quantity Generator (CESQG) or would not require a RCRA generator ID at all.

**OSHA Process Safety Management (PSM), 29 CFR 1910.119:** PSM applies to processes involving highly hazardous chemicals above threshold quantities. Helium is not listed as a highly hazardous chemical under PSM. However, OSHA General Duty Clause (29 U.S.C. 654) requires employers to maintain a workplace free from recognized hazards -- specifically, oxygen displacement risk in enclosed spaces where helium can accumulate. Compliance requires oxygen depletion monitoring (two independent O2 sensors with audible/visual alarms), ventilation design per ANSI/AIHA Z9.5, and confined space entry procedures per 29 CFR 1910.146.

### Washington State Requirements

**State Environmental Policy Act (SEPA), RCW 43.21C; WAC 197-11:** All non-exempt projects in Washington require SEPA review. The applicant (co-op) files a SEPA Environmental Checklist (WAC 197-11-960) with the lead agency (typically the local jurisdiction -- city or county planning department). Based on the checklist, the lead agency issues one of three determinations:

- **Determination of Non-Significance (DNS):** No probable significant adverse environmental impact. No further review required. **This is the expected determination for a helium processing hub.**
- **Mitigated DNS (MDNS):** Impacts can be mitigated to below significance thresholds. Conditions attached.
- **Determination of Significance (DS):** Requires a full Environmental Impact Statement (EIS). **Not expected for this project.**

SEPA categorical exemptions (WAC 197-11-800) may apply if the facility is below certain thresholds for new industrial construction, but most hub-scale facilities would exceed these thresholds and require the full checklist process.

**Air Quality Permits -- Washington:**
- **WAC 173-400 (General Regulations for Air Pollution Sources):** The Department of Ecology administers air quality permits through regional clean air agencies. For the I-5 corridor, the relevant agencies are:
  - **Puget Sound Clean Air Agency (PSCAA):** King, Kitsap, Pierce, Snohomish counties
  - **Northwest Clean Air Agency (NWCAA):** Whatcom, Skagit, Island counties
  - **Southwest Clean Air Agency (SWCAA):** Clark, Cowlitz, Lewis, Skamania, Wahkiakum counties
- A helium processing facility with no combustion sources and no process emissions would likely be classified as an **insignificant emission unit** under WAC 173-400-110(5) and would not require an air quality permit. The emergency generator, if diesel-powered and above 50 hp, would require registration with the relevant clean air agency.

**Pressure Vessel Safety -- WAC 296-104:** The Department of Labor & Industries regulates pressure vessels and boilers. High-pressure gas storage (tube banks at 2,400-3,600 psig) and cryogenic vessels require inspection and certification per ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code. This is a standard industrial compliance requirement with well-established processes.

### Oregon Requirements

If the hub is sited in Oregon (Portland industrial district, Hillsboro/Beaverton, or Wilsonville per Document 18):

- **Oregon DEQ Air Quality (OAR 340-200 through 340-268):** Similar to Washington -- no process emissions means no significant air quality permit requirements. Emergency generator registration may apply.
- **Oregon Land Use:** Oregon's land use planning system (ORS 197) requires consistency with comprehensive plans and zoning. Industrial-zoned sites are pre-approved for industrial use.
- **Oregon OSHA (Oregon Safe Employment Act, ORS 654):** Compressed gas handling, cryogenic safety, confined space entry -- standard industrial compliance.

Oregon does not have a direct equivalent to SEPA. Land use review under ORS 197 and local development code would be the primary environmental review mechanism. For a facility on industrial-zoned land with no significant environmental impacts, this is a straightforward administrative review.

## Impact Categories (Detailed)

### Air Emissions

**Direct process emissions: zero.** This is not an approximation. Helium processing involves physical separation (membrane permeation, pressure swing adsorption) and cryogenic cooling. No chemical reactions occur. No fuels are burned. No VOCs, HAPs, SOx, NOx, CO, or particulate matter are produced by the process itself.

**Indirect emissions:**
- Emergency generator (if diesel): <50 hours/year operation, producing negligible cumulative emissions
- Delivery vehicles: 10-14 truck trips/month (receiving and delivery combined), generating standard diesel vehicle emissions. This is equivalent to the truck traffic of a small warehouse or distribution center -- well below the traffic of a grocery store.
- Fugitive helium: Helium released to atmosphere has no greenhouse warming potential (GWP = 0), no ozone depletion potential (ODP = 0), and no toxicity. It is the second most abundant element in the universe and the most chemically inert substance known. Atmospheric release of helium is an economic loss (Document 12), not an environmental impact.

| Compressor cooling | 5-10 GPM | ~2.6-5.3M gal (recirculated) | >95% (closed-loop cooling tower) |
| Liquefier cooling | 5-15 GPM | ~2.6-7.9M gal (recirculated) | >95% (closed-loop) |

**Context:** 180,000 gallons/year is approximately:
- 2.5x a single-family home's annual water use in the PNW (70,000-80,000 gal/year)
- Equal to a small coffee shop or restaurant
- 0.001% of a large semiconductor fab's water use (fabs consume millions of gallons per day for ultrapure water systems)
- Negligible relative to municipal water system capacity in any I-5 corridor city

**No process water discharge.** Water is used exclusively for cooling (closed-loop) and domestic purposes. Cooling tower blowdown -- the periodic discharge of concentrated minerals from the recirculating water -- is the only non-domestic wastewater stream. Volume: approximately 5-10% of recirculated flow, discharged to municipal sewer under standard pretreatment authorization. This contains no process chemicals, only concentrated dissolved minerals from the makeup water source.

### Electricity Consumption and Carbon Footprint

The hub's electricity consumption is the primary environmental footprint. Cryogenic processes (cooling helium from room temperature to -269C / 4.2 K) are inherently energy-intensive because they work against thermodynamic gradients.

**Hub power demand:** 200-300 kW connected load, running 24/7.

| Cooling tower/chillers | 20 kW | 14,400 | 172,800 |
| Facility (lighting, HVAC, lab) | 15 kW | 10,800 | 129,600 |
| **Total** | **210-310 kW** | **~137,000-209,000** | **~1,640,000-2,510,000** |

**Carbon footprint by grid location:**

| Grid Region | Carbon Intensity (g CO2/kWh) | Annual CO2 (tonnes) | Equivalent Passenger Cars |
|-------------|-----------------------------|--------------------|-------------------------|
| PNW (WA/OR) | 80-150 (EPA eGRID NWPP 2023) | 130-375 | 28-82 |

| Upper Midwest (MROW) | 450-550 | 740-1,380 | 161-300 |

**The PNW carbon advantage is substantial and structural.** Washington state's grid is approximately 75% hydroelectric (Bonneville Power Administration dams on the Columbia and Snake rivers). Oregon's grid includes significant hydro plus growing wind capacity. A helium hub in the PNW operates at roughly one-third the carbon intensity of the same facility in Texas and roughly one-quarter the intensity of one in the Upper Midwest.

This advantage is not temporary or policy-dependent -- it is a consequence of geography (rivers) and infrastructure (dams) that has been in place for 80+ years. It is the same structural advantage that attracts data centers to the PNW (Microsoft, Google, Amazon all operate major facilities in Washington and Oregon in part because of grid carbon intensity).

**For context:** 130-375 tonnes CO2/year is equivalent to:
- 28-82 passenger cars (US average of 4.6 tonnes CO2/car/year per EPA)
- 15-40 single-family homes (US average of ~9.5 tonnes CO2/home/year)
- 0.0001% of a coal-fired power plant's annual emissions
- Less than the carbon footprint of a single commercial flight from Seattle to Tokyo

## Community Engagement Pathway

Environmental data alone does not build community support. The cooperative structure (Documents 19 and 22) provides a natural framework for community engagement because the co-op's members are local and the co-op's governance is democratic and transparent. But proactive engagement is still necessary, especially given general public unfamiliarity with helium processing (as opposed to more visible industries).

### Phase 1: Pre-Application Community Outreach (Months 1-3)

Before filing any permit applications, the co-op should:

1. **Brief the local jurisdiction's planning department.** Describe the project, confirm zoning compatibility, ask about any known community concerns related to the proposed site. Planning staff who understand the project in advance will process permits more efficiently and can advise on potential issues.

2. **Meet with immediate neighbors.** For an industrial-zoned site, this means adjacent industrial and commercial property owners/tenants. Explain what the facility does (and does not do -- no combustion, no chemicals, no hazardous materials). Provide this environmental assessment document. Ask about any specific concerns.

3. **Contact the local economic development agency.** In Washington: the local Economic Development Council (Snohomish County EDC, Economic Development Board for Tacoma-Pierce County, etc.). In Oregon: Business Oregon. These agencies can facilitate introductions, provide support letters for permit applications, and connect the co-op with workforce development resources.

4. **Engage the relevant Clean Air Agency.** A pre-application meeting with PSCAA, NWCAA, or SWCAA (depending on site location) to confirm the facility's emission profile and determine whether a permit is required. This prevents surprises during the formal permit process.

### Phase 2: Permit Application and Public Process (Months 3-6)

5. **SEPA Environmental Checklist filing.** The checklist is a public document. When the lead agency issues a Determination of Non-Significance (DNS), there is a 14-day public comment period (WAC 197-11-340). During this period, any person can comment or appeal. Having completed Phase 1 outreach reduces the likelihood of opposition during this window.

6. **Building permit and fire marshal review.** These are administrative processes, not public hearings, but the co-op should proactively share facility plans with the fire marshal to address compressed gas storage and cryogenic safety requirements.

7. **Workforce development announcement.** Announce the co-op's hiring plans and training partnerships (Sno-Isle TECH, community colleges per Document 22) to local workforce development boards. This generates positive community coverage and demonstrates local economic benefit.

### Phase 3: Construction and Ongoing Relations (Months 6-24)

8. **Construction communication.** Notify neighbors of the construction timeline, expected truck traffic, and working hours. Provide a contact number for questions or complaints. This is standard good practice for any industrial construction project.

9. **Facility tours.** Once operational, offer tours to community members, local government officials, and educational institutions. Seeing the facility -- a clean, quiet building with no visible emissions, no odors, no heavy traffic -- is the most effective community relations tool available.

10. **Annual environmental report.** Publish an annual summary of actual water use, energy consumption, waste generated, and any incidents. Transparency builds trust. The cooperative governance structure (Document 19) already requires annual member meetings and financial audits; adding an environmental report is minimal additional effort.

## Risk and Mitigation

| Environmental Risk | Probability | Consequence | Mitigation | Regulatory Trigger |
|-------------------|------------|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| Helium release (large-volume vent) | Low-Medium | No environmental impact (inert gas); potential O2 displacement indoors | Ventilation design per ANSI/AIHA Z9.5; O2 monitors with auto-vent; outdoor gas bags | OSHA 29 CFR 1910.146 |
| Cooling water leak | Low | Minor water release (<500 gal); no chemical contamination | Containment curbing around cooling tower; leak detection sensors | Clean Water Act stormwater BMPs |
| Compressor oil spill | Low | Small volume (<20 gal); standard cleanup | Spill kit on-site; secondary containment under compressor | SPCC plan if aggregate oil storage >1,320 gal (40 CFR 112) |
| Power outage | Medium | System shutdown; controlled helium vent through relief valves | Emergency generator; UPS for critical controls; controlled shutdown procedure | None (operational, not environmental) |
| Community noise complaint | Low | Investigation by local code enforcement | Sound-dampening enclosures; property-line noise monitoring during commissioning | WAC 173-60 (WA noise regulation) |
| Seismic event | Low (but non-zero in PNW) | Equipment displacement; potential cryogenic spill | Seismic anchoring per IBC/ASCE 7; pressure relief systems; emergency shutdown | Building code seismic design requirements |

**Worst-case environmental scenario:** A complete loss of containment of the liquid helium storage dewar (5,000 L) during a seismic event. The helium rapidly boils (expansion ratio: ~740:1 liquid to gas at 1 atm) and disperses to atmosphere. Environmental impact: zero. Helium is non-toxic and non-reactive. The risk is exclusively to on-site personnel (oxygen displacement in the immediate vicinity) and property (cryogenic damage to equipment and structures). This worst case has no environmental consequence beyond the facility boundary. Compare to the worst case for a chemical warehouse, fuel storage facility, or even a data center (diesel fuel spill, battery electrolyte release).

## The Environmental Argument for PNW Siting

This environmental assessment connects directly to the corridor strategy developed across Documents 7 (I-5 corridor), 16 (PNW geology), and 18 (hub design). The PNW is not the right place to extract helium (Document 16 confirms this). But it is an exceptionally good place to process helium, for environmental reasons:

1. **Grid carbon intensity.** The PNW grid at 80-150 g CO2/kWh produces one-third the carbon footprint of the US average and one-quarter the footprint of Upper Midwest grids. For an operation whose primary environmental impact is electricity consumption, this is the decisive siting factor.

2. **Hydroelectric reliability.** PNW grid reliability is high and not dependent on fossil fuel price volatility. The co-op's electricity costs (Document 18: ~$12K/month at $0.07/kWh) are both low and predictable.

3. **Clean water.** PNW water sources are abundant and high-quality, reducing cooling water treatment requirements and costs.

4. **Moderate climate.** PNW temperatures rarely exceed 95F, reducing cooling load compared to Southwest or Gulf Coast locations. This translates to lower water consumption from evaporative cooling towers and lower electricity demand for HVAC.

5. **Environmental regulatory culture.** Washington and Oregon have strong but navigable environmental regulatory frameworks. Projects that genuinely have minimal environmental impact -- like helium processing -- receive efficient, predictable permitting. The SEPA process is well-established and the agencies are experienced with industrial facility review.

## The Bottom Line

A helium processing hub in the I-5 corridor is environmentally benign by any reasonable standard. It produces no air emissions. It consumes less water than a restaurant. It generates no hazardous waste. Its noise is enclosed and comparable to commercial HVAC. Its carbon footprint -- the only meaningful environmental impact -- is minimized by the PNW's hydroelectric grid to levels equivalent to a few dozen passenger cars.

The SEPA review process will confirm this. The community engagement pathway outlined above ensures that honest, documented answers exist for every environmental question before the question is asked. This is not an operation that communities should fear. It is an operation that communities should welcome: clean jobs, local tax revenue, supply chain resilience for hospitals and universities, and cooperative ownership that keeps economic benefit in the community.

The environment is not a reason not to do this. It is a reason to do it here, in the Pacific Northwest, where the grid is clean, the water is abundant, the regulatory framework is clear, and the cooperative culture (Document 22) is ready to support exactly this kind of community-owned infrastructure.
