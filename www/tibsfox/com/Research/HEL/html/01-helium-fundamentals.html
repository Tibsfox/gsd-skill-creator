Helium Fundamentals — Formation, Extraction, Purification

Natural Formation

Helium is not manufactured through chemical reactions. It is extracted from underground natural gas deposits where it has accumulated over millions of years. While it is the second most abundant element in the universe, it is rare on Earth because it is light enough to escape our atmosphere and drift into space.

Helium is a byproduct of the radioactive decay of heavy elements like uranium and thorium deep within the Earth’s crust. As these elements decay, they emit alpha particles (helium nuclei), which eventually pick up electrons to become stable helium atoms. This gas migrates through the crust and becomes trapped in the same impermeable rock formations that hold natural gas.

Commercial Extraction Process

Helium is typically present in natural gas at low concentrations — often less than 1%, though some “helium-rich” fields reach 7–10%. It is recovered as a byproduct of natural gas processing.

Pre-treatment

Raw natural gas is first scrubbed to remove impurities like water vapor, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen sulfide, which could freeze and clog equipment during later cryogenic stages.

Cryogenic Distillation

This is the primary industrial method. The gas is cooled to extremely low temperatures (around -200°C). Because helium has the lowest boiling point of any element (-269°C / -452°F), almost all other gases — methane, nitrogen, and heavier hydrocarbons — liquefy first and are drained away. This leaves behind crude helium at roughly 50–70% purity.

Fractional Distillation

The remaining mixture undergoes further cooling and pressure adjustments to separate any leftover nitrogen and methane from the crude helium stream.

Final Purification to Grade-A (99.99%+)

Hydrogen Removal

Small amounts of oxygen are added to the mixture. When heated, the oxygen reacts with trace hydrogen to form water, which is then removed through standard drying processes.

Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA)

The gas is passed through a bed of activated carbon or specialized molecular sieves. These materials trap any remaining non-helium molecules in their microscopic pores, allowing high-purity helium to pass through. PSA is the key technology that enables small-scale, modular extraction — units can be deployed at individual wellheads rather than requiring a centralized plant.

Liquefaction

For transport and storage, purified gas is cooled to -269°C (-452°F) to become a liquid. This is extremely energy-intensive, which is why the cost of electricity at the liquefaction facility is a critical factor in production economics.

Why This Matters

Helium is a non-renewable resource on human timescales. Once released into the atmosphere, it escapes to space. Every cubic foot extracted from the Earth is one that will not be replaced for millions of years. This makes efficient extraction, aggressive recycling (90–95% recovery is achievable with modern closed-loop systems), and careful supply chain management not just economically important but geologically necessary.

The development of modular PSA technology is a game-changer: it means helium extraction no longer requires billion-dollar centralized plants. A $50K–$250K unit at a wellhead can begin producing crude helium in months, not years. This democratizes production and enables the distributed, resilient supply chains that the world needs.
