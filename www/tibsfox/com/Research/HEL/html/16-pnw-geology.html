PNW Geological Assessment — Helium Potential and Strategic Implications

Bottom Line Up Front

The PNW has the wrong geology for helium accumulation. This is a genuine geological conclusion, not a data gap. The absence of helium resources in Washington, Oregon, and Idaho is a predictable consequence of the regional geology – young volcanics, accreted terranes, and active tectonics that generate and release helium but do not trap it.

This finding is not a disappointment. It is a strategic clarification that shapes the entire corridor model: the PNW’s role is value-added processing (purification, liquefaction, distribution), not extraction. Crude helium must be sourced from geological provinces that have the right conditions – the Four Corners, Kansas, Wyoming, Saskatchewan, Colorado, Minnesota, Arizona. See Document 17 for how crude helium moves from these sources to a PNW hub, and Document 23 for the financial model that accounts for this transport cost.

Why Helium Needs Specific Geology

Commercial helium accumulation requires three conditions, all present simultaneously:

  1. Source rock: Uranium and thorium in crustal rocks. Alpha decay of U-238, U-235, and Th-232 produces He-4 (alpha particles that capture electrons and become helium atoms). Every granite, every phosphate deposit, every uranium-bearing sediment produces helium. The source is not rare.

  2. Time: Hundreds of millions of years of accumulation. Helium is produced at extraordinarily low rates – a typical crustal rock generates roughly 1 cubic centimeter of helium per ton of rock per million years. To build a commercially extractable reservoir, helium must accumulate for 200-500 million years without escaping.

  3. Trap and seal: An impermeable geological structure that captures and holds the helium. Natural gas traps work – anticlines, fault traps, stratigraphic traps – with cap rocks (shale, anhydrite, salt) that are tight enough to hold helium’s exceptionally small atoms. Helium diffuses through rock more readily than any other gas, so the seal must be excellent.

The PNW fails on time and trap. The rock is too young and too fractured. This is not a marginal failure – it is a categorical mismatch between what helium accumulation requires and what the PNW provides.

Formation-by-Formation Assessment

Columbia River Basalt Group (CRBG)

The most geologically interesting candidate in the PNW. The CRBG covers approximately 210,000 square kilometers of flood basalt across eastern Washington, northeastern Oregon, and western Idaho. Individual basalt flows are 10-100 meters thick, stacked in sequences totaling up to 4 kilometers. Between flows, interflow zones (sedimentary interbeds, flow-top breccias, vesicular basalt) can trap gases – this is how the Mist Gas Field in Oregon and small gas accumulations in the Yakima Fold Belt formed.

What the data shows: - DOE deep borehole programs at the Hanford Site (the DC-6, DC-7, DC-8, and RRL series boreholes) characterized gases in CRBG interflow zones as part of nuclear waste repository site characterization in the 1970s-1980s. These are the deepest and most thoroughly analyzed boreholes in PNW basalt. - Dissolved gases are predominantly nitrogen and methane, with minor CO2. - Helium concentrations are elevated above atmospheric background (0.0005%) but consistently below 0.1%. - Commercial helium threshold is approximately 0.3% minimum (see Document 9 for the economics of extraction at various concentrations). - The basalts are young: 6-17 million years (Miocene). Radiogenic helium accumulation over 6-17 million years is 1-2 orders of magnitude less than accumulation over 200-500 million years in Paleozoic sedimentary basins where commercial helium is found. - The interflow zones are laterally discontinuous and hydraulically connected through fractures, fault zones, and erosional windows. Even where gas is trapped, the trap volumes are small and the seals are imperfect.

Verdict: Not viable for commercial helium. The CRBG has the wrong age, inadequate seal quality, and insufficient accumulation time. A well drilled into CRBG interflow zones would encounter helium concentrations too low to process economically.

What would need to be true for this conclusion to be wrong: A previously unidentified, thick, regionally extensive evaporite or clay seal within the CRBG that has maintained integrity for 10+ million years, overlying an interflow zone with anomalously high helium. No such feature has been identified in any of the hundreds of boreholes drilled through CRBG.

Idaho Batholith and Okanogan Granites

The Idaho Batholith is a massive granitic intrusion covering approximately 25,000 square kilometers of central Idaho. Okanogan granites extend through northeastern Washington into British Columbia. Both have typical crustal uranium and thorium concentrations (2-4 ppm U, 8-12 ppm Th) – these are active helium source rocks. They are producing helium right now through radioactive decay, and have been for 70-100 million years (Cretaceous emplacement age).

Problem: The helium goes nowhere useful. Granitic rocks are fractured crystalline basement. Helium generated in the rock matrix migrates through grain boundaries and microfractures to larger fracture networks that connect to the surface. There is no sealed reservoir. The helium dissipates continuously into groundwater and ultimately into the atmosphere. This is why hot springs in granitic terrain sometimes show elevated helium – they are sampling the continuous flux, not a stored accumulation.

Quantitative context: Typical helium flux from granitic basement is 10^6 to 10^8 atoms per square centimeter per second. This sounds large, but when distributed through a fracture network connected to the surface, it produces only parts-per-million concentrations in groundwater and soil gas – scientifically measurable, commercially irrelevant.

Verdict: Source exists, trap does not. The Idaho Batholith is a helium generator, not a helium reservoir.

Republic Graben (Northeastern Washington)

The Republic Graben contains Eocene volcanic and volcaniclastic rocks (50-45 million years old) with locally elevated uranium. Small uranium mining operations historically extracted ore from this district (the Midnite Mine near Wellpinit was the largest uranium mine in Washington state, operated 1954-1981 by Dawn Mining Company on the Spokane Indian Reservation).

Problem: Same as granites – uranium-bearing rock produces helium, but the helium has no trap. The Republic Graben is an extensional feature with faulted margins and fractured fill. Gas migrates upward through fractures and dissipates. The uranium concentrations are higher than average crustal values, which means helium production rates are higher, but without a seal this only means the helium escapes faster.

Verdict: Source without trap. Not viable.

Idaho Phosphorite Deposits (Southeastern Idaho)

The Permian Phosphoria Formation in southeastern Idaho contains elevated uranium (50-300 ppm in phosphatic zones, vs. 2-4 ppm in average crust). This is a documented helium source rock – the same Phosphoria Formation contributes to helium accumulations in Wyoming and Montana where appropriate traps exist. Southeastern Idaho is geologically transitional between the PNW and the Rocky Mountain foreland.

Problem: The Phosphoria Formation in southeastern Idaho is faulted, folded, and uplifted by Basin and Range extension and Sevier/Laramide thrust faulting. The structural traps that preserved helium in Wyoming (simple anticlines with Paleozoic evaporite seals) are disrupted in Idaho. The helium generated by the Phosphoria in Idaho has had 260 million years of production time (Permian age) but no intact trap in which to accumulate.

Opportunity assessment: Southeastern Idaho is the closest PNW-adjacent geology that might warrant a systematic helium survey. If any PNW-region helium discovery is possible, this is where it would be. However, the probability remains low because the structural geology works against accumulation. A speculative exploration well would cost $500K-$2M with a low probability of success. This is not recommended as a co-op investment when proven sources exist 800-1,200 miles away (see Document 17).

Verdict: Documented helium source rock, but the local geology lacks adequate trapping. Marginally more prospective than the rest of the PNW, but still not commercially viable based on current geological understanding.

Cascade Volcanic Arc

Hot springs throughout the Cascades (Breitenbush and Bagby in Oregon; Sol Duc and Olympic Hot Springs in Washington; Baker Hot Springs near Mount Baker) release gases that include helium. He-3/He-4 isotope ratios in these gases range from 1 to 6 Ra (where Ra = atmospheric ratio of 1.4 x 10^-6), confirming significant mantle helium contributions. This is scientifically interesting – it indicates active degassing from the mantle wedge above the Cascadia subduction zone.

Problem: Concentrations are scientifically measurable but orders of magnitude below economic interest. Typical hot spring gas compositions are 90-98% nitrogen and CO2 with helium at parts-per-million levels. Even a dedicated collection system at the highest-flow hot springs would recover negligible helium volumes.

Deeper issue: Volcanic systems are helium sources, not helium traps. The active tectonics, high heat flow, and fracture permeability that characterize the Cascades ensure that any helium produced (whether from mantle degassing or crustal radioactive decay) reaches the surface quickly. There is no mechanism for long-term storage.

Verdict: Geothermal helium in the Cascades is a subject for academic research, not commercial extraction. The isotope data confirm an active helium flux, but the flux is too diffuse to capture economically.

Yakima Fold Belt and Pasco Basin

The Yakima Fold Belt (a series of east-west trending anticlines in the CRBG of south-central Washington) has been explored for natural gas. Small gas accumulations have been found in CRBG interflow zones, trapped in anticlinal closures. The Pasco Basin (the structural low between the folds) has been extensively drilled as part of Hanford Site characterization.

Problem: Helium was not a target of historical gas exploration. Systematic helium assays are not in the public record for most of these wells. However, the geological constraints are the same as for CRBG generally: young rock (6-17 Ma), inadequate seal quality, small trap volumes. Even if helium is present in Yakima Fold Belt gas shows, concentrations in CRBG interflows are consistently below 0.3% based on Hanford borehole data.

What a formal survey would look like: Re-entering 5-10 existing wells in the Yakima Fold Belt, collecting gas samples from interflow zones, and analyzing for helium content. Cost: approximately $50K-$200K. This would definitively characterize helium concentrations in the most prospective PNW structural traps. It is worth doing for scientific completeness, but the expected result (sub-0.3% helium) would confirm what the geology already predicts.

Verdict: The data gap is real but the expected outcome is negative. A formal survey would satisfy scientific rigor without changing the strategic conclusion.

What About Adjacent Regions?

Williston Basin (Eastern Montana / Western North Dakota)

The Williston Basin is a major Paleozoic sedimentary basin with known natural gas and helium shows. It does not extend into the PNW. The geological boundary is sharp: the PNW consists of accreted oceanic terranes (Wrangellia, Siletzia) and flood basalts plastered onto the western margin of the North American craton. The Williston Basin sits on the craton itself. There is no geological continuity.

Distance from PNW: The western edge of the Williston Basin is approximately 600-800 miles from the I-5 corridor.

Sweetgrass Arch (North-Central Montana)

The Sweetgrass Arch is the nearest helium-prospective geology to the PNW. Historic gas wells in this area have shown elevated helium concentrations, and the geological setting (Paleozoic carbonates with evaporite seals on a cratonic arch) is favorable for helium accumulation. The Kevin-Sunburst Dome field in this area produced gas with up to 2% helium historically.

Distance: Approximately 500 miles from Seattle, 600 miles from Portland. This is closer than Wyoming but still too far for the Sweetgrass Arch to be considered “PNW” geology. It could, however, serve as a crude helium source for a PNW corridor hub alongside Wyoming, Colorado, and Saskatchewan sources (see Document 17 for transport logistics).

Riley Ridge, Wyoming

Riley Ridge is not adjacent to the PNW, but it is the single most important helium source for a PNW corridor. ExxonMobil’s Shute Creek gas processing facility at Riley Ridge produces approximately 50% of current US helium supply. Riley Ridge is approximately 800 miles from Portland and 900 miles from Seattle – the shortest distance of any major helium source to the I-5 corridor.

Relevance: Riley Ridge crude helium (or enriched helium from Shute Creek) is the most logical primary supply for a PNW hub. See Document 17 for routing and cost analysis.

The Missing Data: What We Do Not Know

There is genuinely no systematic helium survey for the PNW. The USGS National Helium Reserve assessments, the BLM helium resource maps, and industry exploration databases all focus on the Four Corners region, Kansas, the Texas Panhandle, Wyoming, and the Midcontinent Rift. Washington, Oregon, and Idaho are absent from federal helium resource maps because no one has looked – not because the data is classified or restricted, but because the geology provided no reason to look.

Specific data gaps: - No systematic helium analysis of Mist Gas Field production gas (the only conventional gas field in Oregon) - No helium assays from the handful of wildcat gas wells drilled in western Washington’s Puget Sound lowland - No comprehensive survey of CRBG interflow gases for helium content outside the Hanford Site boreholes - No analysis of gas shows from oil and gas exploration wells in the Snake River Plain of Idaho

Assessment of whether filling these gaps would change the conclusion: Unlikely. The geological constraints (young rock, fractured basement, active tectonics, absence of Paleozoic evaporite seals) are regional in nature and apply to all of these settings. A systematic survey would almost certainly confirm sub-commercial helium concentrations throughout the PNW. The cost of such a survey ($200K-$500K for a multi-well sampling program) would be better invested in crude helium supply contracts or processing equipment.

Strategic Implications: The Decision This Document Supports

The geological assessment supports a specific strategic decision: Do not invest in PNW helium exploration. Invest in PNW helium processing infrastructure.

If you are a potential co-op member reading this: The geology tells you where NOT to spend money. Do not fund exploration wells in Washington or Oregon. Do not chase hot spring helium in the Cascades. Do not attempt to extract helium from CRBG basalts. Instead, spend that capital on the membrane + PSA + liquefier equipment described in Document 15, installed in the hub facility described in Document 18, processing crude helium transported from the proven sources described in Document 17.

If you are a banker or investor reading this: The PNW helium corridor is not a mining play. It is a manufacturing and logistics play. The raw material comes from elsewhere (proven, operating sources). The PNW adds value through purification, liquefaction, quality control, and distribution. Evaluate it as you would a specialty chemical refinery, not as you would a mineral exploration venture. The geological risk is zero because the model does not depend on PNW geology.

If you are an engineer reading this: The geology constrains the system design. Feed gas will always be transported, never locally extracted. Design the hub for receiving and processing externally sourced crude helium, not for wellhead integration. This simplifies the process flow and eliminates the most variable input (well gas composition changes over time; purchased crude helium has a guaranteed specification).

The PNW’s competitive advantage is in processing, logistics, and market access – not geology. The corridor model is correct: buy crude, purify locally, distribute fast. The geology confirms this is not a limitation – it is the strategy.
