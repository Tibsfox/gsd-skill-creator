PNW Geological Assessment — Helium Potential

Bottom Line Up Front

The PNW has the wrong geology for helium accumulation. This is a genuine geological conclusion, not a data gap. The absence of helium resources in Washington, Oregon, and Idaho is a predictable consequence of the regional geology — young volcanics, accreted terranes, and active tectonics that generate and release helium but do not trap it.

This finding reinforces the corridor model: the PNW’s role is value-added processing (purification, liquefaction, distribution), not extraction. Crude helium must be sourced from geological provinces that do have the right conditions — the Four Corners, Kansas, Wyoming, Saskatchewan, Colorado, Minnesota, Arizona.

Why Helium Needs Specific Geology

Commercial helium accumulation requires three things: 1. Source: Uranium and thorium in crustal rocks (alpha decay produces He-4) 2. Time: Hundreds of millions of years of accumulation 3. Trap: Sealed, impermeable reservoir rock (like natural gas traps)

The PNW fails on time and trap. The rock is too young and too fractured.

Formation-by-Formation Assessment

Columbia River Basalt Group (CRBG)

The most geologically interesting candidate — 210,000 km² of flood basalt across eastern Washington, NE Oregon, and western Idaho. Basalt flows can trap gases between flow units (interflow zones).

What the data shows: - DOE deep borehole programs at Hanford characterized gases in CRBG interflow zones - Dissolved gases are predominantly nitrogen and methane - Helium concentrations are elevated above atmospheric but not commercially interesting (typically <0.1%) - Commercial threshold is ~0.3% minimum - The basalts are young (6–17 million years) — insufficient time for significant radiogenic accumulation

Verdict: Not viable.

Idaho Batholith and Okanogan Granites

Granitic basement rocks with typical crustal U/Th concentrations (2–4 ppm U, 8–12 ppm Th). These are helium source rocks — they generate helium through radioactive decay.

Problem: Helium generated in fractured crystalline rock dissipates without trapping. No sealed reservoir.

Verdict: Source exists, trap does not.

Republic Graben (NE Washington)

Eocene volcanic rocks with elevated uranium — enough to have supported small uranium mining operations historically.

Problem: Same as granites — source without trap.

Idaho Phosphorite Deposits (SE Idaho)

Phosphoria Formation contains elevated uranium. Documented helium source candidate.

Problem: Needs a trap and seal to accumulate. The geology provides neither.

Cascade Volcanic Arc

Hot springs in the Cascades (Breitenbush, Bagby, Sol Duc, Olympic Hot Springs) release helium. He-3/He-4 ratios confirm mantle contributions (1–6 Ra, indicating mixing of mantle and crustal sources).

Problem: Concentrations are scientifically measurable but orders of magnitude below economic interest. Volcanic systems release helium, they don’t trap it.

Yakima Fold Belt and Pasco Basin

Explored for natural gas in CRBG interflows. Gas shows are minor and stratigraphically trapped.

Problem: Helium was not a target. Systematic He assays are not in the public record. Even if helium is present, concentrations in CRBG interflows are well below 0.3%.

What About Adjacent Regions?

Williston Basin

Does not extend into the PNW. Hard geological boundary — the PNW is accreted terranes and flood basalts, not the cratonic sedimentary basins where helium accumulates.

Montana (Sweetgrass Arch)

The nearest helium-prospective geology. Some historic helium shows. ~500 miles from the I-5 corridor.

Wyoming (Riley Ridge)

Already producing ~50% of remaining US supply. ~800 miles from Portland. This is where the corridor model sources crude helium.

The Missing Data

There is genuinely no systematic helium survey for the PNW. The USGS National Helium Reserve data and BLM assessments focus on the Four Corners, Kansas, Wyoming, and the Midcontinent. Washington and Oregon are absent from federal helium resource maps.

The few natural gas wells drilled in western Washington and the Mist Gas Field in Oregon were not systematically assayed for helium.

However: This is not a gap that, if filled, would likely change the conclusion. The geology is understood well enough to predict that commercial helium accumulations are not present. A formal survey would confirm what the geology already tells us.
