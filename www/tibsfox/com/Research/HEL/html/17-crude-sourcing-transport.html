Crude Helium Sourcing & Transport Logistics

The Supply Chain Spine

The corridor model requires crude helium (50–70% purity) transported from US extraction sites to a PNW purification hub. This document maps the specific routes, vehicles, costs, and logistics.

Source Regions

Wyoming (Riley Ridge) ~800 mi ~900 mi ExxonMobil (existing)

Closest sources: Wyoming (~800 mi) and Saskatchewan (~1,100 mi). Colorado and Arizona are mid-range. Minnesota is farthest.

Transport Vehicles

High-Pressure Tube Trailers (Primary Method)

Standard for crude and purified gaseous helium transport: - Capacity: 180,000–250,000 SCF per trailer (~5,000–7,000 Nm³) - Pressure: 2,400–3,600 psig (DOT 3AAX or 3T spec tubes) - Tractor-trailer combination: Standard Class 8 truck - Hazmat classification: DOT Division 2.2 (non-flammable gas) - Speed: Highway speed, ~500 mi/day with single driver, ~800 mi/day team drivers - Cost to own/lease: $150K–$250K per trailer

ISO Containers (For Liquid Helium)

Used for long-distance and international transport: - Capacity: ~11,000 gallons (~41,000 liters) liquid helium per 40-ft ISO container - Holding time: 35–48 days before boil-off venting - Intermodal: Truck, rail, or ship-compatible - Cost: $300K–$500K per container (super-insulated vacuum-jacketed)

Rail

  • Intermodal rail: ISO containers on flatcars. BNSF and Union Pacific serve PNW from all source regions.
  • Transit time: 3–5 days from Colorado/Wyoming to Portland/Seattle
  • Cost advantage: ~40–60% cheaper per ton-mile than truck for distances >500 miles
  • Disadvantage: Less flexible scheduling, terminal-to-terminal (requires truck for first/last mile)

Carrier Requirements

Carriers transporting helium need: - DOT Hazmat registration (Division 2.2 non-flammable gas) - CDL with hazmat endorsement (driver) - DOT-specification equipment (3AAX or 3T tube trailers) - Shipping papers per 49 CFR 172 (UN 1046 for compressed, UN 1963 for liquid) - Placards: Green diamond (non-flammable gas)

Existing hazmat carriers on these routes include specialty gas haulers and general hazmat freight companies. The routes are well-traveled for industrial gases — this is not pioneering logistics.

Rail Alternative

For regular, scheduled deliveries, rail is significantly cheaper:

Route Rail Transit Truck Transit Rail Cost Advantage
Wyoming → Portland 3–4 days 14 hours ~50% cheaper per unit
Colorado → Portland 4–5 days 20 hours ~55% cheaper per unit
Saskatchewan → Seattle 3–4 days 18 hours ~45% cheaper per unit

Trade-off: Rail is slower and less flexible but substantially cheaper for regular bulk shipments. A mature co-op would likely use rail for baseline supply and truck for surge/emergency deliveries.

BNSF serves all source regions and has intermodal terminals in Seattle/Tacoma and Portland. Union Pacific also connects through Portland.

First Steps

  1. Contact crude helium producers (Blue Star, Pulsar, North American Helium, Desert Mountain) for pricing and minimum order quantities
  2. Get freight quotes from hazmat carriers for tube trailer loads on specific routes
  3. Evaluate rail intermodal through BNSF and UP for regular scheduled service
  4. Negotiate supply agreements before building the hub — secure feed stock first

The transport logistics are solvable with standard industrial gas infrastructure. Nothing here requires invention — it requires execution.
