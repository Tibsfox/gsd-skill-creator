The Silicon Forest — Portland Metro Semiconductor Ecosystem

Origins

The Silicon Forest traces to Tektronix, founded in Portland in 1946. Tek became the world’s leading oscilloscope manufacturer and trained a generation of engineers who spun off companies or attracted others to the region. Intel arrived in 1974, choosing Aloha, Oregon for its first fab outside Santa Clara — drawn by cheap land, clean water, reliable power, and Tek-trained talent. That decision seeded the ecosystem that exists today.

Intel in Hillsboro: The Anchor

Intel’s Oregon operations are the largest single-site employer in the state and the company’s global technology development center.

Ronler Acres Campus

Established 1992. Intel’s largest site globally by headcount, spanning ~2,400+ acres in Hillsboro. Houses R&D, advanced technology development, and manufacturing.

Employment Impact

The Portland metro semiconductor industry — including EDA, equipment, materials, and direct manufacturing — supports an estimated 35,000–40,000 direct jobs, with Intel representing roughly half. Including the broader supply chain and indirect employment, the ecosystem’s regional footprint is substantially larger.

The Helium Connection

Intel’s D1X fab complex is the largest helium consumer in the Pacific Northwest. Helium is used in:

  1. EUV lithography — helium-purged environments prevent light absorption in extreme ultraviolet systems
  2. Wafer cooling — helium’s thermal conductivity (highest of any gas) enables backside wafer cooling during plasma etch and ion implantation
  3. Leak detection — helium’s small atomic size detects the tiniest vacuum system leaks
  4. Carrier gas — transports process chemicals in the gas phase

Currently, all helium arrives from out-of-region sources through national distributors. There is no local purification or recycling infrastructure. For a facility that consumes thousands of liters per month at the highest purity specifications, this creates supply chain risk that the March 2026 crisis has made viscerally real.

The Opportunity

The Silicon Forest has everything except upstream helium infrastructure:

Present Missing
World-class fabs (Intel D1X) Local helium purification
Equipment ecosystem Closed-loop helium recycling
Academic pipeline Regional helium storage/buffer
Distribution network R&D-grade gas blending
CHIPS Act funding Supply chain redundancy

A regional helium processing capability — even modest — would reduce Intel’s supply chain risk, lower costs through avoided long-distance transportation, enable R&D-grade custom gas mixtures with rapid turnaround, and position the Silicon Forest for resilience as domestic semiconductor fabrication expands under the CHIPS Act.

The Silicon Forest was built by one decision — Intel choosing Aloha in 1974. Fifty years later, the ecosystem that decision created is the reason a regional helium infrastructure makes sense here and not somewhere else. The customer base already exists. The distribution network already exists. The gap is upstream.
