Global Helium Production — Fast-Track Primary Projects

The Shift to Primary Helium

The global helium industry is undergoing a fundamental shift. Traditional production relied on helium as a byproduct of natural gas processing — meaning helium supply was tied to LNG demand. The new generation of “primary helium” projects target underground reservoirs where helium is the main resource, decoupling production from the natural gas market.

United States: Active Fast-Track Projects

Galactica Project (Colorado)

  • Operator: Blue Star Helium (joint venture with Helium One Global)
  • Status (March 2026): First helium sales in January 2026. Transitioned to 24/7 operations to meet crisis demand.
  • Significance: Demonstrates that small-scale primary helium can reach market quickly.

Topaz Project (Minnesota)

  • Operator: Pulsar Helium
  • Status (March 2026): Seventh appraisal well completed with 100% success rate. Detailed engineering phase for full-scale recovery plant underway. Final investment decision targeted for 2026.
  • Concentration: Up to 8.1% helium — one of the highest in North America
  • Significance: New helium province, not an extension of existing fields.

North American Helium (Saskatchewan + US expansion)

  • Status: Currently the largest producer of non-hydrocarbon-based helium worldwide
  • Expansion: Actively exploring in Utah, Arizona, and Montana
  • Significance: Proving that primary helium production at scale is commercially viable.

Desert Mountain Energy (New Mexico)

  • Status: Commercial production since late 2024
  • Distribution: Direct delivery via tube trailers to end-users
  • Significance: Operating, revenue-generating — not a prospect, a business.

Altura Energy (Arizona — Holbrook Basin)

  • Status (March 2026): Multi-well development program underway. Pipeline upgrades expected by May 2026 to bring three idle wells back online.

Australia: Re-establishing Domestic Capability

Australia’s only helium plant (Darwin) closed in late 2023, leaving the country — and its East Asian trading partners — without a nearby production source.

Amadeus Basin (Northern Territory)

  • Operators: Central Petroleum, Santos, Peak Helium (joint investigation)
  • Potential resource: 44 billion cubic feet — roughly seven times current annual global demand
  • Significance: If developed, this would be a globally significant source

Georgina Energy (Northern Territory)

  • Focus: Amadeus Basin subsalt zones
  • Results: Reported helium grades up to 9%
  • Significance: Ultra-high concentration, potentially among the richest known deposits

Ramsay Project (South Australia)

  • Operator: Gold Hydrogen
  • Results: Reported purities as high as 36.9%
  • Impact: Triggered an international “land rush” of companies into the region

Darwin LNG Vent Stream

  • Operator: BOC
  • Approach: Exploring technology to extract helium from existing LNG processing vent streams
  • Significance: Re-uses existing infrastructure rather than requiring new wells

Fast-Tracking Strategies

Three approaches are accelerating the timeline from discovery to production:

1. Modular Processing Plants

Pre-fabricated, modular units (from manufacturers like Chart Industries) allow for faster on-site assembly compared to traditional custom-built refineries. A modular PSA system can be operational in months; a traditional plant takes 3–5 years.

2. Vertical Integration

Producers are engaging directly with end-users — semiconductor fabs and space agencies — to secure long-term offtake agreements before construction begins. This provides the capital certainty needed for rapid drilling and facility development.

3. Helium-3 Co-Extraction

Some projects are assessing the feasibility of extracting Helium-3 (a rare isotope essential for quantum computing and specialized detectors) alongside standard Helium-4. The premium value of He-3 significantly improves project economics and can justify the capital investment for primary extraction.

Key Takeaway

The helium production landscape is diversifying rapidly. The era of dependence on a handful of sources (Qatar, the now-privatized Federal Helium Reserve, and legacy natural gas byproduct) is ending. Dozens of primary helium projects across two continents are moving toward production. The question is no longer whether alternative sources exist — it is how fast they can scale.
