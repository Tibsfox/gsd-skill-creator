Global Helium Production — Fast-Track Primary Projects

The Shift to Primary Helium

The global helium industry is undergoing its most fundamental structural transformation since the U.S. government began stockpiling helium in 1925. For the past century, virtually all commercial helium was produced as a byproduct of natural gas processing. When a gas field happened to contain helium at economically recoverable concentrations (typically above 0.3%), the helium was separated during the cryogenic processing of methane and sold as a secondary revenue stream. This meant that helium supply was structurally coupled to natural gas demand: if LNG markets contracted, helium production contracted with them, regardless of helium-specific demand.

This coupling produced a persistent and worsening imbalance. Global helium demand has grown at 2-4% annually for the past two decades, driven by semiconductor fabrication expansion, MRI installations in developing economies, growing space launch cadence, and the emergence of quantum computing. Natural gas production, meanwhile, has been increasingly shaped by decarbonization policies, LNG market volatility, and the shift toward renewable energy — factors that have nothing to do with helium demand but that directly control helium supply.

The new generation of “primary helium” projects represents a fundamental decoupling. These ventures target underground reservoirs where helium is the principal economic resource — not a byproduct of natural gas processing. The gas extracted from these formations may contain helium concentrations of 0.5% to 10% or higher, with the balance being primarily nitrogen (non-combustible and non-hazardous) rather than methane. This changes the safety profile, the regulatory pathway, and the economics simultaneously: primary helium operations do not require natural gas processing infrastructure, are not subject to natural gas market fluctuations, and in many jurisdictions face simpler permitting requirements because they do not involve hydrocarbon production.

The primary helium sector has grown from a handful of exploratory ventures in 2018-2019 to a global industry with producing assets, publicly traded companies, and institutional investment. The March 2026 Qatar crisis (see Document 05: Market Crisis) has dramatically accelerated this shift by demonstrating the vulnerability of the byproduct-dependent supply model.

United States: Active Fast-Track Projects

Galactica Project (Colorado)

  • Operator: Blue Star Helium Ltd (ASX: BNL), an Australian-listed company focused exclusively on primary helium in the United States, operating through a joint venture with Helium One Global Ltd (AIM: HE1).
  • Location: Las Animas County, southeastern Colorado. The project area covers approximately 38,000 acres in the Raton Basin, targeting helium-bearing formations at relatively shallow depths (1,500-3,000 feet).
  • Status (March 2026): First helium sales commenced in January 2026 following completion of a 4-well pilot program. Production has been transitioned to 24/7 continuous operations to meet crisis-driven spot market demand. Current production is estimated at 1-3 MMcf per month of crude helium.
  • Helium concentration: 1.0-3.5% across the producing wells, with nitrogen as the dominant balance gas.
  • Processing: On-site modular PSA unit producing crude helium at 50-70% purity for sale via tube trailers to regional purification facilities.
  • Financial significance: Blue Star’s market capitalization increased approximately 300% between January and March 2026 as the Qatar crisis validated the primary helium business model. The company demonstrates that small-scale primary helium can reach first revenue within 18-24 months of initial drilling — a timeline that would have been considered impossible for a new helium production venture five years ago.
  • Implication for PNW: Galactica proves the fast-track model works. A similar geological assessment and modular deployment approach could be applied to prospective helium-bearing formations in the broader western U.S., potentially including sites within truck-haul distance of PNW distribution networks (see Document 03: PNW Distribution).

Topaz Project (Minnesota)

  • Operator: Pulsar Helium Inc. (TSXV: PLSR; OTCQB: PLSRF), a Canadian-listed company.
  • Location: Babbitt, Minnesota, on the southeastern edge of the Mesabi Iron Range in the Midcontinent Rift System — a 1.1-billion-year-old failed continental rift that created the geological structures now trapping helium.
  • Status (March 2026): Seven appraisal wells completed with a 100% success rate — every well encountered helium at or above expected concentrations. Detailed engineering for a full-scale helium recovery plant is underway with multiple engineering firms bidding on the front-end engineering design (FEED). Final investment decision (FID) targeted for Q3-Q4 2026, with first production from the commercial plant expected in 2028.
  • Concentration: Up to 8.1% helium measured in drill stem tests — one of the highest concentrations ever recorded in North America. At this concentration, extraction economics are exceptional: PSA systems operate at peak efficiency, and the ratio of helium revenue to processing cost is highly favorable.
  • Geological significance: The Topaz discovery represents an entirely new helium province. Unlike the traditional U.S. helium sources in Kansas, Texas, and Wyoming (which are all associated with Permian-age sedimentary basins), Topaz helium is trapped in Precambrian rift structures. This means the North American helium resource base is significantly larger and more geographically diverse than previously understood. If similar rift-associated helium accumulations exist in other Proterozoic structures across the Canadian Shield and northern U.S., the global helium resource picture could change materially.
  • Resource estimate: Pulsar has published a preliminary resource estimate suggesting hundreds of millions of cubic feet of recoverable helium — sufficient to support a 20+ year production operation.
  • Implication for PNW: Topaz’s 8.1% concentration and the Precambrian rift geological model suggest that high-concentration helium accumulations may exist in other ancient geological structures across western North America, including the Columbia Plateau basalts and rift zones of the inland Northwest. While no exploration has been conducted specifically for helium in these formations, the Topaz precedent expands the range of geological settings worth evaluating.

North American Helium (Saskatchewan + U.S. expansion)

  • Operator: North American Helium Inc. (private), headquartered in Calgary, Alberta.
  • Status: Currently the world’s largest producer of primary (non-hydrocarbon-associated) helium. Operating multiple producing wells in southwestern Saskatchewan, with processing through two operational helium purification facilities.
  • Production: Estimated current output of 50-100 MMcf per year of Grade-A helium, making it a globally significant producer.
  • Expansion: Actively exploring and acquiring acreage in Utah (Paradox Basin), Arizona (Holbrook Basin), and Montana (Kevin Dome and other prospects). The Montana exploration is particularly relevant to PNW interests, as Montana’s helium-bearing formations are approximately 500-700 miles from the Seattle-Portland corridor — within practical tube trailer transport distance.
  • Business model: Vertically integrated from wellhead to customer delivery. NAH drills, extracts, purifies, and delivers helium directly to end-users and distributors via tube trailers, eliminating intermediary markups.
  • Financial backing: NAH has raised over $100 million in private equity funding, with institutional investors including pension funds and sovereign wealth interests. This level of capitalization enables aggressive drilling programs without the delays of public market fundraising.
  • Significance: NAH proves that primary helium production at commercial scale is viable, profitable, and scalable. Their operational track record provides a benchmark against which newer projects can be measured.

Desert Mountain Energy (New Mexico)

  • Operator: Desert Mountain Energy Corp. (TSXV: DME; OTCQB: DMEHF).
  • Location: Holbrook Basin, northeastern Arizona and northwestern New Mexico. Producing from the McCauley Helium Field.
  • Status (March 2026): Commercial production has been ongoing since late 2024. Revenue-generating operations — this is not a prospect or a pre-production asset, it is an operating business.
  • Distribution model: Direct delivery via tube trailers to end-users, primarily in the southwestern U.S. The company has negotiated off-take agreements with industrial gas companies and direct consumers.
  • Concentration: Reported concentrations of 3-7% helium across producing wells, with nitrogen as the balance gas.
  • Significance: Desert Mountain Energy demonstrates the complete pathway from exploration through to revenue-generating operations for a primary helium company. Their timeline — from initial drilling (2019) to commercial production (late 2024) — illustrates a roughly 5-year cycle from exploration to revenue, with the potential for significant acceleration using fast-track approaches.

Altura Energy (Arizona — Holbrook Basin)

  • Operator: Altura Energy (private consortium).
  • Location: Holbrook Basin, Arizona — the same broad geological province as Desert Mountain Energy but targeting distinct structural traps.
  • Status (March 2026): Multi-well development program underway with encouraging initial results. Pipeline upgrades and surface facility construction expected to be completed by May 2026, enabling three previously drilled but idle wells to begin production.
  • Significance: Altura’s presence alongside Desert Mountain Energy in the Holbrook Basin indicates a maturing play with multiple operators, reducing the risk that any single company’s success is anomalous. The Holbrook Basin is becoming a recognized helium province.

Australia: Re-establishing Domestic Capability

Australia’s helium situation illustrates the consequences of concentration risk. The country’s sole helium production facility — the Darwin LNG helium plant, operated by BOC (a subsidiary of Linde) — closed in late 2023 when the ConocoPhillips-operated Darwin LNG plant ceased processing gas from the Bayu-Undan field (which was depleted). This single closure eliminated all domestic helium production for Australia and removed a significant supply source for the entire Asia-Pacific region, including Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore — all major semiconductor manufacturing economies.

The strategic implications were immediate. East Asian semiconductor fabs that had sourced helium from Darwin were forced to rely almost entirely on Qatari exports. When Qatar declared force majeure in March 2026, these fabs found themselves with no regional supply source at all. The resulting scramble has accelerated Australian helium exploration dramatically.

Amadeus Basin (Northern Territory)

  • Operators: A consortium approach involving Central Petroleum Ltd (ASX: CTP), Santos Ltd (ASX: STO), and Peak Helium Pty Ltd, with active seismic and appraisal programs.
  • Location: Central Australia, approximately 200 km south of Alice Springs, Northern Territory.
  • Potential resource: Preliminary estimates suggest up to 44 billion cubic feet (Bcf) of helium in the Amadeus Basin — roughly seven times current annual global consumption of approximately 6.2 Bcf. If even 10-20% of this resource proves recoverable, it would be globally transformative.
  • Geological setting: The helium is associated with Proterozoic and Paleozoic sedimentary formations overlying granitic basement rocks rich in uranium and thorium — the radiogenic sources that generate helium over geological time. The basin has known natural gas shows with elevated helium concentrations.
  • Infrastructure challenge: The Amadeus Basin is remote, approximately 1,500 km from the nearest major port (Darwin). Development would require either a pipeline to Darwin (capital-intensive but enabling liquefaction and export) or on-site liquefaction with road tanker transport. The Stuart Highway provides the only road access.
  • Significance: If developed at scale, the Amadeus Basin would restore Australia as a major helium producer and provide a desperately needed Asia-Pacific supply source. However, the timeline from current exploration to meaningful production is likely 5-8 years, which means it cannot address the immediate 2026 crisis.

Georgina Energy (Northern Territory)

  • Operator: Georgina Energy Ltd (ASX: GRG).
  • Focus: Amadeus Basin subsalt zones — deeper structural targets that may concentrate helium at higher grades.
  • Results: Reported helium grades up to 9% in drill-stem tests — extraordinary concentrations that, if confirmed over commercial volumes, would make these among the richest helium deposits ever characterized.
  • Status: Early-stage exploration with encouraging results. Additional appraisal drilling planned for 2026-2027.
  • Significance: Ultra-high concentration fundamentally changes extraction economics. At 9% helium, a simple membrane separator can produce crude helium economically — PSA may not even be required for the initial concentration step. This reduces capital requirements and accelerates time-to-production.

Ramsay Project (South Australia)

  • Operator: Gold Hydrogen Ltd (ASX: GHY).
  • Location: Ramsay area, Yorke Peninsula, South Australia, approximately 150 km northwest of Adelaide.
  • Results: During exploration for natural hydrogen, Gold Hydrogen encountered helium at purities as high as 36.9% — a figure so extraordinary that it attracted international attention and required independent verification. For context, the richest known conventional helium deposits (in Tanzania) contain approximately 10-12% helium. A 36.9% concentration would be, by a wide margin, the highest ever recorded in a geological reservoir.
  • Verification status: Independent sampling and analysis are ongoing. If confirmed, this concentration would fundamentally alter global helium economics.
  • Impact: The Ramsay results triggered what the Australian mining press termed a “land rush” — multiple companies staking exploration tenements across South Australia specifically for helium. Whether or not the 36.9% figure is representative of a larger deposit, it has established South Australia as a helium exploration frontier.
  • Co-production potential: Gold Hydrogen’s primary target is natural hydrogen (also known as “gold hydrogen” or “white hydrogen”), with helium as a co-product. The economics of co-producing hydrogen and helium from the same well could be highly attractive, as both gases have strong and growing markets.

Darwin LNG Vent Stream Recovery

  • Operator: BOC (Linde subsidiary), in partnership with Inpex (operator of the Ichthys LNG project that now feeds the Darwin LNG facility).
  • Approach: Exploring technology to capture and purify helium from the vent gas stream of the Darwin LNG plant. When LNG is produced from natural gas, helium and other light gases accumulate in the process and are typically vented to atmosphere. Recovering this helium does not require new wells — only additional processing equipment at the existing plant.
  • Significance: This is the fastest potential pathway to restore Australian helium production, because it leverages existing infrastructure. The helium concentration in LNG vent streams is typically 1-5%, well within the range for PSA recovery. However, the volumes are limited by the throughput of the host LNG plant, and the economics depend on negotiating favorable terms with Inpex for access to the vent stream.
  • Timeline: If technical and commercial agreements are reached, a modular PSA recovery unit could be operational within 12-18 months — significantly faster than any greenfield exploration project.

Fast-Tracking Strategies

Three technological and commercial strategies are compressing the traditional timeline from helium discovery to commercial production — a cycle that historically took 7-15 years but is now being accomplished in 2-5 years.

1. Modular Processing Plants

The shift from custom-built, site-specific processing facilities to pre-fabricated, standardized modular units is the single most important enabler of fast-track helium development. Companies like Chart Industries (which acquired Howden in 2023), Air Liquide Engineering, and Linde Engineering manufacture modular PSA and membrane separation systems that are:

  • Pre-engineered: Designed as standard products with well-characterized performance envelopes, eliminating 12-18 months of custom engineering.
  • Factory-built: Assembled and tested in controlled factory conditions, then shipped as complete skid-mounted units. This eliminates on-site construction risk and accelerates the schedule.
  • Rapidly deployable: A modular PSA system can be installed and commissioned in 3-6 months from order placement. A traditional custom plant takes 3-5 years from initial engineering to first production.
  • Scalable: Multiple modules can be deployed in parallel as production scales. A single node might start with one PSA skid and add additional capacity as more wells are drilled.
  • Cost-efficient: A modular PSA unit costs $50,000-$250,000 depending on capacity and specifications, compared to hundreds of millions of dollars for a centralized cryogenic separation plant.

The modular approach is the enabling technology for the Virtual Helium Plant concept described in Document 06.

2. Vertical Integration and Off-take Agreements

Traditional helium producers sold crude helium into an opaque intermediary market. The new generation of primary helium companies is bypassing intermediaries by engaging directly with end-users — semiconductor fabs, space agencies, MRI manufacturers, quantum computing companies — to secure long-term off-take agreements before construction begins.

This approach provides multiple advantages:

  • Capital certainty: A signed off-take agreement with a creditworthy counterparty (e.g., Intel, TSMC, or a national space agency) provides the revenue certainty that project finance lenders require. This enables debt financing at favorable terms, reducing the equity investment needed.
  • Price stability: Off-take agreements typically include price floors or fixed-price tranches that protect the producer from spot market volatility on the downside while allowing some participation in upside through indexed pricing.
  • Customer lock-in: In a supply-constrained market, customers are willing to commit to multi-year contracts with volume guarantees in exchange for supply security. These contracts create a defensible competitive position for the producer.
  • Demand intelligence: Direct relationships with end-users provide visibility into demand trends (e.g., fab expansion plans, MRI installation forecasts) that inform production scaling decisions.

North American Helium’s model — drilling, extracting, purifying, and delivering directly to customers — exemplifies this approach and has been commercially validated.

3. Helium-3 Co-Extraction

Some primary helium deposits contain trace concentrations of Helium-3 (He-3), a rare isotope with a nucleus of two protons and one neutron (compared to He-4’s two protons and two neutrons). He-3 is present in natural helium at a ratio of approximately 1.37 parts per million (the He-3/He-4 isotope ratio varies by source, from 0.01 to 10 ppm, depending on the geological origin of the helium).

He-3 has extraordinary value because of its unique physical properties:

  • Quantum computing: He-3/He-4 dilution refrigerators are the only technology capable of achieving the millikelvin temperatures required by superconducting quantum processors (IBM, Google, Rigetti, IQM, and others). A single dilution refrigerator requires approximately 30-50 liters of He-3, priced at approximately $2,000 per liter — representing $60,000-$100,000 of He-3 per quantum computer.
  • Neutron detection: He-3 is the most sensitive neutron detector material known, used in nuclear security (port screening for smuggled nuclear materials), nuclear physics research, and oil well logging. Post-9/11 demand for neutron detectors rapidly depleted the global He-3 stockpile.
  • Medical imaging: He-3 can be hyperpolarized and inhaled for functional lung MRI — a technique that provides detailed images of lung ventilation patterns without radiation exposure.

The global He-3 supply is extremely constrained. The primary source is the radioactive decay of tritium (hydrogen-3) in the U.S. nuclear weapons stockpile, which produces approximately 15,000-20,000 liters per year. Demand consistently exceeds supply, maintaining prices at approximately $2,000 per liter — roughly 100 times the price of He-4 on a per-liter basis.

If a primary helium operation can economically separate He-3 from its He-4 production stream, the premium revenue from He-3 sales can significantly improve overall project economics — potentially covering 5-15% of total operating costs despite He-3 representing less than 0.001% of the helium produced by volume. This co-extraction capability requires specialized isotope separation equipment (typically cryogenic distillation or pressure-driven diffusion), but the capital cost can be justified by the extraordinary per-unit value of He-3.

Key Takeaway

The helium production landscape is diversifying at a pace that would have seemed improbable five years ago. The era of dependence on three sources — Qatar, the now-privatized Federal Helium Reserve, and the Riley Ridge byproduct stream — is ending. Dozens of primary helium projects across four continents (North America, Australia, Africa, and Europe) are in various stages from exploration through commercial production. The question is no longer whether alternative sources exist — it is how fast they can scale.

For decision-makers evaluating helium supply chain investments, the evidence supports several conclusions:

  1. Primary helium is commercially proven. North American Helium and Desert Mountain Energy have demonstrated that dedicated helium extraction is profitable at current and pre-crisis prices. Blue Star Helium has shown that first revenue can be achieved within 18-24 months of drilling.

  2. Geographic diversification is real but incomplete. The concentration of new projects in the western U.S. and central Australia means supply remains geographically clustered. The PNW, despite being a significant demand center, remains far from any producing primary helium source — a gap that the Virtual Helium Plant concept (see Document 06) is designed to address.

  3. The 2026 crisis is an accelerant, not the cause. The shift to primary helium was underway before Qatar’s force majeure. The crisis has compressed timelines, validated business models, and attracted capital — but the structural drivers (growing semiconductor demand, Federal Reserve privatization, LNG market decoupling) were already in motion.

  4. Australia’s timeline is 5-8 years for meaningful impact. Despite extraordinary geological results, the remoteness of the Amadeus Basin, the early-stage nature of most Australian exploration, and the infrastructure requirements mean that Australian production cannot address the current crisis. It is a medium-term play, not a short-term solution.

  5. The technology enablers are ready. Modular PSA systems, vertical integration models, and He-3 co-extraction capabilities are all commercially available today. The constraint on new production is not technology — it is geological knowledge (finding the helium), capital deployment (funding the development), and regulatory approval (permitting the operations). These are solvable problems on 2-5 year timescales.
