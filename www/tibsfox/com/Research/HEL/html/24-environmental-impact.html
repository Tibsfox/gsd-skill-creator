Environmental Impact Assessment

The Short Version

Helium processing is one of the cleanest industrial operations possible. Helium is inert, non-toxic, non-flammable, and non-reactive. It does not produce emissions, does not contaminate water, and does not generate hazardous waste. The primary environmental impact is electricity consumption for compression and cryogenic cooling — and in the PNW, that electricity is predominantly hydroelectric.

Impact Categories

Air Emissions

Essentially zero direct emissions. Helium processing does not involve combustion, chemical reactions, or volatile compounds. The only atmospheric release is helium itself (an inert noble gas that has no greenhouse effect, no ozone interaction, and no toxicity).

Minor indirect emissions from: - Compressor lubrication systems (oil-free compressors eliminate this) - Emergency generator (diesel, if installed — intermittent use only) - Vehicle emissions from delivery trucks (standard industrial transport)

Water Use

A helium processing hub requires cooling water for compressors and potentially for the liquefier:

Component Water Use Recirculation
Compressor cooling 5–10 GPM Closed-loop cooling tower (>95% recirculated)
Liquefier cooling 5–15 GPM Closed-loop (>95% recirculated)
Facility domestic Minimal Standard plumbing
Net consumption <500 gallons/day Evaporation losses from cooling tower

For context: a single-family home in the PNW uses ~150–200 gallons/day. A helium hub uses 2–3x that — comparable to a small restaurant, not an industrial water consumer.

No process water discharge. Helium processing does not use water in the process itself — only for cooling. Cooling tower water is recirculated, not discharged.

Electricity Consumption

This is the primary environmental footprint. A small hub consuming 200–300 kW runs approximately: - ~175,000 kWh/month - ~2.1 million kWh/year

PNW Grid Carbon Intensity

The PNW electrical grid is among the cleanest in the US due to hydroelectric dominance:

Grid Carbon Intensity Annual CO2 for Hub
PNW (WA/OR) ~100–150 g CO2/kWh ~210–315 tonnes/year
US average ~400 g CO2/kWh ~840 tonnes/year
Texas (ERCOT) ~350 g CO2/kWh ~735 tonnes/year

A PNW helium hub has roughly one-third the carbon footprint of an equivalent facility in Texas or the Midwest. This is a genuine environmental advantage, not greenwashing — it comes from the region’s hydroelectric infrastructure.

For comparison: 210–315 tonnes CO2/year is equivalent to ~45–70 passenger cars.

Waste Streams

Waste Type Source Volume Handling
Spent PSA adsorbent Bed replacement (every 5–8 years) 500–2,000 lbs per change Non-hazardous solid waste — landfill or adsorbent recycler
Spent membrane modules Module replacement (every 5–10 years) 50–200 lbs per change Polymer — standard recycling or landfill
Compressor oil Oil changes (if not oil-free) 5–20 gallons/year Standard used oil recycling
Filter elements Pre-treatment filters (quarterly) <50 lbs/year Non-hazardous solid waste

No hazardous waste. Helium processing generates no RCRA-listed waste, no radioactive waste, no toxic chemicals.

Noise

Compressors and cryogenic equipment generate noise (typically 70–85 dB at 1 meter). Standard mitigation: - Equipment enclosed in building - Sound-dampening enclosures on compressors - Property line setbacks per local zoning - Comparable to a commercial HVAC system

Land Use

1–3 acres for the facility. No significant habitat impact on industrial-zoned land. No wetland fill anticipated for any of the candidate corridor locations identified in the hub design document.

SEPA Environmental Checklist (Washington State)

A helium processing facility in Washington would require a SEPA Environmental Checklist (WAC 197-11-960). Expected determination: Determination of Non-Significance (DNS) — the project does not have a probable significant adverse environmental impact.

Key checklist items and expected answers:

Item Assessment
Earth/geology No excavation beyond building foundation. Industrial-zoned land.
Air No significant emissions. De minimis for NWCAA/PSCAA
Water Closed-loop cooling, <500 gal/day net consumption, no process discharge
Plants/animals Industrial-zoned site, no habitat impact
Energy 200–300 kW. Served by existing grid.
Noise Enclosed equipment, standard industrial levels
Land use Consistent with industrial zoning
Transportation 2–4 truck deliveries/month (receiving), 5–10 deliveries/month (outgoing)
Public services No additional demand

The Community Trust Document

This research exists so that when a co-op proposes a helium hub in a PNW community, the environmental questions have honest, documented answers. Not marketing — data. The operation is clean, the footprint is small, the water use is minimal, the waste is non-hazardous, and the grid carbon is low because of where we are.

The environment is not a reason not to do this. It’s a reason to do it here.
