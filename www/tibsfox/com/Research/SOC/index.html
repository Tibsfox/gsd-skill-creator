Research Modules

M1: The Migration River

History Track A · Origins

The Second Great Migration (1940–1970) sent millions from the Jim Crow South to the West Coast. The same origin states — Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Arkansas — fed both the LA Basin and the Pacific Northwest. This module maps the shared genealogy and the rail corridors that carried it.

M2: Compton's Crucible

Structural Track A · Transformation

From cornfield suburb to national symbol. Compton's transformation through white flight, blockbusting, redlining, deindustrialization, and the crack epidemic — the structural machinery that produced the landscape Eric Wright was born into in 1964.

M3: The Seattle Parallel

Geography Track A · Parallel arc

Seattle's Central District and Portland's Albina neighborhood underwent nearly identical structural transformations at nearly the same historical moments. Same forces, different latitudes. The parallel that reveals the corridor.

M4: The Crack Highway

Corridor Track B · Transmission

The epidemic moved north from Los Angeles through direct human networks. By 1985–86, crack had reached Seattle. Aaron Dixon's testimony documents families fleeing LA only to find the gangs had preceded them. The northward migration of structural violence.

M5: Music as Infrastructure

Music Track B · Architecture

Eazy-E converted street capital into institutional infrastructure: Ruthless Records, 1987. The $12,000 budget, the TR-808, the six-week session at Audio Achievements. Nastymix Records in Seattle. Two independent labels, one architectural principle.

M6: 31 Years Later — Legacy and Resonance

Legacy Track C · Resonance

Eazy-E died March 26, 1995. His final statement converted dying into public health information. Bone Thugs' “Tha Crossroads” hit #1. The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inducted N.W.A in 2016. The corridor still organizes West Coast cultural geography.
