# Cross-Module Synthesis: The Integrated PNW Garden

> When all five modules operate together, the maritime Pacific Northwest garden ceases to be a collection of separate concerns — climate, soil, native plants, food production, pest management — and becomes a single managed ecosystem. This synthesis document traces the connections, identifies the hub species that anchor the system, maps the year as an integrated cycle, and demonstrates that ecological pattern recognition across modules is the defining skill of the accomplished PNW gardener.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The five modules of this series — M1 (Climate & Microclimates), M2 (Soil Science & Management), M3 (Native Plant Integration), M4 (Food Production & Seasonal Calendar), and M5 (Pest, Disease & Climate Adaptation) — each describe a layer of the PNW garden system. Read individually, they provide authoritative guidance on their respective domains. Read together, they reveal something more: a garden that functions as a coherent ecological unit, where decisions in one domain cascade through all others.

The maritime Pacific Northwest's distinctive two-season climate — wet winters (October–April receiving 70–80% of annual precipitation [EXT-07]) and dry summers (June–September with 60–90 days of negligible rainfall [GOV-02]) — is the master variable. Every module orbits this reality. Soil management (M2) responds to winter waterlogging and summer drought. Native plants (M3) are pre-adapted to this exact rhythm. Food production (M4) exploits the cool-season windows that continental-climate guides ignore. Pest and disease management (M5) leverages the seasonal transitions that suppress some organisms while favoring others.

The synthesis reveals five integration cascades — chains of cause and effect that cross module boundaries and produce outcomes greater than any single module could achieve alone. It identifies hub species — plants that appear across three or more modules, serving simultaneous roles in pollination, pest suppression, soil health, food production, and habitat creation. And it maps the year as a single integrated system, showing how the gardener's actions in each month serve multiple modules simultaneously.

**The central thesis:** A PNW garden designed with cross-module awareness requires less labor, fewer inputs, and produces more food, more habitat, and more resilience than one managed as separate, siloed concerns. The modules are not additive — they are multiplicative.

## 2. The Five Integration Cascades

Each cascade traces a chain of ecological cause and effect that crosses module boundaries. These are not theoretical — they are observable processes in well-designed PNW gardens.

### Cascade 1: Native Plants → Beneficial Insects → Pest Suppression (M3 → M5)

**The chain:** Native insectary plants attract and sustain populations of predatory and parasitoid insects → these beneficial insects suppress pest populations on food crops → the gardener reduces or eliminates pesticide use → reduced pesticide use protects the beneficial insects → the cycle self-reinforces.

**How it works in practice:**

Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*), the premier insectary plant in the PNW [PRO-04, EXT-05], attracts parasitoid wasps, lacewings, syrphid flies, and lady beetles with its flat-topped flower clusters and extended bloom period (June–August). These beneficial insects don't just visit yarrow — they establish resident populations near it. A yarrow border within 30–50 feet of food crops provides effective pest suppression across multiple pest species [EXT-05].

The parasitoid wasps attracted to yarrow (*Cotesia glomerata*, *Trichogramma* spp.) parasitize cabbageworm larvae at rates of 50–80% in gardens with established insectary plantings [EXT-05]. This is more effective than any single organic spray — and it requires no repeated application, no timing decisions, and no non-target impacts.

Douglas aster (*Symphyotrichum subspicatum*) extends this cascade into late season (August–October) [PRO-04], covering the critical fall window when brassica pests (cabbage aphids, cabbageworms) are most active on fall-planted kale, broccoli, and cauliflower. Without late-season native bloom, beneficial insect populations crash before the fall food garden reaches peak production.

Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*) anchors the early end of the cascade (February–April) [PRO-04], providing the first pollen and nectar for emerging bumblebee queens. These queens found colonies that produce the workers and larvae that consume aphids throughout the spring and summer. The February bloom of Oregon grape determines the July aphid population in the food garden — a two-module cascade with a five-month delay.

**Key M5 connection — the aphid gap:** M5 documents the "aphid gap" — the 2–4 weeks in April–May when aphid populations outpace predator response [PRO-09, EXT-05]. Gardens with strong native plantings (M3) close this gap faster because predator populations overwinter in native habitat (hedgerows, ground cover, permanent plantings) and emerge earlier and in greater numbers. The gap is a symptom of insufficient ecological infrastructure, not an inevitable consequence of PNW climate.

**Cascade metrics:**
- Parasitism rates of 50–80% on cabbageworms in gardens with insectary plantings [EXT-05]
- Effective foraging range of beneficial insects: 30–50 feet from native plantings [EXT-05]
- Predator establishment time after native planting: 1–3 seasons [PRO-04]
- Pesticide cost savings: effectively 100% for cabbageworm management once row covers + parasitoid populations are established

### Cascade 2: Soil Health → Mycorrhizal Networks → Climate Resilience (M2 → M5)

**The chain:** No-till soil management preserves mycorrhizal fungal networks → mycorrhizae extend root systems, improving water and nutrient access → plants with intact mycorrhizal connections resist drought stress and nutrient deficiency → healthier plants resist pest and disease pressure → reduced pest/disease pressure reduces the need for soil-disrupting interventions → the cycle self-reinforces.

**The soil biology foundation:**

M2 establishes that a single teaspoon of healthy PNW garden soil contains approximately 1 billion bacteria, several yards of fungal hyphae, thousands of protozoa, and dozens of nematodes [EXT-04]. This biological community is not incidental — it is the engine that drives nutrient cycling, disease suppression, and soil structure formation. The maritime PNW's mild temperatures, consistent moisture, and abundant organic matter support exceptionally rich soil biology compared to drier or colder regions [GOV-04].

**The mycorrhizal mechanism:**

Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM) — the type that associates with most vegetable crops — extend fungal hyphae into soil micropores too small for roots to penetrate, accessing phosphorus, zinc, and copper that would otherwise be unavailable [EXT-04]. This extended root network doesn't just improve nutrition — it improves drought resilience. Plants with intact mycorrhizal connections can access water from soil horizons beyond the reach of their physical roots.

Tilling severs these networks. M2 documents that mycorrhizal recovery takes weeks to months after tilling [EXT-04]. In a PNW garden where the growing season is already constrained by cool spring temperatures, losing weeks of mycorrhizal function during the critical early-growth period compounds every subsequent challenge.

**The disease suppression pathway:**

M5 connects soil health directly to disease resistance: mycorrhizal networks enhance plant disease resistance through induced systemic resistance (ISR), priming the plant's immune response before pathogens arrive [EXT-01]. Soil rich in diverse microbial communities suppresses soil-borne pathogens through competitive exclusion — beneficial organisms occupy the ecological niches that pathogenic fungi (*Phytophthora*, *Pythium*, *Fusarium*) would otherwise colonize [EXT-05].

**Climate adaptation dimension:**

M5 frames this cascade as a climate adaptation strategy. As PNW summers become warmer and drier (heat domes increasing in frequency [EXT-07]), plants with intact soil biology — mycorrhizal networks, deep organic matter reserves, and healthy aggregate structure — will outperform plants in biologically depleted soil. The investment in soil health today is insurance against the climate of tomorrow.

**Cascade metrics:**
- Each 1% increase in organic matter helps soil hold approximately 20,000 additional gallons of water per acre [EXT-04]
- No-till soil warms 2–4 weeks earlier in raised beds [PRO-07]
- Cover crops contribute 50–200 lb nitrogen per acre per year (legumes) [EXT-01]
- Bare soil loses 2–5 tons of topsoil per acre per year to erosion [EXT-04]

### Cascade 3: Climate Zones → Planting Calendar → Variety Selection (M1 → M4)

**The chain:** USDA zone and microclimate determine frost dates and heat accumulation → frost dates and soil temperature drive the planting calendar → the planting calendar constrains variety selection to short-season cultivars → variety selection determines pest exposure windows → pest exposure windows inform the IPM strategy.

**The master variable:**

M1 establishes climate as the master variable — every gardening decision flows from the region's maritime-Mediterranean pattern [GOV-03]. The 2023 USDA revision shifted much of the maritime PNW approximately half a zone warmer [GOV-01], which M4 translates into practical consequences: longer potential growing seasons, earlier last frost dates, and the possibility of crops (figs, certain peppers) that were marginal a decade ago.

**The critical timing decisions:**

M4's month-by-month calendar is built directly on M1's climate data. The "second spring" of August — the single most important planting month in the PNW calendar [PRO-06] — exists because of the specific interaction between the PNW's cooling autumn temperatures and day-length dynamics documented in M1. Most fall crops need to reach near-maturity before day length drops below 10 hours (approximately November 1), after which growth essentially stops [PRO-07].

**Variety selection as climate adaptation:**

The PNW tomato challenge illustrates this cascade perfectly. M1 documents that the maritime PNW accumulates growing degree days much more slowly than continental climates. M4 translates this into a concrete rule: only short-season varieties (Sungold 57 days, Stupice 55 days, Siletz 52 days, Oregon Spring 58 days [PRO-07, EXT-06]) will reliably ripen outdoors. This is not a suggestion — it is a climate-driven constraint.

The cascade extends into M5: shorter-season varieties ripen before the peak late blight window (cool, wet weather in late summer), reducing disease exposure. Legend, bred at Oregon State University specifically for PNW conditions, combines early maturity (68 days) with late blight resistance [EXT-06] — a variety that exists because breeders understood this exact cascade.

**Microclimate exploitation:**

M1's microclimate analysis feeds directly into M4's placement strategy. A south-facing wall (documented as 5–15°F warmer than north-facing exposure [EXT-07]) isn't just "warmer" — it shifts the effective USDA zone by half to one full zone, potentially making the difference between Zone 8b (challenging for peppers) and Zone 9a (feasible for peppers with season extension). This microclimate data determines not just what to plant but where to place it — the tomatoes against the south wall, the lettuce in the afternoon shade, the cold frames positioned to capture maximum solar gain.

**Cascade metrics:**
- Growing season: 180–250 frost-free days depending on sub-region and microclimate [EXT-02]
- South-facing walls: 5–15°F warmer than north-facing [EXT-07]
- Raised beds warm 2–4 weeks earlier than in-ground soil [PRO-07]
- August is the critical planting window — fall crops outperform spring in the PNW [PRO-06]

### Cascade 4: Native Habitat + Food Production as Integrated Landscape (M3 → M4)

**The chain:** Native border plantings provide pollinator habitat → pollinators service food crop flowers → improved pollination increases fruit and vegetable yields → the food garden provides nectar gaps (culinary herbs allowed to flower) → combined native + food garden supports more diverse pollinator populations than either alone.

**The pollination bridge:**

M3 documents that the PNW supports over 300 native bee species [PRO-04], along with syrphid flies, parasitic wasps, and other beneficial insects. M4's food crops depend on these pollinators — blueberries, strawberries, squash, cucumbers, beans, peppers, and fruit trees all require or benefit from insect pollination.

Mason bees (*Osmia lignaria*), profiled in M3, are the most effective native spring pollinators — a single mason bee can pollinate as many flowers as 60–100 honeybees [PRO-04]. Their active season (March–May) coincides exactly with the bloom window of early fruit crops (blueberries, strawberries, fruit trees). Oregon grape, camas, and red-flowering currant (M3) provide the early-season nectar and pollen that sustain mason bee populations before food crops begin flowering.

**The five integration design patterns (from M3 Section 9):**

1. **Native border around food garden** — yarrow, Oregon grape, and native roses within 30–50 feet of vegetables
2. **Native hedgerow as garden room divider** — wax myrtle and tall Oregon grape between garden zones
3. **Native understory in fruit orchard** — sword fern, wild ginger, and groundcovers beneath fruit trees instead of turf
4. **Pollinator corridor** — yarrow, aster, and native grasses connecting native areas to food garden
5. **Edible-native matrix** — native huckleberries interplanted with blueberries (shared soil preferences: acidic, pH 4.5–5.5 [PRO-05])

**The hedgerow effect:**

M3's native hedgerow design (layered: Pacific wax myrtle + Nootka rose + Oregon grape + kinnikinnick) reduces wind speed by 50% for a distance of 10× the hedge height [PRO-01]. A 10-foot hedgerow protects food crops for 100 feet downwind — significant for exposed maritime PNW gardens facing prevailing southwesterly winter winds. This microclimate modification (connecting back to M1) extends the growing season for adjacent food crops by reducing wind chill, evapotranspiration, and mechanical stress.

**Cascade metrics:**
- Mason bee pollination equivalence: 1 mason bee = 60–100 honeybees [PRO-04]
- Hedgerow wind reduction: 50% for 10× hedge height distance [PRO-01]
- Beneficial insect effective range: 30–50 feet from native plantings [EXT-05]
- Edible native overlap: huckleberries, coastal strawberry, camas share habitat with food crops

### Cascade 5: Cover Crops → Soil Biology → Reduced Disease → Better Yields (M2 → M4 → M5)

**The chain:** Cover crops protect soil over winter → cover crops fix nitrogen (legumes) and build organic matter → improved soil biology suppresses soil-borne pathogens → reduced disease pressure produces healthier food crops → healthier crops require less intervention → less intervention preserves soil biology → the cycle self-reinforces.

**The winter gap problem:**

M2 documents that leaving PNW soil bare from October through March exposes it to erosion (2–5 tons of topsoil per acre per year [EXT-04]), nutrient leaching, weed colonization, and soil structure degradation. Cover crops prevent all four problems while actively improving soil for the following food production season [PRO-01].

**The nitrogen bridge:**

M2's cover crop selection table translates directly into M4's food production strategy. Crimson clover contributes 70–130 lb nitrogen per acre [PRO-01]; fava beans contribute 100–200 lb per acre [PRO-01]. A fall-planted cover crop of fava beans + oats, terminated in spring, provides the nitrogen foundation for the following season's heavy-feeding crops — tomatoes, squash, corn, peppers. This eliminates or dramatically reduces the need for purchased nitrogen fertilizer while simultaneously protecting soil biology.

**The disease suppression pathway:**

M5 establishes that soil-borne diseases — clubroot (*Plasmodiophora brassicae*), late blight (*Phytophthora infestans*), damping off (*Pythium*, *Rhizoctonia*), Fusarium wilt — are the PNW's primary disease challenge, promoted by the wet maritime climate [EXT-05]. Cover crops suppress these pathogens through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Competitive exclusion** — Living roots support rhizosphere bacteria that outcompete pathogens [EXT-04]
2. **Mycorrhizal maintenance** — Living roots keep mycorrhizal networks active through winter, ready to colonize spring-planted food crops without the weeks of recovery needed after tilling [EXT-04]
3. **Improved drainage** — Cover crop root channels improve soil structure and water infiltration, reducing the waterlogged conditions that favor *Phytophthora* and *Pythium* [PRO-01]
4. **Biofumigation potential** — Certain brassica cover crops (mustard) release compounds that suppress soil-borne pathogens, though this must be balanced against clubroot risk in brassica rotation schemes [EXT-05]

**The crop rotation connection:**

M4's four-bed rotation plan (Brassicas → Legumes → Alliums/Roots → Solanaceae) is strengthened by strategic cover crop selection. Cover crop species should follow the same rotation rules as food crops — crimson clover (legume) before brassica beds, not before other legume beds; cereal rye (grass family) is rotation-neutral and can follow any crop family [PRO-01].

**Cascade metrics:**
- Nitrogen contribution: 50–200 lb/acre/year from legume cover crops [EXT-01]
- Winter erosion prevention: cover crops save 2–5 tons topsoil/acre/year [EXT-04]
- Clubroot persistence: up to 20 years in infected soil — rotation is the only management [EXT-05]
- Recommended mixes: Crimson clover 60% + winter rye 40% (standard); Fava 70% + oats 30% (heavy feeder prep) [PRO-01]

## 3. The Year as a System — Month-by-Month Integration

Each month's activities serve multiple modules simultaneously. This calendar shows how a single garden task creates cascading benefits across the entire system.

### January — Planning and Soil Rest

| Domain | Activity | Modules Served |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Soil** | Soil rests under winter mulch/cover crops; biology processes fall amendments | M2 |
| **Food** | Order seeds (short-season varieties per M1 zone data); plan crop rotation per M4 | M1, M4 |
| **Native** | Dormant season — native plantings provide overwintering cover for beneficial insects | M3, M5 |
| **Pest/Disease** | Ground beetles active under mulch, hunting slug eggs; overwintering beneficial habitat intact | M5 |
| **Harvest** | Overwintered kale (frost-sweetened [PRO-01]), leeks, Brussels sprouts, parsnips under mulch | M4 |

**Integration insight:** The cover crops planted in September (M2) are simultaneously protecting soil (M2), fixing nitrogen for spring crops (M4), providing habitat for ground beetles that hunt slug eggs (M5), and maintaining the living root system that keeps mycorrhizal networks active (M2→M5).

### February — Emergence Begins

| Domain | Activity | Modules Served |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Soil** | Apply lime 4–6 weeks before spring planting where pH < 6.0 [EXT-01] | M2, M4 |
| **Food** | Start brassica and pepper seeds indoors; plant bare-root fruit trees | M4 |
| **Native** | Indian plum and Oregon grape begin blooming — first food for bumblebee queens [PRO-02] | M3, M5 |
| **Pest/Disease** | Bumblebee queens emerge from hibernation; mason bees emerge | M3, M5 |
| **Climate** | Monitor soil temperature at 4-inch depth — planting guide more reliable than air temp [PRO-07] | M1 |

**Integration insight:** Oregon grape bloom (M3) in February sustains the bumblebee queens that will found the colonies providing pest suppression all summer (M5). The February lime application (M2) adjusts pH for the brassica seeds being started indoors (M4) — a two-module chain timed by M1's climate data.

### March — The First Outdoor Planting

| Domain | Activity | Modules Served |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Soil** | Apply 1–2 inches compost to beds; fork compost into top layer in no-till beds | M2 |
| **Food** | Direct-sow peas, carrots, beets, spinach, lettuce as soil reaches 40°F [EXT-02]; plant potatoes | M4 |
| **Native** | Red-flowering currant blooms — primary rufous hummingbird migration fuel [PRO-02] | M3 |
| **Pest/Disease** | Set slug barriers (copper tape, iron phosphate bait); row covers on brassicas | M5 |
| **Climate** | Terminate cover crops 2–4 weeks before planting in those beds [PRO-01] | M1, M2, M4 |

**Integration insight:** Cover crop termination (M2) releases nitrogen for spring food crops (M4) while the residue mulch feeds soil biology (M2) and suppresses weeds. Meanwhile, red-flowering currant (M3) fuels arriving hummingbirds that will contribute to pollination and insect control (M5). Slug management begins (M5) — iron phosphate bait protects the seedlings being planted (M4) without harming the ground beetles that are also hunting slugs (M5).

### April — The System Accelerates

| Domain | Activity | Modules Served |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Soil** | Maintain mulch; begin building compost pile from spring trimmings | M2 |
| **Food** | Succession-sow lettuce, radishes, carrots every 2–3 weeks [EXT-02]; transplant brassicas | M4 |
| **Native** | Camas, salal, kinnikinnick, bleeding heart in bloom — mason bee peak [PRO-04] | M3 |
| **Pest/Disease** | APHID GAP begins — DO NOT spray [PRO-09, EXT-05]; monitor and wait for predators | M5 |
| **Climate** | Frost risk diminishes; soil warming tracked for warm-season timing decisions | M1 |

**Integration insight:** The aphid gap (M5) is the system's most important test of cross-module understanding. Gardens with strong native plantings (M3) and intact soil biology (M2) close this gap faster because predator populations overwinter in native habitat and emerge in greater numbers. The gardener's patience during April — trusting the system to self-correct — is the single most important pest management decision of the year.

**Integration insight:** May's critical tomato-timing decision (M4) is driven entirely by M1's microclimate analysis. The south-facing wall that M1 identifies as 5–15°F warmer [EXT-07] is where M4 places the warm-season crops. The mulch applied now (M2) serves dual duty: conserving moisture for the coming dry season and suppressing the weed germination that bare soil would trigger. Native bloom (M3) sustains the beneficial insects that are now catching up with aphid populations (M5).

## 4. Hub Species — Plants That Anchor the System

Hub species appear across three or more modules, serving simultaneous roles that create disproportionate ecological value. Removing a hub species disrupts multiple cascades; adding one strengthens the entire system.

### Tier 1: Keystone Hub Species (4+ modules)

#### Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium* — native form)

| Module | Role | Evidence |
|--------|------|----------|
| **M2 (Soil)** | Deep-rooted perennial that improves soil structure; drought-tolerant, reducing irrigation demand | Adapted to dry-to-moderately-moist PNW soils [EXT-01] |
| **M3 (Native)** | Premier insectary plant; parasitoid wasps, lacewings, syrphid flies, lady beetles, small bees, beetles all visit | Flat-topped flowers accessible to short-tongued beneficial insects [PRO-04, EXT-05] |
| **M4 (Food)** | Companion plant for food garden borders; attracts pollinators that service food crops; edible (tea) with extension citation | Design principle: plant within 30–50 feet of food crops [EXT-05] |
| **M5 (Pest)** | Attracts parasitoid wasps that parasitize cabbageworms at 50–80% rates; syrphid fly larvae consume aphids | Premier beneficial insect attractant across the PNW growing season [PRO-04, EXT-05] |

## 5. The PNW Garden Ecosystem Model

### How a Well-Designed PNW Garden Functions

A PNW garden managed with cross-module awareness operates as a managed ecosystem — not a factory, not a wilderness, but something in between. It is a landscape where the gardener makes strategic interventions (planting, mulching, timing) while allowing natural processes (predation, decomposition, nutrient cycling, pollination) to do the majority of the work.

### The Three Operating Principles

**Principle 1: The garden is always working.**

In a well-designed system, there is no "off season." Winter cover crops fix nitrogen (M2) and shelter ground beetles (M5). Fall rains carry lime into the root zone (M2). Native evergreens (M3) shelter overwintering mason bee cocoons. December kale is converting frost to sugar (M4). The gardener rests; the system does not.

**Principle 2: Every organism serves multiple functions.**

Hub species thinking applies at every scale. The slug is not just a pest — it is food for ground beetles, garter snakes, and ducks [EXT-05, PRO-04, PRO-09]. The cover crop is not just nitrogen — it is erosion control, weed suppression, habitat, and biomass. The native hedgerow is not just habitat — it is windbreak, privacy screen, pollinator corridor, and aesthetic frame. Designing for multiple functions at every point reduces inputs and increases resilience.

**Principle 3: Patience is a management strategy.**

The aphid gap (M5) is the system's calibration test. A gardener who sprays during the gap breaks the biological control cycle. A gardener who waits allows predator populations to self-correct — typically within 2–3 weeks [PRO-09, EXT-05]. This patience — accepting short-term imperfection for long-term system health — is the hardest skill to develop and the most valuable one to master. It applies at every timescale: waiting for cover crop termination before planting (M2→M4), waiting for soil to pass the ball test before working clay (M2), waiting for soil temperature rather than air temperature to signal transplant timing (M1→M4).

### The Input Reduction Model

A garden managed as an integrated ecosystem progressively reduces external inputs over time:

| Input | Year 1–2 | Year 3–5 | Year 5+ |
|-------|---------|---------|--------|
| **Nitrogen fertilizer** | Moderate (soil building phase) | Reduced (cover crops establishing) | Minimal to zero (cover crop cycling + compost maintenance) |
| **Pesticides** | Organic products as needed | Targeted applications only | Rarely needed (beneficial insect populations established) |
| **Irrigation** | Standard setup | Mulch reducing demand | Deep mulch + native groundcovers + efficient drip only where needed |
| **Soil amendments** | Heavy (initial pH correction, organic matter building) | Moderate (maintenance compost, periodic liming) | Light (annual compost top-dress, lime every 2–3 years per soil test) |
| **Labor (pest management)** | Active — slug patrol, row covers, monitoring | Moderate — monitoring, targeted intervention | Passive — monitoring only; system self-regulates most pest pressure |

**The 5-year trajectory:** A new garden requires significant input and intervention. By year 5, a well-managed garden has: soil biology established and self-sustaining (M2), native plantings mature enough to support resident beneficial insect populations (M3→M5), cover crop routines that maintain fertility without purchased fertilizer (M2→M4), and crop rotation patterns that prevent disease buildup (M4→M5). The gardener's role shifts from producer to manager — from doing the work to designing the system that does the work.

### Energy Flow in the PNW Garden Ecosystem

The PNW garden ecosystem can be understood as a network of energy and nutrient flows:

**Solar energy** → captured by food crops (M4), native plants (M3), and cover crops (M2) → converted to biomass → consumed by:
- **Harvest** (food for the gardener — M4)
- **Pollinators** (nectar and pollen — M3, M4)
- **Beneficial insects** (prey consumption — M5)
- **Soil biology** (decomposition — M2)

**Atmospheric nitrogen** → fixed by cover crop legumes (M2) and native nitrogen-fixers (red alder, Pacific wax myrtle — M3) → converted to plant-available ammonium → absorbed by food crops (M4)

**Rainfall** → managed by drainage planning (M2), rain gardens with native plants (M3), mulch retention (M2) → feeds soil biology year-round → excess directed to habitat features rather than storm drains

**Organic matter** → produced by all plantings → recycled through composting, mulching, and cover cropping (M2) → feeds the soil food web that drives all other processes → the system's central engine

The critical insight: **organic matter cycling (M2) is the flywheel of the entire system.** Every other process — nutrient cycling, pest suppression, water management, plant health — depends on the biological activity that organic matter sustains. Stop feeding the soil, and every cascade weakens.

### Resilience Under Climate Change

M5's climate adaptation section and M1's climate projections combine to paint a clear picture: the PNW garden of 2030 will face warmer summers, more intense heat domes, potentially shifting precipitation patterns, and expanded ranges for some pests and diseases [EXT-07, GOV-02, GOV-03]. The integrated garden is inherently more resilient to these changes than the module-by-module garden:

- **Soil with high organic matter** (M2) holds more water during drought and drains better during deluges — buffering both extremes
- **Mycorrhizal networks** (M2) extend root access to deeper water during dry periods
- **Native plant diversity** (M3) provides redundancy — if one species suffers under changed conditions, others fill the ecological gap
- **Diverse food crop selection** (M4) — cool-season and warm-season crops, early and late varieties — hedges against any single season being anomalous
- **Beneficial insect populations** (M5) maintained by native habitat (M3) provide pest management that adapts automatically to shifting pest dynamics — predators follow prey without gardener intervention
