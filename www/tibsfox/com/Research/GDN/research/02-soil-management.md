# Module 2: Soil Science & Management for the Pacific Northwest Garden

> Understanding the ground beneath your feet is the single most consequential step in maritime PNW gardening. The soils west of the Cascades — shaped by volcanic eruptions, continental glaciation, and ten thousand years of temperate rainforest cycling — are unlike soils anywhere else in North America. They are simultaneously rich and challenging, fertile and frustrating. This module maps the geology, chemistry, biology, and practical management of PNW soils so that every planting decision rests on solid ground.

---

## 1. Soil Origins and Types

### 1.1 Geologic Context

The soils of the maritime Pacific Northwest are products of three dominant geologic forces: volcanism, glaciation, and prolonged forest-floor accumulation [GOV-04]. The Cascade Range volcanoes — Mount Rainier, Mount St. Helens, Mount Hood, Mount Baker, Glacier Peak — have deposited ash and pumice across western Washington and Oregon for millennia. These volcanic deposits contribute mineral-rich parent material that weathers into productive but sometimes poorly structured soils [GOV-04].

Continental glaciation during the Pleistocene epoch left behind a complex mosaic of deposits. The Puget Lobe of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet advanced as far south as present-day Olympia, Washington, approximately 16,000 years ago [GOV-04]. As it retreated, it deposited till (unsorted mixtures of clay, sand, gravel, and boulders), outwash (sorted sands and gravels carried by meltwater), and lacustrine sediments (fine silts and clays deposited in glacial lakes). This is why soil character in the Puget Sound lowlands can change dramatically within a single residential lot — one corner may be heavy clay sitting on hardpan, while another is sandy gravel over glacial outwash [GOV-04, EXT-04].

South of the glacial boundary, the Willamette Valley and lower Columbia Basin soils are dominated by alluvial deposits from the Missoula Floods — catastrophic glacial lake outbursts that scoured eastern Washington and deposited deep layers of silt across the valley floor [GOV-04]. These silty soils are among the most agriculturally productive in the region but present their own drainage challenges.

On top of all this geology, millennia of conifer-dominated forest cover have created a natural soil profile rich in organic acids, tannins, and decomposed plant material. The native soil pH across most of the maritime PNW trends acidic — typically between 4.5 and 6.0 — as a direct consequence of conifer needle decomposition and high annual rainfall leaching base cations [EXT-01, GOV-04].

### 1.2 The Four Primary Soil Types

Maritime PNW gardeners will encounter four dominant soil types, often in combination. Understanding which type (or types) you're working with is essential before any amendment, planting, or drainage decision [GOV-04, EXT-04].

#### Heavy Clay

**Characteristics:** Dense, sticky when wet, rock-hard when dry. Forms a ribbon when squeezed between thumb and forefinger that extends 2 inches or more [EXT-04]. Particle size is less than 0.002 mm. Drains slowly — saturated clay may take 24–48 hours to drain 1 inch of standing water [GOV-04]. Compacts easily under foot traffic or machinery.

**Nutrient profile:** High cation exchange capacity (CEC), meaning clay soils hold nutrients effectively. Naturally rich in potassium, calcium, and magnesium when not severely leached [EXT-01]. However, nutrients may be chemically bound and less available to plants in strongly acidic conditions (below pH 5.5) [EXT-01].

**Where found:** Puget Sound lowlands, glacial till deposits throughout western Washington, marine sediment areas in the San Juan Islands and coastal zones [GOV-04]. Parts of Portland metro area, especially east of the Tualatin Mountains.

**Gardening implications:** Requires significant organic matter addition to improve structure. Never work clay soil when wet — this destroys soil aggregates and creates long-lasting compaction [EXT-04]. Wait until soil passes the "ball test": squeeze a handful, and if it crumbles when poked rather than holding its shape, it's dry enough to work [EXT-04]. Raised beds are often the most practical solution for heavy clay sites [PRO-07].

#### Sandy Loam

**Characteristics:** Loose, gritty texture. Individual sand grains visible to the naked eye. Particle size ranges from 0.05 mm to 2.0 mm [EXT-04]. Drains rapidly — water moves through sandy soil at rates of 2–6 inches per hour [GOV-04]. Easy to dig and work at any moisture level. Warms quickly in spring.

**Nutrient profile:** Low CEC — nutrients and water pass through quickly. Requires more frequent fertilization and irrigation. Organic matter breaks down faster in sandy soils due to higher oxygen availability [EXT-01].

**Where found:** Glacial outwash plains (particularly south Puget Sound — Thurston, Pierce, and south King counties), river terraces, coastal deposits [GOV-04]. Parts of the Fraser Valley in British Columbia. River deltas and alluvial fans.

**Gardening implications:** Excellent for root vegetables (carrots, parsnips, beets), Mediterranean herbs, and plants requiring sharp drainage [PRO-07]. Poor water and nutrient retention means more frequent irrigation in summer and consistent organic matter additions to build water-holding capacity [EXT-04]. Mulching is critical to reduce moisture loss.

#### Silty Soil

**Characteristics:** Fine-textured, smooth and silky to the touch (like flour). Particle size between 0.002 mm and 0.05 mm [EXT-04]. Holds water well — sometimes too well. Compacts readily when walked on or worked wet. Forms a weak ribbon when squeezed (shorter than clay's ribbon) [EXT-04].

**Nutrient profile:** Moderate CEC. Generally fertile when properly managed. Silty soils in the Willamette Valley and lower river valleys are among the most productive agricultural soils in the PNW [GOV-04].

**Where found:** River valleys, floodplains, and areas of Missoula Flood deposition. Willamette Valley, Cowlitz River valley, Skagit Valley, lower Fraser Valley [GOV-04]. Low-lying areas adjacent to major waterways.

**Gardening implications:** Mixing coarse materials (perlite, coarse sand, or fine bark) improves structure and prevents compaction [EXT-04]. Silty soils benefit greatly from permanent mulch cover and minimal tillage. They are prone to crusting when left bare — rain impact seals the surface, preventing water infiltration and seedling emergence [EXT-04].

#### Mixed Glacial Deposits

**Characteristics:** Many PNW residential properties — particularly in the Puget Sound lowlands — contain variable soils that change character within feet. A single garden may have pockets of clay, lenses of sand, layers of gravel, and bands of silt [GOV-04]. Glacial till (unsorted material deposited directly by ice) often underlies these mixed deposits.

**The hardpan problem:** Glacial till frequently forms a compacted, nearly impermeable layer — called hardpan or "tillite" — at depths of 18–36 inches below the surface [GOV-04]. This hardpan blocks root penetration and creates a perched water table, causing waterlogging above even when conditions below are dry. Testing for hardpan is essential before installing fruit trees or deep-rooted perennials [EXT-04].

**Gardening implications:** Requires zone-by-zone assessment. Dig test holes in multiple locations before planning garden layout. Each zone may need different amendments and drainage strategies [GOV-04, EXT-04]. Raised beds provide consistent growing conditions regardless of what lies beneath.

### 1.3 Identifying Your Soil Type

**The jar test** is the simplest home soil assessment method [EXT-04]:

1. Collect a representative sample from 6–8 inches deep
2. Fill a quart jar one-third full with soil
3. Add water to near the top, plus 1 tablespoon of dishwashing liquid (dispersant)
4. Shake vigorously for 2 minutes
5. Let settle undisturbed for 48 hours
6. Measure layers: sand settles in 1 minute (bottom), silt in 2–4 hours (middle), clay in 24–48 hours (top)

**Interpreting results [EXT-04]:**
- More than 50% sand layer → sandy soil, focus on water retention
- More than 40% clay layer → clay soil, focus on drainage and structure
- Roughly equal layers → loam (congratulations — this is the ideal)
- Dominant silt layer → silty soil, focus on preventing compaction

**The ribbon test** provides quick field identification [EXT-04]: Take a moist handful of soil and squeeze it into a ribbon between thumb and forefinger. Clay forms a ribbon longer than 2 inches. Silt forms a short, weak ribbon (under 1 inch). Sand won't form a ribbon at all. Loam forms a ribbon of about 1 inch that breaks when bent.

## 2. Soil pH and Acidity Management

### 2.1 Why PNW Soils Are Acidic

The maritime Pacific Northwest is naturally prone to soil acidity for three reinforcing reasons [EXT-01, PRO-07]:

**High rainfall.** Western Washington receives 35–60+ inches of precipitation annually; some areas (Quinault, Hoh) exceed 140 inches [GOV-04]. This constant throughput of water leaches calcium (Ca²⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺) — the base cations that buffer soil pH — out of the root zone and into groundwater [EXT-01]. The result is a gradual but persistent acidification.

**Conifer dominance.** The native forest canopy — Douglas fir, western red cedar, western hemlock, Sitka spruce — produces needles that decompose into organic acids [EXT-01]. Thousands of years of conifer litter accumulation have pushed many native forest soils to pH 4.5–5.5 [GOV-04].

**Organic acid cycling.** Decomposing organic matter releases humic and fulvic acids that further lower pH. The combination of high organic matter and high rainfall creates a steady acid drip through the soil profile [EXT-01].

**Practical consequence:** Many garden soils in the maritime PNW test below pH 6.0 without amendment [PRO-07, EXT-01]. Some urban soils, particularly those near old-growth stumps or beneath mature conifers, may test as low as pH 4.5 [EXT-01]. At these pH levels, essential nutrients — particularly phosphorus, calcium, and magnesium — become chemically less available to plants even when physically present in the soil [EXT-01].

### 2.2 pH Ranges for Common PNW Crops

Understanding the optimal pH range for your crops determines whether liming is necessary [EXT-01, PRO-07]:

| Crop Category | Specific Crops | Optimal pH Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Brassicas** | Broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale | 6.0–7.0 | Lime essential in most PNW soils [EXT-01] |
| **Root vegetables** | Carrots, beets, parsnips | 6.0–6.8 | Beets particularly sensitive to acidity [EXT-01] |
| **Legumes** | Peas, beans, fava beans | 6.0–7.0 | Nitrogen fixation impaired below pH 5.5 [EXT-01] |
| **Solanaceae** | Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant | 5.5–6.8 | More tolerant of mild acidity [EXT-01] |
| **Cucurbits** | Squash, cucumbers, melons | 5.5–6.8 | Moderate tolerance [EXT-01] |
| **Alliums** | Onions, garlic, leeks | 6.0–7.0 | Prefer neutral conditions [EXT-01] |
| **Leafy greens** | Lettuce, spinach, chard | 6.0–7.0 | Spinach especially intolerant of acidity [EXT-01] |
| **Potatoes** | All varieties | 4.8–5.5 | Actually prefer acid soils; liming promotes scab [EXT-01] |
| **Blueberries** | All varieties | 4.5–5.5 | Native PNW soils often perfect as-is [PRO-07] |
| **Strawberries** | All varieties | 5.5–6.5 | Moderate acid tolerance [EXT-01] |
| **Raspberries** | All varieties | 5.5–6.5 | Well-suited to mildly acidic PNW soils [PRO-07] |
| **Fruit trees** | Apple, pear, cherry, plum | 6.0–7.0 | Lime the planting zone before establishing [EXT-01] |
| **Herbs** | Rosemary, thyme, lavender | 6.0–7.5 | Mediterranean herbs prefer higher pH [PRO-07] |
| **Native plants** | Salal, Oregon grape, sword fern | 4.5–6.0 | Adapted to native acid conditions [PRO-03] |

### 2.3 Liming Protocols

When soil tests reveal pH below 6.0 and your target crops require higher pH, garden lime (calcium carbonate, CaCO₃) is the standard amendment [EXT-01].

**Application rates by soil type [EXT-01]:**

| Starting pH | Sandy Soil (lb/100 sq ft) | Loamy Soil (lb/100 sq ft) | Clay Soil (lb/100 sq ft) | Target pH |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4.5 | 8–10 | 12–15 | 15–20 | 6.5 |
| 5.0 | 5–7 | 8–10 | 10–13 | 6.5 |
| 5.5 | 3–4 | 5–6 | 6–8 | 6.5 |
| 6.0 | 1–2 | 2–3 | 3–4 | 6.5 |

**Lime types [EXT-01]:**
- **Agricultural lime (calcium carbonate):** Safest, slowest acting. Takes 2–3 months to fully react. Apply in fall for spring effect, or in early spring for summer crops. Standard recommendation for home gardens.
- **Dolomitic lime:** Contains both calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate. Preferred when soil tests show magnesium deficiency — common in heavily leached PNW soils [EXT-01]. Same application rates as agricultural lime.
- **Hydrated lime (calcium hydroxide):** Fast-acting but caustic. Can burn plant roots and soil organisms if over-applied. Not recommended for home gardens unless soil test specifically recommends it [EXT-01].
- **Wood ash:** Contains calcium carbonate equivalent plus potassium. Apply at 5–10 lb per 100 sq ft maximum. Test soil first — wood ash raises pH quickly and can cause potassium toxicity if over-applied [EXT-01, PRO-01].

**Application timing [EXT-01]:**
- Best: Fall application (October–November), allowing winter rains to incorporate lime into the root zone
- Acceptable: Early spring (February–March), 4–6 weeks before planting
- Avoid: Summer application on growing crops (soil disturbance + possible root contact)

**Critical exceptions:**
- Do NOT lime potato beds — potatoes prefer pH 4.8–5.5, and liming promotes potato scab disease [EXT-01]
- Do NOT lime blueberry beds — blueberries require pH 4.5–5.5 [PRO-07]
- Do NOT lime rhododendron, azalea, or heather plantings — these ericaceous plants are adapted to acid conditions [PRO-03]

### 2.4 When Compost Becomes the Problem

A common pitfall in PNW gardening is excessive compost application, leading to nutrient overload [PRO-03].

**Warning signs of compost overload [PRO-03]:**
- Soil as dark as coffee grounds throughout the top 12 inches
- Lush, dark green foliage but poor fruiting (nitrogen excess)
- Phosphorus levels above 100 ppm on soil tests (environmental concern — phosphorus runoff contributes to water quality degradation)
- Plants showing symptoms of micronutrient deficiency despite "rich" soil (excess phosphorus locks out zinc, iron, and manganese) [EXT-01]

**When this occurs [PRO-03]:**
- Stop adding compost entirely for 1–2 growing seasons
- Switch to coarse-textured surface mulch: 2–4 inches of wood chips, arborist chips, or bark
- Mulch feeds soil biology slowly without adding concentrated nutrients
- Resume compost additions only after soil test confirms nutrient levels have normalized

### 2.5 Soil Testing Frequency

Test every 2–3 years for established garden beds [EXT-01, EXT-04]. Test annually when:
- Starting a new garden in unknown soil
- Correcting a significant pH imbalance (tracking progress)
- Growing crops with specific pH requirements (blueberries, potatoes)
- Symptoms of nutrient deficiency or toxicity appear

## 3. Organic Matter and Compost

### 3.1 The Organic Matter Paradox

PNW forest soils are naturally high in organic matter — undisturbed forest floor typically contains 8–15% organic matter in the top 6 inches [GOV-04]. However, residential garden soils in the PNW are frequently depleted to 2–4% organic matter due to construction disturbance, topsoil removal during grading, and years of bare-soil cultivation [EXT-04].

Restoring organic matter is the single most impactful improvement for most PNW garden soils [EXT-04, PRO-01]. Organic matter:
- Improves water-holding capacity in sandy soils (each 1% increase in organic matter helps soil hold approximately 20,000 additional gallons of water per acre) [EXT-04]
- Improves drainage and aeration in clay soils by promoting aggregate formation
- Feeds soil biology — bacteria, fungi, earthworms, beneficial nematodes
- Buffers soil pH, reducing the severity of acidity swings
- Provides slow-release nutrients as it decomposes
- Reduces soil temperature fluctuations (insulation effect)

### 3.2 Compost Types and Their Uses

**Yard waste compost [EXT-04, PRO-01]:**
Municipal or commercial compost produced from yard debris (leaves, grass clippings, woody material). Widely available in the PNW — Cedar Grove (Seattle area), Rexius (Eugene area), and municipal programs throughout the region produce high-quality compost. Typical analysis: 1–2% nitrogen, 0.5–1% phosphorus, 0.5–1% potassium [EXT-04]. Best for general soil building.

**Leaf mold [PRO-01]:**
Partially decomposed leaves, typically aged 1–2 years. Lower in nutrients than compost but excellent for soil structure improvement. PNW gardeners can produce leaf mold easily — bigleaf maple leaves are abundant and decompose in 12–18 months when piled and kept moist [PRO-01]. Ideal as mulch or soil conditioner for woodland plantings.

**Composted manure [EXT-04]:**
Must be fully composted (aged 6–12 months minimum) to reduce pathogen risk and eliminate weed seeds. Horse manure is the most commonly available in the PNW but may contain persistent herbicides (aminopyralid, clopyralid) that survive composting and damage garden plants — particularly tomatoes, beans, and peas [EXT-04, EXT-05]. Test by growing beans in a small pot of the compost before applying to garden beds.

**Worm castings (vermicompost) [PRO-01]:**
Produced by red wiggler worms (Eisenia fetida). Highly concentrated — typical analysis: 2–3% nitrogen, 1–2% phosphorus, 1–2% potassium, plus humic acids and beneficial microorganisms [PRO-01]. Excellent for seed starting mixes, container gardening, and as a top-dress for high-value crops. Too concentrated and expensive for bulk soil amendment. Apply at 10–20% of potting mix volume for containers, or a thin top-dress (0.25–0.5 inch) around established plants [PRO-01].

**Composted bark and wood fiber [EXT-04]:**
Partially decomposed bark, typically from Douglas fir or hemlock. Lower in nutrients than other composts. Used primarily as a soil conditioner to improve drainage in clay soils. Fine bark compost (less than 0.5 inch particles) can be incorporated into soil; coarser material is better used as surface mulch [EXT-04].

### 3.3 Application Rates

**For depleted soils (new garden, post-construction) [EXT-04]:**
- Apply 2–3 inches of compost over the soil surface annually for the first 3–5 years
- Incorporate into the top 6–8 inches if tilling (or leave on surface if using no-till methods)
- This is equivalent to approximately 6–9 cubic yards per 1,000 square feet [EXT-04]

**For maintenance (established garden beds) [EXT-04, PRO-01]:**
- Apply 1 inch of compost annually, typically in early spring or fall
- This is equivalent to approximately 3 cubic yards per 1,000 square feet [EXT-04]
- Side-dress heavy-feeding crops (tomatoes, squash, corn) with an additional 1–2 inches mid-season

**For raised beds [PRO-07]:**
- Initial fill: 1/3 compost, 1/3 topsoil, 1/3 coarse material (perlite, pumice, or coarse bark)
- Annual top-up: 1–2 inches of compost as beds settle (raised beds lose 2–3 inches of volume annually through decomposition) [PRO-07]
- After 3–5 years, test soil nutrients before adding more compost — established raised beds can accumulate excess phosphorus [PRO-03]

### 3.4 When NOT to Add More Compost

Compost is not universally beneficial. Stop adding compost when [PRO-03, EXT-01]:

1. **Soil test shows phosphorus above 75 ppm.** Excess phosphorus is an environmental pollutant and interferes with micronutrient uptake [EXT-01].
2. **Soil organic matter exceeds 10%.** Additional organic matter may cause nitrogen tie-up as it decomposes, or may create excessively water-retentive conditions [EXT-04].
3. **Plants show lush foliage but poor fruiting.** This indicates nitrogen excess — switch to lower-nitrogen amendments or mulch only [PRO-03].
4. **Soil is "fluffy" and won't hold transplants upright.** Over-amended soil lacks mineral structure. Add coarse sand or pumice to restore structural integrity [EXT-04].

### 3.5 Cedar and Compost Tea Considerations

**Cedar concerns [PRO-01]:** Fresh western red cedar is allelopathic — it contains thujaplicins and other compounds that inhibit the growth of some plants. However, composted or aged cedar (bark mulch, aged chips) has reduced allelopathic effect and is an excellent, rot-resistant mulch for PNW conditions [PRO-01]. Wait 6–12 months after chipping before using fresh cedar as mulch around vegetable gardens. Cedar is safe around established trees, shrubs, and perennials at any stage [PRO-01].

**Compost tea [PRO-03]:** Aerated compost tea (compost steeped in water with an air pump for 24–48 hours) is popular among PNW gardeners as a foliar spray and soil drench. Research results are mixed [EXT-04]. Extension services generally note that while compost tea may provide some beneficial microorganisms, the evidence for disease suppression or yield improvement is inconsistent [EXT-04]. If using compost tea, apply it within 4 hours of brewing and never apply to food crops within 120 days of harvest if using manure-based compost (food safety) [EXT-04].

## 4. No-Till and Sheet Mulching

### 4.1 The Case for No-Till in the PNW

The maritime PNW is uniquely suited to no-till gardening methods. The natural ecosystem — temperate rainforest — builds soil through surface deposition (leaf fall, needle drop, woody debris) rather than through disturbance [PRO-01, PRO-03]. No-till gardening mimics this process.

**Benefits of no-till [PRO-01, PRO-03]:**
- Preserves soil aggregate structure, which is critical for drainage in clay soils
- Maintains mycorrhizal fungal networks (see Section 5) that connect plant roots and facilitate nutrient exchange
- Reduces erosion — particularly important during PNW winter rainstorms
- Retains soil moisture by maintaining organic mulch layer
- Suppresses weed germination by leaving weed seeds buried rather than bringing them to the surface
- Reduces labor — no annual tilling, double-digging, or heavy soil turning

**Shifting to no-till from conventional beds [PRO-01]:**

1. **Stop tilling.** This is the foundational step. If soil is compacted, address compaction through surface amendment rather than tillage.
2. **Maintain permanent mulch.** Keep 2–4 inches of organic mulch (wood chips, leaf mold, straw) on all beds at all times [PRO-01].
3. **Use a broadfork for compaction relief.** A broadfork loosens soil without inverting layers — preserving soil biology while relieving compaction [PRO-01].
4. **Plant through the mulch.** Pull mulch aside for direct seeding; transplant directly through mulch layer for starts.
5. **Add compost on top, not incorporated.** Surface-applied compost feeds soil biology and is drawn down by earthworms and decomposition [PRO-03].

### 4.2 Sheet Mulching: Lawn to Garden Without Tilling

Sheet mulching converts grass, weeds, or bare ground into a plantable garden bed through layered organic materials. It is the standard no-till bed-creation method in the PNW [PRO-01, PRO-03].

**Method [PRO-01]:**

1. **Mow existing vegetation** as short as possible. Leave clippings in place.
2. **Apply a weed barrier layer.** Use unwaxed, unprinted cardboard or 6–8 sheets of newspaper. Overlap pieces by 6 inches to prevent gaps [PRO-01]. Remove tape and staples from cardboard.
3. **Wet the cardboard layer thoroughly.** It should be saturated — this initiates decomposition and helps it conform to the soil surface [PRO-01].
4. **Add 3–4 inches of compost** on top of the cardboard [PRO-01].
5. **Add 2–4 inches of coarse mulch** (wood chips, arborist chips, or bark) on top of the compost [PRO-01].
6. **Wait 2–4 months** before planting through the layers (fall sheet-mulching for spring planting is ideal in the PNW) [PRO-01].

**For immediate planting:** Cut X-shaped holes through the cardboard, fill with compost, and transplant starts directly. This works well for transplants but not for direct-seeded crops [PRO-01].

**PNW timing [PRO-01]:**
- Best: Sheet-mulch in September–October. Winter rains break down cardboard, and beds are ready for spring planting.
- Acceptable: Sheet-mulch in early spring (March). Allow 6–8 weeks before planting — cardboard takes longer to break down in cooler spring conditions.
- Avoid: Sheet-mulching in summer — materials dry out and decomposition stalls without irrigation.

### 4.3 Horticultural Coir as a Peat Alternative

In the PNW gardening context, horticultural coir (coconut fiber) is increasingly recommended over peat moss for soil amendment and seed-starting mixes [PRO-03].

**Advantages of coir [PRO-03]:**
- Contains naturally occurring root-stimulating hormones that promote root development
- Provides modest nutrient levels (particularly potassium)
- pH-neutral (6.0–6.8), unlike peat moss which is acidic (pH 3.5–4.5)
- Renewable resource with lower environmental footprint than peat harvesting
- Holds 8–9 times its weight in water while maintaining air porosity [PRO-03]

**Use cases [PRO-03]:**
- Seed-starting mix: 1/3 coir, 1/3 perlite, 1/3 compost or worm castings
- Container gardening: Replace peat moss 1:1 with coir in any recipe
- Soil amendment for clay soils: Incorporate 2–3 inches of coir to improve drainage and aeration
- Mulch for seed beds: A thin layer (0.5 inch) of coir retains moisture for germinating seeds

**Consideration:** Rinse coir bricks thoroughly before use to remove excess salts from oceanic processing [PRO-03].

### 4.4 Cover Crops as No-Till Partners

Cover crops (discussed in detail in Section 8) are natural companions to no-till gardening. Rather than leaving soil bare over winter — which invites erosion from PNW rains — cover crops protect the soil surface, fix nitrogen (legumes), and add organic matter when terminated [PRO-01]. In a no-till system, cover crops are cut at the base and left as mulch rather than tilled in (see Section 8 for protocols) [PRO-01].

## 5. Soil Biology — The Underground Ecosystem

### 5.1 The Living Soil

A single teaspoon of healthy PNW garden soil contains approximately 1 billion bacteria, several yards of fungal hyphae, thousands of protozoa, and dozens of nematodes [EXT-04]. This biological community is not incidental — it is the engine that drives nutrient cycling, disease suppression, and soil structure formation.

The maritime PNW, with its mild temperatures, consistent moisture, and abundant organic matter, supports exceptionally rich soil biology compared to drier or colder regions [GOV-04]. Understanding and supporting this biology is the foundation of sustainable PNW gardening.

### 5.2 Mycorrhizal Networks

Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic relationships with plant roots, extending the effective root system by orders of magnitude [EXT-04]. There are two primary types relevant to PNW gardeners:

**Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM):** Penetrate root cells, forming branched structures (arbuscules) for nutrient exchange. Associated with most vegetable crops, grasses, and herbaceous perennials. These fungi extend fungal hyphae into soil micropores too small for roots to penetrate, accessing phosphorus, zinc, and copper that would otherwise be unavailable [EXT-04].

**Ectomycorrhizae (ECM):** Form sheaths around root tips without penetrating cells. Associated with PNW native trees — Douglas fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, oaks, willows, and alders [GOV-04]. These are the fungi that produce the familiar mushrooms of PNW forests — chanterelles, matsutake, king boletes. A single mature Douglas fir may be connected to dozens of fungal species through its root system [GOV-04].

**The "wood wide web" [GOV-04]:** Research in PNW forests has demonstrated that mycorrhizal networks connect trees of different species and ages, allowing transfer of carbon, water, and nutrients between individuals. While the garden-scale implications are less dramatic, maintaining fungal networks supports overall plant health and resilience.

**Supporting mycorrhizal fungi in the garden [EXT-04, PRO-03]:**
- Minimize soil disturbance — tilling severs fungal hyphae networks
- Maintain living roots in soil year-round (cover crops, perennial plantings)
- Avoid excessive phosphorus fertilization — high soil phosphorus suppresses mycorrhizal colonization [EXT-01]
- Reduce fungicide use — fungicides can kill beneficial soil fungi
- Use mulch — fungal communities thrive under organic mulch layers
- Note: Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, kale) do not form mycorrhizal associations; they have evolved alternative nutrient-acquisition strategies [EXT-04]

### 5.3 Beneficial Bacteria

Soil bacteria drive nitrogen cycling through two critical processes [EXT-04]:

**Nitrogen fixation:** Rhizobium bacteria, living in nodules on legume roots (peas, beans, clover, fava beans, alders), convert atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into plant-available ammonium (NH₄⁺). This biological nitrogen fixation can contribute 50–200 lb of nitrogen per acre per year in productive legume stands [EXT-01]. In PNW gardens, fall-planted fava beans and crimson clover are the most effective nitrogen-fixing cover crops.

**Decomposition and nutrient mineralization:** Bacterial communities break down organic matter, releasing bound nutrients into plant-available forms. This process is temperature-dependent — bacterial activity in PNW soils slows significantly below 50°F (10°C) soil temperature and is minimal below 40°F (4°C) [EXT-04]. This is why spring nutrient release is delayed in the PNW compared to warmer regions, and why early-season crops often benefit from supplemental fertilization even in compost-rich soils.

**Plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) [EXT-04]:** Certain soil bacteria produce compounds that stimulate root growth, suppress pathogenic organisms, and solubilize bound nutrients. These beneficial bacteria are supported by the same practices that support fungi: organic matter, minimal disturbance, and living roots.

### 5.4 Earthworms, Nematodes, and Soil Fauna

**Earthworms [EXT-04]:** PNW gardens host both native and non-native earthworm species. Earthworms create macropores (channels) that improve drainage and aeration, mix organic surface material into deeper soil layers (bioturbation), and produce castings rich in plant-available nutrients. A healthy PNW garden soil may contain 10–25 earthworms per square foot of surface area in the top 6 inches [EXT-04]. Support earthworm populations by maintaining organic mulch, avoiding tillage, and minimizing pesticide use.

**Beneficial nematodes [EXT-04]:** Not all nematodes are pests. Beneficial nematodes prey on soil-dwelling insect larvae (grubs, root maggots, cutworms) and help regulate populations of pest organisms. Heterorhabditis and Steinernema species are commercially available for targeted pest control in PNW gardens [EXT-05].

**Microarthropods:** Springtails, mites, and other tiny soil-dwelling arthropods shred organic debris into smaller pieces, making it accessible to bacterial and fungal decomposition. They are invisible but essential members of the soil food web [EXT-04].

### 5.5 Why Tilling Disrupts the Ecosystem

Tilling — especially rototilling — disrupts soil biology through multiple mechanisms [PRO-01, PRO-03]:

1. **Physical severing** of fungal hyphae networks. Mycorrhizal recovery takes weeks to months after tilling [EXT-04].
2. **Destruction of soil aggregates.** Aggregates are clusters of soil particles bound together by fungal hyphae and bacterial polysaccharides. They create the pore structure that allows water infiltration and air exchange. Tilling shatters these structures [EXT-04].
3. **Exposure of buried organic matter** to rapid oxidation. Surface-exposed organic matter decomposes much faster, releasing a pulse of CO₂ and depleting long-term soil carbon stores [EXT-04].
4. **Disruption of earthworm burrows** and habitat for soil-dwelling organisms.
5. **Bringing buried weed seeds to the surface,** where they germinate. A single square foot of PNW garden soil may contain thousands of dormant weed seeds — tilling creates a fresh weed flush with every pass [PRO-01].

### 5.6 Supporting Soil Biology

**Practical actions for PNW gardeners [PRO-01, PRO-03, EXT-04]:**

| Practice | Benefit | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| Maintain permanent mulch | Feeds fungal communities, retains moisture, moderates temperature | 2–4 inches of wood chips, bark, or leaf mold year-round |
| Minimize tillage | Preserves fungal networks, aggregates, earthworm habitat | Use broadfork for compaction; sheet-mulch for new beds |
| Grow diverse species | Different root exudates feed different microbial communities | Interplant vegetables with herbs and flowers |
| Keep living roots | Root exudates feed rhizosphere bacteria year-round | Cover crops in winter, perennials as garden anchors |
| Avoid synthetic pesticides | Many pesticides harm non-target soil organisms | Prioritize biological pest control (see M5 cross-ref) |
| Compost as inoculation | Finished compost introduces billions of beneficial microorganisms | Apply 1 inch annually; use as a microbial starter |
| Avoid overwatering | Waterlogged soil becomes anaerobic, killing aerobic organisms | Drainage planning (Section 7); appropriate irrigation |

## 6. Raised Bed Construction and Strategy

### 6.1 Why Raised Beds Excel in the PNW

Raised beds solve the two biggest soil management challenges in maritime PNW gardening: poor drainage and cold soil temperatures [PRO-07].

**Drainage advantage:** A raised bed elevates the root zone above the water table. In a region where 6+ months of rain can saturate in-ground soil, this elevation is the difference between thriving roots and root rot [PRO-07]. Water drains laterally and downward out of the raised bed volume, maintaining the aerobic conditions that roots require.

**Temperature advantage:** Raised beds warm 2–4 weeks earlier than in-ground soil in spring [PRO-07]. In the maritime PNW, where the growing season is defined by soil temperature as much as air temperature, this means earlier planting of warm-season crops like tomatoes, peppers, and squash. Dark-colored raised bed soil exposed to spring sun can reach 60°F (15°C) — the minimum for warm-season planting — while adjacent in-ground soil remains at 50°F (10°C) [PRO-07].

**Additional PNW-specific benefits [PRO-07]:**
- Reduced slug and snail access (copper tape on raised bed walls provides an additional barrier — see M5 cross-ref)
- Cleaner working conditions during wet months
- Defined pathways prevent soil compaction from foot traffic
- Easier to cover with row covers or cold frames for season extension (cross-ref M1)
- Better root development for shallow-rooted crops in areas with hardpan

### 6.2 Construction: Materials and Dimensions

**Recommended material: Untreated western red cedar [PRO-07]**

Western red cedar is the gold standard for PNW raised beds. Naturally rot-resistant due to thujaplicins (aromatic compounds in the heartwood), untreated cedar lasts 8–15 years in PNW conditions depending on soil contact and moisture exposure [PRO-07]. Heartwood (the darker inner wood) lasts significantly longer than sapwood (the lighter outer wood).

**Material comparison [PRO-07]:**

| Material | Lifespan in PNW | Food Safe? | Cost (relative) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Western red cedar (heartwood) | 10–15 years | Yes | Medium-high | Best overall choice [PRO-07] |
| Western red cedar (sapwood) | 5–8 years | Yes | Medium | Adequate but shorter lived |
| Douglas fir (untreated) | 3–5 years | Yes | Low | Budget option; replace frequently |
| Galvanized steel | 20+ years | Yes | High | Heats up in sun; insulate interior |
| Concrete blocks | 30+ years | Yes | Medium | Alkaline leaching affects pH initially |
| Composite lumber | 15–20 years | Varies | High | Check for chemical leaching |
| Pressure-treated lumber | 15–20 years | **No** | Medium | Avoid near food crops — contains copper compounds [PRO-07] |
| Railroad ties | 15–20 years | **No** | Low | Contain creosote — never use for food gardens [PRO-07] |

**Dimensions [PRO-07]:**

- **Width:** 4 feet maximum. This allows reaching the center from either side without stepping into the bed (stepping in compacts soil, negating the primary raised bed advantage) [PRO-07]. For beds against a wall or fence, 2–3 feet maximum.
- **Length:** Any practical length. 8–12 feet is common for residential gardens.
- **Depth:** 10–12 inches minimum for annual vegetables [PRO-07]. 18–24 inches for root crops (carrots, parsnips) or where native soil is especially poor or hardpan is present. Deeper beds require more soil fill but provide better root development and drainage.
- **Path width:** 18–24 inches between beds for walking; 36 inches minimum for wheelbarrow access [PRO-07].

**Construction tips [PRO-07]:**
- Use 2-inch-thick lumber (nominal) for structural integrity — 1-inch boards bow under soil pressure
- Corner brackets or 4×4 corner posts provide rigidity for beds longer than 6 feet
- Level the bed frame before filling — unlevel beds cause uneven watering
- No bottom is needed — open bottom allows root penetration into native soil and provides drainage
- Line interior walls with landscape fabric if you want to slow wood decomposition (optional — this is cosmetic, not structural)

### 6.3 Raised Bed Soil Mix

**Standard raised bed mix (the "thirds" formula) [PRO-07]:**

| Component | Proportion | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Quality compost | 1/3 (by volume) | Nutrients, biology, water retention |
| Topsoil | 1/3 | Mineral structure, weight, stability |
| Coarse material (pumice, perlite, or coarse bark) | 1/3 | Drainage, aeration, prevents compaction |

**Volume calculation [PRO-07]:**
A 4×8-foot bed, 12 inches deep, requires approximately 1 cubic yard (27 cubic feet) of soil mix.

| Bed Size (L×W×D) | Volume Needed |
|---|---|
| 4×4×1 ft | 0.6 cubic yards |
| 4×8×1 ft | 1.0 cubic yards |
| 4×12×1 ft | 1.5 cubic yards |
| 4×8×1.5 ft | 1.5 cubic yards |

**Avoid [PRO-07]:** Filling beds with pure compost (settles excessively, nutrient overload, poor structure). Filling with native soil only (negates raised bed benefits). Using bagged potting mix exclusively (too lightweight, dries out, expensive at volume).

### 6.4 In-Ground vs. Raised Bed Decision Matrix

| Factor | In-Ground Better | Raised Bed Better |
|---|---|---|
| **Soil type** | Sandy loam, good drainage | Heavy clay, hardpan, poor drainage [PRO-07] |
| **Budget** | Lower cost (amendment only) | Higher upfront cost (materials + fill) |
| **Space** | Large garden, row cropping | Small or medium gardens, intensive planting |
| **Accessibility** | Able-bodied gardeners | Mobility limitations, back problems (18"+ height) |
| **Perennials** | Fruit trees, large shrubs, asparagus | Annual vegetables, herbs, strawberries |
| **Season extension** | Less effective | Easier to add covers, cold frames [PRO-07] |
| **Drainage** | Already good drainage | Poor drainage or high water table |
| **Warming** | Adequate growing season | Need earlier/longer season [PRO-07] |
| **Aesthetics** | Naturalistic, cottage garden | Formal, organized, defined beds |

## 7. Drainage Planning

### 7.1 The PNW Drainage Challenge

The maritime Pacific Northwest receives 35–60+ inches of precipitation annually, with the vast majority falling between October and April [GOV-04]. In the wettest areas — the Olympic Peninsula, coastal zones, and Cascade foothills — annual rainfall exceeds 80 inches [GOV-04]. This volume of water, combined with the clay and till soils common in the region, creates winter waterlogging as the single most destructive soil condition for gardens.

**Consequences of poor drainage [EXT-04, PRO-07]:**
- Root rot in perennials and fruit trees (Phytophthora, Pythium, and Armillaria species thrive in waterlogged conditions) [EXT-05]
- Soil compaction from saturated conditions (water fills pore spaces, destroying soil structure under any weight)
- Nutrient leaching as excess water carries dissolved nutrients below the root zone [EXT-01]
- Anaerobic conditions that kill beneficial soil organisms and produce toxic compounds (hydrogen sulfide, ethylene) [EXT-04]
- Frozen waterlogged soil heaves shallow-rooted plants out of the ground during cold snaps [EXT-04]

### 7.2 Assessing Drainage

**Percolation test [EXT-04]:**
1. Dig a hole 12 inches wide and 12 inches deep
2. Fill with water and let it drain completely (this saturates surrounding soil)
3. Refill immediately and time how long it takes to drain
4. **Well-drained:** Drains in 1–4 hours (1–6 inches per hour) [EXT-04]
5. **Moderately drained:** Drains in 4–8 hours
6. **Poorly drained:** Takes more than 8 hours — intervention needed
7. **Very poorly drained:** Standing water after 24 hours — serious drainage work required

Perform this test in winter (November–February) when soils are at maximum saturation. A summer percolation test may give falsely optimistic results [EXT-04].

### 7.3 Drainage Solutions

**French drains [EXT-04]:**
A perforated pipe laid in a gravel-filled trench that collects and redirects subsurface water.

- Trench depth: 18–24 inches [EXT-04]
- Trench width: 12–18 inches
- Pipe: 4-inch perforated PVC or corrugated drain pipe, holes facing down
- Bedding: 2–3 inches of washed gravel (0.75–1.5 inch diameter) below and above pipe
- Grade: Minimum 1% slope (1 inch per 8 feet) toward outlet [EXT-04]
- Wrap pipe and gravel in landscape fabric to prevent soil intrusion
- Outlet: Daylight to lower ground, dry well, or approved storm drain connection

**Gravel beds [PRO-07]:**
For persistently wet areas where raised beds sit on saturated ground:
- Excavate 6–8 inches below the planned bed location
- Fill with washed gravel or crushed rock
- Place raised bed on top of gravel pad
- Water drains through bed, through gravel, and disperses laterally

**Crown planting for fruit trees [EXT-04, PRO-05]:**
In heavy clay or poorly drained soils, planting fruit trees on raised mounds (crowns) prevents crown rot — the number one killer of fruit trees in the PNW [PRO-05].

- Build a mound 12–18 inches high and 4–6 feet in diameter [PRO-05]
- Use amended soil: native soil mixed with 25–30% compost and coarse material
- Plant tree at the top of the mound with the graft union 2–4 inches above the mound surface
- Mulch the mound but keep mulch 6 inches away from the trunk [PRO-05]
- The mound will settle over time — add soil to maintain height as needed

**Swales and berms [EXT-04]:**
Landscape-scale water management features that slow, spread, and sink rainwater.

- **Swale:** A shallow, vegetated channel dug on contour (following a line of equal elevation). Water flows into the swale, slows down, and soaks into the ground.
- **Berm:** A raised ridge of soil on the downhill side of a swale. Often planted with deep-rooted perennials or trees that benefit from the extra moisture.
- Swale depth: 6–12 inches [EXT-04]
- Berm height: 12–18 inches above surrounding grade
- Plant swale bottoms with water-tolerant species: sedges, rushes, native ferns, red-twig dogwood

**Rain gardens [PRO-03]:**
A designed depression planted with water-tolerant native plants that captures and filters stormwater runoff from roofs, driveways, and paths (cross-ref M3 for native plant selection).

- Size: 10–30% of the impervious surface area draining into it [PRO-03]
- Depth: 6–12 inches below surrounding grade
- Soil: Sandy loam or engineered rain garden mix (60% sand, 20% compost, 20% topsoil)
- Plants: Native species adapted to seasonal wet-dry cycles — Oregon iris (Iris tenax), red-twig dogwood (Cornus sericea), sedges (Carex species), Pacific ninebark (Physocarpus capitatus) [PRO-03]
- Location: At least 10 feet from building foundations; downhill from impervious surfaces

### 7.4 Drainage Planning Principles

**Rule of 1% [EXT-04]:** All drainage features should slope at minimum 1% (1 foot of drop per 100 feet of horizontal distance). This is barely perceptible by eye but sufficient to move water.

**Don't redirect your neighbor's problem:** PNW drainage solutions should infiltrate water on-site whenever possible rather than directing it onto adjacent properties. Rain gardens, swales, and dry wells are preferred over surface drainage that crosses property lines [PRO-03].

**Downspout disconnection:** Redirecting roof downspouts from storm drains into garden rain gardens or infiltration areas is encouraged by many PNW municipalities (Portland, Seattle, Vancouver BC) and may qualify for utility credits [PRO-03].

## 8. Cover Cropping for the PNW

### 8.1 Why Cover Crops Matter in the PNW

The maritime PNW has a long, mild, wet winter — October through March — during which garden beds are typically fallow. Leaving soil bare during this period exposes it to [PRO-01]:

- **Erosion:** Rain impact dislodges soil particles; runoff carries them away. A bare-soil garden can lose 2–5 tons of topsoil per acre per year to erosion [EXT-04].
- **Nutrient leaching:** Winter rains carry dissolved nitrogen, potassium, and other nutrients below the root zone [EXT-01].
- **Weed colonization:** Bare soil invites winter annual weeds (chickweed, bittercress, annual bluegrass) that establish while gardens are dormant [PRO-01].
- **Soil structure degradation:** Rain compacts bare soil; freeze-thaw cycles pulverize surface structure [EXT-04].

Cover crops prevent all four problems while actively improving soil for the following season [PRO-01].

### 8.2 PNW Cover Crop Selection

**Nitrogen-fixing legumes [PRO-01, EXT-01]:**

| Cover Crop | Seeding Rate (lb/1000 sq ft) | Planting Window | N Contribution (lb/acre) | Winter Hardiness | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Crimson clover | 1.0–1.5 | Sept 1–Oct 15 | 70–130 | Hardy to 10°F (-12°C) | Beautiful spring bloom; bee forage [PRO-01] |
| Fava beans | 4.0–6.0 | Sept 1–Oct 15 | 100–200 | Hardy to 15°F (-9°C) | Tallest cover; edible if desired [PRO-01] |
| Austrian winter peas | 2.0–4.0 | Sept 1–Oct 15 | 80–150 | Hardy to 10°F (-12°C) | Vigorous; good weed suppressor [PRO-01] |
| White clover | 0.5–1.0 | Mar–Sept | 75–150 | Very hardy | Living mulch; perennial option [PRO-01] |
| Red clover | 0.5–1.0 | Mar–Oct | 75–175 | Hardy to 0°F (-18°C) | Biennial; deep taproot [EXT-01] |

**Biomass/grain cover crops [PRO-01, EXT-01]:**

| Cover Crop | Seeding Rate (lb/1000 sq ft) | Planting Window | Primary Benefit | Winter Hardiness | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Winter rye (cereal rye) | 2.0–3.0 | Sept 1–Nov 1 | Massive biomass, weed suppression | Very hardy | Latest planting window; allelopathic — wait 2 weeks after termination to plant [PRO-01] |
| Annual ryegrass | 1.0–2.0 | Sept 1–Oct 15 | Quick establishment, erosion control | Hardy to 0°F (-18°C) | Can become weedy if allowed to seed [PRO-01] |
| Oats | 2.0–3.0 | Sept 1–Oct 1 | Fast biomass | Winter-killed below 20°F (-7°C) | "Self-terminating" in colder PNW areas [EXT-01] |
| Buckwheat | 2.0–3.0 | May–Aug (summer) | Pollinator support, phosphorus scavenging | Frost-killed | Summer cover crop only [PRO-01] |
| Phacelia | 0.5–1.0 | Mar–Sept | Pollinator magnet, quick cover | Frost-killed | Excellent bee plant; not related to any crop family [PRO-01] |

**Recommended PNW mixes [PRO-01]:**
- **Standard winter mix:** Crimson clover (60%) + winter rye (40%) — nitrogen fixation + biomass
- **Heavy feeder prep:** Fava beans (70%) + oats (30%) — maximum nitrogen for following tomatoes, squash, corn
- **Weed-smothering mix:** Winter rye (80%) + Austrian winter peas (20%) — dense canopy eliminates weeds
- **Pollinator mix (summer):** Buckwheat (50%) + phacelia (50%) — between spring and fall crops

### 8.3 PNW-Specific Timing

**Critical date: Plant fall cover crops by October 1 in the Puget Sound lowlands [PRO-01].** In the Willamette Valley, the window extends to October 15. In coastal areas with milder winters, planting as late as November 1 is possible for rye and fava beans [PRO-01].

Cover crops need 4–6 weeks of growth before the first hard frost (typically late November in the lowlands) to establish enough root and top growth to provide winter protection [PRO-01]. Planting after October 15 in most areas results in poor establishment and minimal benefit.

**Spring termination timing [PRO-01]:**
- Cut or mow cover crops 2–4 weeks before intended spring planting date
- For no-till: Cut at base, leave as mulch, plant through residue
- For incorporation: Mow, then lightly work into top 2–4 inches of soil
- **Wait period after rye:** Cereal rye releases allelopathic compounds that inhibit seed germination. Wait a minimum of 2 weeks after cutting/incorporation before direct-seeding small-seeded crops (lettuce, carrots, beets) [PRO-01]. Transplants are less affected.

### 8.4 Cover Crops as Living Mulch

In a summer garden, low-growing cover crops can be used as living mulch between crop rows [PRO-01]:

- **White clover** seeded between rows of tomatoes, peppers, or squash after crops are established (June). Fixes nitrogen, suppresses weeds, retains moisture. Mow or trim to prevent competition.
- **Dutch white clover** as permanent pathway cover between raised beds — tolerates foot traffic, fixes nitrogen, and eliminates muddy paths during PNW winters [PRO-01].

## 9. Soil Testing Protocols

### 9.1 What to Test

A comprehensive PNW soil test should include [EXT-01, EXT-04]:

| Parameter | Why It Matters in the PNW | Ideal Range |
|---|---|---|
| **pH** | Most PNW soils are acidic; determines nutrient availability | 6.0–6.8 for most vegetables [EXT-01] |
| **Organic matter (%)** | Indicator of soil health and biology | 5–8% for garden soils [EXT-04] |
| **Nitrogen (N)** | Often adequate in compost-amended soils; mobile and variable | Context-dependent; test as nitrate-N |
| **Phosphorus (P)** | Commonly over-applied; environmental concern | 25–75 ppm Bray-1 [EXT-01] |
| **Potassium (K)** | Leached by PNW rains; often deficient | 150–250 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **Calcium (Ca)** | Leached by rain; linked to pH | 1,000–3,000 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **Magnesium (Mg)** | Commonly deficient in leached PNW soils | 100–300 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **Sulfur (S)** | Generally adequate in PNW (from organic matter and rainfall) | 10–30 ppm |
| **Boron (B)** | Deficiency causes hollow stem in broccoli, brown heart in beets | 0.5–2.0 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **Zinc (Zn)** | Can be locked out by excess phosphorus | 1–5 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **Manganese (Mn)** | Availability drops above pH 6.5 | 5–50 ppm [EXT-01] |
| **CEC** | Cation exchange capacity — soil's ability to hold nutrients | Higher is better; clay > loam > sand |
| **Soil texture** | Sand/silt/clay proportions; determines management approach | Lab measures; or use jar test (Section 1.3) |

### 9.3 How to Take a Representative Soil Sample

A soil test is only as good as the sample [EXT-01, EXT-04]. Poor sampling technique produces misleading results.

**Protocol [EXT-01]:**

1. **Timing:** Sample in spring (March–April) or fall (September–October) when soil is moist but not saturated. Avoid sampling within 6 weeks of adding lime, fertilizer, or compost.

2. **Tools:** Clean trowel or soil probe, clean plastic bucket, quart-size ziplock bag.

3. **Sampling depth:** 6–8 inches for garden beds and lawns [EXT-01].

4. **Composite sampling:** Take 8–12 subsamples from across the area being tested. Walk a zigzag pattern through the garden bed, taking a trowel-depth core at each point [EXT-01]. Combine all subsamples in the bucket and mix thoroughly. This composite accounts for natural soil variability.

5. **Separate zones:** Submit separate samples for areas that are clearly different — a clay bed vs. a sandy bed, a limed area vs. an unlimed area, an old garden vs. a new garden [EXT-01]. Do not combine samples from these distinct zones.

6. **Avoid contamination:** Do not sample within 6 inches of fence posts (treated wood), concrete foundations (alkaline leaching), compost piles (concentrated nutrients), or areas where fertilizer was recently spilled [EXT-01].

7. **Submission:** Fill a quart-size bag with 1–2 cups of the mixed composite. Label clearly. Air-dry before mailing if the lab requires it (check their instructions). Include information about what you plan to grow — labs calibrate recommendations to crop needs.

### 9.4 Interpreting Results for PNW Conditions

PNW soil test interpretation differs from national averages because of our specific soil chemistry [EXT-01]:

**pH:** National recommendations often target pH 7.0 (neutral). For PNW, pH 6.0–6.5 is the practical target for most vegetables — the naturally acidic conditions mean pH 7.0 is difficult to maintain and often unnecessary [EXT-01].

**Phosphorus:** If your test shows phosphorus above 75 ppm, do not add phosphorus-containing amendments (bone meal, rock phosphate, triple superphosphate, or high-phosphorus compost) [EXT-01]. Many long-amended PNW gardens have excessive phosphorus. Choose fertilizers with low or zero phosphorus content.

**Potassium:** Commonly deficient in heavily leached PNW soils. Supplement with kelp meal (1–2 lb per 100 sq ft), greensand, or potassium sulfate (Sul-Po-Mag) at rates indicated by soil test [EXT-01].

**Calcium and magnesium:** Both are leached by PNW rainfall. If both are low, use dolomitic lime (provides both Ca and Mg while raising pH) [EXT-01]. If only calcium is low, use calcitic lime or gypsum (gypsum provides calcium without changing pH) [EXT-01].

**Boron:** PNW soils in the Puget Sound region are frequently boron-deficient [EXT-01]. Symptoms include hollow-stem broccoli, poor fruit set, and distorted new growth. Apply agricultural borax at 1 tablespoon per 100 sq ft — caution: boron toxicity occurs at only slightly above optimal levels, so do not exceed recommended rates [EXT-01].

### 9.5 Testing Frequency

| Scenario | Recommended Frequency | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Established garden, stable management | Every 2–3 years [EXT-01] | Monitoring trends, adjusting amendments |
| New garden, unknown soil history | Annually for first 3 years | Establishing baseline and tracking improvement |
| Active pH correction (liming program) | Annually until target reached [EXT-01] | Confirming lime is working; adjusting rates |
| High-value specialty crops (blueberries) | Annually | pH critical; small deviations significant |
| Suspected deficiency or toxicity | Immediately | Diagnosis before treatment |
| After significant amendment change | 6 months later | Confirming new amendment program effect |

### 10.3 Special Amendment Situations

**Converting a construction site to a garden [EXT-04]:**
New construction often strips topsoil, compacts subsoil with machinery, and mixes clay subsoil with fill material. Recovery protocol:
1. Test soil — it may be subsoil with minimal organic matter and skewed pH
2. Break compaction with broadfork or, for severe compaction, a single rototilling pass (this is the one acceptable use of tilling — initial compaction relief) [EXT-04]
3. Apply 4–6 inches of quality compost and incorporate
4. Add lime per soil test recommendations
5. Plant a cover crop for the first season to rebuild biology
6. Add 2–3 inches of mulch
7. Begin regular gardening the following season

**Inherited gardens with unknown history [EXT-01]:**
1. Soil test immediately — previous owners may have over-applied chemicals, herbicides, or heavy metals
2. Test for lead if the property was built before 1978 (lead paint) or is near a busy road (leaded gasoline legacy) [EXT-01]
3. If lead exceeds 400 ppm, use raised beds with imported soil for food production [EXT-01]
4. Check for persistent herbicide contamination (aminopyralid, clopyralid) by growing a bioassay of beans or tomatoes in a sample of the soil [EXT-05]

**Restoring forest soil for gardening [GOV-04]:**
Many PNW gardens are carved from conifer forest. The native soil may be:
- Extremely acidic (pH 4.5–5.0): Requires significant liming [EXT-01]
- High in organic matter but low in available nutrients: Forest organic matter is tannin-rich and slow to release nutrients [GOV-04]
- Dominated by fungal biology (ectomycorrhizal): Good for trees and shrubs, needs bacterial inoculation (compost) for annual vegetables [EXT-04]
- Rooty: Old root systems take years to decompose. Sheet mulching is often the best approach to establish beds over old root zones [PRO-01]

## Cross-Module Connections

### Link to M1 (Climate Zones & Season Planning)
Soil temperature drives planting timing more than air temperature in the maritime PNW. A soil thermometer at 4-inch depth is the most reliable planting guide [PRO-07]. Raised beds warm 2–4 weeks earlier than in-ground soil, extending the effective growing season [PRO-07]. Mulch retains moisture during the dry summer months (July–September), reducing irrigation needs.

### Link to M3 (Native Plants & Ecological Gardening)
Native PNW plants are adapted to the region's acidic, organic-rich soils and do not require liming or amendment [PRO-03]. Rain gardens planted with native species (Oregon iris, sedges, red-twig dogwood) manage excess water while providing habitat. Mycorrhizal networks connecting native trees and garden plants support overall ecosystem health [GOV-04].

### Link to M4 (Food Production Calendar)
Soil preparation timing directly determines planting success. Heavy-feeding crops (tomatoes, squash, corn) require pre-season compost application and supplemental nutrition. Crop rotation prevents nutrient depletion and disease buildup in specific soil zones. Cover crops between food-producing seasons maintain soil fertility for the next planting cycle [PRO-01].

### Link to M5 (Pest & Disease Management / Climate Adaptation)
Healthy soil biology is the first line of defense against soilborne diseases [EXT-05]. Adequate drainage prevents root rot (Phytophthora, Pythium) — the most common soilborne disease in PNW gardens [EXT-05]. No-till practices support beneficial soil organisms that suppress pathogenic fungi and bacteria. Proper soil pH ensures plants can access nutrients needed for disease resistance. Cover crops and no-till are climate adaptation strategies: they sequester carbon, reduce erosion, and build resilience to increasingly variable weather patterns [PRO-01, PRO-03].

## Quick Reference Summary

### The Five Pillars of PNW Soil Management

1. **Know your soil.** Test pH and nutrients every 2–3 years. Identify your soil type (clay, sand, silt, or mixed). Understand your drainage characteristics [EXT-01, EXT-04, GOV-04].

2. **Manage acidity.** Most PNW soils need lime for vegetable production (target pH 6.0–6.5). Exceptions: potatoes, blueberries, and native plants prefer acid conditions [EXT-01, PRO-07].

3. **Build organic matter.** Annual compost applications (1–3 inches depending on soil state) restore what construction and cultivation remove. Stop when soil tests show nutrient overload [EXT-04, PRO-03].

4. **Protect the surface.** Permanent mulch, cover crops, and no-till practices prevent erosion, retain moisture, feed soil biology, and suppress weeds [PRO-01, PRO-03].

5. **Solve drainage.** In a region with 35–60+ inches of annual rainfall, drainage is not optional. Raised beds, French drains, crown planting, and rain gardens are essential tools [GOV-04, PRO-07, EXT-04].
