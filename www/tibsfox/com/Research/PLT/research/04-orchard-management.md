## 1. The Late-March Orchard Window

Late March in the PNW orchard is a convergence point. Multiple critical tasks share the same narrow window, and the order in which they are completed matters. Pruning must happen before bud break. Dormant oil must be applied before buds swell. Companion plantings must go in while the soil is workable but before the growing season demands all the gardener's attention on vegetables. Codling moth traps must be deployed before the first generation emerges. Fire blight cankers must be identified and removed before warm, wet weather triggers bacterial ooze [1][2][3][4].

This is the orchard's equivalent of the garden's "spaces between the rain" -- a narrow window where the convergence of weather, phenology, and pest biology creates the optimal moment for action. Miss this window and the consequences compound throughout the growing season: unpruned trees produce less fruit, unsprayed trees harbor more pests, and un-monitored fire blight spreads to healthy wood [1][2][3].

For a PNW orchard property with established apple, pear, and plum trees, the week of March 26 is the final safe week for most of these tasks. Bud break in western Washington lowlands typically occurs in the first two weeks of April for apples, slightly earlier for plums and pears. Everything on this list should be completed before April 10 [1][2][5].

## 2. Dormant Pruning

### Why Prune in Late Winter

Dormant pruning -- cutting while the tree is still leafless and the sap has not begun flowing -- is the safest time to make structural cuts on fruit trees. The benefits of dormant pruning over summer pruning are significant [1][2][3]:

- **Disease prevention:** Open wounds heal fastest in dry, cool weather. Cuts made in warm, wet weather (April-May) are entry points for bacterial and fungal infections, particularly fire blight in apples and pears [1][3].
- **Structural visibility:** Without leaves, the tree's architecture is completely visible. Crossing branches, water sprouts, dead wood, and structural weaknesses are easy to identify [1][2].
- **Energy conservation:** Cuts made before sap flow begins cost the tree minimal stored energy. The tree does not have to replace leaves and growing tips that were just produced [1][2].
- **Wound closure:** Dormant pruning cuts begin healing when the tree breaks dormancy in spring. The growth callus forms quickly over fresh cuts [1].

### Pruning Protocol for Established Trees

**Apple Trees**

1. **Remove dead, diseased, and damaged wood first.** Cut back to healthy wood -- look for green cambium under the bark. Diseased wood (fire blight, canker) should be cut 12 inches below the visible infection margin [1][3].
2. **Remove water sprouts.** These are vigorous vertical shoots growing from horizontal branches. They produce no fruit and crowd the canopy. Cut flush with the parent branch [1][2].
3. **Remove crossing branches.** Where two branches rub against each other, remove the weaker one. Rubbing wounds are infection entry points [1][2].
4. **Thin the interior canopy.** Air circulation through the canopy is the single most effective disease prevention measure. Remove inward-growing branches that crowd the center. Target 30-40% light penetration through the mature canopy [1][2].
5. **Maintain the central leader or open vase shape.** Apple trees trained to a central leader (single dominant trunk) or modified central leader should have the leader's dominance maintained by shortening competing branches. Open-vase trained trees should have the center kept open [1][2].
6. **Do not remove more than 25% of live wood in a single season.** Excessive pruning triggers a stress response (water sprout explosion) that defeats the purpose [1].

**Pear Trees**

Pear pruning follows the same principles as apple pruning with one critical addition: pears are extremely susceptible to fire blight, and every pruning cut is a potential infection site. Disinfect pruning tools between each tree (10% bleach solution or isopropyl alcohol). Make clean, angled cuts that shed water. Do not prune pears in warm, wet weather -- late March dry days are the optimal window [1][3][5].

Pear trees tend toward narrow, upright growth. Spreading the branching angle with limb spreaders (wooden or plastic devices that push branches toward horizontal) increases fruit production and improves light penetration [1][2].

**Plum Trees**

Plums are less susceptible to fire blight than apples and pears but more susceptible to silver leaf disease (Chondrostereum purpureum), a fungal infection that enters through pruning wounds in wet weather. The same late-March dry window used for apple and pear pruning applies to plums [1][2][5].

Japanese plums (Santa Rosa, Methley) tend toward vigorous growth and need more aggressive thinning than European plums (Italian prune). Remove 30-40% of last year's growth to maintain manageable tree size and fruit quality [1][2].

### Tools Required

| Tool | Use | Maintenance |
|------|-----|-------------|
| Hand pruners (bypass) | Branches up to 3/4 inch | Sharpen before use; disinfect between trees |
| Loppers (bypass) | Branches 3/4 to 2 inches | Sharpen; oil pivot bolt |
| Pruning saw (curved) | Branches over 2 inches | Clean teeth; oil blade |
| Pole pruner | High branches without ladder | Check rope/spring mechanism |
| Disinfectant | Between trees and after disease cuts | 10% bleach or 70% isopropyl alcohol |

## 3. Dormant Oil Spray

### Purpose and Timing

Dormant oil (also called horticultural oil) is a refined petroleum or plant-based oil applied to fruit trees while dormant. It smothers overwintering pests -- aphid eggs, mite eggs, and scale insects that hide in bark crevices and around bud scales -- preventing them from emerging to damage new growth [1][2][4].

**Timing is critical:** Apply dormant oil after the coldest winter weather has passed (no hard freezes forecast in the next 48 hours) but before buds begin to swell. Once buds are showing green tissue (the "green tip" stage), oil application risks damaging the emerging leaves and flowers. In western Washington, the dormant oil window typically closes in the first week of April [1][2][4].

### Application Protocol

1. **Check the forecast.** Do not apply if rain is expected within 24 hours (oil needs time to dry and suffocate pests). Do not apply if temperatures will drop below 32F within 48 hours (oil on frozen tissue causes damage) [4].
2. **Mix according to label directions.** Typical dilution is 2-3% oil concentration (2-3 tablespoons per gallon of water). Higher concentrations can damage bark [4].
3. **Spray thoroughly.** Cover all bark surfaces -- trunk, main branches, smaller branches, and around bud clusters. Pests hide in bark crevices, so complete coverage is essential [4].
4. **Spray all fruit trees on the same day.** If some trees are treated and others are not, pests from untreated trees will reinfest treated ones [1][4].
5. **Clean sprayer immediately after use.** Oil residue clogs spray nozzles [4].

### What Dormant Oil Controls

| Pest | Stage Controlled | Severity in PNW |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Green apple aphid | Overwintering eggs | High -- every PNW apple tree |
| European red mite | Overwintering eggs | Moderate -- worse in dry springs |
| San Jose scale | Overwintering crawlers | Moderate -- established orchards |
| Pear psylla | Overwintering adults | High -- major PNW pear pest |
| Tent caterpillar | Overwintering egg masses | Moderate -- cyclical outbreaks |

*Sources: Pacific Northwest Arborist [1]; WSU Extension [4]; Raintree Nursery [5]*

## 4. Codling Moth Trap Deployment

### The Apple's Worst Enemy

Codling moth (Cydia pomonella) is the primary insect pest of apples worldwide and the most significant pest in PNW home orchards. The larva tunnels into the apple core, producing the classic "wormy apple" -- the single most common fruit defect that drives home orchardists to despair [4][6].

### Trap Timing

Pheromone traps should be deployed 2-4 weeks before the first generation of codling moth adults emerges. In western Washington lowlands, first emergence typically occurs in late April to early May, driven by accumulated degree-days (approximately 250 degree-days above a 50F base since January 1). Deploying traps in late March ensures they are in place well before the first flight [4][6].

### Trap Protocol

1. **Purchase codling moth pheromone lures and red sticky traps** from garden supply stores or online (Gemplers, Great Lakes IPM, Peaceful Valley) [4][6].
2. **Hang one trap per tree** (for home orchards with fewer than 10 trees) at eye level on the south or southeast side of the canopy. For larger orchards, one trap per 5 trees is sufficient [4][6].
3. **Check traps weekly** beginning in April. Record the number of moths caught per week. The first moths in the traps signal the beginning of the egg-laying period [4][6].
4. **Action threshold:** When traps catch 5+ moths per week, begin organic control measures -- Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis var. kurstaki) spray timed to egg hatch, or CYD-X (codling moth granulovirus) for organic orchards [4][6].
5. **Replace lures every 4-6 weeks** through September [4].

### Degree-Day Model

The WSU Decision Aid System provides codling moth degree-day calculations by weather station. Home orchardists can use the simplified version:

| Event | Degree-Days (Base 50F) | Typical Date (W. WA) |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|
| First adult emergence | 250 | Late April - Early May |
| First egg laying | 350 | Mid May |
| First egg hatch (spray timing) | 500 | Late May - Early June |
| Peak first generation | 600-800 | June |
| Second generation emergence | 1,250 | Late July - August |

*Source: WSU Extension Tree Fruit [4][6]*

## 5. Fire Blight Inspection

### Why Inspect Now

Fire blight (Erwinia amylovora) is the most destructive bacterial disease of apple and pear trees in the Pacific Northwest. The bacteria overwinter in cankers -- sunken, discolored areas on branches where last year's infections are harbored. In spring, when temperatures rise above 60F and rain or dew provides moisture, the cankers produce bacterial ooze that insects and rain splash carry to open flowers, starting a new cycle of infection [1][3][5].

Late March inspection targets last year's cankers before this year's cycle begins. Removing cankers now prevents the bacterial ooze that drives spring infection [3][5].

### Inspection Protocol

1. **Walk the entire tree.** Inspect every branch from ground level to canopy top. Use a pole pruner with mirror attachment or binoculars for high branches [3].
2. **Identify cankers.** Look for: sunken bark with a sharp boundary between healthy and diseased tissue; dark, discolored bark sometimes with cracking; last year's blighted shoot tips (the characteristic "shepherd's crook" bend at the tip, where the shoot curled over and died) [3][5].
3. **Mark cankers with flagging tape** before cutting. This prevents confusion between diseased and healthy wood during pruning [3].
4. **Cut 12 inches below visible infection.** The bacteria extend well beyond the visible canker margin. Cutting too close to the canker risks leaving infected tissue in the tree [3][5].
5. **Disinfect tools between each cut.** Pruning tools contaminated with fire blight bacteria can inoculate healthy tissue on the next cut. Dip or spray with 10% bleach solution or 70% isopropyl alcohol between cuts [3][5].
6. **Destroy removed wood.** Do not compost fire blight cuttings -- the bacteria survive in compost piles. Burn, bag for landfill disposal, or bury deeply [3][5].

### Fire Blight Susceptibility by Species

| Tree | Susceptibility | PNW Risk Level | Notes |
|------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| Pear (Bartlett) | Very high | Critical | Most susceptible common PNW pear |
| Pear (Anjou) | High | High | Somewhat more tolerant than Bartlett |
| Apple (Gala) | Moderate-high | High | Popular PNW variety with moderate susceptibility |
| Apple (Honeycrisp) | Moderate | Moderate | Newer orchards; moderate resistance |
| Apple (Cosmic Crisp) | Moderate-low | Moderate | WSU breeding included some blight resistance |
| Plum | Low | Low | Rarely affected; inspect but low priority |

*Sources: WSU Extension Plant Pathology [3]; Raintree Nursery [5]; AGR Module 05*

## 6. Fertilization and Soil Care

### Spring Fertilization Timing

Late March to mid-April is the optimal window for fertilizing established fruit trees in western Washington. The trees are about to break dormancy and will immediately begin using the nutrients for new growth, flowers, and fruit set [1][2][5].

### Fertilization Protocol

**Established Trees (3+ years old)**

1. **Soil test first** (if you have not tested in the past 2 years). WSU Extension offers soil testing through county offices. Without a test, use a balanced approach [4].
2. **Broadcast granular fertilizer** in a ring from 12 inches out from the trunk to 12 inches beyond the drip line (the outer edge of the canopy's shadow). Do not pile fertilizer against the trunk -- this promotes crown rot [1][2].
3. **Rate:** 1/10 pound actual nitrogen per year of tree age, up to a maximum of 1 pound per tree. For a 10-year-old apple tree, apply 1 pound actual N. For a balanced organic fertilizer (such as 4-4-4), this means 25 pounds of product per tree [1][2][5].
4. **Organic options:** Composted manure (2-3 inches spread under the canopy), blood meal (high nitrogen), bone meal (phosphorus for root development), kelp meal (micronutrients and potassium). Apply singly or in combination based on soil test results [5].
5. **Do not fertilize trees that were heavily pruned.** Heavy pruning combined with heavy fertilization produces explosive vegetative growth (water sprouts) at the expense of fruit production. If you removed more than 20% of live wood during pruning, reduce fertilizer by 50% this spring [1][2].

**Young Trees (1-3 years old)**

Young trees need less nitrogen and more phosphorus than established trees. Apply a balanced fertilizer with equal NPK ratio at 1/4 the rate of established trees. Focus on root development, not canopy growth [1][2][5].

## 7. Mulch Management

### Spring Mulch Protocol

Mulch around fruit trees serves multiple functions: moisture retention, weed suppression, soil temperature moderation, and organic matter contribution as it decomposes. But improper mulching causes more problems than it solves [1][2][5].

**The Mulch Volcano Problem**

The most common mulching error in PNW orchards is the "mulch volcano" -- piling mulch against the tree trunk in a cone shape. This traps moisture against the bark, promoting crown rot and providing cover for rodents (mice and voles) that girdle the trunk. The correct technique is the "mulch donut" -- a ring of mulch 2-4 inches deep extending from 6 inches out from the trunk to the drip line, with the area immediately around the trunk left bare [1][2][5].

**Spring Mulch Actions:**

1. **Pull existing mulch 6 inches back from trunk** if it has migrated inward over winter [1][5].
2. **Add 2-3 inches of new mulch** (wood chips, arborist chips, or composted bark) if existing layer has decomposed below 2 inches [1][5].
3. **Inspect the trunk base** while mulch is pulled back. Look for rodent damage (girdling -- bark chewed in a ring around the trunk), canker infections, and root suckers. Address any findings before re-mulching [1][5].
4. **Leave bare ground in a 6-inch radius from trunk.** This prevents moisture-induced bark disease and makes rodent activity visible [1][2][5].
5. **Do not use fresh grass clippings as mulch.** They mat into an impermeable layer that blocks water and air. Composted grass is acceptable [5].

## 8. Bare Root Planting Window

### Window Status: Closing

The bare root planting window in western Washington typically runs from February through early April. By late March, most nurseries are selling their last bare root stock, and trees that have not been planted yet are at risk of breaking dormancy in the nursery pot or holding bin [1][5].

### If You Have Unplanted Bare Root Trees

1. **Plant immediately** during the March 27-28 dry window. This is the last optimal planting weather before April's warmer temperatures force bud break [1][5].
2. **Dig the hole wide, not deep.** The hole should be 2-3 times the width of the root spread but only as deep as the root system. The graft union (the bulge where the scion meets the rootstock) must remain 2-4 inches above soil level [1][5].
3. **Soak roots for 1-2 hours** in a bucket of water before planting. Bare root trees lose moisture quickly in air [5].
4. **Backfill with native soil** (do not amend the planting hole). Research consistently shows that trees planted in native soil develop stronger root systems than those planted in amended holes -- the roots must eventually grow into native soil anyway [1][5].
5. **Water deeply after planting** -- 5-10 gallons per tree. Continue weekly deep watering through the first growing season [5].
6. **Stake only if necessary.** Trees that can stand without staking develop stronger trunks faster. If staking is needed (windy sites, dwarfing rootstocks), use two stakes with flexible ties that allow trunk movement [1][5].

## 9. Rose Pruning and Companion Shrubs

### Why Include Roses in the Orchard Module

Roses are closely related to apple and pear trees (all Rosaceae family) and share many of the same pests and diseases. Swansons Nursery (Seattle) recommends March as the optimal rose pruning month in the PNW, and the pruning principles overlap significantly with fruit tree pruning [2][7].

### March Rose Pruning Protocol

1. **Remove dead canes** (brown/black, no green under bark scratch test) [7].
2. **Remove suckers** from below the graft union [7].
3. **Select 3-5 strongest canes** and cut back by one-third [7].
4. **Make cuts 1/4 inch above an outward-facing bud** at a 45-degree angle [7].
5. **Remove any remaining leaves** from last season to reduce overwintering disease spores [7].

### Companion Shrubs for the Orchard Edge

Several shrubs serve dual duty as ornamentals and ecological support for the orchard:

| Shrub | Function | PNW Notes |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Pacific Serviceberry | Native pollinator support + edible fruit | Amelanchier alnifolia; blueberry-like berries |
| Oregon Grape | Native ground cover + pollinator support | Mahonia aquifolium; deer-resistant |
| Red Flowering Currant | Early spring pollinator magnet | Ribes sanguineum; PNW native |
| Elderberry | Pollinator + bird habitat + edible fruit | Sambucus; fast-growing; prune annually |
| Blueberry | Edible fruit + ornamental fall color | Same pH needs as rhododendrons |
