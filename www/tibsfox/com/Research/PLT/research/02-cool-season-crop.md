## 1. Cool-Season Planting Philosophy

Cool-season crops are the foundation of PNW spring gardening. These are the plants that thrive in the conditions that frustrate warm-season growers: temperatures in the 40-60F range, consistent moisture, overcast skies that prevent bolting, and cool nights that develop flavor in greens and root vegetables [1][2][3].

The PNW advantage for cool-season crops is profound. While gardeners in the Midwest and Northeast wait until May for soil to warm and dry enough for planting, the maritime PNW offers a March-through-June cool-season window -- four full months of optimal growing conditions for crops that most of the country can only grow in a six-week spring window or a fall season [2][4].

The key constraint is not cold but wet. PNW soil in late March is typically saturated from winter rains. The gardener's first task is not warming the soil but draining it. Raised beds are the PNW cool-season gardener's most important tool -- they drain faster, warm earlier, and dry to workable moisture days before in-ground beds [2][3][5].

### The Squeeze Test

Before planting, test soil workability: grab a handful of soil and squeeze it into a ball. If water drips out, the soil is too wet -- wait a day. If the ball holds together but crumbles when poked with a finger, the soil is perfect for planting. If the soil won't form a ball at all, it is too dry (rare in March but possible in raised beds after 3+ dry days) [4][5].

### Direct Sow vs. Indoor Start

For late March cool-season planting, most crops go directly into the ground. Indoor starting is unnecessary for frost-tolerant crops and can actually be counterproductive -- cool-season seedlings grown indoors under lights are often leggy and weak compared to direct-sown plants that germinate in the cool, bright conditions of outdoor PNW spring [2][3].

The exceptions: onions (too late to start from seed; buy starts), brassica transplants for April planting (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage started indoors in March for 4-6 week head start), and celery (requires a long warm start indoors) [2][3][4].

## 2. Legumes

### Peas: The Premier Late-March Crop

Peas are the single most recommended late-March direct-sow crop across every PNW planting guide consulted. Multiple PNW sources confirm that snow peas, snap peas, and shelling peas can go into the ground as soon as soil is workable, with March sowing providing the longest harvest window before summer heat causes decline [1][2][3][4].

**Snow Peas**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 60-70
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 28F; light frost improves sweetness
- PNW notes: Earliest pea type to mature. Flat, edible pods harvested when seeds are barely visible. Excellent raw in salads or stir-fried. Provide 4-5 foot trellis or netting [1][2][3].

**Snap Peas (Sugar Snap)**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 60-75
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 28F
- PNW notes: Round, edible pods with fully developed peas inside. The all-purpose pea for fresh eating, cooking, and freezing. Most popular type in PNW home gardens. Provide 5-6 foot trellis [1][2][3].

**Shelling Peas (English Peas)**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 65-80
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 28F
- PNW notes: Traditional garden pea -- shell the pod, eat the peas. Longer time to harvest than snow or snap types. Best for freezing. Lower-growing varieties (2-3 feet) may not need trellising [1][3].

### Fava Beans

Fava beans (broad beans) are the hardiest legume for PNW spring planting, tolerating harder frost than peas and serving as heavy nitrogen fixers that build soil fertility for subsequent plantings [2][4].

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F
- Days to harvest: 80-100
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F (the hardiest common garden legume)
- PNW notes: Deep Harvest Farm recommends succession-sowing favas beginning in March. Plant seeds 1-2 inches deep, 6 inches apart. No trellis needed but staking helps in windy locations. Excellent cover crop function -- nitrogen-fixing root nodules remain in soil after harvest. Dual-purpose: harvest young pods as snap beans or let mature for shelling [2][4].
- Companion value: Nitrogen fixation enriches soil for following crops. Excellent pre-crop for heavy feeders (tomatoes, corn, squash) planted in the same bed after fava harvest [4][6].

## 3. Salad Greens

### Lettuce

Loose-leaf lettuce varieties are the easiest salad green for PNW beginners and the fastest path to a harvest after peas and radishes [1][2][3].

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F (germinates best at 45-65F)
- Days to harvest: 30-45 (baby leaf) to 55-70 (full head)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 28F; hard freeze kills outer leaves but crowns regrow
- PNW notes: The Odd Garden emphasizes loose-leaf varieties for beginners -- they produce continuously for weeks when harvested as "cut and come again" rather than pulling entire plants. Sow every 2 weeks through April for continuous harvest. Red-leaf varieties add visual interest and are slightly more slug-resistant than green types [1][2][3].

### Arugula

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 21-30 (baby leaf); 40-50 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Deep Harvest Farm recommends sowing arugula weekly from March through mid-September for continuous harvest. Fastest-growing salad green after radish microgreens. Flavor intensifies with heat -- spring arugula is milder and more universally appealing than summer plantings. Bolt-prone in warm weather; spring is the optimal PNW arugula season [2][4].

### Spinach

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F (one of the earliest-germinating greens)
- Days to harvest: 37-45 (baby) to 50-60 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F
- PNW notes: Thrives in the cool, moist conditions of late March PNW weather. Slow to bolt in the maritime climate -- PNW spinach season extends well into June in most years, unlike inland regions where May heat triggers bolting. Savoy (crinkled) types are more cold-hardy; flat-leaf types are easier to clean. Direct-sow 1/2 inch deep, 2 inches apart [1][2][3].

### Kale

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 50-65 (baby leaf) to 70-90 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 15F (the hardiest common salad green)
- PNW notes: The defining PNW garden crop. Grows year-round in western Washington. March-sown kale produces through the following winter if not destroyed by cabbage aphids in fall. Lacinato (dinosaur/Tuscan) kale is the most cold-hardy variety. Red Russian is the most tender for salads. Curly kale is the most productive by weight [1][2][3][4].

### Mustard Greens

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 25-35 (baby) to 45-60 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Fast-growing, spicy greens that add heat to salad mixes. Red varieties (Red Giant, Ruby Streaks) are visually striking. Bolt-prone in warm weather but excellent through late March to May in PNW. Succession-sow every 3 weeks [3][4].

### Mache (Corn Salad)

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F
- Days to harvest: 45-60
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 10F (one of the most frost-hardy salad greens)
- PNW notes: An underappreciated gem for PNW gardens. Grows in conditions too cold and wet for lettuce. Mild, nutty flavor. Tiny rosettes are slow-growing but incredibly cold-hardy. Self-seeds readily -- once established, a patch of mache will return year after year. Plant in early spring or fall for best results [3][4].

### Swiss Chard

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 50-60 (baby leaf) to 55-65 (full stalks)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Dual-purpose -- young leaves for salads, mature stems for cooking. Bright Lights variety produces multicolored stems (red, yellow, orange, white, pink). More heat-tolerant than spinach -- continues producing through PNW summer without bolting. A single March sowing can produce harvests from June through the following March [2][3][4].

## 4. Root Vegetables

### Radish

The fastest-maturing vegetable in the garden and the best crop for teaching children that seeds become food [1][3][4].

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 22-30 (spring round types); 50-70 (daikon/winter types)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 28F
- PNW notes: Cherry Belle and French Breakfast are classic PNW spring varieties. Sow every 2 weeks for continuous harvest. Radishes are excellent as row markers for slow-germinating crops like carrots -- sow a radish seed every 6 inches along the carrot row, and the fast-emerging radish leaves mark where the carrots were planted. Harvest the radishes before they crowd the carrots [1][3][4].

### Carrot

- Minimum soil temperature: 45F (will germinate at 40F but very slowly)
- Days to harvest: 65-80 (Nantes types) to 75-90 (Chantenay/Danvers)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F; frost sweetens flavor
- PNW notes: Carrots require patience -- germination in March soil temperatures takes 14-21 days. Keep the soil surface consistently moist during germination; cover with burlap or row cover to prevent drying. St. Clare's guide lists carrots under March "Roots" for direct sow. Nantes types are the best choice for PNW clay soils -- their blunt ends push through compacted soil better than tapered types [4][5].

### Beet

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 50-65
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Deep Harvest Farm recommends sowing beets every three weeks from March through July for continuous harvest. Each "seed" is actually a cluster of 2-4 seeds -- thin to 3-4 inches apart after emergence. Both roots and greens are edible. Golden beet varieties do not bleed red juice into other foods. Chioggia (candy cane) beets add visual interest [2][4].

### Turnip

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 35-50 (baby turnips) to 50-70 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F
- PNW notes: Hakurei (Japanese salad turnip) is a revelation for gardeners who think they dislike turnips -- sweet, crisp, mild, eaten raw like an apple. Matures in 35-40 days. Traditional purple-top turnips are better for storage and cooking. Both types are excellent cool-season performers in the PNW [3][4].

### Parsnip

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F (among the earliest germinating root crops)
- Days to harvest: 100-130
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 10F; requires frost to develop full sweetness
- PNW notes: The longest-season root crop -- March-sown parsnips harvest in July-August, but the best parsnips are left in the ground through fall and harvested after the first hard frost, which converts starches to sugars. Parsnips require deeply loosened soil (12+ inches) and the slowest germination of any common root vegetable (21-28 days in March soil). Mark the row carefully [4][5].

## 5. Alliums and Potatoes

### Onion Sets and Starts

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F (sets and transplants are cold-hardy)
- Days to harvest: 90-120 from transplants; 100-150 from sets
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F
- PNW notes: NW Edible Life notes that it is too late to start onions from seed by March -- the season is too short. Purchase nursery-grown onion starts (bare-root bundles of 50-75 plants) or onion sets (small dormant bulbs). Plant starts 4-6 inches apart, 1 inch deep. Long-day varieties are essential for PNW latitude -- short-day varieties grown in the South will not bulb properly this far north [2][3][4].

### Shallots

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F
- Days to harvest: 90-120
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F
- PNW notes: Plant individual shallot cloves 6 inches apart, 1 inch deep. Each clove multiplies into a cluster of 4-8 shallots by harvest. Easier than onions and more forgiving of PNW conditions. French Grey shallot is the gourmet standard; Dutch Yellow is more productive [3][4].

### Garlic (Spring Planting)

- Minimum soil temperature: 35F
- Days to harvest: 90-120 (spring-planted garlic produces smaller bulbs than fall-planted)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 10F
- PNW notes: Fall planting (October-November) is the standard for garlic in the PNW, producing larger bulbs with more cloves. Spring planting is "functional but inferior" -- it works, but bulbs will be smaller and may not divide into distinct cloves as cleanly. If you missed fall planting, spring garlic is better than no garlic. Softneck varieties are better suited to spring planting than hardneck types [2][3].

### Chives

- Minimum soil temperature: 40F (transplants; seed germination is slow)
- Days to harvest: Perennial -- harvest continuously once established
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to -20F (one of the hardiest kitchen herbs)
- PNW notes: Divide existing clumps in March or plant new divisions. Chives serve triple duty in the PNW garden: culinary herb, ornamental flower (purple globes attract pollinators), and orchard companion plant (planted around fruit tree bases, chives help prevent apple scab). Every PNW garden should have at least one chive clump [2][4][6].

### Potatoes

- Minimum soil temperature: 45F
- Days to harvest: 70-90 (new potatoes) to 90-120 (full size)
- Frost tolerance: Foliage killed at 28F; tubers survive underground
- PNW notes: NW Edible Life recommends planting potatoes on St. Patrick's Day (March 17) as the PNW gardener tradition, noting that chitting (pre-sprouting) potatoes before sowing in loose soil with straw insulation protects against late frost. By March 26, potatoes are right on schedule. Plant seed potatoes 4-6 inches deep, 12 inches apart, in trenches or containers. Hill soil around stems as they grow to increase tuber production. Yukon Gold and Red Pontiac are reliable PNW varieties [2][3][5].

## 6. Hardy Herbs

### Direct-Sow Herbs for Late March

**Cilantro (Coriander)**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 30-45 (leaf) to 90-120 (seed/coriander)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Bolt-prone in warm weather -- spring is the best PNW cilantro season. Sow every 2 weeks through May for continuous leaf harvest. Let some plants bolt and set seed for coriander spice and self-seeding. Slow bolt varieties (Calypso, Santo) extend the harvest window [2][3][4].

**Dill**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 40-60 (leaf) to 90-110 (seed)
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 25F
- PNW notes: Direct-sow in place -- dill resents transplanting. Attracts beneficial insects (parasitic wasps, hoverflies) that prey on garden pests. Self-seeds enthusiastically; one planting often establishes a permanent patch [2][3].

**Parsley**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F (germinates slowly -- 14-21 days)
- Days to harvest: 70-90
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 15F (biennial -- survives PNW winters)
- PNW notes: Italian flat-leaf parsley is more flavorful; curly parsley is more decorative and productive. Soaking seeds overnight before planting speeds germination. Parsley is biennial -- first-year plants produce leaves, second-year plants bolt and set seed. Plant every year for continuous harvest [2][3][4].

**Chervil**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: 45-60
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 20F
- PNW notes: An underused herb in PNW gardens, despite being perfectly suited to the cool, moist climate. Delicate anise flavor, essential in French cooking (fines herbes blend). Prefers partial shade -- one of the few herbs that does not need full sun. Bolt-prone in heat; spring is the only reliable PNW season [3][4].

**Oregano**
- Minimum soil temperature: 45F (transplant divisions; seed germination slow)
- Days to harvest: Perennial -- harvest continuously once established
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to -20F
- PNW notes: Greek oregano is the culinary standard. Divide existing clumps in March or plant nursery-purchased starts. Once established, oregano is nearly indestructible in PNW gardens. Spreads aggressively -- plant in contained beds or use root barriers [3][4].

**Mint**
- Minimum soil temperature: 40F
- Days to harvest: Perennial -- harvest continuously once established
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to -30F (hardiest common herb)
- PNW notes: **Mint must be planted in containers or with root barriers.** It is an aggressive spreader that will colonize an entire garden bed within one season. That said, it is an excellent companion plant -- the strong aroma confuses and repels aphids and ants while attracting bee pollinators. Spearmint and peppermint are the most common culinary types [2][3][4][6].

**Sorrel**
- Minimum soil temperature: 35F
- Days to harvest: 60-80 (first season); perennial thereafter
- Frost tolerance: Hardy to 0F
- PNW notes: Lemony, tart leaves used in French cuisine (sorrel soup, sorrel sauce). Perennial -- once established, it returns every spring with minimal care. One of the earliest greens to emerge in PNW spring. Red-veined sorrel is ornamental as well as edible [3][4].

## 9. PNW-Specific Growing Notes

### The Slug Factor

Every crop in this guide is vulnerable to slug damage in the first 2 weeks after germination. March is peak slug season in maritime Washington. Slug defense (Module 05) is not optional -- it is a prerequisite for successful cool-season planting. Transplants survive slug pressure that would destroy direct-sown seedlings, so consider starting slug-vulnerable crops (lettuce, basil, brassicas) indoors and transplanting as 3-4 inch seedlings rather than direct-sowing [1][3][4].

### The Overcast Advantage

PNW overcast skies in spring are an advantage, not a limitation, for cool-season crops. The diffused light prevents bolting in lettuce, spinach, and cilantro -- these crops run to seed in the intense sun of inland gardens but produce for months in the gentle light of maritime PNW spring. The same overcast keeps soil temperatures moderate, extending the cool-season growing window well into June [1][2][3].

### Water Management

Late March soil is typically saturated. The risk is overwatering, not underwatering. Do not irrigate newly planted beds unless the soil surface dries visibly (rare in March). As April progresses and storm frequency decreases, begin supplemental watering -- especially for shallow-rooted crops like lettuce, spinach, and radish in raised beds that drain quickly [2][3].
