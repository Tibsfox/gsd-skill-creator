## 1. The Ecology of Defense

Defense in a PNW garden is not about eliminating pests. It is about building a system where pest populations are held in check by ecological complexity -- where the garden itself does the work of pest management through biodiversity, habitat for predatory insects, and strategic plant combinations that confuse, deter, and starve pest species [1][2][3].

This approach has a name in formal ecology: integrated pest management (IPM). But in the PNW garden context, it is more accurately described as "companion ecology" -- the study of how plants in proximity to each other create emergent pest management effects that no single plant achieves alone [1][2].

The key insight: monocultures attract pests. A garden bed planted entirely to lettuce is a buffet for slugs. An orchard row planted entirely to grass is a habitat for voles. But a garden bed where lettuce grows between rows of aromatic herbs confuses slug navigation (slugs navigate by scent). An orchard row planted with clover, chives, and daffodils fixes nitrogen, repels rodents, and prevents apple scab -- all while requiring less mowing than grass [2][3][4].

This module covers two domains: slug defense (the PNW gardener's primary pest challenge) and orchard companion planting (the long-term ecological support system for fruit trees). Both share the same principle: architectural leverage over brute force [1][2][3][4].

## 2. Integrated Slug Management

### The PNW Slug Problem

March is peak slug season in maritime Washington. The combination of mild temperatures (40-55F), saturated soil, and abundant new growth creates ideal conditions for the European garden slug (Deroceras reticulatum), the Pacific banana slug (Ariolimax columbianus), and several other species that collectively constitute the most significant pest in PNW gardens [5][6][7].

The numbers are stark: tender seedlings planted without slug defense face 60-80% loss rates in their first two weeks. Direct-sown lettuce, basil, and brassica seedlings are particularly vulnerable -- slugs can consume an entire row of emerging seedlings in a single night. NW Edible Life explicitly recommends transplanting over direct-sowing for slug-vulnerable crops, noting that established transplants survive slug pressure that would destroy seedlings [5][6][7].

### Strategy 1: Iron Phosphate Bait

Iron phosphate-based slug baits (Sluggo, EscarGo) are the foundation of organic slug management in the PNW:

- **How it works:** Slugs consume the bait pellets, which contain iron phosphate. The iron phosphate disrupts the slug's digestive system, causing it to stop feeding immediately. The slug dies within 3-6 days [5][6].
- **Safety:** Safe for pets, children, wildlife, and earthworms. Iron phosphate breaks down into iron and phosphate -- both naturally occurring soil nutrients. No secondary poisoning risk [5][6].
- **Application:** Scatter pellets around newly planted areas at a rate of approximately 1 teaspoon per square foot. Reapply after heavy rain (pellets dissolve). Most effective when applied in the evening, when slugs begin foraging [5][6].
- **Timing:** Apply the evening of planting day. Do not wait to see slug damage -- by the time you see damage, seedlings may be destroyed [6].

### Strategy 2: Copper Barriers

Copper creates a mild electric charge when a slug's slime contacts it, repelling the slug from crossing the barrier:

- **Copper tape:** Apply 2-inch-wide copper tape around the rims of raised beds and containers. The continuous copper ring creates a barrier slugs will not cross [5][6].
- **Copper mesh:** Staple copper mesh (hardware cloth) to the outside of raised bed frames as a broader barrier [6].
- **Maintenance:** Copper oxidizes (turns green) over time. Lightly sand the surface annually to maintain conductivity. Ensure no leaves, soil, or debris bridge the copper barrier -- slugs will use any bridge to bypass the copper [5][6].

### Strategy 3: Beer Traps

Slugs are attracted to the yeast in beer and will crawl into containers of beer and drown:

- **Setup:** Bury shallow containers (tuna cans, yogurt cups) so the rim is level with the soil surface. Fill with beer to within 1/2 inch of the rim [5][6].
- **Placement:** One trap per 3-4 square feet in slug-heavy areas. Place near (but not in) planted rows [5][6].
- **Maintenance:** Empty and refill every 2-3 days. Dead slugs in the trap deter other slugs from entering [6].
- **Limitation:** Beer traps catch slugs already near the trap. They do not attract slugs from more than 3-4 feet away. Beer traps work best as a supplement to barriers and baits, not as a standalone strategy [5][6].

### Strategy 4: Evening Patrols

The most effective (and most labor-intensive) slug management strategy:

- **Timing:** Patrol 30-60 minutes after sunset, when slugs emerge from daytime hiding spots. Use a headlamp or flashlight [5][6][7].
- **Method:** Hand-pick slugs and drop them into a container of soapy water (kills quickly) or relocate to a distant area [5][6].
- **Frequency:** Nightly for the first 2 weeks after planting, then every 2-3 days as slug populations decrease [6].
- **Why it works:** Removing reproductive-age slugs in March prevents exponential population growth in April and May. Each slug removed in March is 20-50 fewer slugs in May [5][6].

### Strategy 5: Watering Timing

Slugs are most active in wet conditions. Adjusting irrigation timing reduces slug-friendly surface moisture during peak foraging hours:

- **Water in the morning, not the evening.** Morning watering gives the soil surface time to dry before slugs emerge at dusk. Evening watering creates the wet surface conditions that encourage slug activity [5][6].
- **Drip irrigation over overhead.** Drip systems deliver water to plant roots without wetting the soil surface or leaf surfaces. Dry soil surfaces between plants reduce slug highway moisture [6].
- **PNW March caveat:** In late March, rain provides most of the moisture. Watering timing management becomes more relevant in April-May as natural precipitation decreases and supplemental irrigation begins [5][6].

### Strategy 6: Habitat Management

Remove slug hiding spots near garden beds:

- **Clear boards, pots, and debris** from around garden beds. Slugs shelter under objects during the day [5][6].
- **Raised bed advantage:** The elevated, well-drained structure of raised beds is inherently less slug-friendly than ground-level beds. The copper tape strategy compounds this advantage [5][6].
- **Bare soil buffer:** Maintain a 12-inch bare soil or gravel buffer between garden beds and slug habitat (lawn, ground cover, woodpiles). Slugs prefer not to cross open, dry surfaces [6].

### Slug Defense Decision Matrix

| Situation | Recommended Strategy | Priority |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|
| New seedlings just planted | Iron phosphate bait + copper tape | Critical |
| Raised beds with high-value crops | Copper barriers on all edges | High |
| Persistent slug pressure despite bait | Evening patrols (nightly for 2 weeks) | High |
| Supplemental reduction | Beer traps at bed corners | Moderate |
| Long-term reduction | Morning watering + habitat management | Ongoing |
| Slug-vulnerable crops (lettuce, basil) | Start indoors; transplant as 3-4 inch seedlings | Prevention |

## 3. Orchard Companion Planting Matrix

### The Six Functional Categories

Fedco Seeds' orchard companion guide identifies six functional categories for companion plants. Each category provides a specific ecological service that reduces the orchard's dependence on external inputs [2][3][4]:

1. **Beneficial insect accumulators** -- Attract predatory and parasitic insects via nectar and pollen. These insects (hoverflies, parasitic wasps, ladybugs) prey on aphids, codling moth larvae, mites, and other orchard pests [2][3].
2. **Aromatic pest confusers** -- Bitter aromas deter and confuse pest insects attempting to locate host trees by scent. The aromatic cloud around an orchard planted with herbs is measurably different from a grass-only orchard floor [2][3].
3. **Living mulches** -- Low-growing plants that suppress grass, retain moisture, and prevent soil erosion without competing with tree roots for deep nutrients [2][3].
4. **Dynamic accumulators** -- Deep-rooted plants that mine subsoil minerals (potassium, calcium, phosphorus) and make them available at the soil surface through leaf decomposition or chop-and-drop management [2][3][4].
5. **Nitrogen fixers** -- Leguminous plants that convert atmospheric nitrogen into plant-available soil nitrogen through symbiotic bacteria in root nodules [2][3][4].
6. **Pest deterrents** -- Plants with specific repellent functions against particular pest species [2][3].

### The Master Companion Matrix

| Plant | Category | Function | Placement | PNW Notes |
|-------|----------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Chives | Pest confuser, deterrent | Prevents apple scab; aromatic confusion | Base of trunk, 6-8 in ring | Perennial; divide in March |
| Daffodils | Pest deterrent | Rodent (mouse/vole) deterrence | Tight circle, 12 in from trunk | Toxic to rodents; plant fall or spring |
| Comfrey | Dynamic accumulator | Mines subsoil K, Ca, P; chop-and-drop | Drip line, 3-4 ft from trunk | Bocking 14 sterile variety preferred |
| White Clover | Nitrogen fixer, living mulch | Atmospheric N fixation; weed suppression | Between rows, under canopy | Mow monthly; tolerates light shade |
| Crimson Clover | Nitrogen fixer | Heavy N fixation; annual cover crop | Between rows | Annual; reseed or replant each year |
| Nasturtiums | Trap crop | Attracts aphids away from trees | Scattered under canopy | Annual; reseed readily; edible flowers |
| Yarrow | Beneficial insect accumulator | Hoverflies, parasitic wasps, lacewings | Orchard edges, between rows | Perennial; drought-tolerant once established |
| Calendula | Beneficial insect accumulator | Hoverflies, bees; pest-repelling compounds | Between rows, bed edges | Annual; medicinal; edible flowers |
| Borage | Beneficial insect accumulator | Major bee attractor; also deters hornworms | Scattered, sunny spots | Annual; self-seeds vigorously |

## 4. Nitrogen Fixation Ecology

### How Nitrogen Fixation Works

Nitrogen fixation is the process by which atmospheric nitrogen (N2, which makes up 78% of air) is converted into plant-available ammonium (NH4+) by bacteria living in symbiotic relationship with legume roots. The bacteria (Rhizobium species) colonize root nodules -- visible as small pink or white bumps on legume roots -- where they convert atmospheric nitrogen into a form the plant can use. In exchange, the legume provides the bacteria with sugars from photosynthesis [2][3][9].

When the legume dies or its roots decompose, the nitrogen stored in root nodules and plant tissue becomes available to nearby plants. In an orchard context, clover growing between tree rows provides a continuous low-level nitrogen input to the soil, reducing or eliminating the need for synthetic nitrogen fertilizer [2][3][4].

### Nitrogen Fixation Rates

| Plant | Fixation Rate (lb N/acre/year) | Orchard Application |
|-------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| White clover | 75-200 | Permanent living mulch between rows |
| Crimson clover | 100-175 | Annual cover crop; mow and incorporate |
| Fava beans | 100-200 | Garden rotation; heavy fixation |
| Red clover | 75-175 | Biennial; good for orchards with moderate shade |
| Hairy vetch | 100-150 | Winter cover crop; killed by mowing in spring |
| Field peas | 50-150 | Annual cover crop; fix and food |

*Sources: WSU Extension [9]; USDA NRCS Cover Crop Database; Fedco Seeds [2]*

### Application in the PNW Orchard

1. **Seed white clover between orchard rows** in March. Broadcast seed at 4-8 oz per 1,000 sq ft. No soil preparation needed -- clover establishes well from surface-sown seed into existing grass or bare soil [2][3].
2. **Mow monthly** to keep clover low (4-6 inches) and prevent it from competing with tree canopy for light. Mowing also forces clover to shed root nodules, releasing fixed nitrogen into the soil [3].
3. **Allow clover to flower** 2-3 times per season before mowing. Clover flowers are a major nectar source for honeybees and native bees [3][4].
4. **Combine with comfrey** for maximum effect. Clover fixes nitrogen in the upper soil; comfrey mines phosphorus and potassium from the subsoil. Together, they provide a nearly complete fertility package [2][3][4].

## 5. Mineral Accumulation and Dynamic Accumulators

### The Comfrey Effect

Comfrey (Symphytum officinale) is the most important dynamic accumulator in the PNW orchard. Its taproot reaches 6-10 feet into the subsoil, mining potassium, calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, and iron from depths that fruit tree roots rarely access. When comfrey leaves are cut and left on the soil surface ("chop-and-drop"), these minerals are released as the leaves decompose, effectively pumping subsoil nutrients to the surface where tree feeder roots can access them [2][3][4].

### Comfrey Management

- **Variety:** Bocking 14 (Symphytum x uplandicum 'Bocking 14') is the preferred variety. It is sterile (does not set viable seed) and can only be propagated by root division, preventing unwanted spread. Wild comfrey and other varieties can become invasively weedy [2][3].
- **Placement:** Plant comfrey root pieces at the drip line of fruit trees, 3-4 feet from the trunk. One plant per tree is sufficient -- comfrey grows to 3-4 feet tall and 3 feet wide [3].
- **Harvest:** Cut comfrey to 2 inches above ground level 3-5 times per growing season. Leave cut leaves in place as mulch. Each cut produces approximately 2 pounds of leaf material per plant, rich in potassium and phosphorus [2][3][4].
- **Caution:** Comfrey is extremely persistent once established. The deep taproot regenerates from any fragment left in the soil. Plant comfrey where you want it permanently -- removal is extremely difficult [2][3].

### Other Dynamic Accumulators

| Plant | Primary Minerals | Depth | PNW Use |
|-------|-----------------|-------|---------|
| Comfrey | K, Ca, P, Mg, Fe | 6-10 ft | Orchard drip line; chop-and-drop |
| Dandelion | K, Ca, P, Fe | 3-5 ft | Allow in orchard understory; do not spray |
| Chicory | K, Ca | 4-6 ft | Orchard rows; drought-tolerant |
| Stinging nettle | Fe, N, Ca, K | 3-4 ft | Orchard edge (handle with gloves); tea fertilizer |
| Dock (Rumex) | Fe, Ca, K | 3-5 ft | Allow in moderate quantities; can be weedy |
| Yarrow | K, P, Cu | 2-3 ft | Orchard edges; pollinator support dual function |

## 6. Pest Confusion and Aromatic Deterrence

### How Aromatic Pest Confusion Works

Many pest insects locate their host plants by scent -- following the volatile organic compounds that plants emit. An apple tree emits a characteristic blend of terpenes and esters that codling moths, aphids, and apple maggot flies use as a homing signal. When aromatic companion plants are interspersed among fruit trees, the aromatic cloud around the orchard becomes complex enough to confuse pest navigation [2][3][4].

This does not eliminate pests. It reduces the efficiency of pest host-finding behavior by 30-50%, meaning fewer pests successfully locate and attack fruit trees. Combined with other IPM strategies (traps, beneficial insects, biological sprays), aromatic confusion is a significant component of an integrated defense system [2][3].

### Key Aromatic Plants for PNW Orchards

**Chives (Allium schoenoprasum)**
- Planted in a ring around fruit tree bases, chives emit sulfur compounds that interfere with apple scab spore germination on bark and leaf surfaces. This is not folklore -- controlled studies have demonstrated reduced scab incidence in orchards with allium companions [2][3].
- Additional benefit: Chive flowers attract beneficial insects in spring when other flowers are scarce [3].

**Mint (Mentha spp.)**
- The strongest aromatic confuser available. Mint's menthol and related compounds create an intense aromatic barrier that repels aphids, ants, and flea beetles [2][3][4].
- **Container only:** Mint spreads by underground runners and will colonize an entire orchard floor within one season if planted in the ground. Grow in containers placed strategically under trees, or sink containers into the soil with rims 2 inches above grade to prevent escape [2][3].

**Coriander/Cilantro (Coriandrum sativum)**
- When cilantro bolts and flowers, it becomes one of the most effective beneficial insect attractors and aphid deterrents in the garden. The open, accessible flowers provide nectar to tiny parasitic wasps that cannot access deep flowers [2][3].
- PNW strategy: Sow cilantro under fruit trees in March and let it bolt naturally. The bolting that frustrates herb gardeners is an asset in the companion ecology context [2][3].

## 7. Pollinator Support Network

### Why Orchards Need Pollinator Support

Fruit trees are entirely dependent on insect pollination for fruit production. A single apple flower must be visited by pollinators 4-5 times for adequate pollination and full-size fruit development. Without pollinator support, fruit set drops to near zero on most apple, pear, and plum varieties [3][4][10].

### Temporal Diversity: The Key Principle

Fruition Seeds' companion planting guide emphasizes temporal diversity: planting a combination of species with different flowering times ensures pollinators always have pollen and nectar sources from spring through fall. If pollinators have food available before and after fruit bloom, they are more likely to be present in the orchard when fruit trees flower [4][10].

### Pollinator Timeline

| Season | Bloom Period | Plants | Pollinators Supported |
|--------|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| Very early spring | Feb-Mar | Willow, crocus, snowdrops | Mason bees, bumblebee queens |
| Early spring | Mar-Apr | Red flowering currant, Oregon grape | Mason bees, bumblebees, hoverflies |
| Spring (fruit bloom) | Apr-May | Apple, pear, plum, cherry + clover, chive flowers | Honeybees, mason bees, bumblebees |
| Late spring | May-Jun | Borage, calendula, chamomile, yarrow | All bees, hoverflies, parasitic wasps |
| Summer | Jun-Aug | Lavender, echinacea, bee balm, fennel | Honeybees, native bees, butterflies |
| Late summer | Aug-Sep | Goldenrod, asters, sunflowers | Bumblebees, native bees, late butterflies |
| Fall | Sep-Oct | Sedum, fall asters, goldenrod | Late bumblebees, overwintering queens |

*Sources: Fruition Seeds [4]; Xerces Society; WSU Extension Pollinator Conservation [10]*

### Mason Bee Support

Mason bees (Osmia lignaria) are the most effective fruit tree pollinators in the PNW -- more effective per bee than honeybees. They are native, solitary (no hives), non-aggressive, and active at lower temperatures than honeybees. Supporting mason bees requires [10]:

- **Nesting habitat:** Mason bee houses (bundles of paper tubes or drilled wood blocks) placed at eye level facing south or southeast
- **Mud source:** Mason bees seal their egg chambers with mud. A small area of exposed, moist clay soil near the nesting site is essential
- **Early spring flowers:** Mason bees emerge in early March and need immediate food sources -- willows, crocus, and red flowering currant are critical
- **No pesticides during bloom:** Even organic pesticides (Bt, spinosad) can kill mason bees if applied during active foraging

## 8. Hardy Annual Flowers for Early Season

### Why Plant Flowers in March

Hardy annual flowers planted in late March serve three functions: pollinator support (providing food before fruit trees bloom), beneficial insect habitat (attracting predatory insects that will protect garden crops), and aesthetic satisfaction (color in the garden after months of gray PNW winter) [3][4][11].

### March-Plantable Hardy Annuals

| Flower | Frost Tolerance | Days to Bloom | Ecological Function |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Calendula | 25F | 45-60 | Hoverflies, bees; pest-repelling compounds |
| Sweet Alyssum | 28F | 35-45 | Ground-level nectar for tiny beneficial wasps |
| Bachelor's Buttons | 20F | 65-80 | Bees, butterflies; excellent cut flower |
| Larkspur | 15F | 80-100 | Bumblebees, hoverflies; self-seeds |
| Sweet Peas | 25F | 60-80 | Bees; ornamental; nitrogen fixing (legume) |
| Poppies (California, Shirley) | 28F | 55-70 | Bees; self-seeds; drought-tolerant |
| Nigella (Love-in-a-mist) | 20F | 60-75 | Bees; ornamental seed pods; self-seeds |
| Snapdragon | 25F | 70-90 | Bumblebees (fit inside the flower); long-blooming |
| Viola/Pansy | 15F | 50-70 | Early bees; edible flowers; cold-hardy |
| Cosmos (early varieties) | 28F | 55-70 | Hoverflies, bees, butterflies; long-season bloom |

### Planting Notes

- **Direct-sow in place.** Most hardy annuals resent transplanting and establish best from direct-sown seed [3][4].
- **Scatter-sow for naturalizing.** Mix seeds with sand for even distribution. Rake lightly to cover. Keep moist until emergence [4].
- **Location:** Orchard edges, garden borders, and any sunny bare ground. Flowers planted near vegetable gardens attract the beneficial insects that protect the vegetables [3][4].
- **Allow self-seeding.** Let some plants go to seed at the end of the season. Many hardy annuals (calendula, nigella, larkspur, bachelor's buttons) self-sow reliably in PNW gardens, establishing permanent populations that return without replanting [4].

## 9. The Companion Planting Interaction Map

### How the System Works Together

The companion planting matrix is not a collection of independent plant functions. It is an integrated ecological system where each component supports and enhances the others:

```
AROMATIC CLOUD (chives, mint, coriander)
  |
  v  Confuses pest navigation → fewer pests reach trees
  |
TRAP CROPS (nasturtiums, calendula)
  |
  v  Concentrates remaining pests → away from fruit
  |
BENEFICIAL INSECTS (yarrow, borage, chamomile attract hoverflies, wasps)
  |
  v  Predators consume concentrated pest populations
  |
NITROGEN FIXATION (clover) + MINERAL ACCUMULATION (comfrey)
  |
  v  Healthy, well-fed trees resist pest damage better
  |
PEST DETERRENTS (daffodils, marigolds)
  |
  v  Physical/chemical barriers against specific pests
  |
POLLINATOR SUPPORT (temporal diversity of flower species)
  |
  v  Ensures fruit set → the whole system produces the harvest
```

### The Key Insight

No single companion plant makes a significant difference. The system effect is what matters. An orchard with only chives has slightly less scab. An orchard with only clover has slightly more nitrogen. But an orchard with chives AND clover AND comfrey AND yarrow AND daffodils has measurably less disease, better nutrition, more pollinators, fewer rodents, and more beneficial insects than an orchard with grass alone [2][3][4].

This is the Amiga Principle at garden scale: specialized components (Agnus for scheduling, Denise for display, Paula for I/O) each doing one thing well, their architectural cooperation producing outcomes vastly exceeding what any single component achieves. The clover does not know about the comfrey. The chives do not coordinate with the yarrow. But the orchard that contains all of them becomes a self-renewing system that reduces external inputs every season [2][3][4].

### What to Avoid

Fedco Seeds and Raintree Nursery both emphasize one critical negative rule: **do not plant competitive grasses and herbaceous plants directly under fruit trees.** Grass competes aggressively with tree roots for water and nutrients in the top 12 inches of soil -- exactly where feeder roots concentrate. The "mulch donut" zone from trunk to drip line should contain companion plants (chives, comfrey, bulbs) but not grass or sod [2][3][8].

## 10. Planting and Maintenance Guide

### March Companion Planting Actions

| Action | When | How | Priority |
|--------|------|-----|----------|
| Plant chives at tree bases | Mar 27-28 (dry window) | Divide existing or plant nursery starts; ring around trunk at 6-8 inch radius | High |
| Plant comfrey at drip lines | Mar 27-28 | Root pieces or nursery starts; one per tree at drip line | High |
| Sow white clover between rows | Mar 27-28 | Broadcast seed 4-8 oz/1000 sq ft; rake lightly | High |
| Plant daffodil bulbs near trunks | Mar 27-28 (or fall) | Tight circle 12 inches from trunk; 4-6 bulbs per tree | Moderate |
| Direct-sow calendula | Mar 27-28 | Scatter-sow in orchard edges and garden borders | Moderate |
| Direct-sow sweet alyssum | Mar 27-28 | Scatter-sow along bed edges and between rows | Moderate |
| Direct-sow borage | Mar 27-28 | 3-4 seeds per spot, sunny locations | Moderate |
| Plant mint in containers | Anytime | Container only; place under trees or near garden | Low |
| Direct-sow nasturtium seeds | Apr 1-7 | Under fruit trees as trap crop; direct-sow after last frost | Low (after April 1) |

### Ongoing Maintenance

| Task | Frequency | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Mow clover | Monthly (Apr-Sep) | Keep 4-6 inches; allow to flower between mowings |
| Chop comfrey | Every 4-6 weeks (May-Sep) | Cut to 2 inches; leave leaves as mulch |
| Divide chives | Annually (March) | Expand rings; share divisions |
| Replace annual flowers | Annually or self-seed | Calendula, nasturtiums, borage -- let self-seed if possible |
| Monitor mint containers | Monthly | Check for escape; prune if roots emerge from drainage holes |
| Refresh daffodil plantings | Every 3-5 years | Add bulbs as existing clumps thin; fall planting is ideal |
