## 1. The Warm-Season Challenge in the PNW

The Pacific Northwest's maritime climate is paradise for cool-season crops and purgatory for warm-season ones. Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, and basil evolved in Mediterranean and tropical climates -- they want hot days, warm nights, and intense sunshine. The PNW offers mild days, cool nights, and 200+ days of overcast skies per year [1][2][3].

This does not mean warm-season crops cannot be grown in the PNW. It means they require a fundamentally different approach than the one that works in California, the Midwest, or the Southeast. The PNW warm-season strategy is built on three principles [1][2][3]:

1. **Start early indoors.** The outdoor growing season for heat-loving crops is May through September at best -- barely enough time for long-season tomatoes to ripen. Starting seeds indoors in late March buys 8-10 weeks of growth before transplanting, giving plants a crucial head start.

2. **Transplant late.** The PNW's marine influence keeps nighttime temperatures below the 55F minimum that warm-season crops need for active growth well into May. Premature transplanting does not give plants a head start -- it stunts them.

3. **Choose short-season varieties.** A 90-day tomato variety in Portland has the same chance of ripening as a 75-day variety in Topeka. Choose varieties bred for short seasons and cool conditions: Stupice, Early Girl, Sungold, Oregon Spring, Siletz.

### The Numbers

The key temperature thresholds for warm-season crops:

| Threshold | Temperature | Significance |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Seed germination | 70-80F soil | Seeds rot below 60F; germination halts below 55F |
| Minimum night temp | 55F air | Growth stalls below this; fruit set fails below 50F |
| Optimal growth | 65-85F day, 60-70F night | Active vegetative and fruit production |
| Stress threshold | Below 50F or above 95F | Growth stops; blossom drop occurs |
| Frost kill | 32F | All warm-season crops die at first frost |

*Sources: St. Clare Heirloom Seeds [1]; WSU Extension [4]; NW Edible Life [2]*

## 2. Indoor Start Timing

### The Critical Calendar

For western Washington lowlands (Seattle/Tacoma/Olympia), the safe outdoor transplant window opens in late May. Working backward from that target:

| Crop | Weeks to Transplant Size | Start Date | Target Transplant Date |
|------|-------------------------|------------|----------------------|
| Tomatoes | 6-8 weeks | Mar 25 - Apr 5 | May 25 - Jun 1 |
| Peppers | 8-10 weeks | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | May 25 - Jun 5 |
| Eggplant | 8-10 weeks | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | May 25 - Jun 5 |
| Basil | 4-6 weeks | Apr 15 - May 1 | May 25 - Jun 5 |
| Broccoli | 4-6 weeks | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | Apr 25 - May 15 |
| Cauliflower | 4-6 weeks | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | Apr 25 - May 15 |
| Cabbage | 4-6 weeks | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | Apr 25 - May 15 |
| Cucumber | 3-4 weeks | May 1 - May 15 | May 25 - Jun 10 |
| Summer Squash | 3-4 weeks | May 1 - May 15 | May 25 - Jun 10 |
| Winter Squash | 3-4 weeks | May 1 - May 15 | May 25 - Jun 10 |

*Sources: St. Clare Heirloom Seeds [1]; NW Edible Life [2]; Deep Harvest Farm [5]*

### The March 26 Action

This week is the ideal tomato and pepper start date. Starting tomatoes in the last week of March gives them 8-9 weeks of indoor growth before the late-May transplant window -- enough time to develop strong root systems and stocky stems without becoming leggy and root-bound [1][2].

Peppers started this week will be 8-9 weeks old at transplant time. Peppers are slower to germinate and grow than tomatoes, so the same start date produces a smaller plant at transplant time -- this is normal and expected. Peppers catch up quickly once they hit warm outdoor soil [1][2][3].

## 3. Tomatoes

### Germination Requirements

- **Soil temperature for germination:** 70-80F (use a heat mat)
- **Germination time:** 5-10 days at 75F; 14-21 days at 65F; fails below 55F
- **Light after germination:** 14-16 hours under grow lights; seedlings stretch and weaken without adequate light
- **Container:** Start in small cells (1.5-2 inch); pot up to 4-inch pots at 3-4 weeks

### PNW-Adapted Varieties

| Variety | Days to Harvest | Type | PNW Notes |
|---------|----------------|------|-----------|
| Stupice | 55-60 | Indeterminate, small | Czech variety; earliest reliable PNW tomato |
| Sungold | 60-65 | Indeterminate, cherry | Orange cherry; sweetest tomato available; PNW standard |
| Early Girl | 62-65 | Indeterminate, medium | Reliable mid-size; good disease resistance |
| Oregon Spring | 58-65 | Determinate, medium | OSU breeding; specifically for PNW conditions |
| Siletz | 65-70 | Determinate, large | Another OSU variety; full-size fruit in short seasons |
| Legend | 68-72 | Determinate, large | OSU; late blight resistant; PNW rain-season performer |
| Black Krim | 75-80 | Indeterminate, large | Heirloom; needs warm site but exceptional flavor |
| San Marzano | 78-85 | Indeterminate, paste | Sauce tomato; needs the warmest PNW microclimate |

*Sources: NW Edible Life [2]; Territorial Seed Company; OSU Extension [4]*

### PNW Tomato Growing Strategy

The successful PNW tomato grower follows these principles:

1. **Warm the soil before transplanting.** Lay black plastic mulch or dark landscape fabric over the planting area 2-3 weeks before transplanting. This raises soil temperature 5-10F above ambient -- the difference between a tomato plant that thrives and one that sulks [2][3].

2. **Plant deep.** Bury the stem up to the first set of leaves. Tomatoes produce adventitious roots along buried stems, building a stronger root system than top-planting allows [2].

3. **Provide a warm microclimate.** South-facing wall, heat-absorbing rocks or pavers nearby, protection from north winds. The best PNW tomato spots are south-facing house walls with concrete walkways reflecting heat -- an urban heat island effect at garden scale [2][3].

4. **Cloche or row cover for the first 2 weeks.** Even after the safe transplant date, nighttime temperatures in late May can drop into the mid-40s. Cloches, Wall-o-Waters, or floating row cover provide 5-8F of frost protection and raise daytime temperatures inside the cover [2][3].

5. **Choose determinates for reliability, indeterminates for abundance.** Determinate varieties ripen their crop in a concentrated window -- useful for canning and sauce-making. Indeterminate varieties produce continuously until frost -- better for fresh eating over the longest possible season [2].

## 4. Peppers

### Germination Requirements

- **Soil temperature for germination:** 75-85F (warmer than tomatoes)
- **Germination time:** 7-14 days at 80F; 21-28 days at 70F; fails below 60F
- **Light after germination:** 14-16 hours under grow lights
- **Container:** Start in small cells; pot up at 4 weeks; peppers resent transplanting less than tomatoes

### PNW-Adapted Varieties

| Variety | Days to Harvest | Type | PNW Notes |
|---------|----------------|------|-----------|
| Jimmy Nardello | 75-80 | Sweet frying | Italian heirloom; productive in PNW |
| Shishito | 60-70 | Mild to medium | Japanese; prolific in cool conditions |
| Carmen | 70-75 | Sweet Italian | AAS winner; reliable in short seasons |
| Early Jalapeno | 65-70 | Hot | Earliest reliable hot pepper for PNW |
| Hungarian Hot Wax | 65-70 | Medium hot | Good cool-weather pepper |
| Padron | 60-65 | Mild to hot | Spanish tapas pepper; compact plants |
| King of the North | 65-70 | Sweet bell | Bred for northern climates; best PNW bell |

*Sources: NW Edible Life [2]; Territorial Seed Company; Adaptive Seeds*

### PNW Pepper Strategy

Peppers are slower and more heat-demanding than tomatoes. In the PNW, peppers require:

- **The warmest possible microclimate.** Same south-facing wall strategy as tomatoes, but more critical for peppers. Hot peppers especially need reflected heat [2][3].
- **Black plastic mulch.** Non-negotiable for PNW pepper growing. Soil temperature below 65F stunts pepper growth even when air temperature is adequate [2][3].
- **Patience.** Peppers started in March will look small and slow compared to tomatoes started the same week. This is normal. Peppers accelerate once soil temperature exceeds 70F, typically not until July in the PNW [1][2].
- **Container growing option.** Peppers excel in large containers (5+ gallon) on south-facing patios or decks. Container soil warms faster than ground soil, and containers can be moved to follow the sun [2].

## 5. Eggplant

### Germination Requirements

- **Soil temperature for germination:** 75-85F (same as peppers)
- **Germination time:** 7-14 days at 80F; very slow below 75F
- **Light after germination:** 14-16 hours under grow lights
- **PNW viability:** Marginal. Eggplant is the most heat-demanding common garden vegetable. Only reliably productive in the warmest PNW microclimates [1][2].

### PNW-Adapted Varieties

| Variety | Days to Harvest | PNW Notes |
|---------|----------------|-----------|
| Ichiban | 58-65 | Japanese; long, slender; best PNW eggplant |
| Little Fingers | 60-68 | Compact plants; multiple small fruits |
| Fairy Tale | 65-70 | AAS winner; compact; ornamental and productive |
| Rosa Bianca | 75-85 | Italian heirloom; needs warmest microclimate |

### PNW Eggplant Strategy

Eggplant is the "stretch goal" for PNW warm-season growers:

- Start indoors on heat mat no later than mid-March (earlier than tomatoes and peppers)
- Transplant to the absolute warmest garden spot -- south-facing wall with black plastic mulch
- Consider growing exclusively in containers (dark-colored pots absorb more solar heat)
- In a cool summer, eggplant may produce little or no harvestable fruit -- accept this possibility and focus efforts on tomatoes and peppers as the reliable warm-season crops [1][2][3]

## 6. Basil

### Germination Requirements

- **Soil temperature for germination:** 70-75F
- **Germination time:** 5-10 days at 70F; fails below 55F
- **Light after germination:** 14-16 hours under grow lights
- **Start date:** Mid-April (later than tomatoes/peppers because basil grows fast)

### PNW Basil Strategy

Basil is often started too early. A basil seedling started in late March will be leggy and root-bound by late May transplanting. Mid-April is the optimal start date for PNW basil -- 4-6 weeks before transplant gives basil the compact, bushy growth habit that produces the most leaves [1][2][3].

Varieties for PNW:

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Genovese | The classic Italian basil. Standard for pesto. Moderate cold tolerance |
| Italian Large Leaf | Larger leaves than Genovese; easier harvesting |
| Thai Basil | More cold-tolerant than sweet basil; doesn't blacken as quickly in cool weather |
| African Blue | Perennial in mild PNW winters; ornamental purple flowers; camphor flavor |
| Lemon Basil | Citrus-flavored; good for fish and tea; moderately cold-tolerant |

PNW basil tips: Pinch off flower buds to extend leaf production. Harvest from the top to encourage bushy growth. Basil blackens at temperatures below 45F -- bring containers indoors on cold nights in September to extend the season [2][3].

## 7. Brassicas for April Transplant

### Why Start Brassicas Now

Unlike tomatoes and peppers, brassica transplants (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage) are destined for outdoor planting in April, not late May. These are cool-season crops that tolerate frost and thrive in the 50-65F temperature range. The indoor start gives them a 4-6 week head start, producing stocky transplants ready to go out in late April when the weather is still cool enough for optimal growth [1][3][5].

### Brassica Start Specifications

| Crop | Germination Temp | Germination Days | Start Now | Transplant Target |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Broccoli | 60-70F | 5-10 | Yes | Apr 25 - May 10 |
| Cauliflower | 60-70F | 5-10 | Yes | Apr 25 - May 10 |
| Cabbage | 60-70F | 5-10 | Yes | Apr 25 - May 10 |
| Brussels Sprouts | 60-70F | 5-10 | Yes | May 1 - May 15 |
| Kohlrabi | 60-70F | 4-8 | Yes | Apr 20 - May 5 |

### PNW Brassica Notes

Brassicas are the transition crops -- cool-season plants that bridge the gap between spring's direct-sown greens and summer's heat-loving transplants. They are also among the most slug-vulnerable transplants in the PNW garden. Transplant as 4-6 inch seedlings with well-developed root systems, and deploy slug defense immediately upon planting [1][3][4][5].

Purple and red cabbage varieties are significantly more slug-resistant than green varieties. Broccoli side shoots continue producing for weeks after the main head is harvested -- do not pull the plant after the first harvest [3][5].

## 8. The May Mistake -- Avoidance Protocol

### What Is the May Mistake?

St. Clare Heirloom Seeds explicitly identifies the "May Mistake" as the most common PNW gardening error: planting warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, basil) outdoors during a warm spell in early May, only to have them stunted by the cool, overcast conditions that typically follow -- often called "June Gloom" in the PNW [1].

### Why It Happens

May in the PNW often begins with a "false summer" -- 3-5 days of 70-75F temperatures that feel like summer has arrived. Gardeners, eager after months of rain, rush their warm-season transplants outdoors. Then the marine layer reasserts itself: overcast skies, highs in the mid-50s, nighttime temperatures in the mid-40s, and occasional drizzle. This is normal May weather in the maritime PNW, but it is devastating for warm-season crops that were expecting sustained warmth [1][2][3].

### The Damage

Warm-season crops planted during early May in the PNW suffer:

- **Growth stalling:** Tomatoes stop growing below 55F nighttime temperatures. They do not die, but they do not grow either. A tomato planted in early May and a tomato planted in late May will be the same size by mid-June [2].
- **Root rot risk:** Cool, wet soil promotes fungal root diseases (Pythium, Fusarium) that would not develop in warm soil. Tomatoes planted in 50F soil are at significant risk [2][3].
- **Blossom drop:** Tomato flowers set fruit in a narrow temperature window (60-85F). Below 55F or above 90F, flowers drop without setting fruit. Early May PNW temperatures are frequently below the fruit-set threshold at night [2].
- **Physiological stress:** Peppers exposed to temperatures below 55F for extended periods develop a purple discoloration on leaves and stems -- a stress response that takes weeks to recover from, even after warm weather arrives [2][3].

### The Protocol

1. **Do not transplant warm-season crops before Memorial Day weekend** (late May) in western Washington. This date is not arbitrary -- it aligns with the statistical onset of reliably warm nighttime temperatures [1][2].
2. **Monitor nighttime lows, not daytime highs.** A 75F day followed by a 48F night is worse for warm-season crops than a 65F day followed by a 58F night [2].
3. **Use soil temperature as the definitive gate.** Do not transplant tomatoes until soil at 4-inch depth has reached 60F for 5 consecutive days. This typically occurs in the last week of May in PNW lowlands [2][3].
4. **Wall-o-Water or cloche as insurance.** If you must plant before Memorial Day, use individual plant protectors that raise nighttime temperatures around the plant by 5-8F [2].
5. **Accept the wait.** Tomatoes planted May 25 in warm soil will outperform tomatoes planted May 5 in cold soil by July 1. The late planter wins [1][2].

## 9. Indoor Growing Setup

### Minimum Equipment for Indoor Starts

| Item | Purpose | Budget Option | Quality Option |
|------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Heat mat | Seed germination (70-80F) | $15-25 single mat | $35-50 thermostat-controlled |
| Grow lights | 14-16 hr light for seedlings | $20-40 shop light with T8 tubes | $50-100 LED grow panel |
| Seed trays | Starting containers | $5-10 recycled containers | $15-25 cell trays with humidity dome |
| Seed starting mix | Sterile, well-draining | $8-12 bagged mix | $15-20 premium blend |
| Timer | Automate light cycle | $5-10 mechanical timer | $15-25 digital timer |

### Setup Notes

- **Heat mat placement:** Under seed trays during germination. Remove (or turn off thermostat) after seedlings emerge -- roots in overly warm soil after germination produces weak, leggy growth [1][2].
- **Light distance:** 2-4 inches above seedling tops. Raise lights as plants grow. If seedlings are stretching (long, thin stems leaning toward light), the lights are too far away [2].
- **Watering:** Bottom-water by filling the tray under the cells rather than watering from above. Bottom watering prevents damping off (a fungal disease that kills seedlings at the soil line) and encourages roots to grow downward [2][3].
- **Air circulation:** A small fan on low speed strengthens stems and prevents fungal disease. Run for 2-4 hours per day [2].

## 10. Transplant Hardening Protocol

### Why Hardening Off Matters

Indoor-grown seedlings are adapted to stable temperature, consistent light, no wind, and high humidity. Moving them directly outdoors is like sending someone from a heated office into a blizzard -- the shock can kill or severely stunt them. Hardening off gradually acclimates seedlings to outdoor conditions over 7-10 days [1][2][3].

### The 10-Day Hardening Schedule

| Day | Location | Duration | Conditions |
|-----|----------|----------|------------|
| 1-2 | Shaded porch, sheltered | 2-3 hours | No direct sun, no wind |
| 3-4 | Partial shade, some breeze | 3-4 hours | Morning sun OK; bring in if below 50F |
| 5-6 | Morning sun, light breeze | 5-6 hours | Introduce direct morning sunlight |
| 7-8 | Full sun, outdoors all day | 8-10 hours | Bring in at night if below 50F |
| 9-10 | Full sun, outdoors day and night | 24 hours | Leave out overnight if above 50F |
| 11+ | Transplant | Permanent | In-ground or final container |

### Hardening Notes

- Reduce watering frequency during hardening to toughen plant tissues [2].
- If leaves wilt during early days, move plants to shade -- they are not ready for full sun yet [2].
- Wind exposure is as important as sun exposure -- stems need to develop structural strength before transplanting [2].
- If a cold snap (below 45F) is forecast during the hardening period, bring plants indoors. Do not risk losing 8 weeks of indoor growing to a single cold night [1][2].

## 11. Succession Planting Through April

### The April Indoor Start Calendar

Beyond the March 26 starts (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant), additional warm-season crops should be started throughout April:

| Week | Start Indoors | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| Apr 1-7 | More tomatoes (second variety), more peppers | Insurance against seedling failure; variety diversification |
| Apr 8-14 | Basil, ground cherries, tomatillos | Basil needs only 4-6 weeks; fast-growing |
| Apr 15-21 | Cucumbers, summer squash (in biodegradable pots) | Handle roots gently; do not disturb when transplanting |
| Apr 22-30 | Winter squash, melons (in biodegradable pots) | Only in warmest PNW microclimates; stretch goal |

### Biodegradable Pots for Cucurbits

St. Clare Heirloom Seeds recommends biodegradable pots (peat pots, CowPots, newspaper pots) for cucumbers, squash, and melons. These crops have extremely sensitive root systems that are damaged by transplanting. Biodegradable pots allow the entire pot to be planted in the ground, eliminating transplant shock [1][5].

### The Succession Principle

Do not put all your eggs in one basket. Start 2-3 tomato varieties, 2-3 pepper varieties, and 2-3 basil plants rather than 6 of the same variety. Different varieties have different temperature tolerances, disease resistance, and maturation timing. A cool summer that kills your San Marzano tomatoes may be perfect for your Stupice. Diversity is insurance [2][3].
