## 1. The PNW Weather Pattern

The Pacific Northwest spring is not a gradual warming. It is a series of storm systems rolling off the Pacific Ocean, each followed by a brief dry window before the next system arrives. The skilled PNW gardener reads this rhythm like a tide chart -- the two-day dry window between Wednesday's departing storm and Sunday's incoming front is the operational window for planting [1][2][3].

This pattern is driven by the jet stream's position relative to the Pacific coast. In late March, the jet stream oscillates between 45N and 50N latitude, steering storm systems through the Pacific Northwest on a roughly 3-5 day cycle. Each storm delivers 0.5-1.5 inches of precipitation to the Puget Sound lowlands, followed by 1-3 days of clearing as the next system organizes over the open Pacific [1][3].

The gardener's advantage: seeds planted during the dry window receive free irrigation from both the departing and arriving storm systems. The soil is workable because the previous rain has had 24-48 hours to drain, and the incoming rain will settle the seeds without the gardener lifting a hose. This is not a metaphor -- it is a literal operational advantage that experienced PNW growers exploit every spring [2][4].

Understanding this rhythm requires fusing data from multiple sources: NOAA for the large-scale pattern, the National Weather Service for the 7-day operational forecast, AccuWeather for daily temperature trends, and WSU AgWeatherNet for soil temperature data that generic forecasts do not provide. The weather intelligence in this module synthesizes all four [1][2][5][6].

This same system delivered 1.2-1.8 inches of rain to the Puget Sound lowlands over 48 hours [7].

For gardeners, this storm context matters: the preceding 48 hours of heavy rain mean Thursday's soil is saturated. Friday morning soil will still be wet at the surface but draining well in raised beds with good structure. By Friday afternoon, raised beds in full sun will be workable. In-ground beds may need until Saturday morning to reach optimal moisture for planting [5][6].

## 3. ENSO Phase Analysis

### Current Status: La Nina to Neutral Transition

The Washington State Climate Office March 2026 newsletter confirms that La Nina conditions persisted through early 2026 but are now transitioning to neutral. The NOAA Climate Prediction Center maintains the La Nina advisory as of late February 2026, but projects neutral conditions by spring with growing confidence in El Nino development by June-August 2026 [8][9][10].

### What La Nina Meant for This Winter

La Nina in the Pacific Northwest historically produces:

- **Normal to below-average temperatures** across the region
- **Above-average precipitation**, particularly in western Washington
- **Above-average mountain snowpack** in the Cascades and Olympics
- **Slightly delayed spring onset** compared to El Nino years

The 2025-2026 La Nina was classified as "weak," meaning departures from normal were modest. Western Washington received approximately 105-115% of normal precipitation through March, consistent with a weak La Nina signal. Mountain snowpack, however, was mixed -- the northern Cascades (Mt. Baker, North Cascades) ran near to above average, while the central and southern Cascades (Mt. Rainier, Mt. Hood) ran below average due to higher-than-normal snow levels during several storm events [8][9].

### What the Transition Means for Planting

The shift from La Nina to neutral ENSO conditions has direct implications for the April-May growing season:

- **Temperature outlook:** The Climate Prediction Center's April 2026 outlook projects increased likelihood of ridging over the western US, bringing warmer than normal conditions. This is favorable for warm-season crop transplanting and accelerates cool-season crop maturation [10].

- **Precipitation outlook:** Below-normal precipitation is projected for April, meaning the reliable free irrigation of March storm cycles will diminish. Gardeners should plan for supplemental watering by mid-April [10].

- **Growing confidence in El Nino by summer:** If El Nino develops by June-August 2026, the PNW summer would tend warmer and drier than average -- favorable for tomatoes, peppers, and other heat-loving crops but requiring more irrigation. This suggests aggressive warm-season planting once the transplant window opens in late May [9][10].

- **The Old Farmer's Almanac concurs:** Their long-range forecast projects April and May cooler-than-normal in northern Washington but warmer in the south, with below-normal precipitation overall. This is broadly consistent with the CPC outlook [11].

### ENSO Timeline

| Period | Status | Confidence | Gardening Impact |
|--------|--------|------------|-----------------|
| Jan-Mar 2026 | Weak La Nina | Observed | Wet March, above-average precip |
| Apr-May 2026 | Neutral | High | Warmer, drier than March; good transplant conditions |
| Jun-Aug 2026 | Possible El Nino | Moderate | Warm summer; aggressive warm-season planting pays off |
| Sep-Nov 2026 | Possible El Nino | Low | Extended warm season possible; late harvests |

*Sources: NOAA CPC [10], WA State Climate Office [8], NIFC NW Predictive Services [9]*

## 4. Soil Temperature Estimation

### Why Soil Temperature Matters More Than Air Temperature

Air temperature determines whether the gardener is comfortable working outside. Soil temperature determines whether seeds will germinate. A 55F sunny day feels perfect for planting, but if the soil at 4-inch depth is still 38F from winter's cold mass, cool-season seeds will sit dormant for weeks rather than days, and warm-season seeds will rot [5][6][12].

Soil temperature lags air temperature by 2-4 weeks in spring. The enormous thermal mass of the earth means that even after several warm days, soil at planting depth (2-6 inches) reflects the average temperatures of the preceding weeks, not the current forecast [5][12].

### Soil Temperature by Bed Type

Late March soil temperatures at 4-inch depth in western Washington lowlands, estimated from WSU AgWeatherNet data and USDA NRCS soil characteristics:

| Bed Type | Est. Temp (F) | Notes | Best Crops |
|----------|---------------|-------|------------|
| Raised bed, full sun | 48-52 | +5-10F over ground; best drainage | All cool-season; earliest starts |
| Raised bed, partial shade | 44-48 | +3-6F over ground | Cool-season greens, peas, favas |
| In-ground, full sun | 42-46 | Standard lowland temp | Most cool-season with patience |
| In-ground, partial shade | 38-42 | Slowest to warm | Only hardiest: peas, favas, radish |
| Under mulch (3-4 inch) | 36-40 | Mulch insulates both ways | Remove mulch 2 weeks before planting |
| South-facing slope | 50-55 | Earliest warming microclimate | Prime tomato/pepper transplant site |

*Sources: WSU AgWeatherNet soil temp summaries [6]; USDA NRCS Seattle Series description [12]; North Bend weather station data March 21, 2026 [5]*

### Interpreting the Numbers

The North Bend weather station data from March 21, 2026 provides a real-world anchor: air temperature 41.8F at 7:55am with 95% humidity, barometric pressure 30.187 inHg and rising, light rain. Mean annual soil temperature for the Seattle soil series (King County) ranges 47-52F, with a 15-30F difference between winter and summer means [5][12].

Key thresholds for PNW gardeners:

- **35F:** Absolute minimum for any seed germination (peas, favas only)
- **40F:** Peas, spinach, radish, lettuce will germinate (slowly)
- **45F:** Most cool-season crops germinate within 7-14 days
- **50F:** Optimal for cool-season germination; potatoes go in
- **60F:** Cool-season crops germinate rapidly; warm-season transplant minimum
- **70F:** Warm-season seed germination (tomatoes, peppers indoors)

As of March 26, 2026, only raised beds in full sun are reliably above 48F at planting depth. In-ground beds in partial shade are marginal for anything except the hardiest crops. This is normal for late March in western Washington -- the soil temperature crossover to "plant anything cool-season" typically occurs in the first two weeks of April [6][12].

| Vancouver, WA | Mar 20 | Apr 5 | Apr 25 | Past average; moderate risk |

*Sources: NOAA NCEI 1991-2020 normals [13]; Garden.org frost date calculator [14]*

### Interpreting Frost Dates for Planting Decisions

The "average last frost date" is the 30% probability threshold -- meaning there is a 30% chance of frost occurring after that date. For cool-season crops with frost tolerance (peas, spinach, kale, radish), planting at or before this date is appropriate because these crops tolerate light frost (28-32F) without damage [13][14].

For frost-sensitive transplants (tomatoes, peppers, basil), the 90% safe date is the conservative target. In Seattle, that is April 28 -- a full month from now. This is why warm-season crops are indoor starts in late March, not outdoor transplants [13][14].

### The Warming Trend

UW climate research documents a consistent trend toward earlier spring onset across Washington since the 1960s, with last frost dates shifting earlier at most long-term monitoring stations. SeaTac is noted as a "known warm spot" for minimum temperatures in the Puget Sound lowlands, benefiting from urban heat island effect and proximity to Puget Sound's thermal mass [8][15].

This trend does not eliminate frost risk -- it shifts the probabilities. A 2026 gardener in Seattle can plant frost-tolerant crops with reasonable confidence that the March 16 average last frost has likely passed. But a hard freeze (below 28F) remains possible through April in exposed locations, and the March 26 forecast low of 36F is a reminder that near-frost temperatures are still occurring [1][8].

## 6. The Wet-March-to-Dry-April Transition

### The Inflection Point

The ENSO transition from La Nina to neutral typically produces a marked shift in Pacific Northwest weather patterns between March and April. March is the last reliably wet month; April begins the gradual drying that continues through September [8][10].

For the PNW gardener, this inflection point creates a strategic opportunity:

- **March planting** catches free irrigation from storm systems. Seeds sown in late March receive 1-3 inches of natural precipitation before April arrives. The gardener pays nothing for water, and the soil stays consistently moist during the critical germination period [2][4].

- **April planting** requires supplemental watering. As storm frequency decreases, the intervals between rain events extend from 3-5 days to 7-10 days. Seeds sown in early April may need hand watering during dry stretches to maintain germination moisture [2][4].

- **The optimal strategy:** Plant cool-season crops that need consistent moisture (carrots, parsnips, spinach) in late March to catch the free irrigation. Plant crops that tolerate drier conditions (peas, beans, potatoes) in early April when the soil has warmed another 3-5 degrees [2][4][6].

- Start pepper seeds indoors on heat mat (75F soil target)

- Review slug defense supplies: iron phosphate bait, copper tape
- Sort seed packets by planting sequence: direct-sow first, indoor starts second

**Friday, March 27 -- Prime Window Day 1**
- AM: Direct-sow peas (snow, snap, shelling) in raised beds
- AM: Direct-sow fava beans along fence lines or supports
- Midday: Plant potato sets in prepared trenches (chitted/pre-sprouted preferred)
- PM: Direct-sow radish, spinach, lettuce in raised beds
- PM: Set out onion starts and shallot sets
- Evening: Deploy iron phosphate slug bait around all newly planted areas

**Saturday, March 28 -- Prime Window Day 2**
- AM: Complete dormant pruning on apple, pear, and plum trees
- AM: Apply dormant oil spray to all fruit trees (before bud break)
- Midday: Direct-sow carrots, beets, turnips in prepared beds
- PM: Plant hardy herbs: cilantro, dill, parsley, chervil direct-sow
- PM: Divide and transplant existing chive, mint, oregano clumps
- PM: Plant companion species at fruit tree drip lines (comfrey, chives, clover)

**Sunday, March 29 -- Rain Returns (Indoor/Covered Tasks)**
- Start basil seeds indoors (requires warmth)
- Start brassica transplants: broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage (for April transplant)
- Apply copper tape barriers to raised bed edges
- Plan succession planting schedule for April lettuce, radish, arugula

**Monday, March 30 -- Rain Day**
- Review and update garden journal
- Research companion planting layouts for orchard guild
- Order any needed seeds for April succession planting
- Indoor seed care: check heat mat temperatures, mist seedlings

**Tuesday, March 31 -- Showers, Possible PM Clearing**
- If afternoon clears: direct-sow arugula, mustard greens, mache
- Deploy codling moth traps in orchard (timing: before bud break)
- Evening patrol for slugs (wet weather = peak activity)

**Wednesday, April 1 -- Variable**
- If dry: sow kale, chard, and remaining salad greens
- Mulch newly planted areas with 1-2 inches of compost
- Inspect fruit trees for fire blight cankers; prune and destroy any found
- Plan the April 3-4 planting window (next dry spell)

## 8. Snowpack and Water Supply Context

### Why Snowpack Matters to Gardeners

PNW gardeners in the lowlands rarely think about snowpack, but mountain snow is the region's water reservoir. The snowpack accumulated in the Cascades and Olympics between November and March melts slowly through spring and summer, feeding rivers, recharging aquifers, and ultimately supplying the municipal water systems that fill garden hoses in July and August [8][9].

### 2025-2026 Snowpack Status

The NIFC NW Predictive Services report (February 2026) documents a mixed snowpack season shaped by the weak La Nina:

- **Northern Cascades (Mt. Baker, North Cascades):** Near to above average. The March 24-26 storm added 18-25 inches at ski area elevation, pushing the northern Cascades snowpack to approximately 110% of median [7][9].
- **Central Cascades (Snoqualmie Pass, Mt. Rainier):** Near average. Several mid-winter storms delivered rain rather than snow at pass elevation (3,000-4,000 ft), limiting accumulation despite adequate total precipitation [9].
- **Southern Cascades (Mt. Hood, Mt. Jefferson):** Below average. Oregon experienced the lowest snowpack ratios in the region, with some stations running 75-85% of median as of March 1 [9].
- **Olympics:** Variable by aspect. West-facing slopes at or above average; east-facing slopes below average due to rain shadow effects [9].

### Implications for Summer Watering

Below-average snowpack in the central and southern Cascades suggests earlier snowmelt and potentially restricted water supplies by late summer. Gardeners should plan for:

- **Rain barrel installation** during the March wet season to capture free irrigation water for July-August use [8].
- **Mulch investment** -- 3-4 inches of mulch reduces summer watering needs by 50-70% through moisture retention [8].
- **Drip irrigation conversion** -- drip systems use 30-50% less water than overhead sprinklers and deliver water directly to roots where plants can use it [8].
- **Drought-tolerant variety selection** for summer crops, particularly if ENSO transitions to El Nino by June, which would amplify the warm, dry summer trend [9][10].

*Sources: NIFC NW Predictive Services [9]; WA State Climate Office [8]; OpenSnow 2025-2026 Update [16]*
