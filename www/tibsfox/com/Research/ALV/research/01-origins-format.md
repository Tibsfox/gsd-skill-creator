# Origins & Format -- Almost Live! Founding 1984-1999

## The Pilot (September 23, 1984)

The pilot episode of Almost Live! (initially called "Take 5") aired September 23, 1984, on KING-TV, Seattle's NBC affiliate. The show was created by KING VP of Programming Bob Jones, who saw an opportunity to fill the local late-night gap with something that could compete against national programming by being aggressively, unapologetically local.

The title "Almost Live!" was coined by original head writer Jim Sharp, who described it as "subtle, really easy, humorous if you think about it." The name carried a double meaning -- the show was taped earlier in the day but presented as though it were live, and the energy of the production walked the line between polished television and the rawness of a live broadcast. That tension between professional production values and improvisational looseness would define the show's identity for fifteen years.

KING-TV (Channel 5) was Seattle's NBC affiliate and the flagship station of the King Broadcasting Company, founded by Dorothy Stimson Bullitt in 1948. The station had a long tradition of local programming investment that set it apart from most network affiliates, which were already trending toward syndicated content by the mid-1980s. KING's commitment to local production was not merely corporate policy -- it was the Bullitt family's editorial philosophy, a belief that television stations owed their communities more than network pass-through and local news.

The show premiered to modest expectations. Nobody at KING-TV in September 1984 imagined that "Take 5" would run for fifteen seasons, accumulate over 100 Emmy Awards, and launch careers that would span from PBS to Hollywood to the White House. The ambition was simply to make something local that was worth watching.

That modest ambition, sustained over fifteen years, would produce results that no grand plan could have engineered. The show's longevity itself became a competitive advantage -- by the time it hit its stride in the early 1990s, it had built an institutional memory, a loyal audience, and a production rhythm that newer competitors could not replicate.

## The Letterman Model

The show was initially patterned after Late Night with David Letterman at the suggestion of director Steve Wilson. The format featured a host behind a desk, local celebrity guests, a live band, and pre-taped video pieces -- a structure familiar to anyone who had watched late-night television, but grounded in Pacific Northwest specificity.

Ross Shafer, who had been running a Puyallup stereo store and pet shop while doing stand-up comedy on the side, was selected as host after an audition process that drew from Seattle's small but active comedy community. Shafer brought a salesman's charisma and an everyman quality that connected with local audiences. He was not a polished Hollywood performer -- he was recognizably one of them.

The early writing staff included Jim Sharp as head writer, with contributions from the cast members themselves. The writers' room operated more like a comedy club green room than a traditional television writing operation, with ideas pitched informally and sketches often developed from local observations and personal experiences.

## Early Success and the Louie Louie Campaign

Almost Live! became popular quickly, winning nearly 40 Northwest Emmy Awards in its first seasons. KING-TV expanded it to one hour and aired it twice a week. The show found its audience by being the only television program in the market that spoke directly to Seattle's self-image -- its neighborhoods, its weather complaints, its class anxieties, its provincial pride.

Shafer's most famous publicity stunt was a campaign to make "Louie Louie" the Washington State song, drawing an estimated 5,000 people to the State Capitol in Olympia on April 12, 1985. The campaign was quintessential Almost Live! -- equal parts genuine civic enthusiasm and absurdist comedy, impossible to tell where the joke ended and the sincerity began. The Kingsmen's 1963 recording of the song, with its famously unintelligible lyrics that had triggered an FBI investigation, was already a Pacific Northwest anthem. Shafer's campaign simply made the implicit explicit.

The stunt demonstrated something important about the show's relationship with its audience: Almost Live! did not observe Seattle culture from a distance. It participated in it, amplified it, and occasionally created it.

The show's early success also attracted attention from other markets. Stations in Portland, Spokane, and other Pacific Northwest cities occasionally aired Almost Live! episodes, though the humor's Seattle specificity limited its reach even within the region. A joke about Ballard landed differently in Portland, where the neighborhood had no meaning. This geographic limitation was, paradoxically, the show's greatest strength -- it forced the writing to remain grounded in the specific rather than drifting toward the generic.

## The Keister Transition (1988)

When Shafer left in 1988 to host the Fox Network's The Late Show (a short-lived competitor to Johnny Carson), John Keister assumed the host role. Keister -- a Franklin High School alumnus who had covered music for The Rocket (Seattle's alternative music newspaper) and hosted KING's music video program REV -- brought a fundamentally different comedy sensibility.

Where Shafer was warm and populist, Keister was dry and cerebral. Where Shafer's humor came from enthusiasm, Keister's came from observation. The transition was not smooth. Keister's first season attempted to maintain the talk show format, with results he later described as "falling flat." The guest interviews felt forced, the live band segments lacked the spontaneity they'd had under Shafer, and the audience could sense the mismatch between the new host's strengths and the inherited format.

The struggle of 1988-1989 is significant because it forced the show to reinvent itself. A smoother transition might have preserved the talk show format indefinitely, producing a competent but unremarkable local program. Instead, the friction between Keister's sensibility and the existing format created the pressure that drove the show's most important evolution.

## The Sketch Comedy Transformation (1990)

The pivotal transformation came in April 1990, when the show moved to Saturday 11:30 PM -- a slot requiring special permission from NBC, since it delayed Saturday Night Live by half an hour in the Seattle market. The trial was meant to last six months. NBC agreed because the Seattle affiliate was a strong performer and the request came with data showing local programming outperformed the first half-hour of SNL in the market.

Almost Live! won a national award from the National Association of Television Programming Executives (NATPE) for best local show in America while SNL "tanked" in local ratings. The trial was extended indefinitely. For the remainder of the show's run, Seattle viewers watched Almost Live! at 11:30 and SNL at midnight -- a scheduling arrangement that existed nowhere else in the country and that gave the local show a structural advantage no amount of marketing could have purchased.

The show was shaved to a half hour and converted fully to sketch comedy. The desk was gone. The band was gone. The guest interviews were gone. What remained was a lean, fast-paced sketch show that could be written, rehearsed, and taped in a weekly cycle with a budget that was a fraction of what SNL spent on a single episode.

This format would define the show for the remaining nine seasons and produce its most memorable work.

The April 1990 move was also significant for what it represented about the relationship between local and national programming. KING-TV was not rebelling against NBC -- it was negotiating from a position of strength. The station's willingness to delay SNL for a local show, and NBC's willingness to accept that arrangement, reflected a broadcasting ecosystem where affiliates still had meaningful autonomy. That autonomy would erode dramatically over the following decades as networks tightened control over their affiliates' schedules.

## The Production Model

The weekly production cycle operated on a rhythm:
- **Monday:** Writers' room pitching session, reviewing local news and events for material
- **Tuesday-Wednesday:** Script development and revision
- **Thursday:** Rehearsal and blocking at the KING-TV studios
- **Friday:** Taping day, typically two complete shows taped back-to-back
- **Saturday:** Air date at 11:30 PM

The cast doubled as writers, with most performers contributing sketch ideas and dialogue. This blurred the line between writing and performing in ways that gave the show an organic quality -- performers wrote for their own strengths, and the best sketches often emerged from a cast member's personal observation about Seattle life.

Bill Stainton served as producer throughout much of this period, managing a production that operated on what he later estimated was roughly one-twentieth of SNL's per-episode budget. The constraint was generative: with no money for elaborate sets or guest stars, the show had to be funny on the strength of writing and performance alone.

## Comedy Central Syndication (1992-1993)

In summer 1992, the show taped 65 special episodes for Comedy Central, bringing Almost Live! to a national cable audience. The same year, Microsoft, Starbucks, and the national grunge explosion were all amplifying Seattle's profile. The Comedy Central deal represented the show's only significant national exposure and introduced its hyperlocal humor to viewers who had never heard of Ballard or Mercer Island.

The syndication experiment revealed an interesting dynamic: jokes that were written for an audience of Seattleites often landed with national viewers who recognized the archetypes even if they didn't know the specific neighborhoods. The dynamics of gentrification, suburban pretension, and regional identity that Almost Live! satirized were not unique to Seattle -- they were universal patterns rendered in specific, local detail.

Jim Sharp, the show's original head writer, left for Comedy Central around this period, eventually becoming Senior Vice President -- a career trajectory that demonstrated how the show functioned as an informal talent pipeline for the national comedy industry.

The 65 Comedy Central episodes were a distinct production run -- not simply reruns of the KING-TV broadcasts. The episodes were tailored for a national audience, though the show resisted the temptation to explain its local references. Instead, it trusted that specificity would translate. A joke about Ballard driving did not need a footnote explaining where Ballard was; the comedy of slow, cautious, infuriating driving was universally legible. The decision not to dilute the local flavor for national consumption was both an artistic choice and a commercial risk that paid off.

## The Ratings Paradox

Almost Live! presents a paradox that illuminates the economics of local television. The show was consistently the number-one program in its time slot in the Seattle market. It won more regional Emmy Awards than any other local show. Its audience was loyal, engaged, and demographically valuable. By every metric that should matter in television, Almost Live! was a success.

Yet the show was canceled -- not for poor performance but because its excellent local performance did not translate into the kind of revenue that a corporate owner optimized for national scale could justify. The show's budget was modest and its profitability was real, but a corporate parent comparing the return on local comedy production to the return on syndicated content or national advertising saw an asset underperforming its theoretical potential.

This paradox -- a show that succeeds completely on its own terms but fails to meet the terms imposed by corporate ownership -- became a recurring pattern in American media. Almost Live!'s cancellation in 1999 anticipated the decimation of local news, local radio, and local cultural programming that would accelerate over the following two decades.

## The SNL Comparison

The comparison to Saturday Night Live is unavoidable and illuminating. Both shows were weekly sketch comedy programs. Both developed recurring characters. Both launched careers. But the differences are more revealing than the similarities:

| Dimension | SNL | Almost Live! |
|---|---|---|
| Budget per episode | ~$1.5M (1990s) | ~$75K estimated |
| Cast source | National auditions, improv schools | Local comedy clubs, radio, engineering |
| Writing staff | 15-20 dedicated writers | Cast members who doubled as writers |
| Guest hosts | A-list celebrities | None (self-contained ensemble) |
| Audience | National (30M+ viewers peak) | Regional (~200K Seattle market) |
| Career pipeline | Direct to film/TV stardom | Indirect, through intermediate steps |

Almost Live! could not compete with SNL on any conventional metric. It competed by being something SNL could never be: genuinely local, genuinely specific, and genuinely connected to the community it served.

The scheduling arrangement itself told the story. For nine years, Seattle viewers watched their local show first and SNL second. The implicit message -- that Almost Live! was the appetizer and SNL the main course -- was inverted by the ratings: local audiences consistently preferred the local show. NBC tolerated this arrangement because the alternative was losing the affiliate's cooperation entirely.

## The Emmy Record

Almost Live! accumulated over 100 Northwest Regional Emmy Awards during its fifteen-year run -- a total that dwarfed every other local program in the region's history. The Emmys were not participation trophies; the Pacific Northwest chapter covered a competitive market that included stations in Seattle, Portland, Spokane, and Boise.

The show's Emmy categories spanned writing, performance, directing, and production -- reflecting the breadth of talent involved. Individual cast members and writers won repeatedly: Pat Cashman, John Keister, Bill Stainton, and the writing team were perennial nominees. The cumulative total became a point of pride for KING-TV and a recruitment tool for attracting new talent to the show.

The regional Emmy dominance also highlighted a structural irony: Almost Live! was the most awarded local program in the Pacific Northwest but received zero recognition from the national Television Academy. The show existed in a gap between local and national television that the industry's award structures were not designed to recognize.

## Format Innovations

Several of Almost Live!'s format choices were ahead of their time or distinctive within the sketch comedy genre:

- **No guest hosts:** Unlike SNL's celebrity-dependent model, Almost Live! was entirely self-contained. The ensemble carried every episode without external star power, forcing the show to succeed on writing and performance rather than celebrity curiosity.
- **Topical speed:** Because the show taped weekly with a small team, it could respond to local events faster than any other comedy program. A news story on Monday could become a sketch by Friday.
- **Direct audience address:** The Lame List and similar segments broke the fourth wall regularly, speaking directly to the Seattle audience as peers rather than performing for a generic national viewership.
- **Location shooting:** The show frequently shot on location throughout Seattle, using real neighborhoods as backdrops rather than relying entirely on studio sets. This gave sketches a documentary quality that studio-bound shows could not match.
- **Minimal set design:** Budget constraints forced creative solutions -- sketches were staged with minimal props and sets, putting the emphasis on performance and writing. This constraint became an aesthetic: the show looked handmade in a way that felt authentic rather than cheap.

## The Audience Relationship

Almost Live!'s relationship with its audience was qualitatively different from that of a national show. The audience was not an abstraction -- it was a specific community of people who lived in the same neighborhoods, drove the same roads, and shared the same complaints that the show satirized.

This proximity created a feedback loop that national shows could not replicate. When the Lame List named a local business or neighborhood, the people involved watched. When a sketch parodied a specific Seattle behavior, the people who exhibited that behavior were in the audience. The comedy was not about distant targets -- it was about the audience itself, reflected back with enough affection to prevent the humor from becoming cruel.

The show also generated community events. Viewers organized watch parties. Catchphrases entered daily conversation. The Lame List became a genuine social force -- businesses lobbied to avoid it, and neighborhoods took perverse pride in being named. Almost Live! was not just television that Seattle watched; it was a civic institution that Seattle participated in.

Fan loyalty persisted long after the show's cancellation. The 2024 MOHAI exhibit drew crowds that surprised the museum's planners, and online communities dedicated to the show continued to share clips and memories decades after the final episode aired. The depth of this attachment suggests that Almost Live! filled a need that Seattle has not found another way to meet.

## The KING Broadcasting Context

Understanding Almost Live! requires understanding KING Broadcasting. Founded by Dorothy Stimson Bullitt, KING was one of the last family-owned broadcasting companies in America. The Bullitt family's commitment to local programming was not just a business strategy -- it was an editorial philosophy rooted in the belief that a television station had obligations to its community beyond profit maximization.

This philosophy created the institutional conditions that made Almost Live! possible. Few other network affiliates in the 1980s and 1990s would have invested in original local comedy programming, let alone sustained it for fifteen years through format changes and host transitions.

When Belo Corporation acquired KING Broadcasting in 1999, that institutional philosophy was replaced by the logic of media consolidation. The cancellation of Almost Live! was one of the first consequences.
