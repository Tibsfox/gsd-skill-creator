# Study Guide: Identify 15 PNW Woods by Sight and Touch

## Field and Shop Identification for the Pacific Northwest Woodworker

Research module WDC-SG-01: a practical identification guide for fifteen wood species commonly encountered in the Pacific Northwest, organized for hands-on identification by color, grain, weight, smell, hardness, and common uses. Designed for field walks and shop sorting alike.

**Cross-references:** [WDC-02] Wood Science, [WDC-09] PNW Timber, [WDC-06] Finishing, [WDC-07] Furniture Making, [ECO] Forest Ecology.

**College of Knowledge:** Nature-Studies Department — [plants-fungi], [outdoor-observation]. Trades Department — [woodworking]. Materials Department — [material-properties], [materials-families].

---

## How to Use This Guide

This is a working identification guide, not a botanical reference. For each species, you get the characteristics that matter in the field and at the bench — the things you can see, feel, smell, and scratch with a fingernail without specialized equipment.

The best way to learn wood identification is to handle actual samples. Build a reference collection: label pieces of each species and keep them where you can compare new mystery wood against known specimens. Lumber yards, woodworking clubs, arborist crews, and firewood piles are all sources of identified samples.

The self-test at the end describes fifteen samples without naming them. See how many you can identify from the descriptions alone.

## The Fifteen Species

### 1. Douglas Fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*)

**The PNW's defining timber tree.** If you have been inside a building in the Pacific Northwest, you have been inside Douglas fir.

**Color:** Heartwood ranges from yellowish-tan to reddish-brown, often with a warm salmon or orange cast that deepens with age and light exposure. Sapwood is a narrow band of pale yellowish-white. Old-growth Douglas fir has a distinctly warmer, redder tone than second-growth.

**Grain pattern:** Pronounced, straight grain with strong contrast between earlywood (soft, pale) and latewood (hard, dark). Flat-sawn boards show bold, cathedral-like arches. Quarter-sawn boards show tight, parallel lines and sometimes display a subtle figure.

**Weight:** Medium — noticeably heavier than cedar, lighter than maple. A 3/4-inch board feels substantial in your hands.

**Smell:** Fresh-cut Douglas fir has a resinous, piney tang — sharp and clean, not sweet. The smell is strongest when you plane or sand across fresh wood. Dried lumber retains a faint resinous scent.

**Hardness:** Moderate for a softwood. Your fingernail will leave a mark with firm pressure, but the latewood bands resist indentation. The hardness varies dramatically between earlywood and latewood, which is why Douglas fir is hard to sand smooth — the soft earlywood sands faster, leaving the latewood bands standing proud.

**Common uses:** Structural framing (the backbone of PNW construction), timber framing, exterior decking, interior trim and molding, plywood, boat building (masts, spars). Reclaimed old-growth Douglas fir is prized for furniture — tight grain, rich color, free of knots.

### 2. Western Red Cedar (*Thuja plicata*)

**The smell gives it away every time.** Western red cedar is identifiable at twenty feet by nose alone.

**Color:** Heartwood is a warm reddish-brown, often with streaks of darker brown and lighter pinkish tan. The color is highly variable even within a single board. Sapwood is a thin band of creamy white. Over time, unfinished cedar exposed to weather turns silver-gray — one of the most beautiful aging processes in any wood.

**Grain pattern:** Straight, even grain with subtle distinction between earlywood and latewood. The grain lines are tight and uniform. Cedar lacks the bold figure of Douglas fir — it has a quieter, more refined grain.

**Weight:** Light. Notably, surprisingly light. Pick up a cedar board after handling fir or maple and the difference is immediate. Cedar's low density is one of its defining characteristics — it makes the wood easy to work but prone to crushing under concentrated loads.

**Smell:** Unmistakable. A sweet, aromatic, almost spicy scent that is one of the most recognizable wood smells in the world. The scent comes from thujaplicin, a natural oil that also provides the wood's famous decay resistance. Fresh-cut cedar fills a room with its smell. Even old, dry cedar releases the scent when sanded or scratched.

**Hardness:** Soft. Your fingernail leaves a clear impression with light pressure. Cedar dents easily and is unsuitable for surfaces that take impact or heavy use. This is a finish-and-protect wood, not a workhorse structural wood (despite its extensive use in siding and fencing, where its decay resistance matters more than its hardness).

**Common uses:** Exterior siding and shingles (natural decay resistance makes it last decades without preservative treatment), fence boards, deck boards, outdoor furniture, closet lining (the scent repels moths), interior paneling, canoe and kayak building, Coast Salish cultural carving (totem poles, longhouse planks, bentwood boxes).

### 3. Sitka Spruce (*Picea sitchensis*)

**The tonewood.** Sitka spruce has the highest strength-to-weight ratio of any commercial softwood, which is why it built airplanes and guitars.

**Color:** Heartwood is a pale pinkish-white to light yellowish-tan — lighter than Douglas fir, warmer than hemlock. Sapwood is nearly indistinguishable from heartwood. The overall appearance is bright and clean.

**Grain pattern:** Straight, fine, and even. The distinction between earlywood and latewood is moderate — less pronounced than Douglas fir but more visible than cedar. Quarter-sawn Sitka spruce has a remarkably uniform, tight grain.

**Weight:** Light to medium — lighter than Douglas fir, heavier than cedar. The combination of light weight and high stiffness is what makes Sitka spruce exceptional.

**Smell:** Mild, slightly resinous, and not particularly distinctive. Compared to the bold scent of cedar or the tangy resin of Douglas fir, Sitka spruce smells neutral — clean wood with a faint sweetness.

**Hardness:** Soft to moderate. Slightly harder than cedar, softer than Douglas fir. Your fingernail will dent it with moderate pressure.

**Common uses:** Instrument soundboards (guitar tops, piano soundboards, violin bellies — the acoustic properties of Sitka spruce are legendary), aircraft construction (World War II Mosquito bomber, Howard Hughes' Spruce Goose), boat building (planking and spars), ladder rails, oars. Sitka spruce grows to enormous size in the PNW coastal rainforests — specimens over 200 feet tall and 15 feet in diameter have been recorded.

### 4. Western Hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*)

**The workhorse softwood.** Western hemlock does everything Douglas fir does, a little less spectacularly, for a little less money.

**Color:** Heartwood is pale brown to light reddish-brown, without the warm orange tones of Douglas fir. Sapwood is nearly indistinguishable from heartwood. The overall impression is cooler and paler than fir.

**Grain pattern:** Straight and fairly fine, with moderate earlywood/latewood contrast. Less bold than Douglas fir. Flat-sawn boards show subtle, understated grain patterns that some prefer for their quietness.

**Weight:** Medium — similar to Douglas fir, perhaps very slightly lighter. Not a helpful distinguishing characteristic.

**Smell:** Nearly odorless when dry. Fresh-cut hemlock has a faint, slightly sour scent — not unpleasant, but not as characterful as cedar or fir. The lack of smell is actually a useful identification trait — if a pale softwood smells like almost nothing, it may well be hemlock.

**Hardness:** Moderate — comparable to Douglas fir. Slightly harder than spruce, slightly softer than the latewood bands in fir.

**Common uses:** Construction lumber (framing, studs, joists), plywood, pulp and paper (hemlock is one of the dominant pulp species in the PNW), interior trim, door and window frames, crates and pallets.

---

### 5. Big-Leaf Maple (*Acer macrophyllum*)

**The PNW's premier hardwood.** Big-leaf maple is the only native Pacific Northwest hardwood commonly available in commercial quantities for furniture and woodworking.

**Color:** Heartwood is a warm creamy-white to light reddish-tan. Sapwood is paler but similar. The overall color is lighter than Eastern hard maple. With a clear finish, big-leaf maple glows with a warm, honey-like luminosity. Spalted big-leaf maple (partially decayed by fungi) displays dramatic black zone lines against the pale background — highly prized for decorative work.

**Grain pattern:** Variable, and this is big-leaf maple's great distinction. Straight-grained boards are common and unremarkable. But figured big-leaf maple — quilted, curly, birds-eye, or blistered — is among the most spectacular figured wood on earth. Quilted maple displays three-dimensional bubble-like patterns that seem to shift and move under changing light. Curly (fiddleback) maple shows undulating stripes across the face.

**Weight:** Medium to heavy. Noticeably heavier than any of the softwoods listed above. A big-leaf maple board feels dense and solid.

**Smell:** Minimal when dry. Fresh-cut maple has a faint, slightly sweet smell. Not a useful identification characteristic.

**Hardness:** Moderate hardwood. Harder than all PNW softwoods, softer than oak or Eastern hard maple. Your fingernail cannot dent it under normal pressure.

**Common uses:** Furniture (especially when figured), musical instruments (electric guitar bodies, drum shells), turned bowls and vessels, cutting boards, flooring, cabinetry. Quilted and curly big-leaf maple commands premium prices in the specialty lumber market.

---

### 6. Red Alder (*Alnus rubra*)

**The quiet utility player.** Red alder is the most common hardwood tree in the PNW, often dismissed as "weed wood" and dramatically underrated.

**Color:** Heartwood is pale yellowish-white to light reddish-brown when freshly cut, darkening with exposure to air and light to a warm medium-tan, sometimes with pinkish or reddish overtones. Sapwood is barely distinguishable from heartwood. The color is uniform and even — no dramatic grain contrast.

**Grain pattern:** Straight, fine, and even — almost featureless. The grain is subtle to the point of near-invisibility. This uniformity is actually a virtue: alder takes stain more evenly than almost any other wood, which is why it is the furniture industry's go-to substrate for stained finishes. It can be stained to mimic cherry, walnut, or mahogany convincingly.

**Weight:** Light to medium for a hardwood. Lighter than maple, heavier than cedar. It feels "just right" in the hand — not heavy, not flimsy.

**Smell:** Nearly odorless. Fresh-cut alder has no distinctive scent. This is a reliable identification trait — if a reddish-tan hardwood smells like almost nothing, alder is a strong candidate.

**Hardness:** Soft for a hardwood. Softer than maple, harder than all PNW softwoods. Your fingernail can leave a slight mark with firm pressure. Alder machines easily and cleanly — it does not dull tools quickly.

**Common uses:** Cabinet frames and doors (the commercial cabinetry industry uses vast quantities of alder), furniture (especially stained pieces), turned ware, smoking wood for salmon (alder-smoked salmon is a PNW culinary tradition), firewood.

### 7. Oregon White Oak (*Quercus garrulus*)

**The PNW's native oak.** Less famous than Eastern white oak, but structurally and visually its equal.

**Color:** Heartwood is medium to dark brown with olive or yellowish undertones. Sapwood is a narrow band of light brown to nearly white. The heartwood darkens richly with age and finish — old Oregon white oak approaches the color of dark honey.

**Grain pattern:** Ring-porous — meaning the earlywood pores are large and visible, creating prominent grain lines on flat-sawn surfaces. The grain is bold and dramatic. Quarter-sawn Oregon white oak displays medullary rays (flecks of reflected light that flash across the surface), though less prominently than Eastern white oak.

**Weight:** Heavy. Oregon white oak is dense and solid. Pick up an oak board after handling alder or maple and the heft is immediately apparent.

**Smell:** White oak has a distinctive tannic smell — slightly acidic, reminiscent of wine barrels (white oak is the barrel wood for wine and whiskey). The smell is not strong but is recognizable once you know it.

**Hardness:** Hard. Your fingernail will not dent Oregon white oak. It resists impact, wear, and abrasion. It is also hard to work — dull tools tear the grain, and the open pores require filling for a smooth finish.

**Common uses:** Furniture (especially Arts and Crafts style), flooring, barrel making (cooperage), boat building (frames and keels — white oak's natural tyloses make it resistant to water penetration), timber framing, fence posts (heartwood is naturally decay-resistant).

### 8. Pacific Madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*)

**The beautiful outlaw.** Madrone is one of the most visually stunning PNW woods and one of the most difficult to work.

**Color:** Heartwood is a rich reddish-brown to pinkish-tan — warmer and more vivid than any other PNW native wood. Sapwood is a contrasting pale creamy-white. The boundary between heartwood and sapwood is abrupt and visually striking.

**Grain pattern:** Fine, close, and irregular — often interlocked, meaning the grain direction reverses between adjacent growth rings. Interlocked grain produces beautiful figure when planed or finished but is notoriously difficult to work without tearout.

**Weight:** Heavy. Madrone is one of the densest PNW hardwoods. It feels notably heavy for its size.

**Smell:** Minimal and non-distinctive when dry.

**Hardness:** Hard. Comparable to oak. The density and hardness, combined with the interlocked grain, make madrone challenging to machine, plane, and sand. But the finished surface is gorgeous — smooth, warm, and luminous.

**Common uses:** Turned bowls and vessels (the color and grain are spectacular on the lathe), decorative veneer, firewood (madrone produces excellent heat and long-lasting coals), flooring (rare but beautiful). Madrone is not commercially logged in significant quantities — most woodworking stock comes from salvage, land clearing, and small-scale harvesting.

### 9. Black Walnut (*Juglans nigra*) — Planted

**Not native, but thoroughly adopted.** Black walnut trees were planted throughout the PNW by settlers and continue to be cultivated. The wood is the undisputed king of American furniture hardwoods.

**Color:** Heartwood is deep chocolate brown to purplish-brown — rich, dark, and immediately recognizable. Sapwood is a dramatic contrast: pale creamy-white. The heartwood color darkens slightly with age, then gradually lightens and mellows over decades. No other PNW-available wood has this color.

**Grain pattern:** Straight to slightly wavy, with a medium texture. The grain is distinctive but not as bold as oak. Figured walnut (crotch figure, burl, curly) is among the most prized decorative wood in the world.

**Weight:** Medium to heavy. Solid and substantial, but not as heavy as oak or madrone.

**Smell:** Distinctive. Fresh-cut walnut has a mild, pleasant, slightly sweet, earthy scent — not strong, but recognizable. Green walnut is more pungent. The sawdust can cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals.

**Hardness:** Moderate. Harder than alder, softer than oak. A good balance of workability and durability.

**Common uses:** Fine furniture (the premier American furniture wood since the colonial era), gun stocks (the traditional choice — walnut absorbs recoil without splitting), turned ware, musical instruments, veneer, architectural millwork.

---

### 10. Black Cherry (*Prunus serotina*) — Planted

**The wood that ages like wine.** Black cherry is planted in the PNW and available from specialty lumber dealers.

**Color:** Heartwood is a warm pinkish-tan when freshly cut, deepening to a rich reddish-brown over months and years of light exposure. This color change is dramatic and beloved — a cherry table that starts as pale salmon becomes deep burnished mahogany over a decade. Sapwood is a contrasting pale creamy-white.

**Grain pattern:** Fine, straight, and even — delicate and refined. The grain is visible but never bold. Cherry has a satiny quality that catches light beautifully, especially under a clear finish.

**Weight:** Medium. Lighter than walnut or oak, heavier than alder.

**Smell:** Mild and slightly sweet when freshly cut — some detect a faint scent resembling the fruit.

**Hardness:** Moderate. Similar to walnut — workable but not soft. Cherry machines beautifully and takes a hand-plane finish that rivals anything in woodworking.

**Common uses:** Fine furniture (especially Federal and Shaker traditions), cabinetry, musical instruments, turned ware, architectural millwork, carving.

---

### 11. Western White Pine (*Pinus monticola*)

**The gentlest softwood.** Western white pine is the easiest PNW conifer to carve, shape, and work with hand tools.

**Color:** Heartwood is pale straw-yellow to light reddish-brown, aging to a warm honey tone. Sapwood is nearly white and usually wider than the heartwood in younger trees.

**Grain pattern:** Fine, straight, and even — the most uniform grain of any PNW conifer. The earlywood/latewood contrast is minimal, giving western white pine a smooth, almost bland appearance compared to Douglas fir.

**Weight:** Light. Noticeably lighter than Douglas fir. Among the lightest of PNW conifers.

**Smell:** Mild, sweet, piney — pleasant but not as sharp as Douglas fir or as aromatic as cedar.

**Hardness:** Soft. Easily dented by a fingernail. This softness, combined with the fine, even grain, is what makes it ideal for carving and pattern-making.

**Common uses:** Carving, pattern-making (foundry patterns, architectural models), interior trim and molding, match-grade lumber (selected for uniformity), window frames, door panels, knotty pine paneling.

---

### 12. Port Orford Cedar (*Chamaecyparis lawsoniana*)

**The rare treasure.** Port Orford cedar's native range is limited to a narrow strip of southwestern Oregon and far northwestern California. It is one of the most valued softwoods in the world.

**Color:** Heartwood is a pale yellowish-white to light straw-tan — lighter than western red cedar, with a more uniform, less streaky coloration. Sapwood is barely distinguishable.

**Grain pattern:** Extremely fine, straight, and even. The texture is almost creamy — smoother than any other PNW conifer. Quarter-sawn surfaces have a silky luster.

**Weight:** Light to medium — slightly heavier than western red cedar.

**Smell:** A distinctive spicy, ginger-like scent that is entirely different from western red cedar's sweet aromatics. The smell is one of the most reliable identification traits for Port Orford cedar. Once you know it, you cannot mistake it for any other species.

**Hardness:** Moderate for a softwood — harder than western red cedar, comparable to Douglas fir's softer bands.

**Common uses:** Japanese temple construction (Port Orford cedar is the closest available substitute for Japanese hinoki cypress, and significant quantities have been exported to Japan), boat building (planking, decking), arrow shafts (traditional and modern archery), battery separators, specialty outdoor applications.

---

### 13. Pacific Yew (*Taxus brevifolia*)

**The understory legend.** Pacific yew is a small, slow-growing tree of the deep forest understory. Its wood has been valued for millennia for one property above all others: elasticity.

**Color:** Heartwood is a striking orange-brown to purplish-red — one of the most vivid colors of any PNW native wood. Sapwood is a narrow band of pale yellowish-white. The color contrast is dramatic.

**Grain pattern:** Very fine, tight, and often irregular or wavy. The annual rings are extremely close together (yew grows slowly). The surface has a dense, almost oily feel.

**Weight:** Heavy. Pacific yew is among the densest PNW woods — heavier than any native softwood and many hardwoods. It sinks in water.

**Smell:** Minimal — slight sweetness, not distinctive.

**Hardness:** Very hard for a softwood — harder than many hardwoods. Yew resists indentation stubbornly.

**Common uses:** Traditional bows (the English longbow was made from European yew; Pacific yew is equally suitable), canoe paddles (Coast Salish tradition), tool handles, carving, turned objects. Pacific yew bark was the original source of the cancer drug Taxol (paclitaxel). The tree is not commercially logged — specimens are encountered in old-growth forest salvage and occasional windfalls.

---

### 14. Incense Cedar (*Calocedrus decurrens*)

**The pencil wood.** Incense cedar is the wood inside every traditional wooden pencil.

**Color:** Heartwood is light reddish-brown to pinkish-tan, similar to western red cedar but generally lighter and more uniform. Sapwood is creamy white.

**Grain pattern:** Straight, fine, and even — very similar to western red cedar but with less color variation across the grain.

**Weight:** Light — comparable to western red cedar.

**Smell:** Aromatic, sweet, and distinctly "pencil-like" — the scent most people associate with freshly sharpened pencils. Slightly different from western red cedar: less spicy, more sweet and balsamic. If a light, aromatic softwood smells like a pencil sharpener, it is incense cedar.

**Hardness:** Soft. Very similar to western red cedar. Easily dented.

**Common uses:** Pencils (the global standard wood for wooden pencils due to its fine, even grain that sharpens cleanly), fence posts and rails (natural decay resistance), closet lining, exterior siding and decking (in its native range), chests and boxes.

---

### 15. Ponderosa Pine (*Pinus ponderosa*)

**The pine of the dry side.** Ponderosa pine dominates the forests east of the Cascade crest, where the climate is drier and sunnier than the west-side rainforests.

**Color:** Heartwood is a warm yellowish-orange to light reddish-brown — warmer and more orange than Douglas fir, paler than the deep amber of old-growth fir. Sapwood is a wide band of pale cream to yellowish-white. In young trees, the sapwood may constitute most of the board.

**Grain pattern:** Straight, moderate texture, with mild earlywood/latewood contrast. Less dramatic grain than Douglas fir. Flat-sawn boards have a gentle, open grain. Knotty ponderosa pine (with its characteristic dark, tight knots against the pale background) is a distinctive interior finish material.

**Weight:** Light to medium — lighter than Douglas fir, heavier than white pine.

**Smell:** Fresh-cut ponderosa pine has a distinctive sweet, vanilla-like scent — some describe it as butterscotch. This is one of the most pleasant and recognizable wood smells of any species. Large ponderosa pines in sun-warmed forests often release this scent from the bark on warm days.

**Hardness:** Moderate softwood — harder than white pine and cedar, softer than Douglas fir.

**Common uses:** Interior millwork and trim, knotty pine paneling, window and door frames, furniture (especially rustic styles), turned ware, pattern-making. Historically, ponderosa pine was the dominant lumber species in central Oregon and eastern Washington.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Nature-Studies** — [plants-fungi]: Wood identification is applied dendrology — the study of trees through their wood products. Understanding the botanical families (conifers vs. angiosperms), growth habits (understory vs. canopy, fast-growing vs. slow-growing), and native ranges of these species connects shop knowledge to ecological knowledge. A woodworker who can identify fifteen species by sight and touch has a deeper understanding of PNW forests than many hikers.
- **Nature-Studies** — [outdoor-observation]: The smell, texture, weight, and color of wood are observation skills developed through hands-on experience. The self-test in this guide is an observation exercise: synthesizing multiple sensory inputs (color + grain + weight + smell + hardness) to reach an identification. This multi-sensory observation practice transfers directly to field identification of living trees, fungi, soils, and ecosystems.
- **Trades** — [woodworking]: Material selection is the first decision in every woodworking project. Choosing the right species for a project requires knowing each wood's properties — hardness for durability, weight for portability, grain for appearance, decay resistance for outdoor use, workability for the tools and techniques you will employ. This guide is the trades department's material selection reference.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: Every characteristic in this guide — hardness, density, grain pattern, decay resistance, smell — is a material property with a scientific explanation. Cedar's decay resistance comes from thujaplicin. Oak's hardness comes from dense fiber cells. Sitka spruce's strength-to-weight ratio comes from its exceptional stiffness-to-density relationship. Understanding these properties as materials science rather than woodworking lore deepens both the science and the craft.
- **Materials** — [materials-families]: The fifteen species span two major material families: softwoods (gymnosperms — the conifers) and hardwoods (angiosperms — the broadleaf trees). Within each family, species group by density, workability, and use patterns. Recognizing these family relationships helps predict the properties of unfamiliar species by analogy with known relatives.
