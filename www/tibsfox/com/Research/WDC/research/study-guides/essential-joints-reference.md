# Study Guide: The 10 Essential Woodworking Joints

## Visual Reference Card — From Butt Joint to Sliding Dovetail

Research module WDC-SG-02: a practical reference for the ten joints every woodworker needs to know, organized by complexity and application. For each joint: when to use it, strength rating, difficulty rating, tools needed, where it appears in furniture, and a text diagram showing the geometry. Designed for quick reference at the bench and for study before attempting a new joint.

**Cross-references:** [WDC-03] Hand Tools, [WDC-04] Power Tools, [WDC-05] Joinery, [WDC-07] Furniture Making, [WDC-08] Construction & Framing, [WDC-TS-01] Sharpen a Chisel, [WDC-TS-02] First Dovetail, [WDC-TS-03] Mortise and Tenon.

## How to Use This Guide

This is a bench reference, not a textbook. Each joint is described in a format designed for quick consultation:

- **When to use it** — the applications where this joint is the right choice
- **Strength** — rated Low / Medium / High / Very High, based on resistance to pulling apart and racking
- **Difficulty** — rated Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced, based on the precision required and the skills involved
- **Tools needed** — the minimum toolkit for cutting the joint by hand, with power tool alternatives noted
- **Where it appears** — real furniture and construction applications
- **Diagram** — a text representation of the joint geometry (view from the end or exploded view)

Read through all ten joints once to understand the landscape. Then use the reference as needed when planning a project — the "which joint for...?" decision is the first design choice in any woodworking project, and having the full menu in front of you prevents the common mistake of using the same joint for everything.

The self-test at the end presents scenarios and asks you to choose the right joint. It is more useful than it sounds — the ability to select the correct joint for a given application is the mark of a woodworker who understands structure, not just technique.

## 1. Butt Joint

### The Simplest Joint — And the Weakest

**When to use it:** Quick construction, shop jigs, temporary assemblies, rough framing, any application where speed matters more than strength and appearance.

**Strength:** Low. The butt joint relies entirely on glue and/or mechanical fasteners (screws, nails) because the joint surfaces are end grain to long grain or end grain to face grain. End-grain glue bonds are weak — the porous end grain absorbs glue before it can cure, starving the joint of adhesive.

**Difficulty:** Beginner. The only skill required is cutting the pieces to length and aligning them.

**Tools needed:** Saw (any), drill/driver (for screws), square, clamps.

**Where it appears:** Utility shelving, shop jigs, temporary frames, 2x4 framing (with nails or screws), back panels (with glue and brads).

**Diagram (corner butt, end view):**
```
    +----------+
    |          |
    |  Piece A |
    |          |
    +----------+
    |          |
    |  Piece B |
    |          |
    +----------+
End of B meets face of A.
Glue + screws or nails.
```

**Improving the butt joint:** Add a corner block (a triangular or square reinforcement piece glued into the inside corner). Or use pocket screws (Kreg jig), which pull the joint tight and add mechanical strength. A pocket-screwed butt joint is surprisingly strong and is used in face frames, simple cabinets, and production furniture.

## 2. Dado Joint

### The Shelf Joint

**When to use it:** Shelving, bookcase construction, cabinet dividers — any application where a horizontal piece meets a vertical piece at a right angle and must resist downward load.

**Strength:** Medium. The dado (a rectangular groove cut across the grain) provides a mechanical ledge that supports the shelf's weight. The long-grain glue surfaces on the dado walls add bond strength. Under pure downward load (books on a shelf), a dado joint is strong. Under racking load (sideways force), it is weaker.

**Difficulty:** Beginner to Intermediate. Cutting a clean dado requires either a router with a straight bit and a guide fence, or a table saw with a dado blade. By hand, a dado can be cut with a backsaw and chisel — score the walls with a saw, chop out the waste with a chisel.

**Tools needed:** Router + straight bit + fence (power), or backsaw + chisel (hand). Marking gauge, square.

**Where it appears:** Bookshelves, display cases, tool cabinets, desk dividers, entertainment centers. Every bookcase you have ever owned uses dado joints.

**Diagram (cross-section):**
```
    +-----------+
    |           |
    |  Side     |
    |           |
    |  +-----+  |
    |  | ~~~ |  |  <-- Dado groove (1/4 to 3/4 depth)
    |  +-----+  |
    |           |
    |           |
    +-----------+

    ====Shelf====  slides into the dado
```

**Variations:** A *stopped dado* (also called a blind dado) does not extend to the front edge of the side piece — the groove stops short, and the shelf is notched to fit. This hides the joint from the front, producing a cleaner appearance.

## 3. Rabbet Joint

### The Back and Bottom Joint

**When to use it:** Attaching backs to cabinets, bottoms to boxes, joining panels at right angles where one piece overlaps the edge of the other. A rabbet is an L-shaped step cut along the edge or end of a board.

**Strength:** Low to Medium. Better than a butt joint because the rabbet creates a ledge and additional glue surface. But the joint still relies heavily on glue and fasteners. A rabbeted back panel on a bookcase, reinforced with brads or small screws, is standard practice.

**Difficulty:** Beginner. A rabbet is one of the easiest joints to cut — a single L-shaped cut, achievable with a router and rabbet bit, a table saw, or a hand plane (a rabbet plane or a shoulder plane).

**Tools needed:** Router + rabbet bit (power), or rabbet plane (hand), or table saw with the blade set to width and depth. Square, marking gauge.

**Where it appears:** Cabinet backs, box bottoms, picture frames (the rabbet holds the glass and backing), window sashes, door panels.

**Diagram (corner rabbet, end view):**
```
    +---------+
    |         |
    | Piece A |
    |    +----+
    |    |
    +----+----+
         |    |
         | B  |
         |    |
         +----+
The step in A wraps over the end of B.
```

## 4. Mortise and Tenon

### The Structural Backbone

**When to use it:** Frame construction — connecting rails to legs, aprons to posts, stretchers to stiles. Any joint where a horizontal member meets a vertical member and must resist both pulling apart and racking.

**Strength:** Very High. The long-grain glue surfaces of the tenon cheeks bonding to the mortise walls create an extremely strong connection. The shoulder resists racking. The geometry is self-locating. A well-cut mortise and tenon in hardwood, glued with PVA, is stronger than the wood around it — the wood will break before the joint fails.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate. Requires precise layout (marking gauge), controlled chopping (mortise chisel), accurate sawing (tenon saw), and patient fitting. See the [Mortise and Tenon Try Session](../try-sessions/hand-cut-mortise-tenon.md) for the complete guided process.

**Tools needed:** Marking gauge, mortise chisel, mallet, tenon saw, paring chisel, square, marking knife. Power alternative: drill press to hog out the mortise, bandsaw or table saw for the tenon, with chisel cleanup.

**Where it appears:** Every table has four mortise and tenon joints (apron to legs). Every chair has eight or more. Every door frame. Every face frame. Every window sash. The mortise and tenon is the most common structural joint in furniture.

**Diagram (exploded, from above):**
```
    +========+
    | Tenon  |-----+
    | (rail) |     |
    +========+     |
                   |
    +---------+    |
    |  +---+  |    |
    |  |   |  | <--+  Mortise (in leg)
    |  |   |  |
    |  +---+  |
    |  Leg    |
    +---------+
Tenon slides into mortise.
Shoulder seats against leg face.
```

**Variations:** Through tenon (visible on far side), blind tenon (hidden), wedged tenon (mechanically locked), drawbored tenon (pegged with offset holes for self-tightening), haunched tenon (with panel groove), twin tenon (two smaller tenons for wide rails), tusk tenon (removable, locked with a transverse wedge).

## 5. Through Dovetail

### The Showpiece Joint

**When to use it:** Corners of boxes, chests, tool cabinets, and any application where maximum mechanical strength and visible craftsmanship are desired. The interlocking tails and pins resist being pulled apart by geometry, not just by glue.

**Strength:** Very High. The dovetail shape creates a mechanical interlock — the flared tails cannot pass through the narrow spaces between the pins. The joint resists tension in one direction (pulling the tail board away from the pin board) by geometry alone. With glue, it is effectively permanent.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate to Advanced. Requires accurate sawing (dovetail saw), precise chisel work, careful layout, and the patience to fit the joint by paring. See the [First Dovetail Try Session](../try-sessions/first-joint.md).

**Tools needed:** Dovetail saw, chisels (1/4", 1/2", 3/4"), marking gauge, marking knife, square, mallet, coping saw (optional, for waste removal). Power alternative: dovetail jig + router (produces half-blind dovetails, not through dovetails).

**Where it appears:** Blanket chests, tool chests, jewelry boxes, display cases, high-end drawer construction (rear corners), any visible corner joint where the maker wants to declare hand craftsmanship.

**Diagram (corner, from outside face):**
```
    Tail Board
    +--+  +--+  +--+
    |  \  /  \  /  |
    |   \/    \/   |
    +---+------+---+---
    |   /\    /\   |
    |  /  \  /  \  |
    +--+  +--+  +--+
    Pin Board

Tails flare outward (wider at face).
Pins are the narrow pieces between.
```

## 6. Half-Blind Dovetail

### The Drawer Front Joint

**When to use it:** Drawer construction — specifically, joining the drawer side to the drawer front. The tails are hidden on the front face, so the drawer front presents a clean, unjointed surface while the side reveals the interlocking pins.

**Strength:** Very High. Same mechanical interlock as the through dovetail, but concealed on one face.

**Difficulty:** Advanced. Harder than through dovetails because the pin sockets are blind — you must chop waste from closed-ended cavities, working in a confined space. The fit must be precise because there is no opportunity to flush-trim the joint after assembly (the front face is already finished).

**Tools needed:** Dovetail saw, chisels (narrow — 1/4" and 3/8" are essential for clearing sockets), marking gauge, marking knife, square, mallet. Power alternative: dovetail jig + router (the most common use of a dovetail jig).

**Where it appears:** Fine furniture drawers (the front corners). Every high-quality piece of furniture with drawers — from Chippendale to Shaker to contemporary studio furniture — uses half-blind dovetails to join the sides to the fronts.

**Diagram (cross-section from top):**
```
    Front face
    +==========+
    |  Socket  |  Socket
    |  +----+  |  +----+
    |  |    |  |  |    |
    |  | T  |  |  | T  |  T = Tail (from side)
    |  |    |  |  |    |
    |  +----+  |  +----+
    |   Pin    |   Pin
    +==========+
    Drawer Front

Tails housed in sockets that stop
short of front face. Front face is clean.
```

## 7. Box Joint (Finger Joint)

### The Machine-Cut Dovetail Alternative

**When to use it:** Small boxes, shop jigs, decorative containers, any corner joint where maximum glue surface is desired and machine repeatability is more important than hand-cut aesthetics.

**Strength:** High. Enormous glue surface (every finger is a long-grain-to-long-grain bond). No mechanical interlock (the fingers are parallel, not flared), so the joint depends on glue for strength. With glue, extremely strong.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate. Setting up the table saw jig or router jig requires precision (finger width must exactly equal gap width), but once set up, the joint cuts quickly and repeatably.

**Tools needed:** Table saw + dado blade + indexing jig, or router table + straight bit + indexing jig. Hand alternative: mark out the fingers, saw to the lines, chop waste (tedious but possible).

**Where it appears:** Shop jigs and fixtures, craft boxes, jewelry boxes, cigar humidors, speaker cabinets, any application where a strong, visible corner joint is needed and the regular, geometric pattern of interlocking fingers is aesthetically appropriate.

**Diagram (corner, end view):**
```
    Board A        Board B
    +--+  +--+     +--+  +--+
    |  |  |  |     |  |  |  |
    |  +--+  |     |  +--+  |
    |        | <-> |        |
    +--+  +--+     +--+  +--+
    |  |  |  |     |  |  |  |
    +--+  +--+     +--+  +--+

Square fingers interlock.
No flare — strength is from glue.
```

## 8. Bridle Joint

### The Open Mortise and Tenon

**When to use it:** Frame construction — particularly where the joint is at the end of both pieces (a corner bridle) or where a cross-member meets a vertical at a T-junction (a T-bridle). Lighter-duty than a mortise and tenon but faster to cut and more visible.

**Strength:** Medium to High. The bridle joint is essentially an open mortise and tenon — the mortise is cut through the end of one piece, and the tenon fits into it. The long-grain glue surfaces are substantial, and the mechanical interlock resists racking. Weaker than a closed mortise and tenon because the open mortise walls can spread under load.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate. Requires accurate sawing (both pieces involve rip cuts to a line) and chisel work (cleaning the open mortise). Easier than a closed mortise because the open mortise is accessible from all sides.

**Tools needed:** Tenon saw (or backsaw), chisels, marking gauge, square. The same toolkit as a mortise and tenon, with less chopping.

**Where it appears:** Picture frames, mirror frames, small table frames, arts and crafts style furniture (where exposed joinery is a design feature), gate and fence construction, workbench stretcher joints.

**Diagram (corner bridle, end view):**
```
    +---+     +---+
    |   |     |   |
    | A +-----+ A |   A = Open mortise cheeks
    |   |  B  |   |   B = Tenon (from other piece)
    |   +-----+   |
    |   |     |   |
    +---+     +---+
```

## 9. Lap Joint

### Simple, Effective, Underestimated

**When to use it:** Frames, stretcher joints, cross-bracing, face frames, lightweight furniture, garden structures, any application where two pieces cross or meet and you need a flat, flush surface on at least one face.

**Strength:** Medium. A lap joint removes half the thickness of each piece where they overlap, creating a long-grain-to-long-grain glue surface that is surprisingly strong. The joint resists racking reasonably well because of the large glue area. For added strength, pin the joint with a screw or dowel through the overlap.

**Difficulty:** Beginner. A lap joint requires nothing more than sawing to a line and chiseling (or routing) a flat-bottomed recess half the thickness of the stock. It is one of the first joints a beginner should learn — quick, forgiving, and useful.

**Tools needed:** Backsaw, chisel, mallet, marking gauge, square. Power: dado blade on table saw, or router with straight bit.

**Where it appears:** Face frames (where rails cross stiles), workbench bases, picture frames (quick and strong), crossed stretchers, garden gates, lattice work, timber frame scarf joints (at larger scale).

**Variations:**
- *Half-lap* (T-lap): One piece is notched at the end, the other in the middle. The most common form.
- *Cross-lap*: Both pieces are notched in the middle, so they cross flush. Used for X-bracing and crossed stretchers.
- *Dovetail lap*: The halved area is cut with a dovetail shape, adding mechanical resistance to the joint. A refined upgrade for visible locations.

**Diagram (half-lap, from side):**
```
    +--------+        +--------+
    |  +=====|========|=====+  |
    |  | Lap ||overlap|| Lap|  |
    +--+     +--------+     +--+
    
    Each piece is halved in thickness
    where they overlap. Flush on one face.
```

## 10. Sliding Dovetail

### The Shelf-in-Case Joint

**When to use it:** Fixed shelves in bookcases and cabinets, attaching tabletops to pedestals, joining drawer dividers to case sides — any application where a board must slide into a case side and be permanently locked in position.

**Strength:** High. The dovetail-shaped tongue locks into a matching groove and cannot be pulled straight out — it must slide out the way it went in (from front to back). Once assembled and glued, the joint resists both pulling apart and racking. It is a significant step up from a plain dado for structural shelving.

**Difficulty:** Advanced. Cutting a sliding dovetail requires precise routing (a dovetail bit cuts the groove in the case side and shapes the tongue on the shelf end) and careful fitting. The fit must be tight enough to hold without glue (though glue is standard) but loose enough to slide home with hand pressure. Too tight and the joint binds halfway in, which is a disaster (you cannot clamp a sliding dovetail — it must slide). Too loose and it rattles.

**Tools needed:** Router + dovetail bit (14-degree is standard) + edge guide. Hand alternative: cut the groove with a combination of saw and chisel (a dovetail plane, if you have one, is the traditional hand tool for the tongue). Power: router table for the tongue, handheld router with guide for the groove.

**Where it appears:** Bookcase shelving (where the shelf must resist the outward pressure of a full row of books), desk construction (kneespace dividers), display case shelving, traditional cabinet work. The sliding dovetail is less common than the dado but structurally superior.

**Diagram (cross-section):**
```
    +------------+
    |            |
    | Case side  |
    |  +------+  |
    |  /      \  |  <-- Dovetail groove
    | /________\ |
    |            |
    +------------+

      /========\
     / Shelf    \     <-- Dovetail tongue
    /____________\
    
    Tongue slides into groove from front.
    Dovetail shape prevents pullout.
```

## Self-Test: Which Joint For...?

Test your joint selection skills. For each scenario, choose the best joint from the ten above. Answers follow.

**1.** You are building a bookcase and need to attach fixed shelves to the sides. The shelves will support heavy books. Which joint?

**2.** You are building a small jewelry box from walnut. The corners should be decorative, showing off your hand tool skill. Which joint?

**3.** You are building a kitchen table. Four legs, four aprons connecting the legs. The table must resist the racking force of someone leaning on one edge. Which joint?

**4.** You need to attach a plywood back to a cabinet. The back provides racking resistance but is not a visual element. Which joint?

**5.** You are making a picture frame from mitered molding. The miters keep gapping. You want a stronger joint at each corner. Which joint?

**6.** You are building shop jigs from plywood. Speed and repeatability matter. Appearance does not. Which joint?

**7.** You are building a drawer for a dresser. The drawer front must show no joinery. The sides connect to the front with a joint hidden on the front face. Which joint?

**8.** You are building a garden gate from cedar. Two horizontal rails cross two vertical stiles. The joint must be flush on one face for aesthetics. Which joint?

**9.** You are building a workbench and need to join a thick stretcher to the legs. The joint should be bombproof and disassemblable. Which joint variation?

**10.** You are building display case shelving. The shelves must resist the outward pressure of the displayed objects and must not slide out. Which joint?

### Answers

1. **Sliding dovetail** (or dado for lighter loads). The sliding dovetail resists the outward pressure of heavy books better than a plain dado.
2. **Through dovetail.** The visible interlocking tails and pins are the declaration of hand craftsmanship. Walnut end grain in a dovetail is beautiful.
3. **Mortise and tenon.** Four M&T joints (apron to leg) create a rigid frame that resists racking in all directions. This is what the joint was designed for.
4. **Rabbet.** Cut a rabbet along the back edges of the case sides, set the plywood back into the rabbet, and secure with brads or small screws. Clean, simple, functional.
5. **Bridle joint** (or a spline across the miter). A corner bridle at each corner of a picture frame provides far more glue surface than a mitered butt joint and resists the seasonal wood movement that opens miters.
6. **Butt joint** (with screws or pocket screws). Speed and function, not beauty. Save the dovetails for the furniture.
7. **Half-blind dovetail.** The tails from the side are housed in sockets in the front, invisible from the front face. The standard fine-furniture drawer joint.
8. **Lap joint** (half-lap). Rails and stiles halved where they meet, flush on the face side. Strong, simple, appropriate for outdoor use.
9. **Tusk tenon** (a through mortise and tenon variation, locked with a transverse wedge). The wedge can be driven out to disassemble the joint for moving or rebuilding. Traditional workbench and trestle table joint.
10. **Sliding dovetail.** The dovetail geometry locks the shelf in the case, preventing pullout. Superior to a dado for display shelving where items press against the shelf front.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** — [woodworking]: This reference card is the joint-selection curriculum for woodworking. Every project begins with a joint decision, and making the right decision requires understanding the full menu of options. A woodworker who knows only butt joints and pocket screws builds differently from one who knows mortise-and-tenon, dovetails, and sliding dovetails. The vocabulary of available joints defines the vocabulary of available furniture.
- **Trades** — [joinery]: Each of the ten joints embodies a different joinery principle — mechanical interlock (dovetails), glue surface maximization (box joints, lap joints), self-locating geometry (mortise and tenon), and gravity resistance (dado, sliding dovetail). Understanding the principles behind the joints — not just the cutting procedure — enables the woodworker to adapt, combine, and invent joints for novel applications.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: Joint selection depends on material properties. End grain does not glue well (which is why butt joints are weak). Long grain glues excellently (which is why dovetails and mortise-and-tenon joints are strong). Wood moves across the grain but not along it (which is why tabletops are attached with clips that allow cross-grain movement, not glued directly to the apron). Every joint decision is a material decision.
- **Materials** — [wood-properties]: Different species respond differently to different joints. Softwoods (pine, cedar) benefit from wider dovetail angles (1:6) because the softer fibers need more mechanical lock. Hardwoods (oak, maple) use tighter angles (1:8) because the stronger fibers hold with less geometry. Knowing your species informs your joint choices.
