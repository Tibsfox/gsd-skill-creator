# The Adirondack Chair -- A Classic Outdoor Project

## A Chair That Earns Its Place on Every Porch

The Adirondack chair is the most recognizable piece of outdoor furniture in North America. Its profile -- wide, flat arms, a fanned slatted back reclined at a generous angle, a low seat that puts your center of gravity somewhere between sitting and lounging -- is so iconic that a silhouette alone identifies it. No other chair design says "sit down, slow down, stay awhile" with the same authority.

The design works because the geometry works. The seat tilts backward at about 25 degrees. The back reclines at about 100-105 degrees from the seat. This angle puts your weight behind your hips rather than over them, which is why an Adirondack chair is comfortable for hours in a way that an upright dining chair is not. The wide arms (typically 5 to 7 inches) serve as side tables -- a drink, a book, a plate of food, a phone all fit. The low seat (13 to 14 inches from the ground) brings your legs up, encouraging a relaxed, semi-reclined posture. Everything about the design says: you are done working for the day.

Thomas Lee designed the original in 1903 at his summer home in Westport, New York, on the western shore of Lake Champlain in the Adirondack Mountains. He tested various angles and proportions on his family (reportedly making and discarding many prototypes before arriving at the geometry that felt right) and then gave the design to a carpenter friend, Harry Bunnell, who patented it in 1905 and produced them commercially for twenty years. Lee never profited from the design. Bunnell never credited Lee. The chair outlived both of them by a century and counting.

Building an Adirondack chair is a project that every woodworker should attempt at least once. It teaches compound angles (the main challenge), introduces outdoor joinery and finishing, and produces a piece of furniture that will sit on your porch or in your yard for twenty years, slowly silvering in the weather, reminding you every evening that you made something real.

## Materials: Western Red Cedar

### Why Cedar

Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) is the ideal Adirondack chair material for the Pacific Northwest, and the reasons are both practical and poetic.

**Decay resistance:** Cedar heartwood contains thujaplicins -- naturally occurring fungicides that prevent rot and repel insects without chemical treatment. A cedar Adirondack chair left outdoors year-round in the PNW's 40+ inches of annual rainfall will last 15 to 25 years. The same chair in untreated Douglas fir or pine would begin showing rot in 3 to 5 years.

**Weight:** Cedar is light (Janka 350 lbf, specific gravity ~0.32), making the finished chair easy to move. A cedar Adirondack weighs 25 to 35 pounds. The same chair in white oak would weigh 45 to 55 pounds.

**Workability:** Cedar is soft enough to cut cleanly with a jigsaw (which you will use for the curved back slats and arm profiles), drills without splitting, and takes screws without pre-drilling in most cases (though pre-drilling is still good practice, especially near edges).

**Weathering:** Left unfinished, cedar weathers to a beautiful silver-gray that many people consider the ideal look for outdoor furniture. The silvering is a surface-only phenomenon -- UV light degrades the surface lignin, but the structural integrity of the wood is unaffected. A silvered cedar chair is not damaged. It is patinated. Whether you prefer the warm reddish-brown of freshly oiled cedar or the silver of aged cedar is an aesthetic choice, not a quality judgment.

**The PNW connection:** Cedar Adirondack chairs on porches and decks are as much a part of the Pacific Northwest residential landscape as moss on the roof and ferns in the garden. Building one from PNW-grown cedar connects you to a regional material culture that stretches back thousands of years to the Coast Salish canoe builders who understood this tree's virtues long before anyone built a porch to put a chair on.

### Buying Cedar for the Project

You need approximately 20 to 25 board feet of western red cedar in 5/4 (1-1/4 inch rough, approximately 1 inch finished) thickness for a standard Adirondack chair. This includes waste from curved cuts.

**Where to buy:** Home centers carry cedar decking and fence boards, but the quality varies and the selection of widths is limited. For better quality and wider boards (you need some pieces at least 5-1/2 inches wide for the arms), go to a lumber yard: **Dunn Lumber** (Seattle/Bellevue), **Mr. Plywood** (Portland), or any PNW lumber yard that stocks clear and #1 common cedar. Look for all-heartwood boards (uniform dark color, no pale sapwood) with straight grain and minimal knots.

**Cost:** Cedar lumber for one Adirondack chair costs approximately $60-100, depending on the grade and where you buy it. Clear (knot-free) cedar is at the top of that range. #1 Common (occasional small, tight knots) is at the bottom and is perfectly appropriate for outdoor furniture -- the knots add character.

### Alternatives to Cedar

**Pressure-treated lumber** (southern yellow pine or Douglas fir treated with ACQ or CA-B): Cheaper ($30-50 for a chair's worth of material), rot-resistant through chemical treatment, and widely available. The downsides: the chemicals leave a greenish tint that takes a year to weather out before it accepts stain evenly, the wood is heavier and wetter (allow it to dry for several weeks before building to reduce post-construction warping), and the chemical treatment raises environmental concerns for some makers. That said, millions of perfectly functional outdoor furniture pieces have been built from pressure-treated lumber.

**White oak:** Naturally decay-resistant (the heartwood contains tyloses that seal the pores against moisture), hard, and beautiful. White oak Adirondack chairs are heirloom pieces that will last a lifetime. The downsides: white oak is significantly more expensive than cedar ($8-14 per board foot versus $4-8 for cedar), much heavier, and harder to work (curved cuts require a fresh jigsaw blade and patient feed rate). If you want to build one chair that will outlast you, white oak is the material.

## The Build: Key Components

An Adirondack chair has six basic components: the back slats, the seat slats, the arms, the front legs, the back legs, and the stretchers/braces. The compound angle of the back legs is the main challenge.

### Back Slats: The Compound Angle

The back of the chair is a fan of slats (typically 5 to 7 slats, ranging from 2-1/2 to 3-1/2 inches wide) that arc outward from a narrower bottom to a wider top. The top profile of the assembled back is typically a gentle curve -- an arc or a pointed crown.

The compound angle is this: the back is tilted rearward (the recline angle, typically 100-105 degrees from the seat plane), AND the outer slats are canted slightly outward (splayed) to create the fan shape. This means the slots or screw holes that attach the slats to the cross-brace are not perpendicular to the slat faces -- they are angled in two planes.

For a first build, the simplest approach is to screw the slats to two horizontal cross-braces (one near the bottom, one near the top of the back). The bottom brace attaches to the back legs. The top brace is concealed behind the top slat. The slats are screwed to the braces, and the fan is created by spacing the bottoms close together and the tops farther apart. The compound angle resolves itself through the slight flex of the cedar slats as they are drawn into position by the screws.

Cut the top profile (the crown curve) after the slats are assembled. Stack the slats, clamp them together, draw your crown curve across the top with a flexible batten or a freehand pencil line, and cut the curve with a jigsaw. Sand the sawn edge smooth with 80 and then 120 grit on a sanding block.

### Seat Slats

The seat is 5 to 7 slats (typically 5-1/2 inches wide, 3/4 to 1 inch thick) screwed to the seat cross-braces. The seat slats run front-to-back. Leave a 1/4-inch gap between slats for drainage -- water that pools on an outdoor seat accelerates rot and makes the chair uncomfortable to sit on after rain.

The seat front is typically radiused -- the front edge of the outermost seat slat is rounded with a router (1/4-inch roundover bit) or sanded to prevent the sharp edge from digging into the backs of your legs.

### Arms

The arms are the chair's signature feature. They are wide (5-1/2 to 7 inches), flat, and positioned at approximately 24 inches from the ground -- high enough to rest your forearms comfortably, wide enough to hold a drink.

The arm profile (viewed from above) typically tapers from a narrower attachment point at the back to a wider, rounded front. Many plans use a paddle or teardrop shape. Cut the profile with a jigsaw and sand smooth. The arm attaches to the front leg (which supports it vertically) and to the back leg or back assembly (which anchors it at the rear).

### Legs

The **front legs** are straight pieces that support the arms. They are typically 2x4 or 5/4 stock, cut to length and attached to the front of the seat assembly.

The **back legs** are the structural spine of the chair. Each back leg is a single piece that runs from the ground, through the seat, and up to support the back assembly. The lower portion angles backward (creating the seat recline). The upper portion continues upward to support the back. The transition -- where the seat meets the back -- is the compound angle. The back leg piece is cut from a wide board (typically a 1x10 or 1x12) using a template that defines the seat angle, the back angle, and the overall proportions. The jigsaw cut that shapes the back leg is the most critical cut in the project.

Most published plans provide a full-size template for the back legs that you trace onto the cedar and cut out. The **Popular Woodworking Adirondack chair plan** (available at popularwoodworking.com, $8-12) and the **Fine Woodworking Adirondack plan** (finewoodworking.com, $10-15) are both well-tested designs with clear templates and detailed instructions. For a free option, the **Ana White Adirondack Chair plan** (ana-white.com) is a simplified design that is suitable for beginners, though the proportions and angles are less refined than the magazine plans.

### Stretchers and Braces

Cross-braces under the seat connect the back legs to the front legs, providing racking resistance and structural integrity. A rear stretcher between the two back legs (at approximately seat height) ties the assembly together.

## Fasteners: Stainless Steel Only

This is non-negotiable. Standard zinc-plated or galvanized screws react chemically with the tannins and acids in cedar. The reaction produces black stains that bleed out around every screw head and run down the wood surface in streaks. The stains are permanent. They cannot be sanded out because the discoloration penetrates deep into the wood fibers.

**Stainless steel screws** are the solution. Type 305 or 316 stainless steel deck screws will not react with cedar and will not corrode in the PNW's wet climate. They cost more than zinc-plated screws -- approximately $12-18 per pound compared to $6-8 for standard deck screws -- but for a project that uses perhaps a half-pound of screws, the cost difference is $5-6. That is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

Recommended: **GRK RT Composite** (Type 305 stainless, star drive, trim head) or **Hillman Stainless Deck Screws** (#8 x 2-inch and #8 x 1-5/8-inch are the most-used sizes for Adirondack chair construction). Pre-drill every screw hole with a 3/32-inch bit to prevent splitting, even in soft cedar. Cedar does not split easily, but the slats are thin enough that a screw near an edge without a pilot hole is a risk.

## Finishing

### Option 1: Leave Bare (The PNW Classic)

Leave the cedar unfinished and let it weather. Over the first year, the warm reddish-brown color will fade through a blond phase to a uniform silver-gray. The silver-gray color is stable -- it will not change further. The chair's structural integrity is unaffected by the color change. This is the zero-maintenance option, and many PNW homeowners consider the silvered look the most beautiful finish for cedar outdoor furniture.

The silver will be uniform on all surfaces that receive equal UV exposure. Surfaces that are shaded (the underside of the arms, the back of the legs) will weather slower, producing an uneven look for the first year or two. This resolves itself as all surfaces eventually reach the same silver tone.

### Option 2: Penetrating Oil (Warm and Protected)

A penetrating oil finish maintains the warm, natural color of fresh cedar while providing some protection against UV degradation and moisture. This extends the time before the silvering begins but does not prevent it permanently -- the oil finish must be reapplied every 1-2 years to maintain the color.

**Cabot Australian Timber Oil** ($28-35/quart) is the PNW standard for cedar outdoor furniture. It is a penetrating oil finish (not a film finish) that soaks into the wood and provides UV protection and water resistance without forming a surface film that can crack and peel. Available in Natural (transparent, shows the cedar's own color), Honey Teak, Mahogany, and other tones. Apply with a brush or rag, two coats, following the label directions. Reapply annually or when the wood starts to look dry.

**TWP (Total Wood Preservative) 1500 Series** ($35-45/gallon) is another excellent penetrating oil, highly regarded by PNW deck builders and outdoor furniture makers. Available in Natural and several stained tones.

**Watco Exterior Wood Finish** ($12-15/quart) is a more budget-friendly option that provides decent UV protection and is easy to apply.

### Option 3: Paint (The Classic Look)

White-painted Adirondack chairs are an American icon. The paint conceals the wood entirely but provides the most durable protection against UV and moisture. Use a high-quality exterior latex paint (Benjamin Moore Aura Exterior or Sherwin-Williams Duration are the premium options, $50-65/gallon; Behr Ultra Exterior is the solid budget choice, $35-40/gallon). Prime first with an exterior primer that blocks cedar's tannin bleed (Zinsser Cover Stain oil-based primer, $20/quart, is the best tannin-blocking primer available).

Painted Adirondack chairs need repainting every 5-7 years in PNW conditions. The maintenance interval is longer than oil, but the maintenance itself is more labor-intensive (scraping, sanding, repriming any bare spots, repainting).

## The Build in Summary

1. **Acquire materials:** 20-25 board feet of western red cedar (5/4 thickness), stainless steel screws, waterproof glue (Titebond III), sandpaper (80, 120, 150 grit), chosen finish.
2. **Obtain or create plans:** Purchase a tested plan with full-size templates for the back legs and arm profiles.
3. **Mill the stock:** Joint, plane, and rip the cedar to the dimensions specified in the plans. Cedar dust is a respiratory sensitizer -- wear a dust mask or respirator for all milling operations.
4. **Cut the back legs:** Transfer the template to the cedar, cut with a jigsaw, sand smooth. These are the most critical cuts.
5. **Cut the seat slats, back slats, arms, front legs, and braces:** Straight cuts on the table saw, curved profiles with the jigsaw.
6. **Pre-drill all screw holes.** Stainless steel screws are harder than regular steel and can snap if driven into dense grain without a pilot hole.
7. **Assemble the seat frame:** Attach the back legs to the seat cross-braces. Attach the front legs. Screw on the seat slats with 1/4-inch gaps.
8. **Assemble and attach the back:** Screw the back slats to the cross-braces. Attach the back assembly to the back legs.
9. **Attach the arms:** The arms connect the front legs to the back assembly. Check that the arms are level and parallel.
10. **Sand the entire chair:** 120 grit to remove tool marks and soften edges, 150 grit for a smooth surface. Round all sharp edges with sandpaper -- outdoor furniture should have no sharp corners that catch bare skin or snag clothing.
11. **Apply finish:** Two coats of chosen finish, following product directions.

**Time estimate:** Two weekends. The first weekend for milling and cutting all parts. The second weekend for assembly and finishing. Active work time: 10-15 hours.

**Cost estimate:** $80-150 for materials (cedar, screws, finish), plus the cost of plans if purchased. Tools required: table saw, jigsaw, drill/driver, random orbit sander, clamps.

## The Chair on the Porch

There is a moment, after the last screw is driven and the finish has dried, when you carry the chair out to the porch and set it down and sit in it for the first time. The arms are wide enough for a book and a cup of coffee. The back reclines at exactly the angle that says evening. The cedar smells like the tree it was.

You built this. From rough boards, through jigsaw curves and compound angles and stainless steel screws, through two weekends of sawdust and measuring and the particular satisfaction of watching a shape emerge from flat material. It is on your porch now. It will be on your porch for twenty years. It will silver in the rain and warm in the sun and hold the weight of everyone you invite to sit in it.

Cedar Adirondack chairs sit on every porch from Bellingham to Portland. Most of them were bought from a store. Yours was not. Every time you sit in it, you know the difference.
