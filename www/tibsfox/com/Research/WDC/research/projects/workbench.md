# The Woodworker's Workbench — The Most Important Tool in the Shop

## Why the Bench Comes First

A workbench is not furniture. It is a tool — the most important tool in the shop. Every other tool in a woodworking shop depends on the bench. You cannot hand-plane a board without something to hold it. You cannot chisel a joint without something to brace against. You cannot saw accurately without something to clamp to. You cannot assemble a project without a flat, heavy surface to work on. The bench is the foundation, the anchor, the thing that makes all the other things possible.

And yet most hobbyist woodworkers either do not have a proper bench (they work on a wobbly folding table, a repurposed door on sawhorses, or a flimsy commercial workbench that weighs 40 pounds and slides across the floor when you push a hand plane) or they defer building one because it seems too ambitious for a beginner.

This is backwards. The bench should be the first project, not the last. A good bench transforms every other project you will ever build, and it does not have to be complicated, expensive, or made from exotic hardwood. The best beginner workbench design in existence — the Nicholson bench, as refined and popularized by Christopher Schwarz — can be built from construction lumber in a weekend for less than $200.

## Why Commercial Workbenches Fail

The workbenches sold at home centers and in woodworking catalogs — the ones with a price tag of $200-400 and a photo of a smiling person in a clean apron — share three fatal flaws:

**Too light.** A bench that weighs 50 or 80 pounds moves when you push a hand plane across it. It walks across the floor when you chisel. It rocks when you saw. You spend half your working time chasing the bench instead of working on the bench. A proper workbench should weigh at least 200 pounds. 300 is better. Mass is stability. There is no substitute.

**Wrong height.** Commercial benches are almost always too high — 34 to 36 inches, which is kitchen counter height, designed for standing and looking down at work. But a woodworking bench used with hand tools should be lower: knuckle height when you stand straight with your arms at your sides. For most people, that is 31 to 34 inches. At knuckle height, you can put your weight behind a hand plane without hunching your shoulders. You can chop with a chisel using your arm weight instead of just your wrist. You can work for hours without back pain.

**No real work-holding.** Commercial benches have a small, toy-like vise and sometimes a row of dog holes that do not actually line up with anything useful. A real workbench needs a front vise that can clamp work firmly to the front edge of the bench, dog holes that accept holdfasts or bench dogs for securing work on the top, and a planing stop at one end for butting a board against while planing. These three holding methods handle 95% of all workbench operations.

## The Nicholson Bench

The Nicholson bench is named after Peter Nicholson, who published the design in his 1812 carpentry manual. Christopher Schwarz, editor of Lost Art Press and author of "Workbenches: From Design & Theory to Construction & Use," refined the design for modern builders using commonly available construction lumber. The result is a bench that is simple to build, cheap, heavy, flat, and genuinely useful.

The Nicholson is not a Roubo (the French traditional bench with massive laminated slab top and complex leg joinery). It is not a Scandinavian bench with a trestle base and shoulder vise. It is simpler than all of those. It is a thick slab top on stout legs, with a shelf below for mass and storage, a front vise, and a row of dog holes. That is it. No complex joinery. No exotic materials. No apology needed.

### Design Specifications

**Height:** Knuckle height — stand straight, arms at your sides, curl your fingers into a fist. The top of the bench should be at or slightly below your knuckles. For a 5'8" person, this is approximately 32-33 inches. For a 6'0" person, approximately 33-35 inches. Measure yourself. This dimension matters more than any other.

**Width:** 20 to 24 inches. Narrow enough to reach across comfortably (so you can clamp to the far edge without climbing on the bench), wide enough to support large panels and provide a stable work surface. 22 inches is the sweet spot for most people.

**Length:** 60 to 84 inches (5 to 7 feet). Longer is better, within the limits of your shop space. A longer bench supports longer boards, provides more clamping surface, and feels more solid. 72 inches (6 feet) is a good target.

**Mass:** The finished bench should weigh 200 to 300 pounds. This seems absurd until you experience it. A heavy bench does not move. When you push a jack plane across a 200-pound bench, the bench stays still and the plane does the work. When you strike a chisel with a mallet on a heavy bench, the energy goes into the wood, not into vibrating the bench across the floor. Mass is the single most important characteristic of a workbench, and it is free — you get it by using thick lumber and adding a shelf filled with heavy things (planes, hand tools, sharpening stones, a bucket of sand — anything).

## Materials — PNW Douglas Fir

The Nicholson bench is built from construction-grade softwood. In the Pacific Northwest, that means Douglas fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii), and there is no better wood for this purpose anywhere in North America.

### Why Douglas Fir Is Ideal

**Stiffness.** Douglas fir has a modulus of elasticity of approximately 1.95 million psi — stiffer than any other common construction softwood. A bench top made from laminated Douglas fir 2x12s resists deflection under hand-plane pressure better than construction-grade pine, spruce, or hemlock.

**Availability.** Douglas fir is the default framing lumber in the Pacific Northwest. Every lumber yard, every home center, every building supply store has stacks of it. You can pick through the rack for straight, clear boards without special ordering.

**Length.** Douglas fir is routinely available in long lengths — 8, 10, 12, even 16-foot 2x12s. Long, clear boards make bench construction much easier. You can build the entire top from two or three boards without any end joints.

**Cost.** Construction-grade Douglas fir 2x12s cost $1-2 per linear foot at PNW lumber yards (2026 prices). A bench requires approximately 40-50 linear feet of 2x12 and 2x4 lumber. Total lumber cost: $80-150, depending on local pricing and the specific dimensions you choose. This is a fraction of the cost of hardwood lumber for a traditional bench.

**Working properties.** Douglas fir planes well with sharp tools. It accepts holdfasts. It takes a linseed oil finish beautifully, developing a warm amber patina over years of use. The grain is straight and even in clear stock. It dents under heavy use, but those dents are part of the bench's character — a well-used bench is a beautiful object precisely because it shows the marks of the work done on it.

### What to Buy

For a 72 x 22 x 33 inch bench:

- **Top:** Three 8-foot Douglas fir 2x12s. Select the straightest, clearest boards in the rack. Avoid boards with large knots, heavy twist, or significant bow. A few small, tight knots are fine — this is a workbench, not a dining table.
- **Legs:** Two 8-foot Douglas fir 2x4s. You will laminate pairs of 2x4s to make 4x4 legs (approximately 3 x 3.5 inches actual dimension).
- **Stretchers:** Two 8-foot Douglas fir 2x4s for the long stretchers (connecting front legs to back legs along the length).
- **Shelf:** Two 8-foot Douglas fir 2x12s or 1x12s for the lower shelf.
- **Hardware:** One front vise (see below), 8-10 lag bolts (3/8 x 5 inch) with washers, wood screws (3 inch and 2.5 inch), wood glue (Titebond III).

Total materials cost, including hardware: $200-300. The vise is the most expensive single item.

## Building the Top

The bench top is the single most important component. It must be flat, heavy, and thick enough to absorb mallet blows without bouncing.

### Laminating the Slab

1. **Prepare the 2x12s.** Construction lumber is sold surfaced (S4S), but the surfaces are not truly flat — they are close enough for framing but not for a bench top. If you have a jointer and planer, joint one face and one edge of each board, then plane to uniform thickness. If you do not have these tools (and most beginners do not), you can work with the boards as-is and flatten the assembled top afterward.

2. **Glue the boards face to face.** Apply Titebond III (exterior-grade, waterproof) to one face of each board. Spread it evenly with a roller or brush. Press the boards together — face to face, so the finished top is 3 laminated 2x12s standing on edge. The top thickness (the dimension you measure from front to back of the bench) comes from the width of the 2x12s: approximately 11.25 inches actual width times 3 boards = approximately 22 inches, minus some material lost to flattening. The bench height comes from the original thickness of the 2x12s: approximately 1.5 inches times 2 or 3 boards laminated, depending on orientation.

Wait. Let me clarify the orientation, because this is where people get confused.

**Option A: Boards flat (face up).** Lay the three 2x12s flat, faces up, side by side, and glue the edges together. This produces a top that is 1.5 inches thick and about 33 inches wide. This is too thin. You need at least 3 inches of thickness for a proper workbench top.

**Option B: Boards on edge (the correct method).** Turn the three 2x12s on their edges and glue the faces together. Now the top is approximately 11.25 inches wide (the original width of a 2x12) times... no. Let me be precise.

A 2x12 has an actual dimension of 1.5 inches thick by 11.25 inches wide. Three 2x12s laminated face to face produce a slab that is 4.5 inches thick by 11.25 inches wide. That is thick enough but too narrow.

**The practical approach:** Laminate the top from 2x12s laid flat, stacked two high (3 inches thick) and three wide (approximately 33 inches — trim to 22-24 inches). Or laminate them in a single layer with the faces up and add a 2x4 apron underneath for thickness.

The simplest Nicholson top: take two or three 2x12s, lay them flat on the bench frame (faces up, edges glued together), and screw them down. Add a 2x4 apron along the front and back edges, screwed from below. The total top assembly is approximately 3.5-4 inches thick (1.5 inch slab + 1.5 inch apron, overlapping) and weighs significantly more than any commercial bench top.

3. **Clamp and cure.** Use pipe clamps or bar clamps spaced every 12-16 inches along the length. Alternate clamps above and below to distribute pressure evenly. Tighten until glue squeezes out along every joint line. Let cure 24 hours.

4. **Flatten.** If the top is not flat (and it will not be perfectly flat), flatten it with hand planes (a jack plane with a cambered blade for rough work, then a jointer plane for final flattening) or with a router sled. A long straightedge (a 4-foot level works) helps you identify high spots. Take your time — the top does not need to be furniture-flat, but it should be flat enough that a board laid on it contacts the surface uniformly, without rocking.

## Legs and Base

### The Legs

Each leg is made from two 2x4s laminated face to face, producing a leg approximately 3 x 3.5 inches. Cut four legs to length: bench height minus top thickness. If your bench height is 33 inches and your top is 3.5 inches thick, the legs are 29.5 inches long.

Glue and screw the two 2x4s together for each leg. Use three 3-inch screws per leg, countersunk. Let the glue cure overnight.

### Leg-to-Top Connection

The Nicholson bench uses a simple and robust connection: the legs are notched into the underside of the top and secured with lag bolts.

1. Cut a notch (a shallow dado) in the underside of the top at each leg location — 3.5 inches wide, 3 inches deep (matching the leg cross-section), and 3/4 inch deep. This notch registers the leg and prevents it from sliding.
2. Place each leg into its notch. Drill a pilot hole through the leg and into the top. Drive a 3/8 x 5 inch lag bolt with a washer through the leg into the top. Two lag bolts per leg. Tighten firmly.

The lag bolts can be loosened if you ever need to disassemble the bench for moving. They can be tightened if the legs loosen over time (they will not, but the option exists).

### Stretchers

Two long stretchers connect the front-to-back leg pairs along the length of the bench, approximately 6-8 inches above the floor. Each stretcher is a 2x4 cut to span between the inner faces of the legs. Attach with lag bolts or long screws through the legs into the end grain of the stretchers.

The stretchers serve two purposes: they prevent the legs from racking (folding sideways under lateral force) and they support the lower shelf.

### The Shelf

The lower shelf spans the stretchers and fills the space between the legs at the bottom of the bench. Build it from 2x12s or 1x12s laid across the stretchers. Screw them down or just let them rest — the weight of the tools you store on the shelf will hold them in place.

The shelf adds mass (critical for stability), provides tool storage (planes, chisels, saws, sharpening stones live on the shelf within arm's reach), and visually grounds the bench. A bench without a shelf looks skeletal. A bench with a loaded shelf looks like a working tool.

## Work-Holding — The Front Vise

A front vise is essential. It clamps work to the front edge of the bench for planing, sawing, and chiseling. Without a front vise, you cannot hold a board on edge for jointing, you cannot clamp a board flat for crosscutting, and you cannot secure a workpiece for dovetailing.

### Quick-Release Vise ($40-80)

A cast-iron quick-release woodworking vise (Eclipse or a generic import — look for "9-inch quick release woodworking vise") bolts to the underside of the bench top, with the front jaw flush with the front edge. The quick-release mechanism allows you to slide the jaw open rapidly without turning the screw — pull a trigger, slide the jaw open, position your work, and tighten with a half-turn. This dramatically speeds up clamping and unclamping.

Installation: position the vise so the top of the jaws is flush with the bench top surface. Drill bolt holes through the top and lag-bolt the vise flange to the underside. Attach wooden jaw faces (scrap hardwood, 3/4 inch thick) to both the front and rear jaws — the wood protects your workpieces from the cast iron and provides a grip surface.

### The Moxon Vise (Alternative for Dovetailing)

A Moxon vise is a wide, twin-screw vise that clamps to the front edge of the bench top, raising the work to a comfortable height for dovetail work. It is a supplement to the front vise, not a replacement. You can build one from two wooden jaws and two 3/4-inch Acme threaded rods with hand wheels. Total cost for hardware: $20-30. It is worth building once you are cutting dovetails regularly.

## The Planing Stop

The simplest work-holding device in the shop: a 3/4-inch hardwood dowel (oak, maple, or birch — not softwood) that sits in a 3/4-inch hole drilled through the bench top near one end.

Drill a 3/4-inch hole about 3 inches from the end of the bench, centered on the width. Push the dowel through the hole. Tap it up so it protrudes 1/8 to 1/4 inch above the bench surface. To plane a board, butt the end of the board against the protruding dowel and plane away from the stop. The dowel holds the board in place through friction and the planing force pushing the board against it.

When you are not planing, tap the dowel flush with the surface (or below it) so it does not interfere with other work.

This simple device — a dowel in a hole — replaces the elaborate planing stops of traditional European benches. It is the Nicholson bench's minimalist genius.

## Dog Holes

Dog holes are 3/4-inch holes drilled through the bench top in a regular pattern, typically along the front edge. They accept:

- **Bench dogs:** Pegs (metal or wooden) that protrude above the bench surface. A board placed between a bench dog and the front vise jaw is clamped between the two — tighten the vise and the dog acts as an end stop. This holds boards flat on the bench for planing the face.
- **Holdfasts:** L-shaped metal bars (Gramercy Tools holdfasts are the standard, $30 per pair) that drop into a dog hole and lock when struck with a mallet. The holdfast clamps work flat to the bench top with enormous holding power. Strike the back of the shaft to release. Holdfasts are the fastest clamping method for most bench operations — strike, lock, work. Faster than any vise.

Drill a row of 3/4-inch dog holes along the front edge of the bench, spaced 4 inches on center (measure from center of one hole to center of the next). Drill the holes perpendicular to the bench surface, all the way through. A brad-point drill bit in a hand drill or drill press produces clean holes with minimal tearout.

A single row of 8-10 dog holes along the front edge, combined with the front vise and the planing stop, provides all the work-holding you need for 95% of shop operations. You can add more rows later if you find you need them — the bench is made of soft, drillable wood. Adding a dog hole takes 30 seconds.

## Finish

A workbench finish should protect the surface from glue, moisture, and stains without creating a slippery surface that workpieces slide on.

**Boiled linseed oil (BLO)** is the traditional and correct finish for a workbench. Apply it liberally with a rag, let it soak for 30 minutes, wipe off the excess. Repeat in 24 hours. Two to three coats is sufficient. BLO penetrates the Douglas fir, hardens within the wood fibers, and produces a warm amber tone that deepens with age and use. It provides moderate moisture resistance, resists glue adhesion (dried glue pops off an oiled surface more easily than off bare wood), and does not create a film that can be scratched or chipped.

Reapply BLO once or twice a year, or whenever the surface starts looking dry and pale. The entire bench can be re-oiled in 15 minutes.

**Important:** Rags soaked in boiled linseed oil can spontaneously combust. Lay used rags flat to dry before disposing, or submerge them in a sealed can of water. Never crumple oily rags into a pile or stuff them in a trash can.

## The PNW Connection

Building a workbench from Pacific Northwest Douglas fir is not just practical. It is local in the deepest sense. The trees that produced your bench lumber grew within a few hundred miles of your shop — on the slopes of the Cascades, in the foothills of the Olympics, in the managed forests of the Coast Range. Douglas fir is the defining tree of the Pacific Northwest landscape, the tallest and most abundant conifer in the region, the wood that built the houses and ships and bridges and railroads of the Pacific Northwest.

When you plane a Douglas fir bench top, the grain tells a story — wide growth rings from wet years, narrow rings from dry years, the occasional resin pocket from a wound the tree healed, the straight, even figure of a tree that grew tall and fast in PNW rain and fog. The bench smells like the forest it came from. After a few years of use and linseed oil, it develops a deep amber patina that looks like old-growth timber in a lodge.

Every lumber yard in the Puget Sound region, the Willamette Valley, and the Portland-Vancouver metro area carries Douglas fir in long, clear lengths at prices that hardwood users elsewhere in the country would envy. A 12-foot, clear 2x12 of Doug fir costs what a 4-foot board of hard maple costs on the East Coast. This abundance is a gift. Use it.

## The Bench as First Project and Lifelong Tool

The workbench occupies a unique position in woodworking: it is both the first real project and the tool that makes every future project possible. Building the bench teaches you lamination, flattening, drilling, lag-bolting, and finishing. Using the bench teaches you how to hold work, how to plane, how to chisel, how to saw. The bench is where your skill lives — literally, physically, in the dog holes that hold your work, in the vise that grips your boards, in the flat top that supports every operation.

A well-built Nicholson bench will last for decades. Douglas fir is soft enough to dent, and over the years the bench top will accumulate the marks of every project you build on it — chisel marks, saw kerfs, plane tracks, drill holes, glue stains, pencil marks. These are not defects. They are the record of work done. A pristine workbench is an unused workbench. A workbench covered in the scars of a thousand projects is a woodworker's autobiography written in wood.

Build the bench. It takes a weekend for the construction and a few evenings for finishing and fitting the vise. The materials cost less than a mediocre commercial bench. And when you are done, you have a 200-pound platform that does not move, does not wobble, holds your work firmly, and stands ready for every project that follows — the cutting board, the dovetailed box, the bookcase, the dining table, the chest of drawers. All of it starts here, on the bench.

Every serious woodworker will tell you the same thing: build the bench first. Everything else follows.
