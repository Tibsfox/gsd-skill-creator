# DIY Project: Build a Birdhouse for PNW Cavity Nesters

## Woodcraft Meets Wildlife — A Project That Bridges WDC and AVI

Research module WDC-TS-04: a guided project that takes you from raw lumber to a mounted, species-appropriate birdhouse for Pacific Northwest cavity-nesting birds. Uses basic tools, PNW-local wood, and field-tested dimensions backed by ornithological research. Time: 3 to 4 hours for your first build, faster thereafter.

## Why Build a Birdhouse?

A birdhouse is the simplest woodworking project that produces a real, functional object — something that exists in the world and serves a purpose beyond sitting on a shelf. The joinery is basic (butt joints with screws or nails). The tolerances are forgiving (birds do not care about hairline gaps). The materials are inexpensive (one board of cedar makes three or four houses). And the reward is tangible in a way that few woodworking projects can match: a family of birds moves in, raises young, and returns year after year to the house you built with your hands.

This project bridges woodcraft and natural history. To build a birdhouse that actually gets used — not a decorative box that birds ignore — you need to know which species nest in cavities, what dimensions each species requires, where to mount the house, and what features to include or avoid. This ecological knowledge makes the difference between a birdhouse that sits empty and one that fledges a brood of chickadees every spring.

In the Pacific Northwest, cavity-nesting birds face a specific challenge: the loss of old-growth forest and the snags (dead standing trees) that provide natural nesting cavities. Urban and suburban landscapes are particularly cavity-poor. A well-designed birdhouse partially offsets this deficit. It is not conservation at scale, but it is conservation at the scale of one family of birds in your yard, and that is real.

## Species-Specific Dimensions

Cavity nesters are particular about their houses. A hole that is too large admits predators and competing species. A hole that is too small excludes the target species. A floor that is too wide wastes the bird's energy heating a cold space. A floor that is too narrow does not accommodate a full clutch of eggs. These dimensions are not arbitrary — they come from decades of nest box research by ornithologists and bird conservation organizations.

### Black-Capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*)

The most common cavity nester in PNW yards and one of the most beloved birds in the region. Chickadees are year-round residents, bold, curious, and immediately recognizable by their "chick-a-dee-dee-dee" call.

- **Floor:** 4 x 4 inches (interior dimension)
- **Entrance hole:** 1-1/8 inches diameter
- **Hole height:** 6-8 inches above floor (measure from the inside floor surface to the bottom edge of the hole)
- **Interior height:** 8 inches (floor to underside of roof)
- **Mounting height:** 4-8 feet above ground

The 1-1/8 inch hole is critical. It admits chickadees (which squeeze through remarkably small openings) but excludes House Sparrows (which need 1-1/4 inches or larger). House Sparrows are aggressive cavity competitors that destroy chickadee nests and eggs. The tight entrance is the chickadee's first defense.

### House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*)

Tiny, fierce, and absurdly loud for their size. House Wrens are summer visitors to the PNW (arriving in April, departing by October) and will nest in almost anything that has a small hole — birdhouses, mailboxes, boots left on porches, pockets of hanging coats.

- **Floor:** 4 x 4 inches
- **Entrance hole:** 1 inch diameter (the smallest standard cavity entrance)
- **Hole height:** 4-6 inches above floor
- **Interior height:** 6 inches
- **Mounting height:** 5-10 feet above ground

The 1-inch hole excludes nearly everything except wrens and the occasional nuthatch. Wrens prefer their houses in brushy, semi-open habitat near shrubs and garden beds.

### Violet-Green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*)

One of the most beautiful PNW birds — iridescent green-violet above, pure white below, with white patches that extend above the eye and wrap around the rump. Violet-green Swallows are summer residents and enthusiastic users of nest boxes, especially in open areas near water.

- **Floor:** 5 x 5 inches
- **Entrance hole:** 1-1/2 inches diameter
- **Hole height:** 6-7 inches above floor
- **Interior height:** 8 inches
- **Mounting height:** 5-15 feet above ground (higher is better for swallows — they prefer altitude)

Mount swallow boxes in open areas with clear flight paths to the entrance. Swallows catch insects on the wing and need unobstructed airspace around the house.

### Western Bluebird (*Sialia mexicana*)

Less common than the other species on this list but present in open grasslands, meadows, and rural areas of the PNW, particularly east of the Cascades and in the San Juan Islands and Skagit Valley.

- **Floor:** 5 x 5 inches
- **Entrance hole:** 1-9/16 inches diameter
- **Hole height:** 6-7 inches above floor
- **Interior height:** 8-9 inches
- **Mounting height:** 4-6 feet above ground, on a post in open habitat

Bluebird boxes should be mounted in pairs, 15-20 feet apart, because Tree Swallows (which use the same hole size) will claim one box, allowing bluebirds to use the other without competition.

## Materials

### Wood: Western Red Cedar

**Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*)** is the ideal birdhouse wood and the most PNW material you can use. Cedar is naturally rot-resistant — the thujaplicin oil that gives it its distinctive smell also prevents fungal decay, which means a cedar birdhouse mounted outdoors will last 10-20 years without preservative treatment. Cedar is lightweight, easy to work with hand tools, and available at every lumber yard in the Pacific Northwest.

Buy a single 1x6 (nominal) cedar board, 6 feet long. This provides enough material for one complete birdhouse with waste left over for a second. The actual dimensions of 1x6 are approximately 3/4 inch thick by 5-1/2 inches wide.

**Do NOT use:**
- Pressure-treated lumber — the copper and chemical preservatives are toxic to nesting birds and developing embryos.
- Plywood — the glue delaminates when exposed to rain, and the off-gassing from formaldehyde-based adhesives can harm nestlings in the enclosed space.
- Painted or stained wood on the interior — fumes in an enclosed box are concentrated enough to be harmful. The exterior can be painted or stained if desired (use water-based, low-VOC exterior paint and let it cure for at least two weeks before mounting), but the interior must be bare wood.
- Metal — overheats in sun and kills nestlings. Never use a metal roof without a wood barrier beneath it.

### Hardware

- **Screws:** 1-5/8 inch exterior-grade screws (deck screws or stainless steel). Screws are better than nails — they hold more firmly, can be backed out for repairs, and do not split cedar as easily. Pre-drill pilot holes in cedar to prevent splitting.
- **Hinges (optional):** One small brass or stainless steel hinge for the cleanout door. A single hinge at the top of the front panel allows the front to swing open for annual cleaning.
- **Hook or latch:** A simple hook-and-eye or a pivot nail to keep the cleanout door closed.

### Tools

**Minimum (hand tools):**
- Handsaw or Japanese pull saw
- Drill with 1-1/8 inch (or appropriate) spade bit or Forstner bit for the entrance hole
- Drill bits for pilot holes (3/32 inch)
- Screwdriver or drill with screwdriver bit
- Square
- Pencil
- Measuring tape
- Sandpaper (80-grit, for rough edges only — do not sand the interior smooth, as birds need traction to climb to the entrance)

**Better (add power tools):**
- Miter saw or table saw for clean, square cuts
- Drill press for a perfectly round entrance hole (Forstner bit in a drill press produces the cleanest hole)
- Jigsaw for optional decorative roof shapes

## The Build

### Cut List (Chickadee House)

All pieces from 3/4-inch cedar, 5-1/2 inches wide:

| Piece | Width | Height | Notes |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|
| Front | 5-1/2" | 9-1/4" | Cut top at 15-degree angle for roof slope. Entrance hole centered, 6-7" above floor. |
| Back | 5-1/2" | 12" | Extends 2-3/4" above and below the box for mounting. |
| Left side | 4" | 8-1/2" | Cut top at 15-degree angle to match front. |
| Right side | 4" | 8-1/2" | Cut top at 15-degree angle to match front. |
| Floor | 4" x 4" | — | Recess 1/4" up from bottom for drainage. Drill four 1/4" drainage holes in corners. |
| Roof | 5-1/2" x 7" | — | Overhangs front by 1-1/2" to shade entrance and shed rain. |

For the Violet-green Swallow or Western Bluebird house, increase the floor to 5 x 5 inches and the entrance hole to 1-1/2 or 1-9/16 inches respectively. Side widths increase to 5 inches.

### Step 1 — Cut All Pieces (30 minutes)

Measure and mark all six pieces on your cedar board. Use a square to mark clean, perpendicular cut lines. For the angled tops of the front and side pieces, mark a 15-degree angle from horizontal — this gives the roof enough slope to shed rain without being so steep that interior space is wasted.

Cut all pieces. If using a handsaw, cut on the waste side of the line and clean up the edge with a block plane or sandpaper on a flat surface. If using a power saw, the cuts should be clean enough to use as-is.

**The entrance hole:** Mark the center of the front piece, 6-7 inches above where the floor will sit (measure from the bottom of the front piece, accounting for the floor's position). Drill the entrance hole. A Forstner bit in a drill press produces the cleanest hole; a spade bit in a handheld drill works but may tear the exit side. To prevent tear-out, drill from the outside (the face that will show) and stop when the pilot bit just pokes through, then flip the piece and finish from the back.

**Do not sand the inside of the front piece below the hole.** Birds need the rough texture to climb from floor to entrance. In fact, if your wood is particularly smooth, score shallow horizontal grooves (kerf cuts with a saw, or scratches with an awl) on the inside of the front piece below the hole. These are "climbing grooves" that give the nestlings traction when they are ready to fledge.

### Step 2 — Drill Drainage and Ventilation (10 minutes)

**Drainage:** Drill four 1/4-inch holes in the floor piece, one near each corner. These prevent rainwater from pooling inside the box. Water enters through the entrance hole during driving rain and must have a way out. Without drainage, nestlings can drown.

**Ventilation:** Drill two 5/8-inch holes near the top of each side piece, just below the roof line. These allow hot air to escape during summer. An enclosed box in direct sun can reach lethal temperatures without ventilation. The holes should be angled slightly upward (drill from inside at a slight upward angle) so rain does not enter.

### Step 3 — Assemble the Box (45 minutes)

Assembly order:

1. **Attach the sides to the back.** Position a side piece against the back piece, flush at the outer edge. Pre-drill two pilot holes through the back into the edge of the side. Drive screws. Repeat for the other side. The back piece should extend above and below the box — this extension provides mounting surface.

2. **Install the floor.** Recess the floor 1/4 inch above the bottom edges of the sides. This gap allows water to drain out the bottom even if the drainage holes clog. Pre-drill and screw through the sides into the floor edges. Two screws per side.

3. **Attach the front.** The front panel should be the cleanout door. Attach it with a single hinge at the top (screwing into the top edge of the front and the underside of the roof or the top of one side piece) or with a single pivot screw at the top of each side. The front swings open at the bottom for annual cleaning. Secure the bottom of the front with a hook-and-eye latch, a bent nail, or a single screw that can be backed out.

   If you prefer a non-opening design (simpler but harder to clean), just screw the front to the sides the same way you attached the back.

4. **Attach the roof.** Position the roof piece so it overhangs the front by 1-1/2 inches (creating a rain shadow over the entrance) and overhangs the sides by 1/2 inch. Screw through the roof into the top edges of the sides and front. Three or four screws hold the roof securely.

### Step 4 — Finish and Mount (30 minutes)

**No perch.** Do not add a perch below the entrance hole. This is the most common birdhouse design error. Cavity-nesting birds (chickadees, wrens, swallows, bluebirds) do not need perches — they grip the entrance hole directly. Perches provide a platform for predators (jays, crows, starlings, House Sparrows) to sit and reach into the box to destroy eggs or kill nestlings. Every perch is a predator platform. Leave it off.

**No paint inside.** The interior is bare wood. Birds need the natural texture for climbing, and fumes from paint or stain in the enclosed space can be harmful.

**Exterior treatment (optional):** If you want to protect the exterior, use a water-based, low-VOC exterior stain or paint. Earth tones (brown, gray, dark green) are appropriate — they blend with the environment and do not attract predators. Do not use bright colors. Let the finish cure for at least two weeks before mounting.

**Mounting:** Mount the birdhouse on a post (4x4 cedar or metal conduit) or on a tree trunk, using screws through the back piece extension. Face the entrance **away from the prevailing wind** — in the PNW, this generally means facing the entrance **east or south**. Pacific storms come from the southwest, and a south- or east-facing entrance stays dry during rain.

Mount at the species-appropriate height:
- Chickadee: 4-8 feet
- Wren: 5-10 feet
- Violet-green Swallow: 5-15 feet
- Bluebird: 4-6 feet

If mounting on a post, add a **predator baffle** — a cone or cylinder of sheet metal mounted below the box that prevents raccoons, cats, and snakes from climbing to the entrance. A simple baffle can be made from a 2-foot section of 6-inch diameter stovepipe mounted on the post below the box.

## Annual Maintenance

Birdhouses need annual cleaning. After the nesting season ends (October in the PNW), open the cleanout door and remove the old nest. Scrape out debris. Check for cracks, loose screws, and wasp nests (paper wasps sometimes build inside unused birdhouses). Leave the box empty and open through winter to dry out.

Close the box in February, before the nesting season begins (chickadees start prospecting nest sites in late February in the PNW). A clean, dry, empty box is the most attractive to prospecting birds.

Do not check the box while birds are actively nesting. Disturbance can cause nest abandonment, especially early in the incubation period. If you want to monitor nesting activity, watch from a distance — a bird entering the box with nesting material (moss, hair, feathers) in its beak is building a nest. A bird entering with food (insects, seeds) in its beak is feeding nestlings. Loud begging calls from inside the box confirm nestlings are growing.

## The Connection to PNW Ecology

Every species that uses your birdhouse is a participant in PNW ecosystem processes. Chickadees consume thousands of insects per day during the breeding season, including spruce budworm, aphids, and scale insects that damage garden plants and forest trees. Violet-green Swallows are aerial insectivores that catch mosquitoes, gnats, and flying ants on the wing. Wrens eat spiders, beetles, and caterpillars in garden beds and shrubbery.

Building a birdhouse from western red cedar connects you to the PNW timber tradition — the same wood that Coast Salish peoples used for longhouses, canoe hulls, and bentwood boxes is the wood that shelters a family of birds in your yard. The rot-resistant thujaplicin oil that makes cedar ideal for birdhouses is the same chemical that makes it ideal for shingle roofs, fence posts, and outdoor furniture. Understanding why cedar works for this application deepens your understanding of wood as a material.

And the birdhouse itself is a bridge between the workshop and the field. You built it with woodworking tools and woodworking skills. You mounted it using knowledge of PNW weather patterns and avian ecology. You monitor it using field observation skills. The intersection of craft and natural history is one of the richest spaces in the PNW maker tradition, and a birdhouse is the simplest project that inhabits that intersection.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** — [woodworking]: The birdhouse is an introductory construction project that teaches measuring, cutting, drilling, and assembly in a low-stakes context. The tolerances are forgiving, the materials are inexpensive, and the result is a functional object. For beginners who have never built anything, this is an ideal first project — simpler than a shelf, more purposeful than a cutting board.
- **Trades** — [basic-construction]: The birdhouse uses the same construction logic as building framing at a miniature scale — a floor, walls, and a roof joined with fasteners. The principles of drainage (holes in the floor), ventilation (holes near the roof line), and weather protection (roof overhang, entrance orientation) are the same principles that apply to full-scale building construction.
- **Nature-Studies** — [avian-ecology]: Choosing the right dimensions, entrance hole size, mounting height, and orientation requires knowledge of the target species' nesting biology. This is applied ornithology — ecological knowledge put to practical use. Monitoring the box through the nesting season develops field observation skills and deepens understanding of avian behavior.
- **Art** — [design]: A birdhouse is a design problem with functional constraints (species dimensions, drainage, ventilation, predator exclusion) and aesthetic possibilities (wood choice, roof shape, mounting style, optional exterior finish). The best birdhouses solve the functional requirements elegantly — they look good because they work well, and they work well because every design decision serves a purpose.
