# Try Session: Cut a Mortise and Tenon Joint

## The Most Important Joint in Furniture

Research module WDC-TS-03: a guided session that walks you through cutting a mortise and tenon joint in pine, by hand, with basic tools. The joint that holds together every table, every chair, every door frame. Master this and you can build anything. Time: 90 minutes to 2 hours for your first attempt.

## Why the Mortise and Tenon?

If the dovetail is the signature joint of the hand tool woodworker — the showpiece, the one that announces itself on the corner of a box — the mortise and tenon is the workhorse. It is the joint that does the structural heavy lifting. Every table apron connects to every table leg with a mortise and tenon. Every chair rail meets every chair leg with a mortise and tenon. Every door frame, every face frame, every paneled wall, every window sash — mortise and tenon. It is so fundamental to furniture construction that learning to cut it is not optional. It is the prerequisite.

The joint is simple in concept. A rectangular tongue (the tenon) on the end of one piece fits into a rectangular hole (the mortise) in another piece. The tenon is glued into the mortise, and the long-grain glue surfaces of the tenon cheeks bond to the mortise walls with tremendous strength. The shoulder of the tenon — the step where the tenon meets the rail — seats firmly against the face of the mortised piece, creating a clean, tight visual line. When done well, a mortise and tenon joint is nearly invisible, absurdly strong, and can last centuries.

The skill of cutting it by hand is worth developing even if you intend to use machines for production work. Cutting a mortise teaches chisel control — chopping, paring, wall-cleaning — in a context where precision matters. Cutting a tenon teaches saw control — ripping to a line, crosscutting to a line — with immediate feedback (the joint either fits or it does not). And the layout process teaches the discipline of measuring, marking, and transferring dimensions accurately. These are transferable skills. Every subsequent joint you cut benefits from the precision habits that mortise and tenon practice builds.

This session uses pine because it is cheap, soft, and available everywhere. The techniques transfer directly to hardwood. Once you can cut a clean joint in pine, switch to oak or maple and feel the difference — the chisel bites differently, the saw cuts more slowly, and the finished joint is tighter and more refined.

## What You Need

### Wood

Two pieces of 3/4-inch (nominal) pine:
- **Mortised piece** (the leg or stile): 3/4 x 3-1/2 x 12 inches. This is a standard piece of 1x4 pine from any lumber yard or home center.
- **Tenoned piece** (the rail or apron): 3/4 x 2-1/2 x 12 inches. This can be ripped from 1x4 or purchased as 1x3.

Get clear pine — no knots in the first four inches of each piece, which is where the joint will be cut. Knots deflect chisels and make layout lines hard to read.

Square up the ends of both pieces. A clean, square end on the tenoned piece is essential — the saw cuts for the tenon are referenced from this end. Use a miter box or a shooting board if you have one.

### Tools

**Marking gauge** ($15-25): You will use this to set the mortise width and to mark the tenon cheeks. A wheel marking gauge (like the Tite-Mark) scores a crisp line that registers a chisel. A pin gauge works too. The gauge must lock securely at the set distance.

**Mortise chisel** (3/8 inch): A mortise chisel is thicker and stiffer than a bench chisel. It is designed to be driven into end grain and levered against without bending. If you do not have a dedicated mortise chisel, a 3/8-inch bench chisel will work for pine — it will flex more, so use lighter mallet blows and take shallower bites. The chisel must be sharp. If it is not, do the [Sharpen a Chisel](sharpen-a-chisel.md) session first.

**Paring chisel or bench chisel** (3/8 or 1/2 inch): For cleaning the mortise walls after chopping. This should be sharp enough to slice end grain cleanly with hand pressure alone — no mallet.

**Mallet:** Wooden joiner's mallet or dead-blow mallet. The mallet drives the mortise chisel into the wood. A hammer will work but damages the chisel handle over time.

**Tenon saw** ($25-50): A backsaw with a stiffening spine, designed for accurate joinery cuts. The blade is typically 10-14 inches long with 12-15 teeth per inch. A Japanese dozuki also works — the thin kerf and fine teeth produce precise cuts.

**Marking knife:** For scoring layout lines. A knife line is more precise than a pencil line and creates a physical notch that guides the saw and chisel.

**Small square:** A combination square or engineer's square, 4-6 inches. For layout and for checking that the tenon shoulders are square to the face.

**Shoulder plane (optional but helpful):** A small, low-angle plane designed to trim tenon shoulders and cheeks. If you do not have one, a sharp chisel and a careful hand will serve the same purpose.

## Understanding the Joint

Before you cut, understand the geometry.

The **mortise** is a rectangular hole cut in the face or edge of the mortised piece. Its width should be approximately one-third the thickness of the stock. For 3/4-inch pine, that is 1/4 inch. But a 1/4-inch mortise chisel is fiddly and weak. Use a 3/8-inch chisel instead — the mortise will be 3/8 inch wide (half the stock thickness), which is slightly wider than the traditional one-third rule but perfectly strong in pine. The mortise walls will be 3/16 inch on each side, which is adequate for a practice joint.

The **mortise depth** should be at least as deep as the tenon is long, plus 1/16 inch clearance at the bottom for excess glue. For this practice joint, cut the mortise 1-1/4 inches deep.

The **tenon** is a rectangular tongue on the end of the rail, sized to fit the mortise exactly. Its thickness matches the mortise width (3/8 inch). Its width matches the width of the rail minus the shoulder allowance on each edge. Its length equals the mortise depth minus the clearance (1-3/16 inches, but cut it to 1-1/4 inches — the same as the mortise depth — and you can trim if it bottoms out).

The **shoulders** are the steps on the tenon where the full-width rail meets the reduced-width tenon. There are four shoulders: two cheek shoulders (top and bottom) and two edge shoulders (left and right). The cheek shoulders are the most visible and must be crisp and square.

## The Session

### Step 1 — Mark the Mortise (10 minutes)

Set your marking gauge to 3/16 inch (half of the stock thickness minus half the chisel width). Score a line on the face of the mortised piece, 3/16 inch from the edge. Without changing the gauge, score a second line from the opposite edge. The two lines define the mortise width — they should be 3/8 inch apart, centered on the stock.

Now mark the mortise length. The mortise should be as long as the tenon is wide — for a 2-1/2 inch rail, the tenon (after accounting for 1/4-inch shoulders on each edge) will be about 2 inches wide. Mark the mortise 2 inches long, centered on the mortised piece where you want the joint.

Use a square and marking knife to score the endpoints of the mortise. These lines mark where you stop chopping.

Score the gauge lines crisply. You should be able to see them clearly and feel them with a fingertip. These scored lines are your guides — the chisel will self-register in the scored groove, keeping the mortise walls straight and parallel.

### Step 2 — Chop the Mortise (25 minutes)

Clamp the mortised piece securely to the bench. The face with the mortise layout should be up, the piece level and stable.

Begin chopping at the **center** of the mortise, not the ends. Starting at the center allows you to lever waste toward the middle without blowing out the end grain at the mortise boundaries.

Position the mortise chisel in the center of the mortise, bevel facing toward one end. Strike the chisel with the mallet to drive it about 1/8 inch into the wood. Move the chisel 1/8 inch toward the same end and strike again. Continue in 1/8-inch steps toward (but not reaching) the end line. Stop 1/8 inch from the end line.

Lever the chisel back toward the center to pop out the waste chips. This lever action is why mortise chisels are thick — they must resist bending when used as a lever.

Now reverse direction. Start at the center again, bevel facing the other end. Chop in 1/8-inch steps toward that end, stopping 1/8 inch short. Lever out the waste.

Repeat this cycle, going deeper with each pass. Each pass removes about 1/8 inch of depth. After two or three passes, you will have a slot about 3/8 inch deep. Continue until you reach 1-1/4 inches deep.

**Key points:**
- Let the gauge lines guide the chisel. Place the chisel in the scored groove and it will track straight.
- Do not try to chop the full depth in one pass. Patience and shallow bites produce clean walls. Aggressive deep cuts produce torn walls and lost control.
- After reaching full depth, clean the end walls. Place the chisel in the scored end line, bevel facing into the mortise, and drive it straight down to square the ends. One or two clean strikes per end.
- Clean the side walls by paring with a sharp bench chisel held flat against the wall. Slice away any rough spots. The walls should be flat and parallel.

**Testing the mortise:** The mortise is done when the walls are flat, the bottom is reasonably flat (it does not need to be perfect — the tenon will not touch it), and the width is consistent from end to end. Test by inserting the chisel blade — it should slide in with light pressure and not wiggle side to side.

### Step 3 — Mark the Tenon (10 minutes)

The tenon dimensions come directly from the mortise — this is the principle of **direct transfer**. You measured and cut the mortise. Now the mortise tells you exactly how to size the tenon.

Set the marking gauge to the depth of the mortise (1-1/4 inches) and score a line around all four faces of the tenoned piece at that distance from the end. This is the **shoulder line** — the saw cuts stop here.

Now set the gauge to the mortise width offsets. Using the same gauge settings you used to mark the mortise (3/16 inch from each face), score the tenon cheek lines on both edges and the end grain of the tenoned piece. These lines define the tenon thickness.

For the edge shoulders: set the gauge to 1/4 inch and score lines on both faces, at the end of the tenoned piece, marking where the tenon narrows relative to the full width of the rail.

Use the marking knife and square to deepen the shoulder line. Score it firmly — this line will guide your shoulder cuts, and a crisp score prevents tear-out.

### Step 4 — Saw the Tenon Cheeks (15 minutes)

Clamp the tenoned piece upright in a vise (or clamp it to the edge of the bench at a steep angle).

Using your tenon saw, cut the **cheeks** first. These are rip cuts (cutting with the grain) that define the tenon thickness. Position the saw on the waste side of the gauge line — the kerf removes material from the waste, not from the tenon. Start the cut at the far corner, angling the saw slightly so you can see both the end-grain line and the face line. As the cut progresses, level the saw to cut straight down to the shoulder line.

Cut both cheeks. The saw should track in the gauge lines (the scored grooves guide the teeth) and stop precisely at the shoulder line. Do not cut past the shoulder line. If in doubt, stop short — you can always pare to the line with a chisel. You cannot un-saw a cut that goes too deep.

### Step 5 — Saw the Shoulders (10 minutes)

Lay the tenoned piece on its side on the bench. Using the tenon saw, make the **shoulder cuts** — crosscuts that remove the cheek waste and define the shoulders. The saw follows the scored shoulder line. Cut from the face toward the cheek cut. When the two cuts meet, the waste piece falls away.

Flip the piece and cut the opposite shoulder. Then cut the edge shoulders if your tenon is narrower than the rail.

The shoulders should be flat and square to the face. Check with a square. If there is a slight bump or unevenness, pare it flat with a chisel or trim it with a shoulder plane. The shoulders are the visible face of the joint — they need to seat flat against the mortised piece with no gaps.

### Step 6 — Test Fit (10 minutes)

This is the moment of truth. Take the tenon and offer it to the mortise. Does it fit?

**Ideal fit:** The tenon slides into the mortise with firm hand pressure. You can push it home without a mallet but not without effort. The shoulders seat flat against the face of the mortised piece with no visible gap. The joint does not wobble. This is a perfect fit.

**Too tight:** The tenon will not enter the mortise, or enters only partway with heavy pressure. Do not force it — you will split the mortise walls. Identify where it is binding (look for shiny spots on the tenon where the wood has compressed against the mortise wall) and pare those spots with a chisel. Remove a shaving at a time. Test again. Repeat.

**Too loose:** The tenon slides in easily and rattles. On a practice joint, this is a learning outcome, not a failure. Note where the gap is. Was the mortise too wide? Were the tenon cheeks not flat? Did the gauge slip during marking? Diagnosis is the lesson. On a real project, a slightly loose tenon can be rescued with veneer shims or thickened glue.

**Shoulder gap:** A gap between the shoulder and the mortised piece means the shoulder is not flat, or the tenon is too long and bottoming out in the mortise. If the tenon bottoms out, trim 1/16 inch from the end and test again. If the shoulder is not flat, pare it with a chisel.

When the joint fits correctly, pull it apart, admire it, and cut another one. The first mortise and tenon takes 90 minutes to two hours. The second takes 60 minutes. By the fifth, you have internalized the sequence and the cuts are confident.

## Why This Is THE Joint

The mortise and tenon has been used for at least four thousand years. The earliest known examples are Egyptian furniture (the tomb of Queen Hetepheres, c. 2600 BCE). The joint appears in Roman furniture, Chinese architecture, Japanese temple construction, medieval European timber framing, Shaker furniture, Arts and Crafts chairs, and every IKEA table that uses a wooden dowel (a dowel joint is a simplified mortise and tenon with a round tenon).

The reason it endures is that it solves the fundamental structural problem of furniture: connecting a horizontal member (a rail, apron, or stretcher) to a vertical member (a leg or post) in a way that resists both pulling apart and racking (the sideways force that makes a table wobble). The tenon's long-grain cheeks glue to the mortise walls with maximum strength. The shoulder resists racking by bracing against the leg. The geometry is self-locating — the tenon can only go into the mortise one way, which means assembly is precise and repeatable.

Every subsequent joint you learn — the bridle joint, the haunched tenon, the twin tenon, the drawbored mortise and tenon, the tusk tenon — is a variation on this fundamental geometry. The bridle joint is an open mortise and tenon. The haunched tenon adds a step for panel grooves. The twin tenon doubles the glue surface for wide rails. Master the basic mortise and tenon and every variation is a logical extension, not a new skill.

This is why woodworking teachers say: "If you can cut a mortise and tenon and a dovetail, you can build anything." These two joints cover 90% of all furniture joinery. The mortise and tenon handles frame construction (tables, chairs, doors, face frames). The dovetail handles box construction (drawers, chests, cases). Together, they are the complete structural vocabulary.

## Progression

**First joint in pine:** Follow this session exactly. Focus on process, not speed. A clean joint in two hours is a success.

**Second and third joints in pine:** Try variations. A through mortise and tenon (the tenon passes completely through, visible on both sides). A wedged tenon (drive thin wedges into saw kerfs in the tenon end to lock it mechanically). A haunched tenon (add a short step that fills a panel groove).

**First joint in hardwood:** Cut a mortise and tenon in white oak, hard maple, or walnut. The hardwood resists the chisel more firmly, the saw cuts more slowly, and the fit is more precise because there is less compression. The finished joint is more satisfying — the surfaces are smooth, the fit is tight, and the strength is dramatically higher.

**First project:** Build a small table or stool using four mortise and tenon joints. The leap from a single practice joint to a four-joint assembly teaches you about alignment (all four joints must be square and coplanar), clamping strategy (you need enough clamps to close all joints simultaneously), and the satisfaction of a structure that supports weight.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** — [woodworking]: The mortise and tenon is the structural backbone of woodworking. Every furniture project that uses frame construction — tables, chairs, doors, face frames, bed frames — relies on this joint. Mastering it is not an elective; it is a core requirement. The skills it teaches (chisel control, saw accuracy, layout precision) are the same skills that every other joinery operation demands.
- **Trades** — [joinery]: The mortise and tenon is the reference joint for understanding all frame joinery. The bridle joint is an open mortise and tenon. The dowel joint is a round mortise and tenon. The floating tenon (Domino) is a loose mortise and tenon. The stub tenon, the twin tenon, the wedged tenon, the drawbored tenon — all are variations on the principle of a tongue fitting into a slot. Understanding the base joint makes every variation immediately comprehensible.
- **Learning** — [precision-skills]: Cutting a mortise and tenon requires precision at every step — marking (gauge setting within 1/64 inch), chopping (chisel placement within the scored line), sawing (tracking the line without wandering), and fitting (testing and adjusting until the joint closes cleanly). This is precision skill development through deliberate practice, with immediate, unambiguous feedback: the joint fits or it does not.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: The mortise and tenon joint works because of wood's material properties — strong long-grain glue bonds, resistance to compression across the grain, and the ability to be cut to precise dimensions with edge tools. Understanding why the joint works (the physics of wood-to-wood bonding, the mechanics of the shoulder bracing against racking forces) connects craft practice to material science.
