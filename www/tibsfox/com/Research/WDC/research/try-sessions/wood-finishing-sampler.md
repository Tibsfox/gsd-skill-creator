# Try Session: The Finishing Sampler Board

## Try Them ALL on the Same Piece of Wood

Research module WDC-TS-06: a guided session that teaches finishing by direct comparison --- eight different finishes applied to eight sections of the same board. No theory, no guessing, no secondhand advice. You see the difference with your own eyes and feel it with your own hands. Materials cost under $60 (less if you already have some finishes). Time: 2 hours of active work, spread over 3 days for drying time.

**Cross-references:** [WDC-02] Wood Science (species properties, grain structure), [WDC-06] Finishing (comprehensive finish reference), [WDC-TS-01] Sharpen a Chisel (tool surface finish connection), [WDC-TS-04] Build a Birdhouse (exterior finish considerations), [WDC projects/cutting-board] (food-safe finish context).

**College of Knowledge:** Materials Department --- [material-properties], [chemistry-of-coatings]. Trades Department --- [finishing-techniques]. Science Department --- [experimental-comparison].

## Why a Sampler?

Finishing is the most confusing topic in woodworking. The number of products is overwhelming: oil, varnish, polyurethane, lacquer, shellac, wax, stain, pre-stain conditioner, sanding sealer, tung oil, Danish oil, boiled linseed oil, teak oil, mineral oil, paste wax, spray lacquer, brush-on lacquer, water-based poly, oil-based poly, gel stain, penetrating stain, wiping varnish, French polish. Every product has advocates who swear it is the only correct choice and detractors who swear it ruins everything. Online forums contain thousands of threads of people arguing about finishing, and half the advice contradicts the other half.

The sampler board ends the confusion. Instead of reading about finishes, you apply them. Instead of trusting someone else's opinion about how shellac looks on cherry, you see how shellac looks on YOUR cherry, with YOUR application technique, in YOUR shop conditions. The sampler board is empirical. It is your own controlled experiment. And once you have made one, you will never wonder which finish to use again, because you will have a physical reference that shows you exactly what each finish does to the wood you are working with.

This session is the single most educational exercise in the finishing curriculum. It teaches more in one afternoon than reading the entirety of [WDC-06 Finishing](../06-finishing.md), not because the chapter is wrong, but because finishing is a visual, tactile, and experiential discipline that resists text description. You must see it and touch it to understand it.

## Materials

### The Board

One piece of wood, approximately 12" x 24" x 3/4". The species matters --- it matters a lot --- because different species react differently to different finishes. Ideally, use the wood you plan to use for your next project. If you do not have a next project in mind, choose one of these common species:

- **White oak** --- shows dramatic grain figure under oil finishes, moderate absorption, excellent all-around test species
- **Cherry** --- notorious for blotching with stain (making it the ideal test species for pre-stain conditioner), deepens beautifully with shellac
- **Hard maple** --- very dense, minimal absorption, subtle grain figure, shows the difference between film and penetrating finishes clearly
- **Walnut** --- dark baseline, rich color under oil, shows how finishes interact with already-dark wood
- **Pine** --- the most challenging species to finish (soft, absorbent, resinous, blotch-prone), making it the most educational to test

**Advanced option:** If you are committed to learning, make two sampler boards from two different species. The comparison between how the same eight finishes behave on oak versus pine teaches an entire semester of finishing theory in one afternoon.

**Surface preparation:** Sand the entire board to 220 grit with the grain. The surface must be uniform across all eight sections --- any variation in sanding will confuse your comparison. Wipe the board with a tack cloth or damp rag to remove all dust. Let it dry completely.

### The Finishes

You need eight finishes. The following list covers the major categories, from zero-protection (raw wood) to maximum-protection (polyurethane over stain). You do not need to buy full cans of everything --- small sample sizes, trial sizes, and single-use packets are available for most finishes. If you already have some of these in your shop, use what you have.

1. **Nothing** (raw wood --- the control section). Cost: $0.
2. **Danish oil** (Watco or similar). A blend of oil and varnish that penetrates the wood and hardens in the pores. One coat, wipe on / wipe off. $8-12 for a pint.
3. **Boiled linseed oil (BLO)**. A pure drying oil that penetrates deeply and hardens slowly. Two coats, wet-sand the second coat with 400-grit paper. $6-10 for a pint.
4. **Water-based polyurethane** (Minwax Polycrylic or General Finishes High Performance). A thin, clear film finish. Three coats, sand lightly with 220-grit between coats. $8-14.
5. **Oil-based polyurethane** (Minwax or Varathane). A thicker, amber-toned film finish. Three coats, sand with 220-grit between coats. $8-14.
6. **Shellac** (Zinsser SealCoat or dewaxed shellac flakes dissolved in denatured alcohol). The oldest film finish in the world. Three coats, French polish the last coat (apply with a pad rather than a brush, in thin, rapid strokes). $8-12.
7. **Paste wax** (Johnson Paste Wax, Briwax, or Renaissance Wax). A surface treatment rather than a true finish. Two coats, buffed vigorously with a clean cloth between coats. $6-12.
8. **Stain + polyurethane** (oil-based stain of your choice + oil-based poly). Apply pre-stain conditioner if using pine, cherry, or birch (species prone to blotching). One coat of stain, wipe off excess, let dry, then three coats of poly. Stain: $6-10. Pre-stain conditioner: $6-8.

**Total cost if purchasing everything new:** $50-80. In practice, most woodworkers already have two or three of these in their shop, reducing the cost significantly.

### Supplies

- Painter's tape (blue or green --- low-tack to avoid lifting the wood surface)
- Clean cotton rags (old t-shirts, cut into palm-sized pieces --- at least 16 rags)
- Foam brushes (for polyurethane --- 1" width, pack of 6-8 for less than $3)
- 220-grit sandpaper (for between coats)
- 400-grit sandpaper (for wet-sanding BLO)
- Tack cloth (for dust removal between coats)
- Permanent marker and ruler (for labeling sections)
- Disposable gloves (BLO and stain will stain your hands for days)

## The Process

### Step 1 --- Divide the Board (15 minutes)

Using a ruler and permanent marker, divide the 24" length of the board into eight equal sections, each 3" wide. Draw light lines across the width of the board at 3", 6", 9", 12", 15", 18", and 21" from the left edge.

Apply painter's tape along each dividing line. Press the tape firmly to prevent finishes from bleeding between sections. The tape creates clean boundaries so you can compare adjacent finishes clearly.

Label each section along the top edge with the permanent marker:

1. RAW
2. DANISH OIL
3. BLO
4. WATER POLY
5. OIL POLY
6. SHELLAC
7. PASTE WAX
8. STAIN + POLY

### Step 2 --- Apply the First Coats (45 minutes)

**Section 1 (RAW):** Do nothing. This is your control --- the baseline against which every other finish is compared. Note the color, texture, and feel of the raw wood. This is what the wood looks like before you touch it.

**Section 2 (DANISH OIL):** Pour a small amount of Danish oil onto a clean rag. Wipe it onto the wood generously, saturating the surface. Let it soak for 15 minutes. Wipe off all excess with a clean, dry rag. The wood should feel dry to the touch, not oily. One coat is the standard application --- Danish oil is designed as a single-application finish.

**Section 3 (BLO):** Apply boiled linseed oil with a rag, saturating the surface. Let it soak for 20 minutes. Wipe off excess. This is the first coat. The second coat (with wet sanding) will be applied tomorrow after this coat has dried overnight.

**Section 4 (WATER POLY):** Using a foam brush, apply a thin, even coat of water-based polyurethane in long strokes, following the grain. Do not overbrush --- water-based poly dries fast and overbrushing creates drag marks. This is coat one of three. Coats two and three will be applied after drying (1-2 hours between coats for water-based).

**Section 5 (OIL POLY):** Using a different foam brush, apply a thin coat of oil-based polyurethane in long strokes, following the grain. Oil-based poly is thicker and more self-leveling than water-based --- let it flow. This is coat one of three. Coats two and three: 4-6 hours between coats.

**Section 6 (SHELLAC):** Using a foam brush or a pad (a wad of lint-free cloth wrapped in a square of old t-shirt), apply a thin coat of shellac in rapid, light strokes. Shellac dries in minutes. Do not go back over an area once it has begun to tack --- you will drag and create a rough surface. This is coat one of three. Second coat: 30-60 minutes.

**Section 7 (PASTE WAX):** Using a clean rag, rub paste wax onto the wood in a thin, even layer. Work in small circles, pressing the wax into the grain. Let the wax haze over (5-10 minutes). Buff vigorously with a clean cloth until the surface is smooth and slightly glossy. This is coat one of two.

**Section 8 (STAIN + POLY):** If using a blotch-prone species (pine, cherry, birch), first apply pre-stain conditioner according to the product instructions (usually wipe on, let soak for 5-15 minutes, wipe off excess, let dry for 15 minutes). Then apply oil-based stain with a rag or brush. Let it penetrate for 5-10 minutes (longer = darker). Wipe off all excess with a clean rag, wiping WITH the grain. Let the stain dry for the time specified on the can (usually 4-8 hours for oil-based stain) before applying the first coat of polyurethane.

### Step 3 --- Apply Subsequent Coats (Day 2 and Day 3)

The beauty of the sampler is that different finishes require different application schedules, and you learn those schedules by living them.

**Day 2:**

- **BLO (Section 3):** Apply the second coat wet. Then wet-sand the surface while the oil is still wet, using 400-grit sandpaper. The sanding creates a slurry of wood dust and oil that fills the pores, producing an ultra-smooth surface. Wipe off excess. Let dry.
- **Water poly (Section 4):** Sand the first coat lightly with 220-grit (the first coat will have raised the wood grain slightly --- feel it with your fingertips). Wipe dust with a tack cloth. Apply coat two. Wait 1-2 hours. Sand lightly. Apply coat three.
- **Oil poly (Section 5):** Sand the first coat lightly with 220-grit. Tack cloth. Apply coat two. Wait 4-6 hours for drying.
- **Shellac (Section 6):** Apply coat two (the first coat has been dry for hours at this point). Shellac is forgiving of fast application because each new coat dissolves slightly into the previous coat, creating a chemical bond. Wait 30-60 minutes. Apply coat three as a French polish: wrap a walnut-sized ball of cotton in a square of old t-shirt material, soak the pad with shellac, and wipe it across the surface in long, overlapping figure-eight motions, applying light, even pressure. The pad leaves a thin, smooth, high-gloss finish.
- **Paste wax (Section 7):** Apply the second coat. Let it haze. Buff vigorously. The surface should now have a soft, satiny sheen.
- **Stain + poly (Section 8):** The stain should be dry. Apply the first coat of oil-based polyurethane over the stain. Use a foam brush and light strokes.

**Day 3:**

- **Oil poly (Section 5):** Sand coat two lightly. Tack cloth. Apply coat three.
- **Stain + poly (Section 8):** Sand the first poly coat lightly. Tack cloth. Apply coat two of poly. Wait 4-6 hours. Sand. Apply coat three.

### Step 4 --- Wait 72 Hours

After the final coats are applied, wait 72 hours before evaluating. Some finishes (particularly oil-based polyurethane and BLO) continue to cure and harden for several days. The 72-hour wait allows all eight finishes to reach a comparable stage of cure, making the comparison fair.

## The Evaluation

After 72 hours, remove all the painter's tape. Set the board in good light --- natural daylight from a window is best. Now compare.

### Visual Comparison

Hold the board at an angle to the light and look at each section:

**Color change.** How much did each finish change the wood's natural color? Raw wood is your baseline. Danish oil and BLO typically deepen and warm the color significantly (the "wet look" that reveals hidden grain figure). Water-based poly adds minimal color change --- it is the most neutral finish. Oil-based poly adds a warm amber cast that deepens with age. Shellac adds a warm amber-orange tone. Paste wax adds a slight deepening. Stain changes the color entirely --- that is its purpose.

**Grain pop.** Which finishes make the grain figure most visible? Oil-based finishes (Danish oil, BLO, oil-based poly) typically produce the most dramatic grain enhancement. The oil penetrates the wood fibers and fills the pores, creating a refractive index change that makes the grain pattern leap off the surface. Water-based finishes sit on top of the wood and do not produce the same effect.

**Sheen.** Look at the light reflection on each section. Raw wood has no sheen. Danish oil and BLO have a soft, low sheen (satin to matte). Water-based poly can range from matte to gloss depending on the product. Oil-based poly tends toward semi-gloss. Shellac (especially French-polished) produces the highest gloss --- a deep, mirror-like surface that has been the standard of fine furniture finishing for centuries. Paste wax produces a soft, warm glow that is lower than any film finish but higher than raw wood.

### Tactile Comparison

Run your fingertips across each section:

**Texture.** Raw wood feels slightly fuzzy (the raised grain). BLO wet-sanded feels glass-smooth (the slurry filling the pores). Polyurethane feels like plastic (because it is, essentially --- a polymer film). Shellac feels silky. Paste wax feels warm and smooth. These tactile differences are as important as the visual ones, especially for pieces that will be handled --- table edges, chair arms, tool handles.

**Temperature.** Wood under a film finish (poly, shellac) feels slightly warmer to the touch than raw wood, because the film insulates your fingertip from the wood's thermal conductivity. Wood under an oil finish feels more like wood --- the oil penetrates rather than coating, so the wood's natural thermal properties remain more accessible.

### The Water Drop Test

Place a single drop of water on each section. Time how long it takes to absorb or bead:

- **Raw wood:** Absorbs within seconds. The water darkens the wood immediately and may raise the grain.
- **Danish oil / BLO:** Beads for a few minutes, then slowly absorbs. Moderate water resistance.
- **Water-based poly / Oil-based poly:** Beads indefinitely. The film is fully waterproof. Wipe it off after testing.
- **Shellac:** Beads initially but softens the finish if left for more than a minute. Shellac is water-resistant but not waterproof --- alcohol and water are its solvents. (This is shellac's main limitation for surfaces that see liquid contact.)
- **Paste wax:** Beads for a few minutes. Wax is water-resistant but not a permanent barrier.
- **Stain + poly:** Same as oil-based poly --- the poly is the protective coat, not the stain.

This test reveals the functional hierarchy of finishes. For a kitchen table that sees spills, polyurethane is the pragmatic choice. For a bookshelf that rarely gets wet, Danish oil or shellac provides beauty without the plastic feel. For a cutting board, none of these --- use food-safe mineral oil (see [WDC projects/cutting-board](../projects/cutting-board.md)).

## Keep It as a Reference

Date the back of the sampler board with a permanent marker. Note the species, the products used (brand and specific product name), and the number of coats. Store it where you can find it.

The next time you stand in the finish aisle at the hardware store, paralyzed by choice, you will not need to read the back of every can or consult a forum. You will pull out your sampler board, hold it next to the wood for your project, and see exactly what each finish does. Decision made. Confidence earned.

If you work with multiple species, make a sampler for each one. A finishing library of four or five boards --- oak, cherry, maple, walnut, pine --- covers virtually every project you will ever build. The total investment is a few hours and a few dollars in materials. The return is a lifetime of confident finishing decisions.

> **Try This:** Build a [pochade box](../../PNT/research/try-sessions/build-a-pochade-box.md) and use what you learned from this sampler to choose the right finish. The pochade box project specifically references the choice between Danish oil and polyurethane --- your sampler board answers that question empirically.

> **Try This:** Once you have chosen a finish for your next project, practice the application on a scrap piece of the same species before touching the project. The sampler teaches you which finish to use; the practice piece teaches you how to apply it cleanly.

> **Try This:** Make a second sampler board from a different species and compare the two. The same finish behaves differently on different woods --- oak absorbs BLO differently than maple does, pine blotches under stain while walnut does not. The two-species comparison is the most educational finishing exercise possible.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Materials** --- [chemistry-of-coatings]: Each finish on the sampler board represents a different chemistry. Danish oil is a reactive oil-varnish blend that polymerizes in the wood pores. BLO is a drying oil that cross-links when exposed to oxygen. Polyurethane is a synthetic polymer that forms a plastic film. Shellac is a natural resin secreted by the lac insect, dissolved in alcohol, that evaporates to leave a hard film. Paste wax is a blend of waxes (carnauba, beeswax, paraffin) that hardens at room temperature. Understanding these chemistries explains why each finish behaves the way it does --- why shellac dissolves in alcohol but polyurethane does not, why BLO takes days to cure but shellac dries in minutes, why paste wax can be buffed to a glow but polyurethane cannot.
- **Materials** --- [material-properties]: The sampler board is a physical demonstration of how surface treatments change the measurable properties of wood. Water resistance, hardness, abrasion resistance, UV protection, color stability --- each finish alters these properties in specific, observable ways. The water drop test alone demonstrates the concept of surface energy and hydrophobicity in a way that no textbook can match.
- **Trades** --- [finishing-techniques]: The application techniques practiced in this session --- wiping, brushing, padding, wet-sanding, buffing, between-coat sanding --- are the core skills of the finisher's trade. Each technique has its own rhythm and requirements: shellac demands speed, poly demands patience, BLO demands thorough wiping. Practicing all eight in sequence builds a physical vocabulary of finishing technique.
- **Science** --- [experimental-comparison]: The sampler board is a controlled experiment. One independent variable (the finish type) is manipulated across eight conditions. All other variables (wood species, surface preparation, application technique, environmental conditions) are held constant. The dependent variables (color change, sheen, texture, water resistance) are observed and compared. This is the scientific method applied to craft, and it produces knowledge that is more reliable than any individual opinion.
