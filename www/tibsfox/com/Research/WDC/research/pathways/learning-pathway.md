# WDC Learning Pathway: From First Cut to Master Craftsperson

A structured learning journey through the craft of woodworking -- tools, joinery, wood science, finishing, and the slow satisfaction of making something useful and beautiful from a tree. You don't need a shop full of tools to start. You don't need a workbench. You need a sharp blade, a piece of wood, and the patience to learn what the wood wants to become.

This pathway organizes all WDC research documents, try-sessions, study-guides, species profiles, and project guides into a coherent sequence from sharpening your first chisel through building furniture. Each level builds on the previous one, but woodworking is a craft that rewards jumping in -- so jump in wherever your skill and curiosity meet.

**Total estimated time:** 100+ hours across all five levels (woodworking is measured in sawdust, not hours)
**Prerequisites:** None. Access to basic hand tools helps at Level 2, but Level 1 requires only a chisel and a sharpening stone.
**College of Knowledge connections:** Trades, Materials, Science (wood science), Art (design, finishing), Nature Studies (tree identification)

---

## Level 1 -- First Cuts (1-2 days)

*"I've never used a chisel. I don't know the difference between pine and oak. Where do I start?"*

Start with the tool in your hand. Woodworking is a physical skill, and the only way to learn it is to cut wood. Level 1 teaches you to sharpen (the most important skill in woodworking, full stop), gives you historical context, and introduces you to the material itself.

### Try-Session -- Do This First

1. **[try-sessions/sharpen-a-chisel.md](../try-sessions/sharpen-a-chisel.md)** -- Your very first woodworking exercise. Sharpening is the foundation of everything that follows. A sharp tool is safe, controllable, and satisfying to use. A dull tool is dangerous, frustrating, and produces bad work. Learn to sharpen before you learn anything else. (~1-2 hours)

### Reading

2. **[01-history-of-woodworking.md](../01-history-of-woodworking.md)** -- The 10,000-year story of humans shaping wood: from Neolithic mortise-and-tenon joints to Japanese temple carpentry to the Arts and Crafts movement. Read this after you've struggled to sharpen a chisel -- you'll have new respect for every craftsperson who came before you. (~30 min)

3. **[02-wood-science.md](../02-wood-science.md)** -- What wood actually is: cell structure, grain direction, heartwood vs. sapwood, how wood moves with moisture, and why understanding the material is the difference between woodworking and fighting with wood. (~25 min)

### Study Guide

4. **[study-guides/wood-identification-guide.md](../study-guides/wood-identification-guide.md)** -- How to identify common wood species by sight, smell, weight, and grain pattern. This is the woodworker's equivalent of a birder's field guide. Start learning the species you'll encounter at the lumber yard. (~20 min + ongoing practice)

### What You'll Learn
- How to sharpen a chisel to a working edge (and why this matters more than any other skill)
- The history of woodworking as a human practice
- Basic wood science: grain, movement, species characteristics
- How to identify common wood species

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Sharpen a chisel well enough to pare cleanly across the grain
- Identify at least 5 common wood species by sight
- Explain why grain direction matters for every cut
- Walk into a lumber yard without feeling completely lost

### College Connections
- **Trades:** Tool maintenance, hand skills, craft fundamentals
- **Materials:** Wood as an engineering material, species properties
- **Science:** Botany (wood cell structure), physics (cutting mechanics)

## Level 2 -- Hand Skills (1-2 weeks)

*"I can sharpen. Now I want to cut joints and make something."*

Now you're building. Level 2 introduces hand tools, your first joint, and your first project. The emphasis is on hand tools because they teach you to feel the wood -- its grain, its resistance, its behavior. Power tools are faster, but hand tools are teachers.

### Reading -- Tools

5. **[03-hand-tools.md](../03-hand-tools.md)** -- The hand tool inventory: chisels, hand planes, saws, marking tools, squares, and clamps. What each tool does, how to choose quality, and the minimal set you need to get started. (~30 min)

### Try-Sessions

6. **[try-sessions/first-joint.md](../try-sessions/first-joint.md)** -- Cut your first joint: a simple butt joint, then a dado, then a rabbet. These are the simplest joints in woodworking, but cutting them well requires accurate layout, controlled sawing, and clean chisel work. Each one teaches something the previous one didn't. (~2-3 hours)

7. **[try-sessions/restore-a-hand-plane.md](../try-sessions/restore-a-hand-plane.md)** -- The most satisfying $15 project in woodworking. Find a rusty Stanley Bailey #4 or #5 at a thrift store or estate sale and restore it to precision condition: disassembly, rust removal, sole flattening, sharpening the iron, reassembly, and tuning. A century-old tool, made better than anything new under $200, brought back to life by your hands. (~4-6 hours over a weekend)

8. **[try-sessions/build-a-birdhouse.md](../try-sessions/build-a-birdhouse.md)** -- Your first complete project. A birdhouse is small, uses simple joints, requires measuring, cutting, assembly, and finishing. When you're done, you have something a bird can live in. That's satisfying in a way that abstract exercises never are. (~3-4 hours)

### Study Guide

8. **[study-guides/essential-joints-reference.md](../study-guides/essential-joints-reference.md)** -- The reference card for woodworking joints: butt, dado, rabbet, miter, lap, mortise-and-tenon, dovetail, box joint, finger joint, tongue-and-groove. Strengths, applications, and difficulty level for each. Keep this handy -- you'll refer to it for years. (~20 min for first read, ongoing reference)

### Species Profile

9. **[species/western-red-cedar.md](../species/western-red-cedar.md)** -- Your first PNW species deep-dive. Western Red Cedar is the quintessential PNW wood: aromatic, rot-resistant, easy to work, and culturally central to Indigenous peoples of the region. It's also what your birdhouse should probably be made of. (~15 min)

### What You'll Learn
- The function and use of essential hand tools
- How to lay out and cut basic woodworking joints
- How to plan, execute, and finish a simple project from start to finish
- The properties and character of Western Red Cedar

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Use a hand saw, chisel, and hand plane with basic control
- Cut three types of joints (butt, dado, rabbet) to acceptable tolerances
- Build a functional birdhouse from raw lumber
- Select appropriate wood species for outdoor projects

### College Connections
- **Trades:** Hand tool skills, project planning and execution
- **Nature Studies:** PNW tree species, building wildlife habitat
- **Art:** Design basics (the birdhouse is a design problem)
- **Materials:** Wood species selection, material properties

## Level 3 -- Joinery (1-3 months)

*"I want to make things that are strong, beautiful, and last."*

Joinery is the soul of woodworking. Level 3 takes you from simple joints to the mortise-and-tenon -- the joint that has held buildings and furniture together for millennia. You'll also learn about power tools (they have their place), study joinery in depth, and build progressively more challenging projects.

### Try-Session

10. **[try-sessions/hand-cut-mortise-tenon.md](../try-sessions/hand-cut-mortise-tenon.md)** -- Cut a mortise-and-tenon joint by hand. This is the Everest of Level 3 -- a joint that requires precise layout, controlled chisel work (mortise), accurate saw cuts (tenon), and careful fitting. When the tenon slides into the mortise with just the right friction, you'll understand why woodworkers call this a "satisfying fit." (~3-4 hours for your first one; you'll make many more)

### Reading

11. **[05-joinery.md](../05-joinery.md)** -- The comprehensive joinery chapter: every major joint type, when to use each one, how they transfer loads, and the aesthetic dimension of exposed joinery. This is the theoretical companion to the hands-on try-session. (~35 min)

12. **[04-power-tools.md](../04-power-tools.md)** -- Power tools: table saw, bandsaw, drill press, router, planer, jointer, and the debate between hand tool and power tool woodworking. This isn't about replacing hand tools -- it's about understanding what power tools do well and where hand tools remain superior. Safety emphasis throughout. (~30 min)

### Projects -- Build Something Real

Choose at least one project that stretches your current ability:

- **[projects/cutting-board.md](../projects/cutting-board.md)** -- A classic first "real" project. Edge-grain or end-grain, glue-up technique, and food-safe finishing. Makes a great gift. (~4-6 hours)
- **[projects/simple-box.md](../projects/simple-box.md)** -- A small box with a fitted lid. Teaches precise dimensioning, mitered corners or dovetails, and the satisfaction of a lid that closes with a soft "thump." (~6-8 hours)
- **[projects/spoon-carving.md](../projects/spoon-carving.md)** -- Pure hand work: hook knife, sloyd knife, and a blank of green wood. Spoon carving is meditative, requires almost no shop space, and connects you to the oldest form of woodworking. (~2-3 hours per spoon)
- **[projects/floating-shelf.md](../projects/floating-shelf.md)** -- A concealed-mounting shelf that appears to float on the wall. Teaches hidden joinery and the engineering of a cantilever. (~4-6 hours)

### Species Profile

13. **[species/douglas-fir-profile.md](../species/douglas-fir-profile.md)** -- The second PNW species deep-dive. Douglas Fir is the backbone of Pacific Northwest construction and one of the most commercially important trees on Earth. Strong, straight-grained, and everywhere you look in PNW architecture. (~15 min)

### What You'll Learn
- How to cut the most important joint in woodworking (mortise-and-tenon)
- The full taxonomy of woodworking joints and their applications
- Power tool safety and capabilities
- Project planning from raw materials through finished piece
- Douglas Fir: the tree that built the Pacific Northwest

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Cut a mortise-and-tenon joint by hand to a usable fit
- Choose the right joint for a given structural and aesthetic situation
- Build at least one finished project suitable for use or gifting
- Work safely with basic power tools
- Identify Douglas Fir and explain its significance to PNW building

### College Connections
- **Trades:** Advanced joinery, power tool operation, project management
- **Materials:** Structural properties of joints, wood engineering
- **Art:** Design, proportion, the aesthetic of joinery
- **Science:** Physics of load transfer, wood mechanics

## Level 4 -- Furniture Maker (6-12 months)

*"I want to build furniture. Real furniture. The kind that lasts generations."*

Furniture making is where all the skills converge: wood selection, joinery, design, finishing, and the ability to visualize a finished piece while staring at rough lumber. Level 4 is ambitious -- you're building things that people sit on, eat at, and live with. The stakes are higher and so is the satisfaction.

### Reading

14. **[07-furniture-making.md](../07-furniture-making.md)** -- The furniture making chapter: design principles, proportion systems (the Golden Ratio shows up here), construction methods, traditional forms (Shaker, Mission, Danish Modern), and the process of designing a piece from scratch. (~40 min)

15. **[06-finishing.md](../06-finishing.md)** -- Everything about wood finishing: oil, varnish, lacquer, shellac, polyurethane, wax, milk paint, and the art of making wood look its best. Finishing is the final 10% of a project that determines 50% of its appearance. A beautiful joint under a bad finish is a tragedy. (~30 min)

### Projects -- Build Furniture

Choose projects that match your growing capability:

- **[projects/live-edge-shelf.md](../projects/live-edge-shelf.md)** -- A shelf that preserves the natural edge of the tree. Part woodworking, part design, part connection to the tree that provided the material. (~6-8 hours)
- **[projects/workbench.md](../projects/workbench.md)** -- Build the tool that helps you build everything else. A proper workbench is a woodworker's most important possession. This is a substantial project that uses every skill you've developed. (~20-40 hours)
- **[projects/adirondack-chair.md](../projects/adirondack-chair.md)** -- An outdoor chair that combines compound angles, comfortable ergonomics, and weather-resistant construction. The Adirondack chair is an American icon for good reason -- it's beautiful, comfortable, and buildable. (~10-15 hours)

### Reading -- PNW Context

16. **[09-pnw-timber.md](../09-pnw-timber.md)** -- The full story of PNW timber: old growth forests, the logging industry, sustainable forestry, and the relationship between the woodworker and the forest. Every board started as a tree. Understanding where your material comes from changes how you use it. (~30 min)

17. **[08-construction-framing.md](../08-construction-framing.md)** -- Construction and framing: the larger-scale application of woodworking in building structures. Even if you never frame a house, understanding construction gives you a deeper appreciation for wood as a structural material. (~25 min)

### What You'll Learn
- Furniture design principles: proportion, form, function
- Wood finishing techniques from oil to lacquer
- How to plan and execute a multi-day, multi-joint furniture project
- The PNW timber story: forests, industry, sustainability
- Construction framing as it relates to woodworking

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Design a piece of furniture from sketch to cut list
- Apply an appropriate finish that enhances the wood and protects the piece
- Build furniture that is structurally sound, aesthetically pleasing, and functional
- Build a workbench (the project that makes all other projects easier)
- Talk intelligently about PNW forestry and sustainable wood sourcing

### College Connections
- **Trades:** Furniture making, finishing, construction
- **Art:** Design, proportion, aesthetics of functional objects
- **Materials:** Advanced wood selection, finishing chemistry
- **Nature Studies:** PNW forests, sustainable forestry, timber ecology
- **Environmental:** Sustainable sourcing, old growth conservation

## Level 5 -- Master Craftsperson (Ongoing)

*"I want to push the boundaries of what I can make. I want my work to outlast me."*

Mastery in woodworking has no endpoint. There are woodworkers who have practiced for 50 years and still say they're learning. Level 5 is about refining your craft to the highest level you can reach: advanced joinery (hand-cut dovetails, Japanese joinery, compound angles), commission work, teaching, and the quiet satisfaction of making something that your grandchildren's grandchildren might use.

### Advanced Practice

18. **Return to [05-joinery.md](../05-joinery.md)** with new eyes. Study the joints you skipped: through dovetails, blind dovetails, Japanese joinery (tsugite and shiguchi), and the compound joints that seem impossible until you've cut them. (~ongoing study and practice)

19. **Commission work.** Build something for someone else, to their specifications. This is a different discipline than building for yourself -- you must interpret someone else's vision, manage expectations, price your time, and deliver on deadline. (~ongoing)

20. **Teach.** Offer to teach a friend to sharpen a chisel (Level 1). Volunteer at a community woodshop. Teaching forces you to articulate knowledge you hold in your hands, and it gives back to the community that supported your learning.

### Cross-References

21. **[10-cross-references-bibliography.md](../10-cross-references-bibliography.md)** -- The full bibliography and connections to other research projects. Primary sources for advanced study, tool maker websites, lumber references, and further reading. (~reference)

### What You'll Learn
- Advanced joinery techniques from multiple woodworking traditions
- How to work to commission (client management, pricing, delivery)
- How to teach woodworking skills effectively
- That "mastery" is a direction, not a destination

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Cut complex joints by hand with consistent accuracy
- Design, build, and finish commission pieces to professional standards
- Mentor beginning woodworkers through Levels 1-3
- Source wood responsibly and work with the material's natural characteristics
- Leave behind objects that will be used and appreciated for generations

### College Connections
- **Trades:** Advanced craft, professional practice, teaching
- **Art:** Furniture as art, design philosophy, Japanese aesthetics
- **Business:** Commission work, pricing, client relationships
- **Community:** Mentorship, community workshops, craft preservation
- **History:** Connecting to woodworking traditions across cultures and centuries

## How to Use This Pathway

**Make sawdust.** Reading about woodworking is preparation. Making sawdust is learning. If you have an hour, spend 15 minutes reading and 45 minutes at the bench.

**Sharpen often.** The single best piece of woodworking advice: when a cut feels hard, check your edge. Sharp tools make woodworking enjoyable. Dull tools make it miserable. You'll sharpen thousands of times over a woodworking life. Each time gets faster.

**Start small, finish completely.** A finished cutting board teaches you more than a half-built bookcase. Finish every project, even when it goes wrong. Especially when it goes wrong.

**Smell the wood.** Western Red Cedar smells like the PNW forest. Douglas Fir smells like sawmills and sunlight. Walnut smells like autumn. Every species has a scent, and that scent is part of the craft experience that can't be captured in a document.

**Connect to other projects.** Woodworking connects to everything:
- **[FWL](../../FWL/research/)** -- Build a chicken coop (the ultimate woodworking + poultry crossover)
- **[RCN](../../RCN/research/)** -- PNW timber species are raccoon habitat. The trees you work with are the trees they live in.
- **[PNT](../../PNT/research/)** -- Paint what you build. Build frames for what you paint.
- **[FSR](../../FSR/research/)** -- Emergency shelter construction (woodworking skills applied to crisis)
- **[FFA](../../FFA/research/)** -- Prop-making for costumes and convention displays

**Use the cross-project learning paths.** This project appears in:
- [The PNW Naturalist](../../../learning-paths/pnw-naturalist.md) -- PNW timber species and tree identification
- [The Creative Maker](../../../learning-paths/creative-maker.md) -- woodworking for prop-making and craft projects
