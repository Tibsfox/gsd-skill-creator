# Building Furniture

## The Discipline of Making Things People Use

Furniture is the intersection of art and engineering. A chair must be beautiful enough to deserve a place in a room and strong enough to survive a decade of daily use by a family that does not think about chairs. A table must present a surface that is flat, stable, and inviting while accommodating the wood's need to move with the seasons. A cabinet must organize space efficiently while concealing the ingenuity of its construction.

Furniture making is where every skill in woodworking converges. Wood selection, joinery, finishing, design — all of them matter, and they all interact. A design decision (the width of a tabletop) creates an engineering problem (how to accommodate movement across that width). A wood selection (figured maple) creates a finishing challenge (curl tears out easily during planing, and blotches under stain). A joinery choice (through dovetails on a drawer) creates an aesthetic statement (this is handmade, and proud of it).

This chapter is about putting it all together.

## Design — Proportion, Function, and the Eye

Good furniture design starts with proportion. A table that is too tall feels like a counter. A chair that is too deep makes the sitter slide forward or perch on the edge. A bookcase with shelves spaced too evenly looks monotonous. Getting the proportions right is the first discipline of furniture making, and it is largely invisible when done well — no one notices that a table is the right height, but everyone notices when it is wrong.

### Standard Dimensions

Some dimensions are non-negotiable because human bodies set them. A dining table is 29 to 30 inches tall. A coffee table is 16 to 18 inches. A kitchen counter is 36 inches. A desk is 28 to 30 inches. A dining chair seat is 17 to 18 inches from the floor. A barstool seat is 24 to 26 inches (counter height) or 28 to 30 inches (bar height). These numbers are not arbitrary. They are the product of centuries of sitting, eating, and working at surfaces, refined to the range that accommodates the majority of human bodies.

Beyond these functional constraints, proportion is a matter of the eye. The golden ratio (approximately 1:1.618) appears in furniture design as it appears throughout nature and art — not as a rigid formula but as a tendency. A cabinet whose height-to-width ratio approximates the golden ratio tends to look balanced. Drawer fronts that decrease in height from bottom to top (following a graduated progression that echoes the golden ratio) look more dynamic than drawers of equal height. These are not rules. They are starting points for the eye, which is the final arbiter.

### Drawing and Prototyping

Full-scale drawings are the traditional design method for furniture. Drawing a piece at actual size reveals proportional problems that are invisible in a sketch or a scaled drawing. A full-scale side view of a chair, drawn on a sheet of plywood, shows immediately whether the angles, the seat height, the back rake, and the leg splay work together or fight each other.

For beginners, building a prototype from cheap material (poplar, pine, or even MDF) before committing to expensive hardwood is wisdom, not waste. A prototype reveals problems that drawings cannot — how the piece feels to use, whether the proportions work in three dimensions, whether the joints can be cut in the actual geometry of the design. The cost of a prototype is a fraction of the cost of discovering a design flaw halfway through a build in walnut.

## Tables — The Fundamental Form

A table is a flat surface supported at a usable height. This is furniture reduced to its essence, and the simplicity is deceptive. A table must be flat (requiring careful stock preparation and proper grain orientation), stable (requiring rigid joinery between top and base), and must accommodate wood movement across the top's width (requiring flexible attachment of the top to the base). Getting all three right simultaneously is the engineering challenge.

### The Apron Table

The most common table construction uses an apron — a rectangular frame of rails joined to legs with mortise-and-tenon joints. The top is attached to the apron with fasteners that allow cross-grain movement (Z-clips, figure-eight connectors, or slotted screw holes in the apron rails). The apron provides racking resistance (preventing the table from folding sideways), and the wide mortise-and-tenon joints between legs and rails provide the strength.

The apron table works because the rails brace the legs in two directions — the long rails resist racking along the table's length, and the short rails resist racking across its width. The joints must be tight and the glue bond must be good, because the apron is the only thing preventing a table from becoming a collapsible structure.

A stretcher — a horizontal member connecting legs near the floor — adds rigidity. Trestle tables use a central stretcher (sometimes with a through-tenon wedged at each end) to brace the two trestle legs. Stretchers are structurally effective but reduce legroom.

### The Trestle Table

Trestle tables predate apron tables by centuries. The medieval trestle was a knockdown design — a flat top resting on two or more trestle supports, held together by a stretcher that could be removed for storage. The design survives today in both traditional and modern forms.

A trestle table's charm is its combination of visual lightness and structural rigor. The trestle base can be elegant — a shaped post with splayed feet, connected to its partner by a single stretcher secured with tusk tenons that can be knocked out for disassembly. The top sits on the trestles, held in place by gravity and optional attachment blocks.

### The Pedestal Table

A pedestal table — a single central support (the pedestal) with feet radiating outward at the base — solves the legroom problem entirely. There are no legs to bump knees against. The tradeoff is stability: a pedestal table is inherently less stable against tipping than a four-legged table. The base must be heavy and the feet must extend far enough to prevent tipping when weight is placed on the edge.

The pedestal-to-top connection is the critical joint. A sliding dovetail or a heavy hanger bolt connects the pedestal to the top. The connection must resist the bending moment created by any off-center load on the top — lean on the edge of a pedestal table and the full leverage of the overhang acts on that single connection.

## Chairs — The Hardest Thing to Build

A chair is the most demanding piece of furniture to build. It must support a dynamic load (a shifting, fidgeting human body) through a structure that is mostly air. The joints are subjected to racking forces in every direction. The angles are compound — legs splay outward and rake backward simultaneously. The seat must be comfortable for extended sitting. And the whole thing has to look good.

### Compound Angles

Chair legs typically splay outward (viewed from the front) and rake backward (viewed from the side). A mortise cut into a seat rail at a compound angle — simultaneously angled in two planes — is one of the most challenging layout and cutting problems in furniture making. Getting it right requires either careful trigonometry (calculating the compound angle from the splay and rake angles) or a full-scale drawing from which angles are taken directly with a sliding bevel.

### Steam Bending

Many chair components — bows, crest rails, arm bows, rockers — are curved. These curves can be cut from thick stock (wasting the wood and producing short grain that is structurally weak) or bent from straight stock using steam.

Steam bending softens the lignin in wood, allowing it to deform plastically. The wood is placed in a steam box (a long, enclosed chamber fed with steam from a boiler) for roughly one hour per inch of thickness. It is then removed and immediately clamped to a bending form. As it cools and dries, it retains the bent shape.

Not all species steam-bend well. White oak, ash, and elm are excellent. Red oak, cherry, and walnut are moderate. Maple and most tropical hardwoods are poor. The key factor is the ratio of compression strength to tension strength along the grain — bending forces compress the inner face and stretch the outer face, and the wood must absorb both without failing.

A metal backing strap — a thin steel strip placed on the outside of the bend — supports the outer (tension) face and prevents it from fracturing. With a backing strap, bends of remarkable radius are possible — bending a piece of white oak into a full semicircle is routine in Windsor chair making.

### Windsor Chairs

The Windsor chair is perhaps the most refined expression of chair making. It consists of a shaped solid seat into which legs, spindles, and a curved bow are inserted. The joints are typically round tenons fitted into drilled holes — no mortises, no square tenons, no flat surfaces. Everything is round, turned, or shaved.

Windsor chair making is a distinct discipline within woodworking. The seat is scooped with an adze, travisher, and inshave. The legs and stretchers are turned on a lathe. The spindles are riven (split) from a billet and shaved with a drawknife and spokeshave. The bow and crest rail are steam-bent. The wood for each component is selected for the properties that component requires: the seat is a soft, easily carved species (pine, tulip poplar, or basswood), the legs and stretchers are a strong, resilient species (maple, birch, or oak), and the spindles and bow are a tough, flexible species (oak, ash, or hickory).

The result is a chair of extraordinary lightness and strength. A well-made Windsor chair weighs less than ten pounds and will support a three-hundred-pound adult. The lightness comes from the material selection (using each species where its properties are most needed) and from the design (every component is pared to its minimum effective cross-section). The strength comes from the geometry — the splayed legs, the raked-back spindles, and the curved bow all contribute to a triangulated structure that distributes load efficiently.

## Cabinets — Organizing Space

Cabinet construction is the art of building boxes and dividing them into useful spaces. The basic cabinet is a case (a box with four sides, a back, and optionally a top and bottom) divided by shelves, fitted with doors or drawers, and finished to suit its location and purpose.

### Frame and Panel

Frame-and-panel construction solves the problem of building wide, flat surfaces from solid wood without the surfaces cracking. A frame — typically four pieces joined with mortise-and-tenon joints — captures a thin panel in grooves. The panel floats in the grooves, free to expand and contract with humidity changes. The frame provides structural rigidity. The panel provides surface area.

This construction is used for cabinet doors, cabinet sides, chest lids, and any application where a wide, flat, stable surface is needed. The panel can be flat, raised (beveled on the edges to fit the groove, with a raised field in the center), or composed of tongue-and-groove boards.

### Face Frame vs. European (Frameless)

North American cabinet construction traditionally uses a face frame — a rectangular frame of stiles and rails attached to the front edges of the cabinet box. The face frame covers the exposed edges of the plywood or solid-wood case, provides a mounting surface for hinges and catches, and adds rigidity to the case.

European (frameless) cabinet construction eliminates the face frame. The doors and drawers cover the full front of the cabinet box, mounted on cup hinges that attach to the inside of the case. Frameless construction is cleaner in appearance (no visible frame between doors and drawers), more efficient in use of space (the face frame does not intrude into the opening), and dominant in modern kitchen design.

Both approaches work. The choice is aesthetic and practical — face frame cabinets are traditional, forgiving of imperfect case dimensions, and easier to scribe to uneven walls. Frameless cabinets are modern, space-efficient, and require more precise construction.

## Drawers — The Dovetailed Box

A drawer is a box that slides. The front, sides, and back form the box. The bottom (a thin panel) floats in a groove. The drawer slides in and out of the cabinet on some form of runner — traditional wood runners, modern ball-bearing slides, or the classic side-hung system where a groove in the drawer side rides on a runner attached to the case.

The drawer front takes the visual attention and the abuse. It is the face of the cabinet, the surface that hands grip daily. Drawer fronts are made from the project's primary wood, carefully selected for grain and figure.

The drawer sides are the structural members. They bear the load of the contents and resist the racking forces of being pulled and pushed thousands of times. Dovetail joints between the front and sides are the traditional and strongest connection — the mechanical interlock of the dovetails prevents the front from pulling off the sides, and the large glue surface of the pins and tails provides bond strength. Half-blind dovetails on the front (invisible from outside) and through dovetails on the back (visible but hidden inside the cabinet) are the classic arrangement.

The drawer bottom is a thin panel — historically solid wood, now often plywood or hardboard — captured in a groove in the front and sides. It is not glued in — it floats, accommodating seasonal movement. In traditional construction, the bottom slides in from the back and is secured with a single screw or pin, allowing it to be removed for repair.

A well-made drawer is one of the most satisfying things in woodworking. When you push it closed and it glides on its runners and stops exactly flush with the cabinet face, with the grain of the front aligned with the grain of its neighbors, with the dovetails tight and the bottom flat — that is craftsmanship made tangible.

## Shaker Style — The Beauty of Nothing Extra

Shaker furniture is the most influential style in American woodworking. Its principles are simple: build for function, remove everything unnecessary, and let the material speak.

The Shakers — a communal religious group that flourished in the northeastern United States in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries — produced furniture that looks remarkably modern despite being designed two centuries ago. Straight lines, tapered legs, flat panels, simple turned knobs, and a complete absence of applied ornament. The beauty is in the proportion, the wood selection, and the quality of construction.

Shaker designs are often recommended as learning projects for furniture makers. A Shaker side table — four tapered legs joined to an apron with mortise-and-tenon joints, supporting a solid-wood top — teaches every fundamental skill: stock preparation, mortise-and-tenon joinery, tapering, finishing, and top attachment. The simplicity of the design leaves nowhere to hide mistakes, which is exactly the point. If the joints are not tight, you can see it. If the tapers are not consistent, you can see it. The design demands precision because there is nothing to distract from imprecision.

## Live Edge and Slab Furniture

On the opposite end of the spectrum from Shaker minimalism is live edge furniture — pieces built from slabs of wood that retain the natural edge of the tree. A live edge tabletop is a cross-section of a tree trunk, split or sawn to reveal the full width of the log, with the bark edge (or the undulating surface beneath it) left intact.

George Nakashima pioneered this aesthetic in American furniture. His tables, desks, and benches celebrated the tree's natural form — voids, cracks, and all. Where a crack threatened the slab's integrity, Nakashima spanned it with a butterfly key (a bowtie-shaped piece of contrasting wood inlaid across the crack). The butterfly key is both repair and ornament, a visible acknowledgment that the wood was damaged and that the damage was accepted rather than hidden.

Live edge work requires a different kind of design sensibility. The wood dictates the form. A slab with a dramatic curve on one edge suggests a different base design than a slab with a straight edge. A void in the center of a slab might be filled with epoxy or left open, depending on the maker's intent. The design conversation is not "what shape should this table be?" but "what shape does this tree want this table to be?"

The practical challenges are significant. Slabs are heavy, expensive, difficult to dry (the thickness and irregular shape make even drying a challenge), and prone to checking and warping. Flattening a large slab requires either a massive planer (router sled, actually — most planers cannot handle slabs this wide) or hours of hand planing. Finishing a live edge requires creative approaches — applying finish to bark-covered edges, stabilizing loose bark with epoxy, and sealing the end grain to prevent further checking.

## Famous Makers — The Lineage of Excellence

The history of furniture making is populated by makers whose work transcends craft and enters the realm of art.

**Sam Maloof** (1916-2009) is perhaps the most beloved American furniture maker. His rocking chairs — sculptural forms in walnut, with flowing lines that make the wood look fluid — are in the collections of every major American art museum. Maloof carved his joints by hand, blending the curves of one piece into another until the junction disappeared. He worked in his Rancho Cucamonga shop for seventy years, making every piece by hand, one at a time. He was self-taught. He learned by doing, and he never stopped.

**George Nakashima** (1905-1990) was an architect-turned-furniture-maker who brought Japanese aesthetic sensibility to American studio furniture. His philosophy — giving wood a "second life" after the tree's death — informed every piece. His book, *The Soul of a Tree*, is required reading. His studio in New Hope, Pennsylvania, continues to produce furniture under the direction of his daughter, Mira Nakashima.

**James Krenov** (1920-2009) was a Russian-born, Swedish-trained furniture maker who wrote four influential books on the craft. His work is quiet and contemplative — small cabinets on stands, with carefully proportioned doors, hinges fashioned from wood, and surfaces finished to a soft sheen. Krenov's influence was primarily as a teacher and writer. His books — *A Cabinetmaker's Notebook*, *The Fine Art of Cabinetmaking*, *The Impractical Cabinetmaker*, *Worker in Wood* — advocate for a deeply personal approach to the craft, listening to the wood, working slowly, and finding the design that the material suggests.

**Wharton Esherick** (1887-1970) pushed furniture toward sculpture. His work is angular, organic, and uncompromising. A spiral staircase in his studio, carved from a single timber with cantilevered treads, is one of the most photographed pieces of American woodwork.

**Wendell Castle** (1932-2018) blurred the line between furniture and sculpture entirely. His stack-laminated pieces — chairs, desks, tables built up from glued layers of wood and then carved — have no visible joinery, no straight lines, no concession to conventional furniture form. Castle's work asks whether a chair can be a sculpture and a sculpture can be a chair. The answer, in his hands, was yes.

These makers matter not because their work should be imitated but because their work demonstrates what the craft is capable of. They show that furniture can be functional art, that wood can express ideas beyond utility, and that the relationship between maker and material can produce objects of lasting significance.

## The First Piece

Everyone starts somewhere. The traditional first furniture project is a small table or a box — something with enough joinery to teach the fundamentals, small enough to complete in a reasonable time, and useful enough to earn a place in the household.

A Shaker step stool. A dovetailed tool tote. A small bookshelf with dado joints. A side table with mortise-and-tenon legs. Any of these teaches the essential sequence: design, stock selection, milling, joinery, assembly, finishing. The first piece will not be perfect. The joints will have gaps. The surfaces may not be as smooth as they should be. The finish may have a run or two.

None of that matters. What matters is that you made something real from a tree, and it stands on its own, and it works. Everything after that is refinement.
