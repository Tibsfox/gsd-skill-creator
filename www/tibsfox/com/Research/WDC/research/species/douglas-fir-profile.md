# Douglas Fir -- The Tree That Built the Northwest

## Not a True Fir, Not a Hemlock, Not Like Anything Else

Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) is the most important tree in Pacific Northwest woodworking. Full stop. It framed every house, every barn, every school, every church in the region for over a century. It is the standard structural lumber, the default framing timber, the species that the Pacific Northwest's built environment is literally constructed from. When a contractor in Seattle or Portland says "lumber" without qualification, they mean Douglas fir. It goes without saying because it goes without saying.

And it is not a fir. Not a spruce. Not a pine. Not a hemlock. Douglas fir is its own genus -- *Pseudotsuga*, which translates to "false hemlock," a name that reflects the taxonomists' struggle to classify a tree that resembled several things but was none of them. The common name honors David Douglas, the Scottish botanist who introduced the species to European science in 1826, and the species name *menziesii* honors Archibald Menzies, the naturalist who first described it in 1791. The tree had been known to Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest for millennia before either European arrived.

This is the profile of a tree that an entire region was built from: its physical characteristics, its wood properties, its uses in construction and furniture, where to buy it, and why it matters to every woodworker in the Pacific Northwest.

## The Tree

### Physical Description

Douglas fir is a massive conifer. In old-growth forests, coastal Douglas firs reach 200 to 250 feet in height and 4 to 6 feet in trunk diameter. The tallest measured specimen -- felled in the early twentieth century -- stood 393 feet, which would make it taller than any tree alive today (the tallest living tree, a coast redwood named Hyperion, stands 380 feet). Old-growth Douglas fir trunks are columnar for 100 feet or more before the first branches, rising through the forest canopy like pillars in a cathedral.

The bark is one of the tree's most distinctive features. On mature trees, it grows to 6 to 12 inches thick -- deeply furrowed, with thick, corky ridges that insulate the cambium from the moderate-intensity ground fires that historically burned through PNW forests every 200 to 500 years. This fire-adapted bark is one reason Douglas fir dominates its range: it survives fires that kill competing species, then colonizes the opened canopy.

The needles are flat, soft, and roughly 1 inch long, arranged spirally around the branch. The cones are 3 to 4 inches long and immediately recognizable by the three-pointed bracts that protrude between the scales -- they look like the hind legs and tail of a tiny mouse diving headfirst into the cone. This feature is unique to *Pseudotsuga* and is the easiest field identification mark for the species.

### Range

Douglas fir grows from central British Columbia to central Mexico and from the Pacific coast to the Rocky Mountains. The coastal variety (*var. menziesii*), growing west of the Cascades in the wet, mild climate of the Pacific Northwest, produces the largest trees and the finest lumber. The interior variety (*var. glauca*), growing in the drier, colder Rockies, produces smaller trees with different wood properties (lighter weight, less strength, more knots).

When woodworkers and builders refer to Douglas fir lumber, they almost always mean the coastal variety. The lumber grades, the structural tables, and the pricing all default to coastal Douglas fir.

### Old-Growth vs. Second-Growth

This distinction matters enormously to woodworkers.

**Old-growth Douglas fir** grew slowly in dense, shaded forest for centuries. The annual rings are tight -- often 20 to 40 rings per inch, sometimes more. The wood is denser, stronger, and more dimensionally stable than second-growth. The grain is straight and even. The heartwood is a rich amber to dark orange-brown. Old-growth vertical grain (quarter-sawn) Douglas fir was the premier trim, flooring, and siding material for Pacific Northwest homes from the 1890s through the 1960s. When you see the beautiful, tight-grained fir trim in a Craftsman bungalow, that is old-growth.

Old-growth Douglas fir is scarce. The vast majority of old-growth forest was logged in the twentieth century. What remains is on federal land (national parks, wilderness areas) and is not harvested. Old-growth fir lumber enters the market through two channels: salvage (standing dead trees on private land, which is rare) and deconstruction (beams and timbers reclaimed from demolished old buildings, which is less rare and is a thriving PNW industry). **Re-Store** (Habitat for Humanity's building materials stores, multiple PNW locations) and specialty salvage dealers like **Crosscut Hardwoods** (Seattle) and **Goby Walnut** (Portland, which also stocks reclaimed fir) occasionally have old-growth Douglas fir available as reclaimed timbers and boards.

**Second-growth Douglas fir** comes from plantation-harvested trees, typically 40 to 80 years old. The rings are wider (5 to 15 per inch), the wood is less dense, and the strength is lower. Second-growth is what fills every home center and every lumber yard today. It is the construction lumber that frames every new building. It is adequate for structural applications -- the span tables and grading rules account for the lower density -- but it lacks the beauty, the stability, and the hardness of old-growth.

For furniture and visible applications, second-growth Douglas fir can be beautiful if you select carefully. Look for boards with tighter-than-average rings, straight grain, and the warm amber color of heartwood. Reject boards that are mostly sapwood (pale, nearly white) or that have excessively wide rings (which indicate fast, weak growth).

## Wood Properties

### Strength-to-Weight Ratio

Douglas fir's signature property is its exceptional strength relative to its weight. It is the reason the species dominates construction framing.

- **Janka hardness:** 660 lbf (moderate for a softwood, harder than cedar at 350 but softer than southern yellow pine at 690)
- **Modulus of rupture (bending strength):** ~12,400 psi for coastal variety. This is among the highest of any softwood, meaning Douglas fir can span greater distances as a joist or rafter than most competing species at the same cross-section.
- **Modulus of elasticity (stiffness):** ~1.95 million psi. High stiffness means less deflection (sagging) under load. A floor framed with Douglas fir joists feels stiffer and more solid underfoot than the same floor framed with spruce or hem-fir.
- **Specific gravity:** 0.48 (second-growth average) to 0.55+ (old-growth). By comparison, western red cedar is 0.32 and white oak is 0.68.

These numbers explain why Douglas fir is the standard structural lumber: it is strong enough to span rooms, stiff enough to resist deflection, and light enough to handle efficiently on a job site. No other widely available North American softwood matches this combination.

### Grain Pattern

Douglas fir has a pronounced grain pattern created by the strong contrast between earlywood and latewood in each annual ring. The earlywood (formed in spring, when growth is fast) is pale, soft, and wide. The latewood (formed in summer, when growth slows) is dark, dense, and narrow. This contrast produces the bold, striped grain pattern that is Douglas fir's visual signature.

In flat-sawn boards, the grain produces dramatic cathedral patterns -- the nested U-shapes that reveal the curvature of the annual rings. In quarter-sawn (vertical grain) boards, the grain runs as straight, parallel lines -- tight, even, and elegant. Vertical grain fir is the premium option for interior trim, paneling, and furniture. It is also more dimensionally stable than flat-sawn, which matters for applications that must hold paint or resist seasonal movement.

### Working Properties

Douglas fir works well with both hand and power tools, with one important caveat: the density difference between earlywood and latewood.

**Hand planing:** The soft earlywood crushes easily under a plane blade set for an aggressive cut, while the dense latewood resists. This creates an uneven surface if you are not careful. The solution: a sharp blade (sharper than you think you need), a light cut (a gossamer shaving, not a thick chip), and a high cutting angle (a York pitch plane at 50 degrees, or a standard bench plane with a back-bevel of 10-15 degrees on the blade, for an effective cutting angle of 55-60 degrees). With proper technique, a hand plane leaves a surface on Douglas fir that is better than any sanding.

**Power planing:** A planer with a helical cutterhead handles Douglas fir better than a straight-knife cutterhead because the helical inserts shear the fibers rather than chopping them, reducing the earlywood/latewood differential. If your planer has straight knives, take light passes (1/32 inch or less for finishing passes) and keep the knives sharp.

**Sanding:** Douglas fir sands easily but has a tendency to clog sandpaper because of the resin content. Use stearated (anti-clog) sandpaper. The earlywood sands faster than the latewood, which can create a subtle ripple on the surface if you sand aggressively with a belt sander or hard sanding block. A random orbit sander with moderate pressure produces the most even results.

**Gluing:** Douglas fir glues well with PVA (Titebond) and polyurethane adhesives. The resin content can occasionally interfere with glue adhesion on freshly surfaced wood -- wiping the glue surfaces with acetone or naphtha before gluing removes the resin and improves the bond.

**Screwing and nailing:** Douglas fir holds fasteners well. It splits less readily than many hardwoods, but pre-drilling is still good practice for screws near edges and ends. The latewood bands are hard enough that nails sometimes deflect along the grain boundary -- pre-drilling eliminates this problem.

### Staining and Finishing

Douglas fir is one of the trickier species to stain evenly, and the reason is the earlywood/latewood contrast that defines its character. The soft, porous earlywood absorbs stain like a sponge. The dense latewood resists it. The result: a blotchy, reversed-grain appearance where the earlywood goes dark and the latewood stays light -- the opposite of what the natural grain looks like.

**To stain Douglas fir evenly:**

1. **Use a pre-stain conditioner:** Apply a washcoat of thinned shellac (1-pound cut -- dissolve 1 ounce of shellac flakes in 8 ounces of denatured alcohol) to the sanded surface. The shellac partially seals the earlywood pores, reducing differential absorption. Let the shellac dry for one hour, sand lightly with 320 grit to remove any roughness, then apply the stain. The result is much more even color.

2. **Use gel stain:** Gel stains (General Finishes gel stain is the benchmark, $15-18/quart) sit on the surface rather than penetrating into the pores. This gives you control over the color depth and virtually eliminates blotchiness. Apply with a rag, wipe to the desired darkness, let dry.

3. **Skip stain entirely:** This is the approach many PNW woodworkers prefer. Douglas fir's natural color -- warm amber to reddish-brown heartwood -- is beautiful on its own. A clear oil-varnish blend (Watco Danish Oil in Natural, $8-12/pint; or General Finishes Arm-R-Seal, $18-22/pint) deepens the color and brings out the grain without adding artificial pigment. Three coats of Arm-R-Seal wiping varnish produce a durable, natural-looking finish that ages beautifully -- the fir darkens to a rich amber over years of UV exposure, developing a warmth and depth that no stain can replicate.

## Uses

### Construction Lumber: The Default

Douglas fir IS construction lumber in the Pacific Northwest. Every standard dimensional size -- 2x4, 2x6, 2x8, 2x10, 2x12 -- is available in Douglas fir at every lumber yard in the region. The grading stamps (Doug Fir-Larch, or DF-L) appear on virtually every framing member in every building.

Construction grades, from structural to economy:
- **Select Structural:** The highest structural grade. Tight knots, straight grain, high bending strength. Used for engineered applications where every stick matters.
- **#1:** Few defects, high strength. Standard for floor joists, rafters, and beams in quality construction.
- **#2:** More knots and grain deviation than #1. The most commonly stocked grade at lumber yards. Adequate for walls, non-critical framing, and most residential applications.
- **Stud grade:** Specifically graded for vertical use (wall studs). Suitable for 10-foot lengths in bearing walls.

Beyond dimensional lumber, Douglas fir is the standard species for:
- **Glulam (glue-laminated timber) beams:** Multiple layers of Douglas fir lumber glued together under pressure to create beams that can span 30 to 60 feet or more. Glulam beams are the workhorses of modern post-and-beam and commercial construction.
- **LVL (laminated veneer lumber):** Douglas fir veneers laminated into structural members with consistent, engineered properties. Used for headers, beams, and hip/ridge members.
- **CLT (cross-laminated timber):** Douglas fir boards laminated in alternating perpendicular layers to create massive structural panels that compete with concrete and steel for mid-rise buildings. The mass timber revolution is built on Douglas fir.

### Interior Trim: The Premium Application

Vertical grain (quarter-sawn) Douglas fir trim is the signature interior material of the Pacific Northwest Craftsman bungalow. The tight, straight grain, the warm amber color, and the way the wood takes a clear finish make it the premium choice for baseboards, door and window casings, wainscoting, and built-in cabinetry.

Appearance grades for trim and millwork:
- **Clear (C & Better):** No visible defects. The premium grade for interior trim. Expensive -- $6-12 per lineal foot for standard profiles, depending on width.
- **C Select:** Near-clear, with minor defects that can be placed where they will not show.
- **D Select:** Small knots and minor defects allowed. Suitable for paint-grade trim or where a more rustic look is desired.

Where to buy: **Dunn Lumber** (Seattle/Bellevue) stocks vertical grain Douglas fir trim in standard profiles. **Crosscut Hardwoods** (Seattle) carries rough-sawn vertical grain fir boards for custom millwork. **Hardwood Industries** (Oregon City) and **Goby Walnut** (Portland) stock Douglas fir for furniture and trim. For reclaimed old-growth fir trim, check Habitat for Humanity **Re-Store** locations throughout the PNW -- demolished Craftsman-era homes yield extraordinary old-growth fir trim that cannot be purchased new at any price.

### Furniture: The Underrated Application

Douglas fir is not a traditional furniture wood. The softness (Janka 660) means it dents under impact, and the bold grain pattern can be visually busy in a piece that demands quiet elegance. But Douglas fir furniture has its own honest, unpretentious beauty -- particularly when built from old-growth or tight-grained second-growth material and finished with a clear oil or varnish.

The **Nicholson workbench** -- the most recommended first workbench for hand tool woodworkers -- is built from construction-grade Douglas fir. A 3-to-4-inch-thick laminated slab top made from 2x6 or 2x8 Douglas fir is heavy enough to resist planing forces, flat enough to serve as a reference surface, and cheap enough that you do not agonize over the inevitable dings and tool marks that a workbench accumulates. Christopher Schwarz's design, documented in *Workbenches: From Design and Theory to Construction and Use* and in the WDC workbench project guide in this series, uses construction-grade Douglas fir throughout.

Beyond workbenches: Douglas fir dining tables (especially from reclaimed old-growth beams), shelving, rustic bed frames, and farmhouse-style furniture all benefit from the species' bold grain and warm color. The wood is easy to work, takes glue well, and is inexpensive. The dent-proneness that disqualifies it from fine furniture becomes a feature in pieces that are meant to look used, lived-with, and real.

## The Tree That Literally Framed Every House

Walk through any neighborhood in western Washington or Oregon -- any neighborhood built before the 1990s -- and you are walking through Douglas fir. The studs behind the walls: Douglas fir. The joists under the floors: Douglas fir. The rafters above the ceilings: Douglas fir. The window casings, the door frames, the baseboards in a Craftsman bungalow: Douglas fir. The glulam beam spanning the great room in a modern house: Douglas fir. The CLT panel forming the floor of a new mid-rise apartment: Douglas fir.

No other tree species has shaped a region's built environment as thoroughly as Douglas fir has shaped the Pacific Northwest. The tree's exceptional structural properties -- the strength, the stiffness, the straight grain, the workability -- made it the obvious choice for construction from the moment the first sawmills opened in the 1850s. A century and a half later, it remains the obvious choice, now re-engineered into mass timber products that are building the Pacific Northwest's future as surely as the original old-growth timbers built its past.

Understanding Douglas fir is not optional for a PNW woodworker. It is the material you will encounter most often, in the widest range of contexts, for the rest of your working life. Whether you are framing a wall, trimming a window, building a workbench, or salvaging a beam from a demolished building to make a dining table -- the wood in your hands is almost certainly Douglas fir. Know it well. It has earned your attention.
