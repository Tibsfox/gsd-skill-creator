# Western Red Cedar -- The Tree of Life

## Every Part Used, Every Grain Earned

Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) is not a true cedar. It is an arborvitae -- a member of the cypress family -- and it is the most culturally significant tree in the Pacific Northwest. The Coast Salish peoples called it the Tree of Life, and the name is not honorary. It is literal. Every part of this tree was used: the wood for houses, canoes, and totem poles; the bark for clothing, rope, and baskets; the roots for baskets and lashing; the boughs for medicine and ceremonial use. No other tree in North America served a civilization as completely as western red cedar served the peoples of the Northwest Coast.

For the modern woodworker, western red cedar remains irreplaceable. It is the most decay-resistant softwood available in the Pacific Northwest. It is dimensionally stable in wet conditions. It smells like a forest. And it is soft enough to work with hand tools on a stump with nothing more than a sharp knife. The Tree of Life earned its name in antiquity and keeps earning it every time someone builds a fence, lines a closet, lays a deck, or carves a canoe.

## 1. The Tree -- Biology and Range

Western red cedar grows from southern Alaska to northern California, and inland to the western slopes of the Rocky Mountains in Montana and Idaho. It thrives in wet, mild climates -- the river bottoms, the fog belts, the rain-soaked valley floors where annual rainfall exceeds 60 inches. In the Pacific Northwest lowlands west of the Cascades, it is one of the dominant trees, growing alongside Douglas fir, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce.

Old-growth western red cedars are immense. The largest specimens exceed 200 feet in height and 20 feet in trunk diameter. The Quinault Lake Red Cedar in Washington's Olympic Peninsula -- one of the largest known -- has a trunk circumference of over 60 feet. Many of the largest old-growth cedars in accessible lowland forests were logged in the 19th and early 20th centuries, but specimens of extraordinary size survive in national parks, wilderness areas, and protected groves throughout the PNW.

The bark is thin, fibrous, and stringy -- reddish-brown on young trees, weathering to gray on old ones. It peels in long, vertical strips, which is exactly how Coast Salish bark harvesters gathered it: pulling long strips from the living tree without killing it, a sustainable harvest practice that allowed the same tree to provide bark for generations.

The foliage is flat, scale-like, and arranged in fan-shaped sprays that give western red cedar its distinctive feathery silhouette. The cones are tiny -- less than half an inch -- and cluster near the branch tips. The tree reproduces both by seed and by layering (low branches touching the ground take root and grow into new trees), which is why old cedar groves often have multiple trunks growing in clusters from a single ancestral root system.

## 2. The Chemistry of Immortality -- Thujaplicin

The heartwood of western red cedar contains a family of chemical compounds called thujaplicins -- tropolone-based molecules that are potent natural fungicides and insecticides. These compounds are the reason western red cedar is the most decay-resistant softwood in North America.

Thujaplicins work by disrupting the metabolism of the fungi and insects that decompose wood. A western red cedar heartwood fence post in ground contact will outlast a Douglas fir post by decades. A cedar log on the forest floor may remain sound for 50-100 years while the hemlock and fir around it have rotted to soil. A cedar shingle roof in the Pacific Northwest's relentless rain can last 30-50 years before needing replacement.

This chemistry is an evolutionary strategy. The Pacific Northwest rainforest floor is one of the most fungally active environments on Earth. A tree that cannot defend its wood against fungal decomposition has no advantage in this environment. Western red cedar's thujaplicin investment is the reason the species persists in a biome that aggressively recycles everything that falls.

The aromatic quality of cedar -- the distinctive, warm, slightly sweet smell that defines cedar closets, cedar chests, and fresh-cut cedar lumber -- comes from these same extractive compounds. The smell is the chemistry. When you open a cedar closet and inhale that scent, you are breathing volatilized thujaplicins and other aromatic hydrocarbons that have been deterring moths and fungi for millions of years.

A practical note: the decay resistance and the aromatics are properties of the **heartwood** only. Cedar sapwood -- the pale outer wood beneath the bark -- contains no thujaplicins and has no more decay resistance than any other softwood. When selecting cedar for exterior applications, specify all-heartwood grades. The color tells the story: heartwood is reddish-brown; sapwood is nearly white.

## 3. Wood Properties

### Janka Hardness: 350 lbf

Western red cedar is soft. Very soft. Softer than eastern white pine (380 lbf), softer than spruce (400-500 lbf), softer than almost everything except balsa (70 lbf) and some tropical species that are technically classified as wood but behave more like foam.

This softness is the primary limitation on cedar's use. It dents easily -- a dropped hammer, a dragged chair leg, a child's toy pushed across a deck, even aggressive fingernail pressure can leave a mark. Cedar is not suitable for flooring, workbench tops, cutting boards, or any surface that will receive regular impact.

But the softness also means cedar works like butter under a sharp blade. A hand plane set for a fine cut glides through cedar effortlessly. A carving knife slices clean, controlled strokes with minimal effort. An axe splits cedar along the grain with a single blow -- the wood's straight, even grain and low density make it one of the easiest species to split, which is why Coast Salish builders could produce house planks from cedar logs using only wooden wedges and stone mauls.

### Dimensional Stability

Cedar has one of the lowest volumetric shrinkage rates of any commercial timber -- roughly 5% from green to oven-dry, compared to 12% for Douglas fir and 16% for red oak. This means cedar moves less with changes in humidity than almost any other wood. A cedar deck board expands and contracts minimally across seasons. A cedar shingle holds its shape through years of wet-dry cycling.

This stability is directly related to the low density. Less wood substance per unit volume means less hygroscopic material to absorb and release moisture, which means less dimensional change.

### Specific Gravity: 0.32

Cedar is light. A board foot of dry western red cedar weighs about 2 pounds. A board foot of Douglas fir weighs about 3 pounds. A board foot of white oak weighs about 4.2 pounds. This lightness makes cedar easy to handle, easy to lift into position on a fence or wall, and easy to carry to a job site. It also means less structural loading on the fasteners and framework that support cedar installations.

## 4. Coast Salish Uses -- The Original Woodworking Tradition

The Coast Salish, Haida, Tlingit, Nuu-chah-nulth, and other Indigenous peoples of the Northwest Coast built civilizations around this tree. The depth and sophistication of their cedar woodworking rivals any tradition in history.

### Longhouses and Planks

Coast Salish longhouses were framed with massive cedar posts and beams, with walls and roofs clad in wide cedar planks. The planks were not sawn -- they were split from standing logs using wooden wedges and stone hammers, producing boards up to three feet wide and twenty feet long from a single log section. The wood's straight, even grain made this splitting reliable and predictable. The planks could be removed and reassembled at seasonal camp sites, making the house a modular, portable structure.

### Canoes

The Coast Salish dugout canoe is one of humanity's great woodworking achievements. A single cedar log -- sometimes 60 feet long -- was felled, hollowed, shaped with adzes and fire, filled with water and hot stones to steam the hull, and spread to a beam (width) greater than the original log diameter. The result was an ocean-going vessel that could carry dozens of people, tons of cargo, and navigate the open Pacific. See [WDC-09] for the full canoe-building description.

### Totem Poles

Totem poles were carved from single western red cedar logs, sometimes exceeding 60 feet in length. The carvings depict family crests, clan histories, mythological narratives, and commemorative events. The art form reached its highest expression among the Haida and Tlingit peoples of the northern coast. The wood's softness allowed detailed carving with stone and bone tools; its decay resistance ensured that the poles stood for decades or longer even in the rainforest climate.

### Bentwood Boxes

Bentwood boxes were made from a single plank of cedar, steamed and bent at three corners to form the sides of a box, with the fourth corner sewn or pegged closed and a separate plank fitted as the bottom. The result was a watertight container used for storage, cooking (water in the box was heated by adding fire-heated stones), and ceremonial purposes. The bending technique -- kerfs cut at the bend points, steam applied to soften the wood, then bending to precise right angles -- required intimate knowledge of the wood's properties.

### Bark, Roots, and Boughs

Cedar bark was stripped from living trees in long sections, then processed into fiber for weaving: clothing (capes, hats, skirts), rope, baskets, and mats. The inner bark -- softer and more pliable than the outer bark -- was beaten with a stone beater until it became as soft as cloth. Cedar-bark clothing was waterproof and insulating -- essential in the rainy Pacific Northwest climate.

Cedar roots were dug, split, and woven into baskets of extraordinary tightness and beauty. Some baskets were woven so tightly they could hold water.

Cedar boughs were used in ceremonial cleansing, as bedding, as insulation, and in traditional medicine.

## 5. Modern Uses

### Fence Posts (50+ Year Ground Contact)

Cedar fence posts in ground contact outlast every alternative except pressure-treated lumber, and they do it without chemicals. A 4x4 all-heartwood cedar fence post, set 2 feet deep in well-drained PNW soil, can last 15-25 years -- longer in dry soil, shorter in standing water. A 6x6 post with substantial heartwood can exceed 30 years. These are conservative estimates based on the heartwood's thujaplicin content; individual results vary with soil conditions, drainage, and the specific post's percentage of heartwood.

For residential fencing, cedar is the default material in the PNW. Cedar fence boards ($2-5 per 6-foot board, depending on grade) weather to silver-gray within 2-3 years if left unfinished, and the gray is considered beautiful in the Pacific Northwest aesthetic. If you prefer to maintain the reddish-brown heartwood color, apply a penetrating semi-transparent stain (Cabot Australian Timber Oil, $35-45/gallon; or TWP 1500 Series, $40-50/gallon) every 2-3 years.

### Deck Boards

Cedar decking is a PNW tradition. The wood stays cool underfoot in summer (unlike composite decking, which absorbs heat), it does not splinter as aggressively as Douglas fir, and its decay resistance handles PNW rain without pressure treatment. Standard cedar decking comes in 2x6 and 5/4x6 profiles, in lengths up to 16 feet.

Cedar decking grades: **Architect Clear** (no knots, all heartwood -- premium and expensive at $3-5 per linear foot), **Architect Knotty** (tight knots allowed -- $2-3.50 per linear foot), **Standard/Better** (more knots, some sapwood -- $1.50-2.50 per linear foot). For a deck that will see heavy foot traffic, the tight-knot grades are often the best balance of cost and performance.

### Siding (Shingles and Clapboard)

Cedar shingles and shakes have been the signature roofing and siding material for Pacific Northwest homes since settlement. Shingles are machine-sawn to a uniform taper; shakes are hand-split (or split-and-resawn) for a rougher, more textured appearance.

Cedar bevel siding (clapboard) -- tapered boards overlapped horizontally -- is the classic PNW house siding. Clear all-heartwood cedar bevel siding, properly installed and maintained with a penetrating stain, lasts 30-50 years. The alternative -- fiber cement or vinyl -- lasts comparably but lacks the warmth and texture that make cedar siding unmistakably Pacific Northwest.

### Outdoor Furniture

Cedar Adirondack chairs are the iconic PNW outdoor seating. The wood is light enough to move easily, decay-resistant enough to survive years of rain, and soft enough to carve and shape with basic tools. A cedar Adirondack chair left unfinished on a PNW patio will gray to silver within a year and continue to serve for a decade or more without any maintenance. Plans for building a cedar Adirondack chair are included in the WDC project guides.

### Sauna Lining

Cedar is the standard interior lining for saunas and steam rooms. The wood stays cool to the touch even in high heat (low thermal conductivity due to low density), it does not exude resin at sauna temperatures (unlike pine or fir), the aromatics are pleasant and therapeutic in steam, and the decay resistance handles the intense moisture cycling of sauna use. Western red cedar sauna lining is available as tongue-and-groove boards in 1x4 and 1x6 profiles at specialty lumber dealers.

### Closet Lining and Cedar Chests

The aromatic compounds that deter fungi also deter moths -- or more precisely, they deter the larvae of clothes moths that would otherwise eat wool, silk, and other animal fibers. Cedar closet lining (thin, tongue-and-groove planks sold in bundles at home centers for $25-40 per 15-square-foot package) freshens a closet and provides mild moth deterrence. The effectiveness diminishes over years as the surface volatiles evaporate; light sanding with 120-grit paper refreshes the surface and releases new aromatics.

## 6. Working with Cedar -- Tools and Techniques

### Cutting

**Carbide blades.** Cedar's natural oils and extractives dull high-speed steel blades faster than other softwoods. Use carbide-tipped saw blades and carbide-insert planer cutterheads for power tool work. A 40-tooth carbide combination blade on a table saw cuts cedar cleanly for years between sharpenings.

For hand saws, standard crosscut and rip teeth work well -- cedar is so soft that even a moderately sharp saw cuts efficiently. A Japanese pull saw (dozuki or ryoba) produces exceptionally clean cuts in cedar.

### Fastening

**Pre-drill for screws.** Cedar splits easily, especially near edges and ends. Pre-drilling pilot holes for all screws prevents splitting. For deck screws, use a countersink bit that creates a pilot hole and a countersink in one pass -- faster and more consistent than drilling two separate operations.

**Use stainless steel or coated fasteners.** Cedar's extractives are mildly acidic and react with bare steel fasteners, producing black staining around the fastener head. Use stainless steel screws (more expensive but zero staining) or coated deck screws (ACQ-compatible coatings resist the reaction). Never use bare galvanized nails or screws in cedar -- the reaction produces ugly black streaks that are extremely difficult to remove.

### Finishing (or Not Finishing)

Cedar outdoors needs no finish. Left bare, it weathers to a consistent silver-gray within 1-3 years, depending on UV exposure and rain. This gray is stable -- the wood does not continue to deteriorate visually after the initial weathering. Many PNW homeowners prefer the gray, considering it the natural expression of cedar in its native climate.

If you want to maintain the original reddish-brown color, apply a penetrating semi-transparent stain. Film-forming finishes (paint, solid stain, polyurethane) do not adhere well to cedar's oily heartwood and will peel, blister, and fail within a few years -- especially on horizontal surfaces that take direct rain and UV. The proven exterior cedar finishes:

- **TWP 1500 Series** ($40-50/gallon): A penetrating oil-based stain formulated specifically for cedar and hardwoods. Excellent UV protection and water repellency. Recoat every 2-3 years.
- **Cabot Australian Timber Oil** ($35-45/gallon): Originally formulated for dense tropical hardwoods but excellent on cedar. Deep penetration, rich color, good durability.
- **Penofin** ($40-55/gallon): A Brazilian rosewood oil-based penetrating finish. Premium performance at a premium price.

For interior cedar -- closet lining, sauna, paneling -- **no finish is needed or wanted.** The aromatic oils ARE the finish. Applying varnish or polyurethane seals in the aromatics, defeating the purpose of using cedar in the first place.

### Gluing

Cedar glues well with PVA (Titebond II or III for exterior applications) if the surfaces are freshly machined or sanded. The natural oils on the surface of aged or weathered cedar can interfere with glue adhesion -- wipe glue surfaces with acetone or denatured alcohol before applying glue to remove surface oils.

## 7. Where to Buy in the PNW

**Dunn Lumber** (Seattle/Bellevue, 10 locations across western Washington): The best cedar selection in the Puget Sound region. Stocks clear and knotty grades in dimensional lumber, decking, siding, fencing, trim, and specialty profiles. Staff knows cedar. Walk in, tell them your project, and they will guide you to the right grade. Prices are fair for the quality -- not the cheapest, but the selection justifies the premium.

**Mr. Plywood** (Portland, multiple locations): Good cedar siding, decking, and fence board selection. Competitive pricing on volume purchases.

**Crosscut Hardwoods** (Seattle, Georgetown): Carries select and clear grades of western red cedar for furniture and fine woodworking applications -- the kind of clear, all-heartwood material that home centers do not stock. Expect to pay $8-15 per board foot for premium clear cedar.

**Hardwood Industries** (Oregon City, OR): Stocks cedar alongside their extensive hardwood inventory. Good source for clear cedar boards in larger dimensions.

**Home centers** (Home Depot, Lowe's): Stock cedar fence boards, deck boards, and basic dimensional lumber. Quality is variable -- expect to sort through the stack to find heartwood-dominant boards. Pricing is the lowest available, but the selection and grading are below what dedicated lumber yards offer.

## 8. Old-Growth vs. Second-Growth -- The Ongoing Issue

Old-growth western red cedar -- the centuries-old trees with tight grain, dense heartwood, and the highest thujaplicin concentrations -- was logged heavily through the 20th century. The remaining old-growth stands are predominantly on protected federal land.

Second-growth cedar (plantation-harvested at 40-80 years) has wider grain, less heartwood proportionally, and lower extractive concentration. It is still decay-resistant -- still cedar, still containing thujaplicins -- but measurably less so than old-growth. A second-growth cedar fence post will not last as long as an old-growth post in the same soil.

The climate change dimension adds urgency. Western red cedar is showing signs of stress across its southern range -- drought-related dieback and increased susceptibility to fungal pathogens in areas where summer temperatures and drought severity are increasing. The species that evolved for the PNW's wet, mild climate may face range contraction as the climate warms. This is not a future concern -- it is a present one, documented by foresters and ecologists across the region.

For woodworkers, the practical implication is to use cedar wisely. Choose it for applications where its properties matter -- exterior, decay-critical, aromatic -- rather than using it generically. And when possible, source from certified sustainable operations (FSC or SFI certification) that manage their cedar harvest for long-term forest health.

## 9. The Logging History

The western red cedar logging history is inseparable from the broader Pacific Northwest timber story, but cedar had particular significance. Cedar's decay resistance made it the first timber harvested for shingle production -- the shingle industry was one of the earliest commercial timber operations in the PNW, predating the large-scale Douglas fir logging that followed.

The shingle weavers -- the workers who operated the shingle-cutting saws in the mills -- had one of the most dangerous jobs in the timber industry. The saws were fast, unguarded, and operated by hand. Lost fingers were so common that the shingle weavers organized some of the earliest timber labor unions in the region.

Cedar's cultural significance to Indigenous peoples added a dimension of loss to the logging that went beyond economics. Sacred groves, culturally modified trees (trees that had been harvested for bark or planks by Indigenous peoples and bore the marks of that harvest), and canoe trees (trees selected for future canoe carving) were logged alongside every other cedar in the path of industrial harvest. The cultural destruction paralleled the ecological destruction.

Today, culturally modified trees are increasingly recognized and protected, and Indigenous cedar harvesting rights are enshrined in treaty law. But the scale of what was lost -- the old-growth cedar groves that sustained Coast Salish civilization for millennia -- is a wound in the landscape that will take centuries to heal, if it heals at all.

## 10. Why Cedar IS the Pacific Northwest

Every region has a material that defines it. Marble defines Tuscany. Oak defines the English countryside. Cedar defines the Pacific Northwest.

The smell of fresh-cut cedar is the smell of building something in the PNW. The silver-gray of weathered cedar is the color of PNW fences, decks, and garden sheds. The feel of a cedar board in your hands -- light, warm, soft, fragrant -- is the tactile signature of PNW woodworking. The sight of a massive old-growth cedar stump, ten feet across, moss-covered and slowly returning to forest soil, is the visual signature of the PNW landscape.

Cedar connects the Pacific Northwest's past to its present in a way that no other material does. The same tree that provided longhouse planks and bentwood boxes now provides deck boards and fence pickets. The same wood that was carved into canoes and totem poles is now used for garden furniture and sauna lining. The same aromatics that once preserved ceremonial garments now preserve wool sweaters in closets. The applications have changed. The tree has not.

To work with western red cedar in the Pacific Northwest is to participate in a material tradition that stretches back thousands of years. The wood in your hands is the same species, with the same properties, serving the same fundamental purpose -- shelter, protection, beauty -- that it has served since humans first arrived on this coast. Use it with the respect its history demands.
