# Cross-References and Bibliography

## Connections Across the Research Series

Woodcraft does not exist in isolation. Wood comes from forests, which are ecosystems. Forests are shaped by climate, geology, water, and the communities of organisms that inhabit them. Wood is harvested by an industry with deep economic and social roots. It is shaped into objects that serve human needs, from shelter to art. And the knowledge of how to work wood — accumulated over millennia and now transmitted through books, schools, and video — connects to every other form of making and knowing.

This chapter maps the connections between the Woodcraft project and other projects in the Research Series, and provides a bibliography of the essential texts, periodicals, and media that define the field.

## Cross-References to Other Research Projects

### ECO — Pacific Northwest Ecology

The ECO project is the most direct neighbor to WDC. Every piece of wood begins as a tree in an ecosystem, and understanding that ecosystem transforms how you think about the material.

The species profiles in ECO provide the ecological context for the timber species discussed in WDC. Douglas fir's dominance as a construction timber is inseparable from its dominance as an ecological species — its fire adaptation, its relationship with mycorrhizal fungi, its role as the climax species of Pacific Northwest lowland forests. Understanding why Douglas fir grows the way it does (responding to fire cycles, competing for light in dense stands, forming heartwood chemistry to resist decay) explains why it performs the way it does as lumber.

Western red cedar's decay resistance, discussed in WDC Chapter 9, is an ecological strategy — the thujaplicins that make heartwood rot-proof evolved as a defense against the fungal-rich environment of the Pacific Northwest rainforest. The tree's ecology predicts its woodworking properties.

Big-leaf maple's figured wood — the quilted, curly, and spalted figure that makes it one of the most valuable specialty timbers in the world — is an ecological anomaly. The causes of figure are not fully understood, but they involve genetic variation, growth stress, and (in the case of spalting) fungal colonization. ECO's treatment of maple ecology provides the context; WDC's treatment of figured wood provides the application.

The forest succession models in ECO — from alder pioneer stands to mixed conifer climax forests — map directly to the timber industry's progression from old-growth harvest to second-growth plantation management. The ecological understanding in ECO informs the sustainability discussion in WDC Chapter 9.

Red alder's role as a nitrogen-fixing pioneer species, explored in ECO, explains why it is the most available hardwood in the region — it colonizes every disturbed site aggressively, reaching harvestable size faster than any other PNW hardwood. Its abundance and fast growth make it economically accessible for cabinet and millwork, a connection that runs directly from ecology to woodshop.

### FSR — First Responders

The FSR project connects to woodcraft through construction — every building that firefighters enter is a wood structure (in the PNW, almost certainly Douglas fir framing), and building construction type determines fire behavior, collapse risk, and firefighter safety. The building construction sections of FSR-02 (Fire Service) are the fireground application of the construction knowledge in WDC-08. The occupational health parallels are also striking: both woodworkers (dust exposure, tool injuries) and firefighters (carcinogen exposure, physical demands) face long-term health consequences from their craft that require systemic prevention.

Key connections:
- Building construction types (WDC-08) determine fire behavior and firefighter tactics (FSR-02)
- Engineered lumber (LVL, I-joists, CLT) behaves differently under fire than solid wood framing — a critical knowledge gap documented in FSR-02
- Western red cedar dust as a respiratory sensitizer (WDC-04) parallels the carcinogen exposure discussion in FSR-09
- The timber frame tradition (WDC-08) connects to the fire-resistive properties of heavy timber construction — large timbers char on the surface but maintain structural integrity far longer than dimensional lumber

### RCN — Urban Wildlife

The connection between woodcraft and urban wildlife is lighter but real. Raccoons are the most frequent animal-related 911 callers in the Pacific Northwest [FSR-10], and they are also the most common uninvited occupants of wood structures — attics, crawl spaces, sheds, and any wooden building with a gap larger than four inches. The woodworker who builds a shed (WDC-08) in PNW raccoon territory will discover that raccoons are excellent at finding poorly fitted doors, loose soffit panels, and any joinery gap that admits a determined paw. Building tight is pest prevention. The craftsmanship standards in WDC-05 (Joinery) serve a practical function beyond aesthetics: a well-fitted joint is a joint that raccoons cannot exploit.

### FFA — Flora, Fauna, and Agriculture

The FFA project addresses Pacific Northwest agriculture and the biological communities that support it. The connections to woodcraft are through the material cycle — trees grow, are harvested, and the land they occupied enters other uses — and through the specific agricultural applications of woodworking.

Fence posts, barn construction, agricultural buildings, raised bed frames, orchard ladders, vineyard trellis systems — the intersection of farming and woodworking is ancient and ongoing. The choice of species for agricultural applications (cedar for rot-resistant fence posts, Douglas fir for structural members, alder for light construction) reflects the same material science discussed in WDC Chapter 2.

FFA's coverage of Pacific Northwest soils and climate provides context for why certain tree species thrive in certain locations — why Douglas fir dominates the well-drained slopes while western red cedar and Sitka spruce dominate the wet lowlands and coastal strips. Soil composition, rainfall patterns, and temperature ranges determine which trees grow where, and therefore which timber is locally available for woodworking.

The pollinator and habitat sections of FFA also connect to the timber industry's environmental impact. Clearcut logging destroys habitat. Selective logging maintains it. The forestry practices discussed in WDC Chapter 9 have direct consequences for the ecological communities documented in FFA.

### THE — Theater, Performance, and Stage Design

The connection between woodcraft and theater is through set construction and scenic design. Theatrical set building is woodworking under unusual constraints — the structures must be lightweight (for scene changes), appear solid (from the audience perspective), be built quickly (tech week deadlines), and be completely disposable (struck after the run).

Set construction uses techniques that a furniture maker would find alien: hollow flats made from 1x3 lumber and lauan plywood, platforms built from 2x4 frames and plywood skins, moldings and architectural details made from foam or vacuum-formed plastic. The wood is structural but not precious. The finish is scenic paint, not hand-rubbed oil.

But the underlying skills — cutting to dimension, building square frames, understanding structural loads, working safely with power tools — are the same skills discussed throughout WDC. Many professional carpenters trace their initial training to high school or college theater scene shops, where the urgency of production deadlines compresses years of casual learning into weeks of intensive practice.

THE's discussion of stage rigging and counterweight systems also connects to timber framing (WDC Chapter 8) — the grid above a theatrical stage is, in many older theaters, a timber frame structure. The load calculations, the joinery, and the structural principles are shared.

### INP — Infrastructure and Public Works

INP covers the infrastructure that underlies the built environment — roads, bridges, water systems, utilities. The connection to woodcraft is through timber bridges, utility poles, and construction formwork.

Timber bridges are still built, particularly in rural areas and park settings. Glulam beams, treated timbers, and CLT decks are used for pedestrian bridges, park access roads, and low-traffic vehicular bridges. The structural engineering principles in INP connect to the construction framing and mass timber discussions in WDC Chapters 8 and 9.

Utility poles — the treated Douglas fir and western red cedar poles that carry power and communication lines throughout the Pacific Northwest — are a major product of the regional timber industry. A utility pole must meet specific structural requirements (strength class, minimum circumference, straightness) and undergo pressure treatment to resist decay for decades of outdoor exposure. The wood science in WDC Chapter 2 and the PNW species profiles in Chapter 9 both contribute to understanding this application.

Construction formwork — the temporary molds into which concrete is poured — is a major consumer of plywood and dimensional lumber. Formwork is not glamorous, but it is essential, and the skills required to build it accurately (the concrete does not care about your opinion; it will take the shape of whatever you built) are genuine woodworking skills.

### PNT — Painting, Printmaking, and Visual Art

The connection between woodcraft and visual art runs in two directions. First, woodworking tools and techniques are used directly in art-making. Woodblock printing (relief printmaking from carved wood blocks) is one of the oldest graphic art forms. Woodcarving — from whittled figures to monumental sculpture — is a major art tradition. Wood panel painting (oil or tempera on prepared wood panels) predates canvas painting by centuries.

Second, the aesthetic principles that govern visual art — proportion, balance, rhythm, the relationship of positive and negative space — are the same principles that govern furniture design (WDC Chapter 7). A well-proportioned chair and a well-composed painting satisfy the eye for the same reasons. The golden ratio, the rule of thirds, the dynamics of asymmetric balance — these tools belong to both disciplines.

PNT's coverage of Pacific Northwest art traditions also connects to the Indigenous woodworking traditions discussed in WDC Chapter 9. Coast Salish, Haida, and Tlingit art — totem poles, bentwood boxes, carved masks, woven cedar bark — is simultaneously visual art and woodcraft. The distinction between art and craft is artificial here. The object is both.

## Essential Texts

### The Foundational Library

**George Nakashima, *The Soul of a Tree: A Woodworker's Reflections* (1981).** This is the most important book about woodworking ever written. Not the most instructional. Not the most comprehensive. The most important, because it articulates the philosophy that elevates the craft from making objects to honoring material. Nakashima writes about giving wood a "second life" — the tree's death in the forest is not an ending but a transformation. His prose is spare, reverent, and occasionally transcendent. The photographs of his furniture — free-edge walnut tables, butterfly-keyed slabs, monumental wall panels — are as moving as the text. Every woodworker should own this book.

**R. Bruce Hoadley, *Understanding Wood: A Craftsman's Guide to Wood Technology* (1980; revised edition 2000).** This is the wood science textbook for woodworkers. Hoadley, a professor of wood technology at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, wrote a book that is simultaneously rigorous and accessible. Everything in WDC Chapter 2 — grain orientation, wood movement, moisture content, mechanical properties, figure, defects — is treated in exhaustive detail in Hoadley's book. If you own one reference on the material science of wood, this is the one.

**James Krenov, *A Cabinetmaker's Notebook* (1976).** Krenov's first book is a meditation on the craft, a call for sensitivity to the material, and a quiet manifesto against production woodworking. His voice is gentle, discursive, and deeply personal. He writes about finding the right piece of wood for a cabinet door — spending hours with a stack of boards, holding each one up to the light, turning it, waiting for the right one to announce itself. This is not efficiency. It is devotion.

**Chris Schwarz, *The Anarchist's Tool Chest* (2011).** Schwarz makes the case for a deliberately limited hand tool kit. His argument: most woodworkers own too many tools and master too few. A small chest of well-chosen, well-maintained hand tools — a few planes, a few chisels, a few saws, a handful of marking and measuring tools — is all you need. The book is opinionated, entertaining, and profoundly practical.

**Chris Schwarz, *Workbenches: From Design and Theory to Construction and Use* (2007).** The definitive book on the workbench. Schwarz surveys the history and design of workbenches from the Roubo to the Nicholson, explains the principles of work-holding, and provides plans for building a bench that will last a lifetime. The workbench chapter in WDC (Chapter 3) draws directly on this tradition.

**Toshio Odate, *Japanese Woodworking Tools: Their Tradition, Spirit and Use* (1984).** Odate, a Japanese woodworker and sculptor who lived and worked in the United States, bridges the gap between Eastern and Western woodworking traditions. His book explains the philosophy, construction, and use of Japanese planes, chisels, and saws with a depth that respects both cultures.

### Specialized References

**Charles H. Hayward, *Woodwork Joints* (1950).** A British reference that catalogs joinery from the simplest butt joint to the most complex compound miter. Out of print but available used, and still one of the most useful joint reference books ever published.

**Graham Blackburn, *Traditional Woodworking Handtools* (1999).** A comprehensive illustrated guide to hand tools — their history, their construction, and their use. Blackburn is an English-trained woodworker whose books combine practical instruction with deep historical knowledge.

**Jack Hill, *Country Chair Making* (2005).** The definitive guide to Windsor and Welsh stick chair construction. Windsor chair making, discussed in WDC Chapter 7, is a specialized discipline, and Hill's book is the entry point.

**The Wood Database (wood-database.com).** An online reference with mechanical properties, workability notes, and photographs for hundreds of wood species. Maintained by Eric Meier, it is the most accessible species-identification and properties resource available.

## Periodicals

**Fine Woodworking Magazine.** Published since 1975 by The Taunton Press, Fine Woodworking is the most respected woodworking periodical in North America. Each issue includes project plans, technique articles, tool reviews, and design discussions at a level that respects the reader's intelligence. The complete archive is available digitally to subscribers, and it is an extraordinary resource — decades of expert instruction on every topic in the craft.

**Popular Woodworking Magazine.** Broader in scope than Fine Woodworking, with more emphasis on projects for intermediate woodworkers and more coverage of power tool techniques. Christopher Schwarz served as editor for years, and his influence on the magazine's approach to hand tools and traditional techniques is evident.

**Woodsmith Magazine.** Focused on detailed project plans with step-by-step instructions. The plans are thorough, the cutting lists are accurate, and the jigs and techniques are well-tested. An excellent resource for woodworkers who learn best by building from plans.

**Wood Magazine.** A general-interest woodworking magazine with projects, tool reviews, and technique articles. More accessible to beginners than Fine Woodworking, with a broader range of project types.

## YouTube Channels and Online Resources

The democratization of woodworking knowledge through online video is one of the most significant developments in the history of the craft. The channels listed here represent the best of what is available — instructional content of remarkable quality, freely accessible.

**Paul Sellers (YouTube and paulsellers.com).** Sellers is the most influential hand tool instructor working today. A Welsh-born craftsman with over fifty years of experience, he demonstrates traditional hand tool techniques with clarity, patience, and a philosophy that emphasizes accessibility — you do not need expensive tools or a fancy shop to do excellent work. His blog and YouTube channel contain hundreds of hours of free instruction, from sharpening to dovetails to building complete projects with hand tools alone.

**The Wood Whisperer (Marc Spagnuolo).** A balanced approach combining hand and power tools, with a focus on furniture projects. Spagnuolo is an excellent teacher — clear, systematic, and honest about mistakes and learning curves. His project videos walk through the entire process from design to finish.

**Jonathan Katz-Moses.** Focused on joinery, tool reviews, and technique, with high production values and a pace suited to modern attention spans. Katz-Moses is particularly strong on dovetail instruction and on bridging the gap between hand and power tool approaches.

**Matthias Wandel (woodgears.ca and YouTube).** An engineer turned woodworker, Wandel approaches the craft from the perspective of problem-solving and mechanism design. His pantorouter (a shop-built joint-cutting machine), his gear-making techniques, and his approach to jig design are unique in the field.

**Rob Cosman.** A Canadian hand tool specialist known for his dovetail instruction and his emphasis on precision. Cosman's dovetails are freakishly clean, and his instructional videos break down the technique into learnable steps.

**Frank Howarth.** Combines woodworking with architecture, stop-motion animation, and visual storytelling. His workshop tours and project videos are among the most visually polished in the field.

**Rex Krueger.** Focused on beginner-accessible woodworking with inexpensive tools. Krueger's "Woodworking for Humans" series demonstrates that entry-level hand tools (many available for under twenty dollars) can produce genuine furniture.

**Ishitani Furniture (YouTube).** A Japanese furniture maker who publishes near-silent videos of his work process. No narration. No music (or minimal ambient sound). Just hours of a skilled craftsman building furniture with hand and power tools in a beautifully organized shop. The videos are meditative, educational, and astonishingly popular — millions of views from an audience that watches a man make furniture in silence.

## Woodworking Schools and Programs

**The Center for Furniture Craftsmanship (Rockport, Maine).** A residential school offering workshops, intensives, and a twelve-week comprehensive course in furniture making. The instructors include some of the finest working furniture makers in North America.

**North Bennet Street School (Boston, Massachusetts).** One of the oldest trade schools in the United States, with a full-time cabinet and furniture making program that trains students to professional-level skill in traditional techniques.

**The Krenov School (College of the Redwoods, Fort Bragg, California).** Founded by James Krenov, this fine furniture program emphasizes hand-tool work, design sensitivity, and the personal relationship between maker and material. The program continues Krenov's philosophy.

**Marc Adams School of Woodworking (Franklin, Indiana).** Offers week-long workshops taught by a rotating roster of nationally known instructors. The breadth of topics — from hand-cut dovetails to CNC programming — reflects the full range of modern woodworking practice.

**Homestead Heritage School of Woodworking (Waco, Texas).** Part of a broader craft community, with courses in traditional hand-tool woodworking, timber framing, and chair making.

## Online Courses and Communities

**Woodworkers Guild of America (WWGOA).** A membership site with video instruction covering tools, techniques, and projects.

**Lost Art Press (lostartpress.com).** Chris Schwarz's publishing imprint, producing high-quality books on traditional woodworking. The blog is also a valuable resource for discussion of hand tools, workbenches, and design.

**LumberJocks (lumberjocks.com).** An online community of woodworkers who share projects, discuss techniques, and review tools. The project gallery is an inspiration.

**Reddit r/woodworking.** A large, active community with everything from beginner questions to professional-grade project showcases. The quality varies, as with all open communities, but the breadth of experience represented is remarkable.

**Sawmill Creek (forums.sawmillcreek.org).** An older forum community with deep expertise in timber framing, sawmilling, and traditional woodworking.

## Museums and Collections

**The Nakashima Woodworkers (New Hope, Pennsylvania).** George Nakashima's studio and showroom, maintained by his daughter Mira. Open to visitors by appointment. The collection of slabs, the workshop, and the finished pieces on display are a pilgrimage for anyone who cares about wood.

**The Wharton Esherick Museum (Malvern, Pennsylvania).** Esherick's studio and home, preserved as a museum. Every surface, every staircase, every door handle was made by Esherick. It is a total work of art.

**The Smithsonian American Art Museum (Washington, D.C.).** The Renwick Gallery's permanent collection includes studio furniture by Maloof, Nakashima, Castle, and others.

**The Museum of Fine Arts, Boston.** One of the finest collections of Ming dynasty Chinese furniture outside of China, alongside American studio furniture.

**The Burke Museum (Seattle, Washington).** Northwest Coast Indigenous art and artifacts, including canoes, bentwood boxes, and carved objects that represent the highest achievement of Pacific Northwest woodcraft.

## A Final Word on Sources

The best source for learning woodworking is not a book, a video, or a class. It is the wood itself. Pick up a board. Look at the end grain. Identify the orientation of the rings. Predict which way it will cup as it dries. Plane a surface and watch the shaving. Listen to the sound of the blade. Feel the moment when the plane catches a bit of reversing grain and the shaving tears instead of curling.

Books and videos and schools prepare you for this conversation with the material. They give you vocabulary, technique, context. But the conversation itself happens in the shop, between your hands and the wood. That conversation is the oldest tradition in the craft, and it needs no bibliography. It only needs you, and a piece of wood, and the willingness to listen.
