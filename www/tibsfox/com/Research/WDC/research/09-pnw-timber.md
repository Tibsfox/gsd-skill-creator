# PNW Timber

## The Trees That Built the Northwest

If you stand in an old-growth forest in the Pacific Northwest — in the Olympics, in the North Cascades, along the Oregon Coast Range, anywhere the logging never reached — you stand among the largest living organisms on Earth. Douglas firs two hundred feet tall, their canopies lost in mist. Western red cedars with trunks so wide that a family could live inside one, and some families did. Sitka spruce at the edge of the ocean, shaped by salt wind into forms that no landscape architect would dare to propose.

These trees built the Pacific Northwest. Not metaphorically. Literally. The cities, the railroads, the ships, the bridges, the homes — all of it came from these forests. The timber industry that extracted these trees was the economic engine of the region for over a century. It shaped the demographics, the politics, the culture, and the landscape in ways that are still visible from any highway in western Oregon or Washington.

This chapter is about the trees themselves — their properties, their histories, their uses — and about the industry that turned them into lumber, the controversies that industry created, and the future that is taking shape in mass timber and sustainable forestry.

## Douglas Fir — THE Construction Timber

Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) is not a true fir. It is not a spruce, not a pine, not a hemlock. It is its own genus — *Pseudotsuga*, "false hemlock" — a tree so distinctive that taxonomists had to invent a category for it. It is also, by virtually any measure, the most important structural timber in North America.

Douglas fir grows from British Columbia to central California, from the Pacific coast to the Rocky Mountains. The coastal variety, growing in the wet, mild climate west of the Cascades, produces the largest trees and the finest lumber. Old-growth coastal Douglas fir reached heights of 300 feet or more, with trunk diameters exceeding 15 feet. The tallest known Douglas fir, in the early twentieth century, was measured at 393 feet — taller than any living tree today.

The wood is exceptional for construction. It has a higher strength-to-weight ratio than any other common softwood. Its modulus of elasticity (stiffness) and modulus of rupture (bending strength) are among the highest in the softwood class. It holds nails and screws well. It accepts pressure treatment. It weathers to a silver-gray that many find beautiful. And it was — and remains — abundant.

Douglas fir is the standard framing lumber of the Pacific Northwest. When a house is framed in western Washington or Oregon, the studs, joists, rafters, and beams are almost certainly Douglas fir. It is also the standard for structural glulam (glue-laminated timber) beams and, increasingly, for cross-laminated timber (CLT) panels.

The grain of Douglas fir is pronounced — wide earlywood bands and narrow, dense latewood bands that create a bold pattern. Old-growth timber, which grew slowly in dense forest shade, has tight, even grain with narrow rings. Second-growth timber, from faster-growing plantation trees, has wider rings and lower density. The difference is measurable: old-growth Douglas fir can have a specific gravity of 0.55 or higher, while plantation-grown second-growth might be 0.45 or below. The structural grades reflect this — premium Douglas fir lumber is still sorted by growth ring density.

Vertical grain (quarter-sawn) Douglas fir was the premier siding, trim, and flooring material for Pacific Northwest homes from the 1890s through the 1960s. Tight, straight grain, minimal movement, beautiful figure, and excellent paint-holding characteristics made it the default choice. Today, old-growth vertical grain Douglas fir is scarce and expensive, but it remains the gold standard.

## Western Red Cedar — The Tree of Life

Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) — not a true cedar, but an arborvitae — is the most culturally significant tree in the Pacific Northwest. The Coast Salish, Haida, Tlingit, and other Indigenous peoples of the region built their civilization around this tree. They called it the Tree of Life, and the name is not hyperbole.

Western red cedar is soft, lightweight, easily split, and extraordinarily resistant to decay. Its heartwood contains thujaplicins — naturally occurring fungicides that prevent rot and repel insects. A western red cedar log can lie on the forest floor for decades and remain sound while the Douglas fir and hemlock around it decompose. This decay resistance made it the material of choice for every application that contacts water or weather.

### Coast Salish Canoes

The Coast Salish dugout canoe is one of the great achievements of woodworking. A single western red cedar log — sometimes sixty feet long or more — was felled, hollowed, shaped, and steamed into a vessel that could navigate ocean swells, river rapids, and the intricate waterways of Puget Sound and the Salish Sea.

The canoe was roughed out from a selected log using controlled fire and stone adzes. The hull was thinned to a uniform wall thickness — remarkably thin, sometimes less than two inches — by a process that required an intimate understanding of the log's internal structure. The builder could not see inside the log. The thickness was gauged by drilling small holes from the outside and measuring with a stick, or by tapping and listening to the sound.

The hull was then filled with water and hot stones to steam the wood from the inside. The softened cedar was spread to the desired beam (width), and thwarts were wedged in to hold the shape as the wood cooled and set. The result was a vessel wider than the log it came from — an engineering technique that was the functional equivalent of steam bending, achieved with fire and hot water.

Beyond canoes, western red cedar provided longhouse planks (split from logs with wooden wedges), totem poles, bentwood boxes (a single plank steamed and bent at three corners, then sewn or pegged at the fourth), clothing (bark stripped and beaten into a soft fiber for weaving), rope, baskets, and medicine. There is no aspect of traditional Coast Salish material culture that does not involve this tree.

### Modern Uses

Today, western red cedar is the premium material for exterior applications. Cedar shingles and shakes are the classic roofing and siding material for Pacific Northwest homes. Cedar decking and fencing resist rot without chemical treatment. Cedar hot tub staves are a regional tradition.

Cedar lumber is also prized for closet lining (the aromatic oils repel moths), sauna construction (the wood stays cool to the touch and withstands heat and humidity), and outdoor furniture (Adirondack chairs, garden benches, planter boxes). For exterior applications, the best source in the PNW is a dedicated cedar mill or lumber yard rather than a home center -- **Dunn Lumber** (Seattle/Bellevue, multiple locations) carries a deep cedar inventory including tight-knot and clear grades. **Mr. Plywood** (Portland, multiple locations) stocks cedar siding, decking, and fence boards. For premium clear cedar suitable for furniture, **Crosscut Hardwoods** (Seattle) and **Hardwood Industries** (Oregon City) carry select and clear grades that home centers do not stock. Expect to pay $6-12 per board foot for clear all-heartwood cedar, depending on grade and width -- significantly more than construction-grade, but the difference in durability and appearance justifies the premium for any piece that will spend its life outdoors in PNW rain.

The limitation of western red cedar is its softness. With a Janka hardness of 350 lbf, it dents easily. It is not suitable for flooring, workbench tops, or any surface that takes impact. Its strength values are modest — adequate for non-structural applications, but not for framing or structural beams.

For a complete species profile covering thujaplicin chemistry, Coast Salish material culture, modern working techniques, finishing recommendations, and detailed PNW sourcing, see [species/western-red-cedar].

## Sitka Spruce — The Resonant Giant

Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) grows in a narrow coastal strip from northern California to Alaska, never more than a few miles from salt air. It is the largest spruce species in the world — old-growth Sitka spruce exceeds 200 feet in height and 15 feet in diameter. The Queets Spruce in Washington's Olympic Peninsula, one of the largest known, stands 191 feet tall with a trunk circumference of 58 feet.

Sitka spruce has the highest strength-to-weight ratio of any wood. Period. This property — exceptional stiffness at very low density — made it the material of choice for two extraordinary applications: aircraft and musical instruments.

### Aircraft

During both World Wars, Sitka spruce was a strategic material. The Wright brothers used spruce for the structural members of their first aircraft. The de Havilland Mosquito, one of the most successful aircraft of World War II, was built largely of wood, with Sitka spruce as the primary structural material. Howard Hughes's H-4 Hercules (the "Spruce Goose") — the largest flying boat ever built — was constructed primarily of birch laminate but earned its nickname because of Sitka spruce's association with aircraft construction.

The "spruce soldiers" of World War I — Army units deployed to the forests of the Pacific Northwest to cut Sitka spruce for the war effort — were a significant chapter in the region's labor history. The demand was urgent enough to mobilize military units for logging, and the conditions in the camps were harsh enough to fuel the labor organizing that shaped the region's politics for decades.

### Guitar Tops

Sitka spruce is the dominant soundboard (top) wood for acoustic guitars. Its combination of stiffness and lightness produces a top that vibrates efficiently across the full frequency range — bright, loud, and responsive. The wood must be clear, straight-grained, quarter-sawn, and from old-growth trees (tight, even growth rings). A guitar-grade Sitka spruce billet — two bookmatched halves from a single quartered section — can cost hundreds of dollars. A top-grade set from master-grade old-growth stock can cost over a thousand.

Martin, Taylor, Gibson, Collings, and virtually every other acoustic guitar maker uses Sitka spruce as their standard soundboard material. The wood's acoustic properties are so well-suited to the application that alternatives (Engelmann spruce, Adirondack spruce, European spruce, cedar) are considered variations on the Sitka standard, not replacements for it.

## Big-Leaf Maple — The Figured Treasure

Big-leaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) is the largest maple species in North America, growing throughout the Pacific Northwest lowlands. As a plain-grained timber, it is a pleasant but unremarkable hardwood — pale, moderately hard (Janka 850 lbf), good for flooring and cabinetry.

But big-leaf maple has a secret. A small percentage of trees — perhaps one in a thousand, perhaps fewer — produce figure of such extraordinary beauty that the wood becomes one of the most valuable timbers in the world.

**Quilted big-leaf maple** displays a three-dimensional, billowed pattern that looks like it is moving. The cause is not fully understood — the grain grows in a rolling, undulating pattern that creates a depth illusion as light plays across the surface. Heavily quilted maple is mesmerizing. A bookmatched pair of quilted maple panels, finished in clear lacquer, looks less like wood than like a pool of liquid gold.

**Curly (fiddleback) big-leaf maple** shows the tiger-stripe pattern familiar from violin backs. The figure ranges from subtle ripple to dramatic washboard.

**Spalted big-leaf maple** — colonized by fungi after the tree falls — shows the dark zone lines and color changes that make spalted wood dramatic. Big-leaf maple spalts readily and evenly, making it one of the best species for spalted turning blanks and veneer.

Figured big-leaf maple is a Pacific Northwest specialty. The major figured-wood dealers — Cook Woods, Northwest Timber, Berkshire Veneer — deal heavily in PNW maple. Quilted maple guitar tops, often paired with a Sitka spruce soundboard, are a signature combination of Pacific Northwest tone woods. Big-leaf maple is also the PNW's signature live-edge wood — wide slabs with quilted or spalted figure, finished in clear lacquer or water-based polyurethane, produce shelves and tables of extraordinary beauty. See [projects/live-edge-shelf] for the complete guide to sourcing, preparing, and finishing live-edge slabs.

## The Other PNW Species

**Oregon white oak** (*Quercus garryana*) is the only native oak of the Pacific Northwest. It is a white oak — ring-porous, hard (Janka 1,360 lbf), with excellent bending properties and natural decay resistance. It was historically used for wagon wheels, ship timbers, and cooperage (barrels). Today, it is valued for furniture and flooring. The species is increasingly scarce, as urban development has consumed much of its native oak savanna habitat.

**Red alder** (*Alnus rubra*) is the most common hardwood in the Pacific Northwest. It grows aggressively in disturbed sites — clearcuts, road edges, riverbanks — and reaches harvestable size in 30 to 40 years. The wood is light (Janka 590 lbf), fine-grained, and takes stain beautifully. It is the standard material for Pacific Northwest cabinet and millwork shops. Its light color and even grain make it an excellent substrate for stained and painted finishes. Alder is also a nitrogen-fixer — its root nodules host bacteria that convert atmospheric nitrogen to plant-usable form, enriching the soil for subsequent tree generations. Red alder is also the PNW's premier spoon-carving wood — fine-grained, moderately hard, free from tree crews, and beautifully responsive to a sharp sloyd knife when green. See [projects/spoon-carving] for the complete guide.

**Pacific yew** (*Taxus brevifolia*) is a small, slow-growing understory tree found throughout the PNW old-growth forests. Its wood is extremely hard (Janka 1,600 lbf), dense, and elastic — the traditional material for archery bows among Indigenous peoples. Pacific yew gained global significance in the 1960s when taxol, a compound extracted from its bark, was found to be an effective treatment for ovarian and breast cancer. The demand for bark nearly threatened the species before taxol was synthesized from other sources.

## The Timber Industry — From First Cut to the Spotted Owl

The Pacific Northwest timber industry began in the mid-nineteenth century and dominated the regional economy for over a hundred years. The forests were vast — an estimated 2 trillion board feet of standing timber in Oregon and Washington alone. The trees were enormous. And the demand for lumber — driven by railroad construction, Western settlement, San Francisco's repeated fires, and the building booms of the twentieth century — seemed inexhaustible.

It was not inexhaustible. By the mid-twentieth century, the old-growth forests of the Pacific Northwest had been reduced to a fraction of their original extent. The remaining old growth was concentrated on federal lands — national forests, national parks, and Bureau of Land Management (BLM) holdings.

The conflict between timber extraction and old-growth preservation came to a head in the 1980s and 1990s with the northern spotted owl controversy. The spotted owl, a species dependent on old-growth forest habitat, was listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act in 1990. Federal court injunctions halted logging on millions of acres of federal land. Timber communities across the Pacific Northwest — towns whose economies were entirely dependent on logging and sawmilling — were devastated. Mills closed. Families lost homes. The cultural identity of an entire region was upended.

The spotted owl controversy was never just about an owl. It was about whether the remaining old-growth forests — the last large stands of thousand-year-old trees in the continental United States — would be logged for short-term economic gain or preserved for ecological, scientific, and aesthetic values that are difficult to monetize but impossible to replace once lost. The question has not been fully resolved. Logging continues on some federal lands under managed harvest plans. Old-growth preservation continues to expand. The timber industry has shifted toward second-growth plantation forestry, which produces smaller, faster-growing trees for a different set of products.

## Sustainable Forestry and Certification

Modern forestry in the Pacific Northwest is increasingly managed under sustainability principles — harvesting trees at a rate that allows the forest to regenerate, maintaining ecological diversity, protecting waterways, and preserving wildlife habitat.

The **Forest Stewardship Council (FSC)** and the **Sustainable Forestry Initiative (SFI)** are the two major certification systems. FSC certification is generally considered more rigorous, requiring compliance with standards that address ecological integrity, community relations, and worker rights. SFI certification is more industry-aligned but still represents a significant improvement over uncertified logging practices.

Certified lumber allows consumers to choose wood that was harvested responsibly. FSC-certified Douglas fir, cedar, and other PNW species are increasingly available from major lumber dealers. The premium is modest — typically 5 to 15 percent above uncertified prices — and the assurance that the wood came from a well-managed forest is worth that premium.

## Cross-Laminated Timber and the Mass Timber Revolution

The most significant development in timber construction in the last fifty years is cross-laminated timber (CLT). CLT panels are made by gluing layers of dimensional lumber together at right angles — like plywood, but with full-dimension lumber instead of thin veneers. The resulting panels — typically five, seven, or nine layers, up to 12 inches thick and 60 feet long — have structural properties comparable to concrete and steel for many applications.

CLT allows wood to be used for building types that were previously the exclusive domain of steel and concrete: mid-rise apartments, office buildings, schools, and public structures. The Brock Commons Tallwood House at the University of British Columbia, completed in 2017, is an 18-story student residence built with a CLT and glulam structure. The Ascent tower in Milwaukee, completed in 2022, is a 25-story mixed-use building with a mass timber structure — the tallest mass timber building in the world at the time of its completion.

The advantages of mass timber are compelling. Wood sequesters carbon — a CLT building stores the carbon that the trees absorbed during growth, rather than releasing it as would happen if the trees died and decomposed. The manufacturing is less energy-intensive than steel or concrete production. The construction is faster (CLT panels are prefabricated and assembled on site with crane operations, like steel). The weight is less (reducing foundation costs). And the exposed wood surfaces in a mass timber building create interior environments that are warm, acoustically pleasant, and psychologically calming — a phenomenon documented in studies on biophilic design.

Douglas fir from the Pacific Northwest is ideally suited for CLT production. Its strength, stiffness, and availability in the long, straight dimensions that CLT production requires make it a natural fit. Several CLT manufacturing plants now operate in Oregon and Washington, and the mass timber industry in the Pacific Northwest is growing rapidly.

The mass timber revolution represents a genuine paradigm shift. For the first time in more than a century, wood is competing with steel and concrete for major urban construction — not as a traditional material looking backward, but as an engineered material looking forward. The trees that built the Northwest's past are being re-engineered to build its future.

## The Forest Remembers

There is a Douglas fir in the Hoh Rain Forest on the Olympic Peninsula that is over a thousand years old. It was a sapling when Norse longships were crossing the Atlantic. It was a young tree when the cathedrals of Europe were being framed in oak. It has survived fire, wind, ice storms, and centuries of Pacific weather. Its trunk is scarred and its crown is broken, but it is alive, and it is still growing.

A board cut from a tree like that carries the memory of a millennium in its grain. The tight rings of dry years and the wide rings of wet ones. The compression wood formed when a neighboring tree fell against it and it grew at an angle for decades before pushing back upright. The dark heartwood at its core, laid down when the Normans invaded England.

This is what it means to work with Pacific Northwest timber. You are working with material that was alive for centuries before you were born and that, properly cared for, will outlast you by centuries more. The forests that produced this timber were not a resource. They were — and where they survive, still are — an ecosystem of almost unimaginable complexity. The wood in your hands is a gift from that ecosystem. Use it with the respect it deserves.
