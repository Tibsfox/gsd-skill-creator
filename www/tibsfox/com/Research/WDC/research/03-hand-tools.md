# The Hand Tool Workshop

## Where the Craft Lives in Your Hands

There is a quality of attention that hand tools demand which power tools do not. When you push a hand plane across a board, you feel the grain through your palms. You hear the change in pitch when the blade encounters a knot. You see the shaving curl and thicken or thin depending on the angle and depth. Every sense participates. The feedback loop between material and maker is unmediated, immediate, and honest.

This is not an argument against power tools. It is an argument for understanding what hand tools teach you about wood before you let a motor make the decisions. Every serious woodworker — even those whose shops are built around machinery — reaches for hand tools at critical moments. The final fitting of a joint. The last whisper off a tenon shoulder. The chamfer that softens an edge. These are hand tool moments because they require judgment that changes with every stroke.

This chapter is about the tools themselves: what they are, how they work, and how to make them sing.

## Chisels — The Essential Edge

If you could own only one woodworking tool, it should be a chisel. A sharp chisel can do nearly everything — cut joints, pare surfaces, chop mortises, clean up machine cuts, carve decorative details. It is the most versatile tool in the shop and the most dependent on the skill of the user.

### Bench Chisels

Bench chisels are the workhorses. They have blades roughly four to six inches long, beveled on one face, flat on the back, and fitted with handles designed to be struck with a mallet. Widths range from a quarter inch to an inch and a half, with quarter-inch, half-inch, three-quarter-inch, and one-inch being the most commonly used sizes.

A good set of bench chisels is the backbone of a hand tool kit. They are used for chopping (driving the chisel into the wood with mallet blows, as in cutting a mortise), paring (pushing the chisel by hand for fine, controlled cuts), and cleaning (removing waste from the bottom of a dado or the corner of a joint).

For beginners, the **Narex Richter** set of four (1/4", 1/2", 3/4", 1", approximately $80-100) is the best value in the market -- Czech-made, Cr-V steel, comfortable handles, and they arrive sharp enough to work after a quick honing. The **Stanley Sweetheart 750** set ($60-80 for four) is a solid budget alternative. For the serious hand tool worker ready to invest, **Lie-Nielsen** bench chisels ($45-55 each, A2 steel) and **Blue Spruce Toolworks** chisels ($85-110 each, handmade in Portland, Oregon) represent the premium tier -- tools you will use for decades.

The steel matters. Modern bench chisels are made from carbon steel, chrome-vanadium steel, or high-speed steel. Carbon steel (like O1) is easy to sharpen and takes a keen edge, but dulls faster. High-speed steel (like A2 or PM-V11) holds its edge longer but takes more effort to sharpen. Japanese chisels (oire-nomi) use a laminated construction — a thin layer of very hard white or blue steel forge-welded to a softer iron body. The hard steel takes an extraordinary edge. The soft body absorbs impact without chipping. This laminated approach is ancient and brilliant.

### Mortise Chisels

Mortise chisels are built heavier than bench chisels — thicker blades, sturdier handles, designed to be driven hard into wood and levered to remove waste. A mortise chisel is essentially a narrow, blunt wedge. You chop straight down across the grain, then pry the waste forward. Repeat, working from the center toward the layout lines. The thick blade resists bending under the prying forces that would snap a bench chisel.

Dedicated mortise chisels are not strictly necessary — many woodworkers use their bench chisels for light mortising. But for cutting deep mortises in hardwood, a proper mortise chisel makes the work faster and safer.

### Paring Chisels

At the other end of the spectrum, paring chisels are long, thin, and light. Their blades can be eight inches or longer, and they are never struck with a mallet. They are pushed by hand, using body weight and careful pressure to slice thin shavings from a surface. Paring chisels are precision instruments, used for fitting joints, trimming tenon cheeks, and cleaning the bottoms of housings.

The long blade provides leverage and control — you can work with your hands well back from the cutting edge, maintaining a consistent angle over a long stroke. A sharp paring chisel in skilled hands can remove shavings so thin they are translucent.

## Planes — The Geometry of Flat

The hand plane is the tool that turns rough wood into a finished surface. At its simplest, a plane is a blade held at a fixed angle in a body that rides on the wood surface. The body registers the blade at a consistent depth, so each stroke removes a uniform shaving. The result, repeated across the surface, is flatness.

### The Bailey Number System

Most Western-style metal bench planes follow the numbering system established by Leonard Bailey and later manufactured by Stanley. The numbers indicate size:

The **#3 smoothing plane** is about nine inches long. It is the finishing plane — the last tool to touch the wood before sanding or applying a finish. Its short sole bridges local low spots, and its fine-set blade produces gossamer shavings that leave a surface so smooth it reflects light.

The **#4 smoothing plane** is ten inches long and the most popular size. It serves the same purpose as the #3 but covers slightly more area. Many woodworkers consider the #4 the one indispensable plane.

The **#5 jack plane** is fourteen inches long. It is the general-purpose plane — long enough to begin flattening a board, short enough to be nimble. The jack plane is typically the first plane to touch a rough board, set for a moderately aggressive cut to remove high spots and get the surface approximately flat.

The **#6 fore plane** is eighteen inches long. It bridges wider areas than the jack, making it better for flattening wide boards and jointing shorter edges.

The **#7 jointer plane** is twenty-two inches long, and the **#8** is twenty-four. These are the planes used for jointing — planing the edges of boards straight and square so they can be glued together for wide panels. The long sole rides over low spots and only cuts the high points, progressively bringing the edge to straight. A well-tuned #7 on a properly prepared edge produces a surface so flat that two boards can be pressed together and held by atmospheric pressure alone, with no glue.

### Specialty Planes

Beyond bench planes, the world of hand planes is vast.

The **block plane** is a small, one-handed plane used for trimming end grain, easing edges, and fine-tuning joints. It has a blade set at a lower angle than bench planes (typically 20 degrees with a bevel-up blade, for an effective cutting angle of about 37 degrees), which helps it slice cleanly through end grain where a steeper angle would tear.

The **shoulder plane** has a blade that extends to the full width of the body, allowing it to trim right into the corner of a rabbet or the shoulder of a tenon. It is a precision fitting tool, used after the joint is roughed out to bring it to a perfect fit.

The **router plane** is an odd-looking device — an L-shaped blade projecting down through a flat sole — used for cleaning the bottoms of dadoes, grooves, and hinge mortises to a consistent depth. It works like a depth stop that cuts.

The **spokeshave** is a short-soled plane with handles on either side, used for shaping curves. Chair makers, boatbuilders, and anyone who works with curved surfaces reaches for the spokeshave constantly. It excels at smoothing convex curves — chair legs, tool handles, spindles, oars.

## Saws — Cutting to the Line

Saws divide into two broad families based on the direction of cut relative to the grain.

### Rip and Crosscut

A **rip saw** cuts along the grain — the fibers run parallel to the cut. The teeth are filed straight across, like a row of tiny chisels, each one severing fibers in sequence. Ripping is the more aggressive cut. A dedicated rip saw for cutting boards to width has large teeth — typically 4 to 7 teeth per inch (TPI) — set for fast material removal.

A **crosscut saw** cuts across the grain — the fibers run perpendicular to the cut. The teeth are filed at an angle, creating knife-like points that sever the fibers on both sides of the kerf before the center waste crumbles out. Crosscut saws have more teeth per inch (8 to 12 TPI) and produce a cleaner, smoother cut.

### Joinery Saws

For cutting joints — tenons, dovetails, miters — smaller saws with finer teeth and thinner blades are used.

The **tenon saw** (also called a back saw) has a steel or brass spine stiffening the top of the blade. This rigidity keeps the blade from flexing during the cut, producing a straight, accurate kerf. A typical tenon saw has 12 to 14 TPI and is filed rip (for cutting tenon cheeks along the grain).

The **dovetail saw** is a smaller backsaw with finer teeth (15 to 20 TPI), used for the delicate cuts required in dovetail joinery. The thin kerf and fine teeth allow precise control at the layout lines.

The **carcass saw** falls between tenon and dovetail, with crosscut teeth for cutting across the grain — tenon shoulders, miter joints, and carcass work.

### Japanese Saws

Japanese saws cut on the pull stroke, which is a fundamental difference from Western saws that cut on the push stroke. Because the blade is in tension during the cut (being pulled taut) rather than compression (being pushed and potentially buckling), Japanese saw blades can be much thinner — often half the thickness of a Western saw blade. Thinner blade means less waste, less effort, and a smoother cut.

The **ryoba** is a double-edged saw with rip teeth on one side and crosscut teeth on the other — a two-in-one tool.

The **dozuki** is a Japanese backsaw, the equivalent of a Western tenon or dovetail saw. Its extremely thin blade and fine teeth (20 to 28 TPI) produce cuts of remarkable precision.

The **kataba** is a single-edged saw without a spine, used for deeper cuts where a backed saw would bottom out.

Many Western woodworkers have adopted Japanese saws for joinery work, keeping Western saws for heavier ripping and crosscutting. The pull-stroke action feels unusual at first but becomes natural quickly, and the quality of cut from a good dozuki is hard to match with any Western saw.

## Marking and Measuring — The Foundation of Accuracy

Every cut begins with a line. The accuracy of the finished piece can never exceed the accuracy of the layout.

The **marking gauge** scribes a line parallel to an edge by dragging a pin or knife blade along the surface at a fixed distance from a fence that rides against the edge. It is used for laying out tenon cheeks, dovetail baselines, rabbet lines, and any operation that requires a line at a consistent distance from a reference surface. A cutting gauge (with a knife blade instead of a pin) produces a cleaner line that also severs the surface fibers, reducing tearout when you cut to the line.

The **marking knife** is preferable to a pencil for precision work. A knife line is thinner and more precise than a pencil line, and it creates a tiny groove that registers a chisel or saw blade exactly at the mark. The difference between knife-line accuracy and pencil-line accuracy is the difference between a tight joint and a sloppy one.

The **combination square** checks for square (90 degrees) and miter (45 degrees), measures depth, and serves as a short straightedge. A good combination square — truly accurate to 90 degrees — is a precision instrument. A cheap combination square is worse than useless because it lies to you.

The **try square** is a dedicated 90-degree reference. A high-quality try square with a hardened steel blade is the most trustworthy right-angle reference in the shop.

The **sliding bevel** (also called a bevel gauge) locks at any angle and transfers that angle to the workpiece. It is essential for dovetails (transferring the dovetail angle), compound miter joints, and any work involving non-right angles.

**Dividers** and **calipers** handle proportional layout and thickness measurement. Dividers can step off equal spaces along a line — invaluable for dovetail layout, where equal spacing matters more than specific dimensions.

## Sharpening — The Skill Behind Every Skill

A dull chisel is not a chisel. It is a dull piece of steel. A dull plane is not a plane. It is a paperweight that makes shavings out of your patience. Sharpening is the gateway skill of hand tool woodworking. Everything depends on it.

The goal is simple in principle: create two flat, polished surfaces that meet at a precise angle to form a keen edge. The bevel face is ground to the appropriate angle (typically 25 degrees for plane blades and bench chisels, 30 degrees for chisels used in harder woods) and then honed through progressively finer abrasives until the edge is sharp enough to shave hair.

### Waterstones

Japanese waterstones are the most popular sharpening medium among hand tool woodworkers. They are synthetic or natural stones that use water as a lubricant. They cut fast, are available in a wide range of grits (from 220 for coarse grinding to 10,000 or higher for final polishing), and provide excellent tactile feedback. The progression is typically: 1,000 grit to establish the bevel, 4,000 to 6,000 grit to refine it, and 8,000 to 10,000 for a mirror polish. A sharp edge from a fine waterstone will slice end grain cleanly and leave a polished surface on soft pine.

The downside of waterstones is maintenance — they must be flattened regularly (they dish as you work) and stored with care (some can crack if they freeze while saturated).

### Diamond Plates

Diamond plates — steel plates with embedded diamond particles — are flat, fast-cutting, and virtually maintenance-free. They never dish. They work with water or dry. They are available in coarse, fine, and extra-fine grits. DMT (Diamond Machining Technology) is the dominant brand, and their DiaSharp line (a continuous diamond surface bonded to a steel plate) is the most popular among woodworkers. A set of three — coarse (325 mesh), fine (600 mesh), and extra-fine (1200 mesh) — covers most sharpening needs.

Many woodworkers use a coarse diamond plate for flattening the back of new chisels and plane blades (a critical step, since the back must be dead flat for the edge to work properly) and then switch to waterstones for the final honing. Others use diamonds exclusively, accepting a slightly less polished edge in exchange for the convenience of a system that never needs maintenance. In the PNW's damp climate, diamond plates have a practical advantage over waterstones: they do not absorb water, they cannot crack from freeze-thaw, and they require no soaking before use. You can sharpen a chisel on a diamond plate in the time it takes a waterstone to soak.

### Stropping

After honing on stones, many woodworkers strop the edge on a leather surface charged with fine abrasive compound (chromium oxide is common — the green compound sold by Woodcraft, Lee Valley, and others). Stropping removes the microscopic burr left by the stones and polishes the edge to its final keenness. A stropped edge is perceptibly sharper than a stone-only edge, and the difference shows in the surface quality of the cut.

A strop is nothing more than a piece of leather (smooth side up, or rough side up — both work, rough side is slightly more aggressive) glued to a flat board, with a thin layer of compound rubbed into the surface. The technique is simple: draw the bevel across the leather, away from the edge (the opposite direction of cutting — you are polishing, not cutting the leather). Five or six strokes on the bevel, flip, two strokes on the back. Ten seconds total. The edge should now shave hair cleanly from your forearm, which is the traditional test and a perfectly practical one.

### The Sharpening Habit

The key insight about sharpening is that it should not be an event — it should be a habit. A chisel that is touched up on a fine stone every few minutes of use never gets truly dull. A plane blade that is stropped between passes stays keen for the entire session. The woodworkers who produce the best work sharpen constantly, almost unconsciously, the way a cook keeps a knife sharp through the rhythm of service.

Paul Sellers teaches beginners to sharpen freehand — no honing guide, just hands on the stone. His argument is that the guide introduces a dependency that slows the work. If you can sharpen freehand, you can touch up an edge in thirty seconds and get back to work. Chris Schwarz advocates for honing guides, particularly the Eclipse-style guide, arguing that consistency matters more than speed for most woodworkers. Both approaches produce sharp edges. The method matters less than the result.

## The Workbench — The Most Important Tool

A workbench is not a table. It is a work-holding system. Its purpose is to hold the workpiece securely while you plane, chisel, saw, and assemble. A good workbench is heavy (so it does not move under planing forces), flat (so it serves as a reference surface), and equipped with vises and dogs that grip work of any size in any orientation.

### The Roubo Bench

The Roubo bench, named for the eighteenth-century French writer Andre-Jacob Roubo who described it in his encyclopedic work on woodworking, is a slab-topped bench with a leg vise on the front and a crochet (hook) or planing stop at the end. Modern interpretations typically add a tail vise or wagon vise for gripping boards between bench dogs.

The Roubo is the bench of choice for most hand tool woodworkers. Its massive slab top (typically three to four inches thick, made from glued-up hardwood or dense softwood) provides a dead-flat reference surface and enough mass to absorb planing forces without vibration. The leg vise, with its large wooden jaw, grips wide boards for edge planing and provides a surface for clamping workpieces at any angle.

Chris Schwarz's book *Workbenches: From Design and Theory to Construction and Use* is the definitive modern reference on bench design. His argument is that the bench is the most important tool in the shop — more important than any plane, any saw, any chisel — because every other hand tool operation depends on solid work-holding.

### The Nicholson Bench

The Nicholson bench (named for Peter Nicholson, the eighteenth-century Scottish architect who described it) is a simpler, more accessible design. It is essentially a stout table with a thick top, a planing stop, and a face vise. It can be built from construction lumber in a weekend, at a cost of well under a hundred dollars.

The Nicholson is an excellent first bench. It teaches the principles of work-holding without the intimidation of a full Roubo build. Many experienced woodworkers keep a Nicholson as a secondary bench or assembly table even after building a more sophisticated primary bench.

## Philosophy — Hand Tools as a Way of Working

Hand tool woodworking is not just a set of techniques. For many practitioners, it is a philosophy — a deliberate choice about the relationship between maker and material.

Paul Sellers, who has taught hand tool woodworking for over fifty years, argues that hand tools are the most accessible entry point to the craft. A beginner can buy a secondhand #4 plane, a set of chisels, a backsaw, and a sharpening stone for under a hundred dollars and begin making furniture. The barrier to entry is not money — it is willingness to practice.

Chris Schwarz, editor and author, has been influential in reviving interest in traditional workbenches and hand tool techniques. His books — *The Anarchist's Tool Chest*, *The Anarchist's Design Book*, *Workbenches* — make a case for a deliberately limited toolkit. His argument is that a small number of high-quality hand tools, thoroughly mastered, can produce better work than a shop full of machines used casually. The limitation forces focus.

The Japanese tradition takes this further. A Japanese carpenter's tool kit fits in a single chest. The tools are few, exquisitely made, and maintained with religious care. The philosophy is that each tool should be fully understood — its geometry, its metallurgy, its behavior in every species of wood — before another tool is added to the kit.

None of this means that hand tools are superior to power tools in some absolute sense. The question is not which is better. The question is what kind of relationship you want with the material. Hand tools slow you down. They make you pay attention. They force you to understand the wood because the wood tells you immediately — through feel, through sound, through the quality of the shaving — whether you are doing it right. That feedback, for many woodworkers, is not the cost of hand tool work. It is the point.

## PNW Hand Tool Culture

The Pacific Northwest has a particularly strong hand tool tradition, fed by several streams. The Japanese pull saw and chisel traditions arrived with Japanese immigrants in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and the proximity to Japan has kept the channel open — PNW woodworkers were among the earliest Americans to adopt Japanese hand tools, and shops in Portland and Seattle carry Japanese tool inventories that rival East Coast specialist dealers. The studio furniture movement in the PNW, influenced by Krenov and by the region's emphasis on working with local figured woods, leans toward hand-tool-intensive approaches — quilted big-leaf maple demands careful hand planing to avoid tearout that a machine planer would produce, and many PNW furniture makers finish their figured surfaces with a hand plane set for a gossamer cut rather than sanding.

The PNW weather also shapes hand tool practice. The damp climate means tools rust fast. A carbon steel chisel left on a bench overnight in an unheated PNW shop will show rust spots by morning. PNW hand tool workers learn early to oil their tools after every session — a habit that becomes as automatic as sharpening. Camellia oil (traditional Japanese tool maintenance oil) and jojoba oil are popular choices. The climate also means that wood in PNW shops tends toward the wet side of equilibrium — a moisture meter is not optional, it is essential, and the hand tool worker develops a feel for the difference between a board at 8% and one at 12% through the way it planes.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** — [tools-safety]: Hand tool safety is fundamentally about sharpness and control. A sharp tool requires less force, gives more control, and produces cleaner results. Every tool in this chapter — chisel, plane, saw, spokeshave — is safer when sharp and properly maintained. The tools-safety concept begins with the tool in your hand.
- **Trades** — [woodworking]: Hand tools are where woodworking lives closest to the material. The feedback loop between hand, tool, and wood is unmediated — you feel the grain, hear the cut, see the shaving. This direct connection is what makes hand tool woodworking the foundation of the woodworking curriculum.
- **Materials** — [fabrication-processes]: Hand tool work is a fabrication process — subtractive manufacturing at the bench scale. Planing, chiseling, and sawing remove material to achieve a desired geometry. The principles of cutting mechanics, surface finish, and dimensional control apply at the hand tool scale just as they do in industrial machining.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: The steel in hand tools — carbon steel, alloy steel, high-speed steel, laminated steel — has measurable properties (hardness, toughness, edge retention) that determine tool performance. Understanding tool steel is materials science applied to the workshop.
