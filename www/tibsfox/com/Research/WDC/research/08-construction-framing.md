# Building Structures

## From Shed to Shelter

There is a threshold that every woodworker eventually approaches: the transition from building things that sit inside a building to building the building itself. The skills are related — you are still cutting wood and joining it together — but the scale changes everything. A furniture joint measured in fractions of an inch becomes a framing connection measured in feet. A tabletop that must be flat across 36 inches becomes a floor that must be flat across 36 feet. A piece of furniture that must last a lifetime becomes a structure that must last generations and protect the people inside it from weather, wind, snow load, and seismic forces.

Construction framing is where woodworking meets civil engineering. Building codes, structural calculations, moisture management, and inspections enter the picture. The improvisation that is acceptable (even celebrated) in furniture making is not acceptable here. A load-bearing wall that is wrong is not an aesthetic failure. It is a safety failure. Understanding this distinction — and respecting it — is the starting point for any woodworker who wants to build structures.

## Platform Framing — How Most Buildings Are Built

Platform framing is the standard construction method for residential buildings in North America. It has been since the mid-twentieth century, when it replaced balloon framing as the dominant technique. The system is elegant in its simplicity: each story is built as a separate platform. The first floor platform is built on the foundation. The first-floor walls are framed on the platform and tilted up into position. A second platform (the second floor) is built on top of the first-floor walls. Second-floor walls are framed on that platform and tilted up. And so on.

The framing members are dimensional lumber — standardized sizes (2x4, 2x6, 2x8, 2x10, 2x12) that are available at any lumber yard. A 2x4 is not actually 2 inches by 4 inches — it is 1.5 by 3.5 inches, the result of surfacing the rough-sawn lumber. This dimensional shrinkage is standard and accounted for in all framing design. The species is typically spruce-pine-fir (SPF) in most of North America, or Douglas fir and hem-fir in the Pacific Northwest.

A wall frame consists of vertical studs (typically 2x4 or 2x6, spaced 16 or 24 inches on center), a bottom plate, and a double top plate. Headers span window and door openings, transferring the load from above around the opening to the studs on either side (jack studs, which support the header, and king studs, which frame the opening). Cripple studs fill the space above headers and below window sills.

Floor framing consists of joists (2x8, 2x10, 2x12, or engineered lumber like I-joists and LVL beams) spanning between bearing walls or beams. The joists are covered with sheathing (plywood or oriented strand board, OSB) to create the platform. Joist size and spacing are determined by the span (the distance between supports) and the expected load — these values come from code-specified span tables that every framer references.

Roof framing is either stick-framed (individual rafters cut and installed on site) or built with trusses (factory-engineered assemblies of lumber and metal connector plates). Trusses have largely replaced stick framing for simple roof geometries because they are faster to install, span greater distances without interior bearing walls, and are engineered for the specific loads. Stick framing is still used for complex roof geometries, exposed rafter tails, and vaulted ceilings.

## Post and Beam — The Ancient Method Modernized

Post-and-beam construction uses large timbers (posts and beams) to create the structural skeleton. The beams carry the loads. The posts support the beams. The spaces between the structural members are filled with non-structural wall systems — stud walls, SIPs (structural insulated panels), or infill framing.

The distinction between post-and-beam and timber framing is sometimes blurred, but the key difference is the joinery. Post-and-beam construction typically uses metal connectors (steel brackets, bolts, lag screws, Simpson ties) to join the timbers. Timber framing uses wood joinery — mortise and tenon, dovetails, scarf joints — with wooden pegs. Both produce buildings with exposed structural timbers, but the feel is different. Metal connectors are utilitarian. Wooden joinery is craft.

Post-and-beam construction is popular for barns, workshops, and homes where the homeowner wants the aesthetic of exposed timbers without the cost and complexity of traditional joinery. The structural timbers can be Douglas fir, oak, white pine, or any species with adequate strength and availability. The connections are designed by a structural engineer to meet code requirements.

## Timber Framing — The Craft at Building Scale

Timber framing is woodworking at its most monumental. The joints are the same ones used in furniture — mortise and tenon, dovetails, scarf joints — but executed in timbers that weigh hundreds of pounds and must be precisely fitted in three dimensions.

A timber frame building begins with design and engineering. The frame must carry roof loads, wind loads, snow loads, and seismic loads. Each joint must be sized for the forces acting on it. A tie beam connecting two posts at the top of a wall might carry a tension load of thousands of pounds — the joint holding it must be sized accordingly.

The layout uses a system of reference faces and marks that ensure each timber is oriented correctly in the finished frame. Every timber is numbered. Every joint is identified by its position in the frame. The timbers are cut on the ground — often in a temporary shop or on a leveled work platform — and the joints are test-fitted before the raising.

The raising is the dramatic climax of a timber frame project. The frame is assembled in sections (bents — the cross-sectional frames of posts and beams that define the building's shape). Each bent is assembled flat on the ground, then raised upright using a combination of ropes, pikes, and muscle power. For a small building, a crew of ten to twenty people can raise the bents by hand. For larger structures, a crane assists.

Modern timber framing has experienced a significant revival since the 1970s, driven by organizations like the Timber Framers Guild and by builders who recognized that the tradition was on the verge of being lost. Today, timber frame homes, barns, churches, and public buildings are being built throughout North America and Europe, using both traditional hand-tool methods and CNC-cut joinery.

## The Shed — The First Project

For a woodworker who wants to build a structure, the shed is the ideal first project. It is small enough to be manageable, simple enough to build without advanced skills, and practical enough to earn its place on the property.

A basic shed — 8 by 12 feet, with a simple gable roof — teaches every fundamental of construction framing: foundation (concrete blocks, gravel pad, or treated-lumber skids), floor framing (joists on the foundation, covered with plywood), wall framing (studs, plates, headers for the door opening), roof framing (rafters or simple trusses), sheathing, roofing, siding, and a door.

A shed does not require a building permit in most jurisdictions if it is under a certain size (often 120 or 200 square feet, depending on the local code). Check with your local building department before starting. Even if a permit is not required, building to code standards is good practice — proper foundation, adequate structural connections, weather-resistant materials.

The shed is also a test of project management. Material lists, cutting lists, a logical assembly sequence, weather contingencies — a shed teaches you how a building comes together, step by step, from foundation to ridge cap. The lessons transfer directly to larger projects.

## Decks — Code and Consequence

A deck is an exterior platform attached to a house. It is one of the most common DIY construction projects and one of the most frequently done wrong. Deck failures — collapses that injure or kill the people on them — occur because the connections between the deck and the house are inadequate, the structural members are undersized, or the hardware is missing.

Building codes for decks have been significantly strengthened in recent decades, driven by the record of catastrophic failures. Key requirements include: the ledger board (the framing member that attaches the deck to the house) must be through-bolted or lagged to the house framing with proper flashing to prevent water intrusion. The posts must be connected to the footings with code-approved hardware. The joists must be connected to the beams and ledger with approved joist hangers. Guardrails must be at least 36 inches high (42 in some jurisdictions) and must resist a 200-pound lateral load at the top.

Deck materials fall into three categories. **Pressure-treated lumber** (ACQ or CA-B treated southern yellow pine or Douglas fir) is the most common and least expensive. It resists rot and insect attack but requires regular maintenance (staining or sealing every two to three years) and eventually weathers to gray. **Composite decking** (wood fiber and plastic, or pure PVC) requires no staining, resists rot and insects, and is available in colors that mimic natural wood. It is more expensive than treated lumber but lower maintenance over its lifespan. **Tropical hardwoods** (ipe, garapa, cumaru) are extraordinarily durable but extraordinarily expensive and difficult to work — ipe is so dense that every fastener must be pre-drilled, and the wood dulls saw blades rapidly.

The structural framing of a deck — posts, beams, joists, ledger — is almost always pressure-treated lumber or treated Douglas fir, regardless of the decking material. The structural members are hidden, and the premium decking materials would be wasted there.

## Foundations — Where the Building Meets the Ground

Every structure needs a foundation — a connection to the ground that distributes the building's weight, resists movement, and separates the wood framing from the moisture in the soil.

**Concrete slab** foundations are poured directly on compacted gravel over prepared soil. They serve as both foundation and floor. They are common in warm climates and for garages, shops, and simple structures. In cold climates, the slab edges must extend below the frost line to prevent heaving.

**Crawl space** foundations use concrete or block perimeter walls that support the floor framing above grade, with a ventilated or conditioned space between the ground and the floor. They are common in moderate climates and on sloped sites. The crawl space provides access to plumbing and ductwork but must be managed for moisture — a vapor barrier on the ground and adequate ventilation or dehumidification are essential.

**Full basement** foundations are the deepest and most expensive. They provide additional usable space below the main floor. In cold climates, where footings must extend below the frost line anyway (four feet or more below grade), the incremental cost of excavating to full-basement depth is modest compared to the value of the additional space.

**Pier** foundations use isolated concrete footings (piers) at the corners and at intermediate points along the building's perimeter. Beams span between the piers and support the floor framing. Pier foundations are economical, minimize site disturbance, and are well-suited for decks, outbuildings, and structures on uneven ground.

The wood framing must be separated from the concrete by a sill seal (a foam gasket) and a moisture barrier, and the sill plates (the first pieces of wood, bolted to the foundation) must be pressure-treated lumber rated for ground contact.

## Roof Framing — The Geometry of Shelter

Roof framing is the most geometrically complex part of residential construction. A simple gable roof is just two planes meeting at a ridge. But add a hip (an angled section at the end of the gable), a valley (where two roof planes intersect at an internal angle), a dormer (a framed projection through the roof plane), or a combination of these, and the framing becomes an exercise in applied geometry.

The **common rafter** runs from the ridge to the wall plate at a consistent pitch (the slope of the roof, expressed as rise over run — a 6/12 roof rises 6 inches for every 12 inches of horizontal run). Every rafter is identical. The **hip rafter** runs from the ridge to the corner of the building at an angle, creating the sloped edge of a hipped roof. The **valley rafter** runs from the ridge into the internal intersection of two roof planes. **Jack rafters** are shortened rafters that run from the hip or valley rafter to the wall plate.

The math involved in calculating rafter lengths, plumb cuts (vertical cuts at the ridge and tail), seat cuts (horizontal cuts where the rafter sits on the wall plate), and the compound angles of hip and valley rafters is straightforward but exacting. A framing square and a set of rafter tables (printed on the square itself, or in a pocket reference) provide all the numbers. The skill is in translating the numbers into accurate cuts on actual lumber.

Trusses bypass most of this geometry. A factory-engineered truss is delivered to the job site ready to install. The trusses are set on the wall plates, braced, and sheathed. For simple roof shapes, trusses are faster, more economical, and structurally superior to stick framing. For complex shapes, custom geometries, and exposed rafter tails, stick framing remains essential.

## Sheathing, Insulation, and the Building Envelope

The framing is the skeleton. The sheathing, insulation, and weather-resistive barrier form the skin.

**Wall sheathing** (typically 7/16-inch OSB or 1/2-inch plywood) is nailed to the outside of the wall framing. It provides racking resistance (preventing the wall from folding sideways), a nailing surface for siding, and a substrate for the weather-resistive barrier (house wrap — a plastic membrane that sheds liquid water but allows water vapor to pass through).

**Roof sheathing** (typically 1/2-inch or 5/8-inch plywood or OSB) is nailed to the rafters or trusses. It provides the substrate for the roofing material (asphalt shingles, metal roofing, or other). An underlayment (tar paper or synthetic underlayment) is installed over the sheathing before the roofing material.

**Insulation** fills the wall and ceiling cavities to reduce heat transfer. Fiberglass batts are the most common and least expensive. Rigid foam board (EPS, XPS, polyiso) provides higher R-value per inch and can be installed on the exterior of the sheathing for a continuous thermal break. Spray foam (open-cell or closed-cell) fills cavities completely and doubles as an air barrier, but is more expensive.

The building envelope — the continuous boundary between conditioned interior space and unconditioned exterior — must be airtight and properly managed for moisture. Air leaks waste energy and carry moisture into wall and roof cavities where it can condense and cause rot. The air barrier (typically the house wrap, sealed at all penetrations and seams) and the vapor retarder (which limits moisture diffusion through the wall assembly) must be continuous and correctly positioned.

Getting the building envelope right is arguably more important than any other aspect of construction. A well-insulated, airtight building is comfortable, energy-efficient, and durable. A poorly sealed building is drafty, expensive to heat, and prone to moisture damage.

## Electrical and Plumbing — The Rough-In

After the framing is complete and before the insulation goes in, the electrical and plumbing systems are roughed in — the wires and pipes are run through the framing to their approximate locations.

**Electrical rough-in** involves running Romex (NM-B cable) from the electrical panel to each outlet, switch, and fixture location. Holes are drilled through studs and joists, the cable is pulled through, and the boxes (electrical boxes at each outlet and switch location) are nailed to the studs. The circuit layout must follow the National Electrical Code (NEC): kitchens and bathrooms require dedicated 20-amp circuits, outlets must be no more than 12 feet apart along walls, GFCI protection is required in wet locations, AFCI protection is required in bedrooms and living areas.

**Plumbing rough-in** involves running supply pipes (copper, PEX, or CPVC) from the water heater and main supply to each fixture, and running drain-waste-vent (DWV) pipes (ABS or PVC) from each fixture to the main drain. The DWV system must be properly vented — every fixture drain must connect to a vent pipe that extends through the roof, allowing air into the system so drains flow freely and sewer gases are exhausted above the building.

Both electrical and plumbing rough-in are inspected before the walls are closed up with insulation and drywall. The inspector verifies that the work meets code requirements. Failing inspection means opening walls to correct the deficiency, which is expensive and demoralizing.

## Building Codes — The Non-Negotiable Framework

Building codes exist because buildings kill people when they fail. Every requirement in the code — the minimum size of a floor joist, the spacing of nails in sheathing, the height of a guardrail, the fire rating of a wall assembly — exists because someone, somewhere, was injured or killed when that requirement was not met.

The International Residential Code (IRC) is the model code adopted (with local amendments) by most jurisdictions in the United States for one- and two-family dwellings. It covers everything: structural design, fire safety, plumbing, mechanical systems, electrical, and energy efficiency.

A building permit is a legal requirement before beginning construction of any structure that meets the local threshold for permitting (typically any building over a certain size, or any building with electrical or plumbing). The permit process involves submitting plans, paying fees, and scheduling inspections at prescribed stages of construction (foundation, framing, rough-in, insulation, final). The inspector's job is to verify that the work meets code. The permit holder's job is to build to code.

Working without a permit is a gamble with serious consequences. Unpermitted work can be ordered removed. It creates liability issues if someone is injured. It complicates insurance claims. And it can derail a future sale of the property.

## DIY vs. Hire — Knowing the Boundary

The question of what to build yourself and what to hire out is one of the most important decisions in construction.

**DIY-friendly tasks:** framing (with study and careful work), sheathing, insulation, siding, roofing (simple roof shapes on single-story buildings), trim carpentry, painting, decking, simple sheds, and basic finish work.

**Hire a professional for:** foundation work (excavation and concrete), structural engineering (any design that deviates from prescriptive code requirements), electrical (unless you are willing to learn the code thoroughly and do it right — a wiring mistake can kill), plumbing (especially DWV systems, which require understanding of slope, venting, and trap seal), roofing on steep or complex roofs, and any work that is beyond your physical capacity or risk tolerance.

The boundary is personal. Some homeowners wire their own houses, pull their own permits, and pass inspection. Others prefer to swing a hammer and leave the systems to licensed trades. The honest assessment is this: if you are willing to study the relevant code requirements, work carefully, have the work inspected, and fix anything the inspector flags, you can do most residential construction yourself. If you are not willing to do all of those things, hire someone who is.

The woodworking skills that brought you to this point — reading plans, cutting accurately, thinking in three dimensions, understanding how materials behave — transfer directly to construction framing. The new skills you need are code literacy, structural awareness, and the humility to know when a task exceeds your competence. That humility, more than any tool in the shop, is what keeps a building standing.
