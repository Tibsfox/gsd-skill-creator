# Understanding Wood

## The Science of the Material

Before you cut a single joint or plane a single surface, you need to understand what wood actually is. Not the abstraction of it — not "building material" or "natural resource" — but the physical reality. Wood is the structural tissue of a tree. It was engineered by evolution over hundreds of millions of years to do specific things: transport water, store energy, resist wind, support weight. Every property that matters to a woodworker — strength, flexibility, figure, movement, rot resistance — exists because it served the tree first. You are working with a material that had a job before it had yours.

This is what makes wood different from every other building material. Steel is manufactured to specification. Concrete is poured to a formula. Wood grew. Every board is a cross-section of a life, and no two lives are the same. Understanding the science of wood is not academic exercise. It is the foundation of every successful project and the explanation for every failed one.

## Hardwood and Softwood — The Botanical Divide

The first distinction most people learn about wood is the division between hardwood and softwood, and the first thing they learn about that distinction is that the names are misleading. Balsa is classified as a hardwood. It is softer than most softwoods. Southern yellow pine is a softwood. It is harder than many hardwoods. The terms refer not to the physical hardness of the wood but to the botanical classification of the tree that produced it.

Softwoods come from gymnosperms — cone-bearing trees, almost exclusively conifers. Pine, spruce, fir, cedar, redwood, hemlock, larch. Their wood is anatomically simpler, composed primarily of a single cell type called tracheids, which handle both structural support and water transport. Under a microscope, softwood looks relatively uniform — long, narrow cells arranged in tidy radial rows.

Hardwoods come from angiosperms — flowering trees, the broadleaf deciduous and tropical species. Oak, maple, cherry, walnut, ash, mahogany, teak, ebony. Their wood is anatomically more complex, with specialized cells for different functions. Vessels (also called pores) handle water transport. Fibers handle structural support. Ray cells handle lateral transport and storage. Under a microscope, hardwood looks like a city — different districts, different architectures, different densities.

This anatomical difference explains why hardwoods and softwoods behave differently under tools, accept finishes differently, and serve different purposes in construction and furniture making. The vessels in ring-porous hardwoods like oak create an open grain that absorbs finish unevenly unless the pores are filled first. The uniform cell structure of softwoods makes them easier to cut but more prone to crushing under concentrated loads. The dense fiber structure of tropical hardwoods makes them extraordinarily durable but difficult to work with dull tools.

The gymnosperm-angiosperm split occurred roughly 300 million years ago. When you choose between pine and oak for a project, you are choosing between two evolutionary lineages that diverged before the dinosaurs.

## How a Tree Grows — Rings, Heartwood, and Sapwood

A tree grows from the outside. The cambium layer — a thin sheath of dividing cells just beneath the bark — adds new wood on its inner face and new bark on its outer face every growing season. In temperate climates, this creates the familiar annual rings: earlywood (large cells, grown fast in spring when water is abundant) and latewood (smaller, denser cells, grown slowly in summer). The contrast between earlywood and latewood is what creates visible grain pattern.

As the tree ages, the inner wood undergoes a transformation. The living cells in the inner rings die. The tree fills them with extractives — resins, tannins, oils, and other chemical compounds that serve as preservatives and pest deterrents. This inner core becomes heartwood. It is typically darker in color, more dimensionally stable, and more resistant to decay than the outer sapwood. The sapwood — the living outer rings that are still actively transporting water — is lighter in color and more susceptible to rot and insect attack.

For the woodworker, this matters. Heartwood is almost always preferred for furniture and exterior applications. The rich color of walnut, the red-brown of cherry, the golden warmth of white oak — these are heartwood colors. Sapwood in these species is pale and bland. Some traditions consider sapwood in fine furniture a defect. Others celebrate the contrast. But the practical difference is real: heartwood resists decay, sapwood does not.

The boundary between heartwood and sapwood is visible on the end grain of a log. In species like black walnut, it is dramatic — dark chocolate heartwood ringed by nearly white sapwood. In species like hard maple, the distinction is subtle. Either way, understanding where your board came from in the tree — how far from the center, how far from the bark — helps predict how it will behave.

## Grain — Flat, Quarter, and Rift

The way a log is cut into boards determines the orientation of the annual rings in the resulting lumber, and this orientation profoundly affects appearance, stability, and strength.

**Flat sawn** (also called plain sawn) lumber is produced by cutting straight through the log in parallel slices. It is the most efficient method — the least waste. The boards near the outside of the log have rings that meet the face at a low angle, producing the characteristic cathedral or flame pattern that most people associate with wood grain. Flat sawn lumber is the most common and least expensive. It also moves the most — it expands and contracts across its width more than any other cut, and it is prone to cupping (curving across its width as one face dries faster than the other).

**Quarter sawn** lumber is produced by first quartering the log and then cutting boards so that the annual rings meet the face at roughly 60 to 90 degrees. The resulting grain pattern is straight and parallel. In species with prominent ray cells — white oak is the classic example — quarter sawing exposes the rays as dramatic flecks of shimmering figure called ray flake or medullary ray figure. Quarter sawn lumber moves less across its width than flat sawn, making it the preferred choice for tabletops, drawer sides, and any application where seasonal movement must be minimized. It is more expensive because the sawing process produces more waste.

**Rift sawn** lumber falls between the two, with rings meeting the face at 30 to 60 degrees. The grain is straight and even, without the cathedral patterns of flat sawn or the ray flake of quarter sawn. Rift sawn lumber is prized for table and chair legs, where consistent straight grain on all four faces creates a visual harmony that flat sawn lumber cannot match.

Understanding these three cuts is not optional knowledge. A tabletop made from flat sawn boards will cup. A drawer side made from flat sawn lumber will expand enough to bind in its case. A door panel that is not accounted for will crack its frame. The cut of the lumber determines the behavior of the project.

## Wood Movement — The Rule That Cannot Be Broken

Wood moves. This is the single most important fact in woodworking, and ignoring it is the single most common cause of project failure.

Wood is hygroscopic — it absorbs and releases moisture from the surrounding air. As it absorbs moisture, it expands. As it releases moisture, it shrinks. This movement occurs almost entirely across the grain — tangentially (parallel to the growth rings) and radially (perpendicular to the growth rings). Movement along the grain is so small as to be negligible.

The magnitude of this movement is not trivial. A 12-inch-wide flat sawn board of red oak can change width by a quarter inch or more between summer humidity and winter dryness. A tabletop made from a 36-inch-wide glue-up of flat sawn oak can move three-quarters of an inch across its width over the course of a year. If the tabletop is rigidly fastened to the apron rails — screwed down tight, with no allowance for movement — something will break. The wood does not care about your joinery. It will move, and it will break whatever tries to prevent it from moving.

Every traditional joinery solution for wide panels accommodates movement. Frame and panel construction lets the panel float in grooves, expanding and contracting without stressing the frame. Tabletop fasteners (Z-clips, figure-eight fasteners, slotted screw holes) allow the top to slide on the apron. Breadboard ends are attached with a single glued tenon at the center and elongated holes for the outer pins, so the top can move while the breadboard keeps it flat.

Moisture content is measured as a percentage: the weight of water in the wood divided by the weight of the wood when completely dry. Green (freshly cut) wood can have moisture content above 50 percent. Kiln-dried lumber is typically brought to 6 to 8 percent. Air-dried lumber settles to the equilibrium moisture content of its environment — roughly 12 to 14 percent in most of North America.

The target for furniture-grade lumber is 6 to 8 percent — matching the typical indoor environment. Wood that is too wet when worked will shrink as it dries, opening joints and warping panels. Wood that is too dry will expand when it reaches its environment, potentially splitting glue joints. A moisture meter is not a luxury. It is a fundamental tool.

## Janka Hardness and Mechanical Properties

The Janka hardness test measures the force required to embed an 11.28mm (0.444 inch) steel ball halfway into a piece of wood. The result, expressed in pounds-force (lbf), gives a comparative measure of how resistant a species is to denting and wear.

At the soft end: balsa (70 lbf), eastern white pine (380 lbf), western red cedar (350 lbf). In the middle range: black cherry (950 lbf), black walnut (1,010 lbf), white oak (1,360 lbf), hard maple (1,450 lbf). At the hard end: hickory (1,820 lbf), Santos mahogany (2,400 lbf), Brazilian cherry (jatoba, 2,350 lbf). And at the extreme: lignum vitae (4,500 lbf), a wood so dense it sinks in water and was historically used for ship propeller shaft bearings.

Janka hardness matters for flooring, workbench tops, cutting boards, and any surface that will take impact. It matters less for structural applications, where bending strength and stiffness (modulus of rupture and modulus of elasticity) are more relevant. Douglas fir, for example, has a modest Janka rating (660 lbf) but excellent bending strength, which is why it dominates construction framing.

Other mechanical properties that matter: compression strength (relevant for legs and posts), shear strength (relevant for joinery), and resistance to splitting (relevant for nailing and screwing near edges). These properties vary not just between species but within a species, depending on growth rate, growing conditions, and the specific location within the tree.

## Figure — The Beauty in the Anomaly

Figure is the visual pattern in wood that goes beyond simple grain. It is caused by irregularities in cell growth, and it is what makes certain pieces of wood extraordinary.

**Curly figure** (also called tiger stripe or fiddleback) is caused by undulating grain that produces alternating bands of light and dark as the viewing angle changes. The optical effect is called chatoyance — the same phenomenon that gives cat's eye gemstones their shimmer. Curly maple is the most famous example, but figure can appear in almost any species. The back and sides of fine violins have been made from curly maple since the time of Stradivari.

**Quilted figure** is found primarily in big-leaf maple from the Pacific Northwest. It produces a three-dimensional, pillowy surface that seems to move as light plays across it. The cause is not fully understood — the grain appears to grow in a rolling, billowed pattern. Quilted maple is highly prized for guitar tops and luxury furniture. A single quilted maple slab can sell for thousands of dollars.

**Bird's eye figure** appears almost exclusively in hard maple. Small, swirling eye-shaped patterns scatter across the wood surface like a field of tiny knots. The cause is debated — one theory attributes it to fungal infection, another to aborted bud growth. Bird's eye maple has been prized for decorative veneers and instrument backs for centuries.

**Spalting** is caused by fungal colonization of dead or dying wood. Zone lines — thin, dark lines where competing fungal colonies meet — create dramatic black patterns against the lighter wood. Spalted maple, beech, and birch are common. The challenge is catching the wood at the right stage: enough spalting for dramatic figure, but before the fungi have softened the wood to the point of structural failure.

**Burl** is an outgrowth on a tree caused by stress, injury, or disease. The grain inside a burl is wildly chaotic — swirling, folding, doubling back on itself. When sliced open, burls reveal patterns of extraordinary complexity. Burl veneers and turning blanks are among the most expensive wood products in the world. Redwood burls, madrone burls, and maple burls are particularly prized.

## Defects — What the Tree Went Through

Not every irregularity is beautiful. Wood defects are conditions that reduce structural integrity, workability, or aesthetic value.

**Knots** are the remnants of branches embedded in the trunk as the tree grew around them. Tight knots (where the branch was alive when enclosed) are structurally sound but create grain disruption that can tear out during planing. Loose knots (where the branch died before being enclosed) can fall out entirely, leaving holes. Knots are graded out of premium lumber but embraced in rustic and character-grade work.

**Checks and splits** are separations along the grain caused by uneven drying. End checks (on the cut faces of boards) are nearly universal and are managed by cutting a few inches off each end. Surface checks are more problematic and indicate that the wood dried too fast.

**Warp** takes four forms: bow (curve along the face in the length), crook (curve along the edge in the length), twist (corners that don't lie in the same plane), and cup (curve across the width). All are caused by uneven drying or by internal stress released during sawing. Mild warp can be removed by jointing and planing. Severe warp means the board is firewood.

**Reaction wood** — compression wood in softwoods, tension wood in hardwoods — forms when a tree grows at an angle and compensates by producing denser wood on one side. Reaction wood behaves unpredictably when dried and machined, often warping and fuzzing.

## Lumber Grading — FAS, Select, and Common

Hardwood lumber in North America is graded by the National Hardwood Lumber Association (NHLA). The grading system is based on the percentage of a board that can be cut into clear (defect-free) pieces of specified minimum sizes.

**FAS (Firsts and Seconds)** is the highest grade. Boards must be at least 6 inches wide and 8 feet long, with a minimum of 83.3 percent clear face cuttings. This is the grade for fine furniture and architectural millwork. It is expensive.

**FAS One Face (F1F)** requires FAS quality on one face and #1 Common on the other. This is a practical compromise — if only one face will show, F1F saves money without sacrificing visible quality.

**Select** is similar to F1F, with slightly different dimensional requirements. In practice, Select and F1F are often used interchangeably.

**#1 Common** requires 66.7 percent clear cuttings. This is a popular grade for furniture making because it balances quality and cost. A skilled woodworker can cut around the defects and extract plenty of clear material, often at a significant savings over FAS.

**#2 Common** requires 50 percent clear cuttings. This grade is commonly used for flooring, cabinet parts, and applications where shorter clear pieces are acceptable.

Softwood grading uses a different system, administered by agencies like the Western Wood Products Association. Construction lumber is graded for structural properties (Select Structural, #1, #2, #3, Stud). Appearance lumber is graded for visual quality (Clear, Select, Common grades). The two systems — hardwood and softwood — are not interchangeable.

## Board Feet and Buying Lumber

Hardwood lumber is sold by the board foot — a unit of volume equal to a piece of wood one inch thick, twelve inches wide, and twelve inches long (144 cubic inches). The formula: thickness in inches times width in inches times length in inches, divided by 144.

Hardwood lumber is typically sold in random widths and lengths at specified thicknesses. Thickness is expressed in quarters of an inch: 4/4 (four-quarter, one inch rough), 5/4 (inch and a quarter), 6/4 (inch and a half), 8/4 (two inches), and so on up to 16/4 or thicker for specialty orders. These are rough-sawn dimensions — after surfacing (planing smooth), a 4/4 board will be approximately 13/16 inch thick. Planning for material loss during surfacing is critical.

Buying from a hardwood dealer rather than a home center changes everything. A home center sells pre-surfaced, pre-dimensioned lumber at premium prices with limited species selection. A hardwood dealer sells rough-sawn lumber in bulk at lower per-board-foot prices, with dozens of species available, and you can hand-pick your boards. The tradeoff is that you need a jointer and planer (or hand planes) to surface the lumber yourself.

Learning to read a rough board — assessing grain, checking for defects, gauging figure through the saw marks — is a skill that develops over years. Experienced woodworkers spend time at the lumber dealer the way experienced cooks spend time at the market. The quality of the raw material sets the ceiling for the quality of the finished piece.

## Drying — Kiln and Air

Green wood cannot be used for furniture or finish carpentry. It must be dried to a moisture content appropriate for its intended environment.

**Air drying** is the traditional method. Lumber is stacked with spacers (stickers) between each layer, allowing air to circulate around every surface. The stack is covered on top to shed rain but left open on the sides for airflow. Air drying is slow — roughly one year per inch of thickness as a general rule, though this varies widely with species, climate, and season. Air-dried lumber typically reaches equilibrium at 12 to 14 percent moisture content, which is appropriate for outdoor furniture and construction but too wet for indoor furniture.

**Kiln drying** uses a heated chamber with controlled temperature, humidity, and airflow to accelerate the process. A charge of 4/4 hardwood can be kiln-dried in weeks rather than months. The kiln operator follows a schedule — gradually increasing temperature and decreasing humidity — that is specific to the species and thickness. Done well, kiln drying produces lumber at 6 to 8 percent moisture content with minimal defects. Done poorly, it produces case-hardened lumber (dry on the surface, wet inside) that will warp and split after machining.

Many serious woodworkers air-dry lumber to 15 to 20 percent and then finish in a kiln, or simply bring air-dried lumber into their heated shop for several weeks before working it. The goal is always the same: get the wood to the moisture content it will experience in service, and let it stabilize there before cutting joints.

## The Living Material

There is a moment in every woodworker's education when the science stops being abstract and becomes visceral. You glue up a wide panel, and a week later one edge has risen a sixteenth of an inch because the wood moved. You plane a board flat, set it on the bench, and watch it slowly bow as the freshly exposed face releases moisture faster than the back. You cut a board from a log and it immediately springs open as decades of internal stress find release.

Wood is not inert. It is responsive, dynamic, and sometimes obstinate. The science in this chapter — the anatomy, the physics of movement, the mechanical properties — is not background knowledge. It is operating knowledge. Every successful project begins with understanding the material, and the material begins with a tree that spent decades growing in wind and rain, in drought and flood, in a specific place on a specific hillside. That history is encoded in every ring, every ray, every cell.

Understanding wood means understanding that you are not imposing your will on raw material. You are entering into a negotiation with something that has its own nature, its own tendencies, its own memory. The woodworkers who produce the finest work are the ones who listen to what the wood is telling them.

## PNW Species in Context

The wood science described in this chapter comes alive in the Pacific Northwest's species. Douglas fir's exceptional bending strength (modulus of rupture ~12,400 psi) at a modest Janka hardness (660 lbf) makes it the ideal framing timber — strong enough to span rooms as joists and rafters, stiff enough to resist deflection, yet workable enough to cut and fasten efficiently. Western red cedar's extractive-rich heartwood (thujaplicins) provides natural decay resistance that is an evolutionary strategy against the Pacific Northwest's fungal-rich rainforest floor [ECO-05]. Big-leaf maple's figured wood — quilted, curly, spalted — represents the intersection of genetics, growth stress, and fungal colonization that wood science can describe but not fully explain.

The PNW woodworker develops an intuitive grasp of these properties through daily work. You learn that Douglas fir's pronounced earlywood-latewood contrast means the softer earlywood crushes under a plane blade set too aggressively, while the dense latewood resists — requiring a sharp blade and a light touch. You learn that western red cedar's softness means it dents if you look at it sternly, but its dimensional stability makes it the best choice for exterior trim that must hold paint through wet PNW winters. You learn that big-leaf maple moves dramatically with humidity — a quilted maple guitar top must be finished on both sides simultaneously, or it will cup overnight in a coastal shop.
