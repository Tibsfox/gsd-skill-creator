# The Power Tool Shop

## Speed, Precision, and Respect

A table saw does not care about your fingers. This is the first thing to understand about power tools, and it is the thing that experienced woodworkers never forget. A motor spinning a blade at four thousand RPM will cut wood and flesh with identical indifference. Power tools extend your capability enormously — they allow one person to do in minutes what once required a crew of apprentices working for hours — but they demand a level of respect and attention that hand tools do not.

This chapter covers the major power tools found in a woodworking shop, what they do, how to use them safely, and how to build a shop around them. It is not a substitute for hands-on instruction. If you have never used a table saw, find someone who has and learn from them in person. Watch their hands. Watch where they stand. Watch what they do before they turn the machine on. Then, and only then, turn it on yourself.

## The Table Saw — The Center of the Shop

The table saw is the most versatile and most dangerous stationary power tool. It rips boards to width, crosscuts them to length (with a sled or miter gauge), cuts dadoes, rabbets, tenons, tapers, and bevels. Most woodworking shops are organized around the table saw because most operations either start or end there.

A table saw consists of a circular blade protruding through a slot in a flat table, driven by a motor. The workpiece is pushed through the blade along a fence (for rip cuts) or guided by a miter gauge or sled (for crosscuts). The blade height and angle are adjustable.

### SawStop and the Safety Revolution

In 2004, SawStop introduced a table saw with a flesh-detection system. The blade carries a small electrical signal. When skin contacts the blade, the change in signal triggers an aluminum brake that slams into the blade, stopping it in milliseconds. The blade also retracts below the table. The result: instead of a severed finger, a small nick. The brake cartridge and blade are destroyed and must be replaced (roughly $100), but the finger is saved.

SawStop has changed the conversation around table saw safety. Before SawStop, the table saw was responsible for an estimated 67,000 injuries per year in the United States, including roughly 4,000 amputations. The technology works. The debate is about whether it should be mandated — SawStop's patent holder has lobbied for regulation requiring the technology on all table saws, which competitors view as anticompetitive. Regardless of the politics, if you are equipping a new shop and can afford a SawStop, buy one. No project is worth a finger.

### Kickback

Kickback is the most dangerous table saw event. It occurs when the workpiece is pinched between the blade and the fence, or when the blade catches the trailing end of the workpiece. The spinning blade launches the wood back toward the operator at speeds that can exceed 100 miles per hour. A piece of wood propelled by a 3-horsepower motor is a projectile.

Preventing kickback requires understanding its causes. The riving knife — a curved metal fin mounted behind the blade, rising and falling with it — prevents the two halves of a rip cut from closing on the back of the blade. The riving knife is the single most important safety device on a table saw after the blade guard itself. European table saws have required riving knives for decades. American saws adopted them more recently. Never remove the riving knife.

Other kickback prevention measures: never stand directly behind the blade (stand to the side of the cut line). Use a push stick or push shoe for narrow rips. Never freehand a rip cut — always use the fence. Never crosscut against the rip fence (the cutoff piece can bind between blade and fence). Use a crosscut sled for crosscuts.

### Blade Selection

A general-purpose combination blade (40 to 50 teeth, alternate top bevel grind) handles both ripping and crosscutting reasonably well. A dedicated rip blade (24 teeth, flat top grind) cuts faster along the grain with less motor strain. A dedicated crosscut blade (60 to 80 teeth, alternate top bevel) produces cleaner cuts across the grain. A good combination blade is the starting point. Add dedicated rip and crosscut blades as your work demands.

Specific recommendations: the **Freud Diablo D1050X** 50-tooth combination blade ($35-45) is the best value in a general-purpose blade -- it cuts clean in both rip and crosscut, leaves a smooth surface, and lasts through hundreds of board feet before needing sharpening. The **Forrest Woodworker II** ($85-100) is the premium choice -- it produces a surface so clean that many woodworkers glue directly from the saw without jointing. For dedicated ripping, the **Freud Diablo D0724A** 24-tooth rip blade ($25-30) cuts Douglas fir and hardwood with minimal effort. For dedicated crosscutting, the **Freud LU85R010** 80-tooth ultra-fine crosscut ($55-65) leaves a polished surface on miters and end grain.

Thin-kerf blades remove less material per cut and require less motor power, making them suitable for smaller saws (contractor saws with 1.5-2 HP motors). Full-kerf blades are stiffer and produce cleaner cuts on larger saws with more power (cabinet saws with 3+ HP).

## The Bandsaw — The Versatile Second Saw

If the table saw is the center of the shop, the bandsaw is the most underappreciated tool in it. A bandsaw can resaw (slice thick boards into thinner ones), cut curves, rip lumber, cut joinery (tenons, dovetails), and handle irregular stock that would be dangerous on a table saw.

The bandsaw uses a continuous loop of thin, flexible blade running over two (or three) wheels. The blade passes through a table with guides above and below that prevent it from twisting or deflecting. Because the blade is thin (1/8 inch to 3/4 inch wide for common woodworking bandsaws) and cuts in a single direction (downward into the table), there is almost no kickback risk. The blade pushes the workpiece down onto the table, not back at the operator.

Resawing — cutting a thick board through its width to produce two thinner boards — is the bandsaw's superpower. A 6-inch-wide board of figured walnut, resawed into two book-matched halves, produces a symmetrical mirror-image pair that can be used for box lids, cabinet doors, or guitar tops. No other tool in the shop can do this.

Blade selection matters enormously for bandsaw performance. A wide blade (1/2 to 3/4 inch) with few teeth per inch (2 to 3 TPI) excels at resawing. A narrow blade (1/8 to 1/4 inch) with more teeth handles tight curves. Blade tension must be set correctly — too loose and the blade wanders; too tight and it stresses the wheels and bearings. A well-tuned bandsaw with a quality blade and proper tension can produce surfaces smooth enough to glue directly from the saw.

## The Router — The Shape Machine

The router is a high-speed motor (typically 10,000 to 25,000 RPM) holding a shaped cutter (a router bit) that is plunged into or run along the edge of a workpiece. It cuts profiles (ogees, round-overs, chamfers, coves), creates grooves and dadoes, cuts rabbets, trims laminate, patterns parts using templates, and — with the right jigs — cuts joints including dovetails, mortises, box joints, and raised panels.

Routers come in two configurations. A **fixed-base router** has the motor locked at a set depth — the bit projects a fixed amount below the base. The router is tilted onto the workpiece or the workpiece is brought to the bit (in a router table). A **plunge router** allows the motor to plunge downward on spring-loaded columns, lowering the bit into the workpiece from above. Plunge routers are essential for cutting mortises, stopped grooves, and inlay cavities.

A **router table** inverts the router and mounts it under a flat table, with the bit projecting upward through a hole. The workpiece is pushed along a fence and past the spinning bit. This converts the router into a miniature shaper and is the preferred setup for edge profiles, raised panels, and many joinery operations.

Router safety depends on understanding climb cutting. In normal operation (conventional cut), the bit's rotation opposes the feed direction, pulling the workpiece into the fence. In a climb cut, the bit's rotation assists the feed direction, grabbing the workpiece and potentially throwing it. Climb cuts are occasionally useful for preventing tearout on difficult grain, but they must be done with light passes and firm control.

## The Planer and the Jointer — Making Lumber Flat

These two machines work as a pair. The jointer makes one face flat and one edge straight and square. The planer makes the opposite face parallel to the jointed face at a desired thickness.

### The Jointer

A jointer is an infeed table, an outfeed table, and a rotating cutterhead between them. The infeed table is set slightly lower than the outfeed table (the difference is the depth of cut). The workpiece rides over the cutterhead, which removes a thin layer from the bottom face. Each pass removes a high spot. After several passes, the face is flat.

The jointer's fence, set at 90 degrees, allows you to joint an edge straight and square to the face. This jointed edge becomes the reference surface that rides against the table saw fence when ripping to width.

A jointer cannot make a board uniformly thick. It can only flatten the face that touches the tables. The opposite face retains whatever irregularity it had. That is the planer's job.

### The Planer

A thickness planer feeds a board through on a flat bed while a rotating cutterhead above removes material from the top face. The bed-to-cutterhead distance is adjustable, allowing you to plane to any desired thickness.

The critical point: the planer references off the bottom face. If the bottom face is not flat (because you skipped the jointer), the planer will reproduce the bottom face's irregularities on the top face, producing a board of uniform thickness that is still not flat. Joint first, then plane. This is the foundational sequence of lumber preparation.

A helical cutterhead — a spiral arrangement of small, square, indexable carbide inserts — produces a smoother cut with less noise than traditional straight-knife cutterheads. The inserts can be individually rotated to present a fresh edge when one dulls, and replacement is inexpensive. Helical cutterheads have become the standard upgrade for serious planers.

## The Drill Press — Precision Holes

A drill press does one thing: it drills holes straight and to a controlled depth. A handheld drill cannot match the drill press for perpendicularity. For dowel joints, shelf pin holes, hardware mounting, and any operation where a hole must be exactly perpendicular to the surface, the drill press is the right tool.

The drill press also accepts accessories beyond drill bits: Forstner bits (for flat-bottomed, clean-edged holes), hole saws, drum sanders, and mortising attachments (a hollow chisel that chops a square hole while a drill bit inside removes the core). A benchtop mortiser is essentially a dedicated drill press optimized for this one operation.

## The Lathe — Turning Round

The wood lathe is unlike every other tool in the shop. Instead of moving the tool through a stationary workpiece, the lathe spins the workpiece and you bring the tool to it. The cutting action is controlled by your hands, and the shapes you can produce — bowls, spindles, platters, boxes, balusters, tool handles — are limited only by your skill.

Lathe work divides into two categories. **Spindle turning** mounts the workpiece between centers (headstock and tailstock), with the grain running parallel to the axis of rotation. Spindle turning produces table legs, chair spindles, tool handles, pens, and architectural turnings. **Faceplate turning** mounts the workpiece on the headstock only, with the grain running perpendicular to the axis. Faceplate turning produces bowls, platters, hollow forms, and lidded boxes.

The tools are different from other woodworking tools. Turning tools — gouges, skew chisels, parting tools, scrapers — are held freehand against a tool rest, with the cutting edge presented to the spinning workpiece at angles that vary continuously as the shape develops. Learning to turn is learning to feel the tool's relationship to the wood through vibration, sound, and the shape of the shavings.

Turning is addictive. The immediate feedback — you can see the shape developing in real time as the tool contacts the wood — provides a satisfaction that is different from any other woodworking operation. Many woodworkers who begin turning as a supplement to their furniture work end up building their entire practice around the lathe.

## The Miter Saw — The Crosscut Station

A compound miter saw (commonly called a chop saw) crosscuts boards to length with speed and accuracy. The blade pivots left and right for miter cuts and tilts for bevel cuts. A sliding miter saw adds a rail system that allows the blade to slide forward, increasing the crosscut capacity to handle wider boards.

For furniture work, a miter saw handles cutting parts to length, cutting miters for frames and moldings, and cutting compound miters for boxes and complex joinery. For construction work, it is indispensable — framing, trim carpentry, deck building, and any task that requires repeated accurate crosscuts.

The miter saw's limitation is accuracy. The built-in fences and miter scales on most saws are not precise enough for fine furniture work. Adding a sacrificial fence, setting miter angles with a digital protractor, and verifying cuts with a square brings the miter saw up to furniture-grade accuracy.

## The Sander — The Necessary Evil

Sanding is the operation most woodworkers love to hate. It is dusty, tedious, and time-consuming. It is also necessary for most finishes. A well-planed surface may not need sanding, but a machined surface almost always does — power tool marks (planer scallops, router burns, saw blade marks) must be removed before finishing.

The **random orbit sander** is the workhorse. Its pad moves in a random, non-repeating pattern that avoids the swirl marks produced by orbital sanders. A 5-inch random orbit sander with hook-and-loop discs, progressing through 80, 120, 150, and 220 grit, handles the vast majority of sanding tasks.

A **belt sander** removes material aggressively, useful for rough dimensioning and leveling but too aggressive for finish sanding. A **drum sander** or **wide-belt sander** feeds boards through a sanding drum, producing a uniformly sanded surface — essentially a planer that sands instead of cutting. These are expensive machines, but for production work or frequent glue-up panel sanding, they save enormous time.

## Biscuit Joiners and Domino Joiners

A **biscuit joiner** (plate joiner) cuts a small, crescent-shaped slot in both mating faces of a joint. A compressed beechwood biscuit, inserted with glue, swells and locks the joint. Biscuits provide alignment more than strength — they keep boards flush during glue-up. They are fast, easy, and sufficient for many applications.

The **Festool Domino** is a more robust version of the same concept. It cuts a mortise (not a crescent slot) and uses a loose tenon (a small, rounded-edge piece of beechwood or other hardwood) as the connecting element. Dominos are stronger than biscuits and can be used for structural joints — face frames, table aprons, door frames — where biscuits would be inadequate. The Domino joiner is expensive, but the speed and strength of its joints have made it one of the most popular power tools among professional furniture makers.

## Dust Collection — The Invisible Hazard

Wood dust is a health hazard. Fine dust — particles smaller than 10 microns, invisible to the naked eye — penetrates deep into the lungs. Prolonged exposure causes respiratory problems, and some species (western red cedar, in particular) are sensitizers that can trigger asthma and allergic reactions. Several tropical hardwoods are classified as carcinogenic.

A dust collection system is not optional in a power tool shop. At minimum, every major tool (table saw, planer, jointer, router table, sander) should be connected to a dust collector or shop vacuum. The dust collector — a motor-driven impeller that pulls air through ductwork — is the standard solution for stationary tools. A single-stage collector with a canister filter is adequate for a small shop. A two-stage collector with a cyclone separator (which drops heavy particles into a drum before the fine dust reaches the filter) is more efficient and easier to maintain.

Beyond machine dust collection, ambient air filtration handles the fine particles that escape collection at the source. A ceiling-mounted air filtration unit with a fine filter (1 micron or better) circulates and cleans the shop air continuously.

A dust mask or respirator is the last line of defense and should be worn during any sanding, routing, or other operation that generates fine dust. An N95 disposable mask is adequate for occasional use. A half-face respirator with P100 filters is more comfortable and more effective for regular shop work.

## Shop Electrical

Power tools need power, and the wiring in a typical residential garage is often inadequate for a woodworking shop.

Most portable power tools (routers, sanders, biscuit joiners) run on 120V/15A or 20A circuits. Stationary tools vary: a contractor-style table saw typically runs on a 120V/20A circuit, while a cabinet saw requires a 240V/30A or 50A circuit. A planer, jointer, or dust collector may need a dedicated 240V/20A circuit.

The key principles: every stationary tool should be on its own dedicated circuit (not sharing with other tools or with lighting, so that a tripped breaker does not plunge the shop into darkness while a blade is spinning). The dust collector should be able to run simultaneously with any tool it serves, which often means it needs its own circuit too. GFCI (ground fault circuit interrupter) protection is required by code in most garage and workshop installations.

If the existing wiring is inadequate, hire an electrician. The cost of a proper subpanel with dedicated circuits is modest compared to the cost of the tools it serves, and it is infinitely cheaper than the cost of a fire caused by overloaded wiring.

## Safety — The Culture of Attention

Safety in a power tool shop is not a set of rules. It is a culture. It is the habit of pushing the off switch before reaching for a cutoff near the blade. It is the reflex of checking the fence lock before starting a rip cut. It is the practice of wearing safety glasses every time, without exception, even when you are "just making one quick cut."

The rules matter — use the riving knife, use push sticks, wear eye and ear protection, never wear loose clothing or jewelry near spinning machinery, disconnect power before changing blades. But the rules are downstream of the attitude. The attitude is: this machine can hurt me in less time than it takes to register pain, and the only thing preventing that is my attention.

Fatigue is the enemy of attention. The most dangerous time in the shop is the end of a long day, when you are tired but want to make "just one more cut." That is when accidents happen. Knowing when to stop is as much a safety skill as knowing how to set a riving knife.

The shop is your space. You get to decide how it is organized, what tools it contains, and what rules govern it. The one rule that is not negotiable is this: everyone who walks out of the shop should have the same number of fingers they walked in with.

- **Trades** — [tools-safety]: Power tool safety is non-negotiable. The table saw alone causes approximately 67,000 injuries and 4,000 amputations per year in the United States. Understanding blade guards, riving knives, push sticks, kickback mechanics, and dust collection is the trades department's most critical safety curriculum. SawStop flesh-detection technology demonstrates how engineering can address safety failures.
- **Trades** — [woodworking]: Power tools extend the woodworker's capacity — they do not replace the woodworker's judgment. Understanding what a machine does to the wood (how a planer reads grain, how a table saw blade creates kerf, how a router bit climbs or cuts cleanly) requires the same material understanding as hand tool work.
- **Materials** — [fabrication-processes]: Power tools are industrial fabrication machines at the shop scale. Understanding these parameters improves results and safety.
