# The Art of Joining Wood

## Where Craft Becomes Engineering

Joinery is the soul of woodworking. You can plane a board flat. You can sand it smooth. You can apply a flawless finish. But none of that matters if the joints fail. A piece of furniture is only as good as the connections between its parts, and those connections must do two things simultaneously: hold the structure together under load, and accommodate the inevitable movement of the wood.

This is the tension at the heart of joinery. Wood expands and contracts with changes in humidity — across the grain, always across the grain — and it does so relentlessly, season after season, for the life of the piece. A joint must be tight enough to resist racking forces, strong enough to bear weight, and forgiving enough to let the wood breathe. The best joints manage all three with nothing more than the geometry of the wood itself.

There are hundreds of named joints in the Western and Eastern traditions, but they are all variations on a handful of principles. Understanding those principles matters more than memorizing a catalog.

## The Dovetail — The Joint That Announces Itself

The dovetail is the most recognizable joint in woodworking and, for many people, the emblem of the craft itself. It is a mechanical interlock — flared pins on one board mesh with corresponding tails on the other, and the geometry prevents the boards from pulling apart. A well-cut dovetail holds without glue, though glue is standard practice.

### Through Dovetails

The through dovetail is the classic form. Both pins and tails are visible on both faces of the corner. It is the joint of choice for the corners of boxes, chests, tool cabinets, and any application where the exposed joinery is part of the aesthetic. The ratio of the dovetail angle — the flare of the tails — varies by tradition and species. A 1:6 ratio (about 9.5 degrees) is common for softwoods. A 1:8 ratio (about 7 degrees) is typical for hardwoods. Steeper angles provide more mechanical lock. Shallower angles look more refined.

Cutting dovetails by hand is a rite of passage. The sequence is: lay out the tails on the tail board, saw to the lines, chop the waste, then use the cut tails to mark the pin board, saw the pins, and chop the waste. When the pins and tails come together with a firm tap of the mallet and the faces close flush — that first perfect dovetail — the feeling is unlike anything else in the craft. It is geometry made physical. Two pieces of wood, each useless alone, that lock together into something stronger than either.

The anatomy of a through dovetail, in cross-section, looks like this: the tails are the wider, trapezoidal projections — they flare outward from baseline to end grain like a dove's spread tail feathers, which is where the name comes from. The pins are the narrower pieces between the tails on the mating board. The baseline is the scribed line that marks the depth of the joint on both boards. When assembled, the pins nestle between the tails, and the flared geometry means the joint can only come apart in one direction — you can pull the tail board away from the pin board, but you cannot pull the pin board away from the tail board. That one-directional mechanical lock is why dovetails have been the drawer joint of choice for four centuries: the front of the drawer gets pulled thousands of times, and the dovetails resist every pull.

The learning curve is real. Early dovetails will have gaps. The tails will not quite align. The baselines will not be crisp. This is normal. The skill is in the repetition. By the tenth set, you understand the saw's relationship to the line. By the fiftieth, you can cut them in your sleep.

### Half-Blind Dovetails

Half-blind dovetails conceal the joint on one face. The tails do not extend through the pin board — they are housed in sockets that stop short of the outer face. The result is a corner that shows the pin board on the front and the joinery only on the side. This is the standard drawer joint. The front of a dovetailed drawer shows a smooth face of the pin board (the drawer front), while the sides reveal the interlocking tails.

Half-blind dovetails are harder to cut than through dovetails because you are working in a confined socket — clearing waste from the bottom of the pin sockets requires a chisel and a steady hand. But the visual result — a clean front face with the strength of a dovetail — is worth the effort.

### Sliding Dovetails

The sliding dovetail is a different animal entirely. A dovetail-shaped tongue on one piece slides into a matching groove in another. It is used for joining shelves to case sides, attaching table legs to pedestals, and any connection that must resist both pulling apart and racking.

A sliding dovetail is typically cut with a router — a dovetail bit cuts the groove in the case side, and the same bit shapes the tongue on the shelf end. The fit must be precise: too tight and the joint cannot be assembled (or can only be assembled with heroic force), too loose and it rattles. The sweet spot is a firm slide that closes completely with hand pressure alone.

## Mortise and Tenon — The Structural Backbone

If the dovetail is the aesthetic showpiece, the mortise and tenon is the structural workhorse. It connects rails to legs, aprons to posts, stretchers to stiles. Every table, every chair, every door frame, every face frame — the mortise and tenon is there, doing the structural work.

The tenon is a projecting tongue on the end of one piece. The mortise is a rectangular hole in the other piece. The tenon fits into the mortise, is glued (and sometimes pinned with a wooden dowel), and the joint is complete. The long-grain glue surface of the tenon cheeks provides excellent bond strength. The mechanical interlock resists racking. The geometry can be adjusted — tenon thickness, length, shoulder width — to suit the specific load and aesthetic requirements.

### Variations

A **through mortise and tenon** passes completely through the mortised piece, with the tenon end visible on the far side. This can be purely structural, or it can be decorative — a contrasting wood tenon visible through the mortised piece is a classic design element.

A **blind (or stopped) mortise and tenon** does not pass through. The tenon is hidden entirely within the mortised piece. This is the standard joint for most furniture applications.

A **haunched mortise and tenon** adds a short step (the haunch) at the top of the tenon. The haunch fills the groove that was cut in the stile to receive the panel in frame-and-panel construction. It prevents the groove from being visible at the end of the stile.

A **wedged tenon** is pinched open after assembly by driving small wedges into saw kerfs cut in the end of the tenon. The wedges expand the tenon inside the mortise, creating a mechanical lock that cannot be pulled apart. Tusk tenons — through tenons locked with a wedge driven through a slot in the projecting tenon end — are a beautiful variation used in trestle tables and knockdown furniture.

A **double tenon** uses two smaller tenons instead of one large one. This increases glue surface and resists twisting. It is common on wide rails where a single tenon would be impractically large.

A **drawbored mortise and tenon** is a traditional technique where the peg hole through the tenon is offset slightly from the holes in the mortise cheeks — typically 1/16 inch closer to the tenon shoulder. When the tapered peg is driven through, it draws the tenon tight into the mortise, compressing the shoulder against the mortised piece. This creates a joint that is mechanically locked even without glue. Timber framers use this technique at building scale (where it is called draw-boring); furniture makers use it at furniture scale for joints that must be bombproof — trestle table stretchers, workbench leg joints, any connection where seasonal racking will test the glue bond over decades.

### Sizing Rules

Traditional tenon sizing follows guidelines refined over centuries. The tenon thickness should be approximately one-third the thickness of the rail. The tenon length should be at least five times the tenon thickness (longer is stronger, up to the practical limit of the mortised piece). The mortise should be at least as deep as the tenon is long, plus a small clearance at the bottom for excess glue.

These are guidelines, not laws. The best joint for a given application depends on the species, the load, the aesthetic, and the available tooling. But the guidelines exist because they work. A tenon that is one-third the rail thickness leaves adequate material on both sides of the mortise — too thin a tenon and the glue surface is inadequate; too thick and the mortise walls are weakened.

## Box Joints — The Dovetail's Squared-Off Cousin

Box joints (also called finger joints) are interlocking square fingers cut in the ends of two boards. They provide enormous glue surface — each finger is a long-grain-to-long-grain bond — and a distinctive visual pattern. Unlike dovetails, box joints have no mechanical interlock (the fingers are parallel, not flared), so they depend entirely on glue for strength.

Box joints are typically cut on a table saw with a dado blade and a simple indexing jig, or on a router table with a straight bit. The jig ensures that each finger is exactly the same width as the gap between fingers, and that the fingers on one board align perfectly with the gaps on the other. The setup takes time, but once dialed in, the joint can be cut quickly and with excellent repeatability.

Box joints are the standard choice for shop jigs, utility boxes, and any application where strength and speed of construction matter more than the visual refinement of dovetails.

---

## Bridle Joints — Open Mortise and Tenon

A bridle joint is essentially an open mortise and tenon — the mortise is cut in the end of a piece, forming a fork, and the tenon on the mating piece fits into the fork. Corner bridle joints (where rail meets stile at the corner of a frame) and T-bridle joints (where a rail meets a stile at an intermediate point) are common in frame construction, workbench bases, and garden furniture.

The bridle joint is easier to cut than a traditional mortise and tenon because the mortise is open on one end — it can be cut entirely with a saw, without any chisel work. The tradeoff is that the open end reduces the mechanical interlock. A bridle joint depends more on glue than a closed mortise and tenon. But for light-to-moderate duty applications, it is fast, strong, and attractive.

## Dado, Rabbet, and Groove

These three joints are variations on the same idea: a channel cut into one piece to receive the edge or end of another.

A **dado** is a flat-bottomed channel cut across the grain. It is the standard joint for shelves in a bookcase — the shelf end sits in a dado cut in the case side, and the case side supports the shelf's weight.

A **rabbet** is an L-shaped step cut along the edge or end of a board. Rabbets are used for cabinet backs (the back panel sits in a rabbet cut around the inside of the case), for box construction (rabbeted corners), and for joining the top and bottom of a cabinet to the sides.

A **groove** is a flat-bottomed channel cut with the grain. Grooves house the panels in frame-and-panel construction and receive the bottom panel in a drawer.

All three can be cut with a dado blade on the table saw, a router with a straight bit, or a hand plane (a dado plane, rabbet plane, or plow plane). The router is the most versatile option for most shops.

## Biscuits, Dominos, and Dowels — Modern Loose-Tenon Joinery

Not every joint needs to be a hand-cut masterpiece. Modern joinery systems provide speed and reliability for production work and for joints where appearance is secondary to strength.

**Biscuits** are compressed beechwood wafers that fit into slots cut by a biscuit joiner. They provide alignment during glue-up (keeping boards flush at the joint line) and modest shear strength. Biscuits are ideal for edge-joining boards for tabletops and panels.

**Dominos** (Festool's system) are loose tenons — small rectangular pieces of beechwood or other hardwood that fit into mortises cut by the Domino joiner. A Domino is a true mortise-and-tenon joint, just made with a machine and a separate tenon rather than with a tenon integral to the workpiece. Domino joints are significantly stronger than biscuit joints and can be used for structural connections where traditional joinery would be appropriate but time does not allow.

**Dowels** are round wooden pins glued into matching holes in both pieces. Dowel joinery was the standard for factory furniture for decades. It is simple, strong, and requires only a drill press and a doweling jig. The weakness of dowel joints is alignment — if the holes in the two pieces do not align perfectly, the joint will not close. Doweling jigs solve this, but they are slower to set up than a Domino joiner.

## Japanese Joinery — The Four Hundred Joints

Japanese wood joinery (sashimono, in the furniture tradition, and kigumi in timber framing) catalogs over four hundred named joints. Many of these have no Western equivalent. They are designed for a world without nails, screws, or metal fasteners of any kind.

The **shachi-sen** joint is one of the most elegant. It is a through mortise and tenon locked with a dovetail-shaped key inserted from the top. The key prevents the tenon from withdrawing, but it can be removed to disassemble the joint — making the piece of furniture knockdown without hardware.

Japanese scarf joints (tsugite) connect two timbers end to end to create a single structural member longer than either piece. Some scarf joints interlock in three dimensions, with mating surfaces that resist tension, compression, and shear simultaneously. The geometry is complex enough to require three-dimensional layout and cutting, and the fit must be precise — there is no glue, no fasteners, only wood against wood.

What makes Japanese joinery remarkable is not complexity for its own sake. Every joint exists because it solves a specific structural problem in a specific way. The complexity serves function. A timber frame joint that must resist earthquake forces is designed differently from a furniture joint that must accommodate seasonal movement, which is designed differently from a tea house joint that must be assembled and disassembled for seasonal relocation. The four hundred joints are four hundred solutions to four hundred problems.

For Western woodworkers, Japanese joinery offers both inspiration and humility. The level of precision required — tolerances measured in hundredths of a millimeter, achieved with hand tools — represents a standard that most will never reach but that illuminates what the material is capable of.

## Timber Frame Joinery — Building Scale

Timber framing scales the principles of furniture joinery up to building size. The joints are the same in concept — mortise and tenon, dovetail, scarf — but the execution involves timbers measured in inches of cross-section rather than fractions.

The **housed mortise and tenon** is the bread and butter of timber framing. A beam end is tenoned and fitted into a mortise in a post, with the beam shoulder bearing on the post face. The joint is pinned with a wooden peg (treenail or trunnel) that draws the joint tight through draw-boring — the hole in the tenon is offset slightly from the hole in the mortise, so driving the peg pulls the tenon fully home.

The **dovetail housing** joins a horizontal beam to a post where the beam must resist being pulled out. The dovetail shape provides mechanical resistance to withdrawal, and the housing provides bearing surface for vertical load.

Timber frame joinery is cut with large chisels, mallets, slicks (extra-wide paring chisels), and boring machines. The layout uses a system of reference faces and numbered joints that allows the entire frame to be cut flat on the ground and then raised as a unit. The tradition survives today in both traditional and hybrid forms — some modern timber framers use CNC machines to cut the joints, while others work entirely with hand tools. Either way, the joinery is the structure. There is no plywood, no metal connectors, no hidden hardware. Just wood, fitted to wood, holding a building together.

## Glue — When Chemistry Meets Craft

Joinery and glue are partners, not alternatives. In most modern furniture, joints are both mechanically interlocked and glued. The joinery resists racking and provides alignment. The glue provides the bond that holds the joint closed.

**Titebond Original (aliphatic resin glue)** is the standard for interior furniture. It sets in about an hour, reaches full strength in 24 hours, and produces a bond that is stronger than the wood itself (a properly glued joint will break in the wood, not at the glue line). It cleans up with water when wet, sands cleanly when dry, and is inexpensive.

**Titebond III** adds water resistance (Type I water resistance per ASTM D5572), making it suitable for cutting boards, outdoor furniture, and any application with occasional moisture exposure. It is not waterproof — for true waterproof bonds, marine-grade epoxy is required.

**Epoxy** is a two-part adhesive (resin and hardener) that bonds to almost anything, fills gaps, and cures to a rigid, waterproof bond. Epoxy is the adhesive of choice for boat building, for bonding dissimilar materials (wood to metal, wood to glass), and for filling voids and stabilizing punky wood. It is more expensive than PVA glues and more difficult to clean up, but its versatility and gap-filling ability make it indispensable for certain applications.

**Hide glue** — the traditional adhesive made from animal collagen — has properties that no modern glue matches. It is reversible (a joint can be disassembled with heat and moisture), it does not creep under load (unlike some PVA glues), and it provides an excellent bond for musical instruments and antique restoration. Liquid hide glue (bottled, ready to use) is convenient. Hot hide glue (granules dissolved in water and heated in a glue pot) provides a stronger bond and faster grab but requires a more committed setup.

**CA glue (cyanoacrylate)** — superglue — has specialized uses in woodworking. Thin CA wicks into hairline cracks to stabilize them. Medium CA bonds small parts instantly. Thick CA fills small gaps. CA accelerator speeds the cure to seconds. For turning (lathe work), CA is the standard adhesive for attaching waste blocks and for stabilizing punky areas in bowls.

## When to Use Screws, Glue, or Joinery

The choice between screws, glue, and cut joinery depends on the application.

**Cut joinery** (dovetails, mortise and tenon, etc.) is the strongest, most durable, and most time-consuming option. It is appropriate for fine furniture, heirloom pieces, and any work where longevity and aesthetics justify the investment of time and skill.

**Glue alone** (butt joints, edge joints) is appropriate when the glue surface is long grain to long grain and the joint is not subjected to racking forces. Edge-joining boards for a tabletop, for example, requires only glue — a properly glued long-grain edge joint is stronger than the wood.

**Screws** are appropriate for utilitarian construction, jigs, fixtures, and any assembly that may need to be disassembled. Screws provide poor resistance to racking (they can work loose under cyclical loading), and they split wood near edges and ends if not pre-drilled. But they are fast, adjustable, and require no drying time.

**Pocket screws** (screws driven at an angle through a pocket hole) are a popular hybrid — they provide mechanical connection without visible fasteners and are widely used for face frames, cabinet boxes, and quick furniture construction. They are not a substitute for proper joinery in fine furniture, but they are a legitimate and effective technique for appropriate applications.

## Accommodating Wood Movement

The most elegant joinery in the world will fail if it does not accommodate wood movement. A wide solid-wood panel attached rigidly to a frame will crack the frame or split itself. A tabletop screwed tight to its apron will cup or tear free. The wood does not care about the joint. It will move.

The solutions are ancient. Frame-and-panel construction captures the panel in grooves, allowing it to expand and contract without stressing the frame. Tabletop fasteners allow the top to slide on the apron. Breadboard ends use a single glued tenon at center with the outer tenons floating in elongated mortises. Slotted screw holes allow movement while maintaining the connection.

The principle is consistent: attach rigidly at one point (or along one axis), and allow movement everywhere else. The point of rigid attachment is typically the center, so the panel expands equally in both directions from the fixed point.

Understanding wood movement is not an advanced topic. It is a prerequisite. Every joint in this chapter — every dovetail, every mortise and tenon, every dado — must be designed with movement in mind. The wood is going to move. The only question is whether you planned for it.

## PNW Joinery Traditions

Two joinery traditions converge in the Pacific Northwest. The Western European tradition — mortise and tenon, dovetails, frame and panel — arrived with the settlers and the carpenters who framed every building in the region out of Douglas fir. The Japanese tradition — the four hundred joints, the pull-saw technique, the tolerance-obsessed fitting — arrived with Japanese immigrants and was reinforced by the region's cultural connection to the Pacific Rim. PNW furniture makers work in both traditions, and many work in a hybrid: Western-style case construction with Japanese-influenced hand tool technique, using PNW species (big-leaf maple, Oregon white oak, Douglas fir, western red cedar) that respond to Japanese tools as well as they respond to Western ones.

The timber frame revival in the PNW draws on Douglas fir's exceptional suitability for large-scale joinery. A Douglas fir mortise-and-tenon in an 8x8 timber is a version of the same joint that holds a dining table together — scaled up, draw-bored with an oak peg, and carrying the weight of a building. The tradition connects [WDC-08] construction framing to the craft joinery in this chapter: the principles are identical, the scale is different.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** — [woodworking]: Joinery is the core skill of woodworking. Every project — furniture, cabinets, structures, boxes — is held together by joints. Understanding which joint serves which purpose (mortise-and-tenon for frame construction, dovetails for boxes and drawers, finger joints for small boxes, dados for shelving) is the woodworking curriculum's central organizing principle.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: Every joinery decision is a material decision. Wood moves. It shrinks across the grain, barely at all along the grain. Joints must accommodate this movement or fail. The physics of wood movement — different coefficients of expansion in radial, tangential, and longitudinal directions — determines which joints work and which crack.
- **Art** — [craft as expression]: Exposed joinery is a design choice. Visible dovetails, through-tenons with contrasting wedges, butterfly keys spanning cracks — these are functional elements that become decorative features. Joinery is where engineering meets aesthetics.
- **History** — [continuity-change]: The mortise-and-tenon joint is at least four thousand years old. The dovetail is nearly as ancient. These joints have not been improved because they cannot be improved — the geometry is optimal. Alongside this continuity, modern adhesives and fasteners have expanded the joinery vocabulary. The tradition endures alongside innovation.
