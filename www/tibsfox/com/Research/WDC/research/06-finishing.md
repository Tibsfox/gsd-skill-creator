# The Final Surface

## Where the Wood Comes Alive

There is a moment, after hours or days of cutting and fitting and shaping, when you wipe the first coat of finish across a piece of wood and the grain explodes into visibility. Colors deepen. Figure shimmers. The flat, pale surface you have been staring at transforms into something luminous and three-dimensional. This is the moment that finishing exists for. Everything before it — sanding, surface preparation, choosing the right product — is in service of this moment.

Finishing is also where projects die. A beautiful piece of furniture, expertly joined and carefully shaped, can be ruined by a bad finish. Runs, drips, blotchiness, fisheyes, brush marks, dust nibs — the list of finishing failures is long, and every woodworker has a story about the piece they nearly destroyed at the last step. The irony of finishing is that it is the final operation but requires as much knowledge and skill as any other phase of the build.

This chapter covers surface preparation, the major finish types, and the common problems that send woodworkers to the internet at midnight searching for answers.

## Sanding — The Foundation of Every Finish

Sanding is not optional, and it is not mindless. It is a systematic process of creating progressively finer scratches until the scratches are too small to see. Each grit removes the scratches left by the previous grit. Skipping a grit leaves scratches that the next grit cannot fully remove, and those scratches will be visible under the finish — magnified, in fact, because the finish fills the scratches and turns them into dark lines.

The standard progression for most finishes is 80 grit (if needed for heavy stock removal or to clean up machine marks), 120 grit, 150 grit, and 220 grit. Some finishes benefit from sanding to higher grits (320 or 400), but for oil and varnish finishes, 220 is typically the stopping point. Sanding beyond 220 can actually reduce the ability of finishes to absorb into the wood — the surface becomes so smooth that the finish sits on top rather than penetrating.

**Sand with the grain.** Cross-grain scratches are visible under finish. Always. A random orbit sander minimizes this risk because its random pattern avoids consistent directional scratching, but final sanding should still follow the grain direction. A light final pass by hand with 220-grit paper, following the grain, removes any residual cross-grain marks from the power sander.

**Raise the grain** before final sanding of surfaces that will receive water-based finishes. Wet the surface with a damp cloth. The water swells the wood fibers, raising them into a fuzzy texture. Let the surface dry completely, then sand lightly with 220 grit. The raised fibers are cut off, and when the water-based finish is applied, the surface stays smooth. Without this step, the first coat of water-based finish raises the grain and you are stuck sanding between coats to deal with it.

**Check your work** with raking light. A low-angle light source — a flashlight held almost parallel to the surface — reveals scratches, dips, and machine marks that are invisible under overhead lighting. Check every surface with raking light before applying finish. The finish will reveal every defect you missed.

## Oil Finishes — The Simplest and Most Forgiving

Oil finishes penetrate the wood, harden within the fibers, and leave a natural, low-sheen surface that shows the wood's true color and texture. They are easy to apply, easy to repair, and nearly impossible to mess up. They also provide the least protection of any finish category — no oil finish approaches the durability of a film finish like polyurethane or lacquer.

### Boiled Linseed Oil (BLO)

Boiled linseed oil is one of the oldest finishes in woodworking. Despite the name, it is not boiled — it is raw linseed oil treated with metallic driers that accelerate curing. BLO penetrates deeply, enhances grain, and cures to a soft, matte film. Multiple coats, applied over several days, build a warm, amber tone.

The application is simple: flood the surface, let it soak for 15 to 20 minutes, wipe off all excess. All excess. Linseed oil that is not wiped off remains a sticky, soft film that never fully hardens. The "wipe on, wipe off" mantra is the entire technique.

BLO has two significant drawbacks. It yellows with age, which is either a feature (on dark woods like walnut, the ambering adds warmth) or a flaw (on light woods like maple, the yellowing can be objectionable). And it provides almost no protection against water, heat, or abrasion. A table finished with BLO alone will ring-stain from a glass of cold water.

### Tung Oil

True tung oil (from the nut of the tung tree) is a harder, more water-resistant oil than linseed. It cures to a slightly harder film with more sheen, and it does not yellow as much. Pure tung oil takes longer to cure — days between coats — and can be difficult to find. Most products labeled "tung oil finish" are actually thinned varnish, not pure tung oil. Read the ingredients.

### Danish Oil

Danish oil is a blend of oil (usually BLO or tung oil), varnish, and thinner. It penetrates like an oil and builds a thin protective film like a varnish. It is easier to apply than straight oil (the thinner aids penetration) and provides better protection (the varnish component adds durability). Watco Danish Oil is the best-known brand. It is an excellent finish for shop furniture, tool handles, cutting boards, and any piece where a natural, low-maintenance finish is appropriate.

## Varnish — The Protective Film

Varnish is a film finish — it cures on the surface of the wood, building a protective layer that resists water, heat, abrasion, and chemicals. Modern varnishes are based on polyurethane, alkyd, or phenolic resins dissolved in a solvent. When the solvent evaporates and the resin cross-links (cures), the result is a tough, flexible film.

### Polyurethane Varnish

Oil-based polyurethane is the most common film finish for furniture and trim. It is available in matte, satin, semi-gloss, and gloss sheens. It is applied with a brush (a natural-bristle brush for oil-based, a synthetic brush for water-based) or wiped on with a rag.

Brush application is the traditional method, and it is where most finishing problems occur. The two enemies of brushed polyurethane are bubbles and dust. Bubbles form when the brush is worked too aggressively — the varnish is thinned enough to sag into a smooth film, but not so thin that air bubbles pop before the surface skins over. The solution: apply thin coats, lay the varnish on with long, smooth strokes, and do not go back over an area that has started to set.

Dust nibs — tiny specks of dust embedded in the finish — are the other plague. The wet varnish acts as flypaper for every particle in the air. Working in a clean space, wetting the floor to suppress airborne dust, and timing your finish coats for calm, still conditions all help. Between coats, light sanding with 320 grit removes dust nibs and provides tooth for the next coat.

**Wiping varnish** — polyurethane thinned 50/50 with mineral spirits — is the solution for woodworkers who hate brushing. A thin coat of wiping varnish is applied with a rag or foam applicator, building the film gradually over many thin coats. Each coat is so thin that dust nibs are minimal and brush marks are nonexistent. The tradeoff is time: a three-coat brushed finish might require six to eight coats of wiping varnish. But the result is professional-grade, and the technique is accessible to beginners.

### Water-Based Polyurethane

Water-based poly dries fast (hours instead of overnight), produces little odor, and does not yellow with age. It is the preferred finish for light-colored woods (maple, ash, birch) where yellowing would alter the appearance. The tradeoff is that water-based poly raises the grain (requiring extra sanding), does not penetrate as deeply as oil-based, and produces a thinner film per coat.

## Lacquer — The Professional's Finish

Lacquer is a fast-drying film finish applied by spraying. It dries by solvent evaporation — the lacquer solvents evaporate and the film hardens, but each new coat dissolves slightly into the previous coat, creating a single bonded film. This "melting in" property makes lacquer self-leveling and easy to repair — a dust nib can be sanded and the area resprayed without visible blending.

Spray lacquer is the standard finish for production furniture. It goes on fast (a piece can receive three coats in an hour), dries to the touch in minutes, and rubs out to a beautiful sheen. CAB-acrylic lacquer (cellulose acetate butyrate) adds UV resistance and clarity, making it the lacquer of choice for natural-looking finishes.

The downside is equipment and environment. Spraying lacquer requires a spray gun, a compressor (or an HVLP turbine), and a spray booth with adequate ventilation. Lacquer fumes are flammable and toxic. The investment in equipment and the need for proper ventilation put spraying beyond the reach of many hobbyists, though small-project spraying with aerosol lacquer cans is a viable alternative.

## Shellac — The Ancient Film Finish

Shellac is the original film finish. It is a natural resin secreted by the lac insect (Kerria lacca), dissolved in denatured alcohol. It has been used as a wood finish for centuries and remains one of the most useful and underappreciated finishes available.

Shellac dries fast (minutes), seals well (it is the universal sanding sealer and the best barrier coat for blocking odors, stains, and contaminants), and is available in a range of colors from nearly clear (super blonde) to warm amber (orange). It is non-toxic when cured — it is the finish used on pharmaceutical tablets and candy coatings. It is food-safe.

Shellac's weakness is its vulnerability to water and alcohol. A ring of condensation from a cold glass will leave a white mark. A spilled cocktail will dissolve the finish. For this reason, shellac is typically used as a sealer coat under lacquer or polyurethane, or as a stand-alone finish for pieces that will not see heavy use — bookshelves, display cabinets, picture frames, musical instruments.

### French Polish

French polish is shellac applied by a pad (a ball of cotton wrapped in a cotton or linen cloth) charged with shellac and a drop of oil. The pad is worked across the surface in overlapping circles, building the shellac film gradually through dozens or hundreds of passes. Each pass deposits a microscopic layer of shellac. The oil lubricates the pad and prevents it from sticking to the still-tacky previous layer.

French polishing produces a finish of extraordinary depth and clarity — a mirror-like surface that seems to float above the wood. It is the traditional finish for fine antiques, classical guitars, and other instruments where acoustic properties matter (shellac adds almost no mass and does not dampen the wood's vibration).

French polishing is an art. Learning it takes patience and practice — the hand pressure, the amount of shellac on the pad, the speed of movement, the addition of oil, the "spiriting off" of the oil in the final passes — all must be felt rather than measured. It is perhaps the most satisfying finish technique in woodworking, and the most demanding.

## Wax — The Softest Finish

Paste wax (carnauba wax, beeswax, or blends) provides a soft, satiny sheen and a pleasant tactile quality. It offers almost no protection — wax is softer than fingernails — and is used primarily over other finishes to add depth and smoothness, or alone on pieces that require no durability (turned bowls that are decorative, carved ornaments, hand tool handles).

Beeswax is the classic stand-alone wax finish. Melted with a small amount of mineral oil or turpentine, it produces a paste that is easy to apply and buff. The scent is warm and natural. On dark woods like walnut, a beeswax finish is beautiful — understated, natural, inviting to the touch.

## Paint and Milk Paint — The Opaque Options

Not every piece of wood deserves to be seen. Utilitarian furniture, painted cabinets, built-ins, and pieces made from unattractive species or assembled from mixed woods are all candidates for an opaque finish.

Standard latex or oil-based paint works on wood as it works on any surface. The keys are proper surface preparation (sanding, priming with a wood primer that blocks tannin bleed) and thin coats. Brush marks in paint can be minimized by using a quality brush, adding a paint conditioner (Floetrol for latex, Penetrol for oil-based), and laying off each coat with long, light strokes.

**Milk paint** is a traditional finish made from milk protein (casein), lime, and pigment. It produces a distinctively flat, chalky finish that looks old the day it is applied. It adheres directly to bare wood without primer, bonds tenaciously, and ages beautifully. Milk paint is the historically correct finish for Shaker furniture, early American furniture, and folk art. It is available in powdered form (mix with water) from companies like the Old Fashioned Milk Paint Company.

Milk paint's signature quality is its imperfection. It chips and wears in a way that reveals the wood beneath, creating a naturally distressed appearance. For furniture that is meant to look like it has been in use for generations, milk paint delivers authenticity that no faux-distressing technique can match.

## Stains — Adding Color Without Hiding Wood

Stains change the color of wood without obscuring the grain. They are used to even out color variations between boards, to simulate a different species (staining poplar to look like walnut, for example), or simply to achieve a color that the natural wood does not provide.

**Oil-based stains** are pigment or dye suspended in an oil/solvent carrier. They are applied, allowed to sit briefly, and wiped off. The longer the stain sits, the deeper the color. Oil stains are forgiving — they can be wiped immediately for a light color or left longer for intensity.

**Water-based stains** use water as the carrier. They raise the grain (requiring pre-wetting and sanding), but they are low-odor, dry quickly, and come in a wider range of colors.

**Dye stains** are dissolved colorants (not suspended particles). They penetrate more deeply and evenly than pigment stains, color the wood rather than the surface, and are less prone to blotchiness. Dye stains are the preferred choice for blotch-prone species like cherry, birch, and pine.

**Gel stains** have a thick, gel consistency that sits on the surface rather than penetrating. This gives more control over the color depth and significantly reduces blotchiness on problematic species. General Finishes gel stains are the benchmark product in this category.

## Food-Safe Finishes

Cutting boards, salad bowls, spoons, and other items that contact food require finishes that are non-toxic when cured.

The safest options are mineral oil (food-grade, available at any pharmacy) and beeswax. Mineral oil does not cure — it remains a soft, penetrating oil that must be reapplied regularly. A blend of mineral oil and beeswax (melted together at roughly 4:1 ratio) provides slightly better protection and a pleasant feel.

Walnut oil and raw linseed oil are food-safe when cured, but they are slow to cure and can go rancid.

All film finishes (polyurethane, lacquer, shellac) are food-safe when fully cured. The solvents evaporate completely during curing, and the cured resins are inert. The FDA does not regulate wood finishes specifically, but the consensus among finishing experts is that any finish, once fully cured, is safe for food contact. The practical distinction is that a cutting board finished with mineral oil can be refreshed easily when the oil wears away, while a cutting board finished with polyurethane will eventually chip and peel from knife cuts and washing.

## Common Finishing Problems

**Blotchiness** occurs when stain absorbs unevenly, creating dark splotches and light areas. It is most common on soft or porous species (pine, cherry, birch, maple). Prevention: apply a washcoat of thinned shellac (1-pound cut) before staining. The shellac partially seals the surface, evening out the absorption. Gel stain is another solution.

**Runs and drips** occur when too much finish is applied to a vertical surface. The finish sags before it sets. Prevention: thin coats. Especially on vertical surfaces. You can always add another coat.

**Fisheyes** are small, crater-like defects where the finish pulls away from the surface in circular patterns. They are caused by silicone contamination — from furniture polish, spray lubricants, or even some sandpapers. Prevention: wipe the surface with naphtha before finishing to remove silicone. If fisheyes appear, add a small amount of fisheye eliminator (a silicone additive) to the finish.

**White haze** under a finish (blushing) is caused by moisture trapped in the film. It is common with lacquer applied in high humidity. The moisture condenses in the fast-evaporating solvent, creating a milky cloudiness. Prevention: add retarder solvent to slow the drying, or wait for lower humidity.

**Orange peel** (a bumpy texture resembling citrus skin) in sprayed finishes is caused by insufficient atomization — the droplets are too large and do not flow together before they set. Prevention: increase air pressure, move closer to the surface, thin the finish, or warm the finish to reduce viscosity.

## The Finished Surface

A well-applied finish does not call attention to itself. It calls attention to the wood. The best finish is the one that makes people reach out and touch the surface, drawn by the warmth and depth that the finish reveals in the grain. Whether that finish is a simple coat of oil or a museum-grade French polish depends on the piece, the wood, and the intent. But in every case, the finish is the final act of a conversation between maker and material — the moment when the wood's character is revealed in full, and the maker steps back to let the wood speak.
