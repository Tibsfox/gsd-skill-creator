## 1. Color Is Not What You Think It Is

Color does not exist in the world. Color exists in your brain. What exists in the world is electromagnetic radiation at various wavelengths, and surfaces that absorb some wavelengths and reflect others. Your eye contains cone cells sensitive to roughly three ranges of wavelengths (short/blue, medium/green, long/red), and your visual cortex constructs the experience of color from the relative signals those cones produce. Color is a neurological interpretation, not a physical fact.

This matters for painters because it means color is always contextual. The same patch of paint looks different depending on what surrounds it. A gray square on a white background looks darker than the same gray square on a black background. A neutral gray next to a vivid orange appears to take on a bluish tint. These are not optical illusions -- they are the normal operation of color perception. Your visual system does not measure absolute color. It measures relationships.

This is why painters learn to see color relationships rather than identify individual colors. The question is never "what color is that?" but "what color is that *relative to what is next to it*?" Josef Albers' *Interaction of Color* (1963) is the essential text on this subject, and every painter should work through it.

## 2. Color Models

### RGB -- Additive Color (Light)

RGB (Red, Green, Blue) is the color model of light. Computer screens, televisions, projectors, and your eyes all work with additive color: combining wavelengths of light. Red light plus green light equals yellow light. All three primaries at full intensity produce white. The absence of all light is black.

RGB matters to painters because digital painting tools (Procreate, Photoshop, Clip Studio Paint, Krita) work in RGB. Understanding how RGB creates color helps you use digital color pickers intelligently rather than randomly. It also matters because photographs -- the primary reference material for most contemporary painters -- are captured and displayed in RGB. The colors you see on your monitor are RGB interpretations of scene light.

### CMYK -- Subtractive Color (Print)

CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black) is the color model of commercial printing. Inks absorb (subtract) wavelengths of light reflected from white paper. Cyan ink absorbs red, magenta absorbs green, yellow absorbs blue. Combining all three theoretically produces black (all light absorbed) but in practice produces a muddy dark brown, so a separate black (Key) ink is added.

CMYK matters to painters primarily when they make prints of their work. The gamut (range of reproducible colors) of CMYK printing is smaller than RGB, which is why vivid paintings sometimes look duller in print. Understanding this gap helps you make better decisions when preparing work for reproduction.

### RYB -- The Painter's Traditional Primaries

RYB (Red, Yellow, Blue) is the traditional color model taught in art classes. It is old -- traced back to the 18th century color theory of Moses Harris and later refined by Goethe, Itten, and others. In this model, red, yellow, and blue are primary (cannot be mixed from other colors), and the secondaries are orange (red + yellow), green (yellow + blue), and purple (red + blue).

RYB is not physically accurate. It does not correspond to how light works or how pigments actually interact. Blue and yellow pigments do not produce a vibrant green through subtractive mixing -- they produce a dull, desaturated green because the overlap of their reflectance curves is narrow. The "real" subtractive primaries are closer to cyan, magenta, and yellow (as in CMYK). But RYB persists because it maps reasonably well to common painting pigments (cadmium red, cadmium yellow, ultramarine blue) and because generations of painters have internalized its framework.

Many contemporary color educators (particularly James Gurney) advocate moving beyond strict RYB thinking toward a more nuanced understanding of color based on pigment properties rather than theoretical models. The gamut mapping approach -- understanding the specific mixing range of each pigment pair -- is more useful than memorizing a color wheel based on theoretical primaries.

### Munsell Color System

The Munsell system, developed by painter and educator Albert Henry Munsell in the early 20th century, describes color using three independent dimensions: **Hue** (the color family -- red, yellow-red, yellow, green-yellow, green, etc., arranged in a circle), **Value** (lightness to darkness, on a scale from 0/black to 10/white), and **Chroma** (saturation or intensity, from neutral gray outward to maximum saturation).

The Munsell system is the most precise and useful color notation system for painters. Its great insight is the independence of the three dimensions. You can change value without changing hue. You can change chroma without changing value. You can change hue while keeping value and chroma constant. This framework teaches you to think about color in terms of its component properties rather than as a single undifferentiated sensation.

Many serious painters -- particularly those trained in the tradition of Boston School realism and the atelier movement -- learn to identify colors in Munsell terms: "that shadow is a 5YR (yellow-red) at value 3, chroma 2." This precision accelerates color mixing because you know exactly what you are aiming for.

## 3. The Color Wheel and Color Relationships

### The Wheel

The color wheel is a circular arrangement of hues in spectral order. Regardless of which model underlies it (RYB, CMY, or Munsell), the basic structure is the same: warm colors (red, orange, yellow) on one side, cool colors (green, blue, violet) on the other, with transitions between them. The wheel is a map. Like all maps, it simplifies reality, but it is useful for navigating color relationships.

### Complementary Colors

Complementary colors sit opposite each other on the color wheel: red/green, blue/orange, yellow/violet. When placed next to each other, complementary colors create maximum contrast -- each makes the other appear more vivid. When mixed together, complements neutralize each other, producing a chromatic gray or muted tone (the exact result depends on the specific pigments used).

This dual nature of complements is one of the painter's most powerful tools. Use complements side by side for vibrant contrast. Mix complements to create rich, natural-looking neutrals. The shadows on an orange object are not gray -- they are orange's complement (blue) mixed into the orange, producing a warm, chromatic dark. Learning to mix neutrals from complements rather than reaching for black paint is a turning point in a painter's development.

### Analogous Colors

Analogous colors are neighbors on the color wheel -- blue, blue-green, and green, for example. Analogous color schemes produce harmony and unity because the colors share underlying wavelengths. Most of the natural world is analogous: a forest is green, yellow-green, blue-green, and the browns are just desaturated oranges and yellows. The Pacific Northwest landscape is an exercise in analogous color -- a hundred variations of green, modulated by the blue-gray atmosphere.

### Triadic and Split-Complementary

A triadic scheme uses three colors equally spaced around the wheel (red, yellow, blue is the obvious example). It produces a balanced, vibrant palette. A split-complementary scheme uses one color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement -- for example, blue, yellow-orange, and red-orange. This provides contrast with less tension than a straight complementary pair.

These schemes are starting points, not rules. No great painting was ever made by strictly following a color scheme. But they provide frameworks for thinking about color balance when you are stuck or when a composition feels like it lacks harmony.

In practice, here is how to use these schemes: before you start painting, make a small (2 x 3 inch) color thumbnail -- not a drawing, but a rough arrangement of the three or four major color areas in your composition. If you are painting a PNW landscape with a blue-gray sky, green forest, and a rusty dock, you have a near-triadic scheme (blue, green, red-orange). The thumbnail tells you whether those proportions work before you commit to a full painting. The general principle: use your dominant color for 60-70% of the painting, the secondary for 20-30%, and the accent for 5-10%. A mostly green landscape with a gray sky and a tiny red barn is a classic example of this proportion -- the red barn is the accent that makes the green sing.

### Warm and Cool

The warm/cool distinction is one of the most important concepts in painting. Warm colors (those tending toward red, orange, yellow) appear to advance toward the viewer. Cool colors (those tending toward blue, green, violet) appear to recede. This is atmospheric perspective in action: distant mountains appear bluer than near ones because the intervening atmosphere scatters short (blue) wavelengths.

But warm and cool are relative, not absolute. A red can be warm (cadmium red, which leans toward orange) or cool (alizarin crimson, which leans toward violet). A blue can be warm (ultramarine, which leans toward violet/red) or cool (cerulean, which leans toward green). Learning to see the temperature within each hue -- to ask "is this a warm red or a cool red?" -- is essential for sophisticated color mixing and for creating the illusion of light and space.

## 4. Value -- The Most Important Element

### Why Value Trumps Everything

If you had to choose one element to get right in a painting, choose value. Value is the lightness or darkness of a color, independent of its hue. A black-and-white photograph strips away all hue and chroma information, leaving only value -- and you can still read every form, every space, every light condition in the image. Value is the structure. Color is the decoration.

This is not an abstract principle. It is the most practical truth in painting. A painting with correct values and wrong colors will still read as a convincing image. A painting with beautiful colors and wrong values will look flat, confused, and unconvincing. Beginning painters almost universally focus on color and neglect value. The single biggest leap in painting skill comes from learning to see and control value.

### The Squint Test

The simplest and most powerful tool for seeing value: squint your eyes. When you squint, you reduce the amount of light reaching your retina, which collapses detail and color information and lets you see the big value relationships -- the pattern of light and dark. If you squint at your painting and the composition reads clearly -- light shapes against dark shapes, a clear hierarchy of values -- the painting is working. If it dissolves into a confusing mess of similar tones, the values need work.

Professional painters squint constantly. It looks eccentric. It is the most important thing they do.

### Notan

Notan (from the Japanese characters for "light" and "dark") is a design concept that emphasizes the pattern created by the arrangement of light and dark shapes. A notan study reduces a composition to two values (black and white) or three values (black, white, and one gray). This radical simplification forces you to think about the structure of your composition in the most fundamental terms: where is it light, and where is it dark?

Many painters begin every painting with a notan study -- a small (2 x 3 inch), quick (5 minute) thumbnail sketch in just two or three values. If the notan does not work as a design, the painting will not work as a painting, regardless of how well you render the details. This five-minute thumbnail saves hours of struggling with a painting that was doomed from the start because the underlying value structure was weak.

### Value Scales and Key

A value scale typically runs from 1 (white) to 9 or 10 (black). Most painters work with a simplified five-step scale in practice: white, light, mid-tone, dark, black. The key of a painting describes its overall value range. A high-key painting uses predominantly light values (think sun-drenched beach scenes). A low-key painting uses predominantly dark values (think Rembrandt interiors). Most paintings use a full range with emphasis on either end.

## 5. Color Mixing

### The Zorn Palette

Anders Zorn (1860-1920), the Swedish painter, created masterful portraits and figure paintings using just four colors: **cadmium red light** (or vermilion), **yellow ochre**, **ivory black** (which leans slightly blue when thinned), and **titanium white**. This limited palette -- sometimes called the Zorn palette -- demonstrates that you can mix a surprisingly complete range of colors from very few pigments.

The Zorn palette works because ivory black, when thinned with white, produces a cool, blue-gray that serves as the blue in the palette. Red plus this "blue" makes violet. Yellow ochre plus black makes green. The palette naturally produces the flesh tones, warm earth colors, and cool atmospheric tones that a portrait painter needs. It also forces you to focus on value relationships (because the palette has limited chroma range) and on warm/cool temperature shifts (because those shifts do most of the heavy lifting in a limited palette).

### Earth Tones

Earth tones are the workhorses of representational painting. Yellow ochre, raw sienna, burnt sienna, raw umber, burnt umber, and Venetian red (or its relatives: Indian red, English red light, Mars red) are all iron oxide pigments -- ground earth, literally. They are cheap, permanent, opaque, and they mix cleanly. A painter who masters earth tones can paint almost any subject found in nature.

Earth tones are naturally muted (low chroma), which means they mix well without creating the garish, over-saturated passages that plague beginners who reach for cadmium and phthalo colors first. Starting with an earth-based palette and gradually adding more intense colors as needed is a sound strategy for developing color sensitivity.

### Limited Palette Mastery

The paradox of color mixing: fewer colors on your palette often produce better paintings. A limited palette (3-6 colors plus white) forces every color in the painting to be mixed from the same few pigments, which means every color shares DNA with every other color. The result is inherent color harmony -- the painting holds together because the colors are all cousins, related through their shared parent pigments.

A powerful limited palette for oil or acrylic: **titanium white**, **cadmium yellow light**, **cadmium red light**, **alizarin crimson** (or quinacridone red), **ultramarine blue**, and **burnt sienna** (or raw umber). These six colors, mixed in various combinations, can produce most colors found in nature. You will not be able to mix the most vivid greens (add phthalo green if you need them) or the most vivid violets (add dioxazine purple), but for the vast majority of painting tasks, this palette is sufficient.

### Mixing Clean Color

Two pigments mixed together produce a color that is always less saturated than either parent. This is because each pigment absorbs a different range of wavelengths, and mixing them combines those absorptions -- more wavelengths absorbed means less light reflected means less chroma. Every time you add another pigment to a mixture, you absorb more light and move closer to a neutral gray.

This is why clean color mixing uses the fewest pigments possible. A green mixed from cadmium yellow and phthalo blue (two pigments) will be more vivid than a green mixed by adding a touch of yellow to a mixture of blue and white (three pigments). Pre-mixed tube greens like viridian or phthalo green are single-pigment colors with maximum chroma -- you cannot mix a green that vivid from any combination of yellow and blue.

Understanding this principle changes how you approach mixing. You learn to reach for the two pigments that most directly produce the color you need, rather than starting with three or four pigments and muddying your way toward a target.

## 6. Painting Techniques

### Alla Prima (Direct Painting)

Alla prima ("at the first," in Italian) means completing a painting in a single session, working wet-into-wet, without allowing layers to dry between applications. The Impressionists were alla prima painters -- Monet's plein air studies were captured in a few hours of direct, spontaneous paint application. Alla prima is the default technique for plein air painting, where changing light conditions demand fast work.

Alla prima produces a freshness and immediacy that layered techniques cannot replicate. The brushstrokes are visible, the color mixing happens on the canvas as well as the palette, and the painting retains the energy of its making. The risk is that overworking wet paint produces muddy, lifeless passages. Alla prima demands decisiveness -- put the stroke down and leave it.

In watercolor, alla prima means working the entire painting wet, completing it before any area dries, so that colors flow into each other and the whole surface has a unified, soft quality. This is extremely difficult because watercolor dries quickly and cannot be reworked once it loses its wet sheen.

### Glazing

Glazing is the application of thin, transparent layers of paint over fully dried opaque layers. The transparent glaze modifies the color of the layer beneath it without hiding it -- like looking at the underpainting through tinted glass. Oil painting and acrylic painting both support glazing, though oil glazes have a particular luminosity because the drying oil binder is naturally transparent.

The Old Masters built their paintings in layers: a monochrome underpainting (grisaille) established the values, then successive glazes of transparent color added hue and depth. A red glaze over a gray underpainting produces a red that has more depth and luminosity than opaque red applied directly, because light penetrates the glaze, bounces off the lighter layer beneath, and returns through the glaze to the viewer's eye. The color appears to glow from within.

Glazing requires patience -- each layer must be fully dry before the next is applied. In oils, this means days between layers (unless using alkyd mediums to speed drying). In acrylics, you can glaze within hours.

### Scumbling

Scumbling is the opposite of glazing: a thin, semi-opaque layer of lighter paint dragged over a darker dried layer. The scumble partially covers the dark layer, creating a broken, atmospheric effect -- like looking at a dark surface through fog. Scumbling softens, cools, and mutes the layer beneath it. It is the technique that creates atmospheric distance in landscape painting, that makes a background recede, that softens a shadow's edge.

The brush should be relatively dry for scumbling -- loaded lightly and dragged across the surface so that paint catches on the peaks of the texture (canvas weave, brushstrokes of the layer below) while the valleys remain exposed. The result is a beautiful optical mix of two colors that is more complex than either alone.

### Impasto

Impasto is paint applied so thickly that it stands up from the surface in three-dimensional ridges. A palette knife loaded with thick paint and dragged across the canvas leaves a ridge of color with its own shadows and highlights. Impasto exploits the physical, sculptural nature of paint. Rembrandt used impasto for highlights that literally catch real light. Van Gogh's swirling skies are pure impasto -- the paint surface is a topographic map of brushstrokes.

Impasto is most naturally an oil painting technique (oil paint's body and slow drying time support thick application), but heavy-body acrylics with gel medium can also be applied in impasto. Watercolor does not support impasto at all -- it is a thin, transparent medium by nature.

### Grisaille and Verdaccio

Grisaille is a monochrome underpainting in gray tones (from the French *gris*, gray). The painter completes the entire composition in values of gray before adding color through glazes. This separates the two hardest problems in painting -- getting the values right and getting the colors right -- into two sequential steps instead of trying to solve both simultaneously.

Verdaccio is the Italian variation: an underpainting in green-gray tones (verde = green), traditionally used for skin tones. The green underpainting, visible through transparent warm glazes applied over it, counteracts the tendency of flesh tones to look too hot and creates the complex, lifelike color shifts seen in Renaissance skin painting. When you see the subtle greenish shadows in a Botticelli face, you are seeing verdaccio showing through.

### Wet-on-Wet vs. Wet-on-Dry

These terms describe the state of the surface when you apply new paint. **Wet-on-wet** (also called *wet-into-wet*): new paint applied to a still-wet surface. Colors blend, edges soften, transitions are smooth and organic. Risk: overworking produces mud. **Wet-on-dry**: new paint applied to a fully dried surface. Edges are crisp, layers are distinct, each application is a controlled decision. Risk: the painting can look stiff and labored if every edge is hard.

Master painters use both techniques within a single painting, choosing wet-on-wet for soft areas (skies, backgrounds, blended transitions) and wet-on-dry for crisp areas (hard edges, sharp details, final accents). The ability to control edges -- to choose where edges are soft and where they are sharp -- is one of the defining skills of accomplished painting.

## 7. Composition

### Rule of Thirds

The rule of thirds divides the picture plane into a 3x3 grid by placing two horizontal and two vertical lines at the one-third and two-third positions. Placing key elements at the intersections of these lines (called "power points") or along the lines themselves tends to create more dynamic, visually interesting compositions than centering everything.

The rule of thirds is a simplification of the more complex Golden Ratio (approximately 1:1.618), which appears throughout nature and has been used in art and architecture for millennia. The Golden Ratio produces a slightly off-center placement that feels natural and balanced. The rule of thirds approximates this placement and is easier to apply.

### The Golden Ratio and the Golden Spiral

The Golden Ratio appears when a line is divided so that the ratio of the whole to the larger segment equals the ratio of the larger segment to the smaller. This ratio (approximately 1:1.618, denoted by the Greek letter phi) generates a spiral (the Golden Spiral or Fibonacci spiral) that appears in nautilus shells, sunflower seed heads, galaxy arms, and the compositions of countless masterworks.

Whether the Golden Ratio's aesthetic power is real or a matter of pattern-seeking in art history is debated. What is not debated: placing key compositional elements along a Golden Spiral or at Golden Ratio divisions produces compositions that feel balanced yet dynamic. It is a tool, not a rule.

### Focal Point and Visual Hierarchy

Every painting needs a focal point -- the area where the viewer's eye goes first. The focal point should have the highest contrast (lightest light against darkest dark), the most saturated color, the sharpest edges, and the most detail. Everything else in the painting should be subordinate to the focal point -- less contrast, less color, softer edges, less detail.

This is visual hierarchy: the deliberate ranking of elements so the viewer's eye follows a planned path through the painting. Without hierarchy, every area competes for attention and the painting feels chaotic. With clear hierarchy, the eye enters at the focal point, travels through the composition along planned paths (created by directional lines, value gradients, and color temperature shifts), and arrives at secondary and tertiary points of interest.

### Leading Lines and Directional Flow

Lines within the composition -- edges of roads, tree trunks, horizon lines, the direction of a figure's gaze, the tilt of a head -- guide the viewer's eye. Diagonal lines create energy and movement. Horizontal lines create calm and stability. Vertical lines create strength and presence. A winding road that leads the eye from the foreground into the distance is a compositional device as old as painting itself.

The painter's job is to arrange these lines so they guide the eye toward the focal point and keep it circulating within the picture plane rather than leading it out of the frame. Lines that point toward the edge of the painting create an exit -- the viewer's eye follows them out and may not come back. Lines that curve back into the composition create containment.

## 8. Plein Air Painting

### Painting in the Real World

Plein air painting is the practice of painting outdoors, on location, directly from the landscape or cityscape. It is one of the most valuable training methods available to any painter because it forces you to deal with the full complexity of natural light, color, and atmosphere in real time. A photograph compresses dynamic range, shifts colors, and flattens space. The real world does none of these things. It presents you with the full challenge: a scene of infinite complexity that you must simplify into a painting in the time the light holds steady.

### Equipment

The plein air setup is designed for portability. A **pochade box** (from the French *pochade*, quick sketch) is a compact painting box that opens to hold a panel, a palette, a brush holder, and compartments for paint and medium. The box clamps to a tripod. The whole setup -- box, tripod, paints, panels -- fits in a backpack.

Popular pochade boxes, from entry-level to premium: the **Guerrilla Painter 9x12 Guerrilla Box** ($150-180) is the entry point that established the category -- sturdy, functional, and widely available. The **Open Box M** ($180-220) offers a more refined design with a larger mixing area and better panel storage. The **Strada Easel Mini** ($250-300) is the premium compact option -- aluminum construction, ultralight, with an elegant clamping system that holds panels securely in wind. For painters who want maximum panel size in a portable package, the **En Plein Air Pro Traveler** ($200-250) accommodates panels up to 12x16 inches. A suitable tripod (Manfrotto 190 series or a basic photography tripod with a flat head, $60-120) completes the rig.

Plein air painters typically work small: 6x8, 8x10, or 9x12 inch panels. Smaller panels can be completed in 2-3 hours, which is roughly how long a light condition holds before the sun moves enough to change the shadow pattern fundamentally. Working small also forces simplification -- there is no room for fussy detail on a 6x8 panel, which is the point.

### The Light Changes

This is the central challenge of plein air painting: the light is changing constantly. The shadow that was here when you started is now there. The warm light on the hillside has cooled. The sky color has shifted. Experienced plein air painters commit to a specific light condition in the first fifteen minutes and hold to it, resisting the temptation to chase changes. They establish the big value relationships (sky, ground, shadows, lights) immediately and work from general to specific. If you spend twenty minutes drawing detailed tree branches before establishing the big shapes, the light will have changed by the time you start painting and you will be chasing it for the rest of the session.

### PNW Plein Air Specifics

Painting outdoors in the Pacific Northwest presents specific challenges and gifts. The light is predominantly diffused -- overcast skies scatter light evenly, eliminating harsh shadows and revealing subtle color relationships. This makes the PNW ideal for learning to see color temperature (the slight differences between warm and cool tones become visible when strong shadows are not dominating the scene).

The green challenge is real. The PNW landscape from April through October is overwhelmingly green. Painting convincing, varied greens without muddy monotony is the specific technical challenge of PNW landscape painting. The solution lies in color temperature: warm greens (yellow-green, olive) in sunlit areas, cool greens (blue-green, viridian) in shadows, and constant attention to the muted blue-gray atmosphere that modifies everything. A practical PNW plein air palette for managing greens: yellow ochre and cadmium yellow light for the warm end, ultramarine blue and viridian for the cool end, burnt sienna for neutralizing, and alizarin crimson for the purple-grays in distant conifers. Resist the temptation to use sap green or hooker's green straight from the tube -- they produce the monotone green you are trying to avoid. Mix every green fresh from yellow and blue, and each mixture will be unique.

Rain is the other reality. Plein air painting in the PNW means painting in drizzle, under tarps, in the twenty-minute windows between showers. A pochade box with a lid offers some protection for wet paintings. Watercolor painters face the ironic challenge of too much water -- rain spots on a half-finished watercolor are destructive. Many PNW watercolorists work under portable canopies or paint from the shelter of a car with the hatch open.

## 9. Color in the Furry Art Community

### Character Color as Identity

Color in the furry art community carries weight that goes beyond aesthetics. A character's colors are part of their identity -- chosen deliberately, documented precisely on reference sheets, and expected to be reproduced faithfully in every commission and every piece of artwork. Getting the colors right is not a stylistic preference. It is a requirement. A furry artist who mismatches a character's markings has committed the equivalent of getting a portrait sitter's eye color wrong.

This means furry artists develop acute color sensitivity. They learn to match specific hues, to maintain consistency across different mediums and different lighting conditions depicted in artwork, and to communicate color precisely (Hex codes, Pantone references, and detailed verbal descriptions all appear on reference sheets). The color communication infrastructure of the furry art community -- reference sheets with color swatches, hex codes, and description annotations -- is more systematic and precise than what most traditional portrait commissioners provide.

### Vivid Palettes and Color Harmony

Furry character design frequently uses palettes far more vivid and varied than what representational painting typically demands. A character might combine electric blue, neon green, and hot pink -- a triadic explosion that would be unusual in a landscape painting but must still read as a coherent, harmonious design. This pushes furry artists to develop strong color harmony skills, managing highly saturated palettes that could easily become garish if the relationships between hues, values, and saturations are not carefully controlled.

The best furry character artists understand that vivid colors need anchoring. Neutral tones, value contrast, and areas of rest (low-chroma passages that give the eye a break from intensity) are what make a vivid palette work. The color theory is the same color theory. The application is pushed to a more demanding extreme.

### Badge Painting Color Matching

Convention badge painting is live color mixing under pressure. The artist receives a reference sheet, must match the character's colors precisely using whatever medium they have at their table (typically watercolor, gouache, or markers), and must do it quickly -- a badge takes 20 to 60 minutes. There is no time for extended color studies or test swatches. You look at the reference, you mix, you commit. This is color mixing as performance, and the artists who do it well have internalized color relationships deeply enough to translate a hex code or a printed reference into a wet paint mixture by instinct.
