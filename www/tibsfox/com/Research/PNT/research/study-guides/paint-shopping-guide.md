# Study Guide: What to Buy — The Painter's Shopping Guide by Budget

## Three Tiers, No Wasted Money, Every Medium

Research module PNT-SG-02: a practical purchasing guide for painters at three budget levels, covering oils, acrylics, watercolor, gouache, brushes, surfaces, and studio equipment. Includes the furry artist kit for convention badge and commission work. Designed to prevent the two most common purchasing mistakes: buying too cheap (wasting money on materials that fight you) and buying too expensive (spending on gear you cannot yet appreciate).

## The Philosophy: Buy Quality Where It Matters

Art supply stores are designed to separate painters from their money. Aisles of gleaming tubes, exotic brushes, novelty tools, and specialty surfaces create the impression that painting requires a vast arsenal of expensive equipment. It does not. Most professional painters use fewer than twenty tubes of paint, three to six brushes, and one or two surface types. Mastery comes from knowing your materials deeply, not from owning every material broadly.

The rule of thumb: **spend money on paint and brushes, save money on everything else.** Good paint has more pigment and less filler, which means more intense color, better mixing, and longer working time. Good brushes hold a point, carry the right amount of paint, and spring back to shape after every stroke. These two investments make every painting session better. Cheap paint and cheap brushes make every session worse.

Surfaces, palettes, easels, and accessories are important, but they are less sensitive to quality. A canvas panel from Blick Studio performs 90% as well as a panel from a premium brand at a third of the cost. A disposable palette paper pad works as well as a glass palette for learning. An inexpensive tabletop easel holds the painting just as well as a studio easel for small formats.

This guide gives you three tiers. Start at the tier that matches your budget and your commitment. You can always upgrade components later as your skill develops and your needs become clearer.

## Tier 1: STARTER — $30-60

### Who This Is For

You want to try painting. You have never bought paint before, or you bought a set years ago and it dried up in a closet. You want to spend as little as possible while getting materials that actually work — not dollar-store supplies that will frustrate you into quitting.

### Paint: Acrylic ($12-20)

**Liquitex Basics** 6-tube set (75ml tubes) or **Amsterdam Standard** 6-tube set (20ml tubes). These are student-grade acrylics with decent pigment load and smooth consistency. The six essential colors:

1. Titanium White
2. Ivory Black
3. Cadmium Red Hue (or Naphthol Red)
4. Cadmium Yellow Hue (or Primary Yellow)
5. Ultramarine Blue
6. Burnt Sienna (or Raw Umber)

From these six, you can mix virtually any color. Yellow + Blue = greens. Red + Blue = violets. Burnt sienna tones everything down to earth colors. White and black control value. This is your complete working palette.

Why acrylic at this level: acrylics are the most forgiving medium for beginners. They dry fast (no waiting days for layers to set), clean up with water, have no toxic solvents, and are available in student-grade quality that is genuinely usable. Oil paint at the cheapest tier is frustrating because cheap oils are gritty and weak. Cheap acrylics are functional.

### Brushes: $8-12

**Princeton Snap!** series — three brushes:
- Size 10 flat (for backgrounds and big shapes)
- Size 6 flat (for general painting)
- Size 2 round (for detail and edges)

Princeton Snap brushes are synthetic, durable, and hold their shape well for the price. They handle acrylic paint cleanly and survive the abuse that beginning painters inflict (leaving them in water, scrubbing too hard, forgetting to wash them — wash them anyway, but these brushes forgive occasional neglect).

### Surface: $5-10

**Blick Studio canvas pad** (10 sheets, 9x12 inches) or **Fredrix canvas pads**. Canvas pads are sheets of primed canvas bound like a paper pad. They lie flat, accept acrylic well, and are cheap enough that you do not feel precious about each sheet. Ten sheets is ten paintings, which is a solid learning runway.

Alternative: **Strathmore 400 Series mixed media paper** pad. Cheaper than canvas pads, and the heavy paper accepts acrylic without warping. Good for small studies and practice.

### Palette: $3-5

A **disposable palette paper pad** (gray or white, tear-off sheets) or a sheet of freezer paper taped to a flat surface. Do not buy an expensive palette at this stage. You will figure out what you prefer after a few sessions.

### What You Do NOT Need at This Tier

- Mediums (gels, retarders, glazing medium) — learn to paint with paint and water first
- An easel — prop the canvas pad against a stack of books on a table
- A palette knife — use the brushes
- More than six colors — mixing is how you learn color theory
- Canvas panels or stretched canvas — too expensive per unit for learning
- Anything "specialty" — iridescent paint, crackle medium, pouring medium, texture paste. These are fun but they are not painting. Learn to paint first.

### Total: $28-47

## Tier 2: INTERMEDIATE — $100-200

### Who This Is For

You have been painting for a few months or more. You know you enjoy it. Your Starter materials are starting to feel limiting — the paint does not mix as cleanly as you would like, the brushes are losing their shape, and you want to try oil or watercolor. You are ready to invest in materials that reward your developing skill.

### Paint Option A: Upgrade Acrylics ($40-60)

**Golden Heavy Body** — individual tubes (2oz). Buy these colors:
1. Titanium White (get the large 5oz tube — you use more white than anything else)
2. Ivory Black
3. Cadmium Red Medium (genuine cadmium — the difference from hue is dramatic)
4. Cadmium Yellow Medium (genuine cadmium)
5. Ultramarine Blue
6. Burnt Sienna
7. Yellow Ochre
8. Alizarin Crimson (or Quinacridone Crimson)

Golden Heavy Body is artist-grade. The pigment load is high, the binder is consistent, and the colors mix cleanly without the muddiness that plagues student-grade paint. The difference between student-grade and artist-grade paint is the single largest quality jump in painting materials. Everything else is incremental. This jump is transformative.

Alternative: **Liquitex Professional Heavy Body** or **Winsor & Newton Professional Acrylic** — same tier, slightly different feel. Try a single tube of each brand to see which you prefer before buying a full set.

### Paint Option B: Start Oils ($50-80)

**Gamblin Artist's Oil Colors** — individual tubes (37ml). Buy:
1. Titanium-Zinc White (or Gamblin's Radiant White — faster drying)
2. Ivory Black
3. Cadmium Red Light
4. Cadmium Yellow Light
5. Ultramarine Blue
6. Burnt Sienna
7. Yellow Ochre
8. Alizarin Crimson

Plus: **Gamsol** odorless mineral spirits (4oz bottle, $5-8) for thinning and brush cleaning, and **Gamblin Solvent-Free Gel** medium ($8) if you want to thicken the paint without toxic solvents.

Gamblin oils are made in Portland, Oregon — PNW local. They grind their own pigments, and the consistency is excellent. The colors are strong, the range is wide, and the price is reasonable for artist-grade oil paint.

Oil painting requires more setup than acrylic (a glass palette, solvent or solvent-free medium, rags for cleanup, longer drying times) but rewards you with unmatched color depth and blending capability.

### Paint Option C: Add Watercolor ($30-50)

**Daniel Smith Extra Fine Watercolors** — 5ml tubes, the essentials:
1. Hansa Yellow Medium
2. Pyrrol Red (or Quinacridone Rose)
3. French Ultramarine
4. Burnt Sienna
5. Sap Green (Daniel Smith's version is superb)

Five tubes. That is enough for landscapes, portraits, still life, and badge work. Daniel Smith watercolors are made in Seattle — PNW local. Their pigment load is among the highest in the industry, and their granulating colors (Lunar Earth, Lunar Black, Primatek minerals) are unlike anything else on the market. Start with the five essentials and add specialty colors as your watercolor practice develops.

### Brushes: $20-35

**Rosemary & Co.** (UK, order online — excellent quality at remarkable prices) or **Princeton Heritage** (widely available in US stores):
- Series 4050 or equivalent — Size 8 flat
- Series 4050 — Size 4 flat
- Series 4000 or equivalent — Size 6 round
- Series 4000 — Size 2 round
- Size 0 round or rigger — for fine detail

For watercolor, add one **mop brush** (size 8 or 10) for washes.

These brushes hold a point, carry the right amount of paint, and spring back to shape. They are a significant step up from Snap brushes and will last years with proper care (rinse after every session, reshape the tip, store upright in a jar or flat in a brush roll — never bristle-down in a jar).

### Surfaces: $15-30

**Stretched canvas** (two-pack, 11x14 or 12x16) for oil or acrylic — Blick Premiere or Fredrix Red Label. These are cotton canvas on stretcher bars, pre-primed with gesso. The difference from a canvas pad is real: the surface has spring, the edges wrap, and the finished painting can be hung as-is or framed.

**Watercolor paper:** Arches 140lb cold press (a quarter-sheet pack or a block). Arches is the gold standard for watercolor paper — it handles wash, lifting, and layering without pilling or warping.

### Palette: $10-15

**Glass palette** (for oil or acrylic): A piece of tempered glass (8x10 or larger) from a picture frame, with a sheet of gray paper placed beneath it. The glass is easy to clean (scrape with a razor blade), the gray paper shows color temperature accurately, and the smooth surface makes mixing effortless.

For watercolor: a **ceramic or enamel mixing palette** with wells for individual colors and flat mixing areas.

### Optional Upgrades: $10-25

- **Palette knife** (size 2 or 3, diamond shape) — $5-8. For mixing paint on the palette and for palette knife painting techniques.
- **Tabletop easel** — $15-25. A simple H-frame tabletop easel holds panels and small canvases at a comfortable angle.

### Total: $95-195

## Tier 3: ADVANCED — $300+

### Who This Is For

You are a committed painter. You paint regularly — several times a week. You understand your medium. You know what you like and what you need. You are ready for the best materials available, not as a luxury but as a tool investment that serves your practice for years.

### Paint: Professional Grade ($100-200+)

**For oils:**
- **Old Holland** — the most expensive oil paint on Earth, and worth every cent for the pigments that matter. Their Cadmiums, Cobalts, and earth colors are extraordinarily pure and intense.
- **Michael Harding** — British, handmade, legendary pigment load. His paints feel like colored butter. French Ultramarine, Indian Yellow, and the earth colors are exceptional.
- **Williamsburg** — handmade in Brooklyn. Known for distinctive grinds — some colors are intentionally coarse for textural character.

You do not need to buy all your colors from a premium brand. A practical approach: buy whites, cadmiums, and specialty colors (Cobalt Blue, Viridian, Indian Yellow) from Old Holland or Michael Harding. Buy earth colors and blacks from Gamblin or Winsor & Newton Artist — these pigments are less variable between brands, so the premium is smaller.

**For watercolor:**
- **Schmincke Horadam** — German, old-world quality. Rich, vibrant, beautifully granulating.
- **Daniel Smith** (expanded palette) — add Lunar Earth, Shadow Violet, Cascade Green, Undersea Green to your essentials.
- **Winsor & Newton Professional** — the original artist-grade watercolor. Consistent, reliable, archival.

### Brushes: $50-150

**Kolinsky sable watercolor brushes:**
- **Escoda Reserva** (Series 1212) — Spanish, top-tier Kolinsky sable. Sizes 4, 6, 8, and 12. These brushes hold a point that defies belief. They carry enormous amounts of water, release it evenly, and spring back to a perfect tip for decades with care.
- **Da Vinci Maestro** (Series 10) — German, exceptional Kolinsky. Slightly stiffer spring than Escoda.
- **Winsor & Newton Series 7** — the classic. The standard against which all watercolor brushes are measured since the 1860s.

A single high-quality Kolinsky sable round (size 8) costs $30-60. It replaces three or four synthetic brushes, and if cared for (never leave in water, clean gently, reshape, store in a brush roll with moth repellent), it lasts 10-20 years.

**For oil:** **Rosemary & Co. Ultimate series** (Kolinsky sable for oil) or **Robert Simmons Signet** (hog bristle for impasto). The best oil painting brushes combine a natural hair (sable or bristle) that interacts with the oil medium in ways synthetic fibers cannot replicate.

### Surfaces: $50-100

**Belgian linen canvas** (Claessens or comparable) — double-oil-primed, portrait weight. The finest painting surface available. The linen weave has a distinctive texture that holds paint differently from cotton, and the double-oil priming creates a surface with a subtle tooth that is a pleasure to paint on. Available on stretcher bars or as rolls for custom stretching.

**Cradled wood panels** (Ampersand Gessobord or Artefex) — smooth, rigid, and archival. Excellent for detailed work and for painters who prefer a hard surface.

### Studio Equipment: $100-300

- **Studio easel** — a full-size H-frame easel ($100-200) or a counterbalance easel ($200-400) for large canvases. The studio easel is the painter's workbench. It holds the painting at the right height, allows you to step back without moving the work, and tilts forward for varnishing.
- **Pochade box** — a compact, portable painting setup ($80-200) that holds a small panel, has a built-in palette, and stores tubes and brushes in a compartment. The pochade box is the plein air painter's essential tool. It mounts on a tripod or sits on your lap.
- **Lighting** — a 5000K daylight LED panel or clamp light ($30-60). Painting under correct light is critical for accurate color mixing. Incandescent bulbs are too warm. Cool-white LEDs are too blue. 5000K is the standard for color-accurate studio lighting.

### Total: $300-750+

## What NOT to Waste Money On

Regardless of your budget tier, avoid these common money pits:

**Cheap canvas that warps.** The cheapest stretched canvas (dollar-store or bulk-pack) uses thin stretcher bars that bow under the tension of primed canvas. The surface dips in the center, the edges flex when you press with a brush, and the cotton is so thin you can see through it. Spend a few dollars more on Fredrix Red Label or Blick Premiere, or use canvas pads (which lie flat on a table and have no stretcher bars to warp).

**Novelty brush shapes.** Fan brushes, angular flats, oval mops, multi-loaders, and "one-stroke" brushes are marketed to beginners as shortcuts. They are not. A flat and a round will do everything you need. Special brushes solve problems you do not have yet. If you develop a specific need (a fan brush for blending, a mop for watercolor washes), buy it then. Do not buy a 24-brush set because it looks impressive.

**Gimmick tools and accessories.** Color wheels (learn color mixing by mixing, not by spinning a wheel), painting medium sets (you need one medium at most), texture paste kits, pouring medium kits, and "painting systems" that promise results without practice. There is no substitute for practice. No accessory replaces the hours.

**Expensive storage before you need it.** A fishing tackle box or a plastic toolbox holds everything a beginning painter needs. Do not buy a rolling art cart, a custom brush holder, or a dedicated art supply cabinet until you have accumulated enough materials to justify it.

**Too many colors too soon.** A 48-tube set looks generous. In practice, you will use 8-10 of those colors regularly and the rest will dry up unused. Buy individual tubes in the colors you need. Add one new color at a time as your mixing practice reveals gaps. The expanded palette should serve your painting, not the other way around.

## The Furry Artist Kit

For convention badge painting, commission illustration, and character art:

### Markers: Copic Ciao or Sketch ($40-80 for a starter set)

Build your set slowly, character by character. Start with:
- 6-8 markers in your fursona's colors (base fur, shadow fur, accent colors, skin tones)
- E00, E11, E31 (skin/cream tones — useful for most character types)
- C1, C3, C5 (cool grays for shading any color)
- 0 Colorless Blender (for smooth gradients and corrections)

Copics are $4-8 each. Do not buy a 72-marker set. Buy markers as you need them, one commission at a time. After a year of commission work, you will have 30-50 markers that you actually use, and you will not have wasted money on colors you never touch.

### Pens: Sakura Pigma Micron ($8-12 for a set)

- Micron 005 (0.20mm) — fine detail, fur texture
- Micron 01 (0.25mm) — standard inking
- Micron 05 (0.45mm) — bold outlines
- Micron 08 (0.50mm) — heavy outlines, name text

These are archival, waterproof when dry, and the industry standard for inking illustration at any scale.

### Paper: Hot Press Watercolor ($8-12)

**Strathmore 400 Series** or **Canson XL** hot press watercolor paper (smooth surface). Cut to 2.5 x 3.5 inches for badges. Hot press is essential for Copic work — the smooth surface prevents markers from bleeding and feathering. Cold press works for watercolor badges but creates a textured look.

### Laminator ($25-40)

A basic 9-inch laminator (Amazon Basics, Scotch, or similar). Uses 3mil or 5mil pouches. Buy badge-sized (2.5 x 3.75 inch) or credit-card-sized pouches. 5mil is the standard for badges — sturdy enough to survive a weekend of convention wear.

### White Gel Pen ($3-5)

**Uni-ball Signo Broad** (white) or **Sakura Gelly Roll** (white) for highlights over dark ink and marker. The Signo is more opaque; the Gelly Roll is smoother to write with. Both work.

### Furry Artist Kit Total: $84-147

This covers badge painting, small commissions, and Artist Trading Card work. As your commission business grows, add watercolor supplies (for traditional badges), Copic Sketch upgrades (larger ink reservoir), and specialty items (metallic gel pens, holographic lamination pouches, shaped badge cutters).

## The Upgrade Path

Materials upgrades should follow your skill development, not precede it. The path:

1. **Month 1-3:** Starter tier. Learn to mix colors, control values, handle a brush. The materials are adequate and the focus is on practice, not gear.
2. **Month 4-8:** Upgrade to Intermediate brushes first (the biggest single improvement), then artist-grade paint. These two upgrades transform your experience.
3. **Month 9-12:** Add a second medium (oil, watercolor, or gouache). Each medium teaches different skills and the cross-pollination improves your work in every medium.
4. **Year 2+:** Invest in Advanced surfaces and studio equipment as your practice demands. By now you know what you need, and purchases are driven by specific requirements rather than general aspiration.

The most common mistake is upgrading too fast. A painter with $300 of supplies and 20 hours of practice is not better equipped than a painter with $40 of supplies and 200 hours of practice. The hours matter more than the gear. Always.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [materials-making]: This entire guide is an applied materials curriculum. Understanding why artist-grade paint outperforms student-grade (pigment concentration, binder quality, lightfastness), why Kolinsky sable holds a point (the microscopic structure of sable hair creates capillary action), and why Belgian linen outlasts cotton canvas (linen fibers are longer and stronger) deepens the painter's relationship with their tools. Material knowledge is craft knowledge.
- **Home-Economics** — [budgeting]: The three-tier structure teaches budget allocation — the idea that you can achieve excellent results at any budget level by prioritizing spending on the items that matter most (paint quality, brush quality) and economizing on items where the quality differential is smaller (surfaces, accessories). This is the budgeting principle of directed spending applied to a specific domain.
- **Home-Economics** — [value-purchasing]: The guide distinguishes between price and value. A $6 tube of Gamblin oil paint is more expensive than a $3 tube of student-grade oil paint, but it mixes 4-5 times more clean color per tube because the pigment density is higher. The cost per usable painting session is actually lower for the artist-grade paint. Understanding lifetime cost versus purchase price is a transferable value-purchasing skill.
- **Learning** — [study-strategies]: The upgrade path follows the learning science principle of progressive complexity — start with simple tools, add complexity as skill develops, and let each upgrade arrive when the painter has the skill to appreciate and utilize it.
