# Study Guide: Art History for Painters — A Visual Timeline

## The Movements Every Painter Should Know

Research module PNT-SG-01: a chronological survey of the major art movements organized as a study resource, with key dates, key artists, key paintings, and an explanation of what changed at each transition. Designed for painters who want to understand the lineage they are part of.

**Cross-references:** [PNT-01] History of Painting, [PNT-06] Genres & Movements, [PNT-05] Color Theory, [PNT-09] PNW Painting Scene.

**College of Knowledge:** Art Department — [art-in-context]. History Department — [continuity-change], [perspectives-empathy].

---

## How to Use This Guide

This is not a comprehensive art history textbook. It is a working reference for painters — the movements you need to know, the artists you need to study, and the paintings you need to see. For each period, you get:

- **Dates** — approximate, because movements overlap and definitions vary
- **What changed** — the key innovation, rebellion, or shift in thinking
- **Key artists** — two or three names you should be able to discuss
- **Must-know painting** — one work that defines or epitomizes the movement
- **Why painters care** — what you can learn from this movement for your own work

Read it once through for the arc of the story. Then use it as a reference when you encounter a new artist or painting and need to place them in context. The self-test at the end will tell you how much you have internalized.

## 1. Prehistoric Art (c. 45,000 BCE - c. 3,000 BCE)

**What changed:** Everything began. Humans started making images — handprints, animals, abstract marks — on cave walls and rock faces using earth pigments and simple tools. This is the origin of every painting tradition that followed.

**Key artists:** Anonymous. We know no names. But the hands that painted the Chauvet cave animals were as skilled and deliberate as any named master.

**Must-know painting:** The Sulawesi warty pig (c. 45,500 BCE, Leang Tedongnge cave, Indonesia) — the oldest known figurative painting in the world. Or the Hall of Bulls at Lascaux (c. 17,000 BCE, France) — the most famous painted cave.

**Why painters care:** Prehistoric painters understood value, silhouette, and the power of the negative image (blown-hand stencils). They exploited the three-dimensional surface of cave walls to suggest volume. They worked with a limited palette of earth pigments and made it sufficient. Every constraint you face has a 40,000-year-old precedent.

---

## 2. Ancient Egyptian Painting (c. 3,100 BCE - 30 BCE)

**What changed:** Painting became systematic. Egyptian tomb painters worked within strict conventions — profile heads, frontal torsos, hierarchical scale — not from artistic limitation but theological precision. Every figure had to be shown in its most complete, recognizable form for use in the afterlife.

**Key artists:** Again, mostly anonymous, though a few names survive. Nebamun's tomb paintings (c. 1350 BCE, now in the British Museum) are attributed to an unnamed painter of exceptional skill.

**Must-know painting:** The *Fowling Scene* from the Tomb of Nebamun (c. 1350 BCE). A man stands in a papyrus boat, surrounded by birds, fish, and his family. The composition is encyclopedic — every form shown in its most characteristic view. The color is vivid, the line is confident, the design is timeless.

**Why painters care:** Egyptian painting demonstrates that painting is not obligated to pursue optical realism. It can be symbolic, diagrammatic, and functional while remaining visually powerful. The flat, unmodulated color areas and strong outlines influence graphic design and illustration to this day.

---

## 3. Greek and Roman Painting (c. 700 BCE - 476 CE)

**What changed:** The pursuit of optical illusion began. Greek painters (we are told by ancient writers) developed shading, highlights, foreshortening, and spatial depth — the tools of pictorial realism. Roman painters inherited and expanded these techniques, covering the walls of villas and public buildings with elaborate architectural illusions and mythological scenes.

**Key artists:** Apelles (4th century BCE, Greek — regarded as the greatest painter of antiquity, no surviving work). For surviving Roman painting, the anonymous masters of Pompeii and Herculaneum.

**Must-know painting:** The *Villa of the Mysteries* frieze (c. 60 BCE, Pompeii). Life-sized figures against deep cinnabar red, depicting what appears to be a Dionysian initiation. Confident anatomy, convincing drapery, dramatic narrative. Also: the Fayum mummy portraits (1st-3rd century CE, Roman Egypt) — the oldest surviving realistic portraits, painted in encaustic on wood panels, startlingly modern in appearance.

**Why painters care:** The Pompeian painters understood atmospheric perspective, cast shadows, highlights, and reflected light two thousand years ago. The Fayum portraits prove that naturalistic portraiture was achieved in the ancient world and then lost for a millennium. Studying what was known, lost, and rediscovered puts our own era in perspective.

---

## 4. Medieval and Byzantine Painting (c. 500 - 1400 CE)

**What changed:** Painting served the church. Byzantine icons and medieval illuminated manuscripts prioritized spiritual presence over physical accuracy. Gold backgrounds replaced sky. Figures were hieratic and frontal. Space was symbolic, not geometric. This was not regression — it was redirection. The goal was transcendence, not illusion.

**Key artists:** Andrei Rublev (c. 1360-1430, Russian — the greatest icon painter). The Limbourg Brothers (fl. 1400-1416, Franco-Netherlandish — creators of the most lavish medieval manuscript).

**Must-know painting:** Rublev's *Trinity* (c. 1411). Three angels seated at a table, their forms flowing into one another in a composition of extraordinary grace and geometric harmony. Or the Limbourg Brothers' *Tres Riches Heures du Duc de Berry* (c. 1412-16) — the calendar pages, with their vivid landscapes and court scenes, standing at the threshold between the medieval and the modern.

**Why painters care:** Medieval painters were master colorists. They used the most expensive pigments available — lapis lazuli, gold leaf, vermilion — and understood how to create visual impact through flat color juxtaposition. The icon painting tradition's emphasis on inner light rather than external illumination offers an alternative to the Western realist tradition that remains relevant.

---

## 5. The Renaissance (c. 1400 - 1600)

**What changed:** The invention (or rediscovery) of linear perspective, the development of oil painting, the study of human anatomy from direct observation, and the emergence of the individual artist as a named genius rather than an anonymous craftsman. Painting became a science as well as an art.

**Key artists:** Leonardo da Vinci (1452-1519, Italian — sfumato, anatomical study, the universal genius). Michelangelo (1475-1564, Italian — the Sistine Chapel ceiling, sculptural painting). Jan van Eyck (c. 1390-1441, Netherlandish — pioneered oil painting technique, microscopic detail).

**Must-know painting:** Leonardo's *Mona Lisa* (c. 1503-19). Not because it is the most important Renaissance painting (it may not be), but because it demonstrates sfumato (the smoky blending of edges), atmospheric perspective, psychological complexity, and oil painting technique at the highest level. Alternatively: Van Eyck's *Arnolfini Portrait* (1434) — a technical marvel of oil painting that anticipates the Northern tradition of detailed realism.

**Why painters care:** The Renaissance established the technical foundation that Western painting built on for the next five centuries: linear perspective, chiaroscuro (light-dark modeling), anatomical accuracy, and oil painting as the dominant medium. Every painter working in a realist tradition is, to some degree, still working with Renaissance tools.

---

## 6. The Baroque (c. 1600 - 1750)

**What changed:** Drama. Extreme contrasts of light and dark (chiaroscuro pushed to its limit, called *tenebrism*). Diagonal compositions. Emotional intensity. Movement and energy replacing Renaissance calm and balance. Painting became theatrical.

**Key artists:** Caravaggio (1571-1610, Italian — invented tenebrism, painted from life models including street people, revolutionized religious painting). Rembrandt van Rijn (1606-1669, Dutch — the greatest portraitist, master of light, psychological depth beyond anything before him). Artemisia Gentileschi (1593-c.1656, Italian — powerful, visceral narrative painting, one of the first recognized female painters).

**Must-know painting:** Caravaggio's *The Calling of Saint Matthew* (1599-1600). A beam of light cuts across a dark tavern, falling on Matthew the tax collector. The drama is entirely in the light. Also: Rembrandt's *Self-Portrait with Two Circles* (c. 1665-69) — late Rembrandt, where the paint itself becomes the subject.

**Why painters care:** The Baroque teaches light as drama. Caravaggio's tenebrism is the ancestor of every dramatically lit photograph, film scene, and contemporary realist painting. Rembrandt's late work teaches that paint handling — the physical texture and application of pigment — is an expressive element in itself.

---

## 7. Rococo (c. 1720 - 1780)

**What changed:** Lightness replaced weight. The Baroque's dark drama gave way to pastel colors, ornamental curves, playful subjects, and aristocratic elegance. Painting became decorative, intimate, and hedonistic.

**Key artists:** Jean-Antoine Watteau (1684-1721, French — invented the *fete galante*, paintings of aristocratic leisure in idyllic landscapes). Francois Boucher (1703-1770, French — decorative painter to the court of Louis XV, sensual mythological scenes).

**Must-know painting:** Watteau's *Pilgrimage to Cythera* (1717). Elegantly dressed figures in a golden landscape, departing for (or returning from) the island of love. Dreamy atmosphere, shimmering color, a sense of transience that lifts it above mere decoration.

**Why painters care:** Rococo painters were extraordinary colorists. Their pastel palettes and broken-color brushwork anticipated Impressionism by 150 years. Watteau's atmospheric landscapes demonstrate that painting can be about mood and feeling rather than narrative or dogma.

---

## 8. Neoclassicism (c. 1760 - 1840)

**What changed:** A reaction against Rococo excess. Inspired by the rediscovery of Pompeii and Herculaneum, painters returned to classical subjects, restrained compositions, and the idealized human form. Painting became a vehicle for moral and political messages, particularly during the French Revolution and Napoleonic era.

**Key artists:** Jacques-Louis David (1748-1825, French — the painter of the French Revolution, neoclassical austerity). Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (1780-1867, French — the supreme draftsman, linear precision, sensuously idealized form).

**Must-know painting:** David's *The Death of Marat* (1793). A murdered revolutionary in his bathtub, painted with photographic precision and classical restraint. The simplicity of the composition — a figure, a box, a void — gives it emotional power that more elaborate compositions cannot match.

**Why painters care:** Neoclassicism demonstrates the power of drawing. Ingres's contour lines are as precise as engineering drawings. David's compositions are as structured as architecture. For painters who struggle with form, studying the Neoclassicists is a corrective.

---

## 9. Romanticism (c. 1800 - 1850)

**What changed:** Feeling replaced reason. Where Neoclassicism valued order, clarity, and the ideal, Romanticism valued emotion, the individual, the sublime, and the terrifying power of nature. Color became dominant over line. Landscapes rivaled (and surpassed) history painting in prestige for the first time.

**Key artists:** J.M.W. Turner (1775-1851, English — light, atmosphere, color dissolving form; the most radical painter before Impressionism). Caspar David Friedrich (1774-1840, German — solitary figures dwarfed by vast, sublime landscapes).

**Must-know painting:** Turner's *Rain, Steam and Speed — The Great Western Railway* (1844). A locomotive hurtles through a veil of golden rain and mist. The painting is almost abstract — form dissolved into light and atmosphere. It anticipates Impressionism by thirty years. Also: Friedrich's *Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog* (c. 1818) — the iconic image of the Romantic individual confronting nature.

**Why painters care:** Turner proved that painting could be about light and atmosphere rather than objects and narratives. His late works are essentially abstract paintings made sixty years before abstract art was "invented." Friedrich demonstrated that landscape could carry the emotional weight previously reserved for religious and historical subjects.

---

## 10. Realism (c. 1840 - 1880)

**What changed:** Painters turned away from classical ideals and romantic fantasies to paint ordinary life as it actually was. Workers, peasants, and everyday scenes replaced gods, heroes, and aristocrats. This was a political as well as an artistic revolution — a declaration that common life was worthy of art.

**Key artists:** Gustave Courbet (1819-1877, French — declared himself a Realist, painted working people at monumental scale). Jean-Francois Millet (1814-1875, French — peasant life dignified and monumental).

**Must-know painting:** Courbet's *A Burial at Ornans* (1849-50). A life-sized village funeral, with real people from Ornans as models, painted at the scale previously reserved for historical and mythological subjects. The art establishment was scandalized. Courbet did not care.

**Why painters care:** Realism gave painters permission to paint what they actually saw around them. This is the direct ancestor of contemporary realism, plein air painting, and every artist who chooses everyday subjects over dramatic narratives.

---

## 11. Impressionism (c. 1860 - 1886)

**What changed:** Painters took their easels outdoors and painted what the eye actually perceived: light, color, atmosphere, and the fleeting effects of a specific moment. Visible brushstrokes, broken color, and an emphasis on capturing the sensation of light rather than rendering objects precisely. The most influential single movement in the history of painting.

**Key artists:** Claude Monet (1840-1926, French — the purest Impressionist, devoted his life to painting light). Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841-1919, French — Impressionist color applied to figures and social scenes). Berthe Morisot (1841-1895, French — intimate domestic scenes with luminous color).

**Must-know painting:** Monet's *Impression, Sunrise* (1872). The painting that gave the movement its name (originally meant as an insult). A harbor at dawn, rendered in quick dashes of blue, orange, and gray. The sun is a single blob of vermilion. It looks like nothing from close up. From ten feet away, it looks like dawn.

**Why painters care:** Impressionism teaches color mixing through juxtaposition (placing complementary colors side by side so the eye mixes them at a distance), the importance of painting from direct observation, and the power of leaving marks visible rather than blending everything smooth. Every plein air painter working today is an heir to Impressionism.

---

## 12. Post-Impressionism (c. 1886 - 1910)

**What changed:** The generation after the Impressionists kept the bright color and direct observation but pushed in individual directions that shattered the movement's unity and pointed toward the twentieth century. Structure, expression, symbolism, and personal vision replaced Impressionism's focus on optical sensation.

**Key artists:** Paul Cezanne (1839-1906, French — "the father of modern art," reconstructed form from color, bridged Impressionism and Cubism). Vincent van Gogh (1853-1890, Dutch — color as emotional expression, brushwork as personal handwriting). Paul Gauguin (1848-1903, French — flat color, bold outline, escape from European naturalism).

**Must-know painting:** Cezanne's *Mont Sainte-Victoire* series (1880s-1906). A single mountain painted dozens of times, each version analyzing and reconstructing the landscape into planes of color. Also: Van Gogh's *The Starry Night* (1889) — emotion made visible through swirling brushwork and heightened color.

**Why painters care:** Cezanne teaches that painting is construction — building a coherent image from individual marks of color, each one a decision about hue, value, and temperature. Van Gogh teaches that brushwork is a personal vocabulary. Gauguin teaches that color can be freed from local observation.

---

## 13. Fauvism (c. 1905 - 1908)

**What changed:** Color exploded. The Fauves (French for "wild beasts") used color for emotional and decorative impact rather than naturalistic description. A face could be green. A tree could be red. The only question was whether the color felt right in the context of the painting.

**Key artists:** Henri Matisse (1869-1954, French — the leader of the Fauves, devoted his life to the expressive power of color and simplified form). Andre Derain (1880-1954, French — landscapes in blazing, non-naturalistic color).

**Must-know painting:** Matisse's *The Joy of Life (Le Bonheur de vivre)* (1905-06). Figures in a landscape, rendered in broad areas of pure, saturated, non-naturalistic color. The painting is a declaration that color is an independent expressive force, not a servant of realistic description.

**Why painters care:** Fauvism is the permission to use color expressively. If you have ever wanted to paint a shadow purple or a tree orange because it felt right, you are working in the Fauvist tradition. Matisse's color harmonies remain among the most studied and admired in painting.

---

## 14. Cubism (c. 1907 - 1920s)

**What changed:** The picture plane shattered. Cubist painters depicted objects from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, fracturing form into geometric facets and reassembling them in compositions that acknowledged the flatness of the canvas rather than pretending it was a window. This was the most radical break with Renaissance perspective since the Renaissance invented it.

**Key artists:** Pablo Picasso (1881-1973, Spanish — co-inventor of Cubism, relentlessly inventive across every phase of his career). Georges Braque (1882-1963, French — co-inventor of Cubism, quieter than Picasso, equally brilliant in his Cubist work).

**Must-know painting:** Picasso's *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon* (1907). Five female figures with fractured, angular bodies and faces inspired by African masks and Iberian sculpture. Not fully Cubist yet, but the painting that detonated the explosion.

**Why painters care:** Even painters who work in realist traditions benefit from understanding Cubist composition. The idea that a painting is a flat surface with shapes, colors, and edges — not a window into a three-dimensional illusion — is liberating. It frees you to compose in terms of the painting's actual surface, not just the subject it depicts.

---

## 15. Abstract Expressionism (c. 1940s - 1960s)

**What changed:** The act of painting itself became the subject. Abstract Expressionists made large-scale, non-representational paintings that communicated through gesture, color, texture, and scale rather than depiction. The painter's physical engagement with the canvas — dripping, pouring, slashing, staining — became visible in the finished work. The center of the art world shifted from Paris to New York.

**Key artists:** Jackson Pollock (1912-1956, American — drip paintings, the canvas on the floor, all-over composition with no focal point). Mark Rothko (1903-1970, American — luminous rectangles of color floating on color, meditative and transcendent). Willem de Kooning (1904-1997, Dutch-American — gestural abstraction that kept one foot in figuration).

**Must-know painting:** Pollock's *Number 1A, 1948* (1948). House paint dripped, poured, and flung onto a canvas laid on the floor. No focal point, no hierarchy, no representation — just the record of the painter's movement through space and time. Or: Rothko's *No. 61 (Rust and Blue)* (1953) — two rectangles of color that, in person, produce an emotional response that reproductions cannot convey. You have to see a Rothko in person. There is no substitute.

**Why painters care:** Abstract Expressionism expanded the definition of what painting could be. Even if you paint representationally, the AbEx painters demonstrated that the physical quality of paint — its texture, viscosity, opacity, and the energy of its application — communicates meaning independent of subject matter. Every mark you make carries gestural energy.

---

## 16. Pop Art (c. 1958 - 1970s)

**What changed:** Art looked at popular culture and asked, "Why not this?" Comic strips, advertisements, consumer products, and celebrity images became painting subjects, rendered in bright, flat colors with commercial printing techniques. Pop Art blurred the line between high art and mass culture.

**Key artists:** Andy Warhol (1928-1987, American — silk-screened icons of consumer culture and celebrity, the artist as brand). Roy Lichtenstein (1923-1997, American — enlarged comic book panels, Ben-Day dots, questioning what painting is by mimicking mechanical reproduction).

**Must-know painting:** Warhol's *Campbell's Soup Cans* (1962). Thirty-two canvases, each depicting a different flavor of Campbell's soup. The question the work asks — Is this art? Is repetition expression? Is a painting of a commercial product different from the product itself? — reverberates through every subsequent discussion of art and commerce.

**Why painters care:** Pop Art freed painters from the obligation to be "serious." It demonstrated that subject matter is a choice, not a hierarchy. And Lichtenstein's work is a masterclass in how color, line, and composition function — he distilled complex images into their graphic essentials.

---

## 17. Contemporary Art (c. 1970 - present)

**What changed:** Everything and nothing. Contemporary art encompasses an extraordinary diversity of approaches, mediums, and intentions. Painting has been declared dead multiple times and continues to thrive. Photorealism, neo-expressionism, digital painting, street art, and hybrid practices coexist. The single narrative of art history has given way to multiple simultaneous narratives.

**Key artists:** Gerhard Richter (1932-, German — works simultaneously in photorealistic, abstract, and blurred styles, questioning the nature of representation). Kerry James Marshall (1955-, American — large-scale figurative paintings centering Black life, reclaiming the canon of Western painting). Jenny Saville (1970-, English — monumental figurative painting, the body as landscape).

**Must-know painting:** Richter's *Abstraktes Bild (809-4)* (1994) — a large abstract painting made by dragging a squeegee across layers of wet oil paint, creating a surface of extraordinary complexity through a simple mechanical gesture. Or: Marshall's *Mastry* series — paintings that place Black figures into the visual language of Old Master painting, simultaneously honoring and challenging the tradition.

**Why painters care:** Contemporary painting proves the medium is not dead and not exhausted. There are more ways to paint, more subjects to paint, and more purposes for painting than at any point in history. The question for a contemporary painter is not "What should painting be?" but "What do I need painting to be?"

## Self-Test

Cover the answers and test yourself. These questions check whether you can navigate the timeline and understand the *why* behind each movement, not just memorize dates and names.

**1.** Which movement rejected Impressionism's focus on optical sensation in favor of structured, analytical composition? Who was its key figure?

**2.** Name the painting that gave Impressionism its name, and the artist who painted it. What year?

**3.** What technique did Caravaggio pioneer that uses extreme light-dark contrast for dramatic effect? Name one painting that demonstrates it.

**4.** Why were Egyptian tomb paintings not intended for living viewers? How did this affect their visual conventions?

**5.** Which two artists co-invented Cubism? What was the first painting that pointed toward the movement?

**6.** What distinguishes Abstract Expressionism from all previous movements in terms of the physical act of painting?

**7.** Turner is often called a precursor to Impressionism. His late paintings anticipated the movement by how many decades? Name one.

**8.** What was a "masterpiece" in the medieval guild system, and what purpose did it serve?

**9.** The Fauves used color for what purpose instead of naturalistic description? Who led the group?

**10.** Why do you need to see a Rothko painting in person rather than in a reproduction?

**11.** Which contemporary painter works simultaneously in photorealistic, abstract, and blurred styles?

**12.** Place these movements in chronological order: Cubism, Romanticism, Baroque, Impressionism, Renaissance, Pop Art.

**13.** The oldest known figurative painting in the world is in which country? What does it depict?

**14.** What principle did Xie He identify as the most important in Chinese painting? How does it differ from the Western pursuit of optical realism?

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [art-in-context]: This entire study guide is an exercise in art-in-context — understanding paintings not as isolated objects but as products of specific historical moments, cultural values, and artistic conversations. Each movement responded to what came before it, and understanding that chain of responses is essential for understanding any individual painting.
- **History** — [continuity-change]: Art history is a case study in continuity and change. The continuous thread (humans making marks on surfaces with pigment) persists across 45,000 years. The changes (what counts as a worthy subject, how space should be represented, what color means) define the movements. This guide makes both the continuity and the changes visible.
- **History** — [perspectives-empathy]: Studying art from different eras requires perspective-taking. Egyptian painters were not failed realists — they were successful theologians. Medieval icon painters were not primitive — they were pursuing transcendence. Understanding each movement on its own terms, rather than judging it by contemporary standards, is a historical thinking skill.
- **Art** — [creative-process]: The timeline reveals that creative breakthroughs almost always involve breaking a rule that the previous generation considered unbreakable. Impressionists broke the rule of smooth finish. Cubists broke the rule of single viewpoint. Abstract Expressionists broke the rule of representation. Understanding which rules were broken and why is a guide to creative thinking.
