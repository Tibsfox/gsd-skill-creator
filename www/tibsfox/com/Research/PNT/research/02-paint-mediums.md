# The Painter's Palette — A Complete Guide to Paint Mediums

## Understanding What You're Actually Putting on the Surface

Research module PNT-02: a comprehensive guide to every major paint medium, from oil and acrylic to encaustic, ink, airbrush, and digital. Includes pigment science, lightfastness ratings, and the direct connection between airbrush painting and fursuit fabrication.

**Cross-references:** [PNT-01] History, [PNT-03] Brushes & Tools, [PNT-04] Surfaces, [PNT-05] Color Theory, [FFA-04] Fursuit Fabrication, [FFA-10] Fursuit Makers Index.

---

Every paint is the same thing: pigment held together by a binder. That is it. Pigment is the color — ground-up minerals, synthetic chemicals, organic compounds, carbon. Binder is the glue — the substance that holds the pigment particles together and adheres them to the surface. The binder is what defines the medium. Oil paint is pigment in drying oil. Watercolor is pigment in gum arabic. Acrylic is pigment in polymer emulsion. Encaustic is pigment in hot beeswax. Change the binder, change everything: drying time, texture, transparency, permanence, working properties, cleanup, and the entire experience of painting.

This chapter walks through every major paint medium a contemporary painter might encounter, from the oldest to the newest, with enough practical detail to actually use them.

## Oil Paint

### The King of Mediums

Oil paint has dominated Western painting for nearly six hundred years, and for good reason. No other medium offers the same combination of slow working time, rich color depth, smooth blending, and archival permanence. When someone says "painting" without qualification, they usually mean oil painting.

### How It Works

Oil paint consists of pigment ground into a drying oil, most commonly linseed oil (pressed from flax seeds). The oil does not evaporate like water — it undergoes a chemical reaction called oxidative polymerization, absorbing oxygen from the air and cross-linking into a tough, flexible, insoluble film. This is why oil paint dries so slowly (days to weeks for thin layers, months for thick impasto) and why it remains workable for so long. It is also why oil paint eventually becomes extremely durable — a fully cured oil painting is essentially a thin sheet of flexible plastic with pigment embedded in it.

### The Fat-Over-Lean Rule

This is the single most important technical principle in oil painting. Each successive layer of paint should contain more oil (be "fatter") than the layer beneath it. If you put a lean (low-oil) layer over a fat (high-oil) layer, the lean layer dries first and becomes rigid while the fat layer underneath is still moving. The result: cracking. This is not optional advice. This is paint chemistry. Violate fat-over-lean and your painting will crack, possibly within years, certainly within decades.

In practice, this means starting with thin layers thinned with solvent (lean) and progressively adding more medium (oil) to later layers. Many painters thin their initial block-in with odorless mineral spirits (OMS), then switch to a painting medium containing oil for subsequent layers.

### Solvents and Mediums

**Solvents** thin paint and speed drying by evaporating. Traditional turpentine (distilled from pine resin) is the historical standard but produces fumes that many painters find objectionable or harmful. Odorless mineral spirits (OMS), sold under brand names like Gamsol (Gamblin) and Sansodor (Winsor & Newton), are the modern alternative — they dissolve paint effectively with minimal odor and lower toxicity. Some painters have eliminated solvents entirely, using oil or alkyd mediums to thin paint instead.

**Mediums** modify paint behavior. Linseed oil (the same oil used to grind the paint) makes paint fatter and more flowing. Stand oil (linseed oil heated to high temperature without air) is thicker, produces a smooth enamel-like surface, and resists yellowing. Alkyd mediums like Liquin (Winsor & Newton) speed drying dramatically — overnight instead of days — while maintaining fat-over-lean compatibility. Walnut oil and safflower oil yellow less than linseed but dry more slowly. Each painter eventually develops a preferred medium recipe.

### Brands and Grades

**Artist grade** oil paint contains high pigment loads, uses quality binders, and provides full tinting strength and lightfastness. Expect to pay $10-40+ per tube depending on pigment cost. **Student grade** uses less pigment, more filler, and sometimes substitutes cheaper pigments for expensive ones (labeling them as "hues"). Student grade is fine for learning but lacks the richness and handling of professional paint.

Notable brands: **Winsor & Newton Artists'** (reliable standard), **Gamblin** (American, excellent value, strong environmental ethos — they make Gamsol), **Old Holland** (Dutch, extremely high pigment load, traditional grinding, expensive), **Michael Harding** (British, handmade, extraordinary quality), **Williamsburg** (American, handmade by Golden, distinctive textures because each color is ground to optimize that specific pigment).

### Toxicity and Safety

Oil painting's reputation for toxicity is partly deserved and partly exaggerated. The paint itself is not especially toxic — linseed oil is food-grade, and most modern pigments are safe for skin contact. The dangerous elements are: **(1)** solvents, which release volatile organic compounds and should be used with ventilation; **(2)** certain pigments, particularly cadmium (Cd), cobalt (Co), and lead (Pb) compounds, which are toxic if ingested or inhaled as dust. Never sand dry oil paint containing these pigments without a respirator. Never eat while painting. Wash hands before touching food.

The good news: you can paint in oils with zero solvent by using alkyd mediums or walnut oil for thinning, and modern synthetic pigments offer alternatives to every toxic traditional color.

## Acrylic Paint

### The Versatile Upstart

Acrylic paint was invented in the mid-20th century and has rapidly become the world's most widely used painting medium. It consists of pigment suspended in an acrylic polymer emulsion — essentially liquid plastic. When water evaporates from the emulsion, the polymer particles fuse together into a continuous, flexible, water-resistant film. Drying time: minutes to hours, depending on thickness and humidity.

### Why Painters Love (and Hate) Acrylics

Acrylics are extraordinarily versatile. Thin them with water for watercolor-like washes. Apply them thick for oil-like impasto. They adhere to almost any surface. They clean up with soap and water. They do not yellow with age. They are not toxic. They layer without fat-over-lean concerns (each layer is chemically identical).

Acrylics also dry darker than they look wet (a property called "value shift" that drives many painters crazy), they dry fast (too fast for some blending techniques), and they form a plastic film that feels different from oil under the brush. The fast drying can be mitigated with retarders (glycol-based additives that slow evaporation) or by using open acrylics (formulated with slower-evaporating components). Golden Open acrylics, for example, stay workable for hours.

### Types of Acrylic Paint

- **Heavy body**: Thick, buttery consistency similar to oil paint. Holds brush and knife marks. Golden Heavy Body and Liquitex Professional are the standards.
- **Soft body**: Thinner, more flowing, but still opaque. Good for detail work and smooth applications. Golden and Liquitex both offer soft body lines.
- **Fluid acrylics**: Ink-like consistency with full pigment strength. Excellent for glazing, staining, and detail. Golden Fluid Acrylics are the benchmark.
- **High flow**: Even thinner than fluid — almost like colored water. For airbrush, pen, and dip applications. Golden High Flow is the primary option.
- **Acrylic gouache**: Matte-drying acrylic with the opacity and finish of gouache but the permanence of acrylic (water-resistant when dry). Holbein Acryla Gouache and Turner Acryl Gouache are popular. Excellent for illustration.
- **Acrylic ink**: Pigmented acrylic in dropper bottles. Liquitex Professional Acrylic Ink and Daler-Rowney FW are common. Waterproof when dry.

### Mediums and Additives

The acrylic medium ecosystem is vast. **Gel medium** (matte, gloss, semi-gloss) extends paint and adds body. **Modeling paste** creates thick, sculptural texture. **Glazing liquid** slows drying and improves flow for transparent layering. **Pouring medium** (Liquitex Pouring Medium, Floetrol, Golden GAC 800) reduces viscosity and prevents crazing in pour painting. **Retarder** extends open time. **Flow improver** reduces surface tension for smoother applications. Golden alone offers over 40 different mediums and additives.

### Brands

**Golden** (American, enormous range, technical excellence, the serious acrylic painter's first choice), **Liquitex** (the original artist acrylic, wide availability, good quality), **Amsterdam** (Royal Talens, excellent student-to-intermediate range, large tubes at reasonable prices), **Nova Color** (California-based, sold in bulk, popular with muralists).

## Watercolor

### Painting with Light and Water

Watercolor is pigment bound in gum arabic (sap from acacia trees), applied in transparent washes on white paper. The paper is the white — there is no white watercolor paint in traditional practice (Chinese White exists but is used sparingly). Light passes through the transparent color, reflects off the white paper, and returns through the color to the eye. This double passage through pigment gives watercolor its characteristic luminosity — a glow that no opaque medium can match.

### The Discipline of Transparency

Watercolor is often called the most difficult painting medium, and it earns the title. You cannot cover mistakes — every mark is permanent (or nearly so). You work from light to dark, preserving white areas by painting around them or masking them with frisket/masking fluid. A single overworked passage turns muddy and dead. The medium demands planning, decisiveness, and the ability to stop.

### Techniques

- **Flat wash**: Even, uniform color across an area. Applied with a loaded brush in overlapping horizontal strokes, working wet-into-wet, tilting the paper slightly so gravity pulls the bead of paint downward.
- **Graded wash**: Color that transitions smoothly from dark to light (or from one color to another), achieved by progressively adding more water with each stroke.
- **Variegated wash**: Multiple colors allowed to flow into each other wet-in-wet, creating unpredictable, organic transitions.
- **Wet-in-wet**: Applying paint to wet paper or wet paint. Produces soft, diffused edges. The wetter the surface, the more the paint spreads.
- **Wet-on-dry**: Applying paint to dry paper. Produces crisp, hard edges. Most detailed work uses wet-on-dry.
- **Glazing**: Applying a transparent wash over a fully dry previous wash. Colors mix optically (like stained glass layers) rather than physically.
- **Lifting**: Removing paint from wet or damp areas with a clean, thirsty brush, sponge, or tissue. Some pigments lift easily (non-staining); others bond permanently to the paper (staining).
- **Granulation**: Some pigments (particularly earth colors and cobalts) settle into the paper's texture, creating a grainy, speckled effect. Daniel Smith is famous for offering heavily granulating colors.

### Brands and Formats

**Daniel Smith** (American, enormous color range including unique mineral pigments like Primatek series ground from genuine lapis, garnet, amethyst — painter's paradise), **Winsor & Newton Professional** (the historical standard, reliable, widely available), **Schmincke Horadam** (German, luxurious handling, honey-based formula), **Sennelier** (French, honey-based, rich and vibrant).

Watercolor comes in **tubes** (moist paste, squeeze onto palette, reconstitute with water) and **pans** (dried cakes in small plastic containers, rewet with a brush). Tubes are better for large washes; pans are more portable and convenient for plein air and travel.

## Gouache

### The Opaque Cousin

Gouache (pronounced "gwash") is essentially opaque watercolor. Same gum arabic binder, but with higher pigment concentration and often the addition of a white extender (chalk/calcium carbonate) that makes it opaque. Where watercolor glows through transparency, gouache sits on the surface as a matte, velvety, opaque layer.

Gouache is the medium of choice for illustration, design, and any application where flat, even color and precise edges matter. It rewets and reworks — you can reactivate dry gouache with water and blend or correct it. This is both a strength (forgiving) and a weakness (accidental reactivation of lower layers when applying new ones). See [techniques/gouache-basics] for a full guide to gouache painting, including techniques for managing reactivation in complex layered work.

**Designers gouache** (Winsor & Newton, $5-9 per 14ml tube, Primary Set of 6 at $25-30) is formulated for maximum opacity and flatness. **Fine art gouache** (Schmincke Horadam Gouache, $7-12 per 15ml tube) emphasizes pigment quality and lightfastness. **Himi Miya jelly gouache** ($12-18 for 24 colors in stacking cups) has become enormously popular as an affordable, convenient option for beginners and for plein air painting — the TikTok-driven renaissance that has introduced hundreds of thousands of new painters to gouache.

**Holbein Acryla Gouache** ($5-8 per 20ml tube, 100+ colors) and **Turner Acryl Gouache** ($4-7 per 20ml tube, 200+ colors) are acrylic-based gouaches: they have the matte, opaque appearance of traditional gouache but dry water-resistant, allowing confident layering without reactivating lower layers. Many illustrators and animation background painters swear by these.

## Tempera

### The Renaissance Workhorse

True egg tempera uses egg yolk as its binder. You crack an egg, separate the yolk, puncture the yolk sac, let the pure yolk drain out (discarding the membrane), and mix it roughly 1:1 with pigment paste. The result is a fast-drying, luminous, matte paint that has been used since at least ancient Egypt and dominated European panel painting from the medieval period through the early Renaissance.

Tempera dries within seconds, which means you cannot blend it on the surface. Instead, tempera painters build up form through tiny, crosshatched brushstrokes — thousands of small lines layered to create gradual transitions of color and value. The result has a distinctive, slightly dry, jewel-like quality that no other medium replicates. Botticelli's *Birth of Venus* and *Primavera* are tempera paintings. Andrew Wyeth's *Christina's World* is egg tempera.

**Modern PVA tempera** (the "tempera paint" sold in schools) bears little resemblance to true egg tempera. It uses polyvinyl acetate as a binder, has low pigment quality, and is designed for children. It is not archival and is not used for serious painting.

## Encaustic

### Fire and Wax

Encaustic painting uses beeswax melted with damar resin as a binder for pigment. The wax is kept molten on a heated palette, applied with brushes or tools, and then fused (reheated to bond layers together) with a heat gun, torch, or heated iron. The result is a translucent, luminous surface with a tactile, waxy quality that no other medium possesses.

The Fayum mummy portraits (1st-3rd century CE) — the most vivid surviving paintings from the ancient world — were painted in encaustic. The medium fell out of common use for centuries but has experienced a strong revival among contemporary painters.

Encaustic requires specialized equipment and a tolerance for working with molten materials. The basic setup: a heated palette or griddle (an electric pancake griddle works for beginners), a heat gun or propane torch for fusing, natural-bristle brushes (synthetics melt in hot wax), and metal tools for scraping and incising. Ventilation is essential -- beeswax fumes are not highly toxic but can irritate the respiratory system during extended sessions.

The working process is additive and subtractive in alternation. You apply molten wax with a brush, let it cool, then fuse it with a heat gun (holding the gun six to eight inches from the surface, moving steadily to avoid scorching). Each layer bonds to the layer below through fusing. Between fused layers, you can scrape back to reveal earlier colors, incise lines with heated tools, embed collage elements (paper, fabric, found objects), or polish the surface with a soft cloth for a luminous sheen.

Encaustic medium (purified beeswax mixed with damar resin, typically at a 4:1 to 8:1 ratio) is available pre-mixed from R&F Handmade Paints (the leading encaustic paint manufacturer, based in Kingston, NY) and Enkaustikos. R&F also produces a full range of pigmented encaustic paints -- over 80 colors -- that are the standard for serious encaustic painters. For beginners, their starter set of 10 colors plus medium is a reasonable entry point at around $80-100.

The medium rewards experimentation and has a strong tactile, almost sculptural dimension. Encaustic surfaces have a physical depth that flat media cannot replicate -- light penetrates the translucent wax layers and bounces back through them, creating a glow that is unique to the medium.

## Ink

### The Line and the Wash

Ink painting spans a vast range of practices. In the West, **India ink** (carbon black particles suspended in water with shellac binder) is the workhorse — waterproof when dry, intensely black, used with dip pens, brushes, and technical pens. **Sumi ink** (Japanese) and Chinese stick ink are carbon-based inks ground from inksticks on inkstones with water, central to the sumi-e and Chinese brush painting traditions that have developed over millennia.

East Asian ink painting (*sumi-e* in Japanese, *shui-mo hua* in Chinese) is a complete painting tradition built around a single pigment: black ink in varying dilutions. The ink is applied with soft, responsive brushes on absorbent paper or silk, producing marks that range from dense black to pale, translucent gray. The tradition emphasizes calligraphic brushwork — each stroke is made with deliberate, confident gesture, and the quality of the brush's movement is as important as what it depicts.

**Alcohol inks** (Ranger Tim Holtz, Copic, Jacquard Pinata) are a different beast entirely — pigment or dye in an alcohol solvent, used on non-porous surfaces like yupo paper, tile, glass, or resin. They flow, bloom, and interact in unpredictable, organic ways. Alcohol ink painting has a strong hobbyist following and produces vivid, fluid abstract effects.

## Spray Paint and Airbrush

### Painting with Air

**Spray paint** (aerosol cans) is pigment in a solvent propelled by compressed gas. The graffiti and street art traditions have elevated spray painting to a legitimate fine art practice. Premium spray paint brands — **Montana** (Gold and Black lines), **Molotow** (Premium and One4All), **Ironlak** — offer wide color ranges, low pressure for fine control, interchangeable caps for different spray patterns, and archival pigments.

**Airbrush** painting uses a small, precision spray gun connected to an air compressor. Paint (thinned to milk-like consistency) is atomized by the air stream and deposited in fine, controllable layers. The result is smooth gradients, precise detail, and a photographic smoothness that no brush can replicate.

Airbrush equipment:
- **Gravity feed** (paint cup on top): most common for fine art, uses less paint, easier to clean
- **Siphon feed** (paint bottle below): holds more paint, better for large areas and high-volume work
- **Single action**: pressing the trigger releases air and paint simultaneously at a fixed ratio. Simpler, less control.
- **Dual action**: pressing down releases air; pulling back releases paint. Independent control of air and paint flow. This is what you want for serious work.
- **Needle sizes**: 0.2mm (fine detail), 0.3mm (general purpose), 0.5mm (coverage and broad work)
- **Brands**: Iwata (Japanese, the professional standard — Eclipse and Revolution series), Badger (American, workhorse), Harder & Steenbeck (German, precision engineering)
- **Compressors**: 20-40 PSI is the typical working range. A quiet compressor with a tank and moisture trap is essential.

**The fursuit connection is direct and literal.** Fursuit makers use airbrushes — typically Iwata Eclipse or Badger Patriot models — to apply color gradients, markings, shading, and detail work to fursuit heads, bodies, and tails. The fur is masked with tape and plastic, and thinned acrylic or textile paint is airbrushed in layers exactly as a fine artist would airbrush an illustration. Techniques cross over completely: masking, frisket film, stencils, freehand gradients, color mixing. The FFA project in this research series covers fursuit construction in detail; here we note that airbrush technique for fursuits is the same discipline as airbrush illustration, using the same tools, the same physics of atomized paint, and the same eye for color and form.

## Digital Paint

### Pixels as Pigment

Digital painting uses software to simulate traditional painting on a screen, typically using a pressure-sensitive stylus and tablet. See [techniques/digital-painting-basics] for a complete guide to hardware, software, and the transition from traditional to digital painting. The major tools:

- **Procreate** (iPad): Intuitive, powerful, enormous brush library, the most popular digital painting app. $12.99 one-time purchase. The furry art community standard — estimated 80%+ of digital furry commissions are created in Procreate.
- **Adobe Photoshop** (desktop): The original digital painting tool, industry standard for over 30 years. $22.99/month (Photography plan). Kyle T. Webster's brush packs (included free) are the gold standard.
- **Clip Studio Paint** (desktop and mobile): Favored by comic and manga artists, excellent brush engine. $49.99 one-time (Pro) or $219.99 (EX).
- **Krita** (desktop): Free, open-source, specifically designed for digital painting. Excellent brush engine, active development community. The best option for budget-conscious painters on desktop.

Digital brushes simulate traditional media by responding to stylus pressure, tilt, and speed. A watercolor brush in Procreate simulates wet-edge behavior, pigment spread, and paper texture. An oil brush simulates paint mixing and bristle texture. The simulations have become remarkably sophisticated, but they remain simulations — they model the visual appearance of traditional media, not the physics.

Here is the crucial insight: **understanding traditional painting makes digital painting better.** Knowing how watercolor granulates teaches you which digital brush settings to use and why. Knowing how oil paint layers transparently over opaque underpainting teaches you digital layering strategies. Knowing color theory and value structure translates directly. Digital tools give you unlimited undo, infinite canvas, and no material cost — but the knowledge of how paint works on a surface, in the physical world, is what gives digital painting depth and authenticity.

## Pigment Science: What Color Actually Is

### The Numbers Behind the Names

Every professional-grade paint tube carries a **pigment index number** — a standardized code that tells you exactly what chemical compound provides the color. Examples:

- **PY42** (Pigment Yellow 42): Synthetic iron oxide. Yellow ochre.
- **PB29** (Pigment Blue 29): Synthetic ultramarine. The artificial version of ground lapis lazuli.
- **PR101** (Pigment Red 101): Synthetic iron oxide. Mars red, Venetian red, English red — all the same pigment, different brands.
- **PW6** (Pigment White 6): Titanium dioxide. The universal modern white.
- **PBk7** (Pigment Black 7): Carbon black. The most common black pigment.

These numbers matter because paint names are not standardized. One brand's "Sap Green" might be PG36+PY110, another's might be PG7+PY42. The pigment index number tells you what you are actually buying. Single-pigment paints (one pigment index number) are generally preferred for mixing because they produce cleaner, more predictable results. Multi-pigment paints can be convenient but tend to produce muddier mixes.

### Lightfastness

**Lightfastness** is a pigment's resistance to fading when exposed to light. The ASTM (American Society for Testing and Materials) rates lightfastness on a scale:

- **ASTM I**: Excellent lightfastness (will not fade perceptibly in 100+ years of gallery exposure)
- **ASTM II**: Very good lightfastness (slight fading possible over very long periods)
- **ASTM III**: Fair (will fade noticeably — not recommended for permanent work)
- **ASTM IV-V**: Poor to very poor (fugitive — will fade significantly)

Professional painters generally use only ASTM I and II pigments. Some historically beloved colors are fugitive — Alizarin Crimson (PR83, ASTM III) fades over decades. Its modern replacement, Quinacridone Red or Permanent Alizarin Crimson (PR206 or PV19), offers similar hue with ASTM I-II lightfastness.

### Opacity and Transparency

Pigments are inherently opaque, semi-opaque, semi-transparent, or transparent. This is a physical property of the pigment particles and their refractive index relative to the binder. Transparent pigments (like Phthalo Blue PB15, Quinacridone Rose PV19) are ideal for glazing — they allow light to pass through and reflect off lower layers. Opaque pigments (like Cadmium Yellow PY37, Titanium White PW6) cover what is beneath them. Understanding which pigments are transparent and which are opaque is essential for effective layering in any medium.

### Tinting Strength

Some pigments are powerful tinters — a tiny amount shifts a mixture dramatically. Phthalo Blue (PB15) and Phthalo Green (PG7) are notorious for overwhelming mixes. Earth colors (ochres, umbers, siennas) are low in tinting strength. Knowing your pigments' tinting strengths prevents waste and frustration.

### Safety

Most modern pigments are safe with basic hygiene (wash hands, do not eat while painting, do not sand without a mask). The primary exceptions:

- **Cadmium compounds** (PY37, PR108, PO20): Toxic if ingested or inhaled as dust. Do not spray or sand. Do not put brushes in mouth.
- **Cobalt compounds** (PB28, PB36, PG50, PV14): Similar precautions to cadmium.
- **Lead white** (PW1): Traditional oil painting white, still used by some painters. Highly toxic. Handle with care, do not sand.
- **Chrome pigments**: Hexavalent chromium compounds are carcinogenic. Modern formulations are less hazardous.

Modern synthetic alternatives exist for every toxic pigment. Cadmium-free alternatives from brands like Gamblin, Winsor & Newton, and Schmincke offer similar hue and handling without the heavy metal concerns. Whether to use traditional cadmium and cobalt pigments or their safer substitutes is a personal choice informed by working habits and risk tolerance.

## Choosing Your Medium

There is no best painting medium. There is only the medium that serves what you want to make. Oil if you want slow, rich, deep color and do not mind solvents and long drying times. Acrylic if you want versatility, fast layering, and easy cleanup. Watercolor if you want luminous transparency and embrace the discipline of working light-to-dark with no forgiveness. Gouache if you want matte, opaque, illustration-ready color. Tempera if you want the Renaissance glow and have patience for crosshatching. Ink if the line is your primary language. Airbrush if you need smooth gradients and photographic precision. Digital if you want infinite undo and zero material cost.

Most painters eventually work in more than one medium. Understanding them all -- even the ones you do not use regularly -- makes you a better painter in the ones you do, because you understand what pigment and binder actually do, and you can choose your tools with informed intent rather than habit.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [materials-making]: Every paint medium is a material system — pigment plus binder plus solvent. Understanding why oil paint behaves differently from acrylic or watercolor is materials science applied to art. The art department's materials-making concept connects the physical chemistry of paint to the creative decisions painters make.
- **Science** — [chemistry]: Paint chemistry is real chemistry: oxidative polymerization in oil paint, polymer emulsion in acrylic, crystallization of gum arabic in watercolor. Understanding these reactions explains drying times, film flexibility, yellowing, cracking, and every other material behavior a painter encounters.
- **Materials** — [material-properties]: Each medium has measurable properties — viscosity, opacity, drying time, lightfastness, flexibility, adhesion. These properties determine what each medium can and cannot do, and they are the basis for choosing the right medium for a given purpose.
