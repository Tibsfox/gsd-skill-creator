# Try Session: Paint an Apple in One Hour

## The Classic First Painting Exercise

Research module PNT-TS-01: a guided, timed painting session that teaches value structure, edge control, and simplification through the most traditional subject in painting education. Any medium: oil, acrylic, or gouache.

**Cross-references:** [PNT-01] History, [PNT-02] Mediums, [PNT-03] Brushes & Tools, [PNT-05] Color Theory, [PNT-07] Learning Path.

**College of Knowledge:** Art Department — [seeing-drawing], [color-composition].

## Why an Apple?

Every painting school in the world starts with an apple. Not because apples are interesting (though they are, once you really look), but because an apple contains every problem a painter will ever face, compressed into a single object small enough to finish in one sitting.

An apple has a simple silhouette — you cannot hide behind complex outlines. It has a clear light side and shadow side — you must understand value. It has a highlight where the light source reflects directly — you must control your brightest marks. It has reflected light in the shadow where light bounces off the surface below — you must resist the temptation to make it too bright. It has a cast shadow — you must see the shape that light's absence makes on the surface behind the object. It has color variation across its surface — reds blending to green, yellow fading to russet. It has a stem that creates a small dark accent. And it has edges: hard where the apple's contour meets bright light, soft where the form turns gradually into shadow.

One object. Every fundamental principle.

This session is designed to be completed in sixty minutes. Not approximately sixty minutes. Exactly sixty. You will use a timer. The time constraint is not an inconvenience — it is the teacher. When you have unlimited time, you fiddle. You overwork. You add detail that destroys the freshness of your block-in. The timer forces you to make decisions and commit to them, which is the single most important habit a painter can develop.

## Setup

### What You Need

**Medium** (choose one):
- **Oil paint** — the most forgiving for beginners because of its slow drying time. You can push it around for the full hour.
- **Acrylic paint** — dries faster, so you will need to work more decisively. Use a stay-wet palette or add retarder medium.
- **Gouache** — opaque watercolor. Dries fast but can be reactivated with water. Excellent for learning value because it dries slightly lighter than it looks wet.

**Colors** (limited palette):
- Titanium white
- Cadmium red (or cadmium red hue)
- Yellow ochre
- Ivory black

This four-color setup is close to the **Zorn palette** (see the variant section below). Limiting your colors forces you to focus on value rather than chasing color accuracy. You can mix a surprising range of warm tones from these four pigments.

**Brushes:**
- One medium flat (size 6-8 for oil/acrylic, or 1/2" flat for gouache)
- One small round (size 2-4) for edge refinement
- One large flat or filbert (size 10-12) for the initial block-in

**Surface:**
- Canvas panel (8x10 or 9x12 inches) for oil or acrylic
- Heavy watercolor paper or mixed-media paper for gouache
- Toned surface preferred — a mid-value gray or warm neutral eliminates the intimidation of white and gives you a middle value for free. For canvas, pre-tone with a thin wash of yellow ochre + a touch of black, thinned with solvent or water, and let it dry before the session.

**The Subject:**
- One real apple. Not a photograph. Real.
- Place it on a white or off-white cloth (a kitchen towel works fine).
- Single light source from the upper left. A desk lamp or clamp light positioned about two feet away, angled down at roughly 45 degrees. Turn off overhead room lights if possible — you want one clear light direction creating one clear shadow pattern.

**Timer:**
- Your phone, a kitchen timer, anything that counts down. You will set it five times during this session.

## The Session: 60 Minutes, 6 Steps

### Step 1 — Thumbnail Value Sketch (5 minutes)

Set your timer for **5 minutes**.

Before you touch your painting surface, make a thumbnail sketch on a scrap of paper. This sketch is tiny — two inches tall at most — and uses only three values: **light** (the paper), **middle** (a medium pencil tone), and **dark** (heavy pencil pressure or a marker).

Squint at the apple setup. Squinting collapses the subtle value variations into broad shapes. You should see three big zones:

1. **Light shape** — the lit side of the apple and the bright surface of the cloth
2. **Middle shape** — the turning form of the apple between light and shadow, and any middletone areas of the cloth
3. **Dark shape** — the shadow side of the apple and the cast shadow on the cloth

Draw these three shapes as a simple map. No detail. No outline of the apple. Just the shapes that light and dark make. This thumbnail is your plan. It tells you where every value goes before you start painting.

If you cannot see three clear values when you squint, adjust your light source until you can. A weak light or multiple light sources destroy the value structure. You need one strong light making one clear shadow.

**What you are learning:** Composition is value arrangement. Before color, before detail, before texture — the painting must work as a pattern of light, middle, and dark shapes. This thumbnail takes five minutes but prevents hours of frustration.

### Step 2 — Draw the Shape (10 minutes)

Set your timer for **10 minutes**.

Using a thin mix of your darkest color (ivory black thinned with solvent for oil, thinned with water for acrylic or gouache), draw the apple's silhouette on your painting surface. Not the details — no stem, no highlight, no color variation. Just the outside edge of the apple and the shape of its cast shadow.

Draw from the shoulder, not the wrist. Stand if you can (or sit far enough back that you are using your whole arm). Hold the brush near the end of the handle, not near the ferrule. You are drawing big shapes, not writing your name.

Check the proportions. Is the apple wider than it is tall? (Usually yes, slightly.) Is the cast shadow the right length relative to the apple? Where does the shadow touch the apple's base?

Keep the lines thin and light. This drawing is scaffolding — it will be painted over completely. If something is wrong, wipe it off with a rag and redraw. You have ten minutes. Use them for accuracy, not detail.

**What you are learning:** Drawing is measuring. The ability to see proportions accurately — how wide relative to how tall, where one shape sits relative to another — is the foundation of representational painting. No amount of color skill saves a painting with wrong proportions.

### Step 3 — Block In Shadows (15 minutes)

Set your timer for **15 minutes**.

Mix your shadow color. For this limited palette: ivory black + cadmium red + a touch of yellow ochre. You want a dark, warm brown — not pure black, which looks dead. The exact mix does not matter as long as it is clearly darker than the middle value of your toned surface.

Now paint every shadow shape as a flat, single-value mass. The shadow side of the apple. The cast shadow on the cloth. Any dark accents (the stem hole, the area under the apple where it sits on the cloth). Do not try to vary the value within the shadows. Do not add reflected light yet. Just fill in the dark shapes from your thumbnail.

Use your medium flat brush. Load it generously — you want smooth, confident strokes, not scrubbing. Follow the form of the apple with your brushstrokes (curving around the spherical form) rather than painting randomly.

When you are done, squint at your painting and compare it to the subject. You should see a clear separation between the light areas (still the bare toned canvas) and the dark areas (your shadow block-in). If the separation is not clear, your shadow mix is not dark enough. Darken it and repaint. Timid darks are the most common beginner mistake. Your darkest dark should look shockingly dark on the palette — it will look right on the canvas.

**What you are learning:** Value is the skeleton of painting. A painting with correct values and wrong colors will read. A painting with beautiful colors and wrong values will not. By blocking in the shadows first, you establish the structure that everything else hangs on.

### Step 4 — Block In Midtones (15 minutes)

Set your timer for **15 minutes**.

Now mix your midtone — the color of the apple's lit side. For a red apple: cadmium red + yellow ochre + a small amount of white. For a green apple: yellow ochre + ivory black + white (this makes an olive green — surprisingly convincing). Adjust the value so it is clearly lighter than your shadow block-in but not as light as white.

Paint the lit side of the apple with this midtone mix, again as a fairly flat mass. You are not yet adding highlights or subtle color shifts. You are filling in the second big value shape from your thumbnail.

Where the lit side meets the shadow side, you will encounter the **terminator** — the line where light transitions to shadow. On a sphere (which an apple approximates), the terminator is a curved line running roughly vertically. Pay attention to it. This is where the form turns, and it is the most important edge in the painting.

Also block in the lit areas of the cloth using white + yellow ochre + a trace of the apple's color (light bounces off the apple onto the cloth, tinting it slightly). Keep it lighter than the apple's lit side.

Now squint again. You should see three clear value zones matching your thumbnail: light cloth, middle-value lit apple, dark shadows. If the relationships are right, the apple will already look three-dimensional despite having almost no detail.

**What you are learning:** Color is relative. The midtone only looks right because of its relationship to the shadow below it and the highlight above it. Change one value and you change the perception of all the others. This is simultaneous contrast — the foundation of all color perception.

### Step 5 — Add Highlight and Reflected Light (10 minutes)

Set your timer for **10 minutes**.

The highlight is the brightest point on the apple — the spot where the light source reflects directly toward your eye. On a shiny apple under a single light, this is a small, bright shape (nearly pure white with a slight warm tint). Place it precisely. Its position tells the viewer where the light is coming from. A misplaced highlight makes the entire lighting feel wrong.

Mix your highlight: titanium white + a tiny touch of yellow ochre (to keep it from looking chalky). Apply it with a small brush. Do not blend it out — let it sit on the surface as a distinct, bright mark. The highlight on a shiny apple has a hard edge. On a matte apple, it would be softer and more diffuse.

Now the reflected light. This is the subtle brightening on the shadow side of the apple, near the bottom, where light bouncing off the cloth illuminates the form from below. This is the most commonly overstated element in beginner painting. Reflected light is real, and seeing it proves you are observing accurately. But it must remain darker than anything on the lit side of the apple. The moment reflected light becomes as bright as the midtone, the illusion of a single light source collapses.

Mix reflected light: your shadow color + a small amount of white and the cloth color. Apply it gently into the lower shadow area of the apple. It should be a subtle warming and lightening within the shadow mass, not a second light source.

**What you are learning:** The hierarchy of light. Highlight is brightest. Lit side is next. Reflected light is in the shadow family — always darker than the lit side. Understanding this hierarchy prevents the "glowing shadow" problem that plagues beginners.

### Step 6 — Refine Edges (5 minutes)

Set your timer for **5 minutes**.

This is the step that separates a painting from a coloring exercise. Edges are how the eye reads form, and controlling them is one of the most powerful tools in painting.

Look at your apple. Where the contour of the apple meets the bright light background, the edge should be relatively **hard** — a crisp, clean transition. This is where the greatest value contrast occurs, and hard edges draw the eye.

Where the shadow side of the apple blends into the cast shadow or the dark cloth beneath it, the edge should be **soft** — a gradual, blurred transition. The eye slides past soft edges, creating the illusion of air and atmosphere around the form.

Where the form turns from light to shadow (the terminator), the edge should be **firm but not hard** — you can see the boundary, but it is not knife-sharp. This is the edge that describes the curvature of the form.

Use a clean, dry brush to soften edges that are too hard (lightly drag across the boundary). Use your small round brush to sharpen edges that are too soft (restate the boundary with a clean stroke of the adjacent color).

Five minutes. Do not overwork. Stop when the timer sounds.

**What you are learning:** Edge control is the difference between a painting that feels photographic (all hard edges) and a painting that feels alive (varied edges). Your eye naturally focuses on hard edges and relaxes past soft ones. A painting with three well-placed hard edges and everything else soft will feel more real than one with every edge equally defined.

## The Zorn Palette Version

Anders Zorn (1860-1920), the Swedish painter famous for luminous flesh tones and sparkling light effects, worked with a palette of just four colors: **yellow ochre, cadmium red, ivory black, and titanium white**. This is exactly the palette you already used in this session.

The power of the Zorn palette is that ivory black is not a neutral gray — it has a blue undertone. When mixed with white, it produces a range of cool blue-grays. When mixed with yellow ochre, it produces greens. The full Zorn palette can produce warm reds, cool blues, muted greens, rich browns, and a complete value range from white to black. It cannot produce vivid purples or intense greens, but it handles most natural subjects convincingly.

Try this apple exercise again using the Zorn palette consciously: notice how black + white gives you the cool notes for reflected light and shadow, while red + ochre + white gives you the warm tones for the lit apple. You are painting with temperature as much as value.

## What You Learned

If you followed these six steps with honest observation, you learned more in one hour than most beginners learn in weeks of unsupervised practice. Specifically:

**Value is everything.** The painting worked (or didn't) based on the relationships between your three main value zones. Color was secondary. If the values are right, the painting reads across a room, even at thumbnail size.

**Edges matter.** A painting with uniform edges feels flat and mechanical. Varied edges — hard where the eye should focus, soft where it should rest — create the illusion of light, air, and three-dimensional form.

**Less is more.** You used four colors. You painted for sixty minutes. The result has more presence than a painting fussed over for six hours with twenty colors. The constraint was your ally, not your enemy.

**The timer is the teacher.** Time pressure forced you to simplify, to commit, to resist overworking. These are the habits of effective painting at any level.

## After the Session

Do not throw this painting away. Date it and put it somewhere safe. In six months, paint another apple the same way. Compare the two. The improvement will be visible and encouraging.

If the painting failed — and it may have, this is the first one — diagnose the failure honestly. Was the shadow too light? Were the values too close together? Did you overwork one area? Each failure tells you exactly what to practice next.

If you want to go deeper, try the [30-Day Value Study Challenge](value-study-challenge.md) — it trains the value-seeing skills that make everything else in painting possible.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [seeing-drawing]: This entire session is an exercise in learning to see. You are not painting an apple — you are painting the light falling on an apple. The seeing-drawing concept teaches that drawing (and painting) is fundamentally an act of observation, not hand skill. The thumbnail sketch, the squinting, the value comparison — all of these train your eye before they train your hand.
- **Art** — [color-composition]: The Zorn palette and the limited color approach connect directly to color-composition principles. By restricting your palette, you learn how color relationships create harmony. The value structure of your three-zone plan is a compositional decision — you are arranging abstract shapes of light and dark before you are painting a recognizable object.
- **Art** — [creative-process]: The timed structure of this session models the creative process itself — plan (thumbnail), execute (block-in), refine (edges). Each stage requires a different mode of thinking: analytical for planning, bold for execution, subtle for refinement.
- **Art** — [materials-making]: Your choice of medium (oil, acrylic, gouache) and your understanding of how each behaves on the surface connects to the physical reality of art materials. The fat-over-lean principle in oil, the drying shift in gouache, the rapid set of acrylic — all are material properties that shape your decisions.
