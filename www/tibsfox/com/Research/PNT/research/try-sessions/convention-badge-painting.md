# Try Session: Paint a Furry Convention Badge

## The Art Form That Launched a Thousand Careers

Research module PNT-TS-04: a guided session that walks you through creating a hand-painted furry convention badge from sketch to lamination. The entry point to the furry art economy, the most social form of painting, and a genuinely useful craft skill. Materials cost under $20. Time: 45 to 90 minutes per badge.

## Why Convention Badges?

Every subculture has a gateway art form — the first creative act that draws people in and gives them a reason to pick up tools and make something. In the furry fandom, that gateway is the convention badge. A badge is a 2.5 x 3.5 inch painting of your character (your fursona) that you wear at conventions, and it serves a purpose beyond decoration: it identifies you. In a fandom where people go by character names and many attendees wear fursuits, the badge is how you are known. It displays your name, your character, and — if the artist is good — your personality.

Convention badge painting is where a remarkable number of professional furry artists started. The format is small enough to be non-intimidating, the audience is immediate and enthusiastic, the feedback loop is instant (you paint a badge, the commissioner squeals with delight, you feel the satisfaction of making someone's day), and the economics are accessible. A badge sells for $20 to $50. The materials cost less than $2 per badge. The time investment is 30 to 90 minutes. For a beginning artist, painting badges at a convention is the first experience of being paid for art, and that experience changes everything.

This session teaches the complete process from blank paper to laminated badge. You do not need to be at a convention to do it. Paint a badge of your own character, your pet, your friend's character, or a character you invent for practice. The skills transfer to any small-format illustration: artist trading cards, gift tags, miniature portraits, bookmarks, pet portraits.

## What You Need

### Paper

**2.5 x 3.5 inch watercolor paper.** This is Artist Trading Card (ATC) size, also called ACEO (Art Cards, Editions and Originals). You can buy pre-cut ATC blanks, or cut them from a larger sheet.

Recommended paper:
- **Strathmore 400 Series watercolor paper** ($8 for a 12-sheet pad of 5x7 — cut each sheet into 2 badges plus a test strip). Hot press for smooth lines, cold press for more texture.
- **Canson XL watercolor paper** ($6 for a pad) — good budget option. Cut to size.
- **Bee Paper hot press watercolor** — smooth surface ideal for fine detail.

The paper should be at least 140lb (300gsm) weight. Thinner paper buckles when wet and does not survive lamination gracefully.

Cut your blanks precisely. Use a ruler and a craft knife on a cutting mat. Precise edges matter because the badge will be laminated, and uneven edges look sloppy under a lamination pouch.

### Drawing Tools

**Pencil:** A mechanical pencil (0.5mm, HB lead) for clean, erasable sketching. Avoid soft pencils — graphite smears under wet media.

**Eraser:** A kneaded eraser (lifts graphite gently without damaging paper surface) or a white vinyl eraser (for clean removal).

### Inking

**Fine liner pen:** Micron 01 (0.25mm) for outlines. Micron 005 (0.20mm) for fine detail (fur texture, eye detail). Micron 05 (0.45mm) for bold outlines and name text. Micron pens use archival pigment ink that is waterproof when dry — essential if you are painting over the lines with watercolor.

Let the ink dry completely before painting. At least 3 minutes. Impatience here smears your linework and ruins the badge. Set the inked badge aside and work on the next sketch while the ink dries.

### Color

Choose one color medium:

**Watercolor** (recommended for beginners): Transparent, forgiving of mistakes (you can lift color while wet), and produces luminous results at badge scale. A basic set of 12 pan watercolors ($10-15) covers all common character colors. Supplemented with a size 2 and size 4 round watercolor brush (Princeton Heritage or similar).

**Copic markers** ($4-8 each): The industry standard for furry badge art. Alcohol-based, blendable, and fast. The refillable system means long-term costs are manageable. Start with 6-8 markers in the colors of your character. The Ciao line is the most affordable entry point. Copics blend smoothly on the right paper — hot press watercolor paper or Copic-specific marker paper.

**Gouache** (advanced): Opaque watercolor. Allows you to paint light colors over dark, which watercolor cannot do. Excellent for fur highlights and white markings. Holbein Acryla Gouache or Winsor & Newton Designers Gouache in small tubes.

### Finishing

**White gel pen** (Uni-ball Signo or Sakura Gelly Roll): For highlights in eyes, nose shine, whisker dots, and sparkle effects. Applied last, over dry color.

**White gouache** (Winsor & Newton Permanent White): For stronger highlights than a gel pen can produce. Applied with a small brush.

**Lamination:** A laminator ($25-40, available at office supply stores) and lamination pouches (2.5x3.5 inch badge size, or 5mil ID card size pouches). Alternatively, an iron on low heat with a 5mil lamination pouch placed between two sheets of parchment paper — press slowly and evenly. A third option: self-adhesive lamination pouches that require no heat.

**Badge clip or lanyard:** A pin-back badge holder, a bulldog clip with a lanyard ribbon, or a retractable badge reel. Convention attendees wear badges at chest height, visible to people they are talking to.

## The Process

### Step 1 — Pencil Sketch (10 minutes)

On your 2.5 x 3.5 inch blank, sketch the character in pencil. The standard badge composition is:

**Head and shoulders, three-quarter view.** The character faces slightly to one side (not straight on, not full profile). Three-quarter view shows both the front structure of the face and the curve of the muzzle or beak, which gives the most information about the character's species and personality. The shoulders anchor the composition at the bottom and give the character physical presence.

Fill the frame. The head should occupy the upper two-thirds of the badge. Shoulders fill the lower third. Leave a small margin at the bottom for the character name.

Common mistakes at this stage:
- **Drawing too small** — the head floats in the center with empty space around it. Fill the frame. The character should touch or nearly touch all four edges.
- **Centering the face** — a perfectly centered face is static and dull. Shift the head slightly off-center for a more dynamic composition. Let the character's gaze lead toward the larger space.
- **Including too much body** — a badge is not a full-body illustration. Head and shoulders only. If you include arms, they should be cropped by the badge edge.

Keep the pencil lines light. You will ink over them and then erase the pencil — heavy graphite is harder to erase cleanly.

### Step 2 — Ink Outlines (10 minutes)

Using your Micron pens, ink the outlines of the character. Work from the inside out:

1. **Eyes first** — the focal point. Use the finest pen (005) for iris detail and eyelashes. Use the 01 for the eye outline.
2. **Facial features** — nose, mouth, ears, inner ear detail, brow lines.
3. **Head outline and hair/fur contour** — use the 05 for the bold outer edge. This thick outline separates the character from the background and makes the badge readable from a few feet away (important in a crowded convention hall).
4. **Shoulders and clothing** — simple shapes. Do not over-detail the clothing; the focus is the face.
5. **Fur texture** — optional, and use sparingly. Short parallel lines at the edges of the cheeks, the top of the head, the neck ruff. These suggest fur without filling every surface with texture marks.

Let the ink dry completely. Set a timer for 3 minutes. Do not touch it. Go get water for your paints.

When the ink is dry, gently erase all visible pencil lines with a kneaded eraser. Pat and lift — do not rub. Rubbing can smear remaining graphite or pull up paper fibers.

### Step 3 — Flat Color Washes (15 minutes)

Apply base colors to every area of the character. The goal is to fill each region with a single, flat tone — no shading yet. This establishes the character's color scheme.

**For watercolor:** Load the brush with clean, medium-saturation color. Apply in smooth, even washes within the ink outlines. Work one section at a time. Do not let adjacent wet washes touch each other — one will bleed into the other (unless you want that effect for fur blending). Let each section dry before painting the adjacent section. A hair dryer on low can accelerate drying.

**For Copic markers:** Apply base color in smooth, overlapping strokes. Work quickly — Copics blend into themselves while wet but create hard edges when dry. The blending window is about 10-15 seconds. For large areas (body fur), use circular motions to avoid streaking.

**For gouache:** Mix each color to a creamy consistency (not too thick, not too thin). Apply flat with a small flat brush. Gouache can be reworked while wet but dries quickly. One smooth pass is better than repeated brushing over the same area.

Standard character color areas: base fur color, secondary fur color (belly, inner ear, markings), eye color (iris), eye whites, hair color, nose/tongue, skin areas, clothing/accessory colors.

### Step 4 — Add Shadows (10 minutes)

Shadows give the character dimension. Without them, the badge looks flat. With them, the face has structure and the character has presence.

**The rule:** Shadows are one step darker and slightly cooler than the base color. If the base fur is warm orange, the shadow is a cooler, darker orange-brown. If the base is white, the shadow is cool gray or pale blue-gray. Never use black for shadows — black deadens color. Use the dark version of whatever the base color is.

**Where to shadow:**
- Under the hair/bangs, casting shadow on the forehead
- The side of the muzzle opposite the light source
- Under the chin, on the neck
- Inside the ears (deeper tone)
- Under the jaw where it meets the neck
- The clothing areas that face away from the light

**For watercolor:** Apply a second, darker wash over the base. The base must be completely dry first. The transparent layering creates luminous shadows.

**For Copics:** Use a marker one or two numbers darker in the same color family. Apply to shadow areas and blend the edge into the base color with the lighter marker.

**For gouache:** Mix a darker version of the base color and apply directly. The opacity of gouache means you can paint shadows as opaque shapes.

### Step 5 — Add Highlights (5 minutes)

Highlights bring the character to life. They are the final layer, applied on top of everything else.

**White gel pen highlights:**
- One bright dot in each eye (the "life spark" — without it, the eyes look dead)
- A small highlight on the nose
- A highlight on the lower lip if visible
- Optional: small dots along the edge of the ear, suggesting light catching the ear fluff

**White gouache highlights (for stronger effects):**
- A streak of light on the hair
- Light-catching edges on fur tufts
- Bright whites of the eyes if watercolor made them too dull

Use highlights sparingly. Three well-placed highlights do more than twenty scattered ones. The eye highlights are non-negotiable — every character badge needs them. Everything else is judgment.

### Step 6 — Add Name and Laminate (10 minutes)

**The name:** At the bottom of the badge, in the margin you left, write the character name. Use a Micron 05 or 08 for legibility. Block letters are the most readable at badge scale. If your handwriting is uncertain, practice the name on scrap paper first. Some artists use a white gel pen on a dark background, or paint a small banner or nameplate area.

**Badge etiquette:** Convention badges display the character name (the name you go by in the fandom), not your legal name. Some badges also include the artist's signature (a small mark or initials in one corner). If painting for a commissioner, confirm the name spelling before you ink it.

**Lamination:** Place the finished badge in a lamination pouch. If using a laminator, run it through at the recommended temperature for your pouch thickness (usually the 5mil or 7mil setting). If using an iron, place the pouch between parchment paper sheets and press with firm, even pressure on low heat. Move the iron slowly. Check after one pass — if the edges are not sealed, press again.

Trim the lamination to leave a 2-3mm border around the badge. Round the corners with scissors for comfort (sharp laminate corners poke through clothing and scratch skin).

Attach a badge clip, pin back, or lanyard. The badge hangs at chest height, face out, character visible to anyone you are talking to.

## Convention Badge Etiquette

Convention badges are identity in the furry fandom. When someone approaches you at a convention, they read your badge to learn your name. In the headless lounge (where fursuiters remove their heads to cool down), badges are the only visible identification. A well-painted badge is a conversation starter. A badge painted by a known artist carries social weight. Collecting badges from different artists across different conventions is a common hobby.

**If selling badges at conventions:**

Painted-live badges are the tradition. You set up at an Artist Alley table, take commissions throughout the convention, and paint each badge while the commissioner watches (or wanders the con and comes back later). This live painting is part of the appeal — watching the badge take shape from blank paper to finished portrait is entertainment, and it builds trust between artist and commissioner.

**Price guide (2026):**
- Simple painted badge (head only, flat color, no background): $20-30
- Standard badge (head and shoulders, shadows and highlights, simple background): $30-45
- Premium badge (detailed, complex character design, full background): $45-75
- Specialty formats (shaped badges, holographic laminate, multi-character): $50-100+

These prices reflect the furry art market in North America as of 2026. Prices at larger conventions (Anthrocon, Midwest FurFest, Further Confusion) tend toward the higher end. Regional conventions and first-time sellers may start lower.

**Material cost per badge:** Under $2 (paper + ink + color + lamination pouch). The profit margin on convention badges is excellent, which is why they are the most accessible entry point into the professional furry art economy.

**Volume:** An experienced badge painter can complete 8-15 badges per day at a convention. At $35 average, that is $280-525 per day. A three-day convention with strong foot traffic can generate $800-1,500 in badge sales alone. This is real income for beginning artists, and for established artists, it is supplemental income on top of pre-arranged commissions and art show sales.

## Building Your Badge Portfolio

Your first ten badges will not be your best work. That is expected. The badge format teaches speed, efficiency, and the ability to paint on demand — skills that transfer to all other painting contexts. Here is a progression path:

**Badges 1-5:** Paint your own character (or a friend's, with permission). Focus on getting the process smooth: sketch, ink, color, shadow, highlight, laminate. Do not worry about speed. Work through the steps deliberately.

**Badges 6-15:** Paint from reference sheets (publicly available character references on platforms like Fur Affinity, DeviantArt, or Toyhouse — with the character owner's permission). Focus on color matching and likeness. Can you capture the character's personality in a 2.5 x 3.5 inch space?

**Badges 16-30:** Set a timer. Try to complete a badge in 45 minutes. Then 30. Speed matters at conventions because time at the table is money, and a customer who watches you paint for two hours is a customer whose friend cannot get a badge from you that day. Speed comes from practice, not from rushing.

**After 30 badges:** You are ready for a convention table. Your skills are solid, your process is efficient, your materials are dialed in. Apply for Artist Alley at a local furry convention. The experience of painting live, under pressure, for paying commissioners, will teach you more in one weekend than a semester of art school.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [materials-making]: Badge painting is materials knowledge applied to a specific, practical format. The choice of paper weight (must survive lamination), ink type (must be waterproof under wet media), color medium (must be lightfast and lamination-compatible), and finishing technique (lamination temperature, trim technique) are all material decisions with direct consequences on the finished product. This is art as craft, where material knowledge is inseparable from aesthetic quality.
- **Art** — [creative-process]: The badge painting process follows the creative arc: concept (what character, what composition), planning (pencil sketch), commitment (inking — the irreversible step), development (color, shadow, highlight), and finishing (lamination, the physical completion). Each step narrows the possibilities and demands decisions. The badge format compresses this arc into under an hour, making the entire creative process visible and learnable.
- **Learning** — [study-strategies]: Badge painting is deliberate practice with immediate feedback. Each badge teaches the same core skills (composition, color mixing, value, edge control) in a new context (different character, different color scheme, different reference complexity). The repetition with variation is exactly what learning science identifies as optimal for skill acquisition.
- **Business** — [economics]: The badge economy is a microeconomics lesson. Supply (the number of badge artists at a convention), demand (the number of attendees who want badges), pricing (based on complexity and artist reputation), and marketing (portfolio display, social media presence) all operate at a scale small enough to observe and understand. For many furry artists, badges are the first experience of market economics applied to their own work.
