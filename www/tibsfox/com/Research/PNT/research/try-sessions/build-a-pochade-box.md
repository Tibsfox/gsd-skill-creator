# DIY Project: Build a Pochade Box for Plein Air Painting

## Where Painting Meets Woodcraft

Research module PNT-TS-06: a guided build project that crosses the boundary between PNT (Painting) and WDC (Woodcraft). You will construct a compact painting station from Baltic birch plywood --- a pochade box that holds your palette, panel, and brushes inside a single box that mounts on a camera tripod. Materials cost under $50. Time: one full day for a first build.

**Cross-references:** [PNT-03] Brushes & Tools, [PNT techniques/plein-air-guide], [PNT-TS-03] Paint a Landscape, [WDC-05] Joinery (box joints), [WDC-06] Finishing (Danish oil, polyurethane), [WDC-02] Wood Science (plywood properties), [WDC-TS-02] First Dovetail Joint, [WDC-TS-03] Hand-Cut Mortise & Tenon.

## Why Build Your Own?

A pochade box is the plein air painter's essential tool. It holds a painting panel in its lid, a mixing palette in the body, and brush storage inside --- all in a compact box that clamps onto a camera tripod via a standard 1/4-20 threaded mount. It lets you stand at a comfortable working height outdoors, with everything within arm's reach, without balancing a palette on your knee and a panel against a tree stump.

Commercial pochade boxes work well. The Guerrilla Painter Thumbox, the Open Box M, the Strada Mini --- these are proven designs, well-made, thoughtfully engineered. They cost between $150 and $350. For a working plein air painter who needs to trust their gear in the field, a commercial box is a sound investment.

But building your own pochade box is a different kind of investment --- an investment in understanding. When you build the box, you understand the box. You know why the panel clip is positioned where it is (to balance the weight over the tripod mount). You know why the palette tray is removable (so you can clean it without disassembling the box). You know why the lid hinge needs to be stiff enough to hold the panel at any angle (because the wind at Deception Pass does not care about your painting). When something loosens or breaks in the field --- and it will, because you are working outdoors on rough ground in weather --- you can fix it, because you built it.

There is also the satisfaction. Setting up your tripod at a viewpoint, opening a box you built with your own hands, squeezing paint onto a palette you fitted yourself, and painting the landscape on a panel held by a clip you designed --- that is a closed loop of making. You made the tool that makes the art. The pochade box you build becomes part of the story of every painting you make with it.

This project is a genuine crossover between PNT and WDC. If you have been working through the woodcraft try sessions --- [sharpening a chisel](../../WDC/research/try-sessions/sharpen-a-chisel.md), [cutting your first joint](../../WDC/research/try-sessions/first-joint.md) --- this project puts those skills to practical use. If you are a painter with no woodworking experience, this project will teach you basic joinery, finishing, and hardware installation in the context of building something you will use every time you paint outdoors.

## Design Specifications

### Dimensions

The interior dimensions of the box are **8 inches wide by 10 inches tall** (when the lid is closed, portrait orientation). This size holds standard 8x10 painting panels, which are the most common plein air panel size --- large enough to produce a finished study, small enough to complete in a two-hour session.

- **Interior:** 8" W x 10" H x 2.5" D (body) + 1.5" D (lid)
- **Exterior** (approximate, depends on joint choice): 8.5" W x 10.5" H x 4.25" D (closed)
- **Weight** (empty): approximately 2-3 lbs, depending on wood choice and hardware

### Components

1. **Box body** --- holds the removable palette tray and brush storage
2. **Lid** --- holds the painting panel via an adjustable clip
3. **Piano hinge** --- connects lid to body, allows the lid to open past 90 degrees for a comfortable painting angle
4. **Panel clip** --- holds the 8x10 panel in the lid (binder clip on a dowel, or a thumbscrew clamp)
5. **Palette tray** --- removable insert that fits inside the box body, acts as your mixing surface
6. **Lid stay** --- a chain, cord, or folding arm that holds the lid open at the desired angle
7. **Tripod mount** --- 1/4-20 T-nut installed in the bottom of the box body
8. **Latch** --- keeps the box closed during transport (hook-and-eye, draw latch, or magnetic catch)

## Materials List (~$30 in Wood + ~$15 in Hardware)

### Wood

**Baltic birch plywood** is the ideal material for a pochade box. It is dimensionally stable (minimal expansion and contraction with humidity changes, critical for a box that lives outdoors), strong relative to its thickness, available in precise metric thicknesses, and has a clean, attractive edge grain that looks good without edge banding.

- **1/4" (6mm) Baltic birch** --- sides, top, and back of the body and lid. Two pieces approximately 12" x 18" will yield all the parts with some to spare.
- **1/2" (12mm) Baltic birch** --- bottom of the body (carries the tripod mount and must resist the cantilever force of the loaded box on the tripod). One piece approximately 9" x 11".
- **1/4" (6mm) Baltic birch** --- palette tray (one piece approximately 7.5" x 9.5").

Baltic birch plywood is sold at woodworking supply stores (Rockler, Woodcraft, and their online equivalents) and some hardwood dealers. It is typically sold in 5' x 5' sheets or partial sheets. A half-sheet or quarter-sheet is more than enough for this project. Many stores sell pre-cut project panels.

**Alternative:** If you cannot source Baltic birch, use 1/4" and 1/2" cabinet-grade plywood (maple or birch veneer). Avoid construction plywood (CDX, sheathing) --- the voids in the inner plies make it structurally unreliable at these small dimensions and produce ugly edges.

### Hardware

- **Piano hinge:** 8" long, brass or stainless steel (stainless resists outdoor corrosion better). $4-6.
- **1/4-20 T-nut:** One. This is the standard camera tripod thread. $0.50 or part of a multi-pack.
- **1/4-20 bolt:** One, 3/4" long, with a washer. For testing the tripod mount. $0.50.
- **Small brass hook-and-eye latch** or draw latch for the front closure. $2-4.
- **Lid stay:** A small brass chain (6" length), or a folding lid support, or a length of paracord with a screw eye at each end. $2-5.
- **Wood screws:** #4 x 1/2" brass or stainless, for the hinge. A small box. $3-4.
- **Spring clamp or binder clip:** Large (2") binder clip, plus a 3/8" hardwood dowel, 3" long. This becomes your panel clip. $2.
- **Wood glue:** Any PVA woodworking glue (Titebond I, II, or III). You likely already have this if you have done any WDC projects. $5 if purchasing new.

### Finish

- **Danish oil** (one coat inside and out for a natural, low-sheen finish that is easy to maintain), or
- **Marine-grade polyurethane** (three coats for maximum weather protection --- better for wet PNW conditions, but heavier and slower to apply).

The finish is functional, not decorative. The box will get paint on it, scratched by brush handles, and rained on. Choose a finish you can reapply easily.

## Construction

### Step 1 --- Cut the Parts (1-2 hours)

**Safety note:** If you are using a table saw or miter saw, review the safety guidance in [WDC-04 Power Tools](../../WDC/research/04-power-tools.md). If you are cutting by hand, a fine-tooth backsaw (Japanese pull saw or Western dovetail saw) and a straightedge will produce clean cuts in plywood.

Cut all parts to dimension before any assembly:

**Body:**
- Two sides: 1/4" x 2.5" x 10" (left and right walls)
- Top rail: 1/4" x 2.5" x 8" (top of the body, where the hinge attaches)
- Bottom rail: 1/4" x 2.5" x 8" (bottom of the body, below the palette tray)
- Back panel: 1/4" x 8" x 10" (the back of the body)
- Bottom: 1/2" x 8.5" x 10.5" (slightly oversized to allow for flush-trimming after assembly)

**Lid:**
- Two sides: 1/4" x 1.5" x 10" (left and right walls)
- Top rail: 1/4" x 1.5" x 8" (top of the lid)
- Bottom rail: 1/4" x 1.5" x 8" (bottom of the lid --- this is the hinge edge)
- Back panel: 1/4" x 8" x 10" (the back of the lid --- the panel sits against this surface)

**Palette tray:**
- Bottom: 1/4" x 7.5" x 9.5" (sized to sit inside the body with a small gap on all sides)
- Two side rails: 1/4" x 3/8" x 9.5" (keeps the palette from sliding side to side)
- One front rail: 1/4" x 3/8" x 7.5" (keeps paint from sliding off the front edge)

### Step 2 --- Joinery (2-3 hours)

You have three joinery options, from simplest to most refined:

**Option A: Butt joints with glue and pin nails.** The fastest method. Glue the side walls to the rails, pin-nail with 3/4" brad nails for clamping pressure while the glue sets, then glue and nail the back panel. This produces a strong box quickly. The joint is not visible from the outside (the back panel covers the rail ends), but it relies entirely on the glue bond for strength. For a pochade box (which experiences gentle loads), this is perfectly adequate.

**Option B: Rabbet joints.** Cut a 1/4" x 1/4" rabbet along the inside edge of each side wall, so the rails and back panel sit into the rabbet rather than butting against the flat edge. This provides more glue surface and mechanical alignment than a butt joint. A rabbet plane or a table saw with a dado blade cuts these quickly. This is the joinery used in most commercial pochade boxes.

**Option C: Box joints (finger joints).** The most refined option and a direct application of [WDC-05 Joinery](../../WDC/research/05-joinery.md). Cut interlocking fingers at the corners of the body and lid. Box joints are exceptionally strong in plywood because each finger provides cross-grain glue surface. They also look beautiful --- the layered plies of Baltic birch create a decorative pattern at each corner. A box joint jig on a table saw or a hand-cut approach with a chisel both work. This is the most time-consuming option but produces the most durable and attractive result.

For a first build, Option A or B is recommended. You can always build a second, nicer box with box joints once you know the design works for you.

**Assemble the body:** Glue and join the two side walls to the top rail and bottom rail, forming a rectangular frame. Then attach the back panel. Check for square --- measure the diagonals. If they are equal, the box is square. If not, adjust before the glue sets. Clamp and let dry.

**Assemble the lid:** Same process, smaller depth. The lid frame should be the same exterior width and height as the body, so they nest together when closed.

**Note on the palette tray:** Glue the side rails and front rail to the palette tray bottom. This creates a shallow tray that sits inside the body. Do not attach it to the body --- it must be removable for cleaning. Seal the tray surface with two coats of polyurethane to make paint cleanup easy (dried acrylic peels off a smooth poly surface; dried oil paint can be scraped off with a palette knife).

### Step 3 --- Install Hardware (1 hour)

**Piano hinge:** The hinge connects the bottom rail of the lid to the top rail of the body. When the box is closed, the lid sits on top of the body like a clamshell. When open, the lid swings back, presenting the panel to you at a comfortable angle. Mark the screw holes, pre-drill with a 1/16" bit (essential in plywood to prevent splitting), and drive the screws. Brass screws are traditional but soft --- stainless steel is more practical.

**Tripod mount:** Drill a 3/8" hole through the center of the 1/2" bottom panel, from the outside. Hammer the 1/4-20 T-nut into the hole from the inside (the prongs grip the wood and prevent the T-nut from spinning). The tripod's quick-release plate or mounting screw threads into this T-nut. Test by screwing in the 1/4-20 bolt from the outside. The bolt should thread smoothly and pull the T-nut tight against the inside of the bottom.

**Position the tripod mount carefully.** It should be centered left-to-right, and positioned slightly toward the back of the box (about 1/3 of the depth from the back). This offset counterbalances the weight of the open lid and wet painting, keeping the box from tipping forward on the tripod.

**Panel clip:** Drill a 3/8" hole through the top rail of the lid, centered. Insert the 3/8" dowel through the hole so it protrudes inside the lid. Attach the large binder clip to the top of the dowel (open the clip, slide the handle over the dowel, close). The binder clip grips the top edge of the painting panel. The panel rests against the inside back of the lid, held at the top by the clip. For panels of different sizes, slide the dowel in or out.

**Lid stay:** Attach one end of the chain or cord to the inside of the body side wall (about 1" below the hinge rail). Attach the other end to the inside of the lid side wall (about 2" from the hinge edge). When the lid opens, the chain limits the opening angle to approximately 100-110 degrees --- enough to present the panel comfortably but not so far that the lid flops backward.

**Latch:** Install the hook-and-eye (or draw latch) on the front edge, centered, so the box stays closed during transport.

### Step 4 --- Finish (1 hour active, plus drying time)

Sand all surfaces to 220 grit. Pay attention to the edges --- Baltic birch plywood has beautiful layered edge grain that benefits from careful sanding.

Apply your chosen finish:

**Danish oil:** Apply with a rag, wipe on liberally, let it soak for 15 minutes, wipe off the excess. One coat is sufficient for a working tool. Danish oil penetrates the wood, hardens slightly, and provides moderate moisture resistance without building a film on the surface. It is easy to reapply in the field if needed. See [WDC-06 Finishing](../../WDC/research/06-finishing.md) for detailed technique. If you made the [Finishing Sampler Board](../../WDC/research/try-sessions/wood-finishing-sampler.md), you already know how Danish oil behaves.

**Polyurethane:** Apply three thin coats with a brush or foam applicator, sanding lightly with 320-grit between coats. This builds a durable, water-resistant film that stands up to PNW rain. Use marine-grade or spar polyurethane for the best UV and moisture resistance. Drying time between coats: 4-6 hours for oil-based, 1-2 hours for water-based.

Do not finish the inside of the palette tray with Danish oil --- the oil will contaminate your paint mixing. Use polyurethane for the tray, or leave it raw and seal it with a coat of white gesso (which also provides a neutral mixing surface).

## The Test

Mount the box on a tripod. Load the palette tray with paint (or wet paper towels to simulate weight). Clip an 8x10 panel into the lid. Open the lid to painting angle. Tilt the tripod on uneven ground. Does the box stay stable? Does the panel clip hold? Does the palette tray stay in place?

If everything holds, take it outside and paint something. The [Paint a PNW Landscape](paint-a-landscape.md) session is ideal for the maiden voyage of a new pochade box. Set up at your favorite viewpoint, open the box, and paint.

## Comparison: DIY vs. Commercial

| Feature | DIY Pochade Box | Guerrilla Painter ($180) | Open Box M ($300) |
|---------|----------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Cost | ~$45 | ~$180 | ~$300 |
| Panel size | 8x10 | 8x10 | 8x10 to 12x16 |
| Weight | 2-3 lbs | 2 lbs | 3.5 lbs |
| Brush storage | Basic (in body) | Dedicated clip | Dedicated drawer |
| Lid stay | Chain/cord | Friction hinge | Friction hinge |
| Panel clip | Binder clip/dowel | Spring clip | Sliding clamp |
| Weather resistance | Depends on finish | Factory finish | Factory finish |
| Repairability | Total (you built it) | Limited | Limited |
| Satisfaction | Immeasurable | N/A | N/A |

The commercial boxes win on refinement --- friction hinges are smoother than chains, spring clips are more adjustable than binder clips, dedicated brush storage is more organized. But the DIY box wins on cost, repairability, customizability, and the irreplaceable satisfaction of painting outdoors with a tool you made yourself.

## Modifications and Upgrades

Once you have used your pochade box for a few plein air sessions, you will know what you want to change:

**Larger panel size.** Build a second box with 9x12 or 11x14 interior dimensions. The construction is identical; only the cut list changes.

**Integrated brush holder.** Glue a strip of elastic or a row of small spring clips inside the lid to hold brushes during transport.

**Wet panel carrier.** Build a shallow frame that clamps to the outside of the closed box, holding a wet painting face-out during transport. Two strips of wood with thumb screws, creating a channel that grips the edges of the panel.

**Quick-release palette.** Instead of a loose tray, install two small wooden ledges inside the body that the palette tray slides onto, like a drawer.

> **Try This:** If box joints interest you, practice them on scrap plywood first. The [WDC joinery chapter](../../WDC/research/05-joinery.md) covers the technique, and the [first joint session](../../WDC/research/try-sessions/first-joint.md) walks through cutting them step by step.

> **Try This:** Once your pochade box is built, take it out for the [PNW Landscape painting session](paint-a-landscape.md). See also the [Plein Air Guide](../techniques/plein-air-guide.md) for field painting technique.

> **Try This:** Build a [woodworking mallet](../../WDC/research/try-sessions/build-a-mallet.md) in the same shop session --- the mallet uses similar hardwood skills and gives you a tool you will need for the box joint joinery.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Trades** --- [woodworking]: This project is a complete woodworking exercise in miniature --- cutting, joinery, hardware installation, and finishing. The skills scale directly to larger projects. If you can build a pochade box with clean joints and a proper finish, you can build a toolbox, a storage chest, a small cabinet. The box joint option connects to the broader joinery curriculum in [WDC-05](../../WDC/research/05-joinery.md).
- **Trades** --- [tools-safety]: Power tool operations (table saw cuts, dado blade joinery) and hand tool operations (chisel work for fitting joints) both require the safety habits developed in earlier WDC sessions. The tripod mount installation introduces metalworking hardware (T-nuts, threading) in a woodworking context.
- **Art** --- [plein-air-practice]: The pochade box is the enabling tool for plein air painting. Without it (or a commercial equivalent), outdoor painting is an ergonomic compromise. With it, outdoor painting becomes a focused, comfortable practice. Building the tool yourself deepens the connection to the practice --- you understand why it works, which makes you a more adaptable painter when conditions in the field demand improvisation.
- **Art** --- [materials-making]: Choosing between Danish oil and polyurethane for the finish, understanding plywood stability versus solid wood movement, sealing the palette tray for easy cleanup --- these are all material-knowledge decisions that connect woodcraft to painting practice.
