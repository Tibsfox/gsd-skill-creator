# Try Session: Paint Your Pet -- A Portrait from Photo Reference

## The Most Popular Commission in Painting

Research module PNT-TS-05: a guided painting session that teaches portrait composition, value structure in fur and feathers, eye rendering, and working from photographic reference --- through the subject people care about most. Medium: acrylic or oil. Time: 3 to 5 hours over one or two sessions.

## Why Pet Portraits?

Every painter, at some point, gets asked the same question: "Can you paint my dog?" It does not matter whether you are an abstract expressionist, a landscape painter, a furry artist, or someone who just finished their first apple study. If people know you paint, they will ask you to paint their pet. This is not an annoyance. It is an opportunity --- arguably the most reliable and emotionally rewarding opportunity in the entire painting economy.

Pet portraits are the single most popular commission type in both fine art and furry art communities. The reason is obvious once you think about it: people love their animals with an intensity that borders on the irrational, and a painted portrait of that animal --- not a photograph, but a painting, made by a human hand, interpreting the animal's personality through color and brushwork --- is one of the most emotionally resonant gifts a person can give or receive. A photograph captures what the animal looks like. A painting captures what the animal feels like. That distinction is why people pay for paintings.

The skills required for a pet portrait are the same skills required for any portrait: seeing accurate proportions, understanding value structure, rendering texture convincingly, and --- most critically --- capturing the personality of the subject through the eyes. A pet portrait with dead eyes is a failed painting, regardless of how technically accomplished the fur rendering is. A pet portrait with lively, engaging eyes can carry a multitude of other technical shortcomings. The eyes are the painting.

This try session walks you through the complete process: choosing a reference photo, sketching the composition, blocking in values, building up fur texture, and finishing with the eyes. The subject can be any pet --- dog, cat, bird, horse, rabbit, snake, ferret, lizard. The principles are universal. The species-specific details are covered in the tips section.

## Choosing Your Reference Photo

This step is more important than any brushwork technique you will learn in this session. A bad reference photo produces a bad painting. No amount of skill compensates for a blurry, flat-lit, dead-on reference.

### What Makes a Good Reference

**Three-quarter angle.** Not straight-on (passport photo), not profile (side view). A three-quarter angle shows one full eye, the bridge of the nose, and the near side of the face, with the far side receding into a gentle turn. This angle gives the face dimension. It also creates asymmetry, which the human brain reads as natural and engaging. A straight-on photo feels confrontational. A profile feels distant. The three-quarter angle invites.

**Strong directional light.** One light source, from the side or above and to one side. The light should create a clear pattern of light and shadow across the face --- bright side and shadow side, with a defined transition between them. This light pattern is what gives the painting its sense of three-dimensional form. Flat, even lighting (from a flash or from overhead fluorescents) produces a flat, lifeless reference. If you are taking the reference photo yourself, photograph near a large window with the animal turned slightly away from the window. Natural window light is soft, directional, and warm --- ideal painting light.

**Sharp focus on the eyes.** Everything else can be slightly soft. The eyes must be sharp. If the eyes are out of focus in the reference, you will be guessing at the catchlight position, the iris color, the pupil shape, and the subtle reflections that make eyes read as alive. Guess wrong, and the painting fails. Camera phones are good enough for this if the animal is still and the light is adequate. If your pet is a maniac who will not hold still (most cats, many dogs), take fifty photos and pick the one where the eyes are sharp.

**Good resolution.** At least 2000 pixels on the longest side. You will be zooming in on details --- the texture of the iris, the direction of individual whiskers, the way fur lays over the brow ridge. A low-resolution reference forces you to invent details rather than observe them, and invented details always look invented.

**Character and personality.** The best reference photos capture the animal being itself. The head tilt. The one-ear-up one-ear-down expression. The suspicious squint. The tongue-out pant. A technically perfect photo of a bored animal makes a technically correct but emotionally empty painting. Wait for the moment when the animal's personality surfaces, and photograph that.

### What to Avoid

- Flash photography (kills the shadows, produces red-eye or green-eye, flattens everything)
- Multiple overlapping shadows (from multiple light sources or mixed artificial/natural light)
- Extreme close-ups that distort proportions (wide-angle lens inches from a nose)
- Photos where the animal is backlit (silhouetted against a bright window or sky)
- Blurry references with the hope that you will "sharpen it up in the painting" (you will not)

## Setup

### Medium

Choose **acrylic** or **oil**. Both work well for pet portraits; each has advantages.

**Acrylic** dries fast, which means you can layer quickly. It is easier to build up fur texture through successive thin layers (scumbling, dry-brushing). It cleans up with water. For a first pet portrait, acrylic is the more forgiving choice because you can paint over mistakes within minutes.

**Oil** stays wet longer, which means you can blend more smoothly --- ideal for the soft gradients of fur, the subtle transitions in the eyes, and the background. Oil produces richer darks and more luminous color. If you have experience with oil, use it. If this is your first portrait, start with acrylic.

### Colors (Limited Palette)

- Titanium white
- Burnt umber (the workhorse for pet portraits --- it mixes warm darks, cool darks with blue, and serves as the base for most fur colors)
- Yellow ochre
- Cadmium red (or cadmium red hue)
- Ultramarine blue
- Ivory black

This six-color palette can mix virtually any fur color: golden retrievers (yellow ochre + white + burnt umber), tuxedo cats (ivory black + ultramarine + white for the cool blacks), tabby stripes (burnt umber + yellow ochre), red setters (cadmium red + burnt umber + yellow ochre), gray tabbies (ultramarine + burnt umber + white). If your pet has unusual coloring --- a blue merle, a calico, a parrot --- you may want to add one or two specific pigments, but try the limited palette first. You will be surprised how much it covers.

### Surface

Canvas or canvas panel, 11x14 or 12x16 inches. Larger than the apple session because pet portraits benefit from room to work --- you need space for the fur texture. Pre-tone the canvas with a thin wash of burnt umber + yellow ochre, thinned to transparency. Let it dry completely. This warm middle-value ground eliminates the intimidation of white, gives you a base tone that reads as natural light through fur, and lets you establish darks and lights relative to a middle value instead of building up from nothing.

### Brushes

- Large flat (size 10-12) for the background and initial block-in
- Medium flat (size 6-8) for the main fur work
- Small flat or bright (size 4) for detail in the face
- Small round (size 1-2) for the eyes, whiskers, and finest details
- Old scruffy flat brush (any size) for dry-brushing fur texture

## The Process

### Step 1 --- Sketch the Proportions (30 minutes)

The envelope method is the most reliable way to get proportions right from a photo reference. Here is how it works:

1. **Draw the bounding rectangle.** Look at your reference photo and identify the outermost points of the pet's head (or head and shoulders, depending on your crop). Draw a rectangle on your canvas that represents those bounds. This rectangle is your envelope --- everything in the portrait fits inside it.

2. **Divide into quadrants.** Draw a light vertical centerline and horizontal centerline through the rectangle. These four quadrants give you a grid for placing landmarks. "The left eye sits in the upper-left quadrant, one-third of the way down and one-quarter of the way in from the centerline." This kind of relational thinking is how you transfer proportions accurately from photo to canvas.

3. **Place the landmarks.** The five critical landmarks for any animal face: the two eyes, the nose, the top of the head, and the chin (or lower jaw). Place these five points first, in their correct positions relative to the quadrant grid. Then connect them with light lines to build the outline of the face.

4. **Check proportions.** Hold a pencil at arm's length to measure the width of the head in the photo relative to the height. Transfer that ratio to your canvas. Check: is the distance between the eyes correct relative to the head width? Is the nose the right distance below the eyes? Is the ear placement right? Proportion errors are invisible when you are drawing them and screaming obvious when you step back. Step back frequently.

Use a thin mix of burnt umber for this sketch. Keep the lines light --- they will be painted over.

### Step 2 --- Block in the Background FIRST (20 minutes)

This is the most common mistake in pet portrait painting: painting the pet first, finishing it, and then trying to paint the background around it. The result is a stiff outline where the pet meets the background, visible edges that should be soft, and a background that looks like an afterthought because it was.

Paint the background first. For a pet portrait, the background should be simple --- a soft gradient, a blurred suggestion of the environment, or a flat color that complements the fur. Do not paint a detailed background that competes with the subject. The pet is the subject. Everything else supports it.

**Technique:** Load your large flat brush with a mixed background color (something that contrasts with the fur --- cool blues or greens for warm-toned animals, warm neutrals for cool-toned animals). Apply broadly, working in loose strokes. Let the background overlap slightly into where the pet will be. This overlap ensures that when you paint the pet's edges, you are painting into wet (or recently dried) background, creating soft, natural-looking transitions rather than hard cutout lines.

### Step 3 --- Dark Values First (45 minutes)

With the background in place, begin building the pet from dark to light. This is the classical approach to alla prima painting, and it works because dark values establish the form before detail distracts you.

Mix your darkest fur color (the shadow color --- burnt umber + ultramarine for warm-dark, ivory black + ultramarine for cool-dark). Using your medium flat brush, block in all the shadow areas: the underside of the jaw, the shadow side of the face, the interior of the ears, the dark markings, the area under the brow, the nostrils. Paint them as flat shapes for now, matching the value you see in the reference photo.

Squint at the reference (the same technique from the [apple session](paint-an-apple.md)). Squinting collapses the subtle variations into broad value masses. You should see two or three clear value zones: dark, middle, light. Block in the darks first. Then the middle tones. Then the lights.

**Resist the temptation to add detail at this stage.** No individual hairs. No whiskers. No eye detail. You are building the value map of the face --- the three-dimensional form created by light falling on a solid object. If you get the value map right, the portrait will read from across the room even without any detail. If you get the value map wrong, no amount of detail will save it.

### Step 4 --- Build Up Layers and Fur Texture (60-90 minutes)

This is where the painting comes alive. Working from dark to light, begin building up fur texture through layered brushwork.

**Fur direction follows muscle contours.** This is the single most important thing to understand about painting fur. Hair does not grow in random directions. It grows in patterns dictated by the underlying anatomy --- flowing along the muscles of the face, fanning out from the brow, sweeping back from the muzzle, curling inside the ears. If you paint fur strokes that follow these natural growth patterns, the fur reads as convincingly three-dimensional even if the individual strokes are rough. If you paint fur strokes in random directions, the animal looks like a stuffed toy no matter how refined your technique.

**Technique for fur:** Load a medium flat or the scruffy brush with paint that is thicker than your block-in but not impasto. Drag the brush in the direction the fur grows, using a flicking motion that starts with pressure (depositing paint at the root) and lifts at the end (tapering the stroke at the tip). Overlap strokes. Vary the color slightly --- fur is never one uniform color. A golden retriever's fur has strands of cream, gold, amber, and warm brown all intermingled. A black cat has undertones of dark blue, brown, and green depending on the light. Mix variations on the palette and alternate between them as you paint.

**Dry-brushing** is the pet portrait painter's secret weapon. Load the scruffy brush with very little paint, fan the bristles slightly, and drag across the surface with light pressure. The paint catches on the texture of the canvas (or the existing paint layer) and deposits a broken, irregular pattern that reads as fine fur texture. It is faster and more convincing than painting individual hairs. Use dry-brushing to add highlights on the top of the head, light catching the tips of fur along the ears, and the soft fuzz on the muzzle.

### Step 5 --- Species-Specific Tips

**Dogs.** Fur direction is your primary challenge. Study the reference carefully and map out the fur growth patterns before you start painting texture. The muzzle fur radiates outward from the nose. The forehead fur flows back toward the ears. The cheek fur sweeps down and back. Floppy ears have fur that hangs vertically. Erect ears have fur that radiates from the base upward. The eyes of most dogs are round, warm-toned (brown, amber, hazel), and deeply expressive. The catchlight is critical --- a small, bright spot of nearly pure white in the upper portion of the eye, usually reflecting the light source. Without the catchlight, the eyes look dead.

**Cats.** The big challenge with cats is reflected light in dark fur. A black cat is never actually black --- the fur reflects blue, green, purple, and warm brown depending on the light source and the angle of the fur relative to the viewer. Paint the dark base first, then add the reflected color with transparent glazes (thin paint with medium until it is nearly transparent, then brush it over the dry dark layer). Cat ears are translucent --- when backlit, they glow pink or warm red because of the blood vessels visible through the thin skin. Paint this translucency for realism. Cat eyes are extraordinary subjects: vertical pupils, brilliant iris colors (green, gold, copper, blue), and a slit of reflected light that cuts across the pupil. Give the eyes extra time.

**Birds.** Feather structure follows tracts --- groups of feathers that grow in specific patterns across the body. The contour feathers of the head follow the skull shape closely, overlapping like roof tiles. The wing feathers are layered in precise order (coverts over flight feathers). Paint feathers by painting the shadows between them rather than outlining each feather --- the shadow defines the overlap, and the highlight on each feather's surface gives it volume. For parrots and other brightly colored birds, be bold with saturated color. Birds earn it.

**Horses.** Anatomy knowledge is essential. The horse's face is all bone and muscle with a thin covering of short hair, so the underlying structure is visible in the painting. The cheekbone, the jaw, the brow ridge, the nostril flare --- all of these read as distinct forms under the skin. If you do not understand the anatomy, the portrait will look soft and shapeless. Study reference photos of horse skulls and facial muscles. The coat is short enough that individual hair texture is less important than the smooth gradation of value and color across the large planes of the face.

### Step 6 --- The Eyes LAST (30-45 minutes)

The eyes are the focal point of any portrait. They are the first place the viewer looks, the last place the viewer leaves, and the element that determines whether the portrait feels alive or dead. Paint them last, when the rest of the portrait is resolved, so you can give them your full attention and your steadiest hand.

**Structure of an animal eye (simplified):**
1. The white (sclera) --- usually not very visible in animals, unlike humans. Paint it if you can see it, but keep it subtle.
2. The iris --- the colored ring. Paint it as a disc of color, not a flat circle. The iris has depth and radial structure. In dogs, the iris is typically warm brown with radial striations. In cats, the iris can be vivid green, gold, or blue with visible texture.
3. The pupil --- black, but not dead black. Use ivory black + ultramarine for a deep, warm-leaning black that has life.
4. The catchlight --- the reflection of the light source. This is the spark of life. A small dot or crescent of nearly pure titanium white, placed in the upper portion of the eye. The catchlight must be placed identically in both eyes (or proportionally correct if the head is at an angle). A misplaced catchlight makes the animal look cross-eyed. An absent catchlight makes the animal look blind.

**Process:** Paint the entire eye socket shadow first. Then paint the iris over the shadow. Then the pupil over the iris. Then the catchlight over the pupil. Each layer is smaller and more precise than the last. Use your smallest round brush for the catchlight. Take your time. The difference between a good eye and a great eye is often one tiny adjustment to the catchlight position.

## The Furry Art Connection

If you have painted a pet portrait, you have already painted a furry character portrait. The skills are identical. A furry character portrait is a pet portrait with personality dialed up --- more expressive eyes, more dynamic poses, more intentional use of color and design. But the fundamentals --- fur direction, value structure, eye rendering, background integration --- are the same skills you just practiced.

Convention badge painting ([PNT-TS-04](convention-badge-painting.md)) is essentially miniature pet portraiture applied to original characters. The commission market for furry character portraits operates on the same principles as the pet portrait market: the client loves this character the way a pet owner loves their animal, and they want a painting that captures the character's personality, not just their appearance.

The crossover goes the other direction too. Furry artists who learn anatomical rendering for character work --- understanding how muscles create fur flow patterns, how light interacts with different fur textures, how eyes convey emotion --- bring those skills directly to realistic pet portraits. The two disciplines are closer than most people in either community realize.

## Selling Pet Portraits

Pet portraits are one of the most reliable income streams for painters at any skill level. The market is enormous (there are more pet owners than art collectors), the emotional investment of the buyer is high (they are not buying "art" --- they are buying a permanent tribute to a beloved family member), and the price range accommodates a wide spectrum of skill and experience.

**Pricing guidelines:**
- **Beginning painters** (less than a year of regular practice): $100-200 for an 8x10 or 11x14 acrylic portrait. At this level, you are selling sincerity and charm more than technical mastery, and that is perfectly legitimate. Many buyers prefer a slightly rough, heartfelt portrait to a slick, soulless one.
- **Intermediate painters** (1-3 years of regular practice): $200-500 for 11x14 to 16x20, acrylic or oil. At this level, you should be producing portraits with convincing value structure, readable eyes, and coherent fur texture.
- **Advanced painters** (3+ years, strong portfolio): $500-2000 for 16x20 and up, oil preferred. At this level, you are producing gallery-quality work, and your clients are paying for a genuine art object as well as a portrait.

**Where to sell:** Local pet stores (hang samples with your card), veterinary offices (same approach), farmers markets (set up with samples and take commissions on the spot), Instagram and social media (post process videos --- pet owners love watching their animal come to life on canvas), Etsy, and word of mouth. One good pet portrait sells the next one. Every client shows their portrait to friends and family, and at least one of those people owns a pet they want painted.

## After the Session

Date this painting and photograph it. If you are building a portfolio, pet portraits are some of the most engaging pieces you can show --- people respond to them immediately and emotionally.

If the painting failed, diagnose specifically. Were the proportions off? Go back to the envelope method and practice measuring. Were the values flat? Try the [Value Study Challenge](value-study-challenge.md) to train your value-seeing. Did the eyes look dead? Practice painting eyes alone --- buy cheap small canvases and paint nothing but eyes for an afternoon. Were the colors muddy? Revisit the limited palette approach and focus on keeping your mixing clean.

If the painting succeeded, paint another. Different animal, different lighting, different angle. Each portrait teaches you something the last one did not.

> **Try This:** Build a [pochade box](build-a-pochade-box.md) and try painting your pet from life rather than from a photo. The animal will not hold still, which forces you to work fast and capture the essence rather than the details. This is the hardest and most rewarding form of pet portraiture.

> **Try This:** Use the airbrush techniques from [FFA Airbrush Basics](../../FFA/research/try-sessions/airbrush-basics-for-fursuits.md) to create smooth gradients on a pet portrait --- especially effective for backgrounds and soft-focus areas.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [seeing-drawing]: The entire pet portrait process is an exercise in observation. You are not painting an animal --- you are painting the light falling on an animal. The envelope method, the squinting, the value comparison, the study of fur growth patterns --- all of these train your eye to see what is actually there rather than what you think is there. The gap between "what I think a dog looks like" and "what this specific dog actually looks like in this specific light" is where portrait painting lives.
- **Art** --- [color-composition]: The limited palette approach forces you to mix every color from a small set of pigments, which teaches color relationships at a deeper level than using pre-mixed colors. The background color choice (cool behind warm subjects, warm behind cool subjects) is a compositional decision that supports the focal hierarchy of the portrait.
- **Learning** --- [observation-skills]: Working from a photo reference is a translation exercise: converting a two-dimensional photographic image into a two-dimensional painted image, while understanding the three-dimensional form that both represent. This triangulation between reference, subject, and painting trains the kind of careful, structured observation that applies far beyond art.
- **Art** --- [materials-making]: The choice between acrylic and oil, the dry-brushing technique for fur, the glazing technique for reflected light in dark fur --- these are material-specific decisions that require understanding how paint behaves physically. Thick paint catches light differently than thin paint. Opaque paint covers; transparent paint reveals what is beneath. Every brushstroke is a material decision.
