# Try Session: Paint a PNW Landscape in 2 Hours

## Plein Air or From a Window — Any Medium, One Afternoon

Research module PNT-TS-03: a structured, timed painting session that teaches atmospheric perspective, value grouping, color temperature, and simplification through landscape painting in the Pacific Northwest. Works from life (plein air) or from a window. Any medium: oil, acrylic, watercolor, or gouache.

## Why a Landscape?

Landscape painting is the great teacher. It confronts you with problems no still life can offer: infinite depth, changing light, massive value shifts from foreground to sky, and the particular challenge of painting what you see rather than what you know. An apple sits still and waits. A landscape is alive. The light moves. The clouds shift. The shadows crawl across the ground. You must learn to capture the essence of a moment before that moment dissolves.

In the Pacific Northwest, landscape painting carries an additional gift: the light here is different. It is filtered, diffused, often softened by marine overcast into a luminous silver-gray that compresses the value range and shifts every color toward cool. Painters from sunnier climates often struggle in PNW light because their instincts are calibrated for hard shadows and warm yellows. PNW light demands that you see subtle distinctions — the difference between a blue-gray cloud base and a lavender-gray horizon, between the warm green of a backlit alder leaf and the cool green of a shadowed fir. This session teaches you to see PNW light for what it is and to paint it honestly.

Two hours is enough. It is more than the one-hour apple session because landscape introduces spatial depth and atmospheric perspective, and those require more time to develop. But it is not so long that you will lose the freshness of your initial impression. The great plein air painters — Monet, Sargent, Sorolla, Joaquin Clausell — all worked in sessions of two to three hours, because that is how long the light holds a particular character before shifting into something else.

## Setup

### Location

**Outdoors (plein air):** Find a spot with a view. It does not need to be dramatic. A park bench looking at a tree line. A backyard with a mountain in the distance. A waterfront with a sky. The view should have three distinct depth planes: foreground (within 50 feet), middle ground (50 feet to a half mile), and distance (beyond a half mile). These three planes give you atmospheric perspective — the way things become lighter, bluer, and less detailed as they recede.

PNW locations that work beautifully: any Puget Sound waterfront (the Olympic Mountains across the water give you distance), any viewpoint in the Cascades foothills, Kerry Park in Seattle (the full skyline and Mount Rainier), Deception Pass overlooks, Skagit Valley farmland with the Cascades behind. But your backyard works too. So does a parking lot with trees and sky. The subject is less important than the light.

**From a window:** If weather or mobility prevents going outside, paint what you see from a window. A window frames the composition for you, which is actually helpful — it gives you clear edges and forces you to commit to one view. Open the curtains fully. Turn off interior lights. You want your eyes adapted to the outdoor light, not confused by artificial color temperature.

### Materials

**Canvas or paper:** 8x10 inches maximum. Small format forces simplification. A landscape painted on a small surface cannot include every tree, every rock, every ripple — and that is exactly the point. You will paint the big shapes of land, sky, and water, not the individual leaves.

For oil or acrylic: a canvas panel or primed canvas pad. Pre-tone the surface with a thin wash of yellow ochre or a warm neutral and let it dry. The toned surface eliminates white and gives you a middle value to work against.

For watercolor: cold-press watercolor paper, 140lb, taped to a rigid board with artist tape on all four edges. The tape prevents buckling. Leave the paper white — watercolor builds from light to dark.

For gouache: mixed-media paper or watercolor paper, toned or white.

**Limited palette:**
- Yellow ochre
- Ultramarine blue
- Alizarin crimson
- Sap green (or viridian)
- Burnt sienna
- Titanium white (oil/acrylic/gouache) or leave the paper for white (watercolor)

This six-color palette covers the full landscape range. Yellow ochre and burnt sienna handle warm earth and light tones. Ultramarine and alizarin mix clean cool shadows and atmospheric purples. Sap green with yellow ochre makes the living greens of PNW foliage. Ultramarine with sap green makes the deep, dark greens of Douglas fir and western red cedar. Every green you need comes from mixing — never paint straight from the tube.

**Brushes:**
- One large flat (size 10-12) for sky and big shapes
- One medium flat (size 6-8) for middle-ground forms
- One small round (size 2-4) for detail at the focal point
- One rigger or liner (size 0-1) for branches, masts, power lines — use sparingly

**Other supplies:**
- Paper towels or rags
- Water container (watercolor/gouache/acrylic) or solvent cup (oil)
- Palette — disposable palette paper, glass, or a wooden palette
- A hat with a brim if outdoors (keeps sun off the painting surface and your eyes)
- Clips or tape to keep the painting from blowing off the easel (PNW wind is real)

**Timer:** You will set it four times during this session.

## The Session: 120 Minutes, 6 Steps

### Step 1 — Thumbnail Sketches (15 minutes)

Set your timer for **15 minutes**.

Before you touch the painting surface, make three thumbnail sketches on a scrap of paper. Each thumbnail is small — two inches wide at most — and uses only three values: light, middle, dark. Use a pencil or a marker.

For each thumbnail, try a different composition:
1. **Low horizon** — big sky, small land. Emphasizes clouds, atmosphere, weather.
2. **High horizon** — small sky, big foreground. Emphasizes texture, pattern, the immediate environment.
3. **Divided** — horizon roughly in the middle, but broken by a strong vertical (a tree, a building, a mountain peak) that creates asymmetry.

Squint at the landscape. Squinting collapses the subtle tones into broad value masses. You should be able to see the scene as three to five big shapes of light and dark. Draw those shapes, not the objects.

Choose the best thumbnail. "Best" means the strongest value pattern — the one where the light, middle, and dark shapes create the most interesting arrangement. This thumbnail is your plan. Keep it visible while you paint.

**What you are learning:** Composition is decided before the brush touches the canvas. The three-thumbnail method forces you to explore options. Most beginners paint the first composition they see. This exercise proves there are always better options.

### Step 2 — Draw the Big Shapes (15 minutes)

Set your timer for **15 minutes**.

Using a thin mix of your darkest color (burnt sienna + ultramarine, thinned), draw the big shapes of the landscape on your painting surface. You are looking for four to six shapes maximum:

1. **Sky** — its overall shape, including where clouds break the silhouette of the land
2. **Distant mountains or hills** — the farthest land plane
3. **Middle-ground tree line or buildings** — the middle depth plane
4. **Foreground ground plane** — the nearest land
5. **Water** (if present) — the shape it makes between the land masses

Draw these as flat shapes, like a paper cutout collage. No interior detail. No individual trees. No texture. Just the silhouettes.

Check the proportions against your thumbnail. Is the sky the right height? Is the tree line in the right position? Is the foreground the right depth? Adjust now. Once you start laying color, corrections become much harder.

**What you are learning:** A landscape is not a collection of objects — it is an arrangement of flat shapes stacked front to back. This step trains you to see shapes, not things. When you look at a mountain and see a shape rather than a mountain, you are thinking like a painter.

### Step 3 — Block In Values (30 minutes)

Set your timer for **30 minutes**.

This is the most important step. You are filling each shape with the correct value — how light or dark it is relative to everything else. Do not worry about color accuracy yet. Worry about value accuracy.

**Squint constantly.** Squinting is not a suggestion. It is a technique. Every time you look at the landscape, squint first to see the big value relationships before opening your eyes to see color and detail.

Work from the sky down (for watercolor, work light to dark; for oil/acrylic/gouache, work in any order, but the sky first is traditional because it sets the light key for the rest of the painting).

**The sky:** PNW skies are rarely pure blue. Overcast PNW sky is a symphony of subtle warm and cool grays — blue-gray at the zenith fading to pink-gray or yellow-gray at the horizon. Even on a clear day, the PNW sky near the horizon has a haze layer that warms and lightens the blue. Mix your sky colors carefully. Ultramarine + white + a touch of alizarin gives a cool blue-gray. Add yellow ochre for warmth near the horizon.

**The distance:** Distant mountains and hills are lighter and bluer than your instinct suggests. Atmospheric perspective steals contrast and shifts color toward blue-violet. A mountain that is dark green in reality reads as pale blue-gray in a painting because miles of atmosphere intervene. Mix your distant tones cooler and lighter than you think they should be.

**The middle ground:** This is where most of the color lives. Trees, buildings, hillsides — they have local color that is still visible but softened by some atmospheric haze. Green trees in the middle ground are not as vivid as green trees in the foreground.

**The foreground:** The strongest contrast, the warmest colors, the most texture. Foreground objects have their full local color, hard edges, and visible detail. The eye reads the foreground as "close" because of these qualities.

Paint each shape as a flat patch of the correct value. You are making a value map, not a finished painting. Fill everything in. Leave no white canvas (unless working in watercolor where white paper serves as your lightest light). When you are done, step back six feet. Can you see three clear depth planes? Does the painting read as "near, middle, far"? If yes, the value structure is working.

**What you are learning:** Value creates space. The lighter, cooler, softer the shape, the farther away it reads. The darker, warmer, harder the shape, the closer it reads. This is atmospheric perspective, and it is the single most powerful depth cue available to a painter.

### Step 4 — Develop Color Temperature (30 minutes)

Set your timer for **30 minutes**.

Now refine. The value map is in place. The depth reads. It is time to develop the color relationships that bring the painting to life.

**The warm/cool principle:** Things in light are warm (yellow, orange, red influence). Things in shadow are cool (blue, violet influence). Things in the distance are cool. Things in the foreground are warm. These are generalizations with exceptions, but they hold true enough to guide your color decisions.

**The green problem:** The PNW is green. It is relentlessly, overwhelmingly, magnificently green from October through September. And painting green is hard. Every beginning painter reaches for sap green or viridian and covers the landscape with one shade of green, and the result is a painting that looks like a green carpet.

The solution is temperature variation. No two greens in a landscape are the same temperature.

- Sunlit grass: yellow-green (sap green + yellow ochre + white)
- Shadowed grass: cool green (sap green + ultramarine)
- Douglas fir: deep blue-green (ultramarine + sap green + burnt sienna to darken)
- Alder leaves backlit: warm yellow-green (yellow ochre + sap green)
- Distant forest: blue-green grayed with alizarin (sap green + ultramarine + alizarin + white)
- Dead grass or dry field: yellow-brown (yellow ochre + burnt sienna)

Mix every green on your palette. Never apply a tube green directly. Mix, compare to the landscape, adjust, then apply. If you have ten green shapes in your painting, you should have ten different green mixtures on your palette.

**The gray sky (it is NOT gray):** PNW overcast is the other signature challenge. Beginners mix white + black and paint the sky neutral gray. But PNW overcast is never neutral. It shifts across the sky in subtle gradients:

- **Blue-gray** overhead, where the thinning clouds let sky color through
- **Pink-gray** or **peach-gray** near the horizon, where the low sun angle warms the atmosphere
- **Lavender-gray** in the middle, where blue and warm tones mix
- **Yellow-gray** in breaks where sun threatens to appear

Train your eye to see these shifts. Squint, then relax your gaze and let the subtle colors come forward. Mix each one. A PNW sky painted with five or six gray variations reads as luminous and alive. A PNW sky painted with one flat gray reads as dead.

**What you are learning:** Color temperature is how painters separate shapes that are similar in value. Two greens that are the same lightness but different in temperature (one warm, one cool) read as different surfaces at different distances. Temperature is the dimension beyond value.

### Step 5 — Add Selective Detail (20 minutes)

Set your timer for **20 minutes**.

You have a painting with correct values, spatial depth, and color temperature variation. Now — and only now — add detail. But not everywhere. Detail goes in one place: the **focal point**.

The focal point is the area of the painting where you want the viewer's eye to land. It might be a tree against the sky, a building catching light, a break in the clouds where sun illuminates a hillside. Wherever it is, that is where you put your sharpest edges, your strongest value contrast, your most vivid color, and your finest brushwork.

Everything else stays loose. The periphery of the painting should be softer, less detailed, less contrasty. This is how the eye works — we see detail at the center of our gaze and softness at the periphery. A painting that is equally detailed everywhere has no focal point, and the viewer's eye wanders without settling.

For the PNW landscape, effective focal points include:
- A bright spot where sun breaks through the overcast
- A red barn or roofline against green hills
- The snow-covered peak of a Cascade volcano above the tree line
- A bright reflection on water
- A single tree silhouetted against a lighter sky

Use your smallest brush. Add branches to the focal tree (not every tree — just the one). Sharpen the edge where the mountain meets the sky (at the focal point only — let other edges stay soft). Add a few warm accents in the foreground (a wildflower, a fence post, sunlit grass).

Resist the urge to detail everything. Every time you reach for detail, ask: "Is this at or near the focal point?" If the answer is no, put the small brush down and walk away from the easel.

**What you are learning:** Editing is the hardest painting skill. Knowing what to leave out is more important than knowing what to put in. A painting with one area of focus surrounded by simplified supporting areas reads as confident and intentional. A painting where every square inch is equally developed reads as anxious and overworked.

### Step 6 — Evaluate and Sign (10 minutes)

Set your timer for **10 minutes**.

Step back at least six feet from the painting. Better yet, turn your back on it for two minutes — look at the actual landscape, then turn around and look at the painting with fresh eyes. What do you see?

**Value check:** Squint at both the landscape and the painting. Do the big value relationships match? Is the sky lighter than the land? Is the foreground darker than the distance? If you photographed both in black and white, would the patterns match?

**Temperature check:** Does the painting feel cool in the distance and warm in the foreground? Are there at least three different greens visible? Is the sky more than one flat tone?

**Focal point check:** Where does your eye go first? Is it the focal point? If your eye goes to a high-contrast area that is not the focal point, soften that area — knock back the value contrast, blur the edges, cool the color.

**Edge check:** Are there at least three types of edges in the painting? Hard edges at the focal point, soft edges in the distance, and lost edges where two shapes of similar value merge? If every edge is hard, the painting looks like a coloring book. Soften the edges in the distance and the periphery.

Make final adjustments. Touch nothing that is already working. The most common mistake in the last ten minutes is "improving" something that was fine, and making it worse.

Sign the painting. Date the back. Note the location and weather. In a year, this information will be meaningful.

## PNW-Specific Challenges and Solutions

### The Green Problem (Expanded)

The Pacific Northwest is one of the greenest places on Earth. Western Washington receives 37 inches of rain annually (75+ inches on the Olympic Peninsula), and the result is a landscape dominated by conifers and broad-leaf trees in an intensity of green that challenges every painter.

The key insight: **green is not a color, it is a family of colors.** There is no single "green" in a PNW landscape. There are hundreds, varying by:

- **Species:** Douglas fir is blue-green. Western red cedar is yellow-green. Big-leaf maple is warm yellow-green in spring and gold in autumn. Red alder is gray-green. Sitka spruce is silver-green.
- **Light:** Sunlit canopy is warm green. Shadowed understory is cool dark green. Backlit leaves are the brightest, most vivid green you will see.
- **Distance:** Near greens are vivid and warm. Distant greens are muted and cool. A forested mountain two miles away is not green — it is blue-green-gray.
- **Season:** Spring greens are bright and yellow. Summer greens are deep and saturated. Autumn (for deciduous species) brings gold, orange, and the rusts of dying bracken fern. Winter greens are darker and grayer under low-angle light.

Practice mixing greens before you paint the landscape. On a scrap of paper or palette, mix and label ten different greens from your palette colors. Tape this color chart where you can see it while painting. When you need a green, match it to your chart, then adjust.

### The Gray Sky (Expanded)

PNW overcast is not a painter's enemy. It is a painter's gift. The diffused light of an overcast sky eliminates harsh shadows, creating a soft, even illumination that reveals color in the landscape that would be bleached out by direct sun. Impressionist painters traveled to Normandy for the same quality of light. PNW painters live in it.

But painting it requires seeing past the word "gray." Your brain labels the sky "gray" and your hand mixes a neutral gray. Your eye, if you let it, sees something more complex:

- Hold a white card up against the sky in different positions. Compare the sky tone to the card. You will see that the sky is darker than you assumed, and that it has color — blue, pink, yellow, violet — that the word "gray" erased.
- Compare the zenith (directly overhead) to the horizon. The zenith is cooler and darker. The horizon is warmer and lighter. The gradient between them is the structure of the sky.
- Look for cloud edges. Even in heavy overcast, clouds have edges — some sharp, some soft. Painting these edges gives the sky volume and prevents it from reading as a flat, dead wall.

The overcast PNW sky, painted honestly, is one of the most beautiful skies in painting. It has the luminous quality of a pearl — light from within, color shifting with angle and position. Master this sky and you have a skill that few painters outside the PNW develop.

## After the Session

Do not assess the painting while you are standing next to the landscape. Take it home. Lean it against a wall where you will see it throughout the day. Your opinion of it will change — things you thought were failures will reveal themselves as strengths, and things you were proud of will reveal themselves as weaknesses. Both revelations are valuable.

If you worked outdoors, make notes about the experience. What was hard? (Probably the greens, or the sky, or both.) What surprised you? (Probably how fast the light changed, or how different the colors looked in shade versus sun.) What would you do differently next time?

Then do it again. Paint the same view, same location, different weather. Paint it on an overcast day and a sunny day and a rainy day. Each light condition teaches different lessons. The Impressionists understood this — Monet's haystacks and cathedrals are the same subject under different light, and the differences are what the paintings are about.

If you want to build the foundational skills that make landscape painting easier, try the [30-Day Value Study Challenge](value-study-challenge.md) — value is the skeleton that every landscape hangs on. And if you have not yet done the [Paint an Apple in One Hour](paint-an-apple.md) session, start there — it teaches value structure on a simpler subject before you tackle the complexity of landscape.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** — [color-composition]: Landscape painting is composition at scale. The arrangement of sky, land, and water — the big shapes — is the compositional decision. The color temperature shifts from warm foreground to cool distance are color-composition principles applied to spatial depth. The focal point strategy (detail one area, simplify the rest) is the core of compositional hierarchy.
- **Art** — [seeing-drawing]: Plein air painting is the ultimate seeing exercise. You cannot paint what you do not see, and the PNW landscape demands that you see subtle value differences, temperature shifts, and color variations that studio painting from photographs cannot teach. The overcast sky alone is a master class in observation — if you can see five different colors in a "gray" sky, your seeing is improving.
- **Nature-Studies** — [ecology-habitats]: Every species in the landscape has a visual signature. Douglas fir reads differently from western red cedar reads differently from big-leaf maple. As your landscape painting improves, your ecological literacy improves with it — you begin to identify species by color and form at a distance, the way birders identify birds by silhouette and flight pattern.
- **Nature-Studies** — [outdoor-observation]: Plein air painting is structured outdoor observation. Two hours of focused looking at a PNW landscape teaches more about the environment than two hours of hiking, because painting forces you to notice things that walking past does not: the exact color of moss on a branch, the way a shadow moves across a clearing, the direction of the prevailing wind by the lean of the trees.
