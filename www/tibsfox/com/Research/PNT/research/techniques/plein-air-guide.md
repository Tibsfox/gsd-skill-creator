# Painting Outside -- A PNW Plein Air Guide

## The Challenge and the Gift

Plein air painting is the practice of painting outdoors, directly from the landscape, in the conditions the landscape gives you. It is the most valuable training a painter can undertake, and it is the most humbling. Indoors, you control everything -- the light, the temperature, the arrangement of objects, the timing. Outdoors, you control nothing. The light moves. The clouds change. The temperature drops. The wind knocks your pochade box off the tripod. A golden retriever investigates your open paint kit. A curious six-year-old asks what you are doing (the correct answer is "painting," delivered with patience, because that child might become a painter).

The Pacific Northwest is one of the most rewarding and most challenging plein air painting environments in North America. The rewards: the most varied light conditions on the continent (from luminous overcast to blazing alpine sun, sometimes in the same hour), landscapes of staggering beauty and diversity (ocean, rainforest, volcanic peaks, agricultural flatlands, urban waterfronts), and a mild climate that allows year-round outdoor painting. The challenges: rain (obviously), the overwhelming green palette from April through October, and light conditions that change faster than almost anywhere else because of the region's active weather systems.

This guide is practical. It covers what to bring, how to set up, how to manage a two-hour painting session, and how to handle the specific conditions the PNW throws at you.

## The Pochade Box Setup

A pochade box is a compact painting station that holds a panel, a palette, brushes, and paint in a single box that clamps onto a tripod. It is the tool that makes plein air painting practical rather than an awkward juggling act.

### Build Your Own ($45 in materials)

If you have basic woodworking skills --- or want to develop them --- you can build a pochade box from Baltic birch plywood for under $50. The [DIY Pochade Box project](../try-sessions/build-a-pochade-box.md) walks through the complete build: box joints or rabbet joints, piano hinge, tripod mount, panel clip, and finishing. The crossover with WDC (Woodcraft) makes it a two-discipline project, and the satisfaction of painting outdoors with a box you built yourself is hard to overstate.

### Budget Setup ($150-200 total)

If you are testing whether plein air painting is for you, start minimal:

- **Guerrilla Painter 9x12 Guerrilla Box** ($150-180): The box that established the modern pochade category. Rugged construction, holds panels up to 9x12 inches, has a built-in palette and brush holder. It is not the lightest or the most elegant, but it works, and it has been field-tested by thousands of painters in every climate.
- **A basic photography tripod** ($40-60): Any tripod that supports 5+ pounds and has a flat head or quick-release plate works. The Guerrilla Box clamps to the tripod head. You do not need a fancy tripod -- you need one that does not blow over in a moderate breeze.
- **Total:** $190-240 including tripod.

### Mid-Range Setup ($250-350 total)

For the painter who has done a few sessions and is committed:

- **Open Box M** ($180-220): A more refined pochade box with a larger mixing area, better panel clamping, and a design that opens flat for maximum workspace. The Open Box M is the most popular pochade box among serious plein air painters who work at 9x12 or smaller.
- **Manfrotto 190 series tripod** ($100-130): Heavier and more stable than a budget tripod. The center column inverts for low angles, and the leg locks are solid. This tripod will last a decade of field use.
- **Total:** $280-350 including tripod.

### Premium Setup ($400-600 total)

For the dedicated plein air painter:

- **Strada Easel Mini** ($250-300): Aluminum construction, ultralight (under 2 pounds), accommodates panels up to 11x14. The Strada's clamping system holds panels securely even in wind, and the design is so compact it fits in a daypack. This is the pochade box that plein air painters upgrade to and keep for life.
- **En Plein Air Pro Traveler** ($200-250): If you want to work larger (up to 12x16), this box accommodates bigger panels than the Strada while remaining backpack-portable.
- **Manfrotto 190 or equivalent** ($100-130).
- **Total:** $350-430 for the Strada kit, $300-380 for the En Plein Air Pro kit.

### Beyond the Box

You also need:

- **Panels:** Raymar Art pre-primed linen panels ($3-5 each for 8x10 or 9x12) or homemade panels (buy Ampersand Gessobord or cut your own from 1/8" hardboard and apply two coats of gesso). Bring more panels than you think you need -- at least three per session. Sometimes the first attempt is a warm-up.
- **A wet panel carrier** ($25-40): Two panels clamp face-to-face with spacers that keep the wet paint surfaces from touching. The **Raymar wet panel carrier** or the **Guerrilla Painter 9x12 Wet Panel Box** ($30-40) solves the problem of transporting wet paintings without smearing.
- **A backpack or messenger bag** that fits the box, tripod, panels, and supplies. A good hiking daypack works. Plein air painting is a portable sport.

## The Limited Palette for Speed

Plein air painting rewards a limited palette. Fewer colors mean faster mixing, more harmonious color relationships (because every mixture shares parent pigments), and less to carry. Here is the palette that handles the PNW landscape:

### The Five-Color PNW Plein Air Palette

1. **Yellow Ochre (PY42):** The warm earth tone. Sunlit grass, sandy beaches, autumn foliage, warm highlights. Yellow ochre is the workhorse of landscape painting.
2. **Ultramarine Blue (PB29):** The warm blue. Skies, distant mountains, shadow colors, the blue-gray atmosphere that defines PNW overcast. Mixed with burnt sienna, it makes the richest darks. Mixed with yellow ochre, it makes natural greens.
3. **Alizarin Crimson (PR83 or PV19):** The cool red. Mixed with ultramarine, it makes deep violets for distant conifers and cloud shadows. Mixed with yellow ochre, it makes warm oranges for autumn maples. A touch in a green mixture prevents the green from going electric.
4. **Titanium White (PW6):** Everything needs white. Buy the biggest tube you can carry.
5. **Burnt Sienna (PBr7):** The warm dark. The essential mixing companion to ultramarine blue -- together they produce a dark so rich it replaces black. Burnt sienna also warms any mixture it touches and provides the foundation for tree trunks, wet earth, and the deep warm tones in shadow.

**Optional sixth color: Cadmium Yellow Light (PY37).** If you need brighter, more saturated greens than yellow ochre + ultramarine can produce (spring foliage, sunlit meadows), cadmium yellow light extends the palette's range into vivid territory.

This palette fits in a Pochade box with room to spare. Six tubes, plus a medium (Gamblin Gamsol for oil, or just water for acrylic). The limited palette forces you to mix every color you see, which is the fastest path to understanding color relationships. After six months of mixing every green from ochre or cadmium yellow plus ultramarine, you will understand green the way a PNW painter must.

## The Two-Hour Painting Session

Two hours is the sweet spot for plein air painting. The light holds reasonably steady for about two hours before the sun moves enough to change the shadow pattern fundamentally. Working within this window prevents the temptation to chase changing light, which produces paintings that look like they were painted under three different lighting conditions (because they were).

Here is how those two hours break down:

### Minutes 0-15: Composition and Sketch (15 minutes)

Before you touch paint, make a thumbnail sketch. A notan study -- a 2x3 inch sketch in two or three values (dark, medium, light) that maps the big shapes of the composition. Use a marker, a pen, or a brush with dark paint. This takes five minutes and saves you from starting a painting with a weak composition.

Then draw the composition lightly on your panel. A thin line of thinned paint (burnt sienna thinned with solvent or water) placed with a small round brush. Establish the horizon line, the major shape breaks, and the placement of your focal point. Do not draw details. You are placing shapes, not rendering them.

During these fifteen minutes, commit to the light. Look at the shadow pattern. This is the shadow pattern you will paint for the entire session, even if the shadows move. The painting records a specific moment. If you update the shadows as they move, you lose the coherence of that moment.

### Minutes 15-45: Block-In (30 minutes)

This is the most important phase. Cover the entire panel with paint, establishing the major value relationships and color temperatures. Work from dark to light, or from the sky down, or from the big shapes inward -- whatever system keeps you moving. The goal is to have no white panel showing by the end of 30 minutes.

Use a medium flat brush. Mix colors quickly. Do not blend -- place each stroke and move on. You are building a mosaic of color notes that, when you step back, reads as the scene. The block-in should look rough up close and surprisingly accurate from six feet away.

Key decisions during the block-in: What is the lightest light? (Usually the sky, or sunlit snow.) What is the darkest dark? (Usually the shadow under a tree or the interior of a forest.) How do the midtones relate to these extremes? Getting these three values right -- light, dark, and the relationship of the midtones -- is 80% of a successful plein air painting.

### Minutes 45-90: Develop (45 minutes)

With the block-in established, you can now refine. Add color variations within the big shapes. Define the edges -- some sharp (the silhouette of a tree against the sky), some soft (the transition from sunlit meadow to shadow). Add the secondary shapes: individual trees within the forest mass, the curve of a path, the reflection in a puddle.

Work from general to specific. The block-in is general. Each pass adds specificity. But resist the urge to jump to details too soon. A painting where the big shapes are strong and the details are merely suggested is better than a painting where a few details are exquisite but the big shapes are wrong.

This is the phase where you earn or lose the painting. The temptation to overwork -- to fix, to blend, to noodle -- is strongest here. Fight it. Make a stroke, evaluate it, and move on. If a stroke is wrong, paint over it with a single correcting stroke. Do not scrub.

### Minutes 90-120: Refine (30 minutes)

The final half hour is for accents, highlights, and the selective details that make the painting sing. The highlight on the water. The dark trunk of a foreground tree. A few calligraphic strokes suggesting branches against the sky. The warm note of a rooftop or a distant figure.

This is also the phase for edge control -- the sophisticated skill that separates a good plein air painting from a great one. Hard edges draw the eye and suggest nearness. Soft edges suggest distance and atmosphere. Lost edges (where two adjacent values are so close that the boundary disappears) create mystery. Walk the painting with your eyes: sharp edge at the focal point, soft edges as you move toward the periphery, lost edges where the subject dissolves into the background.

Stop before you think you should. A painting that is 90% finished and left with some areas merely suggested has more life than a painting that is 100% finished and overworked everywhere. Plein air paintings are studies -- they capture a moment, not a rendering. Their charm is in the freshness, the spontaneity, the evidence of speed. Protect that freshness by knowing when to stop.

## PNW-Specific Challenges and Strategies

### Painting in Overcast Light

The PNW's overcast sky is not an obstacle -- it is a specific and beautiful light condition that many painters travel here to experience. Diffused light eliminates harsh shadows and reveals the subtle color relationships within the landscape: the warm greens versus the cool greens, the slight warmth in the shadows versus the slight coolness of the open sky, the hundred variations of gray in the cloud cover.

The key to painting overcast light: **value compression.** The value range under overcast is narrower than under direct sun. There are no brilliant highlights and no deep, sharp shadows. Everything lives in the middle of the value range. The painter must be precise within this narrow range -- the difference between a value 4 shadow and a value 5 midtone is the difference between a form that reads and one that goes flat. Squint hard. The subtle value differences that are invisible at a casual glance become visible when you squint.

Color temperature does the heavy lifting in overcast light. Where direct sunlight creates contrast through value (bright light versus dark shadow), overcast light creates contrast through temperature. The top of an object (facing the sky) is slightly cool. The bottom (facing the warm ground) is slightly warm. The side facing the lighter part of the sky is cooler than the side facing the darker part. These temperature shifts are subtle but real, and painting them accurately is what makes an overcast PNW landscape feel true.

### Rain Strategies

It rains. This is the Pacific Northwest. The question is not whether it will rain during your plein air session but how you will handle it when it does.

**Oil painters:** Rain on wet oil paint is less catastrophic than you might expect. Raindrops sit on the oily surface and can be blotted off with a paper towel without disturbing the paint underneath. A light drizzle is manageable. A downpour is a signal to close the box and retreat to cover. A pochade box with a lid provides some protection for the painting when closed quickly.

**Watercolor painters:** Rain on watercolor is genuinely destructive -- droplets create blooms and backruns that cannot be removed. Watercolorists must work under cover: a portable canopy, a large umbrella clamped to the tripod (the **UC Shelter** or similar -- $30-40), or from the shelter of a car with the hatch open. Some watercolorists embrace the rain -- letting drops fall on a nearly finished painting and incorporating the blooms as happy accidents that read as atmosphere. This is a legitimate technique, but it requires a painting that is sturdy enough to survive the accidents.

**Acrylic painters:** Rain is less destructive to acrylics than to watercolor but more annoying than for oil. Raindrops on wet acrylic create water marks as the drops thin the paint. Once the acrylic dries, rain cannot hurt it. Work under an umbrella or canopy, and accept that the PNW plein air experience includes managing moisture.

**The car-as-studio approach:** Many PNW plein air painters work from their car on rainy days. Park facing the view. Set the pochade box on the dashboard or a lap board. Open the window a crack for ventilation (essential if using oil paint and solvents). This is not cheating. It is practical. You are still painting from life, in the conditions of the moment, responding to real light and real atmosphere. The car is just a portable shelter with a great view.

### The Green Problem

The PNW landscape from April through October is fifty shades of green, and painting it convincingly without monotone mush is the region's signature technical challenge. The comprehensive approach:

**Never use green from the tube.** Sap green, hooker's green, permanent green -- leave them all at home. Every green in your painting should be mixed from a yellow and a blue (and often modified with a red or an earth tone). This guarantees that every green is unique, because no two mixtures are identical.

**Vary temperature aggressively.** Sunlit greens are warm: cadmium yellow + ultramarine, leaning toward yellow. Shadow greens are cool: ultramarine + a touch of yellow ochre, leaning toward blue. Distant greens are cooler still, grayed with a complement (a touch of alizarin crimson or burnt sienna). If you paint warm greens in the sunlit areas and cool greens in the shadows, the painting will read as three-dimensional even if the value differences are small.

**Use the complement to control saturation.** A touch of red (alizarin crimson or cadmium red) in a green mixture desaturates it without making it muddy. This is how you paint the muted, atmospheric greens of distant PNW forests -- greens that are almost gray, with just enough color to register as green rather than neutral.

**Paint the not-green things first.** The sky, the water, the tree trunks, the path, the rocks, the distant mountain -- paint everything that is not green before you tackle the greens. This establishes the context against which the greens must work. A green that looks monotone in isolation looks varied and rich when surrounded by the warm grays, blues, and earth tones of the non-green elements.

## The Best PNW Plein Air Spots

### San Juan Islands

The San Juan Islands offer some of the most dramatic painting subjects in the PNW: rocky shorelines, Garry oak meadows, pastoral farmland, and views of the Cascades and Olympics across open water. The light on the islands is often better than the mainland -- the rain shadow effect means more sun breaks and more dramatic cloud formations. **American Camp** on San Juan Island (open prairie sloping to the Strait of Juan de Fuca with views to Mount Baker and the Olympics) and **Lime Kiln Point State Park** (rocky shoreline with lighthouse, prime orca-watching spot) are legendary painting locations. Access via the Washington State Ferry from Anacortes.

### Skagit Valley

The Skagit Valley is the historic heart of PNW plein air painting, and for good reason. The flat agricultural landscape (daffodil fields in March, tulip fields in April -- the most vivid color fields a painter will encounter in the PNW) meets water and sky along a low horizon. The light quality is extraordinary -- rapid weather changes mean that dramatic cloud shadows sweeping across the fields can transform the scene minute by minute. **Fir Island** (farmland along the Skagit River delta), **La Conner waterfront** (boats, pilings, the Swinomish Channel), and the **tulip fields south of Mount Vernon** during the Skagit Valley Tulip Festival (April) are classic locations.

### Deception Pass

Deception Pass State Park, at the northern tip of Whidbey Island, offers cliff-top views of the churning narrows where the Strait of Juan de Fuca meets the Saratoga Passage. The bridge itself is a dramatic subject. The rocky shoreline, the tide pools, the wind-sculpted Douglas firs clinging to the cliffs -- the raw, elemental quality of the landscape demands bold painting. Work from the viewpoint at the bridge or hike down to North Beach or Rosario Beach for shoreline subjects. Wind is a factor -- secure your pochade box firmly and bring a wind-resistant setup.

### Mount Rainier / Paradise

Paradise, at 5,400 feet on Mount Rainier's south side, offers subalpine meadows (wildflowers in July-August) with the glacier-clad peak rising directly above. The scale is humbling -- the mountain is so large that it does not fit in a normal field of view. Painting Rainier teaches you to manage atmospheric perspective (the mountain's lower slopes are always hazier than the peak) and to resist the temptation to make the mountain too small (it is always bigger than you think). The road to Paradise is open late May through October, weather permitting. Bring warm layers -- the temperature at Paradise can be 20 degrees cooler than the lowlands.

### Discovery Park, Seattle

For the urban painter, Discovery Park in Seattle's Magnolia neighborhood offers 534 acres of forest, meadow, and shoreline within the city limits. The West Point Lighthouse, visible from the beach trail, is a classic PNW painting subject -- white lighthouse, driftwood-strewn beach, the Olympics across Puget Sound. The forest trails offer intimate compositions of sword fern, bigleaf maple, and Douglas fir that are quintessentially PNW. Discovery Park is the easiest plein air location to reach for Seattle painters, and its variety of subjects means you can paint there a hundred times without repeating yourself.

## Joining the Community

Plein air painting improves faster in community. Painting alongside more experienced painters, seeing how they compose, how they mix, how they handle the light -- this accelerates your development in ways that solitary practice cannot.

**Puget Sound Plein Air Painters (PSPAP):** The most active plein air group in the Seattle area. Regular organized paint-outs at locations throughout Puget Sound. Open to all skill levels. Find them through their Facebook group.

**Portland Plein Air:** Portland's primary plein air group, organizing paint-outs in the Portland metro area and beyond. Monthly events, annual exhibitions.

**Skagit Plein Air:** Based in the Skagit Valley, the historical heart of PNW plein air painting. Paint-outs in La Conner, the tulip fields, and throughout the valley.

**Plein Air Washington Artists (PAWA):** A statewide organization that organizes events, workshops, and juried exhibitions. Their annual event brings painters from across the region to a featured location.

Show up. Set up next to someone who has been painting for twenty years. Watch how they start. Ask questions between sessions (not during -- do not interrupt a painter who is working). Most plein air painters are generous with their knowledge and delighted by someone who is genuinely interested in learning. The plein air community is one of the most welcoming corners of the art world.
