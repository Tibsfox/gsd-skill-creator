# Color Mixing — Hands-On Exercises

## Learning What Your Paints Actually Do

Color theory in books is abstract. Color mixing on the palette is concrete. You can read about complementary colors for hours and still not know what happens when you push cadmium red into viridian green with a palette knife. The only way to learn color mixing is to mix color — systematically, repeatedly, with your actual paints on your actual palette, recording what happens.

Every pigment has its own personality. Ultramarine blue is warm and granulating. Phthalo blue is cold and staining and ten times stronger than you expect. Yellow ochre is opaque and gentle. Cadmium yellow is opaque and aggressive. Burnt sienna is transparent and warm. Alizarin crimson is transparent and cool. You cannot predict these behaviors from a color wheel diagram. You have to experience them with your hands.

This chapter is entirely exercises. Five of them. Each one teaches a fundamental principle of color mixing through direct practice. Do them in order. Do them with the actual paints you own, on the actual surfaces you paint on, in the actual medium you work in (watercolor, oil, or acrylic — the exercises apply to all three, with minor adjustments for handling). Write your notes on each exercise. These are your personal color reference charts, and they will be more useful than any book.

**Cross-references:** [PNT-02] Paint Mediums (pigment science), [PNT-05] Color Theory & Technique, [PNT Techniques] Watercolor Basics, [PNT Techniques] Oil Painting Basics.

## Exercise 1: Paint a Color Wheel from Primaries

### What This Teaches

How your specific primary pigments actually mix. Not how a textbook diagram says they should mix. Your yellow plus your blue makes a specific green — what green? How bright is it? How dark? Does it lean warm or cool? You will not know until you mix it.

### Setup

You need three primary colors: one red, one yellow, one blue. For this exercise, use the following pigments (or the closest equivalents you have):

- **Red:** Cadmium Red Medium (PR108) or Pyrrole Red (PR254)
- **Yellow:** Cadmium Yellow Medium (PY35) or Hansa Yellow Medium (PY97)
- **Blue:** Ultramarine Blue (PB29)

You also need white (titanium white PW6, for oil or acrylic) and your painting surface: watercolor paper, canvas panel, or a primed board.

### Step by Step

1. Draw a circle on your surface and divide it into 12 equal segments (like a clock face). Pencil is fine.

2. Place your three primaries at the 12, 4, and 8 o'clock positions:
   - 12 o'clock: Yellow
   - 4 o'clock: Red
   - 8 o'clock: Blue

3. Paint each primary in its segment — full strength, straight from the tube (or concentrated wash for watercolor).

4. Now mix the secondaries. These go between each pair of primaries:
   - 2 o'clock: **Orange** (red + yellow, equal parts). Mix on the palette until uniform, then paint the segment.
   - 6 o'clock: **Purple/Violet** (red + blue, equal parts). This mixture is where many beginners get their first surprise. Cadmium red + ultramarine blue often produces a dull, brownish purple — not the bright violet you expected. That is because cadmium red contains a yellow bias that muddies the mixture. Note this. It matters.
   - 10 o'clock: **Green** (blue + yellow, equal parts). Ultramarine + cadmium yellow makes a reasonably bright green, though not as vivid as you might expect. The green will be somewhat muted.

5. Mix the tertiaries. These go in the remaining six segments, halfway between each primary and its adjacent secondary:
   - 1 o'clock: Yellow-orange (yellow + orange, or yellow + a small amount of red)
   - 3 o'clock: Red-orange (red + orange, or red + a small amount of yellow)
   - 5 o'clock: Red-violet (red + purple)
   - 7 o'clock: Blue-violet (blue + purple)
   - 9 o'clock: Blue-green (blue + green)
   - 11 o'clock: Yellow-green (yellow + green)

6. Label every segment with the colors used and the pigment codes. This is your reference chart.

### What to Notice

- The orange and yellow-orange segments will be the brightest, most saturated mixtures. Red and yellow are natural partners.
- The purple/violet segment will likely be disappointing. This is normal with cadmium red + ultramarine. For a vivid purple, you need a cool red (alizarin crimson, quinacridone magenta) and a warm blue (ultramarine).
- The greens will vary from muted to moderately bright. For the most vivid greens, you would need phthalo blue + cadmium lemon (cool blue + cool yellow). This is why many painters keep a warm and cool version of each primary.

### The Split-Primary Lesson

If time allows, paint a second color wheel using six colors instead of three — a warm and cool version of each primary:

- Warm red: Cadmium Red Medium (PR108)
- Cool red: Alizarin Crimson (PR83) or Quinacridone Rose (PV19)
- Warm yellow: Cadmium Yellow Medium (PY35)
- Cool yellow: Lemon Yellow (PY3) or Hansa Yellow Light (PY3)
- Warm blue: Ultramarine Blue (PB29)
- Cool blue: Phthalo Blue (PB15:3) or Cerulean Blue (PB35)

Now you can mix vivid secondaries by combining the primaries that lean toward each other: cool red + warm blue = vivid violet. Warm red + warm yellow = vivid orange. Cool blue + cool yellow = vivid green. The split-primary system is the foundation of deliberate color mixing, and this exercise makes it immediately tangible.

## Exercise 2: The Nine-Step Value Scale

### What This Teaches

That value is more important than color. A painting with correct values and wrong colors still reads correctly from across a room. A painting with wrong values and correct colors looks broken no matter how beautiful the individual hues are. Value is the skeleton. Color is the clothing.

### Why This Is the Most Important Exercise in Painting

Squint at any great painting and you will see that it works as a pattern of light and dark shapes — before you register any color at all. Every master, from Rembrandt to Sargent to Richard Schmid, has said some version of the same thing: get the values right and the painting works. This exercise trains your eye to see value independent of color.

### Setup

You need ivory black (PBk9) and titanium white (PW6). Any medium: oil, acrylic, or gouache. (For watercolor, use Payne's gray or lamp black — you will dilute toward white paper instead of mixing with white paint.)

Draw a row of nine squares on your surface. Each square should be approximately 1.5 x 1.5 inches.

### Step by Step

1. **Square 1 (far left):** Pure titanium white. Apply it opaquely. This is value 1 — the lightest possible value.

2. **Square 9 (far right):** Pure ivory black. As dark as you can make it. This is value 9 — the darkest possible value.

3. **Square 5 (center):** Mix a middle gray — exactly halfway between white and black. This is harder than it sounds. Your eye will tell you a mixture is "middle gray" when it is actually quite light. Test it: hold the mixture next to the white square and the black square. It should look equally distant from both. Start with white on your palette and add tiny amounts of black (black has enormous tinting strength — it overwhelms white if you add too much at once). When you think you have the right value, go a touch darker. You are almost certainly too light.

4. **Square 3:** Mix a light gray — halfway between white (square 1) and middle gray (square 5).

5. **Square 7:** Mix a dark gray — halfway between middle gray (square 5) and black (square 9).

6. **Squares 2, 4, 6, 8:** Fill in the remaining steps. Each square should be a visibly distinct, even step from its neighbors. The progression from white to black should be smooth and linear.

7. Let it dry. Then evaluate. Squint at the scale from a distance. Do any two adjacent values jump too much? Do any two look the same? Remix and repaint until the progression is even.

### The Hard Part

The jumps between values 1-3 (the lights) will seem easy. The jumps between values 7-9 (the darks) will seem impossible — dark gray, darker gray, and black all start to look the same. This is a known perceptual limitation. Our eyes are more sensitive to differences in light values than in dark values. Training yourself to see distinctions in the dark range is one of the most valuable perceptual skills a painter can develop.

### Application

Once you have a reliable nine-step value scale, use it as a reference tool every time you paint. Hold it next to your painting to check: is this shadow really a value 7, or is it only a 5? Most beginners do not push their darks dark enough or keep their lights light enough. The value scale makes the diagnosis objective.

## Exercise 3: Complementary Mixing

### What This Teaches

How complementary colors neutralize each other. This is the most useful mixing principle for painting from life, because natural subjects are full of neutral, muted colors — the gray of a cloudy sky, the brown of a tree trunk, the soft mauve of a distant mountain. These are not neutral because they lack color. They are neutral because they contain complementary colors in near-equal proportions.

### The Three Complementary Pairs

Complementary colors sit opposite each other on the color wheel. The three primary complementary pairs are:

- **Red and Green**
- **Blue and Orange**
- **Yellow and Purple/Violet**

When mixed in equal proportions, complements theoretically produce a neutral gray. In practice, each pair produces a different character of gray or brown, because real pigments are not theoretically perfect primaries.

### Step by Step

For each complementary pair, paint a mixing strip: a row of seven swatches transitioning from pure Color A on the left to pure Color B on the right, with the neutral midpoint at center.

**Pair 1: Red + Green**

Use cadmium red (or pyrrole red) and viridian green (PG18) or phthalo green (PG7).

1. Swatch 1: Pure red.
2. Swatch 2: Red + a small amount of green. The red becomes muted, duller, heading toward a brick tone.
3. Swatch 3: Red + more green. Now it is a warm, reddish brown.
4. Swatch 4 (center): Equal proportions. This should be a neutral — likely a warm gray-brown. Not a clean gray — real pigments do not produce clean grays. But it will be distinctly neutral, with neither red nor green dominant.
5. Swatch 5: Green + more red. A dull, olive green leaning toward brown.
6. Swatch 6: Green + a small amount of red. A muted green.
7. Swatch 7: Pure green.

**Pair 2: Blue + Orange**

Use ultramarine blue and cadmium orange (PO20) or a mix of cadmium red + cadmium yellow.

Repeat the seven-swatch strip. The midpoint should produce a gray with more temperature variation than the red-green pair. Ultramarine blue + cadmium orange is one of the most useful mixing pairs in painting — the resulting grays range from warm (orange-dominant) to cool (blue-dominant) and are perfect for shadows, stone, concrete, overcast skies, and any muted natural tone.

**Pair 3: Yellow + Purple**

Use cadmium yellow and a mixed purple (ultramarine + alizarin crimson) or dioxazine purple (PV23).

This pair produces the widest range of browns and khakis. Yellow + purple crosses through gold, raw sienna, raw umber territory, and eventually reaches dull gray-violets. The midpoint is often a surprisingly warm brown. This is where many of the natural earth tones live.

### What to Notice

Each complementary pair produces a different family of neutrals. Red + green makes warm, reddish browns. Blue + orange makes cool or warm grays. Yellow + purple makes golden browns and khakis. A painter who understands these three families can mix any neutral they encounter in nature without reaching for a tube of brown or gray paint.

### The Mud Diagnosis

Mud in a painting almost always means you have accidentally mixed near-complementary colors on the paper or canvas. A green sky-reflected tone painted over a red-toned underpainting will go brown. A purple shadow glazed over a yellow-toned surface will go dull. This is complementary neutralization happening unintentionally.

The fix: premix on the palette. Hold your mixed color up against your value scale. Is it the value and chroma you intended? If not, adjust on the palette, not on the painting.

## Exercise 4: Temperature Strings

### What This Teaches

That every color has a temperature — a warmth or coolness relative to its neighbors — and that temperature contrast is one of the most powerful tools in painting for creating the illusion of light, depth, and atmosphere.

Warm colors (reds, oranges, yellows) appear to advance. Cool colors (blues, blue-greens, blue-violets) appear to recede. A sunlit surface is warm. A shadow is cool (lit by the blue sky, not by the warm sun). A distant mountain is cooler and bluer than a nearby one. Temperature contrast is how painters create space.

### Step by Step

For each primary color, paint a temperature string — a row of five swatches transitioning from the warmest version to the coolest version of that color.

**Red Temperature String:**

1. Swatch 1: Cadmium red orange (PR108 + PO20) or cadmium scarlet — the warmest red, leaning heavily toward orange.
2. Swatch 2: Cadmium red medium (PR108) — warm but centered.
3. Swatch 3: Pyrrole red (PR254) or naphthol red — a red that sits near the middle of the warm-cool spectrum.
4. Swatch 4: Alizarin crimson (PR83) or quinacridone red — a cool red, leaning toward violet.
5. Swatch 5: Quinacridone magenta (PR122) or permanent rose — the coolest red, almost violet.

Notice how the warm reds seem to come forward and the cool reds seem to recede, even though they are all painted flat on the same surface. This is temperature-driven spatial illusion.

**Blue Temperature String:**

1. Swatch 1: Ultramarine blue (PB29) — the warmest common blue, leaning toward violet.
2. Swatch 2: Cobalt blue (PB28) — a centered, neutral blue.
3. Swatch 3: Cerulean blue (PB35) or manganese blue — a cool blue leaning toward green.
4. Swatch 4: Phthalo blue green shade (PB15:3) — a cold, intense blue.
5. Swatch 5: Prussian blue (PB27) or phthalo turquoise — the coolest blue, almost teal.

**Yellow Temperature String:**

1. Swatch 1: Cadmium yellow deep (PY35) or Indian yellow — the warmest yellow, leaning toward orange.
2. Swatch 2: Cadmium yellow medium (PY35) — centered, pure yellow.
3. Swatch 3: Cadmium yellow light (PY35) or hansa yellow medium (PY97) — a lighter, slightly cooler yellow.
4. Swatch 4: Hansa yellow light (PY3) or lemon yellow — a cool yellow, leaning toward green.
5. Swatch 5: Cadmium lemon or bismuth yellow — the coolest yellow, distinctly greenish.

### The Lesson

After completing all three strings, you understand something that no amount of reading can convey: that "red" is not a single color, it is a range. "Blue" is a range. "Yellow" is a range. And the temperature within that range is as important as the hue itself.

When you paint a sunlit face, the warm planes (forehead, cheekbones, nose bridge) demand warm reds — cadmium red, pyrrole red. The cool planes (temples, under the chin, the shadow side of the nose) demand cool reds — alizarin, quinacridone. The face is all "red," but the temperature shifts within that red family create the modeling, the turn of the form, the sense of three-dimensional light falling on living flesh.

## Exercise 5: Limited Palette Landscape

### What This Teaches

That you can paint a complete, convincing, full-color landscape with three colors plus white. This exercise proves that color mixing skill — not a large collection of paint tubes — is what produces rich, harmonious color in a painting.

### The Palette

- **Yellow Ochre (PY42)** — The sunlight, the warm ground, the dry grass.
- **Ultramarine Blue (PB29)** — The sky, the distant hills, the cool shadows.
- **Burnt Sienna (PBr7)** — The earth, the tree trunks, the warm shadows.
- **Titanium White (PW6)** — For tinting (oil/acrylic). In watercolor, the paper is your white.

Three pigments. That is all. No green tube. No purple tube. No black tube.

### Step by Step

1. **Choose your subject.** A simple landscape: sky, distant hill or treeline, middle-ground field or meadow, foreground. Work from a photograph or from life. Choose something with clear light-dark separation and not too much detail.

2. **Mix your greens.** Ultramarine + yellow ochre makes a muted, olive-khaki green. This is not a vivid green — it is the green of real trees in real light, not the green of a crayon. Add white to lighten it for sunlit foliage. Add more ultramarine for shadowed foliage. Add burnt sienna for autumn-tinged foliage. You will be amazed at the range of greens available from just these two colors.

3. **Mix your sky.** Ultramarine + white for the zenith (darker, warmer blue at the top of the sky). Add more white and a touch of yellow ochre as you descend toward the horizon (lighter, slightly warmer — the sky is always lighter and warmer near the horizon due to atmospheric scattering).

4. **Mix your darks.** Ultramarine + burnt sienna in roughly equal proportions makes a deep, dark tone that functions as black but has more life than tube black — it can lean warm (more sienna) or cool (more ultramarine). This mixture is the single most useful dark in landscape painting.

5. **Mix your lights.** Yellow ochre + white + a touch of burnt sienna for warm sunlit ground. Yellow ochre + white + a touch of ultramarine for cool sunlit areas.

6. **Paint the landscape.** Start with the sky (largest area, most distant). Paint the distant hills (muted, cool — more ultramarine). Paint the middle ground (warmer, more contrast). Paint the foreground (warmest, most detailed, strongest darks and lights). This back-to-front sequence is standard landscape procedure.

7. **Step back and evaluate.** Does it look like a complete painting with full color? It should. Three pigments, all from the earth — ochre, sienna, ultramarine — produce a color range that matches the natural world because these pigments literally come from the natural world. Earth pigments in earth-tone paintings. The color harmony is built in.

### Why This Works

Color harmony happens automatically when you limit your palette. Every green in the painting contains yellow ochre and ultramarine — the same two pigments that appear in the sky, the distant hills, and the ground. Every warm tone contains yellow ochre or burnt sienna — the same pigments that appear in the shadows and the darks. Because every color in the painting shares pigment DNA, the colors relate to each other naturally. There are no jarring notes, no colors that look like they belong in a different painting.

This is why the limited palette is not a limitation. It is a strategy. Professional painters who own a hundred tubes of paint often choose to paint with three or four, not because they cannot afford more, but because the color harmony of a limited palette is more beautiful than the color chaos of an unlimited one.

## Tinting Strength — The Hidden Variable

One concept that trips up every beginner is tinting strength — the ability of a pigment to affect a mixture relative to its volume.

Phthalo blue (PB15) has roughly ten times the tinting strength of yellow ochre (PY42). This means that if you put equal blobs of phthalo blue and yellow ochre on your palette and mix them together, you will not get a middle-green. You will get a slightly yellowish blue. To get a true middle-green, you need approximately ten parts ochre to one part phthalo.

This surprises people because they expect color mixing to work like arithmetic: equal amounts of blue and yellow should make green. And they do — but "equal amounts" means equal tinting strength, not equal volume. A tiny speck of phthalo blue has the same tinting power as a large blob of ochre.

### High Tinting Strength (use sparingly)

- All phthalo colors (phthalo blue PB15, phthalo green PG7) — extremely powerful
- Dioxazine purple (PV23) — violently strong, stains everything
- Carbon black (PBk7) — small amounts dominate mixtures
- Quinacridone magenta (PR122) — transparent but potent

### Low Tinting Strength (use generously)

- Yellow ochre (PY42) — gentle, needs volume to hold its own
- Cerulean blue (PB35) — weak tinter, thick and opaque
- Naples yellow (PBr24 or PY41) — very weak
- Raw sienna (PBr7) — gentle

### The Practical Rule

When mixing a strong pigment with a weak one, always start with the weaker pigment on your palette and add the stronger one in tiny increments. Start with a mound of ochre and add a speck of phthalo. Never the reverse — you will use your entire tube of ochre trying to neutralize a blob of phthalo.

This applies to white as well. Titanium white has moderate tinting strength — enough to overpower transparent colors quickly. When mixing a tint (any color + white), start with white and add the color. This gives you control over the final value. Starting with color and adding white leads to using far too much white before you reach the value you want.

## Building Your Mixing Confidence

These five exercises, done honestly with your own paints, give you a practical understanding of color that no amount of reading or video watching can replace. Your color wheel tells you what your primaries actually produce. Your value scale tells you where any mixture falls on the light-to-dark spectrum. Your complementary strips tell you how to neutralize and mute any color. Your temperature strings tell you the warm-cool range within each hue. Your limited palette landscape tells you that three colors are enough.

After completing all five exercises, you will notice something: you stop reaching for tubes and start reaching for the palette. You stop looking for the "right" premixed color and start mixing what you need from what you have. This shift — from paint consumer to paint mixer — is the moment color stops being a mystery and becomes a tool.

Keep your exercise sheets. Pin them to the wall near your painting station. Reference them constantly. When you buy a new pigment, repeat exercises 1 and 3 to integrate it into your understanding. When you cannot figure out why a passage in your painting looks wrong, hold your value scale next to it. When a color mixture goes muddy, check it against your complementary strips — you probably mixed near-complements by accident.

Color mixing is a physical skill, like throwing a ball or playing a chord on a guitar. It improves with repetition. Every time you mix a color, you are training your eye and your hand. The exercises in this chapter are the starting drills. The paintings you make afterward are the practice. There is no shortcut, and there does not need to be. The mixing itself is one of the great pleasures of painting.
