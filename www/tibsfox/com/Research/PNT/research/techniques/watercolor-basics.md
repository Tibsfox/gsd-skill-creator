# Watercolor for Beginners — Water, Pigment, Paper

## The Medium That Paints With Light

Watercolor is the only major painting medium where your lightest value is not paint. It is the paper itself. Every other medium — oil, acrylic, gouache, pastel — allows you to add white or light color on top of dark. Watercolor works in reverse. You start with the lightest possible surface (white paper) and build toward darkness by layering transparent washes. The light in a watercolor painting is not painted. It is preserved. It is the paper shining through the pigment, and it gives watercolor a luminosity that no other medium can match.

This fundamental difference changes everything about how you think while painting. In oil or acrylic, you can always go lighter — squeeze out some titanium white and paint over your mistake. In watercolor, once you lose the white of the paper, it is extremely difficult to get it back. This means you must plan your lights before you touch the brush to paper. It means you must think in reverse. It means you must be brave enough to leave untouched paper in a painting and trust that it reads as light.

This is why beginners find watercolor humbling. It is also why experienced watercolorists find it exhilarating. The medium rewards confidence, punishes overworking, and produces results that look effortless precisely because they require so much deliberate restraint.

## The Three Fundamental Washes

Every watercolor painting is built from some combination of three basic washes. Master these three and you can paint anything. Skip them and you will spend years fighting the medium instead of working with it.

### The Flat Wash

A flat wash is a single, even tone applied over an area. It sounds simple. It is not. Laying down a perfectly even, streak-free wash is harder than it looks, and it is the first skill you should practice.

**Step by step:**

1. Mix a generous puddle of paint on your palette — far more than you think you need. Running out of paint mid-wash is the most common beginner disaster. For a half-sheet painting (15 x 22 inches), mix at least a tablespoon of liquid.
2. Tilt your board 15 to 20 degrees from horizontal. This lets gravity pull the paint downward in a controlled bead.
3. Load your largest round brush (a size 12 or 14) or a flat wash brush (1 to 1.5 inches wide) fully with paint.
4. Starting at the top of your wash area, draw the brush across in a single horizontal stroke. You should see a bead of paint accumulating at the bottom edge of the stroke.
5. Reload the brush, then overlap slightly with the bottom of the previous stroke, picking up the bead and carrying it across again.
6. Repeat until you reach the bottom of the wash area. The bead should remain consistent along the bottom edge of each stroke.
7. At the final stroke, squeeze your brush dry and wick up the remaining bead. If you leave it, it will dry as a dark line (a "bloom" or "backrun").

The goal is a perfectly even tone from top to bottom. Your first attempts will have streaks, hard edges, and blooms. That is normal. Practice flat washes on scrap paper before you attempt them in a painting. Ten minutes a day for a week will transform your wash technique.

### The Graded Wash

A graded wash transitions smoothly from dark to light (or from one intensity to another). This is how you paint skies, the curve of a vase, the recession of a distant hillside — any gradual tonal transition.

**Step by step:**

1. Mix a rich puddle of paint as before.
2. Tilt your board 15 to 20 degrees.
3. Lay the first stroke exactly as in a flat wash — full strength.
4. Before reloading, dip just the tip of the brush in clean water. This slightly dilutes the paint in the brush.
5. Lay the second stroke, overlapping the bead from the first.
6. Continue, adding a little more water to the brush before each stroke. The paint progressively lightens.
7. By the final strokes, you should be applying almost pure water, blending the wash out to the white of the paper.
8. Wick up the remaining bead.

The key is consistency. Each stroke should be only slightly lighter than the one above. Abrupt jumps in value create visible bands. If you are getting bands, you are adding too much water between strokes. Slow down and add less.

### The Variegated Wash

A variegated wash transitions from one color to another — blue fading into orange, green blending into gold, warm gray cooling into blue-gray. This is watercolor at its most magical and the technique that makes the medium unique.

**Step by step:**

1. Pre-mix both colors in separate puddles on your palette. Both should be at the same consistency.
2. Tilt your board.
3. Begin the wash with Color A, laying strokes exactly as in a flat wash.
4. After two or three strokes, begin loading the brush with a mixture of Color A and Color B. The transition is happening in the brush, not on the paper.
5. Progressively shift the ratio until you are painting with pure Color B.
6. Continue with Color B to the bottom of the wash. Wick the bead.

The magic happens where the two colors meet. Watercolor pigments do not mix the way opaque paints do — they mingle, they granulate, they push and pull in the water. A variegated wash of ultramarine blue into burnt sienna produces effects that you could never achieve by pre-mixing the two colors on the palette. The transition zone is alive in a way that mixed paint is not.

## Wet-in-Wet vs. Wet-on-Dry

These are the two fundamental approaches to applying watercolor, and every painting uses both.

### Wet-in-Wet

Wet-in-wet means applying wet paint to wet paper (or to a wet wash that has not yet dried). The result is soft, diffused edges — paint spreads and mingles freely because the water on the paper carries the pigment outward. This is the technique for soft skies, atmospheric fog, reflections in water, blurred backgrounds, and any passage where you want edges to disappear.

**How wet is wet?** This matters enormously. There is a spectrum:

- **Soaking wet** (paper glistens, standing water visible): paint spreads rapidly and uncontrollably. Hard to manage. Best for abstract effects or large atmospheric washes.
- **Damp** (paper has a satin sheen, no standing water): paint spreads moderately. The sweet spot for most wet-in-wet work. Edges are soft but controllable.
- **Just damp** (paper looks matte but feels cool to the touch on the back): paint barely spreads. Edges are slightly soft — a beautiful transitional state that lasts only seconds.

Timing is everything. You are painting during a window that opens when you wet the paper and closes as it dries. Work too soon (paper too wet) and the paint diffuses into an uncontrollable wash. Work too late (paper too dry) and the paint sits on top with hard edges. The damp-sheen stage is where the control lives, and learning to recognize it by sight is one of the most important skills in watercolor.

### Wet-on-Dry

Wet-on-dry means applying wet paint to dry paper (or to a previously dried layer). The result is crisp, defined edges. This is the technique for details, sharp edges, linear elements, branches against the sky, the hard edge of a building, calligraphic brushstrokes, and anything that requires precision.

Most watercolor paintings alternate between wet-in-wet and wet-on-dry passages. You lay a wet-in-wet wash for a soft sky, let it dry completely, then paint trees with crisp wet-on-dry strokes on top. The contrast between soft-edged and hard-edged passages is one of the things that makes watercolor visually dynamic. A painting that is entirely wet-in-wet looks vague. A painting that is entirely wet-on-dry looks stiff. The interplay between the two is what sings.

## Why Paper Matters More Than Paint

The single most important investment in watercolor painting is paper. Not paint. Not brushes. Paper. Cheap paper will ruin your experience. Good paper will make watercolor feel like the medium it is supposed to be.

### The Problem with Cheap Paper

Student-grade watercolor paper (90 lb / 190 gsm or thinner) buckles violently when wet, creating hills and valleys that pool paint in unpredictable ways. It pills — the surface disintegrates under repeated brushwork, rolling up into ugly little balls of fiber. It does not hold a wash — the sizing (the starch or gelatin that controls absorption) is inconsistent or absent, so paint sinks unevenly into the fiber instead of sitting on the surface where you can control it. Cheap paper makes watercolor feel impossible. It is not the medium's fault. It is the paper's fault.

### What Good Paper Does

Professional-grade watercolor paper (140 lb / 300 gsm cold press is the standard) stays relatively flat when wet (and can be stretched or taped to stay completely flat). It does not pill. It has consistent internal and surface sizing that allows paint to sit on the surface, giving you time to manipulate it. It allows you to lift paint (rewet and blot to reclaim lost lights). It survives multiple layers without damage. It dries evenly. It makes watercolor feel controllable and rewarding.

### Specifications That Matter

**Weight:** 140 lb (300 gsm) is the minimum for serious work. This is heavy enough to resist buckling without stretching. 90 lb paper must be stretched (soaked, taped to a board, dried flat) before use, which adds a step that discourages beginners. 300 lb (640 gsm) paper lies perfectly flat without any stretching — it is essentially a board — but it costs significantly more.

**Surface texture:**
- **Cold press (NOT)** is the standard — a moderately textured surface that holds washes, allows detail, and provides the characteristic watercolor tooth. This is what you should start with.
- **Hot press (HP)** is smooth. Paint slides and pools unpredictably. Beautiful for detailed work and illustration once you understand the medium, but harder for beginners.
- **Rough** has pronounced texture. Excellent for dry-brush effects and landscape work where you want the texture of the paper to show through.

**Fiber:** The best watercolor papers are 100% cotton rag (not wood pulp). Cotton fibers are longer and more absorbent than wood pulp, producing a surface that handles water gracefully and does not yellow with age. Arches (French, machine-made), Fabriano Artistico (Italian, machine-made), and Saunders Waterford (British, mould-made) are the three benchmark cotton papers. All are excellent. Arches is the most widely available in North America and the standard against which all other papers are measured.

**The bottom line:** Buy Arches 140 lb cold press paper. Blocks (paper glued on all four edges to resist buckling) are convenient. Sheets (22 x 30 inches) are more economical. A pad of 12 sheets in 9 x 12 inch format costs about $15-20 and will transform your painting experience compared to student-grade paper.

## A Starter Palette — Six Colors That Do Everything

You do not need 24 colors. You need six. A limited palette forces you to learn color mixing, which is the foundation of all painting. It also produces color harmony automatically — because every mixture in your painting shares the same small family of pigments, the colors relate naturally to each other.

### The Six

1. **Ultramarine Blue (PB29)** — A warm, slightly reddish blue with beautiful granulation. The backbone of landscapes and shadows. Mixes with burnt sienna to make the most useful gray in painting.

2. **Burnt Sienna (PBr7)** — A warm, transparent reddish-brown earth. The workhorse of watercolor palettes. Mixed with ultramarine, it makes grays. Mixed with yellow ochre, it makes warm golds. On its own, it handles tree trunks, earth, skin tones, and shadows.

3. **Yellow Ochre (PY42)** — A warm, opaque yellow earth. Not a bright yellow — a subtle, natural gold that appears everywhere in the landscape. Sunlit fields, sandy soil, warm highlights, autumn leaves beginning to turn. Its opacity makes it useful for warm undertones.

4. **Alizarin Crimson (PR83)** — A cool, transparent red that leans toward purple. Essential for mixing purples (alizarin + ultramarine = deep violet) and for giving warmth to shadows. Note: traditional alizarin crimson (PR83) has moderate lightfastness. Permanent alizarin crimson substitutes (often PR206, PV19, or PR264) are more lightfast and recommended for work intended to last. Winsor & Newton Permanent Alizarin Crimson and Daniel Smith Alizarin Crimson (Quinacridone) are both excellent.

5. **Sap Green (PG36 + PY150, varies by brand)** — A convenience green that saves you from mixing every green from scratch. Most sap greens are warm and natural-looking. It can be cooled with ultramarine, warmed with yellow ochre, or grayed with burnt sienna. Having one pre-mixed green on the palette speeds up landscape painting enormously.

6. **Payne's Gray (PBk6 + PB15, varies by brand)** — A blue-gray that functions as a dark neutral. Use it for darkest darks, cool shadows, atmospheric depth. Some painters use ivory black instead, but Payne's gray is more versatile because its blue undertone stays luminous in washes. It is also useful as a mixing color — add it to any color to darken and cool it simultaneously.

### Why These Six?

Between them, these six pigments can mix virtually any color you encounter in nature. You can mix bright greens (ultramarine + yellow ochre), warm oranges (burnt sienna + yellow ochre), subtle purples (alizarin + ultramarine), cool grays (ultramarine + burnt sienna), warm grays (Payne's gray + burnt sienna), deep darks (Payne's gray + alizarin), and every nuance in between. Once you are comfortable mixing with six, you can add colors one at a time — a bright cadmium yellow, a phthalo blue, a quinacridone rose — each time understanding exactly what the new color adds to your range.

## Brushes — The Size 8 Round Does 80% of the Work

You need far fewer brushes than you think. A single good round brush in size 8 will handle the vast majority of watercolor work — broad washes with the belly, fine lines with the tip, dry-brush texture with a splayed brush dragged sideways.

### Essential Brushes

- **Size 8 round** — Your primary brush. A good one holds a generous amount of water in its belly and springs back to a sharp point. Kolinsky sable (Winsor & Newton Series 7 is the benchmark) is the gold standard but costs $40-80 for a single brush. Synthetic alternatives (Princeton Neptune, Escoda Versatil, Da Vinci Cosmotop Spin) have improved dramatically and cost $10-20. A good synthetic round is more than adequate for learning.

- **Size 2 or 4 round** — For details and fine lines. Same criteria: good point, adequate water capacity.

- **1-inch flat wash brush** — For laying large washes quickly. A cheap hake brush (wide, flat, soft) works for this purpose and costs $5-8.

- **Old toothbrush** — For spattering texture (flick the bristles loaded with paint). Not a joke. This is a legitimate watercolor technique.

That is it. Four brushes. You can paint a complete watercolor with just the size 8 round and the wash brush. Add the detail round and the toothbrush, and there is no technique you cannot execute.

## Essential Exercises

### Exercise 1: Flat Wash

Cut a 6 x 8 inch piece of good watercolor paper. Mix a large puddle of ultramarine blue at medium intensity. Lay a flat wash over the entire sheet following the steps described above. Do this five times. Your fifth wash should be noticeably smoother than your first.

### Exercise 2: Graded Wash

Same setup. Lay a graded wash from full-strength ultramarine at the top to pure white paper at the bottom. The transition should be smooth and even, with no visible banding. Do this five times.

### Exercise 3: Wet-in-Wet Bloom

Wet an area of paper with clean water (use the wash brush). While the paper is at damp-sheen stage, drop in a loaded brushstroke of ultramarine blue and watch it bloom outward. Then drop in burnt sienna nearby and watch the two colors mingle. Do not touch it. Let the water carry the pigment. This exercise teaches you to trust the medium and to recognize the damp-sheen stage.

Repeat at different wetness levels — soaking wet, damp sheen, barely damp — and observe how the bloom changes character at each stage. This is the most important exercise for understanding wet-in-wet control.

### Exercise 4: Dry Brush Texture

Load your size 8 round with thick paint (very little water), then splay the bristles by pressing them against your palette and dragging sideways across dry paper. The brush skips over the textured surface, leaving broken marks that suggest bark, rock, distant foliage, rough fabric. Practice this on cold press and rough paper. The texture of the paper does half the work.

### Exercise 5: Value Study

Using only Payne's gray, paint a simple still life (a cup, an apple, a box) in five values: white paper, light gray, medium gray, dark gray, and near-black. This exercise forces you to see values rather than colors. Value is the skeleton of every painting. If the values are right, the painting works even without color. If the values are wrong, no amount of color will save it.

## The White of the Paper — Your Lightest Value

This point deserves its own section because it is the hardest concept for beginners coming from opaque media.

In watercolor, you do not paint white. You save it. Every highlight, every glint on water, every sunlit edge, every white flower petal — these are areas where you deliberately avoided putting paint. This requires planning. Before you start a painting, identify your lightest areas and make a mental (or pencil) note to protect them.

Techniques for preserving whites:

- **Just painting around them.** This is the purest approach and the one that produces the most luminous results. It requires confidence and planning.
- **Masking fluid (liquid frisket).** A latex solution applied to dry paper before painting. It resists watercolor. Once your wash is dry, you rub off the mask to reveal the white paper beneath. Useful for complex white shapes (tree branches against sky, scattered highlights on water). Apply with a cheap brush or a ruling pen — masking fluid will destroy good brushes. Remove it gently with a rubber cement eraser or your fingertip. Do not leave it on for more than a few hours, as it bonds permanently to the paper.
- **Wax resist.** A white crayon or candle rubbed on dry paper resists watercolor. The effect is more textured and organic than masking fluid. Permanent — wax cannot be removed.

## Lifting and Blotting

Watercolor is not entirely unforgiving. You can reclaim some lost lights by lifting — rewetting a dry area with a clean, damp brush and then blotting with a tissue or paper towel. Staining pigments (phthalo blue, alizarin crimson, sap green) resist lifting. Non-staining pigments (ultramarine, burnt sienna, yellow ochre) lift relatively cleanly. This is one reason the recommended starter palette leans toward non-staining earth pigments — they are more forgiving.

Blotting a wet wash with a crumpled tissue creates cloud textures. Pressing a flat tissue into a wet wash creates soft-edged highlights. These are not corrections — they are techniques in their own right. Many watercolorists plan their blotting as deliberately as their brushstrokes.

## Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

### Overworking

The number one watercolor killer. Once a wash is applied, leave it alone. Do not go back into a drying wash to fix something — your brush will displace settling pigment, creating ugly hard-edged blooms ("cauliflowers" or "backruns"). If a wash is not right, either let it dry completely and glaze over it, or wash it off under running water (this works on good paper). The rule: decide, commit, leave it.

### Mud — Too Many Layers, Too Many Pigments

Watercolor transparency works because light passes through pigment, bounces off white paper, and returns to your eye. Each layer absorbs some of that light. Stack too many layers and the result is dull, opaque mud. Limit yourself to two or three layers maximum. Mix color on the palette rather than layering different colors on the paper. Mixing more than three pigments in a single wash almost always produces mud — this is why the limited palette approach works so well.

### Fear of Going Dark Enough

Beginners consistently paint too light. Watercolor dries lighter than it looks wet — your boldest strokes lose 10-20% of their value as they dry. Push your darks. Hold your painting next to something black. If you can see a clear difference in your darkest passage, you have not gone dark enough.

### Not Enough Water

Beginners use paint that is too thick, applied to paper that is too dry, with a brush that is not wet enough. Use more water than you think you need. Let the paint flow. Trust the medium.

## The Convention Badge Connection

Watercolor is the number one badge medium at furry conventions, and for good reason. Convention badge commissions — small (2.5 x 3.5 inch trading-card-sized) character portraits on heavy card stock — play directly to watercolor's strengths: vibrant transparent color on white paper, small format that can be completed quickly (15-30 minutes per badge for an experienced artist), minimal setup (a travel palette, two brushes, a cup of water), and a hand-painted look that distinguishes the badge from a printed digital image.

The standard workflow: pencil the character design on Strathmore 500 Bristol (plate or vellum surface) or a pre-cut badge blank. Lay in base colors with dilute washes. Build up shadows and details wet-on-dry with progressively less dilute paint. Preserve highlights on the eyes and nose as white paper. Add final line work with a size 0 or 1 round brush in black or dark brown. Seal with a UV-protective spray fixative. Slot into a badge holder, clip to a lanyard.

Convention badge painting is an excellent entry point to watercolor because the small format is forgiving (a ruined badge costs 15 minutes, not an afternoon), the subject matter is engaging (you are painting characters you or your commissioner care about), and the feedback loop is immediate (the commissioner is standing right there, watching, often delighted). Many professional watercolorists trace their origins to a convention badge table.

## Getting Started — What to Buy Today

If you are reading this and want to start painting this week, here is the minimum viable purchase:

1. **Paper:** Arches 140 lb cold press watercolor block, 9 x 12 inches. About $15-20. This is non-negotiable. Do not substitute with cheap paper.
2. **Paint:** Winsor & Newton Cotman (student grade) or Van Gogh (student grade) watercolor set in the six colors listed above, or as close as available. A 12-half-pan set costs $15-25 and includes most of these colors. Alternatively, buy six individual tubes of artist-grade paint (Daniel Smith or Winsor & Newton Professional) for $6-10 each — the tubes last far longer and the paint quality is noticeably better.
3. **Brushes:** One size 8 round (Princeton Neptune 4750 is excellent value at $12), one cheap 1-inch flat brush ($5).
4. **Other:** A white dinner plate or ceramic palette ($0 to $5), two cups of water (one for rinsing, one for clean water), a roll of paper towels, a pencil, and a piece of board to tape or clip the paper to.

Total investment: $40-60. You can start painting today.

You learn by painting — not by reading about painting, not by watching videos. Your first paintings will be bad. Your twentieth will be less bad. Your fiftieth will surprise you. Start with flat washes, work through the exercises, paint small and paint often. The medium will teach you, if you let it.
