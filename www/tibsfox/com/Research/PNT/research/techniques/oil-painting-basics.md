# Oil Painting for Beginners — Fat Over Lean

## The Medium That Built Western Art

Oil paint is the reason we have the paintings we have. From the Van Eycks in 15th-century Flanders through Rembrandt, Vermeer, the Impressionists, the Abstract Expressionists, and every portrait and landscape that hangs in every major museum in the Western world — oil paint made those possible. Its slow drying time allows blending that no other medium can match. Its rich, buttery consistency makes every brushstroke feel purposeful. Its archival permanence means that a well-made oil painting will outlast the painter, the building it hangs in, and likely the civilization that built the building.

Oil painting has a reputation for being difficult, messy, and toxic. The first two are somewhat true. The third is manageable with modern materials and basic precautions. What oil painting actually requires is patience — patience to set up properly, patience to let the slow drying time work for you instead of against you, and patience to learn the one fundamental rule that governs everything: fat over lean.

## Setting Up Your Workspace

Before you touch a brush, you need a workspace that is organized, well-ventilated, and equipped with the right materials. Oil painting setup is more involved than watercolor or acrylic, but once your station is established, it stays the same every session.

### The Palette

You mix oil paint on a palette — a flat, non-porous surface large enough to hold your paint squeezed from tubes around the edges and a generous mixing area in the center.

**Disposable palette pads** (Gray Matters by New Wave, or any brand of disposable gray or white palette paper) are the easiest option for beginners. Tear off the top sheet when you are done painting, throw it away. No cleanup. The gray surface helps you judge color values more accurately than a white surface (which makes paint appear darker than it will look on canvas).

**Glass palette** (a piece of tempered glass from a picture frame, placed over a gray sheet of paper or cardboard) is the professional standard. The smooth surface is ideal for mixing. Cleanup is easy — let the paint dry, then scrape it off with a razor blade. A 16 x 20 inch piece of glass is the minimum useful size. Tape the edges for safety.

**Wooden palettes** are traditional and beautiful but require seasoning with oil before first use and regular maintenance. Most beginners find glass or disposable pads more practical.

### Solvents and Mediums

**Gamsol (Gamblin Odorless Mineral Spirits)** is the standard solvent for thinning paint and cleaning brushes during a painting session. It is the most refined OMS on the market — low aromatic content, minimal odor, lower toxicity than turpentine. You need a small jar (4-8 ounces) at your painting station. A lidded jar (the Silicoil brush-cleaning jar is excellent — a steel coil at the bottom scrubs paint off the brush, and the lid seals between sessions) keeps the solvent from evaporating.

**Linseed oil** is the standard medium for adjusting paint consistency and increasing fat content as you build up layers. You can use it straight from the bottle. A small dipping cup (clip it to the edge of your palette) is standard. Refined linseed oil is clearer and yellows less than cold-pressed linseed oil. Gamblin Refined Linseed Oil is widely available.

**Liquin Original (Winsor & Newton)** is an alkyd medium that speeds drying time (touch-dry in 1-2 days instead of 3-7) and adds a smooth, flowing consistency. Many contemporary painters use Liquin instead of or in addition to linseed oil. It counts as "fat" for fat-over-lean purposes.

For your first session, Gamsol and refined linseed oil are sufficient. Add Liquin later if you find yourself frustrated by drying times.

### Brushes

Four brushes will handle everything for a beginner:

1. **Small round (size 2-4)** — For details, signatures, fine lines. A filbert (rounded flat) also works here.
2. **Medium round (size 6-8)** — Your most-used brush. Blocking in, shaping forms, general work.
3. **Medium flat (size 6-8, 1/2 to 3/4 inch)** — For broad strokes, backgrounds, blocking in large areas. Flats lay down a lot of paint quickly and produce characteristic squared-off strokes.
4. **Large flat (size 10-12, 1 inch)** — For covering large areas, toning the canvas, and applying the initial block-in.

**Natural bristle (hog)** brushes are the traditional choice for oil painting. They are stiff enough to push thick paint around and leave visible brushstrokes that are part of the painting's texture. Winsor & Newton Artisan or Princeton Snap are good budget options ($5-8 each). Robert Simmons Signet is a step up.

**Synthetic brushes** work well with oil paint thinned to a fluid consistency but lack the stiffness for heavy impasto. Use them for detail work and smooth blending.

**Palette knives** — Buy one. A small diamond-shaped painting knife ($5-8) is used for mixing paint on the palette (saves wear on your brushes) and for applying thick paint directly to the canvas (a technique called palette knife painting or impasto). Even if you never paint with a knife, you will use it for mixing.

## Fat Over Lean — The One Rule You Cannot Break

This is the single most important concept in oil painting. Every technical decision you make — what medium to add, how thin to mix the paint, how to build up layers — flows from this rule.

**Fat over lean means: each successive layer of paint should contain more oil than the layer beneath it.**

Here is why. Oil paint dries by absorbing oxygen from the air and undergoing a chemical reaction (oxidative polymerization) that turns the liquid oil into a solid film. Lean paint (less oil, more solvent or pigment) dries faster because there is less oil to polymerize. Fat paint (more oil) dries slower.

If you put lean paint over fat paint, the lean top layer dries and becomes rigid while the fat bottom layer is still flexible, still moving, still polymerizing. The rigid top layer cannot accommodate the movement of the flexible bottom layer. The result: cracking. Sometimes within months. Sometimes it takes years. But it always happens. This is paint chemistry, not opinion. Every cracked old painting in every museum was probably the result of a violation of fat-over-lean (or a contaminated ground, or both).

**In practice, this is simple:**

- **Layer 1 (the block-in):** Thin your paint with a small amount of Gamsol (solvent only, no oil). This is the leanest layer. It dries fastest.
- **Layer 2 (building form):** Use paint straight from the tube, or thinned slightly with a mixture of solvent and a tiny amount of linseed oil. Slightly fatter than layer 1.
- **Layer 3 and beyond (details, thick paint, final layers):** Add linseed oil or Liquin to your paint. Each layer is progressively fatter. The final impasto passages are the fattest.

For a painting completed in one session (alla prima), fat-over-lean is largely irrelevant because all the paint dries together. It matters when you paint in multiple sessions over multiple days with distinct dry layers.

## Alla Prima — The Beginner's Best Friend

Alla prima (Italian for "at the first") means completing a painting in a single session while the paint is still wet. This is wet-on-wet oil painting. You lay down paint, blend into it, adjust colors, add details, and finish before anything dries. Because all the paint is wet simultaneously, fat-over-lean does not apply — there are no separate dry layers.

Alla prima is the best approach for beginners for several reasons:

1. **No waiting.** You start and finish in one sitting (2-4 hours is typical).
2. **No fat-over-lean decisions.** Everything is one wet layer.
3. **Beautiful blending.** Oil paint's slow drying time is your ally. You can push colors into each other, soften edges, and make adjustments for hours.
4. **Immediate results.** You walk away with a finished (or nearly finished) painting, not an unresolved underpainting that needs three more sessions.

The Impressionists — Monet, Renoir, Pissarro — painted almost entirely alla prima, often outdoors (en plein air) in a single session. Their work demonstrates that alla prima is not a shortcut or a compromise. It is a complete and legitimate painting method that produces some of the most vibrant and lively paintings in the history of art.

## Your First Exercise — Paint an Apple on Cloth

This is the classic first oil painting exercise because it contains every fundamental challenge in miniature: a sphere with a highlight, a midtone, a shadow, a cast shadow, and reflected light; a simple background; and a cloth surface with soft folds.

### Materials

- A small canvas or canvas panel (8 x 10 inches or 9 x 12 inches)
- The Zorn palette (see below): yellow ochre, cadmium red, ivory black, titanium white
- Four brushes
- Gamsol and linseed oil
- A real apple and a piece of cloth, arranged on a table with a single light source (desk lamp) from one side

### The Zorn Palette

Named after Anders Zorn (1860-1920), a Swedish painter who demonstrated that four colors are sufficient for full-color painting. The palette:

- **Yellow Ochre (PY42)** — A warm, muted yellow earth. The sunlit side of things.
- **Cadmium Red Medium (PR108)** — A warm, opaque red. Mixes with ochre for oranges, with black for deep earth tones. (Note: cadmium is toxic if ingested. Wear nitrile gloves if you have cuts on your hands, and wash hands before eating.)
- **Ivory Black (PBk9)** — A warm black that, when mixed with white, produces blue-gray tones. This is the Zorn palette's secret: ivory black + titanium white = a cool blue-gray that functions as the blue in your palette. You do not need a tube of blue.
- **Titanium White (PW6)** — Opaque, strong tinting power. You will use more white than any other color.

These four colors, mixed in various proportions, produce a full range of skin tones, earth tones, greens (black + ochre), grays, and warm/cool contrasts. It is a complete palette in four tubes. Total cost: $20-35 for artist-grade.

### Step by Step

1. **Tone the canvas.** Thin some yellow ochre with Gamsol to a milky consistency. Scrub it over the entire canvas with a large flat brush. Wipe off the excess with a paper towel. You should have a warm, transparent tint — not white, not opaque. This toned ground eliminates the intimidating white canvas and provides a warm middle value to paint on.

2. **Draw the composition.** Using a small round brush and thin ivory black (thinned with Gamsol), draw the apple and cloth as simple shapes. Circle for the apple, loose curves for the cloth folds. Keep it loose — this is a guide, not a finished drawing.

3. **Block in the shadows.** Identify the shadow side of the apple, the cast shadow on the cloth, and the darkest folds in the cloth. Block these in with a mixture of ivory black + cadmium red (makes a warm dark brown). Use the medium flat brush. Keep the paint thin. Do not worry about blending. You are establishing the dark pattern of the composition.

4. **Block in the midtones.** Mix yellow ochre + a small amount of cadmium red + a small amount of white for the apple's lit midtone. Mix ivory black + white + a touch of ochre for the cloth midtones. Apply with the medium round or flat. These should be opaque enough to cover the toned ground. You are now painting with paint straight from the tube — slightly fatter than the thinned shadows (fat over lean, even in alla prima).

5. **Add the light side.** Mix a lighter value: more white, more ochre, for the brightest part of the apple. Place it confidently. Do not blend yet.

6. **Blend edges.** Now — with a clean, dry brush or a soft brush — gently soften the transition between shadow and light on the apple. The edge should turn, not jump. Leave the cast shadow edge relatively hard (cast shadows have crisp edges near the object and softer edges as they recede).

7. **Highlight.** Mix pure titanium white with a tiny amount of yellow ochre. Place a small highlight where the light source reflects off the apple's skin. One confident stroke. Do not fiddle with it.

8. **Refine.** Step back. Squint. Are the values right? Is the shadow dark enough? Is the background receding? Adjust. Add the stem. Suggest the cloth texture with a few calligraphic strokes.

Total time: 1-2 hours. This exercise teaches you value structure (three values: light, middle, dark), color mixing from a limited palette, brush handling with oil paint, and the alla prima workflow. Paint it five times with five different apples, and you will understand oil painting well enough to tackle any subject.

## Cleaning Up Safely

This section is critical. Oil painting cleanup involves solvents, which are toxic to the environment and to you if handled carelessly.

### During the Session

- Wipe excess paint from brushes on a paper towel before rinsing in solvent. This reduces the amount of pigment that goes into the solvent and extends the solvent's useful life.
- Use as little solvent as possible. A quarter-inch of Gamsol in a brush washer is enough.
- Keep the lid on your solvent container when you are not actively cleaning a brush. This reduces fumes and slows evaporation.

### End of Session

1. **Brushes:** Wipe off as much paint as possible with paper towels. Rinse in Gamsol, working the solvent through the bristles with your fingers. Wipe again. Then wash with brush soap (The Masters Brush Cleaner is the standard — it is a solid soap in a tin that cleans oil, acrylic, and watercolor brushes). Work the soap into the bristles at the ferrule (where the bristles meet the metal) — this is where paint hides and eventually dries, splaying the brush permanently. Rinse with warm water. Shape the brush and let it dry bristles-up in a jar.

2. **Palette:** If you are using a disposable palette, fold and discard. If using glass, you can leave the paint if you are painting again tomorrow (oil paint stays wet for days). If you are done for the week, scrape the paint off with a razor blade and wipe the glass with a paper towel dampened with Gamsol.

3. **Solvent disposal:** NEVER pour used solvent down the drain. Never pour it on the ground. Never pour it in the trash while liquid. Used Gamsol should be poured into a sealed container and set aside. The pigment settles to the bottom, and the clear solvent on top can be carefully poured off and reused. When you have accumulated sediment or truly spent solvent, let it evaporate in a well-ventilated area (outdoors or in a garage) in a shallow pan until all the liquid is gone, then dispose of the dried residue in the regular trash.

4. **Rags and paper towels:** Oil-soaked rags can spontaneously combust. This is not a myth — linseed oil generates heat as it polymerizes, and crumpled, oil-soaked rags can generate enough heat to ignite. Lay used rags flat to dry (not crumpled, not piled) or submerge them in a sealed can of water. Once dry, they are safe for regular trash. A metal trash can with a tight-fitting lid (the kind sold as "oily waste cans") is the safest option if you generate a lot of oily rags.

## Drying Times

Understanding drying times prevents frustration and helps you plan your painting sessions.

- **Touch-dry (surface dry):** 2-7 days for most colors at average room temperature and humidity. Thin paint dries faster. Thick impasto takes longer. Earth colors (ochres, siennas, umbers) dry fastest because iron oxide pigments catalyze the polymerization of the oil. Ivory black dries slowly. Titanium white dries at moderate speed. Cadmium colors dry slowly.
- **Fully cured:** 6-12 months. This is how long it takes for the oil film to fully polymerize and harden throughout its thickness. You can handle the painting, hang it, and transport it long before it is fully cured, but you should not varnish it until it has cured — varnish applied over uncured paint traps solvents and can cause problems. Six months is the standard minimum wait time before varnishing.
- **Speeding up drying:** Alkyd mediums (Liquin) reduce touch-dry time to 1-2 days. Cobalt drier (a metallic salt added to the paint) accelerates drying dramatically but must be used very sparingly — excess drier causes brittleness and cracking. Most painters prefer Liquin over drier.
- **Slowing down drying:** Clove oil added to paint slows drying (painters who work outdoors in hot, dry climates sometimes use this). Working in a cool room also slows drying.

### Between Sessions

When you stop painting for the day, you have options:

- **Leave the palette as-is** if you are painting again within a day or two. Cover the palette with plastic wrap if you want to slow the skin from forming.
- **Store mixed colors in the freezer.** Seriously. Scrape your mixed piles into a sealed container or wrap a disposable palette in plastic and put it in the freezer. The cold stops polymerization entirely. Your paint will be workable when you take it out the next day (or the next week, or the next month). This trick alone saves more paint than any other single habit.

## Health and Safety

Oil painting is not inherently dangerous, but it involves materials that require respect.

### Ventilation

Work in a well-ventilated space. This means a room with a window that opens and, ideally, a fan pulling air out. You do not need laboratory-grade ventilation for Gamsol (its vapor pressure is low), but you should not be breathing concentrated solvent fumes for hours. If you can smell the solvent strongly, you need more ventilation.

Solvent-free painting is entirely possible. Thin your initial block-in with a small amount of linseed oil instead of solvent. Clean brushes with walnut oil (works as a solvent for oil paint) followed by soap and water. Some painters have worked exclusively solvent-free for decades.

### Gloves

Wear nitrile gloves when handling cadmium (Cd) and cobalt (Co) pigments, especially if you have any cuts or broken skin. These metals are toxic if they enter the bloodstream. For routine painting with low-toxicity colors (earth colors, titanium white, most modern organic pigments), gloves are optional but never a bad idea. A box of 100 nitrile gloves costs $8-12.

### No Eating in the Studio

Do not eat, drink, or smoke while painting. Pigment dust and solvent residue on your hands transfer to food and cigarettes. Cadmium and cobalt ingestion is cumulative. Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water before touching food or your face.

### Pigment Safety Reference

- **Safe:** All earth colors (ochres, siennas, umbers), titanium white, zinc white, most modern organic pigments (pyrroles, quinacridones, phthalo, hansa).
- **Use with care:** Cadmium colors (cadmium yellow, cadmium red, cadmium orange) — toxic if ingested, harmful dust. Cobalt colors (cobalt blue, cobalt violet) — toxic if ingested. Chromium oxide green — low toxicity. Manganese colors — neurotoxic dust.
- **Avoid:** Lead white (flake white, Cremnitz white) — seriously toxic. Historical material. Some painters still use it for its unique handling properties, but beginners have no reason to use lead pigments.

## Where to Go From Here

You have the Zorn palette, four brushes, Gamsol, linseed oil, and a stack of cheap canvas panels. Paint the apple exercise five times. Then paint from life — a coffee mug, a pear, a shoe, whatever is on your desk. Small paintings (8 x 10 or smaller), alla prima, limited palette. Do ten of these.

By the tenth small painting, you will understand how oil paint moves under the brush. You will have internalized fat-over-lean without thinking about it. You will have found a rhythm: block in shadows, establish midtones, place lights, refine edges, add highlights. That rhythm is the foundation of all oil painting, from a still life on a kitchen table to a six-foot canvas.

When you are ready to move beyond alla prima, try a two-session painting: block in the entire composition thinly in session one (a "dead layer" or "umber layer" using only ivory black + white, or burnt umber + white), let it dry for several days, then paint color over the dry layer in session two. This introduces the fat-over-lean layering system in the simplest possible way — two layers, lean then fat — and connects you to the tradition of indirect painting that goes back to the Renaissance.

Oil painting rewards persistence. The medium is forgiving in ways that watercolor is not — you can scrape paint off, paint over mistakes, rework areas, and take days to finish what you started. The slow drying time that can frustrate beginners is actually a profound advantage: it gives you time to look, to think, to adjust. No other medium allows that luxury. Use it.
