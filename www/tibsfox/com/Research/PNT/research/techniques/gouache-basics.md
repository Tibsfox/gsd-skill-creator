# Gouache -- The Designer's Secret

## Opaque Watercolor and the Medium Having Its Moment

Gouache is the medium that painters keep rediscovering. Illustrators have known about it for a century. Designers have used it since the Art Nouveau era. Animation background painters built entire worlds in it. And now, driven by Instagram plein air painters, TikTok art supply videos, and a generation of artists looking for something that is not acrylic and not watercolor but somehow occupies the best territory of both, gouache is having a genuine renaissance.

The word is pronounced "gwash" (rhymes with "squash"). The paint is pigment bound in gum arabic -- the same binder as watercolor -- but with higher pigment concentration and typically the addition of a white extender (chalk or calcium carbonate) that makes it opaque. Where watercolor glows through transparency, letting the white paper provide the light, gouache sits on the surface as a matte, velvety, opaque layer. It is watercolor's bold, confident cousin.

This guide covers what gouache is, how it works, what to buy, and why it might be the medium you did not know you were looking for.

## 1. What Makes Gouache Different

Gouache occupies the space between watercolor and acrylic, sharing DNA with both but behaving like neither.

Like watercolor, gouache is water-soluble. You thin it with water. You clean it with water. You can reactivate dried gouache on your palette by adding water -- a pile of gouache that has been sitting on your palette for a week can be brought back to life with a spray bottle and a wet brush. This reactivation property means gouache has almost zero waste. The paint on your palette is never dead.

Unlike watercolor, gouache is opaque. You can paint light over dark. You can cover mistakes. You can lay down a dark background and paint bright highlights on top of it. This single property -- opacity -- removes watercolor's most intimidating constraint and opens up a working method that feels more intuitive to most beginners.

Like acrylic, gouache can be applied in flat, even layers that cover the surface completely. But unlike acrylic, gouache dries to a matte, chalky finish that is distinctly different from acrylic's slight plastic sheen. This matte finish photographs beautifully -- no glare, no hot spots, no reflections -- which is one reason gouache has historically been the medium of choice for illustration work destined for reproduction.

The defining characteristic of gouache, and the property that shapes every decision you make while using it, is its **reactivation**. Because gum arabic remains water-soluble after drying, a wet brush dragged across a dry gouache layer will pick up the underlying color. This is simultaneously a strength (you can blend and rework areas that have dried) and a challenge (careless brushwork on upper layers can disturb the layers below).

The chalky, matte finish that gouache dries to is often described as a defect by painters accustomed to the richness of oil or the luminosity of watercolor. But experienced gouache painters know it as a feature. The matte surface gives gouache paintings a graphic quality -- flat, bold, poster-like -- that is distinctive and impossible to replicate in other media. When you see a painting that looks like a vintage travel poster, an animation background, or a mid-century modern illustration, you are almost certainly looking at gouache.

### The Value Shift -- Gouache's Quirk

Gouache dries lighter than it looks wet. The degree varies by color (some pigments shift more than others) and by brand, but as a general rule, a wet gouache stroke will be 5-15% darker than the same stroke after it dries. This is the opposite of watercolor (which dries lighter) and the same direction as acrylic (which also dries slightly darker, though for different chemical reasons -- acrylic polymer is more transparent when wet).

The value shift takes practice to anticipate. Experienced gouache painters learn to mix slightly darker than their target, knowing the paint will lighten as it dries. Over time, this compensation becomes unconscious -- you stop thinking about it and just mix the right color. Until then, make a test stroke on scrap paper and let it dry before committing to your painting.

## 2. Gouache vs. Watercolor vs. Acrylic -- When to Choose Which

These three water-based media occupy overlapping but distinct niches. Choosing the right one depends on what you want to make and how you want to work.

**Choose watercolor** when you want luminous transparency, when the white of the paper is your lightest value, when you want the unpredictable magic of wet-in-wet interactions, and when you are willing to plan your lights in advance. Watercolor rewards boldness and punishes overworking. It is the medium of atmospheric landscapes, delicate florals, and loose, expressive studies. See [techniques/watercolor-basics].

**Choose gouache** when you want flat, opaque coverage with a matte finish. When you want to paint light over dark. When you want the ability to rework and correct without penalty. When you are illustrating, designing, or making studies where graphic clarity matters more than atmospheric subtlety. When you want a water-based medium that feels more forgiving than watercolor but less plastic than acrylic.

**Choose acrylic** when you want permanence and durability. When you need a paint that is waterproof after drying. When you are painting on canvas (gouache is best on paper or board). When you want to build thick impasto texture. When you need a medium that layers without reactivating -- the ability to paint clean upper layers without disturbing lower ones. See [techniques/acrylic-basics].

**The hybrid zone:** Many painters use gouache and watercolor together in the same painting. Watercolor washes establish the transparent atmospheric areas, and gouache provides opaque highlights, details, and corrections on top. This mixed technique is the standard approach for many plein air painters and illustrators. The two media are perfectly compatible (same binder, same paper, same brushes) and complement each other's strengths.

## 3. Brands and Types -- What to Buy

### Traditional Gouache (Reactivates with Water)

**Winsor & Newton Designers Gouache** ($5-9 per 14ml tube): The professional standard for designers and illustrators. Formulated for maximum opacity and flatness of coverage. The 14ml tubes are small but the pigment concentration is high -- a little goes far. Wide color range (over 80 colors). Available at virtually every art supply store. If you are buying your first set, the Primary Set of 6 ($25-30) covers the essentials: Permanent White, Ivory Black, Primary Red, Primary Yellow, Primary Blue, and Burnt Sienna.

**Schmincke Horadam Gouache** ($7-12 per 15ml tube): The fine art gouache for painters who prioritize pigment quality and lightfastness over flat design coverage. Schmincke's gouache is creamy, smooth, and uses the same high-quality pigments as their acclaimed watercolor line. More expensive than Winsor & Newton but noticeably better handling. The 5-tube Primary Set ($35-40) is a good starting point.

**Holbein Artists' Gouache** ($4-7 per 15ml tube): Japanese-made, widely respected, and available in an enormous range of over 80 colors. Holbein gouache is known for consistent quality and excellent opacity. The price point is attractive for building a large palette.

**Himi Miya Jelly Gouache** ($12-18 for 24 colors): The TikTok sensation. Himi Miya gouache comes pre-mixed in small jelly cups arranged in a stacking palette. You open the lid, wet a brush, and paint. No squeezing tubes, no mixing on a palette, no setup time. The convenience is real and the price is extraordinary -- 24 colors for under $20, less than the cost of three tubes of professional gouache.

Is Himi Miya professional quality? No. The pigment quality is lower, the lightfastness is variable, and the opacity does not match Winsor & Newton or Schmincke. But for sketchbook work, plein air studies, practice, and the pure enjoyment of painting without financial anxiety, Himi Miya is brilliant. It has brought gouache to hundreds of thousands of new painters who would never have tried it at professional prices. Many of those painters will eventually upgrade to professional brands. Some will stick with Himi Miya because the joy of painting does not require museum-grade materials. Both are valid choices.

### Acrylic Gouache (Does NOT Reactivate -- Permanent When Dry)

**Holbein Acryla Gouache** ($5-8 per 20ml tube): Acrylic polymer binder instead of gum arabic. Dries to the same matte, opaque finish as traditional gouache, but once dry, it is waterproof. This means you can layer confidently -- upper layers will never disturb lower ones. No reactivation. No lifting. Just clean, confident layering.

Holbein Acryla Gouache is the preferred medium for many professional illustrators and animation background painters. The matte finish photographs and scans beautifully. The permanent layers allow complex, multi-pass work without the anxiety of disturbing earlier stages. Over 100 colors in the range, plus metallics and fluorescents.

The tradeoff: once it dries on your palette, it is dead. Unlike traditional gouache, which can be reactivated with water indefinitely, acrylic gouache cures permanently. You must work faster, keep your palette moist (a stay-wet palette helps, though the matte-drying tendency can cause acrylic gouache to skin over faster than regular acrylics), and accept that dried-on-palette paint is waste.

**Turner Acryl Gouache** ($4-7 per 20ml tube): The Japanese alternative to Holbein Acryla. Comparable quality, slightly different handling (some painters find Turner slightly more fluid). Wide color range (over 200 colors, one of the largest in any paint line). Popular in Japanese illustration and design education.

## 4. Essential Techniques

### Flat Coverage -- Gouache's Signature Strength

Gouache excels at flat, even coverage. No other painting medium -- not oil, not acrylic, not watercolor -- produces flat, matte color with as little effort as gouache. This is why designers and illustrators love it.

The technique: mix your color to the consistency of heavy cream -- wet enough to flow smoothly from the brush, thick enough that the brush marks level out before the paint dries. Load a flat brush generously. Apply the paint in even, overlapping strokes, working quickly to maintain a wet edge. Do not go back over areas that have started to dry -- the reactivation will lift the partially set paint and create streaks.

For large areas, work from one edge to the other in a single pass. For small, precise areas, use a round brush and fill in carefully, keeping the paint at consistent viscosity. If the paint is too thick, it will show brush marks. If it is too thin, it will not cover in one coat. The sweet spot -- smooth, opaque, and self-leveling -- comes with practice.

### Layering Light Over Dark -- The Impossible Made Easy

This is the technique that makes gouache feel like magic to a watercolor painter. In watercolor, painting light over dark is essentially impossible -- the transparent medium cannot cover a dark value with a light one. In gouache, it is trivial.

Mix a light, opaque color (any color mixed with white). Apply it over a fully dry dark area. The light paint covers the dark completely. You can paint white stars on a black sky, yellow flowers on a green background, bright highlights on dark shadows. The opacity handles everything.

The key is making sure the underlying layer is completely dry. If it is even slightly damp, the reactivation will mix the wet upper layer with the damp lower layer, producing an unintended blend. Gouache dries in 5-15 minutes depending on thickness and humidity. Wait. Touch-test a corner. Then paint over.

### Reworking and Correcting -- The Forgiving Medium

Made a mistake? Wet the area with a clean, damp brush. The gouache reactivates. Lift it gently with the brush or blot with a paper towel. The surface returns to (nearly) its original state. Apply fresh paint.

This reworking ability makes gouache the most forgiving painting medium -- more forgiving than oil (where you scrape and repaint), more forgiving than acrylic (which cannot be lifted once dry), and vastly more forgiving than watercolor (which fights you at every correction attempt).

The limit of reworking: repeated wetting and lifting can roughen the paper surface, which changes how subsequent paint layers sit. After three or four reworking cycles in the same area, the paper may start to pill or fuzz. At that point, it is better to paint over the area opaquely than to keep lifting.

### Managing Reactivation in Complex Work

For paintings with many layers, reactivation becomes the primary technical challenge. Here are the strategies:

**Work from back to front.** Paint the background first. Let it dry completely. Paint the midground. Let it dry. Paint the foreground. Each successive layer covers the one below without disturbing it -- as long as you apply the upper layer quickly and confidently, with a well-loaded brush, without scrubbing.

**Use a light touch.** The difference between "painting over a dry layer" and "reactivating a dry layer" is brush pressure. A well-loaded brush applied gently deposits paint on the surface without disturbing what is underneath. A damp brush pressed hard and scrubbed across the surface will lift the lower layer. Let the brush glide. Do not scrub.

**Spray fixative between layers.** A light spray of workable fixative (Krylon Workable Fixativ, $8-10/can) between layers creates a barrier that reduces reactivation of lower layers. This is a useful technique for complex, multi-layer work but changes the surface texture slightly and is not necessary for most gouache painting.

**Consider acrylic gouache for lower layers.** A hybrid approach: use Holbein Acryla Gouache for the base layers (permanent, non-reactivating) and traditional gouache for the upper layers (reworkable, forgiving). The acrylic base provides a stable foundation. The traditional gouache top layer provides the ability to adjust and correct. This is a professional technique used by many illustrators.

## 5. Sketchbook Gouache -- The Portable Studio

Gouache is the ideal sketchbook medium. It is portable (a small palette, a water brush, and a sketchbook fit in a jacket pocket), it covers in a single layer (no waiting for multiple washes), it works on virtually any paper including toned and colored stocks, and it dries fast enough that you can close the sketchbook within minutes of finishing.

### The Sketchbook Gouache Kit

**Paint:** A limited palette of 5-6 colors in a small travel palette. If using Himi Miya, the stacking cups are already portable. If using tubes, squeeze a small amount of each color into a half-pan palette (a Schmincke metal travel palette, $15-20, holds 12 half-pans and includes a built-in mixing area). Essential colors: titanium white, ivory black or Payne's gray, cadmium yellow or yellow ochre, cadmium red or primary magenta, ultramarine blue, and burnt sienna.

**Brushes:** Two water brushes (Pentel Aquash, $8 for a set of 3 -- the barrel holds water, so no separate water container needed) cover most needs. A medium round for general work and a fine round for details. Or pack two conventional travel brushes and a small water container.

**Sketchbook:** A sketchbook with heavy paper (140 lb / 300 gsm watercolor paper, or a mixed-media sketchbook with 160+ gsm paper). Strathmore Mixed Media Visual Journal ($12-15 for 9x12, 34 sheets of 190 gsm paper) is an excellent choice -- the paper handles gouache without buckling and accepts both opaque and wash techniques.

**Toned paper** is a game-changer for gouache sketching. A gray or tan toned paper provides a middle value for free. You only need to paint the lights (add white/light colors) and the darks (add dark colors). The paper IS the mid-tone. This speeds up sketching enormously and produces work with a unified, cohesive tonality. Strathmore 400 Series Toned Mixed Media ($10-12 for 9x12, 15 sheets) comes in tan and gray. Both are excellent for gouache.

### The Practice

Take the kit everywhere. Paint your coffee cup. Paint the view from a window. Paint the person sitting across from you on the bus (quickly, before they notice). Paint the sky during your lunch break. Gouache's opacity and fast drying make these quick studies possible in a way that watercolor (which requires planning and patience) and oil (which requires setup and cleanup) do not.

The Instagram gouache renaissance is driven largely by these kinds of small, quick, accessible studies -- painters posting daily sketchbook pages of cafe scenes, urban landscapes, food studies, and travel sketches. The matte finish of gouache photographs cleanly for social media. The small format keeps the time investment low. The opacity keeps the results bold and readable even at phone-screen resolution.

## 6. The Illustration Tradition

Gouache is not a new medium experiencing a novelty boom. It is one of the oldest and most established illustration media, with a tradition stretching back over a century.

### The Golden Age Illustrators

The great illustrators of the late 19th and early 20th centuries -- the so-called "Golden Age of Illustration" -- used gouache extensively. **J.C. Leyendecker** (the *Saturday Evening Post* cover artist whose Arrow Collar Man was the visual ideal of American masculinity for a generation) worked in oil and gouache. His gouache preliminary studies, with their flat graphic quality and bold color, are as compelling as his finished oil paintings.

**Alphonse Mucha** (the Czech Art Nouveau master whose posters defined the aesthetic of Belle Epoque Paris) used gouache for his preliminary designs and for finished works where the flat, decorative quality of the medium served his graphic sensibility.

**Norman Rockwell** used gouache for studies and preliminary color work before moving to oil for finished paintings. His gouache studies are looser, fresher, and more spontaneous than the carefully rendered oil paintings they served -- many collectors prefer the studies.

### Animation Background Painting

The traditional animation industry is built on gouache. Animation backgrounds -- the painted environments that characters move through -- were painted in gouache on heavy illustration board from the earliest Disney features through the 1990s. The matte finish ensures that background paintings do not create reflections or hotspots when photographed frame by frame under the animation camera.

The classic Disney backgrounds (*Snow White*, *Pinocchio*, *Bambi*, *Sleeping Beauty*) are gouache paintings of extraordinary skill and beauty. The background painters -- artists like Mary Blair, Eyvind Earle, and Tyrus Wong -- developed a gouache technique that could produce everything from photorealistic forests to abstract, stylized landscapes to impressionistic light effects. Their work is painting at the highest level, and it was done in gouache because the medium's properties -- opacity, matte finish, fast drying, ability to work light over dark -- were perfectly suited to the production requirements.

### Contemporary Illustration

Gouache remains a primary medium for contemporary illustrators, particularly those working in editorial, children's book, and gallery illustration. The medium's graphic quality, its flat matte finish, and its clean photographic reproduction make it ideal for work destined for print reproduction. Many illustrators who work primarily in digital maintain a gouache practice for gallery work, personal projects, and the simple pleasure of working with physical materials.

## 7. Getting Started -- Your First Gouache Painting

### Exercise: Flat Shapes on Toned Paper

Materials: toned paper (gray or tan), 5 colors of gouache (white, black, one warm, one cool, one earth), two brushes (medium flat, medium round), water container, paper towel.

1. **Choose a simple subject** with clear shapes: a house, a piece of fruit, a coffee mug.
2. **Block in the darkest shapes first.** Mix your darkest value and paint the shadow areas as flat shapes. No blending. Just shape.
3. **Add the mid-tones.** These might be the local color of the object. The toned paper provides the mid-value baseline, so you may leave some areas as bare paper.
4. **Add the lights.** Mix in white to create your lightest values. Paint highlights and lit areas on top of the darker layers. This is the moment gouache's opacity shines -- literally. Bright lights on dark shapes, no problem.
5. **Refine edges.** Soften some edges with a damp brush. Leave other edges crisp. The contrast between hard and soft edges is what gives the painting life.

This exercise teaches the fundamental gouache workflow: dark first, mid-tones, lights on top. It teaches the value of toned paper as a time-saver. And it teaches the core experience of gouache -- the opacity, the matte finish, the ability to work light over dark -- in 30-45 minutes.

Paint this exercise ten times with ten different subjects. By the fifth painting, you will understand gouache's handling. By the tenth, you will have an opinion about whether this is your medium. If the matte finish feels right, if the opacity feels liberating, if the speed feels addictive -- gouache might be the medium you did not know you were looking for.
