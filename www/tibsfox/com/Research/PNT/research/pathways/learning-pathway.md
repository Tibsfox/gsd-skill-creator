# PNT Learning Pathway: From First Brushstroke to Working Painter

A structured learning journey through the art of painting -- history, materials, technique, and the leap from "I wish I could paint" to "I paint." You don't need to be an expert to start. You don't need talent. You don't need expensive supplies. You need a brush, some paint, a surface, and the willingness to make something bad on purpose, knowing it will get better.

This pathway organizes all PNT research documents, try-sessions, study-guides, technique guides, and artist profiles into a coherent sequence from absolute beginner through exhibiting and selling work. Each level builds on the previous one, but art isn't linear -- follow your curiosity whenever it pulls you sideways.

**Total estimated time:** 100+ hours across all five levels (the honest answer is "the rest of your life")
**Prerequisites:** None. If you can hold a pencil, you can start.
**College of Knowledge connections:** Art, History, Science (color theory, optics), Nature Studies (plein air), Mind-Body (meditative practice)

## Level 1 -- First Marks (1-2 days)

*"I haven't painted since grade school. I don't know where to start."*

Start here. Right here. The single biggest barrier to learning to paint is the belief that you need to be ready before you can begin. You don't. You need paint and something to put it on. Level 1 gets your hands dirty on Day 1 and gives you just enough context to understand what you're doing.

### Try-Session -- Do This First

1. **[try-sessions/paint-an-apple.md](../try-sessions/paint-an-apple.md)** -- Your very first painting exercise. One apple, one light source, limited palette. This isn't about making a good painting -- it's about experiencing what paint does: how it flows, how colors mix on the surface, how you translate a 3D object onto a 2D surface. Everyone's first apple looks weird. That's the point. (~1-2 hours)

### Reading

2. **[01-history-of-painting.md](../01-history-of-painting.md)** -- A compressed history of painting from Lascaux to Hockney. Read this after your first painting, not before. Once you've struggled with paint, the history means something -- you understand what the cave painters accomplished with burnt pigment and spit. (~30 min)

3. **[07-learning-path.md](../07-learning-path.md)** -- The project's built-in guide to learning to paint: the truth about talent, why drawing matters, foundational skills, learning methods, and common mistakes. Think of this as the philosophical companion to the practical pathway you're on. (~40 min)

### Try-Session

4. **[try-sessions/value-study-challenge.md](../try-sessions/value-study-challenge.md)** -- Paint a simple scene using only black and white (and the grays between them). This strips away the complexity of color and forces you to see values -- the light and dark structure that gives a painting its form. Value is the skeleton of a painting. Color is the skin. (~1-2 hours)

### What You'll Learn
- How paint actually behaves on a surface (nothing like you imagined)
- The 40,000-year history of painting in compressed form
- Why value (light/dark) matters more than color at the beginning
- That your first painting being bad is not a sign you should stop

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Mix paint and apply it to a surface with basic control
- See and paint a simple value scale from dark to light
- Talk about painting history with more depth than "old paintings in museums"
- Continue to Level 2 with confidence that you belong here

### College Connections
- **Art:** Foundational mark-making, painting history
- **Mind-Body:** Overcoming perfectionism, presence through practice

## Level 2 -- Foundations (1-2 weeks)

*"I've made some marks. Now I want to understand what I'm doing."*

Level 1 was about getting paint on surface. Level 2 is about understanding your materials, learning color theory, and building the foundational skills that every painter -- from hobbyist to professional -- relies on. This is the most reading-heavy level, but each reading is paired with practice.

### Reading -- Materials

5. **[02-paint-mediums.md](../02-paint-mediums.md)** -- The definitive guide to paint types: oil, acrylic, watercolor, gouache, encaustic, and more. Each medium has a personality. This chapter helps you find yours. (~30 min)

6. **[03-brushes-tools.md](../03-brushes-tools.md)** -- Brushes, palette knives, sponges, and every other tool painters use. What to buy first, what to skip, and why expensive brushes aren't always better. (~20 min)

7. **[04-surfaces-supports.md](../04-surfaces-supports.md)** -- Canvas, paper, board, panel, and everything you can paint on. Includes preparation (gesso, sizing) and why surface choice affects your painting more than you'd think. (~20 min)

### Study Guide

8. **[study-guides/paint-shopping-guide.md](../study-guides/paint-shopping-guide.md)** -- The practical "what to actually buy" guide. Budget-conscious, beginner-focused, with specific product recommendations. This saves you from the overwhelming art supply store experience. (~15 min)

### Reading -- The Big One

9. **[05-color-theory-technique.md](../05-color-theory-technique.md)** -- Color theory for painters: the color wheel, complementary colors, warm/cool, saturation, color mixing, and how to create the color you see in your head. This is the single most important technical document in the project. Read it, then re-read it every few months for a year. (~45 min)

### Try-Session

10. **[try-sessions/paint-a-landscape.md](../try-sessions/paint-a-landscape.md)** -- Your second real painting, building on what you learned from the apple. A simple landscape with sky, ground, and a focal point. Now you're managing multiple elements, atmospheric perspective, and larger areas of color. (~2-3 hours)

### Technique Guides -- Pick Your Medium

Choose at least one technique guide for the medium that calls to you. You can always come back for the others.

- **[techniques/acrylic-basics.md](../techniques/acrylic-basics.md)** -- The forgiving medium. Dries fast, cleans up with water, layers well. Best for beginners who want to learn without the toxicity concerns of oils. (~25 min)
- **[techniques/watercolor-basics.md](../techniques/watercolor-basics.md)** -- The unforgiving medium. Beautiful, transparent, and teaches you to plan because there's no painting over mistakes. Builds discipline. (~25 min)
- **[techniques/gouache-basics.md](../techniques/gouache-basics.md)** -- The designer's medium. Opaque watercolor that dries matte and flat. Great for studies, illustrations, and the furry art community. (~20 min)
- **[techniques/oil-painting-basics.md](../techniques/oil-painting-basics.md)** -- The classic medium. Slow-drying, blendable, capable of unmatched depth and richness. More setup and cleanup, but the results are worth it. (~25 min)
- **[techniques/digital-painting-basics.md](../techniques/digital-painting-basics.md)** -- The modern medium. Infinite undo, infinite canvas, no drying time. Different skills, but the fundamentals of color and value still apply. (~25 min)

### What You'll Learn
- The properties, strengths, and limitations of major paint mediums
- What tools and materials you actually need (and what you don't)
- Color theory that you can apply immediately to your painting
- The basics of at least one painting medium in depth

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Walk into an art supply store and buy what you need without panic
- Mix any color you can see (approximately -- this takes years to refine)
- Paint a simple landscape with atmospheric depth
- Work confidently in at least one medium

### College Connections
- **Art:** Color theory, materials science for artists, painting fundamentals
- **Science:** Optics, pigment chemistry, color perception
- **Home Economics:** Budgeting for art supplies, workspace setup

## Level 3 -- Growing (1-3 months)

*"I can paint. I want to paint better. I want to find my subjects."*

You can get paint on canvas and you understand the basics. Now the real work begins: developing your eye, finding your subjects, learning from the masters, and building a regular practice. This level is about mileage -- the 50 paintings between "beginner" and "intermediate."

### Try-Session

11. **[try-sessions/paint-from-life-not-photos.md](../try-sessions/paint-from-life-not-photos.md)** -- The most important practice shift you can make. Why cameras lie about color, value, space, and edges -- and why painting from life (starting with your coffee cup, a shoe, your hand, the view from your window) transforms your work in ways photographs never can. (~20 min first exercise, ongoing practice)

12. **[try-sessions/convention-badge-painting.md](../try-sessions/convention-badge-painting.md)** -- Paint a convention badge (the small, laminated character paintings sold at conventions). This is a focused, small-format exercise that teaches tight detail work, character painting, and finishing a piece to "done." Connected to the furry art community and the FFA project. (~2-3 hours)

### Technique Guide

12. **[techniques/color-mixing-exercises.md](../techniques/color-mixing-exercises.md)** -- Structured exercises for training your color-mixing skills. Paint color charts, match colors from photographs, mix from a limited palette. This is the gym workout for your color sense. (~multiple sessions, 30-60 min each)

### Reading -- Genres & Movements

13. **[06-genres-movements.md](../06-genres-movements.md)** -- The major genres (portrait, landscape, still life, figure, abstract) and art movements (Impressionism, Expressionism, Abstract Expressionism, Contemporary) explained in terms of what painters were trying to DO, not just what they made. This helps you find your own direction. (~35 min)

### Artist Profiles

14. **[artists/northwest-school.md](../artists/northwest-school.md)** -- The Northwest School: Mark Tobey, Morris Graves, Kenneth Callahan, and the painters who defined PNW art. Their influence is everywhere in the region -- in galleries, museums, and the landscape painting tradition. Understanding them is understanding where you paint. (~25 min)

15. **[artists/furry-art-history.md](../artists/furry-art-history.md)** -- The history of furry art as a genre: from anthropomorphic illustration through underground comix to the contemporary furry art scene. A legitimate art movement with its own masters, conventions, and aesthetic. (~25 min)

### Study Guide

16. **[study-guides/art-history-timeline.md](../study-guides/art-history-timeline.md)** -- The timeline view: major movements, key artists, and how they connect across centuries. Keep this as a reference -- it maps the territory of painting history. (~20 min for first read, ongoing reference)

### Technique Guide -- Plein Air

17. **[techniques/plein-air-guide.md](../techniques/plein-air-guide.md)** -- Painting outdoors, from life, in the PNW. Equipment, setup, dealing with weather (it will rain), managing changing light, and the unique challenges of painting a landscape that's actively happening around you. This is where painting meets naturalism. (~30 min reading + ongoing practice)

### What You'll Learn
- How to paint tight, detailed work at convention badge scale
- Advanced color mixing through structured practice
- Major art genres and movements and how they relate to your own interests
- The PNW painting tradition and the furry art community
- How to paint outdoors (plein air) in Pacific Northwest conditions

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Complete a painting to a "finished" standard (not just a study)
- Mix colors with confidence from a limited palette
- Name your artistic influences and explain what draws you to them
- Paint outdoors in variable weather conditions
- Produce a convention badge or small character painting

### College Connections
- **Art:** Genres, movements, regional art history, plein air painting
- **Nature Studies:** Landscape observation, PNW natural environments
- **History:** Art history as cultural history
- **Community:** Convention art scene, furry art community

## Level 4 -- Developing (6-12 months)

*"I have a practice. I'm developing a voice. I want to go deeper."*

You're painting regularly. You have preferred subjects and a medium you know well. Level 4 is about depth: studying additional mediums, learning from specific artists, developing a body of work, and beginning to think about painting as a practice rather than just an activity. This is also where you start thinking about whether you want to show or sell.

### Technique Guides -- Expand Your Range

18. **Return to the medium guides you skipped in Level 2.** If you started with acrylics, try watercolor. If you started with digital, try gouache. Each medium teaches you something the others can't. Cross-training makes you better in your primary medium. (~25 min each + practice time)

### Reading

19. **[08-painting-business.md](../08-painting-business.md)** -- The practical reality of selling art: commissions, prints, conventions, galleries, online sales, pricing your work, and the emotional minefield of putting a dollar amount on something you made with your soul. Read this even if you never plan to sell -- it changes how you think about the relationship between maker and audience. (~30 min)

20. **[09-pnw-painting-scene.md](../09-pnw-painting-scene.md)** -- The contemporary PNW painting scene: galleries, artist collectives, plein air groups, open studios, and where to show your work if you choose to. Seattle, Portland, and the smaller towns in between all have active painting communities. (~25 min)

### Practice

21. **Build a body of work.** Paint the same subject 10 different ways. Paint a series of 20 landscapes from the same viewpoint in different seasons and weather. Paint every breed of chicken from the FWL project. The body of work is where your voice emerges -- not from a single painting, but from the patterns across many paintings.

22. **Start a sketchbook practice.** Daily sketches -- 15 minutes minimum -- in pencil, pen, or watercolor. The sketchbook is your laboratory. It's where you experiment without the pressure of making a "painting." (~15 min/day, ongoing)

### What You'll Learn
- How to work in multiple mediums and how they inform each other
- The business realities of selling art
- The PNW painting community and where you fit in it
- What a "body of work" means and how to build one

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Work competently in at least two painting mediums
- Price and sell artwork if you choose to
- Identify your artistic voice and the subjects that are uniquely yours
- Maintain a daily practice that builds skills consistently
- Participate in the PNW painting community (shows, open studios, plein air groups)

### College Connections
- **Art:** Multi-medium practice, artistic identity, body of work
- **Business:** Pricing, commissions, the economics of creative work
- **Community:** PNW art scene, plein air groups, convention art

## Level 5 -- Working Painter (Ongoing)

*"I paint. It's part of who I am. I want to share it with the world."*

The final level isn't a destination -- it's a mode of being. You paint regularly. You show and possibly sell. You teach or mentor. You're part of a painting community. The pathway doesn't end; it opens into a landscape as wide as the one outside your window.

### Ongoing Practice

23. **Exhibit.** Find a venue -- a coffee shop, a co-op gallery, an art walk, a convention art show -- and hang your work. The first time someone stands in front of your painting and really looks at it, everything changes. It's terrifying and it's necessary.

24. **Teach.** The fastest way to deepen your understanding is to explain it to someone else. Offer to teach a friend, volunteer at a community center, or write about your process. Teaching forces you to articulate what you know intuitively.

25. **Return to the beginning.** Re-read [01-history-of-painting.md](../01-history-of-painting.md) and [05-color-theory-technique.md](../05-color-theory-technique.md) with two years of painting behind you. They'll read completely differently. The information hasn't changed -- you have.

### Cross-References

26. **[10-cross-references-bibliography.md](../10-cross-references-bibliography.md)** -- The full bibliography and connections to other research projects. Your gateway to deeper reading and adjacent disciplines. (~reference)

### What You'll Learn
- That mastery is not a destination but a direction
- How teaching transforms your own understanding
- How exhibition changes your relationship to your work
- That the fundamentals are never "done" -- they deepen forever

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Exhibit work in public venues
- Accept and complete commissions with professional standards
- Mentor beginning painters through Levels 1-2
- Maintain a sustainable painting practice alongside the rest of your life
- See the world differently than you did before you started

### College Connections
- **Art:** Exhibition, curation, professional practice
- **Community:** Mentorship, teaching, artistic community
- **Mind-Body:** Painting as meditative and contemplative practice
- **Business:** Professional art career, sustainable creative practice

## How to Use This Pathway

**Make bad paintings on purpose.** Seriously. Every bad painting teaches you something. The painters you admire made hundreds of bad paintings before the ones you've seen. Your bad painting phase is not a bug -- it's the process.

**Practice beats study.** Reading about painting is useful. Painting is essential. If you have an hour, spend 15 minutes reading and 45 minutes painting. The ratio should always favor doing.

**Find your subject.** Landscapes? Portraits? Birds from the FWL project? Raccoons from RCN? Convention badges from FFA? Furniture designs from WDC? The best subject is the one you can't stop looking at.

**Connect to other projects.** The research network makes painting richer:
- **[FWL](../../FWL/research/)** -- Paint chickens. They're beautiful, they hold still (sometimes), and their feather patterns are a masterclass in color.
- **[RCN](../../RCN/research/)** -- Raccoon sketching from trail camera footage
- **[FFA](../../FFA/research/)** -- Convention art, character design, furry art community
- **[WDC](../../WDC/research/)** -- Paint the PNW timber species you're learning about

**Use the cross-project learning paths.** This project appears in:
- [The PNW Naturalist](../../../learning-paths/pnw-naturalist.md) -- plein air nature painting
- [The Creative Maker](../../../learning-paths/creative-maker.md) -- painting techniques for creative projects
