# PNW Broadcasting Heritage -- KING-TV, KOMO, KCTS & Regional Stations

## Pioneer Era: KOMO and the First PNW Television

In 1929, KOMO radio engineer Francis Brott televised the ace of hearts to a one-inch screen in Seattle -- one of the earliest television demonstrations west of the Mississippi. This was not broadcasting in any modern sense: it was a radio engineer with parts in a radio shack, proving the concept worked.

## KING-TV: Dorothy Bullitt's Vision

KING-TV, Seattle's first commercial television station, signed on November 25, 1948 as KRSC-TV on Channel 5. Dorothy Bullitt purchased the station in 1949, building King Broadcasting into the Pacific Northwest's dominant media company. During the FCC's 1948-1952 license freeze, KING-TV held a virtual monopoly, carrying programming from all four networks (NBC, CBS, ABC, DuMont) simultaneously.

Bullitt's editorial independence shaped the station's culture. KING's documentary unit produced national-caliber journalism from a regional market, winning Peabody and DuPont Awards.

## KCTS/9: Educational Television

KCTS (Channel 9) signed on December 7, 1954, as Seattle's educational television station, one of the earliest PBS affiliates in the nation. KCTS later became the production home of Bill Nye the Science Guy (1993-1998), one of the most successful educational series in PBS history.

## Portland Stations

Portland's television landscape developed alongside Seattle's:
- **KPTV** (Channel 12): Oregon's first commercial station, 1952
- **KGW** (Channel 8): NBC affiliate, launched 1956
- **KOIN** (Channel 6): CBS affiliate, long-running Portland institution
- **KATU** (Channel 2): ABC affiliate, Fisher Broadcasting

## The Columbus Day Storm (1962)

The Columbus Day Storm of October 12, 1962 -- the strongest non-tropical windstorm in recorded US history -- tested PNW broadcasting infrastructure. Stations lost transmitter sites, towers bent, and power outages disrupted service for days. The storm's coverage demonstrated television's role as emergency infrastructure.

## Mount St. Helens (1980)

KOMO's coverage of the May 18, 1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens became defining PNW broadcast journalism. Dave Crockett's filming from a helicopter as the lateral blast cloud approached earned an Emmy and became iconic footage.

## Consolidation Era

The PNW broadcast landscape underwent corporate consolidation:
- Fisher Communications (Portland/Seattle) merged into Sinclair Broadcasting
- KING Broadcasting sold to Belo Corporation (1999), then Tegna
- Sinclair Broadcast Group acquired multiple PNW stations
