# FCC Licensing & Legal Framework -- 47 CFR, LPTV, Class A

## Communications Act of 1934

The Communications Act of 1934 established the Federal Communications Commission and mandated that all broadcast stations operate "in the public interest, convenience, and necessity." This phrase remains the foundational legal standard for broadcast regulation.

## 47 CFR Parts 73 and 74

The Code of Federal Regulations governs broadcast television:
- **Part 73:** Full-power commercial and noncommercial educational TV stations
- **Part 74:** Low-power TV (LPTV), TV translators, and TV booster stations

## Station Classes

| Class | Power (typical) | Coverage | Secondary? | Interference Protection |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Full Power | 100 kW - 5 MW ERP | 50-75 mile radius | No | Full protection |
| Class A | 10-150 kW ERP | 20-40 miles | No (since 2000) | Primary protection |
| LPTV | 0.05-3 kW ERP | 5-20 miles | Yes | No protection |
| TV Translator | 0.05-3 kW ERP | 5-20 miles | Yes | No protection |

## LPTV Licensing

Low Power Television was established by the FCC in 1982, creating a new class of stations intended for community-oriented broadcasting. As of 2026, over 5,300 LPTV stations are licensed.

**Key LPTV characteristics:**
- Secondary spectrum status (must yield to full-power stations)
- Simplified licensing process via FCC Form 2100
- No minimum programming requirements (unlike full-power)
- Can originate programming or retransmit
- Power limits: 3 kW for UHF, 10 watts for VHF

### December 2025 LPTV Rule Changes

The FCC adopted new rules in December 2025:
- Streamlined construction permit extensions
- Clarified digital transition requirements for remaining analog LPTV
- Updated technical standards for ATSC 3.0 operation on LPTV
- Modified Class A conversion eligibility criteria

## Class A Conversion

The Community Broadcasters Protection Act of 1999 created Class A status for LPTV stations meeting specific criteria. The May 2024-May 2025 conversion window allowed qualifying LPTV stations to upgrade.

**Class A benefits:**
- Primary spectrum status (interference protection)
- Must meet programming requirements (minimum 18 hours/day)
- Must maintain Online Public Inspection File (OPIF)
- Must comply with EEO and political broadcasting rules

## License Application Process

1. **Spectrum search:** Identify available channels in target market via FCC database
2. **Engineering study:** Demonstrate coverage and interference compliance
3. **Form 2100 filing:** Submit application with technical exhibits
4. **Construction permit:** FCC grants CP with build-out deadline
5. **Equipment procurement:** Install transmitter, antenna, STL
6. **License application:** After construction, apply for station license
7. **Renewal:** License renewal every 8 years (2028-2031 cycle)
