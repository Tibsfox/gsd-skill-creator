# Television History -- Year-by-Year US Broadcast 1920s-2026

## Overview

The history of American television spans over a century, from the first mechanical scanning demonstrations in the 1920s to the IP-native ATSC 3.0 broadcasts of 2026. This chronology traces the technical, regulatory, and cultural arc of the most influential mass medium in human history.

## The Experimental Era (1920s-1938)

The first television-like transmissions in the United States were mechanical. Charles Francis Jenkins demonstrated moving silhouette images in 1923, and by 1928 his station W3XK became what many credit as the first commercially licensed US television station. That same year, radio station WGY in Schenectady, New York launched regularly scheduled broadcasts -- thirty-minute programs three days a week.

Philo Farnsworth, who had described the image dissector as a teenager in Utah, built the first working all-electronic television camera tube in San Francisco in 1927. Vladimir Zworykin at RCA independently developed the iconoscope; a patent battle followed for years before RCA paid Farnsworth royalties.

Meanwhile, W2XBS (predecessor to NBC) began continuous test broadcasts from New York, most famously rotating a papier-mache Felix the Cat doll on a turntable for hours as engineers calibrated signals.

## The All-Electronic Era and World's Fair (1939-1947)

In April 1939, RCA's National Broadcasting Company introduced the first regularly scheduled all-electronic television broadcasting in America, anchored by the dedication of the RCA pavilion at the New York World's Fair. David Sarnoff himself introduced the broadcast.

Television advertising began formally on July 1, 1941, when WNBT (later WNBC) aired a ten-second Bulova watch advertisement during a Brooklyn Dodgers game -- establishing the commercial-broadcast economic model that would define the medium for seventy years.

World War II halted civilian television development entirely. The post-war years brought rapid expansion: by the late 1940s, stations were appearing in every major city, and RCA's 1947 decision to manufacture affordable consumer television sets triggered the mass-market adoption that made television, by the early 1950s, the dominant American home medium.

## The FCC Freeze and Post-Freeze Expansion (1948-1962)

In September 1948, the FCC placed a freeze on new television station license applications, halting growth at 108 stations while engineers worked out co-channel interference and technical standards. The freeze lasted until April 1952. Its lifting, under the Sixth Report and Order, opened hundreds of new VHF and UHF channels and reserved spectrum for non-commercial educational broadcasting.

The NTSC color standard -- RCA's system -- was approved by the FCC in December 1953 and went to air in January 1954. In January 1963, Congress mandated that all television sets manufactured in the US include UHF tuners, a critical act that opened UHF spectrum to meaningful viewership.

### The Freeze's Impact

During the freeze, cities with existing stations held monopolies. KING-TV in Seattle carried all four networks simultaneously -- a circumstance impossible to replicate but which shaped a culture of editorial independence that persists in the region's broadcasting DNA.

## Network Golden Age (1952-1975)

The Big Three (NBC, CBS, ABC) dominated from the freeze's lifting until cable's rise. Key cultural milestones:

- **1963:** Kennedy assassination -- four days of continuous TV coverage, the first event demonstrating television's power as a national communal medium
- **1967:** Corporation for Public Broadcasting established, funding PBS
- **1969:** Apollo 11 Moon landing broadcast to a global audience -- the most-watched live event in history at the time
- **1972:** HBO launched via satellite, creating the first premium cable service
- **1979:** ESPN launched, proving niche cable programming viable
- **1983:** M*A*S*H finale -- still among the most-watched programs in US history (105.9 million viewers)

The FCC's Fairness Doctrine, requiring balanced coverage of controversial public issues, was enforced from 1949 until its repeal in 1987.

## Cable and UHF Era (1975-1996)

Community Antenna Television (CATV) began in 1948 in Pennsylvania mountain towns to improve broadcast reception. HBO's 1975 satellite distribution via SATCOM 1 created the premium cable model. Cable grew from 16 million to 50 million subscribing homes between 1980 and 1990.

The networks that defined the cable era:
- **CNN** (June 1, 1980) -- 24-hour news
- **ESPN** (September 7, 1979) -- all sports
- **Nickelodeon** (April 1, 1979) -- children's programming
- **MTV** (August 1, 1981) -- music television, "Video Killed the Radio Star"

Regulatory milestones: the 1984 Cable Act deregulated pricing; the 1992 Cable Act re-regulated and mandated retransmission consent; the 1996 Telecommunications Act further deregulated ownership limits, enabling the media consolidation wave that followed.

## LPTV Era (1982-Present)

The FCC created Low Power Television in 1982, establishing a new class of stations intended for community-oriented broadcasting. By 2026, over 5,300 LPTV stations are licensed across the United States, serving ethnic communities, religious broadcasters, rural local news, and educational institutions.

## Digital Transition and Streaming (2005-2026)

The FCC mandated the digital transition, completed June 12, 2009, ending analog broadcasting. ATSC 1.0 became the standard for all over-the-air television.

YouTube launched in 2005; Netflix streaming began in 2007. The cord-cutting acceleration was dramatic: 5 million US households dropped cable in 2023 alone. By 2025, streaming had surpassed cable in total viewing hours.

ATSC 3.0 voluntary adoption began November 2017. By 2026, the standard reached 76% of US households, with 62 markets broadcasting. Its IP-native architecture enables the convergence of internet and antenna that the Streamer Bridge exploits.
