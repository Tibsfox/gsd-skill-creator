Television did not arrive fully formed. It emerged the way the best systems always do — from the spaces between disciplines, between inventors, between eras. Philo Farnsworth drew his image dissector in a potato field in Idaho; a radio engineer at KOMO in Seattle televised the ace of hearts to a one-inch screen in 1929. The medium was never one thing. It was always a negotiation between transmission and reception.

Research Modules

M-A: Television History

History Track A · 1920s–2026

Year-by-year annotated timeline of US broadcast television. Farnsworth/Zworykin patents, 1939 World's Fair, FCC freeze, NTSC color, Big Three era, Moon landing, digital transition 2009, ATSC 3.0 rollout to present.

M-B: PNW Broadcasting Heritage

PNW Track B · Regional history

Station-by-station history: KING-TV (1948), KOMO's 1929 experiment, KCTS educational TV, Portland stations, Columbus Day Storm, Mount St. Helens coverage, Spokane/Tri-Cities markets, Sinclair/Tegna consolidation.

M-C: Technical Standards

Tech Track A · Spectrum & standards

Mechanical scanning, NTSC (1941/1953), VHF/UHF bands, all-channel mandate 1963, ATSC 1.0 digital transition, ATSC 3.0 IP-native architecture, 5G Broadcast, power limits by station class.

M-D: FCC Licensing & Legal

FCC Track B · Regulatory framework

Communications Act of 1934, 47 CFR Parts 73/74, station classes, Form 2100, construction permits, EEO/OPIF requirements, Low Power Protection Act 2023, Class A conversion, December 2025 LPTV rules.

M-E: Cable Era & Streaming

Cable Track A · Cultural eras

CATV origins (1948 Pennsylvania), HBO satellite 1975, cable explosion, CNN/ESPN/MTV, 1984 Cable Act, TCI consolidation, YouTube 2005, Netflix streaming, cord-cutting acceleration. From antenna to algorithm.

M-F: Streamer Bridge

Bridge Synthesis · Convergence pathway

PEG access television, LPTV community origination, ATSC 3.0 Run3TV platform, 5G Broadcast petitions, operational model for internet producers acquiring LPTV licenses, Class A upgrade pathway. Signal returns to the air.
