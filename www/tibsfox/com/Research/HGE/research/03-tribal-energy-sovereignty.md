## 1. The Colville Confederated Tribes and Grand Coulee Dam

The Confederated Tribes of the Colville Reservation are a confederacy of 12 tribes occupying 1.4 million acres of a reservation originally established at 2.9 million acres in 1872. The reservation was reduced by half before Grand Coulee Dam was ever proposed.

**The dam's relationship to the Tribes:**
- Grand Coulee Dam is located on the Colville Reservation
- Chief Joseph Dam is also located on the Colville Reservation
- Both dams were built on Colville land

**The 1940 payment:** Under the Act of June 29, 1940, the federal government paid:
- $63,000 to the Colville Tribes for tribal land taken
- $4,700 to the Spokane Tribe for their displaced acreage

A dam rated at 6,809 MW of generating capacity -- generating billions in electricity revenue annually -- was built on sovereign reservation land for $63,000. The disproportion is not a rounding error. It is the full measure of what "fair compensation" meant in 1940 for tribal land.

**What was lost beyond land:**
- Kettle Falls -- a sacred fishing site used for 9,000+ years by Columbia Plateau tribes -- was inundated by Lake Roosevelt
- Salmon runs that had sustained tribes for millennia were blocked by the dam (no fish passage)
- Communities were displaced from land along the reservoir
- The river's seasonal flooding patterns -- which had shaped the region's ecosystems and the tribes' relationships with those ecosystems -- were permanently altered

The Colville Tribes receive annual revenue-sharing checks from BPA calculated per the settlement agreement terms. They are compensation recipients. The Fox model proposes a different relationship.

## 2. The 1994 Settlement Act

The Confederated Tribes of the Colville Reservation Grand Coulee Dam Settlement Act (P.L. 103-436, signed October 1994) addressed 54 years of disputes about dam use of tribal land:

**Settlement terms:**
- **Lump-sum payment:** $53,000,000 for past use of tribal lands (fiscal year 1994)
- **Annual payments:** $15,250,000, adjusted annually based on BPA power sales revenues
- **Resolution:** The settlement was intended to provide "a final settlement of claims" for Grand Coulee Dam's use of Colville tribal lands

**The BPA calculation:** Annual payments are tied to BPA's net revenues from power sales. When BPA sells more power at higher prices, Colville payments increase. When BPA's revenues fall (drought years, market competition), payments can decrease.

**What the settlement does not include:**
- Operational role in dam management
- Veto power over operational decisions that affect tribal lands
- Ownership stake in the generating infrastructure
- Right of first refusal on power from the dam

The settlement converts displacement into a revenue stream. It does not convert displacement into governance.

## 3. The Spokane Tribe Equitable Compensation Act

The Spokane Tribe's situation parallels the Colville Tribes' but was not settled until 2020:

**P.L. 116-100 (signed January 2020):**
- Trust fund deposit: $53,000,000 (same base amount as Colville)
- Annual payments: 25% of the Colville Computed Annual Payment through 2029
- Annual payments increase to 32% from 2030 onward

**Congressional findings:** The legislation noted that Spokane tribal acreage taken for Grand Coulee Dam construction equaled approximately 39% of Colville tribal acreage -- hence the 25% payment ratio (less land taken relative to Colville).

**The 80-year gap:** The Spokane Tribe waited 80 years -- from the 1940 construction to the 2020 legislation -- for a settlement even approaching adequate compensation. The Colville Tribes waited 54 years. Both settlements are structured as revenue payments, not as restoration of sovereignty or operational control.

## 4. From Compensation Recipient to Operational Lead

The BIA has documented the Colville Reservation's green energy development potential in terms that make the Fox model's feasibility clear:

**BIA on Colville green energy potential:**
> "The Tribes' ownership stake in both Grand Coulee and Wells Dams provides readily available cheap, reliable, renewable power with six transmission lines crossing reservation lands."

The infrastructure is already there:
- Six transmission lines crossing the reservation
- Established BPA interconnection relationships
- Institutional knowledge of power marketing through BPA
- Legal precedent for tribal energy development
- A site (Electric City) already powered, already permitted, already distressed-community-designated

The transition from "recipient of annual BPA check" to "majority equity holder in a data center powered by that electricity" is a structural shift in economic relationship. The same power. Different governance.

**The legal pathway:** Tribes have extensive authority to develop energy on their lands. The federal trust responsibility creates a duty to assist tribal energy development. TERA (Tribal Energy Resource Agreements, 25 U.S.C. § 3504) allows tribes to enter into business agreements for energy resource development without individual federal approval for each transaction.

## 5. Tribal Clean Energy Precedents

Several tribal nations have successfully transitioned from resource revenue recipients to energy developers and owners:

**Navajo Transitional Energy Company (NTEC):** The Navajo Nation purchased coal mines and a power plant not as an endorsement of coal but as a transition strategy -- controlling their own resource instead of being controlled by it.

**Lummi Nation Solar:** The Lummi Nation (Washington State, adjacent to the Fox corridor) has developed solar installations on tribal lands, with tribal employment for installation and maintenance.

**Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes:** The CSKT have been central to the relicensing of Kerr Dam (now Seli'š Ksanka Qlispe' Dam) on the Flathead Reservation -- ultimately purchasing the dam and becoming its operators, the first tribal nation to own a major hydroelectric dam in the United States.

**The CSKT precedent is the closest analogue to the Fox model:** A hydroelectric facility that was originally built on tribal land, operated by non-tribal entities for decades, and eventually transitioned to tribal ownership and operation. The Colville / Electric City path mirrors this trajectory, applied to a data center rather than a dam.

## 6. DOE Office of Indian Energy

The Department of Energy's Office of Indian Energy is the primary federal funding source for tribal clean energy development:

**Program functions:**
- Grants and technical assistance for tribal energy development
- Funding for tribal energy feasibility studies
- Support for tribal energy codes and regulatory frameworks
- Training for tribal energy professionals

**Funding availability (2026):** The Inflation Reduction Act extended and expanded tribal energy tax credits, including:
- 30% investment tax credit for tribal clean energy (same as non-tribal)
- Bonus 10% credit for projects on Tribal lands (additive)
- Direct pay option (tribes do not always have federal tax liability to offset)

**The Electric City application:** The DOE Office of Indian Energy is identified in the Fox funding architecture as the primary funding source for Electric City tribal clean energy development. The combination of BPA wholesale rates ($0.02/kWh), tribal energy tax credits (40% ITC with tribal bonus), and DOE technical assistance makes the Electric City data center financially viable without requiring venture capital.

## 7. Cultural Sovereignty Frameworks

Any tribal energy development project must navigate cultural sovereignty frameworks that govern data, research, and decision-making:

**OCAP Principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession):**
Developed by the First Nations Information Governance Centre, OCAP asserts that Indigenous communities own their data, control data collection processes, control access to data, and maintain physical possession of data. In the context of a data center, OCAP means that a tribally operated facility on tribal land has the right to establish data governance policies that reflect tribal sovereignty -- not just commercial terms.

**CARE Principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics):**
Developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance, CARE principles address the governance of Indigenous data within research and commercial contexts. A Fox/Colville data center operating under CARE principles would prioritize Indigenous data sovereignty in its terms of service and governance structure.

**UNDRIP Article 31:**
The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Article 31 affirms Indigenous peoples' right to maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions. For the Electric City project, this principle argues for Colville majority control not as a political gesture but as a legal right recognized under international standards the US endorsed in 2010.

## 8. The Fox Model: Majority Equity and Operational Lead

The Fox Infrastructure Group's position on Electric City:

**Structure:**
- Colville Confederated Tribes: majority equity holder
- Colville Confederated Tribes: operating lead (CEO and senior management)
- Fox Infrastructure Group: minority equity, technical and financial support role
- FoxCompute: data center operations partner

**Power source:**
- BPA hydroelectric at $0.02/kWh
- Future addition: geothermal baseload from Cascade Range development

**Why majority equity matters:**
- Revenue distribution favors the tribal partner
- Board control favors the tribal partner
- Employment and procurement priorities favor tribal members
- Cultural and community decisions are made by the tribe, not by outside investors
- If the enterprise succeeds, the success belongs to the Colville Confederation

**Why operational lead matters:**
- The people running the facility are accountable to the tribal community
- Hiring practices, environmental standards, and community engagement are tribal decisions
- The facility is a tribal institution, not a corporate installation on tribal land

**The long-term vision:**
The Electric City facility is not the destination. It is the first step in a transition from annual BPA check to a diversified tribal energy economy -- hydroelectric revenue, geothermal development, data center operations, and the skills and institutions that make future development possible.
