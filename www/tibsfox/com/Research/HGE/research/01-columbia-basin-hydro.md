## 1. Columbia Basin Scale and Capacity

The Columbia River Basin is the hydroelectric backbone of North America's Pacific Northwest. Its scale:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Basin area | ~250,000 square miles | EIA |
| Total dams in basin | 250+ | EIA |
| Cumulative capacity | ~40,570 MW | NWPCC |
| Basin share of US hydropower | ~40% | EIA |
| PNW electricity share from hydro | ~50% annual energy | NWPCC |
| PNW flexible capacity from hydro | ~54% | NWPCC |
| Washington State hydro output | ~37% of national total | EIA |

The Federal Columbia River Power System (FCRPS) -- operated collaboratively by the Bonneville Power Administration (BPA), the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, and the Bureau of Reclamation -- generates approximately 35% of the entire power supply of the Pacific Northwest [Bureau of Reclamation].

Washington State generates more hydroelectric power than any other state in the nation, contributing roughly 37% of total national hydropower output [EIA].

## 2. Grand Coulee Dam: The Keystone

Grand Coulee Dam, located in Grant County, Washington, is the largest hydroelectric facility in the United States:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Nameplate capacity | 6,809 MW | Bureau of Reclamation |
| Nathaniel Washington Power Plant | 4,485 MW alone | Bureau of Reclamation |
| Annual average generation | ~21 billion kWh | Bureau of Reclamation |
| Number of powerhouses | 4 | Bureau of Reclamation |
| Number of generators | 33 | Bureau of Reclamation |
| Construction era | 1933-1942 | Historical record |
| Tribal land taken | Colville + Spokane reservations | Congress |

**The history:** Grand Coulee Dam was built on the land of the Colville Confederated Tribes and the Spokane Tribe. Under the Act of June 29, 1940, the federal government paid $63,000 to the Colville Tribes and $4,700 to the Spokane Tribe for tribal land taken for dam construction [U.S. Senate Report 113-202]. These payments -- a few thousand dollars for a million-dollar dam that took sovereign reservation land -- represent one of the starkest examples of infrastructure-as-displacement in American history.

**The current settlement:** In 1994, Congress enacted the Confederated Tribes of the Colville Reservation Grand Coulee Dam Settlement Act (P.L. 103-436), providing:
- A lump-sum payment of $53,000,000 for past use
- Annual payments of $15,250,000, adjusted based on BPA power sales revenues

In 2020, the Spokane Tribe Equitable Compensation Act (P.L. 116-100) provided parallel compensation: a $53,000,000 trust fund deposit and annual payments equal to 25% of the Colville Computed Annual Payment through 2029, increasing to 32% from 2030 onward.

These settlements represent a transition from the 1940 payments -- but they are revenue payments, not operational control.

## 3. BPA Operations and the Provider of Choice Contracts

The Bonneville Power Administration (BPA) is the federal agency that markets wholesale power from the Columbia River system.

**BPA scale:**
- Markets wholesale power from 31 federal hydroelectric projects with combined installed capacity of 22.5 GW
- Provides 28-33% of electric power consumed in the Pacific Northwest
- Operates approximately 15,000 miles of high-voltage transmission

**BPA rates:** BPA's hydroelectric wholesale rate is approximately $0.02/kWh -- roughly six times cheaper than the U.S. average commercial electricity price and eleven times cheaper than California rates. This rate advantage is what makes the Electric City data center thesis compelling: the cheapest clean electricity in North America, already permitted and delivered.

**Provider of Choice contracts (December 2025):** BPA executed new long-term power purchase agreements with more than 130 Northwest public utility customers, securing two additional decades of reliable, low-cost power supply. These contracts lock in BPA rates through the mid-2040s -- providing rate stability for the Fox corridor's FoxCompute infrastructure planning.

**Efficiency investments:** BPA investments through 2050 in seven federal projects will provide up to 330 average megawatts of additional energy through high-efficiency turbine runners with improved fish passage, generator rewinds, and new turbine installations.

## 4. Climate Change Impacts on PNW Hydropower

Climate change is reshaping the seasonal pattern of Columbia Basin hydropower generation:

**2024 PNNL study findings:**
- Overall hydropower generation is expected to rise with climate change due to increased precipitation
- But seasonal redistribution is significant and growing
- Winter generation may increase **12%** in the near term (2020-2039) and **18%** in the midterm (2040-2059)
- Summer generation may **decrease 1-5%** in western regions

**The core mechanism:** Warming temperatures convert mountain snowpack from snow to rain. This means:
- Less winter storage in snowpack (the natural reservoir)
- Runoff arrives earlier in spring rather than slowly through summer
- By the 2030s: higher average winter streamflows, earlier peak spring runoff, longer periods of low summer flows

**The seasonal mismatch problem:** PNW electricity demand peaks in summer (cooling loads, agricultural irrigation). Hydropower generation increasingly peaks in winter and early spring. The gap between when the grid needs power and when the river provides it is growing -- creating both reliability and economic challenges for BPA.

## 5. Drought Vulnerability

The Columbia Basin's hydropower system is highly drought-sensitive. In 2023-2024:

| State | September Reservoir Storage | Normal |
|-------|---------------------------|--------|
| Oregon | 48% of capacity | ~80% |
| Washington | 67% of capacity | ~85% |
| Montana | 76% of capacity | ~88% |
| Idaho | 60% of capacity | ~82% |

*Source: EIA drought impact analysis*

When reservoirs are low, generation falls -- not because the dams lose capacity, but because there is not enough water to run through the turbines at full power. In 2023-2024, PNW hydropower generation fell close to or below the lower end of the 10-year range [EIA].

**The strategic implication:** A corridor-scale energy portfolio cannot rely on hydro alone. The portfolio needs a source that is weather-independent -- one that generates power during drought years as reliably as wet years. This is the geothermal thesis: not replacing hydroelectric flexibility, but providing the weather-independent floor it cannot provide itself.

## 6. Salmon, Fish Passage, and Operational Constraints

The lower Snake River dams represent the most politically contentious element of PNW hydroelectric operations:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Lower Snake River dams (4) annual generation | ~1,100 average MW | Utility Dive |
| Peak winter peaking capacity | Up to 2,500 MW | Utility Dive |
| Replacement cost estimate | ~$800M/year | Federal agencies |
| Regional electric bill increase if removed | ~25% | Federal agencies |
| Seattle power equivalent of 4 dams | 1 city | Utility Dive |

**Fish passage constraints:** During spring high water conditions, water must be spilled over dams rather than run through turbines to meet salmon water quality standards. This "spill" reduces generation and causes tension between hydropower operations and salmon recovery obligations.

**The spring oversupply problem:** In spring, when snowmelt creates high river flows AND wind generation is strong, BPA must sometimes curtail non-hydro generation (wind and solar) under its Oversupply Management Protocol. This is not a sign that the system has too much clean energy -- it is a sign that the system lacks sufficient flexible demand or storage to absorb the surplus.

**Geothermal as partial solution:** Displacing thermal baseload (gas, nuclear) with geothermal baseload changes the calculus. When hydro has a surplus in spring, the question becomes "what do we do with extra generation?" If the thermal baseload is geothermal rather than gas, the spring surplus can be stored in pumped hydro, used for hydrogen electrolysis, or sold to California markets -- rather than spilled.

## 7. The Electric City Thesis

The Fox Infrastructure Group's Electric City thesis begins at the confluence of three facts:

1. **The BPA rate:** $0.02/kWh for hydroelectric power -- the cheapest clean electricity in North America
2. **The site:** The abandoned Vanalco aluminum smelter at Electric City, Washington -- with hundreds of MW of already-permitted industrial electrical infrastructure, Columbia River cooling water, and a site already designated as a distressed community
3. **The ownership model:** Colville Confederated Tribes as majority equity holders and operating lead

The Colville Tribes' BIA documentation notes that the Tribes' ownership stake in both Grand Coulee and Wells Dams provides readily available cheap, reliable, renewable power with six transmission lines crossing reservation lands.

**The moral architecture:** The power that displaced the Colville Tribes now funds an enterprise they own. This is not a metaphor. It is a specific legal and financial structure: tribal majority equity, tribal operational lead, tribal sovereignty over the resource that was taken from their land.

The same dam. The same river. The same power. Community owned.
