## 1. What Enhanced Geothermal Systems Are

Conventional geothermal power requires three things to coincide naturally: heat, permeability, and fluid. These conditions exist in geologically active areas like Iceland, the western US Basin and Range, and Indonesia. They represent a small fraction of the earth's geothermally active crust.

Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) remove the natural permeability requirement. Instead of finding a natural reservoir, EGS creates one:

1. **Drill into hot dry rock** -- typically at depths of 3-5 km where rock temperatures exceed 150-300°C
2. **Hydraulically stimulate** -- inject fluid under pressure to open fracture pathways through otherwise impermeable rock
3. **Create the reservoir** -- the engineered fracture network becomes the fluid pathway
4. **Circulate fluid** -- cold water goes down, hot water (or steam) comes up, drives a generator

**The analogy to oil and gas:** EGS borrows horizontal drilling, fiber-optic sensing, and hydraulic stimulation directly from the oil and gas industry's 40 years of fracking experience. The techniques are proven. The application to geothermal is the innovation.

**Why EGS changes everything:** Conventional geothermal works where nature put the plumbing. EGS works wherever the rock is hot enough -- which is almost everywhere, given sufficient depth. The DOE's GeoVision analysis identified hundreds of GW of accessible EGS resource across the continental US.

**EGS vs hydraulic fracturing (fracking):**
EGS and oil/gas fracking both use hydraulic stimulation, but with critical differences:
- EGS uses water as the working fluid (not the hydrocarbon-bearing fluid itself)
- EGS targets hot dry rock at greater depth than most fracking operations
- EGS fluid is recirculated in a closed loop, not extracted and disposed of
- EGS purpose is heat extraction, not hydrocarbon extraction
- Induced seismicity risk exists but is managed through monitoring protocols (FORGE program data)

## 2. Fervo Energy: The Cape Station Results

Fervo Energy's Cape Station project in Utah is the most significant validation of commercial EGS technology to date:

| Metric | Value | Date |
|--------|-------|------|
| Max flow rate | 107 kg/s at high temperature | September 2024 |
| Per-well electric production | 10+ MW | September 2024 |
| Per-well output vs. Project Red pilot | 3x increase | September 2024 |
| Well cost reduction | 49% across first 4 Cape Station wells | 2024 |
| Sugarloaf appraisal well depth | 15,765 feet true vertical | June 2025 |
| Sugarloaf projected temperature | 520°F bottomhole | June 2025 |
| Sugarloaf drilling time | 16 days | June 2025 |
| Drilling time reduction vs DOE baseline | 79% | June 2025 |

**Phase development:**
- Cape Station Phase I: 100 MW, on track for 2026 commercial operation
- Cape Station Phase II: 400 MW, by 2028
- Permits for expansion to 2 GW secured
- Independent reserves report (DeGolyer & MacNaughton): Cape Station area can support >5 GW

**Financing validation (December 2025):** Fervo secured $462 million in Series E funding and $206 million in additional project financing -- institutional validation that EGS at commercial scale is financeable risk.

## 3. National Scale and DOE Projections

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| US geothermal nameplate capacity (2024) | 3.97 GW | NREL 2025 Geothermal Market Report |
| Growth since 2020 | +8% (from 3.67 GW) | NREL |
| DOE Enhanced Geothermal Shot target | 90 GW by 2050 | DOE |
| Share of US electricity at 90 GW | ~12% | DOE / Congressional Research Service |
| Princeton 2025 Joule study projection | 250+ GW by 2050 (baseline costs) | Princeton / Joule |
| Princeton ranking at 250 GW | 3rd most significant clean energy (behind wind and solar) | Princeton |

**The Enhanced Geothermal Shot:** The DOE's 2022 Enhanced Geothermal Shot targets a 90% reduction in EGS costs by 2035, bringing levelized cost to $45/MWh. The current trajectory -- with Fervo's 79% drilling time reduction and 49% well cost reduction at Cape Station -- suggests $60-70/MWh by 2030 is achievable.

**Historical comparison:** Offshore wind costs fell 70% in a decade. Solar costs fell 90% in a decade. Early EGS is at the same point on the learning curve that wind was in 2012 and solar was in 2008. The question is not whether EGS will be cost-competitive -- it is when.

## 4. Pacific Northwest Geothermal Resources

The Pacific Northwest sits on the Ring of Fire. The Cascade Range is one of the most volcanically active mountain chains on Earth. The geothermal resource base is vast -- and almost entirely undeveloped:

| State | Estimated Conventional Geothermal Potential | Current Installed | Gap |
|-------|---------------------------------------------|-------------------|-----|
| Oregon | 2,200 MW (Cascade Mountains) | ~18 MW | 99% undeveloped |
| Washington | Extensive (Baker-Snoqualmie, Rainier area) | 0 MW utility-scale | 100% undeveloped |
| Idaho | Significant (geothermal aquifer systems) | ~32 MW | Mostly undeveloped |

**Why is the PNW so underutilized?** Several factors:
1. Cheap hydroelectric power reduces the financial urgency for alternatives
2. National forest land use restrictions slowed exploration permitting
3. Historical lack of grid-scale geothermal investment relative to Nevada/California
4. The capital intensity of exploration (high risk, long permitting times)

The BLM categorical exclusion (January 2025) -- which streamlines permitting for small geothermal exploration projects -- and the state-level permitting acceleration (California October 2025) are beginning to change this landscape.

## 5. Newberry Volcano and Oregon Potential

Newberry Volcano, located in central Oregon, is the largest volcano in the Cascades -- a shield volcano with a 17-mile-wide caldera. It is the site of the most significant geothermal EGS research in the PNW:

**NEWGEN EGS Research Partnership:**
- Partners: Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL), Oregon State University, AltaRock Energy
- Purpose: demonstrating EGS viability in the Cascade Range basalt formations
- Location: Newberry Volcano caldera area, Deschutes National Forest

**The Oregon resource:** The USGS Northwest Volcanic Aquifer Study is assessing groundwater and geothermal potential across a region shaped by 17 million years of volcanic eruptions: eastern Oregon, northeastern California, northwestern Nevada, and southeastern Idaho. This assessment is expected to substantially revise upward the estimated accessible resource.

**Oregon installed geothermal (existing):**
- River Raft I: Southern Idaho border region
- Neal Hot Springs: Malheur County, OR
- Paisley: Lake County, OR
- Oregon Tech: Klamath Falls campus (district heating)

Four facilities generating approximately 18 MW. Oregon has 2,200 MW of identified potential.

## 6. Washington State Prospects

Washington has zero utility-scale geothermal generation despite significant identified resources:

**Mount Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest:** The U.S. Forest Service designated over 80,000 acres of the Mount Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest for potential geothermal development. Snohomish PUD invested approximately $5 million in exploration in this area.

**Mt. Rainier / Tahoma area:** The Cascade Range volcanics that define the PNW skyline are the same geological structures that produce the region's geothermal heat. Mt. Rainier (Tahoma) is an active stratovolcano. The heat beneath it is real and measurable.

**The regulatory context:** Washington's CETA (Clean Energy Transformation Act) requires the state's electricity grid to be fossil-free by 2045. This creates a powerful market signal for firm clean generation -- exactly what geothermal provides. As EGS costs fall and the 2045 deadline approaches, Washington State is an increasingly attractive geothermal development target.

## 7. Superhot Geothermal and Lithium Co-Production

Two emerging frontiers expand the EGS value proposition:

**Superhot geothermal (SHGR):**
Deep drilling (5-10+ km) into rock exceeding 400°C produces supercritical water or steam with dramatically higher energy density than conventional geothermal. A single superhot well can produce 10-100x more energy than a conventional well at the same flow rate. Iceland's IDDP-2 project drilled into magma at 426°C and demonstrated supercritical fluid production.

**Lithium co-production:**
Geothermal brine -- the heated fluid that circulates through EGS reservoirs -- often contains dissolved lithium. As geothermal development expands, lithium extraction from brine can become a significant co-revenue stream. Fervo's Cape Station brine has measurable lithium concentrations. If EGS and direct lithium extraction scale together, geothermal becomes both an energy source and a critical minerals source -- directly relevant to battery supply chains for the electrification transition.

## 8. Cost Trajectory

| Year | EGS LCOE ($/MWh) | Source/Basis |
|------|------------------|-------------|
| 2020 | ~150-200 | Project Red pilot (Fervo) |
| 2024 | ~80-100 | Cape Station Phase I trajectory |
| 2030 | ~60-70 | DOE Liftoff report projection |
| 2035 | ~45 | Enhanced Geothermal Shot target |
| 2050 | ~30-40 | Learning curve projection (Princeton) |

**Comparison to alternatives (2024-2026):**
- Utility-scale solar PV: $30-45/MWh (but intermittent; no firm capacity)
- Onshore wind: $25-50/MWh (but intermittent; no firm capacity)
- Nuclear (new build): $80-120/MWh (firm capacity but >10-year construction)
- Gas peaker (firm): $90-150/MWh (carbon-intensive)
- Geothermal at $60-70/MWh delivers firm, 24/7, carbon-free power at a cost competitive with gas and approaching intermittent renewables when firm capacity value is included
