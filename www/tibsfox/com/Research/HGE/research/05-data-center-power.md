## 1. The AI Electricity Demand Surge

The convergence of AI training demand, cloud infrastructure scaling, and cryptocurrency mining has transformed electricity from a commodity cost into a strategic constraint for technology companies:

**Data points (2024-2026):**
- Electricity demand from data centers is growing faster than new generation can come online [Fervo Energy, July 2025]
- OpenAI CEO Sam Altman, 2025 Senate testimony: "AI costs will converge to energy costs, and AI abundance will be limited by energy abundance"
- BPA projects demand growth that existing hydro and renewables cannot meet without new supply
- US data center electricity consumption expected to double or triple by 2030

**The grid impact:**
- Virginia (primary US data center hub): grid operators are delaying data center interconnections by 5-10 years due to transmission constraints
- Texas (ERCOT): data center demand drove multiple near-grid-emergency events in 2024
- Pacific Northwest: protected by existing hydro but approaching capacity limits

**The opportunity:** The locations with the most available clean firm power -- particularly the Pacific Northwest hydro system -- are underutilized for data centers relative to their electricity advantage. Companies willing to locate in Eastern Washington rather than Northern Virginia gain a 6-11x electricity cost advantage.

## 2. Data Center Power Density Requirements

Modern AI data centers have dramatically higher power density requirements than traditional enterprise data centers:

| Era | Power Density | Cooling Method |
|-----|--------------|----------------|
| Traditional (2010s) | 5-15 kW per rack | Air cooling |
| Cloud (2018-2022) | 15-30 kW per rack | Air + precision cooling |
| AI training (2023+) | 30-100+ kW per rack | Liquid cooling required |
| Next-gen GPU clusters | 100-300 kW per rack | Immersion cooling |

**The Columbia River cooling advantage:**
The Columbia River provides free cooling for the Electric City site. At typical data center PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) of 1.2-1.5, cooling is 20-50% of total facility power consumption. Columbia River water at 10-15°C in summer (vs. 35°C+ air in Eastern Washington summer) provides dramatic cooling efficiency advantages.

**The cooling cost arithmetic:**
- Traditional cooling (chillers): $20-40/MWh of IT load in cooling energy
- Columbia River cooling: ~$1-3/MWh in pumping energy
- Savings at 100 MW IT load: $17-37M/year in cooling energy alone

## 3. The Electric City Site Advantages

Electric City, Washington (Grant County) -- site of the former Vanalco aluminum smelter:

| Advantage | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| BPA hydroelectric rate | $0.02/kWh | BPA Provider of Choice |
| US average electricity rate | ~$0.12/kWh | EIA |
| California commercial rate | ~$0.22/kWh | EIA |
| Cost advantage vs US average | 6x cheaper | Calculation |
| Cost advantage vs California | 11x cheaper | Calculation |
| Columbia River cooling | Free (pumping only) | Site geography |
| Permitted infrastructure | Hundreds of MW | Former aluminum smelter |
| Transmission | Already connected to BPA grid | Existing infrastructure |
| Community designation | Distressed community (EDA eligible) | Grant County |

**The smelter infrastructure:** Aluminum smelting is one of the most electricity-intensive industrial processes. The Vanalco smelter was built to consume massive quantities of cheap BPA power. When aluminum prices dropped and global competition made the smelter uncompetitive, the site was abandoned -- but the electrical infrastructure (transformers, switchgear, transmission connections) remained. A data center can take advantage of this existing infrastructure without rebuilding the electrical plant from scratch.

**EDA distressed community designation:** Grant County's designation as a distressed community makes the Electric City site eligible for EDA economic development grants -- reducing the capital cost of redevelopment and providing justification for federal and state support.

## 4. Fervo's Geothermal Data Center Thesis

Fervo Energy has articulated a thesis about geothermal energy and data center development that directly aligns with the Fox corridor model:

**Fervo's thesis (July 2025 whitepaper):**
> "Electricity demand from data centers is growing faster than new generation can come online. Geothermal is uniquely positioned to serve AI computing because it provides firm, always-on, carbon-free power with no weather dependency -- the only renewable that can guarantee the uptime AI workloads require."

**Fervo's data center corridor concept:**
Fervo envisions geothermal EGS resources co-located with data centers in geothermal-rich regions, eliminating transmission costs and providing 100% renewable power with 90%+ capacity factor. This is precisely the model for the Fox corridor's Cascade Range geothermal resources.

**The Cape Station-to-data-center path:**
- Cape Station Phase I (100 MW): demonstrates EGS at commercial scale
- Cape Station Phase II (400 MW): establishes cost trajectory
- Cascade Range EGS (hypothetical, 2030+): applies proven techniques to PNW geology
- Fox corridor data centers: first load served by Cascade EGS

## 5. Competitive Comparison

The Fox corridor's clean firm power portfolio competes against several alternatives:

**Nuclear restart (Three Mile Island / Microsoft):**
- Advantage: firm, clean, very high capacity factor (~93%)
- Disadvantage: 10+ year construction timeline, $5,000-8,000/kW capital cost, complex NRC licensing
- Verdict: Competitive on power quality; not competitive on timeline or cost

**Natural gas + carbon capture (ExxonMobil model):**
- Advantage: firm power, fast construction (2-3 years)
- Disadvantage: carbon-intensive even with CCS, fuel price volatility, CCUS not fully proven at scale
- Verdict: Not aligned with Washington's 2045 fossil-free mandate

**Grid-scale renewables (wind + solar + storage):**
- Advantage: cheapest levelized cost for energy ($25-50/MWh)
- Disadvantage: not firm; storage adds $100-200/MWh for multi-day backup; cannot guarantee data center SLAs
- Verdict: Competitive on energy cost; not competitive on firm capacity for AI workloads

**Hydro + geothermal (Fox corridor):**
- Advantage: firm, clean, $0.02/kWh baseload hydro + $60-70/MWh geothermal by 2030, river cooling
- Disadvantage: geothermal requires 5-7 years development timeline, EGS risk
- Verdict: Best long-term position for clean firm power in the Pacific Northwest

## 6. The Fox Corridor Phase Development

The Fox Infrastructure Group's Electric City development phases:

**Phase 1 (2026-2028): 50 MW Pilot**
- Power source: BPA hydro exclusively
- Infrastructure: Aluminum smelter building retrofit, 50 MW electrical connection
- Colville Confederated Tribes: majority equity, land use agreement, employment commitments
- Target customers: AI training companies willing to trade location for power cost
- Validation goal: demonstrate tribal-owned data center operations at scale

**Phase 2 (2028-2032): 250 MW Expansion**
- Power source: BPA hydro + first geothermal development (Cascade Range, if Fervo-type EGS matures)
- Infrastructure: New data hall construction, geothermal plant connection
- Target customers: Hyperscale cloud providers seeking clean firm power
- Validation goal: demonstrate clean firm power portfolio operations

**Phase 3 (2032-2040): 1 GW Corridor**
- Power source: Integrated hydro + geothermal + potential nuclear (Columbia Generating Station)
- Infrastructure: Multi-site corridor from Electric City south along Columbia
- Target customers: Federal government computing (defense, weather, finance), global cloud
- Vision: The most electricity-advantaged, tribally governed, clean firm data center corridor in North America
