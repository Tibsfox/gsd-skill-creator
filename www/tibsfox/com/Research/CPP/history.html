Origins — 1979 to 1985

Bjarne Stroustrup at Bell Labs

Bjarne Stroustrup (born 30 December 1950, Aarhus, Denmark) joined the Computing Science Research Center at AT&T Bell Laboratories in Murray Hill, New Jersey, in 1979. Bell Labs was the same building complex where Dennis Ritchie had created C in 1972 and where Ken Thompson had written Unix. Stroustrup was assigned to Center 1127, the Computing Sciences Research Center that gave the world Unix, C, awk, sed, and yacc.

The Cambridge years and Simula

Stroustrup’s PhD (1979, University of Cambridge, Computer Laboratory, supervised by David Wheeler) was on the design of distributed systems. He used Simula 67 — the language created by Ole-Johan Dahl and Kristen Nygaard at the Norwegian Computing Center in Oslo, the first language with classes, objects, inheritance, and virtual procedures. Simula made it possible to express the simulation of operating-system-level concurrency cleanly, but the implementation was so slow that link times exceeded the time available on the Cambridge IBM 360/165. He also encountered BCPL (Martin Richards, Cambridge), which gave him an appreciation for low-level control.

C with Classes — October 1979

The first work began in October 1979. Stroustrup needed to analyze the UNIX kernel for distributability and wanted Simula’s classes plus C’s efficiency. He named the project “C with Classes.” The first preprocessor, Cpre, was written in 1979–1980 and ran on a DEC PDP-11. Cpre translated C-with-Classes source into ordinary C, which the standard C compiler then compiled.

C with Classes (1980) features: classes with public/private members, single inheritance, constructors/destructors, friend functions, function argument type checking, inline functions, default arguments, overloading of =, and the this pointer. There were no virtual functions, no general operator overloading, no references, no templates, no exceptions, no multiple inheritance. The semicolon after a class definition still echoes Simula 67.

Cfront and the rename to C++

In 1982–1983, Stroustrup rewrote the language and the compiler. The new compiler was Cfront, written in C with Classes (then in C++ itself), and like Cpre it generated portable C as its target. Cfront 1.0 was released internally in October 1983.

The name “C++” was suggested by Rick Mascitti in summer 1983. The ++ in C is the post-increment operator, so “C++” reads as “the next C.” It was first used in print in December 1983. From Design and Evolution (page 47): “The name signifies the evolutionary nature of the changes from C.”

First commercial release (October 1985)

C++ was first commercially released by AT&T on 14 October 1985. The same month, Addison-Wesley published the first edition of The C++ Programming Language by Bjarne Stroustrup — October 1985, 327 pages, ISBN 0-201-12078-X. This first edition is now known to C++ historians as TC++PL1 and was the de facto language definition for several years. Cfront Release 1.0 was the reference implementation.

The 1985 language added to C with Classes: virtual functions, function and operator overloading, references (the T& syntax), the const keyword (later adopted into ANSI C), new and delete operators, type-safe linkage, and single-line comments // (borrowed from BCPL).

The Pre-Standard Years — 1985 to 1998

Cfront 2.0 (June 1989)

C++ 2.0 added multiple inheritance, abstract classes (pure virtual functions, the = 0 syntax), static member functions, const member functions, protected access, and pointers to members. Multiple inheritance was controversial from the start; the diamond problem was solved with virtual base classes.

Templates and Exceptions (1990)

Both were added to C++ in 1990. Templates were proposed in Stroustrup’s paper “Parameterized Types for C++” (USENIX C++ Conference, Denver, October 1988). Exceptions were designed jointly by Stroustrup and Andrew Koenig, described in “Exception Handling for C++” (USENIX C++ Conference, San Francisco, April 1990). Cfront 3.0 (1991) was the first compiler to ship templates.

The ARM (1990)

Margaret A. Ellis and Bjarne Stroustrup published The Annotated C++ Reference Manual (Addison-Wesley, 1990, ISBN 0-201-51459-1, 447 pages), known universally as the ARM. The ARM was the de facto standard from 1990 until 1998 and was used as the base document by the ISO/ANSI committee.

The STL (July 1994)

Alexander Stepanov, working at HP Labs Palo Alto with Meng Lee and David Musser, produced the Standard Template Library. Stepanov presented it to the C++ committee in November 1993 and it was voted into the standard at the Waterloo, Ontario meeting in July 1994. Andrew Koenig was Stepanov’s strongest committee advocate. The original STL was placed in the public domain by HP in August 1994.

ISO/IEC committee

ISO/IEC JTC1/SC22/WG21 was formed to standardize C++. The first WG21 meeting was held jointly with the US body X3J16 at the Doubletree Hotel in Somerset, New Jersey, in March 1990, with about thirty attendees, chaired by Dmitry Lenkov of Hewlett-Packard. Committee meetings have run roughly three per year ever since.

C++98 — The First Standard

The first International Standard for C++ was published as ISO/IEC 14882:1998 on 1 September 1998, after eight years of committee work. The standard was 776 pages. The chair of WG21 at the time of publication was Tom Plum (Plum Hall). The project editor was Andrew Koenig. C++03 (October 2003) was a bug-fix release, mostly incorporating defect reports (TC1). TR1 (2005) was a technical report introducing many features that would become C++11.

The Lost Decade and C++11

C++0x dragged on through the 2000s. The most consequential removal was Concepts — paper N2906, dropped at the Frankfurt meeting in July 2009 after years of work. Removing concepts was wrenching but let the rest of the standard ship.

C++11 was finally approved at the Madrid meeting on 12 August 2011. Stroustrup’s famous quote appears in the C++11 FAQ and in the 4th edition of The C++ Programming Language (2013):

“C++11 feels like a new language. That is, I can express my ideas more clearly, more simply, and more directly in C++11 than I could in C++98. Furthermore, the resulting programs are better checked by the compiler and run faster.”

Why C++ Matters

C++ is the backbone of every domain that needs predictable performance and tight control over hardware: web browsers (Chromium, Firefox, WebKit), game engines (Unreal, Unity’s core, id Tech, Godot), databases (MongoDB, ClickHouse, DuckDB, RocksDB), operating systems (Windows kernel components, macOS I/O Kit), scientific computing (ROOT, Eigen, Armadillo), machine learning (TensorFlow, PyTorch, ONNX Runtime cores), high-frequency trading (Hudson River, Citadel, Jump, Tower, Optiver, DRW), and embedded systems (AUTOSAR C++14, MISRA C++). Bloomberg’s codebase is reportedly ~100 million lines of mostly C++. TIOBE and Stack Overflow surveys consistently place C++ in the top 5 languages.

Philosophy and Criticism

Stroustrup’s design rules, stated across his writing, include:

  • Zero-overhead principle: “What you don’t use, you don’t pay for. What you do use, you couldn’t hand code any better.”
  • Trust the programmer, but give them the tools to express intent so the compiler can help.
  • “Make it fit, don’t build a new language.” Additions should feel like C++, not like something bolted on.
  • Don’t deviate from the C model of computation without good reason.

Criticism has targeted: complexity, undefined behavior, template error messages, compile times, the preprocessor, and the C+1 mindset that some shops never left.

Torvalds’ 2007 rant

Linus Torvalds’ famous C++ rant appeared on the git mailing list on 6 September 2007. Asked why git was in C, Torvalds replied:

“C++ is a horrible language. It’s made more horrible by the fact that a lot of substandard programmers use it … In other words, the only way to do good, efficient, and system-level and portable C++ ends up to limit yourself to all the things that are basically available in C.”

The memory safety debate

The NSA’s November 2022 Software Memory Safety guidance, the White House ONCD’s February 2024 report Back to the Building Blocks, and CISA’s 2023 guidance all called on software developers to migrate to memory-safe languages for new development. C and C++ were named as languages “where memory safety is not guaranteed.”

The C++ committee’s response has been Stroustrup’s Safety Profiles proposal — static-analysis-enforceable subsets that reject raw owning pointers, out-of-bounds access, and uninitialized reads. Combined with modern-style RAII and smart pointers, the argument is that most memory safety issues can be caught without rewriting in another language. The debate is ongoing.
