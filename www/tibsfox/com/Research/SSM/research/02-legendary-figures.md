## 1. The Canonical Six

Pike Place Market's busking tradition is carried by hundreds of performers across five decades. But six figures stand as the canonical representatives of the tradition -- the ones whose careers are inseparable from the Market itself, whose stories define what it means to be a Pike Place busker, and whose collective arc traces the full history from pre-legalization through the COVID era.

These six are not simply the most famous Market performers (though some of them are). They are the ones whose lives most fully embody the tradition: who shaped the legal framework, who demonstrated the career pipeline, who persisted through decades of daily performance, and who articulated -- in their music and in their words -- what the painted note on the sidewalk means.

## 2. Artis the Spoonman

### Early Life and Arrival

Artis the Spoonman was born October 3, 1948. He began performing spoons at age ten -- a start so early that by the time he reached Pike Place Market, he had been playing for over two decades. After a stint in the US Navy, he began hitchhiking and busking across the country in the early 1970s [1].

He moved to Seattle from Santa Cruz. In those early Seattle years, he frequented Fremont taverns, playing what he called "jukebox duets" -- accompanying whatever was on the jukebox with his spoons, performing for tips from bar patrons. He established his Pike Place Market presence beginning in 1972, making him one of the earliest documented performers in the Market's modern busking era [1].

His account of the pre-legalization Market is a key primary source: "Aside from street fairs, the Market was one of the only venues for buskers in the early 1970s" [1]. This places Artis at the Market two years before Jim Page's legalization campaign -- performing without legal protection, in a city where street music was technically a citable offense.

### The Instrument as Identity

Before discussing Artis's career milestones, it is worth considering his instrument. Spoons are not a conventional musical instrument. They are kitchen utensils. The decision to perform professionally with spoons -- to build an entire career on an instrument that most people associate with eating soup -- is a radical artistic statement.

Spoon percussion is ancient, appearing in folk traditions across Europe, Africa, and the Americas. But professional spoon performance -- the elevation of spoon-playing from folk novelty to a concert-grade percussion art -- is vanishingly rare. Artis is, by most accounts, the most accomplished and most visible spoon percussionist in American music history. His technique involves complex polyrhythmic patterns created by striking spoons against various surfaces of his own body -- legs, arms, chest -- creating a one-man percussion ensemble from two pieces of flatware.

The spoons are also a visual spectacle. Unlike a drummer hidden behind a kit, a spoon percussionist's body IS the instrument. Every strike is visible. The performance is inherently physical, theatrical, and immediate -- qualities that make it perfectly suited to street performance, where the audience's first engagement is visual (seeing something unusual) before it becomes auditory (hearing the rhythm).

### The Frank Zappa Collaboration

Artis's first major collaboration came in 1981, when he appeared onstage with Frank Zappa in Eugene, Oregon, and at New York's Palladium [1]. Zappa, whose career was built on genre-defiant musicianship and an appreciation for unconventional instruments, recognized in Artis a performer whose medium was as inventive as any guitarist or drummer.

The Zappa collaboration established a pattern that would define Artis's career: the street performer moving into the formal music world not by abandoning his instrument or his style, but by bringing them whole. He did not learn guitar to get a record deal. He played spoons on Zappa's stage, on Soundgarden's record, on national television -- and then he went back to the Market.

### Soundgarden and "Spoonman"

The career-defining moment came in 1992 at Bumbershoot Seattle, where Artis performed as a "tweener" between Soundgarden and the Melvins. Soundgarden manager Susan Silver told him that Chris Cornell was writing a song called "Spoonman" and asked if Artis would like to record on it [2].

In 1993, Artis went to the studio. He cut his part in four takes [2].

"Spoonman" was released February 14, 1994, as the lead single from Soundgarden's *Superunknown*. Artis played the spoon solo in the bridge -- the rhythmic percussion break that gives the song its distinctive texture. The song peaked at No. 3 on the Billboard Mainstream Rock Tracks chart and No. 9 on the Modern Rock Tracks chart [2] [3].

At the 1995 Grammy Awards, "Spoonman" won Best Metal Performance. Chris Cornell described the song's theme in terms that captured the paradox of street performance: "It's more about the paradox of who he is and what people perceive him as. He's a street musician, but when he's playing on the street, he is given a value and judged completely wrong by someone else" [3].

### Compensation and the Grammy Paradox

Artis received a flat fee of $1,000 for performing on the recording and $7,000 for appearing in the music video. In the video, Soundgarden appears only in black-and-white still photographs, making Artis -- the street performer -- the film's sole moving subject [2].

He earned no Grammy invitation. He received no royalties beyond his flat fee. For a multi-platinum single that won the highest award the recording industry bestows, the street musician at its center was paid $8,000 total [2].

Artis remains sanguine about this arrangement: "Who ever had a Grammy Award-winning song, a multiplatinum song about himself?" [2].

This is the essential Artis paradox -- and, in a larger sense, the essential busking paradox. The street musician's value is both celebrated and systematically undercompensated. Cornell's song recognizes the paradox. The compensation structure embodies it.

### The Music Video as Document

The "Spoonman" music video deserves separate analysis as a cultural document. In the video, Soundgarden's members appear only in black-and-white still photographs. Artis is the sole moving subject -- the only person performing in real time on screen [2]. This creative decision, presumably made by the director, inadvertently produced the most honest visual representation of the street performer's condition: the musician in motion, the world around him frozen.

The video inverts the normal power dynamic of music videos, where the band is the center and guests are decorative. Here, the street musician is the center. The platinum-selling grunge band is literally still. It is a remarkable document -- perhaps the only major-label music video in which a busker, performing his busking instrument, is the visual and narrative protagonist.

### The Cultural Bridge

Artis's significance extends beyond his own career. He functions as a cultural bridge between the street performance tradition and the mainstream music industry. Before "Spoonman," the idea that a street musician's instrument could anchor a hit single was implausible. After "Spoonman," it was proven. The bridge existed. Other performers could see it.

This bridge function is not metaphorical. When The Head and the Heart busked in the Market stairwell a decade later, they did so in a cultural context that Artis had helped create -- one in which Pike Place busking was not merely tolerated but recognized as a legitimate origin story for a serious music career.

### Later Years

Artis suffered a heart attack in 2002. A benefit concert at a Seattle festival raised over $3,200 for his medical expenses [2]. The fact that a community benefit was necessary -- that a performer with a Grammy credit to his name had no health insurance and no savings to cover medical expenses -- underscores the economic precarity that defines street performance even at its most celebrated.

He has lived in Port Townsend, Washington since 2009. His longtime performing partner at the Market is Jim Page. The move to Port Townsend -- a small arts-oriented town on the Olympic Peninsula -- represents a common trajectory for aging performers in the Pacific Northwest: migration from the urban busking scene to a smaller community where the cost of living is lower and the artistic community is intimate.

## 3. Jim Page

### Background and Arrival

Jim Page was born in 1949 in Palo Alto, California. He spent a year in New York's Greenwich Village before arriving in Seattle in 1971 [4]. He brought with him a guitar, a repertoire of political folk songs in the tradition of Woody Guthrie and Buffy Sainte-Marie, and an implicit understanding -- forged in the Village's tradition of music as political action -- that the street is not merely a venue but a constitutional space.

### The Legalization Campaign

Page's 1974 campaign to legalize busking in Seattle is documented in full in [Module 1: Historical Roots](01-historical-roots.md). The essential facts: a motorcycle cop threatened him at Oliver's Meats on lower Pike Street; he discovered that permits were available only to blind and disabled persons; he persuaded City Council member Randy Revelle to arrange a public hearing; he posted flyers reading "Jim Page -- Live at City Council!"; he composed and performed "Now's the Time for Talking" before a packed chamber; the vote was unanimous; Ordinance 103824 was signed September 20, 1974 [4] [5].

The significance of Page's campaign extends far beyond Seattle. By establishing the legal principle that street performance is protected expression rather than commercial vending, Ordinance 103824 provided a template that cities across the country would reference in their own busking legislation.

### The Buskers' Guild

In 2000-2001, Page co-founded the Pike Place Market Buskers' Guild -- the first formal organization of Market performers, which organized the annual Buskers' Festival beginning in September 2001 [4] [6]. The Guild represented a maturation of the busking community from an informal assemblage of individual performers into a self-governing collective with the capacity to negotiate with the Market PDA, coordinate scheduling, and advocate for performer interests.

### Career Scope

Seattle Metropolitan Magazine named Jim Page one of the 50 Most Influential Musicians in Seattle History -- a list he shares with Jimi Hendrix, Nirvana, and Bill Frisell [7]. He has toured 17 countries and released 23 full-length albums. His songs have been covered by the Doobie Brothers, Christy Moore, and Michael Hedges. He continues to perform at the Market [4].

Page's career arc is significant precisely because it does not follow the conventional "discovered on the street, moved to the studio" trajectory. He has maintained his Market presence for over five decades while simultaneously building an international touring and recording career. The street and the stage are not, for Page, sequential phases of a career. They are simultaneous practices.

### Page as Institutional Builder

Beyond his personal career, Page's contribution to the busking ecosystem is structural. He legalized the practice. He co-founded the Guild that gave performers collective representation. He organized the Festival that gave the tradition public visibility. He is, in institutional terms, both a performer and an architect -- someone who not only practiced within the system but built the system in which others could practice.

This dual role -- artist and organizer -- places Page in a lineage that runs through the folk music tradition from Woody Guthrie through Pete Seeger through the modern singer-songwriter movement. The folk tradition has always understood that making music and making the conditions for music are not separate activities. Page's 1974 testimony is the purest expression of this understanding: the act of singing at city hall was simultaneously a performance and a political action, and neither could have been effective without the other.

### Page's Enduring Practice

As of 2025, Page continues to perform at Pike Place Market. His presence connects the current generation of buskers directly to the tradition's founding moment. A new performer who stands at a painted note in 2025 is standing in a legal space that Jim Page created by singing at city hall in 1974. The lineage is not abstract. It is a living connection maintained by daily practice.

## 4. Jeanie Towne

### The Blind Harpist

For decades, Jeanie Towne was known as "the blind harpist at the Market." She was blind, but she was emphatic that blindness not define her. This insistence is itself a primary source datum: any documentation of Towne must follow her own framing, not the disability narrative that outsiders might impose [8].

### Daily Practice

Towne rode a bus from Ballard to the Market every day with her instruments. She lived off her busking. The logistics of this daily commute -- a blind woman carrying a harp and associated equipment on public transit from Ballard to Pike Place Market -- represent a physical commitment to the practice of street music that is difficult to overstate [8].

### Writing and Advocacy

Towne was not only a performer but a writer. She contributed to the Pike Place Market News, publishing articles on subjects ranging from kosher food to algae. She appeared as the inspiration for a 1990s comic strip called "Adventures of a Sightless Streetsinger" [8].

Her writing for the Seattle Times in the 1980s articulated the philosophy of street music with clarity that few academic treatments have matched: "I feel that soothing the grind of city noises, raining out peoples' blues, and kindling some hope and cheer are as useful and challenging an occupation as any" [8].

This quotation is significant because it frames busking not as entertainment but as a vocation -- a calling with social utility comparable to any conventional profession. The "grind of city noises" is not merely noise but a condition to be addressed. The busker is not a distraction from urban life but a treatment for it.

### The Accessibility Pioneer

Towne's daily practice of commuting from Ballard to the Market with her instruments makes her, in practical terms, an accessibility pioneer. She demonstrated that blindness was not a barrier to professional street performance -- not through accommodation or assistance, but through sheer practice and refusal to accept limitation.

In the pre-ADA era when Towne established her Market presence, public transit accessibility was not guaranteed. Navigating Seattle's bus system with a harp while blind required an intimate knowledge of routes, stops, and vehicle layouts that sighted riders could take for granted. Her daily commute was itself a performance of competence -- proof that the barriers to participation were not in her body but in the assumptions others made about her body.

This dimension of Towne's story connects to the legal history documented in Module 3. Before 1974, street performing permits were available only to blind and disabled persons. The law assumed that blindness made someone a suitable object of public charity -- hence the permit. Towne inverted this assumption. She performed not because she was blind but because she was a musician. The blindness was incidental. The music was the point.

### COVID and Death

The Market prohibited busking in March 2020 due to COVID-19. Towne was too frail to return when performances resumed. She died after the pandemic. She had been asking to be driven to the Market to play "as recently as a few weeks ago" before her death [8].

Artis the Spoonman's assessment: "A legend." "You practically had to put your ear up to her harp to hear her, but you did. There was nobody like..." [8].

The unfinished sentence in Artis's quotation is preserved here exactly as published. It says more about Towne than any complete sentence could.

## 5. Jim Hinde

### The Quiet Anchor

Jim Hinde was a long-time Market fixture who, according to the Seattle Times, "helped define Pike Place Market" [9]. The details of his performing style and repertoire are less thoroughly documented than those of Artis or Page, but his significance to the Market community is attested by the scale of his memorial and the consistency of his presence.

Hinde represents a type of performer that every busking community depends on but rarely celebrates: the daily anchor. Not the performer who wins a Grammy or plays the Grand Ole Opry, but the one who is simply there -- day after day, year after year -- creating the consistency that makes the Market feel like a place where music always happens. Without performers like Hinde, the Market's busking tradition would be a series of isolated events rather than a continuous presence.

### The Veterans Connection

Hinde died in 2008. His memorial was held inside the Market's main arcade on July 2, 2008 -- a venue choice that was itself a statement about where he belonged. Jim Page performed "Jim's Song" in his honor. Artis the Spoonman was a close associate. Donations to the Jim Hinde Memorial Fund were directed to veterans organizations [9].

The direction of memorial donations to veterans organizations suggests a military connection in Hinde's background, placing him in the same lineage as Artis (US Navy) and connecting the Market's busking tradition to the broader pattern of military veterans finding purpose and community in street performance.

### Memorial as Statement

The fact that Hinde's memorial was held in the main arcade -- the same space where he had performed for decades -- speaks to the relationship between performer and place that defines Pike Place busking. The Market was not merely where Hinde worked. It was where he was mourned. The community chose to gather at the painted notes, in the arcade, surrounded by the vendors and the fish and the flowers, because that was where Hinde belonged. The memorial was a performance in the deepest sense: the community performing its grief in the space where it had performed its joy.

## 6. Baby Gramps

### Style and Recognition

Baby Gramps is a guitarist and steel-guitar player recognizable by his wide-brimmed signature hat. He plays with finger-picks and occasional elbow-chords on steel guitar -- a technique that is as visually arresting as it is musically distinctive [10].

His genre is fluid. "Vaudeville-adjacent" is the most common descriptor, but it captures only part of a repertoire that draws on blues, ragtime, hokum, and a performance style that is equal parts concert and theatrical event. He is documented in the Market's "Street Music" profile series [10].

### Performance Style as Cultural Preservation

Baby Gramps's performance style is itself a form of cultural preservation. The techniques he employs -- finger-picking, elbow-chords on steel guitar, the theatrical persona, the audience banter -- are derived from vaudeville-era performance practices that have largely disappeared from mainstream music. By performing these techniques daily at the Market, Baby Gramps keeps alive a performance tradition that predates the recording industry.

This preservation function is not deliberate in the academic sense. Baby Gramps does not perform vaudeville techniques because he is trying to preserve them. He performs them because they are his art. But the effect is the same: a living repository of performance knowledge that exists in practice rather than in archives.

### National Cult Following

Baby Gramps has achieved cult national recognition from his Market origins. His trajectory illustrates a different version of the pavement-to-stage pipeline than The Head and the Heart's or Sierra Ferrell's. Where those artists left the Market for conventional recording careers, Baby Gramps built a national following while remaining aesthetically rooted in the street performance tradition. His concerts feel like busking -- the intimacy, the audience interaction, the sense that the music could start or stop at any moment -- regardless of whether they take place on a sidewalk or in a theater.

## 7. Jonny Hahn

### 34 Years of Piano

Jonny Hahn has performed at Pike Place Market for 34 years. His instrument is a 64-key acoustic piano -- not a keyboard, not an electronic piano, but a full acoustic upright that he has wrestled to his corner every day for over three decades [11].

The physicality of this commitment is worth pausing on. A 64-key acoustic piano weighs approximately 200-300 pounds. Transporting it to the Market, positioning it at the performance spot, performing for the duration of the set, and then transporting it back -- this is not a musician casually playing some tunes. This is an athletic and logistical undertaking repeated daily for 34 years.

### The COVID Experience

Hahn's op-ed in the Seattle Times (July 24, 2020) is the single most significant primary source document on the COVID-19 impact on Market busking. It documents the lived experience of a performer whose livelihood was eliminated by the pandemic restrictions [11].

Key facts from Hahn's account:

- The Market prohibited busking in March 2020
- A brief reopening occurred July 5-July 20, 2020
- The second prohibition was rescinded even as indoor businesses reopened under Phase 2
- Hahn described the policy as "highly unjust" given the outdoor nature of busking
- He relocated to perform beneath the old Green Lake Aqua Theater during the prohibition
- He wrote: "How strange and unsettling it is to suddenly find that my livelihood has been banned by the governor"

When the Market reopened to performers on June 25, 2021, Hahn was among the first to return. His account of the emotional response: "It was just heart energy spilling over. People just kept saying how glad they were to have me back" [11].

### The Smartphone Thesis

Hahn's most analytically significant observation concerns not COVID but the longer-term erosion of public attention. With 30+ years of comparative data from the same performance location, Hahn has a longitudinal dataset that no academic study can match:

"It started with smartphones. People's attention spans were diminished by orders of magnitude. Constant texting and Googling and taking photos completely altered public space" [11].

This observation identifies what Hahn describes as a fundamental transformation of the public-attention economy that predates COVID and will outlast it. The smartphone did not merely distract individuals -- it changed the nature of public space itself, converting pedestrians from potential audience members into screen-gazers who pass through without looking up.

Hahn's conclusion is pointed: the Market was once "a busker's paradise." Whether it remains one is an open question [11].

## 8. The Oral Tradition Problem

### What Is Not Written Down

The canonical six represent the documented performers -- those whose careers generated enough media coverage, published interviews, and institutional records to be reconstructable from primary sources. But the Pike Place busking tradition includes hundreds of performers whose careers are not documented at this level.

The oral history of the Market's busking scene -- the stories performers tell each other, the informal rules they transmit, the practical knowledge about which spots work best in which weather, which times of day draw which audiences, which repertoire connects with tourists versus locals -- exists primarily in memory. When a performer retires, relocates, or dies, their contribution to this oral tradition goes with them unless someone has recorded it.

Jeanie Towne's death illustrates this problem acutely. She was a writer herself -- she published in the Market News, she wrote to the Seattle Times -- but the details of her daily practice, her performance techniques, her audience relationships, her knowledge of the Market's acoustic spaces are not preserved in her published writing. They existed in her daily presence at the Market, and when she could no longer be present, they were gone.

### The Documentation Imperative

This module is itself an attempt to address the oral tradition problem. By documenting the canonical six with as much primary-source detail as available, it creates a reference point for future researchers, performers, and community members who want to understand what the tradition was and how its practitioners experienced it.

But documentation is not preservation. The written record of a performer's career is not the same as the performer's presence. Artis's spoons sound different on a recording than they do in the arcade. Towne's harp was meant to be heard from two feet away, not read about in a research module. The documentation preserves facts. The tradition preserves experience. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

## 9. The Community They Built

### Interconnections

The six canonical performers are not isolated figures. They are a community, connected by decades of shared space, mutual support, and collaborative performance.

- **Artis and Jim Page** are longtime performing partners at the Market
- **Jim Hinde** was a close associate of Artis; Page performed at his memorial
- **Jeanie Towne** was described as "a legend" by Artis
- **The Buskers' Guild** that Page co-founded formalized the community structures that all six inhabited
- **Hahn's COVID op-ed** documented the collective experience of a community, not merely an individual grievance

### The Economic Mutual Aid

The community also functioned as an informal mutual aid network. When Artis suffered a heart attack in 2002, the community organized a benefit concert. When Jim Hinde died in 2008, Page performed at his memorial. When Jeanie Towne could no longer travel to the Market, other performers maintained her memory and her significance in the community's oral tradition.

This mutual aid function is not formalized in the Guild structure or the permit system. It exists in the relationships between performers -- the decades of shared experience, the daily interactions at the painted notes, the knowledge that the person playing at the next spot has been doing this as long as you have and faces the same economic precarity, the same weather, the same indifferent crowds.

The economic vulnerability of busking makes mutual aid essential. Without health insurance, retirement plans, or stable income, performers depend on each other in ways that salaried workers do not. A performer who suffers a health crisis has no employer to provide sick leave. A performer who cannot perform has no income. The community fills the gap -- imperfectly, informally, but consistently.

### The Generational Thread

Together, the six span the entire modern history of Pike Place busking:

- **Pre-legalization pioneers:** Artis (1972), Page (1971)
- **Post-legalization growth:** Towne (late 1970s), Hinde (1970s), Baby Gramps (1980s)
- **Institutional maturation:** Hahn (c. 1986), arriving after the 1983 Hildt Agreement formalized busker governance
- **COVID and aftermath:** Hahn (op-ed and return), Towne (death), Artis (Port Townsend relocation)

This generational arc -- from illegality through legalization through formalization through crisis -- is the story of Pike Place busking told through six lives.
