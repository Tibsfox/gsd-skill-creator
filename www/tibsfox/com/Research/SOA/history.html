History & Origins

In the spring of 2003, a marketing executive at Gartner Group dropped a phrase into a research note that would, within twenty-four months, become the single most contested piece of jargon in enterprise computing. The phrase was Service-Oriented Architecture. By 2006, every CIO had an “SOA strategy.” By 2009, an analyst named Anne Thomas Manes would declare the same three letters dead. By 2014, the ghost of SOA would shamble back into the conversation wearing a new suit and calling itself microservices. By 2024, practitioners would quietly admit that SOA had been right all along — it had just been too early, too heavy, and too married to a generation of vendors who mistook standards bodies for revenue models.

Part I — Prehistory (1960s–1988)

Time-sharing and the first shared services

Before you can have a service-oriented architecture you need to have the concept of a service. The earliest intellectual ancestor is time-sharing. John McCarthy at MIT, in a famous 1959 memo, proposed that a single computer could be shared across many interactive terminals, each user getting the illusion of a private machine. CTSS — the Compatible Time-Sharing System — went live at MIT in November 1961. Multics (1964, MIT + Bell Labs + GE) made the abstraction explicit: the computer as a utility. By the late 1960s, GE Information Services and Tymshare were selling computing cycles by the minute. The essential SOA move — “I call a thing out there, it does work for me, I get a response” — was already present.

Birrell & Nelson — the RPC paper (1984)

In February 1984, ACM Transactions on Computer Systems published the Ur-text of distributed computing: “Implementing Remote Procedure Calls” by Andrew D. Birrell and Bruce Jay Nelson, both at Xerox PARC. The intellectual move: treat the network as invisible. Calling a procedure on another machine should look exactly like calling a procedure on the local machine. Cedar RPC achieved about 1,100 null RPCs per second on an Alto-to-Alto link — blistering for 1984. Nelson had coined “remote procedure call” in his 1981 CMU PhD under Jim Morris.

The critical thing to notice: the RPC abstraction leaks. Jim Waldo, Geoff Wyant, Ann Wollrath, and Sam Kendall would later write a 1994 Sun technical report, A Note on Distributed Computing (SMLI TR-94-29), arguing with mordant clarity that local and remote calls differ in four fundamental ways: latency, memory access, partial failure, and concurrency. Pretending these differences do not exist leads to systems that work in demo and fail in production. This critique simmered throughout the 1980s and 1990s, and eventually became the REST critique of SOAP/WSDL.

Sun followed in 1985 with Sun RPC (later ONC RPC, RFC 1057, 1988), which became the foundation for NFS. Apollo Computer built NCS (Network Computing System), which became HP’s contribution to OSF DCE (Distributed Computing Environment, 1992). DCE bundled DCE RPC, Kerberos v5, directory services, distributed time, DFS, and threads. It worked. It was also legendarily painful to administer — the lesson, which SOA would rediscover at scale, was that distributed systems have an operational tax that scales nonlinearly with the number of services.

Part II — The CORBA Era (1989–2000)

The Object Management Group

On April 11, 1989, eleven companies (3Com, American Airlines, Canon, Data General, HP, Philips, Sun, Unisys, and others) formed the Object Management Group. The thesis: if objects were going to be the organizing principle of software, distributed objects must be the organizing principle of enterprise software.

CORBA 1.0 (October 1991) defined IDL, the Object Request Broker architecture, and C language bindings. CORBA 2.0 (August 1996) added IIOP (Internet Inter-ORB Protocol) as the mandatory wire protocol — finally allowing ORBs from different vendors to interoperate, at least in principle.

The ORB vendor wars

The mid-1990s saw a genuine commercial ORB market:

  • IONA Technologies (Dublin, 1991) — Orbix. IPO’d 1997 at ~$550M valuation.
  • Visigenic (1993) — VisiBroker. Acquired by Borland/Inprise 1997 for $133M.
  • BEA Systems (1995) — WebLogic started as an ORB.
  • HP ORB Plus, Sun NEO, IBM Component Broker.

On top of the core ORB, CORBAservices added Naming, Events, Transactions (OTS), Security, Query, Trader, Time, Licensing, and Property services. In theory, these combined into a complete enterprise platform. In practice, only a handful saw meaningful adoption and vendors implemented them inconsistently.

Why CORBA is remembered as a partial failure

  1. Complexity. The full stack was vast. Learning curve was punishing.
  2. Vendor lock-in. Despite IIOP, interoperability between vendors was fragile.
  3. The firewall problem. IIOP ran on arbitrary TCP ports. Corporate firewalls blocked it.
  4. Byte order and wire format surprises. Cross-vendor CDR incompatibilities were endemic.
  5. C++ impedance mismatch. Memory management of CORBA objects (reference counting, _duplicate, _release) was a minefield.
  6. The rise of Java and RMI (1997), and then Java EE, which swallowed CORBA from inside (RMI-over-IIOP).
  7. The rise of the Web. HTTP was simple, firewall-friendly, ubiquitous.

CORBA was deployed (telecoms, banking, air traffic control, global financial settlement networks are still running CORBA in 2026) and it worked in production. But it never became the universal substrate of enterprise computing. Its IDL pattern, however, directly influenced gRPC Protocol Buffers, Thrift, and OpenAPI — CORBA’s best idea outlived the framework.

Part III — Microsoft and the Component Wars (1993–1999)

Microsoft’s path was different. COM (Component Object Model, 1993) — the work of Tony Williams, Kraig Brockschmidt, Bob Atkinson, Mark Ryland — defined a binary interface standard rather than an object model. A COM interface was a vtable with a UUID. Objects exposed interfaces via QueryInterface. This was the sharpest binary interface standard ever designed.

DCOM (1996) extended COM across the network, forking DCE RPC as the wire protocol. MTS (Microsoft Transaction Server, December 1996) provided declarative transactions and component lifecycle — directly inspiring Enterprise JavaBeans.

By 1997, enterprise architects faced a three-way religious war: CORBA vs DCOM vs Enterprise JavaBeans. Conferences were organized around these camps. Trade magazines ran cover stories. Consultants billed massive fees. The religious intensity is hard to convey to anyone who wasn’t there.

The resolution came from an unlikely direction: none of them won. What won was a new approach that treated distributed computing not as object invocation but as document exchange. That approach was called Web Services, and its arrival would kill the component wars within five years by obsoleting the premise of the argument.

Part IV — Java EE and the Application Server Era (1998–2005)

J2EE 1.0 was announced December 1998. It bundled servlets, JSP, EJB 1.0, JDBC, JNDI, JMS, JTA/JTS (built on CORBA OTS), RMI-IIOP (the CORBA wire protocol as the EJB substrate), and JavaMail.

EJB: the beautiful and the damned

EJB had three bean types: Session, Entity, Message-Driven. You wrote a remote interface, a home interface, an implementation class, and an XML deployment descriptor. In theory: beautiful separation of business logic from infrastructure. In practice: widely considered a nightmare.

  • Entity beans were slow. Container-managed persistence in EJB 2.0 could be 100× worse than hand-tuned JDBC.
  • Deployment descriptors ballooned. Nontrivial applications had thousands of lines of ejb-jar.xml and vendor-specific equivalents.
  • Tooling was fragile. Moving an EJB JAR from WebLogic to WebSphere was a multi-week porting exercise.

The app server market exploded: BEA WebLogic (acquired by Oracle 2008 for $8.5B), IBM WebSphere, Sun GlassFish, JBoss (Marc Fleury, 1999; acquired by Red Hat 2006 for $350M). WebSphere alone was doing >$1 billion annually.

Rod Johnson and Spring

In 2002, an Australian consultant named Rod Johnson — a former physicist with a PhD in musicology — was assigned to a project drowning in EJB complexity. He wrote a 700-page book published October 2002 as Expert One-on-One J2EE Design and Development. The book argued, with exhaustive evidence, that most J2EE applications should not use EJB at all. He distributed the code samples from the book as a framework. In March 2004 he and Juergen Hoeller released Spring Framework 1.0.

Spring was, in the most literal sense, a counter-revolution against EJB. Dependency injection, AOP, POJO programming model, no forced inheritance. By 2006 it was dominant. Interface21 (later SpringSource) was acquired by VMware in August 2009 for $420M.

The Spring vs EJB debate is directly relevant to the SOA story because it crystallized a cultural split that would be repeated throughout the SOA era: big vendors + big specs + big consulting fees vs. lightweight frameworks + developer productivity + convention over configuration. REST vs SOAP, microservices vs ESB, YAGNI culture of the mid-2010s — all carry the same emotional DNA as Spring vs EJB.

Part V — XML-RPC, SOAP, and the WS-* Swamp

Dave Winer and XML-RPC (1998)

In spring 1998, Dave Winer — creator of Frontier, future co-creator of RSS — was running UserLand Software in Woodside, California. He needed a way for his CMS to call scripts on other servers. He corresponded with Microsoft engineers Bob Atkinson and Mohsen Al-Ghosein, and by April 1998 they had a working specification: XML over HTTP POST. XML-RPC was published June 15, 1998. Winer insisted on keeping the spec minimal. The whole specification fit on a single web page.

Winer’s philosophy: keep it simple. He would later tell the story that when Microsoft wanted to add more features, he resisted. “We had something that worked. We were going to break it by making it complicated.” This is a theme that comes back again and again: the simpler ancestor of every protocol family is usually the one that actually gets used.

SOAP: the Microsoft extension (1999–2000)

Microsoft wanted more. In 1999, a team led by Don Box (DevelopMentor, then Microsoft), Dave Winer, Mohsen Al-Ghosein, Bob Atkinson, and others began work on a more elaborate protocol. SOAP 1.1 was published as a W3C Note on May 8, 2000, co-authored by Don Box, David Ehnebuske (IBM), Gopal Kakivaya (Microsoft), Andrew Layman (Microsoft), Noah Mendelsohn (Lotus), Henrik Frystyk Nielsen (Microsoft), Satish Thatte (Microsoft), and Dave Winer. The coalition was Microsoft, IBM, Lotus, UserLand, DevelopMentor.

Don Box wrote one of the most influential essays of the early Web Services era: A Young Person’s Guide to the Simple Object Access Protocol (MSDN Magazine, March 2000). Box had the rare gift of writing about enterprise middleware without sounding like a committee press release.

WSDL and UDDI (2000–2001)

WSDL was submitted to W3C in September 2000 as a joint IBM/Microsoft/Ariba proposal. Lead authors: Erik Christensen (Microsoft), Francisco Curbera (IBM), Greg Meredith (Microsoft), Sanjiva Weerawarana (IBM, later WSO2). WSDL 1.1 became a W3C Note in March 2001. A nontrivial WSDL file was 500–2000 lines of dense XML.

UDDI (Universal Description, Discovery, and Integration) — the “Yellow Pages of the global service economy.” Announced September 2000 by IBM/Microsoft/Ariba. IBM and Microsoft ran the public UDDI Business Registry. In January 2006, IBM, Microsoft, and SAP simultaneously shut down their public UDDI nodes. The Yellow Pages had no listings. UDDI was dead.

The WS-* swamp

Once SOAP, WSDL, and UDDI were in place, the industry embarked on the WS-* explosion:

  • WS-Security (OASIS, April 2004) — signing and encrypting SOAP messages
  • WS-Addressing (W3C, May 2006)
  • WS-ReliableMessaging (OASIS, June 2007)
  • WS-AtomicTransaction / WS-Coordination / WS-BusinessActivity (OASIS, 2005)
  • WS-Policy (W3C, September 2007)
  • WS-Trust, WS-Federation, WS-SecureConversation, WS-MetadataExchange
  • WS-Notification, WS-Eventing, WS-Discovery, WS-ResourceFramework
  • WS-BPEL (OASIS, April 2007) — business process orchestration
  • WS-I Basic Profile — an attempt to specify a minimum interoperable subset, effectively admitting the full stack was too sprawling to interoperate

By 2007, there were over sixty WS-* specifications. The set became known informally as the WS-* swamp. The problem was not any single spec — many addressed real problems thoughtfully. The problem was aggregate complexity. A production deployment using WS-Security + WS-Addressing + WS-ReliableMessaging + WS-AtomicTransaction + WS-Policy required engineers to understand each spec, the specific vendor interpretations, and the interactions between them. The learning curve was measured in person-years.

Part VI — Roy Fielding and REST (1994–2000)

Roy Thomas Fielding was a graduate student at UC Irvine under Professor Richard N. Taylor in the mid-1990s. He was also one of the principal authors of HTTP 1.0 and HTTP 1.1 at the W3C, a founding member of the Apache HTTP Server Project (1995), and a regular IETF participant. Fielding was in a uniquely position: simultaneously helping design the protocol that ran the Web, helping implement one of the most-used server packages in the world, and thinking academically about what made the Web’s architecture work.

The dissertation (2000)

Fielding’s 2000 PhD dissertation, Architectural Styles and the Design of Network-based Software Architectures, is 180 pages. It is one of the most widely-read and cited documents in the history of software architecture. The core contribution is a taxonomy of architectural styles, and a new style Fielding named REST (Representational State Transfer), defined by six constraints:

  1. Client-server
  2. Statelessness
  3. Cacheability
  4. Uniform interface (with four sub-constraints, including HATEOAS)
  5. Layered system
  6. Code-on-demand (optional)

The argument: the Web, as it actually exists, is an implementation of REST. HTTP + URIs + HTML (or JSON) forms a REST architecture, and the Web’s success is attributable to the REST constraints.

REST as a critique of RPC

The dissertation contains an implicit and often-explicit critique of the RPC tradition all the way back to Birrell and Nelson. RPC tries to make a distributed system look like a local program by hiding the network; REST embraces the network by exposing it.

SOAP + WSDL was, at its heart, an RPC mechanism layered on HTTP. It used HTTP only as a transport for tunneling SOAP-encoded method calls. It ignored HTTP verbs (almost all SOAP calls were POSTs), ignored URIs (all calls went to the same endpoint with an operation name in the body), ignored caching, and ignored content negotiation. In Fielding’s terms, SOAP was not a Web architecture — it was an RPC architecture that happened to run over HTTP.

When Fielding was asked, years later, whether architectures built on SOAP and WSDL were “RESTful,” his response (paraphrasing): no, they were RPC systems mislabeled as Web architectures. He became famous — and, among WS-* advocates, notorious — for publishing strict definitions and calling out misuse of the term.

The Richardson Maturity Model (2008)

In 2008, software architect Leonard Richardson gave a QCon San Francisco talk proposing a four-level maturity model:

  • Level 0: The Swamp of POX — single endpoint, single verb. SOAP lives here.
  • Level 1: Resources — distinct URIs, but one HTTP verb.
  • Level 2: HTTP Verbs — proper verbs and status codes. Most “REST APIs” live here.
  • Level 3: Hypermedia Controls — HATEOAS. Almost nobody.

Martin Fowler popularized the model in a March 2010 blog post. Fielding himself noted that only Level 3 is actually REST. Levels 0, 1, and 2 are HTTP-based web APIs, but they are not REST architectures. The vast majority of “RESTful APIs” in production are Level 2, and Fielding-purists continue to point this out, to the continued annoyance of everyone else.

Why “RESTful” won anyway

For all the pedantry, the name won. By 2008–2010, “RESTful API” was the default thing you built when someone asked for a web API:

  • JSON over XML — Rails and Django communities shifted from XML to JSON.
  • The iPhone effect — iPhone launched June 2007, App Store July 2008. Every company needed a mobile backend. REST+JSON was perfect; SOAP+XML was a disaster.
  • Tooling disappeared — curl | jq replaced an entire category of code generation.
  • Documentation by example — human-written pages beat 2000-line WSDL.
  • The Rails movement — Rails 1.0 (December 2005), Rails 2.0 (December 2007) made REST-by-convention the default.
  • Twitter and public APIs — Twitter’s 2006 API proved public APIs could be simple and popular.

The lesson: approximately-right-but-simple almost always beats exactly-right-but-complicated in infrastructure technology.

Part VII — The SOA Hype Era (2003–2008)

ZapThink, Thomas Erl, and the analyst ecology

ZapThink — founded 2000 by Ronald Schmelzer and Jason Bloomberg — became the boutique analyst firm for Web Services and SOA. They ran the “Licensed ZapThink Architect” certification program. Acquired by Dovel Technologies in 2011, a sign the boom was over.

Thomas Erl, based in Vancouver, authored the Prentice Hall Service-Oriented Computing series beginning 2004. His breakthrough book was Service-Oriented Architecture: Concepts, Technology, and Design (August 2005), which became the de facto textbook. Erl laid out the eight SOA design principles: Standardized Service Contract, Service Loose Coupling, Service Abstraction, Service Reusability, Service Autonomy, Service Statelessness, Service Discoverability, Service Composability. In retrospect, these principles describe microservices accurately, and reading his 2005 book in 2020 feels strangely prescient.

The Enterprise Service Bus era

The ESB was the product category that defined the SOA era and also killed it. The term was coined (or popularized) by Gartner analyst Roy Schulte in 2002. The concept: a middleware backbone handling routing, protocol translation, transformation, orchestration, and security for all Web Services in an enterprise.

The ESB vendor landscape:

  • Sonic Software (acquired by Progress 2006) — first commercial ESB (2002)
  • TIBCO BusinessWorks (acquired by Vista Equity 2014 for $4.3B)
  • webMethods (acquired by Software AG 2007 for $546M)
  • IBM WebSphere Message Broker, descended from MQSeries
  • Microsoft BizTalk Server (2000)
  • Oracle Service Bus (AquaLogic Service Bus from BEA)
  • SAP NetWeaver PI
  • MuleSoft (founded 2006 by Ross Mason; IPO’d 2017; acquired by Salesforce 2018 for $6.5 billion — one of the great exits of the SOA era)
  • WSO2 (Sanjiva Weerawarana, 2005)
  • Apache ServiceMix (2005)

ESB projects typically took 18–36 months and cost millions of dollars. “Governance” promised by the vendor usually turned into a political bottleneck where the ESB team became a choke point for every cross-team integration. This was the genesis of what would later become a major cultural force: the rejection of the central integration layer in favor of smart endpoints and dumb pipes.

BPEL and the orchestration dream

WS-BPEL 2.0 was ratified in April 2007. Every ESB vendor built a BPEL engine: IBM WebSphere Process Server, Oracle BPEL Process Manager, Microsoft BizTalk, SAP NetWeaver, Apache ODE, ActiveBPEL. For a moment around 2007–2008, BPEL was the most hyped language in enterprise computing. By 2015, it was effectively dead, replaced by workflow engines (Zeebe, Camunda, Temporal, Airflow, Argo) that used code or YAML rather than XML.

The SOA Manifesto (October 2009)

By 2009, the backlash was in full swing. A group of practitioners convened in Rotterdam, Netherlands, October 2009, to write the SOA Manifesto. Signatories included Thomas Erl, Nicolai Josuttis, Mark Little, Grady Booch, Jim Webber, and about two dozen others:

Business value over technical strategy
Strategic goals over project-specific benefits
Intrinsic interoperability over custom integration
Shared services over specific-purpose implementations
Flexibility over optimization
Evolutionary refinement over pursuit of initial perfection

The manifesto was, in retrospect, a plea: stop buying ESB products and start thinking about the architecture. By the time it was published, Anne Thomas Manes’s “SOA is Dead” post had already been live for nine months.

Part VIII — Why Enterprise SOA Failed (2008–2011)

Anne Thomas Manes: “SOA is Dead; Long Live Services”

On January 5, 2009, Anne Thomas Manes, vice president and research director at the Burton Group, published a blog post titled SOA is Dead; Long Live Services. The post became the most-cited single statement in the history of the SOA movement.

SOA met its demise on January 1, 2009, when it was wiped out by the catastrophic impact of the economic recession. SOA is survived by its offspring: mashups, BPM, SaaS, Cloud Computing, and all other architectural approaches that depend on “services”.

Manes’s argument was cathartic. She did not claim service-oriented architecture as an idea was wrong. What had died was SOA as a buzzword, a marketing category, a thing you bought from vendors. The 2008–2009 recession had forced CIOs to examine budgets, and SOA projects — expensive, long-running, often failing to deliver promised reuse — were early casualties.

The failure modes — what actually went wrong

  1. Complexity overwhelm. The WS-* stack was too large for most teams to fully understand.
  2. Vendor lock-in via the ESB. Once you built on TIBCO/IBM/webMethods, moving off was a multi-year rewrite.
  3. The reuse gap. Most services were used by exactly one other system. Cross-project reuse required service designs that anticipated multiple consumers — which was impossible because the future consumers had not yet been identified.
  4. Interop bugs everywhere. Even WS-I-compliant implementations had cross-vendor interop issues at the WS-Security, WS-Addressing, or WS-ReliableMessaging layer.
  5. Integration projects took years. 18 months planned, 36 months actual. By delivery, requirements had drifted and some systems had been replaced.
  6. The political dimension. SOA governance boards used governance as a power tool, producing a “permission to ship” culture where the central team became a bottleneck.
  7. Tool churn. A project started in 2004 on Systinet + Sonic + ActiveBPEL might by 2008 find all three acquired and deprecated.
  8. Canonical data models nobody agreed on. Every business unit had slightly different semantics for “Customer” or “Order.” The model always became either useless or unusable.

By 2010, “SOA” had become, among developers, almost a slur. The Agile community treated SOA as the “waterfall of architectures.” The backlash was partly unfair — many of the principles were sound, and many failures were implementation failures rather than architectural ones — but culture does not do nuance.

Part IX — The Microservices Turn (2011–Present)

Amazon and the Bezos API Mandate (2002)

Somewhere around 2002, Jeff Bezos sent an internal email to every software engineering team at Amazon. The canonical leaked version comes from Steve Yegge’s Google Platforms Rant, a public Google+ post from October 12, 2011 (accidentally made public before Yegge tried to walk it back — by which point the internet had archived it permanently):

1. All teams will henceforth expose their data and functionality through service interfaces.
2. Teams must communicate with each other through these interfaces.
3. There will be no other form of interprocess communication allowed: no direct linking, no direct reads of another team’s data store, no shared-memory model, no back-doors whatsoever. The only communication allowed is via service interface calls over the network.
4. It doesn’t matter what technology they use. HTTP, Corba, Pubsub, custom protocols — doesn’t matter. Bezos doesn’t care.
5. All service interfaces, without exception, must be designed from the ground up to be externalizable. That is to say, the team must plan and design to be able to expose the interface to developers in the outside world. No exceptions.
6. Anyone who doesn’t do this will be fired.
7. Thank you; have a nice day!

Bezos meant it. Amazon’s internal culture reorganized around this mandate. When AWS launched its first public products in 2006 (S3 in March, EC2 in August), it was the externalization of four years of internal service-oriented practice. AWS was the externalization of the internal Amazon.

What’s worth noting historically: the Bezos memo does not use the phrase “microservices.” It does not even use “service-oriented architecture.” Amazon’s approach was invented in parallel with, not downstream of, the SOA movement. And Amazon’s approach was radically simpler: no ESB, no BPEL, no WS-*, no governance board. Just services, over the network, owned by teams, with no exceptions.

Netflix and the cloud migration (2009–2016)

Netflix’s legendary August 2008 outage — a corrupted database disabled shipping for three days — convinced leadership they could not continue running their own datacenters. Over the next seven years, Netflix moved from a monolithic Oracle-backed Java application to distributed microservices on AWS.

The driving force was Adrian Cockcroft, who joined Netflix in 2007 from eBay. Under his leadership, Netflix built and open-sourced a remarkable suite of tools:

  • Hystrix (2012) — circuit breaker library
  • Eureka (2012) — service discovery registry
  • Zuul (2013) — edge service / API gateway
  • Ribbon (2013) — client-side load balancer
  • Archaius — dynamic configuration
  • Asgard — deployment and cloud management console
  • Chaos Monkey — the chaos engineering touchstone

All released under Netflix OSS, starting around 2012. They influenced Spring Cloud (Pivotal, 2014), which wrapped the Netflix OSS libraries in Spring idioms and became the de facto microservices framework for Java shops. Cockcroft left Netflix in 2014 for Battery Ventures, then AWS (2016–2022). His conference talks during the 2013–2018 period are some of the most-watched architecture talks of the decade.

The Fowler-Lewis Article (March 25, 2014)

On March 25, 2014, Martin Fowler and James Lewis co-published an article titled simply Microservices on martinfowler.com. The term crystallized and entered the mainstream.

In short, the microservice architectural style is an approach to developing a single application as a suite of small services, each running in its own process and communicating with lightweight mechanisms, often an HTTP resource API. These services are built around business capabilities and independently deployable by fully automated deployment machinery. There is a bare minimum of centralized management of these services, which may be written in different programming languages and use different data storage technologies.

The article enumerated nine characteristics:

  1. Componentization via Services
  2. Organized around Business Capabilities
  3. Products not Projects
  4. Smart endpoints and dumb pipes
  5. Decentralized Governance
  6. Decentralized Data Management
  7. Infrastructure Automation
  8. Design for failure
  9. Evolutionary Design

Every characteristic was, to a reader familiar with the SOA era, a pointed response to something that had gone wrong. “Smart endpoints and dumb pipes” was a rejection of the ESB. “Decentralized Governance” rejected SOA governance boards. “Decentralized Data Management” rejected canonical data models. “Design for failure” rejected the WS-ReliableMessaging fantasy.

Containers, Kubernetes, Service Mesh (2013–2018)

Docker 1.0 shipped in 2013, providing the deployment unit microservices needed. Kubernetes was announced in 2014 at Google (based on internal Borg, 2003), open-sourced under Apache 2.0, and reached 1.0 in July 2015. The CNCF (Cloud Native Computing Foundation) was founded that same year at the Linux Foundation.

Linkerd 0.1 (Buoyant / William Morgan, 2016) invented the service mesh category. Envoy (Matt Klein / Lyft, 2016) became the dominant data-plane proxy. Istio 0.1 (Google + IBM + Lyft, 2017) made service mesh a mainstream category. The sidecar pattern — every pod gets a proxy handling traffic — became the canonical deployment model.

The pendulum swings back (2023–2026)

In March 2023, Amazon Prime Video engineering published a now-famous AWS blog post: Scaling up the Prime Video audio/video monitoring service and reducing costs by 90%. The punchline: they had moved back from microservices to a monolith and saved 90% of costs. The post triggered a massive industry conversation about whether the microservices pendulum had swung too far. DHH (Rails) wrote “The Majestic Monolith.” Shopify publicly defended its “modular monolith” approach. Sam Newman admitted some overreach.

By 2026, the consensus is more nuanced: microservices are organizational scaling, not technical scaling. Use them when your team boundaries and deployment velocity demand them. Use a monolith when they don’t.

Epilogue — “We Were Right All Along”

The arc of service-oriented thinking is a hype cycle inside a long upward curve. The idea — decompose systems into independently deployable services communicating through contracts — has been continuously reinvented under different names for four decades. Each generation makes the same core mistakes (underestimating the operational tax, overselling reuse, building vendor lock-in into the substrate) and each generation fixes most of them before moving on.

The people who lived through the enterprise SOA era and then watched the microservices era mostly arrive at the same conclusion: the ideas were always right, but the implementations needed to be simpler. Bezos knew this in 2002. Fielding knew it in 2000. Hohpe & Woolf knew it in 2003. Amazon built AWS on it, Netflix built a business on it, Stripe built an entire company culture on it. The 2020s pendulum swing back toward monoliths is not a rejection of service-oriented principles — it is a rediscovery of the fact that you should pay the service-oriented tax only when you need the service-oriented benefits.

In 2026, every major cloud provider, every fintech, every streaming service, every modern bank, and every AI-era company is service-oriented. They do not call it SOA. They do not call it microservices. They just call it how we build software. The word wars are over. The architecture remains.
