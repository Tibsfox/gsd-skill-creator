Historical Timeline

From Spanish charts to artists' studios — an island that stayed rural while the mainland urbanized.

time imm. Kikialos (Stillaguamish) and other Coast Salish peoples use the island seasonally
1790 Spanish explorer Jacinto Caamaño charts the island; his name attaches
1850s Euro-American settlers arrive; logging and farming operations begin
1909 First bridge connects Camano Island to the mainland via Stanwood
1930s Cama Beach Resort opens, drawing families for fishing vacations
1949 Camano Island State Park established on the southwest shore
2008 Cama Beach Historical State Park opens, preserving the resort cabins
2024 Cama Beach cabins permanently closed due to coastal erosion; day-use continues

Parks & Beaches

Two state parks, preserved shoreline, and community green spaces on 52 miles of coastline.

Camano Island State Park

134 acres6,700 ft shoreline

A 134-acre camping park on the southwest shore with 6,700 feet of rocky shoreline and beach. 87 campsites (including 26 with hookups), hiking trails through old-growth forest, picnic shelters, and an amphitheater. The beach offers tide pool exploration, sunset views across Saratoga Passage toward Whidbey Island, and some of the best eagle-watching on the island.

Access: Lowell Point Rd, southwest Camano

Cama Beach Historical State Park

433 acresbeach

A 433-acre park preserving the legacy of the 1930s Cama Beach fishing resort. Historic waterfront cabins (now closed to overnight use due to seawall and septic issues), the Center for Wooden Boats Camano outpost, and a day-use beach with drift logs and views of Whidbey. The park hosts interpretive programs on fishing heritage and Coast Salish history.

Access: W Camano Dr, west Camano

Iverson Spit Preserve

birdsspit

A natural shoreline preserve on the east side of the island. The spit extends into Port Susan Bay, providing critical habitat for shorebirds, herons, and migrating waterfowl. Walking trails lead through native vegetation to the beach. A key bird-watching site, especially during fall migration.

English Boom Historical Park

logging heritagebeach

Named for a log boom that operated here during the timber era. The park preserves the logging heritage of the island with interpretive signs and a beach with views of the mainland. A quiet spot for beach walks and picnicking on the east shore.

Wildlife

An island where nature still has the upper hand.

Bald Eagles: Camano Island is one of the densest bald eagle habitats in the lower 48 states. Nesting pairs are visible year-round along the shoreline and in the old-growth stands. Great blue herons maintain rookeries on the island. Harbor seals haul out on rocky points. Orcas (Southern Resident and transient pods) pass through Saratoga Passage seasonally, typically in summer and fall, pursuing salmon runs. The island's relatively undeveloped shoreline makes it a critical corridor for marine mammals and shorebirds.

Getting Here from Mukilteo

Camano Island is 35 miles north of Mukilteo. No ferry needed — bridge access via Stanwood.

By Car (Primary Access)

I-5 north from Mukilteo through Everett and Marysville to Stanwood (Exit 212), then west on SR-532 across the bridge to Camano Island. Approximately 40–50 minutes depending on traffic. The bridge crosses Davis Slough and West Pass — no toll, no ferry.

By Transit (Limited)

Snow Goose Transit: A local minibus service connecting Stanwood and Camano Island. From Mukilteo, first reach Stanwood via I-5 north (car or limited bus service through Everett/Marysville). Island transit service is minimal — a car is strongly recommended for exploring Camano.

Weekend and evening service is extremely limited or nonexistent on the island.
