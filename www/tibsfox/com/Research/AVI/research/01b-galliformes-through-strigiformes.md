# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 1B: Galliformes through Strigiformes

> **Module 1B deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document profiles 78 species across 8 taxonomic orders spanning the ground-dwelling landfowl (Galliformes), diving grebes (Podicipediformes), pigeons and doves (Columbiformes), nightjars (Caprimulgiformes), swifts and hummingbirds (Apodiformes), rails and cranes (Gruiformes), herons and pelicans (Pelecaniformes), raptors (Accipitriformes), and owls (Strigiformes). These orders encompass some of the PNW's most iconic conservation stories — the Greater Sage-Grouse and its sagebrush sea, the Bald Eagle's DDT recovery, the Northern Spotted Owl and old-growth logging conflict, and the Rufous Hummingbird's extraordinary migration-to-mass ratio. Together they anchor the terrestrial, aquatic, and aerial food webs that define PNW landscapes from alpine snowfields to coastal estuaries.
>
> **Species count:** ~78 species (12 Galliformes, 6 Podicipediformes, 4 Columbiformes, 3 Caprimulgiformes, 9 Apodiformes, 6 Gruiformes, 10 Pelecaniformes, 14 Accipitriformes, 14 Strigiformes)
>
> **Design principle:** These orders are linked by ecological dependency chains. Grouse and quail are primary prey for goshawks and great horned owls. Herons and egrets track the same wetland health indicators as rails and grebes. Eagles and ospreys sit atop aquatic food webs whose productivity is measured by pelican and grebe abundance. Owls partition the nocturnal niche that hawks occupy by day. No order in this module can be understood in isolation.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Only Level 1 (publicly available) cultural content. All Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations. AOS taxonomic authority for all nomenclature.

## Order Galliformes — Landfowl

### Family Phasianidae — Grouse, Ptarmigan, Quail, and Allies

The Phasianidae in the PNW encompass a radiation of ground-dwelling birds adapted to habitats ranging from alpine snowfields to sagebrush steppe. Native grouse and ptarmigan are indicators of ecosystem integrity — their populations track the health of the specific plant communities they depend upon. The Greater Sage-Grouse and its obligate relationship with big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) represents the most extensive single-species conservation effort in western North America. East-side forest grouse (Dusky, Ruffed, Spruce) partition coniferous and deciduous forest habitats along elevation and canopy-density gradients. Introduced gallinaceous birds (Ring-necked Pheasant, Chukar, Gray Partridge) occupy agricultural and steppe niches largely distinct from native species but create localized competition where ranges overlap [P14, O1].

PNW quail species — California Quail and Mountain Quail — represent the northern extent of a primarily Neotropical radiation. Their covey-based social systems, with communal roosting and sentinel behavior, contrast sharply with the solitary or loosely social grouse. Mountain Quail undertake the only known altitudinal migration on foot among North American birds [O1, O7].

### Greater Sage-Grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Centrocercus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (2015 "not warranted" finding; subject to ongoing litigation and policy revision)
- IUCN: Near Threatened
- State WA: Threatened
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- State ID: Species of Greatest Conservation Need
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (steep decline)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** Males 66-76 cm (26-30 in); females 48-58 cm (19-23 in) — the largest grouse in North America
- **Wingspan:** Males 95-105 cm (37-41 in); females 80-90 cm (31-35 in)
- **Mass:** Males 2.0-3.2 kg (4.4-7.1 lb); females 1.0-1.7 kg (2.2-3.7 lb). Males average nearly twice female mass — among the most extreme sexual size dimorphism in gallinaceous birds.
- **Plumage:** Males: mottled gray-brown above, black belly, white breast with filoplume ruff, yellow-green combs above eyes inflated during display. Long, pointed tail feathers displayed in fan during courtship. Females: cryptic gray-brown overall, well-camouflaged against sagebrush understory. Dark belly patch smaller than male.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Extreme. Males substantially larger with elaborate display structures — inflatable esophageal air sacs (yellow-green skin patches on breast), elongated filoplumes, and specialized tail feathers absent in females.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Sagebrush steppe dominated by big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) with >15% canopy cover. Lek sites (communal display grounds) are typically on open, relatively flat areas surrounded by sagebrush — ridgetops, clearings, dry lakebeds, or slightly elevated benches. Nesting occurs under sagebrush canopy within 6.5 km of lek; hens select nest sites with >25% sagebrush cover and >18 cm residual grass/forb height for concealment [P14, G1].
- **Brood-rearing:** Mesic habitats — wet meadows, irrigated hay fields, riparian edges, and areas with abundant forbs (especially *Taraxacum*, *Astragalus*, and *Phlox* spp.) that support insect prey critical for chick growth during the first 3 weeks post-hatch.
- **Wintering:** Sagebrush stands with >25 cm canopy height visible above snow, often on wind-swept ridges or south-facing slopes where sagebrush is exposed. Winter diet is 100% sagebrush leaves and buds — no other food is consumed from November through March.
- **Elevation range:** 1,200-2,700 m (4,000-9,000 ft) in the PNW; primarily 1,400-2,100 m (4,500-7,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — margins)
- **Microhabitat:** Obligate sagebrush dependent. Cannot persist in any landscape lacking extensive, contiguous sagebrush cover. Lek-site fidelity is extreme — males return to the same lek year after year, and abandonment of a lek typically indicates local population collapse rather than relocation.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Sagebrush leaves and buds comprise >99% of winter diet and 60-80% of year-round diet. Among the most specialized diets of any North American bird. The enlarged, muscular crop and specialized gut flora enable digestion of monoterpenes and sesquiterpene lactones that make sagebrush toxic or unpalatable to most vertebrates.
- **Seasonal variation:** Spring and summer diet diversifies to include forbs (dandelion, clover, hawksbeard) and insects (ants, beetles, grasshoppers). Chick diet is heavily insectivorous during the first 3 weeks — insect availability on brood-rearing habitat is a critical bottleneck for recruitment.
- **Foraging strategy:** Terrestrial gleaning. Picks sagebrush leaves and buds directly from plants while walking through stands. Does not scratch or dig. Chicks forage actively on exposed ground among forbs, catching insects by sight.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Lek-based polygyny — males gather at traditional display grounds (leks) from March through May and perform elaborate strutting displays. Dominant males (typically 2-4 per lek) achieve 80-90% of all matings. Females visit leks, select mates, and depart to nest alone.
- **Lek display:** Males inflate paired esophageal air sacs, producing 2-3 loud, popping sounds audible at 2+ km ("swish-swish-coo-oo-poink"). Display includes fanning the long, pointed tail feathers into a starburst pattern, erecting the white filoplume ruff, and rhythmic strutting. Display bouts last 2-3 seconds, repeated 5-10 times per minute during peak activity (pre-dawn).
- **Nest type:** Ground scraper under sagebrush canopy, lined with dead leaves and grass. Mean distance from lek: 3.2 km (range 0.5-16 km).
- **Clutch size:** 6-9 eggs (mean 7.4)
- **Incubation:** 25-27 days; female only
- **Fledging:** Chicks are precocial, leave nest within 24 hours of hatching. Flight capability at 10-14 days. Brood break-up at 10-12 weeks.
- **Broods per year:** 1 (renesting uncommon — approximately 15-30% of females attempt renesting after nest failure)
- **Breeding season:** Lek display March-May; nesting April-June; brood-rearing June-September

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining. Rangewide population declined ~80% from historical estimates of 1.6 million to current estimates of 200,000-500,000. Washington population critically small: estimated 700-1,100 birds in the Douglas/Grant County core area, down from >5,000 in the 1970s. Oregon populations more stable but fragmented. Idaho holds the largest PNW populations (estimated 20,000-30,000) [P14, G1, G9].
- **Estimate:** Rangewide 200,000-500,000 individuals (USFWS 2022 status review). PNW (WA+OR+ID): approximately 25,000-35,000 [G1, G9, G13].
- **Threats:** Sagebrush habitat loss and fragmentation (agricultural conversion, energy development, urbanization, wildfire), invasive annual grasses (cheatgrass *Bromus tectorum* altering fire regimes from 60-100 year intervals to 5-10 year intervals), West Nile virus (first documented PNW mortality 2006), predation on nests and adults (ravens, coyotes, raptors — exacerbated by habitat fragmentation that increases edge effects), livestock grazing reducing residual grass height at nest sites, powerline and fence collision mortality, conifer encroachment into sagebrush stands [P14, G1].
- **Source:** Schroeder et al. 2004 [P14], USFWS [G1], WDFW [G9], IDFG [G13]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 6: Shrub-Steppe | Year-round | Uncommon to locally common | Sagebrush obligate, lek-breeding indicator |
| Zone 7: Grassland/Ag | Seasonal | Rare | Brood-rearing on mesic margins |

#### Vocalizations

- **Lek display:** Males produce a diagnostic sequence of swishing, gurgling, and popping sounds during strutting display. The "swish-swish-coo-oo-poink" (2-3 pops) is produced by rapid inflation and deflation of esophageal air sacs. Audible at 2+ km in calm conditions. Each male's display is individually recognizable by tempo and pitch.
- **Female calls:** Low, guttural clucking (*cluk-cluk-cluk*) when flushed or alarmed. Brood hen uses soft purring notes to gather chicks.
- **Alarm:** Sharp, nasal *bark* when flushed, followed by rapid wing-flutter takeoff.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Centrocercus* is an ancient, monotypic or ditypic genus (with Gunnison Sage-Grouse, *C. minimus*, described as a separate species in 2000) endemic to the sagebrush biome of western North America. Molecular phylogenetics place *Centrocercus* as sister to the prairie-grouse clade (*Tympanuchus*), with divergence estimated at 5-7 Ma — coinciding with the late Miocene expansion of *Artemisia* steppe across the intermountain West. The genus is thus as old as its habitat. The extreme dietary specialization on sagebrush — including physiological adaptations for detoxifying terpenes that are unique among grouse — indicates a deep coevolutionary history. Loss of the sagebrush biome would mean extinction of a lineage with no ecological substitute [O1, P14].

#### Cultural Significance

The Greater Sage-Grouse lek display has been observed and incorporated into ceremonial practice by Great Basin and Plateau peoples for millennia. The Shoshone-Bannock and Northern Paiute peoples of the Snake River Plain have traditional ecological knowledge of sage-grouse lek locations, seasonal movements, and habitat requirements that predates and in some cases exceeds the resolution of Western scientific surveys. Sage-grouse feathers and dance movements appear in the cultural traditions of multiple Great Basin nations [C2].

### Dusky Grouse (*Dendragapus obscurus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Dendragapus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (game species)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 43-51 cm (17-20 in)
- **Wingspan:** 66-71 cm (26-28 in)
- **Mass:** Males 1.0-1.4 kg (2.2-3.1 lb); females 0.7-1.0 kg (1.5-2.2 lb)
- **Plumage:** Males: dark gray-blue overall with orange-yellow combs above eyes, purplish-red cervical air sacs displayed during courtship. Gray tail with pale terminal band. Females: mottled brown, heavily barred for camouflage. Both sexes feathered to the base of toes.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males larger, darker, with display structures (air sacs, combs) absent in females.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open montane conifer forest, forest edges, and subalpine meadow margins. Prefers Douglas-fir, true fir, and mountain hemlock zones with open understory. Males display from elevated perches (logs, rocks, low branches) in partially open forest.
- **Wintering:** Moves upslope to dense subalpine conifer stands (subalpine fir, Engelmann spruce) — one of the few North American birds that migrates uphill in winter. Winter range is often 300-600 m higher than breeding range. Feeds almost exclusively on conifer needles during winter months.
- **Elevation range:** 1,000-3,000 m (3,300-10,000 ft); breeding concentrated 1,500-2,400 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — winter), Zone 4 (Foothill — lower breeding limit)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Conifer needles (Douglas-fir, true fir, spruce) dominate winter diet (>95%). Summer diet includes berries (huckleberry, serviceberry, snowberry), forbs, seeds, and insects. Chicks heavily insectivorous for first 2-3 weeks.
- **Foraging strategy:** Terrestrial gleaning and arboreal browsing. Feeds on ground in summer; perches in conifer canopy to clip needles in winter.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper under shrub or log, lined with grass and feathers
- **Clutch size:** 5-9 eggs (typically 6-8)
- **Incubation:** 25-28 days; female only
- **Fledging:** Precocial; chicks fly at 7-10 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-July

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS trend -0.2% per year in WA/OR, 2006-2019, not statistically significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** Continental population ~5.8 million (PIF 2020). PNW populations considered secure [O4].
- **Threats:** Loss of subalpine winter habitat to climate change (upslope vegetation shift), forest fragmentation, livestock grazing reducing ground-cover nesting habitat [O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Winter | Fairly common | Conifer-needle browser |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Fairly common | Ground-nesting prey base |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding | Uncommon | Lower elevation limit |

#### Vocalizations

- **Male display:** Series of 5-6 low-frequency hooting notes (*hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo*) produced by inflating cervical air sacs. Extremely low-pitched (80-100 Hz), more felt than heard at close range. Carries poorly through forest — effective range ~200 m.
- **Alarm:** Loud clucking when flushed; explosive wingbeat takeoff.

#### Evolutionary Note

Formerly lumped with Sooty Grouse as "Blue Grouse" (*Dendragapus obscurus* sensu lato). Split by AOS in 2006 based on vocal, plumage, and genetic differences. Dusky Grouse occupies the interior ranges (Cascades east slope, Rocky Mountains), while Sooty Grouse is found on the west slope and coastal mountains. The split reflects isolation during Pleistocene glacial cycles, with secondary contact zones on the Cascade crest where occasional hybridization occurs [O1, O3].

### Sooty Grouse (*Dendragapus fuliginosus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Dendragapus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (game species)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 43-51 cm (17-20 in)
- **Wingspan:** 66-71 cm (26-28 in)
- **Mass:** Males 1.0-1.3 kg (2.2-2.9 lb); females 0.7-0.9 kg (1.5-2.0 lb)
- **Plumage:** Similar to Dusky Grouse but overall darker and sootier gray (hence name). Males have yellow cervical air sacs (versus purplish-red in Dusky). Tail lacks the pale terminal band of Dusky — uniformly dark. Females slightly darker brown than Dusky females.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males larger, darker, with yellow cervical air sacs.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** West-side montane conifer forest — Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Pacific silver fir zones. More closely tied to mesic, coastal-influenced forests than Dusky Grouse. Forest edges, clearcuts with regrowth, and subalpine meadow margins.
- **Wintering:** Upslope migration into dense conifer canopy, as with Dusky Grouse. Winter diet nearly 100% conifer needles (Douglas-fir, western hemlock).
- **Elevation range:** 300-2,500 m (1,000-8,200 ft); breeding concentrated 500-1,800 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest — west slope)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — winter)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Conifer needles in winter (Douglas-fir, hemlock). Summer: berries (salal, huckleberry, Oregon grape), forbs, insects. Chicks insectivorous initially.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper concealed under shrub, log, or low branch
- **Clutch size:** 5-9 eggs
- **Incubation:** 25-28 days; female only
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-July

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS data limited due to detection difficulty) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~3.5 million continental (PIF 2020) [O4]
- **Threats:** Old-growth forest loss, climate-driven changes to subalpine wintering habitat, nest predation by corvids and mesocarnivores [O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Vocalizations

- **Male display:** Deep, resonant hooting (*hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo*), slightly higher-pitched than Dusky Grouse (100-120 Hz). Display often given from elevated perch in dense canopy, making localization extremely difficult. Males may call for hours from a single tree.

#### Evolutionary Note

Sister species to Dusky Grouse; split by AOS in 2006. The yellow (Sooty) versus purplish-red (Dusky) cervical air sac color is the most reliable field distinction in the narrow contact zone along the Cascade crest. Genetic divergence estimated at ~0.5-1.0 Ma [O1, O3].

### Ruffed Grouse (*Bonasa umbellus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Bonasa*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (game species)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 40-50 cm (16-20 in)
- **Wingspan:** 56-64 cm (22-25 in)
- **Mass:** 500-750 g (1.1-1.7 lb); males slightly heavier
- **Plumage:** Two color morphs — gray morph (predominant in interior and higher elevations) and rufous morph (predominant on wet west side). Both morphs have broad black subterminal tail band, erectile neck ruff (black in males, reduced in females), and extensively barred and mottled plumage providing superb camouflage in deciduous understory.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Slight — males have more complete tail band, larger neck ruff, and average slightly heavier. Sexes difficult to distinguish in the field without close inspection.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Early-successional deciduous and mixed forest with dense understory — alder thickets, young aspen stands, brushy forest edges, riparian corridors with willow and cottonwood. Requires a mosaic of age classes: dense cover for nesting, openings for brood-rearing, mature trees with catkins for winter food.
- **Wintering:** Same habitats; may shift to stands with more alder and birch catkins as winter food source.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,800 m (6,000 ft); most common below 1,200 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — lower elevations with deciduous component)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Buds, catkins, and twigs of deciduous trees (alder, birch, aspen, willow) dominate winter diet. Summer: berries (strawberry, huckleberry, rose hip), seeds, leaves, and insects. Chicks primarily insectivorous for first 2-3 weeks.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper at base of tree, stump, or log, concealed in leaf litter
- **Clutch size:** 9-14 eggs (among the largest clutch sizes of PNW upland birds)
- **Incubation:** 23-24 days; female only
- **Fledging:** Precocial; chicks can make short flights at 10-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-June

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable in PNW (BBS trend +0.3% per year, 2006-2019, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~26 million continental (PIF 2020). PNW populations healthy where early-successional habitat persists [O4].
- **Threats:** Fire suppression and forest maturation reducing early-successional habitat, West Nile virus (emerging in eastern WA/ID), forest conversion [O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Vocalizations

- **Drumming:** Males produce a distinctive accelerating series of low-frequency wing-beats (non-vocal) while standing on a drumming log — *thump...thump...thump..thump.thump-thump-brrrrrr*. The deep, resonant sound (40-50 Hz fundamental) carries 400-800 m and is often mistaken for a distant engine or heartbeat. Drumming occurs year-round but peaks March-May. The sound is produced by rapid forward wing strokes that compress air, not by striking the log.
- **Alarm:** Short, sharp *quit-quit* when flushed; explosive wingbeat takeoff.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Bonasa umbellus* is the sole New World member of the genus *Bonasa*, whose only other species is the Hazel Grouse (*B. bonasia*) of Eurasia. The lineage dates to the late Pliocene (~3 Ma). Ruffed Grouse exhibit a classic 8-11 year population cycle across boreal and northern forests, driven by complex predator-prey dynamics and potentially autocorrelated with sunspot cycles, though the mechanistic basis remains debated. PNW populations show dampened or absent cycling compared to boreal populations [O1].

### White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Lagopus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not documented as breeding (occasional reports)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 30-33 cm (12-13 in) — the smallest grouse in North America
- **Wingspan:** 51-56 cm (20-22 in)
- **Mass:** 300-450 g (10.6-15.9 oz); males slightly heavier than females
- **Plumage:** Three seasonal plumages — the most extensive molt cycle of any PNW bird. Winter: entirely white except for black eyes, bill, and toe-claws. Summer: mottled brown, gray, and white above, white below, with brown-barred breast and flanks. Spring/autumn: transitional patchwork of brown and white. White tail retained year-round (diagnostic versus other *Lagopus* species, which have black tail feathers). Feathered toes function as snowshoes and thermal insulation.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Subtle. Males in summer plumage are slightly grayer on breast; females more heavily barred with warm brown tones. Males have small red combs above eyes, visible at close range during breeding.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Alpine tundra above treeline — fell-fields, alpine meadows, talus slopes with scattered cushion plants, and krummholz edges. Selects areas with a mosaic of rocky cover (for escape) and vegetated patches (for foraging). Nests on ground among rocks, typically adjacent to a snowmelt seep or moist alpine meadow with *Salix* and *Dryas* spp.
- **Wintering:** Descends slightly from exposed ridgetops to sheltered alpine basins, krummholz edges, and upper subalpine meadows. Roosts in snow burrows during severe weather, reducing metabolic heat loss by 40-50%. Snow depth and hardness determine winter habitat quality — powder snow must be deep enough for burrowing (>20 cm).
- **Elevation range:** 1,800-3,300 m (6,000-10,800 ft) in the Cascades; lower in the North Cascades (1,500 m) due to lower treeline. Essentially never found below treeline except during exceptional winter weather events.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine) — obligate
- **Ecoregion secondary:** None regularly
- **Microhabitat:** The most elevation-restricted bird species in the PNW. Entire life cycle occurs within the alpine zone. Requires fell-field terrain with a specific ratio of rock cover (escape from raptors) to vegetation patches (foraging). Snowfield edges are critical foraging microhabitats in early summer, where recently exposed ground supports fresh plant growth and concentrated insect emergence.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Alpine plant material year-round — buds, catkins, and leaves of alpine willows (*Salix arctica*, *S. nivalis*), *Dryas* leaves, *Bistorta* shoots, seeds, flowers (especially *Ranunculus*, *Silene*), and mosses. Insects (beetles, flies, caterpillars) supplement the diet in summer and are critical for chick growth.
- **Foraging strategy:** Terrestrial gleaning. Walks slowly through alpine vegetation, picking buds and leaves. Forages at snowfield margins where newly exposed vegetation is most nutritious. Winter foraging concentrated on wind-swept ridges where alpine willow catkins protrude above snow.
- **Metabolic adaptation:** Remarkably low basal metabolic rate for body size, enabling survival on the nutrient-poor alpine diet. Cecal fermentation of plant cellulose is more developed than in any other PNW grouse species.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper among rocks, lined with feathers, lichen, and plant fragments. Minimal nest construction — reliance on crypsis and rocky terrain for predator avoidance.
- **Clutch size:** 4-8 eggs (typically 5-6), creamy buff with fine brown speckling
- **Incubation:** 22-24 days; female only. Male defends territory during early incubation but does not attend nest.
- **Fledging:** Precocial; chicks leave nest within hours of hatching. Flight capability at 7-10 days. Family group remains together until autumn.
- **Broods per year:** 1 (renesting rare due to short alpine growing season)
- **Breeding season:** Late May-August (compressed by alpine snow season; nesting initiation tracks snowmelt date)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Uncertain but potentially declining. No reliable BBS data due to alpine habitat inaccessibility. Point-count surveys in North Cascades suggest stable populations at current treeline but vulnerability to upward treeline shift [G4, G7].
- **Estimate:** ~30,000-50,000 continent-wide (PIF 2020, low confidence). PNW population poorly quantified; estimated hundreds to low thousands in WA Cascades [O4].
- **Threats:** Climate change is the primary long-term threat — alpine zone contraction, upward treeline advance, reduced snowpack duration, and heat stress during summer. A 3°C mean temperature increase would eliminate 40-70% of current alpine habitat in the Washington Cascades. Additional threats include increased predation as generalist predators (Common Raven, Golden Eagle) expand into warming alpine zones, recreational disturbance on alpine trails during nesting season, and disease transmission from introduced grouse populations at lower elevations [G7, O1].
- **Source:** USFS [G7], Partners in Flight [O4], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Year-round | Uncommon | Alpine-obligate indicator species |

#### Vocalizations

- **Male territorial call:** Soft, clucking series (*cluk-cluk-cluk-cluk*) given from rocky prominence. Low amplitude — carries only 100-200 m. Among the quietest territorial advertisements of any grouse.
- **Alarm:** High-pitched, screaming *kree-ee-ee* when flushed at close range. Explosive burst flight, typically dropping downslope and landing behind a rock outcrop within 50-100 m.
- **Brood calls:** Hen uses soft purring and clucking to maintain contact with chicks among boulders.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Lagopus leucura* is the southernmost member of the ptarmigan genus, which includes the circumpolar Willow Ptarmigan (*L. lagopus*) and Rock Ptarmigan (*L. muta*). White-tailed Ptarmigan is the only ptarmigan endemic to North America and the only one native to the contiguous United States. The genus *Lagopus* diverged from other grouse ~7-8 Ma. White-tailed Ptarmigan's three-plumage molt cycle — the most complete seasonal color change of any PNW bird — evolved under intense selection for crypsis in alpine environments with extreme seasonal variation. The retention of white tail feathers year-round (unique in the genus) suggests either relaxed selection on tail coloration (tail is rarely exposed during ground-sitting posture) or a signaling function during courtship displays. PNW populations survived Pleistocene glaciations on ice-free alpine refugia (nunataks) in the North Cascades and likely recolonized from both northern and southern refugia as glaciers retreated [O1, P17].

#### Climate Vulnerability

White-tailed Ptarmigan is considered the most climate-vulnerable bird species in the PNW. Unlike species that can shift range northward, alpine-obligate species face a contracting habitat ceiling — they are already at the tops of mountains with no higher ground available. Phenological mismatches between snowmelt timing and chick hatch dates may reduce survival if insect emergence peaks before chicks are old enough to forage efficiently. Population monitoring programs in the North Cascades and Mt. Rainier National Park represent critical baselines for tracking climate impacts on alpine ecosystems [G7, O1].

### California Quail (*Callipepla californica*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Odontophoridae, Genus *Callipepla*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (game species)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 24-27 cm (9.5-10.6 in)
- **Wingspan:** 33-36 cm (13-14 in)
- **Mass:** 140-230 g (4.9-8.1 oz)
- **Plumage:** Males: blue-gray breast, chestnut flanks with white streaks, scaled belly, forward-drooping black topknot (comma-shaped plume), black throat bordered by white stripes. Females: gray-brown overall, shorter topknot, scaled belly pattern less defined.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Brushy edges, chaparral, oak woodland understory, suburban gardens, agricultural margins with cover. Requires dense low brush for escape cover (<3 m from feeding areas). Most common in low-elevation, west-side habitats south of Puget Sound.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,200 m (4,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — brushy margins), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest edges)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (annual grasses, clover, lupine, acorns), green leaves, berries, insects (especially during breeding season). Forages on ground in coveys of 10-75+ birds, scratching and picking through leaf litter.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper concealed in dense brush
- **Clutch size:** 12-16 eggs (among the largest clutches of PNW birds)
- **Incubation:** 22-23 days; female only (male serves as sentinel)
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (double-brooding documented; male may tend first brood while female incubates second clutch)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining in agricultural areas (BBS trend -0.8% per year in OR, 2006-2019) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~3.8 million continental (PIF 2020). Common in suitable habitat throughout western OR and WA [O4].
- **Threats:** Habitat fragmentation, feral cat predation (major threat in suburban interface), brush removal in agricultural landscapes [O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Vocalizations

- **Assembly call:** Loud, three-note *chi-CA-go* (emphasis on second syllable) — iconic sound of PNW lowland shrublands. Given by separated covey members to reassemble.
- **Alarm:** Sharp *pit-pit-pit* when predator detected.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Callipepla* is a New World quail genus endemic to western North America, with closest relatives in Mexico. California Quail and Gambel's Quail (*C. gambelii*) form a superspecies pair, separated by the arid interior. The forward-drooping topknot plume is a sexually selected ornament unique to *Callipepla* and may function in both mate choice and covey recognition [O1].

### Mountain Quail (*Oreortyx pictus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Odontophoridae, Genus *Oreortyx*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (game species; local declines noted)
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Moderate concern (declining in parts of range)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 26-30 cm (10.2-11.8 in) — the largest New World quail
- **Wingspan:** 35-40 cm (14-16 in)
- **Mass:** 200-290 g (7.1-10.2 oz)
- **Plumage:** Rich blue-gray breast, chestnut throat bordered by white, bold white bars on chestnut flanks, two long, straight head plumes projecting vertically (not recurved like California Quail). Sexes similar, though females have slightly shorter plumes and duller throat.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense montane shrubland — manzanita, ceanothus, and mixed-conifer forest understory with dense brush component. Higher elevation than California Quail, typically 600-2,400 m. Prefers steep, brushy slopes with rock outcrops.
- **Wintering:** Descends to lower elevations on foot — the only North American quail known to undertake altitudinal migration, and one of the few birds that migrates primarily by walking. Coveys walk downslope 500-1,500 m elevation in autumn, returning upslope in spring.
- **Elevation range:** 300-2,400 m (1,000-8,000 ft); breeding concentrated 600-1,800 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — brushy understory)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds, bulbs (especially *Triteleia*, *Brodiaea*), acorns, berries, green leaves, fungi, and insects. Digs for bulbs with strong feet — the most fossorial foraging behavior of any PNW quail.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 9-15 eggs
- **Incubation:** 24-25 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (double brooding documented with role reversal — male incubates second clutch)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining in northern portions of range (WA), stable in OR core range [G4, G9]
- **Estimate:** ~1.6 million continental (PIF 2020) [O4]
- **Threats:** Fire suppression reducing brushy early-successional habitat, conifer encroachment, nest predation, habitat fragmentation [O1, G9].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9]

#### Vocalizations

- **Male territorial call:** Loud, clear, single descending whistle: *QUEEE-ark* — carries 0.5-1 km. One of the most distinctive sounds in PNW montane shrublands.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Oreortyx* is monotypic — Mountain Quail is the sole species in its genus, with no close relatives. Its phylogenetic isolation, altitudinal foot-migration, and fossorial foraging set it apart from all other New World quail. The vertical head plumes evolved independently from the recurved topknot of *Callipepla* [O1, O3].

## Order Podicipediformes — Grebes

### Family Podicipedidae — Grebes

Grebes are diving waterbirds convergent with loons in body plan but phylogenetically distant — molecular evidence places grebes closest to flamingos (order Phoenicopteriformes), not loons. PNW grebes occupy freshwater lakes and marshes for breeding, with several species moving to marine waters in winter. Their lobed toes (not webbed feet), rear-set legs, and dense waterproof plumage represent independent evolution of the diving lifestyle. Grebes are unique among birds in regularly ingesting their own feathers, which form a felt-like plug in the pylorus (gizzard outlet), thought to protect the intestines from sharp fish bones. All PNW grebes carry young on their backs — a behavior shared with no other bird family in the region [O1].

### Western Grebe (*Aechmophorus occidentalis*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Podicipediformes, Family Podicipedidae, Genus *Aechmophorus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor and year-round resident (local)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (declining)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 56-66 cm (22-26 in) — the largest grebe in North America
- **Wingspan:** 79-91 cm (31-36 in)
- **Mass:** 800-1,800 g (1.8-4.0 lb); averaging ~1,100 g. Males 10-15% larger than females.
- **Plumage:** Striking black-and-white pattern — black crown, nape, and upperparts; white throat, foreneck, breast, and underparts. Long, slender, yellow-green bill. Red eyes. Breeding adults have a narrow black cap extending below eye level (key distinction from Clark's Grebe, where cap sits above the eye). Non-breeding plumage similar but slightly duller.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males average larger with slightly longer bills. Plumage identical between sexes.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Large, open freshwater lakes and reservoirs with emergent vegetation (tule, bulrush, cattail) for nesting along margins. Colonial nester — requires large water bodies supporting colony sizes of 20-500+ pairs. Major PNW breeding colonies on Upper Klamath Lake, Lake Abert, Malheur NWR, Banks Lake, and Columbia NWR.
- **Wintering:** Coastal marine waters — protected bays, estuaries, and nearshore ocean. Major wintering concentrations in Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and along OR/WA outer coast. Also on large inland reservoirs that remain ice-free.
- **Elevation range:** Breeding typically 600-1,400 m (2,000-4,500 ft) on interior lakes; wintering at sea level
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland — breeding), Zone 7 (Coastal waters — winter)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe — large lakes within steppe landscape)
- **Microhabitat:** Requires open water >1 m deep adjacent to emergent vegetation. Extremely sensitive to water-level fluctuations — drought or water diversion that exposes nesting substrate to terrestrial predators causes colony abandonment.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small fish (3-15 cm) — primarily species of low commercial value: carp, shiners, sculpin, silverside, stickleback. Also crayfish, aquatic insects, and salamander larvae.
- **Foraging strategy:** Pursuit diver — propels underwater with lobed feet, using laterally compressed body to maneuver. Dives typically 3-10 m depth, lasting 20-40 seconds. Often forages in coordinated groups, driving fish schools toward shallows. This cooperative foraging behavior is rare among waterbirds and may function similarly to cormorant group-fishing.
- **Feather ingestion:** Regularly ingests own breast feathers, which accumulate in the stomach as a felt-like plug. Function debated but most likely protects intestinal lining from fish-bone puncture.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous with spectacular courtship display
- **Courtship display:** The "rushing ceremony" — paired birds rise from the water in synchrony and sprint across the surface side by side, bodies fully upright, necks arched, for 10-20 meters before diving simultaneously. This is one of the most elaborate courtship displays of any North American bird, requiring extraordinary coordination between partners. Pairs also perform "weed ceremony" — diving and surfacing with aquatic vegetation held in bills, presenting weeds to each other in mirrored posture.
- **Nest type:** Floating platform of aquatic vegetation anchored to emergent stems, in colonies of 20 to >1,000 nests
- **Clutch size:** 2-4 eggs (typically 3), pale blue becoming nest-stained
- **Incubation:** 23-24 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** Chicks ride on parents' backs for first 2-4 weeks. Full independence at 8-10 weeks. Flight capability at ~10 weeks.
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-August

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — significant colony abandonment across interior West since 1990s. BBS trend -3.5% per year in WA/OR (2006-2019) — one of the steepest declines of any PNW waterbird [G4].
- **Estimate:** ~110,000 continent-wide (Waterbird Conservation Council). PNW breeding populations have declined 50-70% from 1980s baselines at several key colonies (Upper Klamath Lake, Lake Abert) [G1, G9].
- **Threats:** Water-level management on breeding lakes (irrigation diversion, drought), gill-net entanglement on marine wintering grounds, oil spills, organochlorine contamination (eggshell thinning documented in 1970s, partially recovered), disturbance at colonial nesting sites by boaters and personal watercraft, botulism outbreaks on warm-water breeding lakes. Lake Abert (OR) colony collapsed from ~10,000 pairs to near-zero following water diversions that raised salinity beyond the threshold supporting brine shrimp and fish prey [G9, G12, O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Breeding | Locally common | Colonial fish predator |
| Zone 6: Shrub-Steppe | Breeding | Locally common | Large-lake breeding colonies |
| Zone 7: Coastal/Marine | Winter | Common | Nearshore fish predator |

#### Vocalizations

- **Advertising call:** Loud, two-note *cree-creet* — high-pitched, carrying across open water. Given by both sexes, especially during pair formation and colony establishment.
- **Contact call:** Softer *crip* between pair members.
- **Alarm:** Sharp, nasal *krek* when disturbed at colony.

#### Evolutionary Note

Western Grebe and Clark's Grebe (*A. clarkii*) were split as separate species by the AOS in 1985, having previously been considered color morphs. The two species breed sympatrically at many PNW colonies, with assortative mating based on facial pattern (black cap extending below vs. above the eye) and bill color (greenish-yellow vs. bright orange). Hybridization rates are 1-3%, confirming reproductive isolation. The genus *Aechmophorus* is endemic to western North America and has no close Old World relatives. The elaborate rushing display may function as a species-recognition mechanism maintaining reproductive isolation where the two species co-occur [O1, O3].

### Eared Grebe (*Podiceps nigricollis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Podicipediformes, Family Podicipedidae, Genus *Podiceps*
**Status:** Breeding visitor, winter resident (local)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 30-35 cm (12-14 in)
- **Wingspan:** 41-44 cm (16-17 in)
- **Mass:** 200-450 g (7.1-15.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Breeding: black head and upperparts, golden ear tufts fanning behind red eyes, dark rufous flanks. Non-breeding: gray-brown above, whitish below, much less distinctive. Bill slightly upturned.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Shallow, productive freshwater and alkaline lakes with abundant invertebrate prey. Colonial — often nests in dense aggregations on marshy lake margins. In PNW, breeds primarily on interior basin lakes in eastern OR and WA.
- **Wintering:** Alkaline lakes (Mono Lake, Great Salt Lake) and coastal marine waters. Major staging concentrations on hypersaline lakes where brine shrimp (*Artemia*) provide fuel for pre-migratory fattening.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe lakes)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Aquatic invertebrates — brine shrimp, aquatic insects, small crustaceans. Fish comprise a smaller dietary fraction than in larger grebes.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs on floating nest
- **Incubation:** 21-22 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining in PNW breeding range [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~3.6 million continent-wide — the most abundant grebe in North America [O4]
- **Threats:** Water diversion reducing lake levels at breeding and staging sites; botulism on warm, shallow lakes [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Eared Grebe undergoes the most extreme seasonal organ hypertrophy of any bird — pectoral flight muscles atrophy by 50% during flightless staging periods on alkaline lakes, while digestive organs enlarge to process enormous quantities of brine shrimp. Birds become temporarily flightless for 2-3 months during moult and staging [O1].

Tolerates small, shallow water bodies unsuitable for other grebes. The most widely distributed grebe in the PNW, found from farm ponds to urban park lakes.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — farm ponds)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Crayfish, aquatic insects, small fish, amphibians. More generalized diet than fish-specialist grebes.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 5-7 eggs on floating nest anchored to emergent vegetation
- **Incubation:** 23-27 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS trend -0.3% per year, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~2 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Wetland drainage, water-level fluctuation [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** Extraordinary loud, laughing series — *cow-cow-cow-cow-COW-COW-cow-cow-cow-cowp* — accelerating then decelerating. One of the most recognizable marsh bird sounds in the PNW. Carries 0.5-1 km across open water.

### Red-necked Grebe (*Podiceps grisegena*)

**Status:** Winter visitor, rare breeder
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

#### Habitat

## Order Columbiformes — Pigeons and Doves

### Family Columbidae — Pigeons and Doves

PNW Columbidae include one native forest species of conservation interest (Band-tailed Pigeon), one recently arrived invasive (Eurasian Collared-Dove), one widespread native (Mourning Dove), and the ubiquitous feral Rock Pigeon. Pigeons and doves are unique among birds in producing "crop milk" — a protein-rich secretion of the crop lining (controlled by prolactin) fed to nestlings during the first week of life, functionally analogous to mammalian lactation [O1].

---

### Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Columbiformes, Family Columbidae, Genus *Patagioenas*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (mostly), some year-round residents
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Game species (managed with restricted harvest)
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (declining)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 33-40 cm (13-15.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 66 cm (26 in)
- **Mass:** 300-450 g (10.6-15.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Blue-gray overall with purplish-pink head and breast, narrow white crescent on nape bordered by iridescent bronze-green patch, yellow bill with black tip, yellow legs and feet. Tail broadly banded with pale gray terminal band (diagnostic in flight).

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Coniferous and mixed forest — Douglas-fir, western red cedar, Pacific silver fir. Often nests near mineral springs and river gravel bars (mineral supplementation sites critical for breeding). Also uses oak woodland and suburban areas with large conifers.
- **Wintering:** Partially migratory — northern populations move south to SW OR and NW CA. Southern PNW populations largely resident. Concentrated at food sources (acorn crops, berry patches, bird feeders in suburban areas).
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,000 m (6,500 ft); breeding concentrated below 1,200 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Acorns (especially Oregon white oak and tanoak), berries (elderberry, cascara, madrone), conifer seeds, grain, and garden fruits. Requires mineral supplementation — visits mineral springs, river gravel bars, and salt licks, especially during breeding season when calcium demand for egg production is high.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Flimsy platform of twigs in conifer or deciduous tree, 3-12 m above ground
- **Clutch size:** 1 egg (occasionally 2) — smallest clutch of any PNW pigeon/dove
- **Incubation:** 18-20 days; both sexes
- **Broods per year:** 1-2

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -1.8% per year in PNW (2006-2019). Population declined an estimated 50% from 1960s baselines [G4, O4].
- **Estimate:** ~2.4 million continent-wide (PIF 2020) [O4]
- **Threats:** Overharvest (historically), habitat loss (old-growth forest, oak woodland), lead poisoning from ingested shot at mineral spring sites, West Nile virus [G9, O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Vocalizations

- **Advertising call:** Low, owl-like hooting — *whoo-whoo* or *who-WHO-who* — rhythmic and slow. Often confused with owl calls by casual listeners.

#### Evolutionary Note

Band-tailed Pigeon is the closest living relative of the extinct Passenger Pigeon (*Ectopistes migratorius*), sharing the genus *Patagioenas* in some older taxonomic treatments but now placed in separate genera. Molecular phylogenetics confirm a relatively recent common ancestor (~5 Ma). The species fills a similar ecological niche — mast-dependent, partially nomadic, and forming large flocks — but never achieved the astronomical abundance of the Passenger Pigeon [O1, O3].

### Eurasian Collared-Dove (*Streptopelia decaocto*) — Brief

**Taxonomic position:** Order Columbiformes, Family Columbidae, Genus *Streptopelia*
**Status:** Year-round resident (invasive, self-introduced)
**Conservation status:** Not listed. Non-native invasive.

- **Origin:** Native to South Asia. Colonized Europe in the 20th century, then arrived in the Americas via the Bahamas in the 1980s. Reached PNW by early 2000s; now established throughout agricultural lowlands of eastern and western WA and OR.
- **Length:** 31-34 cm (12.2-13.4 in)
- **Mass:** 150-240 g (5.3-8.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Pale buffy-gray overall with distinctive black half-collar on nape. Squared tail with white terminal corners. Larger and paler than Mourning Dove with different tail shape and call.
- **Habitat:** Urban and agricultural areas — grain elevators, feedlots, suburban yards, agricultural towns. Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture) and urban areas.
- **Population:** Rapidly expanding — doubling time estimated at 5-7 years in PNW. Potential competitive effects on Mourning Dove debated but not yet documented [O1, O2].

### Mourning Dove (*Zenaida macroura*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Columbiformes, Family Columbidae, Genus *Zenaida*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Game species

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 23-34 cm (9-13.4 in)
- **Wingspan:** 45 cm (17.7 in)
- **Mass:** 96-170 g (3.4-6.0 oz)
- **Plumage:** Slender, long-tailed dove with buff-brown plumage, black spots on wings, iridescent pink-green patch on neck sides, blue-gray orbital skin. Long, pointed, white-edged tail distinctive in flight.

#### Habitat

- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe edges)

#### Vocalizations

### Rock Pigeon (*Columba livia*) — Brief

**Taxonomic position:** Order Columbiformes, Family Columbidae, Genus *Columba*
**Status:** Year-round resident (feral, introduced)
**Conservation status:** Not listed. Non-native.

- **Origin:** Native to Europe, North Africa, and western Asia. Domesticated ~5,000 years ago. Feral populations established worldwide. In PNW, ubiquitous in urban and agricultural settings.
- **Length:** 29-37 cm (11.4-14.6 in)
- **Mass:** 238-380 g (8.4-13.4 oz)
- **Habitat:** Urban areas, bridges, agricultural buildings, grain elevators, cliff faces (substitute for native cliff-nesting habitat). Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture) and urban.

## Order Caprimulgiformes — Nightjars

### Family Caprimulgidae — Nightjars and Nighthawks

Nightjars are crepuscular and nocturnal insectivores with enormous gapes surrounded by rictal bristles, cryptic plumage, and nearly silent flight. Their flattened heads, tiny bills, and massive mouths represent extreme adaptation for aerial insect capture in low-light conditions. PNW species occupy open habitats — grassland, steppe, and forest clearings — where they hawk insects at dusk and dawn. All PNW Caprimulgidae are long-distance migrants, wintering in Central and South America [O1].

---

### Common Nighthawk (*Chordeiles minor*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Caprimulgiformes, Family Caprimulgidae, Genus *Chordeiles*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Moderate concern (declining)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 22-25 cm (8.7-9.8 in)
- **Wingspan:** 51-61 cm (20-24 in)
- **Mass:** 55-98 g (1.9-3.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Mottled gray-brown above and below with bold white wing patches (visible in flight) crossing primaries. White throat band (male) or buffy (female). Long, pointed, slightly notched wings. Males have white tail band near tip, absent in females.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open habitats — gravelly areas, logged clearings, burned forest, grassland, flat gravel rooftops in urban areas. Nests on bare ground (no nest construction). One of the few PNW species to have expanded into urban environments by adopting flat gravel rooftops as nesting substrate.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,500 m (8,200 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill — open areas), Zone 2 (Montane — post-fire clearings)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Flying insects — beetles, moths, mayflies, caddisflies, mosquitoes, ants. Forages at dusk and dawn (crepuscular), with bouts continuing into darkness. Captures insects in flight with enormous gape (30+ mm wide). A single bird may consume 500+ insects per foraging bout.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** None — eggs laid directly on bare ground, gravel, or flat rooftop
- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs, heavily camouflaged
- **Incubation:** 18-20 days; primarily female
- **Broods per year:** 1

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -2.0% per year in PNW (2006-2019). Steep declines documented across North America [G4, O4].
- **Estimate:** ~16 million continent-wide (PIF 2020), but declining rapidly [O4]
- **Threats:** Insect population declines (neonicotinoid pesticides, habitat loss), replacement of flat gravel rooftops with smooth membrane roofing in urban areas, vehicle collision on road-resting birds [O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Vocalizations

- **Flight call:** Nasal, buzzy *peent* repeated regularly in flight — the diagnostic nighthawk sound of summer evenings in the PNW.
- **Boom display:** Male performs steep power-dive, pulling up sharply at bottom; the rush of air through wing feathers produces a deep, resonant *BOOM* (non-vocal). Display given at dusk during breeding season.

### Common Poorwill (*Phalaenoptilus nuttallii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Caprimulgiformes, Family Caprimulgidae, Genus *Phalaenoptilus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 18-21 cm (7.1-8.3 in)
- **Wingspan:** 30-36 cm (12-14 in)
- **Mass:** 36-58 g (1.3-2.0 oz) — the smallest nightjar in North America

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Arid, rocky terrain — sagebrush steppe, rocky slopes, canyon rims, open ponderosa pine. More arid-adapted than Common Nighthawk.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill — dry east side)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Moths, beetles, and other night-flying insects captured in low, sallying flights from ground or rock perch. Sits on ground or road and flies up to catch passing insects — less aerial than nighthawk.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs on bare ground
- **Incubation:** 20-21 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable but poorly monitored (nocturnal, low detectability) [G4]
- **Threats:** Road mortality (sits on warm pavement at night), insect declines [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Clear, whistled *poor-will* (two syllables, rising on second) — the source of its common name. Repeated monotonously from dusk through night during breeding season.

#### Evolutionary Note

Common Poorwill is the only bird known to enter true hibernation (torpor lasting weeks to months). Winter torpor was first scientifically documented in 1946 in a desert canyon, with body temperature dropping to ~5°C. The Hopi people knew of this behavior long before Western science, calling the poorwill *holchko* ("the sleeping one"). PNW populations likely migrate rather than hibernate, but torpor episodes of days to weeks are used during cold snaps in spring and autumn [O1, C2].

### Lesser Nighthawk (*Chordeiles acutipennis*) — Brief

**Taxonomic position:** Order Caprimulgiformes, Family Caprimulgidae, Genus *Chordeiles*
**Status:** Rare visitor (at northern range limit in PNW)
**Conservation status:** Not listed at any level in PNW.

- **Length:** 20-23 cm (7.9-9.1 in)
- **Mass:** 40-65 g (1.4-2.3 oz)
- **Habitat:** Arid, open terrain — desert scrub, agricultural fields. In PNW, rare summer visitor to southernmost OR (Klamath Basin, Harney County).
- **Population:** Peripheral in PNW — main range is southwestern US and Mexico. Occasional breeding suspected but not confirmed in southern OR [O2].
- **Distinction from Common Nighthawk:** Smaller, more buffy, white wing patch positioned closer to wing tip (not mid-wing), and a more buoyant, moth-like flight pattern. Call is a rapid trill rather than the single *peent* [O1].

## Order Apodiformes — Swifts and Hummingbirds

### Family Apodidae — Swifts

Swifts are the most aerial of all PNW birds — they feed, drink, and even sleep on the wing. Their scythe-shaped wings, tiny feet (the name *Apodidae* means "footless"), and wide gapes are extreme adaptations for continuous flight. PNW swifts nest in dark cavities — hollow trees, chimneys, rock crevices, and caves — and their populations are tightly linked to the availability of old-growth snags with internal cavities large enough for colonial roosting [O1, P18].

---

### Vaux's Swift (*Chaetura vauxi*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Apodiformes, Family Apodidae, Genus *Chaetura*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (declining)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-12 cm (4.3-4.7 in) — one of the smallest swifts in the world
- **Wingspan:** 27-30 cm (10.6-11.8 in)
- **Mass:** 15-21 g (0.5-0.7 oz) — smaller than most PNW hummingbirds by mass
- **Plumage:** Dark sooty brown overall, slightly paler on throat and upper breast. Very short, squared tail with stiffened shaft tips (used as brace against vertical surfaces). Cigar-shaped body with long, narrow, scythe-shaped wings. No sexual dimorphism in plumage or size.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Old-growth coniferous forest with large-diameter hollow snags — the primary natural nesting and roosting substrate. Requires trees >75 cm DBH with internal cavities created by heartwood decay (typically Douglas-fir, western red cedar, or Sitka spruce). Forages over forest canopy, lakes, rivers, and clearings within flight range of nest snag. Increasingly uses brick chimneys and concrete shafts as surrogate nesting and roosting sites as old-growth snags decline.
- **Migration roosting:** During spring and fall migration, enormous aggregations (hundreds to thousands) spiral into communal roost sites — historically hollow old-growth snags, now predominantly large brick chimneys (e.g., Chapman Elementary School chimney in Portland, OR, where 10,000-30,000+ swifts roost during September migration). These communal roost events are among the most spectacular bird phenomena in the PNW.
- **Wintering:** Central America and northern South America (Mexico to Venezuela). Entirely absent from PNW November-March.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,800 m (6,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — old-growth snags), Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian — foraging over rivers)
- **Microhabitat:** The most old-growth-dependent swift in North America. Each breeding pair requires a single hollow snag with a vertical interior chimney ≥2 m tall and ≥20 cm diameter. The swift's tiny feet and stiffened tail spines are exclusively adapted for clinging to vertical interior walls of hollow trees or masonry — it cannot perch on branches, wires, or flat surfaces.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small flying insects captured in sustained aerial flight — true bugs, flies, winged ants, beetles, caddisflies, mayflies, spiders (ballooning on silk). All food captured on the wing; never forages from a perch or surface.
- **Foraging strategy:** Continuous flight with rapid, flickering wingbeats interspersed with short glides. Forages at all altitudes from treetop level to >300 m above ground. Mouth opens to ~15 mm gape width — smaller than nighthawks but sufficient for the small insect prey targeted. Flight speed 30-50 km/h with maneuverability exceeding all PNW birds except hummingbirds.
- **Water intake:** Drinks by skimming water surface in low flight — dipping lower mandible while maintaining full flight speed.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Small half-cup of conifer needles and twigs cemented with saliva to vertical interior wall of hollow snag or chimney, 1-5 m below the rim opening. Saliva is the primary structural adhesive — a trait shared with all swiftlets (Apodidae) and exploited commercially in Asian "bird's nest" harvesting of the related Edible-nest Swiftlet.
- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs (typically 5-6), pure white
- **Incubation:** 18-19 days; both sexes, with overlap periods during changeover
- **Fledging:** 28-32 days — unusually long for body size, reflecting the complexity of aerial life skills that must be mastered before independence
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-August (arrives PNW mid-April to early May; departs September-October)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -2.5% per year in WA/OR (2006-2019). Steep declines correlate with loss of old-growth nesting snags and demolition or capping of large brick chimneys used as surrogate roost sites [G4, G9].
- **Estimate:** ~560,000 continent-wide (PIF 2020). PNW breeding population poorly quantified but estimated at 50,000-100,000 individuals based on roost-count extrapolation [O4].
- **Threats:** Old-growth forest loss (primary historical driver), demolition or capping of large brick chimneys (removing surrogate habitat faster than old-growth snags can regrow), aerial insect declines (neonicotinoids, habitat loss), climate-driven phenological mismatch between migration timing and insect emergence peaks. Community conservation efforts to protect chimney roost sites (e.g., Portland's Chapman School chimney, Seattle's Wagner Performing Arts Center) have become important local conservation actions [G6, G9, O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Breeding | Uncommon | Old-growth cavity nester |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Breeding/Migration | Fairly common | Aerial insectivore, chimney rooster |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Uncommon | Foraging over river corridors |

#### Vocalizations

- **Flight call:** Rapid, high-pitched chattering *chi-chi-chi-chi-chi* — a continuous, insect-like buzz given in flight. Flocks produce a collective "rushing" sound at communal roost sites as hundreds of birds spiral into a chimney at dusk.
- **Nest call:** Softer twittering between pair members at nest site.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Chaetura* swifts diverged from Old World swifts approximately 20-25 Ma. Vaux's Swift is the western counterpart of the Chimney Swift (*C. pelagica*) of eastern North America — the two species are parapatric, with a narrow range gap across the Great Plains. Both species share the salivary nest-cementation and hollow-tree nesting strategy. The shift from old-growth snags to masonry chimneys represents one of the most successful anthropogenic habitat substitutions in PNW ornithology, but depends on continued availability of large, open-topped brick chimneys — an architectural style now rapidly disappearing from PNW cities [O1, P18].

### Black Swift (*Cypseloides niger*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Apodiformes, Family Apodidae, Genus *Cypseloides*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (uncommon)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing 2010; review pending)
- IUCN: Least Concern (but poorly surveyed)
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Yellow Watch List

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-18 cm (5.9-7.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 38-45 cm (15-17.7 in)
- **Mass:** 35-55 g (1.2-1.9 oz) — the largest swift in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Entirely blackish-brown with slightly forked tail. Slightly paler on forehead. Broader-winged and slower-flying than Vaux's Swift.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Nests behind or adjacent to waterfalls, on wet cliff faces with permanent water seepage, and occasionally in sea caves. One of the most specialized nest-site requirements of any PNW bird. Forages over mountain forests, alpine meadows, and lowlands far from nest sites.
- **Elevation range:** Nest sites typically 300-2,000 m; foraging range extends to >3,000 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — nest cliffs), Zone 2 (Montane — foraging)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Flying insects captured at extreme altitudes — may forage >3,000 m above sea level, higher than any other PNW bird except raptors on migration. Capable of foraging 100+ km from nest site in a single bout.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 1 egg — the smallest clutch of any PNW swift
- **Incubation:** 24-27 days
- **Fledging:** 45-49 days — extraordinarily long, reflecting the single-egg investment strategy

#### Population

- **Trend:** Poorly known. Declining suspected based on limited survey data [G9, O1].
- **Threats:** Climate-driven changes to waterfall flow regimes (glacier recession reducing summer flows), human disturbance at nest sites (rock climbing, waterfall tourism), aerial insect declines [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

*Cypseloides* is an ancient swift lineage representing the basal (oldest-diverging) branch of New World swifts. The waterfall-nesting habit is shared with tropical *Cypseloides* species and likely represents the ancestral condition for the family — before the derived cavity-nesting strategy of *Chaetura* evolved. Black Swift's breeding biology remained almost completely unknown until the 1940s, making it one of the last North American breeding birds to have its nest described [O1].

### Family Trochilidae — Hummingbirds

PNW hummingbirds represent the northern frontier of the world's most speciose bird family (~360 species, predominantly Neotropical). Only four species breed regularly in the PNW, with another four occurring as rare visitors or at the extreme northern edge of their range. Hummingbirds are the only birds capable of sustained hovering flight, powered by a unique shoulder joint that allows the wing to rotate 180° and generate lift on both the forward and backward stroke. Their iridescent plumage is structural — produced by multilayer thin-film interference in melanosomes, not by pigment [O1].

### Anna's Hummingbird (*Calypte anna*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Apodiformes, Family Trochilidae, Genus *Calypte*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 9.9-10.9 cm (3.9-4.3 in)
- **Wingspan:** 12 cm (4.7 in)
- **Mass:** 3.0-5.7 g (0.11-0.20 oz) — one of the smallest warm-blooded vertebrates in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Males: brilliant iridescent rose-pink gorget (throat) and crown — the only North American hummingbird with a fully iridescent crown. Green back, grayish-white underparts with green flanking. Females: green above, gray below with small central cluster of rose-pink gorget feathers (variable). Both sexes have relatively short, straight bills.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males have full rose-pink gorget and crown; females have reduced gorget spotting and green crown.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Urban gardens, parks, suburban landscapes with flowering plants and feeders. Native habitats include coastal scrub, oak woodland, riparian margins, and open forest edges. Anna's Hummingbird's range has expanded dramatically northward since the 1960s, driven by the proliferation of backyard nectar feeders and non-native winter-flowering plants (especially *Mahonia* spp., *Camellia*, *Rosmarinus*). Prior to 1950, the species was essentially absent from the PNW — its historical range was coastal California and Baja California.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,500 m (5,000 ft); concentrated below 600 m in PNW
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — urban/suburban interface)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Microhabitat:** The only hummingbird in the PNW that regularly overwinters. Survives winter cold by entering nightly torpor — body temperature drops from 40°C to as low as 9°C, reducing metabolic rate by ~90%. Warms up over 20-30 minutes at dawn using rapid shivering thermogenesis. Requires reliable winter nectar sources (feeders or winter-blooming plants) within commuting distance of sheltered roost sites.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Nectar from flowering plants (primary energy source) supplemented by small insects and spiders (primary protein source). In PNW, uses native red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*), salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*), and dozens of cultivated garden flowers. Winter diet heavily reliant on artificial feeders and non-native winter-blooming plants.
- **Foraging strategy:** Hovers at flowers, inserting bill and extending bifurcated tongue (forked, with fringed tips that wick nectar by capillary action) up to 13 times per second. Also hawks small insects in flight ("hawking") and gleans spiders from bark and leaf surfaces ("gleaning"). Defends territory aggressively — a single male may defend a patch of 200+ flowers against all competitors.
- **Energy budget:** Consumes approximately 1.5-3x body mass in nectar daily. Heart rate during hovering: 1,200+ beats per minute. Metabolic rate during hovering is the highest mass-specific metabolic rate of any vertebrate.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Tiny cup (2.5-3 cm diameter) of plant down, lichen, and spider silk, bound with spider silk to branch, wire, or other support. Often decorated with lichen flakes for camouflage. Nest is elastic — expands as chicks grow, held together by spider silk's extensibility.
- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs, white, each ~12 mm long and weighing ~0.5 g
- **Incubation:** 14-19 days; female only. Male provides no parental care.
- **Fledging:** 18-23 days; fledglings continue to be fed by female for 1-2 weeks post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 2-3 (can begin nesting as early as December in PNW — the earliest-nesting bird in the region)
- **Breeding season:** December-August (PNW); peak February-May. Nesting in December-January, when nighttime temperatures may drop below freezing, is enabled by torpor and dependence on artificial feeders.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Strongly increasing — BBS trend +2.4% per year in WA/OR (2006-2019). One of the fastest-expanding bird species in the PNW. Range expansion northward at ~10-15 km per year since the 1970s, now breeding as far north as southern BC [G4, G14, O2].
- **Estimate:** ~8.3 million continent-wide (PIF 2020). PNW population likely 200,000-500,000 and growing [O4].
- **Threats:** Severe cold snaps (multi-day freezes exceeding torpor survival capacity), feral cat predation, window strikes (significant mortality source), dependence on anthropogenic nectar sources creating vulnerability to feeder removal. Despite population increase, the species' reliance on human-provided resources in PNW is a novel ecological dependency with uncertain long-term stability [O1, O2].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], eBird [O2], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Common | Pollinator, territorial nectar defender |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common | Pollinator in woodland edges |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Uncommon | Pollinator along riparian corridors |

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Complex, scratchy, buzzy warble lasting 5-10 seconds — surprisingly loud for body size. Delivered from exposed perch. The most complex song of any PNW hummingbird.
- **Dive display:** Male performs J-shaped power dive from 30-40 m height, producing a loud, explosive *chirp* (non-vocal) at the bottom of the dive arc. The sound is generated by air rushing through modified outer tail feathers (R5) at ~385 Hz. The dive display is directed at females during courtship and at intruders (including humans, cats, and hawks) year-round.
- **Chase call:** Rapid, buzzy *bzzt-bzzt-bzzt* during aggressive territorial pursuit.

#### Evolutionary Note

Anna's Hummingbird's northward range expansion into the PNW represents one of the most dramatic avian range shifts of the 20th century, driven not by climate change alone but by the creation of an entirely new anthropogenic nectar resource — the hummingbird feeder. The species has effectively colonized an ecological niche that did not previously exist in the PNW (year-round nectar availability above 45°N latitude). This human-facilitated range expansion illustrates how anthropogenic resource subsidies can restructure avian biogeography within decades. The species' cold tolerance — surviving nights at -10°C through torpor — was likely a pre-existing adaptation from cold nights in its native California coastal range, pre-adapting it for PNW winters [O1, O2].

### Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Apodiformes, Family Trochilidae, Genus *Selasphorus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing; under review)
- IUCN: Near Threatened (uplisted from LC in 2021)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Red Watch List (steep decline)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 7.5-9 cm (3.0-3.5 in) — one of the smallest birds in the PNW
- **Wingspan:** 11 cm (4.3 in)
- **Mass:** 2.7-5.0 g (0.10-0.18 oz) — males average 3.2 g; females 3.4 g (females slightly heavier due to egg production demands)
- **Plumage:** Males: brilliant rufous (coppery-orange) above and on flanks, iridescent orange-red gorget (appearing dark until struck by direct sunlight, then exploding into flame-orange), white breast band, rufous tail. Some males show variable amounts of green on back ("green-backed" variants — extensive intergradation with Allen's Hummingbird in southern OR). Females: green above, rufous flanks, rufous-washed tail with white tips on outer feathers, small central cluster of orange-red gorget spots.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males have full gorget and rufous back; females green-backed with spotted gorget and white-tipped tail.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Forest openings, meadow edges, riparian corridors, and early-successional habitats with abundant nectar flowers. Prefers forest-edge mosaics where red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*), salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), columbine (*Aquilegia formosa*), Indian paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.), and penstemon (*Penstemon* spp.) bloom in sequence from April through July. Breeds from sea level to timberline.
- **Migration:** The longest migration relative to body size of any bird — approximately 6,400 km (4,000 mi) round trip for a 3-gram bird. Spring migration follows the Pacific Coast northward (February-May), tracking the sequential bloom of flowering plants. Fall migration follows a more inland route through the Rocky Mountain corridor, fueled by mountain wildflower meadows at 2,000-3,500 m elevation. This clockwise elliptical migration pattern maximizes access to peak nectar availability across the entire western landscape.
- **Wintering:** Western Mexico (Guerrero to Sinaloa). A few individuals winter along the Gulf Coast of the southeastern US — the longest-distance wintering vagrancy of any North American hummingbird.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 3,000 m (10,000 ft); breeding concentrated sea level to 2,000 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest edges), Zone 2 (Montane Conifer openings)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — fall migration), Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Microhabitat:** The most pugnacious hummingbird in the PNW — defends nectar patches against all comers, including birds 10x its mass (Steller's Jay, Western Tanager). Territory defense is so energetically costly that males maintain territories only during periods of surplus nectar availability; during scarcity, they switch to "traplining" — visiting scattered flowers along a regular circuit without territorial defense.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Nectar from tubular red and orange flowers (color preference is innate, not learned). Critical PNW nectar plants in seasonal sequence: red-flowering currant (April), salmonberry (May), red columbine (May-June), Indian paintbrush (June-July), fireweed (July-August). Also takes small insects (gnats, aphids, spiders) by hawking and gleaning — insects provide essential protein, fat, and amino acids not available from nectar.
- **Foraging strategy:** Hovers at flowers with tongue extension rate of 12-14 licks per second. Aggressive territorial defense of concentrated nectar patches interspersed with trap-lining of dispersed resources. Pre-migratory fattening involves gaining 40-50% of body mass in fat stores over 7-10 days — the most rapid proportional weight gain of any PNW bird.
- **Energy budget:** Daily energy expenditure during breeding: approximately 30-40 kJ — equivalent to a 70 kg human running 200+ km per day. Enters torpor on cold nights to conserve energy, reducing metabolic rate by 90%.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Tiny cup of plant down, moss, and spider silk, decorated with lichen, attached to drooping conifer branch 1-10 m above ground. Often near forest-edge clearings with abundant flowers.
- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs, white, 12.9 x 8.6 mm
- **Incubation:** 15-17 days; female only
- **Fledging:** 20-22 days; female feeds fledglings for 3-5 days post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 1 (rarely 2 in southern PNW — time-constrained by migration schedule)
- **Breeding season:** April-July (males depart breeding grounds as early as late June; females and young follow July-August)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Steeply declining — BBS trend -2.6% per year across range (1970-2019), representing a cumulative ~60% decline since 1970. One of the most concerning population trends among PNW landbirds [G4, O4].
- **Estimate:** ~11 million continent-wide (PIF 2020), down from an estimated ~28 million in 1970 [O4].
- **Threats:** Loss and degradation of flowering meadow habitat (fire suppression, forest encroachment, overgrazing), climate-driven phenological mismatch between migration timing and peak bloom of nectar plants, pesticide impacts on insect prey, competition with expanding Anna's Hummingbird populations (year-round resident Anna's may pre-empt nectar territories before migratory Rufous arrive), habitat loss on wintering grounds in western Mexico, window and cat mortality at suburban feeders [G4, O1, O4].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Breeding | Fairly common | Pollinator of forest-edge flowers |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Breeding | Common | Primary hummingbird pollinator |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Spring migration | Fairly common | Transient pollinator |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Common | Riparian flower pollinator |
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Fall migration | Uncommon | High-meadow fueling stop |

#### Vocalizations

- **Chase call:** Sharp, buzzy *ze-chuppity-chup* — an aggressive chatter given during territorial chases. The most frequently heard hummingbird vocalization in PNW forests during breeding season.
- **Wing sound:** Males produce a high-pitched trill during dive display, generated by air through modified tail feathers. Dive arc is a steep J-shape, with the sound burst occurring at the nadir.
- **Perch song:** Thin, buzzy warble delivered from exposed perch — less complex than Anna's Hummingbird song.

#### Evolutionary Note

Rufous Hummingbird holds the record for the longest migration relative to body mass of any bird — its 6,400 km round trip represents approximately 78.5 million body lengths. (By comparison, the Arctic Tern's 40,000 km migration is "only" ~49 million body lengths.) The species' elliptical migration route — Pacific Coast northward in spring, Rocky Mountain corridor southward in fall — tracks the phenology of nectar flower blooming across an enormous latitudinal and elevational gradient, effectively chasing an endless spring. Rufous Hummingbird is the primary pollinator for dozens of native PNW wildflower species adapted for hummingbird pollination (red tubular corollas, no landing platform, no scent). Its decline threatens a coevolved pollination network that has no adequate substitute pollinator [O1, O3].

### Calliope Hummingbird (*Selasphorus calliope*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Apodiformes, Family Trochilidae, Genus *Selasphorus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 7-8 cm (2.8-3.1 in) — the smallest bird in the United States and smallest long-distance migrant bird in the world
- **Wingspan:** 10.5 cm (4.1 in)
- **Mass:** 2.3-3.4 g (0.08-0.12 oz)
- **Plumage:** Males: iridescent magenta gorget with elongated streaks (rays) extending below onto white breast (diagnostic — no other North American hummingbird has a streaked gorget), green crown and back, buffy flanks. Short tail does not extend beyond wingtips at rest. Females: green above, buffy below with faint streaking on throat, peachy wash on flanks.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Mountain meadows, forest edges, and regenerating clearcuts at mid to high elevations (1,000-2,500 m). Prefers subalpine and montane ecotones with abundant wildflowers. More strongly associated with mountain habitats than other PNW hummingbirds.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — meadow edges)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — upper breeding limit)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Nectar from mountain wildflowers — Indian paintbrush, penstemon, columbine. Also small insects hawked in flight and gleaned from vegetation.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs
- **Incubation:** 15-16 days; female only
- **Broods per year:** 1

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS trend -0.8% per year, 2006-2019) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~4.5 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Mountain meadow loss to conifer encroachment (fire suppression), climate-driven phenological mismatch, competition with Rufous Hummingbird at shared nectar patches [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Calliope Hummingbird was recently reclassified from *Stellula* to *Selasphorus* based on molecular phylogenetics — it is deeply nested within *Selasphorus* and closely related to Rufous and Allen's Hummingbirds. Its streaked gorget (elongated individual feather plumes rather than a solid shield) is a derived trait unique in the genus [O1, O3].

## Order Gruiformes — Rails and Cranes

### Family Rallidae — Rails, Coots, and Gallinules

Rails are the phantoms of PNW marshes — heard far more often than seen, navigating through dense emergent vegetation with laterally compressed bodies (hence "thin as a rail"). Their loud, distinctive calls emanating from reed beds are a diagnostic feature of healthy freshwater wetlands. American Coot, while technically a rail, has adopted an open-water lifestyle convergent with ducks. Sandhill Crane represents the opposite extreme — one of the tallest and most visible birds in the PNW, whose bugling calls carry for kilometers across open valleys [O1].

### Sandhill Crane (*Antigone canadensis*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Gruiformes, Family Gruidae, Genus *Antigone*
**Status:** Breeding visitor and migrant (locally year-round in southern OR)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (Greater Sandhill Crane subspecies *A. c. tabida* — PNW breeding population — is a species of concern in multiple states)
- IUCN: Least Concern (species level)
- State WA: Endangered (breeding population)
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- State ID: Species of Greatest Conservation Need
- PIF: Not assessed (non-passerine)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 100-120 cm (39-47 in) — one of the tallest birds in the PNW, standing up to 1.2 m
- **Wingspan:** 170-210 cm (67-83 in) — wingspan exceeds that of Bald Eagle
- **Mass:** 3.0-5.5 kg (6.6-12.1 lb); males averaging 10-15% heavier than females
- **Plumage:** Overall gray with variable rusty-brown staining on body feathers (acquired by preening with iron-rich mud — not a pigment-based plumage color). Bare red skin patch on forehead and crown. White cheek patch. Long, dark legs, large feet. Juveniles lack red crown, more uniformly brownish. Bushy "tail" is actually elongated tertial feathers draped over the true tail — a unique silhouette among PNW birds.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males average larger. Plumage identical between sexes.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Freshwater marshes, wet meadows, agricultural fields adjacent to wetlands. Greater Sandhill Cranes (the PNW breeding subspecies) require extensive open wetlands with emergent vegetation for nesting and adjacent upland meadows or agricultural fields for foraging. Major PNW breeding areas include the Klamath Basin (OR), Malheur NWR (OR), Ridgefield NWR (WA), Conboy Lake NWR (WA), and upper Snake River Plain (ID).
- **Migration staging:** Large concentrations gather at traditional stopover sites during spring and fall migration — grain fields and wet meadows provide energy for long-distance flights. Central Oregon (Fort Rock, Summer Lake) and Columbia Basin are key PNW staging areas.
- **Wintering:** Central Valley of California (primary), southern OR (Klamath Basin — partial), and Gulf Coast. PNW breeding birds are long-distance migrants; some Klamath Basin birds are short-distance migrants or year-round residents.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,000 m (6,500 ft); breeding concentrated 1,000-1,500 m in OR interior basins
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — foraging, staging)
- **Microhabitat:** Nesting pairs require wetland territories of 2-6 hectares with shallow water (<30 cm) and dense emergent vegetation (cattail, bulrush) for nest concealment. Extremely sensitive to human disturbance during early nesting — will abandon nests if repeatedly flushed during the first week of incubation.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Omnivorous — grain (waste corn, wheat, barley from agricultural fields), tubers and roots of wetland plants (especially bulrush, sedge, and cattail rhizomes), berries, insects (grasshoppers, beetles, earthworms), small vertebrates (frogs, mice, snakes, nestling birds). Agricultural grain is the primary caloric source during migration and winter.
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks slowly through shallow water and upland habitat, probing soft substrate with long bill and picking food from the surface. Uses the bill to excavate tubers from muddy bottoms. Agricultural gleaning in harvested grain fields is a critical fueling behavior during migration.
- **Seasonal variation:** Breeding diet heavier in animal matter (invertebrates and small vertebrates) to meet protein demands of egg production and chick growth. Migration and winter diet dominated by agricultural grain.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous — pair bonds typically lifelong. Pairs perform elaborate unison calling and dancing displays during courtship and pair maintenance. Dance involves leaping, bowing, wing-spreading, stick-tossing, and synchronized calling — among the most complex courtship displays of any PNW bird.
- **Nest type:** Large mound of marsh vegetation (cattail, bulrush, sedge) in shallow water, 1-2 m diameter. Both sexes build.
- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs (rarely 1 or 3), olive-buff with brown markings
- **Incubation:** 29-32 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** Precocial; chicks leave nest within 24 hours but are dependent on parents for 9-10 months. Flight capability at ~70 days. Young migrate with parents and remain with them through the first winter.
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-August (egg-laying typically late April-May)
- **Age at first breeding:** 3-5 years. Long-lived — maximum recorded age 36 years.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Increasing from severely depressed historical levels, but still vulnerable. WA breeding population recovered from ~20 pairs in the 1940s to ~80-100 pairs currently (still Endangered status). OR breeding population ~500-700 pairs, concentrated in Klamath and Harney basins. ID populations stable at ~500-800 breeding pairs. Overall, Greater Sandhill Crane populations in the Pacific Flyway have increased 2-3% per year since the 1980s [G1, G9, G12, G13].
- **Estimate:** Species-wide ~800,000 individuals (primarily Lesser Sandhill Crane *A. c. canadensis*). Greater Sandhill Crane Pacific Flyway Management Population ~25,000 [G1, G9].
- **Threats:** Wetland loss and degradation (agricultural conversion, water diversion, drought), powerline collision (leading cause of non-hunting mortality — cranes fly at wire height during low-visibility conditions), predation on eggs and chicks by coyotes, ravens, and raccoons, recreational disturbance during nesting, and genetic isolation of small breeding populations (WA population is critically small and genetically depauperate) [G9, G12].
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], IDFG [G13]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Breeding | Uncommon | Wetland nester, invertebrate predator |
| Zone 7: Grassland/Ag | Migration/Breeding | Locally common | Agricultural gleaner, staging concentration |

#### Vocalizations

- **Unison call:** Paired male and female call simultaneously — the male produces a deeper, resonant *garooo-a-a-a* while the female overlaps with a higher-pitched *garooo-a-a-a-a*. The combined duet is audible at 3-5 km and is one of the most evocative wild sounds in the PNW interior. The call is produced by an extremely elongated trachea (windpipe) that coils within a hollow keel of the sternum — acting as a resonating chamber that amplifies the low-frequency sound.
- **Guard call:** Loud, trumpeting *garoo-a-a* given by single bird when alarmed or defending territory.
- **Flight call:** Continuous, rolling *garoo-a-a* calls given by migrating flocks — audible from the ground as V-formations pass overhead at altitude.

#### Evolutionary Note

Cranes (Gruidae) are among the oldest extant bird families — the fossil record extends to the Eocene (~40 Ma), and a Sandhill Crane fossil from Nebraska dates to ~2.5 Ma, making it a candidate for the oldest known living bird species (though taxonomic assignment of fossils is debated). The elongated, coiled trachea that produces the crane's resonant call is an anatomical feature shared with swans but evolved independently — convergent evolution for long-distance acoustic communication across open landscapes. The Greater Sandhill Crane (*A. c. tabida*) breeding in the PNW is the largest subspecies and the one most impacted by historical wetland drainage [O1, O3].

#### Cultural Significance

Sandhill Cranes hold deep significance for multiple Indigenous peoples of the Columbia Plateau and Great Basin. The Klamath people (*Ewauna*) of southern Oregon have documented relationships with cranes spanning thousands of years at marshes they have stewarded since time immemorial. Crane dances and crane imagery appear in the material culture of Plateau peoples. The crane's annual return to nesting marshes is a seasonal marker in traditional ecological calendars [C2, C9].

### American Coot (*Fulica americana*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Gruiformes, Family Rallidae, Genus *Fulica*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (game species)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 34-43 cm (13.4-16.9 in)
- **Wingspan:** 58-71 cm (23-28 in)
- **Mass:** 427-848 g (15.1-29.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Slate-gray to black overall with white bill and frontal shield (reddish-brown callus at bill base), white undertail coverts visible when swimming. Lobed toes (not webbed) — a rail family trait enabling both swimming and walking on floating vegetation.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Freshwater marshes, ponds, lakes, and slow rivers with emergent vegetation. The most aquatic member of the rail family in the PNW — swims in open water like a duck, dives for aquatic vegetation, and walks on floating mats. Also forages on land in adjacent grass and lawn areas.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — farm ponds, urban parks)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Aquatic vegetation (pondweed, algae, duckweed, cattail), supplemented by insects, snails, small fish, and terrestrial grass. Dives to 2-3 m to collect aquatic plants; also "kleptoparasitizes" diving ducks by stealing food items brought to the surface.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Floating platform of marsh vegetation with ramp, anchored to emergent plants
- **Clutch size:** 8-12 eggs (up to 22 documented — may include eggs from conspecific brood parasitism)
- **Incubation:** 21-25 days; both sexes

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS trend +0.2% per year, 2006-2019) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~6 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Wetland loss, botulism outbreaks on warm-water lakes [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** Variety of grunting, clucking, and cackling sounds — *kuk-kuk-kuk-kuk*, *putt-putt*, and harsh *kak*. Among the most vocal waterbirds; coot-dominated marshes are noisy environments.

## Order Pelecaniformes — Pelicans, Herons, Egrets, and Bitterns

### Families Ardeidae and Pelecanidae

The Pelecaniformes in the PNW include two disparate groups unified by recent molecular phylogenetics: the wading herons, egrets, and bitterns (Ardeidae), and the fish-scooping pelicans (Pelecanidae). Herons are the dominant wading predators of PNW wetlands and shorelines, using patient ambush strategies to capture fish, frogs, and invertebrates. Great Blue Heron is the iconic wader of PNW rivers and estuaries; its colonial nesting rookeries are among the most visible concentrations of large birds in the region. Pelicans represent boom-and-bust population dynamics tied to water management and fish availability at a few critical breeding colonies [O1].

---

### Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Pelecaniformes, Family Ardeidae, Genus *Ardea*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (rookeries monitored as Priority Habitats)
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 97-137 cm (38-54 in) — the tallest bird in the PNW, standing up to 1.3 m
- **Wingspan:** 167-201 cm (66-79 in) — the largest wingspan of any PNW heron
- **Mass:** 2.1-3.6 kg (4.6-7.9 lb); males average ~10% heavier than females
- **Plumage:** Blue-gray overall with black stripe extending above the eye to long, trailing black occipital plumes. White crown, rufous thighs, long yellowish bill, long gray-black legs. Breeding adults develop elongated plumes on breast, back, and head. In flight, folds neck into S-shape (diagnostic — distinguishes herons from cranes, which fly with necks extended).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males average larger with slightly longer bills. Plumage identical.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Virtually any aquatic habitat in the PNW — riverbanks, lake shores, tidal flats, estuaries, marshes, ditches, farm ponds, urban waterfronts. The most habitat-generalist wading bird in the region. Requires only shallow water with fish, amphibian, or invertebrate prey and adjacent trees or structures for nesting.
- **Nesting:** Colonial — breeds in rookeries (heronries) of 5-500+ pairs, typically in tall trees (cottonwood, alder, Douglas-fir) near water. Some colonies on islands or in remote wetlands; others in urban parks and suburban neighborhoods. Rookery sites may be used for decades if undisturbed.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,500 m (5,000 ft); most common below 600 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — urban/suburban waterways), Zone 7 (Agricultural — irrigation ditches, farm ponds), Zone 4 (Foothill — streams)
- **Microhabitat:** Foraging requires shallow water (<50 cm) with clear visibility for visual prey detection. Stands motionless for extended periods (15-30 minutes), then strikes with explosive speed — the bill functions as a spear, propelled by a specialized coiled-spring neck mechanism.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish (sculpin, stickleback, juvenile salmonids, sunfish, carp) comprise 60-80% of diet. Also frogs, salamanders, crayfish, small mammals (voles, mice, gophers), snakes, nestling birds, and large insects. The most catholic diet of any PNW wading bird.
- **Foraging strategy:** Patient ambush predator — stands motionless in shallow water or on bank, then strikes with a rapid, downward neck extension. Strike speed exceeds 0.05 seconds from initiation to contact. The sixth cervical vertebra is modified with an elongated ventral process that functions as a fulcrum for the explosive S-strike. Also walks slowly through shallows, stirring prey with feet ("foot-stirring"). Occasionally forages in dry fields for voles and gophers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Fish dominate diet in warmer months when aquatic prey is abundant. Winter diet may shift to include more terrestrial prey (voles, mice) when water bodies freeze in the interior.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous within breeding season; pair bonds reformed annually
- **Nest type:** Large stick platform (60-120 cm diameter) in tree canopy, 10-30 m above ground. Nests are reused and enlarged annually, becoming very bulky over multiple seasons. Both sexes build.
- **Clutch size:** 3-6 eggs (typically 4), pale blue-green
- **Incubation:** 25-30 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** 55-65 days; young return to nest for feeding for 2-3 weeks after first flights. Full independence at ~3 months.
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** February-August (nest building begins February; egg-laying March-April)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS trend +0.3% per year, 2006-2019) [G4]. Rookery monitoring in WA shows stable colony numbers but occasional colony abandonment due to Bald Eagle disturbance (recovered eagle populations prey on heron chicks and cause colony desertion) [G9].
- **Estimate:** ~390,000 continent-wide (Waterbird Conservation Council). PNW populations healthy — one of the most reliably encountered large birds in the region [G9, O4].
- **Threats:** Rookery disturbance (construction, logging, human recreation near colonies), Bald Eagle predation and disturbance at rookeries (an increasing issue as eagle populations recover), loss of large cottonwood and alder trees used for nesting, water pollution reducing prey fish populations, botulism on warm-water lakes [G9, O1].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Year-round | Common | Apex wading predator |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Common | Urban waterway stalker |
| Zone 7: Grassland/Ag | Year-round | Fairly common | Farm-pond and ditch predator |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Uncommon | Stream corridors |

#### Vocalizations

- **Flight call:** Deep, hoarse, prehistoric-sounding *FRAAHNK* — a guttural croak given when flushed or arriving at rookery. One of the most distinctive bird sounds in PNW riparian habitats.
- **Colony sounds:** At rookeries, a cacophony of croaking, bill-clapping, and squawking — often audible 0.5 km from colony.
- **Alarm:** Harsh *rawk-rawk* when disturbed at foraging sites.

#### Evolutionary Note

Great Blue Heron is the largest heron in North America and one of the most widely distributed wading birds on the continent. The species exhibits remarkable behavioral flexibility — hunting in salt and fresh water, from estuaries to alpine streams, and taking prey from fish to field mice. The West Coast subspecies (*A. h. fannini*) is slightly darker and more sedentary than interior populations. A distinctive all-white morph ("Great White Heron") occurs in Florida and the Caribbean but not in the PNW. The S-strike neck mechanism — a kinematic system involving a modified sixth cervical vertebra — generates strike velocities comparable to a snake strike and is shared across the heron family (Ardeidae) as a synapomorphy [O1, O3].

### American White Pelican (*Pelecanus erythrorhynchos*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Pelecaniformes, Family Pelecanidae, Genus *Pelecanus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor and migrant
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Endangered (as breeder)
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 127-165 cm (50-65 in)
- **Wingspan:** 244-290 cm (96-114 in) — the widest wingspan of any PNW bird
- **Mass:** 5.0-9.0 kg (11-20 lb) — among the heaviest flying birds in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Entirely white with black primaries and outer secondaries (visible in flight). Enormous orange-yellow bill with expandable gular pouch. Breeding adults develop a laterally flattened horn-like projection on the upper mandible (shed after breeding season) and pale yellowish breast feathers.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Colonial — nests on islands in large, shallow freshwater lakes (protection from terrestrial predators). PNW breeding colonies concentrated in the Klamath Basin and Malheur NWR, with smaller colonies in eastern WA and ID.
- **Foraging:** Shallow lakes, marshes, and rivers within commuting distance (up to 50 km) of breeding colonies. Cooperative group-fishing — lines of pelicans herd fish into shallows.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe — interior basin lakes)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish — caught by dipping bill and scooping prey in expandable gular pouch (does not plunge-dive like Brown Pelican). Cooperative herding behavior — groups of 5-20 pelicans form semicircles and drive fish toward shallows.
- **Daily food intake:** ~1.8 kg of fish per day during breeding season.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs (but typically raises only 1 chick — obligate siblicide in most broods)
- **Incubation:** 29-36 days
- **Fledging:** ~65 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Increasing following historical near-extirpation. PNW breeding populations have recovered from <1,000 pairs in the 1960s to ~5,000-7,000 pairs currently, primarily at Klamath/Malheur colonies [G1, G12].
- **Threats:** Water management at breeding colonies (island flooding or predator access), human disturbance causing colony abandonment, competition with commercial fisheries (real or perceived — a source of human-wildlife conflict at Klamath fish hatcheries), West Nile virus [O1, G12].

### Brown Pelican (*Pelecanus occidentalis*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Pelecaniformes, Family Pelecanidae, Genus *Pelecanus*
**Status:** Non-breeding visitor (post-breeding dispersal — does not breed in PNW)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Delisted (recovered) — listed as Endangered 1970, delisted 2009
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Endangered (as occurring species)
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 106-137 cm (42-54 in)
- **Wingspan:** 183-228 cm (72-90 in)
- **Mass:** 2.0-5.0 kg (4.4-11.0 lb)
- **Plumage:** Brown-gray overall with white to yellowish head and neck (adults). Dark belly, large gray bill with extensive gular pouch. Breeding adults have dark chestnut hindneck and bright red gular pouch (not seen in PNW — birds present during non-breeding season). Immatures brownish overall with pale belly.

#### Habitat

- **Post-breeding dispersal (PNW):** Coastal marine waters — bays, estuaries, harbors, jetties, rocky headlands, offshore rocks. Arrives along OR and WA coast June-October, with peak numbers August-September. Birds present in PNW are post-breeding adults and juveniles from California breeding colonies (Channel Islands, primarily) dispersing northward to exploit summer fish abundance.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Coastal/Marine)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Schooling fish — anchovy, sardine, herring, smelt — captured by spectacular plunge-diving from heights of 10-20 m. Impacts water at ~60 km/h; air sacs under facial skin cushion the impact. The expandable gular pouch (capacity ~11 liters) functions as a net, straining water while retaining fish.

#### Population — The DDT Recovery Story

- **Historical trend:** One of the most dramatic conservation recovery stories in North American wildlife. Brown Pelican was listed as federally Endangered in 1970 after DDT-induced eggshell thinning caused catastrophic reproductive failure. DDE (the primary DDT metabolite) interfered with calcium carbonate deposition in the eggshell gland, reducing shell thickness by 15-25% — sufficient to cause eggs to break under the weight of incubating adults. The Anacapa Island (CA) colony declined from ~5,000 pairs to fewer than 100 nesting attempts, with near-zero productivity, during the late 1960s.
- **Recovery:** Following the 1972 US ban on DDT, eggshell thickness gradually recovered over 10-15 years. By the 1990s, reproductive success returned to pre-DDT levels, and the species was delisted in 2009. The California/Pacific population grew from a nadir of ~1,000 breeding pairs in the early 1970s to >80,000 pairs by 2010.
- **Current PNW status:** Post-breeding visitors to PNW coast number 5,000-15,000 annually (July-October). Numbers have increased substantially since the 1990s, tracking breeding population recovery to the south. Increasingly regular year-round along the southern OR coast [G1, G15, O1].
- **Ongoing threats:** Oil spills (Deepwater Horizon 2010 killed an estimated 80,000+ pelicans across the Gulf Coast population), domoic acid poisoning from harmful algal blooms (*Pseudo-nitzschia* diatoms producing the neurotoxin in anchovies and sardines), fishing line and hook entanglement, reduction of anchovy/sardine prey stocks by commercial fisheries, and climate-driven shifts in prey distribution [G1, G15, O1].
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], NOAA [G15], Cornell [O1]

#### Vocalizations

- **Generally silent** outside of breeding colonies (which are not in the PNW). Occasional low grunting when perched in groups.

#### Evolutionary Note

Brown Pelican is the only pelican species that plunge-dives (all others are surface-feeders). This foraging specialization required multiple anatomical modifications: subcutaneous air sacs that cushion the impact, a reinforced skull, nictitating membranes that protect the eyes, and a flexible gular pouch that expands instantaneously on water contact. The DDT/Brown Pelican story was a foundational case study for Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962) and became a central argument for the establishment of the Environmental Protection Agency (1970) and the Endangered Species Act (1973) [O1].

### American Bittern (*Botaurus lentiginosus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Pelecaniformes, Family Ardeidae, Genus *Botaurus*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Monitor species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 59-85 cm (23-33 in)
- **Wingspan:** 92-115 cm (36-45 in)
- **Mass:** 370-500 g (13-17.6 oz)
- **Plumage:** Brown streaked plumage — extraordinary camouflage in marsh vegetation. When alarmed, points bill skyward and compresses body, aligning brown streaks with surrounding cattails and reeds — virtually invisible. Black neck patch (moustachial stripe) visible at close range.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Large, densely vegetated freshwater marshes with tall emergent cover (cattail, bulrush, sedge). Extremely secretive — rarely seen in the open. Requires large, undisturbed marsh tracts (>10 hectares minimum).
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish, frogs, crayfish, snakes, voles, large insects. Patient ambush predator in dense marsh — walks slowly through vegetation and strikes at prey.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs in a platform nest hidden in dense marsh vegetation
- **Incubation:** 24-28 days; female only

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — associated with wetland loss throughout PNW [G4, G9]
- **Threats:** Wetland drainage, marsh fragmentation, disturbance [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** The "thunder-pump" or "stake-driver" — an extraordinary deep, resonant, three-syllable booming: *PUMP-er-LUNK*... *PUMP-er-LUNK* — among the lowest-frequency vocalizations of any PNW bird (~100 Hz). Produced by inflating the esophagus and expelling air in gulping contractions. Audible at 0.5-1 km across open marsh. Primarily given at dusk and dawn during breeding season (April-June). This vocalization is the most reliable indicator of American Bittern presence [O1].

## Order Accipitriformes — Hawks, Eagles, and Allies

### Families Accipitridae and Pandionidae

The diurnal raptors of the PNW include some of the most charismatic and ecologically influential birds in the region. Bald Eagles sit atop aquatic food webs, their population recovery from DDT-era collapse mirroring the Brown Pelican story. Northern Goshawk is the apex predator of closed-canopy old-growth forest, and its habitat requirements have made it a management indicator species for forest planning. Red-tailed Hawk is the most common and visible raptor in the PNW, occupying every ecoregion from alpine to agricultural. Osprey, placed in its own family (Pandionidae), is the only raptor that plunge-dives for fish and has experienced dramatic population recovery in the PNW following DDT restrictions [O1].

---

### Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Haliaeetus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Delisted (recovered) — listed as Endangered 1967, reclassified to Threatened 1995, delisted 2007. Still protected under Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (1940).
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Sensitive — Recovery
- State OR: Not listed (recovered)
- PIF: Not assessed (non-passerine)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 71-96 cm (28-38 in); females 25% larger than males
- **Wingspan:** 178-229 cm (70-90 in) — among the largest wingspans of PNW land birds (exceeded only by American White Pelican and California Condor)
- **Mass:** Males 3.0-4.5 kg (6.6-9.9 lb); females 4.0-6.3 kg (8.8-13.9 lb) — pronounced reverse sexual size dimorphism (females larger), typical of raptors
- **Plumage:** Adults (5th year and later): diagnostic white head and tail, dark brown body, yellow bill, eyes, and feet. Immatures (1st-4th year): variable dark brown with progressively increasing white mottling; full adult plumage not attained until 4-5 years of age. First-year birds all dark brown with dark bill. Immature plumage stages are a common source of misidentification (often confused with Golden Eagle).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Females substantially larger (25-30% heavier). Plumage identical between sexes.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Closely associated with large water bodies — rivers, lakes, estuaries, and marine coastline. Requires large trees (or artificial structures) within 1.6 km of water for nesting. Old-growth conifers with broken tops or emergent crowns are preferred nest trees. Forages over open water, river bars, and shorelines.
- **Winter concentration:** Large winter aggregations form along salmon-spawning rivers (Skagit, Nooksack, Chilkat in BC) and at open-water reservoirs. The Skagit River in WA hosts 400-700+ wintering eagles feeding on chum salmon carcasses — one of the largest wintering concentrations in the lower 48 states.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,000 m (6,500 ft); concentrated below 600 m near major water bodies
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — near water)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Coastal/Marine), Zone 2 (Montane — high-elevation lakes)
- **Microhabitat:** Nest trees must be the largest in the stand — average nest tree DBH in PNW is >75 cm, with trees >100 cm DBH strongly preferred. Nest trees must have clear flight paths to and from the nest and a commanding view of surrounding water. Proximity to reliable fish prey (within 5 km) is the primary driver of territory selection.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish comprise 50-90% of diet depending on season and locality. In PNW: spawning and post-spawning salmon and steelhead (primary fall-winter food), bottom fish (suckers, carp), and marine fish. Supplemented by waterfowl (coots, ducks, grebes — often kleptoparasitized from other raptors), small mammals (rabbits, muskrats), carrion (road-killed deer, livestock), and colonial waterbird nestlings (heron, cormorant).
- **Foraging strategy:** Perch-hunting from shoreline trees and snags, soaring over water with plunge to water surface (feet-first, snatching fish from top 30 cm of water — does not fully submerge like Osprey). Kleptoparasitism is a significant foraging strategy — steals prey from Osprey (primary victim), other eagles, and herons. Carrion feeding is common, especially in winter.
- **Pirating behavior:** Bald Eagles routinely harass Osprey carrying fish, forcing them to drop their catch. This kleptoparasitic behavior was observed by Benjamin Franklin and contributed to his famous (likely apocryphal) objection to the Bald Eagle as the national symbol.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous — pair bonds typically lifelong. Pairs perform dramatic aerial courtship displays including mutual soaring, talon-locking, and cartwheeling descents.
- **Nest type:** Massive stick nest (the largest of any North American bird) — average 1.5-2.0 m diameter and 1.0-1.5 m deep. Nests are reused and enlarged annually; some PNW nests exceed 3 m diameter, 4 m depth, and 2,000 kg (4,400 lb). Typically built in the crown or upper branches of the tallest tree in a stand, 15-40 m above ground.
- **Clutch size:** 1-3 eggs (typically 2), white
- **Incubation:** 34-36 days; both sexes (female does ~70%)
- **Fledging:** 10-12 weeks; young remain dependent on parents for an additional 4-8 weeks post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** Nest building/refurbishment November-February; egg-laying February-April; fledging May-August

#### Population — The DDT Recovery

- **Historical decline:** Bald Eagle populations crashed across the lower 48 states during the 1950s-1970s due to DDT bioaccumulation. DDE (the primary metabolite) accumulated through the aquatic food chain — water→plankton→fish→eagle — concentrating at each trophic level. DDE inhibited eggshell calcification, producing shells 15-20% thinner than normal. Eggs broke under the weight of incubating adults. Washington's breeding population declined from an estimated 300+ pairs in the 1940s to fewer than 105 occupied territories by 1980. Oregon experienced similar declines.
- **Recovery trajectory:** Following the 1972 DDT ban: WA population grew from 105 pairs (1980) to 500+ (2000) to 900+ (2005) to an estimated 1,100-1,400 pairs by 2020. Oregon tracked a similar trajectory from ~100 pairs (1978) to 600+ pairs (2020). Federal delisting occurred in 2007. The PNW now supports some of the densest Bald Eagle populations in the contiguous US [G1, G9, G12].
- **Current estimate:** ~316,000 individuals continent-wide (USFWS 2021 status report — a 4-fold increase since 2009). PNW breeding population estimated at 3,000-4,000 pairs across WA, OR, and ID [G1, G9, G12].
- **Ongoing threats:** Lead poisoning from ingested ammunition fragments in gut piles and carcasses (the leading cause of eagle mortality in some populations), electrocution on power lines and transformers, wind-turbine collision, vehicle collision on roadside carrion, disturbance at nest sites by human development, and secondary poisoning from rodenticides. The continued presence of legacy DDT/DDE in aquatic sediments means low-level contamination persists in some food chains, though at levels insufficient to cause eggshell thinning [G1, G9, O1].
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Year-round | Common | Apex aquatic predator |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Fairly common | Nesting in large conifers near water |
| Zone 7: Coastal/Marine | Year-round | Common | Marine fish and waterfowl predator |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Summer | Uncommon | High-elevation lake territories |

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** High-pitched, chattering *kleek-kik-ik-ik-ik* — surprisingly thin and weak for such a large bird. The Hollywood "eagle scream" used in movies is actually a Red-tailed Hawk call — real Bald Eagles sound far less dramatic.
- **Duet:** Paired adults produce synchronized chattering during courtship and nest defense.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Haliaeetus leucocephalus* is one of eight species in the sea-eagle genus *Haliaeetus*, distributed across all continents except Antarctica and South America. Its closest relative is the White-tailed Eagle (*H. albicilla*) of Eurasia, from which it diverged ~1-2 Ma. The iconic white head and tail evolved as adult signaling plumage — the 5-year maturation period during which immatures gradually acquire adult plumage may serve to reduce territorial aggression from established adults toward non-breeding subadults [O1, O3].

#### Cultural Significance

Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) holds profound ceremonial and spiritual significance for Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, and virtually all other Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples. Eagle feathers are among the most sacred items in many traditions, governed by protocols that restrict their collection, possession, and use. Federal law (Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act, 1940) provides for legal distribution of naturally shed or salvaged eagle feathers to enrolled members of federally recognized tribes through the National Eagle Repository in Commerce City, Colorado [C1, C2, C4].

### Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Aquila*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (protected under Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 66-100 cm (26-39 in); females larger
- **Wingspan:** 180-234 cm (71-92 in)
- **Mass:** Males 2.5-4.5 kg (5.5-9.9 lb); females 3.6-6.4 kg (7.9-14.1 lb)
- **Plumage:** Dark brown overall with golden-tawny hackles on nape and hindneck (diagnostic). In flight, appears uniformly dark from below. Immatures have white patches at base of primaries and a white-based tail with dark terminal band (progressively lost over 4-5 years as adult plumage develops).

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Open and semi-open terrain — alpine ridges, grassland, shrub-steppe, canyon country. Avoids dense forest (contrasting with Bald Eagle). Nests on cliff ledges, rocky outcrops, and occasionally in large trees. In PNW, concentrated on the dry east side — Cascade foothills, Blue Mountains, Snake River canyon country, Columbia Basin.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — soaring), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Grassland), Zone 4 (Foothill — open terrain)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Medium-sized mammals — jackrabbits, ground squirrels, marmots, young pronghorn, and domestic livestock (lambs — a historical source of human persecution). Also takes game birds (sage-grouse, pheasant), snakes, and carrion.
- **Foraging strategy:** Soars on ridge thermals and mountain updrafts, scanning for prey. Stoops from height at speeds up to 240 km/h — among the fastest predatory dives of any PNW bird. Also low-contour hunting along canyon rims and sagebrush ridges.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Large stick nest on cliff ledge (preferred) or large tree — typically multiple alternate nests within territory, used in rotation
- **Clutch size:** 1-3 eggs (typically 2)
- **Incubation:** 41-45 days
- **Fledging:** 63-70 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining in PNW (BBS data limited) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~40,000 in western North America [O4]
- **Threats:** Wind-turbine collision (significant and growing mortality source), electrocution on power infrastructure, lead poisoning from ammunition fragments, loss of open rangeland to development, human disturbance at cliff nest sites [G1, O1].

### Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Accipiter*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing multiple times; reviews concluded "not warranted" but with monitoring recommendations)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Yellow Watch List

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 46-61 cm (18-24 in); females substantially larger than males (reverse sexual size dimorphism typical of accipiters)
- **Wingspan:** 98-115 cm (39-45 in)
- **Mass:** Males 630-1,100 g (1.4-2.4 lb); females 860-1,500 g (1.9-3.3 lb)
- **Plumage:** Adults: blue-gray above, pale gray below with fine dark barring, prominent white superciliary (eyebrow) stripe, red-orange eyes, dark crown. Immatures: brown above, heavily streaked below (not barred like adults). Long, rounded tail with dark bands. In flight, appears broad-winged and long-tailed compared to buteos.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Extreme size dimorphism — females average 25-35% heavier than males. This allows size-based partitioning of prey: males take smaller prey (thrushes, jays, squirrels); females take larger prey (grouse, rabbits, hares).

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Mature and old-growth conifer forest with closed canopy and relatively open understory — the forest structure that allows high-speed pursuit flight through the interior of the stand. Requires large, contiguous forest tracts — territory size 2,000-6,000 hectares in PNW, with a core nesting area of 30-60 hectares of mature forest centered on the nest tree. Douglas-fir, western hemlock, grand fir, and ponderosa pine stands of 80+ year age class are preferred. Uses both west-side and east-side forest types.
- **Nest area requirements:** Nest stands characterized by high canopy closure (>60%), large-diameter trees (>40 cm DBH mean), open understory, multi-layered canopy, and proximity to forest openings or edges used as hunting lanes. Plucking posts (horizontal branches or logs where prey is processed before delivery to nest) are diagnostic sign of goshawk territory.
- **Elevation range:** 300-2,000 m (1,000-6,500 ft); concentrated 500-1,500 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — mature stands)
- **Microhabitat:** The quintessential old-growth predator. Northern Goshawk is a USFS Management Indicator Species for mature forest habitat in PNW National Forests. Its presence is used as a proxy for forest structural complexity — stands occupied by goshawks reliably support the full complement of old-growth-dependent species. Forest management guidelines require retention of 30-60 hectare "post-fledging areas" (PFAs) around confirmed nest sites, where commercial timber harvest is restricted [G6, G8].

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Medium-sized birds (Steller's Jay, Varied Thrush, Ruffed Grouse, Dusky Grouse, Band-tailed Pigeon, woodpeckers) and mammals (Douglas squirrel, Snowshoe Hare, Northern Flying Squirrel). Diet composition varies by sex due to size dimorphism — males take more birds, females more mammals.
- **Foraging strategy:** Short-range ambush and pursuit. Hunts from concealed perch within or below canopy, then launches in explosive, maneuverable pursuit through dense forest — threading between tree trunks at speeds up to 60 km/h. Also contour-hunts along forest edges, using terrain and vegetation as visual barriers to approach prey undetected. Among the most agile fliers in the PNW relative to body size — the long tail functions as a rudder enabling sharp turns between trees.
- **Seasonal variation:** Snowshoe Hare importance increases in winter when deep snow forces hares into more predictable travel corridors along packed trails.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous; pair bonds often long-term. Dramatic aerial courtship with slow, undulating "sky-dance" display flights above the canopy.
- **Nest type:** Large stick nest (60-90 cm diameter) in the main crotch or against the trunk of a large conifer, typically 10-25 m above ground. Lined with green conifer sprigs (which may have antimicrobial function). Multiple alternate nests within territory, used in rotation.
- **Clutch size:** 2-4 eggs (typically 3), pale bluish-white
- **Incubation:** 32-38 days; primarily female (male delivers food to female on nest)
- **Fledging:** 35-42 days; post-fledging dependency period extends 4-6 weeks — fledglings remain in the PFA while developing hunting skills
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** March-August (nest building March-April; egg-laying April-May)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Uncertain — difficult to survey in dense forest. Regional assessments suggest stable to slightly declining, with populations tracking the availability of mature forest habitat. PNW populations estimated at 3,000-5,000 breeding pairs [G6, G9].
- **Estimate:** Continental population poorly known — estimated 100,000-200,000 individuals [O4].
- **Threats:** Logging of mature and old-growth forest (historical and ongoing primary threat), forest fragmentation reducing territory viability, West Nile virus (emerging threat), great horned owl predation on adults and fledglings, reduction of key prey species (especially Snowshoe Hare populations in some areas). Fire suppression paradoxically threatens east-side goshawk habitat by promoting dense, uniform stands that are vulnerable to stand-replacing wildfire rather than the mosaic of age classes created by historical frequent fire regimes [G6, G8, O1].
- **Source:** USFS [G6, G8], WDFW [G9], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Uncommon | Apex forest predator, MIS |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Rare | Mature stand specialist |

#### Vocalizations

- **Alarm call:** Loud, rapid *ki-ki-ki-ki-ki* (accipiter alarm) — a harsh, staccato series given near nest during intrusion. Extremely aggressive at nest defense — goshawks are among the few PNW raptors that will physically strike human intruders near the nest (talons-first contact is documented by wildlife biologists).
- **Contact call:** Higher-pitched, more drawn-out *keeee-yah* between pair members.

#### Evolutionary Note

Northern Goshawk is a Holarctic species — the same species occurs across North America, Europe, and Asia, one of the most widely distributed raptors on Earth. PNW populations belong to the subspecies *A. g. atricapillus*. Recent molecular work has proposed splitting the Eurasian and North American populations as separate species, but the AOS has not yet adopted this split. The name "goshawk" derives from Old English *gōshafoc* — "goose hawk" — referencing its size and power, though geese are not a regular prey item. In medieval European falconry, the goshawk was the "cook's bird" — prized for providing game for the table more reliably than the more prestigious but less versatile Peregrine Falcon [O1, O3].

### Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Buteo*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 45-65 cm (18-26 in)
- **Wingspan:** 110-141 cm (43-56 in)
- **Mass:** Males 690-1,300 g (1.5-2.9 lb); females 900-1,460 g (2.0-3.2 lb)
- **Plumage:** Extremely variable — at least 4 named subspecies in PNW with multiple color morphs. "Western" (*B. j. calurus*) is the most common PNW form: dark head, pale breast with streaked belly-band, dark patagials (leading edge of underwing), and the diagnostic rufous-red tail of adults. Dark morphs (entirely dark brown below) and rufous morphs occur. "Harlan's" Hawk (*B. j. harlani*) — a rare winter visitor — is mostly blackish with a mottled gray-white tail.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** The most habitat-generalist raptor in the PNW — found in virtually every ecoregion from sea level to timberline. Prefers open and semi-open landscapes with elevated perches for hunting: grassland, agricultural fields, shrub-steppe, forest edges, highway medians, suburban parks. Avoids dense closed-canopy forest interior (niche occupied by Northern Goshawk).
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** All zones where open terrain exists

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small to medium mammals — voles (primary), ground squirrels, pocket gophers, rabbits, mice. Also snakes, birds, and large insects. Voles (*Microtus* spp.) may comprise >60% of diet in agricultural landscapes.
- **Foraging strategy:** Perch-hunting (sits on poles, trees, fences, and soars on thermals scanning below) and hover-hunting (kiting into wind). Strike from above with feet-first impact.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2-3 eggs in a large stick nest in tree, cliff, or artificial structure
- **Incubation:** 28-35 days
- **Fledging:** 42-46 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to increasing (BBS trend +0.7% per year) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~2.3 million continent-wide — the most abundant raptor in North America [O4]
- **Threats:** Vehicle collision (frequently forages along roadsides), secondary rodenticide poisoning (consuming poisoned rodents), electrocution, wind-turbine collision [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** The iconic raptor scream — a descending, raspy *keeee-arrr* lasting 2-3 seconds. This is the "eagle scream" used in virtually every movie, television show, and advertisement to represent any raptor. The actual Bald Eagle call is far less dramatic. The Red-tailed Hawk's scream is arguably the single most recognizable bird sound in American popular culture [O1].

### Cooper's Hawk (*Accipiter cooperii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Accipiter*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 35-46 cm (14-18 in)
- **Wingspan:** 62-90 cm (24-35 in)
- **Mass:** Males 220-350 g; females 330-680 g (extreme reverse sexual size dimorphism)
- **Plumage:** Adults: blue-gray above, rufous-barred below, dark cap contrasting with lighter nape, long rounded tail with dark bands and white terminal band. Immatures: brown above, streaked below. Larger than Sharp-shinned Hawk with proportionally larger head, longer tail with rounded (not square) tip.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Woodland, forest edges, riparian corridors, and increasingly suburban and urban areas. One of the most successful urban-adapted raptors — has colonized city parks and neighborhoods throughout PNW, where bird feeders concentrate prey.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 2 (Montane — lower elevations)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Medium-sized birds (robins, jays, starlings, doves, flickers) — the primary "bird hawk" in suburban PNW. Also takes small mammals (chipmunks, mice). Has become the major predator at backyard bird feeders.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs in a stick nest in dense canopy
- **Incubation:** 30-36 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Increasing — one of the strongest population increases of any PNW raptor (BBS trend +2.1% per year) [G4]. Urban expansion and bird-feeder proliferation have created abundant prey concentrations.

### Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Accipiter*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 24-34 cm (9.4-13.4 in) — the smallest accipiter in the PNW
- **Wingspan:** 42-58 cm (17-23 in)
- **Mass:** Males 87-114 g; females 150-219 g
- **Plumage:** Similar to Cooper's Hawk but smaller, with proportionally smaller head (does not project beyond wing leading edge in flight), shorter tail with square tip, and thinner legs.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Dense coniferous and mixed forest — more forest-interior than Cooper's Hawk. Also edges and second-growth. Migrant and winter populations appear at bird feeders and in urban areas.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small birds (sparrows, warblers, kinglets, chickadees). The smallest "bird hawk" — takes prey proportionally smaller than Cooper's Hawk.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS trend -0.2% per year, not significant) [G4]

### Northern Harrier (*Circus hudsonius*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Circus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 41-52 cm (16-20 in)
- **Wingspan:** 97-122 cm (38-48 in)
- **Mass:** Males 290-400 g; females 390-600 g
- **Plumage:** Males: pale gray above, white below, black wingtips. Females: brown above, streaked brown below. Both sexes have diagnostic white rump patch (visible in flight) and owl-like facial disc. Long tail and wings held in a shallow dihedral (V-shape) during characteristic low quartering flight over open ground.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Open grassland, marsh, agricultural fields, and shrub-steppe. The most ground-associated raptor in the PNW — hunts by low quartering flight 1-3 m above ground, using both vision and hearing (the facial disc enhances sound localization, as in owls).
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland margins)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Voles (primary), mice, small birds, snakes, frogs. The facial disc — unique among PNW hawks — enables prey detection by sound in dense grass.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs in a ground nest in dense grass or marsh vegetation
- **Mating system:** Occasionally polygynous — a single male may provision 2-3 females simultaneously

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining [G4]
- **Threats:** Grassland loss to agriculture and development, ground-nest disturbance by farm machinery [O1].

### Swainson's Hawk (*Buteo swainsoni*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Buteo*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 43-56 cm (17-22 in)
- **Wingspan:** 117-137 cm (46-54 in)
- **Mass:** Males 600-900 g; females 800-1,400 g
- **Plumage:** Light morph (most common): dark breast (bib), white belly and wing linings, dark flight feathers. Dark morph: entirely dark brown. Intermediate morphs occur. In flight, two-toned underwing (pale linings, dark flight feathers) is diagnostic.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open grassland and agricultural land with scattered trees for nesting. In PNW, concentrated in the Columbia Basin, Snake River Plain, and Klamath Basin. Long-distance migrant — winters on Argentine pampas (17,000 km round trip).
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (grasshoppers, crickets) during breeding and on wintering grounds — the most insectivorous large raptor in the PNW. Also voles, ground squirrels, snakes, and small birds during nesting.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable in PNW breeding range [G4]
- **Threats:** Pesticide mortality on Argentine wintering grounds (organophosphate poisoning of entire flocks documented in 1990s), grassland conversion [O1].

### Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Accipitridae, Genus *Buteo*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (uncommon)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Threatened
- State OR: Sensitive — Critical

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 51-69 cm (20-27 in) — the largest *Buteo* in North America
- **Wingspan:** 134-152 cm (53-60 in)

- **Plumage:** Light morph: pale head, rusty (ferruginous) back and leg feathers, white underparts, white tail washed with rust. Dark morph (rare in PNW): entirely dark brown with white flight feathers. In flight, feathered legs form a distinctive rusty V against white belly.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining in PNW — WA population estimated at only 25-35 breeding pairs [G9]
- **Threats:** Shrub-steppe conversion, poisoning of ground squirrel prey, disturbance at nest sites, loss of isolated nest trees [G9, O1].

### Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Pandionidae, Genus *Pandion*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (some year-round residents in mild areas)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (recovered from DDT-era declines)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (recovered)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 50-66 cm (20-26 in)
- **Wingspan:** 127-180 cm (50-71 in)
- **Mass:** Males 1.2-1.6 kg (2.6-3.5 lb); females 1.6-2.0 kg (3.5-4.4 lb)
- **Plumage:** Dark brown above, white below with dark carpal patches on underwing and dark eye stripe. White crown. In flight, wings held with distinct crook at carpal joint — "M-shape" or gull-like profile diagnostic from below. Uniquely adapted feet: reversible outer toe (can grip with 2 toes forward, 2 back), spiny pads on soles for gripping slippery fish, and closable nostrils for plunge-diving.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Near any large water body with clear, fish-bearing water — rivers, lakes, reservoirs, estuaries, and coastal bays. Nests on elevated structures near water — originally large snags and tree tops, now predominantly artificial platforms, power poles, channel markers, and communication towers. The PNW's most conspicuous raptor nest — large stick structures visible from great distance atop utility poles.
- **Wintering:** Partially migratory — northern PNW populations migrate to Central and South America. Southern OR and coastal WA populations partially resident.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,500 m (5,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — near water), Zone 7 (Coastal/Marine)
- **Microhabitat:** The only raptor in the world whose diet is almost 100% live fish. Requires water with fish visible from the air — clear water with fish in the top 1 m. Turbid water bodies are unsuitable regardless of fish abundance.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish — 99%+ of diet. In PNW: trout, suckers, carp, bass, perch, bullhead, and (near coast) flounder, smelt, and juvenile salmon. Takes fish 150-600 g (optimal size ~300 g).
- **Foraging strategy:** Soars and hovers 10-40 m above water, then plunge-dives feet-first, fully submerging to grab fish with taloned feet. Success rate: approximately 25-70% of dives (varies with water clarity and fish density). Always carries fish headfirst in flight — the fish is rotated in the talons mid-air after capture to minimize aerodynamic drag. This fish-carrying posture is unique to Osprey and provides reliable in-flight identification.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Massive stick nest — 1-2 m diameter, on elevated platform near water. Nests reused annually, growing to enormous size (up to 2 m tall). Both sexes build.
- **Clutch size:** 2-4 eggs (typically 3), white with brown markings
- **Incubation:** 36-42 days; primarily female (male delivers fish to female on nest)
- **Fledging:** 50-55 days; post-fledging dependence 2-8 weeks

#### Population

- **Trend:** Strongly increasing — BBS trend +2.6% per year in PNW (2006-2019). One of the most successful DDT-recovery stories in North American ornithology. WA population grew from ~100 pairs (1970s) to >800 pairs (2010s). OR populations similarly recovered [G4, G9, G12].
- **Estimate:** ~500,000 continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Limited current threats in PNW. Ongoing: fishing-line entanglement, lead sinker ingestion, vehicle collision, nest platform conflicts with utility companies, and contaminant exposure (mercury, PCBs) in fish prey [O1].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Breeding | Common | Fish predator, nest-platform icon |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Breeding | Fairly common | Near-water nester |
| Zone 7: Coastal/Marine | Breeding | Fairly common | Coastal fish predator |

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** High-pitched, whistled *cheep-cheep-cheep-cheep* — a clear, carrying series given near nest and during fish-delivery. More musical and less harsh than Bald Eagle.
- **Alarm:** Rapid, insistent *cheep-cheep-cheep* escalating in tempo when intruder (often Bald Eagle) approaches nest.

#### Evolutionary Note

Osprey is the sole member of the family Pandionidae — so distinctive that it has been placed in its own family since the earliest days of modern taxonomy. It is the only raptor with a reversible outer toe, closable nostrils, and spiny foot pads — all adaptations for catching and holding live fish. The species is cosmopolitan, breeding on every continent except Antarctica. PNW Osprey belong to the subspecies *P. h. carolinensis*. The willingness of Osprey to nest on artificial structures (nest platforms, power poles, stadium lights) has been critical to population recovery — nesting platform programs administered by utility companies and wildlife agencies have created thousands of safe nest sites throughout the PNW [O1, O3].

### Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Accipitriformes, Family Cathartidae, Genus *Cathartes*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (mostly), some year-round residents in mild areas
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 64-81 cm (25-32 in)
- **Wingspan:** 170-183 cm (67-72 in)
- **Mass:** 850-2,000 g (1.9-4.4 lb)
- **Plumage:** Dark brown-black overall, small bare red head (adults; immatures have gray head), long tail, silvery flight feathers visible from below. In flight, holds wings in a distinctive dihedral (V-shape) and teeters/wobbles — diagnostic flight style distinguishing it from eagles and hawks.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open and semi-open landscapes — nests in caves, rock crevices, hollow logs, and abandoned buildings. Forages over all open habitats. In PNW, common from March through October; most populations migrate south for winter (southern OR residents may stay year-round).
- **Ecoregion presence:** All zones — the most broadly distributed raptor in the PNW by ecoregion

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Carrion — exclusively a scavenger (the only obligate scavenger among PNW raptors). Locates carcasses primarily by smell — one of the few birds with a well-developed sense of olfaction. Olfactory bulb is proportionally the largest of any bird. Can detect ethyl mercaptan (a decomposition gas) at parts-per-trillion concentrations.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to increasing [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~18 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Lead poisoning from ingested ammunition fragments in carcasses, vehicle collision on roadside carrion [O1].

## Order Strigiformes — Owls

### Families Strigidae and Tytonidae

Owls are the nocturnal counterpart to the diurnal raptors — partitioning the same prey base across the 24-hour cycle. PNW owls range from the massive Great Horned Owl (capable of killing skunks, porcupines, and other owls) to the diminutive Northern Pygmy-Owl (smaller than a starling but fierce enough to take birds its own size). The order's defining adaptations for nocturnal predation — asymmetrical ear placement for binaural sound localization, facial disc that functions as a parabolic reflector, and comb-like leading-edge feather fringes that suppress flight noise — are among the most remarkable sensory and aerodynamic specializations in the avian world.

The PNW is the epicenter of the most consequential owl conservation conflict in North American history: the Northern Spotted Owl, whose ESA listing in 1990 fundamentally reshaped federal timber policy and ignited a decades-long conflict between old-growth forest conservation and timber industry interests. The subsequent invasion of the Barred Owl from eastern North America has created a novel competitive dynamic that now threatens the Spotted Owl more severely than habitat loss alone [G6, O1].

### Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Strix*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Threatened (listed June 26, 1990 — the listing that launched the "timber wars")
- IUCN: Near Threatened (species level, *S. occidentalis*)
- State WA: Endangered
- State OR: Threatened
- Critical Habitat: Designated 1992, revised 2008, 2012 — approximately 3.6 million hectares across WA, OR, and northern CA

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 41-48 cm (16-19 in)
- **Wingspan:** 107-114 cm (42-45 in)
- **Mass:** Males 490-610 g (1.1-1.3 lb); females 570-690 g (1.3-1.5 lb) — females average 15-20% heavier
- **Plumage:** Dark brown overall with large white spots on head, back, and underparts (the "spotted" pattern diagnostic for the species). Large dark eyes (no yellow — a consistent distinction from Barred Owl, which has dark eyes but different facial disc pattern). Round head without ear tufts. Facial disc brown with concentric darker rings. Underparts spotted with white on dark brown ground.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Females average larger. Plumage identical between sexes.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Old-growth and mature coniferous forest (>200 years) with complex, multi-layered canopy structure. The Northern Spotted Owl is the most old-growth-dependent bird in the PNW and among the most habitat-specialized raptors in North America. Required habitat characteristics include: canopy closure >60%, multiple canopy layers (subcanopy, midstory, overstory), large-diameter trees (>80 cm DBH), high density of large snags and downed logs, and a moderate-to-dense understory of shade-tolerant species. In the PNW, this habitat type is predominantly Douglas-fir/western hemlock old-growth on the west slope and mixed-conifer old-growth on the east slope.
- **Territory size:** 800-3,000 hectares per pair (mean ~1,200 ha in WA, ~1,500 ha in OR), of which 30-50% must be suitable old-growth or mature forest. The large territory size reflects the relatively low prey density within closed-canopy forest.
- **Elevation range:** 200-1,500 m (650-5,000 ft); concentrated 300-1,200 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — old-growth remnants)
- **Microhabitat:** Roosts during the day in the dense canopy of large conifers, selecting microsites with deep shade and thermal buffering (important for thermoregulation — Spotted Owls are heat-sensitive and can experience heat stress at temperatures above 30°C). Nesting occurs in natural platforms — broken-top snags, large cavities, stick nests built by Northern Goshawk or other raptors, and mistletoe broom structures. Does not excavate its own cavity.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) is the single most important prey species, comprising 30-60% of diet by biomass in most PNW studies. Red tree voles (*Arborimus longicaudus*) are the second-most important prey in west-side forests (10-30% of diet) — both prey species are old-growth specialists themselves, reinforcing the Spotted Owl's dependence on late-successional habitat. Also takes woodrats (*Neotoma* spp. — important on east side), Snowshoe Hare, birds, and insects.
- **Foraging strategy:** Nocturnal sit-and-wait predation — perches on a low to mid-canopy branch and listens for prey movements on the forest floor or in the canopy. Drops silently onto prey from above. Hunts primarily in the first 3-4 hours after dusk and the last 2-3 hours before dawn. Silent flight (comb-like feather fringes suppress turbulence noise) enables approach without alerting prey.
- **Foraging habitat:** The old-growth forest floor and canopy — a structurally complex three-dimensional space where the owl's superior hearing and silent flight provide decisive advantages over prey. Simplified second-growth forest with uniform canopy and sparse understory provides inferior foraging habitat because prey density is lower and prey escape routes more limited.

#### Reproduction

- **Mating system:** Monogamous — pair bonds lifelong. Low divorce rate. Pairs maintain the same territory for multiple years (territory fidelity is high).
- **Nest type:** Does not build a nest — uses natural platforms: broken-top snags, large cavities in old-growth trees, abandoned stick nests of goshawk or other raptors, mistletoe broom platforms, and cliff ledges (rare). Platform selection appears to prioritize overhead canopy protection and proximity to large trees for fledgling branching.
- **Clutch size:** 2 eggs (range 1-3), white
- **Incubation:** 28-32 days; female only (male delivers all food to female during incubation and brooding)
- **Fledging:** 34-36 days; young "branch" (move to adjacent branches near nest) before true flight at ~40-45 days. Post-fledging dependency is extended — young remain with adults for several months, gradually developing hunting skills through autumn.
- **Broods per year:** 1. Does not renest if clutch is lost (extremely low reproductive rate for a raptor this size).
- **Breeding season:** March-September (courtship calling February-March; egg-laying March-April; fledging June-July)
- **Reproductive rate:** Extremely low — mean annual productivity is 0.4-0.8 fledglings per pair per year across surveyed populations, declining to <0.3 in populations experiencing heavy Barred Owl competition. The Northern Spotted Owl's life-history strategy is predicated on high adult survival compensating for low annual reproductive output — a strategy that becomes untenable when adult survival drops even slightly.

#### Population — The ESA Listing and Its Consequences

- **Historical context:** The Northern Spotted Owl became a symbol of the old-growth logging debate in the 1980s-1990s. By the late 1980s, approximately 80% of historical old-growth habitat in the PNW had been logged, and owl populations were declining at 3-5% per year. USFWS listed the Northern Spotted Owl as Threatened on June 26, 1990, under the Endangered Species Act. The listing and subsequent designation of Critical Habitat led to the Northwest Forest Plan (1994), which reduced federal timber harvest in the range of the Spotted Owl by approximately 80% from 1980s peak levels and established a network of Late-Successional Reserves on National Forests.
- **Current population trend:** Declining — despite habitat protections, Northern Spotted Owl populations have continued to decline at 3-8% per year across most of the range. WA populations have declined by approximately 70% since monitoring began in the 1990s. OR populations have declined ~50-60%. The cause of ongoing decline has shifted from habitat loss (which has stabilized on federal lands) to competition and predation by Barred Owl [G6].
- **Current estimate:** Approximately 2,000-3,000 pairs remaining rangewide (WA, OR, northern CA). WA population estimated at fewer than 500 individuals — critically small. OR population approximately 1,500-2,500 individuals. Northern CA population approximately 500-1,000 individuals [G6, G1, G9].
- **Barred Owl impact:** The arrival and rapid population expansion of Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) — a closely related species native to eastern North America that has colonized the PNW since the 1970s — is now the primary threat to Northern Spotted Owl survival. Barred Owls are larger, more aggressive, more behaviorally flexible, less habitat-specialized, and more productive than Spotted Owls. In areas of sympatry, Barred Owls displace Spotted Owls from territories, outcompete them for prey, and occasionally hybridize with them (producing fertile "Sparred Owls"). USFWS initiated an experimental Barred Owl removal program in 2015 to test whether Spotted Owl populations would recover in the absence of Barred Owl competition — preliminary results through 2023 show Spotted Owl territory occupancy stabilizing or increasing in removal areas, supporting the hypothesis that Barred Owl competition is the proximate cause of decline [G6, G1].
- **Threats summary:** (1) Barred Owl competition and predation (primary current threat), (2) continued loss and degradation of old-growth habitat on private and state lands, (3) high-severity wildfire eliminating occupied habitat (exacerbated by fire suppression creating uniform, dense fuel loads), (4) climate change (heat stress, altered prey availability, increased fire risk), (5) West Nile virus (emerging), (6) low reproductive rate making population recovery slow even under optimal conditions [G6, G1, G9].
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], USFS Lesmeister et al. 2018 [G6], WDFW [G9]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Rare (declining) | Old-growth obligate, ESA indicator |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Very rare | Old-growth remnant occupancy |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Four-note barking hoot — *hoo...hoo-hoo...HOOO* (accent on terminal note). Sometimes described as "Who cooks for you?" but with a different rhythm and quality than Barred Owl's similar mnemonic. The Spotted Owl's call is higher-pitched, less resonant, and more "barking" in quality than Barred Owl.
- **Contact call:** Hollow, whistled *co-WEEoop* given between pair members — a rising whistle followed by a descending note. Diagnostic — not produced by Barred Owl.
- **Location call:** Series of 7-15 rapid hooting notes — *hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo* — used to locate mate across territory.
- **Agitation bark:** Rapid, harsh *whek-whek-whek* when disturbed.

#### Evolutionary Note

The Northern Spotted Owl is one of three subspecies of Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis*): Northern (*caurina* — PNW), California (*occidentalis* — Sierra Nevada and coastal CA), and Mexican (*lucida* — SW US and Mexico). Divergence among subspecies is estimated at 0.5-1.0 Ma. The species belongs to the wood-owl genus *Strix*, a Holarctic radiation of forest-dwelling, cavity-nesting owls. Barred Owl (*S. varia*) is the closest relative — the two species diverged approximately 4-5 Ma and remained geographically isolated (Barred Owl in eastern North America, Spotted Owl in western) until Barred Owl expanded westward across the boreal forest of Canada in the 20th century. The hybridization between the two species confirms they are very recently diverged and not yet fully reproductively isolated [O1, G6, P20].

#### The Larger Significance

The Northern Spotted Owl ESA listing was a watershed moment in American environmental policy — it established that the Endangered Species Act could override economic interests (federal timber sales worth billions of dollars annually), that habitat destruction constitutes "harm" under the ESA, and that federal agencies have a legal obligation to manage lands for the conservation of listed species. The subsequent legal and political conflicts (including multiple congressional riders, court injunctions, and the creation of the Northwest Forest Plan) fundamentally reshaped how federal forests are managed in the PNW. The owl remains a test case for whether a species can be saved when the primary threat shifts from habitat loss (which policy can address) to interspecific competition from an invasive congener (which presents novel management challenges including the ethically controversial lethal removal of a native North American bird species) [G6, G1].

### Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Strix*
**Status:** Year-round resident (invasive in PNW)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (subject of experimental removal program to protect Northern Spotted Owl)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed (but designated as threat to ESA-listed Northern Spotted Owl)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 43-50 cm (17-20 in) — 10-15% larger than Northern Spotted Owl
- **Wingspan:** 99-110 cm (39-43 in)
- **Mass:** Males 580-770 g (1.3-1.7 lb); females 630-1,050 g (1.4-2.3 lb) — 15-25% heavier than Spotted Owl
- **Plumage:** Gray-brown overall with bold horizontal barring on upper breast (hence "barred") and vertical streaking on lower breast and belly (Spotted Owl has spots throughout). Dark eyes (shared with Spotted Owl). Round head without ear tufts. Facial disc with concentric rings similar to Spotted Owl but grayer overall with a more pronounced border.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Females larger. Plumage identical.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Much broader habitat tolerance than Northern Spotted Owl — uses mature and second-growth forest, mixed woodland, riparian corridors, suburban parks with large trees, and fragmented forest landscapes. This habitat flexibility is the key competitive advantage over the more specialized Spotted Owl. Barred Owl can sustain viable populations in 60-80-year-old second-growth forest where Spotted Owl cannot persist.
- **Native range:** Eastern North America — from Nova Scotia to Florida, west to the Great Plains. Expanded westward across boreal Canada during the 20th century, reaching British Columbia by the 1960s and colonizing Washington and Oregon by the 1970s-1980s. Now found throughout the PNW from coastal lowlands to mid-elevation montane forest. The invasion route followed the transcontinental boreal forest, possibly facilitated by European settlement creating fire-suppressed, continuous forest corridors across previously open landscapes.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,800 m (6,000 ft) — overlaps entirely with Northern Spotted Owl range and extends into lower elevations and more fragmented habitats where Spotted Owl is absent.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** More generalized than Northern Spotted Owl — takes a wider range of prey including small mammals (voles, mice, shrews, squirrels), crayfish, frogs, salamanders, snakes, fish, birds, and large invertebrates. The inclusion of aquatic and terrestrial prey (crayfish, frogs) in significant quantities expands the Barred Owl's resource base beyond what Spotted Owl can access. Northern Flying Squirrel and woodrats are taken where available, creating direct dietary overlap with Spotted Owl.
- **Foraging strategy:** Nocturnal sit-and-wait, as with Spotted Owl, but also crepuscular and occasionally diurnal. More likely to hunt from edges, clearings, and riparian corridors — habitats avoided by Spotted Owl. The broader temporal and spatial foraging envelope gives Barred Owl a decisive competitive advantage in energy acquisition.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2-3 eggs (occasionally 4) — same range as Spotted Owl
- **Incubation:** 28-33 days; female only
- **Fledging:** 35-40 days
- **Reproductive rate:** Higher than Northern Spotted Owl — mean annual productivity 0.8-1.5 fledglings per pair per year (versus 0.4-0.8 for Spotted Owl). This higher productivity, combined with broader habitat tolerance, drives the population growth rate that enables the Barred Owl invasion.
- **Age at first breeding:** 2-3 years (versus 3-5 for Spotted Owl)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Rapidly increasing throughout PNW. Barred Owl density in many PNW forest landscapes now exceeds historical Spotted Owl density by 2-5x. The population growth rate in newly colonized PNW habitat has been 10-15% per year during the expansion phase [G6, O1].
- **Estimate:** PNW population poorly quantified but likely tens of thousands of pairs. Continental population estimated at >3 million individuals [O4].
- **Impacts on native species:** Beyond the Northern Spotted Owl, Barred Owl expansion may be affecting other native PNW species — Western Screech-Owl (possible competitive displacement), Northern Pygmy-Owl (possible predation), and small mammal communities (altered predation pressure). The full ecological consequences of adding a large, generalist predator to the PNW forest ecosystem are still unfolding [G6, O1].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Common (increasing) | Invasive competitor to Spotted Owl |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Common | Generalist forest predator |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common | Edge and woodland predator |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Fairly common | Riparian corridor predator |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Eight-note hooting phrase — *hoo-hoo-ho-HOO, hoo-hoo-ho-HOOaw* — commonly rendered as "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-ALL?" The terminal note drops and has a distinctive hoarse quality. Much more resonant and lower-pitched than Spotted Owl's four-note bark. Given year-round, with peak calling February-April.
- **Caterwauling:** During courtship, pairs engage in spectacular vocal duets — a cacophony of hooting, barking, cackling, and screaming that can last several minutes and sounds uncannily like maniacal laughter. This duetting behavior is more frequent and conspicuous than any Spotted Owl vocalization, and its audibility at long range may function in territory establishment within newly colonized habitat.
- **Alarm:** Harsh barking *hoo-AW* when disturbed.

#### Evolutionary Note

Barred Owl is the closest living relative of Northern Spotted Owl — the two species diverged approximately 4-5 Ma, when the uplift of the Rocky Mountains and the expansion of the Great Plains created a geographic barrier between eastern and western *Strix* populations. They remained allopatric (geographically separated) for millions of years until human modification of the landscape enabled Barred Owl to cross the continent. The hybridization between the two species ("Sparred Owls") produces fertile offspring, confirming recent divergence and incomplete reproductive isolation. The Barred Owl invasion of the PNW is often cited as an example of "competitive exclusion in real time" — a textbook ecological process rarely observed at this scale in vertebrate communities [O1, G6].

### Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Bubo*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 46-63 cm (18-25 in)
- **Wingspan:** 91-153 cm (36-60 in) — the widest wingspan range of any PNW owl
- **Mass:** Males 910-1,800 g (2.0-4.0 lb); females 1,000-2,500 g (2.2-5.5 lb) — the heaviest owl in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Mottled gray-brown with heavy barring and streaking, large ear tufts ("horns"), bright yellow eyes, white throat bib. Facial disc with dark border. Highly variable in overall color — desert populations paler, forest populations darker. The quintessential "owl" in silhouette and popular imagination.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** The most habitat-generalist owl in the PNW — found in forests (all types), suburban areas, agricultural land, riparian corridors, canyon country, desert edges, and city parks. Absent only from dense, high-elevation alpine and treeless grassland. Uses abandoned hawk, eagle, and crow nests; also cliff ledges, tree cavities, and building ledges.
- **Ecoregion primary:** All zones where trees or cliff nesting sites exist
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,500 m (8,200 ft)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** The most powerful and least selective predator among PNW owls — takes anything from large insects to full-grown Snowshoe Hare. Regular prey includes rabbits, hares, skunks (tolerates spray — poor sense of smell), squirrels, rats, mice, voles, waterfowl, grouse, other owls (including Barred Owl and Spotted Owl), hawks, crows, domestic cats, opossums, porcupines, and medium-sized raptors. This is the only PNW predator that regularly kills skunks and porcupines.
- **Foraging strategy:** Nocturnal perch-and-pounce. Talons exert an estimated 500 psi grip force — among the most powerful of any raptor relative to body size.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Does not build own nest — appropriates stick nests of Red-tailed Hawk, Bald Eagle, crow, or Great Blue Heron. Also uses cliff ledges, tree cavities, and building structures.
- **Clutch size:** 2-3 eggs (range 1-5)
- **Incubation:** 30-37 days; female only
- **Breeding season:** The earliest-nesting raptor in the PNW — egg-laying begins January-February, when snow may still cover the ground. This early nesting ensures that young fledge and achieve independence before the autumn, when prey becomes scarce.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~5.3 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Ecological role:** The apex nocturnal predator of the PNW — controls populations of medium-sized mammals and is a significant predator of other raptors (including owls and hawks). Great Horned Owl predation is a major source of mortality for Northern Spotted Owl, particularly fledglings [O1, G6].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** The deep, resonant hooting — *hoo-hoo-hoooo, hoo-hoo* — instantly recognizable as the "classic owl hoot." Both sexes call; female's pitch is higher. Duetting between paired birds is a common winter night sound across all PNW habitats.

### Barn Owl (*Tyto alba*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Tytonidae, Genus *Tyto*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 33-39 cm (13-15.4 in)
- **Wingspan:** 80-95 cm (31-37 in)
- **Mass:** 400-700 g (14.1-24.7 oz)
- **Plumage:** Heart-shaped white facial disc (diagnostic), golden-buff upperparts with gray vermiculation, white to buff underparts with fine dark spots. Long legs. Dark eyes. The most distinctive facial structure of any PNW owl — the heart-shaped disc is a highly refined parabolic reflector for sound localization.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Open agricultural land, grassland, marsh edges. Nests in barns, silos, church steeples, nest boxes, tree cavities, and cliff crevices. More closely tied to open farmland than any other PNW owl. Absent from dense forest.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland margins)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Voles and mice — >90% of diet. The most specialized rodent predator among PNW owls. A single Barn Owl family consumes an estimated 3,000-4,000 rodents per year. This rodent-control service has made the species a target for "barn owl box" programs by farmers seeking biological pest control.
- **Foraging strategy:** Low quartering flight over open fields, using asymmetrical ear placement for precise sound localization. Can capture prey in complete darkness using sound alone — the most acoustically precise predator in the PNW [O1].

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cavity — barns, tree hollows, cliff crevices, nest boxes
- **Clutch size:** 4-7 eggs (up to 18 documented — adjusted to prey abundance)
- **Incubation:** 29-34 days; female only
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (may double-brood in years of high vole abundance)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining in PNW — loss of old barns and farm structures, conversion of grassland, secondary rodenticide poisoning [G4, G9]
- **Threats:** Secondary rodenticide poisoning (the single greatest current threat — Barn Owls consuming poisoned rodents suffer lethal anticoagulant toxicity), loss of nest sites (old barn demolition), agricultural intensification reducing vole habitat [O1, G9].

#### Vocalizations

- **Call:** Eerie, rasping screech — *SHREEEEE* — a prolonged, hissing scream that carries across open fields. Does not hoot. This screech is the origin of many "ghost" and "banshee" legends associated with old barns and churches.

#### Evolutionary Note

Barn Owl is the sole PNW representative of the family Tytonidae, which diverged from the Strigidae (typical owls) approximately 50-55 Ma — one of the deepest splits in the owl radiation. Barn Owl has the most cosmopolitan distribution of any non-pelagic bird — found on every continent except Antarctica. The species complex (*Tyto alba* sensu lato) may eventually be split into 3-4 species based on molecular data [O1, O3].

### Western Screech-Owl (*Megascops kennicottii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Megascops*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 19-25 cm (7.5-9.8 in)
- **Wingspan:** 55-62 cm (22-24 in)
- **Mass:** 100-200 g (3.5-7.1 oz)
- **Plumage:** Two color morphs — gray (most common in PNW) and brown/rufous. Both morphs heavily streaked and cross-barred with intricate camouflage pattern matching bark. Small ear tufts, yellow eyes, dark bill. In roosting posture against a tree trunk, virtually invisible.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Open woodland, riparian corridors, suburban areas with mature trees, oak woodland, and forest edges. Cavity nester — uses natural tree cavities, old woodpecker holes, and nest boxes. More common in lowland and foothill habitats than in dense montane forest.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — suburban)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Large insects (moths, beetles, crickets), small mammals (mice, voles), small birds, earthworms, crayfish. Diet shifts seasonally — more insects in summer, more mammals in winter.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs in tree cavity or nest box
- **Incubation:** 26-28 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to potentially declining (declining in areas where Barred Owl has expanded — possible competitive displacement or predation) [G4, O1]
- **Threats:** Barred Owl predation (emerging concern), cavity-tree loss, vehicle collision (perches on roadside wires) [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Accelerating series of hollow whistled notes — *hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoohoohoo* — bouncing ball pattern (accelerating toward end). Given year-round, peak January-April.
- **Duet:** Paired birds perform antiphonal duets — male and female alternate phrases in a continuous exchange.

### Great Gray Owl (*Strix nebulosa*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Strix*
**Status:** Year-round resident (uncommon)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 61-84 cm (24-33 in) — the tallest owl in the PNW by body length
- **Wingspan:** 137-152 cm (54-60 in) — the widest wingspan of any PNW owl
- **Mass:** 790-1,450 g (1.7-3.2 lb) — despite its enormous apparent size, weighs less than Great Horned Owl (much of the bulk is thick, insulating plumage and a large facial disc). Leg feathering so dense that the actual leg diameter is ~1/3 of the apparent feathered leg width.
- **Plumage:** Gray overall with fine barring and streaking, enormous facial disc with concentric dark rings (the largest facial disc of any owl — up to 50 cm diameter), bright yellow eyes, white "bowtie" marking below bill. No ear tufts.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Montane forest edges adjacent to meadows — the transitional zone between dense conifer forest (roosting and nesting) and open meadow (foraging). Requires broken-top snags or old raptor/crow nests within 200 m of open meadow. In PNW, concentrated in the Blue Mountains (OR), eastern Cascades, and northeastern WA.
- **Elevation range:** 900-2,000 m (3,000-6,500 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — forest-meadow ecotone)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Voles (*Microtus* spp.) — >90% of diet in most studies. The most vole-specialized owl in the PNW. Hunts from low perch at meadow edge, using the enormous facial disc as a sound-collection antenna. Can locate and capture voles through 45 cm of snow by sound alone — plunging feet-first through the snowpack to seize prey in subnivean tunnels [O1].

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2-5 eggs in abandoned raptor or crow nest
- **Incubation:** 28-36 days
- **Breeding frequency:** May skip breeding in years of low vole abundance

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable but naturally rare in PNW [G4]
- **Estimate:** PNW population small — estimated hundreds of pairs [O4]
- **Threats:** Logging of meadow-edge nesting habitat, climate-driven reduction in snowpack (may reduce subnivean vole populations), road mortality during winter irruptive movements [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Deep, evenly spaced hooting — *hoo...hoo...hoo...hoo...hoo* — 5-10 notes, each on the same pitch, at 1-2 second intervals. Lower-pitched and more evenly spaced than Great Horned Owl. Among the deepest-voiced owls in the PNW.

### Short-eared Owl (*Asio flammeus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Asio*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (declining)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 34-43 cm (13.4-16.9 in)
- **Wingspan:** 85-110 cm (33-43 in)
- **Mass:** 200-475 g (7.1-16.8 oz)
- **Plumage:** Tawny-brown with dark streaking, small ear tufts (usually invisible), bright yellow eyes with dark eye patches ("mascara"), pale facial disc. In flight, buffy wing patches and dark carpal crescents are diagnostic. Long wings relative to body — more moth-like flight than other owls.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Open grassland, marsh, agricultural fields, shrub-steppe. The most open-country owl in the PNW — avoids forest. Ground nester. Often active at dusk and dawn (crepuscular) in addition to fully nocturnal hunting.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture), Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland margins)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Voles (*Microtus* spp.) — 80-95% of diet. Hunts by low quartering flight over open ground, similar to Northern Harrier. May hunt simultaneously with harriers during crepuscular periods, partitioning the same vole resource by temporal overlap.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Ground scraper in dense grass or marsh vegetation — the only PNW owl that nests exclusively on the ground
- **Clutch size:** 4-7 eggs (up to 14 in vole outbreak years)
- **Breeding frequency:** Clutch size and breeding success strongly tied to vole population cycles

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -2.5% per year across range [G4]
- **Threats:** Grassland loss to agriculture and development, ground-nest destruction by farm machinery, secondary rodenticide poisoning [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Display flight:** Male performs barking *vip-vip-vip-vip* calls during moth-like display flight over breeding territory, interspersed with wing-clapping. One of the most conspicuous owl display behaviors — visible at dusk over open fields during breeding season.

### Long-eared Owl (*Asio otus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Asio*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 31-40 cm (12.2-15.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 86-102 cm (34-40 in)
- **Mass:** 200-350 g (7.1-12.3 oz)
- **Plumage:** Mottled brown and buff with long, close-set ear tufts (much longer and more central than Great Horned Owl's widely spaced tufts). Orange-yellow eyes, rufous facial disc with dark border. Very slender when alarmed — elongates body and compresses plumage to resemble a broken branch.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Dense woodland patches adjacent to open grassland or agricultural fields. Uses abandoned stick nests (crow, magpie, hawk) in shelterbelts, riparian strips, and small groves. More dependent on open-country foraging habitat than most forest owls. Communal winter roosts of 5-50+ birds in dense conifers.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture — shelterbelts), Zone 5 (Riparian — cottonwood groves)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Voles and mice — >95% small mammals. Nocturnal low quartering flight over open ground, similar to Short-eared Owl but exclusively nocturnal (temporal partitioning with the crepuscular Short-eared).

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs in an abandoned stick nest
- **Incubation:** 25-30 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Poorly known — secretive and under-surveyed [G4]
- **Threats:** Loss of shelterbelt and riparian grove nesting habitat, secondary rodenticide poisoning [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Low, resonant *hoo...hoo...hoo...hoo* — evenly spaced, single notes at 2-3 second intervals. Much quieter and less varied than Great Horned Owl.

### Northern Saw-whet Owl (*Aegolius acadicus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Aegolius*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 18-21 cm (7.1-8.3 in)
- **Wingspan:** 42-48 cm (17-19 in)
- **Mass:** 54-151 g (1.9-5.3 oz) — one of the smallest owls in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Brown above with white spots, white below with rufous streaking, large round head without ear tufts, bright yellow eyes, white facial disc with dark radial streaks. Juveniles distinctive — uniformly chocolate-brown with prominent white eyebrows (a plumage seen in no other PNW owl).

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Dense coniferous and mixed forest — breeds in mature forest with cavity trees, winters in dense thickets (often young conifers, holly, or ivy). Cavity nester — uses old woodpecker holes (especially Northern Flicker and Pileated Woodpecker cavities). Responds readily to nest boxes.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Deer mice (*Peromyscus maniculatus*) and voles — small mammals comprise >90% of diet. Also small birds, large insects. The most strictly nocturnal small owl in the PNW — almost never active during daylight.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 4-7 eggs in woodpecker cavity or nest box
- **Incubation:** 26-28 days; female only

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (widespread but secretive; likely under-counted) [G4]
- **Estimate:** ~2 million continent-wide [O4]
- **Threats:** Cavity-tree loss, forest fragmentation [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Monotonous, rhythmic tooting — *too-too-too-too-too-too-too* — repeated at a rate of ~2 notes per second, continuing for minutes to hours on end during breeding season (January-May). One of the most persistent and recognizable nocturnal sounds in PNW forests. The name "saw-whet" derives from a different call — a harsh, rasping note reminiscent of a saw being sharpened on a whetstone.

### Burrowing Owl (*Athene cunicularia*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Athene*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (seasonal) and rare year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (global, but declining regionally)
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Critical
- State ID: Species of Greatest Conservation Need

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 19-28 cm (7.5-11 in)
- **Wingspan:** 51-61 cm (20-24 in)
- **Mass:** 150-240 g (5.3-8.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Sandy brown above with white spotting, white below with brown barring, long legs (the longest relative to body size of any PNW owl — adapted for terrestrial locomotion), bright yellow eyes, white eyebrows, no ear tufts. Flat-topped head profile is distinctive. Frequently bobs and bows when agitated — a characteristic behavioral quirk.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open, treeless terrain with short vegetation and available burrows — grassland, shrub-steppe, agricultural field edges, airports, golf courses, and vacant lots. Nests underground in burrows excavated by badgers, ground squirrels, prairie dogs, or other mammals. Cannot excavate its own burrow in hard soil (occasionally digs in very soft substrate). In PNW, restricted to the Columbia Basin, Snake River Plain, and eastern OR steppe — all areas with active burrowing mammal populations.
- **Wintering:** Partially migratory — most PNW birds migrate south to SW US and Mexico. Some year-round residents in mild areas of southern ID and eastern OR.
- **Elevation range:** 200-1,500 m (650-5,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe), Zone 7 (Grassland/Agriculture)
- **Microhabitat:** Entirely dependent on burrowing mammals for nest sites — the loss of badgers, ground squirrels, and other burrow-digging species from a landscape makes it unsuitable for Burrowing Owls regardless of other habitat features. This obligate dependency on another species' engineering activity creates a cascading vulnerability chain: poison ground squirrels → lose burrows → lose Burrowing Owls.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Large insects (grasshoppers, beetles, crickets) and small mammals (voles, mice, ground squirrels). More insectivorous than most PNW owls — insects may comprise 60-70% of prey items (though mammals dominate by biomass). Also scorpions, small snakes, small birds, and earthworms.
- **Foraging strategy:** Crepuscular and partly diurnal — the most daytime-active owl in the PNW. Hunts from low perches (fence posts, dirt mounds, burrow entrances) and on foot, chasing prey across open ground. Also hover-hunts, kiting into wind to scan below. Uses mammalian dung to attract dung beetles to burrow entrance — one of the few documented cases of tool-use (bait-provisioning) in owls [O1].

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Underground — in a mammal burrow, typically 1-3 m long with a terminal nesting chamber. Nest chamber lined with shredded livestock dung, dried grass, and feathers. Multiple satellite burrows within territory used for escape cover and food caching.
- **Clutch size:** 6-12 eggs (one of the largest clutch sizes of any PNW owl)
- **Incubation:** 28-30 days; female only (male provisions female and guards territory)
- **Fledging:** 44-53 days; young appear at burrow entrance at ~2 weeks but do not fly until ~6-7 weeks
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** March-August (egg-laying April-May)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining significantly — PNW populations have declined an estimated 60-80% since the 1980s. WA population estimated at 200-400 breeding pairs (down from >1,000 in the 1970s). OR population estimated at fewer than 300 pairs. ID populations larger but declining [G9, G12, G13].
- **Estimate:** PNW population (WA+OR+ID): estimated 1,000-2,000 breeding pairs [G9, G12, G13].
- **Threats:** Poisoning and elimination of burrowing mammals (ground squirrel and prairie dog control programs directly destroy nest-site availability), agricultural conversion of native steppe, pesticide reduction of insect prey, vehicle collision (nests near roads and airports), predation by domestic cats and dogs in suburban-steppe interface, loss of livestock grazing (moderate grazing maintains short vegetation preferred by Burrowing Owls — cessation of grazing allows vegetation to grow too tall), and climate-driven changes in insect and small mammal prey availability [G9, G12, O1].
- **Source:** WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], IDFG [G13], Cornell [O1]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 6: Shrub-Steppe | Breeding | Uncommon (declining) | Burrow-dependent steppe specialist |
| Zone 7: Grassland/Ag | Breeding | Rare | Agricultural margin nester |

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Two-note cooing — *coo-COOO* (second note higher) — given from burrow mound or fence post, primarily at dusk. Repeated at 2-3 second intervals during breeding season.
- **Alarm:** Rapid chattering *kek-kek-kek-kek* when disturbed near burrow. When young are threatened, adults give a "rattlesnake mimicry" call — a buzzing hiss (*ssssssssss*) from within the burrow that sounds remarkably like a rattlesnake rattle and is thought to deter mammalian predators from entering [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Burrowing Owl (*Athene cunicularia*) is one of two species in the genus *Athene* (the other is the Little Owl, *A. noctua*, of Eurasia — introduced to New Zealand and formerly considered for introduction to North America). The genus is named for Athena, the Greek goddess of wisdom, whose companion was the Little Owl depicted on Athenian coins. The terrestrial and partially diurnal lifestyle of Burrowing Owl is unique among New World owls and represents a radical departure from the arboreal, nocturnal ancestral condition. The obligate dependency on mammalian burrows is an ecological mutualism — owls benefit from shelter, while their insect-consuming activity around burrow entrances may reduce ectoparasite loads for the mammalian burrow creator [O1, O3].

### Northern Pygmy-Owl (*Glaucidium gnoma*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Glaucidium*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA/OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-18 cm (5.9-7.1 in) — tied with Flammulated Owl as the smallest owl in the PNW
- **Wingspan:** 38 cm (15 in)
- **Mass:** 54-73 g (1.9-2.6 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray-brown or rufous-brown above with fine white spots, white below with dark streaking, long barred tail (often held at an angle — unusual for an owl). Two black "eyespots" (false eyes) on nape — a predator-deterrence feature shared with some tropical pygmy-owls. Yellow eyes, no ear tufts. Disproportionately small head for an owl.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Coniferous and mixed forest, forest edges, riparian woodland. Cavity nester — uses old woodpecker holes (especially Northern Flicker). Unlike most PNW owls, primarily diurnal — hunts during daylight hours, especially at dawn and dusk.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small birds (chickadees, nuthatches, warblers, sparrows), small mammals (voles, mice, shrews), and large insects. Remarkably aggressive for its size — regularly attacks and kills prey equal to or larger than itself. When prey is surplus, caches food in tree cavities (larder-hoarding).
- **Mobbing target:** Northern Pygmy-Owl is the most intensely mobbed raptor in PNW forests — its presence triggers alarm-calling and mobbing by dozens of songbirds. Birders use recordings of Pygmy-Owl calls to attract mixed-species flocks for observation (a practice that should be used sparingly to avoid undue stress) [O1].

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs in a woodpecker cavity
- **Incubation:** 28-29 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable [G4]
- **Threats:** Cavity-tree loss, potential predation by expanding Barred Owl [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Monotonous, evenly spaced tooting — *toot...toot...toot...toot* — single notes at ~2-second intervals. Higher-pitched and slower than Northern Saw-whet Owl's rapid tooting. Given primarily at dawn and dusk.

### Flammulated Owl (*Psiloscops flammeolus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Psiloscops*
**Status:** Breeding visitor
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-17 cm (5.9-6.7 in) — tied with Northern Pygmy-Owl as the smallest PNW owl
- **Wingspan:** 36-41 cm (14-16 in)
- **Mass:** 43-63 g (1.5-2.2 oz) — the lightest owl in the PNW
- **Plumage:** Gray and rufous morphs. Both cryptically mottled to match tree bark. Small ear tufts. Unique among PNW owls: **dark brown eyes** (all other PNW owls have yellow, orange, or dark eyes with visible iris — Flammulated Owl's dark eyes are thought to be an adaptation for its exclusively insectivorous diet, reducing reflected light that might alert insect prey).

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dry, open montane conifer forest — ponderosa pine and Douglas-fir with grassy understory at 900-2,300 m. Cavity nester (old woodpecker holes). In PNW, restricted to the dry east side — eastern Cascades, Blue Mountains, Okanogan. Entirely migratory — winters in Mexico and Central America.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — dry east-side ponderosa/Douglas-fir)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Nocturnal flying insects — moths, beetles, crickets, caterpillars. The only exclusively insectivorous owl in the PNW. This dietary specialization drives its migratory behavior — insects are unavailable during PNW winters.

#### Reproduction

- **Clutch size:** 2-4 eggs in a woodpecker cavity
- **Incubation:** 21-24 days

#### Population

- **Trend:** Poorly known — secretive, nocturnal, and weakly-voiced. Probably more common than detection rates suggest [O1].
- **Threats:** Logging of mature ponderosa pine (cavity tree loss), fire suppression altering forest structure, insect declines [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Soft, low-pitched, ventriloquial *poot...poot...poot* — single notes at 2-3 second intervals. Remarkably difficult to localize — the ventriloquial quality causes the owl to appear to be in a completely different direction than its actual position. This weak, easily overlooked call is the main reason the species was long considered rare before targeted surveys revealed it to be locally common [O1].

### Boreal Owl (*Aegolius funereus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Aegolius*
**Status:** Year-round resident (very rare in PNW)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (global)
- State WA: Not listed (very local)
- State ID: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 21-28 cm (8.3-11 in)
- **Wingspan:** 50-62 cm (20-24 in)
- **Mass:** 90-210 g (3.2-7.4 oz)
- **Plumage:** Similar to Northern Saw-whet Owl but larger, with a more defined facial disc bordered by black, and white spotting on forehead giving a "surprised" expression. Yellow eyes.

#### Habitat

- **Year-round:** Dense boreal-type spruce-fir forest at high elevations. In PNW, restricted to the Selkirk Mountains (northeast WA, northern ID) and possibly the highest elevations of the North Cascades. At the extreme southern edge of its circumpolar boreal distribution.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — high-elevation boreal-type stands)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small mammals (voles, mice, shrews), occasionally small birds.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Unknown in PNW — extremely rare and poorly surveyed. Likely fewer than 100 breeding pairs in WA/ID combined [O1, G9].
- **Threats:** Climate-driven loss of high-elevation boreal-type forest at southern range margin [O1].

#### Vocalizations

- **Song:** Rapid, staccato series of hollow notes — *hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo* — reminiscent of the winnowing of a snipe. Faster and more musical than Saw-whet Owl. Primarily February-April.

### Snowy Owl (*Bubo scandiacus*) — Brief

**Taxonomic position:** Order Strigiformes, Family Strigidae, Genus *Bubo*
**Status:** Winter visitor (irruptive — irregular, some years common, some years absent)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Vulnerable (global — uplisted 2017 due to Arctic breeding habitat loss)
- State WA/OR: Not listed

- **Length:** 51-68 cm (20-27 in)
- **Wingspan:** 126-145 cm (50-57 in)
- **Mass:** 1,100-2,500 g (2.4-5.5 lb) — the heaviest owl in the PNW during irruption years
- **Plumage:** Adult males: nearly pure white with few dark markings. Females and immatures: white with variable amounts of dark barring (more barred = younger/female). Bright yellow eyes. No ear tufts. Round head.
- **PNW occurrence:** Winter visitor — appears in PNW during irruption years (roughly every 3-7 years) when Arctic lemming populations crash, forcing owls south. Irruption birds appear November-March in open habitats — coastal beaches, agricultural fields, airports, and dune systems. Ocean Shores, WA, and Boundary Bay, BC, are famous irruption concentration points. Most birds return north by April. Does not breed in PNW [O1, O2, O16].
- **Population:** Arctic breeding population estimated at ~200,000 (declining due to climate-driven changes in lemming cycles and tundra habitat). PNW winter occurrence ranges from zero in non-irruption years to 100-500+ individuals in major irruption years [O1, O16].
- **Ecological note:** During irruptions, Snowy Owls occupy habitat and ecological niches not used by resident PNW owls — open beaches, dune flats, airport grasslands — creating temporary predation pressure on shorebirds, waterfowl, and small mammals that does not exist in non-irruption years [O1].
