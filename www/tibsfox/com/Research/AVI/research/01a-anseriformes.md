# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 1A: Resident Waterfowl (Anseriformes)

> **Module 1A deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document profiles 35 waterfowl species (Order Anseriformes, Family Anatidae) that breed in, reside year-round in, or winter significantly in the Pacific Northwest. Anseriformes are the oldest lineage of modern birds with a continuous PNW fossil record, and they dominate the region's wetland, estuarine, and marine habitats in both species count and biomass. Coverage spans swans, geese, dabbling ducks, diving ducks, sea ducks, mergansers, and stiff-tailed ducks. Extended treatments are given to species with exceptional ecological, cultural, or conservation significance in the PNW: Trumpeter Swan, Snow Goose, Wood Duck, and Harlequin Duck.
>
> **Design principle:** Waterfowl are the most visible avian presence on PNW waterways. This module treats each species as a node in the broader wetland ecosystem — connected to aquatic vegetation, invertebrate communities, predator guilds, and human land use. Morphometrics, habitat, diet, reproduction, population status, vocalizations, evolutionary context, and ecoregion presence are documented for every species following the schema in `00-taxonomy-schema.md`.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Indigenous references name specific nations. AOS Check-list is taxonomic authority.

## I. Swans (Tribe Cygnini)

---

### Trumpeter Swan (*Cygnus buccinator*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; recovered from near-extinction

#### Morphometrics

The Trumpeter Swan is the largest native waterfowl species in North America and the heaviest flying bird on the continent. Adults measure 138–165 cm in total length with a wingspan of 185–250 cm. Males average 11.8 kg (range 9.1–13.6 kg); females average 9.4 kg (range 7.3–10.9 kg). The entirely white plumage of adults develops by the second year; juveniles retain grayish-brown feathering through their first winter. The bill is entirely black, distinguishing the species from the Tundra Swan, which typically shows a yellow loral spot. The bill profile is a key field mark: the Trumpeter's culmen is straight or slightly concave from the base, creating a heavier, more wedge-shaped head profile than the Tundra Swan's gently curved bill. The neck is proportionally longer than any other PNW waterfowl species, comprising nearly half the bird's total length when extended. Feet are black. No seasonal plumage variation occurs in adults, though breast and head feathering may become stained rust-orange from iron-rich sediments in breeding ponds [O1, P16].

Sexual dimorphism is limited to size: males average 20–25% heavier than females. Both sexes share identical plumage at all ages.

#### Habitat

The Trumpeter Swan's PNW range has expanded dramatically since the species' near-extinction in the early 20th century. Historically, Trumpeters bred across western North America from the Great Lakes to the Pacific, but by 1932 the known breeding population had been reduced to 69 birds in the Yellowstone region, with a then-unknown population persisting in Alaska. Recovery efforts — among the earliest successful large-scale waterfowl restoration programs in North America — have restored breeding populations to several PNW locations, though the species remains far below its historical abundance in the region.

**Breeding habitat:** Shallow freshwater marshes, beaver ponds, lake margins, and slow river oxbows with emergent vegetation (cattail, bulrush, sedge) for nest concealment and abundant submerged aquatic vegetation for foraging. Breeding pairs require large territories (typically 40–150 hectares of wetland) and show extreme nest-site fidelity, returning to the same marsh or pond for decades. PNW breeding sites include the Skagit River floodplain (Washington), the Turnbull NWR complex (eastern Washington), Malheur NWR (Oregon), and scattered sites in the Idaho panhandle and southern British Columbia.

**Wintering habitat:** The Pacific Northwest is the primary wintering ground for the Pacific population of Trumpeter Swans, one of three recognized populations in North America (Pacific, Rocky Mountain, Interior). The Skagit Valley in northwestern Washington hosts the largest single wintering concentration in the contiguous United States: 8,000–12,000 birds gather annually in the Skagit and Samish river deltas and surrounding agricultural fields from November through March. Wintering birds use a mosaic of habitats: estuarine mudflats at low tide, flooded agricultural fields (especially harvested potato and corn fields where waste grain remains), and freshwater sloughs and ponds. The Skagit concentration is internationally significant, representing roughly 15–20% of the entire Pacific population of approximately 63,000 birds [G1, G3].

Additional important PNW wintering sites include the Ridgefield NWR and lower Columbia River floodplain (Washington/Oregon border), Sauvie Island (lower Willamette River, Oregon), the Fraser River delta (British Columbia), and Comox Valley (Vancouver Island). Eastern Washington populations winter on open water in the Spokane River system and at Turnbull NWR when ice conditions permit [G9, G14].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 3,500 ft (breeding marshes in eastern Washington and Idaho); wintering concentrations are almost exclusively below 500 ft in western lowlands.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily herbivorous. Adults consume submerged aquatic vegetation (pondweed *Potamogeton* spp., wild celery *Vallisneria americana*, water milfoil *Myriophyllum* spp.) by tipping or submerging the head and neck — the extended neck allows Trumpeters to reach vegetation at depths inaccessible to smaller waterfowl, giving them a competitive foraging advantage in deeper wetlands. During winter in the Skagit Valley, diet shifts substantially to agricultural waste: leftover potatoes, corn, and grain gleaned from harvested fields. This agricultural foraging is a relatively recent behavioral adaptation (documented since the 1970s as wintering populations grew beyond the carrying capacity of natural wetland forage) and has become ecologically important — without access to agricultural fields, the Skagit wintering population would likely exceed the natural food supply of the delta's wetlands [G3, O1].

Cygnets supplement their plant diet with aquatic invertebrates (insects, small crustaceans, snails) during their first weeks of life, transitioning to a fully herbivorous diet by approximately 8 weeks.

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Massive platform mound of aquatic vegetation (cattail, bulrush, sedge, and mud), typically 1.5–3.5 meters in diameter and 0.5–1.0 meters above water level. Nest mounds are built up and reused across years, growing in size annually. The largest documented nests in the PNW approach 4 meters across after decades of reuse.

**Nest location:** Emergent vegetation in shallow water, on muskrat or beaver lodges, or on small islands in breeding marshes. Islands are preferred for predator avoidance.

**Clutch size:** 4–6 eggs (range 3–9), laid at 2-day intervals. Eggs are creamy white, unmarked, and exceptionally large (average 113 x 73 mm, approximately 320 g).

**Incubation:** 33–37 days, by the female alone. The male guards the nest territory aggressively; adult Trumpeters are capable of injuring or killing predators (coyotes, raccoons, foxes) with powerful wing strikes. Territorial defense is fierce — pairs have been documented attacking canoes, kayaks, and researchers who approach nest sites.

**Fledging:** Cygnets fledge at 100–120 days, one of the longest fledging periods of any North American bird. The compressed PNW breeding season (eggs laid April–May, fledging September–October) means that late-nesting pairs risk cygnets being unable to fly before the onset of winter weather.

**Broods per year:** 1. Renesting after nest loss is rare due to the long incubation and fledging period.

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation late March–early May; hatching May–June; fledging September–October.

**Family bonds:** Trumpeter Swans are monogamous with lifelong pair bonds (documented pairs remaining together for 20+ years). Both parents attend cygnets through the first winter; family groups migrate and winter together, with young birds separating from parents the following spring. This extended parental care is one of the longest in any waterfowl species [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Increasing. The Pacific population has grown from approximately 3,700 birds in 1968 to an estimated 63,000 birds in 2020, a recovery rate among the most successful for any large-bodied North American bird [G1, G3].

**Estimate:** Pacific population approximately 63,000 (2020 USFWS Pacific Flyway survey). Rocky Mountain population approximately 11,000. Total North American population approximately 80,000 [G3].

**Threats:** Collision with power lines (the leading cause of adult mortality in PNW wintering areas), lead poisoning from ingestion of spent lead shot and fishing sinkers in wetland sediments, habitat loss from wetland drainage and development, disturbance at breeding sites, and climate-driven changes to wetland hydrology. Competition with non-native Mute Swans (*Cygnus olor*), established in some PNW waterways, is a growing concern for nest-site access [G1, G9].

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway Trumpeter Swan Survey, 2020; WDFW Trumpeter Swan Winter Survey.

#### Vocalizations

The species' common name derives from its call: a deep, resonant, horn-like *ko-HOH* or *oh-OH* that carries over a kilometer across open wetlands. The call is lower-pitched, louder, and more sonorous than the Tundra Swan's higher, more musical yelping. Trumpeters vocalize during pair bonding (coordinated duet calling with necks raised and wings partially lifted), territorial defense, and flight. The trachea of the Trumpeter Swan is uniquely elongated, coiling within the sternum much like a French horn's tubing — this anatomical specialization produces the species' distinctive resonance and is more developed in the Trumpeter than in any other swan species. Flight calls are given by both sexes and serve as contact calls within family groups during migration [O1, P16].

#### Evolutionary Note

Swans (Tribe Cygnini) represent one of the oldest lineages within Anatidae, diverging from geese approximately 20–23 million years ago during the early Miocene. The Trumpeter Swan is the most derived member of the subgenus *Cygnus*, more closely related to the Eurasian Whooper Swan (*C. cygnus*) than to the Tundra Swan (*C. columbianus*), despite the Tundra Swan's geographic proximity. The elongated tracheal loop — the acoustic apparatus responsible for the species' resonant call — has evolved independently in several swan lineages (Trumpeter, Whooper, and the extinct New Zealand Swan) but is most extreme in the Trumpeter, suggesting strong sexual selection for low-frequency vocal displays in open wetland habitats where low sounds propagate most efficiently. Fossil *Cygnus* specimens from the Pliocene of Oregon (approximately 3–5 MYA) indicate that large swans have inhabited PNW wetlands for millions of years, predating the modern Cascade volcanism that shaped the region's current hydrology [O1, O3].

### Tundra Swan (*Cygnus columbianus*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Smaller than the Trumpeter Swan but still among the largest birds in the PNW. Adults measure 120–150 cm in length with a wingspan of 168–211 cm. Males average 7.1 kg (range 5.5–9.6 kg); females average 6.2 kg (range 4.3–7.7 kg). Adult plumage entirely white; juveniles dusky gray-brown in first winter. The key field mark distinguishing Tundra from Trumpeter Swans is the yellow loral spot at the base of the upper mandible, present in approximately 90% of adults (variably sized; absent in some individuals, creating identification challenges). The bill profile is gently concave, giving a rounder, less wedge-shaped head than the Trumpeter. Overall proportions appear more "goose-like" — shorter neck, more compact body, slightly smaller bill relative to head size [P16, O1].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Arctic tundra of Alaska and northern Canada — does not breed in the PNW. Breeds on shallow tundra ponds and lakeshores above treeline.

**Wintering:** Arrives in the PNW in late October through November, departing by mid-March. Significant wintering concentrations occur in the lower Columbia River floodplain, Ridgefield NWR (Washington), Sauvie Island (Oregon), the Klamath Basin (Oregon/California border), and the Fraser River delta (British Columbia). Uses flooded agricultural fields, shallow freshwater marshes, and estuarine wetlands. Often found in mixed flocks with Trumpeter Swans, requiring careful identification.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 500 ft (wintering). Essentially a lowland wintering species in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Herbivorous. Wintering diet consists of submerged aquatic vegetation (pondweed, water celery), waste grain and crops from harvested agricultural fields, and tubers dug from wetland substrates. Foraging style is similar to Trumpeter Swan — tipping and head-submersion in shallow water — but the shorter neck limits foraging depth to approximately 60–80 cm, placing the Tundra Swan at a competitive disadvantage where both species coexist. On agricultural fields, forages by walking and gleaning waste grain, potatoes, and root vegetables [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Arctic breeding biology: nest is a mound of tundra vegetation on elevated ground near water; clutch 3–5 eggs; incubation 30–32 days by female; fledging 65–75 days. The shorter fledging period compared to the Trumpeter Swan reflects the extreme time pressure of the Arctic breeding season — cygnets must fly before freeze-up in September [O1, G3].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to slightly increasing. The Western Population (Pacific Flyway) has fluctuated between 80,000 and 120,000 birds over recent decades.

**Estimate:** Western Population approximately 105,000 (2019 USFWS midwinter survey). Eastern Population approximately 100,000. Total North American population approximately 200,000 [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Lead poisoning from ingestion of spent shot in wetland sediments, habitat loss at key wintering sites, climate change affecting Arctic breeding habitat (earlier snowmelt benefiting nest success short-term but altering tundra vegetation communities long-term), collision with power lines.

**Source:** USFWS Midwinter Waterfowl Survey; Pacific Flyway Council.

#### Vocalizations

A high-pitched, melodious barking: *woo-HOO, woo-HOO* or *hoo-hoo-hoo*, distinctly higher and more musical than the Trumpeter's deep horn-like call. Flock calling in flight is a diagnostic identification cue — a distant flock of Tundra Swans produces a higher, more yapping chorus compared to the lower, more sonorous calls of Trumpeters. The trachea is not elongated or coiled within the sternum as in the Trumpeter, explaining the higher pitch [O1, P16].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Tundra Swan (*C. columbianus*) and the Bewick's Swan of Eurasia (*C. bewickii*) are now generally treated as conspecific by most authorities outside North America, though AOS maintains specific status. Genetic divergence between the two forms is minimal (mtDNA divergence <1%), suggesting separation during the last glacial period approximately 20,000–50,000 years ago. The Tundra Swan's breeding range is entirely Arctic, unlike the Trumpeter's more temperate historical range — this ecological divergence despite close phylogenetic relationship suggests niche partitioning occurred during Pleistocene range fragmentation, with Trumpeters occupying temperate wetlands and Tundra Swans specializing on Arctic tundra breeding habitat [O1, O3].

### Snow Goose (*Anser caerulescens*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; overabundant (Arctic habitat degradation concern)

#### Morphometrics

A medium-large goose measuring 64–79 cm in length with a wingspan of 133–156 cm. Two distinct color morphs occur: the white morph (white body with black primaries) and the blue morph (dark gray-blue body with white head and upper neck). Both morphs occur in PNW wintering flocks, though white morphs predominate in the Pacific Flyway (>95% of birds). Males average 2.8 kg; females average 2.5 kg. The pink bill with a distinctive black "grinning patch" (the exposed lamellae visible as a dark line along the bill's cutting edge) is diagnostic in both morphs. Legs and feet are pink. Juveniles of the white morph are pale gray above with dark bill; blue morph juveniles are uniformly dark gray-brown. The two morphs are genetic variants controlled by a single gene locus with incomplete dominance — they interbreed freely and are not subspecifically distinct [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds colonially on Arctic tundra (Wrangel Island, Banks Island, western Arctic Canada, and Alaska's North Slope) in vast colonies numbering tens of thousands of pairs.

**Wintering:** The PNW hosts one of the most spectacular waterfowl concentrations on the Pacific Flyway. The Skagit River delta and Fir Island area in northwestern Washington support wintering flocks of 30,000–70,000 Snow Geese, with peak numbers typically in late November through February. The birds arrive from breeding grounds on Wrangel Island (Russia) — one of the longest migration routes of any PNW-wintering waterfowl, spanning approximately 4,800 km across the Bering Sea and down the Pacific coast. Additional significant PNW wintering concentrations occur in the Fraser River delta (British Columbia; 40,000–80,000 birds), the Ridgefield NWR / lower Columbia River (5,000–15,000), and the Klamath Basin (variable, 5,000–20,000). The combined Skagit-Fraser population represents the bulk of the Wrangel Island breeding colony's wintering distribution [G1, G3, G9].

The Skagit Snow Goose spectacle is a defining wildlife event of the PNW winter. Flocks numbering in the tens of thousands lift simultaneously from agricultural fields in response to Bald Eagle passes — the collective wingbeats create an audible roar, and the swirling white mass against overcast skies or the North Cascades backdrop is among the most dramatic avian displays in western North America. Local communities have built cultural identity around this annual event: the Skagit Valley hosts a Snow Goose and Art Festival, and the birds are an economic draw for ecotourism.

**Habitat use:** Wintering Snow Geese in the PNW depend overwhelmingly on agricultural fields — harvested potato, corn, winter wheat, pasture, and cover crop fields provide the caloric base. Natural salt marsh and estuarine habitats are used for roosting (especially nocturnal roosting on tidal flats) and grit ingestion, but the birds spend the majority of daylight hours on farmland. This agricultural dependency means that Snow Goose distribution in the PNW is determined as much by farming practices as by natural habitat [G3, O2].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 200 ft. Exclusively a lowland wintering species.

#### Diet and Foraging

Herbivorous. Wintering diet in the PNW consists primarily of agricultural crops: waste potatoes, sprouting winter wheat, pasture grasses, and cover crops. On salt marsh, feeds on three-square bulrush (*Schoenoplectus americanus*), saltgrass (*Distichlis spicata*), and sea arrow-grass (*Triglochin maritima*). Foraging is social: flocks move across fields in a rolling front, with birds at the rear flying to the front of the advancing line — a behavior called "rolling feeding" that ensures no individual is consistently at the trailing edge where food has been depleted [O1].

On Arctic breeding grounds, the species' intensive grubbing of root systems and rhizomes has caused widespread degradation of salt marsh and tundra vegetation. The "eat-out" phenomenon — where overabundant goose populations destroy vegetation faster than it can regenerate, creating barren mudflats — has been documented extensively at colonies in Hudson Bay and is a growing concern at Wrangel Island. This Arctic habitat degradation is the paradoxical conservation challenge of the Snow Goose: the species is superabundant, and its overabundance degrades habitat for itself and for dozens of other Arctic-breeding species [G1, G3].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Arctic breeding biology: colonial nester on tundra, clutch 3–5 eggs, incubation 22–25 days by female, fledging approximately 42–50 days. Snow Geese show extreme natal philopatry — females return to breed within meters of their own natal colony, and mate choice is strongly influenced by parental morph (white morph birds preferentially pair with white morph mates, and vice versa), a phenomenon termed sexual imprinting that maintains the morph polymorphism within populations [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Increasing / overabundant. The mid-continent and Pacific populations have grown explosively since the 1970s, driven by agricultural food subsidies on wintering grounds that have dramatically increased winter survival.

**Estimate:** Total North American population approximately 15,000,000 (2018 USFWS estimate — up from approximately 2,000,000 in the early 1970s). The Wrangel Island breeding population (source of most PNW wintering birds) is approximately 200,000–400,000, substantially smaller than the mid-continent populations but still at levels that cause concern for Arctic breeding habitat condition [G1, G3].

**Threats:** The conservation paradigm for Snow Geese is inverted: the species itself is not threatened, but its overabundance threatens Arctic breeding habitat. In response, USFWS has implemented a Conservation Order (special hunting season) to reduce mid-continent populations. PNW wintering populations have been more stable than mid-continent populations but are increasing. Local agricultural conflicts occur when large flocks damage winter wheat and cover crops, though most Skagit Valley farmers view geese as compatible with post-harvest field use [G1, G3].

**Source:** USFWS Midwinter Waterfowl Survey; Pacific Flyway Council; WDFW Snow Goose Monitoring Program.

#### Vocalizations

Flocks produce a continuous, high-pitched cacophony: a nasal *luk-luk* or *wuk-wuk* that, when produced by thousands of birds simultaneously, creates a characteristic wall of sound audible from over a kilometer. Individual calls are higher-pitched and more nasal than those of Canada Geese. Flight calls are given continuously during take-off and landing, making flock movements extremely noisy events [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The genus *Anser* (gray geese and white geese) diverged from the *Branta* lineage (black geese) approximately 10–12 million years ago. The Snow Goose's blue and white morph polymorphism is one of the best-studied examples of single-locus plumage variation in birds, controlled by the *MC1R* gene (melanocortin-1 receptor) — the same gene responsible for melanistic variation in numerous vertebrate species from jaguars to rock pocket mice. The blue morph is dominant over white; heterozygous birds (one blue, one white allele) are phenotypically blue but typically show more white belly feathering than homozygous blue birds. The morph ratio varies clinally across the species' range: blue morphs predominate in eastern Arctic colonies (80–90% at Baffin Island) but are rare in western colonies including Wrangel Island (<5%), explaining the near-universal whiteness of PNW wintering flocks. This geographic morph gradient is maintained by the strong natal philopatry and assortative mating described above [O1, O3].

### Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding / Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The most familiar goose in the PNW, highly variable in size due to multiple subspecies occurring in the region. Total length 76–110 cm; wingspan 127–185 cm. Mass varies enormously by subspecies: the large *B. c. moffitti* (Great Basin Canada Goose, the primary PNW resident subspecies) averages 4.5–5.5 kg, while the smaller *B. c. parvipes* (Lesser Canada Goose) averages 2.5–3.5 kg. All subspecies share the diagnostic black head and neck with white chinstrap, brown body, pale breast, black tail, and white undertail coverts. Bill, legs, and feet are black. Sexual dimorphism limited to size (males 5–15% larger). Breast color varies from pale tan (*moffitti*) to darker brown (smaller subspecies), and overall body tone ranges from pale to dark across the subspecies complex [O1, P16].

The PNW hosts at least four subspecies of Canada Goose as residents or regular winter visitors: *B. c. moffitti* (resident, breeding), *B. c. occidentalis* (Dusky Canada Goose — breeds Copper River Delta, Alaska; winters Willamette Valley), *B. c. fulva* (Vancouver Canada Goose — coastal BC/SE Alaska), and *B. c. parvipes* (wintering). The recently split Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*) was formerly treated as a small subspecies of Canada Goose (see next account).

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** The resident *moffitti* subspecies breeds throughout the PNW lowlands and east-side steppe, using almost any water body with adjacent grassy areas: urban parks, golf courses, reservoirs, farm ponds, river islands, marshes, and lakeshores. This subspecies has adapted extensively to human-modified landscapes and is one of the most successful urban colonizers among PNW birds. Breeding densities are highest in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, the Columbia Basin (irrigated agricultural areas), and the Klamath Basin.

**Wintering:** Resident populations are largely non-migratory, remaining in the PNW year-round. Migratory populations from Alaska and interior Canada augment PNW winter numbers substantially. The Dusky Canada Goose (*B. c. occidentalis*) is of particular conservation interest: this subspecies breeds almost exclusively on the Copper River Delta in Alaska and winters almost exclusively in the Willamette Valley — one of the most geographically restricted migration routes of any goose population. WDFW and ODFW manage Dusky Canada Goose wintering habitat at Ridgefield NWR and Baskett Slough NWR specifically [G1, G9, G12].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding in eastern Oregon high desert lakes, Klamath Basin). Wintering primarily below 1,000 ft.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily herbivorous. Grazes on grasses, agricultural crops (winter wheat, ryegrass, clover), lawn grass in urban settings, aquatic vegetation, and grain. Grazing style is goose-typical: walking slowly through grass or crop fields, clipping vegetation with the serrated bill. Agricultural foraging creates local conflicts — large resident flocks can damage sprouting grain fields and pastures. In urban settings, lawns, parks, and golf courses provide year-round grazing substrate, which partly explains the species' urban success. Dabbles in shallow water for aquatic vegetation; occasionally eats invertebrates and small fish opportunistically [O1, O2].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest, a shallow scrape lined with down and vegetation.

**Nest location:** Near water, often on islands, dike tops, elevated ground, or artificial platforms. Urban birds nest on rooftops, planters, median strips, and other human structures. Islands provide predator protection and are strongly preferred where available.

**Clutch size:** 4–7 eggs (range 2–12).

**Incubation:** 25–28 days, by female. Male guards territory.

**Fledging:** 42–56 days.

**Broods per year:** 1.

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation March–April; hatching April–May; fledging June–July.

#### Population

**Trend:** Increasing. Resident populations have expanded dramatically since the mid-20th century restoration efforts and adaptation to urban/suburban landscapes.

**Estimate:** Total Pacific Flyway population approximately 3,500,000 (all subspecies combined; 2020 USFWS). The PNW resident *moffitti* population numbers several hundred thousand. The Dusky Canada Goose (*occidentalis*) is far less abundant: approximately 10,000–15,000 total population, managed as a species of concern [G1, G3, G12].

**Threats:** The conservation picture is bifurcated. Resident *moffitti* populations are overabundant in many areas, causing agricultural damage, eutrophication of urban waterways, and airport strike hazards. The Dusky Canada Goose, by contrast, faces genuine conservation concern: its tiny breeding range (Copper River Delta) is vulnerable to earthquake-driven habitat change (the 1964 Alaska earthquake uplifted the delta, altering goose nesting habitat), predation from increasing Bald Eagle populations, and habitat degradation on its Willamette Valley wintering grounds [G1, G9, G12].

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway surveys; WDFW; ODFW.

#### Vocalizations

The familiar *ha-HONK* or *a-HONK* is the characteristic sound of PNW lowland waterways and urban parks. Males give a lower, two-syllable honk; females give a higher-pitched, more rapid *hink* call. Family groups maintain contact with continuous low conversational calls. V-formation flight flocks produce a continuous honking chorus. The Dusky subspecies gives a notably lower-pitched, slower honk than resident *moffitti* birds [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Canada Goose complex underwent a major taxonomic revision in 2004 when AOS split the smaller-bodied forms into the Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*), recognizing deep genetic divergence within what had been treated as a single species. The split was supported by molecular evidence showing that *hutchinsii* and *canadensis* are not sister taxa — the Cackling Goose is more closely related to the Barnacle Goose (*B. leucopsis*) than to the Canada Goose, indicating convergent evolution of similar body plans in Arctic (Cackling) and temperate (Canada) breeding environments. The Canada Goose subspecies complex itself exhibits a remarkable size cline: body mass varies by a factor of three from the largest (*maxima*, Giant Canada Goose) to the smallest remaining *canadensis* subspecies, one of the widest intraspecific size ranges in any bird [O1, O3, P20].

### Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Transient | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Formerly treated as small subspecies of Canada Goose; split as a full species by AOS in 2004. Noticeably smaller than most Canada Goose subspecies. Length 56–64 cm; wingspan 110–119 cm; mass 1.1–1.8 kg (females) to 1.4–2.3 kg (males). The PNW-wintering form is primarily the Ridgway's Cackling Goose (*B. h. minima*), the smallest of the complex. Plumage pattern identical to Canada Goose — black head and neck, white chinstrap, brown body — but the shorter neck, stubbier bill, rounder head, and overall compact proportions create a distinctly different gestalt. The bill is notably short and triangular. Some individuals show a thin white neck ring at the base of the black stocking. Separation from the smallest Canada Goose subspecies in mixed wintering flocks remains one of the most challenging field identification problems in PNW waterfowl birding [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Arctic tundra of western Alaska (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta is the primary breeding area for *minima*). Does not breed in the PNW.

**Wintering:** Winters primarily in the Willamette Valley of Oregon and the lower Columbia River, with smaller numbers in the Skagit Valley (Washington) and southern BC. Uses harvested agricultural fields (grass seed, grain), wet pasture, and marsh edges. Often found in mixed flocks with Canada Geese, requiring careful size comparison for identification. Peak numbers in the Willamette Valley reach 200,000–300,000 birds in December–January [G1, G3, G12].

#### Diet and Foraging

Herbivorous. Grazes on agricultural grasses, winter wheat, and waste grain. Foraging ecology similar to Canada Goose but concentrated on grass seed fields in the Willamette Valley, which provide the caloric backbone of the wintering population [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Arctic breeding biology similar to Canada Goose but adapted to shorter Arctic season: clutch 4–6 eggs, incubation 24–27 days, fledging 40–45 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing after declines in the 1980s. The *minima* population declined to approximately 25,000 in the 1980s due to poor breeding success on the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta (fox predation, subsistence harvest pressure). Recovery following predator management and harvest restrictions has brought the population to approximately 250,000 [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Concentrated wintering range in the Willamette Valley makes the population vulnerable to habitat change in a single agricultural region. Continued conversion of grass seed fields to other crops or development could reduce carrying capacity.

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway population estimates; ODFW.

#### Vocalizations

Higher-pitched than Canada Goose: a rapid, squeaky *lik-lik* or *yip-yip* that in large flocks creates a higher, more staccato chorus compared to the lower honking of Canada Geese. The voice difference is often the first cue alerting observers to the presence of Cackling Geese within a Canada Goose flock [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The split of Cackling Goose from Canada Goose (AOS 2004) was one of the most significant recent taxonomic changes in North American waterfowl. Molecular phylogenetics revealed that the small-bodied Arctic forms (*minima*, *leucopareia*, *hutchinsii*, *taverneri*) form a clade more closely related to Barnacle Goose (*B. leucopsis*) and Hawaiian Goose (*B. sandvicensis*) than to the large-bodied temperate forms of Canada Goose. The implication is that the similar plumage pattern shared by Canada and Cackling Geese — black head, white chinstrap — is ancestral to the *Branta* genus rather than evidence of a single species. Convergent body-size evolution in similar habitats had obscured the phylogenetic signal for over a century [O1, O3, P20].

### Greater White-fronted Goose (*Anser albifrons*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Transient | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium goose, 64–81 cm in length, wingspan 130–165 cm, mass 1.9–3.3 kg. Adult plumage is distinctive: brownish-gray body with irregular black barring on the belly (the "specklebelly" of hunter parlance), white patch around the base of the bill (the "white front"), pink to orange bill, and orange legs and feet. Juveniles lack the white face patch and belly barring, appearing plain brown with a pale bill — juvenile White-fronts are among the most nondescript geese and are frequently misidentified. No sexual dimorphism beyond slight size difference (males ~10% larger) [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Occurs throughout the PNW as a winter visitor and passage migrant, with concentrations in the Klamath Basin, Willamette Valley, lower Columbia, Skagit Valley, and Fraser River delta. Uses agricultural fields, wet meadows, and marsh edges. Often found in mixed flocks with Snow Geese and Canada Geese. Less abundant than Snow or Canada Geese in most PNW locations but a regular component of winter goose flocks. The Klamath Basin hosts the largest PNW concentrations (20,000–50,000 birds in November) [G1, G3, G12].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft (Klamath Basin).

#### Diet and Foraging

Herbivorous. Grazes on grasses, sprouting grain, agricultural crops, and marsh vegetation. Foraging behavior similar to other geese; walks through fields clipping vegetation. Shows stronger preference for natural wet meadow habitat than Snow Geese, which favor agricultural fields more heavily [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds on Arctic tundra across Alaska and northern Canada.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Pacific Flyway population approximately 600,000. Total North American population approximately 3,200,000 [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Minimal immediate threats; population robust. Climate change effects on Arctic breeding habitat are a long-term concern.

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway surveys.

#### Vocalizations

A distinctive laughing *kow-kow-kow* or *yow-yow-yow*, higher-pitched and more musical than Canada Goose honking. The call pattern has been compared to laughter, giving rise to the colloquial name "laughing goose." Flocks in flight produce a gabbling chorus that is readily distinguishable from other goose species at distance [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

*Anser albifrons* is a Holarctic species with populations breeding across the Arctic from Greenland to Siberia. The PNW-wintering birds originate primarily from Alaskan breeding populations. Molecular analysis reveals deep population structure corresponding to discrete flyway populations — Pacific, Central, Mississippi, and Atlantic — with limited gene flow between flyways, suggesting that flyway fidelity functions as a soft reproductive barrier within the species. The Pacific Flyway population (subspecies *frontalis*) shows distinct allele frequencies from other populations despite the lack of physical breeding range barriers [O1, O3].

### Brant (*Branta bernicla*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Transient | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A small, compact, dark goose — the most marine-adapted of all PNW geese. Length 56–66 cm; wingspan 106–121 cm; mass 1.0–1.8 kg. The PNW-wintering form is primarily the Black Brant (*B. b. nigricans*), distinguished from the Atlantic Brant (*B. b. hrota*) by its darker belly (nearly black, extending from breast to undertail, with only minimal white flanking). The head, neck, and breast are black, with a small white necklace on the upper neck. The back and wings are dark brownish-gray. Bill, legs, and feet are black. The overall impression is of a small, uniformly dark goose. Juveniles are similar but lack the white necklace and show pale wing-covert edging [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Brant is the most maritime goose in the PNW, rarely found far from saltwater. Wintering concentrations occur in the bays and estuaries of the outer coast and Salish Sea: Padilla Bay and adjacent Skagit County embayments (Washington's most important Brant wintering area, hosting 5,000–15,000 birds), Willapa Bay, Grays Harbor, Boundary Bay (BC), and scattered sites in Puget Sound. Spring staging areas along the outer coast (particularly Baja California to BC) are critical for pre-breeding fat deposition.

The species is almost entirely dependent on eelgrass (*Zostera marina*) and sea lettuce (*Ulva* spp.) during winter — making it the only PNW goose whose winter distribution is determined primarily by a single marine plant genus. Where eelgrass beds are healthy and extensive, Brant thrive; where eelgrass has declined (as in parts of Puget Sound affected by shoreline development and water quality degradation), Brant numbers have dropped correspondingly [G1, G9].

**Elevation range:** Sea level exclusively.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily herbivorous, specializing on marine vegetation. Eelgrass (*Zostera marina*) is the overwhelmingly dominant food item, supplemented by sea lettuce (*Ulva* spp.) and, to a lesser extent, other marine algae. Brant feed by tipping in shallow tidal flats at low to moderate tide, pulling eelgrass blades from the substrate. At high tide, when eelgrass beds are submerged beyond reach, Brant roost on open water or shorelines. This tidal foraging cycle dictates daily activity patterns more rigidly than in any other PNW waterfowl species. On spring staging areas, Brant may supplement eelgrass with upland grasses, but this is a minor dietary component [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds on Arctic coastal tundra (primarily western Alaska and Arctic Canada). Clutch 3–5 eggs; incubation 22–26 days; fledging 40–50 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining to stable. The Pacific Flyway Black Brant population has declined from approximately 150,000 in the 1960s to approximately 110,000–130,000 in recent surveys. The decline is linked to eelgrass habitat loss across the wintering range [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Eelgrass decline from water quality degradation, coastal development, warming sea temperatures, and ocean acidification. Eelgrass beds in the Salish Sea have experienced localized die-offs associated with marine heat waves. Climate change effects on Arctic breeding habitat. Predation by increasing Bald Eagle populations at wintering sites (Brant are small-bodied and vulnerable to eagle predation). Disturbance from boat traffic and recreational use of tidal flats [G1, G9, G15].

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway Brant Survey; WDFW.

#### Vocalizations

A low, guttural, rolling *rr-rrONK* or *cr-r-r-unk*, distinctly different from the honking of other geese. The call has a growling quality that, in flocks, produces a continuous muttering. Flight calls are lower and less voluminous than those of larger geese [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Black Brant (*B. b. nigricans*) and Atlantic Brant (*B. b. hrota*) were formerly considered full species and are still sometimes treated as such. Intermediate "Gray-bellied Brant" occur where the two forms' breeding ranges meet in the central Canadian Arctic, and hybridization is documented. The Black Brant's extreme dependence on eelgrass has no parallel among other goose species and represents a marine foraging specialization unique within the *Branta* genus. The adaptation is relatively recent in evolutionary terms — eelgrass beds expanded dramatically in the North Pacific following post-Pleistocene sea-level stabilization approximately 6,000–8,000 years ago, suggesting that the Brant-eelgrass feeding relationship is a Holocene specialization [O1, O3].

### Mallard (*Anas platyrhynchos*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The most abundant and widely distributed duck in the PNW and North America. Length 50–65 cm; wingspan 82–95 cm; mass 0.7–1.6 kg (males heavier). The male in breeding plumage is unmistakable: iridescent green head, narrow white collar, rich chestnut breast, gray body, black rump with upturled central tail feathers, and a yellow-green bill. The speculum (secondary wing feathers) is iridescent blue bordered by white bars, visible in flight and at rest. Females are mottled brown with an orange-and-brown bill. Eclipses males (July–September) resemble females but retain the yellow-green bill. Domestic and feral Mallards in urban settings produce a wide range of variant plumages (white, piebald, blond) through selective breeding and hybridization with domestic stock [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

Essentially ubiquitous in the PNW wherever freshwater occurs. Breeds on ponds, lakes, marshes, slow rivers, irrigation ditches, urban parks, storm-water retention ponds, and seasonal wetlands. One of the few PNW ducks equally at home in urban and wild settings. Uses agricultural fields for grain gleaning in winter. The Mallard's habitat generalism is the foundation of its abundance — it exploits a wider range of wetland types and human-modified landscapes than any other PNW duck [O1, O2].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding at high-elevation lakes in eastern Oregon and Idaho).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Dabbles and tips in shallow water for seeds, aquatic vegetation, and invertebrates (insect larvae, snails, crustaceans). On land, gleans grain, weed seeds, and agricultural crops from fields. Diet shifts seasonally: more invertebrate-rich during breeding (protein for egg production and molt), more seed and grain-based in winter. Ducklings are almost entirely insectivorous in their first weeks, gradually shifting to a more plant-based diet [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest, a shallow bowl lined with down and vegetation.

**Nest location:** On the ground near water, often concealed in dense vegetation (tall grass, shrubs, cattails). Urban birds nest in flower boxes, raised planters, and rooftop gardens — sometimes at surprising distances from water.

**Clutch size:** 7–10 eggs (range 5–14).

**Incubation:** 26–28 days, by female alone.

**Fledging:** 50–60 days.

**Broods per year:** 1 (will renest if nest is destroyed early in the season).

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation March–May; hatching April–June; fledging June–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. The Mallard is the most abundant duck in North America and the baseline species against which all other waterfowl population trends are measured.

**Estimate:** North American population approximately 11,600,000 (2019 USFWS Waterfowl Status Report). PNW populations are stable; the species is the most commonly harvested waterfowl in Washington and Oregon [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Hybridization with domestic Mallards and feral stock degrades genetic integrity of wild populations (particularly concerning for Mexican Duck and American Black Duck populations outside the PNW, but also documented locally). Lead poisoning from spent shot in wetland sediments. Predation of nests by raccoons, skunks, crows, and other generalist predators. Habitat loss from wetland drainage — mitigated in the PNW by conservation easement programs and NWR system [G1].

**Source:** USFWS Waterfowl Status Report; WDFW.

#### Vocalizations

The female's loud *QUACK-quack-quack-quack* (descending cadence, the "decrescendo call") is the prototypical duck vocalization — it is the sound most people associate with "a duck." Males give a softer, nasal *raeb* or *rab* and a sharp, single-note whistle during courtship displays. The female's decrescendo call functions as a contact call, an alarm, and a nest-solicitation call. Male courtship displays include the "grunt-whistle" (head-throw with simultaneous vocalization), the "head-up-tail-up," and the "down-up" — a synchronized series of ritualized postures shared across the *Anas* genus and studied extensively as a model system for the evolution of courtship behavior [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Mallard occupies a pivotal position in waterfowl evolutionary biology. It is the wild ancestor of virtually all domestic duck breeds (except those derived from the Muscovy Duck), and its genome serves as the reference assembly for anatid genomics. The species hybridizes readily with at least 40 other duck species — one of the highest known interspecific hybridization rates in birds — raising ongoing questions about species boundaries within the *Anas* clade. In the PNW, Mallards hybridize regularly with American Black Ducks (rare vagrants), Northern Pintails, and feral domestic stock. The species' capacity for hybridization may reflect a recent evolutionary radiation: the *Anas* mallard group (Mallard, Black Duck, Mottled Duck, Mexican Duck, Hawaiian Duck) appears to have diversified within the last 500,000 years, and incomplete reproductive isolation among these forms suggests ongoing speciation [O1, O3].

### Northern Pintail (*Anas acuta*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; population declining

#### Morphometrics

An exceptionally elegant duck — long-necked, slender-bodied, and elongated by the male's namesake pintail feathers. Males measure 59–76 cm (including tail streamers up to 10 cm); females 51–64 cm. Wingspan 80–95 cm. Mass 0.7–1.3 kg. Breeding males are unmistakable: chocolate-brown head, white breast extending in a thin stripe up the side of the neck, gray body with fine vermiculations, black rump, and long central tail feathers extending well beyond the tail. Females are warm buff-brown with a plain face, slender neck, and pointed (but not elongated) tail. The overall proportions — long neck, small head, slim body — create an aerodynamic silhouette that is distinctive in flight and at rest [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Breeds in open wetland habitats, particularly shallow marshes, prairie potholes, and wet meadows with low vegetation. PNW breeding is concentrated in eastern Oregon and Washington (Malheur NWR, Columbia Basin, Klamath Basin) and is uncommon west of the Cascades. Prefers more open, less vegetated wetlands than the Mallard — avoids dense cattail marshes.

**Wintering:** Common throughout PNW lowlands. Uses flooded agricultural fields, estuaries, tidal mudflats, and shallow freshwater marshes. The Klamath Basin, Willamette Valley, lower Columbia, and Skagit Valley host significant wintering concentrations [G1, G3].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft (breeding at Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous dabbler. Tips and dabbles in shallow water for seeds, waste grain, aquatic invertebrates, and aquatic vegetation. The long neck allows deeper dabbling reach than most other dabbling ducks (exceeded only by swans). In winter, heavily dependent on flooded agricultural fields for waste grain. During breeding, females increase invertebrate consumption for protein demands of egg production [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest in open or sparsely vegetated terrain.

**Clutch size:** 6–9 eggs.

**Incubation:** 22–24 days, by female.

**Fledging:** 46–47 days.

**Breeding season in PNW:** April–July.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. The Northern Pintail has experienced one of the most sustained population declines of any North American dabbling duck. The continental population has declined from approximately 10,000,000 in the 1950s to approximately 2,600,000–3,500,000 in recent surveys — a decline of 60–75% despite liberal hunting regulations adjustments and habitat conservation efforts. The cause is not fully understood but is attributed to a combination of agricultural intensification on breeding grounds (early cultivation destroying nests in the Great Plains), drought cycles, and habitat loss in key wintering areas [G1, G3].

**Estimate:** Continental population approximately 2,900,000 (2019 USFWS). Pacific Flyway approximately 900,000.

**Threats:** Agricultural nest destruction, drought, wetland loss on breeding and wintering grounds. The pintail's preference for open, sparsely vegetated nesting habitat makes it disproportionately vulnerable to agricultural machinery — nests placed in crop stubble and low vegetation are destroyed at high rates by spring tillage [G1, G4].

**Source:** USFWS Waterfowl Status Report; USGS trend data.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a mellow, whistled *prrrp* during courtship. Females give a soft, raspy quack similar to but softer than a Mallard female. Overall, the Pintail is a quieter duck than the Mallard — less prone to loud vocalization, reflecting its preference for open habitats where visual display (the white neck-stripe, the long tail) may carry more communicative weight than sound [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Northern Pintail has the widest breeding range of any duck species on Earth, spanning the entire Northern Hemisphere from North America through Europe and Asia. Despite this enormous range, the species shows remarkably low genetic differentiation across its Holarctic distribution — mtDNA divergence between North American and Eurasian populations is <0.5%, suggesting either very recent range expansion or ongoing gene flow across the Bering Strait. Fossil pintails from the Pleistocene of Oregon confirm the species' long presence in the PNW. The elongated central tail feathers of breeding males represent a classic case of Fisherian runaway sexual selection — the streamers provide no aerodynamic advantage and may slightly impair flight performance, but female preference for longer tails has driven their elaboration [O1, O3].

### American Wigeon (*Mareca americana*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A compact, short-billed dabbling duck. Length 45–58 cm; wingspan 76–89 cm; mass 0.5–1.2 kg. Breeding males have a distinctive head pattern: white forehead and crown (the "baldpate"), iridescent green postocular stripe sweeping to the nape, and grayish face with fine black speckling. Breast and flanks are pinkish-brown; a white hip patch is conspicuous at rest and in flight. The speculum is green. Bill is pale blue-gray with a black tip. Females are warm brown with a grayish head and the same small blue-gray bill. The bill is notably smaller and stubbier than other dabbling ducks of similar size, adapted for grazing on short vegetation [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** One of the most abundant wintering ducks in the PNW. Concentrations occur in estuaries, coastal marshes, flooded agricultural fields, urban parks, and freshwater marshes throughout western Washington and Oregon. The Skagit, Nisqually, Willapa, and Columbia estuary systems host large wintering flocks. Frequently grazes on park lawns and golf courses — the only PNW dabbling duck that regularly forages on mowed grass like a goose.

**Breeding:** Breeds uncommonly in the PNW, primarily in eastern Washington and Oregon (Malheur NWR, Columbia Basin) and rarely west of the Cascades. Most PNW wintering birds breed in interior Alaska and western Canada.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,000 ft (breeding in eastern interior).

#### Diet and Foraging

More herbivorous than most dabbling ducks. Grazes on short grasses, clover, and agricultural crops on land — a behavior unusual among dabbling ducks but convergent with geese. In water, dabbles for aquatic vegetation, seeds, and (less frequently) invertebrates. Frequently kleptoparasitizes diving ducks and coots: wigeons wait for diving birds to surface with aquatic vegetation and then snatch food items from their bills. This piracy behavior is a widespread and well-documented foraging strategy — American Wigeons at Puget Sound sites routinely steal eelgrass and widgeon grass brought to the surface by diving American Coots and diving ducks [O1, O2].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest in dense grassland, often far from water.

**Clutch size:** 6–11 eggs.

**Incubation:** 23–25 days.

**Fledging:** 45–63 days.

**Breeding season in PNW:** May–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 2,500,000 (2019 USFWS). Common and widespread in the PNW during winter [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland loss, agricultural conversion of grassland breeding habitat.

**Source:** USFWS Waterfowl Status Report.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a distinctive three-note whistle: *whi-WHEE-oo*, a clear, carrying call that is one of the most recognizable winter waterbird sounds in PNW estuaries. Females give a low, growling *qua-awk*. The male whistle often alerts observers to wigeon presence before the birds are seen [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The American Wigeon was recently moved from *Anas* to the resurrected genus *Mareca* (along with Eurasian Wigeon, Chiloe Wigeon, and Gadwall) based on molecular phylogenetic analyses showing that wigeons and Gadwall form a clade distinct from the core *Anas* dabbling ducks. The American Wigeon's terrestrial grazing habit and short bill are convergent with goose-like foraging and represent a niche divergence from the aquatic dabbling specialization of most *Anas* ducks [O1, O3].

### Green-winged Teal (*Anas crecca*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The smallest dabbling duck in North America. Length 31–39 cm; wingspan 52–59 cm; mass 200–450 g. The diminutive size is immediately apparent in mixed flocks. Breeding males are ornate: chestnut head with a broad iridescent green postocular crescent bordered by thin cream lines, gray body with a horizontal white flank stripe, buff-yellow undertail coverts bordered by black, and a green speculum. Females are small and brown with a dark eye-line and green speculum. The compact body, short neck, and rapid wingbeats create a fast, darting flight style — Green-winged Teal are among the fastest-flying ducks in level flight, reaching speeds of 80 km/h [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common throughout PNW lowland wetlands, mudflats, and flooded agricultural fields. Uses shallow, muddy margins of ponds and marshes — often feeding in water only a few centimeters deep where larger ducks cannot access. The Klamath Basin, Willamette Valley, and Skagit lowlands host significant winter concentrations.

**Breeding:** Breeds in moderate numbers across eastern Washington and Oregon, the Idaho panhandle, and interior BC. Nests in dense ground cover near freshwater marshes, beaver ponds, and seasonal wetlands.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Dabbles in shallow water and wet mud for seeds, aquatic invertebrates, and plant material. The small bill is well-suited to gleaning seeds from muddy substrates — the species specializes on the shallow, muddy margins that larger ducks cannot efficiently exploit. During winter, heavily uses flooded agricultural fields and shallow estuary edges [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest concealed in dense vegetation.

**Clutch size:** 6–9 eggs.

**Incubation:** 20–23 days.

**Fledging:** 34–35 days — one of the shortest fledging periods among PNW ducks, consistent with the small body size.

**Breeding season in PNW:** May–July.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to slightly increasing. Continental population approximately 4,700,000 (2019 USFWS). The species' use of small, ephemeral wetlands — habitats not well-surveyed by standardized methods — may mean population estimates are conservative [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland drainage and loss of small, seasonal wetlands (vernal pools, beaver ponds). The species' dependence on small-scale wetland habitats makes it vulnerable to cumulative habitat loss that may not trigger regulatory protections applicable to larger wetland systems.

**Source:** USFWS Waterfowl Status Report.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a clear, high-pitched *kriick* or *crick-et* — a short, liquid whistle that carries well across marshes. Females give a faint, high quack. The male's call is a useful auditory cue for detecting the species in dense marsh vegetation [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The taxonomic relationship between the North American Green-winged Teal and the Eurasian Common Teal (*A. crecca crecca*) remains debated. AOS currently treats the North American form as a subspecies (*A. c. carolinensis*), but some authorities recognize it as a full species based on differences in male head plumage pattern (the American form has a vertical white breast stripe absent in Eurasian birds) and vocalizations. Molecular divergence is low, consistent with recent separation — likely during the last glacial maximum when Beringian ice separated Pacific and Atlantic waterfowl populations. Eurasian Common Teal vagrants occur rarely in the PNW and hybridize with American birds where they co-occur [O1, O3].

### Cinnamon Teal (*Spatula cyanoptera*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Length 38–43 cm; wingspan 56–64 cm; mass 280–500 g. The breeding male is striking: entirely deep cinnamon-red on head, neck, breast, flanks, and belly — unlike any other PNW duck. The eye is red. Wings show a large sky-blue upper wing patch (shared with Blue-winged Teal and Northern Shoveler). Females are virtually identical to female Blue-winged Teal: warm brown, mottled, with a plain face and blue wing patch visible only in flight. The similar appearance of females in these two species and the Northern Shoveler (all three in the genus *Spatula*) creates one of the more challenging female duck identification problems in the PNW [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Breeds throughout eastern Oregon and Washington, the Willamette Valley, and sporadically west of the Cascades. Prefers shallow freshwater marshes with dense emergent vegetation (cattail, bulrush, hardstem). The Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin, and Summer Lake WMA are core PNW breeding areas. Arrives late (April–May) and departs early (August–September), spending the briefest portion of the year in the PNW of any breeding dabbling duck.

**Wintering:** Winters primarily in Mexico and the southern US; rare in the PNW in winter. One of the few PNW breeding ducks that is essentially absent from the region in winter.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous dabbler. Feeds in shallow water on seeds, aquatic vegetation, and invertebrates (aquatic insects, snails, zooplankton). Foraging style is typical of small dabbling ducks: dabbling, tipping, and filtering water through the bill's lamellae [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest concealed in dense emergent vegetation.

**Clutch size:** 8–12 eggs.

**Incubation:** 21–25 days.

**Fledging:** 49 days.

**Breeding season in PNW:** May–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to slightly declining. Continental population not precisely known due to difficulty separating from Blue-winged Teal in aerial surveys. Estimated at approximately 300,000–500,000 [G1].

**Threats:** Wetland loss, drought affecting Great Basin breeding habitats, competition with non-native species in degraded wetlands.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a low, chattering *chuk-chuk-chuk*. Females give a soft quack similar to other small dabblers. The Cinnamon Teal is among the quieter PNW ducks [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Cinnamon Teal is one of only two duck species endemic to the Western Hemisphere (the other being the Blue-winged Teal). The genus *Spatula* was resurrected from synonymy with *Anas* based on molecular phylogenetics; it includes the teal-shoveler clade (Cinnamon Teal, Blue-winged Teal, Northern Shoveler, and their Australasian and Old World relatives). The Cinnamon Teal has a disjunct distribution — the North American subspecies (*S. c. cyanoptera*) is separated from four South American subspecies by 3,000+ km of Central American and tropical habitat where the species does not breed. This disjunction suggests either recent range contraction or long-distance dispersal [O1, O3].

### Blue-winged Teal (*Spatula discors*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Breeding (uncommon) | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Length 37–41 cm; wingspan 56–62 cm; mass 270–410 g. Breeding males have a distinctive slate-gray head with a bold white crescent between the eye and bill. Body is warm brown with dark spotting; flanks show a white hip patch. The large sky-blue upper wing patch is shared with Cinnamon Teal and Northern Shoveler. Females are nearly identical to female Cinnamon Teal (warm brown, mottled, plain-faced) — reliable separation requires close examination of bill size (slightly smaller in Blue-winged), face pattern (slightly more contrasting supercilium), and overall coloring (slightly cooler brown tones) [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Uncommon breeder in the PNW, primarily in eastern Washington and Oregon marshes. Much more common as a breeder in the prairie pothole region of the northern Great Plains. Prefers shallow freshwater marshes with a mix of open water and emergent vegetation.

**Wintering:** Winters in the tropics — Central America, northern South America, and the Caribbean. Among the longest-distance migrants in North American waterfowl. Essentially absent from the PNW October–March.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,000 ft (breeding).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds on seeds, aquatic vegetation, and invertebrates in shallow water. Foraging behavior similar to Cinnamon Teal [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 8–11 eggs. **Incubation:** 23–27 days. **Fledging:** 38–49 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 6,400,000 — the species is much more abundant east of the Rockies. PNW breeding population is small [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland loss. Long-distance migration exposes the species to habitat loss and hunting pressure across multiple countries.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a thin, nasal *peew*. Females give a soft quack. Both are quiet vocalizations [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Blue-winged Teal is the most migratory of all North American ducks, with some individuals traveling from Canada to Argentina — a round trip of over 12,000 km. This extreme migration behavior is unusual for a small-bodied duck and contrasts with the closely related Cinnamon Teal, which migrates much shorter distances. The species' tropical wintering strategy is more similar to Neotropical migrant passerines than to most other North American waterfowl [O1, O3].

### Northern Shoveler (*Spatula clypeata*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Length 44–51 cm; wingspan 69–84 cm; mass 400–800 g. The oversized, spatulate bill is the defining feature and is proportionally the largest bill relative to head size of any PNW duck. Breeding males have a green head (similar shade to Mallard), white breast, chestnut flanks and belly, blue upper wing patch, and green speculum. Females are mottled brown with the same enormous bill — the bill shape alone identifies female shovelers. The bill is densely fringed with fine lamellae (comb-like structures) along both mandibles, forming a highly efficient filtering apparatus [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common in PNW lowland marshes, sewage treatment ponds, flooded agricultural fields, and estuarine margins. Sewage settling ponds are unusually important habitat — the nutrient-rich water supports dense invertebrate populations exploited by filter-feeding shovelers.

**Breeding:** Breeds in moderate numbers in eastern Oregon and Washington (Malheur NWR, Summer Lake, Klamath Basin). Uncommon breeder west of the Cascades.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft.

#### Diet and Foraging

The most specialized filter-feeder among PNW dabbling ducks. Swims forward with the bill submerged and partially open, pumping water through the densely packed lamellae to strain zooplankton, aquatic invertebrates, seeds, and algae. This filter-feeding technique is convergent with flamingo feeding and is unique among PNW ducks in its efficiency. Shovelers often feed in spinning circles (pairs or small groups swimming in tight circles to create a vortex that concentrates food particles), a cooperative foraging behavior not seen in other PNW dabblers [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 8–12 eggs. **Incubation:** 22–25 days. **Fledging:** 52–66 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** April–July.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. Continental population approximately 4,400,000 (2019 USFWS). A widespread and secure species [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Minimal. Wetland loss is the primary concern.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a low, nasal *took-took* during courtship. Females give a weak, descending quack. Generally a quiet species [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The shoveler's bill is a remarkable example of morphological adaptation for filter-feeding. The lamellae density in *Spatula clypeata* exceeds that of any other dabbling duck — up to 220 lamellae per mandible — approaching the filtering efficiency of some baleen-analogue systems. The genus *Spatula* (shovelers and blue-winged teal) diverged from *Anas* approximately 8–10 million years ago, and the bill elaboration has evolved convergently in at least four shoveler species on different continents (Northern, Cape, Red, and Australian Shoveler), suggesting strong selection for filter-feeding in shallow, nutrient-rich wetlands worldwide [O1, O3].

### Gadwall (*Mareca strepera*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

Length 46–57 cm; wingspan 78–90 cm; mass 700–1,100 g. The Gadwall is often described as the most subtly plumaged dabbling duck — a characterization that understates the intricate beauty of the breeding male's vermiculated gray body, black rump, and chestnut-and-black wing coverts. The white speculum (visible as a small white square on the trailing edge of the folded wing) is unique among PNW ducks and diagnostic. Females are brown and mottled, similar to female Mallards but slimmer-billed and with the white speculum. Bill is narrower than the Mallard's; in males, dark gray; in females, orange-sided with a dark culmen [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common in PNW lowland marshes, ponds, and estuaries. Uses freshwater wetlands, flooded fields, and shallow estuarine areas.

**Breeding:** Breeds in eastern Oregon and Washington (Malheur NWR, Summer Lake, Klamath Basin, Columbia Basin). Uncommon breeder west of the Cascades. Prefers seasonal wetlands with adjacent upland grassland for nesting.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft.

#### Diet and Foraging

More herbivorous than the Mallard. Feeds primarily on submerged aquatic vegetation, seeds, and algae. Less reliant on invertebrates than most dabblers. The Gadwall is the only PNW dabbling duck that regularly dives for food (shallow dives of 1–2 meters), though it more commonly dabbles and tips. Like the American Wigeon, frequently kleptoparasitizes coots and diving ducks [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 7–13 eggs. **Incubation:** 24–27 days. **Fledging:** 48–59 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. Continental population approximately 3,400,000 (2019 USFWS). The species has expanded its breeding range westward and northward over the past century [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland loss. Generally a secure species.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a short, nasal *nheck* and a whistle. Females give a Mallard-like quack but higher-pitched and more nasal. A relatively quiet duck [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Gadwall was recently moved to genus *Mareca* along with wigeons, based on molecular phylogenetics. Its closest relative is the Falcated Duck (*M. falcata*) of East Asia — an unexpected pairing given the dramatic difference in male plumage between the subtly vermiculated Gadwall and the spectacularly crested Falcated Duck. The contrast illustrates how sexual selection can drive rapid plumage divergence between closely related species [O1, O3].

### Wood Duck (*Aix sponsa*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; recovered from severe historical decline

#### Morphometrics

Length 47–54 cm; wingspan 66–73 cm; mass 450–870 g. The breeding male Wood Duck is widely considered the most ornate waterfowl in North America and among the most beautiful ducks in the world. The iridescent head combines deep green, purple, and blue hues with sweeping white stripes: a thin white line arches from the eye to the crest tip, another from the bill base across the cheek to the crest, and a third borders the throat. The crest itself — a long, swept-back structure — is unique among PNW ducks and gives the head a helmeted appearance. The eye is bright red. The breast is rich chestnut with white speckling at the junction with the white belly. Flanks are buff with fine black-and-white vermiculations. The back is iridescent green-bronze. The bill is patterned in red, white, and black. Females are gray-brown with a distinctive broad white eye-ring that tapers to a teardrop shape behind the eye — the eye-ring is the best field mark for separating female Wood Ducks from other female ducks. Both sexes have long, squared tails and short, clawed feet adapted for perching on branches, features reflecting the species' arboreal habits [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

The Wood Duck is the only PNW duck that nests exclusively in tree cavities, making it ecologically distinct from every other species in this module. Its habitat requirements are defined by the intersection of two elements: standing water for foraging and tree cavities for nesting.

**Breeding habitat:** Forested wetlands, riparian corridors with mature hardwoods (especially black cottonwood *Populus trichocarpa*, Oregon white oak *Quercus garryana*, and bigleaf maple *Acer macrophyllum*), beaver ponds surrounded by mature forest, and wooded swamps. The species requires large natural cavities (typically created by Pileated Woodpeckers or natural decay in old trees) or artificial nest boxes. Nest cavities are typically 3–20 meters above ground in trees within 500 meters of water, though distances up to 1.5 km have been documented.

In the PNW, the Wood Duck breeds most commonly in the Willamette Valley (where riparian cottonwood and oak provide cavities and seasonal flooding provides foraging habitat), the lower Columbia River floodplain, Puget Sound lowland riparian corridors, and the Okanogan Valley of north-central Washington. West-side populations have benefited enormously from nest box programs: thousands of artificial nest boxes have been installed along PNW rivers and wetlands since the 1930s, and at many sites these boxes now support the majority of breeding pairs. The Ridgefield NWR and Steigerwald Lake NWR on the lower Columbia host some of the highest Wood Duck densities in the PNW [G9, G12].

**Wintering habitat:** The PNW lies at the northern limit of the Wood Duck's year-round range. Many PNW birds are year-round residents in the milder western lowlands (Willamette Valley, Puget Sound) but individuals breeding in colder interior areas (eastern Washington, Idaho) migrate south to California and Mexico. Wintering birds use the same forested wetland and riparian habitats as breeding birds, adding flooded oak woodland and bottomland forest where acorn mast is available.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 2,500 ft. A lowland species closely tied to riparian floodplains.

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous with a strong emphasis on seeds, acorns, and fruits — more of a seed-and-mast specialist than other PNW dabbling ducks. Oregon white oak acorns, Pacific dogwood berries, blackberry fruits, and various seeds (sedge, wild rice, smartweed) constitute the bulk of the fall and winter diet. In spring and summer, invertebrates (aquatic insects, snails, spiders) become more important, particularly for egg-laying females and growing ducklings. Forages by dabbling in shallow water, walking through flooded woodland, and occasionally perching in trees to access fruits and acorns directly — the only PNW duck that regularly feeds while perched in trees. The clawed feet (unusual among ducks, which typically have webbed feet adapted for swimming rather than perching) enable this arboreal foraging [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Cavity nester — uses natural tree cavities (Pileated Woodpecker excavations, natural decay cavities) and artificial nest boxes. This is the Wood Duck's most ecologically significant characteristic: it is the only PNW dabbling duck that requires tree cavities, linking its population directly to old-growth and mature forest conservation.

**Nest location:** Cavities in deciduous or mixed-forest trees, typically 3–20 meters above ground. Cavity entrance must be at least 10 cm in diameter (horizontal oval). Will use nest boxes readily — nest box programs have been the primary management tool for Wood Duck conservation since the 1930s.

**Clutch size:** 9–14 eggs (range 6–16). Dump nesting (multiple females laying in a single cavity) is extremely common — "dump nests" with 20–40+ eggs from multiple females are regularly found, though incubation success of dump nests is low.

**Incubation:** 28–37 days (longer incubation periods reflect dump-nest clutch sizes), by female.

**Fledging:** The day-old duckling's leap from the nest cavity to the ground is one of the most remarkable events in PNW bird biology. Within 24 hours of hatching, ducklings climb the interior cavity wall using their sharp claws, reach the entrance hole, and jump — falling 3–20 meters to the ground or water below. The ducklings' extremely low body mass (~20 g) and downy plumage create sufficient air resistance that they survive the fall uninjured. The female calls from below, and ducklings walk or swim (often considerable distances) to the nearest water. Ducklings are independent at approximately 56–70 days [O1].

**Broods per year:** 1–2 in southern PNW (Willamette Valley, where the long season allows a second attempt); typically 1 in northern areas.

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation March–April (early for PNW ducks); hatching April–June; fledging June–August. Second broods (where they occur) hatch July–August.

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. The Wood Duck's recovery is one of the great conservation success stories in North American waterfowl management. By the early 1900s, the species had been reduced to critically low numbers by unregulated market hunting (the ornate male plumage was in high demand for fishing fly-tying and hat decoration) and habitat loss from logging of bottomland forest and draining of forested wetlands. Protection under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act (1918), hunting closures (1918–1941), and the massive deployment of artificial nest boxes beginning in the 1930s drove recovery. Today the Wood Duck is one of the most common breeding ducks in eastern North America and is common in the PNW west of the Cascades [G1, G9].

**Estimate:** Continental population approximately 3,500,000 (USFWS). PNW breeding population not precisely estimated but considered stable and self-sustaining where nest box programs are maintained [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Loss of mature riparian forest and old-growth cavity trees. Removal of large snags and dead trees (the source of natural cavities) for firewood, forest management, and development. Nest box maintenance — boxes require annual cleaning and predator-guard installation to remain effective; unmaintained boxes become predator traps as European Starlings, rat snakes, and raccoons exploit them. Competition for natural cavities from European Starlings, Eastern Gray Squirrels, and Hooded Mergansers. Forested wetland drainage [G1, G9].

**Source:** USFWS Waterfowl Status Report; WDFW; ODFW.

#### Vocalizations

The female gives a loud, rising squeal: *oo-EEK, oo-EEK* — a distinctive flight call often heard as birds fly through forested corridors at dawn and dusk. The call is the most recognizable sound of PNW riparian habitat in spring. Males give a thin, rising whistle: *jib* or *ji-ihb*. Both sexes are relatively vocal during pair formation and nesting, with the female's squeal serving as a contact call between pair members in dense forest habitat where visual contact is limited [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The genus *Aix* contains only two species: the Wood Duck of North America and the Mandarin Duck (*A. galericulata*) of East Asia. The two species are each other's closest relative, separated by the entire Pacific Ocean. Both share the same core biology: cavity nesting, arboreal perching, ornate male plumage, and temperate forest-wetland habitat requirements. This trans-Pacific disjunction is a classic example of a Holarctic ancestor fragmented by Pleistocene glaciation, with the North American and Asian remnant populations diverging into the current species. The extreme ornamentation of males in both species — among the most elaborate in all Anatidae — may reflect intense sexual selection in cavity-nesting species where females exercise strong mate choice (female-controlled nest sites give females leverage in mate selection, driving male ornamentation through Fisherian selection). The *Aix* lineage diverged from other Anatidae approximately 15–20 million years ago, making it one of the oldest living waterfowl lineages [O1, O3].

### Canvasback (*Aythya valisineria*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Rare breeder | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The largest diving duck in North America. Length 48–56 cm; wingspan 79–89 cm; mass 860–1,600 g (males average 1,250 g; females average 1,050 g). The breeding male is unmistakable: rich chestnut-red head and neck, black breast and rump, and a pale gray-white back and flanks that appear nearly white at distance — the "canvas" that gives the species its name. The long, sloping forehead merges seamlessly into the black bill, creating a distinctive wedge-shaped head profile unique among PNW ducks. This profile is the single most reliable field mark at any distance and distinguishes the Canvasback from the superficially similar Redhead, which has a rounded head with an abrupt forehead. Females are pale brown overall with the same elongated head-bill profile. The eye is red in males, dark brown in females. Bill is black in both sexes. In flight, both sexes show plain gray upperwings without speculum or wing patches. Sexual dimorphism is moderate — males are approximately 15–20% heavier and show the dramatic plumage contrast; females are cryptic brown [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** The PNW is an important wintering region for the Pacific Flyway Canvasback population. Concentrations occur in Puget Sound (particularly the bays and inlets of the central Sound), the lower Columbia River, Sauvie Island, the Klamath Basin, and the southern Oregon lakes. Marine-influenced bays and estuaries are used more frequently than by other *Aythya* species. Canvasbacks forage in deeper water than other diving ducks — typically 1–9 meters — and prefer areas with abundant submerged aquatic vegetation, particularly wild celery (*Vallisneria americana*), the plant for which the species is named (*valisineria*).

**Breeding:** Rare and localized breeder in the PNW. Small breeding populations persist at Malheur NWR, Summer Lake, and scattered eastern Oregon and Washington marshes. The species' primary breeding range is the prairie pothole region of the northern Great Plains and parkland Canada. PNW breeding birds require deep, permanent marshes with extensive beds of emergent vegetation (bulrush, cattail) and adjacent open water.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft (breeding marshes in eastern Oregon).

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily herbivorous among the diving ducks — the Canvasback's diet is more plant-dominated than any other *Aythya* species. Feeds by diving to pull up roots, tubers, and stems of submerged aquatic vegetation: wild celery (*Vallisneria*), sago pondweed (*Stuckenia pectinata*), and various *Potamogeton* species are the primary foods. The long, sloping bill is adapted for digging in soft substrates to reach buried tubers. On marine wintering areas, diet shifts to include more mollusks (clams, mussels) and aquatic invertebrates, particularly where preferred plant foods are unavailable. Dives average 5–15 seconds, reaching depths of 1–9 meters. Dive frequency is high — actively foraging birds may dive 40–60 times per hour [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 7–12 eggs. **Incubation:** 24–29 days, by female. **Fledging:** 56–77 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. Canvasbacks are frequent victims of brood parasitism by Redheads — female Redheads lay eggs in Canvasback nests at high rates (up to 50% of Canvasback nests in some marshes contain Redhead eggs), and the Canvasback incubates and raises the parasitic young, often at the cost of reduced survival for her own offspring [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to fluctuating. The Canvasback has one of the most variable population trajectories among North American waterfowl, driven by drought cycles on the prairie breeding grounds. Pacific Flyway population approximately 700,000 (2020 USFWS). Total continental population approximately 700,000–800,000. The species was the subject of some of the earliest restrictive waterfowl harvest regulations in North America — Canvasback hunting was closed entirely from 1953–1970 due to population declines [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Drought on prairie breeding grounds (the primary driver of population fluctuation), wetland drainage and degradation, lead poisoning from ingested shot in wetland sediments, and decline of submerged aquatic vegetation beds on wintering areas.

**Source:** USFWS Pacific Flyway Waterfowl Survey; Pacific Flyway Council.

#### Vocalizations

Generally quiet. Males give a soft, low cooing during courtship. Females give a low, hoarse *krrr* or growl. Neither sex is notably vocal outside the breeding season [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Canvasback is the sole North American representative of its lineage within *Aythya* and is not closely related to the Eurasian Pochard (*A. ferina*), despite superficial plumage similarity (both have chestnut heads and pale bodies). Molecular phylogenetics place the Canvasback as sister to the Redhead (*A. americana*), with the two species having diverged approximately 3–5 million years ago in North America. The elongated bill-head profile — the Canvasback's most distinctive morphological feature — appears to be an adaptation for digging in aquatic substrates, a foraging specialization not shared by other *Aythya* species. The species' unusually strong dietary preference for *Vallisneria* tubers creates a tight plant-herbivore linkage that makes Canvasback populations sensitive indicators of aquatic vegetation health in continental wetlands [O1, O3].

### Redhead (*Aythya americana*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Rare breeder | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium-large diving duck. Length 42–49 cm; wingspan 75–81 cm; mass 700–1,100 g. The breeding male has a rounded, rufous-red head (brighter red and more uniformly rounded than the Canvasback's chestnut wedge), black breast, gray back and flanks, and black rump. The abrupt, rounded forehead — contrasting sharply with the Canvasback's smoothly sloping profile — is the key structural field mark separating the two species. The bill is pale blue-gray with a black tip and a subtle white subterminal ring. Females are warm brown overall with a pale area around the bill base and a rounded head profile. The eye is yellow in males, brown in females. In flight, the gray wing stripe (lighter than scoters, darker than Canvasback) is visible. Sexual dimorphism follows the standard *Aythya* pattern: conspicuous in breeding plumage, reduced in eclipse [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Uncommon to fairly common winter visitor in the PNW. Uses large lakes, reservoirs, estuaries, and sheltered coastal bays. Less marine-oriented than Canvasback or scaup; prefers freshwater or brackish habitats. Found in the Klamath Basin, Malheur NWR (spring staging), Sauvie Island, and scattered Puget Sound locations.

**Breeding:** Rare and localized breeder in the PNW, primarily in the Great Basin marshes of southeastern Oregon (Malheur NWR, Harney Basin, Summer Lake). The primary breeding range is the prairie pothole region.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft.

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds on submerged aquatic vegetation, seeds, aquatic invertebrates (insect larvae, snails, amphipods), and small amounts of fish. Dives in water 0.5–4 meters deep, averaging shorter dives than the Canvasback. Less specialized than the Canvasback in diet — the Redhead is a generalist feeder among the *Aythya* pochards [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 7–14 eggs (in own nest). **Incubation:** 24–28 days. **Fledging:** 56–73 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. The Redhead is North America's most prolific obligate brood parasite among waterfowl — females frequently lay eggs in the nests of other ducks, particularly Canvasbacks, and some females may not build nests at all, relying entirely on parasitic reproduction. This dual strategy (nesting + parasitism) is unusual in waterfowl and has significant implications for the population dynamics of host species [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 1,000,000 (2019 USFWS). The species is far more abundant on the prairies than in the PNW [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland drainage on breeding grounds, degradation of aquatic vegetation on wintering areas, lead poisoning.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a distinctive wheezy, cat-like *meow* or *whee-ough* during courtship — one of the more unusual vocalizations among PNW ducks. Females give a low, rolling growl. Generally quiet outside breeding season [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Redhead's obligate brood parasitism strategy is one of the most sophisticated among waterfowl. Molecular studies reveal that parasitic and nesting females are not genetically distinct — individual females may switch strategies between years depending on body condition and nest-site availability, suggesting phenotypic plasticity rather than fixed alternative reproductive tactics. The high frequency of parasitism on Canvasback nests (the Redhead's closest relative) may reflect ancestral nest-site competition between sister species, with parasitism evolving as a low-cost alternative when nest sites are limited [O1, O3].

### Ring-necked Duck (*Aythya collaris*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium diving duck and the most commonly encountered *Aythya* in the PNW. Length 39–46 cm; wingspan 62–77 cm; mass 490–910 g. The breeding male has a glossy purple-black head with a subtle iridescent sheen, black breast and back, gray flanks with a bold white vertical bar at the waterline (the "spur"), and a distinctive white ring near the bill tip. The pale gray bill band — not the obscure chestnut neck ring for which the species was unfortunately named — is the most useful field mark. The actual neck ring (a dark chestnut collar at the base of the neck) is virtually invisible in the field and represents one of the most misleading common names in North American ornithology. Females are brown with a white eye-ring, pale face patch at the bill base, and the same bill pattern. The peaked crown creates a distinctive angular head shape, contrasting with the rounder heads of scaup. In flight, the gray wing stripe is duller than in scaup [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** The most abundant diving duck in PNW freshwater habitats during winter. Common on lakes, ponds, reservoirs, flooded fields, and slow rivers throughout the western lowlands. Shows a strong preference for shallow freshwater over marine or estuarine habitats, distinguishing it from scaup. Concentrations in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, Klamath Basin, and Columbia Basin. Often found on small bodies of water where other diving ducks are absent — farm ponds, city park ponds, and beaver impoundments host Ring-necked Ducks regularly.

**Breeding:** Breeds in moderate numbers in the PNW, primarily at higher elevations and in forested wetlands of the Cascades and northeastern mountains. Bog-edge ponds, beaver impoundments surrounded by conifer forest, and small mountain lakes with sedge margins are preferred breeding habitat. Also breeds at lower elevations at Malheur NWR and in the Klamath Basin. The species' breeding range has expanded westward and southward over the past century, and PNW breeding numbers have increased correspondingly [G9, G12].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,500 ft (mountain breeding lakes).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds on seeds, tubers, and vegetative parts of aquatic plants (pondweed, wild rice, sedge seeds) and aquatic invertebrates (insect larvae, snails, leeches). Forages by diving in shallow water, typically 0.5–3 meters deep — shallower on average than other diving ducks, approaching the foraging depth of dabbling ducks. This shallow-water foraging niche partially explains the species' use of small wetlands and ponds that are too shallow for other divers. Also dabbles and tips up occasionally, a behavioral flexibility unusual for a diving duck [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 6–14 eggs (typically 8–10). **Incubation:** 25–29 days, by female. **Fledging:** 49–56 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. Nests in dense emergent vegetation (sedge, cattail) or on floating mats of vegetation over shallow water, often on small wooded ponds or bog margins [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. Continental population approximately 1,500,000 (2019 USFWS). The species has expanded both its breeding and wintering range over the past several decades and is increasingly common in the PNW. This range expansion is one of the more notable recent distribution shifts among North American waterfowl [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland loss, particularly degradation of small forested wetlands and mountain beaver ponds on the breeding range. Generally a secure species.

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW; ODFW.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a low, wheezy whistle during courtship. Females give a soft, growling *rrrr*. Generally a quiet species. The male's courtship display includes a neck-throw with head tipped back and bill pointed skyward, accompanied by the whistle [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Despite its common name, the Ring-necked Duck's nearest relative is not the Eurasian Tufted Duck (*A. fuligula*) — which it superficially resembles — but rather the Southern Pochard complex of the Southern Hemisphere. Molecular phylogenetics place *A. collaris* as somewhat isolated within *Aythya*, diverging from other North American species approximately 5–7 million years ago. The species' preference for freshwater habitats and shallow-water foraging distinguishes it ecologically from the more marine-adapted scaup, and this habitat partitioning likely reduces interspecific competition within the genus on PNW wintering grounds [O1, O3].

### Greater Scaup (*Aythya marila*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium-large diving duck. Length 39–56 cm; wingspan 72–84 cm; mass 730–1,310 g. The breeding male has a glossy dark green-black head (the green sheen visible only in good light), black breast, pale gray-white back and flanks, and black rump. Females are dark brown with a prominent white patch at the bill base. The bill is pale blue-gray with a wider black nail than the Lesser Scaup. The key structural difference from Lesser Scaup — and one of the most challenging identification problems in PNW waterfowl birding — is head shape: Greater Scaup has a smoothly rounded head, while Lesser Scaup has a peaked crown with a slight tuft at the rear. In flight, Greater Scaup shows a bold white wing stripe extending well into the primaries (the stripe in Lesser Scaup fades at the secondaries). Males average larger and heavier than Lesser Scaup, but overlap is extensive [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Primarily a marine and estuarine species in the PNW during winter — this marine orientation is the key ecological difference from the freshwater-preferring Lesser Scaup. Concentrations occur in Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the San Juan Islands, Boundary Bay, Padilla Bay, and Comox Harbour (BC). Also found on the outer coast in sheltered bays and harbors. Large rafts of several hundred to several thousand birds form on protected marine waters, often mixed with Lesser Scaup, scoters, and goldeneyes.

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds across the Arctic and subarctic from Alaska to eastern Canada, on tundra lakes and marshes.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Exclusively a marine and coastal species in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous, with a strong animal component. Feeds on bivalve mollusks (clams, mussels), amphipods, snails, aquatic insects, and some aquatic vegetation. On marine wintering areas, blue mussels (*Mytilus* spp.) and small clams are the primary prey. Dives to 1–6 meters in marine habitats, averaging 20–30 seconds per dive. The diet is substantially more animal-dominated than the freshwater-foraging Ring-necked Duck or Canvasback, reflecting the marine foraging niche [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Arctic breeding biology: clutch 7–10 eggs, incubation 24–28 days, fledging 40–45 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. The Greater Scaup has shown one of the steepest and most sustained population declines among North American waterfowl since the 1980s. Continental population has declined from approximately 4,000,000 to approximately 3,200,000 over three decades. The cause of the decline is not fully resolved — hypotheses include contaminant accumulation (selenium, lead) at migration staging areas, declining invertebrate prey in the Great Lakes, and reduced breeding success potentially linked to climate change effects on Arctic habitats [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Broad-scale population decline from unclear causes, contaminant exposure, oil spill vulnerability on marine wintering areas, marine prey availability changes linked to ocean warming.

**Source:** USFWS; Pacific Flyway Council.

#### Vocalizations

Generally quiet. Males give a soft, wheezy *waah-whoo* during courtship. Females give a low, harsh *arrr*. Scaup are among the least vocal waterfowl outside of courtship displays [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Greater and Lesser Scaup represent one of the most challenging species pairs in the Northern Hemisphere — the two species are nearly identical in plumage, overlap broadly in size, winter in mixed flocks, and interbreed at low frequency where breeding ranges overlap. Molecular divergence between the two species is surprisingly high given the plumage similarity, suggesting relatively ancient separation (estimated 2–3 million years ago) followed by morphological stasis rather than recent divergence. The Greater Scaup is a Holarctic species (occurring across North America and Eurasia), while the Lesser is a Nearctic endemic — this distributional difference suggests that the Lesser Scaup originated from isolation of a North American population of an ancestral Greater Scaup-like species during a Pleistocene glaciation [O1, O3].

### Lesser Scaup (*Aythya affinis*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; declining

#### Morphometrics

A medium diving duck, slightly smaller on average than Greater Scaup but with extensive size overlap. Length 38–48 cm; wingspan 68–78 cm; mass 550–1,090 g. The breeding male has a glossy purple-black head (the purple sheen, versus the green of Greater Scaup, is a classic field mark but difficult to assess in variable light), black breast, gray-white flanks (often with finer vermiculation than Greater Scaup), and black rump. Females are dark brown with a white patch at the bill base similar to Greater Scaup but often smaller and less clearly defined. The peaked crown with a slight rear tuft — creating an angular head profile — is the most reliable structural mark for separating Lesser from Greater Scaup. In flight, the white wing stripe is restricted to the secondaries (not extending into the primaries as in Greater). The nail on the bill tip is slightly narrower. All of these marks are subtle, and experienced PNW birders acknowledge that a significant percentage of scaup on mixed wintering waters cannot be confidently identified to species [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** The freshwater counterpart to the marine Greater Scaup. Lesser Scaup prefer lakes, reservoirs, sewage treatment ponds, and large rivers over marine or estuarine habitats. This is the most abundant diving duck on inland PNW waters during winter. Common at Sauvie Island, Klamath Basin, Columbia Basin reservoirs, larger Willamette Valley lakes, and eastern Washington potholes. Also occurs on protected marine waters (Puget Sound, Salish Sea), especially where freshwater input reduces salinity, but marine concentrations are generally smaller than those of Greater Scaup.

**Breeding:** Does not breed regularly in the PNW. Breeds across interior Canada and Alaska, primarily in the boreal forest and prairie-parkland transition zone.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,500 ft (interior lakes and reservoirs).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds on aquatic invertebrates (amphipods, insect larvae, snails), seeds, and aquatic vegetation. Less dependent on bivalve mollusks than Greater Scaup, reflecting the freshwater habitat preference. On sewage treatment ponds — an increasingly important habitat for the species — feeds on chironomid larvae and other invertebrates that thrive in nutrient-enriched water. Dives to 1–4 meters, typically in shallower water than Greater Scaup [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed regularly in the PNW. Boreal breeding biology: clutch 8–11 eggs, incubation 21–27 days, fledging 47–54 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. Like Greater Scaup, the Lesser Scaup has experienced a long-term continental decline since the 1980s. The combined scaup population has declined from approximately 7,000,000 to approximately 4,000,000–5,000,000. Hypothesized causes include selenium contamination at staging areas in the western Great Plains, reduced invertebrate prey availability, and possible disease factors. The "scaup decline" is one of the most significant unresolved conservation questions in North American waterfowl management [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Continental population decline from uncertain causes, contaminant exposure (selenium, lead), wetland habitat degradation, potential prey-base shifts linked to invasive species (zebra mussels altering invertebrate communities at staging areas).

**Source:** USFWS; Pacific Flyway Council.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a faint, wheezy whistle and a soft *scaup* note (the origin of the common name) during courtship. Females give a low growl. Very quiet outside courtship [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The continental decline of Lesser and Greater Scaup since the 1980s has prompted extensive research into contaminant ecology — studies of selenium accumulation at staging areas on the Great Plains have revealed that scaup feeding on selenium-contaminated invertebrates in prairie wetlands may carry sublethal but reproductively significant contaminant loads to breeding areas. This research has broader implications for understanding how point-source contaminants at migration staging areas can drive population-level effects in migratory species that breed and winter thousands of kilometers away. The work represents one of the clearest demonstrations of how migratory connectivity translates environmental quality at one site into demographic effects at distant locations [O1, O3, G4].

### Ruddy Duck (*Oxyura jamaicensis*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A small, compact, stiff-tailed duck unlike any other PNW waterfowl in structure and behavior. Length 35–43 cm; wingspan 56–62 cm; mass 310–650 g. The breeding male is striking and unmistakable: rich chestnut body, black cap, bold white cheeks, and a sky-blue bill — the only PNW duck with a bright blue bill. The tail is long, stiff, and often held cocked upright at a 45–90° angle, a posture unique among PNW ducks and the hallmark of the stiff-tailed duck tribe (Oxyurini). In non-breeding plumage, males become dull gray-brown with the white cheeks retained and the bill turning dark. Females are gray-brown year-round with a single dark horizontal line across the pale cheek. The body is extremely compact and round, the tail long and stiff, and the legs set far back on the body — adaptations for diving that make the Ruddy Duck extremely clumsy on land (it can barely walk). The small wings relative to body mass require an exceptionally rapid wingbeat and a long, labored take-off run across the water surface [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common on PNW freshwater lakes, reservoirs, sewage treatment ponds, estuaries, and protected marine waters. Large wintering concentrations form on the larger lakes and reservoirs of the Columbia Basin, Willamette Valley, Puget Sound, and Klamath Basin. Sewage treatment ponds are disproportionately important habitat for wintering Ruddy Ducks throughout the PNW.

**Breeding:** Breeds in moderate numbers throughout eastern Oregon and Washington, primarily in freshwater marshes with dense emergent vegetation (bulrush, cattail). Malheur NWR, the Klamath Basin, Summer Lake, and Columbia Basin marshes host the largest PNW breeding concentrations. Uncommon breeder west of the Cascades.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding marshes).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds primarily on aquatic invertebrates — chironomid larvae (midges), amphipods, and other small invertebrates dominate the diet, supplemented by seeds and vegetative parts of aquatic plants. Forages by diving in shallow to moderate-depth water (0.5–5 meters), straining soft-bottom mud through lamellae in the bill — a foraging method more similar to the shovelers' filter-feeding than to the substrate-digging of other diving ducks. Dive duration averages 10–25 seconds. Feeds predominantly at night in many wintering locations, spending daylight hours loafing on the water surface [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 6–10 eggs, which are remarkably large relative to female body mass — Ruddy Duck eggs average 63 g, among the largest eggs relative to body size of any duck (a 450-g female lays eggs that are 14% of her body mass). **Incubation:** 23–26 days, by female. **Fledging:** 42–49 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. The male's courtship display is among the most elaborate and comical in the duck family: the male swims in front of the female with tail cocked vertically, inflates the neck, and rapidly beats the bill against the swollen chest, producing a hollow, accelerating *bub-bub-bub-bub-brrrrrt* accompanied by a plume of bubbles from air forced through the breast feathers. The display terminates with a short, belch-like call [O1, O7].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 1,500,000 (2019 USFWS). The species is widespread and generally secure [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Wetland drainage, particularly loss of dense-emergent-vegetation marshes on the breeding range. An introduced population of Ruddy Ducks in Europe (originating from escaped captive birds) has become a major conservation crisis for the closely related White-headed Duck (*Oxyura leucocephala*) in Spain — the two species hybridize, threatening the genetic integrity of the endangered White-headed Duck. European Ruddy Ducks are being eradicated under a UK-led program, making this the rare case where a North American native species is an invasive threat on another continent [G1, O1].

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

The courtship "bubbling display" described above is the primary sound — a rapidly accelerating *bub-bub-bub-bub-bub-brrrt* produced by the bill drumming against the inflated chest, terminating in a belch-like *tuck*. Females give a low nasal call. Outside courtship, the species is largely silent [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Ruddy Duck belongs to the stiff-tailed ducks (Tribe Oxyurini), a small, ancient lineage that diverged from other Anatidae approximately 20–25 million years ago — making Oxyurini one of the oldest living waterfowl tribes. Stiff-tailed ducks are distributed across all continents except Antarctica, and their phylogenetic isolation within Anatidae is so extreme that some systematists have suggested tribal- or subfamily-level separation. The extraordinarily large eggs, elaborate courtship displays, stiff tail feathers (used as rudders during diving and as display structures during courtship), and rear-mounted legs represent a convergent suite of adaptations for the diving-specialist niche, evolved independently from the Aythya diving ducks. The Ruddy Duck's relationship to the other stiff-tailed species (Argentine Blue-billed Duck, Maccoa Duck, White-headed Duck) reveals a pattern of vicariant speciation on different continents from a once-widespread Holarctic ancestor [O1, O3].

### Harlequin Duck (*Histrionicus histrionicus*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; State Candidate (WA); sensitive habitat requirements

#### Morphometrics

A small, compact sea duck and one of the most visually striking waterfowl in the world. Length 33–46 cm; wingspan 56–66 cm; mass 500–680 g (males average 600 g; females average 540 g). The breeding male is unmistakable and has no visual analogue among PNW birds: the body is slate-blue, overlaid with a bold pattern of white crescents, spots, and stripes bordered in black, arranged across the head, neck, breast, and flanks. A white crescent in front of the eye, a round white ear spot, a white collar stripe, chestnut flanks, and a white hip stripe create a harlequin's costume — the source of both the common name and the species epithet (*histrionicus*, from the Latin for actor or performer). The overall impression is of a small, dark duck painted with theatrical precision. Females are entirely dull brown with two or three pale facial spots and no wing speculum — one of the most nondescript female ducks, dramatically contrasting with the ornate male. The bill is small and narrow. Both sexes have a compact, round body with a relatively long tail often held cocked slightly. In flight, both sexes appear uniformly dark with no wing patches or stripes — identification depends on the small size, compact shape, and rapid wingbeat. Sexual dimorphism is extreme in plumage but minimal in size [O1, P16, O7].

The Harlequin Duck's appearance is so distinctive that it has inspired a disproportionate volume of artistic representation relative to its abundance — it is arguably the most frequently painted duck in Pacific Northwest wildlife art, and its image appears on conservation stamps, field guide covers, and nature center murals throughout the region.

#### Habitat

The Harlequin Duck is the only PNW waterfowl species that maintains a dual-habitat life cycle, alternating between fast-flowing mountain streams for breeding and rocky marine coastlines for wintering. This ecological duality — freshwater torrents in summer, pounding ocean surf in winter — is unique among North American ducks and defines the species' conservation profile.

**Breeding habitat:** Fast-flowing, clear mountain streams at elevations of 1,000–5,000 ft in the Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rocky Mountains (Idaho, BC). Breeding streams are characterized by swift current, rocky substrate, overhanging riparian vegetation, log jams, and abundant aquatic invertebrate prey. The species requires streams with intact riparian forest canopy — shading maintains cool water temperatures, controls sediment, and provides cover from aerial predators. Breeding territories are linear, following stream courses for 1–5 km per pair. In Washington, breeding populations are concentrated on streams draining the Olympic Mountains (particularly the Sol Duc, Hoh, Queets, Elwha, and Dosewallips rivers) and the North Cascades (Skagit River tributaries, Nooksack River tributaries). In Oregon, breeding occurs on rivers of the western Cascades (McKenzie, North Umpqua, Clackamas) and the Wallowa Mountains. Idaho populations breed on streams of the Clearwater and Salmon River drainages. British Columbia supports the largest PNW breeding population, with streams throughout the Coast Range, Cascades, and interior wet belt [G9, G14].

The breeding stream habitat requirement is extremely specific: streams must be fast enough to support the aquatic invertebrate communities the species depends on (primarily stonefly and caddisfly larvae), clear enough for visual foraging, and shaded by intact riparian forest. Logging or road construction that removes riparian buffers, increases sedimentation, or raises water temperature degrades breeding habitat directly. The species' sensitivity to stream-reach habitat quality makes it an indicator of watershed integrity — the presence of breeding Harlequin Ducks signals a high-quality, minimally disturbed mountain stream.

**Wintering habitat:** Rocky marine shorelines and jetties, exposed to moderate to heavy surf. The Harlequin Duck is one of the few waterfowl species that prefers high-energy rocky coastline over sheltered bays and estuaries. Wintering concentrations in the PNW occur along the outer coast of Washington (Cape Flattery to Cape Disappointment), the Olympic Peninsula shoreline, the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the rocky coastline of British Columbia (including Haida Gwaii, the Gulf Islands, and the west coast of Vancouver Island). In Oregon, wintering birds concentrate at rocky headlands and jetties (Yaquina Head, Depoe Bay, Cape Arago, jetties at the mouths of the Columbia, Umpqua, and Coos rivers). The species is rare on sandy beaches or in sheltered estuarine waters — it selects for rocky substrate with heavy surf action, where it forages in turbulent water that most other ducks avoid [G9, G14, O7].

**Molt migration:** After breeding, males leave the breeding streams in June–July and return to marine waters for their annual molt. Females follow after ducklings fledge in August–September. Molting birds congregate at traditional sites along the marine coast, where they undergo simultaneous wing feather molt and are flightless for approximately 3 weeks. These molt-staging sites are critical and site-faithful — individual birds return to the same 1–2 km stretch of coastline for molting year after year.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering, marine) to approximately 5,000 ft (breeding streams). This is the widest seasonal elevation range of any PNW waterfowl species.

#### Diet and Foraging

Aquatic invertebrates dominate the diet in both freshwater and marine habitats, making the Harlequin Duck one of the most exclusively animal-eating ducks in the PNW.

**Breeding season (freshwater):** Stonefly larvae (Plecoptera), caddisfly larvae (Trichoptera), mayfly larvae (Ephemeroptera), blackfly larvae (Simuliidae), and small fish. Forages by diving in fast-flowing water, swimming against the current along the stream bottom, turning over rocks with the bill to expose invertebrates. The compact body, powerful legs set far back, and strong claws enable the species to walk on the bottom of swift streams — a foraging technique unlike any other PNW duck. Foraging dives in streams are typically short (5–15 seconds) and shallow (0.5–2 meters).

**Wintering (marine):** Amphipods, marine snails (*Littorina* spp.), limpets, chitons, small crabs, sea urchins, and marine worms. Forages by diving in turbulent surf zones among rocks, where wave action dislodges invertebrates from substrate. Marine foraging dives are deeper and longer (10–40 seconds, 1–6 meters). The bill is smaller and narrower than in other sea ducks, adapted for picking individual invertebrates from rock surfaces rather than crushing mollusks or straining sediment [O1].

The energetic cost of foraging in swift-current and heavy-surf environments is high, and the Harlequin Duck's metabolic rate is correspondingly elevated — it consumes invertebrates almost continuously during daylight hours and has little metabolic margin during winter storms when foraging is interrupted.

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Ground nest, a shallow scrape lined with down, concealed in dense vegetation, rock crevices, or under logs along breeding streams. Nests are always near water, typically within 5 meters of the stream bank.

**Nest location:** Dense riparian vegetation, root wads, log jam cavities, and rock crevices along breeding streams. The nest is one of the most difficult to find of any North American duck — the cryptic female blends perfectly with the rocky, vegetated streamside habitat.

**Clutch size:** 5–7 eggs (range 3–8), smaller than most similarly sized ducks. Eggs are pale cream.

**Incubation:** 27–30 days, by the female alone. The male departs the breeding stream before or shortly after incubation begins, returning to marine waters. This early male departure means females incubate, brood, and rear ducklings entirely unassisted.

**Fledging:** 35–42 days. Ducklings are precocial and follow the female on the breeding stream, learning to forage in fast water within days of hatching. Duckling mortality is high (typically 30–60%) — fast current, cold water temperature, and predation by fish (bull trout, Dolly Varden), American Dipper, Barred Owl, and mink are the primary causes. The number of young that survive to fledging per successful nest averages 2–3 in PNW populations.

**Broods per year:** 1. No renesting after nest failure due to the short mountain breeding season.

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation late April–May; hatching late May–June; fledging July–August.

**Pair bonds:** Harlequin Ducks form pair bonds on the wintering grounds (January–March) and migrate together to the breeding stream. Pair bonds are seasonal — most pairs do not persist across years, and both sexes may pair with different mates in subsequent seasons. However, strong site fidelity to specific breeding streams means that some pairs re-form in successive years through shared stream fidelity rather than active pair maintenance [O1, G9].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to uncertain. The Pacific population of Harlequin Duck is estimated at approximately 200,000–300,000 birds, with the majority in Alaska and British Columbia. Washington's breeding population is small — estimated at 1,000–2,000 pairs, concentrated in the Olympic Mountains and North Cascades. Oregon's breeding population is estimated at fewer than 500 pairs. Idaho supports a small, poorly surveyed population in the Clearwater and Salmon River drainages. The species' low reproductive rate (small clutch, high duckling mortality, no renesting) makes populations slow to recover from declines [G1, G9, G14].

**Threats:** The Harlequin Duck's dual-habitat requirement creates a uniquely broad vulnerability profile:
- **Breeding habitat:** Logging and road construction in headwater watersheds (sedimentation degrades stream invertebrate communities), water diversion, dam construction on breeding streams, recreational disturbance (whitewater kayaking on breeding streams during nesting season)
- **Marine habitat:** Oil spills (the species' preference for rocky coastlines places it directly in the impact zone of nearshore spills; the 1989 *Exxon Valdez* spill in Alaska killed thousands of Harlequin Ducks and caused measurable population effects lasting over a decade), chronic low-level petroleum pollution near ports and shipping lanes, disturbance at traditional molting sites
- **Cross-habitat:** Climate change effects on both stream hydrology (earlier snowmelt, reduced summer flows) and marine invertebrate communities; low reproductive rate limits recovery capacity

The Harlequin Duck is classified as a State Candidate species in Washington, reflecting concern about the small, somewhat isolated breeding population in the state and the species' sensitivity to habitat disturbance at both freshwater and marine sites [G9].

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW; ODFW; Environment Canada.

#### Vocalizations

The Harlequin Duck is one of the most vocal sea ducks. Males give a high-pitched, squeaky *whi-whi-whi-whi* or *gia-gia-gia* — a surprisingly mouse-like sound from a duck, earning the species the colloquial name "squeaker" among some PNW coastal birders. Females give a harsh, nasal *ek-ek-ek*. Vocalizations are frequent in winter flocks and during pair formation; on breeding streams, the male's squeaky calls are often the first indication of the species' presence in a mountain stream valley. The voice is diagnostic — no other PNW duck produces such consistently high-pitched, squeaky calls [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Harlequin Duck is the sole living member of its genus (*Histrionicus*), with no close living relative — the genus is phylogenetically isolated within the sea duck tribe Mergini, having diverged from other sea ducks approximately 10–15 million years ago. Fossil *Histrionicus* from the Miocene suggest that the genus was once more diverse, with at least two additional species now extinct. The Harlequin Duck's dual-habitat life cycle — mountain stream breeding, marine wintering — is unique among waterfowl globally and represents an extreme ecological specialization that likely originated during the Pliocene or early Pleistocene as glaciation created the montane stream habitats the species now depends on. The Pacific and Atlantic populations of Harlequin Duck are genetically distinct (mtDNA divergence approximately 1.5–2%), suggesting limited gene flow across the continent for at least 500,000 years. The Pacific population is substantially larger and more stable than the declining Atlantic population, which has been listed as endangered in eastern Canada [O1, O3].

The male's ornate plumage — white spots and crescents on a slate-blue ground — has no parallel in Anatidae. The pattern does not match the iridescent display plumage of most sexually dimorphic ducks (which rely on structural coloration from feather nanostructure) but instead uses a bold graphic pattern of pigmented white markings. This pattern may function in the low-light, high-turbulence environments where pair formation occurs (overcast coastal waters in winter), where high-contrast markings are more visible than iridescent shimmer.

### Surf Scoter (*Melanitta perspicillata*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A large, bulky sea duck and the most abundant scoter in the PNW. Length 44–51 cm; wingspan 77–84 cm; mass 800–1,200 g. The breeding male is jet black with bold white patches on the forehead and nape and a massive, multicolored bill — orange, white, and black with a large black basal knob. The effect is of a heavy, entirely black duck with a bright clown-like face. Females are dark brownish-black with pale whitish patches at the lores and behind the ear; the bill is dark and lacks the male's dramatic swelling. Immatures resemble females but show a whiter belly. In flight, all ages and sexes appear uniformly dark with no wing patches. The Surf Scoter is the only scoter endemic to North America — both White-winged and Black Scoters also occur in the Old World. Sexual dimorphism is moderate in size, extreme in bill ornamentation [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** The most abundant wintering sea duck in PNW marine waters. Enormous rafts — sometimes numbering in the tens of thousands — form in Puget Sound, the Strait of Georgia, Boundary Bay, Padilla Bay, and the San Juan Islands. Also common along the outer coast in sheltered bays and nearshore waters. Prefers shallow marine waters over sandy or muddy substrates where bivalve mollusks are accessible — a habitat preference that complements the Harlequin Duck's rocky shoreline specialization.

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds across the boreal forest of Alaska and northern Canada, on forested lakes and ponds. The breeding biology of the Surf Scoter is among the least studied of any North American waterfowl — nests are rarely found and the species' remote boreal breeding range has limited research access.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Exclusively marine in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Feeds primarily on bivalve mollusks — mussels (*Mytilus* spp.), small clams, and cockles dominate the diet on marine wintering grounds. Also takes herring roe during spawning events (a nutritionally critical food source in late winter), amphipods, crabs, and marine worms. Dives to 1–10 meters, averaging 20–40 seconds per dive. Foraging flocks often move in coordinated formations, with birds at the rear flying to the front — a rolling foraging pattern similar to that described for Snow Geese on agricultural fields [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Boreal breeding biology: clutch 5–9 eggs, incubation 28–30 days, fledging 55+ days (poorly documented).

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. The Surf Scoter has shown a significant long-term decline in PNW wintering numbers since the 1970s. Puget Sound Christmas Bird Count data show approximately 50% reduction in Surf Scoter abundance over three decades. The cause is not fully resolved — hypotheses include herring population declines in the Salish Sea, marine ecosystem shifts linked to climate change, and potential problems on the poorly understood boreal breeding grounds [G1, G9, O16].

**Threats:** Herring spawning declines in the Salish Sea (reducing a key late-winter food source), oil spill vulnerability, marine habitat degradation, and unknown threats on boreal breeding grounds.

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW marine bird surveys.

#### Vocalizations

Generally quiet. Males give a low, gurgling croak during courtship. Females give a harsh *kraak*. Scoters are among the least vocal sea ducks [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The three scoter species — Surf, White-winged, and Black — form a well-supported monophyletic group within the sea duck tribe Mergini. The Surf Scoter is the only Nearctic endemic among the three (the others are Holarctic). Molecular phylogenetics suggest that the Surf Scoter diverged from the common ancestor of the three scoters approximately 4–6 million years ago, with the White-winged and Black Scoters diverging more recently from each other. The Surf Scoter's massive, colorful bill — the most ornamented bill of any scoter — is a secondary sexual characteristic that has no clear foraging function and likely evolved entirely through sexual selection for male display [O1, O3].

### White-winged Scoter (*Melanitta deglandi*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The largest scoter in the PNW. Length 48–58 cm; wingspan 80–90 cm; mass 1,100–1,900 g. Males are entirely black with a small white comma-shaped patch around the eye and a bold white secondary wing patch (the "white wing" visible in flight and often when sitting, as a white flash along the folded wing). The bill is orange with a prominent black knob at the base. Females are dark brownish-black with two pale face spots (variable) and the same white wing patch — the wing patch is the most reliable identification mark for separating White-winged from Surf and Black Scoters at all ages and sexes. In flight, the white secondaries flash conspicuously. The species is appreciably larger and bulkier than either Surf or Black Scoter, and experienced birders often identify it by size alone in mixed scoter flocks [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common on PNW marine waters, overlapping broadly with Surf Scoters. Large flocks occur in Puget Sound, the Strait of Georgia, Boundary Bay, and along the outer coast. Tends to use somewhat deeper water than Surf Scoters, foraging over sandy and rocky substrates in water 3–15 meters deep. Mixed scoter flocks — Surf, White-winged, and Black Scoters together — are a characteristic sight on PNW marine waters from November through April.

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds across interior Alaska and northern Canada on large lakes in boreal and subarctic zones.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Exclusively marine in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Feeds primarily on bivalve mollusks (mussels, clams) and other benthic invertebrates (crabs, sea urchins, marine worms). The larger body size and heavier bill enable White-winged Scoters to crush larger-shelled prey than other scoters. Dives to 2–15 meters, averaging 30–60 seconds per dive — the longest average dive duration of the three PNW scoters. Swallows bivalves whole and crushes the shells in the muscular gizzard [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Boreal breeding biology: clutch 9–11 eggs, incubation 25–30 days, fledging approximately 63–77 days — one of the longest fledging periods of any North American duck, which constrains breeding to regions with sufficiently long ice-free seasons.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. Like Surf Scoter, the White-winged Scoter has declined in PNW wintering abundance over recent decades. The decline appears less severe than in Surf Scoter but is consistent across multiple data sources. Continental population approximately 700,000 [G1, G9, O16].

**Threats:** Oil spill vulnerability (marine concentration makes the species highly susceptible to spills), decline of bivalve prey from marine ecosystem changes, unknown breeding-ground threats.

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW.

#### Vocalizations

Generally silent. Males give a thin, bell-like whistle during courtship. Females give a harsh croak. Among the quietest sea ducks [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The White-winged Scoter has been the subject of a recent taxonomic split: the North American population (*M. deglandi*) is now recognized as a separate species from the Eurasian Velvet Scoter (*M. fusca*), with which it was formerly lumped. Genetic divergence between the two forms is substantial (mtDNA divergence approximately 3–4%), supporting species-level separation. The split (adopted by AOS in 2019) also recognized the Stejneger's Scoter (*M. stejnegeri*) of East Asia as a third species. This three-way split of the former "Velvet/White-winged Scoter" complex reflects the broader trend in waterfowl taxonomy toward recognizing populations separated by ocean basins as distinct evolutionary lineages [O1, O3].

### Black Scoter (*Melanitta americana*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The smallest and rarest of the three PNW scoters. Length 43–49 cm; wingspan 71–79 cm; mass 700–1,200 g. The breeding male is entirely black — the only entirely black duck in the PNW with no white markings on the body — with a prominent bright orange-yellow knob at the base of the bill. Females are dark brown with a contrasting pale cheek and throat that is more sharply defined than in female Surf or White-winged Scoters. The absence of any wing patches distinguishes both sexes from White-winged Scoter in flight. The overall impression is of the plainest, most uniformly dark scoter, but the male's bill knob is conspicuous at moderate distances [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** The least common scoter on PNW marine waters. Found in Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the outer Washington and Oregon coasts, and the Strait of Georgia. Typically occurs in smaller numbers than Surf or White-winged Scoters, often in single-species groups or in small numbers within mixed scoter flocks. Shows a slight preference for shallower nearshore waters compared to White-winged Scoter.

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds in western Alaska and across northern Canada on tundra ponds and boreal lake margins.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Exclusively marine in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Feeds on bivalve mollusks (mussels, small clams), marine snails, amphipods, and small crabs. Diet is broadly similar to other scoters but the smaller bill size limits the maximum prey size relative to White-winged Scoter. Dives to 2–10 meters, averaging 20–30 seconds per dive [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Tundra/boreal breeding biology: clutch 6–9 eggs, incubation 27–31 days, fledging approximately 50–55 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Poorly known; possibly stable or declining. The Black Scoter is the least studied of the three PNW scoters. Continental population estimates range from 300,000 to 600,000, with wide uncertainty. The Pacific Flyway component is substantially smaller than the Atlantic population [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Oil spill vulnerability, marine prey availability changes, limited data on population trends and breeding biology.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a distinctive, mellow whistled *pur-ew* or *wheee-ow* — a more musical call than the other scoters and one of the more pleasant duck vocalizations. Females give a harsh growl. The male's whistle is audible at considerable distance and can be a useful identification cue in mixed scoter flocks [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

Like the White-winged Scoter, the Black Scoter was recently split from its Old World counterpart — the Common Scoter (*M. nigra*) of Eurasia. The North American Black Scoter (*M. americana*) is now recognized as a full species based on genetic divergence (approximately 2–3% mtDNA divergence), differences in bill knob shape and color, and vocal differences. The scoter genus *Melanitta* thus now contains six recognized species (three in North America, three in the Old World), up from three prior to the taxonomic revisions. This pattern of trans-oceanic splitting within a genus mirrors similar revisions in goldeneyes and mergansers [O1, O3].

### Long-tailed Duck (*Clangula hyemalis*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN VU (globally vulnerable)

#### Morphometrics

A medium sea duck with the most complex molt sequence of any bird in the world. Length 38–60 cm (males, including tail streamers; females 37–41 cm); wingspan 73–79 cm; mass 600–1,000 g. The species undergoes three distinct plumages annually (rather than the two of most ducks), producing a constantly shifting appearance that confounds field identification across seasons. Breeding males (seen briefly in the PNW in late winter/spring) have a dark head with a white face patch, dark breast, white flanks, and the diagnostic elongated central tail feathers (10–15 cm beyond the tail tip). Winter males show the reverse pattern: white head with a dark cheek patch, white neck, dark breast. Females are duller, with a dark crown, pale face, and brownish body; they lack the tail streamers. In flight, the all-dark wings with no patches or stripes, combined with the small body and frenetic wingbeat, create a distinctive jizz. The Long-tailed Duck is the deepest-diving duck in the world, regularly reaching depths of 60 meters and recorded at over 70 meters — depths approaching those of marine mammals [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** An offshore marine specialist in the PNW. Found on open waters of Puget Sound, the Strait of Georgia, the outer coast (typically farther offshore than scoters), and the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Prefers deeper water than other sea ducks and is often found far from shore, making it more difficult to observe than inshore species. Less common in the PNW than in its primary wintering areas on the Great Lakes and Atlantic coast.

**Breeding:** Does not breed in the PNW. Breeds on Arctic tundra across the Holarctic, on tundra lakes, pools, and coastal lagoons.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Exclusively marine and offshore in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Feeds on bivalve mollusks, amphipods, fish, marine worms, and crustaceans. The Long-tailed Duck's remarkable diving ability allows it to exploit benthic prey at depths inaccessible to any other duck. Dives routinely to 10–30 meters and has been documented at 60+ meters in the Great Lakes and Baltic Sea — depths at which dive duration exceeds 60 seconds and the duck must manage significant hydrostatic pressure. The foraging strategy is unique: rather than short, rapid dives like most diving ducks, the Long-tailed Duck makes relatively few, long, deep dives, spending more time underwater than on the surface during active foraging periods [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed in the PNW. Arctic breeding biology: clutch 6–9 eggs, incubation 24–29 days, fledging 35–40 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining globally. The species was uplisted from IUCN Least Concern to Vulnerable in 2012 based on steep declines in the European/Siberian population (estimated 65% decline in Baltic Sea wintering population). North American populations are less well monitored but appear stable to declining. Global population approximately 3,200,000, down from approximately 6,300,000 in the late 20th century [G1, O1].

**Threats:** Bycatch in commercial fishing nets (gillnet bycatch is the primary mortality source in European waters), oil spill vulnerability (offshore distribution puts the species at risk from ship-source and platform-source spills), marine ecosystem changes affecting prey availability, and the poorly understood drivers of the European population collapse.

**Source:** USFWS; IUCN Red List.

#### Vocalizations

The Long-tailed Duck is the most vocal sea duck in the PNW. Males give a loud, yodeling *ow-ow-oww-OW-OLEET* that carries far across open water — a sound frequently described as musical or melodic by observers, and markedly different from the grunts and croaks of other sea ducks. The call has an almost celebratory quality and is given throughout the winter, particularly during calm weather. The species' former common name — "Oldsquaw" — was derived from a perceived similarity between the male's persistent calling and human conversation; the name was changed to Long-tailed Duck by AOS in 2000 following recommendations from indigenous organizations that the former name was culturally inappropriate [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Long-tailed Duck is the sole member of genus *Clangula*, an ancient lineage within Mergini with no close living relative. The three-plumage annual molt cycle is unparalleled in birds — most ducks have two plumages (breeding and non-breeding), and the Long-tailed Duck's third plumage (supplemental plumage, acquired in early winter) creates a molt schedule so complex that describing the species' appearance requires specifying month rather than just "breeding" or "non-breeding." This extreme molt complexity may be related to the species' Arctic-breeding, marine-wintering life cycle, where different plumage adaptations are needed for tundra breeding, open-ocean wintering, and the transitional molt period. The species' extraordinary diving depth — exceeding 60 meters — places it in the realm of pursuit-diving seabirds (murres, penguins) rather than typical waterfowl, and the physiological adaptations enabling these dives (oxygen storage, bradycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction) are convergent with those of marine mammals [O1, O3].

### Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Rare breeder | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The smallest diving duck in North America. Length 32–40 cm; wingspan 55–60 cm; mass 270–500 g (males average 450 g; females average 330 g). The breeding male is boldly patterned: glossy purple-green-black head with a large, wedge-shaped white patch extending from behind the eye to the nape (the "buffalo head" that gives the species its common name), white body with a black back. The head appears oversized relative to the compact body. Females are dark gray-brown above, paler below, with a single horizontal white oval patch on the cheek behind the eye — smaller and more sharply defined than the male's white patch. In flight, males show extensive white on the inner wing; females show a smaller white wing patch. The small size is the single most useful field mark — a Bufflehead on the water is immediately distinguished from all other diving ducks by its diminutive proportions [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** One of the most common and widespread wintering diving ducks in the PNW. Found on virtually every type of water body: marine bays, estuaries, freshwater lakes, rivers, farm ponds, city park ponds, and sewage treatment lagoons. Less particular about habitat than most diving ducks. Concentrations in Puget Sound, Salish Sea, Willamette Valley, Klamath Basin, and Columbia Basin. The Bufflehead's catholic habitat use and ubiquity make it one of the most frequently encountered winter ducks in the PNW.

**Breeding:** Rare and localized breeder in the PNW. The primary breeding range is the boreal forest of western Canada and Alaska. Small breeding populations occur in the northern Cascades and northeastern Washington/Idaho, where birds nest in tree cavities near mountain lakes and ponds. The Bufflehead nests almost exclusively in old Northern Flicker (*Colaptes auratus*) cavities — it is the only North American duck small enough to use flicker holes (entrance diameter approximately 6–7 cm), and this cavity-size requirement ties its breeding distribution tightly to the range of the Northern Flicker and the availability of mature aspens and other trees that retain old flicker holes [G9].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,000 ft (breeding).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Marine wintering diet dominated by invertebrates: amphipods, shrimp, isopods, small crabs, marine snails, and insect larvae. Freshwater diet includes aquatic insect larvae (especially chironomids and damselfly nymphs), small crustaceans, and seeds. Dives rapidly in shallow water (typically 1–4 meters, rarely deeper), averaging 10–15 seconds per dive. The rapid, shallow dive cycle allows high feeding rates — actively foraging Buffleheads may dive 30–50 times per hour. Often forages singly or in small groups rather than in the large rafts typical of other wintering diving ducks [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 6–11 eggs. **Incubation:** 28–33 days, by female. **Fledging:** 50–55 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. Nests in tree cavities, almost exclusively old Northern Flicker holes — this single-host cavity dependency is unique among North American ducks and more restrictive than even the Wood Duck's cavity requirements [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. Continental population approximately 1,400,000 (2019 USFWS). The species is widespread and secure [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Loss of cavity trees (mature aspen stands) on the breeding range, competition for nest cavities with European Starlings and other secondary cavity nesters.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a squeaky, nasal chattering during courtship. Females give a harsh, grating *err-err*. Generally a quiet species [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Bufflehead is the smallest member of the goldeneye genus *Bucephala* and is the closest living relative of the Common and Barrow's Goldeneyes. The species' miniaturization — it is approximately 60% the mass of a Common Goldeneye — is linked to its unique cavity-nesting requirement: by evolving small body size, the Bufflehead accessed the abundant cavity resource created by Northern Flickers (the most common woodpecker in North America), avoiding competition with larger cavity-nesting ducks for the larger cavities produced by Pileated Woodpeckers. This body size–cavity size coevolutionary dynamic is one of the cleanest examples in waterfowl ecology of how a physical constraint (cavity entrance diameter) can drive morphological divergence. The Bufflehead's boreal breeding range overlaps almost perfectly with the range of trembling aspen (*Populus tremuloides*), the primary tree species in which Northern Flickers excavate cavities [O1, O3].

### Common Goldeneye (*Bucephala clangula*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering / Rare breeder | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium, compact diving duck. Length 40–51 cm; wingspan 65–80 cm; mass 600–1,200 g. The breeding male has a glossy dark green-black head (appearing black at distance), a round white facial spot between the eye and bill, bright yellow eye, white body, and black back with white scapular stripes. The head shape is distinctive — puffy and triangular, with a peaked crown creating a more angular profile than the Barrow's Goldeneye. Females are gray-bodied with a chocolate-brown head, yellow-tipped bill, and the same yellow eye. In flight, both sexes show bold white wing patches on the secondaries and inner wing coverts. The wings produce a distinctive, loud whistling sound in flight — a ringing *WING-WING-WING* audible at considerable distance, earning the species the colloquial name "whistler." This wing whistle is one of the most characteristic winter sounds on PNW waterways [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Common on PNW freshwater and marine habitats. Uses lakes, rivers, protected marine bays, estuaries, and harbors. Found throughout the western lowlands, with concentrations in Puget Sound, the Columbia River, Willamette Valley, and Klamath Basin. Less strictly marine than goldeneyes' close relatives the scoters, but more marine-tolerant than Ring-necked Duck.

**Breeding:** Rare breeder in the PNW. A boreal cavity nester, breeding primarily in the forests of Alaska and Canada. Small PNW breeding populations occur in northeastern Washington, northern Idaho, and southern British Columbia, using tree cavities near mountain lakes and rivers. Like the Bufflehead, a secondary cavity nester — but uses larger cavities than the Bufflehead (typically Pileated Woodpecker excavations or natural decay cavities), placing it in competition with Wood Ducks and Hooded Mergansers [G9].

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 4,000 ft (breeding).

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous. Feeds on aquatic invertebrates (crustaceans, aquatic insects, mollusks) and small fish, supplemented by aquatic vegetation and seeds. Marine diet emphasizes crabs, shrimp, and mussels. Freshwater diet emphasizes insect larvae and crustaceans. Dives to 1–7 meters, averaging 15–25 seconds per dive [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 6–11 eggs. **Incubation:** 27–32 days, by female. **Fledging:** 56–66 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** May–August. Cavity nester using Pileated Woodpecker excavations, natural decay cavities, and nest boxes. Dump nesting (multiple females laying in one cavity) occurs at moderate frequency [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 1,500,000 (2019 USFWS). A widespread, secure species across its Holarctic range [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Loss of large cavity trees, competition for nest cavities, oil spill exposure on marine wintering areas.

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

The wing whistle in flight is the most diagnostic sound — a metallic, ringing *WING-WING-WING* produced by air passing through the narrow outer primary feathers. This is not a vocalization but a mechanical sound, and it functions as a species-recognition signal (the wing whistle differs between Common and Barrow's Goldeneye, and between goldeneyes and other ducks). Vocal calls include a nasal, buzzy *pee-EET* (male courtship) and a harsh *gack* (female) [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Common Goldeneye is a Holarctic species with populations across both North America and Eurasia — the Eurasian birds are phenotypically identical to North American birds and no taxonomic split has been proposed (in contrast to the scoters and mergansers, where trans-oceanic populations have been split). The close relationship with Barrow's Goldeneye — the two species hybridize at low frequency and produce fertile offspring — indicates relatively recent speciation, likely during Pleistocene glacial isolation. The wing whistle, produced by the modified ninth primary feather, is a remarkable example of a non-vocal acoustic signal serving a communicative function: experimental studies have shown that goldeneyes respond to playback of wing whistles by turning to look, suggesting the sound functions as an aerial identification signal among flock members [O1, O3].

### Barrow's Goldeneye (*Bucephala islandica*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding / Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC; State Monitored (WA); PNW specialty species

#### Morphometrics

A medium, compact diving duck closely resembling the Common Goldeneye but with key structural and plumage differences. Length 42–51 cm; wingspan 68–77 cm; mass 590–1,300 g (males average 1,050 g; females average 730 g). The breeding male has a glossy purple-black head (the purple sheen contrasts with the green of Common Goldeneye, but this mark is light-dependent and unreliable), a white crescent-shaped facial patch between the eye and bill (crescent in Barrow's versus round spot in Common — the single most reliable head mark), bright yellow eye, white body, and black back. The back pattern differs from Common Goldeneye: Barrow's shows a row of white spots along the wing coverts rather than the long white scapular stripes of the Common. The head shape is subtly different — more steeply angled forehead creating a more vertical face profile, and a shorter, more triangular bill. Females are very similar to female Common Goldeneye: gray body, chocolate-brown head, yellow eye. The female's bill is typically more extensively yellow-orange than the Common Goldeneye female's (the entire bill may appear orange rather than just the tip), but overlap exists. In flight, the wing pattern differs: Barrow's shows less white in the wing than Common, with the dark greater coverts creating a dark bar through the white wing patch that is absent in Common Goldeneye [O1, P16, O7].

Separating Barrow's from Common Goldeneye is one of the classic field identification challenges of PNW waterfowl birding. The two species winter side by side, interbreed occasionally, and share many ecological features. Experienced observers rely on a gestalt combination of head shape, facial mark shape, back pattern, bill color (in females), and wing pattern rather than any single definitive mark.

#### Habitat

The Barrow's Goldeneye is a PNW specialty — a species whose North American center of abundance lies in the Pacific Northwest and British Columbia, in contrast to the Common Goldeneye's broader Holarctic distribution. The PNW is the most important region globally for this species, hosting the largest breeding and wintering concentrations.

**Breeding habitat:** Mountain lakes and streams in the Cascades, Olympics, northern Rockies, and the interior ranges of British Columbia. Breeds at elevations of 2,000–6,500 ft, using clear mountain lakes, beaver ponds, and slow stretches of mountain rivers. Nests in tree cavities (primarily old Pileated Woodpecker and Northern Flicker cavities in large-diameter snags and trees) near mountain water bodies. In British Columbia, also nests in rock crevices, cliff faces, and nest boxes in treeless alpine terrain — a cavity-nesting flexibility not shown by Common Goldeneye. The species shows extreme natal philopatry: females return to nest within meters of their natal site, and some cavity sites have been used continuously for decades.

Important PNW breeding areas include the alpine and subalpine lakes of the North Cascades (Washington), the Methow Valley and Okanogan drainage (Washington), the high lakes of the Oregon Cascades (Deschutes, Klamath, and Hood River drainages), the lake basins of central Idaho, and the interior plateau of British Columbia (Cariboo, Chilcotin, and Thompson regions) — the last of these supports the single largest known breeding concentration of Barrow's Goldeneye in the world [G9, G14].

**Wintering habitat:** Marine bays, harbors, and nearshore waters of Puget Sound, the Strait of Georgia, the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the outer coast (particularly harbor and jetty areas). Unlike the Common Goldeneye, Barrow's shows a strong preference for rocky marine habitats and is rarely found on freshwater during winter in the PNW. The species' winter distribution maps closely onto rocky shorelines, breakwaters, and pier pilings — microhabitats rich in the mussel and crustacean prey it specializes on.

The PNW marine wintering population represents the core of the species' Pacific flyway distribution. Puget Sound, the Salish Sea, and the BC coast together host the majority of the western North American wintering population of approximately 150,000–200,000 birds. This concentration gives the PNW outsized responsibility for the species' conservation [G9, G14, O16].

**Molt migration:** Like Harlequin Ducks, male Barrow's Goldeneyes undergo a molt migration after breeding, departing mountain lakes in June–July and congregating at traditional molt-staging sites on large interior lakes or marine waters. The largest known molt-staging concentration occurs on lakes in the interior of British Columbia (particularly Stum Lake and surrounding lakes in the Cariboo region), where tens of thousands of males gather for simultaneous wing molt — one of the most remarkable aggregations of any sea duck species. These molt sites are few, traditional, and irreplaceable: disturbance or habitat degradation at a single site could affect a significant fraction of the Pacific population's males during their most vulnerable period (flightless molt) [G14].

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering) to approximately 6,500 ft (breeding). A strong elevational migrant.

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous, with a strongly animal-dominated diet. Marine wintering diet consists primarily of crustaceans (amphipods, isopods, small crabs), mussels, barnacles, marine snails, and marine worms. The species forages in shallower, rockier water than Common Goldeneye, and its diet reflects this habitat — mussel and crustacean prey plucked from rock surfaces rather than soft-bottom bivalves. On mountain breeding lakes, feeds on aquatic insects (caddisfly and dragonfly larvae are important), freshwater crustaceans, and fish eggs. Dives to 1–8 meters, averaging 15–30 seconds per dive [O1].

The dietary difference between Barrow's and Common Goldeneye on shared wintering grounds — Barrow's specializing on rocky-substrate prey, Common using softer substrates — represents an example of ecological character displacement that reduces interspecific competition where the two species co-occur.

#### Reproduction

**Nest type:** Cavity nester — uses old Pileated Woodpecker and Northern Flicker excavations, natural decay cavities, rock crevices, and nest boxes. The cavity-nesting requirement links the species to mature mountain forests with large-diameter snag retention.

**Nest location:** Tree cavities near mountain lakes and streams, typically within 200 meters of water. Cavity height varies widely (2–25 meters). In treeless areas of British Columbia, nests in rock crevices, cliff ledges, and earth banks — an unusual flexibility for a cavity-nesting duck.

**Clutch size:** 6–12 eggs (average 9). **Incubation:** 28–34 days, by female. The male departs after incubation begins.

**Fledging:** 56–63 days. Ducklings leave the nest cavity within 24–48 hours of hatching (the same cavity-exit behavior as Wood Duck) and are led by the female to the breeding lake or stream.

**Broods per year:** 1.

**Breeding season in PNW:** Nest initiation May–June; hatching June–July; fledging August–September. The breeding season at high-elevation mountain lakes is compressed by snowmelt timing — late snowpack years can delay nesting by 2–3 weeks, reducing fledging success.

**Pair bonds:** Seasonal. Pairs form on wintering grounds in January–March and migrate together to breeding areas.

**Brood amalgamation:** Multiple broods of ducklings frequently merge on mountain breeding lakes, forming crèches of 20–60 ducklings attended by 2–4 females. This communal brood-rearing is more common in Barrow's than in Common Goldeneye and may represent a cooperative strategy to reduce predation risk on small mountain lakes where escape cover is limited [O1, G9].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to slightly declining in some areas. Total North American population estimated at approximately 200,000–300,000. The Pacific population (British Columbia through Oregon) constitutes approximately 80% of the total. An isolated population of approximately 2,000 birds breeds in Iceland — the only European breeding population and the source of the species epithet *islandica*. A small, disjunct eastern North American population breeds in Quebec and Labrador. The species' concentration in the PNW and BC means that this region is disproportionately responsible for global conservation of Barrow's Goldeneye [G1, G9, G14].

**Threats:**
- **Breeding habitat:** Loss of large-diameter cavity trees through logging (particularly salvage logging of fire-killed snags, which removes the most productive nesting substrate), mountain lake water-level management, and fire suppression that prevents snag recruitment. Climate change threatens high-elevation breeding habitats through reduced snowpack (altering lake levels and breeding phenology) and upslope treeline advance that may reduce alpine lake habitat.
- **Wintering habitat:** Oil spill vulnerability (concentration in nearshore marine waters), contamination of mussel beds by urban and industrial runoff, shoreline hardening that eliminates rocky intertidal foraging habitat.
- **Molt staging:** The extreme concentration of males at a handful of interior BC lakes during the flightless molt period creates catastrophic vulnerability — a single oil spill, disease outbreak, or disturbance event at a major molt lake could affect tens of thousands of birds simultaneously.
- **Overall:** The species' narrow global range (essentially BC to Oregon, with small outlier populations in Iceland and Quebec) and dependence on specific habitat types at multiple life stages make it more vulnerable to regional-scale threats than its IUCN LC status might suggest. Some ornithologists have argued that the species warrants a higher concern rating given the concentration of the global population in a single flyway [G9, G14].

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW; Environment Canada; Pacific Flyway Council.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a nasal, grunting *ka-KAA* during courtship — harsher and lower than the Common Goldeneye's courtship call. Females give a harsh *gack*, similar to Common Goldeneye. The wing whistle in flight is subtly different from Common Goldeneye's — slightly lower-pitched and less ringing, reflecting minor differences in primary feather shape, though this difference is difficult to distinguish in the field. During courtship displays, males perform the "head-throw" display (head thrown back until the crown touches the rump), accompanied by the nasal call — a dramatic performance visible at long distances on winter marine waters [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Barrow's Goldeneye occupies one of the most restricted global ranges of any waterfowl species — essentially the Pacific Northwest and British Columbia, with small relict populations in Iceland and eastern Canada. This fragmented distribution is a legacy of Pleistocene glaciation: fossil and genetic evidence suggests that the species' ancestor was once widespread across the Northern Hemisphere, and that glacial advances isolated populations in three refugia (Pacific Northwest, Iceland, and northeast North America). The Iceland population, separated from North American birds by the Atlantic Ocean, shows distinct mtDNA haplotypes and may represent an incipient speciation event — if isolation persists, the Iceland population could eventually merit species-level recognition.

The close relationship between Barrow's and Common Goldeneye — hybridization occurs regularly where breeding ranges overlap, and hybrids are fertile — indicates that the two species diverged relatively recently, likely during a Pleistocene glacial cycle approximately 1–2 million years ago. The ecological character displacement evident on shared wintering grounds (Barrow's on rocky substrate, Common on soft substrate) and the habitat partitioning on breeding areas (Barrow's at higher elevations, Common lower) suggest that competition-driven niche differentiation maintains species boundaries despite ongoing hybridization. This goldeneye species pair is one of the best examples in waterfowl of how ecological divergence can maintain species integrity in the face of incomplete reproductive isolation [O1, O3].

The species' name honors Sir John Barrow (1764–1848), Second Secretary of the Admiralty, who promoted Arctic exploration but never visited the bird's habitat — a common pattern in 19th-century ornithological naming, where species were named for patrons rather than their discoverers or ecological characteristics.

### Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A small, elegant merganser and one of the most visually distinctive ducks in the PNW. Length 40–49 cm; wingspan 60–66 cm; mass 450–880 g. The breeding male has a black head with a spectacular fan-shaped white crest bordered in black — when raised, the crest forms a dramatic half-circle that dominates the bird's profile; when lowered, it compresses to a white stripe along the nape. The body is black above, chestnut on the flanks, and white below, with two black bars crossing the white breast. The eye is bright yellow. Females are gray-brown with a warm cinnamon crest (smaller than the male's but still prominent), dark eye, and dark bill. Both sexes have the thin, serrated bill characteristic of mergansers — the "saw bill" adapted for gripping slippery fish. In flight, males show a white wing patch with dark leading edges; females show a gray wing patch. The overall impression is of a small, long-tailed duck with an oversized, erectile head crest [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Forested wetlands and riparian corridors, with habitat requirements closely paralleling the Wood Duck. Nests in tree cavities (Pileated Woodpecker excavations, natural decay cavities, and nest boxes) near slow-moving water — forest ponds, beaver impoundments, backwater sloughs, and wooded swamps. Breeds throughout the PNW lowlands west of the Cascades, with highest densities in the Willamette Valley, lower Columbia floodplain, Puget Sound riparian corridors, and Vancouver Island. Also breeds sparingly in northeastern Washington and Idaho.

**Wintering:** Year-round resident in the milder portions of the PNW. Winters on freshwater ponds, rivers, and sheltered estuarine waters. Rarely ventures onto open marine waters. More tied to fresh water year-round than the larger mergansers.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 2,500 ft. A lowland species.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily piscivorous, but with a more diverse diet than the larger mergansers. Feeds on small fish (sculpins, sticklebacks, minnows), aquatic insects, crayfish, frogs, tadpoles, and occasionally aquatic vegetation. Forages by diving in shallow to moderate-depth water (typically 1–4 meters), using visual hunting — the forward-facing eyes provide excellent binocular vision for tracking prey underwater. The thin, serrated bill is used to grip fish rather than crushing mollusk prey. Often forages in very shallow, murky water where larger mergansers do not feed, exploiting the small-prey niche [O1].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 7–13 eggs. **Incubation:** 26–41 days (the wide range reflects frequent dump nesting — multiple females laying in one cavity). **Fledging:** approximately 71 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** March–July. Cavity nester competing directly with Wood Ducks for nest cavities — at many PNW sites, Hooded Mergansers and Wood Ducks use the same nest boxes, and interspecific dump nesting (a Hooded Merganser laying in a Wood Duck nest or vice versa) is documented [O1, G9].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable to increasing. Continental population approximately 650,000 (Partners in Flight estimate). The species has benefited from nest box programs initially deployed for Wood Ducks [G1, O4].

**Threats:** Loss of mature riparian forest and cavity trees. Competition for nest cavities with European Starlings and Wood Ducks. Degradation of forested wetlands.

**Source:** USFWS; Partners in Flight.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a deep, rolling, frog-like *crrroo-crrroo* during courtship — a surprising sound from a duck, more reminiscent of an amphibian than a bird. The sound is produced during the dramatic crest-raising display, in which the male erects the full fan-shaped crest, throws the head back, and vibrates the bill against the water surface. Females give a hoarse croak. The male's courtship display is one of the most elaborate of any PNW waterfowl species [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Hooded Merganser is the sole member of genus *Lophodytes* and is the most phylogenetically isolated merganser — it diverged from the *Mergus* lineage (Common and Red-breasted Mergansers) approximately 10–12 million years ago. Despite its merganser bill and fish-eating habits, the Hooded Merganser is actually more closely related to the goldeneyes (*Bucephala*) than to the *Mergus* mergansers, based on molecular phylogenetics. This unexpected relationship indicates that the serrated "merganser" bill has evolved convergently at least twice within the sea duck tribe Mergini — once in the *Mergus* lineage and once in the *Lophodytes*/*Bucephala* lineage — a remarkable example of parallel evolution of a feeding apparatus in response to similar selective pressures (piscivory in freshwater habitats) [O1, O3].

### Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Resident / Breeding | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

The largest merganser in North America and the largest duck regularly breeding on PNW rivers. Length 54–68 cm; wingspan 78–97 cm; mass 900–2,100 g. The breeding male is striking: glossy dark green-black head (smooth-profiled, without a crest), white body with a faint salmon-pink wash (visible at close range), and black back. The overall impression is of a large, sleek, white-bodied duck with a dark head — far more elongated and streamlined than the blocky diving ducks. The long, thin, bright red-orange bill with a hooked tip and serrated edges (the saw-toothed lamellae that give mergansers the colloquial name "sawbill") is diagnostic. Females have a gray body, rich chestnut head with a shaggy, swept-back crest, white chin patch, and the same red-orange bill. In flight, both sexes show extensive white on the inner wing. The species is appreciably larger than the Red-breasted Merganser, with a heavier bill and more compact head profile (no crest in males) [O1, P16].

#### Habitat

**Breeding:** Clear rivers and large mountain lakes throughout the PNW. The Common Merganser is the characteristic fish-eating duck of PNW rivers — present on virtually every clear-water river system from sea level to approximately 5,000 ft. Breeds along the Columbia River and its tributaries, the Skagit, Nooksack, Snoqualmie, Deschutes, McKenzie, Rogue, Umpqua, Clearwater, and Salmon rivers, and on mountain lakes throughout the Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rockies. Requires clear water for visual foraging — avoids turbid or heavily silted rivers. Nests in tree cavities, cliff crevices, rock piles, and occasionally on the ground under dense vegetation. Cavity nesting is more common in the PNW than in other parts of the range, where ground nesting predominates.

**Wintering:** Year-round resident on many PNW rivers and lakes. Winters on unfrozen rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and occasionally on marine waters in protected bays. River-dwelling populations remain on territory year-round where water remains ice-free.

**Elevation range:** Sea level to approximately 5,000 ft.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily piscivorous — the most fish-dependent of all PNW ducks. Feeds on salmonids (juvenile salmon and trout), sculpins, whitefish, and other fish up to approximately 25 cm in length. The serrated bill functions as a fish-gripping tool, and the species captures prey through underwater pursuit — swimming after fish with powerful foot strokes, the streamlined body providing hydrodynamic efficiency. Foraging dives in rivers are typically 15–40 seconds, covering considerable distance upstream or downstream. In clear mountain rivers, the species' foraging can be observed from banks or bridges: the submerged merganser is visible as a pale torpedo shape rocketing through pools in pursuit of fish.

The Common Merganser's heavy fish predation has generated decades of conflict with sport fishermen and fisheries managers who view the species as a competitor for salmonids. Scientific studies have generally shown that merganser predation on juvenile salmon is compensatory rather than additive — that is, the fish taken by mergansers would largely have died from other causes (starvation, predation by other fish, disease). However, the perception of competition persists, and the species was historically persecuted through bounty programs and nest destruction at fish hatcheries [O1, P11].

#### Reproduction

**Clutch size:** 6–17 eggs (typically 9–12; high clutch counts often include dump-nest contributions). **Incubation:** 28–35 days, by female. **Fledging:** 60–75 days. **Breeding season in PNW:** April–August. Nests in large tree cavities, cliff ledges, rock crevices, or on the ground under overhanging vegetation near water. Ducklings leave the nest cavity 24–48 hours after hatching. Female and young swim downstream on the breeding river, sometimes traveling 10–30 km to reach rearing habitat. Large brood amalgamations (crèches of 30–70+ young attended by 1–3 females) are common on PNW rivers [O1].

#### Population

**Trend:** Stable. Continental population approximately 1,700,000 (2019 USFWS). A widespread, secure species across its Holarctic range [G1, G3].

**Threats:** Degradation of river water clarity (sedimentation from logging, road construction, and agriculture reduces foraging efficiency), loss of large cavity trees along rivers, dam construction (eliminates river habitat and blocks access to upstream breeding sites), and historical persecution at fish hatcheries (largely ceased).

**Source:** USFWS.

#### Vocalizations

Males give a twanging, bell-like *uig-a* during courtship. Females give a harsh, rasping *karr-karr* — a rough, grating call that carries well along river corridors and is often the first indication of the species' presence on a PNW river. The female's alarm call, given when flushed from a brood, is a loud, rough croak [O1, O7].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Common Merganser is a Holarctic species with populations across North America and Eurasia (where it is called the Goosander). Unlike the scoters and goldeneyes, no taxonomic split between the Old World and New World populations has been proposed, despite some plumage differences (Eurasian males lack the salmon-pink body wash of North American birds). Molecular divergence is low (<0.5% mtDNA), suggesting recent gene flow across the Atlantic or Pacific, possibly via the Arctic. The genus *Mergus* — the "true" mergansers — represents the most piscivorous lineage in Anatidae, with the serrated bill, streamlined body, and underwater pursuit-diving behavior representing a suite of co-adapted traits for fish predation that evolved approximately 15–20 million years ago. The PNW's extensive network of clear, salmon-bearing rivers provides ideal habitat, and the Common Merganser is among the species most closely tied to the ecological health of the region's river systems [O1, O3, P11].

### Red-breasted Merganser (*Mergus serrator*)

**Family:** Anatidae | **Status:** Wintering | **Conservation:** Not listed (ESA); IUCN LC

#### Morphometrics

A medium merganser, smaller and slimmer than the Common Merganser. Length 51–62 cm; wingspan 67–82 cm; mass 800–1,350 g. The breeding male has a dark green-black head with a conspicuous shaggy, double-pointed crest projecting from the rear crown — this wild, unkempt crest is the most reliable field mark separating the species from Common Merganser (which has a smooth-headed male). The breast is reddish-brown with dark streaking (the "red breast"), the flanks are gray, and the white collar and neck are prominent. The bill is thin, serrated, and red-orange, similar to Common Merganser but proportionally thinner. Females have a gray body, rufous-brown head with a wispy crest (less dramatic than the male's), and a gradual transition from head color to throat color (in contrast to the female Common Merganser's sharply defined white chin patch). In flight, both sexes show white wing patches, but with more black barring through the white than in Common Merganser [O1, P16, O7].

#### Habitat

**Wintering:** Primarily a marine and estuarine species in the PNW during winter — in contrast to the freshwater-oriented Common Merganser. Common on protected marine waters of Puget Sound, the Strait of Georgia, the San Juan Islands, and along the outer coast in harbors and bays. Also found on estuaries, tidal rivers, and occasionally on large freshwater lakes. The Red-breasted Merganser is the most marine-adapted merganser, found farther from shore and in more saline water than the Common or Hooded Merganser.

**Breeding:** Does not breed regularly in the PNW. Breeds across the boreal and Arctic zones of Alaska and Canada, on tundra lakes, river deltas, and coastal islands. Unlike the cavity-nesting Common and Hooded Mergansers, the Red-breasted Merganser nests on the ground — a fundamental ecological difference from its congener.

**Elevation range:** Sea level (wintering). Essentially an exclusive marine and estuarine species in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

Piscivorous. Feeds primarily on small marine fish — herring, sand lance, shiner perch, and sculpins on PNW marine waters. Also takes crustaceans (shrimp, crabs) and marine worms. Forages by pursuit-diving in shallow to moderate-depth marine water, using the same swimming pursuit technique as Common Merganser but in marine rather than riverine settings. Cooperative foraging has been documented: groups of Red-breasted Mergansers sometimes line up and drive fish into shallow water, an organized hunting strategy unusual among ducks [O1].

#### Reproduction

Does not breed regularly in the PNW. Arctic/boreal breeding biology: ground nester (concealed in dense vegetation, rock piles, or drift debris near water), clutch 7–12 eggs, incubation 29–35 days, fledging 59–65 days.

#### Population

**Trend:** Declining. Continental population approximately 250,000–400,000 (poorly estimated due to dispersed marine wintering distribution). PNW wintering numbers have declined in Puget Sound-based surveys over recent decades, paralleling declines in other marine-dependent species (scoters, Western Grebe) [G1, G9].

**Threats:** Marine prey fish declines (herring, sand lance) in the Salish Sea, oil spill vulnerability, marine ecosystem changes linked to climate warming, and limited understanding of population drivers.

**Source:** USFWS; WDFW marine bird surveys.

#### Vocalizations

Generally quiet. Males give a rasping, cat-like *yeow* during courtship. Females give a harsh croak. Less vocal than Common Merganser [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

The Red-breasted Merganser and Common Merganser are sister species that diverged approximately 3–5 million years ago, likely during Pliocene climate oscillations that separated ancestral populations into freshwater (Common) and marine (Red-breasted) ecological niches. The Red-breasted Merganser's ground-nesting habit — unique among PNW mergansers — reflects its Arctic/tundra breeding ecology where tree cavities are unavailable. This nesting divergence (cavity vs. ground) between the two *Mergus* species is a clear example of ecological character displacement in breeding habitat, complementing the marine vs. freshwater habitat partitioning on the wintering grounds. Together, these divergences allow two closely related fish-eating ducks to coexist in the PNW during winter without direct competition [O1, O3].

The species holds an unofficial distinction: the Red-breasted Merganser is the fastest bird in level flight, with credible records of 100+ mph (161 km/h) measured behind aircraft. This speed — far exceeding any other duck — reflects the species' extremely high wing loading (the ratio of body mass to wing area), which demands rapid wingbeats and high airspeed but may also confer advantages in the energetically expensive pursuit-diving foraging style.
