> **Module 1E deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document profiles the Passeriformes families Parulidae (New World warblers), Cardinalidae (cardinals, tanagers, grosbeaks), and Passerellidae (New World sparrows) occurring in the Pacific Northwest study region. Extended treatments (~5-8KB) are given for species of exceptional PNW ecological significance; standard treatments (~2-3KB) for remaining regularly occurring species. > **Design principle:** Warblers, sparrows, and their allies form the insectivore-granivore backbone of PNW terrestrial bird communities. Parulidae are the dominant canopy and shrub-layer insectivores in breeding-season forests; Passerellidae are the dominant ground-level seed-and-insect foragers across virtually every PNW terrestrial habitat. Their abundance, diversity, and sensitivity to habitat change make them primary indicators of ecosystem health from alpine meadow to coastal scrub.

# FAMILY PARULIDAE — New World Warblers

**Order:** Passeriformes
**Family:** Parulidae (119 species worldwide; ~14 breeding regularly in PNW)
**Phylogenetic position:** Parulidae is a large New World radiation within the nine-primaried oscines, sister to Icteriidae (Yellow-breasted Chat, recently split) and closely related to Cardinalidae and Icteridae. The family diversified primarily in the Neotropics, with the *Setophaga* radiation producing the majority of North American breeding species. Molecular phylogenetics (Lovette et al. 2010) resolved long-standing questions about warbler relationships, confirming that plumage similarity between distantly related species reflects convergent evolution rather than close kinship [P23].

**PNW significance:** Warblers are the dominant canopy and shrub-layer insectivores in PNW breeding forests. The Townsend's-Hermit Warbler hybrid zone in the Oregon and Washington Cascades is one of the best-studied avian hybrid zones in North America and a model system for understanding speciation, competitive displacement, and introgression [P21, P22].

## Yellow-rumped Warbler (*Setophaga coronata*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding, wintering (year-round in some lowland areas)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF score low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 19-23 cm
- **Mass:** 11-14 g (males average slightly heavier)
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: blue-gray upperparts with black streaking, bright yellow rump patch, yellow patches on sides of breast and crown, white (Myrtle form) or yellow (Audubon's form) throat, black breast band, white wing bars. Breeding female: similar pattern but duller, brownish-gray replacing blue-gray. Non-breeding: dull brownish-gray overall, yellow rump patch retained (diagnostic year-round). Audubon's form (*auduboni*) predominates in PNW; Myrtle form (*coronata*) regular in migration and winter.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Males brighter with more extensive black breast markings; females browner with diffuse streaking.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Coniferous and mixed forests from lowland to subalpine, 0-7,000 ft. Prefers open canopy with moderate understory development. Common in Douglas-fir, western hemlock, ponderosa pine, and mixed conifer stands. Adapts readily to second-growth forest, parks, and suburban areas with mature conifers.
- **Wintering:** Lowland areas, riparian corridors, urban parks, coastal scrub. More habitat-flexible in winter than any other PNW warbler. Readily uses berry-producing shrubs (wax myrtle, juniper, poison oak fruit) and will forage in open areas including parking lots and beach wrack.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (breeding); primarily below 2,000 ft (wintering)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 4, 5, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Canopy and mid-story forager during breeding; highly flexible in winter, using ground, shrub, and canopy layers.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (breeding season: caterpillars, beetles, flies, aphids, spiders); fruit and berries (fall-winter: wax myrtle, juniper, poison oak, dogwood, madrone)
- **Foraging strategy:** Aerial sallying from canopy perches during breeding; gleaning from bark, needles, and leaves; hovering at branch tips. In winter, switches to a fruit-dominated diet supplemented by insects. The unique ability to digest wax myrtle (*Myrica*) berries — enabled by a specialized lipase enzyme absent in most warblers — allows Yellow-rumped Warblers to winter farther north than any other North American warbler species.
- **Seasonal variation:** Near-complete diet switch from insectivory (May-August) to frugivory (October-March). This metabolic flexibility is unmatched among PNW Parulidae.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Open cup
- **Nest location:** Conifer branch, typically 4-50 ft above ground, often against trunk on horizontal limb
- **Clutch size:** 4-5 eggs
- **Incubation:** 12-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 10-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (occasionally 2 in PNW lowlands)
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly increasing (BBS 1966-2019: +0.3% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 130 million (North America); among the most abundant warblers on the continent [O4]
- **Threats:** Generally low concern. Localized habitat loss in lowland breeding areas. Window collision mortality in urban areas during migration.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Slow, loose trill, often with a change in pitch partway through: *sweet-sweet-sweet-sweet-SWEE-swee*. Variable; Audubon's song averages higher-pitched and more musical than Myrtle form.
- **Calls:** Sharp *check* or *tchep*, given frequently in all seasons. Flight call a thin rising *seet*.

### Evolutionary Note

The Yellow-rumped Warbler complex is a textbook case in avian speciation research. Audubon's (*auduboni*) and Myrtle (*coronata*) forms were treated as separate species until 1973 when AOS lumped them based on extensive hybridization in the Canadian Rockies. Genomic studies reveal that despite widespread hybridization, strong assortative mating and divergent plumage signaling maintain phenotypic distinctness across a narrow hybrid zone. The two forms diverged approximately 200,000-400,000 years ago during Pleistocene glacial cycles. AOS is currently reviewing whether to re-split them into separate species — a decision that would affect PNW taxonomy since Audubon's is the primary breeding form and Myrtle the primary winter visitor. Two additional forms — Goldman's (*goldmani*, Guatemala) and Black-fronted (*nigrifrons*, Mexico) — further complicate the species limits question [O3, P23].

### Migration

- **Flyway:** Pacific
- **Spring arrival:** Late March to mid-April (Audubon's); late April to May (Myrtle)
- **Fall departure:** September-October (most); some Audubon's overwinter in lowlands
- **Wintering grounds:** PNW lowlands (partial migrant), Pacific coast south to Mexico
- **Distance:** Short to medium-distance migrant; 0-3,000 km

## Townsend's Warbler (*Setophaga townsendi*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 19-21 cm
- **Mass:** 8-11 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: striking black-and-yellow face pattern with black crown, black cheek patch bordered by yellow supercilium and malar, bright yellow breast with bold black streaking on flanks, olive-green back, two white wing bars, dark tail with white outer tail feathers. Female: similar pattern but black replaced by olive-green on crown and auriculars, streaking less extensive.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male has solid black facial markings; female olive-green in same areas.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense coniferous forest, especially old-growth and mature Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Pacific silver fir, and Sitka spruce. Canopy specialist, foraging primarily in upper third of canopy. Prefers structurally complex forest with dense canopy closure.
- **Wintering:** Coastal live oaks, mixed broadleaf-conifer in southern OR, southward through Mexico and Central America. Small numbers winter in lowland PNW in mild years.
- **Elevation range:** 500-6,000 ft (breeding)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 3, 4
- **Microhabitat:** Upper canopy foliage gleaner; strongly associated with dense conifer canopy.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders (caterpillars, beetles, leafhoppers, flies, aphids)
- **Foraging strategy:** Active gleaner in upper canopy foliage, hovering at branch tips and undersides of needles. Occasionally sallies for flying insects. On wintering grounds, supplements diet with honeydew from Homoptera (scale insects), a behavior rare among warblers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Almost exclusively insectivorous during breeding; adds honeydew and small fruits on wintering grounds.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Open cup of moss, bark strips, plant fibers, lined with hair and feathers
- **Nest location:** High in conifer, typically 10-60 ft up on horizontal limb near trunk
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.1% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 26 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Old-growth forest loss reduces optimal breeding habitat. Competitive interactions with Hermit Warbler in hybrid zone. Wintering habitat loss in Central American cloud forests.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Buzzy ascending phrases: *ZEE-ZEE-ZEE-zee-ZEET*. Higher-pitched and buzzier than Hermit Warbler. Individual males typically sing 4-8 song types.
- **Calls:** Sharp *chip*, similar to other *Setophaga* but slightly lower-pitched.

### Evolutionary Note

Townsend's Warbler is the northern member of a *Setophaga* species complex including Hermit Warbler to the south and Black-throated Green Warbler (*S. virens*) in eastern North America. The three species diverged during Pleistocene glacial fragmentation of western conifer forests. Townsend's Warbler is behaviorally dominant over Hermit Warbler: in their hybrid zone (see Hermit Warbler, below), Townsend's males are more aggressive, win territorial disputes, and the hybrid zone has been moving southward at approximately 0.3-1.0 km/year. Townsend's mitochondrial DNA has introgressed far south of the plumage contact zone, suggesting long-term competitive displacement [P21, P22, P23].

### Ecoregion Presence

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Breeding (upper edge) | Uncommon | Visitor |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Migration | Common | Visitor |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Migration, rare breeding | Uncommon | Visitor |

### Migration

- **Spring arrival:** Late April to mid-May
- **Fall departure:** Late August to October
- **Wintering grounds:** Southern Oregon coast south to Mexico and Central America
- **Distance:** Medium-distance migrant; 1,500-4,500 km

## Hermit Warbler (*Setophaga occidentalis*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 19-21 cm
- **Mass:** 8-11 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: unmistakable bright yellow face and crown with no dark auricular patch, gray nape and back, white underparts, dark throat variable (absent to solid black), two bold white wing bars, dark tail with white outer tail feathers. Female: duller yellow face, olive-gray crown, minimal or no dark throat. In the hybrid zone with Townsend's, a continuous range of intermediate phenotypes occurs — from Hermit-like birds with faint cheek smudge to Townsend's-like birds with reduced yellow.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male has brighter yellow face and darker throat; female olive-washed with minimal throat markings.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Mature and old-growth coniferous forests, particularly Douglas-fir, white fir, and Shasta red fir at mid to upper elevations. Canopy specialist like Townsend's Warbler. Core breeding range in PNW is southern Oregon Cascades and Siskiyou Mountains, extending north through western Oregon and into southwestern Washington where the hybrid zone begins.
- **Wintering:** Mountain pine-oak forests of Mexico and Central America, 5,000-10,000 ft elevation.
- **Elevation range:** 1,500-6,500 ft (breeding)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak, in southern OR)
- **Microhabitat:** Upper canopy of tall conifers; foraging behavior nearly identical to Townsend's Warbler.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders gleaned from conifer foliage (caterpillars, beetles, flies, aphids, spiders)
- **Foraging strategy:** Active foliage gleaner in upper canopy. Hover-gleans at branch tips. Foraging niche virtually identical to Townsend's Warbler — the two species are ecological equivalents, which intensifies competitive dynamics in the hybrid zone.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round; limited frugivory on wintering grounds.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Open cup of conifer needles, plant fibers, spider silk, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** High on horizontal conifer branch, 20-60 ft up, usually away from trunk
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-10 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.2% annually, not statistically significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 4 million (North America) — substantially smaller population than Townsend's [O4]
- **Threats:** Hybrid zone expansion by Townsend's Warbler represents a form of genetic displacement. Old-growth habitat loss reduces breeding territory. Wintering habitat loss in Mexican mountain forests.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Buzzy rising phrases ending in a lower-pitched note: *zeedle-zeedle-zeedle-ZEEP*. Generally lower-pitched and less buzzy than Townsend's. In the hybrid zone, intermediate song types occur and males may sing songs of either parental type.
- **Calls:** Sharp *chip*, very similar to Townsend's.

### The Townsend's x Hermit Hybrid Zone — An Extended Treatment

The hybrid zone between Townsend's and Hermit Warblers is one of the most thoroughly studied avian hybrid zones in North America and a model system for understanding speciation, competitive displacement, and the dynamics of species boundaries [P21, P22].

**Geographic extent:** Three geographically distinct hybrid zones exist in the PNW, all on the west slope of the Oregon and Washington Cascades: (1) southern Washington Cascades (approximately 46-47N latitude), (2) central Oregon Cascades (approximately 44-45N), and (3) southern Oregon Cascades/Siskiyou Mountains (approximately 42-43N). The northern and central zones are narrow (10-30 km wide); the southern zone is broader. Pure Townsend's Warblers breed north and at higher elevations; pure Hermit Warblers breed to the south and at lower elevations in southern Oregon [P21].

**Dynamics:** The hybrid zone is moving southward. Townsend's Warbler males are behaviorally dominant — they are more aggressive in territorial disputes, arrive earlier on breeding territories, and in mixed-species encounters, Townsend's males consistently displace Hermit males. This behavioral asymmetry drives the directional movement of the hybrid zone. The rate of southward advance is estimated at 0.3-1.0 km per decade, based on comparison of historical and contemporary phenotype distributions [P22].

**Genetic introgression:** Townsend's Warbler mitochondrial DNA has introgressed far south of the plumage contact zone — Hermit Warbler phenotype birds in southern Oregon may carry Townsend's mitochondria, indicating that hybridization has been occurring for thousands of years and that Townsend's genetic material has been "captured" by Hermit Warbler populations. However, nuclear genomic divergence between the species is maintained despite introgression, suggesting strong selection on plumage genes even as neutral markers flow freely across the zone [P21].

**Ecological implications:** If current displacement trends continue, Hermit Warbler's breeding range may eventually be restricted to southern Oregon and northern California. This would represent a case of speciation reversal — two species that diverged during Pleistocene glaciations re-merging through competitive hybridization. The dynamic is not a simple case of habitat loss driving decline; it is intrinsic competitive superiority of one species over its close relative.

**Conservation relevance:** Hermit Warbler's population is one-sixth the size of Townsend's (4 million vs. 26 million). While not currently threatened, the long-term prognosis involves progressive range contraction from the north. Climate change may alter the dynamics if it shifts competitive balance or habitat suitability differentially for the two species. The hybrid zone serves as a natural experiment in real-time speciation and competitive exclusion [P21, P22].

### Evolutionary Note

Hermit Warbler diverged from Townsend's Warbler approximately 200,000-500,000 years ago during Pleistocene glacial fragmentation of Pacific Coast forests. The two species, along with Black-throated Green Warbler of eastern North America and Golden-cheeked Warbler of Texas, form the *virens* superspecies complex within *Setophaga*. All four species are canopy-dwelling insectivores of mature conifer or mixed forest — a remarkable case of ecological conservatism across multiple speciation events. The Hermit Warbler's bright yellow face without dark auricular markings may represent a derived loss of the ancestral black cheek pattern retained by Townsend's and Black-throated Green, or alternatively, the ancestral condition with black cheek evolving independently in the northern species [P23].

## Wilson's Warbler (*Cardellina pusilla*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern (Pacific population stable)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 10-12 cm
- **Wingspan:** 14-17 cm
- **Mass:** 6-9 g
- **Plumage:** Bright olive-green above, bright yellow below including face and underparts. Male with distinctive round black cap; female cap reduced or absent. Beady dark eye on plain yellow face. No wing bars, no tail spots. One of the smallest PNW warblers.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Slight. Male has full black cap; female has reduced olive or absent cap.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense shrubby understory along streams, wetland edges, alder thickets, avalanche chutes, and shrubby subalpine meadow margins. Unlike most PNW warblers, avoids interior of closed-canopy forest. Requires dense, low vegetation (0.5-3 m height) near water.
- **Wintering:** Mexico and Central America, lowland to mid-elevation scrub and forest edges.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (breeding)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 2 (Montane — shrubby openings)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 3, 4
- **Microhabitat:** Dense shrub layer, typically within 3 m of ground in wet or mesic habitats.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders (caterpillars, beetles, flies, aphids)
- **Foraging strategy:** Active gleaner in low shrubs and dense vegetation, frequently hover-gleaning undersides of leaves. Sallies for flying insects. Forages lower in vegetation than most PNW warblers, primarily in 0.5-3 m height band.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of leaves, grass, moss, bark strips
- **Nest location:** On or near ground in dense shrub cover, often at base of shrub or on mossy bank
- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs (typically 5)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.7% annually for western populations) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 40 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Wintering habitat loss in Latin American lowlands. Deer browse reducing shrub understory in some breeding areas. Climate-driven changes in shrub phenology.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rapid descending chatter: *chi-chi-chi-chi-chi-chet-chet*. Accelerates and drops in pitch. Loud for body size.
- **Calls:** Flat *chimp* or *jit*.

### Evolutionary Note

Wilson's Warbler was recently moved from *Wilsonia* to *Cardellina* based on molecular phylogenetics, placing it closer to Red-faced Warbler (*C. rubrifrons*) and Red Warbler (*C. rubra*) of Mexico than to the *Setophaga* warblers it superficially resembles. Pacific coast (*chryseola*), Rocky Mountain (*pileolata*), and boreal (*pusilla*) populations show significant genetic structure, suggesting possible future taxonomic splitting. The Pacific coast form breeding in the PNW is brightest yellow with the most extensive black cap [P23, O3].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Breeding (shrub edges) | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Breeding (openings, streams) | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Migration | Common | Visitor |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding (riparian) | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

### Migration

- **Fall departure:** Late August to September (one of the earlier-departing warblers)
- **Wintering grounds:** Mexico through Panama
- **Distance:** Medium to long-distance migrant; 2,000-5,000 km

## Orange-crowned Warbler (*Leiothlypis celata*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 17-19 cm
- **Mass:** 7-11 g
- **Plumage:** Dull olive-green overall — the most uniformly plain warbler in the PNW. Faint blurry streaking on breast, indistinct pale supercilium and broken eye-ring, yellowish undertail coverts (often the brightest yellow on the bird). The orange crown patch is rarely visible in the field (usually concealed, erected only during aggressive displays). Pacific coast subspecies (*lutescens*) is the brightest, most yellow of the four North American subspecies.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Males slightly brighter with marginally more visible crown patch.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Brushy hillsides, chaparral, shrubby forest openings, riparian thickets, second-growth edges. Favors early-successional and edge habitats rather than dense forest interior. Common in salal, ocean spray, and snowberry thickets.
- **Wintering:** Southern OR/CA coastal scrub southward; a few overwinter in mild PNW lowlands.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 6,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest edges), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 2, 5
- **Microhabitat:** Dense deciduous and mixed shrub layer, 1-5 m height.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects, spiders, nectar (probes flowers), fruit
- **Foraging strategy:** Active leaf gleaner in shrubs and low trees. Distinctive behavior of piercing the base of tubular flowers and robbing nectar — a form of nectar larceny that avoids pollination. Also probes leaf clusters and bark crevices.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominate in breeding season; fruit and nectar increase in fall-winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Open cup of bark strips, grass, plant fibers, lined with hair and fine grass
- **Nest location:** On or near ground, concealed in dense vegetation, often on steep hillside or cut bank
- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 10-13 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-July (one of the earliest-breeding PNW warblers)

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 82 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low overall concern. Some local breeding habitat loss from development.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Simple musical trill that typically fades at end: *see-see-see-see-see-si-si*. Loose and musical, less buzzy than most warblers. Variable across range.
- **Calls:** Sharp metallic *tik*.

### Evolutionary Note

Orange-crowned Warbler was recently reclassified from *Vermivora* to *Leiothlypis* based on molecular evidence revealing it is more closely related to Tennessee and Nashville Warblers than to the Blue-winged/Golden-winged complex that retained the *Vermivora* name. Four subspecies show a clinal east-west brightening trend: the Pacific coast *lutescens* is brightest (greenish-yellow), the interior *orestera* is intermediate, and the boreal *celata* is dullest (grayish-olive). The Catalina Island subspecies *sordida* is the largest and darkest [P23, O3].

## MacGillivray's Warbler (*Geothlypis tolmiei*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-20 cm
- **Mass:** 9-13 g
- **Plumage:** Male: slate-gray hood covering head and upper breast with bold white crescents above and below eye (broken eye-ring), olive-green upperparts, bright yellow underparts from lower breast to undertail. Female: paler gray hood, more olive tones, white crescents less distinct but present.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male has darker, blacker hood; female grayer and less contrasting.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense shrubby undergrowth in montane forest openings, avalanche chutes, clear-cut regrowth, stream-side thickets, and burn regeneration areas. Strongly associated with dense cover 0.5-3 m in height. One of the most skulking PNW warblers.
- **Wintering:** Mexico to Panama in shrubby undergrowth and forest edges.
- **Elevation range:** 500-7,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — openings)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 4, 5
- **Microhabitat:** Dense shrub thickets, especially snowberry, thimbleberry, ocean spray, and regenerating conifers. Rarely forages higher than 3 m.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders
- **Foraging strategy:** Deliberate low-shrub gleaner. Hops through dense understory, gleaning insects from leaves and stems. Rarely flies more than a few meters. Ground-level foraging more frequent than most warblers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of dry grass, bark strips, weed stems
- **Nest location:** Low in dense shrub, 0.5-1.5 m above ground, well concealed
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.4% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 19 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Wintering habitat loss. Some breeding habitat loss where fire suppression converts open shrubby forest to closed canopy.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rolling, buzzy series: *CHEE-chee-chee-chee-CHER-cher*. Louder and more emphatic than most skulking warblers. Song carries well through dense vegetation.
- **Calls:** Hard *tsik*, sharper than most warbler calls.

### Evolutionary Note

MacGillivray's Warbler was formerly classified in *Oporornis* along with Mourning and Connecticut Warblers, but molecular phylogenetics placed it firmly in *Geothlypis* with Common Yellowthroat. Despite their striking plumage similarity, MacGillivray's and Mourning Warblers are not each other's closest relative — convergent plumage evolution in dense undergrowth habitats produced similar gray-hooded, yellow-bellied morphotypes independently. The two species hybridize very rarely in the Great Plains where their ranges approach [P23, O3].

## Nashville Warbler (*Leiothlypis ruficapilla*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-12 cm
- **Wingspan:** 17-19 cm
- **Mass:** 7-11 g
- **Plumage:** Gray head with bold white eye-ring, olive-green back, bright yellow underparts from throat through undertail coverts, no wing bars. Rufous crown patch concealed (rarely visible in the field). Western populations (*ridgwayi*) average brighter yellow on breast and more contrasting gray head than eastern populations.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Slight. Male has slightly grayer head and brighter yellow underparts.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open mixed woodland, second-growth forests with shrubby understory, brushy clearings, chaparral, and regenerating burns. In the PNW, commonly found in dry mixed conifer with manzanita or ceanothus understory, especially on the east slope of the Cascades and in the Siskiyou Mountains.
- **Wintering:** Mexico, primarily western slope pine-oak forests.
- **Elevation range:** 1,000-6,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 2 (Montane — east side)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (east-side riparian edges)
- **Microhabitat:** Shrubby understory of open woodland; manzanita, ceanothus, and oak-scrub associations.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders
- **Foraging strategy:** Active gleaner in shrubs and lower canopy, hovering and probing leaf clusters. Occasionally descends to ground.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of bark strips, grass, moss, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground concealed under dense vegetation, often at base of shrub or fern on hillside
- **Clutch size:** 4-5 eggs
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 10-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.2% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 36 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Breeding habitat relatively secure in PNW.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Two-part song: rapid series of high notes followed by a lower trill: *SEE-bit SEE-bit SEE-bit titititi*. Western birds (*ridgwayi*) sing a distinctly different song type than eastern birds — potential future split criterion.
- **Calls:** Metallic *pink* or *tink*.

### Evolutionary Note

Nashville Warbler eastern (*ruficapilla*) and western (*ridgwayi*) populations are genetically and vocally distinct. Western birds sing a fundamentally different song, have brighter plumage, and show a more bobbing tail behavior. Some authorities have proposed splitting them as separate species. Like Orange-crowned Warbler, Nashville was moved from *Vermivora* to *Leiothlypis* based on molecular phylogenetic evidence [P23, O3].

## Black-throated Gray Warbler (*Setophaga nigrescens*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 17-20 cm
- **Mass:** 7-10 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: bold black-and-white head pattern with black crown, black cheek patch bordered by white supercilium and white malar, black throat and upper breast, gray back with black streaking, white underparts with black flank streaking, two white wing bars, tiny yellow spot before eye (loral spot). Female: similar but grayer, reduced black on throat, replaced partially by white.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male has solid black throat; female has mixed gray and white throat.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dry mixed woodland, particularly oak-conifer associations, juniper woodland, madrone-Douglas-fir, manzanita-chaparral, and open ponderosa pine. The quintessential warbler of dry PNW woodland. Avoids wet dense forests preferred by Townsend's and Hermit Warblers.
- **Wintering:** Mexico and southwestern US in oak-pine woodland.
- **Elevation range:** 500-5,500 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 2, 7 (juniper edges)
- **Microhabitat:** Mid-canopy of open woodland, especially where oaks mix with conifers or in juniper.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and spiders (caterpillars, beetles, leafhoppers, true bugs, spiders)
- **Foraging strategy:** Methodical foliage gleaner in mid-canopy, probing leaf clusters and bark crevices. Less active and more deliberate than Townsend's Warbler. Frequently forages in oaks when available.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact cup of plant fibers, grass, spider silk, lined with feathers and hair
- **Nest location:** In tree or large shrub, 3-35 ft up, typically on horizontal branch
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.1% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 8 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern overall. Oak woodland loss in Willamette Valley and southern OR reduces optimal habitat. Juniper encroachment into sagebrush creates some new habitat but at the cost of steppe species.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Buzzy rising series: *zee-zee-zee-zee-ZEET*. Superficially similar to Townsend's Warbler but typically slower and with less tonal variation.
- **Calls:** Dull *tup*, softer than most *Setophaga* chip notes.

### Evolutionary Note

Black-throated Gray Warbler is a member of the *virens* complex of *Setophaga* but occupies the dry-woodland niche rather than the wet conifer niche of Townsend's and Hermit. Its black-and-white plumage with minimal yellow (only the tiny loral spot) is unique in the complex and may represent adaptation to open, sun-exposed habitats where bright yellow would increase predation visibility. The yellow loral spot is interpreted as a vestigial remnant of the ancestral yellow plumage shared by other *virens*-complex members [P23].

| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

## Yellow Warbler (*Setophaga petechia*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 16-20 cm
- **Mass:** 8-13 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: entirely bright yellow with reddish-chestnut streaking on breast and flanks — the most uniformly yellow bird in the PNW. Olive-yellow back, yellow wing edgings (no true wing bars), dark eye prominent on yellow face, yellow tail spots visible in flight. Female: similar but paler yellow, breast streaking faint or absent.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male brighter with bold chestnut breast streaking; female duller, less streaked.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Riparian thickets of willow, alder, and cottonwood; wetland shrub margins; second-growth deciduous woodland; urban parks and gardens with shrubs near water. Quintessential riparian warbler.
- **Wintering:** Mexico through South America in lowland shrub habitats.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (primarily below 3,000 ft in PNW)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 3, 7 (riparian corridors)
- **Microhabitat:** Deciduous shrub and low tree canopy in riparian zones, 1-8 m height.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (caterpillars — especially spruce budworm and tent caterpillars — beetles, damselflies, mosquitoes, spiders)
- **Foraging strategy:** Active gleaner in shrub canopy and outer branches. Hover-gleans from leaf undersides. Occasionally sallies for aerial insects.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact cup of plant fibers, grass, spider silk, lined with plant down and fine grass
- **Nest location:** In shrub or small tree fork, 3-12 ft up, usually in willow or alder
- **Clutch size:** 4-5 eggs
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1 (occasionally 2)
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 90 million (North America) — among the most abundant warblers [O4]
- **Threats:** Riparian habitat degradation from livestock grazing, water diversion, and development. Significant host for Brown-headed Cowbird brood parasitism — Yellow Warblers are one of the few warbler species known to regularly recognize and reject cowbird eggs by burying them under a new nest floor.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Bright, cheerful phrase: *sweet-sweet-sweet-I'm-so-sweet!* or *sweet-sweet-sweet-ti-ti-SWEE*. One of the most recognizable warbler songs in PNW riparian areas.
- **Calls:** Soft *chip*, less sharp than most *Setophaga*.

### Evolutionary Note

Yellow Warbler is the most widespread *Setophaga* species in the Western Hemisphere, breeding from Alaska to Mexico. The northern migratory group (*aestiva*) breeds across North America; two largely resident groups (*erithachorides* of mangroves and *petechia* of Caribbean islands) have been considered for species-level splitting. Within the PNW, the subspecies *morcomi* breeds throughout the region. The species' response to brood parasitism — burying cowbird eggs beneath a new nest layer, sometimes resulting in "tiered" nests 6 layers deep — is a remarkable behavioral adaptation and a model system for studying coevolutionary arms races [O1, O3].

## Common Yellowthroat (*Geothlypis trichas*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 15-19 cm
- **Mass:** 9-13 g
- **Plumage:** Male: bold black face mask extending from bill through auriculars, bordered above by a narrow whitish-gray band, olive-green upperparts, bright yellow throat and upper breast, whitish to pale yellow lower belly, no wing bars. Female: olive-brown above, yellow throat, pale buffy below, no mask (occasionally faint dusky wash on lores).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Strong. Male's black mask is diagnostic; female lacks mask entirely.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense emergent marsh vegetation (cattails, bulrushes, sedges), shrubby wetland margins, dense riparian thickets, overgrown fields with standing water or saturated soil. The most aquatic-associated of PNW warblers.
- **Wintering:** Southern US through Central America in marshes and wet thickets. A few overwinter in mild PNW lowland marshes.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (primarily lowland)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 3, 7 (irrigated marshes)
- **Microhabitat:** Dense emergent vegetation 0.5-2 m height, typically with standing water beneath.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, caterpillars, damselflies, dragonflies, grasshoppers, spiders, small snails)
- **Foraging strategy:** Low gleaner in marsh vegetation. Hops through cattails and sedges near water surface. Occasionally gleans from mud or water surface.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round; occasional seeds in winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of grass, sedge, and leaves, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** Low in dense marsh vegetation, 0.1-1 m above water or wet ground
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-10 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.2% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 77 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Wetland drainage and degradation. Heavy Brown-headed Cowbird parasitism in some areas.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Loud, rolling *WITCHITY-witchity-witchity*. Unmistakable and carries well across open marshes. Variable; 2-5 repetitions per phrase.
- **Calls:** Harsh *tchat* or *chek*.

### Evolutionary Note

Common Yellowthroat is the most widespread *Geothlypis* species and one of the most geographically variable warblers in North America, with 13 recognized subspecies showing extensive variation in facial mask extent, yellow intensity, and body size. PNW populations (*occidentalis*) have brighter yellow and broader mask border than eastern forms. The genus *Geothlypis* now includes the formerly separate *Oporornis* warblers (MacGillivray's, Mourning, Connecticut) based on molecular phylogenetics [P23, O3].

## Yellow-breasted Chat (*Icteria virens*)

**Family:** Icteriidae (recently split from Parulidae)
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; WDFW Species of Concern (WA); PIF Yellow Watch List

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 17-19 cm
- **Wingspan:** 23-27 cm
- **Mass:** 20-34 g — by far the largest "warbler," more than twice the mass of any Parulidae
- **Plumage:** Olive-green above, bright yellow throat and breast, white belly, heavy black bill (unlike any warbler), bold white spectacles (eye-ring and loral stripe), long tail. Sexes similar; male slightly brighter.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Slight. Male marginally brighter yellow.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense, shrubby thickets in riparian corridors and regenerating clearcuts — particularly wild rose, blackberry, willow, and hawthorn tangles. Requires extensive patches of dense, low-to-medium height shrub cover (1-3 m). Avoids both open ground and closed forest canopy.
- **Wintering:** Mexico and Central America in dense scrub.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 3,500 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 7 (east-side riparian)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (regenerating areas)
- **Microhabitat:** Interior of dense shrub thickets, 1-3 m height. Requires large patches (>2 ha) of contiguous shrub cover.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, caterpillars, grasshoppers, ants, wasps), berries (blackberry, elderberry, grape), occasional small vertebrates
- **Foraging strategy:** Heavy-billed gleaner and harvester in dense shrub. More omnivorous than true warblers. Berry consumption substantial in late summer and fall.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominant May-July; fruits increase August-September.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of bark strips, leaves, grass, lined with fine grass
- **Nest location:** In dense shrub, 1-3 m above ground
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining in western Washington (BBS 1966-2019: -1.2% annually regionally, though some local recovery) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 9 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Riparian habitat degradation, succession of early-successional habitat to closed canopy, Brown-headed Cowbird parasitism. Listed as Species of Concern in Washington due to localized breeding population decline and habitat loss in Puget Trough [G9].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Extraordinary repertoire of whistles, cackles, gurgles, mews, and chattering phrases — unlike any other PNW bird. Often sings at night. Display flight involves the male rising above shrub cover, dangling legs, and singing while floating down — highly unusual for a passerine. Individual males may have 40+ distinct syllable types.
- **Calls:** Harsh *chak*, nasal *myah*, and a variety of scolding notes.

### Evolutionary Note

The Yellow-breasted Chat's taxonomic placement has been debated for over a century. Its large size, heavy bill, aberrant vocalizations, and distinct behavior were always anomalous for Parulidae. Molecular phylogenetics finally resolved the question: the chat is not a warbler at all. It was placed in its own monotypic family Icteriidae in 2017 by AOS, confirmed as sister to Icteridae (blackbirds and orioles) rather than Parulidae. Despite its superficially warbler-like plumage, it is an ancient lineage that diverged from the warbler/blackbird common ancestor early in the radiation of the nine-primaried oscines. It is included in this module for historical and practical reasons — most field guides still treat it alongside warblers [P27, O3].

## American Redstart (*Setophaga ruticilla*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding (localized in PNW)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 11-13 cm
- **Wingspan:** 16-19 cm
- **Mass:** 6-11 g
- **Plumage:** Adult male: jet black upperparts and head with vivid orange patches on wings, tail sides, and flanks — a striking visual unlike any other PNW warbler. White belly. First-year male: olive-gray replacing black, yellow replacing orange (takes two years to acquire full adult male plumage — unusual among warblers). Female: olive-gray above, white below with yellow patches where male is orange.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Strong. Adult male strikingly black-and-orange; female olive-and-yellow.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Deciduous and mixed riparian woodland, especially cottonwood, alder, and maple forests with well-developed understory. In the PNW, breeding is localized primarily in eastern Washington and Oregon riparian corridors and northeastern Washington forests. Uncommon to rare west of the Cascades.
- **Wintering:** Central America, Caribbean, and northern South America in forest and scrub.
- **Elevation range:** 500-4,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian — eastern PNW)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (east-side montane riparian)
- **Microhabitat:** Mid-canopy of deciduous trees, frequently in dappled sunlight at forest edges.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Flying insects (diptera, lepidoptera, small hymenoptera), spiders, caterpillars
- **Foraging strategy:** Distinctive active foraging style — fans tail and droops wings to flash orange/yellow patches, startling insects into flight, then pursuing them with agile aerial sallies. One of the most acrobatic foragers among warblers. The tail-fanning behavior is unique among North American wood-warblers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous during breeding; adds small fruits on wintering grounds.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact cup of bark strips, grass, plant down, spider silk
- **Nest location:** In tree fork, 5-25 ft up, in deciduous tree
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-9 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually continent-wide; PNW populations small but stable) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 24 million (North America); PNW is western range periphery [O4]
- **Threats:** Riparian habitat loss in eastern PNW. The PNW population is at the western extreme of the species' range and may be limited by habitat connectivity.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** High, thin, accented series: *TSEEta TSEEta TSEEta* or *tsi-tsi-tsi-tsi-TSEE*. Variable; males sing multiple song types. Songs are thinner and higher-pitched than most PNW warblers.
- **Calls:** Thin *chip*, sharper and higher than *Setophaga* congeners.

### Evolutionary Note

American Redstart is the sole member of the genus *Setophaga* in many older classifications — the genus was originally erected for this species alone before molecular work merged the former *Dendroica* warblers into *Setophaga*. The redstart's basal phylogenetic position within *Setophaga*, combined with its unique morphology (broad, flat bill; fan-shaped tail with large patches), delayed plumage maturation (two-year male maturation — first-year males in female-like plumage can breed), and distinctive flycatching foraging behavior, marks it as an ancient lineage within the radiation. Its closest relative is not another North American warbler but rather the Slate-throated Redstart (*Myioborus miniatus*) complex of tropical montane forests [P23].

## Northern Waterthrush (*Parkesia noveboracensis*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Breeding (localized), transient
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 13-15 cm
- **Wingspan:** 21-24 cm
- **Mass:** 13-22 g
- **Plumage:** Dark olive-brown above, creamy-white to yellowish below with bold dark streaking across breast and flanks. Prominent pale supercilium (buffy-white), dark eye line. Constantly teeters and bobs tail while walking — a behavior shared with wagtails and dippers (convergent evolution with other aquatic-associated passerines). Sexes alike.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** None apparent in the field.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Wooded swamps, bogs, and margins of slow-moving streams and ponds with dense ground cover. In PNW, breeding confined to northeastern Washington and northern Idaho in spruce-bog and alder-swamp habitats. Primarily a passage migrant through most of the region.
- **Wintering:** Central America, Caribbean, and northern South America in mangroves and forested wetlands.
- **Elevation range:** 1,500-4,500 ft (PNW breeding); sea level to 3,000 ft (migration)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland — northeastern PNW breeding)
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level along shaded stream banks and bog margins. Walks on substrates (logs, mud, mossy banks) rather than hopping through vegetation.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Aquatic and terrestrial invertebrates (insect larvae, beetles, flies, snails, small crustaceans, worms)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground walker along stream margins and bog edges. Probes wet substrate, leaf litter, and shallow water edges. Behavior more reminiscent of a small sandpiper than a warbler — convergent adaptation to aquatic foraging niche.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round; diet shifts from terrestrial insects to more aquatic invertebrates in wetland wintering habitats.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of moss, leaves, bark strips, lined with fine rootlets and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in upturned root mass, typically concealed under overhanging moss or vegetation along stream bank
- **Clutch size:** 4-5 eggs
- **Incubation:** 12-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-10 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.1% annually continent-wide) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 12 million (North America); PNW is western range periphery [O4]
- **Threats:** Wetland degradation on breeding grounds. Mangrove loss on wintering grounds. PNW breeding populations are small and isolated.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Loud, ringing series of accelerating notes ending in a descending flourish: *SWEET sweet sweet swi-swi-swi chew-chew*. Carries well across water. Sings from exposed perch (log, stump) near water.
- **Calls:** Loud, sharp *chink* — distinctly metallic.

### Evolutionary Note

Northern Waterthrush and Louisiana Waterthrush were split from *Seiurus* into the resurrected genus *Parkesia* based on molecular phylogenetics. Despite their "waterthrush" name and superficial resemblance to Old World wagtails (*Motacilla*), they are true warblers (Parulidae). The tail-bobbing behavior shared with wagtails, dippers, and sandpipers is a textbook case of convergent evolution — unrelated birds in aquatic habitats independently evolved similar locomotor behaviors, possibly to startle aquatic prey or maintain visual contact with moving water [P23, O3].

## Ovenbird (*Seiurus aurocapilla*)

**Family:** Parulidae
**Status:** Rare breeder (northeastern PNW periphery), vagrant
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern (range-wide)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 14-16 cm
- **Wingspan:** 22-26 cm
- **Mass:** 16-26 g
- **Plumage:** Olive-green above, white below with bold black streaking on breast and flanks forming a "necklace" pattern. Bold orange-rufous crown stripe bordered by dark lateral crown stripes (distinctive at close range). Bold white eye-ring. Pink legs. Walks (does not hop) on forest floor. Sexes alike.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** None apparent.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Interior of mature deciduous and mixed forest with closed canopy and deep leaf litter. In the PNW, breeding is extremely localized in northeastern Washington (Spokane area, Pend Oreille County) and northern Idaho where suitable mixed deciduous forest exists. A characteristic species of eastern deciduous forests — the PNW represents the far western range margin.
- **Wintering:** Central America and Caribbean in forest understory.
- **Elevation range:** 1,500-4,000 ft (PNW breeding)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane — mixed deciduous stands, NE WA/ID)
- **Microhabitat:** Forest floor with deep leaf litter under closed canopy.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Terrestrial invertebrates (beetles, ants, caterpillars, earthworms, snails, spiders)
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks on forest floor, flipping leaf litter to expose invertebrates. Rarely ascends above ground level. Behavior convergent with thrushes and antpittas.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous year-round.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Domed structure on ground resembling a miniature Dutch oven (hence the common name) — built of dead leaves and grass with side entrance
- **Nest location:** On forest floor in deep leaf litter, usually near a small opening
- **Clutch size:** 4-5 eggs
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.5% annually range-wide) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 26 million (North America); PNW population negligible [O4]
- **Threats:** Forest fragmentation (area-sensitive — requires large unfragmented forest blocks). Nest predation increases with fragmentation. Cowbird parasitism in fragmented forests.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Emphatic, loud, ringing *TEACHER TEACHER TEACHER TEACHER* — crescendo of two-syllable phrases increasing in volume. One of the loudest passerine songs per unit body mass. Audible at 250+ m. Also sings a complex flight song at dawn and dusk — a rambling warble delivered during display flights above the canopy.
- **Calls:** Sharp *tschuk*.

### Evolutionary Note

Ovenbird is the sole member of genus *Seiurus* after the waterthrushes were moved to *Parkesia*. Its domed ground nest is unique among North American warblers and convergent with the true ovenbirds (Furnariidae) of South America, for which it was named — a case of independent evolution of similar nest architecture on different continents in unrelated bird families. The species is strongly area-sensitive, requiring forest blocks >100 ha for successful breeding, making it an indicator of forest tract size and integrity [P23, O3].

# FAMILY CARDINALIDAE — Cardinals, Tanagers, and Grosbeaks

**Order:** Passeriformes
**Family:** Cardinalidae (53 species worldwide; 3 breeding regularly in PNW)
**Phylogenetic position:** Cardinalidae is a New World family within the nine-primaried oscines, sister to Thraupidae (tanagers sensu stricto). The family's common name is misleading — it includes not only cardinals but also several species formerly classified as tanagers (including Western Tanager) and grosbeaks (including Black-headed and Rose-breasted). Molecular phylogenetics in the 2000s-2010s dramatically rearranged tanager and cardinal relationships, revealing that Western Tanager is not closely related to the Neotropical tanagers (Thraupidae) despite being called a "tanager" — it is a true cardinalid [P27].

**PNW significance:** Western Tanager is among the most visually striking PNW breeding birds and an iconic species of open montane conifer forests. Black-headed Grosbeak and Lazuli Bunting represent the cardinalid contribution to PNW riparian and woodland breeding communities. All three species are Neotropical migrants.

---

## Western Tanager (*Piranga ludoviciana*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Cardinalidae (not Thraupidae — see Evolutionary Note)
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 16-19 cm
- **Wingspan:** 28-30 cm
- **Mass:** 24-36 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: one of the most visually striking PNW birds — brilliant red-orange head, bright yellow body, jet black back, wings, and tail, two wing bars (upper yellow, lower white). The red head pigment is rhodoxanthin, a rare carotenoid not synthesized by the bird — it is obtained from insects in the diet, making male head color a direct indicator of foraging success and habitat quality. Non-breeding male: red fades to yellow-orange. Female: olive-green above, yellow-green below with grayish wings and two wing bars; lacks red.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Strong. Breeding male vividly tricolored (red-yellow-black); female muted olive-yellow.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open coniferous and mixed forests, especially ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, and mixed conifer with open canopy at mid elevations. The quintessential warbler-tanager of east-side and south-side montane forests. Also common in west-side montane where canopy is not too dense. Prefers forest with 40-70% canopy closure — open enough for aerial sallying but with sufficient tree density for nesting.
- **Wintering:** Mexico and Central America in pine-oak forests and forest edges, 3,000-8,000 ft elevation.
- **Elevation range:** 1,000-7,000 ft (breeding); primarily 2,000-5,500 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 4, 7 (woodland edges)
- **Microhabitat:** Upper canopy of open conifer forest; frequently uses exposed snag tops as singing and sallying perches.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (wasps, bees, beetles, caterpillars, grasshoppers, dragonflies, cicadas), supplemented with fruit (cherries, elderberry, hawthorn)
- **Foraging strategy:** Deliberate canopy gleaner and aerial sallier. Perches on exposed branch and sallies out to catch large flying insects — a foraging style more reminiscent of a flycatcher than a typical tanager. Also gleans insects from bark and foliage. Fruit consumption increases in late summer and during migration.
- **Seasonal variation:** Primarily insectivorous May-July; frugivory increases August-September and dominates winter diet.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Loosely constructed shallow cup of twigs, rootlets, grass, lined with rootlets and hair — often surprisingly flimsy for the bird's size
- **Nest location:** On horizontal conifer branch, typically 15-65 ft up, well out from trunk in upper canopy
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 11-15 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July (males arrive 1-2 weeks before females)

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.3% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 11 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Some localized habitat modification from fire suppression converting open pine forest to dense canopy. Wintering habitat loss in Mexican pine-oak forests.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Robin-like series of hoarse, burry phrases: *CHEER-up CHEER-o CHEER-ee*. Similar in cadence to American Robin but rougher, huskier quality — sometimes described as "a robin with a sore throat." Individual males sing 3-6 phrase types.
- **Calls:** Rising, burry *pri-dit* or *pit-a-rik*. Distinctive rising two-note call frequently given in flight.

### Evolutionary Note

Despite its common name "tanager," Western Tanager is not a true tanager (Thraupidae). Molecular phylogenetics revealed that the four North American *Piranga* species (Western, Scarlet, Summer, Hepatic) are cardinalids, more closely related to cardinals, grosbeaks, and buntings than to the Neotropical tanager radiation. This reclassification, formalized by AOS in 2008, illustrates how convergent plumage evolution — bright carotenoid-based coloration in frugivorous/insectivorous birds — can mislead taxonomists relying on morphology alone. The red pigment in Western Tanager's head is rhodoxanthin, a carotenoid pigment extremely rare among birds. Unlike most red-plumaged birds that use ketocarotenoids synthesized metabolically from dietary yellow carotenoids, Western Tanagers acquire rhodoxanthin directly from their insect prey (particularly sawfly larvae that feed on conifers containing rhodoxanthin). This dietary pigment pathway is unique among North American passerines [P27, O3, O1].

- **Fall departure:** August-September
- **Wintering grounds:** Mexico and Central America, pine-oak forests
- **Distance:** Medium to long-distance migrant; 2,000-5,000 km

## Black-headed Grosbeak (*Pheucticus melanocephalus*)

**Family:** Cardinalidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 18-21 cm
- **Wingspan:** 31-33 cm
- **Mass:** 35-49 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: black head, cinnamon-orange breast and collar, black back with white streaking, black wings with bold white patches and wing bars, yellow wing linings (visible in flight). Large conical pale bill. Female: buffy-brown with dark streaking on back, buffy breast with fine streaking, bold white supercilium, two wing bars. Non-breeding male retains pattern but duller.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Strong. Male black-headed with orange breast; female brown-streaked like a large sparrow.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Deciduous and mixed riparian woodland, oak-conifer woodlands, suburban areas with large deciduous trees. Common at bird feeders in wooded suburbs. Prefers habitats with both tall trees (singing perches, canopy foraging) and dense understory (nesting).
- **Wintering:** Mexico in woodland and edge habitats.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 6,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 2, 3, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Mid-canopy of deciduous and mixed broadleaf-conifer woodland; uses both canopy and understory.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, caterpillars, wasps), seeds, fruit, buds. One of the few PNW passerines known to readily eat Monarch butterflies — Black-headed Grosbeaks appear tolerant of milkweed cardenolide toxins.
- **Foraging strategy:** Canopy and mid-story gleaner. Uses heavy conical bill to crack seeds and extract insect larvae from bark. Readily visits sunflower seed feeders.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominate during breeding; seeds and fruit increase in fall.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Loose, bulky cup of twigs, rootlets, pine needles, and plant stems — often so loosely constructed that eggs are visible from below through the nest floor
- **Nest location:** In tree or large shrub, 4-25 ft up, typically at fork of deciduous branch
- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days (both sexes — male incubates and sings from nest, unusual among songbirds)
- **Fledging:** 11-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.2% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 14 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Adapts well to suburban habitats with mature trees.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rich, fluid, robin-like warbling — one of the most beautiful songs in PNW riparian woodlands. Faster, more liquid, and more continuous than American Robin. Males and females both sing (female song is less common in PNW songbirds). Males sing from nest during incubation — a rare behavior.
- **Calls:** Sharp, squeaky *ik* or *eek*.

### Evolutionary Note

Black-headed Grosbeak and Rose-breasted Grosbeak (*P. ludovicianus*) are sister species that diverged during Pleistocene glaciations — Black-headed in western North America, Rose-breasted in eastern. They hybridize extensively in the Great Plains (Nebraska through the Dakotas). Occasional Rose-breasted Grosbeaks and apparent hybrids appear in the PNW as rare vagrants. Both sexes in both species sing and incubate, a trait shared with no other North American Cardinalidae, suggesting the behavior evolved before their divergence. The male's singing while incubating may function in territory maintenance when the male is otherwise unable to patrol boundaries [P27, O1].

## Lazuli Bunting (*Passerina amoena*)

**Family:** Cardinalidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 13-15 cm
- **Wingspan:** 22-23 cm
- **Mass:** 13-18 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding male: brilliant cerulean-blue head, back, and rump (structural color, not pigment-based), cinnamon-orange breast band, white belly, two white wing bars. The blue is produced by nanostructured feather barbs that scatter light — if backlit, the male appears dark. Female: plain brown-gray above, warm buffy below with faint wing bars, no blue. First-year male: patchy blue and brown.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Extreme. Male vivid blue-orange-white; female plain brown.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Brushy hillsides, open shrub with scattered trees, riparian edges with dense low cover, agricultural margins, regenerating clearcuts and burns. Requires open habitat with scattered elevated song perches (fenceposts, shrub tops, small trees) overlooking dense shrub cover for nesting.
- **Wintering:** Mexico in brushy habitats.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 7 (shrub-steppe edges)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 3, 5
- **Microhabitat:** Ecotone between open ground and dense shrub. Song perches on exposed tops of shrubs or fenceposts.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weeds), insects (grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars, ants)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground and low-shrub forager. Hops on ground gleaning seeds; also gleans insects from low vegetation. More ground-oriented than most warblers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominant during breeding (higher protein for nestlings); seeds dominate fall-winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact cup of grass, bark strips, weed stems, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** Low in dense shrub, 1-4 ft above ground, well concealed
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1 (occasionally 2)
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.3% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 14 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern overall. Some habitat loss from agricultural intensification and fire suppression.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rapid, high-pitched warbling series of paired phrases: *see-see CHEW-chew zee-zee tew-tew*. Each male develops a unique song by selectively copying elements from neighboring males heard during the first breeding season — a cultural learning process that produces neighborhood song "dialects" that shift over time. This song-learning mechanism has been a model system for understanding cultural evolution in birds.
- **Calls:** Thin, dry *pit*.

### Evolutionary Note

Lazuli Bunting and Indigo Bunting (*P. cyanea*) are sister species that diverged during Pleistocene glaciations — Lazuli in western North America, Indigo in eastern. They hybridize extensively on the Great Plains (Nebraska, the Dakotas, Montana). The hybrid zone is one of the best-studied in North American ornithology. Male Lazuli Buntings learn songs culturally from neighbors, resulting in rapidly evolving local dialects — a rare example of cultural transmission dominating song development in a passerine. First-year males actively avoid copying the songs of established territorial males who are their immediate neighbors, instead sampling from a wider neighborhood and combining elements into novel songs — a strategy interpreted as reducing acoustic similarity that might trigger aggression from territory-holding adults [P27, O1].

# FAMILY PASSERELLIDAE — New World Sparrows, Towhees, and Juncos

**Order:** Passeriformes
**Family:** Passerellidae (138 species worldwide; ~20 breeding or regularly wintering in PNW)
**Phylogenetic position:** Passerellidae was elevated to full family rank from within Emberizidae based on molecular phylogenetics (Barker et al. 2015), recognizing New World sparrows as a distinct radiation separate from Old World buntings. The family includes sparrows, towhees, juncos, and several genera formerly scattered across Emberizidae. Passerellidae is sister to Icteridae (New World blackbirds) within the nine-primaried oscine assemblage, and more distantly related to Parulidae and Cardinalidae [P27, O3].

**PNW significance:** Sparrows and towhees are the dominant ground-level seed-and-insect foragers across virtually every PNW terrestrial habitat from alpine meadow to coastal salt marsh to sagebrush steppe. Song Sparrow alone may be the single most abundant breeding passerine across the lowland PNW west of the Cascades. The family's extraordinary subspecific diversity in the region — Song Sparrow has 6+ recognized subspecies in the PNW alone — makes it a primary system for studying geographic variation, local adaptation, and incipient speciation. Dark-eyed Junco and White-crowned Sparrow are among the most studied birds in North American behavioral ecology and neurobiology, with foundational research on song learning, photoperiodism, and hormonal regulation conducted on PNW populations [P24, P25].

## Song Sparrow (*Melospiza melodia*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Year-round resident (most PNW populations)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF score low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-17 cm (extraordinary size range driven by subspecific variation)
- **Wingspan:** 18-25 cm
- **Mass:** 12-53 g (the upper range represents the largest subspecies, *maxima*, from the Aleutians; PNW mainland birds typically 19-35 g)
- **Plumage:** Brown-streaked upperparts, whitish underparts with heavy brown streaking coalescing into a central breast spot (the "stickpin"). Broad grayish-brown supercilium, dark malar stripe, rounded tail often pumped in flight. PNW subspecific variation is striking: coastal birds (e.g., *morphallax*, *rufina*) are dark, heavily melanized, and large; interior birds (*merrilli*) are paler, grayer, and smaller; island populations show insular gigantism with *maxillaris* on Suisun Marsh and the massive Aleutian forms reaching nearly twice the body mass of continental populations.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Males average slightly larger but sexes indistinguishable in the field by plumage.

### Subspecific Diversity in the PNW

The Song Sparrow complex in the PNW represents one of the most spectacular examples of geographic variation in any North American bird. At least 6 subspecies occur regularly within the PNW study region, with additional forms in adjacent areas:

- ***morphallax*** — Pacific lowland form from southwestern BC through western WA and OR. Medium-large, dark brown with heavy rufous streaking. The default "Song Sparrow" of the Puget Sound lowlands, Willamette Valley, and coastal areas from southern BC to northern California. This is the most abundant subspecies in the PNW.
- ***rufina*** — Coastal BC and Alaska panhandle. Darker and more richly rufous than *morphallax*, with heavier bill. Grades into *morphallax* in southern BC. Associated with dense coastal vegetation and maritime influence.
- ***merrilli*** — Interior PNW from eastern WA and OR through Idaho and Montana. Notably paler and grayer than western forms, with sharper, thinner streaking on whiter underparts. Occupies riparian corridors through sagebrush and dry conifer zones. Partially migratory, unlike the sedentary coastal forms.
- ***maxillaris*** — Tidal marshes of the San Francisco Bay system, occasionally detected as a vagrant in southern OR. Distinctive dark, heavily melanized plumage with a notably large bill adapted for cracking invertebrate shells in marsh habitats. Represents a tidal marsh specialist lineage.
- ***insignis*** — Kodiak Island and adjacent Alaska coast. Very large, dark, and long-billed. Occasionally detected in BC coastal areas during winter dispersal.
- ***kenaiensis*** — Kenai Peninsula, Alaska. Similar to *insignis* but slightly paler. Winter records from coastal BC.

**Clinal patterns:** Song Sparrow subspecific variation follows Bergmann's Rule (larger body size at higher latitudes) and Gloger's Rule (darker pigmentation in more humid environments) with textbook precision. Coastal birds in the wet, mild maritime climate zone are dark and large; interior birds in the dry, continental climate are pale and small. Island populations (Aleutians, Channel Islands) exhibit insular gigantism. Aldrich (1984) documented continuous clinal gradients in 16 morphometric characters across the species' range, noting that the PNW is the epicenter of Song Sparrow morphological diversity, with more recognized subspecies occurring in the region from Alaska to California than in the entire remainder of the species' range combined [P29].

**Microgeographic differentiation:** Even within a single subspecies, Song Sparrow populations in the PNW show remarkable microgeographic structure. Chan and Arcese (2003) demonstrated that island populations in the southern Gulf Islands of BC, separated by as little as 1-5 km of water, show significant morphological and genetic differentiation — one of the finest-scale examples of population structure documented in a continental bird species [P32].

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense shrubby vegetation near water — riparian thickets, marsh edges, suburban gardens, brushy field borders, coastal scrub, blackberry tangles, and any moist habitat with dense ground-level or low-shrub cover. The most habitat-flexible sparrow in the PNW. From tidal salt marshes to montane meadow edges, Song Sparrows require only two elements: dense low cover for nesting and an open ground/water edge for foraging.
- **Wintering:** Same habitats as breeding, plus increased use of weedy fields, urban park edges, and feeder stations. Coastal populations largely sedentary; interior *merrilli* moves to lower elevations and westward.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (breeding); most abundant below 2,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland), Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 4, 6, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level to 3 m in dense shrub cover. Forages on bare ground, shorelines, mud, and leaf litter adjacent to cover.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and other invertebrates (breeding season: beetles, caterpillars, flies, ants, snails, earthworms); seeds and grain (fall-winter: grass seeds, weed seeds, waste grain)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner and scratcher. Hops along ground, flipping leaf litter with double-scratch (both feet simultaneously raking backward — the classic emberizid "towhee kick" scaled down). Gleans from low vegetation. Probes mud and shallow water edges. Uses "foot-trembling" to disturb prey in wet substrates. Readily visits ground feeders.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivory dominates May-August (~75% animal matter); granivory dominates October-March (~65% plant matter). The dietary flexibility mirrors the habitat flexibility.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of grass, bark strips, weed stems, and leaves, lined with fine grass, rootlets, and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in dense low shrub, 0-4 ft above ground. Ground nests concealed under overhanging vegetation. Occasionally higher in dense blackberry or other tangled shrubs.
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days (female only)
- **Fledging:** 9-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 2-3 (up to 4 attempts in PNW lowlands; one of the most prolific multi-brooded sparrows)
- **Breeding season:** March-August (extraordinarily long breeding season in mild PNW coastal climates; among the earliest-nesting passerines)
- **Brood parasitism:** Frequent host of Brown-headed Cowbird. Cowbird parasitism rates vary regionally: 20-50% in some interior PNW populations, lower on the coast where cowbird abundance is reduced.

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.2% annually, not statistically significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 130 million (North America); one of the continent's most abundant sparrows [O4]
- **Threats:** Low overall concern. Localized threats include loss of riparian and wetland shrub habitat, invasive plant monocultures replacing diverse native shrub, cowbird parasitism, and outdoor cat predation in suburban areas. Island and tidal marsh subspecies (e.g., *maxillaris*) face habitat-specific threats from sea-level rise and marsh degradation.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Begins with 2-4 clear introductory notes followed by a complex buzzy trill and variable terminal flourish: *sweet-sweet-sweet — buzzz trrrrr chip-chip*. Highly variable among individuals — each male sings 6-20 distinct song types delivered in "eventual variety" (singing one type multiple times before switching). Song complexity correlates with territory quality and pairing success. The introductory "sweet sweet sweet" is diagnostic across subspecies.
- **Calls:** Nasal *chimp* or *tchep*, given frequently. Thin high *tseet* flight call. Alarm: harsh *chek-chek-chek* series.
- **Song development:** Song Sparrows are "age-limited learners" — males crystallize their song repertoire during the first year and sing those songs for life. Repertoire size averages 8-12 songs per male but varies from 5 to 20+. Males with larger repertoires hold territories longer and achieve higher lifetime reproductive success.

### Evolutionary Note

Song Sparrow is one of the most polytypic bird species in North America, with 24-31 recognized subspecies depending on taxonomic authority. The species complex ranges from the tiny, pale, short-billed populations of the arid Southwest to the massive, dark, heavy-billed populations of the Aleutian Islands — a morphological span so extreme that the smallest and largest forms would be immediately identified as different species if encountered without knowledge of the intervening clinal variation. The PNW is the geographic center of this morphological radiation. Genomic studies suggest the Song Sparrow complex may ultimately be split into 2-4 species, with the sedentary, melanized Pacific coastal lineage and the migratory, pale interior lineage showing the deepest divergence. However, gene flow between adjacent subspecies along contact zones has so far prevented formal taxonomic splitting. The Song Sparrow remains one of the most studied birds in the world — Nice's (1937, 1943) foundational studies of territory and behavior were conducted on Ohio Song Sparrows, but subsequent work on PNW populations (particularly by Arcese and colleagues in the Gulf Islands of BC) has produced some of the most detailed long-term demographic and evolutionary datasets for any wild bird population [P24, P29, P32, O1].

## Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Year-round resident (most PNW populations)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF score low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 14-16 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-25 cm
- **Mass:** 18-30 g
- **Plumage:** The Oregon group (*oreganus*), which dominates the PNW, is the most commonly encountered form: male has a blackish hood sharply demarcated from warm rufous-brown back and pinkish-brown flanks, white belly, dark gray tail with conspicuous white outer tail feathers (flashed in flight and during distraction displays). Female Oregon similar but hood replaced by gray-brown, less sharply demarcated. Slate-colored group (*hyemalis*), the eastern form, is uniform dark slate-gray above and on breast with white belly; occurs as a regular winter visitor in the PNW, especially east of the Cascades. Pink-sided group occasionally detected in eastern PNW in winter. Bill pale pinkish in all forms. Eye dark.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate in Oregon group. Male hood jet black; female hood gray-brown. Both sexes show white outer tail feathers.

### Subspecific Groups in the PNW

The Dark-eyed Junco complex was famously lumped from five species into one in 1973 by the AOS. The PNW hosts two groups regularly:

- **Oregon group (*oreganus*, *thurberi*, *shufeldti*, *montanus*)** — The dominant PNW junco. *oreganus* (dark-hooded, rufous-backed) breeds throughout western WA, OR, and BC; *shufeldti* breeds in the interior mountains; *thurberi* extends into southern OR and California. Males show a sharp contrast between jet-black hood and rufous back. The Oregon Junco is the quintessential "snowbird" of PNW suburban feeders in winter.
- **Slate-colored group (*hyemalis*)** — Regular winter visitor throughout the PNW, more common east of the Cascades. Uniform dark gray above, lacking the brown back and pink flanks of Oregon birds. Mixed flocks containing both Oregon and Slate-colored birds are common at winter feeding stations, providing a natural demonstration of the group differences.

**Contact zones:** Oregon and Slate-colored groups interbreed in a broad contact zone in the northern Rocky Mountains and interior BC. Hybrid individuals showing intermediate characters (e.g., gray hood with brownish back wash) are regularly detected in eastern WA and ID winter flocks. The persistence of distinct phenotypes despite extensive hybridization is a central puzzle in junco speciation research [P30].

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Coniferous and mixed forests with open understory, forest edges, clearings, and suburban areas with mature conifers. Breeds from sea level to treeline. Common in Douglas-fir, western hemlock, ponderosa pine, and subalpine fir forests. Readily colonizes suburban landscapes with conifers and ground-level foraging areas.
- **Wintering:** Forest edges, brushy fields, suburban yards, roadsides, campgrounds, and feeder stations. Forms mixed-species flocks with other sparrows and Golden-crowned Kinglets. One of the most common winter feeder birds in the PNW.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 8,000 ft (breeding); descends to lowlands in winter
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 4, 5, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level. Forages on bare ground, leaf litter, and snow surface beneath conifers. Nests on ground or on low banks.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weed, conifer), insects (beetles, caterpillars, ants, flies, spiders)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground forager. Hops along ground scratching leaf litter with double-scratch. Picks seeds from snow surface. Rarely forages above ground level except to glean insects from low branches during breeding. In winter, forms feeding flocks of 10-30+ birds with linear dominance hierarchies — males dominant over females, adults over juveniles, residents over newcomers.
- **Seasonal variation:** Seeds dominate fall-winter diet (~75%); insects increase during breeding season (~50% animal matter) for nestling provisioning.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass, moss, rootlets, bark strips, lined with fine grass, hair, and feathers
- **Nest location:** On ground, typically concealed under overhanging bank, log, exposed root mass, or dense vegetation. Occasionally in low shrub, rock crevice, or human structure (flowerpot, hanging basket, building ledge).
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-13 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (typically 2 in PNW lowlands)
- **Breeding season:** April-August

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.6% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 200 million (North America) — one of the most abundant birds on the continent [O4]
- **Threats:** Low overall concern despite slight long-term decline. Outdoor cat predation significant in suburban habitats. Window collisions. Cowbird parasitism in some areas. Climate-driven upslope shift of breeding range may reduce total breeding habitat area as treeline habitats contract.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Simple musical trill on one pitch, variable in tempo and pitch among individuals: *tew-tew-tew-tew-tew-tew*. Occasionally a more complex two-part trill. Surprisingly variable — neighboring males often sing distinct trill types. Song remarkably similar to Chipping Sparrow's trill but typically more musical, slower, and richer in tone.
- **Calls:** Sharp *smack* or *tik*, given frequently in flocks. Thin high *tseet* flight call. In winter flocks, a complex vocabulary of soft contact calls maintains flock cohesion.
- **Notable:** Dark-eyed Juncos are "age-limited learners" like Song Sparrows — males crystallize their song repertoire in their first year. However, individual juncos typically sing only 1-3 song types, far fewer than Song Sparrow's repertoire.

### Evolutionary Note

The Dark-eyed Junco complex is one of the most important model systems in avian evolutionary biology. The 1973 lumping of five species — Oregon, Slate-colored, White-winged, Gray-headed, and Guadalupe Juncos — into a single species was based on extensive hybridization at contact zones. However, the lumped species show dramatic plumage divergence that evolved remarkably rapidly: molecular clock estimates suggest the Oregon and Slate-colored groups diverged only 10,000-20,000 years ago during post-Pleistocene range expansion — among the fastest documented rates of plumage evolution in birds. The complex has been the subject of foundational studies on hormonal control of aggression (testosterone implant experiments), photoperiodism (juncos were among the first birds shown to respond to day-length changes for migration timing), and plumage signaling (the white tail flash is used in predator escape and agonistic displays). Colonization of the University of California San Diego campus by a sedentary population derived from Oregon Juncos in the 1980s has provided a natural experiment in rapid adaptation to urban environments [P30, P27, O1].

## White-crowned Sparrow (*Zonotrichia leucophrys*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Year-round resident (coastal); breeding and wintering (interior)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF score low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-18 cm
- **Wingspan:** 21-24 cm
- **Mass:** 25-36 g
- **Plumage:** Adult: bold black-and-white crown stripes (two broad white stripes with black borders on crown center and lateral crown), pale gray breast unstreaked, brown-streaked back, two white wing bars, pinkish-orange or yellowish bill (varies by subspecies). Immature: crown stripes are tan-and-brown instead of black-and-white, otherwise similar. Clean, unstreaked gray breast distinguishes from all other PNW sparrows.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Males average slightly larger with marginally bolder crown stripes; sexes not reliably distinguished in the field.

### Subspecific Variation and Song Dialect Geography

Three subspecies occur regularly in the PNW, each with distinctive ecology and distribution:

- ***pugetensis*** — The Pacific lowland form. Year-round resident (or very short-distance migrant) from southwestern BC through western WA and OR coastal and lowland areas. Breeds in suburban edges, coastal scrub, agricultural hedgerows, and blackberry tangles at low elevations. Smaller-billed than other forms, with a tendency toward yellowish (not orange) bill. The most sedentary subspecies. Nests on or near the ground in dense shrub cover.
- ***oriantha*** — Mountain form. Breeds in subalpine meadows and alpine krummholz from the Cascades and Blue Mountains eastward through the Rocky Mountains, 5,000-10,000 ft. Medium-distance migrant wintering in the lowlands of the PNW, California, and Mexico. Larger and longer-winged than *pugetensis*. White loral stripe (between eye and bill). Orange bill.
- ***gambelii*** — Boreal form. Breeds across interior Alaska and northern Canada. Long-distance migrant, wintering commonly throughout the PNW lowlands October-April. Largest-billed form with bright orange bill. White loral stripe. The most common wintering White-crowned Sparrow in interior PNW.

**Song dialects — cultural transmission of vocal tradition:** White-crowned Sparrow song dialects are among the best-documented examples of cultural transmission in any animal. Chilton et al. (1995) mapped song dialect boundaries across the PNW, revealing that *pugetensis* populations in the Puget Sound lowlands sing in geographically discrete dialect groups, each covering 5-50 km, with sharp boundaries between them. Young males learn their local dialect during a sensitive period in the first 50-100 days of life; once crystallized, the song is sung unchanged for the remainder of the bird's life. Dialect boundaries are stable over decades, maintained by cultural learning rather than genetic divergence — birds transplanted as eggs adopt the dialect of their foster population, not their natal population. The system has been foundational in understanding the neurobiology of song learning (Marler's classic experiments used White-crowned Sparrows), and PNW populations continue to serve as primary study subjects for research on cultural evolution, vocal learning windows, and the relationship between song and speciation [P25, P31].

**Dialect and mate choice:** Females preferentially pair with males singing the local dialect, creating a cultural barrier to gene flow between dialect populations even in the absence of geographic separation. This assortative mating based on culturally learned song may represent an early stage in the speciation process — "speciation by cultural drift" [P25].

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Varies by subspecies. *pugetensis*: suburban edges, coastal scrub, agricultural hedgerows, blackberry tangles, and weedy fields at low elevations. *oriantha*: subalpine meadow edges, alpine krummholz, willow thickets above timberline. The two subspecies occupy radically different breeding habitats — *pugetensis* is a lowland suburban bird while *oriantha* is an alpine specialist.
- **Wintering:** Weedy fields, suburban edges, agricultural margins, feeder stations, coastal scrub. All three subspecies mix on wintering grounds in PNW lowlands, creating flocks where bill color, size, and song can be compared directly.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 1,500 ft (*pugetensis* breeding); 5,000-10,000 ft (*oriantha* breeding); sea level to 3,000 ft (winter, all forms)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban), Zone 1 (Alpine — *oriantha*)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 4, 5, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level to low shrub. Forages on bare ground, lawns, and paths adjacent to dense cover. Nests on ground or in low shrub.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weed, grain), insects (beetles, caterpillars, flies, ants, aphids), buds, and some fruit
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner. Hops along ground picking seeds from surface; double-scratches leaf litter. Feeds on lawns, paths, parking lot edges, and agricultural fields. Readily visits ground feeders. In alpine breeding habitat, forages along snowmelt edges and meadow margins.
- **Seasonal variation:** Seeds dominate fall-winter; insects increase during breeding for nestling provisioning. Alpine *oriantha* populations eat proportionally more insects during the compressed breeding season.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of grass, twigs, bark strips, moss, lined with fine grass, rootlets, and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low dense shrub, 0-5 ft above ground (*pugetensis*); on ground under dwarf shrub or rock overhang (*oriantha*)
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (*pugetensis*: often 2; *oriantha*: 1 due to short alpine breeding season)
- **Breeding season:** April-July (*pugetensis*); June-August (*oriantha*)

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.1% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 79 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. *pugetensis* benefits from suburban and agricultural edge habitats. *oriantha* may face long-term habitat reduction from climate-driven treeline advance compressing alpine meadow habitat. Window collisions during migration.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Opens with 1-2 clear whistled notes, followed by a complex phrase of buzzy trills and whistles. Highly variable by dialect region (see above). In PNW *pugetensis*, a typical dialect pattern is: *oh-dear-me, I'm-so-weary* or *I-gotta-go-WEE-wee-wee* (the mnemonic varies by dialect). *oriantha* sings a distinctly different song structure from *pugetensis*. Each male sings a single song type for life.
- **Calls:** Sharp metallic *pink* or *tink*. Thin *seep* flight call.

### Evolutionary Note

White-crowned Sparrow is arguably the single most important species in the history of birdsong research. Peter Marler's experiments in the 1960s-1970s, using California and PNW populations, established the foundational concepts of the sensitive period for song learning, the song template (innate predisposition to learn conspecific song), and crystallization (the developmental endpoint after which song is fixed). These studies launched the field of neuroethology of vocal learning and directly influenced research on human language acquisition. The five recognized subspecies show a continuum from sedentary, small-billed, short-distance forms (*pugetensis*, *nuttalli*) to migratory, large-billed, long-distance forms (*gambelii*, *leucophrys*). This variation in migratory strategy within a single species has made White-crowned Sparrow a primary system for studying the genetics and physiology of migration [P25, P31, O1].

| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Breeding (*oriantha*) | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round (*pugetensis*), wintering (all) | Common to Abundant | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Wintering | Common | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Wintering | Common | Visitor |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Wintering (*gambelii*) | Common | Visitor |

### Migration

- **Flyway:** Pacific
- **Spring arrival:** *pugetensis*: resident. *oriantha*: April-May to alpine breeding grounds. *gambelii*: departs PNW lowlands April-May for northern breeding grounds.
- **Fall departure:** *oriantha*: September-October descent to lowlands. *gambelii*: arrives in PNW lowlands September-October.
- **Wintering grounds:** PNW lowlands (*pugetensis* resident; *oriantha* and *gambelii* overwinter)
- **Distance:** Sedentary (*pugetensis*) to long-distance (*gambelii*: 2,000-6,000 km)

## Spotted Towhee (*Pipilo maculatus*) — EXTENDED

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF score low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 17-21 cm
- **Wingspan:** 25-29 cm
- **Mass:** 33-49 g (one of the largest sparrows in the PNW)
- **Plumage:** Male: jet black head, upper breast, back, wings, and tail, with bold white spots on wing coverts and scapulars (the "spotted" pattern diagnostic of the species), rufous flanks, white belly, red eye. Female: same pattern but black replaced by dark brown. White corners on tail visible in flight. Stout, dark, conical bill. PNW birds (*oregonus* complex) show heavier white spotting on back and wings than interior forms and are larger than southern California populations.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Moderate. Male jet black where female is dark brown; otherwise pattern identical.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense understory shrub and brushy thickets in virtually any PNW forest type — Douglas-fir, oak woodland, riparian, suburban, and parkland with well-developed shrub layer. The quintessential PNW understory bird. Requires dense ground-level to shrub-level cover (0-3 m) with adjacent leaf litter for foraging. Commonly associated with salal, Oregon grape, snowberry, hazel, and blackberry.
- **Wintering:** Same habitats as breeding; largely sedentary. Slightly increased use of suburban yards and feeder stations in winter.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (occasionally higher on east side)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 2, 5, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level and dense shrub. The bird is heard far more often than seen — the double-scratch foraging sound in leaf litter is one of the most characteristic sounds of PNW understory habitat.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and invertebrates (beetles, caterpillars, ants, crickets, spiders, snails, millipedes), seeds, fruit (blackberry, madrone, elderberry, salal, snowberry), and acorns
- **Foraging strategy:** The double-scratch: the towhee lunges forward on both feet simultaneously, raking backward through leaf litter with a vigorous, audible scratching motion, then hopping backward to inspect exposed prey. This signature foraging behavior is so energetic that it moves leaf litter 12-18 inches and is audible at 30+ meters. The double-scratch evolved in the towhee lineage and is performed by all *Pipilo* species. Also gleans from low vegetation and takes fruit from shrubs.
- **Seasonal variation:** Invertebrates dominate spring-summer diet; seeds, fruit, and acorns increase in fall-winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of bark strips, grass, leaves, and weed stems, lined with fine grass, rootlets, and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low dense shrub, 0-5 ft above ground. Ground nests concealed under dense shrub canopy, often in leaf litter at base of shrub.
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2 (commonly 2 in PNW)
- **Breeding season:** April-July
- **Brood parasitism:** Regular Brown-headed Cowbird host, particularly in suburban and edge habitats.

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 33 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Adapts well to suburban habitats with shrub cover. Fire suppression that converts open shrubby understory to dense closed canopy can reduce habitat quality. Outdoor cat predation significant given ground-nesting and ground-foraging habits.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Begins with 1-3 buzzy introductory notes followed by a trill: *drink-your-TEEEEE* or *ZHREE ZHREE trrrrrrrrr*. Variable across the PNW. Interior birds tend to sing faster, simpler trills; coastal birds produce more complex and slower songs. Males sing from exposed perches (shrub tops, fencepost, low tree branch) — one of the few times this skulking species is readily visible.
- **Calls:** Emphatic rising *mew* or *zhreeee*, a diagnostic sound of PNW understory. Also a sharp *chink* alarm note. The catlike *mew* call is given year-round and is often the first indication of a towhee's presence.

### Evolutionary Note

Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee (*P. erythrophthalmus*) were treated as a single species ("Rufous-sided Towhee") until 1995 when AOS split them based on limited hybridization in the Great Plains and substantial plumage, vocal, and genetic differences. The split was one of the most anticipated and least controversial of the 1990s AOS decisions. Spotted Towhee shows significant geographic variation: PNW *oregonus* group birds have extensive white spotting on back and wings (the "spotted" character strongest in the northwest of the range), while interior and Mexican populations have reduced spotting. Multiple subspecies are recognized within the PNW, with clinal variation in spotting extent, body size, and plumage saturation. The genus *Pipilo* as traditionally constituted may not be monophyletic — California Towhee and Canyon Towhee (the brown towhees) are more distantly related to the Spotted/Eastern complex than previously assumed [P24, P27, O3].

## Golden-crowned Sparrow (*Zonotrichia atricapilla*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Wintering (regular), rare breeder (northern BC only within PNW)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-18 cm
- **Wingspan:** 21-24 cm
- **Mass:** 28-36 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding adult: striking golden-yellow central crown stripe bordered by broad black lateral crown stripes, gray face and nape, brown-streaked back, plain gray-brown breast fading to white belly, two faint wing bars. Non-breeding adult: crown pattern duller, yellow reduced, black replaced by brown, overall browner. Immature (first winter): crown stripes indistinct, dull brownish-yellow crown with faint dark borders; most easily confused with immature White-crowned Sparrow but warmer brown overall with less distinct head pattern. Bill dusky above, paler below.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Males average slightly brighter crown.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Dense subalpine and alpine scrub, krummholz, coastal alder thickets, and willow-birch tundra margins in Alaska and northern BC. Not a regular breeder within the PNW study region south of BC.
- **Wintering:** Dense shrubby tangles, blackberry thickets, suburban hedges, brushy field edges, and weedy lots in PNW lowlands. Often forages on lawns, paths, and mulched garden beds adjacent to dense shrub cover. Readily visits ground feeders. Mixed flocks with White-crowned Sparrows and juncos are typical.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 2,000 ft (wintering in PNW); breeds above 3,000 ft in Alaska/BC
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban — winter)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 4, 5 (winter)
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level adjacent to dense shrub. Forages on leaf litter, mulch, lawns, and bare ground.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds, buds, flowers, fruit, and insects. More herbivorous than most PNW sparrows — readily eats plant buds, flowers (pulling apart blossoms), and green vegetation in addition to seeds and insects.
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner and low-shrub forager. Double-scratches leaf litter like other Passerellidae. Notable for feeding on garden plants — historically called "weeds-puller" by California gardeners for habit of pulling up seedlings.
- **Seasonal variation:** Seeds and plant matter dominate winter diet; insects increase if breeding in subalpine habitats.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of twigs, grass, moss, bark strips, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low shrub in dense subalpine scrub
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** June-August (in Alaska/BC)

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS data limited due to remote breeding range) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 4 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Wintering habitat in PNW generally secure. Remote northern breeding range relatively undisturbed. Climate change could affect tundra-edge breeding habitat.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Hauntingly beautiful descending three-note whistle: *oh-dear-me* — a melancholy descending minor triad. One of the most recognizable and evocative sounds of the Pacific Northwest winter, though rarely heard in full on wintering grounds (more commonly on breeding grounds). Partial songs and subsong given occasionally in late winter before spring departure.
- **Calls:** Sharp metallic *tink*, very similar to White-crowned Sparrow.

### Evolutionary Note

Golden-crowned Sparrow is sister to White-throated Sparrow (*Z. albicolnis*) within *Zonotrichia*, not to White-crowned Sparrow as their superficial crown-stripe similarity might suggest. The relationship with White-throated Sparrow implies a shared ancestor with a crowned plumage pattern, with the yellow and white crown colors evolving independently. Golden-crowned Sparrow is the least-studied *Zonotrichia* due to its remote breeding range — much of its breeding biology was not described until the late 20th century. Hybridization with White-crowned Sparrow is extremely rare despite regular syntopy on wintering grounds [P24, O1].

## Fox Sparrow (*Passerella iliaca*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding (mountains), wintering (lowlands), year-round (some coastal populations)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-19 cm
- **Wingspan:** 24-29 cm
- **Mass:** 26-49 g (substantial size variation among groups)
- **Plumage:** The PNW is dominated by the **Sooty group** (*unalaschcensis*, *fuliginosa*, *townsendi*): uniformly dark chocolate-brown to sooty-brown above and below, with heavy dark brown streaking on breast that coalesces into a central blotch, dark brown face with indistinct supercilium, large stout bill. The **Slate-colored group** (*schistacea*) breeds in interior mountains: gray head and back, contrasting with rufous wings and tail, white underparts with rufous-brown triangular spots. Red Fox Sparrow group (eastern) is rare vagrant. The Sooty group is so dark and uniform that beginning birders may fail to recognize it as the "same species" as the brightly patterned Red or Slate-colored forms.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike in plumage.

### Fox Sparrow Groups and Potential Species Split

Fox Sparrow is widely considered a candidate for taxonomic splitting into 2-4 species. The four currently recognized groups differ dramatically in plumage, morphology, ecology, habitat, and genetics:

- **Sooty group** (*unalaschcensis* and allies) — The dominant PNW form. Breeds in dense coastal and montane shrub from southern Alaska through coastal BC, WA, and OR. Winters in PNW lowlands. Dark, uniform chocolate-brown plumage. Associated with the maritime Pacific coast climate zone.
- **Slate-colored group** (*schistacea* and allies) — Breeds in interior mountain shrub, sagebrush edges, and montane riparian in eastern WA, OR, and ID. Gray head contrasting with rufous wings and tail. Migrates to lowlands and southward in winter.

Both groups breed in the PNW but in different habitats and elevational zones. Where they come into contact, hybridization appears limited, supporting the case for eventual splitting. Molecular divergence between the Sooty and Slate-colored groups is comparable to that between many recognized species pairs in Passerellidae [P24, O3].

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Sooty group: dense coastal and montane shrub — alder thickets, salal, devil's club, avalanche chute shrub, and subalpine scrub. Slate-colored group: montane riparian willow, snowberry and bitterbrush thickets, sagebrush-mountain mahogany ecotone.
- **Wintering:** Dense shrub tangles, blackberry thickets, suburban hedges, riparian undergrowth, and brush piles. Both groups winter in PNW lowlands, often in mixed flocks with Song Sparrows, juncos, and towhees.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (breeding varies by group); sea level to 2,000 ft (wintering)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane — breeding), Zone 3 (Lowland — wintering)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 4, 5, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level in dense shrub. Among the most skulking of PNW sparrows — typically detected by voice or by vigorous double-scratching sounds.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects and invertebrates (beetles, caterpillars, millipedes, snails, spiders), seeds, fruit (salal, elderberry, blackberry), and leaf buds
- **Foraging strategy:** Powerful double-scratch forager — the largest and most vigorous leaf-litter scratcher among PNW sparrows. The scratch is audible at considerable distance and moves leaf litter with force proportional to the bird's large size. Also gleans from low vegetation and overturns debris.
- **Seasonal variation:** Invertebrates dominate breeding diet; seeds and fruit increase in winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky, well-insulated cup of moss, grass, bark strips, leaves, lined with fine grass, hair, and feathers
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low dense shrub, 0-4 ft above ground, concealed by dense vegetation
- **Clutch size:** 2-5 eggs (typically 3-4)
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually, limited data) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 21 million (North America, all groups combined) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Breeding habitat largely intact in remote coastal and montane areas. Wintering habitat loss from shrub clearing and development locally.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rich, musical, warbling song with clear whistled notes — one of the finest songsters among PNW sparrows. Sooty group: song tends to be slower, lower-pitched, and more minor-key. Slate-colored group: brighter, higher-pitched, with more varied phrase structure. The vocal differences between groups are among the characters supporting potential taxonomic splitting.
- **Calls:** Hard, metallic *tchk* or sharp *smack* — louder and more emphatic than Song Sparrow's call. Also a thin high *tseet*.

### Evolutionary Note

Fox Sparrow may represent the most significant unsplit species complex in North American ornithology. The four groups — Red, Sooty, Slate-colored, and Thick-billed — differ in plumage, morphology, genetics, song, and ecology to a degree exceeding many recognized species pairs. The AOS has been reviewing the complex for potential splitting for decades. The PNW's Sooty and Slate-colored groups represent two of the strongest candidates for species-level recognition. The genus *Passerella* is monotypic (containing only Fox Sparrow), reflecting the species' isolated phylogenetic position within Passerellidae [P24, P27, O3].

## Chipping Sparrow (*Spizella passerina*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 19-22 cm
- **Mass:** 11-16 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding: bright rufous crown, clean white supercilium, black eye-line, gray nape and rump, brown-streaked back, unstreaked pale gray breast, two faint wing bars. Non-breeding: crown streaked brown and gray, duller overall. One of the "clean-breasted" sparrows — the unstreaked gray underparts help distinguish from many PNW sparrows.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open woodland with short grass or bare ground — ponderosa pine savanna, park-like Douglas-fir, orchards, suburban yards with scattered conifers, campgrounds, and forest edges. Prefers habitat with scattered trees (for song perches and nest sites) over short-cropped ground (for foraging). One of the most familiar suburban-nesting sparrows in the interior PNW.
- **Wintering:** Most PNW populations migrate south; rare in winter.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 6,500 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 2, 7
- **Microhabitat:** Ground-level forager in short grass; nests in conifers 3-25 ft up.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weed) and insects (grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars, ants)
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks and hops on short grass and bare ground picking seeds from surface. Gleans insects from low vegetation during breeding. Often forages on lawns, paths, and roadsides.
- **Seasonal variation:** Seeds dominate except during breeding when insects increase substantially for nestling provisioning.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact, neat cup of fine grass and rootlets, lined with hair (often horsehair — historically nicknamed "hairbird")
- **Nest location:** In conifer or dense shrub, 3-25 ft above ground, usually on horizontal branch
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July
- **Brood parasitism:** Frequent Brown-headed Cowbird host; one of the most heavily parasitized sparrows in the PNW interior.

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -1.0% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 230 million (North America) — abundant despite declines [O4]
- **Threats:** Heavy Brown-headed Cowbird parasitism. Habitat changes from fire suppression converting open pine forest to dense canopy. Pesticide reduction of insect prey.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Dry, mechanical trill on one pitch: *trrrrrrrrrrrrr*. Remarkably similar to Dark-eyed Junco's trill but typically faster, drier, and less musical. Pine Warbler's song is also similar. Song speed varies geographically.
- **Calls:** Thin, high *tsit* or *seep*.

### Evolutionary Note

Chipping Sparrow is closely related to Clay-colored and Brewer's Sparrows in the genus *Spizella*. All three share the compact body plan, fine bill, and ground-foraging ecology. Chipping Sparrow's adaptation to human-modified landscapes — lawns, parks, campgrounds — has made it one of the most successful sparrows across North America, though this success masks a long-term population decline driven primarily by cowbird parasitism and habitat homogenization [P24, O1].

## Savannah Sparrow (*Passerculus sandwichensis*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding (some year-round in coastal areas)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern (mainland subspecies)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-15 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-22 cm
- **Mass:** 15-28 g (varies by subspecies)
- **Plumage:** Brown-streaked above, white below with brown streaking on breast and flanks, often with a small central breast spot. Diagnostic yellow or yellowish-white supraloral stripe (between eye and bill) — when visible, this is the best field mark. Short, notched tail. Pink legs. PNW coastal populations tend to be darker and more heavily streaked than interior birds.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open grassland, meadows, agricultural fields, salt marsh, coastal dune grass, airport margins, and alpine meadows. The most broadly distributed open-grassland sparrow in the PNW. Avoids shrubby or forested habitats. Requires grass 15-50 cm tall for nesting with shorter patches for foraging.
- **Wintering:** Coastal marshes, agricultural fields, weedy lots, and short-grass areas. More coastal in winter than during breeding.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side grassland/steppe), Zone 6 (Coastal — salt marsh)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zones 1, 3, 5
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level in open grass. Never in shrub or forest.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, grasshoppers, flies, caterpillars, spiders) and seeds (grass, weed)
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks and hops on ground in short grass. Runs along ground like a mouse when flushed, reluctant to fly. Gleans from grass stems and picks from ground surface.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominate during breeding; seeds increase in fall-winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass lined with fine grass and hair, set into a scrape on the ground
- **Nest location:** On ground concealed in grass tuft, typically with overhanging grass forming a canopy
- **Clutch size:** 3-6 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 10-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -1.3% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 170 million (North America) — still abundant but declining [O4]
- **Threats:** Grassland conversion to agriculture and development. Mowing and haying during nesting season. Pesticide reduction of insect prey. Coastal salt marsh habitat loss from sea-level rise. Interior grassland loss.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Thin, insect-like buzzy trill: *tsit-tsit-tsit-TSEEEE-tsay*. Begins with 2-3 soft introductory notes, then a high-pitched buzzy trill, ending with a lower note. One of the most insect-like sparrow songs — easily overlooked.
- **Calls:** Thin *tsit* or *seep*.

### Evolutionary Note

Savannah Sparrow exhibits extreme subspecific variation, with 17-21 described subspecies ranging from tiny pale interior forms to the massive, dark, sedentary Ipswich Sparrow (*princeps*) of Sable Island, Nova Scotia — once considered a separate species. PNW coastal salt marsh populations (*sandwichensis*, *brooksi*) are darker and larger-billed than interior grassland birds, showing parallel adaptation to tidal habitats seen in Song Sparrow *maxillaris*. The genus *Passerculus* is monotypic, and molecular phylogenetics places it closest to *Ammodramus* (grasshopper sparrows) within Passerellidae [P24, O3].

## Vesper Sparrow (*Pooecetes gramineus*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF moderate concern (western populations declining)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 14-17 cm
- **Wingspan:** 23-26 cm
- **Mass:** 20-29 g
- **Plumage:** Brown-streaked above, white below with brown streaking on breast and flanks. Diagnostic features: white outer tail feathers (conspicuous in flight), rufous lesser wing coverts (shoulder patch, often hard to see when perched), bold white eye-ring, no central breast spot. Larger and longer-tailed than Savannah Sparrow.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open grassland and sagebrush with scattered bare ground patches — a habitat specialist requiring a mosaic of short grass, bare ground, and sparse shrub. Typical habitat includes bunchgrass prairie, fallow agricultural fields, sagebrush flats with grass understory, roadsides, and grazed pasture. More tolerant of sparse, dry habitats than Savannah Sparrow.
- **Wintering:** Southern US and Mexico in similar open habitats; rare in PNW winter.
- **Elevation range:** 500-6,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (dry grassland-oak edges)
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level in open, dry grassland with substantial bare ground component.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, grasshoppers, caterpillars, ants) and seeds (grass, weed)
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks on bare ground and through sparse grass picking insects and seeds from surface. Often forages on roads and road shoulders, making it vulnerable to vehicle strike.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insect-dominated in breeding season; seeds dominate fall.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass in ground scrape, lined with fine grass, rootlets, hair
- **Nest location:** On ground in sparse grass or at base of bunchgrass tuft, often adjacent to bare ground
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-13 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -0.9% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 28 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Grassland conversion and degradation. Agricultural intensification replacing fallow field/bunchgrass mosaic with uniform crop. Fire suppression allowing sagebrush encroachment on grassland. Climate-driven changes to bunchgrass communities. Listed as a Species of Greatest Conservation Need in WA and OR [G9, G12].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Beautiful, complex song beginning with 2-4 clear minor-key whistled notes, followed by a descending trill: *here-here, where-where, all-together-down-the-hill*. Notably more melodious than most grassland sparrows. Often sings at dusk (hence "vesper" — the evening sparrow). One of the last sparrows singing at day's end.
- **Calls:** Thin *tsit*.

### Evolutionary Note

Vesper Sparrow is the sole member of *Pooecetes*, an isolated genus within Passerellidae. Its white outer tail feathers, terrestrial habits, and open-grassland specialization make it morphologically and ecologically distinctive. The species appears to be an early-diverging lineage in the sparrow radiation, not closely allied to any other PNW genus [P24, P27].

- **Fall departure:** September-October

## Sagebrush Sparrow (*Artemisiospiza nevadensis*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF moderate concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 14-16 cm
- **Wingspan:** 20-23 cm
- **Mass:** 15-22 g
- **Plumage:** Gray-brown above with faint streaking, gray head with white eye-ring and white loral spot, dark malar stripe, white underparts with small dark central breast spot, buffy-brown flanks. Tail dark with white outer tail feathers. Runs on ground with tail cocked upward — a distinctive behavioral field mark.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) steppe — a strict sagebrush obligate in the PNW. Requires large, contiguous stands of big sagebrush with open ground beneath for foraging. The most habitat-restricted sparrow in the PNW, entirely dependent on the sagebrush ecosystem.
- **Wintering:** Southern Great Basin and Sonoran desert scrub; largely vacates PNW.
- **Elevation range:** 2,000-6,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — sagebrush)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** None significant
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level beneath sagebrush canopy. Runs between shrubs rather than flying.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, ants, grasshoppers, caterpillars) and seeds (sagebrush, grass, weed)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground runner. Hops and runs beneath sagebrush canopy, picking prey from ground surface and from sagebrush stems. Characteristic habit of running with tail cocked. Rarely flies; escapes threats by running through sagebrush understory.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominant in breeding season; seeds (including sagebrush seeds) dominate in fall.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of sagebrush bark strips, grass, lined with wool, hair, feathers
- **Nest location:** In sagebrush, 0.5-3 ft above ground, concealed within dense shrub
- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs (typically 3)
- **Incubation:** 12-16 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** April-July (early for a sagebrush species)

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -2.1% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 4.6 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Sagebrush habitat loss from conversion to agriculture, overgrazing, invasive cheatgrass, and altered fire regimes. Shares the same habitat-loss trajectory as Greater Sage-Grouse. Climate-driven cheatgrass expansion increases fire frequency, converting sagebrush to annual grassland unsuitable for Sagebrush Sparrow.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Thin, tinkling, jumbled warble: a weak but musical series of high notes, lower in volume than most sparrow songs. Sings from sagebrush top or during short aerial song flight.
- **Calls:** Thin high *tink* or *tseet*.

### Evolutionary Note

Sagebrush Sparrow was split from Bell's Sparrow (*A. belli*) in 2014 by AOS, restoring a taxonomic arrangement that recognized the distinctiveness of the interior sagebrush-obligate form from the coastal California scrub form. The two species hybridize in a narrow zone in the eastern Mojave Desert. The genus *Artemisiospiza* was erected to reflect the molecular distance of these sparrows from the *Amphispiza* (Black-throated Sparrow) to which they were formerly allied. The genus name itself — derived from *Artemisia*, the sagebrush genus — encodes the species' obligate dependence on sagebrush ecosystem [P28, O3].

- **Spring arrival:** Late March to April
- **Fall departure:** September-October
- **Wintering grounds:** Southern Great Basin and Sonoran desert
- **Distance:** Short to medium-distance migrant; 500-2,000 km

## Brewer's Sparrow (*Spizella breweri*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF moderate concern (declining with sagebrush loss)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-20 cm
- **Mass:** 10-14 g
- **Plumage:** The plainest sparrow in the PNW — pale grayish-brown above with fine dark streaking, unstreaked pale gray-brown breast, faint pale eye-ring, pale lores, fine bill. No wing bars, no central breast spot, no distinctive head pattern. Identified largely by its plainness and sagebrush habitat association.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Big sagebrush steppe — another sagebrush obligate, co-occurring with Sagebrush Sparrow but typically in denser, taller sagebrush stands. Also uses bitterbrush, rabbitbrush, and mountain mahogany shrub at higher elevations. The subspecies *taverneri* ("Timberline Sparrow") breeds in alpine willow scrub in the northern Rockies and BC — some authorities consider it a separate species.
- **Wintering:** Desert scrub in southwestern US and Mexico; rare in PNW winter.
- **Elevation range:** 2,500-6,500 ft (sagebrush breeding); to 9,000 ft (*taverneri* in alpine)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Alpine — *taverneri* only)
- **Microhabitat:** Within and beneath sagebrush canopy. Sings from sagebrush tops.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, caterpillars, grasshoppers, leafhoppers) and seeds (grass, sagebrush, weed)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner beneath sagebrush. Hops on ground picking insects and seeds from surface and base of shrubs. Also gleans from sagebrush stems and leaves.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insectivorous during breeding; seeds dominate late summer and fall.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Compact cup of grass and sagebrush bark, lined with fine grass, hair, rootlets
- **Nest location:** In sagebrush, 0.5-3 ft above ground
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 8-9 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -2.0% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 16 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Sagebrush habitat loss — same suite of threats as Sagebrush Sparrow and Greater Sage-Grouse: cheatgrass invasion, altered fire regimes, agricultural conversion, energy development. The long-term decline directly tracks sagebrush ecosystem degradation across the Great Basin and intermountain West.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Remarkably complex and long for such a small, plain bird — a sustained canary-like trilling series of varied buzzy phrases lasting 5-10+ seconds, often described as the most complex song of any North American sparrow. Males may sing for 30+ minutes continuously at dawn. The song's complexity and duration are startlingly disproportionate to the bird's physical plainness.
- **Calls:** Thin, high *tsip*.

### Evolutionary Note

Brewer's Sparrow is closely related to Clay-colored and Chipping Sparrows in *Spizella*. The "Timberline Sparrow" (*taverneri*), which breeds in alpine willow scrub in BC and the northern Rockies, differs from nominate *breweri* in habitat, elevational range, song, and plumage (darker, more heavily streaked). Some molecular evidence supports species-level recognition for *taverneri*, but hybridization at contact zones with *breweri* has prevented formal splitting. The disparity between Brewer's Sparrow's cryptic plumage and extraordinarily complex song has been interpreted as an adaptation to the open sagebrush habitat where acoustic display is more effective than visual display [P24, O1].

- **Fall departure:** August-September
- **Wintering grounds:** Southwestern US and Mexico
- **Distance:** Medium-distance migrant; 1,000-3,000 km

## Lincoln's Sparrow (*Melospiza lincolnii*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding (mountains), migration and wintering (lowlands)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 13-15 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-22 cm
- **Mass:** 15-22 g
- **Plumage:** The most finely marked of the *Melospiza* sparrows. Brown-streaked above, with a distinctive warm buffy breast band bearing fine, crisp dark streaking — sharper and finer than Song Sparrow's coarser streaking. Broad gray supercilium, buffy malar, narrow dark malar stripe. Raised crown feathers give a slightly peaked head shape. The overall impression is of a more delicate, more elegantly patterned Song Sparrow.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Wet mountain meadows with dense willow, alder, and sedge. Subalpine bogs, beaver pond margins, and montane riparian thickets with saturated ground. One of the most habitat-specific breeding sparrows in the PNW — strongly tied to wet, shrubby montane meadows.
- **Wintering:** Dense riparian thickets, marsh edges, and weedy tangles in PNW lowlands. Secretive and skulking in winter, often overlooked among Song Sparrows.
- **Elevation range:** 3,000-8,000 ft (breeding); sea level to 2,000 ft (wintering)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — breeding), Zone 5 (Riparian — wintering)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (Montane meadows)
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level to 1 m in dense wet shrub. Among the most skulking PNW sparrows.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, flies, caterpillars, ants) and seeds (grass, sedge, weed)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner in dense cover. Hops through low vegetation and on wet ground, gleaning insects from stems and leaf litter. Rarely ventures into open areas. Double-scratches less vigorously than Song Sparrow.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominate during montane breeding season; seeds increase in lowland winter.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass lined with fine grass and hair, well concealed in ground vegetation
- **Nest location:** On ground in dense sedge or grass tussock, typically in wet meadow with overhanging vegetation
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: +0.3% annually, limited data) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 44 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Montane meadow breeding habitat relatively secure. Potential long-term risk from climate-driven drying of mountain meadows.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Musical, bubbly warble — sweet, rolling series of trills and buzzes reminiscent of House Wren in quality but with sparrow-like structure. More complex and musical than Song Sparrow's song, delivered from low shrub perch. One of the more melodious sparrow songs in the PNW.
- **Calls:** Distinctive flat *tsup* or *tchup* — sharper and lower than Song Sparrow's call; a useful identification point.

### Evolutionary Note

Lincoln's Sparrow is the third member of the *Melospiza* trio (with Song and Swamp Sparrows), closely related but ecologically differentiated. Lincoln's is the montane-meadow specialist, Song Sparrow the lowland-riparian generalist, and Swamp Sparrow the marsh specialist. All three show the classic *Melospiza* combination of brown-streaked plumage and ground-level foraging, but Lincoln's is the most refined in streaking pattern and the most restricted in breeding habitat. The species was first described from a specimen collected by Thomas Lincoln, a young companion of John James Audubon, during Audubon's 1833 Labrador expedition [P24, O1].

| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Migration, wintering | Uncommon | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Migration, wintering | Fairly common | Visitor |

### Migration

- **Spring arrival:** April-May (passage through lowlands to montane breeding habitat)
- **Fall departure:** September-October (descent to lowlands)
- **Wintering grounds:** PNW lowlands through Mexico
- **Distance:** Short to medium-distance; 500-3,000 km

## Swamp Sparrow (*Melospiza georgiana*) — brief

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Rare winter visitor and migrant; does not breed in PNW
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 13-15 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-20 cm
- **Mass:** 15-23 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding: rufous crown, gray face and breast, dark-streaked brown back, rufous wings. Non-breeding: streaked brown crown, buffy breast wash. Darker and more richly rufous-winged than Lincoln's Sparrow, with less distinct breast streaking.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal.

### Habitat

- **Wintering (PNW):** Dense cattail and bulrush marshes, wet sedge meadows, and marsh edges. Rare but regular in suitable marsh habitat west of the Cascades.

- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (wetland grass, sedge) and aquatic insects
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground and low-vegetation gleaner in marsh habitats. Wades in shallow water more regularly than other *Melospiza*.

### Population

- **PNW status:** Rare but annual winter visitor. Breeds primarily in eastern North America and boreal Canada. A few winter records annually from western WA and OR marshes.
- **Source:** [O2] eBird. [O7] Aversa et al.

### Evolutionary Note

Swamp Sparrow is the most aquatic-adapted *Melospiza*, with longer legs and toes than its congeners — adaptations for wading in marsh substrates. Its rarity in the PNW places it at the extreme western edge of its winter range [P24, O1].

## American Tree Sparrow (*Spizelloides arborea*) — brief

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Rare winter visitor; does not breed in PNW
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 14-16 cm

- **Plumage:** Rufous crown and eye-line, gray face and nape, unstreaked gray breast with dark central breast spot, two white wing bars, rufous-brown streaked back. Bicolored bill: dark upper mandible, yellow lower mandible. Resembles a larger, longer-tailed Chipping Sparrow with a breast spot.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal.

### Habitat

- **Wintering (PNW):** Brushy edges, weedy fields, hedgerows, and feeder stations. Rare but regular east of the Cascades in cold winters; very rare west of the Cascades. Breeds in arctic and subarctic willow-birch scrub across northern Canada and Alaska.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Foraging strategy:** Ground gleaner in winter flocks. Picks seeds from snow surface and low vegetation.

### Population

- **PNW status:** Rare but annual winter visitor, primarily east of the Cascades. More common in northern Great Basin winter. Numbers vary with winter severity — irruptive in harsh winters.
- **Source:** [O2] eBird. [O7] Aversa et al.

### Evolutionary Note

American Tree Sparrow was recently moved from *Spizella* to the monotypic genus *Spizelloides* based on molecular phylogenetics, reflecting its deeper divergence from the *Spizella* sparrows (Chipping, Brewer's, Clay-colored) than morphological similarity suggested. Despite its common name, it is not particularly arboreal — the name was given by early European settlers who associated it with the Eurasian Tree Sparrow [P24, P27, O3].

## Green-tailed Towhee (*Pipilo chlorurus*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 16-19 cm
- **Wingspan:** 24-26 cm
- **Mass:** 21-33 g
- **Plumage:** Rufous crown, gray face with white loral spot and white throat bordered by dark malar stripes, olive-green back, wings, rump, and tail (the "green tail" — unusual among PNW sparrows), gray breast, white belly. A strikingly colorful sparrow that is immediately identifiable by the combination of rufous crown and green-olive body.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike; males average slightly brighter.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Montane shrub, sagebrush-mountain mahogany ecotone, regenerating burns with dense shrub regrowth, and brushy mountain slopes. Typically at higher elevations than Spotted Towhee, occupying the transition zone between sagebrush steppe and montane forest. In the PNW, most common in the Blue Mountains, eastern Cascades, and mountains of southern Oregon.
- **Wintering:** Southwestern US and Mexico in dense desert scrub. Rarely overwinters in PNW.
- **Elevation range:** 3,000-8,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side shrub-steppe transition)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (Montane — brushy openings)
- **Microhabitat:** Dense mountain shrub, 1-3 m height. Double-scratches in leaf litter like Spotted Towhee but in drier, higher-elevation habitats.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (beetles, caterpillars, ants, grasshoppers), seeds (grass, weed, sagebrush), and berries
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground scratcher. Double-scratches leaf litter beneath shrub canopy. Also gleans from shrub stems and low branches. Less likely to use suburban habitats than Spotted Towhee.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects dominate breeding season; seeds increase in late summer and fall pre-migration.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky cup of bark strips, grass, and weed stems, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low shrub, 0-3 ft above ground, concealed in dense mountain shrub
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 11-13 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 10-11 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July

### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (BBS 1966-2019: -0.1% annually, not significant) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 5 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Low concern. Mountain shrub habitat relatively intact. Some loss from fire suppression and sagebrush conversion. Wintering habitat loss in Mexico.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Musical, finch-like warble beginning with 2-3 clear whistled notes and breaking into a buzzy trill. More musical and varied than Spotted Towhee's song. Often sings from exposed shrub top.
- **Calls:** Distinctive catlike *mew* — similar to Spotted Towhee's call but thinner and higher-pitched.

### Evolutionary Note

Green-tailed Towhee is the smallest *Pipilo* towhee and the only one with prominently olive-green plumage. Its placement in *Pipilo* has been debated — some molecular analyses suggest it may be sister to the *Pipilo* clade rather than embedded within it, potentially warranting generic separation. The rufous crown combined with green body produces a color combination unique among North American sparrows. The species replaces Spotted Towhee at higher elevations and in drier mountain shrub habitats across the interior West, with minimal range overlap [P24, P27, O1].

## Lark Sparrow (*Chondestes grammacus*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; Not ESA-listed; PIF low concern (PNW populations stable to declining)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-17 cm
- **Wingspan:** 25-28 cm
- **Mass:** 24-33 g
- **Plumage:** One of the most distinctively marked sparrows in North America. Bold head pattern: chestnut and white crown stripes, white supercilium, chestnut auriculars bordered by black, white submoustachial, black malar. Unstreaked gray-white breast with central dark breast spot. Brown-streaked back. Long, rounded tail with bold white corners and edges (visible in flight). The elaborate head pattern is unmistakable.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike; immatures show duller head pattern.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open grassland with scattered trees or fenceposts for song perches, dry oak savanna, agricultural margins, sagebrush-grassland edges, and open woodland. Requires a mosaic of short grass or bare ground (foraging), scattered elevated perches (singing), and sparse shrub or grass (nesting). In the PNW, most common in the arid interior valleys — the Okanogan, Columbia Basin, and Rogue and Umpqua valleys.
- **Wintering:** Southern US and Mexico; mostly vacates PNW.
- **Elevation range:** 500-4,500 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — grassland-woodland edge)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — dry savanna)
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level in open areas adjacent to scattered trees or fenceposts.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weed, grain) and insects (grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars)
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks and hops on bare ground and short grass, picking seeds and insects from surface. Often forages on roads and road shoulders. Gregarious outside breeding season, forming post-breeding flocks.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insects increase during breeding; seeds dominate otherwise.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass, weed stems, twigs, lined with fine grass and hair
- **Nest location:** On ground or in low shrub, 0-3 ft above ground, often at base of grass tuft or small shrub
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (primarily female)
- **Fledging:** 9-12 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-July
- **Courtship display:** Males perform a distinctive ground display, strutting before females with tail fanned to show white markings, wings spread and drooped — one of the most elaborate courtship displays among PNW sparrows.

### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS 1966-2019: -1.8% annually) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 13 million (North America) [O4]
- **Threats:** Grassland and open-woodland habitat loss. Agricultural intensification. Fire suppression converting open savanna to closed woodland. The species has declined across much of its western range as open grassland-woodland mosaic habitats are lost.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Rich, varied, musical song — a complex series of clear whistles, buzzes, and trills, often including mimicked phrases from other species. Each male's song lasts 5-15 seconds and contains 10-20+ distinct phrase types. One of the most complex and varied songs among North American sparrows, rivaling Brewer's Sparrow.
- **Calls:** Metallic *tink*.

### Evolutionary Note

Lark Sparrow is the sole member of *Chondestes*, an isolated genus within Passerellidae. Its elaborate head pattern, long fan-shaped tail with bold white markings, and complex courtship display are unique among PNW sparrows. The tail-fanning courtship display is functionally convergent with lark behavior (Alaudidae) — a parallel noted in both the common name and the display behavior. The species' phylogenetic isolation within the family suggests an ancient divergence from other sparrow lineages [P24, P27].

| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |

### Migration

- **Spring arrival:** Late April to May
- **Fall departure:** August-September
- **Wintering grounds:** Southern US and Mexico
- **Distance:** Medium-distance migrant; 1,500-3,500 km

## Grasshopper Sparrow (*Ammodramus savannarum*)

**Family:** Passerellidae
**Status:** Breeding (declining)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC globally; PIF high concern for western populations; State-listed as Endangered (WA), Sensitive (OR)

### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 18-20 cm
- **Mass:** 14-20 g
- **Plumage:** Small, flat-headed, short-tailed. Buffy-brown above with dark streaking and rufous-edged feathers, buffy unstreaked breast and flanks (one of the few PNW sparrows with completely unstreaked underparts), buffy face with dark crown stripes and a narrow pale central crown stripe, yellowish-orange loral spot. The flat head profile and stubby tail give a distinctive "hunched" silhouette unlike other PNW sparrows.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal. Sexes alike.

### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Native bunchgrass prairie and open grassland with minimal shrub encroachment. Requires large contiguous grassland patches (>40 hectares) with mixed-height grass and minimal to no woody cover. In the PNW, restricted to remnant native prairies in eastern WA and OR — the Columbia Basin bunchgrass steppe, Palouse prairie remnants, and scattered grassland patches in the Willamette Valley.
- **Wintering:** Southern US and Mexico; entirely vacates PNW.
- **Elevation range:** 500-4,000 ft
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — native grassland)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** None significant in PNW
- **Microhabitat:** Ground level in bunchgrass. Strongly area-sensitive — absent from small or fragmented grassland patches.

### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Grasshoppers and other orthopterans (the most insectivorous PNW sparrow during breeding season), beetles, caterpillars, spiders, and seeds
- **Foraging strategy:** Walks on ground through bunchgrass, catching grasshoppers and other large insects. Runs rather than flies when disturbed. The heavy insect diet (especially grasshoppers — hence the name) distinguishes it from most other grassland sparrows.
- **Seasonal variation:** Strongly insectivorous during breeding (~65% animal matter); seeds increase in fall.

### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cup of grass with overarching canopy of woven grass stems forming a domed or partially domed structure — one of the few PNW sparrows to build a partially domed nest
- **Nest location:** On ground concealed in dense bunchgrass, often at base of grass tuft with overhanging stems providing cover
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 11-12 days (female)
- **Fledging:** 9-10 days
- **Broods per year:** 1-2
- **Breeding season:** May-August (later than most PNW sparrows; often renests after early failures)

### Population

- **Trend:** Severely declining (BBS 1966-2019: -3.4% annually — one of the steepest declines among PNW sparrows) [G4]
- **Estimate:** 31 million (North America, all subspecies) — but western populations constitute a small and declining fraction [O4]
- **Threats:** Native prairie conversion to agriculture (>99% of Palouse prairie is gone). Grassland fragmentation below minimum area thresholds. Invasive grass (cheatgrass, medusahead) replacing native bunchgrass. Mowing and haying during nesting season. Shrub encroachment from fire suppression. Climate-driven changes to grassland composition. The Grasshopper Sparrow's decline in the PNW mirrors the broader crisis of North American grassland birds — the most rapidly declining avian guild on the continent.
- **State status:** Endangered in WA [G9]; Sensitive in OR [G12]. Listed as a Species of Greatest Conservation Need in both states.
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12]

### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Thin, insect-like buzz — a brief, high-pitched *tik tik tzzzzzzzzzz*. So insect-like that it is frequently mistaken for a grasshopper or cricket. One of the least melodious sparrow songs. A secondary song, longer and more complex, is delivered during dawn or flight displays but rarely heard.
- **Calls:** Thin, high *tsip*.

### Evolutionary Note

Grasshopper Sparrow is the most widespread member of *Ammodramus*, a small genus of short-tailed grassland specialists. The genus includes some of the most cryptic and secretive North American sparrows — birds adapted to spend their entire lives in dense grass at ground level, rarely flying, and singing songs that sound more like insects than birds. The flat-headed profile, short tail, and terrestrial habits of *Ammodramus* represent a morphological syndrome for life in open grassland — convergent with Old World *Cisticola* warblers in similar habitats. The PNW subspecies *perpallidus* is among the palest and most range-restricted forms, tied to remnant native prairies [P24, P27, O3].

### Migration

- **Spring arrival:** Late April to May
- **Fall departure:** August-September
- **Wintering grounds:** Southern US through Central America
- **Distance:** Medium to long-distance migrant; 1,500-4,000 km
