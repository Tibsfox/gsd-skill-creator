# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 1F: Icteridae, Fringillidae, Remaining Families, and PNW Subspecies Complexes

> **Module 1F deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document covers the final passerine families in the AOS taxonomic sequence — Icteridae (blackbirds, meadowlarks, orioles), Fringillidae (finches, crossbills, grosbeaks), Passeridae (Old World sparrows), and Calcariidae (longspurs, buntings) — followed by an extended treatment of seven PNW subspecies complexes whose geographic variation, hybridization patterns, and cryptic diversity define the region's evolutionary landscape.
>
> **Design principle:** The finches and blackbirds bookend the passerine radiation. Fringillidae, the true finches, represent one of the most recent radiations in songbird evolution — their bill morphology, irruptive population dynamics, and cryptic species boundaries (particularly in Red Crossbill) make them a living laboratory for speciation research. Icteridae, the New World blackbirds, demonstrate extreme ecological versatility — from the most abundant land bird in North America (Red-winged Blackbird) to brood parasites (Brown-headed Cowbird) to species in precipitous decline (Tricolored Blackbird). The subspecies complexes section synthesizes geographic variation data across all Module 1 families, documenting the PNW as a region where isolation, intergradation, and adaptive radiation produce taxonomic complexity that challenges species boundaries.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations.

## Family Icteridae — Blackbirds, Orioles, Meadowlarks

Order Passeriformes. New World family of ~100 species; 7 regularly occurring in the PNW. Icterids are characterized by strong sexual dichromatism, diverse mating systems (from monogamy to extreme polygyny), and broad ecological range. The family includes granivores, insectivores, frugivores, and an obligate brood parasite. PNW icterids span all terrestrial ecoregion zones, with highest diversity in wetland (Zone 5) and steppe (Zone 7) habitats.

### ★ Red-winged Blackbird (*Agelaius phoeniceus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Agelaius* > *A. phoeniceus*
**Status:** Resident and breeding migrant
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed federal or state; PIF assessment: common bird in steep decline

#### Morphometrics

Length 17–23 cm; wingspan 31–40 cm; mass 32–77 g (males significantly larger; sexual size dimorphism ratio ~1.5). Males: glossy black with brilliant scarlet-and-yellow epaulets (lesser and median coverts) displayed during territorial advertisement; epaulets concealed when subordinate. Females: heavily streaked dark brown, superficially resembling large sparrows; pale supercilium, pinkish wash on throat in some populations. Juveniles resemble females. PNW subspecies *A. p. caurinus* (western coastal) and *A. p. nevadensis* (Great Basin/interior) differ subtly in female plumage saturation and bill dimensions [P16].

#### Abundance and Population

The Red-winged Blackbird holds a position without parallel in North American ornithology: it is the most abundant native land bird on the continent, with a breeding population estimated at 210 million individuals across North America [O4]. Despite this extraordinary abundance, BBS data document a cumulative decline of approximately 30% since 1966 [G4]. In the PNW, the species remains common to abundant across all lowland zones (Zones 3, 4, 5, 7), with highest densities in freshwater marshes (Zone 5) where breeding aggregations can exceed 100 nesting females per hectare in optimal cattail (*Typha*) stands.

The species' abundance masks important population dynamics. Winter roost aggregations in the Willamette Valley and Columbia Basin can reach 100,000–500,000 individuals, mixing local breeders with migrants from northern populations. These winter concentrations create agricultural conflicts (grain damage) that have historically driven large-scale lethal control programs, particularly in the 1960s–1970s when USDA killed tens of millions annually with surfactant sprays. Current management relies primarily on non-lethal deterrents [G1].

#### Habitat

Breeds in freshwater and brackish marshes (primary), wet meadows, agricultural ditches, roadside swales, and lake margins. Requires emergent vegetation — cattails (*Typha*), bulrushes (*Schoenoplectus*), or willows — for nest attachment. In the PNW, highest breeding densities occur in Klamath Basin marshes (Zone 5), Malheur NWR wetlands (Zone 5/7), Skagit Valley floodplain (Zone 5), and Columbia Basin irrigated agriculture (Zone 7).

Winter habitat shifts to agricultural fields, feedlots, and open grasslands. Roosts communally in marshes and riparian thickets. Elevation range: sea level to approximately 5,000 ft, occasionally higher in east-side valleys [O1, O2].

#### Diet and Foraging

Breeding season: primarily insectivorous — dragonflies, damselflies, mayflies, beetles, caterpillars, spiders gleaned from marsh vegetation and captured in short sallies. Non-breeding: shifts to granivorous diet — waste grain (corn, wheat, rice), weed seeds, sunflower. This dietary flexibility underlies the species' extraordinary abundance and its agricultural pest status. Nestlings fed almost exclusively arthropods [O1].

#### Reproduction

Nest: open cup woven from marsh grasses and sedges, lined with fine grass, attached to emergent stems 0.3–1.5 m above water. Clutch: 3–4 eggs (range 2–5). Incubation: 11–13 days (female only). Fledging: 11–14 days. Broods: 1–2 per season in PNW.

**Mating system:** Highly polygynous. Territorial males defend 0.1–0.5 ha of marsh, attracting 1–15 females (average 5). Males display by spreading wings to expose scarlet epaulets and singing the characteristic *konk-la-ree* song from prominent perches. Female choice is driven primarily by territory quality (nest site safety, food availability) rather than male phenotype. Extra-pair paternity is high (25–50% of nestlings sired by non-territorial males), making the social mating system even more complex than the polygyny implies [O1].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding + winter | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Year-round | Abundant | Indicator (marsh health) |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Breeding + winter | Common | Characteristic |

#### Vocalizations

Primary song: *konk-la-ree* or *okaLEE* — a liquid, burry trill preceded by harsh introductory notes. One of the most recognized bird songs in North America. Males sing persistently from territory perches February–July. Call: sharp *check* or *chak*. Alarm: buzzy *dzeet*. Females give a rapid chattering *ch-ch-ch-ch* when disturbed near nest. Regional song dialects documented but less studied than in some passerines [O1].

#### Evolutionary Note

*Agelaius* blackbirds diversified in the Americas during the Pliocene (~3–5 Ma). Red-winged Blackbird is sister to Tricolored Blackbird (*A. tricolor*), with which it occasionally hybridizes in California. The Red-winged Blackbird's continent-spanning range and enormous population size make it a model for studies of sexual selection, mating system evolution, and the maintenance of polygyny. The iconic red epaulet functions as a signal of male quality and territory-holding ability — experimental epaulet-blackening studies demonstrated that males with concealed epaulets lose territories to intruders within hours, establishing the epaulet as one of the best-documented honest signals in avian behavioral ecology [O1, P2].

### ★ Western Meadowlark (*Sturnella neglecta*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Sturnella* > *S. neglecta*
**Status:** Resident (partial migrant; northern populations withdraw in winter)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not federally listed; PIF assessment: common bird in steep decline

#### Morphometrics

Length 19–26 cm; wingspan 36–41 cm; mass 89–115 g. Bright yellow underparts with bold black V-shaped breast band; upperparts brown with dark streaking providing camouflage against grassland substrate. White outer tail feathers conspicuous in flight. Sexes similar; males slightly larger. Bill long, pointed, slightly decurved — adapted for probing soil. Distinguished from Eastern Meadowlark (*S. magna*, rare in PNW) by song, call notes, and subtle plumage differences (paler flanks, less extensive malar stripe) [P16].

#### Abundance and Population

The Western Meadowlark is the state bird of both Oregon and Montana, yet it is in significant continental decline. BBS data show a cumulative decline of approximately 45% since 1966, mirroring the broader crisis affecting grassland birds — the most imperiled avian guild in North America [G4, O4]. PNW populations remain locally common in appropriate habitat (east-side steppe, agricultural grasslands, Willamette Valley prairies) but are declining as native grassland continues to be converted to agriculture, developed, or encroached by invasive vegetation.

North American breeding population estimated at 73 million, down from approximately 130 million in the late 1960s [O4]. The decline is driven primarily by habitat loss: native grassland in the PNW has been reduced by over 90% since European settlement, with the Willamette Valley and Palouse prairie particularly impacted.

#### Habitat

Breeds in native grasslands, pastures, hay fields, agricultural margins, and sagebrush steppe with grass understory. Requires open terrain with short-to-medium grass and elevated song perches (fence posts, shrubs, wires). Avoids dense vegetation, closed canopy, and heavily grazed or tilled ground. In the PNW, highest breeding densities in Columbia Basin grasslands (Zone 7), Malheur Basin (Zone 7), Willamette Valley remnant prairies (Zone 3/4), and Klamath Basin margins [O2].

Winter habitat similar but with a shift to agricultural stubble, feedlots, and open lowlands. Northern PNW breeders (northern WA, BC) partially migratory; southern populations largely resident. Elevation range: sea level to 6,000 ft in open east-side valleys.

#### Diet and Foraging

Primarily insectivorous during breeding season: beetles, grasshoppers, crickets, caterpillars, cutworms — obtained by walking through grass and probing soil with bill. The meadowlark's bill is specialized for "gaping" — inserting the closed bill into soil or grass tussocks and then opening it to expose prey, a foraging technique shared with starlings (convergent evolution, not homology). Non-breeding diet shifts to grain and weed seeds. The species' insectivory makes it valuable for agricultural pest control, a service estimated at millions of dollars annually across the western US [O1].

#### Reproduction

Nest: domed or partially domed ground nest woven from dry grasses, placed in dense grass tussock. The nest's domed architecture with side entrance provides concealment from above — an adaptation against avian predators. Clutch: 5 (range 3–7). Incubation: 13–15 days (female only). Fledging: 11–12 days. Broods: 1–2 per season. Nest parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbird is frequent and a significant source of reproductive failure in fragmented grasslands [O1].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Uncommon, declining | Visitor |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Migration, winter | Uncommon | Visitor |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Year-round | Common | Indicator (grassland health) |

#### Vocalizations

Primary song: a complex, rich, flute-like series of 7–10 gurgling and whistled phrases, descending in pitch — widely considered one of the most beautiful bird songs in western North America. The song of *S. neglecta* ("neglecta" meaning "overlooked" — Audubon initially failed to distinguish it from Eastern Meadowlark) is distinctly more complex and musical than the simple whistles of Eastern Meadowlark, and this vocal difference was the primary basis for species recognition [O3]. Males sing from conspicuous perches year-round but most intensely February–June. Flight song delivered on fluttering wings during territory establishment. Call: sharp *chupp* or *blackbird-like* rattle.

#### Evolutionary Note

The *Sturnella* meadowlarks present a classic case study in cryptic speciation driven by vocal divergence. Western and Eastern Meadowlarks are nearly identical in plumage yet completely distinct in song, with limited hybridization where ranges overlap (eastern OR, eastern WA). Molecular phylogenetics revealed that *Sturnella* is nested within a clade of South American icterids, confirming a southern origin and northward radiation. The genus diverged approximately 2.5 Ma (late Pliocene), with Western and Eastern meadowlarks diverging ~1.5 Ma, suggesting that vocal divergence preceded and drove reproductive isolation while plumage remained conserved — the opposite of the pattern seen in many wood-warbler radiations where plumage diverges first [O1, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [O4], [G4], [P16]

### Bullock's Oriole (*Icterus bullockii*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Icterus* > *I. bullockii*
**Status:** Breeding migrant (arrives April–May, departs August–September)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: moderate concern (declining)

#### Profile

Length 17–19 cm; wingspan 31 cm; mass 29–43 g. Males: brilliant orange underparts, face, and rump with black crown, back, wings, and central tail; bold white wing patch. Females and immatures: dull yellow-orange below, grayish-brown above. One of the most colorful breeding birds in the PNW east-side, arriving in riparian cottonwood galleries along major river systems (Snake, Deschutes, John Day, Okanogan) in late April [P16].

Breeds exclusively in deciduous riparian corridors within Zone 5 and Zone 7. Constructs pendant, pouch-shaped nest (8–15 cm deep) woven from plant fibers, hair, and string, suspended from outer branch tips of cottonwoods or willows — a remarkable weaving behavior. Clutch: 4–5 eggs; incubation 11 days; fledging 14 days. Diet: insects (caterpillars, beetles), nectar (from native and garden flowers), and fruit. Winters in Mexico and Central America [O1].

Bullock's and Baltimore Oriole (*I. galbula*) were formerly lumped as "Northern Oriole" (AOS 1973–1995). Hybridization in the Great Plains led to lumping, but subsequent research showed stable, narrow hybrid zone with assortative mating and strong pre-mating isolation, prompting re-split in 1995. Genomic analysis confirms approximately 0.5 Ma divergence with ongoing but limited introgression [O3].

BBS data show 34% decline since 1966 [G4]. Loss of riparian cottonwood habitat is the primary driver — cottonwood recruitment has declined precipitously along dammed PNW rivers where natural flood cycles are suppressed.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 5 (breeding, common), Zone 7 (breeding, fairly common where riparian corridors exist), Zone 4 (uncommon breeder in oak-riparian ecotone).

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [G4]

### Brewer's Blackbird (*Euphagus cyanocephalus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Euphagus* > *E. cyanocephalus*
**Status:** Resident (partial migrant; some northern withdrawal in winter)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: low concern but declining

#### Profile

Length 20–25 cm; wingspan 37 cm; mass 50–67 g. Males: entirely glossy black with purplish iridescence on head and greenish iridescence on body; pale yellow eye diagnostic. Females: dark grayish-brown, dark eye. Named for Thomas Mayo Brewer, ornithologist [P16].

The most ubiquitous and human-adapted blackbird in the PNW. Breeds in open habitats across all terrestrial zones: parking lots, agricultural fields, suburban lawns, pastures, shorelines, steppe, and mountain meadows up to 8,000 ft. Nests colonially in small groups (5–30 pairs) in shrubs, conifers, or on buildings. Clutch: 4–6 eggs; incubation 12–13 days; fledging 13 days. Diet: omnivorous — insects, grain, garbage, spiders, small vertebrates. Forages by walking on ground, often in mixed flocks with starlings and cowbirds in winter [O1].

Breeds across Zones 3, 4, 5, and 7; winters in lowland Zones 3 and 7. BBS trend: -1.5% annually since 1966, cumulative ~50% decline [G4]. Decline attributed to pesticide exposure (granular carbofuran, now banned) and competition with expanding Common Grackle populations (grackles rare in PNW, so this factor is minimal here). In the PNW, the species remains common but is declining in agricultural landscapes where nesting habitat (hedgerows, shrub margins) is being eliminated [O1, O4].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### Brown-headed Cowbird (*Molothrus ater*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Molothrus* > *M. ater*
**Status:** Breeding migrant and partial resident
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: low concern (population stable to increasing)

#### Profile

Length 17–22 cm; wingspan 36 cm; mass 38–50 g. Males: glossy black body with distinctive chocolate-brown head; short, finch-like bill. Females: entirely dull grayish-brown, faintly streaked below; pale throat. Juvenile: scaly-looking with pale feather edges [P16].

The Brown-headed Cowbird is North America's most widespread obligate brood parasite — the only PNW passerine that never builds a nest, incubates eggs, or raises its own young. Females lay eggs in the nests of other species (over 220 host species documented continent-wide), removing one or more host eggs in the process. The cowbird egg typically hatches 1–2 days before host eggs, giving the cowbird chick a size advantage. Many host species raise the cowbird young at the expense of their own offspring [O1].

**Ecological impact in the PNW:** Cowbird parasitism is a significant conservation concern for small passerines in fragmented habitats. Species particularly vulnerable in the PNW include: Song Sparrow, Yellow Warbler, Warbling Vireo, Willow Flycatcher (especially the ESA-endangered *E. t. extimus* in southern populations), and Lazuli Bunting. Parasitism rates increase with habitat fragmentation because cowbirds are edge-adapted — they forage in open areas (agricultural fields, feedlots, corrals) but penetrate forest edges to parasitize nests of interior forest species. In intact PNW old-growth, cowbird penetration is minimal; in fragmented second-growth and riparian strips, parasitism rates can reach 40–70% of host nests [O1, G4].

**Evolutionary ecology:** The cowbird's brood parasitic strategy evolved in association with nomadic bison herds on the Great Plains. Cowbirds followed bison to feed on insects flushed by the herds, making nest-building and territory defense impractical. The species expanded westward with European settlement as forests were cleared and cattle replaced bison. In the PNW, cowbirds were historically rare or absent west of the Cascades; they expanded into western WA and OR in the mid-20th century as logging created the open, fragmented habitats they exploit. This range expansion exposed naive host species (those lacking evolutionary history with brood parasites) to parasitism for the first time [O1].

Breeds across Zones 3, 4, 5, and 7; most common in Zone 7 agricultural landscapes. Female cowbirds can lay 30–40 eggs per season across multiple host nests, an extraordinary reproductive output enabled by the parasitic strategy. Diet: primarily granivorous (grass and weed seeds, waste grain), supplemented with insects during breeding season [O1].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 3 (breeding, fairly common), Zone 4 (breeding, fairly common), Zone 5 (breeding, common — riparian corridors), Zone 7 (breeding, common). Winters primarily south of PNW; some overwintering in lowland OR and southern WA.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### Yellow-headed Blackbird (*Xanthocephalus xanthocephalus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Xanthocephalus* > *X. xanthocephalus*
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: low concern

#### Profile

Length 21–26 cm; wingspan 42 cm; mass 44–100 g (strong sexual size dimorphism). Males unmistakable: bright golden-yellow head, neck, and breast contrasting with black body; white wing patch. Females: dark brown with dull yellow breast and face. One of the most striking blackbirds in North America [P16].

Breeds in large, deep freshwater marshes with dense stands of cattail (*Typha*) and bulrush (*Schoenoplectus*). In the PNW, primary breeding habitat is in Zone 5 and Zone 7 wetlands: Klamath Basin, Malheur NWR, Columbia Basin irrigation reservoirs, Turnbull NWR, and Summer Lake WMA. Absent or very rare as a breeder west of the Cascades — requires deeper, more extensive marsh than available in most western PNW wetlands. Colonial breeder; males polygynous (1–6 females per territory). Competitively dominant over Red-winged Blackbird — displaces RWBL from the deepest marsh center, relegating RWBL to shallower marsh periphery. This habitat partitioning is one of the best-documented examples of interspecific competition structuring bird community spatial organization [O1].

Nest: open cup woven around emergent stems, over water. Clutch: 3–5 eggs; incubation 11–13 days; fledging 9–12 days. Diet: insects (breeding), grain and seeds (non-breeding). Song: a harsh, mechanical, strangled buzzing — widely described as one of the least musical bird songs in North America. Winters in large flocks in Mexico and the southwestern US [O1].

BBS trend: approximately -0.7% annually since 1966 [G4]. Dependent on large, intact marshes increasingly threatened by water diversion, drought, and agricultural conversion.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### Tricolored Blackbird (*Agelaius tricolor*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Icteridae > *Agelaius* > *A. tricolor*
**Status:** Rare breeding visitor (irregular, range periphery)
**Conservation:** IUCN EN (Endangered); not federally listed; California State Candidate Endangered; ODFW sensitive species; BLM sensitive species

#### Profile

Length 18–24 cm; wingspan 31–37 cm; mass 40–65 g. Males similar to Red-winged Blackbird but with deeper red (dark crimson) epaulet bordered by white (not yellow) median covert bar. Females darker and more uniformly dark than female RWBL. Distinguished from Red-winged Blackbird by white epaulet border, darker female plumage, and sharply different vocalizations (harsher, more nasal than RWBL) [P16].

The Tricolored Blackbird is one of the most range-restricted and rapidly declining icterids in North America. Over 99% of the global population breeds in California's Central Valley, with a total population estimated at approximately 250,000–400,000 individuals — down from an estimated several million in the mid-20th century [O4, G1]. In the PNW, the species occurs as an irregular breeder in extreme southern Oregon (Klamath Basin, Modoc Plateau margins) and as a rare vagrant elsewhere. Oregon breeding records are sporadic, involving small colonies (10–100 pairs) in large marshes [G12].

The species' extreme colonial breeding behavior — single colonies of 10,000–80,000 nests concentrated in a few hectares of marsh or grain field — makes it catastrophically vulnerable to single-site disturbances. Agricultural mowing of colonies during the nesting season has caused mass nest failure affecting tens of thousands of nests in single events. In the PNW, habitat availability and colony site fidelity are unpredictable; the species' status in Oregon is best described as marginal and precarious [G12, O4].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 5 (rare breeding, Klamath Basin marshes), Zone 7 (vagrant).

**Sources:** [O1], [O4], [G1], [G12], [P16]

## Family Fringillidae — True Finches

Order Passeriformes. Old World origin family of ~230 species worldwide; 11 species regularly occurring in the PNW plus additional irruptive winter visitors. Fringillidae are characterized by conical, seed-crushing bills, undulating flight, and in many species, irruptive population dynamics driven by boreal seed crop failures. The PNW's diverse conifer forests support an exceptionally rich fringillid assemblage — more finch species breed here than in any comparably sized region of North America. The family includes the Red Crossbill complex, arguably the most taxonomically challenging group of birds in the Western Hemisphere.

### ★ Red Crossbill (*Loxia curvirostra*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Loxia* > *L. curvirostra*
**Status:** Resident, irruptive
**Conservation:** IUCN LC (species level); individual call types may warrant separate conservation assessment; Type 1 (Cassia Crossbill) recently split as ESA candidate

#### Morphometrics

Length 14–17 cm; wingspan 25–29 cm; mass 24–40 g (varies by call type). Males: brick-red to orange-red body with dark brown wings and tail; no wing bars. Females: olive-yellow to greenish-yellow with dark wings. Juveniles: heavily streaked. The bill is the species' defining feature: uniquely crossed mandible tips, with the lower mandible curving either left or right past the upper mandible — an adaptation for prying open conifer cone scales to extract seeds. Bill depth, width, and palate structure vary among call types, reflecting specialization on different conifer species [P16, P21, P22].

#### The Call Type Problem — Cryptic Species in the PNW

Red Crossbills present the most complex taxonomic puzzle among PNW birds. What appears to be a single, widespread species is actually a complex of 10+ morphologically similar but ecologically and vocally distinct populations (call types), each specialized on a different conifer's cone. These call types differ consistently in:

1. **Flight call** — stereotyped *jip-jip* calls differing in frequency, duration, and structure; sonographic analysis reliably distinguishes types
2. **Bill morphology** — bill depth and palate groove width match the cone scale thickness of the preferred conifer
3. **Body size** — correlated with bill and cone size
4. **Preferred conifer** — each type demonstrates measurable foraging efficiency advantages on its specialist conifer
5. **Assortative mating** — pairs form primarily within call types, not between them

Jeffrey Groth's foundational work [P21] and Craig Benkman's ecological studies [P22] established the framework for recognizing call types as potentially distinct species. The AOS has so far split only one — Type 9 (South Hills Crossbill, now Cassia Crossbill *Loxia sinesciuris*) — as a full species, based on its sedentary behavior, genetic differentiation, and geographic isolation in the South Hills of Idaho where it coexists with lodgepole pine lacking red squirrel competition.

#### PNW Call Types (Extended)

**Type 2** — Bill depth ~9.0 mm. Specializes on ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*). The most common call type in east-side PNW montane forests (Zone 2 east slope, Zone 4 ponderosa zone, Zone 7 ponderosa margins). Flight call: a distinctive *jip-jip* lower in pitch than Type 3. Body size medium-large. In the PNW, Type 2 is the characteristic crossbill of the east Cascades ponderosa belt from the Okanogan to the Klamath Basin [P21, P22].

**Type 3** — Bill depth ~8.4 mm. Specializes on western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*). The dominant call type in west-side PNW montane and lowland forests (Zone 2 west slope, Zone 3 where conifers present). Flight call: higher-pitched, faster *jip-jip* than Type 2. Small to medium body size. This is the crossbill most often encountered by west-side PNW birders, foraging in hemlock-dominated forests from sea level to treeline. Its abundance tracks hemlock cone crops, which are variable and periodic [P21, P22].

**Type 4** — Bill depth ~8.8 mm. Specializes on Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*). Common across both slopes of the PNW Cascades where Douglas-fir dominates (Zones 2, 3, 4). Medium bill and body size. Flight call intermediate between Type 2 and Type 3. Douglas-fir is the most abundant commercial timber species in the PNW, and Type 4 crossbills are widespread wherever mature Douglas-fir produces cone crops [P21, P22].

**Type 10** — Bill depth ~7.5 mm. Specializes on Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*). The smallest-billed PNW call type, reflecting the small, thin-scaled cones of Sitka spruce. Found primarily in coastal Zone 6/Zone 3 Sitka spruce forests from northern California to southeastern Alaska. Less common and more restricted than Types 2–4 due to the narrow coastal range of Sitka spruce. This type demonstrates the finest-scale bill-cone coevolution in the complex [P21, P22].

**Other types present in PNW:** Type 5 (lodgepole pine specialist — common in Rocky Mountain influence zones and eastern OR/WA), Type 1 (now Cassia Crossbill — restricted to South Hills, ID; formerly classified within the crossbill complex). Types 7 and 8 occur as rare visitors.

#### Irruptive Dynamics

Red Crossbills are nomadic and irruptive. When the preferred conifer produces a bumper cone crop, crossbills concentrate and breed — even in midwinter. When cone crops fail, crossbills erupt from their typical range, sometimes appearing in unusual habitats and locations far from normal range. This nomadism means that crossbill abundance at any PNW location fluctuates dramatically year-to-year. A site may host hundreds of crossbills one year and none the next.

Breeding season is remarkably flexible: crossbills can nest in any month of the year when food is sufficient, though peak breeding in the PNW is February–May. Nest: bulky cup of twigs, rootlets, and bark strips on a conifer branch, often on the sheltered side. Clutch: 3–4 eggs; incubation 12–16 days (female only, fed by male); fledging 15–25 days [O1].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Year-round, irruptive | Uncommon to locally common | Characteristic (whitebark pine, subalpine fir) |
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round, irruptive | Common to abundant (cone crop dependent) | Keystone (conifer seed predator/disperser) |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Irruptive, irregular | Rare to uncommon | Visitor |

#### Evolutionary Note

The Red Crossbill complex is a textbook example of ecological speciation driven by coevolutionary arms races with conifers. Bill morphology evolves rapidly in response to cone structure — a process analogous to Darwin's finches in the Galapagos. The PNW, with its exceptional diversity of conifer species (hemlock, Douglas-fir, ponderosa, lodgepole, Sitka spruce, whitebark pine), has likely served as a diversification center for crossbill call types. The key evolutionary question — whether call types represent full species or incipient species — remains unresolved for most types. Genetic divergence among types is shallow (0.1–0.5% mitochondrial sequence divergence), suggesting recent divergence, but assortative mating by call type is strong, implying reproductive isolation may be sufficient for speciation despite gene flow [P21, P22, O3].

### ★ Evening Grosbeak (*Coccothraustes vespertinus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Coccothraustes* > *C. vespertinus*
**Status:** Resident, irruptive
**Conservation:** IUCN VU (Vulnerable — 2024 uplisting); not federally listed; PIF: Red Watch List (high concern); WDFW candidate species

#### Morphometrics

Length 16–22 cm; wingspan 30–36 cm; mass 39–73 g (heavy-bodied for a finch). Males: stunning plumage — bright yellow body with dark head, bold white secondary wing patch, massive pale greenish-yellow conical bill. Dark brown-to-black crown with bright yellow supercilium. Females: gray-brown with yellow wash on neck, black-and-white wings, large pale bill. The bill is disproportionately large and powerful, capable of crushing cherry pits and hard seeds that few other passerines can process [P16].

#### Population Crisis

The Evening Grosbeak has experienced one of the most dramatic population declines of any North American songbird. BBS data document an 92% decline across the species' range since 1970 — a collapse so severe that IUCN uplisted the species to Vulnerable in 2024 [G4, O4]. Christmas Bird Count data corroborate the trend, showing a precipitous decline in winter occurrence across the PNW and eastern North America beginning in the 1980s.

The causes of this decline remain incompletely understood, which amplifies conservation concern. Hypothesized factors include:

1. **Spruce budworm decline** — Evening Grosbeaks historically tracked outbreaks of spruce budworm (*Choristoneura*), which provided abundant breeding-season food. Widespread aerial insecticide spraying of budworm outbreaks in eastern Canada and the northern US in the 1970s–1990s reduced this food pulse.
2. **Window collision mortality** — grosbeaks are exceptionally vulnerable to window strikes; their large flocks visiting backyard feeders brought them into lethal proximity with glass.
3. **Habitat change** — logging of mature mixed conifer-hardwood forests reduces seed availability (maple, ash, cherry, box elder).
4. **Disease** — Salmonellosis at bird feeders documented as a cause of winter mortality in flock-feeding grosbeaks.
5. **Reduced winter irruptions** — climate change may be reducing boreal seed crop failure frequency, keeping northern populations sedentary rather than irrupting south where they were historically counted [O1, O4, G4].

In the PNW, Evening Grosbeaks remain locally fairly common in montane conifer and mixed forests (Zone 2), but the species is now uncommon to rare at lowland feeders where it was formerly regular in winter. Breeding population in the PNW is poorly estimated due to the species' irregular distribution and nomadic tendencies.

#### Habitat

Breeds in mature mixed conifer-hardwood forests at montane elevations (Zone 2), particularly where Douglas-fir, grand fir, or Engelmann spruce co-occur with deciduous trees (big-leaf maple, bitter cherry, Pacific dogwood) providing fruit and seed resources. Nests on horizontal conifer branches, 6–20 m above ground. Clutch: 3–4 eggs; incubation 12–14 days; fledging 13–14 days. Winter habitat shifts to lower elevations (Zone 3, Zone 4) where seed and fruit resources concentrate, and to bird feeders — formerly the primary way most people encountered this species [O1].

#### Diet and Foraging

Omnivorous with strong seasonal shifts. Breeding season: primarily insectivorous — spruce budworm larvae, beetles, caterpillars fed to nestlings. Non-breeding: frugivorous and granivorous — maple samaras, ash seeds, cherry pits, box elder seeds, sunflower seeds at feeders. The massive bill enables processing of hard seeds unavailable to smaller finches. Grosbeaks are messy feeders — they crack and discard seed hulls, creating food resources for smaller birds (chickadees, juncos) that follow grosbeak flocks [O1].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Occasional (irruptive) | Rare | Visitor |
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round | Fairly common (declining) | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Winter (irruptive) | Uncommon (formerly common, declining) | Visitor |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Winter | Uncommon | Visitor |

#### Evolutionary Note

Despite its superficial resemblance to Old World hawfinches (*Coccothraustes coccothraustes*), molecular phylogenetics placed the Evening Grosbeak as sister to the Asian hawfinch genus, confirming a trans-Beringian dispersal. The species colonized North America from Asia, likely during a Pleistocene interglacial, and subsequently radiated into a distinct New World lineage. Three subspecies are recognized: *C. v. brooksi* (Pacific Northwest — smaller, brighter), *C. v. vespertinus* (eastern), and *C. v. montanus* (Mexican highlands). The Pacific form *brooksi* may warrant separate species status based on vocal and morphometric differences [O1, O3].

### Pine Siskin (*Spinus pinus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Spinus* > *S. pinus*
**Status:** Resident, irruptive
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: moderate concern (declining)

#### Profile

Length 11–14 cm; wingspan 18–22 cm; mass 12–18 g. Small, streaked, brown finch with pointed bill, notched tail, and distinctive yellow wing stripe and tail base visible in flight. Sexes similar; males slightly more yellow. The most heavily streaked PNW finch — distinguishable from female goldfinches by smaller size, sharper bill, and stronger streaking [P16].

Pine Siskins are the quintessential irruptive finch of PNW conifer forests. Breeding population in the PNW is substantial during cone crop years, with the species common to abundant across Zones 1, 2, 3, and 4. During boreal seed crop failures, northern siskin populations irrupt southward in enormous flights — winter irruption years can bring millions of siskins into the PNW lowlands, swarming feeders and weedy fields. In non-irruption years, the species may be uncommon at lowland sites [O1, O2].

Breeds in conifer and mixed forests across all forested zones. Nest: small cup on conifer branch, often well-concealed. Clutch: 3–4 eggs; incubation 13 days; fledging 14–15 days. Diet: conifer seeds (primary), alder catkins, birch seeds, weed seeds, and insects during breeding. Forages acrobatically, often hanging upside down on cone clusters — behavior convergent with goldfinches and titmice. Winter flocks of 50–500+ at feeders are characteristic of irruption years [O1].

BBS trend: -1.6% annually since 1966 [G4]. Population fluctuations make trend assessment difficult, but the long-term trajectory is downward. Vulnerable to salmonellosis at bird feeders — major die-offs documented at PNW feeding stations during wet, mild winters when bacterial growth is favored.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 1 (breeding, uncommon), Zone 2 (breeding, common to abundant), Zone 3 (year-round, variable), Zone 4 (year-round, fairly common), Zone 5 (riparian, uncommon). Irruptive dynamics mean abundance varies 10-fold between years.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### American Goldfinch (*Spinus tristis*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Spinus* > *S. tristis*
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; state bird of Washington

#### Profile

Length 11–14 cm; wingspan 19–22 cm; mass 11–20 g. Breeding male: brilliant lemon-yellow body, black cap, black wings with white wing bars, white undertail coverts. Non-breeding male and female: dull olive-yellow to brownish with wing bars retained. The only PNW finch with a complete pre-basic and pre-alternate molt — breeding males undergo a dramatic transformation from winter drab to summer brilliance [P16].

The American Goldfinch is one of the latest-nesting passerines in the PNW, timing its breeding to coincide with the peak seed production of thistles (*Cirsium*), dandelions, and other composites in July–August. This late breeding schedule distinguishes it from nearly all other PNW songbirds, which nest April–June. Nest: tightly woven cup of plant fibers and thistle down, placed in deciduous shrub or tree, 1–10 m above ground. Clutch: 4–6 eggs; incubation 12–14 days; fledging 11–17 days. Diet: almost exclusively seeds — composite seeds (thistle, dandelion, sunflower), tree seeds (birch, alder), and weed seeds. One of the strictest granivores among PNW passerines; feeds nestlings regurgitated seeds rather than insects, unusual among songbirds [O1].

Common across Zones 3, 4, and 5 year-round; less common in Zone 7 (primarily riparian corridors). BBS trend: stable to slightly declining (-0.4% annually) [G4]. Washington's state bird since 1951.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### Lesser Goldfinch (*Spinus psaltria*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Spinus* > *S. psaltria*
**Status:** Resident (expanding northward)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed

#### Profile

Length 9–12 cm; wingspan 20 cm; mass 8–12 g. The smallest PNW finch. Males: black cap extending to nape (green-backed form *S. p. hesperophilus* dominates in PNW), bright yellow underparts, white wing patches. Females: dull olive-green above, yellowish below. Distinguished from American Goldfinch by smaller size, dark (not pink) bill, and dark back [P16].

A primarily Mexican and southwestern species reaching the northern edge of its range in the PNW. In Oregon, breeds regularly in the Rogue Valley, Umpqua Valley, and southern Willamette Valley (Zone 3, Zone 4). In Washington, rare but increasing, with breeding records expanding northward — documented nesting in the Puget Trough since the 2000s, a northward range expansion of approximately 200 km in 30 years consistent with climate warming [O2, G9].

Habitat: open woodlands, forest edges, gardens, weedy fields with seed-producing annuals. Nests in shrubs and trees, typically lower than American Goldfinch. Clutch: 3–5 eggs; incubation 12 days; fledging 11–15 days. Diet: weed seeds, flower buds, some insects. Mixed flocks with American Goldfinches are common in winter, complicating identification [O1].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 3 (resident, uncommon — expanding), Zone 4 (resident, fairly common in southern OR), Zone 5 (riparian, uncommon).

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [G9]

### House Finch (*Haemorhous mexicanus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Haemorhous* > *H. mexicanus*
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed

#### Profile

Length 12–15 cm; wingspan 20–25 cm; mass 16–27 g. Males: red (occasionally orange or yellow) forehead, breast, and rump; brown-streaked back, flanks, and belly. Females: brown-streaked throughout, lacking red. The red pigmentation is carotenoid-based, derived from dietary pigments — males feeding on carotenoid-poor diets develop orange or yellow plumage, an honest signal of foraging quality and potentially health [P2, P16].

Native to western North America, including the PNW, where it is an abundant resident across all lowland zones. The species underwent a dramatic range expansion in eastern North America after pet trade releases in New York City (1940s), eventually connecting eastern and western populations across the continent. In the PNW, House Finches are abundant in urban, suburban, and agricultural habitats (Zone 3, Zone 4, Zone 7), nesting on buildings, in hanging planters, in conifers, and in virtually any sheltered site. Clutch: 4–5 eggs; incubation 13–14 days; fledging 12–19 days; 2–3 broods per season. Diet: seeds, buds, fruit, and some insects [O1].

The House Finch is of medical interest for the emergence of mycoplasmal conjunctivitis (*Mycoplasma gallisepticum*) — a bacterial eye disease first documented in eastern House Finch populations in 1994 that caused significant mortality and population decline in the east. The disease has since been detected in PNW populations but at lower prevalence [O1].

Frequently confused with Purple Finch and Cassin's Finch. Key distinctions: House Finch has brown-streaked flanks (not clean flanks like Purple), shorter and more curved culmen, and a smaller, rounder head profile. BBS trend in western North America: stable to slightly declining [G4].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 3 (year-round, abundant), Zone 4 (year-round, common), Zone 5 (year-round, fairly common), Zone 7 (year-round, common in towns and agriculture).

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [G4], [P2], [P16]

### Purple Finch (*Haemorhous purpureus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Haemorhous* > *H. purpureus*
**Status:** Resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: moderate concern (declining)

#### Profile

Length 12–16 cm; wingspan 22–26 cm; mass 18–32 g. Males: raspberry-red (not orange-red like House Finch) head, breast, and back, diffusing into whitish unstreaked belly and undertail. Females: boldly patterned brown-and-white face with strong supercilium and malar stripe; heavily streaked below but cleaner flanks than House Finch female. Bill larger and less curved than House Finch. PNW subspecies *H. p. californicus* (Pacific — darker, browner female plumage than eastern *purpureus*) [P16].

A forest finch, not an urban bird — this distinction separates Purple Finch from House Finch ecologically. Breeds in moist conifer and mixed forests (Zone 2, Zone 3 forested areas), particularly in edges, openings, and riparian corridors within forest matrix. Nests on conifer branches, 3–18 m above ground. Clutch: 3–5 eggs; incubation 13 days; fledging 14 days. Diet: seeds, buds, fruit (especially in winter), and insects (breeding season). Visits feeders but less commonly than House Finch; prefers forest edge and suburban locations with mature trees over densely urbanized sites [O1].

BBS trend: -1.3% annually since 1966, cumulative ~50% decline [G4]. Decline attributed to competition with expanding House Finch populations (House Finch displaced Purple Finch from many suburban habitats) and habitat loss of mature forest edges. In the PNW, remains fairly common in appropriate forest habitat but declining in suburban and lowland areas where House Finch dominates.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 2 (breeding, fairly common), Zone 3 (year-round, uncommon — declining), Zone 4 (breeding, fairly common in riparian-oak edge). Pacific subspecies *californicus* is the resident form.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4], [P16]

### Cassin's Finch (*Haemorhous cassinii*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Haemorhous* > *H. cassinii*
**Status:** Resident (elevational migrant)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: moderate concern (declining)

#### Profile

Length 14–16 cm; wingspan 25–27 cm; mass 24–34 g. Males: bright crimson-red crown contrasting with brown nape, pinkish breast and flanks with fine streaking. Females: finely streaked brown with indistinct face pattern. Distinguished from Purple Finch by crisper red crown patch (like a beret), longer and more pointed bill, finer breast streaking, and distinctly different call. The third member of the *Haemorhous* complex (House, Purple, Cassin's), Cassin's is the high-elevation specialist [P16].

The montane counterpart to Purple Finch, breeding in open montane and subalpine conifer forests (Zones 1 and 2) on both Cascade slopes, Blue Mountains, Wallowas, and Selkirks. Prefers open canopy — ponderosa pine, lodgepole pine, and subalpine fir woodlands rather than dense, closed-canopy forest. Nests on outer conifer branches. Clutch: 4–5 eggs; incubation 12–14 days; fledging 14 days. Diet: conifer seeds (primary — buds and seeds of pine, fir, spruce), berries, and insects. An elevational migrant: breeds at 4,000–8,000+ ft, descends to foothill and lowland areas in winter, sometimes visiting feeders [O1].

BBS trend: -2.1% annually since 1966 [G4]. The steepest decline of the three *Haemorhous* finches, likely related to changing fire regimes (fire suppression closing open montane forests) and climate-driven shifts in conifer phenology affecting cone crop timing.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 1 (breeding, fairly common), Zone 2 (breeding, fairly common — east-side montane primary), Zone 4 (wintering, uncommon), Zone 7 (wintering, uncommon at ponderosa margins).

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4], [P16]

### Pine Grosbeak (*Pinicola enucleator*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Pinicola* > *P. enucleator*
**Status:** Resident (rare, irruptive)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: low concern

#### Profile

Length 20–25 cm; wingspan 33 cm; mass 52–78 g. The largest PNW finch. Males: rosy-pink head, breast, and back; gray flanks and belly; dark wings with two white wing bars. Females and immature males: gray body with olive-yellow to rusty head and rump. Bill stubby, dark, slightly hooked — adapted for crushing fruit and buds. Slow, tame demeanor — often remarkably approachable [P16].

A boreal and subalpine species at the southern edge of its North American range in the PNW. Breeds in subalpine conifer forests (Zone 1, upper Zone 2) in the North Cascades, northern Selkirks, and very locally in the Blue Mountains and Olympics. In most of the PNW, encountered primarily as a rare and irregular winter visitor at lower elevations during irruption years. Winter irruptions bring small flocks (5–30) to mountain ash, crabapple, and ornamental fruit trees at montane and lowland sites [O1, O2].

Nest: bulky cup on dense conifer branch. Clutch: 3–4 eggs; incubation 13–14 days; fledging 14–18 days. Diet: buds, berries, and seeds year-round; insects during breeding. Feeds heavily on mountain ash berries in winter, a dietary reliance shared with Bohemian Waxwing. The species' distribution in the PNW is poorly mapped due to its rarity, remote habitat, and irruptive nature [O1].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 1 (breeding, rare — local), Zone 2 (wintering, rare — irruptive), Zone 3 (winter vagrant, rare in irruption years).

### Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Leucosticte* > *L. tephrocotis*
**Status:** Resident (elevational migrant)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; PIF: Yellow Watch List (monitoring concern)

#### Profile

Length 14–18 cm; wingspan 33 cm; mass 22–60 g. Brown body with rosy-pink wash on wings, rump, and belly; gray crown extending to below eye (extent variable by subspecies); dark brown forehead and lores; dark bill in breeding season, yellow in winter. Sexes similar; males slightly pinker. The most alpine-adapted passerine in the PNW [P16].

**Alpine specialist.** Breeds exclusively in alpine tundra, snowfield edges, and cliff faces above treeline in Zone 1 — Cascades (Mt. Baker to Three Sisters), Olympics (very locally), Wallowa Mountains, and Steens Mountain. Nests in rock crevices and cliff cavities, often behind waterfalls or in inaccessible cliff faces — one of the most difficult PNW passerines to study on breeding territory. Clutch: 3–5 eggs; incubation 12–14 days; fledging 16–22 days [O1].

Diet: seeds and insects gleaned from snowfield edges. The retreating snowfield-edge foraging strategy is unique among PNW passerines — rosy-finches follow the meltline as it recedes upslope through spring and summer, feeding on insects and seeds deposited on the snow surface by wind. This "aeolian zone" feeding — consuming wind-transported arthropods that land on snow surfaces — is a remarkable ecological niche with no parallel among other PNW birds [O1, P17].

In winter, descends to subalpine and montane elevations, occasionally to lowland sites during severe weather. Winter flocks of 20–200 at ski areas, mountain passes, and exposed ridges. Kittitas County, WA (Ellensburg area) is a regular wintering site where rosy-finch flocks are accessible [O2].

Two PNW subspecies: *L. t. tephrocotis* (interior — more extensive gray crown, breeds in Cascades, Blue Mountains) and *L. t. littoralis* (Hepburn's Rosy-Finch — darker overall, more gray on face, breeds in coastal mountains including Olympics). Some authorities have treated *littoralis* as a separate species [O3].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 1 (breeding, uncommon — locally fairly common), Zone 2 (wintering, uncommon). PIF Yellow Watch List due to remote habitat, small population, and climate vulnerability [O4].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [O4], [P17], [P16]

### Common Redpoll (*Acanthis flammea*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Fringillidae > *Acanthis* > *A. flammea*
**Status:** Irruptive winter visitor
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed

#### Profile

Length 12–14 cm; wingspan 19–22 cm; mass 11–16 g. Small, streaked finch with red forehead patch, tiny yellow bill, dark chin, streaked flanks. Males show variable pink wash on breast. Distinguished from Pine Siskin by red forehead, smaller bill, less streaking, and absence of yellow wing stripe [P16].

An Arctic and subarctic breeder that reaches the PNW only during winter irruption years when boreal birch and alder seed crops fail. Irruptions are highly irregular — absent most winters, then appearing in large flocks (50–500+) at birch groves, alder thickets, and weedy fields. In the PNW, most irruptive records are from northeastern WA, northern ID, and southern BC, with decreasing frequency southward. Rare in western WA and OR even during major irruptions [O2].

Diet in PNW: birch catkins, alder seeds, weed seeds. Forages acrobatically on branch tips, often in mixed flocks with Pine Siskins and goldfinches. Remarkably cold-tolerant — metabolic studies show Common Redpolls can survive ambient temperatures of -50°C with access to adequate food, the most extreme cold tolerance documented in any songbird. Achieved through increased fat deposition (can store fat equal to 10% body mass in a single day), regulated hypothermia (overnight torpor reduces metabolic rate 20–40%), and esophageal diverticulum (a crop-like pouch storing up to 2 g of seeds for overnight digestion) [O1].

**Taxonomic note:** The AOS lumped Common Redpoll and Hoary Redpoll (*A. hornemanni*) into a single species ("Redpoll") in 2024, based on genomic evidence showing minimal genetic differentiation. This decision is controversial; field observers continue to report distinguishable phenotypes. Under the lumped taxonomy, the combined species is simply Redpoll (*Acanthis flammea*) [O3].

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 2 (irruptive winter, rare), Zone 3 (irruptive winter, rare), Zone 7 (irruptive winter, rare). Absent most years.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3]

## Family Passeridae — Old World Sparrows

### House Sparrow (*Passer domesticus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Passeridae > *Passer* > *P. domesticus*
**Status:** Resident (introduced, non-native)
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed; no conservation protections (unprotected under MBTA)

#### Profile

Length 14–18 cm; wingspan 19–25 cm; mass 24–39 g. Males: gray crown, chestnut nape, black bib (size variable — honest signal of dominance), white cheek, brown-streaked back. Females: plain brown-gray, unstreaked below, with pale supercilium and buffy eye stripe. Often confused with native sparrows by beginning birders, but stouter body, thicker bill, and urban habitat distinguish it [P16].

The House Sparrow is arguably the most successful avian invasive species globally. Introduced to North America in the 1850s (Brooklyn, New York), it spread across the continent within 50 years, reaching the PNW by the 1890s. It is now a year-round resident in virtually every PNW town and city, nesting in building cavities, crevices, signage, traffic lights, and nest boxes. Absent from contiguous forest and wilderness — distribution is entirely commensal with human habitation [O1].

**Ecological impact:** House Sparrows compete with native cavity-nesting species for nest sites. In the PNW, documented competitive displacement of: Purple Martin (evicted from nest boxes), Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Western Bluebird, and Black-capped Chickadee. House Sparrows aggressively defend cavities, destroying eggs and killing nestlings and adults of competing species. Nest box programs for bluebirds and swallows in the PNW routinely require House Sparrow management [O1].

Clutch: 3–7 eggs; incubation 10–14 days; fledging 14–17 days; 2–4 broods per season (one of the highest reproductive outputs of any PNW passerine). Diet: primarily grain and seeds, supplemented by human food scraps, insects (breeding season). Roosts communally in dense evergreen vegetation or building crevices [O1].

BBS trend in North America: -3.5% annually since 1966, cumulative decline >84% [G4]. Despite being an abundant invasive, House Sparrow populations have declined dramatically — a pattern documented globally and attributed to changes in urban food availability (reduced grain spillage, cleaner streets), building renovation eliminating nest sites, and competition from other urban-adapted species.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 3 (year-round, common — urban only), Zone 4 (year-round, fairly common — towns), Zone 7 (year-round, fairly common — agricultural towns). Absent from Zones 1, 2, 6.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [G4]

## Family Calcariidae — Longspurs and Snow Buntings

A small family (6 species) of Arctic and grassland breeders formerly classified within Emberizidae. Two species reach the PNW as winter visitors.

### Lapland Longspur (*Calcarius lapponicus*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Calcariidae > *Calcarius* > *C. lapponicus*
**Status:** Winter visitor
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed

#### Profile

Length 14–17 cm; wingspan 22–28 cm; mass 20–33 g. Non-breeding (as seen in PNW): streaked brown upperparts, pale underparts with streaked flanks, rusty edges to greater coverts, indistinct face pattern with buffy ear-covert border. Breeding males (rarely seen in PNW): black face and breast, chestnut nape, white face stripe — striking Arctic breeding plumage. Long hind claw (the "spur") adapted for walking on flat terrain [P16].

An Arctic tundra breeder (circumpolar distribution) that winters in open agricultural fields, stubble, and plowed ground in the PNW. Most common in eastern WA and OR (Zone 7 — Columbia Basin, Malheur Basin), less frequent west of Cascades. Winter flocks of 10–200+ associate with Horned Larks and Snow Buntings on bare agricultural ground. Diet: grass and weed seeds gleaned from ground surface. Arrives October–November, departs March–April [O1, O2].

Difficult to detect — highly cryptic in winter plumage, hugging the ground and flushing only when nearly stepped upon, giving a dry rattling *tick-tick-tick* flight call. More commonly heard than seen.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 7 (wintering, uncommon — locally fairly common in Columbia Basin), Zone 3 (wintering, rare). Winters primarily on bare agricultural ground and short-grass fields.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2]

### Snow Bunting (*Plectrophenax nivalis*)

**Taxonomic Position:** Passeriformes > Calcariidae > *Plectrophenax* > *P. nivalis*
**Status:** Winter visitor
**Conservation:** IUCN LC; not listed

#### Profile

Length 15–18 cm; wingspan 28–36 cm; mass 28–50 g. Non-breeding (PNW): white underparts and wing patches contrasting with tawny-brown head and back; large white wing patches flash conspicuously in flight. Breeding males (not in PNW): pure white with black back, wingtips, and tail center — the most extensively white passerine in the Northern Hemisphere [P16].

A high-Arctic breeder (northernmost breeding passerine in the world — recorded breeding at 83°N in Greenland) that reaches the PNW as an uncommon winter visitor. In the PNW, found primarily on exposed beaches (Zone 6 — outer coast, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Ocean Shores), agricultural fields (Zone 7), and alpine summits during early winter storms. Flocks of 5–50 at coastal and agricultural sites November–March. Feeds on weed seeds and grain [O1, O2].

Snow Buntings are the only PNW passerine whose breeding range is entirely Arctic. Their winter occurrence in the PNW represents the extreme southern edge of their non-breeding range. More regular in interior BC and northeastern WA than in western lowlands.

**Ecoregion presence:** Zone 6 (wintering, uncommon — coastal beaches), Zone 7 (wintering, uncommon — agricultural fields), Zone 1 (rare early winter, alpine — transient). Absent most of PNW; localized in appropriate open habitat.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2]

## PNW Subspecies Complexes

The Pacific Northwest is a natural laboratory for studying geographic variation in birds. The region's topographic complexity — a north-south mountain spine bisecting wet coastal forests from dry interior steppe, interspersed with islands, river valleys, and isolated mountain ranges — creates a mosaic of geographic barriers and corridors that fragment populations, promote local adaptation, and generate clinal variation. Seven subspecies complexes are treated in depth here, each illustrating a different mode of divergence: insular gigantism, vocal evolution, hybrid zone dynamics, plumage clines, phylogeographic structure, introgression, and cultural transmission.

These complexes are not academic curiosities. They have direct conservation implications: subspecies are eligible for ESA protection [P20], hybrid zones inform species boundary decisions that affect land management, and cryptic diversity within nominal species means that population declines may mask the loss of distinct evolutionary lineages.

---

### ◆ Song Sparrow Complex (*Melospiza melodia*)

#### Overview

The Song Sparrow is the most geographically variable bird species in North America, with 24 recognized subspecies continent-wide and 6+ subspecies occurring in the PNW. This extraordinary morphological diversity — spanning a 2.5-fold range in body mass (12–51 g) and from pale, small, streaked desert forms to massive, dark, heavily pigmented coastal-island forms — has made *M. melodia* a foundational model organism for studies of geographic variation, adaptation, and the subspecies concept since the early 20th century [P24].

Patten and Pruett (2009) [P24] argued that Song Sparrow geographic variation across western North America exhibits ring-species-like properties: adjacent populations intergrade freely, but terminal forms (e.g., the tiny desert *fallax* and the massive Aleutian *maxima*) are so different that they would likely fail to interbreed if brought into secondary contact. In the PNW, the Song Sparrow complex demonstrates three evolutionary phenomena simultaneously: (1) latitudinal and coastal melanin clines (darker and more heavily pigmented toward the humid coast), (2) insular gigantism (island populations are larger-bodied than adjacent mainland populations), and (3) adaptive bill size variation correlated with local seed/prey size distributions.

#### PNW Subspecies

**Melospiza melodia morphallax** — The coastal giant. Breeds from the outer coast of Vancouver Island south through coastal WA and OR. This is the largest Song Sparrow subspecies in the contiguous US: mass 28–38 g (up to 40 g in some coastal island populations), length 16–18 cm. Dark sooty-brown overall with heavy, diffuse breast streaking. Breeds in coastal scrub, driftwood margins, salt marsh edges, and beach wrack habitat (Zone 6). The large body size is consistent with Bergmann's Rule (larger body in cooler, more humid climates) and may also reflect adaptation to marine-influenced diet including intertidal invertebrates. Bill is large and robust compared to interior subspecies [P16, P24].

**Melospiza melodia rufina** — Breeds throughout the Pacific lowlands from southern BC through western WA and OR (Zone 3, Zone 5). Medium-large (mass 24–32 g), dark brown with rufous-tinted streaking. The most commonly encountered Song Sparrow subspecies in the PNW lowlands — the bird of suburban hedgerows, wetland margins, and riparian thickets from Vancouver BC to Eugene OR. Intergrades with *morphallax* on the outer coast and with *merrilli* east of the Cascades. The large, contiguous population of *rufina* functions as a genetic bridge between coastal and interior forms [P24].

**Melospiza melodia merrilli** — The interior form. Breeds in intermontane valleys and riparian corridors east of the Cascades (Zone 5, Zone 7) from southern BC through eastern WA and OR. Paler and grayer than west-side subspecies, with crisper, narrower breast streaks on a white background. Smaller than coastal forms (mass 20–28 g). Adapted to drier, more continental climate. Found in willow-cottonwood riparian corridors along major east-side rivers (Deschutes, John Day, Grande Ronde, Okanogan). Migratory (partially) — unlike the largely sedentary coastal forms, some *merrilli* populations withdraw southward in winter [P16, P24].

**Melospiza melodia maxillaris** — Suisun Marsh and San Francisco Bay form, reaching the southern PNW periphery in the Klamath Basin margins. Small-billed salt-marsh specialist. Included here for biogeographic completeness; its presence at the PNW southern boundary connects the regional subspecies gradient to the California coast radiation.

**Melospiza melodia insignis** and **M. m. kenaiensis** — Northern forms from coastal BC and Alaska that enter the PNW study area in coastal BC. *Insignis* (Kodiak Island) and *kenaiensis* (Kenai Peninsula) are among the largest subspecies; their geographic proximity to PNW *morphallax* populations suggests continuous clinal variation rather than sharp subspecies boundaries [P24].

#### Melanin Clines and Gloger's Rule

PNW Song Sparrows provide one of the best examples of Gloger's Rule in North American birds: populations in humid environments develop darker, more melanized plumage, while populations in arid environments are paler. Moving from the Olympic coast (annual precipitation >100 inches) to the Columbia Basin (annual precipitation <10 inches), Song Sparrow plumage shifts from dark sooty-brown (*morphallax*) to pale gray-brown (*merrilli*) — a continuous cline trackable across individual drainages. The melanin gradient correlates with humidity, not temperature: equally cold but humid Sitka spruce forests produce dark sparrows, while equally cold but dry high-desert basins produce pale sparrows. The adaptive basis may involve feather durability (melanized feathers resist bacterial degradation better in humid environments) or crypsis (dark plumage matches dark, wet substrates) [P24].

#### Conservation Implications

Song Sparrow remains common across most of the PNW (BBS trend: stable, ~0.1% annually [G4]), but subspecies-level conservation is warranted for distinctive coastal and insular forms. The salt-marsh populations of *morphallax* on the outer coast face habitat loss from sea-level rise, shoreline armoring, and coastal development. If these genetically distinctive populations are lost, the species continues — but a unique evolutionary lineage shaped by thousands of years of coastal adaptation disappears with them [P20, P24].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [G4], [P16], [P20], [P24]

### ◆ Red Crossbill Call Types — Cryptic Species Complex

#### Overview

(Extended treatment above in Fringillidae section; this entry synthesizes the subspecies-complex perspective.)

The Red Crossbill call type system represents the most extreme case of cryptic diversity in any PNW bird. Ten or more call types, each ecologically specialized on a different conifer cone, coexist across the region with limited hybridization despite being morphologically similar and genetically shallow in their divergence. The key question — how many species does this represent? — has direct conservation implications. If each call type is a separate species, several have small populations and restricted ranges that would qualify for conservation attention. Under the single-species model, the Red Crossbill is a common, widespread species of low concern [P21, P22].

#### Speciation Mechanism

The crossbill complex is a model for **ecological speciation** — divergence driven by adaptation to different ecological niches rather than by geographic isolation. The speciation sequence appears to be:

1. **Resource partitioning** — different populations specialize on different conifer cones
2. **Morphological divergence** — bill size and palate structure evolve to match cone characteristics
3. **Vocal divergence** — flight calls diverge as a byproduct of or signal for assortative mating
4. **Assortative mating** — pairs form within call types, reducing gene flow
5. **Reproductive isolation** — gene flow reduced sufficiently to maintain type-specific adaptations

In the PNW, this sequence is observable in real time. Types 2, 3, 4, and 10 co-occur at sites where multiple conifer species grow (e.g., mixed hemlock-Douglas-fir-ponderosa forests on the east Cascade slope), yet they maintain distinct bill morphology and call type. Captive studies show that crossbill pairs form assortatively by call type even when housed together, and that call type is culturally transmitted (young crossbills learn their call type from parents, not from genetics alone) — raising the question of whether cultural evolution is driving speciation in this complex [P21, P22].

#### Conservation Status by Type

| Call Type | Preferred Conifer | PNW Range | Est. Population | Concern |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Type 2 | Ponderosa pine | East Cascades, Blue Mtns | Large | Low |
| Type 3 | Western hemlock | West Cascades, coast | Moderate | Moderate |
| Type 4 | Douglas-fir | Both Cascade slopes | Large | Low |
| Type 5 | Lodgepole pine | East OR/WA, Rockies | Large | Low |
| Type 10 | Sitka spruce | Coastal | Small | Elevated |
| Type 9 (Cassia) | Lodgepole pine (South Hills, ID) | South Hills ID only | ~5,800 | High (ESA candidate) |

The Cassia Crossbill (Type 9, *Loxia sinesciuris*) was split as a full species in 2017 — the first new bird species described from the contiguous US in over a century. It is sedentary in the South Hills and Albion Mountains of Idaho, where lodgepole pines lack red squirrels as seed competitors. This absence of squirrel competition allowed the crossbill population to evolve a larger bill and sedentary behavior. The estimated population of ~5,800 individuals in a 70 km2 range makes it one of the most range-restricted bird species in North America [P22, O3, G1].

**Sources:** [O1], [O3], [P21], [P22], [G1]

### ◆ Northern Flicker Hybrid Zone (*Colaptes auratus*)

#### Overview

The Northern Flicker presents one of the best-documented avian hybrid zones in North America. Two formerly recognized species — Red-shafted Flicker (*C. a. cafer*, western) and Yellow-shafted Flicker (*C. a. auratus*, eastern) — interbreed extensively through a hybrid zone running from southern Alaska through British Columbia and south along the eastern slope of the Rocky Mountains. The PNW sits squarely within this zone of contact and intergradation [P3].

#### Phenotypic Differences

**Red-shafted form** (*C. a. cafer*): salmon-red shafts and underwing linings, gray face, brown crown, no red nape crescent (or reduced), red malar stripe (male). The dominant form throughout the PNW west of the Cascades and in most of Oregon and Washington.

**Yellow-shafted form** (*C. a. auratus*): golden-yellow shafts and underwing linings, tan face with gray crown, red nape crescent, black malar stripe (male). Occurs as a pure form only in northeastern extremes of the PNW study area (northeastern WA, northern ID, eastern BC).

**Hybrids**: any combination of parental characters — orange shafts (intermediate), mixed malar color, variable nape crescents. In the PNW east Cascades (Okanogan, Methow Valley, eastern BC), hybrid individuals constitute 20–50% of the population. Hybrid frequency decreases rapidly westward and southwestward; by the Willamette Valley and Puget Trough, virtually all flickers are phenotypically pure Red-shafted [P3].

#### Genomic Analysis

Aguillon et al. (2018) [P3] used genome-wide markers to characterize the genomic architecture of the Red-shafted × Yellow-shafted hybrid zone. Key findings:

1. **Genomic divergence is remarkably low** — Red-shafted and Yellow-shafted flickers differ at only ~0.1% of the genome, far less than typical for recognized bird species
2. **Divergence is concentrated in a few genomic regions** — including genes controlling feather shaft color (carotenoid pigmentation pathways), suggesting strong selection on plumage despite high gene flow
3. **The hybrid zone is stable** — width and position have not changed substantially over decades of observation, indicating a balance between selection against hybrids and dispersal into the zone
4. **Assortative mating is weak** — flickers do not preferentially mate with their own phenotype, yet the hybrid zone remains narrow, suggesting post-mating selection (reduced hybrid fitness) maintains the boundary

The flicker hybrid zone challenges the biological species concept: Red-shafted and Yellow-shafted flickers interbreed freely where they meet, produce fertile offspring, and show minimal genetic divergence — yet maintain distinct phenotypes across most of their range. The AOS lumped them as a single species ("Northern Flicker") in 1973 based on the extensive hybridization, but the genomic data show that despite gene flow, the forms remain distinct, raising the question of whether they represent incipient species maintained by divergent selection on plumage [P3, O3].

#### PNW Hybrid Zone Geography

The contact zone enters the PNW study area through southern BC (Okanogan region, Kamloops corridor) and runs south through eastern WA (Okanogan County, Chelan County, Kittitas County) into eastern OR (Deschutes, Grant, Union counties). West of this zone, populations are essentially pure Red-shafted. East of the zone (northern ID, northeastern WA), populations approach pure Yellow-shafted but always show some introgression of Red-shafted characters.

In practice, birders in eastern WA and OR should expect to encounter flickers showing every possible combination of Red-shafted and Yellow-shafted characters, particularly in fall and winter when post-breeding dispersal mixes populations. The hybrid zone width is approximately 200–400 km — broad by avian hybrid zone standards, indicating substantial dispersal and limited selection against hybrids [P3].

#### Conservation Note

The Northern Flicker (all forms combined) shows a BBS decline of approximately 1.2% annually since 1966 [G4]. Declines are attributed to loss of snag habitat (cavity nester), competition with European Starlings for nest cavities, and reduced ant availability from pesticide use (ants are the primary food). Because hybrids are fertile and common, the hybrid zone itself is not a conservation target — but the loss of the zone would represent loss of a natural evolutionary experiment in real time.

**Sources:** [O1], [O3], [P3], [G4]

### ◆ Dark-eyed Junco Complex (*Junco hyemalis*)

#### Overview

The Dark-eyed Junco complex is among the most familiar birds in the PNW — and among the most taxonomically complex. What is now classified as a single species (lumped by the AOS in 1973) was formerly divided into five species: Oregon Junco, Slate-colored Junco, White-winged Junco, Gray-headed Junco, and Pink-sided Junco. All five forms occur in the PNW, with the Oregon group dominant as a breeder and the others as winter visitors or peripheral breeders [O3].

#### PNW Forms

**Oregon Junco** (*J. h. oreganus* group — multiple subspecies): The breeding junco of the PNW. Males: sharply defined black (northern) to dark gray (southern) hood contrasting with brown back, pink-brown flanks, white belly, and white outer tail feathers. Females: gray-brown hood, less contrast. Several subspecies within the Oregon group occur in the PNW:

- *J. h. oreganus* — dark-hooded, breeds from SE Alaska through coastal BC and WA
- *J. h. shufeldti* — slightly browner hood, breeds in OR and interior WA/BC
- *J. h. thurberi* — paler hood, approaches from California into southern OR
- *J. h. montanus* — breeds in Rocky Mountain influence zones (NE WA, northern ID)

**Slate-colored Junco** (*J. h. hyemalis*): The eastern form. Uniformly dark gray above and on hood, white belly, no contrast between hood and back. Winters uncommonly in the PNW, primarily in eastern WA and OR. Always outnumbered by Oregon form in PNW winter flocks.

**Pink-sided Junco** (*J. h. mearnsi*): Gray hood, broad pink-cinnamon flanks, breeds in central Rockies. Winters in eastern PNW (Zone 7, Zone 4), uncommon but regular.

**Gray-headed Junco** (*J. h. caniceps*): Gray overall with rufous back, breeds in southern Rockies. Rare winter visitor to southern OR.

**Cassiar Junco** (*J. h. cismontanus*): An intergrade population breeding in BC and wintering south through the PNW. Intermediate between Oregon and Slate-colored — dark hood with partial contrast to grayish back. Common in PNW winter flocks but often overlooked or not separated from Oregon.

#### Phylogeography

Molecular phylogenetics reveals that the junco complex radiated rapidly during the Pleistocene from a common ancestor in Mexico (the Yellow-eyed Junco, *J. phaeonotus*, is the outgroup). Glacial cycles isolated populations in different refugia, allowing divergence in plumage and body size, followed by secondary contact and hybridization during interglacials. The current pattern — multiple distinctive forms that hybridize where ranges meet — represents a snapshot of an ongoing speciation process that has not yet reached completion [O1].

In the PNW, the Oregon Junco group is the product of a Pacific coastal refugium during the last glacial maximum (~20,000 years ago). As glaciers retreated, Oregon Juncos expanded northward from an Oregon-California refugium and met Slate-colored Juncos expanding westward from an interior refugium. The Cassiar Junco intergrade population in BC marks this secondary contact zone [O1, O3].

#### PNW Population and Ecology

Dark-eyed Juncos are among the most abundant birds in North America (~630 million, all forms combined [O4]) and one of the most common PNW breeding passerines. Oregon Juncos breed across all forested zones (Zones 1–4), from sea-level urban gardens to subalpine forest edges. Nest: ground nest under overhanging vegetation, roots, or cut banks. Clutch: 3–5 eggs; incubation 12–13 days; fledging 10–13 days; 1–2 broods. Diet: seeds and insects. Ubiquitous at bird feeders in winter, where mixed-form flocks allow direct comparison of plumage variation [O1].

BBS trend: -0.7% annually since 1966 for the species complex overall [G4]. Oregon group trend in the PNW: stable to slightly declining.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [O4], [G4], [P16]

### ◆ Fox Sparrow Complex (*Passerella iliaca*)

#### Overview

The Fox Sparrow complex presents one of the most challenging taxonomic problems in North American ornithology. Four morphologically distinct groups — Sooty, Slate-colored, Thick-billed, and Red — are currently lumped as a single species, but molecular phylogenetics and morphological analysis suggest they may represent 2–4 species. The PNW is the stronghold of the Sooty group, which dominates the region's breeding and wintering Fox Sparrow populations, while portions of the Slate-colored and Red groups occur peripherally [O3].

#### The Four Groups

**Sooty group** (*P. i. unalaschcensis* and allies): Dark chocolate-brown overall with heavy, blurred breast streaking; bill medium-sized, dark. The PNW's breeding Fox Sparrow. Breeds in dense montane and subalpine scrub (Zones 1, 2) — avalanche chutes, krummholz edges, regenerating clearcuts, and subalpine meadow margins. Winters in lowland thickets and brush piles (Zone 3, Zone 5). The darkest Fox Sparrow group, consistent with Gloger's Rule in the humid PNW environment.

Multiple Sooty subspecies recognized in the PNW:
- *P. i. unalaschcensis* — dark, breeds in coastal Alaska and BC, winters through PNW
- *P. i. townsendi* — slightly paler, breeds in BC interior, winters in PNW
- *P. i. fuliginosa* — darkest, breeds on outer coast islands and coastal mainland
- *P. i. sinuosa* — very dark, coastal BC and SE Alaska

**Slate-colored group** (*P. i. schistacea* and allies): Gray head and back, brown wings, sharply defined white breast with crisp dark streaking. Breeds in montane scrub of the interior West — in the PNW, breeds in Blue Mountains, Wallowas, and east-side Cascade scrub (Zone 1, 2 east side). Wintering birds regular in lowland eastern OR and WA (Zone 7 riparian). Distinct enough from Sooty that field identification is straightforward where ranges overlap.

**Red group** (*P. i. iliaca* and allies): Bright rufous tail and wings, coarsely streaked breast, breeds in boreal Canada. Winters rarely in the PNW — occasional records from northeastern WA and northern ID. The most genetically divergent group from the Sooty and Slate-colored groups.

**Thick-billed group** (*P. i. megarhyncha* and allies): Large pale bill, gray-brown plumage, breeds in montane scrub of California and southern OR (Siskiyous, Klamath region). Reaches the PNW study area in the Klamath Mountains and southern OR Cascades. Bill adapted for crushing manzanita and ceanothus seeds.

#### Species Split Potential

Molecular phylogenetic studies consistently recover two major clades: (1) Red group and (2) Sooty + Slate-colored + Thick-billed. Within the second clade, Thick-billed is sister to Slate-colored, and Sooty is sister to both. Genetic divergence between Red and Sooty groups (2.5–3.5% mtDNA sequence divergence) exceeds that between many recognized species pairs. The AOS has considered but not yet enacted a split; current taxonomic treatment keeps all four groups within *Passerella iliaca* pending further study [O3].

For PNW conservation, a split would matter: the Sooty group would become a near-endemic Pacific Northwest species, with most of its global breeding range within the study area. Under the single-species model, Fox Sparrow is a widespread, secure species. Under a four-species split, the Sooty Fox Sparrow would still be secure but would have a much more restricted range, warranting regional monitoring [P20].

#### PNW Ecology

Fox Sparrows are one of the most powerful scratchers among PNW sparrows — they forage by vigorous double-foot scratching (both feet simultaneously raking leaf litter backward), uncovering seeds and invertebrates from under debris. This behavior is audible at considerable distance and often reveals the bird's presence before it is seen. Breeding habitat in the PNW is subalpine scrub and montane thicket (Zones 1, 2); wintering habitat is lowland brush piles, hedgerows, and riparian thickets (Zone 3, Zone 5). Song: a rich, clear, whistled series — among the most musical of PNW sparrow songs. BBS trend: stable overall [G4].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [G4], [P16], [P20]

### ◆ Townsend's × Hermit Warbler Hybrid Zone

#### Overview

The contact zone between Townsend's Warbler (*Setophaga townsendi*) and Hermit Warbler (*S. occidentalis*) in the Oregon and Washington Cascades is one of the most intensively studied avian hybrid zones in western North America. These two closely related wood-warblers replace each other latitudinally — Hermit Warbler breeds in montane conifer forests from northern California through southern Oregon and into the southern Cascades of Washington, while Townsend's Warbler breeds from the central Oregon Cascades north through Washington, BC, and Alaska. Where they meet, hybridization is extensive and asymmetric: Townsend's Warbler is displacing Hermit Warbler southward [P25].

#### The Hybrid Zone

**Location:** The contact zone runs through the central Oregon Cascades (approximate latitude 43–45°N), with some extension into the southern Washington Cascades. The zone is approximately 100–200 km wide, centered on the forests between Eugene and Bend, OR.

**Phenotypic intermediates:** Hybrid warblers show intermediate plumage — Townsend's dark cheek patch partially replacing Hermit's plain yellow face, variable amounts of green back streaking, and intermediate crown patterns. Pure Townsend's: black cheek patch, heavily streaked sides, green-and-black crown. Pure Hermit: plain yellow face, gray back, unstreaked sides, yellow crown. Hybrids span the entire continuum.

#### Asymmetric Introgression

The hybrid zone is not stable — it is moving southward. Over the past century, the contact zone has shifted approximately 1–2 km per decade to the south, with Townsend's Warbler genotypes progressively replacing Hermit Warbler genotypes. This asymmetry is driven by a behavioral dominance advantage: Townsend's Warbler males are competitively dominant over Hermit Warbler males in territorial disputes, and Townsend's × Hermit hybrid males are also dominant over pure Hermit males. This creates a one-way introgression pattern — Townsend's genes flow into Hermit populations faster than Hermit genes flow into Townsend's populations [P25].

Toews et al. (2016) [P25] found that genomic divergence between Townsend's and Hermit Warblers is concentrated in a very small number of loci — primarily genes controlling plumage pattern, particularly the extent of melanin-based cheek patch and streak intensity. Across the rest of the genome, the two species are nearly identical. This pattern — "plumage genes and little else" — suggests that divergent sexual selection on plumage pattern is the primary force maintaining species boundaries, even as the hybrid zone persists and introgression continues.

#### Conservation Implications

If the southward displacement continues at historical rates, Hermit Warbler could lose much of its northern breeding range within the PNW over the coming century. Hermit Warbler would persist in California, but the PNW populations of this species would be absorbed into the Townsend's Warbler gene pool — a process sometimes called "genetic swamping" or "speciation in reverse." This scenario illustrates how hybridization can threaten species persistence even without habitat loss [P25].

Currently, both species remain common breeders in PNW montane forests (Zone 2), and the hybrid zone itself supports abundant warbler populations. Neither species is listed or of conservation concern at the species level. The hybrid zone is monitored as a research site but does not drive management action [O1].

**Ecoregion relevance:** Zone 2 (montane conifer forest) — both species are upper-canopy insectivores breeding in mature and old-growth conifer stands. Townsend's Warbler extends into Zone 1 (subalpine) and winters along the Pacific coast (Zone 6). Hermit Warbler winters in Mexico and Central America.

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [P25], [G4]

### ◆ White-crowned Sparrow Dialects (*Zonotrichia leucophrys*)

#### Overview

The White-crowned Sparrow complex in the PNW encompasses three subspecies that differ in morphology, ecology, migratory behavior, and — most remarkably — song dialect. White-crowned Sparrow song is culturally transmitted (learned from tutors during a sensitive period in early life), and PNW populations exhibit geographically structured song dialects that have been studied since the 1960s, making this species one of the primary model organisms for understanding vocal learning, cultural evolution, and the role of song in population structure [O1].

#### PNW Subspecies

**Puget Sound White-crowned Sparrow** (*Z. l. pugetensis*): Resident to partial migrant. Breeds along the Pacific coast from southern BC through WA and OR into northern California (Zones 3, 6). Brown lores (not black). The most sedentary PNW form — many populations resident year-round, particularly in coastal scrub and suburban habitats. Songs characterized by relatively simple structure with local dialects that persist for decades at specific sites. Nuttall's White-crowned Sparrow (*Z. l. nuttalli*) of California is closely related and sometimes lumped with *pugetensis* [P16, O1].

**Mountain White-crowned Sparrow** (*Z. l. oriantha*): Long-distance migrant. Breeds in alpine and subalpine meadows (Zone 1) of the Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains, and Selkirks. Black lores, pink bill, paler plumage. Arrives at alpine breeding sites in late May–June, departs September. Winters in Mexico. The high-elevation specialist of the complex, adapted to the compressed alpine breeding season. Songs structurally distinct from *pugetensis* — typically more complex, with additional trill elements [P16, O1].

**Gambel's White-crowned Sparrow** (*Z. l. gambelii*): Long-distance migrant. Breeds in subarctic and boreal scrub from northern BC to Alaska. White lores, orange bill. Passes through the PNW in large numbers during migration (April–May, September–October), and winters uncommonly in lowland PNW (Zones 3, 4, 7). The largest and palest PNW form. Songs structurally distinct from both *pugetensis* and *oriantha* [P16, O1].

#### Song Dialect Geography

White-crowned Sparrow song consists of a complex phrase of whistles and buzzes lasting 2–4 seconds. Young males learn their song during a sensitive period (10–50 days post-hatching), crystallizing the song type of their natal dialect by age 200 days. Once crystallized, the song is fixed for life. This cultural transmission creates **microgeographic song dialects** — neighborhoods of males singing the same song type, with dialect boundaries as sharp as 100–200 meters [O1].

In the PNW, the best-studied dialect system is in *pugetensis* along the Pacific coast. Key findings:

1. **Dialect neighborhoods** of 5–50 males sharing the same song type persist for decades at specific locations — parks, coastal scrub patches, suburban neighborhoods. San Francisco Bay Area dialects have been continuously tracked since the 1960s; PNW coastal dialects show similar stability.
2. **Dialect boundaries** are remarkably sharp — adjacent dialects separated by as little as 200 m of unsuitable habitat (a road, a forested ravine) may differ completely in song structure.
3. **Dialect change** occurs through cultural mutation — individual birds occasionally introduce novel song elements that, if copied, spread through a dialect neighborhood. Dialect turnover rates average 5–15 years per element in coastal populations.
4. **Dialect and gene flow** — the critical question is whether song dialects affect genetic structure. Studies show modest assortative mating by dialect (females preferentially pair with males singing familiar dialect), but gene flow among dialects is sufficient to prevent genetic differentiation. Song dialects are cultural, not genetic, boundaries [O1].

**Oriantha vs. pugetensis contact:** In the PNW, *oriantha* breeds at alpine elevations and *pugetensis* breeds in lowland scrub, creating elevational separation on the same mountain. At mid-elevation (Zone 2 edges, subalpine meadow margins), the two subspecies can come into close proximity during the breeding season. Hybridization has been documented but is uncommon — the combination of habitat segregation, timing differences (alpine *oriantha* arrives 4–6 weeks after lowland *pugetensis* begins nesting), and song divergence maintains reproductive isolation [O1].

**Gambelii in migration:** When *gambelii* passes through the PNW in spring, its distinctive song (often described as more "buzzy" and complex than *pugetensis*) can be heard alongside resident *pugetensis* song — creating an audible demonstration of subspecific vocal divergence in the same location.

#### Conservation Status

White-crowned Sparrow (all subspecies combined) is abundant in the PNW and not of conservation concern. BBS trend: stable to slightly declining (-0.4% annually) [G4]. The *pugetensis* form is particularly secure given its adaptability to suburban habitats. The *oriantha* form, restricted to alpine meadows, is potentially vulnerable to climate-driven habitat loss (parallel to other alpine specialists). The song dialect system itself — a cultural heritage of learned vocalizations passed across generations — has no formal conservation framework, but represents a remarkable example of cultural evolution in a non-human species [O1, O4].
