“Lisp is the greatest single programming language ever designed.”

— Alan Kay (who invented Smalltalk, not Lisp)

Alan Kay’s Claim

Alan Kay, the computer scientist who invented Smalltalk and coined the phrase “object-oriented programming” and worked at Xerox PARC during its golden age, has said on many occasions some version of the above. Kay is not a Lisper. He is the creator of a competing tradition — Smalltalk and object-oriented programming. He has every reason, if he were a partisan, to dismiss Lisp. But Kay keeps saying this, and when he elaborates, his reasoning is essentially mathematical.

// Maxwell’s Equations of Software //

Maxwell’s equations, in physics, are four short equations that together describe the entire behavior of the classical electromagnetic field: electricity, magnetism, light, radio, X-rays, everything. They fit on a T-shirt. From those four equations, with enough mathematical manipulation, you can derive every phenomenon in classical electromagnetism.

Kay’s claim is that McCarthy’s eval function is software’s equivalent. In half a page of Lisp, McCarthy defines a universal interpreter — a program that, given any well-formed program as input, produces the value that program computes. The eval function refers to only a handful of primitive operations (quote, atom, eq, car, cdr, cons, cond, lambda), and from those primitives, via recursion, it generates the entire semantics of a Turing-complete programming language.

If you believe the Church-Turing thesis, then whatever you can compute, you can compute in Lisp, and the full description of how you compute it in Lisp fits on half a page. No other programming language can claim this. Python’s specification is a thousand pages. C’s specification is hundreds of pages. Java’s specification is longer than most novels. Lisp’s specification is half a page of Lisp.

Lisp was built outward from the lambda calculus, which is itself the most minimal formalism for computation anyone has ever found. Everything in Lisp — functions, data, control flow, iteration — is expressed as the application of a function to arguments. The evaluator only has to know one rule: to evaluate (f a b c), evaluate f, evaluate a, evaluate b, evaluate c, and apply the value of f to the values of a, b, c. That’s it. Everything else is built out of that one rule, plus a small number of special forms (quote, if, lambda, define).

Homoiconicity

The word homoiconicity, from the Greek “homo” (same) and “icon” (representation), was coined in the late 1960s to describe languages in which the primary representation of programs is also a primary data structure of the language. Calvin Mooers used the term to describe TRAC. The word did not quite stick to Lisp as its canonical name, but the idea is pure Lisp.

In Lisp, a program is a list of lists. When you write (+ 1 (* 2 3)), you are writing a list whose first element is the symbol +, whose second element is the number 1, and whose third element is a list. This is a data structure in the normal sense — you can construct it, walk it, modify it, pass it around. It is also a program — you can hand it to eval and get back the value 7. The same tree, read in two different modes, is either data (inert, manipulable) or code (active, computable).

The parentheses are not a mistake. They are the price of homoiconicity. You cannot have a powerful macro system without a regular, parseable syntax. The regular, parseable syntax is parentheses. Parentheses are Lisp’s payment for the most expressive programming model anyone has ever designed. It turns out to be a reasonable price, if you can get past the aesthetic shock, and Lispers pay it gladly.

Greenspun’s Tenth Rule

“Any sufficiently complicated C or Fortran program contains an ad hoc, informally-specified, bug-ridden, slow implementation of half of Common Lisp.”

— Philip Greenspun, 1993

There is no first through ninth rule; Greenspun wrote this and called it the tenth because he thought it sounded grander. It has become the most frequently cited Lisp aphorism in the entire language community.

The meaning: as a program grows, its author starts needing the features Lisp has — dynamic dispatch, garbage collection, macros, reflection, closures. The C programmer does not know they are reinventing Lisp; they just know that their program has grown a strange function pointer dispatch system, and a custom object model, and a reference counter, and a config-file interpreter that has started to look suspiciously like a programming language. The C programmer has built, by hand, a bad implementation of half of Common Lisp, and they do not know it because they have never used Common Lisp.

Every Language Becomes Lisp (Slowly)

There is a running joke in the Lisp community: “Every sufficiently advanced programming language reinvents Lisp, badly.” It is a joke that has become one of those jokes that are funny because they are almost entirely true.

The pattern is clear: good ideas from Lisp propagate slowly outward to other languages, usually arriving ten to thirty years after they were first developed in Lisp, usually in a form that is weaker or more constrained than the Lisp version. The reason is that most language designers start with a fixed syntax and a fixed parser, and then find that adding “macros” or “metaprogramming” to an existing parsed language requires extensive special-case support in the compiler. Lisp can add features by writing libraries, while other languages have to add them by changing the compiler.

The Lisp Curse

Lisp is so powerful that every Lisp programmer builds their own solution to every problem. The community cannot converge on shared libraries the way Python or Ruby or Go communities do. This produces a fragmented ecosystem that is hard for newcomers to navigate.

This is the Lisp curse, and it is a direct consequence of the language’s expressive power. When building your own is easy, fewer people use what others have built. When you can define your own control structure in 10 lines, you don’t import someone else’s. When a DSL is a weekend project, you write your own instead of learning theirs. The community becomes a scattered archipelago of small, brilliant, incompatible solutions.

Clojure partially broke the curse by enforcing a strong central culture around Rich Hickey’s design decisions and by living inside the JVM, where there is one canonical build system, one canonical dependency format, and one canonical way to ship a library. The Common Lisp and Scheme worlds remain cursed, beautifully so.

Worse Is Better

Richard P. Gabriel’s 1989 essay “The Rise of ‘Worse Is Better’” is the essay the Lisp community has spent three decades arguing with itself about. Gabriel, a Lisp pioneer himself, tried to explain why Lisp was losing to Unix/C despite being the better-designed system. His answer: because it was better-designed.

The MIT approach (which Gabriel calls “the Right Thing”): Simplicity, correctness, consistency, and completeness are all important. In case of conflict, completeness wins, then correctness, then consistency, then simplicity. The result is a beautiful design that takes forever to ship and is hard to adapt.

The New Jersey approach (which Gabriel calls “Worse Is Better”): Simplicity of implementation is the most important consideration. In case of conflict, simplicity beats everything else. The interface might be ugly, but the implementation fits in your head. You ship fast, you iterate, you survive.

Gabriel’s thesis: Unix and C (the New Jersey approach) won because they were worse, and worse was better. The simpler implementation meant faster iteration, wider adoption, larger ecosystem, more bugs fixed, more platforms supported. Meanwhile Lisp Machines (the Right Thing) shipped slowly, cost $100K, and died when workstations caught up.

Gabriel wrote the essay partly as a warning to himself and his Lisp-loving colleagues: be careful, elegant design can lose to ugly design, and this is not a moral failing of the universe. Years later he wrote a rebuttal to his own essay under a pseudonym (“Nickieben Bourbaki”) arguing that Worse Is Better was itself wrong, and then he wrote a response to that, and the debate has been running inside his head and inside the Lisp community ever since.

The Blub Paradox

Paul Graham’s essay “Beating the Averages” (2001) introduced the Blub Paradox. Graham imagines a programmer whose favorite language is Blub. Blub is somewhere in the middle of the power continuum. When the Blub programmer looks down at less-powerful languages, they can see that those languages are weaker than Blub because they lack features Blub has. But when the Blub programmer looks up at more-powerful languages, they cannot see the power difference. They just see weird languages that don’t have the features Blub has.

The punchline: every language’s programmers think they are at the top of the power continuum. They cannot see the power above them because the power above them is expressed in terms they don’t have. The only way to discover it is to go learn a more powerful language and come back changed.

Graham’s implicit conclusion: if you haven’t learned Lisp, you might be a Blub programmer and not know it. The only way to know is to learn Lisp and see.

“The more of an IT flavor the job descriptions had, the less dangerous the company was. The safest kind were the ones that wanted Oracle experience. You never had to worry about those. You were also safe if they said they wanted C++ or Java developers. If they wanted Perl or Python programmers, that would be a bit frightening — that’s starting to sound like a company where the technical side, at least, is run by real hackers. If I had ever seen a job posting looking for Lisp hackers, I would have been really worried.”

— Paul Graham, “Beating the Averages”

The Constraint Connection — Bridge to PCH

There is a connection between Lisp and the broader tradition of constraint thinking, explored at length in the companion Punch Cards philosophy document. Punch-card-era programmers, because they had to minimize re-runs, became exceptionally good at thinking before coding — at figuring out the constraints of a problem and then writing code that followed from those constraints rather than code that had been iteratively debugged into shape.

Lisp has a similar relationship to constraint thinking, but from a different direction. Lisp encourages you to think about your problem domain before you start writing code. The standard Lisp workflow is to define the data structures first — to figure out what kind of tree you are working with — and then to define the functions that operate on those structures, and then to define the macros that give you a nicer notation for writing those functions. At each level, you are thinking about constraints.

A well-written Lisp program is a tower of abstraction where each level is built on the level below it, and each level makes the next level cleaner and shorter. A well-written Lisp program is not a sequence of instructions; it is a custom language designed for the problem domain, in which the solution is a short program.

This is what Paul Graham meant by bottom-up programming. You are not writing a program that solves the problem directly. You are building the tools you need to express the solution, and then using those tools. The Lisp community has been doing this since the 1960s, long before the phrase “domain-specific language” existed.

The culture that produced Lisp believed that computation was a medium for expressing ideas, and that the best way to express a complicated idea was to build, first, a language in which the idea became simple. The meta-circular evaluator is the ultimate example: you express what it means to run Lisp by writing a very short program in Lisp. The Lisp in which you write the program is a language so suited to expressing evaluation that evaluation can be described in half a page.

Why Isn’t Lisp More Popular?

This is the question Lispers are asked most often. The answers are many and not entirely consistent with each other:

  1. Hardware economics killed the Lisp Machine. Custom hardware had to pay for itself in speed, and generic workstations caught up in the late 1980s just as C/Unix was ascending.
  2. The language was too large by the time it was standardized. Common Lisp emerged from committee in 1984 with thousands of features and a 1,300-page spec. Not a language that could compete with the lightweight scripting languages of the 1990s on approachability.
  3. The parentheses are a social barrier. Not an actual cognitive barrier after a short adaptation period, but they look weird, and “looking weird” is a significant obstacle to mass adoption.
  4. Lisp’s culture was bad at evangelism. Lispers tended to believe that being better was enough. It wasn’t.
  5. The Lisp curse — too much power means too much fragmentation.
  6. Lisp lacked a killer app. Python had Django and the scientific stack. Ruby had Rails. JavaScript had the browser. C had Unix. Lisp’s killer app (symbolic AI) collapsed in the 1980s, and Lisp never found a replacement until Clojure in 2007.
  7. Network effects. Because Lisp is not widely known, companies cannot easily hire Lisp programmers, which means they do not use Lisp, which means there are no jobs for Lisp programmers, which means nobody learns Lisp. A classic trap.
  8. The good ideas from Lisp have spread to other languages. In 1980, Lisp’s garbage collection and first-class functions were unique. In 2026, Python has all of those things. The gap has narrowed.

The real answer, if there is one, is probably that Lisp is a language for a certain kind of programmer — one who cares about power and expressiveness more than about approachability, one who is willing to think before writing, one who enjoys the discipline of building abstractions from the ground up — and that kind of programmer has always been a minority.

The Question That Won’t Go Away

Something interesting happens over and over in the history of programming languages: every generation of programmers rediscovers Lisp. Some twentysomething, working in Java or Python or JavaScript or Rust, runs into a problem that feels like it should be simpler than it is. They start reading around. They stumble across Paul Graham’s essays, or Rich Hickey’s talks, or the SICP lectures on YouTube, or an old-timer on Hacker News talking about the MIT AI Lab. They open a Lisp tutorial. They write their first (+ 1 2). And something clicks. They realize that the thing they were struggling with in Java is not a thing in Lisp because Lisp solved it forty years ago and never forgot.

This happens every year. It happens to students who take 6.001 at MIT. It happens to programmers who discover Clojure because their company is using it. It happens to people who read Peter Norvig’s essays. The recurring rediscovery of Lisp is one of the most interesting sociological phenomena in programming.

What does it mean that Lisp keeps getting rediscovered?

// The Questions Are Still Open //

How much can you express with how little? Can code and data be the same kind of thing? Can the interpreter be written in the language it interprets? Can a language be extended by its users without changes to the compiler? Can a program be built bottom-up as a custom language in which the solution is short?

These questions are not resolved. They are still, seventy years after McCarthy’s first sketch, among the deepest questions in programming language design. Lisp is the language that asked them first, and the language that keeps answering them with the greatest force, and for that reason Lisp will keep mattering as long as people write programs.

Make Computer Science Fun Again

“Love my work, hate my job.”

— Every hacker who ever held down a day job

There is a distinction that every working programmer eventually learns to make. There is the work — the craft, the ideas, the problems you can’t stop thinking about at 2 AM, the satisfaction of a data structure that finally clicks, the joy of writing a macro that makes a hard thing easy. And then there is the job — the Jira tickets, the standups, the quarterly OKRs, the pull request templates, the code review comments that are really status games, the sprint retrospective that devolves into recrimination, the manager who does not understand what you do but has opinions about how fast you should do it.

You can love writing Lisp macros and hate writing TPS reports. You can love inventing data structures and hate explaining them to a project manager who wants to know the ETA. You can love the field of computer science and recoil from the industry it has become. This is a philosophical aside, and it belongs on the Lisp philosophy page because Lisp is one of the surviving branches where fun is still the primary currency.

What We Have Lost

Computer science used to be fun. Not because the work was easy — it was never easy — but because the work was, overwhelmingly, a thing people chose to do because they loved it. The MIT AI Lab ran on passion. The Berkeley hackers who wrote BSD wrote it because they wanted to. Alan Emtage wrote Archie (the first Internet search engine, at McGill in 1990) because he was tired of finding files on FTP sites by hand. Robey Pointer wrote Eggdrop (1993) because his IRC channel needed a bot. Richard Stallman wrote GNU Emacs and fought the Symbolics war and founded the Free Software Foundation because the hacker ethic mattered to him more than a paycheck. McCarthy built Lisp because he wanted to do AI research and there was no language for it. Hickey built Clojure because he wanted a better Lisp for the JVM.

None of these people had a quarterly OKR. None of them had a pull request template. None of them were bottlenecked on a code review from a senior engineer who was themselves bottlenecked on a design review from a principal architect who was themselves bottlenecked on a planning meeting with a product manager. They had an idea. They had a computer. They had time. They built the thing. They gave it away. They moved to the next thing.

Somewhere between 1995 and now, computer science stopped being a craft that hackers did for fun and became an industry that employed them for money. Both things are still true — you can still be a hacker who writes Lisp macros for the joy of it — but the industry has gotten so large and so process-heavy that most working programmers spend most of their working hours not building things they love, but attending meetings, filing tickets, answering Slack, and navigating org charts. The fun is still there, if you know where to find it, but you increasingly have to find it outside the job.

The Hacker Culture Thread

The Lisp tradition never lived in isolation. It was one thread in a larger hacker culture that ran from the MIT AI Lab through McGill, Berkeley, Carnegie Mellon, and out onto the early Internet. The culture where Archie — the first Internet search engine, four years before the web existed — was written by a graduate student and given away, because he was tired of finding files by hand. The culture where Eggdrop and its descendants ran as IRC channel bots maintained by 14-year-olds with Tcl scripts and too much time on their hands. The culture where ERC (the Emacs IRC Client, written entirely in elisp) let you run a chat session inside your editor and write live bot responses as elisp functions you evaluated with C-x C-e. None of this was strictly Lisp, but all of it came from the same soil: see a problem, write code, share the code, move to the next problem.

For the deep dive on Archie, Eggdrop, ERC, and the IRC botting culture that grew up alongside the Lisp tradition, see the hacker-culture section of the Rosetta page.

The Escape Hatches

The Lisp tradition offers several escape hatches from the job-that-is-not-the-work:

  • The bot path. Run ERC inside Emacs. Write a bot in elisp. Watch it respond to channel events in real time. Tweak it. Share it with a friend. Nobody is tracking your velocity on this.
  • The Emacs path. Emacs is a Lisp Machine in a terminal window. Every piece of it can be rewritten. Every workflow can be automated. Every frustration can be smoothed over with fifteen lines of elisp. Three months in, you have a text editor that fits your brain better than anything else you have ever used.
  • The Racket path. Racket’s #lang system lets you build a new language in a weekend. Learn more about programming languages in a week than a semester-long compilers course would teach.
  • The Clojure path. If you need a day job, Clojure is the Lisp with jobs attached. Nubank, Metabase, Circle CI, Juxt. The work is often more craftwork-flavored because Clojure attracts people who care.
  • The Scheme path. Read SICP. Write a meta-circular evaluator. Write another one with lazy evaluation. Write a third with a type system. None will make you money; all will teach you what programming is.

The only thing you need to make computer science fun again is permission to follow what interests you, time to do it, and a willingness to build things that do not appear on any roadmap. The Lisp tradition is a permission slip. Pick it up and use it. Write your own editor. Write your own language. Write your own bot. Give it away. Move on to the next thing. That is what the people who invented this whole field were doing, and it is still what the best work in this field comes from, and nobody is going to give you permission except yourself.

Love the work. The job will sort itself out.

Afterword

Lisp is the language you learn when you want to understand what programming languages are. It is not necessarily the language you use every day, though some people do and they are not wrong. It is the language that sits at the intellectual foundation of the field, the reference point against which every other language is, implicitly or explicitly, measured. Every programming language textbook that is serious about its subject eventually gets around to explaining how Lisp works, because without Lisp you cannot explain what a closure is, or what a macro is, or what homoiconicity is, or why the lambda calculus matters, or how to write an interpreter, or why the Church-Turing thesis has teeth. You can learn those things without learning Lisp — people do, every year — but you learn them slower and more painfully, because you are learning them in a language that was designed to hide them from you, and Lisp is the language that was designed to show them to you.

The argument of this document, in one sentence, is that Lisp is the most important programming language in the history of computing because it is the only one that started from mathematical foundations and followed those foundations wherever they led, without compromising for hardware or committees or corporate roadmaps. Every other language is an engineering artifact, designed for some combination of execution speed, ease of learning, hardware compatibility, market adoption, and employer demand. Lisp is those things too, sometimes, but Lisp is, first and foundationally, a mathematical idea that happens to run.

That is what Alan Kay meant by “the greatest single programming language ever designed.” Kay was not saying Lisp was the most practical or the most popular or the easiest to hire for. He was saying that Lisp was the one language that took the underlying mathematics seriously and let the mathematics drive the design. Every other language is a translation of that mathematics into something more convenient. Lisp is the mathematics.

This is why Lisp keeps getting rediscovered. Every time programmers get lost in the accidental complexity of whatever language they are currently using, someone points back at Lisp and says: look, it does not have to be this way, you can strip out the accidental stuff and leave only the essential, and the essential fits on a page. That demonstration never stops being useful, because programmers keep adding accidental complexity, and someone keeps needing to point back. Lisp is the baseline. It is the null hypothesis. It is the version of programming that would still exist if every commercial language vanished overnight and we had to start over from the mathematical foundations.

If you want to know what programming is — not what it has become, not what the market wants, not what your employer pays you to do, but what it is in its purest form — read the 1960 McCarthy paper. Read SICP. Write a meta-circular evaluator. You will come out changed. You will come out understanding something about computation that you did not understand before, and that almost nobody in the industry understands, and that will nonetheless be true whether or not the industry understands it.

The parentheses are fine. You will get used to them in a week. The ideas will take longer, and they will be worth it.

Canonical Texts

  • Graham, Paul. On Lisp (1993). The bible of advanced macro programming. Free PDF at paulgraham.com/onlisp.html.
  • Graham, Paul. Hackers & Painters (2004). Contains “Beating the Averages,” “The Hundred-Year Language,” “Revenge of the Nerds.”
  • Norvig, Peter. Paradigms of Artificial Intelligence Programming: Case Studies in Common Lisp (1992). The book that demonstrates why Common Lisp was the right language for AI research.
  • Abelson & Sussman. Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs (SICP, 1984; 2e 1996). The preface alone is one of the most eloquent statements ever written about what programming is.
  • Gabriel, Richard P. “The Rise of ‘Worse Is Better’” (1989/1991). The essay the Lisp community has spent three decades arguing with itself about.
  • McCarthy, John. “Recursive Functions of Symbolic Expressions and Their Computation by Machine, Part I” (CACM, April 1960). The foundational document.
  • Steele & Sussman. The Lambda Papers (1975–1979). The mathematical justification for Scheme and, through Scheme, for modern functional programming.
  • Kiczales, des Rivières, Bobrow. The Art of the Metaobject Protocol (1991). The book that explains what object-oriented systems can be.
