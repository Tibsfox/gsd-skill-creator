Pre-History — Alonzo Church and the Lambda Calculus

In 1932, a Princeton mathematician named Alonzo Church introduced an austere formal system for defining and applying functions. He called it the λ-calculus. It had three rules and three kinds of expression:

  • Variables: x, y, z …
  • Abstraction: λx.M — read as “a function that takes x and returns the expression M”
  • Application: M N — read as “apply M to N”

That’s the entire language. There are no numbers, no booleans, no data structures — yet Church and his student Stephen Kleene showed you could encode all of them using just functions. Church numerals encode positive integers as functions that apply a given function n times. Church booleans encode true and false as functions that select one of two arguments. Pairs, lists, recursion, conditionals — all derivable from λ alone.

In 1936, Church published a paper titled “An Unsolvable Problem of Elementary Number Theory” in which he used the λ-calculus to prove the undecidability of the Entscheidungsproblem (Hilbert’s decision problem). The same year, across the Atlantic, Alan Turing arrived at the same conclusion using a very different formalism — his infinite tape and state-machine head. The two approaches turned out to be equivalent, and the Church-Turing thesis gave computability its modern foundation.

For twenty years, the λ-calculus remained an artifact of mathematical logic. It was written on blackboards, not on computers. Computers themselves had barely arrived, and the programming languages of the early 1950s were Fortran and machine code — oriented around arithmetic, loops, and numerical storage. Nobody thought Church’s logic was a practical programming tool.

Then in 1956, an assistant professor of mathematics at Dartmouth named John McCarthy read Church’s monograph The Calculi of Lambda Conversion, and something clicked.

Dartmouth, 1956 — The Birth of AI

In August 1955, John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon submitted a proposal to the Rockefeller Foundation for a summer research project at Dartmouth College. The opening sentence coined a term that didn’t yet exist:

“We propose that a 2 month, 10 man study of artificial intelligence be carried out during the summer of 1956 at Dartmouth College. The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it.”

— McCarthy, Minsky, Rochester, Shannon, August 31, 1955

McCarthy coined “artificial intelligence” to distinguish the project from Norbert Wiener’s cybernetics and to give it a clean label for funding proposals. The 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence is now recognized as the founding event of the AI field.

Among the topics discussed that summer was a list-processing language called IPL (Information Processing Language), developed by Allen Newell, Herbert Simon, and J.C. Shaw at RAND. IPL used linked-list data structures to represent symbolic information — a radical departure from the numeric arrays of Fortran. Their Logic Theorist program, demonstrated at Dartmouth, proved 38 of the first 52 theorems in Russell and Whitehead’s Principia Mathematica. It was arguably the first AI program ever written.

McCarthy watched IPL closely. He liked the idea of lists as a universal data structure. But IPL was cryptic, assembler-like, and tied to specific machines. McCarthy wanted something cleaner — a language whose notation was as close to mathematical function notation as he could make it. He started sketching ideas on the plane home from Dartmouth.

MIT, 1958 — The Invention of Lisp

McCarthy moved from Dartmouth to MIT in 1958 and joined the Research Laboratory of Electronics, where Marvin Minsky had just established a small AI group. McCarthy’s goal was to build a system that could do advice taking — accept declarative knowledge and reason from it. He needed a language.

Over the fall and winter of 1958, McCarthy wrote a series of internal memos. The most famous is AI Memo No. 1, published in September 1958 as “An Algebraic Language for the Manipulation of Symbolic Expressions.” In it, he described a language based on lambda abstractions, conditional expressions, recursive function definitions, and a small set of primitive operations on lists: car (first element), cdr (rest), cons (prepend), atom, eq, and a conditional.

The names car and cdr are historical accidents. The IBM 704 had a 36-bit word with two 15-bit address fields called the address part and the decrement part. McCarthy’s team stored list nodes with the “first element” pointer in the address part and the “rest of list” pointer in the decrement part. The machine-level macros to extract them were CAR (Contents of Address part of Register) and CDR (Contents of Decrement part of Register). The names survived the hardware and are still used in 2026.

By early 1959, McCarthy had two separate notations. M-expressions were the intended surface syntax — a cleaner mathematical form that was supposed to be what programmers wrote. S-expressions were a parenthesis-heavy internal representation, intended only as a way for the computer to store programs as data. M-expressions would be translated into S-expressions before evaluation.

Nobody liked M-expressions. They were verbose, required a pre-processor that didn’t exist, and were no clearer than the raw S-expressions. Programmers started writing directly in S-expressions because that’s what the interpreter could actually read. The “temporary” data format became the permanent surface syntax of the language. The parentheses were never supposed to be the point — but they stayed anyway, because they turned out to be a feature, not a bug. Code as data was baked into the language from day one.

Steve Russell and the Accident That Started Everything

In his 1960 paper, McCarthy included a definition of a function he called eval, which took an S-expression (representing a Lisp program) and an environment, and produced the result of evaluating that program. It was intended as a mathematical specification — a formal description of what the language meant, comparable to the way a denotational semantics defines a language.

One of McCarthy’s graduate students, Steve Russell, read the paper and noticed something: if you translated eval from paper into IBM 704 machine code, you would have an interpreter for Lisp, written in machine code, that could run any Lisp program. McCarthy hadn’t thought of it that way — eval was supposed to be theory, not implementation.

Russell did the translation by hand. It worked. McCarthy later said: “Steve Russell said, look, why don’t I program this eval… and I said to him, ho, ho, you’re confusing theory with practice, this eval is intended for reading, not for computing. But he went ahead and did it.”

That accidental implementation is the ancestor of every Lisp interpreter ever written. The meta-circular evaluator — a Lisp that can evaluate Lisp — has been the spine of the language ever since.

LISP 1, LISP 1.5, and the Dominant Paradigm

The first Lisp ran on an IBM 704 at MIT in late 1959. McCarthy and the AI group published LISP 1.5 in 1962 as a reference manual with source code listings — the first widely-distributed Lisp. The book, known informally as the “LISP 1.5 Programmer’s Manual,” is a landmark document that introduces the language, the evaluator, and garbage collection.

Garbage collection was another McCarthy invention, born of necessity. Lisp programs allocated list cells constantly and had no way to track which cells were still in use. McCarthy’s mark-and-sweep algorithm, first implemented for LISP 1.5, walked the tree of live references, marked reachable cells, and reclaimed the rest. It was the first automatic memory management system in any programming language. Every modern garbage-collected runtime — Java, .NET, Python, JavaScript, Go, Swift — descends from McCarthy’s 1960 algorithm.

Conditional expressions were also introduced to computing by Lisp. Before Lisp, languages had IF statements (Fortran’s arithmetic IF, ALGOL’s IF-THEN-ELSE as a control-flow construct) but not expressions that returned values based on a condition. McCarthy needed something that could appear as a term in a function body, so he defined (cond (p1 e1) (p2 e2) ...) as an expression that returned e_i for the first true p_i. The ternary operator a ? b : c in C and every other conditional expression in every other language is a descendant.

Recursion as a first-class concept also came from Lisp. Fortran I (1957) did not support recursion — the language specification actually forbade it, and the runtime allocated a single static activation record per function. Lisp was built on recursive function definitions from the start, and its early implementations had to solve the recursion problem as a matter of survival. Every modern language that takes recursion for granted owes that to Lisp.

The MIT AI Lab — The Hacker Era

In 1959, John McCarthy and Marvin Minsky founded the MIT Artificial Intelligence Project, which grew into the MIT AI Lab. For the next twenty years, the AI Lab on the ninth floor of Tech Square in Cambridge was the spiritual home of Lisp. A rotating cast of graduate students, research staff, and self-taught hackers built the tools, the culture, and the mythology that Steven Levy would later document in his 1984 book Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution.

The Lab ran on Maclisp, a descendant of LISP 1.5 built by Richard Greenblatt, Jon L. White, and others, targeting the PDP-6 and later the PDP-10. Maclisp was fast. It pioneered compilation of Lisp to native code and supported arbitrary-precision integers, hash tables, and a sophisticated debugger. It became the default AI language at MIT, Stanford, CMU, and beyond.

Maclisp was where the tooling culture was born: the structure editor that edited S-expressions as trees rather than lines of text; the stepper that let you walk through an evaluation one eval call at a time; the inspector that let you click through a data structure; the live-debuggable runtime where you could patch a bug in a running program and continue. All of this happened between 1965 and 1975, at a time when most programmers were still writing batch jobs on punch cards.

The list of Lab alumni reads like a who’s who of computing: Gerald Sussman, Guy Steele (who would co-design Scheme and later lead Common Lisp standardization), Richard Stallman (who would write GNU Emacs and found the Free Software Foundation after watching Symbolics take proprietary control of the Lisp Machine code), Tom Knight (who designed the first Lisp Machine), Bill Gosper (mathematician and HAKMEM contributor), Carl Hewitt (inventor of the Actor model), and David Moon (lead architect on ZetaLisp and Symbolics Genera).

The Dialect Wars

By the mid-1970s there were too many Lisps. Each site had its own. Interlisp grew out of BBN Lisp at Bolt Beranek and Newman, moved to Xerox PARC, and became the West Coast standard. Franz Lisp (named after the composer Franz Liszt) came from Berkeley and was designed as a compatible Maclisp subset that ran on VAX. Stanford Lisp 1.6, UCI Lisp, Portable Standard Lisp, and a dozen other dialects proliferated.

Each dialect had its own semantics, its own library conventions, its own debugger, its own incompatible surface syntax. A program written for Maclisp would not run on Interlisp without significant rewriting. This was the “Lisp curse”: the language was so malleable that every group built a slightly different variant to suit their preferences, and no one variant could serve as a common ground.

Two attempts emerged to fix the problem. The first was a radical simplification.

Scheme — The Minimalist Reform

In 1975, Gerald Sussman and Guy Steele, both at the MIT AI Lab, wrote a small Lisp interpreter to explore Carl Hewitt’s Actor model. Hewitt had proposed that computation could be modeled as message-passing between independent actors, and Sussman and Steele wanted to see whether the model was really different from lambda calculus with closures. They called their interpreter Scheme.

The surprise: once they implemented closures and tail calls correctly, the distinction between actors and lambda expressions dissolved. A function call was an actor message send. A closure was an actor state. The Actor model and the λ-calculus were the same thing viewed from two angles. That insight led to the Lambda Papers, a series of MIT AI memos Sussman and Steele wrote between 1975 and 1980 with titles like “Lambda: The Ultimate Imperative,” “Lambda: The Ultimate Declarative,” and “Lambda: The Ultimate GOTO.” The papers argued that lambda expressions were sufficient to encode any control structure a programmer could want.

Scheme introduced or popularized several ideas that older Lisps had handled poorly. Lexical scoping became the default (Maclisp and LISP 1.5 used dynamic scoping, where a variable’s binding was inherited from the call stack). Proper tail calls were required by the language: a function that called itself as its last action must reuse its stack frame, allowing unlimited recursion with constant stack space. This made recursive algorithms the natural default for iteration. First-class continuations, exposed via call-with-current-continuation, gave the programmer direct access to the rest of the computation as a reified object — a feature so powerful it could implement any control structure including exceptions, generators, coroutines, and backtracking search.

Scheme was small — the entire language could be described in about 50 pages. It was conceptually clean, designed as a teaching language and as a research platform. The Revised Report on the Algorithmic Language Scheme, known as RnRS (the nth Revised Report), went through several editions: R2RS, R3RS, R4RS, R5RS (1998, long considered the classic), and the controversial R6RS (2007) that split the community, followed by R7RS (2013) which tried to unify small (teaching-focused) and large (production-focused) variants.

Common Lisp — The Industrial Unification

The second approach to the dialect wars was unification by committee. In 1981, ARPA, worried that fragmentation would kill Lisp in the military AI market, sponsored a meeting at Carnegie Mellon to design a single industrial-strength Lisp that would subsume the major dialects. The committee included Guy Steele, Scott Fahlman (CMU), David Moon (MIT/Symbolics), Daniel Weinreb (MIT/Symbolics), Richard Gabriel (Stanford/Lucid), and representatives from every major Lisp implementation.

The result, published in 1984 as Common Lisp: The Language (CLtL1, Steele as editor), was a massive, pragmatic, inclusive standard. It unified Maclisp, Lisp Machine Lisp, and elements of Interlisp and others. It included features from all the big dialects: a rich numeric tower, packages (namespaces), keyword arguments, multiple return values, a powerful format function, a condition system with restarts, and support for eventual inclusion of CLOS (the Common Lisp Object System, standardized in 1988 and added to the language in 1990’s CLtL2 edition).

Common Lisp was officially standardized by ANSI in 1994 as ANSI X3.226-1994. It remains the standard today. The ANSI standard has never been revised, because Common Lisp programmers generally consider it complete. Implementations like SBCL, CCL, CLISP, ABCL, and Allegro Common Lisp have continued to evolve around the stable standard.

The Lisp Machine Era — Hardware for Software

By 1974, the AI Lab had outgrown the PDP-10. Lisp programs were large and slow on general-purpose hardware. Richard Greenblatt designed a dedicated Lisp computer called the CONS, and then an improved version called the CADR. These machines had tagged pointers in hardware, microcoded type dispatch, hardware-assisted garbage collection, and incremental compilation, all tuned to run Lisp as fast as possible.

In 1979, two commercial ventures split out of the Lab. Russell Noftsker, the Lab’s administrator, founded Symbolics with plans to sell Lisp Machines to industry. Greenblatt preferred a slower, community-oriented approach and founded Lisp Machines Inc (LMI). The split was bitter. Both companies licensed the MIT-developed software and raced to productize it. Symbolics won commercially; LMI struggled and eventually failed.

Symbolics introduced the LM-2 (1981), the 3600 series (1983), and the Ivory-processor-based Macivory, Symbolics XL1200, and UX boards (late 1980s). Their software environment, Genera, is still considered by many old-timers to be the most sophisticated programming environment ever built. Every object in the system was inspectable, every function could be redefined at runtime, the debugger was integrated into every stack frame, and the entire user interface was itself Lisp code that could be modified live.

For the full story of the Lisp Machines, including the Stallman-Symbolics schism that gave birth to the GNU project, see Lisp Machines.

The AI Winter and the Fall

The mid-1980s were the peak of the Lisp commercial era. Expert-system startups like Teknowledge, Inference Corporation, and Carnegie Group raised millions. Symbolics’ stock soared. Corporate customers bought $100K Lisp Machines to run their rule-based reasoning systems. Venture capital flowed. The AI boom was real.

It collapsed around 1988. Expert systems turned out to be brittle — they could answer questions within their rule base but failed catastrophically on questions outside it. The knowledge-acquisition bottleneck — getting experts to articulate their knowledge in the form of rules — was harder than expected. Meanwhile, Sun Microsystems workstations with Common Lisp could do 80% of what a Symbolics machine could do, for 20% of the price. The market dried up fast. Symbolics went bankrupt in 1993. Lisp Machines Inc had already closed. Teknowledge and Inference shrank or pivoted.

The industry narrative became: AI failed. The implicit corollary was: Lisp failed. For about a decade, Lisp was the language of “that AI thing that didn’t work.” Corporate IT departments refused to touch it. Universities quietly switched their intro programming courses from Scheme to Java or Python. Lisp developers found themselves stigmatized.

But the language didn’t die. It went underground.

Paul Graham and the Viaweb Revelation

In 1995, a Harvard PhD named Paul Graham and his partner Robert Morris started a company called Viaweb to build web-based store software. They chose Common Lisp as their implementation language — partly because Graham knew it well, partly because they calculated that the macro system would let them adapt to changing requirements faster than the competition. Viaweb was bought by Yahoo in 1998 for $50 million and became Yahoo Store.

In 2001, Graham wrote an essay called “Beating the Averages” in which he argued that Viaweb had won partly because they used Lisp. He introduced the Blub Paradox: a programmer using an average language (he called it Blub) can see that all the languages below Blub on the power spectrum are inferior, because they’re missing features Blub has. But the programmer cannot see the languages above Blub as more powerful — they look weird and incomprehensible, because the features they have are ones Blub doesn’t provide any way to express.

Graham’s essay went viral in the early blogosphere. Thousands of programmers who had never touched a parenthesis picked up On Lisp (Graham’s 1993 book on macro programming, which he released as a free PDF) and ANSI Common Lisp (his 1995 textbook). Graham became a mentor figure to a new generation of startup hackers and later co-founded Y Combinator in 2005. The Y Combinator application site was written in Arc, Graham’s own experimental Lisp dialect. Hacker News still runs on Arc in 2026.

Rich Hickey and the Clojure Renaissance

In 2007, a veteran programmer named Rich Hickey released Clojure, a new Lisp dialect that ran on the Java Virtual Machine. Hickey had been using Common Lisp for years but wanted something with better concurrency primitives, first-class immutability, and seamless interop with the vast Java library ecosystem. Clojure provided all three.

Clojure’s core data structures are persistent (in the functional-programming sense): operations that appear to modify a collection actually return a new collection that shares structure with the original, making mutation-free programming feasible even for large data. Clojure also introduced a novel approach to state management through atoms, refs, and agents, each with different concurrency semantics.

Hickey’s talks — “Simple Made Easy” (2011), “The Value of Values” (2012), “Hammock Driven Development” (2010), and “Effective Programs: 10 Years of Clojure” (2017) — became some of the most-watched programming lectures of the 2010s. “Simple Made Easy” is frequently cited as the single most influential programming talk of the decade.

Clojure found its biggest success at Nubank, a Brazilian fintech that became the largest Clojure shop in the world, with over 1,000 Clojure developers running a fully functional banking platform. Other notable users include Walmart Labs, Apple, Metabase, Puppet, Cisco, Citi, and portions of NASA JPL. ClojureScript (2011) brought Clojure to the browser, where libraries like Reagent and re-frame made React feel native to Lispers. Babashka (2019) compiled Clojure to GraalVM native images, solving the startup-time problem that had kept Clojure out of scripting niches.

The Living Present

As of 2026, the Lisp family is healthier than it has been since the 1980s. Common Lisp survives in production at ITA/Google (QPX airline booking), Grammarly (early backend), Mirai Animation, Opusmodus, and a long tail of quiet deployments. Scheme lives on in Racket’s language-creation ecosystem, in Chez Scheme (Cisco’s production-grade implementation, now open source), and in GNU Guile (the official GNU extension language). Emacs Lisp runs in tens of millions of Emacs installations. Clojure has a thriving enterprise community. AutoLISP is still shipped with every copy of AutoCAD, used by thousands of drafters.

And critically, the ideas from Lisp are everywhere, even in languages that look nothing like Lisp. Python’s lambda and list comprehensions come from Lisp. Ruby’s blocks are closures. JavaScript’s first-class functions and its entire asynchronous model trace back to Scheme’s continuations. Julia’s multiple dispatch and macros are directly borrowed from Common Lisp. Rust’s procedural macros echo Lisp’s defmacro. Modern TypeScript type-system tricks look suspiciously like what Typed Racket did in 2006.

Even outside the explicit Lisp lineage, the spread of functional programming since 2010 — map/reduce/filter everywhere, immutable data as default, pattern matching, algebraic data types in mainstream languages — is Lisp-adjacent. The 1958 ideas have won, even if the 1958 syntax didn’t.

Selected Timeline

1932 Church introduces the λ-calculus

Alonzo Church’s formal system for functions becomes the theoretical ancestor of every functional language.

1936 Church and Turing prove undecidability

Church via λ-calculus, Turing via the Turing machine. The two approaches are equivalent; the Church-Turing thesis is born.

1956 Dartmouth Summer AI Project

McCarthy coins “artificial intelligence” in the proposal. Newell, Simon, and Shaw demo the Logic Theorist in IPL.

1958 McCarthy invents Lisp at MIT

AI Memo No. 1 describes a language based on λ-abstractions and list operations. S-expressions start as a temporary notation.

1959 Garbage collection is invented

McCarthy’s mark-and-sweep algorithm, built to keep LISP 1 from running out of list cells, is the first automatic memory management system.

1960 Steve Russell implements eval by hand

McCarthy’s theoretical specification becomes a working interpreter when Russell translates it into IBM 704 machine code. The first Lisp interpreter.

1962 LISP 1.5 Programmer’s Manual published

The first widely-distributed Lisp reference. Still readable today; still coherent.

1966 Maclisp begins at MIT

Richard Greenblatt and others build the successor to LISP 1.5 for the PDP-6. Maclisp becomes the dominant research Lisp for the next decade.

1968 MACSYMA starts at MIT

Carl Engelman and others begin the computer algebra system that becomes the ancestor of Mathematica and Maple.

1970 SHRDLU demonstrated

Terry Winograd’s natural-language understanding program, written in Planner (a Lisp dialect), manipulates a virtual blocks world.

1974 CONS Lisp Machine project begins at MIT

Greenblatt starts building hardware optimized for running Lisp.

1975 Sussman and Steele create Scheme

The Lambda Papers begin to appear. Scheme introduces lexical scoping and proper tail calls.

1979 Symbolics and LMI split off

Russell Noftsker founds Symbolics; Richard Greenblatt founds Lisp Machines Inc. The bitter split sets the pattern for the commercial AI era.

1981 Common Lisp committee convenes

ARPA sponsors an effort to unify the Lisp dialects. Guy Steele edits.

1983 Symbolics 3600 ships

The flagship Lisp Machine enters production. Prices run from $70K to $150K.

1984 Common Lisp: The Language published

CLtL1 becomes the de facto standard. Steven Levy’s Hackers documents the MIT AI Lab culture.

1985 symbolics.com registered

On March 15, Symbolics registers the first commercial domain name in Internet history.

1985 SICP published

Abelson and Sussman’s Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs becomes the intro CS textbook at MIT and many other schools for two decades.

1987 CLOS specified

The Common Lisp Object System, with multiple dispatch and a metaobject protocol, extends Common Lisp.

1988 The AI bubble bursts

Expert system companies falter. Lisp Machine sales collapse. The “AI winter” begins.

1990 CLtL2 published

Second edition of Common Lisp: The Language, with CLOS and the condition system.

1992 Koza publishes Genetic Programming

Using Lisp S-expressions as evolving chromosomes, Koza kicks off a new subfield.

1993 Symbolics files for bankruptcy

The flagship Lisp Machine company falls. Genera lives on as a port to DEC Alpha and later x86.

1994 ANSI X3.226-1994 ratified

Common Lisp becomes an ANSI standard. The standard has not been revised since; it is considered complete.

1995 Viaweb founded

Paul Graham and Robert Morris bet a company on Common Lisp and win.

1998 R5RS Scheme published

The elegant, stable Scheme standard that most textbooks still teach from.

2001 “Beating the Averages” essay

Graham’s article introduces the Blub Paradox and starts a Lisp revival.

2007 Clojure 1.0 released

Rich Hickey brings Lisp to the JVM with immutable data structures and a fresh take on concurrency.

2010 Quicklisp launches

Zach Beane’s package manager finally solves the Common Lisp library-distribution problem.

2010 ITA/Google acquisition

Google buys ITA Software for $700 million. ITA’s QPX flight-search engine is written in Common Lisp.

2011 ClojureScript released

Clojure now runs in the browser. Reagent and re-frame make React feel lispy.

2011 “Simple Made Easy”

Rich Hickey’s talk at Strange Loop changes how a generation of programmers thinks about complexity.

2013 R7RS-small published

The Scheme community produces a smaller core and defers the large standard to ongoing work.

2015 Chez Scheme goes open source

Cisco releases R. Kent Dybvig’s production-grade Scheme compiler as open source. It is the fastest Scheme in the world.

2019 Babashka released

Michiel Borkent ships Clojure compiled to GraalVM native images, opening the scripting niche.

2022 Native compilation in Emacs 28

Andrea Corallo’s gccemacs project lands in mainline, adding ahead-of-time compilation of Emacs Lisp.

2026 The loop closes

LLMs generate code, autodiff systems transform programs, neurosymbolic research bridges the symbolic and neural traditions. Every tool the modern AI programmer uses has a Lisp ancestor.
