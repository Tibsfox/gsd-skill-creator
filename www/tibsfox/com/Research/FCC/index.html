Every radio wave transmitted in the United States passes through a legal membrane. Every WiFi chip in a laptop, every Starlink terminal bolted to a barn roof, every FM station transmitting across a valley, every ship calling the Coast Guard on Channel 16 — all of it operates under the authority of a single, massive regulatory document: Title 47 of the Code of Federal Regulations. The FCC catalog is not just bureaucratic paper. It is the architectural specification of American telecommunications.

Research Modules

M1: Structural Anatomy

Structure Track A · Architecture

Title 47 architecture mapped in full: five chapters, four FCC subchapters, five printed volumes, the eCFR/GPO relationship, Federal Register amendment pipeline, and bureau-to-Part organizational mapping covering all eight FCC bureaus and offices.

M2: Spectrum & Unlicensed Operations

Spectrum Track A · Part 15 Deep Dive

Part 2 frequency allocations, Part 5 experimental licensing, Part 15 unlicensed RF devices in full — all eleven subparts, band-by-band power limits, device authorization paths, Class A/B distinction, and the 2026 6 GHz GVP expansion order.

M3: Common Carrier & Wireless Services

Carrier Track B · Subchapter B

Subchapter B in full: commercial mobile (Part 20), PCS (Part 24), satellite (Part 25), broadband (Part 27), Universal Service (Part 54), interconnection (Part 51). The Sixth Circuit net neutrality ruling and its Title I/II implications.

M4: Broadcast Radio Services

Broadcast Track B · Subchapter C

Part 73 AM/FM/TV broadcast rules with allotment table structure, Part 74 auxiliary and LPTV, Part 76 cable television, Part 78 relay, Part 79 accessibility. The ATSC 3.0 transition and digital broadcast architecture.

M5: Private Radio Services

Private Track C · Subchapter D

Part 80 maritime, Part 87 aviation, Part 90 land mobile, Part 95 GMRS/FRS/CB/MURS, Part 97 amateur radio with AREDN mesh deep-dive, Part 101 fixed microwave. Spread spectrum authority and digital mode provisions.

M6: Regulatory Flux & Compliance

Flux Track D · Synthesis

The 2025–2026 deregulatory surge: Carr's "Delete, Delete, Delete" initiative, spectrum auction pipeline, device authorization workflow, NTIA/BEAD, FirstNet, pending NPRMs, and the GSD infrastructure compliance cross-reference index.
