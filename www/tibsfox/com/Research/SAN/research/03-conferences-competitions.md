## 1. The Conference Model

SANS conferences are not academic conferences. They do not feature keynote speakers presenting peer-reviewed papers to an audience of researchers. They are intensive training delivery events where attendees take 5-6 day courses, compete in cyber range tournaments, attend evening bonus sessions, and build professional networks with other practitioners [1][2].

The distinction matters because it reflects a fundamental difference in educational philosophy. Academic conferences advance the knowledge boundary of a field. SANS conferences advance practitioners to the current knowledge boundary. One pushes the frontier; the other brings the workforce to it. Both are necessary. SANS chose the second mission [1][2].

SANS operates dozens of events annually across multiple continents. The scale is substantial -- the largest events offer 40-50+ simultaneous courses with thousands of attendees. The events range from massive multi-track flagship conferences to focused single-domain summits [2][3].

The major SANS training conferences anchor the annual calendar:

| Event | Location | Scale | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|-------|
| SANS 2026 | Orlando, FL | 50+ courses | Largest annual event; full curriculum representation |
| Security West 2026 | San Diego, CA | 40+ courses | Flagship West Coast event |
| SANS Surge | San Diego, CA | Focused event | Intensive training concentration |
| SANS Arlington | Arlington, VA | Regional event | DC-area training for government/defense |
| SANS London June | London, UK | Major European | Primary UK/European training event |
| SANS Amsterdam June | Amsterdam, NL | European hub | Continental European training |
| SANS Singapore | Singapore | Asia-Pacific hub | Regional training for APAC |

These flagship events rotate through consistent locations, building institutional relationships with venues and creating predictable annual touchpoints for the professional community. Many attendees attend the same event year after year, creating a rhythm of training and community engagement [2][3].

Beyond the flagship conferences, SANS runs focused summits targeting specific cybersecurity domains:

| Summit | Location (2026) | Focus | Annual |
|--------|----------------|-------|--------|
| **Cybersecurity Leadership Summit** | Arlington, VA (March) | CISOs, directors, managers; strategy and governance | Yes |
| **AI Cybersecurity Summit** | Arlington, VA | AI threats, AI-assisted defense, LLM security | Newest addition |
| **ICS Security Summit** | Orlando, FL (June) | Industrial control systems, OT defense, critical infrastructure | Long-running |
| **Security Awareness & Culture Summit** | Las Vegas, NV (August) | Human risk management, behavior change | 13th annual |
| **DFIRCON** | TBD | Digital forensics, incident response, open-source DFIR tools | Yes |

The summit model allows SANS to provide domain-specific community building and content that flagship events cannot. The ICS Security Summit, for example, brings together OT security professionals who might be scattered across general-purpose SANS events. The concentration creates a critical mass of domain expertise in one place [3][4].

## 4. Event Anatomy

A typical SANS conference week includes multiple concurrent activities [2][3]:

**Daytime: Training Courses.** The core offering. Students attend their selected 5-6 day course from morning through late afternoon. Multiple courses run simultaneously -- a large event might have 40+ courses underway at once.

**Evening: SANS@Night.** Bonus sessions held in the evening, featuring talks by faculty and guest speakers on topics outside the formal course catalog. SANS@Night sessions are exclusive to in-person attendees and are consistently cited as a high-value benefit of physical attendance.

**Evening: NetWars Tournaments.** Competitive cyber range events where attendees test and develop their skills in gamified environments. NetWars tournaments at conferences serve as qualifying rounds for the annual Tournament of Champions.

**Networking: Solutions Expo & Welcome Reception.** Vendor showcases and structured networking events. The Welcome Reception on the first evening provides an informal setting for attendees to meet before their courses begin.

**Hands-on Workshops.** Supplementary workshops that provide additional practical experience beyond the formal course labs.

**CPE Credits.** Many conference activities earn Continuing Professional Education credits, which are required for maintaining GIAC and other cybersecurity certifications.

## 5. NetWars Cyber Ranges

**NetWars** is SANS' suite of interactive cyber range platforms used for hands-on skill development and competition. The platform creates realistic network environments where participants practice attack and defense techniques in controlled settings [1][5].

NetWars variants include:

| Variant | Domain | Focus |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Core NetWars** | Multi-disciplinary | General cybersecurity challenges across domains |
| **DFIR NetWars** | Forensics | Digital forensic investigation and incident response |
| **ICS NetWars** | Industrial | Factory floor simulation; OT attack and defense |
| **Cyber Defense NetWars** | Blue team | Defensive monitoring, detection, and response |
| **Grid NetWars** | Critical infrastructure | Power generation and grid security |

NetWars has been adopted by military organizations for cybersecurity training. The U.S. Air Force and U.S. Army use NetWars platforms for military cybersecurity skill development. The military adoption validates the platform's effectiveness for high-stakes training [5].

The **NetWars Tournament of Champions** is an annual competition bringing together top performers from throughout the year. The tournament serves as both a competitive event and a community builder, connecting elite practitioners from different organizations and domains [5].

**NetWars Continuous** provides ongoing access to cyber range environments outside of conference events, enabling year-round skill practice and development [5].

## 6. Holiday Hack Challenge

The **SANS Holiday Hack Challenge** is an annual free cybersecurity competition created by Ed Skoudis and the Counter Hack team. Running for 21+ years, it has become one of the most beloved traditions in the cybersecurity community [1][6].

Key characteristics:

- **Free.** The entire challenge is available at no cost. No registration fee, no paywall, no subscription required.
- **All skill levels.** Challenges span from complete beginners to expert practitioners. A newcomer can participate alongside a 20-year veteran.
- **Immersive.** The challenge features a gamified virtual environment with a holiday-themed storyline. The 2025 edition ("Revenge of the Gnome(s)") introduced a pure CTF mode alongside the traditional game world.
- **Diverse topics.** Challenges cover network security, cloud exploitation, LLM vulnerabilities, post-quantum cryptography, forensics, and more.
- **Scale.** More than 15,000 players participate annually.
- **Year-round access.** The challenge remains open for practice after the competition period ends.
- **Prizes.** Winners receive SANS OnDemand course access and NetWars Continuous subscriptions.

The Holiday Hack Challenge embodies Alan Paller's philosophy that the cybersecurity community's baseline knowledge should be free to elevate. The challenge is not a marketing funnel (though it naturally introduces participants to SANS training); it is a genuine gift to the community [6].

A micro-challenge system introduced in recent years provides bite-sized challenges for accessibility, lowering the barrier for time-constrained participants. This design choice reflects SANS' attention to the full spectrum of engagement -- from a 15-minute micro-challenge to a week-long deep dive [6].

## 7. Capture the Flag

SANS CTF (Capture the Flag) events run at conferences and as standalone competitions. CTF challenges test practical cybersecurity skills in competitive formats: participants find "flags" (hidden strings) by exploiting vulnerabilities, analyzing network traffic, reverse-engineering malware, or performing forensic investigations [1][5].

CTF competitions serve multiple functions:

**Skill validation.** CTF performance demonstrates practical ability in ways that certifications cannot. Solving a forensics challenge under time pressure is a different skill than answering exam questions about forensics.

**Community building.** CTF teams create bonds between practitioners who might never interact in their day-to-day work. The shared experience of solving problems together builds professional relationships.

**Recruitment pipeline.** Employers increasingly recognize CTF performance as evidence of practical capability. CTF competitions serve as informal talent identification events.

## 8. How SANS Conferences Differ

The distinction between SANS conferences and academic security conferences is fundamental [1][2]:

| Dimension | SANS Conference | Academic Conference |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Primary content** | Multi-day training courses | Paper presentations |
| **Attendees** | Working practitioners | Researchers and academics |
| **Goal** | Develop practitioner skills | Advance research knowledge |
| **Duration** | 5-6 day courses | 2-4 day paper sessions |
| **Evaluation** | GIAC certification exam | Peer review of papers |
| **Hands-on** | Labs, NetWars, CTF | Minimal or none |
| **Cost model** | Training fees ($8,500+) | Registration fees ($500-$1,000) |

Both models are essential. IEEE S&P, USENIX Security, and ACM CCS advance the field's knowledge boundary through peer-reviewed research. SANS advances the field's practitioners to the current knowledge boundary through hands-on training. The two models are complementary, not competitive [1][2].

## 9. The Networking Premium

SANS consistently finds that attendees rate networking as one of the top benefits of in-person attendance. This is not accidental -- it is architectural [2][3]:

In-person SANS events concentrate hundreds or thousands of cybersecurity practitioners from different organizations, sectors, and specializations. The structured networking events (Welcome Reception, Solutions Expo), informal social time (SANS@Night, hotel bar conversations), and course-based interactions create a dense web of professional connections.

These connections have practical value. A security analyst who meets an incident responder from another organization at a SANS event now has someone to call when they encounter a novel attack. A CISO who meets peers at the Leadership Summit gains perspective on how other organizations handle the same challenges. The network is as valuable as the training [3].
