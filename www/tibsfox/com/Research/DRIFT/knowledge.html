Overview

Knowledge drift is the gradual erosion of factual accuracy as an LLM generates longer outputs, encounters misleading training data, or operates on a knowledge base that has aged since its pretraining cutoff. Module A covers five primary phenomena across this surface, from the SD score (a paragraph-level drift metric) through intermediate-layer probing methods that detect drift before it reaches the output layer.

1. Semantic Drift Score

Spataru et al. (2024) introduced the Semantic Drift (SD) score as a paragraph-level metric computed against atomic facts extracted from a reference document. For each paragraph, the SD score measures cosine-similarity decay between the model's claim embeddings and the ground-truth fact embeddings (Spataru et al. 2024, arXiv:2404.05411). Across major LLMs the SD score sits at 0.77–0.79, and 37% of paragraphs show measurable drift within the first 10% of output tokens (Spataru et al. 2024).

The practical implication: factual grounding is most reliable in the first few sentences of any LLM response. Long-form generation tasks (summaries, reports, research synthesis) face increasing drift risk as output length grows. The SD score provides a standardized way to set length-dependent reliability thresholds for downstream applications.

2. Misinformation-Induced Knowledge Drift

Fastowski et al. (2024) studied how exposure to misinformation during fine-tuning or RAG injection shifts LLM factual beliefs (arXiv:2409.07085). The key finding: misinformation injection produces asymmetric confidence swings — +56.6% overconfidence on injected false facts and −52.8% uncertainty on displaced true facts (Fastowski et al. 2024). The asymmetry is crucial: the model does not simply become uncertain; it becomes wrongly confident on the injected falsehoods while losing confidence in genuine facts.

This finding has direct implications for RAG systems that ingest unvetted document collections. A single misinformation document can produce durable belief shifts that persist across subsequent queries even when the misleading document is removed from the retrieval index.

3. Natural Context Drift vs. Pretraining Snapshot

Wu et al. (2025) tested how natural language understanding degrades when context (questions, world state) drifts from the distribution seen at pretraining (arXiv:2509.01093). On BoolQ, a standard reading-comprehension benchmark, performance dropped by 30 percentage points when questions were reformulated to reflect post-cutoff world knowledge that the model had not seen (Wu et al. 2025). This is not a memorization failure — the model architecture is unchanged; the world has moved.

Natural context drift is especially relevant for deployed systems with static model weights. The practical response is either continual updating (via fine-tuning or RAG) or calibrated uncertainty output that flags responses in domains with high post-cutoff drift risk.

4. Layer-wise Semantic Dynamics (LSD)

Mir et al. (2025) introduced LSD, a probing method that tracks semantic trajectories across transformer layers to detect hallucination onset before it reaches the output layer (arXiv:2510.04933). LSD achieves F1 of 0.92 and AUROC of 0.96 for hallucination detection, substantially outperforming output-only classifiers (Mir et al. 2025). The key insight: when a model is about to hallucinate, the semantic trajectory in intermediate layers diverges from the factually-grounded trajectory that precedes accurate outputs. This divergence is measurable and predictive.

LSD enables early stopping strategies: a model can be prompted to hedge or decline to continue if layer-wise divergence is detected, rather than producing a confident hallucination.

5. DRIFT Probe

The DRIFT probe (2026, arXiv:2601.14210) extends intermediate-representation probing directly to factual truthfulness, targeting the residual stream at mid-depth layers to classify whether the model’s internal representation of a claim is truth-consistent. The DRIFT probe achieves +13 AUROC points over prior SOTA methods (2026), establishing a new baseline for real-time factuality monitoring. Unlike LSD (which detects hallucination onset), DRIFT operates on completed representations and can be deployed as a verification gate on model outputs.
