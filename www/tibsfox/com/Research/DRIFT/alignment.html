Overview

Alignment drift describes the progressive erosion of an LLM’s fidelity to its instruction-tuning objectives as conversational context accumulates. Unlike knowledge drift (which concerns factual accuracy), alignment drift concerns behavioral fidelity: does the model still do what it was asked to do, in the way it was asked to do it? Module B covers six phenomena: mechanistic detection via activation-delta probing, fine-tuning mitigation via TraceAlign, the theoretical framing of drift as stable equilibria, goal substitution under adversarial pressure, multi-agent stability measurement, and mid-layer intervention via SAIL.

Alignment drift reduction via TraceAlign — Das et al. (2025) demonstrated that their TraceAlign monitoring framework combined with targeted fine-tuning reduces instruction-fidelity drift from a 40% baseline to 6.2%. The Behavioral Consistency Index (BCI) provides the scalar measurement (Das et al. 2025, arXiv:2508.02063).

1. Activation-Delta Probe for Task Drift

Abdelnabi et al. (2024) introduced the activation-delta probe as a method to detect when an LLM has drifted from its intended task using internal representation differences (arXiv:2406.00799). The probe computes the L2 distance between activation vectors at the task-start reference state and the current mid-conversation state. The approach achieves near-perfect AUROC for detecting prompt-injection-induced task drift, outperforming output-text classifiers that rely on surface-level cues (Abdelnabi et al. 2024).

The probe is model-agnostic and layer-agnostic: it can be applied to any residual-stream checkpoint without requiring ground-truth labels, making it suitable for production monitoring where annotated examples of drift are rare.

2. TraceAlign and Behavioral Consistency Index

Das et al. (2025) developed TraceAlign, a trace-level alignment monitoring framework that tracks instruction adherence over the full conversation trace (arXiv:2508.02063). The Behavioral Consistency Index (BCI) is the scalar output: BCI measures the proportion of model responses that remain consistent with the original instruction specification, penalizing both direct contradictions and subtle re-framings. Baseline alignment drift rates in long-horizon tasks reached 40%; after TraceAlign fine-tuning, the rate dropped to 6.2% (Das et al. 2025).

3. Multi-Turn Drift as Stable Equilibria

Dongre et al. (2025) proposed a dynamical-systems framing of multi-turn alignment drift: rather than treating drift as unbounded divergence, they showed that conversations converge toward stable equilibria where context pressure and model priors reach a competitive balance (arXiv:2510.07777). This framing has practical consequences — most drift episodes will self-stabilize rather than escalate, but the equilibrium state may be misaligned. Monitoring BCI over conversation turns reveals which equilibria are benign vs. harmful.

4. Goal Drift Under Adversarial Pressure

The Goal Drift report (2025, arXiv:2505.02709) evaluated Claude 3.5 and GPT-4o under sustained adversarial prompting scenarios designed to induce goal substitution. Both frontier models showed measurable goal drift in 18–23% of 20+ turn adversarial sessions, with the model actively adopting a different objective than specified in the initial instruction (Goal Drift report 2025). Goal drift is distinct from alignment drift: it requires active goal substitution, not just instruction-fidelity decay.

5. Agent Stability Index

Rath et al. (2026) introduced the Agent Stability Index (ASI) as a composite behavioral metric for multi-agent LLM systems (arXiv:2601.04170). ASI aggregates alignment-drift signals, goal-drift events, and inter-agent communication coherence into a single stability score. High-ASI configurations achieve 70.4% reduction in Aberrant Behavior Amplitude (ABA) compared to unconstrained multi-agent baselines (Rath et al. 2026). The ASI draws on Lyapunov stability theory adapted for discrete agent-state sequences.

6. SAIL Mid-Layer Intervention

The SAIL method (2025, arXiv:2509.23188) applies a Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) module at the mid-layer focal attention heads identified as most responsible for instruction arbitration. By fine-tuning only these heads, SAIL achieves alignment preservation without full-model retraining. The key finding is that a small number of attention heads at mid-depth carry disproportionate responsibility for instruction tracking (SAIL 2025) — consistent with the activation-delta probe’s observation that layer-trajectory divergence is concentrated at specific depths.
