## 1. The Cooperative Model

A worker cooperative is a business owned and democratically governed by its workers. Unlike a traditional corporation where shareholders (who may have no relationship to the actual work) control the enterprise, a worker co-op places ownership and governance in the hands of the people who show up every day, run the machines, manage the projects, and deliver the product.

The International Cooperative Alliance defines cooperatives through seven principles: voluntary and open membership, democratic member control, member economic participation, autonomy and independence, education and training, cooperation among cooperatives, and concern for community [1]. These are not aspirational -- they are structural. A cooperative that violates these principles is no longer, in any meaningful sense, a cooperative.

For the PNW Millwork Co-op Network, the cooperative model is not a philosophical preference. It is the specific structural defense against the failure mode that destroyed the predecessor organization. Synsor Corporation operated for 46 years as a conventional corporation. It was acquired by PremierXD, a New York consolidator, in April 2017. The Everett operation wound down. The workers lost their jobs. The community lost its shop. The clients lost their supplier. Under a worker cooperative structure with acquisition-protection bylaws, that transaction is structurally impossible -- the shares are not for sale to outside buyers [2].

```
OWNERSHIP STRUCTURE COMPARISON
================================================================

  TRADITIONAL CORPORATION         WORKER COOPERATIVE
  +-----------------------+       +-----------------------+
  | Shareholders          |       | Worker-Members        |
  | (may be anywhere)     |       | (work in the shop)    |
  |                       |       |                       |
  | Shares traded freely  |       | Shares personal to    |
  | on open market        |       | member, non-          |
  |                       |       | transferable outside   |
  | Profit -> dividends   |       | Profit -> patronage   |
  | (by capital invested) |       | (by labor contributed)|
  |                       |       |                       |
  | Board answers to      |       | Board answers to      |
  | shareholders          |       | worker-members        |
  |                       |       |                       |
  | VULNERABLE to         |       | IMMUNE to hostile     |
  | hostile acquisition   |       | acquisition by design |
  +-----------------------+       +-----------------------+
```

## 2. Washington State Law: RCW 23.86

Washington State's cooperative corporation statute is RCW 23.86. It provides the legal framework for forming and operating cooperative associations in Washington. Key provisions relevant to the millwork network [3]:

### Formation Requirements

- **Incorporators:** Minimum three natural persons
- **Filing:** Articles of incorporation filed with the Washington Secretary of State
- **Filing fee:** Approximately $180 (subject to change; verify with SOS office)
- **Required articles provisions:** Name (must include "cooperative" or "co-op"), purpose, registered agent, principal office, member classes (if any), initial board of directors

### Member Definition

A member of a worker cooperative under RCW 23.86 is a worker who has completed a probationary period (established by bylaws, typically six months) and purchased a membership share. The probationary period serves a dual purpose: it allows the existing membership to assess the candidate's fit, and it allows the candidate to understand the cooperative's operations before making a financial commitment.

### Voting Rights

RCW 23.86 establishes the one-member-one-vote principle as the default governance structure. This cannot be overridden by creating "super-voting" share classes. Each worker-member gets exactly one vote regardless of seniority, capital contribution, or production output.

### Equity and Shares

Membership shares represent the member's equity stake in the cooperative. Under the bylaws designed for this network, shares are explicitly non-transferable to non-members. When a member departs (retirement, resignation, or expulsion), their equity is redeemed by the cooperative at book value or a formula-determined price specified in the bylaws.

> **SAFETY WARNING:** The non-transferability of shares is the acquisition-protection mechanism. Any proposed bylaw amendment that weakens this provision must be treated as a critical security event. The board must flag it, the full membership must vote, and the threshold for approval should be set at a supermajority (two-thirds or higher).

### Annual Requirements

- Annual report filing with the Secretary of State
- Annual member meeting with board election
- Financial reporting to membership (transparency is a cooperative principle, not optional)

## 3. Worker Cooperative Formation

The formation process for a worker cooperative under RCW 23.86 follows a specific sequence. The Northwest Cooperative Development Center (NWCDC) in Olympia, Washington, is the primary formation support organization for the OR/WA/ID region and should be engaged before any filings are made [4].

### Formation Sequence

1. **Convene founding cohort** -- Identify 8-12 founding worker-owners with the required skill mix (CNC operators, finish room workers, project managers, BOM engineers)
2. **Engage NWCDC** -- Contact NWCDC for co-op formation advisory services. They have assisted manufacturing co-ops in the region and understand the specific challenges
3. **Draft articles of incorporation** -- Prepared in consultation with NWCDC and a co-op formation attorney. Must include all RCW 23.86 mandatory provisions
4. **Draft bylaws** -- The operational governance document. Must include: acquisition-protection equity transfer restriction, one-member-one-vote, patronage dividend formula, probationary period, board election process, expulsion provisions, dispute resolution
5. **File with Secretary of State** -- Submit articles, pay filing fee, receive certificate of incorporation
6. **Initial member meeting** -- Adopt bylaws, elect initial board, establish membership share price, set probationary period
7. **Obtain EIN** -- Apply to IRS for Employer Identification Number
8. **Open bank accounts** -- Business checking, payroll, and capitalization accounts
9. **Register with WA Department of Revenue** -- Sales tax, B&O tax registrations

### Timeline Estimate

| Step | Estimated Duration | Dependencies |
|------|-------------------|-------------|
| Founding cohort assembly | 2-4 months | Outreach, Synsor alumni network |
| NWCDC engagement | 1-2 months | Cohort assembled |
| Articles and bylaws drafting | 1-2 months | NWCDC advisory |
| Secretary of State filing | 2-4 weeks | Drafts complete |
| Initial member meeting | 1 day | Filing approved |
| EIN and bank accounts | 1-2 weeks | Incorporation complete |
| **Total formation timeline** | **4-9 months** | |

## 4. The Acquisition-Protection Mechanism

The central legal innovation of this network is the acquisition-protection bylaw. It is not optional. It is not a nice-to-have. It is the structural defense that prevents the Synsor failure mode from recurring.

### The Bylaw Provision

The bylaws must include language substantially equivalent to:

> "Membership shares in this cooperative are personal to the member and may not be sold, transferred, pledged, or otherwise conveyed to any person or entity other than the cooperative itself. Any purported transfer of a membership share in violation of this section is void."

This provision does three things simultaneously:

1. **Prevents hostile acquisition.** A buyer cannot accumulate shares by purchasing them from individual members. There is no mechanism by which an outsider gains a controlling interest.
2. **Prevents friendly acquisition.** Even if a majority of members wanted to sell, the bylaws prevent the transfer. The only path is a bylaw amendment -- which requires a membership vote at a supermajority threshold.
3. **Ensures departing members are compensated.** When a member leaves, the cooperative redeems their shares at book value. The member gets their money; the co-op retains the membership slot.

### Amendment Protection

The acquisition-protection provision should itself be protected by a supermajority amendment threshold. A standard bylaw amendment might require a simple majority (51%). The acquisition-protection provision should require two-thirds (67%) or three-quarters (75%) approval to amend. This creates a structural barrier: even a well-funded acquirer who convinced a bare majority of members to sell would be blocked by the supermajority requirement.

### Legal Precedent

The PNW plywood cooperatives have maintained similar provisions for over a century. The Olympia Veneer Company (founded 1921) and its successors operated with non-transferable member shares through multiple economic cycles, including the 2008 financial crisis. The legal framework is proven in Washington State courts [5].

## 5. Federation Architecture

A single co-op node is viable as a standalone business. But the vision for the PNW Millwork Co-op Network requires a federated structure -- multiple production nodes coordinated through a shared contracting entity. This is how a network of small shops bids competitively against national consolidators on 200-unit rollout contracts.

### Two-Layer Structure

```
FEDERATION ARCHITECTURE
================================================================

  LAYER 1: INDIVIDUAL NODE CO-OPS
  +------------------+  +------------------+  +------------------+
  | Node North       |  | Node Central     |  | Node South       |
  | (Everett)        |  | (South King Co.) |  | (Portland)       |
  | RCW 23.86 entity |  | RCW 23.86 entity |  | RCW 23.86 entity |
  | Own workers      |  | Own workers      |  | Own workers      |
  | Own equipment    |  | Own equipment    |  | Own equipment    |
  +--------+---------+  +--------+---------+  +--------+---------+
           |                      |                      |
           +----------------------+----------------------+
                                  |
  LAYER 2: FEDERATED ENTITY      v
  +-------------------------------------------------------+
  | PNW MILLWORK CO-OP FEDERATION                         |
  | (Multi-member LLC or second co-op entity)             |
  |                                                       |
  | Holds: master client contracts, shared BOM database,  |
  |        brand identity, quality standards, FSC cert    |
  | Does NOT hold: production equipment, real estate,     |
  |                individual worker employment           |
  +-------------------------------------------------------+
```

### What the Federation Holds

- **Master client contracts.** When Starbucks signs a 200-unit fixture contract, they sign with the federation, not with an individual node. This presents a single point of contact and a unified brand to the client.
- **Shared BOM database.** Engineering BOMs are the network's intellectual property. They are created by engineering talent across all nodes and stored centrally for consistency.
- **Quality standards.** AWI grade compliance is enforced at the federation level. A unit produced at Node South must be indistinguishable from a unit produced at Node North.
- **FSC Chain of Custody certification.** The federation holds the CoC certificate. Individual nodes operate under the federation's FSC scope.
- **Brand and reputation.** The federation is the entity that clients know, trust, and return to.

### What the Federation Does NOT Hold

- **Equipment.** Each node owns its own CNC routers, sanders, finish room equipment. This prevents a single equipment lien from threatening the whole network.
- **Real estate.** Each node holds its own lease or property. Geographic distribution is a feature, not a liability.
- **Employment relationships.** Each worker is a member of their local node co-op, not an employee of the federation. This preserves the one-member-one-vote principle at the local level.

### Inter-Co-op Agreement

The inter-co-op agreement is the contract between the node co-ops that governs federation operations. Key provisions:

- **Production allocation formula.** How a 200-unit order is split across nodes. Factors: capacity, geography (ship closest to delivery point), specialization (some nodes may specialize in specific finish types).
- **Revenue split formula.** Revenue distributed to nodes in proportion to labor hours contributed to each contract. The federation retains a percentage (typically 3-8%) for shared overhead.
- **Dispute resolution.** Binding arbitration before any litigation. The arbitrator pool should include NWCDC-recommended cooperative dispute specialists.
- **New node admission.** Requirements for a new node to join the federation: minimum cohort size, equipment baseline, AWI grade certification, NWCDC advisory review.

## 6. Governance: One Member, One Vote

Democratic governance is not just a cooperative principle -- in RCW 23.86, it is the law. Each member gets one vote. This applies to:

- **Board elections.** Directors are elected by the membership. Term limits should be established in bylaws (recommended: 3-year terms, staggered so the entire board does not turn over simultaneously).
- **Bylaw amendments.** Any change to the governing documents requires a membership vote.
- **Major decisions.** Capital expenditures above a threshold (set in bylaws), new node formation, federation agreement changes, and admission of new member classes all require membership votes.
- **Expulsion.** Removing a member from the cooperative requires a specific process (defined in bylaws) with due process protections and a membership vote.

### Board Structure

For a founding node with 8-12 members, a 3-5 member board is appropriate. Recommended composition:

| Role | Responsibility | Skills Required |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Chair | Meeting facilitation, external representation | Leadership, communication |
| Secretary | Records, filings, compliance | Organizational, detail-oriented |
| Treasurer | Financial oversight, reporting to membership | Accounting, financial literacy |
| Production Director | Shop floor operations, quality | CNC expertise, AWI knowledge |
| Member-at-Large | General membership advocacy | Any production skill |

### Meeting Cadence

- **Board meetings:** Monthly
- **Full membership meetings:** Quarterly minimum; annual meeting required by RCW 23.86
- **Special meetings:** Can be called by the board or by petition of 25% of membership
- **Kaizen events:** Quarterly (these are operational, not governance, but should align with membership meeting cadence)

## 7. Patronage Dividends and Revenue Distribution

In a worker cooperative, profits are distributed as patronage dividends -- allocated to members in proportion to their labor contribution, not their capital investment. This is the fundamental difference between cooperative and corporate economics.

### The Formula

```
PATRONAGE DIVIDEND CALCULATION
================================================================

  Gross Revenue (from all contracts)
    - Cost of Goods Sold (materials, supplies, subcontract)
    - Operating Expenses (rent, utilities, insurance, maintenance)
    - Federation Overhead (3-8% to federation shared costs)
    - Reserve Allocation (10-20% to retained earnings)
    - Tax Provision
    ────────────────────────────────────────────
  = Net Patronage Available for Distribution

  Individual Patronage Dividend =
    (Member's Labor Hours / Total Labor Hours) x Net Patronage

  Example:
    Net Patronage: $120,000
    Member A: 2,000 hours / 20,000 total hours = 10%
    Member A's Dividend: $12,000
```

### Reserve Allocation

Cooperatives must retain a portion of earnings for capital reserves. This is the mechanism by which the co-op builds equity over time without relying on outside investment. The bylaws should specify:

- **Minimum reserve allocation:** 10-20% of net patronage, non-distributable
- **Equipment reserve:** A separate allocation for CNC equipment replacement and upgrade (CNC routers have a useful life of 10-15 years)
- **Emergency reserve:** Three months of operating expenses, maintained as a floor

### Tax Treatment

Worker cooperatives that distribute patronage dividends based on labor (rather than capital) qualify for pass-through taxation under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code. The cooperative deducts patronage dividends from its taxable income; members report the dividends as personal income. This avoids the double-taxation problem that conventional corporations face [6].

## 8. Capitalization Pathways

A manufacturing co-op requires significant upfront capital -- CNC equipment, leasehold improvements, working capital, and formation costs. The vision document specifies three pathways. No single-pathway capitalization plan is acceptable; redundancy is required.

### Pathway 1: CDFI Lending (Craft3)

Craft3 is a Community Development Financial Institution operating across Washington and Oregon with specific experience in manufacturing and cooperative lending [7].

- **Loan range:** $50,000 - $3,000,000
- **Terms:** Market or below-market rates for community-benefit enterprises
- **Co-op advantage:** CDFIs are specifically chartered to serve underbanked communities and cooperative enterprises
- **Process:** Application, business plan review, site visit, underwriting, approval
- **Timeline:** 60-120 days from application to funding

### Pathway 2: SBA 7(a) Loans

The Small Business Administration's 7(a) loan program is available to worker cooperatives. NWCDC can connect to SBA-preferred lenders with cooperative experience [8].

- **Maximum loan:** $5,000,000
- **Terms:** Up to 25 years for real estate, 10 years for equipment
- **SBA guarantee:** Up to 85% of loans up to $150K; 75% for larger loans
- **Process:** Application through SBA-preferred lender, SBA review, approval
- **Timeline:** 30-90 days depending on lender

### Pathway 3: Member Equity Buy-In

Each founding worker-owner purchases a membership share. This builds initial capitalization and aligns incentive from day one.

- **Typical share price:** $1,000 - $10,000 per member
- **Recommended for Node North:** $5,000 per founding member (8-12 members = $40,000 - $60,000 initial member equity)
- **Payment plan option:** Bylaws may permit installment payment over 12-24 months
- **Advantage:** Zero interest cost; demonstrates member commitment to lenders

### Pathway 4: USDA Rural Development (Contingent)

If Node North's location qualifies as a rural-designated area in Snohomish County, USDA Business & Industry loan guarantees may apply [9].

- **Maximum guarantee:** 80% of loan up to $5M
- **Eligibility:** Rural area with population under 50,000 (portions of Snohomish County qualify)
- **Process:** Application through approved lender with USDA guarantee application
- **Note:** This is contingent on the specific site location. Verify eligibility before planning around it.

## 9. The PNW Plywood Co-op Precedent

The Pacific Northwest has the longest-running tradition of manufacturing cooperatives in North America. The plywood co-op movement, which began with the Olympia Veneer Company in 1921, produced dozens of worker-owned plywood mills across Washington and Oregon over the following decades [10].

### Key Facts

- **Founding:** Olympia Veneer Company, 1921, Olympia, Washington
- **Scale:** Individual co-ops had 80-450 worker-owners each
- **Duration:** Many operated for 40-60 years continuously
- **Products:** Plywood sheets, veneer, specialty panels
- **Market:** Competed successfully against both small independent mills and large corporate producers
- **Governance:** One member, one vote; shares non-transferable to non-workers

### What They Proved

The plywood co-ops proved that worker-owned manufacturing cooperatives can:

1. Operate at commercial scale (hundreds of workers, millions in annual revenue)
2. Compete against conventionally-owned competitors on price, quality, and delivery
3. Maintain democratic governance over decades without degenerating into oligarchy
4. Withstand economic downturns that bankrupted conventional competitors (co-op members absorbed wage cuts rather than layoffs, preserving institutional knowledge)
5. Function within the PNW timber supply chain specifically

### What They Got Wrong

The plywood co-op movement eventually contracted, primarily due to:

- **Raw material supply pressure.** Timber supply tightened as old-growth forests were logged out. Co-ops that owned no timberland had no hedge against supply price increases.
- **Aging membership without succession planning.** Some co-ops failed to recruit younger workers, leading to an aging workforce with no apprenticeship pipeline.
- **Failure to federate.** Individual co-ops competed against each other rather than coordinating. They lacked a federation layer for shared contracts and market intelligence.

The PNW Millwork Co-op Network addresses all three failure modes explicitly: the supply chain module maps multiple suppliers with backup protocols, the workforce pipeline ensures continuous recruitment, and the federation architecture coordinates rather than competes.

## 11. Community Economics and Regional Impact

A worker-owned manufacturing cooperative has fundamentally different economic impact on its community than a conventionally-owned factory.

### The Multiplier Effect

When a worker-owner earns a wage plus a patronage dividend, that income stays in the community at a higher rate than wages paid by a distant corporate owner. Studies of cooperative economics suggest a local economic multiplier of 1.5x to 2.0x compared to branch plants of non-local corporations [11]. The reasoning is structural:

- **Wages stay local.** Worker-owners live in the community where they work. They spend at local businesses, pay local property taxes, and invest in local schools.
- **Profits stay local.** Patronage dividends are distributed to local worker-members, not remitted to distant shareholders.
- **Governance stays local.** Decisions about plant operations, expansion, and contraction are made by people who live with the consequences.
- **Institutional knowledge stays local.** Worker-owned shops do not relocate to lower-cost regions. The Boeing Everett precedent is instructive: even massive corporate pressure could not dislodge decades of accumulated precision manufacturing culture from Snohomish County.

### Specific Regional Benefits

For the Everett/Snohomish County economy:

- **Job creation:** 8-12 founding worker-owner positions at Node North, growing with network expansion
- **Apprenticeship pipeline:** Sno-Isle TECH partnership creates a formal training-to-employment pathway
- **Supply chain spending:** Wholesale purchasing from regional suppliers (Emerson Hardwood, OrePac) keeps supply chain dollars in the PNW
- **Tax revenue:** Business & Occupation tax, sales tax on materials, property tax on leased space
- **Skill preservation:** The precision woodworking skills that Synsor developed over 46 years are at risk of being lost. The co-op is the vessel that preserves and transmits them.
