## 1. Synsor Corporation: The Primary Source Record

Synsor Corporation was founded in 1971 as a manufacturing company in the Everett/Mukilteo, Washington area. It operated from 1920 Merrill Creek Pkwy, Everett, WA 98203, near Paine Field and the Boeing Everett factory. It was licensed as a Construction Contractor -- Cabinets, Millwork and Finish Carpentry (Washington State License SYNSOC871BD) [1].

### Documented Client Portfolio

Primary source evidence (Superpages business listing, verified through industry databases) confirms the following client relationships [1]:

| Client | Product Category | Duration |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Starbucks | Store casework, espresso bar fixtures | Multi-year rollout |
| Alaska Airlines | Gate fixtures, cabin millwork | Cyclical refresh |
| AT&T | Retail store fixtures | Multi-location rollout |
| Charlotte Russe | Retail store fixtures | Chain-wide |

Synsor also operated under the trade name "Synsor Retail Innovation," indicating a specific focus on retail store fixture design and manufacturing as a branded service line.

### Production Profile

A 16-year employee review (Indeed.com, the longest-tenured employee review available) provides the most detailed operational description [2]:

- Engineered mechanical, electrical, case goods, and architectural millwork
- Composites and sheet metal fabrication (in addition to wood)
- Freestanding store fixtures
- Lean manufacturing environment
- BOM-based production engineering
- Product development running through project management, bills of material, and manufacturing engineering

This profile describes a full-service fixture manufacturer -- not just wood cutting but complete engineering from client scope of work through manufacturing engineering to finished product. The BOM-based methodology is the centerpiece: every project starts with a complete bill of materials before a single machine runs.

### Financial Scale

At the time of its 2017 acquisition, Synsor had approximately $45 million in annual revenue and approximately 145 employees (Manta.com estimate) [3]. This places it firmly in the mid-market for commercial fixture manufacturers -- large enough to service national rollout contracts, small enough to maintain the precision and relationship quality that its clients valued.

### Industry Standing

Synsor was listed alongside Lozier Corporation, JSI Store Fixtures, and Visual Creations in the U.S. Custom Architectural Woodwork and Millwork Market analysis published by Research and Markets / Business Wire in 2017 [4]. This positions it as a nationally recognized player in the commercial millwork sector, not just a regional shop.

## 2. The Everett Manufacturing Corridor

Everett and Snohomish County sit at the northern anchor of the Puget Sound manufacturing corridor. The area's manufacturing identity was shaped by three overlapping industries: timber and wood products, aerospace (Boeing), and naval shipbuilding (Everett Naval Station).

### Geographic Advantages

- **Industrial zoning:** The Merrill Creek / Paine Field area has extensive light-industrial and manufacturing zoning. Commercial real estate suitable for a 10,000-20,000 sq ft millwork operation is available.
- **Transportation:** I-5 access to the full corridor from British Columbia to Portland. Paine Field airport for airfreight. Port of Everett for materials receiving.
- **Workforce proximity:** Snohomish County has an active skilled-trades labor pool, drawn by Boeing's Everett plant (the largest building in the world by volume) and its supply chain.
- **Cost structure:** Lower real estate costs than Seattle proper, with better access to manufacturing-suitable spaces (high ceilings, heavy electrical service, industrial ventilation).

### The Merrill Creek Area

Synsor's former location at 1920 Merrill Creek Pkwy is in the industrial corridor between Paine Field and I-5. This area offers:

- Heavy industrial zoning allowing CNC equipment, spray booths, and delivery truck staging
- Proximity to Boeing's main workforce (many precision manufacturing skills transfer directly)
- Access to I-5 for supply chain deliveries and finished goods shipping
- Proximity to Sno-Isle TECH Skills Center for workforce pipeline
- Existing industrial infrastructure: heavy-duty electrical, industrial-grade ventilation, compressed air

## 3. Boeing-Adjacent Precision Culture

The Boeing Everett factory produces 747s, 767s, 777s, and 787s. The precision manufacturing culture that surrounds Boeing is not just about aerospace -- it permeates the entire regional workforce. CNC operators, finish specialists, quality inspectors, and manufacturing engineers who work in Boeing's supply chain develop skills that transfer directly to precision millwork.

### Transferable Skills

| Boeing/Aerospace Skill | Millwork Application |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| CNC machining (aluminum, composites) | CNC routing (wood, MDF, hardwood plywood) |
| Composite layup and finishing | Veneer layup, laminate application |
| Quality inspection to AS9100 | Quality inspection to AWI standards |
| BOM-based production planning | BOM-first engineering for fixture production |
| Lean manufacturing (Boeing Production System) | Lean millwork production (pull scheduling, 5S) |
| Finish room (paint, primer, sealant) | Finish room (stain, topcoat, sealant) |

The precision tolerance culture is the deepest transfer. Boeing's supply chain workers are trained to measure in thousandths of an inch and to reject work that doesn't meet spec. AWI Custom grade millwork requires tolerances of 1/32" to 1/16" -- looser than aerospace, but the mindset of never shipping out-of-spec work is identical.

### The Implicit Apprenticeship

Many Synsor employees likely came from the Boeing supply chain or moved between the two sectors during their careers. This cross-pollination created a manufacturing culture in Snohomish County where precision is not aspirational -- it is the baseline expectation. A founding cohort for Node North can draw on this culture explicitly.

## 4. The Acquisition and Its Consequences

In April 2017, Synsor Corporation was acquired by PremierXD, a New York-based consolidator operating in the retail fixture manufacturing sector [3]. The Everett operation was subsequently wound down.

### The Consolidation Pattern

Synsor's acquisition follows a well-documented pattern in American manufacturing:

1. A locally-owned, community-embedded manufacturer builds a reputation over decades
2. The founder(s) approach retirement age with no succession plan inside the company
3. A private equity firm or industry consolidator offers an acquisition price that values the company's client relationships and revenue stream
4. The new owner extracts the client contracts and intellectual property
5. Production is consolidated to lower-cost facilities (often out of region)
6. The local workforce is laid off; the community loses both the jobs and the institutional knowledge

This is not a conspiracy theory -- it is the documented lifecycle of thousands of mid-market American manufacturers. The structural defense against it is worker cooperative ownership, where the shares are non-transferable and the workers make the decisions.

### What Was Lost

When Synsor closed its Everett operation, the following was lost:

- **Client relationships:** 46 years of trust built with Starbucks, Alaska Airlines, AT&T, and other PNW-anchored brands
- **Production knowledge:** BOM engineering methodologies, CNC programming libraries, finish formulas, quality inspection protocols
- **Workforce pipeline:** The informal apprenticeship that brought new workers into the precision millwork trade
- **Community economic impact:** $45 million in annual revenue, 145 jobs, and the multiplier effect of a locally-owned manufacturer
- **Supply chain relationships:** Wholesale accounts, credit terms, and delivery schedules built over decades with regional timber and materials suppliers

### What Was Not Lost

The acquisition dispersed Synsor's knowledge but did not destroy it. The following remains recoverable:

- **Alumni network:** Former Synsor employees remain in the PNW. A LinkedIn search on "Synsor Corporation" returns a small but identifiable alumni network. Many are in their 40s-60s and remain active in manufacturing, procurement, and related fields.
- **Client demand:** Starbucks, Alaska Airlines, and hotel renovation chains still need the same products Synsor produced. The demand did not go away when the supplier did.
- **Regional supply infrastructure:** Emerson Hardwood, OrePac, Crosscut Hardwoods, and other PNW suppliers are still operating. The supply chain is intact.
- **Precision culture:** Boeing and its supply chain continue to train precision manufacturing workers in Snohomish County.

> **SAFETY WARNING:** The acquisition-protection bylaws specified in the Cooperative Economics module are the direct response to this failure mode. Any formation process that omits explicit equity transfer restrictions is building on the same structural vulnerability that destroyed Synsor.

## 6. PNW Timber Industry Heritage

The Pacific Northwest's identity as a timber region predates statehood. Douglas-fir, western red cedar, Sitka spruce, and hemlock forests produced the raw material that built cities from San Francisco to Honolulu. The timber industry shaped every aspect of PNW culture: land use, labor relations, transportation networks, education systems, and community identity [6].

### Old Growth and Second Growth

The old-growth forests that built the early timber industry are largely gone from commercial production. Modern PNW forestry operates primarily on second-growth and plantation timber, managed on 40-80 year rotation cycles. For millwork applications, the relevant shift is:

- **Old growth:** Large-diameter, tight-grained, dimensionally stable. Ideal for clear, premium-grade lumber. Increasingly unavailable and prohibitively expensive.
- **Second growth:** Smaller-diameter, wider-grained, more knots. Suitable for most commercial millwork when properly graded and selected. More abundant and sustainably managed.

The modern millwork supply chain has adapted to second-growth timber and supplements with imported hardwoods (cherry, maple, walnut) and engineered materials (MDF, hardwood plywood, veneer). The co-op's supply chain module maps these modern sources.

### The Environmental Context

The PNW timber industry exists in tension with environmental values. FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) certification is the market's response to this tension -- it verifies that timber is sourced from responsibly managed forests. For the co-op network, FSC Chain of Custody certification is both an ethical commitment and a commercial requirement. Starbucks, hotel chains, and LEED-certified projects all require FSC-certified materials [7].

## 7. The Plywood Co-op Era

The PNW plywood cooperative movement represents the longest and most successful experiment in worker-owned manufacturing in North American history. Beginning with the Olympia Veneer Company in 1921, plywood co-ops operated across Washington and Oregon for over six decades [8].

### Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1921 | Olympia Veneer Company founded (Olympia, WA) -- first plywood co-op |
| 1930s | Additional co-ops form during the Great Depression (worker buyouts of failed mills) |
| 1940s | Wartime production drives expansion; plywood co-ops serve military construction contracts |
| 1950s-60s | Peak era: 20+ plywood co-ops operating in WA and OR; 80-450 worker-owners per mill |
| 1970s | Timber supply tightens; some co-ops close or convert to conventional ownership |
| 1980s-90s | Further contraction; environmental regulation increases costs; foreign competition |
| 2000s-present | A few co-ops remain; the model is proven but requires adaptation to modern conditions |

### Operating Characteristics

- **Scale:** Individual co-ops ranged from 80 to 450 worker-owners
- **Governance:** One member, one vote; shares non-transferable; patronage dividends by labor hours
- **Compensation:** Worker-owners often earned higher total compensation (wages + patronage) than workers at conventional mills
- **Resilience:** During downturns, co-ops cut wages temporarily rather than laying off workers, preserving institutional knowledge and enabling faster recovery
- **Quality:** Co-op plywood was consistently rated among the highest quality in the industry because worker-owners had direct financial incentive for quality output

### Lessons for the Millwork Co-op

The plywood co-ops provide both positive and cautionary lessons:

**Positive:**
- Worker ownership works at manufacturing scale in the PNW specifically
- Democratic governance does not impair production efficiency
- Co-ops can compete on quality and price with conventional manufacturers
- Worker-owners accept temporary sacrifice (wage cuts) to preserve the enterprise

**Cautionary:**
- Supply chain dependency must be diversified (multiple suppliers, not one)
- Workforce succession must be systematic (apprenticeship pipeline, not passive recruitment)
- Federation is essential for market power (individual co-ops should coordinate, not compete)
- Capitalization reserves must be maintained (equipment replacement, market downturns)

## 8. Architectural Millwork in the Pacific Northwest

Architectural millwork is a specific category of wood manufacturing distinguished from furniture, cabinetry, and rough construction. It encompasses: commercial casework (store fixtures, display cases, checkout counters), architectural panels and wall systems, reception desks and lobby millwork, custom mouldings and trim, and hotel FF&E (furniture, fixtures, and equipment) [9].

### The AWI Standards Framework

The Architectural Woodwork Institute (AWI) sets the quality standards that all commercial millwork is built to. Three grades define the quality spectrum:

| Grade | Application | Tolerance | Finish Quality |
|-------|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Economy | Back-of-house, utility | Visible defects acceptable | Functional only |
| Custom | Standard commercial retail, hotel rooms | 1/32" to 1/16" | Consistent color and sheen |
| Premium | Flagship retail, luxury hotel lobbies | 1/64" to 1/32" | Museum-quality finish |

Starbucks and Alaska Airlines fixture contracts specify AWI Custom or Premium grade. The production model must be built to Custom at minimum, with Premium capability for high-end projects.

### The PNW Advantage

PNW-based architectural millwork manufacturers have specific advantages over out-of-region competitors:

- **Proximity to clients.** Starbucks (Seattle), Alaska Airlines (Seattle), hotel chains with major PNW portfolios -- the client base is local
- **Timber supply access.** Emerson Hardwood, OrePac, and Crosscut Hardwoods provide next-day delivery along the I-5 corridor
- **Workforce quality.** Boeing-adjacent precision culture produces workers who meet AWI grade requirements instinctively
- **Shipping costs.** Commercial millwork is bulky and fragile. Shipping from the PNW to PNW clients costs a fraction of shipping from the Southeast or Midwest
- **Relationship continuity.** A local manufacturer who will still be in Everett in ten years for the next renovation cycle has a fundamental advantage over a distant consolidator

## 9. The I-5 Corridor as Manufacturing Geography

Interstate 5 runs from the Canadian border through Everett, Seattle, Tacoma, Olympia, and Portland to the California border. For manufacturing logistics, it is the spine of the Pacific Northwest economy.

```
I-5 CORRIDOR -- MANUFACTURING GEOGRAPHY
================================================================

  BELLINGHAM ---- (timber mills, specialty woods)
       |
  EVERETT ------- NODE NORTH: CNC production, primary mfg
       |            Paine Field, Boeing-adjacent precision
       |            Sno-Isle TECH workforce pipeline
       |
  SEATTLE ------- Emerson Hardwood (Seattle), client HQ
       |            (Starbucks, Alaska Airlines)
       |
  SOUTH KING --- NODE CENTRAL: finishing, assembly, logistics
       |            SeaTac airport access, distribution hub
       |
  TACOMA -------- Port of Tacoma (import hardwoods)
       |
  OLYMPIA ------- NWCDC headquarters, co-op formation support
       |
  LONGVIEW ------ (timber country, sawmill corridor)
       |
  PORTLAND ------ NODE SOUTH: secondary production, hardwood supply
       |            Emerson Hardwood HQ (est. 1907)
       |            Crosscut Hardwoods
       |            OrePac Building Products
       |
  EUGENE -------- Crosscut Hardwoods (specialty species)
```

The three-node network topology maps to this corridor precisely. Each node occupies a geographic position that optimizes for a specific function within the production and delivery chain.

The recovery of Synsor's institutional knowledge is not a historical exercise -- it is a critical path item for the co-op's founding. The knowledge exists in dispersed form across the PNW. The co-op is the reconvening mechanism.

### Recovery Priorities

| Priority | Knowledge Domain | Recovery Source | Timeline |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | BOM engineering methodology | Former Synsor production/materials managers | Founding cohort recruitment |
| 2 | CNC programming libraries | Former Synsor CNC operators (note: programs themselves are proprietary; methodology is transferable) | Apprenticeship Phase 1 |
| 3 | Finish room formulas | Former Synsor finish room leads | Apprenticeship Phase 1 |
| 4 | Client relationship intelligence | Former Synsor sales/account managers; alumni in procurement roles | Client pipeline activation |
| 5 | Supply chain relationships | Former Synsor materials managers; direct outreach to Emerson, OrePac | Supply chain establishment |
| 6 | Quality inspection protocols | Former Synsor QC/QA personnel | Production startup |

### The Master Instructor Model

The most effective mechanism for institutional memory transfer is the master-apprentice relationship. The workforce pipeline module specifies recruiting 2-3 former Synsor senior production employees as founding master instructors. These individuals carry knowledge that no document can fully capture: the feel of a properly-cut dado joint, the sound of a CNC router running at correct feed rate, the visual assessment of a finish that will or will not meet AWI Custom grade.

This is generational knowledge transfer. It is the same mechanism by which the PNW timber industry transmitted craft knowledge for a century. The co-op formalizes it within a modern apprenticeship structure.
