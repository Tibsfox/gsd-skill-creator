## 1. The Client Acquisition Strategy

The co-op does not cold-call. The client acquisition strategy is built on three pillars:

1. **Alumni network.** Former Synsor employees who are now in purchasing, procurement, or fixture industry roles at potential anchor clients. These people understand the product, the quality level, and the production methodology because they learned it at Synsor.
2. **PNW proximity.** The co-op is local to its primary clients. Starbucks is headquartered in Seattle. Alaska Airlines is headquartered in Seattle. Every major hotel chain has a significant PNW property portfolio. Local supplier = lower shipping cost, faster delivery, on-site support.
3. **Cooperative ownership as value proposition.** The co-op's structure is a selling point, not just an internal governance choice. When a client signs a contract with a worker-owned cooperative, they are signing with an organization that cannot be acquired and relocated. The workers who build the fixtures today will be the same workers building them in ten years.

### Client Acquisition Funnel

```
CLIENT ACQUISITION FUNNEL
================================================================

  Stage 1: IDENTIFICATION
  |  Map potential clients: PNW-headquartered, commercial millwork needs
  |  Identify warm contacts: Synsor alumni in purchasing roles
  |
  Stage 2: WARM OUTREACH
  |  Personal contact through alumni network or industry relationship
  |  Not a cold pitch -- shared PNW history, Synsor lineage
  |
  Stage 3: CAPABILITY PRESENTATION
  |  Present co-op capabilities: AWI Custom/Premium, FSC-certified,
  |  BOM-first methodology, I-5 corridor production capacity
  |  Tour Node North production facility
  |
  Stage 4: SAMPLE PROJECT
  |  Small initial project to demonstrate quality and reliability
  |  5-10 units, AWI Custom grade, on-spec, on-time
  |
  Stage 5: ANCHOR CONTRACT
  |  Full rollout contract: 50-200+ units
  |  Multi-node production if volume requires
  |  Ongoing relationship established
```

## 2. Priority Target 1: Starbucks

Starbucks is the highest-priority anchor client target. The rationale is overwhelming:

### Why Starbucks

- **Volume.** Starbucks operates over 16,000 stores in the US alone and is in continuous buildout and renovation mode. Even a small slice of their regional casework demand represents significant volume.
- **Historical relationship.** Synsor was a confirmed Starbucks supplier. The relationship has precedent.
- **Alumni connection.** A former Synsor Materials Manager (1990-1994) served as Starbucks Casework Manager (1997-2004) and is now owner of International Millwork & Fixtures [1]. This person understands Starbucks' needs from the inside and Synsor's production methodology from the inside. They are the primary warm outreach target.
- **PNW headquarters.** Starbucks is headquartered in Seattle. A PNW-based supplier has logistics and relationship advantages.
- **Sustainability commitment.** Starbucks has active sustainability goals including FSC-certified materials and LEED-certified stores. The co-op's FSC certification and CARB-compliant finishes align directly.

### What Starbucks Needs

| Product | Specification | Quantity (Regional Rollout) |
|---------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Espresso bar casework | AWI Custom, cherry or maple veneer, FSC-certified | 50-200 per rollout |
| Service counter | AWI Custom, solid surface + millwork combination | 50-200 per rollout |
| Merchandising fixtures | AWI Custom, modular shelving systems | 100-500 per rollout |
| Drive-through speaker cabinets | AWI Economy/Custom, weather-resistant finish | 50-200 per rollout |

### Entry Point

The primary warm outreach target is the former Synsor Materials Manager / Starbucks Casework Manager who now runs International Millwork & Fixtures. The approach:

1. **Direct outreach** to this individual (LinkedIn or direct contact through industry networks)
2. **Message:** We are rebuilding the Synsor model under worker ownership. We want to discuss the current state of Starbucks casework procurement and whether there's an opportunity for a PNW-based, FSC-certified co-op to serve regional rollouts.
3. **Ask:** An introduction to the current Starbucks casework/fixture procurement team. Even if this person no longer has direct purchasing authority at Starbucks, they have the network and the credibility to open the door.

## 3. Priority Target 2: Alaska Airlines

### Why Alaska Airlines

- **PNW headquarters.** Alaska Airlines is headquartered in Seattle with primary hub operations at Seattle-Tacoma International Airport.

- **Precision requirements.** Aviation-adjacent millwork requires aerospace-adjacent precision. The Boeing-adjacent workforce at Node North understands this culture.

- **Synsor alumni.** Former Synsor employees who worked on Alaska Airlines projects may have contacts in Alaska's procurement organization.

## 4. Priority Target 3: Hotel Renovation Chains

### Why Hotel Chains

Hotel renovation (Property Improvement Plans, or PIPs) operates on predictable 7-10 year cycles mandated by the brand. Marriott, Hilton, IHG, and Hyatt all require their franchisees to renovate on schedule. This creates a steady, predictable demand for hotel millwork: lobby casework, reception desks, in-room furniture (headboards, nightstands, desks, closet systems), elevator cab millwork, and conference room built-ins [2].

### The FF&E Procurement Pathway

Hotel millwork is not purchased directly by the hotel chain. The procurement path is:

```
HOTEL FF&E PROCUREMENT PATHWAY
================================================================

  Hotel Brand (Marriott, Hilton, IHG)
  |  Issues PIP (Property Improvement Plan) to franchisee
  |
  Franchisee
  |  Hires interior design firm to design renovation
  |
  Interior Design Firm
  |  Creates specifications, selects materials, chooses AWI grade
  |  Issues FF&E specification package
  |
  FF&E Procurement Agent (or design firm directly)
  |  Solicits bids from millwork manufacturers
  |
  MILLWORK CO-OP (this is where we enter)
  |  Responds to FF&E bid with SOW response
  |  Wins bid on quality, price, delivery, FSC/LEED compliance
  |
  Production and Delivery
```

### Entry Point

The co-op enters the hotel pipeline through **PNW-based hospitality interior design firms**. These firms specify the millwork and control or influence the vendor selection. Target firms:

- Interior design firms with active PNW hotel renovation portfolios
- FF&E procurement firms operating in the Pacific Northwest
- Hotel property management companies overseeing PNW-region PIPs

The approach: get on the approved vendor list for 2-3 active PNW hospitality interior design firms. Once on the list, the co-op is eligible for bid invitations on every project those firms manage.

### What Hotels Need

| Product | Specification | Quantity per PIP |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Reception desk | AWI Premium, feature species, custom design | 1-2 per property |
| Lobby casework | AWI Custom/Premium, brand-standard finish | 10-20 units |
| In-room casework (headboard, desk, nightstand) | AWI Custom, identical across 200+ rooms | 200-500 units |
| Elevator cab millwork | AWI Custom, high-durability finish | 2-10 per property |
| Conference room built-ins | AWI Custom, AV integration provisions | 5-20 units |

The 200-room hotel renovation is the sweet spot for the co-op network. It requires enough volume to justify network production capacity but not so much volume that only mega-manufacturers can bid.

## 6. The Warm Outreach Method

The co-op does not cold-pitch. Every outreach is warm -- connected by shared PNW history, Synsor lineage, or an industry relationship.

### Principles

1. **Lead with shared history.** Reference the Synsor connection, the PNW manufacturing heritage, the I-5 corridor.
2. **Lead with capability.** AWI Custom/Premium, FSC-certified, BOM-first methodology, multi-node production capacity.
3. **Lead with structure.** Worker-owned cooperative. Acquisition-proof. The people building the fixtures today will be building them in ten years.
4. **Do not lead with price.** The co-op is not the cheapest option. It is the most reliable, most precise, most embedded option. Price comes after capability and relationship are established.
5. **Ask for a conversation, not a contract.** The first outreach asks for a meeting, not a purchase order. Build the relationship first.

## 8. SOW Response Protocol

When a client or design firm issues a Scope of Work for competitive bid, the co-op responds with a structured SOW response.

### SOW Response Template

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Acknowledgment** | Confirm receipt of SOW, project name, and document version |
| **BOM Review Timeline** | Specify number of business days to complete BOM review and return cost estimate (typically 5-10 days for standard projects) |
| **AWI Grade Commitment** | State the AWI grade the co-op will produce to (Custom or Premium, as specified in SOW) |
| **FSC/LEED Status** | Confirm FSC CoC certification number and expiry. Confirm CARB-compliant finish products |
| **Production Capacity** | Specify unit capacity per week, production node(s) assigned, and whether multi-node production applies |
| **Delivery Timeline** | Proposed delivery schedule from BOM approval to final delivery, including finish curing time |
| **Sample Unit** | Offer to produce a sample unit for approval before full production run (typically at cost) |
| **Warranty** | Standard warranty terms: 1 year on materials and finish, 5 years on structural integrity |
| **Cost Estimate** | Provide per-unit and total project cost estimate based on preliminary BOM review |
| **References** | Provide references from completed projects (once available; for first project, reference founding team's collective experience) |

## 9. Contract Intake Checklist

Before accepting any bid, the co-op's engineering team reviews the SOW against this checklist:

| Question | Required Answer | Gate Level |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| What AWI grade is required? | Economy, Custom, or Premium (documented) | GATE |
| Is FSC certification required? | If yes: confirm FSC CoC is current | GATE |
| Is LEED compliance required? | If yes: confirm CARB-compliant finishes and FSC materials | GATE |
| How many units? | Documented quantity | REQUIRED |
| What is the delivery date? | Confirmed, realistic given capacity | REQUIRED |
| Is installation required? | If yes: budget for install team and travel | REQUIRED |
| Is a sample unit required? | If yes: budget for sample production, approval timeline | REQUIRED |
| What species and materials? | All species available from primary or backup suppliers? | GATE |
| Is there a project architect? | If yes: coordinate BOM review with architect | ADVISORY |
| What is the payment structure? | 30% deposit / 40% at production / 30% at delivery (standard) | REQUIRED |

> **SAFETY WARNING:** No contract may be accepted before confirming that the co-op's FSC Chain of Custody certification is current and covers the materials required. Accepting a contract that requires FSC materials while the CoC is lapsed or expired is a certification violation.

## 10. Pricing and Competitive Position

### Pricing Philosophy

The co-op does not compete on lowest price. It competes on:

1. **Reliability.** On-spec, on-time, every time. The BOM-first methodology ensures cost accuracy and delivery predictability.
2. **Quality.** AWI Custom/Premium grade with documented inspection at every unit.
3. **Continuity.** Worker-owned, acquisition-proof. The client relationship is with an organization that will be here in ten years.
4. **Proximity.** I-5 corridor production. Lower shipping cost, faster delivery, on-site support capability.
5. **Certification.** FSC-certified, CARB-compliant, LEED-ready. No client has to worry about compliance gaps.

### Competitive Pricing Guidance

| Component | Target Range | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Material cost | 35-45% of unit price | BOM-calculated, verified at current supplier pricing |
| Labor cost | 25-35% of unit price | Based on production hours by operation |
| Overhead | 15-20% of unit price | Facility, equipment, insurance, administration |
| Federation overhead | 3-8% of unit price | Shared engineering, FSC cert, contract management |
| Margin | 8-15% of unit price | Retained for reserves, equipment, and patronage |

The co-op's total pricing should be competitive with regional millwork shops and 10-20% below national consolidators on I-5 corridor delivery (because shipping costs are dramatically lower).
