Weyerhaeuser & Sustainable Timber

From the 900,000-acre purchase to the working forest — timber, ecology, and the land between

On January 3, 1900, Friedrich Weyerhaeuser and fifteen associates purchased 900,000 acres of Pacific Northwest timberland from the Northern Pacific Railway for six dollars an acre. The timber on those acres was worth many times the purchase price. From that single transaction — the largest private land deal in American history at the time — grew the largest private timberland owner on Earth: 10.4 million acres, 100 million seedlings planted per year, a 125-year arc from extraction to the “working forest” model that claims production and ecology can coexist in the same stand of trees. Whether that claim holds is the question this research investigates. The spaces between the timber and the truth are where the story grows.

What Was Lost

Logging & Timber Wars

Fifteen million acres of ancient forest, reduced to five. The spotted owl listing, the Timber Wars, the Northwest Forest Plan. Mill town devastation, watershed damage, and salmon habitat destruction. The fault line that never healed.

The Working Forest

Sustainable Forestry

From extraction to management. SFI certification, 100 million seedlings per year, genetic improvement programs since the 1960s. The Clemons Tree Farm. The “working forest” concept: production AND ecology, not production OR ecology.

The Carbon Argument

Carbon & Climate

Forests as carbon sinks. The managed forest vs. old-growth carbon debate. Carbon credit markets, 2030 emissions targets, biomass energy. Douglas fir climate vulnerability — range shifting northward and upslope.

From Tree to Truss

Wood Products

The supply chain from seedling nursery to structural beam. TJI joists, Microllam LVL, TimberStrand LSL. The mass timber revolution: CLT buildings to 18 stories. 30–50% lower emissions than concrete and steel.

Land & Culture

The Stevens treaties, the Boldt Decision, tribal forestry programs. Environmental justice: who bears the costs of extraction? Cedar as cultural material. The forests don’t belong to anyone — but someone always claims them.
