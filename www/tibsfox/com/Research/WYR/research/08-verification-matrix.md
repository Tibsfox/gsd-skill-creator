## 7. The Through-Line

The Clemons Tree Farm was planted in 1941 because someone at Weyerhaeuser recognized that what you take, you must also grow back. The recognition came late -- after 40 years of extraction that destroyed 95% of old-growth on private lands. But it came. The tree farm model -- plant, grow, harvest, replant -- is the working forest in embryo: the idea that managed forests can produce timber indefinitely if you invest in the cycle.

Verification works the same way. You build something. You check it against what you promised. Where it falls short, you document the gap. Where it succeeds, you record the evidence. The Clemons Tree Farm was Weyerhaeuser's first verification -- the test of whether cutover land could grow timber again. It took 20 years to get the answer. The answer was yes. But the question that followed -- whether managed forests can replace what old-growth provided -- is still being answered, 85 years later.

This research project attempts to verify Weyerhaeuser's claims against available evidence, to document both achievements and shortfalls honestly, and to hold all the truths about these forests at once. The land was here before the company. The people were here before the land grants. The forests don't belong to anyone. But someone always claims them. What matters is how the claim is exercised -- with care or with appetite, with patience or with urgency, with the long view or the quarterly report.

The first tree farm proved that replanting works. The question is whether replanting is enough.
