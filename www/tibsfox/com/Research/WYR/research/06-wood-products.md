## 1. The Supply Chain

The supply chain runs from seed to structure, spanning decades between the first planting and the final installation [1][2][3]:

### 1.1 Seedling Nurseries

Genetically improved seedlings are grown in nurseries for 1-2 years before field planting. Each seedling is the product of decades of selective breeding -- chosen for growth rate, wood density, disease resistance, and site adaptability. Modern nurseries produce millions of seedlings annually, each tagged to its genetic family and origin site [1][2].

### 1.2 Planting

Over 100 million seedlings planted per year, by hand and machine. In the Pacific Northwest, planting crews work through winter and early spring rains, spacing seedlings at intervals calculated to optimize growth while allowing adequate sunlight and root space. In the South, machine planting is more common on the flat, accessible terrain of pine plantations [1][2].

### 1.3 Growth

40-60 years for Douglas fir rotation in the Pacific Northwest. 25-30 years for Southern pine. During this period, the trees are managed: thinning removes smaller, weaker trees to give remaining trees more light and resources; fertilization supplements soil nutrients; pruning removes lower branches to reduce knots in future lumber [1][2].

### 1.4 Harvest

Mechanized feller-bunchers, processors, and forwarders handle modern harvesting. A single feller-buncher can cut and stack hundreds of trees per day. Processors strip branches and cut logs to specified lengths at the stump. Forwarders carry logs from the harvest site to the landing. Modern harvesting is far more selective and controlled than historical clear-cutting, though clear-cutting (now called "regeneration harvest") is still used where appropriate for even-aged management [1][2][3].

### 1.5 Transport and Milling

Log trucks haul timber from harvest sites to sawmills. The era of river-rafting logs is largely past, though some water transport remains on major rivers. At the sawmill, computerized scanning systems analyze each log for diameter, length, taper, sweep, and defects before determining the optimal cutting pattern. The scanner produces a cut plan that maximizes the value of lumber extracted from each log [1][2].

### 1.6 Distribution

Finished lumber moves through Weyerhaeuser's distribution network -- one of the largest in North America -- to lumber yards, home centers, and contractors. The distribution network connects forest regions (where timber is produced) with construction markets (where it is consumed), managing inventory, pricing, and logistics across a continental supply chain [2][3].

## 2. Lumber Grades and Standards

Not all lumber is equal. Grading systems classify lumber by strength, appearance, and intended use [2][3]:

### 2.1 Structural Grades

Structural lumber is graded for strength -- the ability to support loads. Douglas fir is the premium structural species in North America, with strength grades (Select Structural, No. 1, No. 2, No. 3) based on the number and size of knots, grain slope, and other characteristics that affect load-bearing capacity. Each grade has specific design values for bending strength, tensile strength, and modulus of elasticity that engineers use in structural calculations [2][3].

### 2.2 Dimensional Standards

The "2x4" is not actually 2 inches by 4 inches. Nominal dimensions (2x4, 2x6, 2x10, 2x12) are larger than actual dimensions (1.5x3.5, 1.5x5.5, 1.5x9.25, 1.5x11.25) because lumber shrinks during kiln-drying. These standards are codified in American Lumber Standard Committee (ALSC) specifications and are consistent across the industry [3].

## 3. Engineered Wood Products

Weyerhaeuser's Trus Joist division produces some of the most widely used engineered wood products in North American construction [1][2][3][4]:

### 3.1 TJI Joists

The I-joist -- an engineered floor and roof framing member with a plywood or OSB web and solid-sawn or LVL flanges. Developed approximately 50 years ago, TJI joists represent a fundamental engineering advance: by concentrating material where stress is highest (the flanges) and using minimal material where stress is lowest (the web), the I-joist achieves greater strength-to-weight ratio than solid-sawn lumber. TJI joists are lighter, stronger, and more dimensionally stable than solid lumber. They resist warping and twisting, providing squeak-free floors. Available in multiple depth and strength grades (TJI 110, 210, 230, 360, 560) [4].

### 3.2 Microllam LVL (Laminated Veneer Lumber)

Thin veneers (approximately 1/8 inch thick) peeled from logs, dried, graded, and glued together with grain aligned under heat and pressure to form beams and headers. LVL can span greater distances than dimensional lumber and is more consistent in strength because its manufacturing process disperses natural defects (knots, grain variation) across many veneers rather than concentrating them as in a single piece of solid wood [4].

### 3.3 TimberStrand LSL (Laminated Strand Lumber)

Strands of wood approximately 12 inches long, oriented and bonded under heat and pressure into structural members for rim boards, headers, and columns. LSL can use smaller-diameter, faster-growing trees and wood species that are not suitable for solid-sawn lumber, making efficient use of the resource. Weyerhaeuser is investing $500 million in a new TimberStrand facility in southeast Arkansas [3][4].

### 3.4 Parallam PSL (Parallel Strand Lumber)

Long strands of veneer (up to 8 feet long) bonded under high pressure for heavy-load beams and columns. PSL has the highest strength of the engineered wood products and is used where extreme loads must be supported -- long-span beams, transfer beams, building columns. Its distinctive appearance (the visible strand pattern) makes it suitable for exposed structural applications [4].

## 4. The Mass Timber Revolution

The most significant development in wood construction since the invention of plywood [2][3][5][6]:

### 4.1 Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT)

Alternating layers of lumber glued crosswise into thick panels, analogous to plywood but at structural scale. CLT panels 3-12 inches thick serve as walls, floors, and roofs. The cross-lamination gives CLT dimensional stability and strength in both directions, allowing it to function as a load-bearing structural system. CLT can be manufactured from small-diameter, fast-growth trees -- the same plantation timber that Weyerhaeuser grows [5][6].

### 4.2 Glue-Laminated Timber (Glulam)

Layers of lumber glued together to form beams and columns of virtually any size and shape. Glulam beams can span distances that solid-sawn timber cannot, making them suitable for gymnasiums, bridges, and commercial structures. Glulam has been used in construction since the early 20th century but is experiencing renewed interest as part of the mass timber movement [5].

### 4.3 Code Changes

The 2021 International Building Code now allows mass timber buildings up to 270 feet -- roughly 18 stories. This represents a dramatic expansion from previous limits of 85 feet (6 stories) for wood construction. States including California, Oregon, Washington, and Utah have adopted or amended codes to permit taller mass timber structures. The code changes have opened the construction industry's tallest buildings to wood-based structural systems [5][6].

### 4.4 Market Projections

The global CLT market is projected to grow at 14.5% annually from $1.1 billion (2021) to $2.5 billion by 2027. Mass timber projects are projected to grow from roughly 100 in 2018 to nearly 25,000 by 2034. Buildings account for approximately 39% of global carbon emissions (28% operational, 11% embodied). Mass timber addresses the embodied carbon fraction by substituting wood for steel and concrete [5][6].

## 5. Embodied Carbon: Wood vs. Concrete vs. Steel

Peer-reviewed life cycle assessments consistently show that mass timber buildings produce fewer emissions than comparable structures in concrete or steel [5][6][7]:

### 5.1 The Data

- CLT buildings produce approximately **30% fewer CO2 emissions** than concrete structures
- CLT buildings produce approximately **50% fewer CO2 emissions** than steel structures
- A systematic review of 62 peer-reviewed articles found that on average, approximately **43% of GHG emissions are avoided** when reinforced concrete structures are substituted with mass timber alternatives
- Houses constructed with wood show **17-58% lower embodied energy** than steel-framed and **16-55% lower** than concrete structures [5][6][7]

### 5.2 The Adohi Hall Study

The U.S. Forest Products Laboratory studied Adohi Hall at the University of Arkansas -- a mass timber residence hall. The mass timber structure emitted 198 kg CO2 eq./m2 versus 243 kg for an equivalent steel structure, a 19% reduction. The building also stores approximately 2,757 tons of CO2 in its wood structure -- carbon that remains sequestered for the life of the building [7].

### 5.3 Construction Speed

Mass timber construction reduces build schedules by an average of 20% compared to concrete -- 12.7 months versus 15.4 months across real case studies. Prefabricated CLT panels arrive at the construction site precisely cut and labeled, requiring only assembly rather than on-site forming and curing. Faster construction means lower financing costs, earlier occupancy, and reduced environmental disruption at the construction site [5][6].

## 6. Tall Wood Buildings

Mass timber has enabled a new generation of tall wood buildings that challenge the dominance of concrete and steel in mid-rise and high-rise construction [5][6]:

### 6.1 Landmark Projects

- **T3 Minneapolis** (2016): 7 stories, one of the first modern mass timber office buildings in the United States. Named for "Timber, Transit, Technology"
- **Brock Commons Tallwood House, Vancouver** (2017): 18 stories, University of British Columbia student residence. For several years, the tallest mass timber building in the world. Hybrid structure with CLT floors and glulam columns over a concrete podium
- **Ascent, Milwaukee** (2022): 25 stories (19 stories of mass timber over a 6-story concrete podium). Currently the tallest mass timber building in the world. Residential tower demonstrating the viability of mass timber at significant height
- **Earth Systems Science Building, UBC**: Incorporates Weyerhaeuser's TimberStrand LSL in a hybrid timber-concrete floor system

### 6.2 Fire Performance

A common concern about tall wood buildings is fire safety. Mass timber performs differently from light-frame wood in fire: large timber members char on the surface, forming an insulating layer that protects the structural core. CLT panels maintain structural integrity for extended periods during fire exposure -- in some cases outperforming steel, which can lose strength rapidly at temperatures above 500 degrees Celsius. Mass timber buildings are designed to meet or exceed the fire resistance requirements of comparable concrete and steel structures [5][6].

## 7. The Housing Connection

Weyerhaeuser is, in a fundamental sense, the foundation of American homebuilding. The company's lumber and engineered wood products frame a significant portion of the roughly 1.5 million housing starts per year in the United States [1][2][3]:

### 7.1 Price Cycles

Timber pricing cycles track housing demand almost perfectly. When housing starts rise, lumber prices rise, and Weyerhaeuser's revenue follows. The 2020-2021 lumber price spike -- driven by pandemic-era housing demand combined with supply chain disruptions -- sent lumber futures to record highs (over $1,700 per thousand board feet, versus a historical average of approximately $400) and Weyerhaeuser's stock with them [2][3].

### 7.2 The Shelter Equation

Every house starts as a tree. The average American home contains approximately 16,000 board feet of lumber and engineered wood products. From foundation sill plate to roof ridge, wood provides the structural skeleton, the sheathing, the subfloor, the roof deck, and increasingly the insulation (via wood-fiber insulation products). Weyerhaeuser's supply chain connects the forest where the tree grew to the neighborhood where the family lives. The time bridge spans 40-60 years from seedling to structure [2][3].

## 8. Weyerhaeuser's Manufacturing Footprint

The company operates a network of sawmills, engineered wood products facilities, and distribution centers across North America [2][3][4]:

### 8.1 Wood Sourcing

Approximately 40% of manufacturing wood comes from Weyerhaeuser's own SFI-certified forests. 22% comes from other certified landowners. 38% comes from non-certified landowners, typically small family forests. All wood comes from legal, non-controversial, responsible sources [3].

### 8.2 Investments

The $500 million TimberStrand facility in southeast Arkansas represents Weyerhaeuser's largest single manufacturing investment. The facility will produce engineered wood products for the construction market, drawing on the company's Southern pine timberlands. Weyerhaeuser maintains partnerships with the University of Arkansas Fay Jones School of Architecture and Clemson University's Wood Utilization + Design Institute [3][4].
