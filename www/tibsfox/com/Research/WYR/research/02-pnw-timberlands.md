## 1. The Railroad Checkerboard

The geography of Weyerhaeuser's holdings cannot be understood without understanding the Northern Pacific land grant. In 1864, Congress chartered the Northern Pacific Railway and granted it every other section of land (one square mile, 640 acres) in a swath 40 miles wide on each side of the tracks through territories, and 20 miles wide through states. This created a "checkerboard" pattern of alternating private and public sections stretching from Lake Superior to Puget Sound -- nearly 40 million acres in total, the largest land grant any railroad received [1][2][3].

The grant was an act of continental ambition and sovereign presumption. The lands Congress gave away were not empty. They were inhabited by dozens of indigenous nations who had managed them for millennia. The treaty process of the 1850s -- conducted under territorial governors like Isaac Stevens -- converted indigenous homelands into "public domain" available for railroad grants. The checkerboard was stamped on top of a human geography that had existed for ten thousand years [1][3].

### 1.1 Why It Persists

When Weyerhaeuser bought 900,000 of those acres in 1900, he inherited this checkerboard. The pattern persists today: drive Interstate 90 through the central Cascades and you can see the result -- clear-cuts on private sections, standing timber on the adjacent Forest Service sections. The visual effect is jarring: a patchwork of harvest and preservation, private management and public stewardship, alternating across the mountainside like squares on a chessboard [1][2].

The checkerboard makes coordinated land management extraordinarily difficult. A watershed doesn't respect section lines. A spotted owl's territory spans multiple sections owned by different entities. Wildfire doesn't stop at property boundaries. Salmon spawn in streams that flow through alternating private and public land. Every management decision on one section affects the ecology of adjacent sections. The 1864 land grant created a pattern of fragmented ownership that foresters, biologists, and land managers have been struggling with for 160 years [2][3].

### 1.2 Consolidation Through Merger

Plum Creek Timber Company, the Northern Pacific's corporate grandchild, inherited much of the remaining checkerboard. When Weyerhaeuser merged with Plum Creek in 2016 for $8.4 billion, it consolidated much of this fragmented ownership under one entity -- reuniting timberland that had been separated for over a century, since Weyerhaeuser's original 1900 purchase from the railroad [2][4].

## 2. Where the Lands Are

After the Plum Creek merger, the combined company held approximately 13 million acres. Current U.S. holdings stand at approximately 10.4 million acres (the company has divested some holdings and optimized its portfolio), plus 14 million acres managed under long-term licenses in Canada. The portfolio breaks down roughly as follows [2][4][5]:

### 2.1 Pacific Northwest (Washington and Oregon)

Approximately 3 million acres of Douglas fir forest. This is the historical heart of the company -- the original 900,000-acre purchase plus a century of acquisitions. Key areas include the western Cascades, the Olympic Peninsula foothills, the Willapa Hills, and the Columbia River corridor. These are the most productive softwood timberlands in North America: Douglas fir grows faster and larger here than anywhere else on Earth, fed by 80-120 inches of annual rainfall on the west slope of the Cascades [2][4].

### 2.2 U.S. South

Approximately 7.3 million acres of Southern yellow pine (loblolly, slash, longleaf) across the Gulf states and Southeast -- Alabama, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, Virginia, Arkansas. This is now the company's largest timber region by acreage. Southern pine grows on shorter rotation cycles (25-30 years vs. 40-60 years for Douglas fir), producing more frequent harvests. Weyerhaeuser is investing $500 million in a new engineered wood products facility in southeast Arkansas [4][5].

### 2.3 Northern Region

Approximately 2.6 million acres of mixed hardwoods and conifers in Montana, Idaho, Michigan, Wisconsin, and the Northeast (principally Maine) -- largely inherited from Plum Creek. The Maine holdings are the site of Weyerhaeuser's first Improved Forest Management (IFM) carbon credit project, covering approximately 50,000 acres [2][4].

## 3. Douglas Fir: The Species That Built the Company

Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) is the species that built Weyerhaeuser and the Pacific Northwest. Named for Scottish botanist David Douglas, who documented it during expeditions to the Pacific Northwest in the 1820s, Douglas fir dominates the forests west of the Cascade crest from British Columbia to northern California [1][2][6].

### 3.1 Commercial Value

Douglas fir is the most commercially valuable timber species in North America. Its properties read like an engineering specification: strong (high modulus of elasticity), straight-grained, dimensionally stable, excellent for structural framing, resistant to warping. It takes fasteners well, machines cleanly, and accepts finishes evenly. For structural framing -- the 2x4s, 2x6s, 2x10s, and 2x12s that hold up American houses -- Douglas fir is the gold standard [2][6].

### 3.2 Biological Magnificence

Old-growth Douglas fir can live over 1,000 years and reach heights exceeding 300 feet with trunk diameters of 6 to 8 feet. The largest known Douglas fir, the Doerner Fir in Coos County, Oregon, stands at 327 feet. These ancient trees develop complex bark patterns -- thick, deeply furrowed, fire-resistant plates -- that distinguish them from younger specimens [2][6].

Old-growth Douglas fir forests are multi-layered ecosystems of extraordinary complexity: emergent crowns towering above the main canopy, a subcanopy of shade-tolerant western hemlock and western red cedar, an understory of vine maple and salal, a ground layer of sword fern and oxalis, and a forest floor of nurse logs, downed wood, and mycorrhizal networks connecting the root systems of living trees. These forests took centuries to develop and cannot be replicated on any human timescale [2][6].

### 3.3 Range and Climate

Douglas fir grows in two distinct varieties: the coastal variety (*var. menziesii*) west of the Cascades, which reaches the greatest size, and the interior variety (*var. glauca*, Rocky Mountain Douglas fir) east of the Cascades, which is smaller, slower-growing, and more cold-tolerant. The coastal variety thrives in the maritime climate of western Washington and Oregon -- cool, wet winters and warm, dry summers -- where it grows at extraordinary rates: a well-managed Douglas fir plantation can produce 200+ board feet per acre per year [6].

## 4. Old-Growth, Second-Growth, Plantation

The distinction between forest types matters enormously to understanding what Weyerhaeuser manages and what was lost [2][6][7]:

### 4.1 Old-Growth

Trees 200+ years old, with complex multi-story canopy structure, large-diameter trees (many exceeding 4 feet DBH), abundant standing dead trees (snags) and downed logs, deep understory, and rich mycorrhizal networks. Old-growth provides habitat for spotted owls, marbled murrelets, red tree voles, and hundreds of other species that require large, structurally complex forests. Old-growth Douglas fir forests store enormous amounts of carbon -- up to 1,000 metric tons per hectare in some stands. On private lands in western Washington and Oregon, approximately 95% of original old-growth has been logged. What remains is primarily on federal lands -- national forests and BLM lands [2][6][7].

Weyerhaeuser defines old-growth as stands that are: (1) 50+ acres; (2) have 50%+ dominant native tree species; and (3) are 200+ years old west of the Cascade crest or 250+ years old east of the crest. The company's policy states it will not harvest old-growth forests under this definition [5].

### 4.2 Second-Growth

Trees 60-120 years old, regrown naturally after initial logging. Simpler canopy structure than old-growth but gradually developing complexity. Second-growth forests are maturing across much of the PNW, particularly on federal lands where harvest restrictions since the 1990s have allowed recovery. These forests are intermediate between old-growth and plantation -- more structurally diverse than plantations but far less complex than the ancient forests they replaced [6].

### 4.3 Plantation Forestry

What Weyerhaeuser grows today. Even-aged monocultures of genetically improved Douglas fir (or Southern pine in the South), planted in rows on 40-60 year rotation cycles in the PNW (25-30 years in the South). Managed for maximum fiber yield: thinned, fertilized, sometimes pruned. These plantations bear little resemblance to the old-growth ecosystems they replaced -- they lack the structural complexity, the multi-layered canopy, the large snags and nurse logs, the centuries of accumulated organic matter. But they are forests, not parking lots, and they provide ecological services -- carbon sequestration, water filtration, wildlife habitat, soil stabilization -- that developed land does not [2][5].

## 5. The Cascade Range Timberlands

The Cascade Range is the spine of the Pacific Northwest, running from British Columbia to northern California. It divides the region into two fundamentally different climate zones: wet and mild to the west, dry and continental to the east. Weyerhaeuser holds land on both sides, but the west-slope Douglas fir forests are the crown jewels [2][6][8].

### 5.1 West Slope

West-slope Cascades receive 80-120 inches of rain annually. Douglas fir grows here at rates that foresters elsewhere can only envy. The combination of abundant moisture, deep volcanic soils, and moderate temperatures creates growing conditions unmatched anywhere on Earth for softwood timber. A Douglas fir planted on a good west-slope site can reach merchantable size in 40-50 years, producing lumber-grade logs of 12-18 inches in diameter [6][8].

The west-slope timberlands are where the checkerboard pattern is most visible. Interstate 90 between North Bend and Ellensburg crosses the Cascade crest through alternating sections of Weyerhaeuser land and national forest. The contrast between clear-cut private sections and standing timber on public sections is stark and politically charged [2].

### 5.2 East Slope

East of the Cascade crest, the climate shifts dramatically. Annual precipitation drops to 15-30 inches. The forest transitions from Douglas fir dominance to ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) and lodgepole pine (*Pinus contorta*) at lower and middle elevations. These drier forests grow more slowly, produce less timber volume per acre, and face different management challenges -- principally fire and bark beetle outbreaks. Weyerhaeuser's east-slope holdings, largely inherited from Plum Creek, are managed on longer rotation cycles with more emphasis on fire management [6][8].

## 6. The Olympic Peninsula

Some of the wettest forests in North America grow on the Olympic Peninsula. The Hoh Rainforest receives over 140 inches of rain per year. Sitka spruce and western hemlock reach enormous size in these temperate rainforests, draped in epiphytic mosses and lichens. Weyerhaeuser's holdings on the Olympic Peninsula foothills -- not the National Park itself, which is protected -- include some of the most productive timberland in the world [2][6].

The Olympic Peninsula forests are significant for several reasons beyond timber production. They represent some of the most intact temperate rainforest remaining in the lower 48 states. They are habitat for Roosevelt elk, marbled murrelets, and northern spotted owls. And they are the headwaters of salmon-bearing rivers that flow to the Pacific -- rivers whose health depends on forest management practices upstream [6][9].

## 7. The Columbia River Corridor

The Columbia River was historically the highway for timber transport. Logs were rafted down tributaries and the main stem to mills at Longview, Vancouver, and Portland. The river connected the interior forests to Pacific shipping routes and Asian export markets. Longview, Washington, was essentially built as a timber town -- its Weyerhaeuser mill was one of the largest in the world [1][2].

The Columbia corridor remains central to the timber economy. Log trucks, rail, and barge transport move timber from harvest sites to mills and distribution centers along the river. The Longview mill, though now focused on lumber rather than the pulp operations of earlier decades, continues to process Douglas fir and other species from Weyerhaeuser timberlands [2][10].

## 8. Peer Companies and the Industry Landscape

Weyerhaeuser does not operate in isolation. The PNW timber industry includes several major landholders [2][4]:

### 8.1 Plum Creek Timber (Merged 2016)

Descended from the Northern Pacific Railway's timberland holdings. Held approximately 6.8 million acres before the Weyerhaeuser merger. Pioneered the timber REIT structure in 1999. The merger created the world's largest private timberland owner [2][4].

### 8.2 Longview Fibre Company

Founded in Longview, Washington, to use waste wood from nearby sawmills for paper production. Accumulated 525,000 acres of timberland in Oregon and Washington by the early 1990s. Operated a sustained-yield forest management program since 1951. Reflected the shift toward recycled fiber as environmental pressures reduced virgin wood supply [2].

### 8.3 Georgia-Pacific

Atlanta-based but with major PNW operations. One of the world's largest manufacturers of tissue, pulp, packaging, and building products. Owned by Koch Industries since 2005 (taken private). Operates mills across Oregon and Washington [2].

### 8.4 PotlatchDeltic

Founded in 1903 by investors including Friedrich Weyerhaeuser himself, who had seen Idaho timberland at the 1893 Chicago World's Fair. Originally operated in Idaho and was disadvantaged when the 1914 Panama Canal opening allowed Pacific Coast timber to reach eastern markets by ship at one-third the rail cost. In October 2025, PotlatchDeltic and Rayonier agreed to a merger of equals creating a $7.1 billion forestry company with roughly 4.2 million acres -- second only to Weyerhaeuser [2][4].

### 8.5 Rayonier

Based in Jacksonville, Florida, Rayonier manages approximately 2.7 million acres of timberland in the U.S. South, Pacific Northwest, and New Zealand. The company's PNW holdings in western Washington and Oregon include some of the most productive Douglas fir timberland in the region. The pending PotlatchDeltic-Rayonier merger would create a combined entity with geographic diversification across all major U.S. timber regions [2][4].

### 8.6 The Timber REIT Landscape

The timber REIT structure -- pioneered by Plum Creek in 1999 and adopted by Weyerhaeuser in 2010 -- has reshaped the industry. Timber REITs comprised approximately 7.5% of total public REIT market capitalization at the time of Weyerhaeuser's conversion. The structure aligns timber's long-term biological growth (trees grow regardless of market conditions) with patient capital seeking inflation hedges and dividend income. The PotlatchDeltic-Rayonier merger and Weyerhaeuser's continued portfolio optimization suggest that consolidation in the timber REIT sector will continue, concentrating more timberland under fewer, larger owners [2][4].
