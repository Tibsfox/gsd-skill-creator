Mission Pack

The original research brief and mission package that initiated the Weyerhaeuser & Sustainable Timber research project. This document provides the foundational research from which all eight modules were developed, covering the complete arc of Weyerhaeuser’s 125-year history from Friedrich Weyerhaeuser’s $6/acre land purchase to the modern working forest model, sustainable forestry certification, carbon credit markets, mass timber construction, and the cultural sovereignty questions that underlie all timber operations in the Pacific Northwest.

Source Documents

The mission pack source material comprises a comprehensive 1,014-line TeX mission document (6 modules covering vision, research reference, mission specification, wave execution plan, test plan, and source bibliography) and a 366-line deep research document covering company history, PNW geography, logging impact, sustainable practices, wood products, economics, environmental context, and connections to the broader research series.

Research Scope

  • Founding & Dynasty: Friedrich Weyerhaeuser (1834–1914), the 1900 Northern Pacific purchase of 900,000 acres at $6/acre, four generations of family leadership, George Weyerhaeuser’s 1935 kidnapping, REIT conversion (2010), Plum Creek merger ($8.4B, 2016)
  • PNW Timberlands: Railroad checkerboard from 1864 land grant, Douglas fir as commercial cornerstone, old-growth vs. plantation forestry, 10.4M US acres + 14M Canadian acres, Cascade Range and Olympic Peninsula holdings
  • Logging & Timber Wars: 1900–1970s extraction era, 15M acres of old-growth reduced to ~5M, spotted owl ESA listing (1990), Earth First! direct action, Northwest Forest Plan (1994), mill town economic devastation
  • Sustainable Forestry: SFI certification (140+ indicators), 100M+ seedlings/year, genetic improvement programs, Clemons Tree Farm (1941), the “working forest” concept, riparian buffers, wildlife management
  • Carbon & Climate: 2.3–3.6B metric tons sequestered, $100M carbon credit pipeline, IFM methodology, biomass energy, Douglas fir climate vulnerability, managed vs. old-growth carbon debate
  • Wood Products: TJI joists, Microllam LVL, TimberStrand LSL, Parallam PSL, mass timber (CLT to 18 stories), 30–50% lower emissions than concrete/steel, $500M Arkansas EWP facility
  • Land & Culture: Stevens treaties (1854–55), Boldt Decision (1974), tribal forestry, environmental justice, cedar as cultural material, the Evergreen State identity
  • 40+ Sources: USDA Forest Service, GAO, Weyerhaeuser SEC filings, SFI, FSC, American Carbon Registry, Carbon Direct, HistoryLink.org, Nature Conservancy, peer-reviewed LCA studies

Key Findings

Weyerhaeuser sits at the intersection of nearly every PNW research module in this project. The company’s footprint IS the PNW landscape — from the Columbia Valley rainforests it logged first to the Cascade Range timberlands it still manages, from the salmon rivers its practices altered to the mass timber products reshaping construction. The “working forest” concept is the Rosetta Stone of this project: a single idea that translates between extraction and conservation, proving that both can exist in the same stand of trees. Whether that translation is faithful or merely fluent is the question this research pursues. The answer matters for the forests, the communities, and anyone who believes that sustainable resource management is possible at industrial scale.
