Embodied Capability Modules (Qin et al., 2026)

The Learning Without Losing Identity paper introduces Embodied Capability Modules (ECMs) — modular, versioned units of functionality that can be learned, refined, and composed over time, with the defining architectural claim that capability evolution is decoupled from agent identity. A runtime layer enforces invariant safety envelopes independently of the ECMs; gated deployment checks block unsafe new versions before they can take effect.

Two empirical findings do the load-bearing work:

  • 11.8% of episodes produce unsafe actions without policy enforcement — this is the counterfactual baseline.
  • The runtime layer blocks 100% of unsafe actions at 2.3ms overhead per decision — this is the cost-of-safety upper bound.

Why it matters for gsd-skill-creator

The ECM pattern is the Amiga Principle in formal clothes. Where gsd-skill-creator has been articulating, informally, that persistent agent identity should remain stable while capabilities evolve separately (the chipset / Instruction-Set-Architecture split), Qin et al. state the claim with empirical backing. The 2.3ms upper bound on safety-layer overhead is the number our Safety Warden enforcement should aim to beat.

The pattern tightens the pedagogical Teacher/Student/Support decomposition planned for v1.50 Half-B proof companion: Teacher is the identity layer, Student is the ECM pool, Support mediates the loading and safety-envelope enforcement.

Relation to the Four-Tier Trust Framework

ECMs and the four-tier trust framework (T1-T4, from Jiang et al.'s survey) compose naturally: each tier transition in the trust framework is a gate decision about whether an ECM may be promoted into that deployment tier. T1 ECMs (first-party, audited) promote freely; T4 ECMs (third-party, unaudited) remain sandboxed until audit evidence accumulates.
