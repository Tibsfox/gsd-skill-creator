1. Quantum Error Correction Threshold

Open PCS QBS

Challenge: Achieve fault-tolerant quantum computation with error rates below the threshold theorem requirement, enabling arbitrary-length quantum algorithms.

Current State of the Art

  • Surface codes: threshold ~1% physical error rate; current best qubits achieve ~0.1–0.5%
  • Google Willow (2024): demonstrated error rate reduction with increasing code distance (first scaling evidence)
  • IBM Heron: 1,121 qubits with improved error rates, but still above threshold for useful algorithms
  • Trapped ions (IonQ, Quantinuum): highest gate fidelity (~99.9%) but lowest qubit counts

Key Metrics

  • Target: physical error rate < 10-3 with >1,000 logical qubits
  • Current gap: need ~1,000x more physical qubits per logical qubit for surface codes
  • Alternative approaches: topological qubits (Microsoft), bosonic codes, LDPC codes

2. Room-Temperature Superconductivity

Open ENE HPC

Challenge: Achieve superconductivity at ambient temperature and pressure, enabling lossless power transmission, levitation, and revolutionary computing.

Current State of the Art

  • High-pressure hydrides: LaH10 superconducts at 250K but requires 170 GPa pressure
  • LK-99 (2023): room-temperature claims retracted; sample was not superconducting
  • Cuprate record: HgBa2Ca2Cu3O8 at 133K (ambient pressure), 164K under pressure
  • MgB2: conventional BCS superconductor at 39K; widely used in commercial MRI

Why It Matters

  • Power grid losses: 5–10% of generated electricity lost in transmission
  • Computing: superconducting interconnects would eliminate data movement energy (60–90% of system energy)
  • Fusion: high-field superconducting magnets are the enabling technology for compact fusion

3. Dark Matter Detection

Open OPEN BPS

Challenge: Directly detect dark matter particles, confirming the particle nature of the 85% of matter that has only been observed gravitationally.

Current State of the Art

  • Direct detection: LZ (LUX-ZEPLIN) and XENONnT — world-leading sensitivity, no confirmed signal
  • Indirect detection: Fermi LAT gamma-ray observations of dwarf galaxies — no excess above background
  • Collider production: LHC searches for missing energy signatures — no evidence for WIMP production
  • Axion searches: ADMX reaching sensitivity to QCD axion parameter space

Open Questions

  • Is dark matter a single particle species or a dark sector with multiple components?
  • Have we been looking in the wrong mass range? (Shift from WIMP to lighter candidates)
  • Could modified gravity (MOND) explain observations without new particles?

4. Fusion Energy — Net Positive Q>1 Sustained

Open ENE THE

Challenge: Achieve sustained fusion energy with net energy gain (Q > 1 measured at the wall plug, not just plasma gain).

Current State of the Art

  • NIF (2022): achieved Qplasma > 1 (3.15 MJ out from 2.05 MJ laser input) — but Qwall-plug ~0.01
  • ITER: under construction in France; target Qplasma = 10 by mid-2030s
  • Private sector: Commonwealth Fusion (SPARC), TAE Technologies, Helion Energy — all targeting 2030s
  • sCO2 turbines: 48–50% thermal efficiency would apply to fusion heat extraction

Key Metrics

  • Target: Qwall-plug > 1 with sustained burn (>1 hour)
  • ITER target: Qplasma = 10, which translates to Qwall-plug ~1–2
  • Commercial viability: Qwall-plug > 5 likely needed for economic competitiveness

5. Carbon Capture at Gigaton Scale

Open GPE ENE

Challenge: Remove CO2 from the atmosphere at gigatons-per-year scale with net negative energy and economically sustainable costs.

Current State of the Art

  • DAC (Direct Air Capture): Climeworks Orca/Mammoth plants — ~36,000 tons/year total capacity
  • Cost: $400–600/ton CO2 for DAC; target <$100/ton for gigatonne scale
  • BECCS: bioenergy with carbon capture — scalable but competes with food production
  • Ocean alkalinity: enhanced weathering could sequester gigatonnes but ecosystem impacts unknown

Scale Gap

  • Current DAC: ~0.000036 Gt/year
  • IPCC target: 6–10 Gt/year removal by 2050
  • Gap: ~200,000x current capacity (comparable to scaling early aviation to modern air traffic)

6. Battery Energy Density Theoretical Limits

Open ENE HGE

Challenge: Approach the theoretical energy density limits of electrochemical storage while maintaining cycle life, safety, and cost targets.

Current State of the Art

  • Li-ion (NMC811): ~250–300 Wh/kg practical; ~400 Wh/kg theoretical
  • Solid-state (lithium metal anode): ~400–500 Wh/kg theoretical; Samsung/QuantumScape prototypes
  • Li-S: ~600 Wh/kg theoretical; degradation and polysulfide shuttle unsolved
  • Li-air: ~3,500 Wh/kg theoretical; orders of magnitude from practical

Key Metrics

  • Gasoline: ~12,700 Wh/kg (but ~25% efficient = ~3,175 Wh/kg effective)
  • Current practical gap: batteries at ~250 Wh/kg vs gasoline effective ~3,175 Wh/kg = ~13x
  • Target for aviation: >800 Wh/kg (would enable regional electric aircraft)

7. Photonic Computing Practical Integration

Open HPC ENE

Challenge: Integrate photonic computing elements into practical systems, replacing electronic interconnects and potentially compute elements with light.

Current State of the Art

  • WDM micro-resonator comb lasers: generate hundreds of wavelengths from a single source (AGT research)
  • Silicon photonics: Intel, TSMC integrating optical interconnects on chip packages
  • Lightmatter: photonic AI accelerator using Mach-Zehnder interferometers for matrix multiplication
  • Memory bottleneck: 50%+ CPU time waiting for memory; photonic interconnects could reduce latency

Open Questions

  • Can photonic matrix multiplication achieve the precision needed for training (not just inference)?
  • What is the minimum component count for a practical photonic computer?
  • Can we build optical memory that is competitive with SRAM/DRAM?
  • Energy: 60–90% of system energy is data movement — photonic interconnects could cut this dramatically
