The Pollinator Bed

A complete garden design — 20+ species, February through October, zero bloom gaps
Feed the Bees Windowsill Container Full Calendar Research

This is the full design: a 4×8 foot (or larger) pollinator garden bed with 20+ species providing continuous bloom from February through October. Native-first, with non-native gap-fillers where needed. Designed for PNW conditions at 47–48°N, USDA Zone 8a–8b.

Design Principles

1. Native first. Native plants support the widest array of native pollinators. Non-natives fill gaps only.
2. Bloom succession. At least 3 species flowering in every month, March through October. See the full calendar.
3. Plant in clumps. 3–5 of the same species together. Isolated singles are hard for pollinators to find (Xerces Society).
4. Layers. Tall shrubs in back, medium perennials in middle, groundcovers in front. Vertical diversity = more habitat.
5. Nesting habitat. Leave bare soil patches for ground-nesters. Leave dead stems standing through winter for cavity-nesters.
6. No pesticides. Zero. None. A diverse garden has natural pest control.
7. Water source. Shallow dish with pebbles. Bees need water. Change daily.

The 4-Layer Design

Back Layer: Shrubs (5–15 ft)

Red flowering currant (Ribes sanguineum) — Mar–Apr. Critical early food for bumble bee queens.
Saskatoon serviceberry (Amelanchier alnifolia) — Apr–May. Mason bees, bumble bees. Edible berries.
Mock orange (Philadelphus lewisii) — May–Jun. WA state flower. Fragrant.
Oceanspray (Holodiscus discolor) — Jun–Jul. Also provides pithy nesting stems.

Middle Layer: Perennials (1–4 ft)

Common camas (Camassia quamash) — Apr–May. Historically critical PNW species.
Broadleaf lupine (Lupinus latifolius) — May–Jul. Buzz-pollinated. Nitrogen-fixer.
Cascade penstemon (Penstemon serrulatus) — Jun–Aug. Bumble bees + hummingbirds.
Blanketflower (Gaillardia aristata) — Jun–Sep. Long bloom, easy from seed.
Canada goldenrod (Solidago canadensis) — Aug–Oct. Critical pre-hibernation food.
Douglas aster (Symphyotrichum subspicatum) — Aug–Oct. #1 for bees (OSU).
Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) — Jun–Aug. Exceptional nectar. Non-native.
Purple coneflower (Echinacea purpurea) — Jun–Sep. Goldfinch seeds. Non-native.
Agastache (Agastache spp.) — Jul–Sep. Bridges the July gap. Non-native.

Front Layer: Low Perennials & Groundcovers (under 1 ft)

Kinnikinnick (Arctostaphylos uva-ursi) — Mar–May. Early bees. Evergreen groundcover.
Wild strawberry (Fragaria virginiana) — Apr–Jun. Small bees. Edible.
Oregon sunshine (Eriophyllum lanatum) — May–Jul. 20+ bee genera documented.
Self-heal (Prunella vulgaris) — Jun–Sep. Native PNW. Purple flowers.
Sedum (Sedum spectabile) — Aug–frost. Last flowers standing. Non-native.
Hardy geranium ‘Rozanne’ — May–frost. Longest bloom of any perennial. Insurance plant.

Edge & Habitat Layer

Bare soil patch (2×2 ft, south-facing) — For ground-nesting bees (70% of species).
Dead wood or stem bundle — Leave standing through winter. Cavity-nesting bees overwinter inside.
Shallow water dish with pebbles — Hydration station. Change water daily.
Mason bee house (optional) — East/southeast facing, 3–5 ft high, near early flowers.

Site Selection

  • Full sun minimum: 6+ hours direct sun. Most of these plants need it. South or west-facing is ideal.
  • Drainage: Well-drained soil. Amend clay with compost. Raised beds solve drainage problems.
  • Size: Minimum 4×8 feet for the full design. Scale up by adding more clumps of each species. Scale down by choosing fewer species per layer (minimum: 1 shrub, 3 perennials, 2 groundcovers).
  • Proximity to house: Close enough to watch from a window. You’ll want to see the bees.

Planting Timeline

WhenWhat
Fall (Oct–Nov)Plant shrubs and bulbs (camas). Best time for root establishment before winter.
JanuaryBegin cold stratification for native seeds (see seed starting guide).
March–AprilPlant perennials from nursery pots. Transplant indoor-started seedlings after hardening off.
MayFill remaining gaps with nursery plants or direct-sown seeds. Mulch 2–3 inches (but leave bare soil patches).
Year 2+Self-seeding species fill in. Divide and share perennials. The garden matures and requires less work each year.
Year 1 cost estimate: Shrubs ($8–15 each × 4 = $32–60). Perennials ($4–7 each × 9 = $36–63). Groundcovers ($3–5 each × 6 = $18–30). Soil amendment ($20–40). Total: $106–193. Much less if you start from seed ($30–50 for the whole bed). The garden is an investment that appreciates: it gets better every year, self-seeds, and produces plants to share.
