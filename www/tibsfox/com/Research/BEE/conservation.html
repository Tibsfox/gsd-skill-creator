Colony Collapse Disorder

CCD is defined by the EPA as the phenomenon where the majority of worker bees disappear from a colony, leaving behind a queen, food, and immature bees. First identified in winter 2006–2007 when beekeepers reported 30–90% hive losses.

2025 USDA-ARS Breakthrough

Dr. Judy Chen at the USDA Bee Research Laboratory (Beltsville, MD) identified viruses from amitraz-resistant Varroa mites as the proximate cause of recent mass colony collapses. Deformed wing virus A/B and acute bee paralysis virus were found in all sampled bees. Amitraz resistance detected in “virtually all collected Varroa” from collapsed colonies — amitraz being the critical miticide most US beekeepers depend on.

Impact: Over 60% of commercial beekeeping colonies lost since prior summer. 1.7 million colonies affected. Estimated financial impact: $600 million.

Source: USDA ARS Research News, June 2, 2025 · USDA NASS Honey Bee Colonies Report, August 1, 2025

What’s Working: Grassroots Programs

Bee City USA (Xerces Society)

Founded 2012. 300+ affiliate cities, counties, colleges. Requirements: standing pollinator committee, native plant lists, integrated pest management plan, community awareness events. Certifies communities committed to pollinator conservation.

beecityusa.org · 270+ renewing affiliates (2024)

Million Pollinator Garden Challenge

Launched June 2015 by the National Pollinator Garden Network. Achieved 1,040,000 gardens registered by December 2018 — exceeding the million-garden goal. Partners: NWF, Pollinator Partnership, American Seed Trade Association, dozens of conservation orgs.

Challenge concluded 2021 · 1M+ gardens planted

Bee Better Certified (Xerces Society)

First third-party certification focused on pollinator conservation (2017). Tens of thousands of certified acres. Minimum 5% of farmland dedicated to pollinator habitat. Products with the seal at Kroger, Trader Joe’s, Costco. Silk (Danone) committed 100% almond acreage to Bee Better practices by 2025.

beebettercertified.org · Retail presence at major chains

Pollinator Partnership

World’s largest nonprofit dedicated exclusively to pollinator health. Programs: ecoregional planting guides (region-specific native plants), National Pollinator Week (third week of June, US Senate designation), NAPPC tri-national initiative, direct grants for conservation projects.

pollinator.org · Ecoregional planting guides for every US zip code

Citizen Science: How You Can Contribute Data

The Great Sunflower Project

Founded 2008 by Dr. Gretchen LeBuhn (SFSU). Over 100,000 members — the largest pollinator-focused citizen science project. Count pollinators visiting plants in your garden. Three focus areas: neonicotinoid effects, pollinator-friendly plants by region, habitat quality evaluation.

greatsunflower.org · 100K+ participants · US & Canada

Bumble Bee Watch

Operated by Xerces Society + University of Ottawa + partners. ~75,000 observations across 49 states. Photograph bumble bees and upload for expert identification. 2024: 7,800+ sightings. Companion Bumble Bee Atlas program deploys trained community scientists for systematic surveys.

bumblebeewatch.org · 75K+ observations · 49 states

iNaturalist Bee Surveys

Global platform (California Academy of Sciences / National Geographic). 2024 NE US data: 31,953 pollinator observations, 1,136 species, 3,126 observers. Backyard Bumble Bee Count: 444 research-grade observations including 24 of the federally endangered rusty patched bumble bee. Vermont: 2,500+ scientists documenting 350+ wild bee species.

inaturalist.org · Research-grade data cited in peer-reviewed papers

Policy Context

European Union — Neonicotinoid Ban

The most comprehensive pollinator protection policy globally. Full outdoor ban on three neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam) effective May 2018. Thiacloprid withdrawn 2020. European Court of Justice closed emergency authorization loophole January 2023. EFSA confirmed “high acute risk” to bees (February 2018 assessment).

United States — Pollinator Health Task Force

Established June 2014 by Presidential Memorandum. Three goals: reduce overwinter losses to 15% (by ~2025), increase monarch population to 225M (by 2020), restore 7M acres of habitat (by ~2020). EPA proposed cancellation of imidacloprid residential turf spray uses (2020).

What You Can Do Right Now

  1. Plant native flowers. Even one pot on a windowsill. Start here.
  2. Stop using pesticides on your lawn and garden. Especially neonicotinoids.
  3. Leave bare soil patches for ground-nesting bees (70% of native species).
  4. Leave dead stems and wood standing through winter for cavity-nesting bees.
  5. Join a citizen science program. Your observations become data that drives policy.
  6. Buy Bee Better Certified products when available. Market demand drives farm practice.
  7. Convert lawn to bee lawn. Clover, self-heal, creeping thyme. Guide here.
  8. Talk to your neighbors. Habitat works at the neighborhood scale. One garden is a node; a block of gardens is a corridor.
