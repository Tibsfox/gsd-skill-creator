The Container Patio

Three-season pollinator garden on a 4-foot balcony — under $50
Feed the Bees Windowsill Garden Bed Bloom Calendar Research

Three containers. Three seasons. Continuous bloom from April through October on a balcony, patio, or stoop. The Xerces Society confirms that container gardens provide meaningful pollinator habitat when designed with bloom succession in mind.

The Three-Container Plan

Container 1: Spring (March–May)

Oregon Iris (Iris tenax) — 12-inch pot, full sun. Native. Bumble bees.
Western Columbine (Aquilegia formosa) — 10-inch pot, part shade okay. Native. Bumble bees, hummingbirds.
Wild Strawberry (Fragaria virginiana) — wide pot or window box. Trails over edge. Small bees. Edible fruit bonus.
Bloom: March–June · All native · Feeds early-season bees when they need it most

Container 2: Summer (June–August)

Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) — 12-inch pot, full sun, well-drained. Exceptional nectar.
Cascade Penstemon (Penstemon serrulatus) — 10-inch pot, full sun. Native. Bumble bees, hummingbirds.
Catmint (Nepeta spp.) — 12-inch pot, full sun. Deadhead for rebloom. May–September.
Bloom: May–September · Bridges the July gap · Deadhead catmint for continuous flowers

Container 3: Fall (August–Frost)

Sedum / Stonecrop (Sedum spectabile) — 10-inch pot, full sun. Drought-tolerant. Blooms through frost.
Douglas Aster (Symphyotrichum subspicatum) — 12-inch pot, sun to part shade. Native. #1 for bees (OSU).
Hardy Geranium ‘Rozanne’ — 12-inch pot. Blooms May through hard frost. Insurance plant.
Bloom: August–November · Critical pre-hibernation food for bumble bee queens

Setup Tips

  • Drainage: Every pot needs holes in the bottom. Elevate on pot feet or bricks so water flows freely.
  • Group containers together. Clumped flowers are easier for pollinators to find than scattered singles. Three pots in a cluster is a destination, not a drive-by.
  • Add a water source. Shallow dish with pebbles and water. Bees land on the pebbles and drink from the edges. Change water daily (mosquito prevention).
  • Leave a bare soil patch in your largest container. Some solitary bees will nest in it.
  • No pesticides. Ever. On anything. A container garden thrives without them.
  • Overwintering: In PNW (Zone 8a–8b), most of these plants survive in containers year-round. In rare hard-freeze events, cluster pots together against a wall for insulation.
Bonus container: The Herb Garden
Basil, mint, and lavender in a single large pot. Let them flower. Xerces Society specifically recommends herb containers as pollinator habitat. The blooms are among the most visited by bees. You eat the leaves; the bees eat the flowers. Everyone wins.
The math: If 10 balconies in one apartment building each have 3 containers, that’s 30 containers with 90 pollinator plants covering 8 months of bloom. That building is a vertical pollinator corridor. No one planned it. Everyone just put out three pots.
