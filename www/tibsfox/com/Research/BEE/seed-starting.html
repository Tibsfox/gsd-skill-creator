Indoor Seed Starting

From cold stratification to transplant — turn your kitchen into a native plant nursery
Feed the Bees Bloom Calendar Native Bees Research

Many PNW native wildflowers need a period of cold-moist conditions to break seed dormancy — simulating winter. Your refrigerator does this perfectly. Start stratification in January, sow under lights in February–March, transplant outdoors in April–May. By summer, you have a pollinator garden grown from seed for a fraction of nursery prices.

Cold Stratification Guide

Place seeds in moist sand or peat in a sealed bag in the refrigerator (35–40°F / 2–4°C). Check weekly for mold. This simulates the winter dormancy period that tells seeds it’s safe to germinate.

Timeline: November to May

November–December

Order seeds from native plant nurseries (Inside Passage Seeds, Native Seeds Inc., Fourth Corner Nurseries). Begin fall-sown species outdoors (camas bulbs, lupine). Research your frost dates.

January

Begin cold stratification for milkweed, penstemon, camas, aster, echinacea. Place sealed bags in refrigerator. Label with start date and species. Check weekly.

February

Start columbine and Oregon sunshine stratification. Sow blanketflower and lavender indoors under lights (no stratification needed). Set up grow light station.

March

Sow stratified seeds under lights. Start lupine (scarified) and echinacea. Maintain 60–70°F. Mist to keep evenly moist. Germination 14–30 days after stratification ends.

April

Begin hardening off: gradually acclimate seedlings over 7–10 days. Start with a few hours in sheltered shade, slowly increasing sun exposure. Transplant cold-hardy species outdoors after mid-April (Puget Sound).

May

Transplant remaining seedlings after last frost. Direct-sow warm-season annuals. Your garden is planted. First blooms expected in 6–8 weeks.

Equipment

Basic Indoor Seed Starting Setup

  • Seed trays or cell packs — 72-cell flats work well for small native seeds ($3–5)
  • Sterile seed-starting mix — NOT garden soil. Light, well-draining, pathogen-free ($5–8)
  • Humidity dome or clear plastic wrap — maintains moisture during germination ($2–4)
  • Grow lights — 14–16 hours/day, 2–4 inches above seedlings. LED shop lights work fine ($15–30)
  • Heat mat — 65–75°F for germination. Remove after emergence for most natives ($15–25)
  • Spray bottle — for misting. Never pour water on tiny seedlings ($1–2)
  • 4-inch pots — for transplanting when first true leaves appear ($5 for 20)

Total setup cost: $46–74. Reusable year after year. One setup grows hundreds of plants.

Method for Stratified Seeds

  1. Begin stratification in refrigerator in January (sealed bag, moist medium)
  2. After stratification period, sow in moist seed-starting mix under lights
  3. Cover very lightly or press onto surface — many native seeds need light to germinate
  4. Maintain 60–70°F; mist to keep evenly moist. Never let mix dry out completely.
  5. Germination typically 14–30 days after stratification ends
  6. Transplant to 4-inch pots when first true leaves appear (not the seed leaves)
  7. Harden off 7–10 days before transplanting outdoors
Hardening off (OSU Extension): Before transplanting outdoors, gradually acclimate seedlings over 7–10 days. Start with a few hours in sheltered shade, slowly increasing sun exposure and outdoor time each day. This reduces transplant shock and dramatically improves survival rates. Skip this step and you’ll lose plants to sunscald and temperature shock.
Cost comparison: A 4-inch nursery pot of native wildflowers costs $4–8. A seed packet with 50–200 seeds costs $3–5. Your $50 seed-starting setup pays for itself in the first season if you grow more than 10 plants.
