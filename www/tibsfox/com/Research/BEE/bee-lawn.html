The Bee Lawn

Replace grass with flowers — 111 bee species found in reduced-mow lawns
Feed the Bees Bloom Calendar Windowsill Research

A traditional lawn is a pollinator desert — no flowers, no food. A bee lawn replaces some or all of the grass with low-growing flowering plants that tolerate foot traffic and mowing. The result: a green surface that looks like a lawn but feeds bees from spring through fall.

Research finding: University of Minnesota studies documented 111 bee species in suburban lawns managed with reduced mowing frequency. The key is simply letting things flower.

How to Convert Your Lawn

1. Core aerate in early fall (September) or early spring (March)

Rent a core aerator or hire a lawn service for one pass. This creates holes in the soil for seed-to-soil contact. Skip this step and the seeds just sit on top of the grass and fail.

2. Overseed with bee lawn mix

Spread at 2–3 oz per 1,000 sq ft. Use a broadcast spreader for even coverage. Mix tiny seeds (clover, self-heal) with sand for more even distribution. Water lightly after seeding.

3. Stop herbicides immediately

Broadleaf herbicides (2,4-D, dicamba) kill clover and self-heal — they’re the “weeds” these products target. Stop all herbicide use permanently. The flowering plants ARE the lawn now.

4. Reduce mowing to every 2–3 weeks

Set mower height to 3–4 inches (highest setting on most mowers). Mow less frequently to let flowers bloom between cuts. The UMN research that found 111 bee species used a reduced-mow protocol.

5. Stop fertilizing (or reduce dramatically)

Clover is a nitrogen-fixer — it fertilizes itself and the surrounding grass. Heavy nitrogen fertilizer favors grass over flowers. If you must fertilize, use a low-nitrogen organic option once in fall.

Season 2Clover and self-heal spread to fill gaps. Lawn is visibly mixed — green with white and purple flowers throughout.
Season 3+Self-sustaining. Clover self-seeds. Self-heal spreads by runners. The bee lawn maintains itself.

What to Expect

A bee lawn does not look like a golf course. It looks like a meadow at ankle height. There will be clover flowers, tiny purple self-heal spikes, and English daisies mixed through the grass. Some neighbors may think it’s “messy.” The bees think it’s a buffet.

You can convert your entire lawn or start with a test patch. A 10×10 foot section is enough to see results within one season. If it works (it will), expand the next year.

Cost: A bag of Dutch white clover seed (1 lb, covers 4,000 sq ft) costs $8–15. Self-heal seed packets: $3–5. Core aeration rental: $40–60 for a half day. Total for a typical suburban lawn: under $75. Annual savings from reduced mowing, fertilizing, and watering: potentially more than the conversion cost in the first year.
