Bloom Succession Calendar

Continuous nectar and pollen from February through October — zero gaps
Feed the Bees Native Bees Seed Starting Conservation Research

The goal: at least 3 species flowering in every month from March through October. No single plant blooms continuously — different bee species emerge throughout the season, and each visits different flowers. Plant for every window. Overlap is intentional.

July — The Gap Risk

The “July gap” is a known PNW challenge. Many spring bloomers are done, fall bloomers haven’t started. Plant deliberately for this window.
Warning: This is where most gardens fail. If you only plant spring favorites, your bees starve in July. The plants below are specifically selected to bridge this gap.

Key principle from OSU Extension: “No single plant blooms from April through September continuously. Different bee species emerge throughout the growing season, so a bee flying in July visits different flowers than one flying in April.” Plant for every window. Overlap is intentional and beneficial.
OSU Research Finding: Native straight species attract significantly more pollinators than cultivated varieties (cultivars). Douglas aster (Symphyotrichum subspicatum) ranked #1 among all native plants tested for total bee visitation in OSU Garden Ecology Lab research. Source: OSU Extension EM-9363.
