PNW Native Bee Guide

600 species across 6 families — the pollinators in your backyard
Feed the Bees Bloom Calendar Seed Starting Conservation Research

Washington State hosts approximately 600 native bee species across 6 families (WSU Extension). Most are solitary — no hive, no queen, no honey. They nest in the ground or in cavities, emerge in spring, pollinate your garden, and disappear by fall. They’re more efficient pollinators than honey bees for most native plants, and they’re declining.

The Six Families

Halictidae — Sweat Bees

The most specimen-abundant family in Puget Sound surveys. Ground-nesting, small-bodied. Lasioglossum is the most species-rich genus in the region (21+ species in marginal lands alone). Peak activity mid-summer. Often metallic green or bronze. Attracted to human perspiration (hence the name).

Apidae — Bumble Bees, Long-horned Bees, Cuckoo Bees

Includes all Bombus species — the only native social bees in the PNW. Queens overwinter alone, found new colonies in spring. Melissodes (long-horned bees) specialize on Asteraceae and peak in late summer. Some species are cleptoparasitic (“cuckoo bees”) — they lay eggs in other bees’ nests.

Megachilidae — Mason Bees & Leafcutter Bees

200+ PNW species. Cavity-nesters that use mud (mason bees) or cut leaf pieces (leafcutter bees) for nest construction. Osmia lignaria (blue orchard bee) pollinates ~95% of flowers visited — far more efficient than honey bees. Mason bee houses are a simple way to support this family.

Andrenidae — Mining Bees

Solitary ground-nesters. Andrena is ubiquitous and diverse across the region. Multiple new county records documented through Washington Bee Atlas surveys (2025–2026). Often the first bees to emerge in spring. Create small mounds of excavated soil around nest entrances.

Colletidae — Plasterer Bees & Yellow-faced Bees

~33 PNW species. Hylaeus (yellow-faced bees) are tiny and often mistaken for wasps. Nest in pre-existing cavities in stems and wood. Produce a cellophane-like lining inside their nest cells — the “plaster” of their common name.

Melittidae — Oil-collecting Bees

Small family with specialist species. Uncommon in the PNW but present. Collect floral oils rather than (or in addition to) nectar and pollen.

Nesting: Where Bees Live

Ground-Nesting (~70% of all native bee species)

Most native bees nest underground. They excavate tunnels 6–36+ inches deep in bare, well-drained soil. South-facing slopes are preferred for warmth. Mowing, tilling, and compaction destroy nests. The simplest thing you can do: leave patches of bare, undisturbed soil in your garden. Sandy or loamy soils are ideal.

Cavity-Nesting (~30%)

These bees nest in beetle galleries in dead wood, hollow stems, and other pre-existing cavities. Leave dead wood and plant stems standing through winter — they’re full of overwintering bees. Key PNW pithy-stemmed plants for nesting: elderberry, oceanspray, mock orange, thimbleberry, snowberry, Pacific ninebark, roses, and raspberry/blackberry canes.

Mason bee houses work — but only if you maintain them. Clean tubes annually (or use replaceable paper liners) to prevent disease buildup. Place houses facing east/southeast, 3–5 feet off the ground, near early-blooming flowers. Osmia lignaria is active March–June.

Species of Conservation Concern

Western Bumble Bee

Bombus occidentalis
>90% decline

Largely disappeared from western WA, OR, and CA since late 1990s. Viable populations persist east of the Cascades and in Alaska. Collapse likely driven by pathogen spillover from commercially reared bumble bees (Nosema bombi).

Rusty Patched Bumble Bee

Bombus affinis
ESA Endangered

First bumble bee listed under the Endangered Species Act (2017). Lost from 87% of historic range. IUCN Critically Endangered. Once common across eastern North America. 24 observations recorded in 2024 Backyard Bumble Bee Count — every sighting matters.

Franklin’s Bumble Bee

Bombus franklini
ESA Endangered — possibly extinct

Not observed in the wild since 2006. Most restricted range of any North American bumble bee (~60 mi wide in Siskiyou Mountains, S. Oregon / N. California). ESA Endangered listing 2021.

Suckley Cuckoo Bumble Bee

Bombus suckleyi
Declining — CA ESA candidate

Obligate cleptoparasite of B. occidentalis. As the host species collapsed, so did the parasite. Declines mirror host species exactly — a cascade effect through the ecological network.

American Bumble Bee

Bombus pensylvanicus
Under ESA review

IUCN Vulnerable. Positive 90-day finding from USFWS (September 2021) — full ESA review in progress. Historic range-wide decline documented. Once one of the most common bumble bees in North America.

The Convergence Zone as Habitat

Sugden et al. (2025) documented 118 bee species in just 3 marginal urban sites in the Puget Sound region — significantly more than diversified farms. Power line corridors and airport perimeters function as unexpected biodiversity hotspots: regularly disturbed, open, sun-exposed, with bare ground for nesting.

The mild maritime climate (47–48°N) extends the active season from February through October — longer than most temperate zones. But heavy urbanization, invasive species (Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry, English ivy), and 90%+ loss of native prairie habitat since European settlement make every garden a critical habitat fragment.

WSU 2024 climate research: Warmer falls cause excessive worker bee foraging, shortening lifespans. By 2050, outdoor bumble bee colonies in eastern WA projected to drop below 9,000 adults (collapse threshold). By 2100, below 5,000 adults. Mitigation: cold storage October–April preserves colony populations above 15,000. Lead researchers: Kirti Rajagopalan (WSU), Gloria DeGrandi-Hoffman (USDA), Brandon Hopkins (WSU).
