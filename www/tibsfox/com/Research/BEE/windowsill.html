The Windowsill Garden

Feed the bees from your kitchen window — under $20, zero experience needed

You don’t need a yard. You don’t need experience. You need a sunny window, a pot, and three plants. This guide gets you from zero to feeding bees in one trip to the garden center.

Steps

Fill the pot

Fill your pot with potting mix to about 1 inch below the rim. Moisten the mix until water drains from the bottom hole. Let it drain completely.

Plant the trio

Lavender in the back (tallest). Thyme in front (lowest, will cascade over the edge). Basil on the side. Dig holes the same depth as the nursery pots, place the plants, and firm the soil around them. Water gently.

Place in your sunniest window

South-facing is ideal. West-facing works. East-facing is okay. North-facing won’t work — these plants need at least 6 hours of direct sun. If you don’t have a sunny window, put the pot on a balcony or stoop.

Water when dry

Stick your finger in the soil. If the top inch is dry, water thoroughly until it drains from the bottom. In PNW summer, that’s every 2–3 days. In spring, maybe once a week. Never let the pot sit in standing water.

Let the basil flower

This is the key step most people get wrong. Do NOT pinch off the basil flowers. Yes, the leaves get slightly bitter after flowering. That’s fine — you’re feeding bees now, not just making pesto. The white flower spikes are covered in bees within days of opening. You can still harvest leaves from the lower stems.

Watch and enjoy

Within 1–2 weeks of the first flowers opening, you’ll see visitors. Bumble bees on the lavender. Small native bees on the thyme. Honey bees on the basil flowers. You’re feeding the network.

What Happens Next

Timeline:
  • Week 1: Plants settle in. Water as needed. No flowers yet if transplanted from nursery.
  • Week 2–3: Lavender sends up flower spikes. Thyme begins to bloom. Basil grows rapidly.
  • Week 4: First bees arrive. You’ll hear them before you see them.
  • Month 2–3: Lavender at peak bloom. Basil flowering. Thyme covered in tiny flowers. Your windowsill is a pollinator station.
  • Fall: Lavender and thyme are perennial — they’ll come back next year. Basil is annual — replace it in spring, or let it self-seed.

Level Up

Once you’re comfortable with the windowsill trio, try these next steps:

  • Add a second pot with native Oregon grape (Mahonia aquifolium) for early spring blooms before the lavender starts
  • Add a shallow water dish with pebbles — bees need water too. Change it daily to prevent mosquitoes.

Why This Works

One pot with three plants seems small. It is small. But pollinators navigate by scent, and lavender is detectable from remarkable distances. A single flowering lavender plant on a third-floor balcony will attract bees that were foraging on street trees below. Your windowsill is a beacon.

And here’s the math that matters: if 10 apartments in one building each put out a flowering pot, that building becomes a pollinator corridor. If every block has a building like that, the neighborhood has continuous habitat. No one planned it. No one coordinated it. Everyone just planted a pot. This is how distributed systems work.
