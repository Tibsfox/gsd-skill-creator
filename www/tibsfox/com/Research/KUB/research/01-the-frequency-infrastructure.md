# The Frequency -- Infrastructure

> **Module:** 1 -- The Frequency (1964--2026)

But the infrastructure never changed: 93.3 MHz, Cougar Mountain, 100,000 watts, Olympia to Mount Vernon. A frequency is a commons. The signal carries whatever is placed upon it.*

## 1. Sign-On: KBLE-FM (May 6, 1964)

On May 6, 1964, radio station KBLE-FM signed on the air at 93.3 MHz in Seattle, Washington. The station was owned by Eastside Broadcasting, which also operated AM sister station KNBX at 1050 AM. The initial format was Christian radio programming, broadcast at 6,600 watts effective radiated power (ERP). The transmitter location was in Seattle proper, with limited coverage of the metropolitan area [1][2].

The call letters KBLE had already been associated with the AM station. The FM launch was part of a broader trend in the 1960s where AM operators added FM frequencies as inexpensive simulcasts or format complements. FM radio in 1964 was still a secondary medium -- most listeners consumed radio through AM, and FM receivers were not standard in automobiles. The decision to launch an FM station with Christian programming reflected the economics of the moment: religious formats were inexpensive to program and generated reliable revenue from church sponsorships and listener donations [1].

Eastside Broadcasting's decision to pair a Christian FM with a country AM was typical of small-market radio operators hedging across demographics. Neither format required expensive talent or complex production. Both depended on loyal, habitual listeners rather than competitive ratings performance. The two stations shared infrastructure, studios, and likely engineering staff, keeping overhead costs manageable for a small broadcaster operating in a major market where larger competitors dominated the commercial landscape [2].

The 6,600-watt power level was modest even by 1964 standards. Major-market FM stations in the early 1960s were already operating at tens of thousands of watts. The 6,600-watt allocation reflected KBLE-FM's status as a small-market operation within a large market -- a station that served a niche audience within the Seattle radio landscape rather than competing for mass-market attention. The signal covered portions of Seattle proper but did not reach the suburbs, exurbs, or outlying communities that would later fall within the station's coverage after power increases and transmitter relocation [1].

Nobody listening to hymns and sermons on 93.3 FM in 1964 could have imagined that within thirty years, the same frequency would be broadcasting Sir Mix-A-Lot's "Baby Got Back" to every car stereo from Olympia to Mount Vernon.

## 2. Eastside Broadcasting and the AM-FM Swap

Within a few years of the FM launch, formats swapped between the two Eastside Broadcasting stations. KBLE-FM shifted to country programming while the AM took on the KBLE call sign and carried religious content. This swap reflected a pragmatic assessment of audience habits: country music listeners in the mid-1960s Seattle market were more likely to be FM-curious early adopters, while religious listeners -- often older, more habitual -- remained committed to AM dial positions they already knew [1][2].

Country music on FM also offered better audio quality -- the genre's acoustic instruments and vocal harmonics benefited from FM's wider frequency response and stereo capability.

The AM-FM format swap was a common small-market strategy. FM frequencies were still undervalued in the 1960s, and operators experimented with format placement to find configurations that maximized total revenue across both frequencies. The underlying economics were straightforward: country music attracted different advertisers than religious programming, and separating the two formats across AM and FM allowed the station group to sell two distinct audience demographics without cannibalization [2].

This period of the station's history is sparsely documented. The Eastside Broadcasting operation was a modest enterprise -- two stations in a competitive market, neither commanding significant audience share or industry attention. The company's contribution to the story of 93.3 FM is foundational but quiet: they secured the frequency, maintained the license, and operated the transmitter infrastructure that would later be inherited by operators with larger ambitions and deeper pockets.

## 3. The Cougar Mountain Transmitter

The transmitter for 93.3 FM was relocated to Cougar Mountain in the Issaquah Alps, east of Seattle. The Issaquah Alps are a series of forested ridges between Lake Washington and the Cascade foothills, reaching elevations between 1,000 and 1,600 feet -- modest by Cascade Range standards but substantial relative to the Puget Sound lowland that surrounds them. The move represented a fundamental upgrade in the station's signal architecture. Cougar Mountain rises to approximately 1,595 feet above sea level, providing significantly better line-of-sight coverage across the Puget Sound basin than any transmitter site within Seattle proper [1][2].

Cougar Mountain is one of several transmitter sites used by Seattle-area FM and television stations, selected for its combination of elevation, proximity to the metropolitan core, and terrain that allows signals to propagate both north-south along the I-5 corridor and west across Puget Sound. The site's elevation advantage is compounded by the relatively flat terrain to the north (toward Everett and Mount Vernon) and south (toward Tacoma and Olympia), creating a coverage footprint that extends well beyond the Seattle metro area [1].

The transmitter site would become one of the most significant elements of the KUBE story. When iHeartMedia moved the KUBE format to 104.9 FM in 2016, the replacement signal operated from a different transmitter location with substantially less coverage. The Cougar Mountain site's 100,000-watt signal reached from Olympia to Mount Vernon -- essentially the entire I-5 corridor through western Washington. The 104.9 signal was described as a "rimshot" targeting Tacoma, unable to reach much of Seattle or the Eastside. The infrastructure -- the transmitter, the mountain, the power level -- was the difference between a regional cultural institution and a marginal broadcast [1][2].

The Cougar Mountain transmitter site demonstrates a principle that recurs throughout radio history: geography is architecture. The physical location of a transmitter, combined with its power level and antenna height, determines not just how many people can hear a signal but which communities can participate in the culture that forms around it. The transmitter on the mountain is not a technical detail. It is the foundation of everything that follows.

## 4. Power Increases: 6,600 to 20,000 Watts

The station's power was increased from the original 6,600 watts to 20,000 watts ERP, likely during the Cougar Mountain transmitter relocation or shortly thereafter. The power increase expanded the station's coverage from a limited Seattle footprint to a more substantial regional signal [1].

A jump from 6,600 to 20,000 watts is approximately a tripling of effective radiated power, but due to the logarithmic nature of radio propagation, the coverage area increase was less dramatic than the numbers suggest. The more significant factor was the transmitter relocation to Cougar Mountain, which improved the signal's Height Above Average Terrain (HAAT) -- the single most important variable in FM coverage after power level itself. A 20,000-watt signal from Cougar Mountain covered substantially more area than a 6,600-watt signal from a Seattle rooftop, not primarily because of the power increase but because of the superior antenna elevation [1][2].

The 20,000-watt era coincided with the station's period under Ostrander-Wilson ownership. The religious format remained, but the improved signal suggested an operator with long-term investment intent. Power increases require FCC approval, engineering studies, and capital expenditure for new transmitter equipment. An operator planning to sell or abandon a frequency does not invest in power upgrades.

## 5. Ostrander-Wilson and the Religious Return

In the 1970s, Ostrander-Wilson acquired both stations and returned the religious format to FM. The AM station continued under the KBLE call letters. This ownership change and format reversion returned 93.3 FM to its original Christian programming identity after the mid-1960s country experiment [1].

Ostrander-Wilson's tenure is notable primarily for maintaining the license and infrastructure through a period when FM radio was transitioning from secondary medium to primary. By the late 1970s, FM listenership was surpassing AM nationally, driven by FM's superior audio quality and the growth of album-oriented rock formats that demanded high-fidelity transmission. Religious formats on FM were increasingly anachronistic in major markets -- not because the audience disappeared, but because the opportunity cost of programming religion on a high-power FM frequency rose as FM's commercial potential became clear [2].

By the early 1980s, KBLE-FM was carrying religious programming including Dave Ballard's "Reflections" program. The station was a modest operation in a market where FM competitors were commanding significant ratings and advertising revenue with music formats. The 93.3 frequency, with its Cougar Mountain transmitter and 20,000-watt signal, was commercially underperforming relative to its technical capability -- a situation that attracted the attention of buyers who saw the gap between the station's signal footprint and its revenue generation [1][2].

## 6. First Media and the Top 40 Flip

On March 17, 1981, First Media Corporation purchased KBLE-FM and immediately flipped the format from religious to automated Top 40 programming. The station was identified on-air as "The New 93" while First Media built new studios and assembled a live air staff. The religious programming that had occupied 93.3 FM for seventeen years disappeared overnight, replaced by pop music and the promise of mass-market commercial radio [1][2].

The First Media acquisition was a textbook FM flip of the early 1980s. A well-capitalized broadcaster acquires an underperforming FM frequency in a major market, strips the low-revenue format, and installs a high-revenue format that leverages the signal's technical reach. The speed of the flip -- religious to Top 40 in a single day -- reflects the economics: there was no audience to transition gradually, because the incoming format targeted an entirely different demographic than the outgoing one [1].

First Media used an automated system during the transition period, playing continuous Top 40 music while studios were prepared for live operations. Automation in 1981 meant cart machines loaded with songs on an electronic timer -- functional but lacking the personality and local engagement that live DJs provided. The automation was a bridge, not a destination. First Media's plan required live talent to compete in the Seattle Top 40 market, where established stations had strong morning shows and recognizable voices [2].

The purchase price and transaction details are not well-documented in available sources. What is documented is the intent: First Media saw 93.3 FM as a frequency with significant technical infrastructure (Cougar Mountain, 20,000+ watts) that was dramatically underperforming its revenue potential. The religious format generated modest income from a loyal but small audience. A Top 40 format on the same signal could compete for the mass-market advertising dollars that funded the Seattle radio industry's top stations.

## 7. The New 93: July 8, 1981

On July 8, 1981, at 5:04 PM, "The New 93" launched with live DJs, replacing the automated Top 40 placeholder that had been running since March. The station made an aggressive marketing promise: $10,000 to anyone who caught them playing fewer than 51 minutes of music per hour. The guarantee was a competitive weapon -- positioning The New 93 as the station that played more music and talked less, a pitch aimed at listeners frustrated with competitors' extended commercial breaks and DJ chatter [1][2].

General Manager Michael O'Shea oversaw the launch. The $10,000 guarantee was characteristic of the promotional tactics that aggressive FM operators used in the early 1980s to generate attention in crowded markets. The claim itself was difficult to verify from a listener's perspective -- timing exactly 51 minutes of music within a clock hour required a stopwatch and dedication -- but the marketing value was in the positioning, not the enforcement. "We play more music" was a message that resonated with FM listeners who were choosing between stations based on perceived entertainment density [1].

The live DJ launch marked the real beginning of 93.3 FM's commercial era. The automated period had served as a placeholder, establishing the frequency in listeners' presets while infrastructure was built. With live talent on the air, the station could develop personality-driven programming, participate in local community events, and build the listener relationships that sustained ratings over time [2].

The morning team that launched with The New 93 included Charlie Brown (formerly of KJR, Seattle's long-dominant AM Top 40 station), Ty Flint (formerly of KVI), and Mary White. Hiring Charlie Brown was a strategic raid on KJR's talent pool -- bringing a recognized voice from the most famous station in Seattle radio history to a new FM competitor signaled serious competitive intent [1].

## 8. KUBE Call Letters: April 2, 1982

On April 2, 1982, the station changed its call letters from KBLE-FM to KUBE, rebranding as "KUBE 93 FM." The KUBE call letters would become one of the most recognized radio brands in Pacific Northwest broadcasting over the next four decades [1][2].

The call letter change was both practical and symbolic. KBLE was associated with the station's religious era and carried no brand equity with the Top 40 audience that First Media was targeting. KUBE was a fresh identity -- memorable, distinctive, and free from any association with the station's previous format. The "KUBE" name also worked phonetically with promotional slogans and jingles, important considerations in an era when radio brands were primarily audio identities heard through speakers, not visual brands seen on screens [1].

The timing of the call letter change -- approximately one year after the format flip and nine months after the live launch -- suggests a deliberate sequencing: establish the format, build initial audience, then lock in the brand identity. This is a standard practice in radio rebranding. Changing call letters too early risks confusing listeners who have not yet found the station. Changing too late risks the old call letters becoming embedded in industry databases and advertising contracts [2].

## 9. Class C1: The 100,000-Watt Signal

Power was eventually increased to approximately 98,000 to 100,000 watts ERP, making KUBE a Class C1 FM station -- one of the most powerful FM signals in the Seattle market. The Class C1 designation reflects maximum permissible power for the station's assigned frequency, transmitter height, and geographic zone under FCC regulations [1][2].

The difference between 20,000 watts and 100,000 watts is substantial. In practical terms, a fivefold power increase extends reliable coverage range by roughly 50 percent or more, depending on terrain and atmospheric conditions. From the Cougar Mountain transmitter, a 100,000-watt signal reaches from Olympia (approximately 60 miles south) to Mount Vernon (approximately 60 miles north), covering the entire western Washington I-5 corridor. This coverage footprint encompasses Seattle, Tacoma, Everett, Bellevue, Olympia, and dozens of smaller communities -- a combined population of several million people [1].

The 100,000-watt signal from Cougar Mountain would become the defining infrastructure asset of the KUBE brand. When the format was moved to 104.9 FM in 2016, the loss of the Cougar Mountain signal -- not just the format change itself -- was the critical damage. The 104.9 signal operated from a lower-power transmitter targeting Tacoma, unable to reach much of Seattle proper or the Eastside communities that had been part of KUBE's audience for decades. The signal was the commons. When the signal changed, the community lost access [2].

## 10. Audio Processing: The Blue Optimod Chain

First-person accounts from station engineers on RadioDiscussions.com provide rare technical detail about KUBE's audio processing chain in the early years. The station used a Blue Optimod 8100 audio processor -- called "blue" because it had a blue faceplate, a result of being purchased through Broadcast Electronics rather than directly from Orban. The distinction was cosmetic, not functional, but it became part of the station's engineering lore [3].

The main audio chain included a CRL audio processor and a Harris stereo generator. The CRL unit incorporated a pop/click filter designed to remove surface noise that was introduced when records were dubbed onto broadcast cartridges (carts). Cart dubbing was standard practice: stations transferred music from vinyl records to NAB broadcast cartridges for on-air playback, and the transfer process could introduce pops, clicks, and surface noise that degraded the on-air sound [3].

The pop/click filter became technically problematic when hip-hop entered the rotation in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Hip-hop production frequently used intentional record scratching, sampling from vinyl, and percussive elements that the filter interpreted as defects to be removed. The filter had to be bypassed to accurately reproduce hip-hop recordings -- a small technical adaptation that symbolized a larger cultural transition. Equipment designed for Top 40 pop and rock had to be reconfigured for a genre whose sonic characteristics included the very "defects" the equipment was built to eliminate [3].

This engineering detail captures something that format histories typically miss: the transition from one musical genre to another is not just a programming decision. It requires physical modifications to the signal chain. The technology reflects assumptions about what music sounds like, and when those assumptions change, the technology must adapt. The Blue Optimod and its pop/click filter were designed for a world of clean vocals and polished production. Hip-hop brought samples, scratches, and bass frequencies that challenged every link in the chain.

## 11. Coverage Geography: Olympia to Mount Vernon

The 100,000-watt signal from Cougar Mountain created a coverage footprint that extended from Olympia in the south to Mount Vernon in the north -- approximately 120 miles of the western Washington I-5 corridor. This coverage area shaped which communities could participate in the culture that formed around KUBE's programming [1][2].

The geography of KUBE's signal is inseparable from the geography of Pacific Northwest hip-hop. The Central District in Seattle was the scene's geographic anchor, but the audience extended through Tacoma (home to Joint Base Lewis-McChord, where military personnel provided early hip-hop fan base infrastructure), Everett, Bellevue, and dozens of smaller communities along the I-5 corridor. When Nasty Nes played hip-hop on KUBE, the signal reached not just the CD but the entire region. A teenager in Olympia heard the same broadcast as a teenager in Mount Vernon. The frequency was a shared space -- a commons that connected communities separated by sixty miles of interstate highway [1].

The coverage geography also defined KUBE's competitive landscape. Competing stations with weaker signals or less favorable transmitter locations could not match KUBE's reach. KPLZ (Star 101.5), KQMV (Movin' 92.5), and later Hot 103.7 all operated in the Seattle market, but none matched the combination of power and transmitter location that gave KUBE its geographic dominance. The mountain was the multiplier [1][2].

When iHeartMedia moved KUBE's format to 104.9 FM in January 2016, the coverage loss was immediate and devastating. The 104.9 signal was described as a "rimshot" -- industry jargon for a signal that targets the fringe of a market rather than the center. Much of Seattle and the entire Eastside lost access. The format was the same; the infrastructure was not. The audience fragmented because the signal no longer reached them. Infrastructure is culture [2].

## 12. HD Radio Operations

KUBE added HD Radio capability in approximately 2006, gaining the ability to broadcast sub-channels alongside the main analog signal. HD Radio is a hybrid system -- the main channel broadcasts in both analog FM and digital simultaneously, while HD2, HD3, and additional sub-channels are digital-only [1][2].

The HD2 sub-channel would become significant in KUBE's later history as a format exile destination. When the main 93.3 signal was reprogrammed, KUBE's hip-hop format was repeatedly relegated to the HD2 channel -- a digital signal that requires either an HD Radio receiver (a relatively uncommon consumer device) or access through the iHeartRadio streaming app. The HD2 channel has cycled through formats: "KUBE 2" hip-hop (2006), "WiLD" rhythmic (2009), "Kids Club Radio," "KUBE 93 HD2" rhythmic CHR (2017--2018), classic hip-hop, Pride Radio (2019), and most recently "KUBE 93.3" hip-hop (since February 2022) [1][2].

The HD2 exile is technologically significant because it fundamentally changes the nature of radio listening. Analog FM is passive discovery -- a listener scanning the dial encounters whatever is being broadcast. HD2 requires active seeking -- the listener must know the channel exists, own compatible equipment, and deliberately tune to it. The audience model shifts from broadcast (one-to-many, passive) to narrowcast (one-to-few, active). The cultural power of FM radio has always depended on passive discovery: someone tunes in, hears something they did not expect, and their world changes. HD2 eliminates that possibility for all but the most determined seekers [2].

## 14. The 2016 Signal Loss: A Technical Analysis

The January 2016 format move from 93.3 FM to 104.9 FM provides a natural experiment in how signal infrastructure determines cultural reach. The two frequencies operated from different transmitter sites at different power levels, creating dramatically different coverage footprints while carrying identical programming content [1][2].

93.3 FM operated from Cougar Mountain at approximately 100,000 watts ERP with a Class C1 designation. The Height Above Average Terrain (HAAT) from Cougar Mountain provided clear line-of-sight propagation across the Puget Sound basin. The signal penetrated buildings effectively, maintained consistent mobile reception along the I-5 corridor, and reached automobile receivers at highway speed without the signal dropouts that plague weaker stations [1].

104.9 FM (KKBW) operated from a transmitter site with substantially less elevation advantage and lower effective power. The station's coverage was oriented toward Tacoma rather than centered on Seattle -- a "rimshot" configuration that served the southern portion of the market while leaving the core Seattle metro and Eastside communities with marginal or no reception. Listeners in Bellevue, Redmond, Kirkland, and Issaquah -- communities that had received strong 93.3 signal for decades -- found 104.9 unlistenable or absent entirely [1][6].

The technical difference between the two signals was not subtle. It was the difference between a regional cultural institution and a fringe broadcast. The format, programming, and brand were identical on both frequencies. The infrastructure was not. The audience's experience was determined not by what was being broadcast but by whether they could receive it. For much of KUBE's traditional coverage area, the answer after January 2016 was no [1][2].

This natural experiment confirms the core argument of this module: infrastructure is culture. The signal is not a neutral carrier. It is the foundation upon which cultural community is built. Change the signal, and you change the community -- not because the people are different, but because the physical infrastructure that connected them has been severed [2].

## 15. The Frequency as Commons

A radio frequency is a shared physical resource. It exists whether or not anyone broadcasts on it. The FCC licenses its use, but the frequency itself belongs to the electromagnetic spectrum -- a commons in the most literal sense. What makes a frequency culturally significant is not the signal that carries it but the community that forms around it [1][2].

93.3 MHz in Seattle carried six distinct cultural identities across six decades: Christian devotion, country music, Top 40 pop, rhythmic contemporary, hip-hop, and sports talk. Each format attracted a different community, generated different economic activity, and produced different cultural artifacts. The frequency was the same. The infrastructure -- Cougar Mountain, 100,000 watts, Olympia to Mount Vernon -- was the same. What changed was the signal placed upon the commons.

The KUBE era (roughly 1982--2022) is the most consequential chapter because the hip-hop format did something that Christian radio, country, and Top 40 did not: it created a cultural ecosystem that extended beyond the broadcast itself. KUBE's signal did not just carry music. It carried identity. It connected the Central District to Tacoma to Olympia to Mount Vernon. It created a shared cultural space that listeners organized their lives around. When the signal was moved or silenced, the ecosystem did not simply tune to a different station. It fragmented, because the commons that held it together was gone.

This is the infrastructure lesson of 93.3 FM: a frequency is a commons, and a commons is only as durable as the institution that maintains it. When ownership prioritizes portfolio optimization over community service, the commons collapses. The frequency remains. The community does not.

## 16. FM Propagation and the Puget Sound Basin

The physics of FM radio propagation in the Puget Sound basin are uniquely favorable for a transmitter sited on Cougar Mountain. FM signals travel primarily by line-of-sight, with some diffraction around terrain obstacles. The Puget Sound basin is a glacially carved trough running roughly north-south, flanked by the Cascade Range to the east and the Olympic Mountains to the west. The basin floor is relatively flat, with the I-5 corridor following the lowland from Olympia through Tacoma, Seattle, and Everett to Mount Vernon [1].

A transmitter on Cougar Mountain sits above the basin floor with clear sightlines in all directions except due east (blocked by the higher Cascades) and due west (partially blocked by the Olympics after the signal crosses Puget Sound). The north-south propagation path along the I-5 corridor is essentially unobstructed -- the terrain gently rises and falls through suburban development and farmland without the major elevation changes that would attenuate the signal [1].

This propagation geometry means that a 100,000-watt signal from Cougar Mountain delivers consistent, strong reception along the entire I-5 corridor. Automobile receivers -- the primary radio listening context for most Americans -- can maintain lock on the signal at highway speed without fading or multipath distortion. The listening experience is seamless: a driver from Olympia to Mount Vernon hears the same clear signal for the entire 120-mile journey. The frequency becomes a shared corridor experience -- everyone on the interstate, in the same moment, hearing the same broadcast [1][2].

The maritime influence of Puget Sound adds a propagation bonus. FM signals propagate more efficiently over water than over land, because water surfaces do not attenuate radio waves the way buildings, trees, and terrain do. The Sound itself acts as a signal conduit, carrying the broadcast from the east side of the basin (where Cougar Mountain sits) to communities on the Kitsap Peninsula and islands. This water-enhanced propagation expanded KUBE's effective audience beyond the I-5 corridor to include maritime communities that a land-locked signal would not have reached [1].

The contrast with AM radio propagation is instructive. AM signals follow the ground wave and are reflected by the ionosphere, giving them different coverage characteristics -- better at night (when ionospheric reflection extends range) but worse during the day and in urban environments (where buildings and electrical interference degrade the signal). Nasty Nes Rodriguez's FreshTracks on KFOX AM reached a limited audience partly because AM propagation in the Seattle metro area was inferior to FM. The move from AM specialty shows to FM full-format broadcasting was not just a cultural upgrade but a physics upgrade -- FM signals from Cougar Mountain reached more people more clearly than any AM signal in the market [1][3].

The Puget Sound basin's geography also creates multipath effects in urban environments. FM signals bounce off buildings, overpasses, and terrain features, arriving at the receiver from multiple paths with slight timing differences. A strong signal from a high transmitter site minimizes multipath distortion by ensuring that the direct path signal dominates the reflected paths. Cougar Mountain's elevation advantage reduced multipath effects for KUBE's signal compared to lower-sited competitors, contributing to a cleaner, more reliable listening experience in downtown Seattle and the urban core [1].

## 18. Antenna Patterns and Directional Considerations

FM stations can operate with either omnidirectional or directional antenna patterns. An omnidirectional antenna radiates equal power in all horizontal directions, creating a circular coverage footprint centered on the transmitter. A directional antenna concentrates power in specific directions, creating a coverage footprint that is elongated toward some areas and reduced toward others. The choice between omnidirectional and directional patterns is determined by FCC regulations, interference considerations with co-channel and adjacent-channel stations, and the geographic distribution of the target audience [1][7].

KUBE's 93.3 FM signal from Cougar Mountain operated with a pattern that maximized coverage along the I-5 corridor. The specific antenna configuration -- whether omnidirectional or directionally optimized -- is not fully documented in available public sources, but the coverage characteristics (strong north-south performance from Olympia to Mount Vernon, with east-west coverage extending across Puget Sound to the Kitsap Peninsula) suggest either an omnidirectional pattern from an elevated site or a slightly directional pattern optimized for the north-south axis [1].

Adjacent-channel coordination is a factor for any Class C1 station. 93.3 MHz must coordinate with stations on 93.1 and 93.5 MHz to avoid interference. In the Seattle market, adjacent-channel stations operate at lower power or from locations that minimize overlap. The coordination requirements are managed through FCC licensing, with each station's antenna pattern, power, and location designed to minimize interference while maximizing coverage of the licensed service area [7].

The HD Radio system adds a digital signal on either side of the analog carrier, occupying bandwidth that extends slightly beyond the traditional FM channel allocation. This expanded bandwidth can create interference with adjacent-channel stations that are closely spaced. The HD Radio interference dynamics are a factor in signal quality but do not significantly affect KUBE's primary coverage area, where the 100,000-watt analog signal dominates [1][2].

## 19. The Cougar Mountain Site: Shared Infrastructure

The Cougar Mountain transmitter site is not exclusive to 93.3 FM. Multiple Seattle-area broadcasters share the mountaintop, colocating their transmitters and antennas on shared tower infrastructure. Colocation is standard practice in the broadcasting industry: a single tower site on a favorable mountaintop can serve multiple stations, with each broadcaster paying rent to the tower operator for space on the structure [1].

The colocation model also provides redundancy: shared generators, shared access maintenance, and shared tower structural inspections reduce per-station costs while increasing overall site reliability. A broadcaster on a shared tower benefits from infrastructure investments made by all tenants, not just their own.

The shared nature of the Cougar Mountain site means that KUBE's transmitter infrastructure was not unique to the station. The tower, the access road, the power supply, and the general site maintenance were shared costs. What was unique was the combination of frequency (93.3 MHz), power (100,000 watts), and antenna height that together determined the station's specific coverage footprint [1][7].

When iHeartMedia made decisions about KUBE's format, the transmitter infrastructure on Cougar Mountain was not affected. The tower continued to broadcast on 93.3 MHz. The power level remained 100,000 watts. The antenna continued to radiate from the same height. What changed was the signal content -- the programming placed upon the frequency. The infrastructure remained. The culture it carried was replaced. This separation between infrastructure and content is the fundamental dynamic of radio as a cultural medium: the physical layer is durable, but the cultural layer is contingent on the decisions of whoever holds the license [1][2].
