On May 6, 1964, a 6,600-watt Christian radio station signed on at 93.3 FM in Seattle. Within thirty years, the same frequency — boosted to 100,000 watts and rechristened KUBE — would be broadcasting Sir Mix-A-Lot to every car stereo from Olympia to Mount Vernon. A frequency is a commons. What you build on it is the architecture that matters.

The Frequency — Infrastructure

From 6,600 Watts to 100,000

Infrastructure

May 6, 1964: KBLE-FM signs on with religious programming and 6,600 watts ERP. Through Eastside Broadcasting, Ostrander-Wilson, and First Media, the frequency evolves from Christian radio to the most powerful FM signal in the Seattle market. Cougar Mountain transmitter, HD Radio operations, and the technical architecture of a cultural commons.

The People — Biographical

Builders and Voices

People

Nasty Nes Rodriguez (1962–2025): Filipino immigrant, FreshTracks host, NastyMix Records co-founder, godfather of Northwest hip-hop. Sir Mix-A-Lot: Central District, Boys & Girls Club, "Baby Got Back." The T-Man Show. Charlie Brown. Eric Powers. DJ B-Mello. The Emerald Street Boys. The community that built a scene.

The Hip-Hop Era

Cultural Formation on 93.3 MHz

Hip-Hop Era

The 1991 shift to "93 Jams." The Central District ecosystem. NastyMix Records and the proof that hip-hop was national music. "Baby Got Back" at #1. Military bases seeding the fan base. The 1994 hybrid rock experiment. Competition, streaming, and the rhythmic CHR movement from KPWR to KMEL to KUBE.

The Corporate Era

Consolidation and Erasure

Corporate Era

Clear Channel acquires in 2001. Three exiles: the 2016 four-station shuffle, the 2018 return, the 2022 final flip to KJR sports talk. Russell Wilson's trade accelerates the end. KUBE exiled to HD2. When a cultural commons becomes a programming variable, the network scatters.
