IoT Security & Nuclear Energy: Convergent Frontiers in Critical Infrastructure Protection

Research Module | Tibsfox Research Series
Topics: Connected Vehicle Security, IoT Governance, Supply Chain Integrity, Nuclear Regulatory Modernization, AI in Regulation
Published: April 2026 | Sources: DEF CON proceedings, NRC publications, NIST frameworks, EU regulatory documents, industry analysis

The security of interconnected systems—from consumer IoT devices to nuclear power plants—represents one of the defining challenges of the 2020s. This research module examines two converging domains: the maturation of IoT security as a discipline, and the modernization of nuclear energy regulation in the United States. Both fields grapple with the tension between innovation velocity and safety assurance, between market forces and regulatory mandates, and between the promise of AI-driven efficiency and the necessity of human oversight in critical systems.

What emerges from this investigation is a picture of industries at inflection points. IoT security is transitioning from a fragmented afterthought to a regulated, standards-driven practice. Nuclear energy is undergoing its most significant regulatory transformation since 1956, with new reactor designs, new fuel supply chains, and new computational tools reshaping a sector that had been largely static for decades. The threads connecting them—supply chain integrity, AI adoption in safety-critical contexts, and the fundamental question of who bears responsibility when complex systems fail—make their simultaneous study essential.

1. Hacking the Tesla Model S: The Talk That Changed Automotive Cybersecurity

The Researchers and the Mission

At DEF CON 23 in August 2015, Marc Rogers and Kevin Mahaffey delivered a presentation that would reshape the conversation about connected vehicle security. Rogers, then Principal Security Researcher at Cloudflare, and Mahaffey, co-founder and CTO of Lookout, had spent months methodically probing the Tesla Model S—at the time the most software-dependent production vehicle on the road—and discovered six distinct vulnerabilities that allowed both local and remote control of critical vehicle functions.

Marc Rogers' career trajectory reads as a map of modern cybersecurity itself. Beginning as a lone hacker in the 1980s under the handle "Cjunky," Rogers went on to lead security at Vodafone (2003–2009), served as Principal Security Researcher at Lookout, then became Head of Information Security at Cloudflare, and later Vice President of Cybersecurity Strategy at Okta (2018–2022). He is one of the organizers and the Director of Security at DEF CON itself. Beyond corporate roles, Rogers co-founded the CTI League, an award-winning multinational cybersecurity initiative combining security professionals, government agencies, and law enforcement from over 80 countries. He also serves as a Senior Technical Advisor to the Institute for Security and Technology and is a member of the Ransomware Taskforce.

Technical Anatomy of the Attack

The attack began with physical access. Rogers and Mahaffey discovered a network cable behind the Model S dashboard, hidden in a maintenance panel. By connecting a laptop to this Ethernet port, they gained access to the car's infotainment system. From there, they exploited several interconnected weaknesses:

  • Internal Ethernet network exposure: The car's internal network lacked adequate segmentation, allowing the researchers to move laterally from the infotainment system to more sensitive subsystems.
  • Webkit browser vulnerabilities: The Model S ran an outdated version of the Webkit-based browser, riddled with known vulnerabilities that provided an initial foothold.
  • Gateway ECU command injection: The vehicle's Gateway Electronic Control Unit was susceptible to commands that could allow installation of malicious software capable of sending messages directly to the vehicle's CAN bus.
  • Authentication weaknesses: By impersonating the car's identity, the researchers convinced Tesla's headquarters systems that their laptop was the vehicle, extracting additional data and permissions.

The capabilities they demonstrated were alarming: remotely opening and closing the trunk (front and rear), locking and unlocking doors, manipulating the speedometer display, altering the suspension settings, controlling windows, and—most critically—starting and stopping the vehicle. Tesla's safety systems did provide a backstop: if power was cut to a moving vehicle, the system would activate the handbrake. At speeds below 8 km/h, the car would lurch to a stop; at higher speeds, it would shift to neutral while the driver retained control of steering and brakes.

The CAN Bus: Automotive Security's Fundamental Challenge

The Controller Area Network (CAN) bus, developed by Bosch in 1986, remains the backbone of automotive internal communications. It was designed for reliability in an era when vehicles were isolated systems—it includes no authentication, no encryption, and no access control. Any node on the bus can send any message, and all other nodes accept it. When Rogers and Mahaffey demonstrated CAN bus message injection, they exposed a vulnerability inherent to the architecture of virtually every vehicle on the road.

The UNECE WP.29 regulation, now mandatory for new vehicle models sold in UNECE member countries since 2024, catalogs 69 distinct cyber threats and vulnerabilities across seven sub-categories, with 23 required mitigations. ISO/SAE 21434, the complementary engineering standard, mandates cybersecurity risk management across the entire vehicle lifecycle. Yet the CAN bus remains: a 40-year-old protocol carrying safety-critical messages in modern software-defined vehicles.

Tesla's Response and the Broader Legacy

Tesla's response was, by the standards of the automotive industry, exemplary. The company pushed over-the-air software updates to its entire fleet within days, patching all six vulnerabilities. Tesla subsequently launched a formal bug bounty program in 2014 (expanded after the DEF CON disclosure) offering up to $15,000 per vulnerability. The company has since become a fixture at Pwn2Own competitions, where researchers have earned substantial rewards:

Other documented vulnerabilities include Gateway Firmware Signature Validation Bypass in Model 3, heap-based buffer overflow in the bsa_server via Bluetooth, and LTE modem vulnerabilities enabling IMSI catching and rogue base station attachment (documented in arXiv:2510.22024v1).

2. Who Owns Security in IoT? The Accountability Crisis

The Shared Responsibility Vacuum

The fundamental challenge of IoT security is not technical but organizational: no single entity owns security from end to end. Manufacturers ship devices, distributors rebrand them, platform providers host their data, and consumers connect them to networks. When something goes wrong, every party points elsewhere, creating what researchers have called a "chain of vulnerability" rather than a chain of custody.

This accountability vacuum has produced predictable consequences. The IoT Security Foundation's 2025 report on vulnerability disclosure in global consumer IoT found that only 40.53% of manufacturers have any published mechanism for security researchers to report vulnerabilities. While this represents an improvement of 4.94 percentage points over the 2024 figure of 35.59%, it means that nearly 60% of IoT manufacturers worldwide have no documented process for receiving security bug reports—let alone fixing and disclosing them.

Regulatory Frameworks Attempting to Assign Responsibility

Three major regulatory initiatives are reshaping IoT accountability:

The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA): Entered into force on December 10, 2024, with main obligations applying from December 11, 2027, and reporting obligations beginning September 11, 2026. The CRA holds manufacturers directly liable for security flaws and requires security updates throughout the device lifecycle. All manufacturers, importers, distributors, and developers of digital products or software used within the EU must comply. Key requirements include secure-by-design and secure-by-default development, continuous vulnerability management, mandatory incident reporting (within 24 hours for actively exploited vulnerabilities), five-year product lifecycle support, CE conformity marking, and SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) documentation.

NIST IoT Cybersecurity (NISTIR 8259 Series): Published by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology, the 8259 series establishes a foundational baseline split into two domains: technical capabilities that must be built into devices (8259A) and non-technical support that manufacturers must provide (8259B). The U.S. IoT Cybersecurity Improvement Act makes compliance a condition for selling IoT devices to federal government agencies. Core technical capabilities include device identity management, secure configuration, data protection, logical access control, software update mechanisms, and cybersecurity state awareness.

The UK NCSC Device Security Principles: The UK National Cyber Security Centre published Device Security Principles for Manufacturers, acknowledging that some security responsibilities "are not solely the responsibility of the device manufacturer and in certain situations depend on the implementation approach taken by a device management platform provider and the endpoint software they provide." This framework explicitly recognizes the shared nature of IoT security while still centering manufacturer accountability.

The IoT Security Foundation's Framework

The IoT Security Foundation (IoTSF) provides the most comprehensive pre-compliance framework available. The IoT Security Assurance Framework guides vendors through a risk management process across six domains: Governance (risk assessment, security culture, third-party collaboration), Secure Product Lifecycle (design through decommissioning), Secure Software Development Lifecycle, Secure Device (hardware, software, and data protections), End-User Services (privacy and security), and Operational Resilience (incident response and vulnerability reporting).

The Consumer's Role and Its Limits

While security is described as a "shared responsibility" involving developers, manufacturers, IT staff, and end users, the reality is that consumer agency in IoT security is severely constrained. Users cannot audit firmware, cannot verify supply chain integrity, and often cannot even change default configurations. The emerging regulatory consensus, reflected in both the EU CRA and the U.S. labeling initiatives, is that the primary burden must fall on manufacturers—with regulation serving as the enforcement mechanism that market forces alone have failed to provide.

"Governments and large buyers should favor secure devices in procurement and penalize vendors who ship insecure products, with consideration given to legal frameworks that hold manufacturers accountable when negligence in security causes supply-side systemic risk." — IIoT World, "IoT Cybersecurity Challenges and Policy Priorities for 2025"

3. IoT Security: Not Just Devices — The Full Attack Surface

Beyond the Endpoint

The instinct to equate "IoT security" with "device security" obscures the true attack surface. A modern IoT deployment comprises at least five distinct layers, each with its own vulnerability profile: the device itself, the gateway or edge computing layer, the cloud backend, mobile applications used for management, and the APIs connecting them all. A compromise at any layer can cascade to the others.

The scale of the problem defies easy comprehension. An estimated 18–19 billion IoT devices were connected in 2024–2025, projected to exceed 30 billion by 2030. In mid-2025, a research study documented that a typical IoT device faces attack attempts within 52 hours of being connected to the internet. About 35% of global DDoS attacks now originate from IoT botnets—the Mirai variant "Murdoc_Botnet" alone compromised over 1,300 Avtech cameras and Huawei routers in a single campaign. The record-setting DDoS attack in October 2024 reached 5.6 Tbps using over 13,000 compromised IoT devices.

Supply Chain Attacks on IoT Firmware

IoT firmware represents a uniquely opaque attack vector. Devices frequently rely on outdated third-party libraries embedded within monolithic binary blobs, making conventional vulnerability scanning ineffective. Research published in early 2025 (arXiv:2601.01308) proposed automated SBOM-driven vulnerability triage specifically for IoT firmware, addressing the challenge of identifying known-vulnerable components within firmware images that lack standard package management.

The supply chain threat is not theoretical. The FBI disrupted a massive state-linked botnet in 2024 that had compromised more than 260,000 devices worldwide, linked to the threat group Flax Typhoon. The botnet targeted critical manufacturing, IT, telecom, and government organizations. In a separate incident, a misconfiguration at Mars Hydro exposed 2.7 billion IoT device records, illustrating the data exposure risks inherent in cloud-connected device ecosystems.

SBOM Requirements and Firmware Transparency

The Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) has emerged as the central mechanism for firmware transparency. The EU CRA defines an SBOM as "a formal record containing details and supply-chain relationships of components included in the software elements of a product"—essentially an ingredients label for software. Under the CRA, manufacturers must provide SBOMs for all digital products sold in the EU by December 2027, with vulnerability reporting obligations beginning September 2026.

Building a CRA-ready SBOM pipeline for IoT requires integrating firmware analysis tools into the development lifecycle, automating dependency scanning, and maintaining continuous update mechanisms. The pipeline must handle the specific challenges of embedded systems: cross-compiled binaries, stripped symbols, proprietary bootloaders, and hardware-specific drivers that may not appear in standard vulnerability databases.

Default Credential Elimination and Secure Boot

The elimination of default credentials remains perhaps the single highest-impact security improvement available. Billions of IoT devices continue to ship with factory-default credentials (admin/admin, root/root, or device-specific defaults published in manuals). The NISTIR 8259A baseline explicitly requires unique device credentials out of the box.

Secure boot and firmware signing form the other essential device-level defense. These mechanisms ensure that only authorized, cryptographically signed firmware can execute on a device, preventing both unauthorized modification and rollback to vulnerable versions. NIST guidance requires that "the software update includes an authentication of the image or firmware so that only authorized entities can update the device."

MITRE ATT&CK for ICS: Mapping the Threat

The MITRE ATT&CK framework for Industrial Control Systems (ICS) provides a structured taxonomy of adversary tactics and techniques specifically tailored to industrial environments. The framework covers twelve tactical categories: Initial Access, Execution, Persistence, Privilege Escalation, Evasion, Discovery, Lateral Movement, Collection, Command and Control, Inhibit Response Function, Impair Process Control, and Impact. ATT&CK v19, scheduled for release April 28, 2026, will incorporate updated TTPs reflecting the IT-OT convergence that defines modern smart manufacturing and IIoT systems.

Gartner's first Magic Quadrant for Cyber-Physical Systems Protection Platforms identified five leaders: Claroty, Dragos, Microsoft, Armis, and Nozomi Networks. These vendors, along with Palo Alto Networks' IoT Security division, represent the core of the market. Each brings a distinct approach:

The OT/IT Convergence Driver

The single largest force driving IoT security investment is the convergence of operational technology and information technology networks. A 2024 survey found that 80% of manufacturers experienced more security incidents after integrating enterprise IT resources with plant networks. The drivers of convergence—real-time data analytics, predictive maintenance, supply chain optimization—are economically compelling, but they systematically dismantle the air gaps that historically protected industrial systems.

CISA's 2025 guidance on Secure Connectivity Principles for Operational Technology, developed with the UK's National Cyber Security Centre, establishes eight principles for designing, securing, and managing OT connectivity. The guidance explicitly acknowledges that many OT environments continue to use "insecure legacy industrial protocols that lack basic authentication and integrity checks," creating attack surfaces where threat actors can "impersonate a device or modify a message in transit to an OT device."

Critical Infrastructure Protection Requirements

Regulatory mandates are accelerating market growth. CISA's Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPG 2.0), updated for both IT and OT environments, establish baseline cybersecurity requirements that critical infrastructure operators must meet. ICS-CERT (now part of CISA) continues to publish advisories for vulnerabilities in industrial control systems—the volume of which has increased steadily as researchers apply IT security methodologies to OT environments. The convergence of these regulatory drivers with insurance industry requirements (cyber insurance underwriters increasingly require OT security assessments) creates a compounding demand signal that market analysts expect to sustain double-digit growth through at least 2030.

Strategic Consolidation

The market is consolidating rapidly. Cisco's $28 billion acquisition of Splunk unified observability and security telemetry, providing an integrated platform for IoT security monitoring. Palo Alto Networks, Fortinet, and other platform vendors are incorporating IoT and OT security capabilities through both acquisition and organic development. The trend points toward IoT security becoming a feature of enterprise security platforms rather than a standalone market—though pure-play specialists like Claroty, Nozomi, and Dragos continue to differentiate through domain depth. The tension between platform breadth and domain depth will likely define the competitive landscape for the next several years, with enterprises increasingly seeking unified visibility across IT, OT, and IoT assets while still requiring the protocol-level expertise that only dedicated OT security vendors currently provide.

5. NRC: One Government, One Mission — Safe Nuclear Deployment in a New Era

The Regulatory Transformation

On March 25, 2026, the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission approved the most significant change to reactor licensing in American history: 10 CFR Part 53, a risk-informed, performance-based, and technology-inclusive regulatory framework for commercial nuclear plants. Effective April 29, 2026, Part 53 is the first new set of regulations to address initial reactor licensing since Part 52 was created in 1989, and the first major update to reactor licensing standards since the Atomic Energy Commission issued Part 50 in 1956.

Part 53 was mandated by the Nuclear Energy Innovation and Modernization Act (NEIMA), signed into law in January 2019, which required the NRC to develop a technology-inclusive regulatory framework by the end of 2027. The NRC delivered more than a year ahead of schedule. NEIMA also required the NRC to develop performance metrics, budget transparency, and licensing process improvements—reflecting Congressional recognition that the existing regulatory apparatus was designed for large light-water reactors and was poorly suited to the advanced reactor designs now entering the pipeline.

What Part 53 Changes

Part 53 is an optional alternative to the existing Part 50 and Part 52 frameworks, designed to accommodate reactor technologies that do not fit within the current LWR-focused structure. Key innovations include:

  • Flexible risk methodologies: Applicants may use probabilistic risk assessment (PRA), other systematic risk evaluation methods, or hybrid approaches. Risk insights inform safety classification of structures, systems, and components (SSCs), potentially allowing the use of non-nuclear codes and standards for SSCs composed of commercial-grade components.
  • Manufacturing and transport: Section 53.620 allows fuel loading at the manufacturing site, with subsequent transport of fueled reactors to operating locations—critical for factory-built microreactors.
  • "Self-reliant-mitigation facilities": Plants whose designs demonstrate compliance with safety criteria without reliance on human action for credited event mitigation. This concept enables reduced staffing requirements for highly automated or passively safe designs.
  • Accelerated review timelines: NRC officials estimate Part 53 could cut design review timelines to 18 months or less and reduce application costs by half or more.

Microreactor Licensing Strategy

A particularly important development within the NRC's modernization agenda is the microreactor licensing strategy. In June 2025, the NRC staff delivered SECY-25-0052, "Nth-of-a-Kind Microreactor Licensing and Deployment Considerations," which seeks Commission approval for affording finality to standard operational programs reviewed and approved by NRC staff. This is critical for microreactors, which are designed to be factory-built and deployed in multiples—potentially dozens or hundreds of identical units at diverse sites. The traditional model, where each installation undergoes independent full-scope review, would negate the economic advantages of serial production. The staff's strategy aims to enable "safe and secure deployment on optimized timeframes" by establishing licensing precedent that carries forward across identical deployments.

The NRC has also undergone a major organizational restructuring to handle the increased licensing workload. With multiple advanced reactor applications arriving simultaneously, alongside the continued oversight of 93 operating reactors, the agency faces resource allocation challenges that Part 53's streamlined processes are specifically designed to address.

HALEU: The Fuel That Makes It Possible

High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, enriched to between 5% and 20% U-235 (compared to less than 5% for conventional reactor fuel), is required by most advanced reactor designs. The HALEU supply chain represents both the greatest enabler and the greatest bottleneck for the advanced nuclear sector—a challenge examined in detail in Section 6.

6. Critical Links in the Nuclear Supply Chain: From Counterfeit Parts to HALEU

The Counterfeit Parts Crisis

The NRC Office of the Inspector General (OIG) issued two reports that delivered an unsparing assessment of nuclear supply chain integrity. The central finding: counterfeit, fraudulent, and suspect items (CFSI) are believed to be present in "most, if not all" U.S. nuclear power plants, presenting "nuclear safety and security concerns that could have serious consequences for nuclear power plant equipment required to perform a safety function."

The specific incidents documented are sobering. Counterfeit parts found at reactor facilities have included emergency service water pump shafts (one of which was discovered only after it snapped in service), temperature sensors used to identify steam line breaks, and breaker switches designed to prevent fires. The Department of Energy identified more than 100 instances of fraudulent, counterfeit, or suspect components in a single fiscal year, including five involving "safety-significant" components in operating reactors.

The OIG identified a systemic oversight failure: the NRC does not require licensees to track CFSI unless a situation rises to the level of being a "significant condition adverse to quality." The NRC lacks a unified process for collecting, assessing, and disseminating CFSI information—reports arrive through different channels and are treated differently by different NRC offices. The OIG recommended that the NRC clarify its CFSI information handling, improve staff awareness of CFSI issues, and strengthen the connection between CFSI intelligence and reactor inspections.

Quality Assurance in Nuclear-Grade Components

The NRC requires nuclear power plants to use "only those products and services exhibiting the highest quality in agency-regulated activities." Vendors, suppliers, and plants must verify the quality of items destined for safety-related functions through extensive inspection of critical physical characteristics combined with rigorous performance testing. The QA framework is codified in 10 CFR Part 50, Appendix B, which establishes 18 criteria for quality assurance programs.

Yet the OIG reports reveal a gap between the regulatory requirement and its implementation. The nuclear supply chain is global, multi-tiered, and often opaque. Components may pass through multiple intermediaries between manufacturer and installation, each representing a potential point of substitution or fraud. The challenge is compounded by an aging fleet—many U.S. reactors are operating under license extensions beyond their original 40-year design life, requiring replacement parts for systems whose original manufacturers may no longer exist.

The HALEU Supply Chain Challenge

The supply chain for High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium presents a distinct but equally consequential challenge. Currently, only Russia and China possess the infrastructure to produce HALEU at commercial scale. The United States enacted a ban on imports of Russian uranium products in May 2024, with deliveries set to end entirely by 2028 under the prohibition. This creates an urgent need for domestic and allied production capacity that does not yet exist at scale.

Progress is being made, but the timeline is tight:

  • Centrus Energy: Began operating a demonstration HALEU cascade at Piketon, Ohio in October 2023. By mid-2025, Centrus had produced and delivered over 920 kg of HALEU to the DOE. The contract has been extended to June 30, 2026, with options for up to eight additional years.
  • Urenco: Received NRC authorization to enrich up to 10% U-235 in the USA in 2025. Urenco is also building a HALEU facility at Capenhurst in the UK, targeting production by 2031.
  • Government investment: Congress has appropriated $3.4 billion to support domestic enrichment. In January 2026, the DOE committed $2.7 billion over ten years to expand domestic uranium enrichment capacity. Four companies—Centrus Energy, Urenco USA, Orano USA, and General Matter—were awarded HALEU production contracts in October 2024.

The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

The fundamental challenge is economic circularity. Without assured long-term commercial demand for HALEU, private fuel-cycle companies cannot commit the capital required to build enrichment infrastructure. Without enrichment infrastructure, advanced reactor developers cannot guarantee fuel supply, which undermines the business case for reactor deployment, which in turn fails to generate the demand signal that would justify enrichment investment. Government funding is bridging this gap, but the market must ultimately become self-sustaining.

"A declassified cable reinforces proliferation concerns about high-assay low-enriched uranium fuel"—highlighting that the HALEU supply chain is not merely an economic and logistical challenge but also a nonproliferation concern, given that material enriched to 20% U-235 is significantly closer to weapons-grade than conventional reactor fuel. — Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, November 2025

7. NRC: Modernizing Mission Work — AI Vision Into Action

The NRC's AI Strategic Plan

Published on September 29, 2025, the NRC's Artificial Intelligence Strategic Plan (NUREG-2261) establishes five goals for AI adoption across the agency's regulatory mission for fiscal years 2023–2027:

  1. Regulatory readiness: Ensure NRC preparedness for reviewing AI applications submitted by licensees and applicants in safety-critical nuclear systems.
  2. Organizational framework: Establish governance structures and processes for evaluating AI applications within the NRC's own operations.
  3. Partnerships: Strengthen and expand AI collaboration with DOE, international regulators, national laboratories, and industry.
  4. Workforce development: Cultivate AI proficiency across the agency, including broadening generative AI training to staff and contractors.
  5. Foundation building: Pursue specific use cases to build an AI foundation, including the Data Warehouse project for ML/NLP processing of regulatory documents.

Active AI Use Cases

The NRC is deploying AI across several regulatory functions, though the agency's Chief Data Officer has emphasized taking "small steps" to avoid setbacks that could undermine institutional confidence in AI tools:

Inspection planning optimization: Machine learning models are being used to inform inspection planning by analyzing patterns in operational event data, licensee performance indicators, and historical inspection findings. The goal is to allocate inspection resources more effectively by identifying facilities and systems that warrant heightened scrutiny.

Natural language processing for document analysis: The NRC is applying NLP models to analyze regulatory documents, operating experience reports, and event narratives. A structured framework is being developed specifically for nuclear oversight, with the U.S. contributing work on applying NLP to classify and analyze narrative information in event reports—an international collaboration with other nuclear regulatory bodies.

Cybersecurity state characterization: AI/ML techniques are being applied to characterize the cybersecurity posture of nuclear facilities, supporting the NRC's oversight of digital instrumentation and control systems in operating plants.

Regulatory framework gap assessment: In June 2023, the NRC initiated a comprehensive review of its regulations and more than 500 Regulatory Guides to determine whether the existing framework could accommodate AI technologies used by licensees. The October 2024 report (ML24290A059) concluded that the current regulatory framework is "generally sufficient" to accommodate AI, though specific guidance updates may be needed.

DOE's Parallel AI Initiative for Nuclear Licensing

The Department of Energy is pursuing a complementary initiative focused on using AI to accelerate the nuclear licensing process itself. Everstar's Gordian AI solution demonstrated the potential by converting a safety analysis into an NRC license application—producing a 208-page document in one day, a process that typically takes four to six weeks. The DOE estimates that AI has the potential to reduce both document development time and regulatory review cycles by as much as 50%, while improving accuracy, consistency, and traceability.

The Tension Between Efficiency and Regulatory Conservatism

The NRC faces a tension that is inherent to AI adoption in any safety-critical regulatory context. The agency's mission is fundamentally conservative: its purpose is to ensure that nuclear activities do not pose unreasonable risks to public health and safety. AI promises efficiency gains that are urgently needed as the licensing workload grows with advanced reactor applications, but the consequences of error are uniquely severe.

A 2025 executive order asked the NRC to consult with the Department of Defense and Department of Energy about radiation exposure limits, raising concerns about regulatory independence. Critics argue that DoD and DOE, as agencies incentivized to accelerate nuclear deployment, lack the requisite independence to advise on safety standards. The broader concern is that AI adoption could become a vector for regulatory capture—where efficiency gains serve the regulated industry's interests at the expense of safety margins.

Synthesis: Threads Connecting IoT Security and Nuclear Energy

The seven topics examined in this module converge on several themes that transcend their individual domains:

Supply Chain Integrity as a Universal Challenge

Whether the concern is counterfeit nuclear-grade pump shafts, compromised IoT firmware with embedded vulnerable libraries, or HALEU fuel dependent on Russian enrichment capacity, supply chain integrity is the common thread. The SBOM requirements emerging in IoT regulation and the quality assurance frameworks in nuclear regulation are parallel responses to the same fundamental problem: complex systems built from components whose provenance and integrity cannot be verified without deliberate, systematic effort.

The Responsibility Assignment Problem

IoT's "who owns security?" crisis has a direct analog in nuclear regulation. The NRC's OIG found that counterfeit parts information enters the agency through different channels and is treated differently by different offices—a structural accountability failure remarkably similar to the IoT ecosystem's fragmented responsibility model. The EU CRA and NRC Part 53 both represent attempts to clarify accountability in complex, multi-stakeholder systems.

AI as Both Tool and Risk

The NRC's cautious AI adoption and the IoT industry's AI-powered security tools (Nozomi Networks' positioning of AI cybersecurity as "table stakes" for 2026) represent two sides of the same coin. AI can improve security monitoring, accelerate regulatory review, and detect anomalies that human analysts miss. But AI also introduces new attack surfaces, new failure modes, and new dependencies. The NRC's insistence on "small steps" may be the most important lesson for any domain adopting AI in safety-critical contexts.

The Regulatory Inflection Point

Both domains are experiencing once-in-a-generation regulatory transformations. The EU CRA, NIST 8259, and CISA's OT connectivity principles are reshaping IoT security governance. NRC Part 53 is the first new reactor licensing framework in 37 years. These are not incremental updates—they are structural changes that will define how security and safety are managed for the next decade or more.
