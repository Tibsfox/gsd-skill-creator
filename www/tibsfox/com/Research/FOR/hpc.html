BLAS — The Most Durable API in Computing

The story begins in 1979 with Level 1 BLAS: vector operations — dot products, vector norms, scalar-vector multiplies. Charles Lawson, Richard Hanson, David Kincaid, and Fred Krogh published the original spec as Fortran 66 subroutines: SAXPY (single-precision a*x + y), SDOT, SSWAP, SNRM2. Those names survive today, 47 years later, in every BLAS implementation shipped on Earth.

LevelYearOperationsPerformance Limit
Level 11979Vector-vector: SAXPY, SDOT, SNRM2Memory bandwidth-bound
Level 21988Matrix-vector: SGEMV, STRSV, rank-1 updatesCache-bound
Level 31990Matrix-matrix: SGEMM, STRSM, SSYRKCompute-bound (peak FLOPS)

Level 3 BLAS was the revolution. The O(n³) arithmetic over O(n²) data meant a well-tuned SGEMM could reach a substantial fraction of peak floating-point throughput. Every dense linear algebra algorithm anyone cared about was rewritten in terms of Level 3 BLAS calls. LAPACK is, in a deep sense, a book of recipes for expressing numerical linear algebra as sequences of GEMMs.

LAPACK — The Successor to LINPACK and EISPACK

LAPACK (Linear Algebra PACKage) was released in 1992. It superseded LINPACK (1979, the linear systems library) and EISPACK (eigenvalues/eigenvectors). LAPACK is written primarily in Fortran 77, with some Fortran 90 features in LAPACK 3.x. It is the standard for dense linear algebra worldwide.

LAPACK’s key insight: build everything on top of BLAS. The Level 3 BLAS GEMM is the workhorse. LAPACK provides high-level routines (LU, QR, Cholesky, SVD, eigenvalue solvers) that decompose into sequences of BLAS calls. Vendors optimize BLAS; LAPACK gets the speedup for free.

Why Fortran is Fast — The Seven Reasons

#ReasonDetail
1Aliasing rulesFortran assumes function arguments don’t alias unless explicitly declared. C/C++ require restrict for similar guarantees. Compilers vectorize Fortran more aggressively.
2Array semanticsFirst-class arrays enable bounds analysis, SIMD vectorization, and fusion of operations.
3Mathematical formula expressionc = a + b on whole arrays compiles to optimized SIMD loops, not slow elementwise operations.
4Column-major storageBetter cache behavior for traditional numerical algorithms (matrices traversed column-by-column).
5No undefined behavior in arithmeticInteger overflow wraps. Floating-point follows IEEE 754. No signed/unsigned mixing issues.
6Strong typingNo implicit conversions between INTEGER and REAL. Type errors caught at compile time.
7Compiler maturity60+ years of optimization research applied to Fortran compilers. Every loop transformation in the literature has a Fortran implementation somewhere.

TOP500 and the LINPACK Benchmark

The TOP500 ranking has been published twice yearly since 1993. The ranking metric is the HPL (High Performance LINPACK) benchmark: solve a dense linear system of equations, measure FLOPS. The benchmark code is C/Fortran with MPI; the inner kernels are BLAS GEMM calls; the BLAS is ultimately Fortran (or hand-tuned assembly that mimics Fortran semantics).

HPCG (High Performance Conjugate Gradients), introduced 2014, is a complementary benchmark using sparse iterative solvers. It produces results roughly 30x lower than HPL and better represents real workloads. Both benchmarks are Fortran-derived.

  • Finance: Quant libraries, Monte Carlo simulations. Many investment banks have Fortran codebases.

The takeaway: Fortran is not a museum piece. It is the production language for the most computationally demanding work humans do — weather forecasts that affect billions of people, climate models that inform policy, drug simulations, materials discovery, fusion research, supercomputer benchmarks. The next ten million lines of Fortran will be written by people in their twenties, on machines that didn’t exist when CESM was started in 2010, running code whose oldest comments are from 1987.
