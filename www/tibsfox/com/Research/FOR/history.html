1. The Man — John Warner Backus

John Warner Backus (December 3, 1924 – March 17, 2007). Born in Philadelphia. A mediocre student. Expelled from the University of Virginia for poor grades. Drafted into the Army in 1943. Trained as a medic, then a cryptographer. Discharged 1946. Enrolled at Columbia University intending to study medicine, switched to mathematics. BS 1949, MS 1950.

The decisive moment came on a 1950 visit to the IBM showroom at 590 Madison Avenue, where he saw the Selective Sequence Electronic Calculator (SSEC). He asked for a job on impulse, took an aptitude test, and was hired. His first assignment: programming the SSEC. His second: the IBM 701, IBM’s first commercial scientific computer.

2. The Problem — Why Programmers Cost More Than Computers

By 1953, programming was a brutal undertaking. Assembly was the standard. Speedcoding, an interpretive floating-point system Backus himself wrote in 1953, was perhaps fifty times slower than hand-tuned machine code but ten times faster to write. That ratio captured the central economic problem of early computing.

ResourceCost (1954)Equivalent (2025)
IBM 701 time~$200 / hour~$2,300 / hour
Skilled programmer~$10,000 / year~$115,000 / year

The economic case was undeniable: programmers spent more time writing and debugging code than the computer spent running it. Any tool that reduced programmer time, even at the cost of machine time, would pay for itself.

3. The Proposal — December 8, 1953

Backus sent a memo to his boss Cuthbert Hurd: a programming language for the IBM 704 in which mathematical formulas could be written “almost as they appear on paper.” Hurd approved in January 1954. The team began with Backus, Irving Ziller, and Harlan Herrick, eventually growing to include Sheldon Best, Lois Haibt (the only woman on the team), Roy Nutt, and others.

4. The Compiler — 18 Person-Years

The first FORTRAN compiler took 18 person-years to build. It was released in April 1957 for the IBM 704. The team had to prove not just that compiled code could work, but that it could be fast enough to compete with hand-written assembly. They invented sophisticated optimizations: register allocation, instruction scheduling, common subexpression elimination, and loop transformations — many for the first time in any compiler.

The result: FORTRAN code typically ran within a few percent of hand-tuned assembly. The skepticism collapsed. The high-level language era began.

2004Fortran 2003 (ISO/IEC 1539-1:2004)Object-oriented programming. ISO_C_BINDING for C interop. IEEE arithmetic. The biggest revision since F90.
2010Fortran 2008 (ISO/IEC 1539-1:2010)Coarrays. Submodules. DO CONCURRENT. The parallel revolution.
2018Fortran 2018 (ISO/IEC 1539-1:2018)Enhanced coarrays. Atomic operations. Teams. Better C interop.
2023Fortran 2023 (ISO/IEC 1539-1:2023)Generics. Conditional expressions. Token improvements. The latest.

7. Backus’s Later Work and Disillusionment

Backus received the Turing Award in 1977 for FORTRAN. His acceptance lecture, “Can Programming Be Liberated from the von Neumann Style?,” was a famous critique of imperative programming — including his own creation. He spent the rest of his career advocating for functional programming via FP and FL languages, neither of which achieved wide adoption.

Backus also invented Backus-Naur Form (BNF), the metasyntax notation used to describe nearly every programming language grammar since ALGOL 60. His Turing Award citation reads: “For profound, influential, and lasting contributions to the design of practical high-level programming systems, notably through his work on FORTRAN, and for publication of formal procedures for the specification of programming languages.”

9. Why Fortran Won — The Four Reasons

  1. Performance. Compiler maturity (60+ years), strict aliasing rules, array semantics, column-major storage. Fortran code routinely outperforms equivalent C/C++ code.
  2. Portability. ANSI standardized in 1966. Code from 1970 still compiles. The same source runs on every platform from a laptop to Frontier.
  3. Ecosystem. BLAS, LAPACK, ScaLAPACK, NetCDF, HDF5, MPI. The numerical libraries that everything else wraps.
  4. Continuity. Once an institution invests millions of lines of Fortran in their flagship code (CESM, WRF, ECMWF IFS, Gaussian, NWChem), the cost of rewriting is enormous. Fortran code persists.

10. The Modern Renaissance (2020s)

For decades, the Fortran community was small and aging. The 2020s changed that. Ondřej Čertík founded LFortran and the fortran-lang community. fpm (Fortran Package Manager) shipped in 2020. stdlib began the same year. fortran-lang.org and the Fortran Discourse forum became active hubs. Flang matured inside LLVM. Intel made ifx free for personal use. NVIDIA made nvfortran free for non-commercial use.

The result: for the first time in 30 years, a young researcher can pick up Fortran with modern tooling, modern community, modern documentation. The renaissance is real and ongoing.
