1. Topographic Survey & Landslide Mapping

The existing parking area at Picnic Point Park sits on a bluff-top plateau at approximately 79 feet elevation, with the terrain dropping steeply to the west toward the BNSF Railway corridor and Puget Sound. A LiDAR-based topographic survey would establish the precise ground surface model required for structure placement, grading design, and stormwater drainage analysis. The survey envelope should extend beyond the parking footprint to capture the bluff edge geometry and any existing retaining structures.

The definitive hazard mapping for this segment comes from USGS Miscellaneous Field Studies Map MF-2346 (Troost, Booth, Wisher, and Shimel, 2005), which documents landslide activity on coastal bluffs from Shilshole Bay to Everett. The Picnic Point segment carries a "recent and active" landslide designation — the highest hazard classification in the mapping system. Multiple overlapping landslide deposits are mapped across the bluff face, with bluff heights ranging from 150 to 250 feet. The dominant failure modes are shallow translational slides and debris flows within the Esperance Sand unit, and deep-seated rotational failures within the underlying Lawton Clay (Troost et al., 2005). This mapping establishes the baseline hazard framework against which any bluff-top development must be evaluated.

Key deliverables from the topographic survey include 1-foot contour mapping of the parking area and adjacent slopes, identification of existing utilities (water, sewer, storm, power), and location of the pedestrian overpass abutment on the east (parking) side. The survey must also document the BNSF right-of-way boundary, which constrains the western extent of any structure footprint. Aerial LiDAR data from the Puget Sound LiDAR Consortium, supplemented by ground-truthed survey control points, would provide the most cost-effective approach for a feasibility-level topographic model. Higher-resolution terrestrial LiDAR or photogrammetric survey would be deferred to the schematic design phase.

Comparable geotechnical work in the same bluff system has been completed by Shannon & Wilson for the Meadowdale Beach Park project (same geological formation, same failure modes). The Mukilteo Multimodal Terminal (completed 2020) also required extensive bluff-adjacent geotechnical investigation, confirming SDC D conditions and employing steel pipe piles driven to the till bearing stratum. Point Wells, immediately south of Picnic Point, has experienced active slope failures in the same stratigraphic sequence, providing additional empirical data on local bluff behavior.

2. Subsurface Exploration & Stratigraphic Sequence

The subsurface conditions beneath the existing parking area consist of the glacial and interglacial deposits characteristic of the Puget Lowland, deposited during and between Vashon-age ice sheet advances approximately 14,000–17,000 years ago. USGS MF-2346 (Troost et al., 2005) and regional geologic mapping identify a well-defined four-unit stratigraphic sequence at Picnic Point, listed from top to bottom:

UnitThicknessSPT N-valueBearing CapacityEngineering Character
Vashon Till5–30 ft>50 (often refusal)4,000–8,000 psfDense, overconsolidated diamicton; excellent bearing stratum
Esperance Sand (Advance Outwash)30–100 ft20–60+2,000–4,000 psfPermeable aquifer; primary hazard unit for groundwater-triggered slides
Lawton Clay (Glaciolacustrine)20–80 ft10–30—Low-permeability aquitard; deep-seated rotational failure surface
Pre-Vashon depositsVariableVariable—Older glacial and interglacial units; encountered in deep borings

The Esperance Sand / Lawton Clay contact is the critical hydrogeological interface at this site. The Esperance Sand is a permeable aquifer that transmits groundwater laterally through the bluff, while the underlying Lawton Clay acts as an aquitard. Groundwater seeping along this contact emerges as springs on the bluff face, saturating the Esperance Sand and triggering the shallow translational slides and debris flows documented in MF-2346 (Troost et al., 2005). This mechanism is the primary landslide driver along the entire Shilshole Bay to Everett corridor and must be a central focus of any subsurface exploration program.

A feasibility-level subsurface exploration program would include Standard Penetration Test (SPT) borings and/or Cone Penetration Tests (CPT) at the proposed structure corners and interior column locations. For a parking structure of this scale, a minimum of 4–6 borings to 30–50 feet below existing grade is expected per WSDOT Geotechnical Design Manual M 46-03.16, with laboratory testing for grain-size distribution, moisture content, and unconfined compressive strength of cohesive soils. The borings must penetrate through the Vashon Till into the Esperance Sand to characterize both the bearing stratum and the groundwater conditions that drive slope instability. Piezometers installed in at least two borings should monitor seasonal groundwater fluctuations, particularly during the wet season (October–April) when recharge is highest.

3. Slope Stability & Setback Requirements

The proximity of the proposed structure to the bluff edge introduces slope stability as the governing geotechnical concern. USGS MF-2346 maps the Picnic Point bluffs at the highest hazard level — "recent and active" landslide designation — with multiple overlapping failure deposits visible in the mapping (Troost et al., 2005). The bluffs in this segment are 150–250 feet high and experience two distinct failure modes: shallow translational slides in the Esperance Sand driven by groundwater seepage, and deep-seated rotational failures with slip surfaces in or near the Lawton Clay contact.

Bluff recession rates along this coastal segment average 0.1–0.5 ft/year during periods of chronic erosion, but episodic catastrophic failures can remove 10–50+ feet of bluff-top material in a single event (Troost et al., 2005). These episodic failures, rather than the average recession rate, govern the required setback for permanent structures. Snohomish County Critical Areas Regulations SCC 30.62B.340 establish the setback formula: the greater of one-third the bluff height or 50 feet from the bluff edge, measured from the top of the slope. For bluffs in the 150–250-foot range, this translates to setbacks of 50–83 feet, which will significantly constrain the buildable envelope on the bluff-top plateau.

Slope stability analysis for the feasibility study would use limit-equilibrium methods (Spencer's or Morgenstern-Price) to evaluate the factor of safety under static and seismic loading conditions. The analysis must account for the surcharge load imposed by the parking structure on the bluff-top soils, pore pressure conditions during wet-season groundwater recharge at the Esperance/Lawton contact, and pseudo-static seismic forces consistent with SDC D. Per SCC 30.62B.340 and WSDOT M 46-03.16, the minimum acceptable factors of safety are:

ConditionRequired Factor of SafetyGoverning Standard
Static (long-term, drained)1.5SCC 30.62B.340 / WSDOT M 46-03.16
Dynamic (pseudo-static seismic)1.1SCC 30.62B.340 / ASCE 7-22

A geotechnical report per SCC 30.62B.140 is required for any development within 200 feet of steep slopes (defined as 33% gradient or steeper). This report must be prepared and stamped by a Washington State-licensed geotechnical engineer or engineering geologist, and must address slope stability, erosion potential, groundwater conditions, and the proposed structure's impact on geologic hazard conditions. The report directly satisfies the Critical Areas review requirement identified in Module C.

4. Foundation System Recommendation

Foundation system selection depends on the bearing capacity and depth of the Vashon Till at the proposed structure location. Where intact, Vashon Till exhibits excellent bearing characteristics: SPT N-values routinely exceed 50 blows (often reaching refusal), and allowable bearing capacity ranges from 4,000 to 8,000 psf (Troost et al., 2005; WSDOT M 46-03.16). However, the bluff-top setting introduces the possibility of weathered or reworked till near the surface, with reduced strength parameters that must be confirmed by site-specific borings.

WSDOT M 46-03.16 permits spread footings on intact Vashon Till with a presumptive bearing capacity of 4,000 psf for structures meeting standard dead-plus-live load criteria. This is the preferred foundation system where competent till is encountered within 5–10 feet of the proposed foundation bearing elevation, as it minimizes both cost and construction complexity. For a 3-level precast concrete parking structure with column loads in the range of 200–500 kips, spread footings on till provide adequate capacity with a comfortable margin of safety.

Where the till surface is deeper, weathered, or absent (as may occur near the bluff edge where erosion has removed surficial deposits), drilled shafts socketed into intact till are the recommended alternative per WSDOT M 46-03.16. Drilled shafts transfer column loads through any weak surficial soils directly into the dense bearing stratum. For marine-adjacent or highly corrosive conditions, steel pipe piles with corrosion protection are the standard WSDOT approach — this is the system employed at the Mukilteo Multimodal Terminal (2020), which was constructed on the same geological formation under SDC D conditions with steel pipe piles driven to refusal in the Vashon Till.

Settlement analysis must confirm that total and differential settlements under the structure are within acceptable limits for precast concrete connections — typically 1 inch total and 0.5 inch differential between adjacent columns. Vashon Till's overconsolidated nature generally produces favorable settlement characteristics (elastic settlement dominant, negligible consolidation settlement), but this must be confirmed with site-specific data. Corrosion protection is required for any foundation elements in the marine environment per WSDOT corrosion design guidelines, including increased steel section for sacrificial loss, epoxy coating, or cathodic protection systems.

5. Seismic Design Parameters

The Picnic Point Park site is subject to seismic hazard from four distinct source zones, each capable of producing damaging ground motions at the site. The USGS Seismic Design Maps tool, applying ASCE 7-22 criteria for the site coordinates, produces the following design parameters:

SDC D is confirmed independently by both the USGS design maps and the Snohomish County building code (SCC 30.52F Part 300). The four seismic sources contributing to the site hazard are, in order of expected ground motion severity:

  1. Cascadia Subduction Zone: Magnitude 9.0+ megathrust earthquake. Recurrence interval approximately 200–600 years. Would produce long-duration shaking (2–5 minutes) at moderate amplitude, with potential for tsunami and coastal subsidence. The last full-margin rupture occurred January 26, 1700.
  2. South Whidbey Island Fault Zone (SWIFZ): Located approximately 15 km from the site. Capable of M6.5–7.0+ shallow crustal earthquakes. Would produce the highest peak ground accelerations at the site due to proximity. Active Quaternary displacement is documented along the fault trace.
  3. Seattle Fault Zone: Capable of M7.0+ events. Last major rupture approximately 1,100 years ago produced >1 meter of coastal uplift in the Puget Sound region, documented by raised shoreline deposits and tsunami deposits.
  4. Deep intraslab earthquakes: Occurring within the subducting Juan de Fuca plate at depths of 40–70 km. The 2001 Nisqually earthquake (M6.8, depth 52 km) is the most recent significant event in this category. Recurrence interval approximately 30–50 years for M6.5+ events.

SDC D imposes significant requirements on both the structural system (Module B) and the geotechnical design. For the foundation, this includes evaluation of liquefaction potential in any granular soils below the water table (primarily the Esperance Sand if saturated), lateral spreading potential near the bluff edge, and seismic bearing capacity reduction factors. The proximity to four distinct seismic sources, combined with the "recent and active" landslide designation, makes seismically-induced slope failure the highest-consequence geotechnical risk at this site. The geotechnical report must provide site-specific seismic design parameters including SDS, SD1, site classification per ASCE 7-22 Chapter 20, and recommendations for seismic earth pressure coefficients for any below-grade retaining walls.
