The +9.1% materials escalation is the most significant cost pressure, driven by structural steel and reinforcing bar supply constraints, concrete aggregate pricing, and precast plant capacity utilization in the Pacific Northwest. 3. Phased Construction Economics

A two-phase construction approach addresses both budget constraints and construction logistics at a constrained site. Phase 1 would construct the ground level and Level 2, providing an initial parking capacity increase and establishing the structural frame. Phase 2 would add the Level 3 deck and the overpass tie-in bridge, completing the full 3-level structure and the integrated access system.

Research from Nitterhouse Concrete on phased parking structure construction identifies the key economic tradeoffs:

  • Foundation oversizing for future expansion: Adds a 15–25% premium on Phase 1 foundation costs, but this is dramatically less expensive than retrofitting foundations after the fact.
  • Phase 2 crane access: Limited to the perimeter of the existing structure, because the Phase 1 structure may remain operational during Phase 2 construction. This constraint increases erection costs and may require smaller crane configurations.
  • Operational continuity: The existing structure can remain in use during Phase 2, maintaining park access — a significant advantage for a public facility.

The Villanova University parking structure expansion provides the closest documented precedent for a precast phased expansion: 2 levels expanded to 4 levels, 270 stalls expanded to 493 stalls, with a new elevator tower constructed within the original footprint (Nitterhouse, 2023). The key lesson from Villanova is that the Phase 1 foundation and column design must anticipate the full Phase 2 loading from the outset. Retrofitting after the fact is dramatically more expensive — the University of North Carolina experience with post-hoc expansion required micropiles, structural strengthening of existing columns, and temporary shoring, all of which added substantial cost and complexity that would have been avoided by forward-designed foundations.

Phase 1 construction duration is estimated at 8–12 months from groundbreaking for a precast structure of this scale. Phase 2, involving Level 3 deck erection and bridge construction, would add 4–8 months. The phasing allows the park to maintain partial parking access during construction and distributes the capital expenditure across two budget cycles, which may be critical for grant funding strategies that operate on biennial appropriation cycles.

The FRA Railroad Crossing Elimination (RCE) or Consolidated Rail Infrastructure and Safety Improvements (CRISI) grant is the highest-value individual source, with 80/20 federal/local match and direct eligibility for the overpass and railroad interface work that constitutes the project's most expensive element.

The WWRP Recreation grant deserves particular attention because parking is explicitly listed as an eligible expense under the recreation access category — an unusual provision that makes this one of the few grant programs where the parking structure itself (not just the bridge or ADA elements) qualifies for funding. The 2026 grant cycle opens February 11, 2026, with applications due April 30, 2026, and RCO Board funding decisions scheduled for October 27, 2026.

Key contacts for grant strategy: Rob Marchand (Senior Park Planner, Snohomish County Parks) managed the Meadowdale Beach multi-source funding stack and has direct experience with each of these grant programs. The Recreation and Conservation Office administers WWRP, LWCF, and ALEA through a unified application process.

5. 20-Year Life-Cycle Cost Analysis

A 20-year life-cycle cost analysis provides the total cost of ownership framework for comparing the parking structure against alternatives. The major life-cycle cost categories include initial construction, annual maintenance, periodic rehabilitation (deck sealing, joint replacement, painting), and eventual major repair or replacement of building systems. The precast vs. cast-in-place lifecycle comparison from Module B (Schaefer Inc data) is a critical input: precast structures carry potentially >$100K/year in joint maintenance after year 5, while cast-in-place structures have lower maintenance costs due to fewer joints (Schaefer, 2024).

Precast concrete parking structures in the Pacific Northwest have demonstrated service lives of 40–60 years with proper maintenance. The 20-year analysis horizon captures two full cycles of deck sealing (every 5–7 years at $0.50–1.00/sqft), one cycle of expansion joint replacement (year 15–20 at $50–100/linear foot), and ongoing annual maintenance for lighting, elevator, stormwater facilities, and structural inspection (estimated at $1.50–2.50/sqft/year for the full structure).

At a total annual O&M cost of approximately $180K–$400K for a 120,000–165,000 sqft structure, the 20-year life-cycle cost adds $3.6M–$8.0M to the initial construction cost. For a county parks facility where maintenance budgets are politically constrained, the lifecycle cost advantage of cast-in-place construction may outweigh the initial cost savings of precast — a finding that should be evaluated by the county's asset management and facilities maintenance divisions before the structural system selection is finalized.
