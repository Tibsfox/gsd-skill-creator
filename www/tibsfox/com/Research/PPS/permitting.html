1. SEPA Environmental Review

The State Environmental Policy Act (SEPA, RCW 43.21C) requires environmental review for projects that are not categorically exempt. A parking structure of the proposed scale exceeds the categorical exemption thresholds in WAC 197-11-800 and will require SEPA review administered by Snohomish County as the lead agency. The applicant must submit a SEPA Environmental Checklist (WAC 197-11-960) addressing the full range of environmental elements: earth, air, water, plants, animals, energy and natural resources, environmental health, land and shoreline use, housing, aesthetics, light and glare, recreation, historic and cultural preservation, and transportation.

Per Snohomish County PDS (SCC Chapter 30.61, WAC 197-11), SEPA review occurs concurrently with underlying land use permits. Two threshold determinations are possible: Non-significance (DNS) or Significance (DS). When a DS is issued, the applicant must select a consultant from the county's approved roster to prepare an EIS per SCC 30.61.130. In practice, a third outcome — Mitigated Determination of Non-Significance (MDNS) — is frequently applied, imposing specific mitigation conditions without requiring a full EIS. Given the project's proximity to Puget Sound shoreline, location above a geologically hazardous bluff, adjacency to BNSF Railway, and potential impacts on stormwater and shellfish habitat, an MDNS with mitigation conditions is the most commonly observed outcome for comparable projects. However, the SEPA determination is made by the responsible official and cannot be predicted at the feasibility stage. SEPA appeals carry a $1,500 filing fee per SCC 30.71.050(5)(a)-(d).

Key SEPA considerations unique to this site include the visual impact of a 3-level structure on the bluff-top viewscape, the stormwater quality implications for nearshore shellfish habitat (connecting to Module D and the PPP Mission 1 shellfish closure analysis), and the Tulalip Tribes' potential interest in ground disturbance within the historical territory defined by the Treaty of Point Elliott (1855). The Unanticipated Discovery Plan requirement applies to all ground-disturbing construction within areas of potential cultural resource sensitivity.

2. PDS Building Permit

The primary land use authorization is a Commercial Building Permit issued by Snohomish County Planning and Development Services (PDS) per SCC 30.52A. All construction documents must be prepared and stamped by a Washington State-licensed Professional Engineer (structural) and Architect. The permit application submittal package must include:

  • Building elevations (all four sides plus sections)
  • Floor plans for each level including parking layout, accessible stalls, and circulation
  • Foundation plans and details (informed by Module A geotechnical report)
  • Wall details and connection details (for precast system per Module B)
  • Structural calculations with design criteria summary (loads, SDC, soil parameters)
  • Energy code compliance documentation (limited for open parking structures)

Permit fees are calculated per SCC Table 30.86.400, which uses a tiered valuation schedule based on total project construction cost. For a project in the $13M–$21M range (Module E estimate), permit fees are a significant line item in the soft cost budget. PDS accepts applications through the online Permit Portal or in person at 3000 Rockefeller Avenue, Everett, WA 98201 (phone: 425-388-3311). Plan review typically requires 3–6 months for commercial projects of this complexity, with potential for multiple review cycles if corrections are required.

3. Shoreline Substantial Development Permit (SDP)

The Washington Shoreline Management Act (SMA, RCW 90.58) requires a Shoreline Substantial Development Permit for development within 200 feet of the ordinary high water mark (OHWM) of marine waters. The Picnic Point Park parking area is "very likely within 200 feet of OHWM" based on aerial measurement from the bluff edge to the shoreline, which means an SDP is required unless the site-specific survey from Module A demonstrates otherwise.

The Snohomish County Shoreline Management Program (SMP) designates the Picnic Point shoreline segment as likely Urban Conservancy, which carries the following development constraints:

The SDP review process runs concurrently with SEPA and building permit review but adds approximately 30–60 days for the mandatory public comment period and Department of Ecology review. This additional timeline must be factored into the overall permit pathway.

4. Critical Areas Review

Snohomish County's Critical Areas Regulations (SCC 30.62) protect four main categories: (1) wetlands, fish & wildlife conservation areas, marine waters, and buffers; (2) geologically hazardous areas including erosion/landslide hazard zones and seismic areas; (3) channel migration zones; and (4) critical aquifer recharge areas. The Picnic Point Park site triggers at least two: geologically hazardous areas (the coastal bluff carrying "recent and active" landslide designation per USGS MF-2346) and potential fish & wildlife habitat conservation areas (Picnic Point Creek and the nearshore environment documented in PPP Mission 1).

The critical areas setback for geologically hazardous slopes is established in SCC 30.62B.340: the greater of one-third the bluff height or 50 feet from the top of the slope. For the 150–250-foot bluffs at Picnic Point (Module A), this translates to setbacks of 50–83 feet. The required factors of safety are 1.5 static and 1.1 dynamic (pseudo-static seismic), consistent with the geotechnical parameters established in Module A.

A geotechnical report per SCC 30.62B.140 is required for any development within 200 feet of steep slopes (defined as 33% gradient or steeper). This report must be prepared and stamped by a Washington State-licensed geotechnical engineer or engineering geologist, addressing slope stability, erosion potential, groundwater conditions, and the proposed structure's impact on geologic hazard conditions. The report directly satisfies both the critical areas review requirement and feeds into the Module A geotechnical investigation scope.

A Critical Area Site Plan (CASP) must be prepared showing all identified critical areas and the proposed development footprint, then recorded with the Snohomish County Auditor once approved by county staff. The county's PDS GIS portal provides pre-built cartographic products showing recorded CASPs linked to County Auditor documents — useful for evaluating whether prior critical area determinations exist for the Picnic Point Park parcel.

5. BNSF Railway Coordination

Any construction activity adjacent to the BNSF Railway mainline right-of-way, and particularly any physical connection to the existing pedestrian overpass that spans the tracks, requires coordination with BNSF Railway's Public Projects Team. BNSF maintains strict requirements for structural clearances, construction activity within the right-of-way, crane swing paths over active tracks, and structural modifications to overpass structures. The standard BNSF process for public agency projects requires:

  • Engineering plans submitted in 11" x 17" format, English units only
  • Minimum vertical clearance of 21 feet 6 inches above top of rail for any new or modified structure
  • Construction plans demonstrating "no interruption to Railroad operations"
  • A BNSF Inspector/Coordinator assigned to monitor all construction activity on or adjacent to the right-of-way
  • If construction does not commence within 6 months of the executed agreement, BNSF reserves the right to revise cost estimates

The Meadowdale Beach Park project (completed 2023) provides the closest comparable precedent for BNSF coordination on a Snohomish County parks project. That project required 9 years from feasibility to completion (2014–2023), with BNSF engaged as a collaborative partner from the beginning of the process. Anchor QEA served as prime engineer with Swenson Say Faget providing structural engineering. During the bridge installation phase, only two 24-hour BNSF track closures were required — a remarkably efficient outcome that reflected years of pre-construction coordination and relationship-building with the railroad.

The Meadowdale timeline establishes the realistic expectation for BNSF coordination: this is a multi-year process that operates on the railroad's schedule, not the county's. Early engagement with BNSF's Public Projects group is the single most important critical-path action for the feasibility study.

6. Land Disturbing Activity (LDA) Permit

A Land Disturbing Activity permit is triggered when a project creates more than 2,000 square feet of new impervious surface per SCC 30.63B. The parking structure project will far exceed this threshold (Module D estimates 40,000–55,000 square feet of ground-level footprint alone), making the LDA permit a mandatory component of the permit package. The LDA fee is calculated per SCC 30.86.510 and is payable in three installments: 50% at application, 25% at construction start, and 25% at final inspection.

The LDA permit integrates with the stormwater review (Module D) and requires submission of a stormwater site plan demonstrating compliance with all nine Minimum Requirements of the Snohomish County Drainage Manual. The LDA review runs concurrently with the building permit and is typically completed within the same review cycle.

Capital Planning Context

It is important to note that Picnic Point Park is not currently in the Snohomish County Parks, Recreation, and Volunteer Programs (PRVP) Capital Improvement Program as of the 2022 plan update. No capital plan for parking improvements at this site exists in the county system. The comparable project in the current CIP is Kayak Point Phase 2, which involves parking relocation away from the shoreline — a programmatically similar scope (parking infrastructure at a county beach park) that provides a planning and funding precedent.

Key county contacts for advancing the project into the CIP include Rob Marchand, Senior Park Planner (who managed the Meadowdale Beach and Lord Hill Park projects through the same permitting pathway), and Carol Ohlfs, Principal Park Planner (425-388-6609). Marchand's experience with the Meadowdale BNSF coordination and multi-source grant funding stack (Module E) makes him the most directly relevant county staff contact for this project.
