1. Overflow Parking Staging Sites

Three facilities on or near Picnic Point Road could serve as overflow parking and shuttle staging locations, eliminating the need for visitors to drive to the park’s small lot. All three are within a 5–7 minute shuttle ride of the park entrance.

FacilityAddressDistance to ParkEst. CapacityAvailability
Serene Lake Elementary School 4709 Picnic Point Road, Edmonds, WA 98026 ~0.5–1 mile 40–60 spaces Weekends, holidays, summer break
Alderwood Water & Wastewater District 6315 Picnic Point Road, Edmonds, WA 98026 ~0.3–0.5 miles 20–40 spaces (est.) Weekends, evenings (utility office hours M–F)
Picnic Point Elementary School 5819 140th St SW, Edmonds, WA 98026 ~1.5–2 miles 40–60 spaces Weekends, holidays, summer break

Serene Lake Elementary (Preferred Staging Site)

Serene Lake Elementary is the strongest candidate: it sits directly on Picnic Point Road, minimizing shuttle route complexity. The school is part of the Mukilteo School District (grades K–5) and its parking lot and bus loop are unused on weekends, holidays, and during summer break (late June through early September). The proximity means a shuttle loop of under 2 minutes one-way, enabling high-frequency service with a single vehicle.

Alderwood Water & Wastewater District Facility

The Alderwood Water & Wastewater District (AWWD) facility at 6315 Picnic Point Road is a utility operations building serving Washington’s largest water and sewer district (~305,000 customers). The facility sits between Serene Lake Elementary and the park, making it a natural midpoint stop on any shuttle route. As a public utility with standard business hours (Monday–Friday), the parking lot is available on weekends. A joint-use agreement between Snohomish County Parks and AWWD — two public entities — would be straightforward under the Interlocal Cooperation Act (RCW 39.34). Combined with Serene Lake Elementary, the two sites could provide 60–100 overflow spaces within a mile of the park.

Picnic Point Elementary (Secondary Site)

Picnic Point Elementary at 5819 140th St SW is farther from the park (~1.5–2 miles via 140th St SW to Shelby Road to Picnic Point Road) but offers additional capacity. It would be most useful for high-demand events like the April 2026 City Nature Challenge bioblitz or summer restoration volunteer days when Serene Lake and AWWD lots reach capacity.

Model B: Year-Round Weekend Shuttle

Extending service to year-round weekends (104 days) would serve winter beachcombers, storm-watchers, and restoration volunteers. The Olympic Fly Fishers of Edmonds have organized 9 community restoration events since 2023, many during fall and winter months when the parking lot still fills for events. Year-round estimated cost: $50,000–$110,000/year depending on self-operated vs. contracted service.

3. Weekly Service — Full Transit Extension

A weekday shuttle service would transform Picnic Point Park from a car-dependent destination into a transit-accessible one — addressing equity and environmental goals simultaneously. Three tiers of weekly service expansion:

Tier 1: Peak Season Daily (May–September, 7 days/week)

Extends the weekend shuttle to include weekdays during summer. Serves summer camp programs, senior visitors, tourists, and car-free households. Estimated additional cost over weekend-only: $25,000–$55,000/season (110 additional days of service). This tier could launch in Year 2 after weekend ridership data validates demand.

Tier 2: Year-Round Weekday Peak Hours

Morning and afternoon shuttle runs (7–9 AM, 3–6 PM) connecting Picnic Point Park to Edmonds Station or Lynnwood City Center Station. This serves commuting park staff, restoration volunteers on regular schedules, and dog walkers/joggers who visit daily. Estimated cost: $80,000–$120,000/year for a contracted 5-day, 5-hour/day service.

Tier 3: Full Community Transit Integration

The ultimate goal: incorporate the Picnic Point shuttle into Community Transit’s regular service network as a Zip Shuttle zone or a Route 166 deviation. Community Transit’s Zip Shuttle (on-demand micro-transit) launched in Alderwood (Lynnwood) in October 2022 and was made permanent in October 2023. Three additional zones have since launched in Lake Stevens, Arlington, and Darrington. A Picnic Point zone — connecting the park, Serene Lake Elementary, AWWD, and Picnic Point Elementary to the Route 166 corridor — would integrate with the existing fare structure ($2.50/ride) and booking app (GOIN’).

Precedent for transit agency park service exists: King County Metro’s Trailhead Direct has operated weekend shuttle service from Link light rail stations to trailheads since 2017, accumulating 88,500+ rides over 8 seasons. SDOT funds 50% through the Seattle Transit Measure. A comparable Snohomish County model could be funded through a parks-transit interlocal agreement.

4. Regional Transit Integration

Two regional transit hubs could anchor the Picnic Point shuttle, connecting the park to the broader Puget Sound transit network:

HubDistanceDrive TimeServicesWeekend ServiceAssessment
Lynnwood City Center Station ~6–7 miles 15–18 min Link 1 Line + 2 Line, CT Routes 102/130, parking garage Yes — trains every 8–15 min, 5 AM–1 AM (Sat), 6 AM–midnight (Sun) PREFERRED 7-day all-day Link service
Edmonds Station ~5–6 miles 12–15 min Sounder N, Amtrak, Kingston ferry, CT Routes 102/130/166/909 Limited — Sounder weekdays only; bus Routes 166 runs weekends SECONDARY Ferry + bus connections

Lynnwood City Center Station (opened August 30, 2024) is the stronger integration point. Link light rail runs 7 days/week with 8–10 minute headways during peak, connecting directly to downtown Seattle, UW, Capitol Hill, Sea-Tac Airport, and Bellevue (via 2 Line). A visitor arriving at Sea-Tac could reach Picnic Point Park via Link → shuttle in approximately 75–90 minutes without a car.

Edmonds Station is secondary for shuttle purposes (Sounder is weekday-only) but remains important as a scenic transit gateway: the Edmonds-Kingston ferry and Amtrak Cascades (4 daily roundtrips Seattle–Vancouver BC) serve a tourist/recreation demographic that would value park shuttle access. Community Transit Route 166 already connects Edmonds Station to the Picnic Point area on weekends (34 Saturday trips, 31 Sunday trips).

Recommended Shuttle Route

Primary: Lynnwood City Center Station → 44th Ave W → 164th St SW → Picnic Point Road → Serene Lake Elementary → AWWD facility → Picnic Point Park. ~7 miles, 18–20 minutes.

Local loop (on weekends): Serene Lake Elementary → AWWD → Picnic Point Park → return. ~1 mile, 8–10 minutes round trip. Single vehicle, 10–15 minute frequency.

5. Micro-Transit & On-Demand Service

Community Transit’s Zip Shuttle is an on-demand micro-transit service already operating in Snohomish County. Key parameters:

  • Launched: October 2022 (Alderwood/Lynnwood zone); made permanent October 2023
  • Expansion: Lake Stevens, Arlington, Darrington zones added
  • Hours: 5 AM – 10 PM daily
  • Fare: $2.50/ride (same as local bus; ORCA accepted)
  • Vehicles: Wheelchair-accessible, seats up to 7 passengers
  • Wait time: 10–15 minutes typical
  • Booking: GOIN’ app or phone

A Picnic Point Zip Shuttle zone could serve the triangle between Picnic Point Park, the schools/AWWD, and the Route 166 bus stops on 148th St SW — solving the “last mile” problem without dedicated fixed-route service. This approach would integrate with Community Transit’s existing fare system, app, and fleet operations rather than requiring Snohomish County Parks to operate its own vehicles.

The case for a Picnic Point zone is strengthened by the park’s upcoming City Nature Challenge bioblitz (April 25, 2026), community restoration events, and the fact that no Snohomish County park currently has transit-accessible shuttle service — making Picnic Point a potential pilot site for a county-wide parks micro-transit model.

Key lesson from Trailhead Direct: The 2017 pilot cost ~$44,000 for the entire season with only 900 riders ($48.89/rider). By 2024, ridership reached 9,971 rides per season. The program required 8 seasons to mature. A Picnic Point shuttle should be designed with a 3–5 year growth trajectory, not evaluated on Year 1 ridership alone.

Even the most expensive shuttle model (year-round daily + transit, $300K/year) would cost less per year than the annual debt service on the parking structure. At 4% interest over 20 years, a $17M structure requires ~$1.25M/year in debt service — more than 4x the cost of the most comprehensive shuttle program. The shuttle also avoids the $100K+/year maintenance costs identified for precast structures after year 5 (Module B).

8. Joint-Use Agreement Framework

Washington State provides a robust legal framework for shared-use agreements between public entities:

Interlocal Cooperation Act (RCW 39.34)

Enables any two or more public entities to cooperate through interlocal agreements. This is the standard vehicle for school-parks joint use in Washington State. An agreement between Snohomish County Parks and Mukilteo School District (for Serene Lake and Picnic Point Elementary) and between Snohomish County and AWWD (for the utility facility) would fall under this act.

Mukilteo School District Facility Use Program

The district has an existing 5-tier facility use classification. A county parks partnership would fall under Group C (Priority 4): Governmental organizations. Requirements include:

  • Certificate of insurance: $1,000,000 Combined Single Limit per occurrence
  • Fee schedule per district rates (published annually)
  • Application through district facility use process

Snohomish County Precedent

Glacier Peak High School / Willis Tucker Park: An existing parking pass arrangement between Snohomish County Parks and a local school demonstrates direct county precedent for shared parking between parks and schools. Esperance Park: Snohomish County purchased adjacent school district property specifically to provide parking — demonstrating the county’s willingness to invest in school-adjacent parking solutions for parks.

SCC 30.26.055 — Conditions for Joint Use

Snohomish County Code specifically addresses joint parking: facilities must be within 300 feet of each other, with no substantial overlap in peak parking demand, and governed by a recorded legal instrument (lease, covenant, or agreement) approved by the county and prosecuting attorney, recorded with the Snohomish County Auditor.
