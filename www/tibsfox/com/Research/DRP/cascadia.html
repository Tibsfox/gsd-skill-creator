The Fault

The Cascadia Subduction Zone is a 1,000-kilometer (620-mile) megathrust fault stretching from Cape Mendocino, California to northern Vancouver Island, British Columbia. The Juan de Fuca plate subducts beneath the North American plate at approximately 40 mm/year. The shallow portion of the fault is locked — the two plates are stuck together, accumulating elastic strain that will release in a future earthquake.

How We Know

The Ghost Forest

In the late 1980s, geologist Brian Atwater discovered drowned forests along the Washington coast — trees killed by sudden land subsidence during past earthquakes. Radiocarbon dating showed multiple events over the last several thousand years. The most recent killed trees that were alive in the late 1690s.

The Japanese Orphan Tsunami

Japanese historical records document a tsunami on January 27, 1700 that arrived without a preceding local earthquake — an “orphan tsunami.” Kenji Satake and colleagues traced it back across the Pacific to a source: the Cascadia Subduction Zone. The timing, combined with tree-ring dating and coastal subsidence evidence, pinpoints the event to approximately 9 PM local time on January 26, 1700. Magnitude: approximately 9.0.

Turbidite Record

Chris Goldfinger’s work on deep-sea sediment cores off the Pacific Northwest coast identified 41 turbidites (underwater landslide deposits) in the last 10,000 years. These correlate across multiple sites, indicating 19 full-margin ruptures and 22 southern-segment ruptures. The average recurrence interval for full-margin events: ~500 years. For southern segment events: ~240 years. Both are overdue.

Scenario: Full-Margin M9.0

This is not speculative. This is the documented behavior of this fault.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone has produced at least 19 M8.7–9.2 earthquakes in the last 10,000 years. It will produce another. The only unknowns are when and how much of the fault ruptures.

Shaking

Duration: 3–5 minutes of strong ground motion (a typical earthquake lasts 10–30 seconds). The long duration is due to the fault length — the rupture propagates at ~3 km/s along 1,000 km. Amplification in sedimentary basins (Seattle, Portland, Willamette Valley) extends and intensifies shaking. Expected Modified Mercalli Intensity: VII–IX across western Washington and Oregon.

Tsunami

The seafloor lifts meters during the rupture, displacing the entire water column. Tsunami waves reach the outer coast in 15–30 minutes. Run-up heights: 6–20 meters depending on coastal geometry. Inundation extends hundreds of meters to several kilometers inland on low-lying coasts. Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, and the lower Columbia River are particularly vulnerable.

ShakeAlert: Early Warning

The USGS ShakeAlert system detects earthquakes and sends warnings before strong shaking arrives at your location. For a Cascadia event originating offshore, Seattle could receive 1–3 minutes of warning — enough to Drop-Cover-Hold On, stop elevators, open fire station doors, slow trains, and halt surgical procedures.

ShakeAlert uses a network of seismometers across the West Coast. When P-waves (the faster, weaker first arrival) are detected, the system calculates magnitude and location within seconds and broadcasts alerts through the Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA) system, Android/iOS earthquake warnings, and dedicated receivers at hospitals, fire stations, and transit systems.

shakealert.org — USGS official. Currently serves 50+ million residents in CA, OR, WA.

What You Can Do

Water: 1 gallon per person per day, minimum 14 days

Municipal water will fail. Store water in food-grade containers. A 55-gallon drum per household is reasonable. Water purification tablets (iodine or chlorine dioxide) and a portable filter extend your supply indefinitely from natural sources. The Snohomish River, Lake Stevens, and Silver Lake are within reach of most Everett/Mukilteo residents.

Food: 14 days minimum, no-cook preferred

Canned goods, dried foods, energy bars, peanut butter. Rotate stock every 6 months. A camp stove with fuel extends options. Our food heritage research documents the history of PNW food systems — the same resilience patterns apply: local sourcing, preservation, community sharing.

Communications: Don’t depend on cell towers

Cell networks will be overwhelmed within minutes. Alternatives: NOAA Weather Radio (battery/hand-crank), FRS/GMRS walkie-talkies (no license), ham radio (license required, most capable). Our zero-trust communications page covers the full stack: Meshtastic mesh, JS8Call weak-signal, AREDN high-bandwidth, Winlink email over radio.

Power: Plan for 2 weeks without grid

Solar panels + battery bank for essentials (phone charging, radio, lights). A 100W panel + 500Wh battery covers basics. Generators require fuel — which may be unavailable. Hand-crank and solar are self-sustaining. LED lanterns over candles (fire risk in damaged structures).

Go-bag: 72 hours of survival on your back

Water, food, first aid, medications, documents (copies), cash, flashlight, multi-tool, change of clothes, emergency blanket. One bag per person, stored by the door. Practice carrying it once — if it’s too heavy, remove items until you can actually move with it.

Community: Your neighborhood is your first responder

After a major earthquake, professional responders will be overwhelmed for days. Your neighbors are your emergency team. Know them. Map who has medical training, tools, generators, ham radios. The Map Your Neighborhood program (Washington EMD) provides a simple framework. Our co-op network architecture maps directly onto this: distributed resources, local coordination, federated communication.

Cascadia Rising Exercises

Cascadia Rising is a series of regional emergency exercises testing response to a M9.0 CSZ earthquake. The most recent full-scale exercise involved federal, state, and local agencies across WA, OR, CA, and BC. Key findings: communication failures between jurisdictions, hospital surge capacity inadequate, fuel distribution bottleneck at Portland CEI hub, and transportation network collapse within 24 hours. Each exercise identifies gaps. Fixing them takes years of infrastructure investment.
