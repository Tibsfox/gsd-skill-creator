## 1. PNW Bioregions and Microclimate Zones

The Pacific Northwest spans a dramatic ecological gradient from the Pacific Ocean to the Rocky Mountain foothills. Rainfall ranges from over 3,000 mm/year on the Olympic Peninsula to under 200 mm/year in the rain shadow east of the Cascades. This gradient defines three distinct microclimate zones, each requiring a different native seed palette [1].

### Zone Definitions

| Zone | Region | Annual Rainfall | Soil Type | Summer Drought | Winter Low |
|------|--------|----------------|-----------|----------------|------------|
| Maritime Coast | Western WA/OR coast, fog belt | 1,500-3,000+ mm | Clay, loam; acidic (pH 5.0-6.5) | Minimal (summer fog) | Mild (rarely below -5C) |
| Puget Sound Lowland | Seattle metro, Willamette Valley | 900-1,200 mm | Glacial till, varied urban | Moderate (July-Sept dry) | Moderate (-5 to -10C) |
| Inland Northwest | Eastern WA/OR, northern Idaho | 250-600 mm | Loess, volcanic ash, silt | Severe (June-Sept) | Cold (-15 to -25C) |

## 2. Seed Selection Criteria

Every species included in the Paper That Grows seed palette must pass a multi-gate screening process. No species is included by default; each must earn its place through documented ecological value and manufacturing compatibility [2].

### Mandatory Criteria (All Must Pass)

1. **Non-invasive:** Not listed on Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board Class A, B, or C lists; not listed on Oregon Noxious Weed List; no invasive tendency documented in regional literature
2. **Non-GMO:** USDA certified organic or certified non-GMO seed source. No genetically modified organisms.
3. **Small seed:** Diameter 0.5-3.0 mm. Compatible with paper pulp embedding without sheet deformation.
4. **Ground-placement germination:** Capable of germinating on soil surface without burial, tillage, or specialized pre-treatment beyond natural cold stratification.
5. **PNW native:** Indigenous to the specified microclimate zone. Not merely naturalized or adapted -- actually native.
6. **Documented food chain value:** Supports at least one identified pollinator, bird, or beneficial insect species.

### Desirable Criteria (Weighted Ranking)

| Criterion | Weight | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Bloom timing fills seasonal gap | High | Continuous pollinator forage across growing season |
| Attracts specialist pollinators | High | Specialist species most vulnerable to habitat loss |
| Perennial (self-sustaining) | Medium | One-time planting establishes long-term habitat |
| Drought-tolerant | Medium | Reduces maintenance; critical for Inland NW zone |
| Culturally significant to Coast Salish/Plateau peoples | Medium | Ecological significance often correlates with cultural significance |
| Aesthetic appeal | Low | Newsletter readers more likely to plant attractive species |
| Seed viability > 2 years | Medium | Allows newsletter shelf-life buffer |

> **SAFETY WARNING:** Every species in the seed palette must be verified against the Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board (nwcb.wa.gov) Class A and B lists, the Oregon Department of Agriculture Noxious Weed List, and the USDA PLANTS invasive species database BEFORE inclusion. Introduction of invasive species through seed paper is an ecological and legal liability. This is a BLOCK-level safety gate [3].

## 4. Puget Sound Lowland Seed Palette

### Zone Characteristics

The Puget Sound Lowland encompasses the urban and suburban landscape from Olympia through Seattle to Bellingham, plus the Willamette Valley. Rainfall is moderate (900-1,200 mm/year) with a pronounced summer dry season (July-September). Soils are glacial till, often compacted in urban settings. The zone supports the highest density of potential newsletter subscribers [5].

### Recommended Species

| Species | Common Name | Seed Size (mm) | Bloom Period | Primary Pollinators | Food Chain Value |
|---------|-------------|----------------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| *Camassia quamash* | Common camas | 2.0-3.0 | April-May | Bumblebees, mason bees | Historically significant food plant; major pollinator resource |
| *Symphyotrichum subspicatum* | Douglas aster | 1.0-1.5 | August-October | Migrating butterflies, late-season bees | Critical late-season bloom for overwintering pollinators |
| *Clarkia amoena* | Farewell-to-spring | 0.5-1.0 | June-August | Native bees, hummingbirds | Mid-summer nectar bridge |
| *Achillea millefolium* | Yarrow | < 0.5 | June-September | Parasitoid wasps, hoverflies | Beneficial insect support; natural pest control |
| *Eriophyllum lanatum* | Oregon sunshine | 1.0-2.0 | May-July | Native bees, small butterflies | Drought-tolerant; thrives in disturbed urban soils |
| *Potentilla gracilis* | Slender cinquefoil | 0.5-1.0 | June-August | Small native bees, beetles | Persistent perennial; meadow restoration anchor |
| *Gilia capitata* | Blue-headed gilia | 0.5-1.0 | May-July | Native bees, syrphid flies | Exceptional bee attractant; annual self-seeder |

### Urban Habitat Connectivity

Research demonstrates that yards with 70% or more native plants in the Puget Sound region provide essential wildlife habitat comparable to natural areas. The "Homegrown National Park" concept, advanced by Doug Tallamy (University of Delaware), argues that residential landscapes collectively represent the largest potential habitat restoration surface in North America [6].

The Paper That Grows newsletter creates a passive distribution system for this concept: every newsletter placed on the ground adds native species to the residential landscape without requiring the homeowner to visit a nursery, identify appropriate species, or understand planting technique.

## 5. Inland Northwest Seed Palette

### Zone Characteristics

The Inland Northwest (eastern Washington and Oregon, northern Idaho) is a semi-arid landscape with hot summers (35C+), cold winters (-25C possible), and low annual rainfall (250-600 mm). The dominant vegetation is sagebrush steppe (*Artemisia tridentata*) and bunchgrass prairie. Seed selection must prioritize extreme drought tolerance and cold hardiness [7].

### Recommended Species

| Species | Common Name | Seed Size (mm) | Bloom Period | Primary Pollinators | Food Chain Value |
|---------|-------------|----------------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| *Balsamorhiza sagittata* | Arrowleaf balsamroot | 2.0-3.0 | April-June | Native bees, beetles | Major food source for sage-grouse; deep taproot stabilizes soil |
| *Penstemon speciosus* | Royal penstemon | 1.0-1.5 | May-July | Bumblebees, hummingbirds | Tubular flowers attract long-tongued pollinators |
| *Sphaeralcea munroana* | Munro's globemallow | 1.5-2.0 | May-August | Specialist Diadasia bees | Sole host plant for specialist globe mallow bees |
| *Linum lewisii* | Lewis flax | 1.5-2.5 | May-July | Native bees, syrphid flies | Named for Meriwether Lewis; drought-tolerant perennial |
| *Eriogonum umbellatum* | Sulfur buckwheat | 1.0-2.0 | June-September | Native bees, butterflies, wasps | Late-season nectar; supports 60+ insect species |
| *Phacelia hastata* | Whiteleaf phacelia | 0.5-1.0 | May-July | Mining bees, bumblebees | Exceptional pollen production; dry-site specialist |
| *Achillea millefolium* | Yarrow | < 0.5 | June-September | Parasitoid wasps, hoverflies | Cosmopolitan; succeeds across all three zones |

### Dryland Adaptation

Inland Northwest native plants have evolved specific drought survival strategies that make them uniquely suited to the Paper That Grows model:

- **Deep taproots:** Balsamroot develops taproots up to 2.5 meters that access deep soil moisture
- **Leaf pubescence:** Silver-haired leaves (globemallow, phacelia) reduce transpiration
- **Summer dormancy:** Many species go dormant during July-September drought, resuming growth with fall rains
- **Seed banking:** Seeds persist in soil for years, germinating only when conditions are favorable

These adaptations mean that even if a newsletter placed on dry ground in July does not germinate immediately, the seeds will persist until fall or spring moisture triggers germination. The design accommodates delayed germination [8].

## 6. Pollinator Support and Food Chain Mapping

### PNW Pollinator Crisis

The Pacific Northwest hosts over 600 native bee species, plus hundreds of butterfly, moth, hoverfly, and beetle species that serve as pollinators. These populations are under pressure from habitat loss (urban expansion), pesticide exposure (neonicotinoids), climate change (phenological mismatch), and invasive species competition [9].

Over 30 commercially important crops in the PNW depend on insect pollination, including apples, blueberries, cherries, cranberries, pears, and alfalfa. The annual value of pollination services in Washington State alone exceeds $500 million [10].

### Food Chain Architecture

```
SEED PAPER --> FOOD CHAIN CASCADE
================================================================

  Level 1: PAPER DECOMPOSES
  Substrate --> Soil fungi + bacteria --> Soil organic matter

  Level 2: SEEDS GERMINATE
  Native plants establish --> Root zone stabilization --> Soil improvement

  Level 3: FLOWERS BLOOM
  Nectar + pollen --> Native bees, butterflies, hoverflies
                  --> Hummingbirds (Anna's, rufous, calliope)

  Level 4: POLLINATORS REPRODUCE
  Healthy pollinator populations --> Crop pollination services
                                 --> Seed production in wild plants

  Level 5: BIRDS FEED
  Seeds + insects --> Song sparrows, juncos, chickadees, goldfinches
  Insect larvae --> Swallows, warblers, vireos

  Level 6: ECOSYSTEM SERVICES
  Soil stabilization, water filtration, carbon sequestration,
  pest control (beneficial insects), cultural/aesthetic value
```

### Specialist Pollinator Relationships

Several seed palette species support specialist pollinators -- insects that depend on a single plant genus or family for food or reproduction:

| Plant Species | Specialist Pollinator | Relationship Type | Conservation Status |
|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| *Sidalcea malviflora* | Fender's blue butterfly (*Icaricia icarioides fenderi*) | Sole nectar source | Federally Endangered |
| *Sphaeralcea munroana* | Globe mallow bee (*Diadasia diminuta*) | Sole pollen source | Declining |
| *Camassia quamash* | Camas mining bee (*Andrena* sp.) | Primary pollen source | Data deficient |
| *Penstemon speciosus* | Penstemon specialist bees | Primary pollen source | Varies by species |

Including these plants in seed palettes creates disproportionate conservation value: losing the host plant means losing the specialist pollinator, which may have no alternative food source [11].

## 7. Wild-Type vs. Cultivar Selection

### The Cultivar Problem

Research by Hayes et al. (2025) in *Environmental Entomology* provides direct evidence that wild-type native plants support greater pollinator species richness and functional diversity than named cultivars of the same species. The study compared wild-type PNW native plants against commercially available cultivars across multiple metrics [12]:

| Metric | Wild-Type | Cultivars | Difference |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Total pollinator visits | Baseline | -15 to -30% | Significant reduction |
| Specialist bee visits | Baseline | -40 to -60% | Dramatic reduction |
| Pollen quality (protein content) | Natural range | Often reduced | Breeding for aesthetics reduces nutritional value |
| Nectar volume | Natural range | Variable; often reduced | Double flowers eliminate or reduce nectaries |
| Flower morphology compatibility | Natural | Often altered | Petal changes exclude long-tongued pollinators |

### Implications for Seed Paper

The Paper That Grows seed palette must use **wild-type, locally-sourced seed stock** -- not ornamental cultivars. "Locally-sourced" means seed harvested or propagated from populations within the same ecoregion, preserving local genetic adaptation [13].

Named cultivars bred for flower color, size, or growth habit often reduce pollinator value through:

- **Double flowers:** Extra petals replace stamens and nectaries, eliminating or reducing pollen and nectar
- **Altered flower shape:** Changes to petal geometry exclude specialist pollinators adapted to the wild-type flower
- **Reduced scent:** Breeding for visual appeal often inadvertently reduces volatile organic compounds that attract pollinators
- **Sterility:** Some cultivars are bred sterile, producing no seed for the food chain

> **SAFETY WARNING:** Cultivar seeds may be protected by Plant Variety Protection (PVP) certificates or utility patents, making their propagation without license legally prohibited. Wild-type native seed from public sources (USDA Plant Materials Centers, native plant societies, conservation seed banks) avoids all intellectual property concerns [14].

## 8. Seed Sourcing and Certification

### Recommended Source Categories

| Source Type | Examples | Advantages | Limitations |
|------------|---------|------------|------------|
| USDA Plant Materials Centers | Pullman PMC (WA), Corvallis PMC (OR) | Genetically diverse; free public domain seed | Limited species selection; bulk quantities |
| Native plant nurseries (wholesale) | Sound Native Plants (WA), Heritage Seedlings (OR) | Large variety; local ecotype | Commercial cost; minimum orders |
| Conservation seed banks | Seeds of Success (BLM), Millennium Seed Bank (Kew) | Wild-collected; documented provenance | Research-focused; not always commercially available |
| Native plant societies | Washington Native Plant Society, Oregon Flora Project | Member seed exchanges; hyper-local ecotypes | Small quantities; volunteer-dependent |
| Contract grow-outs | Custom propagation from local wild seed | Exact ecotype; scalable | 1-2 year lead time; contract cost |

### Certification Requirements

All seed used in Paper That Grows products must meet:

1. **Non-GMO verification:** USDA Organic certification OR Non-GMO Project Verified OR documented wild-collected provenance
2. **Noxious weed testing:** Seed lot tested and certified free of noxious weed seed contamination (per state seed law requirements)
3. **Germination testing:** Minimum 60% germination rate verified per ISTA (International Seed Testing Association) rules
4. **Provenance documentation:** Geographic origin of seed collection documented to county level minimum [15]

## 9. Phenological Timing and Climate Adaptation

### Phenological Mismatch

Climate change is shifting bloom timing in the PNW. Spring bloom is advancing 2-5 days per decade on average, but pollinator emergence is advancing at different rates. This "phenological mismatch" means that some pollinators emerge before their food plants bloom, or vice versa [16].

The Paper That Grows seed palette mitigates mismatch risk through:

- **Bloom season diversity:** Each palette includes species covering early spring through late fall
- **Redundancy:** Multiple species bloom in each season, so if one species shifts timing, others provide coverage
- **Annual + perennial mix:** Annuals establish quickly (first season); perennials provide long-term stability

### Climate Zone Shifts

Under moderate climate projections (RCP 4.5), the Puget Sound Lowland zone is expected to experience conditions more similar to today's Inland Northwest within 50 years: hotter summers, reduced summer precipitation, and increased wildfire smoke days. The seed palette includes drought-tolerant species (yarrow, Oregon sunshine, gilia) that will remain viable as conditions shift [17].

```
CLIMATE PROJECTION -- PUGET SOUND LOWLAND
================================================================

  Current (2020s):
  - Summer high avg: 25C
  - Summer rainfall: 50mm (Jul-Sep)
  - Growing season: 220 days

  Projected (2070s, RCP 4.5):
  - Summer high avg: 28-30C
  - Summer rainfall: 30-40mm (Jul-Sep)
  - Growing season: 240-260 days

  Adaptation strategy:
  - Include drought-tolerant species from Inland NW palette
  - Favor deep-rooted perennials over shallow-rooted annuals
  - Maintain bloom-season coverage through redundancy
```

## 10. Ground Placement Germination Requirements

### No-Intervention Design

The Paper That Grows specification calls for germination without any action beyond placing the newsletter on the ground. This means:

1. **No burial required:** Seeds must germinate on the soil surface, not in a furrow
2. **No watering required:** PNW spring rainfall (March-May, 150-300mm) provides adequate moisture
3. **No soil amendment:** Paper substrate decomposes directly on existing soil
4. **No weeding:** Selected species must tolerate competition from existing vegetation
5. **No transplanting:** Seeds germinate in place; no seedling handling

### Surface Germination Biology

Not all seeds germinate on the soil surface. Seeds requiring light for germination (photoblastic) do; seeds requiring darkness (skotoblastic) do not. The Paper That Grows palette exclusively selects species whose seeds germinate readily at or near the soil surface [18].

| Germination Type | Light Requirement | Seed Paper Compatibility | Example Species |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Photoblastic (positive) | Light triggers germination | Excellent -- surface placement works | Yarrow, poppy, gilia |
| Neutral | Germinates in light or dark | Good -- surface or shallow coverage | Camas, aster, clarkia |
| Skotoblastic (negative) | Darkness required | Poor -- requires burial; exclude | Penstemon (some species) |

### Paper as Germination Medium

The decomposing paper substrate creates a microenvironment favorable to germination:

- **Moisture retention:** Paper absorbs and holds rainwater against the seed surface
- **Temperature moderation:** Paper mulch layer moderates soil surface temperature extremes
- **Weed suppression:** Paper layer temporarily suppresses competing weed germination
- **Nutrient release:** Decomposing cellulose adds organic carbon to the soil surface

This combination means that seeds in paper actually have a germination advantage over bare-soil surface planting in some conditions. The paper acts as both seed carrier and germination medium [19].

## 11. Noxious Weed Exclusion Protocol

### Verification Process

Before any species is included in a seed palette, it must be checked against:

1. **Washington State Noxious Weed Control Board** (nwcb.wa.gov) -- Class A, B, C lists
2. **Oregon Department of Agriculture Noxious Weed Control Program** -- A, B, T lists
3. **USDA PLANTS Database** -- National invasive species status
4. **Pacific Northwest Weed Management Handbook** -- Regional weed concern species
5. **Local county-level weed boards** -- County-specific noxious weed designations

### Current Palette Status

All 21 species across three palettes (7 per zone) have been verified as NOT listed on any state noxious weed list as of March 2026. Annual re-verification is required, as noxious weed lists are updated periodically when new invasive species are identified [20].

### Contamination Prevention

Seed lot contamination (noxious weed seeds mixed into the intended seed lot) is a risk at any production scale. Mitigation:

- Purchase from certified seed dealers with lot-level weed testing
- Require seed analysis certificates per state seed law (minimum 98% purity)
- Visual inspection of seed lots before integration into pulp
- Maintain traceability: every production run linked to specific seed lot numbers
