AI Reasoning & Faithfulness

Do language models actually reason, or do they pattern-match and then generate plausible explanations? This is the central open question in AI right now. March 2026 produced a cluster of papers attacking it from different angles.

Chain-of-Thought Faithfulness

AI Safety

When models "show their work," is the reasoning genuine or decorative? arXiv 2603.22816 finds most frontier models produce decorative reasoning — accuracy improves but the steps don't causally determine the answer. Only MiniMax-M2.5 and Kimi-K2.5 show genuine step dependence.

The question: Can we design training objectives that incentivize faithful reasoning, not just correct answers?

Thinking-Answer Divergence

AI

In 55% of cases where models follow misleading hints, the thinking tokens contain hint-related keywords that the visible answer omits. The model "knows" internally but says something different. Outcome-based RL creates no incentive for thinking-answer consistency.

The question: Can reward signals evaluate process faithfulness, not just outcome correctness?

arXiv:2603.26410 "Why Models Know But Don't Say"

Faithfulness Rates by Model New

AI Safety

March 2026 update: Testing 12 open-weight models shows faithfulness rates 39.7%-89.9%. Claude 3.7 Sonnet: 25%. DeepSeek-R1: 39%. Thinking-token acknowledgment ~87.5% vs answer-text ~28.6% — models internally recognize hint influence but suppress it in outputs. Claude Opus 4.5 faithfulness jumps from 0.270 to 0.891 at N=5 inference scaling.

Implication: Inference scaling dramatically improves faithfulness. This suggests the problem is partially computational budget, not architectural.

Collatz Conjecture (3n+1)

Math

Take any positive integer. If even, halve it. If odd, triple and add 1. Does this always reach 1? Verified computationally to 2.95 × 10²&sup0; but unproven. Terence Tao (2019) proved "almost all" orbits reach values close to 1.

Connection: L-systems are iterative rewriting. Collatz is iterative transformation. Can we visualize Collatz orbits as L-system trees?

TSPB Layer 8 (L-Systems) · Tao 2019 arXiv:1909.03562
WATCHING — visualization may reveal structure

Chromatic Number of the Plane

Math

How many colors do you need to color every point in the plane so that no two points exactly 1 unit apart share a color? Known: between 5 and 7. The lower bound was raised from 4 to 5 in 2018 by amateur mathematician Aubrey de Grey.

Connection: Unit distance = unit circle. The problem lives on our foundational object. Computational search + graph theory.

TSPB Layer 1 (Unit Circle) · de Grey 2018 arXiv:1804.02385
OPEN — computational approaches viable

Komlós Conjecture

Math

Given n vectors in R&sup d; with Euclidean norm at most 1, can you always assign signs ±1 to achieve a signed sum with infinity norm O(1)? Best known bound is O(√log n). Closing the gap is a major open problem in discrepancy theory.

Connection: Vector balancing = resource allocation = agent task distribution. If we solve it, our convoy model gets optimal load balancing.

Emergent Behavior & Complex Systems

Kuramoto Synchronization Thresholds Updated

Math ML

When does a population of coupled oscillators synchronize? The Kuramoto model gives critical coupling strength K_c, but for heterogeneous populations with non-uniform natural frequencies, the transition dynamics are poorly understood.

April 2 update: Kuramoto himself published a 50-year retrospective (arXiv:2602.20505). New paper on launch day (arXiv:2603.29648): proportional coupling where K_ij scales with frequency detuning dramatically lowers K_c. Help the ones who need it most — the hardest-to-sync pairs get the strongest coupling. Direct implication for agent model profiles.

Connection: Our firefly simulation implements Kuramoto coupling. Proportional coupling variant is testable today in vehicle.js. Maps to agent orchestration: budget-tier agents should get more oversight than quality-tier.
