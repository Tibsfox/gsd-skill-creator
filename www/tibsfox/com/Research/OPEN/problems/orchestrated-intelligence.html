The Problem

How do you coordinate multiple AI agents working on the same problem? Fully centralized systems become bottlenecks. Fully distributed systems lose coherence. The engineering challenge is finding the right balance: enough central coordination to maintain consistency, enough agent autonomy to achieve parallelism.

This is not a new problem -- distributed computing has studied it for decades. But AI agents add a new dimension: they are non-deterministic, capable of creative problem-solving, and prone to failure modes that traditional distributed systems do not exhibit (hallucination, sycophancy, context drift). The orchestration layer must handle both the classical distributed systems problems (consensus, fault tolerance, load balancing) and the AI-specific problems (faithfulness, alignment, capability boundaries).

Two protocol families are converging on this space: MCP (Model Context Protocol) for tool access and context sharing, and A2A (Agent-to-Agent) for peer coordination. The research question is whether these protocols are sufficient for production-scale orchestration, or whether higher-level abstractions are needed.

Latest Research

Multi-Agent Orchestration: A Survey

arXiv:2601.13671

Comprehensive survey of multi-agent orchestration patterns as of early 2026. Identifies five architectural paradigms: centralized coordinator, peer mesh, hierarchical delegation, blackboard systems, and market-based allocation. Each has tradeoffs in latency, fault tolerance, and coherence. No single paradigm dominates across all dimensions -- the choice depends on task structure, agent capabilities, and reliability requirements.

AgenticCyOps: Operational Multi-Agent Systems

arXiv:2603.09134

Case study of multi-agent systems deployed for cybersecurity operations. Documents the practical challenges of agent coordination at scale: context window exhaustion, cascading failures when one agent's error propagates through the system, and the "telephone problem" where information degrades as it passes between agents. Proposes operational patterns including circuit breakers, context checkpoints, and agent health monitoring.

Practical Guide to Agentic AI

arXiv:2602.10122

Engineering-focused guide to building multi-agent systems that actually work in production. Key findings: most failures come from poor task decomposition (not from the agents themselves), state management is the hardest engineering problem, and the filesystem-as-communication-bus pattern outperforms message-passing for most workloads. Recommends starting with simple coordinator patterns and adding complexity only when measured bottlenecks demand it.

Our Connection

We are not watching this problem from the sidelines. We are building and operating a distributed intelligence system every day. The Gastown chipset is our implementation of ODI, and it works. The question is whether our patterns generalize, and whether the research community's formalisms can improve our operational system.

  • Gastown Chipset: Mayor (coordinator), Polecat (worker), Witness (observer), Refinery (merge queue). This is a hierarchical delegation architecture with health monitoring -- one of the five paradigms identified in the survey literature.
  • ACE M3: Agent Compute Engine milestone focused on multi-agent coordination. The research literature provides vocabulary and formalism for patterns we discovered empirically.
  • MCP + A2A Protocols: We use MCP for tool access. A2A for peer coordination maps to our mail-async and nudge-sync skills. The protocol convergence validates our architecture.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

A formal theory of multi-agent AI orchestration that provides provable guarantees about system-level properties (consistency, liveness, safety) given assumptions about individual agent capabilities and failure modes. This would be the distributed systems equivalent of the CAP theorem but for AI agent coordination.

A practical breakthrough would be a self-organizing orchestration layer that automatically discovers the optimal coordination pattern for a given task structure -- choosing between centralized, hierarchical, and mesh coordination based on measured task characteristics, rather than requiring the engineer to choose the architecture upfront.
