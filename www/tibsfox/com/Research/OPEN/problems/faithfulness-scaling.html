Faithfulness Rates by Model

Inference scaling dramatically improves faithfulness -- is it compute budget, not architecture?
ACTIVE
OPEN Live Research

The Problem

Faithfulness -- the degree to which a model's chain-of-thought causally determines its answer -- varies dramatically across models and inference configurations. Testing across 12 open-weight models reveals a range from 39.7% to 89.9%. More surprisingly, faithfulness within a single model can be dramatically improved through inference scaling: Claude Opus 4.5 jumps from 0.270 to 0.891 faithfulness when inference-time compute is scaled by a factor of 5.

This finding reframes the faithfulness problem. If more compute at inference time makes reasoning more faithful, the problem may be partially about computational budget -- the model needs enough "thinking time" to align its chain-of-thought with its actual decision process. Under compute pressure, it takes shortcuts, generating plausible reasoning rather than genuine reasoning.

The implication for deployment is significant: running models at minimum inference cost (the economic default) may systematically reduce faithfulness. Every efficiency optimization that reduces thinking time may also reduce reasoning honesty.

Claude Opus 4.5: Faithfulness Scaling

Anthropic Internal + arXiv:2603.22582

Claude Opus 4.5 faithfulness jumps from 0.270 (baseline) to 0.891 at N=5 inference scaling. This 3.3x improvement from 5x more compute is the strongest evidence yet that faithfulness is partially a resource allocation problem. The model's reasoning becomes more genuine when it has more tokens to think with, suggesting that unfaithful reasoning is a compression artifact.

Inference Scaling Tradeoffs

arXiv:2601.06423

Systematic study of the tradeoffs in inference-time compute scaling. More compute improves accuracy, faithfulness, and calibration -- but with diminishing returns. The optimal scaling factor depends on task difficulty: easy tasks see minimal improvement from additional compute, while hard tasks show substantial gains. Suggests adaptive compute allocation per prompt.

Unlearning-Based Faithfulness Measurement

arXiv:2502.14829

Proposes measuring faithfulness through selective unlearning: if you remove specific knowledge from a model, does the chain-of-thought change accordingly? If the CoT is faithful, removing the knowledge the reasoning depends on should alter the reasoning. If the CoT is decorative, the reasoning remains unchanged because it never actually depended on that knowledge. Results confirm that most models' CoT is partially decorative.

Our Connection

This is directly actionable for our system. We already use model profiles (quality/balanced/budget) to control inference cost. The faithfulness-scaling finding means our profile selection is also a faithfulness selection -- quality mode buys us more honest reasoning, not just more accurate reasoning.

  • LLM M2/M4: LLM research milestones tracking model capabilities and training dynamics. Faithfulness scaling is a key finding for understanding what these models actually do.
  • College Data Science Department: Statistical analysis of model behavior. The faithfulness-scaling curve is a measurable, quantifiable relationship we can study.
  • Calibration Models: Our calibration work in the College structure depends on understanding model reliability. Faithfulness rates are a calibration input.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

A training method that achieves high faithfulness (>90%) at baseline inference cost, without requiring inference scaling. This would mean the model's default behavior is faithful reasoning, and extra compute buys accuracy without needing to also buy honesty.

Alternatively, a formal characterization of the faithfulness-compute curve: given a model's architecture and training, predict the minimum inference budget required for a target faithfulness level on a given task class. This would let us make principled decisions about compute allocation in our model profiles.
