The simplest unsolved problem in mathematics

The Problem

Take any positive integer n. Apply this rule repeatedly:

If n is even: n → n/2
If n is odd: n → 3n + 1

The Collatz conjecture states that no matter what positive integer you start with, you will always eventually reach 1. Once you reach 1, the sequence cycles: 1 → 4 → 2 → 1.

Example: starting at 7: 7 → 22 → 11 → 34 → 17 → 52 → 26 → 13 → 40 → 20 → 10 → 5 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1.

This has been verified computationally for all integers up to 2.95 × 1020. Terence Tao proved in 2019 that "almost all" Collatz orbits attain almost bounded values — but the full conjecture remains wide open. Paul Erdős said: "Mathematics may not be ready for such problems."

Latest Research

Collatz High Cycles Do Not Exist

Discrete Mathematics, 2025

Eliminates the possibility of high-altitude periodic cycles in the Collatz sequence. Shows that any non-trivial cycle (other than 4-2-1) would require astronomically large starting values, effectively ruling out an entire class of counterexamples. This constrains where a proof or counterexample could live.

Structural Reduction to One-Bit Orbit Mixing

arXiv:2603.25753 · March 2026

Reduces the Collatz problem to a question about mixing properties of individual bit positions in the binary representation. If you can prove that the least significant bit behaves "randomly enough" under iteration, the conjecture follows. Connects Collatz dynamics to ergodic theory and symbolic dynamics.

Human-LLM Collaboration on Collatz

arXiv:2603.11066 · March 2026

Documents a structured collaboration between human mathematicians and language models working on the Collatz conjecture. Finds that LLMs are most useful for generating conjectures about sub-problems, exploring parameter spaces, and identifying structural patterns — but require human guidance to avoid circular reasoning. Directly relevant to our AI-for-math-discovery work.

Our Connection

TSPB Math Collatz connects to our work through TSPB Layer 8 (L-Systems) and the College Mathematics department.

L-System visualization: L-systems are iterative rewriting systems — you start with a symbol and apply production rules repeatedly. Collatz is an iterative transformation — you start with a number and apply the 3n+1 rule repeatedly. The structural parallel is exact. Collatz orbits can be visualized as branching trees where each node is a number and each branch represents the even/odd decision. The resulting tree structures resemble L-system fractals.

Iterative rewriting: Both L-systems and Collatz sequences exhibit self-similar structure at different scales. The stopping time distribution (how many steps to reach 1) has fractal properties that may be amenable to L-system analysis.

The Human-LLM collaboration paper (arXiv:2603.11066) directly validates our approach in the AIH project — using AI as a mathematical collaborator, not a replacement for human insight.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Full proof: Showing that every positive integer eventually reaches 1. This would likely require connecting Collatz dynamics to a well-understood area of mathematics (ergodic theory, p-adic analysis, or dynamical systems). Tao's 2019 result is the strongest partial progress, proving the conjecture holds for "almost all" integers in a logarithmic density sense.

Counterexample: Finding a starting value that either diverges to infinity or enters a cycle other than 4-2-1. Given computational verification to 2.95 × 1020, any counterexample would be astronomically large.

Structural reduction: Showing that Collatz is equivalent to a known-hard or known-decidable problem in another domain. The arXiv:2603.25753 paper's reduction to one-bit orbit mixing is a step in this direction — if the mixing property can be proved, the conjecture follows.

Our angle: We are unlikely to prove the conjecture directly. But we can contribute by (1) visualizing Collatz orbits as L-system trees to reveal structural patterns, (2) using the math coprocessor for computational experiments on orbit statistics, and (3) documenting what approaches don't work, which is valuable data.
