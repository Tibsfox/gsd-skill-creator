The Problem

Can AI systems — particularly large language models — make meaningful contributions to mathematical research? Not just solve textbook problems, but discover new patterns, generate novel conjectures, and assist in proving theorems that humans have struggled with?

The evidence so far is mixed. The FrontierMath benchmark, designed with research-level mathematics problems, finds that current frontier models solve less than 2% of problems. This is humbling. Yet AI has already contributed to mathematical discovery in specific domains: DeepMind's AlphaGeometry solved IMO geometry problems, and LLM-assisted pattern recognition has found new identities in combinatorics and knot theory.

The open question is not "can AI do math" (clearly it struggles) but rather: what is the right human-AI collaboration model for mathematical research? Where are LLMs strongest, and where do they fail systematically?

Latest Research

FrontierMath Benchmark

Epoch AI, 2025 · Research-level mathematics evaluation

A benchmark of research-level mathematics problems designed to be unsolvable by pattern matching alone. Problems require multi-step reasoning, novel proof techniques, and deep domain knowledge. Current frontier models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) solve less than 2% of problems. This establishes a clear baseline: LLMs are not yet capable of independent mathematical research at the research frontier. The gap between "can solve competition math" and "can do research math" is enormous.

Human-LLM Collaboration on Collatz

arXiv:2603.11066 · March 2026

Documents a structured collaboration between human mathematicians and LLMs on the Collatz conjecture. Key findings: LLMs are most useful for (1) generating conjectures about sub-problems, (2) exploring large parameter spaces, (3) identifying structural patterns in data, and (4) translating between mathematical formalisms. They fail at (1) multi-step logical reasoning, (2) novel proof construction, and (3) maintaining consistency across long arguments. The collaboration model matters more than the model's raw capability.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

FrontierMath solve rate >25%: A model that reliably solves a quarter of research-level mathematics problems would demonstrate genuine mathematical reasoning, not just pattern matching. This would fundamentally change how mathematics is done.

AI-discovered theorem: An AI system generating a novel, non-trivial mathematical theorem with a correct proof that was not previously known. This has arguably happened in narrow domains (AlphaGeometry), but not yet in mainstream mathematics.

Automated cross-reference discovery: An AI system that can read a new paper and automatically identify non-obvious connections to existing work — connections that human experts would agree are meaningful and surprising. Our cross-reference graph is a training dataset for this capability.

Our angle: We contribute by (1) maintaining and growing the cross-reference graph as a dataset for computational pattern discovery, (2) documenting what works and doesn't work in human-AI math collaboration through our sweep process, (3) building the Rosetta Core translation engine that operationalizes cross-domain connection-finding, and (4) running the experiment at scale — 190+ research projects provide enough data to study the process itself.
