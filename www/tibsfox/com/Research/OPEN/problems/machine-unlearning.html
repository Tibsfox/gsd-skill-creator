Machine Unlearning

Can a model truly forget what it has learned?
Watching
OPEN Live Research

The Problem

Once a machine learning model has been trained on data, can you make it forget specific training examples without retraining from scratch? This is machine unlearning — the inverse of learning.

The need is urgent. GDPR's "right to erasure" requires organizations to delete personal data on request. If that data was used to train a model, you need to remove its influence. Retraining from scratch is computationally prohibitive for large models (GPT-4 training cost: ~$100M). So we need surgical removal — unlearning specific data points while preserving everything else the model knows.

Current approaches approximate unlearning through gradient ascent (increasing loss on target data), influence function estimation, or parameter isolation. But none provide formal guarantees that the data's influence is truly gone.

Key challenge: Quantization can "un-unlearn" deleted knowledge. When a model is quantized (reduced to lower precision for deployment), the compression process can recover information that was supposedly removed. The deleted knowledge is latent in the weight structure and resurfaces when the representation changes. This means unlearning at full precision doesn't guarantee unlearning at deployment precision.

Latest Research

Source-Free Certified Unlearning

CVPR 2025 · UC Riverside

Proposes a method for certified machine unlearning that does not require access to the original training data — only the trained model and the data to be forgotten. Uses a combination of Fisher information and noise calibration to provide probabilistic guarantees. Achieves certified unlearning in vision models without source data access, which is critical for real-world deployments where the original training set may be unavailable or too large to reload. The certification gives mathematical bounds on how much influence remains.

SoK: LLM Unlearning

arXiv:2506.09227 · Systematization of Knowledge

Comprehensive systematization of knowledge for machine unlearning in large language models. Categorizes approaches into exact unlearning (retraining-based, provably complete but expensive), approximate unlearning (gradient-based, fast but incomplete), and knowledge editing (targeted weight modifications). Key finding: no current method achieves all three desiderata simultaneously — completeness (truly forgotten), utility preservation (everything else still works), and efficiency (cheaper than retraining). The field is converging on approximate methods with probabilistic guarantees.

Comprehensive Survey: 180+ Papers

arXiv:2503.01854 · March 2025

Surveys over 180 papers on machine unlearning, organizing them by technique (exact, approximate, knowledge editing), domain (vision, NLP, tabular, graphs), and evaluation methodology. Identifies the critical gap: evaluation metrics for unlearning are themselves unreliable. Membership inference attacks (the standard test for whether data was "forgotten") have high false positive rates. We may not even be able to reliably measure whether unlearning worked. Proposes a taxonomy of evaluation approaches and identifies open challenges.

Our Connection

Trust AI Safety Machine unlearning connects to our work through structural parallels in the trust system, hook-persistence, and AIH M5 (Alignment/Safety).

Trust system (relationship archive/purge): Our trust system has explicit relationship lifecycle management — relationships can be archived (soft removal, metadata preserved) or purged (hard removal, all data deleted). This is the same conceptual problem as machine unlearning: how do you remove a relationship's influence from the system while preserving the integrity of everything else? Our trust-relationship.ts implements this with deterministic state transitions, but the question of whether archived influence truly disappears is analogous to the quantization un-unlearning problem.

Hook-persistence (state removal): The hook-persistence system manages per-agent state that must be cleanly removable. When an agent's work item is completed or abandoned, its hooks must be cleanly purged. Incomplete state removal creates ghost state — the agent system equivalent of un-unlearned knowledge.

AIH M5 (Alignment/Safety): The AIH project M5 milestone covers AI alignment and safety, where machine unlearning is a critical capability. The ability to remove dangerous capabilities from a deployed model — without retraining — is essential for AI safety. The quantization attack (un-unlearning via precision reduction) is particularly concerning because it means deployment-time transformations can recover supposedly removed capabilities.

College Logic and Data Science departments: Unlearning connects to formal verification (can you prove something was forgotten?), information theory (how much information about the training data is encoded in the weights?), and privacy (differential privacy as a framework for measuring unlearning completeness).

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Provably complete unlearning without retraining: A method that provides formal guarantees (not just probabilistic bounds) that specific training data has been completely removed from a model's weights, at a cost substantially less than retraining. This is the holy grail — current methods are either complete but expensive (retraining) or cheap but incomplete (gradient ascent).

Quantization-robust unlearning: A method that remains effective after quantization, distillation, or other model compression techniques. The current vulnerability — quantization recovering "unlearned" knowledge — is a fundamental barrier to deploying unlearning in production. Solving this requires understanding how knowledge is distributed across weight precision levels.

Reliable evaluation metrics: Better ways to measure whether unlearning actually worked. Current membership inference attacks have high false positive rates. We need ground-truth evaluation methods that don't rely on adversarial probing — perhaps information-theoretic bounds on residual influence.

Our angle: We contribute by (1) implementing clean state removal patterns in the trust system and hook-persistence that could serve as architectural templates for deterministic unlearning, (2) studying the structural parallel between relationship purging and weight-level unlearning, (3) tracking the field through AIH M5 to understand implications for AI safety, and (4) testing whether our archive/purge lifecycle can be made formally verifiable — proving that purged relationships leave no trace.
