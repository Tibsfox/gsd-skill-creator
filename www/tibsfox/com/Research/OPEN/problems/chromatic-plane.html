The Problem

Color every point in the Euclidean plane such that no two points exactly 1 unit apart receive the same color. What is the minimum number of colors required?

χ(R²) = ?     Known: 5 ≤ χ(R²) ≤ 7

This is the Hadwiger–Nelson problem, posed in 1950. For over 60 years, the best known bounds were 4 ≤ χ ≤ 7. The lower bound of 4 came from the Moser spindle (7 vertices), and the upper bound of 7 comes from a hexagonal tiling that has held since 1950.

In 2018, amateur mathematician Aubrey de Grey shattered the lower bound by constructing a unit-distance graph requiring 5 colors. His original graph had 1,581 vertices. Subsequent work by Marijn Heule using SAT solvers reduced this to a 553-vertex graph that still requires 5 colors.

The key insight: unit distance IS the unit circle. Every point in the plane has a circle of radius 1 around it, and every point on that circle must have a different color. The problem lives on our foundational mathematical object.

Latest Research

SAT Solver Reduction to 553 Vertices

Heule, 2024 · SAT-based graph coloring

Marijn Heule applied modern SAT solvers to systematically reduce de Grey's 1,581-vertex 5-chromatic unit-distance graph down to 553 vertices. SAT solvers are emerging as the most promising computational tool for this problem — they can explore vast combinatorial spaces and produce machine-verifiable proofs. The reduction from 1,581 to 553 suggests further compression may be possible, potentially revealing structural reasons why 5 colors are necessary.

Six-Coloring Extensions

Pokutta et al., 2024 · Computational geometry

Explores what happens when you allow 6 colors instead of the minimum. Shows that 6-colorings have significantly more structure and symmetry than 5-colorings, suggesting the true chromatic number may be closer to 5 than 7. The gap between 5 and 7 remains the central open question, and understanding the 6-coloring landscape constrains where the answer can be.

Chromatic Number of Spacetime

arXiv:2308.16885 · 2023

Extends the Hadwiger–Nelson problem to Minkowski spacetime, asking: what is the chromatic number when "distance" is defined by the Lorentzian metric instead of Euclidean? The problem changes dramatically because the Lorentz metric is not positive definite. Shows that the spacetime version has different bounds and connects to relativistic geometry — a direction no one expected.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Raising the lower bound to 6: Constructing a unit-distance graph that provably requires 6 colors. This would narrow the gap to 6–7 and would likely require either much larger graphs or fundamentally new construction techniques. SAT solvers are the most promising path here.

Lowering the upper bound to 6: Proving that 6 colors always suffice. This would require a new coloring scheme for the entire plane, replacing the 1950 hexagonal tiling. The Pokutta six-coloring work is exploring this direction.

Resolving the exact value: Proving χ(R²) = 5, 6, or 7. Any of these would close a 75-year-old problem. Most experts lean toward 5 or 7, with 6 considered least likely.

Our angle: This problem is primarily computational at the frontier. Contributions could come from (1) developing better SAT encoding strategies for unit-distance graphs, (2) using GPU-accelerated search for small 5-chromatic graphs, or (3) exploring the spacetime variant as a fresh direction where less is known.
