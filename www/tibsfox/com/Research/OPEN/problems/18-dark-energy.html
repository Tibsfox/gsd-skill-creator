The Problem

Quantum field theory predicts the vacuum has an energy density. Cutting off at the Planck scale:

ρvacuum (QFT) ~ 10113 J/m³

The observed dark energy density (from supernovae, CMB, and large-scale structure):

ρdark (observed) ~ 10−9 J/m³     ratio: 10122

This is the cosmological constant problem — a 120 orders of magnitude discrepancy, the worst prediction in the history of physics. Something must cancel the QFT prediction to extraordinary precision, leaving the tiny residual we observe. No mechanism for this near-perfect cancellation is known.

The coincidence problem asks: why do dark energy and dark matter have comparable densities today? Dark energy density is roughly constant while dark matter dilutes as the universe expands. That we live at the cosmic moment when they are comparable seems coincidental — unless there is a dynamical reason.

The cosmological constant Λ ≈ 1.11 × 10−52 m−2 appears in Einstein's field equations — the same equations governing Artemis II's trajectory.

Latest Research

1998 Supernova Discovery of Cosmic Acceleration

Perlmutter et al. 1999, Riess et al. 1998 · Nobel Prize 2011

Two independent teams measured Type Ia supernovae at cosmological distances and found them fainter (farther) than expected. The universe's expansion is accelerating. This established ΛCDM as the concordance cosmology: 68% dark energy, 27% dark matter, 5% ordinary matter. The measurement is now precise to ~1%; the explanation is absent.

The Hubble Tension

Riess et al. 2022, Planck 2020 · 4–5σ discrepancy

Early-universe measurements (CMB, Planck: H0 = 67.4 ± 0.5 km/s/Mpc) disagree with late-universe measurements (Cepheid/supernova ladder: H0 = 73.2 ± 1.3 km/s/Mpc) at 4–5σ. This may indicate new physics beyond ΛCDM — potentially evolving dark energy.

Weinberg's Cosmological Constant Review

Rev. Mod. Phys. 61 (1989) · Foundational

Crystallized the cosmological constant problem as a precision fine-tuning problem. Weinberg's 1987 anthropic prediction (the value of Λ must be small enough to permit galaxy formation) correctly predicted Λ ≠ 0 before it was observed. In a landscape of ~10500 string theory vacua, we necessarily find ourselves in a universe with small Λ. Many physicists consider this unsatisfying.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Explaining the value of Λ: A first-principles calculation from quantum gravity that predicts the observed dark energy density. This would simultaneously resolve the cosmological constant problem and provide the first experimental confirmation of quantum gravity.

Detecting dark energy evolution: Measuring the equation of state w ≠ −1 at high significance, showing dark energy is not a constant but a dynamical field (quintessence). DESI and Euclid are actively pursuing this.

Resolving the Hubble tension: New physics that explains the discrepancy between early-universe and late-universe H0 measurements. This could indicate dark energy evolution, new particles, or modified gravity.
