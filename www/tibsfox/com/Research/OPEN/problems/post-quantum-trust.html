Cryptographic foundations for human-AI trust in a post-quantum world

The Problem

AI agent systems require trust delegation: a human authorizes an agent to act on their behalf, within bounds. Current delegation frameworks (OAuth 2.0, TLS client certificates, Macaroons) were designed for deterministic software making predictable API calls. Probabilistic AI agents break these assumptions — they make unpredictable tool calls, generate novel outputs, and may drift from their delegated scope in ways that deterministic software never could.

Compounding this, quantum computing threatens the cryptographic foundations of existing trust systems. RSA and ECDSA — the algorithms that sign delegation certificates today — will be broken by sufficiently large quantum computers running Shor's algorithm. Post-quantum cryptography (NIST FIPS 203/204/205) provides replacements, but these have not been integrated into AI trust delegation protocols.

The intersection is underexplored: how do you build a trust delegation system that is both quantum-resistant and designed for the probabilistic, drifting nature of AI agents? This requires new protocol design, not just swapping algorithms.

Latest Research

AITH: Post-Quantum Continuous Delegation for Human-AI Trust

arXiv:2604.07695 · Chen, April 2026

The first protocol specifically designed for post-quantum human-AI trust delegation. Uses ML-DSA-87 (FIPS 204, NIST Level 5) for signing continuous delegation certificates rather than per-operation authorization. Achieves 4.7M boundary checks per second with zero cryptographic overhead on the critical path through a six-check boundary engine that enforces hard constraints, rate limits, and escalation triggers. Includes push-based revocation within one second and a three-tier SHA-256 audit chain. Formally verified via Tamarin Prover under the Dolev-Yao model (5 security theorems). Simulation of 100,000 operations: 79.5% autonomous, 6.1% human escalation, 14.4% blocked.

Our trust system is the pre-quantum version of this. The trust-relationship.ts + trust-relationship-provider.ts subsystem (95 tests) implements earned trust with escalation and revocation. AITH provides the post-quantum cryptographic upgrade path for what we already do operationally. Their boundary engine maps to our GUPP constraints; their escalation triggers map to our watchdog; their audit chain maps to our beads-state persistence.

Our Connection

Trust Crypto AI This problem sits at the intersection of our trust system and our security-hygiene skill.

  • Trust System (Stage 1 complete): trust-relationship.ts implements trust levels, delegation bounds, and revocation. Currently uses no cryptographic signing — trust is enforced by the runtime, not by cryptographic proofs. AITH shows what a cryptographically-backed version looks like.

AITH's six-check boundary engine formalizes this pattern with cryptographic attestation. The 79.5% autonomous / 6.1% escalation ratio closely mirrors our operational experience.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

A production-ready post-quantum trust delegation protocol integrated into an actual AI agent framework, with sub-millisecond overhead per tool call, formal verification of the core security properties, and a migration path from current TLS/OAuth-based systems. The protocol must handle the key challenge: continuous authorization of probabilistic agents, not just one-time authentication of deterministic software.

For our system specifically, a breakthrough would be cryptographic attestation of GUPP contracts: each agent receives a signed delegation certificate specifying its tool access, token budget, and scope boundaries, verifiable by any observer without trusting the runtime. This would make our trust system trustless in the cryptographic sense.
