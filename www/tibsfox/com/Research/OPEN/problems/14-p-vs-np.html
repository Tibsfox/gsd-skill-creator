The Problem

P is the class of decision problems solvable in polynomial time. NP is the class of problems whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time, given a witness:

x ∈ L   iff   ∃ w with |w| ≤ p(|x|) such that V(x, w) = 1

Clearly P ⊆ NP (a polynomial-time algorithm is its own witness). The P vs NP question asks: does P = NP? Equivalently: if you can recognize the answer quickly, can you find the answer quickly?

A problem is NP-complete if it is in NP and every NP problem reduces to it in polynomial time. If any NP-complete problem is in P, then P = NP. Known NP-complete problems include SAT, CLIQUE, VERTEX-COVER, HAMILTONIAN-PATH, GRAPH-COLORING (k ≥ 3), and TRAVELING-SALESMAN.

The overwhelming consensus is P ≠ NP. Three known barriers — relativization (Baker-Gill-Solovay, 1975), natural proofs (Razborov-Rudich, 1994), and algebrization (Aaronson-Wigderson, 2009) — prevent all current proof techniques from resolving the question.

Latest Research

Cook's Theorem and the NP-Completeness Framework

Cook 1971, Karp 1972 · Foundational results

Cook proved SAT is NP-complete; Karp showed 21 natural combinatorial problems are NP-complete. This established NP-completeness as a widespread phenomenon, not an artifact of one problem. The reductions form a web connecting problems across combinatorics, graph theory, scheduling, and logic.

The Three Barriers

Baker-Gill-Solovay 1975, Razborov-Rudich 1994, Aaronson-Wigderson 2009

Relativization: there exist oracles making P = NP and others making P ≠ NP, so standard diagonal arguments fail. Natural proofs: any "natural" circuit lower bound technique cannot work if pseudorandom functions exist. Algebrization: algebraic extensions of relativization also fail. A proof must simultaneously evade all three barriers — leaving almost no known technique intact.

Restricted Circuit Lower Bounds

Razborov 1985, Håstad 1987, Raz 2009 · Partial progress

Super-polynomial lower bounds are known for restricted computation models: exponential bounds for CLIQUE in monotone circuits, super-polynomial bounds for PARITY in constant-depth circuits (AC0), and lower bounds for the permanent in multilinear formulas. None extends to unrestricted Boolean circuits — the gap between restricted and general computation is the central mystery.

Our Connection

TSPB Math CS Millennium P vs NP is the mathematical foundation of our verification architecture.

The witness-observer pattern. The GSD system is built on: verification is cheaper than execution. The verifier subagent checks the executor's work without re-executing. This is exactly the NP witness model. If P = NP, the verifier would be no faster than re-running the executor. The entire efficiency gain of the convoy model depends on P ≠ NP.

Curry-Howard and paper #34. The Artemis II paper on Typed Chain-of-Thought maps the question "does this reasoning step have the right type?" to type-checking, which is in P. Finding a proof from scratch is NP-hard. Paper #34's PC-CoT framework builds NP-style verification on top of an NP-hard generation process — the P vs NP divide instantiated in LLM reasoning.

NP-complete scheduling. Wave scheduling in the convoy model (minimum-wave decomposition of task dependency graphs) is equivalent to graph coloring, which is NP-complete. Our practical use of greedy heuristics is the engineering response to NP-hardness.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Proving P ≠ NP: Showing a specific NP problem requires super-polynomial time. This would require a proof technique that evades all three barriers simultaneously. No known approach can do this.

Proving P = NP: Constructing a polynomial-time algorithm for an NP-complete problem. This would collapse cryptography, invalidate zero-knowledge proofs, and fundamentally restructure computer science. Almost no one expects this outcome.

Our angle: We cannot resolve P vs NP, but we can (1) track the executor/verifier time ratio across 21,298 tests as empirical data on the verification gap, (2) formally analyze wave-scheduling as a graph coloring instance to map easy-to-hard phase transitions, and (3) use the Curry-Howard framework (paper #34) as a concrete instantiation of polynomial-time verification for chain-of-thought reasoning.
