Komlós Conjecture

Optimal vector balancing in high dimensions
Open
OPEN Live Research

The Problem

Given n vectors v1, v2, ..., vn in Rd, each with Euclidean norm at most 1, can you always assign signs εi ∈ {+1, −1} such that the signed sum has bounded infinity norm?

∃ ε1, ..., εn ∈ {±1}   such that   || ∑ εi vi ||∞ ≤ C

The Komlós conjecture asserts that the constant C can be chosen independently of n — it depends only on the dimension d (or not at all). In plain language: no matter how many unit vectors you have, you can always split them into two groups whose vector sums nearly cancel in every coordinate.

The best known bound is O(√log n), achieved by Banaszczyk in 1998 using Gaussian measures. Closing the gap between O(1) (the conjecture) and O(√log n) (the best result) has been a major open problem in discrepancy theory for over 40 years.

Latest Research

The O(√log n) Barrier

Banaszczyk 1998, Bansal 2010 · Discrepancy theory

Banaszczyk's 1998 result using Gaussian measures gives the best known bound of O(√log n). Bansal's 2010 breakthrough made this constructive using semidefinite programming, turning an existential result into an algorithm. But neither approach seems capable of reaching O(1). New ideas are needed to cross the √log n barrier.

Ben Green's Open Problems List

Green, ongoing · Cambridge combinatorics

The Komlós conjecture appears on several curated lists of important open problems in combinatorics and analysis, including Ben Green's collection. These lists contextualize the problem within broader questions about balancing, coloring, and discrepancy — connecting it to the Beck–Fiala conjecture and the Tusnády problem. Progress on any of these related conjectures would likely inform the others.

Discrepancy Theory and Algorithm Design

Lovett & Meka 2015, Rothvoss 2017 · Theoretical CS

Recent work has established deep connections between discrepancy theory and algorithm design. The partial coloring method and random walk approaches have yielded new constructive proofs for special cases. Rothvoss's work on bin packing used discrepancy ideas to achieve breakthrough approximation ratios. The Komlós conjecture, if proved, would have immediate algorithmic applications.

Our Connection

TSPB Math Komlós connects to our work through TSPB Layer 4 (Vector Calculus), the College Data Science department, and the GSD convoy model.

Vector balancing = task distribution: The Komlós conjecture is fundamentally about distributing vectors into two balanced groups. Our GSD convoy model distributes work items across agents — each work item has a "resource vector" (estimated time, complexity, file count, dependency depth). Optimal convoy formation IS a vector balancing problem. If the conjecture holds, it guarantees that any set of work items can be split into near-perfectly balanced teams.

Practical connection: The ACE project convoy model uses heuristic load balancing. Understanding the theoretical guarantees from discrepancy theory could improve our allocation algorithms — or at least confirm that our heuristics are near-optimal.

Data Science department: Discrepancy theory connects to experimental design (balanced treatment assignment), random sampling, and numerical integration — all areas where balanced vector sums matter.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

Proving the conjecture: Showing that the constant C is indeed O(1), independent of n. This would require fundamentally new techniques beyond Gaussian measures and semidefinite programming. The most likely path is through probabilistic combinatorics or novel convex geometry arguments.

Breaking the √log n barrier: Even improving the bound to O(log log n) or O((log n)1/3) would be a major result, since no improvement on Banaszczyk's 1998 bound has been achieved in nearly three decades.

Constructive O(1) for special cases: Proving the conjecture for structured vector families (sparse vectors, low-rank collections, random vectors) with efficient algorithms. This would have immediate applications in algorithm design and scheduling.

Our angle: We can contribute by (1) implementing discrepancy-based load balancing in the convoy model and measuring whether it outperforms our current heuristics, (2) running computational experiments on random vector balancing to build intuition about the constant, and (3) connecting the problem to our multi-agent orchestration work where balanced task allocation is a real-world requirement.
