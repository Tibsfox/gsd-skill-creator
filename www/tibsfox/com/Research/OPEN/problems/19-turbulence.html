The Problem

Turbulence is the chaotic, multi-scale, dissipative regime of fluid flow at high Reynolds numbers. It is governed by the Navier-Stokes equations (P15), but its statistical behavior is not derivable from those equations without additional assumptions.

Re = U · L / ν     Laminar: Re << 1     Turbulent: Re > 2,300 (pipe)

Kolmogorov's 1941 theory (K41) provides the most successful description: energy is injected at large scales and cascades to the Kolmogorov dissipation scale η = (ν³/ε)1/4, where it is converted to heat. In the inertial subrange:

E(k) = C · ε2/3 · k−5/3     [Kolmogorov spectrum]

This −5/3 power law is one of the most verified relationships in physics. Yet it is derived from dimensional analysis, not from first principles. To resolve all scales in atmospheric turbulence (integral scale ~100 km, Kolmogorov scale ~1 mm) would require (108)³ = 1024 grid points — computationally impossible. This is why turbulence must be parametrized.

The open sub-problems: Can K41 be derived rigorously from Navier-Stokes? What are the correct intermittency exponents? Why does turbulence appear when linear stability theory says the flow is stable? How does turbulence behave in stratified, rotating, or compressible settings?

Latest Research

Kolmogorov 1941/1962: Energy Cascade and Intermittency

Proc. USSR Acad. Sci. 30 (1941), J. Fluid Mech. 13 (1962)

K41 establishes the energy cascade, inertial subrange, and universality hypothesis from dimensional analysis. K62 introduces intermittency corrections via the lognormal model for dissipation fluctuations. The structure function exponents ζp deviate from K41's prediction ζp = p/3 for p > 3 (anomalous scaling). The multifractal model (Parisi-Frisch, 1985) parametrizes this but does not derive it.

Machine Learning for Turbulence Closure

Kochkov et al. PNAS 2021, Vinuesa & Brunton 2022

Neural networks (neural ODEs, physics-informed networks, graph networks) significantly outperform hand-tuned RANS models for sub-grid-scale stress prediction. But they lack interpretability and fail outside their training distribution — the same faithfulness problem we track in P1. ML turbulence closure is one of the most active applied-ML research fronts.

Quantum Turbulence: K41 in Superfluid Helium

Vinen, Niemela et al. 2001–present · Superfluid dynamics

Turbulence in quantum fluids (superfluid helium-4, Bose-Einstein condensates) follows a K41 spectrum at large scales despite the quantum nature of the vortex filaments. The vortices are quantized (circulation = n·h/m), yet the statistical behavior is classical Kolmogorov. This unexpected universality deepens the mystery of turbulence's relationship to the underlying equations.

Our Connection

TSPB Physics Math Turbulence is the most directly relevant open problem to our daily infrastructure.

KPAE weather pipeline and MUK convergence zone. Every wind speed measurement from the MUK weather-map.html (8 METAR, 7 NWS/CWOP/NDBC, 4 satellite sources) samples a turbulent atmospheric boundary layer. The Mukilteo convergence zone forms when Puget Sound marine air collides with Cascades-blocked continental air, generating turbulent mixing. The NWP models our pipeline queries (GFS, NAM) parametrize sub-grid turbulence using k-ε models. The forecast uncertainty IS turbulence uncertainty.

Living forest simulation. Wind through forest canopy is a rough-wall boundary layer problem at Re ~ 106, with coherent structures (ramp patterns, sweeps, ejections). The planned enhancements (wind-driven seed dispersal, water flow) require a turbulence parametrization — likely the Massman-Weil canopy flow model with empirically calibrated constants.

Computational irreducibility. Turbulent flows cannot be predicted far ahead without simulating every intermediate state. This is why weather forecasts degrade beyond 7–10 days (Lorenz butterfly effect). Our sweep.py pipeline respects this by re-sampling actual state hourly rather than extrapolating.

The energy cascade as multi-scale execution. Large eddies break into smaller eddies, down to the dissipation scale. The GSD convoy model also cascades: phases → milestones → tasks → sub-operations. If "energy" is the rate of information production, this is a turbulent cascade in plan-space.

What Would Constitute a Breakthrough

First-principles derivation of K41: Rigorously deriving the −5/3 energy spectrum from the Navier-Stokes equations, not from dimensional analysis. This would require proving that the energy cascade is a necessary consequence of the NS dynamics at high Reynolds numbers.

Explaining intermittency: Deriving the anomalous scaling exponents ζp from theory. The multifractal model fits the data but has no derivation from NS. Understanding intermittency would also clarify the relationship between turbulence and potential NS blowup (P15).

Understanding transition: Explaining why pipe flow becomes turbulent at Re ~ 2,300 when linear stability theory predicts stability at all Reynolds numbers. The subcritical transition mechanism is one of the oldest unsolved problems in fluid mechanics.

Our angle: We can contribute by (1) verifying the −5/3 spectrum in KPAE wind data using the Welch PSD method, (2) using the math co-processor's FFT tool for spectral analysis of simulated flows, and (3) exploring whether ML turbulence closure techniques (PINNs) can inform agent behavior prediction in the convoy model.
