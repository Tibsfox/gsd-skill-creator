Performance, Metrics & Risk

CHAOS Reports, the Iron Triangle, EVM, Agile Metrics, OKRs, Risk Frameworks, and the Metrics Trap
Overview History Methodologies Metrics AI & Tools GSD Rosetta Learn
What gets measured gets managed — and what gets measured wrong gets managed wrong. Metrics are not goals. They are signals.

This page examines the evidence base for project success and failure, the frameworks used to measure progress, and the risk management practices that mature teams apply. It also examines the dark side of measurement — the ways metrics can mislead, incentivize bad behavior, or create the illusion of control. The final section frames metrics as feedback signals for experimentation rather than targets to hit — the philosophy that the GSD page develops in full.

Part 1: The Standish Group CHAOS Reports

The Standish Group’s CHAOS Report is the single most referenced dataset in the entire field of project management. Since 1994, this annual study has shaped how the industry thinks about project outcomes, what “success” means, and why projects fail. It has been cited in tens of thousands of academic papers, industry presentations, and corporate strategy documents. It is also one of the most methodologically controversial research programs in the field.

The 1994 Report: The Original “Crisis”

The first CHAOS Report was published in 1994 by The Standish Group International. The headline numbers were stark: only 16% successful, 53% challenged, and 31% failed. These numbers immediately became the most-cited statistics in IT project management.

A critical shift occurred in 2015. The Standish Group changed the definition of project success from “completed on time, on budget, with all specified features” to “completed on time, on budget, with a satisfactory result” — a deliberately subjective criterion. This caused a ~7% decrease in reported success rates and created a break in the time series that is frequently overlooked.

The correlation between project size and failure rate is one of the most robust findings across all CHAOS Reports. Large projects (over $10M) succeed less than 10% of the time. This finding alone justifies the entire research program.

Criticism of the CHAOS Reports

The CHAOS Reports have attracted sustained and serious criticism. The most comprehensive critique is Eveleens and Verhoef’s 2010 paper “The Rise and Fall of the Chaos Report Figures” in IEEE Software. Their objections:

  • Misleading: Success is measured against estimation accuracy, not against business value delivered or user satisfaction.
  • One-sided: The measure is asymmetric. A project over-delivering features but missing dates is “challenged,” same as one under-delivering.
  • Perverse incentives: Teams that massively over-estimate will appear “successful” under the methodology.
  • Meaningless aggregation: CHAOS averages figures from organizations with vastly different estimation processes.
  • Selection bias: The survey population may skew toward organizations experiencing problems.
  • Non-transparency: Underlying data and methodology are proprietary, preventing independent verification.

Despite these criticisms, the CHAOS longitudinal data remains useful directionally. “About a third of IT projects succeed as planned” is a defensible summary. “Exactly 31% of projects succeed” implies a precision the methodology cannot support.

Part 2: The Iron Triangle

The Iron Triangle is the oldest framework in PM: scope, time (schedule), and cost (budget) are the three constraints, and changing one affects the others. Often a fourth dimension — quality — is placed at the center, since quality is affected by how the three constraints are balanced. The cliche: “Good, fast, cheap — pick two.”

The Iron Triangle is conceptually simple but practically powerful. Every tradeoff conversation in PM is, at root, an Iron Triangle conversation. Want more features? Either extend the schedule, add budget (resources), or reduce quality. Want to ship sooner? Either cut scope, add resources (Brooks’s Law permitting), or accept lower quality. The framework forces honesty: you cannot simultaneously demand more scope, shorter schedule, lower budget, and higher quality.

Modern PM literature has expanded the triangle. PMBOK 4 expanded the “triple constraints” to six: scope, quality, schedule, budget, resources, and risk. The insight remains the same: project success is multi-dimensional, and tradeoffs are inescapable.

Part 3: Earned Value Management (EVM)

Earned Value Management was developed by the U.S. Department of Defense in the 1960s as an extension of PERT/CPM. It integrates scope, schedule, and cost measurements to provide objective metrics of project performance. EVM remains the standard for performance measurement on large government projects.

Core EVM Metrics

  • PV (Planned Value): Budget authorized for work scheduled, as of a given date.
  • EV (Earned Value): Budget authorized for work actually completed, as of a given date.
  • AC (Actual Cost): Actual cost incurred for work completed, as of a given date.
  • SV (Schedule Variance) = EV − PV. Positive = ahead of schedule.
  • CV (Cost Variance) = EV − AC. Positive = under budget.
  • SPI (Schedule Performance Index) = EV / PV. > 1.0 = ahead of schedule.
  • CPI (Cost Performance Index) = EV / AC. > 1.0 = under budget.
  • EAC (Estimate at Completion) = BAC / CPI (one common formula). Projected total cost.
  • ETC (Estimate to Complete) = EAC − AC. Remaining cost to finish.

When EVM Works

EVM works when the scope is well-defined upfront and can be decomposed into measurable work packages with baselines. It is most useful for construction, defense procurement, and other domains where scope change is discouraged and disciplined cost accounting is already in place. EVM’s power is that it gives early warning of trouble: a CPI below 1.0 three months into a project is nearly always a signal that the baseline was wrong.

When EVM Fails

EVM fails on projects with evolving scope, novel work, or knowledge-intensive activities. If you don’t know what “50% done” means for a given work package, EV is a fiction. If scope changes every sprint, the baseline is a historical artifact, not a measurement reference. Many organizations apply EVM to agile projects through contortions that satisfy auditors but provide no actual insight.

Part 4: Agile Metrics

Velocity

Velocity is the most widely (and most widely abused) agile metric. It measures the sum of story points completed per sprint. Properly used, velocity is a planning tool — it lets a team estimate how much work they can reasonably commit to in the next sprint based on historical throughput. Improperly used, velocity becomes a performance metric, and teams are pressured to increase it. The result is story-point inflation: teams estimate larger and larger stories to show “improvement.”

Velocity should never be used to compare teams, appraise individual performance, or justify management decisions. It is a noisy, context-dependent number that is useful only within a single team as a planning input.

Burndown and Burnup Charts

Burndown charts show remaining work over time, ideally converging to zero at the end of the sprint. Burnup charts show completed work against a total-scope line. Burnup is generally preferred because it distinguishes between progress and scope change — a burndown chart that fails to reach zero might indicate insufficient velocity or scope addition, and you cannot tell which.

Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD)

The Cumulative Flow Diagram, primarily used in Kanban, shows the number of items in each workflow state over time. A healthy CFD shows bands of roughly constant width. Widening bands indicate bottlenecks; converging bands indicate WIP starvation downstream. The CFD exposes flow problems that velocity hides.

Cycle Time and Lead Time

Cycle time measures how long it takes a work item to go from “started” to “done.” Lead time measures how long it takes from when a work item is requested to when it is delivered. Lead time includes queue time before work starts; cycle time does not. Both are more useful than velocity for teams doing continuous flow work because they answer the question customers actually care about: “How long until I get what I asked for?”

DORA Metrics

The DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) metrics — formalized by Forsgren, Humble, and Kim in Accelerate (2018) — are four numbers that, together, predict organizational software delivery performance:

  • Deployment Frequency: How often you ship to production.
  • Lead Time for Changes: From commit to production.
  • Change Failure Rate: Percentage of deployments causing incidents.
  • Mean Time to Restore (MTTR): How quickly you recover from failures.

High performers ship multiple times per day with change failure rates below 15% and MTTR under an hour. Low performers ship monthly or less, with change failure rates above 60%. DORA metrics are the most rigorously validated performance metrics in modern software delivery.

Part 5: OKRs — Objectives and Key Results

OKRs originated with Andy Grove at Intel in the 1970s, building on Peter Drucker’s Management by Objectives. Grove’s framework — described in his 1983 book High Output Management — paired ambitious qualitative Objectives with measurable quantitative Key Results.

John Doerr brought OKRs to Google in 1999 (he had learned them from Grove at Intel) and popularized them worldwide through his 2018 book Measure What Matters. Google’s explosive growth made OKRs a staple of Silicon Valley management and they spread to companies and organizations around the world.

The format:

  • Objective: What you want to achieve. Qualitative, memorable, aspirational.
  • Key Results: How you measure progress. Quantitative, time-bound, verifiable. Typically 3–5 per Objective.

OKRs are explicitly designed to be uncomfortable. Teams are expected to achieve roughly 70% of their Key Results on average. If they hit 100%, the targets were too easy. If they hit 30%, the targets were delusional. The 70% norm creates a culture of ambitious targeting without punishing failure.

OKRs are most useful at the organizational and team level, not the individual level. They are a strategic alignment tool, not a performance review tool. Organizations that use OKRs for compensation decisions rapidly destroy the psychological safety that makes ambitious targeting possible.

Part 6: Risk Management Frameworks

PMI defines risk management as a six-step process: plan risk management, identify risks, perform qualitative analysis, perform quantitative analysis, plan risk responses, and monitor risks. The PMBOK Guide 6th Edition devoted an entire knowledge area to risk, with detailed treatment of techniques.

Risk Identification

Common techniques: brainstorming sessions, Delphi technique (expert consensus via structured polling), SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats), interviews, checklist analysis based on historical data, assumption analysis, and cause-and-effect diagrams.

Qualitative Risk Analysis: Probability × Impact Matrix

The most common risk assessment tool is the probability × impact matrix, a grid that rates each risk on both axes (typically 1–5) and scores them by the product. Risks in the top-right quadrant (high probability, high impact) demand attention; risks in the bottom-left (low/low) may be accepted. The matrix is simple enough to use in a meeting and communicates risk priorities visually.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation runs thousands of scenarios with randomly sampled task durations, cost values, or other uncertain variables, producing a probability distribution of outcomes rather than a single point estimate. If you ask “what is the probability we finish by December 15?”, Monte Carlo can tell you: perhaps 40%. Traditional CPM can only tell you what happens if all estimates are exactly right.

Modern tools (@RISK, Primavera Risk Analysis, and others) make Monte Carlo accessible to project managers without statistical training. The outputs — S-curves, tornado charts, sensitivity analysis — have become the standard way to communicate schedule risk on large projects.

Pre-Mortem

The pre-mortem, popularized by Gary Klein, is a powerful qualitative technique. Before starting a project, the team imagines that it has failed spectacularly and writes a post-mortem explaining why. This inverts the usual optimistic framing and surfaces risks that no one would mention in a standard kickoff meeting. Pre-mortems are cheap, fast, and remarkably effective at exposing the risks teams would otherwise miss or minimize.

Part 7: Risk Response Strategies

PMBOK distinguishes risk response strategies for threats (negative risks) and opportunities (positive risks).

For Threats

  • Avoid: Eliminate the risk by changing the project plan. Cancel the risky work, change the approach, clarify requirements.
  • Mitigate: Reduce the probability or impact of the risk. Add testing, use proven technology, hire experts.
  • Transfer: Shift the risk to another party. Insurance, fixed-price contracts, warranties, performance bonds.
  • Accept: Acknowledge the risk and decide to live with it. Active acceptance = contingency reserves. Passive acceptance = do nothing and deal with it if it happens.

For Opportunities

  • Exploit: Make the opportunity happen. Assign best resources, invest in the capability.
  • Enhance: Increase probability or impact of the opportunity.
  • Share: Partner with another party to capture the opportunity jointly.
  • Accept: Take advantage of the opportunity if it happens, but do not actively pursue it.

Part 8: Stakeholder Analysis

Mendelow’s Power/Interest Grid

Aubrey Mendelow’s 1991 power/interest grid classifies stakeholders on two axes: their power to affect the project and their interest in it. The four quadrants suggest different engagement strategies:

  • High Power, High Interest: Manage closely. Executive sponsors, key customers.
  • High Power, Low Interest: Keep satisfied. Senior executives not directly invested.
  • Low Power, High Interest: Keep informed. End users, advocates.
  • Low Power, Low Interest: Monitor. Minimal effort required.

Mitchell Salience Model

Ronald Mitchell, Bradley Agle, and Donna Wood proposed in 1997 a more sophisticated model based on three attributes: power (ability to influence), legitimacy (right to be involved), and urgency (time-critical claims). Stakeholders possessing all three are “definitive” and require the most attention. Those with only one attribute are “latent” and need minimal engagement. The model is more nuanced than the simple 2x2 grid and works better for complex projects with many stakeholder groups.

Part 9: The Metrics Trap

The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor. — Campbell’s Law

Every metric eventually becomes a target, and every target eventually gets gamed. This is not a failure of particular metrics or particular people — it is a structural property of measurement under pressure. Velocity gets inflated. Bug counts get reclassified. Lines-of-code become kilolines. Customer satisfaction scores are coerced by pushy follow-up emails. The more important the metric to careers and budgets, the more energy goes into corrupting it.

The defense is not to stop measuring. Not measuring is worse. The defense is to:

  • Use multiple metrics that cannot all be gamed simultaneously (DORA’s four metrics work this way — you cannot ship more often while also failing less without actually improving).
  • Audit the definitions periodically. What counts as “done”? What counts as a “bug”? Shifting definitions are the first sign of gaming.
  • Keep metrics away from compensation. The moment a metric affects someone’s pay, it stops measuring reality and starts measuring what people do to make the metric look good.
  • Rotate metrics. Use different metrics at different times to prevent long-term optimization for a single number.
  • Treat metrics as feedback, not truth. A metric that disagrees with your intuition might be right, or it might be the first sign of gaming. Investigate.

Part 10: The Metrics Philosophy

Metrics are not goals. They are signals.

The purpose of measurement in project management is not to hit numbers. It is to give the team enough data to conduct informed experiments with their own process. A metric that tells you nothing actionable is overhead. A metric that tells you something actionable but that you then optimize blindly becomes a trap.

The GSD approach to metrics — developed in depth on the GSD page — treats every measurement as feedback for experimentation. Token consumption per phase measures planning-to-execution ratio. Commit frequency measures work rate. Research-to-code ratio measures context-building versus producing. Verification pass rates measure planning quality. None of these are targets. All of them are signals that help the team decide what to try next. Low verification pass rate? The plan step needs more rigor. High research-to-code ratio? Maybe over-researching. High token-to-code ratio? Maybe the plan is too abstract.

The goal is not to optimize any single metric. The goal is to have enough data to run informed experiments with your process, and enough memory (STATE.md, handoff documents, session reports) to remember what worked and what didn’t across projects. This is what the philosophy on the GSD page calls metrics as feedback for hybridization.
