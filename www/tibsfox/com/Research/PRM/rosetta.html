Every PM methodology is a set of assumptions about requirements stability, team capability, feedback speed, and tolerance for change. The methodology itself is not right or wrong — it is either aligned with your context or misaligned. The Rosetta view makes the assumptions explicit.

The PM Paradigm Spectrum

  1. Ad hoc — no formal method. Work happens through individual initiative, tribal knowledge, and heroics. Common in startups under 5 people. Fails predictably past 10.
  2. Waterfall — sequential phases (requirements → design → implementation → testing → deployment), full upfront planning. Works when requirements are stable and well-understood. Fails when they change.
  3. Iterative — repeated development cycles, each refining the product. Requirements evolve across iterations. RUP (Rational Unified Process) and Spiral Model live here.
  4. Agile — short iterations (1–4 weeks), continuous feedback, minimal documentation, self-organizing teams. Scrum, Kanban, and XP are the major flavors. Dominant paradigm for software since ~2010.
  5. Lean — eliminate waste, optimize flow, defer commitment, deliver fast, respect people. Borrowed from the Toyota Production System. Kanban is the PM expression of Lean thinking.
  6. Hybrid — structured governance and milestone planning from Waterfall/PRINCE2, combined with agile execution within phases. Common in regulated industries (finance, healthcare, defense).
  7. AI-native (GSD) — AI plans the work, AI executes the work, humans gate at phase boundaries. Ceremony overhead drops to near-zero. Planning is the hard part; once plans are done, execution is deterministic. The 2024–26 frontier.

GSD vs Scrum — Detailed Side-by-Side

Scrum is the world’s most widely adopted agile framework. GSD shares its iterative structure but replaces human ceremonies with CLI commands and human team members with AI agents. The comparison reveals how AI-native PM inherits agile’s values while discarding its ceremony overhead.

GSD vs Kanban

Kanban optimizes flow by limiting work in progress. GSD achieves a similar effect through wave-based parallelism within phases: each wave is a bounded unit of concurrent work, and the next wave cannot start until the current wave completes. Both systems prioritize finishing over starting.

GSD vs SAFe

SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) solves the problem of coordinating dozens of Scrum teams across an enterprise. GSD solves a fundamentally different problem: enabling a single human to direct multiple AI agents through complex projects. They operate at different scales, with different assumptions about what the “team” is.

The Three Paradoxes of AI-Native PM

  1. The planning paradox: AI makes execution nearly free, which makes planning the most valuable activity. In traditional PM, planning is overhead. In GSD, planning is the work.
  2. The ceremony paradox: removing human ceremonies makes human judgment more important, not less. When AI executes autonomously, the human gate at phase boundaries is the only quality control. That gate must be rigorous.
  3. The scale paradox: AI-native PM scales down, not up. SAFe scales Scrum to 125 humans. GSD scales one human to the output of a team, through AI agents. The organizational complexity moves from human coordination to agent orchestration.
