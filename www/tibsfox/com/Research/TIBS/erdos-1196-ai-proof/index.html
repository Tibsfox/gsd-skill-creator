2. Erdős Problem #1196 — Solved by AI, April 13, 2026

The conjecture

A set A ⊂ ℕ is called primitive if no element of A divides any other element. The Erdős–Sárközy–Szemerédi conjecture, catalogued at erdosproblems.com/1196, asks whether for any primitive A ⊂ [x, ∞), the sum ∑a ∈ A1/(alog a) < 1 + o(1) as x → ∞. Erdős himself proved in 1934 that the infinite sum converges for every primitive set — the famous Erdős primitive sum (Erdős, J. London Math. Soc. 9, 1934, pp. 282–288). The sharpest prior human bound came from Jared Duker Lichtman in 2023:

$$\sum_{a \in A} \frac{1}{a \log a} < e^{\gamma}\, \frac{\pi}{4} + o(1) \approx 1.399 + o(1),$$

where γ ≈ 0.5772 is the Euler–Mascheroni constant (Lichtman, “A proof of the Erdős primitive set conjecture,” Forum of Mathematics, Pi, 2023). Lichtman had earlier shown (2020) that for Ak — the set of integers with exactly k distinct prime factors — the sum equals 1 to leading order, proving the conjectural bound of 1 is tight. Gorodetsky, Lichtman, and Wong (2024) refined the lower-order correction to roughly 1 − (c + o(1))k22−k with c ≈ 0.0656.

Resolution by GPT-5.4 Pro

On April 13, 2026, a single run of OpenAI’s GPT-5.4 Pro, prompted by Liam “Eric” Price, produced a complete proof of the conjecture after approximately eighty minutes of reasoning (teorth/erdosproblems wiki entry for #1196, last edited 13 Apr 2026; Hacker News discussion, 14 Apr 2026). The proof established the stronger asymptotic

$$\sum_{\substack{a \in A \\ a > x}} \frac{1}{a \log a} \le 1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{\log x}\right)$$

for every primitive A ⊂ ℕ, which immediately implies the conjecture. Commentary on social media noted the method’s unusual character: since Erdős’s 1934 paper, researchers had typically transferred the problem from discrete integers into continuous real analysis; GPT-5.4 Pro instead remained in the arithmetic domain and deployed the von Mangoldt function in a way that had not been attempted in ninety years of work on the problem (webiano.digital, “The proof that forced mathematics to take AI seriously,” April 2026).

Lichtman, who had worked on the problem for seven years, commented (reported by multiple outlets, April 2026):

“I care deeply about this problem, and I’ve been thinking about it for the past 7 years.”

He also described the solution on Mathstodon as “a Book Proof of the result,” invoking Erdős’s notion of “Proofs from The Book” (Lichtman, via Tao, Mathstodon, April 2026).

Status

The problem is marked formally solved on the erdosproblems.com entry for #1196 (page last edited April 15, 2026), and a Lean formalisation now exists in the Google DeepMind formal-conjectures repository (teorth/erdosproblems wiki, April 2026). Because this result is fresh and its long-term historical reading is still forming, we flag it under SC-VER as a fast-moving number: attribution is taken directly from the erdosproblems.com forum thread for #1196 and the teorth/erdosproblems wiki, both maintained by Tao.

All dates, systems, and outcomes are logged directly on the wiki. The wiki explicitly warns readers not to treat the page as a benchmark: “This page is not a benchmark” (wiki caveats section). Further caveats are:

  • Many problems on the site lack a thorough literature review; apparent priority can evaporate once human proofs are unearthed (exemplified by #1196 and #152, where pre-existing human literature was located only after the AI proof).
  • “Absence of past progress may reflect obscurity rather than difficulty” (wiki, methodological caveats).
  • AI contributions are heterogeneous — standalone solutions, solutions alongside rediscovered literature, solutions building on known prior work, and human-AI collaborations — so “contributions should be evaluated holistically” rather than counted (wiki).

The most important methodological concern is survivorship bias: only successful AI runs are reported, and neither the global success rate across Erdős problems nor the failure catalogue is known (wiki; Tao, Mathstodon, 2026-03). Tao has also publicly verified several of these proofs himself, most recently the three Erdős problems that fell in a seven-day span in late March 2026 (Cogni Down Under, Medium, April 2026, synthesising Mathstodon posts 115788262274999408 and 115855840223258103). His verification is a human-in-the-loop signal rather than a peer-reviewed journal gate.

Arc 3 — AI frontier (M5 AI-weather ↔︎ M6 AI-proof)

GraphCast (Dec 2023), Pangu-Weather (Nov 2022), GenCast (Dec 2024) + AIFS operational (Feb 2025) on one side; GPT-5.4 Pro’s 80-minute #1196 trace (Apr 13, 2026) on the other. Same sociological phenomenon at different domain scales:

  1. ML systems compete credibly with decades-old principled pipelines
  2. Both require careful attribution, benchmarking caveats, survivorship-bias audits
  3. Both trigger methodological debates about epistemic standing (is a learned solution a proof? is a 97.2% win rate on Z500 RMSE forecast skill?)
  4. Both are converging toward hybrid pipelines: AIFS is a hybrid ML/physics system, not a pure neural net; Lean-formalized AI proofs are hybrid reasoning/verification systems, not either alone

The Amiga-principle leverage (user’s standing rule): both domains replace a single expensive monolithic pipeline with specialised chips (a reasoning model, a verifier, a physics baseline) composed at clearly defined interfaces.
