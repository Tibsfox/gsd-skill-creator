Two Ways of Listening

A comparative study of two traditions that share a foundational conviction: the natural world is communicating, and the primary human task is to learn to listen. Ted Andrews' eclectic neo-shamanism — accessible, universalist, grounded in natural history — and the Pacific Northwest's tribally distinct, place-rooted, orally transmitted shamanic traditions represent fundamentally different epistemological approaches to the same longing: to be in conversation with the non-human world.

This study holds both traditions simultaneously, with honesty about what separates them and curiosity about what connects them. It does not privilege one over the other as spiritually "true." It asks: what does each offer, where do they genuinely converge, and what does the enormous popular appetite for Andrews' work tell us about the cultural moment that produced it?
