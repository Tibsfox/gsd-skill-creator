## 1. The New Age Movement Context (1987-2009)

### 1.1 Periodization

Ted Andrews' publishing career (1987-2009) sits squarely within the peak years of the New Age movement's mainstream cultural influence in North America. His first publication, *Simplified Qabala Magic* [Andrews 1987], appeared the same year as the Harmonic Convergence (August 16-17, 1987) — the globally coordinated meditation event that is widely cited as the moment the New Age movement entered popular consciousness. His final works appeared shortly before his death on October 24, 2009. This twenty-two-year span corresponds almost exactly to the period in which New Age publishing moved from specialty bookstores to mainstream retail, from subcultural practice to mass-market phenomenon [Andrews-Wiki].

### 1.2 The New Age Synthesis

The New Age movement was not a single doctrine but a syncretic field drawing from multiple spiritual traditions. Andrews' work reflects this eclecticism. The major source traditions feeding the New Age synthesis included:

| Tradition | Contribution to New Age | Presence in Andrews' Work |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Native American traditions | Guardian spirits, vision quests, animal medicine, sacred landscapes, medicine wheel | Central to *Animal Speak* [Andrews 1993a]; power animal concept; directional correspondences; nature-based spirituality framework |
| Tibetan Buddhist practice | Meditation technique, mandala, visualization, energy body concepts | Visualization and meditation exercises; energy perception frameworks |
| Celtic traditions | Nature spirituality, Wheel of the Year, fairy faith, tree lore | *Enchantment of the Faerie Realm* [Andrews 1993d]; nature spirits; seasonal cycles |
| Wiccan traditions | Ritual magic, correspondences, elemental system, goddess spirituality | Ritual frameworks; elemental correspondences; sacred space creation |
| Theosophical tradition | Auras, chakras (via Hindu/Buddhist reinterpretation), subtle bodies, spiritual evolution | *How to See and Read the Aura* [Andrews 1996a]; energy healing concepts; *The Healer's Manual* [Andrews 1993b] |
| Kabbalistic tradition | Tree of Life, pathworking, gematria, mystical correspondences | *Simplified Qabala Magic* [Andrews 1987]; *Pathworking and the Tree of Life* [Andrews 2010]; correspondences as structural principle |
| Western esoteric tradition | Divination, scrying, dream interpretation, ceremonial magic | *Crystal Balls & Crystal Bowls* [Andrews 1993c]; *Dream Alchemy* [Andrews 1998]; *Sacred Sounds* [Andrews 1992] |

### 1.3 Llewellyn Publications

Andrews' primary publisher, Llewellyn Publications, is the world's oldest and largest independent publisher of body/mind/spirit books, founded in 1901 by Llewellyn George as an astrology publisher and gradually expanding into the full spectrum of New Age, occult, and alternative spirituality publishing [Llewellyn-Catalog].

Llewellyn's role in the New Age ecosystem was significant: it provided the publishing infrastructure, editorial standards, and distribution network that allowed individual authors like Andrews to reach a mass audience. Llewellyn's house style prioritized accessibility and practical instruction — "how to" books that invited readers to try techniques themselves. This editorial approach had consequences for the cultural appropriation question: Llewellyn books characteristically presented spiritual techniques as available for individual experimentation, regardless of their tradition of origin [Llewellyn-Catalog].

Andrews published at least fifteen titles through Llewellyn between 1987 and his death, covering an extraordinary range of subjects: Kabbalah, auras, color healing, psychic touch, dance magic, faerie lore, crystal scrying, dream work, past lives, spirit guides, sacred sound, animal communication, and energy healing [see BIB for complete annotated bibliography]. This breadth is characteristic of the New Age publishing model, in which a single author functions as a generalist spiritual teacher across multiple traditions.

### 1.4 What Made Andrews Unusual

Within the New Age publishing landscape, Andrews occupied a distinctive position. Several features distinguished his work from that of his contemporaries:

**Rigor of natural history content.** Andrews' treatment of animals in *Animal Speak* [Andrews 1993a] and *Animal Wise* [Andrews 1999] was grounded in genuine naturalist knowledge. His years of volunteer work at the Brukner Nature Center in Troy, Ohio [Brukner-NC], and the Aullwood Audubon Center in Dayton, Ohio [Aullwood-AC], gave him direct experience with raptor behavior, avian ecology, and wildlife rehabilitation that most New Age authors lacked entirely. His descriptions of hawk flight behavior, owl sensory capabilities, and the ecological roles of various species were informed by observation and study, not merely by symbolic association [Andrews 1993a].

**Restraint from marketing as "authentic Native American totemism."** While Andrews drew extensively on Indigenous concepts (power animals, medicine wheels, directional correspondences), he generally avoided claiming to teach authentic Native American practices. His framing was syncretic and eclectic — drawing from multiple traditions and presenting a personal synthesis rather than positioning himself as an authorized representative of any specific Indigenous tradition. This distinction, while subtle, placed him in a somewhat different category from New Age authors who explicitly claimed Indigenous initiation or authorization [Andrews 1993a], [Andrews-Wiki].

**Behavioral-mythological dual framework.** Andrews' signature contribution was a method that paired empirical animal behavior with mythological and spiritual interpretation. For each animal in *Animal Speak*, he provided both a naturalist account (habitat, diet, physical characteristics, behavioral patterns) and a spiritual interpretation (what the animal's characteristics suggest as spiritual messages or qualities). This dual framework — nature observation as the basis for spiritual reflection — was more grounded than the purely symbolic approach of many competitors [Andrews 1993a].

## 2. Critical Reception of Animal Speak

### 2.1 Commercial and Popular Reception

*Animal Speak: The Spiritual & Magical Powers of Creatures Great and Small* [Andrews 1993a] has remained continuously in print since its 1993 publication — a remarkable commercial longevity for any book, and exceptional for a New Age title. As of 2026, it carries a Goodreads rating of 4.29 out of 5 based on 564 ratings [Goodreads-AS]. It is widely cited as the foundational text in the "animal totem" genre and has spawned an entire category of imitators, successors, and response texts.

The book's enduring popularity rests on its core contribution: treating animals as serious objects of spiritual attention with behavioral and mythological depth. For many readers, *Animal Speak* was their first encounter with the idea that observing wild animals could be a spiritual practice — that paying attention to which birds appeared, how they behaved, and what ecological roles they filled could yield insights about one's own life and spiritual condition [Goodreads-AS].

### 2.2 Scholarly and Critical Assessment

Critical assessment of *Animal Speak* clusters around three areas:

#### 2.2.1 Bibliographic Basis

Andrews' source material was composite and eclectic, drawing from ethnographic literature, popular natural history, mythology compilations, and other New Age texts without systematic scholarly apparatus. Goodreads reviewers have characterized the bibliographic approach as "rose-tinted idealistic generalising" about "ancient shamans" presented as a unified monoculture — collapsing the distinct traditions of dozens of Indigenous nations into a single "Native American shamanism" [Goodreads-AS].

This critique has merit. Andrews' text does not systematically distinguish between Coast Salish guardian spirit traditions, Chinookan animal mythology, Tlingit shaman practices, and Plains Indian medicine wheel teachings. These traditions are drawn upon as a shared reservoir of "ancient wisdom" without adequate attention to their distinct cultural contexts, historical development, or the specific nations from which they originate [Andrews 1993a].

#### 2.2.2 Cultural Appropriation

The appropriation critique centers on the book's treatment of Indigenous spiritual knowledge. The bird magic and animal medicine sections present details drawn from specific Indigenous spiritual practices with a "go try it yourself" attitude, without discussion of:

- Cultural ownership: who holds authority over these practices and teachings
- Social power dynamics: a white author from Ohio teaching techniques rooted in Indigenous traditions to a predominantly white audience
- Consent: whether the nations from which these practices originate have consented to their being taught in this format
- Compensation: whether Indigenous communities benefit from the commercial success of works drawing on their spiritual heritage

This critique is detailed in Section 4 below.

#### 2.2.3 Beginner Level Versus Depth

*Animal Speak* is widely recommended as a beginner text and criticized as insufficiently deep for serious study. The Llewellyn house style prioritizes breadth and accessibility over scholarly depth, resulting in a text that covers dozens of animals with necessarily brief treatments. For practitioners seeking deeper engagement with animal consciousness or specific cultural traditions, *Animal Speak* functions better as a doorway than a destination [Goodreads-AS].

### 2.3 Net Assessment

Despite the critiques above, *Animal Speak* made a genuine contribution: it invited millions of readers to pay close attention to the animals in their environment and to take animal consciousness seriously as a subject of spiritual reflection. The natural history content, informed by Andrews' direct experience with wildlife, gave the book a grounding that purely symbolic treatments lack. The question is not whether the book has value — it clearly does, as evidenced by its thirty-year commercial life and continuing readership — but whether its uncredited dependence on Indigenous spiritual concepts represents an acceptable form of synthesis or an unacceptable form of extraction. This question is addressed in Section 4.

## 3. The "Power Animal" Migration

### 3.1 Origin: Coast Salish Guardian Spirit Tradition

The term "power animal" as used in contemporary spiritual practice traces its origin to the Coast Salish guardian spirit tradition [see TRB-3.3]. In Coast Salish spiritual life, a guardian spirit that appeared in animal form conferred specific powers and characteristics upon its human partner. This was not a metaphor or a psychological exercise; it was understood as an actual spiritual relationship, established through the Spirit Quest (a rigorous process of fasting, isolation, and purification) and validated through the community's winter ceremonial [Jilek 1982]. [SENSITIVITY: HIGH]

The guardian spirit tradition was broadly participatory — not restricted to shamans — but the most powerful spirits, and the capacity to work with multiple spirits simultaneously, were associated with the shaman's calling. The relationship between a person and their guardian spirit in animal form was understood as reciprocal: the human received power, guidance, and protection, while the spirit received acknowledgment, ceremonial honor, and an active presence in the human world [Jilek 1982], [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

### 3.2 Vector: Michael Harner's The Way of the Shaman (1980)

The critical vector for the migration of the "power animal" concept from Coast Salish tradition to Western popular culture was Michael Harner's *The Way of the Shaman*, published in 1980 [Harner 1980].

Harner, an anthropologist who had conducted fieldwork with the Jivaro (Shuar) of Ecuador and the Conibo of Peru, developed what he called "core shamanism" — a deliberately decontextualized shamanic practice that extracted what Harner considered the universal structural elements of shamanism from their specific cultural contexts and presented them as techniques available to anyone. Among the central practices of Harner's core shamanism was the "shamanic journey" to meet one's "power animal" — a guided visualization exercise in which a practitioner, usually accompanied by rhythmic drumming, imagined descending into the earth to encounter an animal spirit [Harner 1980].

Harner's framework accomplished several things simultaneously:

| Effect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Universalization | Extracted practices from specific cultural contexts and presented them as universal human capacities |
| Democratization | Made shamanic techniques available to anyone willing to try them, regardless of cultural background or training |
| Commodification | Created a format (workshops, trainings, certifications) through which shamanic practice could be packaged and sold |
| Decontextualization | Removed practices from the ceremonial, social, and ecological frameworks that gave them meaning in their cultures of origin |
| Terminological migration | Brought specific terms ("power animal," "shamanic journey," "spirit helper") from Indigenous contexts into popular English |

The Foundation for Shamanic Studies, founded by Harner, became the institutional vehicle for disseminating core shamanism through workshops, training programs, and publications. By the time Andrews published *Animal Speak* in 1993, the term "power animal" had already entered mainstream spiritual vocabulary through Harner's work and the broader neo-shamanic movement [Harner 1980].

### 3.3 Popularization: Andrews' Animal Speak (1993)

Andrews' *Animal Speak* [Andrews 1993a] further popularized the power animal concept, but with a significant shift in emphasis. Where Harner's approach centered on the shamanic journey as technique (with the power animal as one element of that technique), Andrews centered on the animals themselves — their behavior, ecology, mythology, and spiritual significance. Andrews' readers did not necessarily practice shamanic journeying; they observed hawks in their backyards, noticed the appearance of owls, and consulted Andrews' animal dictionary for spiritual interpretation [Andrews 1993a].

This shift from technique-centered to animal-centered spirituality was Andrews' distinctive contribution. It was also, in terms of the appropriation question, a further step of abstraction: the power animal concept, already decontextualized by Harner from Coast Salish ceremonial life, was now further abstracted from even the neo-shamanic journey framework and applied to everyday encounters with wildlife.

### 3.4 The Complete Migration Path

```
Coast Salish guardian spirit tradition
  (community ceremonial; Spirit Quest; winter dance; reciprocal relationship)
     |
     v  [Documented by Jilek 1982, ethnographic literature]
     |
Michael Harner's "core shamanism" (1980)
  (decontextualized technique; guided visualization; drumming workshops)
     |
     v  [Popularized through Foundation for Shamanic Studies]
     |
Ted Andrews' Animal Speak (1993)
  (animal-centered; behavioral + mythological; Llewellyn mass market)
     |
     v  [30+ years in print; entire genre of imitators]
     |
Contemporary popular culture
  ("What's your spirit animal?"; social media; personality quizzes)
```

At each stage, the concept became more accessible and more distant from its origin. What began as a specific spiritual relationship within a specific cultural tradition — requiring rigorous preparation, community validation, and ongoing reciprocal obligation — became a casual pop-culture concept requiring nothing more than a personality quiz or a chance encounter with wildlife. ## 4. The Appropriation Framework

### 4.1 Defining the Issue

The migration of the "power animal" concept from Coast Salish tradition to mainstream Western popular culture represents a well-documented case of spiritual concept migration: the movement of a concept from an Indigenous cultural context to a Western popular context without compensation, attribution of the origin nation, or tribal consent. This is distinct from cultural exchange (which implies reciprocity) or cultural influence (which implies agency on the part of the originating culture). It is concept migration — the unilateral adoption of a spiritual concept by a culture with greater economic and political power, facilitated by individual intermediaries (Harner, Andrews) operating within publishing and workshop economies [Harner 1980], [Andrews 1993a].

### 4.2 Multiple Scholarly Positions

The academic and Indigenous discourse on spiritual appropriation encompasses a range of positions. This section presents three major positions without advocating for any specific policy outcome. [SENSITIVITY: MODERATE — Multiple positions are presented for readers to evaluate.]

#### Position 1: Strong Critique — Appropriation as Colonial Continuation

Indigenous scholars and activists who hold this position view the appropriation of ceremonial concepts as a direct continuation of colonial extraction. The argument runs as follows:

- Colonial powers physically dispossessed Indigenous nations of their lands, forcibly removed children to boarding schools, banned ceremonial practices (including the potlatch), and directly attacked spiritual practitioners (shaving shamans' heads, removing them from communities) [see TRB-8.1]
- Having disrupted the material and social conditions that sustained Indigenous spiritual life, the colonizing culture now extracts the spiritual concepts themselves — using them for commercial profit (bestselling books, paid workshops, certification programs) without compensation to the communities of origin
- This extraction is made possible by the same power differential that enabled the original colonial dispossession: Indigenous communities lack the political, economic, and legal mechanisms to prevent the unauthorized use of their spiritual heritage
- The "democratization" of shamanic practice through core shamanism and New Age publishing is, in this reading, a final phase of dispossession — taking not just land, children, and ceremony, but the very concepts and vocabulary of Indigenous spiritual life
- When a white author from Ohio publishes a bestselling book that depends centrally on Coast Salish concepts without naming Coast Salish communities, paying royalties, or seeking authorization, this is not "sharing" — it is extraction by a member of the colonizing culture from a colonized people

Proponents of this position note that the economic asymmetry is stark: Andrews' *Animal Speak* has been continuously in print for over thirty years, generating ongoing revenue for the author's estate and for Llewellyn Publications. Coast Salish communities receive nothing from this commercial success, despite the book's foundational dependence on Coast Salish spiritual concepts [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

#### Position 2: Moderate — Distinguishing Sacred Ceremonial Knowledge from General Spiritual Concepts

A moderate position, held by some scholars and practitioners, acknowledges that borrowing occurred and that Indigenous communities have legitimate grievances, while drawing a distinction between two categories of knowledge:

**Sacred ceremonial knowledge** — specific ritual procedures, songs, dances, and techniques that belong to particular nations, clans, or spiritual specialists and are transmitted through authorized channels. This category includes the Coast Salish winter ceremonial, Tlingit shaman mask traditions, Haida potlatch protocols, and other practices that are understood within their cultures of origin as proprietary. This knowledge should be protected; its unauthorized transmission is a genuine violation of Indigenous intellectual sovereignty.

**General spiritual concepts** — broad ideas about the spiritual significance of animals, the interconnectedness of natural and spiritual worlds, and the possibility of communication with non-human beings. These concepts, while they may have been articulated with particular depth and sophistication in Indigenous traditions, are arguably part of a global human heritage that appears in cultures worldwide (European animal folklore, Chinese zodiac, Hindu animal deities, African oral traditions, Australian Aboriginal Dreamtime).

In this reading, Andrews' error was not in proposing that animals have spiritual significance — an idea shared by cultures across the planet — but in drawing on specific Indigenous ceremonial details without adequate attribution or cultural sensitivity. The appropriate remedy is better attribution and cultural education, not the prohibition of cross-cultural spiritual inquiry.

Proponents of this position acknowledge the power differential and the history of colonial extraction, but argue that the appropriate response is to strengthen Indigenous cultural sovereignty (supporting language revitalization, repatriation of ceremonial objects, protection of sacred sites, and Indigenous self-determination in cultural matters) rather than to prohibit all cross-cultural engagement with spiritual concepts [Eliade 1964].

#### Position 3: Sympathetic Reading — Andrews as Gateway to Animal Consciousness

A sympathetic reading of Andrews' work, held by some practitioners and reviewers, argues that:

- Andrews opened doors for millions of people to take animal consciousness seriously, in a culture that predominantly views animals as resources, pests, or pets
- His behavioral-ecological grounding gave readers concrete tools for observing and appreciating wildlife, leading many to deeper engagement with conservation, ecology, and environmental ethics
- The syncretic framework, while culturally imprecise, made the idea of spiritual relationship with animals accessible to people who would never encounter Coast Salish guardian spirit traditions, Tlingit shaman practices, or any Indigenous ceremonial context
- The alternative to Andrews is not a world in which Western readers study Coast Salish traditions with proper attribution; it is a world in which Western readers never encounter the idea of animal as spiritual ally at all
- Andrews' work, whatever its shortcomings in attribution and cultural specificity, contributed to a broader cultural shift toward ecological consciousness, animal welfare, and respect for the natural world

Proponents of this position do not deny that appropriation occurred but argue that the net cultural effect of Andrews' work was positive — that it created a constituency of people who pay attention to animals, value biodiversity, and are open to non-materialist understandings of the natural world. They view the attribution failure as a genuine flaw that should be corrected through better scholarship, not as grounds for dismissing the work's contribution [Goodreads-AS].

### 4.3 The Research Posture of This Study

This study adopts a documentation posture: the migration of the "power animal" concept from Coast Salish tradition to Western popular culture is presented as a historical and cultural fact. The origin nation (Coast Salish) is named. The intermediaries (Harner, Andrews) are identified. The economic and power dynamics are described. Multiple scholarly positions are presented.

This study does not:

- Advocate for specific restitution policies
- Propose legal or regulatory remedies
- Claim authority to speak for Coast Salish or any other Indigenous community
- Resolve the appropriation debate

This study does:

- Name specific nations rather than using generic terms
- Document the migration path with citation evidence
- Present the full range of scholarly positions
- Respect the sensitivity protocols established in the shared schema [see SCH]

## 5. Andrews' Eclectic Synthesis: Accurate Description

Ted Andrews' body of work is accurately described as a **syncretic New Age synthesis** — a personal integration of concepts drawn from multiple spiritual traditions, informed by direct experience with wildlife and nature, and presented through the editorial conventions of Llewellyn Publications' body/mind/spirit catalog [Andrews 1993a], [Llewellyn-Catalog].

This characterization is descriptively neutral. It does not:

- Imply that Andrews' work is "inauthentic" (sincerity of belief is not in question)
- Imply that syncretic works have no value (syncretism is a well-documented process in the history of religions)
- Imply that Andrews represented any specific Indigenous tradition (he generally did not make this claim)
- Imply that Andrews' natural history content is unreliable (his wildlife knowledge was genuine)

It does accurately state that:

- Andrews drew from multiple traditions without authorization from any of them
- The resulting synthesis is his own creation, not a faithful transmission of any single tradition
- Readers should understand that *Animal Speak* represents Ted Andrews' personal synthesis, not Coast Salish tradition, not Tlingit tradition, not any other specific Indigenous tradition
- The book's value lies in its own terms — as a gateway to animal consciousness, a framework for nature-based spiritual reflection, and a work of popular natural history enriched by mythological reference — not in any claim to Indigenous authenticity [Andrews 1993a]

## 6. Safety and Sensitivity Framework

### 6.1 Sensitivity Classification Table

| Area | Level | Protocol |
|------|-------|----------|
| Sacred site locations | ABSOLUTE | No GPS coordinates or navigational information for ceremonial sites, sacred landscapes, or Spirit Quest locations. No descriptions that would enable navigation to specific locations. This is a non-negotiable protocol. |
| Indigenous knowledge references | HIGH | Always name specific nation (Coast Salish, Chinook, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian). Never use generic "Native American," "Indigenous peoples," or "tribal" without qualification. When the specific nation is uncertain, say so explicitly rather than defaulting to a generic term. |
| Living ceremonial traditions | HIGH | Do not provide instructional detail on proprietary ceremonial practices of any living tradition. Describe practices academically (documenting that they exist and what scholarly literature says about them) without providing step-by-step instruction that would enable unauthorized replication. |
| Power animal terminology | HIGH | Always attribute to Coast Salish origin when the term is used. Note the migration path through Harner to Andrews and popular culture. Never present the concept as generic or origin-neutral. |
| Totem / totem pole distinction | HIGH | Distinguish between clan crests/heraldic poles (Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian), guardian spirits (Coast Salish), and Andrews' personal spiritual ally concept. These are not the same thing and must not be conflated. |
| Cultural appropriation analysis | MODERATE | Present multiple scholarly positions (strong critique, moderate, sympathetic). Do not advocate for specific restitution policy. Let readers evaluate the evidence. |
| Colonial suppression history | MODERATE | State documented historical facts with citation. Do not sensationalize or editorialize. Do not minimize. Present the factual record as established in scholarly sources. |
| Andrews' eclectic synthesis | LOW | Describe accurately as syncretic New Age. Do not imply it represents any specific Indigenous tradition. Do not dismiss the work's value on appropriation grounds alone. |
| Medicine wheel attribution | MODERATE | Not a PNW Coast tradition; originates with Plains nations. Andrews uses it; PNW nations (Coast Salish, Chinook, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian) generally do not. Note this distinction when the term appears. |
| Shaman terminology | MODERATE | Note that "shaman" is an external academic term (from Evenki/Tungusic via Eliade [Eliade 1964]). PNW nations have their own terms: ixt' (Tlingit), sgaaga (Haida), halaayt (Tsimshian). Use nation-specific terms where documented. |

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|

| Does this content provide step-by-step instruction in a ceremonial practice of a living tradition? | HIGH — rewrite as academic description | Proceed to next question |

### 6.3 The "Shaman" Problem

The word "shaman" requires ongoing attention throughout this study. It derives from the Evenki (Tungusic) language of Siberia (šaman) and entered Western academic discourse through Russian ethnography, reaching its canonical academic formulation in Mircea Eliade's *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* [Eliade 1964]. Eliade proposed "shamanism" as a cross-cultural category applicable to spiritual specialists worldwide who share certain features: ecstatic trance, spirit travel, healing, and mediation between human and spirit worlds.

The use of "shaman" as a universal category has been criticized on multiple grounds:

- It imposes a Siberian term on traditions that have their own terminology
- It implies a unity of practice and belief that may not exist across the traditions being compared
- It privileges the academic observer's classificatory framework over the self-understanding of practitioners
- It facilitates the kind of decontextualization that enables appropriation (if all spiritual specialists are "shamans," then "shamanism" becomes a generic practice rather than a set of distinct cultural traditions)

This study uses "shaman" as an academic shorthand when discussing the comparative framework, while noting nation-specific terms (ixt', sgaaga, halaayt) where they are documented in the literature. The term is used descriptively, not prescriptively — acknowledging that it is an external category, not one that the nations surveyed would necessarily use to describe their own spiritual specialists [Eliade 1964], [Sturtevant 1990].

### 7.1 Who Profits

The economics of spiritual concept migration are relevant to the appropriation question. A simplified mapping of the economic flows:

| Entity | Role | Economic Benefit |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Ted Andrews (estate) | Author | Royalties from 30+ years of continuous *Animal Speak* sales; additional revenue from 20+ other titles |
| Llewellyn Publications | Publisher | Publisher's share of 30+ years of sales; catalog anchor title; brand association |

| Retail and distribution chain | Distribution | Standard retail margins on continuous backlist sales |

While precise sales figures for *Animal Speak* are not publicly available, the book's thirty-year continuous print run, its Goodreads rating from 564+ ratings, and its status as a genre-defining text suggest cumulative sales in the hundreds of thousands of copies at minimum. The economic value extracted from Indigenous concepts — without compensation, attribution, or consent — is not trivial [Goodreads-AS], [Llewellyn-Catalog].

## 8. Comparative Framework: Andrews in Context

### 8.1 Andrews Versus Other New Age Authors

Situating Andrews within the broader New Age publishing landscape provides useful context:

| Author | Approach to Indigenous Knowledge | Key Difference from Andrews |
|--------|--------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Michael Harner | Deliberately decontextualized "core shamanism"; claimed universality | Harner's framework was explicitly technique-centered; Andrews was animal-centered |
| Carlos Castaneda | Claimed direct Indigenous initiation (disputed); presented as autobiography | Castaneda's authenticity claims were far more aggressive than Andrews'; Andrews did not claim initiation |
| Sun Bear (Chippewa) | Indigenous author who commercialized ceremonies; controversial within Indigenous communities | Sun Bear was Indigenous but criticized by other Indigenous people for selling ceremonies; Andrews was non-Indigenous |
| Jamie Sams | *Medicine Cards* (1988); claimed mixed heritage | More explicitly framed as "Native American" teaching than Andrews; similar appropriation concerns |
| Lynn Andrews (no relation) | Claimed direct Indigenous initiation; highly fictionalized narrative | Far more aggressive authenticity claims than Ted Andrews; widely criticized for fabrication |

Ted Andrews occupies a middle position in this landscape: more restrained than Castaneda or Lynn Andrews in authenticity claims, more natural-history-grounded than Sams or Sun Bear, but still dependent on Indigenous concepts without adequate attribution or community engagement.

### 8.2 What Adequate Attribution Would Look Like

For reference, a version of *Animal Speak* with adequate cultural attribution would:

- Name Coast Salish tradition explicitly as the source of the "power animal" concept
- Distinguish between Coast Salish guardian spirits, Haida/Tlingit/Tsimshian clan crests, and Andrews' personal synthesis
- Note that the medicine wheel is a Plains nations tradition, not a PNW Coast tradition
- Include a section on the history of colonial suppression of the traditions being drawn upon
- Acknowledge the economic asymmetry of a white author profiting from Indigenous concepts
- Recommend primary ethnographic sources (Jilek, Crawford O'Brien, Sturtevant) for readers seeking the original cultural contexts
- Include a statement of the author's own positionality: who he is, what traditions he trained in, what authority he claims and does not claim

The absence of these elements in *Animal Speak* is not evidence of malice; it reflects the norms of 1990s New Age publishing, which did not generally require cultural attribution of this kind. Those norms have since evolved, and contemporary scholarship holds authors to higher standards of cultural specificity and attribution.
