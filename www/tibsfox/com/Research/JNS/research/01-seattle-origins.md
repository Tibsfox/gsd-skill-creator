## 1. Before JanSport

The story begins in 1966, not 1967. Murray Pletz was a young man in Seattle with more ambition than money. He purchased a $180 sewing machine for his girlfriend, Jan Lewis. The plan was specific: sew a prototype backpack with a flexible aluminum frame that Pletz had been designing. This was not a hobby project. Pletz had a competition in mind [1][2].

Seattle in the mid-1960s was a city defined by its geography. The Cascade Range to the east, the Olympic Mountains to the west, Puget Sound at its feet, Mount Rainier visible from every clear vantage point. The outdoor culture was not a lifestyle choice; it was a consequence of place. If you lived in Seattle, you were within two hours of terrain that would test any gear you carried. The people who made gear here made it because they used it [1][2][3].

---

## 2. The Alcoa Contest

Pletz entered the Aluminum Company of America (Alcoa) design contest with his flexible-frame backpack design. The key innovation was the frame itself -- aluminum bent to flex with the wearer's body, distributing weight more naturally than the rigid external frames that dominated the market. He won [1][2].

The Alcoa contest victory gave Pletz validation and a proof of concept. The flexible frame was not merely a design flourish; it was an engineering decision grounded in trail experience. Rigid frames transferred load efficiently on flat ground but fought the wearer on steep terrain, uneven trails, and scrambles. A flexible frame absorbed the wearer's movements rather than resisting them. This principle -- that a pack should adapt to its user rather than demanding the user adapt to it -- would define JanSport's design philosophy for sixty years [1][2].

## 3. The Founding

JanSport was founded in 1967 in Seattle, Washington, by three people:

- **Murray Pletz** -- Designer, contest winner, the engineering mind behind the flexible-frame pack
- **Jan Lewis** -- Seamstress, co-founder, the person whose hands built every early pack and whose name the company carries
- **Skip Yowell** -- Pletz's cousin, salesman, evangelist, the energy that pushed the operation beyond the garage

The name "JanSport" came from Jan Lewis. The company's DNA came from all three. Pletz brought the design. Lewis brought the craft. Yowell brought the drive to sell it. The combination was potent: a designer, a maker, and a salesman, all under thirty, all broke, all living in a city surrounded by mountains [1][2][3].

## 4. Aurora Avenue

Operations began above Norman Pletz's transmission shop on Aurora Avenue -- State Route 99, one of Seattle's oldest commercial corridors running north from downtown through Fremont and past Green Lake. The workspace was modest. Hand-sewn packs, one to three per day, built on tables above the sound of transmissions being repaired below [1][2].

Aurora Avenue in the late 1960s was working-class Seattle. Not the tech corridor it would become, not the polished waterfront, but the kind of commercial strip where a young couple could rent space above a relative's shop and build something. The location mattered: close to the University of Washington campus, close to the trailheads that led into the Cascades, close to the outdoor community that would become JanSport's first customers [2][3].

The garage-to-global trajectory is a familiar American narrative, but JanSport's version has a specific character. This was not a technology startup pivoting toward market fit. This was a craft operation: a sewing machine, aluminum stock, nylon fabric, and two people who could see Mount Rainier from the parking lot. The product was designed for the mountains visible from the workshop window [1][2].

## 5. The D-2 Pack

The first JanSport product was the D-2, an external-frame hiking pack. External-frame packs were the standard for serious backcountry travel in the late 1960s -- the internal-frame revolution was still a decade away. The D-2 used Pletz's Alcoa-winning flexible aluminum frame design, which distributed weight more naturally than competing rigid frames [1][2].

The D-2 was designed for the Cascades. This was not a generic "hiking pack" developed for an abstract market. It was built for the specific conditions of Pacific Northwest mountain travel: steep switchbacks, volcanic scree, sustained rain, rapid weather changes, and trail surfaces that ranged from packed dirt to root-tangled old-growth forest floor. Every design decision reflected the terrain within a two-hour drive of the Aurora Avenue workshop [1][2].

## 6. The Three Founders

**Murray Pletz** was the engineer and designer. His flexible-frame innovation was the technical foundation. After the divorce from Jan Lewis in 1979, Pletz (who changed his name to Murray McCrory) left the company. He died on October 7, 2024. His daughter McCrory Van Brost and son Jeremy Murray McCrory have spoken publicly about growing up immersed in the company's culture of adventure [3][4].

**Jan Lewis** was the maker. Her sewing skills turned Pletz's designs into physical objects. She is the only founder whose name the company carries, and she is the last living connection to the founding era. After the divorce and Pletz's departure, Lewis and Yowell continued to run the company. Lewis later married and became Jan Peterson, then returned to her birth name. She remains active as of 2026 [3][4].

**Skip Yowell** was the cousin who showed up and never left. His role evolved from early sales into Vice President of Global Public Relations, a position he held until retirement. Yowell established the Mount Rainier annual climb tradition in 1972 and was the public face of JanSport's outdoor identity for decades. His personality -- enthusiastic, gregarious, relentlessly outdoors-focused -- became part of the brand's character [2][3].

## 7. The University Connection

In approximately 1970, the University of Washington bookstore manager approached Lewis and Pletz -- both former UW students -- about adapting one of their nylon hiking daypacks for students carrying books. The suggestion was practical: reinforce the bag, adjust dimensions to accommodate textbooks, add a front pocket for pens and small items [2][3][5].

The resulting product, initially known as the "University Bookstore Rucksack," was the seed of the daypack revolution. It spread from the UW campus to colleges across the country. The New York Times later described its descendant, the SuperBreak, as "iconic." As of 2024, a plaque commemorating this origin story had reportedly gone missing from the University Book Store [5].

The bookstore connection is not just origin mythology. It reveals the mechanism by which a mountaineering company became a campus company: a direct conversation between the people who made the packs and the person who sold books to students. No market research, no focus groups, no consultants. A bookstore manager said "make it carry books" and two former students said "okay." That directness -- product shaped by proximity to the user -- is the founding principle that corporate ownership would later struggle to replicate [2][3][5].

## 8. Growth and First Acquisition

By 1971, JanSport had outgrown the garage. A distribution facility was established in Everett, Washington -- approximately 30 miles north of Seattle, in Snohomish County, in the shadow of the Cascades. The same year, JanSport introduced the "Guaranteed for Life" warranty -- a commitment that would outlast every subsequent ownership change [1][2].

In 1972, K2 Corporation (owned by Cummins Engine Company) acquired JanSport. K2 was diversifying from ski equipment into broader leisure goods and saw JanSport's growing daypack business as a promising expansion. The acquisition provided capital for national distribution while the founders continued to run day-to-day operations [1][2].

The K2 acquisition marked the first of four ownership transitions. It brought resources -- manufacturing capacity, distribution networks, capital -- but also introduced the tension between founder-led culture and corporate management that would characterize JanSport's entire corporate history [1][2].

## 9. The Mount Rainier Tradition

In 1972, Skip Yowell established an annual group climb of Mount Rainier to field-test equipment. The tradition ran for 46 consecutive years, making it the longest consecutive annual group climb on record by 2018. Teams of 16 included employees, retailers, international distributors, nonprofit partners, and media [2][3].

The climb was not a team-building exercise. It was a quality assurance protocol. If the pack fails at 12,000 feet, you know. If the zipper jams when your hands are frozen, you know. If the straps cut into your shoulders after eight hours of sustained climbing, you know. No laboratory test, no customer survey, no focus group replicates the feedback loop of carrying the product up the mountain it was designed for [2][3].

Mount Rainier -- Tahoma in Lushootseed, the language of the Puyallup people -- stands at 14,411 feet, visible from Seattle on clear days. It is the most heavily glaciated peak in the contiguous United States, with 26 named glaciers. Weather conditions can change from sunshine to whiteout in minutes. The mountain does not grade on a curve. JanSport's products were tested against a standard that could not be negotiated [2][3].

## 10. Founder Legacy

The founding era ended in stages. Murray Pletz left after the 1979 divorce. Jan Lewis and Skip Yowell continued under K2 and then VF Corporation ownership. The company moved from Seattle to Everett and eventually to Alameda, California. The physical connection to Aurora Avenue, to the garage, to the mountains visible from the workshop -- all of it attenuated as the company scaled [1][2][3].

What survived was the product. The SuperBreak's design, the lifetime warranty, the fundamental identity of simple-durable-affordable -- these persisted through every transition. Whether that persistence represents the founders' enduring influence or the structural fitness of the product itself is the question that runs through every subsequent module [1][2].
