Chapter 23B.02 — The Gateway to Corporate Existence

Chapter 23B.02 RCW is the gateway chapter of the Washington Business Corporation Act. It establishes who may form a corporation, what must appear in the articles of incorporation, what may permissibly be added, when corporate existence begins, how liability is allocated for persons who act before incorporation, and how the first meetings and bylaws are organized. It also contains two features added through successive legislative revisions: emergency bylaws (for catastrophic circumstances) and forum selection clauses (for internal affairs litigation).

The chapter is deliberately short. In the spirit of the minimal leveraged substrate, it provides the primitives necessary to bring a corporation into existence and then steps back, trusting the articles, the bylaws, and the parties themselves to fill in the detail.

Who May Incorporate (RCW 23B.02.010)

“One or more persons may act as the incorporator or incorporators of a corporation by delivering articles of incorporation to the secretary of state for filing.”

Although this sentence occupies a single line of statute, it encodes several important policy choices.

“One or more persons”

At common law and under many older state statutes, incorporation required a minimum of three incorporators — a holdover from nineteenth-century practice of treating corporations as quasi-public chartered entities that demanded a minimum quantum of civic participation. Washington's Business Corporation Act, following the RMBCA, jettisoned that requirement. Today a single natural person may incorporate a Washington corporation, and nothing in RCW 23B.02.010 prohibits that single incorporator from also being the corporation's sole initial director, sole officer, and sole shareholder.

The original 1889 Washington territorial corporation statute required a minimum of three incorporators. The 1965 Business Corporation Act retained that requirement. Only with the 1989 WBCA — Laws of 1989, chapter 165, section 26 — did the “one or more” formulation enter Washington law.

“Persons”

Washington corporate law, like the MBCA, treats “person” broadly. RCW 23B.01.400 (definitions) defines the term to include natural individuals and entities (corporations, LLCs, partnerships, trusts, estates). An incorporator need not be a living human — a holding company may incorporate a subsidiary, a trust may incorporate on behalf of its beneficiaries, and an LLC may cause the formation of a wholly-owned corporate subsidiary. This is the statutory foundation of corporate-group structures.

No Residency Requirement

Section 23B.02.010 imposes no requirement that the incorporator reside in Washington, be a US citizen, or have any physical nexus with the state. The only geographic hooks in the formation process are (a) the requirement of a Washington registered agent with a physical street address, and (b) delivery of the articles to the Washington Secretary of State. A California resident, a Tokyo-based holding company, or an Estonian e-resident may be the sole incorporator of a Washington corporation.

Role of the Incorporator

The incorporator signs and delivers the articles, and — if the articles do not name initial directors — the incorporator holds the organizational meeting at which initial directors are elected and the bylaws adopted. Once the corporation is organized and directors are in place, the incorporator's role is exhausted; thereafter, authority resides with the board. The incorporator is a procedural agent, not a substantive principal.

Articles of Incorporation — Mandatory Content (RCW 23B.02.020(1))

RCW 23B.02.020(1) establishes the minimum informational content that must appear in articles of incorporation. The statute requires four items:

ElementCross-ReferenceRequirement
Corporate name Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW Must be distinguishable on the records of the Secretary of State; must contain a required corporate identifier (“Corporation,” “Incorporated,” “Company,” “Limited,” or abbreviations)
Authorized shares RCW 23B.06.010 and 23B.06.020 Number and classes/series; must include at least one class with unlimited voting rights and at least one class entitled to receive net assets on dissolution (often the same class)
Registered agent Article 4 of chapter 23.95 RCW Name and address of initial registered agent; physical WA street address required
Incorporator RCW 23B.02.010 Name and address of each incorporator

The Distinguishability Standard

A Washington corporate name must be “distinguishable on the records of the secretary of state” from the names of all other business entities and reserved names. RCW 23.95.305. This is a term of art: minor variations in spelling, punctuation, or corporate suffix may or may not render two names distinguishable. The Secretary of State publishes guidelines. In general, a corporate name is distinguishable if an ordinary person examining the records would not confuse it with any existing registered name; merely swapping “Inc.” for “Corp.” or inserting an initial article (“The”) is not sufficient.

Critically: the distinguishability standard is narrow and should never be mistaken for trademark clearance. A name can be available under Washington corporate law while infringing a federal trademark. Name selection is effectively a two-step inquiry: first, run a search against the Secretary of State's online database; second, run a broader commercial-availability search (trademark, domain, trade name).

Authorized Shares — The Minimum Capital Structure

RCW 23B.06.010 requires the articles to authorize (a) one or more classes or series of shares that together have unlimited voting rights, and (b) one or more classes or series of shares (which may be the same class or series as those with voting rights) that together are entitled to receive the net assets of the corporation upon dissolution. Every Washington corporation must have at least one class of voting common stock and at least one class of residual-claimant common stock — though in simple closely held corporations these always collapse into a single class of common stock bearing both votes and residual rights.

RCW 23B.06.020 governs the creation of classes and series, the power to fix preferences and rights by board resolution (a “blank check” preferred stock authorization), and the minimum informational content required when multiple classes or series are created.

Registered Agent

Article 4 of chapter 23.95 RCW requires every Washington corporation to maintain a registered agent with a physical Washington street address. No PO boxes. The agent may be a natural person WA resident or an entity authorized to do business in WA. Commercial registered agent services are widely available in Washington at modest annual cost. Failure to maintain a registered agent is grounds for administrative dissolution under RCW 23B.14.210.

Articles of Incorporation — Optional Content (RCW 23B.02.020(2))

Beyond the four mandatory elements, articles may include any of the following optional provisions:

ProvisionStatutory basisTypical use
Initial directors 23B.02.020(2)(a) Identifies the first board without need for an incorporator meeting
Purpose or powers limitations 23B.02.020(2)(b) Narrower purpose than “any lawful business” (uncommon for for-profit corporations)
Bylaw-equivalent provisions 23B.02.020(2)(c) Provisions that would otherwise appear in bylaws but are elevated to articles for greater permanence
Director liability limitation 23B.08.320 Eliminates or limits director monetary liability for certain breaches of the duty of care; post-Smith v. Van Gorkom standard charter provision
Indemnification provisions 23B.08.560 Makes indemnification mandatory rather than permissive; broadens scope beyond the statutory default
Business opportunity disclaimer 23B.08.735(1)(b) Added in 2020 (Laws of 2020, ch. 194, § 3); opts out of the default corporate opportunity doctrine for directors sitting on multiple boards
Forum selection clause 23B.02.080 Added in 2017; requires internal corporate proceedings to be commenced in specified Washington courts
Par value of shares 23B.06.210 Rarely used under modern practice; the 1979 MBCA revision abandoned par value as a meaningful concept

The optional provisions illustrate Washington's approach to corporate law: a small set of mandatory defaults and a larger set of permissions. Each optional provision represents a decision point at which a Washington corporation can customize its governance relative to the statutory baseline.

Effect of Filing (RCW 23B.02.030)

Corporate existence begins when articles are filed, unless a delayed effective date is specified. The Secretary of State's filing is conclusive proof of incorporation.

This bright-line treatment is one of the most important features of Washington's formation regime. Before the effective date: no corporation, no limited liability, no corporate existence. After the effective date: full corporate existence, immediately and without any further action. The delayed effective date option allows the founders to specify a future effective date (for example, the first day of the calendar year, or a date chosen for tax or accounting reasons).

The “conclusive proof” language is strong. Once the Secretary of State has filed the articles, no party can later contest the corporation's existence on the ground of formation defect (at least in proceedings to which the corporation is a party). Defects in the articles, if discovered, can be cured through articles of amendment under RCW 23B.10 without retroactive invalidation of the corporation's existence.

Abolition of De Facto Corporation and Corporation-by-Estoppel

Along with RCW 23B.02.040, section 23B.02.030 abolished both the de facto corporation doctrine and the corporation-by-estoppel doctrine in Washington. The RMBCA Committee Comment (Official Text at 44–46 (1984)) had concluded that the protective function of these common-law doctrines could be supplied more cleanly through the filing and effective-date mechanisms of the Act itself. Washington accepted that reasoning and codified the abolition in 1989.

Preincorporation Liability (RCW 23B.02.040)

Persons who act on behalf of a corporation knowing there was no incorporation are jointly and severally liable for liabilities they create. Critically, persons who also knew there was no incorporation are excluded from recovery. This provision protects third-party reliance, not insiders.

The practical significance is that preincorporation liability arises from knowingly purporting to act as a corporation when one does not exist, not from mere formation delays or drafting errors. An incorporator who files articles that are technically defective is not exposed to liability under this section, because the defect is not knowing misrepresentation. An incorporator who does business as “Acme Corp.” for six months before actually filing articles is exposed.

Organizational Meeting (RCW 23B.02.050)

After incorporation, the corporation must be organized — directors elected, officers appointed, bylaws adopted, initial share issuances authorized. RCW 23B.02.050 governs the procedure:

  • If initial directors are named in articles: the named directors hold the organizational meeting.
  • If not named: the incorporator(s) hold the meeting to either elect directors or elect a board to complete organization.
  • Written consent alternative: Board action can substitute for meeting via unanimous written consent.
  • Initial report: The corporation must also deliver an initial report per RCW 23.95.255.

Most Washington formations today proceed by written consent rather than in-person meeting. The incorporator (or initial directors) signs a “Unanimous Written Consent in Lieu of Organizational Meeting” that accomplishes all the required organizational acts in a single document.

Bylaws (RCW 23B.02.060)

Initial bylaws must be adopted by the incorporators or by the board at the organizational meeting. Bylaws may address any governance matter not infringing on exclusive board authority under RCW 23B.08.010(2)(b) or conflicting with other law, articles, or a shareholder agreement under RCW 23B.07.320.

The conflict hierarchy in Washington corporate governance:

  1. Shareholder agreement (RCW 23B.07.320) — within its authorized scope, trumps everything below
  2. Articles of incorporation — the corporate constitution
  3. Bylaws — the corporate operating rules
  4. Board resolutions — particular decisions made under the above

Bylaws are where a corporation's governance comes alive. They specify board meeting procedures, shareholder meeting mechanics, officer positions and duties, signature authority, record-keeping requirements, and any of a hundred other operational details.

Emergency Bylaws (RCW 23B.02.070)

Emergency bylaws activate only during a defined emergency: inability to assemble a quorum due to a catastrophic event — natural disaster, terrorist attack, pandemic, loss of key personnel. They may designate substitute directors and modify meeting or quorum requirements. Actions taken in good faith under emergency bylaws bind the corporation and cannot create director liability.

The emergency bylaws framework was, in retrospect, prescient. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2022 created exactly the kind of situation the provisions were designed to address: widespread inability to hold in-person meetings, need for remote deliberation, occasional difficulty in maintaining quorum with directors unable to travel or become ill. Corporations with well-drafted emergency bylaws were better positioned to navigate the pandemic than those relying on default provisions.

Forum Selection (RCW 23B.02.080)

Added in 2017, RCW 23B.02.080 permits articles or bylaws to require that internal corporate proceedings be commenced and maintained in specified Washington courts. An “internal corporate proceeding” includes:

  • Any proceeding asserting a claim based on a violation of a duty under Washington law by a current or former director, officer, or shareholder in such capacity
  • Any derivative proceeding (in the right of the corporation)
  • Any proceeding asserting a claim arising under Title 23B or the corporation's articles or bylaws
  • Any proceeding concerning the internal affairs of the corporation

What Forum Selection Cannot Do

The provision is bounded by three important limits. A forum selection clause cannot:

  1. Confer jurisdiction on any court that would not otherwise have it
  2. Prohibit commencing or maintaining an internal corporate proceeding in the courts of Washington
  3. Require that internal corporate claims be determined by arbitration

These limits reflect Washington's judgment that the corporate law relationship is a creature of Washington law and that Washington courts should always be available to adjudicate disputes about it. If the specified courts lack jurisdiction, the proceeding moves to any Washington court that has it.

Comparative Note

Forum selection bylaws became a major topic in US corporate law in the early 2010s, when Delaware Chancery Court (in the Chevron and FedEx cases) upheld board-adopted bylaws requiring derivative actions to be brought in Delaware. The Delaware General Corporation Law was amended in 2015 to codify this authority. Washington's 2017 amendment followed that national trend but retained the safeguards noted above, preserving Washington courts' availability as a backstop forum.

The Formation Workflow

A typical Washington corporate formation proceeds through the following steps:

  1. Name clearance — Search the Secretary of State's database for distinguishability; optionally reserve the name under RCW 23.95.320; clear federal trademark and domain availability.
  2. Registered agent engagement — Designate a natural person WA resident or engage a commercial registered agent service.
  3. Articles drafting — Prepare articles meeting the mandatory requirements of RCW 23B.02.020(1), plus any optional provisions desired (director liability limitation, indemnification, forum selection, SPC designation, business opportunity disclaimer).
  4. Articles filing — Deliver to the Secretary of State through the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS) or in paper form; pay the filing fee; corporation exists upon acceptance.
  5. Organizational action — Adopt bylaws, elect or confirm directors, appoint officers, authorize share issuances, designate bank accounts, authorize initial contracts. Typically done by unanimous written consent.
  6. Initial report filing — Deliver initial report to Secretary of State under RCW 23.95.255(1).
  7. Stock issuances — Issue shares to founders, record in share register, deliver certificates (if certificated), document consideration received.
  8. Ancillary registrations — UBI via Washington Business License application, Department of Revenue registration, Labor and Industries account if employees anticipated, federal EIN.

Steps 1–4 constitute “formation proper” under Title 23B. Steps 5–8 are the downstream organizational and regulatory matters that, while not part of Title 23B itself, are practically inseparable from the formation process. A properly organized Washington corporation has completed all eight steps within a few days or weeks of filing the articles.
