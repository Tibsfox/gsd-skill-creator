The central thesis is that Washington's corporate law, like many MBCA-family statutes, is best understood not as a detailed prescription of corporate behavior but as a leveraged substrate — a compact layer of enabling rules, mandatory defaults, and opt-out provisions that trust the parties to compose the rest.

Washington as an MBCA-Family Jurisdiction

American corporate law has, since the middle of the twentieth century, bifurcated into two principal traditions. The first is the Delaware tradition, anchored in the Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL), refined through case law by the Delaware Court of Chancery and Delaware Supreme Court, and exported through charter competition. The second is the Model Business Corporation Act tradition — a body of statutory text promulgated and periodically revised by the Corporate Laws Committee of the American Bar Association's Business Law Section, and adopted, in whole or in substantial part, by approximately thirty-six states.

Washington State sits firmly within the MBCA tradition. It has been there since 1965, when the Legislature enacted Title 23A RCW, “substantially patterned after the model business corporation act, which was prepared as a research project of the American Bar Foundation.” It reaffirmed that tradition in 1989 when the Legislature replaced Title 23A with Title 23B — the Washington Business Corporation Act ("WBCA") — based on the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act. It has continued in that tradition through every subsequent amendment cycle, including the 2024 amendments.

To understand Washington corporate law one must first understand the model whose DNA pervades it.

From Uniform to Model: The ABA's Philosophical Shift

One of the more interesting historical features of the MBCA is that it is a model act, not a uniform act. The distinction matters.

Until 1943, there had been a Uniform Business Corporation Act, drafted by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws and promulgated in its original form in 1928. This was the act on which Washington's 1933 statute was patterned. Uniform acts are designed to produce identical text across adopting states, in the service of interstate consistency. But the Uniform Business Corporation Act ran into resistance. State bar associations and legislatures were reluctant to surrender the ability to tailor corporate law to local circumstances.

In 1943, the Uniform Business Corporation Act was formally withdrawn as a "uniform" act and renamed A Model or State Business Corporation Act, reflecting a deliberate shift in philosophy. The revised posture was that states should have the opportunity to depart from the model in ways that would recognize special local considerations and allow experimentation with different approaches. The ABA's subsequent work took up that model-act philosophy: publish a well-drafted template, invite adoption, and anticipate that each adopting state will modify it.

That philosophy has been one of the most important features of the MBCA tradition. Delaware, famously, has never adopted the MBCA. Many other states have adopted it only in part, or have adopted it and then drifted away through amendments that tracked Delaware innovations rather than MBCA updates. The result is a family of statutes that share a common DNA but exhibit real genetic variation. Washington is a fairly faithful MBCA adopter but has its own Washington-specific mutations.

Key Drafters of the Model Act

A full prosopography of the MBCA's drafters would fill a volume of its own. Three names deserve mention in any history of the statute:

Ray Garrett Jr. (1920–1980)

A Chicago lawyer who served on the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws during the formative period of the MBCA. His article “History, Purpose and Summary of the Model Business Corporation Act,” published in Business Lawyer in 1950, is the standard early account of the Act. Garrett later served as chairman of the editorial committee for the Model Business Corporation Act Annotated. He was Chairman of the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission from 1973 to 1975, a position that gave him a national platform for coherent corporate governance. Garrett represents the MBCA's early Chicago orientation and its close ties to federal securities regulation.

Elliott Goldstein

A principal drafter of the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act. His article “Revision of the Model Business Corporation Act,” published in the Texas Law Review in 1985, is the leading contemporaneous account of the revision's goals, methods, and substantive choices. With Robert W. Hamilton, Goldstein also published “The Revised Model Business Corporation Act” in Business Lawyer in 1983, a preview article signaling the direction of the revision to the bar.

Robert W. Hamilton (1931–2015)

Minerva House Drysdale Regents Chair in Law Emeritus at the University of Texas School of Law. Arguably the single most influential academic in the MBCA tradition from the late 1970s through the 2000s. He served as the reporter for the 1984 RMBCA revision, meaning he was primarily responsible for drafting the text and the official commentary. Hamilton's treatises, casebooks, and scholarly articles shaped the thinking of a generation of lawyers and judges in MBCA jurisdictions. He was not a Washington lawyer, but his influence on the 1984 RMBCA — and through it on Title 23B — is unmistakable.

Beyond these three, dozens of distinguished practitioners and academics have served on the Committee on Corporate Laws over the decades. One of the striking features of the MBCA project is how much of the drafting work has been done by practicing lawyers on a volunteer basis. The result is a statute that bears the fingerprints of an enormous number of people, no single one of whom can be credited as its author.

Washington's Statutory Lineage

  1. Territorial incorporation acts (1853–1889) — Brief, formal statutes concerned with chartering mechanics; inherited from mid-nineteenth-century midwestern and eastern state practice.
  2. Statehood-era patchwork (1889–1933) — A body of law that grew by accretion, differed materially from state to state, and was not comprehensively rationalized.
  3. The 1933 Washington Business Corporation Act — Patterned on the 1928 Uniform Business Corporation Act; effective January 1, 1934; established for the first time a single coherent statutory framework for formation, governance, and dissolution. Retained par value stock and relatively prescriptive regulatory impulse.
  4. Title 23A RCW (1965, effective July 1, 1967) — Chapter 53, Laws of 1965; first Washington adoption of the Model Business Corporation Act; modernized with MBCA duty framework, indemnification, and merger provisions, but retained par value and pre-dated key 1970s–1980s refinements.
  5. Title 23B RCW (1989, effective July 1, 1990) — Chapter 165, Laws of 1989; the current statute; tracks the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act in structure and content; 19 chapters; abandons par value; modernizes director duties, indemnification, mergers, and dissolution.
  6. Chapter 23B.25 — Social Purpose Corporations (2012) — HB 2239, effective June 7, 2012; Washington's response to the benefit corporation movement; a permissive “may consider” framework distinct from the B Lab model.
  7. 2014–2023 refinements — Forum selection (23B.02.080), emergency bylaws, business opportunity disclaimer (2020 c 194 § 3), electronic filings, and other incremental improvements.
  8. Chapter 22, Laws of 2024 — Majority-vote default for article amendments (section 20, amending 23B.10.030); effective August 1, 2024, for newly formed corporations; the most recent significant amendment as of early 2026.

The 1989 Enactment of Title 23B RCW

The Drafting Process

The 1989 Washington Business Corporation Act was, as corporate recodifications in American states go, a relatively efficient and well-managed project. The principal drafting venue was the WSBA Business Law Section, within which a Corporate Act Revision Committee undertook the detailed drafting. The Committee was composed of Washington practitioners with significant corporate law experience — large-firm transactional lawyers, small-firm general counsel, academic advisors — together with liaison representatives from the Secretary of State's Corporations Division and the Legislature.

The Committee's mandate was to prepare a replacement for Title 23A RCW that would:

  1. Track the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act in general structure and content;
  2. Preserve those features of Title 23A that had worked well and that Washington practitioners were comfortable with;
  3. Make Washington-specific modifications where Washington law, practice, or policy differed from the national default;
  4. Fix drafting problems and clarify ambiguities that had accumulated over the preceding two decades;
  5. Modernize where the RMBCA represented a clear improvement (par value, mergers, director duties, indemnification); and
  6. Produce a text that would be enacted with minimal amendment and easy for Washington courts to apply.

The Committee worked through the late 1980s, producing draft after draft, each accompanied by commentary explaining the reasoning behind each section and identifying points of divergence from the RMBCA. When the drafts were substantially complete, the Committee presented them to the WSBA Board of Governors and, with the Board's endorsement, to the Legislature. That drafting commentary was later preserved in the WBCA Sourcebook, a practitioner reference published and periodically updated by the WSBA Business Law Section.

The Legislative Enactment

The 1989 WBCA was introduced in the 51st Washington Legislature, enacted as chapter 165 of the Laws of 1989 (customary citation: Laws of 1989, ch. 165), and became effective on July 1, 1990 — giving existing corporations a full year to prepare. The Senate and House Journals contain extensive documentation of the legislative process. The Sourcebook's section-by-section commentary regularly cites specific pages of the 1989 Senate Journal as authority for the understanding of particular provisions.

An important feature of the legislative record is that the Committee's official commentary — prepared by the WSBA Business Law Section — was adopted as part of the legislative history of the Act. This gives the Sourcebook's commentary a quasi-official status: it is not merely a practitioner's treatise but a document that Washington courts have treated as persuasive authority when interpreting ambiguous provisions of Title 23B.

Washington-Specific Divergences from the 1984 RMBCA

Title 23B is not a slavish copy of the RMBCA. In a number of areas, Washington diverged in ways that reflected Washington practice, case law, or policy:

  • De facto corporation and corporation by estoppel. Sections 23B.02.030 and 23B.02.040 together abolished the de facto corporation doctrine and the corporation-by-estoppel doctrine in Washington, consistent with the RMBCA Committee Comment's view that the protection those doctrines provided could be supplied more cleanly through the filing and effective-date mechanisms of the Act itself.
  • Director liability limitations. RCW 23B.08.320 permits corporations to limit or eliminate director personal liability for certain breaches of the duty of care. The scope of the Washington provision is somewhat narrower than Delaware's DGCL § 102(b)(7) but broader than the default RMBCA provision.
  • Indemnification. Chapter 23B.08 contains a detailed indemnification regime with mandatory indemnification for successful defense, advancement of expenses, and non-exclusivity language.
  • Shareholder agreements. RCW 23B.07.320 authorizes shareholder agreements that depart from some of the Act's default governance rules; broadly aligned with RMBCA section 7.32 but with Washington-specific refinements.
  • Dissenters' rights. Chapter 23B.13 tracks the RMBCA but has been amended over time to reflect Washington-specific judgments about the scope of appraisal.
  • Dissolution. Chapter 23B.14 includes a Washington-specific administrative-dissolution procedure for failure to file annual reports or pay fees.

The divergences, taken individually, are incremental. Taken together, they give Title 23B a distinctly Washington character.

Judicial Interpretation, 1987–2010

Washington appellate decisions interpreting Title 23B are not as numerous as Delaware decisions interpreting the DGCL — a fact that reflects Washington's smaller corporate docket rather than any deficiency in the statute — but they include several cases that are important for understanding how the Act has been applied in practice.

CaseCitationSignificance
Para-Medical Leasing, Inc. v. Hangen 48 Wn. App. 389, 739 P.2d 717 (1987) Decided under Title 23A but repeatedly cited post-1989 for its broad application of the business judgment rule to corporate officers acting in management capacity. Dr. Gene Mason, sole shareholder of a small leasing company, sued his interim CPA-manager Todd Hangen for negligent mismanagement; the court held the BJR “shields the corporate officer from liability so long as he acts in good faith without a corrupt motive.”
Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. v. Bucholz 45 Wn. App. 502, 728 P.2d 597 (1986) Minority shareholder Edward Blackburn sued majority shareholder Terry Bucholz for self-dealing expenditures totaling $534,797 between 1980 and September 1984. The court stated the Washington fiduciary framework: “directors and officers stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation they serve and are not permitted to retain any personal profit or advantage gleaned 'on the side.'”
In re Spokane Concrete Products, Inc. 126 Wn.2d 269, 892 P.2d 98 (1995) Washington Supreme Court; standard reference for BJR scope under the 1989 WBCA. Held the BJR presumption "is not limitless" — plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, disloyalty, or bad faith.
Grassmueck v. Barnett 281 F. Supp. 2d 1227 (W.D. Wash. 2003) Federal district court applying Washington law. Identified four categories of conduct that pierce Washington's director exculpation: intentional misconduct, knowing violation of law, conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310, or transactions from which the director receives improper personal benefit.
Rodriguez v. Loudeye Corp. 144 Wn. App. 709, 189 P.3d 168 (2008) Clean articulation of Washington's three primary director fiduciary duties: care, loyalty, and good faith. Tracks the RMBCA duty framework.
Riss v. Angel 131 Wn.2d 612, 934 P.2d 669 (1997) Primarily a condominium case but contains a widely cited statement of the general director fiduciary standard: “exercise ordinary care in performing their duties and to act reasonably and in good faith.” The “ordinary care” formulation anchors Washington's MBCA-style duty of care, in contrast to Delaware's “gross negligence” standard.

Taken together, these cases give a picture of Washington's judicial gloss on the 1989 WBCA. The business judgment rule operates as a rebuttable presumption. Directors are protected when they act in good faith, on an informed basis, and without self-dealing. The duty of loyalty is robust and is enforced through disgorgement. Exculpation provisions are effective within their stated scope but do not reach disloyalty, intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, or transactions from which directors derive improper personal benefit. Washington courts have applied these principles pragmatically and have generally resisted the temptation to import Delaware case law wholesale.

The 2012 Social Purpose Corporation

Background: The Benefit Corporation Movement

In the late 2000s and early 2010s, a number of American states began experimenting with new corporate forms that would allow for-profit corporations to formally incorporate social and environmental goals into their mission statements and their directors' fiduciary duties. The motivation was twofold: first, to address a perceived gap in traditional corporate law; and second, to provide a visible signaling device by which entrepreneurs and investors could identify mission-driven companies.

The movement had two principal strands:

  • The B Lab strand. Associated with the nonprofit B Lab (founded in 2006), this strand promoted a specific Model Benefit Corporation Legislation: tightly structured, with mandatory directorial consideration of specified stakeholder interests, mandatory annual benefit reports, and third-party certification. Maryland was first (April 2010), Vermont followed (signed May 19, 2010, effective July 1, 2011). By the early 2010s a growing list of states had followed.
  • The flexible strand. Less prescriptive. California's S.B. 201 (signed October 9, 2011, effective January 1, 2012) created the “Flexible Purpose Corporation,” which permitted but did not require directors to consider specified non-shareholder interests. In 2014, California renamed the form the “Social Purpose Corporation” by S.B. 1301 (effective January 1, 2015).

Washington's 2012 enactment fits into the second strand. Washington chose to create a Social Purpose Corporation rather than a Benefit Corporation, and it did so in a way that paralleled California's Flexible Purpose Corporation experiment more closely than it paralleled Maryland's B Lab model.

HB 2239 — The Washington Enactment

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation was introduced in the 2012 regular session as House Bill 2239. The bill was the product of roughly two years of drafting work by the Corporate Act Revision Committee of the WSBA Business Law Section. The Committee considered, and ultimately rejected, the direct adoption of the B Lab Model Benefit Corporation Legislation. The principal reasons were:

  1. Rigidity. The B Lab model required directors to consider a specified list of stakeholder interests and imposed a “general public benefit” obligation that the Committee regarded as potentially difficult to apply in practice and potentially ripe for litigation.
  2. Third-party certification. The B Lab model contemplated the use of third-party standards for measuring benefit performance. The Committee was concerned that embedding third-party certification in state law would give a private organization a quasi-regulatory role over Washington corporations.
  3. Reporting burden. The Committee was concerned that the mandatory benefit reporting burden might deter Washington entrepreneurs from adopting the form.
  4. Enforcement concerns. The “benefit enforcement proceedings” contemplated by the B Lab model could create a novel category of litigation risk.

After considering these concerns, the Committee proposed, and the Legislature adopted, a different approach. HB 2239 was signed by Governor Christine Gregoire on March 30, 2012, and became effective on June 7, 2012 as chapter 23B.25 RCW.

The Nomenclature Choice

A small but meaningful point deserves explicit attention: the choice of nomenclature. Washington did not call its new corporate form a “benefit corporation.” It called it a “social purpose corporation.” The difference is not accidental. “Benefit corporation” in common usage carries the specific connotation of the B Lab model — tightly structured form, mandatory stakeholder consideration, implicit or explicit third-party certification. Calling the Washington form a “benefit corporation” would have invited confusion with both the B Lab certification regime (which is a private certification) and the Maryland-style statutory benefit corporation.

“Social purpose corporation” is a term associated with a lighter-touch, more flexible approach. California was using (or would shortly begin using) the same term. The nomenclature choice communicates a substantive message: this is not a B Lab benefit corporation; it is something different.

2014 Corrective Amendments

As often happens with new legislation, experience with the 2012 SPC statute revealed drafting issues that called for corrective amendments. In 2014 the Legislature adopted amendments that clarified formation requirements, director duty language, conversion procedures, and annual report format. The amendments were not controversial; they represented the kind of iterative refinement new corporate law regimes typically require during their first years of operation. The fundamental structure of the SPC remained intact: a permissive, light-touch framework.

The 2014–2023 Refinement Period

Forum Selection (RCW 23B.02.080)

RCW 23B.02.080 authorizes articles of incorporation or bylaws to require that specified categories of “internal corporate proceedings” be commenced and maintained exclusively in a specified court or courts. An “internal corporate proceeding” includes derivative actions, breach of fiduciary duty claims, claims under Title 23B, and internal affairs claims.

The provision's scope is broad but bounded. A forum selection provision may not: confer jurisdiction on any court that would not otherwise have it; prohibit commencing or maintaining an internal corporate proceeding in the courts of Washington; or require that internal corporate claims be determined by arbitration. These limits reflect Washington's judgment that the corporate law relationship is a creature of Washington law and that Washington courts should always be available.

Emergency Bylaws

Another set of amendments during this period addressed emergency situations. The amendments permit emergency bylaws that take effect under specified emergency conditions and authorize remote meetings, alternate directors, and other operational flexibility. These amendments proved prescient: the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2022 created exactly the conditions the provisions were designed to address.

The 2020 Business Opportunity Disclaimer

In 2020, the Washington Legislature enacted amendments (2020 c 194 § 3, building on earlier 2019 c 141 § 1) to permit articles of incorporation to include a business opportunity disclaimer. This addresses a specific problem in venture capital and private equity contexts: directors sitting on multiple boards who, in the ordinary course, encounter opportunities that might be of interest to more than one portfolio company. Under traditional corporate opportunity doctrine, directors may not personally take advantage of opportunities belonging to the corporation — but the rule creates practical difficulties for VC/PE firms placing partners on multiple boards.

The 2020 amendments permit corporations to opt out of the default corporate opportunity doctrine through an articles provision. The underlying provision is RCW 23B.08.735. This is another example of Washington's architectural preference for defaults that can be varied by careful drafting.

The 2024 Amendments — Recalibrating the Default Vote

The most recent significant amendment to Title 23B is the 2024 amendment to RCW 23B.10.030, which changed the default shareholder vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation. The amendment was enacted as section 20 of chapter 22 of the Laws of 2024 (customary citation: Laws of 2024, ch. 22 or 2024 c 22). It took effect on August 1, 2024.

Before and After

Before the 2024 amendment, RCW 23B.10.030 required approval of an amendment to the articles of incorporation by the affirmative vote of two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast, together with two-thirds of each voting group entitled to vote separately. The only exception was for public companies, for which the default was already a simple majority.

After the 2024 amendment, for corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, the default is a simple majority of votes entitled to be cast, together with a simple majority of each separately voting group. For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the prior two-thirds default continues to apply. Articles of incorporation may increase the threshold (to two-thirds, three-fourths, unanimity) but may not decrease it below a majority.

Why the Change?

The change is not a radical departure from national practice. Most MBCA-family states have moved, or are moving, toward a simple majority default for article amendments. Delaware's DGCL section 242(b) has long provided a simple majority default. The RMBCA has been revised in a similar direction. Washington's pre-2024 two-thirds default was, by the late 2010s, an outlier.

The practical effect of the amendment for newly formed corporations is to reduce the friction of article amendments. A corporation that needs to adjust its capitalization, change its name, adopt forum selection, convert to SPC status, or make any of the many other changes that amendments typically accomplish, can now do so with a simple majority rather than a supermajority. This is particularly meaningful for venture-backed startups that amend articles frequently during early-stage financings.

Why Bifurcate by Date of Formation?

The bifurcation of the default by date of formation is an interesting drafting choice. It preserves the settled expectations of existing corporations — which were formed on the understanding that a two-thirds vote would be required, and whose shareholders may have made investment decisions in reliance on that expectation — while shifting the default for new corporations to align with national practice. The alternatives (retroactive change, or no change at all) each had significant drawbacks. The bifurcated approach gives new corporations the benefit of the modern default while leaving old corporations with the familiar rule, and it allows old corporations to opt into the new rule at their own pace.

People and Institutions

The Secretary of State's Corporations Division

The Washington Secretary of State is a constitutional officer responsible for elections administration, corporations and charities filings, archives, and library services. Within the office, the Corporations and Charities Division administers the corporate law regime: receiving and processing articles of incorporation, annual reports, and other filings; maintaining the corporate registry; issuing certificates of good standing; and administering dissolution.

A significant modernization came in 1977, with the introduction of the Unified Business Identifier (UBI) system. The UBI is a nine-digit number assigned to each business entity, serving as a universal identifier across multiple state agencies (Secretary of State, Department of Revenue, Labor & Industries, Employment Security). Before UBI, each agency maintained its own identification scheme; after, all agencies shared a common identifier. The UBI system dramatically improved the coherence of state-business regulatory interactions.

Through the 1990s and 2000s, the Corporations Division transitioned from paper to digital filings. Today, the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS) supports electronic incorporation, amendments, annual reports, and registry searches. The current Secretary of State, Steve Hobbs, assumed office in 2021 after appointment by Governor Jay Inslee and has continued the modernization work. Previous Secretaries — Kim Wyman, Sam Reed, Ralph Munro — each contributed to the Division's evolution over their respective tenures.

The WSBA Business Law Section

The Washington State Bar Association's Business Law Section has been, for most of the history of modern Washington corporate law, the principal venue for drafting and reviewing corporate law legislation. The Section convenes committees — most notably the Corporate Act Revision Committee — to propose, draft, and review amendments to Title 23B. The Section also maintains the WBCA Sourcebook, a practitioner reference collecting the Act, legislative history, Committee commentary, and judicial case notes. The 5th edition was published in 2016.

The Section is a voluntary organization, and its members serve on committees without compensation. This is in keeping with the broader American tradition of bar-led law reform, in which practicing lawyers contribute their time to the improvement of the legal system. The quality of Washington's corporate law is, in significant measure, a reflection of the quality of the Business Law Section's work over the decades since the 1989 WBCA was enacted.

The Leveraged Substrate

The Act provides a small set of mandatory rules, a larger set of default rules that can be varied by the articles of incorporation or bylaws, and an explicit invitation for parties to customize governance through careful drafting.

One of the defining features of the 1989 WBCA is its preference for a minimal leveraged substrate approach to corporate law. The Act does not prescribe a minimum or maximum number of directors; it provides a default of one or more and invites the articles or bylaws to specify. It does not prescribe how directors are elected; it provides a default of plurality voting and permits cumulative voting if the articles so provide. It does not prescribe quorum or voting thresholds for shareholder action on most matters; it provides defaults and permits the articles or bylaws to vary.

The consequence is that a Washington corporation can have a governance structure that looks very similar to the Act's default structure, or it can have a governance structure that has been heavily customized. Both are permitted. The Act does not force a particular governance pattern on all corporations; it provides the building blocks and lets the parties assemble them.

This is the leveraged substrate idea. The substrate is small — the Act's mandatory rules, once identified, are relatively few — but the leverage is high. A small number of well-chosen mandatory rules can produce a legal environment in which a wide range of corporate forms can flourish. The analogy to operating system design, or to the design of a programming language with a small core and a rich standard library, is not incidental. It reflects a particular attitude toward law-drafting: an attitude that trusts the parties, is skeptical of heavy prescription, and prioritizes clarity and composability over comprehensive regulation.
