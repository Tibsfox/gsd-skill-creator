The Washington Social Purpose Corporation

A doctrinal, historical, and comparative analysis of RCW Chapter 23B.25 — the softest benefit corporation statute in the United States, and why that is by design

Nothing here constitutes legal advice or an attorney-client relationship. Anyone contemplating formation of a Washington Social Purpose Corporation should retain a licensed Washington attorney.
The Washington SPC statute is, by design, the weakest of the principal United States benefit corporation regimes measured against the axes of director duty intensity, third-party accountability, and enforcement. It is simultaneously the most flexible, imposing few prescriptive content requirements on social purpose statements or performance reporting. Whether that flexibility is a feature or a defect depends on one's prior about the function benefit corporation legislation should perform.

The Central Claim

In 2012, the Washington State Legislature enacted Chapter 23B.25 RCW, creating the Social Purpose Corporation — a new corporate form authorizing Washington for-profit corporations to pursue enumerated social, environmental, and stakeholder objectives alongside, and not merely incidental to, the creation of economic value. Washington thereby joined a wave of roughly forty United States jurisdictions that, between 2010 and 2020, enacted statutes enabling mission-driven corporate governance.

But Washington conspicuously did not adopt the “model benefit corporation legislation” promoted by B Lab and embraced by Maryland, California, New York, and most other benefit-corporation states. Instead, Washington's drafters chose a distinctly softer and more flexible architecture, rooted not in the B Lab template but in California's Flexible Purpose Corporation (“FPC”) statute of 2011 — itself later renamed the “Social Purpose Corporation” by California S.B. 1301 in 2014.

The central claim of this analysis is descriptive: Washington's drafters deliberately chose the softest duty formulation in the US benefit corporation landscape, and they did so for specific, articulable reasons.

Part I — Historical Context

The Perceived Barrier: Dodge v. Ford and Milton Friedman

No serious discussion of benefit corporation legislation can begin without acknowledging the peculiar position the shareholder primacy doctrine occupies in American corporate law. It is simultaneously the subject of near-universal folk belief among business practitioners, journalists, and even many lawyers — and, as a matter of positive law, an almost entirely judge-made and scholar-constructed gloss rather than anything approaching a statutory rule.

The canonical judicial statement usually invoked in support of shareholder primacy is Dodge v. Ford Motor Co., 170 N.W. 668 (Mich. 1919). Henry Ford, facing a suit from the Dodge brothers over reinvestment of surplus rather than a special dividend, explained: “my ambition is to employ still more men, to spread the benefits of this industrial system to the greatest possible number.” The Michigan Supreme Court held for the Dodges. The language that subsequent generations seized upon:

A business corporation is organized and carried on primarily for the profit of the stockholders. The powers of the directors are to be employed for that end. The discretion of directors is to be exercised in the choice of means to attain that end, and does not extend to a change in the end itself, to the reduction of profits, or to the nondistribution of profits among stockholders in order to devote them to other purposes.

Read in isolation, this passage can be taken as a categorical rule of shareholder primacy. Read in context, it is less obviously so. As Professor Lynn Stout has influentially argued, the passage is dicta, and the subsequent use of Dodge as a statement of positive corporate law obligations is largely a twentieth-century scholarly invention. Stout's empirical observation is striking: in the six decades from 1919 to 1979, Dodge v. Ford was cited in published opinions only thirty-seven times; over the four decades from 1980 to 2019, citations exploded to more than 1,100. The timing of this surge tracks not the emergence of any new legal rule but the rise of a particular ideological position within corporate law scholarship.

If Dodge supplied the perceived juridical foundation, Milton Friedman's 13 September 1970 New York Times Magazine essay — “The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits” — supplied the popular intellectual frame. Friedman argued that a corporate executive is an “employee” of the shareholders with a direct responsibility to make as much money as possible within the basic rules of society. The essay is widely credited with — or blamed for — popularizing a shareholder primacy framing that, combined with Dodge's judicial gloss, gradually hardened into what felt to mission-oriented founders in the 2000s like an impenetrable legal wall.

Whether that wall was real or imagined is debated. Modern scholarship generally concludes that most of the perceived barriers were overstated. But they felt real to founders, and they felt real to the lawyers advising them. Benefit corporation legislation was designed to eliminate the uncertainty, not to change the substantive law of fiduciary duty so much as to insulate mission-driven decision-making from plausible litigation attack.

B Lab and the Model Legislation

B Lab was founded in 2006 by three former corporate executives: Jay Coen Gilbert (formerly of AND 1), Bart Houlahan (AND 1's former president), and Andrew Kassoy (a private equity veteran). Their animating insight was that investors, customers, and employees of socially oriented businesses lacked a reliable way to distinguish genuinely mission-driven enterprises from those merely engaging in marketing-grade corporate social responsibility. B Lab's initial product was the Certified B Corporation mark, earned through the B Impact Assessment.

Certification alone proved insufficient. It did not alter the legal duties of directors. Working with a group of corporate lawyers led by Bill Clark of Drinker Biddle & Reath, B Lab developed the Model Benefit Corporation Legislation. Its principles were fourfold:

  1. Purpose. A benefit corporation must have the purpose of creating “general public benefit,” defined as “a material positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole, as assessed against a third-party standard.”
  2. Accountability. Directors shall consider the effects of any action or inaction upon: (i) shareholders; (ii) employees and workforce; (iii) customers as beneficiaries; (iv) community and societal factors; (v) the local and global environment; (vi) the short-term and long-term interests of the benefit corporation; and (vii) the ability of the benefit corporation to accomplish its public benefit purposes.
  3. Transparency. A benefit corporation must publish an annual benefit report assessing social and environmental performance against a third-party standard.
  4. Enforcement. A new cause of action, the benefit enforcement proceeding, is created.

The “third-party standard” requirement is central. It does not literally require B Lab's own B Impact Assessment — any “recognized standard” developed by an independent organization could qualify — but in practice the B Impact Assessment is the dominant choice.

Washington's Distinctive Path

When the WSBA Business Law Section began in 2011 to consider whether Washington should adopt benefit corporation legislation, its corporate law practitioners looked not primarily at the Maryland or B Lab model but at California's Flexible Purpose Corporation. California's drafters — members of the Corporations Committee of the State Bar of California's Business Law Section — had deliberately crafted a more permissive framework than the B Lab model, for three reasons:

  1. No mandatory third-party standard. The FPC was free to define its own metrics; no external “recognized standard” was required.
  2. Softer director duty. FPC directors were permitted, not required, to consider the special purposes in their decision-making. A “may consider” rule backed by a safe harbor replaced the B Lab “shall consider” formulation.
  3. More flexible reporting. The FPC's annual report described objectives, efforts, and measures, but was not required to conform to any external standard or framework.

The Washington drafters' reasons for rejecting the B Lab model are discussed in three recurring themes in contemporaneous practitioner writing:

  1. The “recognized standard” concern. Effectively mandating reliance on a single private organization's methodology as a statutory obligation raised both normative concerns about de facto regulatory capture and practical concerns about what would happen if B Lab changed its methodology or ceased to exist.
  2. Justiciability. The B Lab model's “general public benefit” language, defined as “a material positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole,” is difficult to assess judicially. How would a court measure whether such an impact had been achieved?
  3. Mainstreaming. A softer, more flexible statute was more likely to secure consensus and less likely to produce unintended doctrinal spillovers for directors of non-benefit Washington corporations.

Substitute House Bill 2239 was introduced, moved through both chambers, and was signed by Governor Christine Gregoire on March 30, 2012, taking effect June 7, 2012. It is codified as Chapter 23B.25 RCW.

Part II — The Statutory Architecture

Chapter 23B.25 comprises sixteen numbered sections, from RCW 23B.25.005 through RCW 23B.25.150. It is organized roughly into: formation and cessation (sections 005, 010, 130, 140); purpose and articles (sections 020, 030, 040); governance (sections 050, 060, 070, 080); fundamental transactions (sections 090, 100, 110, 120); and reporting (section 150).

Section 005 — Formation and Cessation

A corporation may elect SPC status by one of two methods: (a) at formation, by delivering SPC-conforming articles; or (b) by converting an existing Washington corporation via RCW 23B.25.130. Any SPC may elect to cease to be an SPC via RCW 23B.25.140. The symmetric treatment — any corporation may elect in, any SPC may elect out — reflects a deliberate choice to avoid a one-way “mission lock.” Washington SPCs, unlike UK Community Interest Companies with their irrevocable asset lock, can revoke their SPC status, though only through a heightened voting procedure. Washington's mission commitment is procedurally sticky but ultimately reversible.

Section 010 — Subchapter-Wide Integration

Every provision of Washington's Business Corporation Act applies to SPCs by default, except where Chapter 23B.25 provides otherwise. The SPC is, structurally, an ordinary 23B corporation with an additional purpose overlay, not a fundamentally different entity. A Washington SPC's cap table, dividend policy, redemption rights, class structures, and governance look identical to those of an unmarked 23B corporation. What differs is the purpose statement and the reporting obligation.

Section 020 — General Social Purposes

“Every corporation governed by this chapter must be organized to carry out its business purpose under RCW 23B.03.010 in a manner intended to promote positive short-term or long-term effects of, or minimize adverse short-term or long-term effects of, the corporation's activities upon any or all of (1) the corporation's employees, suppliers, or customers; (2) the local, state, national, or world community; or (3) the environment.”

Several features merit close attention:

  • Three categories, not seven. Washington groups employees, suppliers, and customers into a single bucket; keeps community and environment as separate categories; and omits creditors entirely. This is substantially narrower than the B Lab model's seven-category enumeration.
  • Disjunctive, not conjunctive. “Any or all of” — an SPC is not required to pursue benefits across all three categories. It may focus on employees alone, or environment alone, or any combination.
  • Promote positive or minimize adverse. A bidirectional framing that accommodates different business models — active benefit creation for some, minimization of externalities for others.
  • Short-term or long-term. Explicit textual authorization for directors to prefer long-term effects over quarterly pressure.
  • Procedural, not substantive. The phrase “intended to” supports a procedural reading: what is required is organizational intent, not demonstrated impact.

Section 040 — Articles of Incorporation

The articles of an SPC must include:

  1. A corporate name containing “Social Purpose Corporation” or “SPC”.
  2. A statement that the corporation is organized as an SPC subject to Chapter 23B.25.
  3. A statement of the general social purpose(s) from RCW 23B.25.020.
  4. If applicable, any specific social purpose(s) under RCW 23B.25.030.
  5. A mandatory investor-protection disclosure, substantially:
The mission of this social purpose corporation is not necessarily compatible with and may be contrary to maximizing profits and earnings for shareholders, or maximizing shareholder value in any sale, merger, acquisition, or other similar actions of the corporation.

This mandatory disclosure serves multiple functions. It provides notice to prospective investors. It supplies affirmative defense material for directors later sued for allegedly sacrificing profit in favor of mission. And it contributes to a documentary record showing that shareholders had notice, at the time of investment, that the corporation was not organized on a pure shareholder-wealth-maximization basis.

The articles may also include: provisions converting the “may consider” default into a “shall consider” obligation; provisions requiring third-party standard assessment; enhanced voting thresholds; and limitations on corporate duration. The Washington drafters' decision to make these features optional is significant. An SPC whose founders want a B Lab–style duty can get it by writing those provisions into the charter. But the default is soft.

Part III — Section 050: The “May Consider” Duty

RCW 23B.25.050 is, without exaggeration, the most important provision of Chapter 23B.25. It defines the fiduciary duty landscape that Washington SPC directors inhabit, and it is the principal point at which the Washington statute diverges from the B Lab and Delaware PBC alternatives.

Summarizing the statutory text:

  • Directors must discharge their duties in good faith, with the care of an ordinarily prudent person in a like position, reasonably believed to be in the best interests of the corporation, pursuant to the general director duty provisions of RCW 23B.08.300.
  • In discharging those duties, directors may consider — and give weight to — one or more of the corporation's social purposes as the director deems relevant, unless the articles provide otherwise.
  • Any action that the director reasonably believes will promote the social purposes is deemed to be in the best interests of the corporation.
  • A director is not liable to the corporation, shareholders, or any other person for actions taken in good faith in compliance with these provisions.
  • The chapter creates no duties to, or rights in, persons other than the corporation and its shareholders.

“May Consider” — The Softest Formulation

The word “may” in “may consider” is load-bearing. It is the principal marker distinguishing the Washington statute from the Maryland / B Lab model (which uses “shall consider”) and from the Delaware PBC statute (which imposes an affirmative “balance” duty). Washington gives its directors permission to consider social purposes; it does not require them to do so.

The practical consequence: a Washington SPC director who entirely ignores the corporation's stated social purpose in a particular decision is not, by virtue of that fact alone, in breach of fiduciary duty. The director might still be in breach on other grounds — failing to exercise due care, self-dealing — but the mere failure to consider social purpose is not itself actionable.

The Safe Harbor

The safe harbor is structurally critical. Without it, a “may consider” authorization would be almost meaningless: even if a director could consider social purposes, she would still face liability exposure whenever doing so arguably reduced expected shareholder returns. The safe harbor eliminates that exposure by declaring that consideration of social purposes — when undertaken in good faith and with a reasonable belief that the action will promote those purposes — is conclusively in the best interests of the corporation.

The safe harbor is conditioned on the “reasonably believes” language, which imports the business judgment rule's standard form. A court evaluating a challenge to an SPC director's mission-oriented decision would not second-guess the substantive merits of the decision; it would ask only whether the director's belief that the decision would promote social purposes was reasonable in light of the information available at the time. This is a deferential standard, and it effectively extends business judgment rule protection to mission-oriented decision-making.

The Third-Party Bar

The last element of section 050 — that the chapter creates no duties or rights for persons other than the corporation and its shareholders — is a crucial limit on the scope of SPC director duties. Even though SPCs are authorized (and, under the statute's logic, expected) to consider employees, suppliers, customers, community, and environment, those stakeholder groups are not given any right of action against directors under Chapter 23B.25. A Washington SPC employee cannot sue the directors of her employer for failing to consider her interests. A community group cannot sue for failing to consider community impacts. Only the corporation itself, and its shareholders in derivative capacity, have standing.

This is consistent with the B Lab model, the Delaware PBC statute, and Pennsylvania-style constituency statutes. In this respect, Washington's SPC framework participates in a broad American consensus: benefit corporation legislation expands permissive director consideration without expanding the universe of persons who can sue directors.

Part IV — The Duty Formulations, Compared

Washington's “may consider” formulation sits within a larger taxonomy of benefit corporation duty rules. A comparative view:

Why the Difference Matters

Under the B Lab model, a director who failed to consider the enumerated stakeholder categories in a material decision would expose herself to derivative liability in a benefit enforcement proceeding. Under the Washington SPC, the same director is not in breach. The Delaware PBC sits between the two: it requires actual balancing, which is stronger than “may consider” but channels enforcement through an unusually deferential review standard.

In operational terms, Washington's duty is closest to Pennsylvania's 1983 constituency statute — the original “may consider” model — but applied on an opt-in basis only to corporations that elect SPC status, rather than to all Washington corporations.

Part V — The Social Purpose Report (RCW 23B.25.150)

RCW 23B.25.150 is, in operational terms, the heart of SPC compliance. Every Washington SPC must publish an annual Social Purpose Report.

Timing

The report must be furnished to shareholders not later than four months after the close of the corporation's fiscal year. Four months is a relatively generous window compared to the ninety-day SEC 10-K deadline applicable to most US public companies, and more than adequate for a private SPC to compile operational narrative content.

Publication

The report must be “publicly accessible, free of charge, at the corporation's principal internet website address.” What counts as the “principal” website is not further defined. For an SPC with multiple websites (a corporate investor-relations site and a consumer-product site), the statute implicitly requires the SPC to identify which is principal. The report “shall remain available on that website through the end of the corporation's fiscal year” — a somewhat awkward formulation that practitioners generally read to require approximately eight months of availability from the publication date.

Content

The statute enumerates content elements the report may include — a permissive rather than mandatory list:

  • Short-term and long-term objectives related to the social purposes.
  • Material actions taken during the fiscal year in furtherance of the social purposes.
  • Expected future actions.
  • A description of the financial, operating, or other measures used by the corporation during the fiscal year for evaluating its performance in achieving its social purpose.

The use of “may include” rather than “shall include” means the statute imposes a floor (some narrative discussion of social purposes and efforts to promote them) but not a detailed content checklist.

Failure Remedies

Failure to furnish the report does not affect the validity of any corporate action. A delinquent SPC does not lose its corporate existence, its limited liability, or the validity of its contracts merely by failing to publish. However, the superior court of the county where the SPC's registered office is located may summarily order delivery of the report if the SPC has failed for two consecutive fiscal years.

Notable features of this remedy:

  • Two-consecutive-year trigger. A single missed report does not trigger court relief.
  • Summary procedure. Expedited proceedings without full trial on the merits.
  • Specific performance, not damages. The court can compel publication but cannot impose monetary penalties.
  • No attorney general standing. On the face of the statute, only shareholders can enforce.

The relatively toothless enforcement mechanism is one of the sharpest contrasts between the Washington SPC framework and the B Lab model. Under B Lab's benefit enforcement proceeding, shareholders can sue for damages and injunctive relief for a director's failure to pursue the public benefit or comply with reporting requirements. Washington offers only specific performance of the reporting obligation, and only after two consecutive years of non-compliance.

Part VI — Mission Protection: The Three Supermajority Triggers

Beyond Section 050's duty framework and Section 150's reporting obligation, Chapter 23B.25 contains three provisions that provide meaningful mission-protection through enhanced voting thresholds:

SectionTriggerThreshold
23B.25.090 Amendment of articles that materially changes a social purpose Two-thirds of aggregate voting group + two-thirds of each class or series voting separately
23B.25.100 Merger or share exchange in which the SPC would not survive Same two-thirds aggregate + class/series requirement (with SPC-to-SPC exception when social purposes do not materially diverge)
23B.25.110 Sale of all or substantially all assets that would materially alter the social purpose Same two-thirds requirement

Together, these three provisions provide parallel supermajority triggers for the three categories of corporate action that could undermine the social purpose: charter amendments, mergers, and asset sales. Section 100 is arguably the most significant mission-protection device in the chapter. Without it, an SPC could be merged into an ordinary corporation on a simple majority vote, effectively ending the mission commitment through a transactional back door. Section 100 raises the bar, giving founders and early mission-aligned investors meaningful protection against later dilution and exit.

Shareholders who dissent from such actions have dissenters' rights under RCW 23B.25.120, providing an exit mechanism at fair value for shareholders who disagree with a two-thirds majority's decision to, for example, convert the SPC into an ordinary corporation.

Part VIII — Commentary and the Interpretive Vacuum

The Greenwashing Concern

Academic critique of the Washington SPC statute has been measured but consistent. The core concern is that the “may consider” default, combined with the absence of third-party certification and the relatively toothless reporting enforcement, creates a framework in which a corporation can adopt the SPC label as a marketing device without making any meaningful operational commitment. This is the “greenwashing” concern: SPC status as a halo that does not require substantive change.

Proponents respond that:

  1. The mandatory investor-protection disclosure in articles provides real notice to shareholders.
  2. The three supermajority triggers (amendment, merger, asset sale) provide meaningful structural protection for the mission.
  3. The annual report requirement, while enforced only weakly, creates a documentary record against which shareholders and the market can assess the corporation's actual conduct.
  4. Mission-aligned founders who want stronger protections can adopt them through articles provisions — “shall consider” language, third-party standard requirements, enhanced voting thresholds, or all three.

Both positions have merit. The Washington SPC is a less demanding form than the B Lab benefit corporation, and that is by design. Whether the lesser demand produces meaningful results depends on the intentions and practices of the particular corporations that adopt the form.

The Interpretive Vacuum

A feature of the Washington “may consider” formulation that distinguishes it from the Delaware PBC formulation is the near-total absence of judicial interpretation. Delaware's PBC statute has been the subject of substantial academic commentary and at least some judicial treatment. Washington's SPC statute has, as of 2026, generated essentially no appellate case law. The WSBA Business Law Section's drafter commentary in the Washington Business Corporation Act Sourcebook provides the principal authoritative source of interpretive guidance.

This interpretive vacuum has both virtues and vices. It leaves practitioners substantial flexibility in counseling clients but also leaves important questions unresolved:

  • Does the “may consider” default create any affirmative liability for directors who invoke it pretextually?
  • How is the safe harbor's “reasonably believes” standard applied in close cases?
  • What counts as a “material change” to a social purpose for purposes of the enhanced voting triggers?
  • What counts as the “principal internet website” for an SPC with multiple web properties?
  • Does failure to file an annual social purpose report for two consecutive years give rise to any ancillary liability beyond the specific-performance remedy of section 150?

All of these questions await judicial resolution. For now, practitioners rely on the statutory text, the WSBA Sourcebook commentary, and the broader MBCA tradition.

The Hundred-Year Question

Can an SPC structure support multi-generational mission persistence? The question is not merely theoretical. The longest-running mission-driven corporations — Patagonia, Newman's Own, and legacy structures like the German Stiftung model — operate on timescales that far exceed the lifespan of any individual founder. The SPC framework was designed, in part, to enable this kind of durability.

The honest answer, as of 2026, is that we do not know. The statute is 14 years old. No Washington SPC has yet survived its founder. The three supermajority triggers provide structural protection against dilution, but do not themselves ensure that successor boards and shareholders will continue to invest the mission with operational meaning. The question of whether Washington's soft default is sufficient to anchor a century-long mission, or whether tighter mission-lock mechanisms (like the UK CIC's asset lock, or Patagonia's 2022 conversion into a purpose trust structure) are required, is one that only time will answer.

Conclusion

Washington's Social Purpose Corporation statute is, by design, the softest of the principal United States benefit corporation regimes. Its drafters chose flexibility over prescription, trusting that builders would take the statute's permissions seriously without needing a mandatory reporting regime enforced by third parties. Whether that trust is well-placed depends on the intentions of the builders who adopt the form.

What can be said with confidence is that the statute is coherent. Each provision fits with the others. The “may consider” duty, the safe harbor, the third-party bar, the three supermajority triggers, the annual report, the conversion symmetry, and the enumerated social purpose categories are all expressions of a consistent philosophical position: that corporate law is a leveraged substrate, that mission-driven governance is a matter of voluntary commitment rather than statutory compulsion, and that the law's role is to provide a clear framework within which builders can construct the particular enterprises they want, not to prescribe the particulars themselves.

For readers comparing Washington's approach with Delaware's PBC or Maryland's B Lab model, the operative question is not which statute is “better” — the three represent different theories of how legislation should engage with mission-driven enterprise — but which best fits the particular circumstances of a particular corporation. Washington's form is most at home with founders who want flexibility, who are wary of third-party certification, who do not need the rhetorical force of “shall consider” language, and who believe that mission durability is more a matter of persons than of statutes. For such founders, the Washington SPC offers a clean, minimal, leveraged substrate on which a century-long commitment can, in principle, be built.
