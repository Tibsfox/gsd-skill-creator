The annual report is the centerpiece of ongoing Washington corporate compliance. It is a short, mostly informational filing, but the consequences of failure are severe: administrative dissolution, loss of good standing, and eventual termination of legal existence if not cured within the five-year reinstatement window.

Title 23B and the WUBOC: A Two-Chapter Architecture

A reader who attempts to work only from Title 23B RCW will miss half of the applicable compliance law. Washington's corporate compliance regime is split across two interlocking chapters:

  • Title 23B RCW — the Washington Business Corporation Act, containing substantive corporate governance rules specific to business corporations.
  • Chapter 23.95 RCW — the Washington Uniform Business Organizations Code (WUBOC), containing the “hub” rules that apply across all entity types: naming, registered agents, annual reports, filing mechanics, administrative dissolution, and reinstatement.

The 2015 WUBOC recodification (Laws of 2015, ch. 176, effective January 1, 2016) consolidated naming and registered agent rules out of Title 23B and into Chapter 23.95. Older treatises and form books that still cite pre-2016 section numbers are actively misleading. Always cross-check against the current RCW text at app.leg.wa.gov/rcw.

Corporate Name (RCW 23B.04 and 23.95 Art. 3)

RCW 23B.04.010 provides, in full: “A corporate name must comply with the requirements of Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW.” That single sentence is the gateway. Everything substantive about naming lives in RCW 23.95.300 through 23.95.325.

The Distinguishability Standard

The core test is whether a proposed name is “distinguishable on the records of the secretary of state” from names already on file. This language replaces the older “deceptively similar” standard that had been common in US corporate statutes for much of the twentieth century. “Distinguishable on the records” is a more mechanical, records-based test than “deceptively similar,” which invited a consumer-confusion inquiry closer to trademark law.

What Does NOT Create Distinguishability

The statute is explicit that superficial variations do not make a name distinguishable:

Insufficient differenceExample
Entity-type suffix variation“Acme Corp.” vs. “Acme Inc.” vs. “Acme LLC”
Articles and conjunctions“The Acme Company” vs. “Acme and Company”
Punctuation and capitalization“ACME, Inc.” vs. “Acme Inc”
Special characters“Acme+” vs. “Acme”
Singular versus plural“Acme Holding” vs. “Acme Holdings”

The operating principle: names must differ in their substantive lexical content, not merely in formal decoration. Any two names a reasonable reader would pronounce identically will be treated as indistinguishable.

Required Corporate Designators

RCW 23.95.305 requires the name to contain one of: “Corporation,” “Incorporated,” “Company,” “Limited,” or the abbreviations “Corp.,” “Inc.,” “Co.,” or “Ltd.” The purpose is creditor notice: any party dealing with the entity can tell from its name alone that they are contracting with a limited-liability entity.

A Social Purpose Corporation formed under Chapter 23B.25 RCW must additionally contain “Social Purpose Corporation,” “SPC,” or “S.P.C.”

Name Reservation

RCW 23.95.310 permits reservation of a corporate name for a renewable 180-day period. This is useful when the articles are not quite ready but the founders want to lock in the chosen name. A CCFS pre-filing search is the first line of defense, but a reservation is the belt-and-suspenders practice.

Registered Agent (RCW 23B.05 and 23.95 Art. 4)

Every Washington corporation must maintain a registered agent with a physical Washington street address. This is a continuous obligation, not a one-time formation step. The agent receives service of process and other official communications.

Who May Serve

  • A natural person Washington resident with a physical WA street address
  • A domestic entity (corporation, LLC, etc.) with a place of business in Washington
  • A foreign entity authorized to transact business in Washington and having a place of business in the state

The Street Address Requirement — No PO Boxes

The registered office address must be a physical street address where service of process can actually be made during ordinary business hours. Post office boxes, private mailbox services (UPS Store, etc.), and virtual offices are not acceptable as registered agent addresses. This is one of the most frequently missed requirements in DIY formations.

Commercial Registered Agent Services

Washington has a well-developed market for commercial registered agent services (Northwest Registered Agent, CT Corporation, Registered Agents Inc., Washington Registered Agent, and others). Typical annual fees range from $50 to $300. Advantages of commercial services over self-service include: (i) privacy, since the commercial agent's address appears in public records rather than the founder's home address; (ii) reliability, since the commercial agent is always staffed during business hours; (iii) forwarding, since the commercial agent will scan and forward service of process promptly; and (iv) compliance reminders for annual reports and other deadlines.

Consequences of No Registered Agent

Failure to maintain a registered agent is grounds for administrative dissolution under RCW 23B.14.210 and the parallel WUBOC provisions. An administratively dissolved corporation retains existence for wind-up purposes but cannot conduct ordinary business. Reinstatement is available under RCW 23.95.615 within a five-year window.

Amendments to Articles (RCW 23B.10)

Board-Only Amendments (RCW 23B.10.020)

Certain narrow categories of amendment can be made by the board alone without shareholder approval — administrative changes (such as deleting the name of the initial registered agent), changes that affect only the class of shares authorized but not issued, amendments that merely change the corporation's name if no class of stock is outstanding, and other technical corrections.

Shareholder-Approved Amendments (RCW 23B.10.030) — The 2024 Change

This is the most important recent change in Washington corporate law. The default shareholder vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation has bifurcated based on the corporation's formation date.

Formation dateDefault thresholdAuthority
Before August 1, 2024 Two-thirds of votes entitled to be cast on the amendment by each voting group Original 1989 default; inherited from the WBCA enactment
On or after August 1, 2024 Majority of votes entitled to be cast by each voting group Laws of 2024, ch. 22 § 20 (Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5335)

The two-thirds default had long been an outlier. Most MBCA-family states, and Delaware's DGCL § 242(b), had majority defaults. The 2024 amendment brought Washington into line with national practice for new formations while grandfathering existing corporations to preserve settled shareholder expectations.

The practical consequence: practitioners must now track a formation-date-dependent threshold for every Washington corporation they advise. A corporation formed July 31, 2024 operates under the two-thirds default; a corporation formed August 1, 2024 operates under the majority default. This dichotomy will persist for decades. Articles may always specify a higher threshold but not a lower one.

Voting Groups (RCW 23B.10.040)

Holders of a particular class or series of shares are entitled to vote as a separate voting group on amendments that would:

  • Increase the number of authorized shares of the class
  • Effect an exchange or reclassification of all or part of the shares
  • Change the rights, preferences, or limitations of the shares
  • Create a new class of shares having rights or preferences prior, superior, or substantially equal
  • Limit or deny an existing preemptive right
  • Cancel or otherwise affect rights to accumulated but undeclared distributions

Separate voting group treatment means that the amendment must satisfy the threshold within each affected voting group, not just across the aggregate. This is the principal mechanism by which Washington corporate law protects preferred shareholders and other special classes against amendment-driven dilution.

Articles of Amendment (RCW 23B.10.060)

Once approved, amendments must be filed with the Secretary of State. The filing must contain: the corporation's name; the text of each amendment; provisions for implementing share-related amendments if not in the amendment itself; the date of adoption; statements that the amendment was duly approved; and any applicable effective date.

Initial and Annual Reports (RCW 23.95.255)

The Initial Report

RCW 23.95.255(1) requires every new corporation to file an initial report with the Secretary of State after the organizational meeting. The initial report identifies directors, officers, and other basic corporate information. It is typically submitted electronically through the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS).

The Annual Report

RCW 23.95.255(2) requires every Washington corporation to file an annual report. The report is due on the anniversary month of the corporation's formation — a corporation formed in April files annually in April, a corporation formed in October files annually in October. Missing the deadline triggers delinquency processing.

The report content is short: registered agent, principal office address, officers and directors, and (for SPCs) confirmation of SPC status. The filing fee is modest (under $100 as of this writing; always verify the current Secretary of State fee schedule). The entire filing typically takes 15 minutes through CCFS.

The UBI Number

Every Washington business entity is assigned a Unified Business Identifier (UBI), a nine-digit number that serves as a universal identifier across multiple state agencies: Secretary of State, Department of Revenue, Labor and Industries, and Employment Security Department. The UBI is assigned at the time of initial Washington Business License application and follows the entity for its lifetime.

Good Standing Certificate

A corporation in good standing — meaning current on annual reports, fees, and registered agent maintenance — may obtain a certificate of good standing from the Secretary of State. Good standing certificates are required for many transactions: foreign qualification in other states, bank account openings at some institutions, real estate closings, and M&A due diligence.

Delinquency and Administrative Dissolution

Failure to file the annual report triggers a grace period, followed by delinquency status, followed by administrative dissolution under RCW 23B.14.210 and the parallel WUBOC provisions. An administratively dissolved corporation retains existence for wind-up purposes but cannot conduct ordinary business.

Reinstatement under RCW 23.95.615 is available within a five-year window after the effective date of administrative dissolution. The application must include all missed annual reports, past-due fees, and reinstatement fees. Beyond the five-year window, the entity is permanently dissolved and must start over with a new formation if it wants to resume operations.

Records and Reports (RCW 23B.16)

Required Records (RCW 23B.16.010)

Every Washington corporation must maintain:

  • Articles of incorporation, as originally filed and as amended
  • Bylaws, as originally adopted and as amended
  • Resolutions of the board of directors establishing classes or series of shares
  • Minutes of meetings of shareholders and the board, for the past three years
  • Written communications to shareholders generally for the past three years
  • Names and business addresses of current directors and officers
  • Most recent annual report
  • Accounting records
  • Shareholder list

Shareholder Inspection Rights (RCW 23B.16.020)

A shareholder has the right to inspect and copy corporate records, subject to several requirements:

  • Written demand delivered to the corporation at least five business days in advance
  • Good faith and proper purpose. The inspection must be in good faith and for a purpose reasonably related to the person's interest as a shareholder. Commercial purposes (selling the shareholder list to a competitor, for example) do not qualify.
  • Directly connected to the stated purpose

The categories of records subject to inspection vary: articles, bylaws, and board resolutions establishing share classes are available to any shareholder without heightened showing; other records (accounting records, shareholder lists, minutes, written communications) require the good-faith-proper-purpose showing.

Denial and Enforcement

RCW 23B.16.040 provides that the superior court of the county where the corporation's principal office (or registered office) is located may summarily order inspection, including the right to copy, if the corporation wrongfully denies a lawful inspection request. The court may also require the corporation to pay the shareholder's costs (including reasonable attorneys' fees) if denial was without reasonable basis.

The Two Most-Missed Obligations

1. Annual reports. Missing an annual report is the leading cause of administrative dissolution in Washington. The filing is short and the fee is modest; the consequences of failure are severe. Commercial registered agent services generally include annual report reminders as part of their package, which alone can justify the annual fee.

2. Registered agent maintenance. A registered agent who moves, retires, dies, or resigns without the corporation being aware creates a silent compliance gap. The corporation remains in good standing on paper until the Secretary of State is notified (often through an attempted service of process), at which point the clock starts on administrative dissolution.
