The same fundamental idea — a corporation that is legally permitted, or required, to consider non-shareholder interests — has been encoded by legislators across the United States, Europe, and the Commonwealth. The encodings differ in ways that reveal each jurisdiction's theory of the firm.

The Landscape

Between 2010 and 2023, roughly forty US states, the District of Columbia, and several international jurisdictions enacted some form of statutory benefit corporation, social purpose corporation, or mission-driven company. Washington's Social Purpose Corporation (2012) sits within this movement but follows a distinctive path. This page compares the major frameworks across the dimensions that actually matter: who may form, what the director duty is, what must be reported, how enforcement works, and whether a third-party certification is required.

Classification of Frameworks

These statutes fall roughly into four families:

Family 1

B Lab Model Benefit Corporation

The prescriptive standard. Director “shall consider” effects on shareholders, employees, subsidiaries, suppliers, customers, community, environment. Annual benefit report against a third-party standard. Most adopting states (MD, VA, HI, NJ, IL, PA, CO, MA) are in this family.

Family 2

Delaware Public Benefit Corporation

The tripartite balance. Directors must “balance” stockholder pecuniary interests, those materially affected by the corporation's conduct, and the specific public benefit in the certificate. Biennial reporting to shareholders. No third-party standard required.

Family 3

Washington Social Purpose Corporation

The permissive “may consider” formulation. No third-party certification. Annual social purpose report published to corporate website. The softest duty formulation in the US. California's Flexible Purpose Corporation (2011, phased out 2015) was the direct antecedent.

Family 4

International & Unique Models

UK Community Interest Company (2005, charitable asset lock). France Société à Mission (PACTE 2019, raison d'être clause). Italy Società Benefit (2016, close to B Lab). Canada BC Benefit Company (2020).

International Parallels

United Kingdom — Community Interest Company (2005)

Enacted under the Companies (Audit, Investigations and Community Enterprise) Act 2004, effective 2005. A CIC is a limited company (either limited by shares or limited by guarantee) subject to a community interest test administered by the CIC Regulator. Defining features: (1) an asset lock — assets are permanently dedicated to community benefit and cannot be distributed to members except under prescribed caps; (2) a community interest statement filed annually describing how the company served its community; (3) the Regulator has enforcement powers independent of members. As of 2024 there are ~25,000 CICs in the UK. The asset lock is the philosophical distinction: the CIC protects mission by locking capital against private extraction, where Washington SPCs protect mission only through reporting and director duty considerations.

France — Société à Mission (Loi PACTE, 2019)

Article 176 of the Loi PACTE (Plan d'Action pour la Croissance et la Transformation des Entreprises, 2019) created the société à mission as a status that any French company may adopt. Requirements: (1) a raison d'être clause in the articles stating the company's purpose beyond profit; (2) one or more objectifs sociaux et environnementaux; (3) a comité de mission distinct from the board, charged with monitoring execution; (4) independent third-party verification every two years for companies above a size threshold. Uptake through 2024: ~1,500 companies. The French model is closer to Washington's — status-based, reporting-centric — but adds the novel mission committee as a dedicated internal organ.

Italy — Società Benefit (2016)

Italy was the first EU member state to enact a benefit corporation statute, in December 2015 (effective January 2016). The Italian framework is closely modeled on the B Lab / Maryland approach: mandatory “beneficio comune” (common benefit) in the articles, mandatory consideration of stakeholder interests, annual benefit report evaluated against a third-party standard, and enforcement via both state and private-party action. As of 2024, over 2,500 Italian società benefit. The Italian statute arguably did more to popularize the benefit corporation idea in continental Europe than any other single legal instrument.

Canada — British Columbia Benefit Company (2020)

Amendments to British Columbia's Business Corporations Act, effective June 2020, created the benefit company as a corporate form available to any BC company. Defining features: a benefit statement in the notice of articles, a commitment to promoting the public benefit, annual benefit reports, and a derivative action framework for enforcement. BC is the first Canadian province to enact such a statute, though proposals exist in other provinces. The BC model borrows heavily from B Lab but adapts for Canadian corporate law conventions.

The Washington Distinction, Summarized

Washington's SPC is the softest, least prescriptive benefit corporation statute among major US and international frameworks. This is a feature, not a bug. The drafters in 2011–2012 deliberately chose flexibility over prescription, trusting that builders would take the statute's permissions seriously without needing a mandatory reporting regime enforced by third parties.
