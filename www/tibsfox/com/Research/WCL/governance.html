The board has exclusive authority as to substantive decisions concerning management of the corporation's business, subject to any limitation set forth in this title, the articles of incorporation, or a shareholders' agreement. — RCW 23B.08.010(2)

Two Chapters, One Governance Stack

Washington's internal corporate governance is encoded across two chapters: Chapter 23B.07 RCW (Shareholders) and Chapter 23B.08 RCW (Directors and Officers). Together they describe the representative-democracy structure of corporate governance: shareholders select directors who then act as fiduciaries on the shareholders' behalf, subject to statutory duties, the business judgment rule, and the conflict-of-interest safe harbors that make boardroom decision-making predictable.

This module walks through the core provisions: shareholder meeting mechanics, voting and written consent, the powerful shareholder agreement statute at RCW 23B.07.320, board authority and composition, director duties and the business judgment rule in Washington case law, director liability limitation, conflict of interest safe harbors, officers, and the indemnification framework.

Part 1 — Shareholders (RCW 23B.07)

Annual and Special Meetings

RCW 23B.07.010 requires an annual shareholder meeting. RCW 23B.07.020 governs special (non-annual) meetings, which may be called by:

  • The board of directors
  • Any person authorized to do so by articles or bylaws (typically the president or a specified officer)
  • Shareholders holding at least 10% of the votes entitled to be cast on any issue proposed for the meeting

The 10% threshold is a meaningful minority-shareholder right. Articles can raise the threshold up to 25% (for private corporations) or eliminate it entirely (for public companies). Articles cannot lower the threshold below 10% for private corporations. Written demands are aggregated for a rolling 60-day period.

Court-Ordered Meetings

RCW 23B.07.030 provides a judicial remedy when a corporation fails to hold an annual meeting or fails to respond to a valid shareholder demand for a special meeting. The superior court of the county in which the corporation's registered office is located may, on application by a qualifying shareholder, summarily order the meeting, fix its time and place, determine the shareholders entitled to participate, and specify a record date. Court-ordered meetings are rare in Washington, arising most often in closely held corporations where a faction has seized control of the board and refuses to convene the shareholders (typically to stave off a director-removal vote).

Notice

RCW 23B.07.050 requires notice of meetings no fewer than 10 nor more than 60 days before the meeting date. For meetings at which fundamental actions will be considered (amendments to articles, mergers, sale of substantially all assets, dissolution), the timing is 20 to 60 days. For a special meeting, the notice must describe the purpose of the meeting — and only matters within the described purpose can be voted on.

Under RCW 23B.07.060, a shareholder may waive notice in writing or by attending the meeting without objection. Waiver is common in closely held corporations where all shareholders are informal participants in governance.

Action Without a Meeting

RCW 23B.07.040 permits shareholders to act by written consent in lieu of a meeting. This is one of the most important provisions for closely held corporations. Two approval pathways exist:

  1. Unanimous written consent. If all shareholders entitled to vote sign the consent, the action is approved without further procedural requirements. The most common approach in closely held corporations.
  2. Majority written consent with advance notice. If less than all shareholders are needed, the statute permits non-unanimous written consent but requires advance notice to non-consenting shareholders.

Consents delivered to the corporation must, within 60 days of the earliest signed consent, add up to the required approval threshold. Written consent is the workhorse of closely held corporate governance; many closely held Washington corporations conduct their entire year of shareholder business through a single Annual Action by Written Consent.

Quorum and Voting Thresholds

The default rule under RCW 23B.07.250:

  • Default quorum: majority of votes entitled to be cast by the voting group
  • Default approval: votes cast in favor must exceed votes cast opposing (simple majority of votes cast, not of votes entitled)

This last distinction matters: abstentions and broker non-votes do not count as “against” votes; they simply reduce the denominator. Articles can raise the threshold (e.g., to supermajority of shares entitled to vote, which would make abstentions effectively “against” votes).

Sticky quorum: once a share is represented for any purpose at the meeting, it is deemed present for the remainder of the meeting. A shareholder cannot attend, witness the meeting reach quorum, and then walk out to defeat quorum.

Shareholder Agreements (RCW 23B.07.320)

RCW 23B.07.320 is one of the most powerful planning tools in the Washington corporate statute. It permits shareholders to enter into an agreement that deviates, in specified ways, from the default corporate governance rules — even rules that would otherwise be mandatory.

What a Shareholder Agreement Can Do

The statute expressly authorizes agreements that:

  1. Eliminate the board of directors. This is the closest thing Washington has to a statutory close corporation regime. Washington does not have a separate close-corporation statute; instead, 23B.07.320 serves the same function by allowing shareholders to dispense with the board and run the corporation directly.
  2. Govern distributions. Including non-pro-rata distributions — an arrangement impossible under default corporate law, which requires distributions to be pro rata within each class.
  3. Determine directors and officers. Including the identity, terms, and removal procedures. The shareholder agreement can establish an iron-clad board composition that cannot be altered by any later shareholder or board action.
  4. Allocate voting power. Including allocations that would otherwise violate the one-share-one-vote default.
  5. Govern side contracts. Pre-authorize and establish the terms of contracts between the corporation and its shareholders (such as employment agreements, consulting arrangements, equipment leases).
  6. Transfer corporate powers. Including transfers to shareholders or to outside persons. This is the partner-like arrangement by which closely held corporations can simulate a partnership while retaining limited liability.
  7. Require dissolution on specified events. Including deadlock, the death or departure of a key shareholder, or the occurrence of any other specified contingency.
  8. Govern anything else. The catchall is textually broad enough to encompass any arrangement touching corporate governance, the relationship among shareholders, or the operation of the business.

Formal Requirements

To be effective, the shareholder agreement must:

  • Be in writing and executed by all shareholders at the time of the agreement (unanimity at the moment of execution)
  • Be made known to the corporation (delivered so the corporation has notice)
  • Be noted conspicuously on each share certificate or information statement, so future acquirers are on notice
  • Be amended only by unanimous shareholder consent (unless the agreement itself specifies a lower threshold)

The Conflict Hierarchy

A shareholder agreement trumps inconsistent provisions of the articles and bylaws (because the statute explicitly authorizes it to be “inconsistent with one or more other provisions of this title”). This makes the shareholder agreement the highest tier of the corporate governance stack for closely held corporations:

  1. Shareholder agreement (RCW 23B.07.320) — within its authorized scope
  2. Articles of incorporation
  3. Bylaws
  4. Board resolutions

Every closely held Washington corporation with more than one shareholder should consider a 23B.07.320 agreement. It is the vehicle through which founders can commit to governance norms that would otherwise be unenforceable: unanimous director-election voting, pro-rata capital-call obligations, buy-sell provisions on death or departure, drag-along and tag-along rights, non-compete obligations.

Part 2 — Directors and Officers (RCW 23B.08)

Board Authority (RCW 23B.08.010)

RCW 23B.08.010 is the foundational provision of corporate governance. It establishes the board of directors as the organ through which corporate powers are exercised:

“All corporate powers shall be exercised by or under the authority of the corporation's board of directors, and the business and affairs of the corporation shall be managed under the direction of its board of directors, which shall have exclusive authority as to substantive decisions concerning management of the corporation's business, subject to any limitation set forth in this title, the articles of incorporation, or a shareholders' agreement authorized by RCW 23B.07.320.”

The board's exclusive-authority zone includes day-to-day management decisions, strategic planning, officer appointment and compensation, capital allocation, and contract approval. These cannot be transferred to shareholders without running afoul of the exclusive-authority rule — unless the articles or a shareholder agreement expressly transfer them. In venture-backed corporations, “protective provisions” (contractual or charter-based veto rights over specified corporate actions) carve specific matters out of the board's exclusive authority and reserve them to preferred-stock approval.

Board Composition

RCW 23B.08.030 requires a board of one or more individuals. The articles or bylaws may specify a fixed number or a range (e.g., “not less than three nor more than nine directors”). A variable range gives the board flexibility to expand or contract without a formal amendment.

Washington imposes no default qualifications. A director need not be a shareholder, need not be a Washington resident, need not have any particular expertise, and need not be a natural person of any particular age (though the director must be an “individual,” which by implication excludes entities). The articles or bylaws may impose qualification requirements.

Director Elections, Terms, and Removal

RCW 23B.08.050 establishes the default term: one year, from the annual meeting at which the director is elected until the next annual meeting. Directors remain in holdover status until successors are elected, even after their terms expire — a director in holdover status has full authority to act.

RCW 23B.08.060 permits staggered (classified) board terms of two or three years, reducing the number of directors up for election in any single year. RCW 23B.08.100 governs shareholder removal of directors with or without cause.

Standards of Conduct for Directors (RCW 23B.08.300)

“A director shall discharge the duties of a director, including the director's duties as a member of a committee: (a) In good faith; (b) With the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances; and (c) In a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation.”

This is the standard MBCA formulation adopted by Washington in 1989 and left essentially unchanged since. The duty has three components:

  • Good faith. Subjective honesty of purpose; absence of bad motive.
  • Duty of care. The “ordinarily prudent person” standard. Washington's formulation is MBCA-style ordinary care, not Delaware-style “gross negligence.” The difference matters.
  • “Best interests of the corporation.” The reasonably believed standard; the director need not be objectively correct about what is in the corporation's best interests, only reasonable in her belief.

Director Reliance

RCW 23B.08.310 permits directors to rely on information, opinions, reports, and statements prepared by others — officers and employees of the corporation, counsel, public accountants, experts, and board committees — provided the director does not have knowledge that would make reliance unwarranted. This reliance safe harbor is critical in practice: it means a director need not independently verify every factual assertion in a board package to satisfy the duty of care.

The Business Judgment Rule in Washington

The business judgment rule is a judicially created doctrine that shields directors from liability for honest errors of business judgment, provided certain threshold conditions are met. Washington courts have generally followed the MBCA-family formulation of the rule, distinct from the Delaware formulation.

Washington Case Law

CaseCitationHolding
Para-Medical Leasing, Inc. v. Hangen 48 Wn. App. 389, 739 P.2d 717 (1987) The business judgment rule “shields the corporate officer from liability so long as he acts in good faith without a corrupt motive.” Applied the rule broadly to corporate officers acting in management capacity, not just directors.
Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. v. Bucholz 45 Wn. App. 502, 728 P.2d 597 (1986) Directors and officers stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation and are not permitted to retain personal profits or advantages gleaned “on the side.”
In re Spokane Concrete Products, Inc. 126 Wn.2d 269, 892 P.2d 98 (1995) Washington Supreme Court. The BJR presumption — that directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation — “is not limitless.” To overcome the presumption, the plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that the directors defrauded the corporation or were dishonest or incompetent.
Grassmueck v. Barnett 281 F. Supp. 2d 1227 (W.D. Wash. 2003) Federal district court applying Washington law. Identified four categories of conduct that pierce director exculpation: (1) intentional misconduct, (2) knowing violation of law, (3) conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310, or (4) transactions from which the director receives improper personal benefit.
Rodriguez v. Loudeye Corp. 144 Wn. App. 709, 189 P.3d 168 (2008) Clean articulation of Washington's three primary director fiduciary duties: care, loyalty, and good faith. Tracks the RMBCA duty framework.
Riss v. Angel 131 Wn.2d 612, 934 P.2d 669 (1997) “Exercise ordinary care in performing their duties and to act reasonably and in good faith.” The ordinary-care formulation anchors Washington's MBCA-style duty of care, in contrast to Delaware's gross negligence standard.

The Washington Formulation, Summarized

Taken together, the Washington cases establish the following gloss on the 1989 WBCA:

  1. The BJR operates as a rebuttable presumption that directors acted in good faith, on an informed basis, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation.
  2. To overcome the presumption, the plaintiff must plead specific facts demonstrating fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, disloyalty, or bad faith.
  3. The duty of loyalty is robust and is enforced through disgorgement when breached.
  4. Directors are protected when they act in good faith, without self-dealing, and with a reasonable process.
  5. Washington's duty of care uses the “ordinary care” standard, not Delaware's gross negligence.

Director Liability Limitation (RCW 23B.08.320)

In the aftermath of Delaware's 1985 Smith v. Van Gorkom decision — which found the Trans Union board liable for gross negligence in approving a merger despite what the directors had believed was a careful process — Delaware enacted Section 102(b)(7) in 1986, permitting certificate-of-incorporation provisions that eliminate or limit director monetary liability for certain breaches of the duty of care. Most states followed.

RCW 23B.08.320 is Washington's analog. It permits articles of incorporation to eliminate or limit the personal liability of directors for monetary damages for conduct as a director, except for liability arising from:

  • Acts or omissions involving intentional misconduct or knowing violations of law
  • Conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310 (unlawful distributions)
  • Any transaction from which the director will personally receive a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director is not legally entitled

Within these carve-outs, Washington corporations have broad latitude to exculpate directors. The scope is somewhat narrower than Delaware's Section 102(b)(7), which permits elimination of liability for all breaches of duty of care but not duty of loyalty. Washington's version is more explicit about the specific categories that cannot be eliminated.

In practice, nearly every Washington corporation adopts the maximum available director liability limitation in its articles at the time of formation. The provision is essentially standard and has no meaningful downside for the corporation; it simply puts Washington directors on the same footing as their Delaware counterparts with respect to well-intentioned business judgment errors.

Conflicts of Interest (RCW 23B.08.730–.735)

Director self-dealing is one of the most heavily regulated areas of corporate law. Washington follows the MBCA approach of providing “safe harbors” that validate conflicted transactions under specified conditions:

  • Disinterested director approval. A transaction in which a director has a conflicting interest is not voidable if it is approved by a majority of disinterested directors after full disclosure.
  • Disinterested shareholder approval. Alternatively, a transaction can be ratified by shareholders who have no conflicting interest, after full disclosure.
  • Fairness. A conflicted transaction that was never approved can still be upheld if the director establishes that the transaction was fair to the corporation at the time it was authorized.

The 2020 Business Opportunity Disclaimer

RCW 23B.08.735(1)(b), added in 2020 (Laws of 2020, ch. 194 § 3), permits articles of incorporation to limit or eliminate the duty of directors or other specified persons to offer the corporation business opportunities before pursuing them personally. The amendments address a specific problem in venture capital and private equity contexts: directors who sit on multiple boards and encounter opportunities that might be of interest to more than one portfolio company. The provision is an opt-in: corporations without such a clause remain governed by the default corporate opportunity doctrine.

Officers (RCW 23B.08.400–.440)

Washington corporations must have the officers described in their bylaws or appointed by the board. The statute does not mandate specific offices beyond a corporate secretary responsible for authenticating records; the traditional president / vice president / secretary / treasurer structure is a bylaw convention, not a statutory requirement. Officers may include multiple persons holding the same office, or a single person holding multiple offices (including serving as sole director, sole officer, and sole shareholder simultaneously).

Officers owe the same fundamental fiduciary duties as directors — good faith, care, loyalty — and the business judgment rule protects them in their operational capacity (Para-Medical Leasing v. Hangen established this explicitly). Officers may be removed by the board at any time, with or without cause, subject to contractual rights they may have under employment agreements.

Indemnification Framework (RCW 23B.08.500–.600)

Washington's indemnification regime is one of the most detailed and robust in American corporate law. It has four principal components:

Statutory provisionTopicSummary
RCW 23B.08.510 Permissive indemnification Corporation may indemnify a director who acted in good faith and in a manner reasonably believed to be in (or not opposed to) the corporation's best interests, and with no reasonable cause to believe the conduct was unlawful.
RCW 23B.08.520 Mandatory indemnification Corporation must indemnify a director who is wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise, in the defense of a proceeding. Success includes dismissal without liability.
RCW 23B.08.530 Advancement of expenses Corporation may advance expenses to a director during the pendency of a proceeding, subject to an undertaking to repay if indemnification is ultimately unavailable.
RCW 23B.08.540 Court-ordered indemnification A court may order indemnification where it determines that it is fair and reasonable, even if the statutory standard is not met.
RCW 23B.08.560 Non-exclusivity; D&O insurance Statutory indemnification is non-exclusive; corporations may provide broader indemnification by bylaws, agreement, or other means. Corporations are authorized to purchase and maintain D&O liability insurance.

Retroactive Amendments

One of the refinements added to Washington's indemnification regime over the years (RCW 23B.08.603) addresses the situation where an indemnification provision is amended or repealed after the conduct in question has occurred. Washington's answer, like Delaware's: later amendments cannot retroactively reduce indemnification rights absent the indemnified person's consent. This provides certainty to directors relying on indemnification provisions at the time they agree to serve.

The net effect of Washington's indemnification framework is to make director and officer service reasonably low-risk for persons acting in good faith. Directors and officers of Washington corporations have strong statutory and contractual protection against personal liability for conduct within the scope of their duties, and the scope of those protections is clearer today than it was in 1989. That clarity is a function of careful drafting, careful amendment, and careful application by Washington courts.
