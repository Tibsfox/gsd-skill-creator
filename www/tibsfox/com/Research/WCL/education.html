Educational research, not legal advice. These resources are organized for self-directed study and curiosity. They do not substitute for a licensed Washington attorney, nor for formal legal education at an accredited law school.
You do not learn corporate law by reading a statute cover-to-cover. You learn it by reading a problem, looking up the applicable section, reading the commentary, reading the cases, and then re-reading the statute with new understanding. The statute is the destination, not the starting point.

A Staged Learning Path

  1. Start with a plain-language explainer — Before touching RCW, read a general corporate law overview. Clark's Corporate Law (1986) is still the canonical textbook; Eisenberg & Cox Corporations and Other Business Organizations is the current leading casebook.
  2. Learn the Model Business Corporation Act — The ABA's Model Business Corporation Act Annotated is the bridge between general theory and Washington's statute. Washington's WBCA is RMBCA-derived; understanding the Model gives you ~80% of Washington without ever opening RCW 23B.
  3. Read Title 23B RCW in order — Start at 23B.01 definitions, skim 23B.02 through 23B.08, then read the rest. Use app.leg.wa.gov/rcw for the official text. Keep a practice treatise open alongside.
  4. Read the WBCA Sourcebook — Published by the WSBA Business Law Section, now in its fifth edition. Includes the full statute with commentary, legislative history for each section, and judicial case notes. The single most useful Washington-specific resource.
  5. Read cases — Washington Supreme Court and Washington Court of Appeals decisions on fiduciary duties, the business judgment rule, minority oppression, and valuation. Use Casetext or Justia for free access.
  6. Read Delaware — Most of the doctrinal development in US corporate law happens in Delaware Chancery. Understanding Revlon, Unocal, Caremark, Blasius, and Smith v. Van Gorkom is essential context for interpreting Washington case law.
  7. Read the SPC literature — For RCW 23B.25, read the Seattle University Law Review articles on the Washington SPC statute, B Lab's model legislation, and comparative treatments in the Fordham Journal of Corporate and Financial Law.
  8. Do the exercises — Draft a set of articles for a hypothetical SPC. Draft a set of bylaws. Draft a social purpose report. Draft a board resolution. The work trains the eye better than any passive reading.

Washington corporate law practice requires familiarity with several statutes beyond Title 23B RCW:

23.95Uniform Business Organizations CodeNaming (Art. 3), registered agent (Art. 4), annual reports (Art. 6). Cross-referenced throughout 23B.
Title 24Nonprofit CorporationsThe parallel regime for nonprofit corporations. A standard corporation is not a nonprofit; the statutes diverge sharply.

Recommended First Ninety Days of Self-Study

If you are starting from zero and want to reach the level of being able to read a Washington corporate law problem and identify the applicable sections, here is a suggested schedule:

  • Weeks 1–2 — General corporate law overview. Clark or Eisenberg casebook, first 4–5 chapters. Focus on: what is a corporation, what is limited liability, what are the parties (shareholders, directors, officers), what are the main transactions (formation, issuance, distributions, merger, dissolution).
  • Weeks 3–4 — MBCA orientation. Read the MBCA chapters on incorporation, shareholders, directors, amendments. Understand the “default rules” structure: statutes provide defaults, articles and bylaws customize.
  • Weeks 5–6 — Washington-specific orientation. Read RCW 23B.01 through 23B.08 in order. Read the WBCA Sourcebook sections corresponding to these chapters. Take notes on where Washington diverges from the MBCA.
  • Weeks 7–8 — The SPC chapter. Read RCW 23B.25 in full. Read the Seattle University Law Review articles. Compare to the B Lab model and Delaware PBC. Understand the “may consider” vs. “shall consider” distinction.
  • Weeks 9–10 — Transactions and exit. Read RCW 23B.10 (amendments), 23B.11A (mergers), 23B.13 (dissenters), 23B.14 (dissolution), 23B.19 (anti-takeover). Compare to Delaware Section 203 for 23B.19.
  • Weeks 11–12 — Cases and doctrine. Read the canonical cases: Dodge, Van Gorkom, Unocal, Revlon, Caremark, Blasius, eBay. Read a handful of Washington Supreme Court opinions on fiduciary duties. Understand how doctrine shapes interpretation of the statute.
  • Practice throughout — Draft a set of articles of incorporation, a set of bylaws, a social purpose report template, and a board resolution. The work cements the reading.

When to Stop Self-Studying and Hire Counsel

Self-study teaches you to read statutes and ask informed questions. It does not teach you to anticipate the ten issues that will arise in a specific formation, to evaluate which of four capital structures makes sense for a specific plan, or to draft language that will survive litigation. That is what counsel is for. Any real transaction — formation, capital raise, merger, dissolution — should involve a licensed Washington attorney.
