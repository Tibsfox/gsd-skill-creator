23B.25The Flagship Chapter

Washington State corporate law is codified in Title 23B RCW, the Washington Business Corporation Act (WBCA). Enacted in 1989 and modeled on the American Bar Association's Revised Model Business Corporation Act (RMBCA), it spans nineteen chapters covering formation, shares, shareholders, directors, officers, amendments, mergers, dissolution, and a dedicated chapter on Social Purpose Corporations that was added in 2012 — placing Washington among the earliest US states to codify a benefit-corporation-style legal form.

This research series treats the WBCA as an object of study. We trace its historical lineage from the 1933 Washington corporate code through the Model Business Corporation Act (1950) and the Revised Model (1984), examine the 1989 enactment, catalogue the amendments that shaped it, and — in the flagship module — give the Social Purpose Corporation chapter (RCW 23B.25) the same depth of treatment a law review article would provide.

Research method: primary sources (Washington Legislature at app.leg.wa.gov/rcw, Washington Session Laws), Washington case law (Justia, Casetext, Washington Supreme Court opinions), WSBA Business Law Section publications, Seattle University Law Review commentary on the SPC statute, B Lab model benefit corporation legislation, Delaware General Corporation Law for comparative analysis, and the RMBCA commentary tradition.

The WBCA Architecture — A Nineteen-Chapter Ladder

Title 23B RCW is organized as a logical progression from creation through dissolution. Each chapter builds on the prior. The Social Purpose Corporation chapter (23B.25) sits atop the standard corporation framework as an overlay — an SPC is a corporation in every respect, with additional statutory obligations.

  1. 23B.01 — General Provisions — Definitions, filing rules, fee schedule, effective dates, the skeleton of the statute.
  2. 23B.02 — Incorporation — Who may incorporate, articles requirements, effect of filing, organizational meeting, bylaws, emergency bylaws, forum selection.
  3. 23B.03 — Powers and Purposes — The corporate powers granted by statute; purpose clause flexibility.
  4. 23B.04 — Corporate Name — Uniqueness, reservation, distinguishable-on-record standard.
  5. 23B.05 — Office and Agent — Registered agent, registered office, service of process.
  6. 23B.06 — Shares and Distributions — Authorization, classes, preferences, issuance, restrictions, distributions, redemptions.
  7. 23B.07 — Shareholders — Meetings, quorum, voting, written consent, record date, proxy, shareholder agreements.
  8. 23B.08 — Directors and Officers — Board authority, duties of care and loyalty, the business judgment rule, indemnification, conflicts of interest.
  9. 23B.09 — Corporate Conversions — Converting between entity types.
  10. 23B.10 — Amendment of Articles and Bylaws — Procedure, vote thresholds (with the 2024 majority-vote change), voting groups.
  11. 23B.11A — Merger and Share Exchange — Plans, approval, filing, effect.
  12. 23B.12 — Sale of Assets — Ordinary course vs. substantially-all thresholds.
  13. 23B.13 — Dissenters' Rights — Triggering events, perfection procedure, fair value determination.
  14. 23B.14 — Dissolution — Voluntary, administrative, judicial dissolution; winding up; claims procedures.
  15. 23B.15 — Foreign Corporations — Authority to transact business in Washington; certificates of authority.
  16. 23B.16 — Records and Reports — Required records, inspection rights, financial statements.
  17. 23B.17 — Transition Provisions — How corporations formed under prior law migrated to the 1989 Act.
  18. 23B.19 — Significant Business Transactions — The Washington anti-takeover chapter; five-year moratorium.
  19. 23B.25 — Social Purpose Corporations — The 2012 overlay chapter; the flagship of this research.

Each rung presupposes the rungs below it. A practitioner drafting articles of incorporation under 23B.02 is implicitly relying on 23B.01 definitions, 23B.03 powers, 23B.04 naming, 23B.06 share rules, 23.95 RCW registered agent requirements, and — if an SPC — 23B.25 social purpose mechanics.

Why Washington Corporate Law Is Interesting to Study

The short version: Washington is one of about thirty states whose corporate code traces directly to the Model Business Corporation Act, making it representative of a major family of American corporate law. It is also the jurisdiction of some of the most distinctive benefit corporation drafting in the country — the Social Purpose Corporation framework is notably less prescriptive than Delaware's Public Benefit Corporation or the B Lab model legislation, and its director duty formulation (“may consider”) is the softest in the US.

The longer version:

  1. The MBCA lineage — Understanding Washington's statute is a shortcut to understanding the ~35 state corporate codes that share the RMBCA substrate. The differences tell you what each state cared about.
  2. The SPC statute as a design choice — Washington's drafters in 2011–2012 deliberately rejected the B Lab model. Why? They wanted a less prescriptive framework that did not bind directors to third-party certification standards or require prescribed reporting formats. The “may consider” formulation is a conscious decision.
  3. The “load-bearing wall” theory of corporate law — Corporate statutes can be thought of as a substrate: a small set of primitives (articles, bylaws, shares, directors, officers) on which any legal architecture can be assembled. Washington's 19-chapter structure is a particularly clean example of this principle. It does less than Delaware's General Corporation Law, but what it does, it does with a minimum of clutter.
  4. The 2024 amendment — The majority-vote threshold change for amendment approval is a recent, consequential reform with direct practical effect on new formations. It also signals a trend: Washington is willing to modernize, selectively, without wholesale rewrites.
  5. The comparative frame — Washington sits alongside California's defunct Flexible Purpose Corporation (phased out by renaming and consolidation), Delaware's robust Public Benefit Corporation case law, Vermont's first-in-the-nation benefit corporation statute, and international analogs in the UK, France, Italy, and Canada. The comparison is illuminating.

The punchline: Corporate statutes are infrastructure. They are the TCP/IP of enterprise. Most practitioners interact with them at the level of filing forms and drafting articles, but the design choices embedded in a corporate code — who gets default authority, what vote thresholds apply, what duties directors owe to whom, what counts as a valid purpose — determine what can be built on top. Washington's WBCA is a study in restrained drafting that leaves room for builders.
