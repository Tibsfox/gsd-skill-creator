The Transactional End of Corporate Life

This module covers the five chapters of Title 23B that govern the major transactional events in a corporation's life: mergers and share exchanges (23B.11A), asset sales (23B.12), dissenters' rights (23B.13), dissolution in its voluntary, administrative, and judicial forms (23B.14), and the anti-takeover chapter (23B.19). Together they describe how a Washington corporation can be combined with another entity, sold in whole or in part, shrunk to nothing, or defended against a hostile acquirer.

Mergers and Share Exchanges (RCW 23B.11A)

Washington's merger statute was significantly augmented by the addition of Chapter 23B.11A RCW, a modernized merger regime that permits more flexible transaction structures including domestications and conversions.

Core Definitions

  • Merger. A transaction in which one or more domestic or foreign entities merge with a domestic corporation. The surviving entity continues; the non-surviving (“disappearing”) entity ceases to exist.
  • Share exchange. A transaction in which a corporation acquires all of the outstanding shares of one or more classes or series of another corporation in exchange for other consideration. Both corporations continue to exist.
  • Conversion. A transaction in which an entity changes its form (e.g., LLC to corporation, or corporation to SPC) while maintaining its identity.

Plan of Merger Requirements

A merger requires a written plan addressing the names of the parties, the surviving entity, the terms of the merger (including the manner and basis of converting shares of each constituent corporation into shares, obligations, other securities, cash, or any combination), any amendments to the articles of the surviving entity, and any other provisions required or permitted.

Board and Shareholder Approval

Mergers require both board adoption and shareholder approval. The vote thresholds follow the 2024 c 22 formation-date dichotomy:

Formation dateDefault vote threshold
Before August 1, 2024 Two-thirds of votes entitled to be cast, plus two-thirds of each voting group entitled to vote separately
On or after August 1, 2024 Majority of votes entitled to be cast, plus majority of each voting group

SPCs that merge must comply with the enhanced 23B.25.100 two-thirds requirement regardless of formation date, unless the surviving entity is itself an SPC with substantially similar social purposes. This SPC-specific overlay is one of the three mission-protection triggers in Chapter 23B.25.

Short-Form Parent-Subsidiary Merger

A parent corporation that owns at least 90% of a subsidiary can merge the subsidiary into itself via a short-form merger without a subsidiary shareholder vote. The parent's board adopts a plan; articles of merger are filed; the subsidiary disappears. This is a common structure in corporate group restructurings.

Triangular Mergers

Forward and reverse triangular mergers are common in acquisitions. In a forward triangular merger, the target merges into a subsidiary of the acquirer and the subsidiary survives. In a reverse triangular merger, the subsidiary merges into the target and the target survives as a subsidiary of the acquirer. Reverse triangular mergers are often preferred because they preserve the target's contracts, licenses, and regulatory approvals, which might be disrupted by a forward structure.

Effect of Merger

Upon effectiveness, the surviving entity succeeds to all rights, privileges, powers, and properties of the disappearing entity, and assumes all of its liabilities, obligations, and contracts. The disappearing entity ceases to exist. Shares of the disappearing entity are converted into the merger consideration specified in the plan.

Asset Sales (RCW 23B.12)

Chapter 23B.12 RCW governs the sale, lease, exchange, or other disposition of a corporation's assets. The chapter draws a critical line between:

  • Ordinary course transactions — The board may approve these without shareholder action.
  • Sales of “all or substantially all” assets — These require shareholder approval by the same vote thresholds that apply to mergers and amendments.

The “All or Substantially All” Standard

What counts as “all or substantially all” of the assets of a corporation is a fact-specific question that has generated significant litigation, particularly in Delaware. Leading cases include Katz v. Bregman (Delaware Chancery, 1981) and Hollinger Inc. v. Hollinger International, Inc. (Delaware Chancery, 2004). Washington has followed the Delaware approach in substance if not in citation: the inquiry is both quantitative (what percentage of assets, revenues, or operations is being sold?) and qualitative (does the sale fundamentally change the nature of the corporation's business?).

A rough working rule: sales of more than 75% of assets or revenues are presumptively substantial; sales of less than 25% are presumptively not substantial; sales in between are fact-specific. Practitioners prefer to err on the side of shareholder approval when the line is uncertain, because a sale that should have received shareholder approval but did not is vulnerable to rescission or damages claims.

Successor Liability

When one corporation buys the assets of another, the default rule is that the buyer does not inherit the seller's liabilities. But four exceptions are widely recognized (including in Washington):

  1. Express assumption. The buyer agreed to assume specified liabilities.
  2. De facto merger. The transaction, in substance, is a merger even if formally structured as an asset sale.
  3. Mere continuation. The buyer is a mere continuation of the seller — same management, same owners, same business.
  4. Fraudulent transfer. The transaction was designed to escape creditors.

Structuring asset sales to avoid these exceptions — particularly the de facto merger and mere continuation exceptions — is a core concern of M&A practice.

Dissenters' Rights (RCW 23B.13)

Dissenters' rights — also known as appraisal rights — permit shareholders who disagree with certain fundamental transactions to exit at fair value rather than being forced to accept the transaction consideration. Chapter 23B.13 RCW establishes the Washington framework.

Triggering Events

Dissenters' rights attach to specified fundamental transactions:

  • Mergers requiring shareholder approval under RCW 23B.11A
  • Share exchanges
  • Sales of all or substantially all assets requiring shareholder approval under RCW 23B.12
  • Certain amendments to articles that adversely affect the dissenter's rights
  • Significant business transactions under RCW 23B.19 in specified circumstances
  • Conversion from or to SPC status under RCW 23B.25.120

The Perfection Procedure

Exercising dissenters' rights is a multi-step, time-sensitive procedure. Any misstep can forfeit the rights. The standard sequence:

  1. Notice of intent. Before the shareholder vote, the shareholder must deliver written notice of intent to demand payment if the proposed action is effectuated.
  2. No vote in favor. The shareholder must not vote in favor of the proposed action. Abstention or a vote against preserves dissenter status; a vote for it forfeits.
  3. Dissenter notice from corporation. After the vote, the corporation sends a dissenter's notice to all shareholders who satisfied steps 1 and 2, stating how and where to demand payment and deposit shares.
  4. Payment demand. The dissenter delivers a payment demand and deposits share certificates within the specified window.
  5. Corporation's payment. The corporation pays the dissenter what it estimates to be fair value, along with financial information and an opportunity to dispute.
  6. Supplemental demand. If the dissenter disagrees with the corporation's estimate, the dissenter must deliver a supplemental demand within 30 days.
  7. Court proceeding. If the corporation and the dissenter cannot agree, the corporation must commence a court proceeding to determine fair value. If the corporation fails to commence proceedings, it must pay the dissenter's estimate.

Fair Value

“Fair value” is defined in RCW 23B.13.010 as the value of the shares immediately before the effective date of the corporate action, excluding any appreciation or depreciation in anticipation of the corporate action (unless such exclusion would be inequitable). The exclusion clause is important: it prevents dissenters from capturing merger-premium value, but allows them to obtain the corporation's pre-transaction going-concern value.

Fair value determination is expert-driven. Typical methodologies include discounted cash flow analysis, comparable company analysis, and comparable transaction analysis. Litigation over fair value is expensive and uncertain; dissenters should confirm that the likely recovery materially exceeds the deal price plus litigation costs before proceeding.

The Market-Out Exception

Dissenters' rights are not available for shares that were, at the record date, listed on a national securities exchange or held by more than a specified number of shareholders. This is the “market-out” exception, reflecting the judgment that publicly-traded shareholders already have an exit option through the market itself.

Dissolution (RCW 23B.14)

Chapter 23B.14 RCW governs dissolution — the legal ending of the corporation's existence. Washington law recognizes three pathways: voluntary (by the corporation itself), administrative (by the Secretary of State for procedural failures), and judicial (by court order).

Voluntary Dissolution — Pre-Activity (RCW 23B.14.010)

A streamlined dissolution path for corporations that have not yet begun business and have not issued shares. A majority of the incorporators or the initial directors may deliver articles of dissolution to the Secretary of State. Used when a corporation was formed in anticipation of a transaction that did not proceed.

Voluntary Dissolution — Standard Process (RCW 23B.14.020)

After shares are issued or business has begun, voluntary dissolution requires:

  1. Board proposal. The board must propose dissolution and recommend it to shareholders.
  2. Shareholder vote. Two-thirds for pre-August 2024 corporations, majority for post-August 2024 corporations (plus the same threshold for each separately voting group).
  3. Articles of dissolution. Filed with the Secretary of State along with a Washington Department of Revenue revenue clearance certificate.

The revenue clearance certificate is the practical bottleneck. It confirms the corporation has filed all required tax returns and paid all taxes due. Obtaining it requires a DOR application and can take weeks or longer if there are outstanding tax issues. Practitioners initiate DOR clearance at the outset of dissolution planning, not at the end.

Publication of Notice to Claimants

Within 30 days after dissolution becomes effective, the dissolved corporation must publish notice requesting creditors to present claims. The notice is published once a week for three consecutive weeks in a newspaper of general circulation in the county of the principal (or registered) office. Failure to publish does not affect the validity of the dissolution but forfeits the benefit of the three-year unknown-claims bar.

Effect of Dissolution — Continued Existence for Winding Up

Dissolution does not terminate the corporation's legal existence immediately. Instead, the corporation continues to exist for the limited purpose of winding up: collecting assets, disposing of property, discharging liabilities, and distributing remaining property to shareholders. During winding up, the corporation may sue and be sued, retains its directors and officers, and maintains its registered agent.

Known and Unknown Claims

The dissolved corporation can cut off known claims through a written notice procedure: notice to the claimant, 120-day deadline, 90-day window to file suit after rejection. Failure to follow the procedure bars the claim.

Unknown claims are addressed through newspaper publication. Claims by unknown claimants are barred unless the claimant commences a proceeding within three years after the publication date. Even after the three-year bar, late claimants may seek to recover from shareholders who received distributions, up to the amount of the distributions received (the “shareholder clawback” rule).

Administrative Dissolution

Administrative dissolution is by the Secretary of State for procedural failures. The grounds (RCW 23.95.605) include failure to pay fees, failure to file the annual report, failure to maintain a registered agent, failure to file a statement of change of agent, and expiration of the period of duration. The procedure:

  1. The Secretary of State serves written notice identifying the grounds.
  2. The entity has 60 days to cure or demonstrate that grounds do not exist.
  3. If not cured, the Secretary of State files a statement of administrative dissolution.
  4. The entity's existence is restricted to winding up and seeking reinstatement.

Reinstatement is available within a five-year window under RCW 23.95.615, with all back reports, fees, and penalties paid. Reinstatement relates back to the effective date of dissolution; the entity is treated as having been in continuous existence. Beyond five years, the entity is permanently dissolved.

Judicial Dissolution (RCW 23B.14.300)

The superior court may dissolve a corporation in three categories of proceedings:

Attorney General proceedings

Rare. The Washington AG may petition if the corporation obtained its articles through fraud or has continued to exceed or abuse the authority conferred by law. Used primarily against corporations engaged in systematic fraud or serious public welfare violations.

Shareholder proceedings

The most frequently litigated. Five grounds:

  1. Director deadlock threatening irreparable injury
  2. Oppression, illegality, or fraud by those in control
  3. Shareholder deadlock — failure to elect directors at two consecutive annual meetings
  4. Misapplication or waste of corporate assets
  5. Failure to dissolve after ceasing operations

The oppression ground is most common in closely held corporations where the majority has frozen out the minority — removing them from employment, stopping dividends, refusing to redeem shares at fair value. Oppression has been interpreted as conduct that substantially defeats the reasonable expectations of minority shareholders on which they relied when investing.

Creditor proceedings

Available to a creditor with an unsatisfied judgment against an insolvent corporation. Rarely used in practice because creditors typically prefer bankruptcy.

Alternative Remedies

Courts have broad remedial discretion in shareholder dissolution cases. Rather than ordering dissolution itself, the court may order:

  • A buy-out of the petitioning shareholder's shares at fair value
  • Appointment of a provisional director to break a deadlock
  • Appointment of a custodian to oversee corporate affairs temporarily
  • Injunctions against oppressive conduct

Dissolution is often considered the remedy of last resort, used when alternative remedies cannot cure the underlying problem.

Significant Business Transactions — The Anti-Takeover Chapter (RCW 23B.19)

Washington's anti-takeover statute imposes a five-year moratorium on certain transactions between a target corporation and an acquiring person who has obtained ten percent or more of the target's voting power. Five years is longer than any peer jurisdiction's moratorium — Delaware's is three. Washington's statute is one of the most restrictive in the country.

Purpose and History

Washington enacted Chapter 23B.19 in 1987 as part of the broad wave of state anti-takeover statutes adopted in response to the hostile-takeover boom of the 1980s. The policy rationale was to protect Washington corporations and their stakeholders (employees, communities, suppliers) from coercive takeover tactics — particularly the two-tier front-end-loaded tender offer, in which a bidder offered a premium for enough shares to gain control, with the understanding that any remaining shareholders would be squeezed out at a lower price in a subsequent freeze-out merger.

The two-tier offer created a prisoner's-dilemma dynamic: shareholders had to tender their shares at the front-end price (even if they preferred the target to remain independent) because the alternative — being cashed out at the back-end price — was worse. Anti-takeover statutes like Chapter 23B.19 were designed to disrupt this dynamic by imposing a moratorium on the back-end merger, thereby giving target boards leverage to negotiate with bidders and giving shareholders a real choice about whether to tender.

Washington's statute is part of the same family as Delaware Section 203 (1988), Pennsylvania's control-transaction statute, and Ohio's control-share statute. But Washington's is notably stricter than Delaware's on several dimensions: the moratorium is five years (Delaware's is three), the exceptions are narrower, and the permitted transactions within the moratorium are more limited.

Key Definitions

  • Target corporation. A publicly traded Washington corporation, or a private Washington corporation that has affirmatively opted in by articles amendment.
  • Acquiring person. A person who owns 10% or more of the outstanding voting shares of the target.
  • Significant business transaction. Mergers, consolidations, asset sales exceeding specified thresholds, disproportionate share issuances, loans or guarantees exceeding specified thresholds, and plans of liquidation.
  • Share acquisition time. The moment the acquiring person crossed the 10% threshold.

Opting In for Private Corporations

Private Washington corporations are not automatically covered by Chapter 23B.19. They become covered only by amending their articles to opt in. This is a significant distinction from Delaware Section 203, which automatically applies to Delaware public corporations (unless they opt out). In Washington, private corporations that want the moratorium protection must affirmatively elect it.

The Five-Year Moratorium (RCW 23B.19.040)

“A target corporation shall not, for a period of five years following the acquiring person's share acquisition time, engage in a significant business transaction with such acquiring person.”

Five years. This is the central fact of Washington's anti-takeover regime, and it is a longer moratorium than any peer jurisdiction. Delaware imposes a three-year moratorium under Section 203; most other MBCA states that have adopted similar statutes impose three-year moratoriums as well. Washington's five-year period is an outlier that materially affects M&A planning.

Exceptions

  1. Categorical exemptions under RCW 23B.19.030. Certain transactions are categorically excluded — acquisitions of shares from the target itself, transactions approved in advance under specified procedures, and good-faith transactions that do not implicate the statute's anti-takeover purpose.
  2. Pre-acquisition board approval. If the target's board approved either the transaction or the acquisition of shares before the acquiring person crossed the 10% threshold, the moratorium does not apply. This is critical for friendly transactions: if the acquiring person and the target negotiate a friendly deal, the target board can approve the acquisition in advance, qualifying the acquiring person for the exception and avoiding the moratorium.

Comparison to Delaware Section 203

Dimension Washington 23B.19 Delaware Section 203
Enacted 1987 1988
Default coverage Public corporations only; private corporations must opt in All Delaware corporations with listed stock; opt-out available
Triggering threshold 10% voting power 15% voting power
Moratorium length 5 years 3 years
Exceptions Narrower; pre-approval must be unambiguous More flexible; includes an 85%-ownership exception
Effect on M&A planning Materially discourages hostile acquisitions of Washington targets Discourages but permits workarounds

For acquirers considering hostile transactions involving Washington targets, Chapter 23B.19 is a significant obstacle. Friendly transactions can navigate the statute through pre-acquisition board approval, but unwelcome bidders face a five-year moratorium that effectively forecloses most takeover strategies.

Synthesis — The Five-Chapter Transactional Arc

The five chapters covered in this module form a coherent transactional arc:

  1. RCW 23B.11A (Mergers). The mechanism for combining entities. Requires board approval and shareholder approval at the 2024-dichotomy threshold. Short-form and triangular structures provide flexibility for corporate groups.
  2. RCW 23B.12 (Asset Sales). The mechanism for selling a business without merging entities. Ordinary-course sales are board matters; “all or substantially all” sales require shareholder approval at the same thresholds as mergers.
  3. RCW 23B.13 (Dissenters' Rights). The escape valve for shareholders who disagree with fundamental transactions. Fair-value determination, multi-step perfection procedure, market-out exception for public-company shares.
  4. RCW 23B.14 (Dissolution). The ending of corporate existence. Voluntary, administrative, and judicial pathways. Winding-up, known and unknown claims, five-year administrative dissolution reinstatement window.
  5. RCW 23B.19 (Anti-Takeover). The structural defense against hostile control changes. Five-year moratorium, 10% threshold, opt-in for private corporations, pre-approval safe harbor.

Together, these chapters describe the full transactional life of a Washington corporation: how it can be combined, how it can be sold, how disaffected shareholders can exit, how it can be ended, and how it is protected against unwanted change of control. Each chapter represents a distinct policy balance between facilitation and protection — making transactions possible while preserving the legitimate interests of shareholders, creditors, and (in the case of the anti-takeover chapter) Washington stakeholders who would bear the costs of a hostile acquisition.
