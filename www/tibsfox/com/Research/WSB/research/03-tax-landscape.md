## 1. The B&O Tax Model: Revenue Not Profit

Washington's Business & Occupation (B&O) tax is a **gross receipts tax** — it applies to **total revenue with zero deductions** for labor, materials, rent, or any other cost of doing business.

This is fundamentally different from income-based taxes (which apply to profits) and is the most important financial concept for new Washington businesses to internalize:

```
Income Tax (most states):
  Revenue − Expenses = Profit × Tax Rate = Tax Owed
  (A business with $500K revenue and $490K expenses pays tax on $10K)

B&O Tax (Washington):
  Revenue × Tax Rate = Tax Owed
  (A business with $500K revenue pays tax on the full $500K,
   regardless of whether it made or lost money)
```

**Practical implication:** A startup with $300,000 in revenue and $350,000 in expenses is operating at a $50,000 loss — but still owes approximately $4,500 in B&O tax (at the 1.5% service rate). Cash flow planning must account for B&O obligations from the first dollar of revenue.

## 2. B&O Tax Classifications and Rates (2026)

Washington's B&O tax classifies business activity by type. The classification determines the applicable rate:

| Classification | Rate (2026) | Notes |
|----------------|------------|-------|
| **Retailing** | 0.50% | Increased from 0.471% per 2025 legislation |
| **Wholesaling** | 0.50% | Increased from 0.484% |
| **Manufacturing** | 0.50% | MATC (Manufacturing B&O Tax Credit) available |
| **Service & Other Activities (< $5M aggregate)** | 1.5% | Standard service rate for most small businesses |
| **Service & Other Activities (≥ $5M aggregate)** | 2.1% | Increased from 1.75% per October 2025 change |
| **Large business surcharge** | +0.5% on income over $250M | Effective January 1, 2026 |

### 2.1 Classification Determination

The B&O classification is determined by the **nature of the activity generating the revenue**:
- A retail store selling products: **Retailing**
- A manufacturer selling goods they made: **Manufacturing** (on production value)
- An IT consultant providing services: **Service & Other Activities**
- A company buying goods to sell wholesale: **Wholesaling**

Some businesses have revenues in multiple classifications and must report each separately.

### 2.2 The Small Business B&O Tax Credit

The small business B&O tax credit effectively exempts very small businesses:
- Businesses with annual B&O tax liability under $761 owe no B&O tax
- The credit phases out as tax liability increases
- Applies to all B&O classifications combined

This means businesses with very low annual revenues (roughly under $50,000 for service businesses) may qualify for a full credit.

**Source:** Washington Department of Revenue, B&O tax page; Association of Washington Business.

## 3. The 2025 Legislative Tax Changes

Washington's 2025 legislative session produced the largest tax increase in state history. Key changes affecting small businesses:

### 3.1 B&O Rate Changes (Effective October 1, 2025)

| Category | Old Rate | New Rate | Change |
|----------|----------|----------|--------|
| Retailing | 0.471% | 0.50% | +0.029% |
| Wholesaling | 0.484% | 0.50% | +0.016% |
| Manufacturing | 0.484% | 0.50% | +0.016% |
| Service ≥ $5M | 1.75% | 2.1% | +0.35% |

### 3.2 Service Rate Tier Structure

The 2025 legislation introduced a tiered service rate:
- Under $5 million aggregate income: remains at 1.5%
- $5 million or more aggregate income: increases to 2.1%

This tiered structure means most small service businesses (under $5M) see no rate change for the service classification.

### 3.3 Large Business Surcharge (Effective January 1, 2026)

An additional 0.5% B&O surcharge applies to income over $250 million. This does not affect small businesses but is part of the broader 2025 tax package.

**Source:** Association of Washington Business; HCVT LLP analysis; DOR B&O tax guidance.

## 4. Sales Tax Expansion (October 2025)

Washington's 2025 tax package significantly expanded the range of services subject to sales tax, effective **October 1, 2025**. This expansion is particularly relevant for technology and professional service businesses.

### 4.1 Newly Taxable Services (October 1, 2025)

| Service Category | Notes |
|-----------------|-------|
| IT support and training | Help desk, tech support, training services |
| Data processing | Processing, storage, retrieval |
| Website design and development | Including UI/UX design |
| Security services | Physical and logical security services |
| Temporary staffing services | Placement fees (hospital staffing exempted) |
| Advertising services | Digital and traditional advertising placement |
| Live events/training with real-time interaction | Webinars, live online classes |

### 4.2 Explicit Exclusions from Expansion

| Excluded Service | Notes |
|-----------------|-------|
| Web hosting | Hosting remains non-taxable |
| Domain registration | Registration services excluded |
| Newspapers | Traditional media exempted |
| Radio and TV broadcasting | Broadcasting exempted |
| Publishing | Book, magazine, newsletter publishing |
| Out-of-home advertising | Billboards, transit ads excluded |

### 4.3 Compliance Requirements

Businesses newly subject to sales tax must:
1. Register to collect sales tax through BLS (if not already registered)
2. Begin collecting Washington state sales tax + applicable local rates on covered services
3. Remit collected taxes with their regular sales tax filing
4. Update invoicing systems and customer-facing pricing

**Source:** Association of Washington Business; CLA Connect analysis.

## 5. Sales Tax Collection and Filing

### 5.1 Washington Sales Tax Structure

Washington sales tax is a combined state + local rate:
- **State rate:** 6.5%
- **Local rates:** Vary by city and county (average 1.5–3%)
- **Everett combined rate:** Approximately 10.6% (state 6.5% + Snohomish County + Everett city)

The combined rate applies to the delivery location of taxable goods and services.

### 5.2 Nexus: Who Must Collect

Washington requires sales tax collection from businesses that have:
- **Physical nexus:** A physical location, employee, or inventory in Washington
- **Economic nexus (2018+):** More than $100,000 in retail sales or 200 transactions into Washington per year

Out-of-state businesses selling into Washington that meet the economic nexus threshold must collect Washington sales tax.

### 5.3 Sales Tax Filing

Sales tax is reported and remitted through DOR's online portal (My DOR). Filing frequency is assigned by DOR based on estimated sales tax liability:
- Monthly (highest volume): Typically assigned for large retailers
- Quarterly: Mid-size businesses
- Annually: Small businesses with minimal sales tax

## 6. City-Level B&O Taxes

Several Washington cities levy their own B&O taxes in addition to the state B&O tax. These are separate filing and payment obligations:

### 6.1 Seattle (Reference)

Seattle's B&O threshold increased to **$2 million** following voter approval of Proposition 2, effective January 1, 2026. Businesses with gross receipts under $2 million owe **no City of Seattle B&O tax**. Above that threshold, only receipts exceeding $2 million are taxed.

### 6.2 Everett

The City of Everett's B&O tax requirements should be verified directly with the **City of Everett Finance Department**. Local B&O rates and thresholds vary significantly across jurisdictions and are not maintained in a centralized state database.

**Source:** City of Seattle Finance; MRSC city B&O overview.

## 7. Filing Frequency and Deadlines

### 7.1 B&O Filing Frequency

B&O tax filing frequency is assigned by DOR based on annual tax liability:

| Annual B&O Liability | Filing Frequency | Due Date |
|---------------------|-----------------|---------|
| Under $2,500 | Annual | April 15 (following year) |
| $2,500 – $9,999 | Quarterly | Last day of month after quarter end |
| $10,000 or more | Monthly | Last day of following month |

### 7.2 First-Year Caution

New businesses are typically assigned quarterly filing to start. If the business grows rapidly, DOR may reassign to monthly. Missing a reassigned filing deadline results in penalties and interest.

## 8. Tax Credits and Deductions

### 8.1 Small Business B&O Tax Credit

As described in Section 2.2, the small business credit eliminates B&O tax for businesses with very low annual liability.

### 8.2 Manufacturing and Research & Development Credits

| Credit | Benefit |
|--------|---------|
| Manufacturing B&O Tax Credit (MATC) | Credit against B&O tax for certain manufacturing activities |
| R&D B&O Tax Credit | Credit for qualified research and development expenditures |
| New Jobs B&O Tax Credit | Applies in certain distressed areas for new positions created |

### 8.3 B&O Deductions

While B&O has no deductions for costs of goods sold, Washington does allow deductions from gross receipts for:
- Bad debts (on previously reported income that became uncollectible)
- Cash discounts allowed to customers
- Returns and allowances
- Amounts received as agent for another party (only the commission, not the full amount)

**Source:** Washington DOR; Association of Washington Business.

## 9. No State Personal or Corporate Income Tax

Washington has **no personal income tax** and **no state corporate income tax**. The B&O tax is the primary mechanism by which business activity is taxed. Individuals pay no state income tax on wages or pass-through business income.

This is a significant advantage for small business owners compared to states like California, Oregon, or New York. However, the B&O tax, gross receipts model offsets this advantage — especially for lower-margin businesses.

### 9.1 Interaction with Federal Taxes

Washington business owners still pay:
- Federal income tax (individual rates for pass-through entities, corporate rates for C corps)
- Federal self-employment tax (sole proprietors and LLC members)
- Federal payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA)
- Washington state B&O tax (in lieu of state income tax)
- Washington sales tax (on taxable goods and services)
- Washington payroll taxes (workers' comp, PFML, WA Cares — see Module 04)

## 10. Practical Tax Examples

### Example A: Small Service Business in Everett

A solo IT consultant with $180,000 in annual revenue:
- B&O classification: Service & Other Activities (< $5M)
- B&O rate: 1.5%
- B&O tax owed: $180,000 × 1.5% = **$2,700/year**
- Sales tax: IT support is now taxable (post-October 2025) at ~10.6% Everett rate
  - If all clients are in Everett: $180,000 × 10.6% = $19,080 collected and remitted (not a cost to the consultant — collected from clients)
- Filing: Likely quarterly B&O; quarterly sales tax

### Example B: Retail Business

A gift shop with $450,000 in annual retail sales:
- B&O classification: Retailing
- B&O rate: 0.50%
- B&O tax owed: $450,000 × 0.50% = **$2,250/year**
- Sales tax: 10.6% collected on taxable sales, remitted to DOR
- Filing: Likely quarterly or monthly depending on growth
