## 1. The Six Words

Before spreadsheets, before journal entries, before debits and credits — there are six words. Every accounting system in every business in every country ultimately reduces to these six categories.

| Word | Definition | Normal Balance | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Assets** | Resources owned that have future economic value | Debit | Cash, inventory, equipment, accounts receivable, buildings |
| **Liabilities** | Obligations owed to others | Credit | Loans, accounts payable, unearned revenue, bonds payable |
| **Shareholders' Equity** | Residual interest after liabilities subtracted from assets | Credit | Common stock, retained earnings, additional paid-in capital |
| **Revenue** | Income earned from primary business activities | Credit | Sales revenue, service revenue, interest income |
| **Expenses** | Costs incurred to generate revenue | Debit | Rent, salaries, utilities, depreciation, COGS |
| **Dividends** | Distributions of profit to shareholders | Debit | Cash dividends, stock dividends |

These are "not math words — they're language words." Accounting is called the language of business because fluency in these six terms lets you read and understand any company's financial story.

## 2. The Accounting Equation

The single most important equation in business:

### Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

This equation must **always** balance. Every single transaction, no matter how complex, maintains this equality. If total assets are $500,000, then total liabilities plus total equity must also equal $500,000.

**Why it works:** Assets represent everything a company owns. Those assets were either funded by borrowing (liabilities) or by owners' investment and retained profits (equity). There is no third source.

### Expanded Equation

**Assets = Liabilities + Common Stock + Retained Earnings - Dividends + Revenue - Expenses**

This expansion shows how the income statement (revenue and expenses) and the statement of retained earnings (dividends) connect to the balance sheet.

## 3. Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Every transaction has **two** effects. When you buy equipment for cash:
- Equipment (asset) increases → debit
- Cash (asset) decreases → credit

When you earn revenue on credit:
- Accounts receivable (asset) increases → debit
- Revenue increases → credit

### Debit and Credit Rules

| Account Type | Increase | Decrease |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| Assets | Debit | Credit |
| Liabilities | Credit | Debit |
| Equity | Credit | Debit |
| Revenue | Credit | Debit |
| Expenses | Debit | Credit |
| Dividends | Debit | Credit |

**Mnemonic:** Assets, Expenses, and Dividends are "debit normal" (AED). Liabilities, Equity, and Revenue are "credit normal" (LER).

### Journal Entry Format

```
Date    Account Name              Debit     Credit
Jan 15  Equipment                 50,000
          Cash                                50,000
        (Purchased equipment for cash)
```

The debit entry is listed first, indented. The credit entry is indented further. A brief description follows in parentheses.

## 4. The Chart of Accounts

A numbered listing of all accounts used by a business, organized by category:

| Range | Category | Examples |
|-------|----------|----------|
| 1000-1999 | Assets | 1010 Cash, 1200 Accounts Receivable, 1500 Equipment |
| 2000-2999 | Liabilities | 2010 Accounts Payable, 2500 Notes Payable |
| 3000-3999 | Equity | 3010 Common Stock, 3500 Retained Earnings |
| 4000-4999 | Revenue | 4010 Sales Revenue, 4500 Service Revenue |
| 5000-5999 | Cost of Goods Sold | 5010 COGS |
| 6000-6999 | Operating Expenses | 6010 Rent, 6020 Salaries, 6030 Utilities |
| 7000-7999 | Other Income/Expense | 7010 Interest Income, 7500 Interest Expense |

## 5. The Accounting Cycle

The complete sequence from transaction to financial statements:

### Step 1: Analyze Transactions
Identify which accounts are affected and whether they increase or decrease.

### Step 2: Record Journal Entries
Write the dual-effect entries in the general journal, chronologically.

### Step 3: Post to the General Ledger
Transfer journal entries to individual account T-accounts (or ledger accounts).

### Step 4: Prepare a Trial Balance
List all account balances. Total debits must equal total credits. If not, find the error.

### Step 5: Adjusting Entries
Record end-of-period adjustments for:
- **Accrued revenues** — earned but not yet recorded (e.g., interest receivable)
- **Accrued expenses** — incurred but not yet recorded (e.g., wages payable)
- **Deferred revenues** — collected but not yet earned (e.g., unearned rent)
- **Deferred expenses** — paid but not yet used (e.g., prepaid insurance)
- **Depreciation** — allocation of asset cost over useful life

### Step 6: Adjusted Trial Balance
Re-list all accounts with adjustments applied. Verify debits still equal credits.

### Step 7: Financial Statements
Prepare in this order:
1. Income Statement (uses revenue and expense accounts)
2. Statement of Changes in Equity (uses net income from step 1 + dividends)
3. Balance Sheet (uses ending equity from step 2 + assets and liabilities)
4. Cash Flow Statement (reconciles net income to actual cash movements)

### Step 8: Closing Entries
Zero out all temporary accounts (revenue, expenses, dividends) by transferring balances to Retained Earnings.

### Step 9: Post-Closing Trial Balance
Verify that only permanent accounts (assets, liabilities, equity) remain with balances.

## 6. Financial Statements

### Income Statement

Reports **profitability** over a period of time (month, quarter, year).

```
Revenue                          $200,000
  Cost of Goods Sold             (120,000)
Gross Profit                       80,000
  Operating Expenses              (45,000)
Operating Income                   35,000
  Interest Expense                 (5,000)
Income Before Tax                  30,000
  Income Tax (25%)                 (7,500)
Net Income                        $22,500
```

### Statement of Changes in Equity

Shows how equity evolved during the period.

```
Common Shares, Jan 1             $60,000
  + Shares Issued                  10,000
Common Shares, Dec 31             70,000

Retained Earnings, Jan 1          10,000
  + Net Income                     22,500
  - Dividends                      (5,000)
Retained Earnings, Dec 31         27,500

Total Equity, Dec 31             $97,500
```

### Balance Sheet

Reports **financial position** at a specific point in time.

```
ASSETS
  Cash                           $35,000
  Accounts Receivable             25,000
  Inventory                       40,000
  Equipment (net)                100,000
Total Assets                    $200,000

LIABILITIES
  Accounts Payable               $15,000
  Notes Payable                   50,000
  Long-term Debt                  37,500
Total Liabilities                102,500

SHAREHOLDERS' EQUITY
  Common Stock                    70,000
  Retained Earnings               27,500
Total Equity                      97,500

Total Liab + Equity             $200,000
```

**Verification:** Assets ($200,000) = Liabilities ($102,500) + Equity ($97,500). The equation balances.

### Cash Flow Statement

Reconciles net income to actual cash movements across three categories:

| Section | What It Covers | Examples |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| **Operating** | Day-to-day business | Cash from customers, payments to suppliers, wages |
| **Investing** | Long-term assets | Equipment purchases, property sales |
| **Financing** | Capital structure | Loans, stock issuance, dividend payments |

## 7. Common-Size Analysis

Expressing financial statements in percentages rather than dollar amounts to enable cross-company comparison. Covered in detail in Module 09 (Corporate Finance Fundamentals).

**Income Statement:** Revenue = 100%, everything else as percentage of revenue.
**Balance Sheet:** Total Assets = 100%, everything else as percentage of total assets.

## 8. Accounting for Small Business

The CPA content specifically addresses small business owners:

### The 9/10 Statistic
> "9 out of 10 small businesses that fail, fail due to financial mismanagement of their company."

### Three Failure Modes
1. **Failure to make decisions** — without financial data, owners fly blind
2. **Failure to meet obligations** — can't pay employees or rent on time
3. **Failure to pay taxes** — miss deductions, incur penalties

### Practical Advice
- Don't wait until tax time to do accounting
- Separate business and personal finances from day one
- Track every expense, no matter how small
- Understand your break-even point
- Review financial statements monthly, not annually

## 9. Study Topics

### Beginner
1. Memorize the six words and their normal balances
2. Practice the accounting equation with 10 different transactions
3. Record journal entries for common transactions (cash sale, credit purchase, loan payment)
4. Prepare a simple trial balance

### Intermediate
5. Complete the full accounting cycle for a one-month period
6. Prepare all four financial statements from adjusted trial balance
7. Perform bank reconciliation
8. Calculate depreciation using straight-line and declining balance methods

### Advanced
9. Adjusting entries for complex accruals and deferrals
10. Multi-step income statement with discontinued operations
11. Statement of cash flows using indirect method
12. Financial ratio analysis (liquidity, profitability, efficiency, leverage)

## 10. DIY Sessions

### Session 1: Journal Entry Practice (1 hour)
Record journal entries for 20 transactions: cash sales, credit sales, inventory purchases, equipment acquisition, loan receipt, loan payment, wage payment, utility payment, depreciation, dividend declaration, revenue recognition for subscription service.

### Session 2: Full Accounting Cycle (3 hours)
Starting from 20 transactions, complete the entire cycle: journal entries → posting → trial balance → adjusting entries → adjusted trial balance → financial statements → closing entries → post-closing trial balance.

### Session 3: Real Company Analysis (2 hours)
Download Apple's 10-K from SEC EDGAR. Find: total revenue, net income, total assets, total liabilities, shareholders' equity. Verify the accounting equation. Calculate 5 key ratios: current ratio, debt-to-equity, profit margin, ROE, asset turnover.

## 11. Cross-Cluster Connections

### Accounting → Trust System
Double-entry bookkeeping is **symmetric verification** — every transaction creates two entries that must agree. If they don't, something is wrong. This is exactly the principle behind distributed ledger verification: every state change has a cryptographic proof that can be independently checked.

### Accounting → Observability
The four financial statements are the **observability stack** of a business:
- Income statement = **metrics dashboard** (real-time performance)
- Balance sheet = **state snapshot** (system health at a point in time)
- Cash flow statement = **trace log** (where resources actually moved)
- Statement of changes in equity = **audit trail** (how ownership evolved)

### Accounting → Infrastructure
Infrastructure project accounting introduces unique challenges: capitalize vs. expense decisions, construction-in-progress accounts, impairment testing for long-lived assets, government grant accounting.
