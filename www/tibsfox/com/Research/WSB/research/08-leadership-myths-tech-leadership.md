## 1. The Myths of Leadership

Dr. Robin Rose (Associate Professor of the Practice of Leadership, Brown SPS) identifies leadership myths as stories we learned, practiced, and were rewarded for. They are not character flaws -- they are narratives that need conscious examination.

> "One of the biggest jobs we have as leaders is to help other people develop the competency, the confidence, and the courage to step up to difficult tasks." -- Dr. Robin Rose

**The central insight:** Effective leadership requires three legs: theoretical knowledge, skills, and self-awareness. The third leg is the most commonly missing. Smart, well-educated leaders with good skills but no awareness of how they impact others are a recognized failure pattern.

---

## 2. The Three-Legged Leadership Stool

| Leg | Description | Failure Mode |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| **Knowledge** | Theoretical understanding of leadership, management, strategy | "I know what to do but can't execute" |
| **Skills** | Active listening, delegation, communication, facilitation | "I can execute but don't know the theory" |
| **Self-awareness** | Understanding your impact on others, recognizing your narratives | "I'm skilled and knowledgeable but people avoid me" |

The self-awareness leg is literally a somatic/psychological practice, not an intellectual exercise. Dr. Rose's 4-question reframing method:

1. What am I telling myself about this situation?
2. How is this narrative serving me?
3. How is it serving others?
4. What's a different way of thinking about this that serves me and others better?

## 3. Six Leadership Myths Examined

### Myth 1: "I Need to Have All the Answers"

Most destructive in complex situations. The more complex the challenge, the more you need 10-12 people with different perspectives and skills. Hoarding the answer-giving role keeps people on the periphery and stunts organizational capacity.

### Myth 2: "If I Want It Done Right, I Gotta Do It Myself"

Anxiety-driven. Leaders who feel they must do everything themselves become more controlling as anxiety about outcomes increases. The corrective question: "Is this the best use of my time right now, and who around me is looking for an opportunity to step up?"

### Myth 3: "I Need to Make Everyone Happy"

Must be reframed to "be fair." Attempting to satisfy all stakeholders simultaneously produces unsustainable stress. Fairness: listen to all perspectives, then decide.

### Myth 4: "Showing Vulnerability Is Weakness"

Vulnerability is a trust-building mechanism, not a weakness signal. Referencing Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team: the absence of trust stems directly from leaders' inability to convey vulnerability. Leaders who never show emotion message "emotion is not allowed here."

### Myth 5: "Leaders Must Be Extroverts"

Introverts are the "secret weapon" of organizations. Culture is biased toward extroverts who process externally. Practical mitigations:
- Send agendas in advance
- Ask people to write before speaking
- Explicitly invite silent voices
- Use structured silence in meetings

### Myth 6: "Consensus Is Always Better"

Dr. Rose's Quaker camp experience: pure consensus processes create decision paralysis. "Competencies are different" across team members. The leader gathers input, then decides.

## 4. The Leader-as-Coach Model

The shift from directive leadership to coaching is not "doing less" but "doing different":

- Asking instead of telling
- Creating space instead of filling it
- Building ownership instead of absorbing responsibility
- Leading with curiosity instead of certainty

**Coach-like replacements:**
- Instead of "Here's what I'd do" --> "What options are you considering?"
- Instead of "Let me handle this" --> "What support do you need to handle this?"
- Instead of "We need to decide now" --> "What would we need to know to decide well?"

> "Behavior serves a purpose. Always. Sometimes not such a great purpose." -- Dr. Robin Rose

## 5. Technology Leadership in the AI Era

Dr. Banu Ozkazanc-Pan (Barrett Hazelton Professor of the Practice of Engineering, Brown School of Engineering; top 2% of scientists globally) identifies AI strategy as a leadership problem, not a technical problem.

**Three common innovation strategy failures:**

1. **Shiny object syndrome:** Chasing every tool without strategy, mistaking activity for progress
2. **Siloed AI adoption:** Departmental experiments without shared learnings
3. **Misaligned incentives:** Measuring AI usage rather than productive outcomes (Meta is the canonical example)

### The AI Maturity Ladder

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Automate** | Low-value repetitive tasks |
| **Accelerate** | Iterate and fail fast |
| **Augment** | Prediction and insight at scale |
| **Transform** | Reimagine business models and infrastructure |

## 6. The 0% Productivity Problem

> "Of all the companies in the last 18 months that have invested heavily in AI, zero percent -- zero -- have shown any kind of productivity gain." -- Peter Chomowicz, citing MIT Sloan research

Dr. Ozkazanc-Pan reframes the 0% finding not as an indictment of AI but as a leadership diagnosis:

- **"That's a leadership failure"** -- senior leaders wave their hand and say "just use AI" without resourcing or strategy
- **The rework problem:** Auditing AI output for errors creates MORE work, not less
- **Work intensification:** Rather than replacing labor, unstrategic AI adoption causes longer hours
- **Tool-workflow mismatch:** Tools adopted without understanding organizational workflows become a "cognitive burden"

Peter Chomowicz's analogy: the current AI era is like the dot-com Wild West -- massive promise, massive fraud, massive waste, but the infrastructure that emerges on the other side transforms everything.

## 7. The Tech Leadership Stack

| Layer | Description | Required Skills |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Technical Fluency** | Foundation | Understanding the tools, not necessarily building them |
| **Strategic Thinking** | Systems, trade-offs, long-term vision | Reading widely: geopolitical, environmental, financial |
| **People Leadership** | Culture-building, influence up/down/across | Change management, coaching, facilitation |
| **Ethical Judgment** | Responsible AI, inclusive design, trust-building | Humanities-informed critical thinking |

> "Technologies change, but strategic leadership and good leadership doesn't." -- Dr. Banu Ozkazanc-Pan

**Three pillars of technology-agnostic resilience:**
1. **Strategic foresight:** Read widely -- geopolitical, environmental, financial
2. **Entrepreneurial thinking:** Perpetually scan for unmet needs and opportunities
3. **Continuous leadership development:** Always seeking feedback, always growing

## 10. DIY Sessions

### Session 1: Leadership Myth Audit (Individual, 90 min)

1. **Inventory (20 min):** Write down 5 leadership beliefs you operate from daily
2. **4-Question Diagnostic (30 min):** For each belief, answer Rose's four reframing questions
3. **Impact Mapping (20 min):** For your top 2 most entrenched myths, draw the downstream effects on your team
4. **Coach-Like Replacement (20 min):** For each myth, write the question you should be asking instead of the answer you've been giving

### Session 2: AI Strategy Alignment Assessment (Team, 120 min)

1. **Maturity Placement (20 min):** Each team member independently rates where the organization is TODAY on the automate-accelerate-augment-transform ladder
2. **Failure Mode Check (25 min):** Score 1-5 on each of the three failure modes
3. **Incentive Audit (25 min):** Apply the Goodhart's Law test to each AI metric
4. **Leadership Stack Gap Analysis (25 min):** Self-assess weakest layer in the four-layer stack
5. **Rework Estimation (25 min):** For one AI initiative, estimate hours spent auditing vs. hours saved
