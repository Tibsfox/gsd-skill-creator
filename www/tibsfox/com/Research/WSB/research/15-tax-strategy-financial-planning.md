## 1. The Employee-to-Owner Tax Shift

As CPA Navi Mirage explains: when you are an employee, your employer handles tax withholding automatically. Federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and state taxes are all deducted before you see your paycheck. The employer matches the Social Security and Medicare portions and sends everything to the government on your behalf.

When you become a business owner, **no one does this for you.**

| As Employee | As Business Owner |
|------------|------------------|
| Taxes withheld automatically | Must estimate and pay yourself |
| Employer pays half of FICA | You pay both halves of self-employment tax |
| W-2 issued at year end | Responsible for own record-keeping |
| Penalties rare (employer handles) | Penalties for underpayment are your problem |
| Single filing deadline | Quarterly payment deadlines throughout the year |

This is the fundamental shift that catches new business owners off guard. The CPA content from Life Accounting reinforces this: "9 out of 10 small businesses that fail, fail due to financial mismanagement of their company." Tax compliance is a major component of that financial management.

## 2. Entity-Specific Taxation

How your business is structured determines how you are taxed. Each entity type has distinct tax treatment, filing requirements, and strategic implications.

### Sole Proprietorship

The simplest structure — no separate legal entity.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Tax form** | Schedule C (attached to personal Form 1040) |
| **Income tax** | All net profit taxed at personal income tax rates |
| **Self-employment tax** | Full 15.3% on net profit (up to Social Security cap) |
| **Filing deadline** | April 15 (with personal return) |
| **Liability** | Unlimited personal liability |

### LLC (Single-Member)

By default, taxed identically to a sole proprietorship. The LLC provides liability protection without changing tax treatment.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Default tax** | Same as sole proprietorship (Schedule C) |
| **Election options** | Can elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation |
| **Self-employment tax** | Full 15.3% unless S-Corp election is made |
| **Advantage** | Liability shield + tax flexibility |
| **Filing** | Schedule C (default) or Form 1120-S (if S-Corp elected) |

### S Corporation

A pass-through entity with a critical tax planning feature: the salary/distribution split.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Tax form** | Form 1120-S (corporate return) + K-1 to shareholders |
| **Income tax** | All net income passes through to personal return |
| **Payroll tax** | Only on "reasonable" salary — NOT on distributions |
| **Filing deadline** | March 15 (one month before personal return) |
| **Key requirement** | Must pay owner a "reasonable salary" |

### Partnership

Multi-owner pass-through entity.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Tax form** | Form 1065 (partnership return) + K-1 to each partner |
| **Income tax** | Each partner's share passes through to personal return |
| **Self-employment tax** | Active partners pay SE tax on their share |
| **Filing deadline** | March 15 |
| **Key feature** | Flexible profit/loss allocation (doesn't have to match ownership %) |

### C Corporation

Separate taxable entity — the only structure with "double taxation."

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Tax form** | Form 1120 |
| **Corporate tax rate** | 21% flat federal rate |
| **Dividends** | Taxed again at shareholder level (0%, 15%, or 20%) |
| **Filing deadline** | April 15 |
| **When to use** | Retained earnings strategy, venture-backed companies, public companies |

## 3. Quarterly Estimated Payments

Business owners must make estimated tax payments four times per year. The IRS expects you to "pay as you go" — waiting until April 15 triggers underpayment penalties.

### Federal Schedule

| Quarter | Period Covered | Payment Due |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Q1 | January 1 - March 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | April 1 - May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | June 1 - August 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | September 1 - December 31 | January 15 (next year) |

**Form:** 1040-ES (individuals) or corporate equivalent

### Safe Harbor Rules

To avoid underpayment penalties, you must pay at least:

| Method | Requirement |
|--------|-------------|
| **Current year** | 90% of current year's total tax liability |
| **Prior year** | 100% of prior year's total tax liability (110% if AGI > $150K) |

The prior-year method is the "safe harbor" — even if your income doubles, paying 100% (or 110%) of last year's tax means no penalties.

### State Payments

Most states with income tax require separate quarterly estimated payments on their own schedules. Washington State has no personal income tax, but businesses still owe B&O tax quarterly.

## 4. Self-Employment Tax

Self-employment (SE) tax is the self-employed person's equivalent of FICA (Social Security + Medicare).

### Rate Structure

| Component | Rate | Wage Base (2026) |
|-----------|------|-----------------|
| Social Security | 12.4% | First ~$168,600 |
| Medicare | 2.9% | All net earnings |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% | Over $200K (single) / $250K (married) |
| **Total** | **15.3%** | (up to SS cap) |

As an employee, you pay half (7.65%) and your employer pays the other half. As self-employed, you pay **both halves** — the full 15.3%.

### The Deduction

You can deduct 50% of self-employment tax on your personal return (the "employer equivalent" portion). This reduces your adjusted gross income but not your self-employment tax itself.

### Example Calculation

```
Net business income:             $150,000
SE tax base (92.35%):            $138,525
Social Security (12.4%):         $17,177
Medicare (2.9%):                 $4,017
Total SE Tax:                    $21,194
Deductible portion (50%):       ($10,597)
```

This $21,194 is **in addition to** federal and state income tax on the same $150,000.

## 5. The S-Corp Salary Strategy

The single most impactful tax planning move for profitable small businesses: electing S-Corp taxation to split income between salary and distributions.

### How It Works

As a sole proprietor or single-member LLC, all net profit is subject to self-employment tax (15.3%).

As an S-Corp, you split income into:
1. **Salary** — subject to payroll tax (15.3% combined employer + employee)
2. **Distributions** — NOT subject to payroll tax (only income tax)

### Example: $150K Net Income

| | Sole Prop | S-Corp ($80K salary) |
|---|----------|---------------------|
| **Payroll/SE tax base** | $150,000 | $80,000 |
| **SE/Payroll tax** | $21,194 | $12,240 |
| **Tax savings** | — | **$8,954** |

The $70,000 distribution is subject to income tax but NOT the 15.3% self-employment tax.

### The "Reasonable Salary" Requirement

The IRS requires S-Corp owner-employees to pay themselves a "reasonable salary" before taking distributions. This is the primary compliance risk of the S-Corp strategy.

**Factors the IRS considers:**
- Training, education, and experience
- Duties and responsibilities
- Time devoted to the business
- Comparable salaries in the industry
- What similar businesses pay for similar services

**General guidance:** The salary should be comparable to what you would pay someone else to do the same work. Setting it too low invites audit risk.

### When S-Corp Makes Sense

| Factor | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Net business income | Generally > $50K-$80K |
| Administrative cost | $1,500-$5,000/year for payroll and additional tax filings |
| Stability | Income should be fairly consistent |
| Break-even | Tax savings must exceed added administrative costs |

## 6. Business Deductions

Common deductions that reduce taxable business income:

### Ordinary and Necessary Expenses

| Category | Examples | Form |
|----------|----------|------|
| **Home office** | Dedicated space, proportional utilities | Form 8829 or simplified method |
| **Vehicle** | Business mileage or actual expenses | Standard mileage rate ($0.70/mile 2026 est.) |
| **Equipment** | Computers, tools, furniture | Section 179 or depreciation |
| **Professional services** | Legal, accounting, consulting | Schedule C line 17 |
| **Insurance** | Business liability, health (self-employed) | Various |
| **Education** | Courses, conferences, books related to business | Schedule C line 27 |
| **Software/subscriptions** | SaaS tools, cloud hosting | Schedule C line 18 |

### Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation

| Method | What It Does | Limit (2026 est.) |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Section 179** | Immediate full deduction of equipment cost | ~$1,220,000 |
| **Bonus depreciation** | Percentage of cost deducted in year 1 | 40% (phasing down from 100%) |
| **Standard depreciation** | Spread cost over useful life | MACRS schedules |

### Retirement Contributions

Self-employed individuals have access to powerful retirement deductions:

| Plan | Max Contribution (2026 est.) | Notes |
|------|------------------------------|-------|
| **SEP-IRA** | ~$69,000 or 25% of comp | Simple to administer |
| **Solo 401(k)** | ~$69,000 (employee + employer) | Allows Roth contributions |
| **SIMPLE IRA** | ~$16,500 + match | For businesses with employees |

## 7. Nonprofit Financial Management

The NCAA (National Center for Academic Accounting) research network, presented in Milwaukee, addresses a fundamentally different financial model.

### How Nonprofits Differ

| Dimension | For-Profit | Nonprofit |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | Generate shareholder returns | Advance social mission |
| **Owners** | Shareholders | No owners (public benefit) |
| **Revenue** | Sales of goods/services | Grants, donations, endowments, fees |
| **Equity** | Shareholders' equity | Net assets |
| **Tax status** | Taxable | Tax-exempt (501(c)(3) and others) |
| **Financial reporting** | SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q) | Form 990 (public record) |
| **Accountability** | To shareholders and market | To donors, boards, and public |

### Net Asset Classification

Instead of shareholders' equity, nonprofits report **net assets** in two categories (post-ASU 2016-14):

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Without donor restrictions** | Available for any organizational purpose | Unrestricted donations, general revenue |
| **With donor restrictions** | Must be used for specific purpose or time period | Endowments, program-specific grants |

### Revenue Recognition

Nonprofit revenue recognition follows different standards:

| Revenue Type | Standard | Key Issue |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Exchange transactions** | FASB ASC 606 | Same as for-profit |
| **Contributions** | FASB ASC 958 | Conditional vs. unconditional promises |
| **Government grants** | ASC 958-605 | Barrier vs. right-of-return model |

### Functional Expense Reporting

Nonprofits must allocate expenses across three functional categories:

| Function | What It Includes | Why It Matters |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Program services** | Direct mission delivery | Donors want to see high program ratio |
| **Management & general** | Administration, governance | Overhead — donors scrutinize this |
| **Fundraising** | Donor solicitation, events | Cost of raising the next dollar |

**The program ratio** (program expenses / total expenses) is one of the most-watched nonprofit metrics. Donors and rating agencies (Charity Navigator, GuideStar) use it as a quality signal, though it can be misleading if manipulated through creative allocation.

### Form 990

The annual information return filed by tax-exempt organizations. Unlike for-profit tax returns, the 990 is a **public document** — anyone can access it.

Key sections:
- **Part I:** Summary (revenue, expenses, net assets)
- **Part VII:** Compensation of officers, directors, key employees
- **Part VIII:** Statement of Revenue (detailed sources)
- **Part IX:** Statement of Functional Expenses
- **Schedule A:** Public Charity Status
- **Schedule B:** Contributors (partially confidential)

## 8. Small Business Financial Health

The CPA content from Life Accounting (Sherman) provides the practitioner perspective on why financial management is existential for small businesses.

### The 9/10 Statistic

> "9 out of 10 small businesses that fail, fail due to financial mismanagement of their company."

This is not about bad products or weak markets — it's about owners who don't track their numbers.

### Three Failure Modes

| Mode | What Happens | How to Prevent |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Failure to make decisions** | No financial data → flying blind | Review statements monthly |
| **Failure to meet obligations** | Cash flow mismanagement → missed payments | Cash flow forecasting |
| **Failure to pay taxes** | Missed estimates → penalties and compounding | Quarterly estimated payments |

### Monthly Financial Review Checklist

1. **Income statement** — Are revenues growing? Are margins holding?
2. **Balance sheet** — Is cash adequate? Are receivables growing faster than revenue?
3. **Cash flow** — Is the business generating positive operating cash flow?
4. **Tax reserve** — Is enough set aside for quarterly estimated payments?
5. **Accounts receivable aging** — Any invoices past 60 days?
6. **Accounts payable** — Any overdue vendor payments?

### Break-Even Analysis

Every business owner should know their break-even point:

**Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs / (1 - Variable Cost Ratio)**

Or in units:

**Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs / (Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit)**

## 9. Washington State Considerations

Washington presents a unique tax environment relevant to WSB (Washington Small Business):

### No State Income Tax

Washington has no personal or corporate income tax. This means:
- No state estimated income tax payments
- No state income tax withholding for employees
- S-Corp salary strategy savings are purely federal + SE tax
- Pass-through income is not taxed at the state level

### B&O Tax (Business & Occupation)

Washington's primary business tax is the B&O tax — a gross receipts tax:

| Classification | Rate |
|---------------|------|
| Retailing | 0.471% |
| Wholesaling | 0.484% |
| Manufacturing | 0.484% |
| Service & other | 1.5% |

**Critical distinction:** B&O is on **gross revenue**, not profit. A business with $1M revenue and $50K profit still pays B&O on the full $1M.

### Sales Tax

Washington has a combined state + local sales tax:
- State rate: 6.5%
- Local rates vary (Everett: approximately 10.5% combined)
- Applies to retail sales of tangible goods and some services
- B2B manufacturing components generally exempt

## 11. DIY Sessions

### Session 1: Entity Tax Comparison (1.5 hours)
**Materials:** IRS tax brackets, SE tax rates, spreadsheet
**Procedure:**
1. Set up a hypothetical business: $150K net income, single filer, no other income
2. Calculate total tax (federal income + SE/payroll) as sole proprietor
3. Recalculate as S-Corp with $80K salary + $70K distribution
4. Recalculate as C-Corp with $80K salary + retained earnings
5. Add the administrative cost of each structure ($0 sole prop, $3K S-Corp, $5K C-Corp)
6. Create a table comparing total after-tax income for each entity
7. At what income level does S-Corp break even with sole prop (accounting for admin costs)?

### Session 2: Quarterly Estimated Payment Planning (1 hour)
**Materials:** Prior year tax return (or hypothetical), Form 1040-ES worksheet
**Procedure:**
1. Start with prior year total tax liability
2. Calculate the safe harbor amount (100% or 110% of prior year)
3. Divide into four quarterly payments
4. Set up payment reminders for all four deadlines
5. Model: what happens if income increases 50% mid-year? Recalculate using annualized income method

### Session 3: Nonprofit Financial Analysis (2 hours)
**Materials:** Form 990 from a nonprofit you support (available at GuideStar/ProPublica)
**Procedure:**
1. Download the most recent 990
2. Extract: total revenue, total expenses, net assets
3. Calculate program ratio (program expenses / total expenses)
4. Review Part VII — what are the top 5 compensation figures?
5. Compare restricted vs. unrestricted net assets — is the organization building reserves?
6. Look up the organization on Charity Navigator — does your analysis match their rating?

### Session 4: Cash Flow Forecasting (2 hours)
**Materials:** Spreadsheet, hypothetical or real business data
**Procedure:**
1. Project monthly revenue for 12 months (use seasonal patterns if applicable)
2. Estimate monthly fixed costs (rent, insurance, subscriptions, salary)
3. Estimate variable costs as percentage of revenue
4. Calculate monthly net cash flow
5. Add quarterly estimated tax payments to the cash outflow
6. Identify months where cash balance goes negative
7. Develop strategy: credit line, invoice timing, expense reduction, or revenue acceleration

## 12. Cross-Cluster Connections

### Tax Strategy --> Infrastructure
Infrastructure investment decisions are heavily influenced by tax incentives: Section 179 for equipment, bonus depreciation for capital improvements, Opportunity Zone deferrals for real estate development, and energy investment tax credits for renewable installations. The financial planning module from this research connects directly to infrastructure project economics.

### Tax Strategy --> Creative
Creative businesses (content production, software development, digital media) face unique tax considerations: IP amortization under Section 197, R&D tax credits under Section 41, domestic production deductions, and the QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock) exclusion that can eliminate up to $10M in capital gains tax on qualifying startup exits.

### Tax Strategy --> Cooperative Economics
The nonprofit financial management content connects directly to the cooperative economics vision. Nonprofit accounting (FASB ASC 958), functional expense reporting, and donor-restricted fund tracking provide the financial infrastructure for mission-driven organizations. The Fox Companies vision of cooperative networks and habitat restoration would operate within this financial framework.

### Tax Strategy --> Trust System
Quarterly tax compliance is a **deterministic hook system** — trigger conditions (end of quarter), deadline enforcement (specific dates), penalty for missed execution (IRS underpayment penalties), and safe harbor mechanisms (prior-year method). The trust system's hook scheduling infrastructure maps directly to tax compliance calendaring.
