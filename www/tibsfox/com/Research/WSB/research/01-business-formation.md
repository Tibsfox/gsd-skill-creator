## 1. The Dependency Graph of Business Formation

The formation process is not a linear checklist. It is a **dependency graph** where each step enables the next. Understanding the graph prevents the most common startup mistake: starting in the wrong place.

```
[Choose Entity Type]
        ↓
[File with Secretary of State]
        ↓
[Receive UBI Number] ← This is the key
        ↓
   ┌────┴────────────────────┐
   ↓                         ↓
[DOR Business License]    [Open Bank Account]
[ESD Account]             [Hire Employees]
[L&I Account]             [Sign Contracts]
[City/County License]
        ↓
[Operate legally in Washington]
```

The UBI number issued by the Secretary of State is the **state-level business identifier** used across all agencies. Starting at the Department of Revenue, Employment Security, or any other agency before filing with SOS generates duplicate or orphaned records. The Secretary of State filing is the root node. Everything else depends on it.

## 2. Entity Types: The Decision Matrix

Washington recognizes five major business structures for small businesses:

| Structure | Liability Protection | Taxation | Formation | Governance |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Sole Proprietorship** | None — unlimited personal liability | Pass-through (Schedule C) | No state filing | None required |
| **LLC** | Limited to entity assets | Pass-through (default) or corporate election | $180 Certificate of Formation | Operating Agreement |
| **S Corporation** | Limited to entity assets | Pass-through; reasonable salary required | $180 Articles + IRS S-election | Bylaws; Board of Directors |
| **C Corporation** | Limited to entity assets | Double taxation (corporate + dividends) | $180 Articles of Incorporation | Bylaws; Board; Officers |
| **LP / LLP** | Varies by partner type | Pass-through | $180 Certificate of LP | Partnership Agreement |

**Source:** Washington Secretary of State, Corporations and Charities Division; Business.WA.gov Small Business Guide.

## 3. Sole Proprietorship

A sole proprietorship is the default form of business — no state filing is required to operate as a sole proprietor. The individual and the business are the same legal entity.

### 3.1 Advantages

- No formation cost or state filing
- Simplest tax treatment (Schedule C on personal return)
- No ongoing corporate formalities (no board meetings, no minutes)

### 3.2 Disadvantages

- **Unlimited personal liability** — all business debts and judgments can be collected from personal assets
- Cannot issue stock or bring in equity partners
- May be harder to obtain business credit or contracts

### 3.3 When a Business License Is Still Required

A sole proprietor still needs a Washington State Business License if the business:
- Grosses $12,000 or more per year
- Collects sales tax
- Operates under a trade name different from the owner's legal name
- Plans to hire employees within 90 days
- Is required to pay taxes or fees to the Department of Revenue

## 4. Limited Liability Company (LLC)

The LLC is the most popular entity type for Washington small businesses. It combines liability protection with pass-through taxation and minimal formality requirements.

### 4.1 Liability Protection

An LLC limits member liability to the **amount invested in the company** — personal assets are protected from business debts and judgments, provided:
- The LLC is properly formed and maintained
- There is no personal guarantee on business debt
- There is no "piercing the corporate veil" (commingling funds, ignoring formalities)

### 4.2 Taxation Options

By default, a single-member LLC is taxed as a sole proprietorship (Schedule C). A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership (Form 1065). An LLC may elect to be taxed as an S corporation or C corporation if that is advantageous.

### 4.3 Formation

- File a **Certificate of Formation** with the Washington Secretary of State
- Fee: **$180** (online filing)
- Processing: Immediate for online filings
- Annual renewal: **$60/year** to maintain active status

### 4.4 Operating Agreement

An Operating Agreement is the LLC's governing document. It specifies:
- Member ownership percentages and capital contributions
- Management structure (member-managed vs. manager-managed)
- Profit and loss allocation
- Voting rights and decision procedures
- Buy-sell and dissolution provisions

Washington does not require an Operating Agreement to be filed publicly, but it is critical for internal governance and protecting the liability shield.

## 5. S Corporation

The S corporation combines limited liability with pass-through taxation, but adds the requirement to pay owners a **reasonable salary** — unlike LLCs where distributions are not subject to payroll tax.

### 5.1 The Reasonable Salary Requirement

S corporation shareholders who work in the business must receive a reasonable salary — approximately what the business would pay a non-owner employee in the same role. The IRS actively audits S corporations that pay minimal salary and large distributions. Failure to pay reasonable salary results in reclassification of distributions as wages, triggering back payroll taxes, penalties, and interest.

### 5.2 Formation Steps

1. File **Articles of Incorporation** with the Washington Secretary of State ($180)
2. File **IRS Form 2553** to elect S corporation tax status within 75 days of incorporation (or by March 15 for the following year)
3. Issue stock certificates and record in the shareholder ledger
4. Adopt Bylaws

### 5.3 S Corporation Restrictions

- Maximum **100 shareholders**
- Only one class of stock
- Shareholders must be US citizens or permanent residents
- Cannot be owned by other corporations or partnerships

## 6. C Corporation

The C corporation is the default corporate structure — used for businesses seeking venture capital, planning to go public, or requiring complex ownership structures. Most small businesses do not benefit from C corporation taxation.

### 6.1 Double Taxation

C corporations pay **federal corporate income tax** on profits, and shareholders pay **individual income tax** on dividends. This double taxation is the primary disadvantage for small business owners. However, retained earnings stay in the corporation at the corporate rate, which may be advantageous for reinvestment.

### 6.2 When a C Corporation Makes Sense

- Planning for venture capital or outside investment
- Planning to go public (IPO)
- Employee stock option plans (ESOPs and ISOs require C corp structure)
- International ownership or multiple classes of stock needed

## 7. Partnership and Limited Partnership

### 7.1 General Partnership

Two or more people operating a business together, without formal registration, form a **general partnership** by default. All partners share unlimited personal liability. A written partnership agreement is strongly recommended but not legally required.

### 7.2 Limited Partnership (LP)

A Limited Partnership has at least one general partner (unlimited liability, management authority) and one or more limited partners (liability limited to investment, no management role). Formation requires filing a **Certificate of Limited Partnership** ($180).

### 7.3 Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

An LLP provides all partners with liability protection similar to an LLC. LLPs are most commonly used by professional service firms (law, accounting, architecture) where state licensing boards require partnership structures.

## 8. The Formation Procedure

For entities requiring state registration (LLC, corporation, LP, LLP):

**Step 1: Verify business name availability**
Search the SOS business entity database at [https://www.sos.wa.gov/corps/](https://www.sos.wa.gov/corps/) to confirm your desired name is available. Names must be distinguishable from existing registrations.

**Step 2: Designate a registered agent**
A registered agent is a Washington-based person or entity who will receive official service of process (lawsuits, tax notices, Board correspondence). Must have a physical street address in Washington (not a P.O. Box). Owners can serve as their own registered agent.

**Step 3: File the formation document**
- LLC: Certificate of Formation (WAC Form LLC-1)
- Corporation: Articles of Incorporation
- Fee: $180 online; $200 by mail

**Step 4: Receive UBI number**
The SOS issues a **Unified Business Identifier (UBI)** upon successful filing. This is the state-level business identification number used across all agencies.

**Step 5: Create governance documents**
- LLC: Operating Agreement
- Corporation: Bylaws
- These are internal documents and are not filed with the SOS

**Step 6: Apply for federal EIN**
File IRS Form SS-4 online at IRS.gov (free, immediate for online applications).

## 9. The UBI Number

The Unified Business Identifier (UBI) is Washington's nine-digit business identification number. It is assigned by the Secretary of State upon filing and is used by:
- The Department of Revenue (B&O tax accounts, sales tax permits)
- The Employment Security Department (unemployment insurance accounts)
- Labor & Industries (workers' compensation accounts)
- The Department of Licensing (professional licenses)
- City and county licensing portals

The UBI is the **single source of identity** for a Washington business across all state agencies. Starting anywhere other than the SOS means you do not yet have a UBI and cannot properly complete any other registration.

---

## 10. Federal EIN Application

An **Employer Identification Number (EIN)** is the federal tax identification number issued by the IRS. It is required for:
- Hiring employees
- Opening business bank accounts
- Filing business tax returns
- Applying for business credit
- Any entity other than a sole proprietorship (which may use the owner's SSN)

**Application:** Online at IRS.gov, Form SS-4. Free. Immediate assignment for US applicants. No need to engage a paid registered agent service for this — it is a free, 5-minute online process.

## 11. Governance Documents

### 11.1 LLC Operating Agreement

Critical provisions:
- **Capital table:** Who owns what percentage, how much each member contributed
- **Management:** Member-managed (all owners vote) or manager-managed (designated manager runs day-to-day)
- **Voting thresholds:** Routine decisions vs. major decisions (new members, dissolution, sale)
- **Distributions:** When and how profits are distributed
- **Buy-sell:** What happens when a member wants out, dies, or becomes incapacitated

### 11.2 Corporate Bylaws

Critical provisions:
- **Board of Directors:** Composition, election, terms, removal procedures
- **Officers:** CEO, CFO, Secretary roles and appointment process
- **Shareholder meetings:** Annual meeting requirements, notice, quorum
- **Indemnification:** Director and officer protection from personal liability
- **Amendments:** How bylaws may be changed

## 12. The SOS-First Rule

The most important operational advice from formation practitioners:

> **Start with the Secretary of State. Not the Department of Revenue. Not the bank. Not the city.**

Beginning at DOR without a SOS filing creates an account with no UBI, requiring manual correction. Beginning with a bank requires a UBI that doesn't exist yet. Beginning with the city requires a state license that requires a UBI that requires SOS filing.

The correct sequence is:
1. SOS → UBI
2. DOR / ESD / L&I (can be done simultaneously via Business Licensing Service)
3. City/County endorsements
4. Bank account (EIN + UBI + state license)

**Source:** Business.WA.gov Checklist for Opening a Business; WA Secretary of State online filing portal.
