## 1. The Four Pillars of Intellectual Property

Intellectual property is an intangible asset — a piece of property like real estate or equipment, carried on the balance sheet and capable of generating economic value. There are exactly four types of IP protection in U.S. law, and each serves a fundamentally different purpose:

| Type | Protects | Duration | Registration | Cost to Obtain |
|------|----------|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Copyright | Original creative works | Life + 70 years | Optional (automatic on creation) | $35-$85 registration |
| Trademark | Brand identity (source identifiers) | Potentially infinite | Optional (common law) / Recommended (federal) | $250-$350/class + attorney |
| Trade Secret | Confidential business information | Indefinite (while secret) | None (self-enforced) | Administrative costs |
| Patent | Novel inventions and designs | 20 years (utility) / 15 years (design) | Required (USPTO) | $10K-$30K+ (utility) |

The foundational insight is that these four pillars are **complementary, not alternative**. A single product can simultaneously be protected by copyright (its documentation and source code), trademark (its name and logo), trade secret (its manufacturing process), and patent (its novel algorithm). The strategic question is always: which combination provides the best protection for this specific asset?

## 2. Copyright

Copyright is a form of protection provided to the authors or creators of original works of authorship. The key word is "original" — the work must show some minimal degree of creativity beyond mere facts or mechanical reproduction.

**Automatic Protection.** A copyright exists under common law as soon as the work is created and fixed in a tangible medium of expression. No filing, no registration, no notice symbol required. The moment you paint a painting, write a document, record a song, or commit code to a file, the copyright exists. This is the simplest form of IP protection and the one most commonly overlooked.

**What Copyright Covers:**
- Literary works (including software source code)
- Musical works and sound recordings
- Dramatic works
- Pictorial, graphic, and sculptural works
- Motion pictures and audiovisual works
- Architectural works
- Compilations and derivative works

**What Copyright Does NOT Cover:**
- Ideas, concepts, principles, discoveries
- Facts and data
- Procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation
- Titles, names, short phrases, slogans (these may be trademarks)
- Works of the U.S. federal government

**The Bundle of Rights.** Copyright grants the holder six exclusive rights: reproduction, distribution, public performance, public display, creation of derivative works, and digital audio transmission. Collectively, these are "the right to copy" — the etymological core of the word.

**Federal Registration.** While not required for copyright to exist, registration with the U.S. Copyright Office provides significant advantages:
- Establishes a public record of the copyright claim
- Is required before filing a federal infringement lawsuit (since *Fourth Estate Public Benefit Corp. v. Wall-Street.com* (2019))
- Enables statutory damages ($750-$30,000 per work; up to $150,000 for willful infringement) and attorney's fees
- Creates a presumption of validity if registered within 5 years of publication

**Duration.** For works created after January 1, 1978: life of the author plus 70 years. For works made for hire, anonymous works, and pseudonymous works: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter.

**Software Copyright.** Source code is copyrightable as a literary work. Object code and the structure/sequence/organization of a program may also be protected. However, copyright does NOT protect the underlying functionality — only the particular expression of that functionality. This is why patents are sometimes necessary for software innovations where the algorithm itself (not just its expression) is the valuable asset.

## 3. Trademark

A trademark is any word, symbol, design, device, sound, color, scent, or any combination thereof that identifies the source of goods or services and distinguishes them from those of others. The essential function of a trademark is **source identification** — when a consumer sees the mark, they know who made the product.

**Common Law vs. Federal Registration.** The United States uses a first-to-use system. Trademark rights arise from actual use in commerce, not from registration. Common law rights are limited to the geographic area of use. Federal registration extends protection nationwide and provides:
- Constructive notice to all potential infringers
- Presumption of validity and ownership
- Right to use the ® symbol
- Ability to record with U.S. Customs (blocking infringing imports)
- Basis for international registration (Madrid Protocol)
- Listing in the USPTO database (TESS)

**What Can Be Trademarked:**
- Word marks: APPLE, NIKE, GOOGLE
- Design marks: Nike swoosh, Apple logo, McDonald's golden arches
- Sound marks: NBC three-note chime, MGM lion roar, Intel bong
- Color marks: Tiffany blue (Pantone 1837), UPS brown, T-Mobile magenta
- Scent marks: Play-Doh compound scent (registered 2017)
- Trade dress: distinctive product packaging, restaurant decor, website design (if source-identifying)

**The Likelihood of Confusion Test.** Trademark infringement turns on whether consumers are likely to be confused about the source of goods or services. Courts apply multi-factor tests (e.g., the *Sleekcraft* factors in the Ninth Circuit): similarity of marks, relatedness of goods, strength of the mark, evidence of actual confusion, marketing channels, consumer sophistication, intent of the alleged infringer, and likelihood of expansion.

**Maintenance.** Federal trademarks require affirmative maintenance:
- Section 8 Declaration (between years 5-6 after registration): sworn statement of continued use with specimen
- Section 9 Renewal (every 10 years): combined with Section 8
- Section 15 Declaration (after 5 years of continuous use post-registration): achieves "incontestability" — significantly limits available defenses in infringement actions
- Failure to maintain results in cancellation

## 4. Trade Secret

A trade secret is any information that (1) derives independent economic value from not being generally known to or readily ascertainable by others, AND (2) is the subject of reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy. Both elements are required.

**Legal Framework:**
- Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA): adopted in some form by 48 states + DC
- Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA, 2016): provides a federal cause of action for trade secret misappropriation
- Economic Espionage Act (1996): criminalizes trade secret theft

**Examples of Trade Secrets:**
- Formulas (Coca-Cola, WD-40, KFC's "Original Recipe")
- Algorithms (Google PageRank, trading algorithms)
- Customer lists and pricing data
- Manufacturing processes and techniques
- Software architecture and design decisions
- Business strategies and financial projections

**"Reasonable Efforts" Required:**
The requirement to take "reasonable efforts" to maintain secrecy is legally enforced. If a company fails to protect its secrets, courts will refuse to enforce trade secret claims. Reasonable efforts include:
- Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) with employees, contractors, and partners
- Access controls (physical and digital — need-to-know basis)
- Employee training on confidentiality obligations
- Exit interviews and return-of-materials procedures
- Marking documents as confidential
- Encrypting sensitive data
- Restricting physical access to sensitive areas

**The Permanent Loss Principle.** Unlike patents, trademarks, and copyrights — which have defined terms — trade secrets can theoretically last forever. But the moment a trade secret is publicly disclosed (whether by the owner, a departing employee, reverse engineering, or independent discovery), the protection is permanently and irrevocably lost. There is no mechanism to restore trade secret status once secrecy is broken.

**Trade Secrets vs. Patents — The Strategic Tradeoff:**

| Factor | Trade Secret | Patent |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Duration | Indefinite (while secret) | 20 years (utility) |
| Disclosure | Must remain secret | Published to the world |
| Independent discovery defense | Yes — anyone who independently discovers the same thing may use it freely | No — patent blocks all others regardless of how they arrived at the invention |
| Cost | Administrative | $10K-$30K+ |
| Reverse engineering defense | Yes — if someone reverse-engineers your product, they can use what they learn | No — patent blocks regardless |
| Strength against competitors | Weaker (can be legally circumvented) | Stronger (legal monopoly) |

## 5. Patent

A patent is a grant from the federal government (through the United States Patent and Trademark Office) giving the patent holder the right to exclude others from making, using, selling, offering to sell, or importing the claimed invention for a limited period.

**Three Types of Patents:**

1. **Utility Patents** (20 years from filing): Protect new and useful processes, machines, articles of manufacture, compositions of matter, or improvements thereof. This is the most common type (~90% of patents). Examples: pharmaceutical compounds, software algorithms, mechanical devices, chemical processes.

2. **Design Patents** (15 years from grant): Protect new, original, and ornamental designs for articles of manufacture. The design must be inseparable from the article — it cannot exist in the abstract. Examples: iPhone shape, Coca-Cola bottle contour, furniture designs, font designs.

3. **Plant Patents** (20 years from filing): Protect distinct and new varieties of asexually reproduced plants. Examples: new rose varieties, fruit tree cultivars.

**Patentability Requirements:**
- **Novel:** The invention must be new — not previously known, used, patented, or published
- **Non-obvious:** The invention must not be an obvious improvement over existing technology (prior art) to a person of ordinary skill in the field
- **Useful:** The invention must have practical utility
- **Adequately described:** The patent application must describe the invention in sufficient detail to enable a person of ordinary skill to make and use it (enablement requirement)
- **Not abstract:** Post-*Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank* (2014), abstract ideas, laws of nature, and natural phenomena are not patentable. Software patents must claim "something more" than an abstract idea implemented on a generic computer.

## 6. The Negative Right Principle

This is the single most misunderstood concept in patent law, and it is worth isolating:

> **A patent does NOT give you the right to do something. A patent gives you the right to STOP OTHERS from doing something.**

This is called a "negative right." The pharmaceutical example makes it concrete: you can patent a compound for treating cancer, AND you can patent a method of treating cancer with that compound. But the patent alone does not authorize you to treat people — you still need FDA approval. The patent lets you exclude competitors; the regulatory approval lets you operate.

The same principle applies to software: a patent on a novel algorithm does not give you permission to deploy that algorithm in a regulated industry (financial services, healthcare). It gives you the right to prevent competitors from using the same algorithm.

**Practical Implication for Startups:** The negative right matters most when combined with other rights. A patent creates a defensive perimeter. Actual operation requires licenses, regulatory approvals, and compliance with industry standards — none of which a patent provides.

## 7. IP Strategy for Startups

The patents-for-startups transcript reframes patent value for early-stage companies around a critical insight: **the enforcement monopoly is the least important function of patents for startups.**

**Why Enforcement is Impractical for Startups:**
- Patent litigation costs average $1M-$5M+ through trial
- Cases take 2-4 years
- Discovery alone can cost $500K+
- Even winning doesn't guarantee collection
- Startups lack the legal budget and organizational bandwidth for sustained litigation

**The Real Value Stack for Startup Patents:**

1. **Licensing Revenue.** Whether you hold a pending application or an issued patent, you can license it. Exclusive licenses (one licensee) command higher fees; non-exclusive licenses (multiple licensees) generate broader but lower-per-unit revenue. License structures: lump sum, running royalties, milestone-based, cross-licenses.

2. **Asset Value.** Patents are saleable assets. Patent acquisitions by large companies (often post-failure of the patent holder) constitute a significant secondary market. A startup's patent portfolio may be worth more to an acquirer than the startup's operating business.

3. **Company Valuation.** Investors evaluate patent portfolios as part of due diligence. A strong portfolio signals technical differentiation, defensibility, and competence. Patents increase book value and can affect valuation multiples.

4. **Marketing Signal.** "Patent pending" on a product signals innovation to customers and partners. It also signals to competitors that you are investing in defensibility.

5. **Defensive Deterrence.** Mutual assured destruction: if both parties hold patents, neither sues. This is the primary function of patent portfolios at companies like IBM, Samsung, and Google — not offensive enforcement, but defensive deterrence.

## 8. Provisional Patent Applications

The provisional patent application is the startup's most cost-effective IP tool:

- **Filing fee:** $75 (micro entity), $150 (small entity), $320 (large entity)
- **No formal claims required:** Describe the invention in enough detail to support a later full application
- **Establishes a priority date:** The filing date of the provisional becomes the effective filing date for the later non-provisional application
- **12-month window:** Must convert to a non-provisional application (or PCT application) within 12 months, or the provisional expires
- **"Patent pending" status:** Immediately available upon filing
- **Not examined:** USPTO does not review the provisional for patentability

**Strategic Use:**
1. File provisional immediately when you have a novel technical approach
2. Use the 12-month window to validate the market and assess commercial viability
3. If the market validates, convert to a full non-provisional application
4. If the market does not validate, let the provisional lapse — you spent $75-$320 instead of $10K+

## 9. Trademark Clearance and Registration

The Trademark 101 webinar provides the practitioner's workflow:

**Step 1: Preliminary Search**
- Search the USPTO's Trademark Electronic Search System (TESS) at tess2.uspto.gov
- Search state trademark databases
- Search domain registrations, social media handles, business name registrations
- Purpose: identify obvious conflicts before investing in a comprehensive search

**Step 2: Comprehensive Search**
- Typically conducted by a professional search firm ($300-$1,500)
- Covers federal registrations, state registrations, common law marks, domain names, business directories, social media
- Searches for phonetic equivalents, visual similarity, and conceptual similarity — not just exact matches

**Step 3: Attorney Opinion Letter**
- Legal analysis of search results
- Assessment of likelihood of confusion with existing marks
- Risk classification: clear, some risk, significant risk, blocked
- Filing recommendation based on risk tolerance

**Step 4: Application Filing**
- File at trademark.uspto.gov via the Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS)
- Two filing options: TEAS Plus ($250/class) with strict requirements, or TEAS Standard ($350/class) with more flexibility
- Identify the goods/services using the ID Manual classifications
- Include a specimen showing the mark as used in commerce (or submit Statement of Use later for intent-to-use applications)

**Step 5: Examination**
- USPTO examining attorney reviews for compliance with Trademark Act requirements
- Common rejections: likelihood of confusion (Section 2(d)), merely descriptive (Section 2(e)(1)), generic
- Office actions must be responded to within 3-6 months

**Step 6: Publication and Registration**
- If approved, mark is published in the Official Gazette for 30-day opposition period
- If no opposition, registration issues (use-based) or Notice of Allowance issues (intent-to-use, requiring Statement of Use within 6 months, extendable)

## 10. The Spectrum of Distinctiveness

Trademark strength follows a well-established hierarchy:

```
[Fanciful] > [Arbitrary] > [Suggestive] > [Descriptive*] > [Generic]
  ↑ strongest                                                weakest ↑
  
  * Descriptive marks require proof of "secondary meaning"
    (consumer association with source) to be protectable
    
  Generic terms CANNOT be trademarked under any circumstances
```

| Category | Definition | Example | Protectable? |
|----------|-----------|---------|--------------|
| Fanciful | Coined word with no prior meaning | KODAK, XEROX, EXXON | Yes — strongest |
| Arbitrary | Real word applied to unrelated goods | APPLE (computers), AMAZON (retail) | Yes — strong |
| Suggestive | Suggests a quality without describing | NETFLIX, COPPERTONE | Yes — moderate |
| Descriptive | Directly describes goods/services | BEST BUY, SHARP (TVs) | Only with secondary meaning |
| Generic | Common name for the product | "Computer Store," "Aspirin" (originally a trademark) | Never |

**The Genericide Risk:** Successful trademarks can become victims of their own success. When a trademark becomes the generic term for a product category, it loses protection. Historical examples: aspirin, escalator, thermos, zipper, yo-yo. Active enforcement and proper trademark usage (VELCRO brand fasteners, not "velcro") prevent genericide.

## 11. IP Valuation and Portfolio Management

**Valuation Methods:**
- **Cost approach:** What did it cost to develop the IP? (Weakest — ignores market value)
- **Market approach:** What have comparable IP assets sold for? (Requires comparable transactions)
- **Income approach:** What is the present value of future income the IP will generate? (Strongest for business valuation)

**Portfolio Management Best Practices:**
1. Maintain a comprehensive IP inventory (patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets, domain names)
2. Track filing dates, maintenance deadlines, and renewal dates
3. Conduct annual IP audits to identify unprotected assets
4. Align IP strategy with business strategy (what assets are core to competitive advantage?)
5. Budget for enforcement (at minimum, send cease-and-desist letters when infringement is detected)
6. Consider IP insurance for both enforcement and defense

## 12. Layered IP Protection Model

The strongest IP positions use multiple types of protection simultaneously:

```
Product: Novel Software Application

Layer 1: Copyright
  └── Source code, documentation, UI designs, marketing materials
  └── Automatic + registered for key works
  
Layer 2: Trademark
  └── Product name, logo, tagline, trade dress
  └── Federal registration in relevant classes
  
Layer 3: Trade Secret
  └── Algorithms not disclosed in patents, customer data,
      pricing models, internal processes
  └── Protected by NDAs, access controls, training
  
Layer 4: Patent
  └── Novel algorithms, methods, systems architectures
  └── Utility patents for core innovations,
      design patents for distinctive UI elements
```

This model ensures that even if one layer fails (patent invalidated, trade secret leaked), other layers continue to provide protection.

## 13. Washington State IP Considerations

**Washington State Trade Secrets:**
- Washington adopted the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (RCW 19.108)
- 3-year statute of limitations from discovery of misappropriation
- Injunctive relief and damages available
- Attorney's fees available for willful and malicious misappropriation

**Non-compete Agreements (2020 law — RCW 49.62):**
- Non-competes only enforceable for employees earning more than $120,559.99/year (2024, adjusted annually)
- For independent contractors: $301,399.98/year threshold
- Maximum duration: 18 months
- Must be disclosed at or before acceptance of offer
- Employers must pay garden leave (compensation during the restricted period) unless otherwise exempted
- NDA/non-solicitation agreements remain enforceable regardless of compensation threshold

**Work-for-Hire and Assignment:**
- Under federal copyright law, works created by employees within the scope of employment are automatically owned by the employer ("work made for hire")
- For independent contractors, work-for-hire applies only to specific categories (contributions to collective works, parts of audiovisual works, etc.) — otherwise, copyright belongs to the contractor unless assigned in writing
- Washington follows the federal framework; invention assignment agreements are standard in employment contracts for tech companies
