## 1. The Capital Access Stack

Business funding in Washington is a multi-tier ecosystem. Different programs serve different business stages, sizes, and needs:

```
TIER 1: Grants (no repayment required)
  → Highly competitive; specific demographic or sector criteria
  → Working Washington grants, SBIR/STTR (tech), Commerce programs

TIER 2: Microlending (< $50,000, flexible terms)
  → Community lenders, CDFIs, SBA microloan
  → For businesses too small or too new for bank loans

TIER 3: CDFI Lending ($50K – $500K)
  → Craft3, Business Impact NW, SNAP Financial Access
  → For businesses that don't qualify for traditional bank loans

TIER 4: State Programs ($100K – $250K)
  → Small Business Flex Fund 2, SSBCI capital access
  → Market-rate lending with more flexible underwriting

TIER 5: SBA Guaranteed Loans ($250K – $5M+)
  → 7(a) and 504 programs through SBA-approved lenders
  → Backed by federal guarantee; longest terms

TIER 6: Conventional Bank Loans ($500K+)
  → Traditional bank underwriting; strongest businesses
  → Fastest processing; most restrictive eligibility
```

## 2. Federal Loan Programs (SBA)

The U.S. Small Business Administration offers several loan programs accessible to Washington businesses through SBA-approved lenders:

### 2.1 SBA 7(a) Loan Program

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Purpose | General business purposes: working capital, equipment, real estate, debt refinancing |
| Maximum loan | $5 million |
| SBA guarantee | 85% (loans up to $150K) / 75% (over $150K) |
| Terms | 10 years (working capital/equipment), 25 years (real estate) |
| Interest rates | Prime + 2.25% to Prime + 4.75% (depending on term and amount) |
| Use | Working capital, furniture/fixtures, equipment, building purchase |

The SBA 7(a) is the most flexible SBA program. Loans are made by SBA-approved lenders (banks, credit unions); the SBA guarantees a portion, reducing lender risk.

### 2.2 SBA 504 Loan Program

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Purpose | Long-term fixed-rate financing for major fixed assets |
| Structure | 50% bank, 40% Certified Development Company (CDC), 10% borrower equity |
| Maximum loan (CDC portion) | $5 million (up to $5.5M for manufacturing or energy) |
| Terms | 10, 20, or 25 years |
| Use | Land, buildings, major equipment, renovation |

The 504 program is ideal for businesses purchasing real estate or major equipment. The 40% CDC component is backed by the SBA, allowing below-market fixed rates.

### 2.3 SBA Microloan Program

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Maximum loan | $50,000 |
| Average loan | ~$13,000 |
| Terms | Up to 6 years |
| Use | Working capital, inventory, supplies, furniture, equipment |
| Administered by | Nonprofit intermediary lenders (CDFIs and community organizations) |

SBA Microloans are the right fit for the smallest businesses, startups, and entrepreneurs who need modest capital with supportive lending criteria.

### 2.4 SBIR/STTR Grants

Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) provide competitive grant funding from 11 federal agencies for small businesses developing new technology or products. Not loans — grants. Relevant for technology, biotech, and research-based businesses. Application through specific federal agencies (NIH, DOD, NSF, etc.).

**Source:** SBA.gov; Business.WA.gov Loans and Grants page.

## 3. State Programs: SSBCI and Flex Fund

### 3.1 State Small Business Credit Initiative (SSBCI)

The Washington Department of Commerce administers the SSBCI with a **$163.4 million allocation** from the U.S. Treasury. The program funds five capital access programs implemented through CDFIs and participating lenders:

- **Capital Access Programs (CAPs):** Loan loss reserve pools that incentivize banks to lend to risky small businesses
- **Loan Participation Programs:** State funds participate in loans with private lenders to reduce risk
- **Equity investments:** Targeted to very small businesses and socially/economically disadvantaged individuals (SEDIs)

SSBCI is structured as **loans and equity investments, not grants**. Businesses must qualify for and repay these funds.

**Contact:** Washington Department of Commerce, [https://www.commerce.wa.gov/access-to-capital/](https://www.commerce.wa.gov/access-to-capital/)

### 3.2 Small Business Flex Fund 2

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Loan amount | Up to $250,000 |
| Interest rates | 8.25% – 11.25% |
| Terms | 36 – 72 months |
| Prepayment penalty | None |
| Status (early 2026) | **Pausing new applications** during redesign phase |

The Flex Fund 2 was designed for businesses that have been operating for at least 3 months with documented revenue but may not qualify for traditional bank lending. As of early 2026, it is pausing new applications while being redesigned to better serve microloan needs. Check [https://SmallBusinessFlexFund.org](https://SmallBusinessFlexFund.org) for current status.

## 4. Working Washington Grants

Working Washington grants support small businesses with:
- Annual revenue under $5 million
- Demonstrated sustained losses in targeted sectors
- Location in Washington State

The Workforce Development Council has partnered with the Department of Commerce on new grant rounds. Grants are sector-specific and time-limited — announced through the Department of Commerce website and SBDC network. Subscribing to Commerce's small business newsletter is the most reliable way to learn about new grant opportunities.

**Contact:** Washington Department of Commerce, [https://www.commerce.wa.gov](https://www.commerce.wa.gov)

## 5. CDFI and Community Lenders

Community Development Financial Institutions serve businesses that don't qualify for traditional bank loans — due to credit history, collateral limitations, or business stage:

| Lender | Loan Range | Focus Area |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Craft3** | Up to $250,000 | Pacific NW underserved business owners; rural businesses |
| **Business Impact NW** | $5,000 – $500,000 | SEDI business owners in WA, AK, ID, OR |
| **SNAP Financial Access** | $5,000 – $150,000 | Women, POC, veterans, immigrants, LGBTQ+ entrepreneurs |
| **Tacoma Microloan** | $5,000 – $25,000 | Short-term loans for Tacoma-area businesses |
| **Rural Opportunity Fund** | Varies | Eastern Washington counties |

### 5.1 CDFI Advantages

CDFIs use more flexible underwriting criteria than banks:
- Personal credit below 680 may still qualify
- No or minimal collateral required for smaller loans
- First-time business owners accepted
- Technical assistance often paired with lending
- Patient capital with longer terms than commercial lenders

**Source:** Gusto Washington Business Grants and Loans; Business.WA.gov.

## 9. The Washington SBDC Network

Beyond the Everett office, the Washington SBDC network serves businesses across the state with locations in:
- Seattle (Seattle Central College)
- Tacoma (Tacoma Community College)
- Bellingham (Western Washington University)
- Olympia (South Puget Sound Community College)
- Spokane (Community Colleges of Spokane)
- Tri-Cities, Yakima, Wenatchee, and more

All SBDC services are free and confidential. Advisors sign confidentiality agreements. Services include:
- Business plan review
- Financial projections and cash flow modeling
- Loan application preparation (SBA, CDFI, bank)
- Market research support
- Social media and digital marketing strategy
- Export assistance

## 11. Minority, Women, and Veteran Business Programs

### 11.1 Office of Minority and Women's Business Enterprises (OMWBE)

The OMWBE certifies minority-owned businesses (MBEs), women-owned businesses (WBEs), and emerging small businesses (ESBs) for access to state and local government contracting set-asides and programs.

**Linked Deposit Loan Program:** OMWBE-certified businesses may qualify for reduced-interest loans through Washington state's linked deposit program.

### 11.2 Veteran Business Resources

Washington veterans starting businesses have access to:
- **SBA Veterans Advantage:** Reduced fees on SBA 7(a) loans
- **Boots to Business:** Free entrepreneurship training through the SBA
- **SCORE veteran mentoring:** Dedicated veteran mentors in the SCORE network
- **CDFIs:** Several community lenders have veteran-specific loan programs

## 12. Choosing the Right Resource

A decision framework for Snohomish County entrepreneurs:

| Situation | Recommended First Stop |
|-----------|----------------------|
| "I need help writing a business plan" | SBDC Everett (free, confidential) |
| "I need a small loan ($5K–$50K) with flexible credit requirements" | CDFI (Craft3, Business Impact NW, SNAP) or SBA Microloan |
| "I need $50K–$250K and my credit is okay" | SBA 7(a) through local bank + SBDC prep |
| "I'm developing new technology and need non-dilutive funding" | SBIR/STTR grants through NWIRC |
| "I want to sell to government" | APEX (WA Procurement Technical Assistance) |
| "I'm a veteran starting a business" | SBDC + SCORE veteran mentoring + SBA Veterans Advantage |
| "I need to understand the regulatory landscape" | This research module (WSB) + SBDC |
| "I want to connect with the regional business community" | Economic Alliance Snohomish County |
