## 1. Washington Minimum Wage (2026)

Washington's minimum wage is set annually by L&I based on the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W). For 2026:

| Employee Category | Hourly Rate (2026) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Standard (16 years and older) | **$17.13/hr** |
| Youth workers (14–15 years old) | **$14.56/hr** (85% of standard) |

### 1.1 No Tip Credit

Washington does not allow a **tip credit**. All employees must receive the full minimum wage before tips. Tips are in addition to minimum wage, not a substitute for part of it. This is a critical distinction for restaurant, hospitality, and service industry employers.

### 1.2 Annual Adjustment Schedule

The Washington minimum wage adjusts annually on January 1. Employers must update payroll systems before the new rate takes effect. L&I announces the new rate by September 30 of the preceding year.

## 2. Everett Minimum Wage Tiers

Everett has adopted a tiered minimum wage structure above the state minimum:

| Employer Size | Jan–Jun 2026 | Jul–Dec 2026 |
|--------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Large (500+ employees)** | $20.77/hr | $20.77/hr |
| **Mid-size (15–499 employees OR > $2M annual revenue)** | $18.77/hr | $19.77/hr |
| **Small (< 15 employees AND ≤ $2M revenue)** | $17.13/hr (state rate) | $17.13/hr (state rate) |

### 2.1 How to Determine Employer Size

The employer size classification is based on:
- **Employee count:** Count all employees across all locations (not just Everett), including part-time workers
- **Revenue threshold:** Annual gross revenue of $2 million or more in any prior year triggers mid-size classification even if fewer than 15 employees

### 2.2 Multi-Location Employers

A business with 8 employees in Everett and 10 employees in Marysville has 18 total employees — qualifying as **mid-size** under Everett's scale. The location of the employees does not matter for the count; the total enterprise headcount is used.

**Source:** Washington L&I, Minimum Wage page; Mondaq 2026 Minimum Wage Update; PayNW.

## 3. Overtime Exemptions and Thresholds

### 3.1 The Overtime Threshold (2026)

Non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay (1.5× the regular rate) for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek. For 2026:

| Category | Annual Salary Threshold | Weekly Equivalent |
|----------|------------------------|------------------|
| Overtime exemption | **$80,168.40/year** | $1,541.70/week |
| Computer professional (hourly) | **$59.96/hr** | — |

Employees earning below the threshold are **eligible for overtime** regardless of job title or duties. An employee titled "manager" who earns $45,000/year is not exempt from overtime.

### 3.2 Noncompete Enforceability Thresholds

Washington's noncompete agreement enforceability requires employees to earn above a separate threshold:
- **Employees:** $126,858.83/year minimum to enforce a noncompete
- **Independent contractors:** $317,147.09/year minimum

These thresholds adjust annually. Noncompete agreements for employees below these thresholds are not enforceable in Washington.

**Source:** Miller Nash LLP; Perkins Coie 2026 wage update.

## 4. Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML)

### 4.1 Program Overview

Washington's **Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML)** program provides eligible employees with:
- Up to **12 weeks** of paid leave per year for qualifying events
- Up to **16–18 weeks** for combined qualifying events (serious health condition + bonding)
- Wage replacement at 60–90% of wages (up to the statewide average weekly wage cap)

### 4.2 Premium Rate (2026)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Premium rate | **1.13%** of gross wages (excluding tips) |
| Wage cap | Social Security taxable maximum ($184,500) |
| Employee share | ~71.43% of premium |
| Employer share (50+ employees) | ~28.57% of premium |
| Employer obligation (<50 employees) | Not required to pay employer share; must collect and remit employee share |

### 4.3 Small Business Grants

Businesses with fewer than 50 employees can apply for grants of **$3,000 per employee** on PFML (for 7+ continuous days) who is replaced by a temporary worker, up to **10 grants per calendar year**. Receiving a grant obligates the employer to pay the employer share of premiums for three years afterward.

### 4.4 Job Protection (2026 Change)

Effective **January 1, 2026**:
- Job protection extends to employers with **25 or more employees** (previously 50+)
- Employee eligibility reduced from 12 months to **180 calendar days** of employment

**Source:** Washington ESD, paidleave.wa.gov; Paychex PFML guide.

## 5. Workers' Compensation

### 5.1 State Fund Requirement

Washington requires all employers with employees to carry **workers' compensation insurance through the Washington State Fund**, administered by L&I. Washington is a state-funded workers' compensation state — private insurance is not accepted for standard coverage.

Workers' compensation covers:
- Medical treatment for work-related injuries and illnesses
- Wage replacement during recovery
- Vocational rehabilitation
- Disability benefits (temporary and permanent)
- Survivor benefits for fatal injuries

### 5.2 Premium Calculation

Premium rates are based on:
- **Industry classification code** (each industry has a different risk rate)
- **Hours worked** by employees in each classification
- **Experience modification** (employer's claims history over time)

New employers are assigned a standard rate for their industry. Employers with better safety records can earn rate reductions through Washington's Retrospective Rating program.

### 5.3 Independent Contractors

Washington's definition of "employee" is broad. Misclassifying employees as independent contractors to avoid workers' compensation is a significant enforcement risk. L&I uses a multi-factor test (not just IRS criteria) to determine employee status. Misclassification can result in:
- Back premiums plus penalties and interest
- Personal liability for principals who controlled the work
- Criminal referral in egregious cases

**Source:** Washington L&I Workers' Compensation; Connecteam Washington Labor Laws guide.

## 6. Mandatory Paid Sick Leave

Washington requires all employers to provide **paid sick leave** to all employees:

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Accrual rate | 1 hour of paid sick leave per 40 hours worked |
| Carryover | Up to 40 hours may carry over to the following year |
| Usage | Eligible after 90 days of employment |
| Rate | Employee's normal hourly rate |
| Minimum increments | No minimum increment larger than 1 hour |

### 6.1 Permitted Uses

Employees may use paid sick leave for:
- Their own illness or injury
- Family member's illness or injury (broad definition of "family")
- Qualifying public health emergency (quarantine, school closure)
- Domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking (seeking help, legal proceedings)

### 6.2 Interaction with PFML

PFML and paid sick leave are separate obligations. An employee with a qualifying PFML event may use accrued paid sick leave to supplement PFML wage replacement — but the employer may not require simultaneous use.

## 7. WA Cares Fund (Long-Term Care)

The **WA Cares Fund** is a mandatory long-term care insurance program:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Premium rate | 0.58% of gross wages (typical) |
| Administration | Collected alongside PFML through quarterly L&I reporting |
| Benefits availability | **July 2026** (first eligibility for benefits) |

### 7.1 What WA Cares Covers

Eligible individuals can use WA Cares benefits for:
- Professional personal care (in-home aides)
- Healthcare equipment and home modifications
- Memory care and adult day services
- Nursing facility care (up to $36,500 lifetime maximum, indexed)

### 7.2 Opt-Out for Those with Private LTC Insurance

Employees who have approved private long-term care insurance may apply for a permanent exemption from WA Cares premiums. The opt-out window was 2021; no new opt-outs are currently available.

**Source:** PayNW 2026 Compliance Updates.

## 8. New-Hire Reporting

Washington employers must report all new hires and rehires within **20 days** of the start date to the Department of Social and Health Services (DSHS). Required information:
- Employee's name, address, date of birth, SSN
- Employer's name, address, federal EIN, state UBI

New-hire reporting feeds the state's child support enforcement database. Failure to report is a compliance violation but typically results in warnings for first offenses.

## 9. Labor Poster Requirements

Washington employers must display current mandatory posters in a location visible to all employees:

| Poster | Agency |
|--------|--------|
| Your Rights as a Worker | WA L&I (minimum wage, overtime, sick leave) |
| Paid Family and Medical Leave | WA ESD |
| Workers' Compensation Notice | WA L&I |
| Equal Opportunity/Anti-Discrimination | EEOC / WA DCEO |
| OSHA Job Safety & Health | Federal OSHA |

L&I provides free poster downloads and pre-printed poster sets. Posters must be updated when new versions are released; outdated posters create compliance risk.

## 10. The Employer Compliance Checklist

For a small Everett employer (1–14 employees):

**Before first hire:**
- [ ] Obtain EIN (IRS)
- [ ] Register with L&I (workers' compensation)
- [ ] Register with ESD (unemployment insurance)
- [ ] Register for PFML (ESD)
- [ ] Post required labor posters

**Upon each hire:**
- [ ] Verify employment eligibility (I-9 form)
- [ ] Report new hire to DSHS within 20 days
- [ ] Enroll in workers' compensation (classified correctly)
- [ ] Set up PFML and WA Cares withholding
- [ ] Verify applicable minimum wage rate (state or Everett tier)

**Quarterly:**
- [ ] File L&I quarterly report (workers' comp + paid sick leave)
- [ ] File ESD quarterly report (unemployment insurance)
- [ ] File PFML quarterly premium report
- [ ] File WA Cares quarterly premium report
- [ ] Review current minimum wage rates (January updates)
