> **Domain:** Acoustics, Geophysics, Psychoacoustics
> **Module:** 2 -- Sub-Hz and Infrasonic Domain (0.001 to 20 Hz)
> **Through-line:** *The claim that infrasound is inaudible is one of the most persistent misconceptions in acoustics. Humans can perceive frequencies well below 20 Hz at sufficient amplitude -- what changes is not the presence of perception but its character. The tonal sensation gives way to pressure, pulsation, and a qualitative shift that the standard audiogram cannot capture. The infrasonic world is not silent; it is differently heard.*

## 1. Definitions and Boundaries

Infrasound is formally defined as acoustic oscillations below the low-frequency limit of audible sound, approximately 16-20 Hz, per the International Electrotechnical Commission standard (IEC, 1994). The companion term "low-frequency noise" typically spans the range from 10 Hz to 200 Hz, encompassing both the upper infrasonic band and the lower audible band [1][2].

The boundaries are not sharp. There is no single frequency at which hearing "turns off." Rather, the threshold of perception rises steeply with decreasing frequency, and the qualitative character of what is perceived changes fundamentally. The conventional 20 Hz cutoff is an engineering convenience, not a physiological cliff [2].

### Frequency Sub-Bands

```
INFRASONIC DOMAIN: 0.001 Hz to 20 Hz
================================================
0.001 - 0.01 Hz    Deep infrasonic
                   Earth free oscillations, microseisms
                   Period: 100 - 1000 seconds

0.01 - 0.1 Hz     Sub-infrasonic
                   Severe weather, volcanic, oceanic
                   Period: 10 - 100 seconds

0.1 - 1 Hz        Low infrasonic
                   Industrial, transportation, wind turbine
                   Period: 1 - 10 seconds

1 - 5 Hz          Mid infrasonic
                   Vestibular engagement, body resonances
                   Individual cycle perception possible

5 - 20 Hz         Upper infrasonic
                   Transition to tonal perception
                   Schumann resonance overlap (7.83 Hz)
                   Brainwave frequency correspondence
```

## 2. The Perception Threshold Misconception

### The Evidence

The standard claim in textbooks, public health documents, and popular science that "infrasound is inaudible" is demonstrably incorrect. Peer-reviewed research establishes the following [2][3]:

- Human hearing thresholds have been reliably measured down to 4 Hz at sufficient stimulus levels (Yeowart et al., 1967)
- Some research demonstrates perception at even lower frequencies when amplitude is adequate
- At 16 Hz, the hearing threshold is approximately 90-100 dB SPL (compared to approximately 0-10 dB SPL at the ear's most sensitive range of 2-4 kHz)
- The threshold curve does not show a discontinuity at 20 Hz; it is a smooth, steeply rising function

### Perception Character Changes

Below approximately 20 Hz, the character of acoustic perception changes qualitatively through a series of transitions [2][3]:

| Frequency Range | Perceptual Character |
|---|---|
| 20-30 Hz | Continuous tone, but increasingly "rough" |
| 10-20 Hz | Tonal sensation fading, pulsation emerging |
| 5-10 Hz | Individual cycles perceptible, pressure at eardrums |
| 1-5 Hz | No tonal quality; pressure, vibration, vestibular |
| Below 1 Hz | Primarily vestibular and proprioceptive |

### Dynamic Range Compression

A critical phenomenon at infrasonic frequencies is the compression of the auditory dynamic range. At frequencies below about 20 Hz, the difference between the threshold of perception and the threshold of pain narrows dramatically. This means [2]:

- A slight increase in level can change perceived loudness from barely audible to uncomfortably loud
- A sound that is inaudible to one person may be loud to another, even with normal hearing
- This compression explains why infrasonic disturbance complaints are often dismissed as psychological -- the narrow dynamic range means the source level only needs to be slightly above threshold for the percept to be significant

```
Dynamic Range Comparison:
  At 1 kHz:   Threshold ~3 dB SPL    Pain ~120 dB SPL    Range: ~117 dB
  At 100 Hz:  Threshold ~25 dB SPL   Pain ~120 dB SPL    Range: ~95 dB
  At 20 Hz:   Threshold ~75 dB SPL   Pain ~120 dB SPL    Range: ~45 dB
  At 10 Hz:   Threshold ~95 dB SPL   Pain ~125 dB SPL    Range: ~30 dB
  At 4 Hz:    Threshold ~107 dB SPL  Pain ~130 dB SPL    Range: ~23 dB
```

The available dynamic range at 4 Hz is approximately one-fifth of the range at 1 kHz. This is not a deficiency of the ear; it is a consequence of the impedance-matching limitations of the middle ear at wavelengths much longer than the ear canal [2][3].

## 3. Natural Infrasonic Sources

The natural world produces infrasound from sources spanning the full sub-20 Hz spectrum. The following categories represent the major documented sources, with frequency ranges and characteristic sound pressure levels [3][4]:

### Volcanic Eruptions

Volcanic infrasound is generated by explosive eruptions, lava fountaining, and harmonic tremor within the volcanic conduit. Frequencies range from 0.01 Hz to 20 Hz, with the most powerful signals in the 0.1-5 Hz band. Major eruptions (VEI 4+) produce infrasonic signals detectable thousands of kilometers away. The 1883 eruption of Krakatoa generated infrasonic waves that circled the globe multiple times, recorded as barometric pressure fluctuations at monitoring stations worldwide [4].

### Severe Weather Systems

Tornadic storms, hurricanes, and severe thunderstorms generate infrasound through multiple mechanisms: vortex shedding, turbulent flow, convective instability, and lightning channel expansion. Tornadic infrasound (0.5-10 Hz) has been proposed as an early warning signal, potentially detectable before tornado formation is visible on radar [4].

### Ocean Waves (Microbaroms)

Standing ocean waves generate atmospheric infrasound through a mechanism first explained by Longuet-Higgins (1950). When two ocean wave trains of similar frequency travel in opposite directions, their nonlinear interaction produces a pressure oscillation at double the ocean wave frequency. Typical microbarom frequencies range from 0.1 to 0.5 Hz, with a global average period of approximately 5 seconds (0.2 Hz). Microbaroms constitute a persistent, quasi-continuous global infrasonic background [4].

### Wind-Terrain Interaction

Wind flowing over mountain ridges, through valleys, and around large structures generates infrasound through vortex shedding, cavity resonance, and turbulent buffeting. The "mountain roar" phenomenon in certain alpine environments produces infrasound at 0.5-5 Hz during high-wind events [4].

### Avalanches

Snow avalanches generate infrasound through the displacement of air by the moving snow mass and through ground coupling. Frequencies range from 1 to 10 Hz, with the signal duration and spectral content correlating with avalanche size and type (powder vs. wet snow) [4].

### Meteors

Meteors entering the atmosphere generate infrasonic shock waves as they decelerate from hypersonic velocities. The 2013 Chelyabinsk meteor produced an infrasonic signal detected by the CTBTO (Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty Organization) infrasound monitoring network at stations thousands of kilometers distant, with a dominant frequency of approximately 0.03 Hz [4].

### Auroral Electrojet

Geomagnetic disturbances associated with auroral activity generate infrasound through Joule heating and Lorentz force coupling in the upper atmosphere. Frequencies range from 0.001 to 0.1 Hz, with the strongest signals during geomagnetic storms [4].

### Seismic-Acoustic Coupling

Earthquakes generate atmospheric infrasound through direct ground-to-air coupling at the surface. The seismic p-waves compress and rarefact the ground surface, which acts as a vibrating piston coupling energy into the atmosphere. This mechanism produces infrasound at the dominant frequencies of the seismic waves, typically 0.01-5 Hz [4].

## 4. Anthropogenic Infrasonic Sources

Human industrial and transportation activities produce infrasound from at least the following source categories [3][4]:

### Wind Turbines

Modern large-scale wind turbines generate infrasound at blade-pass frequencies (typically 0.5-4 Hz for three-bladed turbines rotating at 10-20 rpm) and their harmonics. The infrasonic output depends on turbine size, rotation speed, and atmospheric conditions. Measured levels at 200-500 meters from a single turbine are typically 50-70 dB SPL at 1-4 Hz -- below the threshold of perception for most individuals, but above it for some [3][4].

### Industrial Machinery

Large reciprocating engines, compressors, blast furnaces, diesel generators, and HVAC systems produce infrasound at their operating frequencies and subharmonics. Industrial infrasound is typically in the 1-20 Hz range and can propagate significant distances through ducts, pipes, and building structures with minimal attenuation [3].

### Transportation

Heavy vehicle traffic, railway operations, and aircraft (particularly during takeoff and landing) generate infrasound through engine vibration, aerodynamic turbulence, and structural resonance. Highway traffic produces broadband infrasound in the 1-20 Hz range with levels dependent on traffic density and vehicle mix [3].

### Gas Turbines and Power Plants

Gas turbine power plants generate low-frequency noise and infrasound through combustion oscillation, exhaust flow, and structural vibration. Combustion-driven oscillations at 5-20 Hz can propagate through exhaust stacks and are among the most common industrial infrasound complaints [3].

### Mining and Quarrying

Blasting operations produce impulsive infrasound with peak levels exceeding 130 dB SPL at the source. The infrasonic component of a blast wave propagates further than the audible component due to lower atmospheric absorption [4].

## 5. Propagation Characteristics

### Extraordinary Low Absorption

The defining physical characteristic of infrasound is its extraordinarily low atmospheric absorption. At a period of 10 seconds (0.1 Hz), the atmospheric absorption coefficient is approximately 2 x 10^-9 dB per kilometer (Cook, 1962; cited in Persinger 2013). This means [3][4]:

```
Absorption at 0.1 Hz:
  Over 1 km:     ~0.000002 dB loss (unmeasurable)
  Over 100 km:   ~0.0002 dB loss (negligible)
  Over 1000 km:  ~0.002 dB loss (negligible)
  Over 20,000 km: ~0.04 dB loss (< 1% energy loss)
```

Energy loss is less than 1% even after propagating halfway around the Earth at 0.1 Hz. By comparison, audible sound at 1 kHz experiences absorption of approximately 5 dB per kilometer in standard atmospheric conditions -- a difference of more than 9 orders of magnitude [4].

### Atmospheric Waveguides

Infrasound propagates through the atmosphere in ducted waveguides formed by temperature and wind gradients. The principal ducts are [4]:

- **Tropospheric duct:** Surface to tropopause (0-12 km), effective for downwind propagation
- **Stratospheric duct:** Refraction by the stratospheric temperature maximum at 50 km altitude and stratospheric winds
- **Thermospheric duct:** Refraction by the strong temperature increase above 90 km altitude

These ducts allow infrasound to propagate over intercontinental distances. The CTBTO operates a global network of 60 infrasound monitoring stations (the International Monitoring System) designed to detect clandestine nuclear tests by their infrasonic signatures. This network routinely detects volcanic eruptions, bolide entries, and large industrial explosions at distances of thousands of kilometers [4].

### Geometric Spreading

In the absence of ducting, infrasound experiences geometric spreading losses of approximately 6 dB per doubling of distance (spherical spreading) or 3 dB per doubling (cylindrical spreading in a waveguide). The actual spreading loss depends on the source geometry and atmospheric conditions [4].

## 6. Earth Free Oscillations

### Background Hum

The Earth itself vibrates continuously at frequencies between approximately 2 and 7 millihertz (periods of 150-500 seconds). These free oscillations, sometimes called the "hum of the Earth," are excited by the interaction between the ocean, atmosphere, and solid Earth. They were first detected in seismographic records in the 1960s and remain continuously present even in the absence of earthquakes [4][5].

The fundamental spheroidal mode (0S2) has a period of approximately 54 minutes (frequency 0.31 mHz). Higher-order modes populate the spectrum up to several millihertz. The coupling with the lower atmosphere occurs at frequencies between 5 and 7 mHz, corresponding to periods of approximately 2.5-3.3 minutes (Nishida et al., 2000) [5].

### Earthquake-Excited Modes

Large earthquakes excite the same family of free oscillation modes but at much higher amplitudes. After the 2004 Sumatra-Andaman earthquake (M9.1), the fundamental mode was detectable for weeks. The spectral signature of Earth's free oscillations provides information about the planet's internal density structure, analogous to how stellar oscillation modes reveal stellar interiors (see [M1](01-deep-space-pressure-waves.md)) [5].

```
Earth Free Oscillation Modes:
  0S2:  Period ~54 min   (0.31 mHz)   Football-shaped deformation
  0S3:  Period ~35.6 min (0.47 mHz)   Pear-shaped deformation
  0S4:  Period ~25.7 min (0.65 mHz)
  ...
  0S29: Period ~5.5 min  (3.0 mHz)

  Background amplitude: ~0.5 nanogal (5 x 10^-12 m/s^2)
  Post-M9 amplitude:    ~100x background for fundamental modes
```

## 7. Biological Interaction

### Convergence with Body Frequencies

A striking finding from physiological research is that infrasound frequencies converge with those generated by the human body itself. Muscle sounds and whole-body vibrations are predominantly within the 5-40 Hz range, with typical oscillation amplitudes of 1-50 micrometers, equivalent to pressures of about 1 Pascal and energies on the order of 10^-11 W/m^2 (Persinger, 2013) [5].

The vestibular system -- the balance and spatial orientation organ of the inner ear -- is directly sensitive to infrasonic frequencies. Below approximately 2 Hz, vestibular stimulation becomes the dominant perceptual mode, producing sensations of motion, imbalance, or spatial disorientation rather than anything recognized as "sound" [5].

### Neural Coupling

The coupling between approximately 7 Hz and 40-45 Hz frequency bands mediates information transfer between the hippocampal formation and the cerebral cortices (Holz et al., 2010). This theta-gamma coupling is one of the fundamental rhythms of mammalian cognition, involved in memory encoding, spatial navigation, and attention. The overlap between these neural oscillation frequencies and the upper infrasonic band has generated significant research interest [5].

### Whole-Body Resonance

The human body contains multiple resonant structures with natural frequencies in the infrasonic and low-audible range [5]:

| Structure | Resonant Frequency |
|---|---|
| Whole body (standing) | 5-8 Hz |
| Thorax-abdomen system | 4-8 Hz |
| Head-neck system | 20-30 Hz |
| Eye-orbit system | 30-80 Hz |
| Abdominal mass | 4-8 Hz |
| Spinal column (axial) | 10-12 Hz |

Exposure to infrasound at or near these resonant frequencies can amplify internal vibration beyond what the external sound pressure level would suggest, particularly when the body is in a seated or standing position that allows free oscillation of the resonant structure [5].

## 8. Infrasound Masking Effects

### Cross-Frequency Interaction

Recent research has demonstrated that infrasound does not merely coexist with audible sound but interacts with it in measurable ways. A 2023 study published in Acta Acustica found that an 8 Hz infrasound sinusoid at 9 dB sensation level produces a significant masking effect on the detection of a 64 Hz pure tone [6]:

- The masking effect raised detection thresholds by an average of 4.6 dB across 19 normal-hearing listeners
- The effect was statistically significant (p < 0.01)
- The masking bandwidth and magnitude were consistent with upward spread of masking from the infrasonic signal

### Amplitude Modulation Perception

Additional research suggests that infrasound may be perceived not as a direct auditory stimulus but as amplitude modulation of simultaneously presented audible tones. When infrasound and audible tones are presented together, listeners report fluctuations in the loudness of the audible tone at the infrasonic rate. This indicates that the perceptual interaction between infrasonic and audible frequencies involves mechanisms more complex than simple energetic masking [6].

### Implications for Environmental Assessment

These masking effects have direct implications for environmental noise assessment. Standard noise measurement procedures that exclude frequencies below 20 Hz may miss a significant perceptual component. An infrasonic source that is itself below the threshold of direct perception may nonetheless alter the perceived character of audible environmental sound through masking and modulation effects [6].
