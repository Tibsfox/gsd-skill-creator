## 1. American Highway Flower (1994)

*Puzzle* had gone Gold, "Dizz Knee Land" had reached #5 on Modern Rock, and I.R.S. Records was still standing -- barely. The sophomore album arrived into a landscape that had shifted under the band's feet.

**Label:** I.R.S. Records | **Produced by:** Jason Corsaro and dada | **Billboard 200 peak:** #178

The album was originally titled *Backyard Fruit* before being renamed [4]. Jason Corsaro's production pushed the band toward darker, more politically charged territory -- a deliberate evolution from the debut's more accessible power pop. Where *Puzzle* had invited you in with harmonies and wordplay, *American Highway Flower* challenged you to stay [4][12].

### 1.2 Key Tracks

**"Feet to the Sun"** received attention for its subject matter, addressing suicide -- a departure from the witty satire that had defined the band's public identity. This was dada without the safety net of irony, engaging directly with darkness rather than deflecting it through wordplay [4].

**"All I Am"** peaked at #27 on the Billboard Modern Rock Tracks chart and spent eight weeks on the chart [4]. It was the album's most commercially viable moment, proving that dada could generate radio singles even as their sound grew more complex and less immediately ingratiating.

**"S.F. Bar '63"** nodded back to the band's Bay Area roots -- the San Francisco of the early 1960s, before the Summer of Love, before the counterculture became a brand. The title anchors the song in a specific time and place that predates the mythology.

### 1.3 Critical Reception

The critical response was divided along predictable lines. The **Chicago Tribune** gave it 3.5 out of 4 stars, recognizing the ambition [4]. **AllMusic** was less enthusiastic at 2 out of 5 stars [15]. **Trouser Press** noted that while "the sweet harmonizing on '8 Track' is notable," the album "feels too long by at least twenty minutes" and observed Gurley's "growing degree of political paranoia." The reviewer noted that the prevalent "sense of disgust" likely "alienated a lot of listeners" [12].

This split -- strong review from a mainstream outlet, lukewarm from genre specialists -- suggests an album that was evolving faster than its audience. The band was growing; the critical framework hadn't caught up.

### 1.4 Sales and Context

Over 50,000 copies in the first year. As of 2019, Nielsen SoundScan reported 77,000 copies sold in the United States [4]. The 2004 Blue Cave Records reissue included three bonus tracks: "I'm Feeling Nothing," "Little Insister," and "All American Suicide" [4].

The album arrived during the peak of grunge dominance. By 1994, alternative rock radio was saturated with bands leaning into distortion and angst. dada's melodic, harmony-driven approach -- the very quality that made them exceptional -- stood outside the prevailing aesthetic [1][12]. The music was there. The cultural moment was elsewhere.

## 2. El Subliminoso (1996)

This was the album that a dying label dropped into the void.

**Label:** I.R.S. Records | **Produced by:** dada, Scott Gordon, and Adam Weiner | **Mixed by:** Tom Lord-Alge | **Recorded at:** IRS Studios, 1995 | **Released:** April 2, 1996

*El Subliminoso* was dada's third and final album for I.R.S. Records. It arrived at the worst possible moment -- as the label was collapsing around it. I.R.S., which had been one of the most important independent imprints of the 1980s, folded in 1996. The album received minimal promotion and marketing support as the infrastructure disintegrated [1][5].

| 2 | Sick In Santorini | 3:36 |

| 5 | The Spirit Of 2009 | 5:54 |

| 9 | Rise | 4:52 |
| 10 | No One | 3:33 |
| 11 | The Fleecing Of America | 5:09 |
| 12 | Hollow Man | 4:38 |

**Total runtime:** approximately 57 minutes.

### 2.2 Key Tracks

**"Bob the Drummer"** became a fan favorite and a live staple -- one of the deeper catalog tracks that Calio has pointed to as more representative of what dada actually is than "Dizz Knee Land" [5][12]. The song is a character study, narrative-driven, with the kind of lyrical specificity that rewards repeated listening.

**"The Spirit of 2009"** -- a title that was forward-looking in 1996 -- demonstrated the band's willingness to write outside the present tense, projecting into an imagined future that would arrive thirteen years later. Trouser Press praised both "Bob the Drummer" and "The Spirit of 2009" as standout achievements [12].

**"The Fleecing of America"** continued the political thread from *American Highway Flower*, sharpening the critique into something more direct.

### 2.3 Critical Reception and Sales

Trouser Press noted the album was "scarcely more ingratiating" than the sophomore effort, observing that the band adopted a "harder-hitting rhythm section" approach while downplaying their trademark harmonies [12]. Among fans, the album became a cult favorite for its more experimental sound.

**Sales:** Approximately 36,000 copies (Nielsen SoundScan) [5]. These numbers tell the story of infrastructure failure more than audience rejection. When your label folds during your promotion cycle, the music doesn't reach the people who would buy it.

### 2.4 The Reissue

The 2004 Blue Cave Records reissue included four bonus tracks: "California Dreamin'" (a cover of The Mamas & the Papas), "Happy Day Violator," "Mystery Date," and "Put Me Down" [5]. An **RSD 2026 reissue** has been announced as a 30th anniversary remastered and expanded 2LP "Plasma" vinyl edition -- proof that the album still commands collector interest three decades later [5].

## 3. dada (1998)

After the I.R.S. collapse, dada signed to MCA Records and released their self-titled fourth album on September 8, 1998.

**Label:** MCA Records | **Produced by:** Danny Kortchmar and dada | **Recorded at:** Conway Recording Studios, Los Angeles | **Heatseekers peak:** #32

The producer was **Danny Kortchmar**, a musician and producer with deep roots in the California music scene. Kortchmar had previously worked with James Taylor, Don Henley, Jackson Browne, and many others [1][6]. His involvement represented a different kind of validation than Ken Scott's -- where Scott connected dada to the British classic rock tradition (Beatles, Bowie), Kortchmar connected them to the California singer-songwriter tradition (Laurel Canyon, the 1970s LA scene).

### 3.2 Key Tracks

**"California Gold"** was released as the first single -- the title itself a nod to the West Coast tradition the band had always inhabited [6]. **"Information Undertow"** was distributed to radio stations as a promotional single later in 1998, its title anticipating the digital information overload that would define the coming decade [6].

**"The Ballad of Earl Grey and Chamomile"** -- a track whose whimsical title belies the kind of narrative specificity that defined the band's best writing. The mundane (tea varieties) becomes a vehicle for something larger.

### 3.3 Critical Reception and Sales

Trouser Press called it an artistic rejuvenation, delivering "cleverness and melody in spades" with "pop hooks" that balanced "loathing" with accessibility. The production created "impressively full sonics" [12]. This was the most polished production of their career -- Kortchmar's Laurel Canyon sheen applied to dada's harmonic architecture.

**Sales:** As of 2019, approximately 25,000 copies (Nielsen SoundScan) [6]. The numbers continued to decline, but not because the music was declining. The band was on their third label in four albums. Audiences don't always follow artists across label changes, especially when the first label's collapse disrupted the relationship between band and audience at a critical moment.

### 3.4 The MCA Sessions

The sessions were notably productive. The band recorded far more material than could fit on one album [7]. The surplus tracks would eventually become the next release -- making the MCA period a double album in everything but name and timing.

## 4. How to Be Found (2004)

**Label:** Blue Cave Records | **Released:** March 2, 2004

This fifth release was technically a compilation rather than a new recording. It contained all of the leftover tracks from the MCA sessions that had produced the 1998 self-titled album. While none of the material had ever been commercially released, it was not "new" in the sense of being freshly written or recorded [7].

### 4.1 Track Listing

|---|-------|
| 1 | The Next Train Out Of My Mind |
| 2 | It's All Mine |
| 3 | How To Be Found |
| 4 | Crumble |
| 5 | Nothing Like You |
| 6 | Guitar Girl |
| 7 | Any Day The Wind Blows |
| 8 | Blue Girl |
| 9 | My Life Could Be Different |
| 10 | What's Happening To Steven |
| 11 | I Wish You Were Here Now |
| 12 | Reason |
| 13 | Love Is a Weird Thing |

### 4.2 Production Pedigree

The tracks were mixed by industry heavyweights including **Bob Clearmountain** (Bruce Springsteen, David Bowie), Scott Gordon, and Tom Lord-Alge [7]. Clearmountain's involvement is notable -- his mix credits constitute a parallel history of popular music from the late 1970s onward. The fact that these were "leftovers" mixed by one of the most accomplished engineers in the business tells you something about the caliber of the MCA sessions.

### 4.3 Critical Reception

**PopMatters** noted that while "it might not be the best album ever created by the trio, it's certainly full of intelligent rock songs that stand above most of what's currently being heard on commercial radio" [21]. The review specifically praised "Reason" for its Hendrix-style riffing [21] -- Gurley channeling Jimi through the filter of a California power trio, three decades and three thousand miles removed from the original.

### 4.4 The Blue Cave Context

Blue Cave Records was the vehicle through which the band managed their own catalog after the major label era. The label also handled the reissues of *Puzzle*, *American Highway Flower*, and *El Subliminoso* with bonus tracks [3][4][5]. This shift from major-label distribution to self-managed release represented a broader industry transition: artists taking control of their own catalogs when the labels that owned their work dissolved or lost interest.

## 5. EPs, Live Albums, and Compilations

### 5.1 Live: Official Bootleg (Vol. 1)

**Label:** Coach House Records | **Released:** 2003

Recorded live at a reunion performance in Santa Ana, California [1]. The "Official Bootleg" framing is itself a Dadaist gesture: legitimizing the illegitimate, making the unofficial official. The recording captured the band's return after the 1999 hiatus and demonstrated that the live power of the trio had survived the gap.

### 5.2 A Friend of Pat Robertson (2006)

**Self-released EP.** New material featuring six tracks: "A Friend of Pat Robertson," "Hours," "Emily Sang To Me," "If Tears Were Balloons," "Letters From Fall," and "Rainbows" [1].

The title track's political commentary continued the thread from "The Fleecing of America" and the politicized passages of *American Highway Flower* -- the band engaging with American culture and politics as ongoing subject matter rather than retreating into purely personal territory.

### 5.3 Limited Edition Vinyl Single (2017)

Two new tracks: "The Bluebird" and "Take Me to the Song" [1]. Released during the 25th anniversary period, these tracks represented the first new studio recordings in over a decade.

### 5.4 Best of the I.R.S. Years

A compilation emphasizing material from the first three albums -- the I.R.S. era that produced the Gold record, the chart hits, and the label collapse [13].

## 7. 7Horse: The Side Project That Hit

In 2011, using studio time originally booked for dada, **Joie Calio** and **Phil Leavitt** recorded a new album together as **7Horse** -- a two-man blues-rock project with Leavitt on drums and lead vocals, Calio on guitars, bass, and vocals [10].

### 7.1 The Scorsese Moment

The project's commercial breakthrough came when **Martin Scorsese** selected their song **"Meth Lab Zoso Sticker"** for his 2013 film *The Wolf of Wall Street*. The track appeared in multiple trailers and on the official soundtrack [10][22]. As Calio noted: "There's a level of validation when Martin Scorsese thinks your song is good enough for his movie" [22].

The song title itself is a miniature essay in American cultural juxtaposition -- meth labs and Led Zeppelin stickers, rural poverty and classic rock aspiration, side by side on the same bumper.

### 7.2 Discography

| Album | Year |
|-------|------|
| *Let The 7Horse Run* | 2011 |
| *Songs For a Voodoo Wedding* | 2014 |
| *Livin' In a Bitch of a World* | 2016 |
| *SUPERFECTA* | 2018 |
| *The Last Resort* | 2022 |

Five albums in eleven years -- a prolific output that exceeded dada's entire studio catalog. 7Horse music also appeared in Ubisoft's *Far Cry 5* (2018) and Amazon's *The Grand Tour* [10].

### 7.3 The Two-Thirds Formula

7Horse is two-thirds of dada without the third. Calio switched from bass to guitar, Leavitt stepped forward from percussion to lead vocals. The roles shifted, the format compressed from trio to duo, the genre moved from California art-rock to stripped-down blues-rock. But the collaborative chemistry between the two musicians remained -- the same intuitive interplay developed across decades of playing together, redirected into a different container.

## 9. The Catalog as Career Map

The discography is not just a list of recordings. It is a map of every systemic force that acts on a working band:

| Album | Year | Label | What Happened |
|-------|------|-------|---------------|
| *Puzzle* | 1992 | I.R.S. | Label functioning. Gold record. |
| *American Highway Flower* | 1994 | I.R.S. | Label on last legs. Tour interrupted. 77K copies. |
| *El Subliminoso* | 1996 | I.R.S. | Label collapsed. No promotion. 36K copies. |
| *dada* | 1998 | MCA | New label, polished production. 25K copies. |
| *How to Be Found* | 2004 | Blue Cave | Self-released MCA leftovers. Catalog reclamation. |

The sales trajectory slopes downward: 1,000,000 to 77,000 to 36,000 to 25,000. But the quality trajectory does not follow the same line. Trouser Press called the fourth album an "artistic rejuvenation" [12]. The best reviews came after the worst sales. The catalog proves that commercial performance and artistic merit are independent variables.

Five albums. Three labels (one collapsed, one departed, one self-founded). Four producers (Ken Scott, Jason Corsaro, Scott Gordon/Adam Weiner, Danny Kortchmar). Over 300 published songs across all projects [1]. One million records sold [1]. The three-part harmony never changed.
