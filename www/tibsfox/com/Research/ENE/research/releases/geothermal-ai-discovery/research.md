## Abstract

If you could drill a perfect geothermal well every time, geothermal would be the cheapest form of power, period -- 5,000-8,000 feet down, hitting 350-400F with high flow rates. Geothermal was the "big dog" of renewables in the late 1970s/early 1980s with more capacity online than wind and solar combined. The industry collapsed twice (1980s oil price crash, 2012 wind/solar cost curves). Zanscar uses custom-built AI and geoscience to discover overlooked geothermal resources tappable with existing drilling technology. A Rhodium Group report estimated geothermal could serve 64% of coming data center power demand.

## Key Findings

### Geothermal's Fundamental Challenge
- Subsurface risk: can drill incredible wells or wells with zero productivity
- "Raisin cookie" model: small, extraordinarily productive pockets scattered in larger matrix
- Must scan large areas for features with small footprints
- Resource productivity uncertain until drilled

### Industry History
- 1960s-80s: boom era, oil majors invested billions (Chevron, Hunt, Phillips, Unocal)
- The Geysers field (California): online since 1960s, America's first production well still operating
- 1980s: oil price crash, oil majors return to core business
- 2008-2012: mini-boom (Obama DOE funding built conceptual frameworks)
- Lost to wind/solar price declines -- couldn't follow the same cost curve
- Current revival: AI/data center demand driving renewed interest

### Zanscar's AI Approach
- Custom-built AI + geoscience for resource discovery
- Unlike other startups: targets resources tappable with existing drilling technology
- Faster path to clean baseload power
- DOE-funded 2008-2012 research enabled current AI-driven approaches

### Geothermal Advantages
- Low land use, low material requirements
- No intermittency -- always-on baseload power
- No sun/wind dependency, no battery storage requirement
- Could serve 64% of data center power demand (Rhodium Group)

### Regulatory Foundation
- 1970 Geothermal Steam Act: essential infrastructure
- Federal government owns majority of Western US land
- Legal framework was prerequisite for development
