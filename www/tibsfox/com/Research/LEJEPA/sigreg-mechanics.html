SIGReg — How the Regularizer Works

LeJEPA Integration · Module 2 · Phase 723 source · v1.49.571 Phase 727

The Problem SIGReg Solves

LeJEPA's Theorem 1 proves that the isotropic Gaussian uniquely minimizes worst-case downstream risk for embeddings. Proving is the easy part; measuring how far an empirical embedding distribution is from an isotropic Gaussian in K dimensions is where everyone had historically gotten stuck. Kernel density estimation suffers the curse of dimensionality. Moment-matching founders on Carleman's 1926 non-identifiability. The field's workaround had been a decade of anti-collapse heuristics (stop-gradient, teacher-student networks, EMA, negative samples) — techniques that work but resist principled explanation.

SIGReg replaces all of that with roughly fifty lines of PyTorch. It does so by factoring the problem through the Hyperspherical Cramér-Wold theorem.

The Cramér-Wold Reduction

Let X, Y be ℝd-valued random vectors. Then ⟨u, X⟩ =d ⟨u, Y⟩ for all u ∈ Sd−1 if and only if X =d Y. — LeJEPA Lemma 3 (Hyperspherical Cramér-Wold)

Comparing two K-dimensional distributions is equivalent to comparing their one-dimensional projections along every unit direction. The every gets softened in practice to enough (sampled uniformly on the sphere), and Theorem 2 of the paper formalizes this into a valid asymptotic statistical test with the expected level-and-power properties.

The Sketching Strategy

Given M random unit directions 𝒜 = {a₁, …, aM}, SIGReg computes: 

SIGReg_T(𝒜, {f_θ(xₙ)}) = (1/|𝒜|) · Σ_{a ∈ 𝒜}  T( {a⊤ f_θ(xₙ)} )

where T is any consistent univariate goodness-of-fit test against the standard one-dimensional Gaussian. The outer operator is an average over directions, not a maximum. This is deliberate: a maximum-over-directions produces sparse gradients (only the worst direction contributes to the backward pass), while the average spreads gradient signal across every sampled direction and trains stably under SGD.

What T Is Not

Moments — rejected by §4.2.1

Failure modeConsequence
Non-identifiability (Theorem 3 + Carleman 1926) Matching the first K moments, for any finite K, does not imply distributional equality. Jarque-Bera extended nails mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis — leaves infinite distributions unconstrained at moments 5+.
Gradient instability The k-th moment's gradient norm scales O(k); its Monte-Carlo variance scales O(k²·m2(k−1)). Pushing k high enough to constrain the tail breaks SGD.

CDFs — rejected by §4.2.2

Failure modeConsequence
Sorting breaks embarrassing parallelism O(N log N) cost + cross-device synchronization. SGD deliberately avoids this; putting it back at the regularizer inverts distributed-training scaling.
Non-differentiable order statistics Cramér-von Mises, Anderson-Darling, Watson, Shapiro-Wilk all need sorted projections. Relaxations (soft sort) add hyperparameters — precisely what LeJEPA aimed to eliminate.
ℓ∞ sparse gradients (Kolmogorov-Smirnov) KS uses a supremum over the CDF difference; only one point contributes to the gradient.

What T Is — Epps-Pulley Empirical Characteristic Function

§4.2.3 settles on the Epps-Pulley test (Epps & Pulley 1983, Biometrika): 

EP = N · ∫  |φ̂_X(t) − φ(t)|²  w(t)  dt,     w(t) = e^{−t²/σ²},   σ=1

where the empirical characteristic function is: 

φ̂_X(t) = (1/n) · Σⱼ e^{i t Xⱼ}

Three load-bearing properties:

  • Linear complexity. O(N) time and memory.
  • Naturally differentiable. The ECF is a sum of smooth functions of the samples; no order statistics, no sorting.
  • Multi-GPU friendly. The ECF is an average, aggregated across devices via a single all_reduce.

And the load-bearing theoretical property: characteristic functions uniquely determine distributions. Matching them gives full distributional identity, not just finite-moment matching.

Defeating the Curse of Dimensionality

A fair objection: the projection reduction does not eliminate dimensionality; it just changes where the dimensionality lives. §4.3 argues that SIGReg's construction defeats the curse because the target (isotropic Gaussian) is smooth in the Sobolev sense. Smoothness coefficients grow rapidly for smooth targets, so a small M suffices to constrain the whole space.

Empirical receipt (Figure 6 of the paper): a 1024-dimensional Gaussian with a perturbation in two coordinates is recovered with M = 10 random directions and N = 100 samples — well inside a single training batch.

The ~50-LOC Reference

The official implementation at rbalestr-lab/lejepa (MIT) factors as: 

import lejepa
univariate_test = lejepa.univariate.EppsPulley(num_points=17)
loss_fn = lejepa.multivariate.SlicingUnivariateTest(
    univariate_test=univariate_test,
    num_slices=1024,
)
loss = loss_fn(embeddings)   # [num_samples, num_dims]
loss.backward()

The single hyperparameter λ balances the JEPA prediction loss against SIGReg: 

L = L_pred + λ · SIGReg(Z)

The paper demonstrates robustness across a wide λ range — the sweet spot is broad, not knife-edge. That robustness is not a corollary of the theory; it's an empirical result the paper reports to justify the "single hyperparameter" claim.
