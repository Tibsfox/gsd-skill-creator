The Machine

ATLAS is one of two general-purpose detectors at the Large Hadron Collider. It sits in a cavern 100 meters underground at Point 1 on the LHC ring, near the CERN main site in Meyrin, Switzerland. It is the largest volume detector ever constructed for a particle collider.

Detector Layers

ATLAS is built like an onion — concentric layers, each measuring different properties of particles produced in collisions. Particles travel outward from the collision point through each layer. What they leave behind (or don’t) tells physicists what they are.

Inner Detector — Tracking

Closest to the collision point. Immersed in a 2 Tesla solenoid magnetic field. Charged particles curve in the field — the curvature measures their momentum, the direction of curvature identifies their charge. Three sub-systems: pixel detector (92 million pixels, closest to beam), semiconductor tracker (SCT), and transition radiation tracker (TRT). Spatial resolution: 10 micrometers.

Electromagnetic Calorimeter — Electrons & Photons

Liquid argon (LAr) sampling calorimeter with lead absorber plates in an accordion geometry. Electrons and photons create electromagnetic showers — cascading pair production and bremsstrahlung. The shower shape and energy deposit identify the particle and measure its energy. The accordion geometry ensures no dead zones. Operating temperature: −183°C (liquid argon boiling point).

Hadronic Calorimeter — Protons, Neutrons, Pions

Tile calorimeter: steel absorber plates interleaved with scintillating tiles. Hadrons (particles made of quarks) create hadronic showers — nuclear interactions that spray secondary particles. The barrel uses iron/scintillator tiles; the endcaps use liquid argon with copper absorbers. Together with the EM calorimeter, these measure the total energy of every particle except muons and neutrinos.

Muon Spectrometer — The Outermost Layer

The defining feature of ATLAS and the reason for its size. Eight superconducting air-core toroid magnets (each 25m long, the largest ever built) create a toroidal magnetic field. Muons — the only charged particles that pass through the calorimeters without stopping — curve in this field. Four types of muon chambers measure their trajectories. The name ATLAS comes from this system: A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS.

Missing Transverse Energy — The Invisible

Neutrinos (and hypothetical dark matter particles) pass through the entire detector without interacting. ATLAS detects them by what’s missing: conservation of momentum means the total transverse momentum of all detected particles should be zero. Any imbalance — missing transverse energy (MET) — signals an invisible particle carried away momentum. This is how ATLAS searches for dark matter.

The Higgs Discovery

On July 4, 2012, the ATLAS and CMS collaborations independently announced the observation of a new particle consistent with the Higgs boson, at a mass of approximately 125 GeV/c². The statistical significance exceeded 5 sigma — a 1-in-3.5-million probability of being a fluctuation.

ATLAS observed the Higgs primarily through two channels:

  • H → γγ (Higgs decaying to two photons) — clean signature in the EM calorimeter, narrow mass peak above smooth background
  • H → ZZ* → 4 leptons (Higgs to four electrons or muons via Z bosons) — the "golden channel," very low background, precise mass measurement

The discovery confirmed the Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism proposed in 1964: a quantum field permeating all space gives mass to fundamental particles through their interaction with it. François Englert and Peter Higgs received the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physics.

The Trigger System

At 40 million bunch crossings per second with ~25 collisions each, ATLAS produces roughly 1 billion events per second. Storing all of them would require ~1 PB/s — impossible. The trigger system decides which events to keep in real time.

Level 1 (hardware)2.5 µs40 MHz~100 kHzCustom ASICs, coarse calorimeter + muon data

The trigger reduces 1 billion events/second to ~1,000 written to storage — a rejection factor of 1 million. Every event that makes it to disk was selected in real time because its physics signature matched patterns worth investigating. The trigger is making a scientific judgment call 40 million times per second.
