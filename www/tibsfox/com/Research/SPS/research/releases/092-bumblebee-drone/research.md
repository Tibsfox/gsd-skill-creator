- **Common name:** Bumblebee drone (collective).

### Sound Production Mechanism

The bumblebee produces sound through **wing vibration** [1, 2]:

- **Flight buzz:** Wings beat approximately 130-240 times per second during flight, producing a deep, resonant tone. The large, densely furred body acts as a resonating chamber, amplifying and enriching the sound.
- **Buzz pollination (sonication):** Flight muscles vibrate at specific frequencies (130-400 Hz) *without moving the wings*, transmitting vibrations directly to the flower. This releases pollen from anthers that require sonication [1, 2].
- **Defensive buzz:** Higher-frequency buzz when threatened, producing a more intense sound.

This makes the bumblebee the **second wing-produced sound** in the engine (after mosquito at 91) but introduces a fundamentally new concept: **sound as ecological tool** (buzz pollination).

## THE SOUND: DEEP RESONANT BUZZ -- 93rd ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Sound

The bumblebee drone is a **deep, resonant buzz** [1, 2]:

**Individual sound:** A single bumblebee in flight produces a warm, full buzz at approximately 130-240 Hz. The sound is clearly audible at several meters -- much louder and richer than the mosquito's thin whine.

**Frequency:** 130-240 Hz (flight buzz). Much lower than the mosquito's 400-600 Hz. Deeper = warmer, richer, more resonant.

**Why deeper and richer?** The bumblebee's body is larger (10-25 mm vs 3-6 mm for mosquitoes) and densely furred. The body acts as a **resonating chamber** -- the fur and body cavity amplify and enrich the wing vibration's harmonics.

**Buzz pollination:** During sonication, the buzz intensifies. The bumblebee grabs the flower and vibrates its flight muscles at 130-400 Hz without moving its wings [1, 2]. The vibrations shake pollen loose from the flower's anthers. This is the first sound in the engine that **performs ecological work**.

### Why E=3

1. **Individual bumblebees are clearly audible** at several meters -- larger body, louder sound.
2. **The buzz is warm and resonant** -- not thin or whining but full and rich.
3. **Multiple bumblebees** in a wildflower meadow create a warm, ambient hum.
4. **Buzz pollination** produces intensified sound during flower visits.
5. **E=3 reflects the warm, present, filling quality** of the meadow buzz.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Ultra-thin hum cloud** (degree 91, mosquito) -- thin, high-pitched, barely audible, E=2. The deep resonant buzz is the opposite: thick, low-pitched, clearly audible, E=3. Two wing-sounds, two characters. Thin whine vs deep buzz.
- **Electric buzzing throb** (degree 89, cicada) -- loud, harsh, electric. The deep resonant buzz is warm and organic. Both loud but different timbres.
- **Rhythmic chirp** (degree 87, cricket) -- rhythmic, structured. The deep resonant buzz is continuous and unstructured but warm.

## ECOLOGY: THE WILDFLOWER MEADOW HUM

### Habitat

Wildflower meadow [1, 2]:

- **Primary:** Open meadows with diverse flowering plants -- alpine and subalpine meadows, valley floors, garden edges, forest clearings.
- **Character:** The brightest and most diverse habitat in the engine. Full of color, scent, and buzzing life.
- **Connection to marmot (75):** Both in mountain/subalpine meadows. Overlapping habitats.
- **Connection to golden-mantled ground squirrel (79):** Both in meadow/edge habitats. Both diurnal.

### Buzz Pollination Ecology

Some PNW wildflowers require buzz pollination [1, 2]:

- **Huckleberry** (*Vaccinium* spp.) -- the iconic PNW berry. Requires sonication for optimal pollination.
- **Blueberry** -- related to huckleberry. Buzz-pollinated.
- **Shooting star** (*Dodecatheon*) -- PNW native wildflower. Buzz-pollinated.
- **Tomato** -- agricultural crop. Buzz-pollinated.

Bumblebees are among the few pollinators capable of sonication. The bumblebee's sound is not just an acoustic phenomenon but an **ecological service** that sustains the wildflower meadow and the agricultural systems that depend on it.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Nectar and pollen from wildflowers. Generalist forager visiting many flower species.
- **Predators:** Birds (especially bee-eaters, flycatchers), dragonflies, spiders, crab spiders (ambush predators on flowers), parasitic flies, badgers (dig up nests).
- **Threats:** Habitat loss, pesticides, climate change, pathogen spillover from managed honeybees [1, 2].
