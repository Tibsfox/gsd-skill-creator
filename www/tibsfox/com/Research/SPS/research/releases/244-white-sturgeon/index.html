FIRST ACIPENSERIDAE — 200+ MILLION YEARS — LARGEST FRESHWATER FISH IN NORTH AMERICA

Acipenser transmontanus. The White Sturgeon. The largest freshwater fish in North America: up to 6 meters (20 feet), over 600 kg (1,300 lbs). Individual fish live for 100+ years. The sturgeon family dates to the Mesozoic Era — early dinosaurs swam in the same waters. Five rows of bony scutes. Cartilaginous skeleton. No teeth. Sensory barbels. The Columbia River hosts the world's most important population. E=4 — the boom of a large sturgeon breaching the surface, landing flat on the river, the sound traveling half a mile.

ANCIENT FISH ARC: DEGREES 243-244. Pacific Lamprey (243, Petromyzontidae, 450 MYA, E=1) + White Sturgeon (244, Acipenseridae, 200 MYA, E=4). Two consecutive living fossils. Two ancient lineages. Both have survived every mass extinction since the Paleozoic. Both are sacred First Foods species. Both have declined severely from historical abundance.

The Living Fossil

White Sturgeon have a cartilaginous skeleton (like sharks, not bony fish). Five rows of bony scutes — armored plates — run along the body instead of scales. A heterocercal tail where the spine extends into the upper lobe (a primitive character shared with sharks). A flattened rostrum with four sensory barbels on the underside, used to detect prey on the river bottom. The body plan visible in Cretaceous fossils is essentially the same as the fish in the Columbia River today.

Size, Longevity, and the Century-Scale Life

North America's Largest Freshwater Fish

Maximum length: ~6.1 m (20 feet) — verified historical records
Maximum weight: 590-630 kg (1,300-1,400 lbs) verified; some historical accounts claim 816 kg (1,800 lbs)
Maximum age: 100+ years (reliably aged via fin-spine ring counts)
Sexual maturity: Females: 15-25 years. Males: 10-20 years.
Spawning frequency: Females spawn every 2-5 years after reaching maturity.

A 100-year-old White Sturgeon was born in 1926. It has lived through the construction of Bonneville Dam (1938), the destruction of Celilo Falls (1957), and the entire industrialization of the Columbia River — all within a single fish's lifetime.

The Columbia River Population

Population estimate

Several hundred thousand fish across all size classes in the Columbia River system. World's most abundant wild White Sturgeon population.

Bonneville reservoir sport fishery

Most-studied, most-fished reach. Catch-and-release + size slot harvest. Generates millions in annual economic activity.

Reservoir fragmentation

Each dam pool is an isolated population. Upper Columbia and Snake River populations severely depleted. Below Hell's Canyon Dam on Snake: functionally absent above.

Celilo Falls (third reference)

Pacific Salmon (236) + Pacific Lamprey (243) + White Sturgeon (244): all three had tribal fishing traditions destroyed by The Dalles Dam in 1957. One dam; three species; irreplaceable cultural losses.

Conservation

Globally, sturgeons are the most threatened fish family: 50%+ of 27 species are critically endangered. The Columbia River White Sturgeon is a conservation success story by comparison — but faces climate change (water temperature), contaminant bioaccumulation, and upper-river depletion. The Columbia River population is intensively managed with size-slot regulations and creel limits to prevent recurrence of the 19th-century commercial overfishing collapse.
