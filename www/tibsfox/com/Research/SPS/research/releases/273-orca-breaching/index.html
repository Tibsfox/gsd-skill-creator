The Physics of the Breach

~165 dB

Mass: Adult male SRKW, 5,400–6,000 kg (approximately six tons). Approach velocity: 17–26 km/h. Maximum height: 3–5 meters above surface for a large male. Impact: Lateral body-slam, full body mass returning to water surface. Sound: ~165 decibels at close range — louder than a rifle shot at the shooter’s position. Propagates both in air (audible kilometers away in calm conditions) and underwater (speed 1,500 m/s, reaches other pods). The white water plume is visible from kilometers away.

Why Do Orcas Breach?

The function of orca breaching is an open question in cetacean behavioral research. Multiple hypotheses have support, and current evidence suggests the behavior is likely multifunctional:

Communication: The breach impact produces underwater sound detectable by other pods at distance. In a highly social, matrilineal species with distinct pod dialects, long-range acoustic signaling is functionally important. Ectoparasite removal: The water-entry shear force may dislodge barnacles and copepods from the skin. Play and social bonding: Breaching is more frequent in socially excited contexts; it is contagious — one animal breaches, others follow. Sensory exploration: The moment of being three to five meters above the surface provides a sensory perspective unavailable from the water. The caloric cost is too high for a purely casual behavior; the social context suggests motivations beyond purely acoustic function.

Southern Residents — ~73 Individuals — ESA Endangered Since 2005

J, K, and L pods. Approximately 73 individuals as of recent surveys. Population peaked at ~98 in 1995. Three primary threats: (1) Chinook salmon decline — SRKWs eat fish, not mammals; they depend on Chinook as primary prey. Cross-reference: Chinook salmon at degree 237. (2) Vessel noise — commercial shipping, ferries, whale-watching vessels interfere with echolocation and communication. (3) Contaminants — PCBs and PBDEs bioaccumulate to levels among the highest measured in any marine mammal globally. These animals are culturally sophisticated: pod dialects, multi-generational knowledge transfer, named individuals recognized across decades.
