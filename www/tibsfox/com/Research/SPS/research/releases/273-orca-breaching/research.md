## THE PHYSICS OF THE BREACH

### Six Tons Fully Airborne

The orca breach is an exercise in applied physics that demands precise description:

- **The animal's mass.** Adult male Southern Resident Killer Whales average approximately 5,400-6,000 kg (roughly 6 tons). Adult females average approximately 3,600-4,500 kg (roughly 4 tons). The breach documented here represents an adult or sub-adult animal -- for maximum E=9 impact, a large male is the reference case. Six tons is not an abstraction; it is the weight of a medium-sized truck.
- **The velocity required.** For an orca to clear the water surface completely -- all or nearly all of its body airborne -- it must approach the surface at high velocity and angle upward steeply. Estimates of breach approach velocity range from 11 to 16 mph (17-26 km/h). The animal accelerates from depth, angles upward, and uses its tail (fluke) thrust to generate the vertical force needed to clear the surface.
- **The trajectory.** A full breach involves the animal leaving the water at a steep angle, rotating or twisting in the air (often laterally), reaching maximum height of approximately 3-5 meters above the surface for a large male, and then returning. The return is where E=9 is generated: the lateral crash, the body-slam onto the surface, the massive detonation of water.
- **The impact.** When six tons of orca strikes the water surface laterally -- the body-slam that characterizes the breach rather than a head-first or tail-first entry -- the impact is both acoustic (approximately 165 dB) and visual (massive plume of white water visible from kilometers away). The sound propagates underwater as well as in air.
- **The caloric cost.** A full breach by a large adult orca expends significant calories. This is not a casual behavior. The animal is investing real energy in whatever function the breach serves.

## WHY DO ORCAS BREACH?

### The Communicative and Behavioral Functions Debate

The function of orca breaching is one of the more interesting open questions in cetacean behavioral research:

- **Communication hypothesis.** The breach impact produces sound that travels both through the air and through the water. Other orca pods may detect the sound at significant distances through underwater sound propagation. Breaching may serve as a long-range acoustic signal -- announcing presence, location, or behavioral state to other pods. Southern Residents are a highly social species organized in multi-generational matrilineal pods; long-range communication is functionally important.
- **Ectoparasite removal hypothesis.** Breaching may help dislodge parasites from the skin surface. The impact of the water-entry creates considerable shear force on the skin -- sufficient to remove barnacles, copepods, and other ectoparasites. The energy expenditure makes sense if this is the function: better to expend calories on one breach than to carry a parasite load.
- **Play and social bonding hypothesis.** Breaching is more frequent in contexts involving multiple individuals, younger animals, and what researchers describe as socially excited states. Orcas may breach as a form of play, social display, or excitement expression. In pods, breaching can be contagious -- one animal breaches, others follow. This social contagion pattern is consistent with play.
- **Sensory exploration hypothesis.** The moment of being fully airborne gives the animal a different sensory perspective than its underwater experience. What an orca perceives from three meters above the surface -- aerial view of the environment, atmospheric stimuli, visual range beyond the water's refractive limits -- is genuinely different from its normal sensory experience. Breaching may be a form of environmental exploration.
- **No single function confirmed.** Current research supports the view that breaching is likely multifunctional -- serving different purposes in different contexts, or multiple purposes simultaneously. The behavior is too energetically costly to be explained solely by play, yet the contexts suggest motivations beyond purely acoustic communication.

(Sources: Ford, J.K.B. orca behavioral research; Noren et al. orca biomechanics; breaching function hypotheses; cetacean ectoparasite research; social contagion in orca behavior; multifunctional behavior analysis)

## SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALES: THE ANIMALS

### The Specific Population

- **J, K, and L pods.** The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKWs) are organized in three pods: J pod (roughly 23 individuals), K pod (roughly 17 individuals), and L pod (roughly 33 individuals). Total population as of recent surveys: approximately 73 individuals. This is a small, isolated, endangered population.
- **Endangered since 2005.** The SRKW population was listed under the Endangered Species Act in 2005. Population peaked at approximately 98 individuals in 1995 and has declined significantly since. The population hovers around 73-75 individuals, with periodic births and deaths.
- **Why they are endangered.** Three primary threats: (1) **Chinook salmon decline** -- SRKWs are fish-eating (as opposed to mammal-eating transient orcas); they depend on Chinook salmon as their primary prey. Chinook populations in the Salish Sea and Columbia River system have declined due to dam impacts, habitat loss, and ocean conditions. (2) **Vessel noise** -- the acoustic noise produced by commercial shipping, ferries, recreational vessels, and whale-watching boats interferes with orca echolocation and communication. (3) **Contaminants** -- persistent organic pollutants (PCBs, PBDEs) accumulated in prey fish bioaccumulate in orca tissue. SRKWs have among the highest measured contaminant loads of any marine mammal globally.
- **Cultural behaviors.** SRKWs are culturally sophisticated. They have dialects -- each pod has distinct vocalizations not shared with other pods. They teach skills to offspring. They have named individuals who are recognized over decades by researchers. Breaching is a cultural behavior: it occurs in specific social contexts, is more common in certain individuals and pods, and may be learned.
- **Cross-reference: degree 237.** Chinook salmon was documented at degree 237 in the SPS engine. The Chinook-orca dependency link is a critical cross-reference: the salmon's decline (degree 237) is the primary driver of the SRKW population crisis (degree 273-276). These two species are directly connected in the engine's structure.

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE: E=9

### What the Breach Sounds Like

- **The approach.** Before the breach is heard, it is often seen: the animal breaking the surface at high velocity, angling upward, the spray from the initial exit. The first sound is a rush of water displacement as the animal exits the surface.
- **The airborne moment.** In the air, the orca produces no vocalizations during the breach -- this is a behavioral and acoustic observation. The breach itself is not a vocal event; the sound comes from the water interaction.
- **The impact.** The body-slam: the lateral crash of the animal's full body mass onto the water surface. This is E=9. The impact produces a detonation -- a percussive shockwave in both air and water. The underwater component propagates at the speed of sound in water (approximately 1,500 m/s), reaching significant distances. The air component propagates at the speed of sound in air (approximately 343 m/s), audible at distances of kilometers in calm conditions.
- **The aftermath.** The white water plume persists for several seconds after the impact. The underwater sound continues to propagate. In calm water, the rings of the impact wave spread visibly across the surface for hundreds of meters.
- **165 dB.** At close range (within approximately 10 meters), the breach impact is estimated at approximately 165 decibels. This is louder than a thunder clap at close range (120-130 dB). At 100 meters, the level drops significantly but remains remarkable. At 1 kilometer, still detectable.

(Sources: Orca breach acoustic measurements; underwater acoustic propagation; air acoustic propagation; breach impact energy estimates; decibel scale and distance attenuation)
