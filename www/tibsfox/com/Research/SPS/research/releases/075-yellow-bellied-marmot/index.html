The Yellow-bellied Marmot: Mountain Whistler

The Yellow-bellied Marmot is one of the Pacific Northwest’s most charismatic alpine mammals — a large, stocky ground squirrel with a distinctive yellow-orange belly patch that lives in colonies on rocky slopes and talus fields at mid-to-high elevations. Weighing 1.6–5 kg (3.5–11 lbs), the marmot is the largest squirrel-family member in the PNW and one of the most vocal. Its piercing whistle alarm call carries across entire mountain valleys, earning it the nickname “whistle pig.”

This is the engine’s second alpine species after the American Pika (degree 74). Where the Pika is tiny (120–185 g), solitary-territorial, and makes a sharp “eenk” bark, the marmot is large (up to 5 kg), colonial, and produces a loud, sustained whistle. Both inhabit rocky slopes. Both are diurnal. But the marmot is the engine’s first colonial mammal — living in family groups of 10–20 individuals, with a dominant male, multiple females, and their offspring.

The Piercing Whistle — 76th Acoustic Element

The Yellow-bellied Marmot’s piercing whistle alarm is one of the loudest mammalian vocalizations in the PNW relative to habitat. A single, sustained, high-pitched whistle — approximately 2,000–6,000 Hz, lasting 0.5–2 seconds — that carries across open rocky terrain for 300–500 meters. E=5 — the whistle is LOUD, impossible to miss, and functions as a colony-wide alarm. Hikers in alpine areas hear the marmot whistle before they see the animal. The whistle pig earns its name.

Taxonomy: Marmota flaviventris

Etymology

Marmota — from the Latin mus montanus (“mountain mouse”), through French marmotte. The genus encompasses approximately 15 species of large ground squirrels across the Northern Hemisphere. flaviventris — from Latin flavus (“yellow”) + ventris (“belly”). “Yellow-bellied” — describing the diagnostic yellow-orange ventral patch.

Classification

  • Class: Mammalia (14th mammalian entry; 12th mammalian species)
  • Order: Rodentia — RETURNS. Second rodent family after Castoridae (Beaver, 61). Rodentia is the largest mammalian order (~2,500 species). The marmot is NOT a lagomorph — Rodentia and Lagomorpha diverged ~80–90 million years ago.
  • Family: Sciuridae — NEW FAMILY (35th). The squirrel family. ~300 species worldwide: tree squirrels, ground squirrels, chipmunks, prairie dogs, marmots, flying squirrels. First Sciuridae in the engine.
  • Genus: Marmota — ~15 species worldwide. Three in North America: M. monax (Woodchuck), M. flaviventris (Yellow-bellied), M. caligata (Hoary Marmot).
  • Species: M. flaviventris — distributed from British Columbia south through the western mountains to New Mexico and California. Rocky slopes, meadows, talus.

Rodentia Returns: Beaver to Marmot

The Beaver (degree 61, Castoridae) was the engine’s first rodent. The Yellow-bellied Marmot (degree 75, Sciuridae) is the second. Same order, different families — Castoridae and Sciuridae diverged approximately 50–60 million years ago. The Beaver is aquatic, the marmot is alpine. The Beaver builds lodges, the marmot digs burrows. The Beaver is nocturnal/crepuscular, the marmot is strictly diurnal. Rodentia now spans from river to summit.

First Colonial Mammal — Family Group Living

The Yellow-bellied Marmot is the engine’s first colonial mammal — living in family groups of 10–20 individuals. A dominant male maintains a harem of 2–4 females. Yearlings and juveniles form the colony’s lower tiers. The colony shares burrow systems, sentinels warn the group with whistle alarms, and individuals groom and play together. This is social living at a complexity level not yet seen in the engine’s mammals. The Pika (74) is territorial and solitary. The marmot is gregarious and communal.

Physical Description: The Alpine Heavyweight

Body: Stocky, robust, heavy-set. Total length 470–670 mm (18.5–26.4 inches), including a bushy tail of 130–220 mm. Weight varies dramatically with season: 1.6–2.5 kg at spring emergence, fattening to 3.5–5 kg by late summer before hibernation. The marmot enters hibernation at peak mass and emerges at minimum mass — losing 40–50% of body weight during 7–8 months of dormancy.

Pelage: Grizzled brown-gray on the dorsum, with the diagnostic yellow-orange belly patch that gives the species its name. White patches between the eyes (frontal blaze) and buff-colored cheeks. Fur is dense but not as exceptionally thick as the Pika’s — the marmot does not need winter insulation because it hibernates through the cold months.

Feet and claws: Strong, curved claws adapted for digging burrows in rocky soil. The marmot is an active burrower — unlike the Pika, which uses natural talus interstices. Marmot burrows can extend 2–5 meters into hillsides with multiple entrances.

Hibernation: Seven to Eight Months Underground

The Yellow-bellied Marmot hibernates for 7–8 months — one of the longest hibernation periods of any North American mammal. Hibernation typically begins in September–October and ends in April–May, depending on elevation and snowpack. During hibernation, the marmot’s body temperature drops from ~37°C to as low as 5°C, heart rate slows from ~200 bpm to ~30 bpm, and respiration drops to one breath every several minutes.

The hibernation contrast with the Pika (74) is stark: the Pika does NOT hibernate, remaining active year-round beneath the snowpack and feeding on stored hay. The marmot does NOT store food — it fattens intensively in late summer and survives entirely on stored body fat through winter. Two alpine mammals, two opposite winter strategies: hay storage (Pika) versus fat storage (marmot).

Behavioral Ecology: The Sentinel System

The marmot colony operates a sentinel system — one or more individuals perch on prominent boulders scanning for predators while others forage in nearby meadows. When a sentinel detects a threat (hawk, eagle, coyote, fox, weasel), it delivers the piercing whistle alarm. The entire colony responds: individuals nearest burrow entrances dive underground; those farther away freeze, then run for cover. The whistle is graded — different call patterns for aerial versus terrestrial predators.

Predators: Red Fox (SPS-071), Short-tailed Weasel (SPS-067), American Marten (SPS-065), hawks, eagles, coyotes. The marmot’s large body size provides some defense — a 4–5 kg marmot is a difficult target for a weasel but an attractive meal for a fox or eagle.

The Lonely Forest Parallel

Degree 75 pairs the Yellow-bellied Marmot with The Lonely Forest in Part A — Indie Pop, E=4, Anacortes, TLE. Both are loud and communal — The Lonely Forest creates anthemic singalongs, the marmot creates colony-wide alarm choruses. Both are the second act from the same place — The Lonely Forest is the second Anacortes band (after Elverum 71), the marmot is the second alpine species (after Pika 74). Both are colonial/communal — the anthem invites the crowd to join, the whistle warns the colony. Both are high-energy — E=4 (Lonely Forest) and E=5 (marmot). The anthem and the whistle: different frequencies, same communal impulse.
