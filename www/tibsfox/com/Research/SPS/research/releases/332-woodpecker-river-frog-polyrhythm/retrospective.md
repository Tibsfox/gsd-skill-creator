## What Worked

1. **The three-stream framework is analytically complete and scientifically grounded.** Each stream is driven by an entirely different physical mechanism: biological motor (woodpecker), fluid dynamics (river), temperature-dependent neural oscillation (frog). The zero-coupling demonstration -- no possible synchronization mechanism between three such different physical systems -- is the entry's core scientific contribution.
2. **The frequency quantification of all three streams is specific and accurate.** Woodpecker: 0.17-0.25 Hz sequence rate, 17-25 Hz roll. River: 0.5-3 Hz standing wave. Frog: 1-3 Hz chorus AM rate. All three in approximately the same macro-scale frequency range but completely uncorrelated. This is the polyrhythm documented numerically.
3. **The old-growth ecological health certificate concept is the entry's most important ecological contribution.** All three streams require specific ecological conditions. All three present simultaneously = riparian old-growth intact. The polyrhythm is a diagnostic tool as well as an acoustic event.
4. **The Pileated Woodpecker as old-growth indicator is well-documented.** The snag requirement (50+ cm diameter) ties the woodpecker's presence directly to forest maturity. The drumming certifies the ecosystem.
5. **The ribbit origin (Pacific Tree Frog as the world's most widely heard Pacific Northwest wildlife sound) is an unexpected and well-documented finding.** Most people who know the ribbit sound don't know it comes from a 1-5 cm frog from the Pacific Coast.

## Discoveries

1. **The spring phenological pulse is the organizing principle of the polyrhythm.** Three processes that peak at different absolute times in different years and locations all peak in the same 3-6 week window (April-May) in riparian old-growth. The spring pulse synchronizes the phenological triggers while the rhythms themselves remain independent. The spring causes all three to be present; it does not cause them to beat together.
2. **The river's tempo is the only one of the three that changes in real-time during the acoustic event.** The woodpecker's biological motor produces a relatively consistent rhythm within a session. The frog's call rate changes with temperature over the course of a day but not rapidly. The river's standing wave oscillation changes with discharge, which changes with precipitation and solar heating continuously. The river is the most dynamic rhythmic partner.
3. **The Elwha River post-dam-removal restoration is the Pacific Northwest's most significant ongoing experiment in riparian old-growth ecological restoration.** With the Elwha and Glines Canyon dams removed (2011-2014), the Elwha River is recovering its natural hydrological dynamics, its floodplain habitat, and its riparian forest. The polyrhythm of degree 332 may be documented there within decades as the riparian ecosystem matures.
