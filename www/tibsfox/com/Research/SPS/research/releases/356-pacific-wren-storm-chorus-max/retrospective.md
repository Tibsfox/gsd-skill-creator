## What Worked

1. **The "output-to-capacity ratio, not absolute scale" E=10 principle is the central achievement of this entry.** The Pacific Wren storm-chorus is objectively the smallest-scale E=10 in the engine. If E=10 were calibrated by absolute volume or absolute mass, this entry would earn E=6 at most. But E=10 is calibrated by the ratio: the Pacific Wren at maximum output fills its acoustic environment as completely as an orca fills the Salish Sea. The 3-5 hectare old-growth stand is the wren's world, and the wren fills it completely. This calibration principle is the most important observation in the entry.
2. **The storm amplification mechanism is both factually correct and acoustically interesting.** Wet vegetation increases acoustic reflectivity. The rain's white noise (lower-frequency dominant from raindrop impact) creates a spectral backdrop that makes the wren's high-frequency, rapidly changing song stand out with greater definition. The storm doesn't silence the wren -- it triggers the wren and amplifies it simultaneously. Both factors operative together is the correct explanation.
3. **The closing sequence arc (354: exterior detonation → 355: foundational emergence → 356: intimate interior) is the most important structural observation.** The engine's closing sequence deliberately moves from the largest exterior events toward the smallest interior ones. The intimacy is not a reduction -- it is a deepening. Degree 356 is the bottom of the intimate register before the sequence begins its final arc toward silence.

## Discoveries

1. **The countersinging behavior -- singing more intensively during storms -- is the most interesting behavioral observation.** This is not fully understood mechanistically but is documented. The interpretation: during storms, normal acoustic monitoring of territory (hearing neighboring males, being heard by them) is compromised. The territorial male responds by increasing vocal output to maintain presence under adverse conditions. This is the bird doing exactly what The Fartz was doing in Capitol Hill clubs: filling the available acoustic space at maximum output regardless of external conditions.
2. **The sequential ignition mechanism for the ten-wren simultaneous chorus is the most interesting acoustic observation.** The ten birds don't sing simultaneously by coincidence. A stimulus triggers one or two; their singing triggers the adjacent territory holders; within seconds, all ten are singing. The "simultaneous" is the end state of a rapid propagation. The propagation itself -- from one bird to ten in seconds -- is the acoustic event's genesis.
