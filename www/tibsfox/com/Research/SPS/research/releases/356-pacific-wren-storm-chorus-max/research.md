## THE PACIFIC WREN -- THE BIRD

### Ten Grams. The Highest Volume-to-Body-Mass Ratio in the Pacific Northwest.

The Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*) is among the smallest birds in the Pacific Northwest: adult body mass approximately 9-11 grams (roughly equivalent to four U.S. pennies). It was treated as a subspecies of the Winter Wren (*Troglodytes troglodytes*) until 2010, when it was elevated to full species status based on genetic and acoustic differences from the eastern North American and Eurasian populations.

The Pacific Wren is a bird of old-growth and mature forest understory. It is closely associated with the specific microhabitats of western Washington and Oregon forests: dense fern understory beneath western hemlock and Sitka spruce, fallen nurse logs, root tangles, stream-edge vegetation. It is a year-round resident in the Pacific Northwest, though it descends to lower elevations in winter when deep snow covers the high-elevation breeding habitat.

The song: the Pacific Wren's song is among the most complex of any North American bird relative to its body size. A single male produces a song lasting 5-10 seconds that includes approximately 36 separate notes or trills in rapid succession. The song is delivered at maximum volume for the bird's size -- typically measured at 80-100 dB at one meter from the source. For comparison, a bird weighing 10 grams producing a 90 dB song is generating acoustic output that would require a proportionally enormous instrument from a larger animal. The volume-to-body-mass ratio of the Pacific Wren song is among the highest documented for any bird species.

The song function: territorial defense and mate attraction. Male Pacific Wrens defend territories in old-growth forest year-round, singing most intensively in spring and early summer but maintaining acoustic presence throughout the breeding and non-breeding seasons. In winter and during storm periods, wren song may actually increase in intensity -- the bird singing against the acoustic backdrop of storm noise in a behavior sometimes described as countersinging the storm.

(Sources: Pacific Wren Troglodytes pacificus 9-11 grams; elevated to species 2010 from Winter Wren subspecies; old-growth understory specialist; western hemlock Sitka spruce association; fern understory root tangles nurse logs; year-round resident; song 5-10 seconds ~36 notes trills; 80-100 dB at 1 meter; volume-to-body-mass highest documented; territorial and mate-attraction function; countersinging storm behavior in winter)

## THE OLD-GROWTH HABITAT -- THE ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT

### Olympic Peninsula and Coast Range. Wet Old-Growth. The Acoustic Cathedral.

The Pacific Wren's preferred habitat in the Pacific Northwest is wet old-growth and mature forest: the coastal hemlock-spruce forests of the Olympic Peninsula, the western Cascades old-growth stands, and the Coast Range of Oregon. These forests have specific acoustic properties that amplify and shape the wren's song:

**Canopy structure.** Old-growth western hemlock and Sitka spruce create a multi-layered canopy of enormous height and density. Sound produced in the understory is reflected by multiple canopy layers and the dense lateral vegetation before escaping the forest. The result is an acoustic environment with high reverberation -- sounds persist longer, travel farther within the forest, and layer over themselves as the reflected signal returns. The wren's complex, rapidly changing song is designed (through evolutionary pressure) to function in this reverberant environment.

**Nurse logs and root tangles.** The old-growth understory is not an open forest floor. It is a dense matrix of fallen logs (nurse logs), root wads, fern mats, and dense shrub vegetation. The wren moves through this matrix, singing from perches at various heights within the structure. The acoustic scattering of the nurse log/root wad matrix creates a micro-acoustic environment where the wren's song diffracts around and through the obstacles, reaching listeners from multiple directions simultaneously.

**Rain and storm amplification.** Wet vegetation has a different acoustic reflectivity than dry vegetation. In a rainstorm, the understory vegetation -- ferns, mosses, shrubs -- becomes a continuous reflective surface that increases the reverberation of the acoustic environment. The wren's song, already designed for the reverberant old-growth interior, gains additional acoustic complexity in rain. The specific spectral qualities of rain (broadband white noise, concentration of energy at lower frequencies from raindrop impact on soil and leaf litter) create a backdrop against which the wren's high-frequency, complex song stands out with greater definition.

(Sources: Olympic Peninsula wet old-growth; western Cascades old-growth; Coast Range Oregon; canopy structure reverberation multi-layered reflections; nurse logs root wads fern mats understory matrix; acoustic scattering from obstacles; rain wet vegetation reflectivity increase; raindrop white noise backdrop; wren song high frequency stands out against lower-frequency rain backdrop)

## TEN WRENS -- THE MAXIMUM

### Why Ten. Why Simultaneously. Why Storm.

The specific event at degree 356 is ten Pacific Wrens singing simultaneously in wet old-growth during a storm. Why these specific parameters?

**Why ten.** In optimal Pacific Northwest old-growth habitat, Pacific Wren territory density can reach 2-4 territories per hectare. In the densest old-growth habitat (the most structurally complex old-growth stands on the Olympic Peninsula), territory density may be higher. A stand of optimal old-growth with ten singing males would cover approximately 3-5 hectares -- a forest stand entirely within acoustic range of each bird of every other bird. Ten singing males simultaneously would mean that the entire forest stand is acoustically occupied: every point within the stand is within hearing distance of at least one to three singing birds simultaneously.

**Why simultaneously.** Male Pacific Wrens do not sing in formal coordination (unlike some thrush species with dawn chorus timing). They sing when territory pressure dictates -- which means when neighboring males sing, territory holders respond. In a 3-5 hectare stand with 10 territories, a stimulus (a storm, a predator alarm, a period of high atmospheric pressure) that triggers singing in one or two birds propagates: the singing of the first males triggers the territorial responses of adjacent males, creating a sequential ignition that within seconds has all ten males singing. The effect: simultaneous, wall-to-wall song filling the entire stand.

**Why storm.** Two factors: (1) Countersinging behavior -- male Pacific Wrens have been documented singing more intensively during storms and high wind, in what appears to be a territorial maintenance behavior under conditions where normal acoustic monitoring is compromised. (2) Acoustic amplification -- wet vegetation, as described above, increases the reverberation of the acoustic environment, giving the ten singing males an acoustic presence that exceeds their dry-conditions output. The storm does not silence the wrens -- it triggers them and amplifies them simultaneously.

**The result.** Ten wrens (100 grams total, combined, ten individual 10-gram birds), in 3-5 hectares of wet old-growth, during a storm, all singing simultaneously at 80-100 dB each, reverberating through the multi-layered old-growth canopy and wet understory vegetation. The acoustic output per unit of biological mass represented is the maximum in the engine. This is why the engine designates it the most intimate E=10: the scale is a single forest stand; the source is 100 grams total; the output fills the entire acoustic environment completely.
