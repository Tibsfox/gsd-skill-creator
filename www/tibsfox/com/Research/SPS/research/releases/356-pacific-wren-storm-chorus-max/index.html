The Bird

The Pacific Wren (Troglodytes pacificus) weighs approximately 9–11 grams. Elevated to full species status in 2010. A bird of old-growth and mature forest understory: dense fern mats, nurse logs, root tangles, stream-edge vegetation beneath western hemlock and Sitka spruce. Year-round resident in the Pacific Northwest.

A single male, 10 grams, produces a 5–10 second song with approximately 36 separate notes or trills in rapid succession, at 80–100 dB at one meter. The highest volume-to-body-mass ratio of any Pacific Northwest bird.

Song function: territorial defense and mate attraction. Male wrens defend territories year-round, singing most intensively in spring but maintaining acoustic presence throughout the year. During storms and high wind, wren song may intensify — the bird countersinging the storm, maintaining territorial presence under conditions where normal monitoring is compromised.

The Old-Growth Acoustic Environment

Canopy reverberation. Old-growth western hemlock and Sitka spruce create a multi-layered canopy of enormous height and density. Sound produced in the understory reflects off multiple canopy layers before escaping. High reverberation: sounds persist longer, travel farther within the forest, layer over reflected signals. The wren’s complex, rapidly changing song is designed for exactly this environment.

Nurse log scattering. The old-growth understory is a dense matrix of nurse logs, root wads, fern mats, and shrub vegetation. The wren’s song diffracts around and through these obstacles, reaching listeners from multiple directions simultaneously.

Storm amplification. Wet vegetation increases acoustic reflectivity. The rain’s broadband white noise (dominated by lower frequencies from raindrop impact) creates a backdrop against which the wren’s high-frequency, complex song stands out with greater definition. The storm amplifies the song acoustically while triggering its intensity behaviorally.
