### A New Family, A New World

After six consecutive Scolopacidae (168-173) -- all shorebirds, all tidal flat and marsh dwellers, all probing or sweeping bills -- the engine shifts fundamentally at degree 174:

| Family | Degrees | Habitat | Feeding |

| **Alcidae** | **174-?** | **Rocky coast, open ocean** | **Wing-propelled diving** |

The transition is total:
- **From shore to sea.** Scolopacidae feed along the waterline. Alcidae feed underwater.
- **From walking to diving.** Scolopacidae walk and probe. Alcidae dive and swim.
- **From bills designed for mud to bills designed for fish.** Scolopacidae bills are long and specialized for substrate probing. Alcidae bills are compact and designed for catching and holding fish.
- **From loud to quiet.** The Scolopacidae streak featured loud, carrying calls (E=3 to E=5). The Pigeon Guillemot is quiet -- E=3. A whisper after a shout.

## E=3 -- THE QUIET WHISTLE

### Why E=3

The Pigeon Guillemot registers at E=3:

- **Thin whistle:** The call is a thin, high-pitched, reedy whistle or trill. It does not carry far. It is given at close range near nest sites, not broadcast across open water.
- **Context-specific:** The call is primarily given during courtship and nest-site interactions. It is not a flight call or alarm call that rings across the landscape.
- **Volume drop:** After six Scolopacidae at E=3 to E=5, the Pigeon Guillemot's E=3 represents a significant volume drop. The transition from tidal flat broadcasting to rocky shoreline intimacy.
- **Functional adequacy:** The whistle is adequate for its purpose -- close-range communication between mates and nest neighbors in rocky shoreline colonies. The sound matches the habitat's acoustic scale.
- **Why not E=2 or E=4?** The call is audible and distinctive but thin. Not silent, not loud. E=3: present, functional, intimate.

## BRIGHT RED FEET AND URBAN COEXISTENCE

### The Most Recognizable Feature

The Pigeon Guillemot's most striking feature is its **bright red-orange feet:**

- **Color:** Vivid red-orange to coral red. Visible at distance against dark rock or water. The feet are used in courtship displays -- the bird raises and shows them.
- **Breeding plumage:** Entirely dark (black-brown) with a large white wing patch and bright red feet. One of the simplest, most striking color combinations in PNW seabirds.
- **Non-breeding:** Paler, mottled gray and white above. White wing patch retained. Red feet somewhat duller.

### Urban Nesting

The Pigeon Guillemot is one of the PNW's most urban-adapted seabirds:

- **Under docks:** Nests in crevices under wharves, piers, and ferry terminals. Common in Seattle's Elliott Bay, along the waterfront, under Colman Dock.
- **In jetties:** Nests in cracks between boulders in rock jetties and breakwaters.
- **In sea cliffs:** Traditional nest sites in natural rocky crevices on sea cliffs.
- **Coexistence:** Breeds within meters of heavy human activity -- ferries, freight, pedestrians. Tolerance of human presence is notable. An urban seabird.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; PNW seabird ecology; Elliott Bay ornithology; engine internal analysis)

## WING-PROPELLED DIVING -- THE ALCID WAY

### Flying Underwater

The Alcidae's defining adaptation is wing-propelled diving:

- **Dual-use wings:** Alcid wings function both in air (flight) and water (diving). The wings are short and stiff, optimized for the compromise between aerial flight and underwater propulsion.
- **Dive depth:** Pigeon Guillemots typically dive to 10-45 meters. They can reach 60+ meters. They pursue fish and invertebrates underwater using their wings to "fly" through the water.
- **Trade-off:** Short wings make aerial flight energetically expensive. Guillemots must beat their wings rapidly in flight -- a characteristic fast, buzzy flight low over the water. But the same short wings are highly efficient underwater.
- **Fish carrying:** Guillemots carry fish crosswise in their bill to feed chicks. They can carry multiple fish at once. The sight of a guillemot flying to its nest with fish dangling from its bill is a classic PNW waterfront image.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; alcid diving ecology; wing morphology; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Pigeon Guillemot

**Thin High Whistle:**
- **Trigger:** Courtship, nest-site interaction, close-range communication between mates.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** A thin, high-pitched, reedy whistle or trill. "Peeeeeer" or "ti-ti-ti-ti-ti." Quiet. Intimate. Given near nest sites on rocky shoreline or under docks.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 3000-6000 Hz. High and thin.
- **Duration:** Each call 0.5-2 seconds. Short trills or sustained thin whistles.
- **Amplitude:** E=3. Quiet. Audible at close range only. The sound of rocky shoreline intimacy.

**Other Sounds:**
- Rapid chattering "ki-ki-ki-ki" during aggressive interactions near nest sites.
- Soft clucking sounds during courtship displays.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; alcid vocalizations; engine internal analysis)
