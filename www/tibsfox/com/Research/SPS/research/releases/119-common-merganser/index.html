Mergus merganser (Anatidae) — Largest Merganser — The Sawbill
guttural croak in flight — second Anatidae entry (after Harlequin Duck at 118) — largest merganser in North America — serrated bill (sawbill) for catching fish — river ecosystem — indicator of healthy rivers — E=4. The Anatidae streak: 118–119 and counting.

Anatidae Streak — Second Consecutive Waterfowl

Degree 119 is the second consecutive Anatidae entry. Harlequin Duck (118, turbulent mountain stream) to Common Merganser (119, river). The engine has entered one of the most species-rich families in ornithology. The water ecosystem theme continues: dipper (116–117) to turbulent stream duck (118) to river merganser (119). Following the water downstream.

The Sawbill — A Fish-Catching Machine

The Common Merganser’s serrated bill is its defining adaptation. Long, narrow, lined with backward-pointing tooth-like tomia. A fish seized crosswise can slide backward (toward being swallowed) but cannot slide forward (toward escape). The bill is a one-way trap. Hooked tip for initial capture. Catches fish up to 30 cm long. Primary prey: salmonids, sculpin, whitefish. The sawbill — an extraordinary adaptation for piscivory.

The Sawbill Adaptation

Serrated Tomia

Backward-pointing tooth-like projections along both mandibles. Grips slippery fish. One-way trap.

Narrow Bill

Cylindrical, not flat like dabbling ducks. Optimized for underwater pursuit and precision striking.

Hooked Tip

Upper mandible hooks at the tip. Aids initial fish capture during rapid underwater lunges.

Set-Back Legs

Positioned far back on body for powerful underwater propulsion. Dives last 15–30 seconds.

Streamlined Body

Long, low profile for hydrodynamic efficiency. Males 1.3–2.1 kg. Built for speed in water.

Cooperative Fishing

Flocks of 10–50+ swim in coordinated lines, driving fish into shallows. Group hunting strategy.

River Ecosystem Indicator

A river with Common Mergansers is a healthy river. The bird requires: clear water (hunts by sight), abundant fish populations, riparian forest (cavity nester), and minimal disturbance. The merganser’s presence is a positive indicator of ecosystem health — clean water, fish, forest. In the Pacific Northwest, it favors the Skagit, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, and Stillaguamish rivers.

The Guttural Croak

The Common Merganser’s primary vocalization is a guttural croak given in flight — a hoarse, low-pitched, croaking call. Not musical. Functional. Frequency range ~500–2,500 Hz. Serves as contact calling during flock movement. The merganser is primarily a visual presence on rivers — the striking white-and-green male gliding on dark water is more diagnostic than any sound it makes. E=4: moderate acoustic presence, audible across river corridors but not dominating.
