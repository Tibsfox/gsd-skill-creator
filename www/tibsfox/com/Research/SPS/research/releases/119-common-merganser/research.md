## COMMON MERGANSER -- ANATIDAE -- THE SAWBILL

### The Species

The Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*) is the **largest merganser** in North America and one of the largest ducks on the continent. Males are striking: bright white body, dark green-black head with a shaggy crest, long red-orange serrated bill. Females are gray-bodied with a warm rufous-brown crested head and the same distinctive serrated bill. The bird is built for speed and power in the water -- long, streamlined body, set-back legs for underwater propulsion, and a bill designed for catching and holding slippery fish.

The Common Merganser is found across the northern United States and Canada, wherever clear rivers and lakes provide fish habitat. In the Pacific Northwest, it is a year-round resident on rivers from the Cascade foothills to the coast. It favors **large, clear rivers** with good visibility for fish-hunting -- the Skagit, the Snoqualmie, the Skykomish, the Stillaguamish, and their major tributaries. It also frequents large lakes during winter when river conditions deteriorate.

The name "merganser" comes from the Latin *mergus* (diving bird) and *anser* (goose) -- a "diving goose." The common name reflects that this is the most widespread and frequently encountered of the three North American merganser species (Common, Red-breasted, and Hooded).

### The Sawbill -- A Fish-Catching Adaptation

The Common Merganser's most distinctive feature is its **serrated bill** -- the feature that gives all mergansers the colloquial name **"sawbill."** The bill is long (approximately 50-60 mm), narrow, and lined along both mandibles with backward-pointing, tooth-like serrations called **tomia**. These serrations are not true teeth (birds lost teeth approximately 100 million years ago) but keratinous projections that function like teeth: they grip slippery fish and prevent them from escaping.

The sawbill is an extraordinary evolutionary adaptation for piscivory (fish-eating):

1. **Backward-pointing serrations** -- the tomia point rearward toward the throat. A fish seized crosswise in the bill can slide backward (toward being swallowed) but cannot slide forward (toward escape). The bill is a one-way trap.

2. **Narrow, elongated shape** -- the bill is much narrower than a typical duck bill. Most ducks have broad, flat bills designed for dabbling, filtering, and sifting. The merganser bill is narrow and cylindrical, optimized for underwater pursuit and precision striking at individual fish.

3. **Hooked tip** -- the upper mandible has a slight hook at the tip, aiding in the initial capture of fish. The merganser strikes at a fish with a rapid lunge, seizes it with the hooked tip, and the serrations prevent escape.

4. **Red-orange coloration** -- the bill is bright red-orange, visible even at distance. The function is likely sexual signaling (both sexes assess mate quality partly through bill coloration) and possibly species recognition.

The sawbill allows the Common Merganser to catch and consume fish up to 30 cm (12 inches) long -- a remarkable prey size for a duck. Primary prey includes salmonids (trout, salmon smolts), sculpin, whitefish, and other stream fish. In the Pacific Northwest, the merganser's diet overlaps significantly with salmon and steelhead -- a connection that has generated historical conflict with fisheries managers, though modern research shows the merganser's impact on salmonid populations is minimal.

### Foraging Behavior

The Common Merganser forages by **diving and underwater pursuit**:

1. The bird swims on the surface, periodically submerging its head to scan for fish (a behavior called "snorkeling" -- the bird dips its face into the water while the body remains on the surface).
2. When a fish is spotted, the merganser dives -- propelling itself underwater with powerful kicks of its large, set-back feet.
3. Underwater, it pursues the fish with remarkable speed and agility, using its feet for propulsion and its tail for steering. Dives typically last 15-30 seconds.
4. The fish is seized with the hooked bill tip and held by the serrated tomia.
5. The merganser surfaces, manipulates the fish headfirst, and swallows it whole.

Common Mergansers also engage in **cooperative group fishing**: flocks of 10-50 or more birds swim together in a coordinated line, driving fish into shallows where they are more easily caught. This cooperative strategy is remarkable for a duck species and resembles the cooperative hunting strategies seen in some fish-eating seabirds (cormorants, pelicans).

### River Ecosystem Role

The Common Merganser is an indicator species for **healthy river ecosystems**. Its presence requires:
- **Clear water** -- the merganser hunts by sight. Turbid water reduces foraging efficiency.
- **Abundant fish populations** -- the merganser needs enough fish to sustain its large body (males: 1.3-2.1 kg).
- **Riparian forest** -- the merganser is a cavity nester, requiring large tree cavities near water for nesting. The loss of riparian forest directly reduces nesting habitat.
- **Minimal disturbance** -- the merganser is moderately sensitive to human disturbance during nesting.

A river with Common Mergansers is a river with clean water, fish, and forest. The bird's presence is a positive indicator of ecosystem health.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mallory & Metz 1999; USGS river ecology)

## Acoustic Profile: The Guttural Croak

The Common Merganser is not a vocal bird by duck standards. Unlike dabbling ducks with their quacks and whistles, the merganser's vocalizations are **harsh, guttural, and infrequent**. The primary vocalization is a **guttural croak** given in flight -- a hoarse, low-pitched, croaking call that carries across river corridors.

### The Sound

The guttural croak manifests as:
- **Frequency range:** approximately 500-2,500 Hz. A low, hoarse call with most energy in the low-frequency range. Unlike songbird vocalizations that occupy the 2,000-8,000 Hz band, the merganser's croak sits in the lower frequencies.
- **Quality:** harsh, guttural, croaking. Not musical. The sound has been compared to a harsh "gak-gak-gak" or a hoarse groaning croak. It is a functional call, not an aesthetic one.
- **Context:** primarily in flight, during disturbance, and during courtship. The merganser is relatively silent while foraging. The flight croak serves as contact calling -- maintaining flock cohesion during movement between foraging sites.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** males and females produce different calls. The male's call is a softer, bell-like "uig-a" during courtship. The female's call is the harsher croak, more frequently heard.

### E=4 -- Moderate Acoustic Presence

E=4 reflects the Common Merganser's moderate acoustic presence. The bird is not silent (like some stealthy predators) but it is not a singer. The guttural croak is audible across river corridors -- a necessary volume for contact calling in the open, windy environments of rivers and lakes -- but it does not dominate the soundscape. The merganser is primarily a visual presence: the striking white-and-green male gliding on a dark river is more diagnostic than any sound it makes.

The acoustic contrast with the degree 116-117 dipper is instructive: the dipper (E=5) must out-sing rushing water with a complex, beautiful song. The merganser (E=4) does not need to out-sing anything -- it calls in flight, above the water, in open air. The acoustic challenge is lower, and the response is correspondingly less elaborate.
