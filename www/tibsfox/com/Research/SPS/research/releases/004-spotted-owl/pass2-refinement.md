### Nocturnal Acoustic Ecology: The Forest After Dark

Pass 1 established the Northern Spotted Owl's nocturnal temporal niche — the first species in the SPS engine to occupy nighttime. Pass 2 examines the acoustic physics of the old-growth forest after sunset: how the sound environment transforms, what physical mechanisms create the nocturnal acoustic space, and why the owl's vocal apparatus is tuned with extraordinary precision to exploit it.

The old-growth Douglas fir forest undergoes a radical acoustic transition at dusk. Ambient noise drops precipitously. The diurnal chorus — Pacific Wrens (degree 1) cascading from the understory, Varied Thrushes (degree 2) sustaining their atmospheric tones from the mid-canopy, Black-capped Chickadees (degree 3) broadcasting alarm calls and fee-bee songs from the edge habitat — falls silent within approximately thirty minutes of sunset. Wind speed decreases as solar heating of the canopy ceases and convective turbulence subsides. Anthropogenic noise attenuates as human activity diminishes. By one hour after sunset, the ambient noise floor in an intact old-growth stand can drop to 20–25 dB SPL — quieter than an empty recording studio, approaching the threshold of human hearing. The forest at night is not merely dark. It is acoustically transformed — a low-noise environment in which sounds that would be masked during the day propagate with startling clarity and range.

Temperature inversions amplify this effect. As the forest floor radiates heat after sunset, a layer of cool, dense air forms near the ground while warmer air persists at canopy height. This temperature gradient creates an acoustic waveguide — sound waves traveling horizontally near the ground are refracted downward by the warm-over-cool boundary, bending back toward the surface rather than dispersing upward into the atmosphere. The effect is analogous to the oceanic SOFAR channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging), where temperature and pressure gradients in the deep ocean create a minimum-speed layer that traps sound waves and allows them to propagate for thousands of kilometers. The nocturnal forest's temperature inversion operates on a smaller scale but the same principle: the owl's four-note hoot, emitted at canopy height, refracts downward through the inversion layer and propagates along the near-ground acoustic channel with significantly less attenuation than it would experience during daytime convective conditions. A hoot that carries 300 meters during the day can carry 800 meters or more at night — not because the owl calls louder, but because the atmosphere itself has become a more efficient transmission medium.

The owl's facial disk exploits this acoustic environment with biological precision. The disk — the concave arrangement of stiff, densely packed feathers surrounding the face — functions as a parabolic reflector, collecting sound energy across a broad area (the approximately 10-centimeter-diameter disk face) and focusing it onto the ear openings located at the disk's focal points. The acoustic gain provided by the facial disk has been measured at approximately 10 dB in the 3–6 kHz range in Barn Owls (Coles and Guppy 1988, *Journal of Comparative Physiology*), and while Spotted Owl-specific measurements are less complete, the morphological similarity suggests comparable performance in the owl's operational frequency range of 300–700 Hz. A 10 dB gain means the owl's ears receive sound energy approximately ten times more intense than what would reach an unshielded ear of the same size. The facial disk transforms the owl's head into a biological directional microphone — a two-channel parabolic antenna system that enhances signal-to-noise ratio in exactly the frequency band where its territorial hoot and its prey's rustling sounds operate.

The asymmetric ear placement adds the vertical dimension. In Northern Spotted Owls, as in most *Strix* species, the right ear opening is positioned slightly higher on the skull than the left ear opening. The asymmetry is subtle — a few millimeters of vertical offset — but its acoustic consequences are profound. A sound source located above the owl's horizontal plane arrives at the higher (right) ear fractionally before the lower (left) ear, producing an interaural time difference (ITD) in the vertical plane. The owl's auditory system — specifically the nucleus laminaris in the brainstem, homologous to the mammalian medial superior olive — computes these ITDs with microsecond resolution. Combined with the interaural level differences (ILDs) produced by the facial disk's directional filtering, the asymmetric ear system allows the Spotted Owl to localize sound sources in both the horizontal and vertical planes simultaneously. Laboratory measurements on closely related species indicate angular resolution of 1–2 degrees in both azimuth and elevation — comparable to the resolution of the human eye's foveal vision but operating in total darkness through sound alone. The owl does not merely hear its prey. It triangulates its prey's position in three-dimensional space, computing a targeting solution from purely acoustic information, accurate enough to strike a mouse beneath leaf litter on a moonless night.

The forest's acoustic transformation at night explains why the owl's four-note hoot uses the frequency range it does. The 300–700 Hz band is optimized for propagation through old-growth canopy: wavelengths of approximately 0.5–1.1 meters, long enough to diffract around tree trunks (whose diameters are 0.5–2 meters) rather than being reflected or absorbed, but short enough to maintain directional information that allows receivers to localize the source. Higher frequencies — the 2–8 kHz range used by the diurnal songbirds at degrees 1 through 3 — attenuate more rapidly through dense foliage and humidity. Lower frequencies — below 200 Hz — would require a vocal apparatus and a body cavity too large for the owl's 600-gram frame. The 300–700 Hz band is the acoustic sweet spot for a medium-large owl vocalizing through old-growth forest at night: maximum range, minimum attenuation, sufficient directional resolution for territorial negotiation across distances of several hundred meters.

The four species across degrees 0–4 now occupy every temporal and vertical niche in the old-growth acoustic environment. Degree 0 (Great Blue Heron): diurnal, open water and estuary edge — the acoustic periphery of the forest. Degree 1 (Pacific Wren): diurnal, old-growth understory floor — the densest acoustic environment, the most complex song. Degree 2 (Varied Thrush): crepuscular, mid-canopy — the atmospheric transition zone. Degree 3 (Black-capped Chickadee): diurnal, edge and mid-canopy — the information broadcast layer. Degree 4 (Northern Spotted Owl): nocturnal, upper canopy — the temporal inverse of degrees 1 through 3, exploiting the acoustic silence they leave behind. The forest's frequency-time grid is now fully populated across both axes. The engine has described a complete diel cycle of acoustic occupation: day, twilight, and night, from estuary edge to canopy crown.

### The Barred Owl Invasion: Acoustic Interference and Ecological Displacement

*Strix varia* — the Barred Owl — is an eastern North American species that evolved in the deciduous and mixed forests east of the Great Plains. Its historical range extended from the Atlantic seaboard to the Great Lakes, from Florida to southern Canada. It did not occur west of the Rockies. And then it began moving.

The westward expansion followed the boreal forest corridor across Canada, enabled by a century of landscape modification. European settlement fragmented the Great Plains grassland barrier that had historically separated eastern and western forest avifaunas. Tree planting in the prairies — homestead shelterbelts, municipal plantings, riparian corridor regrowth after fire suppression — created stepping stones of forested habitat across what had been open grassland. The Barred Owl, a habitat generalist capable of thriving in second-growth, riparian, suburban, and even urban forests, exploited these new corridors. The first confirmed breeding records west of the Rockies appeared in British Columbia in the 1960s. By the 1970s, Barred Owls were breeding in Washington. By the 1990s, they were established throughout the Spotted Owl's range, from British Columbia to northern California. Livezey (2009) documented the chronology: a westward advance of approximately 100 kilometers per decade, one of the most rapid continental-scale range expansions ever recorded in a large raptor.

The acoustic dimension of the invasion is the least studied and potentially the most consequential. The Barred Owl's primary vocalization — the eight-note territorial call transcribed as "who cooks for you, who cooks for you-all" — operates in a frequency band of approximately 400–700 Hz, overlapping almost completely with the Spotted Owl's 300–700 Hz territorial hoot. Two *Strix* species, occupying the same habitat, calling at night, in the same frequency band. The acoustic territories that Spotted Owls established and maintained through their four-note hoot are now contested by Barred Owl calls that propagate through the same nocturnal acoustic channel with the same physical efficiency.

But the Barred Owl's vocal repertoire is far more extensive than the Spotted Owl's. Where the Spotted Owl produces a limited set of vocalizations — the four-note hoot, the barking call, the contact call, the female series hoot — the Barred Owl produces a bewildering array of hoots, barks, cackles, caterwauls, screams, and duetting sequences that can sound like a troop of monkeys fighting in the canopy. The Barred Owl pair duet — in which male and female call simultaneously in an overlapping, accelerating sequence that rises in pitch and intensity — is one of the most acoustically complex vocalizations produced by any North American owl. The effect is acoustic saturation: a Barred Owl pair in full duet fills the nocturnal soundscape with a wall of sound that masks other owls' vocalizations, effectively jamming the acoustic channel.

The Spotted Owl's response to Barred Owl vocal presence is suppression. Field studies in Oregon and Washington have documented that Spotted Owls call less frequently in territories where Barred Owls are present (Crozier et al. 2006, *Condor*). The mechanism is likely twofold: first, the Spotted Owl's territorial hoot is less effective when the acoustic channel is occupied by a louder, more vocally prolific competitor; second, calling attracts the Barred Owl's attention, and direct encounters between the two species frequently result in aggressive displacement of the Spotted Owl. The Spotted Owl has learned, at the population level, that calling in the presence of a Barred Owl is dangerous. The four-note hoot — the rhythmic motif that defines the species' territorial identity — falls silent.

This is acoustic displacement: the loss of a species' ability to communicate effectively in its own habitat, caused not by physical removal of the habitat but by the introduction of a competitor that dominates the same communication channel. The parallel to radio broadcasting is exact. Two stations transmitting on the same frequency in the same geographic area create interference — the stronger signal masks the weaker one, and listeners tuned to the weaker station hear noise. The Barred Owl is the stronger transmitter: larger body, louder call, broader vocal repertoire, more persistent calling behavior. The Spotted Owl is the weaker transmitter being jammed. The old-growth forest's acoustic environment has not been destroyed — the canopy still stands, the temperature inversions still form, the low-frequency propagation channel still functions. But the channel is now occupied by a competitor that the channel's original user cannot out-signal.

The USFWS response — lethal removal of Barred Owls in designated Spotted Owl recovery areas — is, in acoustic terms, an attempt to clear the frequency. The agency authorized the killing of approximately 450,000 Barred Owls over a thirty-year period in the 2024 Final Environmental Impact Statement, targeting areas where Spotted Owl populations are most critically depressed. The ethical and practical complexities are staggering: killing one native species to protect another, at a scale that requires industrial-level trapping and shooting, with no guarantee that the cleared frequency will remain clear (Barred Owls reproduce rapidly and recolonize quickly). The invasion has no clean solution because the underlying cause — the landscape-level habitat connectivity that allowed the westward expansion — cannot be reversed. The stepping stones are planted. The corridor is open. The Barred Owl is here.

### Survey Protocol Acoustics: The Most Consequential Callback in Conservation History

The USFWS standardized protocol for Northern Spotted Owl detection, codified by Forsman (1983) and refined by Franklin et al. (1996), is deceptively simple. A surveyor enters old-growth habitat after dark, plays a recording of the four-note territorial hoot from a portable speaker, waits in silence, and listens for a response. If a Spotted Owl calls back, the surveyor records the location, the time, the bearing to the response, and the call type. If no owl responds, the surveyor moves to the next station — typically spaced 300–500 meters apart along transects through suitable habitat — and repeats. A single survey night covers 6–12 stations over 4–6 hours, from approximately 30 minutes after sunset to approximately 30 minutes before sunrise.

The protocol's detection probability per visit is approximately 60–80% under optimal conditions (Olson et al. 2005, *Ecological Applications*). That is: if a Spotted Owl is present in a surveyed territory, a single night's survey has a 60–80% chance of detecting it. The corollary is sobering: a 20–40% chance of missing an owl that is actually there. The detection failure modes include weather (rain, wind, and cold reduce calling activity and mask responses), seasonal timing (calling peaks during the breeding season, February through April, and declines outside it), Barred Owl interference (Spotted Owls call less frequently when Barred Owls are present), and simple geometry (the surveyor's speaker may not carry to the owl, or the owl's response may not carry back to the surveyor). The protocol therefore requires multiple visits — typically three to six visits per territory per survey season — to achieve a cumulative detection probability above 95%. Three visits at 70% per-visit detection yield a cumulative probability of 1 − (0.30)³ = 97.3%. Six visits at 60% per-visit detection yield 1 − (0.40)⁶ = 99.6%. The mathematics of absence is demanding: proving that an owl is *not* present requires far more effort than confirming that one *is* present.

The protocol transforms an acoustic signal — the four-note hoot — into federal land management data. When a surveyor's playback elicits a response, the data point enters a database that determines whether a timber sale can proceed. Under the Northwest Forest Plan and its associated regulatory framework, documented Spotted Owl territories impose harvest restrictions on surrounding federal forest — buffer zones of 70 acres (core area), 500 acres (activity center), or the full territory (2,400–14,200 acres) depending on the administrative designation and the population's status. A single callback from a single owl on a single survey night can halt a timber sale worth millions of dollars. No other acoustic event in the natural world carries comparable economic and regulatory weight.

The survey protocol is, in information-theoretic terms, a formal query-response system. The surveyor's playback is the query: "Is there a Spotted Owl in this territory?" The owl's callback is the response: "Yes, I am here." The silence — when no owl responds — is an ambiguous null: either "No owl is here" or "An owl is here but did not respond." The protocol cannot distinguish between absence and non-detection on a single visit. Only the accumulation of null responses across multiple visits approaches confidence in true absence. This is Bayesian inference applied to acoustic ecology: prior probability of occupancy, conditional probability of detection given presence, posterior probability of occupancy given silence. The mathematics is published in the occupancy modeling literature (MacKenzie et al. 2002, *Ecology*), and the Spotted Owl survey protocol was one of the primary empirical systems that drove the development of occupancy modeling as a subdiscipline of quantitative ecology.

The survey protocol is therefore the most consequential acoustic monitoring program in United States conservation history — and perhaps in global conservation history. An entire federal timber policy, affecting 24.5 million acres of public land, restructuring the economies of dozens of communities, and redirecting billions of dollars in timber revenue, was built on whether an owl calls back to a speaker in the dark. The protocol converted biology into bureaucracy through acoustics: a bird's vocalization, received by a human ear, transcribed onto a datasheet, entered into a database, analyzed by statisticians, interpreted by biologists, transmitted to land managers, and enacted as harvest restrictions. The four-note hoot traveled from a Douglas fir branch through the nocturnal atmosphere, through a surveyor's auditory cortex, through an institutional processing chain, and into the Federal Register. No other four notes in the natural world have traveled that far.

### Pioneer 3 Resonance: Institutional Frameworks and What Gets Detected

Pioneer 3 was the first Pioneer mission run by the Army — specifically by the Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA) at Redstone Arsenal, with the Jet Propulsion Laboratory providing the spacecraft and scientific payload. The previous three Pioneers had been Air Force missions using the Thor-Able launch vehicle. The institutional switch mattered. The Army's Juno II was a different rocket built by different engineers with different operational experience. The Air Force team had three failures (Pioneer 0's explosion, Pioneer 1's partial success, Pioneer 2's silent third stage). The Army team, on its first attempt, achieved a higher altitude than Pioneer 2 (102,360 km versus 1,550 km) and returned 25 hours of scientific data versus Pioneer 2's 45 minutes. Different institutional framework, different results — not because the Army was inherently better, but because the Juno II derived from the Jupiter IRBM program that had already successfully launched Explorer 1, giving the Army team a more mature and better-tested launch vehicle than the Thor-Able's problematic upper stages.

The Spotted Owl is studied, managed, and protected through an institutional framework — the Endangered Species Act, the USFWS survey protocol, the Northwest Forest Plan — that transforms an acoustic signal into federal policy. The framework determines what gets detected (owls that call back to playback), what gets measured (territory occupancy, population trends, reproductive success), and what gets acted upon (harvest restrictions, critical habitat designation, Barred Owl removal). The framework also determines what gets missed. Spotted Owls that do not call back — because of Barred Owl suppression, weather, individual behavioral variation, or simple chance — are not detected. Their territories are not documented. Their habitat is not protected. The institutional framework's detection probability (60–80% per visit) is also its blindness probability (20–40% per visit). What Pioneer 3 detected — the second Van Allen belt — it detected because its trajectory carried it through the belt's altitude. What it missed — the Moon — it missed because the first stage cut off four seconds early. The institutional framework shapes the trajectory. The trajectory determines what you encounter.

Both the Spotted Owl surveys and Pioneer 3 demonstrate that institutional frameworks are not neutral measurement tools. They are filters that admit certain data and exclude other data based on the framework's design parameters. The USFWS protocol detects calling owls and misses silent ones. Pioneer 3's trajectory mapped the radiation belts and missed the lunar surface. The framework's capabilities and limitations are inseparable — you cannot have the detection without the blindness, because both arise from the same design parameters (survey station spacing, playback volume, detection probability for the owl; burn duration, velocity budget, trajectory design for the rocket). The question is not whether the framework is perfect. The question is whether the framework's detection capabilities are sufficient to support the decisions that depend on its data. For Pioneer 3, the data was sufficient to confirm the second Van Allen belt — a discovery of fundamental importance. For the Spotted Owl surveys, the data has been sufficient to document a population decline of 50–75% in Washington over three decades — a measurement of fundamental alarm.

The *Usnea longissima* parallel connects at the level of indicator species as institutional measurement. Both owl and lichen are old-growth indicators — their presence confirms ecosystem integrity, their absence signals degradation. But "presence" and "absence" are not binary natural facts. They are measurements taken through institutional frameworks. A lichenologist surveys a forest stand using transect methods — walking a predetermined path, recording *Usnea* at fixed intervals, computing a lichen diversity index. A wildlife biologist surveys the same forest stand using the USFWS owl protocol — walking a different predetermined path at a different time of day, playing a different kind of signal, recording a different kind of response. Both practitioners are measuring ecosystem health. Both are using institutional frameworks that determine what gets detected. The lichenologist misses *Usnea* colonies on branches beyond visual range. The biologist misses owls that do not respond. The forest contains what the forest contains. What the institution reports depends on the institution's detection apparatus.

The degree 1 through 4 Pioneer missions now form a complete narrative of institutional learning through failure. Pioneer 0 (degree 1, Air Force): catastrophic destruction — learned that the Thor-Able vehicle's structural integrity was marginal. Pioneer 1 (degree 2, Air Force): partial success — learned that the guidance system could achieve lunar trajectory but not sustain it, discovered the inner Van Allen belt. Pioneer 2 (degree 3, Air Force): silent failure — learned that the third-stage solid motor was unreliable, confirmed Pioneer 1's radiation data. Pioneer 3 (degree 4, Army): productive miss — different institution, different vehicle, higher altitude, discovered the outer Van Allen belt. The sequence demonstrates that institutional diversity produces discovery: the same Air Force team, repeating the same basic mission with the same basic vehicle, achieved diminishing returns across Pioneers 0–2. The switch to a different institution with a different vehicle produced Pioneer 3's breakthrough. The forest analogy is direct: a monoculture — one institution, one method, one trajectory — is less likely to discover what lies outside its detection envelope than a diverse ecosystem of approaches. The old-growth forest sustains both *Usnea* and owl because it sustains diversity. The Pioneer program found the second Van Allen belt because it tried a different rocket.
