Two Great Voices at the Skagit Delta

The Trumpeter Swan is the loudest waterfowl in North America — 90–100 dB at close range, carrying several miles. The Sandhill Crane is the oldest extant bird call — morphologically identical to Pliocene fossils 2.5 million years old. Both at the Skagit River delta in winter, both calling simultaneously at a congregation site. Trumpet meets bugle. Deep resonant horn meets prehistoric rattling bugle. E=8.

Two Species, Two Families, One Delta

Trumpeter Swan

Cygnus buccinator — Family Anatidae
Body mass: 11–14 kg. Wingspan: 2–2.4 m. North America's largest native waterfowl.
Call: Deep resonant trumpet. Fundamental 250–350 Hz. Smooth harmonics at 500–700 Hz. Duration: 1.5–3 sec.
Trachea: Coiled within sternal groove, up to 1.5 m total length. The longest trachea of any waterfowl.
Amplitude: 90–100 dB at source. Carries several miles in open delta.
Recovery: Fewer than 100 in contiguous US (early 20th c.) → 63,000+ (2020s). One of North America's greatest conservation successes.

Sandhill Crane

Antigone canadensis — Family Gruidae
Fossil record: 2.5 million years (Pliocene). Morphologically identical to modern birds. The most ancient acoustic record in the engine.
Call: Rattling bugle. Fundamental 250–350 Hz. Complex harmonics 300–1,200 Hz. Rattling quality from syrinx vibration.
Trachea: 1.2–1.5 m, coiled within sternum. Convergent with Trumpeter Swan anatomy.
Amplitude: 95–105 dB at source. Carries 2–3 miles in still conditions.
Status: Most abundant crane in world. Pacific Flyway migration through eastern WA, wintering at lower Columbia and Skagit delta.

Convergent Anatomy — Different Voices

Both the Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane have independently evolved the same anatomical solution: an elongated trachea coiled within the sternum, acting as a resonating chamber to lower the fundamental frequency and increase carrying power. Two birds, two evolutionary lineages, the same physical solution. But the voices are entirely different: the Swan's call is smooth, bell-like, deep — a brass instrument's resonance. The Crane's call is rattling, complex, penetrating — the voice of geological time. Convergent anatomy, divergent sound. This is the acoustic proof that the same physics can produce different aesthetic outcomes.

The Skagit Delta in Winter

The Skagit River delta (Skagit County, WA) hosts thousands of Trumpeter Swans from November through March in flooded agricultural fields and shallow wetlands. The Samish Flats and the fields around Conway and Mount Vernon are the primary concentration areas. Sandhill Cranes migrate through in spring and fall, with some birds wintering in mild Pacific Northwest years. On a November morning when both species are present and calling, the delta acoustic environment is defined by the dual trumpet-bugle layer rising above the background of Snow Geese, wind across the agricultural flats, and the distant sound of the Skagit River.

The Reifel Migratory Bird Sanctuary in the Fraser River delta (British Columbia) provides a parallel habitat across the border, with some of the largest Trumpeter Swan winter concentrations in the Pacific Northwest and consistent Sandhill Crane migration presence.
