## What Worked

1. **The convergent tracheal anatomy documentation is the entry's strongest scientific finding.** Both species independently evolved elongated tracheae coiled within the sternum. Same physics, different acoustic character: Swan smooth and bell-like, Crane rattling and complex. This is convergent evolution with different acoustic outputs -- a well-defined scientific observation.
2. **The Sandhill Crane's 2.5-million-year fossil record is correctly identified as the engine's oldest acoustic entry.** No other degree documents a sound whose source has been acoustically consistent for geological time. The crane's Pliocene fossils are morphologically identical to modern birds. This is the most ancient voice in the engine.
3. **The Trumpeter Swan recovery story is among the most compelling conservation narratives documented in the engine.** Near-extinction (fewer than 100 birds) to 63,000+ is a transformation on a scale that is difficult to comprehend. The loudest waterfowl call nearly vanished from the Pacific Northwest permanently.
4. **The Skagit delta context is well-documented and specific.** The Samish Flats, Conway fields, Mount Vernon area -- actual locations where both species congregate in actual numbers. The delta habitat creates the acoustic event through the combination of floodwater, waste grain, and winter temperatures.

## Discoveries

1. **Both Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane fundamentals are in the same frequency range (250-350 Hz).** They call at the same pitch but with entirely different spectral character. This means the two calls overlap in fundamental frequency but are distinguished by timbre, not pitch. The human ear sorts them by character rather than by absolute frequency. This is a sophisticated acoustic discrimination task that birders perform instinctively.
2. **The Trumpeter Swan's sternal tracheal coiling is unique among waterfowl.** The Tundra Swan (smaller congener) does not have this coiling, and its call is demonstrably higher-pitched and less resonant as a result. The anatomical difference between two closely related species produces a clear acoustic difference -- a natural experiment in tracheal resonance physics.
3. **Sandhill Crane swarms on migration through eastern Washington (Columbia Basin, Palouse) can number in the thousands.** The Pacific Flyway migration brings large concentrations through the region. The Willow Springs area in Grant County, WA, and the Wenas Wildlife Area in Yakima County are documented staging areas.
