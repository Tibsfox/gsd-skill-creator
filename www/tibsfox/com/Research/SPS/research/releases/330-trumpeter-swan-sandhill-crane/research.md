### The Trumpeter Swan and the Sandhill Crane

The pairing of Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane at degree 330:

- **Two different vocal mechanisms producing two different acoustic profiles.** The Trumpeter Swan's call is produced by a long, coiled trachea -- the trachea in adult Trumpeters is unusually elongated and folded within the sternum, functioning as a resonating chamber that gives the call its deep, brass-instrument quality. ## TRUMPETER SWAN -- CYGNUS BUCCINATOR

### The Loudest Waterfowl in North America

The Trumpeter Swan's biology, call, and Pacific Northwest presence:

- **Taxonomy.** *Cygnus buccinator* (Trumpeter Swan) is the largest native waterfowl in North America and one of the world's largest flying birds. Adult males (cobs) can weigh 11-14 kg (24-30 lbs) with a wingspan of 2-2.4 meters (6.5-8 feet). The species name *buccinator* is Latin for "trumpeter" -- the name refers directly to the call.
- **The call.** The Trumpeter Swan's call is a deep, resonant, carrying trumpet-like sound: a low-pitched honk with the tonal quality of a brass instrument, specifically a French horn or tuba rather than a brighter trumpet. The fundamental frequency is approximately 250-350 Hz, with strong harmonics extending upward. The call is produced by the elongated, coiled trachea that acts as a resonating tube -- the longer the trachea, the lower the fundamental frequency. Adult Trumpeters have the longest trachea of any waterfowl, producing the deepest, most resonant call.
- **Call amplitude.** The Trumpeter Swan's call carries over distances of several miles in open delta habitat. At the bird's location, the call reaches approximately 90-100 dB -- among the loudest of any avian call in the Pacific Northwest. At a winter congregation of 50-100 birds, simultaneous calling produces a sound that fills the entire delta acoustic environment.
- **Near-extinction and recovery.** The Trumpeter Swan was reduced to fewer than 100 birds in the contiguous United States by the early 20th century due to hunting for feathers and meat. Strict protection under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act (1918) and subsequent recovery efforts -- including establishment of protected nesting sites in the Rocky Mountain region, Canada, and Alaska -- restored the population to over 63,000 birds by the 2020s. The Trumpeter Swan recovery is one of the most successful North American bird conservation stories.
- **Pacific Northwest winter habitat.** Trumpeter Swans winter extensively in the Pacific Northwest, particularly in the Skagit River delta (Skagit County, WA), the Samish Flats, the Stillaguamish River delta, the Snohomish River valley, and across the border in the Fraser River delta (British Columbia). The Skagit Valley hosts thousands of Trumpeter Swans from November through March, feeding in flooded agricultural fields and shallow wetlands. The Skagit winter concentration is one of the largest in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Cygnus buccinator taxonomy; adult male weight 11-14 kg wingspan 2-2.4m; buccinator Latin for trumpeter; call fundamental 250-350 Hz strong harmonics; elongated coiled trachea resonating tube; call amplitude 90-100 dB; carries several miles; near-extinction fewer than 100 birds early 20th century; Migratory Bird Treaty Act 1918; recovery to 63,000+ by 2020s; Skagit River delta winter concentration; Samish Flats; Stillaguamish River delta; Fraser River delta British Columbia)

## SANDHILL CRANE -- ANTIGONE CANADENSIS

### The Prehistoric Bugle -- 2.5 Million Years of Evolutionary Record

The Sandhill Crane's biology, call, and Pacific Northwest presence:

- **Taxonomy.** *Antigone canadensis* (Sandhill Crane) is the most abundant crane species in the world and the oldest living bird species with a verified fossil record -- fossils indistinguishable from modern Sandhill Cranes have been found from the Pliocene epoch, approximately 2.5 million years ago. When the Sandhill Crane calls, it calls with a voice that has been essentially unchanged for 2.5 million years. This is the most ancient acoustic record in the engine.
- **The call.** The Sandhill Crane's call is a rolling, bugling, rattling sound -- unmistakable at great distance. The call is produced by a trachea approximately 1.2-1.5 meters in length, coiled within the sternum. The tracheal length creates the call's characteristic resonance and penetrating quality. The fundamental frequency of the Sandhill Crane's call is approximately 250-350 Hz (similar to the Trumpeter Swan's range), but the call's character is entirely different: where the Swan's call is smooth and bell-like, the Crane's is rattling and complex, with multiple harmonics and the distinctive "rattle" quality produced by the vibrating soft tissues in the syrinx.
- **Call amplitude and carrying distance.** The Sandhill Crane's bugle can be heard up to 2-3 miles away in still conditions. At a migration concentration or a winter gathering, simultaneous calling by multiple cranes creates a soundscape that is one of the most awe-inspiring in North American birding. The call's carrying distance is proportionate to its amplitude: 95-105 dB at close range.
- **The 2.5-million-year fossil record.** The Nebraska site (Ashfall Fossil Beds) and other Pliocene fossil localities have yielded Sandhill Crane bones morphologically identical to modern individuals. The crane's morphological and presumably behavioral conservatism over geological time is extraordinary. The call degree 330 documents is, as closely as can be determined, the same call that was produced in the Pliocene -- 2.5 million years of evolutionary stasis in a single acoustic event.
- **Pacific Northwest presence.** Sandhill Cranes pass through the Pacific Northwest in large numbers during spring and fall migration. The Pacific Flyway supports significant crane migration through eastern Washington (Palouse region, Columbia Basin), with some birds wintering in the lower Columbia River and Willamette Valley. The Skagit and Fraser deltas see crane concentrations primarily during migration, with some birds overwintering in mild Pacific Northwest winters. The Pacific Coast subspecies (*A. c. tabida*, the Greater Sandhill Crane) is the subspecies most commonly seen in western Washington.

(Sources: Antigone canadensis taxonomy; most abundant crane in world; Pliocene fossil record 2.5 million years; oldest living bird species with verified fossil record; trachea 1.2-1.5m coiled within sternum; call fundamental 250-350 Hz; rattling quality from syrinx soft tissue vibration; call amplitude 95-105 dB; carrying distance 2-3 miles; Ashfall Fossil Beds Nebraska; Pacific Flyway crane migration; Columbia Basin Palouse region; Pacific Coast subspecies tabida Greater Sandhill Crane; Skagit and Fraser delta migration concentration)

## THE DELTA HABITAT

### Where Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane Meet

The delta ecosystem and why it produces the dual-layer acoustic event:

- **The Skagit River delta.** The Skagit River delta in Skagit County, Washington, is one of the Pacific Northwest's most important winter waterfowl habitats. The delta encompasses the farmland and wetlands at the river's mouth, including the flats around Mount Vernon, Burlington, and the Samish Flats. Winter flooding of agricultural fields creates the shallow-water habitat preferred by Trumpeter Swans; the cranes use both flooded fields and open upland areas. The Skagit Valley hosts some of the largest winter concentrations of Trumpeter Swans in the region, along with Tundra Swans, Snow Geese, Dunlin, and migrating Sandhill Cranes.
- **The Fraser River delta.** Across the border in British Columbia, the Fraser River delta (including Boundary Bay and the Reifel Bird Sanctuary) hosts even larger concentrations of both species. The Reifel Migratory Bird Sanctuary is one of the most important wintering sites for Trumpeter Swans in the Pacific Northwest, and Sandhill Cranes migrate through in large numbers.
- **Agricultural field habitat.** The Pacific Northwest's agricultural deltas provide the combination of shallow water and abundant food (waste grain from cereal crops, roots and tubers in flooded fields) that both species require. The conversion of tidal marsh to agricultural land in the 19th and 20th centuries simultaneously destroyed some habitat and created the flooded agricultural field habitat that these species now use extensively.
- **The acoustic environment of the delta in winter.** A winter morning at the Skagit delta with Trumpeter Swans and Sandhill Cranes simultaneously present: (1) the deep resonant calling of Swan flocks as they move from roost to feeding areas; (2) the penetrating bugle of Crane flocks in migration; (3) the background sound of Snow Geese (*Anser caerulescens*) in the thousands; (4) the wind across the open agricultural flats; (5) the distant sound of the Skagit River in its lower reach. This is the delta acoustic environment at full winter activity.

## THE VOCAL ANATOMY -- TWO DIFFERENT RESONATING SOLUTIONS

### How Two Birds Achieve the Same Acoustic Goal Through Different Anatomy

The comparative vocal anatomy of Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane:

- **Trumpeter Swan trachea.** The Trumpeter Swan's trachea is significantly elongated and coiled within a groove in the keel of the sternum (breastbone). This coiling is not present in Tundra Swan (*Cygnus columbianus*), the Trumpeter's smaller congener -- and the acoustic difference is audible: the Tundra Swan's call is higher-pitched and less resonant. The sternal coiling of the Trumpeter's trachea adds length (total tracheal length up to 1.5 meters) without requiring the bird's neck to be proportionally longer. This anatomical specialization is unique among waterfowl and directly responsible for the characteristic "trumpet" quality of the call.
- **Sandhill Crane trachea.** The Sandhill Crane's trachea is also elongated, coiled within the sternum, and adds resonating length (1.2-1.5 meters total). The crane's call differs from the swan's not in tracheal anatomy (both use sternal coiling) but in the syringeal anatomy -- the crane's syrinx (voice box, located at the junction of the trachea and bronchi) produces the characteristic rattle and complex harmonics that distinguish the crane call from the swan call.
- **Convergent anatomy, divergent sound.** Both species have independently evolved elongated, coiled tracheae as resonating chambers. The convergent anatomical solution produces similar acoustic power (both calls carry miles) but different acoustic character (smooth and bell-like for the swan; rattling and complex for the crane). This is a documented case of convergent evolution in acoustic anatomy producing different outputs.
- **Why tracheal length matters.** The fundamental resonant frequency of a tube is inversely proportional to its length: longer tubes produce lower fundamental frequencies. Both the Trumpeter Swan and Sandhill Crane have maximized tracheal length through coiling, producing fundamentals in the 250-350 Hz range -- lower than most birds of comparable size. This is why both calls carry so far: lower frequencies experience less atmospheric attenuation than high frequencies, and the long-wavelength sound penetrates through habitat more effectively.

(Sources: Trumpeter Swan tracheal coiling within sternal groove; total tracheal length up to 1.5 meters; Tundra Swan comparison (no coiling, higher pitch); Sandhill Crane tracheal coiling within sternum 1.2-1.5 meters; syringeal anatomy rattling quality; convergent evolution elongated trachea; fundamental frequency inverse to tube length 250-350 Hz; low frequency atmospheric attenuation advantage; long-wavelength penetration)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8 DUAL LAYER

### Frequency, Amplitude, and the Combined Effect

The Trumpeter Swan + Sandhill Crane acoustic analysis:

- **Trumpeter Swan call frequency.** Fundamental: 250-350 Hz. Strong harmonics at 500-700 Hz. Smooth spectral character with well-defined harmonics. Duration: 1.5-3 seconds per call. Call rhythm: 1-2 calls per minute during normal activity, more rapid during alarm or group calling.
- **Sandhill Crane call frequency.** Fundamental: 250-350 Hz (similar to swan but different spectral envelope). Rattling quality adds acoustic energy across 300-1,200 Hz range. Duration: 1-4 seconds per call, often in rolling sequences. Call rhythm: single calls or 2-5 call sequences during migration or congregation.
- **The dual layer.** When both species call simultaneously: (1) the Swan's smooth low fundamental at 250-350 Hz; (2) the Crane's rattling fundamental at 250-350 Hz; (3) the Crane's complex harmonics extending to 1,200+ Hz; (4) the combined field of both species calling creates an acoustic environment covering 250-1,200 Hz. The two calls are distinguishable (smooth vs. rattling character) but combine into a single delta acoustic event.
- **Combined amplitude.** Trumpeter Swan at close range: 90-100 dB. Sandhill Crane at close range: 95-105 dB. At a winter congregation where both species are actively calling: the combined acoustic field approaches 95-110 dB. At distance (100-200 meters, typical birding distance at delta sites): 70-85 dB. The calls are individually intelligible at this distance and the species can be distinguished by ear.
- **E=8 calibration.** E=8 reflects the dual-species presence at a winter congregation. The acoustic event is not the most extreme in the engine (E=9-10 events are louder or more physically intense), but it is among the most acoustically distinguished -- two of the most iconic bird calls in North America, both at their full amplitude, in the same delta habitat simultaneously. The cultural and ecological significance of the pairing elevates the E=8 beyond its absolute acoustic level.
