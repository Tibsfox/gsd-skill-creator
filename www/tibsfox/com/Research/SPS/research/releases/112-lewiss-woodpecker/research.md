## LEWIS'S WOODPECKER -- *Melanerpes lewis*

### Overview

Lewis's Woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*) is one of the most unusual and distinctive woodpeckers in North America. Named for **Meriwether Lewis** of the Lewis and Clark Expedition, who first collected a specimen in 1805 along the Clearwater River in present-day Idaho, this bird defies almost every expectation of what a woodpecker should be.

Lewis's Woodpecker is a **medium-large woodpecker** (26-28 cm, 88-138 g) with a dark greenish-black back, a gray collar and breast, a pink-red belly, and a dark red face. The plumage is unlike any other North American woodpecker -- no bold black-and-white pattern, no bright red crest. The overall impression is of a **dark, crow-like bird** with unexpected pink and red tones. In flight, the broad wings and slow, direct flight (unlike the undulating flight of most woodpeckers) contribute to the crow-like appearance, earning it the informal name "the crow-woodpecker."

But the most remarkable thing about Lewis's Woodpecker is not its appearance -- it is its **behavior**. This is a woodpecker that has largely abandoned the woodpecker lifestyle. It rarely drums. It rarely excavates for insects in bark or wood. Instead, it **catches insects on the wing**, sallying out from exposed perches to snatch flying insects in mid-air -- exactly the foraging strategy of a flycatcher, not a woodpecker. Lewis's Woodpecker is a **flycatching woodpecker**: a bird classified in Picidae that behaves like a tyrannid.

### Taxonomy and Nomenclature

Lewis's Woodpecker belongs to the genus *Melanerpes* -- the same genus as the Acorn Woodpecker (degree 107). *Melanerpes* comes from Greek *melanos* ("black") + *herpes* ("creeper") -- "black creeper." The species epithet *lewis* honors Captain Meriwether Lewis, who collected the type specimen on November 20, 1805, during the Corps of Discovery Expedition.

The Lewis and Clark Expedition was one of the most significant natural history expeditions in American history. Lewis and Clark documented hundreds of species new to Western science as they crossed the continent from St. Louis to the Pacific Coast. Lewis's Woodpecker is one of the expedition's most notable avian discoveries -- a bird so unusual that it surprised even 19th-century naturalists who were accustomed to encountering novel species.

Within the genus *Melanerpes*, Lewis's Woodpecker is an outlier. The Acorn Woodpecker (degree 107) is a highly social, granary-building, acorn-storing specialist. Lewis's Woodpecker is relatively solitary, does not build granaries, and catches insects on the wing. Same genus, radically different ecological strategies.

### Physical Description

- **Size:** 26-28 cm length. 49-52 cm wingspan. 88-138 g weight. Medium-large woodpecker, similar in size to the Hairy Woodpecker but bulkier.
- **Plumage:** Dark greenish-black upperparts (back, wings, tail) with an iridescent quality. Gray collar and breast. Pink-red belly (the most distinctive visual feature -- no other North American woodpecker has a pink belly). Dark red face. No white markings on the back or wings -- completely unlike the bold black-and-white pattern of most woodpeckers.
- **Bill:** Relatively short and slightly curved for a woodpecker -- adapted for catching insects, not for heavy drilling. The bill reflects the bird's flycatching lifestyle: it does not need the chisel-like bill of a drilling specialist.
- **Flight:** Broad-winged, slow, direct. Unlike the undulating (flap-glide-flap) flight of most woodpeckers, Lewis's Woodpecker flies with **steady, crow-like wingbeats**. The direct flight is diagnostic -- if a dark, broad-winged woodpecker flies straight across an open area without undulating, it is almost certainly a Lewis's.
- **Feet:** Zygodactyl (two forward, two back), but Lewis's Woodpecker spends less time clinging to tree trunks than any other North American woodpecker. It often perches on exposed branches, fence posts, or wires -- perches more typical of a flycatcher than a woodpecker.

### The Flycatching Behavior -- A Woodpecker That Doesn't Drill

Lewis's Woodpecker is the most **behaviorally anomalous** woodpecker in North America. Its primary foraging strategy is **aerial insectivory** -- catching flying insects on the wing:

1. **Sallying:** Lewis's Woodpecker perches on an exposed branch, snag, or fence post and watches for flying insects. When an insect passes, the bird launches from the perch, catches the insect in mid-air, and returns to the perch. This is the classic flycatcher foraging strategy -- and it is extremely unusual for a woodpecker.

2. **Gleaning:** When not sallying for flying insects, Lewis's Woodpecker gleans insects from bark surfaces and foliage. Again, this is surface foraging, not excavation. The bird picks insects off surfaces rather than drilling into wood to find them.

3. **Minimal drilling:** Lewis's Woodpecker does drill into wood occasionally, but it is not the primary foraging strategy. The drilling is shallow and infrequent compared to other woodpeckers. The bird's relatively short, slightly curved bill reflects this: it is not optimized for heavy excavation.

4. **Nut and fruit consumption:** In fall and winter, Lewis's Woodpecker shifts to eating nuts (acorns, pine nuts) and fruits. It stores food in crevices in bark -- but does not excavate granary holes like the Acorn Woodpecker. The caching behavior is opportunistic and individualistic, not the elaborate social granary system of its genus-mate.

Why does a woodpecker catch insects like a flycatcher? The answer lies in **habitat**. Lewis's Woodpecker lives in **open habitats** -- open ponderosa pine forest, burned forest with standing dead trees, oak savanna, and open woodland. These are habitats with **sparse canopy and abundant air space** -- ideal for aerial insectivory. In dense forest, there is no room to fly-catch. In open pine woodland, the space between trees becomes a hunting ground. Lewis's Woodpecker evolved to exploit the open-air niche that other woodpeckers ignore.

### Habitat Requirements

Lewis's Woodpecker requires a specific combination of habitat features:

- **Open canopy:** Sparse ponderosa pine, burned forest, oak-pine woodland. The canopy must be open enough for aerial insect-catching. Dense forest is unsuitable.
- **Standing dead trees (snags):** For nesting cavities and elevated perches. Post-fire landscapes with abundant snags are prime Lewis's Woodpecker habitat.
- **Insect abundance:** The open habitat must support flying insect populations sufficient for aerial foraging.
- **Food storage sites:** Bark crevices, broken snags, and other natural cavities for caching nuts and fruits for winter.

The **post-fire landscape** is critical. Forest fires create the open, snag-rich habitat that Lewis's Woodpecker requires. Fire suppression -- the decades-long policy of aggressively suppressing all forest fires in western North America -- has significantly reduced the availability of Lewis's Woodpecker habitat. The bird that evolved to exploit fire-created landscapes is declining because the fires that create its habitat are being suppressed.

## Acoustic Profile: Churring Call

### The Churring Call

Lewis's Woodpecker's most distinctive vocalization is a **harsh, churring call** -- a rough, rolling, somewhat raspy series of notes. The call is not musical; it is grating and dry, with a quality that has been compared to a **squeaky gate** or a **dry rattle**. The churring call is used for contact and territorial purposes.

- **Frequency:** Approximately 1-4 kHz, broadly distributed. The churring quality comes from rapid frequency modulation -- the individual notes in the series are rough and slightly buzzy.
- **Duration:** 1-3 seconds per call bout. The churring is a sustained rolling sound, not a single sharp note.
- **Quality:** Harsh, dry, churring. Not the sharp peek! of the Hairy, not the soft pik of the Downy, not the ringing wuk-wuk-wuk of the Pileated. The Lewis's call is its own thing -- rough and unmusical, distinctive once learned.

### Minimal Drumming

Lewis's Woodpecker **barely drums**. Most woodpeckers use drumming (rapid percussive hammering on resonant wood) as a territorial signal and mate attraction. Lewis's Woodpecker's drumming is rare, weak, and inconsistent. This is another expression of the bird's departure from typical woodpecker behavior -- even the acoustic territory-marking strategy has been largely abandoned. The bird that doesn't drill for food also doesn't drum for communication.

When Lewis's Woodpecker does drum, it is a weak, irregular series -- lacking the power and consistency of the Pileated's thunderous roll or even the Downy's gentle rattle. The drum is almost an afterthought -- a vestige of woodpecker ancestry in a bird that has evolved away from the woodpecker lifestyle.

### Energy: E=4

E=4 -- moderate energy. The churring call is distinctive but not loud or powerful. The minimal drumming reduces the overall acoustic footprint. Lewis's Woodpecker is a quiet woodpecker in a quiet landscape (open pine, burned forest). The E=4 matches the Downy Woodpecker at degree 111, continuing the descent from the Pileated's E=6 maximum (108-109).

## Conservation Status -- A Declining Species

Lewis's Woodpecker is classified as a **species of conservation concern**. The population has declined significantly -- estimated at **50-70% decline** from historical levels. The primary threats:

1. **Fire suppression:** Decades of aggressive fire suppression in western North America have reduced the open, snag-rich, post-fire habitats that Lewis's Woodpecker requires. The bird evolved to exploit fire-created landscapes. Without fire, the habitat disappears.

2. **Habitat loss:** Conversion of open ponderosa pine forest and oak savanna to other land uses (agriculture, development, dense plantation forestry). The specific habitat combination Lewis's Woodpecker needs (open canopy + snags + insect abundance) is increasingly rare.

3. **Competition for nest cavities:** European Starlings and other cavity-nesting species compete for the tree cavities Lewis's Woodpecker uses for nesting. Starlings are aggressive competitors that can evict woodpeckers from established cavities.

4. **Snag removal:** Management practices that remove standing dead trees (snags) for safety or aesthetic reasons directly remove Lewis's Woodpecker nesting and perching sites.

Lewis's Woodpecker is listed as a **Partners in Flight Watch List species** and is a species of concern in multiple state wildlife action plans. The bird's conservation depends on restoring fire to western forests -- prescribed burning and managed wildfire that creates the open, snag-rich habitats the species requires.

In the Puget Sound region, Lewis's Woodpecker is a **rare summer visitor** to the eastern slopes of the Cascades. It is not found in the lowland urban and suburban areas where the Downy Woodpecker (degree 111) thrives. The contrast is ecological: the Downy succeeds everywhere, including suburbs. Lewis's Woodpecker succeeds only in a specific, fire-dependent habitat that is disappearing.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Partners in Flight; IUCN Red List; Vierling et al. 2008; Abele et al. 2004)

## The Lewis and Clark Connection

Lewis's Woodpecker carries the name of **Captain Meriwether Lewis** (1774-1809), co-leader of the Corps of Discovery Expedition (1804-1806). The expedition, commissioned by President Thomas Jefferson, was the first American overland expedition to the Pacific Coast. Lewis was the expedition's primary naturalist, collecting specimens and documenting species throughout the journey.

Lewis collected the type specimen of the woodpecker that would bear his name on **November 20, 1805**, along the Clearwater River in present-day Idaho. The bird was formally described by Alexander Wilson in 1811 as *Picus lewis*, later reclassified to *Melanerpes lewis*. The species is one of several named for members of the Lewis and Clark Expedition (others include Clark's Nutcracker, *Nucifraga columbiana*, named for William Clark -- and documented in the SPS engine at degree 104).

The Lewis and Clark connection adds a historical dimension to the species. Lewis's Woodpecker is not just an unusual bird -- it is a bird whose Western scientific documentation begins with one of the most significant exploration expeditions in American history. The bird was unknown to Western science before Lewis encountered it. The open pine forests and post-fire landscapes where Lewis found the bird in 1805 are the same habitats the species requires today -- and the same habitats that are disappearing.

(Sources: Moulton, Gary E., ed. *The Lewis and Clark Journals.* University of Nebraska Press. 2003; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)
