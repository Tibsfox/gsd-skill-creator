Lewis’s Woodpecker

Melanerpes lewis (Picidae) — E=4 — the flycatching woodpecker
churring call — the woodpecker that doesn’t drill. Catches insects on the wing like a flycatcher. Named for Meriwether Lewis (1805). Sixth consecutive Picidae (107–112) — one short of the Corvidae septet record. Pink belly, crow-like flight, open pine habitat. Declining species — fire suppression removing its habitat. E=4, barely drums.

The Flycatching Woodpecker — A Bird That Defies Its Family

Lewis’s Woodpecker has largely abandoned the woodpecker lifestyle. It rarely drums. It rarely drills. Instead, it catches insects on the wing — sallying out from exposed perches to snatch flying insects in mid-air. This is the foraging strategy of a flycatcher, not a woodpecker. In open ponderosa pine forest, the space between trees becomes a hunting ground. A woodpecker that has evolved past drilling — the crow-woodpecker.

Declining Species — 50-70% Population Loss

Lewis’s Woodpecker has declined an estimated 50-70% from historical levels. The primary cause: fire suppression. The bird evolved to exploit post-fire landscapes — open, snag-rich, burned forest. Decades of aggressive fire suppression have removed the habitat this species requires. Without fire, there is no Lewis’s Woodpecker. Listed as a Partners in Flight Watch List species. Conservation depends on restoring fire to western forests.

Named for Meriwether Lewis — Corps of Discovery, 1805

Captain Meriwether Lewis collected the type specimen on November 20, 1805, along the Clearwater River in present-day Idaho. One of the most notable avian discoveries of the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806). Eight degrees earlier, Clark’s Nutcracker (degree 104) documented the other expedition leader’s namesake species. Both expedition-named species now in the engine.

Fire-Dependent Habitat — Open Pine and Burned Forest

Lewis’s Woodpecker requires open ponderosa pine forest, burned forest with standing snags, and oak-pine woodland. The open canopy provides space for aerial insect-catching. Standing dead trees provide perches and nest cavities. Post-fire landscapes are prime habitat. Fire suppression is the primary threat — the bird that evolved to exploit fire is disappearing because the fires are being suppressed.

Acoustic Profile: Churring Call

Lewis’s Woodpecker’s most distinctive vocalization is a harsh, churring call — a rough, rolling, raspy series of notes. Not musical; grating and dry, like a squeaky gate or dry rattle. Frequency approximately 1-4 kHz. Duration 1-3 seconds. The bird barely drums — even the acoustic territory-marking strategy has been largely abandoned. E=4 — moderate energy. A quiet woodpecker in a quiet landscape.

Same Genus as Acorn Woodpecker (107)

Both Lewis’s Woodpecker and the Acorn Woodpecker (degree 107) belong to genus Melanerpes. But their strategies are radically different: the Acorn is colonial, granary-building, and social. Lewis’s is solitary, crevice-caching, and flycatching. Same genus, opposite ecological strategies. The genus Melanerpes spans the full range of woodpecker behavioral diversity.
