## THE RUT -- FALL TERRITORIAL BEHAVIOR

### November-December

The buck grunt occurs during the fall rut:

- **Timing.** The rut (breeding season) occurs in November-December in western Washington. Bucks become increasingly active, territorial, and vocal as the rut approaches.
- **Antlers.** By November, bucks have fully developed antlers -- grown since spring, hardened and polished by rubbing against trees. The antlers serve as both weapons and visual displays. After the rut, antlers are shed (December-March).
- **Territorial behavior.** Bucks establish and patrol territories during the rut. They mark territory by rubbing antlers on trees (creating "buck rubs"), scraping the ground with hooves ("scrapes"), and depositing scent from glands on their forehead and legs.
- **The grunt.** The grunt is the vocal component of territorial display. A low, guttural "uurrh" repeated at intervals. The grunt announces the buck's presence to does (attracting mates) and to rival bucks (warning of territorial claim). The grunt increases in frequency and intensity as the rut peaks.
- **Sparring.** When two bucks encounter each other, they may spar -- locking antlers and pushing. These contests are usually brief and rarely lethal. The grunt often precedes sparring as an escalation signal.

## ANTLER GROWTH AND SHEDDING

### The Annual Cycle

The buck's antler cycle is one of the fastest tissue growth processes in the animal kingdom:

- **Growth.** Antlers grow from pedicles on the skull beginning in spring (April-May). They grow 1-2 cm per day, covered in soft, blood-rich "velvet" tissue.
- **Velvet shedding.** By late August-September, the antlers are fully grown and the velvet dries and is rubbed off against trees and brush.
- **Hardened antlers.** October-November: hard, polished antlers. The weapons are ready for the rut.
- **Shedding.** December-March: after the rut, antlers are shed. The pedicles heal, and the cycle begins again in spring.
- **Size and age.** Antler size correlates with age and nutrition. A healthy mature buck (4-6 years) may have 3-4 points per side. Younger bucks have smaller antlers -- spike antlers in their first year.
- **Acoustic connection.** The antler cycle is connected to the grunt: bucks only grunt during the rut, when antlers are hardened. The grunt, the antlers, and the territorial behavior form a coordinated system.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Black-tailed Deer Buck

**Buck Grunt (Territorial/Rut):**
- **Frequency:** 50-200 Hz. Low, guttural.
- **Character:** Short, forceful "uurrh." Resonant. The sound vibrates in the chest of the buck, amplified by the body cavity.
- **Duration:** 0.3-1.0 seconds per grunt. Repeated at intervals of 5-30 seconds during active display.
- **Context:** Fall rut (November-December). Territorial patrol. Presence announcement. Pre-sparring escalation. Doe attraction.
- **Carry distance:** 100-300 meters in forest. Carried by low frequencies that propagate better through vegetation than the fawn's higher-pitched bleat.

**Snort (Alarm):**
- **Frequency:** 200-800 Hz. Higher than grunt.
- **Character:** Sharp, explosive exhalation through the nose. The alarm call.
- **Context:** Predator detection. Used by both sexes year-round.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. Low, guttural, territorial. More forceful than the fawn bleat (E=3). The sound of controlled assertion. The statement: "I am here."
