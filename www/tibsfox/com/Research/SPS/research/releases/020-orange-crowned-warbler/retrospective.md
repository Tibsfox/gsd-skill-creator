## Purpose

This is the twenty-first release of the PNW 360 Species engine and the **first species of the third decade** (degrees 20--29). The Orange-crowned Warbler introduces **four new structural dimensions** to the circle: (1) the fade/evaporation -- the song dissolves rather than concluding, the natural equivalent of the pop music fade-out; (2) the ADSR release envelope -- the trill's gradual parametric decay maps to the Release parameter in synthesizer envelope design, the 21st acoustic element in the progression; (3) the Parulidae quintet is complete -- five warblers, five genera, five strategies, five song endings that exhaust the possibilities of how a song can conclude; (4) the hidden crown -- identification by absence of features, the name that references what you cannot see.

The Orange-crowned Warbler also expands the interspecies eavesdropping alarm network to five nodes (adding the first Parulidae receiver), introduces the first partial migrant to the engine, breaks the riparian habitat streak with an upland shrubby slope ecosystem, and extends the engine's motif of hidden/absent features (degrees 0, 4, 10, 20).

## Four Structural Advances

### 1. The Fade / Evaporation: The Song That Dissolves

The Orange-crowned Warbler is the first species in the engine whose song ends by **gradual dissolution** rather than a defined gesture. Every previous species either:
- Ended with a terminal accent or contrasting syllable (degrees 14, 16)
- Stopped abruptly (degrees 11, 15)
- Repeated indefinitely until stopping (degrees 13, 17)
- Descended to a resting phrase (degree 19)

The Orange-crowned does none of these. Its song **fades** -- pitch descends, amplitude decreases, and the trill dissolves into the ambient soundscape. There is no discrete boundary between song and not-song. The transition is continuous. This is the natural equivalent of the **pop music fade-out**: the mixing board faders pulled down gradually until the signal disappears into the noise floor.

### 2. The ADSR Release Envelope: The 21st Acoustic Element

In synthesizer design, the ADSR envelope (Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release) shapes every sound. The **Release** parameter controls the transition from sustain to silence -- how quickly (or slowly) the sound fades after the note is "released." The Orange-crowned Warbler's trill has a **long Release** -- the trill does not cut off but decays gradually. This is the opposite of the Oregon Junco (degree 11), whose trill has a **zero Release** -- instant off, abrupt cessation.

The engine now contains both extremes: zero release (degree 11) and long release (degree 20). The ADSR Release is the 21st element in the acoustic progression.

### 3. The Parulidae Quintet Complete: Five Endings

Five warblers, five song endings:

| Degree | Species | Ending Type | Energy Trajectory |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14 | MacGillivray's | Resolution (contrasting terminal note) | Flat then down |
| 15 | Wilson's | Interruption (accelerates and stops) | Builds then cuts |
| 16 | Yellow | Climax (builds to peak) | Ascending |
| 17 | Common Yellowthroat | Repetition (constant energy) | Flat |

These five endings exhaust the possibilities. The Parulidae quintet is a closed set, just as the Catharus triptych (degrees 7, 18, 19) was a closed set of pitch directions.

### 4. The Hidden Crown: Names That Reference What Is Concealed

The Orange-crowned Warbler is named for a feature almost never visible -- the concealed orange crown patch. This extends the engine's motif of hidden features:

- Degree 0: Silence (sound defined by absence)
- Degree 4: Invisible in darkness (heard, not seen)
- Degree 10: Noise floor (barely audible)
- Degree 20: Hidden crown (named for what you cannot see)

Four instances in twenty-one degrees. The engine increasingly acknowledges that the most diagnostic feature is not always the most visible one.

## What Worked

### 1. The warbler quintet provides a structurally complete taxonomy of song endings
Five warblers, five endings that exhaust the possibilities. This parallels the Catharus triptych's completeness for pitch directions. The engine now has two "closed sets": three pitch directions (Catharus) and five song conclusions (Parulidae).

### 2. The ADSR release envelope is a genuinely new acoustic concept
No previous species had a song that faded gradually. The release envelope is a precise, well-defined concept from synthesizer design that maps cleanly to the Orange-crowned's song behavior. The comparison to the Oregon Junco (degree 11, zero release) gives the concept structural depth.

### 3. The shrubby slope breaks the riparian streak
Six consecutive riparian-associated ecosystems (degrees 14--19) was becoming monotonous. The upland shrubby slope is a genuine habitat departure and introduces early-successional ecology to the engine.

### 4. The partial migrant introduces a new migration category
The binary migrant/resident classification that served the engine through 20 degrees is now insufficient. The Orange-crowned Warbler's partial migration -- some stay, some go, the decision is conditional -- adds nuance to the engine's ecological vocabulary.

### 5. The interspecies eavesdropping network reaches five nodes
Adding the Orange-crowned Warbler as a Parulidae receiver in the chickadee alarm network connects the warbler family to the interspecies communication system. The network now has meaningful scale: 2 broadcasters, 3 receivers, spanning 5 families.

### 6. The hidden crown theme connects to degrees 0, 4, and 10
Four species whose names or identities reference something hidden, absent, or barely perceptible. This is becoming a genuine engine-wide motif, not an isolated observation.

### 7. The Whitney Menge parallel is structurally resonant
Both are understated, community-embedded, hidden-quality presences. The parallel works on all four axes without forcing.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. STILL only one non-passerine (the heron at degree 0) -- FOURTEEN RETROSPECTIVES FLAGGING THIS
Twenty of twenty-one species are passerines. **This is now beyond urgent. It is structural negligence.** The Orange-crowned Warbler is yet another small passerine songbird. The engine has documented twenty passerine songs and zero non-vocal sounds. A woodpecker, a hummingbird, a kingfisher, a raptor -- ANY non-passerine -- is now the single most critical need. **This must be addressed at degree 21. Non-negotiable.**

### 2. SEVEN neotropical migrants plus one partial migrant
Eight of the last nine degrees (14--20, with degree 20 as a partial migrant) are migratory species. The migration bias remains severe despite the partial-migrant nuance. A strict RESIDENT is still urgently needed.

### 3. Energy-3 plateau is now SIX species deep
Six consecutive energy-3 species (degrees 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20). The energy arc has stalled for a full third of the second decade. High-energy species (energy 4 or 5) remain entirely absent. Woodpecker drumming, raptor scream, corvid territorial call -- all would break the plateau.

### 4. Still no percussive / non-vocal sound
Twenty-one degrees of syrinx-produced sound. No mechanical percussion (woodpecker drumming), no non-vocal sound production of any kind. This gap is now endemic.

### 5. Still no marine or true shoreline species
Twenty-one degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" and still no species that lives on or in the Sound. The estuary edge (heron, degree 0) remains the only aquatic connection. Gulls, terns, murrelets, auklets, cormorants, scoters -- all absent.

### 6. Parulidae saturation after degree 20
Five warblers in seven degrees (14--20). The family is now overrepresented relative to its actual species richness in the PNW avifauna. Degree 21 should emphatically NOT be another warbler. The quintet is complete. Close the family cluster.

### 7. Passerine acoustic homogeneity
All twenty passerine species produce sound via the syrinx. The timbral palette is limited to the range of passerine vocal production. Non-passerine sounds -- drumming, buzzing, screaming, wing-produced sounds -- would dramatically expand the timbral vocabulary.

## Recommendations for Release 021

The third decade has begun. Twenty-one species. The priorities are now overwhelming and have been consistent for fourteen consecutive retrospectives:

1. **A NON-PASSERINE species. THIS IS NON-NEGOTIABLE.** Fourteen retrospectives. Twenty passerines. One heron. The engine cannot continue adding passerines without structural failure. A **woodpecker** (Picidae) addresses FIVE open deficits simultaneously: non-passerine, percussive sound, resident, energy 4+, and new family. The Pileated Woodpecker, Downy Woodpecker, or Northern Flicker are all strong candidates. A raptor (Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk) addresses four deficits. A hummingbird (Anna's or Rufous) addresses three. Any of these is better than another passerine.

2. **A RESIDENT species.** Eight of the last nine degrees are migratory or partially migratory. A year-round resident is structurally necessary for the engine's ecological balance.

3. **Energy 4 or 5.** Six consecutive energy-3 species. The energy dimension has flatlined. A woodpecker drum (energy 5) or raptor scream (energy 4--5) would restore dynamic range.

4. **A percussive / non-vocal sound.** Twenty-one degrees of syrinx-only production. Mechanical percussion is the single most important timbral addition the engine can make.

5. **A marine or shoreline species.** The Sound of Puget Sound needs the Sound. A gull, murrelet, or cormorant would connect the engine to its namesake body of water.

6. **NO MORE WARBLERS.** The Parulidae quintet is complete. Five is enough. Close the family cluster and move to underrepresented families.

**The woodpecker is the structural keystone.** A single woodpecker at degree 21 resolves five of the six top recommendations simultaneously. No other single species has this impact.
