## FIRST PHOCOENIDAE -- THE PORPOISE FAMILY

### Porpoises Enter the Engine

Degree 186 marks the entry of Phocoenidae (porpoises) into the SPS engine:

- **FIRST PHOCOENIDAE.** The porpoise family enters. Phocoenidae are small cetaceans -- distinguished from dolphins (Delphinidae) by their spade-shaped teeth (vs conical), smaller size, and generally more robust body shape.
- **New cetacean order.** Porpoises are cetaceans (Order Cetacea) -- the same order as whales and dolphins. This is the first cetacean in the engine. The SPS engine's marine mammals so far have been pinnipeds (seals, sea lions, fur seals) and a mustelid (sea otter). With the Dall's Porpoise, the engine enters the cetacean realm.
- **Porpoise vs dolphin.** Porpoises (Phocoenidae) and dolphins (Delphinidae) are different families. Key differences: porpoises have flat, spade-shaped teeth; dolphins have conical teeth. Porpoises are generally smaller and more robust. Porpoises are generally less social than dolphins. The Dall's Porpoise is the first; a dolphin will follow.

## THE ROOSTER TAIL -- 35 MPH ON THE SURFACE

### Speed and Spray

The Dall's Porpoise's defining behavior is its high-speed surface swimming:

- **Speed:** Up to 35 mph (55 km/h). The fastest porpoise in the world. One of the fastest marine mammals overall, rivaling some dolphins.
- **Rooster-tail mechanism:** When swimming at speed just below the surface, the Dall's Porpoise creates a distinctive cone-shaped spray of water -- the "rooster tail." This happens because the animal's body is just below the surface but its dorsal area breaks through, creating a wake that sprays upward and backward.
- **Identification marker:** The rooster tail is the primary field identification marker. Even at great distance, whale watchers and researchers identify Dall's Porpoises by their distinctive splash pattern. No other cetacean creates this exact spray.
- **Bow riding:** Dall's Porpoises frequently ride the bow waves of boats and ships -- accelerating to match vessel speed and surfing the pressure wave. This is the most common interaction between Dall's Porpoises and humans in Puget Sound.
- **Energy expenditure:** The high-speed swimming requires enormous caloric expenditure. Dall's Porpoises eat 12-17 kg of fish and squid daily to fuel their speed. Their metabolism is exceptionally high.

(Sources: Jefferson et al. 2015; Dall's porpoise swimming studies; field identification guides; engine internal analysis)

## BLACK AND WHITE -- THE TUXEDO CETACEAN

### Distinctive Coloring

The Dall's Porpoise has striking black-and-white coloring:

- **Pattern:** Predominantly black body with a large white patch on the flank and belly. The white patch is variable between individuals. The dorsal fin often has white frosting on the tip.
- **Similar to orca:** The black-and-white pattern has led to casual comparison with orcas (killer whales), though the Dall's Porpoise is much smaller (1.8-2.2 m vs 6-8 m for orca). The coloring may serve similar functions: disruptive camouflage, species recognition, or thermoregulation.
- **Robust body:** The Dall's Porpoise has a thick, muscular body -- more robust than most porpoises or dolphins. The body shape is built for power and speed, not acrobatics.

## ULTRASONIC ECHOLOCATION

### Above Human Hearing

The Dall's Porpoise's echolocation is inaudible to humans:

- **Frequency range:** Echolocation clicks operate at frequencies above 100 kHz -- typically 120-160 kHz. Human hearing range is approximately 20 Hz to 20 kHz. The Dall's Porpoise's clicks are 5-8 times higher than the upper limit of human hearing.
- **Narrow-band clicks:** Phocoenidae echolocation clicks are characteristically narrow-band and high-frequency -- different from the broader-band clicks of dolphins. This is a family-level characteristic.
- **NBHF (Narrow-Band High-Frequency) clicks:** Porpoises are classified as NBHF echolocators. This click type may have evolved as a "stealth" adaptation -- orcas (their primary predator) cannot hear these frequencies, making porpoise echolocation invisible to predators.
- **Stealth hypothesis:** The NBHF echolocation may be an anti-predator adaptation. By echolocating above the hearing range of orcas (~100 kHz ceiling), porpoises can navigate and hunt without alerting their primary predator. Silence as survival.

(Sources: Au et al. 1999; Morisaka & Connor 2007; NBHF echolocation research; engine internal analysis)

## PUGET SOUND POPULATIONS

### Resident in Deep Channels

- **Population:** Estimated 2,000-4,000 Dall's Porpoises in Washington inland waters (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands). Tens of thousands more in the outer coast and North Pacific.
- **Habitat:** Prefers deep water channels and areas with strong tidal currents. In Puget Sound, found primarily in the deep channels of the central and northern Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and around the San Juan Islands.
- **Year-round resident:** Present in Puget Sound year-round, though more commonly sighted in fall and winter.
- **Diet:** Squid, lanternfish, herring, anchovies. Forages in deep water, often diving to 500+ meters.
- **Group size:** Typically in groups of 2-10. Occasionally larger aggregations of 100+ during feeding events.

(Sources: WDFW; NOAA; Puget Sound cetacean surveys; engine internal analysis)
