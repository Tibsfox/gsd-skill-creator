Townsend’s Warbler

Setophaga townsendi
buzzy ascending song — the canopy buzz, 7th Parulidae (record), consecutive Setophaga pair with BTG (38), hybridization story

The 40th Acoustic Element: The Canopy Buzz

An ascending buzzy warbler song from the crown of tall conifers: zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee. After the drawl’s lazy flat delivery (degree 38), the canopy buzz adds vertical lift. Same buzzy character, different trajectory. The drawl sat in one place. The canopy buzz climbs. The terminal phrases rise, as if the buzz itself is reaching for the top of the forest.

Drawl → canopy buzz: flat (38, E=4, oak-pine) → ascending (39, E=4, conifer crown). From lazy to deliberate. From open oak to dense conifer. From mid-canopy to crown.

The Hybrid Zone — Evolution in Real Time

Townsend’s Warbler hybridizes with both Black-throated Gray Warbler and Hermit Warbler (S. occidentalis) where ranges overlap in the Cascades [3, 4, 5]. Townsend’s males are behaviorally dominant [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Hybrid offspring carry Townsend’s traits. The hybrid zone moves south and east over decades — into BTG and Hermit territory.

Competitive exclusion via introgression: Townsend’s is displacing both species through genetic absorption. Hermit Warbler’s range has contracted southward. The engine at degrees 38–39 maps both the expanding species and its contested frontier.

Townsend’s Moth-Eater

Setophaga townsendi — “Townsend’s moth-eater.” Named for John Kirk Townsend (1809–1851), naturalist and collector on Nathaniel Wyeth’s 1834 expedition to the Columbia River. A patronym: the third Setophaga in the engine, the third naming convention — morphology (petechia, “spots”), process (nigrescens, “darkening”), person (townsendi). Formerly Dendroica townsendi before the 2011 AOS genus merger.

The Sound: Buzzy Ascending Song

Zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee — 4–6 buzzy phrases at approximately 4,000–8,500 Hz [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The first 3–4 phrases are roughly isometric (similar pitch). The final 1–2 phrases rise, giving the song its distinctive ascending ending. Buzzy, thin, sibilant timbre — more zee than weazy. Moderate tempo (~3–4 phrases/second [NEEDS VERIFICATION]).

Parulidae — 7 Members (Engine Record)

Seventh Parulidae. First family to reach 7. Leads Hirundinidae and Turdidae (5 each) by two. Two structural arcs:

  • Quintet (14–20): Five species, five genera, five song endings. All E=3. Wet/mesic habitats.
  • Pair (38–39): Two Setophaga, hybridizing species. Both E=4. Dry-to-wet gradient.

No other family has two structural arcs. The Parulidae footprint is the deepest in the engine.

Yellow and Black at the Top of the World

Striking yellow-and-black face pattern. Bright yellow face with broad black cheek patch, black crown, yellow supercilium. Yellow breast, white belly, olive-green back. Visible from 30+ meters below — evolved for identification at canopy height.

Setophaga chromatic gradient: Yellow Warbler (16) = maximum yellow. Black-throated Gray (38) = one yellow dot on ink drawing. Townsend’s (39) = bold yellow-and-black pattern. Three genera, three color strategies.

E=4 Continues — 16th Degree, 19 Without E=3

E=4 now at 16 degrees (41.0%). E=3 absent for 19 consecutive degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 26 degrees. E=1 absent for 27 degrees. Degrees 21–39 exclusively E=4 and E=5. The lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum is absent. Dynamic range compression at its worst.

The Conifer Crown

Townsend’s Warbler forages at 15–45 m — the crown of tall conifers [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The highest-foraging warbler in the engine. Douglas fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, mountain hemlock. Breeds in mature and old-growth stands with complex canopy structure. Often heard but not seen — the song is the primary detection method.

Winter exception: Unlike most warblers, a significant population winters along the PNW coast — in parks, gardens, and coastal forests. A 50-meter canopy breeder in summer may visit a suet feeder in Seattle in January.
