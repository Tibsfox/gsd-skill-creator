## Purpose

This is the fortieth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-39), the seventh Parulidae -- a record for any family -- and the second consecutive warbler, forming a *Setophaga* pair with the Black-throated Gray Warbler at degree 38. The engine needed to explore what happens when two congeneric species are placed back-to-back: not just taxonomic proximity but biological interaction. Townsend's Warbler and Black-throated Gray Warbler hybridize. They compete. One is displacing the other. Degrees 38-39 map both sides of an active evolutionary contest.

Five structural contributions:

1. **The canopy buzz -- the 40th acoustic element.** An ascending buzzy warbler song from the crown of tall conifers. After the drawl's lazy flat delivery (degree 38), the canopy buzz adds vertical lift. Same buzzy *Setophaga* timbre, different trajectory: the drawl sits in one place, the canopy buzz climbs. Terminal phrases rise, as if the buzz itself is reaching for the upper canopy. Named for both WHERE it comes from (canopy crown) and HOW it sounds (buzzy ascending phrases).

2. **The *Setophaga* consecutive pair -- degrees 38-39.** The engine's first back-to-back same-family pair and first back-to-back same-genus pair. More importantly: the first consecutive species that directly interact biologically. Previous consecutive species were related taxonomically but did not hybridize. These two do. The pair maps an ecological gradient (dry oak-pine to wet conifer canopy) and an evolutionary dynamic (competitive exclusion through introgression).

3. **Parulidae at seven -- engine record.** No other family has reached seven. Parulidae leads Hirundinidae and Turdidae (five each) by two. The family now has two structural arcs: the quintet (degrees 14-17, 20) mapping five song endings at E=3, and the pair (degrees 38-39) mapping an ecological gradient and hybrid zone at E=4. No other family has two arcs.

4. **Competitive exclusion via introgression.** Townsend's Warbler is displacing both Black-throated Gray and Hermit Warblers through hybridization -- one of the best-documented cases in North American birds. The hybrid zone moves over decades. This is evolution observable on a human timescale. The engine captures a moment in an ongoing process.

5. **Conifer canopy crown -- the highest warbler stratum.** Townsend's Warbler forages at 15-45 m in the crown of tall conifers [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. This completes the engine's warbler vertical gradient: ground (MacGillivray's, 14) through understory, mid-canopy, and now crown (Townsend's, 39). The engine's seven warblers span the full vertical column of the forest.

## The Consecutive Pair

### Degrees 38-39: A Biological Conversation

The engine has never placed two species from the same genus back-to-back before. The closest precedent was the waxwing pair (Cedar at 31, Bohemian at 32) -- same family, different genera, overlapping range but no hybridization. The warbler pair at 38-39 is something new: same genus, hybridizing, one displacing the other.

| Dimension | Degree 38 (Black-throated Gray) | Degree 39 (Townsend's) |
|---|---|---|
| Plumage | Black-white + 1 yellow dot | **Yellow-and-black face** |

Everything shifts except energy and genus. Dry to wet, low to high, flat to ascending, open to dense. The pair maps two sides of the Cascades ecological transition -- the exact boundary where the hybrid zone exists.

### The Hybrid Zone Story

The consecutive placement of BTG and Townsend's allows the engine to tell a story it could not tell with isolated species: the story of a moving boundary. In the southern Washington Cascades and Oregon Cascades, these two species meet at the ecotone between dry oak-pine woodland and wet conifer forest. They hybridize. Townsend's is behaviorally dominant [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The boundary shifts south over decades. Townsend's genes introgress into BTG populations.

The same pattern plays out between Townsend's and Hermit Warbler -- but the Hermit Warbler story is more dire. Hermit Warbler's range has contracted southward. It once bred on the Olympic Peninsula [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. It no longer does. Townsend's occupies those forests now.

The engine at degrees 38-39 captures both the expanding species (Townsend's) and one of its competitors (Black-throated Gray). The Hermit Warbler is not yet in the engine, but its ghost haunts the hybrid zone data.

## What Worked

### 1. The consecutive pair tells a story single releases cannot
Two hybridizing species placed back-to-back creates a narrative that isolated entries cannot achieve. The hybrid zone, the competitive exclusion, the moving boundary -- these emerge from the PAIRING, not from either species alone. The engine has learned that adjacent degrees can map relationships, not just individuals.

### 2. "Canopy buzz" captures habitat and sound
The element name works because it is bimodal: "canopy" for where it comes from (the crown of tall conifers), "buzz" for what it sounds like (buzzy ascending phrases). The name anchors the sound in its habitat. You hear "canopy buzz" and you know: high, buzzy, conifer crown.

### 3. The hybridization story is genuinely important
This is one of the best-documented cases of competitive exclusion through hybridization in North American birds. The engine is not just cataloging species -- it is documenting an ongoing evolutionary process. The research content has real scientific substance.

### 4. The vertical column completion is satisfying
Seven warblers spanning ground to crown. This was not planned but emerged naturally from species selection. The warbler family alone can tell you your elevation in the forest canopy. Good structural result.

### 5. The *Setophaga* trio creates a productive genus cluster
Three members of one genus is enough to see patterns -- chromatic gradient, song contour variety, ecosystem span. The genus becomes a mini-study within the family.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. EXISTENTIAL CRISIS: No marine species in 39 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
**THIRTY-NINE DEGREES.** The water has not spoken. This is now the engine's most severe structural deficit by an even wider margin. The project is named for PUGET SOUND. The Sound has been silent for 39 degrees. This MUST be resolved at degree 40. ABSOLUTE, NON-NEGOTIABLE PRIORITY. ### 2. E=3 absent for 19 degrees -- CRITICAL
Nineteenth consecutive degree without E=3. NINETEENTH. The last E=3 was degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler). E=2 absent since degree 13 (26 degrees). E=1 since degree 12 (27 degrees). Degrees 21-39 have been exclusively E=4 and E=5. The lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum is completely absent. This is structural damage. A marine species at E=2 would address both crises.

### 3. No corvid in 39 degrees
Steller's Jay, American Crow, Common Raven. Thirty-nine degrees. The PNW's most intelligent and acoustically complex birds. URGENT.

### 4. No raptor in 39 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Osprey, Peregrine Falcon. A marine raptor (Bald Eagle, Osprey) could address both the raptor gap and the marine crisis.

### 5. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Thirty-five degrees since the Spotted Owl. The night has been silent too long.

### 6. Summer season continues
Degree 39 is another summer species (though with winter coastal presence). The engine has heavy summer representation. Resident and winter species need attention.

### 7. Parulidae pair may be too much warbler
Two consecutive warblers risks warbler fatigue. The engine has now had 7 of 40 entries (17.5%) from one family. While this reflects biological reality (Parulidae IS the largest family), it risks feeling repetitive. Degree 40 should be as far from Parulidae as possible.

High in the eastern Cascades, where the ponderosa stands open and the air smells of sun-warmed bark and resin, a small bird with a crimson crown sat at the top of the tallest snag and sang a cadenza -- a rolling, tumbling warble incorporating the crossbill's call, the tanager's hoarse phrase, the robin's carol, the chickadee's fee-bee. The Cassin's Finch. The cadenza. The soloist played every instrument. And somewhere in a Douglas fir canopy, a flock of small birds hung upside down from cones, working the scales with bills that cross at the tips. Kip-kip-kip. Sharp. Percussive. The Red Crossbill. The dialect. The type call that sorts the tribe. And then -- a sound that is not a call, not a song, not a drum. A hum. Low, continuous, steady, the sound of air pushed through feathers fifty-two times every second. A copper blur hovering at a red columbine. The Rufous Hummingbird. The thrum. Three grams of defiance. The red flame-bearer. And then a different sound from a different hummingbird. Not a hum but a crack. Not continuous but instantaneous. A small iridescent green bird with a head dipped in molten magenta perches on a bare branch in a Seattle backyard in January. It climbs. Forty meters. It folds its wings. It falls. Twenty-seven meters per second. Three hundred and eighty-five body lengths per second. The tail spreads. The feather vibrates. A chirp. Explosive. The sonic boom. Anna's Hummingbird. The one that stayed. And now -- higher than either of them went, above the tree line, above the urban gardens, above the flower meadows, in a subalpine meadow at 3,000 meters where the paintbrush burns red against gray talus -- a sound so small it might not be there at all. A soft, rising tsee-tsee. The smallest bird in North America. The filament. The thinnest thread of sound. The pilot light that still burns. And now, eighteen degrees after the last warbler evaporated into silence, below the mountain meadow, below the hummingbird trio's extremes, in a dry woodland where Garry oaks stand in open sun and ponderosa pines drop their needles on bunchgrass -- a bird drawn in black ink on white paper, with one small dot of yellow between its eye and its bill, sits on an oak branch in the dappled light and sings. Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy. Slow. Buzzy. Unhurried. The Black-throated Gray Warbler. The drawl. And now -- one degree later, one ecosystem over, the dry woodland giving way to wet conifer forest, the oaks yielding to Douglas fir and western hemlock, the open canopy closing into cathedral columns of bark and needles rising thirty, forty, fifty meters -- at the very top, where the crown meets the sky, where the light is brightest and the wind bends the branches and the arthropods cluster on the sunlit needles -- a bird with a face painted in yellow and black, a bold cheek patch like a mask, a bright supercilium catching the light, sits at the crown of a Douglas fir and sings. Zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee. Buzzy. Ascending. The terminal phrases climbing. The same Setophaga timbre as its oak-dwelling cousin one degree back, but the song goes up where the drawl stayed flat. The Townsend's Warbler. Townsend's moth-eater. The seventh Parulidae. The third Setophaga. The crown singer. A bird that hybridizes with the species that came before it, whose genes mingle at the boundary between dry and wet, whose range is expanding as the Black-throated Gray yields ground at the margins, whose ascending buzz from the canopy crown says what the drawl could not: I am still climbing. After the drawl's deceleration, after the flat contentment of the oak-pine canopy, the canopy buzz lifts. The warbler voice goes up. The forest goes up. The engine goes up. Zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee.
