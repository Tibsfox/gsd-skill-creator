Lesser Goldfinch

Spinus psaltria
high thin tsee-ee-ee — the mosaic, 5th Fringillidae, 3rd Spinus (first genus with three members), consecutive goldfinch pair with degree 43, the smallest North American Spinus, “female lute player of the thorn bush,” remarkable mimic of 39+ species, range expanding northward into the PNW

The 45th Acoustic Element: The Mosaic

A song assembled from fragments of dozens of other species’ vocalizations, woven together with the bird’s own bright twittering phrases into a single continuous performance. Individual males maintain repertoires of 70–105 unique phrase types, with approximately half being mimicry of other species’ songs and calls. 39 avian species plus one mammal documented as mimicry sources at a single site. Each fragment is a copy. The arrangement is unique.

The first acoustic element defined by interspecific vocal appropriation — a song that exists because the singer listened to other species and remembered what they said.

Consecutive Goldfinch Pair — Degrees 43 & 44

American Goldfinch (43) then Lesser Goldfinch (44). Same genus, consecutive degrees. The engine’s third consecutive same-genus pair, after the flicker pair (21–22) and the waxwing pair (31–32). Like the waxwings, the common species enters first and the less common species follows. Like the flickers, consecutive entries from the same lineage reveal different acoustic strategies from the same genetic foundation.

The Spinus Trilogy — First Genus With Three Members

Pine Siskin (degree 12, S. pinus), American Goldfinch (degree 43, S. tristis), and Lesser Goldfinch (degree 44, S. psaltria). No other genus has three entries in the engine. Three species, three acoustic strategies: noise (electronic zhreee), melody (canary warble + undulation), mosaic (mimicry collage of 39+ species). The genus spans E=1 to E=4 and the full range from original composition to borrowed anthology.

Range Expansion — A Bird Arriving in Real Time

The Lesser Goldfinch is actively expanding northward into the PNW. First Washington breeding record: 1975. Washington population increase: 110.5% between 2012 and 2022. Drivers: warming temperatures, river proximity, urban development, increased precipitation. Vagrant records increasing in the Puget Sound region. Once colonized, areas remain occupied. This is the engine’s first species defined primarily by ongoing range expansion.

The Lute Player of the Thorn Bush

Spinus psaltria — “Female lute player of the thorn bush.” Spinus = Latin for “thorn bush” (the weedy plants where goldfinches feed). Psaltria = Ancient Greek for “a female harpist” or “a female lute player,” from psallo, “to pluck a stringed instrument.” Named by Thomas Say in 1822 for the plucked-string quality of the bird’s descending call note. The only species in the engine whose scientific name explicitly references a musical instrument and a human performer.

The Sound: Mimicry Mosaic + Lute String Call

Contact call: A high, thin, whistled tsee-ee or tee-yee, 1–2 descending notes at approximately 5–7 kHz. More whistled and cleaner than the Pine Siskin’s buzzy tee-u. The plucked-string call that earned the name psaltria.

Song: An extended medley of twittering, buzzing, and whistled notes, spanning 1.6–6.7 kHz, lasting up to 10–16+ seconds. Approximately 50% original phrases (bright Spinus twittering) and 50% mimicry (recognizable fragments of other species’ calls and songs). Individual repertoires of 70–105 unique phrase types. The song sounds like a rapid channel-surf through the local bird community.

Mimicry documented: 39 avian species at one Arizona study site. Copied species vary geographically: Bewick’s Wren and Black Phoebe in the Southwest, House Finch and Song Sparrow in California, Eastern Phoebe in Texas. As the species expands into the PNW, its mimicry palette would incorporate local engine species.

Fringillidae Rises to Five — Tied for Second

Fringillidae now has five members (degrees 12, 33, 34, 43, 44), tying with Hirundinidae and Turdidae as the engine’s second-most represented family behind Parulidae (10). Spinus alone has 3 entries — more than entire families like Trochilidae (4), Bombycillidae (2), or Paridae (2). The true finches have become a structural pillar of the engine.

E=4 Continues — 21st Degree, Seven Consecutive

E=4 now at 21 degrees (46.7%). E=3 absent for 24 consecutive degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 31 degrees. E=1 absent for 32 degrees. Seven consecutive E=4 across two families (38–44). Dynamic range compression at its most severe.

The Smallest North American Spinus

At 9–12 cm and 8–11.5 grams (average ~9.7 g), the Lesser Goldfinch is the smallest of the three PNW Spinus and possibly one of the smallest true finches in the world. “Lesser” refers purely to body size — it says nothing about the bird’s vocal complexity, which exceeds its larger congener’s. The smallest bird in the family has the most borrowed voices.

Plumage: Green-Backed Western Form

The PNW subspecies S. p. hesperophilus (“western-loving”) is the green-backed goldfinch: males have olive-green to yellowish-green backs, black caps, bright yellow underparts, and dark wings with bold white patches. Females are olive-green above, dull yellow below. Unlike the American Goldfinch, the Lesser Goldfinch does not molt twice yearly — its plumage is relatively stable across seasons. Where the American Goldfinch transforms, the Lesser Goldfinch holds steady.

Theory Nodes

M

The Mosaic foundational

The 45th acoustic element. A song assembled from mimicry of 39+ species woven with original twittering. 70–105 phrase types per individual. ~50% borrowed, ~50% original. The first element defined by interspecific vocal appropriation — the bird sings the neighborhood.

G

Consecutive Goldfinch Pair foundational

American Goldfinch (43) + Lesser Goldfinch (44). Third consecutive same-genus pair in the engine (after flickers 21–22, waxwings 31–32). Common species first, less common second. Same genus, different acoustic strategies: undulation vs. mosaic.

S

Spinus Trilogy foundational

First genus with three members: Pine Siskin (12), American Goldfinch (43), Lesser Goldfinch (44). Three strategies: noise, melody, mosaic. Energy span E=1 to E=4. The genus that contains everything.

R

Range Expansion Species foundational

First species defined by active northward expansion into the PNW. Washington population +110.5% (2012–2022). Climate-driven, urban-facilitated. A soundscape in motion — the leading edge arrives.

5

Fringillidae at Five intermediate

Fifth Fringillidae. Family rises to tied second (with Hirundinidae and Turdidae) behind Parulidae. Consecutive pair at 43–44. Three Spinus alone exceed most families’ total count.

4

E=4 — 24 Degrees Without E≤3 intermediate

21st degree at E=4 (46.7%). Seven consecutive E=4 across two families (38–44). E=3 absent 24 degrees. Dynamic range compression at its most severe. The Spinus energy bracket holds: E=1 at 12, E=4 at 43 and 44.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Lesser Goldfinch documented as range expander in PNW. First Washington breeding 1975. Increasing in Willamette Valley, southern Washington, and vagrant in Puget Sound.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Weedy shrub ecology. Climate-driven range expansion. Composite-plant seed communities. Riparian corridors as expansion pathways. Urban facilitation of range expansion.
