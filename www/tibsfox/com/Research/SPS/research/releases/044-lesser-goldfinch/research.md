## Taxonomy: The Lute Player

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Fringillidae (True Finches)
- **Genus:** *Spinus* (Koch, 1816)
- **Species:** *Spinus psaltria* (Say, 1822)
- **Common Name:** Lesser Goldfinch; also "Dark-backed Goldfinch," "Arkansas Goldfinch" (historical), "Green-backed Goldfinch" (western subspecies)
- **Former classification:** *Carduelis psaltria*, *Astragalinus psaltria* (both superseded by molecular phylogenetics placing New World goldfinches in *Spinus*)

### Etymology

*Spinus* -- from Latin *spinus*, meaning "thorn bush" or "blackthorn" [1, 2, 5]. The genus name shared with Pine Siskin (*S. pinus*, degree 12) and American Goldfinch (*S. tristis*, degree 43). Three *Spinus* now in the engine, all named for thorny, seedy plants. The genus is a botanical label applied to birds: these are the thorn-bush finches.

*Psaltria* -- from Ancient Greek *psaltria*, meaning "a female lute player" or "a female harpist" [1, 2, 5, 16]. The word derives from *psallo*, meaning "to pluck a stringed instrument," "to play the lyre," or "to sing." Thomas Say, who formally described the species in 1822, chose this name for the bird's descending, plucked-string quality in its call notes -- the high, thin *tsee-ee* that drops in pitch like a finger drawn across a lute string [1, 5, 16]. The name is feminine, unusual for a bird epithet, and specifically musical.

Full translation: **"Female lute player of the thorn bush."** A bird named for an instrument and a habitat. Compare with the engine's full *Spinus* trilogy:

| Degree | Species | Binomial | Translation | Naming Philosophy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | Pine Siskin | *Spinus pinus* | "Thorn-spine of the pine" | Ecological (plant + plant) |
| 43 | American Goldfinch | *Spinus tristis* | "Sad thorn-bird" | Ecological + emotional |
| **44** | **Lesser Goldfinch** | ***Spinus psaltria*** | **"Female lute player of the thorn bush"** | **Ecological + musical** |

Three *Spinus*, three naming philosophies: ecological-ecological, ecological-emotional, ecological-musical. The genus stays botanical. The species epithets diversify: *pinus* names a plant, *tristis* names a mood, *psaltria* names a musician. The Lesser Goldfinch is the only species in the engine whose scientific name explicitly references a musical instrument and a human performer. The lute player in the thorn bush. The name that Linnaeus gave to the American Goldfinch heard sadness. The name that Thomas Say gave to the Lesser Goldfinch heard music.

### The Name "Lesser"

"Lesser" refers simply to **body size** -- the Lesser Goldfinch is smaller than the American Goldfinch [1, 2, 5, 6]. At 9-12 cm in length and 8-11.5 grams (average ~9.7 g), it is the **smallest North American *Spinus*** and possibly one of the smallest true finches (*Fringillidae*) in the world [1, 5, 6, 8]. The American Goldfinch averages 11-14 cm and 11-20 grams. The Pine Siskin averages 11-14 cm and 12-18 grams. The Lesser Goldfinch is measurably smaller than both of its engine congeners.

The name is purely comparative -- it says nothing about the bird's quality, only its dimensions. In the field, the Lesser Goldfinch is darker-backed, shorter-tailed, and more compact than the American Goldfinch. "Lesser" in body, not in voice: its song is arguably the most complex of the three *Spinus* in the engine, incorporating mimicry of dozens of other species into a single extended performance [1, 3, 4, 8, 9, 10].

## The Sound: The Lute Player's Mosaic

### The Contact Call: High, Thin, Descending

The Lesser Goldfinch's most commonly heard vocalization is its **contact call** -- a high, thin, whistled *tsee-ee* or *tee-yee*, typically one or two notes, descending slightly in pitch [1, 2, 6, 8, 9, 11]. This call is given frequently in flight and while perched, particularly during flock movements. It is more whistled and cleaner than the throaty *tee-u* of the Pine Siskin, and lacks the strongly descending character of the American Goldfinch's *dear-me* call [1, 8, 11]. The call is diagnostic in the field -- a thin, bright, slightly plaintive note that sits higher and thinner than either of its *Spinus* congeners.

The call has the plucked-string quality that Thomas Say heard when he named the bird *psaltria* -- a single clean note that descends like a finger drawn across a taut string, brief and musical, the smallest lute in the orchestra [1, 5, 16].

### The Song: A Mosaic of Borrowed Voices

The Lesser Goldfinch's **song** is where the species achieves its acoustic distinction -- and where it separates dramatically from both of its *Spinus* congeners in the engine.

The male Lesser Goldfinch sings a **prolonged, complex song** -- a rapid medley of twittering, buzzing, and whistled notes that can last 10 seconds or more [1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 10, 11]. The song includes bright original phrases interspersed with clear fragments of other species' vocalizations. This is not background noise that happens to resemble other birds. It is **deliberate vocal mimicry** -- the incorporation of recognizable phrases from the songs and calls of dozens of other species into a single composite performance [1, 3, 4, 8, 9, 10].

The extent of this mimicry is extraordinary. In a landmark study by Coutlee (1971) in southeastern Arizona, a single breeding season yielded documented mimicry of **39 avian species plus one mammal** (rock squirrel) [3, 4, 9, 10]. Individual males maintained repertoires of **70 to 105 unique phrase types**, with approximately half of all phrases being copies of other species [3, 4, 9, 10]. The most frequently mimicked species in Arizona included Bewick's Wren, Verdin, House Finch, Pine Siskin, Canyon Towhee, Black Phoebe, and American Robin [3, 4, 10]. In California, the mimicry palette shifts to include House Sparrow, House Finch, Song Sparrow, California Quail, and American Kestrel [3, 4, 9]. In Texas, Eastern Phoebe replaces Black Phoebe. In Oregon and Washington, the copied species shift again to match the local soundscape [3, 4].

This geographic variation in mimicry content is itself significant. The Lesser Goldfinch copies what it hears in its neighborhood. A bird in Arizona incorporates desert species. A bird in Oregon incorporates PNW species. A bird expanding into the Puget Sound lowlands would incorporate the very species that already populate this engine -- House Finches, Song Sparrows, Pine Siskins, and whatever else shares the weedy shrub habitat of its new range. The mimicry is a **local acoustic census** -- a bird that sings a catalog of its neighbors.

### Vocal Learning: Lifelong and Geographic

The mimicry in Lesser Goldfinch song is almost certainly learned, probably during an extended sensitive period in the first year of life, though evidence suggests that vocal learning may continue throughout adulthood [1, 3, 4, 9, 10]. Goldfinches are learning and adding to their repertoires for at least several months during their winter roaming period, remembering calls learned from both conspecifics and heterospecifics [3, 4, 9]. The song is therefore both autobiographical (recording the acoustic environments the bird has experienced) and geographic (reflecting the species composition of its territory).

This contrasts sharply with the American Goldfinch (degree 43), whose song is an **original canary-like warble** -- complex and beautiful but composed of the bird's own phrase types, not copies of other species [1, 8]. The Pine Siskin (degree 12) shows minimal mimicry. The Cassin's Finch (degree 33) was noted as a mimic in its research, but the Lesser Goldfinch's mimicry operates at a fundamentally different scale -- not occasional borrowed phrases but a systematic, lifelong accumulation of dozens of species' vocalizations assembled into every performance.

### The 45th Acoustic Element: The Mosaic

The Lesser Goldfinch introduces the engine's 45th acoustic element (species side): **the mosaic**.

The mosaic is a song assembled from fragments of many sources -- borrowed phrases from dozens of species woven together with original material into a single continuous performance. The bird's song is not a unified composition in the manner of the Hermit Thrush's harmonic spiral (degree 7) or the American Goldfinch's canary warble (degree 43). It is a **collage** -- a patchwork of recognizable pieces from other species' vocalizations, stitched together with the bird's own twittering phrases, creating something that is simultaneously derivative and original. Each fragment is a copy. The arrangement is unique.

The mosaic is distinct from all previous acoustic elements:

| Element | Source | Character |
|---|---|---|
| The Cadenza (33) | Original virtuosic performance | Solo improvisation |
| The Dialect (34) | Population-specific learned patterns | Tribal identity |
| The Undulation (43) | Locomotion-coupled original call | Physics as rhythm |
| **The Mosaic (44)** | **Multi-species mimicry collage** | **Borrowed fragments, original arrangement** |

The mosaic opens a new acoustic category: **composite mimicry** -- a song whose raw material is drawn from the broader soundscape rather than from the singer's own species-typical repertoire. Previous elements were either original compositions (most) or species-typical learned patterns (dialect). The mosaic is neither: it is a **curated collection of other birds' voices**, assembled by a singer who has spent its life listening to its neighborhood and remembering what it heard. The bird is not composing. It is curating.

Named "the mosaic" for four resonances:
1. **The song structure** -- fragments of 39+ species arranged into a continuous performance, like tiles in a mosaic: each piece recognizable, the whole greater than the parts
2. **The geographic variation** -- each bird's mosaic reflects its local soundscape, so the song is a map of the acoustic environment where the bird learned
3. **The range expansion** -- as the Lesser Goldfinch pushes north, it incorporates new species into its mosaic, adding PNW voices to a song that originated in the Southwest
4. **The *Spinus* trilogy completion** -- noise (Pine Siskin), melody (American Goldfinch), mosaic (Lesser Goldfinch). Three strategies in one genus. The mosaic is the synthesis that contains pieces of everything

The mosaic is the engine's first element defined by **interspecific vocal appropriation** -- a song that exists because the singer listened to other species and remembered what they said.

### Vocalization Details

- **Contact call (*tsee-ee* / *tee-yee*):** 1-2 high, thin, descending whistled notes, approximately 5-7 kHz. More whistled than Pine Siskin's buzzy *tee-u*. Given in flight and from perch. Year-round. The "lute string" call that earned the name *psaltria* [1, 2, 8, 11].
- **Song:** Extended medley of twittering, buzzing, and whistled notes, 1.6-6.7 kHz frequency range, duration up to 10-16+ seconds. Approximately half original phrases, half mimicry of other species. Repertoire of 70-105 unique phrase types per individual. Delivered from exposed perch, often at the top of a shrub or small tree. Breeding season [1, 3, 4, 8, 9, 10, 11].
- **Courtship call (*tee-yer*):** Males give a descending call to females during courtship displays [1, 8].
- **Chatter call:** Rapid twittering given during flock interactions and feeding, a social contact vocalization [1, 8].
- **Frequency range:** Approximately 1.6-7 kHz across all vocalizations, with contact call energy peaking in the 5-7 kHz range and song spanning the full 1.6-6.7 kHz range [8, 11].
- **Volume:** Moderate. The contact call is thin but carries across open habitat. The song is delivered at moderate volume from exposed perches in weedy shrub habitat.

## Biology: The Smallest Lute Player

### Body Size and the Meaning of "Lesser"

The Lesser Goldfinch is the smallest of the three PNW *Spinus* species and the smallest North American *Spinus* [1, 2, 5, 6, 8]:

| Species | Length | Weight | Wingspan |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pine Siskin | 11-14 cm | 12-18 g | 18-22 cm |
| American Goldfinch | 11-14 cm | 11-20 g | 19-22 cm |
| **Lesser Goldfinch** | **9-12 cm** | **8-11.5 g (avg ~9.7 g)** | **15-20 cm** |

At approximately 10 grams, the Lesser Goldfinch weighs less than two nickels. It is one of the smallest true finches in the world -- comparable in mass to the Calliope Hummingbird (degree 37, ~3 g) as a study in miniaturization, though the hummingbird is still substantially lighter. The "lesser" in the name is strictly a size comparison with the American Goldfinch; it says nothing about the bird's vocal complexity, which exceeds its larger congener's.

### Plumage: Green-Backed and Black-Backed

The Lesser Goldfinch shows notable **geographic plumage variation** across its range, particularly in males [1, 2, 5, 6, 7]:

**Western birds (*S. p. hesperophilus*):** Males have **green backs** (olive-green to yellowish-green), bright yellow underparts, a black cap, and dark wings with white wing patches. This is the subspecies that occurs in the PNW -- the "green-backed goldfinch." Females are olive-green above, dull yellow below, with dark wings and faint wing bars [1, 2, 5, 6].

**Eastern birds (*S. p. psaltria*):** Males have **entirely black backs**, heads, and napes, with bright yellow underparts and white wing patches. This is the "black-backed goldfinch" of Texas and the interior Southwest [1, 2, 5, 6].

The variation between subspecies appears to be **clinal** -- a gradual change in allele frequency across geography rather than a sharp boundary between discrete populations [5, 7]. In the overlap zone (Arizona, New Mexico), intermediate birds occur. Some taxonomists have questioned whether the subspecies warrant formal recognition, suggesting they may be better treated as color morphs or geographic forms [5, 7].

Critically, the Lesser Goldfinch does **not** molt twice yearly like the American Goldfinch. It undergoes a single annual molt. Its plumage is relatively stable year-round -- the male's green or black back and yellow underparts persist across seasons, dimming slightly in winter but not undergoing the dramatic olive-to-gold transformation that defines the American Goldfinch's annual cycle [1, 2, 5, 6]. Where the American Goldfinch undulates between plumage states, the Lesser Goldfinch holds steady. The visual strategy mirrors the vocal strategy: the American Goldfinch transforms; the Lesser Goldfinch accumulates.

### Breeding Ecology

The Lesser Goldfinch nests in **loose, colonial groups of monogamous pairs** [1, 2, 6, 8]. The breeding season is long in warmer parts of the range (early spring to mid-autumn in the Southwest), but in the PNW, breeding is concentrated in summer months [1, 2, 6, 7].

**Nest:** Compact open cup woven of grass, plant fibers, and bark strips, lined with plant down. Built mostly or entirely by the female. Placed in a vertical fork of twigs in a shrub or tree, typically 5-30 feet above ground, sometimes very low in dense bushes or weeds [1, 2, 6, 8].

**Eggs:** 4-5 (sometimes 3-6), pale blue to pale blue-green, usually unmarked [1, 2, 6].

**Incubation:** 12-13 days, by the female. Male feeds the incubating female on the nest [1, 2, 6].

**Nestling period:** 12-14 days. Both parents feed the young [1, 2, 6].

**Courtship:** The male feeds the female and performs a display flight with wings and tail spread widely, fluttering rapidly while singing his full mimicry-laden song [1, 2, 6, 8]. The display flight is thus a showcase of the mosaic -- the male performs his accumulated vocal repertoire in flight, advertising both his physical fitness and his acoustic range.

Unlike the American Goldfinch, the Lesser Goldfinch does not have such extreme dependence on late-season thistle seeds for nest material, though it does feed heavily on composite seeds and is associated with the same weedy, seed-rich habitats [1, 2, 6]. Its nesting is earlier in the season than the American Goldfinch's famously late schedule.

## Range Expansion: The Northward Advance

### Historical and Current Range

The Lesser Goldfinch's historical core range extends from the southwestern United States through Mexico and Central America to northern South America [1, 2, 5, 7]. It has been a common breeding bird in California, the desert Southwest, and Texas for as long as ornithological records exist. Oregon's Rogue Valley and southern Willamette Valley have hosted breeding populations for decades [2, 7, 12, 15].

But the story at the northern edge of the range is one of **active, ongoing expansion** [14, 15].

### Washington: From Absent to Arriving

The Lesser Goldfinch's Washington history is a story told in decades [2, 7, 14, 15]:

- **Pre-1975:** Essentially absent from Washington as a breeding species.
- **1975:** First confirmed breeding record in Washington [1, 2, 7].
- **1975-2010:** Rare and local, restricted largely to Klickitat County in the Columbia River Gorge area -- the warmest, most arid part of Washington, ecologically more similar to Oregon's interior than to the Puget Sound lowlands [2, 7].
- **2012-2022:** Population increase of **110.5%** in Washington according to eBird and Project FeederWatch data analyzed by researchers at Washington State University and the Cornell Lab of Ornithology [14, 15].
- **Present:** Vagrant records increasingly common in the Puget Sound region. Not yet established as a regular breeding species in the Seattle area, but the trajectory suggests it may become one within the coming decades [14, 15].

### Oregon: Established and Expanding

In Oregon, the Lesser Goldfinch is more established [2, 7, 12, 15]:

- **Rogue Valley (southern Oregon):** Common breeder, greatest abundance in the state.
- **Umpqua Valley:** Fairly common.
- **Southern Willamette Valley:** Fairly common breeder along the western fringe.
- **North Willamette Valley / Portland area:** Scattered and local but increasing.
- **Population trend (2012-2022):** 16.9% increase statewide [14, 15].

### Drivers of Expansion

A 2025 study published in *Ornithology* (formerly *The Auk*) identified four factors strongly associated with the Lesser Goldfinch's northward expansion [14, 15]:

1. **Warmer temperatures** -- climate warming opens previously unsuitable northern habitat.
2. **River proximity** -- riparian corridors serve as expansion pathways, providing seed-rich weedy habitat in otherwise forested landscapes.
3. **Urban development** -- suburban landscapes with gardens, feeders, and weedy edges provide stable food access.
4. **Increased precipitation** -- wetter conditions in previously arid areas increase seed plant productivity.

Once colonized, areas tend to remain occupied, suggesting the expansion is likely permanent [14, 15]. The Lesser Goldfinch is not a vagrant that appears and disappears. It is a **colonizer** -- it arrives, establishes, and stays.

### Significance for the Engine

The Lesser Goldfinch is the engine's first species defined primarily by **active range expansion into the PNW**. Other species in the engine are long-established PNW residents or predictable seasonal visitors. The Lesser Goldfinch is arriving. Its presence in the engine at degree 44 reflects not historical abundance but a trajectory -- a species that was absent from Washington 50 years ago, rare 20 years ago, and increasingly common today. The Sound of Puget Sound captures a soundscape in motion. The Lesser Goldfinch is the leading edge of that motion.

## PNW Habitat: Weedy Shrub

### Habitat Description

The Lesser Goldfinch's primary habitat is **weedy shrub** -- open, semi-arid to semi-disturbed areas with a mix of shrubs, low trees, and seed-producing forbs [1, 2, 6, 7, 12]. This is similar to the American Goldfinch's weedy field (degree 43) but with a stronger **shrub component** -- the Lesser Goldfinch uses shrubs and low trees more extensively for nesting and singing perches than the open-field American Goldfinch.

Habitat types in the PNW include:
- Riparian scrub along rivers and streams
- Oak woodland edges with understory shrubs
- Suburban gardens with seed-bearing plants and shrubby borders
- Vineyard and orchard margins with weedy understory
- Roadside brush with composite plants
- Post-disturbance shrub communities

### Vertical Position

The Lesser Goldfinch feeds and nests at **0-10 m** above ground, slightly higher on average than the American Goldfinch's 0-5 m range, because it uses shrubs and small trees more consistently [1, 2, 6, 7]. Singing perches are typically at the top of a tall shrub or small tree -- the highest available point in the weedy shrub landscape.

## The Mimicry: A Living Acoustic Census

### Scale of Mimicry

The Lesser Goldfinch's mimicry behavior warrants extended discussion because it is the **most extensive vocal mimicry documented in any *Spinus* species** and one of the most remarkable among all North American passerines [3, 4, 9, 10].

Key findings from Coutlee (1971) and subsequent research [3, 4, 9, 10]:

- **39 avian species** mimicked in a single breeding season at one study site in southeastern Arizona
- **One mammal** (rock squirrel) also mimicked
- **70 to 105 unique phrase types** per individual male (minimum estimates)
- **Approximately 50%** of all song phrases are mimicry of other species
- **Geographic variation** in copied species tracks local bird community composition
- **Lifelong learning** -- birds may continue adding to their repertoire throughout adulthood

### What Is Copied vs. What Is Original

The Lesser Goldfinch's song has two components [3, 4, 8, 9, 10]:

1. **Original phrases** (~50% of song): Bright twittering, buzzy notes, and whistles characteristic of *Spinus* -- the bird's own voice, recognizable as a goldfinch. These provide the structural framework of the song.

2. **Mimicry phrases** (~50% of song): Recognizable fragments of other species' songs and calls, woven into the goldfinch's own material. Individual fragments are typically 0.3-1.0 seconds long -- enough to be identifiable but not complete reproductions of full songs.

The result is a continuous medley that sounds like a rapid channel-surf through the local bird community -- a half-second of House Finch, a phrase of Bewick's Wren, a twitter of goldfinch, a snatch of Black Phoebe, more goldfinch twittering, a California Quail call. The bird assembles these fragments in real time, creating a performance that is different every time but consistently includes the same repertoire of borrowed and original phrases [3, 4, 9, 10].

### Mimicry as Acoustic Census

Because the Lesser Goldfinch copies what it hears locally, its song is effectively a **record of the bird community** in its neighborhood [3, 4, 9, 10]. A Lesser Goldfinch in the Rogue Valley of southern Oregon incorporates different species than one in Tucson. As the species expands northward into the Puget Sound lowlands, its mimicry palette would shift to include species like Pine Siskin (already in the engine at degree 12), Song Sparrow, American Robin (degree 25), House Finch, and other common PNW birds.

This means the Lesser Goldfinch's song in the PNW would literally contain fragments of other engine species' vocalizations. The mosaic would incorporate the sawtooth sweep (Pine Siskin), the testimony (American Robin), and the calls of whatever PNW birds share the weedy shrub habitat. The lute player does not just sing in the thorn bush. It sings *the thorn bush* -- every voice it has heard there, reassembled into one small bird's performance.

### Mimicry Across the Fringillidae

| Degree | Species | Mimicry Level | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | Pine Siskin | Minimal | Some vocal copying documented (Sibley 2009), not a primary song feature |
| 33 | Cassin's Finch | Moderate | Mimic, incorporates some other species' phrases into cadenza |
| 34 | Red Crossbill | None significant | Dialect variation is population-specific, not mimicry |
| 43 | American Goldfinch | Minimal | Original canary warble, pair-specific call convergence |
| **44** | **Lesser Goldfinch** | **Extensive** | **39+ species copied, 50% of song is mimicry, lifelong learning** |

The mimicry gradient within Fringillidae: none (Crossbill) -> minimal (Siskin, American Goldfinch) -> moderate (Cassin's Finch) -> extensive (Lesser Goldfinch). The smallest finch in the family has the most borrowed voices.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 5, 12]
- **Global population:** Approximately 15 million (Partners in Flight estimates) [1, 2, 12, 13]
- **Continental Concern Score:** 7/20 (low concern) [12, 13]
- **BBS trend:** Populations appeared stable between 1966 and 2019 according to North American Breeding Bird Survey data [2, 12]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service [1, 2]
- **Range trend:** Actively expanding northward -- populations increasing in Washington (110.5%), Idaho (66.3%), and Oregon (16.9%) between 2012 and 2022 [14, 15]
- **Threats:** Habitat loss in core range, pesticide impacts on seed plants, window strikes, cat predation. None at population-threatening levels [1, 2, 5]

The Lesser Goldfinch's population of ~15 million is smaller than the American Goldfinch's ~44 million but not alarmingly so. The species is secure and expanding. It is one of few North American passerines whose range is growing rather than contracting -- a beneficiary of climate change, urbanization, and the spread of weedy seed plants.

## Energy Level: E=4 Continues

E=4 at degree 44. The 21st degree at E=4 (46.7% of all entries). E=3 absent for 24 consecutive degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 31 degrees (since degree 13). E=1 absent for 32 degrees (since degree 12 -- the Pine Siskin, same genus).

**Seven consecutive E=4** across two families (38-44): five Parulidae (38-42) followed by two Fringillidae (43-44). The dynamic range compression deepens to its most severe point in the engine's history. The goldfinch pair maintains the same energy level as the warbler quintet that preceded it. Different families, different genera, different acoustic strategies -- same dynamic level.

The *Spinus* energy bracket remains the widest within any genus: E=1 (Pine Siskin, degree 12) to E=4 (American Goldfinch at 43, Lesser Goldfinch at 44). The genus spans the full range from minimum to moderate-high energy within the engine.

## Subspecies in the PNW: *S. p. hesperophilus*

The Lesser Goldfinch that reaches the PNW is **subspecies *hesperophilus*** -- the "green-backed goldfinch" [1, 2, 5, 6, 7]. The subspecies name means "western-loving" (from Greek *hesperos* = "evening/western" + *philos* = "loving"), an apt label for a bird whose PNW range lies at the western edge of the continent [5].

Key features of the PNW form:
- **Male:** Green (olive-green to yellowish-green) back, black cap, bright yellow underparts, dark wings with bold white patches
- **Female:** Olive-green above, dull yellowish below, dark wings with faint wing bars
- **Plumage stability:** No dramatic seasonal transformation (unlike the American Goldfinch's twice-yearly molt)
- **Size:** At the smaller end of the species' range, consistent with the general pattern of smaller body size in warmer-climate populations

The green-backed western form is the one that birders in the Willamette Valley, the Columbia River Gorge, and (increasingly) the southern Puget Sound lowlands encounter. It looks distinctly different from the American Goldfinch -- darker-backed, lacking the dramatic black forehead cap on a yellow crown, more compact, and without the undulating flight call that is so diagnostic of its larger congener.
