## Purpose

This is the forty-fifth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-44), the fifth Fringillidae, the third *Spinus*, the second consecutive goldfinch, and the engine's first range expansion species. *Spinus psaltria* -- the "female lute player of the thorn bush" -- enters the engine as the smallest North American *Spinus*, carrying four structural events: the *Spinus* trilogy is completed (first genus with three members), the consecutive goldfinch pair echoes the waxwing pair, Fringillidae rises to five members (tied for second-most represented family), and the engine records its first species defined by active, ongoing northward range expansion into the PNW.

Six structural contributions:

1. **The mosaic -- the 45th acoustic element (species side).** A song assembled from fragments of dozens of other species' vocalizations woven with the bird's own bright twittering phrases. Individual males maintain repertoires of 70-105 unique phrase types, approximately 50% original and 50% mimicry of other species. 39 avian species plus one mammal documented as mimicry sources at a single study site. Named "the mosaic" for four resonances: song structure (fragment-tile assembly), geographic variation (local soundscape reflected), range expansion (new PNW species incorporated), and *Spinus* trilogy completion (noise, melody, mosaic). The first acoustic element defined by **interspecific vocal appropriation**.

2. **Third *Spinus* -- first genus with three members.** No other genus has three entries in the engine. Pine Siskin (degree 12, E=1, electronic zhreee), American Goldfinch (degree 43, E=4, canary warble + undulation), Lesser Goldfinch (degree 44, E=4, mimicry mosaic). Three species, three naming philosophies (plant, mood, musician), three acoustic strategies (noise, melody, mosaic), three range patterns (irruptive nomad, established resident, active expander). The genus spans E=1 to E=4 and the full spectrum from original composition to borrowed anthology.

3. **Consecutive goldfinch pair (degrees 43-44).** The engine's third consecutive same-genus pair after the Northern Flicker (21-22) and the Cedar/Bohemian Waxwing (31-32). Common species first (American Goldfinch), less common second (Lesser Goldfinch). Larger first, smaller second. Original vocalist first, mimic second. Established resident first, range expander second. The pattern is confirmed.

4. **Fifth Fringillidae -- family rises to second (tied).** Fringillidae now has five members (degrees 12, 33, 34, 43, 44), tying with Hirundinidae and Turdidae as the engine's second-most represented family behind Parulidae (10). *Spinus* alone has 3 entries -- more than most entire families. The true finches are a structural pillar.

5. **Range expansion species -- a bird arriving in real time.** First Washington breeding record 1975. Population increase of 110.5% in Washington between 2012 and 2022. Driven by warming temperatures, river proximity, urban development, and increased precipitation. The engine's first species defined by active, ongoing range expansion into the PNW. The soundscape is changing. The Lesser Goldfinch is the leading edge.

6. **The lute player etymology.** *Psaltria* = "female lute player" (Ancient Greek). The only species in the engine whose scientific name explicitly references a musical instrument and a human performer. Named for the plucked-string quality of the descending contact call. The genus names the habitat (thorn bush). The species names the sound as music (a stringed instrument).

## What Worked

### 1. The mosaic captures a genuinely new acoustic phenomenon
No previous element described interspecific vocal mimicry at this scale. The mosaic is distinct from the cadenza (original virtuosic performance), the dialect (population-specific learned patterns), and the undulation (locomotion-coupled original call). A song that is half autobiography and half anthology -- composed by curating the neighborhood's voices -- is a fundamentally different acoustic strategy than anything the engine has documented.

### 2. The *Spinus* trilogy provides genuine structural depth
Three members of one genus, each with a radically different approach to sound, ecology, and life history. The noise-melody-mosaic arc within a single genus is a real biological pattern -- the *Spinus* radiation produced electronic noise generators (siskins), original melodists (goldfinches), and mimicry specialists (lesser goldfinches). The trilogy works because it maps real biology, not imposed narrative.

### 3. The consecutive goldfinch pair confirms the engine's pairing pattern
Three consecutive same-genus pairs (21-22, 31-32, 43-44) is enough to identify a structural pattern. Common first, less common second. The pattern is now documented and can be observed in future entries.

### 4. The range expansion adds a temporal dimension
The engine's first species defined by active arrival. Previous species were described as established residents, seasonal visitors, or irruptive nomads. The Lesser Goldfinch adds a new category: the colonizer, the species whose presence in the PNW is a function of recent climate change and landscape modification. The engine now captures a soundscape in motion.

### 5. The etymology enriches the *Spinus* naming trilogy
*Pinus* (plant), *tristis* (mood), *psaltria* (musician) -- three naming philosophies in one genus. The lute player epithet is the only one in the engine that references a musical instrument and a human performer. The connection between the bird's descending call note and a plucked lute string is both auditorily accurate and poetically rich.

### 6. The mimicry research is well-sourced and quantified
39+ species, 70-105 phrase types, ~50% mimicry -- these are documented numbers from published research (Coutlee 1971, Mundinger 1982). The geographic variation in mimicry content (different species copied in different regions) is both scientifically documented and structurally significant for a range expansion species.
