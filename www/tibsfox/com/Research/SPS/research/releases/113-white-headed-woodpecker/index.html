sharp chick-ick — the only North American woodpecker with a white head on a black body. Ponderosa pine specialist. Bark flaker, not driller. The Inverse Woodpecker — White Head, Black Body

The White-headed Woodpecker is a negative image of a typical woodpecker. Where most Picidae have black heads with white or colored markings, this species has an entirely white head on an entirely black body. No barring, no stripes — just solid black with a solid white head. The simplicity of the pattern is startling. Unmistakable in the field if seen — though its quiet, unobtrusive behavior means it often is not.

Ponderosa Pine Specialist — Bark Flaker, Not Driller

The White-headed Woodpecker pries bark scales rather than drilling into solid wood. It moves methodically along trunks, using its bill to lever off loose bark and expose insects beneath. In winter, it extracts ponderosa pine seeds directly from cones — prying open cone scales to access the seeds inside. No ponderosa pine, no winter food source. The bill is a lever, not a chisel. The foraging is nearly silent.

TIES the Corvidae Septet Record — Seventh Consecutive Picidae

Seven consecutive Picidae: Acorn (107), Pileated laughing (108), Pileated drumming (109), Hairy (110), Downy (111), Lewis’s (112), White-headed (113). The Picidae streak now TIES the Corvidae septet record (100–106). The two longest family streaks in the engine: both seven degrees, both in the second half of the rotation. Does degree 114 break the record?

First Dry-Side Cascades Specialist

The first species strongly associated with east-side Cascades ecology. Found in the ponderosa pine forests of the eastern slopes — where the maritime climate gives way to continental, where dense wet forest becomes open and dry. Two consecutive ponderosa pine specialists (Lewis’s 112, White-headed 113). The engine documents the eastern flank of the PNW after 112 degrees focused primarily on the western side.

Acoustic Profile: Sharp Chick-ick

A sharp chick-ick or pik-ik — two notes in quick succession, the second slightly lower. Higher-pitched (3–7 kHz) and thinner than the Hairy’s peek! Approximately 0.2–0.4 seconds per call. Drums softly and infrequently. Bark-flaking foraging is nearly silent. One of the least acoustically conspicuous North American woodpeckers — you see the white head before you hear the call. E=4 — moderate, quiet, methodical.

Three Dryobates in Four Degrees

Hairy Woodpecker (110), Downy Woodpecker (111), White-headed Woodpecker (113) — three species of genus Dryobates in four degrees. The genus spans the full range of ecological specialization: Hairy = continental generalist, Downy = suburban generalist, White-headed = ponderosa pine specialist. Same genus, radically different ecological breadths.

SPS-108 Pileated Woodpecker (foraging contrast)

Pileated: heavy excavation, deep drilling, massive cavities (E=6). White-headed: bark flaking, prying, minimal drilling (E=4). Power vs. precision -- opposite ends of the Picidae foraging spectrum.

SPS-107 Acorn Woodpecker (streak endpoints)

Acorn at streak start (107), White-headed ties the record (113). Colonial/granary/oak vs. solitary/bark-flaking/ponderosa. Both food specialists in completely different tree systems.
