## Structural Event 2: The Inverse Pattern -- Unique Plumage

- **Situation:** The White-headed Woodpecker is the only North American woodpecker with a white head on a black body. No barring, no stripes -- solid white above the neck, solid black below. A negative image of the typical woodpecker visual template.
- **Root Cause:** The inverse pattern may serve as an intraspecific signal in open, well-lit ponderosa pine habitat. Where dense forest makes bright signals less effective, open forest favors conspicuous plumage for mate attraction and territorial signaling.
- **Recommendation:** Document the inverse pattern as a major visual identity finding. Note the contrast with the typical Picidae plumage documented throughout the streak (bold black-and-white barring in Hairy, Downy; red caps; complex patterns).
- **Carry Item:** CONFIRMED -- Inverse pattern documented. Unique among NA Picidae.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **The inverse pattern is the correct visual center.** The white-head-on-black-body plumage is genuinely unique and is the bird's most remarkable characteristic. Verified.

2. **E=4 is defensible.** The sharp chick-ick is thin and quiet. Drumming is soft and infrequent. Bark flaking is nearly silent. The overall acoustic profile is quiet and methodical.

3. **Ponderosa pine dependency is well-documented.** The species' absolute dependence on ponderosa pine for foraging, winter food, and nesting is confirmed by multiple sources (Garrett et al. 1996, Dixon 1995, Cornell Lab).

4. **The dry-side Cascades expansion is ecologically significant.** The engine's first sustained engagement with east-side ecology at degrees 112-113 represents a genuine geographic and ecological expansion.

5. **Cross-references to Lewis's (112), Hairy (110), Downy (111), Pileated (108), and Acorn (107) are all defensible.**
