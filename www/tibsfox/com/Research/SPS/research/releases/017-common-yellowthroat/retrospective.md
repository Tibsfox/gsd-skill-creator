## Purpose

This is the eighteenth release of the PNW 360 Species engine and the seventh species in the **second decade** (degrees 11--20). The Common Yellowthroat introduces **three new structural dimensions** to the circle: (1) the hook -- the first species whose primary acoustic strategy is MEMORABILITY, the earworm, the catchy phrase that lodges in memory through rhythmic repetition; (2) marsh edge habitat -- the first wetland species, breaking the engine out of riparian (streamside) into marsh (basin), introducing emergent cattail/sedge ecology; (3) the intra-genus pair -- the first time two species from the same genus (*Geothlypis*) appear at different degrees, demonstrating that congeners can employ fundamentally different acoustic strategies (MacGillivray's narrative at degree 14 vs. yellowthroat hook at degree 17).

The Common Yellowthroat also extends the **repetition taxonomy** to three dimensions: ostinato (degree 13, persistence below attention threshold), narrative (degree 14, repetition building to resolution), and hook (degree 17, repetition forcing memorability). Three uses of the same mechanism -- exact repetition -- producing three different psychological effects: habituation, structural expectation, and earworm.

## Three Structural Advances

### 1. The Hook: Memorability as Primary Acoustic Strategy

The Common Yellowthroat is the first species in the engine whose primary acoustic strategy is **CATCHINESS** -- the hook, the earworm, the phrase that lodges in memory involuntarily. Previous species achieved identity through complexity (degree 1), meaning (degree 3), persistence (degree 13), narrative structure (degree 14), urgency (degree 15), or brightness (degree 16). The yellowthroat achieves identity through a fundamentally different mechanism: the song is designed (by evolution) to be **impossible to forget**.

The hook follows every rule of effective earworm design (Jakubowski et al. 2017): simple pitch contour, strong metric rhythm at walking tempo, exact repetition, and phonetic salience. The mnemonic "witchety" has zero semantic content -- it cannot be resolved by the brain's language processing, so it keeps cycling in the phonological loop of working memory. The motor engagement (march-like rhythm activating SMA and premotor cortex) ensures the pattern recruits the body, and motor memory is persistent.

The engine's acoustic strategy taxonomy now includes: silence, complexity, simplicity, meaning, rhythm, pulse, question, harmony, threshold, variation, swarm, trill, noise, persistence, narrative, urgency, brightness, and **catchiness**. Eighteen categories. Eighteen degrees.

### 2. Marsh Edge: The First Wetland Species

All previous water-associated species were either estuarine (degree 0, heron) or riparian (degrees 6, 14, 15, 16 -- streamside). The Common Yellowthroat is the first **marsh/wetland** species: cattails, sedges, wet meadows, emergent vegetation at the edges of ponds, lakes, and slow-moving waterways. This is a fundamentally different habitat:

- **Riparian** is LINEAR (follows streams). **Marsh** is AREAL (fills basins).
- **Riparian** vegetation is woody (willows, alders). **Marsh** vegetation is herbaceous (cattails, bulrushes, sedges).
- **Riparian** acoustics involve canopy filtering. **Marsh** acoustics are open -- less filtering, more wind, greater sound propagation distance.

The yellowthroat's loud, emphatic, rhythmically regular song is adapted to this open acoustic environment. The hook must carry across water and emergent vegetation without canopy amplification. The song's emphasis on rhythm and volume over tonal complexity matches the acoustic demands of the habitat.

### 3. Intra-Genus Pair: Geothlypis at Degrees 14 and 17

The Common Yellowthroat (*Geothlypis trichas*) and the MacGillivray's Warbler (*Geothlypis tolmiei*) are the engine's **first intra-genus pair** -- two species from the same genus appearing at different degrees. The contrast is instructive at every level:

- **Song strategy:** MacGillivray's uses AAB narrative (the storyteller). Yellowthroat uses the hook (the jingle writer).
- **Behavioral visibility:** MacGillivray's skulks (hidden, heard not seen). Yellowthroat is bold (exposed perches, black mask as visual signal).
- **Range:** MacGillivray's is western-only. Yellowthroat is continent-wide.
- **Habitat:** MacGillivray's is dense shrub. Yellowthroat is marsh edge.

The genus that was expanded by DNA evidence (when *Oporornis* was collapsed into *Geothlypis*) is now expanded by the engine's own logic. Same genus, opposite strategies. The engine demonstrates that a recent common ancestor is no guarantee of strategic similarity.

## The Reggie Watts Parallel: The Loop as Music

The Part A parallel at degree 17 maps to **Reggie Watts** from S36. The parallel operates on four axes:

1. **Both impossibly catchy.** Watts' vocal loops and the yellowthroat's witchety are both patterns that lodge in memory and refuse to leave. Once heard, never forgotten. The earworm principle in artistic and biological form.
2. **Phonetic play as music.** Watts uses nonsense syllables, beatboxing, and vocal manipulation to create rhythmic patterns that transcend language. The yellowthroat's "witchety" is a phonetic loop with no semantic content. Both achieve musicality through phonetics rather than lyrics.
3. **Repetition as the art.** Watts loops a pattern until it becomes hypnotic, until the repetition itself generates meaning. The yellowthroat repeats witchety 3--5 times per phrase, hundreds of times per day. The repetition IS the point.
4. **The catchiness IS the art.** For both Watts and the yellowthroat, being catchy is not a means to an end -- it is the end itself. The hook is the entire composition.

## What Worked

### 1. The hook as a new acoustic strategy category is genuinely novel in the engine
Eighteen degrees, and no previous species was defined by CATCHINESS. Complexity, meaning, persistence, narrative, urgency, brightness -- all present. But the earworm, the involuntary auditory memory, the hook that sticks -- this is new. The yellowthroat fills a category that was conspicuously absent. Music theory has studied hooks extensively; the engine now maps the hook to a biological exemplar.

### 2. The three-way repetition taxonomy (ostinato/narrative/hook) is the strongest formal distinction yet
Same mechanism (exact repetition), three psychological outcomes (habituation, structure, memorization). This is a clean, falsifiable taxonomy: the vireo's repetition fades from awareness, the MacGillivray's repetition builds to payoff, the yellowthroat's repetition forces itself into memory. The engine now has a formal framework for analyzing any repetitive vocalization.

### 3. The intra-genus pair demonstrates that taxonomy is not destiny
The MacGillivray's and the yellowthroat share a genus but employ opposite acoustic strategies. This is the first concrete demonstration that evolutionary relatedness does not predict acoustic strategy in the engine. The genus contains both the storyteller and the jingle writer.

### 4. Marsh edge habitat breaks the riparian monotony
Four consecutive water-associated degrees (14--17) could have blurred into habitat sameness. The yellowthroat's marsh habitat is genuinely different from riparian willow -- different vegetation structure, different acoustic environment, different ecological context. The engine's hydrological diversity deepens from linear (streams) to areal (basins).

### 5. The Reggie Watts parallel is structurally precise
Both achieve memorability through rhythmic repetition of phonetically salient nonsense patterns. Both use the loop as the art form. Both make you unable to stop hearing them. The parallel is not metaphorical -- it is mechanistic.

### 6. The cowbird contrast with degree 16 adds behavioral ecology depth
Two Parulidae, both frequent cowbird hosts, different anti-parasitism strategies. The Yellow Warbler builds over the problem. The yellowthroat absorbs it. Architecture vs. acceptance. The engine now documents both responses within the same family.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. Four consecutive Parulidae is now a serious concern
Degrees 14, 15, 16, 17 -- four warblers in a row. The degree 16 retrospective said "three consecutive warblers is the limit -- NO MORE WARBLERS." Degree 17 is a fourth warbler. The justification (return to Geothlypis, new habitat type, new acoustic strategy) is legitimate, but the taxonomic concentration is undeniable. Degree 18 MUST NOT be a warbler. This is non-negotiable.

### 2. Still only one non-passerine (the heron at degree 0)
Seventeen of eighteen species are passerines. The taxonomic diversity gap is now the engine's largest structural deficit. A woodpecker, raptor, seabird, or waterfowl is overdue.

### 3. Five consecutive neotropical migrants (degrees 14--17 plus degree 6)
Five migrants in the engine, four of them consecutive at degrees 14--17. The engine is heavily migrant-biased in its recent degrees. A year-round RESIDENT is urgently needed.

### 4. Energy-3 is now a three-species plateau -- energy 4--5 still absent
Three consecutive energy-3 species (degrees 15, 16, 17) without advancing to 4 or 5. The energy arc has stalled.

### 5. Still no predator species
Eighteen degrees of prey species. The alarm network has broadcasters and receivers but no trigger. A raptor would complete the information loop.

### 6. Still no percussive / non-vocal sound
All eighteen degrees produce sound via the syrinx. A woodpecker's drumming would introduce mechanical percussion -- a fundamentally different sound-production mechanism.

### 7. Still no marine or true shoreline species
Eighteen degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" with no species of the Sound itself. The estuary edge (heron, degree 0) is the closest. A seabird or true marine species is needed.

8. **A woodpecker satisfies items 1, 2, 3, 4, and 7.** A Pileated Woodpecker or Downy Woodpecker would be: non-passerine (Piciformes), not a warbler, resident, percussive, and potentially higher-energy. A woodpecker is the single species that addresses the most recommendations simultaneously.
