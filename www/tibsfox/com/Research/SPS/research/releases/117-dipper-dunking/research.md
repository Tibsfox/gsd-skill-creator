## THE DUNKING BEHAVIOR -- THE PHYSICS OF UNDERWATER FORAGING

### The Plunge

The American Dipper's defining physical behavior is the **plunge** -- the moment when the bird leaves its rock perch and enters fast-flowing water. The plunge takes several forms:

1. **The walk-in** -- The bird walks directly from its rock perch into the water, wading in like a person entering a pool. The transition from air to water is gradual. The bird's feet maintain contact with the substrate throughout. As the water deepens, the bird's body submerges incrementally -- first feet, then belly, then back, then head -- until it is fully submerged and walking along the bottom.

2. **The direct dive** -- The bird flies from its perch and enters the water in flight, plunging in headfirst or feet-first. The transition is sudden. The bird goes from aerial flight to underwater locomotion in an instant. This is the more dramatic form and produces the characteristic **splash** that integrates with song fragments.

3. **The surface swim** -- Less common. The bird swims on the surface like a small duck before submerging. The American Dipper can swim using its wings for propulsion, but this is not its preferred mode. It is a walker, not a swimmer. The underwater walk is the signature behavior.

### Walking Underwater Against the Current

The most extraordinary aspect of the dipper's dunking behavior is its ability to **walk along the bottom of fast-flowing streams against the current**. This is a feat of physics and biomechanics that is unique among songbirds.

**The hydrodynamic problem:** A 60-gram bird must resist being swept downstream by water moving at velocities of 0.5-2.0 meters per second. The dynamic pressure on the bird's body at these flow rates is significant relative to its mass. A human in the same current would struggle to stand. The dipper walks casually.

**The biomechanical solution:**

1. **Wing hydrofoils** -- The key adaptation. When walking underwater, the dipper partially opens its wings and angles them against the current. The wings act as **hydrofoils** -- the water flowing over the angled wing surfaces generates a downward force (negative lift) that presses the bird against the stream bottom. The bird literally uses the current against itself: the faster the water flows, the more downward force the wings generate, the more firmly the bird is pressed against the rocks. This is the same principle that keeps a race car's spoiler pressed against the track, inverted: the dipper's wings are inverted airplane wings underwater, generating downforce instead of lift.

2. **Strong claws on rocky substrate** -- The bird's powerful toes and sharp claws grip the rocky stream bottom. The combination of wing-generated downforce and claw grip enables the bird to maintain position and move forward against currents that would sweep it away if it relied on friction alone.

3. **Streamlined body posture** -- The bird tucks into a compact, streamlined shape underwater, reducing drag. The short tail (unusual for a passerine -- most songbirds have longer tails) is an adaptation that reduces underwater drag. The stocky body shape that looks unremarkable on land is hydrodynamically efficient underwater.

4. **Dense plumage as ballast** -- The air trapped in the dipper's dense plumage provides buoyancy that the bird must overcome to stay submerged. The bird tilts forward and uses the wing hydrofoils to counteract this buoyancy. The dense plumage that insulates also creates a buoyancy management challenge that the bird has solved through active hydrodynamic control.

### The Physics of Foraging

Once on the stream bottom, the dipper forages by **probing between and under rocks** for aquatic invertebrates:

- **Caddisfly larvae** (Trichoptera) -- The dipper's primary prey. Caddisfly larvae build protective cases from sand grains, pebbles, and plant material. The dipper extracts the larvae from their cases, consuming the soft body and discarding the case. The ability to locate and extract case-building larvae from between rocks, underwater, in fast current, is a remarkable foraging skill.

- **Mayfly nymphs** (Ephemeroptera) -- Found clinging to the undersides of rocks. The dipper flips small rocks and probes crevices to find them.

- **Stonefly nymphs** (Plecoptera) -- Large, protein-rich prey found in well-oxygenated mountain streams. The dipper seeks them in the spaces between cobbles.

- **Small fish and fish eggs** -- Occasional prey, especially during salmon spawning season. The dipper forages among salmon redds (gravel nests) for dislodged eggs. This connects the dipper to the salmon cycle that is central to PNW ecology.

- **Aquatic worms and small crustaceans** -- Supplemental prey found in benthic sediments.

The foraging dives typically last **10-30 seconds**, though dives up to 45 seconds have been recorded. The bird can forage in water up to **1 meter deep**. The water temperature is often near freezing (0-4 degrees C). The bird emerges from each dive dry -- its dense, oiled plumage sheds water instantly -- and resumes its perch bobbing within seconds.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sullivan 1973; Kingery 1996; Bakus 1959; hydrodynamic literature)

## THE SPLASH-SONG INTEGRATION

### Sound and Submersion

The acoustic signature of degree 117 is the **splash integrated with song** -- the sound produced when the dipper's dunking behavior occurs during or between song phrases. The integration is not a deliberate musical choice by the bird; it is an emergent acoustic phenomenon created by the bird's dual behavioral streams (singing and foraging) operating in the same time and space.

The sequence:
1. The dipper perches on a rock in mid-stream, singing its loud, bubbly, continuous song.
2. Mid-phrase or between phrases, the bird plunges into the water -- producing a **splash**.
3. The splash is audible against the backdrop of rushing water and the bird's own song.
4. The bird forages underwater for 10-30 seconds (silence from the bird; the stream roars on).
5. The bird surfaces, pops onto a rock, and resumes singing -- sometimes immediately, picking up where it left off.

The acoustic result: **bursts of complex bubbly song, punctuated by splashes, against a continuous backdrop of rushing water**. Three sound layers interweave:
- **Layer 1: The stream** -- constant broadband noise, the bass drone of rushing water
- **Layer 2: The song** -- intermittent, complex, bubbly, high-frequency, cutting above the water noise
- **Layer 3: The splash** -- periodic, sharp, transient -- the impact of the bird entering water

This three-layer acoustic texture is unique in the PNW soundscape. No other species produces anything like it. The splash-song integration is not designed; it is the inevitable acoustic consequence of a singing bird that plunges into water as part of its foraging routine. But the result is musically compelling: rhythm (the periodic splashes), melody (the bubbly song), and harmony (the stream's broadband drone) coexisting in a single acoustic space.

### E=5 -- The Energy of Submersion

E=5 at degree 117 reflects the **physical energy** of the dunking behavior. The plunge into fast-flowing mountain water is an athletic act. The bird must overcome buoyancy, resist current, grip rocks, probe for prey, and return to the surface -- all while maintaining body temperature in near-freezing water. The energy expenditure is enormous for a 60-gram bird. E=5 captures the athletic intensity: this is not a bird sitting on a branch. This is a bird fighting a river.

## ADAPTATION DEEP-DIVE: THE NICTITATING MEMBRANE IN ACTION

The dipper's **nictitating membrane** -- the translucent third eyelid -- deserves special attention in the context of the dunking behavior. When the bird submerges, the nictitating membrane slides across the eye, providing:

1. **Underwater vision** -- The membrane is transparent enough to allow the bird to see and locate prey between rocks on the stream bottom. The clarity is not perfect (the membrane introduces some optical distortion) but sufficient for close-range foraging in the shallow, clear water of mountain streams.

2. **Debris protection** -- Mountain streams carry suspended sediment, sand grains, and organic particles. The nictitating membrane protects the cornea from abrasion during underwater foraging.

3. **Focus compensation** -- The transition from air (low refractive index) to water (high refractive index) changes the optical characteristics of the eye. The nictitating membrane, combined with the bird's accommodative lens, partially compensates for the refractive shift, maintaining useful focus underwater.

The dipper's nictitating membrane is thicker and more optically refined than that of non-aquatic birds. This is an adaptation specifically for underwater use -- one of the suite of aquatic specializations that make the dipper's dunking behavior physically possible.

(Sources: Kingery 1996; comparative avian ophthalmology)

## Acoustic Profile: Splash + Song

### The Sound Signature

The acoustic profile of degree 117 is the **splash + song integration**: the sound of an American Dipper alternating between singing on a rock and plunging into water.

- **Splash characteristics:** Transient broadband noise. Duration approximately 0.1-0.3 seconds. Frequency range: broadband (20 Hz - 10,000+ Hz), concentrated in low frequencies (below 1,000 Hz). The splash is acoustically similar to stream noise but distinct in its transient character -- a sharp impulse against the continuous drone.
- **Song characteristics:** Continuous bubbly song, 2,000-8,000 Hz, loud, complex, varied (as documented at degree 116). The song occupies the high-frequency acoustic window above stream noise.
- **Integration:** The splash punctuates the song like a rhythmic accent. The pattern is: **sing-sing-sing-SPLASH-silence (underwater)-SURFACE-sing-sing-sing-SPLASH**. The periodicity depends on foraging intensity -- during active foraging, splashes may occur every 20-60 seconds. During rest/territory defense, the song is continuous without splashes.

### Frequency Range

- Stream noise: dominant below 2,000 Hz (continuous)
- Song: 2,000-8,000 Hz (intermittent, loud)
- Splash: broadband transient, peaking below 1,000 Hz (periodic)

### Behavioral Context

The splash-song integration occurs most frequently during foraging periods when the bird is also maintaining territorial awareness. The bird alternates between two behavioral states -- singing (territory defense / pair communication) and foraging (food acquisition) -- and the acoustic signature reflects these alternations. The splash is the acoustic boundary between the two states: the moment the singer becomes the diver.
