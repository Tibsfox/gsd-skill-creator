## DRYOCOPUS PILEATUS -- THE SPECIES

### The Pileated Woodpecker: Biology and Significance

*Dryocopus pileatus* (Linnaeus, 1758) is the largest woodpecker in North America:

- **Identification.** Crow-sized (18-19 inches / 45-48 cm body length, wingspan 26-30 inches / 66-75 cm). Jet black body, white neck stripes, white underwing flash in flight, and a brilliant red crest that is the species' defining field mark. In the Pacific Northwest forest, the Pileated's size and color make it one of the most unmistakable birds. Both sexes have red crests; males additionally have a red "mustache" stripe.
- **Scientific name.** *Dryocopus* (from Greek: drys = tree, kopos = striker -- "tree striker"); *pileatus* (Latin: capped or crested -- from pileus, the cap). Named by Linnaeus in 1758. The "crested tree striker" is a direct description of the species' defining behaviors: the red crest and the percussive wood-striking.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** Common and widespread in mature and old-growth Pacific Northwest forest. The Pileated requires large territories (typically 100-400 acres of forest per pair) with substantial dead wood -- snags for foraging and potential nest cavities. It is present in the Cascades, Olympics, Coast Ranges, and the forested lowlands wherever large trees persist. Unlike the Northern Spotted Owl and Northern Goshawk, the Pileated is not exclusively old-growth dependent -- it uses mature second-growth as well -- but the largest and most structurally developed forests support the highest densities.
- **Ecological role.** The Pileated is the Pacific Northwest's primary excavator of large cavities in snags. The rectangular excavation holes it creates in dead wood are diagnostically identifiable (unlike the rounder cavities of smaller woodpeckers). The excavations serve multiple functions: foraging access to carpenter ant galleries, nest cavity creation (primary cavities used by the Pileated and secondary cavities taken over by many other species: Wood Duck, goldeneyes, Barred Owl, Northern Flying Squirrel). The Pileated is a keystone species for cavity-nesting wildlife.
- **Primary food: carpenter ants.** The Pileated's primary food is carpenter ants (*Camponotus* species), which form large colonies in the heartwood of dead and dying trees. The bird's extraordinary bill and skull structure -- adapted for the high-force impact of drumming and for a long, sticky tongue that extracts ants from galleries -- is specifically tuned to this food source. The large rectangular excavations visible on Pacific Northwest snags are the diagnostic evidence of Pileated foraging.

(Sources: Dryocopus pileatus identification; 18-19 inches body length; wingspan 26-30 inches; red crest as defining field mark; Dryocopus = tree striker etymology; pileatus = crested etymology; Linnaeus 1758; Pacific Northwest distribution; territory size 100-400 acres; old-growth and mature second-growth; keystone excavator role; rectangular excavation distinctive; primary food: carpenter ants Camponotus; long sticky tongue anatomy)

## THE FULL DRUM SEQUENCE -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Industrial Machine-Gun on Dead Fir

The Pileated Woodpecker's drum sequence on a large dead snag is one of the most powerful sound-producing events in the Pacific Northwest forest:

- **The drum mechanics.** The Pileated strikes the surface of a dead tree repeatedly and rapidly with its bill. The bill is adapted for maximum force: the skull has a spongy bone structure that absorbs impact, the hyoid bone wraps around the skull to provide additional impact protection, and the bill is slightly asymmetrical at the tip to prevent slippage. The woodpecker can deliver up to 20 strikes per second on a resonant snag.
- **The snag as resonating chamber.** A large dead Douglas-fir or Western red cedar snag acts as a resonating chamber, amplifying the impact into a loud, carrying sound. The larger the snag diameter and the more hollow the interior, the louder and more resonant the drum. Snags of 2-4 feet diameter are the preferred drumming substrate.
- **The sound: industrial machine-gun.** The drum sequence on a large dead fir produces a rapid series of deep, woody thwacks that accelerate slightly at the end of a sequence and carry through the forest at high volume. The carrying distance is 500 meters to over 1 kilometer in favorable conditions. The rhythm is diagnostic -- faster and more sustained than any smaller woodpecker's drum.
- **Frequency characteristics.** The drumming produces broadband impact sounds with energy concentrated at lower frequencies (200-2,000 Hz), plus harmonic content from the resonating wood. The low-frequency component -- the deep thwack that penetrates through forest -- is what makes the Pileated's drum audible at such long distances.
- **Territorial vs. foraging drumming.** There are two distinct contexts for Pileated drumming: territorial drumming (advertising presence, defending territory, communication between mates) and foraging drumming (excavating snags to access carpenter ant galleries). Territorial drumming is typically performed on dead snags with maximum resonance, at prominent locations visible across the territory. Foraging drumming produces the characteristic large rectangular excavations. The full drum sequence at E=7 incorporates both: the territorial resonance of a prominent snag combined with the foraging commitment of full-force excavation.
- **E=7 calibration.** The full drum sequence is E=7: more intense than the laughing call (E=6, dramatic but brief) because it is sustained. The drumming can continue for minutes at a session. The sound carries farther and is sustained longer than the laughing call. E=7 is the correct escalation from E=6.

## OLD-GROWTH SNAG ECOLOGY

### The Snag as Pacific Northwest Infrastructure

The dead standing tree (snag) is one of the Pacific Northwest's most ecologically significant structures:

- **Snag definition.** A snag is a standing dead tree. In the Pacific Northwest, large snags -- Douglas-fir, Western red cedar, Sitka spruce, Western hemlock -- are the result of natural tree death from disease, windthrow, lightning, and old age. The time from tree death to full snag decomposition may span 100-200 years. The different stages of decay provide different habitat values.
- **The snag as community resource.** A single large snag in Pacific Northwest old-growth forest supports dozens of vertebrate species across its decay stages: cavity-nesters using excavated holes (Pileated Woodpecker, Northern Flying Squirrel, Wood Duck, Barred Owl, Vaux's Swift in hollow tops), bark-foragers accessing beetle galleries, ground-level denners using the root system, amphibians in moist interior wood. The snag is not dead infrastructure -- it is active community infrastructure.
- **The Pileated's role in snag ecology.** The Pileated Woodpecker is the primary creator of large cavities in Pacific Northwest snags. Without the Pileated's excavation, the interior galleries of dead trees are inaccessible to most other species. The Pileated opens the snag, and the rest of the community follows. As a keystone species for cavity-nesting, the Pileated's presence determines the cavity-nesting community structure of a forest patch.
- **Old-growth snag vs. young stand snag.** Large, high-quality snags -- the substrate that produces the resonant drum at E=7 -- are almost exclusively found in old-growth or mature forest. Young plantation forests lack large dead trees: the trees have not yet had time to die and reach the 2-4 foot diameter that produces the best resonating substrate. The E=7 drum quality is a function of old-growth. The sound is not possible without the centuries of tree growth that produce the snag.
- **The five-sixths mark and old-growth continuity.** The five-sixths mark landing on the Pileated Woodpecker full drum -- an old-growth snag sound -- is ecologically coherent. The engine began (degree 4, Northern Spotted Owl) in old-growth. The engine's three-quarters mark and other structural moments have repeatedly aligned with old-growth ecology. The five-sixths mark closes the raptor-owl cluster (297-299) and lands on the old-growth snag. Old-growth is the ecological thread of the Pacific Northwest engine.

(Sources: Snag definition; PNW snag decay: 100-200 years; snag community users: cavity-nesters, bark foragers, ground denners, amphibians; Pileated as keystone snag excavator; large cavity creation; community succession from Pileated excavation; old-growth snag vs. young stand snag quality; 2-4 foot diameter snags in old-growth; five-sixths mark and old-growth thread)
