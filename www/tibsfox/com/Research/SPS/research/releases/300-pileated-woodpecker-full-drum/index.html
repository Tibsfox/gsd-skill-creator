Acoustic Profile — Full Drum Sequence

Impact mechanics: Up to 20 strikes per second. Skull spongy bone absorbs impact. Hyoid bone wraps skull for protection. Asymmetric bill tip prevents slippage. The largest woodpecker delivering maximum force.

Snag resonance: Large dead Douglas-fir or Western red cedar (2-4 foot diameter) acts as resonating chamber. The hollower the interior, the louder and more resonant the drum. Old-growth snags produce the best resonance; young stand snags do not.

Frequency: Broadband impact sound, energy concentrated 200-2,000 Hz. Low-frequency component (the deep thwack) carries through forest at long range. Audible 500m to over 1km.

E=7 vs. E=6 laughing: The drum is sustained for minutes at a session; the laughing call is dramatic but brief. E=7 for sustained duration + long-range carry. E=6 for the dramatic but shorter laughing vocalization.

The Old-Growth Snag — Why E=7 Requires Centuries

The full drum at E=7 is only possible on a large old-growth snag. A Douglas-fir of 2-4 feet diameter, dead and partially hollow, acting as a resonating chamber — this substrate requires 150-200+ years of tree growth followed by decades of decay. The E=7 drum is a function of old-growth. Young plantation forests lack this substrate. The sound is not possible without the centuries that produce it.

The Pileated is the Pacific Northwest's keystone cavity excavator: the rectangular holes it creates in snags are taken over by Wood Duck, goldeneye, Barred Owl, Northern Flying Squirrel, Vaux's Swift. The drum is not just territorial announcement — it is the first strike of infrastructure creation.
