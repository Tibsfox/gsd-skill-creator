## The Drum: First Non-Vocal Sound in the Engine

### What Drumming Is

Woodpecker drumming is **instrumental music**. The bird strikes a hard substrate with its bill in rapid, rhythmically regular bursts to produce a loud, resonant sound. Drumming is not foraging -- a drumming bird is not extracting insects or excavating a cavity. Drumming is communication. It serves the same functions as song in passerines: **territorial advertisement** and **mate attraction**. The drum is the woodpecker's substitute for the elaborate vocal song that woodpeckers, as a family, generally lack.

This distinction matters. Passerines have the most complex syringeal muscles in the avian world, producing the richest vocal repertoires. Woodpeckers have comparatively simple syringes. They compensate with percussion. The wick-wick-wick call (degree 21) is the Northern Flicker's vocal contribution -- simple, loud, metronomic. The drum is the species' non-vocal contribution -- also simple, also loud, also metronomic, but produced through an entirely different physical mechanism. Voice and instrument. Air and wood.

### The Mechanics of the Flicker Drum

The Northern Flicker drums by striking its bill against a resonant surface in a rapid burst:

1. **Strike rate.** Approximately **25 strikes per second** (Stark, Dodenhoff, and Johnson 1998, *Condor*). This is the most commonly reported figure for Northern Flicker drumming. The rate is remarkably consistent within individuals and between bouts. Compare to other PNW woodpeckers: the Downy Woodpecker drums at approximately 15--17 strikes per second; the Hairy Woodpecker at approximately 25 per second; the Pileated Woodpecker at approximately 15--17 per second (Dodenhoff, Stark, and Johnson 2001, *Condor*). The flicker drums in the mid-to-fast range of the Picidae drumming spectrum.

2. **Bout duration.** Approximately **0.6--1.2 seconds** per drumming bout (Dodenhoff, Stark, and Johnson 2001). This is notably longer than many woodpecker species. A one-second flicker drum at 25 strikes per second contains approximately 25 individual bill impacts -- a rapid-fire burst that is over before a listener can consciously count the strikes.

3. **Temporal pattern.** The flicker drum is characteristically **even-tempo with a slight deceleration at the end** -- the final few strikes are marginally slower and softer than the opening salvo. This terminal deceleration is a species-diagnostic feature. Other woodpeckers have different temporal signatures: the Downy Woodpecker accelerates slightly; the Red-breasted Sapsucker produces an irregular, stuttering pattern. The flicker's drum pattern is its **acoustic fingerprint** -- as species-specific as a passerine's song.

4. **Amplitude envelope.** The drum starts at full force and maintains high amplitude through most of the bout before the slight terminal decay. This is broadly consistent with the wick call's flat amplitude contour (degree 21) but with a natural mechanical decay: each successive strike is fractionally less forceful as the drumming muscles fatigue over the one-second burst. The overall impression is still one of sustained power, not gradual fade.

5. **Frequency content.** The drum is **broadband** -- it contains energy across a wide frequency range rather than being pitched at a specific fundamental frequency. A typical flicker drum produces peak energy in the **500 Hz to 4 kHz range**, with significant energy extending down into the **100--500 Hz range** and up beyond **8 kHz**. The dominant frequency depends entirely on the resonant properties of the substrate (see below). There is **no pitched fundamental** in the way a vocal call has one. The drum is noise -- structured, rhythmic noise, but noise. Broadband percussive transients, not tonal oscillations.

### How the Drum Differs from the Wick Call

| Dimension | Degree 21: Wick Call | Degree 22: Drum |
|---|---|---|
| Physical mechanism | **Syrinx** -- air vibrating over vocal membranes | **Bill on substrate** -- solid striking solid |
| Energy source | Respiratory (airflow) | Muscular (head/neck strike) |
| Sound character | **Tonal** -- pitched at 2.0--2.5 kHz fundamental, harmonic content | **Broadband** -- no pitch, wide-spectrum percussive transient |
| Duration | **5--8 seconds** (long series of 25--36 notes) | **0.6--1.2 seconds** (short rapid burst of ~25 strikes) |
| Note/strike rate | **6--10 per second** (each note heard individually) | **~25 per second** (fuses into a continuous roll) |
| Perceptual fusion | **Below** -- each wick note is discrete, individually perceived | **Above** -- individual strikes merge into a continuous buzz/roll |
| Amplitude | **LOUD** -- one of the loudest PNW woodland vocalizations | **LOUD** -- amplified by substrate resonance, carries as far or farther |
| Pitch contour | **FLAT** -- every note identical pitch | **None** -- broadband, substrate determines resonant frequency |

The most critical difference: the wick call is **below the perceptual fusion threshold** (each note heard separately, the hammer), while the drum is **above the fusion threshold** (individual strikes merge into a continuous rapid roll). Degree 21 gave the engine discrete metronomic repetition. Degree 22 gives the engine fused mechanical repetition -- the drum roll. The progression from degree 11 (trill, approaching fusion) to degree 21 (hammer, below fusion) to degree 22 (drum, above fusion) maps the entire fusion continuum: blur, then clarity, then blur again -- but through a completely different physical channel.

## Resonance Physics: The Substrate IS the Instrument

### Why Substrate Selection Matters

When a drummer chooses a snare drum versus a floor tom versus a ride cymbal, the instrument shapes the sound. When a Northern Flicker chooses a dead alder snag versus a metal chimney cap versus a cedar utility pole, the substrate shapes the sound. **The flicker selects its instrument.**

This is a concept with no precedent in the engine. Every previous species' sound was determined by anatomy alone -- the syrinx, the body cavity, the bill. The Northern Flicker's wick call (degree 21) is the same: the syrinx produces the sound, and the sound is anatomically fixed. But the drum introduces a variable: the external substrate. The frequency, amplitude, timbre, and propagation distance of the drum are all functions of what the bird strikes. The substrate is part of the instrument.

### Resonance Selection Behavior

Northern Flickers preferentially select **maximally resonant surfaces** for drumming (Dodenhoff, Stark, and Johnson 2001; Kilham 1959, *Wilson Bulletin*). They do not drum on whatever surface is nearest. They actively seek out and return to substrates that produce the loudest, most resonant sound. Field observations document consistent behaviors:

1. **Dead trees (snags).** The preferred natural drumming substrate. Dead wood -- particularly wood that is partially decayed but still structurally sound -- produces a hollow, resonant tone when struck. The internal structure of a dead tree creates an acoustic cavity: the softened interior amplifies the impact like the body of a drum. A standing dead alder or birch with a hollow interior is the flicker's **acoustic ideal** in a natural setting. The resonant frequency depends on the trunk diameter, wall thickness, wood density, and degree of decay. Thin-walled hollow snags produce lower, more resonant tones. Thick-walled solid snags produce higher, less resonant impacts.

2. **Metal surfaces.** Northern Flickers are notorious for drumming on **metal chimney caps, rain gutters, aluminum siding, metal roof flashing, and metal utility pole hardware**. Metal surfaces are dramatically more resonant than wood -- they ring rather than thud. A flicker drumming on a metal chimney cap produces a sound that can carry across an entire residential neighborhood, orders of magnitude louder than the same bird drumming on wood. Homeowners frequently report flickers drumming on their gutters at dawn as one of the most startling wildlife encounters in urban Seattle.

   The physics: metal has a higher **Q factor** (quality factor of resonance) than wood. When struck, metal rings longer and at higher amplitude than wood. The flicker's 25-strike-per-second burst on a metal chimney cap produces a machine-gun rattle that carries hundreds of meters. The bird has discovered, empirically, that metal out-performs wood as a resonant surface. This is not random behavior -- flickers **return to the same metal surface** day after day, defending it as a drumming station.

3. **Utility poles.** Wooden utility poles (especially cedar, the dominant pole material in the PNW) provide a large, cylindrical, elevated resonant surface. Some flickers develop a preference for a specific pole and drum on it throughout the breeding season. The hollow interior of an aging utility pole amplifies the drum.

4. **House siding, fascia boards, and wooden structures.** Flickers drum on the wooden trim of houses, particularly fascia boards beneath the roofline. Cedar and redwood trim resonate more effectively than composite materials. This behavior brings the flicker into direct acoustic contact with human dwellings -- the drum on a house fascia is audible throughout the structure.

### Resonance Physics in Detail

The drum sound is a convolution of two signals: the impact (the bill strike) and the impulse response (the substrate's resonant characteristics). Different substrates produce different sounds from the same strike:

| Substrate | Resonant Frequency | Amplitude | Sustain | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hollow dead alder | Low-mid (200--1,000 Hz dominant) | Moderate | Short | Hollow, woody thud |
| Solid dead conifer | Mid (500--2,000 Hz dominant) | Low-moderate | Very short | Dry, sharp tap |
| Metal chimney cap | High (1,000--6,000 Hz dominant) | **Very high** | Long (ringing) | Metallic rattle, piercing |
| Metal rain gutter | Mid-high (800--4,000 Hz dominant) | **High** | Moderate | Resonant metallic roll |
| Cedar utility pole | Low-mid (300--1,200 Hz dominant) | Moderate-high | Moderate | Deep, resonant knock |
| House fascia board | Mid (400--2,000 Hz dominant) | Moderate | Short-moderate | Wooden rap, house amplifies |

The flicker's selection of maximally resonant substrates is an **optimization problem**: maximize the amplitude and propagation distance of the drum signal to defend the largest possible territory and attract mates from the greatest possible distance. The bird that finds the loudest substrate wins the loudest voice. Metal surfaces represent an acoustic windfall -- a human-provided upgrade to the flicker's instrument. The urban flicker on a chimney cap is louder than any forest flicker on any tree.

### Substrate as Information

The resonant frequency of the drum carries information. Studies on other woodpecker species (Otter et al. 2014, *Animal Behaviour*; Elie and Theunissen 2015, *PNAS*) suggest that drumming substrates encode information about territory quality. A flicker that consistently drums on a large, hollow, resonant snag is implicitly advertising access to high-quality dead wood -- a resource that signals good nesting habitat. The substrate IS the territory report.

This means the drum carries a layer of information that the wick call cannot: **habitat quality**. The wick call says "I am here, this is my territory, I am fit." The drum says "I am here, this is my territory, I am fit, **and listen to this substrate** -- this is good habitat." Two channels, overlapping but not identical information.

## The 23rd Acoustic Element: The Resonant Percussion

Degree 22 introduces the **23rd element** in the engine's acoustic progression:

**The progression (23 elements):** silence -- cascade -- drone -- language -- motif -- metronome -- question -- harmonic spiral -- ultrasonic threshold -- variation -- minimum signal -- trill/tremolo -- sawtooth sweep -- ostinato -- repetition-resolution -- accelerando -- crescendo -- the hook -- ascending spiral -- descending spiral -- release -- the hammer -- **resonant percussion**.

Resonant percussion is **substrate-dependent, broadband, mechanically produced, rhythmically structured noise at or above the perceptual fusion threshold, amplified by the resonant properties of the struck surface**. Where the hammer (degree 21) was a vocal metronomic pulse of discrete identical notes, resonant percussion is a mechanical rapid-fire roll of fused impacts whose timbral character is determined by the instrument (substrate) rather than the performer (syrinx).

The distinction between the hammer and resonant percussion:

The 23rd element brings the engine its first encounter with the concept of **external instrument selection** -- the performer choosing which object to play. Every previous sound was generated internally, by the body. Resonant percussion requires an external object. The bird picks its drum.

## Dual-Channel Communication: Voice and Percussion

### Redundant Channels in the Same Species

The Northern Flicker at degrees 21--22 is the first organism in the engine to communicate through two fundamentally different physical channels: **air-borne vocal sound** (the wick call) and **substrate-borne mechanical percussion** (the drum). These are not two variations on the same mechanism. They are two different mechanisms serving overlapping but not identical functions.

**Vocal (wick call):**
- Propagates through air
- Tonal (frequency-specific, can be filtered by habitat acoustics)
- Range: 500+ meters in open habitat
- Information: identity, territory, fitness
- Timing: produced at any moment, from any perch
- 5--8 seconds duration

**Mechanical (drum):**
- Propagates through air AND through the substrate (vibration conducts through wood and metal)
- Broadband (wide frequency content, harder to mask by habitat noise)
- Range: variable, dependent on substrate -- metal substrates may exceed the wick call's range
- Information: identity, territory, fitness, **substrate quality (habitat assessment)**
- Timing: requires access to a suitable substrate -- location-dependent
- 0.6--1.2 seconds duration

### Why Two Channels?

The redundancy is not waste. Studies of woodpecker communication (Dodenhoff, Stark, and Johnson 2001; Miles and Fuxjager 2018, *Integrative and Comparative Biology*) identify several functional advantages of dual-channel signaling:

1. **Environmental robustness.** Dense vegetation attenuates high-frequency tonal sounds (like the wick call) more than broadband impulsive sounds (like the drum). In a cluttered forest environment, the drum may carry where the call does not. Conversely, in open woodland, the call may carry farther because it occupies a specific frequency band that cuts through ambient noise. Two channels increase the probability that at least one signal reaches the receiver regardless of environmental conditions.

2. **Temporal complementarity.** The wick call is long (5--8 seconds) and can be delivered from any perch. The drum is short (0.6--1.2 seconds) and requires a suitable substrate. A territorial flicker uses both: the call as a sustained, mobile broadcast, and the drum as a punctuating, location-anchored percussion. The two signals create a **temporal rhythm of territory defense** -- call, move, drum, call, move, drum.

3. **Information partitioning.** As noted above, the drum carries substrate-quality information that the call cannot. A territorial male advertising from a highly resonant snag is simultaneously demonstrating territory quality. The call says "I am here." The drum says "I am here, and here is worth being."

4. **Receiver differentiation.** Neighboring flickers can distinguish between the wick calls of different individuals (vocal individuality) AND between the drumming patterns of different individuals (temporal individuality -- slight variations in strike rate and bout duration). Two independent identification channels reduce the probability of territorial misidentification.

### The Dual-Channel Concept in Music Theory

In orchestration, a melody doubled across two instrument families (strings and winds; piano and voice; drum kit and bass guitar) is called a **doubling**. The Northern Flicker's territorial signal is doubled across vocal and mechanical channels. The principle is identical: redundancy ensures the message reaches the audience through at least one path.

But there is a deeper analogy. In recording engineering, the concept of **parallel compression** involves splitting a signal into two paths -- one clean and detailed, one heavily compressed and powerful -- and blending them. The wick call is the clean path: pitched, detailed, long, carrying specific harmonic information. The drum is the compressed path: broadband, powerful, short, carrying maximum energy. Together they create a territorial signal with both clarity and impact.

## Forest Snag Ecology: The Drum Needs Dead Wood

### What a Forest Snag Is

A **snag** is a standing dead or dying tree. Snags are not debris -- they are keystone habitat structures. The SPS engine has documented living trees (old-growth conifers, Garry oaks, alders, willows, cottonwoods) across twenty-one degrees of ecosystem progression. Degree 22 introduces the first ecosystem defined by **dead wood**.

The forest snag is not just the flicker's nesting substrate (covered at degree 21 as cavity excavation ecology). It is the flicker's **instrument**. The snag is what makes drumming possible. Without dead, resonant wood, there is no drum -- only the wick call. The instrument and the habitat are the same thing.

### Snag Formation and Acoustic Properties

Snags form through multiple pathways: wind damage, lightning strike, root disease, bark beetle infestation, fire, age-related senescence. Each pathway produces different internal structures with different acoustic properties:

1. **Fire-killed snags.** External wood charred and hardened; internal wood may remain intact or may char through. Produces a relatively solid, dense-walled acoustic cavity. Sound: sharp, bright, less resonant than hollow snags but louder per strike due to hard surface.

2. **Disease-hollowed snags.** Heart-rot fungi (*Fomitopsis pinicola*, *Phellinus pini*, *Laetiporus sulphureus*) consume the interior softwood while the harder sapwood shell remains standing. This creates the **ideal natural drum**: a thin-walled hollow cylinder. Sound: deep, resonant, with significant sustain. These are the snags flickers prefer for drumming.

3. **Bark beetle-killed snags.** Often retain their internal structure longer than disease-killed snags. The wood dries and hardens, producing a different resonant character. Sound: mid-frequency, moderate resonance, dry timbre.

4. **Wind-broken snags.** The top is sheared off, leaving a shortened trunk. Depending on the break point and subsequent decay, these can develop hollow interiors. Variable acoustics.

### Snag Density and Drumming Territory

The availability of suitable drumming snags directly affects Northern Flicker territory quality and density. Studies of woodpecker habitat in the PNW (Bull, Parks, and Torgersen 1997, *Journal of Wildlife Management*; Bate, Garton, and Wisdom 1999, *Journal of Wildlife Management*) document that:

- Mature and old-growth forests in the PNW contain **8--20 snags per hectare** of suitable woodpecker drumming/nesting size (>15 cm DBH).
- Managed forests and second-growth stands often contain **fewer than 3 snags per hectare**, reducing both nesting and drumming opportunities.
- Urban areas vary widely: neighborhoods with large, aging deciduous trees may provide adequate snags; heavily manicured areas with prompt dead-tree removal provide almost none.

The flicker's drumming is therefore a **direct indicator of snag availability** in the habitat. Where you hear flicker drumming, dead wood is present. Where dead wood is removed -- through forestry practices, urban tree maintenance, or fire suppression -- the drum falls silent. The instrument disappears, and with it, the music.

### The Snag as Ecosystem Hub

The degree 21 retrospective documented the Northern Flicker as a primary cavity excavator whose abandoned nest cavities house dozens of secondary species. Degree 22 extends this ecological role: the snag is not just a nesting site but a **communication hub**. The flicker's drum radiates from the snag, advertising territory and mate quality. Other cavity-nesting species (including those that will eventually use the flicker's excavated cavities) orient to the drum as a signal of habitat quality -- where a flicker drums, there are snags; where there are snags, there are cavities.

The forest snag at degree 22 is thus the engine's first ecosystem defined by a dead structure rather than a living community. The estuary (degree 0) is water and mud. The old-growth (degree 1) is ancient trees. The shrubby slope (degree 20) is living vegetation. The forest snag is a dead tree -- and it is alive with sound.

## Drumming as Territorial Display

### The Dawn Drum Session

In spring (February through June in the PNW), male Northern Flickers establish drumming routines that follow a predictable temporal pattern:

1. **Pre-dawn and dawn.** Peak drumming activity occurs in the hour before and after sunrise. The male flies to a preferred drumming station -- a resonant snag, utility pole, or metal surface -- and delivers repeated drumming bouts separated by pauses of 10--30 seconds. A dawn session may include **20--50 drumming bouts** over 15--30 minutes (Kilham 1959; personal observations documented in PNW birding literature).

2. **Midday.** Drumming frequency decreases but does not cease. Sporadic bouts throughout the day, often in response to hearing a neighboring male drum.

3. **Countersinging (counter-drumming).** Neighboring males engage in **drum exchanges**: one drums, the other responds within seconds, and the exchange continues in alternation. This counter-drumming is the percussive equivalent of countersinging in passerines (degree 14, MacGillivray's Warbler, described the ternary song's territorial countersinging function). The drum exchange escalates territorial disputes without physical contact. The louder, more resonant drum wins.

### Drumming vs. Excavation: How to Tell the Difference

Not all rapid bill strikes are drumming. Foraging (extracting insects from bark) and excavation (creating a nest cavity) also involve bill impacts on wood. The differences:

| Behavior | Strike Rate | Duration | Substrate | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Drumming** | ~25/sec (fast, regular) | 0.6--1.2 sec | Maximally resonant surface | Communication |
| **Excavation** | ~10--15/sec (irregular) | Continuous, minutes | Dead wood at nest site | Cavity construction |
| **Foraging** | ~2--5/sec (irregular) | Sporadic | Bark, soil | Food extraction |

Drumming is **fast, regular, short, and on the most resonant surface available**. Excavation is **slower, irregular, prolonged, and at the nest site regardless of resonance**. Foraging is **slowest and least regular**. A listener can distinguish drumming from excavation by tempo alone: the 25-strike-per-second burst of a drum is unmistakable.

## Instrumental Communication in Birds: The Broader Context

### Non-Vocal Sound Production Across Avian Orders

The Northern Flicker's drum is the engine's first non-vocal sound, but the concept of instrumental (non-vocal) communication in birds is widespread. Degree 22 establishes the principle that the engine can include non-syringeal sounds. Context for what this opens:

1. **Woodpecker drumming (Picidae).** The most familiar form of avian instrumental communication. All woodpecker species drum, with species-specific rates and patterns. The engine's first representative.

2. **Wing sounds.** Many bird species produce communicative sounds with their wings. The American Woodcock's twittering display flight, the Ruffed Grouse's wing-drumming display, the Anna's Hummingbird's dive-bomb chirp (produced by tail feathers), the Common Nighthawk's booming dive. None of these are in the engine yet.

3. **Bill clapping.** Storks clap their mandibles together as a greeting display. Not relevant to PNW species.

4. **Tail sounds.** Wilson's Snipe produces a winnowing sound by air vibrating through spread outer tail feathers during display flights. A PNW candidate for future degrees.

5. **Foot drumming.** Burrowing Owls drum their feet on the ground near burrow entrances.

The Northern Flicker's drum opens the engine to all non-vocal sounds. Degree 22 is the precedent. Any future species with a mechanically produced sound -- winnowing, wing-drumming, tail-buzzing -- can now enter the engine on the basis of that sound, because the principle that the engine includes non-vocal communication has been established.

### Woodpecker Drumming as a Species-Level Signature

Drumming patterns function as species identifiers. In mixed woodpecker communities (common in PNW forests), species recognition by drum pattern is critical. PNW woodpecker drumming signatures:

| Species | Strike Rate | Bout Duration | Pattern | Prevalence in PNW |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Northern Flicker** | **~25/sec** | **0.6--1.2 sec** | **Even with terminal deceleration** | **Common -- lowland, urban** |
| Downy Woodpecker | ~15--17/sec | 0.8--1.5 sec | Even tempo, short bouts | Common |
| Hairy Woodpecker | ~25/sec | 0.5--0.8 sec | Even tempo, shorter than flicker | Common |
| Pileated Woodpecker | ~15--17/sec | 2--3 sec | Accelerating then decelerating | Mature forest |
| Red-breasted Sapsucker | Irregular | Variable | Stuttering, uneven rhythm | Conifer forest |
| Three-toed Woodpecker | ~8--12/sec | 1--2 sec | Slower, deliberate | High elevation |

The flicker's drum is distinguishable from co-occurring species by its specific combination of rate (~25/sec), duration (~1 sec), and the characteristic terminal deceleration. A trained listener in a PNW woodland can identify the species by drum alone without seeing the bird.

## Drumming and the Skull: The Biomechanics of Percussion

### Impact Forces

A Northern Flicker striking a hard substrate at 25 times per second subjects its skull to repeated deceleration forces. Early estimates placed these forces at **600--1,500 g** per impact (May et al. 1976, *Science*; Schwab 2002, *British Journal of Ophthalmology*). More recent work by Van Wassenbergh et al. (2022, *Current Biology*) -- cited at degree 21 for the skull-as-rigid-hammer hypothesis -- measured actual woodpecker impact forces and found them lower than the classic estimates, approximately **100--300 g** for medium-sized woodpeckers, but still far exceeding anything a human skull could tolerate repeatedly.

The biomechanical adaptations that enable this (covered briefly at degree 21 for context) take on specific significance for drumming:

1. **The stiff skull hypothesis** (Van Wassenbergh et al. 2022). Rather than absorbing shock, the woodpecker skull may function as a **rigid hammer** -- transmitting maximum energy to the substrate with minimal internal deformation. A rigid skull drums louder. The skull is not protecting the brain from impact; the brain is small enough relative to the skull that it does not experience dangerous deceleration at the measured force levels. The skull IS the drumstick.

2. **The spongy bone.** The trabecular (spongy) bone between the outer and inner tables of the skull does provide some vibration damping, even if its primary function is structural rigidity rather than shock absorption. The net effect: the flicker's skull is simultaneously a drum mallet and a protective casing.

3. **The hyoid brace.** The hyoid apparatus -- the same tongue-support structure that wraps around the skull for ant extraction (degree 21) -- may function as a tension band during drumming, stabilizing the skull against lateral displacement during impact. The tongue system and the drumming system share anatomy.

### Muscular Endurance

Drumming at 25 strikes per second requires extraordinary neuromuscular coordination. The neck muscles that power each strike must contract and release 25 times per second -- a firing rate comparable to the flight muscles during hovering. The flicker achieves this through:

- **Fast-twitch muscle fibers** in the neck and head musculature, optimized for rapid cycling.
- **Short stroke length** -- the bill moves only a few centimeters per strike, minimizing the distance the head must travel.
- **Brief bout duration** -- the 0.6--1.2 second bout prevents muscle fatigue from degrading the signal. The pause between bouts (10--30 seconds) allows recovery.

---

## The Flicker Pair Complete: Structural Summary

Degrees 21--22 form a complete unit -- the engine's first species-pair. Together they establish:

1. **Two sound-production mechanisms from one species.** Voice (21) and percussion (22). The engine's acoustic vocabulary expands from "sounds produced by the syrinx" to "sounds produced by any anatomical mechanism."

2. **The fusion continuum mapped.** The wick call (degree 21) is below fusion -- each note heard individually. The drum (degree 22) is above fusion -- strikes merge into a roll. Combined with the trill (degree 11, at fusion), the engine now has three positions on the fusion continuum: at, below, and above.

3. **The concept of external instruments.** Degree 22 introduces the first sound whose character depends on an external object (the substrate). Every previous sound was internally generated. The drum requires the world outside the bird.

4. **Forest snag as ecosystem.** Dead wood enters the engine's habitat vocabulary. The instrument IS the habitat.

5. **Energy 4 sustained.** Two consecutive degrees at E=4 (synchronized with Part A at both degrees) establishes the new energy level as a baseline, not a spike.

6. **Broadband sound enters the engine.** Twenty-one degrees of tonal, pitched, frequency-specific sounds followed by the first broadband, non-pitched percussive transient. The engine's spectral vocabulary expands from pitched to pitched + unpitched.
