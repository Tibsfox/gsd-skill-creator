### The Substrate as Instrument: Why the Drumming Surface Matters

The flicker does not drum on random surfaces. It selects resonant substrates — dead snags, hollow branches, metal chimneys, gutters, and any surface that amplifies the bill strike into a louder, more sustained, more broadcast-capable sound. The substrate is the instrument. The bill is the mallet. The flicker is the percussionist who chooses the drum.

This is the most literal instrument-selection behavior in the engine. Every prior species used the syrinx — a fixed, internal instrument that cannot be changed. The flicker can change its instrument by changing its drumming surface. A hollow snag produces a deep, resonant boom. A metal chimney produces a loud, tinny rattle. A dead branch produces a dry, rattling burst. Same bird, same bill, same strike rate — different instrument, different timbre, different broadcast range. The flicker practices **instrument selection** — the acoustic equivalent of a guitarist choosing between a Stratocaster and a Les Paul.

Carpenter's Aurora 7 used the same Mercury capsule as Glenn's Friendship 7 — same instrument, different deployment. The astronaut changed the mission parameters (Carpenter spent more time on science experiments), but the hardware was identical. The flicker changes the substrate; the astronaut changes the experiment protocol. Both demonstrate that the output varies with the deployment context, even when the generating mechanism is constant.
