## === FIRST OTARIIDAE -- THE EARED SEALS ENTER ===

### A New Family

The Otariidae (eared seals) enter the engine at degree 182:

- **FIRST OTARIIDAE.** The eared seal family includes sea lions and fur seals. They are distinguished from Phocidae (true seals, degrees 180-181) by external ear flaps, the ability to rotate hind flippers forward for walking on land, and long fore flippers used for swimming.
- **Phocidae vs Otariidae.** This is the fundamental split in pinnipeds:
  - Phocidae (true seals): No external ears. Cannot rotate hind flippers. Undulate on land. Swim with hind flippers. Harbor Seal (180-181).
  - Otariidae (eared seals): External ear flaps. Rotate hind flippers forward. Walk on land. Swim with fore flippers. Steller Sea Lion (182) begins the sequence.
- **Three Otariidae entries planned.** Steller Sea Lion (182), California Sea Lion (183), Northern Fur Seal (184). The eared seal trio follows the true seal pair (180-181).
- **Marine mammal era.** Degrees 180-184 form the marine mammal block: Phocidae (180-181), Otariidae (182-184). Five consecutive pinniped entries.

## E=6 -- THE COLONY ROAR

### Why E=6

The Steller Sea Lion's vocalization registers at E=6:

- **Bull roar.** The breeding bull's roar is deep, sustained, and thunderous. Males establish and defend territories on breeding rookeries with vocalizations that can be heard across hundreds of meters of rocky coastline. The roar is the primary assertion of dominance.
- **Colony cacophony.** At a breeding rookery with hundreds of Steller Sea Lions, the combined vocalization creates a wall of sound. Bulls roaring, cows calling, pups bleating -- the acoustic environment is intense, continuous, and overwhelming.
- **Volume.** The Steller Sea Lion's roar is the loudest pinniped vocalization in the PNW. Males at 2,500 lbs produce proportionally powerful vocalizations.
- **Sustained.** Unlike the Harbor Seal's intermittent bark, the Steller Sea Lion roar is more sustained -- longer calls, more frequent repetition, continuous at rookeries.
- **Why E=6 (maximum for SPS)?** The colony roar at peak intensity represents the highest sustained natural vocalization among PNW pinnipeds. E=6 is the SPS ceiling for natural vocalizations.

## THE LARGEST SEA LION IN THE WORLD

### Sexual Dimorphism and Scale

The Steller Sea Lion's physical presence is extraordinary:

- **Males:** Up to 3.3 meters (11 feet) long. Weight up to 1,120 kg (2,500 lbs). Massive forequarters, thick neck, prominent sagittal crest. The male is one of the largest pinnipeds on Earth.
- **Females:** Up to 2.5 meters (8 feet) long. Weight up to 350 kg (770 lbs). Still large -- larger than most other sea lion species.
- **Sexual dimorphism ratio:** Males are approximately 3x the weight of females. This extreme dimorphism drives the harem-defense mating system.
- **Comparison:** A male Steller Sea Lion weighs roughly the same as a grizzly bear or a small horse. On a rocky haul-out, the visual impact is startling -- an animal that seems to belong in the age of megafauna.
- **Named for Georg Steller.** Georg Wilhelm Steller (1709-1746) was the naturalist aboard Vitus Bering's Second Kamchatka Expedition (1741). He described the Steller Sea Lion, Steller's Sea Eagle, Steller's Jay, and the now-extinct Steller's Sea Cow. His legacy in PNW natural history is immense.

(Sources: NOAA Steller sea lion fact sheet; Loughlin 2009; sexual dimorphism studies; Georg Steller biography; engine internal analysis)

## ENDANGERED STATUS

### Conservation Crisis

The Steller Sea Lion has experienced significant population decline:

- **Two Distinct Population Segments (DPS):**
  - **Western DPS:** Listed as Endangered under the Endangered Species Act. Experienced approximately 80% population decline from the 1970s to the 2000s. Range: western Alaska and into Asia.
  - **Eastern DPS:** Listed as Threatened, then delisted in 2013 after population recovery. Range: Southeast Alaska through California, including the PNW.
- **Causes of decline:** Nutritional stress (fisheries competition, ecosystem shifts), predation (orcas), entanglement, disease, and historical harvest.
- **PNW populations:** In the PNW (Washington, Oregon, British Columbia), Steller Sea Lion populations have been more stable than the western DPS. Breeding occurs on offshore islands and sea stacks. Key sites include the Oregon coast and outer Washington coast.
- **Recovery efforts:** Critical habitat designation, fisheries management, monitoring, research. The eastern population has recovered sufficiently for delisting, but the species remains a conservation priority.

(Sources: NMFS Steller sea lion recovery plan; ESA listing; population trend data; WDFW; engine internal analysis)

## ROOKERIES AND HAUL-OUTS

### The Rocky Kingdoms

Steller Sea Lion habitat in the PNW:

- **Breeding rookeries.** Offshore islands and sea stacks with rocky surfaces. Males establish territories in spring/summer. Females give birth and nurse pups on these rocky sites. The rookery is an intense social environment -- territorial disputes, mating, nursing, pup rearing, all happening simultaneously.
- **Haul-out sites.** Non-breeding animals (and breeding animals outside the season) haul out on rocks, offshore islands, jetties, and occasionally docks. Haul-outs can contain hundreds of animals.
- **Steller vs Harbor Seal haul-outs.** Harbor Seals (180) haul out on protected beaches and sandbars. Steller Sea Lions haul out on exposed rocky sites and offshore islands. The Steller Sea Lion prefers more rugged, exposed environments.
- **PNW sites.** Key haul-out and rookery sites in the PNW include islands off the Oregon and Washington coasts, British Columbia's outer coast, and the Alaska panhandle.

(Sources: NOAA rookery surveys; WDFW haul-out monitoring; PNW pinniped habitat; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Steller Sea Lion

**Bull Roar:**
- **Trigger:** Territorial defense, breeding display, dominance assertion.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (laryngeal). Deep chest resonance amplified by massive body.
- **Character:** Deep, sustained, thunderous roar. Belching quality. Intimidating.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 100-3000 Hz. Low fundamental (~100-300 Hz) with rich harmonics.
- **Duration:** Individual roar 1-5 seconds. Repeated in series. Continuous at rookeries.
- **Amplitude:** E=6. Maximum for SPS. Loudest PNW pinniped. Carries across rookery.

**Colony Sounds:**
- Cow calls: lower-pitched, shorter than bull roar. Mother-pup recognition calls.
- Pup bleats: higher-pitched, similar to Harbor Seal pup but louder.
- Colony cacophony: continuous wall of overlapping vocalizations at breeding rookeries.

**Contrast with Harbor Seal (Degree 180):**
- Harbor Seal: moderate bark, E=4, intermittent, haul-out.
- Steller Sea Lion: deep roar, E=6, sustained, colony. Much louder, much larger animal.

(Sources: Steller sea lion acoustic studies; rookery recordings; comparative pinniped acoustics; engine internal analysis)
