## What Worked

1. **The principle "the colony does not quiet in the storm -- the colony gets louder" is the analytical core.** This is factually accurate (sea lions continue and in some cases increase vocal activity during storm conditions) and thematically powerful (the colony's persistence is the nature of the entry). The storm is not a suppressor; it is a framing device that makes the colony more audible by contrast.
2. **The distinction between additive and emergent acoustic phenomena is analytically valuable.** A single sea lion at E=4-5. The colony at E=8 is not "many sea lions at E=4-5 added up." It is an emergent phenomenon: arrhythmic overlap of hundreds of individuals responding to different social stimuli at different intervals creates a qualitatively different acoustic experience -- the listener is inside a sound field with no single dominant source direction. E=8 through emergence, not addition.
3. **The storm frequency architecture (wave impact 10-100 Hz, wind 50-400 Hz, colony 100-1,000 Hz, rain 2,000-8,000 Hz) is correctly ordered and analytically useful.** The colony's frequency range (100-1,000 Hz) sits between the wind-dominated storm components, not hidden under them. The colony persists acoustically because point-source assertions (individual sea lion barks) overcome distributed-source noise (storm broadband).
4. **The two-species distinction (Steller's vs. California) is maintained.** Steller's: larger, deeper-voiced, ESA threatened. California: smaller, more nasal, expanding post-MMPA. Both present in Washington. The distinction matters for both acoustic and conservation reasons.
5. **The Ballard Locks urban reference point is correctly placed.** California Sea Lions at the Hiram Chittenden Locks (Seattle) are the most accessible sea lion encounter in the engine's urban geography. The urban reference grounds the entry in the engine's primary geography.

## Discoveries

1. **The mist and spray acoustic effect preferentially preserves low-frequency sea lion vocalizations at range.** High-frequency sounds (above 2,000 Hz) attenuate faster in misty/spray conditions than low-frequency sounds. The sea lion colony's primary vocalizations (100-1,000 Hz) are therefore more detectable at range during storm conditions than they would be in clear weather at high frequencies. The storm actively preserves the colony's acoustic signature at distance.
2. **The winter colony density (200-1,000+ animals) is counterintuitively highest when the weather is most adverse.** Winter non-breeding haul-out density exceeds spring/summer breeding season density in many Pacific Northwest rookery sites. The animals pack onto the available rock surface regardless of storm conditions. The rookery is most acoustically dramatic in the weather that seems most likely to empty it.
3. **The distinction between point-source vs. distributed-source is the key acoustic principle here.** A sea lion bark is a point source -- localized, powerful, directional. A storm is distributed -- present everywhere, from all directions, at relatively uniform intensity. Point sources at sufficient amplitude override distributed backgrounds. The sea lion colony is louder than the storm because 200-1,000+ point sources at 100-800 Hz produce more concentrated acoustic energy than a distributed storm at the same frequency range.
