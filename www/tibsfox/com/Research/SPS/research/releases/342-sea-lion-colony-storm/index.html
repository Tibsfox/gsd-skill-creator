The Colony Does Not Quiet — It Gets Louder

A Pacific winter storm arrives at the rookery. Wind drives across the exposed rock (50–400 Hz sustained roar). Waves impact the base (10–100 Hz percussive). Rain distributes across the colony surface (broadband 2,000–8,000 Hz). And 200–1,000+ sea lions continue to vocalize. The colony does not quiet in the storm — it asserts louder. Each animal’s low-frequency bark (100–1,000 Hz) remains audible above the broadband storm noise because the sea lion is a powerful point source and the storm is a distributed background. E=8 through collective assertion against adverse conditions.

The Two Species

Steller’s Sea Lion

Eumetopias jubatus
100–800 Hz — deep resonant bark
Adult male: 1,000+ kg, 3 m. The largest Otariidae. ESA Threatened (Eastern North Pacific). Offshore Olympic Peninsula rocks and islands. The deep-bass component of the colony roar.

California Sea Lion

Zalophus californianus
200–1,000 Hz — nasal arf-arf-arf
Adult male: 200–400 kg. Post-MMPA 1972: population expanding. Year-round Washington coast and Puget Sound. Urban presence at Ballard Locks, Seattle. The mid-frequency component of the colony roar.

The colony’s vocalizations (100–1,000 Hz) sit in the gap between the storm’s sub-bass (wave impact) and the storm’s mid-bass (wind). The storm does not mask the colony — it frames it. The low-frequency barks are powerful point sources (each animal at 10 meters is acoustically overwhelming); the storm is a distributed background. Point source over distributed noise: the colony wins.

The Emergent Colony

A single sea lion barking is E=4–5. The colony at E=8 is not merely many sea lions added together — it is an emergent acoustic phenomenon. When 200–1,000+ animals vocalize simultaneously with arrhythmic overlap (each responding to different social stimuli at different intervals), the resulting acoustic texture is qualitatively different from scaled-up individual barking. The listener is inside a sound field with no clear spatial center, varying amplitude envelope, and constant spectral change as individuals start and stop. The colony is an acoustic organism.

The Exposed Rookery

The exposed coastal rookery — sea stack, rocky island, or isolated headland on the Olympic Peninsula coast — is the specific habitat of degree 342. No lee shore protection. No forest windbreak. The wind drives directly from the northwest or southwest across open ocean. Wave-washed lower surfaces. Rain and spray. The rock that is battered in winter is still occupied. The colony’s presence is an assertion: this is our rock. The storm cannot remove it. The colony persists and roars.
