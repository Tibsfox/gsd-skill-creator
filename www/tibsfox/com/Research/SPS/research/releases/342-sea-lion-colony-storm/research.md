## THE COLONY -- OTARIIDAE ON THE EXPOSED COAST

### Who the Sea Lions Are

The Otariidae family in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Steller's Sea Lion (Eumetopias jubatus).** The Steller's Sea Lion is the largest member of the Otariidae family -- adult males can reach 1,000+ kg (2,200+ lbs) and 3 meters body length. They are found along the Pacific coast from Japan through Alaska to California. Washington State is within the southern range of their regular distribution; they haul out on rocky offshore islands, sea stacks, and exposed headlands. Steller's Sea Lions are listed as threatened (Eastern North Pacific population) under the U.S. Endangered Species Act.
- **California Sea Lion (Zalophus californianus).** California Sea Lions are smaller (adult males 200-400 kg), more familiar from marine parks and media, and range from Mexico north to British Columbia. They are year-round residents on the Washington coast and in Puget Sound, particularly at Neah Bay and Cape Flattery, the Seattle waterfront, and the locks (where they have historically haul-out on boats and structures). California Sea Lions are the most frequently seen and heard sea lion in urban Puget Sound contexts.
- **The rookery context.** A sea lion rookery is a site where sea lions congregate for breeding, pupping, and resting. Exposed coastal rookeries -- sea stacks, rocky islands, and isolated headlands -- are the characteristic habitat type. Protection Island (Jefferson County), Destruction Island (Jefferson County), and the offshore rocks of the Olympic Peninsula coast are primary Washington sea lion haul-out sites. In winter, non-breeding season animals use these sites for resting and social interaction.
- **The winter colony density.** In winter, sea lion colonies on the Washington coast reach their highest densities: breeding is not occurring (pupping is spring), but animals are using the haul-out sites for rest, temperature regulation, and social interaction. A winter haul-out on an exposed rock may contain 200-1,000+ animals of mixed age and sex, densely packed on available surfaces.

## THE COLONY ROAR -- WHAT THE SOUND IS

### Vocalizations of a Sea Lion Colony in Storm Conditions

The acoustic character of degree 342:

- **The individual male bark.** The primary vocalization of male sea lions (both Steller's and California) is a deep, resonant bark -- a wide-bandwidth, low-frequency assertion that functions as a territorial and social signal. A large Steller's Sea Lion male produces a bark at 100-800 Hz with peak energy around 200-400 Hz; the sound carries for considerable distance over water. A California Sea Lion male produces a slightly higher-pitched bark with more nasal quality (200-1,000 Hz). Both barks are acoustically powerful -- a single large male at 10 meters is genuinely overwhelming.
- **The colony chorus.** Multiple males vocalizing simultaneously on a rookery creates one of the most acoustically dense natural soundscapes in the Pacific Northwest. Each individual's bark overlaps with those of its neighbors; the rhythm is not synchronized (each animal is responding to different social stimuli). The result is a dense, overlapping, arrhythmic chorus of deep barks at varying intensities and slight pitch variations. The colony does not have a single tempo -- it has a collective acoustic texture that is always present and always changing.
- **Females and juveniles.** Female sea lions produce higher-pitched calls (400-2,000 Hz) for mother-pup communication and social interaction. Juveniles produce a wider range of calls including bleats, grunts, and higher-pitched contact calls. In winter, the colony mix includes adults of both sexes and juveniles of various ages; the full acoustic range of the Otariidae family is represented.
- **The storm interaction.** Pacific winter storms do not silence a sea lion colony -- the animals continue to vocalize through the wind and rain, and in some cases the colony's acoustic intensity increases during storm conditions as individuals maintain social contact against the storm noise. The wind and precipitation add broadband noise across the frequency spectrum; the sea lion vocalizations (centered in the low-to-mid range, 100-2,000 Hz) remain audible above the storm because they are acoustically powerful point sources, while the storm provides distributed broadband background.

(Sources: Steller's Sea Lion male bark 100-800 Hz peak 200-400 Hz; California Sea Lion male bark 200-1000 Hz more nasal; colony chorus arrhythmic overlapping; individual bark at 10 meters acoustically overwhelming; female vocalizations 400-2000 Hz; juvenile bleats grunts contact calls; colony vocal range full Otariidae family; storm interaction: vocalizations continue and intensify through wind rain; low-to-mid range sea lion calls audible above broadband storm noise)

## THE STORM CONTEXT -- WINTER ON THE EXPOSED COAST

### Pacific Winter Storms and the Rookery

The winter storm environment of degree 342:

- **The exposed rookery.** A sea lion rookery on a sea stack or rocky island off the Olympic Peninsula coast is exposed to the full force of Pacific winter weather. There is no lee shore protection, no forest windbreak. The wind drives directly from the northwest or southwest across the open ocean. The rock is spray-covered, wave-washed on its lower surfaces, and exposed to full precipitation.
- **The storm acoustics on rock.** Wind across exposed rock produces a sustained roar in the 50-400 Hz range, modulated by the rock's surface geometry. Wave impact against the rookery base adds percussive low-frequency events (10-100 Hz) at irregular intervals. Rain on the colony adds broadband high-frequency distributed sound (2,000-8,000 Hz) across the entire colony surface. The storm provides a three-layer acoustic background: sub-bass wave impact, mid-bass wind roar, and high-frequency rain distributed.
- **The spray and mist acoustic effect.** The mist and spray environment reduces acoustic transmission at high frequencies over distance. The colony's low-frequency vocalizations (100-800 Hz) are less affected by the mist than higher-frequency sounds. The storm conditions preferentially preserve the sea lion colony's deep bark frequencies at range -- the low-frequency colony roar carries further through the storm than it would in calm conditions at high frequencies.
- **The winter behavioral context.** Sea lions in winter storm conditions maintain the rookery despite the conditions. This is a key behavioral point: the animals are not driven off by the storm. The colony persists. The vocalizations persist. The rock that is wave-swept and wind-battered in winter is still occupied -- the colony's presence is an assertion of the same kind that the storm is an assertion of. Both are forces claiming the same exposed rock. The colony wins by persistence.

(Sources: Olympic Peninsula sea stack exposed to full Pacific winter weather; no lee shore protection; wind from northwest/southwest; wave-washed lower surfaces; wind on rock 50-400 Hz sustained; wave impact 10-100 Hz percussive; rain broadband 2,000-8,000 Hz; mist spray effect: high-frequency attenuation at range; low-frequency colony bark 100-800 Hz carries further through mist; winter behavioral persistence: colony stays on rock through storm conditions)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST SEA LION CONSERVATION

### The Populations at Stake

- **Steller's Sea Lion ESA status.** The Eastern North Pacific Distinct Population Segment of the Steller's Sea Lion is listed as threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. The population has declined significantly from historical levels. Primary threats include prey availability decline (pollock, cod, and salmon in the North Pacific), entanglement in fishing gear, and interactions with commercial fisheries. NOAA Fisheries manages recovery planning.
- **California Sea Lion population trends.** The California Sea Lion is not currently listed under the ESA. The population has grown substantially since the Marine Mammal Protection Act (1972) prohibited hunting and harassment. California Sea Lions are now the most abundant pinniped on the Washington coast, with populations in Puget Sound that were not historically present.
- **The locks at Ballard (Seattle).** Since the 1980s, California Sea Lions have used the Ballard Locks (Hiram Chittenden Locks, connecting Lake Washington and Puget Sound) as a feeding site, preying on steelhead and salmon returning to freshwater spawning grounds. The interaction between sea lions and returning salmon at the locks is one of the most visible human-wildlife conflicts in Seattle. The Marine Mammal Protection Act's protections apply to the sea lions; the Endangered Species Act applies to the salmon. NOAA has issued permits for lethal removal of individual problem animals in recent years, a contentious management decision.

(Sources: Steller's Sea Lion ESA threatened Eastern North Pacific; pollock cod salmon prey decline; entanglement fishing gear threats; NOAA Fisheries recovery planning; California Sea Lion not ESA listed; MMPA 1972 population growth; California Sea Lions now most abundant WA pinniped; Ballard Locks Hiram Chittenden Locks feeding site; California Sea Lion vs. steelhead salmon at locks; NOAA lethal removal permits contentious)
