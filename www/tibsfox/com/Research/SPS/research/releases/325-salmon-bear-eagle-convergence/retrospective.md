## Discoveries

1. **The four-component acoustic stack at the spawning creek has a vertical frequency organization analogous to the Hoh winter storm's four-layer structure.** Creek and bear: 100-2000 Hz, the lower register. Salmon: 100-2000 Hz, irregular events in the same register as creek. Eagle: 2000-4000 Hz, the high-frequency counterpoint above. The convergence has an implicit frequency stratification: low-frequency ecological mass (creek, bear, spawning fish) vs. high-frequency biological signaling (eagle calls). This vertical organization is not as complete as the Hoh four layers but it is structurally analogous.
2. **The grizzly bear's jaw-pop threat signal within the convergence is acoustically significant beyond its biological function.** Multiple bears competing for fishing positions at a productive reach use jaw-pops (100-400 Hz, sharp percussive signals) as contact threat signals to each other. Within the dense acoustic environment of the convergence, these sharp low-frequency percussive signals stand out clearly against the continuous broadband creek and salmon background. They are the convergence's most acoustically distinctive discrete events -- not the loudest (the bear's strike lunge is louder) but the most identifiable.
3. **Hyperphagia timing is the key variable for convergence intensity.** The most intense convergences occur in September-October when bears are in hyperphagia (consuming up to 20,000 cal/day) AND the coho and chinook runs are at peak. The double peak -- maximum bear urgency and maximum fish density -- creates the E=8 convergence. Outside hyperphagia, bears at salmon streams are less concentrated and less behaviorally urgent, reducing the acoustic intensity.
