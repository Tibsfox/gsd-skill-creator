## THE CONVERGENCE MECHANISM -- WHY ALL THREE MEET

### The Salmon Run as Ecological Catalyst

The simultaneous presence of all three species at the spawning creek is not accidental -- it is driven by the salmon run's extraordinary energy subsidy to the surrounding ecosystem:

- **The marine-to-terrestrial transfer.** Pacific salmon spend 2-7 years at sea, accumulating oceanic biomass (marine-derived lipids and proteins). When they return to spawn, they carry this oceanic energy into the freshwater ecosystem. A single chinook salmon can weigh 15-30 kg -- a significant energy package. A productive spawning creek running at maximum load (thousands of fish per day) delivers enormous quantities of marine-derived energy into the terrestrial ecosystem through the salmon's bodies. Bears and eagles access this energy transfer directly.
- **The olfactory signal.** The spawning salmon release chemical signals -- pheromones and decomposition compounds from already-spawned fish -- that are detectable at significant distances. Grizzly bears have the most acute sense of smell of any North American mammal (estimated 2,100 times more sensitive than the human nose). A spawning creek at maximum run can attract bears from distances of 20-30 km. The bear arrives at the creek because the olfactory signal is overwhelming.
- **The visual signal.** Bald eagles locate salmon concentrations visually -- their eyesight is 4-8 times sharper than human vision, capable of detecting fish movement below the water surface from heights of 15-60 meters. A productive spawning reach, visible from above as a dense concentration of large fish in shallow, clear water, is visible to eagles at significant distances. A single productive creek can concentrate 10-30+ eagles during the peak run.
- **The competitive dynamics.** Bears and eagles compete for access to the same fish, but the competition is indirect: bears wade into the creek and take live or recently spawned fish directly; eagles take fish from the shallows or from the bears' kills (kleptoparasitism -- stealing food from another species). A bear that catches a large salmon and moves it to the bank to eat may find eagles attempting to take pieces of the carcass. The acoustic record includes both the bear's feeding sounds and the eagles' vocal competition for access.

(Sources: Pacific salmon marine-to-terrestrial energy transfer; chinook 15-30 kg; spawning pheromone chemical signals; grizzly olfactory sensitivity; 20-30 km attraction range; bald eagle visual acuity 4-8x human; eagle fish detection from 15-60m height; 10-30+ eagles at productive creek; eagle kleptoparasitism from bears; competitive dynamics acoustic record)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8 THROUGH SYNTHESIS

### Four Simultaneous Acoustic Components

The E=8 calibration at degree 325 results from four simultaneous acoustic components:

**Component 1: Creek baseline**
The spawning creek at fall flow levels: a medium-small watercourse over gravel and cobble, running at 2-4 m/s. Continuous broadband sound (100-2,000 Hz, dominant energy 200-600 Hz) at 60-70 dB at 10 meters. This is the acoustic substrate -- the constant foundation that would be present regardless of the other species. The creek baseline alone is E=3-4.

**Component 2: Salmon acoustic events**
The spawning salmon add a layer of irregular acoustic events to the creek baseline: (a) salmon leap -- a large chinook clearing the water in a rapid (500-2,000 Hz impact, 70-80 dB brief event, 0.5-1 second duration); (b) spawning thrash -- the lateral thrash of a spawning female cutting a redd in the gravel (100-500 Hz, sustained 3-10 seconds, 65-75 dB); (c) dense fish in shallow water -- the collective disturbance of many fish in a confined shallow reach (broadband 100-2,000 Hz, continuous at elevated levels when fish density is high). Salmon layer adds E=2-3 units above baseline.

**Component 3: Bear acoustic events**
The bear's presence is the highest-amplitude single-source event in the convergence: (a) approach through riparian brush (footfall, vegetation disturbance: 100-500 Hz, brief sequences); (b) entry into the creek -- a 300-400 kg bear entering shallow water generates a distinctive churning turbulence (100-1,000 Hz, 80-90 dB, 1-3 seconds); (c) strike -- the bear lunging for a fish is a rapid displacement event (100-2,000 Hz, 85-95 dB, 0.5-1 second); (d) feeding on land -- if the bear moves a fish to the bank, the feeding sounds (bone crunch, wet impact) add brief percussive events; (e) vocalization -- at a productive creek with multiple bears competing for positions, jaw-pops (contact threat signals, 100-400 Hz, sharp) and occasional brief vocalizations are part of the acoustic record. Bear component raises the event to E=7+ alone.

**Component 4: Eagle acoustic events**
Eagles are the highest-frequency component: (a) territorial kee-kee-kee calls (2,000-4,000 Hz, 70-80 dB at 30 meters) -- continuous or near-continuous when multiple eagles are present and competing for access; (b) wing aerodynamics during low passes over the water (100-500 Hz, brief); (c) distress/alarm calls when a bear approaches an eagle's feeding position (1,000-3,000 Hz, brief high-amplitude events). Eagle component adds the high-frequency counterpoint that makes the convergence a complete acoustic event across the full spectrum.

**The synthesis calibration.**
All four components simultaneously at fall peak: creek baseline (60-70 dB continuous, 100-2,000 Hz) + salmon events (65-80 dB irregular, 100-2,000 Hz) + bear events (80-95 dB peak, 100-2,000 Hz, plus jaw-pop 100-400 Hz sharp) + eagle calls (70-80 dB continuous, 2,000-4,000 Hz). The combined acoustic environment: 85-95 dB at the center of activity; frequency range 100-4,000 Hz; temporal character complex (continuous creek + irregular salmon + episodic bear + continuous eagle call). E=8 through ecological synthesis -- three individually significant species creating a combined acoustic event greater than any individual component.

## THE SPAWNING CREEK SETTING

### The Specific Riparian Corridor

The spawning creek setting is specific and distinct from the larger salmon-run river (SPS 307 -- full river):

- **Scale.** The salmon convergence is most intense at small to medium spawning creeks -- watercourses shallow enough for bears to wade easily and for eagles to spot fish from above, with sufficient salmon density to maintain multiple predators simultaneously. The full Hoh or Quinault River main channels are too large for the characteristic bear-eagle convergence. The productive zone is the tributary creeks and the lower reaches of side channels: widths of 5-20 meters, depths of 0.3-0.8 meters at the productive spawning reaches.
- **Pacific Northwest interior distribution.** The convergence occurs in creek systems across the Pacific Northwest wherever salmon run in significant numbers: the Olympic Peninsula creek systems, the Washington Cascades western slope tributaries, the Olympic National Park rivers, the North Cascades creek systems, and extending into the interior. The engine's convergence event is not location-specific; it is a feature of the fall salmon run across the Pacific Northwest wherever bears and eagles have access to productive spawning reaches.
- **Fall timing.** The fall salmon run peaks from September through November, depending on species and watershed. Chinook and coho arrive in fall. The bear-eagle-salmon convergence peaks with the salmon run's physical peak -- when fish density in the spawning reaches is highest and the energy subsidy is at maximum. This is also the period when bears are in hyperphagia (pre-hibernation feeding frenzy), maximizing caloric intake before denning. Hyperphagia drives bears to salmon streams with unusual urgency: a hyperphagia grizzly may consume 20,000 calories per day, and a productive salmon stream is one of the few food sources that can support this caloric demand.
- **The riparian corridor acoustic setting.** The spawning creek is embedded in a riparian corridor: the narrow band of vegetation along the watercourse. The riparian vegetation (red alder, bigleaf maple, willow, cottonwood, riparian shrubs) creates an acoustic enclosure -- the watercourse is acoustically enclosed by vegetation on both sides, which focuses the sounds of the creek and the convergence into a relatively confined acoustic space. The riparian corridor also provides the structural elements that eagles perch in (large trees with clear sight lines to the water) and that bears move through on approach.

(Sources: Spawning creek scale 5-20 meters width; 0.3-0.8 meters depth; tributary and side channel distribution; Olympic Peninsula creek systems; Washington Cascades; bear hyperphagia 20,000 calories/day; hyperphagia timing September-November; chinook and coho fall run; riparian corridor red alder bigleaf maple; acoustic enclosure; eagle perch structures)

## THE KEYSTONE SPECIES CONCEPT

### Three Keystone Species Simultaneously

The ecological concept of "keystone species" -- a species whose disproportionate impact on the ecosystem far exceeds its biomass -- applies to all three participants in the convergence:

- **Pacific salmon as keystone.** The salmon's marine-to-terrestrial energy transfer subsidizes the entire ecosystem: bears feed on salmon and fertilize the forest interior with partially consumed carcasses; eagles scatter salmon pieces across the riparian zone; the uneaten salmon carcasses decompose in the creek and on the banks, providing marine-derived nitrogen that fertilizes the riparian vegetation. Studies in Pacific Northwest forests have detected marine-derived nitrogen from salmon in trees growing up to 500 meters from spawning streams. The salmon is the mechanism that moves oceanic energy into the terrestrial interior.
- **Grizzly bear as keystone.** The grizzly bear is one of the most important ecosystem engineers in Pacific Northwest ecology: its foraging behavior (turning over logs, digging for roots, moving large woody debris) modifies habitat structure across large areas. Its position as a large predator and scavenger regulates prey populations and distributes carcass energy. The grizzly's salmon-harvesting behavior specifically distributes marine-derived nutrients into the forest interior -- a bear carrying a salmon 50-100 meters from the creek to eat in cover is directly fertilizing that section of forest.
- **Bald eagle as keystone.** The bald eagle's role as an apex avian predator regulates fish and waterfowl populations. At the salmon creek, the eagle's primary function is as a scavenger and kleptoparasite -- accessing marine energy that has been made available by the salmon run and, in some cases, by the bears' harvesting. The eagles also distribute partially consumed salmon across the landscape through their movements, spreading marine-derived nutrients farther than either salmon or bears alone would reach.
- **The simultaneous presence.** No other natural setting in the Pacific Northwest brings three keystone species together at the same time with the same direct ecological interaction. The salmon-bear-eagle convergence is the engine's most ecologically complex multi-species event.
