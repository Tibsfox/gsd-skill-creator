The Marine-to-Terrestrial Transfer

The salmon run is the ecological mechanism that makes the convergence possible. Pacific salmon spend 2–7 years at sea, accumulating oceanic biomass. When they return to spawn, each fish is a concentrated package of marine-derived energy — a large chinook can weigh 15–30 kg. A productive spawning creek at peak run delivers thousands of these packages into the freshwater ecosystem daily. This marine-to-terrestrial energy subsidy is detectable in the forest: marine-derived nitrogen from salmon has been found in trees growing 500 meters from spawning streams. The bear-eagle convergence is the direct acoustic record of this energy transfer in process.

The Convergence Mechanism

Grizzly bears in hyperphagia (pre-hibernation feeding frenzy, consuming up to 20,000 calories/day) are drawn to salmon creeks by olfactory signal detectable at 20–30 km. Bald eagles locate salmon concentrations visually from 15–60 m height — their vision is 4–8 times sharper than human vision. Up to 30+ eagles can be present at a single productive reach simultaneously. The bear-eagle competitive dynamic: bears hunt live fish; eagles take fish from shallows and kleptoparasitize the bears’ kills. When a bear moves a large salmon to the bank to feed, the eagles try to take pieces. The acoustic record includes both.

E=8 Through Ecological Synthesis

This is the third distinct mechanism for E=8 in the post-summit SPS sequence. The Hoh winter storm (degree 324) was E=8 through acoustic completeness (all four physical layers). The salmon-bear-eagle convergence is E=8 through ecological synthesis: three individually documented species (each at E=5–8 alone) creating a combined acoustic event larger than any of them alone. Creek (E=3–4) + salmon (E=5–6) + bear (E=7–8 alone) + eagle (E=5 alone) = combined E=8 through synthesis. The whole is greater than the sum of the species.
