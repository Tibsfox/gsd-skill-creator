## TAHOMA -- THE MOUNTAIN

### Mt. Rainier: Geology and Standing

Mt. Rainier is the dominant physical feature of the western Washington landscape:

- **Name and geography.** Known to the Indigenous peoples of the region by many names, most prominently Tahoma (Lushootseed/Coast Salish: "the mother of all waters" or "big mountain"). The name Rainier was applied by the British explorer George Vancouver in 1792, who named it after his friend Rear Admiral Peter Rainier. The mountain stands at 14,411 feet (4,392 meters) above sea level, making it the highest peak in Washington State and the Cascade Range. It is visible from Seattle, Tacoma, Olympia, and much of the Puget Sound lowlands.
- **Stratovolcano.** Mt. Rainier is a stratovolcano -- a large conical volcano built by the accumulation of lava flows and pyroclastic deposits over time. Stratovolcanoes are characteristically steep and tall; they tend to produce explosive eruptions when they become active because viscous magma traps gas. The Cascade Range contains multiple stratovolcanoes: Rainier, St. Helens (which erupted catastrophically in 1980), Adams, Baker, Hood (in Oregon), Shasta (in California). Rainier is the largest.
- **The ice.** Mt. Rainier has the most glaciated terrain of any peak in the contiguous United States outside of Alaska: approximately 26 named glaciers covering about 35 square miles. This ice is one of the defining characteristics of the mountain and one of the primary sources of volcanic hazard. When Rainier erupts, or even when hydrothermal activity increases substantially, the ice melts rapidly and produces lahars (volcanic mudflows).
- **Active status.** Mt. Rainier is classified as active by the USGS. It has not erupted significantly in historical time (the last minor eruptive activity was approximately 150 years ago), but it remains volcanically active: it has a persistent hydrothermal system, produces volcanic tremor, and poses significant hazard.

## VOLCANIC TREMOR -- THE SOUND

### What the Mountain Produces Below the Surface

Volcanic tremor is a specific category of seismic signal distinct from earthquake shaking:

- **Tremor vs. earthquake.** An earthquake is a discrete seismic event: a rupture in rock that produces a pulse of seismic energy with a defined start and a coda that decays over seconds to minutes. Volcanic tremor is different: it is a continuous or episodic signal that persists for minutes, hours, or days. The tremor is produced by the movement of fluids (magma, hydrothermal water, volcanic gases) through cracks and conduits within the volcanic edifice. As fluid moves through these conduits, it produces a vibration analogous to the sound produced when air moves through a pipe -- a resonant, sustained signal.
- **Frequency characteristics.** Volcanic tremor at Mt. Rainier is typically in the very low frequency range -- below 1 Hz to perhaps 5-10 Hz at most. This is infrasonic (below the 20 Hz lower threshold of human hearing). The tremor is detected by seismometers and infrasonic microphones (pressure sensors designed to detect sound below 20 Hz) rather than conventional microphones or human ears. The signal is real; it is just at frequencies our sensory systems do not process.
- **Harmonic tremor.** A distinctive form of volcanic tremor in which multiple frequencies appear simultaneously, often in harmonic relationships (like an organ pipe producing a fundamental frequency and overtones). Harmonic tremor is associated with fluid movement through volcanic conduits and is considered a signal of increased magmatic activity. Mt. Rainier produces harmonic tremor episodes that are monitored by the Pacific Northwest Seismic Network.
- **What triggers tremor at Rainier.** Rainier's tremor is primarily associated with its hydrothermal system -- a complex network of hot water and steam conduits beneath the summit ice and within the volcanic edifice. Hot springs and fumaroles (steam vents) at the summit indicate active hydrothermal circulation. Changes in the hydrothermal system produce changes in tremor character. Additionally, Rainier's tremor is sensitive to regional stress changes -- large earthquakes elsewhere in the Pacific Northwest can trigger tremor episodes.
- **The infrasonic component.** Infrasound (sound below 20 Hz) propagates differently from audible sound: it travels farther with less attenuation, passes through most barriers with minimal loss of energy, and is detected by pressure sensors rather than microphones. A volcanic tremor event at Rainier can produce infrasound detectable at stations tens to hundreds of kilometers away. The mountain communicates at frequencies we cannot hear.

(Sources: Volcanic tremor definition; tremor vs. earthquake: discrete event vs. sustained signal; fluid movement through conduits as mechanism; frequency range below 1-10 Hz; infrasonic threshold 20 Hz; seismometer and infrasonic microphone detection; harmonic tremor definition; harmonic relationships as organ pipe analogy; Pacific Northwest Seismic Network; Rainier hydrothermal system; hot springs and fumaroles at summit; tremor sensitivity to regional stress changes; infrasound propagation characteristics)

## THE HYDROTHERMAL SYSTEM

### The Mountain's Internal Activity

Mt. Rainier's hydrothermal system is the source of most of its volcanic tremor:

- **The summit crater.** Mt. Rainier has two summit craters: Liberty Cap and the main summit crater (Columbia Crest). Within these craters, fumaroles (steam vents) emit volcanic gases and steam continuously. The temperature of the fumarole emissions indicates the level of hydrothermal activity; increased temperature suggests increased heat flux from below.
- **The cave network.** Beneath the summit ice, volcanic heat has melted an extensive network of ice caves. These caves -- some large enough for humans to stand in -- are the result of sustained thermal emission from the volcanic system. The ice above melts from below; the cave network is the physical record of the hydrothermal activity. This network is a direct interface between the hydrothermal system and the glacial system. When hydrothermal activity increases, the caves expand; when it decreases, they contract.
- **Hydrothermal alteration.** Hydrothermal fluids circulating through the volcanic rock have chemically altered large portions of Rainier's edifice. The altered rock is weaker than unaltered rock -- hydrothermally altered material is one of the primary factors in Rainier's instability. Large portions of the Rainier edifice are made of weakened, altered material that can fail in a collapse even without a major eruption. This is the process that produced the Osceola Mudflow (see SPS 262 for lahar context).
- **The monitoring network.** The USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory (CVO) and the Pacific Northwest Seismic Network maintain a network of seismometers, GPS stations, gas sensors, and tiltmeters on and around Mt. Rainier. The monitoring network is designed to detect early indications of increased volcanic activity. Changes in tremor frequency, amplitude, or duration are among the key indicators that CVO monitors.

(Sources: Summit craters: Liberty Cap, Columbia Crest; fumaroles as continuous steam vents; fumarole temperature as activity indicator; ice cave network at summit; cave expansion/contraction with hydrothermal activity; hydrothermal alteration of volcanic rock; alteration and edifice weakening; Osceola Mudflow connection; USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory; Pacific Northwest Seismic Network monitoring network; seismometers, GPS, gas sensors, tiltmeters)

## THE MOST DANGEROUS VOLCANO IN THE UNITED STATES

### USGS Volcanic Threat Assessment

Mt. Rainier's designation as the most dangerous volcano in the United States (by some assessments) is based on specific hazard characteristics:

- **The USGS Volcanic Threat Assessment.** The USGS periodically assesses the threat level of US volcanoes based on two factors: volcanic hazard (the potential severity of future eruptive activity) and exposure (the number of people, infrastructure, and economic activity within the hazard zone). Mt. Rainier scores high on both factors. Its volcanic hazard is significant (it can produce large lahars, pyroclastic flows, and lava flows); its exposure is enormous (the Puget Sound lowlands, with several million residents, are within lahar pathways).
- **Population exposure.** The Puyallup River valley, the White River valley, and the Carbon River valley are all lahar pathways from Mt. Rainier. These valleys contain the cities of Orting, Puyallup, Sumner, Auburn, and Kent -- collectively home to hundreds of thousands of people. The Port of Tacoma and portions of the industrial infrastructure of the Puget Sound region are within lahar pathways. Seattle-Tacoma International Airport is near the White River lahar pathway.
- **Warning time.** Lahars from Mt. Rainier can travel 50-100 miles in a few hours. Depending on the origin point on the mountain and the lahar's volume, warning times for communities like Orting (which is entirely within a lahar inundation zone) may be as little as 30-45 minutes. This is not enough time for most residents to evacuate by vehicle given modern traffic patterns.
- **The Mt. St. Helens comparison.** The 1980 eruption of Mt. St. Helens killed 57 people and destroyed significant infrastructure. Mt. St. Helens' lahar pathways ran primarily through sparsely populated areas of the Cowlitz River valley. Mt. Rainier's lahar pathways run through the Puget Sound urban corridor, home to orders of magnitude more people. A Rainier eruption comparable in volume to the 1980 St. Helens eruption would have far greater human consequences.

(Sources: USGS Volcanic Threat Assessment; volcanic hazard and exposure as two factors; Puyallup River valley, White River valley, Carbon River valley as lahar pathways; Orting, Puyallup, Sumner, Auburn, Kent in lahar zones; Port of Tacoma; Seattle-Tacoma International Airport; lahar travel time 50-100 miles in hours; Orting 30-45 minute warning time; Mt. St. Helens 1980 comparison; 57 deaths; Cowlitz River valley sparsely populated; Rainier lahar pathways through urban corridor)

### What Comes Below the Storm Arc

The transition from Storm Arc to Geophysics Arc is an architectural move in the engine:

- **The Storm Arc (256-260) documented surface events.** The storm system traversed the PNW landscape from coast to forest. The sounds were in the audible range (or barely above it). ## LEGACY -- THE MOUNTAIN IN THE ENGINE

### Tahoma's Place in the Sound of Puget Sound

- **Rainier as the defining landform.** Mt. Rainier defines the visual and physical character of the Puget Sound region in a way that no other single feature does. The mountain is visible from most of the Seattle metropolitan area on clear days. It appears at the end of streets, above buildings, on the horizon in any direction that is clear. It is present in the ambient visual field in a way that no other feature matches.
- **The sound of the mountain.** The engine's documentation of Rainier's volcanic tremor gives the mountain an acoustic presence in the Sound of Puget Sound. The mountain has a sound; we cannot hear it without instruments; it is real. Degree 261 documents this acoustic reality.
- **The lahar threat.** The monitoring of Rainier's volcanic tremor is inseparable from the monitoring of the lahar threat. The systems that detect the mountain's normal acoustic activity (seismometers, infrasonic arrays) are also the systems that will provide the first warning of increased activity. The background tremor and the warning system are the same system.

(Sources: Rainier as defining visual landform of Puget Sound; visibility from Seattle metropolitan area; acoustic presence of volcanic tremor; lahar threat and monitoring as inseparable; seismometers as dual-function: normal monitoring and early warning)
