*Degree 169 retrospective complete. DUNLIN FLOCK at the one hundred sixty-ninth degree -- THE COLLECTIVE SIGH. On the tidal flats of Puget Sound in winter -- Padilla Bay, Skagit Bay, Nisqually -- the Dunlin are everywhere. Thousands of small gray-brown sandpipers probing the mud with slightly decurved bills, feeding on worms and amphipods, always moving, always probing. They are the most abundant wintering shorebird on the Pacific Coast. And when the tide rises, or when a Peregrine Falcon stoops from above, the thousands take to the air together. What follows is one of nature's most spectacular phenomena: the murmuration. Thousands of Dunlin -- five thousand, ten thousand, fifty thousand -- wheeling in coordinated flight. Each bird responds to the movements of its nearest six or seven neighbors. There is no leader. No choreographer. The coordination emerges from simple local rules applied simultaneously by every individual, and the result is a fluid, organic, pulsating shape that moves as one organism. Ribbons, spheres, pancakes, tornadoes, waterfalls -- the shapes shift every fraction of a second, unpredictable and mesmerizing. The turn waves propagate through the flock at three times individual flight speed. And at close range, the sound: a rushing, sighing, whooshing collective wind-sound -- the aggregate of thousands of small wings beating and turning simultaneously. Not a call. Not a song. The sound of air being displaced by the collective body. The voice of the flock itself. E=3 -- gentle, atmospheric, proximity-dependent. The visual dominates, but the sound accompanies it: the sighing of the swarm. v1.49.309.*
