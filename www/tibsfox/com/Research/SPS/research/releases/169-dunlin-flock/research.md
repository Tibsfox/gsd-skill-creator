### Dunlin in Context

- **Most abundant:** The Dunlin is the most abundant wintering shorebird on the Pacific Coast. Flocks of thousands -- sometimes tens of thousands -- form on tidal flats from fall through spring. The sheer numbers are the essential context.
- **Murmuration:** Dunlin flocks perform spectacular aerial displays -- murmurations -- that rival starling murmurations in complexity and beauty. Thousands of birds wheel, turn, expand, contract, and pulse as a single coordinated organism.
- **Collective sound:** The research focus is not the individual Dunlin's call but the collective sound of the flock in murmuration. Thousands of small wings turning simultaneously create a rushing, sighing sound -- a collective wind-sound that is the voice of the flock itself.

### Why E=3

The Dunlin flock collective wind-sound registers at E=3:

- **Volume:** The collective wing-sound is moderate. Each individual Dunlin is small (approximately 20 cm, 50-65g). The wing noise of a single bird is barely audible. But multiplied by thousands, the aggregate becomes a perceptible rushing sound. The sound is atmospheric rather than loud.
- **Ethereal quality:** The collective wing-sound has a quality similar to a sigh or a rushing breath. It is not a sharp, definable call but an ambient, enveloping sound. The quality is gentle, ethereal, and immersive -- a SIGHING rather than a calling.
- **Visual dominance:** In a Dunlin murmuration, the visual spectacle dominates the acoustic experience. The sound accompanies the visual but does not overpower it. E=3 reflects a sound that is present, atmospheric, and beautiful, but secondary to the visual phenomenon.
- **Context dependence:** The collective wing-sound is audible primarily at close range to the murmuration. From a distance, the visual persists but the sound fades. The sound requires proximity.

## THE MURMURATION PHENOMENON

### Thousands Turning as One

The Dunlin murmuration is one of the most spectacular natural phenomena in the PNW:

- **Scale:** Winter Dunlin flocks on Puget Sound tidal flats can number 5,000 to 50,000+ individuals. The flocks form on mudflats during low tide and take to the air as a coordinated mass.
- **Coordination:** Each bird responds to the movements of its nearest 6-7 neighbors. There is no leader. The coordination emerges from simple local rules applied simultaneously by thousands of individuals. The result is a fluid, organic, pulsating shape that moves as one.
- **Shape-shifting:** The flock expands, contracts, splits, merges, elongates, compresses, twists, and turns. The shapes are unpredictable and mesmerizing -- ribbons, spheres, pancakes, waterfalls, tornadoes. The shapes shift every fraction of a second.
- **Speed:** Dunlin in murmuration fly at 40-60 km/h. The turns propagate through the flock as a wave, moving faster than any individual bird -- the turn wave travels at approximately 3x individual flight speed. This creates the illusion of a single organism.
- **Predator response:** Murmurations are often triggered by the presence of a Peregrine Falcon or Merlin. The murmuration is a predator-defense behavior -- the predator cannot target an individual when thousands are moving unpredictably. The falcon's attack triggers the most dramatic displays.
- **The sound:** At close range, a large Dunlin murmuration creates an audible collective wing-sound -- a rushing, sighing, whooshing sound produced by thousands of small wings beating and turning simultaneously. The sound rises and falls with the flock's movements. It is the sound of air being displaced by the collective body.

(Sources: Ballerini et al. 2008; Cavagna et al. 2010; Cornell Lab; field observations; engine internal analysis)

## TIDAL FLAT HABITAT

### The Mudflat as Stage

The tidal flat is the essential context:

- **Puget Sound tidal flats:** Extensive mudflats at Padilla Bay, Skagit Bay, Nisqually NWR, and other estuarine areas provide winter habitat for massive Dunlin flocks. Low tide exposes the mudflat foraging grounds; high tide concentrates the birds at roost sites.
- **Feeding ecology:** Dunlin probe mudflats with their slightly decurved bills, feeding on polychaete worms, amphipods, and other invertebrates. The feeding is constant -- the birds are always probing, always moving across the mud in loose aggregations.
- **Tidal cycle:** The murmuration is linked to the tidal cycle. Rising tides push the feeding flock off the mudflats. The concentrated birds take flight and perform murmurations. Falling tides draw them back to feed. The murmuration is the transition between states.
- **Winter timing:** Dunlin are present in the PNW from October through April. The murmurations are a winter phenomenon -- part of the cold-season soundscape and visual landscape of Puget Sound.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Puget Sound habitat data; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Flock

The Dunlin flock's acoustic profile centers on the collective phenomenon:

**Collective Wing-Sound (murmuration):**
- **Trigger:** Predator response, tidal displacement, flock movement.
- **Mechanism:** Aggregate wing movement of thousands of birds. Not vocal. The sound is produced by air displaced by collective wingbeats and turns.
- **Character:** A rushing, sighing, whooshing sound. Atmospheric and enveloping. Rises and falls with flock movements. Sounds like wind or a collective breath.
- **Duration:** Murmurations last 1-15+ minutes. The sound is continuous during flight.
- **Amplitude:** E=3. Moderate, atmospheric, proximity-dependent.

**Individual Dunlin Calls:**
- **Flight call:** A nasal, raspy "kreee" or "treeee" -- a diagnostic call given in flight. Thin, reedy, slightly buzzy.
- **Flock chatter:** When thousands call simultaneously, the aggregate creates a continuous chattering backdrop.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
