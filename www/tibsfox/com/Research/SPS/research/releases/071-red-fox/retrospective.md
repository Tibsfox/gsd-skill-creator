## Structural Event 1: Canidae (31st Family) -- First Non-Musteloid Carnivore

- **Situation:** After nine consecutive musteloid carnivore entries (Mustelidae 63-68, Mephitidae 69, Procyonidae 70), degree 71 brings Canidae -- the 31st family and the FIRST non-musteloid carnivore. Canidae is in the suborder Caniformia but NOT in the superfamily Musteloidea.
- **Root Cause:** The CSV places a canid species at degree 71. The musteloid monopoly was a consequence of the CSV's musteloid concentration in the mammalian era (degrees 63-70). The Red Fox breaks this pattern by belonging to a different superfamily entirely.

- **Carry Item:** First non-musteloid carnivore: Red Fox (Canidae, degree 71). The musteloid streak (63-70) is ENDED. Four Carnivora families now represented.

## Structural Event 3: Open Country -- First Open-Habitat Mammal

- **Situation:** The Red Fox is the first truly open-country species in the mammalian era. Every previous mammal was associated with cover: water, forest, edge, or urban structures. The fox needs open space -- grasslands, meadows, agricultural fields.
- **Root Cause:** The Red Fox hunts by eye and ear across open terrain. The mousing pounce requires visibility. The fox's lean, long-legged body plan is built for trotting across open ground. The habitat matches the morphology.
- **Recommendation:** Open-country habitat opens a new category in the mammalian era. The engine's mammalian habitat portfolio now spans from old-growth canopy (Marten, 65) to urban core (Raccoon, 70) to open grassland (Red Fox, 71). Near-complete habitat coverage.
- **Carry Item:** Open-country mammals: 1 (Red Fox, degree 71). The first. Habitat coverage now includes: wetland/river, creek bank, old-growth canopy, dense forest, meadow-forest edge, open woodland, woodland edge, riparian/urban, open country.

## Structural Event 4: PNW Native Fox Debate

- **Situation:** Red Foxes in western Washington are a mix: native Cascade Red Fox (*V. v. cascadensis*, mountain subspecies, conservation concern) and introduced eastern Red Fox (lowland, brought for fur farming). The native mountain fox is rare and possibly threatened. The lowland fox is introduced and common.
- **Root Cause:** 19th-century fur farming introduced eastern Red Foxes to the Pacific Northwest. These foxes established feral lowland populations. The native Cascade Red Fox was always a mountain species -- it did not naturally occupy lowland habitat.
- **Recommendation:** Document the native/introduced complexity. The Red Fox is simultaneously common (introduced lowland population) and potentially threatened (native mountain subspecies). This is the engine's most complex conservation narrative -- neither "common and fine" nor "rare and declining" but both.
- **Carry Item:** Native/introduced complexity: Red Fox (71). Cascade Red Fox (*V. v. cascadensis*) is a conservation concern. Introduced lowland Red Fox is common. One species, two conservation stories.
