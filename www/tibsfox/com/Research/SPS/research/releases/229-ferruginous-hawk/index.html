LARGEST NORTH AMERICAN BUTEO — Buteo regalis

The Ferruginous Hawk is the largest member of the genus Buteo in North America — the regal buteo, named explicitly for its size. Wingspan up to 152 cm (60 inches). At degree 229, it follows the FIRST BUTEO (Red-tailed Hawk, 228) with the LARGEST BUTEO. The sequence documents the genus at two extremes: most widespread (Red-tail) and most massive (Ferruginous).

The Alarm Call — Rough, Descending kree-ah

The Ferruginous Hawk's alarm call is louder and rougher in texture than the Red-tailed Hawk's cleaner scream. A harsh, descending kree-ah or kaah, repeated in rapid succession when the nest is threatened. Breeding Ferruginous Hawks are notably aggressive defenders — they will dive-bomb humans approaching within several hundred meters of the nest.

Acoustic Profile — Alarm Call

Frequency: 1,000–3,500 Hz
Character: loud, rough, raspy, descending
Context: nest defense, territorial intrusion

Lower and more powerful than the Bald Eagle contact call. Shorter and rougher than the Red-tailed Hawk scream. Optimized for open-terrain carrying power.

"The regal hawk. Named for its size. The buteo sequence runs: Red-tail (most widespread), Ferruginous (most massive), Rough-leg (Arctic visitor). Three buteos, three ecological niches."

Open Country Ecology

The Ferruginous Hawk is the premier open-country raptor of western North America. Unlike the forest accipiters (225-227) or the water-edge eagles (221-223), the Ferruginous Hawk requires large, unbroken grassland and shrub-steppe. It hunts by soaring over open terrain, hovering into the wind at large scale (kestrel technique applied to a hawk of eagle size), and — uniquely among large North American raptors — walking on the ground in pursuit of prey.

The prey base is ground mammals: ground squirrels, prairie dogs, jackrabbits, pocket gophers. The Ferruginous Hawk is a keystone predator of the grassland food web, its population health directly tracking the abundance of burrowing mammals. Where prairie dog colonies decline, Ferruginous Hawk productivity follows.

Pacific Northwest Distribution

The PNW distribution is heavily eastern — the Columbia Basin's shrub-steppe (sagebrush, bunchgrass) in central Washington is core habitat. Eastern Oregon's high desert (Malheur County, Harney County) holds additional populations. In winter, some birds move west of the Cascades into valley lowlands — Willamette Valley agricultural fields, Puget lowlands, Skagit flats — using open-country analogs to their grassland breeding habitat.

The Ferruginous Field Mark

Light-morph adults (approximately 90% of population) show distinctive rusty-red upper parts and a V-shaped pattern of rust-colored leg feathers against the white belly — visible in flight from below as the bird soars. The species epithet regalis (regal) acknowledges what the bird looks like in light: a large hawk with rich ferruginous coloration and an eagle-like profile.

Conservation

SPECIES OF CONSERVATION CONCERN. US population: ~4,000-5,000 breeding pairs. Washington State: Species of Conservation Concern. Canada: Threatened (SARA). Primary threat: prairie dog colony decline through poisoning and plague, plus grassland conversion to cropland. IUCN: Least Concern globally, but North American trend is negative.
