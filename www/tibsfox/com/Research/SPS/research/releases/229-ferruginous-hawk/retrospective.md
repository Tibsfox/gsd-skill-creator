## What Worked

1. **Largest buteo is the correct milestone to lead with.** The engine introduced the FIRST BUTEO at degree 228 (Red-tailed Hawk). Following with the LARGEST BUTEO at 229 is structurally sound -- the sequence documents the genus by ecological role and by size. Two degrees, two endpoints of the Buteo range.
2. **The species epithet regalis is load-bearing.** The name "regal" is earned -- this hawk is large enough to be mistaken for an eagle in the field. The etymology anchors the species' place in the buteo sequence correctly.
3. **Prairie dog connection is a keystone finding.** The direct population-level coupling between prairie dog colony abundance and Ferruginous Hawk productivity is one of the engine's clearest predator-prey dependency examples. This is ecologically important and practically relevant to conservation.
4. **Eastern PNW habitat expansion.** Previous SPS entries have emphasized the marine/forest western PNW. The Ferruginous Hawk's Columbia Basin shrub-steppe distribution formally introduces the eastern PNW as a distinct ecosystem zone. The engine's geographic coverage expands.
5. **Ground-walking behavior documented.** The Ferruginous Hawk is one of very few large North American raptors known to pursue prey on foot. This behavioral outlier deserves specific documentation and it is included here.

## Discoveries

1. **The feathered tarsi (leg feathers to feet) are both a field mark and an ecological adaptation.** The rust-colored leg feathers are the species' most distinctive visual signature, but they also function as insulation for a bird that regularly perches on cold ground. The aesthetic feature is the functional feature -- the engine can document this dual purpose.
2. **The extended gape (larger mouth opening) is an adaptation for large prey.** The Ferruginous Hawk can swallow jackrabbits and prairie dogs -- prey substantially larger than most buteo diets. The structural adaptation (gape extending further toward the eye) enables this. It is a morphological note that documents the prey-handling capability.
