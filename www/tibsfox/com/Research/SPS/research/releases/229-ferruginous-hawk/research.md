## BUTEO REGALIS -- THE REGAL BUTEO

### Taxonomy and the Species Epithet

- **The name.** *Buteo regalis* -- the regal hawk. The species epithet *regalis* (Latin: regal, kingly) is earned by size. This is the largest buteo in North America, and the scientific name acknowledges it explicitly. Among hawks, size commands respect.
- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Buteo*. Species: *regalis*. Two subspecies have been proposed, but the species is typically treated as monotypic.
- **Buteo in context.** The genus *Buteo* is the engine's current focus. Degree 228 introduced the Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*) as the FIRST BUTEO. Degree 229 adds the largest buteo. Degree 230 will add the Rough-legged Hawk (*Buteo lagopus*) -- an Arctic visitor. The buteo sequence is building a comprehensive picture of this ecologically critical genus.
- **Size parameters.** The Ferruginous Hawk is large even by raptor standards. Adults weigh 977-2,270 g (2.2-5.0 lbs), with females (as in all raptors) larger than males. Wingspan: 122-152 cm (48-60 inches). This overlap with some eagle measurements places the Ferruginous Hawk at the upper end of hawk size -- it is, in body volume and wing area, the most eagle-like of the North American buteos.
- **The ferruginous coloration.** *Ferruginous* means rust-colored or iron-red (from Latin *ferrum*, iron). The name refers to the distinctive rusty-red coloration on the upper parts and leg feathers of light-morph adults. The leg feathers extend to the feet, forming a V-shape visible in flight -- a key field mark for identification.

## THE ALARM CALL -- LOUD ROUGH kree-ah

### Vocalization Analysis

The Ferruginous Hawk's alarm call is the engine's E=6 sound for degree 229:

- **The call structure.** The primary alarm call is a loud, rough, descending cry -- rendered variously as *kree-ah*, *kaah*, or a harsh *kaaiii*. The sound has a raspy, rough texture distinct from the Red-tailed Hawk's cleaner, longer scream. The descending pitch is rapid, and the call is often repeated 2-4 times in succession when the bird is disturbed.
- **Frequency range.** The alarm call operates in approximately 1,000-3,500 Hz -- lower and more powerful than the Bald Eagle's thin contact call (2,000-5,000 Hz, degree 221), appropriate for carrying across open terrain with less vegetation to absorb sound.
- **Function.** The alarm call signals intrusion -- at the nest site, or in core hunting territory. Breeding Ferruginous Hawks are notably aggressive in defense of the nest, dive-bombing intruders (including humans) that approach within several hundred meters of the nest tree or cliff ledge. The call accompanies this aerial defense.
- **Comparison to Red-tailed Hawk (228).** The Red-tailed Hawk's call (*keeeeeee-arrr*) is longer, more musical, and more carrying -- the sound that Hollywood co-opted for all raptors. The Ferruginous Hawk's alarm call is shorter, rougher, more urgent. Where the Red-tail's call is a sustained scream, the Ferruginous's is a sharp burst of alarm. Both are E=6 in emotional intensity.
- **Contact and courtship calls.** Beyond the alarm call, Ferruginous Hawks produce softer, lower-pitched contact calls during pair interactions at the nest. These are less frequently heard and less documented than the alarm call.

## ECOLOGY -- OPEN COUNTRY SPECIALIST

### The Grassland and Shrub-Steppe Raptor

The Ferruginous Hawk is the premier open-country raptor of western North America:

- **Habitat requirement.** Unlike the accipiters (forest interior hunters, degrees 225-227) or the Bald Eagle (water/forest edge, degrees 221-222), the Ferruginous Hawk requires open grassland, shrub-steppe, and rangeland with minimal tree or vegetation cover over large areas. The bird hunts primarily by soaring over open terrain and watching for ground mammals.
- **Prey base.** Ground squirrels (*Spermophilus* spp.), prairie dogs (*Cynomys* spp.), jackrabbits (*Lepus* spp.), and pocket gophers (*Thomomys* spp.) constitute the bulk of the diet. The Ferruginous Hawk is a keystone predator of the grassland food web -- its population health tracks the availability of burrowing mammals. When prairie dog colonies decline (as they have across the West due to poisoning programs), Ferruginous Hawk populations follow.
- **Hunting technique.** Three primary techniques: (1) soaring high and diving at prey spotted from altitude -- the classic raptor stoop; (2) hovering into wind to hold position while watching for movement below (kestrel-like, but at much larger scale); (3) ground-walking -- the Ferruginous Hawk is one of the few North American raptors known to walk on the ground and pursue prey on foot.
- **The leg feathers.** The ferruginous leg feathers extending to the foot are not just a field mark -- they are insulation adapted for a bird that regularly sits on cold ground and pursues prey at ground level. The feathered tarsi (lower leg) distinguish the Ferruginous Hawk from most other large raptors.
- **Nesting.** Ferruginous Hawks nest in trees where available (cottonwoods, junipers, power poles), on cliff ledges, or on the ground on rocky outcrops. Ground nesting is rare among raptors but occurs in this species when elevated sites are unavailable. Nests are large stick platforms, added to across multiple years.

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION

### Eastern PNW -- The Shrub-Steppe Connection

The Ferruginous Hawk's PNW distribution is heavily eastern -- the dryland side of the Cascades:

- **Columbia Basin.** The Columbia Basin in central Washington is the Ferruginous Hawk's primary Washington State habitat. The shrub-steppe of the Columbia Basin -- dominated by sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) and bunchgrasses -- is structurally similar to the Great Plains grasslands where the species is most abundant. The Basin hosts breeding pairs as well as wintering birds moving south from more northern grasslands.
- **Eastern Oregon.** The high desert and grasslands of eastern Oregon -- Malheur County, Harney County, Lake County -- hold important Ferruginous Hawk populations. The Malheur National Wildlife Refuge, a critical waterfowl area, also hosts raptors along its grassland edges.
- **Western PNW.** In winter, some Ferruginous Hawks move west of the Cascades into valley lowlands -- the Willamette Valley, the Puget lowlands, the Skagit Valley flats. These western winter records are irregular and less common than the eastern populations. The birds use agricultural fields, golf course rough, and airfield grass margins -- open-country analogs to their grassland breeding habitat.
- **Seasonal movement.** The PNW population is partially migratory. Breeding birds in the northern Great Basin may move south and west in winter, with some reaching California. Birds from more northern populations (Montana, Alberta) move through Washington and Oregon in fall.

(Sources: Ferruginous Hawk Columbia Basin distribution; eastern Oregon populations; Malheur NWR; western PNW winter records; Willamette Valley; Puget lowlands; seasonal movement data)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### A Grassland-Dependent Species Under Pressure

The Ferruginous Hawk is not currently listed as endangered but faces significant conservation pressures:

- **US status.** Candidate Species of Concern in several states; Species of Conservation Concern in Washington State. The US population is estimated at 4,000-5,000 breeding pairs -- a relatively small population for a wide-ranging species.
- **Canada.** Threatened status in Canada under the Species at Risk Act (SARA). Canadian populations have declined significantly as grassland habitat has been converted to cropland.
- **IUCN.** Least Concern globally, but the North American population trend is negative in many areas.
- **Prairie dog poisoning.** The greatest single threat is the decline of prairie dog colonies through poisoning programs, habitat fragmentation, and sylvatic plague. Where prairie dog towns are eliminated, Ferruginous Hawk nest productivity drops sharply. The hawk-prairie dog relationship is one of the best-documented predator-prey dependencies in North American ornithology.
- **Grassland conversion.** The conversion of native grassland to cropland, wind energy development, and oil and gas development all reduce available habitat. The Ferruginous Hawk requires large, unbroken grassland blocks -- fragmented grassland patches are less useful than intact expanses.
- **Power pole electrocution.** Large raptors that perch on power poles are at risk of electrocution when they contact two energized conductors simultaneously. The Ferruginous Hawk's large wingspan makes this risk elevated compared to smaller species. Mitigation programs (pole-top guards, modified insulation) reduce but do not eliminate this risk.

(Sources: Ferruginous Hawk conservation status; USFWS Species of Conservation Concern; Canadian SARA status; prairie dog poisoning connection; grassland conversion data; power pole electrocution risk)

## MORPHOLOGY -- THE EAGLE-LIKE BUTEO

### Size, Wing Shape, and Aerial Mastery

The Ferruginous Hawk's large size produces an eagle-like flight profile:

- **Wing shape.** The wings are long and broad relative to body size -- broader in chord than the Red-tailed Hawk, proportionally more like an eagle's wing. This wing shape generates high lift at low airspeeds, enabling the long soaring circuits over open terrain that the species uses for hunting.
- **Light and dark morphs.** Like the Red-tailed Hawk, the Ferruginous Hawk occurs in light and dark morphs. The light morph -- rusty upper parts, white underparts, rusty leg feathers forming a V against the white belly -- is far more common (approximately 90% of population). Dark morphs are entirely dark brown. Intermediate birds exist but are less common than in some other polymorphic raptors.
- **Leg feather V.** The light morph's most distinctive field mark in flight is the rust-colored leg feathers forming a V or comma against the white belly, visible from below as the bird soars. Combined with the white upper tail (light morph), this pattern makes the Ferruginous Hawk identifiable at distances where individual feathers cannot be resolved.
- **The gape.** The gape (corner of the mouth) extends further back toward the eye in Ferruginous Hawks than in other buteos -- a structural feature that allows the bird to swallow larger prey items. This is an adaptation for taking jackrabbits and prairie dogs, which are substantially larger than the rodents that constitute most other buteo diets.
