## THE AMERICAN KESTREL -- *FALCO SPARVERIUS*

### North America's Most Familiar Falcon

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Falconiformes. Family: Falconidae. Genus: *Falco*. Species: *sparverius*. The species name *sparverius* means "pertaining to sparrows" in Latin -- a reference to the Kestrel's appearance (small, sparrow-like for a falcon) and its original common name "sparrow hawk" (a misnomer, since kestrels are falcons, not hawks).
- **Size.** The American Kestrel is the smallest falcon in North America and one of the smallest falcons in the world. Adults are 22-31 cm (8.7-12.2 in) long with a wingspan of 51-61 cm (20-24 in). Weight: 80-165 grams (2.8-5.8 oz). A female Kestrel weighs about the same as a large chicken egg.
- **Sexual dimorphism -- appearance.** American Kestrels are among the most visually distinctive raptors in terms of sexual dimorphism (sex-based visual differences):
  - **Males:** Slate-blue wings, rusty back with black barring, rusty tail, white face with two black facial marks (mustache and nape), cream to buff underparts with black spotting. Strikingly colorful for a falcon.
  - **Females:** Rufous-brown wings and back with black barring, buff-white underparts with reddish-brown streaking, same facial marks as male but less vivid. Less colorful than the male, but still distinctively patterned.
- **The most colorful falcon.** The American Kestrel is, by a significant margin, the most colorful falcon in North America. Other falcon species are primarily brown, gray, and black. The male Kestrel's slate-blue and rusty coloration is unmistakable.

## THE CALL -- killy-killy-killy

### The Kestrel's Vocal Signature

- **The sound.** The American Kestrel's primary call is the killy-killy-killy -- a series of sharp, high-pitched notes delivered at rapid tempo. The killy is typically given in bursts of 3-6 notes, though longer sequences occur in alarm situations. The call is high-pitched (3,000-8,000 Hz), clear, and carrying -- audible from 200-300 meters in open country.
- **Comparison to Merlin.** The Kestrel's killy and the Merlin's ki-ki-ki are superficially similar -- both are rapid, high-pitched falcon calls -- but they differ in texture. The killy has a sharper, more incisive quality; the ki-ki-ki is more of a chatter. Both are E=5 but they have distinct acoustic personalities.
- **Call function.** The killy-killy-killy serves multiple functions:
  - **Alarm:** When a predator (larger raptor, person, vehicle) approaches closely, the Kestrel gives rapid killy calls while flying in agitated circles.
  - **Contact:** Mated pairs maintain contact with quiet killy notes. The calls allow partners to locate each other across the open fields they inhabit.
  - **Territorial:** During the breeding season, males call frequently to advertise territory and attract mates. The call rate increases near the nest box or cavity.
- **Ultraviolet (UV) vision.** American Kestrels can see ultraviolet light -- a sensory capacity that humans lack. Small mammals (mice, voles) mark their runways with urine, which reflects UV light. Kestrels hunting over grassland can see these UV trails, effectively locating prey corridors. This UV vision is particularly relevant because the killy call often accompanies the hovering flight that precedes a UV-guided strike on vole runways.

## ECOLOGY -- THE OPEN FIELD SPECIALIST

### Hovering, Hunting, and the Fence Post Life

- **Habitat.** The American Kestrel is the quintessential open-country raptor. The habitat requirements are simple: open ground for hunting (grasslands, meadows, agricultural fields, roadsides) and elevated perches for watching (fence posts, power lines, telephone poles, dead tree limbs, cliff ledges). The Kestrel is one of the most commonly seen raptors in North America because it lives at the human-agricultural interface -- the fence post world of highways, farms, and rural roadsides.
- **Hovering.** The American Kestrel is one of the few North American raptors that regularly hovers in place while hunting. The bird beats its wings rapidly while holding its head stationary, scanning the ground below for prey movement. When prey is detected, the Kestrel drops in stages -- hovering, diving to a lower hover, diving again -- before the final strike. The hovering flight is the Kestrel's behavioral signature.
- **Prey.** The diet is highly variable:
  - **Insects:** Grasshoppers, beetles, dragonflies, and other large insects are primary prey in summer. A single Kestrel may take dozens of grasshoppers per day.
  - **Small mammals:** Voles, mice, and shrews year-round. The UV vision advantage is most relevant here.
  - **Small birds:** Occasionally taken, particularly in winter when insects are unavailable. The Kestrel's hunting approach (hovering, dropping) is less effective for bird capture than the Merlin's pursuit strategy.
  - **Earthworms and lizards:** Taken opportunistically.
- **Caching.** Kestrels cache prey -- hiding food items in vegetation, grass, or cavities for later consumption. This behavior allows the bird to take advantage of prey surges (a field full of grasshoppers) without wasting the opportunity.

## NESTING AND BREEDING

### The Cavity Nester's Dependency

- **Cavity nesting.** The American Kestrel is a secondary cavity nester -- it cannot excavate its own nest cavity but relies on holes created by other species (primarily woodpeckers) or natural features (hollow trees, cliff ledges, building crevices). This dependency on pre-existing cavities is both the species' ecological niche and its vulnerability.
- **Nest box acceptance.** The American Kestrel is one of North America's most successful users of artificial nest boxes. Nest box programs have provided supplementary cavity supply in areas where natural cavities are scarce. The Kestrel's acceptance of boxes of specific dimensions (a 15 cm x 15 cm interior, 45 cm deep, with a 7.5 cm entrance hole at a specific height) has been used successfully across the continent.
- **Breeding in the PNW.** In Washington State, Kestrels breed from sea level to approximately 2,500 m elevation. The east side (Columbia Plateau, Okanogan Highlands) supports higher breeding densities than the west side due to more extensive open agricultural land. West-side Kestrels breed in river valleys, agricultural areas, and open grasslands.
- **Eggs and young.** A typical clutch is 4-5 eggs (range 3-7). Incubation is approximately 30 days. Chicks fledge at 28-31 days. Both parents share incubation and chick-rearing duties. The chicks are initially covered in white down and develop rapidly.

(Sources: Kestrel cavity nesting; woodpecker holes; nest box programs; PNW breeding distribution; east-side vs west-side populations; breeding biology)

## POPULATION DECLINE -- A CONSERVATION CONCERN

### The Surprising Trajectory of the Common Kestrel

Despite being one of North America's most familiar and abundant raptors, the American Kestrel faces a significant and poorly-understood population decline:

- **The trend.** North American Breeding Bird Survey data shows a long-term decline in American Kestrel populations beginning in the 1970s-80s and continuing through the present. The steepest declines have occurred in the eastern US, where populations have dropped 50% or more in some regions. Western populations, including the PNW, have shown more modest declines but are still trending downward.
- **The puzzle.** The decline is puzzling because the American Kestrel remains common and its habitat (open agricultural land) has not drastically decreased. Proposed factors include:
  - **Cooper's Hawk predation:** Cooper's Hawk populations have recovered dramatically since the DDT era, and Cooper's Hawks prey on Kestrels. The geographic patterns of Kestrel decline correlate with areas of strong Cooper's Hawk recovery.
  - **West Nile virus:** Some evidence for disease-related mortality.
  - **Pesticide use:** Neonicotinoids and other modern agricultural pesticides reduce insect prey availability.
  - **Cavity loss:** Loss of large dead trees and conversion of old orchards reduces natural cavity supply.
  - **Climate change:** Shifts in grasshopper and vole population dynamics affect prey availability.
- **Conservation response.** Nest box programs are the primary conservation intervention, supplementing cavity supply where natural cavities are limited. Research programs (including the American Kestrel Partnership) monitor populations and study the decline's causes.

(Sources: Breeding Bird Survey kestrel trend; Cooper's Hawk recovery correlation; West Nile virus; neonicotinoid effects; cavity loss; American Kestrel Partnership)

## THE KESTREL IN FALCONRY

### The Small Bird for the Apprentice's Fist

The American Kestrel has an interesting relationship with North American falconry:

- **The apprentice bird.** In the US falconry tradition (regulated by state and federal law), the American Kestrel is designated as a species that licensed apprentice falconers may use during their training period. The Kestrel's small size, relative ease of handling, and manageable prey requirements make it an appropriate first bird for falconers learning the discipline.
- **Historical European use.** In European falconry, the Common Kestrel (*Falco tinnunculus* -- a closely related Old World species) occupied a similar low-status position as a bird for beginners or for ladies (women). Both Old World and New World kestrels were at the bottom of the falconry hierarchy -- below the Peregrine, the Gyrfalcon, and the other large falcons.
- **Modern falconry.** Contemporary falconers value the Kestrel differently than medieval tradition did. The bird's intelligence, trainability, and the spectacle of its hovering hunt make it a rewarding species to work with regardless of its historical status.

(Sources: North American falconry regulations; apprentice falconer species; Common Kestrel European falconry tradition; modern kestrel falconry)
