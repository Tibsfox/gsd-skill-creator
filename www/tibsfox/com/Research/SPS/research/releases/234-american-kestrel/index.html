North America's Most Familiar Falcon

The American Kestrel is the fence-post falcon — the raptor that most North Americans have seen perched along a highway or hovering over a grassy field. It is the smallest North American falcon (females 80-165 grams) and the most colorful: the male's slate-blue wings and rusty back are unique among North American raptors. The killy-killy-killy call is the soundscape of open country, audible from 200-300 meters, a carrying, shrill announcement of the bird's presence.

Acoustic Profile — killy-killy-killy

Frequency: 3,000–8,000 Hz
Call structure: sharp, incisive killy notes, 3–6 per burst
Carrying distance: 200–300m in open country

The killy is sharper and more incisive than the Merlin's ki-ki-ki chatter. Function: alarm, mate contact, territorial advertisement. The killy call rate increases near the nest during breeding season. Both sexes call; the male's territorial killy is the most persistent.

"The kestrel sees what we cannot. Ultraviolet light reveals vole runways marked with urine — the killy calling bird hovering over the field is reading a map invisible to humans."

The Hovering Hunter

The American Kestrel is one of the few North American raptors that regularly hovers in place. The bird beats its wings while holding its head stationary, scanning below for prey movement. When detected, it drops in stages — hover, partial dive, hover again — before the final strike. This hovering gives the Kestrel its behavioral signature: the small bird stationary in mid-air above an open field.

UV vision gives it an advantage invisible to competitors: vole runways marked with urine fluoresce in ultraviolet light. The Kestrel can see these trails, effectively locating prey corridors before any movement is detected.

A Surprising Decline

POPULATION DECLINING — CAUSE UNCLEAR. Despite remaining common, Breeding Bird Survey data shows long-term American Kestrel decline since the 1970s-80s. Steepest declines in eastern US (50%+ in some regions); western populations including PNW show modest but real decline. Proposed causes: Cooper's Hawk recovery (correlation with range overlap), neonicotinoid pesticides reducing insect prey, cavity loss, West Nile virus. American Kestrel Partnership monitors population and studies causes.
