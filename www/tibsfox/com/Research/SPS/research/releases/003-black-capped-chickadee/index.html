The forest is no longer just singing. The forest is talking. The Black-capped Chickadee says something that means something — a signal with syntax, with referential content, with interspecific listeners who decode and act on the information. Degree 3 is the leap from sound to language.

Taxonomy & Physical Description

The Black-capped Chickadee is a small, non-migratory passerine of the family Paridae within the order Passeriformes. The PNW population belongs to P. a. occidentalis, the western subspecies. At 9–14 grams and 4.7–5.9 inches in length, it is in the same size class as the Pacific Wren — but where the wren skulks in old-growth understory, the chickadee is bold, conspicuous, and approachable. It is the ambassador species — the bird most likely to be the first wild bird a child in the PNW learns to identify by name and by sound.

Diagnostic field marks: solid black cap and bib, broad white cheek patches, gray-blue back, buffy flanks. One of the most intensively studied songbirds in the world, with thousands of published papers spanning neuroscience, cognitive ecology, social behavior, and vocal communication.

Bioacoustics — Combinatorial Syntax

The “Fee-Bee” Song

A pure-toned, two-note descending whistle: a higher “fee” (3.5–4.5 kHz) followed by a lower “bee” (3.0–3.8 kHz). The interval between the two notes is approximately a minor third — three semitones, a frequency ratio close to 6:5. This is the most common interval in spontaneous human vocalizations across cultures (Nettl 1983; Brandt, Gebrian & Slevc 2012). The chickadee produces the same interval the human auditory system processes most readily. Convergent solution: two species arriving at the same acoustic basin of attraction.

The “Chick-a-dee” Call: Four Note Types, Rule-Governed Combination

The chick-a-dee call is composed of four distinct note types designated A, B, C, and D (Hailman, Ficken & Ficken 1985):

  • A notes (“chick”): Short, high-frequency introductory notes (>5 kHz)
  • B notes (“-a-”): Lower-frequency medial notes (3–5 kHz)
  • C notes (sweep): Downward-sweeping FM notes (broadband)
  • D notes (“dee”): Terminal broadband notes — the information-encoding elements

The syntactic rules: note types always occur in alphabetical order (A before B before C before D). Any note type can be omitted. The D-note count is variable and encodes predator threat level.

D-Note Encoding: The Landmark Finding

Templeton, Greene & Davis (2005, Science) demonstrated that the number of D notes is inversely correlated with predator size:

  • Large predators (Great Horned Owl): fewer D notes (2–4)
  • Small, agile predators (Northern Pygmy-Owl, Sharp-shinned Hawk): more D notes (8–12+)

Smaller predators are more dangerous to chickadee-sized birds because they can pursue through branches with lethal agility. More D notes = more danger. This was confirmed through controlled experiments with taxidermic mounts. Playback of high-D-note calls elicits stronger mobbing responses. The signal is referential. The communication loop is closed.

This is not metaphorical language. It exhibits the defining properties: referential meaning (signal refers to a specific external referent), combinatorial structure (discrete elements combined by rules), audience-dependent variation, and calibrated receiver response.

Interspecies Eavesdropping: The Forest Broadcast Network

The chickadee’s alarm call is a public broadcast. Other species eavesdrop and respond:

  • Red-breasted Nuthatches decode D-note count and calibrate response intensity (Templeton & Greene 2007, PNAS)
  • Downy and Hairy Woodpeckers increase vigilance
  • Dark-eyed Juncos and Song Sparrows flee to cover on high-D-note alarms
  • Pacific Wrens (degree 1°) reduce singing when threat level is elevated

The chickadee is a public information center — a broadcast station transmitting encoded threat intelligence to the mixed-species flock and the wider forest community. This is the first species in the circle that produces information consumed by other species at other degrees. The information crosses species boundaries. The forest is becoming an information network.

Music Theory Mapping

Combinatorial syntax as chord voicing. A chord combines discrete pitch elements (C, E, G) according to rules (root, third, fifth) to produce meaning (major = bright, minor = dark). The chick-a-dee call combines discrete note types (A, B, C, D) according to ordering rules to produce meaning (more D = higher threat). Both are combinatorial systems where a finite element set generates open-ended meaningful output.

The minor third. The fee-bee song’s descending minor third connects to the deepest layer of human musicality: infant-directed speech, playground chants, the universal calling interval. The chickadee and the human child arrive at the same frequency ratio independently — a convergent acoustic constant.

The broadcast bus. In audio engineering, a bus carries signal from sources to destinations. The chickadee’s alarm is the bus master. The forest air is the cable. Nuthatches, woodpeckers, and wrens are receivers with species-specific processing chains. Same broadcast, different decoding.

Dynamics as information. The D-note count is a dynamic marking — not in amplitude but in communicative intensity. More repetitions = higher urgency. This is a more sophisticated use of dynamics than simple volume: meaning encoded through count, not decibels.
