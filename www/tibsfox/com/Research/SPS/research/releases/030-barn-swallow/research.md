- **Order:** Passeriformes

- **Genus:** *Hirundo*

- **Etymology:** *Hirundo* is the Latin word for "swallow" -- no embellishment, no Greek compound, no mythological reference. The genus IS the word. When Linnaeus named the genus, he used the most direct label possible: the Latin common name for the bird. *Rustica* is Latin for "of the countryside" or "rural" -- the country swallow. The full binomial: **"the country swallow."** The simplest, most literal name in the engine's Hirundinidae. Where *Progne* reached for myth, *Tachycineta* for behavior, and *Stelgidopteryx* for anatomy, *Hirundo* simply says: swallow.

The common name "Barn Swallow" reflects the species' deep association with human agricultural structures. It is the primary nesting habitat. The bird has abandoned natural sites (cliff faces, cave entrances) so thoroughly in North America that finding a natural nest site is now exceptional. The barn IS the habitat.

Liquid-liquid-liquid-rough-liquid. The pattern is **ABBA with a coda**: three liquid, one rough, one liquid return. The dissenter at degree 29 was real -- the pattern genuinely broke. But the family's closing voice restores liquid quality. The rough break was not the end of liquid; it was the contrast that makes the return meaningful. The Barn Swallow's warble sounds more liquid BECAUSE it follows the brrit. After sandpaper, water sounds sweeter.

The Barn Swallow's sound is described as a **long liquid twittering warble** -- which combines elements from all three previous liquid swallows:

- **Long:** Like the Purple Martin's extended vocalizations (3-6 seconds), not the Rough-winged's half-second bursts.
- **Liquid:** The timbral quality shared by degrees 26-28 but absent at 29. Restored at 30.
- **Twittering:** The discrete, rapid-note quality of the Violet-green Swallow (degree 27). Individual notes within a flowing stream.
- **Warble:** A melodic, pitch-varying quality that none of the previous swallows achieved at this level. Warbling implies harmonic movement -- notes that change pitch in a musical way, not just repeat.

The Barn Swallow's song is the **synthesis of the previous liquid sounds**. Stream + rain + brook = river. The martin provided the duration. The Violet-green provided the twittering articulation. The Tree Swallow provided the tumbling complexity. The Barn Swallow combines them all into a long, complex, musically rich warble. It is the most elaborate swallow song in the engine.

### Acoustic Description

Key acoustic parameters:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~2.0-7.5 kHz. Wider than the Rough-winged (2.0-6.0 kHz), comparable to the Tree Swallow (2.0-8.0 kHz), narrower than the Violet-green (3.0-9.0 kHz). The frequency range expands from the Rough-winged's compressed buzz back toward the open bandwidth of the liquid swallows.
- **Peak energy band:** ~3.0-6.0 kHz. Broader than the Rough-winged's narrow 3.0-5.0 kHz peak. The sound fills more of the frequency space, as a warble should -- melodic content requires bandwidth for pitch variation.
- **Song duration:** 3-8 seconds per warble phrase. The longest swallow vocalization in the engine. The Purple Martin's gurgle ran 3-6 seconds. The Barn Swallow matches and exceeds it. These are sustained vocal performances, not brief calls.
- **Note rate:** 8-16 notes per second within the warble. Individual notes are rapid but perceptible -- a twittering quality where each element is distinct but the overall stream flows continuously. This straddles the perceptual fusion threshold that the Tree Swallow explored at degree 28.
- **Note structure:** Frequency-modulated tones -- clear pitch sweeps, upslurs, downslurs, and chevron shapes. High spectral purity. Harmonically rich. The opposite of the Rough-winged's broadband noise. Each note has a clear pitch center, and the notes chain together into melodic phrases that genuinely warble -- rise and fall, accelerate and decelerate, creating a musical line.
- **Phrase structure:** Complex and variable. The Barn Swallow's song consists of:
  1. **The warble proper** -- a sustained sequence of rapid, liquid, frequency-modulated notes varying in pitch and rhythm. This is the "long liquid twittering warble." Duration 3-8 seconds. The primary advertising song.
  2. **The terminal buzz** -- many Barn Swallow warble phrases end with a short, dry, buzzy note or rattle. This terminal buzz is acoustically similar to the Rough-winged Swallow's brrit but briefer and embedded within a liquid context. The warble absorbs a taste of the rough. The liquid and the rough coexist within a single phrase.
  3. **The twitter** -- shorter, simpler twittering sequences used as contact calls. Less elaborate than the full warble. Similar in quality to the Violet-green's twitter but longer and more structured.
- **Timbre:** Liquid, twittering, melodic, warm. The sound has the flowing quality of water through a complex channel -- not the smooth stream of the martin, not the crystalline drops of the Violet-green, not the tumbling brook of the Tree Swallow, but a **river**: wide, deep, complex, carrying all the tributaries within it.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate to high. Louder than the Rough-winged and Violet-green. Comparable to the martin and Tree Swallow. The sound projects well across the open farmland habitat. Energy 4 reflects sustained, confident vocal delivery at moderate-to-high volume.
- **Context:** The Barn Swallow sings frequently and conspicuously -- from perches on wires, fences, barn roofs, and in flight. It is the most vocal swallow in the PNW, and its song is the most musically complex. Males sing to defend territories, attract mates, and maintain pair bonds. Song frequency is highest during the breeding season (May-July in the PNW). Dawn singing is common.

## The Deeply Forked Tail: The Barn Swallow's Visual Signature

### The Most Diagnostic Swallow Feature

While the previous four swallows were distinguished by color (iridescent or drab), the Barn Swallow introduces a new visual axis: **shape**. The Barn Swallow has the most deeply forked tail of any North American swallow. The outer tail feathers (rectrices) extend far beyond the inner ones, creating long, elegant streamers that are visible in flight from considerable distance. This tail shape is the bird's most diagnostic field mark and one of the most recognizable silhouettes in the bird world.

The deeply forked tail functions on multiple axes:

**1. Aerodynamic efficiency.** The forked shape creates reduced drag during the high-speed, agile flight the Barn Swallow requires for aerial insectivory. The tail acts as a rudder and airbrake, allowing tight turns and rapid course changes during pursuit of flying insects. Barn Swallows forage lower than most swallows -- often within meters of the ground, following livestock and farm machinery that flush insects. The forked tail is the tool that makes this low, maneuverable flight possible.

**2. Sexual selection.** Tail length is sexually dimorphic -- males have longer outer tail feathers than females. Females prefer males with longer, more symmetrical tail streamers. This has been experimentally demonstrated: studies by Anders Pape Moller showed that males with artificially elongated tails attracted mates faster and produced more offspring than controls or males with shortened tails. The tail is an honest signal of male quality -- longer, more symmetrical streamers indicate genetic fitness and resistance to parasites. The deeply forked tail is both a flight tool and a mate-attraction device.

**3. Parasite indication.** Tail symmetry correlates negatively with ectoparasite load. Birds carrying more blood-feeding mites (*Ornithonyssus*) tend to have shorter, less symmetrical tails. The tail is a visible health certificate -- its length and symmetry advertise the bearer's parasite resistance. Females reading tail shape are reading health data.

### Plumage: Steel Blue and Russet

The Barn Swallow's plumage is distinctive but different from the "iridescent jewel" pattern of the Violet-green and Tree Swallow:

- **Dorsal:** Steel-blue iridescence. Darker and less colorful than the Violet-green's bright green-violet or the Tree Swallow's metallic blue-green. The Barn Swallow's back is uniformly dark blue, not dramatically multicolored.
- **Ventral:** Rusty-orange to buffy-white breast and belly. Unique among PNW swallows. No other local swallow has warm-toned underparts.
- **Forehead:** Rusty-orange forehead patch. Diagnostic in close views.
- **Throat:** Dark rusty-chestnut throat band. The darkest throat of any PNW swallow.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dorsal | Purple-black | Green + violet | Blue-green | Plain brown | **Steel blue** |
| Ventral | Dark (male) | Clean white | Clean white | Dingy white | **Rusty-orange** |

The Barn Swallow restores iridescence after the Rough-winged's drab break, but with a twist: it adds warm tones (russet, orange, chestnut) that no previous swallow showed. The palette evolves from cool iridescence through drabness to warm iridescence. The family's color story does not repeat itself. Each member wears its own palette.

## Human Commensalism: The Barn Swallow and People

### The Most Anthropogenic Ecosystem in the Engine

The Barn Swallow's ecosystem is **open farmland** -- the most human-modified landscape in the engine to date. Previous ecosystems have included old-growth forest, mountain meadow, marsh edge, open field, and river bank. Open farmland is different: it is a landscape created by human agriculture, maintained by human activity, and populated by human structures. The pastures, barns, fences, and livestock that define open farmland are all products of human land use. The Barn Swallow does not merely tolerate human structures -- it **depends** on them.

In North America, the Barn Swallow nests almost exclusively on human-built structures:

- **Barns and outbuildings:** The traditional nesting habitat and the source of the common name. Open-sided barns with wooden beams or ledges provide the sheltered, rough-surfaced attachment points the Barn Swallow needs for its mud-and-grass nest.
- **Bridges and overpasses:** The most important modern nesting habitat. Concrete bridge underpasses provide sheltered, elevated surfaces that mimic natural cliff overhangs. Many PNW Barn Swallow colonies are now on bridges.
- **Culverts:** Large culvert openings provide dark, sheltered nesting sites similar to cave entrances.
- **Porches and eaves:** Residential structures with overhanging eaves or covered porches attract nesting Barn Swallows.
- **Docks and boathouses:** Waterside structures over open water, especially in Puget Sound marine areas.

Prior to European settlement, Barn Swallows in North America nested on cliff faces, cave walls, and rocky overhangs. These natural sites are now almost entirely abandoned in favor of human structures. The transition from natural to anthropogenic nesting is essentially complete. The Barn Swallow has become an **obligate commensal** of human architecture in North America -- a wild bird that cannot breed without human-built structures.

This is the engine's deepest exploration of the human-wildlife interface. Previous species have tolerated human presence (robin on the lawn), used human structures opportunistically (Tree Swallow in nest boxes), or benefited from human modifications (Rough-winged Swallow in road cuts). The Barn Swallow goes further: it has restructured its entire breeding biology around human architecture. The barn is not an option. It is the habitat.

### The Mutualism

The relationship between Barn Swallows and farmers has historically been mutualistic. Barn Swallows consume enormous quantities of flying insects -- each bird eats an estimated 850 insects per day during the breeding season. Many of these are agricultural pests: flies, mosquitoes, beetles, moths. A colony of Barn Swallows nesting in a working barn provides meaningful pest suppression. Farmers have long recognized this, and many traditional farming cultures actively encouraged Barn Swallow nesting by leaving barn doors open and installing nesting ledges.

The name "Barn Swallow" is used across multiple languages (German *Rauchschwalbe* = "chimney swallow," French *hirondelle rustique* = "rustic swallow"), always linking the bird to human agricultural structures. The association is global. On every continent where the Barn Swallow breeds, it is the swallow of the farmyard, the swallow of the human world.

## Global Distribution: Six Continents

### The Most Widespread Landbird

The Barn Swallow breeds on every continent except Antarctica and Australia (though it reaches both as a vagrant or non-breeding visitor). Its breeding range spans:

- **North America:** Alaska to Mexico. Common throughout the lower 48 states and southern Canada.
- **Europe:** From the British Isles and Scandinavia through southern Europe and Turkey. The most common swallow across the continent.
- **Asia:** From the Middle East through Central Asia, India, China, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Multiple subspecies across this vast range.
- **Africa:** Breeding range in North Africa. Wintering range throughout sub-Saharan Africa.
- **South America:** North American populations winter through Central America into South America. The migration spans the Western Hemisphere.

No other landbird species breeds on six continents. The Barn Swallow's success is attributed to its commensalism with humans -- wherever humans build structures and farm land, the Barn Swallow can breed. Human civilization has expanded the species' nesting habitat by orders of magnitude. Before human agriculture, the Barn Swallow was likely limited to cliffs, caves, and rocky overhangs. Human structures freed it from this geological constraint, enabling colonization of any landscape with open country and flying insects.

The Barn Swallow's six-continent distribution makes it the engine's most globally significant species. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) has broad North American range. The American Robin (degree 25) is widespread across North America. The Barn Swallow is worldwide. The engine has moved from local (PNW endemic species), to continental (widespread North American species), to global (six-continent species).

## Conservation: A Declining Global Species

### The Paradox of the Common

Despite being the most widespread landbird, the Barn Swallow is in decline across much of its range. North American Breeding Bird Survey data show a **long-term decline of approximately 1% per year since the mid-1980s**, translating to a cumulative decline of roughly 30-40% over three decades. The decline is steeper in parts of the northeastern United States and maritime Canada.

Causes of decline are multifactorial:

- **Agricultural intensification.** Modern industrial agriculture eliminates the insect populations Barn Swallows depend on. Pesticide use, especially neonicotinoids, reduces flying insect abundance. Monoculture replaces the diverse farmscapes that support insect diversity.
- **Loss of nesting structures.** Modern barns are sealed, climate-controlled buildings that exclude swallows. The open-sided wooden barns that provided traditional nesting habitat are disappearing. Modern bridges are designed to deter nesting. The structures that enabled the Barn Swallow's human commensalism are being replaced by structures that exclude it.
- **Climate change.** Phenological mismatch between spring arrival and peak insect emergence. Barn Swallows that arrive on the breeding grounds too early (or whose insect prey emerges too late) face reduced foraging success during the critical egg-laying period.

The irony is structural: the species that became the most widespread landbird by depending on human agriculture is now declining because human agriculture is changing. The mutualism is breaking. Modern farming does not need or want swallows in its sealed, automated barns. The commensal relationship that made the Barn Swallow the world's most successful swallow is being dissolved by the same force that created it.

This is the first conservation story in the engine that is driven by the **breakdown of a mutualism** rather than by direct habitat loss or persecution. The Purple Martin faces box competition from starlings. The Spotted Owl faces habitat loss from logging. The Barn Swallow faces something subtler: the human partner in a centuries-old mutualism is changing the terms.

Other swallows have tails; the Barn Swallow's is longest. Other swallows have songs; the Barn Swallow's is longest. The Barn Swallow is the elongated swallow -- stretched in both physical and acoustic dimensions.

The elongation in both domains serves the same function: **sexual selection**. Females prefer males with longer tails (Moller's experiments). And across songbirds generally, females prefer males with longer, more complex songs (Catchpole and Slater). The Barn Swallow's signature -- the bird that birders know it by -- is a dual expression of sexual selection pressure operating on two independent channels (visual and acoustic) to produce the same structural effect (elongation).

### SPS Internal

- **SPS-026 (Purple Martin, degree 26):** QUINTET MEMBER 1. The patriarch. The martin's liquid gurgle (stream) and the Barn Swallow's long liquid twittering warble (river) are the quintet's two longest vocalizations. Both are sustained, complex, liquid performances. The martin opens the family; the Barn Swallow closes it. Bookend voices. The stream flows into the river.

- **SPS-027 (Violet-green Swallow, degree 27):** QUINTET MEMBER 2. The cliff-dweller. The Barn Swallow's warble incorporates twittering -- the Violet-green's defining vocal quality. The "twittering" in "long liquid twittering warble" is the Violet-green's contribution to the composite. The cliff-dweller's voice echoes in the archetype's song.

- **SPS-028 (Tree Swallow, degree 28):** QUINTET MEMBER 3. The centrist. Closest in mass (17-25 g vs. 17-20 g) and foraging habitat (open areas near water). Often share airspace. But the Barn Swallow's forked tail and warm-toned underparts visually distinguish them. Song complexity: the Tree Swallow's flexible repertoire vs. the Barn Swallow's extended warble. Both are vocally rich; the Barn Swallow is more melodically sustained.

- **SPS-029 (Rough-winged Swallow, degree 29):** QUINTET MEMBER 4. The dissenter. Maximum acoustic contrast within the family: rough buzzy brrit (29) vs. long liquid twittering warble (30). The Barn Swallow's terminal buzz acknowledges the Rough-winged's contribution -- a fragment of rough embedded in liquid. The dissenter's voice survives inside the archetype's song.

- **SPS-025 (American Robin, degree 25):** ARCHETYPE PARALLEL. *Turdus migratorius* (type genus of Turdidae) arrived last in the thrush sequence. *Hirundo rustica* (type genus of Hirundinidae) arrives last in the swallow sequence. Both are the most familiar, most widespread member of their family. Both are anchor species that define their family in the public mind. The engine's archetype-arriving-last pattern repeats.

- **SPS-012 (Pine Siskin, degree 12):** NOISE BRACKET. The Pine Siskin's zhreee (12) and the Rough-winged's brrit (29) were the engine's two noise-class sounds. The Barn Swallow's warble at degree 30 immediately follows the second noise event, restoring tonal/liquid quality. The engine oscillates: noise (12) -> long tonal run (13-28) -> noise (29) -> tonal return (30).

- **SPS-014-020 (Warbler quintet):** FAMILY QUINTET COMPARISON. Both families achieved five species. The warbler quintet spread across 7 degrees with a gap. The swallow quintet occupies 5 consecutive degrees with zero gaps. Both quintets have an outlier (Orange-crowned Warbler at degree 20, separated by gap; Rough-winged Swallow at degree 29, the acoustic dissenter). The swallow quintet is denser but the warbler quintet was more spread.
