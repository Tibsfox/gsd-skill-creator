Olympic Marmot

Marmota olympus
musical multi-note whistle — THIRD CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75–76–77), the Sciuridae TRIO (marmot trio!), ENDEMIC to the Olympic Peninsula — found NOWHERE else on Earth, rarest and most geographically restricted species in the engine, E=4, Olympic alpine, seventeenth consecutive original CSV

Endemic — Found Nowhere Else on Earth

The Olympic Marmot is ENDEMIC to the Olympic Peninsula — it exists nowhere else on the planet. This is the most geographically restricted species in the entire engine. Found only in the alpine and subalpine meadows of the Olympic Mountains, isolated by the Puget Sound lowlands. E=4 — a musical, multi-note whistle distinct from the single-note calls of its marmot cousins. The rarest marmot. The Olympic treasure.

Third Consecutive Sciuridae (75–76–77) — The Marmot Trio!

Three consecutive marmots: Yellow-bellied (75), Hoary (76), Olympic (77). Three species of genus Marmota in three degrees — the Sciuridae trio. Unprecedented: no family has appeared three times consecutively before. The marmot pair (75–76) extends to a marmot trio. Three PNW marmots. Three elevational niches. Three whistle types: chirp, penetrating whistle, musical multi-note whistle.

Musical Multi-Note Whistle — 78th Acoustic Element

The Olympic Marmot produces a musical, multi-note whistle — a series of ascending or descending notes that sounds more like a melody than an alarm. Unlike the Hoary Marmot’s single piercing whistle (76, E=5) or the Yellow-bellied Marmot’s sharp chirp (75, E=4), the Olympic Marmot’s call has musical phrasing. Multiple notes. Tonal variation. E=4 — lower energy than the Hoary but more melodically complex.

The Mree Parallel

Degree 77 pairs the Olympic Marmot with Mree in Part A — Folk/Pop, E=2, Taiwanese-American. Both create layered, melodic sounds — Mree’s ethereal vocal layering, the marmot’s multi-note whistle melody. Both are geographically specific — Mree in Seattle’s bedroom pop scene, the Olympic Marmot found nowhere else on Earth. Both quiet relative to peers — E=2 vs. E=4, while Hoary Marmot is E=5.

The Sound: Musical Multi-Note Whistle (78th Acoustic Element)

The whistle: A series of ascending or descending notes — typically 3–5 distinct tonal steps, approximately 1,500–6,000 Hz, with musical phrasing that distinguishes it from all other marmot calls. Duration 1.0–2.5 seconds for a full phrase. The call has a melodic, almost songlike quality [1, 2, 3].

Why E=4: Lower volume than the Hoary Marmot (E=5) but more melodically complex. (1) Multi-note structure — musical phrasing. (2) Moderate range — audible at 100–300 meters. (3) Social colony context — used for colony communication. (4) Endemic rarity — heard only in the Olympic Mountains.

Marmot trio contrast: Yellow-bellied (75): sharp chirp, E=4, single note. Hoary (76): penetrating whistle, E=5, single note. Olympic (77): musical multi-note whistle, E=4, multiple notes. Three marmots, three acoustic signatures.

Taxonomy: Marmota olympus

Etymology: Marmota from French marmotte, ultimately from Latin murem montis (“mouse of the mountain”). olympus from the Olympic Mountains where the species is endemic. Named by Merriam (1898). The mountain mouse of Olympus [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Sciuridae — THIRD consecutive Sciuridae (75–76–77). Genus Marmota — the THIRD of three PNW marmots. All three PNW marmots now in the engine: M. flaviventris (75), M. caligata (76), M. olympus (77) [1, 2, 3].

Physical Description: The Olympic Endemic

Size: 560–680 mm total, 3.5–6 kg — intermediate between Yellow-bellied (2–5 kg) and Hoary (5–7 kg). Brown pelage with lighter belly and face patches. Shorter tail than Hoary Marmot [1, 2, 3].

Pelage: Brown to tawny-brown body, distinct from the silver-gray Hoary and the yellowish-brown Yellow-bellied. White patches around muzzle. Darker extremities but less distinct “boots” than the Hoary Marmot.

Ecology: The Island on the Mountain

Habitat: Alpine and subalpine meadows of the Olympic Mountains, 1,500–2,000 meters. ONLY found in the Olympics — isolated by the Puget Sound lowlands and surrounding forests. A sky island species [1, 2, 3].

Social: Highly social, colonial. Family groups of 5–15. Extended family bonds — Olympic Marmots are among the most social marmots, with multi-generational family groups. Playful greeting behaviors. Nose-to-nose contact.

Diet: Alpine wildflowers, grasses, sedges. Lupine and aster are preferred.

Hibernation: 6–8 months (September–May). Family groups hibernate together — communal hibernation, unlike the more solitary hibernation of Yellow-bellied Marmots.

Predators: Coyote (primary), Golden Eagle, Bobcat. The Olympic Marmot has fewer predators than mainland marmots due to the peninsula’s isolation — no wolverines, no grizzly bears.
