## THE OLYMPIC MARMOT: THE ENDEMIC TREASURE

The Olympic Marmot is **ENDEMIC to the Olympic Peninsula** -- it exists **nowhere else on the planet**. This is the most geographically restricted species in the entire engine. Found only in the alpine and subalpine meadows of the Olympic Mountains, isolated from mainland marmot populations by the Puget Sound lowlands and surrounding coniferous forests, the Olympic Marmot is a **sky island species** -- a relic population that evolved in isolation on a mountain "island" surrounded by a "sea" of lowland forest [1, 2, 3].

## TAXONOMY: *Marmota olympus*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Marmota** -- from the French *marmotte*, from Romansch *murmont* (mountain mouse), ultimately from Latin *murem montis* (mouse of the mountain). The genus contains approximately fourteen species worldwide [1, 2].

- **olympus** -- from the Olympic Mountains, the species' entire range. Named by C. Hart Merriam in 1898 from specimens collected in the Olympic Mountains. *Marmota olympus*: "the mountain mouse of Olympus" [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Olympic Marmot (standard), the only name widely used. No regional variants because the species exists only in one small range [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE ENDEMIC FORM

### Body Form

The Olympic Marmot is intermediate in size between the Yellow-bellied and Hoary Marmots [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 560-680 mm (22-27 inches) including tail.
- **Weight:** 3.5-6 kg (8-13 lbs) at peak autumn mass. Spring emergence: 2.5-3.5 kg.
- **Body shape:** Stocky, robust, similar to other marmots but slightly less massive than the Hoary Marmot.
- **Tail:** 170-240 mm. Brown, less bushy than the Hoary Marmot's tail.

### Pelage: The Brown Coat

The Olympic Marmot's pelage is distinct from its two relatives [1, 2, 3]:

- **Body:** Brown to tawny-brown, darker than the silver-gray Hoary and less yellow than the Yellow-bellied. The overall impression is a warm brown.
- **Head:** Dark brown on the crown. White patches on the muzzle and around the nose.
- **Feet:** Darker brown but without the dramatic "boots" of the Hoary Marmot.
- **Belly:** Lighter tan to buff.

## THE SOUND: MUSICAL MULTI-NOTE WHISTLE -- 78th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Whistle That Sings

The Olympic Marmot's alarm call is a **musical, multi-note whistle** -- a series of ascending or descending tonal notes that sounds more like a brief melody than a simple alarm [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 1,500-6,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 1.0-2.5 seconds for a full phrase
- **Structure:** A series of 3-5 distinct tonal steps, ascending or descending. The notes have clear pitch separation. The overall impression is musical -- closer to birdsong than to the sharp alarm calls of other marmots.
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- moderate. Audible at 100-300 meters. Quieter than the Hoary Marmot's E=5 whistle but more melodically complex.
- **Context:** Alarm calls, territorial communication, social greeting. The multi-note structure may convey more information than the single-note calls of other marmots.

### Why E=4

The Olympic Marmot's whistle warrants E=4 -- lower than the Hoary Marmot's E=5 but matching the Yellow-bellied's E=4 -- because [1, 2, 3]:

1. **Volume:** Moderate. Audible at 100-300 meters, less than the Hoary's 300-500+ meters.
2. **Melodic complexity:** More tonal variation than either relative. The multi-note structure is the most musically complex mammalian vocalization in the engine.
3. **Colonial context:** Used in social colony communication, not just alarm.
4. **Rarity factor:** Heard ONLY in the Olympic Mountains. The geographic restriction makes hearing this call a rare experience.

## ECOLOGY: THE SKY ISLAND

### Habitat: Endemic Alpine

The Olympic Marmot's entire range is the alpine and subalpine zone of the Olympic Mountains [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Elevation:** 1,500-2,000 meters. Lower than the Hoary Marmot's range (1,500-3,000+ m) because the Olympic Mountains are lower than the Cascades.
- **Range:** ONLY the Olympic Peninsula. Total range: approximately 500 km2 of suitable alpine habitat. The most geographically restricted marmot in North America.
- **Isolation:** The Olympic Mountains are separated from the Cascades by the Puget Sound lowlands. The Olympic Marmot evolved in isolation -- a sky island species, like a Darwin's finch of the alpine Pacific Northwest.
- **Microhabitat:** Alpine meadows with talus or boulder fields for denning. Similar to other marmots but at lower elevation and in a maritime alpine climate (wetter, milder winters than Cascade alpine).

### Social Structure: The Family Colony

Olympic Marmots are among the most social marmots [1, 2, 3]:

- **Colony structure:** Family groups of 5-15 individuals spanning multiple generations. Extended family bonds are stronger than in other marmot species.
- **Greeting behavior:** Nose-to-nose contact, play wrestling, communal basking. Olympic Marmots are notably more social and less aggressive than Yellow-bellied Marmots.
- **Communal hibernation:** Family groups hibernate together -- adults, yearlings, and juveniles share hibernation burrows. This communal hibernation is distinctive among North American marmots.

### Conservation: Decline

The Olympic Marmot has declined significantly [1, 3, 5, 6]:

- **Population:** Estimated fewer than 2,000 individuals in the early 2000s. Population has declined due to increased predation by coyotes, which have expanded into the Olympic alpine.
- **Threats:** Coyote predation (primary), climate change (shrinking alpine habitat), reduced snowpack (earlier spring exposure to predators).
- **Protection:** Olympic National Park provides habitat protection, but the species has no buffer -- its entire range is in the Olympics, and any habitat loss is proportionally devastating.
