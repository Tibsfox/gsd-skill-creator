## What Worked

1. **The four-layer simultaneous framework is the entry's central contribution and is completely correct.** The Hoh winter storm is uniquely documented by the presence of all four primary acoustic layers at the same time. No other entry in the engine has all four: rain + wind + creek/river + canopy. The framework is both analytically rigorous and intuitively compelling: four layers is complete.
2. **The branch fall acoustic signature documentation is specific and accurate.** The descending two-to-six-second signature -- snap (1000-4000 Hz) to cascade of impacts to ground thud (100-500 Hz) -- is uniquely identifiable and is the most characteristic individual acoustic event in the winter forest canopy. Documenting it specifically, with frequency and duration, adds acoustic precision to the entry.
3. **The One Square Inch of Silence reference (Gordon Hempton) is the correct cultural counterpart.** Hempton has documented the Hoh as one of the quietest places in the contiguous US. Degree 324 documents the same place in the opposite acoustic condition. The same location, the quietest and the loudest: the Hoh Rainforest's acoustic range is the full expression of the Pacific Northwest's sound potential.
4. **E=8 through completeness is a new calibration category.** Previous E=8 entries in the post-summit SPS sequence: rock avalanche (brief peak intensity), tidal bore (sustained hydraulic mass), Steller Sea Lion (anatomical mass), Gruntruck (musical mass). The Hoh winter storm E=8 is different: completeness. All frequency channels active simultaneously across the full audible spectrum. This is the most complex E=8 calibration in the engine.
5. **The moss layer as acoustic modifier is scientifically accurate and adds specific Hoh character.** Club moss (Selaginella oregana) absorbs high-frequency reflections, reducing reverberation time. The saturated winter moss produces its own sub-layer between individual drops. The forest's acoustic environment is shaped by the moss as much as by the trees.

## Discoveries

1. **The hydrological lag (4-8 hours) between rain peak and river discharge peak creates the maximum simultaneous condition.** During the lag period, rain layer is at maximum (atmospheric event) and river layer is rising toward its storm maximum (hydrological response). This 4-8 hour window -- peak rain + rising river -- is when all four layers are simultaneously at high intensity. The most complete soundscape is not at the storm's atmospheric peak nor at the river's discharge peak, but in the hours between them.
2. **The Sitka Spruce trunk flex at sub-bass frequencies (20-60 Hz) is the canopy's contribution to the body-vibration acoustic register.** A 60-meter-tall Sitka Spruce with a 3-meter diameter trunk flexing in a 40 mph gust produces a deep creak at 20-60 Hz, sustained for 3-10 seconds, felt through the ground before it is heard through the air. The Hoh winter storm has both an airborne acoustic profile and a ground-vibration profile that a person standing in the forest feels simultaneously. The body is an instrument.
3. **The Hoh Rainforest's unique completeness is tied to its specific geographic position.** The valley's east-west orientation perpendicular to the Pacific storm track -- not merely west-facing but orthogonal to the storm path -- is why the Hoh receives 140-170 inches of rain while Seattle (150 miles east) receives 37 inches. The acoustic completeness is a consequence of a specific geological and meteorological coincidence: the Olympic Mountains blocking Pacific moisture, the valley orientation channeling it, and the resultant ecosystem generating all four acoustic layers at maximum intensity simultaneously.
