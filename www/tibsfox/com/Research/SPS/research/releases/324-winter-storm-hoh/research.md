## THE FOUR LAYERS -- THE COMPLETE ACOUSTIC STACK

### All Layers Simultaneously

The Hoh winter storm at degree 324 is defined by the simultaneous presence of four distinct acoustic layers. Each layer is documented individually below; their simultaneous presence is what makes this entry the most complete PNW soundscape in the engine:

**Layer 1: Rain**
The most pervasive and continuous layer. Temperate rainforest rain is not intermittent; during a winter storm, it falls for hours or days without pause. The rain produces acoustic output on every surface: on the giant maple leaves (broad-leaf impact, high amplitude per drop), on the moss-covered bark of Sitka Spruce and Douglas-fir (soft impact, muffled), on the fern fronds (medium impact, percussive), on the standing water of the valley floor (splash and ring), on the Hoh River itself (indistinguishable from the river sound at low rain intensity, clearly distinguishable at high intensity). Rain frequency: 1,000-16,000 Hz depending on drop size, fall velocity, and impact surface. The rain layer is continuous, omnidirectional, and changes character as wind speed modulates the drop angle and size distribution.

**Layer 2: Wind**
The most variable and dramatic layer. Wind in the Hoh Rainforest operates at multiple spatial scales simultaneously. At canopy level (50-80 meters), the wind drives the Sitka Spruce crowns -- the large conifers flex, creak, and sweep, producing both the aerodynamic sounds of airflow through foliage and the structural sounds of wood flexing under load. At mid-canopy level (15-30 meters), the bigleaf maple canopy oscillates, the maple limbs flex, and the wind-driven rain changes direction. At ground level, the wind is reduced by the dense vegetation but still produces movement in the fern understory and the moss layers. Wind generates: (a) aerodynamic sound from foliage (400-4,000 Hz, the hiss and roar of air through leaves and needles), (b) structural sound from tree trunk and limb motion (20-200 Hz, the deep creak of wood under load), (c) the Aeolian tones produced when specific wind speeds create resonance in particular tree structures (varying, 100-1,000 Hz), and (d) the sound of windthrow events -- branches or weakened trees falling during the storm.

**Layer 3: Creek / River**
The continuous hydraulic layer. The Hoh River and its tributaries (South Fork Hoh, Owl Creek, and numerous unnamed tributaries that drain the surrounding Olympic peaks) carry the winter's accumulated rainfall through the valley. During a major winter storm, the Hoh River rises rapidly -- rain-on-snow events can produce rapid flood responses in the valley. The river sound is: (a) the main channel flow -- a continuous deep broadband sound (100-2,000 Hz, with dominant energy in the 200-800 Hz range), (b) tributary confluence -- the distinct sound of smaller streams meeting the main channel, each with its own pitch and rhythm, (c) rapids and cascades -- wherever the valley floor is steep enough to produce accelerated flow, the river generates higher-frequency whitewater sounds (500-4,000 Hz), (d) bank erosion sounds -- during high-water events, the river undercuts its banks; the sound of soil and root masses falling into the current is part of the winter storm acoustic record, and (e) log jam dynamics -- the Hoh carries enormous volumes of large woody debris; during storm events, log jams shift and break, producing percussive low-frequency acoustic events.

**Layer 4: Canopy**
The highest and most dramatic layer. The Hoh Rainforest canopy in winter storm is the most acoustically complex layer because it responds to the wind in structural, not fluid, ways. The canopy layer produces: (a) foliage aerodynamics -- the Sitka Spruce needles and the remaining Big Leaf Maple leaves produce continuous aerodynamic sound; during a storm, the maple has shed most of its leaves but the twigs and branches still intercept wind; the Sitka Spruce retains its needles year-round, providing continuous aerodynamic sound even in leafless conditions, (b) trunk flex -- the deep, low-frequency creaking of spruce trunks 3-5 meters in diameter flexing in the wind; these structures produce sound at 20-100 Hz that is felt as vibration through the ground before it is heard as sound, (c) canopy collision -- during high wind events, adjacent large trees' crowns contact each other; the impact of crown-against-crown produces percussive broadband events, (d) branch fall -- winter storms are the primary mechanism for branch fall in the temperate rainforest; the sound of a large branch (diameter 15-30 cm, length 3-10 meters) falling through the canopy -- a descending cascade of impacts through multiple vegetation layers before ground contact -- is one of the most characteristic sounds of the winter storm canopy.

## THE HOH RAINFOREST

### The Most Complete PNW Ecosystem

The Hoh Rainforest is the setting because no other Pacific Northwest ecosystem combines all four layers at the same intensity:

- **Geography.** The Hoh Rainforest occupies the Hoh River valley on the west slope of the Olympic Mountains in Olympic National Park. The valley runs from the Pacific coast inland approximately 30 miles to the base of Mt. Olympus (7,965 feet, the highest peak in the Olympic Range). The valley is oriented east-west, perpendicular to the prevailing Pacific storm track, which channels wet air directly from the ocean into the valley interior.
- **Precipitation.** The Hoh Rainforest receives 140-170 inches (355-430 cm) of precipitation per year at the valley floor -- among the highest precipitation amounts in the contiguous United States outside of Hawaii. The Olympic Mountains intercept moisture-laden Pacific air and force it upward; the resulting orographic precipitation falls on the west slope valleys, of which the Hoh is the largest and most accessible. The visitor center at the Hoh Rain Forest receives approximately 12 feet of rain per year. This is not an anomaly; it is the median.
- **The temperate rainforest ecosystem.** The Hoh Rainforest is one of only a few temperate (not tropical) rainforests in the world. The dominant trees are Sitka Spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), Western Hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), Western Red Cedar (*Thuja plicata*), and Big Leaf Maple (*Acer macrophyllum*). The bigleaf maples in the Hoh are covered in a thick layer of club moss (*Selaginella oregana* and other species) that hangs in curtains from the branches -- creating the characteristic "hall of mosses" appearance of the Hoh Rainforest. The moss layer is acoustically significant: it absorbs high-frequency sound, giving the forest a muffled, intimate acoustic character even in dry conditions.
- **The Hoh River.** The Hoh River drains the west slope of the Olympic Mountains, including the glaciers on Mt. Olympus (Blue Glacier, Hoh Glacier, White Glacier). The river is fed year-round by glacial meltwater, snowmelt, and direct rainfall. Winter discharge is high due to rainfall; spring discharge is the annual peak due to snowmelt. The river carries a very high suspended sediment load during high-water events (glacial flour from the Mt. Olympus glaciers plus storm-mobilized organic material from the valley floor).
- **The moss layer as acoustic modifier.** The Hoh Rainforest's moss layer -- on trees, on the ground, on every horizontal surface -- acts as a broadband sound absorber. The moss layer reduces reverberation time in the forest: in dry temperate forests, sound reflects off hard bark and mineral soil, producing longer reverb. In the Hoh, moss absorbs reflected sound, making the forest acoustically intimate. During rain, the moss becomes saturated and its acoustic properties change: the saturated moss produces a softer impact sound than mineral surfaces (rain on wet moss: 2,000-8,000 Hz but lower amplitude per drop than rain on rock or bark), and the moss runoff -- thin sheets of water running over saturated sphagnum and club moss -- produces its own continuous acoustic layer between the individual drops.

## THE HOH WINTER STORM -- METEOROLOGY

### Why Winter Is the Maximum Event

The Hoh Rainforest winter storm is the acoustic maximum because all four layers are simultaneously active and at high intensity:

- **The Pacific storm system.** Pacific Northwest winter storms originate in the North Pacific as extratropical cyclones -- low-pressure systems that develop where the cold polar jet stream meets warm Pacific air masses. These cyclones track east-northeast across the Pacific, making landfall on the Olympic Peninsula as mature storm systems. The Olympic Peninsula, projecting into the Pacific, is the first significant land mass these storms encounter.
- **Atmospheric river events.** The most intense precipitation events in the Pacific Northwest are associated with atmospheric rivers -- long, narrow corridors of concentrated water vapor in the lower troposphere that connect tropical moisture sources to the Pacific Northwest. When an atmospheric river makes landfall on the Olympic Peninsula, the Hoh Valley receives 3-8 inches of rain in 24-48 hours. These events are the degree-324 storm archetype.
- **Wind at the Hoh.** The Hoh Valley is not the windiest location on the Olympic Peninsula (the coast and the mountain ridges are windier), but wind speeds during major storm events reach 30-50 mph at the valley floor, with gusts to 60+ mph. At canopy level, where the trees intercept and redirect the airflow, wind speeds are higher and more turbulent. The wind in the valley during a major storm is not a steady flow; it is turbulent, gusty, and variable in direction as the storm system's circulation moves through.
- **The convergence zone.** During winter storm events, the Olympic Mountains can create their own localized precipitation enhancement. The orographic lifting that produces the Hoh's extreme precipitation can also create convergence zones -- areas where air flows from both sides of the peninsula converge and rise, producing additional precipitation above and beyond what the storm system delivers. The Hoh Valley's position on the lee side of the main Olympic crest means it can receive both direct Pacific precipitation and convergence-enhanced precipitation simultaneously.
- **Duration.** Hoh winter storms are not brief events. A major Pacific cyclone tracks through in 24-48 hours at the surface; but the associated weather patterns often produce 3-7 days of storm conditions as successive frontal systems follow. The acoustic event documented at degree 324 is not a single storm; it is the winter condition -- the sustained character of the Hoh Rainforest from November through March when every day brings some component of the four-layer soundscape.

The degree-324 Hoh winter storm calibrates to E=8 through maximum completeness of all acoustic layers:

- **Wind amplitude.** Wind at 40-50 mph in the canopy produces continuous aerodynamic sound at 70-85 dB at forest-floor level; gusts and trunk flex events produce brief excursions to 90+ dB. The wind layer adds significant low-frequency energy (20-200 Hz from trunk flex) that is felt as whole-body vibration, not just heard.
- **Creek/river amplitude.** The Hoh River at storm-peak discharge -- when winter rain raises the river 3-5 feet above base level -- produces a continuous broadband sound (80-90 dB at 20 meters from the bank) that covers 100-2,000 Hz with dominant energy in the 200-800 Hz range. The river sound is the acoustic constant: it was there before the storm began and will continue after it ends; but during the storm, it is at maximum amplitude.

- **The superposition.** All four layers simultaneously at storm intensity: the combined acoustic environment in the Hoh Rainforest during a major winter storm event approaches 95-105 dB at many measurement points within the forest. The frequency coverage is 20 Hz to 16,000 Hz -- essentially the full audible spectrum. The temporal character is complex: continuous components (rain, river, aerodynamic wind noise) combine with irregular percussive events (branch fall, trunk flex spikes, canopy collisions) and rhythmic components (wind gusts cycling every 5-15 seconds). The Hoh E=8 is the E=8 of completeness rather than the E=8 of intensity. This is the most acoustically complete entry in the engine.

## THE HOH RAINFOREST IN THE ENGINE

### The Most Remote Pacific Northwest Wilderness

The Hoh Rainforest is the engine's deepest wilderness acoustic setting:

- **Olympic National Park.** The Hoh Rainforest is within Olympic National Park -- the UNESCO World Heritage Site and International Biosphere Reserve that protects the full ecological range of the Olympic Peninsula, from the Pacific coast to the alpine peaks. The Hoh Rainforest unit of the park is accessible by road to the visitor center (at the end of Upper Hoh Road), but the wilderness interior of the Hoh valley -- above the visitor center, up the river trail toward Mt. Olympus base camp -- is accessible only on foot. The degree-324 acoustic event is best documented at the remote interior, not at the road-accessible visitor area.
- **The North Shore / Hall of Mosses.** The Hall of Mosses trail (a 0.8-mile loop from the visitor center) is the most visited section of the Hoh Rainforest -- the classic Big Leaf Maple and club moss setting. During a winter storm, this accessible location already delivers a significant multi-layer acoustic experience. The degree-324 maximum requires penetrating further into the valley -- past the Hoh Lake junction, along the river trail where the forest is older, the canopy is taller, and the distance from road noise is complete.
- **No light pollution, minimal noise pollution.** The Hoh Rainforest interior is among the lowest light-pollution and noise-pollution environments in the contiguous United States. The nearest significant human noise source is the Upper Hoh Road, which ends at the visitor center. Beyond that, the only acoustic intrusions are aircraft (occasional, above the canopy) and the rare backcountry camper. The natural acoustic environment is essentially uncontaminated.
- **One Square Inch of Silence.** The "One Square Inch of Silence" project (founded by acoustic ecologist Gordon Hempton) designates a specific location in the Hoh Rainforest (a small red stone placed on a moss-covered log near the Three Lakes trail junction) as a reference point for the preservation of natural quiet. Hempton has documented the Hoh as one of the quietest places in the country under standard conditions -- and one of the most acoustically complex during winter storms. The degree-324 entry is the winter storm complement to the Hempton silence documentation: the same place, the opposite condition.

## FOUR LAYERS -- DETAILED ACOUSTIC DOCUMENTATION

### The Complete Acoustic Record

A systematic documentation of all four layers in the winter storm context:

**LAYER 1 -- RAIN: the omnidirectional surface response**
- Frequency range: 1,000-16,000 Hz (fundamental); peak energy 2,000-6,000 Hz
- Spatial character: omnidirectional; every horizontal surface is a transducer
- Notable sub-textures: (a) rain on Sitka Spruce bark -- muffled, medium amplitude, the bark's fibrous texture absorbs some impact energy; (b) rain on moss -- soft, low amplitude, high frequency absorbed by moss fiber; (c) rain on standing water -- the characteristic ring-and-splash of drop on water surface, 4,000-12,000 Hz; (d) rain on Big Leaf Maple leaves (in October-November when leaves remain; bare branches from December) -- high amplitude, crisp impact; (e) rain on the river -- indistinguishable from river turbulence at moderate rain intensity
- Dynamic variation with wind: wind-driven rain changes drop angle from vertical (maximum surface impact) to oblique; wind gusts modulate the rain sound rapidly

**LAYER 2 -- WIND: the vertical energy cascade**
- Frequency range: 20-4,000 Hz (structural and aerodynamic combined)
- Spatial character: top-down; wind enters at canopy, cascades down through forest layers
- Sub-textures: (a) Sitka Spruce crown motion -- 300-800 Hz, continuous aerodynamic; (b) trunk flex -- 20-100 Hz, episodic; most dramatic in old-growth trees where trunk diameter creates long resonant structures; (c) bigleaf maple limb oscillation -- higher frequency than spruce due to smaller mass, 200-500 Hz; (d) understory fern motion -- high frequency, very low amplitude, audible only close to the plant; (e) Aeolian tone -- when specific wind speeds create resonance in hollow snags or cavities in large trees, a pure tone may emerge from the noise
- Gust structure: Pacific Northwest winter storms have a characteristic gust frequency of 5-15 seconds between gust peaks; the canopy responds to this gust structure with a corresponding rise-fall in aerodynamic sound amplitude

**LAYER 3 -- HOH RIVER: the constant depth**
- Frequency range: 100-4,000 Hz; dominant energy 200-800 Hz
- Spatial character: directional from river channel; filling the valley floor with the river's continuous presence
- Sub-textures: (a) main channel flow -- the continuous broadband roar of deep fast-moving water; (b) channel margins -- where slower water meets the bank, different turbulence character, slightly higher frequency from more complex flow patterns; (c) tributaries -- each tributary has its own pitch, determined by its volume and gradient; small tributaries (steep gradient, small volume) produce higher-pitched sounds than the main stem; (d) log jam dynamics -- during storm events, the extensive log jams that characterize the Hoh River shift; the sound of a large log rolling against another in the current is a percussive low-frequency event
- Relationship to storm intensity: the Hoh rises visibly and audibly during a major rain event; there is a 4-8 hour lag between maximum rainfall and maximum river flow (the valley's time of concentration); during this lag, the rain layer is at maximum while the river continues to rise, producing the maximum combined amplitude of rain + river simultaneously

**LAYER 4 -- CANOPY: the structural voice**
- Frequency range: 20-8,000 Hz (from trunk flex sub-bass to needle aerodynamics high mid)
- Spatial character: top layer; defines the upper acoustic boundary
- Sub-textures: (a) Sitka Spruce trunk flex -- a 60-meter-tall Sitka Spruce with a 3-meter diameter trunk flexing in a 40 mph gust produces a deep creak at 20-60 Hz, sustained for 3-10 seconds, felt through the ground; (b) crown contact -- when adjacent old-growth crowns touch in a gust, the resulting impact produces a brief broadband event; (c) branch fall -- the most dramatic acoustic event in the canopy: a large branch (20-50 kg, 4-8 meters long) falling through the layers produces a distinctive descending acoustic signature: first the creak and snap of the branch releasing, then the cascade of impacts as it passes through intermediate canopy layers, then the wet thud of impact on the moss-covered forest floor; total duration: 2-6 seconds; frequency: starts high (the snap: 1,000-4,000 Hz), descends through impact frequencies, ends with the dull low thud of ground impact (100-500 Hz); (d) snag fall -- whole dead standing trees (snags) are most likely to fall during winter storms; a full snag fall is one of the highest-amplitude acoustic events in the winter forest, potentially reaching 100+ dB at close range
