The Most Complete PNW Soundscape in the Engine

The Hoh winter storm is the only entry in the engine that documents all four primary acoustic layers of the temperate rainforest simultaneously and at full storm intensity. Rain on every surface. Wind through the full canopy column. Hoh River and tributaries at storm height. The great Sitka Spruce trunks flexing in the Pacific air, branch fall cascading through the layers, the deep 20–60 Hz vibration of old-growth wood under load. All four layers. All channels open. 20 Hz to 16,000 Hz. The Pacific Northwest acoustic ecology at maximum expression.

The Hoh Rainforest sits in the Hoh River valley, oriented east-west perpendicular to the prevailing Pacific storm track. Moist Pacific air is channeled directly from the ocean into the valley interior, producing one of the highest precipitation totals in the contiguous United States. The dominant trees are Sitka Spruce, Western Hemlock, Western Red Cedar, and Big Leaf Maple — the latter draped in curtains of club moss (Selaginella oregana) that give the forest its characteristic appearance and its acoustic character: moss absorbs high-frequency reflection, giving the winter storm soundscape a muffled intimate quality beneath the layers’ combined amplitude.

The forest floor during winter rain: moss saturated, thin water sheets running over sphagnum, the soft continuous sound of moss runoff adding a sublayer between individual drops. The moss changes the acoustic properties of the rain itself: rain on wet Hoh moss sounds different from rain on rock, bark, or standing water. The forest has been tuning its acoustic environment through the moss layer for centuries.

Branch Fall — The Canopy’s Signature Event

The most dramatic acoustic event in the winter forest canopy: a large branch (20–50 kg, 4–8 meters long) releasing from a Sitka Spruce 50–80 meters overhead and falling through the full canopy column. The acoustic signature: (1) the creak and snap of release (1,000–4,000 Hz, brief); (2) the cascade of impacts through intermediate canopy layers, each slightly lower in pitch as the branch slows and loses height; (3) the wet thud of impact on the moss-covered forest floor (100–500 Hz). Total duration: 2–6 seconds. This descending acoustic signature — high-to-low, quick-to-slow, sharp-to-dull — is uniquely identifiable in the winter storm context.

One Square Inch of Silence — The Same Place, Opposite Condition

The “One Square Inch of Silence” project (acoustic ecologist Gordon Hempton) designates a specific location in the Hoh Rainforest interior as a reference for natural quiet preservation. Hempton has documented the Hoh as one of the quietest places in the contiguous United States under standard conditions. Degree 324 documents the same place in the opposite condition: the winter storm maximum. Quiet and loud: both are the Hoh. The forest’s acoustic character is defined by both poles.
