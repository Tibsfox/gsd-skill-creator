## THE MUSICAL LAUGHING CALL -- ANATOMY AND ACOUSTICS

### A Sound Unlike Any Other PNW Goose

The Greater White-fronted Goose's vocalization is the most musically distinctive of all PNW geese:

- **Character:** A high-pitched, laughing, yodeling call -- "kla-ha" or "kow-yow" or "kla-hah-luk." The call has a **liquid, musical quality** with a distinctive laughing cadence. Two to three syllables, with pitch rising on the second syllable. The "laughing" description is apt -- the sound genuinely resembles human laughter, especially when multiple birds call simultaneously.
- **Frequency range:** 500-3,000 Hz. Higher than Canada Goose (200-2,500 Hz) and Brant (200-1,200 Hz). The higher frequency range contributes to the bright, carrying quality. The pitch is centered in a range that is musically pleasant to the human ear.
- **Production mechanism:** The syrinx structure produces the distinctive pitch modulation -- the rising and falling that creates the "laughing" quality. The call is more complex in structure than the simpler honks and cackles of *Branta* geese.
- **Flock chorus:** A flock of Greater White-fronted Geese produces a distinctively **musical, chattering, laughing chorus** -- often described as sounding like a distant cocktail party or a group of people laughing. The collective sound is one of the most pleasant goose sounds in the PNW.
- **Flight calls:** In flight, the laughing call is the primary vocalization. Flocks of Greater White-fronted Geese in V-formation produce a chorus of laughing calls that is immediately distinguishable from Canada Goose honking -- lighter, higher, more musical.

## THE SPECKLEBELLY -- IDENTIFICATION AND FIELD MARKS

### A Distinctive Gray Goose

The Greater White-fronted Goose's appearance is distinctly different from the *Branta* geese (129-131):

- **Plumage:** Gray-brown overall. **No black head or neck** (unlike all three Branta geese). The head and neck are gray-brown, same shade as the body. The white "front" (white band at the base of the pink-orange bill) is the diagnostic field mark -- giving the species its name.
- **Belly markings:** Dark bars and blotches on the belly, creating a "speckled" appearance. This is the source of the nickname "specklebelly" -- a name widely used by hunters and birders in the field. The belly markings are variable and can range from sparse bars to heavy blotching.
- **Bill:** Pink-orange bill with white band at the base. The bill color is unique among PNW geese -- *Branta* species all have black bills.
- **Legs:** Orange legs and feet. Another distinctive feature -- *Branta* species have black legs.
- **Size:** Medium-sized goose, 64-81 cm body length, 1.9-3.3 kg. Larger than Cackling Goose and Brant, smaller than Canada Goose. Falls in the mid-range of the goose quartet.

### The Genus Shift: Branta to Anser

Degree 132 marks a **genus shift** in the goose era:

- **Branta (degrees 129-131):** The black geese. Black heads, black bills, black legs. White cheek patches (Canada, Cackling) or white necklace (Brant). The common feature is dark plumage, especially on the head and neck.
- **Anser (degree 132):** The gray geese. Gray-brown heads, pink/orange bills, orange legs. No black head or white cheek patch. The Greater White-fronted Goose looks fundamentally different from the *Branta* geese -- a visual shift that parallels the acoustic shift (laughing vs. honking/cackling/murmuring).

The genus shift from *Branta* to *Anser* is analogous to the earlier shift from *Cygnus* (swans, 127-128) to *Branta* (geese, 129-131). The Anatidae streak continues to sample the family's generic diversity.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014)

## PNW WINTER PRESENCE -- AGRICULTURAL FIELDS

### Winter Range

The Greater White-fronted Goose is primarily a **winter visitor** to the PNW:

- **Skagit Valley, Washington:** The Skagit Valley hosts Greater White-fronted Geese in agricultural fields alongside Canada Geese, Snow Geese, and Trumpeter/Tundra Swans. Greater White-fronted Geese are less numerous than Canada Geese but present in small to moderate flocks.
- **Willamette Valley, Oregon:** Agricultural fields in the Willamette Valley host wintering Greater White-fronted Geese, often mixed with Cackling Geese and Canada Geese. The Willamette Valley is a significant concentration area.
- **Ridgefield NWR, Washington:** The national wildlife refuge along the Columbia River hosts wintering Greater White-fronted Geese in its agricultural and wetland habitats.
- **Other PNW sites:** Scattered throughout agricultural lowlands in western Washington and Oregon. Less concentrated than Canada Geese (ubiquitous) or Snow Geese (Skagit Valley), but regularly present in appropriate habitat.
- **Breeding range:** Arctic tundra of Alaska and northern Canada. The Pacific population breeds on the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta and North Slope of Alaska. **Purely migratory** -- no PNW resident populations.

### The Specklebelly in the PNW Goose Community

In the PNW's wintering goose community, the Greater White-fronted Goose occupies a specific niche:

- **Less numerous than Canada Goose:** The Canada Goose dominates PNW goose counts with massive resident and migratory populations. Greater White-fronted Geese are uncommon to fairly common but never numerically dominant.
- **Field habitat:** Greater White-fronted Geese use agricultural fields (waste grain, grass, winter wheat) like Canada and Cackling Geese. They are not marine-adapted like Brant.
- **Mixed flocks:** Greater White-fronted Geese are often found in mixed flocks with Canada Geese and Cackling Geese, but they are visually and acoustically distinct -- the gray plumage, orange bill, and laughing call separate them immediately.
- **"The one that sounds different":** For birders checking goose flocks in winter agricultural fields, the Greater White-fronted Goose is often detected first by its **distinctive laughing call** before being located visually in a mixed flock. Voice is the fastest detector.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; eBird data; WDFW)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE ARCTIC FIELD GOOSE

### Breeding Ecology

The Greater White-fronted Goose's breeding ecology reflects its Arctic heritage:

- **Arctic tundra:** Breeds on wet Arctic tundra, typically near lakes and river deltas. The Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta in western Alaska is the primary breeding area for the Pacific population. Nests on the ground in depressions lined with plant material and down.
- **Global distribution:** *Anser albifrons* has a circumpolar distribution -- breeding across the Arctic from Greenland through Siberia to Alaska. The North American populations are a fraction of the global population, which is concentrated in Eurasia. This makes the Greater White-fronted Goose one of the most widely distributed waterfowl in the world.
- **Agricultural specialist in winter:** Unlike the Brant (marine specialist), the Greater White-fronted Goose is a **field specialist** in winter -- feeding on waste grain, grass, winter wheat, and root crops in agricultural landscapes. This agricultural adaptation makes it less ecologically constrained than the Brant but more habitat-specific than the generalist Canada Goose.
- **Population status:** The global population is large and stable. The North American population has fluctuated but is generally healthy. Not a conservation concern. The agricultural adaptation provides reliable food resources in winter.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Ely & Dzubin 1994)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Most Musical Goose Call

The Greater White-fronted Goose's laughing call is the most musically distinctive of any PNW goose:

- **The laugh spectrum:** High-pitched, yodeling, liquid. "Kla-ha" or "kow-yow" at 500-3,000 Hz. The highest-pitched and most musically complex of the four goose calls documented (129-132). The pitch modulation -- rising and falling within each call -- creates the laughing quality.
- **Flock texture:** The collective laughing of a Greater White-fronted Goose flock creates a **musical, chattering chorus** distinctly different from the rolling honk wall (Canada), the rapid chattering (Cackling), or the continuous gabble (Brant). The laughing quality is immediately recognizable.
- **Flight chorus:** In flight, the laughing calls are lighter and higher than any Branta goose flyover. A flock of Greater White-fronted Geese passing overhead sounds fundamentally different from a Canada Goose V-formation -- lighter, more musical, more cheerful.
- **Detection tool:** In mixed goose flocks, the Greater White-fronted Goose is often **first detected by voice** -- the laughing call stands out among the deeper honks and sharper cackles of the more numerous Branta geese.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
