## Taxonomy: The Water Ouzel

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Cinclidae (Dippers)
- **Genus:** *Cinclus* (Borkhausen, 1797)
- **Species:** *Cinclus mexicanus* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Common Name:** American Dipper (formerly: Water Ouzel)

### Etymology

*Cinclus* -- from Ancient Greek *kinklos*, a small tail-wagging waterside bird [1, 2, 5, 8]. The exact identity of Aristotle's *kinklos* is uncertain, but it was likely a wagtail or similar water-associated species. The genus name captures the defining behavioral trait: the constant bobbing and dipping motion that gives the family its common name.

*mexicanus* -- Latin, meaning "of Mexico" [1, 2, 5]. English zoologist William Swainson described the species in 1827 based on a specimen collected in Mexico by English naturalist William Bullock. The type locality is Temascaltepec de Gonzalez in the State of Mexico [1, 2, 5]. The name reflects the collecting locality, not the species' primary range -- American Dippers range from Alaska to Panama, with Mexico near the center.

Full translation: **"The tail-wagging waterside bird of Mexico"** -- a name that connects the ancient Greek observation of bobbing waterside birds to a specimen collected in the mountains of central Mexico. Where *Certhia americana* (degree 51) honored an entire continent, *Cinclus mexicanus* honors a specific nation at the center of the species' range.

### The Name "Dipper"

The common name "dipper" comes from the bird's most distinctive behavioral trait: the constant **bobbing or dipping motion** performed while perched on rocks in or beside streams [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. The bird bends its legs and dips its body in a rhythmic, curtsey-like motion -- up to 40-60 times per minute. This behavior is performed by all five species in the family Cinclidae worldwide. The function is debated: it may signal territory ownership to conspecifics, it may help the bird judge distances over moving water, or it may serve to blend the bird's silhouette with the bobbing motion of water flowing over rocks [1, 2, 5].

### The Name "Water Ouzel"

The American Dipper was historically called the **water ouzel** -- the name used by John Muir and most 19th-century naturalists [1, 2, 5, 14]. "Ouzel" (also "ousel") derives from Old English *osle*, originally referring to the Eurasian Blackbird (*Turdus merula*). The name was transferred to the dipper because early naturalists perceived a resemblance to the dark-plumaged thrushes. The American Ornithologists' Union officially standardized the name "American Dipper" in the 20th century, but "water ouzel" persists in literary and historical contexts, particularly in the writings of John Muir [14, 15].

### Cinclidae Worldwide

The family Cinclidae contains five species, all in the genus *Cinclus* [1, 2, 5, 8]:

1. **White-throated Dipper** (*Cinclus cinclus*) -- Europe, western Asia, North Africa
2. **Brown Dipper** (*Cinclus pallasii*) -- Central and eastern Asia
3. **American Dipper** (*Cinclus mexicanus*) -- Western North America through Central America
4. **White-capped Dipper** (*Cinclus leucocephalus*) -- South America (Andes)
5. **Rufous-throated Dipper** (*Cinclus schulzii*) -- South America (Argentina, Bolivia)

All five species share the same aquatic adaptations, the same dipping behavior, the same dome-shaped nests near water, and the same dependence on clean, fast-flowing streams. The American Dipper is the only species in the Western Hemisphere north of South America.

## The Sound: A Loud Bubbling Wren-Like Song Over Rushing Water

### Song Structure

The American Dipper's song is a **loud, complex, sustained series of high-pitched trills, buzzes, and melodious warbling phrases** that can continue for extended bouts lasting up to 10 minutes [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 11]. The song has been described as "wren-like" in its complexity and bubbling quality, but it is delivered at a volume and intensity far exceeding any wren -- because it must be heard over the roar of rushing water [1, 2, 3, 7].

The song's structure [1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 11]:

1. **Complex, varied phrases** -- the song consists of a mixture of high whistles, trills, and buzzy notes strung together in extended, varied sequences. No simple mnemonic captures it; the song is improvisatory and variable, more like a mockingbird's extended performance than a simple territorial signal [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].
2. **Frequency range:** 2.0-8.0 kHz, with most energy concentrated in the 3.0-6.0 kHz range [6, 11]. This frequency range is well above the dominant frequencies of stream noise (which peaks below 1 kHz for most whitewater), placing the dipper's song in a spectral window that cuts through the ambient sound of rushing water [6, 11].
3. **Duration:** Individual song bouts can last 1-10 minutes. Confrontation songs during territorial disputes can exceed 5 minutes of continuous singing [1, 2, 6]. This is among the longest sustained vocal performances in the engine.
4. **Both sexes sing** -- unlike most passerines, BOTH male and female American Dippers sing year-round [1, 2, 5, 6, 7]. Female song is documented and functional, used in pair coordination and territory defense.
5. **Sings year-round, including through winter** -- one of the very few North American songbirds that sings through the coldest months, even while standing on ice-covered rocks beside frozen streams [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 14]. John Muir noted this with particular admiration: the bird that sings "while icicles still hang thickly from frozen waterfalls" [14, 15].

### Call Notes

The American Dipper has several distinct call types [1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 11]:

- **Jik call:** A sharp, buzzy *jik* or *dzit*. Duration ~46 ms. Frequency 4.0-5.5 kHz [6]. The primary contact and alarm call. Given in flight, often as the bird zips low over the stream surface. Audible over considerable stream noise.
- **Grawk call:** A lower, harsher *grawk* or *zreet*. Duration ~88 ms. Frequency 3.0-4.5 kHz [6]. Given during close-range territorial confrontations. More aggressive than the jik.
- **Weep call:** A softer *weep* given by the female at the nest. Frequency 4.0-6.0 kHz [6]. Similar in length to the grawk but higher and more plaintive.

### The 53rd Acoustic Element: The Torrent-Song

The American Dipper introduces the engine's 53rd acoustic element (species side): **the torrent-song**.

The torrent-song is the organizing principle of the American Dipper's sound -- a **loud, complex, sustained, bubbling series of trills and warbles that must compete with the roar of rushing water to be heard**. Every aspect of the dipper's vocalizations is shaped by its acoustic environment: the mountain stream. The song is loud because the stream is loud. The song is high-frequency because stream noise is low-frequency. The song is sustained because brief signals would be lost in the continuous rush. The bird and the water are locked in an acoustic arms race, and the dipper wins by singing louder, higher, and longer than the torrent demands [1, 2, 6, 7].

The torrent-song is distinct from all nearby acoustic elements:

| Element | Character | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| The Preamble (45) | Clear opening before complexity | Positional structure |
| The Rasp (46) | Concentrated buzzy trill | Textural intensity |
| The Snowbird (47) | Simple even trill on single pitch | Elemental reduction |
| The Dialect (48) | Culturally transmitted geographic variant | Cultural transmission |
| The Ballad (49) | Rich melodious whistled statement | Melodious completeness |
| The Memory (50) | Descending pure-toned whistle | Spatial precision |
| The Bark-Spiral (51) | High thin descending sibilant series | Cryptic frequency |
| **The Torrent-Song (52)** | **Loud bubbling sustained trill over rushing water** | **Environmental competition** |

The torrent-song differs from the bark-spiral (degree 51) because the bark-spiral is defined by crypsis -- sound designed to be undetected. The torrent-song is the exact opposite: sound designed to OVERPOWER. The bark-spiral hides in the forest. The torrent-song fights the river and wins. The Brown Creeper whispers; the American Dipper shouts. Both are E=1 vs. E=3, but the acoustic strategy is diametrically opposed: invisibility vs. dominance.

Named "the torrent-song" for three resonances:
1. **The torrent itself** -- the American Dipper lives on torrents, fast-flowing mountain streams where water crashes over boulders and thunders down falls. The song must COMPETE with this torrent to communicate. The torrent-song is the voice that defeats the river [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].
2. **The torrent of notes** -- the song is itself a torrent. Up to 10 minutes of continuous, complex, bubbling vocalization -- a flood of sound that matches the flood of water. The acoustic output mirrors the habitat [1, 2, 6].
3. **The torrent-song as water quality signal** -- the presence of the torrent-song indicates clean, well-oxygenated water. Where the song falls silent, the stream has been degraded. The torrent-song is an acoustic certificate of water health [7, 9, 10, 16, 17].

## Walking Underwater: The Sole North American Strategy

### The Foraging Behavior

The American Dipper is the **only songbird in North America** that forages by walking, swimming, and diving in fast-flowing water [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 12, 13]:

1. **Wades into the current** -- the bird walks into shallow riffles, submerging partially or completely, using its wings to "fly" underwater against the current [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].
2. **Dives from the surface or from rocks** -- the bird plunges into deeper water from a perch or from the stream surface, tucking its wings and entering headfirst [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].
3. **Walks on the stream bottom** -- once submerged, the dipper walks along the streambed, gripping rocks with its strong toes, using its wings to maintain position against the current [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 12, 13]. The bird can remain submerged for 15-30 seconds on a typical dive.
4. **Forages by flipping rocks** -- the dipper turns over small pebbles and stones on the stream bottom to find aquatic insect larvae (particularly caddisfly, stonefly, and mayfly larvae -- the EPT taxa) hidden underneath [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].
5. **Surfaces and returns to a rock** -- after foraging, the bird pops back to the surface and returns to a mid-stream boulder to consume its prey, often dipping and bobbing in the characteristic motion [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].

No other passerine does this. No other songbird anywhere in the world outside the five species of Cinclidae walks on the bottom of streams. The American Dipper has evolved a suite of anatomical and physiological adaptations that make this unique foraging strategy possible.

### Physical Adaptations for Aquatic Life

The American Dipper's body is a catalog of adaptations for life in and under fast-flowing water [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 12, 13]:

- **Nictitating membrane (third eyelid)** -- a highly developed translucent membrane that can be drawn across the eye to protect it while allowing underwater vision. Functions as a built-in goggle [1, 2, 5, 7, 12, 13].
- **Nasal flaps** -- movable, scaled flaps that seal the nostrils shut when the bird dives, preventing water inhalation [1, 2, 5, 7, 12, 13].
- **Extra-large preen (uropygial) gland** -- approximately ten times larger than that of a similar-sized passerine. Produces copious oil that the bird spreads through its feathers during preening, creating a waterproof barrier [1, 2, 5, 7, 12, 13]. The thick, oily plumage traps a layer of air against the body when submerged, providing both insulation against cold mountain water and a silvery sheen of trapped air bubbles visible when the bird is underwater.
- **Higher hemoglobin concentration** -- the blood carries more hemoglobin than typical passerines, increasing the oxygen-carrying capacity and allowing the bird to remain submerged for extended periods [1, 2, 5, 12, 13].
- **Low metabolic rate** -- lower than expected for a bird of its size, helping maintain body temperature in cold water [1, 2, 5, 12].
- **Dense, waterproof plumage** -- the feather coat is extremely dense, approximately 50% more feathers per unit area than a terrestrial passerine of similar size [1, 2, 5, 12, 13].
- **Strong legs and feet** -- proportionally larger and stronger than typical passerines. The toes are equipped with strong, curved claws for gripping wet, slippery rock surfaces [1, 2, 5, 12].
- **Compact, rotund body** -- the body shape minimizes surface area relative to volume, reducing heat loss in cold water. The short, stubby tail and wings reduce drag [1, 2, 5, 12].

### The Dipping Behavior

The characteristic "dipping" or "bobbing" motion is the signature behavior that gives the family its name [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]:

- While perched on a rock in or beside the stream, the bird rhythmically bends its legs and lowers its body, then straightens back up -- a rapid curtsey-like motion.
- The rate can reach 40-60 dips per minute.
- Both adults and juveniles dip.
- The function is debated: territorial signaling, depth perception over moving water, or visual camouflage (matching the motion of water flowing over rocks).
- The white nictitating membrane flashes visibly when the bird blinks during dipping, creating a rapid white "flash" that is conspicuous even at a distance [18]. David Sibley (2013) has noted that the white eyelid flash may function as a visual signal, analogous to the white tail-flash of the Dark-eyed Junco.

## John Muir's Favorite Bird

### "The Mountain Streams' Own Darling"

The American Dipper holds a unique place in American natural history as the favorite bird of **John Muir**, the father of the national parks movement. Muir devoted an entire chapter of his 1894 book *The Mountains of California* -- Chapter 13, "The Water-Ouzel" -- to a loving, detailed portrait of the dipper's life history [14, 15].

Muir's admiration was unequivocal:

> "He is the mountain streams' own darling, the humming-bird of blooming waters, loving rocky ripple-slopes and sheets of foam as a bee loves flowers, as a lark loves sunshine and meadows. Among all the mountain birds, none has cheered me so much in my lonely wanderings, -- none so unfailingly." [14]

> "He is the bird of all birds the most remarkable of all the birdlings of the Sierra Nevada." [14]

Muir described the dipper singing through winter storms, diving into icy torrents, building its mossy dome nest behind waterfalls, and maintaining its cheerful disposition under conditions that would silence every other bird. For Muir, the water ouzel embodied the essential character of the Sierra Nevada mountains -- hardy, joyful, inseparable from the wild water.

The Muir connection places the American Dipper at the intersection of ornithology and American conservation history. Muir's chapter on the water ouzel in *The Mountains of California* is among the most celebrated pieces of nature writing in the English language. The dipper is not just a bird; it is a symbol of wilderness itself.

### The Muir-Dipper Connection to the PNW

While Muir wrote primarily about Sierra Nevada dippers, the American Dipper ranges throughout the mountain streams of the Pacific Northwest -- the same watersheds that drain into Puget Sound. Every clear, cold stream in the Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Range supports dippers. The bird Muir loved in Yosemite is the same bird that sings on the Snoqualmie, the Skykomish, the Nooksack, and every mountain tributary that feeds the Sound.

## The Dome Nest: Architecture Over Water

### Nest Construction

The American Dipper builds one of the most distinctive nests of any North American bird [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 12, 15]:

- **Structure:** A globe-shaped or dome-shaped structure approximately 30 cm (12 inches) in diameter, with a side entrance low on one side facing the water [1, 2, 5, 7, 15].
- **Materials:** The outer shell is constructed primarily of green, living moss, which continues to grow even after incorporation into the nest structure [1, 2, 5, 7, 15]. Inner layers include coarse grasses, rootlets, and leaves that resist wetting. The thick mossy shell (3-5 cm thick) absorbs moisture from spray while keeping the interior dry and insulated [1, 2, 5, 15].
- **Location:** On a rock ledge, bridge abutment, or cliff face immediately over or beside fast-flowing water [1, 2, 5, 7, 15]. Often placed behind a waterfall, so the bird must fly through the curtain of falling water to access the nest. Also placed under bridges, on midstream boulders, and on mossy rock walls in spray zones [1, 2, 5, 7, 15].
- **Spray requirement:** Many nest sites are continuously wet from flying spray. The nest is adapted for this: the living moss outer shell thrives in the wet environment, and the water-resistant inner lining keeps the eggs and chicks dry [1, 2, 5, 15].
- **Eggs:** 3-5, white, unmarked [1, 2, 5].
- **Incubation:** 15-17 days, female only [1, 2, 5].
- **Nestling period:** 24-25 days -- long for a passerine of this size [1, 2, 5].
- **Broods:** 1-2 per season [1, 2, 5].

The dome nest behind a waterfall is one of the most dramatic nesting strategies in North American ornithology. The nest is almost impossible for terrestrial predators to reach -- it is protected by the rushing water itself. Like the Brown Creeper's bark-flap hammock (degree 51), the dipper's nest uses the physical environment as camouflage and defense. But where the creeper hides behind bark, the dipper hides behind water.

## Water Quality: The Living Indicator

### The Biomonitoring Connection

The American Dipper is one of the most reliable **biological indicators of stream health** in western North America [7, 9, 10, 16, 17]:

**Why dippers indicate water quality:**
- Dippers feed almost exclusively on **EPT taxa** -- Ephemeroptera (mayflies), Plecoptera (stoneflies), and Trichoptera (caddisflies). These aquatic insects are themselves indicators of clean, well-oxygenated water. The presence of robust EPT communities indicates high water quality; their absence indicates degradation [7, 9, 16, 17].
- Because dippers **positively select EPT taxa** for their diet, their presence and breeding success directly reflect the health of the aquatic invertebrate community, which in turn reflects the health of the stream [7, 9, 16, 17].
- Dipper **breeding density, territory size, and reproductive success** all correlate with water quality metrics. On clean streams, territories are smaller (more pairs per kilometer) and reproductive output is higher. On degraded streams, territories expand (fewer pairs) and reproduction declines [7, 9, 16, 17].
- Dippers have been used as **bioindicators for selenium contamination** in coal mine-affected streams in western Canada [16], **trace metal exposure** in British Columbia [17], and **contaminant biotransport** via salmon carcasses in Pacific Northwest watersheds [10].

**The acoustic consequence:** The presence of the torrent-song -- the American Dipper's loud, sustained, bubbling song -- is an acoustic certificate of water health. Where the torrent-song is heard, the stream is clean. Where it falls silent, something has gone wrong. The American Dipper's song is not just a territorial signal; it is an ecological report card delivered in music.

### The EPT Connection

The macroinvertebrate orders that sustain the dipper are among the most widely used bioindicators in freshwater ecology [7, 9, 16, 17]:

| Order | Common Name | Indicator Role | Dipper Diet Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ephemeroptera | Mayflies | Highly sensitive to pollution | Primary prey (larvae) |
| Plecoptera | Stoneflies | Most sensitive to degradation | Primary prey (larvae) |
| Trichoptera | Caddisflies | Moderately sensitive | Primary prey (larvae and cases) |

The EPT Index -- the number of EPT taxa present in a stream sample -- is the standard rapid bioassessment metric used by the EPA and state agencies to evaluate water quality. The American Dipper is, in effect, a feathered EPT sampler: its presence integrates the aquatic invertebrate community over time in a way that snapshot surveys cannot.

## Plumage: The Gray Waterproof Suit

### Physical Description

The American Dipper is a compact, plump, short-tailed passerine with uniformly dark plumage [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 12]:

**Plumage:**
- **Overall:** Uniform slate-gray to dark brownish-gray. No wing bars, no streaking, no patches of contrasting color [1, 2, 5, 7]. The most uniformly colored bird in the engine. Where every previous species has had some diagnostic marking -- a stripe, a patch, a cap, a wing bar -- the dipper has nothing. It is a solid gray bird.
- **Head:** Slightly darker brown than the body in some individuals. The brown tinge is most apparent in fresh plumage [1, 2, 5].
- **Underparts:** Slightly paler gray than upperparts. No markings [1, 2, 5].
- **Bill:** Short, straight, dark. Approximately 18-20 mm. Not decurved like the Brown Creeper (degree 51) or chisel-like like the nuthatch (degree 5) -- it is a general-purpose insectivore bill adapted for picking prey off underwater surfaces [1, 2, 5].
- **Tail:** Short and stubby. Often cocked upward, wren-like [1, 2, 5, 7].
- **Eyes:** Dark brown. The white nictitating membrane (third eyelid) creates a conspicuous white "flash" when the bird blinks, visible even at a distance [1, 2, 5, 18].
- **Legs and feet:** Proportionally large and strong. Pale pinkish-gray to brownish. Strong curved claws [1, 2, 5, 12].

**Size:** Length: 16.5-18 cm (6.5-7.1 in). Wingspan: 23-25 cm (9-10 in). Weight: **46-64 grams** (1.6-2.3 oz) [1, 2, 5].

The American Dipper at 46-64 grams is the **heaviest bird in the engine since the Northern Spotted Owl** (degree 4). It is substantially heavier than every songbird species in degrees 5-51. The compact, dense body is a thermal adaptation: the greater mass-to-surface-area ratio reduces heat loss in cold mountain water.

### Field Identification

The American Dipper is **unmistakable** in its habitat. No other bird perches on rocks in the middle of rushing mountain streams, bobs up and down continuously, and dives underwater. The combination of:
- Uniform gray plumage
- Constant dipping/bobbing motion
- Mid-stream rock perching
- Diving into fast water
- Short, stubby tail cocked upward
- White eyelid flash when blinking

...identifies the species instantly. The dipper is hard to confuse with anything else because no other bird behaves like this.

## The American Dipper in the PNW

### Distribution in Washington State

The American Dipper is a year-round resident of mountain streams throughout Washington State [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]:

- **Western Washington:** Common on fast-flowing streams in the Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Range. Nests on suitable rivers and streams down to sea level in western Washington, though most common at higher elevations where streams are steepest and coldest [3, 7]. Found on streams draining into Puget Sound -- the Snoqualmie, Skykomish, Sauk, Stillaguamish, Nooksack, and their mountain tributaries.
- **Eastern Washington:** Present on cold mountain streams of the eastern Cascades, Blue Mountains, and Selkirk Mountains [3, 7].
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to above timberline, wherever clear, cold, fast-flowing streams with rocky substrates exist [3, 7].
- **Winter movement:** Some dippers make short altitudinal migrations, moving from high-elevation breeding streams to lower-elevation wintering streams when high-elevation streams freeze. This is not a true migration but a vertical shift within the same watershed [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].

### The Sound of Puget Sound Connection

The project is named for Puget Sound. At degree 52, for the first time, the engine enters the water -- not the saltwater of the Sound itself, but the freshwater tributaries that feed it. Every mountain stream that drains into Puget Sound passes through American Dipper habitat. The dipper occupies the headwaters. Its torrent-song is the first acoustic signal of the watershed that becomes, downstream, the Sound.

The American Dipper does not live ON Puget Sound. It lives ABOVE Puget Sound -- in the mountain streams that create it. The water that flows past a dipping, bobbing, singing dipper on a Cascade stream will, days or weeks later, reach the Sound. The dipper is the upstream voice of Puget Sound's watershed.

## E=3: The Stream Energy

### E=3 at Degree 52

## The American Dipper in the Engine's Arc

### Structural Position

Degree 52 carries seven structural contributions to the engine:

1. **The torrent-song -- the 53rd acoustic element (species side).** A loud, complex, sustained, bubbling series of trills and warbles that competes with rushing water. Named for the torrent (the stream habitat), the torrent of notes (up to 10 minutes of continuous song), and the torrent-song as water quality signal (presence indicates clean streams). The first element defined by **environmental competition** -- sound shaped by the need to overpower a natural noise source.

2. **FIRST CINCLIDAE -- the 21st family.** The American Dipper introduces the engine's 21st family. Cinclidae is monotypic in North America -- *Cinclus mexicanus* is the only representative. Five species worldwide, all in genus *Cinclus*. No other Cinclidae will enter the engine. This is the second consecutive new family entry (Certhiidae at 51, Cinclidae at 52).

3. **THE ONLY AQUATIC SONGBIRD.** The American Dipper is the only passerine in North America that forages underwater. It walks on stream bottoms, dives into rapids, and swims through fast-flowing water. No other songbird does this. The engine enters the water for the first time at degree 52.

4. **John Muir's favorite bird.** The American Dipper (water ouzel) received an entire chapter in Muir's *The Mountains of California* (1894). "The mountain streams' own darling." The intersection of ornithology and American conservation history.

5. **Water quality indicator species.** The dipper's presence and breeding success correlate directly with stream health. Where dippers thrive, the water is clean. Where they disappear, the stream is degraded. The torrent-song is an acoustic report card.

6. **The dome nest behind the waterfall.** A globe-shaped nest of living moss, placed on rock ledges in spray zones, sometimes behind waterfalls so the bird must fly through falling water to reach its eggs. The most dramatic nest location of any species in the engine.

7. **CSV substitution documented.** Long-toed Salamander replaced by American Dipper. Eighth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (45-52). The engine continues deep into the amphibian belt of the original CSV.

### Fifteen Consecutive Passeriformes

Degree 52 extends the Passeriformes streak to **fifteen consecutive entries** (degrees 38-52). Forty-five of 53 entries (84.9%) are Passeriformes. The order streak continues. However, the Cinclidae entry is as far from a typical "perching bird" as any Passeriformes can be -- a passerine that dives, swims, and walks underwater. The order streak continues in name, but the ecological territory has shifted dramatically. The engine may still be counting Passeriformes, but it is no longer counting forest birds.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 5]
- **Global population:** ~160,000 breeding birds (Partners in Flight estimate) [1, 2, 5, 7]. Some sources estimate total population around 1.5 million [5].
- **Continental Concern Score:** 12 out of 20 (Partners in Flight) -- moderate conservation concern [7]
- **BBS trend:** Stable to slightly declining, though difficult to monitor due to roadside survey limitations for a stream-dependent species [1, 2, 7]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 [1, 2]
- **Washington State:** Common year-round resident of mountain streams. No special state listing [3, 7].

The American Dipper's conservation story is inseparable from water quality. The bird does not face direct persecution or habitat loss in the traditional sense -- its mountain streams are not being clearcut or paved. But the streams themselves are being degraded: sedimentation from logging and road-building, contamination from mining operations, water temperature increases from climate change, and reduced flow from snowpack decline all threaten the dipper's aquatic food web [1, 2, 7, 9, 16, 17]. The dipper's conservation is ultimately water conservation. Protect the stream, and the dipper protects itself.

## Diet

The American Dipper is primarily insectivorous, specializing in aquatic invertebrates obtained by foraging underwater [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]:

**Primary diet (aquatic invertebrates):**
- Caddisfly larvae (Trichoptera) -- including larvae within their portable cases
- Stonefly nymphs (Plecoptera) -- a primary food source, indicator of cold, clean water
- Mayfly nymphs (Ephemeroptera) -- abundant in clean riffles
- Mosquito and midge larvae (Diptera)
- Aquatic beetle larvae
- Dragonfly and damselfly nymphs (Odonata)
- Worms and leeches

**Secondary diet:**
- Small fish (trout fry, sculpin) -- taken occasionally, especially in winter [1, 2, 5]
- Fish eggs -- particularly salmon and trout eggs during spawning season [1, 2, 5, 10]
- Aquatic snails and other mollusks [1, 2, 5]
- Flying adult insects caught from the air or from rock surfaces [1, 2, 5]

The diet is almost entirely aquatic. The American Dipper is one of very few passerines whose food comes primarily from underwater. This aquatic diet is what makes the dipper such an effective water quality indicator -- the bird cannot eat what the stream does not produce.
