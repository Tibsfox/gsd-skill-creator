## THE SOUND: MASS WING BUZZ OVER WATER -- 96th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Sound

The mayfly mass emergence sound is the **mass wing buzz** of thousands of adult mayflies emerging simultaneously from a body of water [1, 2]:

**The event:** Thousands of mayfly nymphs rise to the water surface simultaneously, split their nymphal exoskeletons, and emerge as winged subimagos (duns). The mass emergence creates a shimmering haze of insects above the water surface. Each individual's wing beats are nearly inaudible, but thousands together produce a distinctive soft, shimmering buzz -- a **mass wing buzz over water**.

**The acoustic character:** A collective shimmering hum. Thousands of delicate wings beating at once. Not a loud buzz like a hornet nest (93) or a rhythmic chirp like a cricket (87). A diffuse, ambient, shimmering sound that fills the air above the water. Moderate amplitude. The sound of thousands of ancient insects completing the transformation from water to air.

**Frequency:** Wing beat frequency approximately 30-50 Hz per individual, but the mass event creates a broad-spectrum shimmer from the combined wing beats, movement, and interaction with the water surface.

### Why E=3

1. **MASS EVENT -- thousands hatching simultaneously.** The collective multiplies the sound.
2. **Individual wing beats are nearly inaudible.** Individual mayfly = barely perceptible.
3. **Moderate energy.** Higher than the stonefly's E=2 (individual, barely audible) because of mass numbers. Lower than cricket chorus E=4 because individual wing beats are far softer than stridulation.
4. **Ambient rather than aggressive.** Not directed, not territorial, not a warning. A byproduct of mass emergence.
5. **Short-lived.** The mass emergence event lasts hours, not days. Ephemeral in every sense.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Wing rustle on stream surface** (degree 94, Perlidae) -- individual, barely audible, E=2. The mass wing buzz multiplies by thousands. Both aquatic insects; different scales.
- **Aggressive papery hum** (degree 93, Vespidae) -- collective and aggressive, E=5. The mass wing buzz is collective but passive. Both collective insect sounds; different intentions.
- **Bumblebee gentle drone** (degree 92, Apidae) -- individual, gentle, continuous. The mass wing buzz is collective, ephemeral, event-driven.

## ECOLOGY: THE EPHEMERAL ADULT AND THE MASS EMERGENCE

### The Ephemeral Adult

**Ephemeroptera** means "lasting a day" -- from Greek *ephemeros* ("short-lived") + *pteron* ("wing") [1]. The name is the biology:

- **Adult lifespan:** Hours to days. Some species live only a few hours as adults.
- **Cannot eat:** Adult mayfly mouthparts are vestigial. They cannot feed.
- **Single purpose:** Emerge, mate, lay eggs, die. The adult stage exists solely for reproduction.
- **Most ephemeral adult in the engine.** No other species has such a brief adult life.

### The Mass Emergence

Mass emergence is the mayfly's survival strategy [1, 2]:

- **Predator satiation:** By emerging simultaneously in massive numbers, mayflies overwhelm predators. Fish, birds, and other predators can only eat a fraction. The majority survive to reproduce.
- **Synchronization:** Emergence is triggered by temperature, photoperiod, and water conditions. Millions can emerge within hours on a single stretch of water.
- **Spectacle:** Major mayfly emergences are visible on weather radar. They can blanket bridges, buildings, and streetlights. One of the most visually dramatic events in freshwater ecology.

### Ancient Lineage

Mayflies are among the most ancient winged insects [1, 2]:

- **Fossil record:** 300+ million years (Carboniferous period). Predates dinosaurs, flowering plants, most other insect orders.
- **Primitive wing design:** Mayflies cannot fold their wings flat against their body (unlike most other insects). This is a primitive trait shared only with dragonflies (Odonata). The wings are held upright at rest.
- **Unique subimago stage:** Mayflies are the only insects that molt after developing functional wings. The subimago (dun) emerges from the water with functional wings, then molts one final time into the imago (spinner) -- the fully adult reproductive form [1].

### Fly Fishing Connection

The entire art and science of **fly fishing** is built on imitating mayfly life stages [3]:

- **Nymph patterns** imitate nymphs on the stream bottom.
- **Emerger patterns** imitate nymphs rising to the surface.
- **Dry flies (dun patterns)** imitate the subimago floating on the surface.
- **Spinner patterns** imitate the spent imago lying on the water after egg-laying.

The mayfly is the foundation species of fly fishing. Every fly fisherman on a trout stream is engaged with mayfly biology.
