## THE CANADA LYNX -- SNOW SPECIALIST

### Built for Winter

The Canada Lynx is among the most specialized predators in the PNW:

- **Snow adaptation.** The lynx's most distinctive feature is its massive paws -- proportionally the largest feet of any felid, evolved for walking on deep snow. The paws function as natural snowshoes, distributing the lynx's weight (8-14 kg) across a large surface area. Where other predators sink, the lynx floats.
- **Body proportions.** Long legs, large paws, short tail, large ear tufts, facial ruff. Every feature is adapted for cold: the short tail minimizes heat loss, the ear tufts funnel sound in snow-muffled forest, the facial ruff acts as a snow shield.
- **Range in Washington.** Canada Lynx in Washington are found primarily in the North Cascades and Okanogan region -- high-elevation coniferous forest with reliable deep snow. The population is small and federally threatened. Estimates suggest fewer than 100 in Washington.
- **Snowshoe hare dependency.** The Canada Lynx is one of the most prey-specialized predators in North America. Snowshoe hares make up 75-97% of the diet. The lynx-hare population cycle -- the famous 10-year boom-bust cycle documented from Hudson's Bay Company fur records since the 1700s -- is one of the best-known predator-prey dynamics in ecology.
- **The 10-year cycle.** Hare populations boom and crash on roughly 10-year cycles. Lynx populations follow 1-2 years behind: when hares are abundant, lynx thrive and reproduce. When hares crash, lynx starve, kitten survival drops, and the population declines. The lynx's fate is tied to the hare.

## THE YOWL AND THE CHATTER

### Two Voices

The Canada Lynx has a distinctive dual vocal identity:

- **The yowl.** Similar to the bobcat's but higher-pitched, more ethereal, and less raspy. Frequency range: 500-2,000 Hz. Duration: 1-4 seconds. Used in mating season and territorial contexts. The lynx yowl has a haunting, almost ghostly quality -- it sounds like it belongs in a snow-covered forest. Less aggressive than the bobcat yowl, more melancholic.
- **The chatter.** A rapid clicking/chattering vocalization: "ck-ck-ck-ck-ck." Frequency: 1,000-4,000 Hz. Duration: 0.5-2 seconds. The chatter is produced when the lynx is excited, typically while tracking prey or watching potential prey from cover. The sound is similar to the chattering that domestic cats make when watching birds through a window -- a frustrated hunting reflex made audible.
- **Combined identity.** The yowl + chatter combination gives the lynx a richer vocal identity than a single call would provide. The yowl is the long-range, emotional, territorial voice. The chatter is the close-range, excited, predatory voice. Together they span the lynx's behavioral spectrum.
- **Snow-muffled context.** In deep-snow boreal forest, sound propagation is heavily dampened. Snow absorbs high frequencies. The lynx's vocalizations must work in an acoustically dead environment -- which may explain the emphasis on lower-frequency yowls for distance and higher-frequency chatters for close range.

## LYNX VS. BOBCAT -- SPECIALIST VS. GENERALIST

### The Same Genus, Different Strategies

The comparison between the two Lynx species reveals divergent evolutionary strategies:

- **Genus Lynx.** Both are genus *Lynx*. The bobcat (*L. rufus*) and Canada Lynx (*L. canadensis*) are the two North American representatives. They can hybridize in zones of overlap.
- **Habitat.** Bobcat: everywhere (forest, brush, desert, suburban). Lynx: boreal forest only. Deep snow, dense conifers, cold. The bobcat is the generalist. The lynx is the specialist.
- **Prey.** Bobcat: diverse (rabbits, rodents, birds, occasionally deer). Lynx: 75-97% snowshoe hares. The bobcat can adapt its diet. The lynx cannot.
- **Population.** Bobcat: 20,000-30,000 in Washington. Lynx: fewer than 100 in Washington. The generalist thrives; the specialist is threatened.
- **Paws.** Bobcat: normal felid paws. Lynx: massive snowshoe paws, 2-3x the surface area of a bobcat's. The paw difference is the clearest physical expression of the specialist strategy.
- **Sound.** Bobcat: raspy, aggressive yowl (400-1,500 Hz, E=5). Lynx: ethereal yowl + hunting chatter (500-2,000 Hz + 1,000-4,000 Hz, E=5). Same energy, different character.
