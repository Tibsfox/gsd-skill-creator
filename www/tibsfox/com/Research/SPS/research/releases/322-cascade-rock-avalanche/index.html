Geophysics Returns — E=8 — Gravity Wins

The Cascade rock avalanche is the acoustic event of gravity converting potential energy into kinetic energy across a rock slope. No biology, no behavior — only fractured rock, steep gradient, and the Cascade summer. The accelerating percussion begins with a single block impact and grows as more mass enters motion, each impact dislodging additional material. The avalanche is self-amplifying: louder, broader in frequency, more massive at the end than at the beginning.

CRACK —— IMPACT —— RUMBLE —— RUMBLE —— RUMBLE
Initiation crack (2,000–8,000 Hz, brief) → first impact (broadband shock wave) → acceleration phase (growing mass, growing volume, 20–2,000 Hz) → deceleration tail (30–120 seconds after primary mass stops)

The Cascade Setting

The Cascade Range produces rock avalanches for specific reasons: the volcanic arc geology creates heterogeneous, structurally complex rock (alternating lava flows, welded tuff, ash deposits, hydrothermally altered zones). Mt. Rainier has extensive zones of clay-mineral replacement where strong crystalline rock has been converted to weak smectite and kaolinite by hydrothermal fluids — these zones are preferential failure sites. The North Cascades crystalline basement produces rock avalanches from glacially oversteepened cliff faces, where post-glacial stress release opens new fractures as glacier support is removed.

Summer is the peak season because: (1) freeze-thaw has progressively widened fractures through the preceding winter; (2) the daily thermal cycle (cold night, warm day) stresses surface rocks; (3) dry conditions remove water cohesion that might otherwise hold marginal blocks in place. The summer Cascade cliff face is a loaded system waiting for the thermal maximum of the afternoon.

Acoustic Profile — The Rock Avalanche Signature

Initiation crack: 2,000–8,000 Hz, 0.1–0.5 seconds. The fracture propagating through the final intact rock mass. High-frequency, brief, preceding the main event.

First impact: Broadband shock wave. 1,000 kg block at 30 m/s delivers ~450,000 joules. Peak at 50–500 Hz. Perceived as thunder crack with low-frequency body component.

Acceleration phase: 20–2,000 Hz continuous rumble. Self-amplifying: each impact dislodges more material. Growing mass at growing velocity. Louder, broader, more massive over time.

Amplitude: Near avalanche (100m): 120+ dB. At 500m: 90–100 dB. At 2km: audible rumble. Duration: 30–90 seconds total.

Valley reverberance: Glacial U-shaped valleys produce wall reflections that extend perceived duration and add diffuse reverb. The mountain becomes an acoustic chamber.

Notable Cascade Sites

Mt. Rainier: Sunset Amphitheater, Willis Wall, Carbon and Emmons Glacier headwalls. Documented avalanches 2006 (South Tahoma), 2015 (Sunset Amphitheater area, recorded on seismographs). Hydrothermal alteration makes Rainier the most rock-avalanche-active major peak in Washington.

North Cascades: Picket Range vertical granite, Liberty Bell group near Washington Pass, Cascade Crest near Glacier Peak. The 2003 Heather Hill avalanche near Cascade Pass triggered by summer afternoon thermal event.

Mt. St. Helens: The May 18, 1980 eruption was itself triggered by the largest rock avalanche in recorded North American history (~2.5 cubic kilometers), releasing the pressurized magmatic system. Ongoing small avalanches in the crater.
