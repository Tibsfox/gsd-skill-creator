## CASCADE RANGE GEOLOGY -- THE SETTING

### Why Cascades Produce Rock Avalanches

The Cascade Range is a geologically active, structurally complex mountain system that produces rock avalanches for specific reasons:

- **The volcanic spine.** The Cascades are a volcanic arc -- a chain of stratovolcanoes (Mt. Rainier, Mt. Baker, Mt. Adams, Mt. St. Helens, Glacier Peak, Mt. Hood) built over a subduction zone where the Juan de Fuca Plate dives beneath the North American Plate. The volcanic rocks (andesite, basalt, volcanic breccia) that constitute the major peaks are heterogeneous -- layered flows of different density and strength alternating with welded tuff, ash deposits, and hydrothermally altered zones. This heterogeneity makes the volcanic peaks structurally weak in ways that non-volcanic mountains are not.
- **Hydrothermal alteration.** Active and dormant volcanoes in the Cascades have extensive hydrothermal systems that circulate hot water and gases through the mountain interior. Hydrothermal alteration replaces strong crystalline rock minerals (feldspar, pyroxene) with weaker clay minerals (smectite, kaolinite). Mt. Rainier, for example, has extensive zones of hydrothermally altered rock in its upper flanks that are mechanically weaker than the surrounding unaltered andesite. These weak zones are preferential sites for cliff face instability.
- **Non-volcanic cliff faces.** Outside the major volcanoes, the Cascades contain extensive cliff faces in crystalline basement rock (granites, metamorphic schists, and gneisses of the North Cascades) and sedimentary-volcanic sequences of the central and southern Cascades. These non-volcanic cliff faces are more structurally uniform but are fractured by glacial action (glacial plucking removes material from cliff faces during ice occupation, leaving oversteepened walls) and by stress release as the rock mass adjusts to the removal of overlying glacial weight (post-glacial rebound and joint development).
- **Glacial steepening.** Glacier activity in the Cascades -- both active glaciers (Mt. Rainier has 26 named glaciers, the most of any continental US peak; Mt. Baker has approximately 10) and former glaciers that shaped the landscape during Pleistocene glaciations -- has steepened valley walls and cliff faces throughout the range. Glacially steepened cliff faces are above their angle of stability for non-glacially supported conditions; as climate warming reduces glacier coverage and thickness, the support that ice provided to cliff bases is removed, accelerating mass movement.
- **The specific cliff face setting.** The degree-322 event occurs on a cliff face 50-300 meters in height on the upper slopes of a Cascade peak or ridgeline, at elevation 4,000-8,000 feet, where steep rock is exposed above and below. The cliff face has been progressively fractured by frost-wedging, glacial action, and thermal stress. On a summer afternoon, a fracture that has been widening through the winter and spring reaches a threshold and the block releases.

## THE MECHANICS OF ROCK AVALANCHE

### Gravity Wins

A rock avalanche is the acoustic event of gravity converting potential energy into kinetic energy across a rock slope:

- **Initiation.** A rock avalanche begins with the failure of a critical block or face section. The failure mechanism is typically: (1) frost-wedging opens a fracture to the point where structural support is lost; (2) thermal expansion on a warm afternoon exceeds the residual friction holding a block; (3) a seismic event (common in the Cascades -- small earthquakes are frequent near active volcanic centers) shakes a pre-stressed cliff face; (4) water infiltration (from snowmelt or summer thunderstorm) reduces pore water pressure in fractures, accelerating failure. The initiating event is often impossible to observe -- a crack propagating at depth until the block moves.
- **The fall phase.** The initiating block falls freely if the cliff face is near-vertical. At 50 meters height, free fall time is approximately 3.2 seconds. During free fall, the block is silent -- there is no friction surface to produce sound. The sound begins at impact.
- **The first impact.** The initial impact of the rock mass on the slope below the cliff face is the acoustic event's most intense moment: a percussive impact that produces a broadband shock wave. A 1,000 kg block impacting at 30 m/s (terminal velocity for this size and density over 50m) delivers approximately 450,000 joules of kinetic energy at the moment of impact. This energy is rapidly converted into heat, deformation, and sound. The acoustic output is an impulsive broadband signal -- percussive but with significant low-frequency content (50-200 Hz) from the shock wave and the momentum transferred to the slope.
- **The acceleration phase.** After the first impact, the rock avalanche transitions from single-block fall to mass movement. The initial block shatters on impact and continues downslope; it is joined by rocks dislodged by the impact's vibration; additional blocks that were marginally stable on the cliff face above are released by the vibration. The mass grows and accelerates. This acceleration phase is the "multi-ton percussion" of the degree-322 description: not a single impact but a continuous, growing, accelerating cascade of impacts as increasing mass moves at increasing velocity down the slope.
- **The full cascade.** A fully developed rock avalanche is a self-amplifying acoustic event: the falling mass dislodges additional material, which increases the mass in motion, which increases the acoustic output, which produces additional vibration in the cliff face, which releases additional material. The acoustic envelope is growing throughout the acceleration phase -- louder as more mass enters motion, broader in frequency as smaller fragments (higher frequency) join larger blocks (lower frequency). The full cascade acoustic profile covers 20-2,000 Hz.
- **Deceleration and termination.** The avalanche decelerates as the slope angle decreases at the base of the cliff -- the transition from steep cliff to talus slope to valley floor reduces gravitational acceleration. The acoustic event terminates not suddenly but progressively: the largest blocks stop first as they reach flat ground; smaller fragments continue moving; fine debris settles in a long tail of decreasing amplitude. The acoustic tail can last 30-120 seconds after the primary mass has stopped.

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8

### What a Rock Avalanche Sounds Like

The degree-322 rock avalanche produces a specific, identifiable acoustic signature:

- **The initiation crack.** Immediately before the block releases, sharp cracking sounds may be audible at close range -- the fracture propagating through the final intact rock mass. This is a high-frequency, brief acoustic event (2,000-8,000 Hz, duration 0.1-0.5 seconds) that precedes the main event by 0-5 seconds. Not always audible; depends on proximity.
- **The impact percussion.** The primary acoustic event is percussive -- a broadband impact that sounds like a thunder crack with a lower-frequency rumble. The initial impact produces peak amplitude at 50-500 Hz with significant high-frequency components (500-2,000 Hz) from the fragmentation of the impacting rock. The sound is directional -- clearly emanating from the cliff face location.
- **The rumble phase.** As the mass movement accelerates and expands, the acoustic character shifts from discrete impacts to continuous rumble. Individual block impacts become too rapid to distinguish; the combined output is a continuous broadband rumble in the 50-2,000 Hz range, with dominant energy in the 100-500 Hz register. The rumble sounds like distant thunder that does not attenuate -- sustained, growing in volume as more mass enters motion.
- **Amplitude.** Near the avalanche (within 100 meters): peak amplitude can exceed 120 dB -- comparable to the acoustic maximum of a Steller Sea Lion bull roar or a heavy artillery discharge. At 500 meters: still clearly audible at 90-100 dB against the alpine ambient (which is low -- alpine environments are acoustically quiet when undisturbed). At 2 kilometers: audible as a distinct rumble event.
- **Duration.** The full acoustic event duration depends on cliff height and slope length: for a 100-meter cliff with 200 meters of active slope below: total acoustic event 30-90 seconds. The initiating impact is 1-3 seconds; the full mass movement acceleration is 10-30 seconds; the deceleration tail is 20-60 seconds.
- **The reverberant environment.** In a Cascade alpine cirque or valley, the rock avalanche acoustic event is amplified by wall reflections. A cliff face avalanche in a U-shaped glacial valley produces multiple reflections from the valley walls, extending the perceived duration and adding a diffuse reverberant quality to the direct signal. The mountain becomes a natural acoustic chamber.

(Sources: Rock avalanche acoustic signature; initiation crack high frequency 2000-8000 Hz; impact percussion 50-500 Hz; rumble phase 50-2000 Hz; amplitude 120 dB at 100m; amplitude 90-100 dB at 500m; duration 30-90 seconds 100m cliff; deceleration tail 20-60 seconds; glacial valley wall reverberance; alpine acoustic chamber effect)

## NOTABLE CASCADE ROCK AVALANCHE SITES

### Where This Happens in the Pacific Northwest

Rock avalanches are documented events at specific sites throughout the Cascades:

- **Mt. Rainier.** The most rock-avalanche-active major peak in Washington State, due to extensive hydrothermal alteration in its upper flanks. The Sunset Amphitheater, the upper Willis Wall, and the headwalls of the Carbon Glacier and Emmons Glacier have all produced documented rock avalanche events. In 2006, a large rock and ice avalanche from the upper South Tahoma Glacier headwall was observed and documented. In 2015, a significant mass movement event from the Sunset Amphitheater area was recorded on seismographs.
- **North Cascades.** The vertical granite walls of the Picket Range, the Cascade Crest near Glacier Peak, and the steep faces of the Liberty Bell group (near Washington Pass) are active rock avalanche sites. The 2003 Heather Hill rock avalanche (near Cascade Pass) was triggered by a summer afternoon thermal event and deposited material across the approach trail.
- **Shuksan Arm and Mt. Baker area.** The volcanic breccia and altered rock of Mt. Baker's upper slopes, combined with active ice loss, makes the Baker area an active mass-movement zone. The Heliotrope Ridge approach route regularly passes below active rock fall zones.
- **Mt. St. Helens.** Post-1980 eruption geology includes extremely unstable material on the crater rim and new dome complex. Frequent small rock avalanches in the crater are an ongoing phenomenon. The May 18, 1980 eruption itself was triggered by a massive rock avalanche (the largest in recorded North American history, approximately 2.5 cubic kilometers of material) that released the pressurized magmatic system.
- **The Enchantments, Okanogan Highlands.** Granite terrain at high elevation with minimal vegetation on cliff faces produces clean rock fall and avalanche events. The polished granite of the Enchantments area above Leavenworth is an active rock fall zone where small events are audible from the trails below.

(Sources: Mt. Rainier hydrothermal alteration rock avalanche; Willis Wall carbon glacier Emmons glacier; South Tahoma 2006 avalanche; Sunset Amphitheater 2015 seismic record; Picket Range vertical granite; Liberty Bell Washington Pass; Heather Hill rock avalanche 2003; Cascade Pass; Mt. Baker Heliotrope Ridge rock fall; Mt. St. Helens 1980 eruption triggered by rock avalanche 2.5 cubic km; Enchantments granite rock fall)
