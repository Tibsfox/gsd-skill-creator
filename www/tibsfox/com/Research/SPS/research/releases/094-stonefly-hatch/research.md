## THE SOUND: WING RUSTLE ON STREAM SURFACE -- 95th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Sound

The stonefly hatch sound is the **wing rustle** of adult stoneflies emerging on stream surfaces [1, 2]:

**The event:** Stonefly nymphs crawl from the stream onto bankside rocks, logs, or streamside vegetation. The adult splits the nymphal exoskeleton and emerges, drying its wings. The wings are held flat over the body and may flutter, producing a **faint rustling** on the stream surface.

**The acoustic character:** A barely audible rustle -- the sound of membranous wings drying and flexing against rock and water surfaces. Not a buzz, not a chirp, not a call. A **physical sound** produced by the interaction of wing membrane, rock, and moving water. The quietest insect sound in the engine.

**Frequency:** Broad-spectrum, low amplitude. The rustle occupies the same frequency range as stream babble (500-8,000 Hz) and is often masked by the stream itself. You hear it when you are close. You hear it when you are paying attention. You hear it when the stream is quiet.

### Why E=2

1. **BARELY AUDIBLE -- the quietest insect sound in the engine.**
2. **Individual event -- one stonefly emerging, not a chorus of hundreds.**
3. **Masked by stream sound -- the rustle exists within the stream's acoustic environment, not above it.**
4. **Short-lived -- adults live days to weeks. The emergence is brief.**
5. **E=2 matches mosquito (91) as the quietest insect energy.** But the stonefly is arguably quieter in practice.
6. **After E=5 (hornet, 93), E=2 is the largest single-degree energy drop since E=2 to E=5 at the Hymenoptera pair (92-93).**

- **Aggressive papery hum** (degree 93, Vespidae) -- collective, aggressive, E=5. The stonefly wing rustle is individual, passive, E=2. The maximum-to-minimum insect energy swing in consecutive degrees.

## ECOLOGY: THE STREAM INDICATOR

### The Indicator Species

The stonefly is one of the most reliable **stream health indicators** in freshwater ecology [1, 2, 3]:

- **Clean water:** Stonefly nymphs require water with low levels of pollution, sediment, and organic waste.
- **Cold water:** Most Perlidae nymphs require cold water (below 20C / 68F). They are among the first species to disappear when streams warm.
- **Well-oxygenated:** Stonefly nymphs breathe through gills and require high dissolved oxygen levels. Oxygen-depleted water kills them.
- **Rocky substrate:** Nymphs live under rocks in fast-flowing riffles. Clean gravel and cobble substrate is essential.

**If stoneflies are present, the stream is healthy. If stoneflies are absent, something is wrong.** This makes the stonefly hatch not just a sound event but a diagnostic event -- the presence of the sound confirms the ecological health of the stream.

### Habitat

Cold stream [1, 2]:

- **Primary:** Mountain streams, cold rivers, and tributaries with rocky substrate and fast-flowing riffles.
- **Riparian context:** Forested banks provide shade (maintaining cold water temperature), leaf litter input (nymphal food for some species), and woody debris (emergence substrate).
- **PNW significance:** PNW stoneflies are among the most diverse in North America. The Cascade Range and Olympic Mountains provide extensive cold-stream habitat. Stonefly diversity is a signature of the PNW's clean, cold, forested stream systems.

### Life Cycle

- **Nymphal stage:** 1-4 years in the stream, living under rocks, feeding on other aquatic invertebrates (Perlidae are predators) or organic matter.
- **Emergence:** Nymphs crawl from stream onto rocks or vegetation. Adults emerge from the nymphal exoskeleton.
- **Adult stage:** Days to weeks. Adults do not feed (or feed minimally). Wings are functional but flight is weak and low.
- **Reproduction:** Males drum their abdomens on substrate to attract females -- a vibrational signal transmitted through the surface, not an airborne sound. Females deposit eggs on the stream surface.
