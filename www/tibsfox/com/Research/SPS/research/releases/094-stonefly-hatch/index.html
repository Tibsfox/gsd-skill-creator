Stream Indicator — Clean Cold Well-Oxygenated Water

The stonefly is one of the most reliable stream health indicators in freshwater ecology. If stoneflies are present, the stream is healthy. Stonefly nymphs require clean water (low pollution), cold water (below 20°C), high dissolved oxygen, and rocky substrate. Their presence IS the data — a living diagnostic confirming that this stream is healthy [1, 2, 3].

Wing Rustle on Stream Surface — 95th Acoustic Element

The quietest insect sound in the engine. A barely audible rustle of membranous wings drying and flexing on bankside rocks during emergence [1, 2]. Not a buzz, not a chirp, not a call — a physical sound of wing membrane on wet rock. E=2. You hear it only when close and attentive. Maximum energy drop from degree 93 (E=5 to E=2).

NEW ORDER Plecoptera — 6th Insect Order / Perlidae — 44th Family

Plecoptera enters as the sixth insect order — and the first with an aquatic life stage. Stonefly nymphs live IN the stream for 1-4 years, under rocks in fast-flowing riffles, before crawling out to emerge as short-lived adults. Perlidae (golden stoneflies) are the largest PNW stoneflies — predatory nymphs that hunt other aquatic invertebrates [1, 2].

The Sound: Wing Rustle on Stream Surface (95th Acoustic Element)

The event: Stonefly nymphs crawl from the stream onto bankside rocks, logs, or vegetation. The adult splits the nymphal exoskeleton and emerges, drying its wings [1, 2]. The wings flex and flutter — a faint, papery rustle against rock and water surface. The sound is barely audible, often masked by the stream itself.

Why E=2: Individual emergence. Barely audible. Masked by stream. Short-lived. The quietest insect sound in the engine. Maximum energy drop from the hornet’s E=5 at degree 93.

Taxonomy: Perlidae (NEW) / Plecoptera (NEW)

Classification: Class Insecta. Order Plecoptera — NEW ORDER (6th insect order). Family Perlidae — NEW FAMILY (44th) [1, 2]. Golden stoneflies. First insect in the engine with an obligate aquatic nymphal stage.

PNW species: Calineuria californica, Hesperoperla pacifica, and other large predatory stoneflies common in Cascade and Olympic mountain streams.

Ecology: The Stream Indicator

Habitat: Cold mountain streams with rocky substrate and fast-flowing riffles. Forested riparian zones provide shade (maintaining cold temperature) and leaf litter input [1, 2].

Life cycle: Nymphs live 1-4 years in the stream under rocks. Predatory — hunting other aquatic invertebrates. Adults emerge in late spring through summer. Short-lived (days to weeks). Males drum abdomens on substrate to attract females (vibrational signal, not airborne).

Return to streams: First stream species since Belted Kingfisher at degree 57. 37-degree gap closed. Three stream species: American Dipper (52), Belted Kingfisher (57), Stonefly (94). The stonefly is the first species that actually LIVES in the stream.
