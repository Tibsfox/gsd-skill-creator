## Taxonomy: The First Cardinal

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Cardinalidae (Cardinals, Grosbeaks, and Allies)
- **Genus:** *Pheucticus*
- **Species:** *Pheucticus melanocephalus* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Common Name:** Black-headed Grosbeak
- **Etymology:** *Pheucticus* from Greek *pheuktikos*, meaning "shy" or "inclined to flee" (a somewhat misleading name -- the species is moderately bold at feeders and nest sites). *Melanocephalus* from Greek *melas* (black) + *kephalos* (head). The species was described by William Swainson from specimens collected in Mexico.

### Cardinalidae: A New Family for the Engine

The family Cardinalidae contains approximately 53 species worldwide, concentrated in the New World. The family is defined not by color (despite the Northern Cardinal's fame) but by a combination of heavy conical bills, seed-crushing jaw musculature, and distinctively structured palates. Cardinalidae are nine-primaried oscine passerines -- they share the suboscine-to-oscine transition with the engine's existing Passeriformes families but belong to a different superfamily (Passeroidea, specifically the New World tanager clade).

### Subspecies and Range

Two subspecies are generally recognized:

1. **P. m. melanocephalus** -- the widespread western form, breeding from British Columbia south through the western United States to Mexico. This is the PNW subspecies. Males show deep orange-cinnamon underparts, bold black-and-white wings, black head.

In the PNW, the Black-headed Grosbeak breeds throughout western Washington and Oregon at low to moderate elevations (sea level to ~1,500 m). It favors mixed deciduous-conifer woodland, riparian corridors, suburban neighborhoods with mature shade trees, forest edges, and oak savanna. It is a **neotropical migrant** -- breeding in the PNW from late April through August, then migrating to western Mexico for winter. Peak song activity occurs June through July.

## The Bill: Seed-Cracking Engineering

### Morphology

The Black-headed Grosbeak's bill is the most structurally distinctive feature of the species and the most powerful seed-processing apparatus in the engine to date. The bill is:

- **Conical** -- broad at the base, tapering to a blunt point. The classic grosbeak shape.
- **Deep** -- bill depth (upper mandible base to lower mandible base) is approximately 15--17 mm in males, proportionally much deeper than any previous engine species. For comparison: Black-capped Chickadee ~7 mm, Oregon Junco ~9 mm, Pine Siskin ~8 mm. The grosbeak bill is nearly twice as deep as a junco's.
- **Wide** -- bill width across the base is approximately 14--16 mm.
- **Strong** -- the jaw adductor muscles (musculus adductor mandibulae externus and musculus pterygoideus) are disproportionately large, filling the space between the skull and the bill base. These muscles generate bite forces estimated at **35--50 N** (Herrel et al. 2005, *Journal of Morphology*; Van der Meij and Bout 2004, *Journal of Experimental Biology*), among the highest of any North American passerine of comparable body mass.

### Seed-Cracking Mechanics

The grosbeak's bill is a precision nutcracker. The process:

1. **Seed placement.** The bird positions a seed (sunflower, safflower, or native seed) in a groove along the cutting edge of the lower mandible -- a longitudinal channel that holds the seed in place during cracking.
2. **Mandibular occlusion.** The upper mandible presses down while the lower mandible pushes up. The seed is compressed between the two cutting edges.
3. **Husk separation.** The tongue (muscular, with a horny tip) works the cracked husk away from the kernel. The husk is expelled laterally; the kernel is swallowed.
4. **Rapid cycling.** A grosbeak can crack and consume a sunflower seed in approximately 2--3 seconds -- faster than many smaller-billed species that must work seeds for 5--10 seconds.

The bill also functions as a fruit processor (crushing berry flesh) and an insect seizer (capturing large beetles, caterpillars, and other prey). The grosbeak is not exclusively granivorous -- it is an **opportunistic omnivore** whose powerful bill gives it access to food items that weaker-billed species cannot exploit.

### Bill-Song Connection

The massive bill has acoustic consequences. In passerines, bill morphology affects the resonant properties of the vocal tract -- the bill acts as a horn, filtering and shaping the frequencies produced by the syrinx. Larger, deeper bills tend to produce lower-frequency, more resonant sounds (Podos 2001, *Nature*; Herrel et al. 2009, *Evolution*). The grosbeak's deep bill contributes to the **low-frequency richness** of its warble -- the "robin-like" quality partly arises because the grosbeak's large bill produces a similarly warm, low-frequency resonance to the robin's equally substantial bill. The bill is simultaneously a tool and a resonator. The seed-cracker IS the sound shaper.

## The Song: A Rich Robin-like Warble with Slurs

### Acoustic Description

The Black-headed Grosbeak's song is one of the most immediately appealing sounds in the PNW mixed forest. It is a rapid, continuous warble of **slurred whistled phrases** that rise and fall in pitch, delivered with a smooth, rolling quality. The song has been consistently described in field guides and ornithological literature as "robin-like" -- and the comparison is apt. Both species produce rich, rolling warbles of similar frequency range, similar phrase structure, and similar rhythmic cadence.

Key acoustic parameters:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~1.5--4.5 kHz. The grosbeak's song spans approximately three octaves of the human-audible mid-range, with most energy concentrated in the 2.0--3.5 kHz band.
- **Song duration:** 2--5 seconds per song bout (variable; some extended bouts reach 6--8 seconds).
- **Phrase structure:** The song consists of **10--20 rapidly delivered phrases** per bout, each phrase a slurred whistle that rises or falls in pitch. Phrases are connected without distinct pauses -- the song flows as a continuous stream. No two phrases in a bout are identical, but the overall melodic contour is consistent within an individual.
- **Slur type:** The defining acoustic feature. Each phrase contains a **continuous pitch glide** -- an upslur, downslur, or arcing slur (up-then-down or down-then-up). The slurs are smooth, not stepped. This continuous pitch modulation gives the song its liquid, rolling quality.
- **Tempo:** Approximately 3--5 phrases per second. Fast enough that individual phrases blur together for an untrained ear, but slow enough that each phrase is perceptually distinct to a trained listener. Notably, this tempo falls **at or near the perceptual fusion threshold** -- the same boundary region explored by the Oregon Junco's trill (degree 11). The grosbeak's phrases are more complex than the junco's identical repeated notes, but the delivery rate occupies similar temporal territory.
- **Timbre:** Rich, warm, full-bodied. The large bill and vocal tract produce a sound with substantial low-frequency content and smooth harmonic structure. Less thin and reedy than warbler songs (degrees 14--17, 20), less ethereal than the Hermit Thrush (degree 7), less buzzy than the Pine Siskin (degree 12). The grosbeak's timbre is **warm and rounded** -- a quality that arises from the bill's acoustic filtering.
- **Amplitude modulation:** Moderate. The song maintains relatively consistent amplitude throughout, with slight dynamic variations between phrases. Not the sustained flat amplitude of the flicker's wick call (degree 21) or the crescendo of the Yellow Warbler (degree 16), but a gently undulating volume contour.
- **Register:** Mid-range to upper-mid, centered on 2.5--3.5 kHz. This places the grosbeak squarely in the vocal register occupied by the American Robin, the Hermit Thrush, and other rich-voiced woodland passerines.

### Why It Sounds "Robin-like": Convergent Song Structure

The grosbeak's resemblance to the American Robin's song is one of the most frequently noted acoustic comparisons in North American ornithology. The comparison operates on multiple levels:

**1. Shared frequency range.** Both species center their songs in the 2.0--3.5 kHz band. Both have substantial energy below 3 kHz, producing a warm, "bassy" quality relative to higher-pitched warblers and sparrows. The grosbeak's large bill and the robin's large bill both produce vocal-tract resonances that emphasize the same frequency region.

**2. Shared phrase structure -- slurred whistles in rapid sequence.** Both species deliver songs composed of rapidly alternating rising and falling slurred phrases. The robin's "cheerily, cheer-up, cheerio" and the grosbeak's continuous warble share the same basic architecture: short slurred phrases delivered at 3--5 per second in a continuous stream with no extended pauses. The phrases rise and fall in pitch, creating a rolling melodic contour.

**3. Shared rhythmic cadence.** Both species deliver their phrases at approximately the same rate (3--5 phrases/second), creating a similar temporal texture. To a casual listener, the two songs share a rhythmic "feel" -- a rolling, unhurried, continuous warble that fills the mid-frequency space.

**4. Key differences that distinguish them.** Despite the similarities, the two species are distinguishable:

| Dimension | Black-headed Grosbeak | American Robin |
|---|---|---|
| Phrase variety | **Higher** -- more varied slurs per bout, rarely repeats a phrase within a song | **Lower** -- tends to repeat phrase types in short cycles (cheerily-cheer-up-cheerio) |
| Tonal quality | Slightly **richer, more mellow** | Slightly **brighter, more clipped** |
| Song structure | **Continuous flow** -- no clear phrase groups | **Grouped** into 2--3 phrase cycles with brief pauses |
| Buzzy quality | Occasional **slight buzz** on some slurs | Rarely buzzy |
| Song length | **2--5+ seconds** continuous | **2--3 seconds** per phrase group, repeated |
| Time of day | **Peak activity dawn, sings throughout day** | **Strongest at dawn and dusk** |
| Singing position | **Mid-canopy** to upper canopy | **Treetop** or prominent exposed perch |

The convergence is **not mimicry**. The grosbeak does not learn its song from robins. The grosbeak and the robin belong to different families (Cardinalidae vs. Turdidae) that diverged approximately 40 million years ago. The resemblance arises from **convergent acoustic evolution**: two species occupying similar acoustic niches in mixed forest, with similarly large bills producing similar vocal-tract resonances, arriving at similar song structures through independent evolutionary paths. The bill shapes the sound. The habitat selects for mid-frequency, rich-toned warbles that propagate well through mixed deciduous-conifer canopy. Two lineages, separated by 40 million years, converged on the same acoustic solution.

This is a new concept for the engine: **acoustic convergence across families**. The engine has documented variation within a family (Turdidae across degrees 2, 7, 18, 19), but never before has it encountered two unrelated families producing remarkably similar songs. The grosbeak at degree 23 and the robin at degree 25 will form a **convergence pair** -- not a species-pair like the flicker (same species, two sounds) but a convergence pair (different families, similar sounds).

## Both Sexes Sing: A Rare Behavioral Trait

### The Phenomenon

One of the most remarkable features of the Black-headed Grosbeak is that **both males and females sing**. This is unusual among North American passerines, where song is predominantly a male behavior. The engine has documented 22 species through degree 22; in every previous species, song (or its equivalent) was either exclusively male or predominantly male with only rudimentary female vocalization.

In the Black-headed Grosbeak:

- **Males sing** a loud, elaborate warble from exposed perches for territorial advertisement and mate attraction. This is the standard passerine territorial song.
- **Females also sing** a song that is structurally similar to the male's -- a warble of slurred phrases -- though typically shorter (1--3 seconds vs. 2--5 seconds), softer, and less elaborate (fewer phrase types per bout). Female song occurs in multiple contexts:
  1. **On the nest.** Females sing while incubating eggs -- a behavior documented by Ritchison (1983, *Condor*) and confirmed in subsequent studies. Nest singing is quiet, possibly functioning as communication between the incubating female and the nearby male.
  2. **Countersinging with the male.** Males and females exchange songs in alternating bouts during territory establishment and pair bonding. This duetting behavior strengthens pair bonds and may coordinate nest defense.
  3. **Post-fledging.** Both parents sing near fledglings, possibly to maintain contact with offspring.

### Why Both Sexes Sing

Female song in passerines is increasingly recognized as more common than historically assumed (Odom et al. 2014, *Nature Communications*). However, the Black-headed Grosbeak remains one of a relatively small number of North American temperate-zone species where **female song is well-documented, structurally elaborate, and functionally significant**.

Several hypotheses explain the phenomenon:

1. **Ancestral retention.** Female song may be the ancestral state in oscine passerines, with female song loss being a derived condition in many temperate-zone species. The grosbeak retains the ancestral dual-sex singing pattern.
2. **Mate coordination.** The shared incubation pattern of the Black-headed Grosbeak (both sexes incubate, unusual among passerines) may favor vocal communication between pair members. Song serves as a coordination signal between partners who alternate nest duties.
3. **Nest defense.** A singing female on the nest may deter predators or competitors by signaling that the nest is actively defended.
4. **Sexual selection symmetry.** In species where both sexes contribute heavily to parental care (as in the grosbeak, where males incubate, brood, and feed young), mutual mate assessment may favor elaborate signals in both sexes.

### Shared Incubation: The Behavioral Context

The Black-headed Grosbeak is one of the few North American passerines where **males regularly incubate eggs**. The incubation pattern:

- Both sexes develop brood patches (bare skin areas for heat transfer to eggs).
- Males incubate during midday (approximately 10:00 AM to 2:00 PM).
- Females incubate during morning, late afternoon, and overnight.
- Nest exchanges involve vocal signaling -- the arriving bird often sings softly, and the departing bird responds before leaving.
- **Males sing while incubating.** This is the most striking behavioral detail: a male grosbeak sitting on eggs, partially concealed in foliage, singing a full territorial warble. The simultaneous performance of incubation (a cryptic, concealed behavior) and territorial song (a conspicuous, advertised behavior) creates a paradox: the bird is simultaneously hiding and broadcasting. The song from the nest may serve to warn approaching males that the territory is occupied, even when the territory holder cannot be seen.

This dual-sex, dual-role pattern (both sexes sing, both sexes incubate) represents a more equitable distribution of reproductive labor than the typical passerine model (male sings and defends, female builds nest and incubates). The engine has not previously encountered a species with this level of behavioral symmetry.

In the PNW, mixed forest is the dominant lowland habitat type wherever fire suppression, urban development, or natural succession has produced a blend of native deciduous species (red alder, bigleaf maple, black cottonwood, Oregon white oak) and conifers (Douglas fir, western red cedar, western hemlock). ### Acoustic Environment of Mixed Forest

Mixed forest creates a particular acoustic environment:

- **Variable canopy structure.** Deciduous and conifer canopy layers create a heterogeneous sound transmission environment. Mid-frequency sounds (2--4 kHz) propagate well through the gaps in a mixed canopy.
- **Reverberant.** Multiple trunk reflections, leaf scatter, and layered canopy create moderate reverberation -- more than open woodland (degree 21), less than closed-canopy old-growth (degree 1).
- **Ambient noise.** Mixed forest in suburban settings includes anthropogenic noise (traffic, human activity). Species that sing in this habitat must produce songs that are audible above ambient noise floors -- favoring loud, rich, mid-frequency warbles. The grosbeak's song is well-suited to this challenge.

## Neotropical Migrant: The Summer Voice

### Migration Pattern

The Black-headed Grosbeak is a **long-distance neotropical migrant**, wintering in western Mexico (primarily the Pacific slope from Sinaloa to Oaxaca) and breeding across the western United States and southern Canada. In the PNW:

- **Arrival:** Late April to early May. Males arrive first, establishing territories through song. Females arrive 5--10 days later.
- **Breeding season:** May through July. Nest building begins within days of pair formation. Incubation period: 12--14 days. Nestling period: 11--12 days. One brood per season (occasionally two in favorable conditions).
- **Departure:** August to mid-September. Post-breeding dispersal begins in late July; most birds have left the PNW by mid-September.
- **Peak song period:** Late May through mid-July -- approximately 6--8 weeks of full territorial singing.

The grosbeak is one of the latest-arriving and earliest-departing migratory species in the PNW. Its presence brackets the warmest weeks of summer -- the period of peak insect abundance, peak fruit availability, and longest day length. The grosbeak is the sound of PNW summer.

### Monarch Butterfly Connection

A remarkable ecological footnote: the Black-headed Grosbeak is one of the few bird species that regularly feeds on **Monarch butterflies** (*Danaus plexippus*) at their overwintering sites in central Mexico. Monarchs sequester toxic cardenolides from milkweed, making them unpalatable to most predators. The grosbeak has evolved partial resistance to these toxins and can consume Monarchs by selectively eating the less-toxic abdominal tissues while discarding the wing and thorax. This grosbeak-Monarch interaction occurs exclusively on the wintering grounds, not in the PNW breeding range, but it demonstrates the species' ecological reach -- the same bird that sings in a Seattle backyard in June feeds on toxic butterflies in a Mexican fir forest in January.

## Brown-headed Cowbird Resistance

### The Cowbird Problem

The Brown-headed Cowbird (*Molothrus ater*) is a brood parasite that lays its eggs in the nests of other species. Host species vary dramatically in their ability to detect and reject cowbird eggs. Many species are "acceptors" -- they incubate and raise the cowbird chick at the expense of their own offspring. A few species are "rejecters" -- they recognize and remove cowbird eggs.

### The Grosbeak Response

The Black-headed Grosbeak is a **moderate rejecter** of cowbird eggs. Studies (Rothstein 1975, *Condor*; Peer and Sealy 2004, *Studies in Avian Biology*) document that:

- Grosbeaks reject approximately **30--40%** of experimentally introduced cowbird eggs (compared to ~100% for Gray Catbirds and ~0% for Song Sparrows).
- Rejection behavior includes egg ejection (grasping the cowbird egg in the bill and removing it from the nest) and nest desertion (abandoning the parasitized nest entirely).
- The grosbeak's large, powerful bill facilitates egg ejection -- it can physically grasp and remove a cowbird egg more easily than smaller-billed species.
- Despite moderate rejection rates, cowbird parasitism still occurs in grosbeak nests. In some PNW populations, parasitism rates of 10--30% have been documented.

The bill that cracks seeds and shapes song also ejects parasitic eggs. The three functions of the grosbeak bill -- feeding, singing, and nest defense -- converge in one anatomical structure.

## Sexual Dimorphism: Bold Male, Cryptic Female

### The Male

The adult male Black-headed Grosbeak is one of the most visually striking passerines in the PNW:

- **Head:** Entirely black, forming a bold hood from crown to chin.
- **Back:** Black with tawny-orange streaks.
- **Breast and underparts:** Rich cinnamon-orange, deepening in the center of the breast.
- **Wings:** Black with bold white wing bars and white patches at the base of the primaries.
- **Tail:** Black with white corners.
- **Bill:** Large, conical, pale grayish-blue.
- **Eye:** Dark brown.
- **Overall impression:** A chunky, front-heavy bird with a massive pale bill, a jet-black head, and warm orange underparts. Unmistakable at a feeder or in the mid-canopy.

### The Female

The adult female is cryptically patterned for nest concealment:

- **Head:** Brown with bold white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) and dark crown.
- **Back:** Brown with buff streaks.
- **Breast and underparts:** Warm buff to ochre, with fine streaking on the flanks.
- **Wings:** Brown with white wing bars (less bold than male's).
- **Bill:** Large, conical, pale grayish-blue (same bill size as male -- the bill is not sexually dimorphic in size).
- **Overall impression:** A large-billed, warmly colored sparrow-like bird. Less dramatic than the male but still recognizable by the massive bill and warm underpart tones.

The sexual dimorphism is substantial in plumage but minimal in bill size -- both sexes need the same seed-cracking apparatus, and both sexes sing with the same vocal-tract resonance properties. The convergence of bill function (feeding) and acoustic function (song resonance) applies equally to both sexes.

The grosbeak's song is its primary signal -- rich, resonant, mid-frequency. Both sustain E=4 through the power of the voice itself, not through amplification or percussion. Third consecutive degree at E=4, now driven by vocal melody rather than mechanical force.

4. **Soul as convergent genre.** Soul music is itself a convergence -- gospel, R&B, jazz, and blues arriving at a unified sound from different traditions. The grosbeak's song is an acoustic convergence -- sounding like a robin through independent evolutionary paths. Allen Stone's soul sound converges gospel and jazz. The grosbeak's warble converges with the robin's. Convergence as creative strategy.
