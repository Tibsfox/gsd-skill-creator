Black-headed Grosbeak

Pheucticus melanocephalus · Family Cardinalidae (Cardinals & Grosbeaks)
Rich Robin-like Warble with Slurs · Mixed Forest · Summer · Energy 4
In mid-June, from the canopy of a bigleaf maple along a suburban creek, a rich warble pours down — a rolling, liquid stream of slurred whistles rising and falling in pitch, each phrase different from the last, delivered at a pace that blurs the boundary between distinct notes and continuous melody. It sounds like a robin. It is not a robin. It is a chunky, front-heavy bird with a jet-black head, warm cinnamon-orange breast, and a pale conical bill so massive it looks borrowed from a bird twice its size. The Black-headed Grosbeak — seed-cracker, Monarch-eater, neotropical migrant, the summer voice of PNW mixed forest. The groove resets. The melody returns.

The Groove Reset: Melody After Percussion

Two degrees of percussion. Degree 21 was the hammer (loud metronomic wick call). Degree 22 was the drum (broadband mechanical percussion — the first non-vocal sound). Now degree 23 returns to tonal melody — a rich, rolling warble of slurred phrases in the warm mid-frequency band. The engine exhales. The groove resets. The 24th acoustic element.

Acoustic Convergence: Sounds Like a Robin, Isn’t a Robin

The Black-headed Grosbeak (Cardinalidae) and the American Robin (Turdidae, degree 25) produce remarkably similar songs — same frequency range (2–3.5 kHz center), same phrase structure (rapidly alternating slurred whistles), same rhythmic cadence. These families diverged approximately 40 million years ago. This is not mimicry. It is convergent acoustic evolution — two lineages arriving at the same solution through independent paths. Similar large bills produce similar vocal-tract resonances. Similar habitats select for similar acoustic properties.

Song Bioacoustics

The Black-headed Grosbeak delivers a rapid, continuous warble of 10–20 slurred whistled phrases per song bout, each phrase a smooth pitch glide — upslur, downslur, or arcing slur. The song lasts 2–5 seconds and is delivered at approximately 3–5 phrases per second, creating a rolling, liquid texture.

Both Sexes Sing

The Black-headed Grosbeak is one of the few North American passerines where both males and females produce elaborate song. Female song is structurally similar to the male’s — a warble of slurred phrases — but typically shorter and softer.

The Bill: Seed-Cracker and Sound Shaper

The grosbeak’s massive conical bill (15–17 mm deep, bite force 35–50 N) is the most powerful seed-processing apparatus in the engine. But the bill also shapes the song. In passerines, bill morphology affects vocal-tract resonance — larger bills produce warmer, lower-frequency sounds (Podos 2001). The grosbeak’s “robin-like” quality partly arises because its large bill produces similar resonance to the robin’s equally substantial bill. The seed-cracker IS the sound shaper.

Neotropical Migrant: The Summer Voice

The Black-headed Grosbeak is a long-distance neotropical migrant, wintering in western Mexico and breeding in the PNW from late April through August. Peak song occurs late May through mid-July — approximately 6–8 weeks of full territorial singing. The grosbeak is the sound of PNW summer.

An ecological footnote: the Black-headed Grosbeak is one of the few birds that feeds on Monarch butterflies at Mexican overwintering sites, tolerating cardenolide toxins that deter most predators. The same bird that sings in a Seattle backyard in June feeds on toxic butterflies in a Mexican fir forest in January.

Sexual Dimorphism

Male: Jet-black head (bold hood from crown to chin), rich cinnamon-orange breast, black back with tawny streaks, bold white wing bars and primary patches, large pale blue-gray conical bill. One of the most striking passerines in the PNW.

Female: Brown head with bold white supercilium, warm buff-to-ochre underparts with fine flank streaking, less bold wing bars, same large pale bill. Cryptic for nest concealment. Bill size is NOT sexually dimorphic — both sexes need the same seed-cracking and vocal-resonance apparatus.
