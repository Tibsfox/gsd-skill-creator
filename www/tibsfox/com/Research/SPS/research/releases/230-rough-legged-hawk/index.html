BUTEO TRILOGY COMPLETE — ARCTIC VISITOR ARRIVES

The Rough-legged Hawk at degree 230 completes the engine's Buteo trilogy: Red-tailed Hawk (228, FIRST BUTEO), Ferruginous Hawk (229, LARGEST BUTEO), Rough-legged Hawk (230, ARCTIC BUTEO). Three buteos, three ecological roles. The Rough-legged Hawk arrives from arctic tundra breeding grounds only in winter — carrying lemming-field ecology into PNW lowland fields.

The Mew — Catlike, E=4

The Rough-legged Hawk's call is the softest raptor vocalization in the engine's 221-232 arc. A high, thin, descending mew — peeeeah or keeaow — with a quality often described as catlike. Long-duration (0.5-1.5 sec), drawn out, almost plaintive. The Ferruginous Hawk (229) produces a loud rough alarm; the Red-tailed Hawk (228) produces the scream that powers all cinema raptors. The Rough-legged Hawk's winter mew is none of these things — it is quiet, present, temporary.

Acoustic Profile — Mew Call

Frequency: 2,000–4,500 Hz
Duration: 0.5–1.5 sec (drawn out, catlike)
Character: soft, descending, plaintive, thin

E=4: the quietest raptor call in the 221-232 arc. Winter visitor energy — present but not claiming. Context: a bird temporarily in the PNW, not defending territory, calling conversationally.

"The hare-footed hawk. Feathered to the toes. Arrived from the Arctic in October. Its call is a soft mew in a January field — the sound of a bird that is here for the winter and knows it."

From the Arctic Tundra

To understand the Rough-legged Hawk in PNW winter fields, understand where it came from: arctic tundra, cliff-nesting on steep riverbanks and rocky outcrops, hunting lemmings across the hummocked tundra landscape. The Rough-legged Hawk is a lemming specialist — one of the species most tightly coupled to lemming population cycles. High-lemming years produce many fledglings that appear in the PNW as irruption years. Lemming crash years thin the winter population.

The species epithet lagopus is Greek for hare-footed. The feathering extends all the way to the toes — a cold-weather adaptation shared with ptarmigan and snowshoe hares. The bird carries arctic adaptation visibly in its feet.

Pacific Northwest Winter Distribution

The Rough-legged Hawk arrives in PNW lowlands October-November and departs March-April. Key winter locations: the Skagit Flats (prime winter hawk watching area north of Seattle), Snohomish Valley, Stillaguamish Flats, Nisqually Flats near Olympia. Open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands, airport grass margins — the winter analog of arctic tundra open terrain. The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the buteos most frequently observed hovering against the wind while hunting — a field mark as useful as plumage.

Buteo Trilogy: 228-230

Three buteos, documented. The genus is complete in the engine. Each has a distinct ecological role, distinct energy signature, distinct PNW relationship. Red-tail: year-round, everywhere, E=6. Ferruginous: eastern PNW shrub-steppe, breeding, E=6. Rough-leg: western PNW winter fields, visiting, E=4. The family identity (Accipitridae) ends at degree 230. Degree 231 brings the Peregrine Falcon and a new family.

Conservation

LEAST CONCERN — STABLE. North American population: ~300,000-500,000 birds. Primary long-term concern: arctic climate change (tundra shrubification reduces lemming habitat, disrupts lemming cycles that Rough-leg productivity depends on). Winter habitat in PNW agricultural lowlands stable. No current conservation crisis.
