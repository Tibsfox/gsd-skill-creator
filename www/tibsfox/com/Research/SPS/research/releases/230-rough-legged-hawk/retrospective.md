## What Worked

1. **The Buteo trilogy structure (228-230) is complete and architecturally clean.** Three buteos, three distinct ecological roles: generalist (Red-tail, 228), grassland giant (Ferruginous, 229), arctic visitor (Rough-leg, 230). The sequence is satisfying and ecologically comprehensive.
2. **E=4 is correct and significant.** The Rough-legged Hawk is the softest raptor in the 221-232 arc. The E=4 reading is not a mistake -- it reflects the behavioral context: a winter visitor in a landscape it does not own, calling conversationally without the territorial aggression of a breeding bird on defended grounds. The energy signature is behaviorally grounded.
3. **The lemming cycle connection is the ecological highlight.** The direct coupling between lemming population abundance (on the Arctic breeding grounds) and Rough-legged Hawk winter irruption events in the PNW is one of the most interesting long-distance ecological linkages the engine has documented. Arctic ecology directly shapes what birders see in January Skagit Valley fields.
4. **The lagopus etymology is load-bearing.** Greek for hare-footed -- the name encodes the adaptation (feathered tarsi) and the ecological context (arctic cold). The etymology connects the name, the physical trait, and the habitat in one word.
5. **Hovering as field mark.** Documenting the hovering behavior is practically useful -- a buteo hovering against the wind is an immediate Rough-legged Hawk indicator in PNW winter fields. Behavior as identification tool.

## Discoveries

1. **The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the very few truly holarctic raptors.** The same species breeds across North America, Scandinavia, Russia, and Siberia. The PNW's winter birds are the same species as the birds wintering in Denmark. This circumpolar distribution is unusual among North American hawks and reflects the Arctic origin of the species -- it evolved in an environment that has no oceanic boundary between continents.
2. **Dark morph frequency (~25-30%) is notably higher than in many other polymorphic buteos.** This means roughly 1 in 4 Rough-legged Hawks the engine might document in winter is dark morph -- not the exception, but a substantial minority. ID challenge and ecological note combined.
