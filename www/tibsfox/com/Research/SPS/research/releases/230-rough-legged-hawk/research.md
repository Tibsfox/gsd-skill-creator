## BUTEO LAGOPUS -- THE HARE-FOOTED HAWK

### Taxonomy and the Arctic Connection

- **The name.** *Buteo lagopus* -- the hare-footed buteo. The species epithet *lagopus* is from Greek: *lagos* (hare) + *pous* (foot). The "rough" in the common name refers to the feathered legs and feet (tarsi feathered to the toes, like a ptarmigan or snowshoe hare) -- an adaptation to arctic cold. The same feathered-leg adaptation seen in the Ferruginous Hawk (229, feathered tarsi) is taken further in the Rough-legged Hawk, which is feathered all the way to the toes.
- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Buteo*. Species: *lagopus*. Four subspecies recognized, with two occurring in North America: *B. l. sanctijohannis* (North American) and the Eurasian subspecies.
- **The Buteo trilogy.** With the Rough-legged Hawk at degree 230, the engine's Buteo trio is complete: Red-tailed Hawk (228, FIRST BUTEO, ubiquitous generalist), Ferruginous Hawk (229, LARGEST BUTEO, open grassland), Rough-legged Hawk (230, ARCTIC BUTEO, winter visitor). Three Buteos, three ecological roles, three positions on the energy scale: E=6, E=6, E=4.
- **Holarctic range.** The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the few truly holarctic raptors -- it breeds across the Arctic from North America through Eurasia (Scandinavia, Russia, Siberia). The North American and Eurasian populations are the same species. This circumpolar distribution is unusual among North American hawks and reflects the species' origin as a tundra specialist.

(Sources: Buteo lagopus taxonomy; lagopus etymology from Greek; feathered tarsi adaptation; four subspecies; North American sanctijohannis; holarctic range; Buteo trilogy at 228-230)

## THE MEW CALL -- CATLIKE, E=4

### Vocalization Analysis

The Rough-legged Hawk's call is the engine's quietest raptor sound:

- **The sound.** The primary call is a high, thin, descending mew -- *peeeeah* or *keeaow* -- with a quality often described as catlike. The call is drawn out (0.5-1.5 seconds), descending in pitch, with a slightly plaintive character. It lacks the roughness of the Ferruginous Hawk's kree-ah (229) and the power of the Red-tailed Hawk's keeeee-arrr (228).
- **Frequency.** The mew call operates in approximately 2,000-4,500 Hz -- similar in range to the Bald Eagle contact call (221), but with a different acoustic character: longer, more drawn out, less staccato.
- **Why E=4.** In the engine's energy scale, E=4 indicates a sound that is present and audible but quiet relative to the context -- more ambient than assertive. The Rough-legged Hawk's call is the sonic expression of a winter visitor: present in the landscape but not claiming it aggressively. The bird is here temporarily; the call reflects that tentativeness.
- **Comparison to Ferruginous Hawk.** The difference between the Rough-legged Hawk's mew (E=4) and the Ferruginous Hawk's alarm call (E=6) is large for two birds of similar size and family. The Ferruginous Hawk is an aggressive territory holder defending a breeding site. The Rough-legged Hawk is a winter visitor in someone else's landscape. The behavioral context shapes the energy signature.
- **Alarm calls.** When seriously disturbed at the nest (on the arctic breeding grounds), the Rough-legged Hawk produces a more forceful alarm -- a longer, harsher series of calls. But in the PNW, where the engine encounters this bird only in winter, the soft mew is the characteristic sound.

(Sources: Rough-legged Hawk vocalization recordings; mew call frequency analysis; catlike character description; comparison to Ferruginous and Red-tailed; breeding alarm call distinction; E=4 calibration)

## ARCTIC BREEDING ECOLOGY

### Where This Bird Comes From

To understand the Rough-legged Hawk in the PNW winter, it is necessary to understand its arctic breeding context:

- **Tundra and taiga.** The Rough-legged Hawk breeds on arctic and subarctic tundra -- open, treeless, dominated by sedges, mosses, lichens, and dwarf shrubs. Nesting occurs on cliff faces, steep riverbanks, and, where trees are available at the tundra-taiga boundary, in sparse tree crowns. The bird is a cliff-nester by preference.
- **Arctic prey.** On the breeding grounds, the primary prey is lemmings (*Lemmus* and *Dicrostonyx* spp.) and voles (*Microtus* spp.). The Rough-legged Hawk is a lemming specialist -- one of the species most tightly coupled to lemming population cycles. In years of lemming abundance, Rough-legged Hawk productivity is high (multiple eggs, multiple fledged young). In years of lemming crashes (which occur on approximately 3-4 year cycles), productivity drops dramatically -- fewer eggs, fewer fledglings.
- **The lemming cycle.** The lemming population cycle is one of the best-known dynamics in arctic ecology. Lemming populations oscillate dramatically -- from very high density (hundreds per hectare) to near-collapse and back over approximately 3-4 years. The Rough-legged Hawk's breeding productivity tracks this cycle closely. The engine documents this because the PNW winter population of Rough-legs is affected: high-lemming years produce more young birds that then appear in southern wintering areas.
- **Irruption years.** In years following high lemming productivity (high-abundance years that produce many young hawks), the PNW and other southern areas may receive "irruption" influxes of Rough-legged Hawks -- more birds than typical years. Birders in Washington and Oregon learn to watch for irruption years by tracking northern lemming cycle reports.

(Sources: Arctic tundra breeding ecology; cliff nesting preference; lemming specialization; lemming population cycle 3-4 years; Rough-leg productivity coupling; irruption years; PNW winter irruption connection)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST WINTER DISTRIBUTION

### The Winter Fields of Western Washington and Oregon

The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the PNW's most reliably encountered winter raptors in open agricultural landscapes:

- **Arrival and departure.** Rough-legged Hawks arrive in the PNW lowlands in October-November, having migrated from arctic breeding grounds through the boreal zone. They depart in March-April to return north. The PNW winter residency period is approximately 4-5 months.
- **Winter habitat.** The species uses open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands, valley floors, and airport grass margins -- the same open-country analogs that Ferruginous Hawks use when they winter in the lowlands. However, Rough-legged Hawks are more commonly encountered in the lowland western PNW than Ferruginous Hawks, which are primarily eastern PNW residents.
- **Key winter locations.** The Skagit Flats (Skagit Valley, north of Seattle) is one of the best Rough-legged Hawk watching locations in Washington. The combination of open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands near Padilla Bay, and high vole/mouse densities makes it optimal winter habitat. Other good locations include the Snohomish Valley, the Stillaguamish Flats, and the Nisqually Flats near Olympia.
- **Hovering.** The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the North American buteos most frequently observed hovering -- holding position against the wind with rapid wingbeats while scanning for prey below. This hovering behavior (shared with kestrels and some other species) is a hunting technique well-adapted to windy, open terrain. A hovering Rough-leg in a Skagit Valley field is one of the PNW's characteristic winter raptor images.
- **Dark morph frequency.** Dark morphs (entirely dark brown) are proportionally more common in the North American Rough-legged Hawk than in most other polymorphic buteos -- approximately 25-30% of the North American population is dark morph. Both morphs winter in the PNW. The dark morph can be confused with other dark-phase raptors; the white-banded tail is the key field mark.

(Sources: PNW winter distribution; arrival October-November; departure March-April; Skagit Flats key location; Snohomish Valley; Stillaguamish Flats; Nisqually Flats; hovering behavior; dark morph frequency)

## MORPHOLOGY AND FIELD IDENTIFICATION

### Light Morph, Dark Morph, and the Black Belly Band

- **Light morph.** White head and upper breast, dark belly band across mid-abdomen, white tail with dark terminal band. In flight from below, the light morph shows: white flight feathers with dark wingtips, contrasting dark belly band, and white tail base. The dark wrist patches (carpal patches) on the underwing are distinctive.
- **Dark morph.** Uniformly dark brown. The tail pattern (white base with dark terminal band) is shared with the light morph and is often the most reliable field mark in dark morphs. The dark morph can appear similar to a dark-phase Ferruginous Hawk (229) -- wing shape and tail pattern help distinguish.
- **Wing shape.** Relatively long and narrow for a buteo -- intermediate between a true buteo's broad wing and an accipiter's short wing. This wing shape, combined with frequent hovering, gives the Rough-legged Hawk a distinctive flight profile.
- **Feathered tarsi.** The fully feathered leg (to the toes) is a definitive structural characteristic. The feathering is visible when the bird is perched and gives the appearance of wearing "leg warmers" -- an adaptation to arctic cold carried into the mild PNW winter.

(Sources: Light morph field marks; dark morph identification; carpal patches; belly band; white tail base; wing shape comparison; feathered tarsi field identification)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### A Stable Species Under Arctic Pressure

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern globally. Population estimated at several hundred thousand individuals globally, with North American population approximately 300,000-500,000 birds.
- **Arctic breeding pressures.** Climate change is the primary long-term concern. Arctic tundra is warming faster than most other habitats on Earth. The shrubification of the tundra (expansion of shrub cover as temperatures warm) reduces the open, lemming-rich tundra habitat that Rough-legged Hawks require. Lemming cycle dynamics may also be disrupted by changing snowpack patterns (lemmings require specific under-snow conditions for winter breeding).
- **Winter habitat stability.** Agricultural fields and open lowlands in the PNW are relatively stable winter habitat. Rough-legged Hawks are adaptable in their winter ecology -- they use a variety of open habitats and are not dependent on any specific winter prey type to the same degree that they are dependent on lemmings in summer.
- **Not currently in crisis.** The Rough-legged Hawk is not a species of immediate conservation concern in the PNW. Monitoring of arctic breeding populations and attention to lemming cycle dynamics are the key conservation actions.

(Sources: Rough-legged Hawk IUCN status; population estimate; arctic climate change effects; tundra shrubification; lemming cycle climate disruption; winter habitat stability)
