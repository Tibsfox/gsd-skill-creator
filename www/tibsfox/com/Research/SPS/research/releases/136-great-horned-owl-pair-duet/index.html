Great Horned Owl — Pair Duet

Bubo virginianus (Strigidae) — E=5 — Call-and-Response in the Night
male-female pitch-different duet — E=5 — the most complex nocturnal vocalization in the PNW forest. Male: 250–400 Hz (deeper). Female: 300–500 Hz (higher, despite being larger). Size-pitch paradox — reversed sexual dimorphism. Third species with double entries (after Pileated WP 108–109, American Dipper 116–117). Winter nesting — earliest nester in North America. Pair bonding, territory declaration.

Call-and-Response — E=5

The male initiates with his deep “hoo-h’HOO-hoo-hoo” (250–400 Hz). The female responds within 2–5 seconds with a higher-pitched version (300–500 Hz), often with more notes (5–7 vs. 4–5). The pair alternates: male-female-male-female, creating a near-continuous vocal presence. Combined acoustic power and territorial significance push the energy from E=4 (solo) to E=5 (duet).

The Size-Pitch Paradox

The female is ~25% heavier than the male (1.4–2.5 kg vs. 0.9–1.8 kg) — reversed sexual dimorphism, common in raptors. But her hoot is higher-pitched, violating the typical size-pitch relationship. Larger body, higher voice. The deeper voice is always the male; the higher voice is always the female. A remarkable reversal with implications for acoustic dimorphism research.

Winter Nesting — January to February

The duet peaks during nesting — the earliest nesting period of any North American bird. Eggs in January–February. The female incubates through ice storms, heavy rain, and sub-zero temperatures for 30–35 days. Great Horned Owls do not build nests — they appropriate nests of Red-tailed Hawks, crows, and ravens. Early nesting = first access to the best sites.
