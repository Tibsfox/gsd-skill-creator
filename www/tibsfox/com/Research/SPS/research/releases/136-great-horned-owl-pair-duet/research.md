## E=5 -- THE PAIRED TERRITORIAL DECLARATION

### Why E=5

The Great Horned Owl pair duet registers at E=5 -- one level above the solo hoot (E=4):

- **Combined acoustic power:** Two voices are louder than one. The male and female hooting in alternation create a near-continuous vocal presence that is acoustically more powerful than either bird hooting alone. The duet fills the silence between individual hoot series, producing a more sustained territorial signal.
- **Territorial significance:** The pair duet is the **most potent territorial declaration** in the Great Horned Owl's behavioral repertoire. A paired territory is more securely held than a single bird's territory. The duet communicates: "this territory is occupied by an established pair." This message is stronger than the solo hoot's message of individual presence.
- **Acoustic complexity:** The pitch difference between male and female creates a **harmonic richness** absent from the solo hoot. The male's deeper voice (fundamental ~250-400 Hz) and the female's higher voice (~300-500 Hz) interleave, creating a more complex acoustic texture. The duet is musically richer than the solo.
- **Behavioral intensity:** Dueting typically occurs during the most intense period of territorial behavior -- November through February, when territories are being established or reinforced and nesting is beginning. The behavioral context amplifies the acoustic impact.
- **E=5 not E=6:** The pair duet is more powerful than the solo hoot but does not reach the acoustic extremes of Trumpeter Swan bugling (E=6) or sustained large-flock vocalizations. Two owls are significantly more impactful than one, but the intermittent, measured pace of the duet keeps it below E=6. E=5 is the appropriate calibration.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998; engine internal analysis)

## THE PAIR DUET -- ANATOMY AND STRUCTURE

### Call-and-Response Architecture

The Great Horned Owl pair duet is a **structured call-and-response** between male and female:

- **Male initiates:** The male typically begins the duet with his deep "hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo" -- the classic four- to five-note hoot at 250-400 Hz fundamental. This is the same vocalization documented at degree 135, but now it serves as the opening statement of a dialogue.
- **Female responds:** The female responds with her own hoot, typically within 2-5 seconds of the male's call. Her hoot is **higher-pitched** (300-500 Hz fundamental), slightly shorter in duration, and often has more notes (five to seven versus the male's four to five). The higher pitch is immediately distinguishable.
- **Alternation:** The pair alternates hoots, creating a **call-and-response pattern** that can continue for several minutes. Male-female-male-female, with each bird waiting for the other to finish before responding. The timing is coordinated -- the birds have learned each other's rhythm through years of partnership.
- **Overlapping crescendo:** During intense territorial encounters, the duet can accelerate and the calls may begin to overlap -- both birds hooting simultaneously for brief moments. This creates a particularly powerful territorial signal.
- **Geographic specificity:** The duet is typically given from the pair's preferred perching sites within their territory -- specific trees, snags, or structures that serve as "broadcast towers." The same pair may duet from the same perches for decades.

### The Size-Pitch Paradox

The Great Horned Owl pair duet exhibits a **remarkable reversal** of the typical size-pitch relationship:

- **The female is larger:** In Great Horned Owls, the female is significantly larger than the male -- approximately 25% heavier (1.4-2.5 kg vs. 0.9-1.8 kg). This is reversed sexual dimorphism (the female being the larger sex), common in raptors.
- **But her hoot is higher-pitched:** Despite being larger, the female produces a **higher-pitched hoot** than the male. In most animals, larger body size correlates with lower-pitched vocalizations (larger syrinx and resonating cavity = lower fundamental frequency). The Great Horned Owl violates this expectation.
- **Possible explanations:** The pitch difference may be related to: (1) differential selection pressure on male and female vocalizations, where the deeper male hoot is optimized for long-range territorial signaling while the female's higher pitch serves different communicative functions; (2) hormonal effects on syrinx morphology that are independent of body size; (3) the female's voice being optimized for close-range communication with the male and nestlings.
- **Acoustic dimorphism:** The pitch difference is sufficient for listeners to reliably distinguish male from female by voice alone -- a useful field identification tool. The deeper voice is always the male; the higher voice is always the female.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998; Sibley 2014)

## WINTER NESTING -- THE EARLIEST NESTERS

### January-February Nesting

The Great Horned Owl pair duet reaches peak intensity during the **nesting period** -- and Great Horned Owls are the earliest nesters in North America:

- **Egg-laying:** January-February in the Pacific Northwest. This is extraordinary early -- most other birds in the PNW do not begin nesting until March-June. The Great Horned Owl is already incubating when most passerines have not yet begun courtship.
- **Why so early:** Two strategic advantages: (1) **First access to nesting sites.** Great Horned Owls do not build nests -- they appropriate the nests of Red-tailed Hawks, crows, and other species. By nesting in January-February, they claim the best sites before the original builders return from migration. (2) **Maximum development time.** Owlets fledge by April-May and have the entire summer and fall to learn hunting skills before their first winter. This long development period is critical for a species that must master complex hunting techniques.
- **Brutal conditions:** The female incubates through the harshest weather of the PNW winter -- ice storms, heavy rain, sub-zero temperatures (in interior and mountain areas), high winds. She sits on the nest essentially continuously for 30-35 days, relying on the male to bring food. Her thick feathering and metabolic heat keep the eggs viable.
- **The duet as nesting signal:** The pair duet intensifies before and during egg-laying. The duet reinforces the pair bond, coordinates nesting behavior, and reinforces territorial boundaries during the most vulnerable period. The duet is not just acoustic -- it is behavioral coordination.

### Pair Bond Duration

Great Horned Owl pairs are typically **lifelong**:

- **Multi-year bonding:** Pairs often remain together for many years, sometimes for the lifetime of both birds (Great Horned Owls can live 15-25 years in the wild). The same pair may occupy the same territory for a decade or more.
- **The duet as bond maintenance:** Annual dueting in fall and winter reinforces the pair bond. The coordinated call-and-response requires familiarity -- pairs that have dueted for years develop tighter timing and more coordinated rhythms than newly formed pairs. The duet is a measure of pair quality.
- **Territory fidelity:** Paired Great Horned Owls show extreme territory fidelity. The same nest sites, perching trees, and hunting grounds are used year after year. The pair's knowledge of their territory -- including prey distribution, safe roost sites, and nest locations -- is a shared cognitive resource that improves with time.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998)

## ACOUSTIC DIMORPHISM -- THE GENDERED HOOT

### Frequency Comparison

The male-female frequency difference creates a distinctive acoustic signature:

| Feature | Male | Female |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Fundamental frequency** | 250-400 Hz | 300-500 Hz |
| **Number of notes** | 4-5 | 5-7 |
| **Duration per series** | 2-3 seconds | 2-4 seconds |
| **Timbre** | Deeper, more resonant | Higher, slightly thinner |
| **Body mass** | 0.9-1.8 kg | 1.4-2.5 kg |
| **Size-pitch** | Smaller body, deeper voice | Larger body, higher voice |

The frequency ranges overlap (300-400 Hz is shared territory), but the overall character is consistently distinguishable. In the field, the male's hoot sounds "bassier" and the female's sounds "brighter." When they duet, the alternation between lower and higher creates a **musical dialogue** with clear harmonic structure.

### Why This Matters for the Engine

The acoustic dimorphism of the Great Horned Owl pair duet introduces a new concept to the SPS engine:

- **Gendered acoustics:** Previous entries have documented species-level vocalizations. The GHO pair duet is the first entry to explicitly document **within-species acoustic variation based on sex**. This adds a new analytical dimension.
- **Size-pitch paradox:** The reversal of the expected size-pitch relationship (larger female = higher pitch) is a significant biological finding with acoustic implications. The engine documents this paradox.
- **Paired vocalization as distinct event:** The duet is acoustically different from the solo hoot (degree 135) in structure, frequency content, and function. The engine treats it as a distinct acoustic contribution, warranting its own entry.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Two Voices in the Night

The Great Horned Owl pair duet is the **most complex nocturnal vocalization** in the PNW forest:

- **The duet spectrum:** Male at 250-400 Hz, female at 300-500 Hz. Alternating calls create a near-continuous vocal presence spanning 250-500 Hz. The combined bandwidth is wider than either voice alone. The harmonic interleaving produces a rich, complex acoustic texture.
- **Call-and-response rhythm:** Male-female-male-female, with 2-5 second gaps. The rhythm is deliberate but can accelerate during intense territorial encounters. The coordinated timing of established pairs creates a musically satisfying call-and-response pattern.
- **Nocturnal dominance:** The pair duet is the **most powerful nocturnal vocalization** in the PNW forest. Two Great Horned Owls dueting can dominate the night soundscape for hundreds of meters in every direction. No other regular nocturnal vocalization in the PNW matches the combined volume and duration.
- **Seasonal acoustic marker:** The duet peaks in November-February, making it a **winter acoustic marker** in the PNW. When you hear the paired hooting on a cold January night, it means: the Great Horned Owls are establishing territory and preparing to nest. The season's earliest nesters are declaring their intent.
- **From solo to pair:** The acoustic step from degree 135 (solo hoot, E=4) to degree 136 (pair duet, E=5) mirrors the shift from individual to partnership. The solo hoot says "I am here." The pair duet says "We are here, and this is ours."

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
