Higher-Pitched Cackling Call — E=4

The Cackling Goose’s call is a quick, sharp “cack-cack-cack” or “yip-yip” at 400–2,000 Hz. Higher and thinner than the Canada Goose’s deep “ah-HONK.” Flock cackling creates a rapid, chattering texture — more frantic than the rolling honk wall of Canada Geese. In mixed flocks, the higher pitch stands out among the deeper honks. Voice is often the fastest field separator between the two species.

The 2004 Split — From One Species to Two

In 2004, the AOU split the four smallest subspecies from Canada Goose as Branta hutchinsii (Cackling Goose). The evidence: distinct evolutionary lineages (molecular genetics), a bimodal size distribution, and different breeding ecology (high Arctic tundra vs. broad temperate range). Four subspecies: minima (Willamette Valley winter), leucopareia (Aleutian, recovered from near-extinction), taverneri (interior Alaska), hutchinsii (Arctic Canada).

Arctic Tundra Breeder — Purely Migratory

Unlike Canada Geese (massive resident populations in PNW parks), every Cackling Goose in the PNW in winter bred in the Arctic and will return there in spring. Purely migratory. Breeds on Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta, Aleutian Islands, Arctic Canada. Short Arctic summer compresses the breeding season. The Aleutian subspecies was driven to near-extinction by introduced foxes — recovered through fox eradication (conservation parallel to Trumpeter Swan at degree 128).
