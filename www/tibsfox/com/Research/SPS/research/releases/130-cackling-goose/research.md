## E=4 -- THE CACKLING FLOCK

### Why E=4

The Cackling Goose registers at E=4 -- one notch below the Canada Goose's E=5:

- **Higher-pitched, less individually powerful:** The Cackling Goose's call is higher-pitched and less resonant than the Canada Goose's deep honk. The individual voice is smaller -- matching the smaller body. The call has been described as a "cack" or a rapid, squeaky yelping that lacks the deep brassy authority of the Canada Goose.
- **Flock-driven volume:** Like the Canada Goose, the Cackling Goose's acoustic impact comes from flocks. Wintering flocks in agricultural fields can number in the thousands, and the combined cackling is substantial. But the higher pitch and thinner quality of each individual call means the flock sound is less sonically dominant than a Canada Goose flock of similar size.
- **E=4 not E=3:** Despite the smaller individual voice, the Cackling Goose's flock cackling is acoustically prominent -- the rapid, high-pitched calls carry well and are audible at considerable distance. The E=4 rating reflects flock-amplified energy that is clearly noticeable but not acoustically dominant in the way that Canada Goose flocks (E=5) are.
- **Comparison pair calibration:** E=4 for Cackling vs. E=5 for Canada creates a clean one-step energy difference within the comparison pair, consistent with the size difference and the acoustic quality difference.

## THE HIGHER-PITCHED CACKLE -- SMALL GOOSE, DIFFERENT SOUND

### A Distinctly Different Voice

The Cackling Goose's vocalization is markedly different from the Canada Goose:

- **Character:** Higher-pitched, more rapid, squeakier. The call is a quick, sharp "cack-cack-cack" or "yip-yip" -- distinctly higher and thinner than the Canada Goose's two-syllable "ah-HONK." The pitch alone is often the fastest way to separate Cackling from Canada Goose in mixed flocks.
- **Frequency range:** 400-2,000 Hz. Higher fundamental than Canada Goose (200-2,500 Hz, with most energy in the lower range). The Cackling Goose's energy is centered higher, giving the call its characteristic squeaky quality.
- **Body-size relationship:** The higher pitch directly reflects the smaller body. Smaller body = smaller syrinx = higher pitch. This is the same acoustic principle documented across the goose era: Canada Goose (large, deep honk), Cackling Goose (small, higher cackle), Brant (small-medium, low musical murmur -- the Brant's low pitch despite small size is an exception, explained by its unique syrinx structure).
- **Flock sound:** In a wintering flock, the combined cackling creates a rapid, chattering texture -- higher, thinner, more agitated-sounding than the Canada Goose's rolling honk wall. The acoustic character of a Cackling Goose flock is more "frantic" in quality.
- **Flight calls:** In flight, Cackling Geese produce the same higher-pitched cackling. A flyover of Cackling Geese sounds distinctly different from a Canada Goose flyover -- the higher pitch and more rapid call rate are the key differences.

### Acoustic Comparison: Canada vs. Cackling

## THE 2004 SPLIT -- FROM ONE SPECIES TO TWO

### The Taxonomic Decision

The Cackling Goose was recognized as a separate species from the Canada Goose in **2004** by the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU):

- **Pre-split:** Prior to 2004, all of the birds now classified as Canada Goose and Cackling Goose were considered one species (*Branta canadensis*) with up to 11 subspecies. The size range was enormous -- the smallest subspecies barely larger than a Mallard, the largest among the heaviest flying birds in North America.
- **The evidence for the split:** Molecular genetic studies in the late 1990s and early 2000s revealed that the small forms (now Cackling Goose) and the large forms (now Canada Goose) represent **distinct evolutionary lineages** -- not just a size gradient within one species. The genetic divergence was deep enough to warrant species separation.
- **The size gap:** There is a distinct size gap between the largest Cackling Goose subspecies and the smallest Canada Goose subspecies. This bimodal size distribution (two peaks rather than a smooth gradient) supported the two-species model.
- **Ecological differences:** The small forms (Cackling Goose) breed primarily on **high Arctic tundra** and have different migratory patterns than the large forms (Canada Goose), which breed in a wider range of habitats from temperate to sub-Arctic.
- **The AOU decision:** The 45th supplement to the AOU Check-list (Banks et al. 2004) elevated the four smallest subspecies to the separate species *Branta hutchinsii* (Cackling Goose). The species name honors John Richardson's naturalist colleague Thomas Hutchins, who described the small tundra geese of Hudson Bay in the 18th century.

### Post-Split Taxonomy

**Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*) -- four subspecies:**
- **B. h. minima** (Cackling or Ridgway's Cackling Goose): The smallest. Breeds on the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta, Alaska. Winters in the Willamette Valley, Oregon. The most common wintering Cackling Goose in the PNW.
- **B. h. leucopareia** (Aleutian Cackling Goose): Breeds on Aleutian Islands. Former endangered species -- recovered through fox eradication on breeding islands. Winters in California.
- **B. h. taverneri** (Taverner's Cackling Goose): Breeds in interior Alaska. Intermediate size within Cackling Goose complex. Winters in Pacific states.
- **B. h. hutchinsii** (Richardson's Cackling Goose): Breeds in Arctic Canada. Winters primarily in the southern Great Plains. Less common in the PNW.

(Sources: Banks et al. 2004; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2002)

## PNW WINTER PRESENCE -- THE WILLAMETTE VALLEY CONNECTION

### Winter Range in the Pacific Northwest

The Cackling Goose is primarily a **winter visitor** to the PNW:

- **Willamette Valley, Oregon:** The primary wintering ground for the smallest subspecies (*B. h. minima*), which breeds on the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta in western Alaska. Tens of thousands of these small geese winter in the agricultural fields of the Willamette Valley, feeding on waste grain, grass, and clover. This is the densest concentration of Cackling Geese in the PNW.
- **Skagit Valley, Washington:** Cackling Geese also winter in the Skagit Valley, mixing with the large concentrations of Canada Geese, Snow Geese, and Trumpeter/Tundra Swans. In the Skagit, Cackling Geese are outnumbered by Canada Geese but are present in agricultural fields.
- **Mixed flocks -- the identification challenge:** Cackling Geese frequently mix with Canada Goose flocks in wintering areas, creating an **identification challenge** for birders. In a mixed flock, the size difference is apparent -- Cackling Geese look noticeably smaller, with shorter necks and stubbier bills. But in a pure Cackling Goose flock without Canada Geese for comparison, the size can be harder to judge.
- **Timing:** Fall migration peaks October-November. Spring migration peaks March-April. The winter presence runs November through March.

### The Identification Challenge

Separating Cackling Goose from Canada Goose in the field is one of the classic identification challenges in PNW birding:

- **Size:** Cackling Goose is notably smaller (0.9-1.8 kg vs. 2.6-6.5 kg). In mixed flocks, the size difference is obvious. In pure flocks, the smaller size can be harder to judge without reference.
- **Bill:** Cackling Goose has a **stubbier, shorter bill** -- proportionally smaller relative to the head. The bill shape is the most reliable field mark after size.
- **Neck:** Shorter neck relative to body. The Cackling Goose looks more compact, less long-necked.
- **Head shape:** Rounder, steeper forehead. The Canada Goose has a more sloping forehead profile.
- **Voice:** Higher-pitched cackling vs. deep honking. The voice is often the first clue in mixed flocks -- the higher-pitched calls stand out among the deeper honks.
- **Breast color:** Some Cackling Goose subspecies have darker breast plumage than Canada Goose, but this is variable and not always reliable.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; eBird data)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE ARCTIC TUNDRA BREEDER

### A Different Breeding Ecology

The Cackling Goose's breeding ecology is distinct from the Canada Goose:

- **Arctic tundra:** Cackling Geese breed on remote Arctic and sub-Arctic tundra -- the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta (Alaska), the Aleutian Islands, and Arctic Canada. This is fundamentally different habitat from the Canada Goose's broad breeding range, which includes urban parks and temperate wetlands.
- **Short breeding season:** The Arctic summer is brief. Cackling Geese must complete nesting, egg incubation, and gosling rearing in a compressed timeframe. This constraint shapes their ecology: quick pair formation, rapid nest building, tight incubation schedules.
- **Predation pressure:** Arctic fox and other tundra predators are significant nest predators. The Aleutian subspecies (*B. h. leucopareia*) was driven to endangered status by Arctic foxes introduced to the Aleutian Islands for fur farming. Fox eradication programs in the 1980s-2000s enabled the population to recover -- a conservation success parallel to the Trumpeter Swan's recovery (degree 128).
- **Purely migratory:** Unlike the Canada Goose, which has massive resident (non-migratory) populations in the PNW, the Cackling Goose is **purely migratory**. Every Cackling Goose in the PNW in winter bred in the Arctic and will return to the Arctic in spring. There are no resident Cackling Goose populations.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; USFWS Aleutian Cackling Goose recovery; Mowbray et al. 2002)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound That Separates

The Cackling Goose's higher-pitched call is the most reliable way to separate it from the Canada Goose in mixed flocks:

- **The cackle spectrum:** Higher-pitched, rapid, squeaky. The "cack-cack-cack" or "yip-yip" is centered at 400-2,000 Hz with most energy in the 600-1,200 Hz band -- distinctly higher than the Canada Goose's 300-1,000 Hz energy center.
- **Flock texture:** The combined cackling of a Cackling Goose flock creates a rapid, chattering, higher-pitched texture -- more "frantic" and agitated than the rolling, deeper honk wall of a Canada Goose flock. In mixed flocks, the two textures overlay each other, creating a complex bimodal soundscape.
- **Flight calls:** In flight, the higher-pitched cackling is even more diagnostic -- the calls carry well and the pitch difference from Canada Goose is unmistakable to experienced ears.
- **The soundscape contribution:** In wintering grounds (Willamette Valley, Skagit Valley), the Cackling Goose's higher chattering adds a treble layer to the bass honking of Canada Geese. The combined soundscape is richer than either species alone.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
