*Degree 151 retrospective complete. EURASIAN COLLARED-DOVE at the one hundred fifty-first degree -- THE INVADER. In the early 2000s, a new sound appeared on PNW rooftops and power lines: "hoo-HOO-hoo" -- a three-note cooing, rhythmic, persistent, repeated for ten or twenty or thirty minutes at a stretch. The sound had not been there before. It was not the Mourning Dove's mournful five-note coo; it was not the Band-tailed Pigeon's deep forest hoot. It was new. The Eurasian Collared-Dove -- a stocky, pale bird with a thin black crescent on the back of its neck -- had arrived. The bird was native to India and southern Asia. In the 1930s it began expanding northwest through Europe, reaching Britain by the 1950s. In the 1970s someone imported Collared-Doves to a pet shop in Nassau, Bahamas. A few escaped. By the early 1980s they had reached Florida. And then the explosion began: Gulf Coast by 1990, interior states by 1995, Pacific Northwest by 2000, all of the continental United States by 2005. One hundred to one hundred fifty kilometers per year. One of the fastest avian range expansions in the history of ornithology. The engine crosses the native/non-native boundary at degree 151. The first two Columbidae were native species that evolved in North America over millions of years. The Collared-Dove is present because of a pet shop escape in the Bahamas less than fifty years ago. Its call is an invasive sound -- as much a newcomer to the acoustic landscape as the bird itself is to the ecological one. The engine documents it without editorial judgment. The three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo" is a fact of the modern PNW soundscape. It was not there a generation ago. It is there now. The landscape has absorbed the newcomer, and the landscape is changed.*
