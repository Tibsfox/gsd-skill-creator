Eurasian Collared-Dove

Streptopelia decaocto (Columbidae) — E=3 — Three-Note hoo-HOO-hoo
THIRD COLUMBIDAE. FIRST NON-NATIVE. Invasive species. Fastest avian range expansion in modern history. From Florida (1982) to PNW (2000s) in two decades. Black neck collar. Persistent three-note call. The native/non-native boundary crossed.

The Fastest Avian Invasion

From a pet shop in the Bahamas (1970s) to every U.S. state in less than 30 years. Florida 1982. Gulf Coast 1990. PNW early 2000s. Rate: 100–150 km/year. Human commensalism. Grain economy. Year-round breeding. No predator pressure at the invasion front. One of the most remarkable events in modern ornithology.

The Black Neck Collar

Diagnostic field mark: thin black crescent on the back of the neck. No native North American dove has this marking. Distinguishes from Mourning Dove: larger (150–240g vs 100–170g), stocky build, pale buff-gray plumage, square white-tipped tail (vs. Mourning Dove’s long, pointed tail).

Three-Note Call — A New Sound

“hoo-HOO-hoo” — three notes, emphasis on the second. 400–700 Hz. Repeated dozens of times with 2–5 second pauses. Calling bouts: 10–30 minutes. Most persistent Columbidae caller. Year-round. This sound did not exist in the PNW before the early 2000s. Also: harsh nasal “kwahh” in flight.
