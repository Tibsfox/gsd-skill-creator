# Degree 151 -- Eurasian Collared-Dove (*Streptopelia decaocto*)
## Deep Research Mission: The Invader -- Fastest Avian Range Expansion, Urban Colonizer, Three-Note Call

**Release:** 151 / 360
**Degree:** 151 (one hundred fifty-first rotation from origin)
**Species:** Eurasian Collared-Dove (*Streptopelia decaocto*, Columbidae)
**Sound:** three-note hoo-HOO-hoo -- a distinctive, rhythmic three-note cooing with emphasis on the second note, repeated persistently from rooftops and power lines
**Energy:** 3
**Ecosystem:** Urban and suburban habitats. Strongly associated with human settlement -- grain elevators, feedlots, bird feeders, shopping centers, residential areas. Avoids deep forest. The Eurasian Collared-Dove is an **invasive species** in North America -- introduced to the Bahamas in the 1970s, escaped/released, reached Florida by the early 1980s, and has since colonized virtually all of the continental United States, reaching the PNW in the early 2000s. **One of the fastest avian range expansions in history.** E=3 reflects the persistent but soft cooing -- louder than the Mourning Dove but still ambient background, the soundtrack of suburban development.
**Status:** Original entry. **THIRD COLUMBIDAE.** First non-native Columbidae in the engine. The native-to-non-native progression continues: Band-tailed (native forest, 149), Mourning Dove (native open, 150), Eurasian Collared-Dove (invasive urban, 151), Rock Pigeon (feral, 152).

### The Native/Non-Native Boundary

Degree 151 crosses a boundary. The first two Columbidae were native species that evolved in North America over millions of years. The Eurasian Collared-Dove is an invader -- present in North America for less than 50 years, spreading at a rate that staggers biologists.

This boundary matters:

- **Native species** have co-evolved with the landscape. Their calls, behaviors, and ecological roles are part of the ecosystem's deep history.
- **Invasive species** are ecological disruptors. Their calls are new sounds in the soundscape. Their presence alters competition, predation, and resource dynamics.

The engine documents both without editorial judgment. The Collared-Dove is part of the PNW soundscape now -- its call is a fact of the acoustic environment. The engine records what is, not what should be.

## E=3 -- THE PERSISTENT CALLER

### Why E=3

The Eurasian Collared-Dove registers at E=3, matching both native Columbidae:

- **Moderate amplitude:** Louder than the Mourning Dove but still not a high-energy call. The three-note coo carries 100-250 meters in urban conditions. More assertive than the Mourning Dove's mournful whisper but not the broadcast call of a wren or thrush.
- **Persistent repetition:** The Collared-Dove calls frequently and persistently -- more than any other PNW Columbidae. A single bird may repeat the three-note call dozens of times with only brief pauses. The persistence is notable but the individual calls remain soft.
- **Urban background:** Like the Mourning Dove, the Collared-Dove's call becomes part of the urban ambient soundscape. Familiar but unremarkable. Background texture. E=3 captures the ambient quality.
- **Monotony:** The three-note pattern is invariable. No variation, no elaboration, no song development. The monotony contributes to the background perception -- the brain tunes it out.

## THE FASTEST AVIAN INVASION

### From Turkey to the World

The Eurasian Collared-Dove's range expansion is one of the most remarkable events in modern ornithology:

**Phase 1: European Expansion (1900s-1970s)**
- **Origin:** Native to India, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar. The species was confined to southern Asia until the early 20th century.
- **European colonization:** Beginning in the 1930s, the Eurasian Collared-Dove began expanding northwest from Turkey through the Balkans and into Europe. By the 1950s it had reached Britain. By the 1970s it occupied virtually all of Europe.
- **Speed:** The European expansion covered approximately 3,000 km in 40 years -- an average range expansion of 75 km per year. At the time, this was considered one of the most remarkable avian range expansions ever documented.

**Phase 2: North American Colonization (1970s-present)**
- **Bahamas introduction:** In the mid-1970s, Eurasian Collared-Doves were imported to a pet shop in Nassau, Bahamas. A few escaped or were released.
- **Florida establishment:** By the early 1980s, Collared-Doves had reached southern Florida (approximately 1982). A founding population established in the Miami area.
- **Continental spread:** From Florida, the expansion was explosive:
  - **1990:** Gulf Coast states (Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas)
  - **1995:** Interior states (Arkansas, Oklahoma, Kansas)
  - **2000:** Pacific Northwest, Northern Plains
  - **2005:** Virtually all of continental United States except heavily forested regions
  - **2010:** British Columbia, Alberta, southern Canada
- **Rate:** The North American expansion averaged approximately 100-150 km per year -- even faster than the European expansion. From Florida to the PNW in approximately 20 years.

### Why So Fast?

Several factors enabled the extraordinary expansion:

- **Human commensalism:** The Collared-Dove thrives near human settlement. It feeds at grain elevators, cattle feedlots, bird feeders, and waste grain sources. As it spread, it found suitable habitat (human settlement) everywhere.
- **Year-round breeding:** Like the Mourning Dove, the Collared-Dove breeds multiple times per year. High reproductive output fuels population growth at the invasion front.
- **No predator pressure:** The expanding front encounters naive predator populations that have not learned to target the new species. Raptors, cats, and other predators eventually learn, but the front moves faster than the learning curve.
- **Grain economy:** The industrial grain economy of the American Midwest and West provides unlimited food. Grain elevators, feedlots, and agricultural waste are Collared-Dove buffets.
- **Climate tolerance:** Despite Asian tropical origins, the Collared-Dove tolerates a wide range of climates -- from Florida heat to PNW rain to Northern Plains cold. The species is far more climate-flexible than its origin range would suggest.

(Sources: Romagosa 2012; Fujisaki et al. 2010; Hengeveld 1988; USGS)

## THE BLACK NECK COLLAR

### Identification and Distinction from Mourning Dove

The Eurasian Collared-Dove is often confused with the native Mourning Dove, but several features distinguish them:

| Feature | Eurasian Collared-Dove | Mourning Dove |
|---------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Collar** | Black crescent on nape | No collar |
| **Tail** | Square, white-tipped | Long, pointed, white-edged |
| **Size** | Larger (150-240g) | Smaller (100-170g) |
| **Build** | Stocky, chunky | Slim, elegant |
| **Color** | Pale buff-gray, uniform | Warm brown, spotted wings |
| **Call** | Three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo" | Five-note "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo" |
| **Habitat** | Near buildings, grain | Open fields, suburban |
| **Origin** | Invasive (Asia) | Native (N. America) |

The **black crescent on the back of the neck** is the diagnostic field mark -- a thin, dark collar that gives the species its common name. No native North American dove has this marking.

### Competition with Native Doves

The relationship between the Eurasian Collared-Dove and native Columbidae is an active area of research:

- **Mourning Dove competition:** The Collared-Dove occupies similar suburban and agricultural habitat as the Mourning Dove. Some studies suggest local Mourning Dove declines where Collared-Doves establish, but continent-wide data does not show a strong competitive displacement effect. The two species may coexist through subtle habitat partitioning.
- **Band-tailed Pigeon isolation:** The Band-tailed Pigeon's forest habitat is distinct from the Collared-Dove's urban/agricultural preference. Direct competition between these species is minimal.
- **Rock Pigeon overlap:** The Collared-Dove and Rock Pigeon overlap extensively in urban areas. Both are non-native. Their coexistence in urban environments suggests sufficient niche separation.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Romagosa 2012; Sibley 2014)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Invasion -- Three-Note Urban Cooing

The Eurasian Collared-Dove's acoustic contribution is **a new sound in the PNW soundscape** -- one that was not present before the early 2000s:

- **Pattern:** "hoo-HOO-hoo" -- three notes, emphasis on the second (higher and louder). The pattern is distinctive and diagnostic. No native North American dove produces a three-note call with this rhythm.
- **Frequency:** Fundamental: 400-700 Hz. Slightly higher than the Mourning Dove. The second note may reach 800 Hz.
- **Duration:** Individual three-note phrase: 1.5-2.0 seconds. Repeated with 2-5 second pauses. A calling bout may last 10-30 minutes with dozens of repetitions.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate. Carries 100-250 meters in urban conditions. Louder than the Mourning Dove, quieter than most songbirds. E=3 but near the upper boundary.
- **Persistence:** The most notable acoustic quality. The Collared-Dove repeats its call far more persistently than the Mourning Dove. The monotonous repetition can dominate the dawn soundscape in areas where the species is abundant.
- **Perch:** Rooftops, power lines, TV antennas, tall trees near buildings. Always in the urban/suburban matrix.
- **Season:** Year-round. Unlike many songbirds, the Collared-Dove calls in every month. The call is slightly more frequent during breeding season but never absent.
- **Harsh call:** In addition to the three-note coo, the Collared-Dove produces a harsh, nasal "kwahh" or "kreear" during flight and aggression. This harsh call is often the first indication of a Collared-Dove's presence.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Romagosa 2012; engine internal analysis)
