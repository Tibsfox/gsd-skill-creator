Hoary Marmot

Marmota caligata
loud penetrating whistle — BACK-TO-BACK SCIURIDAE with Yellow-bellied Marmot (75), MARMOT PAIR, largest North American marmot (5–7 kg), E=5 (highest energy mammal), named WHISTLER MOUNTAIN (BC), longest hibernation (7–8 months), third consecutive alpine (74–75–76), alpine meadows and talus, sixteenth consecutive original CSV

The Whistler — Named a Mountain and a City

The Hoary Marmot’s alarm whistle is so loud and characteristic that Whistler Mountain and the city of Whistler, British Columbia — host of the 2010 Winter Olympics — are named for this species. No other species in the engine has named a major geographic feature through its vocalization. E=5 — the loudest mammalian call in the engine. Audible at 300–500+ meters across open alpine terrain. The sound became a place name.

Back-to-Back Sciuridae (75–76) — The Marmot Pair

The Yellow-bellied Marmot (75) and Hoary Marmot (76) are the marmot pair — same genus (Marmota), different species, consecutive degrees. Fourth family pair after Anatidae (58–59), Castoridae (61–62), Mustelidae (67–68). Lower vs. higher elevation (1,000–2,500 m vs. 1,500–3,000+ m). Smaller vs. larger (2–5 kg vs. 5–7 kg). E=4 vs. E=5. Chirp vs. whistle.

Longest Hibernation in the Engine — 7–8 Months

The Hoary Marmot hibernates 7–8 months per year (October–April) — the longest hibernation of any mammal in the engine. Active only 4–5 months per year. Body temperature drops from 37°C to 3–5°C. Heart rate from 100+ bpm to 3–5 bpm. Nearly doubles body mass pre-hibernation. Contrast with Pika (74): the Pika stores hay and does NOT hibernate. Same alpine habitat, opposite strategies — the two solutions: farm or sleep.

Third Consecutive Alpine/Rocky (74–75–76)

American Pika (74), Yellow-bellied Marmot (75), Hoary Marmot (76). Three consecutive alpine/talus species. The engine’s deepest penetration into high-elevation ecology. Three size classes: tiny (Pika, 120–185 g), medium (Yellow-bellied, 2–5 kg), large (Hoary, 5–7 kg). Three acoustic signatures: bark, chirp, whistle. One alpine habitat, three ecological niches.

The Chris Pureka Parallel

Degree 76 pairs Hoary Marmot with Chris Pureka in Part A — Folk, E=2, self-released, queer/nonbinary. Both defined by voice — Pureka’s androgynous register, the marmot’s penetrating whistle. Both at margins — queer folk circuit, alpine edge. Both carry names that mislead — “hoary” suggests ancient, an androgynous voice defies assumptions.

The Sound: Loud Penetrating Whistle (77th Acoustic Element)

The whistle: A sharp, high-pitched alarm call — approximately 2,000–8,000 Hz, with peak energy at 3,000–5,000 Hz. Duration 0.3–1.0 seconds. A true whistle — tonal, sustained, piercing. Given singly or in series of 2–5. The whistle carries across entire alpine basins [1, 2, 3, 4].

Why E=5: The loudest mammalian vocalization in the engine. (1) Volume — audible at 300–500+ meters. (2) Conspicuousness — diurnal, social, on prominent boulders. (3) Cultural impact — named Whistler Mountain. (4) Ecological prominence — dominant acoustic presence in alpine habitat.

Marmot pair contrast: Yellow-bellied Marmot (75): sharp chirp, E=4, 0.1–0.3 s. Hoary Marmot (76): penetrating whistle, E=5, 0.3–1.0 s. Higher, larger, louder.

Taxonomy: Marmota caligata

Etymology: Marmota from French marmotte, ultimately from Latin murem montis (“mouse of the mountain”). caligata from Latin “wearing boots” — for the dark fur on the lower legs. Named by Eschscholtz (1829). “Hoary” = white/gray-haired [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Sciuridae — back-to-back with Yellow-bellied Marmot (75). Genus Marmota — ~14 species worldwide. Three in the PNW: M. flaviventris (Yellow-bellied, 75), M. caligata (Hoary, 76), M. olympus (Olympic Marmot) [1, 2, 3].

Physical Description: The Alpine Giant

Size: 620–820 mm total, 5–7 kg at peak autumn mass — the largest North American marmot. Nearly doubles body mass between spring (3–4 kg) and autumn (5–7 kg) [1, 2, 3].

Pelage: Silver-gray body with frosted “hoary” appearance. Dark brown/black face. White patches on muzzle and forehead. Dark “boots” on lower legs — the diagnostic feature (Latin caligata).

Ecology: Colonial Herbivore

Habitat: Alpine meadows with adjacent talus. 1,500–3,000+ meters. Requires BOTH open meadow (foraging) and rocky talus (denning). The meadow-talus interface [1, 2, 3].

Social: Colonial, 5–15 individuals. Sentinels on elevated boulders. Playful — juveniles wrestle and chase. Relaxed demeanor when not alarmed.

Diet: Alpine grasses, sedges, wildflowers. No food storage (unlike Pika). Relies on fat reserves for 7–8 months of hibernation.

Predators: Golden Eagle (aerial), Wolverine (burrow invader), Red Fox (71, middle elevations), Short-tailed Weasel (67, burrow access), American Marten (65, treeline transition).
