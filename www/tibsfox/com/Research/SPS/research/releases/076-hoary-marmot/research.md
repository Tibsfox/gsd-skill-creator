## THE HOARY MARMOT: THE WHISTLER

The Hoary Marmot is the **largest North American marmot** and the species that gave **Whistler, British Columbia** its name. A burly, silver-gray alpine ground squirrel weighing 5-7 kg (11-15 lbs), the Hoary Marmot sits on exposed boulders in alpine meadows and delivers a sharp, high-pitched alarm whistle that carries across entire basins. The whistle is so loud and so characteristic that the mountain was named for it. The marmot IS the whistler [1, 2, 3].

This is the engine's **second consecutive marmot** -- the **marmot pair** (Yellow-bellied at 75, Hoary at 76). Back-to-back Sciuridae, like the back-to-back Anatidae (58-59), the beaver pair (61-62), and the weasel pair (67-68). The marmot pair parallels these prior family pairs but with a crucial elevation distinction: the Yellow-bellied Marmot at degree 75 inhabits lower-elevation rocky outcrops and talus at 1,000-2,500 meters, while the Hoary Marmot at degree 76 occupies higher alpine meadows and talus at 1,500-3,000+ meters. The Hoary Marmot is the LARGER, HIGHER, LOUDER of the pair. Same family, same genus, different altitudinal zones.

This is also the **third consecutive alpine/rocky species** in the engine (Pika 74, Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76) -- an alpine run that has given the engine its deepest penetration into high-elevation ecology. The engine has moved from the forest floor to the alpine summit across three consecutive degrees.

## TAXONOMY: *Marmota caligata*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Marmota** -- from the French *marmotte*, itself from the Romansch *murmont* (mountain mouse), ultimately from Latin *murem montis* (mouse of the mountain). The genus contains fourteen species worldwide, distributed across the mountains of North America, Europe, and Asia [1, 2].

- **caligata** -- from Latin *caligata* ("wearing boots" or "booted"), referring to the dark brown to black fur on the Hoary Marmot's lower legs and feet, which contrast with the silvery-gray body and create the appearance of wearing dark boots. Named by Johann Friedrich Eschscholtz in 1829 from specimens collected in Alaska. *Marmota caligata*: "the booted mountain mouse" [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Hoary Marmot (standard -- "hoary" meaning white or gray-haired, describing the silver-gray pelage), whistler (historical, the name that gave Whistler Mountain its name), rock chuck (regional), gray marmot (descriptive). The Hoary Marmot is the archetypal alpine marmot of the Pacific Northwest [1, 2, 3].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (fifteenth mammalian entry; thirteenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- THIRD Rodentia in the engine after American Beaver (61-62) and Yellow-bellied Marmot (75). Rodentia returns.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- BACK-TO-BACK SCIURIDAE with Yellow-bellied Marmot (75). Same family, consecutive degrees. Sciuridae's second entry. The squirrel family at degrees 75 and 76 -- the **marmot pair**.
- **Genus:** *Marmota* -- approximately fourteen species worldwide. Three in the PNW region: *M. flaviventris* (Yellow-bellied, degree 75), *M. caligata* (Hoary, degree 76), and *M. olympus* (Olympic Marmot, endemic to the Olympic Mountains). The marmot pair represents two of three PNW species [1, 2].
- **Species:** *M. caligata* -- the Hoary Marmot. Distributed from Alaska through British Columbia, the Cascades, and the northern Rockies. The most northerly distributed North American marmot [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE ALPINE GIANT

### Body Form

The Hoary Marmot is a massive ground squirrel -- the **largest marmot in North America** [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 620-820 mm (24-32 inches) including tail. Body alone: 450-570 mm.
- **Weight:** 5-7 kg (11-15 lbs) at peak autumn mass (pre-hibernation). Spring emergence: 3-4 kg. The weight gain cycle is dramatic -- a Hoary Marmot may nearly double its body weight between spring emergence and autumn hibernation.
- **Tail:** 170-250 mm (7-10 inches). Bushy, reddish-brown to dark brown. Used for thermoregulation and social signaling.
- **Body shape:** Stocky, robust, broad-chested. Short, powerful legs. The overall impression is of a burly, solidly built animal -- like a small badger but rounder, perched on a boulder.

### Pelage: The Silver-Gray Coat

The Hoary Marmot's pelage is distinctive and gives the species its common name [1, 2, 3]:

- **Body:** Silver-gray with brown or tawny underfur showing through, creating a frosted or "hoary" appearance. The guard hairs are silver-tipped, giving the coat a grizzled quality.
- **Head:** Dark brown to black on the crown and face, with white patches on the muzzle and forehead. The dark face contrasts sharply with the silver body.
- **Feet and lower legs:** Dark brown to black -- the "boots" that give the species its Latin name *caligata*. The dark extremities against the silver body are the most diagnostic visual feature.
- **Belly:** Lighter gray to buff.

### Distinguishing from Yellow-bellied Marmot

In the Pacific Northwest, the two marmot species can overlap at middle elevations [1, 2]:

- **Size:** Hoary Marmots are substantially larger (5-7 kg vs. 2-5 kg).
- **Color:** Hoary Marmots are silver-gray. Yellow-bellied Marmots are yellowish-brown with a distinctive yellow belly patch.
- **Elevation:** Hoary Marmots are generally found at higher elevations (1,500-3,000+ m). Yellow-bellied Marmots are found at lower elevations (1,000-2,500 m). Where ranges overlap, the Hoary is typically higher.
- **Range in PNW:** Hoary Marmots are found throughout the Cascades and Olympics. Yellow-bellied Marmots are less common in the PNW, more typical of the eastern Cascades and Great Basin.

## THE SOUND: LOUD PENETRATING WHISTLE -- 77th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Whistle That Named a Mountain

The Hoary Marmot's alarm whistle is one of the most iconic sounds of the alpine Pacific Northwest. It is the sound that gave **Whistler Mountain** (and by extension, the ski resort and city of Whistler, British Columbia) its name. The whistle is [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 2,000-8,000 Hz, with peak energy at 3,000-5,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 0.3-1.0 seconds per whistle, sharp onset and offset
- **Structure:** A single, sharp, descending whistle -- high to slightly lower pitch. Clean tonal quality. Not a chirp or bark but a true whistle, produced by air forced through a narrow opening.
- **Amplitude:** **E=5** -- LOUD. Audible at 300-500+ meters across open alpine terrain. The loudest mammalian alarm call in the engine. Carries across entire alpine basins. The whistle is far louder than the American Pika's "eenk" bark (degree 74, E=4) and far louder than the Yellow-bellied Marmot's chirp (degree 75, E=4). The energy difference between the marmot pair is the clearest acoustic distinction.
- **Series:** Often given in series of 2-5 whistles when a predator is detected. The first whistle alerts; subsequent whistles provide directional information.

### Why E=5

The Hoary Marmot's whistle warrants E=5 -- the highest energy level assigned to any mammal in the engine -- because [1, 2, 3]:

1. **Volume:** The whistle is genuinely loud. Hikers routinely hear Hoary Marmot whistles before seeing any marmot. The sound carries across alpine basins.
2. **Conspicuousness:** Hoary Marmots are diurnal, social, and frequently visible on prominent boulders. The whistle accompanies a visible, conspicuous animal.
3. **Cultural impact:** The whistle is famous enough to have named a mountain, a city, and a ski resort. No other PNW mammal has named a geographic feature through its vocalization.
4. **Ecological prominence:** In alpine habitat, the Hoary Marmot is the largest and most conspicuous mammal. It is the dominant acoustic presence in its habitat.

### Acoustic Contrast with the Marmot Pair

|---|---|---|

The Hoary Marmot's whistle is longer, louder, lower-pitched at onset, and carries farther. The energy jump from E=4 to E=5 reflects a genuine acoustic difference: the Hoary Marmot's whistle is in a different loudness class from the Yellow-bellied Marmot's chirp.

## ECOLOGY: THE ALPINE MEADOW ARCHITECT

### Habitat

The Hoary Marmot inhabits alpine and subalpine meadows with adjacent talus or boulder fields [1, 2, 3]:

- **Elevation:** 1,500-3,000+ meters in the Washington Cascades and Olympics. Typically at or above treeline.
- **Microhabitat:** The Hoary Marmot requires BOTH open meadow (for foraging) AND rocky talus or boulder fields (for denning and predator escape). The interface between meadow and rock is the ideal habitat. Unlike the American Pika (degree 74), which is an obligate talus species, the Hoary Marmot uses talus as shelter but forages extensively in alpine meadows.
- **Den sites:** Deep burrows in talus or beneath large boulders. Burrow systems can extend 2-4 meters into the ground, with multiple entrances. Winter hibernation burrows are deep enough to remain above freezing even when surface temperatures reach -30 C.

### Hibernation: The Longest in the Engine

The Hoary Marmot hibernates for **7-8 months** per year (October-April) -- the **longest hibernation period of any mammal in the engine** [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Duration:** Approximately 200-240 days. The Hoary Marmot is active for only 4-5 months per year.
- **Depth:** Deep torpor. Body temperature drops from 37 C to approximately 3-5 C. Heart rate drops from 100+ beats per minute to approximately 3-5 beats per minute. Metabolic rate drops to approximately 1-2% of active rate.
- **Fat reserves:** The pre-hibernation weight gain is critical. A Hoary Marmot may weigh 3-4 kg at spring emergence and 5-7 kg at autumn entry -- nearly doubling body mass in 4-5 months of active foraging.
- **Contrast with Pika (74):** The American Pika does NOT hibernate -- it stores hay and remains active year-round beneath snowpack. The Hoary Marmot DOES hibernate -- it stores fat and shuts down. Same alpine habitat, opposite winter strategies. The two species share talus habitat but solve the winter survival problem differently.

### Social Structure

Hoary Marmots are colonial and social [1, 2, 3]:

- **Colony size:** Typically 5-15 individuals. One dominant male, several adult females, juveniles, and yearlings.
- **Communication:** The alarm whistle is the colony's primary communication tool. Different whistle patterns convey different predator types (aerial vs. terrestrial). The colony's sentinels take turns watching from elevated boulders.
- **Play behavior:** Hoary Marmots are notably playful. Juveniles wrestle, chase, and tumble. Adults sun-bathe on warm boulders. The species has a relaxed, social demeanor when not alarmed -- a contrast with the solitary, territorial American Pika.

### Diet

The Hoary Marmot is an alpine herbivore [1, 2, 3]:

- **Primary diet:** Alpine grasses, sedges, wildflowers, and herbs. Lupine, paintbrush, and mountain heather are preferred foods.
- **Foraging strategy:** Graze in alpine meadows during morning and late afternoon, retreating to talus during midday heat. The foraging pattern is constrained by both predator risk and thermoregulation.
- **No food storage:** Unlike the American Pika (degree 74), the Hoary Marmot does NOT cache food for winter. It relies entirely on accumulated fat reserves to survive hibernation. This is the fundamental contrast between the two alpine survival strategies: the Pika farms; the marmot fattens.

### Predators

The Hoary Marmot has several predators in the alpine ecosystem [1, 2, 3]:

- **Golden Eagle:** Primary aerial predator. Can take adult marmots.
- **Wolverine:** Can dig into burrow systems. Rare but significant predator.
- **Red Fox (degree 71):** Hunts marmots at middle elevations where ranges overlap.
- **Short-tailed Weasel (degree 67):** Can enter burrows. More significant predator of juveniles.
- **American Marten (degree 65):** Subalpine forest predator that overlaps at treeline.

## WHISTLER MOUNTAIN: THE MARMOT THAT NAMED A PLACE

The city of **Whistler, British Columbia** -- one of the world's most famous ski resorts and host of the 2010 Winter Olympics -- is named for the Hoary Marmot. The mountain was called "Whistler" by the London Mountain Club in the early 1900s because of the loud, piercing whistles of the Hoary Marmots that inhabited its alpine slopes [1, 3, 6].

No other species in the engine has named a major geographic feature through its vocalization. The Hoary Marmot's whistle is not just ecologically significant -- it is toponymically significant. The sound became a place name. The marmot's voice is permanently embedded in the geography of the Pacific Northwest.

The Whistler connection is also a bridge between the engine's two geographic anchors: the PNW (Washington and Oregon) and the broader Pacific Northwest including British Columbia. Whistler is 120 km north of Vancouver, extending the engine's geographic reach beyond the US border.
