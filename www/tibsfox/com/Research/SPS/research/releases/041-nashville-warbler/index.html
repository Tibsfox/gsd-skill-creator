Nashville Warbler

Leiothlypis ruficapilla
see-bit see-bit teedle-teedle — the partition, 9th Parulidae (extending record), FOURTH consecutive warbler (unprecedented), NEW GENUS Leiothlypis breaks the Setophaga streak — a misnomer species named for a place it merely passes through

The 42nd Acoustic Element: The Partition

A two-part song: see-bit see-bit (rhythmic staccato, measured, crisp) then teedle-teedle (rapid trill, tumbling, blurred). The transition is abrupt — the song flips between two modes as if a switch is thrown. Named “the partition” for five simultaneous divisions: (1) the song’s internal staccato/trill split, (2) the genus partition from Setophaga to Leiothlypis, (3) the habitat partition from canopy to brush, (4) the naming partition between Nashville (misnomer) and its real home (western mountains), (5) the successional partition between old-growth and post-disturbance scrub.

The first acoustic element named for song ARCHITECTURE — how a song is organized, not what it sounds like.

New Genus: Leiothlypis Breaks the Setophaga Streak

Degrees 38–40 were all Setophaga — the displacement trio. Degree 41 shifts to Leiothlypis (“smooth-feathered”), the genus of the Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20). The genus returns after 21 degrees. Both Leiothlypis species serve as closers: Orange-crowned closed the quintet, Nashville closes the quartet. The quiet bookend.

From bold to smooth: Setophaga = bold patterns, wing bars, high contrast. Leiothlypis = smooth plumage, no wing bars, hidden crown patches. The genus shift at degree 41 is a shift from display to subtlety.

The Misnomer: Named for a Rest Stop

Alexander Wilson collected this bird near Nashville, Tennessee, in 1811, during spring migration. The bird was passing through. It does not breed in Nashville. It does not winter in Nashville. The name is a permanent record of a brief encounter — one of ornithology’s most famous naming errors. Compare with Hermit Warbler (occidentalis = “western” — accurate). The engine’s first misnomer species.

The Smooth-Feathered Red-Cap

Leiothlypis ruficapilla — “Smooth-feathered red-cap.” Leiothlypis = Greek leios (smooth) + thlypis (finch). Ruficapilla = Latin rufus (red) + capillus (cap) = “red-capped.” The species is named for a hidden rufous crown patch beneath gray feathers — a feature almost never visible in the field. Formerly Vermivora ruficapilla; split into Leiothlypis by Sangster (2008) based on molecular phylogenetics.

The Sound: Two-Part Song

See-bit see-bit teedle-teedle — 2–4 staccato couplets at ~5,000–8,500 Hz followed by a rapid, lower trill at ~4,000–7,000 Hz [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Total duration ~1.5–2.5 s. The first half is measured and crisp; the second half is rapid and blurred. The engine’s first truly partitioned song — two distinct textures joined at the hip.

Nashville introduces the engine’s first clean binary song. Previous warblers sang in one continuous texture. Nashville divides its song into two halves of different character — announcement (staccato) followed by release (trill).

Parulidae — 9 Members (Extending Record)

Ninth Parulidae. Leads Hirundinidae and Turdidae (5 each) by four. FOUR consecutive Parulidae (38–41) — unprecedented. Three arcs plus coda:

  • Quintet (14–20): Five species, five genera, five song endings. All E=3.
  • Trio (38–40): Three Setophaga, displacement cascade. All E=4.
  • Coda (41): Leiothlypis returns — genus shift, habitat descent to brush.
  • Overarching: Acoustic catalog → ecological drama → successional gradient.

9 of ~115 North American Parulidae ≈ 8%.

The Hidden Crown — A Leiothlypis Signature

Two Leiothlypis species. Two hidden crown patches. Orange-crowned (degree 20) = orange crown hidden under olive (celata = “hidden”). Nashville (degree 41) = rufous crown hidden under gray (ruficapilla = “red-capped”). Both named for features almost never seen in the field. The genus hides its colors.

Contrast with Setophaga: The quartet (16, 38–40) wore bold patterns on display. The Leiothlypis pair conceals its most distinctive features. Billboard vs. whisper.

Brush — A New Ecosystem

Brush: low, dense, scrubby vegetation in early successional habitat. A new ecosystem for the engine. The Nashville Warbler is disturbance-dependent — it breeds in regenerating clearcuts (5–15 years post-logging), post-fire scrub, montane ceanothus/manzanita, and east-slope Cascades openings. It needs habitat created by destruction.

The disturbance paradox: Hermit Warbler (degree 40) needs old-growth mountain conifer. Nashville Warbler (degree 41) needs post-disturbance brush. Consecutive degrees, opposite successional requirements. Preserving old-growth reduces brush. Creating brush destroys old-growth. Cannot optimize both.

E=4 Continues — 18th Degree, 21 Without E≤3

E=4 now at 18 degrees (43.9%). E=3 absent for 21 consecutive degrees (since degree 20) — surpassing the 20-degree milestone. E=2 absent for 28 degrees. E=1 absent for 29 degrees. Four consecutive E=4 from the same family. The lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum is absent from degrees 21–41.

Western Subspecies in the PNW

The PNW Nashville Warbler is L. r. ridgwayi — the western subspecies, with brighter yellow underparts and a bolder eye-ring than eastern birds [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Breeds on the eastern Cascades slopes, Okanogan Highlands, Blue Mountains, and scattered locations in western Washington in disturbed habitats. Strict long-distance migrant — winters in highland scrub of Mexico and Central America. No PNW winter presence.

Ground Nesting

The Nashville Warbler nests on the ground — concealed under brush, grass, or ferns. One of the few warblers in the engine to nest at ground level. The nesting descent mirrors the singing descent: the Setophaga trio nests high (3–40 m) and sings high. Nashville nests on the ground and sings from 1–3 m brush tops. Four consecutive Parulidae, from mountain canopy crown to ground level.

Theory Nodes

P

The Partition foundational

The 42nd acoustic element. Two-part song: staccato couplets + rapid trill. After the thinning’s attenuation (40), the partition introduces structural duality. Five simultaneous divisions: song structure, genus boundary, habitat shift, naming disconnect, successional gradient. First element named for song ARCHITECTURE.

4

Four-Consecutive Parulidae foundational

Unprecedented. Degrees 38–41 across two genera (Setophaga + Leiothlypis), three ecosystems, two stories (displacement + successional gradient). The longest same-family run in the engine.

C

The Hidden Crown foundational

Two Leiothlypis species, two hidden crown patches (orange at 20, rufous at 41). Both named for invisible features. The genus conceals its most distinctive ornament. A Leiothlypis signature.

N

The Misnomer intermediate

Named for Nashville, Tennessee — a place the bird merely passes through during migration. Alexander Wilson, 1811. One of ornithology’s most famous naming errors. The engine’s first misnomer species. The name is a permanent record of a brief encounter.

B

Brush & Disturbance-Dependence intermediate

New ecosystem. The Nashville Warbler needs disturbance — fire, logging, storms — to create its brush habitat. Opposite of Hermit Warbler’s old-growth requirement. The four-warbler run maps a full successional gradient: climax forest to pioneer scrub.

4

E=4 — 21 Degrees Without E≤3 intermediate

18th degree at E=4 (43.9%). Twenty-one-degree milestone for E=3 absence. Four consecutive E=4 from same family. Lower three-fifths of spectrum absent since degree 21.
