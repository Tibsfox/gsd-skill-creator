## Context: The Fourth Consecutive Warbler -- But a New Genus

Four consecutive Parulidae. Degrees 38, 39, 40, 41. This has never happened in the engine. No family has appeared four times in a row. The warbler dominance deepens -- and yet degree 41 is structurally different from what came before.

Degrees 38-39-40 were all *Setophaga* -- the same genus, three consecutive times, mapping a displacement cascade. Degree 41 breaks the genus streak. The Nashville Warbler is *Leiothlypis ruficapilla*, not *Setophaga*. The family continues but the genus shifts. This matters because it means the engine's deepest family run is also its most taxonomically varied at the genus level -- four consecutive Parulidae across TWO genera (*Setophaga* for 38-40, *Leiothlypis* for 41).

And *Leiothlypis* is not new to the engine. The Orange-crowned Warbler at degree 20 was *Leiothlypis celata* -- the species that closed the quintet, the last E=3, the evaporation. Now, twenty-one degrees later, the genus returns. The Nashville Warbler is the Orange-crowned's closest relative in the engine.

The Nashville Warbler is also a **misnomer species** -- named for Nashville, Tennessee, where Alexander Wilson first described it in 1811 during migration [1, 2]. The bird does not breed in Nashville. It does not winter in Nashville. It merely passed through. The name is an accident of encounter -- one of ornithology's most famous naming errors, a species forever identified with a place it barely visits. The engine's first misnomer.

## Taxonomy: The Smooth-Feathered Red-Cap

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Parulidae (New World Warblers)
- **Genus:** *Leiothlypis* (Sangster, 2008)
- **Species:** *Leiothlypis ruficapilla* (Wilson, 1811)
- **Common Name:** Nashville Warbler

### Etymology

*Leiothlypis* -- from Ancient Greek *leios* (smooth) + *thlypis* (a kind of finch, from *thlypo*, "to crush") = "smooth finch" or more precisely "smooth-feathered" [1, 2]. The name refers to the relatively plain, smooth plumage compared to the patterned *Setophaga* warblers. This is the second *Leiothlypis* in the engine after the Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20, *L. celata*).

*Ruficapilla* -- from Latin *rufus* (red, rufous) + *capillus* (hair, cap) = "red-capped" or "rufous-haired" [1, 2]. Referring to the rufous crown patch -- a small, hidden chestnut spot on the crown of the adult bird, usually concealed beneath olive-gray head feathers. The species is literally named for a feature you almost never see.

Full translation: **"Smooth-feathered red-cap."** A description of the bird's two defining traits: plain plumage and hidden crown patch. The name is accurate but invisible -- you need to be holding the bird in your hand to see the feature it is named for.

### The Misnomer

Alexander Wilson collected a Nashville Warbler near Nashville, Tennessee, in 1811, during spring migration [1, 2]. He described and named it based on that encounter. The bird was passing through. Nashville lies in the middle of the species' migration route -- not in its breeding range (northern forests and western mountains) and not in its wintering range (Mexico and Central America). The Nashville Warbler is named for a rest stop.

This is one of the most famous misnomers in North American ornithology. Other examples include the Cape May Warbler (named for Cape May, New Jersey, where it was collected during migration) and the Tennessee Warbler (another Wilson misnomer from the same 1811 trip) [1, 2]. Wilson was on a collecting expedition through the southeastern United States, encountering multiple warbler species during spring migration. He named them for where he found them, not where they lived. The Nashville Warbler has carried the name of a place it barely visits for over two hundred years.

The engine's first misnomer species. Previous species were named for: physical features (*petechia*, *nigrescens*, *ruficapilla*), geographic range (*occidentalis*), people (*townsendi*), or behavior ("hermit"). The Nashville Warbler is named for a geographic accident -- a moment of encounter, not a relationship.

### The Genus Shift: *Setophaga* to *Leiothlypis*

Degrees 38-39-40 were all *Setophaga*. Degree 41 is *Leiothlypis*. This break matters.

*Setophaga* and *Leiothlypis* were formerly lumped into broader genus concepts -- many of the current *Leiothlypis* species were once placed in *Vermivora* (the "worm-eaters") [1, 2, 10]. The genus *Leiothlypis* was erected in 2008 by Sangster based on molecular phylogenetic analysis showing that the orange-crowned, Nashville, Virginia's, Colima, Lucy's, and Tennessee warblers formed a distinct clade separate from the true *Vermivora* species (Golden-winged and Blue-winged Warblers) [10, NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The split recognized that the smooth-plumaged, relatively plain warblers were a natural group distinct from the wing-barred *Vermivora*.

The genus name itself -- "smooth-feathered" -- captures the essential difference from *Setophaga*. The *Setophaga* quartet (16, 38, 39, 40) wore bold patterns: vivid yellow, black-and-white ink, yellow-and-black face, yellow beacon on gray. *Leiothlypis* wears smoother, less contrasting plumage. The genus shift at degree 41 is a shift from bold pattern to subtle wash, from dramatic marking to understated olive-gray-yellow tones.

|---|---|---|
| Plumage style | Bold patterns, high contrast | Smooth, plain, low contrast |
| Wing bars | Usually present | Absent |
| Tail spots | White tail spots present | No white tail markings |

The shift from *Setophaga* to *Leiothlypis* at degree 41 is also a shift in habitat: from canopy-dwellers (*Setophaga* trio at 38-40 sang from the tops of tall conifers) to a brush-dweller (Nashville Warbler sings from low, dense vegetation). The genus change maps a habitat change. The engine descends from the mountain conifer crown (40, Hermit Warbler at 20-50 m) to the brushy clearing (41, Nashville Warbler at 0-5 m).

### Two Subspecies

Two recognized subspecies [1, 2, 7]:

1. **Eastern Nashville Warbler** (*L. r. ruficapilla*) -- breeds from the Great Lakes region through New England and the Maritime Provinces. Migrates through the Mississippi Valley.
2. **Western Nashville Warbler** (*L. r. ridgwayi*) -- breeds from British Columbia south through the western mountains to California. The PNW breeding population.

The western subspecies (*ridgwayi*) was described by Ridgway and differs slightly from the eastern in having brighter yellow underparts, a more extensive white eye-ring, and a slightly longer tail [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The two populations are separated by a wide gap across the Great Plains, suggesting a historical range split during Pleistocene glaciation [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. They are sometimes treated as separate species candidates, though currently retained as subspecies [1, 2].

The PNW Nashville Warbler is the western subspecies -- *ridgwayi*.

## The Sound: See-Bit See-Bit Teedle-Teedle

### The Two-Part Song

The Nashville Warbler's song is distinctive among engine warblers for its **two-part structure** [1, 2, 7, 8, 9]. The song divides cleanly into two halves:

1. **First half -- the rhythmic staccato:** *See-bit see-bit see-bit* -- a series of 2-4 sharp, two-noted phrases, evenly spaced, rhythmic, almost mechanical in their regularity. Each "see-bit" is a quick upslur-downslur couplet. The tempo is measured and deliberate.

2. **Second half -- the rapid trill:** *Teedle-teedle-teedle* -- a lower-pitched, faster trill that tumbles out after the staccato section. The trill is more rapid, less structured, and lower in frequency than the first half. It sounds like the song's orderly beginning dissolving into excited chatter.

The transition between the two halves is abrupt. There is no gradual shift. The song flips from staccato to trill as if a switch has been thrown. This binary structure -- two halves, two tempos, two textures, one song -- is the Nashville Warbler's acoustic signature [1, 2, 7, 8].

### Vocalization Details

- **Primary song:** 2-4 staccato couplets (*see-bit*) followed by a lower, rapid trill (*teedle-teedle*). Total duration approximately 1.5-2.5 seconds [1, 2, 7, 8].
- **Frequency range:** Approximately 4,000-8,500 Hz, with the staccato section higher (approximately 5,000-8,500 Hz) and the trill lower (approximately 4,000-7,000 Hz) [NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Tempo:** The first half is moderate (approximately 3-4 couplets per second). The second half accelerates (approximately 8-12 notes per second in the trill) [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The tempo doubles across the partition.
- **Timbre:** The staccato half is bright, crisp, sharp-edged -- each *see-bit* is clean and distinct. The trill half is softer, more blurred, the individual notes smearing together. Two textures in one song [1, 2, 7, 8].
- **Volume:** Delivered at moderate volume from exposed perches in brush habitat -- low perches (1-3 m), often at the top of a small shrub or sapling. Carries well in open brushy habitat [1, 2].
- **Calling note:** A sharp *tsik* or *pink* -- higher and sharper than the *Setophaga* call notes [1, 2].

The two-part structure is unusual among PNW warblers. Most warbler songs in the engine are relatively uniform in texture throughout: the Yellow Warbler's ascending *sweet-sweet-sweet-sweeter-sweeter* is consistently musical. The Common Yellowthroat's *witchety-witchety-witchety* is consistently rhythmic. The Orange-crowned's descending trill is consistently homogeneous. The Nashville Warbler's song is the first Parulidae song in the engine that **changes character midway through**. It is two songs joined at the hip.

### The 42nd Acoustic Element: The Partition

After the thinning's attenuation and ecological mourning, the partition introduces **structural duality**. The partition is the 42nd acoustic element -- named for the Nashville Warbler's distinctive two-part song that divides cleanly into two halves of different character.

This is the 42nd element in the progression:

Silence (0) --> Cascade (1) --> Drone (2) --> Language (3) --> Motif (4) --> Metronome (5) --> Question (6) --> Harmony (7) --> Threshold (8) --> Variation (9) --> Swarm (10) --> Trill (11) --> Noise (12) --> Persistence (13) --> Narrative (14) --> Urgency (15) --> Brightness (16) --> Catchiness (17) --> Aspiration (18) --> Resolution (19) --> Release (20) --> Hammer (21) --> Resonance (22) --> Groove (23) --> Hoarseness (24) --> Familiarity (25) --> Liquidity (26) --> Articulation (27) --> Flexibility (28) --> Roughness (29) --> Synthesis (30) --> Whisper (31) --> Buzz (32) --> Cadenza (33) --> Dialect (34) --> Thrum (35) --> Sonic Boom (36) --> Filament (37) --> Drawl (38) --> Canopy Buzz (39) --> The Thinning (40) --> **The Partition (41)**

The partition differs from the thinning (degree 40) in fundamental character: the thinning was about diminishment -- a song becoming less. The partition is about **division** -- a song that is explicitly two things. The thinning was one continuous texture, thinning. The partition is two distinct textures, juxtaposed. The thinning mapped a species dissolving. The partition maps a song -- and a genus boundary -- that splits cleanly in two.

The partition carries multiple structural resonances:

1. **Acoustic duality:** The song itself is partitioned into staccato and trill -- two tempos, two textures, two pitch bands in one utterance.
2. **Genus partition:** Degree 41 partitions the four-consecutive Parulidae run into two genus groups: *Setophaga* (38-40) and *Leiothlypis* (41). The partition is the boundary between them.
3. **Habitat partition:** The canopy-dwelling *Setophaga* trio gives way to a brush-dwelling *Leiothlypis*. The vertical partition from high canopy to low scrub.
4. **Naming partition:** The song's two-part structure mirrors the Nashville Warbler's split identity -- named for a place it does not live, carrying a hidden crown patch named in a species epithet nobody sees. The bird is partitioned between what it is called and what it is.
5. **The Leiothlypis return:** Orange-crowned (20) and Nashville (41) are both *Leiothlypis*, separated by 21 degrees. The genus partitions the warbler family's appearances in the engine: *Leiothlypis* bookends the gaps (closing the quintet at 20, closing the quartet at 41), while *Setophaga* fills the dramatic center.

The partition is the engine's first element named for **internal song structure**. Previous elements described qualities (silence, noise, buzz, thrum), processes (thinning, synthesis), or effects (resonance, familiarity). The partition describes how a song is **organized** -- its architecture rather than its timbre or meaning. This opens a new category of acoustic element: structural forms as named elements.

## Plumage: The Hidden Crown

### The Understated Bird

The Nashville Warbler's plumage is a study in subtlety after three degrees of *Setophaga* boldness [1, 2, 7]:

- **Head:** Blue-gray head (adult male) with a conspicuous white eye-ring -- the eye-ring is the most prominent head feature. The eye-ring is bolder and more complete in the western subspecies (*ridgwayi*) [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Crown patch:** A small rufous (chestnut-red) crown patch, usually concealed beneath the gray crown feathers [1, 2, 7]. This is the feature the species is named for -- *ruficapilla*, "red-capped" -- and you almost never see it. The patch is exposed during aggressive displays or when the head feathers are ruffled by wind. It is a hidden color.
- **Throat and breast:** Bright yellow -- vivid, saturated, extending from chin through belly in adult males. The yellow underparts are the bird's most visible feature.
- **Back:** Olive-green, plain, no wing bars, no tail spots. The smooth plumage that gives *Leiothlypis* its name.
- **Overall impression:** A small, compact warbler with a gray head, bold white eye-ring, yellow underparts, and olive back. No wing bars, no tail markings, no bold face pattern. Clean and understated.

### The Hidden Crown Patch -- Echoes of Degree 20

The Nashville Warbler's rufous crown patch is hidden beneath gray feathers. The Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20, *Leiothlypis celata*) has an orange crown patch hidden beneath olive feathers. Two *Leiothlypis* species, both with **hidden crown colors** named in their species epithets, both invisible in the field.

| Species | Degree | Crown Patch | Epithet | Visibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Orange-crowned Warbler | 20 | Orange, hidden under olive | *celata* = "hidden" | Rarely seen |
| **Nashville Warbler** | **41** | **Rufous, hidden under gray** | ***ruficapilla* = "red-capped"** | **Rarely seen** |

The Orange-crowned Warbler was named for its hidden feature (*celata* literally means "hidden" or "concealed"). The Nashville Warbler was named for its hidden feature (*ruficapilla* describes a crown patch you can't see). The two *Leiothlypis* in the engine share not just a genus but a naming convention: species identified by invisible ornaments. The genus itself seems to attract cryptic naming -- smooth-feathered birds with secret crowns.

This is the engine's second "hidden color" after the Orange-crowned Warbler. The hidden crown patch becomes a *Leiothlypis* signature: two species, two hidden crowns, two names describing what you cannot see.

## Brush Ecosystem: The New Habitat

### A New Ecosystem for the Engine

Brush. Low, dense, scrubby vegetation -- the habitat that grows back after disturbance [1, 2, 7]. Not forest canopy. Not riparian willow. Not freshwater marsh. Brush is the engine's newest ecosystem, distinct from everything that has come before.

The Nashville Warbler breeds in **brushy clearings, regenerating logged areas, young second-growth forest, chaparral edges, and montane shrub habitat** [1, 2, 7, 8]. In the PNW, this means:

- **Regenerating clearcuts:** 5-15 years after logging, when the ground cover has grown into dense shrub but before the tree canopy closes [1, 2, 7].
- **Post-fire habitat:** Burned forest areas where brush and shrubs are the dominant vegetation, before conifer regeneration overtops them [1, 2].
- **Mountain shrub zones:** Transitional areas between forest and subalpine meadow, dominated by ceanothus (*Ceanothus* spp.), manzanita (*Arctostaphylos*), and snowbrush [1, 2, 7, NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **East-slope Cascades:** Open, dry, brushy habitat on the eastern slopes of the Cascades, often associated with ponderosa pine or Douglas fir openings [1, 2].
- **Chaparral-like scrub:** Dense, low vegetation in rain-shadow areas.

### The Vertical Descent

The engine's vertical gradient now spans from the extreme canopy crown to the ground-level brush:

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Extreme crown | 40 | Hermit Warbler | 20-50 m [NV] | Mountain conifer |
| Crown canopy | 39 | Townsend's Warbler | 15-45 m [NV] | Conifer canopy |
| Mid canopy (dry) | 38 | Black-throated Gray | 5-15 m [NV] | Oak-pine |
| Mid canopy (wet) | 16, 20 | Yellow, Orange-crowned | 3-12 m | Riparian, shrubby slope |

The four-consecutive Parulidae run traces a dramatic vertical descent: Hermit Warbler at the extreme canopy crown (40), Townsend's at the crown (39), BTG at mid canopy (38) -- and now Nashville down in the brush (41). The engine descends from the top of the tallest mountain conifer to low scrubby vegetation in four degrees. The *Setophaga* trio sang from the trees. The Nashville Warbler sings from beneath where the trees used to be -- from the brush that grows back after the trees are gone.

### Brush as Disturbance Habitat

Brush habitat is transient. It exists in the gap between destruction and regrowth -- after fire, after logging, after windfall, before the forest returns. The Nashville Warbler is a **disturbance-dependent species** [1, 2, 7]. It needs habitats that exist because something was destroyed. In the PNW, this means:

- **Fire creates Nashville Warbler habitat.** A forest fire that destroys mature conifers opens the ground to sunlight. Brush grows. Nashville Warblers colonize. When the conifers regenerate and close the canopy (20-40 years later), the Nashville Warblers leave [1, 2, 7, NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Logging creates Nashville Warbler habitat.** A clearcut goes through the same successional sequence. The Nashville Warbler is among the species that benefit from early successional habitat in managed forests [1, 2].
- **Fire suppression threatens Nashville Warbler habitat.** The long history of fire suppression in western forests has reduced the amount of early successional brush habitat available. Paradoxically, the Nashville Warbler may have been *more* common historically when fire was more frequent [NEEDS VERIFICATION].

This creates a strange conservation dynamic: the Nashville Warbler benefits from disturbance, while the Hermit Warbler (degree 40) needs old-growth mountain conifer. The two species are ecological opposites within the same family -- one needs the forest to be destroyed, the other needs the forest to be ancient. The four-consecutive Parulidae run maps not just a displacement cascade but a **successional gradient**: old-growth canopy (*Setophaga* trio) to post-disturbance brush (*Leiothlypis*).

## The Leiothlypis Return: Degrees 20 and 41

### Orange-crowned and Nashville -- Genus Siblings in the Engine

*Leiothlypis celata* (degree 20) and *Leiothlypis ruficapilla* (degree 41) are the engine's second genus pair (after *Geothlypis* at degrees 14 and 17, and *Setophaga* which has grown to a quartet). The two *Leiothlypis* share:

- **Hidden crown patches** -- orange (*celata*) and rufous (*ruficapilla*), both concealed
- **Smooth, plain plumage** -- no wing bars, no tail spots, no bold face patterns
- **Low-to-mid perch heights** -- shrubby slope (20) and brush (41)
- **Trill-based songs** -- Orange-crowned sings a descending trill; Nashville's second half is a rapid trill
- **Quintet/quartet closers** -- Orange-crowned closed the warbler quintet (degree 20); Nashville closes the warbler quartet (degree 41)

But they also differ meaningfully:

| Feature | Orange-crowned (degree 20) | Nashville (degree 41) |
|---|---|---|
| Energy | 3 (the last E=3) | 4 |
| Season | Partial migrant | Summer (strict migrant) |
| Song structure | One continuous trill | **Two-part (staccato + trill)** |
| Habitat | Shrubby slope (established) | **Brush (post-disturbance)** |
| Crown patch | Orange, described as "hidden" | Rufous, described as "red-capped" |
| Eye-ring | Faint or absent | **Bold, white, complete** |

The Nashville Warbler's bold white eye-ring is its most conspicuous field mark -- a feature the Orange-crowned lacks. In the field, Nashville Warbler is identified by what it *shows* (eye-ring, yellow throat), while Orange-crowned is identified by what it *lacks* (no wing bars, no eye-ring, no strong markings). The two *Leiothlypis* are diagnostically inverse: one is identified by presence, the other by absence.

### The Leiothlypis Bookend Pattern

*Leiothlypis* appears at structurally significant positions in the engine:

- **Degree 20:** Orange-crowned Warbler closes the warbler quintet. Last E=3. The "evaporation" -- the end of Act I of the warbler story.
- **Degree 41:** Nashville Warbler closes the four-consecutive Parulidae run. The partition. The end of the *Setophaga* streak.

Both times, *Leiothlypis* serves as the **closing genus** -- the genus that arrives to end a warbler sequence. The flashy *Setophaga* species occupy the center of the warbler arcs. The plain *Leiothlypis* species close them. Bold drama followed by smooth understatement. The genus is the quiet bookend.

## PNW Distribution: The Western Subspecies

### Breeding Range

The western Nashville Warbler (*L. r. ridgwayi*) breeds across a broad range in western North America [1, 2, 7]:

- **British Columbia:** Southern interior, Okanagan
- **Washington:** Eastern Cascades slopes, Okanogan Highlands, Blue Mountains. Less common on the west side of the Cascades but present in disturbed habitats [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Oregon:** Eastern Cascades slopes, Blue Mountains, Siskiyou Mountains. Associated with brushy clearings and post-fire habitat [1, 2].
- **Southward:** Through the Sierra Nevada, mountains of California, to Baja California [1, 2].

In the PNW specifically, the Nashville Warbler is most common in the **eastern Cascades transition zone** -- where dry forest meets brush, where fire history has created openings, where the landscape is a patchwork of young growth and mature stands [1, 2, 7].

### Migration

The Nashville Warbler is a **long-distance migrant** [1, 2]:

- **Spring arrival:** Late April to mid-May in the PNW [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Breeding season:** May through July
- **Fall departure:** August to September
- **Winter range:** Mexico and Central America -- highland brush and scrub

Like the Hermit Warbler (degree 40), the Nashville Warbler is a strict migrant that abandons the PNW entirely in winter. Unlike the Townsend's Warbler (degree 39), which winters along the PNW coast, the Nashville has no winter PNW presence. Two consecutive degrees (40, 41) of strict summer migrants.

### Nesting

The Nashville Warbler nests **on the ground** -- one of the few warblers in the engine to do so [1, 2, 7]. The nest is placed on the ground, typically concealed under a clump of grass, fern, or brush. This ground-nesting habit is distinctive among the engine's warblers:

| Degree | Species | Nest Location |
|---|---|---|
| 14 | MacGillivray's | Low shrub (0.3-1 m) |
| 15 | Wilson's | Ground to low shrub |
| 16 | Yellow | Shrub to low tree (1-5 m) |
| 17 | Yellowthroat | Ground to low vegetation |
| 20 | Orange-crowned | Ground (concealed) |
| 38 | BTG | Mid canopy (3-10 m) [NV] |
| 39 | Townsend's | High canopy (8-20 m) [NV] |
| 40 | Hermit | High canopy (15-40 m) [NV] |
| **41** | **Nashville** | **Ground (concealed under brush)** |

The nesting height tracks the singing height: the *Setophaga* trio nests high and sings high. The Nashville Warbler nests on the ground and sings from low perches. The four-consecutive Parulidae run maps both a vocal and a nesting descent from mountain canopy crown to ground level.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN Status:** Least Concern [1, 2]
- **Population trend:** Stable to slightly declining in some areas [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Estimated population:** ~45 million [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **BBS trend:** Slight decline in western populations, stable in eastern [NEEDS VERIFICATION]

### Threats

1. **Habitat loss through fire suppression.** The Nashville Warbler is disturbance-dependent. Reduced fire frequency in western forests means less early successional brush habitat [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION].
2. **Loss of scrubby clearings to development.** Urban and agricultural expansion removes brushy habitat.
3. **Brood parasitism.** Brown-headed Cowbirds (*Molothrus ater*) parasitize Nashville Warbler nests [1, 2, NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Ground-nesting makes them vulnerable.
4. **Climate change.** Shifts in forest disturbance patterns could affect availability of brush habitat [NEEDS VERIFICATION].

### The Disturbance Paradox

The Nashville Warbler creates a conservation paradox different from the Hermit Warbler's (degree 40). The Hermit Warbler needs old-growth mountain conifer -- ancient, undisturbed forest. The Nashville Warbler needs the opposite: recently disturbed, regenerating, brushy habitat. The two consecutive species (40, 41) represent opposite ends of the successional spectrum. Conservation for one (preserving old growth) inherently reduces habitat for the other (less disturbance means less brush). The engine's warbler run documents this tension: you cannot simultaneously maximize old-growth conifer and early-successional brush in the same landscape. The forest's successional clock cannot be set to all times at once.
