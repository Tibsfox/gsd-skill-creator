# Release 041 Retrospective -- Nashville Warbler (Degree 41)

**Release:** 041 of 360
**Species:** Nashville Warbler (*Leiothlypis ruficapilla*)
**Sound:** see-bit see-bit teedle-teedle
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the forty-second release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-41), the ninth Parulidae -- extending the family's record lead to four -- and the FOURTH CONSECUTIVE warbler, an unprecedented same-family run. But degree 41 is structurally different from the *Setophaga* trio that preceded it: the Nashville Warbler is *Leiothlypis ruficapilla*, not *Setophaga*. The genus shift breaks the three-consecutive genus streak while continuing the family streak. The engine needed this partition: same family, different genus, different habitat, different story.

Six structural contributions:

1. **The partition -- the 42nd acoustic element.** A two-part song with a rhythmic staccato first half (*see-bit see-bit*) and a rapid trill second half (*teedle-teedle*). The transition is abrupt -- the song flips between two modes. Named "the partition" for five simultaneous divisions: (1) the song's internal staccato/trill split, (2) the genus partition from *Setophaga* to *Leiothlypis*, (3) the habitat partition from canopy to brush, (4) the naming partition between Nashville (misnomer) and the bird's real home (western mountains), (5) the successional partition between old-growth and post-disturbance scrub. The first acoustic element named for song ARCHITECTURE rather than quality, process, or effect.

2. **Four-consecutive Parulidae -- unprecedented.** Degrees 38-39-40-41 form the engine's longest same-family consecutive run. The quartet spans two genera (*Setophaga* for 38-40, *Leiothlypis* for 41), three ecosystems (oak-pine, conifer canopy/mountain conifer, brush), and tells two stories (displacement cascade + successional gradient). No family has appeared four consecutive times before.

3. **The *Leiothlypis* return.** The genus that closed the warbler quintet at degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler) returns at degree 41 to close the warbler quartet. Both *Leiothlypis* species serve as closers -- the quiet bookend after the dramatic center. Both have hidden crown patches (orange and rufous). The genus is a structural signature.

4. **The misnomer.** Alexander Wilson collected the Nashville Warbler near Nashville, Tennessee, in 1811 during migration. The bird was passing through -- it does not breed or winter in Nashville. The engine's first misnomer species, named for a geographic accident rather than a geographic range.

5. **The hidden crown -- a *Leiothlypis* signature.** Two *Leiothlypis* in the engine, both with concealed crown patches named in their species epithets, both invisible in the field. Orange-crowned (*celata* = "hidden") and Nashville (*ruficapilla* = "red-capped"). The genus hides its colors beneath smooth plumage.

6. **Brush ecosystem -- disturbance-dependence.** A new ecosystem for the engine. The Nashville Warbler breeds in post-disturbance brush: regenerating clearcuts, post-fire scrub, montane ceanothus. It needs habitat created by destruction -- the ecological opposite of the Hermit Warbler (degree 40), which needs ancient, undisturbed mountain conifer. The four-consecutive Parulidae run maps a full successional gradient from old-growth canopy to pioneer scrub.

## The Four-Consecutive Warbler Run

### Degrees 38-39-40-41: Unprecedented

| Degree | Species | Genus | Ecosystem | Element | Ecological Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 38 | Black-throated Gray | *Setophaga* | Oak-pine | The Drawl | Secondary displacement victim |
| 39 | Townsend's | *Setophaga* | Conifer canopy | The Canopy Buzz | Displacement aggressor |
| 40 | Hermit | *Setophaga* | Mountain conifer | The Thinning | Primary displacement victim |
| **41** | **Nashville** | ***Leiothlypis*** | **Brush** | **The Partition** | **Disturbance-dependent colonizer** |

The quartet tells two stories:

1. **Degrees 38-40 (Setophaga trio):** A displacement cascade. Three species of the same genus, two being displaced by the third. The aggressor (Townsend's) sandwiched between its victims (BTG and Hermit). An ecological drama in three degrees.

2. **Degree 41 (Leiothlypis coda):** A different story entirely. Not displacement but disturbance-dependence. The Nashville Warbler does not compete with the *Setophaga* trio -- it occupies a different successional stage. It lives in the habitat that grows after forest destruction. The quartet's fourth member is a commentary on the landscape itself: forests are destroyed, brush grows, Nashville Warblers colonize, forests regrow, Nashville Warblers leave.

### The Vertical Descent

The quartet traces a dramatic descent:

| Degree | Species | Singing Height | Habitat |
|---|---|---|---|
| 38 | BTG | 5-15 m [NV] | Mid canopy (oak-pine) |
| 39 | Townsend's | 15-45 m [NV] | Crown canopy (conifer) |
| 40 | Hermit | 20-50 m [NV] | Extreme crown (mountain conifer) |
| **41** | **Nashville** | **1-3 m** | **Brush tops** |

From the extreme crown of the tallest mountain conifer (50 m) to the top of a 2-meter shrub. The engine descends through the entire forest column in four degrees -- and then drops below the forest entirely, into the brush that grows where forest used to be.

### The Genus Partition

The quartet divides cleanly at the genus boundary:

- **Setophaga (38-40):** Bold patterns, high canopy, buzzy songs, displacement narrative. The dramatic center.
- **Leiothlypis (41):** Smooth plumage, low brush, partitioned song, disturbance ecology. The quiet coda.

This maps to the acoustic elements: the *Setophaga* trio contributed drawl, canopy buzz, and thinning -- three variations on the buzzy timbre. The *Leiothlypis* coda contributes the partition -- a fundamentally different song architecture. The genus shift is a structural shift in every dimension: plumage, habitat, altitude, song form, and ecological narrative.

## What Worked

### 1. The genus shift provides relief within the family run
Four consecutive Parulidae is unprecedented and risks warbler fatigue. But the genus shift from *Setophaga* to *Leiothlypis* at degree 41 provides real structural variation -- different plumage style, different habitat, different song architecture, different ecological story. The quartet feels like a narrative with a coda, not a repetitive catalog.

### 2. "The partition" is structurally novel
The first element named for song architecture opens a new category. The five simultaneous divisions (song, genus, habitat, naming, succession) give the name conceptual richness without the ecological weight of "the thinning." The partition is analytical where the thinning was elegiac.

### 3. The hidden crown creates a genus-level pattern
Two *Leiothlypis* species, two hidden crowns, both serving as sequence closers. This is now a demonstrated structural pattern. The pattern recognition adds depth to the engine's taxonomy tracking.

### 4. The disturbance paradox is genuine
The Nashville Warbler needing the opposite of what the Hermit Warbler needs -- in consecutive degrees -- creates a real conservation tension that resists simple resolution. The engine maps real ecological complexity.

### 5. Brush is a new and meaningful ecosystem
The engine's ecosystem vocabulary grows. Brush is ecologically distinct from all previous habitats and carries its own narrative (transience, disturbance-dependence, successional impermanence).

A Black-headed Grosbeak sat in a bigleaf maple and poured out a warble so smooth and rich it sounded like a robin -- but it was not a robin. High in the Douglas fir canopy, a Western Tanager sang the same warble but hoarse, rough, grainy -- a robin's song run through sandpaper. And then on the lawn, on the fence post, on the rooftop at dawn -- the American Robin. Cheerily cheer-up cheerio. The archetype. Over the harbor, a dark shape swept through the air, iridescent purple-black, mouth open, pouring out a rich, gurgling, liquid song. The Purple Martin. The stream. Above a mountain lake, a small iridescent green shape darted and flickered, firing rapid crystalline chips. The Violet-green Swallow. The rain. Over a freshwater marsh, a compact blue-green and white shape lifted from a nest box post, and from it came a tumbling, liquid, twittering gurgle. The Tree Swallow. The brook. Along the river, where the cutbank dropped straight to the water, a small brown bird emerged from a hole in the earth and said brrit -- rough, buzzy, dry. The Rough-winged Swallow. The sandpaper. Over the farmland, where the barn door stood open and the wire fences crossed the pasture, a bird swept in low with its long forked tail streaming behind, and from a wire it poured out a long, liquid, twittering warble -- the most complex swallow song the engine had heard. The Barn Swallow. The river. The quintet closed. The archetype arrived last. In the mountain ash at the edge of the park, a tree full of sleek, crested shapes -- warm brown, black masks, yellow tail bands, tiny drops of red wax -- eating berries and see-see-seeing to each other in a frequency range near the edge of hearing. The Cedar Waxwing. The whisper. And then one morning, without warning, the tree was full of different birds. Larger. Grayer. Chunkier. Bold white and yellow wing markings flashing. Cinnamon undertails catching the light. Buzzing, hissing, assertive -- bzeee-bzeee-bzeee -- hundreds of voices filling the block. The Bohemian Waxwing. The buzz. E=5. High in the eastern Cascades, where the ponderosa stands open and the air smells of sun-warmed bark and resin, a small bird with a crimson crown sat at the top of the tallest snag and sang a cadenza -- a rolling, tumbling warble incorporating the crossbill's call, the tanager's hoarse phrase, the robin's carol, the chickadee's fee-bee. The Cassin's Finch. The cadenza. The soloist played every instrument. And somewhere in a Douglas fir canopy, a flock of small birds hung upside down from cones, working the scales with bills that cross at the tips. Kip-kip-kip. Sharp. Percussive. The Red Crossbill. The dialect. The type call that sorts the tribe. And then -- a sound that is not a call, not a song, not a drum. A hum. Low, continuous, steady, the sound of air pushed through feathers fifty-two times every second. A copper blur hovering at a red columbine. The Rufous Hummingbird. The thrum. Three grams of defiance. The red flame-bearer. And then a different sound from a different hummingbird. Not a hum but a crack. Not continuous but instantaneous. A small iridescent green bird with a head dipped in molten magenta perches on a bare branch in a Seattle backyard in January. It climbs. Forty meters. It folds its wings. It falls. Twenty-seven meters per second. Three hundred and eighty-five body lengths per second. The tail spreads. The feather vibrates. A chirp. Explosive. The sonic boom. Anna's Hummingbird. The one that stayed. And now -- higher than either of them went, above the tree line, above the urban gardens, above the flower meadows, in a subalpine meadow at 3,000 meters where the paintbrush burns red against gray talus -- a sound so small it might not be there at all. A soft, rising tsee-tsee. The smallest bird in North America. The filament. The thinnest thread of sound. The pilot light that still burns. And now, eighteen degrees after the last warbler evaporated into silence, below the mountain meadow, below the hummingbird trio's extremes, in a dry woodland where Garry oaks stand in open sun and ponderosa pines drop their needles on bunchgrass -- a bird drawn in black ink on white paper, with one small dot of yellow between its eye and its bill, sits on an oak branch in the dappled light and sings. Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy. Slow. Buzzy. Unhurried. The Black-throated Gray Warbler. The drawl. And one degree later, one ecosystem over, the dry woodland giving way to wet conifer forest -- at the very top, where the crown meets the sky -- a bird with a face painted in yellow and black sits at the crown of a Douglas fir and sings. Zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee. Buzzy. Ascending. The terminal phrases climbing. The Townsend's Warbler. The canopy buzz. And one degree later, one mountain higher, the wet conifer forest giving way to drier mountain conifers, white fir and red fir replacing Douglas fir and western hemlock -- at the very top of the very tallest tree, a bird with a face made entirely of yellow and a body made entirely of gray sits at the extreme crown of a mountain hemlock and sings. Seely seely seely see. Thin. High. Reedy. The Hermit Warbler. The thinning. And now -- one degree later, the mountain conifer giving way to something else entirely. Not taller forest. Not adjacent canopy. Down. The elevation drops. The tall conifers are gone. In their place: low, dense, scrubby growth -- brush, ceanothus, manzanita, snowbrush, young willows, the thick tangle that springs up after the forest was cut or burned fifteen years ago. The canopy that reached fifty meters at degree 40 has dropped to two meters at degree 41. And at the very top of a snowbrush -- the highest point in the brush, which is barely the height of a person -- a small gray-headed warbler with a bold white eye-ring and bright yellow underparts and an olive-green back and no wing bars and no tail spots and a hidden rufous crown patch that you will almost certainly never see sits and opens its bill and sings. See-bit see-bit teedle-teedle. The first half is rhythmic. Measured. Each see-bit a clean couplet -- upslur, downslur, sharp and crisp. The second half is rapid. A tumbling trill -- teedle-teedle-teedle -- faster, lower, blurred, the notes smearing together. The transition is abrupt. One moment the song is staccato. The next it is trill. Two textures in one song. Two tempos. Two pitch bands. A song partitioned at its center. The Nashville Warbler. Leiothlypis ruficapilla. The smooth-feathered red-cap. Named for Nashville, Tennessee, where Alexander Wilson collected it during spring migration in 1811. The bird was passing through. It does not breed in Nashville. It does not winter in Nashville. It has carried the name of a rest stop for two hundred and fifteen years. The ninth warbler. The fourth consecutive Parulidae. The engine's longest same-family run. But a new genus -- Leiothlypis, not Setophaga. The genus that closed the quintet at degree 20 returns to close the quartet at degree 41. The quiet bookend. The smooth-feathered closer. The hidden crown. The misnomer. The disturbance-dependent bird singing from the top of the brush that grew where the forest used to be. See-bit see-bit teedle-teedle. The partition.*
