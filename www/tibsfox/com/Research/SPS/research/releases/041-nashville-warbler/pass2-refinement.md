# Degree 41 — Nashville Warbler: Pass 2 Refinement

## Paired Release v1.49.542 — NASA 1.42 Alignment

*Cross-referenced with NASA 1.42 (Gemini 7, Borman/Lovell, 14-day endurance record, two weeks in a capsule).*

---

The Nashville Warbler is the ninth Parulidae and the fourth consecutive warbler — but in a new genus, *Leiothlypis*, breaking the *Setophaga* trio at degrees 38-40. It is also the engine's first misnomer species: named for Nashville, Tennessee, where Alexander Wilson collected it during spring migration in 1811, but the bird does not breed in Nashville, does not winter in Nashville, and barely visits Nashville at all. The name is an accident of encounter — a species forever identified with a place it was merely passing through. The two-part song ("see-bit see-bit teedle-teedle") partitions into a rhythmic staccato opening and a rapid trill close, delivered from brushy clearings and regenerating logged areas — the undergrowth that springs back after disturbance.

Gemini 7 was the ultimate endurance mission: Frank Borman and Jim Lovell spent fourteen days inside a spacecraft with the interior volume of the front seat of a Volkswagen Beetle. Fourteen days. Two men. No room to stand, no room to stretch, no privacy, no escape. The capsule was a brushy thicket of wires, switches, and equipment with barely enough space for two human bodies. The Nashville Warbler breeds in exactly this kind of dense, confined habitat — low, thick brush where movement is constrained, where the bird is more heard than seen, where space is measured in centimeters rather than meters. Both the crew and the bird inhabit spaces defined by confinement.

The misnomer resonates with Gemini 7's dual identity. The mission is remembered primarily as the *target vehicle* for Gemini 6A's rendezvous — the passive partner, the thing that was found, rather than the thing that found. But Gemini 7's own endurance record was the mission's primary objective. It broke every duration record. It proved the human body could survive the time needed for a lunar mission. Like the Nashville Warbler, named for the wrong place and associated with the wrong story, Gemini 7 is often remembered for someone else's achievement (the rendezvous) rather than its own (the endurance). The Nashville Warbler passes through Nashville. Gemini 7 passes through the rendezvous narrative. Both carry names and associations that obscure what they actually accomplished.
