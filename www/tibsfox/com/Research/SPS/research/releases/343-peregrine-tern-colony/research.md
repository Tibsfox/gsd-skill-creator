## THE PEREGRINE FALCON -- THE FASTEST PREDATOR

### Falco peregrinus: The Architecture of Speed

The Peregrine Falcon at its maximum:

- **The stoop velocity.** The Peregrine Falcon's stoop is the fastest movement of any animal on Earth. In a full power stoop -- a near-vertical dive from altitude with wings folded -- the Peregrine reaches 240-390 km/h (150-240 mph), with exceptional documented cases exceeding 390 km/h. The highest reliably documented stoop speed in a controlled study (Ken Franklin, 2005) was 389 km/h (242 mph). A hunting Peregrine stooping at full speed creates a perceptible pressure wave in the air ahead of its body; at close range, the rush of air is audible as a whoosh or roar of disturbed atmosphere.
- **The stoop aerodynamics.** In the stoop, the Peregrine folds its wings tightly against its body, reducing frontal area and drag. The falcon's body is shaped for this: dense plumage, a notched bill (the tomial tooth, used to sever vertebrae of prey), nictitating membranes to protect the eyes from airstream debris, and nasal baffles (bony protrusions in the nares) that deflect high-speed airflow to allow breathing during the stoop. The stoop is a total body optimization for velocity.
- **The kill mechanics.** The Peregrine does not typically grab prey from behind -- it hits the prey with a closed foot (the fist) or rakes it with the talons at full speed, delivering a lethal or stunning blow. The prey is struck mid-air and either falls or is caught in a follow-up pass. The impact velocity of the strike -- even accounting for the spread of speed during the approach -- is sufficient to stun or kill small to medium-sized birds immediately.
- **The sound of the stoop.** A Peregrine stooping at full velocity through a tern colony produces two distinct acoustic events above the colony surface: (1) the whoosh or rush of air displaced by the falcon's body at high speed -- audible as a sharp, broadband, directional sound event (hundreds of Hz to several kHz, lasting <1 second) as the falcon passes; (2) the alarm explosion from the tern colony, which begins at the moment the tern colony individuals detect the incoming predator -- typically 1-3 seconds before the actual stoop impact, as the nearest individuals sound the alarm and the alarm cascades outward through the colony.

(Sources: Peregrine Falcon stoop velocity 240-390 km/h maximum; Ken Franklin 2005 controlled study 389 km/h; pressure wave air disturbance at close range; stoop aerodynamics: wing-folded body position reduced frontal area; tomial tooth sever vertebrae; nictitating membrane eye protection; nasal baffles airflow deflection during breathing; kill mechanics closed foot strike vs. raking; stoop acoustic: air rush broadband directional <1 second; alarm cascade 1-3 seconds before stoop impact)

## THE TERN COLONY -- SCREAMING AT MAXIMUM

### Colonial Seabird Alarm: From Tense to Explosive

The tern colony alarm response:

- **The colonial seabird alarm.** Breeding tern colonies (Arctic Tern, Sterna paradisaea; Common Tern, Sterna hirundo; Caspian Tern, Hydroprogne caspia) are acoustically constant during the breeding season -- the colony is always producing some level of vocalization from territorial disputes, mate interactions, chick begging, and parent-chick recognition calls. The baseline colony acoustic level is E=5-6. The alarm response at the moment of raptor detection is qualitatively different: every individual in the colony transitions from baseline vocalizations to alarm calls simultaneously.
- **The alarm vocalization.** Tern alarm calls are sharp, high-frequency, and individually powerful for birds of this size. A single Arctic Tern alarm call (kee-yarrr or equivalent) peaks at 5,000-12,000 Hz with acoustic power sufficient to carry 200-500 meters. A colony of 500-10,000 breeding pairs (typical for Pacific Northwest tern colonies) all alarm-calling simultaneously produces a sound event at E=9: the frequency range is high (concentrated in the 3,000-12,000 Hz range), the spatial distribution is the entire colony (potentially several hundred meters across), and the temporal onset is simultaneous (within 1-2 seconds across the colony as the alarm cascades).
- **The mobbing response.** Tern colonies do not only alarm-call when a Peregrine stoops -- they mob. Individual terns break from the nesting colony and pursue the falcon, calling continuously and sometimes making physical contact (diving at the falcon's head and back). The mobbing behavior extends the acoustic event beyond the initial stoop: if the Peregrine makes multiple passes, the alarm and mobbing continue for minutes. The tern colony's aerial assault of a Peregrine -- dozens of high-speed, agile birds pursuing the fastest avian predator on Earth -- is one of the most intense behavioral events in the Pacific Northwest coast ecosystem.
- **The colony acoustic geography.** A large tern colony on the Pacific Northwest coast is a spatial acoustic phenomenon: the colony is spread across the available nesting substrate (beach, rocky island, gravel bar), and the alarm vocalizations come from every point simultaneously. The listener is inside the sound, not observing it from outside. The high-frequency alarm calls saturate the upper portion of the acoustic spectrum while the wing-noise and aerial activity of thousands of disturbed birds adds broadband mid-frequency sound.

(Sources: Arctic Tern Sterna paradisaea alarm call kee-yarrr 5,000-12,000 Hz peak; single tern alarm 200-500 m carry; colony 500-10,000 breeding pairs typical; simultaneous alarm onset 1-2 seconds cascade; colony alarm E=9 from frequency + spatial distribution + temporal simultaneity; mobbing behavior: individual terns pursuing falcon; multiple passes extend acoustic event; spatial acoustic geography: listener inside sound field; wing-noise broadband mid-frequency aerial activity)

## THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST PEREGRINE -- RECOVERY AND PRESENCE

### From DDT Collapse to Coastal Apex Predator

The Peregrine Falcon's status in the Pacific Northwest:

- **The DDT collapse.** Peregrine Falcon populations across North America collapsed through the 1950s and 1960s as DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane) accumulated in the food chain. Peregrine Falcons are apex predators that eat birds; those birds accumulated DDT from insects and plant matter; the DDT caused eggshell thinning, resulting in broken eggs and reproductive failure. By the 1970s, the Eastern US Peregrine population was functionally extinct, and Western populations (including Pacific Northwest) were dramatically reduced.
- **The recovery.** Following the 1972 US DDT ban, Peregrine populations began recovering. Recovery was accelerated by captive breeding and release programs (notably at Cornell University under Tom Cade). The Pacific Northwest population recovered through the 1970s-1990s and was delisted from the Endangered Species Act in 1999. As of 2026, Peregrine Falcons are common in the Pacific Northwest, breeding on coastal cliffs, river canyon walls, and urban structures (bridges, skyscrapers).
- **The urban nesting.** Peregrine Falcons have colonized urban structures throughout the Pacific Northwest. Seattle, Portland, Tacoma, and smaller cities all host Peregrine pairs nesting on building ledges, bridges, and water towers. The urban Peregrine uses pigeons, starlings, and other urban birds as prey -- the same hunting strategy applied to a food source provided by human activity. The urban colonization is one of the wildlife recovery success stories of the late 20th century.
- **Washington State coastal cliff nesting.** On the Pacific coast, Peregrines nest on the coastal cliffs of the Olympic Peninsula and San Juan Islands. These cliff-nesting Peregrines have access to the seabird colonies of the Washington coast -- Rhinoceros Auklets, Common Murres, Tufted Puffins, and the colonial terns that are degree 343's primary prey species. The coastal Peregrine is the seabird-specialist predator of the Pacific Northwest coast.

(Sources: DDT accumulation food chain Peregrine eggshell thinning reproductive failure; Eastern US Peregrine functionally extinct by 1970s; Pacific Northwest populations dramatically reduced; 1972 US DDT ban; captive breeding Cornell University Tom Cade; ESA delisted 1999; Urban nesting Seattle Portland Tacoma bridges buildings; pigeon starling urban prey; Olympic Peninsula San Juan Islands coastal cliff nesting; Rhinoceros Auklet Common Murre Puffin seabird colony prey)
