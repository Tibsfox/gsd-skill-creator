The Stoop

The Peregrine Falcon’s stoop is the fastest movement of any animal on Earth. In a full power stoop — near-vertical dive, wings folded against the body — the Peregrine reaches 240–390 km/h (150–242 mph). Ken Franklin’s 2005 controlled study produced a documented maximum of 389 km/h (242 mph). At this speed, a perceptible pressure wave precedes the falcon’s body through the air. At close range, the stoop is audible as a sharp directional rush of disturbed atmosphere: broadband, <1 second, directional.

The stoop aerodynamics are total-body optimizations for velocity: wings folded to minimize frontal area, nictitating membranes protect the eyes from debris, nasal baffles deflect high-speed airflow to allow breathing. The tomial tooth (the notch in the upper mandible) severs the prey’s vertebrae on impact. The entire body is an evolved velocity instrument.

The Colony Alarm

The tern colony alarm begins with 1-3 individuals detecting the incoming Peregrine (typically 1-3 seconds before impact) and sounding the alarm call. The alarm cascades through the colony within 1-2 seconds. Each individual Arctic Tern, Common Tern, or Caspian Tern produces its alarm call at 5,000–12,000 Hz with sufficient individual power to carry 200-500 meters. A colony of 500-10,000 breeding pairs all alarming simultaneously produces spectral saturation of the 3,000–12,000 Hz range across the entire colony footprint.

The mobbing response extends the acoustic event. Individual terns break from the nesting colony and pursue the Peregrine in aerial assault — diving at the falcon’s head and back while alarm-calling continuously. The mobbing can continue for 5+ minutes if the Peregrine makes multiple passes. The tern colony’s aerial assault of the world’s fastest bird is one of the most intense behavioral events on the Pacific Northwest coast.

The Peregrine Recovery

Peregrine Falcons were nearly eliminated from eastern North America by the 1970s through DDT-caused eggshell thinning. The 1972 US DDT ban and captive breeding programs (Cornell University, Tom Cade) allowed recovery. The Pacific Northwest population was delisted from the ESA in 1999. As of 2026, Peregrines breed on coastal cliffs, river canyons, and urban structures throughout the Pacific Northwest. The coastal cliff Peregrine that produces the degree-343 event is the result of one of the most successful wildlife recovery programs in US history.
