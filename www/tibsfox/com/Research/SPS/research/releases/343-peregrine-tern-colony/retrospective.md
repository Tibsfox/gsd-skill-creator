## What Worked

1. **The two-phase structure (stoop + alarm explosion) is the analytical framework that makes this entry precise.** Phase 1 (Peregrine stoop, <1 second, broadband directional) produces E=4-5. Phase 2 (colony alarm explosion, 1-2 second onset, 3,000-12,000 Hz spectral saturation) produces E=9. The two phases are distinct in character and energy level. The E=9 is from Phase 2, not Phase 1; the stoop is the trigger, the alarm is the event.
2. **The "fastest energy transition in SPS history" framing is defensible and analytically important.** No other SPS entry transitions from baseline to maximum in 1-2 seconds. The sea lion colony (342) is continuous. The elk+storm+eagle (341) builds over minutes. The orca breach cascade (340) requires 30-90 second intervals between maximum events. The peregrine-tern entry is uniquely fast in its energy transition.
3. **The DDT recovery context elevates this entry.** The Peregrine Falcon stooping at 240-390 km/h in degree 343 is the direct result of the 1972 DDT ban and the Cornell captive breeding program. Without that recovery, the coastal cliff Peregrine population would not exist at the scale that makes this event common in the Pacific Northwest. The predation drama is inseparable from the conservation success.
4. **The stoop aerodynamics (nasal baffles, nictitating membranes, tomial tooth, wing-folded body) are correctly documented as total-body optimization for velocity.** The physical architecture of the stoop is one of evolution's most specialized adaptations. The documentation is factual and adds the technical depth appropriate for E=9.
5. **The mobbing response (terns pursuing the Peregrine) correctly extends the event duration.** The stoop itself is <1 second. The colony alarm and aerial assault extend the event to 5+ minutes. The E=9 is not a point event; it is an extended acoustic episode triggered by the stoop.

## Discoveries

1. **The colony alarm cascade (1-3 individuals → full colony in 1-2 seconds) demonstrates the speed of social information transmission in colonial seabirds.** The alarm propagates through the colony at social communication speed, not at sound speed. Each individual is responding to the behavior of its neighbors, not to the original threat. The cascade is a social network phenomenon.
2. **The documented maximum stoop velocity (389 km/h, Ken Franklin 2005) was measured on a trained Peregrine in controlled conditions, not a wild hunt.** The wild-hunt stoop is typically somewhat lower (240-320 km/h in most documented cases) because the falcon is adjusting approach angle to intercept a moving target, not simply maximizing velocity. The 389 km/h figure is the theoretical maximum; the hunting stoop is the operational maximum.
3. **The Pacific Northwest coastal Peregrine population (post-ESA-delisting 1999) has established urban breeding populations throughout the region.** The coastal cliff Peregrine and the Seattle bridge Peregrine are the same species in different habitats. The urban colonization demonstrates the Peregrine's behavioral flexibility within the apex-predator role.
