## THE SONG STRUCTURE -- UNITS, PHRASES, THEMES, SONGS

### Hierarchical Composition

The Humpback Whale song has a recursive, hierarchical structure:

- **Units.** The smallest elements: individual sounds lasting 0.5-5 seconds. Moans, cries, chirps, grunts, snores, and other vocalizations. Each unit has distinctive frequency, duration, and timbral characteristics.
- **Phrases.** A predictable sequence of units repeated with consistency. A phrase might be: moan-chirp-moan-cry, repeated 3-5 times. Phrase repetition creates the rhythmic backbone of the song.
- **Themes.** A sequence of one or more phrase types. Each theme has a distinctive character. A song typically contains 4-7 themes, performed in a fixed order.
- **Songs.** The complete sequence of themes constitutes a "song." A full song lasts 10-30 minutes. After completing the song, the whale begins again, creating a continuous performance that can last for hours.
- **Sessions.** Extended singing periods of hours to days. The whale repeats the song continuously with gradual variations.

(Sources: Payne & McVay 1971; Guinee & Payne 1988; song structure analysis; engine internal analysis)

## CULTURAL TRANSMISSION -- SONGS THAT SPREAD

### The Evolution of Whale Song

The cultural transmission of Humpback Whale songs is one of the most remarkable phenomena in animal behavior:

- **Songs evolve.** All males in a population sing essentially the same song at any given time. But the song changes gradually over months and years. New phrases appear, old ones disappear, themes are modified. The song of January is different from the song of June.
- **Cultural revolution.** Occasionally, a population rapidly adopts an entirely new song, replacing the previous one within weeks. This "cultural revolution" has been documented in Australian and Pacific populations. The mechanism is unknown but the speed is extraordinary.
- **Trans-oceanic spread.** Song innovations spread from population to population across the Pacific Ocean, traveling eastward from Australia across the South Pacific over several years. This is cultural diffusion at oceanic scale.
- **Learning.** Male humpbacks learn the song from other males. Calves may begin learning elements while still with their mothers. The song is not innate -- it must be learned, practiced, and maintained.
- **Noad et al. (2000).** Documented rapid song change in east Australian humpbacks -- a "cultural revolution" where an entirely new song replaced the existing one in a single breeding season. One of the most cited papers in animal culture research.
- **Garland et al. (2011).** Documented the eastward spread of song innovations across the South Pacific, demonstrating trans-oceanic cultural transmission.

(Sources: Noad et al. 2000; Garland et al. 2011; Payne & McVay 1971; cultural transmission in humpbacks; engine internal analysis)

## PUGET SOUND AND PNW WATERS

### Summer Feeding Grounds

- **Migration.** Humpback Whales undertake one of the longest migrations of any mammal -- from tropical breeding grounds (Hawaii, Mexico, Central America) to high-latitude feeding grounds (Alaska, British Columbia, Washington). Up to 5,000 miles one way.
- **Summer presence.** Humpbacks are present in PNW waters from approximately May to November, feeding on krill, herring, and small fish.
- **Salish Sea.** Increasingly observed in the Salish Sea (including parts of Puget Sound) as populations recover from whaling. The San Juan Islands and Strait of Juan de Fuca are reliable observation areas.
- **Population recovery.** North Pacific humpback populations have recovered significantly since the whaling moratorium. Estimated 20,000+ in the North Pacific. Washington waters: hundreds seasonally.
- **Singing in PNW.** Males primarily sing on breeding grounds, but fragments of song have been recorded in feeding areas. The full song is associated with breeding season.

(Sources: NOAA; WDFW; humpback migration routes; PNW feeding grounds; population recovery; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Humpback Whale Song

**Units (Individual Sounds):**
- **Frequency:** 20 Hz to 10 kHz. Enormous range spanning infrasonic to well audible.
- **Types:** Moans (long, low-frequency), cries (rising or falling), chirps (short, high), grunts (percussive), snores (raspy), and hybrid forms.
- **Duration:** 0.5-5 seconds per unit.

**Phrases:**
- **Structure:** Predictable sequences of units, repeated 2-10+ times.
- **Duration:** 5-30 seconds per phrase.
- **Character:** Each phrase has a distinctive "feel" -- some melodic, some percussive, some harmonic.

**Themes:**
- **Structure:** Sequences of phrases. 4-7 themes per song.
- **Duration:** 1-5 minutes per theme.
- **Progression:** Themes follow a fixed order within the song.

**Complete Song:**
- **Duration:** 10-30 minutes.
- **Repetition:** Sung continuously, repeated for hours to days.
- **Evolution:** Gradual modification over weeks and months. Cultural transmission across populations.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=8. **SPS TIES THE ENGINE MAXIMUM.** The most complex, longest, and most culturally significant animal vocalization on Earth. Hour-long hierarchical compositions with cultural transmission across ocean basins. The song is not just sound -- it is art.
