## THE BOUNCING-BALL TRILL -- THE SIGNATURE CALL

### Acoustic Description

The Western Screech-Owl's primary vocalization is one of the most distinctive owl calls in the PNW -- and one of the most misidentified:

- **The bouncing ball:** The call is a series of whistled notes that accelerate and descend in pitch, producing a rhythm almost exactly like a ball bouncing on a hard surface and coming to rest. The first notes are spaced apart and higher-pitched; the notes become progressively closer together and lower-pitched; the call ends in a rapid, low-pitched trill that fades to silence.
- **Duration:** 3-5 seconds per trill. Much shorter than the Saw-whet Owl's minutes-long tooting.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 400-600 Hz. Lower-pitched than the Saw-whet's tooting (~1000 Hz). The mellow, hollow quality of the notes contributes to the soft, unobtrusive character.
- **Rhythm:** The accelerating rhythm is the diagnostic feature. No other PNW owl produces this distinctive bouncing-ball acceleration. Once heard and identified, it is instantly recognizable.
- **Repetition:** Males repeat the trill at intervals of 10-30 seconds during territorial and courtship calling. The spacing between trills is much longer than the Saw-whet's continuous tooting, creating a pulsed rather than continuous acoustic pattern.

### The Name Problem

"Screech-Owl" is arguably a misnomer for this species:

- **Not a screech:** The primary vocalization -- the bouncing-ball trill -- is not a screech. It is a soft, mellow, whistled trill. The name "screech-owl" is inherited from the Eastern Screech-Owl (*Megascops asio*), whose whinny call is louder and arguably more "screech-like."
- **The bark:** The Western Screech-Owl does produce a sharp bark call -- "BARK!" -- during alarm and territorial disputes. This call is louder and more aggressive than the trill but is heard far less frequently.
- **Historical naming:** The "screech-owl" name derives from historical European taxonomy applied to New World species. The common name persists despite being acoustically misleading for the Western species.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Cannings and Angell 2001; Sibley 2014)

## THE SUBURBAN OWL -- CAMOUFLAGE IN YOUR BACKYARD

### Habitat

The Western Screech-Owl is the **most suburban owl** in the PNW:

- **Backyard resident:** Western Screech-Owls commonly nest in tree cavities in residential neighborhoods, parks, and suburban woodland. They readily use nest boxes. A Screech-Owl may spend its entire life in a suburban neighborhood without ever being noticed by the humans who live there.
- **Habitat flexibility:** While they prefer open woodland, riparian corridors, and forest edges, Screech-Owls are adaptable. They use cottonwoods, oaks, maples, and large conifers -- whatever provides cavities. In western Washington, they are found at low to moderate elevations where large trees persist.
- **Permanent resident:** Non-migratory. A Screech-Owl that occupies a territory stays there year-round, year after year. The same cavity may be used for multiple breeding seasons. This permanence means that homeowners with Screech-Owls in their neighborhood trees have a persistent but invisible neighbor.

### Camouflage

The Western Screech-Owl's camouflage is remarkable:

- **Bark mimicry:** The plumage is gray-brown (in western PNW populations) with intricate dark streaking and crossbarring that perfectly mimics tree bark. When a Screech-Owl presses against a tree trunk and closes its eyes, it becomes virtually invisible.
- **Ear tuft display:** When alarmed, the Screech-Owl raises its short ear tufts and narrows its body, elongating its silhouette to resemble a broken branch stub. This defensive posture combines with the bark-like plumage to create extraordinary concealment.
- **Day roost:** Screech-Owls roost during the day in tree cavities or pressed against tree trunks in dense vegetation. They emerge only after full darkness. Unless you know the exact location of a roost cavity, you will not see a Screech-Owl during the day.
- **Small size:** At 19-25 cm (7.5-10 inches), the Screech-Owl is small enough to disappear into the architecture of a medium-sized tree. It is larger than a Saw-whet Owl but still small -- smaller than a pigeon.

### Diet

The Western Screech-Owl is a generalist predator at its size scale:

- **Insects:** Moths, beetles, crickets, and other large insects form a significant portion of the diet, especially in summer.
- **Small mammals:** Mice, voles, shrews. The standard owl prey base at small scale.
- **Other:** Crayfish, small fish (near water), earthworms, small birds. The Screech-Owl's dietary flexibility contributes to its success in suburban environments where prey variety may differ from wild habitat.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Cannings and Angell 2001; Sibley 2014; WDFW)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Bouncing Ball in the Dark Backyard

The Western Screech-Owl's acoustic contribution is **rhythmic descent** -- the sound of energy dissipating, a ball coming to rest:

- **Bouncing-ball trill:** Whistled notes accelerating and descending in pitch. 400-600 Hz. 3-5 seconds duration. The rhythm is the diagnostic feature: widely spaced notes accelerating to a rapid, low-pitched terminal trill.
- **Bark call:** Sharp "BARK!" for alarm. Less frequent. Higher energy than the trill but rarely heard.
- **Pair duet:** Male and female sometimes duet, with the female's call slightly higher-pitched. The duet is a mutual recognition call rather than a territorial display.
- **Amplitude:** Low. The trill is audible at perhaps 100-200 meters in quiet conditions. In suburban settings with ambient noise, effective range is much less. This is a neighborhood-scale call, not a landscape-scale announcement.
- **Temporal pattern:** Calling peaks February-April (breeding season). Males call from near nest cavities. Calling begins after full darkness and may continue intermittently through the night.
