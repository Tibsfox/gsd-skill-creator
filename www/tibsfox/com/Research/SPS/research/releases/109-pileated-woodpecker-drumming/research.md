## THE DRUM: RESONANT MACHINE-GUN ON DEAD SNAG

### The Mechanics of Pileated Drumming

The Pileated Woodpecker's drum is a **rapid, resonant, powerful burst of bill strikes on a dead snag**. The bird selects a dead tree or large dead branch -- a resonating surface -- and strikes it with its bill at extraordinary speed and force. The result is a deep, booming, machine-gun-like roll that carries over vast distances through the forest.

**Physical mechanics:**

- **Strike rate:** approximately 15-16 strikes per second during the peak of the drum roll. This is slower than smaller woodpeckers (Downy: 15-25/sec, Hairy: 25/sec) but with vastly more force per strike.
- **Force:** each strike delivers approximately 1,000-1,200 g of deceleration to the bird's skull. The Pileated has the largest bill of any North American woodpecker, and each strike displaces significant wood volume.
- **Duration:** a complete drum roll lasts 1-3 seconds, with the most powerful rolls reaching 3 seconds. The roll typically begins with rapid strikes, maintains peak speed, and then decelerates slightly.
- **Resonating surface:** the Pileated deliberately selects dead snags with the best resonance characteristics. A hollow dead tree amplifies the drumming signal dramatically. The bird learns which trees produce the best resonance and returns to them repeatedly -- these become known as **drum trees**.
- **Amplitude:** the Pileated's drum is the **loudest drum of any North American woodpecker**. The combination of the bird's large size, powerful neck musculature, and selection of resonant dead snags produces a drum that can be heard up to 1 kilometer away in forest habitat.

### The Dead Snag as Resonating Chamber

The dead snag is not merely a surface to strike -- it is a **resonating instrument**. The Pileated Woodpecker's selection of drum trees is acoustically sophisticated:

- **Hollow interiors** amplify the sound. A dead tree with a hollow core acts as a resonating chamber, amplifying the bill strikes far beyond what a solid tree would produce.
- **Diameter matters.** Larger-diameter snags produce lower-frequency, more resonant drums. The Pileated preferentially selects large-diameter snags, which are most common in mature and old-growth forest.
- **Height matters.** The Pileated typically drums high on the snag -- 10-25 meters above ground -- where the sound radiates outward over the forest canopy with minimal obstruction.
- **Wood density.** The ideal drum tree has wood that is dead but not yet decomposed -- firm enough to produce a clean, resonant strike but soft enough that the bill can displace material with each hit.

The dead snag is an **acoustic instrument** that the Pileated has learned to play. The bird's relationship with its drum trees is analogous to a musician's relationship with a favored instrument. The same bird returns to the same drum trees repeatedly, year after year. The drum tree is territorial infrastructure.

(Sources: Winkler, Christie & Nurney 2020; Bull & Jackson 2011; Elbroch & Marks 2001)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: THE PERCUSSIVE MAXIMUM

### Frequency and Spectral Character

The Pileated Woodpecker's drum occupies a distinctive acoustic niche:

- **Fundamental frequency:** 40-80 Hz. The drum is **extremely low-frequency** compared to smaller woodpeckers. The large bill and resonant snag produce bass tones that propagate efficiently through forest habitat.
- **Peak energy band:** 80-400 Hz. The majority of the drum's acoustic energy is concentrated in the low-frequency range. This is below the primary frequency range of most songbirds, which means the drum signal encounters minimal acoustic competition.
- **Harmonics:** the resonant snag produces prominent harmonics at integer multiples of the fundamental. A hollow snag with good resonance produces harmonic series extending to 2,000+ Hz, creating a rich, complex timbre.
- **Amplitude:** 80-100+ dB at 1 meter from the drum tree. The loudest drum of any North American woodpecker. The low-frequency energy propagates efficiently through forest, with less atmospheric absorption than higher frequencies.
- **Detection distance:** audible at 500-1,000+ meters in forest habitat. The combination of high amplitude and low frequency allows the drum to propagate through dense forest with less attenuation than higher-frequency signals.

### Comparison with Other Woodpecker Drums

The Pileated's drum is unique among PNW woodpeckers:

| Species | Strike rate | Duration | Frequency | Amplitude | Character |
|---------|------------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Pileated** | 15-16/sec | 1-3 sec | 40-80 Hz | Loudest | Deep, resonant, booming machine-gun |
| **Hairy** (degree 110) | ~25/sec | 0.5-1.5 sec | 100-200 Hz | Loud | Fast, sharp stutter |
| **Downy** (degree 111) | 15-25/sec | 0.5-1 sec | 150-300 Hz | Moderate | Light, rapid, higher-pitched |
| **Northern Flicker** (21-22) | 25-30/sec | 0.5-1 sec | 100-250 Hz | Moderate-loud | Fast, flickering roll |

| **Acorn Woodpecker** (107) | 15-20/sec | 0.5-1 sec | 100-250 Hz | Moderate | Short, rapid bursts |

The Pileated's drum is the **lowest frequency, highest amplitude, and longest duration** of any North American woodpecker drum. It is the percussive maximum.

### The Machine-Gun Analogy

The Pileated's drum is frequently described as a **machine-gun burst** because of its:
- **Regularity:** the strikes are evenly spaced, creating a mechanical rhythm.
- **Power:** each strike is forceful, producing a distinct concussive impact.
- **Acceleration:** the roll begins and reaches peak speed within a fraction of a second.
- **Resonance:** the booming, low-frequency character gives the drum a concussive quality that feels like impacts rather than taps.

The machine-gun analogy captures the physical sensation of hearing the drum in the forest. The sound does not register as "bird" -- it registers as **industrial percussion**. The regularity, power, and low frequency create a sound that seems impossible to attribute to a living creature. E=6 -- maximum energy.

## DRUMMING VS. CALLING: TWO E=6 SOUND SYSTEMS

The Pileated Woodpecker possesses **two independent sound-production systems**, both operating at maximum energy:

### 1. The Vocal System: Wild Laughing Call (Degree 108)

- **Mechanism:** syrinx (vocal organ)
- **Character:** rapid descending series of ringing wuk-wuk-wuk notes
- **Frequency:** 800-3,500 Hz (mid-to-high)
- **Function:** territorial declaration, long-distance contact between pair members
- **Quality:** wild, unhinged, primal -- a scream
- **Energy:** E=6

### 2. The Percussive System: Drumming (Degree 109)

- **Mechanism:** bill strikes on resonant dead snag
- **Character:** deep, booming, machine-gun burst
- **Frequency:** 40-80 Hz fundamental (extremely low)
- **Function:** territorial advertisement, mate attraction, pair-bond maintenance
- **Quality:** mechanical, resonant, concussive -- a detonation
- **Energy:** E=6

The two systems operate in complementary frequency bands. The laughing call (800-3,500 Hz) and the drumming (40-400 Hz) together span a **massive frequency range** -- from sub-bass to upper midrange. The Pileated Woodpecker is acoustically dominant across more of the frequency spectrum than any other PNW bird species. This is why it demands two degrees: each sound system is independently E=6, and together they represent the most complete acoustic dominance of any species in the engine.

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE DEAD SNAG ECONOMY

### Dead Snags as Infrastructure

The Pileated Woodpecker's drumming is ecologically significant beyond acoustic communication. The bird's relationship with dead snags creates a **cascade of ecological benefits**:

- **Cavity excavation:** the Pileated excavates the largest cavities of any North American woodpecker (rectangular holes up to 20 cm wide and 60 cm deep). These cavities, excavated during foraging and nesting, become critical habitat for dozens of other species.
- **Secondary cavity users:** species that use abandoned Pileated cavities include Wood Duck, Barrow's Goldeneye, Bufflehead, American Kestrel, Northern Flicker, flying squirrels, pine martens, and many others. The Pileated is a **keystone cavity creator**.
- **Drum tree maintenance:** the Pileated's repeated drumming on dead snags does not damage the trees (the bird selects already-dead wood). The drumming is a non-destructive use of standing dead wood that advertises the bird's presence to mates and rivals.

### Old-Growth Dependence

The Pileated Woodpecker's drumming is most dramatic in mature and old-growth forest because:

- **Snag availability:** old-growth forest has more standing dead trees (snags) than younger forest, providing more potential drum trees.
- **Snag diameter:** old-growth snags are larger in diameter, producing deeper, more resonant drums.
- **Canopy structure:** the complex canopy structure of old-growth forest creates acoustic channels that propagate the drum signal over long distances.
- **Territory size:** Pileated territories in old-growth forest are 60-200 hectares. The drum must carry across this entire territory. The combination of large-diameter resonant snags and old-growth acoustic channels makes this possible.

The dead snag economy connects directly to the forest-as-instrument concept from degree 108. The old-growth forest provides both the vocal stage (degree 108, laughing call reverberating through canopy) and the percussion instrument (degree 109, dead snag as resonating drum).

(Sources: Bull & Jackson 2011; Thomas, Anderson & Parris 1979)
