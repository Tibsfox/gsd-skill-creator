The Percussive Maximum — E=6 — Loudest Woodpecker Drum in North America

The Pileated Woodpecker’s drum at E=6 MAXIMUM is the loudest drum of any North American woodpecker. The bird selects a dead snag — a resonating instrument — and strikes it with its massive bill at 15–16 strikes per second. The result is a deep, booming, machine-gun-like roll that carries over 1 kilometer through forest habitat. Each strike delivers approximately 1,000–1,200 g of deceleration. The sound does not register as “bird” — it registers as industrial percussion.

Two Sound Systems, Both at Maximum — Voice (108) + Percussion (109)

The Pileated Woodpecker is the only species with two consecutive entries in the engine. Degree 108 documented the wild laughing call (wuk-wuk-wuk) — a vocal sound produced by the syrinx at 800–3,500 Hz. Degree 109 documents the drumming — a mechanical sound produced by bill strikes on dead wood at 40–80 Hz fundamental. Two independent sound-production systems, both at E=6. Together they span the frequency spectrum from sub-bass to upper midrange. No other PNW species achieves this acoustic dominance across such a wide bandwidth.

The Dead Snag as Instrument — Resonating Chamber

The dead snag is not merely a surface to strike — it is a resonating instrument. The Pileated selects snags with optimal acoustic properties: hollow interiors that amplify sound, large diameter for lower-frequency resonance, elevated position (10–25m above ground) for maximum radiation. The bird returns to the same drum trees year after year. The dead snag economy: the Pileated plays the forest’s percussion instruments, and its excavated cavities become homes for dozens of secondary species.

The Machine-Gun Drum

The Pileated Woodpecker’s drum is a rapid, resonant, powerful burst of bill strikes on a carefully selected dead snag. The drum roll lasts 1–3 seconds, with the most powerful rolls sustaining for the full three seconds. The strike rate of 15–16 per second is slower than smaller woodpeckers, but each strike delivers vastly more force. The result is a deep, booming, concussive sound that reverberates through the forest.

The machine-gun analogy captures the physical sensation: the strikes are evenly spaced with mechanical regularity, each impact is forceful and concussive, and the low-frequency character gives the drum a quality that seems impossible to attribute to a living creature. Standing beneath a drum tree while the Pileated drums is one of the most dramatic acoustic experiences in the PNW forest.

Third Consecutive Picidae (107–108–109)

Three consecutive Picidae: Acorn Woodpecker (107, oak savanna, E=5, colonial), Pileated Woodpecker laughing (108, old-growth, E=6, solitary), Pileated Woodpecker drumming (109, dead snag, E=6, resident). After the Corvidae septet (100–106), the engine has committed to woodpeckers. Three consecutive Picidae — the Picidae streak is tracking toward the Sciuridae octet (8 consecutive). The family’s acoustic diversity justifies the streak: each entry is a categorically different sound.
