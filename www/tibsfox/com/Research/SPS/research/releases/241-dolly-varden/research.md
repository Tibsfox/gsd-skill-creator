## *SALVELINUS* -- THE CHAR GENUS ENTERS

### A New Genus Within Salmonidae

The Dolly Varden at degree 241 introduces a new genus within the Salmonidae family:

- **Genus *Salvelinus*.** The chars. Dolly Varden shares the *Salvelinus* genus with Bull Trout (*S. confluentus*, degree 240), Arctic Char (*S. alpinus*), Brook Trout (*S. fontinalis*, native to eastern North America but widely stocked in PNW), and Lake Trout (*S. namaycush*). The char genus is distinguished from the Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus*) and trout (*Salmo*) genera by several characters: spots that are lighter than the body background color (not darker, as in most trout), a distinctive body form, and their preference for cold, pristine, often glacially influenced waters.
- **Distinguishing chars from trout and salmon.** Lay observers often confuse chars (especially Dolly Varden and Bull Trout) with trout. The key diagnostic: chars have light spots (cream, yellow, orange, red) on a darker background; trout typically have dark spots on a lighter background. The confusion between Dolly Varden and Bull Trout was historically so extreme that both species were identified as "Dolly Varden" until genetic and morphological research separated them in the 1980s-1990s.
- **Bull Trout (degree 240) and Dolly Varden (degree 241).** These two entries are closely related species that were long considered a single species. They overlap in range in some areas but have different habitat preferences and distributions: Bull Trout is more interior-focused (cold, high-gradient rivers draining the Cascades and Rockies); Dolly Varden extends further into coastal glacial streams and coastal marine environments. The engine places them consecutively at 240-241, reflecting their biological and taxonomic proximity.
- **Cold water specialists.** All chars require cold, well-oxygenated water. Dolly Varden is among the most cold-adapted of the chars, found in streams and lakes fed by active glaciers and snowfields throughout the Pacific Northwest, Alaska, and across the northern Pacific into Asia. Climate change -- which is reducing glacial ice and warming stream temperatures -- is a documented threat to Dolly Varden habitat throughout its range.

## THE SPECIES -- *SALVELINUS MALMA*

### Biology and Distribution

- **Size and form.** Dolly Varden are medium-sized salmonids. In freshwater-resident populations, adults typically reach 25-50 cm (10-20 in) and weigh 0.5-3 kg (1-6 lbs). Anadromous (sea-run) Dolly Varden in Alaska and coastal British Columbia can reach 60-70 cm (24-28 in) and 3-5 kg (6-11 lbs). The fish has the characteristic char form: slender body, relatively small head, adipose fin (the small fleshy fin between the dorsal fin and tail, characteristic of all salmonids), and the distinctive char coloration.
- **Coloration.** The Dolly Varden is one of the most visually striking Pacific Northwest fish. The base color is olive to dark green or gray-brown on the back and sides, fading to white or cream on the belly. The sides are decorated with spots of pale yellow, cream, and orange-red, sometimes brilliant orange in spawning males. During the spawning season, male Dolly Varden develop intensely colored flanks with vivid red and orange spots against a dark background -- a visual display that rivals the color of spawning Sockeye.
- **Two forms: anadromous and freshwater-resident.** Like many salmonids, Dolly Varden exist in both anadromous (sea-run) and non-anadromous (freshwater-resident) forms. Anadromous Dolly Varden make seasonal migrations between freshwater rearing habitat and coastal marine feeding habitat. Freshwater-resident Dolly Varden spend their entire lives in cold mountain streams and lakes. The engine documents the freshwater-resident form in glacial streams.
- **Range.** Dolly Varden are distributed throughout the Pacific Rim, from northern California and Oregon north through Washington, British Columbia, Alaska, the Aleutian Islands, and across the North Pacific to Japan, Korea, and the Russian Far East. In Washington State, Dolly Varden occur in the Olympic Peninsula, the North Cascades, and coastal drainages. Their distribution within Washington has been reduced from historical levels due to competition with introduced Brook Trout (another *Salvelinus* species) and habitat degradation.
- **Two subspecies.** Two subspecies are recognized: *S. malma malma* (northern Dolly Varden, distributed from the Kuskokwim River in Alaska northward and westward across the Bering region) and *S. malma lordi* (southern Dolly Varden, the form found in Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia). The southern Dolly Varden is the relevant form for the PNW.

(Sources: Dolly Varden size and form; coloration description; anadromous vs. freshwater-resident forms; geographic range; two subspecies; Washington State distribution)

## THE GLACIAL STREAM HABITAT

### Where Dolly Varden Live

The ecosystem documented in this entry -- glacial stream -- is specific and increasingly rare:

- **Glacially fed streams.** The streams preferred by Dolly Varden in the PNW are cold (water temperatures consistently below 10-12°C / 50-54°F), clear, high-gradient, and fed by glacial meltwater or snowfields. These conditions produce the oxygen levels and water temperatures that Dolly Varden require. In the North Cascades, the Olympic Mountains, and the Coast Ranges of British Columbia, streams draining glaciers and permanent snowfields create the habitat network that supports Dolly Varden populations.
- **Stream characteristics.** Glacial streams have several distinctive characteristics: they carry fine glacial rock flour (particles ground by glacial action that give the water a distinctive blue-green color in large volumes), they have highly variable seasonal flows (high in summer as glaciers melt; lower in winter), and they are extremely cold -- sometimes cold enough to impair the survival of other salmonid species that prefer slightly warmer water.
- **The ripple and pool structure.** Dolly Varden use both riffles (fast, shallow water over gravel and small boulders) and pools (deeper, slower water where fish can rest and feed on drift). In riffles, the fish's presence is marked by the gentle disturbance of the water surface -- a fin break, a slight downstream displacement of current, the shimmer of gravel displaced as the fish holds station against the flow. This is the E=2 sound: the near-silence of a cold-water predator in shallow, clear water.
- **Tributary streams vs. mainstem rivers.** Dolly Varden prefer tributary streams to mainstem river habitat in many systems. These smaller streams provide the cold, clean water and fine-gravel spawning habitat the species requires. The preference for tributaries makes Dolly Varden particularly sensitive to land use in upper watersheds: logging, road building, and development that introduces sediment, raises water temperatures, or disrupts the tributary network can eliminate Dolly Varden from entire stream systems.
- **Olympic Peninsula streams.** The Olympic Peninsula hosts some of Washington State's most important Dolly Varden populations. The peninsula's numerous coastal streams -- many with intact headwater glaciers in the Olympic Mountains -- provide the cold, clean water that Dolly Varden require. The Olympic National Park's protections extend to many of these stream systems.

(Sources: Glacial stream ecology; North Cascades and Olympic Peninsula Dolly Varden habitat; stream temperature requirements; riffle-pool structure; tributary preference; Olympic National Park protection)

## LIFECYCLE AND ECOLOGY

### The Char's Life in Cold Water

- **Spawning.** Dolly Varden spawn in fall (September-November in most Washington State populations). Females excavate redds (gravel nests) in clean gravel beds in cold tributary streams, depositing 500-2,000 eggs depending on body size. The eggs incubate over winter in the gravel, hatching in late winter or early spring. Spawning aggregations in glacial tributary streams are one of the most distinctive wildlife events of the fall PNW landscape -- the vivid coloration of spawning male Dolly Varden against the backdrop of clear glacial water and autumn-colored riparian vegetation.
- **Diet.** Dolly Varden are opportunistic predators. Their diet includes aquatic invertebrates (mayflies, caddisflies, stoneflies -- the classic cold-water stream invertebrates), terrestrial insects that fall into the stream, small fish, and in coastal populations, marine invertebrates during the marine phase. Dolly Varden are known to prey on salmon eggs during spawning runs -- following Pacific Salmon redds and consuming eggs that are displaced by redd construction. This behavior has made Dolly Varden controversial in some fishing circles: they were historically (and incorrectly) blamed for substantial salmon egg predation. Research has shown that the impact is minor and that Dolly Varden are a natural component of salmon-stream ecosystems.
- **Predation and ecological role.** Dolly Varden are both predator and prey. They are important food sources for river otters (*Lontra canadensis* -- degree 63 in the SPS engine), Mergansers (degrees 119-120), Osprey (degree 224), Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222), and Grizzly Bear (degrees 217-218). Within the stream ecosystem, they are mid-level predators that consume invertebrates and small fish while themselves being consumed by larger predators.
- **Relationship to Pacific Salmon.** The Dolly Varden-Pacific Salmon relationship is complex and ecologically important. In systems with active salmon runs, Dolly Varden benefit from salmon carcass nutrients (same marine nitrogen cycle documented for Pacific Salmon at degree 236) and from the dislodged salmon eggs. The char species and the salmon species share habitat, interact at the redd, and contribute to each other's ecosystems.

(Sources: Dolly Varden spawning; fall spawning timing; redd construction; egg predation controversy; research on egg predation impact; diet including invertebrates; predation by river otter, merganser, osprey, eagle; salmon-char relationship)

## THE NAME AND CULTURAL HISTORY

### Dolly Varden -- A Victorian Novel Character

- **The name's origin.** "Dolly Varden" is one of the most distinctive common names in North American fish nomenclature. The name derives from a character in Charles Dickens' novel *Barnaby Rudge* (1841): Dolly Varden, a coquettish blacksmith's daughter whose colorful, spotted dress inspired the name applied to this spotted fish in the 1870s. The association between Dickens' brightly dressed character and the vividly spotted fish was made by naturalists who found the resemblance apt.
- **The Victorian aesthetic.** The "Dolly Varden" style in Victorian fashion -- characterized by colorful, polka-dotted or flowered fabric gathered into an overskirt -- was fashionable enough in the 1870s that it gave its name to an entire range of products: Dolly Varden hats, Dolly Varden cakes (still popular in some regions), and the Dolly Varden fish. The fish name is a window into 19th-century popular culture and the relationship between ornamental fashion and the natural world.
- **Indigenous names and relationships.** The Pacific Northwest's indigenous peoples had diverse relationships with Dolly Varden long before Dickens' character gave the fish its English name. Dolly Varden were an important food source for many First Nations groups in the region -- particularly in the fall spawning season when the fish were most accessible. The char's presence in glacial tributary streams made it accessible to communities throughout the coastal ranges and mountain systems.
- **The confusion with Bull Trout.** For most of the 20th century, Dolly Varden and Bull Trout were treated as a single species under the name "Dolly Varden." This taxonomic confusion had management consequences: conservation programs designed for "Dolly Varden" were actually serving two distinct species with somewhat different ecological requirements. The separation of the two species in the late 1980s and 1990s (based on morphological studies followed by genetic confirmation) has improved conservation planning for both species.

(Sources: Barnaby Rudge character; Victorian Dolly Varden fashion; fish naming history; indigenous use of Dolly Varden; Bull Trout taxonomic separation history; management implications)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### The Cold-Water Future

- **Washington State status.** In Washington, Dolly Varden are a Species of Concern in some portions of their range. They are not federally listed under the Endangered Species Act (unlike the closely related Bull Trout, which is listed as Threatened), but populations in the southern portion of the range -- including some Washington State populations -- are reduced from historical levels.
- **Threats.** The primary threats to Dolly Varden in Washington include:
  - **Climate change and glacial retreat.** Dolly Varden are among the most cold-dependent salmonids in Washington. As glaciers retreat and snowpack declines, summer stream flows decrease and temperatures increase. Streams that currently support Dolly Varden will warm beyond tolerable limits over the coming decades. This is a threat without an obvious mitigation -- there is no way to arrest glacial retreat on a meaningful scale.
  - **Competition with Brook Trout.** Brook Trout (*Salvelinus fontinalis*), native to eastern North America but widely introduced throughout the West for sport fishing, compete with and hybridize with Dolly Varden. Brook Trout are highly aggressive and in some systems have displaced native chars. The hybridization between Brook Trout and Dolly Varden (and Bull Trout) produces infertile offspring or hybrids that reduce the genetic integrity of native populations.
  - **Habitat degradation.** Logging and road building in the upper watershed introduce fine sediment that smothers the clean gravel redds required for spawning. Riparian clearing raises water temperatures. These impacts reduce habitat availability throughout the species' range.
  - **Hydropower and diversions.** Water diversions for irrigation and some small hydropower facilities fragment stream networks and reduce flows in critical reaches.
- **Recovery efforts.** Brook Trout removal programs (electrofishing and netting to reduce competition) have shown promise in some systems. Watershed restoration -- riparian replanting, road decommissioning, large wood additions to streams -- can improve habitat quality. Protected area management (Olympic National Park, North Cascades National Park) preserves core habitat.

(Sources: Washington State Dolly Varden status; ESA comparison with Bull Trout; climate change glacial retreat; Brook Trout competition and hybridization; habitat degradation; hydropower impacts; recovery efforts)
