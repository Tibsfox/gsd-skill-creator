FIRST Salvelinus IN ENGINE — THE CHAR GENUS — SECOND SALMONIDAE GENUS

Degree 241 introduces Salvelinus — the char genus, distinct from Pacific salmon (Oncorhynchus at 236-239). The chars have light spots on dark background (the reverse of trout). Cold, pristine, glacially influenced waters. Bull Trout at 240 was the entry predecessor; Dolly Varden at 241 is the second char. Both were classified as "Dolly Varden" until separated by genetic research in the 1980s-90s. E=2 — the near-silence of a cold-water predator in clear glacial water.

MAXIMUM CONTRAST: E=8 (The Sonics) vs. E=2 (Dolly Varden). Spread of 6 — among the widest in the engine. The proto-punk progenitor, screaming maximum aggression in 1964 Tacoma, paired with the near-silent char in a glacial mountain stream. The degree wheel holds both at once.

The Cold-Water Char

Dolly Varden are medium-sized salmonids — 25-50 cm (10-20 in) in freshwater-resident form — distinguished by their brilliant coloration: olive to dark green on the back, pale yellow and orange-red spots on the sides. In spawning males, the spots intensify to vivid orange-red against a dark background, rivaling the visual display of spawning Sockeye. They hold in glacial streams year-round, requiring water temperatures consistently below 10-12°C.

The "sound" at degree 241 is the acoustic minimum of a living fish: the gentle displacement of water over gravel, a fin break at the surface, the shimmer of gravel as the fish holds position in current. E=2. The predator is present. It is barely audible.

Why "Dolly Varden"

The name comes from a character in Dickens' Barnaby Rudge (1841): Dolly Varden, a coquettish blacksmith's daughter whose colorful, spotted dress inspired the name applied to this spotted fish in the 1870s. The Victorian "Dolly Varden" style — polka-dotted fabric gathered into an overskirt — was fashionable enough to name hats, cakes, and eventually this brilliantly spotted Pacific Northwest char.

The Glacial Stream Ecosystem

Habitat requirements

Temperature <10-12°C, glacially fed

Cold, clear, high-gradient streams fed by glacial meltwater or permanent snowfields. Olympic Peninsula and North Cascades core habitat.

Spawning

Fall, September–November

Female excavates redd in clean gravel of cold tributary. 500-2,000 eggs. Spawning males develop vivid orange-red spots.

Diet

Invertebrates, small fish, salmon eggs

Aquatic invertebrates, terrestrial insects, small fish. Known to take dislodged salmon eggs during spawning runs — a natural, minor ecological relationship.

Predators

Osprey, Eagle, Merganser, Otter

Osprey (224), Bald Eagle (221), Merganser (119-120), River Otter (63) all documented Dolly Varden predators.

Conservation

Climate change is the primary threat. As glaciers retreat and snowpack declines, glacial streams warm beyond tolerable limits. Additionally: Brook Trout (Salvelinus fontinalis, introduced eastern species) competes with and hybridizes with native Dolly Varden, reducing genetic integrity. Olympic National Park and North Cascades National Park protect core habitat.
