## FIRST PHALACROCORACIDAE: THE MILESTONE

### Cormorants Enter the Engine

Degree 287 is the first appearance of Phalacrocoracidae in the engine:

- **What are cormorants?** Cormorants (family Phalacrocoracidae) are medium to large waterbirds specialized for pursuit diving. Unlike ducks, which have water-repellent plumage, cormorants have partially wettable feathers that reduce buoyancy and aid underwater propulsion. This adaptation requires the characteristic cormorant behavior of perching with wings spread to dry -- the "spread-eagle" posture seen on any rocky outcrop where cormorants rest. The wettable plumage that makes cormorants excellent divers also makes them vulnerable to cold: they are energetically constrained in a way that waterfowl with more water-repellent plumage are not.
- **Pacific Northwest cormorant species.** Three cormorant species breed in the Pacific Northwest: Brandt's Cormorant (*Urile penicillatus*), Pelagic Cormorant (*Urile pelagicus*), and Double-crested Cormorant (*Nannopterum auritum*). Degree 287 documents Brandt's (the most numerous offshore colonial nester); degree 288 will document the Double-crested (the most widespread and adaptable species).
- **The Brandt's vs. others.** Brandt's Cormorant is the most strictly marine of the three Pacific Northwest species -- it is almost exclusively a saltwater bird that breeds on exposed offshore rocks. Pelagic Cormorant is smaller and nests on steep cliff faces. Double-crested is the inland adaptable species that uses freshwater as well as saltwater habitats. Brandt's is the colonial offshore specialist.

(Sources: Phalacrocoracidae family characters; wettable plumage and diving adaptation; wing-spreading behavior; three PNW cormorant species; Brandt's vs. Pelagic vs. Double-crested comparison)

## URILE PENICILLATUS: THE BRANDT'S CORMORANT

### Taxonomy and Biology

- **Scientific name.** *Urile penicillatus* (formerly *Phalacrocorax penicillatus*). The genus *Urile* was erected following molecular analysis that separated several Pacific cormorant species from the broader *Phalacrocorax* complex. *penicillatus* = "penciled" or "marked with thin lines" (Latin: *penicillus* = brush, pencil) -- referring to the white filoplumes (thread-like decorative feathers) that adult birds display during the breeding season on the neck and rump.
- **The naming honor.** Common name honors Georg von Brandt (1799-1868), director of the Zoological Museum of the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, Russia, who made significant contributions to Pacific natural history through his descriptions of numerous species from Russian naval expedition collections.
- **Size.** 33-35 inches (84-89 cm) length; wingspan 48-50 inches (122-127 cm). Heavier than Pelagic Cormorant; lighter than Great Cormorant. Medium-large cormorant.
- **Appearance.** Adult breeding: iridescent dark blue-green overall; bright blue gular pouch (the throat skin visible during display); white filoplumes on the face and rump; pale buff-beige breast patch. Non-breeding: duller, gular pouch less vibrant. The bright blue gular pouch during breeding is the most reliable Brandt's identification mark -- no other Pacific Northwest cormorant has this feature.
- **The blue gular pouch.** During courtship, the gular pouch inflates and the surrounding skin brightens to an intense bright blue. This is the primary visual display element in courtship; females select for males with the brightest blue gular skin. The brightness of the gular pouch correlates with the male's body condition and parasite load -- it is an honest signal of quality.

(Sources: Urile penicillatus taxonomy; genus Urile molecular basis; penicillatus etymology; Georg von Brandt; size 33-35 inches; breeding plumage; blue gular pouch; filoplumes; honest signal of quality)

## THE COLONY SOUNDSCAPE

### Guttural Croaking Roar at the Nest Site

The Brandt's Cormorant colony sound is unlike anything else in the Pacific Northwest seabird catalog:

- **The call.** Brandt's Cormorant produces a series of low-frequency, guttural, rough vocalizations -- croaks, grunts, and gargling sounds that are individually harsh and collectively overwhelming at a colony site. The calls are used in multiple behavioral contexts: territorial defense of the nest site, mate attraction and pair bonding display, threat communication with neighboring breeders, and parent-chick recognition.
- **The colony acoustic environment.** A large Brandt's Cormorant colony -- 1,000+ breeding pairs on an offshore rock -- produces a constant, overlapping chorus of guttural calls during the active nesting period. The sound is not melodic; it is raw, rough, and dense. Individual calls are distinguishable only at close range; at colony scale, they merge into a low-frequency roaring mass. The sound carries across the water to approaching boats. Rocky islands with active Brandt's colonies announce themselves acoustically before they become visible.
- **Frequency.** Brandt's Cormorant calls are concentrated in the low-frequency range: approximately 300-3,000 Hz, with the most energy in the 500-1,500 Hz band. The calls have a rough, unfiltered character -- there is no musical quality; the calls are purely functional vocalizations produced by large birds in a highly compressed social environment.
- **The nest site soundscape.** At a Brandt's Cormorant nest site, the acoustic environment includes the calls of the breeding adults, the begging calls of chicks (higher-pitched, insistent), and the general noise of a large colonial nesting aggregation. The combination is dense and continuous during peak breeding season.
- **E=7 calibration.** E=7: loud, dense, overlapping colony sound. Less than the Steller Sea Lion bull roar (E=8: single, very large animal, sustained, lower frequency) but more than the storm-petrel colony (E=6: tiny birds with limited individual amplitude). The Brandt's Cormorant colony sound has more individual-level amplitude than a storm-petrel (medium-large bird vs. tiny bird) combined with the collective effect of hundreds of simultaneous calls.

(Sources: Brandt's Cormorant vocalization description; colony acoustic environment; frequency 300-3,000 Hz; behavioral contexts; chick begging calls; E=7 calibration vs. storm-petrel E=6 and Steller E=8)

## COLONIAL NESTING BEHAVIOR

### The Offshore Rock Colony

Brandt's Cormorant is the most densely colonial seabird on the Pacific Northwest outer coast:

- **Nest site selection.** Brandt's Cormorant nests are placed on flat to moderately sloped rocky surfaces on offshore rocks, stacks, and islands. The birds do not use cliff faces (as Pelagic Cormorant does) or freshwater sites (as Double-crested does). The offshore rock colony is the essential habitat.
- **Nest density.** Nests are placed extremely close together -- touching in some cases. Individual territory at the nest site is the area the incubating bird can reach from the nest bowl without moving. The density of nesting at a large Brandt's colony is among the highest of any Pacific seabird.
- **Nest construction.** Nests are built from plant material (eelgrass, seaweed) and, most characteristically, from guano. Brandt's Cormorant nests become substantial guano-cemented structures over successive breeding seasons. The accumulated guano of large colonies turns the nesting rocks white -- cormorant colonies are visible from considerable distance as white patches on otherwise dark rocks.
- **Nest site fidelity.** Brandt's Cormorants show high nest site fidelity -- pairs return to the same colony and often the same nest site in successive years. Site fidelity is an important mechanism for pair bond maintenance; returning pairs locate each other at the colony by returning to the shared nest site.
- **The display cycle.** The blue gular pouch display by males is the primary courtship mechanism. Males orient toward females with gular pouch pulsed, filoplumes displayed, performing head-throwing and bill-clicking displays. Female choice based on gular pouch brightness selects for male quality.

(Sources: Brandt's Cormorant nest site selection; nest density; eelgrass and guano nest construction; guano-whitened rocks as visual indicator; nest site fidelity; gular pouch display cycle; pair bond maintenance)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST COLONIES

### Where Brandt's Nest on the Outer Coast

Brandt's Cormorant colonies are concentrated on the outer Pacific coast, not in inland Puget Sound waters:

- **Three Arch Rocks (Oregon).** Three Arch Rocks National Wildlife Refuge off Cape Meares is one of the most significant seabird colonies on the Oregon coast. Brandt's Cormorant is among the breeding species here, alongside Leach's Storm-Petrel (SPS 286), Common Murre, Tufted Puffin, and Rhinoceros Auklet.
- **Farallon Islands (California, historical context).** The Farallon Islands support the largest Brandt's Cormorant breeding colony in the world -- tens of thousands of breeding pairs. While outside the strict Pacific Northwest geographic scope, the Farallons provide the benchmark population context.
- **Washington outer coast.** The Washington coast supports Brandt's Cormorant colonies on offshore rocks and stacks, primarily off the western Olympic Peninsula coast. The species is less numerous in Washington than in Oregon or California.
- **The Puget Sound boundary.** Brandt's Cormorant is uncommon in Puget Sound proper -- it is a coastal saltwater species that does not typically use inland waters. This distinguishes it from the Double-crested Cormorant, which readily uses Puget Sound. The division between inner (Double-crested) and outer (Brandt's) is ecologically significant.

(Sources: Three Arch Rocks Oregon; Farallon Islands largest colony worldwide; Washington outer coast colonies; Olympic Peninsula; Brandt's vs. Double-crested habitat division: coastal vs. inland)

## CONSERVATION

### Prey Availability and Oceanographic Coupling

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern globally, though Pacific Northwest populations fluctuate with oceanographic conditions.
- **Prey dependence.** Brandt's Cormorant feeds primarily on schooling fish: Pacific sardine, Pacific herring, Pacific anchovy, and juvenile rockfish. The availability of these prey species in Pacific Northwest coastal waters directly controls breeding success. In years of warm water and reduced upwelling (El Niño events), prey availability collapses and breeding success declines dramatically. In cold-water, high-upwelling years, prey availability is excellent and breeding success is high. The species is an indicator species for upwelling productivity.
- **El Niño sensitivity.** Brandt's Cormorant is more sensitive to El Niño oceanographic events than most Pacific Northwest seabirds. During major El Niño events (1982-83, 1997-98, 2015-16), breeding failure was widespread in California populations. The combination of warm water (fewer prey) and extended incubation period makes Brandt's particularly vulnerable to multi-month prey shortfalls.
- **The guano economy.** Historically, Brandt's Cormorant colonies produced guano that was harvested commercially in California. The guano deposits on colony rocks represent concentrated nitrogen and phosphorus from prey fish consumed over many seasons. The ecological role of cormorant colonies in nutrient cycling is significant.

(Sources: Brandt's Cormorant IUCN Least Concern; prey species: sardine, herring, anchovy, rockfish; El Niño sensitivity; 1982-83, 1997-98, 2015-16 El Niño impacts; upwelling productivity indicator; guano historical harvest)
