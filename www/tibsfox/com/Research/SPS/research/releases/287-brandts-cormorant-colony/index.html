First Phalacrocoracidae — Cormorants Enter the Engine

Urile penicillatus — Brandt's Cormorant — is the FIRST PHALACROCORACIDAE (cormorant family) in the engine. The outer-coast colonial specialist: almost exclusively saltwater, dense nesting on exposed offshore rocks, thousands of breeding pairs per colony, bright blue gular pouch display, El Niño-sensitive prey specialist.

The guttural colony chorus: dozens to hundreds of birds producing simultaneous low-frequency croaks and grunts from nest sites inches apart on a Pacific rock. One of the outer coast's defining acoustic environments in summer.

Acoustic Profile — The Colony Roar

Frequency: 300–3,000 Hz; energy 500–1,500 Hz. Rough, unfiltered guttural character. No musical quality: purely functional vocalizations from large birds in compressed social space.

Colony environment: Nest sites inches apart; dozens to hundreds of birds calling simultaneously; includes adult territorial calls, courtship display calls, and chick begging calls (higher-pitched, insistent). Carries across water to approaching vessels. Rocky islands announce themselves acoustically before becoming visible. E=7: more individual amplitude than storm-petrel (larger bird, 33-35 inches vs. 7-8 inches), combined with colony mass effect. Less than Steller Sea Lion (E=8: sustained single-animal roar at much greater individual amplitude).

The Blue Gular Pouch

Brandt's Cormorant breeding plumage features a brilliant blue gular pouch — the throat skin that inflates and brightens to intense blue during courtship. No other Pacific Northwest cormorant has this feature: Pelagic and Double-crested have duller (olive or orange-yellow) gular skin. The blue pouch is the most reliable field identification mark and the primary visual display element in courtship. Female choice correlates with pouch brightness; brightness correlates with the male's body condition and parasite load. The blue gular pouch is an honest signal of male quality.

Adult Brandt's also display white filoplumes (thread-like decorative feathers) on the neck and rump during breeding season — the source of the Latin name penicillatus ("marked with thin lines").

El Niño Sensitivity

Brandt's Cormorant feeds almost exclusively on schooling fish: Pacific sardine, Pacific herring, Pacific anchovy, and juvenile rockfish. The availability of these prey species is directly controlled by oceanographic conditions. In cold-water, high-upwelling years, prey is abundant and breeding success is excellent. In warm-water El Niño years (1982-83, 1997-98, 2015-16), prey availability collapses and breeding failure is widespread. The species is an indicator organism for California Current upwelling productivity: when the current is cold and productive, Brandt's thrive; when warm, breeding fails. No other Pacific Northwest seabird is as tightly coupled to this oceanographic signal.
