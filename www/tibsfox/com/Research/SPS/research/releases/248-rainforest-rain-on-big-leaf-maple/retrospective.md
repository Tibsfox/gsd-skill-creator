## What Worked

1. **The four-layer percussion structure is the entry's most technically detailed element.** High canopy, mid-canopy drips, understory complexity, ground plops -- these are the actual acoustic layers of rainfall in a temperate rainforest. The vertical structure of the forest creates an acoustic gradient from top to bottom.
2. **The Meteorology as family designation is the correct structural choice.** The distinction between Hydrology (water in the landscape: rivers, waterfalls) and Meteorology (water in the atmosphere: rain, fog, mist) is meaningful. The engine's classification system has been extended correctly.
3. **The water arc 245-248 framing is the entry's most important engine-structural contribution.** The four entries form a complete hydrological cycle: atmospheric precipitation → river flow → waterfall → back to precipitation. Documenting this as an arc makes the structure visible.
4. **The leaf size gradient as continuous pitch range is acoustically correct.** Large leaves resonate at lower frequencies; small leaves at higher frequencies. The gradient is continuous, not discrete. The maple is a natural instrument with a continuous spectrum, not a discrete scale.

## Discoveries

1. **The Hoh rain shadow comparison is the most striking climate fact in the 245-248 block.** The Hoh receives 140+ inches per year; Sequim (on the opposite side of the Olympic Peninsula, 60 miles away) receives 16 inches -- less than Los Angeles. The same mountain range creates both extremes simultaneously. This is the most dramatic rain shadow in the continental United States.
2. **The drip cascade effect is under-documented in general rain acoustics descriptions.** The fact that a single raindrop can initiate a cascade through multiple leaf tiers -- each drip generating a new acoustic event at each lower leaf, with time delays between events -- extends the acoustic consequence of a single drop well beyond the initial impact. The rainforest multiplies each rain event acoustically.
3. **The Hoh supports all five Pacific salmon species plus steelhead -- one of very few remaining rivers.** Connecting back to Pacific Salmon (degree 236): the same precipitation documented at degree 248 is what fills the Hoh River and makes it one of the most intact salmon watersheds in Washington State. The abiotic rain feeds the biotic salmon run.
