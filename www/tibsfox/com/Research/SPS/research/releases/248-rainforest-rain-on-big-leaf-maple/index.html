FIRST METEOROLOGY ENTRY — HOH RAINFOREST — WETTEST PLACE IN CONTINENTAL US

The Hoh Rainforest receives 140-170 inches of rain per year — approximately 12-14 feet. Pacific storms moving east encounter the Olympic Mountains and precipitate. The big-leaf maple (Acer macrophyllum), with leaves 6-12 inches across, creates a complex percussion system when rain falls on it. Each leaf is a drum. Each leaf size is a different pitch. The drips from the first strike cascade to lower leaves, which cascade again. The rainforest is playing itself.

The Hoh Rainforest

The Hoh River valley on the western Olympic Peninsula receives more precipitation than any location in the continental United States outside the highest mountain ranges. The climate mechanism: Pacific storms moving east encounter the Olympic Mountains (first major topographic barrier), are forced upward (orographic lift), cool, and precipitate on the west-facing slopes. The Hoh receives 140-170 inches. The rain shadow east of the Olympics (Sequim) receives 16 inches — less than Los Angeles. Same peninsula, opposite slopes, radically different worlds.

The precipitation produces the most biologically productive temperate forest in the world: Sitka spruce reaching 280+ feet, western red cedar over 1,000 years old, western hemlock, and bigleaf maple draped in moss. The canopy is a multi-layered acoustic structure through which rain passes and is modified at every level.

Big-Leaf Maple as Instrument

Acer macrophyllum: the largest maple in North America. Leaves 15-30 cm across (6-12 inches), five-lobed, attached to long flexible petioles. In the Hoh, maple branches are covered in thick moss. The maple is the primary acoustic surface for rainfall in the understory — the instrument the rain plays.

“Each leaf size has a different resonant frequency. The largest leaves as the bass notes, progressively smaller leaves at progressively higher pitches. A continuous gradient of maple-leaf resonances — not discrete pitches but a spectrum.”

The Four Percussion Layers

Layer 1

High Canopy Impacts

First rain on topmost leaves. Soft patter arriving from above. Fine rain drops on large Sitka spruce needles and bigleaf maple leaves. Filtered, distant sound.

Layer 2

Mid-Canopy Drips

Coalesced water dripping from upper to mid-canopy leaves. Larger drops, more percussive. Irregular intervals. The beginning of the patter-drip rhythm.

Layer 3

Understory Complexity

By understory: some direct rain (random), some drips (irregular intervals), some moss-saturated release (slow, large drops). The most complex acoustic layer.

Layer 4

Ground Plops

Large canopy drips hitting saturated soil, standing water in depressions, ferns, fallen logs. The softest layer. Deepest pitch. The final resonance of the rainforest drum.

Acoustic Profile — E=4 Complex Patter-Drip

Large leaf bass impacts (low frequency): 100–400 Hz
Mid-size leaf resonance (mid frequency): 400–2000 Hz
Fine rain on small leaves / moss drip (high): 2000+ Hz
Overall: E=4 (gentle, present, complex, continuous; not overwhelming)

The same element in four modes. Current, plunge, plunge-in-canyon, precipitation. The arc describes the water cycle: precipitation feeds rivers, rivers carve canyons, canyons frame waterfalls. And back to precipitation.
