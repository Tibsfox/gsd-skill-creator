## THE DAWN CHORUS -- THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST SPRING

### The Most Complex Acoustic Event in Temperate North America

The spring dawn chorus in the Pacific Northwest Puget Sound lowlands is one of the richest acoustic environments in North America. At peak (late April through June), the timing and composition:

- **The trigger.** The dawn chorus is triggered primarily by light level, not temperature or weather. Individual species respond to specific light thresholds at dawn, which is why the order in which species begin singing is predictable and consistent: American Robin first (earliest light response), followed by thrushes, then sparrows and chickadees, then blackbirds, then wrens, then juncos. The sequence is determined by each species' light sensitivity threshold.
- **The duration.** At peak spring, the chorus begins approximately 45 minutes before sunrise and sustains for 60-90 minutes. During this window, the maximum number of species are singing simultaneously before the full morning light causes individual birds to shift from territorial singing to foraging.
- **The Pacific Northwest character.** The Puget Sound lowland dawn chorus is characterized by species that include Pacific Wren (one of the most acoustically complex small birds in North America, whose song can contain 30+ distinct syllable types in a single bout), American Robin (the archetypal dawn chorus anchor), and Swainson's Thrush (whose spiral upward song is among the most beautiful bird calls in the Western Hemisphere). The regional composition is distinct from other North American dawn chorus locations.

(Sources: Pacific Northwest dawn chorus species composition; light trigger mechanism; species ordering by light threshold; Robin first; Pacific Wren song complexity; Swainson's Thrush spiral song; American Robin anchor role; April-June peak timing)

## THE PACIFIC STORM -- THE ACOUSTIC AUGMENTATION

### How a Spring Storm Changes the Chorus

A Pacific storm overlapping with the spring dawn chorus produces the E=8 event:

- **The storm timing.** Pacific low-pressure systems can arrive at any time, but spring storms (April-May) frequently arrive on diurnal cycles that overlap with dawn. The atmospheric dynamics that produce spring storm intensification overnight mean that the storm's acoustic peak often coincides with the biological dawn chorus peak.
- **Rain on canopy.** Rain on alder and big-leaf maple canopy (the dominant deciduous trees of Puget Sound lowlands) produces a broadband white noise floor at 40-80 dB depending on intensity. This acoustic floor raises the minimum audible volume for individual bird calls &mdash; birds that cannot be heard over the rain do not contribute to the E=8 calibration. Only species that call loudly enough to rise above the rain floor are included.
- **The volume selection.** The storm's acoustic floor is an involuntary volume selection mechanism: it amplifies the relative presence of loud callers (Robin, Pacific Wren, Red-winged Blackbird) and reduces the contribution of softer callers. The net effect is a chorus that is simultaneously more uniform in apparent volume and richer in tonal character than a fair-weather chorus.
- **Wind in conifers.** Wind through Sitka spruce and Douglas fir tops adds a continuous sub-bass drone (50-200 Hz) beneath the bird calls. This component is felt before it is distinctly heard and provides the physical grounding of the E=8 event.
- **Acoustic carry in humid air.** Saturated air actually carries mid-frequency bird calls (2-5 kHz range) further than dry air in some conditions, because the humidity reduces the high-frequency air absorption that limits call carry distance in dry conditions. Some species may call more effectively in rain conditions at their key communication frequencies.

(Sources: Pacific storm spring timing; rain on alder and big-leaf maple; broadband white noise floor 40-80 dB; volume selection mechanism; wind in Sitka spruce; sub-bass drone 50-200 Hz; acoustic carry humid vs. dry air; humidity-frequency interaction)

## SPECIES PROFILES -- THE STORM CHORUS COMPOSITION

### Eight Primary Species Through the Rain

**American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*):** The first singer at dawn and the storm chorus anchor. Pure whistled phrases 2-8 kHz. The Robin continues singing through moderate to heavy rain more consistently than most other species &mdash; its territorial imperative is strong enough that weather rarely silences it. The phrase structure (cheerily, cheeri-up, cheer-up) is one of the most recognizable bird calls in the Pacific Northwest.

**Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*):** The most acoustically complex small bird in the Pacific Northwest. Body mass: 8-10 g. Song complexity: 30+ distinct syllable types in a single bout, covering 5-9 kHz. The Pacific Wren sings through rain with particular reliability &mdash; the species is associated with the dark, moist forest understory environments that Pacific storms create. The song's complexity in a rainstorm is one of the most acoustically remarkable events in the spring forest.

**Song Sparrow (*Melospiza melodia*):** The most individually variable singer in the dawn chorus. Each Song Sparrow has a repertoire of 8-20 distinct songs and switches between them. Song Sparrows sing through rain reliably and are found throughout the Puget Sound lowlands from shoreline shrubs to forest edge. Their mid-frequency calls (2-6 kHz) rise clearly above the rain white noise floor.

**Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*):** Spring migrant arriving in Puget Sound in April-May. The spiral upward song is distinctive: each phrase ends higher than it began, creating a musical ascent that resolves briefly before the next phrase begins. Storm arrivals sometimes trigger increased Swainson's Thrush calling, making them one of the species most associated with the transition between fair weather and storm in the spring Puget Sound lowlands.

**Black-capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*):** The fee-bee whistle and the various chickadee calls are present year-round and appear reliably in the spring storm chorus. Chickadees produce alarm calls (a distinctive chick-a-dee-dee-dee, with the number of "dee" notes increasing with predator threat level) in response to weather disturbance and also give their spring territorial fee-bee whistle through rain conditions.

**Red-winged Blackbird (*Agelaius phoeniceus*):** The konk-la-ree territorial call of the male Red-winged Blackbird is one of the lowest-frequency contributions to the dawn chorus (mid-frequency anchor, 1-3 kHz fundamental). Males display actively in rain conditions during the breeding season. Wetland edges &mdash; common throughout the Puget Sound lowlands &mdash; provide the habitat from which the species calls into the spring storm chorus.

**Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*):** The musical trill of the Dark-eyed Junco (5-8 kHz) is a high-frequency contribution to the chorus. Both resident and migratory populations are present in spring, with migrant individuals adding to the spring peak. The Junco's trill carries clearly above the rain noise at close range.

**Common Raven (*Corvus corax*):** The deep gurgling, yodeling, and clicking calls of the Common Raven (200-1,500 Hz) provide the lowest-frequency anchor to the dawn chorus. Ravens are highly vocal in rain conditions and in spring pair communication. The pair's calls in storm conditions add the sub-bass component that grounds the chorus's full-spectrum acoustic character.

(Sources: Robin Turdus migratorius; Pacific Wren Troglodytes pacificus 8-10g 30+ syllable types; Song Sparrow Melospiza melodia 8-20 song repertoire; Swainson's Thrush Catharus ustulatus spiral song; Black-capped Chickadee Poecile atricapillus; Red-winged Blackbird Agelaius phoeniceus; Dark-eyed Junco Junco hyemalis; Common Raven Corvus corax 200-1500 Hz)
