Multi-Species at E=8 — The Chorus Through the Storm

Every previous SPS entry documents a single species or a specific paired event (owl pair). Degree 310 documents something different: the multi-species dawn chorus occurring during a Pacific storm. Not one voice but many — the combined acoustic event of multiple bird species calling simultaneously in the driving rain of a spring Pacific low-pressure system moving through the Puget Sound lowlands. The chorus itself (peak spring dawn chorus, April-June) is E=6-7. The Pacific storm acoustic layer adds wind noise, rain percussion on canopy and water surfaces, and the particular atmospheric conditions of reduced visibility and heightened acoustic carry in humid air. The combination calibrates at E=8: the first multi-species acoustic complex in the SPS engine's maximum zone.

The Pacific Storm Dawn Chorus — What It Is

The spring dawn chorus in the Pacific Northwest is one of the most complex acoustic events in temperate North America. At peak (late April through June), the dawn chorus in Puget Sound lowlands begins approximately 45 minutes before sunrise and sustains for 60-90 minutes before quieting as morning light intensifies.

When a Pacific storm (typically a low-pressure system from the North Pacific, arriving as wind-driven rain from the southwest) overlaps with peak dawn chorus timing, the result is a multi-layered acoustic complex:

American Robin

Turdidae. Earliest riser. Pure whistled phrases 2-8 kHz. Calls continue through rain. Often perceived as the storm's "soloist."

Pacific Wren

Troglodytidae. Highest complexity per body mass. 5-9 kHz. Loud for a 10g bird. Sings through storms more reliably than larger species.

Swainson's Thrush

Turdidae. Spiral upward song. Calls at dawn before full light. Storm arrivals trigger calling in some individuals.

Black-capped Chickadee

Paridae. Fee-bee whistle 3-5 kHz. Present year-round. Alarm calls in storm conditions. High-frequency punctuation in the chorus.

Song Sparrow

Passerellidae. Highly variable song. One of the most persistent singers through rain. Each individual has a distinct song phrase.

Red-winged Blackbird

Icteridae. Konk-la-ree territorial call. Male displays in rain. Mid-frequency anchor to the chorus. Wetland edge species.

Dark-eyed Junco

Passerellidae. Trill song 5-8 kHz. Spring migrant and resident. Present at dawn chorus peak. High-frequency element.

Common Raven

Corvidae. Deep gurgling, 300-1,500 Hz. Low-frequency anchor. Pair communication in storm conditions. Broad frequency complement to higher-frequency songbirds.

Acoustic Profile — Storm Chorus at E=8

Frequency range: 300-8,000+ Hz combined. The simultaneous presence of low-frequency corvid calls (300-1,500 Hz) and high-frequency wren/thrush songs (5-9 kHz) produces a full-spectrum acoustic event.

Storm acoustic layer: Pacific storm rain on alder and big-leaf maple canopy produces broadband white noise at 40-80 dB, depending on rain intensity. This acoustic floor does not silence the birds — it raises the threshold below which individual calls are audible. Birds that call through the storm must call louder, creating an involuntary volume selection.

Wind in conifers: Wind through Sitka spruce and Douglas fir tops produces low-frequency drone (50-200 Hz) that adds a sub-bass layer beneath the bird calls and rain noise. This layer is felt as much as heard.

E=8 calibration: Multiple simultaneous species at full calling intensity during peak spring season + Pacific storm acoustic augmentation (rain white noise + wind drone + atmospheric conditions). The combined event exceeds any single-species entry at equivalent energy. E=8: high sustained energy across the full acoustic spectrum.

Why Birds Call Through Storms

The persistence of bird calling through Pacific storms has several explanations:

Territorial imperative: Spring territorial establishment (April-June) is time-critical. A male bird that stops calling during storms allows rival males to advance into its territory unchallenged. The cost of silence in spring exceeds the cost of calling in rain.

Humidity and acoustic carry: Humid air, particularly rain-saturated air, carries certain bird call frequencies more effectively than dry air. Higher humidity reduces the high-frequency absorption that normally limits long-distance acoustic communication. Some species may actually call more effectively in rain conditions than in dry conditions at certain frequencies.

Dawn light as trigger: The dawn chorus is triggered primarily by light level, not weather. When light levels reach the threshold, birds call regardless of storm conditions. The light trigger and the storm's acoustic environment coexist; the birds cannot selectively respond to one and not the other.
