## STRUCTURAL EVENT: STRIGIDAE STREAK BROKEN -- Caprimulgidae Enters

Degree 145 **breaks** the Strigidae dectet and introduces a new family:

- **The streak:** Strigidae held ten consecutive entries (135-144), the all-time record. Seven genera. Eight species. E=6 to E=2 range.
- **The break:** At degree 145, Caprimulgidae (nightjars) enters. The owls are done. A new order of nocturnal birds takes over.
- **Why Caprimulgidae?** The nightjars are the owls' ecological counterparts -- nocturnal, insectivorous, cryptically camouflaged, vocally distinctive. But where owls are predators of vertebrates (mostly), nightjars are aerial insectivores. Where owls perch upright and scan, nightjars sit flat on the ground and hawk insects in flight. The transition from owls to nightjars is a transition from nocturnal predation to nocturnal insectivory.
- **First Caprimulgidae:** The Common Poorwill is the smallest North American nightjar and the one with the most extraordinary physiological adaptation -- torpor/hibernation. Starting the nightjar family with its most remarkable member.

### Caprimulgidae -- The Nightjar Family

The family Caprimulgidae (goatsuckers/nightjars) includes:

- **Defining traits:** Nocturnal or crepuscular insectivores. Large mouths fringed with rictal bristles for catching flying insects. Cryptic plumage (bark-like, leaf-like patterns). Short legs, long wings. Most species are ground-nesters.
- **Name origin:** "Caprimulgidae" comes from Latin *caprimulgus* (goat-milker) -- an ancient superstition that nightjars suckled goats at night. The wide mouths and habit of flying near livestock (to catch insects attracted to the animals) gave rise to this myth.
- **PNW members:** Three nightjar species breed in the PNW -- Common Poorwill, Common Nighthawk, and Lesser Nighthawk (rare). The Poorwill and Nighthawk will appear at degrees 145 and 146, establishing the family quickly.
- **Acoustic tradition:** Nightjars are named for their calls. "Poorwill" = "poor-will." "Nighthawk" = the booming dive sound. "Whip-poor-will" = the Eastern species' call. The family's identity is inseparable from its vocalizations.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## E=3 -- THE HYPNOTIC REPETITION

### Why E=3

The Common Poorwill registers at E=3:

- **Moderate amplitude:** The "poor-will" call is clearly audible at moderate distances (up to 200-300 meters in still conditions). It is not loud like an owl hoot, but it carries well in the still, dry air of its habitat. The sound does not fill the landscape like a Great Horned Owl -- it creates a localized acoustic zone.
- **Repetitive structure:** The two-note "poor-will" is repeated endlessly -- dozens, sometimes hundreds of times in succession. The repetition is hypnotic, metronomic, and unvarying. There is no phrase development, no climax, no variation. The bird says "poor-will" and then says it again. And again.
- **Moderate frequency:** The call is a whistled phrase at approximately 1500-2500 Hz -- a comfortable mid-range frequency, pleasant to the human ear. Not low and booming like an owl, not high and piercing like a wren.
- **E=3 calibration:** E=3 reflects: moderate amplitude (audible but not loud), moderate complexity (two notes, no variation), moderate behavioral context (territorial/pair communication), moderate aesthetic impact (pleasant, hypnotic, but not dramatic). A step up from the Flammulated Owl's E=2 near-silence, but not approaching the energetic displays of higher-rated species.

## THE ONLY HIBERNATING BIRD

### The Discovery

The Common Poorwill's hibernation is one of the most remarkable discoveries in ornithology:

- **Edmund Jaeger's discovery (1946-1948):** In the winter of 1946-1947, biologist Edmund C. Jaeger discovered a Common Poorwill in a state of torpor in a rock crevice in the Chuckwalla Mountains of the Colorado Desert in southern California. The bird was cold, unresponsive, and showed no visible breathing. Jaeger returned to the same spot and found the same bird torpid in subsequent winters (1947-1948, 1948-1949). This was the first documented case of hibernation (prolonged torpor) in any bird species.
- **Physiological extremes:** During torpor, the Poorwill's body temperature drops from approximately 40 degrees C (normal bird temperature) to as low as 5 degrees C. Heart rate drops from approximately 100 beats per minute to fewer than 10. Metabolic rate drops to approximately 1/30th of normal. The bird becomes essentially cold-blooded -- a remarkable reversal for an endotherm.
- **Duration:** Poorwill torpor bouts can last days to weeks. The longest documented bouts approach 100 days -- true hibernation by any physiological definition. No other bird species is known to enter such prolonged torpor states.
- **Hopi knowledge:** The Hopi people of Arizona had long known about the Poorwill's torpor, calling the bird "Holchko" -- "the sleeping one." Indigenous knowledge preceded Western scientific discovery by centuries, possibly millennia.

### Why Hibernate?

The Poorwill's hibernation is directly linked to its ecological constraints:

- **Insect dependency:** Like all nightjars, the Poorwill eats exclusively flying insects. When temperatures drop and insects disappear, the Poorwill cannot eat.
- **Migration alternative:** Most insectivorous birds solve the winter food problem by migrating. The Poorwill's solution is the opposite -- instead of flying thousands of kilometers to find insects, it shuts down metabolically and waits for insects to return. Hibernation is an alternative to migration.
- **Energy savings:** Hibernation is energetically efficient. By dropping body temperature and metabolic rate, the Poorwill can survive weeks or months on stored fat that would last only days at normal metabolic rates. The math favors staying and sleeping over flying and searching.
- **Thermal rock crevices:** Poorwills hibernate in rock crevices on south-facing slopes -- microhabitats where daytime solar heating moderates the coldest temperatures. The choice of hibernation site is critical; the bird needs a spot that stays above freezing but is cold enough to maintain torpor.

(Sources: Jaeger 1948, 1949; Brigham 1992; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Hopi ethnography)

## CRYPTIC CAMOUFLAGE -- INVISIBLE ON THE GROUND

### Ground-Sitting Perfection

The Common Poorwill is one of the most perfectly camouflaged birds in North America:

- **Plumage as substrate:** The Poorwill's plumage is a complex pattern of gray, brown, buff, and black that precisely mimics the rocky, sandy ground it sits on. When a Poorwill is on the ground (which is most of the time), it is virtually invisible -- a feathered stone.
- **No nest:** Poorwills do not build nests. They lay eggs directly on bare ground, rocky surfaces, or gravel. The eggs and the incubating bird are both camouflaged to match the substrate. The entire reproductive strategy relies on invisibility rather than defense.
- **Sit-tight behavior:** When approached, a Poorwill's primary defense is to sit motionless and rely on camouflage. The bird will not flush until nearly stepped on. This sit-tight behavior, combined with the cryptic plumage, makes Poorwills extraordinarily difficult to find during the day.
- **Eye-shine:** At night, Poorwills can be located by eye-shine -- their large eyes reflect light (headlights, flashlights) with a distinctive orange-red glow. This is often the only way to detect the bird in the dark.

### Nocturnal Insect Hunting

The Poorwill is a specialized nocturnal aerial insectivore:

- **Hunting method:** The Poorwill typically hunts from a low perch (rock, fence post, road surface) and sallies out to capture flying insects in its enormous gape. The rictal bristles around the mouth help funnel insects into the wide-open bill.
- **Prey:** Moths, beetles, flying ants, and other nocturnal insects. The Poorwill's large eyes provide excellent night vision for detecting insect silhouettes against the sky.
- **Road-sitting:** Poorwills frequently sit on roads at night, where the warm pavement attracts insects. This behavior makes them visible in headlights (eye-shine) but also vulnerable to vehicle strikes.
- **Low energy hunting:** Unlike the Common Nighthawk's continuous aerial hawking, the Poorwill hunts from perches -- short sallies and returns. This sit-and-wait strategy is lower-energy, consistent with the Poorwill's generally conservative energy expenditure.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Csada & Brigham 1992)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Summer Darkness -- "Poor-Will" Endlessly Repeated

The Common Poorwill's acoustic contribution is **hypnotic repetition** -- the same two-note phrase spoken into the darkness without variation:

- **The call:** "Poor-will" -- a soft, whistled, two-note phrase. The first syllable ("poor") is lower, the second ("will") rises slightly. Some listeners hear a faint third syllable ("poor-will-ip") but the dominant perception is two notes.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 1500-2500 Hz. A comfortable mid-range whistle. Pleasant, clear, and musical without being complex.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate. Clearly audible at 200-300 meters in still conditions. The call carries well in the dry, open habitats where the bird lives -- sagebrush steppe and rocky hillsides where there is little vegetation to absorb sound.
- **Repetition:** The defining acoustic characteristic. The "poor-will" phrase is repeated endlessly -- 20, 50, 100 or more times in succession with metronomic regularity. The interval between phrases is approximately 1-2 seconds. The repetition is hypnotic; after a few minutes the call becomes a rhythmic pulse, part of the ambient soundscape rather than a distinct vocalization.
- **Temporal context:** The call is heard primarily on warm summer nights, from dusk through the night. Peak calling occurs in the hours after sunset. The sound defines summer darkness in dry, eastern Washington landscapes -- if you hear "poor-will, poor-will, poor-will" on a warm night in the sagebrush, you are hearing this bird.
- **Name derivation:** The species name is directly onomatopoeic -- "Poorwill" IS the call. The bird named itself. (The Eastern relative, the Whip-poor-will, follows the same naming tradition.)
- **Ventriloquial quality:** Like many nightjars, the Poorwill's call has a mild ventriloquial quality -- the direction can be difficult to determine, especially when the bird is calling from the ground amid rocks.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
