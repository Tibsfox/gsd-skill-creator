Common Poorwill

Phalaenoptilus nuttallii (Caprimulgidae) — E=3 — “Poor-will” Repeated
FIRST CAPRIMULGIDAE — nightjar family enters the engine. BREAKS STRIGIDAE STREAK at 10 — owls end, nightjars begin. The only bird known to hibernate. Torpor: body temp drops to 5°C, heart rate to <10 bpm, metabolic rate to 1/30th normal. Cryptic camouflage. Nocturnal insect hunter. “Poor-will” endlessly repeated.

STRIGIDAE STREAK BROKEN — Caprimulgidae Enters

The Strigidae dectet (135–144) held the all-time family streak record. Ten consecutive owls. At degree 145, Caprimulgidae (nightjars) enters and the streak ends. Owls → nightjars. Vertebrate predators → aerial insectivores. Perch-and-scan → ground-sitting and hawking. FIRST CAPRIMULGIDAE in the engine.

The Only Hibernating Bird

Discovered by Edmund Jaeger (1946–1948) in the Chuckwalla Mountains. Body temperature drops from 40°C to 5°C. Heart rate from ~100 bpm to <10 bpm. Metabolic rate to 1/30th normal. Torpor bouts approaching 100 days. The Hopi knew: “Holchko” — “the sleeping one.” Hibernation instead of migration. An alternative strategy for surviving winter.

Cryptic Camouflage — Invisible on the Ground

Plumage precisely mimics rocky, sandy ground. No nest — eggs laid directly on bare substrate. Will not flush until nearly stepped on. At night, detected only by eye-shine (orange-red glow). Sits on roads for warmth. A feathered stone.

Acoustic Profile

The call is the name: “poor-will” — a soft, whistled, two-note phrase at 1500–2500 Hz. Repeated endlessly — 20, 50, 100+ times without variation. Interval: 1–2 seconds. Hypnotic, metronomic, part of the ambient soundscape. Heard on warm summer nights in sagebrush steppe and dry rocky country east of the Cascades. Mildly ventriloquial. The sound of summer darkness in arid Washington.
