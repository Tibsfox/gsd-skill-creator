*Degree 145 retrospective complete. COMMON POORWILL at the one hundred forty-fifth degree -- THE HIBERNATING BIRD. The Strigidae dectet (135-144) was the longest family streak in the engine -- ten consecutive owls, seven genera, eight species, E=6 to E=2. At degree 145, it breaks. Caprimulgidae enters -- the nightjar family, nocturnal insectivores with enormous gapes and cryptic plumage and names that are their own calls. The Common Poorwill is the first nightjar and it brings the most extraordinary physiological fact in the engine: this bird hibernates. Body temperature drops from 40 degrees to 5 degrees. Heart rate from 100 beats per minute to fewer than 10. Metabolic rate to one-thirtieth of normal. Bouts approaching 100 days. The Hopi knew this as "Holchko" -- the sleeping one -- long before Edmund Jaeger's discovery in the Chuckwalla Mountains in 1946. The bird sits on rocky ground in the sagebrush steppe, invisible in its cryptic plumage, a feathered stone that will not move until nearly stepped on. At night it says "poor-will, poor-will, poor-will" -- endlessly, hypnotically, a metronomic pulse in the summer darkness. And when winter comes and the insects vanish, it does not fly to Mexico. It finds a rock crevice, drops its body temperature to near-freezing, and sleeps. The sleeping one. The only bird in the world that dreams the long dream of winter.*
