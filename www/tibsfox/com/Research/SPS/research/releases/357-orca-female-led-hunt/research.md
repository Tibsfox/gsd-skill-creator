## THE SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALE -- THE POPULATION

### J, K, and L Pods. The Puget Sound Orca. ESA Endangered.

The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) are the resident killer whale population of the Salish Sea: the inland waters of Washington State (Puget Sound) and British Columbia (Strait of Georgia), along with the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the adjacent outer coast. The population consists of three pods -- J, K, and L pods -- whose social structure, vocal behavior, and movements have been documented continuously since the early 1970s, making them one of the most intensively studied cetacean populations in the world.

The SRKW were listed as endangered under the Endangered Species Act in 2005. The primary threats are prey availability (Chinook salmon decline), vessel disturbance, and contaminants. At the time of ESA listing, the population numbered approximately 90 individuals; current numbers vary and have declined in recent years.

The population is "resident" in the sense of being fish-eating (as opposed to "transient" mammal-eating killer whales, which share the same waters but have a different social structure, different vocal repertoire, and different ecology). The SRKW specialize almost exclusively on Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*), even when other salmon species are more abundant. This prey specialization is a cultural behavior -- not a genetic constraint -- that has been transmitted through generations and maintained by the matriarchal social structure.

## THE MATRIARCHAL SOCIAL STRUCTURE -- FEMALE EXPERTISE AT THE CENTER

### Post-Reproductive Female as Knowledge Center

The Southern Resident killer whale social structure is matriarchal: pods are organized around elder females, and the relationship between a mother and her offspring -- both male and female -- is the primary social bond. Males leave their natal pod temporarily for mating but return; the social group is defined by the matriline.

The matriarch's role is not primarily reproductive. Among killer whales, females undergo menopause and continue to live and actively participate in pod activities for decades after their reproductive period ends. In most mammalian species, post-reproductive females have no obvious fitness function and typically do not survive long past reproductive capacity. In killer whales, post-reproductive females survive for decades and serve a specific function: they are the pod's knowledge repository.

Research published in 2015 (Resident Killer Whale Matriarchs, Brent et al., Current Biology) documented a correlation between the survival of salmon-eating killer whale pods and the presence of post-reproductive females. Pods with older post-reproductive matriarchs showed higher survival rates during years of low salmon abundance. The interpretation: the elder females carry knowledge of salmon location -- the rivers where salmon run, the timing of the runs, the depth at which salmon congregate in particular water column conditions -- accumulated over their decades of foraging. In poor salmon years, this knowledge is survival-critical.

The acoustic implications: the matriarch's vocalizations during foraging serve a specific function in organizing the hunt. Research on SRKW foraging acoustics has documented increased call rates and specific call types during active foraging; the coordinated calls of a pod hunting together are different from the calls produced in social contexts. The matriarch's calls are not simply communication -- they are operational coordination of the hunt.

## THE COOPERATIVE HUNT -- THE ACOUSTIC ARCHITECTURE

### Coordinated Calls. The Hunt as Vocal Event.

The Southern Resident killer whale cooperative hunt is a coordinated acoustic event. The hunt sequence:

**Detection.** Orca detect salmon through echolocation -- the production of clicks (high-frequency, up to 150 kHz) that return from the salmon's gas-filled swim bladder, allowing the whale to locate prey in low-visibility water. Foraging echolocation is directional and functional: the clicks are not communication signals but active sonar. The detection range for a single large Chinook salmon is estimated at 100-200 meters in typical Salish Sea visibility conditions.

**Coordination.** Once prey is located, pod members coordinate through discrete calls -- the SRKW repertoire of species-specific vocalizations that are pod-specific in their fine structure (each pod has its own dialect, distinguishable from other pods). Call rates increase during active foraging: the pod is communicating the location and movement of prey, the positions of pod members, and the coordination of approach vectors.

**The approach.** Coordinated approach involves pod members positioning themselves around salmon -- above, below, from multiple lateral directions -- to prevent escape. The acoustic signature during the approach includes overlapping echolocation trains from multiple whales simultaneously, along with the increasing call rate of discrete vocalizations. The acoustic event during a multi-whale, coordinated approach to a salmon school is the most acoustically complex event the SRKW generate during foraging.

**The capture.** Individual Chinook salmon are captured individually rather than in group herding behavior. The cooperative element is the positioning and approach, not the final capture. The acoustic signature at capture is a brief cessation of the sustained echolocation as the whale's jaw contacts the prey.

**Post-capture.** Food sharing has been documented among SRKW: fish pieces shared between pod members, including males sharing with mothers. The post-capture acoustic environment returns to the lower call rates of satiated foraging.

## THE ACOUSTIC SIGNATURE -- WHAT THE HUNT SOUNDS LIKE

### Echolocation + Discrete Calls + Breathing at Maximum Foraging Rate

The acoustic event of a SRKW female-led hunt at peak Chinook season is distinguished from other SRKW acoustic events by several features:

**Echolocation density.** During active foraging, multiple pod members echolocating simultaneously creates a dense acoustic environment in the 10-150 kHz range. From underwater, the overlapping click trains of three to five simultaneously hunting whales create a broadband acoustic presence that is the sonar equivalent of multiple search beams sweeping the water column simultaneously.

**Discrete call rate and complexity.** The pod's discrete call repertoire is deployed at maximum rate during active foraging. The calls carry pod-specific information that allows pod members to maintain spatial awareness of each other while attending to echolocation returns from prey. The acoustic signature of maximum-rate discrete calls from a full pod is dense, layered, and specific.

**The matriarch's calls.** Research suggests that older females call more during foraging (as opposed to younger animals who may call less during the approach to prey). The matriarch's calls are the organizational axis of the hunt's acoustic architecture.

**Breathing rate.** During active foraging, breathing rate increases: blows at the surface become more frequent. The above-water acoustic signature of an active hunt includes the high-rate respiration sounds of multiple whales surfacing in rapid succession.

**E=10 calibration.** The female-led cooperative hunt at peak Chinook season is E=10 through the convergence principle: multiple acoustic systems (echolocation, discrete calls, breathing) operating simultaneously at maximum rate, organized by the matriarch's knowledge and vocalization. No single acoustic component is uniquely extreme; the convergence of all components in coordinated maximum-foraging activity is the E=10 event.

## THE CHINOOK SALMON CONTEXT -- THE PREY RELATIONSHIP

### Cultural Prey Specialization. The Fish That Defines the Population.

The SRKW's exclusive Chinook salmon prey specialization is one of the most studied aspects of their ecology and one of the primary drivers of their endangered status. Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) are the largest Pacific salmon species (typical adult weight 10-30 kg) and the SRKW's preferred prey -- not genetic preference but cultural preference, transmitted through generations of the matriarchal social structure.

The specialization is operationally defined: SRKW will pass through waters containing abundant pink, coho, or chum salmon without foraging, then travel significantly farther to find Chinook. The cultural behavior is maintained because the matriarchs' knowledge of Chinook locations -- the timing and geography of specific runs in specific years -- is the knowledge that keeps the population fed.

The acoustic consequence of the Chinook specialization: SRKW hunt Chinook in the way that produces the most complex cooperative hunting acoustic event. Chinook are large and individually powerful; the cooperative hunt requires the most coordination, the most echolocation, and the most coordinated calls. If the SRKW hunted smaller, schooling fish in herding behavior, the hunt would be acoustically different and acoustically less complex. The Chinook specialization produces the acoustic event described above.
