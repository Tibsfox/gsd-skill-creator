THE MATRIARCH’S KNOWLEDGE ORGANIZES THE HUNT

Southern Resident Killer Whale. Female-led cooperative hunt. The post-reproductive matriarch carries decades of accumulated knowledge about Chinook salmon location — the timing of runs, the depth preferences in specific water conditions. When Chinook are scarce, this knowledge is survival-critical. The hunt’s acoustic architecture: echolocation + coordinated discrete calls + synchronized breathing. Three acoustic systems at maximum. The Hunt Sequence — Five Acoustic Phases

Detection

Echolocation clicks to 150 kHz. Salmon swim bladder return. 100–200m range in Salish Sea visibility.

Coordination

Discrete calls maximum rate. Pod-specific dialect 1–15 kHz. Prey location + member positions communicated.

Approach

Multi-whale overlapping echolocation trains. Coordinated surround: above, below, lateral. Maximum acoustic complexity.

Capture

Individual Chinook. Echolocation cessation at jaw contact. The silence-in-the-hunt as acoustic event.

Post-Capture

Food sharing. Males with mothers documented. Social acoustic context. Satiated call rate reduction.

The Matriarch — Post-Reproductive Knowledge Center

In most mammalian species, post-reproductive survival has no obvious fitness function. In killer whales, it does. Elder females undergo menopause but survive for decades, carrying the pod’s accumulated knowledge of salmon locations: the rivers where Chinook run, the timing of runs, the depth at which salmon congregate in specific water-column conditions. During low-Chinook years, this knowledge is survival-critical.

Research published in 2015 (Brent et al., Current Biology) documented a correlation between pod survival rates and the presence of older post-reproductive matriarchs. Pods with elder matriarchs showed higher survival during scarce salmon years. The matriarch is the pod’s knowledge center, not merely its social anchor.

“The Chinook prey specialization is cultural behavior, not genetic constraint — transmitted through the matriarchal social structure across generations. The loss of elder matriarchs degrades the pod’s knowledge base.”

Acoustic Profile

10–150 kHz Echolocation clicks. Multiple whales simultaneously during approach. Overlapping click trains = broadband active sonar field.
1–15 kHz Discrete calls, pod-specific dialect. Maximum rate during active foraging. Propagate through fjord waveguide far from animals.
Above-water Increased breathing rate. High-frequency blow sounds. Multiple whales surfacing in rapid succession. Audible from boat or shore.
Three systems Echolocation + discrete calls + breathing converging at simultaneous maximum. Matriarch vocalization as organizational axis. E=10.
