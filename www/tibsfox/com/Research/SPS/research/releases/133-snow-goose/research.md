## E=5 -- THE OVERWHELMING FLOCK

### Why E=5

The Snow Goose registers at E=5 -- breaking the E=4 plateau (130-132) with a significant energy increase:

- **Flock scale:** This is the defining factor. Snow Geese arrive in the Skagit Valley in flocks of **tens of thousands** -- sometimes exceeding 30,000 to 50,000 birds in a single concentration. No other PNW goose approaches this flock scale. The Canada Goose (129, E=5) is ubiquitous but distributed; the Snow Goose is concentrated into massive spectacles.
- **Shrill, dissonant quality:** The individual Snow Goose call is a high-pitched, nasal, one-syllable bark -- "honk" or "whouk" -- that lacks the musical quality of the Greater White-fronted Goose (132) or the baying quality of the Tundra Swan (127). The call is shrill and somewhat harsh. But this individual harshness is what creates the flock effect: thousands of shrill calls overlapping into a continuous **wall of dissonant sound** that is physically overwhelming at close range.
- **Wall of sound:** A flock of 10,000+ Snow Geese in flight or lifting off from a field produces an acoustic experience unlike anything else in the PNW. The sound is a continuous, roaring, shrill cacophony -- not musical, not melodious, but **overwhelming in its intensity**. The volume can be heard from a mile or more away. This wall-of-sound quality drives E=5.
- **E=5 matches the spectacular scale:** E=5 matches the Canada Goose (129) and the Tundra Swan (127) -- both flock-scale acoustic events. The Snow Goose flock is arguably the most acoustically intense of the three, but E=5 (not E=6) is appropriate because the individual call lacks the resonant depth of the Trumpeter Swan's E=6 bugling. The Snow Goose's E=5 is achieved through sheer numbers, not individual vocal power.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; WDFW; engine internal analysis)

## THE SHRILL DISSONANT FLOCK CALL -- ANATOMY AND ACOUSTICS

### A Wall of Sound Built from Simple Calls

The Snow Goose's vocalization is acoustically simple but collectively overwhelming:

- **Character:** A high-pitched, nasal, one-syllable bark -- "honk" or "whouk" or a shrill, yelping "awk." The call is distinctly higher-pitched and shriller than the deep honk of the Canada Goose (129) and lacks the laughing quality of the Greater White-fronted Goose (132). Individual calls are somewhat harsh and unmusical.
- **Frequency range:** 600-4,000 Hz. The highest-pitched goose call in the engine. The shrill quality comes from energy concentrated in the 1,500-3,500 Hz range -- a frequency band that the human ear perceives as particularly piercing.
- **Production mechanism:** The syrinx produces a relatively simple waveform -- less complex than the Greater White-fronted Goose's laughing modulation but delivered with force. The nasal quality comes from resonance in the upper airway.
- **Flock amplification:** The acoustic phenomenon that defines the Snow Goose is **flock amplification on an extraordinary scale**. When 10,000 to 50,000 birds vocalize simultaneously, the overlapping shrill calls create a continuous, roaring, dissonant wall of sound. The dissonance comes from the slight pitch variations between individuals -- no two birds call at exactly the same frequency, so the massive overlap produces a broadband, shrill noise field rather than a single coherent tone.
- **Liftoff sound:** The most intense acoustic moment is the **mass liftoff** -- when a disturbance causes thousands of Snow Geese to take flight simultaneously. The combined wing noise (tens of thousands of wingbeats) plus the collective shrieking call produces a roar that can be heard for miles. Observers regularly describe this as one of the most overwhelming wildlife sounds they've ever experienced.
- **Flight chorus:** In flight, Snow Goose flocks produce a continuous chattering, yelping chorus. The sound moves across the landscape as the flock moves -- a traveling wall of sound.

## THE SKAGIT VALLEY SPECTACLE -- WHITE BLIZZARDS

### One of the PNW's Great Wildlife Events

The Snow Goose flock in the Skagit Valley is one of the most visually and acoustically spectacular wildlife events in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Timing:** Snow Geese arrive in the Skagit Valley in **October and November**, with peak numbers from **December through February**. They depart for Arctic breeding grounds in **March and April**. The winter presence lasts roughly five months.
- **Flock size:** The Skagit-Fraser population (shared between northwestern Washington and southwestern British Columbia) can number **50,000 to 80,000+ birds**. The Skagit Valley typically hosts **30,000 to 50,000** at peak, with additional birds in the Fraser River delta in BC.
- **Visual spectacle:** A field of 20,000+ Snow Geese is one of the PNW's most arresting wildlife sights. The white birds transform agricultural fields into **white blankets** visible from a great distance. When the flock lifts off -- a swirling, spiraling mass of white against the gray PNW winter sky -- the visual effect has been described as a "white blizzard" or "white tornado."
- **Fir Island:** The agricultural fields around **Fir Island** in the Skagit Valley are the primary concentration area. The flat, diked farmland provides waste grain, grass, and winter cover crops that the geese feed on. Birders and wildlife watchers from across the region come to Fir Island during winter to witness the Snow Goose flocks.
- **WDFW management:** The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife manages Snow Goose habitat in the Skagit Valley, including crop damage programs and hunting seasons that help control the population.
- **Tourism:** The Snow Goose spectacle draws significant wildlife tourism to the Skagit Valley. The annual **Skagit Eagle Festival** and winter birding events capitalize on the presence of Snow Geese, Trumpeter Swans, and Bald Eagles in the valley.

### The Acoustic Experience

The acoustic dimension of the Skagit Valley spectacle is as significant as the visual:

- **Background hum:** Even at rest, a flock of 20,000+ Snow Geese produces a continuous background hum of soft contact calls -- a low-level murmur that carries across the flat valley.
- **Alert escalation:** When a disturbance approaches (eagle, predator, vehicle), the flock's vocalization escalates rapidly from murmur to alarm. The shrill calls intensify, spreading through the flock in a wave.
- **Mass liftoff:** The acoustic climax. Tens of thousands of birds lifting off simultaneously -- the combined wing thunder plus the collective shrieking call. Observers within a quarter-mile describe the experience as physically immersive -- the sound surrounds and saturates.
- **Settling:** After the flock settles back down, the vocalization gradually decreases -- the reverse of the alert escalation. The transition from roar back to murmur takes several minutes as the birds calm.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; WDFW; Skagit Audubon Society; eBird data)

## THE BLUE MORPH -- GENETICS AND COLOR VARIATION

### White and Blue

The Snow Goose is notable for its dramatic **color polymorphism**:

- **White morph:** The "classic" Snow Goose -- entirely white plumage with black wingtips. Pink bill with black "grinning patch" (the dark line along the bill's cutting edge). Pink legs. This is the most common morph in the Pacific flyway population that winters in the Skagit Valley.
- **Blue morph:** A striking dark-bodied form -- dark gray to blue-gray body with white head and upper neck. The blue morph was once considered a separate species ("Blue Goose") until genetic studies confirmed it as a color morph of the same species. The blue morph is controlled by a **single gene** with incomplete dominance -- one of the most dramatic examples of simple Mendelian genetics in wild birds.
- **Genetic basis:** The dark (blue) allele is partially dominant over the white allele. Heterozygous birds can show intermediate plumage. The frequency of the blue morph varies geographically -- it is most common in eastern Arctic breeding populations (especially around Hudson Bay and Baffin Island) and rarer in western Arctic populations (which supply the Skagit Valley birds).
- **Blue morph in the Skagit:** Blue-morph Snow Geese are **uncommon but present** in Skagit Valley flocks. A scan of a large flock will typically reveal a few dozen blue-morph individuals among thousands of white birds. They are a birding prize -- finding a "Blue Goose" in the white mass is a satisfying field challenge.
- **Historical taxonomy:** The blue morph's treatment as a separate species ("Blue Goose," *Chen caerulescens*) until the mid-20th century is a classic case of taxonomic revision. The specific epithet *caerulescens* (Latin: "becoming blue") actually refers to the blue morph, not the white -- the species was named from the dark form first.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2000; Cooke & Rockwell 1988)

## CONSERVATION IRONY -- TOO MANY GEESE

### The Population Explosion

The Snow Goose presents one of the most ironic conservation stories in North American ornithology:

- **Historical decline:** Snow Goose populations were reduced by market hunting in the 19th and early 20th centuries. Conservation measures (the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, hunting regulations, protected areas) were implemented to save the species.
- **Population explosion:** The conservation measures worked -- too well. Snow Goose populations have **exploded** since the mid-20th century. The total North American population has grown from roughly 2 million in the 1970s to **15 million or more** today. The Mid-Continent population (which includes birds wintering in the central flyway) has grown most dramatically, but Pacific flyway populations have also increased significantly.
- **Agricultural subsidy:** The primary driver of the population explosion is **agricultural food subsidies**. Snow Geese have shifted from their historical reliance on natural tundra and marsh vegetation to feeding heavily on waste grain, winter wheat, and other crops in agricultural landscapes. This reliable, abundant food source has reduced winter mortality and increased body condition, leading to higher breeding success.
- **Arctic habitat destruction:** The conservation irony: the geese that were saved from overhunting are now **destroying their own breeding habitat**. On the Arctic tundra, the exploding Snow Goose populations are overgrazing vegetation, stripping salt marshes to bare mud. The damage is severe enough that ecologists describe "eat-outs" -- areas where Snow Goose foraging has destroyed the vegetation so thoroughly that the habitat cannot recover for decades. The exposed soil erodes, salinity increases, and the tundra is converted to barren mudflat.
- **Cascading effects:** The habitat destruction cascades through the Arctic ecosystem. Shorebirds that nest in the same tundra habitats lose nesting sites. Small mammals lose food sources. The ecological damage extends well beyond the geese themselves.
- **Management response:** In 1999, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service implemented the **Light Goose Conservation Order** -- essentially an extended hunting season with relaxed bag limits, designed to reduce Snow Goose populations. The measure explicitly acknowledges that the population has grown beyond the carrying capacity of its breeding habitat. Canada has implemented similar measures. Despite decades of liberalized hunting, the population remains at historically high levels.
- **The irony distilled:** Conservationists saved the Snow Goose from overhunting. Agricultural expansion then subsidized an population explosion. The exploding population is now destroying the Arctic habitat the conservation measures were designed to protect. A conservation success story that became an ecological crisis.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2000; Abraham, Jefferies & Alisauskas 2005; USFWS Light Goose Conservation Order)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the White Blizzard

The Snow Goose's acoustic identity is defined by **collective overwhelm**:

- **Individual call:** Shrill, nasal, one-syllable bark. "Honk" or "whouk" or yelping "awk." High-pitched (600-4,000 Hz). Somewhat harsh and unmusical as an individual sound. Lacks the musical complexity of the Greater White-fronted Goose (132) or the resonant depth of the Canada Goose (129).
- **Flock texture:** The collective sound of thousands of shrill individual calls overlapping to create a **continuous, broadband, dissonant roar**. The slight pitch variations between individuals produce constructive and destructive interference patterns that give the flock sound its characteristic shrill, cacophonous quality. Not a single tone but a wall of noise.
- **Mass liftoff:** The acoustic climax of the Snow Goose experience. Tens of thousands of wingbeats plus collective shrieking. Physically immersive at close range. One of the most overwhelming avian sounds in the PNW.
- **Acoustic footprint:** A Snow Goose flock of 20,000+ birds has an acoustic footprint of **a mile or more** -- audible from great distance across the flat Skagit Valley agricultural landscape. The sound announces the flock before you can see it.
- **Dissonance as character:** Where the Greater White-fronted Goose's flock creates musical laughter and the Canada Goose's flock creates rolling honks, the Snow Goose's flock creates **pure dissonance** -- a wall of shrill, conflicting frequencies. This dissonance is the acoustic character. The sound is not beautiful in a conventional sense; it is beautiful in its overwhelming intensity.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Mowbray et al. 2000; engine internal analysis)
