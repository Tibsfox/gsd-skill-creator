## *BUTEO JAMAICENSIS* -- THE SPECIES

### The Most Familiar Raptor in North America

The Red-tailed Hawk is the most widely distributed buteonine hawk in North America:

- **Range.** The Red-tailed Hawk breeds and winters across the full extent of North America, from central Alaska and Canada through Mexico and the Caribbean. In the Pacific Northwest specifically, it is the most common large hawk -- resident in open habitats, agricultural land, forest edges, and city parks year-round. It is not a migratory specialist but a resident generalist: the hawk that is present in the open valley every month of the year.
- **Size.** Adult body length: 45-65 cm (18-26 inches). Wingspan: 105-141 cm (41-55 inches). Weight: 690-1,460 g (approximately 1.5-3 lbs). Females are significantly larger than males in all buteonines -- the reverse sexual dimorphism that characterizes raptors. The female's larger size is relevant to acoustic output: larger body means larger trachea, larger syrinx (the avian vocal organ), and potentially louder calls.
- **Identification.** The Red-tailed Hawk's most distinctive field mark is the rufous (brick-red) tail of adult birds -- visible from below as a warm rufous-orange against the sky. In the PNW, the most common morph is the light morph (pale underparts, dark patagial marks on the leading edge of the wing). The dark Harlan's subspecies (*B.j. harlani*) is also present in the region.
- **The PNW context.** In the Willamette Valley, the Puget Sound lowlands, eastern Washington's Columbia Basin, and the valleys of the Cascades' western slope, Red-tailed Hawks are present year-round on elevated perches: fence posts, snags, utility poles, the upper branches of lone trees in open fields. They are the resident raptors of the agricultural landscape.

## THE TERRITORIAL SCREAM -- THE CALL ITSELF

### *Kee-eeeee-arr* -- The Most Recognized Raptor Vocalization

The Red-tailed Hawk's territorial scream is one of the most acoustically distinctive sounds in North American wildlife:

- **The call description.** A hoarse, descending scream: *kee-eeeee-arr*, typically rendered in field guides. The call starts at high frequency (approximately 2-4 kHz) with a sharp onset, broadens into a sustained mid-frequency tonal element, and descends at the end with a raspy, almost croaking quality. Duration of an individual call: 2-4 seconds. The call carries considerable distance -- 1+ km in still air over open terrain.
- **Frequency range.** Primary acoustic energy: 1-3 kHz. Harmonics to 6 kHz. The call is in the mid-frequency range that propagates well over open terrain: not the ultra-high-frequency squeaks of small songbirds, not the infrasonic low-end of large mammals, but the middle range that human hearing is most sensitive to. The call is designed (by evolution) to propagate across the open territories the hawk defends.
- **The syrinx.** The avian vocal organ is the syrinx, located at the junction of the trachea and bronchi, and it is structurally different from the mammalian larynx. The syrinx enables independent control of two acoustic channels (one per bronchus), allowing birds to produce two independent sounds simultaneously. The Red-tailed Hawk uses this primarily for single-channel territorial calls; the two-channel capability is more exploited by songbirds.
- **Individual variation.** Individual Red-tailed Hawks have identifiable call characteristics -- subtle differences in timing, frequency profile, and harmonic structure that experienced field observers can use to distinguish territories. The acoustic territory is defined by the call's propagation area: the hawk's call covers its home range acoustically, warning intruders and announcing presence to potential mates.

## THE SEQUENCE STRUCTURE -- NOT JUST A CALL, A TERRITORIAL COMMUNICATION

### What a "Sustained Territorial Scream Sequence" Means

The entry documents not a single call but a sustained sequence of territorial calls:

- **The sequence.** An adult Red-tailed Hawk defending territory will issue repeated calls over a period of minutes: the sequence format is typically one call every 3-8 seconds, sustained for 5-20 repetitions, with the bird stationary on a high perch or in soaring flight. The sequence is not random -- it is a structured behavioral communication directed toward a specific stimulus (intruder bird, rival hawk, approaching human or predator).
- **The directional element.** The hawk's call is directional -- it looks at the stimulus while calling. The sequence is often accompanied by postural displays: the hawk raises its wings slightly, holds its head low and forward, fluffs the feathers. The acoustic sequence and the visual display are coordinated behavioral messages.
- **Response to intruders.** When another Red-tailed Hawk enters the territory, the resident bird will typically (1) observe silently from a high perch for 30-60 seconds, (2) initiate a sustained call sequence (5-20 calls), and (3) if the intruder persists, escalate to an aerial display (soaring above the intruder, calling continuously from flight). The sequence structure documents the escalation logic of territorial defense.
- **The resident's acoustic map.** A resident Red-tailed Hawk's territorial calls are delivered from multiple perches across its home range (typically 2-10 km2 in the PNW). The sequence of calls from different perches over a day constitutes the hawk's acoustic mapping of its territory -- each call from a specific perch covers a specific angular sector of the territory. Together, the daily sequence maps the full home range.

## THE HOLLYWOOD CALL MISAPPROPRIATION

### Why This Call Appears in Every Movie with a Flying Eagle

The Red-tailed Hawk's scream is one of the most culturally misappropriated sounds in wildlife audio:

- **The misappropriation.** When Hollywood (and television, advertising, and other media) needs the sound of a majestic eagle or hawk in flight, they almost always use the Red-tailed Hawk's *kee-eeeee-arr* call, regardless of the species actually depicted. The most common victim: the Bald Eagle. The Bald Eagle's actual call is thin, weak, descending -- a high-pitched twittering that does not project cinematic majesty. The Red-tailed Hawk's call is loud, raspy, and descending -- exactly what audiences expect from a large raptor. So the hawk's call is dubbed over the eagle's image.
- **The prevalence.** The Hollywood Red-tailed Hawk call is one of the most-heard wildlife sounds in the world, specifically because it appears in virtually every film and television production that features a large bird of prey. Millions of people who have never seen a Red-tailed Hawk have heard its call, attributed to eagles, falcons, and hawks generically.
- **The acoustic reason.** The misappropriation is acoustically rational: the Red-tailed Hawk's call is the correct frequency and intensity for the application. Film soundtracks require sounds that carry through speaker systems with the energy registration of a large predator. The Bald Eagle's thin twittering fails this test; the Red-tailed Hawk's hoarse raspy scream passes it.
- **Engine significance.** The engine documents the call as it actually exists: a territorial communication in an open valley context. The Hollywood version is an acoustic fiction that has made this one of the most culturally familiar wildlife sounds in North America. Both are real phenomena -- the actual call and the misappropriated cultural symbol.

(Sources: Hollywood raptor call misappropriation; Bald Eagle actual call description; Red-tailed Hawk call as substitute; prevalence in film/TV; acoustic rationality of the substitution; cultural familiarity statistics)

## OPEN VALLEY HABITAT -- THE ACOUSTIC CONTEXT

### The Terrain This Call Is Designed For

The Red-tailed Hawk's call is specifically adapted for open terrain:

- **Open valley acoustics.** An open agricultural valley -- treeless or sparse-tree, with the valley floor visible from ridge perches on both sides -- provides optimal acoustic propagation for the Red-tailed Hawk's mid-frequency call. Sound propagation over flat terrain is approximately twice as efficient as propagation through forest: the call travels further, with less attenuation, and reaches competitors and mates across the full territorial area.
- **The perch economy.** The hawk's strategy of calling from elevated perches (fence posts, lone trees, utility poles) is acoustically efficient: height above the valley floor increases the call's propagation radius. A call issued from 10 meters above ground propagates further than the same call issued from ground level. The hawk's perch selection is an acoustic optimization.
- **PNW open valley examples.** The Willamette Valley (Oregon), the Puget Sound lowlands, the Skagit Valley, eastern Washington's Columbia Plateau and agricultural areas: all are prime Red-tailed Hawk acoustic territories. The hawk is the dominant raptor voice in the PNW's agricultural lowlands.
- **The degree-319 acoustic context.** The entry specifically documents the "sustained territorial scream sequence in open valley context" -- not a single call but the full behavioral sequence that constitutes the hawk's acoustic territory marking. The sequence is the complete communication event; the single call is the unit.

(Sources: Open valley sound propagation; forest vs. open terrain attenuation comparison; perch height acoustic optimization; PNW open valley habitats: Willamette, Puget Sound lowlands, Skagit Valley, Columbia Plateau; agricultural landscape; degree-319 context specificity)
