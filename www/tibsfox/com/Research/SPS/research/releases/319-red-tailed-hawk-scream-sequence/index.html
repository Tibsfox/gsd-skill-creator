The sequence is not random repetition — it is a structured territorial broadcast. The hawk directs the call at the stimulus (intruder bird, rival raptor, approaching human). Call rate increases with intruder persistence. Accompanied by postural display: wings slightly raised, head low and forward, feathers fluffed. Acoustic sequence + visual display = coordinated territorial message at E=7.

The Hollywood Misappropriation — The Call That Appears in Every Movie

The Red-tailed Hawk’s kee-eeeee-arr is heard in virtually every film and television production that features a large bird of prey, regardless of the species depicted. The most frequent victim: the Bald Eagle, which produces a thin, weak, high-pitched twittering call that fails the cinematic majesty test. So Hollywood dubs the Red-tailed Hawk scream over the eagle’s image.

The substitution is acoustically rational: the Red-tailed Hawk’s call is the correct frequency and intensity for speaker system propagation. But the result is a cultural fiction: millions of people who have never seen a Red-tailed Hawk know its call, attributed to eagles. The engine documents the actual call in its actual context — territorial sequence, open valley, directed at a specific stimulus. Not a cinematic symbol. A behavioral communication.

Open Valley Acoustic Territory

The Red-tailed Hawk’s call is specifically adapted for open terrain propagation. Sound propagates approximately twice as efficiently over flat open valley as through forest — the hawk’s mid-frequency call (1–3 kHz) reaches competitors and mates across its full home range (2–10 km²) from elevated perch positions. The perch itself is an acoustic optimization: calling from 10 meters above the valley floor doubles the effective propagation radius compared to ground level.

PNW open valley Red-tailed Hawk territories: Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, Skagit Valley, Columbia Plateau agricultural areas. The hawk is the dominant raptor voice in the PNW’s agricultural landscape — present on every fence post, every lone snag, every utility pole overlooking an open field.

The Species

Buteo jamaicensis, Gmelin, 1788. Most widely distributed buteonine hawk in North America. Body length: 45–65 cm. Wingspan: 105–141 cm. Weight: 690–1,460 g (females larger: reverse sexual dimorphism). Adult identification: rufous (brick-red) tail visible from below against the sky. Primary PNW morph: light morph (pale underparts, dark patagial wing marks). Year-round resident in PNW open habitats. Peak territorial calling: February–June (breeding season). Pair bonds often multi-year; territories maintained across consecutive years.
