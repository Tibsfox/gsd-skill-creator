## E=2 -- NEW SPS MINIMUM: THE QUIETEST ENTRY IN THE ENGINE

### Why E=2

The Flammulated Owl registers at E=2 -- a **new all-time minimum** for the SPS engine:

- **Near-inaudible hoot:** The Flammulated Owl's hoot is so soft that it is nearly impossible to hear beyond 30-50 meters. Field researchers have reported standing directly below a calling Flammulated Owl and being unable to hear it over ambient forest noise. The amplitude is at the very threshold of human perception in a quiet forest setting.
- **Low frequency deception:** The hoot is surprisingly low-pitched for such a small owl (approximately 300-450 Hz). The low frequency, combined with the low amplitude, creates a ventriloquial effect even more extreme than the Saw-whet Owl's -- the sound seems to come from nowhere and everywhere. Listeners often cannot determine the direction of the calling bird.
- **Single note simplicity:** Each hoot is a single, soft "boop" -- one note, no phrase structure, no complexity. The note is repeated at intervals of 2-3 seconds. The simplicity is extreme even by owl standards.
- **E=2 calibration:** E=2 reflects: minimal amplitude (barely audible at 30-50m), minimal complexity (single note repeated), no behavioral display, minimal carrying distance. This is the quietest vocalization documented in the SPS engine. No previous entry has been scored below E=3.
- **Below E=3:** The step from E=3 (Saw-whet, Screech-Owl, Pygmy-Owl) to E=2 is significant. E=3 entries are quiet but clearly audible at moderate distances. E=2 is at the boundary of perceptibility -- a sound you might not notice even when standing close to the source.

## THE DARK-EYED OWL -- UNIQUE AMONG SMALL OWLS

### Dark Eyes in a World of Yellow

The Flammulated Owl is the **only small owl in North America with dark eyes**:

- **Dark brown/black iris:** All other small North American owls (Screech-Owls, Saw-whet Owl, Pygmy-Owl, Elf Owl, Ferruginous Pygmy-Owl) have bright yellow eyes. The Flammulated Owl's dark eyes are immediately diagnostic -- if you see a small owl with dark eyes in North America, it is a Flammulated Owl.
- **Why dark eyes?** The prevailing hypothesis links the dark eyes to the Flammulated Owl's strictly insectivorous diet. Yellow-eyed owls are typically predators of vertebrates (mammals, birds) -- the bright eye may serve an aggressive/intimidation function. The Flammulated Owl, which eats only insects (moths, beetles, grasshoppers), may have relaxed the selection pressure for bright eyes, allowing the ancestral dark eye to persist.
- **Taxonomic signal:** The dark eyes, combined with other unique features (strictly insectivorous, migratory, small size), have led taxonomists to place the Flammulated Owl in its own monotypic genus (*Psiloscops*) -- indicating its evolutionary distinctiveness from other small New World owls.
- **Field identification:** In the ponderosa pine forests of the eastern Cascades, a small owl with dark eyes is unambiguously a Flammulated Owl. The dark eye is the single most reliable field mark.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; McCallum 1994; Sibley 2014)

## PONDEROSA PINE -- THE FLAMMULATED OWL'S WORLD

### East Side of the Cascades

The Flammulated Owl occupies a very specific habitat:

- **Ponderosa pine forests:** The Flammulated Owl is strongly associated with ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) forests, especially open, park-like stands with grassy understory. This is the classic "east-side" forest type in the PNW -- dry, warm, fire-adapted, with widely spaced large pines.
- **East of the Cascades:** In Washington, Flammulated Owls breed primarily east of the Cascades -- in the ponderosa pine zones of the Okanogan, Wenatchee, and Kittitas areas. They are not found in the wet, dense forests of western Washington. This makes the Flammulated Owl an "east-side" species in the engine -- the first owl documented from east-slope habitat.
- **Elevation:** Typically 600-2200 meters. The ponderosa pine zone is a transitional habitat between low-elevation shrub-steppe and high-elevation subalpine forest.
- **Summer resident:** Unlike all other owls in the Strigidae run, the Flammulated Owl is **migratory** -- it breeds in the PNW during summer (May-September) and winters in Mexico and Central America. This is the only migratory owl in the run, and its long-distance migration (thousands of kilometers) is remarkable for such a tiny bird.

### Strictly Insectivorous

The Flammulated Owl is the only owl in the Strigidae run that eats **exclusively insects**:

- **Diet:** Moths, beetles, grasshoppers, crickets, and other large arthropods. No vertebrate prey has been documented. This strict insectivory is unique among PNW owls and drives the species' migratory habit -- when insects disappear in autumn, the Flammulated Owl must leave.
- **Hunting method:** The owl hunts from perches in the ponderosa canopy, sallying out to capture flying insects (hawking) or plucking them from bark and foliage (gleaning). The tiny talons are adapted for insect capture rather than mammal killing.
- **Constraint:** The insect-only diet constrains the Flammulated Owl to habitats and seasons with abundant flying insects. This explains the species' absence from western Washington (too wet, wrong forest type) and its migratory habit (no insects in winter).

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; McCallum 1994; Sibley 2014; WDFW)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Almost Nothing -- The Quietest Voice in the Engine

The Flammulated Owl's acoustic contribution is **near-silence** -- the softest, most subtle vocalization in the SPS engine:

- **Hoot:** A single, soft, low-pitched "boop" or "poot." Approximately 300-450 Hz. Each note 0.2-0.3 seconds duration. Repeated at 2-3 second intervals. The sound has been described as resembling a distant, muffled heartbeat.
- **Amplitude:** The defining characteristic. The hoot is so soft that it is barely audible at 30-50 meters in quiet conditions. In any wind, ambient noise, or from greater distance, the call is inaudible. Field researchers use the Flammulated Owl's call as a test of listening conditions -- if you can hear it, the night is truly quiet.
- **Frequency:** 300-450 Hz. Remarkably low for such a tiny owl (only 54g). The frequency is similar to the Long-eared Owl's deep pumping but at a fraction of the amplitude. The low pitch creates extreme ventriloquial effects -- the sound is almost impossible to localize directionally.
- **Ventriloquial quality:** The combination of low frequency, low amplitude, and uniform repetition creates the most extreme ventriloquial effect in the engine. Listeners report the sound seeming to come from all directions simultaneously, or from a much greater distance than the bird's actual location. Researchers have spent hours trying to localize a calling bird that was 10 meters away.
- **Duration:** Males call for extended periods during the breeding season (May-July), typically beginning after full darkness. Calling may continue for 30 minutes or more in a continuous sequence.
