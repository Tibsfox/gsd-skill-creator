Three Layers — One Soundscape — E=7 Through Superposition

The spring night woodland pond: Pacific Chorus Frog breeding chorus (dense, mid-register), Great Horned Owl territorial call (spaced, low-mid), Western Field Cricket (continuous, high register). Three species, three acoustic channels, all active simultaneously. No single layer reaches E=7 alone — the three together do. The first multi-species simultaneous night soundscape entry in the post-summit sequence.

Pacific Chorus Frog — The Dominant Layer

Pseudacris regilla (Baird and Girard, 1852), family Hylidae. The most abundant and widespread frog in the Pacific Northwest — found at sea level to 10,000 feet elevation, from British Columbia to Baja California. Body length 1.9–5 cm; weight in voice of the spring pond that far exceeds its size.

The breeding chorus at a productive pond (50–500 simultaneous males) is acoustically more complex than the sum of individual calls. The overlapping, staggered calls produce a dense interference pattern — a rich texture that sounds like rain on metal rather than individual voices. This is the dominant layer: continuous, omnidirectional, filling the mid register. The Pacific Chorus Frog is why Hollywood uses “ribbit” as the default frog sound — these recordings provided the generic frog sound effect in American film.

Great Horned Owl — The Punctuation

Bubo virginianus. Already documented in the engine at degree 299 (full territorial sequence, E=7). At degree 321, one or two owls in the woodland adjacent to the pond, calling their five-note sequence at 30–60 second intervals. The owl’s call frequency (300–500 Hz) sits below the bulk of the frog chorus (500–3,000 Hz) — frequency separation is what makes the owl audible within the chorus rather than masked by it. The two species broadcast on different channels.

The owl is not competing with the frogs acoustically; it is occupying a different register. The spatial separation adds another dimension: the frogs call from within the pond; the owl calls from the woodland 20–50 meters away, elevated 10–30 feet on a perch. The soundscape is both spectrally distributed and spatially distributed.

Cricket — The High Carrier

The Western Field Cricket’s stridulation (wing-rubbing, 4,000–8,000 Hz) occupies the high register above both the frog chorus and the owl. It is also the most continuous layer — multiple crickets in ground-level vegetation producing an unbroken high-frequency background. Dolbear’s Law: the chirp rate approximates temperature — at 60°F, approximately 20 chirps per 14 seconds; at 70°F, approximately 30. The cricket layer is simultaneously the acoustic thermometer and the frequency ceiling of the degree-321 stack.
