## THE THREE-LAYER STRUCTURE

### A Vertical Acoustic Stack

The spring night woodland pond soundscape at E=7 is built from three layers that occupy different acoustic registers simultaneously:

- **Layer 1 (low-mid): Pacific Chorus Frog (*Pseudacris regilla*).** The dominant layer in density and volume. The Pacific Chorus Frog is the most abundant and widespread frog in the Pacific Northwest -- its breeding chorus at a pond with multiple individuals is one of the defining acoustic events of the PNW spring. The call is a rapid, ascending "kreek-EK" or "ribbit" -- technically a two-note call that the animal repeats in near-continuous sequences during breeding activity. At an active breeding pond, dozens to hundreds of frogs call simultaneously, producing a dense, layered chorus that fills the low-to-mid frequency range (500-3,000 Hz primary) with a dense, stuttering, rhythmically complex texture. This is the engine's most abundant-species entry in the Hylidae family.
- **Layer 2 (low mid, spaced): Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*).** The punctuation layer. The Great Horned Owl's territorial call at the degree-299 entry (full territorial sequence, E=7) has already been documented in the engine. At degree 321, the owl is part of the soundscape as a spatial element: one or two owls in the woodland adjacent to the pond, calling their deep five-note sequence (hoo-h-hoo-hoo-hoo) at intervals of 30-60 seconds. The owl's low frequency (300-500 Hz) cuts through the frog chorus as a distinct event -- audible against the continuous frog layer because its spacing and low-frequency register make it immediately recognizable as a different source. The owl is hunting, territorial, and acoustically unmistakable.
- **Layer 3 (high): Western Field Cricket (*Gryllus integer*) or Pacific Field Cricket.** The continuous high-frequency carrier. Crickets chirping in spring begin as temperatures warm above 55°F (13°C). The cricket's call is a rhythmic, high-frequency stridulation (4,000-8,000 Hz) produced by wing-rubbing -- the chirp rate increases with temperature (Dolbear's Law: chirp rate as a thermometer). At a woodland pond on a warm spring night, crickets add the high-frequency continuous layer to the frog chorus and owl punctuation, completing the three-register vertical stack.

## PACIFIC CHORUS FROG (*Pseudacris regilla*) -- THE DOMINANT LAYER

### The Engine's Most Abundant Amphibian

The Pacific Chorus Frog is the most acoustically dominant amphibian in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Taxonomy and range.** *Pseudacris regilla* (Baird and Girard, 1852), family Hylidae (tree frogs). Range: Pacific coast from British Columbia through Baja California, eastward to Montana. The most common and widespread frog in the Pacific Northwest -- found at sea level and up to 10,000 feet elevation. The species occupies almost any still or slow-moving water: ponds, marshes, roadside ditches, flooded fields.
- **Size and appearance.** Small: 1.9-5 cm body length. Highly variable in color -- can be brown, green, tan, gray, or reddish-brown, often with a dark eye stripe from snout to shoulder (the diagnostic field mark). The animal's small size belies its acoustic output: a single male produces a call that carries 100+ meters; a chorus of hundreds is audible from a significant distance.
- **Breeding biology.** Breeding occurs from January to July in the Pacific Northwest, with peak activity in February-May depending on elevation and temperature. Males congregate at shallow breeding ponds and call to attract females. The calling is competitive: males space themselves acoustically, adjusting call rate in response to neighboring males (acoustic interference behavior). The result is a dense, interactive, non-random chorus where individual calls are embedded in a collective sound event.
- **The call mechanics.** The Pacific Chorus Frog's call is produced by forcing air from the lungs over the vocal cords and into a single expandable vocal sac under the chin. The inflated sac resonates and amplifies the sound. At maximum calling rate during peak breeding activity, the animal calls at intervals of 0.5-1.5 seconds continuously for hours. The call is frequency-modulated: the ascending two-note structure creates the characteristic "ribbit" that is broadly recognized as the generic frog call in American culture (largely because Pacific Chorus Frog recordings were used in Hollywood films as the standard "frog sound effect").
- **Chorus density as acoustic phenomenon.** At a productive breeding pond in the Pacific Northwest in April, 50-500 individuals may be calling simultaneously. The combined output is not simply the sum of individual calls -- the overlapping, staggered calls produce a complex interference pattern, a rich acoustic texture that sounds more like rain on a metal roof than individual voices. This is the Layer 1 contribution to the degree-321 soundscape.

(Sources: Pseudacris regilla taxonomy Baird and Girard 1852; Hylidae; range Pacific coast to Montana; body length 1.9-5 cm; color variation; diagnostic eye stripe; breeding January-July PNW; peak February-May; calling behavior competitive spacing; acoustic interference; vocal sac inflation; call rate 0.5-1.5 seconds; chorus density 50-500 individuals; acoustic interference pattern; Hollywood ribbit frog sound effect attribution)

## GREAT HORNED OWL (*Bubo virginianus*) -- THE PUNCTUATION LAYER

### Already in the Engine, Now in the Chorus

The Great Horned Owl was documented at SPS degree 299 (full territorial sequence, E=7). At degree 321, the owl occupies the woodland edge adjacent to the breeding pond:

- **The hunting context at night.** The Great Horned Owl is the apex avian predator at the woodland pond edge. While the Pacific Chorus Frog chorus fills the pond zone, the owl patrols the woodland edge, listening for rustling in the vegetation that would indicate small mammal prey. The frogs themselves are potential prey -- Great Horned Owls are opportunistic and will hunt frogs at pond edges, though their primary prey at these woodland sites is small mammals (meadow voles, shrews, rabbits).
- **Calling behavior at the woodland-pond interface.** The owl calling adjacent to a breeding frog pond is engaged in territorial declaration, not necessarily hunting the frogs. The owl's five-note hoot (the characteristic pattern: one long, two-note phrase, then a two-note phrase) is directed at other owls, establishing the territorial boundary that includes the pond edge. The owl's presence at the pond edge is year-round; the spring timing aligns the owl's territorial calling with the frog chorus in a spatial coincidence that is biologically independent but acoustically unified.
- **Frequency contrast.** The Great Horned Owl's call (300-500 Hz primary) sits just below the bulk of the frog chorus (500-3,000 Hz). This frequency separation is what makes the owl audible within the chorus rather than masked by it. The two species broadcast on different channels -- the frog chorus fills the mid range; the owl's call occupies the low-mid register. The result is two acoustic layers that coexist without masking each other.
- **Spatial separation.** The owl calls from the woodland -- from perches 10-30 feet up in conifer or deciduous trees 20-50 meters from the pond edge. The frogs call from within the pond or from pond-edge vegetation. This spatial separation creates a directional acoustic field: the frog chorus emanates from in front (from the water), the owl calls arrive from behind or to the side (from the trees). A listener at the pond edge experiences the three-layer soundscape as spatially distributed.

## WESTERN FIELD CRICKET (*Gryllus integer*) -- THE HIGH CARRIER

### Dolbear's Law and the Spring Thermometer

The field cricket's contribution to the spring night soundscape is the continuous high-frequency carrier layer:

- **Species context.** *Gryllus integer* (Pacific Field Cricket) and related field cricket species are present throughout the Pacific Northwest in open and edge habitats. Field crickets are ground-dwelling insects that stridulate (produce sound by rubbing their wings together) to attract mates and establish male territories.
- **Stridulation mechanics.** The cricket's sound-producing mechanism is a file-and-scraper system on the forewings: the file (a row of teeth on one wing) is rubbed across the scraper (a hardened ridge on the other wing) to produce the chirp sound. The frequency produced is determined by the wing structure -- typically 4,000-8,000 Hz for field crickets, well above the frog chorus (500-3,000 Hz) and the owl call (300-500 Hz). This frequency separation is what allows all three acoustic layers to be simultaneously audible without mutual masking.
- **Dolbear's Law.** The chirp rate of the field cricket is temperature-dependent in a remarkably precise way. Dolbear's Law (formalized by Amos Dolbear in 1897) states that the number of chirps per 14 seconds plus 40 approximates the temperature in Fahrenheit. At 60°F, the cricket chirps approximately 20 times per 14 seconds; at 70°F, approximately 30 times per 14 seconds. The spring night context at degree 321 places temperatures in the 55-65°F range (warm enough for sustained frog breeding activity), producing cricket chirp rates of 15-25 per 14 seconds -- the characteristic medium-tempo spring-night cricket rhythm.
- **Continuous vs. intermittent.** Unlike the frog chorus (which has natural pauses as individual animals cycle in and out of calling) and the owl (which calls at 30-60 second intervals), the cricket layer is effectively continuous. Multiple crickets in a woodland-edge habitat produce a continuous high-frequency background that shifts only with temperature changes. The cricket layer is the acoustic floor of the high register, constant beneath the more variable frog and owl activity.
- **Spatial context.** Field crickets stridulate from ground level -- in leaf litter, at the base of vegetation, under logs and rocks at the pond edge. The sound radiates omnidirectionally from near-ground level. Combined with the frog chorus (at water level and pond vegetation) and the owl (from elevated woodland perches), the cricket layer completes the vertical dimension of the acoustic stack: ground level to canopy level, low register to high register, all three occupied simultaneously.

(Sources: Gryllus integer Pacific Field Cricket; stridulation mechanics file-scraper wing system; frequency 4000-8000 Hz; Dolbear's Law 1897; chirp rate temperature formula; spring temperature 55-65°F; chirp rate 15-25 per 14 seconds; continuous vs. intermittent acoustic layers; spatial ground-level radiation; vertical acoustic stack ground to canopy)

## THE WOODLAND POND ECOSYSTEM

### Where This Soundscape Lives

The degree-321 soundscape occurs at a specific ecosystem type in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Woodland pond definition.** A shallow to medium-depth freshwater pond surrounded by mixed conifer-deciduous woodland -- typically Douglas-fir, Western Red Cedar, or Sitka Spruce overstory with Red Alder, Big-Leaf Maple, or willow understory. The pond is temporary or permanent; Pacific Chorus Frogs use both types. Temporary ponds (vernal pools) are the most productive breeding sites because they lack fish (fish prey heavily on frog eggs and tadpoles).
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** This ecosystem type is common across the Puget Sound lowlands, the Willamette Valley, and the western Cascades foothills. Specific notable woodland pond locations include: Ridgefield National Wildlife Refuge (southern Washington), Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge (south Puget Sound), Woodard Bay Natural Resources Conservation Area (Olympia), and numerous unnamed woodland ponds throughout the lowland forest matrix.
- **Spring phenology timeline.** The degree-321 soundscape assembles in sequence: Pacific Chorus Frogs begin calling when water temperatures exceed 50°F (typically February-March in lowland sites); Great Horned Owls are in full territorial calling by February-March (nesting begins early); crickets become active when air temperatures consistently exceed 55°F at night (April-May in most Pacific Northwest lowland sites). The full three-layer stack is most complete in April-May, when all three species are simultaneously active during the warm spring nights.
- **The edge habitat.** Woodland ponds in the Pacific Northwest typically occur at the edge between wetland and upland -- the transition zone where terrestrial and aquatic habitats meet. This edge character is important for the acoustic ecology: the pond is the frog territory; the woodland edge is the owl territory; the ground-level edge vegetation is the cricket territory. The degree-321 soundscape is intrinsically an edge habitat event.

## THE ACOUSTIC ECOLOGY -- E=7 MULTI-LAYER

### Why Three Layers Together Reach E=7

The degree-321 soundscape calibrates to E=7 through the simultaneous combination of three active acoustic sources:

- **Layer summation effect.** No single element in the degree-321 soundscape is E=7 alone. The Pacific Chorus Frog breeding chorus at a productive pond is E=6-7 in its own right (dense, rhythmically complex, high density). The Great Horned Owl territorial call is E=6 as individual (documented at E=7 in degree 299 as a full sequence; the single owl at degree 321 is one component of a larger soundscape). The Western Field Cricket is E=4-5 individually. The simultaneous layering of all three -- occupying three distinct frequency registers, three distinct spatial zones, three distinct temporal patterns (continuous chorus, spaced hoots, continuous stridulation) -- produces an E=7 combined event through acoustic superposition.
- **Frequency register separation.** The key to E=7 through layering is that the three sources do not compete for the same acoustic space. Layer 1 (chorus frog, 500-3,000 Hz) fills the mid register. Layer 2 (owl, 300-500 Hz) occupies the low-mid. Layer 3 (cricket, 4,000-8,000 Hz) covers the high register. Total frequency coverage: approximately 300 Hz to 8,000 Hz -- roughly the full range of human hearing's most sensitive zone. The combination is acoustically complete in a way that no single species can achieve.
- **Temporal pattern complementarity.** The three layers have different temporal structures that complement each other. The frog chorus has rhythmic density with natural variation (calls overlap and gap in complex patterns). The owl has periodic regularity (call every 30-60 seconds). The cricket is continuous (no gap). The combination of dense-rhythmic + periodic + continuous temporal patterns produces a soundscape that has both texture (the frog chorus) and punctuation (the owl) and carrier (the crickets) -- all three structural roles filled simultaneously.
- **The spring night quality.** The spring night context adds a specific phenomenological quality to the soundscape. Temperature is warm enough for full biological activity (55-65°F) but the night is dark and still. The acoustic ecology is undisturbed by daytime species -- no diurnal birds, no motor noise (in the wilderness pond context). The night accentuates the three layers by reducing acoustic competition.
