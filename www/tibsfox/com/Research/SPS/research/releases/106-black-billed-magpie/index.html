Seventh Consecutive Corvidae — The Corvidae Septet

Seven consecutive Corvidae: Raven (100), Raven pair (101), American Crow (102), Steller’s Jay (103), Gray Jay (104), Clark’s Nutcracker (105), Black-billed Magpie (106). The corvid sextet becomes a septet. Breaks the Mustelidae/Sciuridae sextet ties. Now the second-longest family streak in the entire engine, behind only the Sciuridae octet (75–82). Seven degrees, one family — seven acoustic characters: deep cronk, duet, sharp caw, raucous alarm, soft whistles, harsh grating, and now rolling mek-mek-mek.

Bold Black-and-White — The Long-Tailed Corvid

The Black-billed Magpie is one of the most visually striking birds in the PNW. Its plumage is bold black-and-white — iridescent black head, breast, and back; white belly and scapulars; white wing patches visible in flight. The most distinctive feature is the extraordinarily long tail — longer than the body itself, iridescent green-bronze, and streaming behind the bird in flight. The long tail gives the magpie a silhouette unlike any other corvid in the engine. Among PNW birds, the magpie is unmistakable.

Dome-Shaped Nests — Unique Among Corvids

The Black-billed Magpie builds dome-shaped nests — massive, enclosed structures of sticks and mud with a roof. These nests are unique among corvids and among the largest nest structures of any North American songbird. A magpie nest can be 60–120 cm in diameter and take 40–50 days to build. The dome provides protection from predators and weather. Old magpie nests are reused by other species (owls, hawks, ducks). The nest is architecture — built to last, built to shelter.

Rolling Mek-Mek-Mek + Chatter

The Black-billed Magpie’s primary vocalization is a rolling, nasal mek-mek-mek — a rapid series of harsh, staccato notes that carry across open country. The call is conversational and social — magpies are among the most vocal corvids, chattering frequently within family groups and social flocks.

E=5 — high energy. The magpie’s calls are loud, frequent, and persistent. In open country, the rolling mek-mek-mek carries far. The chatter is rapid-fire — multiple birds calling simultaneously creates a wall of corvid conversation. The acoustic character is distinct from every other corvid in the septet — neither deep (raven) nor sharp (crow) nor raucous (Steller’s Jay) nor soft (Gray Jay) nor grating (nutcracker), but rolling and percussive.

Open Country Habitat

The Black-billed Magpie is a bird of open country — agricultural fields, sagebrush steppe, riparian corridors, ranch lands, and suburban edges. In the PNW, the magpie is found primarily east of the Cascades, in the drier open landscapes of eastern Washington and Oregon. It is uncommon west of the Cascades. The open-country habitat is the broadest-scale landscape in the engine’s corvid septet — from urban (crow) to dense forest (Steller’s Jay, Gray Jay) to alpine (nutcracker) to open steppe (magpie).
