## BLACK-BILLED MAGPIE -- *Pica hudsonia* -- THE OPEN-COUNTRY CORVID

### The Species

The Black-billed Magpie (*Pica hudsonia*) is a large, long-tailed corvid of open country in western North America [1, 2]:

- **Body length:** 45-60 cm (including tail; tail alone = 25-30 cm).
- **Wingspan:** 56-61 cm.
- **Weight:** 145-210 g.
- **Plumage:** Bold black-and-white. Iridescent black head, breast, back, and wings with green-bronze-purple sheen. White belly and scapulars. White wing patches visible in flight. The **extraordinarily long tail** -- longer than the body -- is iridescent green-bronze and streams behind the bird in flight.
- **Distribution:** Western North America from Alaska to the southwestern United States. In the PNW, found primarily east of the Cascades in open country habitats.

### Bold Black-and-White -- The Long-Tailed Corvid

The Black-billed Magpie is one of the most **visually striking** birds in North America [1, 2]:

- **The long tail** is the signature feature -- longer than the body itself, iridescent green-bronze, giving the bird an unmistakable silhouette in flight and at rest.
- **The black-and-white plumage** is bold and graphic -- a stark contrast pattern unlike any other corvid in the septet.
- **The iridescence** -- viewed in good light, the black plumage shimmers with green, bronze, and purple. The iridescence is produced by the microstructure of the feathers, not by pigment.
- **Unmistakable** -- no other PNW bird combines the long tail, bold black-and-white, and iridescent sheen. Field identification is instantaneous.

## THE SOUND: ROLLING MEK-MEK-MEK + CHATTER -- THE OPEN-COUNTRY CORVID

### The Vocal Repertoire

The Black-billed Magpie's primary vocalization is a **rolling, nasal mek-mek-mek** [1, 2]:

**Mek-mek-mek:** The diagnostic call. A rapid series of harsh, nasal, staccato notes -- mek mek mek mek -- delivered at a rolling pace. Conversational and social. Given frequently within family groups and social flocks. The call carries across open country.

**Chatter:** Extended, rapid-fire vocal exchanges between multiple birds. When a flock of magpies is agitated or excited, the chatter creates a **wall of corvid conversation** -- overlapping mek calls, harsh notes, and rapid vocalizations.

**Alarm:** Harsher, more emphatic versions of the mek call. Given when predators are detected. The alarm mobilizes the entire social group.

**Quiet calls:** Softer, more varied vocalizations used for close-range communication between pair members. Rarely heard by observers.

## DOME-SHAPED NESTS -- UNIQUE AMONG CORVIDS

### The Architecture

The Black-billed Magpie builds **dome-shaped nests** -- one of the most remarkable nest structures in North American ornithology [1, 2]:

- **Structure:** Massive, enclosed structures of sticks and mud with a **roof** (dome) of interwoven sticks. The nest has a side entrance. The interior is lined with mud, then with softer materials (grass, rootlets, animal hair).
- **Size:** 60-120 cm in diameter. Among the largest nest structures of any North American songbird.
- **Construction time:** 40-50 days to build. Both members of the pair contribute.
- **Function:** The dome provides protection from predators (particularly raptors) and weather (rain, snow, wind). The enclosed structure is far more protective than the open-cup nests of other corvids.
- **Reuse:** Old magpie nests are frequently reused by other species -- owls, hawks, ducks, and other birds that cannot build dome structures themselves. The magpie's nest is **community infrastructure**.

The magpie's dome nest is unique in the septet and unique among corvids worldwide. Only the magpie builds an enclosed dome. The nest is architecture -- built to last, built to shelter, built for reuse.

## OPEN COUNTRY HABITAT

The Black-billed Magpie occupies **open country** -- the broadest-scale landscape in the corvid septet [1]:

- **Habitat:** Sagebrush steppe, agricultural fields, riparian corridors with scattered trees, ranch lands, rural suburbs. Requires open ground for foraging and scattered trees or tall shrubs for nesting.
- **Geographic distribution in PNW:** Primarily **east of the Cascades** -- in the drier, more open landscapes of eastern Washington and Oregon. Uncommon west of the Cascades. The Cascade Range acts as a dividing line.
- **Elevational niche:** Low to moderate elevation in open country. Below the alpine/subalpine of the nutcracker (105) and Gray Jay (104). Adjacent to the crow's urban/open habitat (102) but more rural and more eastern.
- **Year-round resident:** Does not migrate. Social -- typically found in family groups or loose flocks.

### Open Country Community

The magpie shares its open-country habitat with the **Red Fox** (degree 71) and **Townsend's Ground Squirrel** (degree 78) -- both signature species of the dry open landscapes east of the Cascades. The magpie is an omnivore and scavenger, feeding on insects, carrion, small vertebrates, fruits, and grain.

### Intelligence

The Black-billed Magpie is among the most intelligent corvids [1, 2]:
- **Mirror test:** The European Magpie (*Pica pica*, closely related) passes the mirror self-recognition test -- one of the few non-mammalian species to do so. The Black-billed Magpie likely shares this cognitive capacity.
- **Tool use and problem-solving:** Documented. Consistent with corvid intelligence across the septet.
- **Social intelligence:** Complex social hierarchies, cooperative nest defense, and vocal coordination.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; [2] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)
