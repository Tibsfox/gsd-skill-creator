Dusky Grouse — Deep Booming

Dendragapus obscurus (Phasianidae) — E=5 — Subsonic Thumping Display
SECOND PHASIANIDAE. Formerly “Blue Grouse” (interior populations). The lowest-frequency bird vocalization in the Pacific Northwest. 50–100 Hz fundamental — more felt than heard. Cervical air sacs inflate as Helmholtz resonators. Purplish-red skin surrounded by white feather rosettes. Reverse altitudinal migration — upslope in winter.

Subsonic Booming — More Felt Than Heard

Fundamental frequency: 50–100 Hz — at or below the threshold of easy human hearing. The sound is a physical sensation in the chest before it is an auditory event. Cervical air sacs inflate rapidly, revealing purplish-red skin. Air forced through syrinx from inflated sacs. Helmholtz resonance produces the extremely low frequency. Series of 5–7 deep “whoom” notes. Extremely difficult to localize — seems to come from everywhere.

The Blue Grouse Split (2006)

Blue Grouse split into two species by AOU in 2006:
Dusky Grouse (D. obscurus) — interior mountains, purplish-red air sacs, drier forest. Degree 154.
Sooty Grouse (D. fuliginosus) — coastal mountains, yellow air sacs, wetter forest. Degree 156.
Evidence: genetic divergence, vocalization differences, air sac coloration, plumage. Cascade crest as boundary.

Reverse Migration — Upslope in Winter

Most mountain birds move downslope in winter. The Dusky Grouse moves upslope — from lower breeding habitats to higher wintering habitats. Reason: conifer needle diet. Densest conifer stands at higher elevation. Snow roosting for insulation. Solitary and sedentary in winter, perching in individual conifers for weeks.
