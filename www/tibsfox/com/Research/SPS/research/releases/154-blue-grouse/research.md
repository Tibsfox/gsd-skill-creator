**Ecosystem:** Conifer slopes -- Douglas-fir, true fir, and spruce forests at middle to high elevations. ## STRUCTURAL EVENT: THE BLUE GROUSE SPLIT -- Dusky at 154, Sooty at 156

### The 2006 Taxonomic Split

In 2006, the American Ornithological Union (AOU) split the Blue Grouse (*Dendragapus obscurus*) into two species:

| Species | Scientific Name | Range | Habitat | Air Sac Color |
|---------|----------------|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Dusky Grouse** | *Dendragapus obscurus* | Interior mountains | Drier Douglas-fir, ponderosa pine | Purplish-red |
| **Sooty Grouse** | *Dendragapus fuliginosus* | Coastal mountains | Wetter Sitka spruce, western hemlock | Yellow |

### The Evidence for Splitting

The split was based on multiple lines of evidence:

- **Genetic divergence:** Mitochondrial DNA analysis showed significant genetic differentiation between interior and coastal populations, consistent with species-level separation.
- **Vocalization differences:** The two populations produce acoustically distinct booming sounds. Dusky Grouse booming is slightly higher-pitched and more rapid than Sooty Grouse booming.
- **Air sac coloration:** Males display different cervical air sac colors during the booming display -- purplish-red in Dusky Grouse, yellow in Sooty Grouse. The air sac color is a visual display signal used in mate attraction and is a key diagnostic character.
- **Plumage differences:** Subtle but consistent plumage differences between the two species, with Dusky Grouse being slightly paler and grayer, Sooty Grouse slightly darker and browner.
- **Range separation:** The two species are largely separated by the Cascade crest in Washington and Oregon, with Dusky Grouse to the east and Sooty Grouse to the west. There is a narrow zone of overlap along the crest.

### Engine Treatment

The engine documents both split species:
- **Degree 154:** Dusky Grouse (interior, purplish-red air sacs, drier forest)
- **Degree 156:** Sooty Grouse (coastal, yellow air sacs, wetter forest, ventriloquial booming)

The two-degree separation (154 to 156, with Ruffed Grouse drumming at 155 between them) mirrors the taxonomic relationship: close but distinct, with a different grouse species separating them.

(Sources: AOU 2006; Barrowclough et al. 2004; Zwickel & Bendell 2005; engine internal analysis)

## E=5 -- THE SUBSONIC POWER

### Why E=5

The Dusky Grouse deep booming registers at E=5:

- **Subsonic penetration:** The booming operates at frequencies (approximately 50-100 Hz fundamental) that are at or below the threshold of easy human hearing. The sound is more felt than heard -- a physical sensation in the chest rather than a clear auditory event. This subsonic quality gives the sound an uncanny, almost seismic character.
- **Physical production energy:** The booming requires enormous physical effort. The male inflates cervical air sacs to many times their resting size, compresses the air through the syrinx, and produces the booming through a combination of vocal and pneumatic mechanisms. The energy investment is substantial.
- **Display intensity:** The male performs the booming display repeatedly from display sites on elevated ground or fallen logs. The display involves the full body: tail fanning, wing drooping, head bobbing, air sac inflation/deflation. The combined visual and acoustic display is intense.
- **Territorial function:** The booming serves to establish and defend territories and attract mates. Males may boom for hours during the breeding season, investing enormous energy in the display.
- **Low-frequency propagation:** Low-frequency sounds propagate farther through dense forest than high-frequency sounds (less atmospheric absorption, less scattering by vegetation). The subsonic booming is adapted for communication in the dense conifer forests where the Dusky Grouse lives.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Zwickel & Bendell 2005; engine internal analysis)

## THE BOOMING DISPLAY -- MORE FELT THAN HEARD

### How the Booming Works

The Dusky Grouse booming display is one of the most remarkable acoustic performances in the PNW:

- **Cervical air sacs:** Males possess paired cervical air sacs on either side of the neck. During the booming display, these sacs are inflated to reveal brightly colored skin (purplish-red in Dusky Grouse) surrounded by a rosette of white feathers.
- **Inflation mechanism:** The air sacs are inflated by forcing air from the lungs through the trachea and into the sacs. The inflation is rapid and dramatic -- the neck swells to nearly twice its normal diameter.
- **Sound production:** The booming sound is produced as air is forced from the inflated sacs back through the syrinx. The extremely low frequency is achieved by the resonance properties of the inflated sacs acting as Helmholtz resonators -- cavities that amplify specific low frequencies.
- **Frequency:** The fundamental frequency of the booming is approximately 50-100 Hz -- near the lower limit of human hearing (20 Hz). The sound often falls partially into the infrasonic range, which is why it is "felt" in the chest before it is "heard" by the ears.
- **Temporal pattern:** The booming consists of a series of approximately 5-7 individual booming notes produced in a bout. Each note is a deep, resonant "whoom" or "boop." The notes are spaced at regular intervals of approximately 1-2 seconds.
- **Amplitude:** Despite the low frequency, the sound is not particularly loud in the conventional sense (dB SPL). However, the low-frequency energy is felt as a physical vibration at close range (within 50-100 meters). At greater distances, the sound becomes difficult to localize and seems to emanate from no particular direction.

### The Display Sequence

1. **Site selection:** The male selects a display site -- often an elevated point, fallen log, or rocky outcrop in conifer forest.
2. **Posture:** The male adopts a display posture: tail fanned vertically, wings slightly drooped, body erect.
3. **Inflation:** The cervical air sacs inflate rapidly, revealing the purplish-red skin surrounded by white feather rosettes.
4. **Booming:** A series of 5-7 deep booming notes, each produced by a cycle of air sac inflation and deflation.
5. **Visual display:** Between booming bouts, the male may strut, fan the tail, and bob the head. The air sacs remain partially inflated.
6. **Repetition:** The display is repeated at intervals throughout the breeding season (April-June), particularly in the early morning.

## REVERSE MIGRATION -- UPSLOPE IN WINTER

### The Anomalous Movement

The Dusky Grouse performs one of the most unusual movements in North American ornithology:

- **Reverse altitudinal migration:** While most mountain birds move downslope in winter to avoid harsh conditions at high elevation, the Dusky Grouse moves UPSLOPE -- from lower-elevation breeding habitats to higher-elevation wintering habitats.
- **Conifer needle diet:** The reason is dietary. In winter, Dusky Grouse feed almost exclusively on conifer needles (Douglas-fir, true fir, spruce). The densest stands of these conifers are at higher elevations. The birds move upslope to access the winter food supply.
- **Snow protection:** At high elevation in winter, deep snow and dense conifer canopy provide insulation and protection from wind. The grouse burrows into snow for overnight shelter (snow roosting), using the insulating properties of snow to maintain body temperature.
- **Solitary winter:** In winter, Dusky Grouse become solitary and sedentary, perching in individual conifer trees and feeding on needles for weeks at a time. This is a dramatic contrast to the breeding season's display activity.

(Sources: Zwickel & Bendell 2005; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Conifer Slope -- Subsonic Booming

The Dusky Grouse's deep booming is the focal vocalization for this entry:

**The Booming:**
- **Trigger:** Breeding season (April-June). Dawn and early morning. Male territorial/courtship display.
- **Mechanism:** Cervical air sac inflation/deflation. Air forced through syrinx from inflated sacs. Helmholtz resonance produces extremely low frequency.
- **Character:** Deep, resonant, thumping. More felt than heard. A physical sensation in the chest. "Whoom" or "boop" -- individual notes in a series.
- **Frequency:** Fundamental approximately 50-100 Hz. Partially infrasonic. Near or below the lower limit of human hearing.
- **Duration:** Individual note: ~0.3-0.5 seconds. Bout: 5-7 notes over ~8-12 seconds. Display sessions: repeated bouts over minutes to hours.
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Not conventionally "loud" (moderate dB SPL). The energy is concentrated in extremely low frequencies that are felt as physical vibration rather than heard as sound. Effective range: 100-300 meters.
- **Localization:** Extremely difficult to localize. The low frequency and the forest environment combine to make the sound seem to come from everywhere and nowhere. Listeners often cannot determine the direction of a booming grouse.
- **Seasonal peak:** April-June. Most intense at dawn and early morning.

**Other Vocalizations (secondary):**
- **Clucking:** Various cluck notes for contact and alarm. Higher-frequency, easier to localize.
- **Whining:** Females produce whining calls during nesting.
- **Alarm:** Sharp, loud clucks when disturbed at close range.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Zwickel & Bendell 2005; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
