*Degree 154 retrospective complete. DUSKY GROUSE DEEP BOOMING at the one hundred fifty-fourth degree -- THE SUBSONIC DISPLAY. On the interior mountain slopes east of the Cascade crest, in the Douglas-fir and ponderosa pine forests of the rain shadow, a large gray grouse stands on a fallen log in the pre-dawn darkness of April and inflates two cervical air sacs to many times their resting size, revealing patches of purplish-red skin surrounded by rosettes of white feathers. Air is forced from the inflated sacs back through the syrinx, and the sacs function as Helmholtz resonators -- cavities that amplify a specific, extremely low frequency. The sound that emerges is not quite a sound in the conventional sense. It is a deep, resonant thumping at 50 to 100 Hz -- at or below the threshold of easy human hearing, partially infrasonic, more felt in the chest as a physical vibration than heard by the ears as an identifiable note. The booming is the lowest-frequency bird vocalization in the Pacific Northwest. It propagates through the dense forest where higher frequencies would be absorbed by trunks and needles, and it is almost impossible to localize -- the listener feels the boom but cannot determine where it comes from. The sound seems to emanate from everywhere and nowhere, from the mountain itself, from the trees, from the earth. This is the Dusky Grouse, formerly known as the Blue Grouse before the 2006 taxonomic split that separated interior populations (Dusky, purplish-red air sacs) from coastal populations (Sooty, yellow air sacs). The engine documents both: Dusky at 154, Sooty at 156, with the Ruffed Grouse's mechanical drumming at 155 between them. And in autumn, when most mountain birds move downslope to escape the winter, the Dusky Grouse does the opposite -- it moves upslope, into the deeper snow and denser conifer canopy at higher elevation, because that is where the conifer needles are, the winter food supply that sustains this improbable bird through the darkest months. The subsonic display. The invisible foundation. The sound below sound.*
