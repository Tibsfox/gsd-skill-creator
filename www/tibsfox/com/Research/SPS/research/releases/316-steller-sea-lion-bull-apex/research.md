## *Eumetopias jubatus* -- THE SPECIES

### The Largest Eared Seal in the World

The Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*, Schreber, 1776) is the largest member of the family Otariidae (eared seals, including sea lions and fur seals):

- **Body size.** Adult males (bulls): 700-1,200 lbs (320-545 kg), body length 9-11 feet (2.7-3.3 m). Females (cows): 350-600 lbs (160-270 kg), body length 7.5-9.5 feet. The sexual dimorphism is extreme: adult males are 2-3 times larger than adult females. The massive neck and chest musculature of adult males -- developed through years of competition -- is the structural feature that produces the bull's acoustic power.
- **The mane.** Adult males develop a distinctive mane of coarser, darker fur around the neck and chest that gives the species its common name. The mane is not merely cosmetic -- it provides physical protection to the neck during bull-to-bull combat.
- **Range.** Steller Sea Lions range from the Sea of Japan and the Kuril Islands through the Aleutian Islands, the Gulf of Alaska, and south along the Pacific coast to southern California. Washington State hosts breeding colonies at the San Juan Islands and haul-out sites on the Olympic Peninsula coast. The Steller Sea Lion is the Pacific Northwest's largest pinnipeds year-round.
- **Population status.** The Steller Sea Lion is listed under the Endangered Species Act. The western stock (Alaska/Asia) is classified as Endangered; the eastern stock (California/Oregon/Washington/British Columbia) has recovered and is now classified as Not Threatened, having reached approximately 40,000 animals. The eastern stock's recovery is one of the marine mammal conservation success stories of the Pacific Northwest.
- **Behavior.** Steller Sea Lions are polygynous: dominant bulls defend territories on prime rocks and maintain access to groups of females (harems) during the breeding season. The competition for prime rock is continuous and intense from May through July. Dominant bulls ("beachmasters") can hold prime positions for multiple years, though most do not maintain dominance for more than 2-3 breeding seasons due to the physical cost.

(Sources: Eumetopias jubatus taxonomy Schreber 1776; Otariidae largest member; bull body mass 320-545 kg; cow body mass 160-270 kg; sexual dimorphism 2-3x; mane anatomy and function; range: Sea of Japan through California; Washington colonies San Juan Islands and Olympic Peninsula; ESA listing; eastern stock recovery ~40000 animals; polygynous breeding; beachmaster dynamics)

## THE APEX BULL -- THE DOMINANT INDIVIDUAL

### The Beachmaster at Maximum

The apex bull at degree 316 is the beachmaster: the dominant male who controls prime rock at the rookery. His acoustic output is the individual maximum of the Otariidae:

- **The position.** Prime rock at a Steller Sea Lion rookery is determined by proximity to water (enabling easy entry and exit), elevation above wave wash (protecting pups from drowning), and centrality within the colony. The bull who controls the best rock has the best breeding access. The quality of the rock position directly predicts reproductive success.
- **The sustained roar.** The beachmaster's territorial roar is different from the combat roar of degree 306's beach battle. The battle roar is continuous but interrupted by physical combat -- the acoustic output is punctuated by impact sounds and the physical effort of pushing against another bull. The beachmaster's territorial roar is sustained and rhythmic: the bull raises his head, inflates his chest, and produces a deep, powerful, resonant roar that carries across the rookery and beyond. The roar is the acoustic broadcast of dominance: "I am here, I am large, this rock is mine."
- **Duration and rhythm.** A beachmaster may roar hundreds of times per day during peak breeding season. The individual roar lasts 2-8 seconds. The rhythm of the roar -- the timing and repetition -- becomes the acoustic signature of the dominant animal, recognizable to the cows in his territory and the subordinate bulls at the colony's margins.
- **Amplitude.** The Steller Sea Lion bull roar is one of the loudest vocalizations produced by any pinniped. The resonance chambers in the large neck and chest, the inflated body during vocalization, and the maximum air volume of a 500+ kg animal combine to produce a roar that is audible from considerable distances across the rookery surface and across open water. The rookery ambient -- hundreds of vocalizing animals at various intensities -- forms the background; the beachmaster's roar cuts through it at maximum amplitude.

The Steller Sea Lion bull apex roar calibrates to E=8 through multiple acoustic factors:

- **Frequency.** Primary acoustic energy: 100-1,500 Hz. Peak energy: 200-800 Hz. Harmonics extending to 3,000+ Hz. The low-frequency content (100-400 Hz) is the element that the human body perceives as pressure rather than sound at close range -- the sub-bass component of the roar that is felt as much as heard.
- **Amplitude.** At close range (10-20 meters), the beachmaster's sustained roar approaches 110-120 dB. At rookery ambient distance (100+ meters), the roar remains clearly dominant over the rookery background noise. The amplitude at close range is comparable to a moderately loud rock concert.
- **The resonance anatomy.** The large chest and neck cavity of an adult bull functions as a resonance chamber: the vocal folds produce the fundamental frequency, and the body cavity amplifies specific harmonics. The larger the body cavity, the more resonance amplification is available. At 500+ kg, the Steller Sea Lion bull has the largest resonance chamber of any Otariidae, producing the deepest, most resonant roar in the family.
- **Sustained duration.** Unlike many large mammal vocalizations that are brief (the Cougar scream: 2-7 sec; the elk bugle: 3-10 sec), the beachmaster's territorial roar during peak breeding season is sustained throughout the day -- hundreds of roars over hours of activity. The cumulative acoustic output is the highest sustained acoustic energy output of any Pacific Northwest pinniped.

The apex bull's individual sustained territorial roar reaches E=8 through sustained amplitude, low-frequency body resonance, and the acoustic authority of the dominant individual position. Both events are E=8 through different mechanisms.

## THE WASHINGTON COAST ROOKERIES

### Where This Happens in the Pacific Northwest

The Steller Sea Lion rookeries relevant to the Washington coast:

- **Race Rocks Ecological Reserve, British Columbia.** The nearest major rookery to Washington State, located at the southern tip of Vancouver Island. A large, active Steller Sea Lion colony with year-round presence of non-breeding animals and summer breeding activity. Visible from the San Juan Islands on clear days.
- **Oregon coast rookeries.** Several Steller Sea Lion haul-out sites and small colonies along the Oregon coast, including the famous sea stack sites at Cape Arago, Cape Foulweather, and the Sea Lion Caves near Florence (the largest sea cave in North America, used as a year-round haul-out).
- **San Juan Islands.** Various rocky outcrops in the San Juan Islands host Steller Sea Lion haul-outs year-round, particularly in winter and spring. Not breeding colonies but significant aggregation sites visible from whale-watching vessels.
- **Olympic Peninsula coast.** Haul-out sites at various exposed coastal rocks along the Olympic National Park coastline. The remote nature of the Olympic coast (accessible only by boat or multi-day backpacking) means these sites receive minimal human disturbance.
- **The prime rock concept.** "Prime rock" at a Steller Sea Lion site is specifically: a flat or gently sloping rock shelf at moderate elevation above mean tide, large enough to support multiple animals, with a gradual water approach. The prime rocks are occupied year after year by the same dominant bulls as long as those bulls can maintain their competitive position.

(Sources: Race Rocks BC Steller Sea Lion colony; Oregon coast rookeries; Cape Arago Cape Foulweather; Sea Lion Caves Florence OR; San Juan Islands haul-out sites; Olympic Peninsula coast haul-outs; prime rock characteristics; year-over-year site fidelity)
