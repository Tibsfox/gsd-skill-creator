## THE COUGAR -- *Puma concolor*

### The Solitary Apex Felid

The Cougar (*Puma concolor*, Linnaeus, 1771) -- also called Mountain Lion, Puma, Panther, Catamount -- is the largest felid in the Pacific Northwest and the most widely distributed large cat in the Americas:

- **Washington range.** Cougars are present throughout Washington State's forested and mountainous areas: the Cascades, the Olympics, the Blue Mountains, the Okanagan Highlands. Washington State hosts approximately 2,000 cougars, making it one of the largest cougar populations in the contiguous United States. They are most dense in the North Cascades and the Olympic Peninsula interior.
- **Body.** Adult males 115-220 lbs (52-100 kg); body length 7.5-8 feet including tail. Tawny to gray-brown coloration. Retractable claws. Ambush predator: stalks prey from cover, kills with a bite to the neck or skull. Primary prey in Washington: deer (both mule deer and black-tailed deer). Secondary: elk calves, mountain goats, bighorn sheep, small mammals.
- **The scream.** The cougar's territorial and courtship vocalization -- the "scream" -- is one of the most startling sounds in North American wilderness. Long duration (2-7 seconds), high frequency (primary energy 200-2,000 Hz), human-like timbre that can be confused with a person in distress. The scream is used primarily in courtship (estrous females and responding males) and territorial assertion. It carries through forest for distances exceeding 1 km.
- **Solitary nature.** Cougars are strongly solitary except during mating and when a female is raising kittens. Males maintain large territories (50-150 square miles) that overlap with multiple female territories. The encounter with a wolf or wolf pack represents an unusual territorial overlap event: normally, the cougar's solitary nature and preference for ambush cover means it avoids open confrontation. But when confrontation occurs, the cougar's willingness to hold its ground against wolves (it is physically capable of killing an adult wolf) produces the sustained vocal confrontation that constitutes the E=9 event.

## THE WOLF -- *Canis lupus*

### The Recovered Apex Canid

The Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*, Linnaeus, 1758) was extirpated from Washington State by the 1930s through predator control programs. Its recovery in the 21st century is one of the most significant wildlife conservation stories in the Pacific Northwest:

- **The recovery.** Natural dispersal of wolves from Idaho into northeastern Washington began in the late 1990s. The Lookout Pack was documented in 2008 -- the first confirmed breeding pack in Washington in decades. By 2026, Washington State hosts approximately 25-30 packs, concentrated in the Cascades and the Okanagan region but expanding westward and southward. The North Cascades now have confirmed wolf presence that overlaps with established cougar range.
- **Pack structure and territory.** Gray wolves in Washington typically travel in packs of 4-12 individuals, though lone wolves and dispersing individuals are common. Pack territory in Washington ranges from 200-500 square miles. Territorial howling -- the full pack howl that the engine documents at degree 303 (E=8) -- is used to announce territory to other packs and to coordinate pack movement.
- **The wolf-cougar dynamic.** Gray Wolves and Cougars are the two apex predators in Washington's terrestrial ecosystem. They are not direct competitors in the sense of competing for the same niche (wolves are coursing predators that pursue prey in packs; cougars are solitary ambush predators), but they do compete for the same prey resources (deer, elk) and their territories overlap. Research in areas of wolf recovery has documented wolves displacing cougars from kills, killing cougar kittens (infanticide), and wolves mobbing lone cougars to drive them from kills. Cougars, in turn, are capable of killing wolves and have been documented doing so.
- **The vocalization in confrontation.** When a pack or individual wolf encounters a cougar, the wolf vocalizes: territorial barks, growls, and when the pack is present, the distributed warning calls that serve pack coordination. A cougar responding to wolf presence screams and growls. The encounter produces the overlapping vocal event that constitutes degree 313.

(Sources: Canis lupus taxonomy; Washington wolf extirpation by 1930s; Lookout Pack documented 2008; Washington State wolf recovery 25-30 packs by 2026; North Cascades wolf presence; pack size 4-12; territory 200-500 sq miles; territorial howling function; wolf-cougar competition research; wolves displacing cougars from kills; wolf infanticide on cougar kittens; cougar capability to kill wolves; confrontation vocalization patterns)

## THE OLD-GROWTH CONTEXT

### Why This Happens in Remote Old-Growth

The Cougar-Wolf encounter happens in remote old-growth for structural reasons:

- **Old-growth as apex predator habitat.** Both cougars and wolves require large, intact wilderness areas with sufficient prey density to sustain apex predator populations. Old-growth forest provides this: high deer density in edge habitats, complex terrain for cougar ambush hunting, connected corridors for wolf pack movement. The structural complexity of old-growth -- the snags, the down logs, the multi-layered canopy, the understory diversity -- creates the prey-supporting habitat that brings both predators to the same area.
- **The remoteness factor.** The overlap between cougar and wolf territory that makes this encounter possible is found in the most remote parts of the Cascades -- areas far from human settlement, with minimal road access, where both species can maintain large territories without the human disturbance that drives apex predators away. The encounter does not happen near trailheads. It happens in the interior of wilderness areas, away from human presence.
- **The acoustic landscape.** In remote old-growth, with no human noise, the encounter's acoustic event carries through the forest with nothing to mask it. The cougar's scream at 200-2,000 Hz and the wolf's vocal response at 200-1,200 Hz propagate through the trees, through the understory, up the canyon walls. The forest itself becomes the acoustic space -- not a background for the sound but an active participant, with trees and terrain reflections extending the reach of both animals' vocalizations.

(Sources: Old-growth as apex predator habitat; deer density in old-growth edge habitats; complex terrain for ambush; connected corridors for wolf pack; remoteness and absence of human disturbance; acoustic propagation in old-growth; forest as acoustic space)
