The Two Species

Cougar / Mountain Lion

Puma concolor, Linnaeus, 1771

Adult males 115–220 lbs. Largest felid in the PNW. Solitary ambush predator. Washington population ~2,000. Primary range: North Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains. The scream: long-duration (2–7 sec), high-frequency (200–2,000 Hz), human-like timbre. Carries 1+ km through forest. Used in territorial assertion and courtship. E=8 individually (degree 302).

Gray Wolf

Canis lupus, Linnaeus, 1758

Adult males 70–130 lbs. Recovered to Washington from Idaho dispersal (late 1990s). 25–30 packs as of 2026, concentrated in Cascades and Okanagan. Pack coursing predator. Territorial howl (degree 303, E=8) used to announce territory and coordinate pack. Confrontation vocalization: territorial bark, growl, escalating pack warning calls. E=8 individually (degree 303).

The AIC Pairing — Maximum on Both Tracks

Degree 313: S36 E=9 + SPS E=9. Alice in Chains at grunge summit maximum. Cougar-Wolf encounter at multi-species wilderness maximum. Both irreducibly themselves. Both at the ceiling of their respective tracks.

Two voices in dissonant harmony on the S36 track; two apex predators in overlapping territorial call on the SPS track. The pairing is structurally exact.

The Rarity

The Cougar-Wolf encounter was essentially impossible in Washington State for most of the 20th century. Wolves were extirpated by the 1930s. The Cougar was present throughout the Cascades but had no wolf to encounter. The wolf’s recovery through natural dispersal from Idaho and subsequent conservation management has made this encounter newly possible — and, as of 2026, documented in Washington’s North Cascades. It remains rare. Most wilderness hikers in the Cascades will spend years in the backcountry without ever hearing a cougar scream. The probability of being present during a cougar-wolf territorial encounter is orders of magnitude lower still. This is the rarest acoustic event the engine documents.

The Ecological Significance

The Cougar-Wolf encounter is the acoustic signal that Washington State’s terrestrial apex predator ecology has been restored to a level not seen since the early 20th century. The sound is evidence: two apex predators occupying overlapping territory, both present at sufficient density that random territorial encounters occur. The engine documents this moment in the recovery of the PNW wilderness as a fully functional predator ecosystem. The cougar’s scream and the wolf’s howl overlapping in old-growth forest is not merely an intense acoustic event — it is the proof of ecological restoration.

Acoustic Profile — E=9 Through Superposition

Cougar frequency: 200–2,000 Hz, primary energy 400–1,200 Hz. Duration: 2–7 seconds per scream event, repeated multiple times during confrontation. Human-like timbre: the quality that makes encountering a cougar scream viscerally startling even to experienced wilderness travelers.

Wolf frequency: 200–1,200 Hz. Territorial confrontation vocalizations: barks (200–500 Hz, sharp percussive onset), growls (100–500 Hz, sustained), warning howls (200–1,200 Hz). When pack is present: multiple simultaneous voices adding to acoustic complexity.

Combined: 100–2,000 Hz, covering the full range of apex predator territorial acoustic output. The superposition creates an acoustic event that no single PNW species can produce alone. The old-growth forest, with its complex terrain and tree-mass resonance, extends the carry distance and adds acoustic reflections that make the combined event longer and spatially wider than either source individually.

E=9 rationale: (1) Acoustic intensity -- two maximum-energy vocalizations simultaneously. (2) Frequency breadth -- combined range exceeds either species. (3) Duration -- sustained confrontation minutes, not seconds. (4) Rareness -- rarest acoustic event in the PNW wilderness. (5) Ecological significance -- proof of apex predator ecosystem restoration. Combined: SPS maximum through superposition.
