## HOODED MERGANSER -- ANATIDAE -- THE CRESTED DIVER

### The Species

The Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*) is **North America's smallest merganser** and the **only member of the genus *Lophodytes***. It is also the only merganser species entirely restricted to North America -- unlike the Common Merganser (Holarctic) and the Red-breasted Merganser (Holarctic), the Hooded Merganser breeds and winters exclusively in North America.

The Hooded Merganser is a small duck -- body length 40-49 cm, wingspan 56-70 cm, weight 450-880g. It is significantly smaller than the Common Merganser (119, 1.3-2.1 kg) -- roughly half the weight. But what it lacks in size, it compensates in spectacle.

### The Crest -- One of the Most Dramatic Displays in Duck Taxonomy

The male Hooded Merganser's **fan-shaped crest** is one of the most visually dramatic features of any North American duck. When raised, the crest expands into a large, rounded, **fan-shaped black-and-white display**: black at the edges with a large triangular white patch in the center. The visual effect is startling -- the bird's head appears to double in size when the crest is fully raised.

The crest display functions in:
1. **Courtship** -- males display to females by raising the crest, pumping the head, and emitting the rolling call. The raised crest makes the male's head appear enormously large, signaling health and vitality. Multiple males display simultaneously, and females choose among them.
2. **Territorial signaling** -- the raised crest communicates dominance and territory to other males. A male with a fully raised crest is broadcasting confidence.
3. **Alarm and agitation** -- the crest is partially raised in alert or disturbed states.
4. **Relaxed state** -- when relaxed, the crest lies flat against the head, giving the bird a slicked-back, compact profile. The transformation between flat-crest and raised-crest is dramatic: the same bird looks entirely different.

The female's crest is rufous-brown, smaller, and less dramatic, but still present. Both sexes have crests; the male's is the display crest.

The name *Lophodytes* means "crested diver" -- from Greek *lophos* (crest) and *dytes* (diver). The species name *cucullatus* means "hooded" in Latin. The Hooded Merganser: the hooded crested diver.

### Only Member of Its Genus

The Hooded Merganser is taxonomically unique among mergansers. While the Common and Red-breasted Mergansers belong to genus *Mergus*, the Hooded Merganser is the sole member of genus *Lophodytes*. This generic separation reflects:
- **Evolutionary divergence** -- the Hooded Merganser lineage diverged from other mergansers earlier than the *Mergus* species diverged from each other. It is the merganser most distantly related to the other two.
- **Morphological distinctness** -- smaller body, proportionally larger crest, different bill shape (shorter and more triangular than *Mergus* bills).
- **Ecological niche** -- the Hooded Merganser is a **cavity nester in wooded habitats** near small ponds and streams, unlike the larger *Mergus* mergansers that favor larger rivers and lakes.

### Wooded Pond Ecosystem

The Hooded Merganser's preferred habitat is the **wooded pond** -- small, sheltered bodies of water surrounded by trees. This includes:
- **Woodland ponds** -- natural ponds in forested areas.
- **Beaver ponds** -- created by beaver dams. The Hooded Merganser benefits from beaver activity.
- **Forested swamps** -- shallow, tree-covered wetlands.
- **Quiet backwaters** -- sheltered areas of rivers and streams, away from the main current.

The key habitat requirements:
1. **Trees for nesting** -- the Hooded Merganser is an obligate **cavity nester**. It nests in tree cavities (natural or woodpecker-created) near water. Large trees with suitable cavities are essential. The bird also readily uses nest boxes.
2. **Clear, sheltered water** -- the bird hunts by sight in shallow, clear water. It prefers still or slow-moving water, unlike the Common Merganser's preference for flowing rivers.
3. **Abundant aquatic prey** -- primarily crayfish, small fish, and aquatic insects. The Hooded Merganser's smaller size means it takes smaller prey than the Common Merganser.

In the Pacific Northwest, the Hooded Merganser is found in the lowland forests and wetlands west of the Cascades -- the beaver ponds, swamp forests, and quiet backwaters of the Puget Sound lowland and the river valleys. It is less common than the Common Merganser but regularly seen in appropriate habitat.

### Foraging Behavior

The Hooded Merganser forages by **diving** in shallow, clear water:
1. The bird floats on the surface, often with its crest partially raised.
2. It dives with a quick forward plunge, propelling itself underwater with its feet.
3. Underwater, it pursues prey -- primarily crayfish, small fish (minnows, sticklebacks), and aquatic insect larvae.
4. Dives are typically 10-20 seconds, in water 1-3 meters deep.
5. It surfaces with prey and swallows it on the water surface.

The Hooded Merganser has the same serrated bill (sawbill) adaptation as the Common Merganser, but scaled down. The bill is shorter and more triangular in profile, reflecting smaller prey items. The serrations are present but less pronounced than in the larger *Mergus* species.

An additional remarkable adaptation: the Hooded Merganser's eyes have been shown to have **exceptional underwater visual acuity**. The nictitating membrane is highly adapted for underwater vision, allowing the bird to spot small, cryptic prey (like crayfish among bottom debris) in low-light conditions. Wooded ponds are often shaded and murky near the bottom -- the Hooded Merganser's vision is adapted to hunt in these conditions.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Dugger et al. 2009; cavity nesting ecology)

## Acoustic Profile: The Rolling Frog-Like Call

The Hooded Merganser produces one of the most unusual calls in North American duck taxonomy: a **rolling, frog-like call** that sounds more like an amphibian than a bird. The call is a low, rolling, grunting "crrroo" -- a resonant, almost mechanical sound that carries across wooded ponds with a quality reminiscent of a bullfrog.

### The Sound

- **Frequency range:** approximately 400-2,000 Hz. A low, resonant call with most energy concentrated below 1,500 Hz.
- **Quality:** rolling, guttural, frog-like. The "crrroo" has a rolling trill quality -- the vocal membranes vibrate in a way that produces a buzzy, resonant tone unlike the quacks, whistles, or honks typical of other ducks. The frog-like quality is diagnostic: if you hear what sounds like a large frog from a wooded pond and see a duck, it is likely a Hooded Merganser.
- **Context:** primarily during courtship display. Males produce the rolling call while raising the crest and pumping the head. The call accompanies the visual display.
- **Female call:** a hoarse croak, lower-pitched and less distinctive than the male's rolling call.

### E=4 -- Moderate, Unusual Sound

E=4 reflects the Hooded Merganser's moderate acoustic presence. The rolling frog-like call carries across wooded ponds -- it is not quiet -- but the sound is unusual rather than loud. The call's distinctiveness comes from its **quality** (frog-like, rolling, mechanical) rather than its volume. In the sheltered, enclosed environment of a wooded pond, the sound carries effectively without needing high amplitude.

The acoustic contrast with the Common Merganser (degree 119) is instructive: the Common Merganser's guttural croak (E=4) is harsh and functional. The Hooded Merganser's rolling frog-like call (E=4) is unusual and display-oriented. Same energy level, different quality and context. The Common Merganser calls in flight (contact); the Hooded Merganser calls on the water (courtship).

4. **The Picidae nonet was forest; the Anatidae streak is water.** The engine's two major family streaks represent the two dominant ecosystem types in the Pacific Northwest: forest and water. Both are central to PNW ecology.
