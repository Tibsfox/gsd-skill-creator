## THE BAYING FLOCK: HIGH MELODIOUS CALLS IN WINTER

### A Sound Like No Other

The Tundra Swan's defining acoustic contribution is the **baying of a flock** -- a high, melodious, multi-voiced chorus that carries across winter marshlands. This is not a single bird's call but a **collective sound** produced by dozens to hundreds of swans calling simultaneously:

- **Individual call:** A high-pitched, soft, melodious *whoo-hoo* or *wow-wow-wow* -- a quavering, musical note that rises and falls. Higher-pitched and softer than the Trumpeter Swan's deep bugling. The individual note has a **whistling, singing quality** that gives the species its former common name: **"Whistling Swan"** (the North American subspecies *C. c. columbianus* was historically called the Whistling Swan until taxonomic unification with the Eurasian Bewick's Swan *C. c. bewickii*).
- **Flock sound:** When multiple Tundra Swans call simultaneously -- in flight, on water, during feeding gatherings -- the individual calls merge into a **baying chorus**. The word "baying" captures the quality: it is resonant, rolling, with multiple voices overlapping in different phases. The flock sound is **melodious** rather than cacophonous because the individual notes are musical and the overlapping creates a harmonic texture.
- **Flight calls:** Tundra Swans call frequently in flight, maintaining contact with flock members. A passing flock of Tundra Swans produces a continuous, high-pitched baying that can be heard at great distance over flat terrain -- particularly over the open delta marshes where they winter.

The baying flock of Tundra Swans is one of the most evocative winter sounds in the Pacific Northwest. When hundreds of swans gather in the Skagit Valley or Padilla Bay, the combined voices create a soundscape that announces the season: winter has arrived, the Arctic visitors are here.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Limpert & Earnst 1994; Sibley 2014)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: THE HIGH MELODIOUS BAYING

### Frequency and Character

The Tundra Swan's vocal acoustics:

- **Frequency range:** 500-3,000 Hz. The fundamental call sits in the mid-frequency range but with harmonic overtones reaching higher. Distinctly higher-pitched than the Trumpeter Swan's fundamental of 200-1,500 Hz.
- **Character:** Melodious, whistling, quavering. The *whoo-hoo* call has a musical quality -- it rises and falls in pitch, with a slight tremolo that gives it warmth. The former name "Whistling Swan" reflects this whistling, singing character.
- **Flock dynamics:** The baying chorus of a flock is a complex acoustic event. Dozens of individual calls at slightly different pitches and phases create a **shimmering, layered texture**. The sound is not unison singing but polyphonic baying -- each bird calling independently, the combined effect greater than the sum.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate-high. E=5 energy. An individual Tundra Swan's call is moderate in volume -- softer than the Trumpeter Swan's powerful bugle. But the **flock multiplier** lifts the energy: a flock of 50-200 Tundra Swans produces a volume and presence that fills the landscape. E=5 reflects the flock sound, not the individual.
- **Seasonal timing:** The baying is most prominent during winter in the PNW, when large flocks gather on delta marshes and agricultural fields. Fall arrival and spring departure are marked by especially intense calling.
- **Distance:** Audible at 500-1,500 meters over open terrain, depending on flock size and atmospheric conditions. Over water and flat marshland, the sound carries exceptionally well.

### Why E=5

E=5 for the Tundra Swan reflects:

- **Flock amplification:** The individual call is moderate (E=3-4 range). But the species' acoustic identity is the flock baying, not the individual call. Hundreds of swans calling simultaneously produce E=5 volume.
- **Melodious intensity:** The musical quality of the baying gives it an emotional intensity that registers above its raw decibel level. The sound is stirring, evocative, attention-commanding.
- **Landscape-filling presence:** A Tundra Swan flock on a winter morning fills the delta with sound. The baying carries across open terrain and reflects off water surfaces. The acoustic presence is substantial.
- **Not E=6:** The individual call is too soft for maximum energy. Even the flock sound, while impressive, does not approach the raw power of E=6 entries (the Trumpeter Swan at degree 128 will claim E=6 with its deep resonant bugling).

## ARCTIC-TO-PNW MIGRATION

### The Long Journey

The Tundra Swan breeds on the **Arctic tundra** of Alaska and northern Canada -- one of the most remote breeding habitats of any bird in the engine. The migration to the PNW is one of the longest undertakings in North American waterfowl ecology:

- **Breeding grounds:** Low Arctic tundra from the Aleutian Islands across northern Alaska, northern Yukon, Northwest Territories, Nunavut, and northern Manitoba. The nesting habitat is treeless, wet tundra with shallow pools and sedge meadows.
- **Migration route:** The Pacific population migrates south through interior British Columbia, eastern Washington, and the Willamette Valley to winter in the Pacific lowlands. The Atlantic population migrates to the mid-Atlantic coast. The Pacific flyway population that reaches the PNW numbers approximately 80,000-100,000 birds.
- **Migration distance:** 3,000-4,000 km (1,800-2,500 miles) each way. The migration is accomplished in stages, with stopover sites along the route where flocks rest and feed for days to weeks.
- **Timing:** Fall migration reaches the PNW in October-November. Spring departure occurs in March-April. The winter residency in the PNW is approximately 4-5 months.

### Skagit Valley: Primary Wintering Grounds

The **Skagit Valley** in northwest Washington is one of the most important Tundra Swan wintering areas in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Skagit and Samish Flats:** The agricultural fields and delta marshes of the Skagit and Samish River systems host thousands of Tundra Swans each winter. The swans feed in harvested grain fields (especially corn and potato fields) and roost in shallow wetlands.
- **Padilla Bay:** The estuarine habitats of Padilla Bay, a National Estuarine Research Reserve, provide additional foraging and roosting habitat for wintering swans.
- **Fir Island:** The area around Fir Island (between the north and south forks of the Skagit River) is one of the most reliable viewing locations for Tundra Swans in the PNW. Winter aggregations of 500-2,000+ swans are regular.
- **Skagit Wildlife Area:** The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife manages farmland and wetlands specifically for wintering waterfowl, including Tundra Swans.

The Skagit Valley swan concentrations produce the baying flock sound at its most impressive. A winter morning on the Skagit Flats with hundreds of swans calling, feeding, and flying is one of the defining acoustic experiences of the PNW winter landscape.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Limpert & Earnst 1994; WDFW)

## IDENTIFICATION: TUNDRA VS. TRUMPETER

### The Size and Bill Difference

The Tundra/Trumpeter identification challenge is one of the most important in PNW waterfowl. Both species are large white swans that winter in the same habitats (especially the Skagit Valley). The key differences:

**Size:**
- **Tundra Swan:** Body length 120-150 cm (47-59 inches). Wingspan 168-211 cm (66-83 inches). Weight 3.4-9.6 kg (7.5-21 lbs). The smaller of the two swans -- but still a very large bird.
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Body length 138-165 cm (54-65 inches). Wingspan 185-250 cm (73-98 inches). Weight 7.1-13.6 kg (15.7-30 lbs), exceptionally up to 17.2 kg (38 lbs). North America's largest native bird by weight.

**Bill:**
- **Tundra Swan:** Bill is shorter relative to head, with a **concave profile** (dished appearance). Most individuals show a small **yellow loral spot** (a patch of yellow skin at the base of the upper bill, in front of the eye). The yellow spot is present in most but not all individuals; its absence does not rule out Tundra Swan.
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Bill is longer, more massive, with a **straight or slightly convex profile**. No yellow loral spot. The bill appears heavier and more angular.

**Head shape:**
- **Tundra Swan:** Head appears more rounded. The forehead slopes more gently from the bill to the crown.
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Head appears more angular. The forehead meets the bill at a sharper angle, creating a more wedge-shaped profile.

**Voice (the most reliable field mark):**
- **Tundra Swan:** High, melodious, whistling *whoo-hoo*. Softer. Higher-pitched.
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Deep, resonant, trumpet-like bugling. Louder. Lower-pitched.

In the Skagit Valley, both species winter together. Mixed flocks are common. Direct comparison is possible -- and voice is often the fastest identification tool. Hear the high whistling baying? Tundra. Hear the deep bugling? Trumpeter.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE ARCTIC BREEDER

### Tundra Nesting Ecology

The Tundra Swan's breeding ecology is defined by the Arctic:

- **Nest site:** On the ground in low Arctic tundra, typically on a slightly elevated mound near water. The nest is a large mound of sedges, grasses, and moss, lined with down feathers.
- **Clutch:** 3-5 eggs. Incubation approximately 31-32 days. Both parents tend the nest and young, but the female does the majority of incubation.
- **Breeding timeline:** Eggs laid in late May to early June. Cygnets hatch in late June to early July. The young must grow fast enough to fly before the Arctic winter sets in -- approximately 60-70 days from hatching to fledging.
- **Family groups:** Tundra Swans maintain family bonds through the winter. Family groups (two adults + 1-4 juveniles) migrate together and winter together. The gray-brown juveniles are visible in winter flocks, feeding alongside their white parents.

### Population and Conservation

- **Global population:** Approximately 170,000-180,000 Tundra Swans (both subspecies combined). The North American population (*C. c. columbianus*) is approximately 140,000-150,000.
- **Conservation status:** Least Concern (IUCN). Stable to slightly increasing. The species recovered from 19th-century market hunting through federal protection (Migratory Bird Treaty Act) and habitat conservation.
- **PNW winter population:** The Pacific population that winters in Washington and Oregon is approximately 80,000-100,000 birds. The Skagit Valley hosts a significant fraction.
- **Threats:** Habitat loss in breeding and wintering areas. Lead poisoning from ingested shot pellets (a legacy of historical hunting practices). Climate change is altering Arctic breeding habitat.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Limpert & Earnst 1994; IUCN Red List)
