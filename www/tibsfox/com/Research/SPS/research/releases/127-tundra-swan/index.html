NEW ALL-TIME RECORD — 10th Consecutive Anatidae

The Tundra Swan at degree 127° is the TENTH consecutive Anatidae, surpassing the Picidae nonet record (9 consecutive, degrees 107–115). The streak spans Harlequin Duck (118) through Tundra Swan (127): stream specialist, fish-eating divers, cavity nesters, diving ducks, goldeneyes, scaup, canvasback, and now — swans. The Anatidae streak culminates in the family’s largest and most acoustically majestic members. Energy bookends: E=5 at the start (Harlequin, 118) and E=5 at the record-breaker (Tundra Swan, 127).

The High Melodious Baying — Flock Sound

The Tundra Swan’s defining sound is the baying of a flock — dozens to hundreds of swans calling simultaneously, producing a shimmering, polyphonic chorus. Individual calls are a high-pitched, melodious whoo-hoo at 500–3,000 Hz — the whistling quality that earned the former name “Whistling Swan.” The flock multiplier lifts the energy to E=5: audible at 500–1,500 meters over open delta terrain. One of the most evocative winter sounds in the Pacific Northwest.

Arctic to PNW — 3,000–4,000 km Migration

Breeds on low Arctic tundra from Alaska across northern Canada. Migrates 3,000–4,000 km to winter in PNW lowlands. The Skagit Valley is a primary wintering ground — Skagit and Samish Flats, Padilla Bay, Fir Island host winter aggregations of 500–2,000+ swans. Family groups (adults + gray-brown juveniles) maintain bonds through winter. Pacific population: ~80,000–100,000 birds.

Tundra vs. Trumpeter — Swan Comparison Pair (127–128)

Two North American swans at consecutive degrees. Tundra: smaller, higher-pitched, flock baying, E=5. Trumpeter (degree 128): larger, deeper-voiced, individual bugling, E=6. Key field marks: Tundra has a yellow loral spot, shorter bill with concave profile, rounder head. Voice is the most reliable separator: hear the high whistling? Tundra. Hear the deep bugling? Trumpeter.
