CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Red-legged Frog (Rana aurora) at degree 58. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Wood Duck replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s lowland pond habitat and E=3 energy. The Red-legged Frog inhabits lowland ponds with a soft guttural clucking chorus during spring breeding (E=3). The Wood Duck inhabits precisely the same lowland ponds and is similarly present but not abundant (E=3). Both are spring-breeding species. This is the fourteenth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (Pacific Chorus Frog at 45 through Red-legged Frog at 58) — the engine’s deepest penetration into the amphibian belt.

ANATIDAE ENTERS ENGINE — 25th Family, 8th Order

The Wood Duck brings Anatidae (ducks, geese, swans) into the engine as the 25th family and the first representative of order Anseriformes — the engine’s 8th order. Third consecutive new order — Columbiformes at 56, Coraciiformes at 57, Anseriformes at 58. Three consecutive new-order entries are unprecedented. After a 21-degree gap (35–55), three new orders in three degrees. The largest family to enter the engine by global species count (~174 species). First waterfowl. First swimming species. Passeriformes interrupted for the fourth time in five degrees (54 Piciformes, 55 Passeriformes, 56 Columbiformes, 57 Coraciiformes, 58 Anseriformes).

The 59th Acoustic Element: The Pond Whistle

A thin, rising whistle — the female’s ascending “jeeee” sweeping upward in pitch from ~1,500 to ~4,500 Hz. Not a quack, not a honk, not a rattle. A tonal ascent — a sound that rises like breath from the surface of still water. Named “the pond whistle” for three resonances: (1) the habitat — the whistle maps still water (ponds, swamps), complementing the stream rattle (degree 57) which maps flowing water; (2) the ascending contour — completing a pitch-contour triad with the descending coo (56) and the flat rattle (57); (3) the intimacy — a close-range sound, heard when you are already at the pond. The most intimate non-passerine voice in the engine.

The Bridal Dress: North America’s Most Colorful Duck

Linnaeus named the species sponsa (“bride”) for the male’s breeding plumage — iridescent green and purple head with white stripes and swept-back crest, burgundy-chestnut breast with white spots, buff flanks, multi-colored bill. An almost impossible combination of colors and patterns. Every surface is iridescent, patterned, edged, or spotted. The plumage does not suggest “colorful” — it suggests “impossible.” Where the Belted Kingfisher (degree 57) showed reversed dimorphism (female more colorful), the Wood Duck restores conventional dimorphism at a level of elaboration no previous engine species has approached.

Cavity Nesting: The Tree-Dwelling Duck

One of few North American ducks that nests in tree cavities — connecting the Wood Duck to old-growth forest ecology. Requires large natural cavities or abandoned woodpecker holes in mature trees within ~500m of water. Clutch of 7–15 eggs (average 10–12). “Dump nesting” (multiple females using the same cavity) can produce nests with 20–30+ eggs. Incubation 28–37 days by female only. Dependence on tree cavities drove the species’ historical decline when old-growth was logged — and drove its recovery when nest box programs provided artificial cavities.

The Leap of Faith

24–48 hours after hatching, Wood Duck ducklings — tennis-ball-sized, covered in down, unable to fly — climb to the cavity entrance (2–20 meters high) and jump. They plummet to the ground or water below, surviving because their low mass (~25–30 g) keeps terminal velocity survivable. The mother calls from below. All ducklings jump within 15–30 minutes. The most dramatic natal event of any species in the engine. Parallel to Death Cab for Cutie’s leap from Barsuk to Atlantic Records — following a call from outside the cavity into an uncertain larger world.

The Comeback: Near-Extinction to Abundance

By the early 1900s, market hunting, old-growth logging, and lack of legal protection had driven the Wood Duck toward extinction. The Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, community-driven nest box programs (1930s onward), and beaver recovery (creating new pond habitat) fueled one of North America’s greatest conservation success stories. Today the Wood Duck is abundant throughout its range. The nest box program — a simple, scalable intervention — compensated for the loss of natural cavities and proved that community conservation works.

The Bridal Waterfowl

Aix sponsa — from Ancient Greek aix (a waterfowl mentioned by Aristotle but never precisely identified) + Latin sponsa (“bride, betrothed woman”). Full translation: “Aristotle’s waterfowl in bridal dress.” Named by Linnaeus in 1758. The genus Aix contains only two species worldwide: the Wood Duck (North America) and the Mandarin Duck (East Asia) — both cavity nesters, both spectacularly plumaged, both representing the aesthetic apex of Anatidae. Compare the Belted Kingfisher’s Megaceryle alcyon (“great halcyon kingfisher”): the kingfisher is named for mythology; the Wood Duck is named for beauty.

The Sound: Ascending Whistle

Female alarm/flight call: A distinctive, ascending “jeeee” or “oo-eek” sweeping upward from ~1,500 to ~4,500 Hz in 0.5–1.0 seconds. Thin, breathy, slightly squeezed — surprising for a duck. Often given in rapid series of 2–4 calls when flushed. Male whistle: A thin, finch-like ascending “jib” or “zeee,” 2,000–5,000 Hz, heard mainly at close range. Both sexes’ vocalizations are atypical for ducks — whistled rather than quacked.

Vocalizations are innate — no cultural learning, no geographic dialects. Like the Band-tailed Pigeon’s coo (degree 56) and the Belted Kingfisher’s rattle (degree 57), the Wood Duck’s calls are genetically determined. Three consecutive non-passerine species, all with innate vocalizations — but three maximally different acoustic characters: descending, flat, ascending.

Plumage: The Painted Aristocrat

Male: Iridescent green-and-purple head with swept-back crest, white chin strap, bold white facial lines. Red eye, multi-colored bill. Burgundy-chestnut breast with white triangular spots. Buff flanks separated by black-and-white vertical bar. Dark iridescent back. Widely considered the most beautiful duck in North America.

Female: Gray-brown with bold white teardrop-shaped eye ring — diagnostic field mark. Short crest. Warm brown flanks. Dark gray bill. The eye ring immediately separates her from all other female ducks in the PNW.

Eclipse molt: After breeding, the male sheds his spectacular plumage and becomes temporarily flightless, wearing dull female-like “eclipse” plumage for several weeks. The transformation from the most spectacular to near-invisible and back is the most dramatic seasonal plumage cycle of any species in the engine.

Habitat: The Wooded Pond

A species of wooded waterways — lowland ponds, beaver ponds, oxbow lakes, slow-moving streams, and wooded swamps surrounded by mature forest. Requirements: still or slow water (for feeding and brood-rearing), adjacent forest (for cavity nesting), emergent vegetation and woody debris (for cover). In PNW: breeding resident on lowland waterways throughout western Washington and Oregon. Present at urban ponds with adequate tree cover (Green Lake, Seward Park, Washington Park Arboretum). Exact match to the Red-legged Frog’s original lowland pond habitat.

The Death Cab Parallel

Degree 58 pairs the Wood Duck with Death Cab for Cutie in Part A. Parallel resonances: (1) Hidden-to-visible — Death Cab from Bellingham dorm rooms to major label; Wood Duck from hidden forest pond to spectacular sunlit emergence. (2) The leap of faith — ducklings jumping from tree cavities; Death Cab jumping from Barsuk to Atlantic. (3) Geographic specificity — Death Cab’s Washington landscapes; the Wood Duck’s wooded waterways. (4) Barsuk trio — degree 58 is the fulcrum of the 57–58–59 Barsuk trio in Part A, just as the Wood Duck is the fulcrum between flowing water (kingfisher) and whatever comes next.

Theory Nodes

W

The Pond Whistle foundational

The 59th acoustic element. Thin, ascending whistle sweeping upward from ~1,500 to ~4,500 Hz — a tonal ascent from still water. Completes a pitch-contour triad: descending coo (56), flat rattle (57), ascending whistle (58). Maps still water, complementing the stream rattle’s flowing water. Music-theory parallel: the rising appoggiatura — a grace note ascending into the main note, a question resolving upward.

A

Anatidae Enters — 25th Family, 8th Order foundational

First waterfowl in 58 degrees. New order Anseriformes. Third consecutive new order (56 Columbiformes, 57 Coraciiformes, 58 Anseriformes). Largest family by global count (~174 spp). First swimming species. Brings cavity nesting, spectacular plumage, ascending whistle. Music-theory parallel: the modulation — a shift in the entire tonal framework, opening harmonic possibilities that did not exist before.

B

The Bridal Dress foundational

Named sponsa (“bride”) for the male’s ornate breeding plumage. Iridescent greens, purples, chestnuts, whites. The aesthetic apex of North American waterfowl. Where the kingfisher (57) showed reversed dimorphism, the Wood Duck restores conventional dimorphism at unprecedented elaboration. Music-theory parallel: the orchestral tutti — every instrument at full voice, every color deployed.

C

Cavity Nesting — The Tree-Dwelling Duck foundational

Nests in tree cavities, linking forest ecology to aquatic ecology. Requires old-growth/mature trees near water. Cavity dependence drove historical decline and recovery (nest box programs). Connects to Spotted Owl (degree 4) and Sapsucker (degree 54) through old-growth dependence. Music-theory parallel: the prepared piano — a standard instrument modified for arboreal life.

L

The Leap of Faith foundational

Ducklings jump from tree cavities 2–20 meters high, 24–48 hours after hatching. Tennis-ball-sized, unable to fly, following their mother’s call into the unknown. Parallels Death Cab’s Barsuk-to-Atlantic leap. The most dramatic natal event in the engine. Music-theory parallel: the fermata followed by the drop — held breath at the top, then the resolution plunging downward.

E

Eclipse Molt intermediate

After breeding, the male sheds spectacular plumage for dull female-like “eclipse” plumage. Temporarily flightless, cryptic, gathering on secluded ponds. The most dramatic seasonal transformation in the engine — from the most spectacular to near-invisible and back. Music-theory parallel: the attacca — the pause between movements where instruments retune and character transforms.

R

The Comeback intermediate

Near-extinction by 1900s from market hunting + logging. Recovery through Migratory Bird Treaty Act, nest box programs, and beaver pond recovery. One of North America’s greatest conservation success stories. Music-theory parallel: the reprise — a theme returning after long absence, transformed by the journey.

F

Three Freshwater Specialists intermediate

Dipper (52, fast streams), Kingfisher (57, cold streams), Wood Duck (58, still ponds). Three specialists spanning the entire freshwater gradient from torrent to pond. The engine now maps the complete freshwater landscape through species.

P

Pitch-Contour Triad intermediate

Three consecutive non-passerine voices, three different pitch contours. Coo descends (56). Rattle holds flat (57). Whistle ascends (58). Three consecutive new orders, three families, three contours. Structurally unprecedented.

D

The Death Cab Parallel intermediate

Hidden-to-visible (dorm rooms to major label; forest pond to sunlit spectacle). The leap of faith (ducklings from cavities; band from Barsuk). Geographic specificity (Washington landscapes; wooded waterways). Barsuk trio fulcrum (57–58–59).

G

Garry Oak Connection intermediate

Autumn/winter acorn dependence links the pond-dwelling duck to Garry oak prairie — one of the most endangered PNW ecosystems (~5% remaining). The Wood Duck’s health indicates both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystem integrity. Music-theory parallel: the pedal point — a sustained note connecting two registers.

SPS-056: Band-tailed Pigeon

THREE CONSECUTIVE NEW ORDERS. PITCH-CONTOUR TRIAD. Forest coo descends (56, Columbiformes, E=1), stream rattle holds flat (57, Coraciiformes, E=3), pond whistle ascends (58, Anseriformes, E=3). Three families, three orders, three pitch contours. Unprecedented on all counts.

SPS-000: Great Blue Heron

TWO WATERSIDE SPECIES. The heron (degree 0, wading in shallows, patience as strategy) and the Wood Duck (degree 58, swimming on ponds, concealment as strategy). Both associated with still or slow water. The first and most recent waterside entries in the engine.

SPS-004: Northern Spotted Owl

TWO OLD-GROWTH CAVITY DEPENDENTS. The owl (degree 4, Strigiformes, nests in old-growth platforms/cavities) and the Wood Duck (degree 58, Anseriformes, nests in tree cavities). Both require mature forest. Both impacted by old-growth logging.

SPS-054: Red-breasted Sapsucker

WOODPECKER-DUCK CAVITY CONNECTION. The sapsucker creates cavities; the Wood Duck uses them. Pileated Woodpecker and sapsucker holes provide nesting sites for Wood Ducks. The woodpecker guild builds the infrastructure the duck requires.

SPS-052: American Dipper

THREE FRESHWATER SPECIALISTS. Dipper (52, fast mountain streams), Kingfisher (57, cold streams), Wood Duck (58, still lowland ponds). Torrent to stream to pond. The engine maps the complete freshwater gradient.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Wood Duck breeds on lowland ponds and wooded waterways throughout western Washington and Oregon. Cavity nester in mature trees. Breeding resident with year-round presence. First waterfowl in the engine. Nest box conservation success story.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Forest-water interface species. Old-growth cavity dependence. Garry oak acorn connection to endangered ecosystem. Seed dispersal for aquatic plants. Conservation comeback from near-extinction. Beaver pond habitat mutualism.
