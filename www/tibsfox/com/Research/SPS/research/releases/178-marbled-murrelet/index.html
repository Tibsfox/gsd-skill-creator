Marbled Murrelet — “Keer Keer”

Brachyramphus marmoratus (Alcidae) — E=4 — The Old-Growth Mystery
FIFTH ALCIDAE (174–178). A seabird that nests in old-growth forest — sometimes 50+ miles from the coast. Nest not discovered until 1974. “Keer keer” echoes through ancient trees at dawn. Threatened. The forest-sea connection. E=4. v1.49.318.

Old-Growth Nester — A Seabird in the Trees

The Marbled Murrelet nests on large mossy branches of ancient conifers — Douglas-fir, Sitka spruce, western hemlock. 30–60 meters above the ground. No nest structure — just the mossy branch. One egg. The chick sits motionless, cryptically colored, until ready to fly directly to the sea. The most extreme habitat split of any PNW bird.

The 1974 Discovery — Last Nest Found in North America

For decades, ornithologists knew the Marbled Murrelet was common at sea but could not find its nest. In 1974, a tree surgeon found a chick on a Douglas-fir branch in Big Basin Redwoods State Park, California. One of the last North American birds to have its nest described. A century of mystery, solved by a tree climber.

Threatened Status & Timber Conflict

Listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act in 1992. The bird’s dependence on old-growth forest puts it directly in conflict with timber harvesting. Alongside the Northern Spotted Owl, the Marbled Murrelet became a flagship species in the Pacific Northwest timber wars. A living connection between forest and sea.
