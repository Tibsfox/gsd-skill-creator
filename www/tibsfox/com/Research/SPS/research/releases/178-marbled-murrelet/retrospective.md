*Degree 178 retrospective complete. MARBLED MURRELET at the one hundred seventy-eighth degree -- THE OLD-GROWTH MYSTERY. The Marbled Murrelet is a seabird that nests in old-growth forest. This single sentence contains the most extraordinary nesting adaptation in Pacific Northwest ornithology. A bird that feeds at sea, that dives for fish, that belongs to the auk family -- nests on mossy branches of ancient conifers, sometimes 50 or more miles from the coast. The nest was not described until 1974, when a tree surgeon found a chick on a Douglas-fir branch in California. One of the last North American birds to have its nest described. The "keer keer" call echoes through ancient forest at dawn as the bird commutes between sea and canopy -- 50+ miles each way, daily, during breeding season. Listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act in 1992, the Marbled Murrelet sits at the intersection of the Pacific Northwest timber wars: every acre of old-growth logged is potential nesting habitat destroyed. Alongside the Northern Spotted Owl, the murrelet became a flagship species for old-growth conservation. The bird is a living proof that forest and sea are connected -- a marine bird that needs ancient trees. Fifth Alcidae in a streak (174-178) that has now documented five distinct nesting strategies: crevice, nocturnal burrow, cliff burrow, open cliff ledge, and tree branch. The diversity within one family is extraordinary. And across the degree pair, High Performance from Tacoma represents the same principle: the hidden nester, thriving in unexpected habitat, found where you wouldn't look. The hidden nester. v1.49.318.*
