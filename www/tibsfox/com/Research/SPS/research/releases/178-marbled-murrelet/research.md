### The Most Extreme Nesting Contrast

Every other Alcidae nests at or near the ocean. The Marbled Murrelet nests in TREES -- sometimes more than 50 miles inland. This is the most extreme nesting habitat contrast in any bird family:

- **From cliff to canopy:** After four Alcidae that nest on rocks, in burrows, and on cliffs, the Marbled Murrelet nests on large mossy branches of old-growth conifers. The contrast is staggering.
- **Dual habitat:** The bird feeds at sea during the day but commutes to inland forest nesting sites. Some commutes are 50+ miles each way. A seabird, flying through forest at dawn and dusk.
- **Discovery timeline:** The nest was not described until 1974, when a tree surgeon found a chick on a Douglas-fir branch in Big Basin Redwoods State Park, California. Before that, the nesting habits of this common seabird were completely unknown.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Nelson & Hamer 1995; engine internal analysis)

## E=4 -- THE DAWN COMMUTER

### Why E=4

The Marbled Murrelet's sound registers at E=4:

- **Call character:** The "keer keer" call is high-pitched, sharp, and penetrating. It cuts through forest ambient noise effectively. But it is not loud in absolute terms -- this is a small bird (25 cm, 220g) producing a proportionally small sound.
- **Context intensity:** The call gains its power from context. Hearing a seabird call in old-growth forest, at dawn, 50 miles from the ocean, is one of the most unexpected and eerie experiences in PNW birding. The sound is moderate; the setting is extraordinary.
- **Flight calls:** The "keer keer" is given primarily during flight -- the dawn and dusk commutes between forest and sea. The call echoes through the canopy, bouncing off ancient trunks.
- **Why not E=5?** The call is sharp but small. The bird is small. The volume does not reach the levels of the murre colony or the puffin's deep grunt. E=4 reflects a penetrating, contextually powerful call from a small seabird.

## THE OLD-GROWTH MYSTERY

### A Century of Unknown Nesting

The Marbled Murrelet's nesting mystery is one of the great ornithological detective stories:

- **Pre-1974:** Ornithologists knew the Marbled Murrelet was common at sea along the Pacific Coast. They knew it had to nest somewhere. But no one could find a nest. The bird was so secretive about its nesting behavior that the nest remained undescribed for decades after the species was formally named.
- **The 1974 discovery:** A tree surgeon working in Big Basin Redwoods State Park, California, found a downy chick sitting on a large mossy branch of an old Douglas-fir. This was the first confirmed North American nest -- one of the last bird nests to be discovered in North America.
- **Why old-growth?** The murrelet needs large, flat, mossy branches for nesting -- the kind found only on ancient trees. Young forests lack the structural complexity. The bird lays a single egg on a mossy branch platform, often 30-60 meters above the ground. No nest structure is built -- just the mossy branch.
- **Single egg:** Like other Alcidae, one egg per attempt. The chick is cryptically colored and sits motionless on the branch until it is ready to fly directly to the sea -- no intermediate period on the ground.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Ralph et al. 1995; Nelson & Hamer 1995; Piatt et al. 2007; engine internal analysis)

## THREATENED STATUS AND THE TIMBER WARS

### Conservation Battleground

The Marbled Murrelet is at the center of Pacific Northwest conservation conflict:

- **Threatened status:** Listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act in 1992 (Washington, Oregon, California population). Declining due to old-growth forest loss.
- **Timber connection:** The bird's dependence on old-growth forest puts it directly in conflict with timber harvesting. Every acre of old-growth logged is potential murrelet nesting habitat lost. The bird became a flagship species in the Pacific Northwest timber wars alongside the Northern Spotted Owl.
- **Population decline:** Marbled Murrelet populations have declined significantly. At-sea surveys show reduced numbers across the range. Nesting success is low. The combination of forest loss and marine prey changes threatens the species from both ends of its habitat.
- **PNW significance:** In the Pacific Northwest, the Marbled Murrelet is a living connection between forest and sea. It embodies the ecological truth that these systems are not separate -- a seabird needs ancient trees, and the forest needs the ocean's productivity.

(Sources: USFWS ESA listing 1992; Ralph et al. 1995; Piatt et al. 2007; PNW conservation; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Marbled Murrelet

**"Keer Keer" Flight Call:**
- **Trigger:** Dawn and dusk commute flights between forest and sea. Contact. Alarm.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** High-pitched, sharp, two-syllable "keer keer." Penetrating. Cuts through forest ambient. Given in flight, echoing through old-growth canopy.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 4000-7000 Hz. High-pitched for a seabird.
- **Duration:** Each "keer" approximately 0.15-0.25 seconds. Repeated 2-6 times in sequence.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Penetrating but not loud. Small bird, proportional volume. Context amplifies -- hearing this in forest is extraordinary.

**Nestling Call:**
- Soft peeping from chick on branch. Barely audible from ground level.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
