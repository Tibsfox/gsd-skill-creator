Calliope Hummingbird

Selasphorus calliope
soft rising tsee-tsee — the filament, the smallest bird in North America, named for the muse of epic poetry

The 38th Acoustic Element: The Filament

A thin, barely audible thread of high-pitched vocal sound produced by the smallest bird in North America (~3.1 g, 7–8 cm), so quiet it is almost lost in the ambient noise of a mountain meadow. After the thrum (35, continuous aeromechanical hum) and the sonic boom (36, explosive dive chirp at 385 BL/s), the third hummingbird whispers. The filament is the pilot light — the smallest flame that still burns.

The trio’s diminuendo: thrum (35, E=5) → sonic boom (36, E=5) → filament (37, E=4). Engine → afterburner → candle flame.

The Hummingbird Trio — Unprecedented

Three consecutive Trochilidae. No family has ever held three consecutive degrees. Three species, two genera, three strategies:

  • Rufous (35): Selasphorus. E=5. Thrum. Flower meadow. Fighter.
  • Anna’s (36): Calypte. E=5. Sonic boom. Urban/coastal. Resident.
  • Calliope (37): Selasphorus. E=4. Filament. Mountain meadow. Infiltrator.

Genus pair: Selasphorus bookends Calypte — ABA framing. Two flame-bearers flanking a veiled jewel.

The Smallest Bird in North America

~3.1 g average body mass. 7–8 cm total length. Smaller than a Rufous (3.4 g). Smaller than a chickadee. A nickel weighs 5 grams — the Calliope weighs less than two-thirds of a nickel. And this bird migrates ~5,000 miles round trip from PNW mountain meadows to the highlands of Mexico — the smallest long-distance migrant in the world.

Taxonomy: Calliope’s Flame-Bearer

Selasphorus calliope — “Calliope’s flame-bearer.” Selasphorus = same genus as Rufous Hummingbird (Greek selas + phoros = “flame-bearer”). Calliope = Greek Kalliope, the muse of epic poetry, “beautiful voice.” The grandest muse names the smallest bird.

Formerly Stellula calliope (“Calliope’s little star”). Molecular phylogenetics merged Stellula into Selasphorus in 2017. The little star is now officially a flame-bearer — phylogenetically correct, poetically bittersweet.

The Calliope Paradox

Calliope presided over the Iliad and the Odyssey — the grandest form of literary expression. The bird that bears her name weighs less than a nickel and produces a sound barely louder than the wind. Smallest bird, grandest name.

And yet: this bird’s story IS an epic. Breeds at 3,000 m, nests in a cup the size of a thimble, migrates 5,000 miles at 3 grams. The muse of epic poetry would recognize the subject. The filament carries a story that would fill books.

The Sound: Soft Rising Tsee-Tsee

A soft, high-pitched tsee or tsee-tsee, approximately 6–10 kHz [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Higher in pitch and softer in volume than the Rufous’s chitter or Anna’s metallic chip. Barely carries 10 meters in a mountain meadow. No complex learned song — unlike Anna’s. Simple, presumably innate calls. The flame-bearers (Selasphorus) do not sing.

Shuttle Display (Not Dive)

The Calliope performs a horizontal shuttle display — back-and-forth flights in front of the female — instead of the dramatic vertical dives used by Rufous and Anna’s. The shuttle produces a quiet bzt or zing from wing feathers. No explosive chirp. No 385 BL/s plunge. The smallest bird displays close, quiet, and persistent — a pendulum, not a cannon shot.

Mountain Meadow — High Elevation Specialist

Breeds at 1,200–3,400 m in subalpine meadows of the Cascades, Olympics, and Blue Mountains. Indian paintbrush, penstemon, columbine — red and orange tubular wildflowers of the subalpine zone. The engine’s second montane ecosystem (after mountain conifer at degree 33).

Elevation partitioning: Anna’s holds the lowlands (0–500 m). Rufous ranges broadly (0–3,000+ m). Calliope specializes at the mountaintops (1,200–3,400 m). Three hummingbirds map the full PNW elevation gradient.

Selasphorus Bookends — ABA Genus Framing

Degrees 35 and 37 share genus Selasphorus. Degree 36 (Calypte) sits between them. The flame-bearers bookend the veiled jewel:

  • 35 Selasphorus rufus: Loud, aggressive, broad-range. The torch.
  • 36 Calypte anna: Spectacular, resident, learned singer. The jewel.
  • 37 Selasphorus calliope: Quiet, montane, infiltrator. The candle.

Same genus, different everything else. The loud flame-bearer and the quiet flame-bearer.

E=4 — The Diminuendo

After back-to-back E=5 (degrees 35–36), energy returns to E=4. The smallest bird makes less noise. E=4 now at 14 degrees (37.8%). E=5 stays at 3 degrees (8.1%). E=3 still absent for 17 degrees (since degree 20). The dynamic range compression is not resolved — but the drop from E=5 to E=4 is a step in the right direction.
