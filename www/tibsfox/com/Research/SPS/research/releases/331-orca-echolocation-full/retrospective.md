## What Worked

1. **The three-component framework is the entry's primary contribution.** Previous orca entries documented specific behaviors (greeting ceremony, cross-species encounter). Degree 331 documents the complete repertoire: navigation (clicks), social communication (whistles/calls), and physical expression (breach). The tripartite framing is analytically complete.
2. **The buzz documentation is specific and accurate.** 300+ clicks per second at prey approach, audible through hydrophone as a chainsaw-like sound, completely inaudible to Chinook salmon (whose hearing ceiling is ~1 kHz). The predator's frequency advantage with no prey countermeasure is an important finding.
3. **The Haro Strait waveguide context adds acoustic precision.** The channel geometry as acoustic amplifier for orca communication is a specific and well-documented physical phenomenon. It explains why the San Juan Islands are the primary SRKW research site.
4. **The SRKW conservation status is accurately and specifically documented.** 73 individuals, 3 pods, every individual identified, ESA endangered 2005, Tahlequah 2018. The conservation context is essential for any SRKW entry.
5. **Chinook salmon cannot detect orca echolocation above ~1 kHz.** This is the entry's most striking acoustic insight: the predator's sonar is completely invisible to its primary prey. The orca's frequency advantage has no evolutionary countermeasure possible in the salmon lineage.

## Discoveries

1. **The click source level (160-220 dB re 1 µPa) makes orca clicks among the loudest biological sounds in the ocean.** This is a counterintuitive finding: the most acoustically powerful single sounds produced by the SRKW are ultrasonic and inaudible to humans. The loudest orca sounds are the ones we can't hear.
2. **Serial breaching by multiple pod members suggests a social contagion model.** When one orca breaches and others follow in sequence, the behavior resembles the contagious yawning or laughing observed in primates -- a socially transmitted behavior pattern. The acoustic cascade of serial breaching (each impact audible miles away) is the orca's most spectacular social acoustic event.
3. **The SRKW photo-ID catalog since 1976 is one of the longest-running individual wildlife identification programs in the world.** The 47-year (as of 2023) continuous record of individual identity makes the SRKW the best-documented wild cetacean population in history.
