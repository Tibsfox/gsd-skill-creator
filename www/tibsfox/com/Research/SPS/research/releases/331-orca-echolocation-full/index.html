The Complete Orca Acoustic Repertoire in One Sequence

Previous orca entries in the engine have documented specific behaviors. Degree 331 documents the complete three-component acoustic sequence: click trains (biological sonar, 40–120 kHz), signature whistles and pod calls (social communication, 1–18 kHz), and breach impact (physical percussion, 120+ dB underwater). The Southern Resident Killer Whale in Haro Strait: all channels open. The most complete orca acoustic documentation in the engine.

The Three Components

1
Echolocation Click Trains Produced by phonic lips in nasal passage; focused into forward beam by melon (fatty forehead organ). Frequency: 40–120 kHz (ultrasonic, requires hydrophone). Source level: 160–220 dB re 1 µPa. Scan mode: 1–2 clicks/sec. Buzz (prey capture approach): 300+ clicks/sec. Detection range: 100–300m. Chinook salmon detectable at ~100–150m.
2
Signature Whistles + Pod Calls Social communication: 1–18 kHz (audible range). Signature whistles: individual-specific, functioning as personal identity markers. Discrete calls: pod-specific vocalizations shared by all pod members (SRKW "dialects" documented by Ford and Ellis, 1970s–80s). J-pod, K-pod, L-pod each have distinct call repertoires. Social coordination and long-distance pod cohesion in low-visibility water.
3
Breach Impact Adult male (6–8m, 6,000–8,000 kg) exits water fully, rotates airborne, re-enters with maximum surface contact. Underwater impact: 120–160 dB re 1 µPa (wideband). Airborne splash: 80–100 dB at 100m. Audible several miles in still conditions. Visible up to 5+ miles. Function debated: communication, parasite removal, prey herding, social bonding. Often performed in series by multiple pod members.

The Southern Resident Killer Whale population is one of the most intensively studied wildlife populations on Earth. Every individual is identified, named, and tracked by the Center for Whale Research. The population peaked at 98 individuals in 1995 and has declined since, primarily due to reduced Chinook salmon availability. J-pod uses the inland Salish Sea (Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, Haro Strait) year-round; K- and L-pods range more widely to the outer coast.

In 2018, orca J35 (Tahlequah) carried her dead calf for 17 days across hundreds of miles of Pacific Northwest waters — a documented act of grief behavior that focused worldwide attention on the SRKW population crisis and the importance of Chinook salmon recovery for their survival.

The Buzz — Prey Capture in Acoustic Detail

When a Southern Resident locates a Chinook salmon using long-range scan clicks (1–2/sec), the approach begins. As the orca closes to within 100–150 meters of the fish, the click rate accelerates. At approximately 10–30 meters, the rate reaches 300+ clicks per second — the "buzz." The buzz is audible to human observers near a hydrophone as a sound like a chainsaw or a rapidly vibrating membrane. The fish hears nothing useful; Chinook salmon cannot detect sounds above approximately 1 kHz. The orca's 100 kHz sonar is completely inaudible to its primary prey.

Haro Strait — The Narrow Channel Acoustic Amplifier

Haro Strait, between San Juan Island (WA) and Vancouver Island (BC), is the primary feeding corridor for the Southern Resident Killer Whale population during summer salmon runs. The strait is 3–8 km wide and 50–100+ meters deep — a narrow channel whose geometry creates a waveguide effect that enhances horizontal sound propagation. Orca calls recorded in Haro Strait travel farther and with less distortion than in open ocean conditions. The channel is an acoustic amplifier for the SRKW's communication system, which is one reason the San Juan Islands are the most productive location for SRKW acoustic research.
