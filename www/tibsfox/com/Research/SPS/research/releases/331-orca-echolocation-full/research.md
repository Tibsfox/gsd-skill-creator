## THE THREE COMPONENTS -- THE COMPLETE ACOUSTIC REPERTOIRE

### Click Trains, Signature Whistles, and Breach

The full orca acoustic sequence at degree 331:

- **Component 1: Echolocation click trains.** The primary navigation and prey-detection system. Click trains are produced by the phonic lips (also called "monkey lips") in the nasal passage, focused into a beam by the melon (the fatty organ in the forehead), and projected forward into the water column. Returning echoes are received through the lower jaw's fatty tissue, which transmits vibrations to the inner ear. The click rate varies with function: slow clicks (1-2 per second) for long-range scanning; rapid click trains (100-300+ per second, the "buzz") for prey capture.
- **Component 2: Signature whistles and calls.** The primary communication system. Resident orcas use two distinct call types: (a) signature whistles -- individual-specific sounds in the 1-18 kHz range that function as personal identifiers (analogous to names); and (b) discrete calls -- pod-specific vocalizations that all members of a pod share, used for coordination and cohesion. Southern Resident pods (J, K, L pods) each have distinct call repertoires -- a pod-specific "dialect" that researchers can identify from acoustic recordings alone. Call types are designated by letters and numbers (J-pod call type 1, 2, 3, etc.).
- **Component 3: Breach impact.** A full breach -- an orca leaping clear of the water, rotating, and re-entering with maximum body surface contact -- produces one of the highest-amplitude single-event sounds documented from a marine mammal. An adult male orca (body length 6-8 meters, mass 6,000-8,000 kg) striking the water surface generates an impact sound of 120-160 dB underwater at close range, with wideband acoustic output extending from near-DC to several thousand Hz. The breach is visible and audible from several miles; it is the most spectacular acoustic event in the orca's behavioral repertoire.

## ECHOLOCATION -- THE BIOLOGICAL SONAR

### Click Train Mechanics and Functional Range

Orca echolocation is the most sophisticated biological sonar system in the engine:

- **Click production.** Orcas produce clicks using the phonic lips (also called "monkey lips" by researchers due to their appearance), a pair of fatty-tissue structures in the nasal passage that can be pinched together and released rapidly to produce click pulses. The clicks are high-frequency -- centered between 40 and 120 kHz -- far above the human audible range (20 Hz - 20 kHz). Each click is extremely brief (milliseconds) and extremely loud at the source: click source levels of 160-220 dB re 1 µPa at 1m have been measured, making orca clicks among the loudest biological sounds produced in the ocean.
- **The melon.** The melon -- the fatty, rounded forehead of the orca -- functions as an acoustic lens. Different fats within the melon have different acoustic impedances, creating a gradient that focuses the click energy into a forward-directed beam. The beam pattern of orca echolocation is approximately 20-30 degrees wide -- directional enough to provide detailed information about objects in the path, broad enough to cover a significant scanning field.
- **Echo processing.** Returning echoes travel through the ocean, strike the orca's lower jaw, and are conducted through the panbone -- a thin, semi-transparent bone in the lower jaw -- to the acoustic fat body that connects to the inner ear. The jaw hearing mechanism allows the orca to receive directional echoes with high spatial resolution.
- **The buzz.** When an orca has located prey and is approaching for capture, the click rate increases dramatically to 300+ clicks per second -- so fast that the individual clicks overlap in time. The resulting acoustic output is the "buzz," which sounds to human observers like a chainsaw or a rapidly vibrating membrane. The buzz phase is the final approach in prey capture and the highest-information phase of echolocation.
- **Functional range.** Orca echolocation at 100 kHz (the approximate center of the frequency band) can detect targets at ranges of 200-300 meters under good conditions. Chinook salmon (the Southern Resident Killer Whale's preferred prey) are detectable at approximately 100-150 meters, providing sufficient advance notice for the prey-approach sequence.

## SIGNATURE WHISTLES AND CALLS

### The Social Acoustic System of the Southern Residents

The orca's communicative repertoire:

- **Southern Resident Killer Whale (SRKW) pods.** The Southern Resident population consists of three family groups (pods): J-pod, K-pod, and L-pod. J-pod typically uses the inland waters of Puget Sound and the San Juan Islands year-round. K- and L-pods range more widely, reaching the outer coast. The population numbered 73 individuals as of 2023 -- critically endangered, the subject of intensive research and conservation effort.
- **Pod-specific dialects.** Each SRKW pod has a specific repertoire of discrete calls -- acoustic patterns that all members of the pod share and that are distinct from the calls of other pods. These "dialects" were first documented by researchers John Ford and Graeme Ellis in the 1970s and 1980s and have been studied intensively since. Pod calls function for cohesion and coordination; they allow pod members to identify each other at distance in low-visibility conditions.
- **Signature whistles as individual identity.** Within the call repertoire, each individual orca appears to have a signature whistle -- a specific acoustic pattern that functions as a personal identifier. Signature whistles have been most thoroughly documented in bottlenose dolphins, but evidence for individual-specific vocalizations in orcas exists. The signature whistle allows individual recognition in a pod with complex social structure.
- **Call frequency range.** SRKW discrete calls occur in the 1-18 kHz range -- primarily within the human audible range. These calls are the most widely heard orca vocalizations: they are the sounds recorded by hydrophone arrays in Puget Sound and broadcast in nature documentaries. The clicks (40-120 kHz) are largely inaudible to humans without electronic down-conversion.
- **The acoustic environment of the narrow channel.** In the narrow channels of the San Juan Islands -- particularly Haro Strait, the primary feeding corridor for SRKWs pursuing Chinook salmon -- sound propagates efficiently. The channel depth (50-100 meters in the main channels) and width (3-8 km) create a waveguide effect that enhances the horizontal propagation of low-to-mid frequency sounds. Orca calls travel farther in narrow channels than in open ocean conditions.

(Sources: SRKW J K L pod population; J-pod inland waters year-round; K L pod wider range; 73 individuals 2023 population critically endangered; pod-specific dialects John Ford Graeme Ellis 1970s-80s; discrete calls for cohesion and coordination; signature whistles individual identity; bottlenose dolphin signature whistle comparison; SRKW discrete calls 1-18 kHz; Haro Strait feeding corridor; San Juan Islands narrow channel waveguide effect; channel depth 50-100m)

## BREACH -- THE MAXIMUM ACOUSTIC EVENT

### The Physical Percussion of a Six-Ton Jump

The breach and its acoustic dimensions:

- **Breach mechanics.** A full breach is a dramatic behavior: the orca accelerates vertically from depth, emerges completely from the water (clearing the surface entirely with its body), rotates, and re-enters the water with maximum body surface area contact. Adult male orcas, the largest individual in the population at 6-8 meters and 6,000-8,000 kg, produce the most spectacular breach events. The approach velocity needed to clear the water surface entirely requires significant acceleration from depth -- the orca accelerates to approximately 5-8 m/s vertically at the moment of surface exit.
- **The airborne moment.** A fully breaching adult male orca is 6-8 meters of mass rotating in the air -- a spectacular visual event visible from miles. The splash on re-entry is audible from similar distances in still conditions. The re-entry splash produces the primary acoustic event: the impact of thousands of kilograms of animal with the water surface.
- **Acoustic impact.** Breach impact generates wideband underwater sound at 120-160 dB re 1 µPa (a standard unit of underwater sound pressure). This is among the highest-amplitude single-event biological sounds in the ocean -- comparable to or exceeding the sounds produced by many other marine mammals in any single event. In air, the splash is audible at 80-100 dB at 100 meters.
- **Behavioral function.** The functional purpose of breaching is debated. Hypotheses include: (1) communication -- the impact is audible at great distance and may signal location or emotional state to other pod members; (2) parasite removal -- the impact of re-entry may dislodge ectoparasites; (3) prey herding -- the acoustic impact may startle and concentrate fish below the surface; (4) play and social bonding -- breaching is often performed by multiple pod members in succession, suggesting a social function. The debate is unresolved; the behavior may be multifunctional.

(Sources: Full breach mechanics adult male 6-8m 6000-8000 kg; exit velocity 5-8 m/s vertical; airborne orca visual distance visibility; breach impact underwater 120-160 dB re 1 µPa; airborne splash 80-100 dB at 100m; breach function hypotheses: communication, parasite removal, prey herding, play/social bonding; multi-member serial breaching social function evidence)

## THE SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALE -- CONSERVATION CRISIS

### The Most Studied Wild Orca Population on Earth

The SRKW population's status and significance:

- **Population decline.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale population peaked at 98 individuals in 1995. By 2023, the population has declined to approximately 73 individuals. The primary limiting factor is prey availability -- SRKWs are specialists on Chinook salmon, and Chinook salmon populations in the Pacific Northwest have declined dramatically due to habitat loss, hydropower dam operation, and other anthropogenic factors.
- **Individual recognition.** The SRKW population is one of the few wildlife populations in the world where every living individual is identified, named, and tracked. The Center for Whale Research has maintained a photo-identification catalog of all living SRKWs since 1976. Each individual has a designated identification code (J2, J16, K20, L94, etc.) and often an informal name used in public communication (Granny for J2, Tahlequah for J35, etc.).
- **Tahlequah and the 17-day grief tour.** In 2018, orca J35 (Tahlequah) carried her dead calf for 17 days across hundreds of miles of Pacific Northwest waters -- an act of mourning or behavioral persistence that attracted worldwide attention and became a symbol of the SRKW population's crisis. The event demonstrated the depth of social bonds within orca families and the difficulty of reproduction in a population under nutritional stress.
- **The J-pod year-round presence.** J-pod, the smallest of the three SRKW pods, uses the inland waters of the Salish Sea (Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, Strait of Georgia) year-round. Its presence at degree 331's narrow channel setting -- the Haro Strait and its adjacent channels -- is documented by the Center for Whale Research's continuous monitoring program.
- **NOAA Endangered Species Act listing.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale was listed as endangered under the Endangered Species Act in 2005. Recovery efforts include: Chinook salmon recovery programs, vessel approach distance regulations (minimum 300 yards), noise pollution reduction in SRKW habitat, and ongoing research.

(Sources: SRKW population peak 98 individuals 1995; decline to ~73 individuals 2023; Chinook salmon as primary prey; salmon population decline dam operation habitat loss; Center for Whale Research photo-ID catalog 1976-present; individual designation codes J2 K20 L94; Tahlequah J35 17-day grief tour 2018 calf mortality; J-pod year-round Salish Sea; Haro Strait continuous monitoring; NOAA ESA listing 2005; 300-yard vessel approach regulation)

## THE FULL SEQUENCE -- E=7 ACOUSTIC INTEGRATION

### Why the Three Components Together Reach E=7

The complete orca acoustic sequence at E=7:

- **E=7 calibration.** The full three-component orca acoustic sequence -- click trains, signature whistles, breach -- reaches E=7 as a complete integrated event. No single component alone would reach E=7: clicks are ultrasonic and largely inaudible without equipment; whistles are within the audible range but relatively modest in amplitude at surface listening distance; the breach is high-amplitude but brief and infrequent. The integration of all three, experienced as a complete sequence during close observation of a pod in a narrow channel, produces the E=7 acoustic experience.
- **The three-part temporal sequence.** In a typical close-encounter observation in Haro Strait: (1) the pod approaches, with hydrophone playback revealing the click trains and call sequences below the surface; (2) as the pod passes, signature whistles and discrete calls become audible above the surface if the orca surfaces close by (the water transmits some sound into the air at the surface); (3) a breach event occurs, with the spectacular visual and the crash of impact audible to surface observers. The sequence unfolds over minutes to hours depending on the pod's behavior. This is not simultaneous sound; it is a sequential acoustic narrative.
- **The narrow channel acoustic context.** In Haro Strait, Boundary Pass, or any narrow channel where SRKWs hunt, the channel geometry enhances sound propagation. Calls recorded by hydrophone in these channels are cleaner and longer-range than in open ocean. The channel is an acoustic amplifier for the orca's communication system.
- **The observer experience.** The degree-331 acoustic event is experienced differently depending on instrumentation. Human observers in a boat: they hear whistles and calls above the surface when the orca is very close, and the breach with certainty. Hydrophone array: the full click train + call + (after breach) impact is recorded. The full three-component sequence is best documented through a combination of both.
