Mt. Rainier Wind Above Treeline

Meteorology — E=7 — Ultra-High-Velocity Howl — Summit Zone — Storm — UNMEDIATED ATMOSPHERIC WIND / NO CANOPY
pure atmospheric — 60–100+ mph — 14,411 ft Tahoma — above-treeline exposure — highest-altitude meteorological entry in engine

No Canopy. No Mediation. Pure Atmosphere.

Below treeline, the wind interacts with the Douglas fir’s acoustic architecture (degree 326): three mechanisms, structured moan-roar, Aeolian tones. Above treeline, there are no trees. The wind is unmediated: pure atmospheric turbulence over rock, ice, and snow. The same energy, a different acoustic world. The howl has no structure, no rhythm, no tonal character. It is the Pacific storm in direct contact with the mountain.

The Acoustic Boundary at Treeline

326 — Below Treeline (Douglas Fir)

  • Three mechanisms: aerodynamic + structural + Aeolian
  • Frequency: 20–6,000 Hz with distinct character
  • Amplitude: 70–85 dB at 20m
  • Gust cycle: 5–15 sec rhythmic pulse
  • Aeolian moan: tonal emergence
  • Character: STRUCTURED. The tree mediates.

327 — Above Treeline (Summit Zone)

  • One mechanism: pure atmospheric turbulence
  • Frequency: 500–8,000 Hz broadband dominant
  • Amplitude: 85–95 dB sustained, 95+ dB gust
  • No rhythmic pulse: continuous extreme
  • No Aeolian moan: no appropriate structures
  • Character: UNSTRUCTURED. Nothing mediates.

Same energy (E=7). Different acoustic worlds. The treeline is the acoustic boundary between a sound world mediated by vegetation and a sound world defined by pure atmosphere. Degrees 326 and 327 together document this boundary.

Mt. Rainier — The Pacific Northwest’s Weather Machine

Mt. Rainier (Tahoma) at 14,411 ft is the most topographically prominent peak in the contiguous United States — it rises further above its surrounding terrain than any other peak in the lower 48. This prominence means the mountain intercepts Pacific storms directly, with no upstream terrain to reduce wind loads. Orographic enhancement accelerates wind over the summit cone: Pacific storms delivering 80–100 mph at sea level can produce 120–150 mph at the summit during extreme events. The Paradise Visitor Center (5,400 ft, below treeline) has recorded sustained winds above 100 mph.

Above treeline on Rainier: 25 named glaciers cover 35 square miles. Wind over glacier ice and firn snow creates a sustained high-frequency hiss from turbulence over the acoustically smooth ice surface. Camp Muir (10,188 ft) on the windward south face: guide wires and tent structures add Aeolian whistles (1,000–4,000 Hz) to the unmediated howl. The human structures on the mountain are the above-treeline equivalent of the tree’s Aeolian branch resonance — but smaller, thinner, higher-pitched.

Three Rainier Entries

Degrees 261 (volcanic tremor), 262 (lahar simulation), and 327 (wind above treeline) together document Mt. Rainier’s three extreme character types in the engine: geological (volcanic), hazard (lahar), and meteorological (summit wind). The mountain appears three times because it is three different extreme things simultaneously.
