## MT. RAINIER -- THE SETTING

### The Highest Peak in the Cascades

Mt. Rainier (*Tahoma* in the Lushootseed language of the Puyallup and Nisqually peoples) is the Pacific Northwest's dominant volcanic peak:

- **Elevation.** Mt. Rainier reaches 14,411 feet (4,392 meters) above sea level -- the highest peak in the Cascade Range and the most topographically prominent peak in the contiguous United States (meaning it rises further above its surrounding terrain than any other peak in the lower 48). The prominence makes Rainier's weather severe: the mountain intercepts Pacific storms directly, with no upstream terrain to reduce wind loads.
- **The treeline on Rainier.** On Mt. Rainier, treeline occurs at approximately 6,000-7,000 feet (1,800-2,100 meters) on the south-facing slopes and somewhat lower on north-facing slopes. Above treeline: subalpine meadows (Paradise area, ~5,400 ft) transitioning to alpine terrain (Muir Snowfield, Camp Muir at 10,188 ft) transitioning to glaciers and summit rocks. The acoustic environment above treeline is categorically different from the forested zone below.
- **The glaciers.** Mt. Rainier has more glacial ice than any other peak in the contiguous United States: approximately 25 named glaciers covering 35 square miles. The glaciers are acoustically significant above treeline: wind over ice and firn creates different acoustic textures than wind over rock or vegetation. The ice surface is acoustically smooth relative to rock; wind over a broad glacier produces a sustained high-frequency hiss from turbulence over the ice surface.
- **Mt. Rainier's weather.** The mountain's summit experiences some of the most severe weather in the contiguous United States. Recorded wind speeds at the summit: sustained winds over 100 mph have been recorded during major storm events; the all-time recorded maximum for the Paradise ranger station area (below treeline at 5,400 ft) is over 100 mph. Above treeline and at summit elevation (14,411 ft), wind speeds during major Pacific storms routinely exceed 100 mph.

(Sources: Mt. Rainier elevation 14411 ft; Tahoma Lushootseed Puyallup Nisqually; topographic prominence; treeline elevation 6000-7000 ft; Paradise area 5400 ft; Camp Muir 10188 ft; 25 named glaciers; 35 square miles ice; wind speeds 100+ mph at summit; Paradise station weather records)

## ABOVE TREELINE -- THE ACOUSTIC TRANSITION

### What Disappears at the Treeline

The treeline is an acoustic boundary as well as a biological and ecological boundary:

- **Below treeline.** The wind in the forested zone below treeline is mediated by the vegetation: (1) the canopy absorbs and redirects wind energy, reducing surface wind speeds by 50-80% compared to the open canopy-top surface; (2) the trees convert wind energy into acoustic energy through the three mechanisms documented at degree 326 (aerodynamic, structural, Aeolian); (3) the canopy creates a "ceiling" that partially isolates the acoustic environment below from the full atmospheric wind above. The sound of wind in a forest is the sound of the wind-tree interaction -- not the wind itself.
- **Above treeline.** The wind above treeline encounters no canopy. The acoustic environment is the wind directly: the acoustic output is the interaction of high-velocity air with rock, ice, snow, and any small-scale structures (cairns, rocks, human structures). There is no canopy to mediate, reduce, or transform the wind. The full atmospheric energy of the storm reaches the surface without the forest's intervention.
- **The acoustic character of the transition.** A climber or hiker crossing the treeline on Rainier during a storm experiences one of the most abrupt acoustic transitions in the Pacific Northwest: from the forest's mediated moan-roar (E=7, contained, directional from specific trees) to the unmediated howl (E=7, omnidirectional, coming from everywhere simultaneously). Both are E=7, but they are qualitatively different: the forest sound is structured by the trees' acoustic architecture; the above-treeline sound is unstructured -- pure atmospheric chaos.
- **The psychological boundary.** The treeline is also a psychological boundary for climbers and hikers on Rainier: above it, the terrain offers no shelter from the wind except the lee of rocks and the mountain's own topography. The acoustic environment signals this transition -- the abrupt change in sound character marks the entry into the exposed zone.

(Sources: Wind speed reduction in forest 50-80%; canopy acoustic mediation; treeline acoustic transition; above-treeline unmediated wind; storm acoustic character above vs. below treeline; climber experience on Rainier; psychological treeline boundary)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=7 ULTRA-HIGH-VELOCITY HOWL

### What the Above-Treeline Wind Sounds Like

The acoustic profile of Mt. Rainier wind above treeline is distinct from any other entry in the engine:

- **The howl frequency range.** Wind flowing over rock, ice, and snow produces a broadband acoustic signature in the 500-8,000 Hz range -- higher in frequency than the forested zone's structural resonances (20-200 Hz) because the above-treeline structures (rocks, ice texture) are smaller in scale than trees. The dominant acoustic energy is in the 1,000-4,000 Hz range -- the "voice" of fast-moving air over irregular surfaces.
- **The turbulence structure.** Above treeline, wind flow is highly turbulent: the mountain's complex topography (ridges, bowls, the faces of the volcanic cone) creates turbulent eddies and vortices at large spatial scales. This turbulence produces acoustic output at the frequencies corresponding to the turbulent eddy sizes: large eddies (10-100 meters) produce sound at low frequencies (10-100 Hz); small eddies produce sound at higher frequencies (100-1,000 Hz). The result is a broadband acoustic environment that covers the full range from sub-bass to treble.
- **Human structures.** Camp Muir (10,188 ft), the Public Use Limit Area shelter, guide wires, tent structures, and rock shelters add Aeolian tones and turbulent wake sounds to the natural wind environment. These structures are acoustically significant because they are smaller and more cylindrical than trees -- guide wires can produce high-frequency whistles (1,000-4,000 Hz) in high winds; tent fabric in storm conditions can produce low-frequency flutter (5-50 Hz) and high-frequency flutter (500-2,000 Hz depending on fabric tension).
- **The howl as characteristic sound.** The "howl" designation for the above-treeline Mt. Rainier wind reflects the experience: wind at 60-100+ mph over exposed rock and ice produces a sound that has a strongly directional character (the wind comes from a specific direction; moving into or out of the wind changes the sound dramatically), a wide frequency range (from felt sub-bass vibration through the audible range), and a sustained continuous character (not the gust-and-pause of below-treeline conditions -- above treeline in a storm, the wind is nearly continuous at extreme velocity).
- **Amplitude.** At 60-80 mph wind speeds above treeline, the acoustic amplitude at the surface reaches 85-95 dB in sustained mode, with peaks above 95 dB during maximum gust events. This is higher amplitude than the Douglas fir gale (326, 70-85 dB) because the wind is unmediated -- the full atmospheric energy reaches the acoustic measurement point without the forest's energy absorption.

(Sources: Above-treeline wind frequency range 500-8000 Hz; dominant 1000-4000 Hz; turbulence eddy sizes and acoustic output; Camp Muir 10188 ft; Public Use Limit Area; guide wire Aeolian whistles 1000-4000 Hz; tent flutter frequencies; directional howl character; 60-100 mph above treeline; 85-95 dB sustained; peaks 95+ dB gust)

## THE MOUNTAIN'S WEATHER MACHINE

### Why Rainier Is the Engine's Meteorological Maximum

Mt. Rainier is the Pacific Northwest's most extreme meteorological surface location documented in the engine:

- **The summit zone.** The summit of Mt. Rainier at 14,411 feet is in the mid-troposphere during storm events: the mountain's peak protrudes into the layer of the atmosphere that Pacific frontal systems occupy. The wind speeds at the summit during a major Pacific cyclone are not equivalent to the gale at the ridge crest or the storm at the coast -- they are in the jet-stream-influenced flow that is more characteristic of aircraft altitudes than of surface weather.
- **Orographic enhancement.** The Pacific storm's wind, forced upward and over the Rainier cone, accelerates (by the same Bernoulli/continuity mechanism that creates ridge-crest acceleration) as it flows over the mountain's flanks. The acceleration at the summit can produce wind speeds significantly above the ambient storm wind speed -- 80-100 mph storm winds at sea level can produce 120-150 mph winds at the summit cone.
- **The windward/leeward difference.** The south and southwest faces of Rainier face the prevailing storm track and receive the maximum wind load. The north and northeast faces are relatively sheltered. Camp Muir at 10,188 ft (on the south face) is on the windward side -- it receives the full force of approaching storms. The acoustic environment at Camp Muir is more exposed than at a corresponding elevation on the mountain's lee side.
- **Annual wind record.** The Paradise Visitor Center (5,400 ft, below treeline) has recorded measured wind speeds above 100 mph multiple times since records began. The summit itself is rarely instrumented continuously but estimates from nearby radiosonde data and weather station extrapolation suggest regular events exceeding 100 mph at summit level during major winter storms.

(Sources: Mt. Rainier summit 14411 ft in mid-troposphere; Pacific cyclone wind at summit; orographic enhancement Bernoulli mechanism; 120-150 mph summit wind estimate; Camp Muir 10188 ft windward south face; Paradise 100+ mph measured; radiosonde extrapolation summit wind)

## THE COMPARISON TO DEGREE 326 -- THE ACOUSTIC BOUNDARY

### What the Treeline Divides

Degrees 326 and 327 together document the acoustic boundary at the treeline on a Pacific Northwest mountain:

- **Degree 326 (below treeline).** E=7. Three mechanisms. The forest mediates the wind: the tree architecture converts atmospheric energy into structured acoustic output (aerodynamic, structural, Aeolian). The sound has character -- the moan of Aeolian tones, the rhythmic pulse of the gust cycle, the specific frequency signature of Douglas fir needles. The forest's acoustic output is the product of wind-plus-tree interaction.
- **Degree 327 (above treeline).** E=7. One mechanism. The wind is unmediated: pure atmospheric turbulence over rock and ice. The sound lacks the character that the trees gave it -- there is no Aeolian moan, no rhythmic gust pulse (the gust pattern exists but without tree structure to translate it into identifiable acoustic events). The above-treeline howl is more alien, less structured, more overwhelming.
- **Same energy, different character.** Both are E=7. The energy level is similar. But the perceptual quality is categorically different: structured (326) vs. unstructured (327); mediated vs. unmediated; familiar (the human mind can follow the Douglas fir gale's rhythmic structure) vs. alien (the above-treeline howl has no rhythm, no character, no anchor).
- **The treeline as the engine's acoustic boundary.** Degrees 326 and 327 together document the Pacific Northwest's most important acoustic boundary: the transition from a sound world mediated by vegetation to a sound world defined by pure atmosphere. No other pair of consecutive SPS degrees documents a single acoustic phenomenon before and after its primary mediating structure.

(Sources: Treeline acoustic boundary documentation; mediated vs. unmediated wind; structured vs. unstructured acoustic output; E=7 same calibration different character; consecutive degree documentation of acoustic boundary)
