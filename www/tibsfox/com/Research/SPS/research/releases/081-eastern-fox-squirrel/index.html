The Sciuridae Septet — Breaks the Tie! LONGEST Family Streak!

SEVENTH consecutive Sciuridae: Yellow-bellied Marmot (75), Hoary Marmot (76), Olympic Marmot (77), Townsend’s Ground Squirrel (78), Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (79), Douglas Squirrel (80), Eastern Fox Squirrel (81). Seven consecutive Sciuridae — BREAKS THE TIE with the Mustelidae sextet (63–68). The longest family run in engine history is now SEVEN, held solely by Sciuridae.

Introduced Species — Native to Eastern North America

The Eastern Fox Squirrel is INTRODUCED to the Pacific Northwest — native to eastern and central North America. Brought to urban parks and campuses in the PNW where it now competes with native Douglas Squirrel (80). Second introduced species in the engine after Opossum (72).

Urban Park Habitat — Second Urban Species

The Eastern Fox Squirrel inhabits urban parks, college campuses, and suburban neighborhoods in the PNW. Second urban species in the engine after Raccoon (70). The habitat shift from conifer canopy (80, Douglas Squirrel) to urban park (81, Fox Squirrel) mirrors the introduced-vs-native dynamic.

The Sara Bareilles Parallel

Degree 81 pairs Eastern Fox Squirrel with Sara Bareilles in Part A — Pop/Soul, E=4, Broadway voice. Both E=4. Both outsiders who arrived — the Fox Squirrel is introduced to the PNW from eastern North America; Bareilles is from Eureka, CA, not the PNW. Both thrive in urban/commercial spaces — the squirrel in urban parks, Bareilles on major labels.

The Sound: Bark + Chatter (82nd Acoustic Element)

The bark: A loud, sharp, single-note alarm bark — “quaa!” — delivered at moderate rate. Lower-pitched than Douglas Squirrel. Approximately 1,500–6,000 Hz [1, 2, 3].

The chatter: Following the bark, a rapid chatter of shorter notes. Less aggressive and less sustained than Douglas Squirrel’s machine-gun rattle. More of a scolding conversation than a territorial assault [1, 2].

Why E=4: (1) Volume — audible across urban parks but not as piercing as Douglas Squirrel. (2) Duration — bark-chatter sequences are shorter. (3) Lower pitch — deeper voice reflecting larger body size. (4) Less aggressive — scolding but not the relentless fury of the Douglas Squirrel. E=4 not E=5.

Taxonomy: Sciurus niger

Etymology: Sciurus from Greek skiouros (“squirrel,” from skia “shadow” + oura “tail”). niger Latin for “black,” referring to the melanistic morph (despite the species typically being rusty-brown/orange). Described by Linnaeus (1758) [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Sciuridae — SEVENTH consecutive (75–81). Genus Sciurus — NEW GENUS (fifth Sciuridae genus). Largest tree squirrel in North America [1, 2].

Ecology: The Urban Park Squirrel

Habitat: Urban parks, college campuses, suburban neighborhoods with mature deciduous and mixed trees. The habitat contrast with Douglas Squirrel is stark: conifer canopy (native) vs. urban park (introduced) [1, 2, 3].

Size: The LARGEST tree squirrel in North America. 450–1,000 g (1–2.2 lbs). 45–70 cm total length. Roughly THREE TIMES the mass of the Douglas Squirrel.

Diet: Nuts, acorns, seeds, fruits, fungi. Generalist feeder. The broad diet contributes to urban success.

Competition: Competes with native Douglas Squirrel in urban-forest edge habitats. The Fox Squirrel’s larger size gives it a competitive advantage in direct encounters.
