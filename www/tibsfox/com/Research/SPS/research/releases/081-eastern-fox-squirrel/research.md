## THE EASTERN FOX SQUIRREL: THE URBAN INVADER

The Eastern Fox Squirrel is the **largest tree squirrel in North America** -- a big, rusty-brown, loud, generalist squirrel that has been **INTRODUCED** to the Pacific Northwest from eastern North America. At 450-1,000 grams (1-2.2 lbs), it is roughly THREE TIMES the mass of the native Douglas Squirrel (80). Found in **urban parks**, college campuses, and suburban neighborhoods throughout the PNW where it now competes with native species [1, 2, 3, 4].

This is the engine's **SEVENTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79, Douglas Squirrel 80, Eastern Fox Squirrel 81) -- the **Sciuridae septet**, which **BREAKS THE TIE** with the Mustelidae sextet (63-68). The longest family run in engine history is now **SEVEN**, held solely by Sciuridae.

This is also the engine's **second introduced species** after the Opossum (72), and the **second urban species** after the Raccoon (70).

And this is the **twenty-first consecutive original CSV species**.

## TAXONOMY: *Sciurus niger*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Sciurus** -- from Greek *skiouros* ("squirrel," from *skia* "shadow" + *oura* "tail"). The shadow-tail animal [1, 2].

- **niger** -- Latin for "black," referring to the melanistic color morph that was apparently common when Linnaeus described the species in 1758. Despite the name, most Eastern Fox Squirrels are rusty-brown to orange-brown [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Eastern Fox Squirrel (standard). Fox Squirrel (abbreviated). Named "fox" for its fox-like reddish-orange coloration [1, 2].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (twentieth mammalian entry; eighteenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- EIGHTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- SEVENTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75-76-77-78-79-80-81). The Sciuridae septet. BREAKS THE TIE with Mustelidae (63-68). LONGEST family run: SEVEN.
- **Genus:** *Sciurus* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Fifth Sciuridae genus after *Marmota* (75-77), *Urocitellus* (78), *Callospermophilus* (79), *Tamiasciurus* (80). True squirrels -- the largest genus of tree squirrels.
- **Species:** *S. niger* -- the Eastern Fox Squirrel. Native to eastern and central North America. INTRODUCED to the Pacific Northwest [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE LARGEST TREE SQUIRREL

### Body Form

The Eastern Fox Squirrel is large and robust [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 45-70 cm (18-28 inches) total including tail. The largest tree squirrel in North America.
- **Weight:** 450-1,000 g (1-2.2 lbs). Roughly THREE TIMES the mass of the Douglas Squirrel (150-300 g).
- **Tail:** 20-33 cm. Very bushy, prominent, often with rusty or orange tones.
- **Body shape:** Robust, stocky. Built for both arboreal and ground-based foraging.

### Pelage

- **Body:** Rusty-brown to orange-brown above. Highly variable -- ranges from pale buff to deep russet to melanistic black (the morph that gave the species its Latin name *niger*).
- **Belly:** Pale to orange. Generally paler than the dorsal surface.
- **Tail:** Very bushy, prominent. Often the most colorful part of the animal.
- **Variation:** Color morphs vary by region. PNW introduced populations tend toward the typical rusty-brown form.

## THE SOUND: BARK + CHATTER -- 82nd ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Bark That Announces

The Eastern Fox Squirrel's vocalization is a **loud, sharp alarm bark followed by rapid chatter** -- a two-part vocal sequence that is lower-pitched and less aggressive than the Douglas Squirrel's machine-gun rattle [1, 2, 3]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 1,500-6,000 Hz. Lower-pitched than Douglas Squirrel (3,000-10,000 Hz).
- **Structure:** Two-part: initial sharp bark ("quaa!") followed by rapid chatter of shorter notes. Less sustained than Douglas Squirrel.
- **Duration:** Bark: 0.1-0.3 seconds. Chatter sequences: 1-5 seconds. Shorter than Douglas Squirrel's 2-10+ seconds.
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- audible across urban parks but less piercing than Douglas Squirrel at E=5.
- **Function:** ALARM and TERRITORIAL. Less aggressively territorial than Douglas Squirrel. More generalized alarm response to perceived threats.

### Why E=4 (Not E=5)

1. **Lower pitch:** Deeper voice reflecting larger body size. Less piercing.
2. **Shorter duration:** Bark-chatter sequences are briefer than Douglas Squirrel's sustained rattle.
3. **Less aggressive:** Scolding but not the relentless fury of the Douglas Squirrel.
4. **Urban context:** Audible in parks but not dominating the soundscape the way Douglas Squirrel dominates the forest.

## ECOLOGY: THE URBAN PARK SQUIRREL

### Habitat: Urban Parks -- Second Urban Species

The Eastern Fox Squirrel inhabits **urban parks** in the PNW [1, 2, 4]:

- **Habitats:** Urban parks, college campuses, suburban neighborhoods, golf courses, cemeteries -- anywhere with mature deciduous or mixed trees.
- **Contrast:** The habitat shift from conifer canopy (80, Douglas Squirrel) to urban park (81, Fox Squirrel) is dramatic. Native canopy specialist to introduced urban generalist.
- **Significance:** Second urban species after Raccoon (70). The engine's urban habitat expands.

### The Introduced Dynamic

The Eastern Fox Squirrel is **INTRODUCED** to the PNW [1, 2, 4]:

- **Native range:** Eastern and central North America, from the Great Plains to the Atlantic coast.
- **Introduction:** Brought to PNW urban areas as park animals. Now established in cities from Portland to Seattle.
- **Competition:** Competes with native Douglas Squirrel in urban-forest edge habitats. The Fox Squirrel's larger size (three times the mass) gives it a competitive advantage.
- **Parallel:** Second introduced species after Opossum (72). Both from eastern North America.

### Diet: The Generalist

The Eastern Fox Squirrel is a dietary generalist [1, 2, 3]:

- **Nuts and acorns:** Primary food. Oak acorns, walnuts, hickory nuts (in native range), hazelnuts (in PNW).
- **Seeds and fruits:** Tree seeds, berries, fruit.
- **Fungi:** Mushrooms, truffles.
- **Insects:** Occasional.
- **Urban supplementation:** Bird feeders, garden produce, human food scraps.

### Predators: The Urban Food Web

In the PNW's urban context [1, 2]:

- **Red Fox** (degree 71) -- urban predator that takes Fox Squirrels in parks.
- **Domestic and feral cats** -- significant urban predator.
- **Red-tailed Hawk** -- urban raptor.
- **Coyote** -- suburban areas.
