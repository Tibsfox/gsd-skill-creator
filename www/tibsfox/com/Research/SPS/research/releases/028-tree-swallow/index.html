Tree Swallow

Tachycineta bicolor · Family Hirundinidae (Swallows) · Third Swallow — Trio Complete
Liquid Twittering Gurgle · Open Field near Water · Spring/Summer · Energy 4
Over a freshwater marsh at dawn, a compact iridescent shape perches on a fence post — brilliant blue-green above, clean white below, two colors, no more. It lifts into the air and from it comes a tumbling, liquid, twittering gurgle — neither the martin’s continuous stream nor the Violet-green’s discrete rain, but something between: a brook. Water that has structure within its flow. The Tree Swallow. The two-colored swift-mover. The third Hirundinidae. The centrist. The most-studied swallow in North America. The only swallow that can eat berries. The swallow trio closes here.

Swallow Trio Complete (Degrees 26–28)

Three consecutive Hirundinidae. Three liquid sounds. Three positions on every gradient. The trio is complete: patriarch (martin) → cliff dweller (Violet-green) → centrist (Tree Swallow). Stream → rain → brook. The swallow trio is the engine’s densest family cluster: three consecutive degrees, no gaps.

Tachycineta Pair Complete (Degrees 27–28)

The Violet-green Swallow (T. thalassina, “sea-colored”) and Tree Swallow (T. bicolor, “two-colored”) complete the engine’s second same-genus consecutive pair. Parallels the Catharus pair (18–19). Two swift-movers named for their colors: sea-green cliff specialist vs. blue-green wetland generalist. Same genus, opposite niches. The engine’s finest taxonomic resolution.

Song Bioacoustics: Liquid Twittering Gurgle

The Tree Swallow’s vocalization is a liquid twittering gurgle — a tumbling, semi-continuous sound where individual notes are perceptible but grade into each other. Neither the martin’s unbroken stream nor the Violet-green’s discrete rain. The brook: water that moves in visible pulses over stones.

The Berry Backup: Unique Dietary Flexibility

The Tree Swallow is the only North American swallow that can eat plant material — bayberries (Myrica spp.) and other small waxy fruits. No other Hirundinidae in the engine has this capability. The berry backup enables: (1) overwintering farther north than any other swallow, (2) earliest spring arrival in the PNW (late February), (3) cold-snap survival when insects vanish, (4) the widest breeding range of any North American swallow (Alaska to Newfoundland). The centrist’s unique trick: dietary flexibility that neither bookend possesses.

The Most-Studied Swallow in North America

Decades of nest box monitoring, continent-wide datasets, hundreds of papers. The Tree Swallow has taught science about insect decline (declining clutch sizes tracking the “insect apocalypse”), mercury bioaccumulation (sentinel for contaminated waterways), agricultural impacts on aerial insectivores, climate change phenology (spring arrival advancing 9–12 days over 50 years), and extra-pair paternity (50–80% of broods in some populations). A model organism. A distributed sensor network. A continental environmental monitor.

Taxonomy: Tachycineta bicolor

Tachycineta: Greek takhys (swift) + kineo (to move) — “the swift-mover.” Bicolor: Latin bi- (two) + color — “two-colored.” The full binomial: “the two-colored swift-mover.” Where the Violet-green was “the sea-colored swift-one,” the Tree Swallow is “the two-colored swift-one.” Two congeners named for their colors. The genus tells you how they move. The species tells you how they look.

Third Hirundinidae. The swallow family now holds three species — the engine’s third-largest family behind Turdidae (5) and Parulidae (5). The swallow trio is the densest family cluster in the engine: three consecutive degrees, zero gaps.

Ecosystem: Open Field near Water

Agricultural pastures, freshwater marshes, beaver meadows, lakeshores — wherever open grassland meets standing water. The Tree Swallow forages low (1–30 m), skimming above grass and water to intercept emergent aquatic insects. The swallow trio’s ecosystem gradient descends: sky (martin, 15–150+ m) → cliff/water (Violet-green, 5–100 m) → field/water (Tree Swallow, 1–30 m). Three elevations, three foraging zones, three vertical niches.
