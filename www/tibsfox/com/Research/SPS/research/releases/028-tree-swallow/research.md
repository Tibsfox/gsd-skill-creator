## Context: The Trio Closes

Three degrees. Three swallows. One family. The Purple Martin opened the sky at degree 26 -- the patriarch, the heavy bomber, the liquid gurgle, the colony on the harbor pilings. The Violet-green Swallow took the opposite extreme at degree 27 -- the fighter jet, the piccolo, the liquid twitter, the cliff dweller who needs no human house. Now, at degree 28, the Tree Swallow arrives.

*Tachycineta bicolor* is the centrist. The synthesis. The resolution of the polarity the first two swallows established. Where the Purple Martin is heavy (45-60 g) and the Violet-green is featherweight (14 g), the Tree Swallow is medium (17-25 g). Where the martin is obligately colonial and the Violet-green is solitary, the Tree Swallow is loosely colonial -- social but not dependent on the colony. Where the martin sings a continuous liquid gurgle and the Violet-green fires discrete liquid chips, the Tree Swallow produces a **liquid twittering gurgle** -- a sound that is neither fully continuous nor fully discrete, but something between. The stream (martin) and the rain (Violet-green) become the **brook**: water that moves in visible pulses over stones, neither smooth flow nor separated drops but a rhythmic, tumbling, in-between state.

This is also the completion of the **Tachycineta pair** -- two consecutive same-genus degrees (27-28), the engine's tightest taxonomic adjacency. The Violet-green Swallow (*T. thalassina*, the sea-colored swift-one) and the Tree Swallow (*T. bicolor*, the two-colored swift-one) are sister species in the same genus, placed in consecutive degrees, exploring within-genus variation at the finest evolutionary resolution the engine has attempted. The Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 (Swainson's Thrush and Veery) was the first such pair. The Tachycineta pair at degrees 27-28 is the second. Both pairs are nested inside larger family clusters: Turdidae at degrees 2/7/18/19/25, Hirundinidae at degrees 26/27/28.

But the Tree Swallow is more than a trio closer and a genus-pair partner. It is one of the most remarkable birds in the Western Hemisphere -- the **only swallow in North America that can eat plant material** (bayberries and other small fruits) when insects are unavailable. This dietary flexibility is unique. No other Hirundinidae in the engine, no other swallow in the PNW, can do this. It is the reason the Tree Swallow can overwinter farther north than any other North American swallow, the reason it arrives earliest in spring, the reason its range extends farther into the continental interior. The berry backup plan changes everything.

And it is the **most-studied swallow in North America**. Decades of nest box monitoring programs, continent-wide datasets, hundreds of published papers. The Tree Swallow has become ecology's model swallow -- the species that taught us about clutch size variation, insect phenology, climate change effects on migratory birds, mercury bioaccumulation, and agricultural impacts on aerial insectivores. More is known about this single species than about any other swallow on the continent. The engine's 28th species is also its most documented.

The Tree Swallow is the 27th unique species across 29 degrees. It brings the trio's resolution, the Tachycineta pair's completion, a unique dietary adaptation, the most-studied swallow, and the centrist acoustic position between the martin's gurgle and the Violet-green's twitter.

## The Song: Liquid Twittering Gurgle

### The Third Liquid Sound

Degree 26: **liquid gurgle** (continuous, legato, stream). Degree 27: **liquid twitter** (discrete, staccato, rain). Degree 28: **liquid twittering gurgle** (semi-continuous, mixed articulation, brook). Three consecutive degrees. Three liquid sounds. Three different time-domain structures built on the same timbral foundation.

The Tree Swallow's vocalization is the **synthesis** of the preceding two. It contains elements of both the martin's continuous gurgle and the Violet-green's discrete twitter. The twittering is audible -- individual notes can be perceived as discrete events -- but the notes grade into each other more smoothly than the Violet-green's chips, creating a flow that is not quite continuous (like the martin) and not quite discrete (like the Violet-green). The result is a sound that tumbles: notes overlapping, bleeding into each other, separating briefly, then rejoining. A brook over stones. Water that has visible structure but continuous motion.

### Acoustic Description

Key acoustic parameters:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~2.0-8.0 kHz. Broader than the Violet-green (3.0-9.0 kHz) but with a lower floor, more similar to the martin's range (1.0-8.0 kHz). The Tree Swallow sits between its two Hirundinidae predecessors in frequency space.
- **Peak energy band:** ~3.0-6.0 kHz. Between the martin's peak (2.0-6.0 kHz) and the Violet-green's peak (5.0-8.0 kHz). The centrist frequency. The middle voice.
- **Call duration:** 1-3 seconds per twittering bout. Between the martin's 3-6 second sustained song and the Violet-green's 0.5-2.0 second twitter bursts.
- **Note rate:** 8-15 notes per second. The same range as the martin (8-15/sec), slower than the Violet-green (10-20/sec). At this rate, the notes approach the **perceptual fusion threshold** -- the boundary where the human ear begins to hear individual notes merging into a continuous stream. Some passages fuse. Some remain discrete. The note rate wobbles around the threshold, creating the "twittering gurgle" quality: moments of gurgle (fused) interrupted by moments of twitter (discrete).
- **Note structure:** Each note is a brief frequency-modulated element, but less sharply defined than the Violet-green's chips. The onset and offset are softer, less abrupt. Where the Violet-green's chips have clean boundaries (like plucked strings), the Tree Swallow's notes have slightly blurred edges (like bowed strings with a faster bow change). The effect: individual notes are perceptible but not sharply separated.
- **Phrase structure:** More complex than either predecessor. The Tree Swallow's song includes:
  1. **Twittering gurgle** -- the primary vocalization. A rapid series of semi-fused notes with a liquid, bubbling quality. The closest analogy is a brook: water that moves in visible pulses but maintains continuous flow.
  2. **Three-note call** -- a distinctive *veet-veet-veet* or *pli-pli-pli* contact call used near the nest. Higher-pitched, simpler, more discrete than the twittering song.
  3. **Churring** -- a harsher, buzzy element used in aggressive encounters near the nest cavity. Lower-pitched, noisier, more broadband than the twittering song.
- **Timbre:** Moderate, warm, liquid. Between the martin's rich opacity and the Violet-green's crystalline transparency. The Tree Swallow's timbre has more body than the Violet-green's thin chips but less depth than the martin's resonant gurgle. The sound is often described as "pleasant," "cheerful," or "chirpy" -- words that reflect its moderate, accessible quality.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate. Energy 4 here reflects both volume and persistence. The Tree Swallow is louder than the Violet-green and quieter than the martin. The centrist dynamic.
- **Context:** The Tree Swallow vocalizes from perches near the nest (fence posts, wire, nest box tops) and in flight. Unlike the Violet-green (primarily in-flight vocalizer) and the martin (colony display), the Tree Swallow divides its vocal output roughly equally between perched and aerial delivery. Dawn singing from the nest box is common and conspicuous.

### The Liquid Trilogy: Stream, Rain, Brook

The three consecutive liquid degrees create a complete taxonomy of liquid sound:

| Degree | Species | Liquid Type | Metaphor | Articulation | Continuity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Purple Martin | Liquid gurgle | Stream | Legato | Continuous |
| 27 | Violet-green Swallow | Liquid twitter | Rain | Staccato | Discrete |
| **28** | **Tree Swallow** | **Liquid twittering gurgle** | **Brook** | **Mixed** | **Semi-continuous** |

The trio maps the full spectrum of liquid articulation. Continuous (stream) at one pole. Discrete (rain) at the other. And the Tree Swallow in the middle: the brook, where water tumbles over stones in visible pulses but never stops flowing. The brook is the synthesis. It contains elements of both stream (continuous flow) and rain (individual impacts) -- water that has structure within its flow, pulses within its continuity.

This liquid trilogy is the engine's first exploration of a single timbral quality across three variations. Previous multi-degree arcs explored different timbral qualities (warbler quintet: sweet, chattery, crescendo, hook, trill). The swallow trio explores **one quality -- liquid -- in three temporal organizations**. The lesson: timbre is not a single dimension. "Liquid" is a timbral label. But liquid can be legato, staccato, or mixed. The temporal structure is a second independent dimension. Two parameters define a sound texture: timbre (what it sounds like at any instant) and articulation (how the instants connect). The swallow trio demonstrates this two-dimensional space.

**The progression (29 elements):** silence -- cascade -- drone -- language -- motif -- metronome -- question -- harmonic spiral -- ultrasonic threshold -- variation -- minimum signal -- trill/tremolo -- sawtooth sweep -- ostinato -- repetition-resolution -- accelerando -- crescendo -- the hook -- ascending spiral -- descending spiral -- sustain/the release -- the hammer -- resonant percussion -- the groove reset -- the hoarse warble -- the caroling -- the liquid gurgle -- the liquid twitter -- **the liquid twittering gurgle**.

### What Three Swallows in Three Degrees Reveal

The swallow trio is the engine's third multi-degree arc within a single family (after the warbler quintet at degrees 14-17/20 and the thrush quintet at degrees 2/7/18/19/25). But it is the most concentrated: three consecutive degrees with no gaps. What does this density reveal?

**1. Within-family variation is structured, not random.** The three swallows do not vary chaotically. They map a gradient with clear poles and a center. Size is the organizing variable: the largest swallow is colonial, low-pitched, dependent, and sea-level. The smallest is solitary, high-pitched, independent, and alpine. The medium is moderate on every axis. Body size predicts ecological strategy. The gradient is a function of mass.

**2. Same bauplan, different parameters.** All three share the swallow body plan: long pointed wings, short legs, wide gape, aerial insectivory, cavity nesting, neotropical migration. The bauplan is the constant. Everything else -- size, pitch, sociality, cavity strategy, altitude range, dietary flexibility -- is a parameter that varies across species. The trio demonstrates **parametric variation within a conserved architecture**. The hardware is fixed. The software differs.

**3. The centrist is not average -- it is flexible.** The Tree Swallow's middle position is not merely the arithmetic mean of the two extremes. The Tree Swallow has capabilities neither extreme possesses: bayberry consumption, the broadest continental range, the highest research profile. Being in the middle frees the Tree Swallow from the constraints that push the bookends to their extremes. The martin MUST be colonial because it MUST use large nest boxes in limited harbor locations. The Violet-green MUST be solitary because it MUST use small cliff crevices that cannot host colonies. The Tree Swallow can be either, depending on local conditions. Flexibility is the centrist's advantage. It is not trapped at a pole.

**4. The liquid trilogy is a timbral lesson.** Three sounds that share the word "liquid" but differ in temporal organization: continuous (stream/legato), discrete (rain/staccato), mixed (brook/tumbling). The lesson is explicit: timbral quality (liquid) and temporal structure (articulation) are independent dimensions. Knowing a sound is "liquid" tells you the timbre but not the rhythm. Knowing it is "staccato" tells you the rhythm but not the timbre. You need both coordinates to locate a sound in acoustic space. The swallow trio maps the full articulation axis for a single timbral value. It is the engine's first complete timbral exploration.

**5. This is the minimum number of points needed to define a line (two points) and confirm that the relationship is not simply linear (the third point that reveals curvature or exception). The warbler quintet used five degrees. The thrush quintet used five across twenty-four degrees. The swallow trio achieves comparable resolution in three consecutive degrees. Efficiency. ## The Berry Backup: Unique Dietary Flexibility

### The Only Swallow That Can Eat Fruit

The Tree Swallow possesses a dietary adaptation unique among North American swallows: it can eat **bayberries** (*Myrica* species) and other small waxy fruits when flying insects are unavailable. No other Hirundinidae in the Western Hemisphere can do this. The Purple Martin is an obligate aerial insectivore. The Violet-green Swallow is an obligate aerial insectivore. The Barn Swallow, Cliff Swallow, and Bank Swallow are obligate aerial insectivores. Only the Tree Swallow has the digestive capacity to extract nutrition from plant material.

The bayberry adaptation has profound ecological consequences:

**1. Overwintering range.** The Tree Swallow overwinters farther north than any other North American swallow -- along the Gulf Coast, the Carolinas, and occasionally even the mid-Atlantic states. Other swallows must migrate to Mexico, Central America, or South America because insects are absent during northern winters. The Tree Swallow can survive cold snaps and insect-poor periods by switching to bayberries. The berry backup extends the northern edge of its winter range by hundreds of kilometers.

**2. Spring arrival timing.** The Tree Swallow arrives on PNW breeding grounds earlier than any other swallow -- late February to mid-March in favorable years, weeks ahead of the Purple Martin (late March to April) and the Violet-green Swallow (mid-March to early April). Early arrival secures the best nest cavities. The berry backup fuels early migration: the Tree Swallow can feed during the journey through regions where insects have not yet emerged because it can eat berries along the way. Early arrival is a competitive advantage in the cavity market. The dietary flexibility enables the temporal advantage.

**3. Cold snap survival.** Spring in the PNW is unpredictable. Cold snaps in March and April can eliminate flying insects for days. For an obligate insectivore, a late spring cold snap is a survival crisis -- no food, no energy, potential starvation. The Tree Swallow survives cold snaps by switching to bayberries, other small fruits, and even seeds. The martin and Violet-green cannot do this. During the catastrophic spring cold snap of 2008, Tree Swallow populations in the PNW suffered far less than Violet-green Swallow populations, likely because of their ability to feed on non-insect foods.

**4. Continental range.** The Tree Swallow breeds across the entire North American continent -- from Alaska to Newfoundland, from the Mexican border to the treeline. This is the widest breeding range of any North American swallow. The continental range is enabled partly by the dietary flexibility: the Tree Swallow can colonize habitats where insect availability is marginal because it has a backup food source. The dietary flexibility removes a constraint that limits other swallows to insect-rich habitats.

### The Digestive Adaptation

The bayberry adaptation requires specific digestive modifications:

- **Lipase production.** Bayberries (*Myrica cerifera*, *M. pensylvanica*, and related species) are high in waxy lipids. The Tree Swallow produces digestive lipases that can break down these waxy coatings -- enzymes that other swallows either lack or produce at lower levels.
- **Gut transit time.** The Tree Swallow can adjust its gut transit time depending on food type. Insect meals pass quickly (short retention, high throughput). Berry meals are retained longer (slower passage, more complete lipid extraction). This plasticity in gut function is rare among Hirundinidae.
- **Gizzard flexibility.** The muscular gizzard can process both soft insect prey and harder berry seeds. The gizzard wall thickness and musculature show seasonal variation -- slightly more robust in winter when berry consumption increases.

The digestive flexibility is a physiological innovation. It is not shared by the Violet-green Swallow, despite the two species being congeners in *Tachycineta*. The Tree Swallow evolved this capability independently within the genus. It is a derived trait, not an ancestral one. The berry backup is the Tree Swallow's unique contribution to swallow biology -- the thing it alone can do.

## Nest Box Competition: The War at the Cavity Door

### Bluebirds, Sparrows, and the Cavity Arms Race

The Tree Swallow is a secondary cavity nester that uses both natural cavities (woodpecker holes, tree hollows) and artificial nest boxes. It is not as dependent on nest boxes as the Purple Martin (which needs them for 90%+ of PNW nesting) nor as independent as the Violet-green Swallow (which rarely uses them). The Tree Swallow occupies the middle ground: it thrives with nest boxes but can breed without them.

But where nest boxes are present, the Tree Swallow is drawn into one of the most intense interspecific competitions in North American ornithology: **the cavity war with Eastern Bluebirds and House Sparrows.**

**Tree Swallow vs. Eastern Bluebird:** In areas where both species breed (primarily eastern North America and parts of the Midwest), the Tree Swallow and Eastern Bluebird (*Sialia sialis*) compete directly for nest boxes. Both species are similar in size (20 g vs. 27-34 g), both prefer open habitats with scattered trees, both use cavities with similar entrance hole sizes (~3.8 cm). The competition is intense because the niche overlap is nearly complete. Conservation programs that erect nest boxes for bluebirds often find Tree Swallows occupying 30-50% of the boxes. The solution -- spacing boxes in pairs (one for bluebirds, one for swallows, 5 meters apart) -- was discovered through decades of citizen science monitoring and is one of the most successful interspecific cavity-management strategies ever devised.

**Tree Swallow vs. House Sparrow:** The introduced House Sparrow (*Passer domesticus*) is an aggressive cavity competitor that will destroy Tree Swallow eggs, kill nestlings, and even kill incubating adults. The sparrow threat is more severe than the bluebird competition because it involves predation, not just displacement. Tree Swallows in areas with high House Sparrow density show reduced reproductive success. Nest box programs in agricultural areas must actively manage sparrow intrusion to maintain Tree Swallow populations.

**Tree Swallow vs. Tree Swallow:** Intraspecific competition for cavities is also intense. Female Tree Swallows fight aggressively for nest sites, sometimes displacing other females mid-nesting. Male Tree Swallows defend nest boxes vigorously. The loosely colonial nesting pattern -- boxes scattered across a field, each defended as a territory -- creates a landscape of tiny contested domains. The colony is not cooperative (like the martin's). It is a collection of individual territories that happen to be near each other because the habitat (open field with nest boxes) concentrates the resource.

### The Middle Nesting Strategy

The Tree Swallow's nesting ecology sits between the martin's and the Violet-green's:

| Feature | Purple Martin (26) | Tree Swallow (28) | Violet-green (27) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary nest site | Human nest boxes | Nest boxes + natural cavities | Natural cavities |
| Human dependency | Critical | Moderate | Minimal |
| Altitude range | Sea level | Lowland to ~1,500 m | Sea level to 2,500+ m |
| Coloniality | Obligate colonial | Loosely colonial | Solitary/paired |
| Main competitor | European Starling | Bluebird + House Sparrow | Less severe (small cavities) |
| Conservation approach | Managed nest boxes | Paired nest boxes, sparrow control | None needed |

The Tree Swallow benefits from human nest boxes but is not trapped by them. Remove all nest boxes and the Tree Swallow persists in natural cavities -- reduced in number but not eliminated. This resilience is why the Tree Swallow is classified as abundant and self-sustaining despite cavity competition. It has options. It is the flexible one.

## The Most-Studied Swallow in North America

### Why Scientists Love the Tree Swallow

The Tree Swallow is to avian ecology what the laboratory mouse is to biomedical research: a model organism. It has become the most intensively studied swallow in North America -- and one of the most-studied birds of any kind -- because it possesses a combination of traits that make it ideal for scientific research:

**1. Nest box acceptance.** Tree Swallows readily occupy standardized nest boxes, which means researchers can control nest site location, monitor nests without disturbing natural cavities, and create experimental arrays with precise spacing and orientation. The bird comes to the scientist, not the other way around.

**2. Continent-wide range.** The Tree Swallow breeds from Alaska to Newfoundland, from the Mexican border to the subarctic. This vast range allows researchers across the entire continent to study the same species under different environmental conditions -- climate gradients, agricultural intensification gradients, latitude gradients, altitude gradients. Any ecological variable you want to study, somewhere there is a Tree Swallow population experiencing it.

**3. Tolerance of handling.** Tree Swallows are remarkably tolerant of capture, banding, blood sampling, feather sampling, and nest monitoring. Adults return to boxes that have been repeatedly opened and inspected. They do not readily abandon nests after disturbance. This tolerance reduces observer effects and makes long-term monitoring feasible.

**4. Single-brood strategy.** Most Tree Swallow pairs raise a single brood per season, which simplifies reproductive success measurements. Each pair's output is one nest, one clutch, one set of fledglings. The arithmetic is clean.

**5. Aerial insectivore indicator.** Because the Tree Swallow depends primarily on flying insects, its reproductive success tracks insect abundance with high fidelity. Changes in clutch size, nestling growth rates, and fledging success serve as sensitive bioindicators of local insect populations. The Tree Swallow is a living sensor for insect availability -- and because insect populations are declining globally, the Tree Swallow has become a sentinel species for one of the most important ecological crises of the 21st century.

### What the Tree Swallow Has Taught Us

Decades of Tree Swallow research have produced landmark findings:

- **Insect decline tracking.** Long-term Tree Swallow datasets from multiple North American sites show declining clutch sizes and later laying dates over the past 30-40 years, consistent with decreasing insect availability. These datasets are among the strongest evidence for the "insect apocalypse" hypothesis.
- **Mercury bioaccumulation.** Tree Swallows accumulate mercury from aquatic insect prey. Studies near contaminated waterways have used Tree Swallows as biomonitors for mercury levels in freshwater food webs. The bird concentrates mercury from insects that emerge from contaminated sediments, making invisible pollution visible.
- **Agricultural impacts.** Tree Swallow populations near intensive agriculture (pesticide use, monoculture) show reduced reproductive success compared to populations in less-modified landscapes. The species has become a standard bioindicator for measuring how farming practices affect aerial insectivores.
- **Climate change phenology.** The Tree Swallow's spring arrival date has advanced by approximately 9-12 days over the past 50 years in many parts of North America, tracking warming spring temperatures. The bird's migration timing is one of the most robust datasets documenting phenological shifts due to climate change.
- **Extra-pair paternity.** Tree Swallow research revealed some of the highest rates of extra-pair paternity among socially monogamous birds. In some populations, 50-80% of broods contain at least one nestling sired by a male other than the social mate. This finding revolutionized understanding of mating systems in cavity-nesting birds.

The Tree Swallow is not just a bird. It is a research platform. A distributed sensor network. A continental-scale environmental monitor that has been running for decades, maintained by thousands of citizen scientists and professional researchers, producing some of the most important ecological datasets of the modern era.

## Plumage: Iridescent Blue-Green -- The Third Structural Color

### The Two-Colored Pattern

The Tree Swallow's plumage is a study in simplicity and brilliance:

**Males:** The entire dorsal surface (back, rump, crown, nape) is brilliant iridescent blue-green -- a deep, metallic color that shifts between blue and green depending on the angle of light. The underparts are clean, bright white. The contrast is stark: blue-green above, white below. Two colors. *Bicolor*. The name is literally true.

**Females:** Similar pattern but duller. The dorsal surface is more brown-green in first-year females, becoming increasingly iridescent blue-green with age. Full adult female plumage may not develop until the second or third year. The underparts are white, sometimes with a faint gray-brown breast band.

### Three Iridescent Swallows: A Plumage Gradient

| Feature | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) | Tree Swallow (28) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dorsal color | Iridescent purple-black | Iridescent green + violet | Iridescent blue-green |
| Ventral color | Dark (male), gray (female) | Clean white | Clean white |
| White facial marks | None | Above eye (diagnostic) | None |
| Rump patches | None | White flanks (diagnostic) | None |
| Contrast level | Minimum (dark on dark) | Maximum (vivid green on white + face marks) | High (blue-green on white) |
| Color shift | Purple-blue-black | Green-bronze-violet | Blue-green (narrow shift) |
| Female dimorphism | Moderate | Moderate | **Strong (age-dependent)** |

The Tree Swallow's plumage is between the other two in several dimensions: higher contrast than the martin (white underparts) but less adorned than the Violet-green (no white face patches or rump saddle). The two-toned pattern is clean and simple. The name says it all: two colors.

The female age-dependent plumage is unique in the trio. Female Tree Swallows do not achieve full adult plumage until their second or third year -- a slow maturation that is rare among small passerines. First-year females are brown-backed and can breed successfully despite lacking the iridescent plumage that would signal full adult status. The plumage tells a temporal story: how old is this female? The dorsal color answers.

## Ecosystem: Open Field near Water

### The Third Open Ecosystem

Degree 26: open sky near water. Degree 27: open water. Degree 28: **open field near water.** Three consecutive open, non-forested ecosystems. The engine has been out of the forest for three degrees now. The canopy closed at degree 24 (Western Tanager, conifer forest). Degree 25 (American Robin, lawn and woodland) was transitional. Degrees 26-28 are fully open: sky, water, field.

The Tree Swallow's ecosystem is the most terrestrial of the three open ecosystems. "Open field near water" is the classic Tree Swallow habitat: agricultural pastures, hayfields, freshwater marshes, beaver meadows, lakeshores with mowed grass, roadside ditches with standing water. The bird needs two things: open airspace for foraging (fields, meadows, pastures -- flat, treeless areas where it can see and pursue flying insects) and water nearby (ponds, marshes, streams, ditches -- aquatic habitats that produce emergent insects, the primary food source).

The "near water" component is critical. Tree Swallows forage disproportionately over and near water because aquatic insect emergences (mayflies, midges, caddisflies, damselflies) are the richest, most predictable insect pulses in their habitat. A field without nearby water has far fewer flying insects. A field WITH nearby water has regular, abundant insect hatches timed to water temperature and season. The Tree Swallow's habitat is not just "a field" -- it is "a field that produces insects because there is water nearby." The water is the engine. The field is the flight arena.

### The Ecosystem Gradient

The three swallow ecosystems form a gradient from sky to surface:

| Degree | Ecosystem | Vertical emphasis | Substrate |
|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Open sky near water | High altitude (15-150+ m) | None (pure air column) |
| 27 | Open water | Mid altitude (5-100 m) + cliff faces | Rock, water surface |
| **28** | **Open field near water** | **Low altitude (1-30 m)** | **Grassland, water edge** |

The swallow trio has descended. The martin forages highest, in the open sky. The Violet-green forages at middle heights, over water and along cliffs. The Tree Swallow forages lowest, skimming just above the grass and water surface, pursuing insects in the zone closest to the ground. Three elevations. Three foraging zones. Three swallows partitioning the vertical air column. They can coexist because they work at different heights.
