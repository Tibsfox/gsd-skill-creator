## WOOD DUCK -- ANATIDAE -- THE PAINTED SWIMMER

### The Species

The Wood Duck (*Aix sponsa*) is a **medium-sized perching duck** (family Anatidae, tribe Cairinini) native to North America. It is one of only two living species in the genus *Aix* -- the other being the Mandarin Duck (*Aix galericulata*) of East Asia. Both species are renowned for their extraordinary plumage, and the genus name *Aix* (from Greek, meaning "a kind of waterbird") links the two most spectacularly colored ducks on Earth.

The species name *sponsa* means "betrothed" or "promised one" in Latin -- a reference to the male's ornate plumage, as if dressed for a wedding. The Wood Duck: the betrothed waterbird. The name captures the human response to this bird's beauty -- it looks ceremonial, formal, impossibly decorated.

**Physical dimensions:** Body length 47-54 cm. Wingspan 66-73 cm. Weight 450-680g (male), 430-580g (female). Medium-sized for a duck -- larger than the Hooded Merganser (degree 120, 450-880g) but smaller than the Common Merganser (degree 119, 1.3-2.1 kg). The size places the Wood Duck in the middle range of the Anatidae streak.

### The Plumage -- The Most Beautiful Duck in North America

The male Wood Duck in breeding plumage is a study in **iridescent excess**:

**The head:** The most spectacular feature. The entire head is iridescent -- **deep green** transitioning to **iridescent purple** from crown to nape, with a swept-back crest that extends beyond the head in a smooth, flowing line. **Two thin white stripes** run from the bill over the eye and from behind the eye to the back of the crest -- the "racing stripes" that give the head its distinctive graphic pattern. The chin and throat are white, extending in two prongs up each side of the face. The effect is of a green-and-purple helmet with white racing graphics.

**The eyes:** Bright red, set in the iridescent head like jewels. The red eye is diagnostic in combination with the green/purple head.

**The bill:** Small and compact, with a red base, white tip, and black ridge. The bill coloration is itself multicolored.

**The breast:** Rich **chestnut-maroon** with white speckles that transition into a white belly. The chestnut breast is a warm counterpoint to the cool iridescent head.

**The flanks:** **Warm buff-tan**, set off by black-and-white vermiculated patterns. Vertical white and black bars separate the breast from the flanks.

**The back:** Dark, iridescent blue-green to bronze, with long tail feathers that extend past the body.

**The undertail coverts:** Deep maroon-red.

The overall effect is of a bird that appears to have been **hand-painted** -- no area of the body is plain. Every surface carries color, pattern, or iridescence. The green-to-purple head with white racing stripes is the most recognizable feature, but the entire bird is a composition of colors that should not work together but do. The Wood Duck is an argument for extravagance in nature.

**The female:** More subdued but still attractive. Gray-brown overall with a distinctive white **teardrop-shaped eye ring** (diagnostic), a blue speculum (wing patch), and a subtle iridescent purple-green wash on the crown. The female's elegance is quiet compared to the male's spectacle, but the white eye ring alone makes her identifiable.

**Eclipse plumage:** Males molt into eclipse plumage in late summer, resembling females but retaining the red eye and more prominent crest. The transformation from breeding to eclipse plumage is dramatic -- the most beautiful duck in North America becomes temporarily anonymous.

### Cavity Nesting -- Trees and Nest Boxes

The Wood Duck is an **obligate cavity nester** -- one of the few duck species that nests in tree cavities rather than on the ground. This nesting strategy connects it directly to the Hooded Merganser (degree 120), which shares the same requirement.

**Natural cavities:** Wood Ducks nest in tree cavities created by large woodpeckers (Pileated Woodpecker primarily) or by natural decay in large trees. The cavity must be:
- Located near water (typically within 1 mile, preferably within 100 meters)
- Large enough for the duck to enter (entrance hole 10+ cm)
- Deep enough for a clutch (30+ cm depth)
- Located at sufficient height (1-20 meters, typically 3-15 meters)

The dependence on large tree cavities means the Wood Duck requires **mature forests** near water. The loss of old-growth and mature bottomland hardwood forests was a primary driver of the Wood Duck's historical population decline.

**Nest boxes:** The Wood Duck is one of the greatest **conservation success stories** in North American waterfowl management. In the early 20th century, the species was severely depleted by market hunting and habitat loss (destruction of bottomland forests). The Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 ended market hunting, and beginning in the 1930s, a massive **nest box program** was launched across the species' range.

The nest box program was spectacularly successful. Millions of Wood Duck nest boxes have been erected across North America, providing artificial cavities that supplement the declining natural cavity supply. The Wood Duck population recovered from severe depletion to become one of the most common ducks in eastern North America. The species is now hunted legally (with bag limits) and is not considered threatened.

The nest box as conservation tool: the Wood Duck proved that providing habitat infrastructure (artificial cavities) could reverse population declines. This lesson was applied to other cavity-nesting species -- Hooded Merganser, Common Goldeneye, Barrow's Goldeneye, and others benefit from nest boxes originally designed for Wood Ducks.

**Dump nesting:** Wood Ducks are notorious for **dump nesting** -- females laying eggs in other females' nests. A single nest box may contain 20-40 eggs from multiple females. This brood parasitism complicates nest box management, as over-dumped nests have lower hatching success (the incubating female cannot adequately cover all eggs). Nest box placement must balance accessibility with the risk of dump nesting.

**The leap of faith:** Wood Duck ducklings hatch in the tree cavity and, at one day old, must **leap from the nest to the ground** -- a drop that may be 15 meters or more. The ducklings are tiny (20-30 grams), downy, and unable to fly. They leap on the mother's call, bounce off the ground or water surface (their light weight and downy insulation protect them), and then follow the mother to water. This "leap of faith" is one of the most remarkable behaviors in waterfowl -- newborn birds jumping from heights that would kill most animals their size. The behavior has been widely filmed and is a staple of wildlife documentaries.

### Wooded Pond Ecosystem -- The Second Consecutive

The Wood Duck's preferred habitat overlaps significantly with the Hooded Merganser's (degree 120): **wooded ponds, forested swamps, and quiet backwaters**. Both species require:
- Trees for nesting (cavity requirement)
- Sheltered, shallow water for feeding
- Vegetation for cover (both aquatic and terrestrial)

However, the Wood Duck's dietary preference differs from the merganser's:
- **Hooded Merganser:** primarily animal prey (crayfish, small fish, insects). Diving forager.
- **Wood Duck:** primarily plant material -- acorns, seeds, berries, and aquatic vegetation. Also eats insects and invertebrates, especially during breeding season. **Surface feeder** and **dabbler**, not a diver.

The Wood Duck's association with **oaks and acorns** is particularly strong. In autumn, Wood Ducks congregate in flooded bottomland oak forests to feed on fallen acorns. Oaks near water are the ideal Wood Duck habitat: the tree provides both food (acorns) and potential nesting cavities.

In the Pacific Northwest, Wood Ducks are found in the lowland forests and wetlands west of the Cascades -- the same habitat as the Hooded Merganser. They are year-round residents in western Washington, breeding in cavity-rich riparian zones and wintering on sheltered ponds and slow rivers. The summer ecosystem assignment for this entry reflects the peak nesting and rearing season.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Hepp & Bellrose 1995; North American Waterfowl Management Plan; nest box ecology literature)

## Acoustic Profile: The Rising Ooo-Eek Flight Call

The Wood Duck produces one of the most **distinctive and recognizable** calls in North American waterfowl: a rising **"ooo-eek!"** flight call that is diagnostic and unmistakable once learned.

### The Sound

- **The male flight call:** A sharp, rising, squealed **"ooo-eek!"** or **"jeeee!"** -- a thin, high-pitched, ascending call given primarily in flight. The call rises steeply in pitch over its duration (approximately 0.3-0.5 seconds), starting in the mid-frequency range and sweeping upward. The effect is plaintive, almost electronic in quality -- a rising whistle that cuts through the air.
- **Frequency range:** approximately 1,500-5,000 Hz, with the characteristic rising sweep. The peak frequency at the end of the call is notably high for a duck.
- **The female call:** A louder, more emphatic **"oo-eek, oo-eek!"** -- similar in pattern to the male but more drawn out and given more frequently. The female's flight call is the more commonly heard vocalization, as females call frequently during flight, during nest searching, and when calling ducklings.
- **The brood call:** The female's call to ducklings (especially during the nest-leap) is a rapid, low **"kuk-kuk-kuk"** -- very different from the flight call. This is the call that triggers the ducklings' leap from the nest.
- **Context:** The rising ooo-eek call is primarily an **in-flight contact call** -- the bird calls while flying through or over wooded areas. The call's rising pitch and carrying quality allow it to penetrate forest canopy. Unlike many duck calls, the Wood Duck's call is not a quack or honk -- it is a squealing whistle.

### E=4 -- The Distinctive Squeal

E=4 reflects the Wood Duck's moderate acoustic presence. The ooo-eek call is not loud in the way a Canada Goose honk is loud, but it is **piercing and carrying**. The rising pitch gives it a quality that cuts through ambient forest sound. The energy is moderate -- heard consistently in wooded pond environments but not a dominant sound. The distinctiveness comes from the **unusual quality** (a rising squeal from a duck) rather than volume.

The acoustic contrast with the Hooded Merganser (degree 120) is instructive: the merganser's rolling frog-like call (E=4) is low-pitched, guttural, and amphibian-like. The Wood Duck's ooo-eek (E=4) is high-pitched, rising, and almost electronic. Same energy level, radically different frequency profiles and timbres. Both at E=4, both on wooded ponds, both cavity nesters -- but acoustically opposite.

## The Genus Aix -- Two Beautiful Ducks, Two Continents

The genus *Aix* contains only two living species:
1. **Wood Duck** (*Aix sponsa*) -- North America
2. **Mandarin Duck** (*Aix galericulata*) -- East Asia

Both species are renowned for their extraordinary male plumage. The Mandarin Duck is to Asia what the Wood Duck is to North America -- the most spectacularly colored duck on its continent. Both are cavity nesters. Both are perching ducks (capable of perching on branches, unlike most ducks). Both have been cultural symbols for centuries: the Wood Duck in North American conservation lore, the Mandarin Duck in East Asian art and mythology (symbol of fidelity and love in Chinese and Japanese culture).

The genus *Aix* thus represents a **biogeographic mirror**: two parallel species on two continents, both evolving extraordinary plumage through sexual selection, both cavity nesting, both associated with wooded freshwater habitats. The Wood Duck at degree 121 carries this global parallel -- it is not just a North American species but one half of a two-species genus that spans the Northern Hemisphere.

(Sources: Ornithological taxonomy; biogeographic literature)

## Cultural Significance -- Conservation Icon

The Wood Duck is one of the most culturally significant waterfowl species in North America:

1. **Conservation success story.** The Wood Duck's recovery from severe depletion to abundance through nest box programs is a founding narrative of North American wildlife conservation. The story demonstrates that providing habitat infrastructure can reverse population declines.

2. **Hunting heritage.** The Wood Duck is one of the most popular game birds in eastern North America. Its beauty, its association with wooded waterways, and its challenging flight pattern (fast, darting through trees) make it a prized quarry. The recovery from near-extinction to sustainable harvest is celebrated.

3. **Art and iconography.** The Wood Duck is one of the most frequently depicted birds in North American wildlife art. Its plumage provides extraordinary subject matter. The Federal Duck Stamp program (funding for waterfowl habitat) has featured the Wood Duck multiple times.

4. **Symbol of wild water.** The Wood Duck requires clean water, mature trees, and functioning wetlands. Its presence in a landscape signals ecological health. The species is an indicator of intact riparian ecosystems.

(Sources: North American Waterfowl Management Plan; Federal Duck Stamp program; conservation history)
