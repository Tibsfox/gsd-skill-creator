## THE INFRASONIC DOMAIN -- BELOW THE THRESHOLD

### What "Infrasound" Means and Why It Matters

The infrasonic range and its significance:

- **Definition.** Infrasound is defined as acoustic energy at frequencies below 20 Hz -- the nominal lower threshold of human hearing. Sounds in the infrasonic range are not heard conventionally; they are felt as pressure variations in the body, particularly in the chest and abdomen. At high amplitude, infrasound can cause physiological effects: resonance with organ cavities, the sensation of unease or presence often associated with "hauntings" (many confirmed infrasound sources in reportedly haunted locations), and the feeling of physical weight or oppression that has no apparent source.
- **Infrasound in the natural world.** Many natural phenomena produce infrasound: earthquakes (primary source), volcanic activity, severe weather (tornadoes, hurricanes, large storms), ocean waves, large waterfalls, and the vocalizations of large animals (elephants communicate extensively via infrasound; the lowest baleen whale calls are at infrasonic frequencies). Infrasound in the natural environment carries over enormous distances -- much farther than audible sound -- because atmospheric and oceanic absorption is minimal at these frequencies.
- **The SOFAR channel.** The ocean has a natural acoustic waveguide called the SOFAR channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging channel, approximately 700-1200 meters depth, where temperature and pressure conditions create minimum sound velocity). In the SOFAR channel, low-frequency sound is trapped and propagates around the world with minimal loss. Baleen whales use the SOFAR channel for long-distance communication. Submarine volcanic seismic signals also propagate through the SOFAR channel. The combination of two infrasonic sources propagating through the SOFAR channel is the most far-reaching acoustic event in the SPS engine.

## SOURCE 1: BALEEN WHALE VOCALIZATION

### The Deepest Biological Sound on Earth

Baleen whale (Mysticeti suborder) vocalization and its acoustic properties:

- **The baleen whale species.** The relevant species for the lowest-frequency calls are the largest whales: Blue Whale (*Balaenoptera musculus*, the largest animal on Earth, 25-30 meters body length), Fin Whale (*Balaenoptera physalus*, second-largest, 20-26 meters), and to a lesser extent the other rorquals and right whales. These species are present in the Pacific Ocean, including the North Pacific adjacent to Washington State, and the deep Pacific pelagic zones.
- **Blue Whale vocalization.** The Blue Whale produces the lowest-frequency vocalizations of any animal. Its calls consist of: (1) 20-Hz tones (technically just above infrasound, but at the absolute lower boundary of human hearing); (2) songs in the 14-100 Hz range in various regional dialects; (3) "D calls" and "infrasonic moans" that extend below 20 Hz in some populations. The amplitude of Blue Whale calls is extraordinary: 155-188 decibels referenced to 1 micropascal at 1 meter (the reference used for underwater acoustics), making them among the loudest biological sounds ever recorded.
- **Fin Whale 20-Hz pulses.** The Fin Whale produces a characteristic series of 20-Hz pulses (exactly at the lower boundary of human hearing) in regular patterns of 0.5-1 Hz repetition rate. These pulses are produced primarily by males during the breeding season and are used for long-distance contact. The 20-Hz Fin Whale pulse is one of the most energetically concentrated single-frequency biological sounds in the ocean.
- **Propagation distance.** Low-frequency baleen whale calls propagate through the ocean for thousands of miles via the SOFAR channel. Blue Whale calls have been detected at distances exceeding 1,000 km from the source. Fin Whale 20-Hz pulses have been detected at trans-oceanic distances. The Pacific Ocean acoustic environment at low frequencies is continuously populated by baleen whale calls from animals that may be hundreds or thousands of miles away.
- **Pacific population context.** Blue Whales and Fin Whales are present in the North Pacific, Eastern Pacific, and deep Pacific pelagic zones. Blue Whale populations in the Pacific were severely reduced by commercial whaling in the 20th century; Eastern North Pacific Blue Whale populations (approximately 2,000 animals) are listed as endangered but have recovered slightly since the 1986 global whaling moratorium. Their calls still populate the Pacific infrasonic acoustic environment.

## SOURCE 2: SUBMARINE VOLCANIC SEISMIC TREMOR

### The Deep Floor's Infrasonic Signal

Submarine volcanism and its acoustic output:

- **Submarine volcanic geology.** The Pacific Ocean floor is dominated by the Pacific Ring of Fire -- the chain of volcanic arcs and mid-ocean ridges that encircles the Pacific Basin. The Cascadia Subduction Zone, where the Juan de Fuca and Gorda tectonic plates subduct beneath the North American Plate, extends from northern California to British Columbia and produces seismic activity that affects the entire Pacific Northwest coast. Offshore submarine volcanic systems include the Juan de Fuca Ridge (approximately 300-500 km off the Washington coast), where magmatic activity produces continuous low-level seismic signals.
- **Volcanic tremor.** Volcanic tremor is a sustained, non-impulsive seismic signal produced by the movement of magma through volcanic plumbing systems. Unlike an earthquake (an impulsive event), volcanic tremor is continuous or quasi-continuous, lasting from minutes to months. The frequency of volcanic tremor is typically in the range of 0.5-10 Hz -- entirely within the infrasonic range. The amplitude of volcanic tremor at the Juan de Fuca Ridge is detectable at seismometer stations on shore (the Olympic Peninsula station network detects Juan de Fuca seismicity routinely).
- **The Juan de Fuca Ridge.** The Juan de Fuca Ridge is the submarine spreading center between the Juan de Fuca and Pacific Plates, approximately 300-500 km off the Washington-Oregon coast. This is an active volcanic system: the Axial Seamount (approximately 480 km off the Washington coast) has erupted in 1998, 2011, and 2015, each time producing a seismic swarm detectable worldwide. Between eruptions, the ridge produces continuous low-level volcanic tremor and hydrothermal vent activity. The acoustic output of this system propagates through the SOFAR channel.
- **The acoustic signature.** Submarine volcanic seismic signals reach the water column through the ocean floor -- the coupling between solid earth (seismic wave) and liquid ocean (acoustic wave) at the seafloor interface. In the deep Pacific, where the seafloor is the bottom of the SOFAR channel's waveguide, seismic signals convert efficiently to acoustic signals. The volcanic tremor's 0.5-10 Hz frequency content enters the water column and propagates as infrasound throughout the Pacific.

## THE DEEP PACIFIC -- ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT

### Where the Infrasonic Dual Occurs

- **The deep Pacific pelagic zone.** The deep Pacific Ocean (greater than 2,000 meters depth) occupies the largest contiguous acoustic environment on Earth. The Pacific Ocean covers 165 million square kilometers and has an average depth of 4,280 meters. The pelagic zone (open water, away from coastal influence) contains the SOFAR channel, the whale migration routes, and the submarine volcanic systems that produce the infrasonic dual.
- **The Washington State connection.** The Juan de Fuca Ridge is specifically the submarine volcanic system adjacent to Washington State's offshore zone. The infrasonic signal of Juan de Fuca volcanic activity is literally the Pacific Northwest's deep floor, heard from the deep.
- **The Blue Whale's Pacific range.** Eastern North Pacific Blue Whales (the population most likely to be off the Washington coast) migrate between summer feeding grounds off California/Oregon and winter breeding grounds in the central Pacific. Their annual migration route passes through the offshore Washington zone. Their calls, propagating through the SOFAR channel, are part of the permanent acoustic inventory of the Pacific Northwest's deep water.
