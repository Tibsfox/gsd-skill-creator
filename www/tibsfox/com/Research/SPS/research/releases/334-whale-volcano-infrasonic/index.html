The SOFAR Channel — Planetary Acoustic Waveguide

The SOFAR channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging, approximately 700-1200 meters depth) is the ocean's natural acoustic waveguide. Where temperature and pressure create minimum sound velocity, low-frequency sound is trapped and propagates with minimal absorption. Baleen whales have evolved to use this channel for long-distance communication — Blue Whale calls have been detected 1,000+ km from their source. Submarine volcanic seismic signals couple from the seafloor into the water column and also propagate through this channel. Both infrasonic sources in Degree 334 use the same planetary-scale acoustic delivery system.

The Axial Seamount — Maximum Expression Site

Axial Seamount is an active submarine volcano on the Juan de Fuca Ridge, approximately 480 km off the Washington coast. It erupted in 1998, 2011, and 2015, each time generating a seismic swarm detectable worldwide. During an eruption event, the volcanic tremor reaches its maximum amplitude — and the Eastern North Pacific Blue Whale migration route passes through the offshore Washington zone. When an eruption coincides with nearby calling Blue Whales, the infrasonic dual is at its E=9 maximum.
