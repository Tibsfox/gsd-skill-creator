## What Worked

1. **The E=8 "routine maximum" definition.** The most important conceptual contribution: E=8 is not the absolute maximum of natural sound (volcanic eruptions, major earthquakes exceed it) but the routine maximum -- the most energetic thing that happens predictably, annually. Pacific winter storm surf on the outer coast meets this definition precisely. This grounds the energy rating in observable reality.
2. **The storm formation → fetch → swell organization sequence.** The acoustic event begins thousands of miles away with storm formation in the North Pacific. Documenting the full causal chain (storm → fetch → swell → coast) situates the outer coast acoustic event within the larger Pacific system.
3. **The four-phase acoustic sequence.** Initial crash → roar → infrasound → spray. Each phase has different frequency character and range. The infrasound component (microseismic noise detectable on global seismometers) is the entry's most surprising scientific insight.
4. **Cape Disappointment as central geography.** Oldest lighthouse on the Pacific coast (1856), national rescue swimmer training, the Columbia River bar as "Graveyard of the Pacific": Cape Disappointment is the correct geographic anchor for this entry.
5. **The Storm Arc opening.** Documenting the 256-260 arc structure at the point of its opening: Pacific storm energy from outer coast (256) to interior expressions (257-260). The arc logic is clear.
6. **The Makah Nation.** The only lower-48 tribal group with treaty rights protecting ocean whale hunting. The deepest human engagement with Pacific winter swells in the region -- from a traditional ocean canoe, not from shore.

## Discoveries

1. **Infrasound propagates globally.** Winter storm surf generates sub-20 Hz infrasound that travels as microseismic noise detectable on seismometers worldwide. The winter storm at Cape Disappointment produces a seismic signal that can be read in Japan and Europe. This is one of the most surprising facts in the research.
2. **The roadless 73-mile Olympic coast.** One of the longest undeveloped coastlines in the lower 48 states experiences full Pacific storm intensity, witnessed by almost no one. The acoustic event occurs regardless of human observation.
3. **Storm watching as tourism.** The outer coast has developed winter storm watching as a commercial product: coastal lodges promote the storm season, sell rooms with ocean views during major wave events. The destructive sublime is a marketable experience.
