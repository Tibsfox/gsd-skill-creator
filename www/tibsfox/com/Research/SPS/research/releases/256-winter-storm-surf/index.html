The Storm Forms

Pacific winter storms generate in the North Pacific storm track: the zone of intense cyclonic activity running from Japan northeastward toward the Aleutian Islands and then southeastward toward the Pacific Northwest coast. Winter is the peak season: the temperature differential between cold continent and warm Pacific is greatest, driving the most intense low-pressure systems. Fetch — the distance over which wind blows consistently across open ocean — can be thousands of miles in the North Pacific. The result: long-period swell (12-20 seconds between wave crests) with enormous energy storage. NOAA Grays Harbor buoy (Station 46029) routinely records winter significant wave heights of 15-25 feet; major storm events exceed 30 feet.

The Acoustic Sequence

  • The initial crashWave face collapses: crest pitches forward, trapped air compresses suddenly. Low-frequency concussive thud. Felt in chest. At 25 feet: physically overwhelming.
  • The roarWhite water cascades up beach face: aerated, turbulent, broad-spectrum. The sustained ambient "sound of the surf" between crashes. At E=8: continuous, miles in radius.
  • InfrasoundWave-wave interaction and pressure oscillations generate sub-20 Hz infrasound. Propagates through ocean and ground. Detectable as microseismic noise on global seismometer networks.
  • SprayAt high wind: saltwater spray elevated and carried inland. High-frequency hiss overlaid on the low-frequency roar and crash.

The Outer Coast -- Where It Happens

Cape Disappointment

Mouth of the Columbia River. "Graveyard of the Pacific." Lighthouse est. 1856 (oldest on Pacific coast). Coast Guard national rescue swimmer training. Columbia River bar: closed to shipping in major wave events.

Long Beach Peninsula

28-mile spit north of Cape Disappointment. One of longest beaches in US. Pacific-facing, unobstructed. Winter waves 20+ feet. Storm watching tourism.

Kalaloch & Ruby Beach

Olympic National Park south coast. Sea stacks. Wave refraction creates complex, amplified acoustics. Ruby Beach: one of the most dramatic coastal landscapes in the continental US.

Olympic Coast (roadless)

73 miles, Cape Flattery to Queets River. One of longest undeveloped coastlines in lower 48. Winter storm surf experienced here by almost no one. Full Pacific intensity, wilderness witness.

Cultural Witness

The Makah Nation at Cape Flattery: the only tribal group in the lower 48 with treaty rights specifically protecting ocean whale hunting. Traditional Makah whaling in ocean-going canoes represents the deepest human engagement with Pacific winter swells in the PNW. The acoustic and physical experience of large swells from a traditional canoe is categorically different from shore observation. Cape Flattery is where the Sound begins.
