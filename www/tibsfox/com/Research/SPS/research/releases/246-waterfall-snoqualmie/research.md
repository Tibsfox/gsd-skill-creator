## SNOQUALMIE FALLS -- PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

### 268 Feet of Basalt Cliff

Snoqualmie Falls is one of the most volumetrically significant waterfalls in the Pacific Northwest:

- **The falls.** The Snoqualmie River drops 268 feet (82 meters) over a basalt cliff in the town of Snoqualmie, approximately 30 miles east of Seattle. For scale: Niagara Falls is approximately 167 feet (51 meters) at its Horseshoe Falls. Snoqualmie Falls is 100 feet taller than Niagara.
- **The basalt geology.** The cliff face is Columbia River Basalt Group -- a massive volcanic formation laid down between 17 and 6 million years ago by successive lava flows from fissure eruptions in what is now eastern Washington and Oregon. The basalt flowed westward from the source region, filling river valleys and eventually reaching the Pacific. The flat, columnar structure of basalt, combined with the differential hardness of basalt vs. the softer soils above, creates the vertical cliff face that makes the falls possible. The Snoqualmie River simply runs off the edge of the hard basalt into the softer valley below.
- **The flow volume.** At peak flow (spring snowmelt), Snoqualmie Falls carries approximately 25,000 cubic feet per second. At minimum flow (late summer), the falls may carry less than 1,000 cubic feet per second. The acoustic character changes dramatically between these extremes: at peak flow, the falls is an overwhelming, physically felt roar that obscures all other sounds within hundreds of meters; at low flow, the falls is still powerful but the individual water streams are discernible.
- **The gorge.** Below the falls, the Snoqualmie River enters a steep gorge before widening into the broader Snoqualmie Valley. The gorge walls amplify the falls' sound, creating a resonance that increases apparent volume and adds reverberation. A visitor standing at the base observation platform is immersed in sound that seems to come from every direction.
- **The viewing platforms.** Snoqualmie Falls Preserve (managed by Puget Sound Energy, which operates a hydroelectric plant within the cliff) has two primary viewing areas: the upper observation deck (looking down at the falls) and a lower trail leading to a base area. The base area provides the closest access and the most overwhelming acoustic experience.

(Sources: Snoqualmie Falls height 268 feet; Columbia River Basalt Group geology; lava flow history; flow volume data; gorge amplification; Puget Sound Energy hydroelectric; viewing areas)

## THE SNOQUALMIE TRIBE -- SACRED GEOGRAPHY

### Falls as the Center of the World

The Snoqualmie Falls is not merely a natural landmark to the Snoqualmie Tribe -- it is one of the most sacred sites in their cultural geography:

- **The Snoqualmie Tribe.** The Snoqualmie (also spelled Snoqualmoo) are a Coast Salish people who have lived in the Snoqualmie Valley and the surrounding Cascade foothills since time immemorial. Their territory extended from the Cascade Mountain passes to the shores of Puget Sound, with the Snoqualmie River as the central geographical and cultural axis.
- **The cultural significance of the falls.** In Snoqualmie oral tradition, the falls is *Sahalie Tyee* -- the Transformer. This is the location where the ancestral figure Transformer created the first human beings, shaping them from the land itself. The falls is the origin point, the place where the present world came into being. It is not a metaphor -- it is the literal site of creation in Snoqualmie cosmology.
- **Spiritual practice.** The falls has been a site of spiritual practice, purification, and prayer for the Snoqualmie Tribe for thousands of years. Tribal members still practice these traditions at the site. The water of the falls, falling from the heights, carries spiritual significance: it comes from the mountains (associated with spiritual power) and falls to earth (the transition between worlds). The mist of the falls is itself considered to have purifying properties.
- **The tribal land relationship.** The Snoqualmie Tribe ceded most of their territory in the Point Elliott Treaty of 1855 but retained rights to the falls site as a sacred place. The relationship between the tribe and the falls has been complicated by the subsequent history of ownership (private, then utility company), tourism development, and the hydroelectric facility within the cliff. The tribe has worked to maintain cultural access and to ensure that the falls' spiritual significance is respected by the entities that manage the surrounding land.
- **The Treaty of Point Elliott (1855).** The treaty between the US government and multiple Puget Sound tribes guaranteed fishing rights "in common with all citizens" and reserved certain lands. The Snoqualmie signed this treaty. The subsequent history of land management around the falls involved multiple non-tribal entities taking control of the surrounding property. The tribe's relationship to the falls has required ongoing negotiation and advocacy.
- **Contemporary tribal relations.** The Snoqualmie Tribe is a federally recognized tribe with its headquarters in Snoqualmie, Washington. The tribe has worked on cultural preservation, language revitalization (the Snohomish/Snoqualmie language is endangered), and land stewardship projects in the Snoqualmie Valley. The tribe's relationship to the falls remains active and central to tribal identity.

(Sources: Snoqualmie Tribe history; Coast Salish peoples; Sahalie Tyee; Transformer narrative; Point Elliott Treaty 1855; tribal land rights; Puget Sound Energy relationship; federal recognition; contemporary tribal relations; Snohomish-Snoqualmie language)

## TWIN PEAKS -- THE FALLS AS CULTURAL ICON

### David Lynch, Mark Frost, and the Opening of the Most Influential Television Series of Its Era

Snoqualmie Falls occupies a specific and famous position in American popular culture through its association with *Twin Peaks*:

- ***Twin Peaks* (1990-1991, 2017).** Created by David Lynch and Mark Frost, *Twin Peaks* is one of the most influential television series in American broadcasting history. The show pioneered the serialized narrative, the unreliable narrator, and the blending of soap opera, horror, mystery, and surrealism in a format that would not be matched in ambition until the prestige TV era of the 2000s-2010s. *Twin Peaks* can legitimately claim to be the direct ancestor of *The Sopranos*, *Lost*, *Breaking Bad*, and dozens of other serialized dramas.
- **The opening sequence.** Every episode of *Twin Peaks* opens with a sequence of establishing shots: a bird on a wire, a sawmill, and -- most memorably -- Snoqualmie Falls. The falling water, accompanied by Angelo Badalamenti's iconic theme music, is the visual statement that grounds the show in the Pacific Northwest before any narrative element appears. The falls is Twin Peaks' visual signature.
- **Why Snoqualmie Falls?** Lynch and Frost chose Snoqualmie Falls for its combination of visual grandeur and the sense of hidden depth -- the falls plunges into a gorge that you cannot see into from above. This visual quality -- something powerful and large happening beyond your line of sight, in a place you cannot reach -- is central to the thematic content of the show. *Twin Peaks* is about what lies beneath the beautiful surface of an American small town. The falls, which hides its base from above, is the show's central metaphor made landscape.
- **Filming locations.** The show filmed extensively in and around Snoqualmie, North Bend, and the surrounding Cascade foothills. The Great Northern Hotel (the fictional lodge in the show) was the Salish Lodge at Snoqualmie Falls -- which actually sits on the cliff directly above the falls. The diner, the roadhouse, the high school -- all filmed in actual locations in King County, Washington.
- **The mist and the sound.** In the show's visual language, the mist rising from the falls (the water vapor produced by the falls' collision with the pool below) is aesthetically central. The mist = mystery = the show's tonal identity. The actual acoustic character of the falls -- the overwhelming white-noise roar -- is present in the establishing shots in a way that the television medium cannot fully convey.

(Sources: Twin Peaks 1990-1991; David Lynch; Mark Frost; Angelo Badalamenti theme; opening sequence; Snoqualmie Falls as visual signature; Salish Lodge filming; North Bend, WA filming locations; serialized narrative influence)

## THE SOUND -- ROARING WHITE-NOISE WALL, E=7

### What a 268-Foot Waterfall Sounds Like

The acoustic character of Snoqualmie Falls is distinct from all other waterfall types:

- **White noise as acoustic concept.** White noise is sound that contains equal energy at all frequencies within a defined bandwidth -- it is the audio equivalent of white light (which contains all frequencies of visible light). A large waterfall approaches white noise: the turbulent, chaotic collision of enormous volumes of water with a deep pool generates sound at every audible frequency simultaneously. There is no dominant pitch -- only an omnipresent broadband roar.
- **Volume and directionality.** Snoqualmie Falls produces approximately 85-100 decibels at typical viewing distances (30-50 meters). This is in the range where conversation is difficult and sustained exposure becomes physiologically stressful. The sound has no single source direction -- it surrounds the observer, reflected off the cliff walls and gorge surfaces. Standing at the base platform, the sound is physically felt as well as heard: the chest cavity resonates with low frequencies.
- **The spray and mist.** The water vapor produced by the falls' collision with the pool creates a constant spray of fine mist that rises back up the gorge. The mist carries the sound upward, giving the falls its presence even at the upper observation deck 300 meters from the base.
- **E=7 compared to E=5.** The river current (degree 245) is E=5 -- continuous and present but not overwhelming. The falls at E=7 represents the step change when a river collects its force into a single vertical drop: the distributed energy of a river in motion becomes the concentrated energy of free fall. E=7 is appropriate for a sound that requires raised voices to communicate through.

## HYDROELECTRIC -- THE FALLS AS POWER INFRASTRUCTURE

### Puget Sound Energy and the Underground Powerhouse

Snoqualmie Falls has been used for hydroelectric power generation since 1899:

- **The first plant (1899).** The original hydroelectric plant at Snoqualmie Falls was built in 1899 by the Snoqualmie Falls Power Company. It was one of the first underground powerhouses in the world -- the generators are located inside the cliff, carved into the rock below the falls. Water is diverted from above the falls, channeled through penstocks within the cliff, and released through turbines to generate power before re-entering the river below.
- **The current plant.** The current underground powerhouse is operated by Puget Sound Energy and has a generating capacity of approximately 56 megawatts. The plant continues to supply power to the regional grid.
- **Water diversion effects.** The hydroelectric diversion reduces the volume of water going over the falls. At low-flow periods, the visible curtain of water is smaller than the river's natural flow would produce. The acoustic character of the falls during these periods is also reduced. The diversion is a trade-off between power generation and the aesthetic/ecological experience of the falls at full flow.
- **The visual paradox.** Snoqualmie Falls as a tourist destination and as a power infrastructure are the same physical place. The cliff contains generators while the falls plunges in front of them. The lodge sits on the cliff above. The sacred site, the power plant, the tourist destination, and the film location are all the same location -- a layering of uses and meanings that is unusual even by Pacific Northwest standards.

(Sources: Snoqualmie Falls Power Company 1899; underground powerhouse; Puget Sound Energy; 56 MW generating capacity; hydroelectric diversion effects; water flow reduction)
