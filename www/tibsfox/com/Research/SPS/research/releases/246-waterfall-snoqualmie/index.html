SACRED TO THE SNOQUALMIE TRIBE — THE ORIGIN PLACE

Snoqualmie Falls is *Sahalie Tyee* — the Transformer — in Snoqualmie oral tradition. This is the location where the ancestral Transformer created the first human beings. It is the origin point of the present world in Snoqualmie cosmology. The Snoqualmie Tribe, a federally recognized Coast Salish nation, has maintained spiritual practice at this site for thousands of years. Tribal members continue to practice prayer and purification at the falls today. The engine documents this cultural significance with the respect it is due.

Cultural note: The spiritual significance of Snoqualmie Falls to the Snoqualmie Tribe is living and active, not historical. This entry documents the falls as both natural phenomenon and sacred place, acknowledging that these dimensions coexist for the Snoqualmie people and their relationship to this landscape.

The Falls — Physical Reality

Height: 268 Feet

82 meters. 100 feet taller than Niagara Falls (Horseshoe). One of the most volumetrically significant waterfalls in the Pacific Northwest.

Basalt Geology

Columbia River Basalt Group — 17-6 million years old. Fissure eruptions from eastern Washington/Oregon. The hard basalt cliff makes the vertical drop possible. The Snoqualmie River runs off the edge.

Flow Volume

Peak (spring snowmelt): ~25,000 cubic feet/second. Low (late summer): ~1,000 cfs. The acoustic character changes dramatically between extremes. Peak flow: overwhelming roar, physically felt.

Gorge Amplification

The gorge below the falls reflects sound from all walls. Standing at the base observation platform, sound comes from every direction. The gorge amplifies the apparent volume and adds reverberation.

The Sound — White-Noise Wall, E=7

A 268-foot waterfall at significant flow produces sound at all audible frequencies simultaneously — this is the acoustic definition of white noise. There is no dominant pitch. The roar is omnipresent, reflected off cliff walls and gorge surfaces, felt as vibration in the chest as well as heard. Approximately 85-100 decibels at typical viewing distances (30-50 meters). Conversation requires raised voices.

Acoustic Profile — E=7 White-Noise Wall

Sub-bass impact (20–80 Hz): physically felt resonance in chest cavity
Mid-range roar (80–2000 Hz): full energy, no dominant pitch
High-frequency spray (2000+ Hz): mist and fine water vapor
Overall: E=7 (overwhelms at close range; requires raised voices 30m away)

Twin Peaks

David Lynch • Mark Frost • 1990–1991 • The Opening Image

Every episode of Twin Peaks opens with Snoqualmie Falls as its establishing visual: Angelo Badalamenti’s theme music, a bird on a wire, a sawmill, and the falls. The falling water grounds the show in the Pacific Northwest before any narrative element appears. Lynch chose Snoqualmie Falls for its visual quality — the falls plunges into a gorge you cannot see into from above. Something powerful happening beyond your line of sight: the central metaphor of Twin Peaks, made landscape. The Salish Lodge (above the falls) is the fictional Great Northern Hotel. The show filmed throughout King County, WA.

“The falls plunges into a gorge that you cannot see into from above. Something powerful and large happening beyond your line of sight, in a place you cannot reach — this is the Twin Peaks metaphor made landscape.”

Hydroelectric History

Snoqualmie Falls has generated electricity since 1899 — one of the first underground powerhouses in the world, with generators carved into the basalt cliff. Puget Sound Energy operates the current plant (approximately 56 MW). The water diversion for power generation reduces the visible curtain of water at low-flow periods. The sacred site, the power plant, the tourist destination, and the film location are the same physical place.
