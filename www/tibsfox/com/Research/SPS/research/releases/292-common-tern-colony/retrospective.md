## What Worked

1. **The tern as arc-closing entry is the correct design.** The Laridae arc opens with the most common, most ambient gull species (Glaucous-winged) and closes with the most acoustically distinct family member (Common Tern, Sterninae sub-family). The progression from abundant-to-common, ambient-to-sharp, large-to-smaller, gull-to-tern is a genuine arc with a beginning, middle, and end.
2. **The acoustic shift at 292 is the most important fact about this entry.** The Common Tern's keerr call at 2,000-4,000 Hz fundamental is measurably higher than any preceding arc entry's primary vocalization. The frequency shift marks the sub-family shift. The ear can detect the difference before the taxonomy explains it.
3. **Dive-bombing is the arc's most kinetically intense alarm behavior.** No gull entry features the physical dive-bombing alarm. The Common Tern's willingness to strike an intruder in the head with its bill, while calling sharply, while multiple colony members do the same from different angles simultaneously -- this is a qualitatively different alarm response than the purely acoustic gull entries. The kinetic + acoustic combination is E=7 by a different mechanism than the gull entries.
4. **The dual arc closure at 292 is a structural hinge.** The S36 E=8 streak (290-292) and the SPS Laridae arc (289-292) close at the same degree. This is not coincidental planning by the engine's curators -- it is the degree landing where it does in the rotation. Both Gas Huffer and the Common Tern are the closing entries of their respective arcs. The degree inherits this structural significance.
5. **East Sand Island is the most significant Pacific Northwest Common Tern site and deserves documentation.** The managed colony at the Columbia River mouth is one of the PNW's most important colonial waterbird sites. Its management history (managing to reduce Caspian Tern competition with Chinook salmon smolt, managing for tern breeding success) is Pacific Northwest conservation biology, documented at degree 292.

## Discoveries

1. **The Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*) is arguably the more conspicuous PNW tern but the Common Tern is the sub-family representative.** The choice of Common Tern over Caspian Tern for the arc is justified: the Common Tern's colonial keerr screaming is more emblematic of the tern acoustic mode than the Caspian's louder but more raucous call. The Common Tern's frequency range is the cleanest representation of what makes terns acoustically distinct from gulls.
2. **The Forster's Tern (*Sterna forsteri*) co-breeds with Common Terns at East Sand Island.** Mixed tern colonies produce multi-species acoustic environments where Common Tern and Forster's Tern calls overlap. The distinction between the two species' keerr calls is subtle and requires attention to detail. The mixed colony at East Sand Island is more acoustically complex than a single-species tern colony.
