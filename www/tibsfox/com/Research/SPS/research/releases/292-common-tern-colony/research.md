## FOURTH LARIDAE -- THE ARC CLOSES

### Terns: The Laridae Sub-Family

Degree 292 closes the Laridae arc with the introduction of the tern sub-family:

- **289: Glaucous-winged Gull colony (E=6).** The ambient colonial chorus of the most common PNW gull. Salish Sea. Summer. The arc's lowest energy, the founding entry.
- **290: Herring Gull flock alarm (E=7).** Winter beach. Triggered alarm event. The arc's pivot to winter ecology.
- **291: Western Gull colony (E=7).** Pacific coast rocky island. Second colony soundscape. The Glaucous-winged's hybrid partner.
- **292: Common Tern colony (E=7).** Sandy spit. The tern sub-family enters. The arc closes with a fundamentally different bird: not a gull but a tern. Same family, different sub-family. Different call character, different body form, different flight style, different nesting habitat.
- **The sub-family distinction.** The Common Tern (*Sterna hirundo*) belongs to the tern subfamily Sterninae within Laridae. Terns differ from gulls in several key ways: smaller body, more buoyant flight, forked tail (giving them the folk name "sea swallow"), tendency to hover and plunge-dive for fish rather than surface-foraging like gulls, and significantly sharper and higher-pitched vocalizations. The arc closes with a bird that is recognizably Laridae but acoustically and behaviorally distinct from all three preceding gull entries.
- **Why terns close the arc.** The arc structure moves from the most familiar and common (Glaucous-winged Gull, 289) through two gull variants (Herring Gull alarm, Western Gull colony) to the sub-family representative (Common Tern, 292). The progression from abundant to less common, from gull to tern, from ambient to sharp, is deliberate.

## STERNA HIRUNDO -- THE SPECIES

### The Common Tern: Widespread, Migratory, Precisely Vocal

*Sterna hirundo* (Linnaeus, 1758) is one of the most widely distributed terns in the world:

- **Identification.** A medium-sized tern (wingspan 30-33 inches / 76-84 cm -- substantially smaller than the large gulls at degrees 289-291). Adults in breeding plumage have a black cap, gray back and upperwings, white underparts, deeply forked white tail (the "sea swallow" of traditional sailors), orange-red bill with black tip (in breeding plumage), and red legs. The deeply forked tail is the tern's structural signature -- it allows the buoyant, tern-specific flight style that is completely different from a gull's flat-winged soar.
- **Scientific name.** *Sterna hirundo*. *Sterna* (from Old English *stearn* or similar Scandinavian/Germanic root, meaning tern); *hirundo* (Latin: swallow). The name "swallow tern" captures the tern's flight character -- it flies like a swallow, not like a gull. Named by Linnaeus in 1758.
- **Global distribution.** The Common Tern has one of the most extensive breeding ranges of any bird species: across North America, Europe, Asia, and into the Southern Hemisphere. In North America, the species breeds primarily in the Great Lakes, the Atlantic coast, and locally in interior wetlands. It is migratory across most of its range, wintering in South America and other tropical zones.
- **Pacific Northwest status.** The Common Tern is less common in the Pacific Northwest than on the Atlantic coast. In Washington State, the species breeds locally at specific colony sites -- notably the mouth of the Columbia River, Dungeness Spit (Clallam County), and at some interior lake island sites. It is a migrant and breeding bird, not the year-round resident that the Glaucous-winged Gull is.
- **Pacific vs. Atlantic.** Common Terns are more abundant and have more extensive breeding populations on the Atlantic coast of North America. The Pacific population is smaller. In the Pacific Northwest, the Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*) is arguably more conspicuous than the Common Tern, and the Black Tern (*Chlidonias niger*) breeds inland. But the Common Tern is the species chosen for the arc because it is the tern that best represents the family's acoustic character in a colonial context.

(Sources: Sterna hirundo identification; hirundo = swallow; Linnaeus 1758; global distribution; Pacific Northwest breeding sites: Columbia River mouth, Dungeness Spit; Pacific vs. Atlantic population comparison; Caspian Tern and Black Tern PNW context)

## THE COLONY VOCALIZATION -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Sharp, High-Pitched, Emphatic

The Common Tern colony soundscape is acoustically distinct from the three gull entries that precede it in the arc:

- **The keerr call.** The Common Tern's primary alarm and contact call at the colony is a sharp, emphatic "keerr" or "kee-arr" -- a two-syllable call with a harsh, penetrating quality. The keerr is higher-pitched than any of the gull calls documented at 289-291. Its sharpness is the acoustic signature of the tern sub-family: designed to penetrate and carry at a high pitch rather than the medium-pitch mewing of gulls.
- **Frequency range.** Common Tern vocalizations are concentrated approximately 1,000-6,000 Hz, with the keerr call's fundamental frequency approximately 2,000-4,000 Hz -- significantly higher than the Glaucous-winged Gull's mew (800-1,500 Hz) or the Herring Gull's alarm (800-4,000 Hz). The higher frequency creates the sharp, penetrating quality that is the tern's acoustic character.
- **Colony chorus.** At an active Common Tern colony, the keerr call is continuous -- overlapping from dozens to hundreds of birds simultaneously. The colony chorus is higher-pitched and has a more emphatic, staccato quality than gull colony sounds. It is not the sustained mewing drone of a gull colony but a more urgent, percussive soundscape.
- **Dive-bombing alarm.** The Common Tern's most dramatic colony alarm behavior is dive-bombing: a bird that detects a threat (human intruder, potential predator) will fly at the intruder, calling sharply, and strike or narrowly miss the intruder's head with its bill. At an active colony, multiple birds dive-bomb simultaneously from different angles. This behavior adds a kinetic dimension to the colony's alarm response that is absent in the gull entries -- the sound of sharp keerr calls combined with the audible wingbeat of close-passing terns is a complete sensory experience.
- **E=7 calibration.** The Common Tern colony is rated E=7, matching the Herring Gull alarm (290) and Western Gull colony (291). The higher pitch of the tern's calls gives them a penetrating quality that justifies equal intensity rating despite the tern's smaller body. The dive-bombing alarm behavior, which involves physical proximity to the intruder, is a high-intensity response that the gull entries do not match.

(Sources: Common Tern keerr call; two-syllable kee-arr vocalization; frequency range 1,000-6,000 Hz; keerr fundamental 2,000-4,000 Hz; colony chorus character; dive-bombing alarm behavior; E=7 calibration at higher pitch)

## SANDY SPIT COLONIAL NESTING

### Low-Profile, Exposed Habitat

The Common Tern's nesting habitat is specifically the low-profile open substrate:

- **Sandy spits and barrier beaches.** Common Terns nest on flat, exposed, low-profile substrates: sandy spits at river mouths and coastal inlets, barrier beaches, gravel bars, lake islands. The nesting habitat is exposed and open -- there is no cliff face or elevated rocky island to provide visual advantage or acoustic reflectance. The colony is flat, and the birds' defense of it is behavioral (dive-bombing, alarm calling) rather than topographic.
- **Ground nesting.** Like all terns, the Common Tern is a ground nester: the nest is a shallow scrape in sand or gravel, with minimal construction. The exposure of the nest means the colony is extremely vulnerable to flooding (storm surge, river floods), predation, and human disturbance. Common Tern colonies are sensitive and often managed at maintained colony sites.
- **Colony sites in the Pacific Northwest.** Washington State Common Tern colonies include sites at the mouth of the Columbia River (particularly East Sand Island, one of the most significant Common Tern colonies in the Pacific Northwest), Dungeness Spit, and occasional inland sites. East Sand Island is notable as a managed colony site where multiple tern species (Common, Caspian, Forster's) and other waterbirds nest in proximity.
- **Colony size.** Common Tern colonies in the Pacific Northwest range from a few dozen pairs to several hundred pairs at established sites. Larger colonies produce the most intense acoustic environments. At East Sand Island, mixed tern colonies produce complex multi-species soundscapes.
- **Vulnerability to disturbance.** Common Tern colonies are among the most sensitive colonial waterbird nesting sites to human disturbance. The dive-bombing alarm behavior is a defense mechanism that also signals the colony's high vulnerability: the birds are alarmed precisely because a ground nesting colony on an exposed sandy spit has few passive defenses. Human approach to within the dive-bombing radius disrupts incubation and chick-rearing.

(Sources: Sandy spit and barrier beach nesting habitat; ground nesting shallow scrape; Columbia River mouth colonies: East Sand Island; Dungeness Spit; colony size PNW; mixed tern colonies; sensitivity to disturbance; dive-bombing as high-vulnerability indicator)

### 289-292: Four Degrees, Four Ecological Contexts, One Family

The Laridae arc closes at degree 292 with the Common Tern. The full arc:

- **Energy shape.** E=6 (289, Glaucous-winged colony) → E=7 (290, Herring Gull alarm) → E=7 (291, Western Gull colony) → E=7 (292, Common Tern colony). The arc opens at E=6 and holds at E=7 for three consecutive degrees. The Glaucous-winged colony is the only E=6 entry; all others are E=7.
- **Seasonal shape.** Summer (289, colonial) → Winter (290, flock alarm) → Summer (291, colonial) → Summer (292, colonial). The arc is primarily a summer colonial phenomenon, with one winter exception (290).
- **Acoustic shape.** Ambient chorus → Triggered alarm → Colony chorus → Tern colony screaming. Each entry introduces a distinct acoustic mode or character within the family's repertoire.
- **Geographic shape.** Salish Sea (289) → PNW coast and beach (290) → Pacific coast outer coast (291) → Sandy spit and river mouth (292). The arc's geographic range covers the Pacific Northwest's major gull and tern habitats.
- **Taxonomic shape.** Glaucous-winged Gull (genus Larus) → Herring Gull (genus Larus) → Western Gull (genus Larus) → Common Tern (genus Sterna). The arc moves within the gull genus for three degrees, then steps to the tern genus for the closing entry. The taxonomic shift mirrors the acoustic shift: Sterna is distinctly higher-pitched than Larus.
- **The arc as family portrait.** Together, degrees 289-292 provide a four-entry portrait of the Laridae family in the Pacific Northwest: the most common species (Glaucous-winged), the wintering visitor (Herring Gull), the Pacific coast specialist (Western Gull), and the tern representative (Common Tern). It is not a complete catalog -- there are many more Laridae species in the PNW -- but it is a representative sample that covers the family's ecological range.
