Acoustic Profile — Tern Colony Screaming

Keerr call: Sharp, emphatic "keerr" or "kee-arr" — two syllables, harsh, penetrating. Fundamental frequency 2,000–4,000 Hz. Significantly higher-pitched than all preceding gull entries (289-291).

Colony chorus: Dozens to hundreds of birds simultaneously. Overlapping keerr calls create continuous higher-pitched colony soundscape. More staccato and percussive quality than gull mewing chorus.

Dive-bombing alarm: Physical approach to intruder with sharp calls and near-miss or contact strikes. Multiple birds dive-bombing simultaneously from different angles. Kinetic + acoustic alarm. Unique in the arc.

E=7: Higher pitch gives penetrating quality equal to E=7 despite smaller body. Dive-bombing behavior amplifies alarm intensity above gull behavioral repertoire.

The Tern Sub-Family — A Different Bird

The Common Tern is recognizably Laridae but distinctly not a gull. Smaller, lighter, more buoyant. Deeply forked tail — the structural basis for the tern's flight style, which resembles a swallow more than a gull. Named hirundo (Latin: swallow) by Linnaeus in 1758 for exactly this reason. The tern hovers and plunge-dives for fish; the gull surface-forages and scavenges. The tern is a precision predator; the gull is an opportunist.

These behavioral differences produce acoustic differences. The tern's keerr call is sharp, high-pitched, emphatic — a different register from the gull's mewing. The frequency range of Common Tern vocalizations (1,000-6,000 Hz; keerr fundamental 2,000-4,000 Hz) is substantially higher than the large gulls at degrees 289-291 (500-4,000 Hz; mew fundamental 800-1,500 Hz). The acoustic shift at 292 marks the taxonomic shift from gull to tern.

Sandy Spit — The Most Exposed Colony Habitat

Common Terns nest on flat, exposed, low-profile substrates: sandy spits, barrier beaches, gravel bars, lake islands. There is no cliff face, no elevation, no topographic defense. The colony is completely exposed. The bird's only defenses are behavioral: alarm calling, dive-bombing, and the collective vigilance of the colony. This exposure makes Common Tern colonies among the most sensitive colonial waterbird nesting sites to human disturbance — the dive-bombing alarm is the sign of a colony with no passive defenses.

Key Pacific Northwest colony sites: East Sand Island at the Columbia River mouth (one of the most significant managed colony sites in the PNW, supporting Common Tern alongside Caspian Tern, Forster's Tern, and other waterbirds), Dungeness Spit in Clallam County. The Columbia River mouth sites are ecologically significant for multiple reasons: the river provides rich foraging habitat, but the site is also subject to flooding, predation, and management interventions.
