## THE DAWN CHORUS -- THE ACOUSTIC STRUCTURE

### The Half-Hour Before Sunrise. The Maximum of the Maximum.

The dawn chorus is the most familiar acoustic event in ecological description -- the burst of bird song that begins in the hour before sunrise and reaches maximum intensity at first light. But "the dawn chorus" as a simple bird-acoustic event underrepresents what occurs in a Pacific Northwest coastal spring dawn.

At a coastal edge location -- the land-water interface where tidal flats meet estuary marsh, the marsh transitions to riparian forest, and the open water is within hearing range -- the spring dawn chorus includes acoustic contributions from multiple trophic levels and multiple taxonomic groups. The full event is not "birds singing at dawn." It is an acoustic convergence of every vocalizing species in the ecosystem simultaneously.

**The timing structure.** The dawn chorus has a sequence. The birds begin singing in a species-specific order, from the earliest (typically thrushes and some sparrows, beginning 60-90 minutes before sunrise) to the latest (typically wrens, chickadees, and some warblers, beginning closer to sunrise). The sequence is not random -- it follows the light curve and the specific visual acuity thresholds of different species. The dawn chorus reaches maximum density at approximately civil twilight (when the sun is 6 degrees below the horizon), when most species are singing simultaneously before the light becomes bright enough for foraging to begin competing with singing.

The convergence principle applies: the maximum of the dawn chorus is not the loudest single moment. It is the moment when the greatest number of species-voices are simultaneously active. That moment is not the precise instant of sunrise -- it is the transition from pre-dawn to dawn, when the sky begins to lighten but the territorial pressure of the new day's first visible light has not yet sent males into foraging competition.

(Sources: Dawn chorus timing 60-90 minutes before sunrise earliest species; species-specific sequence light curve visual acuity; maximum density at civil twilight 6 degrees below horizon; convergence principle maximum = most species simultaneous not loudest single moment; territorial pressure foraging competition after full light; transition pre-dawn to dawn maximum)

## THE BIRD LAYER -- FREQUENCY STRATIFICATION

### Each Species in Its Frequency Register. The Occupied Spectrum.

The bird component of the Pacific Northwest spring dawn chorus at a coastal edge location includes approximately 15-30 species actively singing. The frequency stratification:

**Low register (1-3 kHz): thrushes.** Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*) is the dawn chorus anchor in the Pacific Northwest. Its upward-spiraling song (1-3 kHz with harmonics) begins among the earliest of spring migrants and provides the tonal foundation of the early dawn chorus. The flute-like quality of Swainson's Thrush song is the identifying acoustic character of Pacific Northwest spring mornings. On coastal migration staging grounds in April-May, multiple Swainson's Thrushes may be singing simultaneously from the forest edge.

Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*), with its two-syrinx polyphonic song, adds an additional low-register voice in some coastal forest edge locations.

**Mid register (2-5 kHz): warblers and vireos.** Wilson's Warbler (*Cardellina pusilla*), Yellow Warbler (*Setophaga petechia*), and Orange-crowned Warbler (*Leiothlypis celata*) are spring coastal migrants whose combined songs occupy the 2-5 kHz mid-register band of the dawn chorus. Multiple individuals singing simultaneously creates a dense mid-register acoustic texture.

**High register (4-8+ kHz): Pacific Wren and sparrows.** Pacific Wren, as documented at degree 356, contributes its complex rapid-trill song in the 4-8 kHz range -- the highest-pitched territorial song of any forest understory species. Song Sparrow (*Melospiza melodia*) and Savannah Sparrow (*Passerculus sandwichensis*) add high-frequency contribution from the marsh edge.

**Low-register marsh: Red-winged Blackbird.** Red-winged Blackbird (*Agelaius phoeniceus*) is the dominant acoustic presence of Pacific Northwest coastal and freshwater marshes. The male's "konk-la-ree" call (approximately 1-4 kHz) is the defining acoustic signature of marsh habitat at dawn. In coastal marshes in spring, multiple males defending territories creates a sustained red-wing acoustic background.

(Sources: Swainson's Thrush 1-3 kHz upward-spiraling dawn chorus anchor spring migrant Pacific Northwest; Hermit Thrush two-syrinx polyphony; Wilson's Warbler Yellow Warbler Orange-crowned Warbler 2-5 kHz coastal spring migrants; Pacific Wren 4-8 kHz complex rapid trill highest understory; Song Sparrow Savannah Sparrow marsh edge high frequency; Red-winged Blackbird marsh male territorial dawn acoustic; frequency stratification multi-register dawn chorus)

## THE AMPHIBIAN LAYER -- THE FROG CHORUS

### Pacific Tree Frog. The Sub-Tympanic Layer.

The Pacific Tree Frog (*Pseudacris regilla*, now *Pseudacris sierra* in some taxonomies) is the Pacific Northwest's most acoustically prominent amphibian. The male's breeding call is the "ribbit" that is one of the most universally recognized natural sounds -- and the Pacific Tree Frog is the species that Hollywood recordings have used as the generic "frog" sound, meaning this specific PNW species' call has become the de facto global representation of frog sound.

The call frequency: 2-4 kHz, with peak energy at approximately 3.2 kHz. The call pattern is a two-note "krek-ek" or "ribbit" delivered at rates of approximately 60 calls per minute during peak activity. Males call from water's edge, marsh vegetation, or any moist location near standing water.

In the all-species dawn chorus at a coastal edge location, Pacific Tree Frog breeding choruses contribute sustained high-intensity acoustic output in the 2-4 kHz range throughout the pre-dawn and dawn period. The frog chorus does not have a strong dawn peak like the bird chorus -- it may be present from well before midnight through the morning -- but its maximum intensity is reached in the spring breeding season (March-May) and is sustained through the dawn transition.

The acoustic relationship with the bird layer: Pacific Tree Frog calls at approximately the same frequency range as the lower warblers and higher sparrows (2-4 kHz). In the full dawn chorus, the frog chorus provides a sustained, high-rate background against which the bird songs are superimposed. The frog chorus is continuous; the bird songs are structured. The combined effect is a continuous mid-frequency background punctuated by the more complex bird vocalizations.

(Sources: Pacific Tree Frog Pseudacris regilla 2-4 kHz peak 3.2 kHz; "ribbit" Hollywood generic frog sound PNW origin; 60 calls per minute breeding season; calls from water's edge marsh vegetation; dawn chorus pre-dawn through morning not strong dawn peak; March-May spring breeding maximum; sustained background vs bird song structure; continuous mid-frequency background punctuated by complex bird vocalizations)

## THE INSECT LAYER -- THE ULTRASONIC OVERLAY

### Crickets and Other Spring Stridulators at the High-Frequency Edge

In warm spring mornings (particularly late April and May in the Pacific Northwest), insect stridulation begins to contribute to the acoustic environment. The Pacific Northwest is not the insect-acoustic environment of, for example, the American Southeast (where cicada choruses dominate summer acoustics); but spring mornings along coastal edges can include cricket and some katydid species contributing in the 3-15 kHz range.

The spring acoustic transition: in the Pacific Northwest, insect contribution to dawn acoustics is spring-minimal and becomes more prominent as the season advances toward summer. At the specific maximum of the all-species dawn chorus (late April to early May), insect contribution adds high-frequency texture at the upper edge of the dawn chorus frequency range without dominating.

The sub-audible and near-audible contribution: several insect species (particularly some crickets) produce calls near the upper threshold of human hearing (12-20 kHz), contributing to the acoustic environment in a way that is heard as high-frequency presence without distinct call identification. The full acoustic environment of a Pacific Northwest coastal dawn in late spring extends into the ultrasonic range even in the absence of bat echolocation (which would add 20-100 kHz contribution if bats are foraging).

(Sources: Cricket katydid stridulation 3-15 kHz; Pacific Northwest insect acoustics spring-minimal vs Southeast; late April May spring acoustic transition; high-frequency texture upper edge; sub-audible near-audible 12-20 kHz cricket calls; ultrasonic extension of acoustic environment; bat echolocation 20-100 kHz if foraging)

## THE MARINE MAMMAL LAYER -- THE WATER VOICE

### Harbor Seal Calls from the Tidal Flat

At a coastal edge location within the Salish Sea, the all-species dawn chorus includes the possibility of marine mammal acoustic contribution. Harbor Seals (*Phoca vitulina*) haul out on tidal flats throughout the Salish Sea, including coastal locations close to Puget Sound communities. Their vocalizations -- low-frequency grunts and snorts both above and below water, breeding-season growls -- add a qualitatively distinct acoustic register to the dawn chorus: a large-animal, low-frequency voice from the water surface that is absent from terrestrial dawn chorus events.

The harbor seal's above-water vocalizations are most frequent during breeding season (late spring through summer) and during territorial interactions at haul-out sites. A spring dawn at a tidal flat with an active haul-out site provides the full marine mammal acoustic addition to the chorus.

The acoustic convergence with the bird and frog layers: harbor seal vocalizations in the 100-1,500 Hz range add low-frequency content to a dawn chorus that is primarily populated in the 1-8 kHz range. The combined acoustic profile of a full spring Pacific Northwest coastal dawn -- frogs (2-4 kHz), birds (1-8+ kHz), insects (3-15 kHz), and harbor seals (0.1-1.5 kHz) -- covers the full audible frequency range from approximately 100 Hz to 15+ kHz simultaneously.

**The full spectrum coverage as E=10.** As documented at degree 351 (Insect + Bird + Rain Maximum, Hoh Rainforest), the only SPS entry with full-spectrum coverage is exceptional. The all-species coastal dawn chorus achieves the same full-spectrum coverage through a different mechanism: the addition of the low-frequency marine mammal voice extends the frequency coverage downward from the bird/frog layer to the harbor seal's low vocalization range. The coastal edge is the location where this full-spectrum convergence is possible.
