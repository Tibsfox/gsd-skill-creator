SPRING — COASTAL EDGE — CIVIL TWILIGHT — ALL LAYERS SIMULTANEOUS

The Pacific Northwest all-species dawn chorus at the coastal edge. Birds (15–30 species, frequency-stratified), Pacific Tree Frog chorus (2–4 kHz, continuous background), insects (3–15 kHz, spring overlay), Harbor Seal above-water calls (0.1–1.5 kHz, extending the spectrum downward). Four trophic layers. Full audible spectrum 100 Hz to 15+ kHz. Spring breeding maximum for all taxa simultaneously. Civil twilight: the moment most species are active before foraging begins. E=10.

Pairs with Sleater-Kinney Dig Me Out (S36 358, E=10) — convergence of distinct voices in distinct registers. E=20.

The Four Trophic Layers

Bird Layer

1–8+ kHz — Structured

Swainson’s Thrush (anchor, 1–3 kHz, upward-spiral). Warblers (2–5 kHz, spring migrants, dense texture). Pacific Wren (4–8+ kHz, complex rapid trill). Red-winged Blackbird (1–4 kHz, marsh dominant). 15–30 species simultaneously at civil twilight.

Pacific Tree Frog

2–4 kHz — Continuous

Pseudacris regilla. “Ribbit” — the Hollywood de facto global frog sound comes from this PNW species. 60 calls/minute. Continuous chorus from pre-midnight through dawn. Sustained background against which birds are superimposed.

Harbor Seal

0.1–1.5 kHz — Low register

Phoca vitulina. Above-water breeding and contact calls at haul-out sites throughout Salish Sea. Extends full-spectrum coverage downward from the bird/frog layer. Enables coastal edge full-spectrum event.

Insect Layer

3–15 kHz — High edge

Spring cricket and katydid stridulation. Pacific Northwest insect acoustics spring-minimal, becoming prominent late April–May. Adds high-frequency texture at upper edge. Near-audible range (12–20 kHz) present.
