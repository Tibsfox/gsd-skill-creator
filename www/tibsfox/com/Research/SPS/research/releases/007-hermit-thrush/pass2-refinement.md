### The Harmonic Series Bird and the Radiation Belt: Natural Law Discovered by Instruments

Pass 1 documented the Doolittle and Brumm (2014) finding that Hermit Thrushes preferentially select pitches from the harmonic series — the mathematically defined sequence of frequencies produced by integer multiples of a fundamental. Pass 2 connects this to Explorer 1's Van Allen belt discovery as parallel cases of natural law encountered by instruments designed for other purposes.

Explorer 1's Geiger-Mueller counter was designed to measure cosmic ray flux at orbital altitudes. The instrument was calibrated for a certain range of particle intensity based on predictions from balloon-borne measurements at lower altitudes. What Van Allen's team did not predict was that the counter would encounter particle flux so intense at certain altitudes that it saturated — overwhelmed by radiation levels that exceeded its measurement range, causing it to read zero. The counter was designed to measure cosmic rays. It discovered the Van Allen radiation belts instead. The instrument encountered natural law — the magnetic trapping of charged particles in Earth's dipole field — while looking for something else.

The Hermit Thrush's syrinx (vocal organ) was shaped by natural selection for territorial defense and mate attraction. The bird was not designed to produce music. It was designed to produce an effective acoustic signal. But the signal it produces, when analyzed spectrally, follows the harmonic series — the same mathematical relationships that underlie all tonal music in all human cultures. The bird encountered natural law — the physics of vibrating systems and the psychoacoustics of overtone perception — through a biological instrument optimized for a different purpose. The bird was selected for song effectiveness. Harmonic series structure was a byproduct of that selection, or perhaps the mechanism through which effectiveness was achieved. Either way, the bird sings mathematics without having been taught mathematics, the same way Explorer 1's counter mapped magnetic topology without having been designed for magnetometry.

Lobaria pulmonaria's folded surface area is a structural analogy to the harmonic series itself. The harmonic series generates increasing complexity from a simple rule: multiply the fundamental by successive integers. The result is an infinite series of overtones whose frequency ratios define all tonal intervals. Lobaria's thallus generates its enormous surface area from a simple rule: fold the tissue into ridges and wrinkles. The result is a surface that absorbs atmospheric nitrogen across an area far exceeding the lichen's footprint. Both systems generate complexity through iteration of a simple rule — integer multiplication for the harmonic series, folding for the lichen surface. The Hermit Thrush's song and the lichen's thallus are both products of iterated simplicity producing emergent complexity.

### The Thrush Pair: Varied Thrush (Degree 2) vs. Hermit Thrush (Degree 7)

Pass 1 documented the inverse relationship between the two PNW thrushes: the Varied Thrush invests acoustic complexity in timbre (rich overtone spectrum per note, simple one-note phrases) while the Hermit Thrush invests acoustic complexity in melody (pure individual notes, complex multi-note phrases). Pass 2 develops this as the engine's first within-family comparison and connects it to the Explorer program's evolution.

The timbre-melody tradeoff is not merely descriptive. It represents a fundamental allocation decision in acoustic signaling: given a finite acoustic bandwidth, does the species invest that bandwidth in the complexity of each individual sound element (timbre) or in the complexity of the sequence of elements (melody)? The two thrushes have made opposite choices. The Varied Thrush packs maximum information into each note — the AM-modulated buzz contains multiple frequency components that the listener perceives as "rich," "eerie," or "atmospheric." The Hermit Thrush packs minimal information into each note — the pure flute tone is nearly sinusoidal — but chains notes into phrases whose interval structure follows harmonic-series mathematics. Each strategy produces a sound that humans describe as "haunting." The word is the same but the mechanism is opposite.

The Explorer program underwent a parallel evolution. Explorer 1 (mission 1.7, our current degree) was a simple instrument package — a single Geiger-Mueller counter plus temperature sensors on a small satellite. Maximum simplicity per instrument, but the trajectory swept through a complex region (the radiation belts) that the single instrument mapped by accumulating measurements over many orbital passes. Explorer 6 (mission 1.6, degree 6) was a complex multi-instrument package — magnetometer, particle detectors, photocell scanner, micrometeorite sensors — on a sophisticated spacecraft. Maximum complexity per observation pass, but each pass was shorter and the trajectory coverage was less systematic.

The within-family comparison is the engine's first. Two species from the same family (Turdidae) at different degrees (2 and 7), producing sounds that are both described as "haunting" but are acoustically opposite. The engine can now document taxonomic families that contain multiple acoustic strategies, and can use the within-family contrast to illuminate the allocation decisions that shape each species' signal.

### Mountain Forest and the Elevation Dimension: Vertical Range Extension

Pass 1 noted that the Hermit Thrush breeds primarily in mountain forest at elevations above the lowland conifer stands where degrees 0-5 species dominate. Pass 2 develops the elevation dimension as a new axis in the engine's ecological coverage.

The first seven species (degrees 0-6) are lowland species — resident or migrant in Puget Sound lowland habitats from sea level to approximately 500 meters. The Hermit Thrush breeds primarily at elevations of 500 to 1,800 meters — mountain forest, subalpine zones, and the upper limits of the conifer belt on the Cascades and Olympics. It is present in the lowlands during migration and winter (when it descends to lower elevations), but its breeding habitat and primary song production occur at altitude.

### The Song as Harmonic Architecture: Where Biology Meets Mathematics

The Doolittle and Brumm finding (2014) is not merely a curiosity. It is the discovery that a non-human organism independently arrived at the mathematical relationships that underlie all human tonal music. This demands a structural response from the engine.

The harmonic series — f, 2f, 3f, 4f, 5f... — generates intervals: octave (2:1), perfect fifth (3:2), perfect fourth (4:3), major third (5:4), minor third (6:5). These intervals are the building blocks of scales, chords, and harmonic systems across every known musical culture. Pythagorean tuning uses the 3:2 ratio. Just intonation uses the 5:4 and 6:5 ratios. Equal temperament approximates all of them through the twelfth root of two. Every tuning system is a negotiation with the harmonic series — an attempt to organize the natural overtone relationships into a practical system of discrete pitches.

The Hermit Thrush does not negotiate. It selects. From its available vocal range, it chooses notes whose frequencies cluster on harmonic-series ratios of the phrase-initial fundamental. The result is a song that sounds "musical" to human listeners not because the bird has been culturally influenced by human music, but because both the bird and the human are responding to the same physical law: the harmonic series emerges from the physics of vibrating systems, and organisms that process sound through neural tissue will find certain frequency ratios more perceptually salient than others.

The Priester parallel (degree 7 S36) is the trombone's continuous access to the pitch continuum — including the exact harmonic-series intervals that the tempered piano cannot produce. Priester's slide can place his pitch at the exact 5:4 major third or the exact 7:4 natural seventh — intervals that the piano's equal temperament approximates but never hits precisely. The trombonist and the thrush both have direct access to the harmonic series through continuous-pitch mechanisms (slide and syrinx, respectively). Both bypass the compromises of discrete-pitch systems. Both produce sounds that listeners describe as "pure" — because they are, in the mathematical sense, closer to the natural overtone relationships than any tempered instrument can be.
