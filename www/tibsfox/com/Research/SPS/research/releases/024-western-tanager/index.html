Western Tanager

Piranga ludoviciana · Family Cardinalidae (Cardinals & Grosbeaks)
Hoarse Robin-like Warble · Conifer Forest · Summer · Energy 4
High in the Douglas fir canopy, a flash of yellow and flame-red moves along a branch — the most spectacular songbird in the PNW, the Western Tanager, a tropical explosion in the evergreen cathedral. It opens a slender bill and pours out a rolling warble — robin-like in architecture but hoarse, rough, grainy, as if the robin’s song had been run through sandpaper. This is the second robin-like warble in two degrees, from the second Cardinalidae in two degrees. The robin itself arrives at degree 25. Three species converging on one song. The convergence trio. And in the tanager’s flame-red head, a pigment the bird cannot make — rhodoxanthin, stolen from insects, deposited as an honest signal of foraging quality. The color is borrowed. The song is rough. Both are real.

The Convergence Trio: Three Robin-like Warbles in Three Degrees

Degrees 23, 24, 25: Three consecutive species produce robin-like warbles. Black-headed Grosbeak (23, Cardinalidae) — smooth. Western Tanager (24, Cardinalidae) — hoarse. American Robin (25, Turdidae) — clean. Same frequency range (2–3.5 kHz center), same phrase structure (slurred whistles), different timbral textures. Convergent architecture with divergent implementation — the same house plan built with different materials.

The Stolen Pigment: Rhodoxanthin

The male Western Tanager’s flame-red head is colored by rhodoxanthin — a rare ketocarotenoid the bird cannot synthesize. Most red birds (cardinals, House Finches) convert dietary yellow carotenoids to red pigments using their own enzymes. The tanager cannot. It must obtain rhodoxanthin directly from insect prey. The red head is an honest signal — it cannot be faked. Brighter red = better forager = better territory = better mate. The color is dietary proof of quality.

The Hoarse Warble: Timbral Distortion as Identity

“A robin with a sore throat.” The tanager’s song is robin-like in architecture but hoarse, rough, and burry in texture. The phrases contain additional spectral energy — harmonic sidebands, frequency jitter, amplitude irregularities — producing a grainy quality absent from both the grosbeak (smooth) and the robin (clean). This hoarseness may be adaptive: in conifer canopy, a broad-spectrum signal survives needle attenuation better than a pure tone. The roughness is the robustness.

Song Bioacoustics

The Western Tanager delivers a rolling warble of 5–12 hoarse, slurred phrases per bout, each phrase a rough pitch glide alternating in direction. The song lasts 2–4 seconds at approximately 2–4 phrases per second.

The Tanager That Isn’t a Tanager

The Western Tanager was historically placed in Thraupidae (true tanagers). Molecular phylogenetics (Klicka et al. 2007; Barker et al. 2013) moved Piranga into Cardinalidae. The common name “tanager” is retained by convention but is phylogenetically misleading. The Western Tanager is a cardinal, not a tanager — genetically closer to the Black-headed Grosbeak than to any true tanager. Two consecutive Cardinalidae at degrees 23–24: grosbeak and “tanager,” siblings that look nothing alike.

Conifer Forest: Return to the Cathedral

The Western Tanager is a conifer forest specialist — the first since degree 9 (Chestnut-backed Chickadee). After mixed forest (23), forest snag (22), and open woodland (21), the engine returns to closed conifer canopy: Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar. The tanager forages at 15–30 m height, sallying from branch tips to capture flying insects. Its hoarse warble filters down through needle layers to reach ground-level listeners — the song is heard more often than the bird is seen.

Long-Distance Migrant

The Western Tanager is one of the PNW’s longest-distance neotropical migrants, wintering from Mexico through Central America to Costa Rica — up to 4,500 km one-way. It arrives in the PNW in mid-May (later than the grosbeak) and departs by early September. Peak song occurs late May through mid-July.

Plumage: Tropical Explosion

Male: Flame-orange to scarlet head (rhodoxanthin pigment), brilliant lemon-yellow body, black back and wings with bold yellow and white wing bars, slender slightly hooked bill. One of the most visually spectacular PNW birds.

Female: Olive-yellow overall, duller above, brighter below. No red. Two wing bars. Same slender bill shape.

The engine moves from its plainest species (Orange-crowned Warbler, degree 20) to one of its most spectacular in four degrees.
