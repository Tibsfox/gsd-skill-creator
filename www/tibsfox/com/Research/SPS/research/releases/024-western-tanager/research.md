## Taxonomy: The Tanager That Isn't a Tanager

### The Reclassification

The Western Tanager was historically placed in the family **Thraupidae** (true tanagers) -- a massive Neotropical family of approximately 380 species. For over a century, North American field guides listed the four *Piranga* tanagers (Western, Scarlet, Summer, Hepatic) as tanagers, and their common names still carry the word. But molecular phylogenetic studies beginning in the 1990s and culminating in the AOU's formal reclassification (2008) moved *Piranga* out of Thraupidae and into **Cardinalidae**.

The evidence is unambiguous:

1. **DNA-DNA hybridization** (Sibley and Ahlquist 1990) showed *Piranga* clustering with cardinals and grosbeaks, not with true tanagers.
2. **Mitochondrial DNA** (Klicka et al. 2007, *Molecular Phylogenetics and Evolution*) confirmed *Piranga* as nested within Cardinalidae.
3. **Multi-locus phylogenomics** (Barker et al. 2013, 2015) placed *Piranga* as sister to *Habia* (ant-tanagers) within the Cardinalidae radiation.
4. **AOS/AOU classification** (2008-present): *Piranga ludoviciana* is formally placed in Cardinalidae. The common name "tanager" is retained by convention but is phylogenetically misleading.

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Cardinalidae (Cardinals, Grosbeaks, and Allies)
- **Genus:** *Piranga*
- **Species:** *Piranga ludoviciana* (Wilson, 1811)
- **Common Name:** Western Tanager
- **Etymology:** *Piranga* is of uncertain origin, possibly from a Tupi word for a small bird. *Ludoviciana* refers to Louisiana, as Alexander Wilson described the species from a specimen collected during the Lewis and Clark Expedition. Despite the name, the species does not breed in Louisiana -- it is a bird of the western mountains and forests. The specific epithet commemorates the expedition's route, not the bird's range.

### Subspecies and Range

Two subspecies are generally recognized:

1. **P. l. ludoviciana** -- the widespread form, breeding from British Columbia south through the western U.S. mountains to northern Baja California and into the Trans-Pecos region. This is the PNW subspecies. Males show brilliant yellow body, red head wash, black back and wings with bold yellow wing bars.
2. **P. l. cooperi** -- sometimes recognized from the southern portion of the range (Arizona, New Mexico), with slightly different plumage tones. Validity debated; some authorities treat the species as monotypic.

In the PNW, the Western Tanager breeds throughout the Cascades and Olympics at low to moderate elevations (sea level to ~2,100 m), in conifer and mixed conifer-deciduous forest. It is a **neotropical migrant** -- breeding in the PNW from mid-May through August, wintering in Mexico and Central America (south to Costa Rica). Peak song activity occurs late May through July.

## The Song: A Hoarse Robin-like Warble

### Acoustic Description

The Western Tanager's song is a series of **hoarse, burry, slurred phrases** delivered in a rolling warble pattern. Every field guide says the same thing: "sounds like a robin with a sore throat." The comparison is both accurate and structurally revealing. The tanager's song shares the robin's basic architecture -- rapidly alternating rising and falling slurred phrases in the 2-4 kHz range -- but adds a distinctive **hoarse, rough quality** that the robin lacks.

Key acoustic parameters:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~1.5-4.5 kHz. Nearly identical to the grosbeak (1.5-4.5 kHz) and the upcoming robin (~2.0-4.0 kHz). All three species share the same frequency real estate.
- **Song duration:** 2-4 seconds per song bout. Slightly shorter than the grosbeak's 2-5 seconds.
- **Phrase structure:** The song consists of **5-12 slurred phrases** per bout, each phrase a rising or falling pitch glide. Phrases alternate in direction -- up, down, up, down -- creating the rolling, warbling contour. Fewer phrases per bout than the grosbeak (10-20), which gives each phrase more perceptual weight.
- **Phrase rate:** Approximately 2-4 phrases per second. Slightly slower than the grosbeak's 3-5/sec. Each phrase is more distinctly audible as a separate event.
- **Hoarseness:** The defining acoustic feature. The tanager's phrases contain **additional spectral energy** in the form of harmonic sidebands, frequency jitter, and amplitude irregularities that produce a rough, buzzy, or "burry" timbral quality. This hoarseness distinguishes the tanager from both the grosbeak (smooth and rich) and the robin (bright and clean). Spectrographically, the tanager's phrases show wider frequency bands and less regular harmonic structure than either comparison species.
- **Timbre:** Husky, rough-edged, warm but grainy. Not thin or reedy (like warblers), not pure and bell-like (like thrushes), not smooth and full (like the grosbeak). The tanager occupies a unique timbral position -- a robin-like song run through a distortion filter.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate to loud. The tanager sings from high in the conifer canopy and the song carries well through open canopy structure. Energy 4.
- **Call note:** The tanager also gives a distinctive **"pit-ik"** or **"pit-er-ick"** call -- a sharp, two- or three-syllable note that is often heard before the song and is diagnostic. Faster and more clipped than the grosbeak's sharp "pik" call.

It is not one sound -- it is a *family* of sounds occupying the same frequency-temporal space but differing in timbre. The grosbeak version is smooth. The tanager version is rough. The robin version is clean. Three textures of the same architecture. The frequency range, phrase structure, and rhythmic cadence are convergent; the timbral character is divergent. This is **convergent architecture with divergent implementation** -- the same house plan built with different materials.

### Why the Hoarseness?

The tanager's hoarse quality has several potential sources:

1. **Syringeal morphology.** The tanager's syrinx (vocal organ) may produce more irregular oscillations than the grosbeak's or robin's, generating additional harmonic content and frequency jitter. The specific syringeal anatomy of *Piranga* has not been as thoroughly studied as some other passerines, but the hoarseness is consistent across individuals and populations, indicating a species-specific rather than individual trait.

2. **Bill morphology and vocal tract.** The tanager's relatively slender bill (~9-10 mm deep vs. the grosbeak's 15-17 mm) creates a different vocal-tract resonance profile. A narrower bill may emphasize certain harmonic sidebands that contribute to the rough quality. The bill-song connection documented for the grosbeak (Podos 2001) operates here in reverse: a smaller bill produces a different filtering profile, not a smoother one, but one with different resonance characteristics.

3. **Habitat acoustic adaptation.** The tanager sings from high in conifer canopy -- an environment with different reverberant properties than the grosbeak's mixed forest. Conifer canopy produces needle-scatter attenuation that preferentially absorbs higher frequencies. A hoarse, broad-spectrum signal may propagate more reliably through needle-dense canopy than a pure-toned signal would, because the spectral energy is distributed across a wider band. If some frequencies are attenuated, others persist. The hoarseness may be an adaptation for acoustic robustness in conifer canopy.

4. **Species recognition.** In areas where the Western Tanager, Black-headed Grosbeak, and American Robin coexist (which they commonly do in PNW mixed and transitional forest), the hoarseness may serve as a species-specific marker. All three produce robin-like warbles, but the hoarse quality immediately identifies the tanager for conspecifics -- even when the overall song structure overlaps.

Remove it and you have a robin. Keep it and you have a species.

## The Stolen Pigment: Rhodoxanthin and the Red Head

### The Biochemistry

The male Western Tanager's red head is one of the most visually striking features of any PNW bird -- a wash of flame-orange to scarlet across the face, crown, and throat, set against a brilliant lemon-yellow body and jet-black wings and back. The color is spectacular. The biochemistry behind it is unique among songbirds.

Most red and yellow plumage coloration in birds comes from **carotenoid pigments** -- molecules synthesized by plants and algae, obtained through diet, and deposited in growing feathers. Yellow plumage typically involves dietary carotenoids (lutein, zeaxanthin) that are either deposited directly or minimally modified. Red plumage in most birds involves dietary yellow carotenoids that are **enzymatically converted** to red ketocarotenoids (such as 3-hydroxy-echinenone or astaxanthin) by the bird's own metabolic machinery. Cardinals, House Finches, and Scarlet Tanagers all produce their red plumage this way -- they eat yellow carotenoids and convert them to red.

The Western Tanager does something different. The red pigment in its head feathers is **rhodoxanthin** -- a rare ketocarotenoid that the bird **cannot synthesize**. Rhodoxanthin is not produced by the tanager's metabolism. It is obtained directly from the diet -- specifically from insects that themselves contain rhodoxanthin or from plant sources that produce it.

Key findings:

- **Brush and Johnson (1976), *Auk*:** First identified rhodoxanthin as the primary red pigment in Western Tanager head feathers. This was unexpected because rhodoxanthin is extremely rare in avian plumage.
- **Hudon (1991), *Canadian Journal of Zoology*:** Confirmed that Western Tanagers cannot metabolically convert common dietary carotenoids (lutein, zeaxanthin) to rhodoxanthin. The pigment must be ingested in its final form.
- **McGraw et al. (2003), *Naturwissenschaften*:** Confirmed rhodoxanthin deposition and noted its rarity among North American passerines. The Western Tanager is essentially unique in its reliance on this pathway.

### The Source: Insect-Derived Rhodoxanthin

Where does the rhodoxanthin come from? The primary hypothesis:

1. **Insect prey.** Certain insects -- particularly some hemipterans (plant-sucking bugs) and their larvae -- accumulate rhodoxanthin from plant sources. Western Tanagers are primarily insectivorous during the breeding season. Insects captured during the pre-molt period (late summer) may provide the rhodoxanthin deposited in head feathers during the fall/winter molt.

2. **Conifer-associated sources.** Rhodoxanthin has been detected in the arils (fleshy seed coverings) of some conifer species (notably yew, *Taxus*) and in certain lichens. The tanager's association with conifer forest may provide access to rhodoxanthin through conifer-associated food webs.

3. **Seasonal variation.** The intensity of the red head wash varies among individuals and across the season. Males in fresh breeding plumage (spring) show the brightest red. By late summer, the red fades. This pattern is consistent with a dietary pigment that is deposited once during molt and then degrades with UV exposure and feather wear. Birds with better access to rhodoxanthin-rich prey produce brighter red heads -- making the red head an honest signal of foraging quality.

### Honest Signaling: The Red Head as Fitness Indicator

Because the tanager cannot manufacture rhodoxanthin, the intensity of the red head is constrained by dietary access to a rare pigment. This makes the red head an **honest signal** -- it cannot be faked. A male with a bright red head has demonstrably found and consumed rhodoxanthin-rich prey, which requires:

1. **Effective foraging** in insect-rich habitat during the pre-molt period.
2. **Territory quality** that provides access to the right insect communities.
3. **Body condition** sufficient to allocate a rare dietary resource to plumage rather than other metabolic needs.

Females choosing mates with brighter red heads are selecting for males with better foraging ability, better territories, and better body condition. The honest signal hypothesis for carotenoid-based plumage (Hill 2002, *A Red Bird in a Brown Bag*) applies with particular force to the Western Tanager because the signaling molecule is unusually rare and cannot be enzymatically produced. The red head is a direct readout of dietary quality.

## Conifer Forest: Return to the Deep Canopy

### Ecosystem

The Western Tanager is a **conifer forest specialist** -- the first species since degree 9 (Chestnut-backed Chickadee, coastal conifer) to be primarily associated with conifer-dominated habitat. The engine's ecosystem progression has oscillated:

| Degree | Ecosystem | Forest Type |
|---|---|---|
| 20 | Shrubby slope | Open |
| 21 | Open woodland | Scattered trees |
| 22 | Forest snag | Dead wood |
| 23 | Mixed forest | Deciduous-conifer blend |
| **24** | **Conifer forest** | **Closed conifer canopy** |

After four degrees of open, mixed, or degraded habitats, the engine returns to closed conifer canopy. The Western Tanager breeds in Douglas fir, western hemlock, and western red cedar forest -- the cathedral forest that defines the PNW. It forages in the upper canopy, often at heights of 15-30 m, sallying from branch tips to capture flying insects and gleaning from needle clusters. Its high canopy position means that the hoarse warble filters down through layers of conifer needles to reach ground-level listeners -- the song is heard more often than the bird is seen.

### Acoustic Properties of Conifer Forest

Conifer canopy creates a distinctive acoustic environment that differs from the mixed forest of degree 23:

- **Needle attenuation.** Dense conifer needles scatter and absorb high-frequency sound more effectively than broadleaf canopy. Frequencies above 4 kHz are preferentially attenuated. This favors mid-frequency signals (2-4 kHz) -- exactly where the tanager sings.
- **Trunk reverberation.** Large conifer trunks create reflection surfaces that produce moderate reverberation, softening sharp transients and favoring sustained tonal signals over rapid percussive ones.
- **Low ambient noise.** Closed conifer canopy is quieter than mixed forest near suburban areas. The signal-to-noise ratio is higher, allowing moderately loud songs to carry farther.
- **Vertical stratification.** Sound transmission is better along the vertical axis (up through canopy gaps) than the horizontal (through dense needle layers). A bird singing from the upper canopy broadcasts upward and outward more effectively than downward. Ground-level listeners hear an attenuated version of the original signal -- the hoarseness may actually improve detectability at ground level because the broad spectral distribution survives needle attenuation better than a pure tone would.

## Long-Distance Migrant: Farther Than the Grosbeak

### Migration Pattern

The Western Tanager is a **long-distance neotropical migrant** with one of the longest migration routes of any PNW songbird:

- **Wintering range:** Mexico through Central America to Costa Rica and occasionally Panama. The tanager winters farther south than the grosbeak (which winters primarily in western Mexico). Some Western Tanagers travel over 4,000 km one-way from their PNW breeding grounds to Costa Rican wintering sites.
- **Arrival:** Mid-May in the PNW. Later than the grosbeak (late April). Males arrive first and begin singing from conifer canopy.
- **Breeding season:** Late May through July. Nest built on conifer branch, typically 5-20 m above ground. Incubation: 13 days. Nestling period: 11-12 days. One brood per season.
- **Departure:** August to early September. Post-breeding dispersal begins in late July.
- **Peak song period:** Late May through mid-July -- approximately 6-8 weeks.

The tanager's longer migration route has implications for the convergence trio analysis. All three robin-like warble species are neotropical migrants in the PNW, but they winter at different latitudes:

| Species | Winter Range | Migration Distance |
|---|---|---|
| Black-headed Grosbeak | Western Mexico | ~3,000 km |
| Western Tanager | Mexico to Costa Rica | ~3,000-4,500 km |
| American Robin | Resident to short-distance (PNW) | 0-1,500 km |

The robin is the least migratory -- many are year-round residents. The tanager travels farthest. Yet all three converge on the same song in the PNW breeding season. The robin-like warble is a summer sound regardless of how far the singer traveled to produce it.

## Sexual Dimorphism: The Spectacular Male, The Understated Female

### The Male

The adult male Western Tanager in breeding plumage is among the most visually spectacular birds in the PNW:

- **Head:** Flame-orange to scarlet wash across face, crown, and upper throat. The rhodoxanthin-derived color. Intensity varies among individuals -- brighter red indicates higher dietary quality.
- **Body:** Brilliant lemon-yellow. The yellow comes from standard dietary carotenoids (lutein/zeaxanthin), not rhodoxanthin.
- **Back:** Black, sometimes with olive-yellow edging.
- **Wings:** Black with two bold wing bars -- one yellow, one white.
- **Tail:** Black.
- **Bill:** Slender, slightly hooked at tip, grayish with darker culmen. Much thinner than the grosbeak's massive conical bill.
- **Overall impression:** A tropical explosion in the conifer canopy. Yellow body, red head, black wings. Unmistakable.

### The Female

The adult female is olive-yellow, cryptically patterned:

- **Head:** Olive-gray to olive-yellow, no red. Sometimes a faint orange-yellow wash on face.
- **Body:** Olive-yellow, brighter below, duller above.
- **Wings:** Dark with two wing bars (less bold than male's).
- **Bill:** Same shape as male's but slightly smaller.
- **Overall impression:** An olive-yellow bird with wing bars. Could be confused with a female oriole or large warbler at a quick glance. The bill shape and wing bars are the best field marks.
