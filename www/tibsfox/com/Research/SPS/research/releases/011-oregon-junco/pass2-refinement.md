### The Trill as Failed Separation: Discrete Pulses That Don't Quite Come Apart

Pass 1 documented the Oregon Junco's metallic trill as discrete pulses perceived as continuous tone — individual notes repeated so rapidly (15-25/second) that the human auditory system can no longer resolve them. Pass 2 connects this to Explorer 5's failure mode (upper stage contacted booster after separation — components that should have separated cleanly instead touched) and Amanita muscaria's paradox of beauty and toxicity coexisting in one organism.

Explorer 5 failed because two components that were supposed to separate didn't separate cleanly. The upper stage cluster and the booster were designed to fly apart after staging, each continuing on its own trajectory. Instead, the upper stage contacted the booster — a touch, a collision, a failure of the separation sequence. The components that should have been discrete (separate vehicles on separate trajectories) remained coupled (touching, interfering). The mission died at the boundary between discrete and continuous.

The junco's trill lives at exactly this boundary. Each note is a discrete pulse — a separate acoustic event with a defined onset, duration, and offset. Between each pulse is a gap — silence, separation. At slow trill rates (below ~12 notes/second), the ear resolves the individual pulses. The notes are discrete. The separations are clean. But as the trill rate increases past the temporal fusion threshold, the ear can no longer resolve the gaps. The discrete pulses merge into a continuous-sounding tone. The separations fail. The notes touch.

This is the stroboscopic effect applied to acoustics: below the flicker-fusion threshold, you see individual flashes (or hear individual notes). Above it, the flashes merge into continuous illumination (or the notes merge into continuous tone). The junco sings at the threshold — 15-25 notes/second, right at and above the human temporal resolution limit. The trill is the acoustic equivalent of Explorer 5's failed separation: discrete elements that should be individually resolvable but are perceived as continuous because the rate exceeds the system's capacity to distinguish them.

Amanita muscaria embodies the same threshold — beauty and toxicity fused in one organism. The brilliant red cap and the ibotenic acid are not separable. You cannot have the visual beauty without the chemical danger. They coexist in a single fruiting body the way the junco's discrete pulses coexist with their continuous perception — the same object producing two contradictory impressions depending on how you interact with it (look at Amanita and see beauty; eat it and encounter toxicity; listen to the junco trill and hear a continuous tone; analyze it spectrographically and see discrete pulses).

### The Lumping Problem as Taxonomic Trill: Where Species Don't Quite Separate

Pass 1 documented the Oregon Junco's taxonomic instability — five formerly separate species lumped into one (Dark-eyed Junco) in 1973, with the lump under active review and likely future resplitting. Pass 2 develops this as a taxonomic trill: the boundary between "one species" and "five species" oscillates with the classification framework, and the organism at the center doesn't change.

The five junco forms — Oregon, Slate-colored, Pink-sided, Gray-headed, White-winged — are discrete phenotypic entities. They look different. They sound different (trill rate and frequency vary by form). They occupy different geographic ranges. Genetic studies show significant divergence, particularly between Oregon and Slate-colored. But they hybridize where their ranges overlap. The 1973 AOU decision treated the hybridization as evidence that the forms were not reproductively isolated — therefore one species. The Biological Species Concept demands clean separation. The junco forms don't separate cleanly. They touch at the margins. Like Explorer 5's upper stage and booster.

The taxonomic trill oscillates: separate species (pre-1973) → one species (1973 lump) → probable future split (genetic and phylogenetic evidence accumulating). The boundary between "discrete species" and "continuous species complex" shifts with the classification framework the way the boundary between "discrete notes" and "continuous tone" shifts with the listener's temporal resolution. The bird doesn't change. The label does.

This is the third instance of the within-genus comparison in the engine: the two chickadees (degrees 3, 9) showed syntax conservation across speciation. The two thrushes (degrees 2, 7) showed timbre-melody allocation tradeoffs. The Oregon Junco at degree 11 shows the opposite problem: forms that probably should be separate species but were lumped because they hybridize at the margins. The chickadees are clearly two species that share syntax. The juncos are ambiguously one-or-five species that share a song form. The boundary is uncertain — which is the entire point.

### Forest Edge as Habitat and the Edge-Dwelling Signal

The Oregon Junco is a forest-edge specialist — it occupies the transition zone between closed forest and open ground (meadows, clearings, yards, parks). This is the first explicitly edge-associated habitat in the engine. Previous species occupied interiors: conifer forest (wren, thrush, owl, nuthatch, kinglet), estuary (heron), riparian corridor (flycatcher), shrub (bushtit). The junco lives at the edge — not inside the forest and not in the open, but at the boundary between them.

The edge habitat maps to Brazil's salon position (degree 11 S36). Brazil operated at the edge between formal pedagogy and informal tradition — not inside the university (Standifer) and not in the public performance venue (Berry), but at the threshold between institutional and community music-making. The salon is the forest edge of jazz pedagogy: the transitional zone where formal knowledge meets informal practice, where the university's structured curriculum meets the community's oral tradition.

Explorer 5's failure occurred at an edge — the separation point between the first stage and the upper stage cluster, the boundary between one phase of flight and the next. The failure was not in either stage individually (both were functional) but in the transition between them. Edge failures — failures at the boundary between two functional systems — are distinct from component failures. The system works. The transition doesn't.

The junco's trill is also an edge phenomenon — the acoustic boundary between discrete and continuous perception. The song sits at the edge of the temporal resolution limit. The taxonomy sits at the edge of the species concept. The habitat is literally the forest's edge. Degree 11 is the edge degree — every entity occupies a boundary.
