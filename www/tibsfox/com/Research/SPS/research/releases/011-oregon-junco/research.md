## Taxonomy: The Lumping Problem

The Oregon Junco is not a species. Or rather, it was a species, then it wasn't, and it may become one again. This is the most instructive taxonomic case in the PNW avifauna -- a lesson in how the act of classification itself changes what we think we're looking at.

**The current status.** The Oregon Junco is classified as a subspecies group within the **Dark-eyed Junco** (*Junco hyemalis*), family **Emberizidae** (New World sparrows), order Passeriformes. The Dark-eyed Junco complex contains at least five formerly separate species, merged into one by the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU) in 1973:

1. **Oregon Junco** (*J. h. oreganus* group) -- the PNW form. Dark hood, rusty-brown back, pink-brown flanks. The most common junco on the Pacific coast.
2. **Slate-colored Junco** (*J. h. hyemalis* group) -- the eastern form. Uniform dark slate above, white below.
3. **Pink-sided Junco** (*J. h. mearnsi*) -- the Rocky Mountain form. Broad pink flanks, gray hood.
4. **Gray-headed Junco** (*J. h. caniceps*) -- the southern Rocky Mountain form. Gray head, rusty back.
5. **White-winged Junco** (*J. h. aikeni*) -- the Black Hills form. Larger, paler, with white wing bars.

The 1973 lump was based on the observation that these forms hybridize where their ranges overlap. Hybridization was interpreted as evidence that they were not reproductively isolated, and therefore not separate species under the Biological Species Concept. Five species became one.

**The splitting case.** But hybridization alone does not resolve species boundaries -- many unambiguously distinct species hybridize (Mallard and American Black Duck, Townsend's and Hermit Warblers, Blue-winged and Golden-winged Warblers). The key question is whether hybridization is extensive enough to homogenize the populations, or limited enough to maintain distinct forms. In the junco complex, the answer is equivocal.

Molecular phylogenetic studies (Milá et al. 2007, *Evolution*; Friis et al. 2016, *Molecular Ecology*) have shown that the five junco forms have significant genetic divergence -- particularly the Oregon group, which is genetically distinct from the Slate-colored group across most of its range. Phenotypically, the Oregon Junco is instantly distinguishable from the Slate-colored Junco in the field. They look like different birds. They largely behave like different birds. The hybrid zone in the Great Plains is narrow and stable, not expanding. By the criteria of the Phylogenetic Species Concept, or any species concept that emphasizes diagnosability over hybridization, the Oregon Junco is a species.

The AOU's 1973 lump was a product of its time -- a period when the Biological Species Concept dominated and lumping was in vogue. The pendulum has swung toward splitting in subsequent decades. Junco taxonomy is under active review, and a future AOU revision may restore the Oregon Junco to full species status. The bird at the feeder does not care. It was an Oregon Junco before 1973, and it will be one after the next revision, regardless of what the committee decides.

**What this teaches.** Taxonomy is not fixed truth. It is a human classification scheme applied to biological continua. The junco complex is a ring species in slow motion -- five forms radiating from a common ancestor, diverging in plumage, morphology, and genetics, but still connected by narrow hybrid zones at the margins. The act of drawing a boundary (species or subspecies) is a human decision about where to place the line on a gradient. The Oregon Junco's taxonomic history is the history of that decision being made, unmade, and likely remade. The bird doesn't change. The label does.

**Physical description.** The Oregon Junco is a medium-sized sparrow: **5.5--6.3 inches** (14--16 cm) in length, **18--30 grams** in weight, wingspan approximately **7.5--9.8 inches** (19--25 cm). Males have a dark slate to blackish hood sharply contrasting with a warm rusty-brown back and pink-brown to rufous flanks. Females are similar but with a paler, grayer hood. Both sexes show the diagnostic junco field marks: **white outer tail feathers** that flash conspicuously in flight, and a **pale pinkish bill**. The white tail flash is a signal -- we'll return to it.

## Bioacoustics -- THE TRILL: Discrete Pulses as Continuous Tone

### The Song

The Oregon Junco's song is a **metallic trill on a single pitch** -- a rapid repetition of one note at constant frequency. This is the entire song. One note, repeated rapidly, for 1--2 seconds. Frequency typically 3--6 kHz. Trill rate approximately 10--25 notes per second. The notes are distinct -- each one a separate pulse with a brief gap between -- but at trill rates above approximately 15 per second, the human auditory system can no longer resolve the individual notes. The trill is perceived as a continuous, buzzy, metallic tone. Discrete becomes continuous.

This is the defining acoustic property of the junco trill: **individual pulses perceived as a sustained tone**. The note is simple. The repetition is rapid. The perceptual result is a sound that seems continuous but is structurally discrete. The ear hears a buzz. The spectrogram shows individual elements. Reality and perception diverge at the boundary where repetition rate exceeds temporal resolution.

### The Chip Note

In addition to the song, the Oregon Junco produces a sharp **"tick"** or **"tik"** chip note -- a short, dry, metallic contact call used year-round for flock communication, alarm, and general social contact. The chip note is:
- **Short:** 0.02--0.05 seconds
- **Sharp:** Abrupt onset and offset, no frequency sweep
- **Metallic:** Hard, dry quality -- not whistled, not buzzy, but percussive
- **Frequency:** Broadband, centered around 4--7 kHz

The chip note functions like the bushtit's contact seep (degree 10) but is louder, sharper, and more individually distinctive. Where the bushtit's call is at the noise floor, the junco's chip note is a clean, audible signal -- a telegraph click.

## The Trill as Tremolo: Where Discrete Meets Continuous

### The Stroboscopic Principle

The junco's trill is the acoustic equivalent of the **stroboscopic effect**. A strobe light flashes discrete pulses of light. Below a certain frequency (the flicker fusion threshold), the eye sees individual flashes. Above that frequency, the flashes merge into what appears to be continuous illumination. The light hasn't changed -- it is still flashing. The perception has changed -- the brain can no longer resolve the individual pulses.

The junco's trill operates identically. Below approximately 12--15 notes per second, the ear resolves individual notes -- you hear a rattle, a series of clicks. Above that rate, the notes fuse into a continuous-sounding buzz or tone. The junco typically sings at 15--25 notes per second -- above the temporal fusion threshold. The trill sounds continuous. It is not.

This is the **analog-to-digital boundary** in acoustics. The junco's song IS the boundary. The individual notes are digital -- discrete, separated, countable. The perceived tone is analog -- continuous, sustained, smooth. The trill is the conversion point. This is:

- **Tremolo** in music -- the rapid repetition of a single note creating the illusion of sustain. A violinist's bow vibrating rapidly back and forth across the same note. A piano's sustain pedal combined with rapid key repetition. A mandolin's characteristic tremolo picking. All are the same principle: discrete strikes perceived as continuous tone.
- **AM (amplitude modulation) radio** -- a carrier wave switched on and off rapidly. Below the modulation frequency, you hear clicks. Above it, you hear a tone. The carrier is always discrete. The perception is always that it's continuous. The boundary is the modulation rate.
- **Persistence of vision** in film and LED displays -- 24 frames per second (film), 60 Hz refresh (monitor), PWM dimming (LED). All rely on the same fusion threshold: present discrete signals faster than the sensory system can resolve them, and the brain constructs continuity from discreteness.

### The Varied Thrush Contrast

The Varied Thrush (degree 2) produces an **actually sustained tone** -- a single whistle held for 1--2 seconds at constant pitch. There are no discrete pulses. The sound is genuinely continuous from start to finish. The spectrogram shows a solid, unbroken horizontal line.

The Oregon Junco produces what **sounds like** a sustained tone but is structurally a rapid series of discrete pulses. The spectrogram shows a row of individual dashes at constant pitch -- a dotted line where the thrush has a solid line.

This is a fundamental distinction:
- **Degree 2 (Varied Thrush):** Continuous signal. Analog. The tone IS sustained.
- **Degree 11 (Oregon Junco):** Discrete signal perceived as continuous. Digital-to-analog conversion. The tone is CONSTRUCTED from pulses.

The thrush holds a note. The junco builds a note from atoms. The same perceptual result from opposite structural strategies. This is the deepest connection between the two degrees: they sound similar (single pitch, sustained duration) but are architecturally opposite (continuous vs. discrete).

### Connection to BPS and LED

The **BPS** (Built & Physical Systems) project documents the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem: a continuous signal can be perfectly reconstructed from discrete samples if the sampling rate is at least twice the signal's highest frequency component. The junco's trill is the biological demonstration. The "signal" is a sustained tone at a given pitch. The "samples" are the individual trill notes. The trill rate is the sampling rate. When the trill rate exceeds the auditory system's Nyquist threshold (the flicker fusion rate for hearing), the brain reconstructs the continuous tone from the discrete samples. Shannon's theorem in feathers.

The **LED** project documents persistence of vision -- PWM (pulse-width modulation) dimming, where an LED flashes on and off rapidly and the eye perceives steady light at a brightness proportional to the duty cycle. The junco's trill is the acoustic PWM: individual note pulses at a duty cycle that exceeds the ear's fusion threshold, perceived as continuous tone at a "brightness" (volume) proportional to the pulse energy.

## Forest Edge: Ecology of the Threshold

### The Edge Specialist

The Oregon Junco is a **forest edge** species. Not deep forest (the Varied Thrush's domain). Not open field (the Savannah Sparrow's domain). The edge -- the boundary where forest meets clearing, where canopy gives way to shrub, where one world grades into another. This is the **ecotone**, the same transitional zone the bushtit (degree 10) occupies, but at a different structural position within it.

The bushtit works the shrub layer of the edge -- low, dense, interior to the thicket. The junco works the ground layer of the edge -- scratching in leaf litter, hopping on trails, feeding at the boundary between cover and open ground. Both are edge species. They partition the edge vertically: bushtit above, junco below.

In the Puget Sound region, Oregon Junco habitat includes:
- **Forest margins** -- where Douglas fir or western hemlock stands meet clearings, roads, or meadows
- **Second-growth edges** -- regenerating forest with a mosaic of dense young trees and open patches
- **Suburban yards and parks** -- the most common winter feeder bird in Seattle. The junco IS the backyard bird
- **Trailhead parking lots** -- juncos are the birds hopping on the gravel, flashing white outer tail feathers when they fly up
- **Logged clearings** -- edges created by timber harvest, with slash piles and residual canopy

### The Moody Blues Threshold

The MDB (Moody Blues) research project documents **threshold architecture** -- the concept of boundaries between genres, between movements, between the verse and the chorus, between the quiet passage and the loud passage. The threshold is where the music changes character. The edge is where the ecosystem changes character.

The junco lives at the ecological threshold. It is a transitional species between the deep forest interior (Varied Thrush, Spotted Owl, Pacific Wren) and the open landscape (meadow birds, grassland sparrows). It feeds on both sides. It nests at the boundary. Its trill carries across the threshold -- audible from within the forest AND from the clearing. The junco's territory IS the threshold.

In music, the trill itself is a threshold technique. The tremolo marks a transition -- in orchestral writing, a tremolo string passage often signals a shift in mood, key, or dynamics. The trill ornament in Baroque and Classical music decorates the note at a cadence point -- the boundary between phrases. The junco's trill, ecologically and musically, is a threshold signal: the sound of the edge, the sound of transition, the sound of the boundary between states.

## Music Theory Mapping

### Trill / Tremolo: Rapid Repetition as Sustained Tone

The **tremolo** is one of the oldest techniques in Western music. A violinist rapidly alternates bow direction on the same note. A pianist rapidly re-strikes a key. A mandolinist rapidly picks the same string. A timpanist rolls with both mallets on the same drumhead. In every case: discrete attacks perceived as continuous tone. The tremolo is the musical exploitation of the flicker fusion threshold -- the same psychoacoustic principle the junco's trill exploits.

The tremolo differs from vibrato (pitch oscillation), from modulation (frequency change), and from sustain (genuinely continuous energy input). The tremolo is **reconstruction of continuity from discreteness**. It is the musical analog of the stroboscopic effect. And the junco's trill is the biological original.

### Metallic Timbre: The Rich Harmonic Spectrum

The junco's trill is described as **metallic** -- a hard, bright, ringing quality distinct from the woody warmth of a thrush whistle or the breathy softness of a wren's trill. "Metallic" in acoustic terms means **rich in upper harmonics**: the fundamental frequency is accompanied by strong overtone content that extends well above the fundamental, giving the sound a hard, bright edge.

Metallic timbre in instruments: the harpsichord (vs. the softer piano), the steel-string guitar (vs. the nylon-string), the snare drum with snares engaged (vs. the warmer tom-tom), the trumpet (vs. the mellower French horn). In every case, "metallic" means more harmonic energy in the upper partials. The junco's "metallic" quality places it in the bright, forward, harmonically rich zone of the timbral spectrum.

### Single-Pitch Persistence: The Ostinato at Maximum Rate

An **ostinato** is a repeated musical figure -- a short pattern played over and over. A bass ostinato (ground bass, basso ostinato) underpins Baroque chaconnes and passacaglias. A rhythmic ostinato drives minimalist music (Steve Reich, Philip Glass). The junco's trill is an ostinato reduced to its absolute minimum: **one note, repeated at maximum rate**. It is the ostinato's endpoint -- the point where repetition becomes so rapid that the pattern dissolves into a tone. The figure disappears into its own frequency.

### Edge Ecology as Threshold

The forest edge maps to the musical threshold -- the boundary between sections, between keys, between dynamic levels. The trill, placed at the ecological edge, is the sound of the threshold itself. In classical cadence structure, the trill ornaments the penultimate note before resolution -- the note AT the boundary between tension and release. The junco trills at the boundary between forest and field, between winter and spring, between cover and exposure. The trill IS the cadence ornament of the forest edge.

## Conservation Status

The Dark-eyed Junco (including the Oregon form) is listed as **Least Concern** by the IUCN. It is one of the most abundant birds in North America, with an estimated breeding population of approximately **260 million** (Partners in Flight 2020). The Oregon form is the most common subspecies group on the Pacific coast.

The species thrives in edge habitat, suburban landscapes, and disturbed areas -- habitats that human land use tends to create and expand. Oregon Juncos are among the most frequent visitors to bird feeders in the PNW, especially in winter. Their abundance and adaptability make them one of the most resilient species in the region.

**Vulnerability factors.** Despite overall population health, juncos face: domestic cat predation (their ground-foraging behavior makes them vulnerable), window strikes (they readily approach human structures), pesticide exposure from lawn and garden chemicals, and habitat loss in areas where forest edges are converted to dense development without residual vegetation.

**Attribution note (OCAP/CARE compliance).** The forest edges and ecotonal habitats of the Puget Sound region are part of the ancestral and continuing homelands of Coast Salish peoples, including the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Suquamish, Snoqualmie, Tulalip, and other nations. Traditional land management -- particularly controlled burning that maintained forest-meadow mosaics -- created and sustained the edge habitats that juncos depend upon.
