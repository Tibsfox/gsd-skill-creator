Bewick’s Wren

Thryomanes bewickii

CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Oregon Slender Salamander (Batrachoseps wrighti) at degree 55. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Bewick’s Wren replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s low-elevation brushy/understory habitat. Both species are year-round residents of Pacific Northwest lowland habitats. This is the eleventh consecutive amphibian-class replacement (Pacific Chorus Frog at 45, Columbia Spotted Frog at 46, Red-legged Frog at 47, Tailed Frog at 48, Western Toad at 49, Rough-skinned Newt at 50, Northwestern Salamander at 51, Long-toed Salamander at 52, Olympic Torrent Salamander at 53, Clouded Salamander at 54, Oregon Slender Salamander at 55) — the engine remains deep in the lungless salamander zone of the original CSV’s amphibian belt.

TROGLODYTIDAE RETURNS — After 54 Degrees, the Wrens Reunite

The Bewick’s Wren brings Troglodytidae back to the engine after a 54-degree absence (last: degree 1, Pacific Wren) — the longest family return gap in engine history. This is the 2nd Troglodytidae overall (1, 55) and introduces a new genus: Thryomanes (“reed madness”) vs. Troglodytes (“cave diver”). Same family, entirely different ecological strategy: the Pacific Wren is a deep forest specialist singing an innate cascade of 100+ notes. The Bewick’s Wren is a suburban generalist singing a culturally learned repertoire of 9–22 variable song types from exposed perches. Passeriformes returns after the one-degree Piciformes interruption at degree 54.

The 56th Acoustic Element: The Yard Song

A loud, melodious, variable song of whistles, warbles, buzzes, and trills — learned from neighboring territory holders and varying geographically through cultural transmission. Named “the yard song” for three resonances: (1) the habitat — the Bewick’s Wren is the yard bird, singing from fence posts and hedgerows in the suburban edge; (2) the cultural landscape — songs vary geographically because they are taught by neighbors, creating acoustic dialects that mirror physical neighborhoods; (3) the contrast — against the Pacific Wren’s deep-forest cascade at degree 1, the yard song is wrens leaving old-growth and moving into the garden.

The yard song communicates through diversity (9–22 distinct song types per male) rather than density (100+ notes per cascade). Cultural vocal learning as acoustic identity.

Cultural Vocal Learning: Songs Learned from Neighbors

Young male Bewick’s Wrens learn their songs from neighboring territory holders — not from their fathers. A young male settles on a territory and memorizes the songs of males already holding adjacent territories. This horizontal cultural transmission produces geographic dialects that change gradually over distance: Seattle songs differ from Portland songs differ from Medford songs. Individual males hold 9–22 distinct song types in their repertoire, cycling through them during singing bouts. The Bewick’s Wren is a cultural animal — its song is not genetically determined but socially learned.

Two Wrens, Two Worlds: Forest vs. Suburb

The Pacific Wren (degree 1, “the cascade”) and the Bewick’s Wren (degree 55, “the yard song”) represent the two fundamental wren strategies in the Pacific Northwest. Both weigh 8–12 grams. Both are year-round residents. Both are Troglodytidae. But the Pacific Wren hides in old-growth understory and pours out an innately structured cascade of 100+ notes. The Bewick’s Wren perches on a fence post and delivers a culturally learned repertoire of variable songs. The forest and the yard. The cave diver and the reed-frenzy bird. The wrens bracket the engine: degree 1 and degree 55. The first wren sang from the deepest interior. The second sings from the nearest edge.

The Eastern Range Contraction: A Species Lost Its Ground

The Bewick’s Wren has almost completely disappeared from eastern North America — one of the most dramatic range contractions among North American passerines. Once breeding from the Appalachians to the Midwest, the species is now essentially extirpated from its former eastern range. The primary driver: competitive displacement by the House Wren (Troglodytes aedon), an aggressive cavity competitor that destroys eggs and usurps nest sites. In the West, the species remains common and stable — the PNW is its stronghold. This is the opposite of the Lesser Goldfinch’s range expansion (degree 44). The engine now has both vectors: expansion and contraction.

Bewick’s Reed-Frenzy Bird

Thryomanes bewickii — from Ancient Greek thryon (“reed”) + manes (“madness, passion, frenzy”) + bewickii (named by Audubon in 1827 for Thomas Bewick, 1753–1828, English wood engraver and naturalist). Full translation: “Bewick’s reed-frenzy bird.” Monotypic genus — Bewick’s Wren is the only species in Thryomanes. Compare the Pacific Wren’s Troglodytes pacificus (“Pacific cave diver”) at degree 1: the cave diver hides in darkness; the reed-frenzy bird burns with restless energy in the open.

The Sound: Master Vocalist of the Suburban Edge

Primary song: A loud, melodious, variable performance — a string of short whistles, warbles, burrs, buzzes, and musical trills delivered from an exposed perch. Frequency ~2.0–8.0 kHz, peak energy 3.0–6.0 kHz. Duration ~2–4 seconds per song. Structure: whistle-trill-finish three-part architecture — 1–2 clear whistled notes, a rapid trill or buzzy warble, a sharp terminal flourish. Enormously variable in specific notes. Bright, clear, musical quality.

Repertoire: Individual males hold 9–22 distinct song types, each a stereotyped pattern, cycled through in succession. Songs learned from neighboring territory holders, not from fathers. Geographic dialects change gradually over distance.

Plumage: The Bold Supercilium

Bold white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) extending from bill base back past the eye — the defining field mark. Brown above (PNW form: richer brown, warm rufous tinge), pale gray below (PNW form: grayer than eastern). Long tail frequently cocked upward and flicked side to side — a behavioral field mark. Small size: 13–14 cm, 8–12 g — essentially the same mass as the Pacific Wren (degree 1). Slender, slightly decurved bill for probing crevices. Separated from House Wren by bold supercilium; from Pacific Wren by longer tail, suburban habitat; from Marsh Wren by upland brush habitat.

Habitat: The Yard Bird

Breeds in brushy, scrubby, and edge habitats at lower elevations: suburban gardens, hedgerows, brush piles, blackberry tangles, Garry oak savanna, riparian brush, open woodlands, chaparral. Nests in birdhouses, mailboxes, tin cans, tool sheds, garages — extraordinary flexibility in nest site selection. The common thread is structure at ground level: dense, tangled vegetation or human structures with crevices for foraging and nesting. Year-round resident in western Washington. Dramatic contrast with the Pacific Wren’s old-growth forest dependency.

Nesting: The Cavity Opportunist

Cavity nester with extraordinary flexibility: natural cavities, woodpecker holes, birdhouses, mailboxes, old boots, dryer vents, hanging planters. Male builds multiple “dummy nests”; female selects one. 5–7 white eggs with brown speckles. 14–16 day incubation (female only). 14–16 day nestling period. 1–2 broods per season in PNW.

Theory Nodes

Y

The Yard Song foundational

The 56th acoustic element. Loud melodious variable song of whistles, trills, and buzzes, learned from neighbors and varying geographically. First element defined by cultural vocal learning. Music-theory parallel: theme and variations — each song type is a variation on the whistle-trill-finish template, varying within the bird and across the landscape.

W

Troglodytidae Returns After 54 Degrees foundational

2nd Troglodytidae overall (1, 55). Longest family return gap in engine history. New genus Thryomanes vs. Troglodytes. Suburban generalist vs. deep forest specialist. The wren theme returns transformed by 54 degrees of development. Music-theory parallel: the recapitulation — the main theme returns in the home key, changed by everything the music went through.

D

Cultural Vocal Learning and Dialects foundational

Young males learn songs from neighbors, not fathers. 9–22 song types per male. Geographic dialects change gradually across the landscape. Horizontal cultural transmission — the same mechanism that produces human linguistic dialects. Music-theory parallel: the folk tradition — songs that exist because singers learned from other singers, mutating gradually across geography.

C

The Eastern Range Contraction foundational

Near-total disappearance from eastern North America driven by House Wren competition. Opposite of Lesser Goldfinch range expansion (degree 44). The engine has both vectors: expansion and contraction. PNW is the stronghold. The eastern song dialects — an entire cultural tradition — were lost. Music-theory parallel: the lost school — a regional musical tradition that dies when the community disperses.

T

Two Wrens, Two Worlds intermediate

Pacific Wren (degree 1) and Bewick’s Wren (degree 55): forest interior vs. suburban edge. Same mass (8–12 g), opposite strategies. Density vs. diversity. Hidden vs. displayed. Wilderness vs. domesticity. The wrens bracket the engine. Music-theory parallel: the double concerto — two soloists from the same family, different voices, different positions.

S

The Bold White Supercilium intermediate

The defining field mark: a bold white eyebrow stripe extending from bill base past the eye. Long tail cocked and flicked. PNW form richer brown above, grayer below. After the sapsucker’s vivid red head (degree 54), a subtler visual identity — a precise structural mark, not a field of color. Music-theory parallel: the motif — a short, distinctive figure that instantly identifies the character.

SPS-048: White-crowned Sparrow

TWO DIALECT SPECIES. The White-crowned Sparrow shows sharp dialect boundaries. The Bewick’s Wren shows gradual, continuous dialect gradients. Both are cultural learners with neighbor-influenced vocal traditions. Different dialect architectures.

SPS-044: Lesser Goldfinch

OPPOSITE RANGE VECTORS. The Lesser Goldfinch (degree 44) expands northward. The Bewick’s Wren (degree 55) contracted eastward. Range expansion vs. range contraction. The engine has both stories.

SPS-045: Song Sparrow

TWO SUBURBAN SINGERS. Both common suburban PNW birds with loud, melodious songs. Different architectures: the sparrow’s preamble vs. the wren’s whistle-trill-finish. Both frequent the brushy edges of human landscapes.

SPS-053: Steller’s Jay

NEST DESTROYER AND PREY RISK. Steller’s Jays raid Bewick’s Wren nests. The wren’s scold call targets jays. The corvid alarm (degree 53) and the yard song (degree 55) share suburban habitat as predator and prey.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Bewick’s Wren documented across western Washington and British Columbia. Year-round resident of brushy habitats and suburban areas. One of the most common yard birds in western Washington.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Edge habitat ecology. Suburban adaptation. Cultural vocal transmission and geographic dialects. Competitive displacement by House Wrens in eastern North America. The ecological interface between wildness and domesticity.
