First Laridae — The Gull and Tern Chapter Opens

Larus glaucescens — the Glaucous-winged Gull — enters at degree 289. The first Laridae entry in the SPS engine. The most common, most widespread, most ecologically central gull of the Pacific Northwest. The colony soundscape: hundreds of birds calling simultaneously — mewing contact calls, alarm calls, territorial long calls, chick begging — the emergent acoustic aggregate of a breeding colony.

The Laridae arc (289–292) opens here. Four consecutive degrees document the gull and tern family across four PNW ecological contexts. Glaucous-winged Gull is the anchor: the species against which all other PNW gulls are measured.

Acoustic Profile — The Colony Chorus

Mew call: "keeow" or "aaah-aaah-aaah" — the basic contact and alarm call. Fundamental frequency approximately 800–1,500 Hz. At colony, hundreds of individuals produce this simultaneously at different rates and pitches.

Long call: Head thrown back, series of loud descending laughing notes. Used in territorial and social contexts. Punctuates background mewing chorus.

Alarm call: Rapid "kek-kek-kek" or "ga-ga-ga" — approximately 1,500–3,500 Hz. Propagates through colony like a wave: one bird's alarm triggers adjacent birds within seconds.

Colony aggregate: All calls simultaneously — mewing background, long calls, alarm waves, chick begging. Continuous multi-layered soundscape audible at distance. E=6: sustained colonial chorus, not single maximum-intensity event.

The Colony Soundscape as Emergent Sound

The Glaucous-winged Gull colony soundscape introduces a new acoustic category to the SPS engine: the emergent colonial sound. Previous entries have been individual-scale sounds (a single orca's call, a single sea lion's roar, a single whale's pulse) or small-group sounds (pod calls, dolphin bursts). The colony is different: the sound does not originate from any single individual but arises from hundreds of individuals simultaneously. No bird produces "the colony sound." The colony sound is what you hear when all of them are calling at once.

This emergent quality is the defining acoustic characteristic of colonial nesting waterbirds. The Laridae arc (289–292) centers this ecology: three of four degrees are colony soundscapes. The family's acoustic contribution to the PNW soundscape is inseparable from its colonial nesting behavior.

Larus glaucescens — The Founding PNW Gull

The Glaucous-winged Gull is the most common large gull in the Pacific Northwest, present year-round in the Salish Sea, Puget Sound, and the outer coast. The species breeds from the Aleutian Islands south through British Columbia and Washington. In the PNW, it breeds throughout the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and on coastal rocks and islands of Washington and Oregon.

The "glaucous-winged" name refers to the species' key field mark: gray wingtips rather than the black tips of most other large gulls. Adult identification is reliable on this character. The species hybridizes extensively with the Western Gull in the overlap zone (Washington State southward), producing intermediate birds that challenge field identification and make Washington State one of the most intensively studied gull hybrid zones in North America.

The species is an opportunistic omnivore: fish, invertebrates, eggs of other waterbirds, carrion, and garbage. The flexibility of diet is a major factor in the species' abundance and year-round presence. In urban Puget Sound, the Glaucous-winged Gull is the seagull — the bird that follows ferries, congregates at fishing piers, and occupies parking lot light posts near waterfront restaurants.

Colonial Nesting — Why the Colony Works

The Glaucous-winged Gull is an obligate colonial nester: it does not breed as isolated pairs. Colonial nesting confers specific fitness advantages: collective predator detection (the alarm call system scales with colony size; a 500-pair colony detects threats far more effectively than 500 individual pairs), information transfer about food (following other birds' behavior to productive foraging areas), and the physical deterrence of multiple adults defending simultaneously against predators.

Colony sites in the PNW include Protection Island (Jefferson County, WA — one of the largest colonial waterbird concentrations in the Pacific Northwest), Smith and Minor Islands, and various rocks throughout the San Juan archipelago and Salish Sea. The species has also colonized urban rooftops as suitable coastal habitat has been altered — a behavioral flexibility that has maintained the breeding population in the face of disturbance at traditional sites.
