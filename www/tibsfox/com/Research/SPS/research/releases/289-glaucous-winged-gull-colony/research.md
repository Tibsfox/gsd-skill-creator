## FIRST LARIDAE -- THE TAXONOMIC MILESTONE

### Opening the Gull and Tern Chapter

Degree 289 opens the Laridae chapter in the SPS engine. The Laridae (family name from *Larus*, the gull genus) includes the gulls, terns, skimmers, and skuas -- a large and ecologically diverse family of waterbirds. The SPS engine's Laridae arc runs four consecutive degrees (289-292), covering the most representative PNW members of the family:

- **289: Glaucous-winged Gull colony.** The most common gull in the PNW. Colonial nesting. The founding Laridae entry.

- **291: Western Gull colony.** Rocky island specialist. Loud overlapping calls.

The arc represents the Laridae family across four ecological niches: mixed colonial nesting (289), winter beach flock (290), rocky island colony (291), sandy spit colony (292). Together they document the full range of Pacific Northwest gull and tern acoustic environments.

- **Why Glaucous-winged first.** *Larus glaucescens* is the anchor of PNW gull ecology. It is the most abundant, the most visible, the most frequently heard. In Puget Sound, on the coast, at the ferry terminals, at the fishing piers, at the garbage disposal sites -- the Glaucous-winged Gull is the background species against which all other gulls are measured. Opening the Laridae arc with the founding species is correct.
- **The colonial nesting sound.** The specific acoustic signature chosen for degree 289 is the colony soundscape: hundreds of birds calling simultaneously at a nesting site. ## LARUS GLAUCESCENS -- THE SPECIES

### The Most Common Gull in the Pacific Northwest

*Larus glaucescens* (Naumann, 1840) is the defining gull of the Pacific Northwest:

- **Identification.** A large gull (wingspan 54-58 inches / 137-147 cm); adults in breeding plumage have a white head and underparts, gray back and upperwing, and distinctive gray wingtips (the "glaucous-winged" name refers to the gray wingtips, contrasting with the black tips of many other large gulls). The wingtip pattern is the key field mark: gray with white spots at the tip, not black. Eye ring pink-red in adults; yellow orbital ring. Bill yellow with red gonydeal spot.
- **Scientific name.** *Larus glaucescens*. *Larus* (from Latin *larus*, seagull); *glaucescens* (from Latin *glaucescens*, becoming gray-green or glaucous). Named by Johann Friedrich Naumann (1840), German ornithologist.
- **Size.** One of the largest North American gulls. Adults average 24-27 inches (61-68 cm) body length, wingspan 54-58 inches (137-147 cm), weight approximately 2.2-2.9 lbs (1.0-1.3 kg). Among the largest gull species regularly occurring in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Plumage sequence.** Like other large gulls, the Glaucous-winged Gull requires four years to reach adult plumage. First-winter birds are mottled brown-gray throughout. Second-winter birds show the beginning of the adult gray back. Third-winter birds resemble adults but show residual brownish wash. Adults are clean white and gray. The four-year maturation means that at any given colony, multiple plumage classes are present simultaneously.
- **Hybrid zone.** The Glaucous-winged Gull hybridizes extensively with the Western Gull in the Pacific Northwest overlap zone (roughly Washington State coast south to northern California). Glaucous-winged x Western hybrids are common in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea and can be difficult to assign to either parent species. The hybrid zone is one of the most studied in North American ornithology.

(Sources: Larus glaucescens identification; Johann Friedrich Naumann 1840; glaucescens name origin; size measurements; four-year plumage sequence; hybrid zone with Western Gull L. occidentalis; Puget Sound hybrid identification)

## THE COLONY VOCALIZATION -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### The Sound of a Nesting Colony

The Glaucous-winged Gull colony soundscape is the defining acoustic event of Pacific Northwest summer waterbird ecology:

- **The mew call.** The basic contact and alarm call of the Glaucous-winged Gull is a long, drawn-out mewing note: "aaah-aaah-aaah" or "keeow." This is the species' most frequent vocalization. At a colony, hundreds of birds produce this call simultaneously, at different rates and in slightly different pitches, creating an overlapping, layered mewing chorus.
- **The long call.** The long call is used in territorial and social contexts: the bird throws its head back and produces a series of loud, laughing, descending notes. At a colony, multiple birds execute long calls simultaneously, adding a punctuating, emphatic element to the background mewing chorus.
- **The alarm call.** A harsh, rapid "kek-kek-kek" or "ga-ga-ga" series, produced when a threat (aerial predator, human intrusion) approaches the colony. The alarm call spreads through a colony rapidly -- one bird's alarm triggers adjacent birds, producing a wave of alarm calling that can sweep an entire colony in seconds. The colony alarm chorus is the loudest and most intense acoustic event in the Glaucous-winged Gull's behavioral repertoire.
- **The colony aggregate.** At an active nesting colony, all of these calls are occurring simultaneously: mewing background chorus, individual long calls, scattered alarm calls, food-begging calls of chicks. The aggregate is a continuous, multi-layered, overlapping soundscape that is audible at considerable distance. This is the E=6 rating: sustained, loud, multi-layered, but not at the maximum physical intensity of, for example, the Steller Sea Lion bull roar (E=8) or the Botch mathcore attack (E=9).
- **Frequency range.** Glaucous-winged Gull vocalizations are concentrated approximately 500-4,000 Hz, well within human hearing range. The mewing call's fundamental frequency is approximately 800-1,500 Hz. The alarm call is higher, approximately 1,500-3,500 Hz. All vocalizations are easily audible to human observers at colony sites.
- **Chick vocalizations.** Chicks produce begging calls -- a rapid, high-pitched squealing that contributes to the colony soundscape from hatching (late June) through fledging (August). The chick begging calls add a different pitch register to the adult vocalizations, creating additional acoustic complexity in the colony soundscape.

(Sources: Glaucous-winged Gull vocalization catalog; mew call frequency 800-1,500 Hz; long call description; alarm call propagation through colony; colony acoustic aggregate; chick begging call; frequency range 500-4,000 Hz; E=6 calibration)

## COLONIAL NESTING ECOLOGY

### Why Gulls Nest in Colonies

The Glaucous-winged Gull is an obligate colonial nester:

- **Colonial nesting advantages.** Breeding in large groups provides multiple advantages: collective defense against predators (the alarm call system depends on colony size -- a larger colony detects threats sooner and responds more effectively), information transfer about food sources (other birds' behavior indicates productive foraging areas), and the physical deterrence of multiple defending adults toward aerial predators. Colonial nesting is not a coincidence; it is a behavioral strategy with demonstrable fitness advantages.
- **Colony sites in the PNW.** Glaucous-winged Gulls nest on exposed rocky islands, coastal headlands, and (increasingly) on rooftops of low buildings in coastal cities. Traditional colony sites in the Pacific Northwest include Protection Island (Jefferson County, WA), Smith and Minor Islands, various rocks off the outer coast of Washington and Oregon, and numerous small islets throughout the Salish Sea and Puget Sound. Rooftop nesting has increased substantially since the mid-20th century as suitable coastal habitat has been altered.
- **Colony size.** PNW colonies range from a few dozen pairs on small rocks to several thousand pairs on larger islands. Protection Island (Jefferson County, WA) supports one of the largest colonial waterbird concentrations in the Pacific Northwest, including a significant Glaucous-winged Gull colony alongside Rhinoceros Auklets, Tufted Puffins, and other species. The acoustic scale of a colony depends directly on the number of nesting pairs: at a few hundred pairs, the colony chorus is continuous and substantial; at several thousand pairs, it is overwhelming.
- **Nest structure.** Nests are built on the ground (on islands) or on rooftops (urban nesters): a cup of vegetation, feathers, and debris. Territories at dense colony sites are small -- adjacent nests may be within pecking range of each other. The spatial density of the colony contributes directly to the acoustic density: birds at adjacent nests are constantly within earshot of each other's calls.
- **Breeding chronology.** Egg laying begins in May; incubation is approximately 28 days; chicks hatch late June through July; fledging occurs August through early September. The colony is acoustically active from the first territorial establishment in April through fledging in August. Peak acoustic activity coincides with hatching and early chick-rearing, when begging calls from chicks add to the adult vocalization repertoire.

(Sources: Colonial nesting advantages: predator detection, information transfer, collective defense; PNW colony sites: Protection Island, Smith and Minor Islands, Salish Sea islets; rooftop nesting increase; colony size range; nesting territory density; breeding chronology April-August; chick hatching late June)

## DISTRIBUTION AND ECOLOGY

### The PNW's Foundational Gull

- **Range.** The Glaucous-winged Gull breeds from the Bering Sea (Alaska Peninsula, Aleutian Islands, Kodiak) south through British Columbia, Washington, and Oregon. The largest breeding concentrations are in southern Alaska. In Washington State, the species breeds throughout the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and Puget Sound. Non-breeding birds are present year-round in the Pacific Northwest; the breeding population is supplemented by wintering birds from farther north.
- **Year-round presence.** Unlike some waterbirds that are strictly seasonal in the PNW, the Glaucous-winged Gull is present in the region every month of the year. Breeding adults are at colony sites summer; adults and non-breeders are visible at ferry terminals, fishing harbors, beaches, and parking lots year-round. The species is the background gull of everyday Pacific Northwest life.
- **Foraging ecology.** The Glaucous-winged Gull is an opportunistic omnivore: fish (primarily herring, anchovy, smelt), invertebrates (clams, mussels, sea urchins, crabs), bird eggs (the species is a documented predator of other waterbirds' eggs at mixed colonies), carrion, garbage, and handouts from humans. The species' ecological flexibility is a major factor in its success in the Pacific Northwest, where it can exploit a wide range of food sources.
- **Population status.** The Glaucous-winged Gull is listed as Least Concern by the IUCN. North American breeding population estimated at approximately 300,000-400,000 individuals. Long-term trends in Washington State have shown some decline at traditional colony sites, associated with predation pressure (Bald Eagles, Peregrine Falcons, Mink, river otters), habitat disturbance, and food availability changes. The species remains abundant, but population trends at monitored colonies show the effects of changing ecological conditions.

(Sources: Glaucous-winged Gull breeding range Alaska-Oregon; Washington State San Juan Islands; year-round PNW presence; foraging ecology: fish, invertebrates, eggs, carrion, garbage; IUCN Least Concern; population 300,000-400,000; Washington State colony trends; Bald Eagle predation at colonies)

### Four Degrees of Gulls and Terns

The Laridae arc (289-292) documents the Pacific Northwest's gull and tern acoustic environments across four ecological contexts:

- **289 Glaucous-winged Gull colony (E=6).** The most common PNW gull. Colonial nesting. Summer. Mixed mewing and alarm chorus. The founding Laridae entry.
- **290 Herring Gull flock alarm (E=7).** Beach and harbor winter flock. Raucous overlapping alarm screams. E rises from the Glaucous-winged's colony chorus to the more intense Herring Gull alarm.
- **291 Western Gull colony (E=7).** Rocky island specialist. Strong Pacific coast association. Loud overlapping colony calls. Second colony soundscape.
- **292 Common Tern colony (E=7).** Sandy spit nesting. The tern's higher-pitched, sharper colony screaming. The arc closes with the Laridae family's distinct sub-family.

The arc's energy shape: E=6 (founding colony) → E=7 (alarm flock) → E=7 (second colony) → E=7 (tern colony). The opening Laridae entry is the only E=6; the subsequent three are all E=7. The Glaucous-winged colony is slightly lower energy because it is the ambient background colonial soundscape; the subsequent entries are higher-intensity specific acoustic events.

- **Colony ecology as the thread.** Three of the four Laridae arc entries (289, 291, 292) are colony soundscapes; the fourth (290) is a flock alarm. Colonial nesting is the defining behavioral and acoustic characteristic of the Laridae family in the PNW. The arc correctly centers the colony as the primary Laridae sound environment.
