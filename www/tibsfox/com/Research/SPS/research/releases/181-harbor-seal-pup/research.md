## THE PUP'S VOICE -- A DIFFERENT SOUND

### Why a Separate Entry

The Harbor Seal pup's high bleat is acoustically and functionally distinct from the adult bark:

- **Different sound.** The adult Harbor Seal (degree 180) produces a low, guttural bark -- a short, sharp vocalization for alarm and territorial purposes. The pup produces a high-pitched, nasal bleat -- a wavering, urgent call used to locate its mother. The two sounds are as different as a dog's bark and a lamb's cry.
- **Different function.** The adult bark signals alarm, territory, and social dominance. The pup bleat signals need, separation, and recognition. It is a contact call -- the sound of a bond.
- **Mother-pup vocal recognition.** Harbor Seal mothers and pups recognize each other's individual vocalizations within hours of birth. In the noise and chaos of a crowded beach with hundreds of seals, a mother can identify her pup's specific bleat, and a pup can identify its mother's response. This vocal individuality is encoded in the frequency structure and timing of the call.
- **Fifth double entry.** The Harbor Seal joins the select group of species documented twice in the engine, with each entry capturing a different acoustic and ecological dimension.

(Sources: Harbor seal pup vocalization studies; mother-pup recognition research; Riedman 1990; engine internal analysis)

## E=4 -- THE HIGH BLEAT

### Why E=4

The Harbor Seal pup's bleat registers at E=4:

- **Volume:** The pup bleat is moderate -- loud enough to carry across a beach but not the volume of an adult sea lion roar. Designed to be heard by the mother at short to medium range.
- **Persistence:** Pups bleat repeatedly when separated, creating a persistent, urgent sound. The repetition increases effective acoustic presence.
- **Frequency:** Higher-pitched than adult vocalizations. The high frequency cuts through the ambient sound of waves and wind on the beach.
- **Emotional intensity:** The bleat carries urgency -- a pup calling for its mother. The sound is hard to ignore, both for the mother and for human observers.
- **Why E=4 (same as adult)?** Different sound, same energy level. The adult bark is louder per call but intermittent. The pup bleat is softer per call but more persistent. The effective acoustic energy balances at E=4 for both.

(Sources: Harbor seal pup acoustics; amplitude measurements; engine internal analysis)

## MOTHER-PUP BONDING

### The Recognition System

The Harbor Seal mother-pup bond is one of the most studied in marine mammal biology:

- **Birth and imprinting.** Pups are born on beaches and tidal flats in late spring to early summer. Within hours, mother and pup exchange vocalizations that encode individual identity. This vocal "imprinting" happens fast -- critical for survival in crowded haul-out areas.
- **The nursing period.** Harbor Seal pups nurse for 4-6 weeks. During this time, the mother must forage in the water while the pup waits on the beach. Each reunion requires vocal recognition -- the pup bleats, the mother responds, they find each other by sound.
- **Beach separation.** When the mother enters the water to forage, the pup is alone on the beach. If disturbed (by predators, humans, or other seals), the pup bleats -- a distress and contact call combined. This is the sound most people hear when they encounter a seal pup on a PNW beach.
- **"Don't touch the pup."** NOAA and WDFW consistently advise: if you find a seal pup alone on a beach, do not touch it. The mother is likely foraging nearby and will return. Human contact can disrupt the mother-pup bond and cause abandonment. The pup's bleat is normal -- it is calling for a mother who will return.
- **Weaning.** After 4-6 weeks, the pup is weaned abruptly. The mother stops returning. The pup must learn to forage on its own. The transition from dependence to independence happens fast.

(Sources: NOAA marine mammal guidelines; WDFW seal pup advisory; harbor seal pup rearing studies; Riedman 1990; engine internal analysis)

## PUPPING SEASON IN PUGET SOUND

### Summer on the Beaches

Harbor Seal pupping season in the PNW:

- **Timing:** Late May through August, with peak in June-July. Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, and outer coast beaches.
- **Birth sites:** Protected sandy beaches, tidal flats, rocky shores with gentle approaches to water. The same sites are used year after year.
- **Pup appearance:** Born with a short, gray or brown fur coat (lanugo is shed in utero, unlike some seal species). Pups can swim within hours of birth but spend most early time on the beach.
- **Growth rate:** Pups gain approximately 0.5 kg per day during nursing. Harbor Seal milk is approximately 45% fat -- one of the richest milks in nature.
- **Beach crowding:** Popular pupping beaches can have dozens to hundreds of seals -- mothers, pups, and non-breeding adults. The acoustic environment is a mix of adult barks and pup bleats. Vocal recognition becomes critical in this crowded setting.

(Sources: WDFW pupping season data; harbor seal reproductive biology; Puget Sound pupping sites; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Harbor Seal Pup

**High Bleat (contact call):**
- **Trigger:** Separation from mother, disturbance, hunger. Given when alone on beach or when mother departs to forage.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (laryngeal). High-pitched.
- **Character:** High, nasal, wavering bleat. Lamb-like quality. Urgent. Repeated.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 500-3000 Hz. Higher fundamental than adult bark. Nasal resonance.
- **Duration:** Individual bleat 0.3-1.0 seconds. Repeated in series of 3-20+ calls with brief pauses.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Moderate. Carries across beach. Persistent repetition increases effective acoustic reach.

**Vocal Individuality:**
- Each pup's bleat has a unique frequency signature -- recognizable by its mother within the colony noise. Frequency contour, timing, and harmonic structure encode identity.

**Contrast with Adult (Degree 180):**
- Adult bark: low, guttural, 200-2000 Hz, alarm/territorial.
- Pup bleat: high, nasal, 500-3000 Hz, contact/distress.
- Same species, same energy, completely different sound.

(Sources: Harbor seal pup vocalization studies; frequency analysis; mother-pup recognition; engine internal analysis)
