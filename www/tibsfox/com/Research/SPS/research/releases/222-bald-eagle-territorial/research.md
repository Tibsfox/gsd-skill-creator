## THE TWO-DEGREE BALD EAGLE ARC

### Contact Call (221) vs. Territorial Call (222)

The decision to give the Bald Eagle two consecutive engine degrees reflects the genuine distinction between its two most important vocalizations:

- **The contact call (221).** Private. Thin, descending, 4-10 notes. Used between mates, between adults and young. The voice of the familiar relationship. E=5.
- **The territorial call (222).** Public. Long, rapid, piercing, 15-30+ notes. Used in nest defense, boundary announcement, intruder response. The voice of the contested relationship. E=6.
- **The distinction matters.** The two calls are not simply louder/softer versions of the same vocalization. They serve different functions, occur in different behavioral contexts, and have different acoustic profiles. The contact call is a private conversation; the territorial call is a public declaration.
- **Engine precedent.** No other species in the SPS engine has occupied two consecutive degrees. The Bald Eagle earns this distinction because: (1) both vocalizations are acoustically distinct enough to merit separate analysis, (2) both vocalizations serve critically different ecological functions, and (3) the two-degree arc allows the engine to document both the private and public dimensions of the eagle's vocal ecology.
- **222 as escalation.** Degree 222's territorial call escalates from degree 221's contact call in the same way that Hammerbox (S36 222) escalates from Love Battery (S36 221). E=5 to E=6. Private to public. Familiar to contested.

## THE TERRITORIAL CALL -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### A Different Voice from the Same Bird

- **The sound.** The territorial call is a rapid, extended sequence: "kek-kek-kek-kek-kek-kek..." sustained for 15-30 notes or more. The notes are shorter and more percussive than the contact call's descending sequence. The overall impression is of urgent, intense declaration -- the bird is asserting something.
- **Frequency.** Similar range to the contact call (2,000-5,000 Hz) but delivered with more force and intensity. The higher amplitude (louder) and longer sequence give the territorial call greater carrying distance.
- **Directionality.** The territorial call is often directed at the intruder -- the eagle faces the perceived threat and calls toward it. This directional quality distinguishes it from the contact call, which is broadcast more generally.
- **Physical posture.** The territorial call is accompanied by specific postures: head lowered, beak open, body forward. The bird is signaling threat and territorial ownership simultaneously. The call is part of a multimodal display.
- **Escalation sequence.** The territorial encounter often follows a sequence: initial territorial call (warning), approach flight (showing size and willingness to engage), aerial display (wings spread, banking), potential physical contact if intruder does not withdraw. The call initiates the sequence.
- **Comparison to contact call.** The contact call is 4-10 notes, thin, descending, quiet. The territorial call is 15-30+ notes, rapid, percussive, loud. The difference in note count alone is a 3-4x escalation. When an eagle shifts from contact calling to territorial calling, the behavior change is dramatic and immediately recognizable.

(Sources: Bald Eagle territorial call; acoustic comparison; directionality; physical posture; escalation sequence; frequency analysis)

## NEST TERRITORY -- WHAT IS BEING DEFENDED

### The Eyrie and Its Surroundings

Understanding Bald Eagle territorial behavior requires understanding what the eagle is defending:

- **The nest (eyrie/aerie).** Bald Eagles build and maintain the largest bird nests in North America. A typical active nest is 1.5-2 m (5-6 ft) across and 1-2 m (3-6 ft) deep; after years of addition, some nests reach 2-3 m across and 4 m deep, weighing 1,000+ kg. The nest is the centerpiece of the eagle pair's territory -- it is where chicks are raised, where the pair bonds, where the family unit is maintained.
- **The nest territory.** Around the nest, the pair defends a variable territory that excludes other Bald Eagles. The territory size varies by habitat and resource density: in areas with abundant food (salmon rivers, productive coastline), territories may overlap more at the edges; in resource-limited areas, territories may be larger and more strictly defended.
- **Year-round residency and year-round territorial behavior.** PNW Bald Eagles are year-round residents. Unlike migratory species that only defend territory during breeding season, resident eagles maintain a degree of territorial awareness throughout the year. The territorial call is most intense during breeding season (January-August) but can occur at any time.
- **Intruder types.** Bald Eagles defend their nest territory primarily against other Bald Eagles (the most direct competitors for nest sites) but also against large corvids (Ravens, Crows) that may raid nests, Great Horned Owls (which can predate nestlings), and occasionally Red-tailed Hawks, Great Blue Herons, and other large birds.
- **Kleptoparasitism defense.** Interestingly, while Bald Eagles are notorious kleptoparasites of Osprey (stealing fish from them), the eagle also must defend against aerial theft of its own prey by other eagles. The territorial call can function to assert priority over a food resource as well as a nest site.

(Sources: Bald Eagle nest size; eyrie; territory size variation; year-round residency; intruder species; kleptoparasitism dynamics)

## BREEDING BIOLOGY AND THE CALL'S SEASONAL PEAK

### January Through August -- The Breeding Season Arc

In the Pacific Northwest, Bald Eagle breeding follows a distinctive seasonal pattern:

- **January-February: Pair bonding and nest preparation.** Established pairs return to previous nest sites and begin refurbishing. New pairs engage in aerial courtship displays -- dramatic locking-of-talons spirals, undulating flight, close pursuit. Territorial calls increase as pairs establish or reestablish their territory.
- **February-March: Egg laying.** Typically 1-3 eggs, laid at 3-4 day intervals. The female does the majority of incubation; both parents incubate. The nest territory is most intensely defended during incubation -- any intruder approaching the nest elicits immediate territorial calling.
- **April-May: Hatching.** Eggs hatch after approximately 35 days of incubation. The chicks (eaglets) are helpless at hatching -- covered in gray down, unable to thermoregulate, dependent on parental brooding. The parents are maximally alert to threats during this period.
- **May-August: Eaglet development.** The eaglets develop rapidly. At 8-12 weeks, the first flight (fledging) occurs. The young eagles remain in the natal territory for weeks after fledging, learning to hunt. The parents continue to supply food and continue territorial behavior through this period.
- **Late summer-fall: Dispersal.** Juvenile eagles disperse from the natal territory. They will spend 4-5 years as non-breeding wanderers, developing the adult plumage and hunting skills, before establishing their own territory and pair bond.

(Sources: PNW Bald Eagle breeding biology; egg laying February-March; incubation 35 days; fledging 8-12 weeks; juvenile dispersal)

## THE TERRITORIAL CALL IN CONTEXT -- PNW SOUNDSCAPES

### Eagles in the Pacific Northwest Acoustic Environment

- **River soundscapes.** Along PNW salmon rivers (Skagit, Snoqualmie, Hoh), Bald Eagle territorial calls are a prominent winter and spring sound. When multiple eagles congregate near spawning salmon runs, territorial calling is frequent -- the eagles establish feeding hierarchies as well as spatial territories.
- **Coastal soundscapes.** Along Puget Sound shorelines, Bald Eagle territorial calls echo across the water. The aquatic environment is an excellent acoustic transmitter -- the call carries farther over open water than through forest. Eagles calling on the Puget Sound shore can be heard from considerable distance.
- **The call in the forest.** When an eagle calls from a nest tree in old-growth Douglas fir forest, the call reflects off the surrounding trees and carries through the understory. Forest acoustics shape the call's transmission -- the high frequency carries through the dense vegetation that would absorb lower-frequency sounds.
- **Response to human presence.** Bald Eagles in PNW are relatively tolerant of distant human presence but will begin territorial calling if humans approach too close to the nest. Wildlife guidelines suggest maintaining at least 150 m (500 ft) distance from active nests. A territorial call in response to a birdwatcher's approach is the eagle communicating clearly: go away.

(Sources: Skagit River eagle acoustics; Puget Sound coastal soundscapes; forest acoustic propagation; human disturbance response; nest approach guidelines)

## BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY -- THE CONTEST

### When Eagles Meet Eagles

- **Resident pair vs. intruder.** The most common territorial encounter is between a resident breeding pair and an unpaired or transient Bald Eagle entering the territory. The resident birds call first, then approach. If the intruder persists, aerial chasing follows.
- **Aerial displays.** Bald Eagles in territorial disputes engage in dramatic aerial displays: stooping (rapid diving), locking talons and spiraling (similar to courtship), and close parallel flight at high speed. These displays assess both willingness to fight and physical capability. Most territorial disputes are resolved by display without physical contact.
- **The dominant adult pair.** In PNW areas with high eagle density (Skagit Valley, Nisqually, Dungeness), territorial boundaries are well-established and relatively stable from year to year. The dominant adult pairs (those with established nests and successful breeding histories) tend to have the most stable and defended territories.
- **Social hierarchy at food concentrations.** At major food concentrations (salmon die-offs, winter fish aggregations), eagles establish a loose dominance hierarchy based on body size and aggressiveness. Adult eagles displace juvenile and subadult eagles from the best feeding positions. Territorial calling is used to maintain position in this hierarchy.

(Sources: Territorial contest dynamics; aerial display; talon-locking behavior; Skagit Valley density; social hierarchy at food concentrations)
