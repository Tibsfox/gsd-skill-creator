The Public Declaration

The contact call (degree 221) was the private voice: thin, descending, familiar. The territorial call is the public declaration: louder, longer, more urgent. 15-30+ notes in rapid sequence. The eagle is asserting something. The nest is here. This territory is mine. Go away.

What Is Being Defended

The Bald Eagle's territorial call defends the eyrie (nest) and its surrounding territory. The nest itself is a multi-year structure — a platform of sticks added to annually, sometimes reaching 2-3 m across and 4 m deep, weighing 1,000+ kg. The investment in a successful nest site is enormous. The territorial call protects that investment.

Intruders the territorial call addresses: other Bald Eagles (most common), large corvids that may raid nests, Great Horned Owls, and occasionally approaching humans. When a birdwatcher approaches too close to a nest, the eagle's territorial call is unambiguous communication: go away now.

Breeding Season — When the Call Peaks

  • Jan–Feb Pair bonding, nest refurbishment, territory establishment. Territorial calling begins.
  • Feb–Mar Egg laying (1-3 eggs). Incubation begins. Territory defense at maximum intensity.
  • Apr–May Hatching. Helpless chicks. Maximum parental alertness. Most intense territorial calling period.
  • May–Aug Eaglet development. First flight at 8-12 weeks. Parents defend while teaching hunting.
  • Late summer Juvenile dispersal. Territorial intensity decreases. The arc closes.

Acoustic Environment

Along PNW salmon rivers (Skagit, Snoqualmie, Hoh), Bald Eagle territorial calls are a prominent winter and spring sound. When multiple eagles congregate near salmon runs, territorial calling is frequent — feeding hierarchy established through call, approach, and aerial display. Over Puget Sound, the call carries farther over open water, echoing across the inlet. In old-growth Douglas fir forest, the high-frequency call penetrates the understory that would absorb lower-frequency sounds.
