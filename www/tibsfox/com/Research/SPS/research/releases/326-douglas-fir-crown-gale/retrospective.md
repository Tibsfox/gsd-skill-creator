## What Worked

1. **The three-mechanism framework correctly captures the complexity of the Douglas fir gale sound.** The sound is not a single acoustic event -- it is three simultaneous mechanisms at different frequencies. The framework (aerodynamic = hiss, structural = groan, Aeolian = moan) gives the entry structure and allows each mechanism to be documented with appropriate precision.
2. **The Aeolian frequency formula (f = Strouhal × speed / diameter) is the most specific acoustic physics in the post-summit SPS sequence.** Applying the formula to specific branch diameters at typical gale speeds produces concrete frequency predictions (75 Hz for 5 cm branch at 40 mph, 190 Hz for 2 cm branchlet). This level of physical precision is new for the engine's meteorological entries.
3. **The distinction between the moan and the roar is acoustically and perceptually important.** The roar (aerodynamic broadband) is what people typically describe when they report the sound of wind. The moan (Aeolian tonal component) is what makes the Douglas fir gale specifically identifiable -- the pure tones emerging from the noise background. Documenting this distinction adds acoustic depth.
4. **E=7 calibration is correct.** The Douglas fir gale is below E=8 (the Hoh winter storm wind layer is one of four layers there; isolated, it would be E=7). Sustained 70-85 dB at 20m is E=7.

## Discoveries

1. **The gust cycle (5-15 seconds per gust peak) creates a rhythmic pulse in the structural acoustic output that is perceptually distinct from the aerodynamic component.** The aerodynamic component is continuous (the hiss does not pause between gusts). The structural component is rhythmic (the creak and groan intensify at gust peaks and diminish between gusts). This rhythmic contrast within the moan-roar is an important perceptual feature -- the listener experiences both continuous noise and periodic structure simultaneously.
2. **The speed of sound in Douglas fir wood (5,000 m/s) means that structural acoustic events propagate through the trunk much faster than they propagate through the air.** A structural event (internal stress wave) initiated at the root base of a 75-meter tree propagates to the crown in approximately 15 milliseconds. This rapid propagation means the entire trunk is acoustically coupled -- flex at the base immediately affects the crown's acoustic output. The tree is a single acoustically coupled structure, not a collection of independent acoustic elements.
