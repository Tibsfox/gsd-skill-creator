The Tree as Acoustic Instrument

The Douglas fir crown in gale conditions produces a sustained moan-roar through three simultaneous mechanisms: aerodynamic turbulence from needle flow (hiss, 1,000–6,000 Hz), structural resonance from trunk and branch flex (groan, 20–200 Hz), and Aeolian tones from vortex shedding off cylindrical branch elements (moan, 75–500 Hz). The moan in the moan-roar: the tonal component that distinguishes the Douglas fir gale from white-noise wind.

The Three Mechanisms

Aerodynamic — Needle and Small Branch Flow

Air flowing past needle fascicles creates turbulent wake. Random and broadband: the “hiss” of air through the crown. Dominant at 40–50 mph. Highest-frequency component. The roar in the moan-roar.

Pseudotsuga menziesii — the Douglas fir — is the Pacific Northwest’s defining tree and one of North America’s most acoustically complex conifers in wind. Its size (100–200 metric ton mass), its crown complexity (billions of needle fascicles on primary branches extending 5–15 m), and its wood properties (speed of sound 5,000 m/s along grain, high stiffness) make it an unusually effective acoustic instrument in gale conditions. The root system (taproot to 5 m, lateral roots beyond crown radius) anchors it so that the trunk bends and flexes under load rather than failing — the flex is the mechanism for structural acoustic radiation.

The Exposed Ridge Setting

The exposed ridge provides the maximum gale conditions because topographic acceleration amplifies wind speed by 20–40% above valley levels. A Pacific Northwest winter storm delivering 35 mph in the valley may deliver 45–50 mph at the ridge crest. At the subalpine zone transition — where the Douglas fir approaches its upper elevation limit — the trees are shorter and more exposed than at lower elevations, increasing the load per unit of canopy. The gale-force conditions at Pacific Northwest ridge crests occur dozens of times per winter season. The Douglas fir moan-roar is a seasonal expectation, not a rare extreme.
