## THE DOUGLAS FIR -- THE TREE

### Pseudotsuga menziesii: The Engine's Primary Forest Tree

The Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) is the dominant tree of the Pacific Northwest's temperate forests and the most commercially important tree in North America. In the acoustic context, what matters is the tree's physical structure:

- **Size and mass.** Old-growth Douglas firs reach heights of 60-90 meters and diameters of 2-4 meters. The mass of an old-growth Douglas fir is enormous: a typical specimen can weigh 100-200 metric tons. This mass is acoustically significant -- the tree has low resonant frequencies precisely because of its mass. The structural resonance of a 150-ton tree trunk is at frequencies of 5-20 Hz: sub-bass that is felt before it is heard.
- **Crown morphology.** The Douglas fir crown at old-growth scale is not a simple conical shape. The crown of a large old-growth fir is a complex three-dimensional structure: large primary branches extending 5-15 meters from the trunk, secondary and tertiary branching systems, and billions of needle fascicles (clusters of flat needles 2-3 cm long). This crown structure is the primary aerodynamic transducer in the gale: wind flowing through the crown generates acoustic output across a wide frequency range depending on which structural element the wind is interacting with.
- **Root structure.** The Douglas fir's root system is deep and extensive -- a large old-growth fir has a taproot that may extend 3-5 meters deep and a lateral root system extending well beyond the crown radius. The root system anchors the tree structurally, which means that in a gale, the trunk bends and flexes (transmitting the flex as acoustic energy) rather than tipping over. The tree is not rigid; it is flexible within limits set by the root anchorage.
- **Wood properties.** Douglas fir wood is dense (the typical air-dry density is approximately 530 kg/m³) and has high modulus of elasticity (stiffness-to-weight ratio). These properties affect the acoustic behavior: the wood transmits stress waves rapidly (the speed of sound in Douglas fir along the grain is approximately 5,000 m/s, compared to 343 m/s in air). Structural sounds in the tree trunk are transmitted as wood vibration long before they are transmitted into the air.

## THE ACOUSTIC MECHANISMS -- THREE SIMULTANEOUS PROCESSES

### Why the Douglas Fir Gale Sound Is Not Simple

The moan-roar of a Douglas fir crown in gale conditions is the product of three simultaneous acoustic-generation mechanisms:

**Mechanism 1: Aerodynamic (needle and small branch flow)**
At wind speeds above approximately 20-25 mph, the flow of air through the Douglas fir's needle structure becomes acoustically significant. The needles (2-3 cm long, flat, arranged alternately along small branchlets) create turbulent wakes as air flows past them. The turbulence is not rhythmic (as in the Aeolian tones of a true resonator) but is random and broadband: the needle layer converts laminar wind flow into turbulent wake, and the turbulence radiates as acoustic energy. At 40-50 mph gale wind, this aerodynamic mechanism produces a continuous broadband sound (1,000-6,000 Hz at needle scale) that is the characteristic "hiss" component of the gale sound. This is the highest-frequency component of the moan-roar.

**Mechanism 2: Structural (trunk and large branch flex)**
Under sustained gale loads, the Douglas fir trunk flexes. The flex is not visible as rotation or bending at the human eye scale -- it is within the elastic limit of the wood. But the flex is large enough (millimeters to centimeters of displacement at the crown of a 75-meter tree) to generate internal stress waves in the wood. These stress waves radiate from the wood surface as acoustic energy at the resonant frequencies of the structure:
- **Trunk fundamental resonance:** A 75-meter Douglas fir trunk with a 3-meter diameter has a resonant frequency in the range of 0.1-0.5 Hz -- below the audible range. The trunk sways at this frequency but the audible acoustic output from the trunk flex is from higher harmonics: 2-20 Hz range (felt more than heard) and 20-200 Hz range (the deep creak and groan of wood under sustained load).
- **Branch resonance:** Large primary branches (diameter 0.3-0.8 meters, length 5-15 meters) have higher resonant frequencies than the trunk: 2-20 Hz for the lowest modes, with higher harmonics audible in the 20-500 Hz range. The creak and groan of these branches under sustained load is part of the moan-roar's mid-frequency content.
- **The creak and groan.** The structural component of the moan-roar is the deep, slow, rhythmic sound of wood under sustained cyclic stress. As the gust peaks, the trunk and branches flex slightly; as the gust fades, they return. This cycle (5-15 seconds per gust in Pacific Northwest winter storms) creates a slow rhythmic variation in the structural acoustic output -- the moan has a pulse.

**Mechanism 3: Aeolian resonance (specific structural elements)**
Under specific conditions -- particular wind speeds creating specific Reynolds numbers for the cylindrical elements of the tree structure (branches of specific diameter) -- Aeolian tones emerge: pure tones created by the vortex shedding behind a cylinder in wind. The frequency of an Aeolian tone depends on the wind speed and the cylinder diameter: f = (Strouhal number × wind speed) / diameter. For a 5 cm branch at 40 mph (18 m/s) wind: f ≈ (0.21 × 18) / 0.05 ≈ 75 Hz. For a 2 cm branchlet at the same speed: f ≈ (0.21 × 18) / 0.02 ≈ 190 Hz. These Aeolian tones are superimposed on the broadband aerodynamic and structural noise -- they emerge as quasi-pure tones from the noise background when the wind is sufficiently steady and the structural element is sufficiently cylindrical. They are the "moan" in the moan-roar: the sustained tonal component that distinguishes the Douglas fir gale sound from the white-noise roar of the open wind.

## THE EXPOSED RIDGE SETTING

### Why the Ridge Provides the Maximum Gale Event

The exposed ridge ecosystem is the correct setting for the Douglas fir gale event:

- **Exposure.** The exposed ridge is the setting where the Pacific Northwest's prevailing westerly storms encounter the least topographic shelter. On a ridge crest, the wind is unobstructed by valley walls or forest mass -- the full gale speed reaches the individual trees. A tree on an exposed ridge receives the same wind load as a tree on the open coast: the wave-like topography of the Cascades and Olympics creates ridge crests that intercept the wind directly.
- **Wind speed amplification.** Ridge crests amplify wind speed relative to the surrounding terrain -- a well-documented meteorological phenomenon. The topographic acceleration effect (wind forced over a ridge crest must accelerate to conserve mass) can produce wind speeds 20-40% higher at the ridge crest than in the adjacent valley. A Pacific Northwest winter storm that delivers 35 mph winds in the valley may deliver 45-50 mph at the ridge crest.
- **The edge of the subalpine zone.** The exposed ridge in the Pacific Northwest is typically at the edge of the subalpine zone -- the transition between the dense conifer forest of the lower elevations and the open subalpine parkland and alpine terrain above. At this transition, the Douglas fir is near its upper elevation limit: the trees are shorter and more exposed than the same species at lower elevations, but they are still large enough to produce the characteristic gale acoustic event. The combination of exposure and large tree size makes the subalpine ridge Douglas fir the most acoustically productive setting for the gale event.
- **Gale frequency.** Pacific Northwest winter storms produce gale-force winds (sustained 40+ mph) at exposed ridge crests on a regular basis: dozens of events per winter season, each lasting from a few hours to a few days. The gale is not a rare extreme event at the exposed ridge -- it is a seasonal expectation.

(Sources: Exposed ridge wind exposure; topographic acceleration 20-40% wind speed increase at ridge crest; Pacific Northwest westerly storm track; 35 mph valley vs. 45-50 mph ridge crest; subalpine zone transition; Douglas fir upper elevation limit; gale-force definition 40+ mph; Pacific Northwest winter storm gale frequency)

## THE MOAN-ROAR -- E=7 CALIBRATION

### Sustained Force at Intermediate Energy

The Douglas fir crown in gale conditions calibrates to E=7 for specific reasons:

- **Sustained vs. peak.** The gale sound is sustained rather than impulsive. Unlike the rock avalanche (E=8, brief peak) or the tidal bore (E=8, sustained hydraulic mass), the Douglas fir gale is a sustained medium-to-high energy event that continues for the duration of the gale. The E=7 calibration reflects the sustained nature: high enough to dominate the acoustic environment on the ridge, moderate enough that it does not overwhelm the ear at close range.
- **Amplitude.** At 40-50 mph gale speeds, the Douglas fir crown generates 70-85 dB at 20 meters from the trunk -- well above the threshold for affecting attention and behavior but not at the 90-100+ dB range of the highest-amplitude single events in the engine (grizzly bear roar, rock avalanche, storm surf). E=7 is the correct calibration for this amplitude range.
- **Comparison to Hoh wind layer.** The Hoh winter storm wind layer (degree 324) was calibrated as one of four simultaneous layers, contributing to an E=8 combined event. The exposed ridge Douglas fir gale is the same wind applied to a single tree -- without the rain layer, the creek layer, and the canopy layer of the Hoh event. Isolated, the Douglas fir crown in gale is E=7. In combination with other layers, it contributes to E=8 events.
- **The moan-roar as continuous sound.** The three-mechanism moan-roar of the Douglas fir in gale conditions is one of the Pacific Northwest's most continuous and pervasive acoustic events during storm conditions. It is the background against which all other ridge events occur during a gale. Hikers on ridge trails, researchers at subalpine stations, and wildlife on exposed terrain all experience the moan-roar as the defining acoustic environment during storm events.

(Sources: E=7 calibration; sustained vs. peak amplitude; 70-85 dB at 20m; comparison to E=8 events; Hoh wind layer comparison; Douglas fir gale as continuous storm background; exposed ridge acoustic environment during gale)
