## PUGET SOUND -- THE TIDAL SYSTEM

### A Glacially Carved Inland Sea

Puget Sound's physical geography determines its tidal behavior:

- **The glacial origin.** Puget Sound was carved by the Cordilleran Ice Sheet during the last glacial maximum (~17,000 years ago). The ice sheet, at its maximum extent 3,000 to 6,000 feet thick over what is now Seattle, scoured the bedrock into the elongated north-south basin that is Puget Sound. The Sound's characteristic depth (average ~65 meters; maximum ~280 meters near Point Jefferson) and its irregular channel system with multiple sub-basins are direct results of differential glacial erosion. The ice carved deep channels where the subglacial drainage was concentrated and left shallow sills where the erosion was less intense.
- **The channel geometry.** Puget Sound is not a simple bay. It is a complex system of interconnected channels, basins, and passes: the Main Basin (the largest), Hood Canal (a long narrow fjord extending to the southwest), the South Sound (the shallow, narrow extension south of the Narrows), Case Inlet, Carr Inlet, and Colvos Passage. The entire system connects to the Strait of Juan de Fuca through Admiralty Inlet (the northern entrance, between Point Wilson and Whidbey Island) and through Deception Pass (a secondary northern connection). Water enters and exits the Sound primarily through Admiralty Inlet.
- **The sill at the Narrows.** The Tacoma Narrows -- where the Narrows Bridge spans the channel -- is a geological sill: the bedrock rises to within about 50 meters of the surface, compared to depths of 200+ meters in the Main Basin. This sill restricts the tidal exchange between the Main Basin and the South Sound, creating the strong tidal currents at the Narrows (6-10 knots at maximum) and a phase lag in the tidal cycle between the Main Basin and the South Sound.

## THE TIDAL CURRENT -- MECHANICS AND SOUND

### How the Sound Breathes

The tidal current of Puget Sound operates through specific physical mechanisms:

- **The tidal prism.** The "tidal prism" is the volume of water that enters and exits a tidal basin with each tidal cycle. For Puget Sound, the tidal prism is approximately 25 cubic kilometers (25 billion cubic meters) per complete tidal cycle. This is an enormous volume: for reference, this is roughly 10 times the volume of Seattle's Lake Washington. This entire volume enters through Admiralty Inlet and the passes at the northern end of the Sound on the flood tide, and exits on the ebb. The movement of this volume through the inlet is the physical reality behind the tidal current.
- **Mixed semidiurnal pattern.** Puget Sound experiences a mixed semidiurnal tide: two high tides and two low tides per day, but the two highs and two lows are of unequal height. The tidal range at Seattle is typically 10-14 feet (3-4 meters). The highest tides of the year (king tides) can reach 15-16 feet above mean lower low water. The lowest low tides (minus tides) can expose extensive intertidal areas that are normally underwater.
- **Tidal current velocities.** In the open basins of Puget Sound, tidal current velocities are moderate: typically 0.5-1.0 knots (about 1-2 km/h). In the narrow passes and passages, velocities are much higher: 8-9 knots at Deception Pass, 6-10 knots at the Tacoma Narrows, 3-5 knots through Admiralty Inlet. These high velocities in narrow passes are the primary locations where the tidal current produces observable acoustic and hydrodynamic effects.
- **The acoustic character.** The tidal current's acoustic character is defined by what it is not: it is not the impact sound of tidal surge on cobble (252); it is not the surface wave or the waterfall. The tidal current produces sound primarily through turbulence: the irregular, multi-scale swirling of water as it flows past irregular channel geometry, through underwater features, past submerged objects. This turbulence generates broadband low-frequency noise -- typically below 100 Hz, often below 20 Hz (infrasound, technically inaudible to human hearing). The sound is felt more than heard: the subsonic pulse of the moving water mass.
- **E=2 placement.** E=2 is low -- present as background, detectable by sensitive instruments and perhaps by the human body in prolonged proximity to major tidal current flows, but not dominant in the way that rain (E=3) or waterfall (E=5-6) are. The tidal current is the Sound's quietest major physical process, despite moving the largest volume of any water in the system.

(Sources: Puget Sound tidal prism 25 cubic kilometers; mixed semidiurnal tide; tidal range 10-14 feet Seattle; king tides 15-16 feet; minus tides; tidal current velocities; 8-9 knots Deception Pass; 6-10 knots Tacoma Narrows; 3-5 knots Admiralty Inlet; turbulence noise broadband below 100Hz; infrasound below 20Hz; E=2 placement)

## THE UNDERWATER ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT

### What Puget Sound Sounds Like Underwater

The acoustic environment of Puget Sound below the surface is complex:

- **Natural acoustic baseline.** Below the tidal current's turbulence noise, Puget Sound has a rich natural acoustic baseline: invertebrate sounds (snapping shrimp produce a persistent crackling over wide frequency ranges), fish vocalizations (Pacific cod, various rockfish species, herring spawning), and marine mammal vocalizations (harbor seals, Steller sea lions, transient orca). The tidal current's infrasonic contribution is the largest single physical source, but it is the foundation below all these biological sounds.
- **The tidal current as biological rhythm.** The tidal cycle organizes biological life throughout Puget Sound. Species move with the current: forage fish (herring, smelt, anchovy) use tidal currents to migrate vertically and horizontally through the water column. Seabirds (rhinoceros auklets, common murres, pigeon guillemots) aggregate at tide rips -- the convergence zones where opposing tidal currents meet -- to feed on fish concentrated there. The tidal current is not just an acoustic phenomenon; it is the primary physical organizing principle for the Sound's biological community.
- **Tide rips.** Tide rips -- the rough water at current convergence zones -- are an audible version of the tidal current. Where the infrasonic tidal current is below human hearing, the surface expression of the current at a tide rip is audible: the hissing of small waves at the convergence line, the sound of disturbed water, the occasional splash of a seabird diving into the concentrated fish. Tide rips mark the location of the strongest current flows.
- **Underwater turbulence.** At the highest-current locations (Deception Pass, the Narrows), the tidal current generates visible turbulence at the surface: boils, whirlpools, standing waves. Below the surface, this turbulence is intense. The underwater current at Deception Pass at maximum ebb or flood -- 8-9 knots -- produces submarine turbulence that scours the bedrock, re-suspends sediment, and creates a continuous broadband noise field. This is the Sound's loudest underwater acoustic environment.

(Sources: Puget Sound underwater acoustics; snapping shrimp crackling; Pacific cod rockfish herring vocalizations; harbor seals Steller sea lions orca; forage fish tidal migration; rhinoceros auklets at tide rips; tide rip formation and feeding; Deception Pass turbulence; sediment re-suspension)

## CULTURAL AND ECOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE

### The Tidal Current in Human and Ecological Time

- **Coast Salish relationship to the current.** For the Coast Salish peoples -- Lekwungen, Saanich, Lummi, Samish, Suquamish, and others -- the tidal currents of Puget Sound and the Strait of Juan de Fuca were navigational infrastructure. The currents provided free transportation for canoe travelers who knew how to use them: departing at the right tidal phase, the current would carry a canoe from Admiralty Inlet to the Main Basin or from the Narrows to Case Inlet without paddle effort. Traditional knowledge of the tidal cycle -- its timing, its velocity, its direction at specific locations -- was practical navigation knowledge essential for travel, trade, and fishing.
- **The herring reef.** The tidal current aggregates forage fish at specific locations. In historical Puget Sound, massive aggregations of Pacific herring, Pacific smelt, and other forage fish occurred at predictable tidal current features. The convergence zones, the channel margins, the shallow sills where the current accelerates -- these were the productive fishing locations. The tidal current mapped the fish; traditional fishers followed the current to find them.
- **Current monitoring.** The tidal currents of Puget Sound are monitored by NOAA's Center for Operational Oceanographic Products and Services (CO-OPS). Current stations at the Tacoma Narrows, Admiralty Inlet, Deception Pass, and other locations record real-time current velocity and direction. This data is used for navigation, pollution modeling, and ecological research.

(Sources: Coast Salish tidal navigation; Lekwungen Saanich Lummi Samish Suquamish; tidal current as canoe transportation; traditional tidal knowledge; Pacific herring and forage fish at current features; convergence zone fishing; NOAA CO-OPS current monitoring stations; Tacoma Narrows current station)
