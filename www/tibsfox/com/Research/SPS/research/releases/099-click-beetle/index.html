The Mechanical Click — A Novel Sound-Production Mechanism

The click beetle’s sound is MECHANICAL [1, 2]. It snaps a spine-and-groove mechanism on its thorax — a prosternal spine fits into a mesosternal groove, builds tension, then releases with a sudden snap that launches the beetle into the air. The sound is a sharp, audible click. Not wing-produced. Not stridulation (wing-rubbing). Not tymbal (membrane vibration). A completely novel mechanism in the engine. The fourth distinct sound-production mechanism in insects: after stridulation (cricket 87, katydid 88), tymbal (cicada 89), and wing-buzz (mosquito 91). E=2.

NEW ORDER: Coleoptera — The Beetles Arrive

Coleoptera enters the engine [1, 2]. The largest order of insects — approximately 400,000 described species, roughly 25% of all known animal species on Earth. “God has an inordinate fondness for beetles.” Coleoptera is a NEW ORDER in the engine, joining Orthoptera (crickets, katydids), Hemiptera (cicadas), Diptera (mosquito), Plecoptera (stonefly), Ephemeroptera (mayfly). Sixth insect order. The engine finally reaches the largest order of life on Earth.

Fourth Insect Sound Mechanism — After Stridulation, Tymbal, Wing-Buzz

The engine now documents four distinct insect sound-production mechanisms: Stridulation (wing-rubbing: cricket 87, katydid 88, tree cricket 98). Tymbal (membrane vibration: cicada 89). Wing-buzz (flight vibration: mosquito 91). Mechanical click (spine-and-groove: click beetle 99). Each mechanism produces a fundamentally different sound. The click beetle’s snap is percussive, brief, singular — unlike any prior insect sound in the engine.

The Sound: Mechanical Click (99th Acoustic Element, species side)

The sound: The click beetle produces a sharp, audible click when it snaps its prosternal spine out of the mesosternal groove [1, 2]. The mechanism is a biological spring-latch system: the spine hooks into the groove under muscular tension, then releases suddenly, launching the beetle into the air (sometimes 20-30 cm). The click is a byproduct of this launch mechanism — the sound of the latch releasing. Brief, percussive, singular.

Why E=2: The click is quiet and brief [1]. A single snap. Audible at close range but not carrying. No sustained calling, no chorus, no repetition unless the beetle is handled repeatedly. E=2 reflects the brief, close-range, non-vocal nature of the sound. Higher than silence. Lower than any sustained caller. The stonefly (94, E=2) is similarly quiet — both are sounds you must be close to hear.

Taxonomy: Elateridae — NEW FAMILY (48th), NEW ORDER: Coleoptera

Classification: Class Insecta. Order Coleoptera — NEW ORDER [1, 2]. Family Elateridae — NEW FAMILY (48th in engine).

Coleoptera: The largest order of insects. Approximately 400,000 described species. Beetles. Characterized by hardened forewings (elytra) that protect the hindwings. Found in virtually every terrestrial habitat. The click beetles (Elateridae) are one of approximately 170 beetle families.

Elateridae: The click beetles. Approximately 10,000 species worldwide. Named for the clicking mechanism. Found in forests, meadows, and gardens. Larvae often live in dead wood or soil.

Ecology: Dead Wood Habitat

Habitat: Dead wood [1, 2]. Click beetle larvae (wireworms) often develop in rotting logs and dead wood. Adults found on vegetation, bark, and forest floor. Dead wood is a critical habitat — connecting the click beetle to the pileated woodpecker (degree 54, who creates dead wood cavities) and the beaver (degree 62, who creates dead wood through tree-felling).

The click mechanism: The spine-and-groove system is a predator escape mechanism [1, 2]. When a click beetle is placed on its back (often by a predator or by falling), it snaps itself into the air to right itself and escape. The click is a defensive sound — the sound of escape.
