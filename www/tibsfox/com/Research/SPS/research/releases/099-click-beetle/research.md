## THE CLICK BEETLE: COLEOPTERA ENTERS -- A COMPLETELY NOVEL SOUND MECHANISM

## THE SOUND: MECHANICAL CLICK -- 99th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Mechanism

The click beetle's sound is produced by a **spine-and-groove mechanism** on the thorax [1, 2]:

1. **Prosternal spine:** A spine on the prosternum (front of thorax) fits into a groove on the mesosternum (middle of thorax).
2. **Tension buildup:** The beetle arches its body, pressing the spine against the groove edge. Muscles build tension.
3. **Sudden release:** The spine snaps past the groove edge with a sudden, forceful release.
4. **Launch:** The energy release catapults the beetle into the air (sometimes 20-30 cm).
5. **Click:** The audible click is the sound of the spine snapping past the groove -- a mechanical latch releasing.

This is a **biological spring-latch system**. The mechanism is entirely mechanical -- no vibrating membranes, no rubbing surfaces, no wing movement. A snap.

### The Acoustic Character

- **Percussive:** A single, brief snap. No sustain, no resonance, no tonal quality.
- **Broadband:** The click is a broadband impulse, not a tonal frequency. Energy across a wide frequency range.
- **Brief:** Duration less than 10 milliseconds. Instantaneous.
- **Singular:** Each click is a discrete event. Not a sustained call or chorus.
- **Dry:** No reverberation, no ringing. A clean mechanical snap.

### Why E=2

1. **QUIET.** Audible at close range (within ~1 meter) but not carrying.
2. **BRIEF.** A single snap. No sustained sound.
3. **NON-COMMUNICATIVE.** The click is a byproduct of the escape mechanism, not a mating call or territorial signal.
4. **MATCHES STONEFLY (94, E=2).** Both are quiet, close-range insect sounds.
5. **LOWER THAN ALL SUSTAINED CALLERS.** Cricket (87, E=4), cicada (89, E=5), tree cricket (98, E=3) -- all sustained callers are louder.

## ECOLOGY: DEAD WOOD AND THE ESCAPE MECHANISM

### Dead Wood Habitat

**Habitat:** Dead wood [1, 2]. Click beetle larvae (called wireworms) often develop in rotting logs, dead wood, and soil. Adults are found on vegetation, bark, and forest floor. Dead wood is a critical microhabitat:

- **Pileated woodpecker** (degree 54) excavates dead wood to find beetle larvae -- a direct predator-prey relationship with click beetles.
- **Beaver** (degree 62) creates dead wood through tree-felling and flooding -- the beaver creates the habitat that click beetle larvae inhabit.
- Dead wood is one of the most important habitats in PNW forest ecosystems, supporting thousands of invertebrate species.

### The Escape Mechanism

The click mechanism is primarily a **predator escape response** [1, 2]:
- When a click beetle is placed on its back (often by falling from vegetation or by a predator), it cannot easily right itself due to its short legs and flattened body.
- The spine-and-groove snap launches it into the air, allowing it to land right-side-up and escape.
- The audible click may also startle predators, providing an additional moment to escape.
- Some click beetle species can click multiple times in rapid succession when handled.
