## E=6 RETURNS TO SPS -- MAXIMUM ENERGY

### The Most Powerful Avian Voice in North America

E=6 at degree 128 marks the return of maximum energy to the SPS axis. The Trumpeter Swan's deep resonant bugling is one of the loudest sounds produced by any North American bird:

- **Individual volume:** The Trumpeter's bugle carries at distances of **1-3 kilometers** in favorable conditions. The call is produced by the unusually long trachea (windpipe), which loops within the keel of the sternum (breastbone), creating a resonating chamber that amplifies and deepens the sound.
- **Physical basis for E=6:** The Trumpeter Swan's tracheal loop is one of the most remarkable anatomical features in North American ornithology. The trachea can be **over 1 meter long** in an adult bird, with the extra length coiled within the sternum. This extra-long windpipe acts as a resonating tube, producing the deep, brassy, trumpet-like quality that gives the species its name.
- **Why E=6 and not E=5:** The Tundra Swan at degree 127 registers E=5 because its sound is flock-driven -- the individual call is moderate. The Trumpeter Swan registers E=6 because the **individual call is powerful enough** to command maximum energy on its own. A single Trumpeter Swan bugling can fill a valley. The sound does not need flock amplification; it is inherently maximum.
- **The first E=6 in the Anatidae streak:** No previous Anatidae in the streak reached E=6. The Harlequin Duck's stream calls (E=5) were the previous high. The Trumpeter at E=6 is the streak's energetic climax.

## THE DEEP RESONANT BUGLING

### A Sound Produced by Anatomy

The Trumpeter Swan's bugling is among the most distinctive and powerful sounds in North American bird ecology:

- **Character:** A deep, low-pitched, resonant, brassy bugle. The sound has been compared to a French horn or a distant trumpet (hence the common name). The call is not melodious in the way of the Tundra Swan's baying -- it is **commanding, authoritative, deep-chested**. The resonance gives it a quality of physical presence: you feel the sound as much as hear it.
- **Frequency range:** 200-1,500 Hz. The fundamental frequency is notably low for a bird -- lower than most duck and goose vocalizations. The tracheal loop produces rich low harmonics that give the bugle its depth and carrying power.
- **Production mechanism:** The extra-long trachea coiled within the sternum creates a resonating chamber analogous to the tubing of a brass instrument. Air expelled from the lungs passes through this long tube, and the tube's length determines the fundamental pitch. The longer the tube, the lower the pitch. The Trumpeter's trachea is among the longest of any bird relative to body size.
- **Duration:** A single bugle lasts 1-3 seconds. Bugles are typically given in series of 2-5 calls, often accompanied by wing-flapping or head-bobbing displays.
- **Context:** Territorial defense, pair bonding, alarm, and general communication. The bugle serves as a long-distance announcement of presence and ownership. A pair of Trumpeter Swans bugling at their territory boundary can be heard by other swans at great distance.

## NORTH AMERICA'S LARGEST NATIVE BIRD

### Superlative Entry

The Trumpeter Swan is **the largest native bird in North America by weight** -- a superlative that has no close challenger:

- **Weight:** Males typically 10-12.5 kg (22-28 lbs), with exceptional individuals reaching 17.2 kg (38 lbs). Females 8-10.5 kg (18-23 lbs). The average weight exceeds that of all other North American birds including the California Condor, Bald Eagle, and Sandhill Crane.
- **Wingspan:** 185-250 cm (73-98 inches). Over 8 feet at maximum. The wingspan is exceeded only by the California Condor (up to 290 cm) and the American White Pelican (up to 274 cm), but the Trumpeter outweighs both.
- **Body length:** 138-165 cm (54-65 inches). One of the longest birds in North America.
- **Comparison with Tundra Swan:** The Trumpeter is distinctly larger than the Tundra Swan in every dimension. Weight: Trumpeter average ~11 kg vs. Tundra average ~6.5 kg (70% heavier). Wingspan: Trumpeter average ~210 cm vs. Tundra average ~190 cm. The size difference is visible in mixed flocks but can be difficult to judge without direct comparison.

The Trumpeter's size is connected to its acoustic power. The large body cavity houses the long trachea. The large lungs can expel more air at higher pressure through the tracheal resonating chamber. The bugle's depth and volume are direct products of the bird's size -- the largest bird produces the deepest, loudest voice.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mitchell 1994; Sibley 2014)

## THE CONSERVATION SUCCESS STORY

### From Near-Extinction to Recovery

The Trumpeter Swan's conservation history is one of the most dramatic in North American wildlife management:

- **Pre-European contact:** Trumpeter Swans were widespread across North America, breeding from Alaska through central Canada to the northern United States (including the Pacific Northwest, Great Lakes, and northern Great Plains). Population estimates for the pre-contact era range from 100,000 to several hundred thousand.
- **19th century decline:** Market hunting for feathers (used in writing quills, powder puffs, and hat decorations), skin trade, and egg collecting devastated the population. The Hudson's Bay Company traded thousands of swan skins annually. Habitat loss from wetland drainage for agriculture compounded the hunting pressure.
- **1935: The nadir.** A survey by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service counted **fewer than 100 Trumpeter Swans** in the contiguous United States, all in the Greater Yellowstone area (Red Rock Lakes, Montana). A small population persisted in Alaska (estimated 2,000-3,000), but the species was widely considered headed for extinction in the lower 48.
- **Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge (1935):** Established specifically to protect the last remaining Trumpeter Swans in the contiguous US. The refuge in southwestern Montana's Centennial Valley became the species' last stand.

### The Recovery

The Trumpeter Swan recovery is one of the defining conservation achievements of the 20th century:

- **1935-1960:** Strict protection at Red Rock Lakes. Hunting banned. Egg collection eliminated. The Yellowstone population stabilized at approximately 500 birds.
- **1960s-1980s:** Reintroduction programs began. Eggs and cygnets from Red Rock Lakes and Alaska were transplanted to historic breeding areas: Oregon, Washington, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, Ohio, Ontario. Each transplant required careful habitat management and long-term monitoring.
- **1990s-2000s:** Pacific Northwest populations established through transplant programs and natural range expansion. Washington State Trumpeter Swan numbers grew from near zero to hundreds of wintering birds.
- **Current population:** Approximately **63,000 Trumpeter Swans** in North America (2015 survey). The largest population is in Alaska (~23,000). The Rocky Mountain population numbers ~5,000. Pacific Coast populations (including PNW wintering birds) number ~2,000-3,000. The Interior Population (reintroduced Midwest/Great Lakes) numbers ~25,000+.
- **Conservation status:** Successfully delisted from Endangered status. Currently managed as a species of special concern in some states. The recovery from fewer than 100 to 63,000 over eight decades is a landmark in wildlife conservation.

### Skagit Valley Populations

The Skagit Valley in northwest Washington hosts wintering Trumpeter Swans alongside Tundra Swans:

- **Numbers:** Several hundred to ~1,000 Trumpeter Swans winter in the Skagit Valley area. Smaller numbers than the Tundra Swan (~several thousand) but significant.
- **Mixed flocks:** Trumpeter and Tundra Swans often winter in the same fields and wetlands, creating identification challenges. The Skagit Valley is one of the best locations in the PNW for direct comparison of the two species.
- **Fir Island fields:** The agricultural fields around Fir Island host both species feeding on waste grain. The deep bugling of Trumpeters and the high baying of Tundras create an interleaved winter soundscape.

(Sources: USFWS Trumpeter Swan recovery plan; Mitchell 1994; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## IDENTIFICATION: TRUMPETER VS. TUNDRA (COMPLETE)

### From the Trumpeter's Perspective

The identification comparison from degree 128 (completing the pair from degree 127):

**Bill:**
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Bill is longer, heavier, and more massive. Profile is **straight or slightly convex** -- the ridge of the upper mandible runs in a straight line or curves slightly outward. **No yellow loral spot.** The bill appears solid black (sometimes with a narrow pinkish line along the gape in some individuals).
- **Tundra Swan:** Shorter bill with **concave (dished) profile**. Usually shows a **yellow loral spot**. Bill appears lighter overall.

**Head shape:**
- **Trumpeter Swan:** Head shape is more **angular, wedge-shaped**. The forehead meets the bill at a steeper angle. The overall impression is heavier and more block-like.
- **Tundra Swan:** Head is more **rounded**. Gentler forehead slope. Lighter impression.

**Size:**
- **Trumpeter:** Distinctly larger in every dimension. In mixed flocks, the size difference is visible but can be deceptive at distance. Direct comparison is needed for reliable size-based identification.
- **Tundra:** Smaller, but still a large bird. A lone Tundra Swan can look like a Trumpeter if no size reference is available.

**Voice (the most reliable field mark -- again):**
- **Trumpeter:** Deep, resonant, brassy bugle. Low-pitched. Commanding. Unmistakable once learned.
- **Tundra:** High, melodious, whistling baying. Higher-pitched. Softer individually.

Voice remains the single most reliable field mark for separating the two species. Even experienced birders rely primarily on voice in the Skagit Valley mixed flocks.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE WETLAND GIANT

### Habitat and Breeding

The Trumpeter Swan's ecological requirements are defined by its size:

- **Breeding habitat:** Large, shallow freshwater marshes, ponds, and lakes with abundant aquatic vegetation. The nest is enormous -- up to 3.5 meters (11 feet) in diameter, the largest nest of any North American bird. Built from marsh vegetation (bulrushes, cattails, sedges), the nest is a massive mound that the female adds to throughout incubation.
- **Clutch:** 4-6 eggs. Incubation approximately 33-37 days. The female incubates; the male guards the territory aggressively. Trumpeter Swans are notably aggressive in nest defense and will attack intruders including humans, canoes, and other large waterfowl.
- **Cygnets:** Downy gray-white at hatching. Family groups remain together through the first winter. Cygnets can fly at approximately 100 days -- a longer development period than most waterfowl, reflecting the species' large size.
- **Territory:** Breeding pairs defend large territories (often an entire pond or lake section). The bugling serves as territorial advertisement, and physical confrontations between males are common.

### Year-Round Presence in the PNW

Unlike the Tundra Swan (which is exclusively a winter visitor), some Trumpeter Swan populations are **year-round residents** in the PNW:

- **Breeding populations:** Small breeding populations exist in the PNW, including reintroduced birds in Oregon and Washington. The breeding population in the Pacific Northwest is small but growing.
- **Winter populations:** The majority of PNW Trumpeter Swans are winter visitors from Alaska and interior BC/Alberta breeding populations. These birds arrive in fall and depart in spring, overlapping with Tundra Swans on the wintering grounds.
- **The Skagit mix:** In the Skagit Valley, both resident and migratory Trumpeter Swans are present alongside Tundra Swans. The winter waterfowl community in the Skagit is one of the richest in the PNW.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mitchell 1994; WDFW)
