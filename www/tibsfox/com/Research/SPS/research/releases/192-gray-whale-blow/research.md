## FIRST ESCHRICHTIIDAE -- A FAMILY OF ONE

### The Monotypic Family

Degree 192 introduces Eschrichtiidae:

- **FIRST ESCHRICHTIIDAE.** The Gray Whale family enters. Eschrichtiidae is a monotypic family -- one genus (*Eschrichtius*), one species (*E. robustus*). The Gray Whale is alone in its family. This is the third baleen whale family in the engine, but unlike Balaenopteridae (which contains multiple species), Eschrichtiidae has exactly one living member.
- **Seventh marine mammal family.** Phocidae (180-181), Otariidae (182-184), Mustelidae (185), Phocoenidae (186-187), Delphinidae (188-189), Balaenopteridae (190-191), and now Eschrichtiidae (192). Seven families in 13 degrees.
- **Baleen whale lineage continues.** The toothed-to-baleen transition at degree 190 continues. Eschrichtiidae represents a third baleen whale family, distinct from Balaenopteridae in feeding strategy (benthic suction vs lunge feeding).
- **Ancient lineage.** Gray whales are one of the most ancient baleen whale lineages. Fossil evidence suggests they have been migrating along the PNW coast for millions of years.

## E=5 -- THE POWERFUL BLOW

### Why E=5

The Gray Whale registers at E=5:

- **The blow.** The Gray Whale's blow is powerful, loud, and visible. A forceful exhalation that produces a V-shaped or heart-shaped spout 3-4 meters tall. The sound carries over water -- a deep, explosive "whoosh" audible from hundreds of meters. Whale watchers from shore can hear the blow before they see the whale.
- **Blow mechanics.** The blow is exhaled at speeds up to 300 mph from the twin blowholes. The warm, moist air condenses on contact with cooler air, producing the visible spout. The V-shape comes from the slight separation of the two blowholes.
- **Vocalizations.** Gray whales also produce low-frequency moans, pulses, and knocking sounds (100 Hz - 2 kHz). These are relatively simple compared to humpback songs but serve communication functions, especially between mothers and calves.
- **Near-shore audibility.** Because Gray Whales migrate close to shore (often within 1-2 miles), their blows are audible from beaches, headlands, and coastal trails. This makes the Gray Whale the most audible cetacean from land in the PNW.
- **Why E=5?** The blow is powerful and attention-getting. The near-shore migration makes Gray Whales the most visible and audible whale from PNW shores. But the vocalization repertoire is relatively simple (no complex songs like humpbacks) and the blow, while loud, is a breathing function rather than a communicative vocalization. E=5 reflects the impressive blow power and near-shore audibility while acknowledging the modest vocal complexity.

(Sources: Gray whale blow mechanics; vocalization studies; near-shore migration; engine internal analysis)

## THE LONGEST MAMMAL MIGRATION

### Alaska to Mexico

The Gray Whale undertakes the longest migration of any mammal:

- **Distance.** 10,000-12,000 miles round trip per year. From Arctic feeding grounds (Bering, Chukchi, and Beaufort Seas) to subtropical breeding and calving lagoons (Baja California, Mexico).
- **Route.** The migration follows the Pacific coast closely. Southbound whales pass the PNW coast in October-January. Northbound whales pass in March-June. Mothers with calves are the last to migrate north.
- **Duration.** The one-way migration takes 2-3 months. Continuous swimming at 3-5 mph with minimal feeding.
- **Observable from shore.** The close-to-shore migration route makes Gray Whales one of the most observable large whales in the world. Point Reyes (CA), Depoe Bay (OR), Westport (WA), and Long Beach (WA) are renowned whale-watching locations.
- **PNW summer residents.** A subset of Gray Whales -- the "Pacific Coast Feeding Group" (PCFG) -- spend the summer feeding along the PNW coast rather than continuing to the Arctic. These ~250 whales are regularly observed from Oregon through Washington.

(Sources: Gray whale migration; NOAA migration tracking; PCFG studies; engine internal analysis)

## BENTHIC FEEDING -- THE BOTTOM FEEDER

### A Unique Feeding Strategy

The Gray Whale feeds differently from all other baleen whales:

- **Benthic suction feeding.** The Gray Whale rolls on its side on the ocean floor and sucks sediment through its baleen, filtering out amphipods, tube worms, and other bottom-dwelling invertebrates. This is fundamentally different from the lunge feeding of rorquals (Balaenopteridae).
- **Feeding pits.** The suction feeding creates visible pits on the ocean floor -- depressions 1-2 meters across. These feeding pits are a distinctive trace fossil of gray whale activity.
- **Right-side preference.** Most Gray Whales feed preferentially on their right side, resulting in more baleen wear on the right. This behavioral laterality is analogous to human handedness.
- **Baleen.** 130-180 short, coarse baleen plates per side. The baleen is thicker and coarser than in rorquals -- adapted for filtering sediment rather than water.

(Sources: Gray whale feeding behavior; benthic suction feeding; amphipod ecology; engine internal analysis)

## OBSERVABLE FROM PNW SHORES

### The Whale You Can See from Land

- **Migration route.** Gray Whales pass within 1-5 miles of the PNW coast during migration. Many pass within 1 mile.
- **Washington viewing.** Westport, Long Beach, Cape Flattery, and coastal Olympic Peninsula locations offer reliable Gray Whale viewing during migration.
- **The blow from shore.** The V-shaped blow is visible from headlands and beaches. On calm days, the sound of the blow carries to shore -- a deep exhale audible over the surf.
- **Friendly whales.** In the calving lagoons of Baja California, Gray Whales approach whale-watching boats and allow physical contact ("friendly" behavior). This human-whale interaction is unique among baleen whales.
- **Population.** Eastern Pacific population estimated at ~27,000 (recovered from whaling). The Western Pacific population remains critically endangered (~200).

(Sources: Gray whale observation; PNW whale watching; friendly whale behavior; population estimates; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Gray Whale Blow

**Blow (Exhalation):**
- **Frequency:** Broadband, with energy from <100 Hz to >5 kHz.
- **Character:** Powerful, explosive exhalation. Deep "whoosh." V-shaped or heart-shaped spout, 3-4 meters tall.
- **Audibility:** Carries hundreds of meters over water. Audible from shore (1-5 miles) on calm days.
- **Duration:** 1-3 seconds per blow. 3-5 blows per surfacing sequence.

**Vocalizations:**
- **Moans:** 100-400 Hz. Low-frequency, sustained. Communication between individuals.
- **Pulses:** 200-2,000 Hz. Repetitive, rhythmic. Social context.
- **Knocking sounds:** 100-1,000 Hz. Percussive. Mother-calf communication.
- **Complexity:** Low compared to humpbacks. Functional rather than artistic.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=5. The blow is powerful, loud, and carries over distance. The near-shore migration route makes Gray Whales the most audible cetacean from PNW shores. The vocalization repertoire is simple but the blow is impressive. E=5 reflects the blow power and accessibility rather than vocal complexity.

(Sources: Gray whale acoustics; blow mechanics; vocalization studies; engine internal analysis)
