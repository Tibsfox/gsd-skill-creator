## Taxonomy: The Spotted Chirper

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Passerellidae (New World Sparrows)
- **Genus:** *Pipilo* (Vieillot, 1816)
- **Species:** *Pipilo maculatus* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Common Name:** Spotted Towhee
- **Former name:** Rufous-sided Towhee (before 1995 split)
- **Former classification:** Formerly lumped with Eastern Towhee as "Rufous-sided Towhee" (*Pipilo erythrophthalmus*); split in 1995 by the AOU

### Etymology

*Pipilo* -- from Latin *pipilo*, meaning "to chirp" or "to twitter" [1, 2, 5]. The genus name is onomatopoeic, evoking the bird's vocalizations. Compare with the Song Sparrow's genus *Melospiza* ("song finch") -- both genera are named for their voices, but where *Melospiza* implies melody, *Pipilo* implies a simple, repetitive chirp.

*Maculatus* -- from Latin *maculatus*, meaning "spotted" or "stained" [1, 2, 5]. The species epithet refers to the diagnostic white spots on the wings and back -- the plumage feature that distinguishes the Spotted Towhee from the Eastern Towhee and gives the bird its common name.

Full translation: **"The spotted chirper"** -- a bird named for its voice (chirping) and its appearance (spotted). Unlike the Song Sparrow, whose name is entirely acoustic ("melody song-finch"), the Spotted Towhee's binomial splits between sound and sight. One half says what the bird sounds like. The other half says what the bird looks like.

### The Naming Contrast

| Degree | Species | Binomial | Translation | Naming Philosophy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 43 | American Goldfinch | *Spinus tristis* | "Sad thorn-bird" | Ecological + emotional |
| 44 | Lesser Goldfinch | *Spinus psaltria* | "Female lute player of the thorn bush" | Ecological + musical |
| 45 | Song Sparrow | *Melospiza melodia* | "Melody song-finch" | Musical + musical |
| **46** | **Spotted Towhee** | ***Pipilo maculatus*** | **"The spotted chirper"** | **Acoustic + visual** |

The naming rebalances after the Song Sparrow's pure-acoustic double. The Spotted Towhee reasserts the visual: *maculatus* names the white spots that flash from the underbrush. After three degrees of increasingly acoustic naming (emotional, musical, pure music), the eye returns to the naming equation.

### The 1995 Split

Until 1995, the Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee were considered a single species called the "Rufous-sided Towhee" (*Pipilo erythrophthalmus*) [1, 2, 5, 8]. The American Ornithologists' Union split them based on differences in plumage, songs, call notes, and genetics. The two forms had diverged approximately one million years ago as populations expanded west and east from Mexico and became isolated [5, 8].

Key differences that justified the split [1, 2, 5, 8]:
- **Plumage:** Spotted Towhee has bold white spots on wings and back; Eastern Towhee lacks these spots
- **Song:** Spotted Towhee's songs are more variable and harsher in tone; coastal populations often sing trill-only songs without introductory notes
- **Call notes:** Spotted Towhee's calls are more variable and generally harsher
- **Genetics:** Mitochondrial DNA analyses confirmed reproductive isolation despite a narrow hybrid zone in the Great Plains (primarily Nebraska, North and South Dakota)

The split is relevant to the engine because it demonstrates how **vocal differences drive speciation** -- the same theme explored at degree 34 (Red Crossbill dialects). Song divergence is not just cultural; it can become reproductive. The Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee sound different enough that females preferentially respond to their own form's songs, maintaining the species boundary even where the two overlap [5, 8].

### The *Pipilo* Genus

The Spotted Towhee shares its genus with three other species [1, 2, 5]:

| Species | Binomial | Range | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spotted Towhee** | *Pipilo maculatus* | Western N. America | White-spotted, buzzy trill |
| Eastern Towhee | *Pipilo erythrophthalmus* | Eastern N. America | Unspotted, cleaner "drink-your-tea" |
| Green-tailed Towhee | *Pipilo chlorurus* | Interior West | Cat-like mew, olive-green |
| Collared Towhee | *Pipilo ocai* | Mexico | Boldly patterned, restricted range |

All four *Pipilo* are large, heavy-bodied, ground-dwelling sparrows that forage by scratching in leaf litter. The genus is the "ground floor" of Passerellidae -- the heaviest members of the family, spending the most time on the ground, with the most pronounced scratching behavior [1, 2, 5].

## The Sound: The Rasp

### Song Structure

The Spotted Towhee's song is structurally simpler than the Song Sparrow's but texturally more distinctive [1, 2, 3, 6, 7]:

**The song:** Approximately 1.5 seconds in duration. Begins with 1-2 (sometimes up to 8) short, clear introductory notes followed by a fast, buzzy trill. The trill is the song's defining feature -- a rapid, vibrating buzz that sounds like a taut rubber band being plucked, or a piece of paper stuck into a fan. The classic mnemonic is "drink-your-tea!" -- one or two clear introductory syllables ("drink your") followed by a buzzy, trilled terminal phrase ("teeeeeea!") [1, 2, 3, 6, 7].

**Geographic variation:** Song structure varies significantly across the range [1, 2, 3, 7]:
- **Pacific Coast populations** (including PNW *oregonus*): Often sing trill-only songs -- no introductory notes, just the buzzy trill. Simpler, more concentrated, more textural.
- **Interior and mountain populations** (*arcticus*, *curtatus*): Typically include 1-2 introductory notes before the trill. More structured, closer to the "drink-your-tea" mnemonic.

This geographic split in song structure parallels the plumage split: coastal birds are darker and vocally simpler; interior birds are more boldly marked and vocally more structured [1, 2, 3, 7].

**Frequency range:** Approximately 1.5-8.0 kHz across all vocalizations, with most song energy concentrated in the 2-6 kHz band [research literature, spectrographic analyses]. The trill occupies a broad frequency band, contributing to its buzzy, "rough" quality.

### The 47th Acoustic Element: The Rasp

The Spotted Towhee introduces the engine's 47th acoustic element (species side): **the rasp**.

The rasp is a concentrated, vibrating, single-frequency trill -- a sound defined not by melodic complexity but by textural intensity. Where the Song Sparrow's preamble opens a complex passage with clear introductory notes, the Spotted Towhee's rasp *is* the passage. The trill does not introduce something else; it is the event itself. A buzzy, vibrating, insistent sound that fills the underbrush with a frequency so rapidly modulated that the individual notes blur into a continuous, rough-textured tone.

The rasp is distinct from all nearby acoustic elements:

| Element | Character | Texture |
|---|---|---|
| The Preamble (45) | Clear opening notes before complexity | Clean, deliberate, positional |
| The Mosaic (44) | Borrowed phrases from many species | Varied, patchwork, interspecific |
| **The Rasp (46)** | **Concentrated buzzy trill** | **Rough, vibrating, single-frequency** |

The rasp differs from "the buzz" (degree 32, Bohemian Waxwing) because the waxwing's buzz is a high-pitched, thin social contact call -- a narrow-frequency, low-amplitude sound exchanged within flocks. The rasp is a territorial broadcast -- a broad-frequency, moderate-amplitude trill delivered from a fixed perch to stake a claim. The buzz is conversational. The rasp is declarative.

The rasp also differs from "the thrum" (degree 35, Rufous Hummingbird) because the thrum is mechanical -- produced by wing feathers, not by the syrinx. The rasp is vocal, produced by rapid syringeal vibration. Both are buzzy, insistent sounds, but the thrum comes from flight and the rasp comes from the throat.

Named "the rasp" for three resonances:
1. **The sound** -- the Spotted Towhee's trill has the texture of a rasp being drawn across wood: rough, vibrating, concentrated, producing a continuous rough tone from rapid repetition of a single element
2. **The tool** -- a rasp is a coarse file used to shape wood or metal by abrasion, removing material through friction. The Spotted Towhee's song "abrades" the silence of the underbrush, cutting through dense vegetation with a penetrating, textured frequency
3. **The foraging** -- the double-footed backward scratch is itself a rasping action, dragging clawed feet across leaf litter to expose the soil beneath. The bird rasps the ground before it rasps the air.

The rasp is the engine's first element defined by **textural concentration** -- an acoustic event that achieves its character not through melodic elaboration (preamble), harmonic richness (harmonic spiral), or compositional borrowing (mosaic), but through the sheer density of repetition within a narrow frequency band. The individual notes of the trill are so rapid and so tightly packed that they fuse into a continuous rough texture. The rasp is what happens when repetition becomes texture.

### The Mew Call

The Spotted Towhee's other signature vocalization is a **sharp, cat-like mew call** -- an inquisitive, descending "meewww?" that lasts about a half-second [1, 2, 3, 6, 7]. This call is:

- **Distinctive:** One of the most recognizable call notes in PNW birding. Often the first indication that a Spotted Towhee is present in the undergrowth.
- **Cat-like:** The call's descending, nasal quality has earned comparisons to a cat's meow in virtually every field guide [1, 2, 3, 6].
- **Contact call:** Given year-round, used to maintain contact with mates and to express alarm. Pairs exchange a softer, lisping version to stay in contact while foraging in dense cover [1, 2, 3].
- **Contrast with Song Sparrow:** The Song Sparrow's primary call is a sharp "chip" -- a brief, hard, percussive note. The Spotted Towhee's mew is longer, descending, and more nasal. Both are heard year-round; both are identification aids in dense vegetation where the bird is invisible.

### Song Repertoire

Individual male Spotted Towhees maintain repertoires of **4-9 song types** [1, 2, 3, 7]. This is smaller than the Song Sparrow's 8-12 types, reflecting the Spotted Towhee's simpler song structure. Each song type features a different combination of introductory notes and trill patterns.

Key repertoire features [1, 2, 3, 7]:
- **Repertoire size:** 4-9 song types per male
- **Song sharing:** High degree of song-sharing among males on adjacent territories in PNW *oregonus* populations; song structure changes relatively rapidly over short distances
- **Trill types:** Both fast and slow trill types are present in every population studied
- **Coastal simplification:** Pacific Coast birds (including PNW) tend toward trill-only songs, reducing the structural complexity relative to interior populations
- **Song function:** Males begin singing early in the season (typically February in the PNW) to establish territories and attract mates

### Vocal Learning

Like the Song Sparrow, the Spotted Towhee is a vocal learner -- songs are acquired through imitation of neighboring adults rather than inherited genetically [5, 8]. The vocal learning system in towhees has been studied in the context of the Spotted/Eastern Towhee split: song differences between the two species accumulated through cultural transmission and sexual selection, eventually contributing to reproductive isolation [5, 8]. This makes the Spotted Towhee's song an example of how learned vocalizations can drive speciation -- a concept the engine first encountered at degree 34 (Red Crossbill dialects) and now revisits with a species where song divergence led to a formal taxonomic split.

## Biology: The Ground-Scratching Specialist

### The Double-Footed Scratch

The Spotted Towhee's most distinctive behavioral trait is its **double-footed backward scratch** -- a foraging technique in which the bird makes a quick forward hop followed by an immediate backward hop, during which both feet kick backward simultaneously through the leaf litter, scraping it aside to expose seeds, insects, and other food items beneath [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]. The motion is pendular: feet swing forward and back in a single, coordinated action that displaces a spray of leaf litter behind the bird.

This scratching behavior is:
- **Loud.** The sound of a towhee scratching in dry leaves is often the first sign of its presence. In dense cover, the scratching noise is more audible than the bird's song or calls [1, 2, 3, 6].
- **Diagnostic.** While other Passerellidae scratch (Fox Sparrow, juncos), the Spotted Towhee is the most committed ground-scratcher in the PNW -- spending more time on the ground and producing more vigorous scratching than any sympatric species [1, 2, 3].
- **Acoustic.** The scratching sound is itself a component of the Spotted Towhee's acoustic signature. In the understory, you hear three sounds: the scratch, the mew, and the rasp. All three together say "towhee."

The double-scratch is the physical complement to the vocal rasp: both involve rapid, repetitive, friction-based action. The feet rasp the ground. The syrinx rasps the air. The bird's entire behavioral repertoire is organized around the principle of concentrated, textured, abrasive repetition.

### Body Size

The Spotted Towhee is a large New World sparrow -- substantially larger than the Song Sparrow and the largest Passerellidae in the engine [1, 2, 5, 6]:

| Measurement | Spotted Towhee | Song Sparrow (45) | Comparison |
|---|---|---|---|
| Length | 17-21 cm (6.7-8.3 in) | 12-17 cm (4.7-6.7 in) | Larger |
| Weight | 33-49 g (1.2-1.7 oz) | 12-53 g (0.4-1.9 oz)* | Heavier (typical) |
| Wingspan | ~28 cm (11 in) | ~21 cm (8.3 in) | Broader |

*Song Sparrow weight range includes extreme subspecies (Aleutian maxima at 53 g). PNW Song Sparrows (morphna) average 25-28 g. PNW Spotted Towhees average ~37-40 g -- substantially heavier.

The Spotted Towhee is roughly the size of a Gray Catbird -- a notably large sparrow. Its heavy body and strong legs are adapted for ground-scratching, which requires substantial muscle power to displace leaf litter effectively [1, 2, 3, 6].

### Plumage

The Spotted Towhee is one of the most visually striking Passerellidae [1, 2, 5, 6]:

**Male:**
- **Head and upperparts:** Jet black hood, back, wings, and tail
- **Flanks:** Rich rufous (rusty orange-brown)
- **Breast:** White center, bordered by the black hood above and rufous flanks on the sides
- **Wings and back:** Bold white spots -- the diagnostic feature that gives the bird its common and scientific names (*maculatus* = "spotted")
- **Tail:** White outer tail corners (variable, less extensive in Pacific populations)
- **Eye:** Bright red-orange (varies geographically; some populations have dark eyes)
- **Bill:** Dark, conical

**Female:** Similar pattern but with dark brown replacing the male's black. Rufous flanks, white spots, white breast center, brown hood.

**PNW subspecies (*oregonus*):** The darkest Spotted Towhee subspecies [1, 2, 5, 9]. Males are intensely black with reduced white spotting compared to interior populations. Less extensive white tail corners. Follows the PNW pattern of dark, dusky plumage in coastal/northwestern races (compare with the Song Sparrow's dark *morphna* subspecies, the Oregon Junco's dark western form). Gloger's rule in action: wet, shaded habitats select for darker plumage [5, 9].

### Diet

The Spotted Towhee is an **omnivore-generalist** with a seasonal dietary shift [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

**Breeding season (spring/summer):** Primarily insects and arthropods -- ground beetles, weevils, ladybugs, darkling beetles, click beetles, wood-boring beetles, crickets, grasshoppers, caterpillars, moths, bees, wasps, spiders, snails, millipedes. All obtained through the double-footed scratch in leaf litter [1, 2, 3, 6].

**Non-breeding season (fall/winter):** Primarily seeds and fruits -- acorns, grass seeds, weed seeds, berries (blackberry, elderberry, nightshade, poison oak berries). Also visits bird feeders for sunflower seeds and millet [1, 2, 3, 5, 6].

The dietary overlap with Song Sparrow is substantial -- both are omnivore-generalists that shift from insects in summer to seeds in winter. But the Spotted Towhee forages on the ground in dense understory, while the Song Sparrow forages at the wetland edge. The two species partition the landscape by habitat, not by diet [1, 2, 3].

### Breeding Ecology

- **Territory:** Males establish territories in dense shrubby habitat. Territory sizes are not well documented in the literature but males defend territories vocally, singing from elevated perches within the underbrush [1, 2, 3, 5].
- **Pair bond:** Socially monogamous within a breeding season [1, 2, 5].
- **Nest:** Open cup on the ground, sunk into the leaf litter so that the rim is even with the litter surface. Built of bark strips, grass, and leaves, lined with evergreen needles, hair, and fine grass. The female selects the site and builds the nest, typically under low branches or dense shrub cover [1, 2, 3, 5, 6].
- **Eggs:** 3-5, creamy white to grayish with brown spots concentrated at the larger end [1, 2, 5, 6].
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days, by the female [1, 2, 5].
- **Nestling period:** 9-11 days. Both parents feed nestlings. Young remain with parents for some time after fledging [1, 2, 5, 6].
- **Broods per season:** 1-2, rarely 3 [1, 2, 5].
- **Breeding season:** Males begin singing in February in the PNW. Breeding extends through July [1, 2, 3, 4].

### 21 Subspecies

The Spotted Towhee has **21 recognized subspecies** across western North America [1, 2, 5, 9]. A proposed revision by Peter Pyle (2023) suggests reducing this to 7 subspecies based on morphological analysis [9].

**Three subspecies occur in the Pacific Northwest** [4, 5, 9]:

1. ***P. m. oregonus*** -- the dominant western Washington breeding subspecies. The **darkest Spotted Towhee subspecies**. Males intensely black, white spotting reduced, white tail corners less extensive. Year-round resident in Puget Sound lowlands. In the oregonus Group under current BOW classification [4, 5, 9].

2. ***P. m. curtatus*** -- breeds in interior eastern Washington. In the maculatus Group. Lighter than *oregonus*, with more extensive white spotting. More migratory -- some eastern Washington birds move south in winter [4, 5, 9].

3. ***P. m. arcticus*** -- occurs in northeastern Washington and adjacent areas. The most boldly marked subspecies with the most extensive white spotting and largest white tail corners. Migratory [4, 5, 9].

**Geographic variation in the PNW** follows the coast-interior gradient: *oregonus* (dark, dusky, reduced spotting) grades into *curtatus* and *arcticus* (lighter, more boldly spotted) as you move east across the Cascades [5, 9]. This mirrors the Song Sparrow's geographic variation: dark *morphna* in western Washington, paler *merrilli* in eastern Washington.

### Five major subspecies groups [1, 2, 5]:

1. **Oregonus Group** (Pacific Coast) -- *oregonus*, *falcinellus*, *megalonyx*, others. Dark, reduced spotting. Coastal.
2. **Maculatus Group** (Interior) -- *curtatus*, *montanus*, others. Moderate spotting, intermediate color.
3. **Arcticus** (Great Plains) -- boldest white spotting, most extensive tail corners, most migratory.
4. **Gaigei Group** (Mexico) -- darker, reduced spotting, resident.
5. **Southern Mexican Group** -- *oaxacae*, *chiapensis*, and others. Southern, montane.

## PNW Habitat: Forest Shrub Edge and Dense Understory

### Habitat Description

The Spotted Towhee's primary habitat in the PNW is **forest shrub edge and dense understory** -- the tangled zone where forest trees meet shrubby undergrowth, where salal and Oregon grape form impenetrable thickets, where sword fern and blackberry create a wall of green below the canopy [1, 2, 3, 4, 6]. This is the habitat you walk past on a forest trail and hear something scratching loudly in the leaves but cannot see the source. That sound is almost always a Spotted Towhee.

Specific habitat types in the PNW include [1, 2, 3, 4, 6]:
- Forest edges with dense shrub understory (salal, Oregon grape, snowberry)
- Clear-cut regeneration areas with young shrubs and dense ground cover
- Riparian thickets along streams (willows, alders, with dense understory)
- Suburban gardens with dense plantings and accumulated leaf litter
- Blackberry tangles and hedgerows
- Madrone and oak woodlands with shrubby understory (especially in rain shadow areas)
- Mixed conifer-deciduous forest edges with well-developed shrub layer

### Vertical Position

The Spotted Towhee is primarily a **ground-to-low-shrub bird** -- vertical range 0-3 m [1, 2, 3, 6]:
- **Foraging:** On the ground, in leaf litter. This is the bird's primary activity. The double-scratch is performed at ground level, beneath the shrub canopy.
- **Singing:** From low to mid-level perches within shrubs or on low branches -- typically 1-4 m above ground. Males occasionally sing from higher, more exposed perches during peak territorial periods.
- **Nesting:** On the ground. Nests are sunk into leaf litter beneath shrub cover.

Compare with the Song Sparrow's vertical range (also 0-3 m) -- but in different habitat. The Song Sparrow works the open wetland edge. The Spotted Towhee works the closed understory. Same vertical range, different horizontal habitat. They rarely overlap.

### The Understory Niche

The Spotted Towhee occupies a specific acoustic environment within the forest-shrub edge [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Dense vegetation** absorbs and scatters sound, creating a "muffled" acoustic space. The rasp's broad-frequency, buzzy quality cuts through this absorption better than a pure tone would -- the roughness of the sound increases its audibility in cluttered environments.
- **Leaf litter** is both food source and sound source. The scratching sound carries through the understory, functioning as an unintentional acoustic signal of presence.
- **Low canopy** creates an acoustic "tunnel" effect where sound propagates horizontally along the ground plane. The towhee's low singing perch and ground-level foraging place all its sounds within this tunnel.
- **Dense cover** provides protection from aerial predators, making the understory a safe zone for a large, ground-dwelling bird.

### Historical Habitat Change in Washington

Spotted Towhees were formerly **rare in western Washington**, especially along the coast [4]. The extensive clear-cutting of old-growth and mature forests in the 19th and 20th centuries created abundant shrubby, second-growth habitat -- exactly the habitat Spotted Towhees prefer. The species' population in western Washington increased dramatically as clear-cutting provided additional shrub-edge habitat [4]. This is one of the few PNW species that **benefited from logging** -- a counterpoint to the Spotted Owl (degree 4) and Hermit Thrush (degree 7), which depend on old-growth or mature forest and have declined.

This historical context makes the Spotted Towhee a species of **ecological succession** -- it thrives in the early-to-mid stages of forest regrowth, the scrubby years when the forest floor is still sunlit and the shrub layer is dense. As second-growth forests mature and the canopy closes, Spotted Towhee habitat decreases. The bird's abundance in the modern PNW is partly an artifact of a century of forest disturbance [4].

### The Spotted Towhee's E=3

Why E=3 for the Spotted Towhee? The bird sings from within dense cover, projecting its buzzy trill through a muffled acoustic environment [1, 2, 3]. The rasp is louder and more penetrating than the Song Sparrow's moderate preamble (E=2) -- the buzzy quality cuts through vegetation that would absorb a cleaner tone. But the Spotted Towhee does not project like an American Robin (E=4) from a rooftop or a Western Tanager (E=4) from a treetop. It sings from inside the underbrush, not from above it. E=3 captures this: louder than conversational, softer than commanding. Penetrating but enclosed. The rasp fills the thicket without leaving it.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 5]
- **Global population:** Approximately 40 million (Partners in Flight estimates) [1, 2, 5]
- **Continental Concern Score:** 8/20 (low concern) [1, 2]
- **BBS trend:** Numbers remained relatively stable between 1966 and 2019 according to the North American Breeding Bird Survey [1, 2, 5]
- **Washington trend:** Increasing in western Washington due to habitat creation through clear-cutting; stable in eastern Washington [4]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 [1, 2]
- **Threats:** Habitat loss (suburban development of shrubby areas), nest predation by snakes and small mammals, Brown-headed Cowbird nest parasitism. None at population-threatening levels [1, 2, 5]

The Spotted Towhee's population of ~40 million makes it a common but less abundant bird than the Song Sparrow (~130 million). In Washington, the species is a **common year-round resident** in western lowlands and a common breeder in eastern Washington [4]. The PNW population is healthy and, if anything, has benefited from historical landscape changes that created shrubby habitat [4].
