## Purpose

This is the forty-seventh release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-46), the second Passerellidae, the first *Pipilo*, and the degree that restores E=3 after 25 degrees of absence. *Pipilo maculatus* -- "the spotted chirper," formerly "Rufous-sided Towhee" until the 1995 split from Eastern Towhee -- enters the engine as a ground-scratching specialist of the dense understory, completing the Passerellidae pair begun at degree 45. The release carries five structural contributions:

1. **The rasp -- the 47th acoustic element (species side).** The Spotted Towhee's song is a concentrated, buzzy, vibrating trill -- approximately 1.5 seconds of rapid frequency modulation so dense that individual notes fuse into a continuous rough texture. Named "the rasp" for three resonances: the sound's file-like quality (rough, vibrating, concentrated), the tool metaphor (a coarse file that shapes material through friction), and the foraging behavior (the double-footed backward scratch rasps the ground before the syrinx rasps the air). The first acoustic element defined by **textural concentration** -- an event that achieves its character through the sheer density of repetition within a narrow frequency band.

2. **Passerellidae pair complete -- degrees 45-46.** Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) forms a consecutive same-family pair following the pairing pattern (waxwings 31-32, goldfinches 43-44). The pair represents complementary acoustic strategies: melodic generalist (Song Sparrow's complex preamble + body) paired with textural specialist (Spotted Towhee's buzzy rasp). Different genera (*Melospiza* vs. *Pipilo*), different habitats (wetland edge vs. dense understory), same family.

3. **E=3 restored after 25 degrees of absence.** The last E=3 was degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler). E=2 was restored at degree 45. Now E=3 returns at degree 46. Two consecutive energy restorations break the seven-consecutive-E=4 streak (38-44). E=2 + E=3 together now hold 20 degrees (42.5%), nearly matching E=4's 21 degrees (44.7%).

4. **Dense understory habitat -- the engine reaches the forest floor.** After the Song Sparrow's wetland edge (degree 45), the Spotted Towhee occupies the adjacent habitat band -- the closed, tangled shrub layer where forest meets clearing. A species that benefited from logging, the Spotted Towhee is the engine's first ecological succession species.

5. **CSV substitution documented.** The original CSV had Columbia Spotted Frog at degree 46. Because the engine is birds-and-mammals only, Spotted Towhee replaces it, preserving the original's E=3 energy and forest/shrub edge habitat. Second consecutive CSV substitution (both replaced frogs). The word "spotted" threads between original and replacement.

### 3. The 1995 Split

The Spotted Towhee's taxonomic history adds a structural dimension: the demonstration that **learned vocalizations can drive speciation**. The Rufous-sided Towhee was split into Spotted Towhee (west) and Eastern Towhee (east) in 1995, based partly on vocal differences that accumulated through cultural transmission over ~1 million years. The engine first encountered vocal divergence at degree 34 (Red Crossbill call types, potential incipient species). The Spotted Towhee shows the completed outcome: a formal split, two species.

## What Worked

### 1. The rasp names a genuinely distinct acoustic phenomenon
No previous element captured the specific quality of a trill so dense it becomes texture. "The buzz" (degree 32) is thin and high-pitched social contact. "The thrum" (degree 35) is mechanical wing vibration. "The trill/tremolo" (degree 12) is the general category. The rasp is specifically what happens when vocal repetition crosses the threshold into continuous rough texture -- the mandolin tremolo, the square-wave oscillator at high frequency. The Spotted Towhee's buzzy trill is the perfect exemplar: individual notes are inaudible, only the texture remains.

### 2. The Passerellidae pair follows the established pairing pattern
Three consecutive same-family pairs (waxwings 31-32, goldfinches 43-44, sparrows 45-46) each explore a contrast within their family. The sparrow pair's contrast -- melodic vs. textural -- is the most acoustically fundamental of the three. The pairing pattern is now a reliable structural feature of the engine.

### 3. E=3 restoration continues the dynamic decompression
Two consecutive lower-energy entries (E=2 at 45, E=3 at 46) have rebalanced the energy distribution dramatically. E=2 + E=3 now hold 20 degrees, nearly matching E=4's 21 degrees. The lower registers are competitive again. The seven-consecutive-E=4 streak (38-44) is a fading memory.

### 4. The double-footed scratch enriches the species beyond acoustics
The Spotted Towhee's scratching behavior connects the bird's sound to its movement -- the physical rasp of feet through leaf litter mirrors the vocal rasp of the song. Three sounds identify the species: scratch, mew, rasp. This behavioral-acoustic integration gives the species a multi-sensory identity that extends beyond the purely vocal.

### 5. The 1995 split provides a completed vocal speciation case study
After the Red Crossbill's potential incipient species (degree 34), the Spotted Towhee provides the finished product: a vocal divergence that led to a formal taxonomic split. The engine now has both stages of vocal speciation represented -- in-progress (crossbill) and completed (towhee).

### 6. The habitat complements Song Sparrow without overlapping
Wetland edge (45) and dense understory (46) are adjacent habitat bands with minimal species overlap. The engine continues building its habitat map: deep forest -> canopy -> aerial -> lawn -> open canopy -> hummingbird -> conifer canopy -> weedy open -> wetland edge -> dense understory. Each degree adds a habitat the engine has not previously occupied.
