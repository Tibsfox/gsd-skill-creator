Spotted Towhee

Pipilo maculatus

CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Columbia Spotted Frog (Rana luteiventris) at degree 46. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Spotted Towhee replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s E=3 energy level and forest/shrub edge habitat. The word “spotted” threads between the original and the replacement: Columbia Spotted Frog replaced by Spotted Towhee.

The 47th Acoustic Element: The Rasp

The Spotted Towhee’s song is a concentrated, buzzy, vibrating trill — approximately 1.5 seconds of rapid frequency modulation so dense that individual notes fuse into a continuous rough texture. The classic mnemonic is “drink-your-tea!” where the “teeeea!” is the rasp. Named “the rasp” for the sound’s file-like quality, the tool metaphor (coarse abrasion shaping material through friction), and the foraging behavior (the double-footed backward scratch rasps the ground before the syrinx rasps the air).

The first acoustic element defined by textural concentration — an event that achieves its character not through melodic elaboration but through the sheer density of repetition within a narrow frequency band. Repetition becomes texture.

Passerellidae Pair Complete — Degrees 45–46

Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) form the engine’s Passerellidae pair, following the pairing pattern established by the waxwings (31–32) and goldfinches (43–44). The Song Sparrow is the melodic generalist — complex preamble-plus-body songs from wetland edge. The Spotted Towhee is the textural specialist — concentrated buzzy rasp from dense understory. Same family, complementary habitats, complementary acoustics.

E=3 Restored — After 25 Degrees of Absence

The last E=3 was degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler) — 25 degrees ago. E=2 was restored at degree 45 (Song Sparrow). Now E=3 returns at degree 46. Two consecutive energy restorations break the E=4 monopoly. E=2 + E=3 together now hold 20 degrees (42.5%), nearly matching E=4’s 21 degrees (44.7%). The dynamic range decompression continues.

The Double-Footed Backward Scratch

The Spotted Towhee’s most distinctive behavior: a rapid forward-backward hop in which both feet kick backward simultaneously through the leaf litter, displacing it to expose food beneath. The scratching is loud — often more audible than the bird’s song. In the dense understory, three sounds together identify the species: the scratch (foraging), the mew (cat-like call), and the rasp (buzzy song). The feet rasp the ground; the syrinx rasps the air.

The Spotted Chirper

Pipilo maculatus — from Latin pipilo (to chirp, to twitter) + maculatus (spotted, stained). “The spotted chirper” — a bird named for its voice and its plumage. Unlike the Song Sparrow, whose binomial is pure acoustics (“melody song-finch”), the Spotted Towhee’s name splits between sound and sight: one half says what the bird sounds like, the other says what the bird looks like.

The Sound: Rasp and Mew

Song: ~1.5 seconds. One to two short introductory notes followed by a fast, buzzy trill. Pacific Coast populations (including PNW oregonus) often drop the introductory notes entirely, delivering the trill alone. Frequency 1.5–8.0 kHz, most energy 2–6 kHz. Classic mnemonic: “drink-your-tea!”

Mew call: A sharp, descending, cat-like “meewww?” lasting about half a second. Year-round contact and alarm call. One of the most recognizable call notes in PNW birding.

Repertoire: Each male sings 4–9 distinct song types, including both fast and slow trill types. High degree of song-sharing among neighboring males in PNW oregonus populations.

Vocal learning: Songs learned through imitation of neighboring adults. Song differences between Spotted and Eastern Towhee accumulated through cultural transmission over ~1 million years, eventually leading to the 1995 species split.

The Song Sparrow opens a garden of elaboration. The Spotted Towhee’s rasp is the statement — concentrated, vibrating, complete in a single buzzy gesture.

The 1995 Split

Until 1995, the Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee were one species: the “Rufous-sided Towhee” (Pipilo erythrophthalmus). The AOU split them based on differences in plumage, song, call notes, and mitochondrial DNA. The two forms had diverged approximately one million years ago as populations expanded west and east from Mexico. A narrow hybrid zone persists in the Great Plains (Nebraska, Dakotas). The split demonstrates that learned vocalizations can drive speciation — song divergence creates reproductive isolation.

21 Subspecies

The Spotted Towhee has 21 recognized subspecies across western North America (Peter Pyle’s 2023 revision proposes reducing to 7). Three subspecies occur in Washington: oregonus (western WA, the darkest form), curtatus (eastern WA, intermediate), and arcticus (NE WA, most boldly spotted). The coast-interior gradient mirrors Song Sparrow’s morphna-to-merrilli variation — Gloger’s rule in both species.

Dense Understory Habitat intermediate

The engine’s first dense-understory species. The closed, tangled shrub layer where forest meets clearing. After wetland edge (45), the engine moves to the forest floor. A species that benefited from logging — counterpoint to Spotted Owl (4).

Vocal Speciation intermediate

Split from Eastern Towhee in 1995 based partly on vocal differences. Song divergence drove reproductive isolation over ~1 million years. The engine’s second case study in vocal speciation after Red Crossbill (34) — but here, the split is complete.

LOGGING COUNTERPOINT. Spotted Owl declined from old-growth loss. Spotted Towhee INCREASED — clear-cutting created shrubby habitat. Two PNW species, opposite responses to the same disturbance.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Spotted Towhee documented as common year-round resident in western WA. Three subspecies (oregonus, curtatus, arcticus). Dark oregonus form dominant in Puget Sound lowlands. Population increased following historical clear-cutting.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Dense understory ecology. Ecological succession species. Shrub-edge habitat created by disturbance. Double-footed scratch adaptation. Geographic variation across 21 subspecies following Gloger’s rule.
