The Spotted Towhee is the second Passerellidae — *Pipilo maculatus*, "the spotted chirper" — a ground-scratching specialist that forages by kicking backward through leaf litter with both feet simultaneously, a distinctive double-scratch that produces its own percussive acoustic signature alongside the buzzy "drink-your-tea" song. The bird is heard twice: once from the voice, once from the ground. At 37-40 grams it is the largest New World sparrow in the engine, heavy-bodied and low-slung, spending most of its life in the dense understory where forest meets shrub. The Spotted Towhee was lumped with the Eastern Towhee as the "Rufous-sided Towhee" until 1995, when vocal and genetic evidence proved the two forms were reproductively isolated. Song divergence drove speciation.

Gemini 9A's target vehicle arrived in orbit with its launch shroud stuck half-open — the two clamshell halves agape at 25 degrees instead of fully retracted, looking, in Tom Stafford's words, like an "angry alligator." The crew could not dock. The shroud would not release. Stafford and Gene Cernan spent hours attempting workarounds, approaching the Augmented Target Docking Adapter from multiple angles, but the mechanical obstacle was immovable. The Spotted Towhee's leaf-scratch foraging is the same kind of persistent, repetitive attack on an obstacle: both feet kicking backward through matted leaves, over and over, excavating the ground one scratch at a time. The towhee scratches at the surface layer that conceals food. Stafford nudged at the shroud that concealed the docking port. Neither could get through on the first attempt. The towhee eventually finds the seed. Stafford never docked.

Cernan's EVA deepens the connection. Gene Cernan's two-hour spacewalk outside Gemini 9A nearly killed him — his visor fogged, his suit overheated, his heart rate spiked to 195 bpm, and he could barely see or move. The EVA suit was supposed to provide freedom of motion outside the spacecraft. Instead, it became a prison of thermal stress and restricted visibility. The Spotted Towhee's dense understory habitat operates on the same principle: the bird is surrounded by vegetation so thick that sight lines collapse to centimeters. The towhee navigates by sound and touch — the scratch of feet on leaves, the rustle of disturbed litter — because the visual field is obstructed. Cernan navigated by feel because his visor was obscured. Both operated blind in cluttered environments, relying on non-visual feedback to accomplish their work. The angry alligator and the dense thicket: two obstacles that turn a routine task into a survival problem.
