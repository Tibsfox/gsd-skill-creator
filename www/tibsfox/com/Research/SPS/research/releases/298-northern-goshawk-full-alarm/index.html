Acoustic Profile — Full Alarm

Ka-ka-ka call: Rapid staccato bursts of ka notes. Harsher and more insistent than any other PNW raptor alarm. Fundamental frequency 800-2,000 Hz. Penetrates dense conifer forest. Audible 200-400 meters through closed canopy.

Duration: 5-15 minutes of continuous alarm during active nest defense. Most raptors alarm in bursts with pauses. The Goshawk does not pause. Sustained intensity is the diagnostic character.

Physical escalation: Alarm → diving attack on intruder's head from behind → talon contact with scalp or shoulders → multiple birds attacking simultaneously (mated pair). Wearing a helmet and eye protection is standard protocol for raptor biologists working near Goshawk nests.

E=7: Maximum commitment, physical risk to intruder, sustained without modulation. The most behaviorally intense nest defense in the Pacific Northwest. Equal to Bald Eagle combat (297) by a different mechanism: sustained alarm + physical attack vs. aerial tumble.

Accipiter gentilis — The Noble Hawk

Named by Linnaeus in 1758. Accipiter: Latin, "the taker" (from accipere, to grasp). Gentilis: noble. In European falconry tradition, the Goshawk was the nobleman's hawk — large enough to take rabbit and pheasant, maneuverable enough to hunt through dense cover. Reserved for nobility in medieval falconry hierarchies. The "noble hawk" earns its name not through deference but through commitment: it attacks without hesitation, sustains without pause, and will not retreat.

Adult identification: blue-gray back, finely barred underparts, bold white supercilium, deeply red eye (eye color deepens with age to vivid red in mature adults). The red eye is diagnostic. The deep red of a fully adult Goshawk's eye is one of the most striking field marks in North American birds.

Old-Growth Dependency

The Northern Goshawk requires large territories of structurally complex forest: tall conifers for nesting, dense understory for prey ambush, large trees with wide crowns for nest platforms. These conditions are met primarily in old-growth and mature second-growth. The Goshawk is an old-growth indicator species — its presence indicates habitat quality. It shares this old-growth dependency with the Northern Spotted Owl (SPS 4) and the Pileated Woodpecker (SPS 54, 108, 300). Three old-growth specialists across three families: Strigidae, Accipitridae, Picidae.
