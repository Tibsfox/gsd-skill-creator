## ACCIPITER GENTILIS -- THE SPECIES

### The Northern Goshawk: Biology and Pacific Northwest Context

*Accipiter gentilis* (Linnaeus, 1758) is the largest of the accipiters and one of the forest's most formidable predators:

- **Identification.** Adults: blue-gray back and wings, finely barred gray and white underparts, prominent white supercilium (eyebrow stripe), red eyes (eye color deepens with age), yellow-orange cere. Long tail for maneuvering in dense forest. Wingspan 35-50 inches (89-127 cm) -- substantially larger than the Sharp-shinned Hawk and Cooper's Hawk (the other two PNW accipiters) but more maneuverable than the buteos. The red eye in adult plumage is the key field mark.
- **Scientific name.** *Accipiter* (Latin: from *accipere* = to grasp or take -- "the taker"); *gentilis* (Latin: noble, of good family -- "noble hawk"). Named by Linnaeus in 1758. The "noble hawk" is historically the raptor of choice in European falconry -- reserved for nobility, hence *gentilis*. In European tradition, the Goshawk was the "cook's hawk" because it could hunt rabbits and pheasants to supply the kitchen.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** The Northern Goshawk breeds across the Pacific Northwest in mature and old-growth conifer forest. In Washington State, the species breeds primarily in the Cascades and Olympic Mountains, with some populations in the Blue Mountains and Okanogan Highlands. It prefers dense forest with tall conifers and abundant understory -- habitat that provides both nesting substrate and prey cover. Some altitudinal migration in winter: birds breeding at high elevation move to lower elevations when snow covers prey.
- **Hunting style.** The Goshawk is a forest-specialist pursuit predator. It hunts by flying through dense vegetation at high speed, changing direction rapidly, pursuing prey (primarily birds and squirrels) through the forest interior. The long tail is a flight control surface for maneuvering; the short, rounded wings are optimized for thrust rather than soaring. The Goshawk does not soar or circle like a Red-tailed Hawk; it follows prey through the forest, adjusting course with every wingbeat.
- **Prey.** In the Pacific Northwest: Blue Grouse, Ruffed Grouse, American Robin, Steller's Jay (large birds); Douglas squirrel, northern flying squirrel, snowshoe hare (mammals). The Goshawk's prey size range is wider than any other Pacific Northwest accipiter -- it can take prey from small songbird to adult Ring-necked Pheasant.

(Sources: Accipiter gentilis identification; wingspan 35-50 inches; red eye field mark; Accipiter etymology = to grasp; gentilis = noble; falconry history -- Goshawk as nobleman's hawk; Pacific Northwest breeding distribution: Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains; hunting style: forest pursuit predator, long tail for maneuvering; Pacific Northwest prey species)

## THE FULL ALARM CALL -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Ka-Ka-Ka: The Most Intense Raptor Alarm in the Forest

The Northern Goshawk's full alarm call is documented as the most aggressive nest-defense vocalization in North American raptor biology:

- **The ka-ka-ka call.** The primary alarm call is a rapid, staccato series of ka notes: ka-ka-ka-ka-ka, delivered in bursts that can last seconds to minutes without pause. The call is harsh, high-pitched (for a raptor of this size), and penetrating. The individual ka notes are delivered rapidly -- faster than the Bald Eagle's contact call, more staccato than the Red-tailed Hawk's kree screech. The rapid-fire delivery is the diagnostic feature.
- **Frequency and carrying power.** The ka-ka-ka call has a fundamental frequency of approximately 800-2,000 Hz, with harmonics extending higher. The call carries effectively through dense forest -- the frequency range is appropriate for forest propagation. A Goshawk alarming at its nest can be heard from 200-400 meters in forest, and farther in more open habitats.
- **Duration of alarm.** During active nest defense, the Northern Goshawk maintains the alarm call continuously for as long as the intruder is present. Extended sequences of 5-15 minutes of continuous alarm calling have been documented. This sustained intensity is unusual in raptors -- most birds alarm, then wait, then alarm again. The Goshawk alarms without pause.
- **Escalation to physical attack.** The alarm call is only the first component of Goshawk nest defense. As an intruder approaches more closely, the bird escalates from alarm calling to physical attack: diving at the intruder's head with talons extended, making contact or narrowly missing. Multiple documented cases of Goshawks striking humans hard enough to cause bleeding. The alarm call and the physical attack are a continuous escalation, not separate behaviors.
- **E=7 calibration.** The Northern Goshawk full alarm is E=7: the sustained ka-ka-ka at maximum rate, combined with the threat and reality of physical attack. This is behaviorally more intense than the Bald Eagle contact call (E=5) and equivalent in intensity to the Bald Eagle aerial combat (E=7). Both are maximum territorial commitment -- but the Goshawk's commitment is specifically directed at nest defense rather than territorial establishment.

(Sources: Ka-ka-ka alarm call description; frequency range 800-2000 Hz; forest propagation characteristics; sustained alarm duration: 5-15 minutes documented; escalation from calling to physical attack; documented human injuries from Goshawk nest defense; E=7 calibration vs. other raptor entries)

## NEST DEFENSE -- THE MOST AGGRESSIVE IN NORTH AMERICAN RAPTORS

### Why the Goshawk Hits You in the Head

The Northern Goshawk's willingness to physically attack humans is documented extensively in ornithological literature:

- **The nest defense drive.** The Goshawk's nest defense is driven by a strong parental investment response. The species typically produces one brood per year of 2-4 eggs, with an incubation period of approximately 36-38 days and a fledging period of approximately 35-42 days. The investment in each nesting attempt is substantial; the response to perceived threats is correspondingly intense.
- **Human attacks documented.** Multiple ornithologists have documented head wounds from Goshawk attacks. The standard protocol for working near active Goshawk nests now includes head protection (helmets) and eye protection. The birds' commitment to physical contact is unusual among raptors -- most raptors escalate to aerial display without making contact. The Goshawk makes contact regularly.
- **The attack trajectory.** The Goshawk typically attacks from behind and above, diving at the head in a flight path that makes the approach invisible to the intruder. The talons strike the back of the head or the shoulders. Multiple birds may attack simultaneously if a mated pair is defending the nest. The attack is swift, committed, and repeated if the intruder does not retreat.
- **Distance of response.** Goshawks begin alarming when an intruder enters the nest territory -- which may be 100 meters or more from the nest. Physical attack escalation begins when the intruder continues to approach despite the alarm. The spatial structure of the defense (alarm at distance, attack on closer approach) is functional: it warns off many intruders before escalation to physical contact.
- **Comparison with other raptors.** The Bald Eagle will use aerial display (locked-talon combat) for territorial disputes but does not typically attack humans at nests. The Red-tailed Hawk may alarm and dive but makes physical contact with humans infrequently. The Northern Goshawk is uniquely committed to physical contact in nest defense -- earning its reputation as the most aggressive raptor in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Goshawk nest defense literature; 2-4 eggs per clutch; incubation 36-38 days; fledging 35-42 days; human attack documentation; head wounds from Goshawk attacks; helmet requirement for nest surveys; attack from behind/above trajectory; multiple simultaneous attacks by mated pair; spatial defense structure; comparison with Bald Eagle and Red-tailed Hawk)

## ACCIPITRIDAE: THE THIRD ENTRY -- ACCIPITER vs. HALIAEETUS

### Two Genera Within One Family

The Accipitridae family encompasses diverse genera with very different body forms and ecologies:

- **Haliaeetus leucocephalus (221, 297): the sea eagle.** Large body, long wings for soaring and powered flight over water, fish-specialist feeding strategy, open habitats. The Bald Eagle is the family's most visible Pacific Northwest representative.
- **Accipiter gentilis (298): the true hawk.** Medium-large body, short rounded wings and long tail for forest maneuvering, bird-and-mammal pursuit specialist, closed forest habitat. The Goshawk is the family's forest specialist.
- **Why the genus contrast matters.** The Accipitridae family documentation in the engine now includes two very different birds: a soaring fish eagle and a forest-specialist pursuit hawk. The same family, two completely different ecological and morphological strategies. This is the engine's documentation of family-level diversity within Accipitridae.
- **Accipiter as genus.** The Accipiter genus includes three Pacific Northwest species: Northern Goshawk (largest), Cooper's Hawk (medium), Sharp-shinned Hawk (smallest). All three are forest pursuit predators with the same basic body plan: short wings, long tail, accipiter flight style. The Goshawk is documented at 298 as the family's most aggressive member; the Cooper's and Sharp-shinned will appear in later degrees.
- **Family arc potential.** Three Accipitridae appearances (221, 297, 298) have now documented three different behaviors across two genera. The family's representation in the engine is becoming comprehensive.

(Sources: Accipitridae genus comparison: Haliaeetus vs. Accipiter; morphological difference: sea eagle body vs. forest hawk body; ecological difference: fish specialist vs. bird-mammal pursuit; PNW Accipiter species: Goshawk, Cooper's, Sharp-shinned; Accipitridae arc at three appearances)

## OLD-GROWTH AND MATURE FOREST CONTEXT

### The Goshawk as Old-Growth Indicator Species

The Northern Goshawk's habitat requirements connect it to one of the Pacific Northwest's most ecologically significant environments:

- **Old-growth dependency.** The Northern Goshawk is an old-growth and mature forest indicator species in the Pacific Northwest. The species requires large territories (typically 1,500-3,000 acres or more) with structurally complex forest: tall conifers for nesting, dense understory for prey cover, large trees with wide crowns for nest platforms. These conditions are met primarily in old-growth and mature second-growth forest.
- **Old-growth threats.** Pacific Northwest old-growth has been substantially reduced by twentieth-century timber harvest. The Goshawk's population status in Washington State and Oregon is a function of old-growth and mature forest availability. Federal land designations (National Parks, Wilderness Areas, Late-Successional Reserves under the Northwest Forest Plan) provide the most secure habitat.
- **Connection to other old-growth species in the engine.** The Northern Spotted Owl (degree 4, SPS) is the canonical old-growth indicator species in the Pacific Northwest -- the species whose habitat requirements drove the old-growth timber controversy of the late 1980s and 1990s. The Pileated Woodpecker (degrees 54, 108, 300) is the old-growth acoustic icon. The Northern Goshawk is the old-growth pursuit predator. The three together constitute the engine's old-growth predator and acoustic documentation.
- **The Northwest Forest Plan.** The 1994 Northwest Forest Plan established Late-Successional Reserves -- designated areas managed to maintain and develop old-growth forest structure -- across federal lands in Oregon and Washington. The plan was specifically driven by Northern Spotted Owl habitat requirements but benefits multiple old-growth-dependent species including the Goshawk.

(Sources: Northern Goshawk old-growth dependency; territory size 1,500-3,000+ acres; structural requirements: tall conifers, dense understory, large nest trees; old-growth reduction from timber harvest; Late-Successional Reserves under Northwest Forest Plan 1994; connection to Northern Spotted Owl (SPS 4) and Pileated Woodpecker (SPS 54, 108, 300))
