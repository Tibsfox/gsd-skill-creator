Downward Spiraling Veer-Veer · Lowland Riparian · Summer Migrant · Energy 3
As the ascending staircase reaches its final step and the last phrase of the Swainson’s Thrush hangs in the evening air, a different voice answers from a willow thicket at the range’s western edge. The Veery. Warm cinnamon-brown, faint spots, the most ethereal voice. It begins high and spirals downward — veer, veer, veer — each phrase lower, each step descending, two syrinx voices shimmering against each other like a song heard underwater. The third Catharus. The triptych complete. The answer to the question.

Taxonomy — The Third Catharus

The Veery is a medium-small passerine of the family Turdidae (thrushes). The genus Catharus — from Greek katharos, “pure” — now has three representatives in the circle. At degree 7°, the Hermit Thrush introduced the genus. At degree 18°, the Swainson’s Thrush returned to it. Now at degree 19°, the Veery becomes the third Catharus, completing the Catharus triptych. The species epithet fuscescens means “becoming dusky” (Latin). The common name is onomatopoeic — the bird named itself: veer-veer-veer.

The Veery is the least common of the three Catharus in the PNW — at the western edge of its breeding range. It is the warmest-toned: uniform warm cinnamon-brown upperparts, minimal spotting (faint, blurred breast spots that fade quickly), no bold eyering, no rufous tail contrast. The gentlest, plainest, most ethereal of the three.

Bioacoustics — The Descending Spiral

The Song That Falls

The Veery’s song is the mirror image of the Swainson’s Thrush. Where the Swainson’s spirals upward, the Veery spirals downward: a series of ethereal, reedy phrases, each phrase lower than the last, creating a descending helical staircase through pitch space. The quality is “dreamy,” “underwater,” “otherworldly” — the most ethereal voice in North American birdsong. The rapid modulation from its bilateral syrinx creates a shimmering texture that makes the song seem to come from everywhere and nowhere.

Bilateral Syrinx: The Chorus Effect in Biological Form

The Veery’s ethereal shimmer comes from bilateral syrinx polyphony — two independent fundamental frequencies from the left and right syrinx halves, closely spaced, creating beat frequencies that produce the sensation of shimmering. This is the chorus effect in biological form — identical to the guitar chorus pedal (Roland CE-1, 1976).

SpeciesDegreeBilateral Syrinx UseMusical Analogy
Pacific Wren1°Two independent melodic lines — complexityTwo-part counterpoint (Bach fugue)

Same organ, different purpose. The Wren uses two voices for information density. The Veery uses two voices for atmosphere. One is a virtuoso playing two lines. The other is a single voice wrapped in reverb.

The most fundamental pairing in tonal music: tension and resolution. The Swainson’s asks the question. The Veery answers it. Degrees 18–19 enact the oldest story in music: you go out, and then you come home.

The Descending Sequence in Music

In music, descending motion implies resolution, relaxation, return. The descending bass line (lamento bass, the baroque passacaglia), Debussy’s cascading whole-tone passages (the same dreamy, underwater quality), the blues turnaround (V chord descending back to I — coming home). The Veery’s descending spiral carries this weight: each phrase lower, each step settling, the melody unwinding toward resolution. The physics of descending pitch produce the perception of arrival in any pitch-sensitive listener.

Lowland Riparian — The Thicket at the Edge of Range

Habitat at the Western Margin

The Veery breeds in lowland riparian thickets — dense, deciduous undergrowth along streams and rivers. In the PNW, this means willow-alder thickets, cottonwood bottomlands, and dense deciduous understory. But the critical fact: the Veery is uncommon here. This is the western edge of its breeding range. Unlike the abundant Swainson’s Thrush, the Veery is a sparse, local breeder — present in some years, absent in others.

Music Theory Mapping

The descending spiral as resolution. Descending pitch in vocal communication implies closure, completion, finality. A falling inflection is a statement (not a question). A falling melody is resolved tension. The Veery’s descending song carries this emotional content: each phrase lower, the trajectory downward, the resolution in the final phrase at the bottom of the staircase. Where the Swainson’s leaves the listener at the top (unresolved, reaching), the Veery brings the listener to the bottom (resolved, arrived, home).

Bilateral syrinx as chorus effect. Two slightly detuned voices create beat frequencies — periodic amplitude fluctuations perceived as shimmer. This is identical to the guitar chorus pedal: duplicate the signal, detune the copy, mix them. The Veery has been doing this for millions of years. The beat frequency (shimmer rate) depends on the frequency difference between the two syrinx halves — the bird modulates its own chorus effect dynamically within phrases.

The triptych as complete pitch grammar. Three Catharus thrushes map the three fundamental pitch directions: static (7°), ascending (18°), descending (19°). Every melody in every tradition can be decomposed into these three motions: staying, rising, falling. The genus named “pure” maps the complete vocabulary of pitch direction.

The Catharus Triptych Advanced

Three Catharus, three pitch directions: static/harmonic (7°), ascending/tension (18°), descending/resolution (19°). These exhaust the possibilities. The genus named “pure” maps the complete pitch space of music. The most complete within-genus acoustic comparison in the engine.

R

Tension-Resolution Pair Foundational

Degrees 18–19 form a question-and-answer pair: ascending spiral (tension, aspiration) followed by descending spiral (resolution, homecoming). The half cadence and the authentic cadence. The most fundamental structure in tonal music, mapped by two species from the same genus at consecutive positions.

B

Beat Frequency Physics Intermediate

When two tones of slightly different frequency are sounded together, they produce periodic amplitude fluctuation at the difference frequency. 2 Hz difference = 2 beats/second (slow pulsation). 10 Hz = rapid shimmer. The Veery modulates this dynamically — controlling shimmer rate as a musical parameter in real time.

T

Turdidae Acoustic Diversity Advanced

Four thrushes, four strategies: AM drone (Varied, 2°), harmonic series (Hermit, 7°), ascending sequence (Swainson’s, 18°), descending sequence with chorus shimmer (Veery, 19°). The Turdidae extend their lead as the most acoustically diverse family in the circle.
