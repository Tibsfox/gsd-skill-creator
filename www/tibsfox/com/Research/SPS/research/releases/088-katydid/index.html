Back-to-Back Orthoptera — Second Consecutive Insect

The katydid is the second consecutive insect (degrees 87–88) and the second consecutive Orthoptera. Back-to-back same-order entries parallel prior engine patterns: Anatidae (58–59, Wood Duck & Mallard), Mustelidae, Sciuridae. When the engine encounters an order, it tends to linger. Cricket (87) and katydid (88) explore two sides of Orthoptera stridulation: rhythmic chirp vs continuous buzz. Same mechanism (wing stridulation), different acoustic result.

Continuous Electric Buzz — 89th Acoustic Element

The katydid sound is a continuous electric buzz — a droning, high-pitched, sustained sound produced by wing stridulation (rubbing wings together, like crickets). But unlike the cricket’s rhythmic chirps, the katydid produces a continuous drone — an unbroken buzz that sounds almost electrical. “Katy-DID, katy-DIDN’T” gives the animal its common name, but many species produce a sustained buzz rather than the stereotyped phrase. E=4 — the buzz fills the shrub canopy and carries across the meadow.

Tettigoniidae — 39th Family — Same Order, New Family

Tettigoniidae (katydids / bush-crickets) is a NEW FAMILY — the 39th in the engine. Same order as cricket (Orthoptera, degree 87) but a different family. Gryllidae (87) and Tettigoniidae (88) — true crickets and bush-crickets, two branches of the Orthoptera stridulation tree. Back-to-back new families in the same order.

The Kimya Dawson Parallel

Degree 88 pairs Katydid with Kimya Dawson (Solo) in Part A — Lo-Fi Folk, E=3. Both are continuous and stripped-down. The katydid’s continuous buzz is an unbroken, sustained sound. Dawson’s bedroom strum is a continuous, unpolished, stripped-down performance. Both are minimal in structure but persistent in presence. The katydid buzzes all night; Dawson strums through entire songs without embellishment. Both occupy their space completely through persistence, not force.

The Sound: Continuous Electric Buzz (89th Acoustic Element)

The call: Katydids produce sound through stridulation — rubbing a file on one forewing against a scraper on the other, the same mechanism as crickets [1, 2]. But katydid stridulation produces a continuous, sustained buzz rather than discrete chirps. The sound is high-pitched (typically 5,000–20,000 Hz, often near or above the upper limit of human hearing for some species), sustained, and droning. It sounds electric — more like a power line or an electronic oscillator than a biological sound [1, 2, 3].

Why E=4: (1) Individual katydid calls are loud — audible at 15–30 meters. (2) The continuous nature fills the soundscape without gaps. (3) Multiple katydids produce an overlapping sustained drone. (4) Carries through shrub canopy. (5) E=4 reflects the persistent, space-filling quality — the buzz dominates the upper frequency range of the habitat soundscape on summer nights.

Taxonomy: Tettigoniidae (Family-Level Entry)

Note: Like the cricket chorus (degree 87), this is a family-level entry. Multiple katydid species contribute to PNW shrub soundscapes. The common fork-tailed bush katydid (Scudderia furcata) and the broad-winged katydid (Microcentrum rhombifolium) are among the species found in the Pacific Northwest [1, 2].

Classification: Class Insecta (second insect). Order Orthoptera (same as cricket, degree 87). Family Tettigoniidae — NEW FAMILY (39th). Also known as bush-crickets or long-horned grasshoppers [1, 2].

Ecology: The Shrub Canopy Drone

Habitat: Shrub and meadow. Katydids are primarily arboreal or shrub-dwelling, unlike the ground-dwelling crickets (87). They call from elevated positions in bushes, shrubs, and trees. Shrub/meadow habitat connects to Snowshoe Hare (73) at meadow edges [1, 2, 3].

Activity: Primarily nocturnal. Calling peaks on warm summer nights. Males stridulate to attract females. The continuous buzz carries through the shrub canopy.

Diet: Primarily herbivorous. Leaves, flowers, seeds. Some species are omnivorous or predatory.

Predators: Bats (primary predator — echolocation detects katydid calls), birds, spiders, parasitic wasps. Some katydids have evolved to shift call frequency above bat echolocation range.
