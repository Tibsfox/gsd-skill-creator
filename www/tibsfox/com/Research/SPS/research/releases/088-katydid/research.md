## THE SOUND: CONTINUOUS ELECTRIC BUZZ -- 89th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Buzz

The katydid sound is produced by **stridulation** -- the same mechanism as crickets, rubbing a file on one forewing against a scraper on the other [1, 2, 3]:

**Individual call:** A sustained, high-pitched, continuous buzz. Unlike the cricket's discrete rhythmic chirps, the katydid produces an **unbroken drone** that sounds almost electrical. The sound is sustained without gaps -- a continuous oscillation of wing membranes. Some species produce the stereotypical "katy-DID, katy-DIDN'T" phrase, but many PNW species produce a **sustained buzz** rather than the onomatopoeic phrase.

**Frequency:** Approximately 5,000-20,000 Hz for most species, often near or above the upper limit of human hearing for some species. Higher frequency than crickets (3,000-5,000 Hz). The high-pitched buzz sounds electric -- more like a power line or electronic oscillator than a biological sound.

**The collective drone:** When multiple katydids call from adjacent shrubs, the individual buzzes overlap into a **sustained, continuous drone** filling the shrub canopy. Unlike the cricket chorus (which retains rhythmic quality), the katydid drone is truly continuous -- no gaps, no pulses, just a sustained high-frequency buzz.

### Chirp vs Buzz: Two Expressions of Stridulation

Cricket (87) and katydid (88) both use wing stridulation but produce fundamentally different sounds [1, 2]:

| Feature | Cricket (87) | Katydid (88) |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Mechanism | Wing stridulation | Wing stridulation |
| Result | Rhythmic chirps | Continuous buzz |
| Frequency | 3,000-5,000 Hz | 5,000-20,000 Hz |
| Pattern | Discrete pulses, gaps between | Sustained, no gaps |
| Texture | Pulsing, rhythmic | Droning, continuous |
| Position | Ground level | Shrub canopy |

Same mechanism. Different result. The engine explores two expressions of one mechanism in consecutive degrees.

## ECOLOGY: THE SHRUB CANOPY DRONE

### Habitat

Shrub and meadow [1, 2, 3]:

- **Primary:** Shrubs, hedgerows, meadow edges, low trees.
- **Also:** Gardens, forest margins, tall grass and herbaceous vegetation.
- **Contrast with cricket (87):** Cricket = ground-dwelling, meadow floor. Katydid = arboreal, shrub canopy. Same order, different vertical niche. Ground and canopy. The engine moves from the ground to the shrub layer in one degree.
- **Shared with Snowshoe Hare (73):** Both inhabit meadow edges and shrub habitats.

### Behavior

- **Calling:** Males stridulate from elevated positions in shrubs and trees. The elevated position allows the high-frequency buzz to carry through the canopy.
- **Timing:** Nocturnal. Peak calling on warm summer nights.
- **Camouflage:** Many katydids are remarkably leaf-like in appearance -- green, flat, with leaf-vein patterns on wings. Heard far more than seen.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Primarily herbivorous. Leaves, flowers, seeds. Some species are omnivorous or predatory.
- **Predators:** Bats (primary nocturnal predator -- echolocation detects katydid calls), birds, spiders, parasitic wasps. Some katydids have evolved to shift call frequency above bat echolocation range -- a frequency arms race.
