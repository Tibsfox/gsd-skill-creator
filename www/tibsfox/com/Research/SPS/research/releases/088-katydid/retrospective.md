## Structural Event 2: Continuous Electric Buzz

- **Situation:** The katydid produces a continuous, sustained, high-pitched buzz through wing stridulation -- the same mechanism as cricket but with a fundamentally different acoustic result.
- **Root Cause:** Tettigoniidae stridulation produces sustained oscillation rather than discrete pulses. The wing structure and stroke pattern create continuous rather than rhythmic sound.
- **Recommendation:** Document the continuous buzz as the 89th acoustic element. Emphasize the contrast with cricket's rhythmic chirp (87) -- same mechanism, different result. Note the electric quality.

## What Worked

### 1. The Chirp vs Buzz Comparison Is Acoustically Precise
The distinction between cricket's rhythmic chirps and katydid's continuous buzz is audible, verifiable, and well-documented. Same mechanism (wing stridulation), different result (discrete pulses vs sustained drone). The comparison illuminates both entries.

### 2. Back-to-Back Orthoptera Parallels Anatidae (58-59)
The engine's pattern of placing same-order entries in consecutive degrees is now established with multiple examples. Anatidae at 58-59 (Wood Duck and Mallard), now Orthoptera at 87-88 (cricket and katydid). The pattern was not forced -- it emerged from the data.

### 3. The Electric Quality Is a Genuine Observation
The katydid buzz genuinely sounds electric -- more like a power line or oscillator than a biological sound. This observation is widely noted in field guides and is audibly verifiable. The "continuous electric buzz" designation is accurate.

### 4. Shrub vs Ground Vertical Niche Separation Is Clean
The cricket calls from the ground; the katydid calls from the shrub canopy. Same order, different vertical niche. This ecological distinction is well-documented and parallels the acoustic distinction (rhythmic ground chorus vs sustained canopy drone).

### 5. Family-Level Entry Is Consistent with Cricket (87)
Both Orthoptera entries are family-level (Gryllidae, Tettigoniidae). Both represent collective/family-level phenomena rather than single species. The approach is consistent and appropriate.
