## FIRST FISH -- THE MOST SIGNIFICANT TAXONOMIC MILESTONE SINCE THE RAPTOR ERA

### A New Vertebrate Class Enters the Engine

The Pacific Salmon at degree 236 represents the engine's most significant taxonomic expansion since the raptor era began at degree 221:

- **What fish means.** The SPS engine has covered mammals (Felidae through Salmonidae -- wait, mammals through Procyonidae, then birds through Cathartidae), and before that other mammals and birds. The engine has documented vocalizations, behavioral sounds, and the rich acoustic world of Pacific Northwest birds and mammals. The fish is different: it is a vertebrate that produces no vocalization in the biological sense. Fish have no larynx, no syrinx, no vocal cord -- no sound-producing organ designed for communication. The "sound" of spawning salmon is the physical consequence of large fish in shallow water, not a communicative vocalization.
- **Why fish belongs in SPS.** The "Sound of Puget Sound" is not limited to vocalizations. It is the full acoustic environment of the Pacific Northwest -- the sounds that constitute the place. A spawning salmon run on the Hoh River or the Cedar River is one of the most distinctive soundscapes in the PNW: the continuous sound of splashing, tail-slapping, fish moving through gravel. This soundscape is as much a part of the PNW as the Bald Eagle's kee-kee-kee or the Orca's whistle. It belongs in the engine.
- **First non-bird, non-mammal vertebrate.** The SPS engine has covered approximately 36 mammal entries (Felidae through Procyonidae, marine mammals, ungulates, and small mammals) and approximately 15 bird entries (Accipitridae, Pandionidae, Falconidae, Cathartidae). All were either mammals or birds. The Pacific Salmon is a fish -- vertebrate, yes, but a different class of vertebrate (Actinopterygii, the ray-finned fishes). A class boundary has been crossed.
- **Salmonidae as family.** The family Salmonidae includes salmon, trout, char, whitefish, and grayling -- approximately 220 species worldwide. The Pacific salmon (genus *Oncorhynchus*) are the family's Pacific Northwest representatives and, arguably, the most important fish in North America from ecological, cultural, and economic perspectives.

## THE FIVE SPECIES -- PACIFIC SALMON OF WASHINGTON STATE

### *Oncorhynchus* -- The Genus of Pacific Salmon

Five species of Pacific salmon run in Washington State's rivers and streams, each with distinct biology, timing, and ecological roles:

### 1. Chinook Salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) -- King Salmon

- **Size.** The largest Pacific salmon. Adults typically 60-120 cm (24-48 in), weight 4.5-20 kg (10-45 lbs). Record fish exceed 50 kg (110 lbs). The Chinook's size is the origin of its common name "King Salmon" -- it is the king in every dimension.
- **Run timing.** Chinook runs occur in spring (spring Chinook, "springers") and fall (fall Chinook). Spring Chinook enter rivers as early as February-March; fall Chinook enter in August-October. Spring Chinook spawn in late summer; fall Chinook spawn in fall.
- **Habitat.** Chinook require the largest, deepest rivers. Their size demands deep pools for migration and large-gravel beds for spawning. The Columbia River, Skagit River, Elwha River, and Snoqualmie River are major Chinook systems in Washington.
- **Cultural significance.** The Chinook is the most important salmon for indigenous Pacific Northwest peoples. It is the preferred species of the Coast Salish, Chinook, Tillamook, and other nations for cultural, ceremonial, and subsistence use. The Chinook salmon is so central to indigenous Northwest culture that the word "Chinook" itself became applied to the people, the trade language (Chinook Jargon), and the warm winter wind (Chinook wind) of the region.
- **Conservation status.** Multiple Chinook Evolutionarily Significant Units (ESUs) in Washington State are listed under the Endangered Species Act. The Snake River fall Chinook ESU is listed as Threatened. The Puget Sound Chinook ESU is listed as Threatened. These are some of the most consequential ESA listings in the Pacific Northwest.

### 2. Coho Salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*) -- Silver Salmon

- **Size.** Medium-sized. Adults typically 45-75 cm (18-30 in), weight 2.5-8 kg (5.5-17.6 lbs).
- **Run timing.** Coho are fall fish: September-November in most Washington rivers. Some systems have wild variation.
- **Habitat.** Coho are more tolerant of smaller streams than Chinook. They use smaller tributary systems, often with significant woody debris (fallen trees) that creates pools. Coho are one of the most important salmon for PNW stream ecosystems because they penetrate smaller headwater streams that Chinook rarely reach.
- **Urban streams.** Coho show a striking vulnerability to urban stormwater: a syndrome called "urban runoff mortality" or "coho salmon pre-spawn mortality" causes Coho to die before spawning when exposed to stormwater from highways. The causative compound was identified in 2020 as 6PPD-quinone, a tire rubber chemical that washes off roads in rain. Washington State has responded with legislation addressing tire chemicals.
- **Conservation status.** Puget Sound Coho are a Species of Concern. Olympic Peninsula and coastal Coho populations are more robust.

### 3. Sockeye Salmon (*Oncorhynchus nerka*) -- Red Salmon

- **Size.** Adults typically 45-75 cm (18-30 in), weight 1.5-4.5 kg (3.3-10 lbs).
- **Lake dependency.** Sockeye are the most lake-dependent Pacific salmon. Juveniles spend 1-2 years rearing in lakes before migrating to the ocean. Adults return to spawn in streams that connect to or drain from lakes. In Washington State, Lake Wenatchee, Lake Ozette, Lake Quinault, and the Lake Ozette watershed are important Sockeye systems.
- **Spawning coloration.** Sockeye adults develop the most dramatic spawning coloration of any Pacific salmon: the body turns brilliant crimson-red, and the head turns green. Males develop a hooked jaw (kype) and humped back. The transformation is so dramatic that spawning Sockeye look like a different species than the silver ocean-phase fish.
- **Kokanee.** The non-anadromous form of Sockeye (resident in lakes without ocean migration) is called Kokanee. Kokanee are found in many Washington lakes, both naturally and through stocking programs.
- **Conservation status.** Lake Ozette Sockeye are listed as Endangered under the ESA. Snake River Sockeye are listed as Endangered -- this ESU may be the most endangered salmon population in the lower 48 states.

### 4. Pink Salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*) -- Humpy Salmon

- **Size.** The smallest Pacific salmon. Adults typically 45-60 cm (18-24 in), weight 1-2.7 kg (2.2-6 lbs).
- **Two-year cycle.** Pink Salmon have an invariant two-year life cycle. All fish spawn at exactly two years of age. In Washington's Puget Sound, Pinks run in odd years (2025, 2027, etc.) in strong numbers and in even years in smaller numbers. The odd-year/even-year asymmetry creates a predictable cycle across the species' range.
- **Male morphology.** Spawning male Pink Salmon develop a dramatic hump on the back (behind the head), giving the species its alternate name "Humpy." The hump is the most exaggerated secondary sexual characteristic in any Pacific salmon.
- **Abundance.** Despite being the smallest Pacific salmon, the Pink Salmon is the most numerous. Globally, Pink Salmon have the largest total biomass of any Pacific salmon species. The 2023 Pink Salmon run in Puget Sound and Alaskan rivers was one of the largest on record.
- **Conservation status.** Most Pink Salmon ESUs in Washington are not listed under the ESA. Some inner Puget Sound populations are a concern.

### 5. Chum Salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*) -- Dog Salmon

- **Size.** Adults typically 60-100 cm (24-40 in), weight 4-13 kg (8.8-28.6 lbs).
- **Teeth.** Spawning Chum develop distinctive canine-like teeth (giving the species the alternate name "Dog Salmon"). The teeth are used in spawning competition. Male Chum also develop a bold calico pattern of red and green-purple bars on the sides.
- **Spawning habitat.** Chum use smaller streams and tidally influenced reaches closer to the ocean than most other Pacific salmon. They spawn in the lower reaches of rivers, sometimes in streams barely large enough to hold them.
- **Run timing.** Chum run in fall and early winter. The Chum run in Washington's Hood Canal and its tributaries is one of the state's most important.
- **Conservation status.** Hood Canal summer Chum are listed as Threatened under the ESA. Other Washington Chum populations are of concern.

(Sources: Chinook biology, size, run timing, conservation; Coho biology, 6PPD-quinone, urban runoff; Sockeye lake dependency, spawning coloration, Kokanee; Pink two-year cycle, Humpy morphology; Chum teeth, calico pattern, Hood Canal; all five species ESA listings)

## THE PACIFIC SALMON LIFECYCLE

### The Most Remarkable Journey in the Animal Kingdom

The Pacific salmon lifecycle is one of biology's most extraordinary phenomena:

- **Birth.** Salmon eggs are laid in redds (gravel nests) in cold, clean, well-oxygenated rivers and streams. The female digs the redd by turning on her side and sweeping her tail powerfully through the gravel -- this is one of the physical actions that produces the spawning sounds documented in this degree. The female deposits eggs in the depression; the male fertilizes them. The female covers the eggs with gravel and moves upstream to begin a new redd.
- **Alevin.** Eggs hatch into alevin -- tiny fish with an attached yolk sac that provides nutrition for the first weeks of life. Alevin remain in the gravel interstitial spaces, feeding from the yolk sac, until the sac is absorbed.
- **Fry.** When the yolk sac is absorbed, fry emerge from the gravel and begin feeding on invertebrates in the stream. This is the first stage of active life in the stream environment. Fry are vulnerable to predation from many directions: birds, larger fish, mammals.
- **Parr.** As the fry grow, they develop distinctive dark bars along their sides called "parr marks" -- the parr stage. Parr remain in the river for months to years, depending on the species. Chinook parr may spend one year in freshwater; Sockeye spend 1-2 years in a lake.
- **Smolt transformation (Smoltification).** Before migrating to the ocean, parr undergo smoltification -- a profound physiological transformation that allows them to survive in salt water. The fish changes from a freshwater creature to a marine creature: the kidneys reverse their operation (freshwater kidneys excrete dilute urine to prevent cells from waterlogging; marine kidneys concentrate urine to prevent dehydration); the gills develop salt-excreting cells; the body color changes from the spotted parr marks to silver; the behavior changes to facilitate downstream migration in schools.
- **Ocean life.** Smolts migrate to the ocean, where they grow rapidly on the rich marine food web. Pacific salmon in the ocean eat copepods, krill, herring, sand lance, and other fish. The ocean growth is extraordinary: a Chinook smolt entering the ocean at 10 cm and 10 grams may return as an adult at 100 cm and 20 kg -- a 2,000-fold increase in weight in 1-4 years.
- **The return.** After 1-7 years at sea (depending on species), adult salmon return to their natal stream -- the exact stream where they hatched. The mechanism of this navigation is extraordinary: olfactory memory of the chemical signature of the home stream, supplemented by magnetic orientation at sea. A Chinook that hatched in the Elwha River will cross thousands of miles of open ocean and return, from among thousands of possible streams, to the Elwha.
- **Spawning migration.** Returning adults stop eating when they enter freshwater. They migrate on stored energy -- the fat accumulated in the ocean -- sometimes hundreds of miles upstream. They must navigate waterfalls, rapids, fishing nets, dams, bears, eagles, and herons. The physiological changes of the return migration are as dramatic as smoltification: the fish's body begins to break down. Muscle protein is converted to energy. The kype (hooked jaw) develops. Coloration intensifies.
- **Spawning and death.** Pacific salmon are semelparous -- they die after spawning. This is not incidental but adaptive: the salmon's decomposing body delivers marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon) from the ocean into the river ecosystem. A spawned-out salmon carcass is eaten by bears, eagles, ravens, raccoons, and insects -- the marine nutrients move up the watershed into the forest through these connections.

(Sources: Pacific salmon lifecycle; redd construction; alevin; fry; parr marks; smoltification; ocean growth rates; olfactory natal stream homing; magnetic ocean navigation; semelparity; marine-derived nutrient delivery)

## THE SPAWNING SOUND -- E=5

### What the Engine Is Listening To

The sound documented in this degree is not a vocalization -- it is the physical sound of spawning:

- **The sound components.** At a spawning stream during peak run, the acoustic environment includes:
  - **Tail-slapping:** Females sweep their tails through gravel to construct and cover redds. The tail-slap on shallow gravel makes a loud smacking sound -- audible from 50-100 meters.
  - **Body movement:** Large fish (particularly Chinook, weighing 10-30 kg) moving through shallow water create substantial water displacement. The sound is a heavy splashing, different from the light splashing of a small fish.
  - **Fish fighting:** Males compete vigorously for position at redds. The fighting involves chasing, biting, and physically displacing rivals. This creates aggressive splashing and collision sounds.
  - **Jumping:** Salmon jumping at waterfalls and rapids create loud splashing as they re-enter the water. The Cedar Falls salmon runs, where fish attempt to leap Landsburg Dam on the Cedar River, create a concentrated leaping sound.
  - **Background current:** The overall sound of water over gravel provides the ambient bed over which the salmon sounds sit.
- **E=5 placement.** The combined sound of a spawning run at peak is substantial and present -- E=5 is appropriate. Not the roar of an orca pod at the surface, not the near-silence of the Turkey Vulture, but a continuous, energetic, physically impressive soundscape produced by hundreds or thousands of large fish in a confined stream.

(Sources: Spawning sound components; tail-slap mechanics; body displacement; redd construction sounds; fish fighting acoustics; Cedar Falls salmon; E=5 assessment)

## THE ECOLOGICAL WEB -- THE SALMON AND THE FOREST

### How Salmon Feed Bears, Eagles, Orcas, and Trees

The Pacific Salmon is the PNW's keystone species because it connects the ocean to the forest through a network of ecological dependencies:

- **Bears.** Brown Bears (*Ursus arctos*, Grizzly -- degree 217-218 in the engine) and Black Bears (*Ursus americanus*) congregate at salmon streams during the fall run. Bears catch salmon in pools, at falls, and at shallow riffles. A single bear may eat 30-40 salmon per day at peak season, accumulating the fat that allows winter hibernation. Without salmon, interior bear populations would be nutritionally impoverished.
- **Eagles.** Bald Eagles (degrees 221-222 in the engine) congregate at spawning streams in fall and winter to feed on live and dead salmon. The Skagit Valley eagle concentration -- hundreds of eagles feeding on dying spawned-out chum salmon -- is one of the PNW's most important wildlife spectacles and is directly dependent on the salmon run.
- **Osprey.** Osprey (degree 224 in the engine) fish for salmon throughout the season, taking juveniles and adults at the surface.
- **Orcas.** Southern Resident Orca (already in the engine) are salmon-obligate specialists: 90%+ of their diet is Chinook salmon. The Southern Resident Orca population -- about 75 individuals -- is directly limited by Chinook salmon abundance. When Chinook are scarce (which they frequently are), orcas lose condition, fail to reproduce, and die. The Chinook salmon is the single most important factor in Orca conservation in the Salish Sea.
- **The forest.** This is the marine nutrient cycle's most remarkable chapter. Bears, eagles, and other predators carry salmon carcasses away from the stream bank, sometimes hundreds of meters, and consume them in the forest. The decomposing carcasses fertilize the soil with marine-derived nitrogen (nitrogen isotope ratios in PNW forest soil and tree rings show elevated marine nitrogen coinciding with historical salmon runs). Studies of Sitka spruce, Douglas fir, and red alder growing near salmon streams show higher growth rates than equivalent trees without salmon access. The trees grow faster because the salmon die.
- **The insects.** Salmon carcasses support enormous insect communities -- flies, beetles, and their larvae -- that in turn feed songbirds, small mammals, and juvenile salmon. The insect-salmon link is another pathway through which the marine nutrients move into the terrestrial ecosystem.
- **The cross-references.** The SPS engine has already documented many of the salmon's ecological partners:
  - **Bald Eagle (221-222):** Salmon-dependent apex raptor.
  - **Osprey (224):** Primary fish predator.
  - **Grizzly Bear (217-218):** Salmon as critical fall food source.
  - **Orca:** Chinook-obligate apex marine predator.
  - **Now Pacific Salmon (236):** The keystone species that all of the above depend on.

(Sources: Bear salmon predation ecology; Bald Eagle Skagit Valley congregation; Osprey salmon fishing; Orca Chinook dependency; marine-derived nitrogen in forest soils; tree ring growth studies near salmon streams; insect-carcass community; SPS cross-reference network)

## INDIGENOUS RELATIONSHIP -- FIRST FOODS

### Salmon at the Center of Northwest Coast Culture

No ecological relationship in the Pacific Northwest is as culturally central as the human-salmon relationship:

- **First Salmon ceremonies.** For virtually every indigenous nation of the Pacific Northwest -- Coast Salish, Chinook, Tillamook, Quinault, Makah, Lummi, Tulalip, Skokomish, and others -- the First Salmon ceremony is one of the most important ritual events of the year. When the first salmon of the season is caught, it is treated with protocols: the fish is thanked, portions are offered back to the water, and the fish is shared with the community before any individual consumption. The ceremony acknowledges that the salmon returns as a gift and must be treated with respect to ensure future returns.
- **Treaty rights.** The 1854-1855 treaties between the US government and Pacific Northwest tribes (Point Elliott Treaty, Point No Point Treaty, Medicine Creek Treaty, etc.) guaranteed tribal fishing rights "in common with all citizens of the Territory." The meaning of "in common" was contested for over a century. In 1974, US District Court Judge George Boldt issued the "Boldt Decision" (United States v. Washington), ruling that the treaties guaranteed tribes 50% of the harvestable salmon and steelhead in Washington State. The Boldt Decision is one of the most significant legal decisions in the history of US-tribal relations.
- **Salmon as identity.** For many Northwest Coast peoples, the salmon is not merely food or resource -- it is part of identity itself. The Salmon People narrative, in which salmon are understood as people who live in underwater villages and choose to sacrifice themselves to human hunters who treat them respectfully, is a central narrative in Northwest Coast oral literature and culture. The protocols surrounding salmon fishing -- the prayers, the first-fish ceremonies, the prohibition on wasteful take -- derive from this narrative framework.
- **The Columbia River.** Celilo Falls on the Columbia River was, before its inundation by The Dalles Dam in 1957, the most important salmon fishing site in the world. Indigenous peoples from across the continent gathered there. The destruction of Celilo Falls -- witnessed by elders who are still alive today -- is one of the most culturally traumatic events in Pacific Northwest indigenous history.

(Sources: First Salmon ceremonies; Coast Salish protocols; 1854-1855 treaty rights; Boldt Decision 1974; Salmon People narrative; Celilo Falls; The Dalles Dam 1957)

## SALMON CONSERVATION -- THE MOST IMPORTANT ECOLOGICAL ISSUE IN THE PNW

### The Crisis and the Recovery Efforts

- **The decline.** Pacific Salmon populations across the Pacific Northwest have declined dramatically from historical abundance. Pre-contact estimates suggest that 16-40 million adult salmon returned to Washington State rivers annually. Current returns are a fraction of this. Multiple Evolutionarily Significant Units (ESUs) are listed as Threatened or Endangered under the Endangered Species Act.
- **The causes.** The decline has multiple interacting causes:
  - **Dams:** Over 1,000 dams in the Columbia River basin block salmon migration. The four lower Snake River dams (Ice Harbor, Little Goose, Lower Monumental, Lower Granite) have been a subject of controversy for 30 years. Removal of these dams has been proposed and debated; in 2024, the Biden administration and Pacific Northwest tribes reached an agreement providing a framework for potential dam removal alongside alternative energy investment.
  - **Habitat loss:** Logging, agriculture, urban development, and road building have degraded stream habitat throughout the salmon's range. Riparian zones have been cleared, streams have been channelized, fine sediment has smothered spawning gravel.
  - **Hatcheries:** The hatchery system, intended to compensate for habitat loss, has had mixed results. Hatchery fish interbreed with wild fish, reducing genetic diversity and local adaptation. In some systems, hatchery-wild interactions have depressed wild populations.
  - **Ocean conditions:** Climate change is warming ocean temperatures and altering the currents and upwelling patterns that produce the food web supporting salmon. The Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) creates multi-year cycles of favorable and unfavorable ocean conditions for salmon.
  - **6PPD-quinone:** Discovered as the cause of urban coho pre-spawn mortality in 2020 -- a major finding with significant conservation implications.
- **Recovery efforts.** Dam removal (Elwha River dams removed 2011-2014 -- the largest dam removal in US history at the time), habitat restoration, hatchery reform, fishing regulation, and tribal co-management have all contributed to recovery efforts. The Elwha dam removal has produced measurable salmon recovery -- Chinook, Coho, Steelhead, and Pink salmon returning above the former dam sites within a decade of removal.

(Sources: Pre-contact salmon abundance estimates; ESA listings; dam impacts; lower Snake River dam removal discussion; 6PPD-quinone Coho mortality 2020; hatchery genetic concerns; Elwha dam removal 2011-2014; Pacific Decadal Oscillation)
