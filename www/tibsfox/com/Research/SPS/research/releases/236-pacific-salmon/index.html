FIRST FISH IN THE ENGINE — FIRST SALMONIDAE — PNW KEYSTONE SPECIES

Degree 236 is the engine's first non-bird, non-mammal vertebrate. Pacific Salmon are Actinopterygii (ray-finned fishes), a vertebrate class not previously represented in SPS. Five species in Washington: Chinook, Coho, Sockeye, Pink, Chum. The Oncorhynchus genus. FIRST SALMONIDAE. The PNW's keystone species — the ecological center around which bears, eagles, orcas, and forests organize.

The Sound That Is Not a Song

Pacific Salmon have no vocal organ. There is no larynx, no syrinx, no structure designed for sound production. The "sound" of a spawning run is the physical consequence of large fish in shallow water: tail-slapping gravel to build and cover redds, bodies displacing water, males fighting for position, fish jumping at falls. At peak run, a spawning stream produces a continuous, energetic soundscape — but it is the sound of work, not of voice.

Acoustic Profile — Spawning Soundscape

Tail-slap on gravel: broadband impact sound, 50–500 Hz
Body displacement: low-frequency water splash, 20–200 Hz
Combined run density: E=5 (hundreds of fish active simultaneously)

The tail-slap (female sweeping gravel to build the redd) is the most distinctive component — audible from 50-100m. Body-displacement splashing from large Chinook (10-20kg) in shallow water is substantial. Male fighting creates aggressive splashing. Combined: an E=5 soundscape that is distinctive and place-specific.

The Five Species

Chinook — King Salmon

Oncorhynchus tshawytscha

Largest (up to 50kg). Spring and fall runs. Columbia, Skagit, Elwha. Orca-obligate. Multiple ESUs threatened or endangered. The culturally central salmon.

Coho — Silver Salmon

Oncorhynchus kisutch

Fall runs, Sep–Nov. Penetrates small tributaries. Vulnerable to urban stormwater — 6PPD-quinone from tire rubber causes pre-spawn mortality. Puget Sound = Species of Concern.

Sockeye — Red Salmon

Oncorhynchus nerka

Lake-dependent. Brilliant crimson spawning color. Snake River ESU: Endangered. Lake Ozette ESU: Endangered. Kokanee = landlocked form.

Pink — Humpy Salmon

Oncorhynchus gorbuscha

Smallest (1-2.7kg). Strict 2-year cycle. Puget Sound: odd-year strong runs. Dramatic male hump. Most abundant Pacific salmon by biomass globally.

Chum — Dog Salmon

Oncorhynchus keta

Canine teeth in spawning males. Calico coloration. Hood Canal summer Chum: Threatened. Lower river specialist; some spawn in tidally influenced reaches.

The Lifecycle — Birth to Death to Rebirth

Pacific Salmon are semelparous — they spawn once and die. The lifecycle is one of biology's most extraordinary phenomena: egg hatching in gravel, alevin absorbing yolk sac in the redd, fry emerging to feed in the stream, parr developing over months to years, smoltification converting the fish from a freshwater to a marine organism, ocean growth of up to 2,000x body weight in 1-4 years, and then the return — by olfactory memory of the home stream's chemical signature — to the exact gravel bed where they were born.

"A Chinook that hatched in the Elwha River will cross thousands of miles of open ocean and return, from among thousands of possible streams, to the Elwha. The olfactory navigation of the natal stream is one of biology's most precise mechanisms."

The Ecological Web — Keystone Species

The Pacific Salmon connects ocean to forest in a web of dependencies that the engine has been building since degree 217:

  • Grizzly Bear (217-218): 30-40 salmon per day at peak. Marine nutrients in the bear's fat reserves that enable winter hibernation.
  • Bald Eagle (221-222): Winter congregation at salmon streams. Skagit Valley — hundreds of eagles on dying chum salmon.
  • Osprey (224): Live salmon taken throughout the season. Direct salmon dependency.
  • Orca: Southern Resident Orca are Chinook-obligate. 90%+ Chinook diet. The Orca population is directly limited by Chinook abundance.
  • The forest: Salmon carcasses carried away from streams fertilize soil with marine nitrogen. Trees near salmon streams grow measurably faster. The ocean nutrients travel upstream through predators and decomposition.

Conservation

MULTIPLE ESA LISTINGS — CRISIS AND RECOVERY. Pre-contact: 16-40 million adult salmon returned to Washington annually. Current: a fraction. Multiple ESUs Threatened or Endangered. Major threats: dams (1,000+ in Columbia basin), habitat degradation, hatchery genetic dilution, warming ocean, 6PPD-quinone. Elwha dam removal (2011-2014, largest dam removal in US history at the time) has produced measurable salmon recovery within one decade. Lower Snake River dam removal is the next major question.
