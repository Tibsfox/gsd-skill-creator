## What Worked

1. **Nocturnal colony is the defining feature and must be the organizing principle.** The Rhinoceros Auklet's strict nocturnality at colony sites is not a behavioral quirk -- it is the bird's complete behavioral strategy for predator avoidance during the colony period. The engine documents the consequence: an entirely acoustic experience, invisible, filling the night with low moaning calls that navigate both bird and mate in complete darkness.
2. **The predator-prey consecutive pair (295-296) is the engine's most elegant ecological sequence.** The Rhinoceros Auklet's nocturnality is partly evolved Peregrine Falcon avoidance. The engine places the prey's adaptation (nocturnal colony moans, degree 295) immediately before the predator's maximum-performance event (stoop wingcut, degree 296). The ecological logic is preserved in consecutive degree structure.
3. **Tufted Puffin (294) + Rhinoceros Auklet (295) = complete temporal documentation.** Diurnal (294) and nocturnal (295): two consecutive entries, same family, same ecosystem (rocky island colony), same season, inverted temporal strategies. The two-entry Alcidae pair at 294-295 is the most thorough single-family paired documentation in the engine.
4. **Protection Island NWR is correctly documented as the major PNW colony.** ~17,000 breeding pairs, Strait of Juan de Fuca, closed to public, annual USFWS monitoring. The largest PNW Rhinoceros Auklet colony and one of the most intensively monitored seabird sites in the Pacific Northwest.

## Discoveries

1. **The acoustic navigation evidence is one of the most fascinating aspects of this entry.** The hypothesis that Rhinoceros Auklets navigate to their specific burrow entrance among hundreds of identical soil openings in complete darkness using vocalization-and-mate-response exchange is, if confirmed, one of the most behaviorally sophisticated navigation strategies in any colonial seabird. The moan is not just communication -- it may be sonar.
