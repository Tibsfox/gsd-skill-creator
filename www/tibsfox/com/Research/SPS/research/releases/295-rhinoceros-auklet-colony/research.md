## THE RHINOCEROS AUKLET -- THE BIRD

### Identity, Form, and the Horn

The Rhinoceros Auklet (*Cerorhinca monocerata*) is a distinctive and often underappreciated Pacific Northwest seabird:

- **The horn.** The breeding adult Rhinoceros Auklet develops a short, fleshy, horn-like protuberance at the base of the bill -- the "rhinoceros" of the common name. Combined with two pairs of white facial plumes (a recurved white plume above the eye, another along the bill), the breeding-plumage Rhinoceros Auklet has a dramatic face for an otherwise subdued-colored bird. The horn is a secondary sexual characteristic shed after the breeding season.
- **Overall coloring.** The Rhinoceros Auklet is dark -- sooty brown-gray above, slightly paler below -- with no bright colors beyond the white facial plumes and yellowish bill base. It lacks the Tufted Puffin's dramatic white face, golden tufts, and orange bill. In winter, the facial plumes are reduced or absent and the horn is lost; the bird becomes a nondescript dark alcid of the pelagic zone, extremely difficult to identify at sea.
- **Size and build.** Intermediate in size between smaller auklets (Cassin's, Least) and puffins. Body length approximately 13-15 inches, wingspan about 22 inches. The typical alcid profile: stocky, torpedo-shaped for underwater diving, wings small relative to body mass. The whirring wingbeat audible at close range is the same mechanical signature as the Tufted Puffin.
- **Taxonomy.** The Rhinoceros Auklet is placed in the monotypic genus *Cerorhinca* (Greek: keras=horn, rhis/rhinos=nose). Despite its common name including "auklet" (suggesting a small auk), it is more closely related to puffins than to the small auklets of the genus *Aethia*. Its position in Alcidae systematics is as a medium-large alcid, closer to the *Fratercula* puffin lineage than to the small colonial auklets.

(Sources: Cerorhinca monocerata species account; Rhinoceros Auklet taxonomy; alcid systematics; horn morphology; seasonal plumage cycle)

## NOCTURNAL COLONY BEHAVIOR -- THE DEFINING FEATURE

### Why Darkness Matters

The Rhinoceros Auklet's most ecologically significant behavior is its strict nocturnality at colony sites:

- **Predator avoidance.** The Rhinoceros Auklet arrives at and departs from its colony at night, avoiding the daylight hours when avian predators (Bald Eagles, Peregrine Falcons -- degree 296 in this engine) are active. This is not optional behavior or population-level variation -- it is the species' fixed behavioral strategy for predator avoidance at colonial sites. The bird forages in daylight, far offshore; it returns to the colony only after dark.
- **The nightly return.** At dusk, Rhinoceros Auklets begin arriving offshore near their colony islands, gathering in rafts (groups floating on the water surface). As darkness deepens, the birds begin landing on the island -- crash-landing in the vegetation, clambering to their burrow entrances, calling and responding to mates. This arrival sequence, invisible in complete darkness, is entirely acoustic: the sounds of wings, body-impacts with vegetation, and the groaning calls are the only evidence of what is happening.
- **Acoustic navigation.** The evidence suggests that Rhinoceros Auklets use vocalization and acoustic responses from their mates to navigate to their specific burrow entrances in complete darkness. The moaning calls function as both location signals (here is where I am) and pair-bond reinforcement (I am your mate, confirm reception). This acoustic navigation in darkness is one of the most behaviorally sophisticated elements of the species' colony biology.
- **The groaning calls.** The calls made by Rhinoceros Auklets at the colony at night are distinctive: low, groaning, moaning vocalizations, rhythmically repeated, approximately 200-600 Hz in fundamental frequency. The quality is somewhat mournful, somewhat rhythmic -- a slow pulsing moan that is utterly unlike the sharp calls of terns or the ambient chatter of gull colonies. Heard at a colony island at night without visual context, the sound is one of the more unusual wildlife acoustic experiences in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Rhinoceros Auklet colony nocturnal behavior; predator avoidance research; acoustic navigation in burrow-nesting alcids; colony acoustics recordings; nocturnal seabird colony ecology)

## BURROW NESTING -- THE COLONY ARCHITECTURE

### Underground Alcids

Burrow nesting is the Rhinoceros Auklet's colony strategy:

- **Burrow excavation.** The Rhinoceros Auklet excavates burrows in soil on vegetated islands. The burrows can be extensive: some measure 2-3 meters in length, with the nest chamber at the end lined with plant material. The same burrow is used over multiple years by the same pair. In some dense colony sites, hundreds or thousands of burrow entrances may be visible in a relatively small area of ground -- the soil honeycombed with tunnels.
- **The egg and chick.** One egg per year. Both parents incubate (approximately 45 days). After hatching, the chick remains in the burrow for approximately 55 days, provisioned by both parents who arrive at night carrying fish. The provisioning fish (held crosswise in the bill -- a characteristic alcid food-carrying posture) are delivered entirely in darkness, at the burrow entrance, to the waiting chick.
- **Colony sites in the Pacific Northwest.** Major Rhinoceros Auklet colonies: Protection Island National Wildlife Refuge (Strait of Juan de Fuca, Jefferson County, Washington -- one of the largest Rhinoceros Auklet colonies in the world, approximately 17,000 breeding pairs), Destruction Island (Jefferson County, Washington), Three Arch Rocks NWR (Oregon), the Triangle Island colony (British Columbia, across the international border). Protection Island is the most significant PNW colony site and one of the most important seabird nesting locations in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Protection Island.** Protection Island NWR is a 364-acre island at the entrance to Discovery Bay in the Strait of Juan de Fuca. It is closed to the public to protect the breeding seabirds (Rhinoceros Auklets, Tufted Puffins, Pigeon Guillemots, and wintering Black Brant) and is accessible only by permitted researchers and USFWS personnel. The island is visible from the Dungeness Spit recreation area and from the town of Sequim on clear days.

(Sources: Protection Island NWR; Rhinoceros Auklet burrow nesting biology; colony site data; provisioning behavior; USFWS colony surveys)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- NIGHT SOUNDSCAPE OF THE ALCID COLONY

### Moans, Wingbeats, and Darkness

The acoustic character of a Rhinoceros Auklet colony at night:

- **The arriving birds.** As Rhinoceros Auklets arrive at the colony island at night, the first sound is wingbeats: the rapid whirring of alcid flight, descending as the bird crash-lands in the vegetation or at the burrow entrance. The landing is not graceful -- alcids are built for underwater agility, not aerial precision, and the colony landings reflect this.
- **The moaning calls.** The vocalization sequence at the burrow entrance: moaning calls between the arriving bird and its burrow partner (who has been inside the burrow waiting). The moans are low, rhythmic, approximately 0.5-1 second in duration, fundamental frequency 200-600 Hz. Both the arriving bird and the waiting mate call in exchange, a kind of vocal confirmation: I am here, you are there, this is our burrow.
- **The colony texture at peak.** At the peak of nightly arrival (typically 1-2 hours after sunset), a large Rhinoceros Auklet colony site is a continuous acoustic environment of moaning calls, wingbeats, body-sounds of birds moving through vegetation, and the background of ocean and wind. The density of calls from hundreds or thousands of pairs creates a continuous low-frequency textural soundscape -- nothing like the sharp tern colony, but deeply present and atmospheric.
- **E=6 calibration.** E=6 reflects the acoustic character: present, substantial, deeply textured -- but operating at a lower frequency and more ambient register than a tern colony's sharp alarm calls. The Rhinoceros Auklet colony night soundscape is E=6 as deep texture: you feel it as much as you hear it, the sound is below the range where it pierces, but it fills the night completely.

(Sources: Rhinoceros Auklet colony night acoustics; nocturnal seabird colony recordings; Protection Island acoustics data; burrow-entrance call analysis; E=6 calibration)

## THE ALCIDAE ARC -- TWO CONSECUTIVE ENTRIES

### 294 Tufted Puffin and 295 Rhinoceros Auklet

The two consecutive Alcidae entries establish the family in the SPS engine:

- **Complementary temporal profiles.** The Tufted Puffin (294) is primarily diurnal at the colony: it arrives in daylight, launches from cliff edges in daylight, is visible and audible from shore in daylight. The Rhinoceros Auklet (295) is strictly nocturnal at the colony: it arrives after dark, its entire colony behavior is invisible, it exists at colony sites as an acoustic phenomenon rather than a visual one. The two entries together document the full diurnal-to-nocturnal spectrum of alcid colony behavior.
- **Same family, different lifestyle.** Both are burrow-nesting alcids. Both breed on Pacific Northwest rocky islands. Both forage on fish offshore. But the Tufted Puffin is a diurnal, visually dramatic species -- the one you see on greeting cards, the one that draws birding tours. The Rhinoceros Auklet is nocturnal, visually nondescript, known to most people only as the night sounds from an island they cannot see. The contrast is the alcid family's full range.
- **The sound difference.** Tufted Puffin: deep grunts + mechanical wingbeat at takeoff, colony ambient from cliff edges, daylight soundscape. Rhinoceros Auklet: low groaning moans + crash-landing wingbeats, completely nocturnal, darkness soundscape. The acoustic contrast is the temporal contrast made audible.

(Sources: SPS Alcidae arc; degrees 294-295; comparative alcid colony ecology; nocturnal vs. diurnal colony strategies; acoustic contrasts Tufted Puffin vs. Rhinoceros Auklet)

## CONSERVATION AND MONITORING

### Protection Island and Pacific Northwest Status

- **Conservation status.** The Rhinoceros Auklet is listed as Least Concern globally (IUCN), with a large, stable global population distributed across the North Pacific from Japan through Alaska and down the Pacific coast to California. The Pacific Northwest populations are among the healthiest coastal seabird populations in the region.
- **Protection Island management.** Protection Island NWR is one of the Pacific Northwest's most intensively monitored seabird colonies. Annual surveys by USFWS and partner researchers document Rhinoceros Auklet breeding success, population trends, and colony size. The island's closed status to the public is essential to the colony's functioning: disturbance during the nocturnal arrival period can cause mass abandonment of burrow entrances with severe breeding consequences.
- **Climate effects.** Pacific Northwest Rhinoceros Auklet populations are monitored for climate-related productivity shifts: as sea surface temperatures change, prey distribution (primarily juvenile salmon, sand lance, and Pacific herring) changes, affecting the distance parents must travel to provision chicks. Long provisioning distances reduce chick growth rates and breeding success. Protection Island colony productivity is a climate-sensitive indicator species for Pacific Northwest marine ecosystem health.

(Sources: IUCN Rhinoceros Auklet status; Protection Island NWR monitoring; USFWS seabird program; climate change effects on alcid breeding ecology; PNW marine ecosystem indicators)
