The Acoustic Foundation of the Pacific Northwest

Full Pacific Infrasound is the continuous, mostly sub-audible low-frequency acoustic output of the Pacific Ocean during extreme storm conditions. Three distinct physical processes operate simultaneously: microseisms (0.05-0.3 Hz) generated by wave-wave interference and transmitted through the crust continent-wide; infrasound (0.5-20 Hz) from breaking swells propagating thousands of kilometers through the atmosphere; and ocean swell noise (20-200 Hz) fully audible at the coast. During a major Pacific storm, all three bands are simultaneously active. The ocean is literally shaking the continent, filling the sky, and roaring at the shore at once. This is the largest natural sound source on Earth. E=9 through planetary scale.

Three Frequency Bands — Simultaneous

Microseisms

0.05–0.5 Hz

Generated by wave-wave interference at sea. Seismic waves travel through the crust at 3-8 km/s. Detectable on seismometers continent-wide. During major Pacific storms, microseismic amplitude increases by orders of magnitude. The ocean shakes the continent.

Infrasound

0.5–20 Hz

From breaking swells, surf-zone interaction, storm pressure fluctuations. Propagates with very low attenuation through the atmosphere. Travels thousands of km. Animals sensitive to infrasound (whales, elephants) detect Pacific storms at great distances.

Swell Noise

20–200 Hz

The ocean's audible voice. Peak storm energy 50-100 Hz. Fully audible to all mammals. Continuous during storm events. The surf roar audible miles inland. This is the "sound of the ocean" that we recognize — the top layer of a much deeper acoustic event.

Aberdeen and the Pacific Acoustic Environment

Aberdeen, Washington, where the Melvins formed, sits at the confluence of the Wishkah and Chehalis Rivers at Grays Harbor — one of the most exposed natural harbors on the Washington coast. The Pacific is 20-30 miles to the west. The surf at Ocean Shores and Westport produces a continuous low-frequency presence audible throughout the Grays Harbor basin. During extreme storm events, the infrasound from the Pacific permeates the entire region. The microseisms generated by North Pacific storms shake the ground throughout the coastal plain.

The acoustic environment of Aberdeen is defined, at its lowest frequencies, by the Pacific Ocean. The continuous low-frequency presence of the ocean — below the threshold of hearing but present in the body, in the ground, in the atmosphere — is the sonic background against which the Melvins developed their low-frequency aesthetic. Buzz Osborne's B-standard guitar fundamentals (30-62 Hz) sit at the top of the infrasound range and the bottom of the swell noise band. The engine's pairing of Full Pacific Infrasound (SPS 345) with Melvins Peak Sludge (S36 345) is acoustically and geographically coherent. Both operate at the lowest available frequencies. Both originate in the same coastal geography.

Marine Life Within the Infrasound Field

The Pacific's infrasound and low-frequency acoustic output is not only a physical phenomenon; it is the acoustic medium within which all Pacific marine life operates:

Blue whales (Balaenoptera musculus): Their calls (10-40 Hz) occur within the infrasound range. Using the SOFAR channel, these calls propagate across entire ocean basins. During a Pacific storm, blue whale calls are transmitted through water that is simultaneously full of storm acoustic energy at overlapping frequencies. The whales communicate within the acoustic context of the storm, their voices embedded in the ocean's own low-frequency output.

Fin whales (Balaenoptera physalus): Vocalizations at 17-35 Hz. Long-distance calls use the same SOFAR channel propagation. Fin whales of the North Pacific are potentially the largest contributors to the biological component of the low-frequency acoustic environment, though their calls are dwarfed in energy by the storm's physical acoustic output.

Steller sea lions (Eumetopias jubatus): Present on the Washington coast, particularly at Destruction Island and coastal rocks. Their roaring calls (100-500 Hz) are physically present during storm events, adding a biological component to the swell noise band. ESA Threatened.

All Pacific cetaceans: Every whale, dolphin, and porpoise in the Pacific Northeast operates within the acoustic environment defined by Pacific Infrasound. Their calls are shaped by, and must operate above, the ambient noise floor that these physical processes establish. The Pacific's acoustic ecology is built on this foundation.

E=9 — The Summit's Acoustic Foundation

E=9 for Full Pacific Infrasound is calibrated to the scale of the source: the largest body of water on Earth, during an extreme storm event, generating simultaneous acoustic energy across the full low-frequency spectrum. No biological sound source — no whale, no wolf, no bird — generates acoustic energy at the scale of the Pacific Ocean. The E=9 calibration acknowledges this: the Pacific during a storm event is the maximum low-frequency acoustic phenomenon in the PNW ecosystem.

E=10 is reserved for the three absolute cultural maximums in the S36 track. E=9 in the SPS track is the calibration for the physical and biological maximum of a given category. Full Pacific Infrasound is the maximum of the low-frequency category. Nothing in the Pacific Northwest generates comparable energy at 0.05-200 Hz simultaneously.

Combined with Melvins Peak Sludge (S36 345, E=9): E=18. The summit of summits opens at maximum energy in both tracks simultaneously. The acoustic foundation of the Pacific Northwest (E=9) paired with the acoustic foundation of the grunge moment (E=9) as the final four degrees begin.
