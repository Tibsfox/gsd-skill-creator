## WHAT PACIFIC INFRASOUND IS -- THE PHYSICS

### Three Distinct Low-Frequency Sources, Simultaneous

Full Pacific Infrasound is not a single sound but a convergence of three distinct physical processes operating simultaneously across the low-frequency spectrum:

**1. Microseisms (0.1-0.3 Hz)**
Microseisms are continuous seismic vibrations generated by the interaction of ocean waves. When two wave systems of similar frequency travel in opposite directions and meet, they generate a pressure fluctuation in the water column that propagates to the seafloor and generates seismic energy. This happens continuously across the Pacific Ocean wherever opposing wave trains meet. The resulting seismic waves travel through the crust at 3-8 km/s and can be detected on seismometers thousands of kilometers from their source. During a major Pacific storm event, microseismic amplitude increases by orders of magnitude. The 0.1-0.3 Hz frequency range is well below the 20 Hz lower limit of human hearing, but these vibrations are felt by animals sensitive to seismic energy (elephants, elephantine seals, some cetaceans) and by humans in contact with the ground during extreme events.

**2. Infrasound (0.02-20 Hz)**
The Pacific Ocean generates infrasound through multiple mechanisms: breaking wave turbulence (1-20 Hz), surf-zone interactions where swell energy is converted to airborne acoustic energy (1-10 Hz), and atmospheric pressure fluctuations associated with storm systems (0.02-1 Hz). Infrasound travels with very low atmospheric attenuation -- it can propagate thousands of kilometers through the atmosphere, channeled by temperature inversions and wind shear layers. During a high-energy Pacific event, infrasound from breaking swells at the Washington coast propagates inland, over the Cascades, and across the entire Pacific Northwest. Animals sensitive to infrasound -- whales, elephants, some birds -- detect Pacific infrasound from great distances. The frequency range overlaps with the Melvins' guitar fundamentals in the *Bullhead* era (30-62 Hz approaches the upper infrasound range).

**3. Ocean Swell Noise (20-200 Hz)**
As swells approach the coast and begin to break, they generate broadband acoustic energy in the 20-200 Hz range -- audible to humans and to most marine vertebrates. This is the lowest-frequency content of what we conventionally hear as "ocean noise." During a major Pacific storm event with significant swell heights (6-15 meters), the acoustic energy in this range is enormous. Hydrophone recordings from the Pacific shelf during storm events show continuous broadband noise with peak energy in the 50-100 Hz range: this is the ocean's audible voice, the sound of water in massive motion.

## THE PACIFIC OCEAN AS ACOUSTIC SOURCE -- SCALE

### The Numbers Behind E=9

The Pacific Ocean is the largest body of water on Earth. Its acoustic output during extreme events is at a scale that dwarfs almost any other natural sound source:

- **Ocean area:** 165.25 million km² (approximately 30% of Earth's surface area)
- **Average wave height, Pacific during winter storms:** 6-15 meters. Wave energy scales with the square of wave height. A 15-meter wave has 6.25 times the energy of a 6-meter wave.
- **Swell period:** 14-22 seconds during major Pacific events. Long-period swell from distant storms carries enormous energy efficiently, losing very little to atmospheric or bottom friction during propagation.
- **Sound pressure levels:** During major storm events, ocean noise levels in the 50-100 Hz range reach 120-130 dB re 1 µPa at 1 m depth (the standard reference for underwater acoustics). This is exceptionally loud in the marine acoustic environment.
- **Propagation to shore:** The Washington coast receives swell from the North Pacific (typically from storms originating 2,000-6,000 km offshore). The swell has been traveling for days, has organized into regular wave trains, and arrives at the coast with an energy that has been accumulating since the storm's origin. The breaking of this accumulated energy at the coast is one of the most energetic mechanical events in the PNW landscape.
- **Seismic impact:** Major Pacific storms generate microseisms detectable on seismometers throughout North America. The PNSN (Pacific Northwest Seismic Network) regularly records increased microseismic activity during large Pacific storm events. The ocean is literally shaking the continent.

(Sources: Pacific Ocean area statistics; winter storm wave height data NOAA; swell period and energy relationship; underwater sound pressure levels during storm events; Washington coast swell propagation; PNSN microseismic records; Pacific Northwest wave energy)

## MARINE LIFE AND PACIFIC INFRASOUND -- ECOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS

### Who Lives in the Low Frequencies

The Pacific's infrasound and low-frequency acoustic environment is not simply a physical phenomenon; it is an ecological medium:

- **Blue whales (Balaenoptera musculus):** Blue whale calls are in the 10-40 Hz range -- the lowest-frequency vocalizations of any animal. These calls are specifically adapted to propagate in the SOFAR channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging channel), a deep-ocean layer where temperature and pressure combine to minimize acoustic attenuation. A blue whale's call can be detected by hydrophone across entire ocean basins. During a Pacific storm event, blue whale calls occur simultaneously with the storm's own low-frequency output; the whales communicate within the acoustic context of the storm.
- **Fin whales (Balaenoptera physalus):** Fin whale vocalizations are at 17-35 Hz, within the infrasound range. Like blue whales, they use the SOFAR channel for long-distance communication. The Pacific Ocean during a storm is simultaneously full of whale communication and storm acoustic energy at overlapping frequencies.
- **Elephant seals (Mirounga angustirostris):** Breeds on the Washington and Oregon coast. Males produce powerful vocalizations in the 100-500 Hz range during breeding season, but their hearing extends down into the infrasound range. Elephant seals are known to use seismic signals for communication.
- **Pacific white-sided dolphins and other cetaceans:** All cetaceans in the Pacific operate within the acoustic environment defined by the ocean's own low-frequency output. Their communication signals are shaped by, and must operate within, the ambient noise floor that Pacific Infrasound establishes.
- **Migration orientation:** Many migratory species &mdash; whales, sea turtles, some fish species &mdash; are hypothesized to use infrasound and low-frequency acoustic gradients for orientation during migration. The Pacific's continuous infrasound output may function as a long-distance acoustic landmark.

(Sources: Blue whale vocalization frequency 10-40 Hz; SOFAR channel physics; fin whale 17-35 Hz; elephant seal hearing range; Pacific cetacean acoustic ecology; infrasound and migration orientation hypothesis; blue whale call propagation ocean basin scale)
