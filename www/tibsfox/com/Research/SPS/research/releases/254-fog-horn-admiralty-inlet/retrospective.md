## What Worked

1. **The Maritime family entry is the entry's most important structural contribution.** The SPS engine has documented Biology, Meteorology, and Hydrology families. Maritime is the first human-infrastructure family. The fog horn is the correct entry point: it is the most culturally resonant and acoustically distinctive sound in the maritime infrastructure category.
2. **The diaphone is the correct technological centerpiece.** The diaphone (John Philp Northey, 1907) with its characteristic two-tone blast and terminal "grunt" is the fog horn of cultural memory. Documenting its mechanism and specific sound character is the entry's most important technical contribution.
3. **The acoustic frequency rationale.** The fog horn's low pitch is not aesthetic but functional: atmospheric propagation physics favor low frequencies in fog. This is the entry's most useful scientific insight.
4. **Ray Bradbury's "The Fog Horn" (1951).** The story captures the fog horn's emotional content precisely: isolation, longing, the prehistoric creature mistaking the horn for another of its kind. No further analysis needed beyond identifying the reference -- Bradbury did the work.
5. **Port Townsend context.** The Victorian seaport preserved by economic stagnation, the Northwest Maritime Center, the Wooden Boat Festival: Port Townsend is the correct cultural anchor for the fog horn at Point Wilson.

## Discoveries

1. **The fog horn's signal code system.** The timing pattern of blasts (interval, duration, count) identifies the specific station. Published in USCG light lists. This is practical navigation information that transforms the fog horn from ambient sound to navigational instrument.
2. **The two sides of Admiralty Inlet.** Point Wilson Lighthouse (1879, western side) and Admiralty Head Lighthouse (1861, eastern side, decommissioned 1927). The "gates" of Puget Sound have been marked since the Civil War era.
