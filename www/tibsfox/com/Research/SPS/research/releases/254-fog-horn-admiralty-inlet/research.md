## ADMIRALTY INLET -- THE GATEWAY TO PUGET SOUND

### Geography of the Primary Channel

Admiralty Inlet is the principal navigational connection between the Strait of Juan de Fuca and Puget Sound:

- **The geography.** Admiralty Inlet runs roughly north-south, approximately 3-5 miles wide, between Point Wilson at the northeastern tip of the Olympic Peninsula (Port Townsend is the city at Point Wilson) and the southern tip of Whidbey Island. The inlet is the main channel through which all deep-draft vessel traffic must pass to enter or exit Puget Sound. The width constricts to about 2.5 miles at its narrowest, producing moderate tidal currents (3-5 knots) that affect navigation.
- **Point Wilson.** Point Wilson is the critical navigational feature at the western side of Admiralty Inlet. The Point Wilson Lighthouse was established in 1879 (current tower rebuilt 1914) to mark this hazardous turning point where vessels must change course from the Strait to the inlet. Point Wilson has a fog signal -- historically a steam-driven fog horn, later electromechanical, now automated electronic -- that signals to ships in reduced visibility.
- **Whidbey Island.** The eastern side of Admiralty Inlet is defined by the southern tip of Whidbey Island (Admiralty Head), where Fort Casey State Park and the Admiralty Head Lighthouse (established 1861, decommissioned 1927) stand. The combination of Point Wilson and Admiralty Head forms the "gates" of Puget Sound.
- **Traffic.** Admiralty Inlet carries substantial vessel traffic: cargo ships serving Seattle, Tacoma, and Olympia; oil tankers serving refineries at Anacortes; Alaska Marine Highway ferries; Washington State Ferry vessels between Keystone (Whidbey Island) and Port Townsend; recreational vessels; fishing vessels. The inlet is one of the busiest inland waterway passages on the Pacific coast.

## THE FOG HORN -- HISTORY AND TECHNOLOGY

### A Sound Invented for Danger

The fog horn is a technology with a specific history:

- **The problem of fog.** Maritime fog -- dense water vapor suspended at sea level, reducing visibility to near zero -- has been the cause of maritime disasters throughout history. Before electronic navigation (RADAR, GPS), a ship's captain in fog had no way to determine the ship's position except by dead reckoning (calculating from last known position) and by listening. Shore-based fog signals -- sounds produced at known locations -- allowed mariners to determine approximate position by listening for the signal direction and timing.
- **Early fog signals.** Before the steam-powered fog horn, maritime warning sounds were produced by bells (church bells, buoy bells, mechanically rung bells on lighthouses), cannons (fog guns that fired at regular intervals), and hand-powered horns. These were limited in range and reliability. The invention of the steam-powered fog horn in the mid-19th century transformed maritime safety: a steam fog horn could produce a sound audible for miles in dense fog, at a regular interval, powered by the same steam plant that operated the lighthouse.
- **The Diaphone.** The diaphone was a type of fog horn developed in Canada in the early 20th century (patented by John Philp Northey in 1907) that produced the characteristic two-tone "bla-a-at" sound associated with lighthouse fog horns. The diaphone used compressed air forced through a slotted reciprocating piston to produce a sustained tone that dropped in pitch at the end of the blast (the characteristic "grunt" at the end of a diaphone blast). The diaphone tone is one of the most recognizable sounds in maritime culture.
- **The Tyfon.** The Tyfon (also: typhon) is another type of fog horn that uses a diaphragm vibrated by compressed air or steam to produce a sustained tone. Tyfon horns tend to produce a single sustained note rather than the diaphone's two-tone signal.
- **Modern fog signals.** Contemporary fog signals are electric or electronic: electric air horns powered by compressors, electronic oscillators driving piezoelectric transducers, or recorded fog horn sounds played through waterproof speakers. The character of modern fog signals is often less dramatically distinctive than steam-era diaphones -- the characteristic grunt is gone. Some historical fog horns have been preserved as cultural heritage.
- **Automated lighthouses.** The US Coast Guard automated the majority of American lighthouses between 1960 and 1990. Point Wilson Lighthouse was automated in 1977. Automation eliminated the lighthouse keeper but maintained the automated fog signal and light. The fog horn at Point Wilson continues to operate automatically when visibility drops below specified thresholds.

## THE ACOUSTICS OF THE FOG HORN

### Low Frequency, Long Range, Periodic

The fog horn's acoustic properties are specifically designed for its purpose:

- **Low frequency.** Fog horn tones are typically in the range of 100-1000 Hz, with the most characteristic sounds in the 200-400 Hz range. Low frequencies propagate farther through fog and turbulent air than high frequencies: the relationship between frequency and atmospheric absorption means that a 300 Hz tone will travel significantly farther than a 3000 Hz tone in the same conditions. The fog horn's low pitch is not aesthetic but functional.
- **Periodicity.** Fog horns operate on a schedule: the timing of blasts is standardized and published in the light lists maintained by the Coast Guard. The interval, duration, and number of blasts per sequence identify the specific station: a mariner who knows the code can identify which lighthouse or buoy is sounding. The periodic nature of the signal is also a navigation aid: the time between blasts allows the mariner to measure bearing changes.
- **E=3 placement.** The fog horn is E=3: present, clearly audible at distance (effective range in fog: 5-10 miles for major lighthouse fog signals), periodic (not continuous), and moderately loud. Not as energetic as a waterfall or a storm, but significantly louder and more directional than the ambient maritime acoustic environment.
- **The moan quality.** The low frequency combined with the sustained duration of each blast produces the characteristic "moan" quality that gives fog horns their emotional resonance. A fog horn sounds like lamentation: the low note held for several seconds, then silence, then again. In literature and film, the fog horn sound is associated with isolation, danger, melancholy, and the sublime uncertainty of navigating in reduced visibility.
- **Ray Bradbury's "The Fog Horn" (1951).** Bradbury's short story -- in which a prehistoric sea creature is drawn to a lighthouse fog horn, mistaking it for the call of another of its kind -- is the cultural text that most fully articulates the fog horn's emotional content. The story is about isolation, longing, and the possibility of being utterly alone in a vast and ancient world. The fog horn is the sound of that aloneness.

(Sources: Fog horn frequency range 100-1000 Hz; optimal propagation 200-400 Hz; low frequency vs. high frequency atmospheric absorption; fog signal codes in US light lists; effective range 5-10 miles in fog; E=3 placement; moan quality; Ray Bradbury "The Fog Horn" 1951; fog horn in literature)

## PORT TOWNSEND AND THE MARITIME CULTURE OF ADMIRALTY INLET

### The Victorian Seaport

Port Townsend at Point Wilson is one of the Pacific Northwest's most significant maritime heritage cities:

- **Port Townsend's maritime history.** Port Townsend was founded in 1851 and grew as a customs port of entry -- one of the first points of arrival for vessels entering Puget Sound from the Pacific. In the 1880s, Port Townsend was expected to become the dominant city of the Pacific Northwest, with railroad connections planned that would have made it a major commercial center. The railroad never came; Seattle grew instead; Port Townsend declined and its Victorian commercial buildings were preserved by poverty rather than wealth.
- **Jefferson County and the fog.** Port Townsend and Jefferson County are at the receiving end of maritime fog systems. The Strait of Juan de Fuca is a major fog corridor: marine layer fog, generated over the cold Pacific, moves into the Strait from the west and can produce dense fog at Point Wilson and in Admiralty Inlet, particularly in late spring and summer. The fog horn history at Point Wilson reflects the frequency of these fog events.
- **The Port Townsend Wooden Boat Festival.** Port Townsend is home to the Northwest Maritime Center and the annual Port Townsend Wooden Boat Festival -- one of the largest wooden boat festivals in the United States. The festival draws thousands of visitors and hundreds of traditional wooden vessels. The maritime culture of Port Townsend is living, not merely historical.
- **The fog horn in the cultural landscape.** For residents of Port Townsend and the surrounding area, the fog horn at Point Wilson is part of the ambient sonic environment. Heard from shore, it is part of the town's identity: the sound of the harbor, the sound of the inlet, the sound of maritime function. This is the cultural embedding of the fog horn -- it is not just a safety device but a place-sound.

(Sources: Port Townsend founded 1851; customs port of entry; 1880s railroad expectations; Victorian commercial buildings; maritime fog in Strait of Juan de Fuca; marine layer fog in spring/summer; Northwest Maritime Center; Port Townsend Wooden Boat Festival; fog horn as cultural ambient sound)
