THE LOW PERIODIC MOAN — POINT WILSON — THE GATES OF PUGET SOUND

The fog horn at Admiralty Inlet operates at the threshold between safety and disaster. Point Wilson Lighthouse (established 1879, automated 1977) marks the critical navigational turning point where vessels must change course from the Strait of Juan de Fuca into Admiralty Inlet. In dense fog — the marine layer that moves in from the Pacific through the Strait, particularly late spring through summer — visibility can drop to near zero. The fog horn moans its warning: low, periodic, sustained. E=3. The maritime voice of the inlet.

Admiralty Inlet -- The Gates of Puget Sound

Admiralty Inlet is the primary entrance to Puget Sound from the Strait of Juan de Fuca: 3-5 miles wide, running north-south between Point Wilson (Port Townsend, Olympic Peninsula) and the southern tip of Whidbey Island (Admiralty Head, Fort Casey State Park). Tidal currents of 3-5 knots. All deep-draft vessel traffic entering or exiting Puget Sound must transit this channel: cargo ships for Seattle/Tacoma/Olympia, oil tankers for Anacortes refineries, Alaska Marine Highway ferries, Washington State Ferry vessels on the Keystone-Port Townsend crossing, commercial fishing vessels, recreational craft.

"The fog horn sounds like lamentation: the low note held for several seconds, then silence, then again. In maritime culture, this sound is inseparable from isolation, danger, and the uncertain navigation of invisible waters. Ray Bradbury understood this. The prehistoric creature in 'The Fog Horn' (1951) hears the horn and mistakes it for the call of another of its kind."

Fog Horn Technology

  • Steam fog horn (1850s-1920s): Shore-based steam plant drives air through a large horn. Audible for miles. The industrial age's maritime safety device.
  • Diaphone (patented Northey, 1907): Compressed air through slotted reciprocating piston. The characteristic two-tone blast with "grunt" at the end. The fog horn sound of cultural memory.
  • Tyfon / Typhon (early 20th century): Diaphragm vibrated by compressed air or steam. Single sustained note, less dramatic than diaphone.
  • Electric / Electronic (1970s-present): Electric air horn or electronic oscillator. Automated with Point Wilson lighthouse (1977). Triggers when visibility drops below specified threshold.

The Acoustics

Frequency range

100-1000 Hz, characteristic moan in 200-400 Hz range. Low frequency maximizes propagation through fog and turbulent air.

Effective range

5-10 miles in dense fog for major lighthouse signals. Audibility depends on wind direction, temperature inversion, and fog density.

Signal codes

Interval, duration, and number of blasts identify the station. Published in USCG light lists. A mariner can identify the lighthouse by the timing pattern.

E=3 placement

Present and clearly audible at distance. Periodic (not continuous). Moderate energy. Not as energetic as storm; louder and more directional than ambient maritime noise.

Port Townsend -- The Victorian Seaport

Port Townsend (founded 1851, customs port of entry) at Point Wilson is one of the most significant maritime heritage cities in the Pacific Northwest. Expected to be the dominant PNW city in the 1880s — the railroad connection was planned but never arrived; Seattle grew instead. Port Townsend's Victorian commercial buildings were preserved by economic stagnation rather than wealth. Today: Northwest Maritime Center, the annual Port Townsend Wooden Boat Festival (one of the largest wooden boat festivals in the US), and the ambient fog horn of Point Wilson as part of the town's sonic identity.
