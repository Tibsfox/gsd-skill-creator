## THE SOUND: DUCK-LIKE CLUCKING CHORUS -- 97th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Sound

The wood frog breeding chorus is a distinctive **duck-like clucking** [1, 2, 3]:

**The call:** Male wood frogs produce a rapid, low-pitched **clucking** sound that resembles the quacking of ducks. The call is a series of short, raspy notes: *cluck-cluck-cluck-cluck*. When dozens to hundreds of males call simultaneously from a vernal pool, the collective effect is remarkably duck-like -- listeners often mistake a wood frog chorus for a group of ducks.

**The acoustic character:** Raspy, percussive, rhythmic clucking. Not a high-pitched peep (spring peeper, 90) or a loud krek-krek (Pacific Treefrog, 86). A low, guttural, rapid clucking. Warm but rough-edged. The sound of early spring -- often the very first amphibian chorus of the year, calling while snow is still on the ground.

**Frequency:** Fundamental frequency approximately 1,000-2,500 Hz. Lower than the Pacific Treefrog and spring peeper. The duck-like quality comes from the rapid repetition rate and the raspy timbre.

### Why E=4

1. **CHORUS EFFECT -- dozens to hundreds calling simultaneously.** Breeding aggregations are loud and conspicuous.
2. **Audible from a distance.** A wood frog chorus carries well across woodland habitat.
3. **Higher than the mayfly (95, E=3) but lower than the hornet (93, E=5).** Active vocalization, not passive sound.
4. **Brief but intense.** Breeding season is compressed -- often just 1-2 weeks. All energy concentrated.
5. **Vernal pool amplification.** The small, enclosed pool acts as a natural amphitheater.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Mass wing buzz over water** (degree 95, Ephemeroptera) -- passive, diffuse, E=3. The duck-like clucking is active vocalization, rhythmic, E=4. Both water-associated; different sound generation.
- **Amplified krek-krek wall of sound** (degree 86, Pacific Treefrog) -- loud, high-pitched, E=4. The duck-like clucking is lower, raspier, duck-like. Both anuran choruses; different families, different frequencies.
- **Bell-like peep chorus** (degree 90, Spring Peeper-Like Chorus) -- high, bell-like, ethereal. The duck-like clucking is low, guttural, percussive. Both amphibian choruses; opposite registers.

## ECOLOGY: FREEZE TOLERANCE -- SURVIVING BEING FROZEN SOLID

### Freeze Tolerance

The wood frog is the engine's **first freeze-tolerant species** -- and one of the most remarkable survival strategies in vertebrate biology [1, 2, 4]:

- **Frozen solid:** In winter, wood frogs allow their bodies to freeze. Up to **65% of their body water** converts to ice.
- **Heart stops:** The heart stops beating. No breathing. No brain activity detectable. By all conventional measures, the frog appears dead.
- **Cryoprotectants:** The frog produces massive amounts of glucose (blood sugar rises to 100x normal levels) which acts as a cryoprotectant -- preventing ice crystals from destroying cells.
- **Ice forms between cells, not within them.** Extracellular ice formation is controlled; intracellular damage is prevented by glucose.
- **Thaws in spring:** When temperatures rise, the heart restarts, blood flows, the frog resumes normal activity. Within hours of thawing, wood frogs can hop to breeding pools.
- **Multiple freeze-thaw cycles:** Wood frogs can survive being frozen and thawed multiple times in a single winter.

This is not hibernation. This is not torpor. The frog is **frozen solid** -- crystallized, stopped, functionally dead -- and then it comes back.

### Vernal Pool Habitat

The **vernal pool** is the wood frog's breeding habitat [1, 2]:

- **Temporary seasonal pools** that fill with snowmelt and spring rain.
- **Fishless** -- because they dry completely by summer, no fish can establish. This makes them safe for amphibian eggs and larvae.
- **Woodland setting** -- surrounded by forest, which provides leaf litter, shade, and the moist microclimate wood frogs need.
- **Ephemeral** -- they exist for weeks to months, then vanish. The wood frog's entire larval development must complete before the pool dries.

The vernal pool is the first explicitly temporary habitat in the engine. Prior aquatic habitats (streams at 52/57/94, ponds at 58) were permanent. The vernal pool exists only in spring.

### Early Spring Breeding

Wood frogs are among the **very first amphibians to breed** each year [1, 2]:

- Often calling while **snow is still on the ground** and **ice is still on the pond**.
- Breeding season is extremely compressed -- often just **1-2 weeks**.
- Males arrive at vernal pools and begin calling immediately upon thawing.
- The duck-like clucking chorus is one of the earliest sounds of spring in northern forests.
