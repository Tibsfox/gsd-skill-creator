Freeze Tolerance — Survives Being Frozen Solid

The wood frog is the engine’s first freeze-tolerant species — one of the most remarkable survival strategies in vertebrate biology [1, 2, 4]. In winter, wood frogs allow their bodies to freeze solid. Up to 65% of body water converts to ice. The heart stops beating. No breathing. No brain activity. The frog produces massive amounts of glucose as cryoprotectant — blood sugar rises to 100x normal — preventing ice crystals from destroying cells. When spring arrives, the heart restarts, blood flows, and the frog hops to a breeding pool within hours. This is not hibernation. The frog is frozen solid and comes back.

NEW FAMILY Ranidae — 46th Family — True Frogs

Ranidae enters as the 46th family — true frogs [1, 2]. The second anuran family after Hylidae (tree frogs at degrees 86 and 90). The third amphibian entry. The engine returns to Amphibia after nine insect entries (87–95). Lithobates sylvaticus — the wood frog — is one of the most widespread frogs in North America and the northernmost-ranging North American frog.

Vernal Pool — First Temporary Habitat

The vernal pool is the wood frog’s breeding habitat — and the engine’s first temporary habitat [1, 2]. Temporary seasonal pools that fill with snowmelt and spring rain, then dry completely by summer. Because they dry, no fish can establish — making them safe havens for amphibian eggs and larvae. Woodland setting. Ephemeral. The wood frog’s entire larval development must complete before the pool vanishes.

The Sound: Duck-Like Clucking Chorus (97th Acoustic Element)

The call: Male wood frogs produce a rapid, low-pitched clucking sound that resembles duck quacking [1, 2, 3]. A series of short, raspy notes: cluck-cluck-cluck-cluck. When dozens to hundreds of males call simultaneously from a vernal pool, the collective effect is remarkably duck-like — listeners often mistake a wood frog chorus for a group of ducks.

Why E=4: Active vocalization from breeding aggregation. Dozens to hundreds calling simultaneously. Vernal pool amplification. Compressed breeding season (1–2 weeks) concentrates energy. Matches Pacific Treefrog (86, E=4) — both anuran breeding choruses at E=4.

Taxonomy: Ranidae (NEW FAMILY) / 46th Family

Classification: Class Amphibia. Order Anura (continuing). Family Ranidae — NEW FAMILY (46th) [1, 2]. True frogs. Second anuran family after Hylidae.

Etymology: Ranidae from Latin rana (“frog”). Lithobates from Greek lithos (“stone”) + bates (“one who treads”). Sylvaticus from Latin (“of the woods”). The stone-treading frog of the woods.

Ecology: Freeze Tolerance and Early Spring Breeding

Freeze tolerance: Up to 65% of body water as ice [4]. Heart stops. Cryoprotectant glucose at 100x normal levels. Ice forms between cells, not within them. Can survive multiple freeze-thaw cycles per winter. Thaws in spring and resumes normal activity within hours.

Early spring breeding: Among the very first amphibians to breed each year [1, 2]. Often calling while snow is still on the ground. Breeding season extremely compressed — often just 1–2 weeks. Males arrive at vernal pools and begin calling immediately upon thawing.

Physical description: Small to medium frog (35–75 mm). Tan, brown, or rust-colored. Distinctive dark “robber’s mask” across each eye [1]. Light dorsolateral ridges from eye to hip.
