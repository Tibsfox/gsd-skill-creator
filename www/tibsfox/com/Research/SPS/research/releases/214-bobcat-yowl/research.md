## THE BOBCAT -- THE ADAPTABLE PREDATOR

### The Other PNW Felid

The bobcat is the cougar's smaller, more abundant, more adaptable cousin:

- **Size comparison.** Bobcats weigh 7-14 kg (15-30 lbs) -- a fraction of the cougar's 50-100 kg. Body length 65-105 cm with a short "bobbed" tail of 10-18 cm (the feature that gives the species its common name). Despite the small size, bobcats are muscular, powerful for their weight, and fearless.
- **Abundance.** While Washington has ~2,000+ cougars, it has an estimated 20,000-30,000 bobcats. Bobcats are far more common but equally cryptic. Most PNW residents live near bobcats without knowing it.
- **Adaptability.** Bobcats are the most habitat-generalist felid in North America. Forest, brush, desert, swamp, suburban edge -- bobcats thrive anywhere with cover and prey. This adaptability makes them one of the most successful wild felids on the continent.
- **Lynx genus.** The bobcat belongs to the genus *Lynx*, which also includes the Canada Lynx (*Lynx canadensis*, degree 215), the Eurasian Lynx, and the Iberian Lynx. The bobcat is the smallest member of the genus and the most southerly in range.
- **Diet.** Rabbits, rodents, squirrels, and small birds. Occasionally takes larger prey -- young deer, domestic chickens. The bobcat hunts by stalking and pouncing, like a scaled-down cougar.

## THE YOWL -- RASPY AGGRESSION

### The Voice of the Smaller Cat

The bobcat yowl is distinctive and unmistakable:

- **The yowl.** A drawn-out, raspy, caterwauling vocalization -- "RRAAAAOWWW" -- lasting 1-3 seconds. Lower-pitched and coarser than the cougar scream. Frequency range: 400-1,500 Hz. The sound is rough, scratchy, aggressive. It sounds like a large angry house cat -- because house cats and bobcats are relatively close phylogenetically.
- **Mating season.** The yowl is most frequently heard during the bobcat's breeding season (January-March in the PNW). Males yowl to establish territory and attract females. Females may yowl in response. The mating chorus -- multiple bobcats yowling in sequence -- is one of the most memorable nighttime sounds in PNW brushland.
- **Territorial function.** Outside of mating season, yowls serve as territorial markers. Male bobcats have overlapping territories (15-100 km2 depending on habitat quality), and the yowl communicates presence and willingness to defend.
- **The house cat connection.** People who hear bobcat yowls often think "that sounds like a huge cat fight." They're not wrong. The house cat (*Felis catus*) and the bobcat share family Felidae and diverged ~10 million years ago. The vocal similarities are real -- the bobcat yowl is a scaled-up version of the same basic felid vocalization template.
- **Other sounds.** Bobcats also produce hisses (defensive), growls (warning), purrs (contentment -- smaller cats purr), and a distinctive short bark (alarm). The vocal repertoire is diverse but the yowl is the signature.

## BOBCAT VS. COUGAR -- THE TWO PNW FELIDS

### Size, Strategy, Sound

The comparison defines both species:

- **Size.** Cougar: 50-100 kg. Bobcat: 7-14 kg. The cougar is 4-10x heavier. Yet both are ambush predators using the same fundamental strategy: stalk, wait, pounce.
- **Prey.** Cougar: deer, elk (large ungulates). Bobcat: rabbits, rodents (small mammals). The prey size scales with predator size. Same strategy, different targets.
- **Habitat.** Cougar: deep forest, remote wilderness. Bobcat: everywhere -- forest, brush, suburban edge. The bobcat's adaptability gives it a wider distribution and higher population.
- **Vocalization.** Cougar scream: 500-2,500 Hz, piercing, E=7. Bobcat yowl: 400-1,500 Hz, raspy, E=5. The cougar is higher and more powerful. The bobcat is lower and coarser. Different acoustic signatures for different-sized animals in the same family.
- **Cryptic behavior.** Both are cryptic, but the cougar is a ghost. The bobcat is slightly more likely to be glimpsed -- suburban sightings are not uncommon. Still, most people will never see a wild bobcat despite living within their territory.
- **Felidae quartet position.** The bobcat at degree 214 is the third Felidae entry after the cougar scream (212) and chirp (213). The quartet structure: cougar scream (E=7), cougar chirp (E=4), bobcat yowl (E=5), then Canada Lynx (215).

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Bobcat Yowl

**Territorial/Mating Yowl:**
- **Frequency:** 400-1,500 Hz. Lower and coarser than cougar scream.
- **Character:** Raspy, drawn-out, caterwauling. "RRAAAAOWWW." Scratchy overtones.
- **Duration:** 1-3 seconds per yowl. May repeat in sequences during mating season.
- **Context:** Breeding season (Jan-Mar). Territorial disputes. Male advertisement.
- **Carry distance:** 200-500 meters. Less than cougar scream but sufficient for territory.

**Hiss (Defensive):**
- **Frequency:** Broad spectrum, 2,000-8,000 Hz.
- **Character:** Sharp, explosive exhale. Close-range warning.

**Growl (Warning):**
- **Frequency:** 100-500 Hz. Low, sustained rumble.
- **Character:** Threatening. Used in encounters with other predators or dogs.

**Short Bark (Alarm):**
- **Frequency:** 500-1,500 Hz. Single sharp vocalization.
- **Character:** Quick, percussive. Alert signal.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=5. Raspy, aggressive, territorial. Mid-range predator energy. Between the cougar's E=7 scream and E=4 chirp.
