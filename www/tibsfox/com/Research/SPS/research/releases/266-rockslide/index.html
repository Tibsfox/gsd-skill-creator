Cascade Geology: Young Mountains, Constant Erosion

The Cascades are built on Juan de Fuca Plate subduction — ongoing tectonic uplift opposed by continuous erosion. Volcanic rock fractured along cooling joints creates natural failure planes. Freeze-thaw cycles widen fractures through winter; spring melt releases the failures. Canyon walls throughout the range are inherently unstable: exposed faces of slopes that erosion has undercut. Rockslides are not rare events in the PNW — they are routine features of the mountain landscape.

The Acoustic Event: Accelerating Stone-Fall Percussion

A rockslide’s acoustic signature evolves in phases. The initial impact is one or a few rock blocks striking cliff face below — sharp, dry, percussive. Higher frequency than glacial ice fracture. The rock impact has a hard, dense quality: this is stone striking stone.

Then the acceleration phase: falling rocks hit the slope below, bounce, and break apart. Each fragment follows its own trajectory; impacts multiply rapidly as fracture multiplies the number of acoustic events. The percussion accelerates — the time between impacts decreases as more fragments impact simultaneously. This acceleration is the defining character of the rockslide’s acoustic signature.

At the height of a substantial rockslide, the event is continuous percussion — overlapping impacts varying in intensity as different-sized fragments strike different surfaces. Large blocks: deep, resonant. Small fragments: higher-frequency clatter. Gravity as drummer, the canyon as acoustic chamber.

Spring Peak: When Rockslides Happen

Spring is the season of maximum rockslide frequency. Winter’s freeze-thaw has prepared many rock masses for failure: water penetrates fractures, freezes (expanding ~9% in volume, forcing fractures wider), and withdraws. After enough cycles, a rock mass that had been stable suddenly has no support remaining. Spring melt lubricates these prepared failure planes with water. March through May in the Cascades sees the highest frequency of significant rockslide events. WSDOT’s SR-20 (North Cascades Highway) reopening process each spring regularly involves clearing rockslide debris from the road.

Documented Terrain

US-2 through the Skykomish Canyon on the west side of Stevens Pass runs through steep canyon walls prone to rockfall — rockfall nets and catchment ditches are visible along this corridor. SR-20 through the North Cascades has documented rockslide history at multiple locations. The Skagit River Canyon, Icicle Creek Canyon near Leavenworth, and the moraine terrain around Mt Rainier’s glaciers all have active rockfall histories. These are not abstract geological facts — they are events that close highways, alter river channels, and occasionally affect people.
