## CASCADE GEOLOGY: WHY ROCKSLIDES HAPPEN HERE

### Young Mountains, Active Tectonics, Constant Erosion

The Cascades are geologically young and dynamically active:

- **Tectonic context.** The Cascade Range was built primarily by volcanism related to the subduction of the Juan de Fuca Plate beneath the North American Plate. This ongoing subduction produces both volcanic activity (Mt Rainier, Mt St Helens, Mt Baker, Glacier Peak) and uplift. The mountains are being raised by tectonic forces while simultaneously being eroded by water, ice, and gravity. This dynamic tension between uplift and erosion is the fundamental reason for the high frequency of mass-movement events.
- **The rock types.** Cascade bedrock is a complex mixture of volcanic rock (basalt, andesite, rhyolite), intrusive rock (granite, granodiorite), and metamorphic rock, depending on the location. Volcanic rocks tend to be fractured along cooling joints -- these joint systems create natural fracture planes along which rockslides preferentially fail. The jointing controls the geometry of rockfall blocks and the preferred failure planes.
- **Freeze-thaw cycles.** Much of the Cascades spends winter well below freezing. Water penetrates rock fractures and freezes, expanding by approximately 9% in volume. This ice wedging progressively widens fractures, eventually reaching the point where the rock mass on one side of the fracture loses support and falls. Spring thaw releases the failures that freeze-thaw prepared through winter. Spring is the season of maximum rockslide frequency.
- **The canyon environment.** Many Cascade rivers run through steep-walled canyons -- the Skykomish, the Sauk, the Skagit, the Wenatchee. Canyon walls are inherently unstable: they represent the exposed faces of slopes that erosion has undercut. Canyon wall rockslides are common, with debris falling into river channels, sometimes temporarily blocking them, always altering the river's morphology.
- **Highway corridors.** Washington State's mountain highway corridors -- US-2 (Stevens Pass), US-12 (White Pass), SR-20 (North Cascades Highway) -- run through rockslide-prone terrain. WSDOT maintains rockfall nets, catchment ditches, and monitoring systems at the most hazardous locations. Highway closures due to rockslides are a routine feature of winter and spring in the Cascades.

(Sources: Juan de Fuca subduction; Cascade Range geology; volcanic rock joint systems; freeze-thaw weathering mechanics; Cascade canyon geology; WSDOT highway corridor rockslide documentation)

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT: ACCELERATING STONE-FALL PERCUSSION

### From First Strike to Full Cascade

A rockslide's acoustic signature evolves from single impact through full cascade:

- **The initial impact.** A rockslide typically begins with one or a few initial rock failures -- blocks detaching from a cliff face and falling. The first impact against the cliff face below or the talus slope creates a sharp percussive crack. This is distinct from the first crack of a glacier calving event: rock impact is higher in frequency and more percussive than ice fracture. The initial rock impact has a dry, hard quality.
- **The acceleration phase.** As falling rocks hit the slope below, they bounce and break apart, each fragment following its own trajectory and creating its own impact. The impacts multiply rapidly -- two rocks become four become eight as fracture multiplies the number of acoustic events. The percussion accelerates: the time between impacts decreases as more fragments impact simultaneously. This acceleration is the defining character of a rockslide's acoustic signature.
- **Full percussion.** At the height of a substantial rockslide, the acoustic event is continuous percussion -- overlapping impacts at high frequency, varying in intensity as different-sized fragments strike different surfaces. Large blocks produce deep, resonant impacts; small fragments produce higher-frequency clatter. The full rockslide is a percussion instrument being played by gravity.
- **The cascade and tail.** After the main mass has fallen, smaller fragments continue to dislodge and fall in the rockslide's aftermath. Individual rocks and clatter continue for seconds to minutes. The acoustic tail is characteristic: periodic individual impacts against a background of settling debris and fading echoes.
- **Valley and cliff amplification.** Like glacier calving, rockslide acoustics are shaped by terrain. Canyon walls reflect and amplify the percussive events. A rockslide in a narrow canyon produces more reverberant, enclosed sound than the same event on an open slope. Echo from opposing canyon walls creates a complex acoustic environment.
- **E=7 energy level.** Seven is one step below the engine maximum (E=8). A rockslide is intensely percussive and dramatic -- genuinely overwhelming if you are in the canyon when it happens -- but it lacks the wall-of-sound intensity of the highest-energy events. E=7 is the appropriate level: powerful, striking, memorable, but not the absolute ceiling.

## NOTABLE CASCADE ROCKSLIDE TERRAIN

### Documented Locations

Several Cascade locations have particularly significant rockslide histories:

- **North Cascades Highway (SR-20).** The SR-20 corridor through the North Cascades closes every winter and reopens in spring. The reopening process regularly involves clearing rockslide debris from the highway. Newhalem Creek, Skagit Canyon sections near Colonial Creek, and the high passes all have documented rockslide histories.
- **US-2 (Stevens Pass).** The US-2 corridor through the Cascades via Stevens Pass runs through steep canyon walls on both the west and east sides. The valley of the South Fork Skykomish River on the west side is particularly rockslide-prone, with several documented closure events.
- **Skagit River Canyon.** The Skagit River runs through one of the Cascades' deepest canyons before reaching Puget Sound. Canyon walls throughout this reach are subject to rockfall events, some of which affect river navigation or shoreline camping areas.
- **Icicle Canyon (Leavenworth).** The Icicle Creek drainage near Leavenworth is popular for rock climbing on Cascadian granite. Rockfall is a documented hazard in this area and is actively monitored by climbing guide services.
- **Mt Rainier moraine terrain.** The moraine terrain around Mt Rainier's glaciers is inherently unstable -- a mix of glacially transported rock debris and steepened slopes. Rockfall events at Rainier's margins are common and are documented by NPS rangers.

(Sources: WSDOT SR-20 corridor documentation; US-2 Skykomish Canyon; Skagit River Canyon geology; Icicle Canyon climbing hazards; Mt Rainier moraine instability)

## FREQUENCY AND PATTERNS

### When Rockslides Happen

Unlike a single-event geophysical phenomenon, rockslides are part of a continuous erosion process:

- **Episodic vs. continuous.** The rockslide as a dramatic event (large mass failure, acoustic impact, highway closure) is episodic. But the underlying process -- freeze-thaw weathering widening fractures, erosion undercutting slopes, gravity slowly winning -- is continuous. The dramatic rockslide is just the visible, audible expression of a process that never stops.
- **Spring peak.** Spring is the season of maximum rockslide frequency in the Cascades. Winter's freeze-thaw has prepared many rock masses for failure; spring melt lubricates fracture planes with water. March through May sees the highest frequency of significant rockslide events.
- **Rain-on-snow events.** Winter rain-on-snow events -- when warm fronts deliver rainfall to snowpack-covered terrain -- are particularly effective at triggering both rockslides and debris flows. The water percolates through snowpack and saturates soil and rock fractures simultaneously, often producing cascading failures at multiple scales.
- **Seismic triggering.** The Cascadia Subduction Zone and the region's background seismicity provide an additional rockslide trigger. Even moderate earthquakes can shake loose rock masses that are otherwise at the margin of stability. Post-earthquake rockslide events are documented throughout Cascade history.

(Sources: Freeze-thaw seasonal patterns; spring peak rockslide frequency; rain-on-snow event dynamics; seismic triggering; Washington State earthquake-landslide connections)

## THE MASS-MOVEMENT TRILOGY: 265-267

### Three Degrees, Three Materials, One Arc

Degrees 265-267 form the engine's mass-movement trilogy:

- **265: Glacier calving (ice).** Ice fracture. Sharp crack. Rumble cascade in alpine terrain. E=6.
- **266: Rockslide (rock).** Stone-fall percussion. Accelerating cascade. Canyon amplification. E=7.
- **267: Mudslide (saturated soil).** Deep rumbling wall movement. Maximum energy for the arc. E=8.

The energy escalates from ice to rock to saturated soil -- but this energy order reflects acoustic character, not necessarily physical scale. A major rockslide may move more mass than a small mudslide, but the acoustic intensity of a major mudslide's wall of saturated earth in motion is overwhelming in a way that even a large rockslide is not.

The three events also represent three different timescales: glacier calving is essentially instantaneous; a rockslide unfolds over seconds; a major mudslide may unfold over tens of seconds to minutes as the mass moves across terrain. The increasing duration is part of the acoustic character difference.
