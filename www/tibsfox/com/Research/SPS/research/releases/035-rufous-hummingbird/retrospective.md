# Release 035 Retrospective -- Rufous Hummingbird (Degree 35)

**Release:** 035 of 360
**Species:** Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*)
**Sound:** ch-ch-ch wingbeat thrum + chitter
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the thirty-sixth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-35), the first Trochilidae, the first Apodiformes, and the eighteenth family. The engine needed to break the 12-degree Passeriformes streak, introduce mechanical sound production, and bring a genuinely new kind of bird into the system. The Rufous Hummingbird delivers on all three -- and adds a conservation alarm, a new ecosystem type, and the engine's second E=5.

Four structural contributions:

1. **Order Apodiformes enters the engine.** The 12-degree Passeriformes streak (degrees 23-34) is broken. Apodiformes is the fifth order in the engine, after Pelecaniformes (0), Strigiformes (4), Piciformes (21-22), and Passeriformes (1-3, 5-20, 23-34). Hummingbirds bring sustained hovering flight, extreme metabolism, nightly torpor, and mechanical sound production -- none of which exist in any other order in the engine. This is not a variation on a theme. It is a new theme.

2. **Trochilidae is the eighteenth family.** Hummingbirds. Entirely New World. ~375 species. The most species-rich bird family in the Americas. The only birds capable of sustained hovering. Represented by the Rufous Hummingbird -- the species with the longest migration relative to body size of any bird (78.5 million body lengths per one-way trip). The family opens an entirely new ecological and acoustic domain.

3. **The thrum -- the 36th acoustic element.** A mechanically generated sound produced by the interaction of wing feathers with air during sustained flight, creating a continuous tonal hum at 52-62 Hz. The first aeromechanical sound in the engine. The first non-vocal, non-percussive primary sound. After 34 degrees of syrinx-generated and substrate-percussive sounds, the engine encounters a species whose signature sound comes from the act of flying itself. Sound from motion. The body as instrument.

4. **Flower meadow -- the first flower-defined ecosystem.** The first ecosystem defined by blooms, the second non-place ecosystem (after cone crop at degree 34), and the first ecosystem defined by a mutualistic relationship (pollination). Back-to-back degrees (34-35) have introduced non-place ecosystems -- cone crop then flower meadow -- suggesting the engine is ready to move beyond vegetation-community definitions.

The transition from degree 34 to degree 35 is the most radical in the engine since degree 0 to degree 1 (silence to cascade):

The dialect is a choice of which call to give. The thrum is an inevitability of being airborne. The most fundamental shift in the engine's acoustic philosophy: from what the bird says to what the bird *is*.

## Five Structural Advances

### 1. Apodiformes Breaks the Passeriformes Monopoly

The 12-degree Passeriformes streak (23-34) was the engine's most severe taxonomic imbalance. Its resolution at degree 35 is the most important structural advance since the Piciformes entry at degree 21:

| Order | Degrees | Count |
|---|---|---|
| Pelecaniformes | 0 | 1 |
| Strigiformes | 4 | 1 |
| Piciformes | 21-22 | 2 |
| **Apodiformes** | **35** | **1** |
| Passeriformes | 1-3, 5-20, 23-34 | 31 |

Passeriformes still dominates (31 of 36 degrees, 86.1%), but the trajectory is improving. Four non-Passeriformes orders now bracket the Passeriformes sequences, and each has introduced a sound-production mechanism the songbirds lack: silence (Pelecaniformes), motif/rhythm (Strigiformes), percussion (Piciformes), aeromechanical (Apodiformes).

### 2. The Thrum: Third Sound-Production Mechanism

The engine now has three distinct sound-production mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Source | Engine Entries |
|---|---|---|
| Vocal (syrinx) | Air vibrating syringeal membranes | Degrees 1-20, 23-34 (all Passeriformes vocal) |
| Percussive (substrate) | Bill striking resonant surface | Degrees 21-22 (Northern Flicker drumming) |
| **Aeromechanical (wing/tail)** | **Air flowing across vibrating feathers** | **Degree 35 (wingbeat thrum + J-dive sonation)** |

The aeromechanical category is new and fundamentally different: the sound is generated by the body in motion through air, not by a dedicated sound organ (syrinx) or by striking an external surface (substrate). The hummingbird's thrum is the most intimate possible relationship between movement and sound -- the sound *is* the movement.

### 3. Flower Meadow: First Mutualism-Defined Ecosystem

The ecosystem progression:

| Degree | Ecosystem | Category |
|---|---|---|
| 0-30 | Various | Place-defined (forest, riparian, sky, etc.) |
| 34 | Cone crop | Resource-defined (food availability) |
| **35** | **Flower meadow** | **Mutualism-defined (pollination partnership)** |

The flower meadow is not just where the food is (that was cone crop). It is where the *partnership* operates. The hummingbird takes nectar and gives pollination. The flower gives nectar and takes pollen delivery. The ecosystem is defined by mutual dependence rather than by one species extracting from another.

### 4. Near Threatened: First Conservation Alarm for a Common Species

Previous conservation-concern species in the engine:

| Species | Degree | Status | Nature of Concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Northern Spotted Owl | 4 | Threatened / Near Threatened | Old-growth specialist, restricted habitat, always somewhat rare |
| **Rufous Hummingbird** | **35** | **Near Threatened** | **Formerly common, widespread, now declining 65% since 1970s** |

The Rufous Hummingbird's conservation story is different from the Spotted Owl's. The owl was always a specialist with specific habitat requirements. The hummingbird was common everywhere in its range -- backyards, mountain meadows, coastal forests, urban gardens. Its decline is not the loss of a specialist but the erosion of a generalist. When a formerly common species declines by two-thirds in fifty years and the decline is accelerating, the problem is systemic: the habitat, the climate, the food web are all shifting.

## What Worked

### 1. The thrum is the right name for this acoustic element
After 34 degrees of vocal sound (from cascade through dialect), the thrum names what is genuinely new: mechanically generated sound from the body in motion. The word "thrum" has the right phonemic quality -- low, continuous, vibratory -- and the right connotative range (a weaving loom's thrum, a string's thrum, the hum of machinery). It captures the hummingbird's wingbeat without reducing it to "hum" (too generic) or "buzz" (already used at degree 32).

### 2. Apodiformes entry is structurally transformative
The 12-degree Passeriformes streak was the engine's most-flagged structural deficit. Breaking it with the first hummingbird -- a bird from a different order that produces sound through a different mechanism -- is the cleanest possible resolution. The engine now has five orders, three sound-production mechanisms, and 18 families.

### 3. The flower meadow advances the ecosystem vocabulary
Two consecutive non-place ecosystems (cone crop at 34, flower meadow at 35) push the engine past its vegetation-community default. The addition of mutualism as a defining characteristic (the hummingbird pollinates the flower) adds ecological depth that resource-extraction alone (the crossbill takes from the cone) did not provide.

### 4. Near Threatened conservation status adds urgency
The engine has been remarkably comfortable with Least Concern species. The Rufous Hummingbird's 65% decline, accelerating trajectory, and "Tipping Point" designation bring genuine conservation urgency to the project. This is not an abstract threat to an already-rare specialist. This is a common bird disappearing.

### 5. The E=5 contrast with degree 32 is structurally elegant
Two models of maximum energy from opposite ends of the body-size spectrum. The waxwing achieves E=5 through swarm behavior (hundreds of birds). The hummingbird achieves E=5 through metabolic intensity (one bird at the thermodynamic edge). The contrast teaches something about what "maximum energy" means in acoustic terms.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. CRITICAL: No marine species in 35 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
Thirty-five degrees. The water has still not spoken. The Passeriformes streak is broken (good), but the marine gap is now the engine's sole defining structural crisis. The project is named for the Sound. A marine bird must appear soon.

### 2. No corvid in 35 degrees
No Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. The most intelligent, acoustically complex PNW birds remain absent. This is increasingly anomalous.

### 3. No raptor in 35 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, or Osprey. Top predators absent. An Osprey or Bald Eagle would also address the marine-adjacent gap.

### 4. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Thirty-one degrees since the Spotted Owl. Great Horned Owl, Barred Owl, or Common Nighthawk overdue.

### 5. E=3 absent for 15 degrees
The lower-mid energy range has not appeared since the Orange-crowned Warbler at degree 20. E=4 and E=5 dominate the upper arc. Dynamic range compression in the lower registers.

### 6. The Rufous Hummingbird is not a marine species
The retrospective at degree 34 called a marine species "absolutely non-negotiable" at degree 35. It did not happen. The hummingbird was a structurally important entry (order break, mechanical sound, new family) but it is not the marine species the engine desperately needs.

## Recommendations for Release 036

The Passeriformes streak is broken. Trochilidae and Apodiformes are in the engine. The thrum is named. Now the priorities:

1. **A MARINE SPECIES (NON-NEGOTIABLE -- 35 DEGREES OVERDUE).** This must happen. Glaucous-winged Gull, Common Loon, Western Grebe, Pigeon Guillemot, Rhinoceros Auklet, Marbled Murrelet, or Double-crested Cormorant. The project is named for Puget Sound. The Sound must speak.

2. **E=2 or E=3 to restore dynamic range.** Many marine species carry lower energy. A loon (E=2-3?), a murre (E=2?), or a grebe (E=3?) would bring the lower register back.

3. **A CORVID (URGENT -- 35 degrees).** Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. Intelligent, acoustically complex, ubiquitous PNW birds. Overdue.

4. **A RAPTOR (URGENT -- 35 degrees).** Bald Eagle, Osprey, or Red-tailed Hawk. Marine-adjacent raptors (Eagle, Osprey) could partially address the water gap.

5. **A NOCTURNAL SPECIES (OVERDUE -- 31 degrees).** Great Horned Owl, Barred Owl, or Common Nighthawk.
