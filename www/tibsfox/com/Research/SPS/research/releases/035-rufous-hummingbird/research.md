## Context: The Order Breaks

Everything changes at degree 35.

The Red Crossbill (degree 34) closed the Fringillidae pair and completed the four-degree arc of whisper-buzz-cadenza-dialect. That arc -- degrees 31 through 34 -- was the engine's densest structural sequence: four consecutive degrees, three families, four acoustic elements, each introducing a new relationship between sound and social organization. But it was still Passeriformes. Every degree from 23 through 34 has been Passeriformes. Twelve consecutive. The retrospective at degree 34 called it EXTREME. The retrospective at degree 33 called it CRITICAL. The retrospective at degree 32 called it a crisis.

Now the streak ends. Not with a marine species (that gap persists -- 35 degrees and counting), not with a raptor (also absent), not with a corvid (still missing). The streak ends with something more fundamental than any of those: a bird from a different order entirely. **Order Apodiformes.** The hummingbirds. The first Apodiformes in the engine. The first non-Passeriformes since degree 0.

The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) was Pelecaniformes. Degrees 1 through 20 were Passeriformes. Degrees 21 and 22 were Piciformes (Northern Flicker). Degrees 23 through 34 were Passeriformes again. And now, at degree 35, the engine enters Apodiformes -- the order that contains hummingbirds and swifts, an order whose relationship to sound is fundamentally mechanical. Hummingbirds do not just sing. They *thrum*. Their wings generate sound as a physical byproduct of flight, and evolution has shaped that sound into a signal. The wingbeat is not vocal. It is aerodynamic. The sound does not come from the syrinx. It comes from air pushed through feathers at 52-62 beats per second.

This is the engine's **first mechanical sound production.** Every previous species has produced sound vocally (syrinx-generated calls and songs) or percussively (woodpecker drumming on resonant substrates, degree 21-22). The Rufous Hummingbird's wingbeat thrum is neither vocal nor percussive. It is **aeromechanical** -- sound generated by the interaction of wing feathers with air during sustained flight. The chitter that accompanies it is vocal (syrinx-generated), but the thrum is a new sound-production category: the body itself as instrument, the flight feathers as tone generators, the wingbeat frequency as pitch.

And this bird arrives at **E=5**. Only the second time both species and artist energy are at maximum. The Bohemian Waxwing (degree 32) was the first E=5. Now the Rufous Hummingbird matches it -- and surpasses it in ferocity per gram. A bird that weighs 3 grams and attacks hawks.

**Family Trochilidae.** The eighteenth family in the engine. Hummingbirds. Entirely New World. Approximately 375 species. The most species-rich bird family in the Americas. And now represented in the engine by the species with the longest migration relative to body size of any bird on Earth.

## Taxonomy: The Flame-Bearer

### Classification

- **Order:** Apodiformes
- **Family:** Trochilidae (Hummingbirds)
- **Genus:** *Selasphorus* (Swainson, 1832)
- **Species:** *Selasphorus rufus* (Gmelin, 1788)
- **Common Name:** Rufous Hummingbird

### Etymology

*Selasphorus* derives from Ancient Greek: *selas* ("light, brightness, flame") + *phoros* ("carrying, bearing") = **"flame-bearer"** or **"torch-bearer"** [1, 2]. The genus name evokes the iridescent gorget -- the throat patch that blazes copper-orange when light catches it at the right angle, as though the bird carries fire. William Swainson described the genus in 1832, naming what he saw: a bird that bears light.

*Rufus* is Latin for "red" or "reddish-brown" -- the warm copper-orange that suffuses the male's body plumage, flanks, and tail [1, 2]. *Selasphorus rufus* translates to **"red flame-bearer"** or **"rufous torch-bearer"** -- both the genus and species name describe light and color, the incandescence of this 3-gram bird.

Compare with the engine's naming patterns: *Loxia curvirostra* (degree 34) names morphology twice. *Haemorhous cassinii* (degree 33) names color and a person. *Selasphorus rufus* names light and color -- the most luminous binomial in the engine. The name is visual: you see the bird before you hear it. This is fitting for a species whose most famous sound is generated by the visual spectacle of wingbeats.

### Monotypic

The Rufous Hummingbird is considered monotypic -- no subspecies are recognized [1, 2]. This contrasts with the Red Crossbill's extraordinary type system (10+ call types, one split as a species). The Rufous Hummingbird has no such cryptic diversity. It is one species across its entire range, from Alaska to Mexico, from the Pacific Coast to the Rocky Mountains. The Allen's Hummingbird (*Selasphorus sasin*) is its closest relative, and the two hybridize in a narrow zone in southern Oregon and northern California, but they remain separate species [1, 2, 3].

### Apodiformes: A New Order

Apodiformes is the order containing hummingbirds (Trochilidae, ~375 species), swifts (Apodidae, ~113 species), and treeswifts (Hemiprocnidae, 4 species) [4]. The order name derives from Greek *a-* ("without") + *pous* ("foot") -- "footless" -- because swifts were once believed to have no feet (they do, but their feet are tiny and adapted for clinging rather than perching) [4].

The order's relationship to the engine is significant:

- **Apodiformes are not Passeriformes.** This breaks the 12-degree streak (23-34) and the longer pattern of Passeriformes dominance. Only three non-Passeriformes entries exist in the engine: Great Blue Heron (0, Pelecaniformes), Northern Flicker pair (21-22, Piciformes), and now Rufous Hummingbird (35, Apodiformes). Three orders in 36 degrees, against Passeriformes' 32.
- **Apodiformes are not songbirds.** They lack the complex syrinx of oscine passerines. Hummingbird vocalizations are relatively simple compared to warblers, thrushes, or finches. But they compensate with **mechanical sound production** -- wingbeat-generated and tail-feather-generated sounds that no passerine produces.
- **Hummingbirds are the only birds that can sustain hovering flight.** Their wing anatomy, flight musculature, and metabolic rate are unlike any other bird order. They are flying machines that happen to be birds.

### Trochilidae: The Eighteenth Family

Trochilidae enters the engine as the **eighteenth family**. The family is entirely New World -- hummingbirds evolved in South America approximately 22 million years ago and diversified explosively, producing approximately 375 species in 113 genera [1, 4, 5]. They range from Alaska (*Selasphorus rufus*, our species) to Tierra del Fuego. The greatest diversity is in the equatorial Andes, where dozens of species can occur in a single mountain valley, each specialized on different flower altitudes and corolla shapes.

In the Pacific Northwest, four hummingbird species occur regularly [1, 6]:

- **Rufous Hummingbird** (*Selasphorus rufus*): Common summer breeder, most widespread PNW hummingbird, arrives March-April, departs July-September.
- **Anna's Hummingbird** (*Calypte anna*): Year-round resident in western Washington and Oregon, expanding northward. The only winter hummingbird in the PNW.
- **Calliope Hummingbird** (*Selasphorus calliope*): Summer breeder in mountain meadows, smallest bird in North America.
- **Black-chinned Hummingbird** (*Archilochus alexandri*): Summer breeder east of the Cascades.

The Rufous is the most common and widespread of these in the PNW during summer, and it is the most aggressive -- defending flower patches and feeders against all comers, including birds many times its size [1, 6].

## The Sound: Wingbeat Thrum + Chitter

### The Mechanical Voice -- Wingbeat Thrum

The Rufous Hummingbird's wingbeat thrum is the engine's **first aeromechanical sound** -- sound generated by the physics of flight itself, not by the syrinx or by percussion on a substrate [1, 7, 8].

The mechanics:

- **Wingbeat frequency:** 52-62 beats per second during normal hovering flight [1, 2]. This is faster than the human eye can resolve -- the wings appear as a blur, and the sound they generate is perceived as a continuous hum or thrum.
- **Sound generation:** As the wings beat, air flows across the flight feathers. The narrowed tips of certain feathers vibrate at frequencies determined by feather shape, stiffness, and airspeed. The result is a **tonal hum** with a fundamental frequency in the range of 52-62 Hz and harmonics extending into the audible range [7, 8].
- **Pitch control:** Hummingbirds can modulate their wingbeat frequency, changing the pitch and loudness of the wing hum. Both sexes perform "Cobra" maneuvers -- rapid changes in wingbeat frequency accompanied by changes in body position -- that create conspicuous shifts in the pitch and amplitude of the wing sound [7, 8]. The wing hum is not passive noise; it is a controllable signal.
- **Species-specific quality:** Rufous Hummingbirds produce more of a hum with their wings than most hummingbird species, with the exception of the Broad-tailed Hummingbird, which produces a distinctive high-pitched metallic trill from its wing feathers [7].

The wingbeat thrum is categorically different from every previous sound in the engine:

| Sound Type | Mechanism | Examples in Engine |
|---|---|---|
| Vocal (syrinx) | Air vibrating syringeal membranes | Wren cascade, thrush drone, warbler songs |
| Percussive (substrate) | Bill striking resonant surface | Flicker drumming (degrees 21-22) |
| **Aeromechanical (wing)** | **Air flowing across vibrating feathers** | **Rufous Hummingbird wingbeat thrum (degree 35)** |

This is the third sound-production mechanism in the engine, after vocal and percussive. The wingbeat thrum is involuntary in the sense that any flying hummingbird generates it, but voluntary in the sense that the bird controls its intensity, pitch, and timing through wingbeat frequency modulation.

### The J-Dive -- Mechanical Display Sound

The male Rufous Hummingbird's courtship display elevates mechanical sound production to its most dramatic expression [1, 7, 8, 9]:

1. The male ascends to a height of approximately 10-30 meters above a perched female.
2. He dives steeply -- nearly vertically -- reaching speeds that may exceed 25 m/s [NEEDS VERIFICATION for Rufous specifically; 27.3 m/s documented for related species].
3. At the bottom of the dive, as he pulls up in a J-shaped arc, air rushes across specialized tail feathers, producing a sharp mechanical buzz or chirp -- a **sonation** (sound produced by non-vocal structures) [7, 9].
4. The male may also vocalize a rapid "chu-chu-chu" at the nadir of the dive [7, 8].
5. The display synchronizes speed, sound, and the flash of iridescent gorget color in approximately 300 milliseconds [9].

The J-dive sound is entirely mechanical. No syrinx involved. The tail feathers are the instrument. The dive speed is the bow. The air is the medium. This is sound as a byproduct of aerodynamic display -- the bird's body becomes a musical instrument through the physics of its own flight.

### The Chitter -- Vocal Territory Defense

The vocal component of the Rufous Hummingbird's sound repertoire is simpler than any passerine's but no less aggressive [1, 7, 8]:

- **Chitter/chip call:** A rapid series of sharp, buzzy chip notes -- "ch-ch-ch-ch-ch" or "ze-chuppity-chup" -- given during aggressive territorial encounters [1, 7]. This is the sound of a 3-gram bird telling a hawk to leave.
- **Warning calls:** Both males and females produce fast series of warning chip notes at intruding birds [7, 8]. The calls are harsh, buzzy, and insistent.
- **Frequency range:** The vocal chitter occupies a broad frequency band, approximately 3-8 kHz, with harsh, broadband spectral energy rather than the pure tones of passerine song [7, 8].
- **Context:** Almost exclusively aggressive. The Rufous Hummingbird does not have a complex courtship song in the passerine sense. Its vocal repertoire is dominated by territorial aggression -- the chitter is a weapon, not a serenade.

### The Dual Sound: Mechanical + Vocal

The Rufous Hummingbird's complete acoustic identity combines:

1. **Wingbeat thrum** (mechanical, continuous during flight, 52-62 Hz fundamental with audible harmonics)
2. **Chitter** (vocal, syrinx-generated, aggressive territorial defense, 3-8 kHz)
3. **J-dive sonation** (mechanical, tail-feather-generated, courtship display, brief and dramatic)

This is the first species in the engine with a **mechanical primary sound.** The Red Crossbill (degree 34) had a dual voice (flight call + song), but both voices were vocal. The Rufous Hummingbird has a dual voice where one component is not a voice at all -- it is the sound of flight itself.

### The 36th Acoustic Element: The Thrum

After the dialect (degree 34) -- the population-level vocal marker that separates incipient species -- the Rufous Hummingbird introduces **the thrum**: a mechanically generated sound produced by the physical interaction of body structures (wing feathers) with air, creating a continuous tonal hum that is both byproduct of flight and controllable signal.

The dialect is vocal. The thrum is mechanical. The dialect comes from the syrinx. The thrum comes from the wing. The dialect is discrete (a series of sharp kip notes). The thrum is continuous (an unbroken hum at 52-62 Hz). The dialect separates populations. The thrum *is* the individual -- the sound of this body, this wingbeat, this flight, inseparable from the act of being airborne.

**The progression: whisper (31) --> buzz (32) --> cadenza (33) --> dialect (34) --> thrum (35).** The engine has moved through five consecutive degrees that each introduce a fundamentally new relationship between sound and its source:

- Whisper = sound at the threshold of perception (waxwing flock)
- Buzz = sound amplified by numbers (waxwing swarm)
- Cadenza = sound as individual artistry with borrowed material (finch solo)
- Dialect = sound as group identity marker (crossbill type call)
- **Thrum = sound as mechanical byproduct of flight, shaped by evolution into signal (hummingbird wingbeat)**

The thrum is a new *category* of acoustic element. Every previous element has been about how sound is used (whisper, buzz, dialect) or how sound is structured (cadenza, cascade, harmony). The thrum is about how sound is *made* -- not by a voice organ but by the body in motion. After 34 degrees of vocal and percussive sound, the engine encounters a species that generates its most characteristic sound by simply flying.

## Ecology: The Flower Meadow

### The Flower Meadow Ecosystem

The ecosystem designation "flower meadow" is the engine's **first flower-defined ecosystem** -- a habitat characterized not by its trees, water, or elevation but by its blooms [1, 6, 10].

The Rufous Hummingbird's ecology revolves around nectar:

- **Primary food source:** Nectar from tubular flowers, supplemented by small insects caught in flight (for protein) [1, 6].
- **Preferred flower types:** Tubular, brightly colored (especially red, orange, pink) flowers with copious nectar production. In the PNW, these include red columbine (*Aquilegia formosa*), Indian paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.), scarlet gilia (*Ipomopsis aggregata*), salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*), and penstemons (*Penstemon* spp.) [1, 6, 10].
- **Nectar-flower coevolution:** Hummingbirds and their nectar plants have coevolved for millions of years. Tubular flowers are shaped to fit hummingbird bills and exclude insect pollinators. The flowers provide energy-rich nectar; the hummingbird provides pollination [5, 10]. This is one of the most tightly coupled pollination mutualisms in the temperate zone.
- **Garden flowers:** In urban and suburban areas, Rufous Hummingbirds readily visit fuchsia, bee balm, salvia, and other garden plants, as well as sugar-water feeders [1, 6].

The flower meadow is distinct from all previous ecosystems in the engine:

| Ecosystem Type | Defined By | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Forest-defined | Tree species/structure | Old-growth, mountain conifer, mixed woodland |
| Water-defined | Aquatic features | Riparian, estuary |
| Resource-defined | Food availability | Cone crop (degree 34) |
| **Flower-defined** | **Bloom abundance** | **Flower meadow (degree 35)** |

This is the engine's second non-place ecosystem (after cone crop at degree 34) and its first defined by a mutualistic relationship rather than a food resource alone. The crossbill takes from the cone. The hummingbird takes from the flower *and gives back* -- pollination is a two-way exchange. The ecosystem is not just where the food is; it is where the partnership operates.

### The Migration: Longest Journey Per Gram

The Rufous Hummingbird undertakes one of the most remarkable migrations of any bird -- not in absolute distance but in distance relative to body size [1, 2, 11]:

- **One-way distance:** Approximately 3,900 miles (6,300 km) from breeding grounds in Alaska and the Pacific Northwest to wintering grounds in Mexico [1, 2].
- **Body length:** Approximately 3.5 inches (8-9 cm) [1, 2].
- **Body lengths traveled:** Approximately 78,470,000 body lengths per one-way trip -- the longest migration relative to body size of any bird [2, 11].
- **Route:** A clockwise circuit of western North America. Northbound in late winter and spring along the Pacific Coast, following the blooming wave of wildflowers. Southbound in summer and fall through the Rocky Mountain corridor, following the late-summer bloom of mountain wildflowers [1, 2].
- **Timing in the PNW:** Arrives in western Washington in late March to mid-April. Males begin departing as early as late June or July. Females and juveniles follow through August and September [1, 6].

The migration route tracks flower blooms -- the bird follows the nectar. The northbound route follows the spring bloom up the coast. The southbound route follows the summer bloom through the mountains. The Rufous Hummingbird's migration is a flower-tracking journey, a nectar highway that runs the length of western North America.

### Metabolism: The Fastest Engine in the Forest

The Rufous Hummingbird has the highest metabolic rate by mass of any bird -- and among the highest of any vertebrate [1, 2, 4]:

- **Body mass:** 2-5 grams (typically 3-3.5 g) [1, 2]. For reference, a US nickel weighs 5 grams. The Rufous Hummingbird weighs less than a nickel.
- **Heart rate:** Up to 1,200 beats per minute during active flight [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- commonly cited but exact measurement conditions vary].
- **Wingbeat frequency:** 52-62 beats per second in hovering flight [1, 2].
- **Caloric requirement:** Must consume approximately 2-3 times its body weight in nectar daily [1, 4].
- **Torpor:** At night or during cold periods, Rufous Hummingbirds enter **torpor** -- a controlled hypothermic state where metabolic rate drops by up to 95%, body temperature may fall from ~40 C to as low as ~8 C, and heart rate drops to approximately 50 beats per minute [1, 2, 4]. This is not sleep; it is a survival mechanism. Without torpor, a hummingbird would starve to death overnight.
- **Hovering flight:** The only bird capable of sustained hovering. The wing anatomy is fundamentally different from other birds -- the hummingbird wing generates lift on both the downstroke and the upstroke, rotating at the shoulder joint in a figure-eight pattern [4]. This requires enormous energy, which requires enormous nectar intake, which requires defending flowers, which requires the territorial aggression that defines the species' behavior.

The metabolism drives everything. The hummingbird's entire behavioral ecology -- aggression, migration, flower specialization, torpor, even its sound -- is a consequence of its metabolic rate. A 3-gram endotherm that beats its wings 52-62 times per second is living at the thermodynamic edge of what vertebrate physiology permits.

### Territory: Three Grams of Fury

The Rufous Hummingbird is widely described as the **most aggressive hummingbird in North America** [1, 2, 6, 12]:

- **Flower patch defense:** Males establish territories around dense flower patches (natural or artificial) and defend them against all intruders -- other Rufous Hummingbirds, Anna's Hummingbirds, bumblebees, butterflies, and birds many times their size [1, 6, 12].
- **Size-independent aggression:** Rufous Hummingbirds will chase Red-tailed Hawks, Steller's Jays, and other birds weighing 100-1,000 times more [1, 12]. The aggression is metabolically rational: losing a flower patch means losing the energy source that sustains a metabolic rate operating at the limits of vertebrate physiology.
- **Sexual dimorphism in territory:** Males defend the richest flower patches. Females are pushed to areas with more sparsely distributed flowers [1, 2]. Males invest energy in defense; females invest energy in nesting and chick-rearing.
- **Display and combat:** Territory defense involves the J-dive display (mechanical sound from dive), rapid chasing flights, chittering vocalizations, and occasionally direct contact (bill-stabbing) [1, 7, 8].

The aggression is not temperamental. It is thermodynamic. A 3-gram bird with the highest metabolic rate of any bird cannot afford to share. Every flower defended is survival. Every intruder expelled is a meal preserved. The chitter -- the vocal component of this species' sound -- is the voice of this imperative: *this flower is mine, this meadow is mine, leave or I will chase you until one of us runs out of energy, and I will not be the one who runs out first.*

## The Rufous-Allen's Problem

The Rufous Hummingbird's closest relative is the Allen's Hummingbird (*Selasphorus sasin*), and the two species present one of the most challenging identification problems in North American ornithology [1, 2, 3]:

- **Male plumage:** Adult male Rufous Hummingbirds have an entirely rufous (copper-orange) back. Adult male Allen's Hummingbirds have a green back with rufous flanks and tail. However, some male Rufous Hummingbirds show green backs, creating overlap [1, 3].
- **Female and immature plumage:** Essentially identical between species. Field identification of female and immature birds is unreliable without tail feather examination [1, 3].
- **Tail feather shape:** The most reliable diagnostic feature. Rufous Hummingbird has broader outer tail feathers (rectrices); Allen's has narrower, more tapered outer tail feathers. The difference requires close examination or in-hand measurement [1, 3].
- **Range overlap:** The two species overlap in breeding range in a narrow zone in southern Oregon and northern California (~200 miles), where hybridization occurs [1, 3].
- **Behavioral similarity:** Both species are fiercely territorial, with similar courtship dives, chitter calls, and wingbeat sounds.

The Rufous-Allen's identification problem is the hummingbird parallel to the Red Crossbill type system -- both involve closely related forms within *Selasphorus* or *Loxia* that are difficult to distinguish and may represent recent or ongoing divergence. But where the crossbill types are cryptic within a single nominal species, the Rufous and Allen's are recognized as separate species that are simply very similar. The crossbill problem is "how many species are hiding in one name?" The Rufous-Allen's problem is "can you tell which of two names applies to this bird?"

## Conservation: Near Threatened

### Status

- **IUCN Red List:** **Near Threatened** [1, 13, 14]
- **Population decline:** Approximately **65% decline since the 1970s**, with rates of decline accelerating nearly twofold from 2009-2019 [13, 14, 15]
- **North American population:** Declining significantly. Partners in Flight identifies the Rufous Hummingbird as a species of high conservation concern [14, 16].
- **State of the Birds 2022:** Listed as a "Tipping Point" species -- one of the birds closest to potential Endangered listing if current trends continue [15].

The Rufous Hummingbird is the **first Near Threatened species in the engine.** Every previous species has been Least Concern (except the Northern Spotted Owl at degree 4, which is Near Threatened in some assessments and listed as Threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act). The Rufous Hummingbird's conservation status is significant because it represents the decline of a formerly common, widespread species -- not a specialist with a small range but a bird that breeds from Alaska to Oregon and was once abundant everywhere within that range.

### Threats

- **Breeding habitat loss:** Changes in forest management practices that reduce the open, shrubby, flower-rich habitats where Rufous Hummingbirds breed. Fire suppression that allows forest canopy closure, reducing meadow habitat [13, 14].
- **Climate change:** Altered timing of flower blooms relative to migration schedules (phenological mismatch). Extended droughts reducing floral abundance. Shifting ranges of both hummingbirds and their nectar plants [13, 14, 15].
- **Nectar availability:** Droughts and habitat loss reduce the density and diversity of tubular flowers. A hummingbird that cannot find enough flowers cannot sustain its metabolic rate [13, 14].
- **Pesticides and herbicides:** Insecticides reduce the insect prey that hummingbirds need for protein. Herbicides reduce wildflower abundance [14].
- **Window and glass collisions:** Significant mortality source for all hummingbirds [14].
- **Invasive species:** Domestic cats are a predation threat [14].

### The Conservation Significance

The Rufous Hummingbird's decline is a flower meadow alarm. The bird is a pollinator -- its decline may cascade through the plant communities it serves. Fewer hummingbirds means less pollination of tubular wildflowers. Fewer wildflowers means less nectar for remaining hummingbirds. The mutualism that sustains both partners is under pressure from both sides: the habitat that supports the flowers is degrading, and the climate that times the bloom to the bird's arrival is shifting.

This is not the conservation paradox of the Red Crossbill (Least Concern as a species but potentially vulnerable at the call-type level). The Rufous Hummingbird is straightforwardly declining. Sixty-five percent gone in fifty years. Accelerating. A "Tipping Point" species. The engine's first genuine conservation alarm.

## Trochilidae: The Family That Changed Everything

The arrival of Trochilidae in the engine is not just a new family. It is a new *kind* of bird. Hummingbirds occupy a unique position in avian biology:

- **The only birds that can sustain hovering flight.** The wing generates lift on both strokes (downstroke and upstroke), rotating at the shoulder in a figure-eight pattern. No other bird can do this [4].
- **The highest metabolic rate by mass of any bird.** The thermodynamic cost of hovering flight at 3 grams is extreme [4].
- **Torpor as survival strategy.** Without the ability to enter controlled hypothermia every night, hummingbirds would starve before dawn [4].
- **Nectar-flower coevolution.** Hummingbirds are primary pollinators for hundreds of plant species across the Americas. The mutualism has shaped both partners: tubular flowers evolved to fit hummingbird bills; hummingbird bills evolved to fit tubular flowers [5, 10].
- **Mechanical sound production.** Wingbeat-generated sound, tail-feather sonations during courtship dives, and wing-frequency modulation for communication. Sound from flight, not from voice [7, 8, 9].
- **Iridescence as signal.** The gorget's structural color changes with viewing angle -- it can appear brilliant copper-orange or dull black depending on the light. Males use body orientation to flash or conceal the gorget during displays [1, 9].

Every previous family in the engine has been a variation on a theme: birds that perch, birds that sing, birds that eat seeds or berries or insects. Trochilidae is a different theme entirely. A bird that hovers. A bird that weighs less than a nickel. A bird whose primary sound is the sound of its own wings. A bird that must eat its body weight in nectar to survive the day and then shut down its metabolism to survive the night. A bird that migrates 3,900 miles on 3 grams of fuel.
