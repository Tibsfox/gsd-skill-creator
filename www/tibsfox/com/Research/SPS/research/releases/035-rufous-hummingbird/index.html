Rufous Hummingbird

Selasphorus rufus
ch-ch-ch wingbeat thrum + chitter — the mechanical voice, the flame-bearer, the flower guardian

The 36th Acoustic Element: The Thrum

A mechanically generated sound produced by the physical interaction of wing feathers with air at 52–62 beats per second, creating a continuous tonal hum that is both involuntary byproduct of flight and controllable signal. The first non-vocal, non-percussive sound in the engine. After 34 degrees of vocal and percussive sound, the engine encounters a species that generates its signature sound by simply flying. Sound from motion. The body as instrument.

The progression: whisper (31) → buzz (32) → cadenza (33) → dialect (34) → thrum (35). Five consecutive degrees, five fundamentally different relationships between sound and its source.

Order Apodiformes — The Streak Ends

The 12-degree Passeriformes streak (degrees 23–34) is broken. Degree 35 enters Apodiformes — the order of hummingbirds and swifts. The first Apodiformes in the engine. Only the fourth non-Passeriformes entry overall (after Pelecaniformes at 0, Strigiformes at 4, Piciformes at 21–22). Five orders now represented.

Each non-Passeriformes entry has introduced a new sound mechanism: silence (Pelecaniformes), percussion (Piciformes), and now aeromechanical sound (Apodiformes). The hummingbird’s wingbeat thrum is the third sound-production category in the engine.

Taxonomy: The Flame-Bearer

Selasphorus — Ancient Greek selas (“light, flame”) + phoros (“bearing”) = “flame-bearer.” Rufus — Latin for “red, reddish-brown.” The full binomial Selasphorus rufus translates to “red flame-bearer” or “rufous torch-bearer” — both genus and species name describe light and color, the incandescence of a 3-gram bird. The most luminous binomial in the engine.

Described by Gmelin in 1788. Monotypic — no subspecies recognized. Closest relative: Allen’s Hummingbird (Selasphorus sasin), which is nearly identical in female and immature plumage and hybridizes in a narrow zone in southern Oregon.

Trochilidae — The 18th Family

Hummingbirds. Entirely New World. Approximately 375 species in 113 genera, from Alaska to Tierra del Fuego. The most species-rich bird family in the Americas. The only birds capable of sustained hovering flight. The highest metabolic rate by mass of any bird.

Four PNW hummingbirds: Rufous (common summer breeder), Anna’s (year-round resident), Calliope (mountain meadows), Black-chinned (east of Cascades). The Rufous is the most widespread and the most aggressive.

The Sound: Three Sources

1. Wingbeat Thrum (Aeromechanical)

52–62 beats per second in hovering flight. Air flows across narrowed feather tips, generating a continuous tonal hum with a fundamental frequency of 52–62 Hz and audible harmonics. Not vocal. Controllable through wingbeat frequency modulation (“Cobra” maneuvers change pitch and amplitude). The thrum is the engine’s first aeromechanical sound.

2. Chitter (Vocal)

Rapid series of sharp, buzzy chip notes — “ch-ch-ch-ch-ch” — syrinx-generated. Given during aggressive territorial encounters. 3–8 kHz, broadband, harsh. The voice of a 3-gram bird that chases hawks. Almost exclusively aggressive in context.

3. J-Dive Sonation (Aeromechanical)

Male dives from 10–30 meters, reaching speeds exceeding 25 m/s. At the nadir of the J-shaped arc, tail feathers vibrate in the airflow, producing a sharp mechanical buzz. Synchronized with maximum speed and iridescent gorget flash — all in approximately 300 milliseconds.

Flower Meadow — First Flower-Defined Ecosystem

The engine’s first ecosystem defined by blooms. The second non-place ecosystem (after cone crop at degree 34). The first ecosystem defined by a mutualistic relationship: the hummingbird extracts nectar and pollinates the flower. Two-way exchange, not one-way extraction.

Key PNW nectar plants: Red columbine, Indian paintbrush, scarlet gilia, salmonberry, red-flowering currant, penstemons, garden fuchsia.

The migration IS the ecosystem: The Rufous Hummingbird’s clockwise annual circuit — north along the Pacific Coast in spring, south through the Rockies in late summer — tracks the flower bloom. A 3,900-mile nectar corridor.

Ecology: The Fastest Engine

Body mass: 2–5 grams (typically 3–3.5 g). Weighs less than a US nickel.

Metabolism: Highest mass-specific metabolic rate of any bird. Must consume 2–3x body weight in nectar daily. Enters torpor nightly (metabolic rate drops up to 95%, body temperature may fall from ~40°C to ~8°C).

Migration: ~3,900 miles one way, Alaska to Mexico. Approximately 78,470,000 body lengths per trip — the longest migration relative to body size of any bird.

Territory: The most aggressive hummingbird in North America. Guards flower patches against all comers — other hummingbirds, hawks, jays, squirrels. The aggression is thermodynamic: a 3-gram bird with the highest metabolic rate of any bird cannot afford to share.

Near Threatened — The Engine’s First Conservation Alarm

IUCN: Near Threatened. Population decline: ~65% since the 1970s, accelerating. State of the Birds 2022: “Tipping Point” species.

Threats: Breeding habitat loss, climate change (phenological mismatch between bloom timing and migration), drought reducing floral abundance, pesticides, glass collisions.

The first genuinely declining common species in the engine. Not a specialist losing its niche (like the Spotted Owl) but a formerly abundant bird losing its world. Fewer hummingbirds → less pollination → fewer wildflowers → less nectar → fewer hummingbirds. The mutualism is under pressure from both sides.

E=5 — Individual Intensity

Second E=5 in the engine (after Bohemian Waxwing at degree 32). Two models of maximum energy:

  • Bohemian Waxwing (32): Collective E=5. Hundreds of birds buzzing. Mass as force.
  • Rufous Hummingbird (35): Individual E=5. One 3-gram bird defending against everything. Concentration as force.

Fortissimo from a full orchestra vs. fortissimo from a solo trumpet.

Theory Nodes

T

The Thrum foundational

Mechanically generated sound from wing feathers in airflow. 52–62 Hz continuous hum. First aeromechanical sound in the engine. The body as instrument, flight as performance.

A

Apodiformes Entry foundational

First Apodiformes, 18th family. Breaks 12-degree Passeriformes streak. Fifth order in engine. Introduces hovering flight, extreme metabolism, mechanical sound production.

F

Flower Meadow Ecosystem foundational

First flower-defined, first mutualism-defined ecosystem. Hummingbird-plant pollination partnership. The migration tracks the bloom. The ecosystem is a moving wave of nectar.

5

E=5 Individual Intensity intermediate

Second E=5 after Bohemian Waxwing (32). Collective mass (waxwing) vs. individual ferocity (hummingbird). Two models of maximum acoustic energy.

!

Near Threatened Status intermediate

65% decline since 1970s. First genuinely declining common species. Mutualism under pressure — pollinator and plants both threatened.

J

J-Dive Multi-Modal Display intermediate

Speed + tail-feather sonation + iridescent gorget flash, synchronized in ~300 ms. The most complex display behavior in the engine.
