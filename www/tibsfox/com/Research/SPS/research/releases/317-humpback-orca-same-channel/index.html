First Multi-Species Vocalization Entry — E=8 — Convergence at San Juan

The Humpback-Orca same-channel event is the engine’s first multi-species acoustic entry: two of the most vocal marine mammals on the planet simultaneously active in the San Juan Islands. Humpback song (the most complex vocal sequence in the animal kingdom, culturally transmitted, 10-30 minutes) + Orca calls (culturally transmitted dialects, 100+ SRKW call types). Both species in the same acoustic space. E=8.

The Two Species

Humpback Whale

Megaptera novaeangliae

Family Balaenopteridae. 40-50 feet, 25-40 tons. Summer resident: arrives June, departs October. Primary song range: 30-500 Hz. Song duration: 10-30 minutes, repeated continuously. Culturally transmitted: new phrase types spread across populations at 1,500 km/year.

Southern Resident Killer Whale

Orcinus orca

Family Delphinidae. J/K/L pods, ~73 animals. Year-round residents, peak summer. Call range: 100-20,000 Hz. 100+ discrete call types. Culturally transmitted pod dialects. Degree 311 (E=9, greeting ceremony): the engine’s highest SPS energy.

The Frequency Overlap

Acoustic Space — Where the Two Species Meet

Humpback song primary energy: 30–500 Hz
Orca call range: 100–20,000 Hz (full width)
Overlap zone: ~100–500 Hz (both species active)

At 100–500 Hz: Humpback’s moans and cries share acoustic space with Orca’s lower-frequency calls. Research documents behavioral modification in both species during simultaneous vocalization. The overlap zone is where the convergence event is acoustically defined.

Cultural Transmission — Both Species

The degree-317 SPS entry is unique in the engine: both species involved have culturally transmitted vocalizations. Humpback song evolves through social learning — new phrase types emerge, spread laterally at up to 1,500 km/year, and eventually replace previous song types across entire ocean basins (documented by Noad et al. 1999 in the Pacific). Southern Resident Killer Whale pod dialects are culturally distinct from neighboring transient and offshore Orca populations — the J, K, and L pod call repertoires are specific to those family lines and transmitted across generations through social learning.

When both species are simultaneously active in the San Juan Islands, the acoustic event contains two independently evolving cultural traditions in the same water. This is the engine’s richest cultural-acoustic pairing.

The Humpback Recovery

The Humpback whale was commercially hunted to near-extinction in the North Pacific before the International Whaling Commission moratorium (1966). North Pacific Humpback populations have recovered significantly since. The Salish Sea and San Juan Islands area has seen increasing Humpback presence over the past two decades — from rare sightings to regular summer individuals. The simultaneous presence of Humpback and Orca in the same channel is partly a conservation success story: the Humpback’s recovery has restored a multi-species acoustic ecology to the Salish Sea that was absent during the post-whaling depletion period.
