## THE TWO SPECIES -- BACKGROUND

### Different Families, Different Systems, Same Water

*Megaptera novaeangliae* and *Orcinus orca* are not closely related:

- **Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*).** Family Balaenopteridae (baleen whales, rorquals). Body length: 40-50 feet (12-16 m). Weight: 25-40 tons. A migratory species that winters in tropical breeding grounds (Hawaii, Mexico) and summers in productive high-latitude feeding grounds. The San Juan Islands and greater Salish Sea are primary Humpback summer feeding habitat -- the whales arrive in June and remain through September-October. Humpbacks produce the most complex vocal sequences in the animal kingdom: the male "song" is a structured, repeating sequence that can last 10-30 minutes and evolves across breeding seasons. Humpback songs are documented as culturally transmitted acoustic information.
- **Orca (*Orcinus orca*).** Family Delphinidae (dolphins, in the largest member of the family). The Orca was documented at degree 311 (E=9, greeting ceremony) -- the engine's highest SPS energy level. The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) are the primary Orca population in the San Juan Islands: three pods (J, K, L) totaling approximately 73 animals as of 2024. Their vocalizations are socially complex, individually identifiable, and dialects vary between pods. The SRKW acoustic catalog documents 100+ distinct call types.
- **Physical size contrast.** The Humpback is significantly larger than the Orca. A 40-ton Humpback vs. a 6-ton male Orca: the Humpback's body mass generates acoustic energy in a lower and more resonant register. When both are vocalizing simultaneously, the Humpback's song provides the bass foundation while the Orca's calls occupy the mid-to-high frequency ranges.

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILES -- FREQUENCY SPACE

### Where the Two Species Meet in the Spectrum

The frequency overlap between Humpback whale song and Orca calls is partial -- they do not perfectly overlap, but they share acoustic space:

- **Humpback whale song frequency range.** Primary acoustic energy: 20-2,000 Hz, with the most biologically important elements in the 30-500 Hz range. The infrasonic lower components (20-30 Hz) are not audible to humans but are detected by other whales at long distances. The primary song elements (200-1,500 Hz) are audible to humans underwater. The Humpback's song is not a single tone but a structured sequence of phrase types: moans, cries, chirps, sequences. Duration of an individual song: 10-30 minutes; the whale may repeat it continuously for hours.
- **Orca call frequency range.** Primary acoustic energy: 100-20,000 Hz (the SRKW vocalize across a very wide frequency range). The social calls (J pod discrete calls: 1-20 kHz) are in the upper range. The echolocation clicks (20-100 kHz) are above the primary song range. The whistles (1-18 kHz) are mid-to-upper range. The low-frequency calls (100-500 Hz) partially overlap with the Humpback song range.
- **The overlap zone.** At approximately 100-500 Hz, the Humpback's song phrases and the Orca's lower-frequency calls share acoustic space. This is the convergence zone where the two species' vocalizations coexist. Research has documented that Orcas in the presence of actively singing Humpbacks modify their vocal behavior -- in some studies, Orcas reduce low-frequency call rates; in others, they appear to vocalize at different times in a turn-taking pattern.

(Sources: Humpback whale frequency range 20-2000 Hz; primary elements 30-500 Hz; Orca call frequency 100-20,000 Hz; echolocation 20-100 kHz; whistles 1-18 kHz; overlap zone 100-500 Hz; acoustic behavior modification research; turn-taking observations)

## THE SAN JUAN ISLANDS AS MULTI-SPECIES ACOUSTIC HABITAT

### Why Both Species Are Here Simultaneously

The San Juan Islands are one of the few locations on the planet where both Humpback whales and Orca are reliably present in summer:

- **SRKW residency.** The Southern Resident Killer Whales are year-round residents of the Salish Sea, with highest concentration in the San Juans during summer months (June-September) when Chinook salmon runs provide their primary prey. The SRKW presence in the San Juans is not occasional -- it is the species' home habitat.
- **Humpback summer feeding.** Humpback whales arrive in the San Juans in late May or early June following their northward migration from tropical breeding grounds. They remain through October, feeding intensively on herring, sand lance, and krill. The San Juans' productive waters support multiple Humpback individuals simultaneously; whale-watching research documents daily sightings of 5-15 individuals during peak summer.
- **The Humpback recovery.** The Humpback whale was listed as endangered globally following commercial whaling. North Pacific Humpback populations have recovered significantly since commercial whaling ended in 1966. The Salish Sea/San Juan Islands area has seen increasing Humpback presence over the past two decades -- this is a conservation recovery in progress. Both species in the same channel simultaneously is partly a product of successful recovery.
- **Geographic concentration.** The San Juan Islands create a labyrinthine habitat of channels, straits, and passages. The same underwater acoustics that concentrate Orca vocalizations for researchers (hydrophone networks can hear vocalizations from multiple islands away) also propagate Humpback song across the archipelago. Both species' vocalizations carry through this acoustic geography simultaneously.

(Sources: SRKW year-round residency; San Juans summer peak June-September; Chinook salmon prey; Humpback arrival May-June; departure October; herring/sand lance/krill diet; 5-15 Humpback individuals simultaneously; Humpback recovery post-1966; Salish Sea Humpback increase; San Juan acoustic geography; hydrophone network)

## THE MULTI-SPECIES EVENT -- WHAT IT SOUNDS LIKE

### The Acoustic Character of Simultaneous Vocalization

When Humpback and Orca are simultaneously active in the San Juan Islands:

- **The Humpback foundation.** The Humpback's song provides a low-frequency sonic landscape -- the moans and cries (20-500 Hz) create an omnipresent tonal background that carries across kilometers of water. This is not the sharp, burst-pulse sound of Orca calls but a sustained, tonal, quasi-musical sequence. Underwater, the Humpback song is one of the most profoundly immersive acoustic experiences in the ocean.
- **The Orca overlay.** The SRKW vocalizations -- discrete calls, whistles, burst-pulse sounds -- occur at higher frequencies and with shorter temporal structures. They are more staccato, more identifiable as individual calls, more socially contextual. When they overlap with the Humpback song background, the Orca calls stand out as distinct events against the Humpback's tonal foundation.
- **The interaction question.** Whether the two species are acoustically aware of each other during simultaneous vocalization is not fully resolved. Humpbacks and Orcas have a complex relationship in the wild -- Orcas prey on Humpback calves; adult Humpbacks have been documented "mobbing" Orcas in response to calf predation. In the San Juans, the SRKW (fish-eating specialists) do not target Humpbacks, reducing the predation-response dynamic. The acoustic interaction between these two populations in the same channel is a research frontier.
- **E=8 calibration.** The multi-species simultaneous event calibrates to E=8 through: (1) the combined acoustic output of two of the ocean's most vocal species; (2) the Humpback song's extraordinary complexity and duration (10-30 minutes, biologically the most complex song in the animal kingdom); (3) the Orca's established E=9 individual calibration reduced to E=8 in the non-greeting-ceremony context; (4) the rarity and significance of simultaneous same-channel vocalization.

(Sources: Humpback song as tonal foundation 20-500 Hz; Orca calls as staccato overlay; higher frequency Orca vocalization; Humpback-Orca relationship; calf predation; adult mobbing behavior; SRKW as fish-eating specialists; non-targeting of San Juan Humpbacks; acoustic interaction research; E=8 calibration factors)

## CULTURAL TRANSMISSION IN HUMPBACK SONG

### The Biological Feature That Makes This Entry Unique

Humpback whale song is one of the most studied examples of cultural transmission in non-human animals:

- **What cultural transmission means.** Humpback male song evolves across breeding seasons. New phrase types emerge, spread through the population, and eventually replace older phrase types. The mechanism is social learning: males hear other males singing, adopt new elements, and transmit them laterally across the population. This is not genetic; it is learned and culturally transmitted.
- **The spread pattern.** Research by Mike Noad and colleagues (1999) documented a dramatic case: a new song type originated in the eastern Australian Humpback population, spread westward across the Pacific, and within a few breeding seasons had replaced the existing song type across thousands of kilometers. The spread rate (approximately 1,500 km/year) and directionality (east to west) are consistent with social learning transmission.
- **Pacific implications.** The North Pacific Humpback population that summers in the San Juan Islands belongs to the same broad acoustic culture as populations across the Pacific. Song elements that emerge in Hawaii or Alaska may eventually appear in the San Juan Islands population. The song the engine documents in the San Juans is part of a culturally evolving Pacific-wide acoustic tradition.
- **The SPS significance.** Cultural transmission in Humpback song is the SPS engine's only documented example of non-human culturally transmitted acoustic behavior. The Orca's dialect variations (also cultural) were documented at degree 311. The simultaneous presence of two species with culturally transmitted vocalizations in the same channel is the engine's richest cultural-acoustic event.

(Sources: Humpback cultural transmission; Mike Noad 1999 research; eastern Australian spread; 1,500 km/year; east-to-west directionality; North Pacific population; Hawaii to Alaska cultural continuity; Orca dialect cultural transmission; two-species cultural acoustic event)
