## What Worked

1. **Opening Phocidae with the Elephant Seal bull is the correct choice.** The Harbor Seal (the most common Phocidae in Puget Sound) is quiet, solitary, and unobtrusive. Opening the true seal chapter with the most acoustically spectacular representative -- the proboscis-amplified gargling machine-gun call -- makes the chapter introduction as strong as possible. The Harbor Seal will provide contrast later.
2. **The near-extinction recovery story is among the most extreme in any large mammal.** The founding bottleneck (20-100 animals) to 220,000+ is not just a conservation success; it is a genetic fact that permanently defines the species. The bottleneck documentation is essential.
3. **The Phocidae vs. Otariidae comparison (no external ears, rear flippers fixed, rear-flipper swimming vs. front-flipper) provides the taxonomic anchor.** The engine now has entries in both pinniped families; the contrast is documentable.
4. **The 1,500-meter dive depth is one of the engine's more extraordinary animal facts.** Few people know that Northern Elephant Seals forage at these depths, traversing the North Pacific basin. This contextualizes why a California breeding animal appears as a vagrant in Washington state.

## Discoveries

1. **The individual recognition function of the pulsed call is underappreciated.** Bulls recognize rivals by specific pulse pattern across multiple breeding seasons. This is not simple territorial noise; it is an individual identity transmission system.
2. **The proboscis as resonating chamber is the acoustic mechanism.** The frequency shift and amplitude amplification from the inflated proboscis is the reason the call sounds the way it does. Without the proboscis, the call would be higher-frequency and less resonant.
