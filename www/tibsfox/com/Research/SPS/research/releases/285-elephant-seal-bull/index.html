First Phocidae — True Seal Enters the Engine

Mirounga angustirostris — the Northern Elephant Seal bull — is the FIRST PHOCIDAE in the engine. Previous marine mammal entries documented Phocoenidae (281), Delphinidae (282), and Otariidae (283–284). Phocidae — the true seals, earless seals — enters at degree 285 with the most acoustically spectacular representative available.

The pinniped trifecta: Otariidae (eared seals, 283–284) now complete; Phocidae (true seals) opens here. The bull vocalization is extraordinary: a deep, pulsed, proboscis-amplified gargling machine-gun call at E=8 — nothing else in the engine sounds like it.

Acoustic Profile — The Gargling Machine-Gun

Frequency: 50–500 Hz; energy concentrated 100–300 Hz. Very low frequency; the proboscis acts as resonating chamber, shifting the call downward and amplifying amplitude.

Pattern: Rapid-fire pulsed sequence, approximately 4–10 beats per second, sustained for several seconds with brief pauses between bouts. Repetitive, mechanical, rhythmic. No other Pacific Northwest marine mammal produces this pattern. E=8: extremely loud, produced with evident physical effort, carries hundreds of meters across a beach. Comparable effective energy to Steller Sea Lion bull roar (E=8) with different acoustic character: pulsed vs. sustained.

Individual recognition: Bulls can identify rivals by call across multiple breeding seasons. The call encodes individual identity in its specific pulse pattern.

The Proboscis

The Northern Elephant Seal bull's inflatable nasal proboscis is one of the most extreme secondary sexual characteristics in any mammal. It develops at sexual maturity (age 4–5) and reaches full expression in bulls over age 8–9, extending 12–14 inches (30–36 cm) beyond the face when inflated. Inflated during vocalization, it acts as a resonating chamber that amplifies and lowers the frequency of the call, producing the characteristic gargling, rumbling quality that distinguishes this species from all other Pacific Northwest marine mammals.

Adult bulls weigh 1,500–2,500 lbs (700–1,100 kg) and reach 13–16 feet (4–5 m). Females: 400–900 lbs, 9–10 feet. The size difference is among the most extreme of any mammal — 3–4 times heavier in males. Winner-takes-all polygyny drives the selection for large male body size.

Recovery from Near-Extinction

Northern Elephant Seals were hunted to commercial extinction by the 1880s for blubber oil. The remnant founding population of approximately 20–100 animals survived on Guadalupe Island, Mexico. The current population exceeds 220,000. This extreme bottleneck — 220,000 animals descending from perhaps 100 survivors — has left a permanent genetic signature: dramatically reduced diversity compared to the pre-hunting population.

Protection under the Marine Mammal Protection Act (1972) and Mexican federal law allowed the recovery to proceed. New California breeding colonies established within the last century: Ano Nuevo (first pups born in the 1960s), Point Reyes. The recovery is among the most complete in any large mammal's documented history. IUCN: Least Concern.

PNW Status — The Rare Visitor

Northern Elephant Seals breed primarily on the California coast (Ano Nuevo, Point Reyes, Channel Islands, Farallons) and forage across the entire North Pacific basin — individuals dive to depths exceeding 1,500 meters (4,900 feet) on foraging trips that can reach the Aleutians. Sub-adults and juvenile males are documented hauling out on Washington's outer coast and occasionally in Puget Sound. These are vagrant animals, not residents. When a Northern Elephant Seal appears on a Washington beach, it is typically alone, resting or molting. The full bull vocalization display is most likely encountered at California breeding colonies; in PNW context, the call is documented but less frequent without the stimulus of competing bulls.
