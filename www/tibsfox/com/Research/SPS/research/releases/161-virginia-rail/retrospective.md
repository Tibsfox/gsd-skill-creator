*Degree 161 retrospective complete. VIRGINIA RAIL DESCENDING WHINNY GRUNT at the one hundred sixty-first degree -- the marsh skulker, the first rail. In the dense cattails and bulrushes of freshwater marshes across western Washington, the Virginia Rail slips between stems with its laterally compressed body -- "thin as a rail," the phrase that derives from the bird itself. Long toes spread its weight across floating mats of vegetation and soft mud. It skulks at ground level, runs through the marsh rather than flying, and is almost never seen. But its voice carries: a rapid, descending series of pig-like grunting notes -- "kid-ick kid-ick kid-ick-ick-ick-ick" -- that announces its presence across the marsh. The descending whinny grunt is un-bird-like, immediately recognizable, and often the only evidence that Virginia Rails inhabit a marsh. The first Rallidae in the engine. The rail family enters at degree 161 and will continue through the Sora at 162 and the American Coot at 164. Three entries, all E=4, spanning from total secrecy to full visibility.
