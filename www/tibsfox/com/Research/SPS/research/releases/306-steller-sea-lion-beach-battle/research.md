## STELLER SEA LION -- THE SPECIES

### *Eumetopias jubatus* -- The Largest Eared Seal

*Eumetopias jubatus* (Schreber, 1776) is the largest member of the family Otariidae and one of the largest pinnipeds:

- **Size.** Adult males (bulls): typically 2.4-3.0 meters body length, 450-1,000 kg body weight. The largest recorded individual exceeded 1,000 kg. Adult females (cows): 2.3-2.9 meters, 240-350 kg. The sexual dimorphism is extreme: bulls are 2-3 times the mass of cows. A bull Steller Sea Lion is one of the largest mammals regularly encountered on the Pacific Northwest coast.
- **Appearance.** Both sexes are tawny to light yellow-brown, darker on the underside. Bulls develop a massive, enlarged neck (the "mane" region) of thickened skin and muscle -- an adaptation for bull-to-bull combat. The neck bulk of a large bull is the defining visual indicator of species and sex.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** The Steller Sea Lion uses rookeries, haulout sites, and foraging areas throughout the Pacific Northwest. Key Washington State locations include the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the outer coast of the Olympic Peninsula, Race Rocks (BC, adjacent to the SJI), and Point Grenville. Non-breeding haulout aggregations occur at pier structures in Seattle's waterfront and elsewhere in Puget Sound. The species ranges from the Channel Islands (California) north through the Gulf of Alaska and the Aleutians to eastern Russia.
- **Conservation status.** Endangered (IUCN) for the western DPS (distinct population segment, Alaska and western Pacific); Near Threatened for the eastern DPS (California to Washington). The Washington / Oregon populations are eastern DPS and are recovering from decades of low numbers. The eastern DPS has increased substantially since the 1980s; the western DPS remains below historical levels.

(Sources: Eumetopias jubatus; adult male size 2.4-3.0m, 450-1000 kg; female size 2.3-2.9m, 240-350 kg; extreme sexual dimorphism; bull neck mane adaptation; Washington State locations: San Juan Islands, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Olympic Peninsula coast; Range from California to Russia; western DPS Endangered; eastern DPS Near Threatened; eastern DPS recovery)

## ROOKERY DYNAMICS -- SUMMER BREEDING SEASON

### The Context of the Bull Collision

The Steller Sea Lion rookery at peak breeding season is one of the most acoustically intense environments on the Pacific Northwest coast:

- **Rookery structure.** Breeding rookeries form on isolated rocky islands and headlands where access is limited to predators and humans. The rookery structure is territorial: dominant bulls establish territories on the best substrate (near water access, on flat stable rock, at locations where cows aggregate). Each bull defends his territory against other bulls. The best territories contain harems of multiple cows.
- **Bull competition.** Bull Steller Sea Lions begin arriving at rookeries in spring, ahead of the cows. Bull-to-bull competition is intense: the largest and most dominant bulls claim the best territories; subordinate bulls are pushed to the periphery. Competition is initially postural and vocal -- bulls roar, hold positions, and display their size. When bulls are closely matched, physical contact follows.
- **The collision event.** When two bull Steller Sea Lions engage in full combat, they rear up on their forefflippers, lunging chest-to-chest with full body weight. A bull weighing 600+ kg lunging at speed generates an impact force that is physically audible as a distinct percussion. The chest-to-chest collision is accompanied by continuous deep roaring from both animals, foreflipper strikes against the substrate, and often biting at the neck and shoulders (the thickened mane skin provides protection against biting damage).
- **Duration.** Bull combats can last minutes. Extended combats produce extended acoustic events: continuous roaring, periodic impact sounds, and the sustained vocalizations of the colony as a whole.

(Sources: Steller Sea Lion rookery structure; isolated rocky substrate; bull territory establishment; harem defense; spring arrival; bull-to-bull competition: postural, vocal, physical; chest-to-chest collision mechanics; 600+ kg bull impact; neck biting; mane protection; combat duration minutes)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8

### Roaring Bull Collision: The Maximum Otariidae Sound

The Steller Sea Lion bull collision at rookery is the maximum energy event in the engine's Otariidae documentation:

- **Bull roar.** The Steller Sea Lion bull produces a deep, resonant roar that is one of the loudest vocalizations of any Pacific Northwest marine mammal. The roar carries across the rookery and is audible from considerable distance. Frequency: predominantly low, 200-1,500 Hz, with significant energy in the 400-800 Hz range. The roar is produced at high intensity and continues throughout the confrontation.
- **Collision impact.** Two bull Steller Sea Lions at full weight (600-1,000 kg each) making chest-to-chest contact produces a distinct, heavy percussion -- a deep, concussive thump that is felt as well as heard at close range. The impact duration is brief but intense, and repeats multiple times in a sustained combat.
- **Multiple simultaneous acoustic sources.** The collision event occurs within the broader rookery acoustic environment: hundreds or thousands of other sea lions vocalizing, pups calling, cows responding, subordinate bulls maintaining territorial vocalizations. The bull collision is the dominant signal against a continuous, dense background of Otariidae sound.
- **E=8 calibration.** Maximum Otariidae energy. The bull collision is the highest-energy acoustic event that a sea lion colony produces: two of the largest pinnipeds on the Pacific Northwest coast colliding at full body weight, both roaring continuously, within the loudest sustained animal acoustic environment on the PNW coast. E=8 is the correct ceiling.
