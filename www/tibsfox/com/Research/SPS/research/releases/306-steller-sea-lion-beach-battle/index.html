The Rookery Battle — E=8 in Context

The Steller Sea Lion breeding rookery at peak summer is one of the highest sustained acoustic energy environments on the Pacific Northwest coast. Hundreds of animals vocalizing continuously: cows, pups, subordinate bulls maintaining territorial vocalizations. Against this constant background, the bull combat events are the acoustic peaks: two of the largest pinnipeds on the coast colliding at full body weight, both roaring continuously, in competition for territory and breeding access.

The bull collision combines multiple simultaneous acoustic sources: the deep resonant roar (200–1,500 Hz, significant energy 400–800 Hz) of both animals; the heavy concussive percussion of massive body impact; foreflipper strikes against rocky substrate; and the sustained vocalizations throughout the duration of the combat. E=8 is the correct calibration: this is the maximum energy event possible within the Otariidae behavioral repertoire.

Acoustic Profile — Bull Collision

Bull roar: Deep resonant roar, 200–1,500 Hz, primary energy 400–800 Hz. One of the loudest vocalizations produced by any Pacific Northwest marine mammal. Continuous throughout confrontation.

Collision impact: Two bulls at 600–1,000 kg each making chest-to-chest contact. Heavy concussive percussion, felt as well as heard at close range. Repeating during sustained combat.

Foreflipper percussion: Bulls striking substrate as they rear up and lunge. Additional low-frequency impact sounds against rocky rookery surface.

Rookery ambient: Hundreds to thousands of other animals vocalizing simultaneously. The collision is the foreground event against the densest sustained animal sound environment on the PNW coast.

E=8 calibration: Maximum Otariidae energy. Largest species + most intense behavior + maximum-density acoustic environment = E=8 ceiling.

Eumetopias jubatus — The Species

Adult male Steller Sea Lions are 2.4–3.0 meters body length and 450–1,000 kg — the largest eared seal in the world. The extreme sexual dimorphism (bulls 2–3 times the mass of cows) is the result of intense male-male selection: the largest, strongest bulls win territories and mate most. The bull's enlarged neck mane — thickened skin and muscle — is an adaptation specifically for bull combat: it absorbs bite force and cushions collision impact.

Pacific Northwest distribution: San Juan Islands, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Olympic Peninsula coast, Race Rocks (BC), Point Grenville. Non-breeding aggregations at waterfront pier structures in Seattle and Puget Sound. Conservation status: Near Threatened (eastern DPS, which includes Washington/Oregon) and Endangered (western DPS, Alaska and western Pacific).
