The Wolf's Acoustic Ecology

A Gray Wolf howl in the North Cascades subalpine zone carries 10+ km under favorable conditions — cold air, calm winds, open terrain. The high meadow above treeline amplifies this: no forest canopy to scatter or absorb. The wolf is broadcasting territorial status to adjacent packs, to potential mates, to the wide acoustic space of the mountain. The howl is a map: this territory, this pack, this presence. It is held for 5-10 seconds because distance requires duration — the signal needs to reach its distant recipients.

The June-August thunderstorm season in the North Cascades overlaps exactly with summer wolf behavior: rendezvous site occupation, pup-rearing, maximum territorial howling. The habitat coincidence is not rare. The specific moment of the thunder crack cutting the sustained howl — that is the rare event, and it is E=9.
