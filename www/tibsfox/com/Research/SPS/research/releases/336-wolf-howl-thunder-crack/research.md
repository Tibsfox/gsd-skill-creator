## THE GRAY WOLF -- THE BIOLOGICAL SOURCE

### Canis lupus and the Vocalization System

The Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*) and its howl:

- **Taxonomy and distribution.** *Canis lupus* (Gray Wolf) is the largest wild member of the family Canidae. In the Pacific Northwest, Gray Wolves were extirpated from Washington State by the 1930s through predator control programs. Natural recolonization began in the 2000s when wolves from Idaho and Montana began moving into northeastern Washington. As of 2024, Washington State has approximately 30+ packs, primarily in the northeastern Cascades and the Okanogan-Wenatchee region. The Washington Department of Fish & Wildlife tracks wolf pack territories actively. The subalpine meadow habitat of the high Cascades -- the setting for degree 336 -- is within the range of active Washington wolf packs.
- **The howl vocalization.** The Gray Wolf howl is one of the most acoustically sophisticated vocalizations in the mammalian world. It is not a single-frequency call but a complex, modulated sound: the fundamental frequency typically begins at 150-400 Hz and sweeps upward or varies in a characteristic pattern over the 3-11 second duration of a single howl. The wolf modulates the fundamental frequency, the harmonic content (upper partials at 2x, 3x, 4x the fundamental), and the amplitude envelope. Each wolf's howl has individually distinctive characteristics -- experienced field researchers can identify individual wolves by their howl alone.
- **The howl function.** Wolves howl for multiple documented purposes: territorial advertisement (broadcasting pack location and size to neighboring packs or solitary wolves), social cohesion (pack members howl together, reinforcing social bonds), contact calls (separated pack members locating each other), and in some contexts, distress or alarm. In the summer, when packs are raising pups and defending territories around den and rendezvous sites, howling is at annual maximum frequency. A single wolf beginning a howl at a summer rendezvous site is performing territorial advertisement -- the most acoustically assertive of the wolf's vocal behaviors.
- **The acoustic properties.** A single Gray Wolf howl produces approximately 90-105 dB at 1 meter from the source. The fundamental frequency (150-400 Hz) carries the longest distance through forest; the harmonics (up to several kHz) add the tonal quality distinguishable at shorter range. Gray Wolf howls have been detected at distances of 10+ km under favorable acoustic conditions (cold, calm air over open terrain). The howl carries in the high meadow's open acoustic environment better than in the forest below, where tree trunks and canopy absorb and scatter the sound.

## THE LIGHTNING STRIKE -- THE ATMOSPHERIC SOURCE

### Close Lightning and the Thunder Crack

Lightning and its acoustic output:

- **The physics of lightning.** A lightning stroke is a rapid electrical discharge between charged regions of a thunderstorm (cloud-to-cloud or cloud-to-ground). The discharge heats the air in the lightning channel to approximately 30,000 K (five times the surface temperature of the Sun) in less than a millisecond. This extreme rapid heating causes explosive expansion of the air channel, producing the shock wave that propagates as thunder.
- **The thunder crack vs. thunder rumble.** At close range (less than 1-2 km from the strike), thunder is heard as a sharp crack -- a high-amplitude broadband transient that arrives within 3-6 seconds of the lightning flash (at the speed of sound, approximately 340 m/s). The "crack" quality of close thunder is distinct from the "rumble" of distant thunder: close thunder is a single high-amplitude impulsive event, with peak amplitude of 110-120 dB (and higher for extremely close strikes, though these are brief), and a broadband frequency content from below 100 Hz through several kHz. The duration of the peak amplitude portion is less than 0.1 seconds, followed by a decay that may last 2-10 seconds as the sound reflects from terrain and the later arrival of thunder from more distant parts of the same lightning channel.
- **The peak amplitude.** A close lightning strike (100-200 meters) produces thunder at approximately 120+ dB at the listener's position. This is at or above the threshold of physical discomfort for human hearing (and is believed to be at the threshold of auditory pain for many mammals). The peak is brief -- the crack is the peak; the rumble that follows is much lower in amplitude.
- **The Cascades thunderstorm context.** Summer convective thunderstorms in the North Cascades and central Cascades are driven by daytime heating of the terrain (orographic convection), sometimes enhanced by organized synoptic-scale lift from approaching systems. In the subalpine meadow zone (above treeline, 5,000-6,500+ feet), summer thunderstorms can develop rapidly. The open terrain of the subalpine meadow provides no protection from close strikes; in fact, elevation increases the probability of strike as the meadow is closer to the thunderstorm base. The combination of wolf territory and thunderstorm activity in the same subalpine meadow zone is not rare -- this habitat coincidence occurs regularly in the Cascades summer.

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT -- HOW THE HOWL AND THUNDER COMBINE

### The Specific Temporal Structure of the Interruption

The sequence that constitutes the degree-336 acoustic event:

- **Phase 1: The Wolf Begins.** A single Gray Wolf in a summer high meadow initiates a territorial howl. The fundamental begins at 200-300 Hz and sweeps upward or holds, the harmonics present, the amplitude rising to full power (90-105 dB at the wolf's position, 60-80 dB at 1 km distance). The howl is sustained -- a single exhalation lasting 5-10 seconds, the wolf's territorial assertion into the mountain air of the open meadow. Other pack members may or may not join; degree 336 documents the individual howl for maximum acoustic clarity.
- **Phase 2: The Simultaneous Acoustic Space.** During the sustained howl -- at some point in the 5-10 second duration -- the thunder crack arrives from the close lightning strike. The two acoustic events coincide in the acoustic space of the high meadow: the wolf howl at 60-80 dB (at moderate listener distance) and the thunder crack at 110-120+ dB. The thunder crack is approximately 30-50 dB louder than the howl at the listener's position.
- **Phase 3: The Interruption.** The thunder crack acoustically masks the wolf howl completely -- the 30-50 dB amplitude difference is enormous (10 dB = 10x perceived loudness, so 40 dB = 10,000x). During the 0.1 second peak of the thunder crack, the wolf howl is inaudible. As the thunder decays (the following 2-10 second rumble), the wolf howl may be partially audible again in the gaps between thunder reflections -- but the peak interruption is total.
- **Phase 4: The Aftermath.** The wolf, in documented behavioral observations, does not necessarily stop howling immediately upon a close thunder strike. Wolves are not more frightened of thunderstorms than other loud environmental sounds. The howl may continue after the thunder; the pack may respond. The degree-336 event is the moment of the interruption -- the crack cutting the howl -- rather than the full extended behavioral sequence.
- **The "howl cut by lightning" as the acoustic unit.** The engine's documentation of this event is the moment of maximum acoustic drama: the sustained biological vocalization at full expression, then the atmospheric crack. The two sources are not simultaneous in the sense of the degree-332 polyrhythm (three independent rhythmic streams continuously); they are temporally coincident -- one cuts the other.

## GRAY WOLF RECOVERY IN WASHINGTON -- THE ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT

### The Recolonization That Makes This Event Possible

- **The extirpation.** Gray Wolves were eliminated from Washington State through systematic predator control programs in the early 20th century. The last resident Washington wolf pack was eliminated by approximately 1930. The absence of wolves altered the cascade of ecological relationships in Pacific Northwest ecosystems: without wolf predation, elk and deer populations increased and browsed more heavily on streamside vegetation, changing stream morphology and affecting fish habitat (the "trophic cascade" documented in Yellowstone National Park studies).
- **The recolonization.** Wolves from Idaho and Montana populations (which had recovered following the 1995 Yellowstone reintroduction and broader legal protection) began dispersing into northeastern Washington in the early 2000s. The first documented resident Washington pack was the Lookout Pack in Okanogan County in 2008. By 2024, Washington has 30+ active packs, primarily in the northeastern Cascades, Selkirk Mountains, and Blue Mountains.
- **The North Cascades.** The North Cascades region (the northern section of the Cascade Range, north of about 47°N latitude) is the portion of Washington most closely associated with high-elevation wolf habitat. The high meadows and subalpine zones of the North Cascades -- the setting for degree 336 -- are within or adjacent to documented wolf territory. Wolves in the North Cascades occupy territory at elevation, moving seasonally with prey (elk and deer) to higher elevations in summer.
- **The significance for degree 336.** The wolf howl in a Washington high meadow is not a historical artifact -- it is a current, recovering, and legally protected ecological reality. The Gray Wolf's acoustic presence in the subalpine Cascades is a restoration story: these sounds were absent for seven decades and are now returning. The degree-336 event is the sound of recovery.
