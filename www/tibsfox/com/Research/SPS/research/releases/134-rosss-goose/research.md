## E=4 -- THE SMALLEST GOOSE

### Why E=4

Ross's Goose registers at E=4 -- one step down from the Snow Goose's E=5:

- **Small size, reduced volume:** Ross's Goose is North America's smallest goose -- about **two-thirds the size** of a Snow Goose and roughly **half the weight**. The smaller body produces a correspondingly smaller syrinx and less vocal power. The individual call is simply quieter than the Snow Goose's bark.
- **Higher pitch:** The smaller body and syrinx produce a **higher-pitched call** -- a nasal, squeaky quality that is distinctly different from the Snow Goose's shrill bark. The call is sometimes described as a "grunt" or "kak" -- shorter, higher, and weaker than the Snow Goose.
- **Flock dynamics:** Ross's Geese are less numerous than Snow Geese in the PNW and are often mixed in Snow Goose flocks rather than forming large independent flocks. The flock-amplification effect that drives the Snow Goose to E=5 is less pronounced for Ross's Goose because the pure-Ross's flock concentrations are smaller.
- **The comparison pair pattern:** E=4 for Ross's Goose mirrors the energy step-down from Canada Goose (129, E=5) to Cackling Goose (130, E=4). In both pairs, the larger species registers one energy level higher than the smaller: Canada>Cackling, Snow>Ross's. The pattern is consistent.
- **Gentle conclusion:** E=4 provides a gentle energy step-down from the Snow Goose's overwhelming E=5 -- an appropriate conclusion to the Anatidae streak. The streak doesn't end with a climax (that was the Trumpeter at E=6, degree 128) but with a quiet coda.

## THE HIGHER NASAL CALL -- ANATOMY AND ACOUSTICS

### A Sound Distinct from Snow Goose

Ross's Goose's vocalization is diagnostically different from the Snow Goose despite the species' similar appearance:

- **Character:** A higher-pitched, nasal, squeaky call -- "kak" or a short, high grunt. The call is **weaker and squeakier** than the Snow Goose's shrill bark. Where the Snow Goose produces a forceful one-syllable "honk," the Ross's Goose produces a thinner, higher, less forceful version.
- **Frequency range:** 800-5,000 Hz. Higher than the Snow Goose (600-4,000 Hz) -- the highest-pitched goose call in the engine. The upshift in frequency is consistent with the smaller body size (smaller syrinx = higher fundamental frequency).
- **Volume:** Noticeably quieter than the Snow Goose. The smaller body produces less acoustic power. In a mixed Snow/Ross's flock, the Ross's calls are often masked by the louder Snow Goose calls.
- **Diagnostic value:** In the field, the voice is one of the **fastest ways to detect Ross's Goose** in a Snow Goose flock. The higher, squeakier quality stands out against the Snow Goose's deeper bark -- like a piccolo note within an orchestra's brass section. Experienced birders listen for the pitch difference to locate Ross's Geese in mixed flocks.
- **Flock sound:** A pure flock of Ross's Geese produces a higher-pitched, thinner version of the Snow Goose flock sound -- squeaky chattering rather than shrill roaring. The difference is immediately apparent to anyone who has heard both.

## THE SMALLEST GOOSE -- IDENTIFICATION AND SIZE

### Miniature Snow Goose?

Ross's Goose is frequently confused with Snow Goose due to similar plumage, but the size difference is dramatic:

- **Size:** Body length 53-66 cm (21-26 inches). Weight 1.0-1.6 kg (2.2-3.5 lbs). Wingspan 113-123 cm. **About two-thirds the length and half the weight of a Snow Goose.** This is a striking size difference -- like comparing a Cackling Goose to a Canada Goose.
- **Bill:** The bill is the diagnostic field mark. Ross's Goose has a **short, stubby, triangular bill** -- distinctly shorter and stubbier than the Snow Goose's longer bill. Critically, Ross's Goose **lacks the black "grinning patch"** (the dark line along the bill's cutting edge) that is diagnostic of Snow Goose. The bill base may show warty protuberances (caruncles) in adult males -- rough, bluish-gray bumps that are unique to Ross's Goose.
- **Plumage:** White morph Ross's Goose: entirely white with black wingtips, virtually identical to white morph Snow Goose except for size and bill. **Blue morph Ross's Goose** exists but is extremely rare -- far rarer than the blue morph Snow Goose. Most Ross's Geese in the PNW are white morph.
- **Head shape:** Rounder, more compact head than Snow Goose. The overall impression is of a **cuter, more compact** bird -- the Snow Goose miniaturized and rounded.
- **Field separation from Snow Goose:** In mixed flocks (the typical PNW encounter), look for: (1) significantly smaller size, (2) short stubby bill without grinning patch, (3) rounder head, (4) higher-pitched call. These four characters together are diagnostic. At distance, size comparison with adjacent Snow Geese is often the fastest cue.

### Bernard Rogan Ross

The species is named after **Bernard Rogan Ross** (1827-1874), a Hudson's Bay Company chief factor at Fort Resolution on Great Slave Lake in what is now the Northwest Territories of Canada. Ross collected natural history specimens and shipped them to the Smithsonian Institution, where ornithologist John Cassin described the goose as a new species in 1861. Ross never visited the birds' breeding grounds -- the Arctic tundra nesting sites weren't documented until the 1930s.

## PNW WINTER PRESENCE -- HIDING IN THE SNOW GOOSE FLOCKS

### Winter Range

Ross's Goose in the PNW is primarily encountered **mixed in Snow Goose flocks**:

- **Skagit Valley, Washington:** Ross's Geese are present in the Skagit Valley Snow Goose concentrations but in much smaller numbers. A careful scan of a Snow Goose flock may reveal dozens to a few hundred Ross's Geese among thousands of Snow Geese. They are a sought-after find for birders visiting the Skagit in winter.
- **Willamette Valley, Oregon:** Agricultural fields in the Willamette Valley host wintering Ross's Geese, again typically mixed with Snow Goose and other goose flocks.
- **Ridgefield NWR and other sites:** Scattered records throughout lowland western Washington and Oregon.
- **Increasing numbers:** Ross's Goose populations have been **increasing significantly** in recent decades -- a population trend that parallels and may be driven by the same agricultural subsidies fueling the Snow Goose explosion (degree 133). Ross's Geese have expanded their wintering range and increased in numbers, making them more regularly observed in the PNW than they were historically.

### Finding Ross's Goose

Finding a Ross's Goose in the PNW is a **field identification challenge** that birders enjoy:

- **The scan:** Position yourself near a Snow Goose flock. Scan systematically for birds that appear noticeably smaller. The size difference is real but can be hard to judge without direct comparison.
- **The bill:** Once a size candidate is spotted, check the bill. Short, stubby, triangular, lacking the grinning patch. This is the clincher.
- **The voice:** Listen for the higher-pitched, squeakier calls among the Snow Goose din. Voice is often the first detection cue.
- **Satisfaction:** Finding a Ross's Goose in a Snow Goose flock is a satisfying field birding achievement. The small bird hiding in the large flock is a classic identification puzzle.

## POPULATION AND CONSERVATION -- THE OTHER EXPLODING GOOSE

### Parallel to Snow Goose

Ross's Goose shares the Snow Goose's conservation irony (degree 133) in miniature:

- **Historical rarity:** Ross's Goose was once considered rare and vulnerable. The total population was estimated at only 6,000 birds in the 1950s. Conservation measures and harvest restrictions were implemented.
- **Population explosion:** Like the Snow Goose, Ross's Goose populations have **exploded** -- from roughly 6,000 in the 1950s to an estimated **1-2 million or more** today. The growth rate has been even more dramatic than the Snow Goose's in percentage terms.
- **Agricultural subsidy:** The same driver: agricultural food subsidies in winter. Waste grain, winter wheat, and other crops provide abundant food that reduces winter mortality and increases breeding success.
- **Arctic habitat concern:** Ross's Goose breeds primarily in the **Queen Maud Gulf Migratory Bird Sanctuary** in the central Canadian Arctic -- one of the most important waterfowl breeding areas in North America. The growing Ross's Goose population, combined with Snow Goose overgrazing, raises concerns about habitat degradation in this critical area.
- **Light Goose Conservation Order:** Ross's Goose is included alongside Snow Goose in the USFWS Light Goose Conservation Order (1999), which liberalized hunting to reduce populations. The two species' parallel population explosions are managed together.

### The Brighter Side

Unlike the Snow Goose, Ross's Goose was genuinely rare within living memory. Its recovery from ~6,000 to over a million is an extraordinary conservation success -- before it became a potential ecological problem. The species' story encapsulates the full arc: rarity, conservation, recovery, overshoot.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Ryder & Alisauskas 1995; USFWS)
