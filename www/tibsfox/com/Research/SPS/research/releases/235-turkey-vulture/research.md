## FIRST CATHARTIDAE -- THE NEW WORLD VULTURES ENTER

### A New Family, A New Ecological Role

The Turkey Vulture at degree 235 introduces Cathartidae to the SPS engine:

- **Cathartidae defined.** The New World vultures are a family of large, soaring birds of the Western Hemisphere. Seven species in the living family: Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*), Black Vulture (*Coragyps atratus*), California Condor (*Gymnogyps californianus*), Andean Condor (*Vultur gryphus*), Lesser Yellow-headed Vulture (*Cathartes burrovianus*), Greater Yellow-headed Vulture (*Cathartes melambrotus*), and King Vulture (*Sarcoramphus papa*). The family ranges from southern Canada through South America.
- **Old World vs. New World vultures.** Cathartidae (New World vultures) is not closely related to the Old World vultures (Accipitridae subfamily Aegypiinae). The two groups are an example of convergent evolution -- they evolved similar body plans (large, soaring, carrion-eating) independently. Old World vultures are in family Accipitridae (already entered the engine at degree 221). New World vultures are in their own family, Cathartidae, and are more closely related to storks than to Old World vultures. This is one of ornithology's more counterintuitive taxonomic truths.
- **The ecological role.** Vultures are the ecosystem's sanitation department. They eat carrion -- dead animals at various stages of decomposition -- and in doing so they prevent the spread of disease and return nutrients to the ecosystem. Vultures have specialized physiology for this work: strong stomach acid (pH ~1) that can digest and neutralize botulism toxin, anthrax spores, and other lethal pathogens that would kill most other animals. The Turkey Vulture is one of the most important ecological actors in its range.

(Sources: Cathartidae taxonomy; seven species; convergent evolution with Old World vultures; stork relationship; Aegypiinae comparison; carrion ecological role; stomach acid pH)

## THE TURKEY VULTURE -- *CATHARTES AURA*

### The Thermal Rider of Open Country

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Cathartiformes. Family: Cathartidae. Genus: *Cathartes*. Species: *aura*. The genus name *Cathartes* means "purifier" in Greek -- a reference to the bird's carrion-cleaning ecological role. The species name *aura* derives from the Arawak Indian name for the bird. The English common name "Turkey Vulture" refers to the adult's bare red head, which resembles the head of a Wild Turkey.
- **Size.** The Turkey Vulture is a large bird by any standard. Adults: 62-81 cm (24-32 in) in length, wingspan 160-183 cm (63-72 in). Weight: 0.85-2.26 kg (1.9-5 lbs). Despite the large wingspan (easily over 5 feet), the Turkey Vulture weighs less than many large hawks. The skeleton is lightly built for the soaring lifestyle.
- **Appearance.** Adults: overall blackish-brown plumage, bare red head (red in adults, gray in juveniles), pale bill. When viewed from below in flight, the underwing shows a characteristic two-toned pattern: dark wing linings and silvery-gray flight feathers. The bare red head is the adult field mark; juveniles have gray heads. The dihedral (V-shape) in the wings during soaring is the primary flight field mark.
- **The dihedral.** In flight, the Turkey Vulture holds its wings in a pronounced dihedral -- angled upward in a shallow V shape. This dihedral, combined with the bird's characteristic rocking or tilting motion in flight, makes it identifiable at great distances. No other large bird in the PNW soars in quite the same way.

## THE NON-VOICE -- E=2 AND THE MISSING SYRINX

### What Happens When a Bird Has No Voice

The Turkey Vulture's position at E=2 in the engine reflects a biological reality that is unique among birds thus far covered:

- **No syrinx.** Birds produce their vocalizations through the syrinx -- a specialized structure at the base of the trachea that is unique to birds and has no mammalian equivalent. The syrinx in songbirds and many other birds is extraordinarily complex, capable of producing intricate, musical sounds. The Turkey Vulture has no functional syrinx. It is voiceless in the biological sense that applies to most birds.
- **What it can produce.** Without a syrinx, the Turkey Vulture can only produce sounds by forcing air through the esophagus and nasal passages. The result is: hisses, grunts, and low groaning sounds. These are not vocalizations in the bird sense -- they are involuntary sound production, more similar to the digestive sounds of a large mammal than to the songs of a songbird. At the nest, a threatened Turkey Vulture hisses vigorously; the hiss has a defensive function even if it lacks the musical quality of a true bird call.

The Turkey Vulture's hissing grunt barely qualifies as a vocalization -- it is air being forced through an esophagus, not a bird singing. E=2 is the honest assessment.

## OLFACTION -- THE NOSE-USING RAPTOR

### Finding Carrion by Smell

The Turkey Vulture's most remarkable biological adaptation is its sense of smell:

- **The olfaction anomaly.** Most birds have very limited senses of smell. This is such a consistent feature of bird biology that John James Audubon conducted experiments in the 1820s trying to prove that vultures find carrion by sight rather than smell. He was wrong. The Turkey Vulture finds carrion primarily by smell -- it has an extraordinarily large olfactory bulb (the brain structure responsible for processing smell) relative to its overall brain size.
- **The olfactory bulb.** Studies of Turkey Vulture brain anatomy show an olfactory bulb that is approximately 4 times larger (relative to body size) than in a hawk or eagle. This enlarged olfactory processing capacity translates to an ability to detect the smell of carrion at extremely low concentrations -- parts per trillion of certain sulfur-containing compounds.
- **Dimethyl sulfide (DMS).** The key compound the Turkey Vulture detects is dimethyl sulfide (DMS), a sulfur compound produced in the early stages of decomposition. DMS is produced by bacteria acting on animal tissue shortly after death -- within hours of the animal dying. The Turkey Vulture can detect DMS at concentrations of 1 part per billion, allowing it to find carrion under dense forest canopy where visual detection would be impossible.
- **Pipeline gas leak detection.** The Turkey Vulture's sensitivity to DMS has practical applications: in the mid-20th century, natural gas companies discovered that Turkey Vultures congregated above underground gas pipeline leaks (pipeline operators added sulfur compounds to odorless natural gas as a safety measure). By mapping where vultures gathered, companies could locate underground leaks.
- **Black Vulture comparison.** The Black Vulture (*Coragyps atratus*), which overlaps with the Turkey Vulture in range (less so in the PNW), has limited olfaction and finds carrion primarily by watching Turkey Vultures and following them to carcasses. This interspecies kleptoparasitism (the Black Vulture stealing carrion found by Turkey Vultures) is well documented.

(Sources: Turkey Vulture olfactory bulb size; dimethyl sulfide detection; parts per trillion sensitivity; forest canopy carrion detection; pipeline gas leak story; Black Vulture kleptoparasitism)

## THERMAL SOARING -- THE PHYSICS OF EFFORTLESS FLIGHT

### How the Turkey Vulture Travels Without Flapping

- **Thermal dynamics.** Turkey Vultures are masters of thermal soaring -- using rising columns of warm air (thermals) to gain altitude without expending the energy of flapping flight. A thermal is created when the sun heats the ground unevenly; darker surfaces (pavement, bare rock, plowed fields) heat faster than lighter surfaces (grass, water), creating temperature differentials that drive air upward.
- **The soaring circuit.** A Turkey Vulture begins the day waiting for the first thermals to form (typically after 9-10 AM, when the sun has warmed the ground sufficiently). The bird rises in a thermal, circling to gain altitude, then glides to the next thermal source, covering ground in a series of rise-and-glide cycles. This soaring style allows Turkey Vultures to travel 100-200 km per day while spending minimal energy.
- **Metabolic efficiency.** The Turkey Vulture in soaring flight has a metabolic rate that is barely higher than at rest -- the thermal is doing the work. This is one of the most energy-efficient locomotion modes in the animal kingdom. The bird's large wingspan and low wing loading (weight per unit of wing area) are perfectly adapted to thermal riding.
- **The PNW thermal landscape.** The Pacific Northwest's diverse topography creates excellent thermal opportunities. East of the Cascades, the broad agricultural valleys and basalt plateaus generate strong thermals in summer. Ridge lines (particularly the Cascades themselves) create orographic lift -- air forced upward by terrain. Turkey Vultures use both.
- **The dihedral in thermals.** The Turkey Vulture's V-wing dihedral is not just a flight style -- it is a stability mechanism. By holding the wings in a slight V, the bird becomes inherently stable in thermals: if it tilts to one side, aerodynamic forces automatically correct the roll. The dihedral is the structural solution to staying upright in the turbulent air of a rising thermal column.

(Sources: Thermal soaring mechanics; Turkey Vulture wing loading; metabolic efficiency soaring; PNW thermal landscape; east-of-Cascades thermals; orographic lift Cascades; dihedral aerodynamics)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION

### A Summer Visitor

- **Seasonal presence.** Turkey Vultures are migratory in the PNW. They arrive in March-April as temperatures rise and thermals become available, and depart southward in September-October. In winter, they are largely absent from Washington and Oregon (except for occasional individuals along the southern Oregon coast). The bird is a summer resident.
- **Winter range.** PNW Turkey Vultures winter from coastal California through Mexico and Central America. The migration is one of the most impressive soaring-bird movements in North America -- thousands of vultures move through raptor watchpoints in California in fall, moving south on ridge-generated lift.
- **Range expansion.** Turkey Vulture range in the PNW has expanded northward over the past 50 years, likely in response to climate warming extending the thermal season. Breeding records now exist for areas in British Columbia where Turkey Vultures were previously only rare migrants.
- **PNW habitat.** In Washington State, Turkey Vultures are most common east of the Cascades (Columbia Plateau, eastern Washington agricultural areas) where open country, abundant road kill, and strong summer thermals provide optimal conditions. West-side populations exist but are less dense; the more forested terrain generates weaker thermals and the wet, cool climate is less favorable.

(Sources: Turkey Vulture PNW migration timing; winter range California-Mexico; raptor migration counts California; range expansion northward; east-of-Cascades habitat; west-side distribution)

## CONSERVATION

### Stable in North America, Threatened by Human Activity

- **Current status.** The Turkey Vulture is listed as Least Concern by the IUCN. North American populations are stable or growing. The species has benefited from the proliferation of road kill (automotive traffic produces reliable carrion) and adaptation to agricultural landscapes.
- **Threats.** Turkey Vultures face several anthropogenic threats:
  - **Lead poisoning:** Turkey Vultures consume animals killed by lead ammunition. Lead bullet fragments in gut piles and carcasses are ingested during feeding. Lead poisoning is a significant cause of Turkey Vulture mortality and a major factor in California Condor mortality (a related species).
  - **Secondary poisoning:** Rodenticides (particularly anticoagulant rat poisons) accumulate in rodents, which are consumed by vultures.
  - **Collision mortality:** Turkey Vultures are commonly killed by vehicle collision on highways (attracted to road kill) and by power line collision and electrocution.
- **The California Condor comparison.** The California Condor (*Gymnogyps californianus*), another Cathartidae member and the Turkey Vulture's taxonomic relative, is one of North America's most critically endangered birds -- reduced to 27 individuals in 1987, now at ~500+ through captive breeding programs. Lead poisoning is the primary limiting factor. The Turkey Vulture faces the same threat but at a less critical level.

(Sources: Turkey Vulture IUCN status; lead poisoning from ammunition; rodenticide secondary poisoning; vehicle collision mortality; California Condor comparison; lead bullet gut pile problem)
