### The Vocal Cetaceans Arrive

Degree 188 marks the entry of Delphinidae (dolphins):

- **FIRST DELPHINIDAE.** The dolphin family enters. Delphinidae is the largest cetacean family -- over 30 species worldwide, including bottlenose dolphins, orcas, and pilot whales. The Pacific White-sided Dolphin is the representative entry.
- **The silence breaks.** After two Phocoenidae entries -- Dall's Porpoise (186, E=5 rooster-tail splash but silent voice) and Harbor Porpoise (187, E=1 absolute silence) -- the dolphin arrives with full audible vocalizations. Squawks, whistles, clicks in the human-audible range. The cetacean silence is broken.
- **Third cetacean family.** Phocoenidae (186-187) were the first and second cetaceans. Delphinidae (188) is the third. Two porpoises, then a dolphin. The cetacean realm expands.
- **Dolphin vs porpoise.** Dolphins (Delphinidae) differ from porpoises (Phocoenidae): conical teeth (vs spade-shaped), longer beaks, more social, generally larger, and critically -- dolphins whistle. The Pacific White-sided Dolphin's whistles mark a fundamental acoustic difference from the porpoises.

## E=5 -- SQUAWKS, WHISTLES, AND CLICKS

### Why E=5

The Pacific White-sided Dolphin registers at E=5:

- **Audible vocalization.** Unlike the two preceding porpoises, the Pacific White-sided Dolphin produces sounds humans can HEAR. Squawks, whistles, and clicks span a wide frequency range including audible bands (2-20 kHz).
- **Squawks.** Burst-pulse sounds -- rapid sequences of clicks produced so fast they merge into a buzzy, squawk-like sound. Used in social contexts, often during feeding or excitement. Loud and distinctive.
- **Whistles.** Frequency-modulated tonal sounds used for social communication. Individual dolphins may have signature whistles. The whistle is the acoustic hallmark that distinguishes dolphins from porpoises.
- **Clicks.** Echolocation clicks used for navigation and prey detection. Unlike porpoise NBHF clicks, dolphin clicks span a broader frequency range including lower frequencies audible to humans.
- **Volume.** The overall vocal output of a Pacific White-sided Dolphin pod is substantial -- squawks, whistles, and clicks overlapping in a complex social chorus. A pod of 50-100 dolphins is acoustically impressive.
- **Why E=5?** The rich, audible vocal repertoire is a dramatic contrast to the porpoise silence (E=1, E=5-splash-only). But the dolphin's sounds are moderate in absolute volume compared to, say, a humpback whale song or a sea lion colony roar. E=5 reflects: audible, varied, socially rich, and impressively diverse vocalization -- but not the loudest in the marine mammal realm.

(Sources: Pacific White-sided Dolphin acoustics; Oswald et al. 2007; Soldevilla et al. 2010; engine internal analysis)

## THE ACROBAT -- AERIAL DISPLAYS

### Leaping, Spinning, Somersaulting

The Pacific White-sided Dolphin is one of the most acrobatic cetaceans:

- **Aerial displays.** Frequent and spectacular leaps, belly flops, side slaps, and somersaults. The dolphins launch completely out of the water, rotating in mid-air before re-entering. These displays are visible from great distances.
- **Social acrobatics.** The acrobatic behavior is often social -- multiple dolphins leaping simultaneously, apparently in coordination. The displays may serve communication, play, or parasite removal functions.
- **Speed.** Swift swimmers, capable of sustained speeds of 15-20 mph and bursts up to 25 mph. The speed enables the aerial launches.
- **Contrast with porpoises.** The Dall's Porpoise (186) is fast but does not leap -- its rooster tail is a surface phenomenon. The Harbor Porpoise (187) barely breaks the surface. The Pacific White-sided Dolphin LAUNCHES itself into the air. The progression from cryptic surfacing (187) to full aerial display (188) mirrors the acoustic progression from silence to song.

(Sources: Pacific White-sided Dolphin behavior; acrobatic displays; field observations; engine internal analysis)

## COMPLEX SOCIAL COMMUNICATION

### Pods of 10-100+

The Pacific White-sided Dolphin is highly social:

- **Pod size.** Typically travels in groups of 10-100 individuals. Occasionally forms "super-pods" of several hundred to over 1,000. These aggregations are among the largest cetacean groups in the North Pacific.
- **Social structure.** Fluid fission-fusion society -- individuals move between groups. Social bonds vary in duration and strength. The social complexity requires sophisticated communication.
- **Vocal coordination.** The squawks, whistles, and clicks serve social functions: coordinating group movement, maintaining contact, signaling alarm, facilitating cooperative feeding. The vocal repertoire enables the social complexity.
- **Mixed-species associations.** Frequently associates with other cetaceans -- Dall's Porpoises (degree 186!), Northern Right Whale Dolphins, Risso's Dolphins, and humpback whales. These mixed-species groups may enhance foraging or predator detection.
- **Play behavior.** Dolphins engage in play: surfing waves, tossing seaweed, chasing each other, engaging in physical contact games. Play is a hallmark of social intelligence.

(Sources: Pacific White-sided Dolphin social behavior; pod dynamics; mixed-species associations; engine internal analysis)

## PUGET SOUND AND THE OUTER COAST

### Distribution

- **Outer coast.** The primary habitat is the continental shelf and slope of the outer coast -- from the California/Oregon border north through Washington and British Columbia to Alaska.
- **Strait of Juan de Fuca.** Regularly enters the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Visible from whale-watching boats operating from the San Juan Islands.
- **Northern Puget Sound.** Occasionally enters northern Puget Sound, particularly during years with strong prey concentrations.
- **Not a deep-Sound resident.** Unlike the Harbor Porpoise (year-round throughout Puget Sound), the Pacific White-sided Dolphin is primarily an outer coast and straits species. Its Puget Sound presence is intermittent.
- **Population.** Estimated 26,000-30,000 in the eastern North Pacific. Washington waters: several thousand intermittently present.
- **Diet.** Squid, anchovies, herring, hake, lanternfish. Cooperative feeding documented -- dolphins working together to herd fish schools.

(Sources: NOAA; WDFW; North Pacific cetacean surveys; Pacific White-sided Dolphin ecology; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Pacific White-sided Dolphin

**Squawks (Burst-Pulse Sounds):**
- **Frequency:** 2-30 kHz (partially audible to humans).
- **Character:** Rapid click trains merging into buzzy, squawk-like sounds. Loud. Social.
- **Context:** Feeding excitement, social interaction, agonistic encounters.
- **Duration:** 0.1-2 seconds per burst.

**Whistles:**
- **Frequency:** 5-20 kHz (audible to humans).
- **Character:** Frequency-modulated tonal sounds. Rising, falling, or undulating. Individual signature whistles possible.
- **Context:** Social communication, group coordination, contact maintenance.
- **Duration:** 0.2-1.5 seconds per whistle.

**Clicks:**
- **Frequency:** Broadband, 2-130 kHz (lower range audible, upper ultrasonic).
- **Character:** Short, broadband clicks for echolocation. More varied than porpoise NBHF clicks.
- **Context:** Navigation, prey detection, obstacle avoidance.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=5. Rich, diverse, socially complex. Audible vocalizations. A dramatic contrast to the porpoise silence that preceded it. The cetacean silence breaks at degree 188.
