The Orographic Mechanism

Pacific storm systems approach Washington from the west with established wind speeds. As the flow encounters the Cascade Range, it is forced upward by the terrain. At the Cascade Crest, the terrain geometry narrows as the crest approaches, creating a venturi effect: air that was moving at 50-60 mph in the valley accelerates to 80-110 mph at the exposed ridge. The mechanism is self-reinforcing as long as the synoptic-scale conditions (the large-scale atmospheric pressure gradient) remain established. Under the right conditions, the ridge wind holds at E=9 intensity for 6-12+ hours — not a gust, but a sustained wall of maximum acoustic energy.

The Krummholz as Aeolian Instruments

The stunted, wind-flagged conifers at the treeline transition are physically shaped by decades of wind into their Aeolian instrument forms. Subalpine Fir, Mountain Hemlock, and Whitebark Pine grow in krummholz form above 5,000 feet in the Cascades: branches extend horizontally downwind, trunks permanently bent away from the prevailing weather. Their branch diameters — 0.5 to 5 cm — produce Aeolian tones at 132 to 1,320 Hz at hurricane-force wind. The trees were shaped by the wind over decades; in the storm, they give back the wind's energy as tone. The acoustic instrument and the acoustic source are the same system.

Sustained vs. Peak — Why This Is E=9

Most high-wind events produce brief peak gusts. The degree-335 event is specifically the sustained condition: when orographic amplification at the Cascade Crest sustains hurricane-force wind for extended periods, the acoustic event becomes continuous rather than episodic. Speech communication becomes impossible. The dynamic wind pressure of 74+ mph exerts approximately 130 Pa horizontal force. The acoustic field is total: the wind occupies the entire spectrum and the entire space of the ridge. Nothing else is audible. E=9 through sustained maximum duration.
