## THE CASCADE RIDGE SETTING -- WHERE MAXIMUM WIND OCCURS

### The Specific Geography of High-Wind Alpine Environments

The Washington Cascades provide the specific conditions for maximum sustained wind:

- **The Cascade Crest.** The Cascade Range in Washington State runs roughly north-south, creating the primary topographic barrier between the wet maritime climate of western Washington and the dry continental climate of eastern Washington. The Cascade Crest -- the line of high peaks and ridges that defines the range's spine -- is the dividing line. Peaks along or near the crest include Mount Rainier (4,392 m / 14,411 ft), Glacier Peak (3,213 m / 10,541 ft), Mount Baker (3,286 m / 10,781 ft), and many named and unnamed ridges between peaks at 5,000-8,000+ feet elevation.
- **The exposed alpine ridge.** Below treeline, vegetation protects the ground from the full force of wind. Above treeline -- in Washington's Cascades, typically at 5,000-6,500 feet depending on aspect and latitude -- the ridge is exposed. At the crest ridges, there is no forest buffer, no vegetation taller than krummholz (wind-flagged, gnarled conifers that grow nearly horizontal in permanent high-wind zones) and alpine meadow plants. The ridge surface is rock, ice, and occasionally snow -- all highly efficient acoustic reflectors and conductors.
- **The Cascade weather station record.** The National Park Service and NOAA weather stations at Cascade ridge elevations document the wind record. At Paradise weather station on Mount Rainier (1,646 m / 5,400 ft, but exposed), 100+ mph wind gusts have been recorded multiple times. At crystal Mountain summit stations (2,000+ m), wind speeds during major Pacific storm passages regularly exceed 80-100 mph in gusts. The sustained wind records -- not gusts but sustained speeds -- show 60-80 mph averages during major events at the most exposed ridge positions.
- **The orographic Cascade Crest.** The Cascades function as a natural wind concentrator: Pacific storm systems with west-to-east flow encounter the range and are forced upward (the orographic lift that produces the heavy precipitation on the windward western slopes). As the air rises and approaches the Cascade Crest, it accelerates -- the venturi effect of the narrowing terrain geometry concentrates the flow. Ridge-capping winds that are already 50-60 mph in the valleys can reach 80-110 mph at exposed crest ridges. The ridge is the maximum.

## THE ACOUSTIC PHYSICS OF HURRICANE-FORCE WIND

### What Sustained 74+ mph Wind Sounds Like

The acoustic properties of hurricane-force (Beaufort Scale 12: 74+ mph) sustained wind:

- **Beaufort Scale context.** The Beaufort Scale classifies wind speed by its observed effects: Scale 12 (hurricane force) is defined as 64 knots (74 mph) or greater sustained speed. On an exposed alpine ridge, sustained Beaufort 12 wind is a full acoustic and physical event -- it is not simply loud but physically overwhelming. The Beaufort Scale 12 threshold is relevant here: above this threshold, wind produces qualitatively different acoustic and physical effects than lower-speed wind.
- **Aeolian acoustics.** When wind flows past obstacles -- rocks, ridges, ice formations, krummholz, exposed cables or structures if present -- it generates acoustic energy through several mechanisms: (1) Aeolian tone (von Karman vortex street): as wind flows past a circular obstacle (a wire, a thin rock edge, a flagged tree branch), it sheds alternating vortices at a frequency determined by wind speed and obstacle diameter (f = 0.2V/D, where V is wind speed and D is obstacle diameter). At 74 mph, a 1-cm diameter obstacle produces a tone at approximately 660 Hz -- the characteristic "howling wire" sound. (2) Turbulent boundary layer noise: at very high wind speeds, the turbulent boundary layer (the zone of chaotic flow between the ground surface and the freestream flow above) produces broadband noise across the full acoustic range. (3) Impact percussion: loose rock, ice crystals, and debris entrained in hurricane-force wind impact the ridge surface continuously, producing impulsive acoustic events across the spectrum.
- **The ridge howl.** The combination of Aeolian tones from multiple obstacles (each at a different diameter and orientation produces a different frequency), turbulent boundary layer noise (continuous broadband), and impact percussion creates the "ridge howl" -- the characteristic sound of an exposed alpine ridge in high-wind conditions. This is not a single tone but a complex, spectrally rich continuous event with internal structure: the Aeolian tones from specific obstacles create partially tonal components against the broadband turbulence background.
- **The amplitude.** At sustained 74+ mph, the acoustic output of a high Cascade ridge is in the range of 90-110 dB at the surface -- comparable to a large jet aircraft at moderate distance, or a sustained industrial environment. The key difference from these sources is the spatial distribution: the wind sound comes from everywhere simultaneously, not from a point source. The listener is inside the acoustic event rather than adjacent to it.

- **The difference between peak and sustained.** Most weather events have a peak intensity that is brief -- a peak gust lasts seconds. The degree-335 event is specifically a sustained hurricane-force wind that holds at 74+ mph for hours. The acoustic event is not a series of intense moments with recovery between; it is a continuous acoustic engagement at maximum intensity.
- **The orographic mechanism for sustained intensity.** Orographic amplification at a fixed ridge location is not highly variable -- once the synoptic-scale (large-scale atmospheric) conditions are established (the right wind direction, the right pressure gradient, the right moisture loading to prevent stabilization), the ridge wind can be sustained at maximum intensity for extended periods. Unlike valley or coastal winds that are modified by local terrain and thermal effects, a ridge wind in the right synoptic conditions can hold at E=9 intensity for 6-12+ hours.

## THE CASCADE ALPINE RIDGE ENVIRONMENT

### The Physical Character of the Acoustic Setting

The ridge environment in a sustained hurricane-force wind event:

- **Above treeline.** At the crest ridge above treeline, the acoustic environment is stripped of forest damping. In the forest below, wind energy is absorbed, scattered, and converted by the canopy; the acoustic level drops substantially. On the exposed ridge, there is no canopy -- the wind's acoustic energy reaches the surface and the listener directly. The ridge is an acoustic maximum compared to the forest below it.
- **Krummholz as Aeolian instrument.** The stunted, wind-flagged conifers (Subalpine Fir *Abies lasiocarpa*, Mountain Hemlock *Tsuga mertensiana*, Whitebark Pine *Pinus albicaulis*) that survive at the treeline transition zone are physically shaped by the wind over decades. Their branches are extended horizontally downwind, their trunks permanently bent. In hurricane-force wind, these krummholz provide Aeolian tones at the specific frequencies determined by their branch diameters (0.5-5 cm diameter branches produce Aeolian tones at 200-1,500 Hz at 74 mph). The krummholz are acoustic instruments shaped by decades of wind exposure.
- **Rock and ice.** The exposed ridge surface -- volcanic rock (basalt, andesite), metamorphic formations, glacial ice and firn if present at elevation -- reflects and concentrates the wind's acoustic energy. Rock faces perpendicular to the wind direction act as acoustic reflectors; rock faces parallel to the wind become Aeolian tone generators at the sharp edges.
- **The absence of other sounds.** In sustained hurricane-force wind, virtually all other sounds are masked -- bird calls, insect sounds, water sounds, any sounds from below treeline. The wind is acoustically total: it occupies the entire spectrum and the entire acoustic space of the ridge. The silence of everything else is part of the E=9 event.
