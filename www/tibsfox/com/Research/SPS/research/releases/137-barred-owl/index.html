Barred Owl

Strix varia (Strigidae) — E=4 — “Who Cooks for You?”
“Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?” — the rhythmic 8-note phrase. E=4 — measured, deliberate. 400–550 Hz. Mature forest resident. Third consecutive Strigidae (135–137). Eastern invader — range expansion into PNW old-growth. Spotted Owl conflict — the most significant interspecific conflict in PNW conservation.

“Who Cooks for You?” — E=4

The most recognizable owl mnemonic in North American birding. Eight notes in two phrases: “Who cooks for YOU?” (4 notes, rising) followed by “Who cooks for you-ALL?” (4–5 notes, drawn-out finish). 400–550 Hz fundamental — higher than the Great Horned Owl’s 250–400 Hz. Rich, resonant, slightly hoarse timbre. Calls day and night — more diurnal vocal activity than most owls.

Spotted Owl Conflict

The Barred Owl’s expansion into the PNW has created a conservation crisis for the Northern Spotted Owl (Strix occidentalis caurina, SPS degree 4, E=3). The Barred Owl is 20% larger, has a broader diet, and uses a wider range of habitats. In areas of overlap, Spotted Owl populations decline. USFWS has initiated controversial Barred Owl removal programs. Hybridization (“Sparred Owls”) threatens genetic integrity.

Range Expansion — Eastern to Western

Historically an eastern North American species, the Barred Owl expanded westward via the boreal forests of Canada as fire suppression and reforestation created a continuous forest corridor. First documented in British Columbia in the 1940s–1960s. Reached Washington and Oregon by the 1970s–1980s. Now abundant throughout the PNW and expanding into California.
