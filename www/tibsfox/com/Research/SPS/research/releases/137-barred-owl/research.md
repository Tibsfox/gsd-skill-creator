## E=4 -- THE MEASURED PHRASE

### Why E=4

The Barred Owl's "Who cooks for you?" hooting registers at E=4 -- measured, rhythmic, and deliberate:

- **Rhythmic phrasing:** The Barred Owl's signature call is a rhythmic, accented 8-note phrase: "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" (transcribed as "hoo-hoo-to-HOO-oo... hoo-hoo-to-HOO-ooo-aw"). The phrasing is deliberate and measured -- each syllable clearly articulated with a distinct rhythm. This is not the Great Horned Owl's deep, resonant booming; this is a structured, almost conversational phrase.
- **Moderate amplitude:** The Barred Owl's hoot is loud enough to carry through dense forest (audible at 200-400 meters) but does not approach the booming power of the Great Horned Owl or the trumpeting of waterfowl. The amplitude is appropriate for dense canopy communication.
- **Deliberate pace:** The calls are delivered at a measured pace -- approximately one 8-note phrase every 10-20 seconds during active calling bouts. The tempo is unhurried, suggesting confidence and established territory.
- **E=4 vs. GHO E=4:** Both the Barred Owl and the Great Horned Owl solo hoot (degree 135) register at E=4, but for different acoustic reasons. The GHO's E=4 comes from its deep resonance and power moderated by intermittent delivery. The Barred Owl's E=4 comes from its rhythmic phrasing and moderate amplitude. Different acoustic characters at the same energy level.
- **E=4 not E=5:** The "Who cooks for you?" call does not reach the combined power of the GHO pair duet (E=5) or the intensity of battle turntablism (E=6 on S36). It is a measured, deliberate vocalization -- strong but not overwhelming. E=4 is the appropriate calibration for this specific call. (The Barred Owl's caterwauling, documented at degree 138, will reach E=6.)

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## THE VOCAL MNEMONIC -- "WHO COOKS FOR YOU?"

### The Most Recognizable Owl Phrase

The Barred Owl's "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" is arguably the **most memorable vocal mnemonic in North American birding**:

- **The mnemonic:** Birders have transcribed the Barred Owl's primary territorial hoot as "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" The mnemonic captures the rhythm and emphasis of the actual call with remarkable accuracy:
  - "Who cooks for YOU?" -- four notes with emphasis on the final note, a rising inflection
  - "Who cooks for you-ALL?" -- five notes with the final two notes sliding downward in a distinctive drawn-out finish
- **Acoustic structure:** The call consists of 8 notes delivered in two phrases. The fundamental frequency ranges from 400-550 Hz -- significantly higher than the Great Horned Owl's 250-400 Hz. The voice has a distinctive rich, resonant quality described variously as "hoarse," "throaty," and "barking." The sound is less deep than the GHO but richer and more textured.
- **Individual variation:** While the basic structure is consistent, individual Barred Owls show variation in the exact phrasing, timing, and tonal quality. Researchers have used these individual variations to identify specific owls by voice -- acoustic fingerprinting.
- **Learning the sound:** For beginning birders, the Barred Owl's call is often the first owl vocalization they learn, precisely because the mnemonic is so effective. The "Who cooks for you?" phrase, once heard, is impossible to forget. The match between the English mnemonic and the actual acoustic pattern is closer than for almost any other bird species.
- **Calling context:** The "Who cooks for you?" call is primarily a territorial and contact call, given by both males and females (with the female's voice slightly higher). It is most commonly heard at dusk, through the night, and at dawn, but Barred Owls will also call during the day, especially on overcast or rainy days -- more diurnal vocal activity than the Great Horned Owl.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Mazur & James 2000)

## RANGE EXPANSION -- THE EASTERN INVADER

### Historical Range

The Barred Owl was historically an **eastern North American species**:

- **Historical distribution:** Barred Owls were native to eastern North America, from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains. Their preferred habitat -- extensive, mature, deciduous and mixed forests with large trees for nesting cavities -- was abundant in eastern forests.
- **Western absence:** Prior to the 20th century, Barred Owls were absent from western North America. The Great Plains grasslands formed a barrier between the eastern forests and the western forests.
- **Northern route:** The westward expansion followed a **northern route** across the boreal forests of Canada. As European settlement and fire suppression allowed forests to grow across previously open landscapes in the northern prairies and foothills, a corridor of suitable habitat formed. The Barred Owl followed this corridor westward.

### The Westward March

The Barred Owl's expansion into the Pacific Northwest is one of the most significant avian range expansions in North American history:

- **Timeline:** Barred Owls were first documented in British Columbia in the 1940s-1960s. By the 1970s-1980s, they had reached Washington and Oregon. By the 1990s-2000s, they were established throughout the Pacific Northwest and expanding into California. The expansion continues.
- **Causes:** Multiple factors enabled the expansion:
  - **Forest corridor:** Fire suppression and reforestation in the northern prairies created a continuous forest corridor from east to west.
  - **Habitat generalism:** Barred Owls are habitat generalists compared to Spotted Owls -- they can thrive in a wider range of forest types, including younger forests and fragmented landscapes.
  - **Dietary flexibility:** Barred Owls eat a broader range of prey than Spotted Owls -- mammals, birds, amphibians, fish, crayfish, insects. This dietary flexibility allows them to exploit diverse habitats.
  - **Competitive advantage:** In areas of overlap, Barred Owls outcompete Spotted Owls through larger body size, broader diet, and more aggressive behavior.
- **Current status:** Barred Owls are now abundant throughout the PNW and continue to expand southward into California. They occupy habitats ranging from old-growth forest to suburban parks to urban greenbelts.

(Sources: Livezey 2009; Wiens et al. 2014; USFWS)

## THE SPOTTED OWL CONFLICT

### The Most Significant Interspecific Conflict in PNW Conservation

The Barred Owl's arrival in the PNW has created a **conservation crisis** for the Northern Spotted Owl:

- **Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*):** Documented at SPS degree 4 (E=3). The Spotted Owl is a federally listed threatened species, dependent on old-growth and mature forest habitat. Its decline has been one of the most contentious conservation issues in the PNW since the 1990s "timber wars."
- **Competitive exclusion:** In areas where Barred Owls and Spotted Owls co-occur, the Barred Owl consistently dominates:
  - **Larger body size:** Barred Owls average about 20% larger than Spotted Owls. In direct encounters, the larger Barred Owl prevails.
  - **Broader diet:** Barred Owls can exploit food resources that Spotted Owls cannot, giving them a competitive advantage in shared habitat.
  - **Habitat flexibility:** Barred Owls use a wider range of forest types, meaning they are not restricted to the same old-growth habitat that Spotted Owls require.
  - **Hybridization:** Barred Owls and Spotted Owls have been documented hybridizing, producing "Sparred Owls." Hybridization threatens the genetic integrity of the Spotted Owl population.
- **Population decline:** Spotted Owl populations have declined dramatically in areas where Barred Owls have established. Annual survival rates decrease, territory occupancy drops, and reproduction declines when Barred Owls are present.
- **USFWS response:** In 2013, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service initiated experimental Barred Owl removal programs -- killing Barred Owls in Spotted Owl habitat to assess whether Spotted Owl populations recover. This controversial management action highlights the severity of the conflict. The results have shown that Spotted Owl populations do stabilize or recover when Barred Owls are removed, confirming that the Barred Owl is a primary driver of Spotted Owl decline.
- **Ethical complexity:** The Barred Owl removal program raises profound ethical questions: Is it appropriate to kill one native species to protect another? Is the Barred Owl's expansion "natural" (following a natural corridor) or "anthropogenic" (enabled by human land management that created the corridor)? These questions have no easy answers and place the Barred Owl at the center of one of conservation biology's most challenging dilemmas.

(Sources: Wiens et al. 2014; USFWS; Diller et al. 2016; Dugger et al. 2016)

## MATURE FOREST HABITAT

### Habitat Characteristics

The Barred Owl in the PNW occupies habitat that significantly overlaps with the Spotted Owl's:

- **Old-growth and mature forest:** Like the Spotted Owl, the Barred Owl in the PNW is most commonly found in old-growth and mature coniferous forest. Douglas-fir, western red cedar, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce form the primary canopy.
- **Dense canopy:** Barred Owls prefer forests with dense canopy cover (>70%), which provides protection from predators (primarily the Great Horned Owl) and maintains the cool, moist microclimate they favor.
- **Cavity nesting:** Barred Owls nest in large tree cavities, broken-topped snags, and abandoned raptor or crow nests. The availability of large snags and cavity trees is a habitat requirement -- one of the reasons they are associated with mature and old-growth forest.
- **Habitat generalism:** Critically, Barred Owls are more flexible than Spotted Owls in their habitat requirements. They will use second-growth forest, fragmented forest, riparian corridors, and even suburban areas with large trees. This flexibility is a key competitive advantage over the habitat-specialist Spotted Owl.
- **Water association:** Barred Owls show a strong association with water -- streams, rivers, wetlands, and lakeshores. This is partly because they hunt aquatic prey (fish, crayfish, amphibians) and partly because riparian areas provide the dense, structurally complex vegetation they prefer.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mazur & James 2000; Livezey 2009)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the "Who Cooks for You?" Call

The Barred Owl's acoustic contribution is one of the most distinctive in the PNW forest:

- **Frequency range:** 400-550 Hz fundamental, significantly higher than the Great Horned Owl (250-400 Hz). The voice is in a higher register -- rich and resonant but not bass-register.
- **Rhythmic structure:** The 8-note phrase has a distinctive rhythm: four notes ("Who cooks for YOU?") followed by four-to-five notes ("Who cooks for you-ALL?"). The emphasis patterns create a speech-like quality that makes the mnemonic so effective.
- **Tonal quality:** Rich, resonant, slightly hoarse or "barking." The timbre is warmer and more textured than the Great Horned Owl's clean, resonant boom. Some describe it as having a nasal or throaty quality.
- **Calling pattern:** Active calling bouts can last several minutes, with phrases delivered every 10-20 seconds. Both sexes call, with the female's voice slightly higher.
- **Daytime calling:** Unlike the Great Horned Owl, the Barred Owl frequently calls during the day, especially on overcast or rainy days. In the PNW's frequently cloudy climate, daytime Barred Owl calls are common.
- **Seasonal variation:** Calling increases during the breeding season (February-April in the PNW) and during fall territorial establishment. But Barred Owls call year-round -- one of the most vocally active owls.

### Acoustic Comparison with Spotted Owl

The Barred Owl and Spotted Owl have related but distinguishable vocalizations:

| Feature | Barred Owl | Spotted Owl |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Primary call** | "Who cooks for you?" (8 notes) | 4-note "hoo... hoo-hoo... hooo" |
| **Frequency** | 400-550 Hz | 500-700 Hz |
| **Rhythm** | Accented, speech-like | More even, measured |
| **Volume** | Moderately loud | Softer |
| **Energy** | E=4 | E=3 (degree 4) |
| **Calling frequency** | Very vocal, day and night | Less frequent, primarily nocturnal |

The acoustic comparison highlights the Barred Owl's competitive advantage: it is louder, more frequent in calling, and more willing to call during the day. The Spotted Owl's softer, less frequent calling may contribute to its displacement -- in the acoustic landscape, the Barred Owl's voice dominates.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Mazur & James 2000; engine internal analysis)
