### The Irruptive Presence and the Radiation Budget: Stochastic Signal as Climate Data

Pass 1 documented the Pine Siskin as the engine's first irruptive species — present in some years and absent in others, with no fixed calendar schedule, driven by conifer seed crop conditions that are effectively unpredictable at the individual-year level. Pass 2 connects this to Explorer 7's radiation budget measurement and to the concept of stochastic presence as ecological data.

Explorer 7 measured Earth's radiation budget — the balance between incoming solar energy and outgoing thermal/reflected energy. This budget is not constant. It fluctuates with cloud cover, albedo changes, volcanic aerosols, greenhouse gas concentrations, and solar output variations. The fluctuations are not periodic — they are driven by complex, multi-variable conditions that resist simple prediction. The radiation budget is a stochastic signal: its value at any given moment reflects the accumulated state of dozens of interacting variables, and its year-to-year variation is governed by the same kind of ecological complexity that drives Pine Siskin irruptions.

The Pine Siskin's presence in the Puget Sound lowlands is a biological radiation budget: it reflects the balance between seed production (energy input to the siskin population) and seed consumption (energy output). When the conifer seed crop is abundant across the boreal and montane west, siskins stay near their breeding grounds — the energy budget is balanced, and there is no pressure to move. When the seed crop fails (a stochastic event driven by weather, mast-cycling trees, insect damage), the budget goes negative — outgoing energy (metabolic demand) exceeds incoming energy (food availability) — and the siskins irrupt. They flood south and downslope into habitats where they are not normally found, appearing at feeders, in parks, in suburban conifers, filling the air with their ascending zhreee calls.

The irruption IS the measurement. Like Explorer 7's instruments detecting a radiation imbalance, the siskin's presence in the lowlands detects a food imbalance in the boreal zone. The bird is the thermometer. Its appearance says: somewhere to the north, the energy budget has gone negative, and the surplus population has spilled south. Its absence says: the budget is balanced, the birds are where they should be. You do not need to visit the boreal forest to know whether the seed crop succeeded. The siskins tell you by showing up — or not.

Usnea longissima's presence/absence is the same kind of stochastic indicator: the lichen is present when atmospheric conditions have maintained sufficient quality over sufficient time, and absent when pollution or canopy disturbance has degraded conditions below the lichen's tolerance threshold. Like the siskin, the lichen is a biological sensor whose presence or absence conveys information about a complex, multi-variable environmental state.

### The Zhreee as Synthesizer Sound: First Electronic Acoustic Analog

Pass 1 documented the Pine Siskin's ascending zhreee as the engine's first "electronic" sound in biological form — a buzzy, harmonically rich, frequency-sweeping call that sounds like a sawtooth oscillator with an ascending pitch sweep. Pass 2 develops this as the first acoustic analog to electronic synthesis in the engine and connects it to Explorer 7's novel instrumentation (Suomi's radiometer).

Suomi's radiometer on Explorer 7 was the first instrument designed specifically to measure Earth's thermal emission from orbit — a new kind of sensor that had no equivalent in previous Explorer missions. The instrument detected infrared radiation that was invisible to the photocell scanners on earlier missions. It measured what other instruments could not see. The Pine Siskin's zhreee is the acoustic analog: a sound type that no previous species in the engine produces, one that the engine's existing acoustic vocabulary cannot categorize without borrowing from electronic music terminology (sawtooth, LFO, frequency sweep).
