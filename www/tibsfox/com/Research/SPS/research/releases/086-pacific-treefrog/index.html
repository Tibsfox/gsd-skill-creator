AMPHIBIA Returns — A New Class Re-Enters the Engine

After 25 consecutive mammalian entries (degrees 61–85) and before that 16 consecutive bird-to-bird substitutions replacing amphibians in the CSV (degrees 45–60), an AMPHIBIAN finally appears in its own right. The Pacific Treefrog is the first amphibian to enter the engine as a primary species. Amphibia re-enters — a new class returns. This is one of the engine’s most significant structural events: the class barrier breaks.

Night Chorus — Wall of Sound (87th Acoustic Element)

The krek-krek night chorus is not an individual call but a collective breeding chorus. Hundreds or thousands of Pacific Treefrogs calling simultaneously from marsh edges on spring nights. The result is a wall of sound — an amplified, layered, continuous chorus that dominates the marsh soundscape. E=4 — the chorus is LOUD, audible from hundreds of meters. This is the most iconic sound of PNW wetlands — the sound that defines spring nights in the Pacific Northwest.

Hylidae — 37th Family — New Family

Hylidae (tree frog family) is a NEW FAMILY — the 37th family in the engine. This breaks the Cricetidae streak (83–85) and introduces not just a new family but a new CLASS (Amphibia). The family jump from Cricetidae (Mammalia) to Hylidae (Amphibia) crosses the class boundary.

The Postal Service Parallel

Degree 86 pairs Pacific Treefrog with The Postal Service in Part A — Indie Electronic, E=4. Both are walls of sound: the Postal Service layers programmed beats, synths, and voice into a dense electronic texture. The Pacific Treefrog night chorus layers hundreds of individual krek-krek calls into a dense acoustic texture. Both E=4. Both collective: the Postal Service is Gibbard + Tamborello combining distinct contributions. The treefrog chorus is hundreds of individuals combining identical calls. Both create something larger than any individual element.

The Sound: Amplified Krek-Krek Wall of Sound (87th Acoustic Element)

The call: The Pacific Treefrog’s call is a two-note “krek-ek, krek-ek” — a loud, high-pitched, rapidly repeated call produced by males from marsh edges during breeding season (late winter through spring). Individual calls are loud for such a small frog (approximately 80 dB at 1 meter). When hundreds or thousands call simultaneously, the result is a wall of sound — a continuous, pulsing chorus that fills the marsh soundscape [1, 2, 3].

Why E=4: (1) Individual calls are already loud for body size — one of the loudest anurans relative to size. (2) The chorus is amplified by numbers — hundreds calling simultaneously. (3) Audible from hundreds of meters. (4) Dominates the marsh soundscape on spring nights. (5) E=4 reflects the overwhelming collective volume. This is the loudest SPS entry in the Amphibia class.

Taxonomy: Pseudacris regilla

Etymology: Pseudacris from Greek pseudes (“false”) + akris (“locust”), referring to the insect-like call. regilla from Latin “regal” or “little queen.” Formerly classified as Hyla regilla. Reclassified to Pseudacris based on molecular phylogenetics [1, 2].

Classification: Class Amphibia — AMPHIBIA RETURNS. Order Anura (frogs). Family Hylidae — NEW FAMILY (37th in the engine). Genus Pseudacris — NEW GENUS. Also known as Pacific Chorus Frog [1, 2].

Ecology: The Spring Chorus

Habitat: Marshes, ponds, wetlands, wet meadows, streams. Also found in gardens, forests, and grasslands — remarkably adaptable. Breeds in standing water. Marsh habitat shared with Muskrat (85), Wood Duck (58), Mallard (59) [1, 2, 3].

Activity: Primarily nocturnal during breeding season. The night chorus is the defining behavior: males call from marsh edges, often partially submerged, from late winter through spring.

Breeding: Males call to attract females. The chorus intensity peaks on warm, humid spring nights. Females deposit eggs in small clusters attached to submerged vegetation.

Diet: Insectivorous. Spiders, beetles, flies, ants. Small invertebrates.

Predators: Garter snakes, herons, raccoons, bullfrogs (invasive competitor). The Snowshoe Hare (73) is a prey contrast — both common, both preyed upon, entirely different trophic levels.
