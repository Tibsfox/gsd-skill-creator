## TAXONOMY: *Pseudacris regilla*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Pseudacris** -- from Greek *pseudes* ("false") + *akris* ("locust"), referring to the insect-like quality of the call. The krek-ek call was thought to resemble a locust [1, 2].

- **regilla** -- from Latin "regal" or "little queen." A small creature with a large presence [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Pacific Treefrog (standard). Pacific Chorus Frog (alternative, reflecting the chorus behavior). Formerly classified as *Hyla regilla*. Reclassified to *Pseudacris* based on molecular phylogenetic analysis [1, 2].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** **Amphibia** -- RETURNS. Third class in the engine (after Aves and Mammalia). First amphibian entry.
- **Order:** **Anura** -- frogs. NEW ORDER. First anuran in the engine.
- **Family:** **Hylidae** -- NEW FAMILY (37th). Tree frog family. Breaks Cricetidae streak.
- **Genus:** *Pseudacris* -- NEW GENUS. Chorus frogs. First anuran genus.
- **Species:** *P. regilla* -- the Pacific Treefrog. Tiny frog. Huge voice [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: TINY FROG, HUGE VOICE

### Body Form

The Pacific Treefrog is tiny [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 3-5 cm (1-2 inches). One of the smallest vertebrates in the engine.
- **Weight:** 2-5 g. Roughly 1/200th the mass of the Muskrat at degree 85.
- **Toe pads:** Expanded adhesive toe pads for climbing. Typical of tree frogs (Hylidae).
- **Vocal sac:** Males have a large, dark vocal sac that inflates during calling, amplifying the call to approximately 80 dB at 1 meter.

### Coloration

- **Highly variable:** Green, brown, tan, reddish, or gray. Can change color over hours to days in response to temperature and humidity.
- **Diagnostic mark:** **Dark eye stripe** -- a dark stripe from nostril through eye to shoulder. The key field mark. Present in all color morphs.
- **Belly:** Cream to white.
- **Skin:** Smooth, moist. Typical amphibian skin.

## THE SOUND: AMPLIFIED KREK-KREK WALL OF SOUND -- 87th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Night Chorus

The Pacific Treefrog's acoustic contribution is not an individual call but a **collective breeding chorus** [1, 2, 3]:

**Individual call:** A two-note "krek-ek, krek-ek" -- loud, high-pitched, rapidly repeated. Approximately 1,000-4,000 Hz with peak energy at 2,000-2,500 Hz. Produced by males from marsh edges, often partially submerged. Each male calls approximately 2-3 times per second. Individual calls are loud for body size -- approximately 80 dB at 1 meter.

**The chorus:** When hundreds or thousands call simultaneously, the individual calls blend into a **wall of sound** -- a continuous, pulsing, amplified chorus that fills the marsh soundscape. There are no gaps. The asynchronous calling creates a dense, layered texture. The chorus is audible from **hundreds of meters**. It dominates the marsh on warm, humid spring nights.

**The "Hollywood frog":** The Pacific Treefrog's call is the sound used in films, television, and media worldwide to signify "nighttime outdoors" -- even in scenes set far from the Pacific Northwest. It is arguably the most widely heard frog call on Earth, through its use in sound design.

### Why E=4

1. **Individual calls are already loud for body size** -- one of the loudest anurans relative to size.
2. **The chorus is amplified by numbers** -- hundreds or thousands calling simultaneously.
3. **Audible from hundreds of meters** -- the chorus carries across landscapes.
4. **Dominates the marsh soundscape** on spring nights -- nothing else competes.
5. **E=4 reflects the overwhelming collective volume** -- the loudest SPS entry by collective amplification.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Quiet plop** (degree 85, Muskrat) -- E=3. Quiet, personal-range sound. The treefrog chorus is E=4, landscape-scale sound. From the quietest marsh sound to the loudest, in one degree.
- **Chatter + gnaw** (degree 84, Woodrat) -- E=3. Functional sounds. The treefrog chorus is biological communication at maximum volume.
- **Beaver chewing** (degree 62) -- E=4. Functional construction sounds. The treefrog chorus is E=4 through collective calling, not individual effort.

## ECOLOGY: THE SPRING CHORUS

### Breeding Behavior

The night chorus is the defining behavior [1, 2, 3]:

- **Timing:** Late winter through spring. Peaks on warm, humid nights (above ~8°C / 46°F).
- **Calling position:** Males call from marsh edges, often partially submerged in shallow water among cattails and sedges.
- **Purpose:** Males call to attract females. The loudest, most persistent males tend to attract more mates.
- **Chorus dynamics:** The chorus builds through the evening, peaks in the middle of the night, and subsides toward dawn.
- **Eggs:** Females deposit eggs in small clusters attached to submerged vegetation. Tadpoles develop in standing water.

### Habitat

Remarkably adaptable [1, 2, 3]:

- **Primary:** Freshwater marshes, ponds, wetlands, wet meadows.
- **Also:** Gardens, forests, grasslands -- almost any moist habitat with standing water for breeding.
- **Consecutive marsh:** Shares marsh habitat with Muskrat (85). Also with Wood Duck (58) and Mallard (59).
- **Beaver ponds:** Beaver-created ponds (61) become treefrog breeding habitat. The dam builder creates the chorus stage.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Insectivorous. Spiders, beetles, flies, ants. Small invertebrates.
- **Predators:** Garter snakes, herons, raccoons, bullfrogs (invasive competitor). One of the most preyed-upon amphibians.
