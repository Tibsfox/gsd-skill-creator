Acoustic Profile — Rocky Island Colony

Primary call: Loud "wauuuw" or emphatic "keeow" — louder and more assertive than Glaucous-winged's mew. Multiple birds simultaneously in colony chorus.

Long call: Head-back posture, extended descending series. Frequent during territorial displays. Multiple simultaneous long calls at peak territorial activity.

Acoustic reflectance: Rocky island cliff faces and rock surfaces amplify colony sound vs. open-water sites. E=7 reflects both species' assertive character and habitat acoustics.

Colony aggregate: All calls simultaneously -- continuous territorial activity, alarm calls, chick begging. E=7: louder per bird than Glaucous-winged colony; rocky island amplification.

Pacific Coast Rocky Island -- A Different Habitat

The Western Gull's colony habitat is specifically the exposed Pacific coast: rocky islands, sea stacks, and cliff-top sites battered by Pacific swells. This is a physically demanding environment compared to the sheltered Salish Sea island sites of the Glaucous-winged Gull. The Western Gull is adapted to this exposure: large, assertive, territorially aggressive, with louder calls that carry over the ambient noise of the open Pacific coast.

Colony sites include Destruction Island (Washington outer coast), the rocks and islets of the Pacific coast proper, and in Oregon and California: the Farallon Islands (one of the largest Western Gull colonies), Año Nuevo Island, and many smaller coastal rocks. At these sites, mixed seabird colonies develop: Western Gulls alongside Brandt's Cormorant, Common Murre, Pigeon Guillemot, and others. The multi-species acoustic environment of a Pacific coast rocky island colony is one of the richest soundscapes in the PNW/Pacific coast avifauna.

The Glaucous-winged / Western Pair

Degrees 289 and 291 form the Laridae arc's internal pair: the two great large gulls of the Pacific Northwest documented two degrees apart. The Glaucous-winged (289) is the Salish Sea species — the most common inland waterway gull. The Western (291) is the Pacific coast species — the exposed rocky coast specialist. Both are colonial. Both have been hybridizing throughout Washington State for as long as the two species have been in contact.

The hybrid zone (Glaucous-winged x Western) was introduced at degree 289. Degree 291 completes the pair. In Washington State, pure Glaucous-winged and pure Western are found at their respective breeding sites; the Puget Sound and Salish Sea gulls that don't quite fit either species represent the hybrid continuum between them.

Named by Audubon in 1839 — *occidentalis*: western, the western gull, the gull of the west coast. The most literally directional name in the arc.
