## THIRD LARIDAE -- THE SECOND COLONY SOUNDSCAPE

### Rocky Coast, Pacific Side

Degree 291 returns the Laridae arc to the colonial nesting context after the winter flock alarm of degree 290:

- **289: Glaucous-winged Gull colony, Salish Sea.** The most common PNW gull. Protected inland waters. Rocky islands in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and San Juan archipelago. The ambient colonial chorus.
- **290: Herring Gull flock alarm, winter beach.** A different ecological mode -- the triggered flock alarm of winter beach and harbor flocks.
- **291: Western Gull colony, Pacific coast rocky island.** The return to colony soundscape. The Pacific coast exposed rocky island context -- different from the sheltered Salish Sea sites of the Glaucous-winged. The Western Gull is the species of the open Pacific coast, the exposed headlands, the sea stacks battered by Pacific swells.
- **Why E=7 instead of E=6?** The Western Gull colony is rated E=7 (compared to the Glaucous-winged colony's E=6) for several reasons: the Western Gull's calls are generally louder and more aggressive in character; rocky Pacific coast island sites have acoustic reflectance from cliff faces and rock surfaces that amplifies the colony sound; the Western Gull is a more territorial species within the colony than the Glaucous-winged, producing more long calls and alarm calls per bird per hour.

## LARUS OCCIDENTALIS -- THE SPECIES

### The Pacific Coast Specialist

*Larus occidentalis* (Audubon, 1839) is the defining gull of the Pacific coast from Washington to Baja California:

- **Identification.** A large gull (wingspan 54-58 inches, very similar to Glaucous-winged in size). Adults have a white head and underparts, dark gray back and upperwings (darker than Glaucous-winged, closer to Herring Gull gray), and black wingtips with white mirrors. The key distinction from Glaucous-winged: the Western Gull has dark wingtips (like Herring Gull) not gray wingtips. The yellow orbital ring (vs. pink-red of Glaucous-winged). Pink legs. In the hybrid overlap zone, intermediate birds are common.
- **Scientific name.** *Larus occidentalis*. *Larus* (Latin: gull); *occidentalis* (Latin: western). Named by John James Audubon in 1839. The most explicitly directional name in the Laridae arc -- the Western Gull is simply the western gull, the gull of the western coast.
- **Range.** The Western Gull breeds along the Pacific coast from Washington State south through Oregon, California, and northern Baja California. Unlike the Glaucous-winged Gull, which breeds extensively in Alaska and British Columbia, the Western Gull's breeding range is primarily in the lower 48 states. In Washington State, the Western Gull breeds on the outer coast -- the rocks and islands of the Pacific coast proper, not the protected inland waters of the Salish Sea.
- **Plumage sequence.** Four years to adult plumage, like other large gulls. First-winter Western Gulls are mottled brown. Second and third-winter birds show progressive development of adult gray back. Adults are distinctive in the Pacific coast context.
- **The hybrid zone.** The Western Gull hybridizes extensively with the Glaucous-winged Gull in Washington State and the northern Oregon coast -- the contact zone between the two species' breeding ranges. Hybrids (Glaucous-winged x Western) are common throughout Puget Sound and the Salish Sea, where the Western's range extension overlaps with the Glaucous-winged's stronghold. This hybrid zone is one of the most studied in North American gull taxonomy and was referenced in the Glaucous-winged entry at degree 289.

(Sources: Larus occidentalis identification; John James Audubon 1839; occidentalis name etymology; breeding range Washington-Baja California; four-year plumage sequence; Glaucous-winged x Western hybrid zone; Washington State outer coast breeding)

## THE COLONY VOCALIZATION -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Rocky Island Colony Sound

The Western Gull colony soundscape at a Pacific coast rocky island site:

- **The wauuuw call.** The Western Gull's primary call is often described as a loud, emphatic "wauuuw" or "keeow" -- similar to the Glaucous-winged's mew but generally louder and more assertive in character. At a colony, hundreds of birds produce this call simultaneously, creating the overlapping chorus characteristic of colonial Laridae.
- **The long call.** Head-back posture, extended descending series of loud notes. The Western Gull's long call is performed frequently during territorial displays -- a dominant behavior at the colony. Multiple birds long-calling simultaneously during territorial confrontations produce some of the most intense acoustic events in the colony's behavioral repertoire.
- **Alarm calls.** Similar in character to other large gulls: rapid "kek-kek-kek" series that propagates through the colony when a threat is detected. On open coastal rocky island sites, aerial predators (Bald Eagle, Peregrine Falcon, Common Raven) are the primary alarm triggers.
- **Acoustic reflectance.** Rocky Pacific coast island colony sites have a physical acoustic quality different from the open-water Salish Sea colony sites of the Glaucous-winged Gull: cliff faces, rock surfaces, and the enclosed geometry of sea stacks and rocky outcrops create acoustic reflectance and focusing that amplifies the colony sound relative to open water sites. A colony of equivalent size on a rocky island sounds louder than the same colony on a flat, open sand island.
- **E=7 vs. E=6.** The Western Gull colony's E=7 rating (compared to the Glaucous-winged's E=6) reflects both the species' more aggressive vocal character and the acoustic properties of the rocky island habitat. The Western Gull is louder per bird and the habitat amplifies the aggregate.
- **Chick vocalizations.** Chicks produce begging calls from hatching (late June-July) through fledging (August-September). The Western Gull chick's begging call is high-pitched, persistent, and carries well -- a significant addition to the colony soundscape during the peak chick-rearing period.

(Sources: Western Gull wauuuw primary call; long call frequency and character; alarm call propagation; acoustic reflectance of rocky island habitat; E=7 vs. E=6 calibration; chick vocalization contribution)

## ROCKY ISLAND COLONIAL NESTING

### The Exposed Pacific Coast Context

The Western Gull's nesting habitat is specifically the exposed rocky Pacific coast:

- **Colony sites.** Western Gull colony sites on the Washington coast include Protection Island (Jefferson County -- the same major colonial waterbird site supporting Glaucous-winged Gulls, though at separate locations), Destruction Island, the rocks and islets of the outer coast, and various offshore rocks along the Pacific coast. In Oregon and California, the species breeds on the Farallon Islands (one of the largest Western Gull colonies), Año Nuevo Island, and many smaller coastal rocks.
- **Rocky island habitat.** The Western Gull is specifically adapted to exposed rocky coast: it nests on steep slopes, rock ledges, and flat cliff tops on islands where terrestrial predator access is limited. The rocky island habitat is physically demanding -- constant Pacific swell, fog, wind, and the spray of breaking waves. The species' large size and aggressive temperament are adapted to this demanding environment.
- **Territory and density.** Western Gull territories at rocky island colonies are small and aggressively defended. The density of nesting pairs at productive rocky island sites is high, and territorial disputes (leading to long calls, posturing, and physical confrontation) are frequent. The constant territorial activity contributes to the colony's high acoustic output.
- **Breeding chronology.** Egg laying begins in April-May; incubation approximately 25-29 days; chicks hatch late May-July; fledging occurs August-September. The colony is acoustically active from territorial establishment (April) through fledging (September). Peak acoustic activity coincides with the chick-rearing period.
- **Mixed species colonies.** Western Gulls often breed in mixed colonies with other waterbirds: Brandt's Cormorant, Pelagic Cormorant, Common Murre, Pigeon Guillemot, and others. The acoustic environment of a mixed seabird colony on a Pacific coast rock includes Western Gull calls, cormorant grunts, murre growling, and guillemot whistles simultaneously -- a rich multi-species soundscape.

(Sources: Western Gull colony sites: Protection Island, Destruction Island, Farallon Islands, Año Nuevo; rocky island habitat characteristics; territory density and defense; breeding chronology April-September; mixed species colony acoustic environment)

## THE GLAUCOUS-WINGED / WESTERN RELATIONSHIP

### Partners, Hybrids, and Acoustic Contrast

The relationship between the Glaucous-winged Gull (289) and the Western Gull (291) across two degrees:

- **The hybrid zone documented.** The degree 289 entry (Glaucous-winged) introduced the hybrid zone: Glaucous-winged x Western hybrids are common throughout Puget Sound. Degree 291 (Western Gull) is the other half of this pair. The hybrid zone means that in Washington State, the two species exist in a continuum -- pure Glaucous-winged at northern sites, pure Western at Pacific coast outer sites, and hybrids of every intermediate grade throughout Puget Sound.
- **Habitat differentiation.** Glaucous-winged: Salish Sea, protected inland waters, mixed colony sites. Western: Pacific coast, exposed rocky islands, sea stacks. The habitat differentiation between the two species in Washington State is the mechanism that limits hybridization at pure colony sites -- the species are separated by habitat preference even where their ranges overlap geographically.
- **Acoustic differentiation.** The Western Gull's calls are generally louder and more assertive than the Glaucous-winged's -- a character consistent with the more exposed, higher-energy physical environment of the Pacific coast. Birds at sites exposed to constant ocean swell and wind may have evolved higher vocal output as a communication necessity. The E=7 vs. E=6 rating reflects this differentiation.
- **The arc's internal dialogue.** Degrees 289 and 291 are the Laridae arc's two colony soundscapes. Their pairing across the arc creates an internal dialogue between the two great sympatric large gulls of the Pacific Northwest: the Salish Sea species (289) and the Pacific coast species (291). The arc documents both.

(Sources: Glaucous-winged x Western hybrid zone; habitat differentiation: Salish Sea vs. Pacific coast; acoustic differentiation between species; colony site separation mechanism; arc internal dialogue)
