### The Passerine Streak Breaks: First Piciformes, First Woodpecker

The Northern Flicker at degree 21 breaks a twenty-degree passerine streak (degrees 1-20). For twenty consecutive releases, every species was a songbird — order Passeriformes, syrinx-dependent, voice-produced sound. The flicker introduces order Piciformes — the woodpeckers — and with it, a fundamentally different sound-production mechanism. Woodpeckers drum. They use their bills as percussion instruments, striking resonant substrates to produce broadband mechanical sound. The syrinx is not the primary acoustic instrument. The bill is.

Glenn's orbit broke a different kind of streak — the suborbital streak. Mercury flights MR-1 through Liberty Bell 7 (degrees 16-20) were all suborbital: up and down, brief, contained. Glenn's Friendship 7 broke through to orbital flight — three times around the Earth, sustained operation, the leap from ballistic arc to continuous circuit. The passerine-to-Piciformes transition parallels the suborbital-to-orbital transition: a qualitative change in the mode of operation, not just a quantitative increase. Voice to percussion. Arc to orbit.

Chinook salmon completes the parallel through scale: the king salmon is the largest Pacific salmon, making the longest migration. The Northern Flicker is the largest common woodpecker in the PNW — not the Pileated (which is larger but rarer), but the most frequently encountered large woodpecker. Both are kings of their respective domains. Glenn was the American space program's king moment — the flight that made the program a national institution rather than a technical experiment.

### The Loud Wick-Wick-Wick: Volume as Territory

The Northern Flicker's primary vocalization is a loud, rapid "wick-wick-wick-wick" — a piercing, repetitive call that carries hundreds of meters through open woodland. At E=4, it is the loudest bird documented in the engine. The call's volume is its primary function: territory declaration through sheer acoustic power. Where the Hutton's Vireo (degree 13) used monotonous repetition and the Common Yellowthroat (degree 17) used rhythmic insistence, the flicker uses volume. The territory is claimed by being the loudest voice in the landscape.

Glenn's Friendship 7 was the loudest American space mission in public consciousness. The launch was broadcast live to an estimated 40 million American television viewers. Scott Carpenter's famous "Godspeed, John Glenn" was heard by the nation. The mission's volume — its public visibility, its cultural penetration — was its primary territorial claim. America's orbital capability was declared not through quiet technical achievement but through maximum-volume public broadcast.

Polyrhythmics at E=4 operates on the same principle: seven-to-eight pieces playing instrumental funk at full volume. The groove's territorial claim is physical — you feel it in your body, in the floor vibrating, in the air pressure. The flicker's wick-wick-wick, Glenn's televised launch, and Polyrhythmics' live show all use the same strategy: volume as territory. The loudest signal wins the space.

### The Ground-Foraging Woodpecker: Ants as Obsession

The Northern Flicker is unusual among woodpeckers in being primarily a **ground forager**. Where most Picidae species climb tree trunks and excavate wood for insects, the flicker spends much of its time on the ground, probing lawns, meadows, and bare soil for **ants** — its primary food source. The flicker's tongue (extending 5 cm beyond the bill, coated in sticky saliva, barbed at the tip) is specialized for ant extraction. A single flicker can consume over 5,000 ants in a day.

The ground-foraging woodpecker is a taxonomic paradox: a bird built for vertical tree-climbing (stiffened tail, zygodactyl feet, chisel bill) that spends most of its time horizontal on the ground. The adaptations evolved for one purpose (wood excavation) are deployed for another (ground probing). Glenn's Mercury capsule was built for orbital flight but spent most of its mission on the water after splashdown — a vehicle built for space that ended up bobbing on the ocean. Chinook salmon are built for ocean life but spend their final months in freshwater rivers — an organism evolved for one medium deployed in another. The flicker, Glenn, and the salmon all demonstrate the principle of adapted-purpose deployment: built for one environment, functioning in another.

### The Red-Shafted Form and the Species-Pair Setup

The PNW Northern Flicker is the Red-shafted form (*C. a. cafer*) — salmon-red underwings that flash in flight, red moustachial stripe on males. The Eastern form is Yellow-shafted (*C. a. auratus*) — golden-yellow underwings. The two forms hybridize where their ranges meet in the Great Plains. This is another example of the within-species geographic variation documented at degree 11 (Oregon Junco) — forms that may be separate species, held together by hybridization at the margins.
