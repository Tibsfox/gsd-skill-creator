Northern Flicker

Colaptes auratus cafer · Family Picidae (Woodpeckers)
Loud Wick-wick-wick Call · Open Woodland · Year-round Resident · Energy 4
On a bright morning in an open woodland park, a large brown-barred bird lands on the ground — not a branch, not a trunk, the ground. It hops forward, drives its bill into the earth. From the top of a nearby dead alder, a call erupts: wick-wick-wick-wick-wick. Loud. Emphatic. Each note a hammer strike. The same pitch. The same volume. Thirty times. Then silence. The call does not fade. It stops. On the next upward swoop of undulating flight, the white rump patch flashes and salmon-red underwings catch the morning light. The Northern Flicker. The first Picidae. The second non-passerine. The hammer.

The Passerine Streak Is Broken

Twenty consecutive passerines. Degrees 1–20: every species from a single order (Passeriformes). The only non-passerine was the Great Blue Heron at degree 0. The Northern Flicker breaks the streak. The ratio shifts from 20:1 to 20:2. A third avian order enters the circle.

Taxonomy — The Woodpecker That Walks

The Northern Flicker (Colaptes auratus) belongs to the family Picidae (woodpeckers) within the order Piciformes. The PNW subspecies is the Red-shafted Flicker (C. a. cafer) — named by Gmelin in 1788 under the mistaken belief the specimen came from South Africa. Salmon-red underwings and undertail feathers. Males show a red moustachial stripe.

Dimensions: 12.5–14 inches (32–36 cm), wingspan 19–21 inches, weight 110–160 g. The largest bird in the engine — nearly three times the mass of the next-heaviest passerine. Brown-barred back, black bib, bold black belly spots, conspicuous white rump patch, and the flash of salmon-red feather shafts in undulating flight.

Bioacoustics — The Wick-Wick-Wick Call

A loud, far-carrying series of rapidly repeated “wick” notes at 6–10 notes per second, lasting 5–8 seconds (25–36 notes per burst). Each note is sharp, emphatic, and downward-inflected. Frequency band: 1.5–4.0 kHz, fundamental near 2.0–2.5 kHz. Pitch is constant across the series — no ascending, descending, or modulatory contour. Volume is constant — no fade, no crescendo. Then the call simply stops. The hammer.

The Flicker Pair: Degrees 21 & 22

The Northern Flicker appears at two consecutive degrees — the engine’s first species-pair. Degree 21 is the vocal call (wick-wick-wick, syrinx-produced). Degree 22 will be the drum (bill striking a resonant substrate — the first non-vocal, mechanical sound in the engine). Same species, two fundamentally different sound-production mechanisms.

Ground-Foraging Ecology

The most ground-foraging woodpecker in North America. Spends 75%+ of its foraging time on the ground, primarily eating ants (Formica, Camponotus, Lasius). A single stomach was found to contain 5,000+ ants. The flicker’s tongue — supported by hyoid bones that wrap around the skull — extends 2 inches beyond the bill tip, barbed and coated in sticky saliva. A precision extraction tool cycling at two probes per second.

Anting behavior: The flicker deliberately places ants on its feathers or sits on ant mounds, likely for ectoparasite control via formic acid. The most ant-dependent bird in North America engaging in the most frequent anting behavior.

Cavity-nesting ecosystem service: As a primary cavity excavator, the flicker creates nest holes used by dozens of species — swallows, bluebirds, kestrels, saw-whet owls, flying squirrels. Its excavation builds housing for an entire community.

Cross-References

SPS-000: Great Blue Heron

THE OTHER NON-PASSERINE. Degree 0 established silence (Pelecaniformes). Degree 21 brings percussion (Piciformes). The heron hunts by silence; the flicker announces by volume. Two non-passerines bookending the passerine arc.

SPS-004: Spotted Owl

Territorial sound from different orders. The owl’s four-note motif at E=2 in darkness vs. the flicker’s wick series at E=4 in open woodland. Both territorial, both non-passerine-adjacent.

SPS-011: Oregon Junco

METRONOMIC REPETITION — DIFFERENT FUSION. Junco trills above fusion threshold (notes blur into buzz). Flicker repeats below fusion (each wick heard individually). Same principle, different perceptual result.

SPS-013: Hutton’s Vireo

REPETITION AT DIFFERENT SCALES. Vireo repeats slowly (meditative ostinato). Flicker repeats fast (urgent hammer). One whispers forever. The other shouts rapidly and stops.

SPS-017: Common Yellowthroat

THE HOOK VS. THE HAMMER. The hook has groove (patterned cell, internal structure). The hammer has beat (uniform pulse, no internal structure). Melodic repetition vs. rhythmic repetition.

SPS-020: Orange-crowned Warbler

THE FADE VS. THE HAMMER. Degree 20 dissolved (triple descent, evaporation). Degree 21 maintains full power until cessation. Controlled decay vs. sustained force. Maximum contrast.

AVI: Wings of the PNW

One of the most recognizable PNW woodpeckers. Primary cavity excavator documented as a keystone ecosystem service provider in the PNW wildlife survey.

S36: Seattle 360 Music

Part A parallel: Polyrhythmics (8-piece Jazz/Funk, Fremont, E=4). Both are energy-4 presences commanding acoustic space. The hammer meets the polyrhythm. Scale increase in both engines.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Open woodland ecology: Garry oak savanna, forest-meadow edges, urban parkland. Dead wood (snags) as keystone habitat structure for cavity nesters.
