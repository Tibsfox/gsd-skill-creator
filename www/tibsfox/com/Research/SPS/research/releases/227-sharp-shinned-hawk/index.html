Sharp-shinned Hawk

E = 5 Accipiter striatus Accipitridae
ACCIPITER TRIO CLOSES — SMALLEST ACCIPITER. Degree 227 closes the three-degree accipiter sequence. The Sharp-shinned Hawk is the smallest diurnal raptor in North America — barely larger than a robin in male form — and the most numerically abundant at hawkwatch migration sites. Smallest body, highest-frequency call, most precisely built. The accipiter trio: Goshawk (225) → Cooper's (226) → Sharp-shin (227).

The Sharpie

The Sharp-shinned Hawk (Accipiter striatus) is the smallest of the three PNW accipiters and the one most likely to appear in unexpected places — at backyard feeders in winter, at hawkwatch sites in spectacular migration numbers, through suburban neighborhoods in pursuit of small songbirds. Its kik-kik-kik alarm call is the highest-pitched of the three accipiter alarms in the engine, easily missed in forest acoustic clutter, requiring the listener's attention to locate the caller.

"Sharpie" is birding culture's affectionate diminutive for the species. The informality of the nickname reflects the bird's familiarity — it is the raptor that most beginner birders encounter first, appearing in backyard and suburban contexts that beginners are already observing. Small, fast, easily overlooked, and rewarding close attention. The parallel with Hovercraft (S36 227) is precise.

The Identification Problem

The Cooper's Hawk / Sharp-shinned Hawk identification challenge is birding's most discussed puzzle, documented at degree 226. The primary field mark: Sharp-shin = square or notched tail tip. Cooper's Hawk = rounded tail tip. In flight at distance, in wind, with a bird that has not fanned its tail conveniently — this distinction defeats even experienced observers. The overlap zone (large female Sharp-shin, small male Cooper's) is substantial. The problem is real and persistent.

Migration — The Hawkwatch Phenomenon

Chelan Ridge

PNW Hawkwatch

Premier PNW raptor count site in north-central Washington. Thousands of raptors annually. Sharp-shinned Hawks among the most numerous species.

Cape Flattery

Migration Bottleneck

Northwestern tip of Olympic Peninsula. Raptors moving south along coast funnel to the point. Sharp-shins among the most numerous species counted.

Most Abundant Migrant Accipiter

Ecology

More migratory than Cooper's Hawk or Goshawk. Higher total population. Count sites record far more Sharp-shins than the other two accipiters combined.

Hawk Mountain (PA) Data

Conservation

Continuous counts since 1930s. One of the longest wildlife datasets in North America. Sharp-shin DDT decline and post-1972 recovery documented in the count record.

Size and Sexual Dimorphism

The most extreme sexual dimorphism of the three accipiters. Female Sharp-shins weigh nearly twice as much as males. A male Sharp-shin (82-115 g) is barely larger than a robin; a large female (150-218 g) overlaps with a small male Cooper's Hawk. This size difference creates functional niche partitioning within the mated pair: males hunt primarily warblers and chickadees; females can take slightly larger prey (sparrows, finches). Intra-pair competition for prey is reduced.
