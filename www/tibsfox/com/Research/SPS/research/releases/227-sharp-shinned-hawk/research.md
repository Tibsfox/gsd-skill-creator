# Degree 227 -- Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*)
## Deep Research Mission: High kik-kik-kik, Forest, Accipitridae, E=5 -- SMALLEST ACCIPITER / MOST AERIAL / THE SPARROWHAWK OF THE NEW WORLD

**Release:** 227 / 360
**Degree:** 227 (two hundred twenty-seventh rotation from origin)
**Species:** Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*, Accipitridae) -- alarm call
**Sound:** A high, thin series: kik-kik-kik (also rendered ki-ki-ki or kee-kee-kee). The call is higher in pitch than either the Goshawk (degree 225) or the Cooper's Hawk (degree 226) -- consistent with the smallest body producing the highest frequencies. The call is easily missed in forest environments; it requires attention to locate the caller.
**Energy:** 5
**Ecosystem:** Forest. Coniferous and mixed forest, particularly for breeding. Migrates through a wide range of habitats. More associated with dense forest interiors for nesting than the Cooper's Hawk, but uses varied habitats in migration and winter.
**Season:** Resident and migratory (year-round PNW presence; substantial migration movement in fall and spring)
**Family:** Accipitridae (hawks, eagles, kites, harriers)
**Status:** ACCIPITER TRIO DEGREE 3. The smallest and most numerous accipiter -- the one most often counted at hawkwatch sites. The sharp-shin closes the accipiter trio at degree 227. Pairs with Hovercraft (S36 227) -- the most minimal band in the current sequence, operating at precise small scale without vocals.

## THE SMALLEST ACCIPITER -- *ACCIPITER STRIATUS*

### Crow-Sized but Formidable

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Accipiter*. Species: *striatus* -- from Latin *striatus* (striped, streaked), a reference to the juvenile bird's streaked underparts. The common name "Sharp-shinned" refers to the compressed (sharp-edged, keel-like in cross-section) tarsus (lower leg bone), which distinguishes it from the round-tarsal Cooper's Hawk. This anatomical distinction is a reliable in-hand identification character that field birders cannot use but museum specimens document clearly.
- **Size.** The smallest diurnal raptor in North America (smallest hawk). Females: 150-218 g (5.3-7.7 oz), body length 29-37 cm (11.5-14.5 in), wingspan 52-68 cm (20.5-27 in). Males: 82-115 g (2.9-4.1 oz). A male Sharp-shinned Hawk is barely larger than a robin. The size range overlaps significantly with the Cooper's Hawk -- large female Sharp-shins overlap in size with small male Cooper's Hawks, creating the persistent identification challenge. This is the most frequently misidentified bird pair in North American hawkwatching.
- **Appearance.** Adults: slate-gray above, barred rufous and white below (similar to Cooper's but with more distinct barring in some individuals), with a square-to-notched tail tip (vs. Cooper's Hawk's rounded tail). The square tail is the primary field mark distinguishing Sharp-shin from Cooper's Hawk in flight, though tail wear and viewing angle complicate the distinction. Adults have a red eye. Juveniles: brown above, with streaked brown underparts; the streaks are wider and more "blobby" than the teardrop streaks of the juvenile Cooper's Hawk.
- **The keel-shaped tarsus.** The distinctive "sharp shin" -- the laterally compressed tarsus bone -- is the anatomical character that gave the species its common name. In the Goshawk and Cooper's Hawk, the tarsus is round in cross-section. In the Sharp-shinned Hawk, it is flattened, with a distinct ridge or keel on the anterior surface. The function of this anatomical difference is unclear -- it may relate to the bird's specific prey-seizure mechanics, or it may be an evolutionary vestige without current adaptive function.

## THE CALL -- kik-kik-kik

### The High and Thin Alarm

- **The alarm call.** The Sharp-shinned Hawk's alarm call is a rapid, high-pitched series: kik-kik-kik (also described as ki-ki-ki or a thin, repeated squeal). The call is the highest-frequency of the three accipiter alarms in the engine: higher than the Goshawk (degree 225) and higher than the Cooper's Hawk (degree 226). The size-frequency relationship holds: smallest body, smallest vocal structures, highest fundamental frequency.
- **Frequency.** The call operates in the 3,500-7,000 Hz range -- the upper portion of human hearing, where sounds begin to feel thin and piercing rather than substantial. The call has a quality that birders often describe as "wiry" or "needle-thin" -- appropriate for the smallest of the three accipiters.
- **Audibility challenges.** The high frequency and relatively low volume of the Sharp-shin's call makes it one of the more easily overlooked raptor vocalizations. In forest environments with bird song, insect noise, and wind, the kik-kik-kik call can be lost in the acoustic background unless the listener is specifically listening for it. The Goshawk's alarm (degree 225) is unmissable at close range; the Sharp-shin's alarm requires attention.
- **Vocal contexts.** The Sharp-shinned Hawk calls most frequently near the nest during the breeding season, when disturbance elicits alarm calling. Outside the breeding season, the birds are generally quiet -- particularly during migration, when sharp-shins moving through are often seen but not heard. The migration populations are largely silent.
- **E=5.** The call rates E=5 -- present and real, but not the aggressive E=6 of the Goshawk or the assertive urgency of the Cooper's Hawk. The Sharp-shin is the most aerial and the least terrestrially confrontational of the three accipiters; its alarm reflects this -- it calls but does not physically attack intruders with the ferocity that the Goshawk employs.

## ECOLOGY -- THE FOREST HAWK

### Small, Fast, Precise

- **Breeding habitat.** The Sharp-shinned Hawk breeds in coniferous and mixed forest -- typically in denser, more closed-canopy forest than the Cooper's Hawk prefers. In the Pacific Northwest, breeding habitat includes Douglas fir forests, hemlock-cedar associations, and mixed coniferous forests throughout the Cascades, the Olympics, and the Coast Range. The nest is placed in a dense conifer, typically near the trunk in a whorl of branches, well concealed.
- **The nest.** The nest is a flat stick platform, smaller than the Goshawk's substantial eyrie or the Cooper's Hawk's moderate nest. Clutch size: 4-5 eggs. Incubation: 30-35 days. Fledging: approximately 23-27 days after hatching -- faster development than the larger accipiters, consistent with the pattern of smaller raptors developing faster.
- **Prey.** The Sharp-shinned Hawk specializes in very small birds -- principally in the sparrow-to-warbler size range. Warblers, sparrows, finches, and chickadees are primary prey. The small size of the hawk exactly matches the small size of its prey; the proportions are similar to the Cooper's Hawk-dove relationship, scaled down. The hawks pursue songbirds through dense vegetation using their short wings and long tail for maneuverability.
- **The pursuit technique.** The Sharp-shin is the most aerial of the three accipiters -- its hunting style involves more sustained flight pursuit than the ambush-and-short-chase of the Cooper's Hawk or the ambush-and-burst of the Goshawk. Its small, nimble form allows it to chase small birds through surprisingly dense vegetation. The "sharpness" of the species extends beyond the shin -- the entire body is optimized for narrow-channel, high-speed pursuit.
- **Sexual dimorphism and prey size.** The Sharp-shinned Hawk shows the most extreme sexual dimorphism of the three accipiters. Female Sharp-shins are nearly twice the weight of males. This size difference translates to different prey use: males hunt primarily the smallest birds (warblers, chickadees); females can take somewhat larger prey (sparrows, finches). The sexual dimorphism creates functional niche partitioning within the mated pair, reducing intra-pair competition for prey.

(Sources: Sharp-shin breeding habitat; Douglas fir; hemlock-cedar; nest placement; clutch size; development rate; prey species; pursuit technique; sexual dimorphism; intra-pair niche partitioning)

## MIGRATION -- THE MOST ABUNDANT MIGRANT RAPTOR

### The Hawkwatch Phenomenon

The Sharp-shinned Hawk is one of the most spectacular species at North American hawkwatch sites:

- **The migration.** Sharp-shinned Hawks are more strongly migratory than the other two PNW accipiters. Breeding populations from across Canada and the northern US move southward in fall, with substantial movements along geographic ridgelines, coastlines, and other migration corridors. In spring, they return northward. Washington State has significant through-migration of sharp-shins in addition to its resident breeding population.
- **Concentration sites.** Migration-count sites in the Pacific Northwest record impressive Sharp-shin numbers. Chelan Ridge in north-central Washington is one of the premier hawkwatch sites in the Pacific Northwest, recording thousands of raptors annually. The Cascades serve as a geographic funnel directing migration along north-south ridgelines.
- **Cape Flattery and the coast.** The northwestern tip of the Olympic Peninsula (Cape Flattery/Neah Bay area) is a significant migration bottleneck, where raptors moving south along the coast funnel to the point before crossing the Strait of Juan de Fuca or turning inland. Sharp-shinned Hawks are among the most numerous species counted here.
- **Comparative abundance.** Among the three accipiters, the Sharp-shinned Hawk is by far the most numerically abundant at migration count sites. Cooper's Hawks and Northern Goshawks also migrate but in smaller numbers. The Sharp-shin's numerical abundance in migration reflects its higher total population and its more completely migratory behavior in northern populations.
- **The hawkwatch community.** Systematic raptor counting at hawkwatch sites is one of the more citizen-science-intensive forms of bird monitoring. Trained volunteers count migrating raptors at established sites, building long-term datasets that track population trends. The Sharp-shinned Hawk is a cornerstone species for these programs.

(Sources: Sharp-shin migration; Chelan Ridge hawkwatch; Cape Flattery/Neah Bay; raptor migration corridors; Cascades as funnel; comparative abundance vs. Cooper's and Goshawk; citizen science counting)

## THE SHARPIE IN URBAN AND SUBURBAN SEATTLE

### The Feeder-Hunter's Feeder-Hunter

- **Winter presence.** Like the Cooper's Hawk, the Sharp-shinned Hawk appears in Seattle's suburban neighborhoods in winter, hunting at bird feeders. Unlike the Cooper's Hawk (which has established year-round suburban breeding), the Sharp-shin is primarily a winter visitor to suburban areas -- breeding populations are in the forests, not the suburbs.
- **The feeder dynamic.** The Sharp-shin's presence at suburban feeders is a direct result of the feeder concentrations of small songbirds. A feeder attracting dark-eyed juncos, house finches, and black-capped chickadees is an attractive hunting site for a sharp-shin. The hawk's small size allows it to pursue prey through the vegetation immediately adjacent to the feeder -- often low shrubs and dense branches that a Cooper's Hawk could not easily navigate.
- **Identification at the feeder.** Many Seattle residents have difficulty distinguishing a Sharp-shinned Hawk from a Cooper's Hawk at the feeder. The primary clues are size (truly crow-sized or smaller = sharp-shin; blue-jay-sized or larger = Cooper's) and tail shape (squarish vs. rounded). In practice, distant views and brief encounters make definitive identification difficult even for experienced birders.
- **The "Sharpie" nickname.** In North American birding culture, the Sharp-shinned Hawk is affectionately known as the "Sharpie" -- a casual diminutive that reflects both the bird's small size and its status as a familiar, frequently encountered species. The Sharpie is the raptor that most beginner birders encounter first, because it appears in backyard settings that beginners are already observing.

(Sources: Sharp-shin winter suburban presence; feeder hunting; small prey in dense vegetation; Cooper's vs Sharp-shin ID at feeder; "Sharpie" nickname; beginner birder encounter)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### Stable but Monitored

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern. The Sharp-shinned Hawk is widely distributed across North America, from Alaska and Canada through the US into Central and South America. The global population is substantial and considered stable.
- **DDT recovery.** Like the other Accipitridae in the engine, the Sharp-shinned Hawk experienced DDT-related declines in the mid-20th century. The bird-eating specialization meant that DDT accumulated through the food chain to concentrations that impaired reproduction. Post-1972 recovery has been documented at hawkwatch sites, where long-term count data show population increases from the DDT nadir.
- **Hawkwatch trend data.** One of the values of long-term systematic raptor counting at hawkwatch sites is the ability to track population trends over decades. Hawk Mountain Sanctuary in Pennsylvania has maintained systematic counts since the 1930s -- providing one of the longest continuous wildlife population datasets in North America. Sharp-shinned Hawk counts at Hawk Mountain and other sites show the DDT decline and subsequent recovery clearly.
- **Trichomonosis.** Like the Cooper's Hawk (degree 226), the Sharp-shinned Hawk faces the contemporary threat of trichomonosis transmitted through infected prey at backyard feeders. The small size of the Sharp-shin makes it particularly vulnerable -- smaller body mass means less reserve to sustain the disease's effects.
- **Climate considerations.** Climate change is shifting the breeding ranges of many songbird species northward, potentially affecting the Sharp-shin's prey base. Long-term implications for the species depend partly on whether the hawk's breeding range shifts at a similar pace to its prey species.

(Sources: IUCN Least Concern; DDT recovery hawkwatch data; Hawk Mountain Sanctuary long-term counts; trichomonosis vulnerability; climate range shift concerns)

## SHARP-SHINNED HAWK IN THE ENGINE

### The Accipiter Arc Closes

- **The trio completes.** Degree 227 closes the ACCIPITER TRIO. Three species, three degrees, largest to smallest: Northern Goshawk (225) → Cooper's Hawk (226) → Sharp-shinned Hawk (227). The structural descent from apex predator to small bird-hawk mirrors the thematic descent in the S36 pairings: from the grief-monument supergroup (225) to the space rock side project (226) to the wordless instrumental duo (227). Three scales, three registers.
- **Hovercraft pairing.** Hovercraft (S36 227) is defined by formal precision and minimalism -- no vocals, two musicians, sound as primary material. The Sharp-shinned Hawk is the most minimal of the three accipiters: smallest body, thinnest voice, most precisely calibrated hunting technique. Both are defined by precision within constraint. Both are easy to overlook and reward close attention.
- **The size-frequency-energy pattern.** Across the accipiter trio, body size correlates with call frequency (inverse) and with engine energy rating: Goshawk (largest, lowest frequency in trio, E=6), Cooper's Hawk (medium, medium frequency, E=5), Sharp-shin (smallest, highest frequency, E=5). The pattern is not perfectly linear in energy rating -- both Cooper's and Sharp-shin rate E=5 -- but the trend of decreasing presence with decreasing size is coherent.
- **The transition to 228.** After the Sharp-shinned Hawk closes the accipiter trio at degree 227, the engine shifts to the Red-tailed Hawk (degree 228) -- *Buteo jamaicensis*, a different genus entirely. The buteos are the soaring, broad-winged hawks of open country -- the opposite ecological niche from the accipiters' forest interior. The transition from accipiter (forest) to buteo (open country) at the 227-228 boundary is one of the raptor arc's most ecologically significant shifts.
- **Cross-reference to degree 221 and the Hollywood call.** The Red-tailed Hawk (228) is famously the source of the "eagle call" used in film and television -- the call that is attributed to the Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222) but is actually the Red-tail's scream. The Sharp-shin at 227 has no similar cultural appropriation -- it is simply what it is, unnamed in cultural mythology, present in actual forests. The contrast between the cinematically omnipresent Red-tail and the overlooked Sharp-shin is one of the raptor arc's quiet ironies.

(Sources: Accipiter trio completion 225-227; taxonomic descent; Hovercraft precision-minimalism pairing; size-frequency-energy pattern; Buteo transition 228; Hollywood call cross-reference 221)
