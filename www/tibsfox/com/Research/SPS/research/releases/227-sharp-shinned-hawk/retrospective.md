## What Worked

1. **The size-frequency-energy pattern across the accipiter trio is a clean, documentable trend.** Goshawk: largest, 1500-4000 Hz, E=6. Cooper's: medium, 2000-5000 Hz, E=5. Sharp-shin: smallest, 3500-7000 Hz, E=5. The pattern is not perfectly linear in energy (both Cooper's and Sharp-shin rate E=5), but the size-to-frequency inverse relationship holds exactly. The engine documents this as a natural structural fact.
2. **Hawk Mountain Sanctuary's long-term data provides the DDT timeline with unusual specificity.** Continuous counts since the 1930s -- pre-DDT, during DDT, post-ban recovery -- give the Sharp-shin's population history a data quality that most species lack. This is one of wildlife biology's most valuable long-term datasets.
3. **The "Sharpie" nickname is worth documenting.** In birding culture, informal nicknames signal familiarity and affection. The Sharpie is the raptor that most beginner birders encounter first in suburban contexts. The nickname reflects the bird's accessibility.
4. **The accipiter trio closes at 227.** The structural descent from Goshawk (225, largest) to Cooper's (226, medium) to Sharp-shin (227, smallest) is complete. The three-degree sequence is architecturally the engine's most precise.

## Discoveries

1. **Sexual dimorphism in the Sharp-shin is the most extreme of the three accipiters.** Female nearly double the male's weight. This creates intra-pair prey niche partitioning (males take warblers and chickadees; females take slightly larger sparrows) that reduces competition within the mated pair. This is an evolved solution to a resource-sharing problem -- the dimorphism serves an ecological function.
2. **The "sharp shin" (keel-shaped tarsus) is the most specifically anatomical common name of any bird documented in the engine so far.** The name references a specific bone shape visible in in-hand specimens but not in field views. The anatomical specificity of the name documents a pre-binoculars era of bird study -- when bird names were assigned by naturalists examining specimens in hand.
3. **Migration-period silence is ecologically significant.** Sharp-shins moving south in fall are largely silent -- they call at the nest but not in migration. This behavioral silence during the period when most birders encounter the species explains why the kik-kik-kik call is less familiar to non-breeding-season observers than the alarm calls of more vocal raptors.
