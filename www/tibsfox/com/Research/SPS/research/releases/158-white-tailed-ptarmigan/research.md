## E=3 -- THE ALPINE WHISPER

### Why E=3

The White-tailed Ptarmigan cackling + soft clucks registers at E=3:

- **Quiet vocalizations:** The White-tailed Ptarmigan's calls are quiet -- soft clucking contact calls, low-amplitude cackling for alarm and territory. These are not loud or dramatic sounds. They are the quiet communications of a bird that lives in a landscape so exposed that loud calls would carry to predators (golden eagles, gyrfalcons) with no cover nearby.
- **Smallest grouse:** At 300-400 grams, the White-tailed Ptarmigan is the smallest grouse in North America. Smaller body = smaller syrinx = lower maximum amplitude. The physical capacity for loud vocalization is limited.
- **Alpine context:** The alpine zone above treeline is simultaneously vast (open, exposed, wind-swept) and intimate (the ptarmigan stays close to the ground, among rocks and low vegetation). The vocalizations match the intimate scale: bird-to-bird communication in a small group, not long-distance territorial announcements.
- **Wind competition:** The alpine zone is windy. Low-amplitude calls are often masked by wind. The ptarmigan's vocalizations are adapted for calm periods and close-range communication rather than for cutting through ambient noise.
- **Energy floor for Phasianidae:** E=3 represents the Phasianidae energy floor -- the minimum energy for the family's final entry. The family arc from E=6 to E=3 spans the full lower range of the energy scale.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Braun et al. 1993; engine internal analysis)

## THE ALPINE GHOST -- YEAR-ROUND ABOVE TREELINE

### The Only Year-Round Alpine Bird

The White-tailed Ptarmigan's most remarkable characteristic is its permanent residence above treeline:

- **Never descends:** While other alpine birds (rosy-finches, pipits, horned larks) descend to lower elevations in winter, and while other grouse (Dusky, Sooty) perform reverse migration upslope in winter but still remain in the forest, the White-tailed Ptarmigan stays **above treeline year-round**. It endures the full severity of alpine winter: deep snow, extreme cold (-30 to -40C), fierce winds, short days, and near-total absence of food that isn't buried under snow.
- **Snow roosting:** In winter, ptarmigan roost in snow burrows -- digging into the snowpack for insulation. The snow acts as a thermal blanket: interior temperatures in a snow burrow may be -5C when the air temperature is -30C. The bird essentially lives inside the snowpack.
- **Winter diet:** Primarily willow buds and twigs. The ptarmigan seeks out wind-blown ridges and exposed willow patches where snow is thin or absent. The diet is extremely low-quality -- tough, woody, low-calorie. The ptarmigan's digestive system is adapted with an enlarged cecum and powerful gizzard.
- **Energy conservation:** In winter, the ptarmigan minimizes movement. It may sit motionless in its snow burrow for hours, conserving energy. When it does move, it walks rather than flies. Every calorie counts.

### Seasonal Plumage Transformation

The White-tailed Ptarmigan undergoes the most dramatic seasonal plumage change of any PNW bird:

- **Summer:** Mottled brown and gray. Cryptic against the alpine rocks and tundra vegetation. The plumage matches the lichen-covered boulders and dried grasses of the alpine summer.
- **Autumn:** Transitional. Patches of white appearing among the brown. The plumage tracks the arriving snow.
- **Winter:** **Pure white.** The entire bird -- body, wings, head -- turns white. Only the bill and eyes remain dark. Against the snowfield, a motionless White-tailed Ptarmigan is virtually invisible. The white tail (diagnostic -- other ptarmigan species have black tails) provides complete camouflage from above (golden eagles) and below.
- **Spring:** Transitional again. Brown patches appear as snow melts. The plumage tracks the receding snow.

The plumage transformation is driven by photoperiod (day length) and takes approximately 6-8 weeks for each transition. The ptarmigan is in complete camouflage synchrony with its environment -- an evolutionary masterpiece.

(Sources: Braun et al. 1993; Martin et al. 2015; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; engine internal analysis)

## ALPINE ECOLOGY AND ADAPTATION

### The Extreme Habitat

The White-tailed Ptarmigan's habitat is the most extreme of any Phasianidae entry:

- **Elevation:** In Washington, typically above 6,000-7,000 feet (1,800-2,100 meters). Alpine meadows, talus fields, snow basins, krummholz edges. The habitat has no trees -- only low shrubs (willow, heather), grasses, sedges, and alpine flowers in summer, and snow and rock in winter.
- **Temperature range:** Summer highs of 15-25C; winter lows of -30 to -40C. Extreme diurnal variation in summer (warm days, near-freezing nights). The annual temperature range experienced by a White-tailed Ptarmigan exceeds 60C.
- **Wind:** The alpine zone is the windiest habitat in the mountains. Average wind speeds of 30-50 km/h are common. Wind chill can drop effective temperatures far below air temperature.
- **UV exposure:** Above treeline, UV exposure is intense. The ptarmigan's feathered feet and dense plumage provide some protection.
- **Food scarcity:** Winter food is extremely scarce. Willow buds and twigs are the primary winter food source, available only where wind has cleared the snow. The ptarmigan may travel significant distances (for a bird that prefers to walk) to access exposed food patches.

### Washington Distribution

In Washington, the White-tailed Ptarmigan is found in the alpine zones of:

- **North Cascades:** The primary population center. Alpine areas of the North Cascades National Park complex and surrounding wilderness areas.
- **Central Cascades:** Mount Rainier (Paradise area, Sunrise), Alpine Lakes Wilderness.
- **South Cascades:** Mount Adams, Goat Rocks Wilderness.
- **Olympic Mountains:** A small, introduced population exists in the Olympic Mountains.

The species is uncommon and local -- restricted to alpine habitat that represents a tiny fraction of the state's total area. Climate change is a significant threat: as treeline advances upslope with warming temperatures, the alpine zone shrinks, and the ptarmigan's habitat contracts.

(Sources: Braun et al. 1993; Martin et al. 2015; WDFW; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Alpine -- Cackling + Soft Clucks

The White-tailed Ptarmigan's acoustic profile is the most understated of any Phasianidae entry:

**The Cackling:**
- **Trigger:** Alarm, territorial encounters, breeding season interactions.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal, syringeal.
- **Character:** Low, rattling cackle. A series of quick "kuk-kuk-kuk-kuk" notes. Quiet by grouse standards. The cackle has a scratchy, dry quality.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 1000-4000 Hz. Higher-pitched than the booming grouse species.
- **Duration:** Individual cackle: 1-3 seconds. Variable.
- **Amplitude:** E=3. Low. Audible at approximately 20-50 meters in calm conditions. Easily masked by wind.

**The Soft Clucks:**
- **Trigger:** Contact between individuals in a flock. Hen-chick communication. Feeding group cohesion.
- **Character:** Very soft "cluck-cluck" notes. Intimate. The softest sounds in the Phasianidae arc.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 500-2000 Hz.
- **Amplitude:** Very low. Audible at 5-15 meters. Intimate range.

**Other Sounds (secondary):**
- **Screaming:** Alarm call when flushed. Higher-pitched, more intense than cackling. Relatively rare.
- **Wing flutter:** Brief wing sounds during short flights. Quiet.
- **Chick peeping:** Contact calls between hen and chicks.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Braun et al. 1993; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
