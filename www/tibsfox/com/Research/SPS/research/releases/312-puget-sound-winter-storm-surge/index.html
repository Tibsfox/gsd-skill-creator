Puget Sound — The Geographic Context

Puget Sound is the southernmost extension of the Salish Sea, a semi-enclosed marine water body in the Pacific Northwest. It was carved by Pleistocene glaciers that retreated approximately 13,000 years ago, leaving a deep fjord system with complex bathymetry (water depth profiles). The deepest point is approximately 280 meters. The Sound is connected to the Pacific Ocean through the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the San Juan Islands archipelago.

The Sound's semi-enclosed geometry has several acoustic consequences. Pacific Ocean swell energy enters through the Strait of Juan de Fuca and is refracted and concentrated by the Sound's geometry. Storms that drive from the southwest — the dominant winter storm track in the Pacific Northwest — have unobstructed fetch across the Sound's north-south axis in several areas, allowing waves to build to maximum height for a semi-enclosed water body. The outer shores of Whidbey Island, the Kitsap Peninsula, and the Olympic Peninsula coast face these fetch-limited maximum waves directly.

The Winter Storm System — Meteorological Context

Pacific Northwest winter storms are driven by extratropical cyclones forming in the North Pacific and tracking eastward along the polar jet stream. The most intense winter storms are associated with atmospheric rivers — narrow bands of concentrated moisture transport from the subtropical Pacific — that produce sustained high winds and heavy precipitation when they make landfall.

The conditions that produce the maximum storm surge acoustic event:

  • Central pressure: A deep low-pressure system (central pressure below 980 mb) produces the strongest wind gradients. The Puget Sound lowland's location east of the Olympic Mountains creates a rain shadow that can deflect or modify some storms, but south and southeast-tracking systems can reach the Sound directly.
  • Wind speed: Sustained winds of 30-50 knots with gusts to 60+ knots during severe winter storms. The Strait of Juan de Fuca acts as a wind channel in some configurations, accelerating winds beyond their open-water values through the Venturi effect.
  • Wave height: In the outer Salish Sea and along the Olympic coast, waves can reach 3-8 m during severe winter storms. Within Puget Sound proper, the fetch limitation means maximum waves are typically 1-3 m; at outer shore locations with longer fetch, larger waves are possible.
  • Tidal interaction: Storm surge (sea level rise due to low atmospheric pressure and wind-driven water piling) combined with high tidal phase can produce waves reaching well above the normal shoreline, creating the maximum shoreline impact conditions that drive the E=9 calibration.

Acoustic Profile — Storm Surge at E=9

Infrasound component (1-100 Hz): Wave impact against rocky shorelines and headlands produces infrasonic energy (below the 20 Hz conventional auditory threshold) that is felt as vibration in the body before it is consciously heard. Standing at a winter storm shoreline, this component is experienced as a physical pressure wave concurrent with each major wave impact. The infrasonic component carries the E=9 calibration's physical intensity factor.

Impact noise (100 Hz - 2 kHz): The primary audible acoustic event. Wave impact against shore produces a broadband impact noise that peaks in the low-to-mid frequency range. Each impact is a distinct acoustic event with attack (the initial crash), decay (the water rushing back down the shore), and a brief pause before the next wave. At 3-8 m wave height, the impact noise is in the 100-110 dB range at close range.

Spray noise (2 kHz - 20 kHz): Wave spray creates a high-frequency white noise layer: water droplets in the air produce a hissing frequency band that blankets the higher audible frequencies. In high winds, spray noise can reach 80-90 dB at shore.

Wind drone (50-500 Hz): Wind through shoreline vegetation — Sitka spruce, shore pine, red alder at forest-edge positions — produces the characteristic wind drone: a low-to-mid frequency continuous noise that overlaps with the wave impact frequencies and adds a sustained layer between individual wave events.

Shoreline Ecology During Storm Surge

The storm surge acoustic event is not biologically empty. The shoreline communities that experience winter storm surge include several acoustically significant species:

Glaucous-winged Gulls and other large larid species typically call actively during and after storm surge events — the wave action dislodges intertidal invertebrates and drives fish to the surface, triggering foraging responses. The gull calls during storm surge add a biological layer to the meteorological acoustic event.

Harbor seals haul out on protected shorelines and show behavioral responses to storm surge that include increased vocalizations during the surge's peak acoustic intensity. The seal calls — grunts and contact calls — are audible during brief storm pauses.

Sitka spruce and shore pine at the shoreline edge produce the characteristic wind sounds — needles in high wind producing a sibilant high-frequency component, trunks and branches bending and returning producing low-frequency groans — that add a living-forest acoustic layer to the storm surge complex.
