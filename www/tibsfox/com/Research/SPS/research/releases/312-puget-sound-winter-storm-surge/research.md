## PUGET SOUND AND THE SALISH SEA -- THE PHYSICAL CONTEXT

### 13,000-Year-Old Glacial Geometry

Puget Sound is the southernmost major inlet of the Salish Sea, carved by Pleistocene glaciers that advanced from the north approximately 17,000 years ago and retreated approximately 13,000 years ago. The glaciation left:

- **Deep basin topography.** Maximum depth approximately 280 meters in Hood Canal. The main Puget Sound basin averages 60-70 meters. This depth, combined with the Sound's semi-enclosed geometry, creates the specific acoustic propagation environment that characterizes the storm surge.
- **Sill structures.** Underwater sills (shallow ridges) at the Sound's entrance (at Admiralty Inlet) and at other constriction points modulate the exchange of Pacific Ocean water with Puget Sound water. During severe storm events, wave energy is partially channeled through and around these sills, creating complex interference patterns at the coastlines of the inner Sound.
- **The outer coast.** The Olympic Peninsula's outer coast &mdash; the Pacific-facing shore rather than the Puget Sound-facing shore &mdash; experiences the full fetch of the Pacific Ocean and the maximum storm surge heights. The Strait of Juan de Fuca connects the outer coast to the Salish Sea, allowing storm wave energy to propagate inward.

(Sources: Puget Sound glacial formation; Pleistocene glaciation; 280 meter Hood Canal depth; main basin 60-70 meters average; Admiralty Inlet sill; Olympic Peninsula outer coast; Strait of Juan de Fuca connection; Pacific fetch)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST WINTER STORMS -- THE METEOROLOGICAL CONTEXT

### Extratropical Cyclones, Atmospheric Rivers, and the Polar Jet

The winter storms that produce the storm surge acoustic event are products of the North Pacific's meteorological dynamics:

- **Extratropical cyclones.** The primary mechanism: low-pressure systems forming in the central North Pacific, typically in the 40-55°N latitude band, and tracking eastward along the polar jet stream. These systems deepen (intensify) as they track east, sometimes undergoing rapid intensification ("bomb cyclogenesis") if the atmospheric conditions are favorable. A deepened system arriving at the Pacific Northwest coast produces the wind field that generates storm surge.
- **Atmospheric rivers.** Narrow bands (200-400 km wide, 2,000-4,000 km long) of concentrated moisture transport from the subtropical Pacific that arrive as "pineapple express" events in the Pacific Northwest. Atmospheric river events can produce both extreme precipitation and extreme wind, with the wind component generating significant wave heights offshore and surge conditions coastally.
- **Coastal flooding indices.** NOAA uses the Coastal Flooding Index (CFI) to rate storm surge events. The most severe Pacific Northwest winter storms can produce coastal flooding that combines storm surge (sea level rise above mean tide due to low pressure and wind piling) with large wave heights and spring tidal phase alignment. These combined events &mdash; storm + large waves + high tide &mdash; produce the physical conditions that drive the E=9 calibration.

(Sources: Extratropical cyclone formation North Pacific; polar jet stream; bomb cyclogenesis rapid intensification; atmospheric rivers pineapple express; atmospheric river width and length; NOAA Coastal Flooding Index; storm surge mechanism: pressure + wind piling; combined storm + wave + tide events)

## INFRASOUND -- THE PHYSICAL FOUNDATION OF E=9

### What You Feel Before You Hear

Infrasound (sound below 20 Hz, below the conventional auditory threshold) is produced by large-scale wave impact against rocky coastlines and headlands:

- **The generation mechanism.** When a wave 3-8 meters high strikes a rocky headland or cliff face, the deceleration of the water mass over a very short time interval produces a pressure wave that contains significant energy in the infrasonic range (1-20 Hz). This infrasonic component travels through both the ocean water and the adjacent rock and ground as a seismic wave.
- **The physical experience.** Standing on a headland during a severe winter storm, the infrasonic component of wave impact is experienced as a felt vibration: the ground vibrates underfoot, the chest cavity resonates, objects in the vicinity rattle. This physical experience occurs before the audible crack and roar of the wave impact itself, because the infrasonic wave (traveling partly as ground vibration) can reach the observer slightly faster than the audible wave (traveling through air).

Audible wave impact noise alone (100 Hz-2 kHz) would calibrate at E=7-8. The addition of the infrasonic physical impact &mdash; the felt vibration in the body before conscious hearing begins &mdash; is the factor that pushes the calibration to E=9. The storm surge is felt before it is heard. This quality, found in few other acoustic events in the engine, is specific to large-scale wave impact at the shore.

## THE FULL ACOUSTIC COMPLEX -- FOUR LAYERS

### From Infrasound to Spray

The Puget Sound winter storm surge produces four distinct acoustic layers:

1. **Infrasound (1-100 Hz):** Physical impact vibration. Felt in body. Ground conduction. Occurs immediately at wave impact. Primary E=9 factor.

2. **Impact noise (100 Hz-2 kHz):** The primary audible acoustic event. The crack and roar of the wave striking shore. Peaks at 100-110 dB at close range. The most consciously perceived component. Duration: 0.5-3 seconds per wave impact, depending on wave height and shore geometry.

3. **Spray white noise (2 kHz-20 kHz):** Water droplets in the air from wave spray create a high-frequency hissing that persists between wave impacts. The spray layer is sustained by wind keeping the droplets aloft. In storm conditions with 30-50 knot winds, the spray noise layer is continuous rather than pulsed.

4. **Wind drone (50-500 Hz):** Wind through shoreline vegetation (Sitka spruce, shore pine, red alder) produces the characteristic drone: needles produce a high-frequency sibilance, trunks and branches produce low-frequency groans. At storm wind speeds, this drone is at its maximum intensity. It provides the sustained bass layer that underlies the wave impact pulses.

The combination: infrasound (felt before heard) + impact noise (the primary event) + spray (the sustained between-wave layer) + wind drone (the bass foundation) = the full-spectrum acoustic complex that calibrates at E=9. No single layer alone would reach E=9; the combination does.

(Sources: Four-layer acoustic profile; infrasound 1-100 Hz; impact noise 100 Hz-2 kHz 100-110 dB; spray white noise 2-20 kHz; wind drone 50-500 Hz; combination as E=9 calibration factor)
