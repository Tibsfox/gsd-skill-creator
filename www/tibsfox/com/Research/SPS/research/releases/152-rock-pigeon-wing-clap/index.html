Rock Pigeon — Wing Clap

Columba livia (Columbidae) — E=4 — Percussive Wing Clap on Takeoff
FOURTH AND FINAL COLUMBIDAE. The quartet closes with percussion. Third mechanical sound in the engine. Wings slapped above the body — not voice, not feather, but body. Feral. Urban cliff adaptation. 260 million global. Domesticated 5,000–10,000 years ago. The displacement story complete.

The Wing Clap — Percussion, Not Song

Wings physically struck together above the body at the top of the upstroke. Leading edges make contact. Air compression creates a pressure wave. Sharp percussive “crack” or “clap.” Broadband: 200–5000 Hz. Not tonal. Not a vocalization. The body is louder than the voice. Display flight: 3–8 claps during steep climb, then stiff V-wing glide. Alarm: explosive flock burst.

Cliff to City — 10,000 Years of Adaptation

Wild ancestor: sea cliffs and mountain faces in the Mediterranean, Middle East, Central Asia. Narrow ledges with overhanging protection. Domesticated ~5,000–10,000 years ago. Transported to every continent. Escaped. Feral populations recolonized buildings as cliff substitutes: window ledges = cliff ledges, bridge undersides = cliff overhangs. 260 million feral birds worldwide.

The Mechanical Sound Trilogy

Three mechanical sounds in the engine:
124: Common Goldeneye wing whistle (modified primaries, E=5).
150: Mourning Dove wing whistle (9th primary feather, E=3).
152: Rock Pigeon wing clap (wings slapped together, E=4).
Progression: feather-generated to body-generated. Whistle to percussion. Aeroelastic vibration to physical collision.
