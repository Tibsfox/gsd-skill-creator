## THE GRIZZLY BEAR -- THE PNW'S APEX LAND PREDATOR

### Size, Power, Authority

The grizzly bear is the largest and most powerful land animal in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Size.** Adult males (boars): 180-360 kg (400-800 lbs). Females (sows): 130-200 kg. Standing height: up to 2.4 m (8 feet). The grizzly is several times larger than any other land predator in the region.
- **The subspecies.** The North American grizzly bear is *Ursus arctos horribilis* -- literally "horrible bear." The subspecific name, applied by naturalist George Ord in 1815, reflects European settlers' terror of the animal. The grizzly is a subspecies of the brown bear (*Ursus arctos*), which ranges across North America, Europe, and Asia.
- **North Cascades recovery.** Grizzly bears in Washington are primarily in the North Cascades Ecosystem, where a small, critically endangered population persists. Estimates suggest fewer than 10 grizzlies remain in the North Cascades -- one of the most endangered populations in the lower 48. Federal recovery plans aim to restore a viable population, but the process is slow and controversial.
- **Ecological role.** The grizzly is a keystone species and ecosystem engineer. Its digging creates soil disturbance that promotes plant diversity. Salmon carcasses carried into the forest fertilize trees. Bear trails become wildlife corridors. The grizzly's presence reshapes the landscape.
- **Black bear vs. grizzly.** Washington's ~25,000-30,000 Black Bears (degrees 200-202) are common, adaptable, and generally avoidant of humans. The grizzly is rare, habitat-restricted, and commands absolute respect. Both are Ursidae, but the grizzly operates at a different scale.

(Sources: Grizzly bear biology; North Cascades recovery; Ursus arctos horribilis; keystone species; black bear comparison)

## THE ROAR -- ACOUSTIC DOMINANCE

### The Most Powerful Terrestrial Vocalization in the PNW

The grizzly roar is physics made audible:

- **The roar.** A deep, sustained, resonant vocalization -- "RRRROOOAARRR" -- lasting 2-8 seconds. Fundamental frequency: 50-400 Hz, with significant energy below 200 Hz. The low frequencies generate physical vibration -- trees shake, the ground trembles, the observer's chest resonates.
- **Sub-bass component.** A significant portion of the roar's energy is below human hearing threshold (infrasound, below 20 Hz). This infrasonic component may serve to disorient smaller animals and convey the bear's size over longer distances. You feel the roar before you hear it.
- **Carry distance.** A grizzly roar carries 1-3 km through forested terrain. In open mountain valleys, it can travel further. The low frequencies propagate efficiently through terrain -- around trees, over ridges, through valleys.
- **Context.** The roar is used in: dominance displays (between males during breeding season), territorial defense, confrontations with other large predators (wolves, other bears), and high-stress encounters with humans. The roar is not the grizzly's first response -- it is the escalation when other signals (posturing, huffing, jaw-popping) have failed.
- **Acoustic physiology.** The grizzly's massive chest cavity (120+ cm circumference in large males), powerful diaphragm, and elongated vocal tract create a resonant chamber that amplifies low-frequency sound. The roar is an honest signal of the bear's size -- a larger bear produces a deeper, louder roar.
