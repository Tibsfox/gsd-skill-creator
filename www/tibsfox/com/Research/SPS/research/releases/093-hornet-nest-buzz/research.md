## THE SOUND: AGGRESSIVE PAPERY HUM -- 94th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Sound

The hornet nest buzz is the **collective defensive buzzing** of a hornet or wasp colony [1, 2]:

**Individual sound:** Each hornet produces a buzzing flight tone through rapid wing vibration, typically 100-200 Hz. The flight buzz of a single wasp is higher-pitched and more aggressive than a bumblebee's drone.

**The collective defensive display:** When a colony is disturbed, **hundreds of hornets** emerge and vibrate their wings simultaneously, producing a **papery, aggressive hum** that rises in volume and intensity. The sound is not musical -- it is threatening. The frequency content is broad-spectrum, with the fundamental wing-beat frequency overlaid with harmonics and the papery rustling of wings against the nest material.

**Frequency:** Approximately 100-300 Hz fundamental, with harmonics extending higher. Broader and harsher than the bumblebee's purer tone.

**The papery quality:** Vespidae nests are made of chewed wood fiber -- paper. The nest itself resonates with the collective buzzing, adding a papery, rustling quality to the sound. The buzz and the nest are acoustically coupled.

### Why E=5

1. **COLLECTIVE -- hundreds of hornets buzzing simultaneously.**
2. **AGGRESSIVE -- the sound is a warning, designed to intimidate and repel.**
3. **LOUD -- the collective buzz carries across woodland clearings, clearly audible at 10+ meters.**
4. **SUSTAINED -- continues as long as the perceived threat remains.**
5. **THREATENING -- the acoustic signature of a colony that will sting.**
6. **E=5 is the highest insect energy in the engine** -- matching the cicada's electric throb (89, E=5) but with aggressive intent.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Bumblebee gentle drone** (degree 92, Apidae) -- gentle, low-pitched, solitary pollination hum, E=2. The hornet nest buzz is collective, aggressive, high-energy. Hymenoptera pair: gentle vs threatening.
- **Electric buzzing throb** (degree 89, Cicada) -- tymbal mechanism, E=5. Both E=5, but the cicada advertises while the hornet threatens. Same energy, different intent.
- **Mosquito whine** (degree 91, Culicidae) -- individual wing-produced, E=2. The hornet nest buzz is collective wing-produced, E=5. Individual whine vs collective roar.
- **Cricket chorus** (degree 87, Gryllidae) -- rhythmic chirp field, E=4. The hornet buzz is not rhythmic -- it is continuous and escalating.

## ECOLOGY: THE NEST DEFENDERS

### Habitat

Woodland [1, 2]:

- **Primary:** Trees, shrubs, and sheltered structures in forested and forest-edge habitats.
- **Nest type:** Paper nests constructed from chewed wood fiber mixed with saliva. Can be aerial (suspended from branches) or ground-level (in cavities, wall voids).
- **Colony size:** Mature colonies can contain 200-700+ workers depending on species.
- **PNW species:** Bald-faced hornet (*Dolichovespula maculata*), common yellowjacket (*Vespula vulgaris*), western yellowjacket (*Vespula pensylvanica*).

### Defensive Behavior

- **Alarm pheromone:** When a guard wasp is disturbed, it releases alarm pheromone that recruits nestmates. The collective buzz follows.
- **Escalation:** The defensive buzz escalates in volume and intensity as more wasps emerge. The sound is a graduated warning -- each second louder than the last.
- **Sting capacity:** Unlike honeybees, hornets and wasps can sting repeatedly. The defensive capacity of a colony is multiplicative -- hundreds of stings available.
- **Range:** Defensive behavior extends 5-15 meters from the nest depending on species and colony maturity.

### Diet and Ecology

- **Diet:** Adults feed on nectar and fruit sugars. Larvae are fed chewed insects (caterpillars, flies, other insects). Vespidae are significant predators of pest insects.
- **Ecological role:** Top predators in the insect world. Significant population control of caterpillars and other herbivorous insects. Also pollinators (less efficient than bees).
- **Seasonal cycle:** Queens overwinter alone. Colonies build through spring and summer. Peak size in late summer. Colony dies in fall; only new queens survive to overwinter.
