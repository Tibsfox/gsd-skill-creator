## THE PAIR BOND -- BEHAVIORAL CONTEXT

### Why Two Voices Together Produce E=9

Great Horned Owls form monogamous pair bonds, typically mating for life. The pair bond has acoustic signatures that distinguish it from solo territorial behavior:

- **Coordinated calling.** The bonded pair has developed, through repeated seasonal interactions, a calling pattern in which the male's calls fill gaps in the female's sequence and vice versa. The coordination is learned and refined over successive breeding seasons. A pair that has been together for many years coordinates more precisely than a newly formed pair.
- **Simultaneous peak.** At the December-January peak of the territorial cycle &mdash; immediately before egg-laying (January-February) &mdash; both members of the pair reach their individual calling maximum simultaneously. The temporal coincidence of both maximums produces the E=9 event.
- **Mutual reinforcement.** Both owls calling simultaneously reinforce each other's territorial claim in a way that neither call alone achieves. The combined signal reaches further, carries more acoustic information (including information about the pair bond itself), and deters intruding owls more effectively than a solo call.
- **The female's size.** Great Horned Owls exhibit reverse sexual dimorphism: the female is larger than the male (the female is approximately 30% heavier). Despite being larger, the female's voice is higher-pitched than the male's &mdash; a paradox of owl anatomy. The larger body produces a higher call because of different resonance characteristics in the female's syrinx. The combined male (200-500 Hz) + female (300-600 Hz) calling creates a layered frequency stack that spans from the male's deep fundamental to the female's higher counter-call.

(Sources: Great Horned Owl pair bond; monogamy; coordinated calling behavior; seasonal maximum timing; reverse sexual dimorphism; female larger than male; male lower-pitched than female vocal paradox; combined frequency stack 200-600 Hz)

## DECEMBER-JANUARY -- THE WINTER ACOUSTIC PEAK

### Why the Most Intense Calling Happens in Midwinter

Great Horned Owls are the earliest nesting birds in North America &mdash; they lay eggs in January and February, while deep snow is still present in many parts of their range. This early nesting schedule drives the winter timing of territorial establishment:

- **The nesting calendar.** To lay eggs in January, both the territory and the nest site must be fully established beforehand. The calling wars of December-January are the acoustic signature of this preparation. The birds are working on February while other species haven't thought about spring.
- **The acoustic vacuum.** December-January is the quietest period of the Pacific Northwest bird calendar. Migratory species have departed; no amphibians are calling; no insects; the forest is acoustically sparse. The Great Horned Owl pair's E=9 duet fills a soundscape that has almost nothing else in it. The contrast between the acoustic void and the owl pair's maximum calling creates an experiential impact disproportionate to the absolute sound levels.
- **The cold air physics.** Sound travels differently in cold air. Cold winter nights near Puget Sound have low thermal interference (no thermal columns that distort sound propagation) and often low wind. The calls of the pair at full volume, in these conditions, carry with exceptional clarity and at exceptional distances.

(Sources: Great Horned Owl earliest nesting in North America; January-February egg-laying; December-January territorial establishment; winter acoustic vacuum in Pacific Northwest; cold air acoustic physics; carry distance in winter conditions)
