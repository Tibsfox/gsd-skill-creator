## Structural Event 4: *Neogale* Genus Pair with Mink

- **Situation:** The Long-tailed Weasel (*Neogale frenata*) shares a genus with the Mink (*Neogale vison*, degree 64). Two *Neogale* in the sextet. The Long-tailed Weasel was reclassified from *Mustela* to *Neogale* based on molecular phylogenetics.
- **Root Cause:** Molecular phylogenetic analysis reveals that the Long-tailed Weasel is more closely related to the Mink than to the Short-tailed Weasel (*Mustela erminea*). Despite sharing the common name "weasel," the Long-tailed and Short-tailed Weasels are in different genera. The reclassification overturns traditional taxonomy based on superficial morphological similarity.
- **Recommendation:** The *Neogale* connection is a significant taxonomic event within the sextet. It adds a layer of structural complexity: the weasel pair (67-68) is NOT the sextet's closest taxonomic pair -- the *Neogale* pair (64, 68) is. The Mink and the Long-tailed Weasel are closer relatives than the two "weasels." This counterintuitive finding enriches the sextet's narrative.
- **Carry Item:** Sextet genera: *Lontra* (63), *Neogale* (64, 68), *Martes* (65), *Pekania* (66), *Mustela* (67). Five genera, six entries. *Neogale* is the only repeated genus.

## What Worked

### 1. The Sextet Extends Without Redundancy
The sixth Mustelidae is genuinely different from the fifth: different genus (*Neogale* vs. *Mustela*), different habitat (open woodland vs. meadow edge), different acoustic character (sustained chatter vs. explosive bark), different prey range (gophers/ground squirrels vs. voles/mice specialist). The Long-tailed Weasel earns its place by introducing new dimensions rather than repeating existing ones.

### 2. The Weasel Pair Mirrors Established Pair Patterns
The weasel pair (67-68) follows the same structural model as the Beaver pair (61-62) and Flicker pair (21-22): consecutive same-type species differentiated by size, habitat, and behavior. The pair pattern is now a recognized engine structural element with three instances.

### 3. Six Habitats, Six Species Is Remarkable
The six-habitat gradient (river -> creek bank -> old-growth canopy -> dense forest -> meadow edge -> open woodland) is the sextet's strongest justification. No two members share a primary habitat. The family's ecological breadth across six consecutive entries is genuinely extraordinary.

### 4. The *Neogale* Connection Adds Taxonomic Surprise
The revelation that the Long-tailed Weasel is more closely related to the Mink than to the Short-tailed Weasel is counterintuitive and enriching. It demonstrates that common names can be misleading and that molecular phylogenetics reveals hidden relationships. The genus pair (*Neogale* at 64 and 68) adds structural depth.

### 5. The Sam Beam Parallel Is Maximum Contrast
The quiet folk whisper (E=2) paired with the aggressive chattering weasel (E=4) is the most extreme energy contrast in a Part A/Part B pair. Both operate at the edges of their register. The contrast works because both are genuine rather than performative.
