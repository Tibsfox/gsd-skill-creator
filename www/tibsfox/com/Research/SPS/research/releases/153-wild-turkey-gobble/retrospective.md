*Degree 153 retrospective complete. WILD TURKEY GOBBLE at the one hundred fifty-third degree -- LIQUID THUNDER. The gamebird family enters the engine with the most powerful bird vocalization yet documented: the Wild Turkey's gobble, a rapid rolling cascade of approximately thirty pulses per second that merges into a continuous liquid thunder audible at distances exceeding one mile. The gobble is produced by the turkey's muscular syrinx and amplified by the inflated crop and throat sacs, covering an extraordinary spectral range from 100 to 9000 Hz, with peak energy in the 1000-4000 Hz band and an amplitude exceeding 100 decibels at close range. This is E=6 maximum energy returning to the SPS axis after a 45-degree drought -- the longest gap between E=6 events in the engine -- broken by a bird whose voice is so powerful that it can be heard from over a mile away in oak woodland. The Wild Turkey is not native to the Pacific Northwest. It was introduced to Washington by the Department of Fish and Wildlife using three subspecies -- Eastern, Rio Grande, and Merriam's -- placed in eastern Washington's oak-prairie and ponderosa pine habitats. The introduced populations have established and continue to expand, a local chapter of the national restoration success story that recovered the Wild Turkey from an estimated 30,000 birds in the 1930s to over 7 million today. Benjamin Franklin called the Wild Turkey "a Bird of Courage" and "a true original Native of America," preferring it over the Bald Eagle as the national bird. The Phasianidae quartet opens with maximum energy: Wild Turkey gobble at 153 (E=6, liquid thunder), then Dusky Grouse deep booming at 154 (E=5, subsonic), Ruffed Grouse drumming at 155 (E=5, mechanical), and Sooty Grouse muffled booming at 156 (E=5, ventriloquial). From the loudest to the most mysterious. From the sound that carries a mile to the sound that seems to come from everywhere. The gamebirds have arrived.*
