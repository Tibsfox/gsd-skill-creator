## E=6 -- THE GOBBLE AS ACOUSTIC DOMINANCE

### Why E=6

The Wild Turkey gobble registers at E=6, the maximum energy level:

- **Sheer volume:** The gobble is one of the loudest bird vocalizations in North America. It can be heard at distances exceeding one mile (1.6 km) under favorable conditions. Few bird sounds achieve this propagation distance.
- **Acoustic power:** The gobble is produced by the turkey's specialized syrinx and resonated by the inflated throat and chest. Peak sound pressure levels can reach 100+ dB at close range -- comparable to a chainsaw or a rock concert.
- **Rapid liquid modulation:** The gobble is not a simple call but a rapid series of notes -- a "liquid" rolling sound with frequency modulation that covers a broad spectral range (100-9000 Hz). The acoustic complexity contributes to its carrying power.
- **Behavioral dominance:** The gobble is an aggressive display -- a territorial and mating call that announces the male's presence, fitness, and dominance. The energy is not just acoustic but behavioral: the gobbling tom is declaring supremacy.
- **Dawn chorus intensity:** During the breeding season (March-May), gobblers begin calling from their roost trees before dawn, often triggering a chain reaction of gobbles from other males. A dawn chorus of Wild Turkeys in oak woodland is one of the most acoustically intense events in North American bird life.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Dickson 1992; Eaton 1992; engine internal analysis)

## THE GOBBLE -- ACOUSTIC ANALYSIS

### How the Gobble Works

The Wild Turkey gobble is a complex vocalization:

- **Production:** The gobble originates in the syrinx (the avian voice organ at the junction of the trachea and bronchi). The turkey's syrinx is unusually muscular, allowing precise control over the rapid frequency modulations that characterize the gobble.
- **Resonance:** The male turkey inflates its crop and throat sacs during gobbling, creating a resonance chamber that amplifies and modifies the sound. The bare skin of the head and neck (the snood and caruncles) also vibrate during intense gobbling, contributing to the acoustic output.
- **Frequency range:** The gobble spans approximately 100-9000 Hz, with most energy concentrated in the 1000-4000 Hz band. This is a remarkably broad spectral range for a single vocalization.
- **Temporal structure:** A single gobble lasts approximately 1 second and consists of a rapid series of approximately 25-35 individual notes or pulses. The notes are produced so rapidly that they merge into a continuous "rolling" or "liquid" sound -- the characteristic gobbling quality.
- **Amplitude modulation:** The rapid pulsing of the gobble creates an amplitude modulation (tremolo) effect that aids in long-distance propagation. The modulated signal is more resistant to atmospheric absorption than a pure tone.
- **Propagation:** Under favorable acoustic conditions (calm air, open terrain, cool temperatures), the gobble can propagate over 1 mile (1.6 km). In oak woodland with moderate understory, effective gobble range is typically 0.5-0.75 miles. The long-distance propagation is aided by the low-frequency components (100-500 Hz) and the amplitude modulation.

### The Gobble in Context

The gobble serves multiple functions:

1. **Mate attraction:** The primary function. Hens hear the gobble and move toward the gobbling tom. The quality of the gobble (loudness, duration, frequency of repetition) may serve as an honest signal of male fitness.
2. **Male competition:** Gobblers respond to each other's gobbles. A dominant tom gobbles more frequently, more loudly, and from prominent roost sites. Subordinate males may reduce their gobbling in the presence of a dominant male.
3. **Dawn announcement:** Gobblers typically begin the day with gobbling from the roost tree, before flying down to the ground. This pre-dawn gobbling is the loudest and most intense, serving as a general announcement of the male's presence and location.
4. **Response to loud sounds:** Wild Turkeys are famous for gobbling in response to loud environmental sounds -- thunder, car horns, slamming doors, crow calls, owl hoots, coyote howls. This "shock gobble" response is exploited by hunters who use locator calls (crow calls, owl hoots) to trigger gobbles and locate birds.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eaton 1992; Dickson 1992; engine internal analysis)

## WILD TURKEY IN THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST -- A RESTORATION STORY

### Not Originally a PNW Bird

The Wild Turkey's presence in the Pacific Northwest is a restoration and introduction story:

- **Native range:** The Wild Turkey is native to eastern and southern North America -- eastern forests, southeastern pine-oak woodland, and the Southwest (Gould's and Merriam's subspecies). The species was NOT native to the Pacific Northwest.
- **Historical absence:** Wild Turkeys were absent from Washington, Oregon, and the Pacific Northwest when European settlers arrived. The dense coniferous forests and wet climate of western Washington did not match the turkey's preferred oak-woodland and savanna habitats.
- **Introduction programs:** Beginning in the mid-20th century, state wildlife agencies introduced Wild Turkeys to the Pacific Northwest. Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) has conducted multiple introductions:
  - **Eastern subspecies** (*M. g. silvestris*) introduced to eastern Washington oak-pine habitats
  - **Rio Grande subspecies** (*M. g. intermedia*) introduced to central and eastern Washington
  - **Merriam's subspecies** (*M. g. merriami*) introduced to eastern Washington ponderosa pine
- **Establishment:** Introduced populations have established successfully in several areas:
  - **Eastern Washington:** Klickitat County, Yakima County, Okanogan County -- oak-prairie and ponderosa pine habitats
  - **Western Washington foothills:** Small populations in Lewis County, Clark County, and other foothills areas
  - **Expanding range:** The Wild Turkey's range in Washington continues to expand as populations grow and birds colonize new habitat

### Ben Franklin's Bird

The Wild Turkey holds a unique place in American cultural history:

- **Benjamin Franklin's preference:** In a famous 1784 letter to his daughter Sarah Bache, Benjamin Franklin expressed his preference for the Wild Turkey over the Bald Eagle as the national bird. He called the Bald Eagle "a Bird of bad moral Character" who "does not get his Living honestly" (referring to the eagle's habit of stealing fish from Ospreys). Of the turkey, Franklin wrote that it was "a Bird of Courage" and "a true original Native of America."
- **Cultural significance:** The Wild Turkey is deeply embedded in American culture -- Thanksgiving, hunting tradition, conservation success story. The bird was nearly extirpated from much of its range by the early 20th century due to overhunting and habitat loss, then recovered through one of the most successful wildlife restoration programs in North American history.
- **Conservation triumph:** From an estimated 30,000 birds in the 1930s to over 7 million today. The Wild Turkey restoration is one of the great success stories of North American wildlife management, driven by the National Wild Turkey Federation, state wildlife agencies, and hunter-funded conservation programs.

(Sources: WDFW Wild Turkey management; National Wild Turkey Federation; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Franklin 1784; Dickson 1992)

## TURKEY BIOLOGY AND DISPLAY

### The Tom in Full Display

The male Wild Turkey (tom) in full breeding display is one of the most spectacular sights in North American bird life:

- **Strut display:** The tom fans his tail feathers into a full semicircle, puffs his body feathers to appear nearly twice normal size, droops his wings until the primary tips drag on the ground, inflates his crop, and pulls his head back into the body while the snood (the fleshy protuberance above the bill) elongates and hangs to one side.
- **Color changes:** The bare skin of the head and neck changes color rapidly during display -- from red to white to blue -- controlled by blood flow and muscle contraction. The color changes are among the most rapid in any vertebrate.
- **Body feathers:** The iridescent bronze-green body feathers catch light differently as the bird turns during the strut. The breast tuft (beard) -- a modified feather growing from the breast -- hangs prominently. The beard can reach 10-12 inches in mature toms.
- **Spurs:** Males develop sharp leg spurs used in combat with other toms. Spur length correlates with age and is an indicator of dominance.
- **Weight:** Adult male Wild Turkeys weigh 11-24 pounds (5-11 kg), making them among the heaviest flying birds in North America. Domestic turkeys are much heavier; wild birds are lean, muscular, and surprisingly athletic.

### Flight

Despite their size, Wild Turkeys are strong fliers:

- **Burst flight:** Capable of explosive takeoff and short-distance flight at speeds up to 55 mph (88 km/h). The burst flight is used to escape predators and to fly to/from roost trees.
- **Roost flight:** Turkeys roost in trees at night for predator protection. The evening fly-up to the roost and morning fly-down are daily rituals accompanied by vocalizations.
- **Limited sustained flight:** Not a long-distance flier. Flights are typically short -- several hundred yards. The turkey is primarily a walking bird, covering several miles per day on foot.

(Sources: Eaton 1992; Dickson 1992; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Oak Woodland -- The Gobble

The Wild Turkey's gobble is the focal vocalization for this entry:

**The Gobble:**
- **Trigger:** Breeding season (March-May, peak in April). Dawn announcement from roost tree. Response to hens, rival males, loud environmental sounds.
- **Mechanism:** Syringeal production with crop/throat sac resonance amplification. Rapid ~30-pulse series creating liquid rolling quality.
- **Character:** Loud, liquid, rolling, resonant. The classic "gobble-obble-obble-obble" -- a rapid, tremulous, rolling vocalization that is instantly recognizable and audible at extreme distances.
- **Frequency:** 100-9000 Hz (broad spectrum). Peak energy: 1000-4000 Hz. Low-frequency components aid long-distance propagation.
- **Duration:** Individual gobble: ~1 second. Gobbling bouts: multiple gobbles at 5-30 second intervals, continuing for minutes to hours during peak breeding.
- **Amplitude:** E=6. 100+ dB at close range. Audible at 1+ mile under favorable conditions. One of the loudest bird vocalizations in North America.
- **Dawn context:** Pre-dawn gobbling from roost tree. Multiple toms triggering chain-reaction gobbles. The dawn chorus of Wild Turkeys in oak woodland is acoustically overwhelming.
- **Shock gobble:** Response to loud environmental sounds (thunder, horns, slams, crow calls, owl hoots). Involuntary alarm response exploited by hunters.

**Other Vocalizations (secondary):**
- **Yelp:** The hen's most common call. A series of single-note vocalizations. Used for communication within the flock.
- **Cluck:** Short, sharp notes used for attention and assembly.
- **Purr:** Soft, rolling vocalization indicating contentment during feeding.
- **Kee-kee run:** Lost call of young turkeys. High-pitched whistles breaking into yelps.
- **Alarm putt:** Sharp, single-note alarm call that alerts the flock to danger.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eaton 1992; Dickson 1992; engine internal analysis)
