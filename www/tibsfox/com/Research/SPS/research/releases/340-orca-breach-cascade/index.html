The Most Dramatic Behavioral Event in the SPS Engine

Three consecutive full-body breaches from a single Southern Resident Killer Whale (Orcinus orca) in the open Salish Sea. Each breach: 8–10× normal swimming energy cost, full body (6–10 metric tons) entirely airborne, 140+ dB re-entry impact at source, acoustic event detectable at 10–50 km on hydrophone. Three consecutive. Same animal. 30–90 second interval between re-entry and next launch. E=9 through focused behavioral apex — the single most dramatic thing a single animal in this ecosystem can do, performed three times consecutively.

The Biomechanics

An adult Southern Resident Killer Whale weighs 6,000–10,000+ kg (6–10 metric tons). Body length 6–9 meters. The breach requires the animal to reach 8–12 m/s (30–45 km/h) from a subsurface dive, converting all that kinetic energy into a vertical launch that clears the entire body above the surface. The energy cost per breach: 8–10 times normal swimming effort. This is the orca's maximum physical effort, equivalent to a human sprinting at maximum speed after a standing start, repeated three times consecutively.

The re-entry is deliberate — the orca typically lands on its back or side to maximize the impact area and produce the maximum splash and pressure wave. The impact at 140+ dB at source is the loudest natural sound from a single marine mammal in the Salish Sea. Three impacts, separated by 30–90 seconds each: the most acoustically dramatic single-source behavioral event in the engine.

Breach Cascade as Conservation Indicator

A three-breach cascade requires a healthy, well-nourished animal at high energy. Southern Resident Killer Whales in poor nutritional condition — documented in years of low Chinook salmon returns — exhibit reduced surface activity and reduced breach frequency. The cascade is a behavioral health indicator: when the SRKWs are breaching like this, the fish are running. When Chinook runs fail, the breaches stop. The conservation argument for Snake and Columbia River dam removal, for Chinook habitat restoration, for reduced commercial and sport harvest, is audible in the sound of this event.
