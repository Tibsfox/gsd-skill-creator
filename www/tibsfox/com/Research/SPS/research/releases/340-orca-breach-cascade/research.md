## THE BREACH -- BIOMECHANICS AND ACOUSTICS

### What a Single Breach Involves

The mechanics of one breach before characterizing three:

- **The approach.** The orca initiates a breach with a subsurface acceleration sequence. The animal dives 6-10 meters, then accelerates upward through the water column. The approach speed reaches 8-12 meters per second (30-45 km/h) at the surface. During the approach phase, the orca may vocalize -- producing calls within the pod's dialect, often the excited social calls associated with high-activity states. The approach is audible underwater to any hydrophone within range: the orca's bow wave and the acceleration-associated pressure changes produce a characteristic pre-breach acoustic signature.
- **The launch.** The orca exits the water at the surface at breach velocity (8-12 m/s). The full body (dorsal fin, entire torso, pectoral fins, tail flukes) clears the water surface. Adult orca body length is 6-8 meters (females) or 7-9 meters (males); adult weight is 6,000-8,000 kg (females) or 8,000-10,000+ kg (males). The breach clears this entire mass above the water surface. The moment of maximum aerial height occurs approximately 0.5-1.0 second after exit.
- **The aerial posture.** During the breach, the orca is typically oriented at 45-90 degrees from vertical -- some breaches are nearly vertical launches; others are more oblique, the body nearly horizontal at maximum height. The aerial moment is typically 0.5-1.5 seconds total. Observers of orca breaches describe the aerial moment as one of the most striking visual experiences in wildlife observation: a 6-8 metric-ton animal, fully airborne.
- **The re-entry.** The orca returns to the water surface typically on its back or side, maximizing the impact area. This is the dominant acoustic event: the full-body impact at high velocity, producing a pressure wave in the water and a percussive impact at the surface. The sound of the re-entry is the breach sound -- the loud, percussive, wide-bandwidth impact that can be heard for miles.
- **The re-entry acoustics.** The orca re-entry produces: (1) the impact sound at the surface, 140+ dB at source, predominantly 10-500 Hz with significant energy extending to 2,000 Hz; (2) the pressure wave propagating through the water at sound speed (approximately 1,500 m/s in seawater), detectable on hydrophones at 10+ km range; (3) the splash and aeration: the post-impact water displacement produces a sustained splash event audible above the surface at 70-85 dB at 30 meters. The total acoustic event (approach underwater + impact + splash) lasts 10-30 seconds.

(Sources: Orca breach subsurface acceleration 8-12 m/s breach velocity; 6-10m dive depth before breach; pre-breach social vocalizations high-activity state; adult female body length 6-8m weight 6000-8000 kg; adult male body length 7-9m weight 8000-10000+ kg; aerial posture 45-90 degrees from vertical; aerial duration 0.5-1.5 seconds; re-entry back or side landing for maximum impact area; re-entry acoustic 140+ dB at source; frequency range 10-500 Hz dominant; pressure wave 10+ km hydrophone range; splash 70-85 dB at 30m above surface; total acoustic event 10-30 seconds)

## THE CASCADE -- THREE CONSECUTIVE BREACHES

### Why Three Consecutive Breaches Are the Engine's Maximum

The three-breach cascade:

- **The energetics of consecutive breaching.** A single full breach is the most energetically costly locomotor behavior in the orca's repertoire -- estimated at 8-12 times the energy cost of normal swimming. The energy is expended in the acceleration phase: the entire locomotor power of the animal concentrated into a brief burst of maximum-effort swimming to reach breach velocity. Following a breach, the animal re-enters the water and must dissipate the kinetic energy of re-entry before the next acceleration is possible.
- **The three-breach threshold.** The maximum documented consecutive breach count for Southern Resident Killer Whales in the documented behavioral record is typically 2-5 consecutive breaches from a single individual in a high-activity period. Three consecutive breaches represents the point at which the behavior is simultaneously its most dramatic expression and still physically achievable without apparent physiological stress. Beyond three to five consecutive breaches, the behavior typically ceases and the animal returns to normal swimming behavior.
- **The interval between breaches.** In a true cascade (three consecutive breaches), the interval between re-entry and the next breach initiation is 10-30 seconds -- just enough time for the animal to orient for the next approach. The observer above the surface sees: breach 1, splash, 15-second wait, breach 2, splash, 15-second wait, breach 3, splash. The acoustic event on hydrophone: three distinct broadband impact signals, each 10-30 seconds in duration, separated by the characteristic pre-breach acceleration signature.
- **Why this is THE MOST DRAMATIC BEHAVIORAL EVENT.** The SPS engine has documented many behavioral events: orca echolocation (degree 331, E=9 through acoustic complexity); the stonefly hatch (E=9 through biological scale); the Grizzly tri-layer convergence (E=9 through environmental convergence). The orca breach cascade achieves E=9 through something different: the behavioral maximum of the most physically impressive marine mammal in the Pacific Northwest. Three consecutive full-body launches of a 6-10 metric-ton animal into the air, with the associated acoustic events, in the open Salish Sea. This is E=9 through focused behavioral apex -- the single most dramatic thing a single animal in this ecosystem can do, repeated three times consecutively.

## THE SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALES

### The Specific Population

The orca population that produces the degree-340 event:

- **Population identity.** The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) are a genetically and culturally distinct population of Orcinus orca that ranges throughout the Salish Sea, Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and coastal Pacific from British Columbia south to California. They are not the same population as the Northern Residents (British Columbia), the Bigg's killer whales (transients that eat marine mammals), or the offshore killer whales. The SRKWs are fish-eaters, primarily salmon specialists (90%+ Chinook salmon in the diet).
- **The three pods.** The SRKW community consists of three pods: J pod (approximately 20-25 individuals), K pod (approximately 16-18 individuals), and L pod (approximately 30-35 individuals). Total SRKW population as of 2023: approximately 73-75 individuals, down from a high of 98 in 1995. The population has been listed as Endangered under the U.S. Endangered Species Act since 2005.
- **The Chinook dependency.** The SRKW's near-total dependence on Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha) makes them sensitive to every factor affecting Pacific Northwest Chinook runs: dam operations on the Snake and Columbia Rivers, hatchery practices, habitat degradation, commercial and sport harvest. The decline in SRKW population since 1995 is directly correlated with Chinook salmon availability.
- **The behavioral richness.** The SRKW are among the most behaviorally complex animals in the engine's entire catalog. They have documented culture (dialect variations between pods that are socially transmitted across generations), cooperative hunting, complex social bonds (matrilineal groups, male-female relationships, inter-pod associations), and a lifespan comparable to humans (females to 80-90 years, males to 50-60 years). The breach cascade is one behavioral expression within this richly documented behavioral repertoire.

(Sources: Southern Resident Killer Whale distinct population; Salish Sea Puget Sound Strait of Juan de Fuca range; fish-eating 90%+ Chinook salmon diet; J pod 20-25 individuals, K pod 16-18, L pod 30-35; total SRKW population 73-75 as of 2023, high of 98 in 1995; ESA Endangered listing 2005; Chinook salmon dependency; Snake Columbia River dam operations impact; SRKW cultural transmission dialect variation; matrilineal social structure; female lifespan to 80-90 years male to 50-60 years)

## THE ACOUSTIC ECOLOGY OF THE BREACH CASCADE

### Three Impacts in the Open Sound

The acoustic character of the three-breach cascade in Puget Sound / Salish Sea:

- **The open sound environment.** The breach cascade occurs in open Puget Sound or the Salish Sea -- water depths of 20-300 meters, with relatively little bathymetric obstruction compared to a confined estuary or inlet. The orca re-entry pressure wave propagates outward from the impact point in a roughly spherical pattern (modified by the surface reflection and the bottom reflection). In open deep water, the pressure wave can be detected on NOAA-OA hydrophone arrays at ranges of 10-50 km.
- **Three impact events.** The three-breach cascade produces three distinct broadband impact events on hydrophone, separated by 30-90 seconds each (the approach interval). The temporal pattern: impact 1 → 10-500 Hz broadband signal, 140+ dB at source; 30-90 second pause with pre-breach acceleration signature; impact 2 → same; pause; impact 3 → same. The three events are distinguishable as coming from the same individual by their acoustic similarity (same body mass producing similar impact characteristics) and spatial proximity (the animal does not travel far between breaches).
- **Vocalization context.** During high-activity periods that produce breach cascades, SRKW pods are typically vocalizing heavily -- producing the excited pulsed calls and whistles associated with foraging activity, social interaction, or play. The breach cascade is typically embedded in a period of elevated overall acoustic activity. The background of pod vocalization, combined with three successive breach impact events, constitutes one of the richest acoustic environments in the SPS engine.
- **Above-surface sound.** The acoustic event above the water surface: each breach produces 70-85 dB splash impact audible at 30+ meters, preceded by the visible and audible exhalation of the orca clearing water from its blowhole just before the launch, and followed by the water displacement settling event (the post-impact splash that can continue for 3-5 seconds). Above surface, the three-breach cascade is the loudest natural acoustic event from a single biological source that occurs in the Salish Sea.

(Sources: Puget Sound Salish Sea bathymetry 20-300 meters; pressure wave propagation spherical pattern surface and bottom reflection; NOAA-OA hydrophone array 10-50 km detection range; three impact events temporal pattern 30-90 second intervals; pre-breach acceleration signature hydrophone; three events distinguishable by acoustic similarity same body mass; SRKW elevated vocalization during high-activity period; above-surface splash 70-85 dB at 30m; blowhole exhalation before launch; post-impact water displacement 3-5 seconds; loudest single-biological-source natural acoustic event in Salish Sea)

## THE CONSERVATION CONTEXT

### The Endangered Population at the Engine's Climax

- **SRKW at 73-75 individuals.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale population is at approximately 73-75 individuals as of 2023, down from 98 in 1995. The population is endangered. The primary limiting factor is Chinook salmon availability -- the fish these orcas have evolved to eat, in a river system that humans have dammed, diverted, and disrupted.
- **The breach cascade as conservation argument.** A three-breach cascade from an SRKW is an event that requires a healthy, well-nourished, high-energy animal. An animal in poor nutritional condition (as documented in years of poor Chinook returns) cannot sustain three consecutive maximum-effort launches. The breach cascade is therefore not just a dramatic behavioral event -- it is a behavioral health indicator. When the SRKWs are breaching like this, the fish are running.
- **The engine's closing conservation statement.** The SPS engine closes its metal summit arc with the most dramatic behavioral event in the Pacific Northwest marine ecosystem, from a population that is endangered. This is the engine's implicit conservation argument: the Salish Sea produces events of extraordinary natural drama, from a species of extraordinary cultural and ecological significance, in numbers so small that each individual breach cascade is a rare event to witness. What is at stake in Pacific Northwest salmon recovery is not just fish -- it is this.
