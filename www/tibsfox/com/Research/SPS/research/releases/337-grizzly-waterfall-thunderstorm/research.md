## FORCE 1: GRIZZLY BEAR VOCALIZATION

### Ursus arctos horribilis -- Apex Predator Acoustic Behavior

The Grizzly Bear's acoustic contribution:

- **Taxonomy and Pacific Northwest status.** *Ursus arctos horribilis*, the Grizzly Bear, is the apex predator of the Pacific Northwest montane and subalpine ecosystem. In the contiguous United States, Grizzlies survive primarily in the Northern Rockies (Greater Yellowstone and Northern Continental Divide ecosystems) and the North Cascades ecosystem in Washington State. The North Cascades population is critically small (estimated fewer than 10 individuals as of recent surveys), making North Cascades Grizzly encounters extremely rare. The degree-337 convergence is set in this remote wilderness context.
- **The vocalization repertoire.** Grizzly Bears have one of the most complex vocal repertoires of North American terrestrial mammals: (1) The roar: open-mouth, full-lung exhalation reaching 90-100 dB at 10 meters. Used for territorial disputes, threat response, and mate competition. Fundamental frequency 100-300 Hz with harmonics to 1,500 Hz. Duration 2-5 seconds. (2) The jaw-pop: rapid closing of the jaws producing a sharp percussive crack. Used as a warning signal -- the jaw-pop precedes escalation in agonistic encounters. (3) The huffing sequence: short sharp exhalations through the nose, typically in sequences of 3-5 huffs. Signals agitation or threat assessment. (4) The moan: low-frequency continuous vocalization used in social interactions and between mother and cubs. Frequency 80-200 Hz.
- **The roar context.** In the degree-337 convergence, the Grizzly's acoustic contribution is the full roar -- the maximum vocalization, deployed in a territorial or threat context. At a waterfall site in remote wilderness, a Grizzly might be defending a salmon run (below the falls), responding to another bear attempting the same site, or responding to an intruder (including human). The roar at this context is the full territorial declaration: maximum volume, maximum frequency spread, maximum behavioral assertion.
- **The acoustic character.** A Grizzly roar is not simply loud -- it has a specific tonal quality that is distinct from any other sound in the Pacific Northwest wilderness. The low fundamental (100-300 Hz) combined with the harmonic series creates a complex sound that has both a physical (felt in the chest at close range) and a highly specific biological quality. The sound is simultaneously felt and recognized as a specific biological signal. This dual quality -- physical and semantic -- is part of what makes the Grizzly's acoustic contribution to the E=9 convergence distinct from the waterfall's purely physical contribution.

## FORCE 2: PERMANENT WATERFALL

### The Mountain Canyon's Hydrological Engine

The waterfall's acoustic contribution:

- **Permanent waterfall definition.** A permanent waterfall in the Pacific Northwest montane ecosystem is fed by a combination of snowpack and glacier melt -- it flows year-round because the snowfield or glacier above it provides continuous water supply regardless of precipitation. In summer, when snowmelt is at its annual maximum, the permanent waterfall is at its highest flow and highest acoustic output.
- **Acoustic output at maximum summer flow.** A significant permanent waterfall in a Pacific Northwest mountain canyon -- a 30-50 meter drop with 10-50 cubic meters per second flow at peak summer -- produces 85-100 dB at 30 meters. The frequency spectrum is broadband: from approximately 20 Hz (the infrasonic component of the impact zone at the base of the falls) through 8,000+ Hz (the high-frequency spray and mist component). The waterfall is an acoustic white noise source with a low-frequency bias -- most of its energy is below 2,000 Hz.
- **The directional character.** The waterfall's sound is directionally oriented -- it is louder near the falls and drops off with distance (following the inverse square law, modified by canyon reflection and terrain). In a canyon setting, the waterfall's sound is reflected from canyon walls, creating a reverberant environment where the waterfall's acoustic output is reinforced rather than simply attenuated. This canyon reverb can maintain waterfall at 70-80 dB at distances of 100+ meters.
- **The continuous baseline.** The waterfall provides the acoustic baseline against which the other two forces (Grizzly roar and thunder) emerge. The continuous broadband noise of the waterfall is the foundation; the Grizzly's roar is a specific biological signal emerging from and against this continuous background; the thunder is an impulsive atmospheric event that interrupts and overrides everything momentarily. The three layers have different temporal structures: continuous (waterfall), biological-rhythmic (Grizzly), and impulsive-periodic (thunder).

(Sources: Permanent waterfall definition snowpack glacier melt year-round; summer peak flow 10-50 cubic meters per second; acoustic output 85-100 dB at 30m; broadband frequency spectrum 20 Hz to 8000+ Hz; low-frequency bias sub-2000 Hz energy distribution; canyon reverb reinforcement; continuous baseline vs. biological-rhythmic vs. impulsive-periodic temporal structure comparison)

## FORCE 3: SUMMER THUNDERSTORM

### Atmospheric Discharge in Remote Wilderness

The thunderstorm's acoustic contribution:

- **Summer thunderstorm distribution in Pacific Northwest montane wilderness.** Summer (July-August) is the primary thunderstorm season for Pacific Northwest mountains, driven by the convective instability of warm, moist air rising over heated terrain. The Cascades and Olympics experience 20-40 thunderstorm days per year, concentrated in July and August when daytime heating is strongest and atmospheric moisture is adequate. In montane terrain above 4,000 feet, afternoon thunderstorms are a regular summer occurrence.
- **Lightning and thunder acoustics.** A lightning stroke produces a plasma channel that instantaneously reaches approximately 30,000 K (five times the surface temperature of the sun). The rapid heating and expansion of the air creates a shockwave that propagates as thunder. The acoustic characteristics of thunder: (1) The initial crack at close range (within 300 meters): a sharp, very high-amplitude, broadband impulse, 120-130 dB, with frequency content from infrasound to above 20 kHz. (2) The rumble at longer range (300 meters to 10 kilometers): the crack extends into a reverberant rumble as the sound reflects from terrain, thundercell structure, and clouds. The low-frequency rumble dominates at longer ranges, 1-200 Hz. (3) Duration: the total thunder event from crack to decay is 5-30 seconds depending on lightning path length, cloud structure, and terrain.
- **The infrasonic component.** Thunder produces significant infrasound (below 20 Hz) from both the lightning channel and the pressure wave of the storm system itself. Large thunderstorm cells produce infrasonic pressure waves that can be detected hundreds of kilometers from the storm. In the near-field (within 1 km), the infrasonic component of thunder is physically felt as a pressure change -- the concussive character of nearby thunder that is experienced as a physical blow to the body.
- **The storm environment.** In remote wilderness during a summer thunderstorm, all other sounds are subordinated. The wind preceding the cell (30-60+ km/h), the rain beginning (white noise addition to the waterfall backdrop), the lightning flash and thunder crack, and the subsequent storm passage create an acoustic environment that is unlike any other natural sound event. The thunderstorm is the acoustic event that reorders all other sounds -- it sets a new baseline, overrides other signals, and imposes its temporal structure on the environment.

(Sources: Pacific Northwest mountain thunderstorm distribution July-August convective instability; 20-40 thunderstorm days per year Cascades Olympics; 4000+ feet montane afternoon pattern; lightning plasma channel 30,000 K; thunder crack close range 120-130 dB broadband; rumble 1-200 Hz longer range; thunder duration 5-30 seconds; infrasound below 20 Hz; infrasonic physical pressure felt near-field; storm wind rain lightning reorders acoustic environment; storm as acoustic event that overrides other signals)

## THE REMOTE WILDERNESS ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT

### Where All Three Forces Converge

The ecological and geographic setting:

- **Remote wilderness definition.** The degree-337 convergence occurs in remote wilderness -- terrain accessible only by multi-day backcountry travel, above the primary trail network, in habitat that sees few human visitors per year. In the Pacific Northwest, this means the core wilderness zones of the North Cascades, the Olympic Mountains backcountry, or the Selkirk-Purcell wilderness in northeast Washington. These are the zones where Grizzly Bear habitat, permanent snowfield-fed waterfalls, and summer convective storms all coexist.
- **The North Cascades context.** The North Cascades National Park complex (established 1968, 684,000 acres) contains the densest concentration of glaciers in the contiguous United States (more than 300) and supports the highest-relief terrain in Washington State (peaks to 9,206 ft at Goode Mountain). The combination of glacial melt (waterfall) + Grizzly Bear habitat (confirmed but critically small population) + summer convective storms makes the North Cascades the most likely specific location for the degree-337 convergence.
- **The acoustic isolation.** In the North Cascades backcountry at 5,000-7,000 feet elevation in July, the ambient sound level during calm conditions is approximately 20-30 dB -- among the lowest ambient sound levels measurable in the contiguous United States. Against this near-silence baseline, the tri-layer convergence (waterfall at 90+ dB, Grizzly roar at 90-100 dB, thunder crack at 120+ dB) achieves its full dynamic range. The E=9 calibration reflects not just the absolute amplitude but the dynamic range from near-silence to maximum intensity.
- **The seasonal window.** The July-August window is the only time when all three forces can converge: Grizzly Bears are active in subalpine zones for berries and roots; snowmelt waterfalls are at maximum flow; summer convective storms are at peak frequency. Outside this window, the bears descend or hibernate, the waterfalls reduce, and the convective storm pattern ends with autumn's return to Pacific frontal systems.
