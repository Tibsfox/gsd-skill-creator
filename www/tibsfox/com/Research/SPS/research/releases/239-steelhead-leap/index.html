ANADROMOUS RAINBOW TROUT — SAME SPECIES, TWO LIVES

Steelhead is Oncorhynchus mykiss — the same species as Rainbow Trout — but in the anadromous form: born in a river, migrates to the ocean, grows large on the marine food web, returns. The ocean coloration is metallic steel-grey, naming the fish. Steelhead are iteroparous (may spawn multiple times) unlike Pacific salmon (semelparous). Winter steelhead dominate Puget Sound rivers. Multiple ESUs Threatened. Olympic Peninsula wild winter Steelhead in critical decline.

The Metallic Jump

Steelhead leap more frequently and more acrobatically than Pacific salmon — during upstream migration, when hooked by anglers, and for reasons not fully understood. The Steelhead’s slender, torpedo-shaped body produces a different acoustic signature than the Chinook’s heavy body-slap: the Steelhead enters the water more cleanly, at a steeper angle, with less lateral splash. The sound is brighter — higher frequency dominant, shorter in duration. A clean crack of water, then the riffle resumes. Metallic in sound as it is metallic in color.

"The Steelhead does not hit the water like a hammer. It slices back in -- a clean, bright impact that the winter river absorbs quickly. The sound is brief and precise. Then the pool is quiet again."

Acoustic Profile — Steelhead Leap

Water entry (clean): bright broadband, 100–3000+ Hz, short duration
Launch splash: lower frequency, 50–300 Hz, brief
Post-jump turbulence: decreasing, sub-second
Background winter river: low continuous (ambient bed)

E=5: one unit below Chinook leap (E=6). Steelhead mass (2-5 kg typical) is lower than Chinook; slender body enters water more cleanly (less lateral splash). The jump is bright and episodic: brief, distinctive, gone quickly. The winter river is quiet in between.

Same Species, Two Lives

Rainbow Trout and Steelhead are O. mykiss in two life-history forms. The difference is smoltification: at 1–3 years, some juveniles undergo the physiological transformation to marine life (kidneys reverse, gills develop salt cells, body silvers) and migrate to the ocean. These are Steelhead. Others remain in freshwater: these are Rainbow Trout. In rivers where both occur, they interbreed. The ocean changes the fish: a Steelhead returning from the Pacific at 3-4 years weighs 3–6 kg; a Rainbow Trout of the same age in the same river weighs 0.5–1 kg.

Winter vs. Summer Steelhead

  • Winter Steelhead (documented here): Enter Nov–Apr. Spawn Feb–May. Primary Puget Sound rivers and coastal WA rivers. Smaller average size (2–5 kg). Moving toward spawning quickly after river entry.
  • Summer Steelhead: Enter May–Oct. Hold through summer; spawn in spring. Columbia River tributaries primarily. Vulnerable to warm summer river temperatures as climate warms.

Conservation

MULTIPLE ESA LISTINGS + OLYMPIC PENINSULA CRISIS. Puget Sound Steelhead: Threatened. Snake River summer Steelhead: Threatened. Olympic Peninsula wild winter Steelhead: critical decline, WDFW emergency closures on multiple rivers (Hoh, Sol Duc, Quillayute system). The Olympic Peninsula wild winter Steelhead situation is one of the most urgent conservation crises in Washington State fisheries. Hatchery genetic contamination of wild populations is an ongoing management challenge.
