## THE SAME SPECIES, TWO LIVES

### Rainbow Trout and Steelhead -- One *Oncorhynchus mykiss*

Steelhead is not a separate species from Rainbow Trout -- it is the same species in a different life-history form:

- **The biological fact.** *Oncorhynchus mykiss* is a single species with two life-history strategies: resident (Rainbow Trout, which remains in freshwater throughout its life) and anadromous (Steelhead, which migrates to the ocean and returns). In streams where both forms exist, they interbreed. The genetic difference between resident Rainbow Trout and migratory Steelhead from the same river system is minimal. The difference in their lives, however, is maximal.
- **Smoltification.** Steelhead juveniles undergo smoltification -- the physiological transformation from freshwater to marine fish -- at 1-3 years of age and migrate to the Pacific Ocean. The process is the same as described for Pacific salmon at degree 236: kidney reversal, gill salt-cell development, body silver coloration, behavioral shift. The smoltifying Steelhead is becoming a marine animal.
- **The ocean phase.** In the ocean, Steelhead grow rapidly on the marine food web. They may spend 1-4 years at sea. An ocean Steelhead is 2-5 kg typically (occasionally to 8-12+ kg on exceptional fish). The ocean coloration is metallic steel-grey on the dorsal surface -- the color that names the fish. The sides are bright silver. The pink lateral stripe that identifies the Rainbow Trout on land is faded or absent in the fresh-from-ocean phase.
- **The return.** Steelhead return to their natal rivers to spawn, like other Pacific salmon. Unlike other Pacific salmon, Steelhead do not necessarily die after spawning -- they are iteroparous (capable of spawning multiple times), though in practice most do die after their first spawning run due to the physical demands of the migration. Some Steelhead survive to spawn two or even three times. This iteroparous potential distinguishes Steelhead (and some other trout) from the semelparous Pacific salmon (Chinook, Coho, Sockeye, Pink, Chum).

(Sources: *O. mykiss* life-history variation; Rainbow Trout - Steelhead relationship; smoltification; ocean growth; iteroparous vs. semelparous; spawning-and-return biology)

## WINTER AND SUMMER STEELHEAD

### The Two Run Types of Washington State

Washington State Steelhead occur in two ecologically distinct run types:

- **Winter Steelhead.** Enter rivers November-April (peak January-March in most systems). Spawn February-May. Winter Steelhead are the dominant run type in most Washington coastal rivers -- the rivers draining directly to the Pacific Ocean on the Olympic Peninsula, and the major Puget Sound rivers. Winter fish are typically smaller than summer fish (2-5 kg average) and are physiologically closer to spawning condition when they enter the river. They hold in the river for a shorter period before spawning.
- **Summer Steelhead.** Enter rivers May-October (sometimes earlier). Hold in the river through summer and into fall or winter before spawning in spring. Summer Steelhead are found primarily in larger interior rivers -- the Columbia River tributaries, including the Snake River. The extended holding period in the river means summer Steelhead experience summer water temperatures; warming rivers from climate change increasingly stress holding summer fish.
- **Why winter steelhead is documented here.** This degree documents winter Steelhead because winter is the primary Steelhead season in Puget Sound rivers and coastal Washington rivers -- the rivers most directly relevant to the engine's geographic focus. Winter Steelhead returning to the Snoqualmie, Snohomish, Skykomish, and other Puget Sound tributaries are the characteristic Steelhead of the engine's home watershed.

(Sources: Winter Steelhead run timing Washington; Summer Steelhead ecology; Columbia River tributaries; winter vs. summer run distinctions; climate change effects on summer holding fish)

## THE SOUND -- METALLIC SPLASH CLEAN JUMP, E=5

### What Makes the Steelhead Leap Different

Steelhead leaping is acoustically distinct from the species documented in previous Salmonidae entries:

- **Frequency of leaping.** Steelhead leap more frequently and more acrobatically than most other Pacific salmon. They jump at waterfalls (as Chinook do), but they also jump in open river -- apparently to dislodge sea lice or parasites, or for reasons not fully understood. The Steelhead's acrobatic jumping is one of its most distinctive behaviors and one of the reasons it is so highly prized by sport anglers (a hooked Steelhead will leap repeatedly, creating a spectacular fight).
- **The body shape and its acoustic consequence.** Steelhead are more slender, more torpedo-shaped than Chinook. A Steelhead at 3-4 kg has a higher length-to-mass ratio than a Chinook at the same weight. This body shape affects the jump: Steelhead enter the water more cleanly, at a steeper angle, with less lateral splash than the broad-shouldered Chinook. The impact sound is brighter (higher frequencies) and shorter in duration -- a "crack" rather than a "boom."
- **The metallic quality.** The description "metallic splash clean jump" captures the acoustic character: the Steelhead's jump produces a bright, clean, high-frequency-dominant splash that dissipates quickly. The fish's fresh-from-ocean coloration -- metallic steel-grey, bright silver -- has an acoustic parallel. The visual and sonic register are aligned: metallic in color, metallic in sound.
- **E=5 assessment.** Steelhead leaping is E=5 -- the clean middle. Not the heavy impact of a Chinook body-slap (E=6), not the near-silence of the static Bull Trout (E=2 at degree 240). The Steelhead jump is present and distinctive but not sustained or massive. A single Steelhead leap is a brief, bright acoustic event. At holding pools during winter runs, multiple fish may be present and several jumps may occur per hour -- maintaining the E=5 level of acoustic presence.

## STEELHEAD ECOLOGY -- THE RIVER AND THE OCEAN

### A Life Between Two Worlds

The Steelhead's double life -- freshwater birth, ocean growth, freshwater return -- creates a unique ecological position:

- **Habitat use.** Steelhead juveniles (parr and smolts) are stream fish: they use the same small-to-medium river habitat that Coho use. The habitat overlap between Coho (238) and Steelhead (239) is significant -- restoration of small streams benefits both species simultaneously. Juvenile Steelhead rear in streams for 1-3 years, feeding on invertebrates, before smoltifying and migrating to the ocean.
- **The holding fish.** Unlike Pacific salmon, which move aggressively toward spawning grounds immediately upon entering freshwater, winter Steelhead often hold in deep, cool pools for weeks or months before moving to spawn. The holding fish is a specific phase of the Steelhead's river presence: a large, silver fish in a deep pool, not actively swimming, conserving energy. This holding behavior is what makes winter Steelhead available to anglers for an extended season.
- **Ocean feeding.** Steelhead in the ocean eat a diverse prey base: copepods, amphipods, squid, herring, and other small fish. Their diet is similar to Chinook but less specialized. Steelhead growth at sea is rapid; the quality of the ocean food web in any given year affects how large returning fish will be.
- **Iteroparous potential.** Steelhead that survive spawning can return to the ocean and come back again the next year. These repeat-spawning fish are "half-pounders" or "kelts" -- emaciated after spawning, heading back to the ocean for recovery. A kelt returning to the ocean after spawning is in terrible physical condition; those that survive the ocean to return again are in exceptional condition and typically very large. Repeat spawners are rare in most systems but ecologically and genetically significant.

(Sources: Steelhead juvenile rearing habitat; holding behavior in pools; ocean prey base; iteroparous biology; kelt survival and return; habitat overlap with Coho)

## ESA LISTINGS -- THE STEELHEAD IN CRISIS

### Multiple Evolutionarily Significant Units Listed

Steelhead face conservation pressures similar to those documented for Pacific salmon, and multiple ESUs are listed under the ESA:

- **Puget Sound Steelhead ESU -- THREATENED.** The Steelhead of Puget Sound rivers are listed as Threatened under the ESA. Puget Sound Steelhead have declined significantly from historical abundance, driven by habitat loss, hatchery effects, and water temperature increases.
- **Snake River basin Steelhead ESUs -- THREATENED.** Snake River summer Steelhead (Threatened) and Snake River B-run Steelhead face the same dam-related pressures as Snake River salmon. The four lower Snake River dams impede Steelhead passage and have significantly reduced Snake River Steelhead abundance.
- **Middle Columbia River Steelhead -- THREATENED.** Steelhead in the middle Columbia River tributaries (Klickitat, Deschutes, John Day) are listed as Threatened.
- **Upper Columbia River Steelhead -- THREATENED.** The upper Columbia River Steelhead face extreme pressure from dams and thermal warming.
- **Olympic Peninsula Steelhead.** Wild winter Steelhead of Olympic Peninsula rivers (Hoh, Quillayute, Bogachiel, Clearwater, Sol Duc) are in serious decline. The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife has issued emergency rules closing or severely restricting Steelhead fishing on multiple Olympic Peninsula rivers due to critically low wild returns. The Olympic Peninsula wild winter Steelhead situation is one of the most urgent salmon/steelhead conservation crises in Washington State.
- **The hatchery problem.** Steelhead hatcheries have been used extensively in Washington and Oregon to compensate for habitat loss and provide angling opportunity. Hatchery Steelhead interbreed with wild Steelhead, reducing the wild population's genetic diversity and local adaptation. The tension between hatchery fisheries (which support recreational fishing economies) and wild Steelhead conservation is one of the most contentious management debates in Pacific Northwest salmon management.

(Sources: Puget Sound Steelhead ESU; Snake River Steelhead ESUs; Middle Columbia Steelhead; Olympic Peninsula wild winter Steelhead crisis; hatchery genetic impacts; WDFW emergency rules)

## STEELHEAD ANGLING -- THE MOST SOUGHT-AFTER FISH IN THE NORTHWEST

### The "Undisputed King of Freshwater Game Fish"

- **The angling tradition.** Steelhead are widely considered the most prized freshwater game fish in North America by dedicated anglers. The combination of their size (2-8+ kg), their acrobatic fighting behavior (repeated leaps during a fight), their challenging habitat (winter rivers in remote locations), and their relative scarcity makes a wild Steelhead catch one of the most coveted experiences in freshwater fishing.
- **The winter river aesthetic.** Steelhead fishing is a winter sport: standing in a cold river in January, casting a long line, waiting for the take that may not come. The aesthetic of winter Steelhead fishing -- the cold, the remote river, the long hours, the rare reward -- has produced a significant body of angling literature and has shaped how Pacific Northwest anglers relate to rivers.
- **Fly fishing tradition.** The Deschutes River, Hoh River, Sol Duc, Skagit, and other Pacific Northwest rivers have well-established fly fishing traditions for Steelhead. Spey casting (a two-handed rod technique developed for Atlantic salmon fishing in Scotland) is widely practiced for Steelhead in Washington rivers.
- **Conservation impact.** The angling tradition around wild Steelhead has produced significant conservation advocacy: anglers who value wild Steelhead have been among the strongest voices for wild fish protection, hatchery reform, and dam removal. Coastal Conservation Association, Native Fish Society, and Wild Steelheaders United represent anglers who have made the conservation of wild Steelhead a priority.

(Sources: Steelhead angling tradition; winter river aesthetic; fly fishing for Steelhead; Spey casting; conservation advocacy by anglers)
