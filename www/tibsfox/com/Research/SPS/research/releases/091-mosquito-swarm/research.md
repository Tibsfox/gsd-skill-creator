## TAXONOMY: Culicidae -- NEW FAMILY (41st), NEW ORDER: Diptera

### Family-Level Entry

This is a **family-level entry** representing the mosquito swarm sound [1, 2]. Multiple Culicidae genera in the PNW (Aedes, Culex, Anopheles) produce similar wingbeat frequencies. The swarm as a collective phenomenon is the entry's contribution.

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Arthropoda
- **Class:** **Insecta** (returns after amphibian interlude at degree 90)
- **Order:** **Diptera** -- NEW ORDER (true flies, mosquitoes, gnats). Fourth insect order.
- **Family:** **Culicidae** -- NEW FAMILY (41st in engine). First Diptera family.
- **Common name:** Mosquito swarm (collective) [1, 2].

### Sound Production Mechanism

The mosquito's sound is a **mechanical byproduct of flight** [1, 2]:

- **No specialized sound organ.** Unlike crickets (stridulation), cicadas (tymbal), or frogs (vocal sacs), mosquitoes have no organ designed to produce sound.
- **Wing vibration.** Wings beat 300-600 times per second (females) or 600-800 times per second (males), producing an audible frequency.
- **Frequency matching.** During courtship, males and females modulate their wingbeat frequencies to converge, creating acoustic "duets" -- the mechanical sound has been co-opted for communication.

This makes the mosquito the **first entry where sound is a byproduct of locomotion**, not a dedicated signal.

## THE SOUND: ULTRA-THIN HUM CLOUD -- 92nd ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Sound

The mosquito swarm produces a **high-pitched, thin, persistent whine** [1, 2]:

**Individual sound:** A single mosquito in flight produces a thin tone at approximately 400-600 Hz (female) or 600-800 Hz (male). The sound is barely audible beyond a few meters. Thin, reedy, high-pitched -- the characteristic whine near the ear.

**Frequency:** 400-600 Hz (female wingbeat). Higher than the cicada's buzzing throb (degree 89) but much thinner and quieter.

**The swarm:** When hundreds or thousands of mosquitoes hover together (typically males forming mating columns near water), the individual wingbeat tones merge into a **diffuse, barely-there hum cloud**. Not a focused beam of sound but a spread-out, ambient presence at the very threshold of perception.

### Why E=2

1. **Individual mosquitoes are nearly inaudible** beyond a few meters.
2. **Swarm sound is diffuse and unfocused** -- it spreads rather than projects.
3. **The hum exists at the threshold of perception** -- you sense it more than hear it.
4. **E=2 is the quietest energy level** the species engine has used. The mosquito swarm is the quietest sound catalogued.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Bell-like peep chorus** (degree 90, spring peeper-like chorus) -- crystalline, clear, E=4. The ultra-thin hum is the opposite: diffuse, unclear, E=2. The engine drops from maximum chorus clarity to minimum hum.
- **Electric buzzing throb** (degree 89, cicada) -- loud, harsh, electric. The ultra-thin hum is quiet, thin, barely there. Both insect-produced but completely different in character.
- **Rhythmic chirp** (degree 87, cricket) -- rhythmic, structured. The ultra-thin hum is continuous and unstructured.

The 92nd acoustic element.

## ECOLOGY: THE WETLAND HUM

### Habitat

Wetland [1, 2]:

- **Primary:** Standing water, marshes, swamps, wet meadows, pond edges.
- **Larvae:** Aquatic. Develop in standing water.
- **Adults:** Aerial. Swarm near water sources.
- **Connection to muskrat (85):** Muskrat inhabits marshes. Mosquitoes breed in marshes. Overlapping habitat.
- **Connection to spring peeper (90):** Both in wet habitats near standing water. Both crepuscular/nocturnal.

### Behavior

- **Mating swarms:** Males form hovering columns near water at dawn and dusk. Females enter the swarm. Wingbeat frequency matching is part of mate selection [1, 2].
- **Crepuscular activity:** Most active at dawn and dusk. Reduced activity in bright daylight.
- **Blood-feeding:** Females of most species require blood meals for egg production. Males feed on nectar.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Adult females -- blood (from mammals, birds, reptiles). Adult males -- nectar. Larvae -- aquatic microorganisms.
- **Predators:** Dragonflies, bats, birds, spiders, fish (larvae), frogs. The mosquito is prey for many of the engine's prior entries.
