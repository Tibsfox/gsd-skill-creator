## THE CUB -- FIRST SUMMER

### Born in the Den

Black bear cubs enter the world in darkness:

- **Birth.** Cubs are born in the winter den, January-February, while the sow is still in hibernation. Typical litter: 2-3 cubs. Birth weight: 200-400 grams (less than 1 pound). Tiny, blind, nearly hairless.
- **Den months.** Cubs nurse in the den for 2-3 months, growing rapidly on the sow's rich milk (20-25% fat content). By the time the family emerges in April-May, cubs weigh 2-4 kg (5-10 lbs).
- **First summer.** The first summer is the learning period. Cubs follow the sow constantly, learning what to eat, where to forage, how to climb trees (their primary escape behavior), and when to be afraid. The cub distress call is the vocal tether that keeps the family together during this critical period.
- **Weaning.** Cubs nurse through the first summer and are typically weaned by fall. They den with the sow for their second winter and separate in their second spring (age ~16 months).

## THE DISTRESS CALL -- MECHANISM AND FUNCTION

### The Sound of Need

The cub distress call is the most emotionally charged sound in the bear trio:

- **The mew.** A high, thin, sustained mewling -- "meeew, meeew" -- pitched at 300-800 Hz. Higher than any adult bear vocalization. The pitch is produced by the cub's small vocal cords and small body cavity. As the cub grows through summer, the pitch gradually drops.
- **Repetition.** The distress call is repeated insistently -- every 2-5 seconds when the cub is anxious. The repetition rate increases with distress level. A slightly worried cub mews occasionally. A panicked cub mews continuously.
- **Function.** The call serves one primary function: to bring the sow back. It is a contact call, a location beacon, and a plea for help simultaneously. The sow responds to the distress call with urgency -- often crashing through brush to reach the cub. This response is why approaching a bear cub is the most dangerous bear encounter: the sow is coming.
- **Tree-climbing context.** When a sow perceives danger, she sends cubs up a tree by huffing or woofing. The cubs climb. If a cub is too young or too frightened to climb, it may mew in distress from the base of the tree. The sow then positions herself between the threat and the cub.
- **Human-cub encounters.** A mewing bear cub encountered on a trail means the sow is nearby. This is the highest-risk bear encounter for hikers. Leave immediately. Do not approach the cub. The sow will respond to the cub's distress with defensive aggression.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Black Bear Cub

**Cub Distress Call (Separation/Fear):**
- **Frequency:** 300-800 Hz. High for a bear. Higher than any adult vocalization.
- **Character:** Thin, mewling, sustained. "Meeew, meeew." Not a roar or a growl -- a plea. The timbre is thin because of the cub's small body and vocal cords.
- **Duration:** 0.5-2.0 seconds per mew. Repeated every 2-5 seconds. Continuous when panicked.
- **Context:** Separation from sow. Fear. Tree-climbing failure. Close to sow (within 50-150 meters).
- **Carry distance:** 50-150 meters in forest. Sufficient for maternal response. Not designed for long-range.

**Cub Contentment (Nursing/Following):**
- **Frequency:** 100-300 Hz. Lower humming.
- **Character:** Soft, rhythmic humming while nursing. Barely audible beyond a few meters. The sound of safety.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. High, thin, insistent. The sound of infant vulnerability. More vocal than deer fawn (E=3). The emotional inverse of adult intimidation.
