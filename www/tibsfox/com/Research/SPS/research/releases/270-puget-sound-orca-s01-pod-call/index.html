Endangered — ~73 Individuals

The Southern Resident Killer Whale was listed as ENDANGERED under the US Endangered Species Act in 2005. Current population: approximately 73–75 individuals in three pods (J, K, L). The population has not shown sustained recovery under the ESA listing.

Primary threats: Chinook salmon decline (the Southern Residents are obligate Chinook eaters), acoustic disturbance from boat traffic (forces louder vocalizations, interferes with echolocation), and persistent organic pollutants (PCBs concentrated through the salmon they eat). The salmon connection cross-references to engine degrees 236-242.

The Acoustic Dialect

Each Southern Resident pod has a culturally transmitted acoustic repertoire — a set of call types that are learned, not genetically encoded. Calves learn their pod’s calls from their mothers and other pod members. The calls are cultural in the strict biological sense: they are produced by learning, vary among groups, and are maintained by social transmission.

John Ford’s landmark 1989 research established that killer whale pods have distinct acoustic dialects — one of the clearest demonstrations of animal culture in biology. The Southern Resident discrete calls have characteristic frequency contours (pitch change over time), characteristic durations, and characteristic rhythms. Each call type has an acoustic fingerprint recognizable from spectrographic analysis.

Vocalization types: discrete calls (stereotyped, pod-specific, social coordination), whistles (tonally pure, short-range social communication), clicks (echolocation pulses, navigation and prey detection). The S01 pod call refers to the repertoire of discrete calls characteristic of the Southern Resident community — the acoustic identity of the pods.

J, K, and L Pods

The Southern Resident community is structured into three matrilineal pods:

  • J Pod: The smallest and most studied. Approximately 23-25 individuals. Most consistently observed in Puget Sound and the Strait of Juan de Fuca.
  • K Pod: Approximately 18-20 individuals. More offshore-ranging than J Pod. Less frequently observed in inland waters.
  • L Pod: The largest. Approximately 32-35 individuals. Most diverse ranging; frequently in offshore waters.

When pods come together (called a "superpod"), the acoustic environment becomes extraordinarily rich: all three pods’ repertoires overlapping in the water simultaneously. Superpods are celebratory behavioral events -- associated with socializing, play, and high-energy surface behavior.

Puget Sound -- The Orca’s Ocean

Puget Sound is an inland sea: enclosed, deep, tidal, approximately 100 miles long. The Southern Residents historically used all of Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Salish Sea as their core range, following Chinook salmon through the seasons. The summer concentration area has been the waters between the San Juan Islands and the Fraser River (Canada), where Chinook returning to spawn are intercepted.

The acoustic environment of Puget Sound has significantly degraded since the mid-20th century: commercial shipping, recreational boats, and naval operations all contribute to anthropogenic noise. The Southern Residents compensate with louder vocalizations (the Lombard effect) — an energy cost that reduces fitness. Boat noise is a threat documented by NOAA’s SRKW recovery plan.
