## SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALES -- THE POPULATION

### Biology, Taxonomy, Status

- **Taxonomy.** *Orcinus orca* (Linnaeus, 1758). Family Delphinidae. Order Cetacea. The killer whale is the largest member of the dolphin family -- not a true whale in the cetacean phylogenetic sense but historically called "whale" in the common name tradition.
- **The Southern Resident community.** The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) are a genetically distinct population of killer whales whose core range includes Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Salish Sea. They are one of three ecotypes of killer whales in the Pacific Northwest: the Southern Residents (salmon-dependent, resident), the Northern Residents (salmon-dependent, resident, but with a different range), and the Bigg's or transient killer whales (mammal-eating, different range and behavior).
- **Three pods.** The Southern Resident community is divided into three matrilineal pods: **J pod** (the smallest and most studied), **K pod**, and **L pod** (the largest). As of recent census data, the total Southern Resident population is approximately 73-75 individuals -- an endangered population at critically low numbers.
- **Endangered status.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale was listed as endangered under the US Endangered Species Act in 2005. The listing was based on population size (fewer than 100 individuals), habitat degradation, reduced prey availability (Chinook salmon decline), and acoustic disturbance from boat traffic. Recovery has been slow; the population has not shown sustained growth under the ESA listing.
- **Salmon dependence.** Southern Residents are obligate salmon eaters -- specifically, they are highly selective for Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*), which is the largest and most energy-rich Pacific salmon species. The decline of Chinook salmon populations (cross-referenced to engine degrees 236-242, the salmon arc) is the primary driver of Southern Resident nutritional stress and declining reproductive success.

(Sources: NOAA SRKW Status Review; Endangered Species Act listing 2005; J/K/L pod population data; Southern Resident salmon dependence; Chinook salmon Oncorhynchus tshawytscha; orca taxonomy Delphinidae)

## THE S01 POD CALL DIALECT

### Acoustic Repertoire and Community Identity

The "S01 pod" designation refers to a specific research context within the acoustic study of the Southern Residents:

- **Pod-specific acoustic repertoires.** Each Southern Resident pod has a specific, learned acoustic repertoire -- a set of call types that are distinct from those of other pods. The acoustic repertoire is culturally transmitted: calves learn their pod's calls from their mothers and other pod members. The calls are not genetically encoded; they are learned, which makes them cultural in the biological sense.
- **Call types.** Southern Resident vocalizations include: **discrete calls** (stereotyped, pod-specific call types used for social coordination), **variable calls** (more flexible vocalizations), **whistles** (tonally pure signals used at close range for social communication), and **clicks** (echolocation pulses used for navigation and prey detection). The "S01 pod call" refers to the repertoire of discrete calls specific to the S designation within Southern Resident research.
- **Dialect structure.** The Southern Resident acoustic dialect is structured hierarchically: the entire SRKW community shares some calls; each pod has pod-specific calls that other pods do not produce; and within pods, there may be subgroup-specific variations. The dialect structure maps onto the social structure: family groups sharing more calls than non-family groups.
- **J.C. Ford's research.** John Ford's landmark research on killer whale dialects (published in 1989 and subsequent work) established that killer whale pods have culturally transmitted acoustic repertoires -- one of the clearest demonstrations of animal culture in the biological sense. The Southern Residents are among the best-documented examples of cetacean cultural transmission.
- **The S01 designation.** Research contexts sometimes use alphanumeric designations for call types (e.g., S1, S2, S3...) within the Southern Resident repertoire, or they use pod-specific call identifiers. "S01" in the context of the engine refers to the signature call repertoire characteristic of the Southern Resident community, referencing the acoustic distinctiveness of their pod-specific vocalizations.

(Sources: Ford 1989 killer whale dialect research; Southern Resident acoustic repertoire; discrete calls vs. whistles vs. clicks; cultural transmission in cetaceans; SRKW vocal learning; orca dialect structure)

## THE ACOUSTIC CHARACTERISTICS

### What the S01 Pod Sounds Like

- **Discrete calls.** The stereotyped discrete calls of the Southern Residents are the most acoustically distinctive feature of their repertoire. Each call type has a characteristic frequency contour (the pattern of pitch change over time), a characteristic duration, and a characteristic rhythm. The calls can be recognized by trained researchers from spectrographic analysis -- each call type has a "fingerprint."
- **Frequency range.** Southern Resident vocalizations span approximately 500 Hz to 25,000 Hz, with most energy concentrated between 1,000 Hz and 20,000 Hz. The high-frequency range of their echolocation clicks extends well above 20,000 Hz.
- **Amplitude.** E=8 -- maximum energy for SPS. The calls of a pod of Southern Residents are acoustically overwhelming in proximity: loud, complex, permeating. Underwater, the calls carry for kilometers. Above water, the surface slap and the audible components of vocalizations are remarkable. Hydrophone recordings of pod vocalizations capture a soundscape of extraordinary density and complexity.
- **Social context.** Southern Resident calls are primarily social: they coordinate group movement, maintain cohesion during foraging, reinforce social bonds, and identify pod membership. The discrete calls that are most pod-specific are used most frequently when pods are foraging or resting together.
- **Foraging calls vs. resting calls.** Southern Residents are more vocally active when foraging (catching salmon) than when traveling or resting. The vocalization rate drops dramatically when they are in travel mode -- a contrast to the transient orca documented at degree 272, which uses tactical silence during hunting approaches.

(Sources: SRKW acoustic frequency range; discrete call spectrographic characteristics; amplitude of orca vocalizations; social call function; foraging vs. resting vocalization rate)

## PUGET SOUND AND THE SALISH SEA

### The Orca's Ocean

- **The inland sea.** Puget Sound is an arm of the Pacific Ocean extending approximately 100 miles south from the Strait of Juan de Fuca. It is an inland sea -- enclosed, deep, influenced by tidal flow from the Strait. The Southern Residents historically used all of Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Canadian Salish Sea (including the Strait of Georgia) as their core range.
- **Acoustic environment.** Puget Sound's acoustic environment is complex: commercial shipping, recreational boat traffic, naval operations, and industrial activity all contribute to anthropogenic noise. The acoustic environment for the Southern Residents has significantly degraded since the mid-20th century. Increased boat noise forces the Southern Residents to vocalize more loudly (the Lombard effect -- the same acoustic adjustment that humans make in noisy environments) and may interfere with echolocation effectiveness.
- **Chinook salmon distribution.** The Southern Residents follow the distribution of Chinook salmon. The major summer concentration area has historically been the waters between the San Juan Islands and the Fraser River (Canada), where Chinook salmon returning to spawn are intercepted. The Southern Residents are salmon-following animals: their spatial distribution tracks the salmon distribution.
- **Critical habitat.** Critical habitat for the Southern Resident Killer Whale under the ESA includes: the inland waters of Washington State (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca), the offshore waters of Washington, Oregon, and California (winter range), and the Columbia River estuary (where significant Chinook salmon production occurs). The engine documents the inland sea portion of the critical habitat.

(Sources: Puget Sound geography; Salish Sea description; SRKW critical habitat ESA designation; acoustic environment Puget Sound; Lombard effect in orcas; Chinook salmon distribution SRKW foraging)
