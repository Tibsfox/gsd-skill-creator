## THE MUSKRAT: THE MARSH SWIMMER

The Muskrat is a semi-aquatic rodent of freshwater marshes, wetlands, and ponds -- the most common and widespread semi-aquatic rodent in North America. At 700-1,800 grams (1.5-4 lbs), it is the **largest Cricetidae** in the engine and significantly larger than both the Deer Mouse (10-30 g, degree 83) and Bushy-tailed Woodrat (200-600 g, degree 84). It inhabits marshes throughout North America, including the Puget Sound lowlands [1, 2, 3].

This is the engine's **THIRD CONSECUTIVE CRICETIDAE** (83-84-85). The Deer Mouse (83) broke the Sciuridae octet (75-82). The Woodrat (84) continued. The Muskrat extends the run to three. Three consecutive Cricetidae, three radically different habitats: varied (83), rocky outcrop (84), marsh (85).

The Muskrat is the engine's **first marsh species**. Aquatic habitat **RETURNS** after the alpine/forest mammal era.

Despite its name, the Muskrat is **NOT a rat** -- it is Cricetidae (New World mice/voles), not Muridae (Old World rats/mice). The "rat" in "muskrat" is a misnomer.

And this is the **twenty-fifth consecutive original CSV species**.

## TAXONOMY: *Ondatra zibethicus*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Ondatra** -- from the Huron/Wyandot word for the animal. An Indigenous name preserved in scientific nomenclature [1, 2].

- **zibethicus** -- from Latin "musky," referring to the musky scent produced by perineal glands during the breeding season. The "musk" in "muskrat" refers to this scent [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Muskrat (standard). Musquash (historical, from Algonquian). Despite the name, NOT a rat (Cricetidae, not Muridae). Described by Linnaeus (1766) [1, 2].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (twenty-fourth mammalian entry; twenty-second mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- TWELFTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Cricetidae -- THIRD CONSECUTIVE (83-84-85). Cricetidae streak at three.
- **Genus:** *Ondatra* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Monotypic (one species). Third Cricetidae genus after *Peromyscus* (83) and *Neotoma* (84).
- **Species:** *O. zibethicus* -- the Muskrat. Semi-aquatic. Marshes and wetlands [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE SEMI-AQUATIC RODENT

### Body Form

The Muskrat is distinctively adapted for semi-aquatic life [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 40-65 cm (16-26 inches) total including tail.
- **Weight:** 700-1,800 g (1.5-4 lbs). The largest Cricetidae in the engine. Roughly 3-9x the mass of the Woodrat.
- **Tail:** LATERALLY FLATTENED -- compressed side-to-side, nearly hairless, scaly. Used as a rudder while swimming. The key diagnostic feature. Not round like a rat tail. Not dorsoventrally flat like a beaver tail.
- **Hind feet:** PARTIALLY WEBBED -- adapted for swimming. Fringed with stiff hairs for additional propulsion.
- **Ears:** Small, nearly hidden in fur. Reduced for swimming.

### Pelage

- **Body:** Dense, dark brown to reddish-brown. Thick waterproof underfur with longer guard hairs. The fur is commercially valuable.
- **Belly:** Lighter, grayish.
- **Adaptations:** Can close lips behind the incisors to gnaw underwater. Dense waterproof underfur for insulation.

## THE SOUND: QUIET PLOP INTO WATER -- 86th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Muskrat Enters the Water

The Muskrat's primary acoustic contribution is the **quiet plop into water** -- the sound of the animal sliding or dropping from a bank into water [1, 2, 3]:

**The plop:** A NON-VOCAL sound -- not a call or vocalization, but a body-water interaction sound. A soft, concussive splash as the muskrat's body breaks the water surface. Often the first indication of muskrat presence. You hear the plop before you see the muskrat. The plop is followed by a brief ripple and then silence as the muskrat submerges.

**Secondary sounds:** Muskrats also produce quiet squeaks and chatters during social interactions, but the plop is the signature sound -- the sound by which the animal is most commonly detected.

### Why E=3

1. **The plop is audible at moderate range** -- 10-20 meters in quiet marsh conditions.
2. **It is a distinctive sound** -- recognizable once learned. Different from a thrown rock (harder, sharper) or a diving bird (splash + wing sounds).
3. **Dawn and dusk marshes are quiet** -- the plop carries because the ambient soundscape is low.
4. **E=3 reflects clearly audible but not loud** -- a personal-range sound, not a landscape-scale call.

### The Non-Vocal Acoustic Element

The quiet plop is the engine's first **non-vocal mammalian acoustic element**. Previous non-vocal sounds include Beaver chewing (62) and Woodrat gnaw (84), but these are activity sounds. The plop is a **locomotion sound** -- the transition from land to water. The acoustic signature of the semi-aquatic lifestyle.

## ECOLOGY: THE MARSH BUILDER

### Lodge Building: Beaver Parallel

The Muskrat builds **lodges** from cattails, bulrushes, and mud [1, 2, 3]:

- **Construction:** Dome-shaped structures built in shallow water. Cattails and bulrushes piled and woven, cemented with mud. Interior chamber above water level.
- **Size:** Smaller than beaver lodges -- typically 1-2 meters across. Beaver lodges can exceed 3 meters.
- **Also:** Digs bank burrows in stream and pond banks. Dual housing strategy.
- **Parallel:** Lodge builder parallel with **American Beaver** (SPS-061). Both semi-aquatic rodents that build structures in water. Different materials (cattails vs. sticks), different scales, same principle: construct a dry interior space surrounded by water for protection.

### Habitat: First Marsh Species

The Muskrat is the engine's **first marsh species** [1, 2, 3]:

- **Freshwater marshes:** Cattail and bulrush stands.
- **Wetlands:** Swamps, bogs, wet meadows.
- **Ponds and lakes:** Shallow edges with emergent vegetation.
- **Slow-moving streams:** With vegetated banks.
- **Aquatic returns:** After the alpine/forest mammal era (mountain goat, marmots, squirrels, mice, woodrats), the engine returns to aquatic habitat. Marsh shared with Wood Duck (58) and Mallard (59).

### Diet: Aquatic Herbivore

Primarily herbivorous. Cattails, bulrushes, sedges, water lilies, aquatic vegetation. Occasionally eats mussels, crayfish, small fish. Can forage underwater for extended periods [1, 2].

### Predators

One of the most preyed-upon mammals in North America. Mink, foxes, coyotes, raccoons, raptors (especially Great Horned Owl), snapping turtles. The muskrat's abundance makes it a keystone prey species [1, 2, 3].
