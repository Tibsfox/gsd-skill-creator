Coyote Pup Yipping

Canis latrans (Canidae) — E=4 — Den Chorus
Second Coyote entry. Adult-pup pair (208–209). High, thin “yip-yip-yip” at 800–2,500 Hz. A litter of pups at the den entrance, learning to speak coyote. E=4 — enthusiastic small voices. Canidae quartet begins (208–211). Second Canidae.

The Den — Nursery of the Canidae

Born underground in April, blind and deaf, 250–300 g. Emerging at 2–3 weeks. Eyes open at 10–14 days. The den entrance becomes the play area and social arena. Both parents provision. Helpers from previous litters bring food. The den is a provisioned nursery — warm center of the family.

Adult-Pup Pair — 208-209

208: Adult howl (500–1,500 Hz, E=5). Territory, pack cohesion. 209: Pup yipping (800–2,500 Hz, E=4). Play, practice, sibling bonding. The howl communicates. The yip rehearses. −1 energy. The infant step confirmed across four families.

Canidae Quartet — 208-211

208: Coyote howl (E=5). 209: Coyote pup yipping (E=4). 210: Gray Wolf howl (E=7). 211: Red Fox bark (E=4). Four canid voices across four degrees. The predator sequence: coyote → wolf → fox. From the den to the wilderness to the varied landscape.

Den as Provisioned Nursery

Advanced

Underground birth, hillside burrow, both parents provision, helpers contribute. The den is the warm center of the canid family.

V

Vocal Development — Yip to Howl

Intermediate

Pup yips lengthen and deepen over 8–16 weeks. The transition tracks physical growth. By fall, juveniles produce recognizable howls.

Cervidae, Ursidae, Bovidae, Canidae. The infant is always −1 energy. The pattern is now confirmed across four mammalian families.
