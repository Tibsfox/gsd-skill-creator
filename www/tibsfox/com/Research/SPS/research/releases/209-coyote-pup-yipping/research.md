- **Frequency shift.** The pup's yipping is higher (800-2,500 Hz) than the adult howl (500-1,500 Hz). Smaller body, smaller vocal cords, higher pitch. The same infant frequency shift seen across species.
- **Energy drop.** The pup (E=4) is one step below the adult (E=5). The -1 infant step continues -- matching the fawn/buck (195-196), cub/adult (202/200), and kid/adult (204/203) patterns.
- **Function shift.** The adult howl is functional: territory, pack cohesion, mate location. The pup yipping is developmental: learning to vocalize, establishing sibling hierarchy, practicing the sounds they'll use as adults. The howl communicates. The yip rehearses.
- **Group vs. solo.** The adult howl is often solo or duet. The pup yipping is always a group -- a litter of 4-7 pups yipping together, creating a chaotic chorus. The group yip is more complex acoustically but less directed.

## THE DEN -- NURSERY OF THE CANIDAE

### Born Underground

Coyote pups enter the world in a den:

- **Den structure.** Coyotes dig their own dens or appropriate existing burrows (often from badgers or ground squirrels). The den is typically on a hillside with good drainage, offering both shelter and surveillance. The entrance faces downslope for visibility.
- **Birth timing.** Pups are born in April-May, blind and deaf, weighing 250-300 grams. A typical litter is 4-7 pups. The female (alpha or sole breeding female in the group) stays in the den for the first 2-3 weeks.
- **Emergence.** At 2-3 weeks, pups emerge from the den for the first time. Eyes open at 10-14 days. Ears stand up at 3-4 weeks. The transition from underground to above-ground is gradual -- pups initially stay within a few meters of the den entrance.
- **Yipping onset.** Pup yipping begins in earnest once pups emerge and begin interacting with siblings outside the den. The den entrance becomes a play area and social arena. Yipping peaks at 4-8 weeks as pups become more mobile and boisterous.
- **Parental care.** Both parents provision the pups, regurgitating food. In years with helpers (yearling offspring from previous litters), the helper coyotes also bring food to the den. The den is a provisioned nursery -- a warm center of the family unit.

(Sources: Coyote den ecology; pup development; litter size; den emergence; parental care)

## THE PUP YIP -- LEARNING TO SPEAK COYOTE

### Developmental Vocalization

The pup's yip is not yet the adult howl -- it's practice:

- **The yip.** "Yip-yip-yip" -- short, high, staccato bursts. 800-2,500 Hz. Higher and thinner than the adult howl. Each yip is 0.1-0.3 seconds. Rapid-fire. Multiple pups yipping simultaneously creates a chaotic, overlapping chorus.
- **Vocal development.** The yip is the precursor to the adult howl. As pups mature (8-16 weeks), yips begin to lengthen and deepen. The transition from yip to howl tracks physical growth -- larger body, larger vocal cords, lower pitch, longer duration. By fall, juvenile coyotes can produce recognizable howls.
- **Social function.** Yipping during play establishes sibling hierarchy. Dominant pups yip louder and more frequently. Submissive pups may whine or yelp. The den-site play sessions are social training -- the yips are early rank negotiations.
- **The group effect.** A litter of pups yipping together produces a sound that seems like far more individuals than actually present. Coyote pup yipping has long confused observers into overestimating population numbers. The acoustic illusion is a byproduct of overlapping frequencies and timing.
- **Adult response.** Adults returning to the den are greeted by a frenzy of pup yipping -- excited, eager, food-directed. The yipping intensifies as the adult approaches. This is the sound of the family reunion at the den entrance.

(Sources: Coyote pup vocalization; vocal development; sibling hierarchy; group vocal effect; adult-pup interaction)

### Calibration

The pup yipping at E=4 is excited but contained:

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Coyote Pup Yipping

**Pup Group Yipping (Play/Social):**
- **Frequency:** 800-2,500 Hz. Higher than adult howl. Small vocal cords.
- **Character:** High, thin, staccato. "Yip-yip-yip." Rapid-fire bursts. Chaotic when overlapping.
- **Duration:** 0.1-0.3 seconds per yip. Bouts last 10-60 seconds. Repeated throughout play sessions.
- **Context:** Den emergence. Sibling play. Adult greeting. Social hierarchy establishment. Spring (April-June).
- **Carry distance:** 50-200 meters. Higher-pitched than adult howl so less far-carrying.

**Pup Distress (Separation):**
- **Frequency:** 1,000-3,000 Hz. Even higher and thinner.
- **Character:** Whine or yelp. Sustained, not staccato. Urgent tone.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. Enthusiastic group chorus. Small individual voices. The den-site sound. Carry moderate. The group effect creates the illusion of more animals than present.

(Sources: Coyote pup acoustics; developmental vocalization; group vocal illusion; engine internal analysis)
