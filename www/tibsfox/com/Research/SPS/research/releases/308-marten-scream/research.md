## PACIFIC MARTEN -- SPECIES PROFILE

### *Martes caurina* -- The Old-Growth Specialist

*Martes caurina* (Merriam, 1890) -- the Pacific Marten -- is a member of the family Mustelidae, subfamily Guloninae:

- **Taxonomy.** The Pacific Marten was until recently considered a subspecies of the American Marten (*Martes americana*). Genetic and morphological analysis completed in the early 2000s confirmed it as a distinct species: *Martes caurina*. The Pacific Marten is the West Coast species; the American Marten occurs across Canada and in the Rocky Mountains and Northern Appalachians. The distinction matters: *Martes caurina* is specifically adapted to the Pacific Coast old-growth forest ecosystem.
- **Size.** Small-to-medium mustelid: body length 32-45 cm, tail 17-23 cm. Males typically 0.7-1.4 kg; females 0.4-0.9 kg. A Pacific Marten is smaller than a large house cat. The disproportionate volume of the territorial scream -- the sound that calibrates to E=9 -- is produced by an animal that weighs less than 1.5 kg. This is the most extreme acoustic-to-mass ratio of any mammal in the SPS engine.
- **Appearance.** Dark brown to dark reddish-brown body with a yellowish-orange throat patch (the "bib") that is distinctive and visible in the field. Long, bushy tail. Semi-retractile claws adapted for tree climbing.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** Distributed throughout the Pacific Northwest in mature and old-growth coniferous forest: the Cascades, the Olympics, the Coast Ranges, the Klamath Mountains, and mountain ranges south into California. Requires large territories (typically 2-5 km² for females, larger for males) with complex structural characteristics: large-diameter trees, significant dead wood (snags and logs), dense understory. Avoids young plantation forests, which lack the structural complexity required.
- **Conservation status.** Least Concern overall (IUCN), but populations in the southern portion of the range (Sierra Nevada, southern Cascades) are considered sensitive or at risk. The species is old-growth dependent at the structural level; old-growth loss reduces population density and connectivity. The Pacific Marten is a sensitive indicator of old-growth forest quality.

(Sources: Martes caurina taxonomy; split from Martes americana; Merriam 1890; Guloninae; body size 32-45 cm, 0.7-1.4 kg males; yellow-orange throat bib; Pacific Northwest distribution: Cascades, Olympics, Coast Ranges, Klamath, Sierra Nevada; territory size 2-5 km² females; old-growth structural requirements; plantation avoidance; Least Concern but sensitive in south; old-growth indicator)

## THE TERRITORIAL SCREAM -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### What E=9 Sounds Like

The Pacific Marten's territorial scream is the most acoustically startling sound per unit body mass in the SPS engine:

- **The scream itself.** The Pacific Marten produces a sustained high-pitched vocalization that rises and holds -- not a brief call but a sustained cry. The sound is difficult to describe to someone who has not heard it: it is somewhere between a cat's scream at extreme distress, a high-pitched human yell, and something that has no human comparison. It begins high and gets higher. It lasts longer than seems possible for such a small animal.
- **The acoustic physics.** The Marten's scream is produced by a small, powerful vocal apparatus. The frequency is high: primary energy in the range of 1,500-8,000 Hz, well above the fundamental frequencies of most mammal vocalizations. The high frequency combined with sustained duration and high amplitude produces a sound that carries unexpectedly far through forest -- the high frequencies penetrate the forest understory differently than low-frequency mammal vocalizations.
- **The startle effect.** At night in old-growth forest, the Marten scream is one of the most effective acoustic startle events in the PNW. The complete absence of warning -- no footsteps, no snapping branches, just the sudden onset of an extreme high-pitched sustained scream -- combined with the unfamiliarity of the sound (most people cannot identify it) produces a visceral startle response. The sound is fundamentally alarming.
- **The territorial context.** The scream occurs in several contexts: male-male territorial dispute, mating season interactions, and defense against perceived intruders (including humans who enter marten territory at night). The territorial scream is the most sustained and highest-amplitude vocalization in the marten's repertoire.
- **E=9 calibration.** Maximum SPS energy. The E=9 calibration reflects: (1) the extreme acoustic intensity relative to the animal's size, (2) the sustained duration, (3) the high frequency that maximizes acoustic startlement, (4) the nocturnal context that maximizes the startle effect, and (5) the ecological significance of the sound as a territorial assertion in the Pacific Northwest's most structurally complex ecosystem (old-growth). No other individual mammal sound in the SPS engine combines these factors at the same level.

(Sources: Pacific Marten territorial scream description; frequency range 1,500-8,000 Hz; sustained duration; amplitude; startle effect; nocturnal onset without warning; territorial contexts; mating season; intruder defense; E=9 calibration rationale; acoustic-to-mass ratio)

## OLD-GROWTH ECOLOGY -- THE MARTEN'S ENVIRONMENT

### The Structure That Makes the Scream Possible

The Pacific Marten is an old-growth obligate at the structural level:

- **The old-growth requirement.** Pacific Marten territory quality is directly correlated with old-growth structural characteristics: large-diameter snags (for den sites), down logs (for subnivean travel in winter), complex multi-layered canopy (for arboreal travel and hunting), and diverse understory (for prey diversity). Without these structural elements, marten territory cannot be maintained at full carrying capacity.
- **The timber country irony.** Aberdeen is a logging town. The logging industry that built Aberdeen removed old-growth forest. The Pacific Marten requires old-growth forest. The timber economy that produced the Melvins' cultural context destroyed the habitat of the species paired with them. The Melvins-Marten equivalence includes this irony: the band from the logging town is paired with the species that logging destroys.
- **The Pileated Woodpecker connection.** The Pileated Woodpecker (SPS engine namesake species; degrees 54, 108, 300) is the Pacific Northwest's keystone cavity excavator -- it creates the large snag cavities that many species use, including the Pacific Marten. The Marten uses Pileated-excavated cavities as den sites. The engine's most significant recurring species (Pileated) creates the infrastructure that supports the engine's maximum energy species (Marten). The ecological connection is structural.
- **Night in old-growth.** The nocturnal environment that produces the Marten's territorial scream is the most acoustically complex in the Pacific Northwest: Northern Spotted Owl calls, Northern Flying Squirrel movements, Roosevelt Elk distant bulge, and periodically the Marten scream cutting through everything else. The scream is the loudest, highest, most startling sound in a complex nocturnal acoustic environment.
