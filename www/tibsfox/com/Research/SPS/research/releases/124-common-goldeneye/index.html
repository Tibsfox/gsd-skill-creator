Common Goldeneye

Bucephala clangula (Anatidae) — E=4 — 7th Consecutive Anatidae — “The Whistler”
rattling wing-whistle in flight — E=4. The “whistler” — a mechanical sound produced by narrow outer primary feathers, not by vocalization. Seventh consecutive Anatidae (118–124). Completes the comparison pair with Barrow’s Goldeneye (123): round spot vs. crescent. Completes the Bucephala trilogy (122–124). Holarctic distribution — 2.5–4.6 million globally. Clangula = “the resounding one.”

The Wing-Whistle — Mechanical Sound, Not Vocal

The Common Goldeneye’s most distinctive sound is not a vocalization. The rattling wing-whistle is produced by air flowing over the bird’s unusually narrow outer primary feathers during flight. Each wingbeat forces air across these stiff feathers, producing a high-pitched, rattling singing whistle at 1,000–4,000 Hz. The sound is continuous during flight, involuntary, and audible at 50–200 meters. Birders hear the whistler approaching before seeing it. The species name clangula (“the resounding one”) and the common nickname “the whistler” both refer to this mechanical sound.

The Round Spot vs. The Crescent — Common vs. Barrow’s

Completing the comparison pair with Barrow’s Goldeneye (degree 123): Common has a round white spot (not crescent), greenish head gloss (not purple), rounder head shape (not angular), and more extensive white scapulars. The acoustic contrast is equally distinct: Barrow’s has quiet courtship grunts (vocal, E=3, close-range); Common has the wing-whistle (mechanical, E=4, long-range). Two species, two sound-production strategies within one genus.

Cosmopolitan Diver — 2.5–4.6 Million Globally

Common Goldeneye breeds across the entire boreal zone of North America and Eurasia — one of the most widespread diving ducks in the world. Population: 2.5–4.6 million (vs. Barrow’s 150,000–200,000). Breeds in boreal forest from Alaska to Newfoundland and across northern Europe/Asia. Winters on large lakes, rivers, and coastal waters. Cavity nester that readily uses nest boxes. Least Concern status.

Seventh Consecutive Anatidae (118–124)

Seven consecutive Anatidae — one degree short of tying the Picidae nonet record (9 consecutive, 107–115). The Anatidae streak has covered extraordinary family diversity: stream specialist (Harlequin 118), fish-eating divers (mergansers 119–120), forest-pool cavity nester (Wood Duck 121), smallest diver (Bufflehead 122), mountain lake specialist (Barrow’s 123), and the cosmopolitan whistler (Common 124).
