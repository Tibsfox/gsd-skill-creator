## THE WING-WHISTLE: MECHANICAL SOUND, NOT VOCAL

### A Sound Produced by Feathers, Not Throat

The Common Goldeneye's most distinctive sound is not a vocalization at all. The **wing-whistle** is a mechanical sound produced by air flowing over the bird's narrow outer primary feathers during flight. This is one of the most distinctive sounds in PNW waterfowl ecology and the reason the Common Goldeneye is historically known as **"the whistler."**

The wing-whistle is produced by a specific anatomical feature:

- **The outer primaries** (the outermost flight feathers on the wing) are unusually narrow and stiff in Common Goldeneye compared to other ducks.
- **As the bird flies**, the rapid wingbeats force air across these narrow feathers, producing a **high-pitched, rattling whistle** with each wingbeat.
- **The sound is audible** at considerable distance -- a birder can hear a Common Goldeneye approaching in flight before seeing it, identified solely by the distinctive wing-whistle.
- **The sound is continuous** during flight, produced with every wingbeat. It is not a call that can be turned on or off -- it is an unavoidable byproduct of the wing structure during active flight.

This makes the Common Goldeneye's primary sound **unique in the engine**: it is the first species whose defining acoustic contribution is entirely **mechanical** rather than vocal. Other species produce mechanical sounds alongside vocalizations (the Pileated Woodpecker's drum at degree 109, the Northern Flicker's drum at degree 22), but those species also have prominent vocalizations. The Common Goldeneye's wing-whistle is the sound that defines the species, and it is produced by the physics of feather structure, not by any vocal organ.

### The "Whistler" Nickname

The Common Goldeneye has been called **"the whistler"** or **"whistler duck"** by hunters and naturalists for centuries. The nickname predates scientific nomenclature. In many North American hunting traditions, the sound of a whistler's wings approaching was the primary identification cue -- hunters heard the bird before they saw it.

The species name *clangula* comes from Latin *clangere* ("to resound, to clang") -- the scientific name itself refers to the sound. *Bucephala clangula*: the resounding ox-head. The entire naming apparatus -- common name "whistler" and specific epithet "clangula" -- points to the wing-whistle as the bird's defining characteristic.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Mallory & Lumsden 2020; Sibley 2014)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: THE RATTLING WING-WHISTLE

### Frequency and Character

The wing-whistle has distinctive acoustic properties:

- **Frequency range:** 1,000-4,000 Hz. The whistle occupies the upper-mid frequency range, above most duck vocalizations and waterfowl splash sounds.
- **Character:** A **rattling, singing whistle** -- not a pure tone but a modulated, pulsing sound synchronized with the wingbeats. Each downstroke produces a slightly different pitch than the upstroke, creating a characteristic rattling quality.
- **Rhythm:** The whistle pulses with the wingbeat frequency, approximately 8-10 wingbeats per second in cruising flight. The sound is rhythmic, rapid, and continuous.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate to loud. E=4 energy. The wing-whistle is audible at 50-200 meters in calm conditions. It carries well over water, where there are few obstructions and the flat surface reflects sound efficiently.
- **Spectral signature:** The whistle has a distinctive spectral shape -- a primary tone with overtones that shift slightly with each wingbeat. On a spectrogram, the wing-whistle appears as a dense band of energy in the 1,000-4,000 Hz range, pulsing at wingbeat frequency.

### Why E=4

The wing-whistle registers at E=4 because:

- **It is louder than the quiet courtship vocalizations** of most diving ducks (including Barrow's Goldeneye at E=3). The mechanical sound carries farther than the grunts and mews of goldeneye courtship.
- **It is continuous during flight** -- not a brief vocalization but a sustained sound for the duration of the flight. The cumulative acoustic presence is moderate.
- **It is not under voluntary control** -- the bird cannot modulate it. This gives it a **constant, ambient quality** rather than the dynamic variation of intentional vocalizations.
- **It does not reach E=5 or E=6** because the amplitude, while notable, is not overwhelming. The wing-whistle is distinctive and audible but not loud in the way that the Harlequin Duck's stream calls (E=5) or the Pileated Woodpecker's drum (E=6) are loud.

### Comparison with Barrow's Goldeneye Sound

The two goldeneye species at degrees 123-124 present contrasting acoustic profiles:

| **Primary sound** | Quiet grunts (courtship) | Rattling wing-whistle (flight) |

The comparison pair reveals two fundamentally different acoustic strategies within the same genus: Barrow's communicates through deliberate, quiet, close-range vocalizations tied to courtship; Common communicates (involuntarily) through continuous, audible mechanical sounds produced by flight. One is intimate and intentional; the other is ambient and structural.

## IDENTIFICATION: THE ROUND SPOT VS. THE CRESCENT

### Common vs. Barrow's -- The Mirror Comparison

The identification comparison from Common Goldeneye's perspective (mirroring degree 123's Barrow's perspective):

**Male breeding plumage:**

- **Common Goldeneye:** White **round spot** between the eye and the bill. The spot is circular, compact, and bright white. Head has a **greenish gloss** in good light. Back has **extensive white** scapular patches -- more white showing on the back than Barrow's.
- **Barrow's Goldeneye:** White **crescent-shaped** facial patch. Purplish head gloss. Less white on back.

**The round spot vs. the crescent** -- the mirror of degree 123's identification framework. From Common's side: look for the round, not the crescent. The green, not the purple. The rounder head, not the angular.

**Female identification:**

- **Common female:** Longer, more gradually tapering bill. Less extensive bill coloration. More gradual forehead slope -- the head appears more oval in profile.
- **Barrow's female:** Shorter, more triangular bill. More extensive yellow-orange on bill. Steeper forehead -- more angular head profile.

### Range and Habitat Differences

The range difference is the most reliable large-scale identification cue:

- **Common Goldeneye:** **Widespread** across North America and Eurasia. Breeds in boreal forest from Alaska to Newfoundland, and across northern Europe and Asia. Winters on large lakes, rivers, and coastal waters throughout temperate North America. The most widespread goldeneye globally.
- **Barrow's Goldeneye:** **Western North America specialty** with isolated populations in Iceland and eastern Canada. Concentrated in British Columbia, Alberta, and the northwestern US. Limited global range.

In the Puget Sound region, **both species winter together**, making range alone insufficient for identification. Direct comparison of facial patch shape, head gloss color, head shape, and bill structure is required.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE COSMOPOLITAN DIVER

### Habitat and Range

Common Goldeneye breeds in **boreal forest** across the entire Northern Hemisphere -- one of the most widespread diving ducks in the world. The breeding requirements are:

- **Freshwater lakes and rivers** in or adjacent to boreal and mixed forest.
- **Cavity nesting:** Like Barrow's Goldeneye and the Bufflehead, Common Goldeneye is a cavity nester. Large natural cavities and old woodpecker holes in trees near water. Also readily uses nest boxes, which has aided conservation and population management.
- **Boreal ecosystem:** The breeding range corresponds closely to the boreal forest biome -- birch, aspen, spruce, and pine forests of the north.

In winter, Common Goldeneye are found on:

- **Large freshwater lakes** -- the Great Lakes, major reservoirs, and deep river pools.
- **Coastal waters** -- bays, estuaries, and sheltered marine waters. In the PNW, Common Goldeneye winter on Puget Sound alongside Barrow's Goldeneye.
- **Rivers** -- open stretches of large rivers that remain ice-free.

### Diving Ecology

Common Goldeneye is an accomplished **diving duck** that forages by submerging completely:

- **Dive depth:** Typically 1-4 meters, occasionally deeper.
- **Diet:** Aquatic invertebrates (insect larvae, crustaceans, mollusks), supplemented by fish and fish eggs. In marine winter habitat, crustaceans and mollusks dominate.
- **Foraging technique:** Dives from the surface, propels underwater using feet. Average dive duration 15-25 seconds. Forages along the bottom, probing among rocks and substrate.

### Population and Conservation

The global Common Goldeneye population is estimated at **2.5-4.6 million** individuals -- vastly more abundant than Barrow's Goldeneye's 150,000-200,000. The species is classified as **Least Concern** by IUCN. The widespread breeding range, adaptability to nest boxes, and large population make it one of the more secure North American diving ducks.

The contrast with Barrow's Goldeneye is instructive: Common is cosmopolitan (millions, Least Concern), Barrow's is restricted (hundreds of thousands, Near Threatened in some assessments). Same genus, dramatically different conservation profiles.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Mallory & Lumsden 2020)

## THE BUCEPHALA TRILOGY COMPLETE

Degree 124 completes the **Bucephala trilogy** -- three consecutive entries from the genus *Bucephala*:

| Degree | Species | Niche | Range | Energy | Key Sound |
|--------|---------|-------|-------|--------|-----------|
| **122** | Bufflehead | Smallest diving duck | N. America | E=3 | Splashing takeoff |
| **123** | Barrow's Goldeneye | Mountain lake specialist | W. N. America + Iceland | E=3 | Quiet courtship grunts |
| **124** | Common Goldeneye | Cosmopolitan diver | Holarctic | E=4 | Rattling wing-whistle |

The trilogy spans the entire genus: from the miniature Bufflehead to the restricted Barrow's to the cosmopolitan Common. Each species occupies a distinct ecological niche. The acoustic profiles are equally distinct: splash sounds (Bufflehead), quiet grunts (Barrow's), and mechanical wing-whistle (Common).

The *Bucephala* genus name ("ox-headed") is visible in all three species: the characteristically large, rounded heads that give goldeneyes and Buffleheads their distinctive silhouette. The oversized head houses the bright, forward-facing eyes that give the goldeneyes their common name -- the iris is a striking golden yellow in both species.
