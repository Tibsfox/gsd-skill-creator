Varied Thrush

Ixoreus naevius · Family Turdidae (Thrushes) · Monotypic genus
Single Haunting Minor Whistle · Mature Conifer Forest · Winter-Resident · Energy 2
The most haunting sound in the Pacific Northwest forest is one note. A single sustained buzzy whistle, held until it dissolves into the rain-wet air. The Varied Thrush does not cascade, does not trill, does not ornament. It delivers one frequency and lets the silence do the rest.

Taxonomy & Physical Description

The Varied Thrush is the sole living member of the genus Ixoreus — a monotypic genus within the family Turdidae (thrushes). Related to the American Robin but occupying a distinct ecological and acoustic niche. The genus name derives from Greek ixos (mistletoe) and oreos (mountain).

Plumage. Adult males are among the most visually striking birds in the PNW: orange breast with bold black breastband, blue-gray back and wings, orange wing bars, and a prominent orange eyebrow stripe. Length: 7.5–10 inches (19–26 cm). Weight: 65–100 g. Roughly robin-sized — between the heron (2,100+ g) and the wren (8–12 g). The circle does not progress linearly in body size. It moves where the sound demands.

Bioacoustics — The Defining Sound

One Note at a Time

The Varied Thrush’s song consists of a single, long, buzzy whistled note on one pitch, sustained for 1–2 seconds, followed by a pause of 3–8 seconds, then another single note at a different pitch. Each note is held on a single fundamental frequency. No melodic contour within the note — no rising, falling, or sweep. The pitch is set at onset and sustained.

This is the polar opposite of the Pacific Wren. Where the wren delivers 100+ notes in 10 seconds, the thrush delivers one note every 4–10 seconds. Where the wren is Coltrane’s sheets of sound, the thrush is Miles Davis playing one note and letting it ring. In music theory terms: the wren is a virtuosic Presto passage of sixty-fourth notes; the thrush is a Largo of whole notes.

Amplitude Modulation: The Buzzy Quality

The distinctive buzzy, ethereal quality is produced by amplitude modulation (AM). The syrinx generates a carrier tone (2.5–5 kHz) and superimposes rapid amplitude fluctuations at 20–50 Hz. This is AM synthesis in biological form — the same mathematical operation underlying AM radio transmission: carrier wave × modulation signal = complex output with spectral sidebands.

The contrast with degree 1° is illuminating. The Pacific Wren’s song is FM (frequency modulation) — rapid pitch changes producing spectral complexity. The Varied Thrush is AM (amplitude modulation) — rapid volume changes producing spectral complexity. FM and AM: the two fundamental modulation types in both radio engineering and biological acoustics, both delivered within the circle’s first three degrees.

The Silence Between the Notes

The inter-note pause of 3–8 seconds is not dead air. It allows the preceding note to decay naturally through the forest’s reverberant field, creates anticipation for the next note, and provides the negative space that gives each tone its weight. In Japanese aesthetics, this is ma — the interval, the pregnant pause that gives meaning to what surrounds it. In recording engineering, it is pre-delay: allowing the direct sound to be heard before the reverberant field builds.

The Arc: Degrees 0° → 1° → 2°

Silence → cascade complexity → single sustained tone. Not linear. Not monotonic. The first evidence that the circle proceeds by contrast, not by escalation.

Mature Conifer Forest & Winter Descent

The Varied Thrush breeds in dense, mature conifer forest — Douglas fir, western red cedar, western hemlock, Sitka spruce — from sea level to ~6,000 ft. Unlike the year-round residents at degrees 0° and 1°, the thrush undertakes altitudinal migration: breeding in montane forest, descending to lowland parks and residential areas in winter.

In Seattle, the Varied Thrush is a winter visitor (October–April), present in Discovery Park, Washington Park Arboretum, Seward Park, Interlaken Park, and Capitol Hill residential areas with mature conifers. The song of a Varied Thrush on a rainy January morning in Capitol Hill is one of the most distinctive sensory experiences of living in Seattle.

The 2.5–5 kHz frequency range is acoustically optimized for conifer forest propagation. The sustained note duration (1–2 s) provides resistance to masking by transient environmental noise — the same principle used in sonar: a longer ping carries further.

Music Theory Mapping

The thrush’s sustained note is a pedal point — a held tone that serves as an anchor while time passes around it. In Baroque counterpoint, Indian classical music (tanpura), bagpipe tradition, and minimalist composition, the drone is the harmonic reference from which all other motion derives meaning.

The AM synthesis parallel is technically precise. The syrinx performs amplitude modulation — carrier × modulator — producing spectral sidebands identical in mathematical structure to AM radio transmission. Where the wren at degree 1° is an FM synthesizer, the thrush at degree 2° is an AM transmitter. Two fundamental modulation types, two adjacent degrees.

The minimal melody (one pitch per event) maps to Webern’s pointillism, where each note is isolated and given maximum weight by surrounding silence. It maps to La Monte Young’s sustained-tone works and to Brian Eno’s ambient music. The common principle: fewer notes, more weight per note.

The effective tempo of ~6–15 BPM is below any standard Western musical tempo marking. The thrush operates in a temporal domain that is hypometric — slower than slow. And yet the listener perceives rhythm through subjective rhythmization: the brain imposes pattern on approximately periodic events. The bird does not sing in strict time, but the listener hears something like time.
