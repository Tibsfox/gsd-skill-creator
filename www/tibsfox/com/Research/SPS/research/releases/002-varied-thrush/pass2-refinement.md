### Winter Altitudinal Migration and the Two-World Bird

Pass 1 established the Varied Thrush's habitat in dense montane conifer forest. Pass 2 addresses the phenomenon that makes this bird unlike any other species in the first three degrees of the circle: it moves. Not laterally, like a latitudinal migrant flying south for winter, but *vertically* — descending from montane breeding territory to lowland urban habitat in a seasonal elevator that connects two utterly different worlds.

The Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*) is an altitudinal migrant. In Washington State, breeding occurs primarily in mid- to high-elevation conifer forests between 2,000 and 5,000 feet, in the dense, wet canopies of Douglas fir, western hemlock, and western red cedar where light reaches the forest floor as dim green filtered through a hundred feet of foliage. Beginning in September and October, breeding birds descend to lowland forests, parks, suburban gardens, and residential neighborhoods throughout the Puget Sound lowlands. By mid-winter, the Varied Thrush is a common visitor to backyard feeders in Capitol Hill, Seward Park, Discovery Park, and the Washington Park Arboretum — the same neighborhoods where Wayne Horvitz composes and performs, where the Pacific Wren (degree 1) sings its cascading song from the understory of Seward Park's old-growth remnant.

The migration is not a simple north-south journey. Washington's Varied Thrushes are year-round residents of the state — they breed in the state's mountains and winter in its lowlands, making a vertical transit of perhaps 3,000-4,000 feet within a horizontal radius of 50-100 miles. The lowland winter population is further expanded by birds migrating south from Alaska and British Columbia, leapfrogging the Washington residents to reach wintering grounds in Oregon and California. Every two to five years, irruptive events push larger numbers south than usual, triggered by heavy snowfall at breeding elevations that buries the ground-level foraging substrate the birds depend on. In these irruption years, the Varied Thrush becomes conspicuous in lowland urban areas — suddenly appearing in numbers at feeders, under hedges, and on the branches of ornamental conifers in residential neighborhoods.

The two-world quality of this migration is the critical ecological insight. The bird that sings its haunting single-note whistle from a 4,000-foot ridgeline in the North Cascades in June is the same bird that forages under a rhodendron in a Capitol Hill backyard in January. The montane breeding habitat is among the most remote and undisturbed environments in the Pacific Northwest — old-growth forest at elevation, accessible only by logging roads and hiking trails, rarely visited by humans between September and June. The winter habitat is among the most anthropogenic environments in the region — residential neighborhoods, city parks, university campuses. The Varied Thrush occupies both, and it carries the montane wilderness into the urban lowland through the only channel available to it: sound.

When a Varied Thrush sings on a January morning from the top of a western red cedar in a Capitol Hill yard, the sound that reaches the listener's ear is the voice of a montane old-growth forest. The song was evolved for propagation through dense canopy at elevation. Its frequency range (2.5–5 kHz), its sustained duration (1–2 seconds per note), its AM-modulated buzzy timbre — all of these acoustic properties were shaped by the selective pressures of montane conifer forest, where the bird competes for acoustic space not with traffic and leaf blowers but with wind, rain on needles, and the calls of Clark's nutcrackers and mountain chickadees. When that song plays in a suburban garden, it is acoustically out of context — a montane signal in a lowland soundscape — and the disjunction between the sound and its setting is part of what makes the experience so striking. The song does not belong in a backyard. It belongs in a wilderness. The bird brings the wilderness down with it.

The British Columbia Bird Atlas describes the Varied Thrush's winter urban presence with unusual affection, noting that the bird's "striking appearance and familiarity in winter when it visits urban and suburban gardens" earned it the distinction of being the logo species for the entire *Atlas of the Breeding Birds of British Columbia*. In the Pacific Northwest, the annual arrival of the Varied Thrush in lowland neighborhoods is a seasonal marker as reliable as the return of the salmon or the first blooming of the red flowering currant. The bird's appearance signals deep winter — the darkest, wettest months — and its song, delivered into the gray quiet of a January dawn, is the sound of winter itself.

Neither the Great Blue Heron (degree 0) nor the Pacific Wren (degree 1) performs altitudinal migration. The heron is a year-round resident of the Puget Sound lowlands, bound to its rookeries and tidal foraging habitat. The wren is a year-round resident of lowland old-growth, sedentary within its tiny territory. The Varied Thrush introduces a new ecological principle into the circle: seasonal movement between worlds. This is the two-world bird — montane by breeding, lowland by winter, carrying the acoustic signature of one world into the other.

### The AM Synthesis Deep Dive: Bilateral Syringeal Control and Individual Fingerprints

Pass 1 introduced the amplitude modulation (AM) quality of the Varied Thrush's song and established the basic parallel to AM radio transmission. Pass 2 dissects the biomechanics of how the syrinx produces this modulation, the spectral consequences of bilateral control, and the discovery that individual birds can be identified by their modulation signatures — an acoustic fingerprint as unique as a human voice.

The avian syrinx is a dual organ. Unlike the mammalian larynx, which contains a single set of vocal folds, the syrinx sits at the junction of the trachea and the two bronchi, and each bronchial branch contains its own set of vibrating membranes — the tympaniform membranes — controlled by independent musculature. This bilateral architecture means that a bird can, in principle, produce two independent sounds simultaneously, one from each side of the syrinx. In species with complex songs (thrushes of the genus *Catharus*, the Hermit Thrush in particular), the two sides of the syrinx produce different frequencies that interact to create the rich harmonic complexity of the song. The two sides can be coupled (producing a single, complex tone) or decoupled (producing two independent tones). This bilateral independence is the source of what NPR Bird Notes describes as the thrushes' ability to "voice notes independently and simultaneously from each half of its syrinx, notes which blend brilliantly as ethereal, harmonious tones."

In the Varied Thrush, the bilateral syrinx produces the characteristic AM-modulated tone through a mechanism that is still not fully characterized but is understood in outline. The carrier tone — the perceived pitch, typically between 2.5 and 5 kHz — is generated by the vibration of the tympaniform membranes at a fundamental frequency determined by their tension, mass, and the airflow rate through the bronchi. The amplitude modulation — the rapid fluctuation in volume that produces the buzzy timbre — arises from the interaction between the two sides of the syrinx. When the two sides produce frequencies that are very close but not identical, the resulting interference creates a beating pattern — a periodic rise and fall in amplitude at a rate equal to the frequency difference between the two sides. If one side produces a tone at 3,500 Hz and the other at 3,530 Hz, the resulting beat frequency is 30 Hz — squarely in the range (20–50 Hz) of the amplitude modulation observed in spectrograms of the Varied Thrush's song.

This is the biological equivalent of a two-oscillator AM synthesizer. In electronic music, amplitude modulation is produced by multiplying a carrier signal by a modulator signal; the result contains the carrier frequency plus sideband frequencies at carrier ± modulator. In the Varied Thrush's syrinx, the two bronchial sources serve as carrier and modulator, and their slight frequency offset produces the same sideband structure. The spectral signature — the precise pattern of carrier frequency, sideband spacing, and sideband amplitude — is determined by the physical properties of each individual bird's syrinx: membrane tension, mass, elasticity, and the neural control of the muscles that regulate these parameters. Because these properties vary between individuals, each bird's AM signature is unique.

This uniqueness has been exploited in field research. Spectrographic analysis of Varied Thrush recordings reveals that individual birds can be distinguished by three parameters: (1) the carrier frequency of their song (the perceived pitch, which varies by individual across the species' 2.5–5 kHz range); (2) the modulation rate (the speed of the amplitude fluctuation, varying between approximately 20 and 50 Hz depending on the individual); and (3) the modulation depth (how much the amplitude varies between peak and trough, which affects the perceived "buzziness" of the tone). Together, these three parameters constitute an acoustic fingerprint — a spectral identity card that allows researchers to track individual birds across multiple singing bouts without visual identification.

The electronic AM parallel deepens at degree 2. Edwin Armstrong's development of FM (frequency modulation) radio in the 1930s was driven by FM's superior rejection of amplitude noise — static, interference, atmospheric disturbance. AM radio, the older technology, was more susceptible to noise because its information was encoded in amplitude, and amplitude is the parameter that environmental noise most easily corrupts. The Pacific Wren's song (degree 1) is FM — rapid frequency sweeps that encode complexity in pitch variation. The Varied Thrush's song (degree 2) is AM — sustained tones whose complexity resides in amplitude variation. The two modulation types that defined twentieth-century telecommunications are both represented in the PNW forest soundscape. FM (the wren) is resistant to noise but requires rapid frequency variation to encode information. AM (the thrush) is susceptible to noise but allows sustained, stable tones that carry emotional weight through their very persistence. The wren's song dazzles. The thrush's song haunts. FM is virtuosity. AM is atmosphere.

The individual acoustic fingerprint connects to the degree 2 theme of persistence and identity. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) communicates through posture and visual display — its identity is physical, bodily, visible. The Pacific Wren (degree 1) communicates through a song so rapid and complex that individual variation is difficult to parse with the human ear. The Varied Thrush (degree 2) communicates through a song so simple and sustained that individual variation becomes audible, identifiable, personal. The simpler the signal, the more visible the individual. This is a principle from information theory: in a low-bandwidth channel, every variation is meaningful.

### The Haunting-Sound Phenomenon: Why One Note Changes the Forest

The Varied Thrush's song is consistently described, across multiple independent sources, as the most evocative sound in the Pacific Northwest forest. The Cornell Lab of Ornithology calls it "simple" and "ringing" and the voice that "gives a voice to the quiet forests of the Pacific Northwest." The British Columbia Bird Atlas describes the song as "sweet but eerie." WestSound Magazine calls the bird "a welcomed reprieve from the gray days of winter." The consensus is so uniform that it constitutes a phenomenon requiring explanation: why does a single buzzy note, repeated at long intervals, produce a subjective response out of all proportion to its acoustic simplicity?

The psychoacoustic analysis begins with frequency. The Varied Thrush's pitch range of 2.5–5 kHz coincides almost exactly with the human auditory system's region of maximum sensitivity. The ear canal resonates at approximately 2.5–3 kHz, amplifying sounds in this range by 10–15 dB relative to frequencies above or below. The thrush's song arrives at the frequency the human ear is most attuned to receive. It sounds louder, clearer, and more *present* than its actual acoustic amplitude would predict. In a quiet forest, where the ambient noise floor may be 20–25 dB SPL, the thrush's song at the ear canal's resonance peak creates a figure-ground contrast — signal above noise — that is viscerally perceptible. The note does not merely arrive. It *announces itself* against the silence.

The buzzy timbre adds a dimension that a pure tone would lack. Psychoacoustic research has consistently demonstrated that tones with spectral roughness — the quality produced by rapid amplitude modulation — are perceived as more complex, more "felt," and more emotionally engaging than pure sine tones at the same frequency and amplitude. Roughness activates a broader region of the basilar membrane in the cochlea, producing a richer pattern of neural firing that the auditory cortex processes as timbral depth. The thrush's AM modulation rate of 20–50 Hz falls in the range that the human auditory system is most sensitive to as roughness — below this range, the modulation is perceived as separate amplitude changes (tremolo); above it, the modulation is too fast to track and blends into the overall timbre. The 20–50 Hz sweet spot produces the maximum sensation of buzzy, ethereal, not-quite-pure tone — the acoustic quality that listeners describe as "haunting."

The silence between notes is as psychoacoustically significant as the notes themselves. Each inter-note pause of 3–8 seconds allows the preceding note to decay naturally in the forest's reverberant environment. The direct sound fades, the early reflections from nearby tree trunks arrive and dissipate, the late diffuse reverberation fills the air and then dissolves into the ambient noise floor. The listener experiences the full decay envelope — onset, sustain, decay, silence — before the next note begins. This complete articulation of the amplitude envelope, from attack to silence, is rare in natural soundscapes. Most bird songs overlap with their own echoes, with other birds' songs, with wind and water. The Varied Thrush's long inter-note silence provides a clean acoustic frame around each note, allowing the listener to inhabit the sound fully before releasing it.

The successive-note pitch relationships add a harmonic dimension. While individual Varied Thrushes do not produce precisely the same intervals between successive notes on every singing bout, there is a documented tendency toward descending pitch relationships and intervals that approximate minor seconds, minor thirds, and perfect fourths — intervals that in Western music theory are associated with tension, melancholy, and unresolved longing. The song does not resolve. Each note establishes a tonal center; the next note destabilizes it. The listener's ear seeks a harmonic relationship between successive notes, finds one that suggests sadness or mystery, and then waits through the silence for the next note to confirm or revise the implication. The harmonic ambiguity — never a clear major or minor mode, always hovering between possibilities — is the same territory that Horvitz's quartal voicings explore in his chamber music: harmony that refuses the binary of happy and sad, major and minor, resolved and unresolved.

The cumulative effect — frequency optimized for human hearing, timbral roughness maximizing emotional engagement, silence framing each note for complete perceptual processing, pitch relationships suggesting unresolved longing — is a sound that operates on the human nervous system with surgical precision. The Varied Thrush did not evolve to haunt humans; it evolved to communicate with other Varied Thrushes across the acoustic obstacles of dense montane forest. But the same acoustic properties that make its song effective in its ecological niche — the carrying frequency, the sustained duration, the AM timbre, the silence-framing — happen to align with the parameters that produce the strongest emotional response in human listeners. The haunting quality is an accident of convergence between avian communication engineering and human psychoacoustic sensitivity.

David George Haskell, in *The Songs of Trees* (2017), places the Varied Thrush's song in the context of forest acoustic ecology — the study of how sound and forest structure interact to create the distinctive "soundscape" of a given habitat type. The PNW temperate rain forest's soundscape is defined by rain, wind, creek water, and the Varied Thrush. Remove any one of these four elements and the soundscape is recognizably different. The thrush's contribution is not volume or density — it is *character*. The single sustained note, arriving at unpredictable intervals from an invisible source deep in the canopy, gives the forest its quality of inhabited mystery. Something is in there. Something is singing. Something is watching. The forest is not empty. The thrush proves it.

### Pioneer 1 Resonance: Partial Failure as Complete Atmosphere

Pioneer 1 was designed to orbit the Moon, photograph its surface, and return a comprehensive dataset from cislunar space. It achieved none of these objectives. The Thor-Able's guidance error, the accelerometer malfunction, the 500-feet-per-second velocity shortfall — these technical failures meant the probe never came within two-thirds of the distance to the Moon. By the mission's own stated criteria, Pioneer 1 was a failure. But the probe's 43-hour flight, during which it transmitted data continuously from an altitude of 113,854 kilometers, returned the first detailed measurements of the Van Allen radiation belts, the first observations of hydromagnetic oscillations in Earth's magnetic field, and the first micrometeoroid impact measurements in near-Earth space. The mission that failed to reach the Moon succeeded in characterizing the space between the Earth and the Moon — the transit environment through which all future missions would travel.

The Varied Thrush's song fails by the metrics of avian vocal complexity. Compared to the Pacific Wren (degree 1) — which produces over 100 distinct elements in a 10-second bout, with rapid FM sweeps, trills, and harmonic stacking — the Varied Thrush's single sustained AM note is impoverished. One note. One timbre. No melody, no counterpoint, no rhythmic variation. By the metrics of acoustic complexity, the song is a failure to be a song. It is the Pioneer 1 of bird vocalizations: designed, by the logic of the complex-song paradigm, to reach the Moon of vocal virtuosity, and falling dramatically short.

But the Varied Thrush succeeds in a dimension that the complexity metrics do not measure: atmospheric impact. The single sustained note, with its AM-modulated buzz and its silence-framed delivery, produces an emotional and spatial effect that the wren's virtuosic cascade cannot achieve. The wren's song says *I am here, this is my territory, I am complex and vigorous and you should be impressed.* The thrush's song says *this forest is old, and deep, and quiet, and something lives in it.* One communicates individual fitness. The other communicates habitat character. Pioneer 1 failed to characterize the Moon but succeeded in characterizing the near-Earth environment. The thrush fails to demonstrate vocal fitness but succeeds in defining the acoustic character of the PNW forest. The partial failure is the complete atmosphere.

The sword fern parallel — *Polystichum munitum* — connects the thrush and Pioneer 1 at the deepest structural level. The sword fern is the persistent understory of PNW lowland and montane conifer forests, an evergreen perennial whose individual plants may persist for centuries. At Seward Park in Seattle, the sword fern die-off first observed in 2013 revealed how thoroughly the fern defines the forest's character: when the ferns die, the forest floor is transformed — bare soil replaces green fronds, the structural continuity of the understory collapses, and the ecosystem loses the layer that provides cover for ground-nesting birds, stabilizes soil against erosion, and cycles nutrients from fallen canopy material back into the root zone. The sword fern is not the most visible organism in the forest. It is the organism whose absence is most visible.

The Varied Thrush is the acoustic sword fern. Its song is not the loudest or the most complex sound in the forest — that would be the winter wren's cascade or the Steller's jay's rasp. It is the sound whose absence would most change the character of the forest. A PNW forest without the Varied Thrush's single sustained whistle is acoustically recognizable but experientially different — it is a forest without its haunting quality, without the note that hangs in the air and names the silence around it. The song defines the character of the system by being persistently, reliably, atmospherically present. It is the acoustic understory — the sound beneath the canopy, the tone beneath the texture, the note that remains when everything else changes.

Pioneer 1's 43-hour dataset established the operational baseline for all subsequent NASA missions. Before Pioneer 1, the Van Allen belts were a theoretical prediction; after Pioneer 1, they were a measured reality with defined boundaries, radiation intensities, and spatial structure. The mission's data became the baseline — the persistent layer of empirical knowledge on which the Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo programs would build. The sword fern is the ecological baseline of the PNW forest floor. The Varied Thrush is the acoustic baseline of the PNW forest soundscape. Pioneer 1 is the empirical baseline of near-Earth space science. Three baselines. Three persistent layers. Three instances of the same structural principle: the thing that remains, the thing that everything else is measured against, the understory constant.

The degree progression across all three tracks now reads: Degree 0 established the hidden infrastructure (Armillaria underground / Great Blue Heron silence / NASA founding / Quincy Jones industry-building). Degree 1 established the first visible signal (fireweed surface growth / Pacific Wren cascade / Pioneer 0's 77-second failure / Frisell's improvisational openness). Degree 2 establishes the persistent baseline (sword fern understory / Varied Thrush sustained tone / Pioneer 1's 43-hour dataset / Horvitz's community infrastructure). The forest is being assembled from the ground up: root network, pioneer flash, persistent understory. The next degree will begin to build the canopy.
