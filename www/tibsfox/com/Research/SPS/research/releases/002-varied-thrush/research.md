## Taxonomy and Physical Description

The Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*) is the sole living member of the genus *Ixoreus* -- a monotypic genus within the family **Turdidae** (thrushes), order Passeriformes. The genus name derives from the Greek *ixos* (mistletoe) and *oreos* (mountain), reflecting the bird's association with montane forest. The species epithet *naevius* (Latin: spotted, marked) refers to the distinctive patterning of the plumage.

**Taxonomic position within Turdidae.** The Varied Thrush is a thrush -- the same family as the American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*), Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*), and Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*). But it occupies its own genus, separated from the robin by substantial molecular phylogenetic distance (Klicka, Voelker, and Spellman 2005, *Molecular Phylogenetics and Evolution*). Where the robin is a generalist of lawns and edges, the Varied Thrush is a specialist of deep, dark, wet forest. They share a family. They do not share a world.

**Physical description.** The Varied Thrush is among the most visually striking birds in the Pacific Northwest. Adult males display:

- **Orange breast with a bold black breastband** -- a diagnostic field mark visible at distance. The breastband is a single, clean, dark band across the upper breast, sharply contrasting with the rich orange below and above. No other PNW bird has this pattern.
- **Blue-gray back and wings** -- a cool, slate-toned dorsum that blends with the muted light of the conifer understory.
- **Orange wing bars** -- two prominent bars formed by orange-tipped secondary coverts. In flight, these create a flash of warm color against the dark wings.
- **Orange eyebrow stripe (supercilium)** -- a bold stripe running from the bill base over the eye to the nape, framing the dark eye in orange.
- **Dark auricular patch** -- a dark ear patch that connects to the breastband, giving the face a masked appearance.

Adult females exhibit the same pattern but muted: the black breastband is replaced by a grayish band, the orange is paler, and the blue-gray is browner. Juveniles resemble duller females.

**Dimensions.** Length: 7.5--10 inches (19--26 cm). Wingspan: 13--15 inches (33--38 cm). Weight: 2.3--3.5 ounces (65--100 g). This places the Varied Thrush at roughly the same body size as the American Robin -- substantially larger than the Pacific Wren (8--12 g) but far smaller than the Great Blue Heron (2,100--2,500 g). In the context of the first three degrees: degree 0 is the largest bird (4+ feet tall), degree 1 is the smallest (4 inches), and degree 2 is in the middle. The circle does not progress linearly in body size. It moves where the sound demands.

## Bioacoustics -- THE DEFINING SOUND OF THE PNW FOREST

### The Song: One Note at a Time

The Varied Thrush's song is singular among North American birds. It consists of a **single, long, buzzy whistled note on one pitch**, sustained for approximately **1--2 seconds**, followed by a **pause of 3--8 seconds**, then another single note on a **different pitch**. The bird does not sing phrases. It does not cascade. It does not trill or ornament. It delivers one note, lets the silence absorb it, then delivers another.

Each note is sustained on a single fundamental frequency. There is no melodic contour within the note -- no rising, no falling, no frequency sweep. The pitch is set at the onset and held. The note decays naturally as the bird's air supply diminishes, producing a slight decrease in amplitude toward the end of each note, but the frequency remains constant. In music notation, this is a **whole note tied to another whole note** -- a single pitch held for the maximum duration the instrument can sustain.

The pitch changes **between** notes, not within them. Successive notes typically differ by intervals ranging from a minor second to a major third, though the specific intervals vary between individuals and across successive notes. The overall effect is of a slow, deliberate, modal melody -- one note at a time, each separated by a silence that is as important as the sound.

This is the polar opposite of the Pacific Wren's approach. Where the wren delivers 100+ notes in 10 seconds, the Varied Thrush delivers **one note every 4--10 seconds**. Where the wren's song is a cascade of rapid FM-modulated elements, the thrush's song is a single sustained AM-modulated tone. Where the wren's complexity is in the density and variety of its output, the thrush's complexity is in the quality and placement of each individual note. In music theory terms: the wren is a virtuosic *Presto* passage of sixty-fourth notes; the thrush is a *Largo* of whole notes. The wren is Coltrane's sheets of sound. The thrush is Miles Davis playing one note and letting it ring.

### Amplitude Modulation: The Buzzy Quality

The Varied Thrush's whistle is not a pure sine wave. It has a distinctive **buzzy, ethereal quality** -- a roughness overlaid on the tonal foundation that gives the sound its haunting character. This quality is produced by **amplitude modulation (AM)**.

The bird's syrinx generates a carrier tone at the fundamental frequency (typically 2.5--5 kHz). Superimposed on this carrier is a rapid modulation of amplitude -- the volume fluctuates at a rate of approximately 20--50 Hz, producing the characteristic buzzy timbre. This is **AM synthesis in biological form**. The carrier frequency determines the perceived pitch; the modulation frequency determines the timbral quality (buzzy, rough, ethereal). The interaction between carrier and modulation produces sideband frequencies at carrier +/- modulation, creating spectral complexity from a process that starts with a single tone.

In electronic audio engineering, amplitude modulation is the basis of **AM radio** -- a carrier wave modulated by an audio signal to encode information. The Varied Thrush's syrinx performs the identical mathematical operation: a carrier (fundamental tone) modulated by a signal (rapid amplitude fluctuation) to produce a complex output (the buzzy whistle). The bird is a biological AM transmitter. This connects directly to the AMR (AM Radio) research project in the Rosetta series -- same mathematics, different medium. Edwin Armstrong's FM (frequency modulation) superseded AM for broadcast because FM rejects amplitude noise; the Pacific Wren's song is FM. The Varied Thrush's song is AM. The two synthesis methods that defined 20th-century radio are both represented in the first three degrees of the PNW 360 Species circle.

**Spectral analysis.** Published spectrograms of Varied Thrush song (Pyle 1997; Cornell Lab of Ornithology, Birds of the World) show:

- A strong fundamental frequency, typically between **2.5--5.0 kHz**
- Sideband frequencies created by the amplitude modulation, spaced at regular intervals above and below the fundamental
- Minimal harmonic overtones beyond the AM sidebands -- the sound is spectrally narrower than most songbird vocalizations
- Note durations of **1.0--2.5 seconds**, measured from onset to the natural decay below the noise floor
- Inter-note intervals of **3--8 seconds** (the silence between notes is typically 2--5 times longer than the notes themselves)

**Frequency range.** Individual notes fall within the **2.5--5.0 kHz** range, with most individuals using a personal range spanning approximately 1.5 kHz (e.g., one bird might sing between 3.0 and 4.5 kHz; another between 2.5 and 4.0 kHz). The frequency range places the Varied Thrush in the **alto register** of the avian world -- below the Pacific Wren's soprano (3--8 kHz peak) and well above the Great Blue Heron's bass (200--800 Hz). In the first three degrees of the circle: bass (0), soprano (1), alto (2). The registers are not ascending linearly. They are being arranged by the logic of the sound, not the logic of the scale.

### The Silence Between the Notes

The Varied Thrush's inter-note pause -- typically 3--8 seconds of silence -- is not dead air. It is a compositional element. The silence allows the preceding note to decay naturally in the forest acoustic environment, lets the reverberant tail fade into the ambient soundscape, and creates expectation for the next note. The listener (human or avian) waits. The anticipation is part of the experience.

In music theory, this is **rest as structural element** -- the same principle identified at degree 0 (Great Blue Heron), but now applied differently. The heron's silence IS the primary sound. The thrush's silence is the **frame around each note** -- the negative space that gives each tone its weight. In visual art, this is the principle of negative space: the emptiness around a form is as important as the form itself. In Japanese aesthetics, this is **ma** -- the interval, the space, the pregnant pause that gives meaning to what surrounds it.

The effect on a listener standing in a PNW forest is unmistakable. The single note floats out of the darkness between the trees. It hangs in the air. It fades. Silence returns. Just when the silence has been absorbed, another note arrives, at a different pitch. It is not urgent. It is not demanding. It is simply there, like the forest itself -- persistent, unhurried, and profoundly atmospheric.

Multiple naturalists and sound ecologists have independently described the Varied Thrush's song as **"the most haunting sound in the Pacific Northwest forest"** (Cornell Lab of Ornithology; National Audubon Society; Sibley 2000; Wahl, Tweit, and Mlodinow 2005). This consensus is significant. Of all the bird species in the PNW -- including the melodic Hermit Thrush, the virtuosic Pacific Wren, the elaborate Winter Wren, and the charismatic Common Loon -- it is the Varied Thrush's simple, single-note song that is most consistently identified as the acoustic signature of the region. Simplicity can be more evocative than complexity. One note, perfectly placed, can do what a hundred notes cannot.

### The "Single Pitch Sustained" as Theory

The Varied Thrush's song reduces vocalization to its theoretical minimum: **one frequency, held for maximum duration, then silence, then one different frequency**. This is the building block of all sound, expressed in the simplest possible form. A single sustained frequency is a **sinusoid** -- the fundamental waveform from which all complex sounds can be constructed via Fourier synthesis (Fourier 1822). The wren's complex song contains hundreds of frequency components interacting in time. The thrush's song strips all of that away and presents the elemental unit: one pitch, one duration, one amplitude envelope.

In music, this is the **drone** -- a sustained tone that persists while other musical events occur around it (or, in the thrush's case, while silence occurs around it). The drone is foundational across musical traditions:

- **Indian classical music:** The tanpura provides a continuous drone over which the raga unfolds. The drone IS the harmonic reference -- everything is measured against it.
- **Scottish and Irish bagpipe:** The drones sound continuously while the chanter plays the melody. The harmonic tension between melody and drone creates the emotional character.
- **Organ music:** The pedal point (a sustained bass note held while the harmony changes above it) is one of the oldest techniques in Western music. Bach's Passacaglia in C minor holds a bass pedal for the entire opening.
- **Minimalist composition:** La Monte Young's sustained tones, Pauline Oliveros's deep listening, Eliane Radigue's electronic drones -- all explore the aesthetic and psychoacoustic properties of sustained frequencies.
- **Ambient music:** Brian Eno's *Music for Airports* (1978) uses sustained tones and slow harmonic motion to create atmospheric environments. The Varied Thrush's song IS ambient music -- atmospheric, sustained, unhurried, environmental.

The thrush does not drone in the strict sense (it pauses between notes), but each note IS a drone in miniature -- a sustained frequency that establishes a tonal center for its duration. The **pedal tone** is the more precise parallel: a held note that serves as an anchor while time passes around it.

### Amplitude Modulation and the AM Radio Parallel

The buzzy quality of the Varied Thrush's whistle is produced by the same mathematical operation that underlies AM radio transmission. In AM radio:

1. A **carrier wave** at a fixed high frequency is generated
2. An **information signal** (audio) modulates the amplitude of the carrier
3. The receiver demodulates the signal to extract the original audio

In the Varied Thrush's syrinx:

1. The tympaniform membranes generate a **carrier tone** at the fundamental pitch (2.5--5 kHz)
2. Rapid muscular control modulates the **amplitude** of that tone at 20--50 Hz
3. The listener's auditory system perceives the combination as a single buzzy note

The mathematics are identical. The medium is different. The thrush and the radio transmitter are solving the same signal-processing problem: how to encode complexity (timbral information) onto a simple carrier (a single frequency). This is a direct cross-reference to the **AMR (AM Radio)** project in the Rosetta Research series, which documents amplitude modulation as a foundational communications technology.

The contrast with degree 1 is illuminating. The Pacific Wren's song is **FM (frequency modulation)** -- rapid pitch changes that produce spectral complexity through frequency variation. The Varied Thrush's song is **AM (amplitude modulation)** -- rapid volume changes that produce spectral complexity through amplitude variation. FM and AM are the two fundamental modulation types in both radio engineering and biological acoustics. The PNW circle delivers both within its first three degrees: silence (0), FM cascade (1), AM drone (2).

## Mature Conifer Forest Dependency

### Habitat Requirements

The Varied Thrush is strongly associated with **dense, mature, and old-growth coniferous forest** throughout its range. Breeding habitat requirements include:

- **Dense canopy closure.** The bird prefers forests with high canopy cover (>70%), which creates the dim, moist understory conditions it favors. Open or park-like forests are used at much lower densities.
- **Mature conifers.** Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) dominate the breeding habitat. The bird is absent or rare in deciduous forest and grassland.
- **Moss, fern, and shrub understory.** Varied Thrushes forage primarily on the forest floor, turning leaf litter and probing through moss for invertebrates and seeds. The rich duff layer and deep moss carpets of mature conifer forest provide the foraging substrate.
- **Elevational range.** Breeds from sea level to approximately 6,000 feet (1,800 m) in the Cascades and Olympics, with highest densities in montane forest between 2,000--5,000 feet. In the breeding season, the bird is primarily a species of mountain forest.

### Altitudinal Migration: The Winter Descent

Unlike the Great Blue Heron (year-round resident) and the Pacific Wren (year-round resident in lowland old-growth), the Varied Thrush undertakes **altitudinal migration**. It breeds in montane conifer forest during spring and summer, then descends to lowland forests, parks, and residential areas during fall and winter. In Seattle, the Varied Thrush is a **winter visitor** -- arriving in October and departing by April. During winter, it can be found in:

- **Discovery Park** -- mature second-growth forest with dense canopy
- **Washington Park Arboretum** -- mixed conifer and deciduous plantings
- **Seward Park** -- old-growth forest remnant
- **Capitol Hill residential areas** -- especially yards with mature conifers and berry-producing shrubs
- **Interlaken Park** -- dense conifer canopy

The winter presence of the Varied Thrush in Seattle neighborhoods is a cherished annual event for PNW birders. The bird appears at feeders, forages under hedges, and -- most memorably -- sings from the tops of mature conifers in residential areas, delivering its haunting whistled notes into the gray, wet, quiet winter mornings. The song of the Varied Thrush on a rainy January morning in Capitol Hill is one of the most distinctive sensory experiences of living in Seattle.

### Acoustic Propagation in Conifer Forest

The Varied Thrush's song is acoustically optimized for propagation through dense conifer forest. The frequency range (2.5--5 kHz) falls within the window that experiences the least attenuation in forest environments:

- Frequencies below 2 kHz scatter from tree trunks and branches
- Frequencies above 8 kHz attenuate from atmospheric absorption (especially in the humid PNW air)
- The 2.5--5 kHz band propagates efficiently through the mid-story and canopy of conifer forest

The sustained duration of each note (1--2 seconds) provides further propagation advantage. A longer signal is more resistant to masking by transient environmental noise (wind gusts, branch creaks, rain drops) than a short signal. The forest absorbs the onset of the note, but the sustained duration ensures that the middle and end of the note arrive intact at the receiver. This is the same principle used in sonar: a longer ping carries further than a short one because integration time allows the receiver to extract the signal from noise.

The inter-note silence (3--8 seconds) may also serve an acoustic function: it allows the reverberant tail of each note to decay fully before the next note begins, preventing inter-note interference. In recording engineering, this is the principle of **pre-delay** -- allowing the direct sound to be heard clearly before the reverberant field builds. The thrush's silence is pre-delay implemented in biology.

## Acoustic Theory Mapping

### Sustained Tone as Drone (Pedal Point)

The single sustained note maps directly to the concept of the **pedal point** in music theory -- a held note that persists while time and harmonic context shift around it. The pedal point is foundational in:

- **Baroque counterpoint:** Bach's organ works use pedal tones (sustained bass notes) as harmonic anchors while upper voices move in counterpoint above them.
- **Jazz harmony:** Pedal tones in jazz provide a tonal center against which chromatic movement creates tension and release.
- **Electronic music:** The sustained synth pad, the held chord, the bass drone -- all are pedal points in electronic form.

The Varied Thrush's note IS a pedal point -- a single frequency held until it naturally decays. The inter-note silence is the moment of release before the next pedal is established at a different pitch.

### Amplitude Modulation as Timbral Synthesis

The AM synthesis parallel has been described above. In music technology terms, the Varied Thrush implements **ring modulation** -- a special case of AM where two signals are multiplied, producing sum and difference frequencies. Early analog synthesizers (the Moog Modular, the EMS VCS3, the ARP 2600) used ring modulators to produce metallic, bell-like, and otherworldly timbres. The Varied Thrush's buzzy quality IS a ring-modulated tone -- a carrier frequency multiplied by a modulation signal, producing the characteristic spectral sidebands.

### Minimal Melody: One Note at a Time

The Varied Thrush's approach to melody is **reductive** -- it strips melody to its absolute minimum: one pitch, sustained, then a different pitch. This is not simplicity by default. This is simplicity by principle. The thrush is capable of producing more complex vocalizations (alarm calls, flight calls), but its territorial song deliberately delivers one note at a time.

In composition, this approach maps to **serialism's pointillism** (Anton Webern's approach to twelve-tone writing, where each note is isolated in time and register, given maximum weight by the silence around it) and to **minimalism** (the sustained tones of La Monte Young, the gradual processes of Steve Reich). The common principle: fewer notes, more weight per note.

### Inter-Note Silence as Rhythmic Structure

The pause between notes is not absence. It is **rest as rhythmic element** -- the space that defines the tempo and creates the sense of pulse. If each note lasts 1--2 seconds and each pause lasts 3--8 seconds, the resulting rhythm is approximately one event every 4--10 seconds, or **6--15 BPM**. This is below the threshold of what most Western music considers a performable tempo (the slowest standard marking, *Largo*, is 40--66 BPM). The thrush's rhythm exists outside the range of human musical tempo -- it is **hypometric**, slower than slow, operating in a temporal domain that shares more with geomorphological processes than with composed music.

And yet the rhythm IS perceived. A human standing in a forest hearing the Varied Thrush does not experience randomness. They experience a pulse -- slow, irregular, but undeniably structured. The brain imposes rhythmic pattern on the sequence of notes even though the intervals are not metronomic. This is the perceptual phenomenon of **subjective rhythmization** -- the tendency to hear regularity in approximately periodic events. The thrush's song exploits this tendency. The bird does not sing in strict time. But the listener hears something like time.

### Frequency as Emotional Register

The Varied Thrush's pitch range (2.5--5 kHz) and the minor-key quality of its successive-note relationships contribute to the song's emotional character. The perceived "haunting" or "melancholic" quality arises from several acoustic factors:

- **Moderate frequency.** The 2.5--5 kHz range is the human ear's region of highest sensitivity (the ear canal resonance peak is near 3 kHz). The thrush's song arrives at the frequency the human auditory system is most attuned to receive. It sounds louder and more present than its actual amplitude would suggest.
- **Descending pitch tendency.** While not invariant, many Varied Thrush sequences exhibit a tendency toward descending pitch across successive notes. In Western music perception, descending motion is associated with resolution, relaxation, and melancholy. The descending tendency contributes to the song's perceived sadness.
- **AM-produced roughness.** The amplitude modulation creates a spectral roughness that the auditory system processes as a quality distinct from purity. Rough tones are perceived as darker, more complex, more "felt" than pure tones. The buzziness IS the emotional weight.
- **Sustained duration.** Longer notes are perceived as more expressive than short notes. A whole note carries more emotional weight than a quarter note because the listener has time to inhabit the frequency, to feel its resonance, to let it work.

## Cultural Significance: The Voice of the Rain Forest

The Varied Thrush is widely recognized as **the acoustic signature of the Pacific Northwest temperate rain forest**. This status is not assigned arbitrarily -- it is earned through the intersection of the bird's biology, its habitat, and its acoustic properties:

- **Ubiquity in appropriate habitat.** The Varied Thrush is common and widely distributed in PNW conifer forests. Where the habitat is intact, the bird is present and singing.
- **Unmistakable song.** No other PNW bird produces a sound that can be confused with the Varied Thrush's sustained buzzy whistle. It is a single-species acoustic signature.
- **Atmospheric quality.** The song's haunting, ethereal character matches the subjective experience of standing in a PNW rain forest -- dark, moist, quiet, ancient. The sound fits the place in a way that feels inevitable.
- **Documentary use.** The Varied Thrush's song is used extensively in nature documentaries, soundscape recordings, and environmental media as the representative sound of PNW forest. David George Haskell (*The Songs of Trees*, 2017) cites the Varied Thrush specifically as a defining voice of Pacific Northwest forest ecology.

**Attribution note (OCAP/CARE compliance).** The Varied Thrush inhabits forests that are the ancestral and continuing territories of Coast Salish peoples, including the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Suquamish, Snoqualmie, and many other nations. The conifer forests in which the bird sings are culturally significant landscapes that have been managed, tended, and understood by Indigenous peoples for millennia. This section uses only publicly available sources. For nation-specific cultural context regarding the forests and their inhabitants, primary sources are the relevant tribal cultural departments.
