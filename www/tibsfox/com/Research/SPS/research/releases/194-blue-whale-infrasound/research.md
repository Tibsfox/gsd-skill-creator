## THE LARGEST ANIMAL THAT HAS EVER LIVED

### The Absolute Superlative

The Blue Whale holds records that will likely never be broken:

- **Length:** Up to 30 meters (98 feet). The length of a basketball court.
- **Weight:** Up to 200 tons (180 metric tonnes). Heavier than any dinosaur. The heaviest animal that has EVER lived on Earth.
- **Heart:** The size of a Volkswagen Beetle. Weighs approximately 400 pounds.
- **Tongue:** Weighs as much as an elephant.
- **Aorta:** Large enough for a human child to crawl through.
- **Calf size:** A blue whale calf is born at 7-8 meters (23-26 feet) long and weighs 2-3 tons. The calf gains approximately 90 kg (200 lbs) per DAY during nursing.
- **No larger animal has existed.** In the entire 4.5-billion-year history of Earth, no animal has been larger. The blue whale is the absolute superlative of animal scale.

(Sources: Blue whale biology; NOAA; marine mammal records; paleontological comparison)

## 188 dB -- THE LOUDEST ANIMAL SOUND

### Calibration

The Blue Whale produces the loudest sound of any animal:

- **188 dB re 1 uPa at 1 meter.** This is the standard measurement for underwater sound. 188 dB underwater is not directly comparable to 188 dB in air (due to different reference pressures and impedances), but the absolute acoustic power is extraordinary.
- **10-40 Hz fundamental frequency.** Blue whale calls are at 10-40 Hz -- well below the threshold of human hearing (typically 20 Hz minimum, and many humans cannot hear below 30-40 Hz). The blue whale's voice is, to humans, infrasound -- sound you cannot hear but can sometimes feel.
- **Types of calls:**
  - **A calls:** Long, low-frequency moans at 15-20 Hz. Duration: 15-20 seconds. The "classic" blue whale call.
  - **B calls:** Lower-frequency pulses at 10-18 Hz. Shorter duration. Often alternating with A calls in a predictable pattern.
  - **D calls:** Higher-frequency downswept calls, 25-90 Hz. More variable. Sometimes called "arch" sounds.
- **Source level vs. received level.** The 188 dB measurement is at 1 meter from the whale. At distance, the sound attenuates -- but low-frequency sound attenuates very slowly in water. A blue whale call at 188 dB can be detected at distances of hundreds of miles.

(Sources: Cummings & Thompson 1971; McDonald et al. 2006; blue whale acoustic measurements; infrasound physics)

## COMMUNICATION ACROSS OCEAN BASINS

### Hundreds of Miles

The blue whale's infrasound calls travel extraordinary distances:

- **Low-frequency propagation.** Sound at 10-40 Hz propagates through seawater with minimal attenuation. The deep sound channel (SOFAR channel) at approximately 1,000 meters depth acts as a waveguide, trapping low-frequency sound and channeling it horizontally across the ocean basin.
- **Detection range.** Blue whale calls have been detected by hydrophone arrays at distances of 500-1,600 km (300-1,000 miles). A single blue whale can be "heard" (by instruments) across an entire ocean basin.
- **Before ship noise.** Before the age of commercial shipping (pre-1900), ambient ocean noise at low frequencies was much lower. Some researchers estimate that blue whales could have communicated across distances of 1,600+ km -- effectively across entire oceans. Modern ship noise has reduced this range significantly (the "acoustic smog" of the industrial ocean).
- **The loneliest frequency.** The blue whale's calls are so low that no other animal can hear them at the same frequencies with the same sensitivity. Blue whales speak a language that is, in some sense, exclusive to their species. The deepest voice on Earth.

(Sources: Stafford et al. 1999; Payne & Webb 1971; SOFAR channel acoustics; ocean noise research; McDonald et al. 2006)

We live on the same planet, in overlapping waters, and its voice is invisible to us. E=5 honors the power while acknowledging the inaudibility.

## RARE VISITOR TO WASHINGTON WATERS

### Distribution

The Blue Whale is a rare visitor to the Puget Sound region:

- **Open ocean habitat.** Blue whales inhabit deep, open ocean waters. They follow krill concentrations along continental shelf edges and in upwelling zones.
- **Eastern North Pacific population.** Estimated 1,600-2,500 animals. One of the larger remaining populations (global estimate: 5,000-15,000 animals, down from pre-whaling populations of 200,000+).
- **Washington outer coast.** Blue whales are occasionally detected acoustically off the Washington outer coast, particularly along the continental shelf edge. Visual sightings are rare.
- **Not a Puget Sound animal.** Blue whales do not enter Puget Sound. They are deep-ocean animals. Their presence in the engine reflects the wider PNW marine ecosystem -- the deep water beyond the Sound's boundaries.
- **Acoustic detection.** Most blue whale "sightings" in Washington waters are acoustic detections by NOAA hydrophone arrays. The whale is heard (by instruments) but not seen. Fitting for an infrasound animal.

(Sources: NOAA blue whale surveys; eastern North Pacific population estimates; acoustic detection programs; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Blue Whale

**A Calls (Long Moans):**
- **Frequency:** 15-20 Hz. Below human hearing.
- **Character:** Long, low-frequency moans. Steady pitch. Haunting if pitch-shifted into audible range.
- **Duration:** 15-20 seconds per call.
- **Source level:** Up to 188 dB re 1 uPa at 1 m.

**B Calls (Pulses):**
- **Frequency:** 10-18 Hz. Below human hearing.
- **Character:** Low-frequency pulses. Shorter. Often alternating with A calls.
- **Duration:** 1-5 seconds per pulse.

**D Calls (Downswept):**
- **Frequency:** 25-90 Hz. Partially audible to humans at the upper range.
- **Character:** Frequency-modulated downswept sounds. More variable.
- **Duration:** 1-4 seconds.

**Propagation:**
- **Distance:** Detectable at 500-1,600 km via hydrophone arrays.
- **Channel:** SOFAR deep sound channel acts as waveguide for low-frequency propagation.
- **Pre-industrial range:** Potentially communicating across entire ocean basins.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=5. The loudest animal sound on Earth (188 dB) -- but below human hearing. The paradox of inaudibility. Power without audibility. The absolute superlative, invisible to the ear.

(Sources: Cummings & Thompson 1971; McDonald et al. 2006; Stafford et al. 1999; blue whale acoustics; engine internal analysis)
