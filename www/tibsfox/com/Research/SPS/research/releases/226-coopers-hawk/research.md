## THE MEDIUM ACCIPITER -- *ACCIPITER COOPERII*

### Between the Goshawk and the Sharp-shin

The Cooper's Hawk occupies the middle position in the PNW accipiter trio:

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Accipiter*. Species: *cooperii* -- named for William Cooper (1798-1864), an American naturalist and founding member of the New York Lyceum of Natural History. The species was formally described by Charles Lucien Bonaparte in 1828.
- **Size.** Medium. The challenge of accipiter identification is that the Cooper's Hawk overlaps in size with both the Goshawk (from below, large females) and the Sharp-shinned Hawk (from above, small males). Females: 469-678 g (16.5-24 oz), body length 42-50 cm (17-20 in), wingspan 75-94 cm (29-37 in). Males: 220-410 g (7.8-14.5 oz). The overlap zones with the Sharp-shinned Hawk (large female Sharp-shin vs. small male Cooper's) constitute the classic identification problem in North American hawkwatching.
- **Appearance.** Adults: slate-gray above (slightly browner in some individuals), barred rufous and white below, with a dark cap darker than the back (diagnostic when visible), a rounded tail tip (vs. the Sharp-shinned Hawk's squarish or notched tail tip), and a prominent pale nape. The rounded tail is the most reliable field mark for separating Cooper's from Sharp-shinned Hawk. Red eye in adults. Juveniles: brown above, streaked below with thin, teardrop-shaped streaks (vs. the Sharp-shinned Hawk's broader streaks).
- **The identification challenge.** The Cooper's Hawk/Sharp-shinned Hawk identification problem is one of birding's most discussed challenges. Both species are similar in structure, overlapping in size, and often seen in flight at a distance where tail shape is the primary distinction. The identification difficulty has produced an extensive literature and is a standard test of birder expertise.

(Sources: Accipiter cooperii taxonomy; William Cooper namesake; Bonaparte 1828 description; size data; tail shape identification; Cooper's vs Sharp-shin ID problem; plumage details)

## THE CALL -- cak-cak-cak

### The Suburban Alarm

- **The alarm call.** The Cooper's Hawk's alarm call is a rapid, loud series: cak-cak-cak (also kek-kek-kek, or occasionally described as a rapid "kaah-kaah-kaah"). The notes are sharp and repeated quickly, particularly when the bird is disturbed near the nest. The call is similar in pattern to the Northern Goshawk (degree 225) but is faster, slightly higher in pitch, and smaller in acoustic volume -- as appropriate for a medium-sized hawk.
- **Frequency.** The alarm operates in the 2,000-5,000 Hz range -- higher than the Goshawk's alarm, reflecting the smaller body size (smaller vocal structures produce higher frequencies). The call's frequency carries well in both forest edge and suburban environments, where the acoustic landscape is more varied than in dense old-growth forest.
- **Vocal behavior.** The Cooper's Hawk is moderately vocal, most commonly at the nest during the breeding season. It is more vocal than the Northern Goshawk in the sense that it tolerates human proximity in suburban settings and calls more frequently in those contexts -- birders who study suburban Cooper's Hawks report regular calling behavior in breeding territories. The alarm call is the most commonly heard vocalization; the birds also produce a distinct "kuck" call associated with nest activity and mate interaction.
- **The suburban context of the call.** One of the distinctive aspects of the Cooper's Hawk's ecology is that its alarm call is now commonly heard in Seattle's suburbs and parks. A hawk alarm-calling at a backyard bird feeder -- because a Sharp-shinned Hawk or Cooper's Hawk is hunting the feeder's songbird visitors -- is a sound that many Seattle residents know, even if they cannot immediately identify the caller.
- **E=5.** The Cooper's Hawk's alarm call is E=5 -- the middle of the engine's range. The call is assertive and loud when aroused, but the bird is not the explosively aggressive entity that the Northern Goshawk is. The Cooper's Hawk will defend its nest, but in suburban environments it has habituated to human presence to a degree that the forest-interior Goshawk has not. E=5 captures the medium energy of a medium hawk in a medium-disturbance landscape.

(Sources: Cooper's Hawk alarm call; cak-cak-cak description; frequency analysis; suburban vocal behavior; kuck call; backyard feeder hunting context; E=5 assessment)

## ECOLOGY -- THE SUBURBAN RAPTOR

### Forest to Backyard

The Cooper's Hawk's most ecologically significant recent development is its successful colonization of North American cities and suburbs:

- **Historical habitat.** The Cooper's Hawk's ancestral habitat is deciduous and mixed woodland, riparian corridors, and forest edge -- open-forest or forest-edge environments where it could hunt birds in semi-open country. It was never a deep old-growth specialist like the Goshawk.
- **The suburban transition.** Over the past 40-50 years, Cooper's Hawks have established breeding populations in cities and suburbs across North America, including Seattle. The transition is one of the more remarkable wildlife adaptation stories of the late 20th century. Urban and suburban areas provide: (1) abundant prey in the form of House Sparrows, European Starlings, and songbirds visiting feeders; (2) reduced nest predation from great horned owls (which are less common in dense suburbs); (3) suitable nesting trees in parks, cemeteries, and residential areas with mature trees.
- **Breeding in Seattle suburbs.** Cooper's Hawks now breed regularly in Seattle's suburbs and parks. Volunteer Park, Seward Park, the University District, Bellevue, Kirkland, and Redmond all have documented Cooper's Hawk breeding populations. The birds use large ornamental trees -- pines, spruces, maples -- for nesting, and they hunt the bird feeders that suburban residents maintain. This creates the ironic situation where residents attract birds to their feeders specifically to be watched, and the Cooper's Hawk uses those concentrations as a reliable prey source.
- **The bird feeder dynamic.** Cooper's Hawks have learned that bird feeders attract concentrations of prey species. A feeder visited by House Finches, Juncos, and Mourning Doves is also a restaurant for a Cooper's Hawk. The hawk often approaches feeders by flying low through vegetation and then bursting into the feeder area at speed, scattering prey and attempting to catch one before it escapes. This behavior brings Cooper's Hawks into direct, visible contact with humans in ways that the forest-interior Goshawk rarely achieves.
- **The Goshawk-Cooper's division.** The two hawks partition space by habitat quality: the Goshawk requires the old-growth or mature forest interior; the Cooper's Hawk uses the forest edge, suburban parks, and modified landscapes. Together they cover the full gradient from wilderness core to suburban interface.

## PREY AND HUNTING

### The Bird Hunter

- **Primary prey.** The Cooper's Hawk specializes in birds, primarily medium-sized birds in the size range of thrushes, robins, starlings, and mourning doves. In urban environments, it takes House Sparrows, European Starlings, and similar introduced species. The hawk is physically built for bird pursuit: its relatively long wings (for an accipiter), long tail, and powerful talons are optimized for catching flying birds in semi-open or woody environments.
- **Hunting technique.** The Cooper's Hawk hunts by ambush and short pursuit. It approaches from behind cover (vegetation, buildings, fences), then launches a rapid pursuit of a flushed bird. The pursuit is fast, maneuverable, and typically brief -- if the hawk does not catch the bird quickly, it breaks off the chase. The Cooper's Hawk is not built for sustained aerial pursuit; it relies on surprise and short-range speed.
- **The accipiter stoop pattern.** The typical Cooper's Hawk attack sequence involves an approach at low altitude (often below fence or roofline height), followed by a rapid rise and stoop onto prey. The low approach minimizes detection; the speed of the final stoop maximizes success probability.
- **Occasional mammals.** While birds dominate the diet, Cooper's Hawks occasionally take small mammals -- chipmunks, mice, voles -- and rarely lizards or large insects. The bird prey specialization is robust but not absolute.
- **Prey size versus Goshawk.** The Cooper's Hawk takes smaller prey than the Goshawk. Where the Goshawk can pursue and catch jays, squirrels, and grouse, the Cooper's Hawk focuses on the thrush-to-dove size range. This prey size difference reduces competition between the two species that might otherwise occur in overlapping territories.

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST ECOLOGY AND DISTRIBUTION

### Year-Round Resident

- **PNW range.** The Cooper's Hawk breeds throughout western Washington -- in the foothills, lowlands, and suburban areas west of the Cascades, as well as in suitable areas east of the Cascades. The bird is one of the more commonly encountered raptors in lowland western Washington.
- **Breeding season.** Breeding occurs from April through July. The nest is a stick platform built in a live tree, typically in a fork or on a horizontal branch, often using conifers in the Pacific Northwest (Douglas fir, western red cedar) or large deciduous trees. Clutch size: 3-5 eggs. Incubation: 34-36 days. Fledging: approximately 30-34 days after hatching.
- **Year-round residency.** Unlike Sharp-shinned Hawks (which show more migratory movement) and unlike the Osprey (a true migrant), Cooper's Hawks in western Washington are largely resident year-round. Northern populations show more migratory tendency, but PNW lowland birds are present in all months.
- **The Puget Sound lowlands.** The lowland areas of Puget Sound -- the Snohomish, Skykomish, Green, Puyallup, and other river valleys -- provide excellent Cooper's Hawk habitat: mature trees, water features, abundant prey, and the suburban-forested interface the species prefers. The density of mature trees in Seattle's residential neighborhoods, particularly in neighborhoods with 1920s-era landscaping where street trees have grown to substantial size, supports breeding Cooper's Hawks at relatively high densities.
- **Migration at Cape Flattery and other hawkwatch sites.** During fall migration, Cooper's Hawks can be counted in significant numbers at Pacific Northwest hawkwatch sites. Cape Flattery, the San Juan Islands, and Chelan Ridge are among the sites where migrating raptors (including both resident and northern breeding Cooper's Hawks) concentrate along geographic features that redirect their southward movement.

(Sources: Cooper's Hawk PNW range; breeding season; stick nest in conifers; year-round residency; Puget Sound lowlands habitat; hawkwatch sites; Cape Flattery; Chelan Ridge)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### Recovered from DDT

- **Historical decline.** Like the Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222) and the Osprey (degree 224), the Cooper's Hawk experienced significant population declines from DDT in the mid-20th century. Fish-eating raptors were the most severely impacted, but bird-eating raptors also accumulated DDT through their prey. Cooper's Hawk populations declined substantially between the 1940s and early 1970s.
- **Recovery.** After the DDT ban in 1972, Cooper's Hawk populations recovered strongly. By the 1980s, numbers were rebounding; by the 1990s, the suburban colonization was well underway. The recovery coincided with the behavioral shift toward suburban breeding -- the post-DDT population expanding into new habitat types.
- **IUCN and US status.** Least Concern (IUCN). The US population is considered stable or increasing. The suburban colonization has contributed to overall population growth, as the species exploits a habitat resource that was largely unavailable to it before the suburban development of the 20th century.
- **Trichomonosis concern.** One current conservation concern for Cooper's Hawks (and Sharp-shinned Hawks) is the parasitic disease trichomonosis, transmitted through infected prey birds (particularly Mourning Doves and House Finches at feeders). The disease causes oral lesions that prevent feeding and can be fatal. Backyard feeder concentrations of prey birds increase the disease transmission risk. This is a contemporary conservation issue specific to the suburban hawk ecology that the Cooper's Hawk has developed.

(Sources: DDT historical decline; 1972 recovery; suburban colonization; IUCN Least Concern; population stable; trichomonosis; feeder disease transmission)
