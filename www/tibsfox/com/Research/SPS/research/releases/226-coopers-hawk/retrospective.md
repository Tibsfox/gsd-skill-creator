## What Worked

1. **The suburban colonization story is the most culturally accessible natural history fact in the accipiter trio.** Every Seattle resident who maintains a bird feeder has likely encountered a Cooper's Hawk or Sharp-shinned Hawk at that feeder. The suburban adaptation connects the abstract taxonomy to the immediate experience of the urban bird-watcher.
2. **The Goshawk-Cooper's habitat partition analysis is clean and ecologically precise.** Old-growth interior (Goshawk) vs. forest edge and suburban (Cooper's) -- the two species divide the habitat gradient between them, reducing competition. Documenting the partition gives the two consecutive accipiter entries a coherent ecological relationship.
3. **The DDT recovery thread continues.** Bald Eagle (221), Osprey (224), Cooper's Hawk (226) -- three consecutive birds of prey in the DDT recovery narrative. The thread is worth tracking. DDT recovery is one of the engine's implicit conservation success stories across the raptor arc.
4. **The Bonaparte 1828 species description is a specific historical anchor.** Charles Lucien Bonaparte (Napoleon's nephew, a serious ornithologist) formally described this species. The engine benefits from naming the people who did the taxonomic work.

## Discoveries

1. **The trichomonosis issue is the Cooper's Hawk's most contemporary and locally relevant conservation concern.** Feeder concentrations of songbirds transmit the parasite through infected individuals. The same suburban adaptation that enabled population recovery has created a new disease vector. This is a specific, actionable concern for Seattle bird feeder operators -- cleaning feeders regularly reduces risk.
2. **The rounded vs. square tail distinction is genuinely difficult in field conditions.** The ID problem is not a failure of observer attention -- it is a real overlap in morphology and size. The engine should document this honestly rather than suggesting the distinction is straightforward. It is not.
3. **The "kuck" call (associated with nest activity) is distinct from the alarm call.** Most field guides focus on the alarm. The kuck call, heard at active nests in breeding season, is a different vocalization that experienced observers recognize. The engine documents the alarm but acknowledges the fuller vocal repertoire.
