Cooper's Hawk

E = 5 Accipiter cooperii Accipitridae
ACCIPITER TRIO DEGREE 2 — THE SUBURBAN ACCIPITER. Cooper's Hawk is the middle accipiter — between the apex Goshawk (degree 225) and the smallest Sharp-shin (degree 227). It is the accipiter that Seattle residents are most likely to encounter: breeding in suburban parks, hunting at bird feeders, perching on fence posts. The forest hawk that negotiated human-modified landscapes.

The Backyard Predator

The Cooper's Hawk (Accipiter cooperii) is the accipiter that Seattle knows best. Over the past 40-50 years, Cooper's Hawks have established breeding populations in North American cities and suburbs — a remarkable wildlife adaptation story. They nest in mature ornamental trees in Seattle's residential neighborhoods. They hunt the bird feeders that suburban residents maintain. They appear at backyard windows and along fences in Bellevue, Kirkland, and Redmond. The forest hawk came to town.

The alarm call — cak-cak-cak-cak, rapid and emphatic — is familiar to Seattle birders as the sound of a hawk disturbed at the nest or approaching a feeder hunting ground. The call is similar in pattern to the Goshawk's (degree 225) but faster, higher-pitched, and scaled to a medium body and medium territory.

"Cooper's Hawks have learned that bird feeders attract concentrations of prey. A feeder attracting house finches and juncos is also a restaurant for a Cooper's Hawk."

The ID Problem

The Cooper's Hawk/Sharp-shinned Hawk identification challenge is one of birding's most discussed puzzles. Both are similar in structure (short wings, long tail, barred underparts) and overlapping in size. The primary field mark: Cooper's Hawk has a rounded tail tip; Sharp-shinned Hawk has a square or notched tail tip. In flight at distance, this distinction requires experience and favorable viewing conditions. The overlap zone (large female Sharp-shin vs. small male Cooper's) defeats even experienced birders in poor conditions.

Breeding in Seattle

Urban Raptor

Volunteer Park, Seward Park, U-District, Bellevue, Kirkland: all documented breeding sites. Mature ornamental conifers and deciduous trees used for nesting.

Prey Specialization

Accipitridae

Thrush-to-dove size range: robins, starlings, mourning doves, house sparrows. Ambush-and-short-pursuit hunting. Low approach, rapid rise and stoop.

Year-Round Resident

PNW

Western Washington lowland birds are largely resident year-round. Northern populations more migratory. PNW lowland = stable year-round presence.

Goshawk Partition

Ecology

Goshawk (225) = mature forest interior. Cooper's Hawk = forest edge, suburban, modified landscape. Two accipiters, complementary habitat use.

DDT Recovery and Current Concerns

Like the Bald Eagle and Osprey, Cooper's Hawks experienced DDT-related declines in the mid-20th century. After the 1972 DDT ban, populations recovered strongly, with the suburban colonization contributing to overall population growth. The contemporary concern is trichomonosis — a parasitic disease transmitted through infected prey at backyard feeders. Feeder concentrations of prey birds increase transmission risk. The same suburban adaptation that enabled population growth has introduced a new disease pressure.
