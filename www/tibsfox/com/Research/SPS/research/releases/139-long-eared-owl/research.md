## E=4 -- THE MEASURED PUMPING

### Why E=4

The Long-eared Owl's deep hooting registers at E=4 -- measured, spaced, and persistent:

- **Deep frequency:** The Long-eared Owl's hoot is a single, low-frequency "whooo" note, repeated at regular intervals (approximately every 2-4 seconds). The fundamental frequency is 300-400 Hz -- deep, resonant, and penetrating. The sound is felt as much as heard.
- **Spaced delivery:** Unlike the Barred Owl's 8-note phrase or the GHO's multi-note series, the Long-eared Owl delivers single notes with long pauses between them. This spaced delivery creates a meditative, persistent rhythm -- hoot... pause... hoot... pause... -- that can continue for extended periods.
- **Moderate amplitude:** The hoot is moderately loud -- audible at 200-300 meters in quiet conditions -- but does not project with the power of the Great Horned Owl or the intensity of the Barred Owl caterwauling. The sound is designed for dense vegetation communication, not long-range broadcasting.
- **Nocturnal only:** The Long-eared Owl is strictly nocturnal in its vocalizing -- more so than the Barred Owl, which calls during the day. The deep pumping hoots emerge only after dark, adding to the species' secretive reputation.
- **E=4 consistency:** The Long-eared Owl's E=4 matches the GHO solo hoot (135) and the Barred Owl hooting (137) -- measured, deliberate territorial vocalizations. E=4 is the baseline energy for owl territorial hooting in the engine.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Marks et al. 1994; engine internal analysis)

## THE MOST SECRETIVE OWL IN THE PNW

### Cryptic Roosting and Nocturnal Habits

The Long-eared Owl is arguably the **most difficult owl to find** in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Cryptic roosting:** During the day, Long-eared Owls roost in **dense vegetation** -- thick conifers, tangled deciduous thickets, vine-covered trees. They press their bodies against tree trunks, elongate their silhouettes by raising their ear tufts and compressing their feathers, and become nearly invisible against the bark. Their mottled brown plumage provides excellent camouflage.
- **Strict nocturnality:** Long-eared Owls are almost exclusively nocturnal. They emerge from their roosts only after full dark and return before first light. During the day, they remain motionless in their roosting positions, sometimes for 12-14 hours. This strict nocturnality means most people never see them.
- **Silent flight:** Like all owls, Long-eared Owls fly silently (fringed flight feathers eliminate air turbulence noise). But the Long-eared Owl's silence is particularly pronounced -- combined with nocturnal habits and cryptic roosting, it moves through the forest without detection.
- **Underdetection:** The Long-eared Owl is almost certainly more common than surveys suggest. Its secretive habits make it extremely difficult to detect using standard survey methods. Dedicated nocturnal listening surveys during the breeding season reveal far more Long-eared Owls than daytime searches.
- **"Ghost owl":** The Long-eared Owl has been called a "ghost owl" -- present but invisible. In some PNW forests, Long-eared Owls may be roosting within meters of trails without any human awareness of their presence.

### Communal Winter Roosts

One of the Long-eared Owl's most remarkable behaviors is **communal winter roosting**:

- **Dense aggregations:** During winter, Long-eared Owls congregate in communal roosts -- sometimes 20-50 or more birds in a single stand of dense conifers or deciduous trees. These roosts are among the most spectacular (and least-known) wildlife spectacles in the PNW.
- **Site fidelity:** The same roost sites may be used for decades. The owls return to the same stands of trees year after year, sometimes for generations. The knowledge of roost sites is passed from older to younger birds.
- **Pellet evidence:** Communal roosts are often detected not by seeing the owls but by finding accumulations of owl pellets (regurgitated indigestible material) beneath roost trees. A dense carpet of pellets beneath a stand of dense conifers is the signature of a Long-eared Owl communal roost.
- **Social function:** The communal roost may serve multiple functions: predator defense (many eyes watching), thermoregulation (shared body heat in cold conditions), and information exchange (following more successful hunters to feeding areas).

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Marks et al. 1994; Wiggins et al. 2006)

## THE EAR TUFTS -- NOT EARS

### Display Feathers, Not Auditory Structures

The Long-eared Owl's "long ears" are not ears at all:

- **Feather tufts:** The prominent ear tufts that give the species its common name are clusters of feathers that can be raised or lowered. They are not connected to the auditory system and have no hearing function.
- **Display and communication:** The ear tufts serve as visual signals. When raised, they make the owl appear larger and more alert -- potentially deterring predators or signaling alertness to other owls. When lowered, the owl appears rounded and less conspicuous.
- **Cryptic function:** During cryptic roosting, the Long-eared Owl raises its ear tufts to their maximum height. Combined with the compressed body, elongated posture, and bark-matching plumage, the raised ear tufts make the owl resemble a broken tree branch. This is camouflage at its most sophisticated.
- **Actual ears:** The real ears of the Long-eared Owl are asymmetrically placed on the sides of the head (one higher than the other), hidden beneath the facial disc feathers. This asymmetry allows precise sound localization in three dimensions -- the owl can pinpoint prey location by sound alone, even in total darkness.
- **The confusion:** The common name "Long-eared Owl" is thus misleading -- the "ears" are not ears. The scientific name *Asio otus* also references the ear tufts: *otus* from Greek "eared." The Short-eared Owl (*Asio flammeus*, degree 140) is named for its much smaller, barely visible ear tufts.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Marks et al. 1994; Sibley 2014)

## WINTER VISITOR STATUS IN THE PNW

### Seasonal Patterns

The Long-eared Owl's status in the PNW is complex:

- **Winter visitor:** In many parts of western Washington and Oregon, the Long-eared Owl is primarily a winter visitor, arriving in October-November and departing by March-April. Winter birds come from breeding populations further north (British Columbia, Alaska) or from the interior (east of the Cascades).
- **Breeding resident:** East of the Cascades, in drier open forests and grassland-forest mosaics, the Long-eared Owl is a year-round resident and breeder. Breeding habitat in the PNW interior includes ponderosa pine forest edges, juniper woodlands, and riparian corridors.
- **Western Washington status:** In the Puget Sound lowlands, the Long-eared Owl is rare and primarily a winter visitor. Breeding records in western Washington are scarce. The species is far less common than the Barred Owl or Great Horned Owl in the Puget Sound area.
- **Detection challenges:** The species' secretive habits mean its true status is uncertain. Long-eared Owls may breed in western Washington more commonly than records suggest, simply because they are so difficult to find.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Wiggins et al. 2006; WDFW)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Deep, Persistent Pumping in the Night

The Long-eared Owl's acoustic contribution is **the most minimal and meditative** of the Strigidae entries:

- **Single-note hoot:** A deep, low "whooo" -- a single note, not a phrase. The fundamental frequency is approximately 300-400 Hz. The note is pure, resonant, and clean -- without the hoarse quality of the Barred Owl or the booming power of the Great Horned Owl.
- **Pumping rhythm:** The hoots are delivered at regular intervals (every 2-4 seconds), creating a slow, persistent pulse: whooo... whooo... whooo... This pumping rhythm can continue for many minutes. The steady cadence has a hypnotic quality.
- **Low amplitude:** The hoot carries 200-300 meters in quiet conditions but does not project as far as the GHO or Barred Owl. The sound is designed for close-range territorial and mate communication in dense vegetation.
- **Breeding season only:** The deep pumping hoot is primarily a breeding-season vocalization (February-June). Outside the breeding season, Long-eared Owls are largely silent -- adding to their undetectable reputation.
- **Other vocalizations:** Besides the deep hoot, Long-eared Owls produce a variety of other sounds during breeding: a catlike "waow" alarm call, a female begging call, and nestling begging sounds. But the deep pumping hoot is the primary territorial vocalization.
