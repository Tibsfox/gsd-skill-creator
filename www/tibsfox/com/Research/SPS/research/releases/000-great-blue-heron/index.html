Great Blue Heron

Ardea herodias · subspecies fannini (Pacific Northwest resident)
Stalking Silence · Pterodactyl Croak · Puget Sound Estuary · Energy 1
Before there is sound, there is the discipline of silence, and no creature in the Pacific Northwest embodies that discipline more completely than the Great Blue Heron. The circle begins here.

Taxonomy & Physical Description

The Great Blue Heron is the largest heron in North America and one of the most immediately recognizable wading birds on the continent. It belongs to the family Ardeidae (herons, egrets, and bitterns) within the order Pelecaniformes. Adults stand 3.2–4.5 feet tall with a wingspan of 5.5–6.6 feet and a body weight of 4.6–5.5 pounds — a bird built for patience, not speed.

The bird at degree 0° is Ardea herodias fannini — the Pacific Northwest resident subspecies described by Chapman in 1901. Unlike the nominate A. h. herodias, the fannini subspecies is non-migratory, exhibits darker overall plumage, and averages slightly smaller in body size. It is a year-round resident of the Pacific coast from southeast Alaska to Puget Sound. It does not leave. It is always present, always foundational.

Bioacoustics & Sound Profile

The Silence Is the Sound

The Great Blue Heron is defined more by its silence than by any call it produces. A stalking heron stands motionless in shallow water for minutes to hours, the embodiment of structured absence. This silence is not passive — it is active predatory strategy. The entire organism is optimized for stillness. In compositional terms, the heron’s stalking silence is analogous to a whole rest in music: not the absence of sound, but the structured, intentional use of non-sound as a compositional element.

John Cage’s 4′33″ (1952) argued that silence is not empty but filled with incidental sound that becomes the composition. The heron’s hunt makes the same argument — except it has been making it for roughly 1.8 million years.

Primary Vocalizations

When the Great Blue Heron does vocalize, the result is startling precisely because it breaks the silence. The calls are low-pitched, harsh, and unmistakably prehistoric:

  • Flight call (“frawnk”): A deep, hoarse, guttural croak uttered in flight or when flushed. Frequency: 200–600 Hz fundamental with harmonics to 1.5 kHz. Carries over 500 meters across open water.
  • Landing call (“roh-roh-roh”): A series of low-frequency clucks delivered upon arriving at the nest. Frequency: 150–500 Hz, rhythmically spaced at 0.3–0.5 second intervals.
  • Bill clapping: Non-vocal mechanical percussion produced by snapping the mandibles. Sharp, broadband transient below 2 kHz. The body is the instrument.
  • Nestling begging: High-pitched repetitive calls at 1–3 kHz from chicks in the nest.

Colony Acoustics

In stark contrast to solitary stalking silence, heronries are among the noisiest avian habitats in the Pacific Northwest. A large colony produces continuous, layered polyphonic texture — landing calls, bill clapping, nestling begging, wing flaps, and alarm calls triggered by eagle overflights. The same species that embodies silence individually produces overwhelming textural density socially. The solo heron is a John Cage rest; the heronry is a Charles Ives tone cluster.

Ecology & Behavior in Puget Sound

Stand-and-Wait Predation

The Great Blue Heron is a stand-and-wait predator — a strategy that maximizes energy efficiency at the cost of time. The bird wades into shallow water, assumes a motionless posture with neck coiled, and waits. When prey moves within strike range (~50 cm), the neck uncoils and the bill drives through the water surface in less than 0.1 seconds. Diet in Puget Sound: small fish (sculpins, gunnels, perch, juvenile salmonids), Dungeness crab, shore crab, shrimp, and occasionally small mammals.

The Kiwanis Ravine Heronry

The most significant urban heronry in the Puget Sound region is in Kiwanis Ravine within Seattle’s Discovery Park — approximately 80–100 active nests. Nesting begins in February, with chicks fledging by June–July. The colony has experienced significant disturbance from Bald Eagle predation, a well-documented predator-prey dynamic monitored by Seattle Audubon Society and WDFW.

Year-Round Residency

The fannini subspecies does not migrate. The maritime climate of the Salish Sea keeps water temperatures above freezing year-round, providing continuous access to prey. This year-round presence is why the Great Blue Heron occupies degree 0° — the species that is always present, always foundational, always the first thing you see when you walk to the water’s edge in any month.

Music Theory Mapping

The heron’s stalking silence maps directly to the concept of rests in music notation. A whole rest is not nothing — it is structured absence. It occupies time. It shapes the music around it. John Cage’s 4′33″ formalized this principle: silence is impossible; the act of framing silence reveals the sounds already present.

At the heronry, the landing call triggers a predictable response from the mate — call-and-response in its most ancient biological form. This is the foundational structure underlying jazz, gospel, West African drumming, and the ii-V-I chord progression.

The heron’s 200–800 Hz range occupies the bass register — the frequency range that provides harmonic foundation in orchestration. Degree 0° IS the root note. Quincy Jones at degree 0° of Part A is also the foundational voice — the arranger who builds the harmonic bed. Both are bass. Both are foundation.
