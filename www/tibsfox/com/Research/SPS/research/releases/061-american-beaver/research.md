## THE FIRST MAMMAL

This is the most significant taxonomic event in the history of the Sound of Puget Sound engine.

For sixty-one degrees -- from degree 0 (Great Blue Heron) through degree 60 (Pied-billed Grebe) -- every species in the engine has been a bird. Class Aves. Herons, wrens, thrushes, chickadees, owls, sparrows, warblers, finches, swallows, hummingbirds, woodpeckers, jays, dippers, pigeons, kingfishers, ducks, grebes -- sixty-one consecutive avian entries spanning nine or ten orders, twenty-six families, and the full ecological breadth of the Pacific Northwest's birdlife. The engine has been, for its entire existence, an ornithological project masquerading as a general soundscape.

The American Beaver changes everything.

*Castor canadensis* is the **first mammal** to enter the engine. Class Mammalia. Not a bird. Not a variation on a bird. Not another order within Aves. A fundamentally different class of vertebrate -- warm-blooded like birds, but with fur instead of feathers, teeth instead of beaks, mammary glands instead of crop milk, a placental reproductive strategy instead of eggs. The engine, for the first time, crosses the boundary between two classes of animal life.

This is not merely a new order or a new family. Those have been arriving regularly -- four new orders in the five degrees from 56 to 60 alone. This is a new **class**. The last time the engine admitted a new class was degree 0, when the Great Blue Heron established Class Aves as the engine's foundation. For sixty-one degrees, that class boundary has been the engine's invisible wall. Every species, no matter how different from every other species -- the tiny Anna's Hummingbird and the massive Great Blue Heron, the soaring Steller's Jay and the submerging Pied-billed Grebe -- has been a bird. The American Beaver is the first species that is NOT a bird.

Five firsts arrive simultaneously:

1. **FIRST MAMMAL** -- Class Mammalia enters the engine
2. **NEW CLASS: Mammalia** -- only the second class in the engine (after Aves at degree 0)
3. **NEW ORDER: Rodentia** -- the largest mammalian order (2,200+ species globally), entering the engine for the first time
4. **NEW FAMILY: Castoridae** -- the beaver family, with only two living species worldwide (*Castor canadensis* in North America and *Castor fiber* in Eurasia)
5. **THE TAIL SLAP** -- the first non-vocal, non-avian acoustic element in the engine. A percussive warning signal, not a vocalization.

## Taxonomy: The Dam Builder

### Classification

- **Class:** Mammalia (**NEW CLASS** -- only the 2nd class in the engine, after Aves at degree 0)
- **Order:** Rodentia (**NEW ORDER** -- the largest mammalian order, ~2,200 species worldwide)
- **Family:** Castoridae (**NEW FAMILY** -- only 2 living species in the entire family)
- **Genus:** *Castor* (Linnaeus, 1758)
- **Species:** *Castor canadensis* (Kuhl, 1820)
- **Common Name:** American Beaver

### Etymology

*Castor* -- from the Greek *kastor* (beaver), itself possibly derived from a Sanskrit root meaning "to shine" or "to excel" [1, 2, 5]. The word also gives us "castoreum," the oily secretion from the beaver's castor sacs, historically used in perfumery and medicine. The Greek mythological figure Castor (one of the Dioscuri, the Gemini twins) may share the etymological root, though the connection is debated. In any case, the genus name is ancient -- Linnaeus adopted it directly from classical usage. Beavers were known to Greek and Roman naturalists, and the name has been in continuous use for over two millennia.

*canadensis* -- "of Canada," reflecting the type specimen's origin in the vast boreal forests of what is now Canada [1, 2, 5]. The species epithet places the beaver geographically: North America's beaver, distinguished from the Eurasian beaver (*Castor fiber*). Heinrich Kuhl formally described *Castor canadensis* as a distinct species in 1820, separating it from the European beaver.

The full binomial *Castor canadensis* translates simply to **"beaver of Canada"** -- direct, geographic, taxonomic. Compare the previous degree's tautological *Podilymbus podiceps* ("rump-footed rump-foot") and the progression of naming styles across the aquatic sequence:

- **Degree 58:** *Aix sponsa* -- "bridal waterfowl" (mythological, aesthetic)
- **Degree 59:** *Anas platyrhynchos* -- "flat-billed duck" (functional, anatomical)
- **Degree 60:** *Podilymbus podiceps* -- "rump-footed rump-foot" (tautological, anatomical)
- **Degree 61:** *Castor canadensis* -- "beaver of Canada" (geographic, direct)

The naming philosophy shifts again: from anatomy back to geography. The beaver is not named for what it looks like or what its body does. It is named for where it lives.

The common name "beaver" derives from Old English *beofor*, from Proto-Germanic *\*bebruz*, ultimately from a Proto-Indo-European root *\*bhebhrus* meaning "brown" or possibly "the brown animal" [5]. The beaver has been "the brown one" in European languages for thousands of years -- a name so old it predates recorded history.

### A New Class: Mammalia

The engine's most significant taxonomic event. Mammalia -- the class of mammals -- enters the engine at degree 61 for the first time [1, 2, 5]. Every previous species (degrees 0-60) has been Class Aves. The beaver is the first Mammalian entry.

The distance between Aves and Mammalia is vast. These two classes diverged approximately 320 million years ago, in the Carboniferous Period, when the amniote lineage split into synapsids (which became mammals) and sauropsids (which became reptiles and eventually birds). Birds and mammals share warm-bloodedness (endothermy), but they arrived at it independently -- one of the most spectacular examples of convergent evolution in the history of life. The feathered wing and the furred paw have been on separate evolutionary trajectories for over 300 million years.

What does it mean for the engine to cross this boundary? It means the Sound of Puget Sound is no longer a bird project. It is now a multi-class soundscape engine. The acoustic elements will no longer be exclusively vocalizations produced by avian syringes. They will include mammalian sounds produced by entirely different anatomical structures -- and in the beaver's case, not a vocalization at all but a percussive impact.

### A New Order: Rodentia

Rodentia is the largest order of mammals, comprising approximately 2,200 species -- roughly 40% of all mammalian species on Earth [1, 2, 5]. The order includes mice, rats, squirrels, chipmunks, porcupines, capybaras, guinea pigs, and beavers. The defining characteristic is the pair of continuously growing incisors in both the upper and lower jaw, which must be kept sharp through gnawing.

The American Beaver is the largest rodent in North America and the second-largest rodent in the world (after the capybara). It enters the engine as the representative of an order that contains more species than all the avian orders currently in the engine combined. Rodentia alone has more species diversity than Passeriformes.

### A New Family: Castoridae

Castoridae is one of the smallest mammalian families in terms of living species: **only two** [1, 2, 5]. *Castor canadensis* (the American Beaver, found throughout North America) and *Castor fiber* (the Eurasian Beaver, found across northern Eurasia). That is the entire family. Two species, two continents, two beavers.

But the family's fossil record is far richer. Castoridae once included species ranging from small burrowing rodents to *Castoroides ohioensis*, the giant beaver of the Pleistocene -- an animal that stood over a meter tall at the shoulder, weighed approximately 100 kg, and had incisors 15 cm long [1, 5]. Giant beavers went extinct approximately 10,000 years ago, likely due to climate change and habitat loss at the end of the last Ice Age. The family that once included megafauna now contains just two modest-sized species.

The contrast with the previous degree's family is instructive: Podicipedidae (grebes) has approximately 22 living species. Castoridae has 2. The beaver family is an evolutionary lineage approaching its minimum viable diversity.

## Physical Description: The Ecosystem Engineer

The American Beaver is a large, semi-aquatic rodent -- the largest rodent in North America and one of the most immediately recognizable mammals in the Pacific Northwest [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].

### General Appearance

- **Size:** Head-body length 74-90 cm (29-35 in). Tail length 20-35 cm (8-14 in). Total length approximately 90-120 cm (3-4 ft). Weight 11-35 kg (25-75 lbs), with exceptional individuals exceeding 40 kg [1, 2, 5, 7]. This is by far the largest species in the engine. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0/14) weighs 2-3 kg. The Mallard (degree 59) weighs 0.75-1.6 kg. The beaver outweighs every previous species by an order of magnitude.
- **Body:** Stocky, barrel-shaped, with short legs and a low center of gravity. Built for swimming and carrying heavy loads (branches, mud) rather than speed or agility on land. The body is compact and muscular, designed to work.
- **Fur:** Dense, luxurious, waterproof. Two layers: a fine, soft underfur that traps air for insulation and waterproofing, and longer, coarser guard hairs that protect the underfur [1, 2, 5]. The fur is dark brown to reddish-brown. This is the fur that nearly drove the species to extinction -- beaver pelts were the foundation of the North American fur trade from the 1600s through the 1800s.
- **Tail:** The diagnostic feature. **Broad, flat, oval, scaly, and virtually hairless** -- approximately 20-35 cm long and 13-15 cm wide [1, 2, 5, 7]. Unlike any other mammalian tail. It serves multiple functions: swimming rudder, fat storage, thermoregulation (blood vessels in the tail radiate heat), communication prop (used for the tail slap), and structural support (the beaver leans on its tail like a tripod when standing on hind legs to fell trees). The tail is the acoustic instrument of degree 61.
- **Incisors:** Large, prominent, and **orange** [1, 2, 5]. The distinctive orange color comes from iron-rich enamel that makes the front surface harder than the back, creating a self-sharpening chisel edge as the beaver gnaws. The incisors grow continuously throughout life at approximately 4-5 cm per year and must be worn down by constant use. These teeth can fell trees 30 cm in diameter.
- **Feet:** Hind feet are large and **fully webbed** -- powerful swimming paddles [1, 2, 5]. Front feet are smaller, unwebbed, and dexterous -- used for manipulating branches, carrying mud, and grooming. The second toe of the hind foot has a split claw used as a grooming comb for maintaining the waterproof fur.
- **Eyes and Ears:** Small. Clear nictitating membranes protect the eyes underwater. The ears and nostrils have valves that close when submerged [1, 2, 5]. The beaver can remain underwater for up to 15 minutes, though typical dives last 3-5 minutes.
- **Castor Sacs:** Both sexes have paired castor sacs near the base of the tail that produce castoreum, an oily, pungent secretion used for scent marking territory [1, 2, 5]. Castoreum has been used by humans for centuries in perfumery and traditional medicine.

### Sexual Dimorphism

The American Beaver shows **minimal sexual dimorphism** [1, 2, 5] -- males and females are similar in size and appearance, with females sometimes slightly larger. Both sexes build dams, maintain lodges, and raise kits. This continues the pattern from degree 60 (Pied-billed Grebe, minimal dimorphism) and contrasts with degrees 58-59 (Wood Duck and Mallard, extreme dimorphism). The beaver's world is a world of shared labor, not display.

## The Tail Slap: 62nd Acoustic Element

### The Sound

The American Beaver's acoustic contribution to the engine is the **tail slap** -- a sharp, explosive, percussive crack produced by slamming its broad, flat tail against the water surface [1, 2, 3, 5, 7].

The mechanics are straightforward: the beaver raises its tail above the water, arches its back, and brings the tail down flat and hard against the surface. The broad, heavy tail strikes the water like a paddle hitting a drum skin. The result is a **loud, sharp crack** -- a single percussive report that echoes across the pond, audible at distances of several hundred meters in still conditions.

This is not a vocalization. The beaver does not produce this sound with its larynx or any vocal organ. The tail slap is a **percussive warning signal** -- sound produced by physical impact, not by air flowing through biological sound-producing structures. It is the first non-vocal acoustic element from a non-avian species in the engine.

### Acoustic Properties

- **Frequency:** Broadband impulse, approximately 100-5,000 Hz with peak energy in the 200-1,500 Hz range [1, 2]. The sound is not tonal -- it is a sharp transient, like a gunshot or a hand clap, containing energy across a wide frequency range simultaneously.
- **Duration:** Extremely brief -- less than 0.1 seconds for the impact itself. The total event (splash plus reverberations) may last 0.5-1.0 seconds.
- **Amplitude:** E=4 -- loud, sharp, attention-grabbing. Audible at 200-500 meters across calm water. The crack is startling to anyone who has not heard it before. In quiet evening conditions, a beaver tail slap can be one of the loudest sounds on the pond.
- **Pattern:** Usually a single slap, sometimes repeated 2-3 times in quick succession if the threat persists. After the slap, the beaver typically dives and does not resurface for several minutes.

### What the Tail Slap Means

The tail slap is a **warning signal**, not a territorial display [1, 2, 5]. It communicates one message: **danger**. When a beaver detects a potential predator -- a coyote on the bank, a person on the trail, an unfamiliar movement -- it slaps its tail on the water before diving. The slap serves three simultaneous functions:

1. **Warning to other beavers** -- family members within earshot know to dive and take cover. Beavers are social animals that live in family groups; the tail slap is a collective alarm.
2. **Warning to the predator** -- the loud crack says "I see you." Predators that rely on ambush (coyotes, mountain lions) lose their advantage once detected. The tail slap announces that the ambush has failed.
3. **Startle response** -- the sharp crack and accompanying splash can momentarily startle a predator, buying the beaver time to dive.

### The 62nd Acoustic Element: The Tail Slap

The beaver's tail slap becomes the **62nd acoustic element** in the species-side progression -- and it breaks new ground in multiple dimensions:

1. **First non-vocal element from a non-avian species.** The engine has documented percussive sounds before -- the Pileated Woodpecker's drumming (degree 54), the Red-breasted Sapsucker's irregular drumming (degree 21/22). But those are avian percussion. The tail slap is mammalian percussion -- a fundamentally different class of organism producing sound through physical impact.

2. **First pure percussive warning signal.** Previous acoustic elements have been songs, calls, or territorial displays. The tail slap is a single-purpose alarm. It says one thing: danger. No territory, no mating, no identity -- just warning.

3. **First broadband impulse.** Most acoustic elements in the engine are tonal -- they have identifiable pitch, frequency, and melodic character. The tail slap is a broadband impulse: a sharp crack containing energy across the full audible spectrum. It has no pitch. It has no melody. It is a percussive event, like a snare drum hit.

4. **First aquatic percussion.** The tail strikes water, not wood. The medium shapes the sound: the water surface absorbs some energy and reflects the rest, creating a sharp crack followed by a brief splash. The reverberation across a still pond gives the tail slap a spatial quality that tonal vocalizations lack.

## Ecosystem Engineering: The Dam Builder

### The Only Species That Builds Its Own Habitat

The American Beaver is the **only species in the engine that physically reshapes its environment** [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. Every other species -- all sixty previous entries -- lives in habitat that exists independently of its presence. Birds nest in trees they did not grow, sing from branches they did not create, wade in ponds they did not build. The beaver builds the pond.

This is the concept of **ecosystem engineering**: an organism that creates, modifies, or destroys habitats through its own physical activity [1, 2, 5]. The beaver is the textbook example -- arguably the most consequential ecosystem engineer on Earth after humans. The beaver changes the physical structure of the landscape through three primary activities:

### 1. Dam Building

Beavers construct dams across streams and creeks using logs, branches, mud, rocks, and any available material [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. A beaver dam impounds water, converting a flowing stream into a still pond. The engineering is surprisingly sophisticated:

- **Site selection:** Beavers prefer narrow, shallow stream sections where a dam can be anchored to both banks. They select sites where the gradient allows maximum pond area for minimum dam height.
- **Materials:** Primarily branches and small trees felled by the beaver, interwoven and packed with mud and stones. Beavers can fell trees up to 30 cm in diameter (occasionally larger) using their iron-reinforced incisors.
- **Maintenance:** Dams are continuously maintained. The sound of running water triggers repair behavior -- beavers will pack mud and branches into any breach that allows water to flow through. This instinctive response to the sound of flowing water is one of the most reliable behavioral triggers in mammalian biology.
- **Scale:** Individual dams range from a few meters to hundreds of meters in length. The longest known beaver dam, in northern Alberta, is approximately 850 meters long -- visible from satellite imagery.

### 2. Pond Creation

The dam creates the pond. And the pond creates habitat for dozens of other species -- including species already in the engine [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]:

- **Wood Ducks (degree 58)** nest in tree cavities beside beaver ponds. The standing dead trees (snags) created by beaver-pond flooding provide nesting cavities. Without the beaver, many of these cavity sites would not exist.
- **Mallards (degree 59)** breed on beaver ponds. The still water, vegetated margins, and rich invertebrate communities of beaver ponds are prime Mallard habitat.
- **Pied-billed Grebes (degree 60)** swim in beaver ponds. The emergent vegetation that grebes require for nesting often grows in the shallow margins of beaver impoundments. Beaver ponds with cattails and bulrushes are ideal grebe habitat.
- **Belted Kingfishers (degree 57)** fish in beaver ponds. The still, clear water of a mature beaver pond is perfect plunge-diving habitat -- the kingfisher can see fish below the surface and strike from an overhanging branch.
- **Great Blue Herons (degree 0/14)** wade in beaver-pond margins. The shallow edges of beaver ponds, with their rich fish and amphibian populations, are classic heron foraging habitat.

The beaver is the **cause** of the habitats that previous species in the engine occupy. Degree 61 arrives and reveals that the pond ecosystem documented across degrees 57-60 was, in many cases, literally built by the beaver. The ecosystem architect arrives after the species it enables.

### 3. Tree Felling

Beavers fell trees for food (the cambium layer beneath the bark) and for construction material (branches and logs for dams and lodges) [1, 2, 5]. A beaver can fell a tree 15 cm in diameter in approximately 30 minutes. The felling creates:

- **Canopy gaps** that allow sunlight to reach the forest floor, stimulating understory growth
- **Downed wood** that provides habitat for insects, amphibians, and small mammals
- **Stumps and standing dead wood** that woodpeckers (degrees 21, 22, 54) use for foraging and nesting
- **Browse zones** where regenerating shrubs provide food for deer, elk, and other herbivores

### 4. Lodge Construction

Beavers build **lodges** -- dome-shaped structures of branches, logs, and mud, typically located in the center of the pond [1, 2, 5, 7]. The lodge has underwater entrances that provide predator protection. The interior chamber, above the waterline, is insulated by the mass of wood and mud -- interior temperatures remain above freezing even when exterior temperatures drop well below zero. The lodge is a feat of natural architecture: a heated, predator-proof home accessed only through underwater tunnels.

### The Keystone Species

The American Beaver is the classic **keystone species** -- an organism whose impact on its environment is disproportionately large relative to its abundance [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. Remove the beaver, and the dam fails. Remove the dam, and the pond drains. Remove the pond, and the standing water habitat disappears. The ducks leave. The grebes leave. The kingfishers leave. The herons leave. The wetland plants die. The fish die. The amphibians lose their breeding pools. An entire ecosystem collapses because one species was removed.

This is what happened across North America during the fur trade era. Before European contact, an estimated 60-400 million beavers inhabited North America [1, 2, 5]. By 1900, after three centuries of intensive trapping, the population had been reduced to approximately 100,000. The loss of beavers meant the loss of millions of beaver ponds. Streams that had been dammed for centuries reverted to free-flowing channels. Wetlands dried. Water tables dropped. Entire valley bottoms that had been productive wetland complexes became degraded stream corridors.

The beaver's recovery in the 20th and 21st centuries -- thanks to trapping regulations, reintroduction programs, and habitat protection -- has reversed some of this damage. Restored beaver populations create restored wetlands. The species literally rebuilds the landscape.

## The Beaver in the Pacific Northwest

### Historical Context

The Pacific Northwest was one of the epicenters of the beaver fur trade [1, 2, 3, 5]. The Hudson's Bay Company and the North West Company competed fiercely for beaver pelts throughout what is now Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia from the late 1700s through the mid-1800s. The fur brigade system -- teams of trappers working systematically through entire watersheds -- was designed specifically to harvest beaver pelts, which were shipped to Europe for the felt hat industry.

The most devastating episode was the Hudson's Bay Company's deliberate "fur desert" policy in the Columbia River watershed, in which HBC Governor George Simpson ordered the systematic trapping of every beaver in the region between the Columbia River and Mexican California [2, 5]. The goal was explicitly to create a "fur desert" -- a zone so depleted of valuable fur-bearing animals that American settlers would have no economic incentive to move into the territory. The policy was effective: beavers were nearly extirpated from large sections of the Pacific Northwest by the 1840s.

### Recovery

Beaver populations in western Washington have recovered substantially since the early 20th century [1, 2, 3, 7]. The species is now common on lowland streams, rivers, and wetlands throughout the Puget Sound lowlands. Key recovery factors include:

- **Legal protection:** Trapping regulations that limit harvest seasons and methods
- **Habitat availability:** Abundant riparian habitat on lowland streams
- **Urban adaptation:** Beavers have adapted to suburban and urban waterways, living on creeks and rivers within city limits

### Urban Beavers

One of the most remarkable aspects of the PNW beaver population is its presence in urban areas [3, 7]. Beavers are documented on:

- **Piper's Creek** (Carkeek Park, Seattle) -- within Seattle city limits, in a park surrounded by residential neighborhoods
- **Thornton Creek** (Seattle) -- a heavily urbanized watershed where beavers have established territories
- **Duwamish River** (Seattle/Tukwila) -- the most industrialized river in Puget Sound, yet beavers persist
- **Sammamish River/Slough** (Bothell/Redmond) -- suburban creeks with active beaver populations
- **Mill Creek** (various locations) -- small suburban streams where beaver dams create neighborhood-scale ponds

The urban beaver challenges assumptions about wildlife and cities. This is not a species that retreats from human development. It moves in, builds dams on suburban creeks, floods trails, fells ornamental trees, and creates wetland habitat in the middle of residential neighborhoods. The management challenge is real -- beaver dams can flood roads and properties -- but so is the ecological benefit. Urban beaver ponds support more species diversity than the degraded urban streams they replace.

Four degrees, three orders, **two classes**, four fundamentally different relationships with water:

1. **Wood Duck (58)** -- sits on the surface of still ponds, dabbles at the surface. The surface specialist.
2. **Mallard (59)** -- tips forward to feed below the surface, uses every pond. The generalist.
3. **Pied-billed Grebe (60)** -- dives completely below the surface, hunts underwater. The diver.
4. **American Beaver (61)** -- **builds the pond itself**. The engineer.

The sequence reveals a hierarchy: the duck sits on the pond, the grebe dives in the pond, and the beaver *made* the pond. The architect arrives last, after the tenants. The ecosystem builder enters after the species it enables -- the most structurally revelatory ordering the engine has produced.

### The Aquatic Quartet vs. the Aquatic Trilogy

The previous retrospective (degree 60) named degrees 58-59-60 "the aquatic trilogy." At degree 61, the trilogy becomes a **quartet** -- and the addition changes the meaning of the sequence. The trilogy was about aquatic strata: surface, subsurface, submersion. The quartet adds a fourth dimension: **creation**. The beaver does not merely occupy the water. It creates the water. The aquatic sequence now reads: use the surface, use the water, live in the water, *build the water*.

## The Beaver and Prior Species: Ecological Connections

The American Beaver's ecosystem engineering connects it to more prior engine entries than any previous species [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]:

### SPS-060: Pied-billed Grebe

The Pied-billed Grebe nests on floating platforms anchored to emergent vegetation. Beaver ponds create the exact conditions grebes need: still water with emergent cattails and bulrushes growing in the shallow margins of impounded water. Many PNW grebe territories exist because a beaver built a dam upstream. The diving bird of degree 60 swims in the pond that the mammal of degree 61 constructed.

### SPS-059: Mallard

Mallards breed in virtually any freshwater habitat, but beaver ponds are disproportionately productive Mallard habitat. The rich invertebrate communities, abundant aquatic vegetation, and sheltered margins of beaver ponds support higher Mallard breeding density than unimpounded stream channels. The ubiquitous generalist benefits from the engineer's work.

### SPS-058: Wood Duck

Wood Ducks nest in tree cavities beside water. Beaver dams flood riparian forests, killing trees and creating **snags** -- standing dead trees with natural cavities. These snags are prime Wood Duck nesting habitat. The cavity nester of degree 58 depends on the dam builder of degree 61 for nesting sites. The relationship is specific and documented: Wood Duck populations are positively correlated with beaver pond density.

### SPS-057: Belted Kingfisher

Kingfishers plunge-dive for fish in still, clear water. Beaver ponds provide exactly this: calm water where fish are visible from above. The kingfisher's hunting strategy requires the dam builder's product. Many kingfisher territories along small streams are centered on beaver ponds -- the only sections of the stream with water deep and still enough for plunge-diving.

### SPS-014/000: Great Blue Heron

Great Blue Herons wade in shallow water, catching fish and amphibians. Both are ecosystem engineers. Both create more habitat than they consume. The engine now has two infrastructure creators: one avian (the woodpecker), one mammalian (the beaver).

## E=4 Analysis: The Evening Crack

The American Beaver is rated E=4 -- common and acoustically present, but primarily crepuscular and nocturnal [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. The energy level reflects the species' temporal pattern: beavers are most active at dusk and dawn, and the tail slap is most commonly heard in the evening hours when beavers emerge to forage and encounter perceived threats.

## Duwamish/Coast Salish Territorial Acknowledgment

The streams, rivers, and wetlands where the American Beaver builds dams and lodges throughout the Puget Sound lowlands are the ancestral and current homelands of the **Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Suquamish, Tulalip, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, and other Coast Salish peoples** [3, 7]. These peoples have coexisted with beavers on these waterways for millennia. Coast Salish peoples recognized the beaver as a powerful shaper of the landscape and managed beaver populations as a resource -- for fur, for castoreum, and for the wetland habitats that beaver activity created. The ecological relationship between Indigenous peoples and beavers in the Pacific Northwest predates European contact by thousands of years. The beaver ponds that sustain the species documented in this engine exist within a landscape of deep Indigenous knowledge and management.
