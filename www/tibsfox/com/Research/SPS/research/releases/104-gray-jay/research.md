## THE GRAY JAY -- *Perisoreus canadensis* -- THE CAMP ROBBER

### The Species

The Gray Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*), renamed **Canada Jay** by the American Ornithological Society in 2018, is a medium-sized corvid of subalpine and boreal forests [1, 2]:

- **Body length:** 25-33 cm.
- **Wingspan:** 45 cm.
- **Weight:** 62-82 g.
- **Plumage:** Gray above, lighter gray to whitish below. Dark nape and partial dark cap. White forehead. No crest. Fluffy, round-headed appearance.
- **Distribution:** Boreal and subalpine forests across Canada and the northern/montane United States. In the PNW, found in subalpine forests of the Cascade Range, Olympic Mountains, and other high-elevation areas.

### The Friendliest Corvid

The Gray Jay is the **friendliest wild bird** most PNW hikers will encounter [1, 2]:

- **Approaches humans** without hesitation. Lands on outstretched hands, pack frames, tent poles.
- **Steals food** from campsites, day packs, and picnic tables with practiced efficiency.
- **"Camp robber"** -- the most common colloquial name, reflecting the bird's reputation among backcountry travelers.
- **"Whiskey jack"** -- from the Algonquin word *wisakedjak*, a trickster spirit in Indigenous mythology. The name reflects the bird's cunning and boldness.
- **Intelligence, not tameness.** The Gray Jay has learned that humans are a reliable food source in remote mountain environments. The approach behavior is a calculated strategy, not domestication.

### The Name Change

The species was known as "Gray Jay" from 1957 until 2018, when the American Ornithological Society restored the historic name **"Canada Jay"** (*Perisoreus canadensis*). The restoration aligned the common name with the scientific name and with the pre-1957 tradition. Both names remain in common use. This research uses "Gray Jay" as the primary name with "Canada Jay" noted.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; [2] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## THE SOUND: SOFT WHISTLES + MIMICRY -- THE QUIET CORVID

### The Vocal Repertoire

The Gray Jay's vocal repertoire is **softer and more varied** than any other corvid in the quintet [1, 2]:

**Soft whistles:** The primary vocalization. Gentle, clear, tonal whistled notes -- sometimes ascending, sometimes descending, always soft. Used for contact between pair members and family groups. The antithesis of the Steller's Jay's harsh alarm.

**Chuckling:** Quiet, rapid, low-pitched series of soft notes. Social function. Given during close interactions within the family group.

**Mewing:** Low-pitched, slightly nasal calls. Longer range than the whistle. Used for contact when birds are more widely spaced.

**Mimicry:** The Gray Jay mimics other species -- hawks, other corvids, forest birds -- though less conspicuously than the Steller's Jay. Mimicry is learned and culturally transmitted.

**Alarm:** Harsher notes given when threats are detected, but still quieter and less penetrating than the Steller's Jay's shack-shack-shack.

## FOOD CACHING -- SURVIVAL ARCHITECTURE IN THE SUBALPINE

### The Caching Strategy

The Gray Jay stores **thousands of food items per year** using a specialized technique [1, 2]:

- **Sticky saliva:** The bird produces a specialized mucous from enlarged salivary glands. This sticky saliva acts as an adhesive, allowing food items to be glued to tree bark -- a unique caching method among corvids.
- **Bark caches:** Food items (insects, berries, mushrooms, seeds, meat scraps) are pressed into bark crevices and held in place by the saliva adhesive. The caches are distributed throughout the bird's territory.
- **Spatial memory:** The Gray Jay relocates cached items months later, using spatial memory to navigate thousands of scattered cache sites.
- **Late-winter breeding:** The Gray Jay breeds in **February-March** -- earlier than almost any other songbird in the PNW. This extraordinary breeding schedule is possible only because of the cached food supply. The bird feeds nestlings with cached food when the subalpine forest is still buried in snow.

### Caching Comparison

| Species | Degree | Caching Method | Storage Medium |
|---------|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Common Raven | 100 | Ground caches | Soil, leaf litter |
| American Pika | 74 | Hay piles | Dried vegetation on talus |
| Gray Jay | 104 | Bark caches | Tree bark with saliva adhesive |

Three different caching strategies in three different habitats. All three species are adapted to survive PNW winters through food storage. The Gray Jay's bark-caching with sticky saliva is the most unusual method.

## SUBALPINE FOREST HABITAT

The Gray Jay occupies the **high-elevation conifer belt** [1]:

- **Habitat:** Mountain hemlock, subalpine fir, whitebark pine, Engelmann spruce. Typically above 1,200 meters in the PNW Cascades.
- **Elevational niche:** Above the Steller's Jay's lowland conifer territory (degree 103) and below the American Pika's alpine talus (degree 74). The Gray Jay is the corvid of the subalpine -- the high-elevation specialist.
- **Year-round resident:** Does not migrate. Survives the harshest winters of any songbird in the PNW through food caching.
- **Climate sensitivity:** The Gray Jay is increasingly threatened by climate change -- warmer autumn temperatures cause cached food to spoil before winter consumption, reducing breeding success. The species is a climate-change sentinel for subalpine ecosystems.

### Subalpine Community

The Gray Jay shares its subalpine habitat with the **American Marten** (degree 65) -- both year-round residents of the high-elevation conifer belt. The marten is a potential predator of Gray Jay nests; both species depend on intact subalpine forest.
