## Structural Event 2: The Camp Robber -- Human-Approaching Corvid Intelligence

- **Situation:** The Gray Jay approaches humans without hesitation, steals food, and lands on outstretched hands. Known as "camp robber" and "whiskey jack."
- **Root Cause:** The Gray Jay has learned that humans are a reliable food source in remote mountain environments. The behavior is intelligent strategy, not tameness.
- **Recommendation:** Document the camp-robber behavior. Note the Algonquin origin of "whiskey jack" (wisakedjak, trickster spirit). The friendliest corvid in the engine.

## Structural Event 3: Food Caching with Sticky Saliva

- **Situation:** The Gray Jay stores thousands of food items per year by gluing them to tree bark with specialized sticky saliva. This enables late-winter breeding (February-March) in the subalpine.
- **Root Cause:** Enlarged salivary glands produce mucous adhesive. Spatial memory enables relocation months later. The caching strategy is the foundation of the bird's survival in the harshest environment any songbird endures year-round in the PNW.
- **Recommendation:** Document the sticky-saliva caching as unique among corvids. Note the late-winter breeding schedule. Note climate-change vulnerability (warm autumn = spoiled caches).
- **Carry Item:** CONFIRMED -- Food caching. Sticky saliva. Late-winter breeding. Climate sentinel. Track.

## Structural Event 4: Subalpine Forest Habitat

- **Situation:** The Gray Jay occupies the high-elevation conifer belt -- above the Steller's Jay's lowland conifer territory and below the American Pika's alpine talus.
- **Root Cause:** The Gray Jay is adapted to subalpine conditions through food caching, dense feathering, and non-migratory year-round residency.
- **Recommendation:** Document the subalpine niche. Connect to American Marten (65, subalpine shared habitat) and American Pika (74, alpine neighbor). Note the elevational gradient: Steller's Jay (lowland conifer) -> Gray Jay (subalpine conifer) -> Pika (alpine talus).

### Research Process

1. **The soft-whistle acoustic is the correct center.** The Gray Jay's quiet, conversational vocalizations provide maximum contrast with the Steller's Jay's raucous alarm at degree 103. The quintet resolves from volume to intimacy.
