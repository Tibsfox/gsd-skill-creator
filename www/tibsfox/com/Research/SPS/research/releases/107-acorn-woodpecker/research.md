## ACORN WOODPECKER -- *Melanerpes formicivorus* -- THE COLONIAL WOODPECKER

### The Species

The Acorn Woodpecker (*Melanerpes formicivorus*) is a medium-sized woodpecker of oak woodlands in western North America [1, 2]:

- **Body length:** 20-23 cm.
- **Wingspan:** 35-43 cm.
- **Weight:** 65-90 g.
- **Plumage:** Bold "clown face" -- white face and forehead bordered by black, with a bright red crown patch. Black back and wings with white rump and wing patches. White breast with black streaking. Black tail.
- **Distribution:** Western North America from Oregon to Colombia. In the PNW, found in oak woodlands of southern Oregon at the northern edge of range.

### The Clown Face

The Acorn Woodpecker has one of the most distinctive facial patterns of any North American bird [1, 2]:

- **White face** -- bright white forehead and face, giving the bird its "clown" appearance.
- **Black border** -- the white is bordered by glossy black on the crown, nape, and throat.
- **Red crown** -- a bright red patch on the crown. Males: red meets white forehead directly. Females: black band separates red from white.
- **Unmistakable** -- in oak woodland, the clown-faced woodpecker is instantly identifiable. No other PNW species has this pattern.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; [2] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## THE SOUND: WAKA-WAKA-WAKA -- THE COLONIAL WOODPECKER

### The Vocal Repertoire

The Acorn Woodpecker's primary vocalization is a loud, raucous **waka-waka-waka** [1, 2]:

**Waka-waka-waka:** The diagnostic call. A rapid series of nasal, laughing notes -- waka waka waka waka -- delivered at a fast, clownish pace. Social and frequent. Given constantly within colonial groups. The call carries across open oak woodland.

**Contact calls:** Shorter waka or ka notes used for close-range communication between colony members. Softer and less emphatic than the full waka-waka-waka series.

**Alarm calls:** Harsher, more emphatic versions of the waka call. Given when predators (especially acorn-thieving jays and squirrels) approach the granary.

**Drumming:** Territorial drumming on resonant wood, including granary trees and utility poles. The drumming is social and colonial -- multiple birds may drum simultaneously.

## GRANARY TREES -- THOUSANDS OF DRILLED HOLES

### The Architecture

The Acorn Woodpecker's granary tree is one of the most remarkable structures in North American ornithology [1, 2, 3]:

- **Structure:** A tree (typically oak, pine, or utility pole) drilled with **thousands of individually sized holes**, each storing a single acorn. The holes are custom-fitted -- each acorn is wedged tightly to prevent theft by squirrels and jays.
- **Scale:** A single granary tree may hold **50,000 or more acorns**. Large granaries may contain hundreds of thousands of holes accumulated over decades of colonial use.
- **Maintenance:** The entire colonial group maintains the granary. As acorns dry and shrink, they are moved to smaller holes. New holes are drilled as needed. The granary is a **living structure** maintained across generations.
- **Community infrastructure:** Old granary trees are used by successive colonial groups. The infrastructure outlasts individual birds. Other species (woodpeckers, squirrels, insects) may also use granary holes.

### Granary vs. Nutcracker Caching

| Strategy | Species | Degree | Type | Scale |
|----------|---------|--------|------|-------|
| Dispersed | Clark's Nutcracker | 105 | Thousands of scattered caches | Spatial memory |
| Concentrated | Acorn Woodpecker | 107 | Single granary tree | Colonial defense |

Two food-caching strategies, both at massive scale. The nutcracker relies on spatial memory to retrieve dispersed caches. The Acorn Woodpecker relies on colonial defense to protect a concentrated granary. Different evolutionary solutions to the same problem: surviving winter.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; [2] Koenig & Mumme 1987; [3] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## COLONIAL LIVING -- COOPERATIVE BREEDING

The Acorn Woodpecker is one of the most social woodpeckers in the world [1, 2]:

- **Colony size:** Groups of 2-15+ individuals, typically 3-7 adults plus juveniles.
- **Cooperative breeding:** Multiple males and females may breed in the colony. All members help raise young. Helpers at the nest -- non-breeding individuals assist with incubation, feeding, and nest defense.
- **Granary defense:** The colony cooperatively defends its granary against acorn-thieving competitors (Steller's Jays, Western Scrub-Jays, squirrels).
- **Territory:** The colony defends a territory containing the granary tree(s) and feeding areas. Territorial conflicts with neighboring colonies can be intense.
- **Social intelligence:** Complex social hierarchy with coalitions, alliances, and competition for breeding positions. When a breeding vacancy opens, fierce "power struggles" erupt as groups of same-sex birds from other colonies compete for the position.

### Colonial Parallel

The Acorn Woodpecker's colonial system parallels the **Yellow-bellied Marmot** (degree 75) -- both live in colonies with shared infrastructure (granary trees / burrow systems), cooperative defense, and social hierarchies. Two colonial species from entirely different taxonomic groups, both using community-infrastructure strategies.

(Sources: [1] Koenig & Mumme 1987; [2] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## OAK SAVANNA HABITAT

The Acorn Woodpecker occupies **oak savanna** and oak woodland [1]:

- **Habitat:** Open, park-like oak woodland. Requires oaks for acorn production and suitable granary trees. Also uses mixed oak-pine woodland.
- **Geographic distribution in PNW:** **Southern Oregon** -- the Rogue Valley and surrounding oak woodlands at the northern edge of the species' range. Uncommon in the PNW. The oak savanna habitat in southern Oregon is limited and declining.
- **Acorn dependence:** The Acorn Woodpecker's range is defined by oak distribution. Without oaks, there are no acorns, and without acorns, there is no Acorn Woodpecker. The relationship is obligate.
- **Year-round resident:** Does not migrate. The granary's stored acorns provide winter food, eliminating the need for migration. The granary is the survival strategy.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)
