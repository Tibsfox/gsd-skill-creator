## THE SAN JUAN CHANNEL CONTEXT

### Critical Summer Foraging Habitat

The San Juan Channel is the body of water between San Juan Island and the surrounding islands and mainland:

- **Geography.** The San Juan Channel separates San Juan Island (where the town of Friday Harbor is located) from Lopez Island, Shaw Island, and the surrounding archipelago. It connects Haro Strait (to the west, adjacent to Canadian waters) with the Strait of Juan de Fuca (to the south). The channel is deep, cold, and tidal -- with strong current flows that concentrate marine life.
- **Chinook salmon concentration.** Chinook salmon returning to rivers and streams in the Puget Sound watershed, the Fraser River (Canada), and other systems pass through the San Juan Channel and adjacent waters. The concentration of Chinook in this corridor during summer months makes the San Juan Channel one of the most critical foraging habitats for the Southern Resident Killer Whale.
- **Visibility.** The San Juan Channel and adjacent waters are among the best locations in the world to observe killer whales from shore. Lime Kiln Point State Park on San Juan Island ("Whale Watch Park") is a terrestrial observation point from which the Southern Residents are frequently visible during summer foraging. The park is equipped with hydrophones that allow visitors to hear the orcas' vocalizations in real time.
- **Research history.** Ken Balcomb's Center for Whale Research, based on San Juan Island, has conducted continuous photo-identification studies of the Southern Resident Killer Whale community since 1976 -- the longest-running cetacean population study in the world. Every individual Southern Resident is individually identified by dorsal fin shape and saddle patch markings. The Center's database is the foundation of SRKW population monitoring.

(Sources: San Juan Channel geography; Chinook salmon migration routes; Lime Kiln Point State Park; Center for Whale Research Ken Balcomb; photo-identification studies)

## THE WHISTLE SYSTEM

### Social Communication at Close Range

Whistles are the Southern Residents' short-range social communication system:

- **What whistles are.** Whistles are tonally pure (single-frequency) or slightly frequency-modulated vocalizations -- smooth, musical sounds that contrast with the more complex frequency contours of the discrete calls. They are produced in the sound-producing structures (phonic lips) in the nasal region, the same structures that produce discrete calls and clicks.
- **Frequency range.** SRKW whistles typically range from approximately 1,000 Hz to 18,000 Hz. They are most prominent in the mid-frequency range (3,000-12,000 Hz) where the SRKW vocal apparatus produces them most efficiently.
- **Function.** Whistles function at short range -- typically within a few body lengths. They appear to be primarily affiliative: used between individuals in close proximity to maintain social bonds, coordinate movement within a subgroup, and facilitate mother-calf communication. Mother and calf develop individually distinctive whistle patterns (signature whistles, analogous to those studied in bottlenose dolphins) that maintain contact.
- **Signature whistles.** Research suggests that individual Southern Residents develop individually distinctive whistle patterns -- "signature whistles" -- that function as individual acoustic identifiers. The concept was established more firmly in bottlenose dolphins but has been documented in killer whales as well. A calf's signature whistle develops through interaction with its mother and pod mates.
- **Flexibility.** Unlike the stereotyped discrete calls, whistles are more flexible and variable. They can be modulated continuously and are used in a more context-sensitive way. The whistle system is more similar to what we think of as "expressive" communication -- the tone and contour carry emotional or contextual information.

(Sources: SRKW whistle research; signature whistles bottlenose dolphins Tyack; orca whistle function; mother-calf communication cetaceans; frequency-modulated whistles)

## THE ECHOLOCATION CLICK SYSTEM

### Sensory Infrastructure of the Marine World

Echolocation is the SRKW's primary sensory system for detecting prey and navigating:

- **How echolocation works.** The Southern Resident produces trains of high-frequency clicks using the phonic lips (also called "monkey lips" or "museau de singe") in the nasal region. The clicks are focused through the melon (the oil-filled structure in the forehead) into a directional beam. When the beam strikes an object, the returning echo is detected by the highly sensitive lower jaw (the mandible, which contains acoustic fat that conducts sound to the middle ear). The SRKW brain processes the timing, frequency, and intensity of the returning echo to construct a three-dimensional acoustic image of the environment.
- **Click frequency.** SRKW echolocation clicks are broadband pulses spanning from approximately 10,000 Hz to 80,000+ Hz. The peak energy is typically in the 20,000-60,000 Hz range -- well above human hearing (which tops out at approximately 20,000 Hz). The high frequency is necessary for resolution: shorter wavelengths resolve smaller objects more precisely.
- **Foraging clicks.** During salmon-hunting, the SRKW produces rapid click trains directed toward prey. The click rate increases as the distance to prey decreases -- a phenomenon also observed in bats during insect interception. Near prey, click rates can exceed 500 clicks per second.
- **Resolution.** The SRKW echolocation system can detect a salmon-sized object at ranges of 100-200 meters or more under optimal conditions. The system discriminates fish species, fish size, and fish orientation (facing toward or away from the orca) -- all information relevant to prey selection and interception strategy.
- **Click trains in social context.** Click trains are not only used for foraging. SRKW produce "codas" -- patterned click trains used in social contexts -- that may function similarly to the discrete calls. The boundary between echolocation clicks and social click patterns is an active area of research.

(Sources: Odontocete echolocation mechanics; melon acoustic lens; mandibular fat pad acoustic reception; SRKW click frequency Aroyan et al.; foraging click trains; salmon detection by echolocation)

## THE THREE ACOUSTIC SYSTEMS

### A Complete Acoustic Portrait

Degree 270 documented the discrete call system; degree 271 completes the portrait with whistles and clicks:

- **System 1: Discrete calls (degree 270).** Stereotyped, culturally transmitted, pod-specific. Social coordination at range. Pod identity. Repertoire of 12-20+ call types per pod. Most studied element of SRKW acoustics.
- **System 2: Whistles (degree 271, first part).** Flexible, individually variable, short-range. Affiliative. Mother-calf contact. Signature whistle development. Emotional and contextual communication.
- **System 3: Echolocation clicks (degree 271, second part).** Broadband, high-frequency, sensory. Prey detection and navigation. Not primarily social but has social uses (codas). The sensory infrastructure of marine life.

The three systems are complementary: discrete calls project identity and social information at range; whistles maintain close-range social bonds; clicks provide the sensory information necessary to find and catch food. Together, they constitute one of the most sophisticated acoustic repertoires of any non-human animal.

(Sources: SRKW three-system acoustic portrait; discrete calls vs. whistles vs. clicks; acoustic complexity in odontocetes; comparative cetacean communication)

## ENDANGERED -- THE NUMBERS AT DEGREE 271

### The Crisis Continues

- **~73-75 individuals.** Carried forward from degree 270. The number does not change. The three pods continue: J, K, L.
- **Birth rate and mortality.** For the Southern Resident population to recover, birth rates must exceed death rates. In recent years, birth rates have been insufficient for recovery: stillbirths, failed pregnancies (documented through hormonal analysis of fecal samples), and infant mortality have all been documented. A healthy female orca can reproduce approximately every 3-5 years; under nutritional stress, reproduction is suppressed.
- **The acoustic disturbance factor.** Boat noise at degree 271 is specifically relevant to the echolocation system: increased ambient noise forces the Southern Residents to increase click amplitude (Lombard effect in echolocation) and may reduce the effective range of their sonar. If the echolocation range is reduced by noise, the Southern Residents must work harder to find the Chinook salmon they depend on -- increasing energy expenditure during an already nutritionally stressful period.
- **PCB contamination.** Southern Residents have high levels of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) -- persistent organic pollutants that accumulate in the fatty tissue of long-lived apex predators. PCBs are immunosuppressive and disrupt reproductive hormones. The contamination is transmitted through the Chinook salmon they eat and is not easily cleared from the body.

(Sources: SRKW birth rate data; hormonal analysis fecal samples; Lombard effect echolocation; acoustic disturbance SRKW; PCB contamination orca; reproductive suppression nutritional stress)

## THE SAN JUAN ISLAND RESEARCH COMMUNITY

### Where the Science Happens

The San Juan Island scientific community has been the foundation of SRKW research:

- **Center for Whale Research (Ken Balcomb, 1976-present).** The longest-running wild cetacean population study. Every SRKW individual is photo-identified. The annual census provides the population data used by NOAA and all researchers. Balcomb's documentation of the SRKW population provides the 73-75 individual figure.
- **Orca Network.** A volunteer network of whale watchers and researchers that reports SRKW sightings in real time, providing distribution data that complements Center for Whale Research population monitoring.
- **Lime Kiln Point hydrophones.** The shore-based hydrophones at Lime Kiln Point State Park allow public acoustic monitoring of the SRKW. The data is used by researchers and provides real-time acoustic records of vocalizations.
- **NOAA Northwest Fisheries Science Center.** The primary federal research institution for SRKW science, developing the population models and recovery plans required under the ESA.
- **The research community's significance.** The SRKW is one of the most heavily studied wild animal populations on earth. The quality of the science makes the failure to recover the population more striking: we know what the orcas need (Chinook salmon, clean water, acoustic space), we know why they are not recovering, and we have not yet succeeded in providing those conditions.

(Sources: Center for Whale Research; Ken Balcomb; Orca Network; Lime Kiln Point hydrophones; NOAA NWFSC SRKW research)
