Whistles — The Social Fabric

Whistles are the Southern Residents’ short-range social communication system — tonally pure, flexible, expressive. Used between individuals in close proximity: mother and calf, socializing adults, coordinating movement within a subgroup. Individual Southern Residents develop signature whistles — individually distinctive acoustic patterns that function as personal identifiers, like a name.

Unlike the stereotyped discrete calls (degree 270), whistles are flexible and variable. They can be modulated continuously. The contour and tone carry emotional or contextual information. The whistle system is the SRKW’s most expressive acoustic channel.

Clicks — Seeing With Sound

Echolocation clicks are the Southern Residents’ sensory infrastructure. High-frequency broadband pulses (10,000–80,000+ Hz) produced through phonic lips, focused through the melon, received through the acoustic fat of the lower jaw. The returning echo constructs a three-dimensional acoustic image of the environment — seeing with sound.

During salmon hunting: click trains accelerate as distance to prey decreases. At close range, click rates exceed 500 per second. The SRKW echolocation can detect a Chinook salmon at 100–200+ meters, discriminate species, size, and orientation. Boat noise reduces the effective range — forcing the orcas to work harder to find food during an already nutritionally stressed period.

The San Juan Channel

The San Juan Channel separates San Juan Island from Lopez Island, Shaw Island, and the surrounding archipelago. It connects Haro Strait to the Strait of Juan de Fuca, with strong tidal currents that concentrate marine life. Chinook salmon returning to their spawning rivers pass through the San Juan Channel — making it one of the most critical foraging habitats for the Southern Residents during the critical summer months.

From Lime Kiln Point State Park (“Whale Watch Park”) on San Juan Island, the Southern Residents are frequently visible during summer foraging. Shore-based hydrophones transmit their vocalizations in real time to visitors. The San Juan Island scientific community — Ken Balcomb’s Center for Whale Research (1976–present), the longest-running wild cetacean population study — has documented every individual SRKW since 1976.

Three Acoustic Systems -- Complete Portrait

Degree 270 (discrete calls): Stereotyped, culturally transmitted, pod-specific. Social coordination at range. Pod identity. 12-20+ call types per pod.

Degree 271 (whistles + clicks): Whistles -- short-range, flexible, affiliative, signature-developing. Clicks -- echolocation, prey detection, navigation, also used in social "codas."

Together: one of the most sophisticated acoustic repertoires of any non-human animal. Three complementary systems serving three different functions -- long-range social identity, short-range social bonding, and sensory navigation. The acoustic portrait is complete across two consecutive engine degrees.
