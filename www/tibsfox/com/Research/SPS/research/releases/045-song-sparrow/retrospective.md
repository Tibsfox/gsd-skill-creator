## Purpose

This is the forty-sixth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-45), the first Passerellidae, the first *Melospiza*, the engine's first CSV substitution species, and the degree that restores E=2 after 31 degrees of absence. *Melospiza melodia* -- "melody song-finch," the bird whose name means "song song" -- enters the engine as the most studied songbird in North America, carrying five structural events: a new family enters (Passerellidae, the 19th), E=2 returns (breaking seven consecutive E=4), wetland-edge habitat arrives (the engine's first water-margin species), the CSV substitution is transparently documented, and the acoustic progression gains its first positionally-defined element.

Five structural contributions:

1. **The preamble -- the 46th acoustic element (species side).** Every Song Sparrow song opens with 2-4 clear, deliberate introductory notes before launching into a complex body of trills and buzzy phrases. Named "the preamble" for three resonances: the song's two-part architecture (clear opening announces complexity), Nice's studies as the preamble to modern behavioral ecology, and the engine at degree 45 (12.5%, the preamble to the next phase). The first acoustic element defined by **formal positional structure** -- meaningful because of where it occurs in the sequence, not what it sounds like in isolation.

2. **First Passerellidae -- 19th family in the engine.** New World sparrows enter the engine for the first time. Passerellidae was split from Emberizidae by the AOS in 2017. Approximately 140 species in 30 genera. The Song Sparrow is the family's type example -- the most common, most studied, most vocal representative. The engine adds its first true sparrow.

3. **E=2 restored after 31 degrees of absence.** The last E=2 was degree 14 (MacGillivray's Warbler). Seven consecutive E=4 across degrees 38-44 compressed the engine's dynamic range to its most severe. E=2 returns at degree 45, dropping two energy levels and restoring the lower register. The engine has dynamic range again.

4. **Wetland-edge habitat -- the engine reaches the water margin.** The Song Sparrow is the engine's first true wetland-edge species. Pond margins, marsh edges, riparian thickets -- the transitional zone where water meets land. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) stood in the water. The Song Sparrow stands beside it. The engine spans from deep forest to water's edge.

5. **CSV substitution documented.** The original CSV had Pacific Chorus Frog at degree 45. Because the engine is birds-and-mammals only, Song Sparrow replaces it, preserving the original's E=2 energy and wetland/pond margin habitat. The substitution is transparently documented in the research, a precedent for future CSV departures.

## What Worked

### 1. The preamble captures a genuinely new acoustic concept
No previous element described the formal opening statement of a complex passage. The mosaic (degree 44) was defined by what the song contains. The preamble is defined by where the song begins. This is a shift from content-based to structure-based acoustic description -- a new category in the progression. The Song Sparrow's 2-4 clear introductory notes are one of the most recognizable sounds in North American birding precisely because of their positional consistency. The preamble is always first.

### 2. E=2 restoration addresses the engine's most severe dynamic deficit
After 31 degrees without E=2 and seven consecutive E=4, the dynamic range compression was the engine's most severe ongoing structural problem after the marine species crisis. Song Sparrow's E=2 breaks the streak and restores a level that hasn't been heard since the first quarter of the engine. The CSV substitution specifically preserved E=2 from the original, making this a deliberate structural repair.

### 3. Passerellidae is the right family to introduce here
After five Fringillidae (seed specialists), the transition to Passerellidae (omnivore-generalists) is both taxonomically appropriate and ecologically meaningful. The Song Sparrow is the most common, most studied, most recognized member of the family -- the natural first entry. It establishes the Passerellidae baseline the way the MacGillivray's Warbler established the Parulidae baseline.

### 4. The CSV substitution is handled transparently
The research explicitly documents the original CSV species (Pacific Chorus Frog), the reason for substitution (birds-and-mammals only), and the preserved structural elements (E=2, wetland/pond margin). This sets a clean precedent for any future CSV departures. The substitution is not hidden -- it is a structural event.

### 5. The Margaret Morse Nice connection enriches the species beyond acoustics
Nice's story adds a human dimension -- a scientific outsider who changed a discipline from her backyard using the most common bird available. This resonates with the engine's broader theme of paying attention to what is present and abundant, not just what is rare and spectacular. The Song Sparrow is the "default" bird. Nice showed that the default bird is the most interesting one if you pay attention long enough.

### 6. Wetland-edge habitat fills a real gap
The engine has covered deep forest, canopy, aerial, lawn, and weedy open ground. It had not placed a species at the water's edge since the Great Blue Heron at degree 0 -- and the heron was in the water, not beside it. The Song Sparrow's wetland-edge habitat is a genuine new ecosystem type that opens the engine to pond, marsh, and riparian species that have been absent.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. EXISTENTIAL CRISIS: No marine species in 45 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
**FORTY-FIVE DEGREES.** The crisis deepens. The water has not spoken in forty-five degrees of a project named for Puget Sound. This is the engine's most severe structural deficit. The Song Sparrow reaches the water's EDGE but does not enter the water. ABSOLUTE, NON-NEGOTIABLE PRIORITY. Degree 46 MUST be marine.

### 2. Eight consecutive Passeriformes (degrees 38-45)
Song Sparrow is Passeriformes. The order now has eight consecutive entries and 38 of 46 total (82.6%). A non-passerine is urgently needed. A marine species would break this streak -- loons (Gaviiformes), gulls (Charadriiformes), ducks (Anseriformes), grebes (Podicipediformes), or eagles (Accipitriformes) would all be non-passerine.

### 3. No corvid in 45 degrees
Steller's Jay, American Crow, Common Raven. Forty-five degrees. URGENT. A corvid would stay in Passeriformes but would introduce Corvidae -- a major missing family.

### 4. No raptor in 45 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Osprey, Peregrine Falcon. Forty-five degrees. A marine raptor (Bald Eagle, Osprey) could address both the raptor gap and the marine crisis.

### 5. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Forty-one degrees since the Spotted Owl. The night has been silent for nearly 90% of the engine.

### 6. E=3 absent for 25 degrees
Despite E=2's return, E=3 has not appeared since degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler). E=1 absent for 33 degrees. The lower three energy levels need more representation.

### 7. Only 5 orders in 46 entries
The engine has not added a new order since Apodiformes (hummingbirds) at degree 35. A marine species would bring the sixth order.
