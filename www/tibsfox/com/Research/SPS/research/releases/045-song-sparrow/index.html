Song Sparrow

Melospiza melodia
complex melodious song — the preamble, 1st Passerellidae (NEW family), E=2 restored after 31 degrees, “melody song-finch” — the bird whose name means “song song,” most studied songbird in North America, Margaret Morse Nice’s bird, 8–12 song types per male, year-round PNW resident

CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Pacific Chorus Frog (Pseudacris regilla) at degree 45. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Song Sparrow replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s E=2 energy level and wetland/pond margin habitat. The Song Sparrow occupies exactly the pond-edge habitat where the chorus frog calls.

The 46th Acoustic Element: The Preamble

Every Song Sparrow song opens with 2–4 clear, deliberate introductory notes before launching into a complex body of trills and buzzy phrases. The preamble is a formal opening statement — it announces complexity that follows. Named “the preamble” for the song’s structure, for Nice’s studies as the preamble to modern behavioral ecology, and for the engine reaching its one-eighth mark.

The first acoustic element defined by formal positional structure — an event meaningful because of where it occurs in the sequence, not what it sounds like in isolation.

First Passerellidae — 19th Family in the Engine

Passerellidae (New World Sparrows) enters the engine for the first time. Split from Emberizidae by the AOS in 2017, Passerellidae contains approximately 140 species in 30 genera. The Song Sparrow is the family’s type example — the most common, most studied, most vocal New World sparrow. The engine adds its first true sparrow.

E=2 Restored — After 31 Degrees of Absence

The last E=2 was degree 14 (MacGillivray’s Warbler) — 31 degrees ago. Seven consecutive E=4 across degrees 38–44 compressed the dynamic range to its most severe. E=2 returns at degree 45, dropping two levels and restoring the engine’s lower register. The Song Sparrow sings from low perches beside the water — conversational, not declamatory.

Margaret Morse Nice’s Bird

Nice’s eight-year study of Song Sparrows (1928–1936, published 1937 and 1943) invented modern population ecology methodology. Konrad Lorenz: “a major breakthrough in the methods of studying animal behavior.” Ernst Mayr: “the finest piece of life history work ever done.” Nice worked as a scientific outsider — no university position, working from her own backyard, publishing foundational science. The Song Sparrow was the organism; the contribution was epoch-making.

The Bird Whose Name Means “Song Song”

Melospiza melodia — from Greek melos (song) + spiza (finch) and Latin melodia (melody). “Melody song-finch” — the bird so defined by its voice that both halves of its binomial say the same thing. No other species in the engine has a name so completely devoted to sound.

The Sound: Preamble + Body

Song: 2–4 seconds. Opens with 2–4 clear introductory notes (the preamble), then launches into a complex body of trills, buzzy notes, and varied phrases. Frequency 2–8 kHz, most energy in 2–6 kHz. Classic mnemonic: “Maids! Maids! Maids! Put on your tea-kettle-ettle-ettle.”

Repertoire: Each male sings 8–12 distinct song types, each with a different preamble pattern. Males sing in “eventual variety” — repeating one type 5–15 times before switching. A territorial male may sing more than 1,000 songs per day.

Learning: Age-limited learner. Memorizes songs from neighboring adult males during a 40-day sensitive period starting at ~22 days of age. Song crystallizes by first spring and is maintained unchanged for life. The preamble is set once and held forever.

Soft song: A quiet, close-range version of the full song, first described by Margaret Morse Nice. Given during aggressive encounters. A whispered threat.

The mosaic is defined by what the song borrows. The preamble is defined by where the song begins. Content vs. structure.

24 Subspecies — A Continent of Variation

The Song Sparrow has 24–25 recognized subspecies (52 named historically) — one of the most polytypic birds in North America. Size varies from the tiny desert saltonis (~18 g) to the enormous Aleutian maxima (~53 g). PNW birds are subspecies morphna — dark, heavily streaked, year-round residents of the wet Pacific lowlands. Four subspecies occur in Washington, three breeding.

E=2 Restored foundational

First E=2 since degree 14 — 31 degrees of absence. Breaks seven consecutive E=4 (38–44). Dynamic range restored. The engine has a lower register again.

N

Nice’s Model Organism foundational

The most studied songbird in North America. Nice’s 1937–1943 studies invented modern population ecology. Marler’s vocal learning research established the field. The Song Sparrow is to ornithology what Drosophila is to genetics.

R

The Reference Species intermediate

The “default” bird — other species’ songs are described relative to Song Sparrow. The baseline, the comparison point, the bird you learn first. The engine acquires its reference pitch.

W

Wetland Edge Habitat intermediate

The engine’s first true wetland-edge species. Pond margins, marsh edges, riparian thickets. The transition zone where water meets land. The habitat the engine has been missing since degree 0.

Cross-References

SPS-044: Lesser Goldfinch

FAMILY TRANSITION. Fringillidae (44) to Passerellidae (45). E=4 to E=2. Mimicry mosaic (borrowed voices) to preamble (original composition). Lifelong learner to age-limited learner. Summer visitor to year-round resident. The engine pivots.

SPS-014: MacGillivray’s Warbler

LAST E=2. MacGillivray’s at degree 14 was the last E=2 entry — 31 degrees of absence before the Song Sparrow restores the energy level. Both are low-perching, dense-vegetation birds with moderate songs. Both name their sound (churry-churry-churry-sweet vs. the preamble).

SPS-000: Great Blue Heron

WETLAND RETURN. The Great Blue Heron at degree 0 stood in the water. The Song Sparrow at degree 45 stands beside the water. 45 degrees between the engine’s two water-associated species. The heron was silence; the sparrow is the preamble.

SPS-011: Oregon Junco

PASSERELLIDAE LINK. The junco at degree 11 was entered under Emberizidae but is now classified as Passerellidae. The Song Sparrow is the first species explicitly entered as Passerellidae. Two sparrow-family members, 34 degrees apart, bookending the family’s reclassification.

SPS-034: Red Crossbill

VOCAL LEARNING CONTRAST. Crossbill = population-specific dialects (species-internal). Song Sparrow = individually learned repertoires from local tutors (neighbor-specific). Both involve vocal learning; different spatial scales of transmission.

SPS-017: Common Yellowthroat

WETLAND NEIGHBOR. The yellowthroat sings from cattail stalks in the same marshes where Song Sparrows nest. Witchety-witchety vs. Maids-Maids-Maids. E=3 vs. E=2. Both are low-perching wetland-edge birds.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Song Sparrow documented as common year-round resident in western Washington. Four subspecies in WA, three breeding. Dark morphna form dominant in Puget Sound lowlands.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Wetland-edge ecology. Pond margin habitat. Riparian thickets. Geographic adaptation across 24+ subspecies. Gloger’s rule in PNW coloration. Omnivore-generalist diet ecology.
