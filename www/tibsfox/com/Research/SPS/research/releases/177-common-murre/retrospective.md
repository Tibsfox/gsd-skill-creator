*Degree 177 retrospective complete. COMMON MURRE at the one hundred seventy-seventh degree -- THE COLONY ROAR. The Common Murre nests in the densest breeding colonies of any bird -- up to 20 pairs per square meter on narrow cliff ledges. No nest. A single pear-shaped egg laid directly on bare rock, incubated on the adult's feet. The pear shape is evolution's answer to gravity: when bumped, the egg rolls in a tight circle rather than off the ledge. The extraordinary color variation -- blue, green, white, brown, spotted, streaked -- may help parents recognize their own egg in the packed colony. The sound of a murre colony is a wall: individual growling "arrr" calls, guttural and grating, multiplied by thousands into an overwhelming, immersive, continuous roar. E=5 through colony multiplication -- the individual voice is moderate but the colony is an instrument. And when chicks are only 15 to 25 days old, still flightless, they leap from their cliff ledge -- sometimes 300 feet or more -- to the sea below. The male parent accompanies the chick at sea for weeks. Fourth Alcidae in a streak that began at 174 with the Pigeon Guillemot and now includes four distinct nesting strategies: crevice (174), nocturnal burrow (175), cliff burrow (176), and dense open ledge (177). And across the degree pair, Kid Sensation represents the same principle: the colony of Nastymix Records artists clustered around Sir Mix-a-Lot, deriving collective power from proximity and numbers. The colony. v1.49.317.*
