Common Murre — Colony Roar

Uria aalge (Alcidae) — E=5 — The Colony
FOURTH ALCIDAE (174–177). Dense cliff colony. Pear-shaped egg — won’t roll off the ledge. Growling guttural colony roar from thousands. Deep diver. Chick leaps from cliff at 15–25 days. E=5. v1.49.317.

The Densest Colonies — 20 Pairs per Square Meter

Common Murres form some of the densest breeding colonies of any bird. Standing shoulder to shoulder on narrow cliff ledges, barely able to turn. No nest — single egg laid directly on bare rock, incubated on the adult’s feet. The density is the defense: no space for a predator to land.

The Pear-Shaped Egg — Evolution on the Ledge

Strongly pyriform (pear-shaped). When bumped, rolls in a tight circle rather than off the ledge. Extraordinary color variation — blue, green, white, brown, spotted, streaked — may help parents identify their own egg in the dense colony. One egg. One chance.

Cliff Colony Ecology

The “penguin of the Northern Hemisphere” — upright posture, black-and-white plumage, extraordinary diving ability (100+ meters, 3+ minutes). Unlike penguins, murres CAN fly. Chicks leap from cliff ledges at 15–25 days old, still flightless, sometimes falling 300+ feet to the sea. The male accompanies the chick at sea for weeks.
