### The Densest Colonies

The Common Murre forms some of the densest breeding colonies of any bird species:

- **Packing density:** Up to 20 pairs per square meter on narrow cliff ledges. Birds stand shoulder to shoulder, barely able to turn. This density is an anti-predator strategy -- there is no space for a predator to land.
- **No nest:** The Common Murre does not build a nest. It lays its single egg directly on bare rock. The egg sits on the adult's feet, pressed against the brood patch.
- **Cliff ledge specialists:** The narrow ledges that murres use would be unsuitable for almost any other bird. The birds stand upright, penguin-like, packed together on ledges sometimes only a few centimeters wide.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; seabird colony ecology; engine internal analysis)

## E=5 -- THE COLONY ROAR

### Why E=5

The Common Murre's sound registers at E=5:

- **Individual call:** Each murre produces a deep, grating, growling "arrr" vocalization -- guttural and resonant. Individual calls are moderate in volume.
- **Colony multiplication:** But murres nest in colonies of THOUSANDS to HUNDREDS OF THOUSANDS. The individual "arrr" multiplied by tens of thousands becomes an overwhelming wall of sound. The colony roar is continuous, immersive, and inescapable at breeding sites.
- **Frequency range:** The growling calls concentrate in the 500-2500 Hz range -- low and resonant, penetrating.
- **Why not E=6?** The individual call is moderate. E=5 reflects the collective roar -- overwhelming in aggregate but not at the individual level. The colony is the instrument; the individual is one voice in the wall.
- **Comparison:** The Tufted Puffin (176, E=5) is relatively quiet individually. The murre colony is louder in aggregate but also E=5 because the individual contribution is moderate.

## THE PEAR-SHAPED EGG

### Evolution on the Ledge

The Common Murre's egg is one of the most studied eggs in ornithology:

- **Shape:** Strongly pyriform (pear-shaped) -- broad at one end, sharply pointed at the other. This is NOT random variation. It is a specific adaptation to cliff-ledge nesting.
- **Rolling behavior:** When bumped, a pear-shaped egg rolls in a tight circle rather than rolling off the ledge. A spherical egg would roll straight and fall. The pyriform shape converts linear momentum into circular motion.
- **Color variation:** Common Murre eggs show extraordinary variation in color and pattern -- blue, green, white, brown, spotted, streaked, plain. This variation may help parents identify their own egg in the densely packed colony.
- **Single egg:** The murre lays only one egg per breeding attempt. With no nest and a precarious ledge, one egg is the maximum that can be safely incubated.
- **Incubation:** The adult incubates the egg on its feet, pressed against the brood patch, standing upright on the ledge. The egg is never left unattended -- a moment of exposure means predation or chill.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; egg morphology research; Birkhead 2016; engine internal analysis)

## CLIFF COLONY ECOLOGY

### The Vertical World

- **Penguin of the North:** The Common Murre is sometimes called the "penguin of the Northern Hemisphere." It stands upright, has a black-and-white color pattern, and dives deep for fish. Unlike penguins, murres can fly -- they must, to reach their cliff-top colonies.
- **Diving ability:** Murres are extraordinary divers, reaching depths of 100+ meters and holding breath for over 3 minutes. They pursue fish underwater using their wings for propulsion -- essentially "flying" through water.
- **Colony departure:** When chicks are only 15-25 days old and still flightless, they leap from their cliff ledges -- sometimes 300+ feet -- to the sea below. The male parent accompanies the chick at sea for weeks afterward, feeding and protecting it while it grows flight feathers.
- **PNW colonies:** Major colonies exist on offshore rocks and islands along the Oregon and Washington coasts. The colonies are visible (and audible) from shore at many locations.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; seabird diving ecology; PNW colony surveys)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Common Murre

**Colony Roar:**
- **Trigger:** Continuous at breeding colonies. Alarm, contact, territorial.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** Deep, grating, growling "arrr" -- guttural, resonant. Individual calls moderate. Colony aggregate overwhelming. A wall of sound.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 500-2500 Hz. Low, resonant, penetrating.
- **Duration:** Individual calls 0.3-1.0 seconds. Colony roar continuous.
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Individual moderate, colony overwhelming. The multiplication effect.

**Chick Calls:**
- High-pitched peeping from chicks on ledges. Parents recognize individual chick calls in the colony din.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; seabird acoustics; engine internal analysis)
