## MT RAINIER: THE MOST GLACIATED PEAK IN THE LOWER 48

### A Mountain of Ice

Mt Rainier (14,411 feet / 4,392 meters) dominates the Seattle skyline on clear days, visible from throughout the Puget Sound region as a white pyramid against the eastern sky. It is not merely a mountain -- it is a glacier system:

- **25 named glaciers.** Mt Rainier carries more glacial ice than any other peak in the contiguous United States. The 25 named glaciers cover approximately 35 square miles (91 square kilometers) with a total ice volume estimated at roughly 1 cubic mile. This is a substantial water reservoir above the urban regions of western Washington.
- **The major glaciers.** Carbon Glacier on the north flank is the lowest-elevation glacier in the lower 48 states, extending down to approximately 3,500 feet elevation -- remarkably low for a glacier in this latitude. Emmons Glacier on the northeast flank is the largest glacier by area in the lower 48 states. The Nisqually, Cowlitz, Ingraham, Winthrop, and Tahoma glaciers are among the others, each with distinct character, flow rates, and acoustic profiles.
- **Why so much ice?** Rainier's extreme relief (from sea-level-adjacent valleys to 14,411 feet in under 50 miles) combined with the Pacific maritime climate creates conditions favorable for heavy snowfall at elevation and slow ablation. The mountain intercepts moisture from Pacific storm systems and converts it to snow at high elevation, feeding the glaciers. This is the same Pacific moisture system that produces the PNW's famous rainfall.
- **Glacial retreat.** Most of Rainier's glaciers have retreated significantly over the 20th and 21st centuries in response to warming temperatures. The Carbon Glacier has been relatively stable compared to others; the Nisqually, Emmons, and others have shown measurable retreat. The glacier system that exists today is smaller than it was a century ago but still represents the most substantial glacial coverage in the lower 48.

(Sources: Mt Rainier National Park glacier inventory; Carbon Glacier elevation data; Emmons Glacier area data; USGS glacier monitoring; Pacific maritime climate and glacial mass balance)

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT: GLACIER CALVING

### Physics of the Sharp Crack and Rumble Cascade

Glacier calving is not a single simple acoustic event -- it is a complex multi-phase sound that unfolds across time:

- **Phase 1: The fracture crack.** When a section of glacial ice separates from the glacier margin or terminus, the initial event is a fracture crack. Ice is crystalline in structure -- it fractures rather than bends. The fracture propagates through the ice at high velocity (on the order of kilometers per second), releasing stored elastic strain energy in a sudden mechanical impulse. The result is a sharp, explosive crack -- sometimes compared to a gunshot but at larger scale, sometimes described as a cannon shot at close range. This initial crack is the highest-frequency, most impulsive component of the calving acoustic signature.
- **Phase 2: The fall and impact.** After fracture, the separated ice mass falls. Depending on the geometry of the calving event, this may involve a relatively small slab tumbling a few meters or a large mass (thousands of tons) falling onto the glacier surface, into a meltwater lake, or onto the moraine below. The impact creates a low-frequency thud and begins the cascade.
- **Phase 3: The rumble cascade.** As the calved ice mass disintegrates and slides, individual ice blocks and fragments impact each other and the underlying surface, creating a sustained rumbling sound. This phase is the longest-duration component and the most complex acoustically -- overlapping impacts, reverberating between valley walls, decaying over 10-60 seconds or more depending on the volume of ice and the terrain geometry.
- **Terrain amplification.** Rainier's glaciers exist in high-relief terrain with steep valley walls. Sound generated at the glacial front reflects off rock faces and ridges, creating natural amplification and echo. A calving event on the Carbon Glacier in its deep valley context produces a very different acoustic experience than the same volume of ice calving on the gentler slopes of the Emmons. The terrain is part of the instrument.
- **E=6 energy level.** Six on the SPS scale represents a substantial, impressive sound event that is not the absolute maximum but is clearly in the upper portion of the scale. Glacier calving at Rainier is dramatic and memorable -- the kind of event that stops hikers and commands attention -- but it is not the overwhelming wall-of-sound of the lightning strike tree (E=9) or the continuous roar of winter storm surf (E=8). E=6 is correct: dramatic, powerful, geophysically significant, but with the specific impulsive character of a crack-and-cascade rather than sustained roar.

## CARBON GLACIER: THE LOWEST

### A Glacier that Reaches Down

The Carbon Glacier is one of Rainier's most remarkable:

- **Lowest elevation in the lower 48.** The Carbon Glacier terminus descends to approximately 3,500 feet (1,067 meters) elevation -- exceptionally low for a glacier at this latitude. This means the glacier's terminus is accessible in relatively short hiking distance from road access, making it one of the more commonly visited and documented glaciers in the Cascades.
- **The moraine environment.** The Carbon Glacier's terminus is surrounded by a substantial moraine -- deposits of rock and debris carried by the glacier and left as the ice retreated. The moraine terrain is unstable, subject to rockfall and ice-debris events. Calving at the Carbon terminus often involves ice falling onto and interacting with the moraine surface.
- **The carbon river valley.** The Carbon River drains the Carbon Glacier and flows west through a rare inland temperate rainforest -- one of the few temperate rainforests in the continental United States not on the Olympic Peninsula. The carbon river valley's acoustic environment is forest-buffered at lower elevations, opening to the alpine acoustic environment near the glacier.
- **Acoustic character at Carbon.** Calving events at the Carbon Glacier occur in a relatively enclosed valley context, with steep walls amplifying and reflecting the calving sounds. The crack-and-cascade at Carbon can have a pronounced echo character -- the initial crack arriving cleanly, followed by reverberant rumble bouncing between valley walls before decaying.

(Sources: Carbon Glacier elevation documentation; NPS Carbon Glacier visitor information; Carbon River temperate rainforest; valley acoustic characteristics)

## EMMONS GLACIER: THE LARGEST

### Ice at Scale

The Emmons Glacier contrasts with Carbon in almost every dimension:

- **Largest by area in the lower 48.** The Emmons Glacier covers approximately 4.3 square miles (11.1 square kilometers) -- the largest glacier area in the contiguous United States. It flows down Rainier's northeast flank and is visible from many of the mountain's approaches.
- **The White River drainage.** Emmons drains into the White River, which flows north and then west into the Green River and eventually Puget Sound. The White River carries glacial flour -- fine rock particles ground by the glacier -- that gives the water its characteristic milky appearance.
- **Emmons calving events.** On the Emmons, calving tends to occur at crevasse zones and the glacier's margins. The more open terrain of the northeast flank means calving sounds travel farther before encountering reflecting walls. The acoustic signature is more open, less reverberant, and the rumble cascade tends to be lower and more diffuse.

(Sources: Emmons Glacier area documentation; White River glacial drainage; Emmons Glacier visitor documentation; northeast flank terrain characteristics)

## SUMMER CALVING: THERMAL DYNAMICS

### Why Summer Produces the Events

Calving on Rainier's glaciers intensifies in summer for specific physical reasons:

- **Solar loading.** Summer sun at high elevation delivers substantial energy to glacier surfaces. Surface melt increases dramatically in July and August. Meltwater penetrates crevasses, lubricating basal flow and destabilizing ice masses that were frozen in place through winter.
- **Diurnal cycle.** Summer glacier environments have pronounced diurnal acoustic cycles. Morning is relatively quiet -- overnight refreezing stabilizes surface ice. As the sun heats the glacier surface through the day, surface melt increases, ice becomes more unstable, and calving events become more likely. Afternoon hours, particularly on south-facing aspects, produce the highest frequency of small calving events.
- **Seasonal ice dynamics.** Over the winter, glaciers accumulate mass (snow and ice). Over the summer, ablation removes mass. The transition from accumulation to ablation creates mechanical stress at crevasse zones and glacier margins. Summer is when accumulated stress resolves as calving events.
- **Meltwater lakes.** Some of Rainier's glaciers have associated meltwater lakes at their termini (proglacial lakes). Calving into water produces additional acoustic effects: the water impact creates a low-frequency splash and surge in addition to the fracture crack. Calving into water is acoustically richer than calving onto dry moraine.

(Sources: Glacial thermal dynamics; ablation season timing; meltwater penetration and basal flow; diurnal calving cycles; proglacial lake acoustics)
