## THE OLD-GROWTH CANOPY -- 400 YEARS OF ACCUMULATED RESONANCE

### What Makes an Old-Growth Forest Acoustically Distinct

The definition and acoustic character of 400-year-old-growth:

- **Age and structure.** A 400-year-old Pacific Northwest old-growth forest has characteristics that no younger forest possesses: (1) Multiple canopy layers -- dominant trees (Douglas-fir, Sitka Spruce) at 60-80 meters, with sub-canopy layers at 30-50 meters, understory at 10-20 meters, and shrub/herb layers below. The acoustic complexity of this multi-layer structure is fundamentally different from a single-canopy plantation or second-growth forest. (2) Massive individual trees -- Douglas-fir at 400 years can reach 1-1.5 meters diameter at breast height and 70+ meters height. These trees have mass, structural strength, and acoustic resonance properties that young trees cannot match. (3) Standing dead trees (snags) -- as documented in SPS-332, snags are a critical component of old-growth acoustic ecology. In windstorm conditions, snags produce unique sounds: structural groaning under wind load, potential fall events, and the interaction of dry resonant wood with the wind.
- **The biomass accumulation.** A 400-year-old Douglas-fir tree has accumulated mass proportional to its age. When this mass is set in motion by wind, the energy involved is enormous. The combined movement of hundreds of such trees in a forest canopy during a major windstorm involves forces measured in thousands of kilonewtons. The acoustic output of this movement reflects these forces.
- **The acoustic ecology of old-growth.** Old-growth forests have been documented to have acoustic properties distinct from younger forests: lower ambient sound levels during calm (more acoustic absorption by the dense understory and decaying wood), higher acoustic output during wind events (more resonant surfaces, more mechanical movement), and more complex sound patterns during intermediate conditions (wind in the sub-canopy, bird activity in multiple layers). The old-growth acoustic profile is more dynamic -- quieter when calm, louder when disturbed.

(Sources: Old-growth Pacific Northwest structure: multi-layer canopy 60-80m dominant, 30-50m sub-canopy, 10-20m understory; Douglas-fir 400yr diameter 1-1.5m height 70+m; snag windstorm sound: structural groaning, fall events, dry resonant wood interaction; biomass accumulation force calculation; old-growth acoustic ecology: lower calm ambient, higher wind output, complex intermediate patterns)

## THE PACIFIC WINDSTORM

### The Acoustic Driver

The windstorm that sets the old-growth canopy in motion:

- **Pacific storm systems.** The Pacific Northwest's windstorm season runs October through March, driven by the procession of low-pressure systems that form over the North Pacific and track toward the coast. The most intense windstorms are associated with the atmospheric river phenomenon -- long, narrow bands of concentrated atmospheric moisture that deliver both extreme precipitation and the pressure gradient that drives high winds. The strongest Pacific Northwest windstorms (like the Columbus Day Storm of October 1962, with gusts exceeding 100 mph in the Willamette Valley) can cause major forest damage across millions of acres.
- **Windstorm wind speed classification for E=7.** The degree-338 windstorm is a major but not catastrophically destructive event -- sustained winds of 50-70 km/h (30-45 mph) with gusts to 80-100 km/h (50-65 mph). This is Beaufort scale 8-10 (gale to storm). At these wind speeds: canopy trees sway at maximum amplitude without catastrophic structural failure; branches are shed (smaller branches in the 5-15 cm diameter range); sound output is at maximum for a non-catastrophic windstorm. A destructive storm (gusts above 130 km/h) produces different sounds -- structural failure, tree falls -- which shifts the character from E=7 (maximum roar) toward something qualitatively different (catastrophic events add to the acoustic output but are less characteristic of the system in sustained motion).
- **The wind-canopy interaction.** When wind of 50-70 km/h sustained velocity meets an old-growth canopy at 60-80 meters, the interaction is complex: (1) Crown sway -- the upper canopy trees sway in the wind with an oscillation period of 2-8 seconds (determined by tree height and mass). The sway amplitude at 400-year old-growth scale can exceed 2-4 meters at the crown. (2) Branch turbulence -- wind passes through the dense branch systems creating turbulent flow, with acoustic output in the 500-4,000 Hz range. (3) Structural sound -- the bending of the trunk and major branches under wind load produces low-frequency creaking and groaning, 20-200 Hz. (4) Needle and leaf sound -- the millions of individual needles and leaves in the canopy produce a continuous high-frequency component, 2,000-8,000+ Hz.

(Sources: Pacific Northwest windstorm season October-March; atmospheric river phenomenon; Columbus Day Storm October 1962 100+ mph gusts; Beaufort scale 8-10 gale to storm 50-70 km/h sustained 80-100 km/h gusts; non-catastrophic E=7 windstorm threshold; crown sway oscillation 2-8 seconds for old-growth; sway amplitude 2-4 meters at crown; branch turbulence 500-4000 Hz; structural creaking 20-200 Hz; needle/leaf high-frequency 2000-8000+ Hz)

## THE SPECIES IN MOTION

### Old-Growth Tree Acoustics by Species

The specific acoustic contributions of old-growth species:

- **Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*).** The dominant tree of Pacific Northwest old-growth forests below the sub-alpine zone. At 400 years, a Douglas-fir is among the largest living organisms in North America. In windstorm, the Douglas-fir's acoustic contribution is dominated by (1) trunk creaking -- the massive bole (trunk) bending under wind load produces deep, low-frequency structural sounds, 20-80 Hz; (2) branch shedding -- smaller dead branches are shed during windstorm, producing acoustic events ranging from a soft brush (small branches falling through the lower canopy) to a sharp crack (larger branch failures); (3) crown turbulence -- the dense needle-bearing foliage of the upper crown produces a continuous high-frequency hiss as wind passes through it.
- **Sitka Spruce (*Picea sitchensis*).** The dominant old-growth species of the coastal temperate rainforest -- the Olympic Peninsula coast, the Washington and Oregon coast ranges. Sitka Spruce has a notably different acoustic character from Douglas-fir in windstorm: its more flexible structure sways more dramatically; its dense drooping branches produce a more pronounced "singing" sound in wind; its large diameter (up to 3 meters at 400+ years on the coast) amplifies the structural creaking frequency. Sitka Spruce windstorm sound is one of the most distinctive natural acoustic events in Pacific Northwest coastal forests.
- **Western Red Cedar (*Thuja plicata*).** Western Red Cedar's pendulous, lacy branch structure produces the most complex acoustic output per unit volume of any Pacific Northwest old-growth tree in windstorm. The thin, flat branch fans oscillate rapidly in wind, producing a rushing, complex sound in the middle frequency range (500-2,000 Hz). The Cedar's contribution to the old-growth windstorm soundscape is the most textured -- not the deep bass of the Douglas-fir trunk nor the high hiss of the Spruce crown, but a complex mid-frequency wash.
- **Big-Leaf Maple (*Acer macrophyllum*).** A deciduous component of old-growth forests in riparian zones and forest edges. In winter windstorms (October-March), Big-Leaf Maple is often leafless, and its bare branches in high wind produce a distinctive rattling and whooshing sound different from the evergreen species. The maple's contribution varies seasonally: bare branches (December-March) produce a sparse, open sound; leafed branches (May-October) produce a denser, louder sound.
- **Red Alder (*Alnus rubra*).** Like the maple, Red Alder is deciduous and produces different acoustic signatures by season. Alder dominates early-succession and riparian zones in the Pacific Northwest. In windstorm, older alder stands produce structural creaking as their relatively brittle trunks (alders rarely exceed 100 years before structural failure) flex under wind load.

(Sources: Pseudotsuga menziesii trunk creaking 20-80 Hz; Douglas-fir branch shedding acoustic events; crown turbulence continuous high-frequency hiss; Picea sitchensis coastal 3m diameter 400+ years; Sitka Spruce flexible structure pronounced singing in wind; Thuja plicata pendulous lacy branch structure; Cedar middle-frequency wash 500-2000 Hz; Acer macrophyllum seasonal bare branch rattling; Alnus rubra deciduous windstorm acoustic signature)

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT -- E=7 ANALYSIS

### The Full Canopy Roar Characterized

The combined acoustic output of 400-year old-growth in major windstorm:

- **Amplitude.** At the canopy level in a major windstorm, a Pacific Northwest old-growth forest produces sound levels of 70-85 dB in the mid-frequency range (500-2,000 Hz), with lower-frequency structural sounds (20-200 Hz) at 60-75 dB, and high-frequency needle and leaf sounds at 65-80 dB. The combined acoustic output is continuous, broadband, and physically enveloping.
- **Frequency spectrum.** The old-growth windstorm sound covers the full audible spectrum with characteristic peaks: 20-80 Hz (structural creaking of large trunks and roots); 100-500 Hz (branch flexion and major branch turbulence); 500-2,000 Hz (canopy movement, smaller branch turbulence, Cedar mid-frequency); 2,000-8,000+ Hz (needle and leaf sound, high-frequency wind component).
- **Temporal character.** The windstorm sound is sustained but not constant -- it varies with gusts. During a sustained gust, the sound increases by 10-15 dB and the lower-frequency structural sounds become more prominent as the trees approach maximum sway amplitude. Between gusts, the sound drops back to the sustained wind level. This modulation -- 10-15 dB variations at 30-120 second intervals as gust cells pass -- creates the temporal rhythm of the windstorm.
- **Why E=7, not E=8 or E=9.** The old-growth windstorm at E=7 is heavy, complex, and physically present but not at the maximum intensity of the spring river at flood (E=7-8) or the thunder crack at maximum (E=8-9). The windstorm is sustained, enveloping, and sonically rich but lacks the single-moment peaks that push ratings to E=8-9. The E=7 rating reflects the sustained moderate-to-high energy character: always loud, never at the maximum-impulse level.

(Sources: Old-growth windstorm amplitude 70-85 dB mid-frequency; structural sound 60-75 dB 20-200 Hz; high-frequency 65-80 dB; full spectrum peak analysis: 20-80 Hz structural, 100-500 Hz branch flexion, 500-2000 Hz canopy movement Cedar, 2000-8000+ Hz needle leaf; temporal character: gust modulation 10-15 dB variation 30-120 sec intervals; E=7 sustained enveloping vs. E=8-9 impulse distinction)

## THE OLD-GROWTH CONTEXT

### Why This Sound Requires Ancient Forest

The ecological conditions that produce the degree-338 sound:

- **Why old-growth, not second-growth.** A 50-year plantation forest and a 400-year old-growth forest in the same windstorm produce fundamentally different acoustic events. The plantation: single canopy layer, uniform tree heights, limited structural diversity, no large snags. The old-growth: multiple canopy layers, massive individual trees, high structural diversity, large snags, complex branch architecture accumulated over centuries. The old-growth produces more acoustic complexity, more low-frequency content (from larger trunks), more layering, and more spatial variation.
- **The loss quantification.** Pacific Northwest old-growth has been reduced by approximately 80-90% from its pre-European-contact extent through logging, primarily during the 20th century. The acoustic event at degree 338 occurs in the remaining 10-20% of old-growth habitat -- primarily Olympic National Park, Mount Rainier National Park, North Cascades National Park, and a few remaining old-growth stands on national forests and tribal lands.
- **The Olympic Peninsula.** The most intact coastal old-growth in the contiguous United States is found in Olympic National Park's Hoh, Queets, Quinault, and Bogachiel rainforest valleys. These valleys receive 140-200 inches of precipitation annually, producing the densest biomass accumulation in North America north of the tropics. The windstorm acoustic event at degree 338 is fully realized only in this remaining coastal old-growth.

(Sources: Old-growth vs. plantation acoustic comparison: single vs. multi-layer canopy, uniform vs. diverse structure, no vs. large snags; old-growth Pacific Northwest loss 80-90% from pre-contact extent; remaining habitat: Olympic NP, Rainier NP, North Cascades NP, select national forest stands; Olympic Peninsula Hoh Queets Quinault Bogachiel valleys; 140-200 inches annual precipitation; densest North American biomass north of tropics)
