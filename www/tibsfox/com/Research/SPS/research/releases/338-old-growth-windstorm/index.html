The 400-Year Canopy in Full Roar

A major Pacific windstorm (sustained 50–70 km/h, gusts to 100 km/h) meets intact 400-year-old-growth forest on the Olympic Peninsula or in the Cascades. Multiple canopy layers (60–80m dominant, 30–50m sub-canopy, 10–20m understory) all in motion simultaneously. Douglas-fir trunks creaking at 20–80 Hz. Sitka Spruce crowns hissing at 2,000–8,000 Hz. Western Red Cedar lacy branches washing mid-frequency at 500–2,000 Hz. Standing snags groaning under load. Combined output: 70–85 dB, broadband, sustained. E=7 through four centuries of accumulated resonance.

The Old-Growth Species

Douglas-fir Pseudotsuga menziesii

Dominant old-growth species. 400yr: 1-1.5m diameter, 70+m height. Trunk creaking in wind: 20-80 Hz. Branch turbulence: 500-2,000 Hz. Crown hiss: 2,000-5,000 Hz. The deep bass of the windstorm sound.

Sitka Spruce Picea sitchensis

Coastal old-growth dominant. 400+yr: up to 3m diameter on coast. More flexible than Doug-fir; sways dramatically. Dense drooping branches "sing" in wind. The most distinctive windstorm voice on the Olympic coast.

Western Red Cedar Thuja plicata

Pendulous lacy branch fans oscillate rapidly in wind. Mid-frequency wash 500-2,000 Hz. Most textured acoustic output per unit volume. The complex mid-range layer of the windstorm soundscape.

Big-Leaf Maple Acer macrophyllum

Deciduous riparian old-growth component. Bare branches (winter windstorm season) produce sparse open rattling; leafed (spring/summer) produce dense wash. Seasonal acoustic variation.

The Windstorm — Pacific Frontal Systems

Pacific Northwest windstorms (October–March) are driven by low-pressure systems tracking from the North Pacific. The most intense are associated with atmospheric rivers. For degree 338's E=7 event: sustained 50–70 km/h (Beaufort 8–10, gale to storm), gusts to 80–100 km/h. This is the threshold of maximum canopy roar without catastrophic structural failure. Above this threshold (gusts over 130 km/h), the event shifts from sustained roar to catastrophic with tree falls dominating — a qualitatively different acoustic event. E=7 is the non-catastrophic maximum: every tree in full motion, structural sounds at maximum, nothing falling.

Why Old-Growth, Not Second-Growth

A 50-year plantation forest in the same windstorm produces a fundamentally different acoustic event: single canopy layer, uniform tree heights, no large snags, limited structural diversity. The old-growth's multi-layer canopy produces acoustic complexity across multiple heights simultaneously. The massive individual trees produce lower-frequency structural sounds that small-diameter trees cannot generate. The snags add structural groaning. 400 years of accumulated biomass, in motion, produces a sound that no younger forest can reproduce.

Pacific Northwest old-growth has been reduced by 80–90% from pre-contact extent. The degree-338 sound event occurs in the remaining 10–20%: primarily Olympic National Park's Hoh, Queets, Quinault, and Bogachiel rainforest valleys (140–200 inches annual precipitation, densest North American biomass north of the tropics).
