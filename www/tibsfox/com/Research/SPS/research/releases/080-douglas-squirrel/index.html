The Sciuridae Sextet — Matches the Mustelidae Record!

SIXTH consecutive Sciuridae: Yellow-bellied Marmot (75), Hoary Marmot (76), Olympic Marmot (77), Townsend’s Ground Squirrel (78), Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (79), Douglas Squirrel (80). Six consecutive Sciuridae — matching the Mustelidae sextet (63–68). The longest family run in the engine is now a TWO-WAY TIE at SIX, surpassing the Swallow quintet (26–30).

First True Canopy Mammal — The Conifer Treetops

The Douglas Squirrel is the engine’s first true canopy mammal. After alpine (74–77), grassland (78), and forest edge (79), degree 80 ascends to the conifer canopy — the treetops of Douglas-fir, western red cedar, and Sitka spruce. The vertical axis opens. The engine goes UP.

John Muir’s Favorite — “The Most Influential of the Sierra Animals”

John Muir wrote extensively about the Douglas Squirrel (which he called the “Douglas” or the “Chickaree”), declaring it “by far the most influential of all the Sierra animals” in The Mountains of California (1894). Muir devoted an entire chapter to this species — the only mammal to receive that honor. Named after David Douglas, the Scottish botanist who gave us the Douglas-fir.

The Sound: Loud Scolding Chatter Rattle (81st Acoustic Element)

The chatter: A loud, aggressive, rapid scolding chatter — a machine-gun rattle of sharp notes delivered at high volume and sustained for seconds. Approximately 3,000–10,000 Hz, 2–10+ seconds duration. The most sustained and aggressive vocalization in the Sciuridae run [1, 2, 3].

Why E=5: (1) Volume — audible at 200+ meters through dense conifer forest. (2) Duration — sustained for seconds, not brief. (3) Aggression — the chatter is delivered with territorial fury. (4) Dominance — the loudest and most persistent mammal vocalization in PNW forests. The most vocal mammal in the canopy.

Sciuridae sextet contrast: Yellow-bellied (75): chirp, E=4. Hoary (76): whistle, E=5. Olympic (77): multi-note, E=4. Townsend’s (78): chirping trill, E=4. Golden-mantled (79): sharp chip, E=4. Douglas (80): scolding chatter rattle, E=5. Six Sciuridae, six acoustic signatures, two at E=5.

Taxonomy: Tamiasciurus douglasii

Etymology: Tamiasciurus from Greek tamias (“steward/hoarder”) + skiouros (“squirrel,” from skia “shadow” + oura “tail”). douglasii honors David Douglas (1799–1834), the Scottish botanist who explored the PNW and gave his name to the Douglas-fir. Described by Bachman (1839) [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Sciuridae — SIXTH consecutive (75–80). Genus Tamiasciurus — NEW GENUS (tree squirrels). Fourth Sciuridae genus after Marmota, Urocitellus, Callospermophilus [1, 2].

Ecology: The Canopy Sentinel

Habitat: Conifer canopy. Dense coniferous forest — Douglas-fir, western red cedar, Sitka spruce, western hemlock. The treetops. First true canopy mammal in the engine [1, 2, 3].

Social: Highly territorial. Defends individual territories with sustained vocal aggression. The chatter IS the territory defense.

Diet: Conifer seeds (cone scales stripped), fungi (especially truffles), berries, bark. Creates “middens” — massive piles of stripped cone scales at favorite feeding perches.

Predators: American Marten (65) — the primary predator. Canopy predator-prey! Both arboreal. Marten hunts Douglas Squirrel through the canopy. Also: Northern Goshawk, Great Horned Owl, Bobcat.
