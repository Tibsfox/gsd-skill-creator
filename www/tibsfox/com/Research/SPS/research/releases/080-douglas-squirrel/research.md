## THE DOUGLAS SQUIRREL: THE CANOPY SENTINEL

The Douglas Squirrel (also called the **Chickaree**) is the **PNW's signature tree squirrel** -- a small, dark, aggressive, relentlessly vocal arboreal mammal of the conifer canopy. At 150-300 grams (5-10 oz), it is the smallest member of the Sciuridae sextet, but it is by far the **loudest and most persistent**. Its scolding chatter dominates the forest soundscape -- if you walk through a PNW conifer forest, the Douglas Squirrel will announce your presence to everything within earshot [1, 2, 3, 4].

This is the engine's **SIXTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79, Douglas Squirrel 80) -- the **Sciuridae sextet**, which **matches the Mustelidae sextet** (63-68) as the longest family run in the engine. **TWO-WAY TIE at SIX**. The Swallow quintet (26-30) is surpassed. Three marmots + three squirrels. Four genera. Six habitats.

This is also the engine's **first true canopy mammal** -- the vertical axis opens. After alpine (74-77), grassland (78), and forest edge (79), the engine goes UP into the conifer treetops.

## TAXONOMY: *Tamiasciurus douglasii*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Tamiasciurus** -- from Greek *tamias* ("steward" or "hoarder") + *skiouros* ("squirrel," from *skia* "shadow" + *oura* "tail"). *Tamiasciurus* = "hoarder squirrel" -- reflecting the species' intensive food-caching behavior [1, 2].

- **douglasii** -- honors **David Douglas** (1799-1834), the Scottish botanist who explored the Pacific Northwest in the 1820s-1830s and whose name is also given to the Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*, though "Douglas-fir" honors him in common name). The squirrel that lives in the tree named for the same person. Described by Bachman (1839) [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Douglas Squirrel (standard). **Chickaree** (alternate, used by John Muir). The Chickaree name is onomatopoetic -- imitating the chatter call [1, 2, 3].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (nineteenth mammalian entry; seventeenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- SEVENTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- SIXTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75-76-77-78-79-80). The Sciuridae sextet. Ties Mustelidae (63-68). TWO-WAY TIE at SIX.
- **Genus:** *Tamiasciurus* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Fourth Sciuridae genus after *Marmota* (75-77), *Urocitellus* (78), *Callospermophilus* (79). Tree squirrels -- the first arboreal genus.
- **Species:** *T. douglasii* -- the Douglas Squirrel. Distributed along the Pacific coast from British Columbia to central California, in conifer forests from sea level to treeline [1, 2, 4].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE PNW'S SIGNATURE TREE SQUIRREL

### Body Form

The Douglas Squirrel is small and agile [1, 2, 4]:

- **Length:** 270-340 mm (11-13 inches) including tail. Body alone: 170-210 mm.
- **Weight:** 150-300 g (5-10 oz). Similar to Golden-mantled (79) but more slender and arboreal.
- **Tail:** 100-140 mm. Bushy, dark above, reddish-brown below with lighter fringe. Used for balance during canopy locomotion.
- **Body shape:** Slender, agile. Built for arboreal locomotion. Strong claws for gripping bark.

### Pelage

- **Body:** Dark olive-brown to reddish-brown above. Noticeably **darker** than other PNW tree squirrels. The dark color provides camouflage against conifer bark.
- **Belly:** **Orange-russet** -- a distinctive warm underside that distinguishes it from the Eastern Gray Squirrel (introduced). The warmest belly color in the Sciuridae sextet.
- **Eye:** Prominent pale eye ring -- a diagnostic feature.
- **Tail:** Bushy, dark above, reddish-brown below with lighter fringe.

## THE SOUND: LOUD SCOLDING CHATTER RATTLE -- 81st ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Chatter That Owns the Forest

The Douglas Squirrel's vocalization is a **loud, aggressive, sustained scolding chatter** -- the most prominent mammal sound in PNW conifer forests [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 3,000-10,000 Hz. Broad range reflecting the complex chatter structure.
- **Duration:** 2-10+ seconds per chatter bout. Individual notes within the chatter: 0.02-0.05 seconds each. The LONGEST sustained vocalization in the Sciuridae run.
- **Structure:** Continuous rapid stream of sharp notes -- a machine-gun rattle. Not single notes (Golden-mantled 79) or short bursts (Townsend's 78) but CONTINUOUS RATTLING. The chatter varies in tempo and intensity -- building, sustaining, and occasionally punctuated by barks.
- **Amplitude:** **E=5** -- LOUD. Audible at 200+ meters through dense conifer forest. The loudest and most persistent mammal vocalization in PNW forests. Dominates the forest soundscape.
- **Function:** Primarily TERRITORIAL DEFENSE. The chatter IS the territory boundary. Directed at intruding squirrels, hikers, predators, and anything else the squirrel perceives as trespassing. Also functions as alarm, but the territorial function is primary.

### Why E=5

The Douglas Squirrel's chatter warrants E=5 [1, 2, 3, 4]:

1. **Volume:** LOUD. Audible at 200+ meters through dense forest canopy. Penetrates foliage barriers.
2. **Duration:** Sustained for seconds to minutes. Not brief alarm calls but continuous vocal assault.
3. **Aggression:** The chatter is delivered with territorial fury. The squirrel sounds perpetually angry.
4. **Dominance:** The most vocal mammal in PNW forests. Dominates the forest soundscape. If you hear a mammal in a PNW conifer forest, it is almost certainly a Douglas Squirrel.

## ECOLOGY: THE CANOPY SENTINEL

### Habitat: Conifer Canopy -- First in the Engine

The Douglas Squirrel inhabits the **conifer canopy** -- the treetops of PNW coniferous forests [1, 2, 4, 5]:

- **Forest types:** Douglas-fir, western red cedar, Sitka spruce, western hemlock, noble fir, subalpine fir. The full range of PNW conifers.
- **Elevation:** Sea level to treeline. The broadest elevation range in the Sciuridae sextet.
- **Range:** Pacific coast from British Columbia to central California. In the PNW: western Washington, western Oregon, the Cascades, the Olympics. The PNW's signature squirrel.
- **Significance:** The engine's **first true canopy mammal**. The vertical axis opens. After alpine (74-77), grassland (78), and forest edge (79), the engine goes UP. The sextet habitat progression: alpine rock, alpine meadow, Olympic alpine, open grassland, forest edge, conifer canopy. Ground to treetops.

### Territory: The Chatter IS the Boundary

Douglas Squirrels are aggressively territorial [1, 2, 4]:

- **Territory size:** Approximately 0.1-0.5 hectares per individual.
- **Defense:** VOCAL. The scolding chatter IS the territory defense. The squirrel patrols its territory and chatter-assaults any intruder.
- **Middens:** Each territory has one or more "middens" -- massive piles of stripped cone scales at favorite feeding perches. Middens can be meters wide and decades old. The midden is the territorial anchor.

### Diet: The Conifer Seed Specialist

The Douglas Squirrel is a conifer seed specialist [1, 2, 4, 5]:

- **Conifer cones:** Primary food source. The squirrel strips cone scales to access seeds. Different techniques for different cone species. The incisors are specialized for this work.
- **Fungi:** Important food source, especially truffles (hypogeous fungi). The squirrel is a key truffle spore disperser -- spores pass through the digestive system and are deposited in fecal pellets, inoculating new forest soil with mycorrhizal fungi.
- **Caching:** Extensive. Cuts green cones from branches and caches them in cool, moist locations (stream banks, snowfields). Caches fungi by impaling mushrooms on branches to dry.

### Predators: The Canopy Arms Race

The Douglas Squirrel's primary predator is already in the engine [1, 2, 4]:

- **American Marten** (degree 65) -- the PRIMARY PREDATOR. Both arboreal. The marten hunts the Douglas Squirrel through the conifer canopy. This is **cross-sextet predation**: a Mustelidae sextet member (65) hunting a Sciuridae sextet member (80). The canopy arms race.
- **Northern Goshawk** -- forest-interior raptor that hunts squirrels through the canopy.
- **Great Horned Owl** -- nocturnal canopy predator.
- **Bobcat** -- ground-based, takes squirrels when they descend.

## JOHN MUIR AND THE DOUGLAS SQUIRREL

**John Muir** wrote extensively about the Douglas Squirrel in *The Mountains of California* (1894), devoting an entire chapter (Chapter XIV) to the species -- the only mammal to receive a full chapter. Muir called it **"by far the most influential of all the Sierra animals"** and described its personality with characteristic enthusiasm [3]:

Muir observed the squirrel's relentless vocal aggression, its territorial fury, and its role as the forest sentinel -- the animal that announces every visitor's presence. He called it the "Chickaree" and recognized its ecological importance as a seed disperser and forest architect.

The Muir connection embeds this species at the intersection of natural history, conservation, and literature -- a species important enough for America's most famous naturalist to devote an entire chapter.

## DAVID DOUGLAS: THE BOTANIST AND THE SQUIRREL

**David Douglas** (1799-1834) was a Scottish botanist who explored the Pacific Northwest in the 1820s-1830s, collecting plant specimens for the Royal Horticultural Society. His name is given to the Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii* in scientific nomenclature, but "Douglas-fir" in common English), as well as to this squirrel.

The connection is elegant: **the squirrel that lives in the tree named for the same person**. The Douglas Squirrel is the conifer canopy specialist; the Douglas-fir is the PNW's signature conifer. Both named for the Scottish botanist who documented them.
