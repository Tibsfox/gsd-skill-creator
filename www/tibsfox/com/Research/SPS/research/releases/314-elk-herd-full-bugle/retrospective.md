## Discoveries

1. **Full-herd bugling at subalpine meadow-forest interface is the Pacific Northwest's largest terrestrial mammal acoustic event by species population.** Deer bugling (smaller, quieter), bear vocalizations (non-seasonal, dispersed), cougar vocalizations (solitary, non-seasonal rut) -- none produce the concentrated collective acoustic density of Elk rut at the meadow interface. The Elk full-herd bugling event is a unique ecological acoustic phenomenon in the PNW.
2. **The elevated primary frequency (400-600 Hz) for an animal of this mass is the bugle's most interesting acoustic anomaly.** Most mammals this size (300-450 kg) produce primary vocalizations below 300 Hz. The Elk's Phase 2 whistle at 400-600 Hz is produced by a laryngeal structure that functions differently from most large-ungulate vocal apparatus. The carrying distance and emotional signature of the bugle come from this frequency anomaly; a low-frequency bellow from the same animal would not carry the same acoustic weight.
3. **The Mount Rainier and Olympic National Park subalpine meadows are the most acoustically defined Elk bugling habitat in Washington State.** Meadow openness allows carrying distance of 1-2 km in calm conditions. The forest edge reflects and focuses sound toward the meadow interior. The interface geometry amplifies the collective event. Geography matters for the E=8 calibration.
