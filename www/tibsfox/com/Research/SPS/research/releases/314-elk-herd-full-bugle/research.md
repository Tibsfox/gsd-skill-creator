## THE DIFFERENCE FROM DEGREE 301 -- WHY E=8 INSTEAD OF E=7

### Individual vs. Herd: A Qualitative Difference

The S36 engine documents the Roosevelt Elk full bugle at degree 301 (E=7). The elk herd full bugle at degree 314 calibrates to E=8. The difference is not mere addition of identical sounds:

- **Multiple pitches simultaneously.** Different elk bulls produce bugles at different fundamental pitches, reflecting their individual body size and acoustic anatomy. When three to eight bulls are bugling simultaneously in the same valley, the simultaneous different-pitch calls create a harmonic chord of overlapping bugles that is more acoustically complex than any individual call. The listener in the river bottom is surrounded by calls at different frequencies, from different directions, at different distances.
- **The reverb effect.** River bottom terrain -- the valley floor enclosed by forested slopes, with water reflecting acoustic energy and the valley walls channeling and amplifying sound -- creates a natural reverb chamber. A single elk bugle in this space is striking. Multiple simultaneous bugles in this space, from multiple directions, with the reverb extending and overlapping each call, create an acoustic saturation that fills the valley.
- **The antler percussion.** During peak rut, bulls frequently clash antlers in direct competition. The antler-clash sound -- a sharp, loud crack followed by the sustained grinding of locked antlers -- adds a percussive element to the ambient vocal event. The combination of bugling calls and antler percussion is the complete rut soundscape.
- **The duration.** The full herd bugling event during peak rut can be sustained for extended periods -- from pre-dawn through mid-morning, and again in the evening. Individual bulls bugle, other bulls respond, confrontations occur, the cycle repeats. A listener in the river bottom during this period is in the acoustic event for hours, not minutes.

## *Cervus canadensis* -- THE SPECIES

### Two Elk in Washington State

Washington State hosts two elk subspecies with different ecologies:

**Roosevelt Elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*).** The largest North American elk. Bull body weight 700-1,100 lbs. Named for Theodore Roosevelt. Distributed in the Olympic Peninsula (the Olympic herd is the largest remaining Roosevelt elk population in the world), western Washington, and the Coast Range. Habitat: old-growth forest, river bottoms, forest-grassland edges. The Olympic herd is the engine's primary Roosevelt elk reference.

**Rocky Mountain Elk (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*).** Smaller than Roosevelt but still the largest ungulate in most Cascade ecosystems. Bull body weight 600-900 lbs. Distributed primarily in the Cascades, eastern Washington, the Blue Mountains, and the Okanagan region. Colonized parts of western Washington following the decline of Roosevelt elk populations in some areas.

Both subspecies produce bugles during the fall rut. The Roosevelt elk bugle tends to be deeper and lower-pitched due to body mass. The Rocky Mountain elk bugle is slightly higher-pitched and more widely recorded in mainstream wildlife documentation. Both are included in the full herd bugling event documented at degree 314.

(Sources: Cervus canadensis taxonomy; Roosevelt elk roosevelti body mass 700-1100 lbs; Roosevelt elk Olympic Peninsula; largest remaining Roosevelt elk population; Rocky Mountain elk nelsoni body mass 600-900 lbs; Cascade and eastern Washington distribution; subspecies bugle comparison)

## THE BUGLE -- ACOUSTIC ANATOMY OF THE CALL

### The Most Complex Vocalization of Any North American Ungulate

The elk bugle is acoustically complex for a large mammal vocalization:

- **Structure.** The bugle begins with a series of low grunts or chuckles, rises through an ascending glissando to a high-pitched scream, and ends with a series of grunt-coughs. The full sequence: grunt-glissando-scream-grunt-coughs. The ascending glissando -- the characteristic rising pitch that makes the bugle identifiable from a great distance -- spans 1-2 octaves in under two seconds.
- **Frequency.** Fundamental frequency: 200-1,000 Hz for the grunt portion; rising to 800-2,500 Hz at the peak. Harmonics extending to 4,000+ Hz. The harmonic structure of the bugle peak is what makes it carry across valleys: the combination of fundamental and harmonics creates a sound that penetrates both the forest and the open valley floor.
- **Duration.** 3-10 seconds per complete bugle sequence. Bulls bugle repeatedly during rut, with intervals of seconds to minutes between calls.
- **Carry distance.** In river bottom terrain under favorable atmospheric conditions (cool, stable air in the early morning), an elk bugle is audible for 1-3 km. Multiple simultaneous bugles in a valley can be heard from ridge positions several km distant.
- **Function.** The bugle serves three functions: advertising presence and fitness to cows (estrous selection); advertising size and dominance to rival bulls (deterring competition without physical confrontation); and coordinating within a herd unit (bulls maintaining spatial awareness of rival locations through vocal monitoring). The call is honest advertising: larger, older bulls produce lower-frequency, longer-duration bugles that correlate with competitive success.

(Sources: Elk bugle acoustic structure; grunt-glissando-scream-grunt-coughs sequence; frequency 200-2500 Hz primary; harmonics to 4000+ Hz; duration 3-10 seconds; carry distance 1-3 km; bugle function: sexual advertisement and rival deterrence; frequency correlation with bull size and age)

## THE RIVER BOTTOM HABITAT

### Why the Valley Floor Is the Acoustic Stage

The river bottom ecosystem is the elk herd's rut habitat and the acoustic stage for the full bugling event:

- **The vegetation structure.** River bottom forest in the Pacific Northwest -- black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), red alder (*Alnus rubra*), vine maple (*Acer circinatum*), Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) in coastal sites -- creates a specific acoustic environment: the dense overhead canopy traps and reflects sound downward; the vertical stems create lateral diffraction that spreads sound horizontally; the leaf canopy in September-October (still present but beginning to fall) adds a diffuse reflective surface.
- **The valley geometry.** River bottoms occupy valley floors between forested slopes. The valley geometry -- V-shaped or U-shaped cross-section with sloping forested walls -- creates a natural amphitheater that amplifies and focuses sound. Bugles produced in the valley floor reach the slopes and reflect back, creating the reverb effect that makes the river bottom rut soundscape acoustically distinct from the same calls in open terrain.
- **The water.** Rivers and creeks in the valley bottom add acoustic energy: the continuous white noise of flowing water provides an ambient acoustic baseline against which the bugles stand out in sharp contrast. The water also reflects high-frequency sound energy, adding to the spatial complexity of the acoustic environment.
- **The elk habitat preference.** Elk prefer river bottoms during the rut for practical reasons: the dense vegetation provides cover for cows and for bulls competing for them; the flat terrain allows efficient movement; the water provides drinking access; the grass and forb growth in riparian areas provides forage. The river bottom is where the herd concentrates, and where the bulls concentrate around the herd, during peak rut.

(Sources: River bottom vegetation: black cottonwood, red alder, vine maple, Sitka spruce; valley geometry as acoustic amplifier; water reflection and ambient baseline; elk riparian habitat preference during rut; herd concentration in river bottom)

## THE RUT -- SOCIAL DYNAMICS AND SOUND

### September Through November: The Full Event

The elk rut in Washington peaks in October, with the primary acoustic event running from late September through early November:

- **The pre-rut (late August-September).** Bulls' antlers have hardened after shedding velvet (August). Bulls begin bugling sporadically, testing their voices and beginning to establish dominance hierarchies. Cows are not yet in estrus. The soundscape: occasional individual bugles, increasing through September.
- **Peak rut (mid-September to mid-October).** Bulls actively gather harems of cows. The dominant bull (the "herd bull") attempts to maintain exclusive breeding access to a group of 5-20 cows against challengers. Sub-dominant bulls ("satellite bulls") remain at the periphery, bugling constantly to attract cows away from the herd bull and to challenge each other. Peak rut is the period of maximum acoustic activity: multiple bulls bugling continuously from pre-dawn through mid-morning, with afternoon and evening activity also high.
- **The confrontations.** When two bulls decide to fight rather than deter through vocalization alone, the confrontation involves parallel walking, direct approach, and antler clash. The antler clash (sharp crack on impact, sustained grinding as bulls push against each other, duration of seconds to minutes) is audible from considerable distance and adds percussion to the ambient bugling.
- **Post-rut (late October-November).** Breeding activity declines as cows complete estrus. Bugling decreases but does not stop immediately. The soundscape of late October is still characterized by occasional bugles, mostly from satellite bulls that did not secure breeding access and continue advertising.

(Sources: Washington elk rut timing; velvet shed August; pre-rut sporadically September; peak rut mid-September to mid-October; herd bull behavior; satellite bull behavior; antler clash acoustics; post-rut November decline)
