Individual vs. Herd — Why E=8

The individual elk bugle (degree 301, E=7) is the call of one animal. The herd bugling event is qualitatively different: multiple bulls at different pitches create a harmonic chord of overlapping calls covering the full frequency range (200–2,500 Hz) simultaneously. The valley floor’s natural reverb chamber extends each call and overlaps it with the next. Antler clashes add percussion. The sustained duration of the rut — hours of peak activity during October mornings — means the acoustic event is not momentary but environmental: the listener in the river bottom is inside the sound, surrounded by calls from multiple directions, for an extended period. E=8 is the calibration for this complete event.

The Two Elk

Roosevelt Elk

Cervus canadensis roosevelti

Largest North American elk. Bull 700–1,100 lbs. Olympic Peninsula (largest remaining Roosevelt population in the world), western Cascades, Coast Range. Deeper, lower-pitched bugle due to body mass. Named for Theodore Roosevelt.

Rocky Mountain Elk

Cervus canadensis nelsoni

Bull 600–900 lbs. Cascades, eastern Washington, Blue Mountains, Okanagan. Slightly higher-pitched bugle, more widely recorded. Both subspecies present in Washington, both participating in the full herd bugling event.

Acoustic Profile — The Bugle

Structure: Grunt(s) → ascending glissando → high scream → grunt-coughs. The complete sequence spans 3–10 seconds. The ascending glissando is the signature element: rising 1–2 octaves in under 2 seconds.

Frequency: 200–1,000 Hz for grunt; 800–2,500 Hz at peak; harmonics to 4,000+ Hz. The harmonic structure ensures carry across valleys.

Carry distance: 1–3 km in river bottom terrain, cool morning air. Multiple simultaneous bugles audible from ridge positions several km distant.

Herd event frequency range: 200–2,500 Hz (combined multiple bulls). Plus antler clash percussion: sharp transient impact (broadband) + sustained grinding (100–500 Hz). Full sonic spectrum covered.

The River Bottom

River bottom forest — black cottonwood, red alder, vine maple, Sitka spruce in coastal sites — creates the acoustic stage for the rut. The valley floor between forested slopes forms a natural amphitheater: sound produced on the valley floor reaches the slopes and reflects back, creating reverb that extends and overlaps individual calls. The river’s flowing water provides an ambient acoustic baseline against which the bugles stand in sharp contrast. Elk concentrate in the river bottom during rut for practical reasons: dense vegetation provides cover, flat terrain allows efficient movement, water is available, and the riparian grass and forb growth provides forage. The river bottom is where the herd is; the herd is where the sound is.
