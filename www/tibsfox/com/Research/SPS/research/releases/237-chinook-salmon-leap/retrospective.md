## What Worked

1. **The Chinook-Orca nexus is the entry's most important ecological finding.** The direct dependency relationship -- SRKW at 80-90%+ Chinook diet, SRKW recovery impossible without Chinook recovery -- is among the most consequential predator-prey relationships in Pacific Northwest conservation and deserves its own documentation.
2. **The E=6 placement (vs. E=5 at degree 236) is physically grounded.** The mass-velocity argument -- a 15-kg Chinook hitting the water generates more acoustic energy than a 2-kg Coho -- is the correct rationale for the species-specific entry being one unit higher energy than the genus-level entry.
3. **The two run types (spring + fall Chinook) add genuine biological depth.** They are not merely temporal variants -- they represent ecologically distinct life histories with different conservation profiles and different human relationships.
4. **The Celilo Falls entry is historically essential.** The inundation of the most important salmon fishing site in the world in 1957 is a cultural trauma of the first order. It belongs in the engine's permanent record.

## Discoveries

1. **The Kenai River record Chinook (44.2 kg, 97.5 lbs, 1985) provides scale reference.** The world record fish anchors the claim that Chinook are in a different size class from other Pacific salmon. The acoustic implication is real: a fish of this mass hitting water at velocity produces a sound unlike any other salmon.
2. **Spring Chinook warming vulnerability is a near-term climate crisis.** Spring Chinook enter the river months before spawning and hold in cold pools. As summer river temperatures rise with climate change, these holding fish are increasingly stressed before they can spawn. The spring Chinook crisis is immediate and accelerating.
