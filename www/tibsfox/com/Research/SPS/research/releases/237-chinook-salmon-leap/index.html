KING SALMON — FIRST SPECIES-SPECIFIC SALMONIDAE — ORCA-OBLIGATE

Degree 237 is the first individual species entry in the Salmonidae chapter opened at degree 236. Chinook is first because it is largest (to 50 kg / 110 lbs), most culturally central to Pacific Northwest indigenous nations, and ecologically essential as the primary food source of Southern Resident Orca. Multiple ESUs Threatened or Endangered under ESA. Cross-reference: Pacific Salmon overview at degree 236.

The Sound of a King Falling

A Chinook Salmon returning from the ocean weighs 4.5 to 20 kg or more. When it launches from the pool at the base of a falls — powering its body through air, attempting to clear the obstruction in its natal stream — and re-enters the water, the impact is substantial and distinctive. This is not the delicate splash of a small fish. It is a broadband impact sound: water displacement, a large body striking the surface at velocity, turbulence, the churning of powerful fins fighting current. At active falls during a run, the sound is continuous — a cascade of heavy impacts layered over the roar of the water.

"The Chinook does not leap gracefully. It launches, hangs in air for a moment, and falls. The impact is the sound of the largest Pacific salmon asserting itself against gravity, current, and every obstacle the river places in its path."

Acoustic Profile — Waterfall Leap

Body impact on water: broadband, 50–2000+ Hz, short duration
Water displacement (launch): low-frequency, 20–200 Hz
Falls ambient: continuous white-noise roar (background)
Run density: episodic heavy impacts; E=6 during active run

The Chinook leap is E=6 (versus E=5 for the genus-level spawning sound at degree 236) because the Chinook’s mass produces heavier impacts than smaller species. A 15-kg Chinook hitting the water generates more acoustic energy than a 2-kg Coho or Pink Salmon.

Two Run Types

Washington State Chinook are divided into two ecologically distinct run types:

  • Spring Chinook ("Springers"): Enter rivers February–July. Hold in deep cold pools for months before spawning in late summer. Fat-rich, prized by fishers. Most endangered: warming summer river temperatures threaten holding fish before spawning.
  • Fall Chinook: Enter rivers August–October. Spawn in fall. More numerous than spring Chinook in most systems. The Columbia River fall Chinook run is one of the most studied salmon populations in science.

The Chinook–Orca Nexus

Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW — approximately 73–75 individuals in J, K, L pods, listed Endangered 2005) depend on Chinook for 80–90%+ of their caloric intake. This is not substitutable: SRKW are fish-eating specialists who learned Chinook-hunting culturally across generations. When Chinook runs are poor, SRKW lose condition, fail to reproduce, and die. The SRKW population is directly limited by Chinook abundance. Saving the Orca requires restoring the Chinook.

Conservation

MULTIPLE ESA LISTINGS. Puget Sound Chinook ESU: Threatened. Snake River fall Chinook: Threatened. Snake River spring/summer Chinook: Threatened. Upper Columbia spring Chinook: Endangered. Threats: dams, habitat degradation, ocean warming, hatchery genetic effects. Elwha River: dam removal 2011–2014 documented Chinook returns above former dam sites within a decade — proof that habitat restoration works at landscape scale.
