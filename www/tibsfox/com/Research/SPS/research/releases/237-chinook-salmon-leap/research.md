## THE KING SALMON -- WHY CHINOOK IS FIRST

### The Species-Specific Salmonidae Chapter Begins

Degree 236 documented Pacific Salmon as a genus-level overview (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) -- the FIRST FISH in the engine, the first Salmonidae, the keystone species documented at the genus level. From degree 237 onward, the engine is entering individual species within the Salmonidae family. Chinook is first for reasons that are biological, cultural, and ecological:

- **The largest Pacific salmon.** Chinook (*O. tshawytscha*) adults typically range 60-120 cm (24-48 in) in length and 4.5-20 kg (10-45 lbs) in weight. Record fish exceed 50 kg (110 lbs) -- the Kenai River record Chinook was 97.5 lbs (44.2 kg). No other Pacific salmon approaches this size. The Chinook's mass is the origin of its common name: King Salmon.
- **The most culturally significant.** For virtually every Coast Salish, Chinook, Quinault, Lummi, Tulalip, and other Pacific Northwest indigenous nation, Chinook salmon is the First Salmon -- the most ceremonially important, the one whose return triggers the First Salmon ceremony, the one whose size and abundance signals the health of the system. The word "Chinook" itself was applied first to the people of the lower Columbia River (the Chinook Nation), then to the trade language (Chinook Jargon), then to the warm winter wind (Chinook wind), and finally to the salmon. The salmon carries the name of a people.
- **The Orca's essential prey.** Southern Resident Orca (approximately 75 individuals in three pods -- J, K, L) depend on Chinook salmon for 90%+ of their caloric intake. The Southern Resident Orca population is in crisis, and the primary driver of that crisis is Chinook salmon scarcity. The Chinook and the Orca are locked in a dependency relationship that conservation must address simultaneously -- you cannot save the Orca without restoring the Chinook.
- **Cross-reference to degree 236.** The Pacific Salmon overview at degree 236 covered all five Washington species, including Chinook. The engine documented Chinook's size, run timing, cultural significance, and ESA listings in overview form. Degree 237 is the species-specific deep dive: the Chinook's particular ecology, behavior, biology, and -- crucially -- its sound.

## THE SOUND -- THE WATERFALL LEAP, E=6

### Physics of a Large Fish Falling Through Air

The "sound" of this degree is the waterfall leap -- one of the most dramatic and visually iconic behaviors of the Pacific salmon's upstream migration:

- **Why salmon leap waterfalls.** Chinook salmon return from the ocean to their natal stream by smell and magnetic orientation. The route takes them upstream against current, over falls, through rapids, and past every obstacle the river places in their path. They do not eat during this migration -- they live entirely on stored fat. Waterfalls are the most conspicuous obstacle: the fish must leap the falls or die at their base. Some falls are within leaping range; others are not. Falls higher than about 2-3 meters are usually impassable without fish ladders or dam removal.
- **The physics of the leap.** A Chinook does not leap waterfalls gracefully. It launches from the standing wave or pool at the base of a falls, powering its body through the air, and attempts to clear enough height to enter the water above the falls. Large fish (10-20 kg) generate enormous momentum -- the arc through the air is brief and violent. Many attempts fail: the fish hits the falls face, tumbles back to the pool below, recovers, and tries again.
- **The impact sound.** When a large Chinook launches from the pool, the water displacement creates a heavy splash. When it re-enters the water (either in the pool above if successful, or in the pool below if not), the impact produces the characteristic "body-slap" sound: a broadband impact -- frequencies from below 100 Hz to above 1 kHz -- created by a large mass (10-20 kg) hitting water at significant velocity. The sound is distinct from the lighter splash of a smaller fish. The larger the fish, the heavier the impact.
- **At active falls.** At prime falls during a run -- Cedar Falls on the Cedar River, Sunset Falls on the Skykomish, Tumwater Falls in Olympia -- dozens to hundreds of fish may be attempting to leap at any given moment. The acoustic environment is a continuous layering of heavy impacts over the white-noise roar of the falls. The sound is E=6: substantial, physically present, distinctive. Not the E=7-8 of a concentrated salmon redd at peak, but the episodic heavy impact of large fish moving through a specific obstruction point.
- **The "massive body-slap waterfall base" characterization.** The most dramatic moment acoustically is when a large Chinook launches from the standing wave at the very base of a falls -- the point of maximum turbulence, where the falling water strikes the pool and churns. This is where the fish are densest, where the impacts are heaviest, and where the acoustic environment is most intense.

## CHINOOK BIOLOGY -- THE KING'S LIFECYCLE

### The Largest and Most Complex Pacific Salmon

- **Two run types.** Washington State has two distinct Chinook run types, each with different ecology and timing:
  - **Spring Chinook ("Springers").** Enter rivers February-July. Spawn in late summer (August-September). Spring Chinook are fat, firm-fleshed fish that enter the river early and hold in deep cold pools for months before spawning. They have more time in the river and thus more stored energy at river entry than fall Chinook. For this reason, spring Chinook are the most prized by commercial and sport fishers -- their higher fat content produces superior flavor. Spring Chinook are also among the most endangered: many spring Chinook ESUs are listed as Threatened or Endangered because their long river holding period makes them vulnerable to warming water temperatures in summer.
  - **Fall Chinook.** Enter rivers August-October. Spawn in fall. Fall Chinook are more numerous than spring Chinook in most Washington rivers. They enter later, spawn sooner, and spend less time in the river. The Columbia River fall Chinook is one of the most studied salmon populations in the world.
- **Juvenile rearing.** Chinook juveniles show more variation in freshwater rearing time than most Pacific salmon: some juveniles migrate to the ocean within their first year (called "subyearlings" or "ocean-type" Chinook); others remain in freshwater for their first year and migrate as yearlings ("stream-type" or "inland" Chinook). This variation corresponds to geography: stream-type Chinook typically come from interior rivers (Columbia interior, Snake River), while ocean-type Chinook come from coastal rivers closer to the ocean.
- **Ocean migration.** Chinook spend 1-7 years in the ocean before returning to spawn. Their ocean distribution ranges from California to Alaska and into the Bering Sea. Some Chinook populations mix extensively at sea -- a Columbia River Chinook may share feeding grounds with an Alaskan river Chinook thousands of miles from home.
- **Size and mass.** The Chinook's size is partly a function of time at sea: fish that spend more years in the ocean before returning are larger. A two-ocean Chinook returning at age 3 may weigh 5-8 kg; a five-ocean Chinook returning at age 6-7 may weigh 20-40 kg. The largest Chinook on record are ocean survivors that delayed return to an exceptional age.
- **Sexual dimorphism at spawning.** Like all Pacific salmon, Chinook adults undergo dramatic physical transformation during the upstream migration: the kype (hooked jaw) develops in males, the body takes on reddish spawning coloration (less dramatic than Sockeye but present), the flesh changes from orange-pink to pale, and the body begins the irreversible senescence process that will end in death after spawning.

(Sources: Spring and fall Chinook run timing; stream-type vs. ocean-type life history; Chinook ocean range; age-size relationships; spawning transformation biology; *O. tshawytscha* lifecycle)

## THE CHINOOK-ORCA NEXUS -- THE MOST CRITICAL DEPENDENCY IN THE SALISH SEA

### When One Species Cannot Survive Without Another

The relationship between Southern Resident Orca and Chinook salmon is one of the most important ecological facts in Pacific Northwest conservation:

- **Southern Resident Orca (SRKW).** The Southern Resident Killer Whales are a genetically and culturally distinct population of Orca (*Orcinus orca*) that inhabit the Salish Sea -- the inland waters of Washington and British Columbia. Three pods: J, K, and L. Current population approximately 73-75 individuals (2026), having declined from about 100 individuals in the 1990s. Listed as Endangered under the US Endangered Species Act since 2005.
- **Chinook as essential prey.** Unlike Bigg's (transient) Orca that eat marine mammals, Southern Residents are fish-eating specialists. Chinook salmon constitute 80-90% or more of their diet. The preference for Chinook is cultural -- passed from mother to calf over generations -- as much as it is nutritional. Southern Residents know how to find and catch Chinook; other prey items are secondary.
- **The scarcity crisis.** When Chinook returns are poor -- as they have been in many years since the 1990s -- Southern Residents cannot substitute another food source at the same rate. They lose condition. Reproductive success drops. Calves are born and die. The population declines. The direct causal link between Chinook abundance and SRKW survival is among the best-documented predator-prey relationships in marine ecology.
- **Why Chinook scarcity matters for Orca.** The SRKW's nutritional dependency on Chinook means that every ESA-listed Chinook ESU in the SRKW's range is simultaneously a threat to the Orca. The Puget Sound Chinook ESU (Threatened) is the Orca's primary local food source. Snake River Chinook (some ESUs Endangered) were a major component of the SRKW's diet before Snake River dams impeded their runs. The SRKW's recovery depends directly on Chinook recovery.
- **The management complexity.** Managing for Chinook recovery requires addressing dams, habitat, hatcheries, and ocean conditions -- while simultaneously managing Chinook fisheries for tribal treaty rights (guaranteed by the Boldt Decision), commercial interests, and sport fishing. The Chinook is the most managed, most studied, most politically contested species in Pacific Northwest ecology.

(Sources: Southern Resident Killer Whale biology; SRKW Chinook dietary dependency; SRKW population 2024-2026; SRKW Endangered ESA listing 2005; Puget Sound Chinook ESU; Snake River Chinook ESUs; Boldt Decision management framework; SRKW recovery plan)

## CHINOOK ESA LISTINGS -- THE CONSERVATION CRISIS

### Multiple Evolutionarily Significant Units in Crisis

Pacific Chinook are managed as Evolutionarily Significant Units (ESUs) -- genetically and ecologically distinct population segments that Congress intended the ESA to protect. Several Washington-area Chinook ESUs are listed:

- **Puget Sound Chinook ESU** -- THREATENED. The Chinook of Puget Sound rivers (Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, Puyallup, Cedar, Nisqually, and others) are listed as Threatened under the ESA. This is the SRKW's primary local food source. Recovery requires habitat restoration throughout the Puget Sound watershed -- a multi-billion dollar, multi-decade project.
- **Snake River fall Chinook ESU** -- THREATENED. The fall Chinook of the Snake River (the Columbia's primary tributary) are Threatened. The Snake River system has been profoundly altered by four lower Snake River dams (Ice Harbor, Little Goose, Lower Monumental, Lower Granite) that block fish passage and warm the water. The potential removal of these dams has been under policy discussion for three decades; the Biden administration's 2024 framework and ongoing tribal negotiations may result in dam removal.
- **Snake River spring/summer Chinook ESU** -- THREATENED (may be more severely endangered in specific populations). Snake River spring and summer Chinook must travel extraordinary distances (hundreds of miles) through dam-impeded water to reach natal streams in Idaho and Oregon. Their populations have crashed from historical abundances.
- **Upper Columbia spring Chinook ESU** -- ENDANGERED. One of the most critically imperiled Chinook populations in the US. Mainstem Columbia dams and habitat degradation in the upper Columbia system have reduced this ESU to a fraction of historical returns.
- **Elwha River Chinook.** The Elwha River (Olympic Peninsula) Chinook is one of the most watched recovery stories in salmon conservation. The Elwha and Glines Canyon dams (removed 2011-2014 in the largest dam removal project in US history at the time) blocked Chinook from over 70 miles of river habitat. Within a decade of dam removal, Chinook returns above the former dam sites were documented -- habitat was recovering. The Elwha is the proof-of-concept for dam removal as salmon recovery tool.

(Sources: Puget Sound Chinook ESU listing; Snake River fall Chinook ESU; Snake River spring/summer Chinook ESU; Upper Columbia spring Chinook Endangered; lower Snake River dam removal discussions; Elwha dam removal 2011-2014; Chinook recovery documentation)

## CHINOOK AT KEY SITES -- THE ACOUSTIC LANDSCAPE

### Where the Leaping Sound Is Heard

Several Washington State sites are known for spectacular Chinook leaping:

- **Cedar Falls, Cedar River (Maple Valley/Landsburg).** The Cedar River is Seattle's primary water supply and an important Chinook and Coho spawning stream. Cedar Falls (also called Landsburg Dam area) is a location where salmon congregate and attempt to leap. The Cedar River watershed is closed to the public above Landsburg Dam, but the salmon run below is visible and audible from access points. Chinook leaping at this site produce the characteristic heavy-impact sounds documented in this degree.
- **Tumwater Falls, Deschutes River (Olympia).** Tumwater Falls in downtown Olympia is one of the most accessible salmon-viewing sites in Washington. Chinook and Coho can be observed and heard leaping from late summer through fall. The falls are below a fish ladder that allows passage above; the fish are concentrated at the base.
- **Sunset Falls, Skykomish River.** Sunset Falls on the North Fork Skykomish River is a natural waterfall that historically blocked upstream salmon passage. A fish ladder was constructed; Chinook use the ladder but also attempt the natural falls. The site is in the Cascade foothills east of Seattle.
- **Rogue River and Klamath system (Oregon, range extension).** Washington State's southern range connects to Oregon systems where some of the most dramatic Chinook runs occur. While outside Washington proper, the acoustic and behavioral patterns are consistent.
- **The Kenai River (Alaska).** The most famous Chinook river in North America -- home of the world record Chinook (97.5 lbs, 44.2 kg, 1985). The Kenai's Chinook runs produce some of the most dramatic large-fish leaping sounds in the species' range. Beyond the engine's geographic scope but important for scale reference.

(Sources: Cedar River salmon run; Cedar Falls Landsburg; Tumwater Falls Olympia; Sunset Falls Skykomish; Kenai River Chinook; fish ladder technology)

## INDIGENOUS RELATIONSHIP -- THE KING AMONG SALMON

### Chinook at the Center of Northwest Coast Culture

- **The First Salmon.** For most Pacific Northwest indigenous nations, the First Salmon ceremony is focused specifically on Chinook. The first Chinook of the season signals abundance, triggers ceremony, and sets the relational framework for the entire year's harvest. The ceremony's specific protocols vary by nation, but the common elements -- acknowledgment, thanks, return of bones to water, communal sharing -- reflect a relationship between human community and salmon community that indigenous nations have maintained for thousands of years.
- **The Chinook Nation.** The word "Chinook" derives from the Chinook people (Shoalwater Bay Chinook, Chinook Indian Nation, Clatsop-Nehalem Confederated Tribes) of the lower Columbia River -- the people who inhabited the most important salmon fishing sites on the continent. The application of their name to the salmon, the trade language (Chinook Jargon -- the lingua franca of the Pacific Northwest trade network), and the warm winter wind reflects the cultural centrality of this nation at the geographic center of Pacific Northwest civilization.
- **Celilo Falls.** Before its inundation by The Dalles Dam in 1957, Celilo Falls on the Columbia River was possibly the most important salmon fishing site in the world and the most important gathering place in the Pacific Northwest. Chinook were taken there in extraordinary numbers by fishers from dozens of nations. The destruction of Celilo Falls is one of the most culturally traumatic events in Pacific Northwest indigenous history -- living elders remember hearing the roar of the falls go silent as the reservoir filled on March 10, 1957.
- **Treaty fishing rights.** Chinook salmon are the primary subject of Pacific Northwest tribal treaty fishing rights. The Boldt Decision (1974) guaranteed tribes 50% of the harvestable salmon, with Chinook as the most important species. Tribal co-management of Chinook salmon is now a central feature of Washington State salmon management -- tribes and state agencies are co-equal managers under the Boldt framework.

(Sources: First Salmon ceremony; Chinook Nation etymology; Chinook Jargon; Celilo Falls history; The Dalles Dam 1957; Boldt Decision 1974; tribal co-management; treaty fishing rights)
