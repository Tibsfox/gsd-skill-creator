## THE HOLLYWOOD HAWK CALL

### The Most Ubiquitous Wildlife Sound in Cinema

The Red-tailed Hawk's call is the most borrowed sound in the history of American filmmaking:

- **The substitution.** Sometime in the mid-20th century -- the exact origin is disputed, but it appears to have begun in the 1940s-1950s, likely because the Red-tailed Hawk's call was easily available as a library sound and was far more dramatic than the actual calls of the birds being depicted -- Hollywood began using the Red-tailed Hawk's scream as the default raptor vocalization. This substitution has been repeated thousands of times across film and television in the decades since.
- **The Bald Eagle substitution (cross-reference degree 221).** The most famous instance is the Bald Eagle. The Bald Eagle's actual contact call (documented at degree 221) is a thin, descending kee-kee-kee -- reedy, almost plaintive, utterly unsuitable for the visual power of America's national symbol soaring over a landscape. Hollywood replaced it with the Red-tailed Hawk's ragged, descending scream, which projects the power and wildness that the visual image requires.
- **Other substitutions.** The Red-tail's call has been used in films and TV shows for: Bald Eagles, Golden Eagles (degree 223), Peregrine Falcons, Ospreys (degree 224), generic unnamed "eagles" in fantasy and science fiction, and countless other raptor species worldwide. If a raptor appears on screen and screams dramatically, it is almost certainly the Red-tailed Hawk's voice, regardless of what species is depicted.
- **The Tolkien example.** The Great Eagles of Middle-earth in Peter Jackson's *Lord of the Rings* and *The Hobbit* film series call with the Red-tailed Hawk's voice. These are fantasy creatures with no real-world species equivalent, and yet they scream with *Buteo jamaicensis*. The reach of this sound extends from natural history into pure fiction.
- **Why the Red-tail?** The Red-tailed Hawk's call is perfect for cinematic purposes: it is loud, ragged, clearly non-human, emotionally urgent without being funny, and produces an immediate visceral response of "raptor" in an audience that has been conditioned to associate the sound with aerial predators. The conditioning is circular -- audiences hear the Red-tail's call as "eagle" because films taught them to, and films use the Red-tail's call because it sounds like the culturally established "eagle sound."
- **The correction.** The engine documented the Bald Eagle's actual call at degree 221 -- thin, descending, surprising. Degree 228 documents the source of the sound that replaced it. The arc from 221 to 228 is a complete correction of the most widespread wildlife sound misattribution in popular culture.

## THE BUTEO TRANSITION

### From Accipiter to Buteo

Degree 228 marks a significant taxonomic transition within the raptor arc:

- **Accipiters vs. buteos.** Degrees 225-227 documented the three accipiters (*Accipiter gentilis*, *A. cooperii*, *A. striatus*) -- forest hawks with short wings, long tails, and pursuit-hunting strategies optimized for dense vegetation. Degree 228 introduces the first buteo: *Buteo jamaicensis*, the Red-tailed Hawk. Buteos are the opposite in ecological character: broad wings, relatively short tails, and a soaring hunting strategy optimized for open country.
- **The genus *Buteo*.** *Buteo* is the Latin word for a type of hawk -- the name has been used since antiquity. The genus contains approximately 28 species distributed across North and South America, Europe, Africa, and Asia. In North America, the buteos include: Red-tailed Hawk (*B. jamaicensis*), Red-shouldered Hawk (*B. lineatus*), Broad-winged Hawk (*B. platypterus*), Swainson's Hawk (*B. swainsoni*), Rough-legged Hawk (*B. lagopus*), and Ferruginous Hawk (*B. regalis*).
- **Soaring as the buteo strategy.** Where the accipiters hunt by ambush and short pursuit through forest, buteos hunt by soaring on thermals and updrafts at altitude, watching the ground below, and stooping onto prey when located. The Red-tailed Hawk's broad wings are thermals wings -- they generate lift efficiently, allowing sustained soaring with minimal energy expenditure. The accipiter's short wings are maneuver wings -- they generate rapid thrust in tight spaces but are not energy-efficient in sustained flight.
- **The ecological division.** Accipiters = forest. Buteos = open country. This division is the fundamental ecological split between the two major hawk groups in North America, and the transition from the accipiter trio (225-227) to the first buteo (228) is the raptor arc's sharpest ecological shift.

## THE RED-TAILED HAWK -- *BUTEO JAMAICENSIS*

### North America's Most Common Hawk

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Buteo*. Species: *jamaicensis* -- named for Jamaica, where the type specimen was collected (the species is found from Alaska to Panama and the Caribbean). The "Red-tailed" name refers to the brick-red upper tail of adult birds, one of the most reliable field marks for adult identification.
- **Size.** Large. Females (larger sex): 900-2,000 g (2-4.4 lbs), body length 45-65 cm (18-26 in), wingspan 105-141 cm (41-56 in). Males: 690-1,300 g (1.5-2.9 lbs). Substantially larger than the Cooper's Hawk or Sharp-shinned Hawk, but smaller than the Bald Eagle or Golden Eagle.
- **The red tail.** The adult Red-tailed Hawk's upper tail is a distinctive brick-red or rufous color -- visible from above in flight or when the bird is perched with its tail fanned. This is the most diagnostic feature for adults. Juveniles lack the red tail; they have brown-and-buff banded tails and are more easily confused with other buteos. Full adult plumage is typically achieved in the second or third year.
- **The highly variable plumage.** The Red-tailed Hawk is one of North America's most plumage-variable species. Across its range from Alaska to Mexico, the species occurs in numerous recognized subspecies and color morphs, from light (pale cream below) to dark (almost entirely dark brown below) to intermediate (the "classic" pattern with a belly band of dark streaks across a white belly). In the Pacific Northwest, the western subspecies (*B. j. calurus*) is common and includes both light and dark color morphs. This variability makes Red-tail identification a productive challenge.
- **Distribution.** The Red-tailed Hawk is the most widely distributed and most numerically abundant diurnal raptor in North America. It breeds from Alaska to Newfoundland and south through Central America and the Caribbean. In the Pacific Northwest, it is present year-round throughout the lowlands, valleys, and open habitats.

## THE CALL -- keeeeeee-arrr

### The Scream That Defined the Raptor

- **The scream.** The Red-tailed Hawk's territorial and advertising call is a prolonged, ragged, descending scream: "keeeeeee-arrr" (sometimes rendered "tseeeeeer" or simply "the scream"). The call begins at a high pitch and descends irregularly, with a ragged, rough quality -- not a clean whistle like the Osprey's call, but a wild, broken shriek. It is approximately 2-3 seconds in duration.
- **Frequency.** The call operates in the 1,000-4,000 Hz range -- broad-spectrum, which gives it carrying power across open landscapes. The call's ragged quality (irregular harmonic structure) means it has energy across a wide frequency range, making it audible in diverse acoustic environments.
- **Contexts.** The Red-tailed Hawk uses the scream for: territorial advertisement (broadcasting from a perch or in soaring flight), intruder alarm (when another raptor enters the territory), and mate communication. It is the bird's primary long-distance communication tool -- projecting across the open country habitats the bird occupies.
- **The carrying power.** The scream is designed to carry across open agricultural land, meadows, and highway corridors -- the Red-tailed Hawk's primary habitats. Its broad frequency range and ragged texture carry well even in wind, which is a persistent acoustic challenge in open country.
- **E=6.** The scream rates E=6 -- present, assertive, carrying weight. Not the frantic rapid-fire of the Goshawk's ka-ka-ka-ka (E=6 at 225) but a sustained, carrying assertion of presence. The Red-tail calls to be heard across distance; the Goshawk calls to confront an immediate intruder. Two different applications of E=6 energy.

## ECOLOGY -- THE OPEN COUNTRY HAWK

### The Buteo of Highways and Fields

- **Habitat.** The Red-tailed Hawk is the definitive open-country raptor in North America. Primary habitats include: agricultural fields (watching for voles and mice from perches), highway corridors (hunting the grassy medians and roadside verges), meadows and grasslands, suburban parks and golf courses, and forest edges where open and forested habitats meet. The species is virtually absent from dense forest interior (the Goshawk's domain) but thrives everywhere else.
- **The highway perch.** The most familiar Red-tailed Hawk image for most Americans is the bird sitting on a highway light pole or utility pole, watching the road median. This perch-and-watch hunting strategy is perfectly suited to highway corridors, where grassy medians maintain short-grass habitat that supports voles, mice, and small mammals. The Red-tail has become an inadvertent beneficiary of the interstate highway system -- a human infrastructure project that created millions of acres of optimal hawk hunting habitat along its margins.
- **Prey.** The Red-tailed Hawk is a generalist predator. Primary prey: small mammals (voles, mice, ground squirrels, rats, rabbits). Secondary prey: birds (small to medium birds, taken opportunistically), lizards, snakes (the Red-tail is one of North America's most important snake-hunting raptors), and large insects. The prey range is wider than the accipiters' more specialized bird-hunting.
- **The hunting stoop.** The Red-tail hunts by soaring at altitude (sometimes soaring in thermals for extended periods), spotting prey below with acute vision, and then stooping in a rapid dive. The stoop is less aerodynamically spectacular than the Peregrine Falcon's stoop (which can exceed 200 mph), but is effective -- the Red-tail drops from soaring altitude and covers the final distance quickly enough that small mammals in short grass have minimal warning.
- **Pair bonding.** Red-tailed Hawks form long-term pair bonds and return to the same territory year after year. Pairs typically maintain 1-4 nest sites within their territory, alternating between them in successive years. The nest is a large stick platform, added to each year, in a large tree or on a cliff ledge. Pairs begin courtship displays in late winter, with elaborate aerial display flights that include the advertising scream.

(Sources: Red-tail open country habitat; highway pole perch; interstate highway median hunting; prey generalist; voles; snakes; soaring stoop; pair bonding; multiple nest sites; courtship display)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST ECOLOGY

### The Ubiquitous Buteo

- **PNW habitat.** The Red-tailed Hawk is the most commonly encountered raptor throughout the western Washington lowlands, the Willamette Valley (Oregon), and the eastern Washington agricultural areas. It breeds throughout these regions and is present year-round.
- **Urban adaptation.** Like the Cooper's Hawk (degree 226), the Red-tailed Hawk has adapted successfully to urban and suburban environments. It breeds in urban parks (Volunteer Park in Seattle, for instance, has hosted Red-tailed Hawk nests), hunts in suburban open spaces, and is a regular presence along Seattle's arterial roads where highway-style medians and roadside verges provide hunting habitat.
- **The Skagit and other valley habitats.** The major river valleys of western Washington -- the Skagit, Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Snoqualmie, and Green valleys -- provide prime Red-tailed Hawk habitat in their agricultural portions. Open agricultural fields with vole populations support high densities of Red-tails throughout these valleys in winter, when the addition of migratory individuals from further north augments the resident breeding population.
- **Eastern Washington.** East of the Cascades, the Red-tailed Hawk is even more abundant -- the open agricultural and shrub-steppe habitats of the Columbia Basin are prime buteo country. The combination of open country, abundant small mammal prey, and available perch sites (fence posts, utility poles, isolated trees) makes eastern Washington one of the highest-density Red-tailed Hawk regions in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Winter concentrations.** During winter, Red-tailed Hawk populations in the PNW are augmented by northern-breeding birds moving south. This produces higher winter densities than the breeding season population would suggest. Western Washington's mild winters and agricultural fields provide reliable hunting opportunities for wintering hawks.

(Sources: Red-tail PNW lowland distribution; Skagit Valley; Columbia Basin eastern Washington; Volunteer Park breeding; urban adaptation; winter augmentation)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### The Common Hawk

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern. The Red-tailed Hawk is one of the most abundant raptors in North America, with a population estimated at approximately 2 million individuals (US Fish and Wildlife Service). It has benefited from agricultural development, suburban expansion, and highway construction -- all of which created open-country habitats the species exploits.
- **DDT and recovery.** Like other Accipitridae, the Red-tailed Hawk was impacted by DDT, though less severely than fish-eating raptors (its primary prey consists of terrestrial mammals rather than fish, which bioaccumulate DDT more intensely). Recovery post-1972 was rapid and complete.
- **Protected status.** The Red-tailed Hawk is protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act. It may not be possessed, harassed, or killed. This protection extends to feathers, nests, and eggs. The protection is important because Red-tails were historically shot by farmers who viewed them (incorrectly) as a threat to poultry. The "chicken hawk" label -- applied to buteos generally -- led to widespread persecution in the 19th and early 20th centuries. The term is misleading: Red-tailed Hawks rarely take domestic poultry; they primarily eat rodents.
- **Rodent control services.** Red-tailed Hawks are significant natural controllers of rodent populations in agricultural areas. A mated pair on a farm territory may take hundreds of voles and mice per year, providing measurable economic benefit to agriculture. The "pest hawk" label is thus precisely inverted from the reality.
- **Electrocution risk.** One contemporary mortality concern for Red-tailed Hawks is electrocution on utility poles. Hawks perching on power poles occasionally contact live electrical components, particularly where the insulation is inadequate or absent. Utility companies in many regions have retrofitted poles with raptor-safe designs to reduce electrocution mortality.

(Sources: IUCN Least Concern; 2M population estimate; DDT recovery; Migratory Bird Treaty Act; chicken hawk label; rodent control economic benefit; utility pole electrocution risk)
