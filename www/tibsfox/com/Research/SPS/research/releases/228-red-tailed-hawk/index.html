Red-tailed Hawk

E = 6 Buteo jamaicensis Accipitridae
RAPTOR ARC CLOSES — FIRST BUTEO — CINEMA'S HAWK. Degree 228 closes the eight-degree raptor arc (221-228) and introduces the first Buteo genus raptor in the engine. The Red-tailed Hawk's scream is the most widely distributed wildlife sound in cinema: used for Bald Eagles, Golden Eagles, Ospreys, Peregrine Falcons, and every fictional raptor in film and television. The arc opens with the Bald Eagle's real voice (degree 221, thin and surprising). The arc closes with the borrowed voice that replaced it.

The Borrowed Scream

The Red-tailed Hawk's territorial call — a ragged, raspy, descending keeeeeee-arrr — is one of the most recognizable wildlife sounds in the world, not because people have heard it from the bird itself, but because Hollywood has used it for every raptor species in film and television since approximately the mid-20th century. The Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222) calls with the Red-tail's voice in cinema. The Golden Eagle (degree 223) calls with the Red-tail's voice. The Great Eagles of Middle-earth call with the Red-tail's voice.

The arc that opened at degree 221 with the revelation of the Bald Eagle's actual thin, descending kee-kee-kee closes at degree 228 with the source of the sound that replaced it. Seven degrees. One correction. The most widespread wildlife sound misattribution in the history of popular culture, documented at both endpoints.

"The conditioning is circular: audiences hear the Red-tail's call as 'eagle' because films taught them to, and films use the Red-tail's call because it sounds like the culturally established 'eagle sound.'"

The Buteo Transition

Degrees 225-227 documented the three accipiters: short-winged, long-tailed, forest-interior pursuit hunters. Degree 228 introduces the first Buteo — broad-winged, relatively short-tailed, open-country soaring hunters. The ecological transition from accipiter to buteo is the raptor arc's sharpest shift: from the forest interior (Goshawk's closed-canopy old-growth) to the highway median and agricultural field (Red-tail's open-country domain). The genus Buteo contains approximately 28 species in North America, Europe, Africa, and Asia — the soaring hawks of open landscapes worldwide.

Ecology

The Highway Pole Hawk

Urban Raptor

Most familiar Red-tail image: perched on a utility pole watching the highway median. The interstate highway system created millions of acres of optimal hawk hunting habitat along its grassy margins.

Prey Generalist

Buteonidae

Voles, mice, ground squirrels, rabbits. Snakes (one of North America's most important snake-hunting raptors). Birds opportunistically. Wider prey range than the accipiters.

PNW Distribution

Resident

Most commonly encountered raptor in western Washington lowlands. Breeds in urban parks (Volunteer Park). Abundant in eastern Washington agricultural areas and Columbia Basin shrub-steppe.

Plumage Variability

ID

Light, dark, and intermediate color morphs. B. j. calurus (western subspecies) includes all morphs. The red tail (adults only) is the diagnostic field mark. One of North America's most variable species.

The Raptor Arc (221-228) Complete

Eight degrees. Eight species (plus one two-degree Bald Eagle). Three families (Accipitridae, Pandionidae, and now Buteo-genus Accipitridae). Every major ecological niche of PNW raptors: water/forest eagle (221-222), mountain eagle (223), fish-plunge-diver (224), apex forest hawk (225), suburban adapter (226), forest shadow (227), open-country soarer (228). The raptor era that began with America's national bird closes with cinema's most borrowed voice. The air is cleared.
