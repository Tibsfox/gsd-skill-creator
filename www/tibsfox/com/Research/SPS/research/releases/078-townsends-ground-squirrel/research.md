## THE TOWNSEND'S GROUND SQUIRREL: THE GRASSLAND COLONIST

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel is a small, colonial ground squirrel of the **open grasslands** of the Pacific Northwest interior -- sagebrush steppe, bunchgrass prairie, and agricultural fields of eastern Washington, Oregon, and Idaho. At 120-300 grams (4-10 oz), it is dramatically smaller than the three marmots that precede it (75-76-77), but it shares their family (Sciuridae) and their colonial social structure [1, 2, 3].

This is the engine's **FOURTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78) -- the **Sciuridae quartet**, which **matches the Mustelidae quartet** (63-66) as the longest family run in the engine. From marmots (75-77) to ground squirrel (78) -- same family, different genus (*Marmota* to *Urocitellus*), dramatically different habitat (alpine to grassland).

This is also the engine's **first true grassland species** -- a landmark habitat shift. After four consecutive alpine degrees (74-77), degree 78 descends to open grassland. The alpine run ends. A new habitat opens.

## TAXONOMY: *Urocitellus townsendii*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Urocitellus** -- from Greek *oura* ("tail") + Latin *citellus* ("ground squirrel," diminutive of *citus*, "swift"). The genus was split from the broader *Spermophilus* in 2009 based on molecular phylogenetics. *Urocitellus* = "tailed ground squirrel" [1, 2].

- **townsendii** -- honors **John Kirk Townsend** (1809-1851), a Philadelphia naturalist and ornithologist who explored the Columbia River region in 1834 with botanist Thomas Nuttall on the Nathaniel Wyeth expedition. Townsend collected bird and mammal specimens that became foundational to PNW natural history. The ground squirrel was described by Bachman in 1839 from Townsend's Columbia River specimens [1, 2, 4].

- **Common names:** Townsend's Ground Squirrel (standard). Sometimes locally called "picket pin" for its habit of standing erect like a picket pin [1, 2, 3].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (seventeenth mammalian entry; fifteenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- FIFTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- FOURTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75-76-77-78). The Sciuridae quartet. Ties Mustelidae (63-66) for longest family run.
- **Genus:** *Urocitellus* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Ground squirrels (not marmots). The shift from *Marmota* (75-77) to *Urocitellus* (78) within the Sciuridae quartet.
- **Species:** *U. townsendii* -- the Townsend's Ground Squirrel. Distributed across the Columbia Basin and Snake River Plain of eastern Washington, Oregon, Idaho, Nevada, and Utah [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE SMALL COLONIST

### Body Form

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel is small relative to the marmots [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 170-270 mm (7-11 inches) including tail. Body alone: 130-180 mm.
- **Weight:** 120-300 g (4-10 oz). Pre-estivation peak. Dramatically smaller than any marmot -- the Olympic Marmot (77) at 3.5-6 kg is 10-50 times heavier.
- **Tail:** 35-70 mm. Short relative to body -- a ground squirrel characteristic.
- **Body shape:** Slender, elongated. Less stocky than marmots. Built for speed and burrow access rather than boulder basking.

### Pelage

- **Body:** Gray-brown to sandy-brown above. The overall color matches the sagebrush steppe -- cryptic coloration for grassland predator avoidance.
- **Belly:** Lighter, buff to white.
- **Face:** Lighter gray. No dramatic markings. Subtle, understated.
- **No dark "boots"** (contrast with Hoary Marmot), **no yellow belly** (contrast with Yellow-bellied Marmot). Plain and cryptic.

## THE SOUND: CHIRPING TRILL ALARM -- 79th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Trill That Triggers the Colony

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel's alarm call is a **chirping trill** -- a rapid series of short, sharp chirps delivered in quick succession [1, 2, 3]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 4,000-8,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 0.5-2.0 seconds for a trill burst. Individual chirps within the trill: 0.05-0.1 seconds each.
- **Structure:** Rapid repetitive chirps -- a stuttering, machine-gun quality. Unlike the marmots' sustained or musical calls, the ground squirrel's alarm is a rapid-fire burst.
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- moderate to loud. Audible at 100-200 meters across open grassland. The flat, treeless habitat allows sound to carry efficiently.
- **Function:** Colony alarm. One squirrel's trill triggers colony-wide alert response -- all visible squirrels dive for burrows. The system functions as a distributed early warning network.

### Why E=4

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel's trill warrants E=4 [1, 2, 3]:

1. **Volume:** Moderate. Audible at 100-200 meters across flat grassland.
2. **Rapidity:** The chirping trill is delivered fast, creating acoustic urgency.
3. **Colonial alarm function:** Triggers colony-wide response. The aggregate alarm of a disturbed colony (many squirrels trilling simultaneously) can be quite conspicuous.
4. **Open habitat:** Flat grassland allows sound to carry with minimal attenuation.

## ECOLOGY: THE GRASSLAND COLONIST

### Habitat: Open Grassland -- First in the Engine

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel inhabits **open grassland** -- sagebrush steppe, bunchgrass prairie, and agricultural fields [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Elevation:** 200-1,500 meters. Low elevation. The OPPOSITE of the alpine species that precede it (74-77).
- **Vegetation:** Sagebrush (*Artemisia*), bunchgrass, agricultural crops. Open, treeless, flat to gently rolling.
- **Range:** Columbia Basin and Snake River Plain -- eastern Washington, eastern Oregon, southern Idaho, northern Nevada, northern Utah.
- **Significance:** The engine's **first true grassland species**. After four alpine degrees (74-77), degree 78 descends to the dry interior grasslands. This is a habitat the engine has not previously entered. The sagebrush steppe is one of the most threatened ecosystems in North America.

### Dormancy: Estivation + Hibernation

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel has an unusual dormancy pattern [1, 2, 3]:

- **Active period:** Only 4-5 months per year (approximately February-July in many populations).
- **Estivation:** Enters torpor in **late summer** when grassland vegetation dries and temperatures rise. This summer dormancy (estivation) is unusual among PNW mammals.
- **Hibernation:** Continues dormancy through autumn and winter.
- **Combined dormancy:** 7-8 months total. Active only during the brief window when grassland vegetation is green and temperatures are moderate.
- **Contrast with marmots:** The marmots hibernate in winter but are active in summer. The Townsend's Ground Squirrel estivates in summer AND hibernates in winter -- a double-dormancy strategy unique in the engine.

### Social Structure: Dense Colonies

Townsend's Ground Squirrels are colonial [1, 2, 3]:

- **Colony density:** Can be very high in suitable habitat -- hundreds of individuals per hectare.
- **Burrow systems:** Complex underground networks. Multiple entrances. The burrow system provides shelter from predators and temperature extremes.
- **Colonial alarm:** The chirping trill alarm system functions as a distributed sensor network. When one squirrel detects a predator, the trill triggers colony-wide diving response.

### Predators: Keystone Prey

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel is a **keystone prey species** [1, 2, 3]:

- **Red Fox** (degree 71) -- hunts ground squirrels in grassland.
- **Short-tailed Weasel** (degree 67) -- enters burrow systems.
- **Long-tailed Weasel** (degree 68) -- burrow predator.
- **Raptors** -- hawks, eagles, owls. Grassland-hunting raptors depend heavily on ground squirrels.
- **Badgers** -- dig into burrow systems.
- **Coyotes** -- opportunistic grassland predators.

As a keystone prey species, the Townsend's Ground Squirrel supports entire predator communities. Its colonial density makes it a reliable food source for multiple predator species.

## JOHN KIRK TOWNSEND: THE NATURALIST WHO NAMED THE GRASSLAND

**John Kirk Townsend** (1809-1851) was a Philadelphia naturalist and ornithologist who explored the Columbia River region in 1834 as part of the Nathaniel Wyeth expedition. He traveled with botanist Thomas Nuttall. Townsend collected specimens of birds and mammals that became foundational to the scientific documentation of PNW fauna [1, 2, 4].

The ground squirrel was described from Townsend's Columbia River specimens by John James Audubon's collaborator John Bachman in 1839. Townsend's name is also attached to Townsend's chipmunk, Townsend's vole, Townsend's warbler, and Townsend's solitaire -- a testament to the breadth of his collecting during the expedition.

The Townsend connection embeds this species in the history of PNW natural history exploration. The 1834 Columbia River expedition was among the earliest scientific surveys of the region.
