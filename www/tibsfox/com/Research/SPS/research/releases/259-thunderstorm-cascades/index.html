ROLLING CRACK + RUMBLE — SUMMER LIGHTNING IN THE CASCADES — E=8

A lightning bolt heats the air along its 1-8 mile channel to 30,000 Kelvin in a fraction of a millisecond. The explosive thermal expansion produces a supersonic shock wave that decays into sound. The crack is the direct arrival from the nearest point of the channel; the rumble is the extended arrival from the full channel length and the reflections off Cascade valley walls and ridge faces. A single bolt can produce 30-60 seconds of rolling sound in the mountain terrain. July and August. High ridge. The final E=8 before the absolute maximum.

Anatomy of Mountain Thunder

The crackExplosive decompression of air heated to 30,000 K. Direct arrival from the nearest point of the lightning channel. Sharp, instantaneous, spatially specific.
Channel extensionA bolt 1-8 miles long. Sound from the far end of an 8-mile channel arrives 40 seconds after the near end. The rumble is the extended acoustic arrival of the full channel's sound.
Valley reflectionCascade valley walls act as acoustic reflectors. Each wall returns a separate echo at a different delay. A deep valley converts one bolt into 30-60 seconds of multi-directional rolling sound.
Approaching stormDoppler accumulation: each successive rumble louder than the last. The acoustic signature of a storm closing the distance. Then ozone, then charge, then E=9.
"The rolling thunder of the Cascades is not one sound repeating — it is one sound arriving from many directions and distances as reflections propagate through a landscape of valleys, ridges, and rock faces. Mountain terrain converts a single lightning bolt into a minute of sound."

Cascade Thunderstorm Climate

Summer Convection

Orographic lift + afternoon heating + continental air = cumulonimbus development. July-August maximum. High-ridge crest receives the most thunderstorm activity.

Mount Rainier 14,411 ft

Highest peak in Washington. Summit extends above most cumulonimbus tops. Lightning attractor. Thunder at Rainier has the most extreme reflection geometry: the volcano's cone returns reflected sound from multiple angles simultaneously.

Alpine Lakes Wilderness

393,000 acres, most-visited wilderness in Washington, between Snoqualmie and Stevens passes. Thousands of lakes above treeline. Most beautiful, most dangerous location during summer thunderstorm season.

Fire Weather Connection

Summer Cascade thunderstorms are the primary natural wildfire ignition source. Dry lightning (insufficient rain to wet fuel) + high temperature + low humidity = the wildfire recipe. Thunder (259) connects directly to Lightning Strike Tree (260).

Lightning Safety on the High Ridge

The E=8 Cascade thunderstorm has a human safety dimension. Above treeline, a person is the tallest conductor in the landscape. The 30-30 rule: if thunder arrives within 30 seconds of lightning (storm is within 6 miles), move to shelter. Wait 30 minutes after the last thunder. The hair-standing sensation and ozone smell are immediate precursor signs of a very close strike. Lightning kills 20-50 people per year in the Pacific Northwest states, primarily on high ridges. The rolling crack and rumble is also a warning system.

The Transition to E=9

Thunderstorm Cascades (E=8) is the storm as environment: the rolling, multi-directional, sustained acoustic presence of a thunderstorm over the high Cascades. Degree 260, Lightning Strike Tree (E=9), is the storm as event: the specific, instantaneous, irreversible moment a lightning bolt enters a tree. The transition from 259 to 260 is the transition from weather to action. The storm surrounds you at E=8; at E=9, the storm has chosen a specific tree in a specific forest and the sound is of wood splitting.
