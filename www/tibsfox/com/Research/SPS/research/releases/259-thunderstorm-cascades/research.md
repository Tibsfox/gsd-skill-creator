## THUNDER -- THE ACOUSTIC PHENOMENON

### What Produces the Rolling Crack and Rumble

Thunder is produced by the rapid heating and expansion of air along the lightning channel:

- **The lightning channel and thermal expansion.** A lightning bolt heats the air along its path to approximately 30,000 Kelvin (about 5 times the surface temperature of the sun) in a fraction of a millisecond. This superheating causes the air to expand explosively, faster than the speed of sound -- creating a supersonic shock wave. The shock wave immediately decays into a sound wave as it moves outward from the lightning channel.
- **The crack.** The initial sharp crack heard directly overhead (when lightning is close) is the direct acoustic wave from the point of the lightning bolt closest to the observer -- the explosive decompression of the heated air column. The crack is sharp and instantaneous because it comes from a single geometrically close source.
- **The rumble.** The rumble that follows the crack is produced by two mechanisms:
  - **Extended channel length.** A lightning bolt is not a point source but a channel that can be 1-8 miles long. The different parts of the channel are at different distances from the observer. The sound from the near end arrives first (the crack); the sound from the far end arrives later. The acoustic wave from an 8-mile lightning channel arrives over a period of 40 seconds (at the speed of sound, approximately 1 second per 1,100 feet, or 5 miles per second). The rumble is the extended arrival of sound from the full length of the channel.
  - **Reflection and refraction.** In mountain terrain, thunder sounds are reflected off valley walls, ridge faces, and other topographic features. Each reflection creates a separate arrival of the sound at the observer's position. In a complex mountain landscape like the Cascade ridges, a single lightning bolt can produce a dozen reflected arrivals, each arriving at a different time, creating a multi-second, multi-directional rumble. This is the distinctly mountain-thunder quality: the sound does not decay as it does in flat terrain but continues to arrive from new directions as reflections propagate through the landscape.
- **The Doppler effect on moving storms.** When a thunderstorm is approaching, each successive thunder rumble is louder than the preceding one (energy arriving from decreasing distance). When a storm is departing, each rumble is slightly quieter. The approaching storm's acoustic signature is an escalating series of rumbles; the departing storm fades progressively.

## CASCADE RANGE THUNDERSTORM CLIMATOLOGY

### When and Where Summer Thunderstorms Occur

Summer thunderstorms in the Cascade Range have a specific climatological pattern:

- **Summer convection.** The Cascade Range generates thunderstorms primarily through orographic convection: when moist air is forced upward over the mountain barrier, it cools adiabatically, and if the air is unstable enough, convective cells develop and grow into cumulonimbus clouds. Summer is the prime season because: the Pacific storm track has moved north, leaving the Cascades with more continental (drier, more unstable) air; the sun heats the mountain slopes intensely; and the airmass has enough moisture to support deep convection.
- **The July-August maximum.** The thunderstorm season in the Cascades peaks in July and August. This is the period when the combination of afternoon heating, residual atmospheric moisture, and synoptic instability produces the most frequent and most intense convective storms. The mountains receive more thunderstorm activity than the lowlands: the topographic lift that creates the storms is concentrated along the ridge crests.
- **Fire weather and thunderstorms.** Summer thunderstorms in the Cascades are the primary natural ignition source for wildfires. "Lightning fires" are those ignited by dry lightning (lightning with insufficient rainfall to wet the fuel). The combination of high temperatures, low relative humidity, and lightning strike is the wildfire-ignition recipe. The sound of thunderstorm in the Cascades (degree 259) is directly connected to the sound of a lightning-ignited fire (degree 260: Lightning Strike Tree) -- the sequence is natural.
- **Geographic distribution.** Cascade thunderstorms occur across the full length of the range, from the North Cascades (Mount Baker area) to the South Cascades (Mount Adams, near the Oregon border). The highest-frequency thunderstorm activity tends to be in the Central Cascades (around Mount Rainier and the Alpine Lakes Wilderness) and the Eastern Cascades (where the drier continental airmass supports more convective instability).

## THE CASCADE RANGE -- GEOGRAPHY OF THUNDER

### Mountains That Shape the Sound

The Cascade Range's topography creates the specific acoustic character of Cascade thunder:

- **The range extent.** The Cascade Range in Washington State runs approximately 250 miles north-south, from the Canadian border to the Columbia River. The range is 30-80 miles wide, with the highest elevations along the main crest (typically 6,000-9,000+ feet) and lower subsidiary ridges to the west and east. The main crest is the focus of the highest thunderstorm activity.
- **The volcanoes.** The Cascade Range includes three major stratovolcanoes in Washington: Mount Baker (10,781 feet), Glacier Peak (10,541 feet), and Mount Rainier (14,411 feet -- the highest peak in Washington). Mount Rainier's summit extends above most thunderstorm cumulonimbus tops in typical summer events, making it a lightning attractor. Thunder at Rainier has the most extreme reflection geometry in the state: the mountain's massive cone produces reflected thunder from multiple angles simultaneously.
- **Valley acoustics.** The Cascade valleys (Skagit, Snoqualmie, Yakima, Wenatchee) act as acoustic resonators: thunder entering a valley mouth reflects off both valley walls and echoes down the valley. The duration of thunder in a deep Cascade valley can be 30-60 seconds for a single bolt -- the reflections propagate back and forth until the energy dissipates. This valley echo is the most dramatically extended thunder environment in the region.
- **Alpine Lakes Wilderness.** The Alpine Lakes Wilderness (393,000 acres between Snoqualmie Pass and Stevens Pass) is the most-visited wilderness in Washington and the location where most PNW residents encounter Cascade thunderstorms. The thousands of lakes in the wilderness (many above treeline) provide open exposure to electrical storms, making the Alpine Lakes both the most beautiful and the most dangerous location in the Cascades during summer thunderstorm season.

## LIGHTNING SAFETY AND THE HIGH RIDGE

### The E=8 Environment as Survival Context

The sound of Cascade thunder is inseparable from the physical danger of Cascade thunderstorms:

- **The 30-30 rule.** Standard lightning safety guidelines: if the time between lightning and thunder is 30 seconds or less (meaning the lightning is 6 miles or closer), move to shelter. Wait 30 minutes after the last thunder before leaving shelter. The 30-30 rule exists because lightning can travel from cloud to ground over distances of 10+ miles -- a lightning strike 6 miles away is already within lethal striking distance.
- **High ridge as maximum risk zone.** Above treeline, on an exposed ridge, a person is the tallest conductor in the area. This is the maximum-risk position for lightning. The E=8 thunder that sounds dramatic from the valley floor is a life-threatening event when experienced from a Cascade high ridge. Wilderness travelers, climbers, and backpackers in the Cascades take the E=8 environment seriously.
- **The auditory warning.** The rolling crack and rumble of approaching Cascade thunder is a warning system: the storm is advancing; each successive rumble louder than the last; the crackle and smell of ozone when lightning is very close; the hair-raising sensation of charge buildup on exposed skin before a strike. The E=8 environment has an active human safety dimension.
- **The statistics.** Lightning kills approximately 20-50 people per year in the Pacific Northwest states (Washington, Oregon, Idaho). Most deaths occur on high ridges, golf courses, and open water -- places where the victim is the tallest conductor. The Cascades are the primary high-ridge lightning risk zone in the region.
