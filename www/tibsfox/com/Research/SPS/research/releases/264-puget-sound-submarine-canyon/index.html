The Body of Water

Puget Sound was carved by the Puget Lobe of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet during the last ice age (~18,000-15,000 years ago). Glacial overdeepening produced an extraordinarily deep body of water: the main basin reaches 930 feet (283 meters), with an average depth of approximately 450 feet. The Sound extends approximately 100 miles from Olympia south to the Strait of Juan de Fuca north, constrained at its northern entrance by the Admiralty Inlet sill (approximately 65 feet deep) that restricts water exchange with the open ocean.

The Infrasonic Flow Sound — E=2

Water moving through the confined deep channels of Puget Sound produces infrasonic pressure variations through multiple mechanisms: turbulent flow through irregular channel geometry; internal waves at the density stratification boundary; tidal-frequency harmonics and their turbulent by-products. The channel dimensions (hundreds of feet deep, miles long) create specific resonant frequencies in the infrasonic range — like an organ pipe of geological scale, preferentially amplifying the sounds produced by the water that flows through it.

The Deep Puget Sound Ecosystem

Deep Water Species

Pacific spiny dogfish, sablefish (black cod), flatfishes; invertebrates including sea cucumbers, brittle stars, amphipods; microbial communities in bottom sediments. The deep water infrasonic environment is part of their acoustic reality.

Deep Water Oxygen

Puget Sound deep basins experience periodic hypoxia. The Admiralty Inlet sill restricts oxygenated deep water inflow. Nutrient loading from human activity exacerbates the oxygen deficit. Primary ecological management concern.

Sound at Depth

Low-frequency and infrasonic sound dominates the deep water acoustic environment. Organisms detect pressure through lateral line systems. Anthropogenic low-frequency noise (ships, construction) can interfere with the natural deep acoustic environment.

The Sill at Admiralty Inlet

~65 feet deep. Primary constraint on water exchange. Tidal currents exceed 4 knots at the sill during peak tidal exchange. Turbulence and infrasonic pressure variations propagate into the Sound's deep basins. The sill is an acoustic generator.
