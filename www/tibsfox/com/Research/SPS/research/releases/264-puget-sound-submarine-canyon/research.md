## PUGET SOUND -- THE BODY OF WATER

### Geography and Depth

Puget Sound is not a simple coastal bay -- it is a complex glacially carved fjord system:

- **Formation.** Puget Sound was carved by glacial ice during the last ice age (approximately 18,000-15,000 years ago). The Puget Lobe of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet advanced southward from British Columbia, scouring the bedrock and overdeepening the valleys it moved through. When the ice retreated, these overdeepened valleys filled with marine water, creating the network of channels, basins, and passages that constitute Puget Sound.
- **Area and length.** Puget Sound (broadly defined) covers approximately 1,000 square miles of water surface. The main basin is approximately 100 miles long from Olympia in the south to the Strait of Juan de Fuca at the north. The body of water is narrow -- 1-10 miles wide at most locations -- and has numerous subsidiary basins, passages, and inlets.
- **Depth.** The main basin of Puget Sound reaches depths of up to 930 feet (283 meters) at its deepest points, primarily in the area between the Kitsap Peninsula and Seattle. There are multiple deep basins in the Sound: the Main Basin (maximum depth ~930 feet), Hood Canal (maximum depth ~600 feet), and others. The average depth is approximately 450 feet (137 meters).
- **Glacial overdeeepening.** The extreme depths of Puget Sound relative to the surrounding land are the direct result of glacial overdeepening. The Puget Lobe concentrated its erosive energy in the valleys, cutting them much deeper than sea-level rivers can achieve. The result is a body of water that is extraordinarily deep for its proximity to the coast -- comparable in depth to some fjords in Scandinavia.
- **Sill at Admiralty Inlet.** The connection between Puget Sound and the Strait of Juan de Fuca (at Admiralty Inlet, near Port Townsend) includes a sill -- a relatively shallow underwater ridge that restricts the free flow of water between the Sound and the open ocean. This sill (approximately 65 feet deep) is the primary constraint on water exchange between Puget Sound and the Pacific. It shapes the deep water circulation patterns that produce the infrasonic flow sound.

## SUBMARINE TOPOGRAPHY

### The Deep Channels

The underwater topography of Puget Sound is complex and varied:

- **The Main Basin channels.** The main basin of Puget Sound has a complex underwater topography: multiple channels, ridges, and basins. The deepest areas are in the central and northern portions of the main basin, where the glacial erosion was most intense. These deep channels constrain the flow of bottom water and produce the specific infrasonic flow signatures that degree 264 documents.
- **The deep water column.** At 930 feet (283 meters), the bottom of Puget Sound's deepest point is in permanent darkness, at water pressure of approximately 27 atmospheres (387 psi). The temperature is cold and nearly constant -- approximately 7-9°C year-round at depth. This environment is far from the surface dynamics (waves, wind, surface mixing) that characterize most acoustic documentation of the Sound.
- **The submarine canyon analogy.** The deep channels of Puget Sound function acoustically like submarine canyons in the open ocean: confined, narrow passages through which water flows, creating infrasonic pressure variations as the flow moves through the irregular channel geometry. The Sound is not an open-ocean submarine canyon, but the acoustic physics are similar: constrained flow through irregular topography produces infrasonic signals.
- **Density stratification.** Puget Sound exhibits strong density stratification: the upper layers (0-100 feet) are relatively fresh (diluted by river inflow, particularly the Snohomish, Duwamish, Puyallup, and Nisqually rivers), while the deeper layers are more saline (replenished by deep Puget Sound tidal exchange with the salt water of the Strait of Juan de Fuca). This stratification means that sound propagates differently at different depths -- the deep saline layer has specific acoustic properties distinct from the brackish surface layer.
- **The sill as acoustic feature.** The Admiralty Inlet sill (approximately 65 feet deep) is the primary constriction in the Sound's water exchange system. During tidal exchanges, water moving through the sill accelerates significantly (tidal currents at the sill can exceed 4 knots), producing turbulence and infrasonic pressure variations that propagate into the Sound's deep basins. The sill is an acoustic generator as well as a hydraulic feature.

(Sources: Main Basin channel topography; deepest points in central and northern main basin; 930 feet = 283 meters = 27 atmospheres; deep water temperature 7-9°C; submarine canyon acoustic analogy; constrained flow through irregular topography; density stratification Puget Sound; freshwater surface (rivers) vs. saline depth; Admiralty Inlet sill; tidal currents exceeding 4 knots at sill; turbulence and infrasonic pressure variations)

## THE INFRASONIC FLOW SOUND

### The Acoustic Physics of Deep Water

The infrasonic flow sound of the Puget Sound deep channels:

- **What produces infrasound in deep water channels.** Infrasonic sound (below 20 Hz) in a deep water channel is produced primarily by: (1) turbulent flow of water through irregular channel geometry, which produces pressure variations at frequencies determined by the channel dimensions and flow velocity; (2) tidal forcing, which produces cyclic pressure variations at tidal frequencies (roughly 0.00002-0.0001 Hz for the dominant semidiurnal tides -- well below infrasonic range, but the harmonics and turbulent by-products are in the infrasonic range); (3) internal waves, which are wave motions at the density stratification boundary (the pycnocline) that produce pressure variations at frequencies from 0.001-0.01 Hz.
- **The channel resonance.** Deep channels of specific dimensions have resonant frequencies -- like organ pipes, they preferentially amplify infrasonic signals at frequencies related to their length and depth. The deep basins of Puget Sound (hundreds of feet deep, miles long) have resonant frequencies in the infrasonic range. The continuous flow of water through these channels produces a standing acoustic signal at these frequencies.
- **E=2 positioning.** E=2 is among the quietest entries in the entire SPS engine. The infrasonic flow sound at 930 feet is real but requires specialized acoustic instrumentation to detect. It is not audible to the human ear by any means. It is not even near-audible -- it is a continuous physical process that produces no sound in any conventional sense, but that produces real, measurable pressure variations in the infrasonic range. E=2 is the correct energy: present, continuous, real, but at the minimum end of the scale.
- **Continuous character.** Unlike the episodic or periodic entries in the Geophysics Arc (volcanic tremor is periodic; lahar is hypothetical one-time event; earthquake is episodic), the infrasonic flow sound of the deep Puget Sound channels is continuous -- it operates on tidal cycles and density currents that never stop. The Sound is always moving; the deep channels always produce their infrasonic signal.

## PUGET SOUND ECOLOGY AT DEPTH

### The Deep Water Ecosystem

The deep water of Puget Sound at 930 feet is not dead space -- it is a functioning ecosystem:

- **Deep water species.** The deep basins of Puget Sound support specific communities of organisms adapted to the cold, dark, high-pressure environment: various fish species including Pacific spiny dogfish, sablefish (black cod), and various flatfishes; invertebrates including sea cucumbers, brittle stars, and amphipods; and microbial communities in the bottom sediments. These organisms live in the acoustic environment of the infrasonic flow -- the sound is part of their environmental reality, even if they detect it through pressure-sensing lateral line systems rather than hearing.
- **The deep water oxygen issue.** Puget Sound's deep basins experience periodic hypoxia (low oxygen) because the sill at Admiralty Inlet restricts the inflow of oxygenated deep water from the Strait of Juan de Fuca. When bottom water oxygen is depleted and new oxygenated water does not arrive, hypoxic or anoxic conditions can develop. This is one of the primary ecological management concerns for Puget Sound -- exacerbated by nutrient loading from human activities (agriculture, wastewater) that increases the oxygen demand of the biological community.
- **Sound and the deep ecosystem.** The organisms of the deep Puget Sound basins have evolved in an environment where infrasonic and low-frequency sound is the dominant acoustic reality. Anthropogenic low-frequency noise (ship propellers, underwater construction, naval sonar) can interfere with the natural acoustic environment of the deep ecosystem in ways that are not fully characterized.

(Sources: Deep Puget Sound species: Pacific spiny dogfish, sablefish, flatfishes; invertebrates at depth; microbial communities; lateral line pressure detection in fish; hypoxia in deep basins; Admiralty Inlet sill as oxygen supply constraint; nutrient loading and hypoxia; anthropogenic low-frequency noise; deep ecosystem acoustic environment)
