The Annual Hydrological Maximum

The accumulated winter snowpack of the Pacific Northwest Cascades (10–30 feet at 4,000+ ft elevation) releases as liquid through the mountain river canyon system. Peak discharge 3–10× annual average. Bedload transport: boulders rolling at 10–80 Hz irregular drumming beneath the broadband turbulence. Standing wave oscillation: 0.5–3 Hz at canyon constrictions, physically felt. Canyon reverb 1–3 seconds: the river's sound and its reflections overlap. High-frequency spray and mist (2,000–8,000 Hz) from maximum kinetic energy. Total: 65–80 dB sustained, broadband, physically enveloping. E=7 through sustained complexity across all frequencies simultaneously.

The Acoustic Layers

Broadband Turbulence Noise Turbulent water at high velocity. Continuous full-spectrum noise. The acoustic foundation against which all other sounds emerge. Dominant during sustained peak discharge. Cannot be separated from other layers at maximum flow.
20–8k Hz
Bedload Transport Drumming Boulders and rocks rolling and impacting along streambed under high-velocity water. Most distinctive E=7 acoustic signature -- only present at maximum discharge. Deep, irregular, continuous during peak transport. Physically felt at canyon proximity.
10–80 Hz
Standing Wave Resonance Hydraulic oscillation at canyon constrictions. 0.5–3 Hz pulse rate. At snowmelt maximum, amplitude of oscillation is at annual peak. The river's "heartbeat" -- a pulsation that structures the continuous broadband noise into a rhythmic pattern.
0.5–3 Hz pulse
Spray and Mist High-frequency component from maximum kinetic energy at rapids and obstacles. More prominent at maximum discharge than low flow. Superimposed as a high-frequency hiss on the broadband foundation. The air above the river carries sound as well as water.
2k–8k Hz

The Pacific Northwest Rivers

Skagit River

Largest WA State river by volume. Upper canyon: Concrete to Marblemount. North Cascades snowmelt. Most powerful annual surge in the state.

White River

Drains Mt. Rainier southeast flank. Glacial and snowmelt source. William O. Douglas Wilderness canyon. Sustained spring surge.

Methow River

Eastern Cascades, Okanogan County. Continental snowpack. Earlier, more concentrated spring pulse. Drier east-side character.

Hoh River

Olympic Mountains, Olympic NP. Combined snowmelt and glacial source. Surge sustained into July in high snowpack years. Most prolonged spring flood in the state.

The Salmon Connection

The spring snowmelt flood surge is the annual salmon migration's transport corridor. Juvenile salmon (smolts) time their downstream migration to the high spring flows — using the flood pulse to carry them from headwater rearing habitat to the estuary and ocean. The sound at degree 339 is not just the sound of water: it is the sound of the Pacific Northwest salmon lifecycle's most critical annual transition, the outmigration pulse that determines cohort survival.

Climate Change and the Changing Sound

Pacific Northwest snowpack has been declining as warming winter temperatures convert snow to rain at mid-elevations. The snowmelt flood pulse is shifting earlier, compressing into a shorter window, and being followed by lower summer flows. The degree-339 sound — the sustained May-June maximum discharge through the mountain canyons — is increasingly a sound of the past state of the Pacific Northwest hydrological system. Each decade, the peak occurs slightly earlier and the magnitude at the traditional peak dates is slightly less.
