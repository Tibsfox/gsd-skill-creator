## THE SNOWMELT FLOOD PULSE -- HYDROLOGY

### The Annual Water Release

The hydrological mechanism:

- **Pacific Northwest snowpack accumulation.** The Pacific Northwest's mountains (Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains) accumulate snowpack from November through March in most years. The snowpack serves as a water storage system: the winter precipitation that falls as snow at elevation is held in solid form and released as liquid through the spring melt process. In high snowpack years, the Cascades can accumulate 10-30 feet of snow at elevations above 4,000 feet.
- **The melt trigger.** The snowmelt begins when incoming solar radiation and air temperatures exceed the energy required to melt the snowpack. In the Pacific Northwest, this typically begins at lower elevations (below 2,000 feet) in March and progresses upward through April-June. At high elevations (above 5,000 feet), the snowpack may persist into July in high-snowpack years.
- **The flood surge.** The timing of maximum river discharge depends on elevation: lowland rivers peak in March-April; mid-elevation rivers peak in April-May; high-elevation rivers peak in May-June. The peak discharge can be 3-10 times the annual average discharge for a mountain river -- the river at maximum snowmelt is 3-10 times its normal size, moving 3-10 times its normal volume of water per second.
- **The 100-year flood comparison.** The annual snowmelt maximum for a Pacific Northwest mountain river is not a 100-year flood event (which involves catastrophic infrastructure failure). It is the expected annual maximum -- the river at its normal spring maximum, which is still far above any other season's discharge. The river canyon fills to near its banks (or over them into the floodplain), the turbulence is at maximum, and the sound is at its annual peak.

## THE ACOUSTIC CHARACTER -- RIVER CANYON AT MAXIMUM DISCHARGE

### What Maximum Discharge Sounds Like

The specific acoustic output of the snowmelt flood surge:

- **The broadband noise foundation.** Turbulent water at high velocity produces broadband noise across the full audible spectrum. At snowmelt maximum, a Pacific Northwest mountain river in canyon terrain produces a continuous broadband sound in the 65-80 dB range at 30 meters from the channel. This continuous noise is the acoustic foundation -- the bed against which all more specific sounds emerge.
- **The deep hydraulic drumming.** At maximum discharge, Pacific Northwest mountain rivers move rocks and boulders along the streambed -- a process called bedload transport. As rocks roll, slide, and impact each other under the force of high-velocity water, they produce low-frequency impacts: 10-80 Hz, irregular in timing but continuous in character during peak transport events. This bedload transport drumming is a specific acoustic signature of maximum discharge that is absent at low or moderate flow. The deep irregular drumming beneath the broadband noise is the most distinctive acoustic feature of the snowmelt flood surge.
- **The standing wave resonance.** In canyon terrain, high-discharge rivers form standing waves at constrictions and below obstacles. These standing waves oscillate at 0.5-3 Hz (as analyzed in SPS-332 context) but at snowmelt maximum, the amplitude of the oscillation is at its annual peak. The standing wave produces a low-frequency pulsation that can be felt as well as heard at canyon proximity.
- **The spray and mist component.** High-velocity water at obstacles and rapids creates spray and mist that is audible as a high-frequency hiss superimposed on the broadband noise. This high-frequency component (2,000-8,000 Hz) is more prominent during the snowmelt surge than at lower flows because the kinetic energy of the water is greater, producing more vigorous spray and aeration.
- **The canyon amplification.** In canyon terrain, the river's acoustic output is reflected from canyon walls, creating a reverberant environment. The reverb time in a narrow rocky canyon can be 1-3 seconds, producing a layered acoustic environment where the river's direct sound and multiple reflections overlap. The canyon is a natural acoustic amplifier: the high-volume river in narrow canyon terrain is louder than the same river in open valley terrain.

(Sources: Turbulent water broadband noise 65-80 dB at 30m at maximum discharge; bedload transport rock and boulder rolling 10-80 Hz low-frequency irregular impacts; bedload transport acoustic signature specific to maximum discharge; standing wave oscillation 0.5-3 Hz at canyon constrictions; annual amplitude peak at snowmelt maximum; spray and mist high-frequency 2000-8000 Hz at maximum kinetic energy; canyon reverb 1-3 seconds; canyon amplification of river acoustic output)

## THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST MOUNTAIN RIVERS

### The Specific Channels

The rivers where the degree-339 sound is fully realized:

- **The Skagit River.** The Skagit is the largest river system in Washington State by volume, draining the North Cascades and discharging into Puget Sound at the Skagit Delta near Anacortes. The Skagit's annual snowmelt surge is among the most powerful in the state, particularly following high-snowpack years. The upper Skagit canyon (between the communities of Concrete and Marblemount) is the zone where the snowmelt flood surge is most dramatically expressed.
- **The White River.** The White River drains the southeast flank of Mount Rainier and is heavily influenced by Rainier's glaciers and snowpack. The White River canyon above the Enumclaw plateau, in what is now the William O. Douglas Wilderness, is one of the most acoustically dramatic snowmelt flood environments in the state.
- **The Methow River.** The Methow River, draining the eastern slope of the Cascades in Okanogan County, is fed by the dry-side snowpack -- typically lower in volume but high in the spring surge dynamics characteristic of continental snowmelt. The Methow Valley snowmelt pulse is earlier and more concentrated than the wetter west-side rivers.
- **The Hoh River.** The Hoh River, draining the Olympic Mountains and Olympic National Park, has both a large snowpack component and a glacial melt component. The Hoh's spring surge is sustained through June and into July in some years as the Olympic glaciers contribute to the flow after the snowpack has melted. The sound of the Hoh in May-June is one of the most sustained examples of the snowmelt flood surge in the Pacific Northwest.

## THE SPRING HYDROLOGY CONTEXT

### Pacific Northwest Water as Annual Event

The ecological and cultural significance of the snowmelt flood surge:

- **The snowmelt pulse as ecosystem driver.** The annual snowmelt flood surge is not merely an acoustic event -- it is the primary driver of the Pacific Northwest river ecosystem's annual cycle. The flood pulse: (1) delivers sediment and organic material to the river delta and coastal zone; (2) scours the streambed, removing accumulated fine sediment and maintaining the gravel beds where salmon spawn in fall; (3) recharges the riparian aquifer as the floodplain fills and water enters the alluvial system; (4) connects the river to its floodplain, maintaining the shallow-water wetlands where amphibians, waterfowl, and juvenile fish overwinter.
- **The salmon connection.** The spring snowmelt pulse is the foundation of the Pacific Northwest salmon lifecycle. The high spring flows carry juvenile salmon (smolts) from their freshwater rearing habitat in the headwaters to the estuary and ocean. The smolt migration is timed to the spring snowmelt pulse -- the fish use the high-flow period as their downstream transport corridor. The acoustic event at degree 339 is therefore not just the sound of water; it is the sound of the annual salmon migration beginning.
- **Climate change effects.** Pacific Northwest snowpack has been declining due to climate change, with warmer winter temperatures producing more rain and less snow at mid-elevations. The snowmelt flood pulse is predicted to: (1) occur earlier in the year (shifted from May toward April or March); (2) be compressed into a shorter time window; (3) be followed by lower summer flows as the snowpack storage is reduced. The sound at degree 339 is increasingly a sound of the past state of the Pacific Northwest hydrological system.
