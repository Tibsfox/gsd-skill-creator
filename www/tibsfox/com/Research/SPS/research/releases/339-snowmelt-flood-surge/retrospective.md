## What Worked

1. **The bedload transport drumming identification is the entry's most distinctive contribution.** Rock and boulder rolling at 10-80 Hz is present only at maximum discharge -- it is the acoustic fingerprint of peak snowmelt flow. This specific identification distinguishes the snowmelt flood surge from other high-water entries in the engine.
2. **The elevation-dependent peak timing framework is precise.** Lowland rivers peak March-April; mid-elevation April-May; high-elevation May-June. This specificity allows the entry to be located in real time and real space.
3. **The salmon smolt connection enriches the entry beyond acoustics.** The peak discharge that produces the E=7 acoustic event is the same peak discharge that carries juvenile salmon from headwater rearing habitat to the estuary. The sound and the lifecycle event are concurrent. This is one of the engine's most significant ecological cross-references.
4. **The canyon amplification analysis (reverb 1-3 seconds) clarifies why canyon terrain produces a different acoustic event than open valley terrain.** The same discharge in a canyon is louder by 10-15 dB than in an open valley because the canyon walls function as acoustic reflectors and amplifiers.

## Discoveries

1. **Climate change effects on the snowmelt flood pulse are well-documented and directional.** Declining snowpack, earlier melt timing, compressed pulse, lower summer flows. The degree-339 sound is increasingly a record of the past state of Pacific Northwest hydrology. This is a finding that applies across multiple SPS entries (river, waterfall, glacier-adjacent sounds) and should be tracked as a systemic observation.
2. **The Hoh River has the most sustained spring surge in the state** because it has both snowmelt and glacial melt contributions, with the glacial melt extending the high flow period into July in high snowpack years. The Hoh is the longest-duration example of the degree-339 sound.
