Common Nighthawk

Chordeiles minor (Caprimulgidae) — E=5 — Nasal “Peent!” + Booming Dive
SECOND CAPRIMULGIDAE. The most aerial nightjar. Booming dive: male plummets, air through primaries creates deep BOOM. “Peent!” nasal call at twilight. Dual-mode sound: vocal + mechanical. Urban rooftop nester. “Bull-bat.” Flock migration. Crepuscular.

The Booming Dive — Air Through Feathers

The male climbs to altitude, stalls, then plummets earthward in a steep dive. At the bottom, he spreads his wings and the air rushing through his primary feathers creates a deep, resonant BOOM at 100–200 Hz — so low it is felt in the chest. Carries 500+ meters. Dive speeds: 60–100 km/h. The most spectacular aerial display in the SPS engine.

The Aerial Lifestyle — Born to Fly

Hunts by continuous aerial hawking — mouth agape, scooping insects from the air. Eats, drinks, and displays on the wing. Buoyant, erratic flight. Most active at twilight (crepuscular). Migrates in visible flocks — hundreds to thousands of birds. The most aerial of the nightjars.

Urban Rooftop Nester

Adopted flat gravel rooftops as nesting habitat — gravel mimics natural gravel bars. Eggs laid directly on rooftop. Declining as gravel roofs replaced by rubber membrane. Parents shade chicks with wings in extreme heat. The “peent” over parking lots defines urban summer twilight.

“Peent” (vocal, syrinx) + boom (mechanical, feathers). Two distinct sound-production systems. Unique dual modality in engine.
