*Degree 146 retrospective complete. COMMON NIGHTHAWK at the one hundred forty-sixth degree -- THE BOOMING DIVER. At twilight, in the open sky over parking lots and rooftops and gravel bars, the male Nighthawk performs one of the most spectacular displays in North American ornithology. He climbs. He stalls. He dives -- 60, 80, 100 kilometers per hour, gravity-powered, wings half-folded. At the bottom of the plummet he spreads his wings and the air rushing through his specially shaped primary feathers creates a deep, resonant BOOM at 100-200 Hz -- a sound so low it is felt in the chest more than heard by the ear. And then he climbs again. "Peent... peent... peent..." -- the nasal call between dives, the rhythmic base against which the periodic booms land like drumbeats. Dual-mode sound production: voice and feathers, syrinx and aerodynamics, two systems in one bird. The "bull-bat" -- bull for the boom, bat for the flight. On flat gravel rooftops he nests, eggs on bare substrate, chicks shaded by parental wings against the heat of summer asphalt. And when autumn comes he joins his kind in flocks of hundreds, sometimes thousands, and they stream southward to the Amazon -- one of the great migratory spectacles. The nightjar contrast pair is complete: the Poorwill that sleeps through winter on the ground, the Nighthawk that booms through summer in the sky. E=5 + E=5 = the perfect energy match with THEESatisfaction, both performing spectacularly in open air, both named for what they are not, both making the twilight zone their home.*
