### Why E=5

The Greater Yellowlegs registers at E=5:

- **Volume:** The "klew-klew-klew" call is LOUD. It rings out across marshes and mudflats. Often the first bird heard when approaching any wetland. The call carries extraordinary distances for a bird of this size.
- **Alarm function:** The Greater Yellowlegs is the sentinel of the shorebird community. It detects threats before other species and gives alarm, triggering flight in all shorebirds on the flat. This alarm function elevates the energy -- one voice triggers many.
- **Carrying quality:** The call has a clear, ringing quality that cuts through wind, wave noise, and distance. Three descending notes: "KLEW-klew-klew." Unmistakable.
- **Frequency of calling:** Greater Yellowlegs calls FREQUENTLY. It is one of the most vocal shorebirds. Every approach, every disturbance, every perceived threat triggers the alarm.
- **Why not E=6?** The call is loud and carrying but is a simple three-note descending pattern. E=5 reflects a very loud, carrying, frequently given alarm call that functions as the voice of the entire tidal flat community.

## THE SENTINEL SPECIES

### First to Call, Last to Settle

The Greater Yellowlegs occupies a unique ecological role:

- **Alarm sentinel:** When a predator approaches a tidal flat, the Greater Yellowlegs is typically the first to detect it and give alarm. Its loud "klew-klew-klew" triggers flight in Dunlin flocks (169), causes godwits (172) to lift heads, and alerts all other shorebirds.
- **Paranoid by nature:** Greater Yellowlegs are notably wary. They flush at greater distances than most shorebirds. This wariness serves the community -- the sentinel must be the first to detect threat.
- **Long legs as platform:** The "yellowlegs" are not just diagnostic -- the long legs allow the bird to wade in deeper water than most shorebirds, providing a higher vantage point for scanning.
- **Named for the obvious:** "Greater Yellowlegs" is one of the most literal bird names -- the bird is large and has yellow legs. Straightforward. The name tells you exactly what to look for.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; shorebird ecology; engine internal analysis)

## MORPHOLOGY AND IDENTIFICATION

### The Tall Shorebird

- **Size:** 29-33 cm. Noticeably larger than Lesser Yellowlegs (not in engine). Long-legged, long-necked.
- **Bill:** Straight, ~5.5 cm. Slightly upturned appearance. Dark gray. Proportionally longer than Lesser Yellowlegs. Bill is approximately 1.5x head length (Lesser = ~1x).
- **Legs:** Bright yellow. Long. The diagnostic feature. Allow deep wading.
- **Plumage:** Gray-brown above with white spotting. White below with gray-brown barring and streaking. In breeding plumage, heavier barring on flanks.
- **Flight:** Shows a white rump and tail, dark wings. No wing stripe. Long yellow legs trail behind.
- **Comparison with Scolopacidae streak:**

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Greater Yellowlegs

**"Klew-klew-klew" Call:**
- **Trigger:** Alarm, flight, disturbance, contact. Given frequently and loudly.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** A loud, ringing, three-note descending call: "KLEW-klew-klew." The stress is on the first note. Clear, piercing, carrying. Unmistakable at any distance.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 2000-3500 Hz. Clear, ringing quality.
- **Duration:** Each three-note sequence ~0.5-0.8 seconds. Often repeated in series: "klew-klew-klew... klew-klew-klew... klew-klew-klew."
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Very loud. Carries far across open marshes and tidal flats. Functions as community alarm.

**Variant Calls:**
- Single-note "klew!" when mildly alarmed.
- Rapid "kew-kew-kew-kew" during intense alarm or flushing.
- Display call on breeding grounds: repeated series over boreal muskeg.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
