*Degree 149 retrospective complete. BAND-TAILED PIGEON at the one hundred forty-ninth degree -- THE NATIVE PIGEON. The Columbidae opens. In the forest canopy, high above the trail, a sound that most hikers mistake for an owl: a deep, resonant "coo-COO" that seems too low, too rich, too atmospheric to be a pigeon. But it is a pigeon -- the Band-tailed Pigeon, the native wild pigeon of western North America, the species that was here for millennia before the Rock Pigeon arrived on European ships. It is larger than a Rock Pigeon, more colorful (purple breast, white neck crescent, yellow bill, iridescent nape), and infinitely more ecologically important -- a seed disperser, an acorn specialist, a member of the forest regeneration cycle. Yet almost no one knows it exists. The ubiquitous Rock Pigeon has colonized every sidewalk and overpass, and when people think "pigeon" they think of that bird -- the feral import, not the native original. Meanwhile, the Band-tailed Pigeon gathers at traditional mineral springs in flocks of hundreds, drinking mineral water and consuming mineral-rich soil to supplement its fruit-and-nut diet, using the same sites its parents and grandparents used, generation after generation. It was once abundant -- flocks of thousands -- but market hunting at mineral springs and habitat loss have driven a decades-long decline. The Passenger Pigeon parallel is real: the same vulnerability (concentrated, flock-forming pigeon) that drove the Passenger Pigeon to extinction in 1914 has reduced the Band-tailed Pigeon to a fraction of its historical numbers. The engine opens the Columbidae with this bird because the story starts with the native, the original, the real thing. Four degrees, four Columbidae: native forest (149), native open (150), invasive (151), feral mechanical (152). The displacement story begins.*
