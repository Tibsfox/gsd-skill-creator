Band-tailed Pigeon

Patagioenas fasciata (Columbidae) — E=3 — Deep Owl-Like Coo-Coo
FIRST COLUMBIDAE. The PNW’s native wild pigeon. Larger and more colorful than the Rock Pigeon. Purple-tinged breast. White neck crescent. Congregates at mineral springs. Declining populations. Opens the four-degree Columbidae quartet (149–152).

The Native Pigeon

Not your city pigeon. The Band-tailed Pigeon is the native wild pigeon of western North America — the largest pigeon in North America (300–450g). Purple-tinged breast, blue-gray back, white crescent on the nape, yellow bill with dark tip. A bird of the deep forest canopy, not sidewalks. Feeds on fruits, acorns, and grain. Important seed disperser.

Mineral Spring Congregations

Flocks of 50–200+ pigeons gather at natural mineral springs to drink mineral-rich water and consume mineral soil. A form of geophagy — supplementing a low-sodium fruit/nut diet. The same sites used generation after generation. Community gathering behavior — otherwise-solitary birds becoming social at the spring.

Declining Populations

Historically abundant — flocks of thousands. Market hunting at mineral springs devastated numbers. Echoes the Passenger Pigeon extinction. Habitat loss compounds the decline. Currently declining 2–3% per year. Pacific coast population: 1.5–2 million (a fraction of historical). Species of conservation concern.
