## E=3 -- THE FOREST CANOPY MURMUR

### Why E=3

The Band-tailed Pigeon registers at E=3, notably lower than recent entries:

- **Low frequency:** The coo is surprisingly deep -- around 300-600 Hz fundamental frequency. This is lower than most bird vocalizations and gives the call an owl-like quality. Low-frequency sounds carry well through forest but feel muted and ambient rather than piercing.
- **Low amplitude:** The cooing is not loud. Audible at 100-200 meters in forest conditions. Not a broadcast call like a thrush or wren. The bird is large but its voice is restrained.
- **Canopy distance:** Band-tailed Pigeons call from high in the forest canopy -- 20-40 meters above ground. The height adds distance, and the forest canopy absorbs and scatters the sound. The listener hears a diffuse, atmospheric cooing rather than a sharp, localized call.
- **Infrequent calling:** Unlike songbirds that sing for extended periods, the Band-tailed Pigeon coos intermittently. A few coos, then silence. The sporadic nature reduces the overall energy footprint.

## THE NATIVE PIGEON

### Not Your City Pigeon

The Band-tailed Pigeon is the **native** pigeon of western North America -- a species most people don't know exists because the ubiquitous Rock Pigeon (Columba livia) has colonized every city, every park, every bridge:

- **Size:** Length: 33-40 cm. Weight: 300-450 g. Significantly larger than the Rock Pigeon (240-380 g average). The Band-tailed Pigeon is a substantial bird -- the largest pigeon in North America.
- **Plumage:** Far more colorful than the Rock Pigeon. Purple-tinged breast. Blue-gray back. Yellow bill with dark tip. **White crescent** on the back of the neck -- the diagnostic field mark. Gray tail band (hence "band-tailed"). Iridescent green-purple nape. A genuinely beautiful bird.
- **Habitat:** Forest canopy. Not cities, not parks, not sidewalks. The Band-tailed Pigeon is a bird of the deep forest -- old-growth conifer, mixed conifer-deciduous, montane forest. Its primary habitat is as far from a city pigeon coop as a forest can be.
- **Range:** Pacific coast from southern British Columbia to Baja California. Mountain populations from Colorado to Central America. The PNW is the core of the northern range.

### Ecological Role

The Band-tailed Pigeon plays important ecological roles that distinguish it from its feral cousin:

- **Seed dispersal:** As a fruit and acorn eater, the Band-tailed Pigeon is an important seed disperser in Pacific coast forests. Its large gape and digestive system handle fruits and acorns that smaller birds cannot, and it transports seeds over considerable distances.
- **Acorn specialist:** Band-tailed Pigeons are among the few PNW birds that specialize in acorns -- Garry oak, tanoak, and other oak species. This makes them important members of oak woodland ecology, which is itself a threatened habitat in the PNW.
- **Forest regeneration:** By dispersing seeds of native trees and shrubs, the Band-tailed Pigeon contributes to forest regeneration after disturbance -- fire, logging, windthrow.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Keppie & Braun 2000; Sibley 2014; WDFW)

## MINERAL SPRING CONGREGATIONS

### The Mineral Gathering

One of the most distinctive behaviors of the Band-tailed Pigeon is its **congregation at mineral springs**:

- **The behavior:** Band-tailed Pigeons gather at natural mineral springs, mineral licks, and areas of mineral-rich soil. They drink the mineral water and consume mineral-rich soil and mud. Flocks of dozens to hundreds of pigeons may gather at a single mineral spring site.
- **Why minerals?** The pigeons' diet of fruits, nuts, and grain is low in sodium and other minerals. The mineral springs provide essential sodium, calcium, and other mineral supplements that the diet lacks. This is a form of **geophagy** -- deliberate mineral consumption.
- **Traditional sites:** The same mineral spring sites are used year after year, generation after generation. The pigeons appear to learn the locations from their parents and from flock mates. Some mineral spring sites have been documented as pigeon gathering places for decades.
- **Vulnerability:** The mineral spring congregations make Band-tailed Pigeons vulnerable. When dozens of birds gather at a single predictable site, they become easy targets for hunters, predators, and disturbance. Historical market hunting at mineral springs contributed to population declines.
- **PNW sites:** Several mineral spring sites in Washington and Oregon are well-known Band-tailed Pigeon gathering points. The springs are typically in forested areas at low to mid elevation, often near streams or seeps.

### The Mineral Spring as Community Space

The mineral spring congregation is a **community behavior** -- one of the few contexts where the typically dispersed Band-tailed Pigeon becomes highly social:

- Flocks at mineral springs can number 50-200+ birds
- The birds perch in surrounding trees, then descend to the spring in groups
- Social interactions at the spring include pair bonding, dominance displays, and juvenile learning
- The spring functions as a gathering point for an otherwise scattered population

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Keppie & Braun 2000; March & Sadleir 1972; Sanders 2003)

## DECLINING POPULATIONS

### A Native Species in Trouble

The Band-tailed Pigeon has experienced significant population declines:

- **Historical abundance:** Band-tailed Pigeons were once extremely abundant. Early naturalists described flocks of thousands. The species was one of the most common large birds in PNW forests.
- **Market hunting:** In the late 1800s and early 1900s, Band-tailed Pigeons were commercially hunted in large numbers. The mineral spring congregations made them particularly vulnerable -- hunters could kill dozens at a single site.
- **Passenger Pigeon parallel:** The Band-tailed Pigeon's decline echoes the extinction of the Passenger Pigeon (Ectopistes migratorius), which went from billions to zero by 1914. The Band-tailed Pigeon never reached Passenger Pigeon numbers, but the pattern of overhunting a concentrated, flock-forming pigeon species is the same.
- **Habitat loss:** Forest clearing, old-growth logging, and urban expansion have reduced Band-tailed Pigeon habitat. The species depends on mature forest with fruit-bearing and nut-bearing trees.
- **Current status:** Populations have declined 2-3% per year in recent decades according to Breeding Bird Survey data. The Pacific coast population is estimated at 1.5-2 million birds -- a fraction of historical numbers. The species is not listed as endangered but is classified as a species of conservation concern in multiple states.
- **Hunting regulation:** Band-tailed Pigeon hunting is now regulated with short seasons and low bag limits, but hunting pressure continues to contribute to population stress.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; WDFW; Partners in Flight; Breeding Bird Survey data; Keppie & Braun 2000)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Forest Canopy -- Deep Owl-Like Cooing

The Band-tailed Pigeon's acoustic contribution is **the deep background coo of the summer forest**:

- **Individual call:** A deep, resonant "coo-COO" or "whoo-OOO" -- two notes, the second slightly higher and more emphatic. Fundamental frequency approximately 300-600 Hz. Surprisingly low for a pigeon and often mistaken for an owl.
- **Repetition:** The coo is repeated 3-6 times with 2-3 second pauses between repetitions. Then silence. The sporadic pattern creates an intermittent forest soundscape.
- **Tonal quality:** Rich, resonant, with a breathy quality. The sound is produced by the syrinx and amplified by the inflated crop. The resonance gives the call its owl-like depth.
- **Canopy attenuation:** The call carries 100-200 meters through forest. Canopy foliage attenuates high frequencies, so the low-frequency fundamental carries farthest. At distance, the call is a diffuse, atmospheric murmur.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate at the source but perceived as quiet from ground level due to canopy height (20-40m). E=3 reflects the muted, ambient quality.
- **Seasonal pattern:** Calling peaks during breeding season (May-August in PNW). Males call from high perches to attract mates and advertise territory. Outside breeding season, calling is infrequent.
- **Wing sound:** On takeoff, Band-tailed Pigeons produce a loud wing clap -- not as dramatic as the Rock Pigeon's but noticeable. This mechanical sound is a secondary acoustic element.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Keppie & Braun 2000; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
