## PLECOPTERA -- THE ANCIENT ORDER

### 250 Million Years. One of the Earliest Winged Insect Orders.

Stoneflies (order Plecoptera) have a fossil record extending approximately 250-300 million years, placing their origin in the Permian period. They are among the most ancient of the winged insect orders, pre-dating the evolution of most flowering plants. The fact that they are abundant in Pacific Northwest rivers in the twenty-first century is a statement about the ecological integrity of those river systems: stoneflies are among the most sensitive of aquatic invertebrates to water quality and habitat degradation. Their presence and abundance are direct indicators of river health.

The order contains approximately 3,500 described species globally. Pacific Northwest rivers host some of the most diverse stonefly communities in North America, with several river systems supporting over 100 species. The large-bodied spring-emerging species that produce the visible mass emergence events are primarily:

- **Pteronarcys californica (Giant Salmonfly):** The largest North American stonefly, adults reaching 4-5 cm body length. Nymphal period 2-4 years in cold, well-oxygenated cobble streams. The characteristic species of major Pacific Northwest rivers (Deschutes, Yakima, Madison-type systems). The mass emergence of giant salmonflies is among the most dramatic seasonal events in Pacific Northwest river ecology.
- **Calineuria californica:** Medium-large stonefly, emerging in spring, one of the most abundant species in major river systems. Brown adult, medium body length. Major component of the spring emergence mass.
- **Hesperoperla pacifica (Golden Stonefly):** Golden-brown adult, medium-large size, slightly later emergence peak than Pteronarcys. Important prey for fish throughout its emergence period.
- **Skwala species:** Smaller early-spring stoneflies, the first to emerge as water temperatures begin to rise in late winter / early spring. Their emergence precedes the larger species and signals the beginning of the emergence season.

(Sources: Plecoptera fossil record 250-300 million years Permian origin; 3,500 described species globally; Pacific Northwest river stonefly diversity 100+ species some systems; Pteronarcys californica giant salmonfly 4-5 cm adults 2-4 year nymphal period; Calineuria californica spring emergence brown adult; Hesperoperla pacifica golden stonefly; Skwala early-spring first emergence; water quality indicator sensitive to degradation)

## THE EMERGENCE -- THE PHYSICAL EVENT

### From Cobble to Air. Two to Four Years to One Night.

The stonefly life cycle is the context for the emergence event:

**The nymphal phase.** Stonefly eggs are laid in the river, typically in fast-flowing water over cobble and gravel substrate. The eggs hatch and the nymphs develop in the river for 1-4 years depending on species, feeding on algae, diatoms, and organic debris (in smaller species) or preying on other invertebrates (in larger predatory species). During this period they molt 10-20+ times. The nymph is the phase during which the animal exists solely in the aquatic environment -- unseen except by the fish and other predators that consume them.

**The emergence trigger.** In large spring-emerging Pacific Northwest species, emergence is triggered by a combination of water temperature (reaching a species-specific threshold, typically 8-12 degrees C for Pteronarcys) and photoperiod (day length increasing past a threshold in April-May). When conditions are met, nymphs begin moving toward the river bank.

**The emergence mechanics.** Mature nymphs crawl out of the water onto rocks, boulders, bridge supports, and streamside vegetation. The exoskeleton (the nymphal shuck) splits along the back. The adult insect emerges, expands its wings, and after a brief period of hardening, becomes capable of flight. The process takes 10-30 minutes per individual.

**The mass emergence scale.** When emergence conditions are met across a major Pacific Northwest river, the event is not a trickle but a flood. Thousands of nymphs per square meter of cobble substrate crawl out over a period of days to weeks. The riverbanks, boulders, bridge pilings, and streamside vegetation become covered with nymphal shucks and emerging adults. The adult stoneflies accumulate on every surface: walls, vehicles, buildings near the river, trees along the bank. At peak emergence, they cover the water surface itself -- swimming adults, newly emerged adults testing their wings, dead adults that have completed oviposition falling to the surface. The river surface is covered.

(Sources: Stonefly egg oviposition in fast water over cobble; nymphal development 1-4 years; 10-20+ molts; water temperature trigger 8-12 degrees C for Pteronarcys; photoperiod trigger April-May; nymph crawl to bank rock boulder emergence; exoskeleton shuck split; adult wing expansion 10-30 minutes; mass emergence scale thousands per square meter cobble; river surface covered at peak)

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT -- WHAT THE MASS EMERGENCE SOUNDS LIKE

### The Hum of Wings. The Splash of Oviposition. The River Transformed.

The acoustic profile of a major stonefly emergence is not a single defining sound -- it is a composite of overlapping sound sources that together fill the acoustic space of the river corridor:

**Wing hum.** The flight wings of large stoneflies (Pteronarcys in particular) produce a distinctive low buzz or hum during flight. Individual insects are not loud. The aggregate of thousands of insects in flight over the river at once produces a sustained hum that fills the riparian zone -- not a high-pitched insect whine (as with mosquitoes or midges) but a lower, more substantial frequency appropriate to the large wing area of the Pteronarcys adult.

**Surface activity.** Females oviposit (lay eggs) by dipping to the water surface, sometimes multiple times, producing a series of small splashes. At peak emergence density, the river surface is continuously being struck by ovipositing females, generating a texture of small-scale acoustic events across the entire river width.

**Fish feeding activity.** The acoustic signature of the emergence is amplified by the predator response. Trout, whitefish, and other salmonids feed aggressively at the surface during the emergence, their rises generating splashes audible throughout the riparian zone. Large fish targeting large stoneflies produce substantial surface disturbances.

**The combined event.** Wing hum + oviposition strikes + fish rises + the baseline of the river itself (the white noise of moving water amplified by the acoustic reflectivity of the water surface at close range) = the full acoustic character of a major stonefly emergence. It is not a single loud event -- it is an enveloping, multi-source acoustic environment that transforms the river corridor from a background soundscape into a foreground one.

(Sources: Pteronarcys wing hum large wing area low buzz flight; aggregate hum thousands of insects in flight; female oviposition dipping to surface small splashes; fish surface feeding rises during emergence; trout whitefish salmonid predation; river white noise baseline; enveloping multi-source acoustic environment; riparian zone transformation)

## THE FOOD WEB FOUNDATION -- WHY E=10

### The Most Important Ecological Event in the Pacific Northwest Freshwater System

The stonefly emergence is not only an acoustic maximum -- it is the foundational event of the Pacific Northwest freshwater food web. The mass emergence of large stonefly species in spring represents:

**Prey base delivery.** The combined biomass of a major spring stonefly emergence in a large Pacific Northwest river is enormous. Estimates for the Pteronarcys emergence on the Deschutes River (Oregon) document millions of individual insects over a 4-6 week period. Each adult weighing 0.5-2 grams: the total biomass delivered to the river's predator community (fish, birds, bats, other invertebrates) is hundreds to thousands of kilograms per river kilometer. This is the most significant single-period prey delivery event in the river ecosystem's annual cycle.

**Salmonid reproduction connection.** Pacific salmon and steelhead time their upstream migration partially in coordination with the large invertebrate emergence periods. The protein input from mass stonefly emergences supports the energetic demands of spawning adults. The freshwater food web and the anadromous fish cycle are linked through the stonefly emergence.

**Indicator ecology.** Because stoneflies are among the most sensitive aquatic invertebrates to water quality, the scale of the emergence is a direct indicator of river health. A major full-river stonefly emergence as documented at degree 355 -- the entire surface covered -- is only possible in a river system with intact cobble substrate, adequate water quality, appropriate temperature regimes, and minimal chemical contamination. E=10 partly derives from the rarity of intact river systems capable of producing the event at this scale.

The engine assigns E=10 where the event is simultaneously the maximum acoustic output AND the maximum ecological significance. The full-river stonefly emergence satisfies both: it is the most acoustically complex invertebrate emergence event in the PNW, and it is the most ecologically significant single-period freshwater event in the same system.
