## FIRST BALAENOPTERIDAE -- THE GREAT WHALES ARRIVE

### The Rorqual Family Enters

Degree 190 marks the entry of Balaenopteridae (rorqual whales):

- **FIRST BALAENOPTERIDAE.** The rorqual family enters. Balaenopteridae includes the largest animals ever to live on Earth: blue whales, fin whales, humpback whales, sei whales, Bryde's whales, and minke whales. The Minke Whale is the smallest rorqual -- the entry point to the family of giants.
- **Filter feeders.** Balaenopteridae are baleen whales -- they have baleen plates instead of teeth, filtering enormous volumes of water to capture krill, small fish, and plankton. The feeding mechanism is fundamentally different from the toothed cetaceans (dolphins, porpoises) that preceded them.
- **Rorqual innovation.** The defining feature of rorquals is the ventral pleats -- longitudinal grooves running from the chin to the navel that expand during lunge feeding, allowing the whale to engulf massive volumes of water and prey. This biomechanical innovation enables the largest animals on Earth.
- **Fourth cetacean family.** Phocoenidae (186-187), Delphinidae (188-189), and now Balaenopteridae (190). The cetacean diversity in the engine continues to expand.

## E=4 -- THE THUMP AND RATCHET

### Why E=4

The Minke Whale registers at E=4:

- **The "boing."** The most distinctive Minke Whale sound is the "boing" -- a loud, repetitive, mechanical-sounding pulse. First recorded by the U.S. Navy in the 1960s and attributed to biological sources only decades later. The sound was so strange that it was initially blamed on submarines or geological sources.
- **Thump trains.** Repetitive low-frequency thumps produced in regular trains. Each thump is a brief, powerful pulse. The trains can last for hours. The sound is rhythmic, mechanical, and unlike any other cetacean vocalization.
- **Ratchet sounds.** A higher-frequency pulsed sound with a mechanical, ratcheting quality -- like a zipper being pulled. The ratchet is produced in social contexts and during feeding.
- **Downsweeps.** Low-frequency downsweep calls (80-200 Hz) used over long distances. The downsweeps carry well in deep water.
- **Why E=4?** The vocal repertoire is distinctive and real but relatively quiet by whale standards. The Minke Whale's sounds are mechanical and rhythmic rather than melodic or voluminous. Compared to the humpback whale song (forthcoming) or the blue whale's infrasonic booms, the Minke Whale's thump-ratchet combination is modest. E=4 reflects a genuinely interesting but acoustically moderate whale.

(Sources: Minke Whale acoustics; Rankin & Barlow 2005; Thompson & Friedl 1982; "boing" identification; engine internal analysis)

## THE SMALLEST RORQUAL -- MODEST BY WHALE STANDARDS

### Size in Context

The Minke Whale is small -- for a whale:

- **Length.** 7-10 meters (23-33 feet). The smallest of the rorqual whales. For comparison: humpback whales reach 16 m, fin whales 24 m, blue whales 30 m.
- **Weight.** 5,000-10,000 kg (5-10 tonnes). Again, the smallest rorqual. Blue whales can weigh 150,000 kg.
- **But still a whale.** A 10-meter, 10-tonne animal is still enormous by any terrestrial standard. The "smallest rorqual" is larger than any land animal except the largest elephants.
- **The entry point.** Starting with the smallest rorqual and building toward the larger species is a natural progression. The Minke Whale is the threshold of the great whales.

(Sources: Minke Whale morphology; rorqual size comparison; cetacean biology; engine internal analysis)

## FILTER FEEDING -- THE BALEEN MECHANISM

### Lunge Feeding

The Minke Whale's feeding mechanism is a marvel of biomechanics:

- **Baleen plates.** 230-360 baleen plates per side of the upper jaw. Each plate is fringed with bristles that filter small prey from water. The baleen is yellowish-white.
- **Lunge feeding.** The Minke Whale accelerates toward a prey concentration, opens its mouth to nearly 90 degrees, and engulfs a huge volume of water and prey. The ventral pleats expand to accommodate the water. The whale then closes its mouth and uses its tongue to force water through the baleen, trapping the prey.
- **Prey.** Krill, small schooling fish (herring, capelin, sand lance), copepods. Diet varies by region and season.
- **Efficiency.** Each lunge can capture several kilograms of prey. A Minke Whale may perform 100+ lunges per day during peak feeding.

(Sources: Minke Whale feeding behavior; baleen anatomy; lunge feeding biomechanics; engine internal analysis)

## PUGET SOUND AND WASHINGTON WATERS

### Summer Visitor

- **Open sea.** The Minke Whale is primarily an open-sea species -- continental shelf, slope, and oceanic waters.
- **Summer visitor.** Most commonly seen in Washington waters during summer months (June-September) when prey concentrations are highest.
- **San Juan Islands.** One of the best places to see Minke Whales in Washington. They enter the inland waters through the Strait of Juan de Fuca and forage around the San Juan Islands.
- **Solitary or small groups.** Unlike the social dolphins, Minke Whales are typically solitary or in pairs. Larger aggregations occur at rich feeding areas but are not social groups.
- **Population.** Global population estimated at 500,000+. Eastern North Pacific: tens of thousands. Washington waters: dozens to low hundreds seasonally.

(Sources: NOAA; WDFW; Minke Whale distribution; San Juan Islands whale watching; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Minke Whale

**Thump Trains:**
- **Frequency:** 100-500 Hz (infrasonic to low audible).
- **Character:** Repetitive, rhythmic pulses. Mechanical quality. Regular spacing.
- **Context:** Long-distance communication. Often heard at night. Can persist for hours.
- **Duration:** Each thump 0.1-0.5 seconds. Trains last minutes to hours.

**Ratchet:**
- **Frequency:** 500-5,000 Hz (audible).
- **Character:** Pulsed, mechanical, zipper-like sound. Higher frequency than thumps.
- **Context:** Social interaction, feeding contexts.
- **Duration:** 0.5-3 seconds per ratchet sequence.

**Downsweeps:**
- **Frequency:** 80-200 Hz (infrasonic to very low audible).
- **Character:** Descending frequency sweep. Low, powerful. Long-range.
- **Context:** Long-distance contact. Deep-water propagation.

**"Boing":**
- **Frequency:** 1-4 kHz (audible).
- **Character:** Distinctive pulsed sound with mechanical quality. Initially misidentified as non-biological.
- **Context:** Unknown specific function. Possibly territorial or mate attraction.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. Distinctive, mechanical, rhythmic. Genuinely interesting but acoustically moderate compared to larger rorquals. The thump-ratchet combination is unique in the ocean.

(Sources: Rankin & Barlow 2005; Thompson & Friedl 1982; Minke Whale acoustics; engine internal analysis)
