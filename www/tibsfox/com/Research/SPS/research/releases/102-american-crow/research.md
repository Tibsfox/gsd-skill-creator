## THE AMERICAN CROW -- *Corvus brachyrhynchos*

### The Species

The American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*) is one of the **most familiar birds in North America** [1, 2]:
- **Body length:** 40-53 cm (significantly smaller than the raven's 54-67 cm).
- **Wingspan:** 85-100 cm (vs raven's 115-150 cm).
- **Weight:** 0.3-0.6 kg (vs raven's 0.7-1.5 kg).
- **Plumage:** Entirely black, like the raven, but without the raven's pronounced iridescent sheen.
- **Bill:** Shorter and slimmer than the raven's heavy bill.
- **Tail:** Fan-shaped in flight (diagnostic -- ravens have wedge-shaped tails).
- **Distribution:** Across North America. One of the most widely distributed and numerous birds on the continent. In the PNW, found everywhere -- cities, suburbs, farms, forests, beaches.

### The Most Familiar Corvid

The crow is the corvid people know [1, 2]:
- **Urban presence** -- crows are common in every PNW city and town. They walk on lawns, perch on power lines, raid garbage cans, and watch from parking lots.
- **Social visibility** -- crows travel in groups and roost in massive aggregations. Hard to miss.
- **Vocal prominence** -- the caw carries and repeats. The most audible bird in urban environments.
- **Cultural recognition** -- everyone knows what a crow is. The crow is the baseline bird.

### Intelligence -- Face Recognition and Funerals

The American Crow is among the most intelligent animals on Earth [1, 2, 3]:

**Face recognition:** Crows recognize individual human faces and remember threats for years [3]. This was demonstrated experimentally by John Marzluff at the University of Washington -- masked researchers captured crows, and those specific mask-wearing faces were scolded by crows for years afterward. The knowledge spread to crows that had never been directly threatened -- cultural transmission of threat identification.

**Tool use:** Documented but less extensive than raven tool use. Crows have been observed using sticks to extract food and dropping nuts on roads for cars to crack.

**Funerals:** Crows gather around dead crows in apparent mourning or assessment behavior. The function is debated -- it may be threat assessment (learning what killed the crow) rather than grief -- but the behavior is consistent and striking.

**Cooperative mobbing:** Crows cooperate to mob predators (hawks, owls, cats, eagles) with coordinated aerial harassment and alarm calling.

(Sources: [1] Wikipedia; [2] Marzluff & Angell 2012; [3] Marzluff et al. 2010)

## THE SOUND: CAW CAW CAW COMPLEX -- 102nd ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (species side)

### The Caw Complex

The American Crow's "caw" is arguably the **most recognized bird sound in North America** [1, 2]. But it is not one sound -- it is a complex of distinct caw types, each carrying different information:

**Alarm caw:** Rapid, harsh, urgent. "CAW CAW CAW CAW" in quick succession. Signals predator presence. Triggers coordinated mobbing response from nearby crows. High-amplitude, high-urgency.

**Assembly caw:** Rhythmic, moderate pace. Calls crows to a location -- roost site, food source, mob target. Social coordination call.

**Scolding caw:** Directed, persistent, often accompanied by physical approach to the threat. Crows scold specific threats -- hawks, cats, specific humans they recognize [3]. The scolding caw is personalized and persistent. A scolded human can be followed for blocks.

**Feeding call:** Softer, shorter caws that signal food discovery to the group. Less urgent than alarm but carries social information.

**Rattling and clicking:** Softer, more complex vocalizations used in close social interactions among flock members. Not as varied as the raven's 30+ call types but more complex than the "caw" stereotype suggests.

### Why E=4

1. **LOUD AND PERSISTENT.** The caw carries well -- audible at several hundred meters. Crows are among the loudest common birds.
2. **REPETITIVE FORCE.** The caw repeats -- "caw caw caw caw" -- creating a sustained acoustic presence. Persistence adds to impact.
3. **SOCIAL AMPLIFICATION.** A group of crows cawing together is louder than any individual. Roosts of thousands are acoustically overwhelming.
4. **NOT E=5.** The individual crow is not as powerful as the raven pair's coordinated duet (E=5). The caw is loud but not at the peak of the energy scale. E=4 captures the familiar, persistent, strong voice.

### Contrasts with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Common Raven** (degree 100) -- deep cronk, 30+ call types, E=4. The crow has fewer call types but the caw is more recognizable and more ubiquitous. Same energy, different complexity, different familiarity.
- **Raven pair** (degree 101) -- coordinated duet, E=5. The crow is socially vocal rather than pair-duetting. Individual crow voice simpler, but social coordination (hundreds cawing) is a different kind of complexity.
- **Steller's Jay** (degree 53, Corvidae) -- loud, harsh, versatile. The Jay and the Crow share the harsh corvid timbre. The Crow's caw is simpler but more universally recognized.

The 102nd acoustic element (species side). The caw that everyone knows.

### Corvus: Raven and Crow

Both the Common Raven and the American Crow belong to genus *Corvus* -- the largest genus in Corvidae. The size difference is the most obvious distinction:

| Feature | Common Raven | American Crow |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 54-67 cm | 40-53 cm |
| Wingspan | 115-150 cm | 85-100 cm |
| Weight | 0.7-1.5 kg | 0.3-0.6 kg |
| Tail shape | Wedge (diamond) | Fan (rounded) |
| Bill | Heavy, thick, curved | Shorter, slimmer |
| Voice | Deep cronk, 30+ types | Higher caw, fewer types |
| Social | Solitary/pairs | Flocks of hundreds+ |
| Habitat | Open forest, mountains | Everywhere |

Same genus, dramatically different ecologies. The raven is the apex; the crow is the generalist.

## ECOLOGY: URBAN, SOCIAL, HABITAT-GENERALIST

### Varied Habitat -- The Most Generalist Bird

The American Crow is the **most habitat-generalist bird in the engine** [1, 2]:
- **Urban** -- the most urban bird in the engine. Present in every PNW city.
- **Suburban** -- lawns, parks, schools, shopping centers.
- **Rural** -- farms, agricultural land, orchards.
- **Forest** -- forest edges, clearings (not deep forest like the raven).
- **Coastal** -- beaches, intertidal zones.
- **Everywhere** -- parking lots, landfills, highways, bridges.

This generalism is intelligence-driven. The crow adapts to any food source, any shelter, any threat landscape.

### Social Structure -- The Roost

Crows are **intensely social** [1, 2]:
- **Winter roosts:** Communal roosts of hundreds to hundreds of thousands of crows. PNW winter roosts (e.g., Bothell, Renton) can contain 10,000+ crows. The gathering at dusk is one of the PNW's most spectacular urban wildlife events.
- **Family groups:** Crows live in family groups -- parents and offspring from previous years help raise new chicks (cooperative breeding).
- **Information networks:** Roosts function as information hubs -- crows learn food source locations, predator threats, and social relationships within the roost.

### The Crow in PNW Urban Life

The crow is a constant presence in PNW urban life:
- **UW campus connection:** The University of Washington is the site of John Marzluff's famous crow face-recognition experiments [3]. UW crows are among the most studied in the world.
- **Bothell/Renton roosts:** Massive winter roosts along the I-405 corridor. Tens of thousands of crows. A PNW spectacle.
- **Garbage day crows:** Crows that have learned municipal garbage collection schedules. Documented across Seattle neighborhoods.

(Sources: [1] Wikipedia; [2] Marzluff & Angell 2012; [3] Marzluff et al. 2010; local observation)
