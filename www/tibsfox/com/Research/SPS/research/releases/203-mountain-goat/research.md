## FIRST BOVIDAE -- THE FAMILY ENTERS

### Not Actually a Goat

The Mountain Goat's taxonomy is one of the great naming misidentifications:

- **Common name: Mountain Goat.** Everyone calls it a goat. It looks like a goat. It lives on mountains like a goat. Early European naturalists in North America saw it and called it a goat. The name stuck.
- **Actual classification: goat-antelope.** *Oreamnos americanus* is not in the genus *Capra* (true goats). It's in the subfamily Caprinae (which includes goats, sheep, and goat-antelopes), but its closest relative is the chamois (*Rupicapra rupicapra*) of the European Alps. The Mountain Goat is the only living species in the genus *Oreamnos*.
- **Why it matters.** The misidentification is not just trivia -- it reflects how common names can mislead. The Mountain Goat evolved independently on North American mountains, developing similar cliff-dwelling adaptations to European chamois through convergent evolution. It's a goat-antelope that convergently resembles a goat. The name is a lie that happens to describe the behavior accurately.
- **Bovidae enters.** The Bovidae is the largest family of large ungulates: cattle, bison, goats, sheep, antelopes, gazelles. The Mountain Goat is the first Bovidae in the engine. The family will appear again at degree 204 (Mountain Goat kid).

(Sources: Bovidae taxonomy; Oreamnos vs. Capra; convergent evolution; chamois relationship; alpine mammal classification)

## THE IMPOSSIBLE TERRAIN

### Cliff-Dwelling Adaptations

Mountain Goats live where almost nothing else can:

- **Elevation.** In Washington, Mountain Goats occupy alpine and subalpine zones, typically 1,500-3,000 meters (5,000-10,000 feet). They are found in the Cascades, Olympics (introduced population), and North Cascades. They live above treeline for much of the year.
- **Cliff adaptation.** The Mountain Goat's body is built for vertical terrain:
  - **Hooves.** Split hooves with soft, rubbery pads that provide grip on rock. The inner pad conforms to the surface like a climbing shoe. The dewclaws provide additional traction on steep descents.
  - **Musculature.** Powerful shoulders and forelegs for climbing. The center of gravity is low and forward, providing stability on near-vertical surfaces.
  - **Wool.** Dense, double-layered coat: soft undercoat for insulation, longer guard hairs for weather resistance. Can withstand -50 C temperatures and 150 km/h winds. The warmest coat of any North American mammal.
  - **Aggression.** Mountain Goats defend cliff territory with sharp, dagger-like horns. Both sexes have horns (permanent, not shed like antlers). Falls from cliffs are the leading cause of death -- even for Mountain Goats, the terrain is lethal.
- **Why cliffs?** The cliffs are the strategy. Mountain Goats are not fast runners on flat ground. They cannot outrun predators. Their strategy is to go where predators cannot follow. Mountain Lions, wolves, and bears cannot navigate the vertical terrain that Mountain Goats traverse daily. The cliff is the fortress.

(Sources: Mountain Goat anatomy; hoof structure; alpine adaptations; cliff-dwelling behavior; predator avoidance)

## THE BLEAT -- COMMUNICATION ON CLIFFS

### Sound Across Stone

Mountain Goat vocalizations are adapted to their environment:

- **The adult bleat.** "Baaah" -- low, nasal, goat-like. Pitched at 200-600 Hz. Duration 0.5-1.5 seconds. The bleat is a contact call: maintaining awareness of herd members across rocky terrain where visual contact is frequently broken by ridges, outcrops, and cliff faces.
- **Acoustic environment.** Alpine cliff environments are acoustically distinctive: hard rock surfaces reflect sound, creating echoes. Wind is constant and loud. The Mountain Goat's bleat is pitched to carry across rocky basins despite wind interference -- the nasal quality cuts through wind noise.
- **Herd communication.** Mountain Goats live in small herds (typically 5-20 animals). The herd moves together across cliff faces, and the bleat maintains cohesion when visual contact is lost. Nannies (females) and kids bleat most frequently. Billies (males) are more silent except during rut.
- **Rut vocalizations.** During the November-December rut, billies produce a low grunting and a distinctive lip-curl display (flehmen response). The rut vocalizations are lower-pitched than the standard bleat.

(Sources: Mountain Goat vocalization; alpine acoustics; herd communication; rut behavior)

## OLYMPICS POPULATION -- INTRODUCED AND CONTROVERSIAL

### Not Native to the Olympics

Mountain Goats in the Olympic Mountains are an ecological controversy:

- **Introduction.** Mountain Goats were introduced to the Olympics in the 1920s by hunters who brought 11-12 animals from Alaska and British Columbia. The population grew to 1,000+ by the 2000s.
- **Ecological impact.** The Olympics evolved without Mountain Goats. Native alpine plants (many endemic to the Olympics) have no evolutionary defense against goat grazing. Mountain Goats have damaged fragile alpine meadows and rare plant communities.
- **Removal program.** In 2018-2020, the National Park Service relocated approximately 325 Mountain Goats from the Olympics to the North Cascades, where they are native. Some were airlifted by helicopter (an extraordinary operation -- sedated goats in slings, flown across the state). The goal: remove non-native goats from the Olympics while augmenting the declining native Cascades population.
- **Engine note.** The Mountain Goat in this engine represents the Cascades native population, not the Olympics introduced population. The Cascades is the natural range.

(Sources: WDFW Mountain Goat management; Olympic National Park removal program; North Cascades augmentation)

## E=4 -- STEADY ON STONE

### Calibration

The Mountain Goat bleat at E=4 reflects alpine steadiness:

- **Why E=4?** The bleat is moderate -- not loud by ungulate standards. It carries across rocky basins but is not a bugle or a roar. E=4 reflects: audible, nasal, steady, persistent -- but moderate in absolute terms. The Mountain Goat does not need to be loud. The acoustic environment (rock surfaces, echoes) amplifies the sound naturally.
- **Comparison.** E=4 matches the Black Bear cub distress (SPS-202) and the Black-tailed Deer buck grunt (SPS-196). All moderate, all functional, all sufficient for their purpose without being dramatic.
- **Alpine E=4.** E=4 on a cliff is different from E=4 in a forest. The rock amplifies. The echo extends. A moderate sound in the alpine zone carries farther than the same sound in dense forest.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Mountain Goat

**Adult Bleat (Contact Call):**
- **Frequency:** 200-600 Hz. Low to mid-range. Nasal quality.
- **Character:** Goat-like "baaah." Nasal, resonant, slightly wavering. The nasal quality cuts through wind noise.
- **Duration:** 0.5-1.5 seconds per bleat. Repeated at intervals when maintaining herd contact.
- **Context:** Contact call. Herd cohesion across cliff terrain. Nanny-kid communication. General awareness.
- **Carry distance:** 200-500 meters across rocky alpine terrain. Rock surfaces reflect and amplify. Farther than equivalent sound in forest.

**Kid Contact Call (see degree 204):**
- **Frequency:** 400-1,000 Hz. Higher than adult. Thinner.
- **Character:** Higher, thinner "meeeh." The infant version.
- **Context:** Separation distress. Mother-seeking.

**Billy Rut Grunt:**
- **Frequency:** 100-300 Hz. Lower than bleat.
- **Character:** Low grunting during rut (November-December). With lip-curl display.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. Moderate, nasal, steady. Alpine acoustic amplification extends effective range.
