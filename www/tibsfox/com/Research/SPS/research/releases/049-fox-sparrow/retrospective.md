## Purpose

This is the fiftieth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-49), the fifth Passerellidae, the fifth genus in the Passerellidae quintet, and the degree that brings the fox into the sparrow family. *Passerella iliaca* -- "the little sparrow of the foxy flanks," the largest sparrow in the engine, the bird whose common name carries the same fox identity as the Tibsfox brand, the species that may be one bird or four, the most melodious singer in the New World Sparrow family -- enters the engine as the closing statement of the Passerellidae quintet: five consecutive degrees, five genera, five habitats, five organizing principles for birdsong. The release carries seven structural contributions:

1. **The ballad -- the 50th acoustic element (species side).** A rich, melodious, complete whistled statement: 8-12 clear whistled notes, 2-3 seconds, 2-6 kHz. Named "the ballad" for the song's character (widely considered the finest sparrow song in North America), the quintet's closing role (the ballad ends the concert), and the fox connection (a ballad tells a story, and the Fox Sparrow's name tells a story). The first acoustic element defined by **melodious completeness**.

2. **FIFTH consecutive Passerellidae -- TIES the longest same-family streak in the engine.** Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) + White-crowned Sparrow (48) + Fox Sparrow (49). Five genera (*Melospiza*, *Pipilo*, *Junco*, *Zonotrichia*, *Passerella*). Five habitats (wetland edge, dense understory, forest floor, mountain meadow, dense thicket). Five song strategies (improvisation, concentration, reduction, transmission, completeness). TIES the Hirundinidae quintet (26-30) and Parulidae quintet (38-42). THREE families at five-consecutive. No family has exceeded it.

3. **The fox-Tibsfox name resonance.** The "fox" in Fox Sparrow refers to the rufous, fox-colored plumage of the Red subspecies group -- the same warm foxy coloring that gives the Tibsfox brand its name. A structural coincidence surfaced by the CSV substitution process, not engineered but resonant. *Passerella iliaca* = "the little sparrow of the foxy flanks."

4. **The four-way taxonomic debate.** Fox Sparrow's four subspecies groups (Red, Sooty, Slate-colored, Thick-billed) may represent separate species. IOC/BirdLife split them; AOS/eBird lump them. A 2003 AOU proposal was rejected on a split vote. Molecular data confirm four genetically distinct lineages. The engine's most taxonomically volatile species.

5. **The double-scratch connection.** Fox Sparrow and Spotted Towhee (degree 46) both use the same backward-hopping, leaf-raking double-scratch foraging technique. The Fox Sparrow scratches more vigorously -- the larger bird generates more force. Two double-scratchers bookend the quintet's middle (degrees 46 and 49).

6. **The largest sparrow.** At 26-49 g (average ~32 g), the Fox Sparrow is among the heaviest New World Sparrows in the engine and the heaviest member of the Passerellidae quintet.

7. **CSV substitution documented.** Western Toad replaced by Fox Sparrow. Fifth consecutive amphibian replacement (45-46-47-48-49). The engine continues deep into the amphibian belt of the original CSV.

## What Worked

### 1. The ballad is a genuinely distinct closing element
The ballad differs from every nearby element in a way that earns its name. It is not the preamble's structural opening, not the rasp's textural intensity, not the snowbird's radical simplicity, not the dialect's cultural function. It is pure melodious statement -- a song that is beautiful because the voice is rich and every note earns its place. The Fox Sparrow's reputation as "the finest sparrow singer" makes this naming credible and specific. The quintet closes with beauty, not complexity.

### 2. The fox-Tibsfox connection adds an unexpected layer
The CSV substitution system placed Fox Sparrow at degree 49 because it matched Western Toad's energy and habitat -- not because of the name. That the replacement species happens to carry "fox" in its common name, linking to the Tibsfox brand, is a structural coincidence that the engine surfaces honestly. The connection is documented as unplanned resonance, not engineered symbolism.

### 3. The five-degree streak achieves maximum genus diversity
Five genera in five degrees -- every member from a different genus. This matches the Hirundinidae quintet's genus diversity and surpasses the Parulidae quintet's (4 genera in 5 degrees). The Passerellidae quintet is the most taxonomically diverse family run in the engine.

### 4. The three-quintet architecture reveals vertical structure
Air (swallows, E=4), canopy (warblers, E=3-4), ground (sparrows, E=2-3). Three ecological strata mapped by three family quintets. This was not planned -- it emerged from the 360-degree progression. The fact that it maps the PNW forest's vertical structure so cleanly is evidence that the engine's species ordering carries real ecological signal.

### 5. The four-way taxonomic debate adds genuine scientific depth
The Fox Sparrow's potential four-way species split is one of the most actively debated questions in North American ornithology. Placing it in the engine adds taxonomic complexity that no previous species has matched. The contrast with White-crowned Sparrow (degree 48, five subspecies that no one proposes splitting) is particularly sharp -- two consecutive Passerellidae at opposite ends of the taxonomic certainty spectrum.

### 6. The double-scratch connection links degrees 46 and 49
The behavioral link between Fox Sparrow and Spotted Towhee (both double-scratchers) creates a cross-degree connection within the quintet that goes beyond taxonomy and song. Two species, same behavior, different intensities. The quintet's second and fifth members share a signature foraging technique.

### 7. The monotypic genus adds unique taxonomic character
*Passerella* is the only monotypic genus in the quintet -- and in the entire engine's Passerellidae representation. Every other quintet genus has multiple species. The "little sparrow" stands alone. This gives degree 49 a distinct taxonomic identity that no other quintet member shares.

And now -- one degree later, deeper into the forest, where the trail narrows and the salal closes in and the sword fern reaches across the path and the blackberry tangles overhead -- you cannot see the bird. You can hear it. First: a scratching sound. Loud, rhythmic, aggressive. Then -- meewww? -- a sharp, descending, cat-like call. Then the song. Not a melody. Not a cascade. A sound like a taut rubber band being plucked. Drink-your-tea! Pipilo maculatus. The spotted chirper. The rasp. And now -- one degree later, on the forest floor where the Douglas firs stand tall and the light filters down in columns through the canopy, where the trail crosses a parking lot and the parking lot borders a garden and the garden fades into second-growth forest -- the simplest sound in the run. A trill. One note, one pitch, repeated seven times, twelve times, twenty times. A small gray bird with a dark hood and a rusty back and white flashing from its tail when it flies. Junco hyemalis. The winter rush-bird. The snowbird. And now -- one degree later, at the edge of the meadow where the mountain slope opens up and the subalpine shrubs grow in dense patches between bare ground and wildflowers -- a bird with a head painted in bold black-and-white stripes. It sits at the top of a willow bush and lifts its head and opens its bill. A clear, pure whistle rings across the meadow -- one note, held, clean as glass. And then the whistle breaks into a tumble of buzzy trills and complex phrases, rising and falling, each section different from the last, a structured composition in 2-3 seconds. The introductory whistle says: I am a White-crowned Sparrow. The terminal trill says: I am from HERE. The complex notes say: I am THIS individual. Three layers of identity in one song. Learned from the adults who sang here before, during the first hundred days of life, and carried faithfully for a lifetime. One tradition. Twelve dialects along the coast. The same song, different everywhere. The same bird, named for this place. Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis. The white-browed banded-hair bird of Puget Sound. The dialect. And now -- one degree later, deeper into the thicket than any bird in the quintet has gone, where the salal grows head-high and the blackberry closes overhead and the sword fern reaches across the trail and the light does not reach the ground -- a sound. First: a violent rustling in the leaf litter, loud enough to hear from ten meters. Then a sharp tsik from inside the brush. And then, drifting out of the darkest corner of the thicket -- a song. Not complex like the Song Sparrow's. Not buzzy like the towhee's. Not simple like the junco's. Not structured like the White-crowned Sparrow's. Just -- beautiful. Rich. Warm. Full. Eight clear, whistled notes, slow and measured, each one carrying weight, each one landing perfectly. The finest sparrow song in North America, sung from inside a thicket you cannot enter. Passerella iliaca. The little sparrow of the foxy flanks. The fox in the forest. The ballad.*
