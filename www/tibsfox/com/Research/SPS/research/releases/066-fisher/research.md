## TAXONOMY: *Pekania pennanti*

### Etymology and Reclassification

- **Pekania** -- derived from *pekan*, an Abenaki (Algonquian) word for the Fisher [1, 2, 3]. This is one of the few mammalian genera named from a Native American language. The genus was erected when molecular phylogenetic studies demonstrated that the Fisher is NOT a member of *Martes* (the true martens) despite being classified as *Martes pennanti* for over two centuries. The Fisher diverged from the marten lineage approximately 8-12 million years ago and represents its own distinct genus within Mustelidae [1, 2, 4].

- **pennanti** -- honoring Thomas Pennant (1726-1798), the Welsh naturalist and zoologist who described the species. Pennant's *History of Quadrupeds* (1781) provided an early account of the Fisher based on specimens from eastern North America [1, 2].

- **Common name:** Fisher. The name is a persistent misnomer -- Fishers do NOT fish and are not particularly associated with aquatic habitats. The origin is debated: possibly from the French *fiche*, a polecat pelt; possibly from *fisse*, a European polecat; possibly from Dutch *visse* or *fisse* [1, 2]. The name has nothing to do with fishing. This is the engine's most misleadingly named species -- a forest predator called "Fisher" that lives far from water.

The reclassification from *Martes* to *Pekania* is the third genus reclassification in the Mustelidae quartet: the River Otter was reclassified from *Lutra* to *Lontra*, the Mink from *Mustela* to *Neogale*, and the Fisher from *Martes* to *Pekania*. All three reclassifications were driven by the same molecular phylogenetic revolution that swept through mammalian taxonomy in the late 1990s and 2000s. The American Marten remains in *Martes* -- it IS a true marten. The Fisher merely resembled one. Three of the four Mustelidae quartet members have been reclassified out of Old World genera.

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (7th mammalian entry; 5th mammalian species)
- **Order:** Carnivora -- fourth Carnivora entry (Otter 63, Mink 64, Marten 65, Fisher 66). Four consecutive Carnivora. The engine's carnivore count quadruples from 1 to 4 across the Mustelidae quartet.
- **Family:** Mustelidae -- **fourth consecutive Mustelidae entry (degrees 63-66)**. The QUARTET. Four species, four habitats, four sounds. Mustelidae representation increases to 4 of 8 PNW species (50% coverage). Half of Washington's mustelid diversity is now in the engine.
- **Genus:** *Pekania* -- monotypic genus. The Fisher is the sole living member of *Pekania* [1, 2, 4]. There are no other species in this genus. The Fisher stands alone taxonomically, as it stands alone ecologically.
- **Species:** *P. pennanti* -- the Fisher. Two subspecies sometimes recognized: *P. p. pennanti* (eastern) and *P. p. pacifica* (western, including PNW populations) [1, 2].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE LARGEST FOREST MUSTELID

### Body Form

The Fisher is the largest terrestrial mustelid in the Mustelidae quartet and the second-largest mustelid in the PNW (after the River Otter) [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Length:** 75-120 cm (30-47 inches) total, including tail. Body length approximately 50-70 cm; tail approximately 30-40 cm. Males are substantially larger than females -- sexual dimorphism is extreme in Fishers, with males averaging nearly twice the mass of females [1, 2, 5].
- **Weight:** Males 3.5-6 kg (7.7-13.2 lbs); females 2-2.5 kg (4.4-5.5 lbs). This makes the Fisher 2-3 times the mass of the American Marten and 3-4 times the mass of the Mink. The Fisher is no longer the "smallest predator" -- it is a substantial, powerful animal that can take prey the size of a snowshoe hare or a porcupine.
- **Body shape:** The classic mustelid elongation, but more robust and powerful than the Marten or Mink. Broader shoulders, thicker neck, more muscular forequarters. The Fisher's body is built for power and leverage -- it needs both to flip and kill porcupines [1, 2, 3].
- **Legs:** Short but proportionally more robust than the Marten's. The Fisher is an effective climber but spends more time on the forest floor than the Marten. It moves with a bounding gait on the ground and can climb trees rapidly when pursuing prey or accessing den sites.
- **Feet:** Large, furred pads that spread for traction on snow and bark. No webbing (unlike the Mink and Otter). The Fisher's feet are adapted for forest substrates: duff, bark, branches, and snow. In winter, the relatively large feet act as partial snowshoes [1, 2, 5].
- **Tail:** Long, bushy, dark. Used for balance during climbing and bounding. More prominent than the Marten's tail, contributing to the Fisher's overall larger silhouette.

### Fur and Appearance

- **Guard hairs:** Long, coarse, glossy. Dark brown to nearly black, darker than the Marten. The fur grades from dark brown on the body to nearly black on the legs and tail. A grizzled gold-to-silver sheen on the head, neck, and shoulders -- the "frosted" appearance that is diagnostic for the Fisher [1, 2, 3, 5].
- **Underfur:** Dense and soft, insulating against Pacific Northwest winters in montane forest. Not as dense as the Mink's aquatic-adapted fur, but effective for a forest-floor predator.
- **Facial features:** Rounded ears, relatively small for the body size. Broad, flat head with powerful jaw muscles. Dark eyes. The face is broader and more bear-like than the Marten's delicate, fox-like face [1, 2, 5].
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Extreme. Male Fishers are nearly twice the weight of females. This dimorphism is thought to reduce intraspecific competition by allowing males and females to target different prey sizes -- males take larger prey (porcupines, snowshoe hares), females take smaller prey (squirrels, mice, voles) [1, 2, 5].

### The Marten-Fisher Size Comparison

| Feature | American Marten (65) | Fisher (66) |
|---|---|---|
| Weight | 0.5-1.2 kg | 2-6 kg |
| Length | 50-68 cm | 75-120 cm |
| Primary habitat | Old-growth canopy | Dense forest floor/midstory |
| Prey size | Voles, squirrels | Hares, porcupines |
| Climbing ability | Expert (high canopy) | Competent (midstory) |
| Snow adaptation | Light, stays on surface | Large feet, partial snowshoe |

The Fisher is the Marten scaled up. Same family, same general body plan, same predatory strategy -- but bigger, more powerful, more ground-oriented. The Marten is the canopy specialist; the Fisher is the forest-floor generalist with canopy access.

## DENSE FOREST HABITAT: THE CLOSED-CANOPY SPECIALIST

### Habitat Requirements

The Fisher requires **dense, closed-canopy forest** with structural complexity [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7]:

- **Canopy closure:** Fishers select for forests with >60% canopy closure. They avoid openings, clearcuts, and fragmented forest. The closed canopy provides thermal cover, predator avoidance (from aerial predators like eagles and great horned owls), and continuous travel routes through the forest [1, 2, 5, 6].
- **Large trees:** Fishers need large trees for denning (reproductive females use cavities in large-diameter trees, snags, and broken-top trees) and for resting sites (large branches, platform structures in the canopy). Minimum den tree diameter is typically >50 cm DBH [1, 2, 5, 6, 7].
- **Structural complexity:** Downed logs, root wads, brush piles, rocky outcrops. The forest floor must provide cover, denning options, and prey habitat. Fishers use the three-dimensional structure of the forest -- ground, midstory, and canopy -- more completely than any other predator in the engine [1, 2, 5].
- **Forest type:** In the PNW, Fishers use Douglas-fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, and true fir forests. They are associated with mature and old-growth forest but also use younger dense forests with adequate structural complexity. The key is DENSITY and COVER, not old-growth specifically [1, 2, 5, 6, 7].

### Dense Forest vs. Old-Growth: The Fisher-Marten Habitat Distinction

The American Marten (degree 65) requires **old-growth** forest -- the specific structural features of ancient forest (200+ year trees, complex canopy layers, epiphyte mats, deep duff). The Fisher requires **dense** forest -- closed canopy, structural complexity, large trees for denning -- but the forest need not be ancient. Dense second-growth forest with adequate structural features can support Fishers [1, 2, 5, 6, 7].

This is a meaningful ecological distinction:
- The **Marten** is an old-growth obligate. Logging old-growth eliminates Marten habitat.
- The **Fisher** is a dense-forest specialist. Logging that removes canopy cover eliminates Fisher habitat, but dense younger forest can support Fishers if structural features are present.

Both need large trees. Both need canopy cover. Both suffer from forest fragmentation. But the Fisher has slightly broader habitat tolerance than the Marten, which may be one reason conservation efforts to reintroduce Fishers have shown early success.

### The Habitat Gradient: Water to Deep Forest

The Mustelidae quartet traces a habitat gradient from water to deep forest:

| Degree | Habitat | Water Association | Canopy Association |
|---|---|---|---|
| 63 | River/estuary | Aquatic | None |
| 64 | Creek bank | Semi-aquatic | Riparian |
| 65 | Old-growth canopy | None | High canopy |
| **66** | **Dense forest** | **None** | **Closed canopy, ground to midstory** |

The Fisher completes the transition from water to deep forest. No water. No riparian margin. Deep, dense, closed-canopy forest -- the interior, not the edge. The Fisher is the first species in the engine whose habitat is defined by DISTANCE from water rather than proximity to it.

## THE SOUND: LOUD SCREAMING CALL -- 67th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Scream

The Fisher produces one of the most arresting sounds in the Pacific Northwest forest -- a **loud, piercing, almost-human scream** [1, 2, 3, 8]:

The scream is:
- **Loud:** E=5. When a Fisher screams, the sound carries through dense forest for hundreds of meters. It is one of the loudest vocalizations of any mustelid, disproportionately powerful for the animal's size. This is the second E=5 species-side entry (after Mallard at degree 59) and the LOUDEST mammalian vocalization in the engine.
- **Rare:** Unlike the Mallard's ubiquitous quack (E=5, heard constantly), the Fisher's scream is almost never heard. Fishers are secretive, largely nocturnal and crepuscular, and exist at low population densities. A person could spend years in Fisher habitat without hearing the scream. The rarity amplifies the impact -- when you hear it, you remember it [1, 2, 3].
- **Terrifying:** The scream has been described as "blood-curdling," "almost human," "like a woman screaming in the woods," and "the most unsettling sound in the forest" [1, 2, 8]. It is high-pitched, sustained, and emotionally disturbing to human listeners. The acoustic properties trigger primate alarm responses -- the frequency and cadence resemble a human distress scream.
- **Context:** Produced during territorial disputes, mating season encounters, and aggressive interactions. Fisher screams are most commonly reported during the breeding season (March-April) and during territorial encounters between males [1, 2, 3]. The scream may also serve as a long-distance communication signal in dense forest where visual contact is impossible.

### The Paradox of E=5 and Rarity

The Fisher presents a unique acoustic paradox in the engine: **maximum energy, minimum frequency of occurrence**. The Mallard (E=5) quacks everywhere, all the time, in every park and pond. The Fisher (E=5) screams almost never, in the deepest forest, and most people who hear it do not know what they heard.

This is a new acoustic category: **power without prevalence**. Every previous E=5 element has been loud AND common. The Fisher introduces the concept of a vocalization that is loud AND rare -- a sound that is acoustically powerful but experientially scarce. The scream is the engine's rarest acoustic element by a wide margin.

### Additional Vocalizations

Beyond the scream, Fishers produce [1, 2, 3, 8]:
- **Low growling/hissing:** Close-range aggression, similar to other mustelids. Produced during defensive encounters and when cornered.
- **Clucking/chuckling:** Reported in some accounts as a contact call between mother and kits, and occasionally between mates during the breeding season.
- **Chittering:** Similar to but deeper than the Mink's chitter. Less prominent in the Fisher's vocal repertoire.

But the scream dominates the Fisher's acoustic identity. It is the sound that defines the species in human experience, the sound that generates folklore, the sound that hikers report and wildlife agencies field calls about.

But the Fisher's scream is louder, lower, and far more disturbing to human listeners.

## THE CONSERVATION STORY: EXTIRPATION AND REINTRODUCTION

### A Species Lost and Returned

The Fisher is the engine's **first reintroduced species** -- a species that was completely extirpated from Washington State and has been deliberately returned through conservation action [5, 6, 7, 9, 10].

### The Extirpation

Fishers were historically common throughout Washington's forested habitats, from the Olympics to the Cascades to the Blue Mountains [5, 6, 7]. They were eliminated through:

- **Trapping:** Fisher fur was commercially valuable -- less famous than mink fur but prized for its density and warmth. Unregulated trapping in the 19th and early 20th centuries decimated populations [1, 2, 5, 6].
- **Logging:** The removal of old-growth and dense forest eliminated Fisher habitat. Fishers need large trees for denning and closed canopy for travel. Industrial logging of Washington's forests in the late 19th and early 20th centuries destroyed the structural features Fishers require [5, 6, 7].
- **Predator persecution:** Like many carnivores, Fishers were actively killed as perceived threats to poultry and small livestock [1, 2, 5].

By the mid-20th century, Fishers were **functionally extinct in Washington State**. The last confirmed sighting of a native Washington Fisher was in the 1990s, and the population was considered non-viable long before that [5, 6, 7, 9].

### The Reintroduction

The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW), in partnership with the National Park Service, Conservation Northwest, and British Columbia's Ministry of Environment, executed one of the most ambitious carnivore reintroduction programs in the Pacific Northwest [5, 6, 7, 9, 10]:

**Olympic Peninsula (2008-2010):**
- 90 Fishers released in Olympic National Park over three years [9, 10].
- Source population: British Columbia (trapped and translocated).
- Goal: Establish a self-sustaining population in the Olympic Mountains.
- Status: Population established. Reproduction confirmed. Fishers have been documented breeding and expanding their range within the Olympic Peninsula [9, 10].

**South Cascades (2015-2020):**
- Approximately 80 Fishers released in the Gifford Pinchot National Forest and surrounding areas [9, 10].
- Source population: British Columbia (live-trapped and translocated).
- Goal: Establish a second self-sustaining population in the Cascade Range.
- Status: Population establishing. Reproduction confirmed. Range expansion documented [9, 10].

**North Cascades (2018-2020):**
- Additional releases in the North Cascades to connect the South Cascades population with British Columbia source populations [9, 10].
- Goal: Create genetic connectivity across the species' historical range.

### Conservation Significance

The Fisher reintroduction is significant for the engine on multiple levels:

1. **First reintroduced species.** No previous species in 66 degrees has been one that was lost and returned through human intervention. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0), the Northern Spotted Owl (degree 4), the Pileated Woodpecker (degree 54) -- all are present because they survived. The Fisher is present because humans brought it back.

2. **The fur trade connection.** Degrees 61-62 (Beaver) and 64 (Mink) documented species shaped by the fur trade. The Fisher was ELIMINATED by it. The fur trade thread that runs through the mammalian era reaches its most extreme expression here: a species trapped to local extinction.

3. **The dense-forest dependency.** The Fisher's extirpation was caused by BOTH trapping AND habitat loss. Even if trapping had stopped, the loss of dense forest would have prevented recovery. The Fisher's return is possible only because sufficient dense forest has regrown in the Olympics and Cascades to support a viable population [5, 6, 7].

4. **The cross-border connection.** Washington's Fishers come from British Columbia. The reintroduction required international cooperation -- Canadian Fishers transplanted to American forests. The Pacific Northwest does not end at the border, and neither does the Fisher's conservation story.

## THE PORCUPINE PREDATOR: SPECIALIZED KILLING TECHNIQUE

### Killing the Unkillable

The Fisher is one of the few predators in North America capable of regularly killing North American Porcupines (*Erethizon dorsatum*) [1, 2, 3, 5, 11]:

- **Technique:** The Fisher attacks the porcupine's face -- the only part of the body not protected by quills. The Fisher circles the porcupine, darting in to bite the face repeatedly, retreating when the porcupine turns. Over a period of 25-30 minutes, the Fisher inflicts enough facial wounds to exhaust and kill the porcupine [1, 2, 3, 5, 11].
- **Flipping:** Once the porcupine is weakened, the Fisher flips it onto its back, exposing the unprotected belly. The Fisher then feeds from the ventral side, avoiding the dorsal quills entirely [1, 2, 3, 11].
- **Quill tolerance:** Fishers regularly sustain quill injuries during porcupine attacks. Embedded quills are found in the skin and muscle of wild Fishers. The Fisher tolerates quills that would be fatal to most predators -- the quills encyst in the tissue rather than causing systemic infection [1, 2, 11].
- **Specialization:** Not all Fishers kill porcupines. The behavior appears to be learned, possibly culturally transmitted from mother to kits. Fishers in areas with high porcupine density develop the technique; Fishers in areas without porcupines do not [1, 2, 5, 11].

### Ecological Significance of Porcupine Predation

The Fisher-Porcupine relationship has cascading ecological effects [1, 2, 5, 11]:

- **Forest health:** Porcupines girdle trees by feeding on bark and cambium, causing tree mortality. In areas where Fishers have been reintroduced, porcupine populations decline and tree damage decreases. The Fisher is an indirect benefit to forest health [1, 2, 5].
- **Trophic cascade:** Fisher -> porcupine -> tree damage. Remove the Fisher, porcupine populations increase, tree mortality increases. This is a documented trophic cascade in North American forests and one of the justifications for Fisher reintroduction programs [5, 6, 7].
- **The Mink contrast:** The Mink (degree 64) was documented as a surplus killer -- killing more than it needs. The Fisher is a specialist killer -- developing a unique technique to kill prey that no other predator can take. Both represent unusual predatory strategies, but they are opposite in character: the Mink kills indiscriminately in prey-dense environments; the Fisher kills with precision against a heavily defended target.

## DIET AND ECOLOGY: THE FOREST GENERALIST

### Prey Spectrum

The Fisher is a forest-floor generalist predator with a broad diet [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Snowshoe hares:** The primary prey item in many populations. Fishers are effective hare predators, using their speed and agility on the forest floor to run down and kill hares in dense vegetation [1, 2, 5].
- **Squirrels:** Both tree squirrels and ground squirrels. The Fisher can pursue tree squirrels through the canopy but more commonly catches them on the ground [1, 2, 5].
- **Porcupines:** The specialist prey item. Fishers are one of the few predators capable of regularly taking adult porcupines (see above) [1, 2, 3, 5, 11].
- **Small mammals:** Mice, voles, shrews. The opportunistic base of the diet, taken when encountered [1, 2, 5].
- **Birds:** Ground-nesting birds, grouse, and occasionally small owls. The Fisher is a nest predator in forested habitats [1, 2, 5].
- **Carrion:** Fishers scavenge readily, particularly in winter when fresh prey is scarce [1, 2, 5].
- **Fruit and fungi:** Seasonal consumption of berries, fruits, and mushrooms documented. The Fisher is more omnivorous than its reputation suggests [1, 2, 5].

### Comparison to Quartet Members

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Primary prey | Fish, crayfish | Muskrats, frogs | Voles, squirrels | Hares, porcupines |

The Fisher is the most omnivorous mustelid in the quartet and the only one with a specialist killing technique for a single prey species. It represents the endpoint of the quartet's ecological gradient: from aquatic specialist (Otter) through interface generalist (Mink) through arboreal specialist (Marten) to forest-floor generalist with specialist capability (Fisher).

## BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY: SOLITARY FOREST GHOST

### Home Range and Movement

Fishers maintain large home ranges in dense forest [1, 2, 5, 6]:

- **Males:** 30-80 km2 in western forest habitats. Males range widely, overlapping with multiple female territories [1, 2, 5].
- **Females:** 15-35 km2. Smaller than males, more closely tied to denning areas during the reproductive season [1, 2, 5].
- **Movement patterns:** Primarily nocturnal and crepuscular. Fishers travel on the forest floor using game trails, downed logs, and streamside corridors. They can cover 10-20 km in a single night's foraging bout [1, 2, 5].
- **Denning:** Reproductive females den in cavities in large trees, snags, or broken-top trees. Den trees are typically >50 cm DBH and must have natural cavities large enough to accommodate a female and her kits. The availability of den trees is often the limiting factor for Fisher populations [1, 2, 5, 6, 7].

### Reproduction

- **Breeding season:** March-April. Males seek out females for brief mating associations [1, 2, 5].
- **Delayed implantation:** Like most mustelids, the Fisher exhibits delayed implantation -- the fertilized egg does not implant in the uterine wall for approximately 10 months. Total gestation (including delay) is approximately 11-12 months, but active gestation is only 30-50 days [1, 2, 5].
- **Litter size:** Typically 1-4 kits, born in March-April of the following year [1, 2, 5].
- **Maternal care:** The female raises kits alone. Kits remain with the mother for approximately 5-6 months before dispersal [1, 2, 5].

### Predators and Mortality

- **Predators:** Great Horned Owl, Bald Eagle, Bobcat, Coyote, and Cougar are documented predators of Fishers, particularly juveniles and females [1, 2, 5].
- **Vehicle strike:** An increasing cause of mortality in reintroduced populations, as Fishers cross roads during nocturnal foraging [5, 6, 7].
- **Trapping:** Historically the primary cause of mortality. Now illegal in Washington State [5, 6, 7].

## STRUCTURAL EVENTS AT DEGREE 66

### Event 1: The Mustelidae Quartet (63-64-65-66)

Four consecutive Mustelidae. The engine has never gone deeper into a single family. Previous maximum was two consecutive entries from the same family (Anatidae 58-59, Castoridae 61-62, Mustelidae 63-64). The quartet doubles the previous record. It traces a complete habitat gradient from river to deep forest and documents four distinct ecological strategies within a single family.

### Event 2: E=5 -- Loud and Rare

Second E=5 species-side entry. But unlike the Mallard's ubiquitous E=5 quack, the Fisher's E=5 scream is the RAREST acoustic element in the engine. Power without prevalence. A new acoustic category: maximum volume, minimum frequency.

### Event 3: First Reintroduced Species

No previous species in 66 entries was extirpated from Washington and returned through conservation action. The Fisher introduces the concept of managed return -- a species that exists in the engine's geography only because humans chose to bring it back.

### Event 4: Fourth Carnivore

Carnivora now has four entries, all Mustelidae. The order's representation quadruples across the quartet. Carnivora is becoming one of the engine's most documented orders through the Mustelidae quartet alone.

### Event 5: Fifth Mammalian Species (Seventh Mammalian Entry)

Mammals continue to accumulate. Seven entries, five species, two orders, three families (Castoridae, Mustelidae x4, and the Beaver's Castoridae). Mammalian representation rises to 10.4% (7 of 67 entries). Still overwhelmingly avian, but the mammalian era (degrees 61-66) is now six degrees deep.

### Event 6: Sixth Consecutive Original CSV Species

Degrees 61-66: six consecutive original CSV species. The longest original-CSV streak in engine history. Every mammalian entry has been an original CSV species.

### Event 7: The Porcupine Predator

The engine's first documentation of specialized predation technique. The Mink documented surplus killing (excess predation). The Fisher documents specialist predation (unique technique for armored prey). Two different innovations in predatory strategy from back-to-back family members.

## THE NORTHERN SPOTTED OWL CONNECTION (SPS-004)

Both the Fisher and the Northern Spotted Owl are dense-forest-dependent species with conservation stories [1, 2, 5, 6, 7]:

- **Habitat overlap:** Both require dense, closed-canopy forest with large trees. Both are negatively affected by logging and forest fragmentation. In the Olympic Peninsula and Cascades, Fisher and Spotted Owl territories overlap significantly [5, 6].
- **Conservation parallel:** The Spotted Owl became the poster species for old-growth conservation in the Pacific Northwest in the 1990s, leading to the Northwest Forest Plan. The Fisher's extirpation and reintroduction represents a newer chapter in the same conservation story -- forest-dependent predators lost to habitat destruction and human persecution.
- **Acoustic parallel:** The Spotted Owl's four-note hoot (degree 4) is a haunting nocturnal sound from dense forest. The Fisher's scream is a terrifying nocturnal sound from dense forest. Both sounds are rare, both emerge from darkness under closed canopy, and both trigger strong emotional responses in human listeners.

The connection spans 62 degrees (4 to 66) -- the longest cross-reference span with this degree of ecological and conservation overlap.

## THE PILEATED WOODPECKER CONNECTION (SPS-054)

Both species need large trees in mature forest [1, 2, 5]:

- **Large-tree dependency:** The Pileated Woodpecker excavates nest and roost cavities in large-diameter snags. Fishers use natural cavities in large trees for denning. Both species are limited by the availability of large-diameter trees -- remove the big trees, and both species lose critical habitat.
- **Cavity ecosystem:** The Pileated Woodpecker creates cavities that other species use. Fisher females den in natural cavities, but in some areas they may use abandoned Pileated Woodpecker cavities. The woodpecker as cavity architect, the Fisher as cavity tenant.
- **Dense-forest associates:** Both prefer forest with high canopy closure and structural complexity. Both are present in the same forests of the Olympics and Cascades.
