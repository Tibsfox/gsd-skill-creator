## Purpose

This is the fifty-seventh release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-56), the entry of Columbidae as the 23rd family and Columbiformes as the 6th order -- the first pigeon or dove in the engine's entire history, the first new order since Apodiformes at degree 35, the species that opens the bass register and drops the engine's frequency floor by an octave. *Patagioenas fasciata* -- "banded clattering-pigeon," the largest native pigeon in western North America, the one that coos from the canopy of mature mixed forest in a deep, soft, owl-like voice that first-time listeners mistake for an owl, the pigeon with the white crescent on its nape and the iridescent bronze-green hindneck, the one that was hunted nearly to the Passenger Pigeon's fate and saved by the laws that the Passenger Pigeon's death created, the pigeon that lays a single egg and trusts the forest to be there when the squab fledges, the pigeon that congregates at mineral springs to neutralize the tannins in its acorn-heavy diet, the only native pigeon in most PNW forests -- enters the engine as the bass voice of the canopy. The release carries six structural contributions:

1. **COLUMBIDAE ENTERS ENGINE -- 23rd family.** The first pigeon or dove. Columbidae is a cosmopolitan family of approximately 350 species. The engine waited 56 degrees for its first one. The family brings a fundamentally new acoustic signature (cooing), a new body plan (large-bodied frugivore at 240-390 g), and a new ecological niche (canopy frugivore and seed disperser).

2. **COLUMBIFORMES -- 6th order.** The first new order since Apodiformes at degree 35 -- a 21-degree gap without a new order, now broken. Passeriformes interrupted for the second time in three degrees (54 Piciformes, 55 Passeriformes, 56 Columbiformes). The Passeriformes lock is loosening.

3. **The forest coo -- the 57th acoustic element (species side).** A deep, soft, owl-like cooing in the 400-600 Hz fundamental range. The lowest-frequency vocalization in the engine by a wide margin. A vocalization type (the coo) that has no precedent in the engine's 56-degree history.

4. **E=1 rises to 5 entries (8.8%).** The pigeon is E=1 via a different mechanism than previous E=1 species: large body + inherently soft voice type, not small body + thin high-pitched sound.

5. **The Passenger Pigeon's shadow.** Conservation legacy of the most famous bird extinction. Overhunted, crashed, saved by the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. Slow recovery due to single-egg clutch. The ghost of *Ectopistes* enters the engine as context.

6. **CSV substitution documented.** Ensatina replaced by Band-tailed Pigeon. Twelfth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (45-56). The amphibian belt continues through the plethodontid heartland.

## What Worked

### 1. COLUMBIDAE is a structurally massive entry
New family. New order. New vocalization type. New frequency register. New body plan. New ecological niche. The Band-tailed Pigeon is the most structurally significant entry since the Great Blue Heron at degree 0 in terms of sheer novelty across multiple dimensions.

### 2. The bass register was genuinely missing
The engine operated almost entirely in the 1,500-10,000 Hz passerine register for 56 degrees. The pigeon's 400-600 Hz fundamental drops the floor by an octave. The acoustic palette is suddenly richer. The absence of bass was unnoticed until it was filled.

### 3. E=1 via a different path is a genuine insight
Previous E=1 species (kinglet, bushtit, siskin, creeper) are tiny birds with thin, high sounds. The pigeon is E=1 because cooing is inherently soft -- not because the bird is small. Large body, soft voice. A new acoustic pathway to the quietest energy level. The engine documents both paths to quietness.

### 4. The Passenger Pigeon connection adds historical depth
The conservation story is powerful: a species that nearly followed the most famous bird extinction in North American history, saved by the laws that extinction inspired. The single-egg clutch that makes recovery slow. The mineral spring congregations that made the species easy to hunt. The parallel is specific and well-documented.

### 5. Maximum contrast with degree 55
The yard song (degree 55, Bewick's Wren) and the forest coo (degree 56, Band-tailed Pigeon) are maximally different in every measurable dimension: frequency, energy, mechanism, habitat, body mass, order, family. This is the sharpest degree-to-degree contrast in recent engine history.

### 6. First new order in 21 degrees
Columbiformes breaks the order drought that has persisted since Apodiformes at degree 35. The engine now has six orders. The Passeriformes lock is loosening, with two non-passerine orders in three degrees.

### 7. The "only native pigeon" framing is effective
Most people associate "pigeon" with the urban Rock Pigeon. The Band-tailed Pigeon redefines the word for the PNW: this is what a pigeon looks and sounds like when it belongs to the forest, not the city. The perceptual shift is immediate and striking.

And on one of those trunks -- near the base, where moss meets bark -- a tiny brown shape begins to move. Upward. Spiraling. So small it might be a piece of bark peeling in the wind. So brown it might be shadow. So slow it might not be moving at all. But it is. A thin, decurved bill probes a furrow. A stiff tail braces against the trunk. Curved claws grip the bark like fingers on a cliff face. The bird spirals upward, probing every crack, reading the bark like braille. And from somewhere -- so high-pitched you might not hear it, so thin it might be the wind, so quiet it might be nothing at all -- a sound. See. See. See. See. Descending. A tiny spiral of notes unwinding from the top of the frequency range down into the forest like the bird unwinding from the top of the trunk down to the base of the next tree. Certhia americana. The Aristotle-bird of America. The only treecreeper in the New World. The bark-spiral. And now -- the bark gives way to water. The old-growth trunk stands at the edge of a mountain stream where the current runs fast over smooth stones and the spray rises in the air and the sound of rushing water fills the canyon. On a granite boulder in the middle of the stream, a compact gray bird stands. It bobs. Up. Down. Up. Down. Forty times a minute, sixty times a minute, the endless curtsey. It flashes a white eyelid. It dips. It dips again. And then it walks forward off the rock and into the water. The current takes it. It does not fight the current -- it dives through it, wings tucked, body angled down, entering the torrent headfirst. It hits the stream bottom and walks. Walks on the bottom of a mountain stream, gripping stones with curved claws, flipping pebbles with its bill, pulling caddisfly larvae from under the rocks. It surfaces. It pops back onto the boulder. It shakes. And it opens its bill and sings. Loud. Complex. Bubbling. Sustained. A torrent of notes over a torrent of water. The sound carries above the rush, above the falls, above the spray. It carries up the canyon and down the watershed. It carries to where the stream becomes a river becomes an estuary becomes the Sound. Cinclus mexicanus. The tail-wagging waterside bird of Mexico. John Muir's favorite. The only aquatic songbird in North America. The torrent-song. And now -- the water gives way to the canopy. Up from the stream, up through the old-growth, up into the dense crown of a Douglas fir where the branches interlock and the light is green and the air smells of resin. A flash of blue. A sound like nothing else in the forest -- harsh, loud, commanding. SHACK-SHACK-SHACK-SHACK-SHACK. The entire forest stops. Every chickadee freezes. Every nuthatch halts mid-bark. Every creeper presses flat against the trunk. Something has arrived that demands attention. And then silence. And then -- a Red-tailed Hawk scream. Piercing. Authentic. Terrifying. Every small bird dives for cover. But there is no hawk. There is a jay. A large, crested, blue-and-black jay sitting at the top of the Douglas fir, crest erect, dark eyes scanning, having just screamed a perfect hawk imitation into the forest and watched the entire biotic community duck. It cocks its head. It screams again -- shack-shack-shack -- and then drops from the canopy, blue wings flashing, and lands on a picnic table at the trailhead. It steals a peanut. It screams once more. It vanishes into the conifers. Cyanocitta stelleri. Steller's dark-blue jay. The bird that proved America. The first corvid. The camp robber. The hawk mimic. The forest's conductor. The corvid alarm. And now -- the canopy opens to a shaft of light. On a red alder trunk beside the stream, halfway up, a movement. Not a spiral like the creeper. Not a hop like the nuthatch. A deliberate, measured, vertical hitch. A medium-sized bird clings to the bark, bracing with stiff tail feathers, and leans back. Its entire head is red. Not a patch. Not a stripe. Not a cap. The ENTIRE head -- crown, nape, face, throat, upper breast -- is a deep, rich, unbroken crimson. Below the red: yellow. Behind: black and white bars. The most colorful woodpecker in the forest. It leans forward and taps. Not a hammer blow. Not a drum roll. A careful, precise tap into the living bark. It pauses. It moves one centimeter to the right. Taps again. A row of tiny holes opens across the alder trunk -- neat, evenly spaced, systematic. Sap beads at the edges of each hole, catching the light like amber. The bird leans in with a brush-tipped tongue and licks. Behind it, a Rufous Hummingbird hovers, waiting for its turn at the wells. The bird drums on a dead branch overhead -- and the rhythm is wrong. Not steady. Not metronomic. Fast, then slow, then fast again. A stuttering, lurching, jazz-drummer rhythm that refuses to find the beat. And from its throat: not a scream, not a song, but a soft, nasal, descending mew. Like a kitten lost in the forest. Sphyrapicus ruber. The red hammer-woodpecker. The sap-well architect. The sap-well rhythm. And now -- the forest edge. Where the old-growth gives way to brush, where the alder thins to blackberry and the blackberry gives way to hedgerow and the hedgerow borders a garden and the garden has a fence and the fence has a post. On top of that post -- a small brown bird. Long tail cocked upward, flicking side to side. A bold white stripe over the eye like a brushstroke. It has been watching, investigating, probing every crevice in the fence rail, every gap in the brush pile, every crack in the garden shed. It has been here all along -- the yard bird, the suburban wren, the one that stayed when the forest was cut and the houses went up and the gardens were planted and the hedgerows grew in. It opens its bill. The song is loud. The song is melodious. The song is variable -- a string of short whistles, a buzzy warble, a sharp trill, a flourish. Not the cascade of the forest wren at degree 1 -- not 100 notes in five seconds, not an acoustic miracle compressed into eight grams. Something different. Something learned. The bird heard this song from the male on the next territory over. That male heard it from his neighbor. That neighbor heard it from the male who was here before him. The song is a tradition. It changes as it crosses the landscape -- Seattle songs drifting into Tacoma songs drifting into Olympia songs, the way folk songs drift from county to county. Nine song types. Twelve. Twenty-two. A repertoire built not from instinct but from listening. The bird switches to a new type. Whistles. Trills. Buzzes. Finishes with a sharp note. Switches again. The fence post becomes a stage. The garden becomes a concert hall. The yard becomes the world. Thryomanes bewickii. The reed-frenzy bird of Bewick. The yard song. And now -- back into the forest. Past the hedgerow, past the garden, past the brushy edge, back under the canopy of mature Douglas fir and western hemlock and bigleaf maple where the trunks rise tall and the branches interlock a hundred feet overhead. And from somewhere up there, from inside the canopy where you cannot see, a sound. Deep. Soft. Low. Resonant. A sound like a distant owl. A sound like wind through a hollow trunk. A sound like nothing else in the engine. Whooo-whooo. Two notes. Slow. Measured. Breathy. The lowest frequency the engine has ever heard -- 400 hertz, 500 hertz, down in the bass register where no songbird sings. A large gray bird sits on a branch in the canopy. White crescent on the nape. Iridescent bronze-green hindneck catching a shaft of light. Yellow bill. Gold eyes. Two hundred and fifty grams of pigeon -- the largest bird in the engine since the heron at degree 0. It is not a city pigeon. It is not an escapee. It is not lost. It is the native pigeon -- the only one, the forest one, the one that was here before the Rock Pigeons colonized the bridges and the Eurasian Collared-Doves spread through the towns. It coos again. Whooo-whooo. The bass voice of the canopy. The pedal point beneath the songbird chorus. The ghost of the Passenger Pigeon, alive in the forest, cooing softly into the morning. Patagioenas fasciata. The banded clattering-pigeon. The forest coo.*
