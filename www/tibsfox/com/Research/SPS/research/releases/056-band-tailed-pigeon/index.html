Band-tailed Pigeon

Patagioenas fasciata

CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Ensatina (Ensatina eschscholtzii) at degree 56. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Band-tailed Pigeon replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s moist forest habitat and E=1 quiet energy. The Ensatina is a lungless salamander of the moist forest floor — secretive, with a soft squeak if handled (E=1). The Band-tailed Pigeon occupies the canopy of the same mature mixed forests with a deep, soft, owl-like cooing (E=1). Both are year-round residents. This is the twelfth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (Pacific Chorus Frog at 45, Columbia Spotted Frog at 46, Red-legged Frog at 47, Tailed Frog at 48, Western Toad at 49, Rough-skinned Newt at 50, Northwestern Salamander at 51, Long-toed Salamander at 52, Olympic Torrent Salamander at 53, Clouded Salamander at 54, Oregon Slender Salamander at 55, Ensatina at 56) — the engine continues through the plethodontid heartland of the original CSV’s amphibian belt.

COLUMBIDAE ENTERS ENGINE — 23rd Family, 6th Order

The Band-tailed Pigeon brings Columbidae (pigeons and doves) into the engine as the 23rd family and the first representative of order Columbiformes — the engine’s 6th order. First new order since Apodiformes at degree 35 — a 21-degree gap without a new order, now broken. No pigeon. No dove. No cooing. For 56 degrees, every sound in the engine was produced by a passerine, hummingbird, owl, woodpecker, or heron. The engine has never heard a coo — until now. Passeriformes interrupted AGAIN — second time in three degrees (54 Piciformes, 55 Passeriformes, 56 Columbiformes). The Passeriformes lock is loosening.

The 57th Acoustic Element: The Forest Coo

A deep, soft, owl-like cooing in the 400–600 Hz fundamental range, resonating from inside the canopy of mature mixed forest — the lowest-frequency vocalization in the engine by a wide margin. Named “the forest coo” for three resonances: (1) the habitat — this pigeon coos from inside the forest canopy, not from a wire or rooftop; (2) the acoustic novelty — a coo is a fundamentally new vocalization type. Not a song, not a call, not a drum. The Columbidae signature; (3) the frequency register — the engine’s acoustic palette now extends into the bass register, spanning over four octaves from 400 Hz (pigeon) to 10,000+ Hz (kinglet).

The forest coo communicates through bass-register resonance rather than melodic complexity or volume. A large bird, softly cooing. The pedal point beneath the songbird chorus.

E=1 Via a Different Path: Large Bird, Soft Voice

The Band-tailed Pigeon is E=1 — the same energy level as the Golden-crowned Kinglet (degree 8), Bushtit (degree 10), Pine Siskin (degree 12), and Brown Creeper (degree 51). But the pigeon reaches E=1 by an entirely different mechanism. Previous E=1 species are tiny birds (5–18 g) whose soft sounds exist at the edge of perception because of small body mass and high-pitched vocalizations. The Band-tailed Pigeon is a large bird (240–390 g) whose soft sound exists because cooing is inherently a soft vocalization. Quiet not because it is small, but because its voice type is inherently quiet. E=1 rises from 4 to 5 entries (8.8%).

The Ghost of the Passenger Pigeon

The Band-tailed Pigeon carries the conservation legacy of the most famous bird extinction in North American history. The Passenger Pigeon (Ectopistes migratorius) went from billions to zero between the 1870s and 1914. The Band-tailed Pigeon followed the same trajectory — abundant, heavily hunted, population crashing — but was saved by the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, the direct legislative response to the Passenger Pigeon’s extinction. Recovery has been slow: only 1 egg per clutch, 1–2 broods per year. The single-egg strategy is both the pigeon’s evolutionary gamble and its Achilles’ heel.

Conservation: Declining Populations, Ongoing Concern

Despite over a century of legal protection, Band-tailed Pigeon populations remain below historical levels. Breeding Bird Survey data shows long-term declines in parts of the Pacific Northwest and California. The species is designated a game bird of conservation concern by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. Threats include habitat loss (logging of mature forest), continued regulated hunting, climate change effects on mast crops, and competition with invasive Eurasian Collared-Dove. Washington State monitors populations through harvest surveys and mineral spring counts.

The Banded Clattering-Pigeon

Patagioenas fasciata — from Ancient Greek patageo (“to clatter, to crash, to make a noise”) + oinas (“pigeon, dove”) + Latin fasciata (“banded,” from fascia = band, stripe). Full translation: “Banded clattering-pigeon.” Named by Thomas Say in 1823. Genus Patagioenas elevated by Reichenbach (1853), containing approximately 17 New World pigeon species. The genus was formerly placed within Columba (Old World pigeons) but split based on molecular phylogenetics. Compare the Bewick’s Wren’s Thryomanes bewickii (“Bewick’s reed-frenzy bird”) at degree 55: the reed-frenzy bird burns with restless energy; the clattering-pigeon announces itself with wingclaps and settles into a soft, deep coo.

The Sound: Deep, Soft, Owl-Like Cooing

Primary coo: A low, resonant “whooo-whooo” or “coo-coo-coo” — two to three deep, breathy notes in a slow, measured cadence from the canopy of mature forest. Frequency ~300–800 Hz, fundamental 400–600 Hz. Duration ~0.5–1.0 seconds per note. Quality: deep, soft, hollow, resonant, breathy. Woodwind quality — closer to a blown bottle or soft bassoon than to passerine song. First-time hearers frequently mistake it for an owl.

Vocalizations are innate — no cultural learning, no geographic dialects. Dramatically simpler repertoire than any passerine in the engine. The pigeon has one coo type. It repeats it. That is enough.

Plumage: The White Crescent and the Iridescent Nape

White nape crescent — a narrow white band crossing the back of the neck, the primary field mark. Below it, iridescent bronze-green to bronze-purple structural coloration that shifts with the light. Blue-gray to purple-gray overall, with a wine-pink wash on the breast. Yellow bill with dark tip — brighter in breeding condition. Pale gray terminal tail band — the “band” of Band-tailed Pigeon, visible in flight. Pale yellow to gold eyes with narrow red orbital ring. Large: 33–40 cm, 240–390 g — roughly 25–35 times heavier than the wrens at degrees 1 and 55. The largest body mass of any species in the engine since the Great Blue Heron at degree 0.

Habitat: The Canopy Pigeon of Mature Forest

Breeds in mature mixed conifer and hardwood forests: Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, bigleaf maple, red alder, Garry oak. Feeds on acorns, berries, seeds, and buds in the canopy. Strong affinity for mineral springs and salt licks — congregations of hundreds at known springs in western Washington. The only native pigeon in most PNW forests — not the urban Rock Pigeon, not the invasive Eurasian Collared-Dove. Year-round in western Washington with some altitudinal migration.

Nesting: The Single-Egg Strategy

Flimsy twig platform on a horizontal branch in the canopy. One egg per clutch — the lowest clutch size in the engine. 18–20 day incubation (shared by both parents). 25–30 day nestling period. Both parents feed crop milk (pigeon milk) for the first week — a trait shared only with flamingos and some penguins. 1–2 broods per season. The single-egg clutch is the species’ Achilles’ heel: slow reproduction means slow recovery from population declines.

Theory Nodes

F

The Forest Coo foundational

The 57th acoustic element. Deep, soft, owl-like cooing at 400–600 Hz fundamental — the lowest frequency in the engine. A fundamentally new vocalization type: the coo. The Columbidae signature. Opens the bass register. Music-theory parallel: the pedal point — a sustained bass note held beneath changing harmonies above, anchoring the music to a tonal center.

C

Columbidae Enters — 23rd Family, 6th Order foundational

First pigeon/dove in 56 degrees. New order Columbiformes. First new order since Apodiformes at degree 35. Brings cooing vocalizations, crop milk, large-bodied frugivore ecology, single-egg clutches. Music-theory parallel: the new instrument — an instrument silent for the entire piece suddenly enters, both surprising and inevitable.

P

The Passenger Pigeon’s Shadow foundational

The Band-tailed Pigeon carries the legacy of the most famous bird extinction. Overhunted, crashed, saved by laws that the Passenger Pigeon’s death created. Slow recovery — single-egg clutch. The ghost of Ectopistes haunts every Patagioenas. Music-theory parallel: the elegy — a composition expressing grief for the dead. The forest coo as memorial.

E

E=1 Via a Different Path foundational

Previous E=1 species are tiny birds with high-thin sounds. The pigeon is E=1 because cooing is inherently soft — a large bird, quietly cooing. Violin pianissimo (previous E=1) vs. tuba pianissimo (this E=1). Same dynamic level, different timbral universe. Music-theory parallel: pianissimo by different means.

N

The Only Native Pigeon intermediate

In a landscape dominated by introduced Rock Pigeon and invasive Eurasian Collared-Dove, the Band-tailed Pigeon is the only native pigeon in PNW forests. The forest pigeon. The one that was here before the cities. Music-theory parallel: the authentic voice — a tradition performed in its native context, not transplanted.

M

Mineral Spring Congregations intermediate

Strong affinity for mineral springs and salt licks. Hundreds congregate at known springs. Mineral intake helps neutralize plant toxins in frugivorous diet. No other species in the engine shows this behavior. Music-theory parallel: the gathering — musicians converge at traditional sites to play together.

Cross-References

SPS-055: Bewick’s Wren

CONSECUTIVE DEGREES, MAXIMUM CONTRAST. The yard song (degree 55, E=3, 3,000–6,000 Hz, learned, suburban) yields to the forest coo (degree 56, E=1, 400–600 Hz, innate, canopy). Every dimension reversed. Maximum acoustic contrast in a single degree step.

SPS-004: Northern Spotted Owl

TWO LOW-FREQUENCY FOREST VOICES. The owl (~500–800 Hz, E=2, nocturnal hoot) and the pigeon (400–600 Hz, E=1, diurnal coo) are the engine’s only entries below 1,000 Hz. The owl commands. The pigeon murmurs. Day coo and night hoot.

SPS-001: Pacific Wren

SAME FOREST, OPPOSITE FREQUENCIES. The wren (3,000–8,000 Hz, E=2, cascade from understory) and the pigeon (400–600 Hz, E=1, coo from canopy) occupy the same forest at opposite frequency extremes. Treble and bass in the same cathedral.

SPS-051: Brown Creeper

TWO E=1 FOREST SPECIES. The creeper (E=1, 6,000–10,000 Hz, high-thin descent) and the pigeon (E=1, 400–600 Hz, deep coo). Both E=1, both in mature forest, opposite frequency extremes. Two paths to E=1.

SPS-008: Golden-crowned Kinglet

FREQUENCY EXTREMES OF THE ENGINE. The kinglet (7,000–10,000+ Hz, highest, E=1) and the pigeon (400–600 Hz, lowest, E=1). Both E=1. More than four octaves apart. The full span of the engine’s acoustic palette.

SPS-054: Red-breasted Sapsucker

MATURE FOREST GUILD. The sapsucker drills sap wells in living trees. The pigeon feeds on acorns and fruit in the canopy. Both depend on forests that have had time to grow. Part of the mature-forest species guild.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Band-tailed Pigeon documented across western Washington and British Columbia. Year-round resident of mature mixed forests. The only native pigeon in most PNW forests. Concentrated near food sources and mineral springs.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Mature forest ecology. Frugivore and seed disperser role. Mineral spring congregations. Conservation history linked to Passenger Pigeon extinction. Single-egg reproductive strategy and slow population recovery.
