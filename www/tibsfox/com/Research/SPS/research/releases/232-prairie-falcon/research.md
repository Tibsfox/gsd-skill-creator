## FALCO MEXICANUS -- THE PRAIRIE FALCON

### Taxonomy and the Species Epithet

- **The name.** *Falco mexicanus* -- the Mexican falcon. The species epithet *mexicanus* reflects the geographic range of the type specimen collection, not the bird's primary habitat -- the Prairie Falcon is far more abundant in the western United States than in Mexico. The common name "Prairie Falcon" is more accurate: this bird belongs to the open prairies, basin-and-range grasslands, and canyon country of western North America.
- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Falconiformes. Family: Falconidae. Genus: *Falco*. Species: *mexicanus*. Monotypic (no recognized subspecies). The Prairie Falcon is a North American endemic -- it does not breed in Europe, Asia, or South America. This makes it unique among the large falcons.
- **North American endemic.** Unlike the Peregrine Falcon (231), which breeds on every continent except Antarctica, the Prairie Falcon is restricted to North America. Its range extends from southern British Columbia and Alberta south through the Great Plains and Great Basin to northern Mexico. The PNW's eastern shrub-steppe and canyon country (Columbia Plateau, Snake River Plain margins, eastern Oregon canyon systems) represents the northern and western edge of the breeding range.
- **The genus Falco in the engine.** Degrees 231-232 document two *Falco* species in the engine: the cosmopolitan Peregrine (*F. peregrinus*) and the North American endemic Prairie Falcon (*F. mexicanus*). Together they bracket the genus's ecological range in the PNW: cliff and city (Peregrine) vs. canyon and arid plateau (Prairie Falcon).

## THE ALARM CALL -- kik-ki-ki-ki-ki, E=6

### Vocalization Analysis

The Prairie Falcon's alarm call is the Falconidae family's second vocal profile in the engine:

- **The sound.** The primary alarm call is a sharp, rapid series of kik notes: kik-ki-ki-ki-ki or kik-kik-kik-kik-kik. The notes are sharper and more clipped than the Peregrine's kak-kak-kak-kak -- shorter attack, faster decay per note. The effect is more staccato and mechanical. The call is delivered in bursts of 5-15+ notes, often repeated multiple times in quick succession.
- **Frequency.** The kik call operates in approximately 2,500-7,000 Hz -- similar to the Peregrine's alarm call (3,000-8,000 Hz) but slightly lower, consistent with the Prairie Falcon's slightly smaller size. The call character is clearly Falconidae -- staccato, rapid, high-pitched.
- **E=6 calibration.** The Prairie Falcon's call is slightly less forceful than the Peregrine's E=7 -- appropriate for the species' generally slightly smaller size and less extreme ecological role. The Peregrine is the speed record holder; the Prairie Falcon is the open-country specialist without the same performance ceiling.
- **Comparison to Peregrine (231).** The two Falconidae in the engine (231-232) have clearly related call structures: both are rapid staccato series of high-pitched notes, both are alarm calls with similar functions. The difference is in the individual note character -- kak vs. kik, with the kik being sharper and more staccato. This is the sonic fingerprint of being the same genus with a slightly different body size and hunting context.
- **Nest defense.** Prairie Falcons are extremely aggressive nest defenders. They will actively attack intruders at nest sites -- including human climbers, ravens, eagles, and Red-tailed Hawks. The alarm call accompanies these defensive stoop attacks, which are directed at the head and shoulders of intruders.

## ECOLOGY -- CANYON AND ARID CLIFF

### The Dry-Country Falcon

The Prairie Falcon is the arid-country counterpart to the Peregrine's cliff/city ecology:

- **Nesting requirement.** Like the Peregrine, the Prairie Falcon nests on cliff ledges -- the shared Falconidae nesting requirement of a high ledge with an open hunt radius. But the Prairie Falcon is restricted to natural cliff sites: basalt canyon walls, granite outcrops, sandstone escarpments. The species does not colonize urban buildings, distinguishing it ecologically and behaviorally from the Peregrine.
- **Basalt canyon habitat.** The Columbia Plateau's basalt canyon systems are prime Prairie Falcon habitat. The Snake River Canyon, the Deschutes River Canyon, the John Day Canyon system in eastern Oregon, and the lesser canyons of the Columbia Basin all provide the combination of cliff nesting sites and open terrain hunting required by the species.
- **Open terrain hunting.** Prairie Falcons hunt primarily by horizontal pursuit over open terrain -- flying fast and low over grassland, agricultural fields, and shrub-steppe, pursuing ground squirrels, horned larks, meadowlarks, and other open-country species. This is different from the Peregrine's primary technique (high-altitude stoop at airborne prey) -- the Prairie Falcon is more of a low-altitude pursuit hunter in flat terrain.
- **Prey base.** Ground squirrels (*Spermophilus*, *Urocitellus*) are the primary summer prey, especially important for feeding young. Horned larks, meadowlarks, starlings, and other open-country birds are taken during the non-breeding season. The Prairie Falcon's prey flexibility allows it to exploit seasonal abundance.
- **Altitudinal migration.** Some Prairie Falcons in the PNW undertake short-distance altitudinal migrations -- moving to lower elevation valley floors and agricultural landscapes in winter when canyon-top prey availability declines. Others remain year-round on the breeding canyon system if prey is adequate.

(Sources: Prairie Falcon canyon nesting; Columbia Plateau basalt; Snake River Canyon; Deschutes Canyon; John Day Canyon; open-terrain pursuit hunting; ground squirrel prey; horned lark prey; altitudinal migration data)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION

### Eastern PNW Canyon Country

The Prairie Falcon's PNW distribution follows the eastern shrub-steppe and canyon country:

- **Columbia Plateau.** The Columbia Plateau &mdash; the high basalt plateau covering much of central and eastern Washington and Oregon &mdash; is the Prairie Falcon's primary PNW habitat. The plateau's canyon systems (coulee lakes, river canyons carved into the basalt) provide nesting cliffs, and the plateau's open shrub-steppe and agricultural grasslands provide hunting terrain.
- **Key nesting areas.** Known Prairie Falcon nesting areas in the PNW include: the Channeled Scablands of eastern Washington, the Yakima Canyon, the Wenatchee area basalt cliffs, the Deschutes River Canyon in Oregon, and the Snake River Canyon along the Oregon-Idaho border. Many nest sites are located on protected federal lands (Bureau of Land Management, Columbia National Wildlife Refuge).
- **Western PNW.** Prairie Falcons are rarely encountered west of the Cascades. They are not adapted to the marine-influenced forest landscape of western Washington and Oregon. Unlike the Rough-legged Hawk (230), which regularly crosses the Cascades in winter, Prairie Falcons remain in the dry eastern landscapes year-round.
- **Population density.** Population estimates for the PNW are modest: tens to hundreds of breeding pairs in Washington, similar numbers in Oregon. The Prairie Falcon is never abundant -- it requires large territories with adequate cliff nesting sites and extensive open hunting terrain.

(Sources: Columbia Plateau distribution; Channeled Scablands; Yakima Canyon; Wenatchee area; Deschutes Canyon; Snake River Canyon; BLM nest site protection; western PNW absence; population density estimates)

## MORPHOLOGY AND FIELD IDENTIFICATION

### The Brown Open-Country Falcon

- **Overall appearance.** Adults: sandy brown above, pale buff below with brown streaking. The underparts show a pattern of dark streaking and spotting from throat to belly. The "mustache" mark (malar stripe) is less bold than the Peregrine's dark helmet-like facial pattern -- more subtle, brown rather than black-gray. The dark axillary patch (dark area on the underwing near the body) is the Prairie Falcon's key field mark in flight.
- **The axillary patch.** In flight from below, the dark axillary patch (sometimes called the "armpit" or underwing coverts) creates a distinctive dark comma shape against the pale underwing. This mark is visible at considerable distance and is the most reliable field mark for separating Prairie Falcon from other similar-sized raptors in flight.
- **Size.** Medium-large falcon. Females: 625-1,002 g, wingspan 89-115 cm. Males substantially smaller: 412-635 g. The size overlap between Prairie Falcon and smaller Peregrines creates identification challenges, especially in worn or juvenile plumage.
- **Juvenile.** Heavily streaked below, brown above. The axillary patch is present. Juveniles are more heavily marked than adults and may appear darker overall.
- **The Peregrine comparison.** The Prairie Falcon and Peregrine are similar in size and both nest on cliff faces. The key distinctions: Peregrine has a darker, more complete facial helmet (dark crown and malar stripe create a continuous dark "helmet"); Prairie Falcon has a brown, incomplete malar stripe and the diagnostic dark axillary patch that Peregrines lack. In flight, wing shape is similar; the axillary patch clinches the ID.

(Sources: Prairie Falcon morphology; axillary patch field mark; malar stripe comparison; size data; juvenile plumage; Peregrine comparison; field identification tips)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### A Stable but Monitored Species

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern globally. Population trend is stable with some regional fluctuations.
- **US population.** Estimates range from 5,000-10,000 breeding pairs in the western United States. The Prairie Falcon is more abundant than the Peregrine in the arid west but less abundant than the Red-tailed Hawk or other generalist raptors.
- **Conservation concerns.** The primary concerns are: (1) ground squirrel poisoning programs (which reduce the Prairie Falcon's primary prey during the breeding season); (2) disturbance at nest sites during the critical incubation and early chick period; (3) conversion of native grassland to agriculture or energy development. These are the same general threats as the Ferruginous Hawk (229), reflecting the shared open-country dependency.
- **Nest site protection.** BLM and other federal land managers routinely close climbing areas near Prairie Falcon nest sites during the nesting season (typically March-June). This protection is coordinated with the falconry and climbing communities, and compliance has generally been good.
- **Falconry connection.** The Prairie Falcon has been one of the most prized falconry birds in North America. Its speed, aggressiveness, and open-country hunting style made it highly valued. Regulated falconry does not significantly impact wild populations, but the historical harvest of wild birds (before modern falconry regulations) is part of the species' conservation history.

(Sources: Prairie Falcon IUCN status; US population 5,000-10,000 pairs; ground squirrel poisoning threat; nest site disturbance; BLM closure programs; falconry historical use; modern falconry regulation)
