## What Worked

1. **The contrast with the Peregrine (231) is the degree's structural foundation.** The two Falcos in degrees 231-232 document the genus at complementary positions: Peregrine is cosmopolitan, urban-adapted, the performance extreme. Prairie Falcon is endemic, canyon-restricted, the regional specialist. The contrast is clean and informative.
2. **The "canyon stays in the canyon" observation is precise.** The Peregrine colonized the city skyscraper. The Prairie Falcon did not. This behavioral distinction between two closely related birds is ecologically interesting and practically diagnostic. One species made the urban adaptation; the other did not. Documenting this difference is the degree's clearest single finding.
3. **The North American endemic status is correctly highlighted.** The Prairie Falcon is the only large falcon restricted to North America. The Peregrine at 231 is cosmopolitan. The difference in range is not just geographic fact -- it is ecological context. A bird that evolved only in North America and breeds only in North America has a different relationship to the PNW landscape than a worldwide wanderer.
4. **The axillary patch is the right field mark to emphasize.** In the field, the dark underwing patch is the most reliable Prairie Falcon diagnostic. Teaching this mark is practically useful for anyone watching eastern PNW raptors.
5. **The raptor arc 221-232 is properly closed.** Twelve degrees. Three raptor families. All major PNW raptor ecological niches. The structure of the arc is documented in this retrospective. The engine closes a chapter cleanly.

## Discoveries

1. **The species epithet *mexicanus* is misleading.** The type specimen was collected in Mexico, but the species' primary range and ecological significance is in the US West. The name is a historical artifact of where the first scientific specimen was obtained, not where the bird primarily lives. This is a common pattern in binomial nomenclature -- the first collection location, not the primary range, sets the name.
2. **The hunting style difference (horizontal pursuit vs. vertical stoop) maps onto habitat.** Open terrain favors low fast pursuit; the Peregrine's coastal and city environments favor the high-altitude stoop. The behavioral difference is habitat-driven. The Prairie Falcon in canyon terrain uses the walls as wind barriers and hunting launch points; the Peregrine uses altitude as its primary weapon. Habitat shapes behavior.
3. **The falconry connection is historically significant.** The Prairie Falcon was one of the most prized North American falconry birds before modern regulation. Historical wild harvest (before regulations) was a real population impact. The transition from unregulated to regulated falconry is a conservation story worth noting forward.
