## THE RUT ROAR -- COMBAT VOCALIZATION

### Deep Sustained Bellowing

The rut roar is distinct from the bugle:

- **The bugle announces.** The bugle (degree 197) is a display -- a declaration of presence, a long-range signal. The whistle carries for kilometers. The bugle says: "I am here. I am strong."
- **The roar contests.** The rut roar occurs when two bulls are in close proximity, challenging each other directly. It is not a signal for the valley -- it is a signal for the opponent standing 10 meters away. The roar says: "I will not back down."
- **Character.** Deep, sustained, guttural bellowing. Lower and more sustained than the bugle's whistle. The roar vibrates from the chest, amplified by the bull's massive body. It has a raw, primal quality -- less structured than the bugle, more like a forced exhalation of pure aggression.
- **Frequency.** 80-400 Hz. Lower than the bugle's whistle (1,000-4,000 Hz), slightly lower than the bugle's grunt component (100-300 Hz). The roar occupies the deepest part of the elk's vocal range.
- **Duration.** 3-8 seconds per roar. Longer than a bugle. The sustained quality is part of the challenge -- holding the roar demonstrates lung capacity and physical power.
- **Context.** The roar occurs during the most intense moments of the rut: when two bulls face each other before sparring, during antler-locked combat, and immediately after a contest is settled. It is the vocal expression of physical competition.

## ANTLER COMBAT -- THE PHYSICAL CONTEST

### When Roars Aren't Enough

The rut roar accompanies antler combat:

- **Escalation sequence.** The rut begins with bugling (long-range display). As the rut intensifies and bulls encounter each other, the interaction escalates: bugling at distance, parallel walking (assessment), roaring at close range, and finally antler engagement.
- **Antler lock.** Two bulls lower their heads and engage antlers. They push against each other, each trying to force the other off balance. The combined weight of two 300-500 kg bulls creates tremendous force. The roar continues during the push -- grunting, bellowing, the sound of exertion and aggression.
- **Duration of combat.** Most contests last 30 seconds to 5 minutes. Rarely, fights continue for 15-30 minutes. Injuries are possible -- broken antler tines, puncture wounds, exhaustion. Fatalities are rare but documented.
- **Stakes.** The winner controls the harem -- a group of 10-30 cows. The loser retreats. The stakes are reproductive access, the fundamental currency of natural selection. The roar and the combat are not gratuitous -- they are the mechanism by which genetic fitness is contested.
- **Post-combat roar.** The winner often roars after the contest, reaffirming dominance. This post-combat roar is deeper and more sustained -- the sound of victory and exhaustion combined.

(Sources: Elk antler combat; rut escalation; sparring behavior; harem dynamics; PNW elk rut ecology)
