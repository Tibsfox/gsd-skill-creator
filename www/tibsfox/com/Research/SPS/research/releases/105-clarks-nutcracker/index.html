Sixth Consecutive Corvidae — The Corvidae Sextet

Six consecutive Corvidae: Raven (100), Raven pair (101), American Crow (102), Steller’s Jay (103), Gray Jay (104), Clark’s Nutcracker (105). The corvid quintet becomes a sextet. Ties the Mustelidae sextet (63–68) and the Sciuridae sextet (75–80) for longest family streak since Sciuridae’s octet (75–82). Six degrees, one family — six acoustic characters: deep cronk, duet, sharp caw, raucous alarm, soft whistles, and now harsh grating kraa-a-a. The sextet ends with the loudest alpine voice in the corvid family.

The Whitebark Pine Specialist — 98,000 Seeds Per Year

Clark’s Nutcracker is a whitebark pine obligate — its survival and the survival of the whitebark pine (Pinus albicaulis) are deeply intertwined. The bird caches up to 98,000 pine seeds per year in thousands of scattered locations across the alpine landscape. It remembers the locations of these caches with extraordinary spatial memory, recovering them months later — even under snow. The seeds it fails to recover germinate, making the nutcracker the primary dispersal agent for whitebark pine. Without the nutcracker, the pine cannot reproduce effectively. Without the pine, the nutcracker cannot survive. Obligate mutualism.

Seed Caching — The Greatest Memory in the Corvid Family

The nutcracker’s sublingual pouch can carry up to 150 seeds at a time, transporting them kilometers from the source tree to cache sites on south-facing slopes, ridges, and windswept areas where snow melts earliest. Each cache contains 1–15 seeds buried 2–3 cm deep in soil. The bird creates thousands of caches per year and remembers a significant proportion of their locations using spatial landmarks. This spatial memory is among the most remarkable in the animal kingdom — the nutcracker’s hippocampus is proportionally larger than that of non-caching corvids. The caching behavior is the foundation of alpine forest regeneration.

Harsh Grating Kraa-a-a

Clark’s Nutcracker’s primary vocalization is a harsh, grating, far-carrying kraa-a-a — a rough, nasal, almost mechanical call that echoes across alpine ridges and whitebark pine stands. The call is diagnostic: if you hear a harsh grating call above treeline in the Cascades, it is almost certainly a Clark’s Nutcracker.

E=5 — high energy. The nutcracker’s call is loud, carrying, and persistent. It broadcasts across open alpine terrain where sound carries far. The harsh quality is distinct from every other corvid in the sextet — the raven is deep and resonant, the crow is sharp and percussive, the Steller’s Jay is raucous and alarming, the Gray Jay is soft and conversational. The nutcracker is grating and mechanical.

Named for William Clark

Clark’s Nutcracker was described by Alexander Wilson in 1811 from a specimen collected during the Lewis and Clark expedition (1804–1806). The species is named for William Clark, who first described the bird in his journal near the Salmon River in present-day Idaho. The Lewis and Clark expedition encountered the bird in the mountain forests of the Northwest — exactly the alpine pine habitat where the species remains today.
