## CLARK'S NUTCRACKER -- *Nucifraga columbiana* -- THE WHITEBARK PINE SPECIALIST

### The Species

Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) is a medium-large corvid of alpine and subalpine conifer forests [1, 2]:

- **Body length:** 27-30 cm.
- **Wingspan:** 55-61 cm.
- **Weight:** 106-161 g (larger than Gray Jay, smaller than crow).
- **Plumage:** Pale gray body. Black wings with large white wing patches visible in flight. Black central tail feathers with white outer tail feathers. Long, sharp, slightly decurved bill -- a seed-extraction tool.
- **Distribution:** Mountain forests of western North America, from British Columbia to Mexico. In the PNW, found in whitebark pine and other high-elevation pine stands of the Cascade Range and Olympic Mountains.

### Named for William Clark

Clark's Nutcracker was first described scientifically by Alexander Wilson in 1811, based on specimens collected during the **Lewis and Clark expedition** (1804-1806). William Clark described the bird in his journal on August 22, 1805, near the Salmon River in present-day Idaho. The species name *columbiana* references the Columbia River region. The bird was encountered in the mountain forests of the Northwest -- exactly the alpine pine habitat where it remains today.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature; [2] Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## THE SOUND: HARSH GRATING KRAA-A-A -- THE ALPINE BROADCASTER

### The Vocal Repertoire

Clark's Nutcracker's primary vocalization is a **harsh, grating, far-carrying kraa-a-a** [1, 2]:

**Kraa-a-a:** The diagnostic call. Harsh, nasal, grating, almost mechanical. A rough, drawn-out call that echoes across alpine ridges and open pine stands. Given frequently in flight and while perched. The call is immediately identifiable -- no other bird in alpine PNW habitat produces this sound.

**Chatter:** Rapid series of harsh notes, often given in social contexts or during territorial disputes. More complex than the single kraa-a-a but with the same grating quality.

**Soft calls:** Quieter, more modulated calls used for close-range communication between pair members and family groups. Rarely heard by human observers.

## WHITEBARK PINE MUTUALISM -- 98,000 SEEDS PER YEAR

### The Obligate Relationship

Clark's Nutcracker and whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) share an **obligate mutualism** -- one of the most remarkable plant-animal relationships in North American ecology [1, 2, 3]:

- **Seed extraction:** The nutcracker uses its long, sharp bill to pry seeds from whitebark pine cones. The cones are indehiscent -- they do not open on their own. The nutcracker is one of the only species that can access the seeds.
- **Sublingual pouch:** The nutcracker has a specialized sublingual pouch that can carry up to **150 seeds at a time**, transporting them kilometers from the source tree to cache sites.
- **98,000 seeds per year:** A single nutcracker caches up to 98,000 pine seeds per year in thousands of scattered locations across the alpine landscape.
- **Spatial memory:** The nutcracker remembers a significant proportion of its cache locations using spatial landmarks, recovering them months later -- even under snow. The hippocampus (brain region for spatial memory) is proportionally larger than in non-caching corvids.
- **Forest regeneration:** The seeds the nutcracker fails to recover **germinate**, making the nutcracker the **primary dispersal agent** for whitebark pine. Without the nutcracker, whitebark pine cannot effectively reproduce and colonize new sites.

### Whitebark Pine in Crisis

Whitebark pine is in severe decline across western North America due to:
- **White pine blister rust** (introduced fungal pathogen)
- **Mountain pine beetle** (native beetle whose outbreaks are intensified by warming temperatures)
- **Climate change** (shifting snowpack, altered fire regimes)

The decline of whitebark pine threatens the nutcracker. The decline of the nutcracker threatens the pine. The mutualism that built alpine forests over millennia is now under **double pressure**.

(Sources: [1] Tomback 1982, "Dispersal of whitebark pine seeds by Clark's nutcracker"; [2] Lorenz et al. 2011; [3] Ornithological literature)

## SEED CACHING -- THE GREATEST SPATIAL MEMORY IN THE CORVID FAMILY

### The Caching Strategy

Clark's Nutcracker's caching behavior is the most extreme of any corvid [1, 2]:

- **Cache density:** Thousands of caches per year, each containing 1-15 seeds buried 2-3 cm in soil.
- **Cache placement:** South-facing slopes, ridges, and windswept areas where snow melts earliest in spring. The bird preferentially selects sites that will be snow-free when it needs to recover cached seeds for breeding.
- **Transport distance:** Seeds are carried up to 22 km from the source tree -- far beyond the dispersal range of any other mechanism for whitebark pine seeds.
- **Recovery rate:** The nutcracker recovers an estimated 70-90% of cached seeds. The 10-30% left unrecovered are the foundation of whitebark pine regeneration.

## ALPINE PINE HABITAT

Clark's Nutcracker occupies the **highest elevational niche** of any corvid in the sextet [1]:

- **Habitat:** Whitebark pine stands at and above treeline. Open, wind-swept alpine ridges. Also found in other high-elevation pine species (limber pine, ponderosa at elevation).
- **Elevational niche:** Above the Gray Jay's subalpine forest (degree 104) and into the true alpine zone. The nutcracker is the corvid of the highest elevations -- the alpine broadcaster.
- **Year-round resident:** Does not migrate altitudinally in the traditional sense, but makes seasonal movements between whitebark pine stands (summer/fall caching) and lower-elevation cache recovery sites (winter/spring).
- **Relationship to alpine community:** Shared habitat with American Pika (74, alpine talus), Hoary Marmot (76, alpine meadow), and American Marten (65, subalpine forest below). The nutcracker occupies the alpine pine niche that no other species in the engine holds.

(Sources: [1] Ornithological literature)
