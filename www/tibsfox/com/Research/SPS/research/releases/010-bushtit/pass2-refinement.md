### The Minimum Viable Signal and the Background Radiation: What the Noise Floor Contains

Pass 1 documented the Bushtit's call as the simplest acoustic signal in the engine — a thin, high-pitched "tsit" or "seep" that barely rises above ambient noise, carrying only one bit of information: present/absent. Pass 2 connects this to Explorer 4's measurement of the radiation background and to the concept of the noise floor as a habitat rather than an absence.

Explorer 4 measured the radiation environment before, during, and after the Project Argus nuclear detonations. The instrument had to distinguish between three signal sources: the natural Van Allen belt radiation (background), the cosmic ray flux (background), and the artificial Argus electrons (signal). The natural radiation and cosmic rays constituted the noise floor — the persistent background against which the artificial signal had to be detected. But the noise floor was not empty. It contained the Van Allen belts themselves — the foundational discovery of the previous three Explorer missions. What was "noise" for the Argus measurement was "signal" for the belt measurement. The noise floor is not the absence of information. It is information at a different scale.

The Bushtit's continuous "tsit-tsit-tsit" is the acoustic noise floor of the PNW shrub layer. In a recording of a suburban hedgerow or a blackberry thicket, the bushtit flock's contact calls form a near-constant background hum — thin, high-pitched, barely audible individual signals that merge into a collective sibilance. To a birder listening for a song — a thrush melody, a wren cascade, a chickadee alarm — the bushtit calls are noise. They are the acoustic background against which the "real" signals are heard. But the bushtit calls ARE information. They encode the flock's spatial distribution, movement direction, foraging progress, and social cohesion in real time. The noise floor is a network protocol.

Cladonia stellaris grows in mats — dense, continuous carpets of lichen that cover the ground in boreal and subalpine environments. An individual lichen is nearly invisible. The mat is unmistakable. The individual's contribution to the mat's structure is minimal — one more branch-tip, one more star-cluster, one more few millimeters of coverage. But the accumulated effect of thousands of individuals covering a square meter of forest floor creates a visible, functional ecological structure: thermal insulation for the soil, moisture retention, invertebrate habitat. The individual lichen is below the resolution threshold. The mat is above it. Scale transforms noise into signal.

The Bushtit operates identically. One "tsit" is nothing — a 0.05-second pulse at 7 kHz, inaudible beyond a few meters. But a flock of 20-40 bushtits, each producing "tsit" calls continuously while foraging, creates a collective acoustic presence that is the defining sound of the shrub layer. The individual call is below the signal threshold. The flock's accumulated calls exceed it. The Bushtit is the Cladonia of the acoustic landscape: invisible individually, undeniable collectively.

### Swarm Communication and the Flock as Distributed Sensor Network

Pass 1 documented the Bushtit's obligate sociality — it is never alone, always in a flock of 10-40+ birds that moves as a unit through shrubby vegetation. Pass 2 develops the flock's acoustic system as a distributed sensor network and connects it to Explorer 4's role in the early satellite tracking network.

Explorer 4 was one node in the emerging network of Explorer satellites that collectively mapped the near-Earth radiation environment. Explorer 1 (degree 7) made the discovery. Explorer 3 (degree 9) confirmed it. Explorer 4 measured the response to artificial perturbation. No single satellite could map the full spatial and temporal structure of the radiation belts. The network of satellites — each orbiting at a different inclination and altitude, each sampling a different slice of the environment — collectively produced a three-dimensional picture that no single instrument could generate. The Explorer network was a distributed sensor system.

The Bushtit flock is a biological distributed sensor network. Each bird is a node — a small, mobile sensor scanning the shrub layer for food (tiny insects, spiders, insect eggs). Each bird's contact call ("tsit") is a status transmission: "I am here, I am foraging, I have not found danger." The flock's collective acoustic output is a real-time map of the foraging front — a moving wave of tiny signals that encodes the flock's spatial distribution, movement rate, and cohesion. If one bird falls silent (stops calling), the nearest neighbors adjust — moving toward the silent bird's last known position, checking for predators, restoring contact. The flock's acoustic protocol is fault-tolerant: the loss of one node triggers a local search-and-recovery response that maintains the network's integrity.

The alarm network implications at degree 10: the Bushtit does not produce chickadee-style combinatorial alarm calls. Its alarm response is simpler — a faster, higher-pitched chatter that triggers the flock to freeze or flee. But bushtit flocks frequently associate with chickadees, nuthatches, and kinglets in mixed-species foraging groups during winter. In these mixed flocks, the bushtit benefits from the chickadee's alarm broadcast system (documented at degrees 3, 5, 8, 9) without contributing to it. The bushtit is a **free-rider** on the chickadee alarm network — a species that receives the chickadee's encoded threat information without producing equivalent information itself.
