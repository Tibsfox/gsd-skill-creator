## Purpose

This is the twenty-eighth release of the PNW 360 Species engine and the second swallow in the Hirundinidae trio (degrees 26-28). The engine needed to deliver the trio's middle movement, introduce within-family contrast at maximum resolution, establish the Tachycineta genus pair (degrees 27-28), and demonstrate that a single family can produce fundamentally different ecological and acoustic solutions.

Five structural contributions:

1. **The within-family contrast point.** The Violet-green Swallow is the Purple Martin's opposite on nearly every axis: small (14 g vs. 45-60 g), solitary (paired/scattered vs. obligate colonial), high-pitched (5-8 kHz peak vs. 2-6 kHz peak), discrete (staccato chips vs. legato stream), self-sufficient (natural cavities vs. managed nest boxes), vertically distributed (valley to alpine vs. sea level). Same family. Same aerial insectivore lifestyle. Every parameter set to the opposite extreme. The engine's clearest demonstration of within-family divergence.

2. **The Tachycineta consecutive pair.** Degrees 27-28 place two species from the same genus (*Tachycineta thalassina* and *T. bicolor*) in consecutive degrees -- the engine's tightest same-genus adjacency. This parallels the Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 and creates a nested taxonomic hierarchy: Hirundinidae (family, 26-28) contains Tachycineta (genus, 27-28). The engine zooms to its finest taxonomic resolution.

3. **The liquid twitter.** The 28th acoustic element. Rapid descending FM chips -- each chip a discrete acoustic event with onset, peak, and offset. The twitter is the rain to the martin's stream: same "liquid" timbral quality, different time-domain structure (discrete vs. continuous, staccato vs. legato). The engine now holds two liquid textures, demonstrating that a single timbral adjective can produce different acoustic structures depending on temporal organization.

4. **Natural cavity independence.** The Violet-green's small body fits cavity entrances too small for European Starlings -- natural "starling-proofing" through body plan. No nest box dependency. No conservation crisis. No management programs. The bird that solved the starling problem by being small. After the martin's story of managed dependence, the Violet-green shows the alternative: solve it with your body.

5. **The western specialty paradox.** More common than the Purple Martin by 20:1 in the PNW, yet far less known. Self-sufficiency is less newsworthy than dependency. The quiet, common, independent bird lives in the shadow of the loud, rare, managed one. The engine records the unrecognized majority.

## Five Structural Advances

### 1. Maximum Within-Family Divergence

The Violet-green Swallow is the Purple Martin's opposite on every measurable axis while sharing the same family, the same bauplan, and the same lifestyle. This is the engine's most extreme within-family contrast -- more extreme than any pair within Turdidae (5 species), Parulidae (5 species), or Paridae (2 species). The swallow trio's two bookends (martin and Violet-green) are as different as two aerial-insectivore swallows can be. Same wing plan, same syrinx, same gape, same migration pattern -- different size, different pitch, different sociality, different conservation status, different altitude range, different temporal organization. The bauplan is the constant. Everything else varies.

### 2. The Tachycineta Pair: Finest Taxonomic Resolution

The engine's previous finest resolution was the Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 (same genus, consecutive degrees, opposite acoustic direction). The Tachycineta pair at 27-28 matches that resolution AND nests within a family-level cluster (Hirundinidae, 26-28). This is the first time the engine has placed a genus-level pair inside a family-level cluster. The taxonomic hierarchy is expressed in sequential degrees: family (3 degrees) contains genus (2 degrees) contains species (1 degree each). The engine is learning to zoom.

### 3. Two Liquid Textures: The Adjective Needs a Verb

Degrees 26 and 27 both receive the label "liquid" -- but the sounds are fundamentally different in temporal structure. The martin's liquid is continuous (stream). The Violet-green's liquid is discrete (rain). This demonstrates a principle the engine has not previously made explicit: **timbral labels are necessary but not sufficient**. You must also specify the temporal organization. "Liquid legato" and "liquid staccato" are two different things. The engine now holds both, and the distinction matters.

### 4. Size as Strategy: Body Plan Solves the Starling Problem

The Violet-green's small body is not a limitation but a solution. It provides access to small cavities that exclude the European Starling, enabling housing independence that the larger Purple Martin cannot achieve. The same size that produces higher pitch, shorter songs, and smaller prey also produces competitive advantage in the cavity market. Size is the master variable: it determines pitch, prey, cavity access, flight agility, and conservation status. One parameter drives five outcomes. The engine has never had a species where a single morphological variable explains this much.

### 5. The Unrecognized Majority

The Violet-green Swallow is 20 times more common than the Purple Martin in the PNW, yet far less known. The engine records this asymmetry because it is structurally significant: the soundscape is dominated by sounds people do not know. The familiar sounds (robin, martin) are a small fraction of the acoustic environment. The unfamiliar sounds (Violet-green, kinglet, Hutton's vireo) are the majority. Recognition does not correlate with abundance. The quiet, self-sufficient, common species are the acoustic dark matter -- present everywhere, noticed almost nowhere.

## What Worked

### 1. The within-family contrast is clean and informative
Every axis of variation between the martin and the Violet-green is well-defined and measurable. Mass, pitch, coloniality, cavity dependency, altitude range, conservation status, plumage contrast -- all flip from one pole to the other. The engine's clearest comparative pair.

### 2. The stream/rain metaphor for two liquid textures
Continuous vs. discrete versions of the same timbral quality. The metaphor is precise: stream = legato liquid (martin), rain = staccato liquid (Violet-green). Both are water. Both are liquid. Different time-domain structure. The engine gains a vocabulary distinction it needed.

### 3. The Tachycineta pair is well-positioned
Placing same-genus species in consecutive degrees within a family cluster creates a nested taxonomic hierarchy that the engine has not attempted before. The zoom from family to genus to species, expressed in sequential degrees, is structurally elegant.

### 4. The name analysis is strong
"The sea-colored swift-one" -- *Tachycineta thalassina* is one of the most evocative scientific names in ornithology. The etymology section connects the name to the bird's actual appearance and behavior. Name, taxonomy, and phenotype align. This is good science communication.

### 5. Size as master variable is a new analytical lens
Demonstrating that one morphological parameter (body mass) drives five ecological outcomes (pitch, prey, cavity access, flight agility, conservation status) gives the engine a powerful analytical tool for future within-family comparisons.

### 6. The western specialty paradox is honest
Recording that a more common bird is less known because it is self-sufficient and quiet -- and naming that as a structural feature of human attention, not of the bird -- is accurate observation. The engine documents what exists, not what people notice.

Great Blue Heron (Pelecaniformes, degree 0) and Northern Flicker (Piciformes, degrees 21-22). The Violet-green Swallow is Passeriformes. Fifth consecutive Passeriformes (23-27). The swallow trio will extend this to six (23-28). THIS IS CRITICAL.

### 2. Still no marine or shoreline species
TWENTY-EIGHT degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" without a species from the Sound. "Open water" is the ecosystem label but the bird is not ON the water -- it flies above it. The water itself remains silent. **MOST URGENT STRUCTURAL GAP.**

### 3. Still no raptor
No Accipitridae, no Falconidae. Twenty-eight degrees without a bird of prey.

### 4. Still no corvid
No Steller's Jay, no crow, no raven. Approaching untenable.

### 5. Energy 5 remains absent
Seven consecutive E=4 degrees. The longest plateau in the engine. E=5 has never appeared. The engine has never tested its maximum dynamic. A gull scream, corvid caw, or eagle cry could break the plateau.

### 6. The Passeriformes streak continues
Five consecutive Passeriformes (23-27). Extending to six after degree 28. After the trio, the engine MUST leave Passeriformes.

### 7. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Twenty-three degrees since the Spotted Owl. The night has one voice.

### 8. The summer migrant run continues
Third consecutive summer migrant (25-resident, 26-summer, 27-summer). If degree 28 is also summer, the engine will have three or four consecutive summer arrivals.

5. **A corvid (CRITICAL).** Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. Twenty-eight degrees without the most intelligent, acoustically varied birds in the PNW. Overdue.

6. **A raptor.** Bald Eagle or Red-tailed Hawk. Top predators absent from the soundscape.

7. **A nocturnal species.** Twenty-three degrees since the owl. The night is silent.
