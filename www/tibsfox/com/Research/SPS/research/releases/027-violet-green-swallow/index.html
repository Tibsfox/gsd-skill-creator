Violet-green Swallow

Tachycineta thalassina · Family Hirundinidae (Swallows) · Second Swallow
Twitter and Liquid Chips · Open Water · Summer · Energy 4
High above a mountain lake, a small iridescent shape darts and flickers through the air — brilliant green above, clean white below, white patches flashing above the eyes. It twists, banks, snaps upward, drops — and from it comes a rapid, crystalline twitter of liquid chips, each one a tiny water-drop of sound. The Violet-green Swallow. The sea-colored swift-one. The second Hirundinidae. The swallow trio’s middle movement. The western specialty that lives from harbor to alpine and needs no house that humans built.

The Swallow Trio: Middle Movement (Degree 27 of 26–28)

Three consecutive Hirundinidae. The Purple Martin was the patriarch — the heavy bomber. The Violet-green Swallow is the opposite extreme: the lightest, highest-pitched, most independent, most vertically distributed. The fighter jet. The piccolo.

Tachycineta: Same-Genus Consecutive Pair (Degrees 27–28)

The Violet-green Swallow (T. thalassina) and Tree Swallow (T. bicolor) share the genus Tachycineta — the engine’s tightest same-genus adjacency. Two consecutive degrees, same genus, different species. The only precedent is the Catharus pair at degrees 18–19 (Swainson’s Thrush, Veery). Nested within the Hirundinidae trio: family (26–28) contains genus (27–28). The engine’s finest taxonomic resolution yet.

Song Bioacoustics: Twitter and Liquid Chips

The Violet-green Swallow’s vocalization is a rapid, high-pitched twitter of discrete liquid chips — each chip a brief descending FM note with a water-drop quality. Not a continuous stream like the martin’s gurgle, but a rapid series of individual drops. The stream vs. the rain.

Stream vs. rain. The Purple Martin’s song is a continuous flow — legato, notes grading into each other. The Violet-green’s twitter is rapid discrete drops — each chip complete with onset, peak, offset, and a micro-silence before the next. Same “liquid” quality. Different time-domain structure. A stream flows. Rain drips. Both are water.

Iridescent Green and Violet: The Sea-Colored Swift-One

Males: Crown iridescent violet-bronze; back and rump brilliant iridescent green (thalassina — sea-colored); underparts clean white; distinctive white patches above the eye and on the rump flanks. Females: Similar but duller, reduced white patches. Compared to the Purple Martin’s uniformly dark iridescence, the Violet-green shows maximum contrast: vivid green above, bright white below. The name is literally true — violet crown, green back. One of the most accurately named birds in North American ornithology.

Cavity Nesting: Natural Independence

Where the Purple Martin depends on human-built nest boxes, the Violet-green Swallow retains complete housing independence. It nests in woodpecker holes, natural tree hollows, cliff crevices, rock outcrops, and occasionally human structures — from sea level to above alpine treeline (2,500+ m). Its small body (14 g) lets it use cavity entrances too small for European Starlings, providing natural “starling-proofing” that the larger Purple Martin lacks. Size is the Violet-green’s competitive advantage in the cavity market. No conservation crisis. No management dependency. Self-sufficient.

Taxonomy: Tachycineta thalassina

Tachycineta: Greek takhys (swift) + kineo (to move) — “the swift-mover.” Thalassina: Greek thalassa (the sea) — “sea-colored.” The full binomial translates as “the sea-colored swift-one” — one of the most evocative names in North American ornithology. The common name “Violet-green” matches the actual plumage: violet crown, green back. Name, taxonomy, and appearance align perfectly. The bird IS the name.

Second Hirundinidae. The swallow family now holds two species in the engine. After degree 28, it will hold three — becoming the engine’s third-largest family behind Turdidae (5) and Parulidae (5).

The Western Specialty: More Common, Less Known

The Violet-green Swallow outnumbers the Purple Martin roughly 20:1 in the PNW — tens of thousands of breeding pairs vs. the martin’s 800–1,000. Yet it is far less known to the public. Why? The martin nests in conspicuous harbors and has a compelling conservation story. The Violet-green nests in cliff crevices and forest snags, has no drama, and makes a quiet sound. Self-sufficiency is less newsworthy than dependency. The quiet, common, independent bird lives in the shadow of the loud, rare, managed one.

Within-Family Plumage Divergence — Two Iridescent Strategies

Natural Cavity Independence — Size as Competitive Advantage

Small body fits small holes that exclude starlings. No nest box dependency. Valley to alpine altitude range via cliff crevices. The Violet-green solved the starling problem by being small. Conservation through body plan, not human intervention.
