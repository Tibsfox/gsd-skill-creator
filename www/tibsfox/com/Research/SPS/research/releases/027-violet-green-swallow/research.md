## Context: The Sea-Colored Swift-One

The Purple Martin opened the sky at degree 26 -- the largest swallow, the colonial patriarch, the liquid gurgle. The sky was new then. One degree later, it is already becoming familiar. The engine remains aerial, remains Hirundinidae, remains above the water. But the bird has changed completely.

*Tachycineta thalassina* at degree 27 is a different kind of swallow. Where the Purple Martin is heavy (45-60 g), the Violet-green Swallow is light (14 g). Where the martin is colonial, the Violet-green is solitary or loosely paired. Where the martin depends on human-built nest boxes at sea level, the Violet-green nests in natural cavities in cliff crevices, tree holes, and rock faces from valley floor to alpine treeline. Where the martin is conservation-dependent, the Violet-green is self-sufficient. Where the martin's song is a rich, continuous, liquid gurgle, the Violet-green's call is a rapid, high-pitched twitter of discrete liquid chips -- faster, thinner, higher, more staccato than its larger cousin.

The name tells you everything. *Tachycineta* -- from Greek *takhys* (swift) and *kineo* (to move). The swift-mover. *Thalassina* -- from Greek *thalassa* (the sea). Sea-colored. The Violet-green Swallow is the **sea-colored swift-one**: a bird named for the color of its back (iridescent green like sunlit ocean) and the quality of its flight (fast, agile, darting). The name is poetry in taxonomy. The bird IS the name.

This is also the engine's entry into the **Tachycineta** genus -- a genus it will revisit immediately at degree 28 with the Tree Swallow (*T. bicolor*). Two consecutive same-genus species. The last time the engine placed two same-genus species in consecutive degrees was the Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 (Swainson's Thrush and Veery). Before that, the duplicate chickadees at degrees 3 and 9 shared a genus but were separated by six degrees. The Tachycineta pair at 27-28 is the tightest same-genus adjacency in the engine. Same genus, consecutive degrees, different species. The engine zooms in to the finest taxonomic resolution it has ever attempted.

The Violet-green Swallow is the 26th unique species across 28 degrees. It brings the swallow trio's middle movement, the first same-genus consecutive pair, the highest-pitched swallow, and a western endemic that is more common than the Purple Martin in the PNW but far less known. It is the quiet one of the three.

## Taxonomy: The Swift-Mover from the Sea

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Hirundinidae (Swallows and Martins)
- **Genus:** *Tachycineta*
- **Species:** *Tachycineta thalassina* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Common Name:** Violet-green Swallow
- **Etymology:** *Tachycineta* from Greek *takhys* (swift, fast) + *kineo* (to move, to set in motion). The genus name means "the one that moves swiftly" -- the swift-mover, the fast-flyer. *Thalassina* from Greek *thalassa* (the sea), meaning "sea-colored." The specific epithet describes the iridescent green dorsal plumage, which in certain light resembles the color of sunlit ocean water. The full binomial translates roughly as "the sea-colored swift-one" -- one of the most evocative names in North American ornithology.

### The Tachycineta Connection: Degree 27 and 28

The Violet-green Swallow shares its genus *Tachycineta* with the Tree Swallow (*T. bicolor*) at degree 28. This creates a **same-genus consecutive pair** -- the engine's tightest taxonomic adjacency. The genus *Tachycineta* contains nine species, all in the Americas. Two are common in the PNW: the Violet-green Swallow (more western, more montane, more cliff-associated) and the Tree Swallow (more widespread, more wetland-associated, more willing to use nest boxes).

The Tachycineta pair at degrees 27-28 parallels the Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 (Swainson's Thrush and Veery). In both cases, two same-genus species occupy consecutive degrees, allowing direct within-genus comparison. But the patterns differ:

The Catharus triptych (degrees 7, 18, 19 -- three Catharus species with degree 7 separated) and the Tachycineta pair (degrees 27-28 -- two consecutive). The Tachycineta pair is nested within the broader Hirundinidae trio (degrees 26-28), creating a taxonomic nesting structure: family (Hirundinidae, 26-28) contains genus (Tachycineta, 27-28). ## The Song: Twitter and Liquid Chips

### From the Gurgle to the Twitter

The transition from degree 26 to degree 27 is a within-family acoustic shift: from the Purple Martin's rich, continuous, liquid gurgle to the Violet-green Swallow's rapid, discrete, high-pitched twitter of liquid chips. The two sounds share the word "liquid" but express it differently.

The Purple Martin's song is liquid because the notes flow continuously into each other -- legato, stream-like, no discrete boundaries. The Violet-green Swallow's chips are liquid because each individual chip has a smooth, water-drop quality -- a brief, clean, rounded note with a liquid timbre. But the chips are **discrete**. They do not flow together. They are separated by brief silences. The difference: the martin's song is a stream; the Violet-green's song is a series of drops. Same element (water), different states (flowing vs. dripping).

### Acoustic Description

Key acoustic parameters:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~3.0-9.0 kHz. Higher than the Purple Martin (1.0-8.0 kHz). The Violet-green's vocalization sits in the upper portion of the avian frequency spectrum. The peak energy is concentrated between 5.0-8.0 kHz -- well above the martin's peak at 2.0-6.0 kHz. This is the highest-pitched swallow in the engine.
- **Call duration:** 0.5-2.0 seconds per twitter sequence. Much shorter than the martin's 3-6 second song bouts. The Violet-green's vocalizations are brief bursts, not sustained performances.
- **Chip rate:** 10-20 chips per second in rapid twitter sequences. Faster than the martin's 8-15 notes/sec in the gurgling passages. But where the martin's notes merge at high rates, the Violet-green's chips remain individually separable even at maximum rate. Each chip is a clean, distinct unit. The rapid rate creates a twittering quality without reaching the perceptual fusion that defines the martin's gurgle.
- **Chip structure:** Each chip is a brief (15-30 ms) frequency-modulated note with a characteristic downward sweep. The chip begins at a higher frequency and drops 1-2 kHz over its duration. This descending FM contour gives each chip its "liquid" quality -- the pitch drop is heard as a droplet sound. The timbre is clean, thin, and slightly metallic -- a transparent sound compared to the martin's opaque richness.
- **Phrase structure:** The twitter is organized as a rapid series of similar chips, sometimes with a terminal note that differs in pitch or duration. Unlike the martin's continuous stream, the Violet-green's twitter has clear beginning and ending boundaries. It is a **burst**, not a flow.
- **Note types:** Two primary:
  1. **Liquid chips** -- the defining element. Brief descending FM notes with smooth contour. Clean, rounded, slightly metallic. The "twitter" quality comes from rapid repetition of these chips.
  2. **Contact notes** -- simpler, shorter, single-pitch chips used in flight between foraging bouts and during pair interactions. Higher-pitched and less modulated than the twitter chips.
- **Timbre:** Thin, high, liquid, crystalline. Where the martin's song is rich and opaque (many simultaneous frequency components filling a wide band), the Violet-green's twitter is transparent and narrow (a smaller number of components concentrated in a high band). The quality is often described as "sweet" or "tinkling" -- a sound that sparkles rather than pours.
- **Amplitude:** Soft to moderate. Energy 4 in this context reflects the persistent delivery and ecological presence rather than raw volume. The Violet-green's individual chips are quieter than the martin's gurgling phrases, but the bird calls frequently throughout the day, especially during flight. The energy is in the ubiquity, not the volume.
- **Context:** The Violet-green Swallow vocalizes primarily in flight -- during foraging, during territorial interactions, and during courtship. It also calls from perches near the nest cavity. There is no elaborate dawn chorus comparable to the martin's colony display. The sound is more intimate, more personal, more scattered across the landscape.

### The Within-Family Gradient: Martin vs. Violet-green vs. Tree

The swallow trio at degrees 26-28 creates a within-family acoustic gradient:

| Parameter | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) | Tree Swallow (28) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sound type | Rich gurgling liquid song | Twitter and liquid chips | Liquid twittering |
| Pitch center | Low-mid (2.0-6.0 kHz peak) | High (5.0-8.0 kHz peak) | Mid (3.0-6.0 kHz peak) |
| Note rate | 8-15/sec (fusion threshold) | 10-20/sec (discrete) | 8-15/sec (moderate) |

The gradient runs from heavy/low/continuous (martin) through light/high/discrete (Violet-green) to medium/moderate/mixed (Tree Swallow). The Violet-green occupies the high, thin, fast pole of the trio. It is the piccolo of the swallow family -- the martin is the cello, the Tree Swallow is the viola.

### The 28th Acoustic Element: The Liquid Twitter

**The progression (28 elements):** silence -- cascade -- drone -- language -- motif -- metronome -- question -- harmonic spiral -- ultrasonic threshold -- variation -- minimum signal -- trill/tremolo -- sawtooth sweep -- ostinato -- repetition-resolution -- accelerando -- crescendo -- the hook -- ascending spiral -- descending spiral -- sustain/the release -- the hammer -- resonant percussion -- the groove reset -- the hoarse warble -- the caroling -- the liquid gurgle -- **the liquid twitter**.

The liquid twitter differs from the liquid gurgle (degree 26) in articulation. The gurgle was legato: notes flowing into each other without breaks. The twitter is rapid staccato: individual chips separated by micro-silences, each chip a complete acoustic event with onset, peak, and offset. The engine now holds two "liquid" textures -- one flowing, one dripping. The same adjective, two different time-domain structures.

The relationship between degrees 26 and 27 is the relationship between a stream and rain. A stream flows continuously. Rain falls in drops. Both are water. Both are liquid. But the physical structure is different: continuous vs. discrete. The martin's song is the stream. The Violet-green's twitter is the rain.

## Plumage: Iridescent Green and Violet -- Structural Coloration Revisited

### The Colors of the Sea

The Violet-green Swallow's dorsal plumage is among the most stunning in the PNW avifauna. The back and rump are brilliant iridescent green -- not the simple green of a pigmented feather, but the shifting, angle-dependent green of structural coloration. In direct sunlight, the green blazes with an intensity that makes the bird visible at considerable distance despite its small size. The head and nape show iridescent violet, transitioning seamlessly from the green of the back. The overall effect is a gradient: violet crown flowing into green back flowing into green rump.

**Males:** Crown and nape: iridescent violet-bronze. Back and rump: iridescent green. Underparts: clean white. White extends upward in two distinctive patches -- one above and behind the eye (forming a "eyebrow" of white that wraps onto the face) and one on the sides of the rump (white flank patches that are visible in flight and nearly meet across the rump). Wings and tail: dark brownish-black.

**Females:** Similar pattern but duller. The iridescent green and violet are less brilliant, more bronze-toned. The white facial patches are smaller and less distinct. The underparts are white but may show faint gray-brown wash on the breast.

### Structural Coloration: Comparing Two Iridescent Swallows

The Purple Martin and the Violet-green Swallow both display structural coloration -- color produced by nanostructure in the feather barbules rather than by pigment. But the colors are different:

| Feature | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) |
|---|---|---|
| Dorsal color | Iridescent purple-black | Iridescent green + violet |
| Ventral color | Dark (male), gray (female) | Clean white |
| Color mechanism | Thin-film interference (single-layer) | Thin-film interference (multi-layer) |
| Color shift | Blue-violet-black | Green-bronze-violet |
| Contrast | Minimal (all dark) | Maximum (green above, white below) |
| Visibility in flight | Low (dark on dark) | High (white below visible against sky) |

The Violet-green's plumage creates a stronger visual signal in flight than the martin's. When viewed from below -- as a predator or observer would see a flying swallow -- the Violet-green is bright white, easily visible against the blue sky. The martin is dark, hard to see against any background. The white underparts of the Violet-green serve a signaling function: they make the bird visible to conspecifics during aerial social interactions and during the courtship flights that occur near nest sites.

The white patches above the eye and on the rump flanks are species-diagnostic. No other PNW swallow has white extending above the eye onto the face. In flight, the rump patches create a distinctive white "saddle" effect visible from behind. These are the field marks that separate the Violet-green from the Tree Swallow, which also has white underparts and iridescent green back but lacks the facial white patches and has a more uniformly dark rump.

### The Name in the Plumage

"Violet-green" is one of those bird names that is exactly, literally, descriptively correct. The violet is on the crown and nape. The green is on the back and rump. The name describes what you see. And *thalassina* -- sea-colored -- describes the green specifically: the iridescent green of the back, which in strong sunlight shifts through the same range of greens as sunlit ocean water. The taxonomy and the common name align perfectly with the visual reality of the bird. This is rare in ornithology, where many common names are misleading (the Red-bellied Woodpecker barely shows its red belly) or historical (the Tennessee Warbler does not live in Tennessee). The Violet-green Swallow is violet and green. The name is true.

## Cavity Nesting: Natural Independence

### A Different Nesting Strategy

Where the Purple Martin is almost entirely dependent on human-provided nest boxes in the PNW, the Violet-green Swallow retains its independence. It nests in **natural cavities** -- woodpecker holes, natural tree hollows, crevices in cliff faces, gaps in rock outcrops, and occasionally in crevices under eaves or in other human structures. But it does not require human infrastructure. The Violet-green finds its own housing.

The nesting ecology differs from the Purple Martin in several key dimensions:

| Feature | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) |
|---|---|---|
| Nest site type | Human-built nest boxes (90%+) | Natural cavities (majority) |
| Colonial tendency | Obligate colonial | Solitary or loosely paired |
| Nest site altitude | Sea level (harbors, marinas) | Valley floor to alpine treeline |
| Cavity size needed | Large (martin-sized entrance ~5.7 cm) | Small (smaller entrance, excludes starlings naturally) |
| Competition pressure | Severe (starlings, sparrows) | Moderate (less starling pressure at small cavities) |
| Human dependency | Critical | Minimal |
| Conservation status | Managed species | Self-sustaining |

The Violet-green Swallow's smaller body is a competitive advantage in the cavity market. Its small size means it can use cavities with entrances too small for European Starlings (which need approximately 4.5 cm or larger). A Violet-green can squeeze through a hole that excludes the bird's primary cavity competitor. This natural "starling-proofing" is the reason the Violet-green has never suffered the population collapse that devastated the Purple Martin. Size, which seems like a disadvantage in a large-animal world, is the Violet-green's key to housing security.

### Nest Site Diversity

The Violet-green Swallow nests in a wider range of structures than any other PNW swallow:

1. **Woodpecker holes.** Old holes excavated by smaller woodpeckers (Downy Woodpecker, Hairy Woodpecker, Red-breasted Sapsucker). The Violet-green is a secondary cavity nester that depends on woodpecker engineering, just as the martin does -- but the Violet-green needs smaller holes, and smaller woodpeckers are more abundant than the larger species whose holes serve martins.

2. **Natural tree hollows.** Rot cavities in dead or dying trees, especially in the broken tops of old conifers. Common in unmanaged forests where snags are left standing.

3. **Cliff crevices.** Cracks and recesses in rock faces, especially on south-facing cliffs that receive morning sun. This is the habitat feature that earned the Violet-green its "cliff dweller" role in the swallow trio. No other PNW swallow regularly nests in cliff faces (the Cliff Swallow builds mud nests ON cliffs, but does not nest IN cliff crevices).

4. **Rock outcrops.** In montane and alpine habitats, the Violet-green nests in crevices in exposed rock above treeline -- higher elevation than any other PNW swallow.

5. **Human structures.** Occasionally under eaves, in gaps in siding, or in purpose-built nest boxes. But this is opportunistic, not dependent. Remove all human structures and the Violet-green persists. Remove all nest boxes and the Purple Martin collapses.

### Altitude Range: Valley to Alpine

The Violet-green Swallow breeds across a remarkable altitude range -- from sea level to over 2,500 meters (8,000+ feet) in the Cascades. This is the widest altitudinal range of any swallow in the engine and one of the widest of any species. The martin is a sea-level specialist (harbors, marinas, lakeshores). The Tree Swallow is a lowland to mid-elevation species (wetlands, agricultural fields, forest clearings below ~1,500 m). The Violet-green goes higher than either.

In the high Cascades, Violet-green Swallows forage over alpine meadows, along cliff bands, and above timberline lakes -- habitats where neither the Purple Martin nor the Tree Swallow is regularly found. The cliff-nesting habit enables this altitudinal range: at high elevations where trees are sparse and snags are few, cliff crevices provide abundant nest sites. The Violet-green's relationship with rock is what allows it to colonize the vertical axis of the PNW landscape.

## Aerial Insectivory: The Lightweight Fighter

### Flight Style Comparison

The Violet-green Swallow is an aerial insectivore like the Purple Martin, but its flight style differs in ways that reflect its smaller body:

| Feature | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) |
|---|---|---|
| Body mass | 45-60 g | 14 g |
| Wing loading | Moderate | Low |
| Flight style | Powerful, direct, soaring | Agile, darting, fluttery |
| Turning radius | Moderate | Tight |
| Foraging altitude | 15-150+ m | 5-100 m |
| Sustained flight | 30-60 min | 10-30 min (shorter bouts) |
| Prey size | Large insects (dragonflies, beetles) | Small insects (midges, gnats, small flies) |
| Foraging mode | Hawking (mouth open, sweeping) | Pursuit (targeting individual prey) |

The Violet-green is the **fighter jet** of the swallow trio where the martin is the **heavy bomber**. Its low wing loading (small mass relative to wing area) gives it exceptional maneuverability -- it can execute tight turns, abrupt direction changes, and rapid altitude adjustments that the heavier martin cannot match. Watch a foraging Violet-green and you see constant, rapid course corrections: a dart left, a snap upward, a banking turn, a sudden drop. The flight is restless, flickering, almost nervous compared to the martin's powerful sweeping cruises.

The prey size reflects the size difference. The Violet-green takes small flying insects -- midges, gnats, small flies, mosquitoes, winged aphids. These are prey items too small for the martin to bother with. The two species partition the aerial insect resource by size: the martin takes the large items, the Violet-green takes the small ones. When both species forage over the same water body, they occupy the same airspace but eat different food. This is **resource partitioning** within the same foraging guild -- the ecological mechanism that allows two aerial insectivores to coexist.

### The Aerial Ecosystem: "Open Water"

The Violet-green Swallow's ecosystem designation is "open water" -- the second aerial/open ecosystem after the martin's "open sky near water" at degree 26. The progression:

| Degree | Ecosystem | Dimension |
|---|---|---|
| 25 | Lawn and woodland | Terrestrial surface |
| 26 | Open sky near water | Air column above water |
| **27** | **Open water** | **Air column over water + cliff/rock** |

The shift from "open sky near water" to "open water" is subtle but meaningful. The Purple Martin's ecosystem emphasizes the sky -- the three-dimensional air column. The Violet-green's ecosystem emphasizes the water surface itself -- the bird forages lower, closer to the water, often skimming just above the surface to intercept insects emerging from aquatic larval stages. The Violet-green also forages along cliff faces and over mountain lakes, extending the "open" ecosystem into vertical and montane dimensions.

The ecosystem is getting more open. Degree 25 was lawn and woodland (human-managed surface with trees). Degree 26 was open sky near water (air column, no vegetation). Degree 27 is open water (air column over water surface, extending to cliff faces). The vegetation structure that defined every ecosystem through degree 24 has now been absent for three consecutive degrees. The engine is in its most open phase. The landscape is water, rock, and air.

## The Western Specialty: More Common, Less Known

### Distribution and Abundance

The Violet-green Swallow is a **western endemic** -- its breeding range is restricted to western North America, from Alaska through British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, California, and the Rocky Mountain states to Mexico. It does not breed east of the Great Plains. In the PNW, it is one of the most common swallows -- substantially more numerous than the Purple Martin.

Population estimates:

- **Breeding Bird Survey data:** The Violet-green Swallow is among the top 5 most frequently detected swallow species in Washington and Oregon survey routes.
- **Approximate PNW breeding population:** Tens of thousands of breeding pairs across western Washington and Oregon, compared to the martin's 800-1,000 pairs. The Violet-green outnumbers the martin by roughly 20:1 in the PNW.
- **Partners in Flight estimate:** Approximately 7.7 million breeding individuals continent-wide.

Despite being far more numerous than the Purple Martin, the Violet-green Swallow is less well-known to the general public. The reasons are instructive:

1. **Habitat.** The martin nests in conspicuous locations -- harbors, marinas, waterfronts where people gather. The Violet-green nests in cliff crevices, forest snags, and mountain outcrops where fewer people go. Visibility drives familiarity.

2. **Conservation story.** The martin has a compelling narrative: near-extirpation, nest box recovery, ongoing management. The Violet-green has no such drama. It is simply present, year after year, in its natural habitats. Self-sufficiency is less newsworthy than dependency.

3. **Sound.** The martin's rich gurgling song is loud, distinctive, and memorable. The Violet-green's thin twitter is easily overlooked, especially in environments with ambient noise. The quiet bird is the forgotten bird.

4. **Size.** The martin is large enough to attract attention (wingspan 39-41 cm). The Violet-green is small enough to be mistaken for a generic "little bird" by casual observers (wingspan 27-28 cm). Size drives recognition.

The Violet-green Swallow is the PNW's most common swallow in many habitats -- forests, mountains, cliffs, clearings, residential areas with mature trees -- yet it lives in the Purple Martin's shadow in public awareness. The engine records it here: the quiet, common, self-sufficient one.

### Migration

The Violet-green Swallow is a **neotropical migrant** -- it breeds in the PNW from April through August and winters in Mexico and Central America. The spring arrival is typically mid-March to early April in the Puget Sound lowlands, with higher-elevation breeders arriving later (May-June at alpine sites). Fall departure occurs from late August through September.

The migration is notable for the Violet-green's habit of migrating during the day, often in loose flocks mixed with other swallow species. On spring and fall migration days, observers at mountain passes and along major river corridors may see streams of swallows flowing through -- Violet-greens mixed with Tree Swallows, Barn Swallows, and Cliff Swallows. The mixed-species swallow migration is one of the great spectacles of PNW birding, and the Violet-green is typically the most numerous participant.

## The Swallow Trio: Middle Movement

### The Gradient Deepens

At degree 27, the swallow trio's structure becomes clear. The Purple Martin at degree 26 established the family baseline: the largest member, the most colonial, the lowest-pitched, the most conservation-dependent. The Violet-green Swallow at degree 27 provides the **contrast point** -- the opposite extreme on nearly every axis:

| Dimension | Purple Martin (26) | Violet-green Swallow (27) | Gradient |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mass | 45-60 g | 14 g | Heavy -> Light |
| Coloniality | Obligate colonial | Solitary/paired | Social -> Independent |
| Pitch | Low-mid (2-6 kHz peak) | High (5-8 kHz peak) | Low -> High |
| Sound character | Continuous liquid gurgle | Discrete liquid chips | Stream -> Drops |
| Nest dependency | Human-built boxes | Natural cavities | Dependent -> Self-sufficient |
| Altitude range | Sea level | Valley to alpine | Narrow -> Wide |
| Conservation need | Critical management | None | Maximum -> Minimum |
| Plumage (male) | All dark iridescent | Green above, white below | Uniform -> Contrasting |
| Known to public | Moderately well | Poorly | More -> Less |

The Purple Martin and Violet-green Swallow are as different as two Hirundinidae can be while still sharing the fundamental swallow body plan: long pointed wings, aerial insectivory, cavity nesting, neotropical migration. The family unity is in the bauplan; the species diversity is in everything else.

The Tree Swallow at degree 28 will occupy the **middle ground** -- medium mass, semi-colonial, moderate pitch, loosely dependent on nest boxes but also using natural cavities. The trio's structure is bookend extremes (martin and Violet-green) with a centrist (Tree Swallow) in between.

### Within-Family Variation vs. Between-Family Convergence

The swallow trio (26-27-28) inverts the convergence trio (23-24-25):

- **Convergence trio:** Three species from two families (Cardinalidae, Turdidae) that sounded alike. The question was: how do different lineages produce similar sounds?
- **Swallow trio:** Three species from one family (Hirundinidae) that sound different. The question is: how does one lineage produce different sounds?

The answer, at degree 27, is: **body size drives acoustic divergence**. The Violet-green Swallow's small body produces a higher-pitched, thinner, faster sound than the martin's large body. The syrinx is smaller. The resonant cavities are smaller. The fundamental frequencies are higher. The chips are shorter because the smaller syringeal muscles complete their contractions faster. Physics dictates that smaller instruments produce higher sounds -- a piccolo vs. a cello, a tin whistle vs. a bass flute. The swallow trio IS this physical law expressed in biology: same instrument design (swallow syrinx), three sizes, three pitches, three timbres.

## Cross-References

### SPS Internal

- **SPS-026 (Purple Martin, degree 26):** WITHIN-FAMILY CONTRAST. Same family (Hirundinidae), maximum species-level contrast. Martin: large, colonial, low-pitched, liquid stream, sea-level, conservation-dependent. Violet-green: small, solitary, high-pitched, liquid chips, valley-to-alpine, self-sufficient. The heavy bomber and the fighter jet. The cello and the piccolo. Two swallows, two extremes.

- **SPS-018/019 (Swainson's Thrush, Veery, degrees 18-19):** SAME-GENUS CONSECUTIVE PAIR. Both pairs place two same-genus species in consecutive degrees. Catharus: ascending spiral (18) vs. descending spiral (19). Tachycineta: high twitter (27) vs. moderate twitter (28). Both pairs explore within-genus acoustic variation. The engine's finest taxonomic resolution.

- **SPS-023/024/025 (Convergence trio, degrees 23-25):** CONVERGENCE VS. DIVERGENCE. The convergence trio asked how different lineages produce similar sounds. The swallow trio asks how one lineage produces different sounds. Degrees 23-25 converged. Degrees 26-28 diverge.

- **SPS-008 (Golden-crowned Kinglet, degree 8):** HIGH-FREQUENCY COMPARISON. The kinglet's ultra-high zee-zee-zee (6-10 kHz) and the Violet-green's high twitter (5-8 kHz peak) are the two highest-pitched vocalizations in the engine. Both are small-bodied species whose size constrains them to high-frequency production. The kinglet was the canopy apex; the Violet-green is the aerial apex.

- **SPS-021/022 (Northern Flicker, degrees 21-22):** CAVITY ECOLOGY CONNECTION. The flicker excavates cavities. The Violet-green uses old woodpecker holes as nest sites. The flicker's work at degree 21-22 creates the housing that the Violet-green occupies at degree 27. The cavity economy connects woodpeckers and swallows across six degrees.

- **SPS-004 (Spotted Owl, degree 4):** CAVITY NESTING, SELF-SUFFICIENT MODEL. Both use natural cavities. Both are secondary cavity nesters. But the owl's cavities are in old-growth forest (rare, threatened), while the Violet-green's cavities span from snags to cliff crevices (diverse, abundant). The Violet-green represents the cavity nester that found enough options to thrive without help.
