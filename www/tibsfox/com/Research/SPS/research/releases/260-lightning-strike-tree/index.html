The Physics of the Strike

When a lightning bolt strikes a tree, the return stroke current (10,000-100,000+ amperes) flows preferentially through the water in the tree's sap channels. That water heats to steam in a fraction of a millisecond. Steam at atmospheric pressure occupies 1,700 times the volume of liquid water. The expansion is explosive — it blows the bark and wood outward from the trunk in a spiral pattern that follows the sap channel geometry. The spiral scar visible on lightning-struck trees is the physical record of this path: the explosion followed the water, and the water followed the cambium's spiral.

"At 10-30 meters from a lightning strike on a tree, the peak sound pressure level exceeds 150 dB SPL — above the threshold of pain (120 dB), approaching permanent hearing damage from a single exposure. Trees, Lightning, and PNW Forest Ecology

Douglas Fir: Most Common Target

Dominant tree in Cascade foothills and Olympic mid-elevation forest. High moisture content. Large Douglas fir struck: crown blown off, bark stripped in spiral down trunk, sapwood shattered. Damage visible from hundreds of meters away.

The Lightning Scar

A tree that survives develops a spiral groove from crown to ground: the physical record of the steam explosion following the sap channels. Old-growth Cascade and Olympic forests contain many lightning-scarred veterans.

Snag Creation

Trees killed but not felled become snags: standing dead trees essential to old-growth ecology. Dozens of bird species (woodpeckers, owls, raptors) nest in snag cavities. Lightning creates ecological infrastructure.

Fire Ignition

Small percentage of strikes ignite fires (dry summer conditions, East Cascades especially). Lightning fire discovered within 24-48 hours from lookout towers. The E=9 acoustic event can be the beginning of a fire that burns for weeks.
