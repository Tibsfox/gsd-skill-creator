## THE PHYSICS OF LIGHTNING STRIKING A TREE

### What Happens in the Milliseconds of a Strike

A lightning bolt strikes a tree and the physics are specific and violent:

- **The return stroke.** The visible lightning flash is not the initial discharge but the return stroke: after the stepped leader (an invisible, incrementally advancing ionized channel from cloud to ground) makes contact with the ground or a tall object, a massive return stroke of electrical current (up to 30,000 amperes at peak) surges upward from the ground through the channel at approximately one-third the speed of light. This return stroke is the event that produces the thunder (at degree 259) and the strike sound (at degree 260).
- **Current through the tree.** When a lightning bolt strikes a tree, the return stroke current flows through the tree along paths of least electrical resistance -- the water in the living cells and the sap in the phloem tissue. Water is a good conductor; wood is a poor conductor. The current preferentially follows the moisture content of the tree.
- **Steam explosion.** The electrical current (typically 10,000-30,000 amperes, sometimes exceeding 100,000 amperes in exceptional strikes) heats the water in the tree's cells and sap channels to steam in a fraction of a millisecond. Water expanding to steam at atmospheric pressure increases in volume by a factor of approximately 1,700. This expansion is explosive and extremely rapid -- it is the source of the characteristic explosive crack of a tree strike.
- **The explosive crack.** The steam explosion blows the bark, wood, and cambium outward from the tree trunk along the path of the sap channels. In a close-range observation, the crack of a lightning strike on a tree is among the loudest sounds a human can experience without protective equipment. Peak sound pressure levels at close range (10-30 meters) can exceed 150-160 dB SPL. This is above the threshold of pain (120 dB) and approaching the threshold of permanent hearing damage in a single exposure.
- **The crackle.** After the initial explosive crack, the crackle that follows is the sound of wood continuing to split and fall: the steam channels continue to propagate through the wood fibers for fractions of a second after the initial explosion; bark and wood chunks fall to the ground; the struck limbs or sections of trunk continue to crack and settle. The crackle is the tree's structural response to the internal explosion.

## WHAT THE STRIKE SOUNDS LIKE

### The Complete Acoustic Event

A lightning strike on a tree in a forest produces a complete acoustic sequence:

- **The air strike.** Simultaneously with the tree strike, the air surrounding the return stroke channel expands explosively (as documented at degree 259 for the thunder). At close range -- when the strike is on a tree within 10-30 meters -- the air strike and the tree strike arrive simultaneously or within milliseconds. The combined sound is the maximum intensity acoustic event: both the air and the tree are producing explosive sound at the same moment.
- **The explosive crack (0 milliseconds to 10 milliseconds).** The instant of the strike. An explosive, sharp crack of extreme amplitude. Not like any other natural sound in the engine. The closest analogy in the S36 side of the engine would be a gunshot at close range, but the lightning strike crack is wider in frequency and more complex in character -- it is not a percussion instrument but a full-waveform explosion.
- **The crackle (10 milliseconds to 3 seconds).** As the steam channels propagate through the wood, as bark and wood fragments fall, as the tree's structure responds to the internal explosion, the crackling sound continues. The crackle is the aftermath -- the tree processing the energy that has just been delivered.
- **The crash (0.5 to 3 seconds after strike).** In strikes that blow off major limbs or fell the entire tree, the crash of the falling wood is audible on top of the crackle. Large limbs falling through surrounding trees produce additional impact sounds. The crash can last several seconds.
- **The secondary silence.** After the immediate acoustic event, there is often a moment of secondary silence -- the forest's other sounds (bird calls, wind, rain) are momentarily absent or seem to be, as the struck area processes the event. Then the forest returns.
- **Ozone.** The smell of ozone (O₃), produced by the lightning's ionization of atmospheric oxygen, is the olfactory signature of a close lightning strike. The ozone smell arrives within seconds of the acoustic event and persists for minutes. The smell is inseparable from the sound in the full sensory experience of a close lightning strike.

(Sources: Air strike simultaneous with tree strike at close range; explosive crack 0-10 milliseconds; crackle 10 milliseconds to 3 seconds; crash of falling wood; secondary silence; ozone production by lightning ionization; full sensory experience)

## LIGHTNING AND TREES IN PNW FORESTS

### Which Species, Which Damage, Which Ecology

Not all trees respond to lightning strikes the same way:

- **Species susceptibility.** Trees with high moisture content and tall straight form are most susceptible to lightning strikes: Douglas fir, Sitka spruce, and western red cedar are among the most commonly struck in PNW forests. Trees with high sap content (conifers generally) conduct electricity well along sap channels. Deciduous trees (alder, maple) are also struck but represent a smaller proportion of tall trees in PNW forests.
- **Douglas fir strikes.** Douglas fir is the dominant tree in the Cascade foothills and the Olympic Peninsula mid-elevation forests. A large Douglas fir (150-200 feet, several hundred years old) hit by a major lightning strike can have the entire upper third of its crown blown off, the bark stripped in a spiral down the trunk (following the sap channel pattern), and the sapwood shattered. The physical damage is visible at great distance; the acoustic event at the moment of strike is audible at distances of several hundred meters.
- **The lightning scar.** A tree that survives a lightning strike (not all do) develops a characteristic lightning scar: a spiral groove in the bark and wood running from crown to ground, following the path of the steam explosion. Old-growth forests in the Cascades and Olympics contain many lightning-scarred trees -- veterans of previous storms who have incorporated the strike into their structural history.
- **Snag creation.** Trees killed by lightning strikes but not immediately felled become snags -- standing dead trees that are among the most ecologically important structures in old-growth forest. Snags provide nesting cavities for dozens of bird species (woodpeckers, owls, small raptors), roosting habitat for bats, and habitat for invertebrates. The lightning strike is, ecologically, not only a destructive event but a snag-creation event.
- **Fire ignition.** A small percentage of lightning strikes ignite fires. The ignition requires that the lightning energy deposit sufficient heat in combustible fuel (fine dry debris around the tree base, dry moss, dead bark) to sustain combustion after the strike. In dry summer conditions -- which characterize the East Cascades in July-August -- ignition probability is higher. Most fires ignited by lightning are discovered within 24-48 hours because they produce smoke visible from fire lookout towers.

(Sources: Species susceptibility: Douglas fir, Sitka spruce, western red cedar; high moisture content and sap channels; large Douglas fir damage: crown loss, spiral bark stripping; lightning scar spiral groove; old-growth lightning scar veterans; snag creation from lightning kills; snag as ecological structure for cavity nesters; fire ignition probability; dry summer East Cascades; fire lookout tower discovery)
