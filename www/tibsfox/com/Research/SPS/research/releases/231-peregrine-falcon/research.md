## FIRST FALCONIDAE -- THE FAMILY SHIFT

### A New Family, A Different Architecture

The transition from Accipitridae to Falconidae at degree 231 is the raptor arc's most significant structural event:

- **Falconidae vs. Accipitridae -- not closely related.** This is one of ornithology's most important recent findings: Falconidae and Accipitridae are not sister families. They evolved their raptor body plan independently. Falcons (Falconidae) are more closely related to parrots (Psittaciformes) and songbirds (Passeriformes) than they are to hawks and eagles (Accipitriformes). The shared morphology -- sharp beak, talons, binocular vision -- is convergent evolution, not shared ancestry.
- **Molecular phylogenetics.** The 2008 large-scale molecular phylogeny of birds (Hackett et al. *Science*) placed falcons in Australaves, the clade that also contains parrots and passerines, rather than in Accipitriformes where hawks and eagles belong. This is counterintuitive but robustly supported by multiple subsequent molecular analyses.
- **What Falconidae shares with Accipitriformes (and why).** Both evolved:
  - Hooked beak for tearing flesh
  - Sharp talons for catching prey
  - Forward-facing eyes for binocular vision and depth perception
  - High metabolic rate
  - Territorial behavior and complex vocalizations
  All of these are convergent adaptations -- they solve the same ecological problem (aerial predation on vertebrates) with the same structural solutions, arrived at independently through two separate evolutionary lineages.
- **The notch.** Falcons have a distinctive notch (tomial tooth) on the upper mandible -- a sharp projection near the tip of the beak that allows them to dispatch prey by severing the spinal cord at the neck. Accipitridae do not have this tooth. The tomial tooth is one of the clearest morphological distinctions between the two families. Falcons are the only raptors with this structure.
- **Wing shape.** Falcons have long, pointed wings (high aspect ratio) adapted for speed. Accipitridae hawks (buteos, accipiters) have broader, rounded wings adapted for different flight modes. The wing shape is the field identification signature: pointed = falcon, rounded/broad = hawk.

(Sources: Falconidae vs. Accipitriformes evolutionary independence; Hackett et al. 2008 molecular phylogeny; Australaves clade; convergent evolution; tomial tooth; wing shape comparison; independent raptor body plan evolution)

## THE PEREGRINE FALCON -- *FALCO PEREGRINUS*

### The Most Cosmopolitan Raptor

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Falconiformes. Family: Falconidae. Genus: *Falco*. Species: *peregrinus*. The name: *peregrinus* is Latin for wanderer, traveler, foreigner -- the falcon that moves. This is etymologically apt: the Peregrine Falcon is the most cosmopolitan bird of prey in the world, with breeding populations on every continent except Antarctica.
- **Subspecies.** The Peregrine Falcon is one of the most subspecifically diverse raptors, with 17-19 recognized subspecies globally. In the PNW, two subspecies occur: *F. p. anatum* (American Peregrine, breeds widely across North America, historically the subspecies most affected by DDT) and *F. p. pealei* (Peale's Peregrine, resident on coastal islands and the outer coast, larger and darker, less affected by DDT because it feeds primarily on seabirds rather than DDT-accumulating songbirds).
- **Size.** Females (larger, as in all raptors) weigh 910-1,500 g, wingspan 95-115 cm. Males are substantially smaller: 424-750 g, similar wingspan. The size difference (sexual dimorphism) in falcons is among the most pronounced in the raptor world.
- **Appearance.** Adults: slate-gray back and wings, barred pale underparts, dark "helmet" (dark crown and ear coverts forming a mask pattern), yellow cere and eye ring, yellow feet. Juvenile: brown above, streaked (not barred) below. The adult's dark helmet is the Peregrine's most distinctive facial feature -- it reduces glare from bright skies and may enhance visual acuity during the stoop.

(Sources: Falco peregrinus taxonomy; peregrinus etymology; subspecies count 17-19; F. p. anatum; F. p. pealei; size data; plumage description; dark helmet function)

## THE STOOP -- FASTEST ANIMAL ON EARTH

### 240+ mph -- The Physics of the Dive

The Peregrine Falcon's stoop is the fastest self-powered movement of any animal on Earth:

- **The stoop defined.** The stoop is the Peregrine's hunting dive -- a high-altitude, vertical-to-angled dive at prey (typically other birds in flight). The falcon climbs to altitude (sometimes 1,000 m / 3,000+ ft above prey), folds its wings partially or fully, and dives at terminal velocity toward the target. At full stoop, the bird becomes essentially a feathered projectile.
- **The speed record.** The fastest measured Peregrine stoop: 389 km/h (242 mph), recorded in 2005 by researcher Ken Franklin using a specially instrumented falcon (with speed-measuring equipment attached). Other measurements range from 320-389 km/h (200-242 mph) depending on measurement method, dive angle, and atmospheric conditions. The consensus scientific range for maximum stoop speed is 240-320+ km/h (150-200+ mph).
- **Comparison.** For reference: a Golden Eagle stoop: ~300 km/h (190 mph). A cheetah (fastest land animal): ~112 km/h (70 mph). A spine-tailed swift (fastest sustained level flight of any bird): ~170 km/h (106 mph). The Peregrine in a stoop is faster than all of these. It is the fastest self-powered movement in the animal kingdom.
- **Aerodynamic engineering.** The Peregrine's body shape at full stoop is highly aerodynamic:
  - Pointed wings folded close to the body
  - Head held to minimize drag (nictitating membrane covers the eye, preventing wind-blast damage while maintaining vision)
  - Tear-shaped body profile optimizing for drag reduction
  - Nostrils with bony tubercles (small projections) that slow the incoming air, preventing lung damage at extreme speed
- **The kill.** The Peregrine typically does not grab prey with its feet during the stoop at full speed -- the impact would break its own bones. Instead, it strikes prey with closed feet (a clenched fist strike), stunning or killing it, then catches it in the air or follows it to the ground. At lower speeds, it may grasp prey directly.

(Sources: Peregrine stoop speed; Ken Franklin 2005 measurement; 389 km/h record; wing-fold aerodynamics; nictitating membrane protection; nostril tubercles; kill technique; comparison to other fast animals; cheetah comparison; swift comparison)

## THE ALARM CALL -- kak-kak-kak-kak, E=7

### A Falcon Sound, Not a Hawk Sound

The Peregrine's call is distinctly different from the Accipitridae calls documented in degrees 221-230:

- **The sound.** The primary alarm call is a rapid, harsh series of kak notes: kak-kak-kak-kak or kek-kek-kek-kek. The notes are short (0.1-0.2 sec), staccato, uniform in pitch, repeated 4-20+ times in rapid succession. The effect is urgent, mechanical, sharp -- nothing like the Red-tailed Hawk's sustained scream or the Bald Eagle's thin kee-kee-kee.
- **Frequency.** The kak call operates in approximately 3,000-8,000 Hz -- higher than most Accipitridae alarm calls. The high frequency and rapid repetition give the call a distinctive rattling quality.
- **The Falconidae call character.** Falcons as a family tend toward this staccato, rapidly repeated call structure. Compare: American Kestrel (*Falco sparverius*, which will appear later in the engine) produces a series of rapid klee-klee-klee notes at even higher pitch. The staccato repetition is the family's vocal signature.
- **Context.** The kak call is produced: at nest sites when disturbed, during territorial disputes with other raptors, and during hunting when prey has been spotted. Nesting Peregrines are extremely aggressive defenders of the nest ledge, calling repeatedly and dive-bombing intruders.
- **Volume and carrying power.** E=7 is correct. The call carries well across urban canyons and cliff faces. A Peregrine defending its skyscraper nest site can be heard from street level below -- the kak-kak-kak-kak cutting through ambient city noise.

(Sources: Peregrine Falcon vocalization; kak alarm call frequency; staccato character; Falconidae call family signature; kestrel comparison; nest defense behavior; urban carrying power)

## ECOLOGY -- CLIFF AND CITY

### Two Habitats, One Nesting Requirement

The Peregrine Falcon's ecological flexibility -- cliff face or city skyscraper -- is based on a single structural requirement:

- **The nesting requirement.** Peregrines need a high ledge with an open hunting radius. On natural cliff faces (sea cliffs, river canyons, mountain escarpments), this means a narrow rocky ledge on a vertical face, typically with a 100+ m drop below. On buildings, this means a window sill, ventilation ledge, or purpose-built nest box on an upper floor of a tall building. The functional requirement is identical.
- **Natural cliff habitat in PNW.** The Columbia River Gorge (basalt cliffs), the Olympic Peninsula sea cliffs, the Cascades' rocky escarpments, and coastal headlands are all natural Peregrine nesting sites. The species recolonized these sites following the DDT recovery.
- **Urban habitat.** The Peregrine Falcon is one of the most successful urban colonizers among raptors. Major PNW cities with established urban Peregrine pairs: Seattle (multiple pairs nesting on downtown high-rises and the University of Washington campus), Portland (pairs on downtown buildings), Tacoma, and others. The urban Peregrine preys primarily on pigeons, European starlings, and other urban-adapted birds -- essentially an unlimited food supply. The city is a better food environment than many natural cliff sites.
- **Hunting technique.** The stoop is the primary hunting technique for aerial prey. But Peregrines also:
  - Pursue prey in level flight (high-speed chase)
  - Flush prey by repeated passes and then stoop
  - Pirate prey from other raptors (kleptoparasitism, like the Bald Eagle stealing from Osprey)
- **Prey spectrum.** Birds are the primary prey -- the Peregrine is the quintessential avian predator of flying birds. Pigeons, ducks, shorebirds, songbirds, starlings -- any bird from 10g (a sparrow) to several kg (a mallard) is potential prey. The size range is extraordinary.

(Sources: Peregrine cliff nesting requirement; Columbia River Gorge; Olympic Peninsula; urban colonization; Seattle urban Peregrine pairs; University of Washington nest; pigeon prey base; stoop and pursuit techniques; prey size range)

## THE DDT STORY -- CRISIS AND RECOVERY

### From Extinction Risk to Urban Skyscraper

The Peregrine Falcon's conservation story is the North American raptor arc's most dramatic:

- **The DDT collapse.** DDT accumulated in the Peregrine's food chain more severely than in most raptors because the Peregrine eats birds, not fish or rodents. Songbirds that had accumulated DDT from insecticide-treated fields were eaten by Peregrines, concentrating DDT further. The effects were severe: by 1970, the eastern North American subspecies (*F. p. anatum*) had been extirpated east of the Mississippi River. In the western US and PNW, populations collapsed to critically low levels. Breeding surveys in the early 1970s found fewer than 100 breeding pairs in the contiguous US.
- **Captive breeding and hacking.** The Peregrine's recovery was accomplished through one of the most ambitious captive breeding and release programs in wildlife history. Tom Cade and the Cornell Laboratory of Ornithology established the Peregrine Fund in 1970. Captive breeding produced thousands of young falcons. "Hacking" -- the process of acclimating captive-bred birds to wild conditions -- released them at historical nest sites and urban buildings. The program placed over 6,000 captive-bred Peregrines across North America between 1974 and 1999.
- **Urban reintroduction.** A crucial insight of the recovery program was that Peregrines could successfully breed on buildings. Urban reintroductions were often more successful than cliff-site reintroductions because buildings offered abundant prey (urban pigeon populations), safety from ground predators, and reduced human disturbance (no hikers near high-rise nests). The urban Peregrine was an accidental discovery of the recovery program.
- **Delisting.** The Peregrine Falcon (*F. p. anatum*) was removed from the US Endangered Species List in 1999 -- a full recovery after the 1970s near-extinction. Current US population: approximately 3,000+ breeding pairs in the contiguous US, with the western population (including PNW) among the healthiest.
- **IUCN.** Least Concern globally. Worldwide population estimated at 140,000 individuals (all subspecies).

(Sources: DDT impact on Peregrine; eastern anatum extirpation by 1970; Tom Cade; Peregrine Fund; Cornell Laboratory; captive breeding program; hacking technique; 6,000+ released 1974-1999; urban reintroduction discovery; 1999 delisting; current population; IUCN status)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST PEREGRINES

### Two Subspecies, Two Habitats

The PNW hosts two subspecies with distinct ecologies:

- ***F. p. anatum* -- American Peregrine.** The continental subspecies. Breeds at inland cliff sites (Columbia Gorge, Cascades) and urban buildings. This is the subspecies most affected by DDT and most thoroughly recovered through the Peregrine Fund program. Medium-sized, medium-dark plumage.
- ***F. p. pealei* -- Peale's Peregrine.** The outer coast specialist. Resident on the outer coast, the San Juan Islands, the Olympic Peninsula coast, and coastal British Columbia. Larger and darker than *anatum*. Feeds primarily on seabirds (murres, auklets, puffins, pigeons) -- prey that did not accumulate DDT as severely as songbirds, which is why Peale's Peregrines were less severely impacted by the DDT era.
- **Seattle Peregrines.** Seattle has had urban Peregrine Falcons since the 1990s recovery. Known nest sites have included the Washington Mutual Tower (now Chase Tower), the University of Washington Campanile, and other downtown high-rises. Seattle birders and urban wildlife watchers track nest sites actively. The annual spectacle of Peregrine chicks learning to fly from downtown skyscrapers is one of Seattle's most unusual wildlife events.

(Sources: F. p. anatum PNW distribution; F. p. pealei coast specialist; Peale's seabird diet; DDT differential impact; Seattle urban Peregrine sites; UW Campanile; downtown nest boxes; chick fledging spectacle)
