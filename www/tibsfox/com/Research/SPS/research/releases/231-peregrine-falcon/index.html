Peregrine Falcon

Falco peregrinus — Falconidae
kak-kak-kak-kak — cliff and city — E=7 — resident — FIRST FALCONIDAE — FASTEST ANIMAL ON EARTH

FIRST FALCONIDAE — A NEW FAMILY ENTERS

Degree 231 is the engine's FIRST FALCONIDAE. After ten degrees of Accipitridae (221-230), the engine crosses into its second diurnal raptor family. Falconidae and Accipitridae are not closely related. Falcons evolved independently from hawks and eagles — they are more closely related to parrots than to Red-tailed Hawks. The shared raptor body plan is convergent evolution: two lineages solving the same problem with the same solutions. The Peregrine Falcon is the correct entry: fastest animal on Earth. The stoop at 240 mph.

THE STOOP — FASTEST SELF-POWERED MOVEMENT IN THE ANIMAL KINGDOM

389 km/h (242 mph) — measured stoop speed (Ken Franklin, 2005)

Consensus scientific range: 240-320+ km/h (150-200+ mph) depending on dive angle and conditions. For comparison: cheetah (fastest land animal) = 112 km/h. Golden Eagle stoop = ~300 km/h. Spine-tailed swift (fastest sustained level flight) = 170 km/h. The Peregrine stoop exceeds all of them.

The Alarm Call — kak-kak-kak-kak, E=7

The Peregrine's call is immediately recognizable as not an Accipitridae sound. Rapid, staccato, harsh: kak-kak-kak-kak or kek-kek-kek-kek. The notes are short (0.1-0.2 sec), uniform in pitch, repeated 4-20+ times. The staccato repetition is the Falconidae family's vocal signature — fundamentally different from the buteos' sustained screams and the eagles' thin descending series.

Acoustic Profile — Alarm Call

Frequency: 3,000–8,000 Hz (higher than all Accipitridae in arc)
Rate: rapid staccato, 4-20+ notes in quick succession
Character: harsh, rattling, urgent, carrying

E=7. Audible from street level when Peregrine is defending a skyscraper nest. The kak-kak-kak cuts through urban ambient noise. Not the sustained scream of the Red-tail, not the thin kee-kee-kee of the Bald Eagle. A new family, a new call character.

"Falcons are not closely related to hawks. They are more closely related to parrots. The Peregrine's shared morphology with Accipitridae is convergent evolution — two lineages arriving independently at the same aerodynamic solution."

The Aerodynamics of the Stoop

At full stoop, the Peregrine becomes a feathered projectile. Wings folded partially or fully against the body. The nictitating membrane (third eyelid) closes over the eye, preventing wind-blast damage while maintaining vision. Bony tubercles (small projections) in the nostrils slow the incoming air, preventing lung damage. The body profile becomes tear-shaped, minimizing drag. The falcon does not grab prey at full stoop speed — the impact force would break its own bones. It strikes with closed feet (a clenched-fist blow), stunning the prey, then catches it in the air or follows it down.

Cliff and City — One Requirement

The Peregrine's ecological flexibility is based on a single structural requirement: a high ledge with an unobstructed hunt radius. On natural cliff faces (Columbia River Gorge basalt, Olympic Peninsula sea cliffs, Cascades escarpments), a rocky ledge works. On buildings, a window sill or ventilation ledge on an upper floor works. The functional requirement is identical. The city provides additional advantages: abundant urban pigeon prey, protection from ground predators, and reduced hiker disturbance compared to cliff sites. Seattle has multiple established urban Peregrine pairs nesting on downtown high-rises.

Conservation — From Near-Extinction to Recovery

RECOVERY COMPLETE — DELISTED 1999. DDT nearly erased the eastern North American Peregrine (*F. p. anatum*): fewer than 100 breeding pairs in the contiguous US by the early 1970s; eastern population extirpated east of the Mississippi. The Peregrine Fund (Tom Cade, Cornell) released 6,000+ captive-bred birds 1974-1999. Urban reintroduction proved unexpectedly successful. Delisted 1999. Current US population: 3,000+ breeding pairs. IUCN: Least Concern. ~140,000 worldwide.

Two PNW Subspecies

F. p. anatum (American Peregrine): continental subspecies, inland cliff sites and urban buildings, most affected by DDT, most thoroughly recovered. F. p. pealei (Peale's Peregrine): outer coast specialist, San Juan Islands to outer Olympic Peninsula, larger and darker, feeds primarily on seabirds (less DDT accumulation), less affected by the DDT era.

The Tomial Tooth — Structural Distinction

Falcons have a distinctive notch (tomial tooth) on the upper mandible near the beak tip. This sharp projection allows falcons to dispatch prey by severing the spinal cord at the neck. Accipitridae hawks and eagles do not have this tooth. It is one of the clearest morphological distinctions between the two families, and it is present in every member of Falconidae.
