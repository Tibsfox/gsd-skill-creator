The Northern Pygmy-Owl is the PNW's smallest owl and one of its most fearsome -- it hunts prey its own size or larger, and other birds mob it on sight.

## E=3 -- THE PERSISTENT WHISTLE

### Why E=3

The Northern Pygmy-Owl registers at E=3 -- the third consecutive E=3 entry:

- **Hollow tooting:** The Pygmy-Owl's primary call is a series of evenly spaced, hollow "toot" notes -- similar in structure to the Saw-whet Owl's tooting but slower, more deliberate, and slightly lower-pitched. The call is soft and carries a moderate distance through open forest.
- **Slower tempo:** At approximately 1 note per 2 seconds (0.5 notes/sec), the Pygmy-Owl's tooting is much slower than the Saw-whet's metronomic 2 notes/sec. The slower pace gives each note more weight but reduces overall energy.
- **Moderate amplitude:** Similar to the Saw-whet and Screech-Owl -- audible at moderate distances but not projecting across landscapes. A forest-scale call, not a landscape-scale announcement.
- **E=3 calibration:** Three consecutive E=3 entries at 141-142-143 represent the engine's quietest sustained stretch. The energy has descended from the E=6 peak at degree 138 through five degrees of gradual reduction.

## THE FALSE EYES -- OCCIPITAL EYESPOTS

### Deception on the Back of the Head

The Northern Pygmy-Owl's most remarkable physical feature is its **occipital eyespots** -- a pair of dark spots bordered by white on the back of its head that resemble eyes:

- **False face:** When viewed from behind, the Pygmy-Owl appears to have a face looking back at you. The dark spots (with white "eyebrow" borders) create a convincing illusion of a pair of eyes on the nape. The effect is striking -- the owl appears to be facing you even when turned away.
- **Anti-predator function:** The prevailing hypothesis is that the false eyes deter predators (particularly raptors) by creating the impression that the owl is always watching. A predator approaching from behind might hesitate if it believes the prey is aware and alert. This "I see you" signal may reduce ambush success.
- **Anti-mobbing function:** Alternatively, the false eyes may reduce mobbing intensity. Songbirds that mob Pygmy-Owls (which they do vigorously) may be confused by the false eyes, uncertain whether they are approaching from behind or in front. The confusion may reduce the effectiveness and intensity of mobbing.
- **Widespread trait:** Occipital eyespots are found in several small owl species worldwide, suggesting convergent evolution. The trait is beneficial enough to have evolved independently multiple times.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Holt and Petersen 2000; Sibley 2014)

## FEARLESS PREDATOR -- HUNTING ABOVE ITS WEIGHT

### The Smallest Owl, the Biggest Attitude

The Northern Pygmy-Owl is the **smallest owl in the PNW** and one of its most aggressive predators:

- **Size:** 15-18 cm (6-7 inches). Weight: 54-73 g (2-2.6 oz). Smaller than the Saw-whet Owl. Smaller than a robin. About the size of a large sparrow.
- **Prey size:** Pygmy-Owls regularly hunt prey their own size or larger -- including songbirds (chickadees, sparrows, juncos), small mammals (mice, voles), and even birds like thrushes and warblers that outweigh them. A Pygmy-Owl catching a robin-sized bird is taking prey that weighs as much as it does.
- **Hunting method:** Active, fast pursuit through forest. Unlike most owls that hunt by stealth from a perch, the Pygmy-Owl is an active, agile flier that chases prey through the forest canopy and understory. Its long tail provides maneuverability for pursuit through branches.
- **Daylight hunting:** The Pygmy-Owl is **largely diurnal** -- it hunts during the day, especially at dawn and dusk but also in full daylight. This is highly unusual for owls and makes the Pygmy-Owl one of the most visible owls in the PNW. It is the second diurnal owl in the Strigidae run (after the Short-eared Owl at degree 140).
- **Caching:** Like the Saw-whet Owl, the Pygmy-Owl caches prey -- storing kills in tree crevices or wedging them in branch forks. A single Pygmy-Owl may have multiple cached kills at any time, sometimes accumulating a larder far in excess of immediate needs.

### The Mobbing Response

The Northern Pygmy-Owl triggers an **intense mobbing response** from songbirds:

- **Mobbing:** When songbirds discover a Pygmy-Owl, they mob it aggressively -- dive-bombing, alarm-calling, and surrounding the owl. The intensity of the mobbing response is extreme, often involving dozens of birds from multiple species.
- **Why so intense?** The Pygmy-Owl is a genuine, serious threat to songbirds. Unlike the Great Horned Owl (which is too large to catch most songbirds in flight) or the Saw-whet Owl (which hunts rodents), the Pygmy-Owl specifically targets songbirds. Other birds recognize it as an immediate, personal danger.
- **Birder's tool:** The intense mobbing response makes the Pygmy-Owl useful for birders. Playing Pygmy-Owl calls can attract mobbing songbirds from a wide area, revealing species that might otherwise remain hidden. This technique (used sparingly and ethically) leverages the birds' instinctive fear response.
- **Diurnal mobbing:** Because the Pygmy-Owl hunts during the day, the mobbing response occurs in daylight -- making it visible and audible to human observers. The chorus of alarm calls when a Pygmy-Owl is present is one of the most dramatic behavioral events in PNW forests.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Holt and Petersen 2000; Sibley 2014)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Deliberate, Slow Tooting in Open Forest

The Northern Pygmy-Owl's acoustic contribution is **deliberate minimalism** -- the slowest, most contemplative tooting in the Strigidae run:

- **Tooting series:** A series of evenly spaced, hollow "toot" notes at approximately 1 note per 2 seconds. Lower-pitched than the Saw-whet (~800 Hz vs. ~1000 Hz). Each note is a single, clean whistle. The spacing between notes is the defining feature -- much slower than the Saw-whet's rapid-fire tooting.
- **Duration:** Calling bouts of 30 seconds to several minutes. Not as sustained as the Saw-whet's hours-long tooting but more persistent than the Screech-Owl's brief trills.
- **Diurnal calling:** Because the Pygmy-Owl is diurnal, its tooting is heard during the day -- dawn, dusk, and sometimes midday. This is unique in the Strigidae run (the Short-eared Owl's bark + wing-clap at 140 is also diurnal, but the Pygmy-Owl's tooting is a more sustained, regular vocalization).
- **Trill variant:** In addition to the regular tooting, the Pygmy-Owl produces a rapid trill -- a burst of accelerating notes used in territorial encounters and courtship. This trill is higher energy than the tooting but heard less frequently.
- **Mobbing context:** The Pygmy-Owl's tooting often triggers immediate mobbing responses. The sound of a Pygmy-Owl calling is quickly drowned out by the alarm calls of surrounding songbirds. The owl's quiet tooting generates a disproportionately loud response.
