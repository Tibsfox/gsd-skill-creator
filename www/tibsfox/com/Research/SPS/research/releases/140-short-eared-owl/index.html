Short-eared Owl

Asio flammeus (Strigidae) — E=5 — Bark + Wing-Clap Display
Bark call + wing-clap display — E=5 multi-modal performance. Diurnal hunter — the most diurnal owl in the engine. Sixth consecutive Strigidae (135–140). Completes the Long-eared/Short-eared pair (139–140). Open grassland. Moth-like wingbeat. Declining populations.

Wing-Clap Display — Aerial Performance

The Short-eared Owl climbs to 60–100 meters, then claps its wings beneath its body in rapid succession — 5–20 claps per sequence. Combined with sharp “BARK! BARK! BARK!” calls. The most multi-modal display in the engine: vocal + mechanical + visual. E=5 combined impact.

Grassland Decline — Conservation Concern

Native grasslands are among the most converted habitats in North America. The Short-eared Owl — a ground nester dependent on open grassland — faces continental population decline. State species of concern in Washington. The habitat that produces this spectacular display is disappearing.

The Asio Comparison Pair (139–140)

Same genus, opposite everything. Long-eared: nocturnal, forest, secretive, prominent tufts, E=4. Short-eared: diurnal, grassland, visible, tiny tufts, E=5. The only shared trait: communal winter roosts. Yin and yang of the ear-tufted owls.
