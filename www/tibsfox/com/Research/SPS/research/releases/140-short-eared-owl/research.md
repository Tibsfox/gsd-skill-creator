## E=5 -- THE DISPLAY FLIGHT

### Why E=5

The Short-eared Owl registers at E=5 -- one step above the Long-eared Owl's E=4:

- **Wing-clap display:** The Short-eared Owl's courtship and territorial flight includes a spectacular **wing-clap display** -- the bird rises to 60-100 meters, then claps its wings **beneath its body** in rapid succession (5-20 claps) while descending. The clapping produces a distinctive audible "clap-clap-clap" that can be heard from hundreds of meters. This is a multi-sensory display: visual (the bird's silhouette against the sky) and auditory (the wing-claps). The combined impact pushes to E=5.
- **Bark call intensity:** The Short-eared Owl's bark call is a sharp, emphatic "BARK! BARK! BARK!" -- typically given during the display flight, often alternating with the wing-claps. The barks are louder and more percussive than the Long-eared Owl's deep pumping, with a carrying quality that projects across open grassland.
- **Diurnal visibility:** The Short-eared Owl hunts and displays in daylight, meaning its vocalizations and display flights are witnessed by humans far more often than those of nocturnal owls. The visual impact of the display amplifies the acoustic energy.
- **E=5 vs. E=4:** The Long-eared Owl's E=4 reflects minimal, spaced hooting. The Short-eared Owl's E=5 reflects the combined bark + wing-clap display, which is more complex, louder, and more visually dramatic. The energy step from 139 to 140 mirrors the step from GHO solo (E=4) to GHO pair (E=5) -- from simple to complex vocalization.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Wiggins et al. 2006; engine internal analysis)

## THE WING-CLAP DISPLAY -- SPECTACLE IN THE SKY

### Aerial Performance Art

The Short-eared Owl's wing-clap display is one of the most spectacular behavioral performances in PNW ornithology:

- **The flight:** The owl rises on its broad, long wings in a buoyant, moth-like flight. It climbs to 60-100 meters -- high enough to be a silhouette against the sky. The flight is exaggerated and theatrical, with deep, slow wingbeats that emphasize the bird's presence.
- **The clap:** At the peak of the display flight, the owl executes a rapid series of wing-claps -- bringing the wings together **beneath** its body, not above. The sound produced is a sharp, percussive "clap-clap-clap-clap-clap" that carries across open terrain. Each clap is produced by the wing surfaces striking each other below the body during a brief descent.
- **The descent:** After the wing-clapping sequence, the owl descends in a steep glide, sometimes with a dramatic swoop. The entire sequence -- climb, clap, descent -- is repeated multiple times during courtship and territorial display.
- **Number of claps:** Typically 5-20 claps per sequence, delivered rapidly. More claps may indicate greater fitness or motivation -- sexual selection may favor more elaborate displays.
- **Combined with bark:** The bark call often accompanies the display flight, creating a multi-modal performance: the visual spectacle of the moth-like flight, the audible clapping, and the sharp barks. This three-channel display is the most elaborate courtship performance of any PNW owl.

### The Moth-like Wingbeat

Beyond the display, the Short-eared Owl's regular hunting flight is distinctive:

- **Moth-like:** The wingbeat is described as "moth-like" -- buoyant, floppy, with long wings held in a shallow V. The flight looks effortless but covers ground efficiently. The moth-like quality is unmistakable -- once seen, the Short-eared Owl's flight style is immediately identifiable.
- **Low hunting flight:** Short-eared Owls hunt by flying low over open fields, typically 1-3 meters above the ground, scanning for voles and other small rodents. The slow, low, quartering flight pattern is reminiscent of a Northern Harrier.
- **Diurnal hunting:** The Short-eared Owl is one of the few owls that regularly hunts in daylight, especially during winter and at northern latitudes. In the PNW during winter, Short-eared Owls can be seen hunting over open fields in the late afternoon, creating some of the most accessible owl-watching opportunities in the region.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Wiggins et al. 2006; Sibley 2014)

## GRASSLAND HABITAT AND DECLINING POPULATIONS

### An Owl of Open Country

The Short-eared Owl occupies a habitat completely different from the forest owls documented in the previous five degrees:

- **Open grassland:** Short-eared Owls require extensive open grassland, prairie, marsh, or agricultural land. They nest on the ground -- one of the few owls to do so -- in shallow scrapes concealed by grass or low vegetation.
- **No forest needed:** Unlike the Great Horned Owl (forest and open), Barred Owl (dense forest), and Long-eared Owl (dense vegetation), the Short-eared Owl actively avoids dense forest. Its entire ecology is adapted to open country.
- **Vole dependency:** Short-eared Owls are heavily dependent on voles (Microtus spp.) as their primary prey. Their populations fluctuate dramatically with vole abundance -- in years of vole irruptions, Short-eared Owl numbers surge; when voles crash, owls decline or disperse.
- **PNW grasslands:** In western Washington, Short-eared Owls winter in remaining open habitats: agricultural fields, airfields, coastal marshes, and grassland remnants. Breeding in western Washington is rare -- most breeding occurs east of the Cascades in the sagebrush steppe and grassland habitats of the Columbia Basin.

### Population Decline

The Short-eared Owl faces serious **population decline** due to habitat loss:

- **Grassland conversion:** Native grasslands have been among the most converted habitats in North America. Agriculture, development, and fire suppression have dramatically reduced the open grassland and prairie habitats that Short-eared Owls require.
- **Ground nesting vulnerability:** As ground nesters, Short-eared Owls are vulnerable to agricultural activities (mowing, plowing), predation (by foxes, coyotes, ravens, raptors), and disturbance. Ground nests in agricultural fields are frequently destroyed during farming operations.
- **Continental decline:** Breeding Bird Survey data shows long-term population decline across North America. The Short-eared Owl has been identified as a species of conservation concern in multiple states and provinces.
- **PNW status:** In Washington state, the Short-eared Owl is a state-listed species of concern. Winter numbers have declined as open habitats in western Washington are developed. The species was historically more common before intensive development of the Puget Sound lowlands.

(Sources: Wiggins et al. 2006; WDFW; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; NABBS)

## THE LONG-EARED/SHORT-EARED COMPARISON -- THE ASIO PAIR

### Yin and Yang of the Ear-Tufted Owls

The Long-eared Owl (degree 139) and Short-eared Owl (degree 140) form the most complete **comparison pair** in the engine:

- **Same genus (Asio), opposite everything else.** Both are medium-sized, ear-tufted owls in the same genus. But their ecological strategies are diametrically opposed -- nocturnal vs. diurnal, forest vs. grassland, secretive vs. visible, prominent tufts vs. tiny tufts.
- **Ear tufts as diagnostic:** The "ears" (feather tufts) are the naming feature of both species, but they are dramatically different: the Long-eared Owl has conspicuous, elongated tufts that are a key field mark; the Short-eared Owl has tiny, barely visible tufts that are almost never seen in the field. Both species' "ears" are display feathers, not auditory structures.
- **Communal roosting:** Both species form communal winter roosts -- the one behavioral convergence in their otherwise divergent strategies. Communal roosts of Short-eared Owls in open grassland and Long-eared Owls in dense conifers represent the same social strategy adapted to different habitats.
- **Conservation trajectories:** Both species face conservation challenges, but for different reasons. Long-eared Owls are underdetected and their true status is uncertain. Short-eared Owls are declining due to measurable habitat loss. Both require targeted conservation attention.

(Sources: Marks et al. 1994; Wiggins et al. 2006; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Bark and Wing-Clap over Open Fields

The Short-eared Owl's acoustic contribution is **the most visual** of the Strigidae entries -- a sound inseparable from its display flight:

- **Bark call:** Sharp, emphatic "BARK! BARK! BARK!" -- typically 2-5 barks in rapid succession. Frequency range approximately 500-1500 Hz. The barks are percussive and aggressive, designed to carry across open terrain. Louder and sharper than the Long-eared Owl's deep pumping.
- **Wing-clap:** The sound of wings striking each other beneath the body: "clap-clap-clap-clap-clap." 5-20 claps per sequence, rapid-fire. The sound is percussive and rhythmic -- like rapid hand-clapping. Audible at 200-400 meters across open fields.
- **Combined display sound:** Bark + wing-clap creates a multi-layered acoustic display: the sharp barks establishing presence, the wing-claps adding rhythmic percussion. The combination is unique in the owl world -- no other PNW owl produces this type of combined vocal + mechanical display.
- **Diurnal context:** Because the display occurs in daylight, the acoustic and visual components are experienced simultaneously. The moth-like flight, the bark calls, and the wing-claps form a unified performance that is one of the most rewarding wildlife spectacles in the PNW.

### Acoustic Comparison: The Asio Pair

| Feature | Long-eared (139) | Short-eared (140) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Primary sound** | Deep "whooo" pumping | Bark + wing-clap |
| **Frequency** | 300-400 Hz | 500-1500 Hz |
| **Energy** | E=4 | E=5 |
| **Type** | Vocal only | Vocal + mechanical |
| **Rhythm** | Slow, regular, meditative | Rapid, percussive, dramatic |
| **Time** | Strictly nocturnal | Diurnal / crepuscular |
| **Habitat** | Dense forest | Open grassland |

The acoustic contrast is profound: the Long-eared Owl produces the engine's most minimal vocalization; the Short-eared Owl produces the engine's most multi-modal display (vocal + mechanical + visual).
