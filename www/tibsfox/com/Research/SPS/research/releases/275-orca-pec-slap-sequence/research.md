## THE PEC-SLAP: WHAT IT IS

### Pectoral Fin as Percussion Instrument

The pec-slap is one of the most acoustically distinctive behaviors in the orca's surface repertoire:

- **The anatomy.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale's pectoral fins are large, paddle-shaped, and highly mobile. Adult male pectoral fins can reach 1.8 meters in length; adult females proportionally somewhat smaller but still substantial. The fins are the orca's primary horizontal maneuvering appendages -- used for steering, braking, and social tactile contact (petting, touching other individuals).
- **The strike.** In a pec-slap, the orca rolls to one side and lifts a pectoral fin out of the water. The fin may be raised fully above the surface. The orca then brings the fin down against the water surface, creating a loud percussive crack. The fin's large flat surface area creates maximum acoustic impact per strike.
- **The sequence.** The pec-slap sequence (as opposed to a single slap) involves multiple strikes in succession, often with rhythmic spacing. The animal may slap 5, 10, 20 or more times in a sequence, with each slap producing a distinct crack. The sequence creates a rhythmic acoustic pattern -- body percussion.
- **The sound.** Each pec-slap creates a sharp crack at the surface, audible at significant distances in calm conditions. Underwater, the impact propagates as a broadband pressure wave. Multiple slaps in sequence create a rhythmic pattern of underwater sounds audible to other orcas at distance.
- **The roll.** The pec-slap typically involves the animal rolling to expose the pectoral fin -- the body partially on its side. This rolling posture, combined with the repeated fin strikes, creates a distinctive visual signature: the fin rising and falling against the water surface.

(Sources: Orca pectoral fin anatomy; pec-slap behavioral description; acoustic profile of pec-slaps; pec-slap sequence documentation; underwater sound propagation)

## FUNCTION: WHAT ARE THEY DOING?

### Social Percussion and Communication

- **Communication hypothesis.** The sound of a pec-slap propagates both above and below the water surface. The underwater component is detectable by other orcas at significant distances. Pec-slap sequences may serve as long-distance acoustic signals: announcing location, communicating behavioral state, or signaling something about the individual producing the sound.
- **Social/tactile context.** Pec-slapping often occurs in socially active contexts: when pods are aggregated, when there is apparent excitement or arousal in the group. The behavior may be a component of social bonding displays -- a way of expressing excitement or engagement within a social group.
- **Arousal and play.** Pec-slapping is associated with states of behavioral arousal: after a successful hunt, during socializing, in contexts that researchers associate with play. The behavior may be an expression of positive arousal -- analogous to how humans drum on surfaces when excited or engaged.
- **Sexual display.** In some contexts, pec-slapping appears more frequently in male orcas in social situations involving females. This suggests a possible role in sexual display or mate signaling.
- **Thermoregulation.** Some researchers have suggested that pec-slapping may have a thermoregulatory component -- the large vascular pectoral fins are important in temperature regulation, and slapping may increase blood flow to the fin surface. This hypothesis has less direct support than the acoustic communication hypothesis.
- **The rhythm.** The rhythmic quality of pec-slap sequences is notable. Unlike the chaotic, unpredictable timing of breach events, pec-slap sequences often have a consistent beat -- a regular interval between slaps. This rhythmic regularity is consistent with deliberate behavioral production rather than reflexive response.

(Sources: Pec-slap function hypotheses; acoustic communication; social context analysis; arousal and play in orcas; sexual display in cetaceans; thermoregulation hypothesis; rhythmic production in orca behavior)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE: E=7

### Rhythmic Body Percussion

- **E=7 placement.** The pec-slap sequence sits at E=7 -- between the extreme acoustic events (breach at E=9, spy-hop at E=8) and the quiet (echolocation click trains at E=8, vocalizations at various levels). Seven reflects the pec-slap's significant acoustic impact per strike combined with its rhythmic, repeating character. It is not the explosive detonation of a breach; it is sustained, rhythmic percussion.
- **Per-strike energy.** Each individual pec-slap creates a significant acoustic event -- the impact of a large, flat fin on the water surface. For an adult animal, this impact is substantial, audible at hundreds of meters in calm water.
- **The sequence effect.** A sequence of 10-20 pec-slaps produces a sustained rhythmic acoustic pattern lasting 10-60 seconds or more. The total acoustic energy of a long pec-slap sequence is significant -- more than a single breach in terms of total energy, though the per-event energy is lower.
- **Underwater vs. above surface.** Each slap creates both an above-surface crack (air) and an underwater pressure wave. In the orca's primarily underwater communication context, the underwater component is likely the more functionally relevant signal.
- **Frequency range.** Pec-slap impacts are broadband, with energy concentrated in the low-to-mid frequency range (50 Hz to a few kHz for the primary impact). This differs from the high-frequency click trains of echolocation (degree 276) and the broad-frequency range of vocalizations (degree 270).

(Sources: Pec-slap acoustic measurements; E=7 calibration; per-strike energy analysis; sequence duration and total energy; underwater vs. surface acoustic components; frequency range analysis)

## THE PECTORAL FIN: BIOLOGY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### An Extraordinary Appendage

- **Evolutionary origin.** The orca's pectoral fins are homologous to the forelimbs of terrestrial mammals -- specifically, the arm and hand bones are present within the fin (humerus, radius, ulna, and modified digits). What looks like a paddle is, in its internal structure, a highly modified arm. The pec-slap is, at an evolutionary level, a mammal hitting the water surface with its arm.
- **Size.** Adult male Southern Resident pectoral fins average approximately 1.8 meters in length -- about the height of a tall human. The width at the broadest point is approximately 0.9-1.0 meters. The fin is substantial.
- **Social use.** Pectoral fins are used extensively in social contact: rubbing, touching, supporting other individuals. The tactile social use of the fins is consistent with their use in pec-slapping -- both involve deliberate use of the fins as contact surfaces.
- **Thermal regulation.** The pectoral fins contain a dense vascular network (countercurrent heat exchange system) that regulates body temperature. The fins can increase or decrease heat dissipation by adjusting blood flow. The large surface area of the fins is effective for thermoregulation in the Salish Sea's variable water temperatures.

(Sources: Cetacean pectoral fin anatomy; evolutionary homology to forelimbs; adult male SRKW fin measurements; social tactile use; thermoregulation biology)

## PEC-SLAP VS. OTHER SURFACE BEHAVIORS

### Where It Fits

- **Pec-slap vs. breach (273).** The breach is explosive, single-event, maximum force. The pec-slap is rhythmic, repeated, percussive. One is a detonation; the other is a drumbeat.
- **Pec-slap vs. spy-hop (274).** The spy-hop is primarily visual and observational -- the orca rises to see. The pec-slap is primarily acoustic -- the orca creates sound deliberately. One is sensory intake; the other is signal output.
- **Pec-slap vs. tail-slap (lobtailing).** Lobtailing (slapping the tail flukes on the water surface) is related but distinct. Tail-slaps are generally louder and lower-frequency than pec-slaps; they may serve different communicative functions. The engine documents pec-slapping specifically here.
- **Pec-slap vs. echolocation (276).** The pec-slap is a surface mechanical event -- body percussion. Echolocation is a specialized internal acoustic system. Both create underwater sound; the mechanisms and contexts are entirely different.

(Sources: Surface behavior comparison; pec-slap vs. lobtailing; mechanical vs. specialized acoustic production; behavioral context differentiation)

## SOUTHERN RESIDENTS: PEC-SLAPPING IN CONTEXT

### When It Happens

- **Social occasions.** SRKWs pec-slap most frequently in socially active contexts: when multiple pods are aggregated (super-pod events), during active socializing, and in what researchers characterize as "play." The behavior is contagious in some contexts -- one individual slaps, others follow.
- **Post-hunt behavior.** Following successful salmon hunting, SRKW pods often engage in extended socializing including pec-slapping, spy-hopping, and breaching. The pec-slap in this context may be an expression of positive arousal following successful foraging.
- **Super-pod events.** When all three SRKW pods (J, K, L) come together in what is called a super-pod, the activity level increases dramatically. Pec-slapping, breaching, and vocalizations all intensify. These super-pod events are among the most acoustically and behaviorally rich observations in SRKW research.
- **Vessel proximity.** SRKWs have been observed pec-slapping near vessels in some contexts, possibly as a response to the vessel presence or noise. Whether this is exploratory behavior, play, or something else is not established.

(Sources: SRKW social behavior documentation; super-pod events; post-hunt behavior; pec-slap in vessel proximity contexts; Center for Whale Research observations)
