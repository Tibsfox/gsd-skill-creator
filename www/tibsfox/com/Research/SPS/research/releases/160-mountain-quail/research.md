### The Comparison Pair

The California/Mountain Quail pair at degrees 159-160 offers a rich comparison:

- **Size:** California Quail (170-200g) vs. Mountain Quail (240-290g). The Mountain Quail is the largest New World quail in North America -- roughly 40-50% heavier than the California Quail.
- **Head plume:** California: forward-curving comma of 6 feathers. Mountain: two straight feathers pointing backward from the crown -- the longest head plume of any quail, extending 6-8 cm behind the head.
- **Habitat:** California: brushy edges, open areas, gardens, parks. Mountain: dense brush, mountain chaparral, thick understory. The Mountain Quail prefers the most impenetrable brush available.
- **Visibility:** California: common, easily observed, often in suburban settings. Mountain: secretive, extremely difficult to see, retreats deeper into brush when approached. Despite being larger, the Mountain Quail is far more elusive.
- **Call:** California: three-syllable "chi-CA-go!" Mountain: single loud "quee-ark!" Both are distinctive and carrying, but structurally different.
- **Social behavior:** California: large coveys (10-200+). Mountain: smaller coveys (5-20). Both social but at different scales.

## E=4 -- THE LOUD SECRET

### Why E=4

The Mountain Quail quee-ark registers at E=4:

- **Loud call:** The quee-ark is a genuinely loud, far-carrying call -- one of the loudest sounds produced by any quail species. It carries 200-400 meters through dense mountain brush. For a bird that is nearly impossible to see, the call is surprisingly powerful.
- **Single-note emphasis:** Unlike the California Quail's three-syllable assembly call, the Mountain Quail's quee-ark is essentially a single emphatic note (sometimes transcribed as two syllables: "quee-ARK!" with the stress on the second element). The simplicity concentrates the energy into a single burst.
- **Territorial function:** The quee-ark serves as both a territorial announcement and an assembly call. Males call from concealed positions within dense brush -- rarely from exposed perches. The call broadcasts presence without revealing location.
- **Behavioral context:** The Mountain Quail's secretive nature means that the quee-ark is often the only evidence of the species' presence. Birders may hear dozens of Mountain Quail calls without seeing a single bird. The call's importance as a detection tool adds to its significance.
- **E=4 matches California Quail:** The equal energy rating (E=4 for both Odontophoridae entries) reflects comparable loudness and behavioral significance, allowing the comparison pair to be evaluated on other dimensions (size, secretiveness, plume type, habitat).

## THE MOST SECRETIVE QUAIL

### Hidden in Plain Earshot

The Mountain Quail's secretiveness is legendary:

- **Dense brush preference:** The Mountain Quail selects the densest available brush habitat. Dense manzanita, ceanothus, mountain mahogany, thick understory of mixed conifer forest, impenetrable tangles of vine and shrub on mountain slopes. The bird is rarely more than a few meters from dense cover.
- **Reluctance to flush:** Unlike the California Quail (which will flush explosively when pressed), the Mountain Quail will typically run deeper into brush rather than fly. When it does flush, the flight is brief and immediately into the nearest dense cover. Observers rarely see more than a brief glimpse.
- **Low profile:** The Mountain Quail moves through brush at ground level, using the densest cover for travel corridors. Even when calling, the bird is typically well-concealed within brush. The straight backward plumes may actually help the bird navigate through dense vegetation by streamlining the head profile.
- **Small group size:** Mountain Quail coveys are typically 5-20 birds (vs. 10-200+ for California Quail). Smaller groups are harder to detect and disturb less vegetation when moving.
- **Detection paradox:** The Mountain Quail is one of the most commonly heard but least commonly seen birds in mountain habitats of western Washington and Oregon. The loud quee-ark broadcasts presence; the dense brush conceals the bird. This detection paradox -- loud call, invisible bird -- is the Mountain Quail's defining characteristic.

(Sources: Gutierrez & Delehanty 1999; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; engine internal analysis)

## THE LONGEST HEAD PLUME

### Two Straight Feathers

The Mountain Quail's head plume is distinctive:

- **Structure:** Two straight feathers projecting backward and slightly upward from the crown. Unlike the California Quail's forward-curving comma, the Mountain Quail's plume points backward. The two feathers are held close together, appearing as a single straight projection.
- **Length:** 6-8 cm -- the longest head plume of any quail species in North America. The plumes extend well beyond the back of the head and are visible at a distance (when the bird is visible at all).
- **Function:** Display and species recognition. The straight backward plumes are a key field mark that distinguishes Mountain Quail from California Quail even in brief glimpses.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Both sexes have the plumes, but the male's are slightly longer.
- **Comparison:** The California Quail's topknot curves forward and consists of 6 overlapping feathers. The Mountain Quail's plume consists of just 2 straight feathers pointing backward. Forward-curving vs. straight-backward. The contrast is stark and immediately diagnostic.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Gutierrez & Delehanty 1999; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## MOUNTAIN BRUSH ECOLOGY

### The Habitat Specialist

The Mountain Quail occupies a specific ecological niche:

- **Elevation:** Typically mid-elevation mountain slopes (1,000-6,000 feet in Washington). Higher than California Quail (which prefers lowland and foothill habitats) but lower than alpine zone.
- **Vegetation:** Dense chaparral and brush -- manzanita, ceanothus, mountain mahogany, snowbrush, dense understory of Douglas-fir and mixed conifer forest. The key habitat requirement is dense ground-level cover.
- **Diet:** Seeds, berries, bulbs, tubers, insects. The Mountain Quail is a more vigorous digger than the California Quail -- it excavates bulbs and tubers with its strong feet. This digging behavior provides access to food sources that other quail species cannot exploit.
- **Migration:** The Mountain Quail is one of the few quail species that migrates -- not by flying but by **walking.** In autumn, coveys walk downslope from high-elevation summer habitat to lower-elevation winter habitat, covering several miles on foot through dense brush. In spring, they walk back upslope. These walking migrations are remarkable: a small gamebird traveling miles through mountain brush, following traditional routes passed from generation to generation.
- **Washington distribution:** Found in the Cascades, Olympics, and other mountain ranges of western Washington. More common in Oregon. In Washington, the species is patchily distributed and generally uncommon, found primarily in areas with extensive dense brush on mountain slopes.

(Sources: Gutierrez & Delehanty 1999; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; WDFW; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Dense Brush -- Loud Quee-Ark

The Mountain Quail's acoustic profile centers on the powerful quee-ark:

**The Quee-Ark Call:**
- **Trigger:** Territorial announcement. Assembly. Breeding season. General contact.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal, syringeal. Single emphatic note.
- **Character:** A loud, clear, whistled "quee-ARK!" -- the first element slightly rising, the second element emphatic and stressed. Sometimes heard as a single note "QUEE-ark" or "kyow!" The call has a ringing, penetrating quality that cuts through dense brush.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 1500-3500 Hz. Clear and penetrating. Well within the human hearing range.
- **Duration:** Single call: approximately 0.3-0.5 seconds. Repeated persistently but less frequently than California Quail's chi-CA-go.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Loud. Carries 200-400 meters through dense brush. Surprisingly powerful for the bird's secretive habits.
- **Caller position:** Unlike the California Quail (which calls from exposed perches), the Mountain Quail typically calls from within dense brush cover. The call broadcasts presence without revealing the caller's exact position.
- **Detection significance:** Often the ONLY evidence of the species' presence. Birders detect Mountain Quail by ear far more often than by sight.

**Contact Calls:**
- **Character:** Soft clucking. Covey cohesion calls similar to California Quail but less frequent. Close-range.

**Alarm:**
- **Character:** Sharp, short calls triggering covey freeze or deeper retreat into brush.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Gutierrez & Delehanty 1999; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
