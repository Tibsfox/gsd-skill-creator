*Degree 160 retrospective complete. MOUNTAIN QUAIL LOUD QUEE-ARK at the one hundred sixtieth degree -- THE SECRET GIANT. In the dense mountain brush of the western Cascades -- manzanita tangles, ceanothus thickets, impenetrable walls of shrub on steep slopes -- the largest quail in North America hides. The Mountain Quail is 240 to 290 grams of beautifully painted plumage: chestnut throat bordered by white, blue-gray breast, bold white bars on chestnut flanks, and two straight feathers projecting backward from the crown -- the longest head plume of any quail, six to eight centimeters of diagnostic distinction. It is common in appropriate habitat. It is almost never seen. When approached, it does not flush like its California cousin; it runs -- deeper into the brush, away from the threat, using ground-level corridors through vegetation so dense that a human cannot follow. Birders who encounter Mountain Quail typically encounter a voice: "QUEE-ARK!" -- a loud, emphatic, ringing whistle that carries two hundred to four hundred meters through the dense brush. The detection paradox: a call loud enough to hear at a quarter mile from a bird invisible at ten feet. This is the second and final Odontophoridae -- the New World quail pair complete. California Quail at 159 with its forward-curving topknot and "chi-CA-go!" assembly call, common and visible in suburban edges. Mountain Quail at 160 with its straight backward plumes and "quee-ark!" territorial announcement, secretive and invisible in dense mountain brush.
