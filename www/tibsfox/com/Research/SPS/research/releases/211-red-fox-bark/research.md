## THE BARK -- SHARP AND EXPLOSIVE

### The Fox's Signature Sound

The Red Fox bark is unlike any other canid vocalization:

- **The bark.** "WRAHH!" -- a single, sharp, explosive burst. 0.2-0.5 seconds. Frequency range: 500-2,000 Hz, with significant energy in the 800-1,500 Hz band. The bark is percussive rather than tonal -- it begins and ends abruptly, with no sustain. This distinguishes it from the coyote's sustained howl and the wolf's long, resonant howl.
- **Territorial function.** The bark serves as a territorial marker, particularly during the breeding season (January-February in the PNW). Male foxes bark to advertise territory and attract mates. The bark carries 200-500 meters -- far less than a wolf howl but sufficient for the fox's smaller territory.
- **Alarm function.** Foxes bark when startled or when detecting a threat. The alarm bark is sharper and higher-pitched than the territorial bark. Parents bark to warn kits of approaching danger.
- **Contact function.** Foxes use shorter, softer barks as contact calls between family members. These are less explosive than the territorial bark -- more a "checking in" than a declaration.
- **The scream.** Beyond the bark, Red Foxes produce a blood-curdling scream, particularly during the breeding season. The scream (a high, drawn-out wail) is often mistaken for a human in distress. The bark is the everyday voice; the scream is the extreme.

## THE MOST ADAPTABLE CANID

### Crossing Every Boundary

The Red Fox's adaptability is legendary:

- **Global distribution.** *Vulpes vulpes* has the largest natural distribution of any land carnivore -- native across the Northern Hemisphere plus introduced populations in Australia. This is an animal that thrives almost everywhere.
- **Habitat generalism.** In the PNW, Red Foxes occupy forest edges, agricultural fields, suburban neighborhoods, and urban margins. They're not forest specialists (like the wolf) or urban specialists (like the coyote is becoming) -- they're boundary specialists. They thrive where habitats meet.
- **Dietary flexibility.** Red Foxes are true omnivores: small mammals, birds, insects, fruits, garbage, pet food. The dietary flexibility enables the habitat generalism. The fox eats whatever the boundary offers.
- **Behavioral adaptability.** Foxes adjust their activity patterns to human presence -- becoming more nocturnal in disturbed areas, more diurnal in remote areas. They adjust territory size to food availability. They adjust denning behavior to local conditions. Every parameter is tunable.
- **Solitary but social.** Unlike wolves (obligate pack hunters) and coyotes (flexible pack/pair structure), foxes are primarily solitary hunters that maintain loose social networks. A mated pair shares a territory but hunts independently. This solitary-yet-social structure allows maximum flexibility.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Red Fox Bark

**Territorial Bark:**
- **Frequency:** 500-2,000 Hz. Peak energy at 800-1,500 Hz.
- **Character:** Sharp, explosive, percussive. "WRAHH!" Single burst. No sustain.
- **Duration:** 0.2-0.5 seconds per bark. Repeated every 3-10 seconds during territorial bouts.
- **Context:** Territory advertisement. Breeding season (January-February). Male-to-male signaling.
- **Carry distance:** 200-500 meters. Territory-scale, not landscape-scale.

**Alarm Bark:**
- **Frequency:** Higher-pitched than territorial. 800-2,500 Hz.
- **Character:** Sharper, more urgent. Warning kits or family members.

**Breeding Scream:**
- **Frequency:** 1,000-3,000+ Hz.
- **Character:** High, drawn-out wail. Often mistaken for human distress. Breeding season.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=4. Explosive but brief. Sharp but compact. Territory-scale communication. High instantaneous energy, low sustained energy. The fox bark is a flash, not a glow.
