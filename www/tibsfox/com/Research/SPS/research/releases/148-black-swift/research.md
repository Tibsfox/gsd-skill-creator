## THE LAST NEST DISCOVERED

### 1901 -- The Final Find

The Black Swift was the **last North American bird species to have its nest discovered**:

- **The long mystery:** Despite being known to science since the mid-1800s, no one had ever found a Black Swift nest. The bird was seen in flight -- a large, dark swift -- but where it went to breed was unknown. Ornithologists searched for decades.
- **1901 discovery:** The first confirmed Black Swift nest was found in 1901 behind a waterfall in Colorado by A.G. Prill. The nest was on a damp cliff ledge behind the curtain of falling water -- a location so unexpected and inaccessible that it had evaded discovery for half a century of searching.
- **Why so late?** Three factors conspired to hide the nest:
  1. **Waterfall concealment:** The nests are located behind or adjacent to waterfalls, which create a physical barrier to access. Approaching a nest requires climbing wet, mossy cliff faces near or behind falling water.
  2. **High elevation:** Most nesting sites are in mountainous terrain at 1000-3000 meters elevation -- remote, difficult to reach, and rarely visited.
  3. **No material nest:** Black Swifts do not build conventional nests. They construct a small, shallow cup of moss, ferns, and mud on a cliff ledge -- often so minimal that it is nearly invisible against the damp rock.
- **Ongoing discovery:** New Black Swift nesting sites continue to be discovered. The species is so secretive and its nesting habitat so specific that undiscovered colonies almost certainly exist.

### The Waterfall Requirement

The Black Swift's association with waterfalls is one of the most unusual habitat requirements of any North American bird:

- **Moisture:** The nesting site must be consistently moist -- splashed by waterfall spray or seeping cliff moisture. The moss and fern substrate of the nest requires dampness.
- **Inaccessibility:** The waterfall provides protection from predators. Cliff ledges behind or adjacent to waterfalls are extremely difficult for terrestrial predators (mammals, snakes) to reach.
- **Temperature moderation:** The waterfall spray moderates temperature extremes at the nesting site -- cooler in summer heat, warmer during cold nights (the thermal mass of water helps).
- **The trade-off:** The waterfall location protects the nest but creates challenges: the chick must develop in a constantly damp environment, and the adults must navigate through or around the waterfall to reach the nest.

(Sources: Lowther & Collins 2002; Foerster & Collins 1990; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## THE DISAPPEARING ACT

### Where Does It Go?

The Black Swift's daily routine is one of the great mysteries of North American ornithology:

- **Morning departure:** Adults leave the nesting site at dawn. They fly upward and outward, quickly reaching altitudes where they disappear from ground-based observation.
- **The gap:** For most of the day, Black Swifts are unobservable. They are presumed to be feeding at high altitude (1000-3000 meters above ground), but direct observation of their daytime activities has been extremely limited.
- **Evening return:** Adults return to the nesting site at dusk -- often the last birds visible in the fading light. They approach the waterfall at speed, entering the spray zone and landing directly on the nest ledge.
- **Tracking data:** Recent satellite tracking studies have begun to reveal Black Swift movements. The birds appear to fly enormous distances during the day -- potentially hundreds of kilometers from the nest -- foraging at high altitude and returning at dusk. This daily pattern of massive aerial foraging followed by cliff-face nesting is unique.
- **Migration mystery:** Black Swift migration routes were unknown until satellite tracking. The birds winter in western Brazil and Bolivia -- traveling over 6,000 kilometers from PNW breeding sites. The migration route and wintering habitat were only confirmed in the 2010s.

### North America's Largest Swift

The Black Swift is the **largest swift in North America**:

- **Size:** Length: 18-20 cm. Weight: 40-55 g. Wingspan: 38-44 cm. Significantly larger than the Vaux's Swift (11-12 cm, 17-20g).
- **Plumage:** Entirely dark blackish-brown. The darkest swift in North America. In flight: long, broad wings, forked tail, powerful wingbeats.
- **Flight:** More powerful and direct than the flickering Vaux's Swift. The Black Swift's flight is steady, deliberate, and capable of sustained high-altitude soaring.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Lowther & Collins 2002; DeLuca et al. 2015; Sibley 2014)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Water and Stone -- Low Chipping at the Cliff Colony

The Black Swift's acoustic contribution is **environmental acoustics** -- the sound of a bird heard within the dramatic soundscape of a waterfall cliff:

- **Individual call:** A low, soft chip note -- "chip" or "tchip" -- lower-pitched and softer than the Vaux's Swift's high chipping. Frequency approximately 3000-5000 Hz. Each chip 0.03-0.06 seconds.
- **Colony calls:** At nesting colonies (typically 5-20 nests), multiple birds chip simultaneously, creating a soft chorus audible against the waterfall roar. The calls are communication between arriving/departing adults and their mates/chicks.
- **Waterfall masking:** The waterfall creates a constant white-noise backdrop that partially masks the swift's chipping. Hearing Black Swift calls requires standing near the colony and listening through the water sound -- a unique acoustic challenge.
- **Environmental amplification:** The cliff face reflects and amplifies the chipping. The concave surfaces of cliff alcoves (where nests are often placed) act as natural acoustic reflectors, projecting the calls outward.
- **Amplitude:** Quiet individually (audible at 30-80m in still conditions). Against waterfall noise, audible only at close range (10-30m). The environmental context defines the amplitude experience.
- **Temporal pattern:** Most calling occurs during dawn departure and dusk return -- the two brief windows when adults are at the colony. During the day, the colony is silent (adults absent).

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Lowther & Collins 2002; engine internal analysis)
