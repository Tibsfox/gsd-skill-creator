Black Swift

Cypseloides niger (Apodidae) — E=4 — Low Chipping Calls at Cliff Colony
SECOND APODIDAE. North America’s largest and most mysterious swift. Nests behind waterfalls on cliff faces. Last North American bird to have its nest discovered (1901). Disappears at high altitude for hours. A ghost in the sky.

Nests Behind Waterfalls

Cliff ledges behind or adjacent to waterfalls. Constantly moist: spray, seep, mist. Nest: shallow cup of moss, ferns, and mud on damp rock. Nearly invisible. The waterfall conceals and protects. Inaccessible to predators. The most unusual nesting habitat of any N. American bird.

Last Nest Discovered — 1901

The last North American bird species to have its nest found. Searched for decades. A.G. Prill, 1901, Colorado — behind a waterfall. Three factors hid it: waterfall concealment, high elevation, and a nearly invisible minimal nest. New colonies still being discovered today.

The Disappearing Act

Departs colony at dawn. Disappears to high altitude (1000–3000m). Unobservable for hours. Returns at dusk — last bird in fading light. Satellite tracking: flies hundreds of kilometers daily. Winters in western Brazil (confirmed only in 2010s). A ghost that materializes at the waterfall.
