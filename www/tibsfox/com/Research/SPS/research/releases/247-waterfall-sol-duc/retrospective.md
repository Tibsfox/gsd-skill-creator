### Hydrology, E=7. Sol Duc Falls: 48 feet into an enclosed basalt canyon in Olympic National Park. The canyon amplifies, sustains, adds reverberation. Old-growth temperate rainforest. ## What Worked

1. **The canyon resonance as acoustic category is the correct distinguishing feature.** The three-way distinction within the waterfall arc -- river current (polyrhythm), Snoqualmie (white-noise wall), Sol Duc (resonant canyon roar) -- captures three genuinely different acoustic phenomena. The same physical process (water falling) produces different sounds depending on the geometry.
2. **The rainforest-canyon acoustic complementarity is the entry's most technically interesting finding.** Moss absorbs high frequencies; canyon amplifies low frequencies. The result is that the low-frequency component of the falls' sound travels effectively through the forest. The natural environment is acoustically tuned to propagate this specific sound.
3. **The Sol Duc Hot Springs geothermal context adds the Juan de Fuca plate connection.** The same tectonic process that creates the Cascade volcanoes drives the Olympic Peninsula's geothermal activity. The water at the hot springs and the water at the falls are the same water in two different forms: surface runoff and geothermally heated groundwater.
4. **The Cascade vs. Olympic geological distinction is the arc's most important structural observation.** The waterfall arc transitions from Cascade volcanic geology (river current, Snoqualmie) to Olympic accreted oceanic basalt (Sol Duc, Hoh). Different geological origins; different acoustic and ecological contexts.

## Discoveries

1. **The acoustic announcement -- the falls heard before seen -- is acoustically distinctive.** The combination of canyon amplification (longer reverberation, emphasized low frequencies) and rainforest high-frequency absorption creates a sound that travels effectively over long distances. The visitor hears the falls hundreds of meters before seeing it. This is an unusual property for a 48-foot waterfall.
2. **The Roosevelt Elk connection is underdocumented in standard Sol Duc descriptions.** The largest Roosevelt Elk population in the US (5,000 animals) uses the Sol Duc valley. Elk bugling near the falls is acoustically significant. The engine's biotic-abiotic transition doesn't mean the animals disappear -- they are still there, now in the foreground of the abiotic soundscape.
3. **Olympic NP UNESCO World Heritage status is appropriate to document.** UNESCO recognition of Olympic's temperate rainforest ecosystem adds an international significance layer to a site that the engine might otherwise treat primarily as a regional one.
