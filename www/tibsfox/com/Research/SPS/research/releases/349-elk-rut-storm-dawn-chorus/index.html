The Three Layers

Elk Rut

Roosevelt elk bull bugle: guttural growl → high-pitched whistle → grunts. 2–6 seconds per bugle. Multiple competing bulls produce spatial acoustic layering: near/far/subordinate. Peak October. Cow responses and herd grunts as continuous underlying social layer.

Active Storm

Pacific frontal system: wind in coniferous canopy (sustained broadband roar), rainfall on river surface (white-noise broadband), intensifying river flow (rising flood roar). Three acoustic sub-layers within the storm component, all interacting.

Fall Dawn Chorus

Varied Thrush (the defining PNW fall voice — single sustained note, different pitch, pause), Pacific Wren, Steller’s Jay, Red-tailed Hawk, mixed flocks (chickadees, nuthatches, creepers). Fall chorus: resident species in mixed-flock dynamics, different from spring chorus in composition and character.

The Acoustic Profile

150–2,500 Hz+ Roosevelt elk bugle fundamental. Multiple harmonics extend higher. The most acoustically imposing elk subspecies.
Broadband Storm acoustic layer: wind-in-canopy + rainfall + rising river. Continuous, varying with storm intensity. Background to the biological layers.
Varied Thrush Single sustained note at fixed pitch. Cuts through storm layer distinctly without competing. The biological location signal: this is PNW fall.
Valley Geometry River valley walls reflect and concentrate all three layers. Canopy provides acoustic enclosure. The terrain amplifies the convergence.

The Convergence Principle

The E=10 calibration is not the sum of three separately-calibrated events. It is the intersection: three events each at their individual maximum, occurring simultaneously in the same acoustic space. The acoustic layers do not add; they enter into dialogue.

The elk bugle through the storm wind: the storm background positions the bugle acoustically, making near and far bulls distinctly separable. The thrush whistle through the storm layer: the pure tone of the thrush does not compete with the broadband storm noise; it occupies a frequency space the storm leaves clear. The river beneath: the rising flood roar adds a sub-bass foundation to the event as the storm increases flow. Three dialogues simultaneously.

Temporal coincidence: Peak elk rut (mid-September to mid-October) + active Pacific storm + active dawn. All three in the same hour is rare even within the regular occurrence of each individual event.
