The distinction is important: intersection creates dialogue between the layers; summation would create a louder version of the same event. ## Discoveries

1. **Roosevelt elk as largest subspecies adds acoustic scale.** The Roosevelt elk bugle is larger-amplitude than the Rocky Mountain elk bugle (which is more commonly heard in the popular imagination). The Pacific Northwest has the acoustically largest elk. The bugle carries further and is physically more imposing.
2. **The fall dawn chorus vs spring dawn chorus distinction.** The fall chorus is not smaller; it is different. The species composition changes. The fall chorus is populated by residents (not migrants) in mixed-flock dynamics, with the Varied Thrush as the defining voice rather than the warbler and thrush species that define the spring chorus. The fall version is not a diminishment -- it is a different acoustic season.
3. **The storm + elk acoustic interaction.** The storm wind actually enhances the spatial dimensionality of the elk rut landscape by creating a continuous background layer against which the bugles are more distinctly positioned. The near bull's bugle cuts through the storm noise; the far bull's bugle barely breaks through it. The storm becomes a distance indicator for the elk acoustic space.
