## THE THREE LAYERS

### Layer 1: Elk Rut Vocalizations

The North American elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti* -- Roosevelt elk in the western Pacific Northwest) is the largest elk subspecies. The bull elk's rut call -- the bugle -- is one of the most acoustically distinctive sounds in North American wildlife. The bugle begins with a low guttural growl, rises rapidly through a high-pitched whistle that carries significant distance, and concludes with a series of grunts. The full bugle sequence spans approximately 2 to 6 seconds. A bull elk in active rut produces bugles repeatedly, with peak activity at dawn and dusk.

During peak rut (September into October in the Pacific Northwest), multiple bulls are active simultaneously within audible range. The spatial arrangement of competing bulls in a river valley produces an acoustic layering effect: close bulls producing full-amplitude bugles, distant bulls producing the higher-frequency harmonic components filtered through distance and forest canopy, and subordinate bulls producing lower-volume vocalizations at a higher frequency relative to the dominant bull's position. The result is a layered bugle landscape that changes continuously as bulls move through the terrain.

Additionally: the bull elk's estrus calls, the cow elk's responsive vocalizations, and the social grunts of the herd provide a lower-intensity continuous layer beneath the bugle landscape. The elk rut vocal event is not a single call type; it is a complex multi-layer social acoustic event.

**Peak rut acoustic parameters:** Frequency range: 150 Hz to 2,500 Hz (fundamental); harmonics extend higher. Duration of active bugling: dawn to mid-morning (with a secondary peak at dusk). Geographic range: Roosevelt elk populations throughout Olympic Peninsula river valleys and western Cascades foothills.

### Layer 2: Active Storm Acoustics

Fall in the Pacific Northwest is the entry point for the primary storm season. Pacific frontal systems arrive from the west, traveling along the storm track that brings warm moist air from the subtropical Pacific into contact with the cold continental air masses over the Cascades and Coast Range. The first significant fall storm events of the season typically arrive in October, though late September systems can produce significant acoustic events.

The storm acoustic profile in a Pacific Northwest river valley:

**Wind in the forest canopy.** Coniferous forest canopy under storm winds produces a sustained broadband roar at medium-to-high frequencies. The specific frequency profile depends on the dominant species: Sitka spruce (coastal, fine needle structure) produces a higher-pitched roar than Douglas-fir (interior, coarser structure). Wind speeds above 30 mph produce the continuous roar; gusts above 50 mph add the lower-frequency component of larger branches and trunk movement.

**Rainfall on water and ground.** Rainfall on the river surface produces a white-noise-like broadband acoustic layer at the lower-amplitude end of the storm acoustic profile. Heavy rainfall on standing water produces a distinct acoustic signature different from rainfall on ground cover (duff, fern, grass) or tree canopy drip. The combination of these sub-surfaces creates a complex broadband layer.

**River acoustic intensification.** Storm rainfall begins increasing river flow within hours of a significant storm event. The Hoh River, for instance, responds to rainfall events with flow increases that change the acoustic character of the river significantly: from the steady medium-amplitude roar of base flow to the louder, more turbulent, lower-frequency roar of rising flood flow. The storm and the river interact acoustically over the duration of the storm event.

### Layer 3: Fall Dawn Bird Chorus

The Pacific Northwest fall dawn bird chorus is acoustically distinct from the spring dawn chorus. The spring chorus (degrees 332, 351) is defined by the territorial and mate-selection vocalizations of migrant species arriving and resident species establishing breeding territories -- maximum species richness and maximum individual vocal output. The fall chorus is different: it is populated by resident species (not migrants in the departure period), mixed-flock species engaging in contact calls and flock-coordination vocalizations, and some residual territorial singing from late-season holdouts.

The fall chorus is not smaller than the spring chorus -- it is different. The species composition changes: fall dawn in a Pacific Northwest river valley includes Varied Thrush (the deep, pure, slightly oscillating whistle that defines the PNW fall acoustic landscape), Red-tailed Hawk (morning territorial calls), Steller's Jay (complex call sequences), Pacific Wren (the remarkably long and complex song disproportionate to the bird's small size), and mixed flocks of chickadees, nuthatches, and creepers producing continuous contact calls through the canopy.

**The Varied Thrush as defining fall voice.** The Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*) is one of the most acoustically distinctive birds in the Pacific Northwest. Its song is a single sustained note at a fixed pitch, often followed by a note at a different pitch, then a pause. The effect is meditative, fog-and-rain-associated, unmistakably autumnal. When active at dawn during a storm, the thrush's whistle cuts through the storm acoustic layer in a way that is not competitive (it does not try to be louder than the storm) but is distinctly present within it. The thrush is the identifying voice of the PNW fall acoustic landscape.
