Five consecutive Parulidae. Degrees 38, 39, 40, 41, 42. The longest same-family consecutive run in the engine, matching the swallow quintet. Ten warblers total -- nearly a quarter of the engine from a single family. The warbler dominance was absolute.

It ends here.

Degree 43 is **Fringillidae**. The American Goldfinch. *Spinus tristis*. A true finch. The Parulidae streak is broken at five. The engine exhales.

Degree 43 is not just "not a warbler" -- it is a member of Fringillidae, the family of true finches, which last appeared two degrees before the warbler streak began. Fringillidae at degree 34 (Red Crossbill), then three non-warbler degrees (35-37, hummingbirds), then five consecutive Parulidae (38-42), and now Fringillidae returns at degree 43. ## Taxonomy: The Sad Thorn-Bird

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Fringillidae (True Finches)
- **Genus:** *Spinus* (Koch, 1816)
- **Species:** *Spinus tristis* (Linnaeus, 1758)
- **Common Name:** American Goldfinch; also "Wild Canary," "Eastern Goldfinch," "Willow Goldfinch" (the name used in Washington state law)
- **Former classification:** *Carduelis tristis* (until reclassification to *Spinus* based on molecular phylogenetics)

### Etymology

*Spinus* -- from Latin *spinus*, meaning "thorn bush" or "blackthorn" [1, 2, 5]. Referring to the thorny, weedy plants where these birds are characteristically found -- thistles, teasels, and other composite plants with spiny stems and seed-rich heads. The genus was described by Koch in 1816. The Pine Siskin (*Spinus pinus*, degree 12) shares this genus -- both species are birds of thorny, seedy plants. The siskin's name doubles down on conifers (*pinus*), while the goldfinch's epithet goes in a completely different direction.

*Tristis* -- from Latin *tristis*, meaning "sad" or "sorrowful" [1, 2, 5, 9]. This is one of the most ironic scientific names in North American ornithology. The American Goldfinch -- the brilliant lemon-yellow bird with the jaunty black cap, the cheerful undulating flight, the exuberant canary-like song, the bird that schoolchildren across Washington voted as their state symbol for its beauty and cheer -- is named "sad." The epithet was given by Carl Linnaeus in 1758 in the tenth edition of *Systema Naturae* [1, 5], apparently referring to the plaintive quality of certain call notes, particularly the soft, descending *dear-me* or *tee-yee* contact call that has a melancholy, lamenting quality [1, 2, 9]. Linnaeus heard the sorrow in the song. The children heard the joy. Both are there.

Full translation: **"Sad thorn-bird."** A bird named for the thorny plants it feeds on and the mournful note in its voice. The genus names the habitat. The species names the emotional register. Compare with the engine's other Fringillidae names:

| Degree | Species | Binomial | Translation | Naming Philosophy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | Pine Siskin | *Spinus pinus* | "Thorn-spine of the pine" | Ecological (plant + plant) |
| 33 | Cassin's Finch | *Haemorhous cassinii* | "Cassin's blood-rump" | Honorific + anatomical |
| 34 | Red Crossbill | *Loxia curvirostra* | "Cross-billed curved-beak" | Anatomical (bill + bill) |
| **43** | **American Goldfinch** | ***Spinus tristis*** | **"Sad thorn-bird"** | **Ecological + emotional** |

Four naming philosophies in one family: ecological, honorific, anatomical, and now emotional. *Tristis* is the engine's first species epithet that names an emotion rather than a physical feature, a place, or a person. The goldfinch is the sad one. Named not for how it looks but for how it sounds to a listener attuned to melancholy.

### The Willow Goldfinch: Washington's Choice

In 1928, Washington schoolchildren voted to select a state bird. The American Goldfinch won by a wide margin [3, 4, 7]. But the legislature took 23 years to act. A vote passed one house in 1941 but stalled. It was not until March 14, 1951, that the Washington State Legislature unanimously adopted the "Willow Goldfinch" (the colloquial name used in the statute) as the official state bird under RCW 1.20.086 [3, 4, 7].

The name "Willow Goldfinch" in the statute is telling. The legislative language uses a folk name that emphasizes habitat -- willows, the streamside trees where goldfinches are commonly seen in the PNW. The scientific name emphasizes thorn bushes and sadness. The children's vote emphasized beauty and cheer. Three lenses on the same bird: legislative, scientific, popular. Each sees something different.

Washington shares the American Goldfinch as state bird with Iowa and New Jersey [3, 4]. Three states chose the same bright yellow finch. In a nation of 50 states and hundreds of candidate species, the goldfinch's appeal transcended geography.

## The Sound: The Undulating Call and the Canary Song

### The Per-Chick-O-Ree: A Flight Call Synchronized to Flight

The American Goldfinch's most recognizable vocalization is not its song. It is its **flight call** -- the distinctive *per-chick-o-ree* or *po-ta-to-chip* given in undulating flight [1, 2, 6, 8, 9, 11]. This call is one of the most distinctive and frequently heard bird sounds in North America. Once learned, it is never forgotten.

The call consists of **3-4 downslurred notes** at approximately 4 kHz, delivered over 0.3-0.6 seconds [1, 8, 11]. The notes are bright, clear, and slightly buzzy -- sharper than a warbler's chip but rounder than a sparrow's tsik. The transliteration *per-chick-o-ree* captures the rhythmic bounce of the call: a short introductory note, a stressed central syllable, and a trailing resolution.

What makes this call extraordinary is its **synchronization with the flight pattern**. The American Goldfinch flies in a deeply undulating sine-wave pattern -- a series of bounds and dips, rising with several wingbeats and then folding its wings to dip downward in a brief glide. The *per-chick-o-ree* call is given **at the bottom of each undulation**, synchronized to the wingbeat rhythm [1, 2, 8, 9, 11]. The sound and the motion are coupled. The bird rises, falls, and calls -- rises, falls, and calls -- tracing a sinusoidal path through the air while punctuating each trough with its signature phrase. The flight itself IS the vocalization's rhythm section. The wingbeats are the tempo. The undulations are the phrasing. The call is the melody laid over a flight-path ostinato.

No previous species in the engine has this kind of **locomotion-vocalization coupling**. The swallows (degrees 26-30) vocalized in flight, but their calls were not synchronized to their flight pattern. The hummingbirds (35-37) produced sound from their wings, but the sound was mechanical, not vocal. The American Goldfinch is the first species in the engine where the vocal output is rhythmically locked to the flight dynamics -- where the medium of travel IS the rhythmic structure of the sound.

### The Canary Song: Extended Warbling Without Repetition

The American Goldfinch's song is the second half of its acoustic identity, and it is where the "wild canary" nickname originates [1, 2, 6, 8, 9]. The song is a **long, complex, rambling warble** -- a continuous stream of bright, high-pitched, musical phrases that tumbles and shifts without obvious repetition, lasting several seconds to over a minute [1, 2, 9, 11]. It sounds like a canary's song because it shares the canary's essential quality: **extended melodic improvisation** within a bright tonal register.

The song can be divided into two types [1, 8, 9]:

1. **Complex song:** Successive phrases, each approximately 0.3-0.5 seconds, appended together into a continuous stream. Delivered from a perch or in flight. This is the primary territorial and courtship song.

2. **Disjunct song:** Individual phrases delivered every 1-2 seconds with pauses between them. Rarely given in flight. More common in close interactions.

The complex song is the showpiece. It contains multiple phrase types -- rising whistles, buzzy trills, clear warbles, chattering runs -- assembled in what appears to be random order but is actually a sophisticated recombination of a learned repertoire [1, 8, 9]. American Goldfinches retain **call learning into adulthood** and may lack a fixed critical song-learning period, meaning their vocal repertoire can expand throughout their lives [1, 8]. Paired birds develop **pair-specific flight calls** -- mates assume almost identical *per-chick-o-ree* patterns, so pair recognition is encoded in the vocalization itself [1, 8].

### Vocalization Details

- **Flight call (*per-chick-o-ree*):** 3-4 downslurred notes, approximately 4 kHz, duration 0.3-0.6 s. Given at the bottom of each undulation in flight. The primary contact and localization call. Year-round [1, 8, 9, 11].
- **Complex song:** Extended warbling, approximately 2-6 kHz range, duration 2-30+ seconds. Bright, high-pitched, canary-like. Multiple phrase types in seemingly random sequence. Breeding season, from perch or flight [1, 2, 8, 9].
- **Contact call (*tee-yee* or *dear-me*):** A soft, descending two-note phrase with a plaintive quality. The call that Linnaeus heard as "sad." The *tristis* call [1, 2, 9].
- **Aggressive call (*bee-ee*):** A harsh, buzzy call given during territorial disputes and nest defense [1, 8].
- **Feeding call:** Soft twittering during flock feeding, a continuous low-level social chatter [1, 8].
- **Frequency range:** Approximately 2-7 kHz across all vocalizations, with peak energy in the 3-5 kHz band [8, 11].
- **Volume:** Moderate. The flight call carries well across open fields. The song is delivered at moderate volume from exposed perches -- the tops of thistles, fence wires, small trees at field edges.

### The 44th Acoustic Element: The Undulation

The American Goldfinch introduces the engine's 44th acoustic element (species side): **the undulation**.

The undulation is the coupling of vocalization to locomotion -- a sound that rises and falls in synchrony with the physical movement of the singer through space. The *per-chick-o-ree* is not just a call. It is a call embedded in a flight pattern, locked to the sine-wave rhythm of bounding flight, delivered at the trough of each arc. The undulation is sound that has a trajectory -- it moves through space as it moves through time, and its rhythm is not set by an internal clock (like the metronome of degree 5) or by a musical structure (like the partition of degree 41) but by the **physics of flight itself**.

The undulation is distinct from all previous acoustic elements:

| Element | Source | Character |
|---|---|---|
| The Cascade (1) | Internal (syrinx complexity) | Complexity |
| The Metronome (5) | Internal (repetition) | Regularity |
| The Trill (11) | Internal (rapid repetition) | Speed |

| The Sonic Boom (36) | External (dive aerodynamics) | Mechanical violence |
| **The Undulation (43)** | **Coupled (voice + flight path)** | **Locomotion-vocalization synchrony** |

The undulation opens a new acoustic category: **coupled sound** -- vocalization that is rhythmically and structurally synchronized to physical movement. Previous elements were either purely vocal (most) or purely mechanical (thrum, sonic boom). The undulation is both: a vocal call whose timing is set by the sine-wave physics of bounding flight. The bird's body is the metronome. The air is the medium. The call is the melody. The flight path is the score.

Named "the undulation" for four resonances:
1. **The flight call's rhythm** -- locked to the undulating flight path, per-chick-o-ree at each trough
2. **The canary song's melodic contour** -- rising and falling, tumbling through phrases without settling
3. **The sine-wave flight path itself** -- the deepest undulation in any engine species' flight pattern
4. **The annual plumage cycle** -- the dramatic molt from drab olive-brown to brilliant lemon-yellow and back, a visual undulation between states

## Biology: The Bird That Waits for Thistles

### The Late Nester

The American Goldfinch is one of the **latest-nesting songbirds in North America** [1, 2, 6, 9, 10]. While most passerines nest from April through June, the American Goldfinch delays breeding until **late June through September** -- sometimes not beginning until July or even August [1, 2, 6, 10]. In the PNW, peak nesting occurs in July and August [2, 7, 10].

The reason is **thistle**. And milkweed. And other late-season composite plants.

The American Goldfinch is an **obligate seed-eater** during the breeding season -- one of the strictest granivores among North American passerines [1, 2, 6, 9]. It feeds its nestlings almost exclusively on **regurgitated seeds** rather than insects [1, 2, 6]. This is exceptional. Most passerines, even seed-eating adults, switch to insect protein for feeding nestlings because growing chicks need protein for rapid development. The American Goldfinch does not. It feeds its young seeds.

This means the goldfinch cannot breed until **seed sources are abundant and reliable** [1, 2, 6, 9]. Thistles (*Cirsium* spp.), teasels (*Dipsacus*), milkweed (*Asclepias*), sunflowers (*Helianthus*), and other composites do not produce mature seeds until mid-to-late summer. The goldfinch waits for them.

But the thistle dependence goes beyond food. The female goldfinch **lines the nest cup with thistle down** -- the soft, silky fibers from mature thistle seed heads [1, 2, 6, 10]. The nest is one of the most finely constructed of any North American passerine: a compact, tightly woven cup of plant fibers, spider silk, and caterpillar webbing, with an interior lining of thistle down so dense and waterproof that it can **hold water** [1, 2, 6, 10]. If an unattended nest fills with rainwater during a storm, the nestlings can drown because the thistle-down lining holds the water in [1, 2, 6]. A construction so perfect it becomes a vulnerability.

The female selects the nest site (typically in a shrub or small tree at 1-10 m height, often in an upright fork), builds the entire nest over approximately 6 days, and incubates the eggs alone for 12-14 days while the male feeds her on the nest [1, 2, 6, 10]. Both parents feed the nestlings for 11-17 days until fledging [1, 2, 6].

### The Twice-Yearly Molt: The Annual Transformation

The American Goldfinch is the **only cardueline finch in North America that molts twice per year** [1, 2, 5, 6, 9]. This is its most remarkable physiological feature and the source of its "wild canary" reputation.

**The molt cycle** [1, 2, 5, 6]:

1. **Prebasic molt (late summer/fall):** A **complete molt** -- every feather on the body, wings, and tail is replaced. The breeding male's brilliant lemon-yellow body plumage and black cap are replaced by drab **olive-brown** body feathers. The wings and tail become dark brown (female) or black (male). The bird becomes almost unrecognizable -- a small, drab, brownish finch with faint wing bars, easily overlooked. This is the basic (winter) plumage.

2. **Prealternate molt (late winter/spring):** A **partial molt** -- the body feathers are replaced but the wing and tail feathers are retained from the prebasic molt. The drab olive-brown body is replaced by **brilliant lemon-yellow** feathers. The male acquires a **jet-black forehead cap**. The transformation is complete by late April or May -- just before the breeding season. The basic plumage bird becomes the alternate (breeding) plumage bird: the "wild canary."

The twice-yearly molt is **energetically expensive** and is one reason the goldfinch nests late [1, 2, 5, 6]. The spring molt requires substantial nutritional resources -- protein for feather growth, carotenoid pigments for the yellow coloration. The bird cannot afford to molt and breed simultaneously. It molts first (March-May), then breeds (June-September). The late nesting is not just about waiting for thistles. It is about waiting for the molt to finish.

The visual transformation is dramatic. No other bird in the PNW changes appearance so completely between seasons. The Pine Siskin (degree 12, same genus) does not molt twice -- it looks the same year-round. The Red Crossbill (degree 34) molts once. The Cassin's Finch (degree 33) molts once. Only the American Goldfinch, among PNW finches, performs this annual visual undulation between states -- drab to brilliant, olive to gold, invisible to unmistakable.

### Strict Granivory and the Cowbird Defense

The American Goldfinch's strict seed diet provides an accidental defense against **brood parasitism** by Brown-headed Cowbirds [1, 2, 6, 9]. Cowbirds regularly lay eggs in goldfinch nests. The cowbird eggs hatch, and the cowbird chicks beg for food alongside the goldfinch chicks. But the goldfinch parents feed only seeds -- regurgitated, pre-digested seeds. Cowbird chicks require **insect protein** to survive. On the goldfinch's all-seed diet, cowbird chicks typically **starve within 3-5 days** of hatching [1, 2, 6, 9]. The goldfinch does not recognize or reject cowbird eggs. It does not need to. Its diet is the defense. An immune system made of thistle seeds.

## PNW Habitat and Range

### Distribution in Washington and Oregon

The American Goldfinch is a **common to abundant** breeding bird throughout the lowlands of Washington and Oregon [2, 7, 10, 12]. In western Washington, it was formerly rare -- the dense old-growth forests that once dominated the Puget Sound lowlands provided poor habitat for a bird that needs open, weedy fields [2, 7]. With European settlement and the clearing of western Washington forests, the goldfinch expanded dramatically [2, 7]. It is now one of the most common backyard birds in the Seattle metropolitan area and throughout the Puget Sound lowlands.

**Western Washington:** Common from mid-April through mid-October in appropriate habitat. Breeds in weedy fields, roadsides, orchards, suburban gardens, and open brushy areas. Some individuals overwinter, particularly where bird feeders provide nyjer (thistle) seed. Many western Washington breeders are year-round residents [2, 7, 12].

**Eastern Washington:** Common breeder in agricultural areas, riparian corridors, and open habitats. Many eastern Washington breeders migrate south or to lower elevations in winter [2, 7, 12].

**Oregon:** Year-round resident west of the Cascades, particularly in the Willamette Valley. Common in riparian woodlands, orchards, weedy fields, and agricultural land. Nomadic in winter, congregating where food is available [10, 12].

### Habitat: The Weedy Field

The American Goldfinch's primary breeding habitat is the **weedy field** -- an open, disturbed, or semi-natural area dominated by composite plants (Asteraceae), especially thistles, teasels, sunflowers, and other seed-producing forbs [1, 2, 6, 7, 10]. This is the engine's first explicitly **open-field habitat** since the brush at degree 41 (Nashville Warbler), and the most open habitat since the agricultural landscape implied by the Barn Swallow's farmland at degree 30.

The weedy field is a **human-created habitat** in the PNW. Before European settlement, the Puget Sound lowlands were dominated by dense coniferous forest -- unsuitable for goldfinches. The clearing of forests for agriculture, the spread of invasive thistles and other Eurasian weeds, and the creation of suburban landscapes with gardens and feeders **created** the goldfinch's habitat in western Washington [2, 7]. The American Goldfinch is, in this sense, a bird that benefited from the transformation of the PNW landscape. The same logging that threatened the Spotted Owl (degree 4) and the Hermit Warbler (degree 40) created the weedy fields where the goldfinch thrives.

This is the ecological paradox at degree 43: a state bird that exists in its current abundance in the state largely because of the same land-use changes that imperil other species in the engine. The goldfinch did not build the weedy field. Humans did, by cutting down the forest. But the goldfinch moved in and made it home. The state chose the bird that prospered from the state's own transformation of the landscape.

### Vertical Position and Feeding Ecology

The American Goldfinch is a **low-level bird** -- it feeds, nests, and sings primarily at 0-5 m above ground level [1, 2, 6, 7]. It perches on thistle heads, fence wires, low shrubs, and small trees. Its singing perch is typically the tallest available point in a weedy field -- a fence post, a dead stem, the top of a small tree at the field edge -- but this is measured in meters, not in the tens of meters typical of canopy species.

## The Washington State Bird: Significance for the Engine

### The First State Symbol

The American Goldfinch is the **first species in the engine that holds an official government symbol designation**. It was adopted as Washington's state bird in 1951, and it is the 44th entry (degree 43) in a 360-degree survey centered on Washington state. The state bird arrives at approximately 12% of the way through the circle.

The significance is structural. This project -- "The Sound of Puget Sound" -- is explicitly rooted in Washington state. Every degree is referenced to coordinates at 47.6 degrees N, 122.3 degrees W. The waters of Puget Sound, the forests of the Cascades, the meadows of the lowlands -- all Washington. And the bird that the state itself chose to represent its identity, the bird that Washington's schoolchildren voted for in 1928 and the legislature adopted in 1951, has now entered the engine.

### The Children's Choice

The 1928 schoolchildren's vote is significant. The American Goldfinch won over other candidates because of its **visibility, beauty, and ubiquity** [3, 4, 7]. Goldfinches are **obvious** -- bright yellow, active, cheerful, present in every county, frequently seen at backyard feeders and in gardens. They are a bird that children notice. The vote was not about rarity or ecological importance. It was about which bird **delighted** the most observers. The goldfinch won on charm.

This contrasts sharply with many of the engine's other species, which are known primarily to experienced birders: the MacGillivray's Warbler (degree 14) in its salmonberry thicket, the Hermit Warbler (degree 40) at the extreme crown of a mountain hemlock, the Pine Siskin (degree 12) indistinguishable from a generic sparrow to untrained eyes. The American Goldfinch is the people's bird. Everybody sees it. Everybody knows it. The children chose correctly: this is the bird that represents the human experience of Washington's avifauna.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 9, 12]
- **Global population:** Approximately 42-47 million mature individuals [1, 2, 9, 12]
- **Partners in Flight:** Global breeding population estimated at 44 million. Continental Concern Score 6/20 (low concern) [11, 12]
- **BBS trend:** Mixed signals -- approximately -0.6% per year between 1966 and 2019 (cumulative decline of ~27%), but some regional populations stable or increasing [2, 11, 12]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 [1, 2]
- **Threats:** Habitat loss (loss of weedy fields to development), pesticide impacts on seed plants, window strikes, domestic cat predation. None at population-threatening levels [1, 2, 9]

The goldfinch population of ~44 million is about one-quarter the size of the Yellow-rumped Warbler's ~170 million (degree 42). It is abundant but not dominant. It thrives in human-modified landscapes -- the bird that found its niche in the spaces humans created by clearing forests and planting gardens.
