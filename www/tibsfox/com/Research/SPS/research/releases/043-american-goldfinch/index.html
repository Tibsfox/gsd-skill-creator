American Goldfinch

Spinus tristis
per-chick-o-ree flight call + canary song — the undulation, 4th Fringillidae, Washington State Bird (first state symbol in engine), Spinus genus reunion with Pine Siskin (degree 12) after 31 degrees — breaks the five-consecutive Parulidae streak, the sad thorn-bird that is the brightest bird in the field

The 44th Acoustic Element: The Undulation

The coupling of vocalization to locomotion. Per-chick-o-ree — a bright, bouncing flight call synchronized to the sine-wave rhythm of bounding flight, delivered at the trough of each undulation. The bird rises with wingbeats, dips on folded wings, and calls at the bottom of each arc. Sound locked to flight path. Named “the undulation” for four resonances: (1) the flight call’s rhythm locked to the bounding arc, (2) the canary song’s rising and falling melodic contour, (3) the deepest sine-wave flight path in the engine, (4) the annual plumage cycle undulating between drab olive and brilliant gold.

The first acoustic element defined by locomotion-vocalization coupling — the flight path IS the rhythmic structure.

The Warbler Streak Ends — A Finch Enters

Five consecutive Parulidae (38–42) — broken. The unprecedented warbler run is over. Degree 43 is Fringillidae — the true finches. The American Goldfinch is the fourth Fringillidae in the engine (after Pine Siskin at 12, Cassin’s Finch at 33, Red Crossbill at 34). The finches bookend the warbler era: Fringillidae at 34, then hummingbirds (35–37), then five consecutive warblers (38–42), then Fringillidae returns at 43.

Washington State Bird — First State Symbol

The American Goldfinch has been Washington’s official state bird since 1951 (RCW 1.20.086). Schoolchildren voted for it in 1928; the legislature took 23 years to make it law. Chosen for its beauty, ubiquity, and cheer. Found in every county. The first species in the engine that holds an official state symbol designation — in a project named for Puget Sound, centered on Washington state.

Washington shares the American Goldfinch as state bird with Iowa and New Jersey.

Spinus Genus Reunion — 31 Degrees Apart

The American Goldfinch (Spinus tristis) and the Pine Siskin (Spinus pinus, degree 12) are sister species in the same genus. Degrees 12 and 43 — the widest genus gap in the engine (31 degrees). Same genus, opposite in every dimension: E=1 vs. E=4, electronic vs. melodic, irruptive vs. resident, cryptic vs. brilliant, noise vs. music.

The Sad Thorn-Bird

Spinus tristis — “Sad thorn-bird.” Spinus = Latin for “thorn bush” (the weedy plants where goldfinches feed). Tristis = Latin for “sad” (the plaintive quality of the dear-me contact call). Named by Linnaeus in 1758. One of ornithology’s most ironic names: the brightest, most cheerful bird in the field, named for sorrow. The children who voted it state bird heard the joy. Linnaeus heard the sadness. Both are there.

The Sound: Undulating Call + Canary Song

Flight call: Per-chick-o-ree (also rendered po-ta-to-chip) — 3–4 downslurred notes at ~4 kHz, delivered at the trough of each undulation in bounding flight. The most frequently heard vocalization. Year-round.

Song: A long, complex, canary-like warble — a continuous stream of bright, high-pitched phrases (2–6 kHz) lasting 2–30+ seconds. Multiple phrase types in seemingly random order. No fixed repetition pattern. The “wild canary” song that earned the species its nickname. Paired birds develop pair-specific flight calls — mates converge on identical per-chick-o-ree patterns.

Four finches, four radically different acoustic strategies. The Spinus pair — Pine Siskin (electronic) and American Goldfinch (melodic) — spans the widest acoustic range within any single genus in the engine.

The Bird That Waits for Thistles

One of the latest-nesting songbirds in North America — breeds June through September, peaking in July–August. Delays nesting until thistles and composites produce mature seeds. Feeds nestlings regurgitated seeds (not insects). Nest lined with thistle down so dense it can hold water. The only cardueline finch to molt twice yearly: a complete fall molt to drab olive-brown, then a partial spring molt to brilliant lemon-yellow with black cap. The twice-yearly molt is energetically expensive — another reason for late nesting.

Accidental cowbird defense: Brown-headed Cowbird chicks starve on the goldfinch’s all-seed diet within 3–5 days.

Weedy Field — The Human-Created Habitat

Open, disturbed, weedy areas dominated by composite plants — thistles, teasels, sunflowers, milkweed. The goldfinch’s primary habitat in western Washington was created by European settlement: the clearing of dense coniferous forest produced the open, weedy fields where goldfinches thrive. The same land-use changes that threatened the Spotted Owl (degree 4) and the Hermit Warbler (degree 40) created the goldfinch’s habitat. The state bird prospers from the state’s own transformation of the landscape.

E=4 Continues — 20th Degree, 23 Without E≤3

E=4 now at 20 degrees (45.5%). E=3 absent for 23 consecutive degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 30 degrees. E=1 absent for 31 degrees — since degree 12, the Pine Siskin (Spinus pinus), same genus as the American Goldfinch. Six consecutive E=4 across two families (38–43). Dynamic range compression deepens.

The Twice-Yearly Molt

The American Goldfinch is the only cardueline finch in North America that molts twice per year. The prebasic molt (fall) is complete — every feather replaced. Brilliant yellow becomes drab olive-brown. The prealternate molt (spring) is partial — body feathers replaced, wing and tail retained. Olive becomes gold. A visual undulation between states, repeated every year. No other PNW finch does this.
