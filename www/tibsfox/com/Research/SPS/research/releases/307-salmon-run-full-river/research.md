## THE AGGREGATE SOUND -- WHY E=8

### The Full River as Acoustic Event

The sound of a full Pacific Northwest salmon spawning run is qualitatively different from any individual species or behavior:

- **Thousands of fish simultaneously.** A major PNW river at peak spawning run may contain thousands to tens of thousands of salmon at any given moment. Each fish is actively moving, splashing, competing, spawning. The acoustic output of a single salmon in a shallow riffle is modest (E=4-5). The acoustic output of thousands of salmon simultaneously is the superposition of thousands of individual events into a continuous wall of water-sound.
- **The sound wall.** At a river where the spawning run is at peak density, the acoustic character changes from individual events to a continuous texture: a sustained roar of splashing water, occasional loud tail-slaps that rise above the texture, the churning hiss of many bodies moving through shallow water, the gurgle and rush of a river whose usual acoustic character is overlaid by biological activity. The listener on the bank hears not individual fish but the river itself as a living system.
- **Multi-species superposition.** The full run contains multiple species spawning at overlapping times. In major Washington rivers (the Skykomish, Sauk, Skagit, Hoh, Quinault), fall runs include Chinook, Coho, Chum, Pink (in even years), and sometimes overlap with early Steelhead. Each species has slightly different spawning behavior and acoustic signature. The superposition of multiple species produces a richer acoustic texture than any single species alone.
- **The redd as acoustic node.** The individual redd (spawning nest in gravel) is an acoustic focal point: the female excavates and maintains it with vigorous tail-sweeping (audible tail-slap on gravel), males fight for position (splashing, body collision, tail-slap), the gravel moves as fish work it. A section of river with multiple active redds in close proximity is the densest component of the spawning run sound.
- **E=8 calibration.** The full river aggregate exceeds the individual species entries (E=5-7) because it is the superposition of all of them simultaneously. The aggregate is the ecological event; the individual entries documented components. E=8 is the correct calibration for the full-run event.

## THE PNW SALMON CYCLE -- ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT

### The River as Living System at Peak

The full spawning run is more than an acoustic event:

- **The nutrient pulse.** Adult Pacific salmon have spent 2-5 years in the ocean (depending on species), accumulating marine-derived nutrients. When they return to spawn and die, their bodies decompose and release these nutrients into the river and riparian ecosystem. The nitrogen, phosphorus, and marine-derived carbon in salmon carcasses fertilize streamside vegetation, feed aquatic insects, and are carried by scavengers (bears, eagles, ravens, minks, coyotes) into the surrounding forest. The spawning run is a nutrient delivery system as much as a reproductive event.
- **The keystone event.** Pacific salmon are the Pacific Northwest's most important ecological keystone. The spawning run supports: bears (pre-hibernation hyperphagia), Bald Eagles (winter aggregations at spawning rivers), Great Blue Herons, mergansers, otters, minks, ravens, and the full aquatic food web. The run is the seasonal peak of an ecological system that depends on it.
- **The river in fall.** A major Pacific Northwest river in October: deep green pools holding fresh-run Chinook; riffles orange-red with spawning Coho; the smell of decomposing spent fish; bears on the banks; eagles in the cottonwoods; the river running amber with tannins and salmon. This is the Pacific Northwest at its most ecologically concentrated.
- **Cross-reference to Screaming Trees (S36 307).** The Screaming Trees are from Ellensburg, 100 miles east of Seattle, over the Cascades. The Yakima River runs through Ellensburg. The Yakima River has salmon runs -- Chinook and Coho returning to the Columbia River drainage. The geographic origin of the Screaming Trees places them in the river drainage of a salmon system. The pairing is not coincidental in a geographic sense.

(Sources: Marine-derived nutrient pulse; nitrogen and phosphorus in salmon carcasses; riparian fertilization; bears and pre-hibernation hyperphagia; Bald Eagle winter aggregations; river scavenger community; PNW ecological keystone; fall river description; Yakima River salmon; Ellensburg-salmon geographic connection)
