The Aggregate Sound — Why E=8

A major Pacific Northwest river at peak spawning run (Skykomish, Sauk, Skagit, Hoh, Quinault) may contain thousands to tens of thousands of salmon at any given moment. Each fish is actively moving, splashing, competing, spawning. The acoustic output of any single fish is modest (E=4–5). The acoustic output of thousands simultaneously is the superposition of thousands of individual events into a continuous wall of water-sound.

The sound character changes fundamentally at full-run density: individual fish events become indistinguishable within the continuous texture. Occasional loud tail-slaps rise above the background. The overall character is a sustained roar of splashing water, the churning hiss of many bodies through shallow riffles, the percussion of gravel being worked at redds. The river is transformed: its usual acoustic identity is replaced by biological activity at maximum density.

The PNW Salmon Ecology — The Nutrient Pulse

The full spawning run is the Pacific Northwest's most significant annual ecological event. Adult salmon carry 2–5 years of marine-derived nutrients into the river system. Their decomposing carcasses fertilize riparian vegetation, feed aquatic insects, and are distributed deep into the surrounding forest by bears, eagles, ravens, otters, and minks. The salmon run is a nutrient delivery system from the Pacific Ocean to the Cascade forest. The acoustic event is the audible surface of this deeper ecological process.

The run supports the full scavenger community: Grizzly and Black Bear (hyperphagia before hibernation), Bald Eagle (winter aggregations at spawning rivers), Great Blue Heron, Common Merganser, River Otter, Mink. The engine has documented these species elsewhere; degree 307 is where their food source reaches maximum acoustic intensity.
