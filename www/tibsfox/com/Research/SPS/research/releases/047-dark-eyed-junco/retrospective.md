## Purpose

This is the forty-eighth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-47), the third Passerellidae, the second *Junco* appearance in the engine, and the degree that creates the engine's first taxonomic recursion. *Junco hyemalis* -- "the winter rush-bird," one of the most abundant birds in North America, Ellen Ketterson's landmark research subject, the "snowbird" of feeders and forest floors -- enters the engine as the species that CONTAINS degree 11. The Oregon Junco at degree 11 is a subspecies group within the Dark-eyed Junco at degree 47. The release carries six structural contributions:

1. **The snowbird -- the 48th acoustic element (species side).** A simple, musical, single-pitch trill of 7-23 notes repeated over 1-2 seconds. Named "the snowbird" for the bird's cultural identity as the harbinger of winter, the simplicity of its song (one note repeated like one crystal multiplied into a snowfall), and the ubiquity of its signal (~630 million individuals across North America). The first acoustic element defined by **elemental reduction** -- radical simplicity as acoustic strategy. The Passerellidae arc completes: preamble (complex, degree 45) to rasp (textural, degree 46) to snowbird (elemental, degree 47).

2. **Third consecutive Passerellidae -- unprecedented three-degree family streak.** Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47). Three genera (*Melospiza*, *Pipilo*, *Junco*). Three habitats (wetland edge, dense understory, forest floor). Three song strategies (complex, textural, simple). Only the second three-degree family streak in the engine (after Trochilidae at 35-36-37).

3. **Taxonomic recursion -- degree 47 contains degree 11.** The Dark-eyed Junco is the umbrella species that includes the Oregon Junco as one of its five subspecies groups. The first time the engine encounters a species that encloses a previous entry taxonomically. Container and contents at different positions in the sequence.

4. **The 1973 lump as counterpoint to the 1995 split.** Degree 46 documented the Spotted Towhee split (five-to-two). Degree 47 documents the junco lump (five-to-one). The Passerellidae run contains both directions of taxonomic revision within consecutive degrees.

5. **E=3 continues -- dynamic range equilibrium achieved.** Second consecutive E=3. E=2 + E=3 now total 21 degrees (43.8%), exactly matching E=4's 21 degrees (43.8%). The lower registers have caught up for the first time in the engine's history.

6. **CSV substitution documented.** The original CSV had Red-legged Frog at degree 47. Dark-eyed Junco replaces it, preserving E=3 and forest floor habitat. Third consecutive amphibian replacement (45-46-47). The engine is passing through the amphibian belt of the original CSV.

## What Worked

### 1. The taxonomic recursion is a genuinely novel structural event
No previous degree created a nesting relationship with an earlier entry. The Oregon Junco at degree 11 is a subset of the Dark-eyed Junco at degree 47. This forces the engine to think about taxonomic hierarchy in a way it has not before -- the distinction between parts and wholes, between subspecies and species, between entries that stand alone and entries that contain other entries.

### 2. The snowbird names a real acoustic principle
Elemental reduction -- the strategy of simplifying song to its absolute minimum while remaining effective -- is distinct from all previous acoustic elements. The preamble adds structure. The rasp adds texture. The snowbird subtracts. The principle maps cleanly to musical minimalism (Reich, Glass, Riley), giving the theory node genuine depth.

### 3. The Passerellidae streak reveals family-level patterns
Three consecutive entries from one family created opportunities for systematic comparison: habitat transect (water to forest floor), song gradient (complex to simple), size range (medium to large to small), naming evolution (acoustic to visual to non-acoustic). These patterns would be invisible without the streak.

### 4. The lump-split counterpoint is instructive
Documenting both the 1995 split (degree 46) and the 1973 lump (degree 47) within consecutive entries reveals the bidirectional nature of taxonomic revision and the role of different species concepts in reaching opposite conclusions about the same type of evidence.

### 5. The Ketterson/Margaret Morse Nice connection bridges degrees 45 and 47
The Margaret Morse Nice Medal -- named for the Song Sparrow researcher (degree 45) -- was awarded to Ketterson for junco research (degree 47). This cross-degree connection through scientific history enriches both entries and reveals that the Passerellidae run is not just a taxonomic event but a scientific-historical one.

### 6. The UCSD campus evolution case adds depth beyond standard ornithology
Rapid evolution in less than 20 years -- heritable changes in morphology, behavior, song, and breeding -- makes the Dark-eyed Junco a story about evolution in action, not just a species profile. This is the kind of depth that distinguishes SPS research from a field guide entry.

### 7. Dynamic range equilibrium is a genuine milestone
E=2 + E=3 equaling E=4 for the first time is not an arbitrary count -- it represents the engine's successful recovery from the seven-consecutive-E=4 streak (degrees 38-44) that had compressed the dynamic range. Three Passerellidae restored balance.
