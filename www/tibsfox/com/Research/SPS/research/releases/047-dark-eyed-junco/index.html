The 48th Acoustic Element: The Snowbird

The Dark-eyed Junco’s song is a simple, even, musical trill on a single pitch — 7–23 notes repeated over 1–2 seconds. Named “the snowbird” for the bird’s cultural identity as the harbinger of winter, the simplicity of its song (one note repeated like one crystal multiplied into a snowfall), and the ubiquity of its signal (~630 million individuals across North America).

The first acoustic element defined by elemental reduction — radical simplicity as strategy. After the preamble’s complexity (45) and the rasp’s intensity (46), the snowbird strips song to one note, one pitch, repeated.

Three Consecutive Passerellidae — Degrees 45–46–47

Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) — three consecutive entries from the same family. Three genera (Melospiza, Pipilo, Junco). Three song strategies (complex, textural, simple). Three habitats (wetland edge, dense understory, forest floor). The only family besides Trochilidae (35–36–37) to achieve a three-degree consecutive streak.

Ketterson’s Bird — The Premier Model Organism

Ellen D. Ketterson (Indiana University) has spent decades studying Dark-eyed Juncos at the Mountain Lake Biological Station, pioneering “phenotypic engineering” — experimentally manipulating testosterone to reveal life-history trade-offs. The junco is the subject of 1,000+ scientific papers, covering behavioral ecology, neuroendocrinology, speciation, and urban evolution. The Margaret Morse Nice Medal — named for the researcher who studied Song Sparrow (degree 45) — was awarded to Ketterson for junco research (degree 47). Two Passerellidae, one medal.

The Winter Rush-Bird

Junco hyemalis — from Spanish junco (rush plant, from Latin juncus) + Latin hiemalis (of winter). “The winter rush-bird” — named for the season it appears and the ground cover it haunts. Unlike the Song Sparrow’s pure-acoustic naming (“melody song-finch”) or the Spotted Towhee’s acoustic-visual split (“the spotted chirper”), the Dark-eyed Junco’s name contains NO sound reference. The junco is named for its presence, not its voice.

The Sound: Trill and Tik

Song: 1–2 seconds. A simple, even, musical trill — one syllable type repeated 7–23 times at constant pitch. Frequency 2.4–8.2 kHz, peak energy 3–6 kHz. The simplest song in the Passerellidae run.

Tik call: A sharp, hard, metallic “tik” or “tsick” lasting 0.02–0.05 seconds. Year-round contact and alarm call. The most common woodland sound in many PNW forests — so ubiquitous it fades into the background. Shared identically across all five subspecies groups.

Soft song: Low-amplitude song used in close-range aggressive encounters and mate-guarding. Documented by Reichard et al. (2013) — a quiet, aggressive vocal channel beneath the familiar broadcast trill.

The 1973 Lump

Before 1973, North America had at least five separate junco species: Oregon, Slate-colored, White-winged, Pink-sided, and Gray-headed. The AOU lumped all five into the Dark-eyed Junco based on hybridization in overlap zones. The lump remains contested. Molecular studies (Milá et al. 2007; Friis et al. 2016) show significant genetic divergence between forms. The hybrid zones are narrow and stable. Under the Phylogenetic Species Concept, the Oregon Junco is a species.

Taxonomic counterpoint: Degree 46 documented the 1995 Spotted Towhee/Eastern Towhee split. Degree 47 documents the 1973 junco lump. The Passerellidae run at 45–47 contains both splitting and lumping — a tutorial in taxonomic decision-making within a single family.

The UCSD Campus Juncos: Evolution in Real Time

In the early 1980s, Dark-eyed Juncos colonized the UC San Diego campus. In less than 20 years, the population diverged measurably from its mountain source: shorter wings and tails, reduced white tail flash, higher-pitched songs, reduced aggression, increased comfort around humans, extended breeding season (up to 4 clutches), and changed nest sites (buildings instead of ground). Genetic analysis confirmed heritable changes — genuine evolution, not just behavioral plasticity. One of the fastest documented cases of evolution in a wild vertebrate.
