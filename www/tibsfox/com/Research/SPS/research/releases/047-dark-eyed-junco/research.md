## Taxonomy: The Winter Rush-Bird

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Passerellidae (New World Sparrows)
- **Genus:** *Junco* (Wagler, 1831)
- **Species:** *Junco hyemalis* (Linnaeus, 1758)
- **Common Name:** Dark-eyed Junco
- **Nickname:** "Snowbird" -- arrives at lower elevations in winter

### Etymology

*Junco* -- from Spanish *junco*, meaning "rush" (the plant), from Latin *juncus* [1, 2, 5]. The genus name likely refers to the bird's association with rushes and dense ground cover in its habitat. An alternative derivation suggests it refers to the bird's rush-colored plumage (brown and gray).

*Hyemalis* -- from Latin *hiemalis*, meaning "of winter" or "belonging to winter" [1, 2, 5]. Named by Carl Linnaeus in 1758, because in Sweden (Linnaeus's home), juncos appeared only in winter. The seasonal name captures the bird's role as a harbinger of winter across much of North America -- the "snowbird" that arrives at feeders when the first cold fronts push south.

Full translation: **"The winter rush-bird"** -- a bird named for its winter timing and its ground-dwelling association with rushes and thickets. Unlike the Spotted Towhee's split naming (*Pipilo maculatus* = "the spotted chirper," acoustic + visual) or the Song Sparrow's pure-acoustic naming (*Melospiza melodia* = "melody song-finch"), the Dark-eyed Junco's name is entirely ecological and temporal: where it lives (rushes) and when it appears (winter). The sound is absent from the name. The junco is named for its presence, not its voice.

### The 1973 Lump and Its Aftermath

The Dark-eyed Junco is not one bird. It is a committee decision about five birds [1, 2, 5, 8, 13].

Before 1973, North America had at least five separate junco species:

1. **Oregon Junco** (*Junco oreganus*) -- the PNW form. Dark hood, rusty-brown back, pink-brown flanks.
2. **Slate-colored Junco** (*Junco hyemalis*) -- the eastern form. Uniform dark slate above, white below.
3. **White-winged Junco** (*Junco aikeni*) -- the Black Hills form. Larger, paler, white wing bars.
4. **Pink-sided Junco** -- the Rocky Mountain form. Broad pink flanks, gray hood.
5. **Gray-headed Junco** (*Junco caniceps*) -- the southern Rocky Mountain form. Gray head, rusty back.

In 1973, the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU) lumped all five into a single species: the Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*) [1, 2, 5, 8, 13]. The rationale was hybridization -- the five forms interbreed where their ranges overlap, and the AOU interpreted this as evidence of insufficient reproductive isolation under the Biological Species Concept. Five species became one.

**But the lump has always been contested.** Molecular phylogenetic studies (Mila et al. 2007 in *Evolution*; Friis et al. 2016 in *Molecular Ecology*) demonstrate significant genetic divergence between the forms -- particularly between the Oregon group and the Slate-colored group [1, 5, 8, 13]. The hybrid zones are narrow and stable, not expanding. Under the Phylogenetic Species Concept, or any species concept that emphasizes diagnosability over hybridization, the Oregon Junco is a species. The AOU's 1973 lump was a product of the "lumping era" in taxonomy; the pendulum has since swung toward splitting, and a future revision may restore some or all of the forms to full species status [1, 5, 8, 13].

The engine now contains BOTH sides of this taxonomic argument. Degree 11 treated the Oregon Junco as a distinct entity. Degree 47 treats the Dark-eyed Junco as a single species that contains the Oregon Junco. The engine does not resolve the argument; it documents both perspectives. The bird at the feeder does not care what the committee decides.

### Connection to the Spotted Towhee Split

At degree 46, the engine documented the 1995 Spotted Towhee/Eastern Towhee split -- a case where formerly lumped species were SPLIT based on vocal and genetic evidence. At degree 47, the engine documents the opposite event: a case where formerly split species were LUMPED. The Passerellidae run at degrees 45-47 contains both a splitting case (degree 46: Rufous-sided Towhee split into Spotted and Eastern, 1995) and a lumping case (degree 47: five junco species lumped into Dark-eyed Junco, 1973). The family's three-degree run is a tutorial in taxonomic decision-making.

## The Sound: The Snowbird's Trill

### Song Structure

The Dark-eyed Junco's song is a **simple, even, musical trill on a single pitch** -- a rapid repetition of one note, typically 7-23 notes delivered over 1-2 seconds [1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 10]. This is the entire song. One syllable type, repeated at a constant rate and pitch, for a duration that varies by individual and population.

This is the simplest song structure in the Passerellidae run:

| Degree | Species | Song Complexity | Parts | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 45 | Song Sparrow | HIGH -- preamble + elaborate body | Multi-part | 2-4 s |
| 46 | Spotted Towhee | MODERATE -- intro notes + buzzy trill | 1-2 part | ~1.5 s |
| **47** | **Dark-eyed Junco** | **SIMPLE -- even trill on single pitch** | **1 part** | **1-2 s** |

The Passerellidae arc is a simplification gradient: complex (Song Sparrow) to textured (Spotted Towhee) to simple (Dark-eyed Junco). The Song Sparrow composes. The Spotted Towhee concentrates. The Dark-eyed Junco reduces to the elemental: one note, repeated.

### Frequency Analysis

| Vocalization | Frequency Range | Duration | Trill Rate | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Song trill (LRS) | 2.4-8.2 kHz (full range); peak energy 3-6 kHz | 1.3-2.0 s | ~7-23 notes/s | Territory, mate attraction |
| Tik call | 4-8 kHz (broadband, sharp) | 0.02-0.05 s | Single | Contact, alarm, social |
| Soft tsip | 3-6 kHz | 0.03-0.08 s | Single | Flock contact, mild alarm |

The song frequency range (2.4-8.2 kHz, concentrated 3-6 kHz) overlaps broadly with the Oregon Junco's range documented at degree 11 (3-6 kHz). The degree 11 entry described the trill at 10-25 notes per second -- subsequent research on multiple populations documents a broader range of approximately 7-23 notes per trill (not per second) delivered over 1.3-2.0 seconds, yielding trill rates of approximately 5-18 notes per second depending on the song type [7, 10, 11]. Studies of free-living females have documented trill rates up to 24.4 notes per second [10].

**The trill rate matters.** At degree 11, the engine discussed the perceptual fusion threshold -- the rate above which discrete notes are perceived as a continuous buzz. For the Oregon Junco, the fusion threshold was approximately 15 notes/s. At rates below this threshold, the listener hears individual notes (a rattle). Above it, the notes fuse into a sustained metallic tone. The Dark-eyed Junco's song spans this boundary -- some song types are slow enough to be heard as discrete notes, others are fast enough to fuse. The species-wide repertoire contains BOTH sides of the fusion threshold.

This is the key acoustic difference between the degree 11 entry and the degree 47 entry. Degree 11 focused on the Oregon Junco's rapid metallic trill -- the form that fuses. Degree 47 must account for the full range across all five subspecies groups, which includes both slower trills (heard as discrete notes) and faster trills (heard as continuous buzz). The Dark-eyed Junco's song is not a single acoustic phenomenon; it is a spectrum of trill rates, unified by the structural principle of single-pitch repetition.

### The 48th Acoustic Element: The Snowbird

The Dark-eyed Junco introduces the engine's 48th acoustic element (species side): **the snowbird**.

The snowbird is a simple, musical, single-pitch trill that serves as a territorial and mate-attraction broadcast -- the elemental trill stripped to its most basic form. Where the Oregon Junco's "trill/tremolo" at degree 11 was defined by the fusion threshold (discrete pulses perceived as continuous tone), the snowbird at degree 47 is defined by **simplicity as strategy**: the deliberate reduction of song to one note repeated at one pitch for one to two seconds.

The snowbird is distinct from all nearby acoustic elements:

| Element | Character | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| The Preamble (45) | Clear opening notes before complexity | Positional structure |
| The Rasp (46) | Concentrated buzzy trill | Textural intensity |
| **The Snowbird (47)** | **Simple even trill on single pitch** | **Elemental reduction** |

The snowbird differs from "the rasp" (degree 46, Spotted Towhee) because the rasp is defined by textural concentration -- a buzzy, vibrating quality produced by rapid frequency modulation. The snowbird is cleaner and more musical -- individual notes are more tonal, less buzzy. The rasp fills the underbrush with rough texture. The snowbird rings through the forest floor with a clear, metallic simplicity.

The snowbird also differs from "trill/tremolo" (degree 11, Oregon Junco) because degree 11's element was about the PERCEPTUAL phenomenon of fusion -- discrete-to-continuous conversion at the threshold. The snowbird at degree 47 is about the STRATEGIC phenomenon of simplicity -- a species that communicates effectively with the simplest possible song structure. Degree 11 named the process. Degree 47 names the principle.

Named "the snowbird" for three resonances:
1. **The bird's identity** -- the Dark-eyed Junco is colloquially known as the "snowbird" across North America, arriving at feeders with the first winter storms. The name is a cultural artifact: when the juncos appear, winter has arrived.
2. **The simplicity** -- a snowbird's song is like a snowflake's structure: deceptively simple, a single repeated element that covers the landscape. One note repeated is all a junco needs. The song's simplicity is its strength, the way snow's simplicity is its power -- one crystal, but six hundred million of them.
3. **The ubiquity** -- approximately 630 million Dark-eyed Juncos. One of the most abundant birds in North America. The snowbird is everywhere in winter, the way snow is everywhere. The simple trill is one of the most commonly heard bird sounds on the continent.

The snowbird is the engine's first element defined by **elemental reduction** -- an acoustic event stripped to its absolute minimum while remaining maximally effective. After the preamble's structural complexity (45), the rasp's textural intensity (46), and now the snowbird's radical simplicity (47), the Passerellidae run has traversed the full spectrum from elaborate to elemental.

### The Tik Call

The Dark-eyed Junco's other primary vocalization is a sharp **"tik"** or **"tsick"** -- a short, hard, metallic chip note used year-round for flock communication, alarm, and social contact [1, 2, 3, 6, 7]. The tik call is:

- **Short:** 0.02-0.05 seconds -- one of the shortest vocalizations in the engine
- **Sharp:** Abrupt onset and offset, no frequency sweep, no modulation
- **Metallic:** Hard, dry, percussive -- not whistled, not buzzy, but clicked
- **Broadband:** Centered around 4-8 kHz
- **Ubiquitous:** The most common woodland sound in many PNW forests. Walk any trail in Washington in any season, and you will hear junco tik calls. The sound is so common it fades into the background, like the forest's own metronome.

The tik call was documented at degree 11 as the chip note. At degree 47, the engine recognizes that this call note is shared across ALL Dark-eyed Junco subspecies groups -- it is the one vocalization that unifies the complex. Oregon, Slate-colored, Pink-sided, Gray-headed, and Red-backed juncos all produce essentially the same tik call [1, 2, 7]. Song varies by population; the tik does not. The call is more conserved than the song -- a stable signal in an otherwise variable complex.

### Low-Amplitude Song (Soft Song)

Research by Reichard et al. (2013) documented a previously understudied vocalization: the **low-amplitude song** (soft song), produced by male Dark-eyed Juncos in close-range aggressive contexts [10]. Unlike the long-range song (LRS) which broadcasts territory ownership, the soft song is delivered at much lower volume during close encounters with rivals and during mate-guarding. The soft song's existence demonstrates that the junco's vocal repertoire is not limited to the loud trill -- there is a quiet, aggressive channel operating beneath the familiar broadcast.

## Biology: The Six-Hundred-Million Bird

### Population and Abundance

The Dark-eyed Junco is one of the most abundant birds in North America. Estimates of the total population vary by source:

- **~630 million** individuals -- the highest published estimate, making it one of the most numerous land birds on the continent [1, 5]
- **~260 million** -- Partners in Flight estimate of the North American breeding population [2, 14]
- **~220 million** -- Partners in Flight global breeding population estimate [14]

Regardless of which estimate is most accurate, the Dark-eyed Junco is extraordinarily common. It may be the second most abundant bird in North America after the American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*), which entered the engine at degree 25 [1, 2, 5]. The junco and the robin: the two most common birds in the engine, appearing at degrees 47 and 25 respectively.

The Dark-eyed Junco is a medium-small sparrow -- smaller than the Song Sparrow and much smaller than the Spotted Towhee [1, 2, 5, 6]:

|---|---|---|---|
| Length | 13-17 cm (5.1-6.7 in) | 12-17 cm (4.7-6.7 in) | 17-21 cm (6.7-8.3 in) |

### Plumage: The Oregon Form

The Oregon Dark-eyed Junco (*J. h. oreganus*) -- the PNW form and the form most relevant to the engine -- has distinctive plumage [1, 2, 5, 6]:

**Male:**
- **Hood:** Dark slate to blackish, sharply contrasting with the body
- **Back:** Warm rusty-brown to chestnut
- **Flanks:** Pink-brown to rufous
- **Belly:** White
- **Tail:** Dark with conspicuous **white outer tail feathers** -- the diagnostic flash pattern
- **Bill:** Pale pinkish -- the junco's trademark

**Female:** Similar to male but with paler, grayer hood and less contrast overall.

The Oregon form is instantly distinguishable from the Slate-colored form (uniform dark slate above) in the field. The dark-hood-plus-brown-back combination is diagnostic [1, 2, 5, 6].

### The White Tail Flash

The Dark-eyed Junco's **white outer tail feathers** are among the most studied plumage features in ornithology [1, 2, 5, 6, 12]. When a junco flushes, the white outer rectrices flash conspicuously -- a sudden burst of white against the dark tail that disappears the instant the bird lands and folds its tail.

The flash pattern has been proposed to serve multiple functions [1, 2, 5, 6, 12]:
- **Predator confusion:** The sudden flash of white during escape flight may startle or misdirect pursuing predators -- the "flash and freeze" strategy where the predator tracks the white flash and loses the bird when it disappears upon landing.
- **Flock alarm signal:** The white flash alerts other flock members to danger. When one junco flushes, the flash signals others to flush as well.
- **Pursuit deterrent:** The conspicuous flash may signal to predators that the junco is aware and alert, making pursuit energetically unprofitable. "I see you; don't bother."
- **Species recognition:** The amount of white varies among subspecies groups and may play a role in mate recognition and subspecies identification.

The white tail flash connects the Dark-eyed Junco to the Oregon Junco (degree 11), where the engine first documented this signal. At degree 47, the engine can now note that the white tail flash varies geographically across the complex -- Oregon Juncos show moderate white, Slate-colored Juncos show extensive white, and the UCSD campus juncos (see below) show LESS white than their mountain ancestors, suggesting rapid evolution of this signal trait [12, 15].

### Ground Nesting

The Dark-eyed Junco nests on the ground -- a trait shared with the Spotted Towhee (degree 46) but distinct from the Song Sparrow (degree 45), which nests in low shrubs as well as on the ground [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

- **Nest site:** Almost always on the ground, well hidden under overhanging grass, a log, a rock, exposed roots, or in a shallow hole in a dirt bank [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]. The nest is sunk into the ground so the rim is level with the surrounding litter.
- **Nest construction:** Open cup of grass, weeds, and leaves, lined with fine grass, hair, and sometimes feathers. Built mostly by the female [1, 2, 3, 5].
- **Eggs:** 3-6, white to pale bluish-white with brown speckles concentrated at the larger end [1, 2, 5].
- **Incubation:** 12-13 days, by the female alone [1, 2, 5].
- **Nestling period:** 9-13 days. Both parents feed nestlings [1, 2, 5].
- **Broods per season:** 1-3, with up to 4 in the UCSD campus population [1, 2, 5, 15].
- **Breeding season:** Males begin singing in March in the PNW; breeding extends through July [1, 2, 3, 4].

Ground nesting connects all three Passerellidae in the engine. Song Sparrow, Spotted Towhee, and Dark-eyed Junco all nest at or near ground level. The family's life is organized around the forest floor.

### Diet

The Dark-eyed Junco is a **granivore-insectivore** with a seasonal dietary shift [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

**Breeding season:** Primarily insects -- beetles, caterpillars, ants, wasps, flies, spiders, and other arthropods gleaned from the ground and low vegetation.

**Non-breeding season:** Primarily seeds -- grass seeds, weed seeds, pine seeds, and feeder seeds (millet, sunflower). The junco is one of the most common feeder birds in North America, preferring to forage on the ground beneath hanging feeders rather than perching on the feeder itself [1, 2, 3, 5, 6].

## Ellen Ketterson and the Junco as Model Organism

The Dark-eyed Junco is not just a common backyard bird. It is one of the most intensively studied wild songbirds in the world, largely due to the lifetime work of **Ellen D. Ketterson** at Indiana University [9, 11, 16].

### Ketterson's Research Program

Ellen Ketterson is a Distinguished Professor of Biology at Indiana University and the director of the Environmental Resilience Institute. Since the 1970s, she and her longtime collaborator Val Nolan Jr. conducted a landmark long-term study of Dark-eyed Juncos at the Mountain Lake Biological Station in Virginia [9, 11, 16].

Their research pioneered an approach called **"phenotypic engineering"** -- experimentally manipulating hormone levels (primarily testosterone) in free-living birds, then documenting the phenotypic consequences and relating them to fitness [9, 11, 16]. By inserting testosterone implants in male juncos and immediately releasing them back on their home territories, Ketterson's group discovered that:

- **Testosterone increases aggression** -- implanted males defended territories more vigorously
- **Testosterone reduces paternal care** -- implanted males fed nestlings less
- **Testosterone increases singing** -- implanted males sang more long-range songs
- **These effects create trade-offs** -- a male that sings more and feeds less attracts more mates but loses more offspring to starvation

The junco became the premier model organism for understanding **life-history trade-offs** -- the fundamental evolutionary concept that organisms cannot maximize all traits simultaneously, that investment in one function (reproduction, survival, aggression, parental care) comes at the cost of another [9, 11, 16].

### Awards and Recognition

Ketterson has received the American Ornithological Society's Elliott Coues Award (1996), the Wilson Ornithological Society's Margaret Morse Nice Medal (1998, jointly with Val Nolan Jr.), and the Cooper Ornithological Society's Alden Miller Award (2014) [9, 11]. The Margaret Morse Nice Medal is particularly resonant -- Margaret Morse Nice conducted the landmark studies on Song Sparrow (degree 45), and now the medal named for her is awarded for studies on the junco (degree 47). The Passerellidae run connects the two most studied songbirds in North American ornithology.

### The Junco as Research Subject

The Dark-eyed Junco has been the subject of over 1,000 scientific papers. It serves as a model organism for:
- **Behavioral ecology** -- territorial behavior, mate choice, parental investment
- **Neuroendocrinology** -- hormonal control of behavior, testosterone trade-offs
- **Evolutionary biology** -- life-history evolution, phenotypic integration
- **Speciation** -- the junco complex as a model for divergence and hybridization
- **Urban ecology** -- the UCSD campus population as a model for rapid urban evolution
- **Migration biology** -- differential migration patterns between sexes and populations

No other bird in the engine has been the subject of such intensive scientific study. The American Robin (degree 25) is more culturally familiar. The Song Sparrow (degree 45, via Margaret Morse Nice) has a longer research pedigree. But the Dark-eyed Junco has the broadest and deepest modern research program -- a species whose biology has been dissected at every level from genes to hormones to behavior to ecology to evolution.

## The UCSD Campus Juncos: Evolution in Real Time

One of the most remarkable discoveries in recent avian biology is the **rapid evolution of the UCSD campus junco population** [12, 15].

### The Founding Event

In the early 1980s, a small group of Dark-eyed Juncos (Oregon form, *J. h. thurberi* subspecies) from the nearby Laguna Mountains colonized the campus of the University of California, San Diego [12, 15]. This was unusual -- juncos typically breed in montane conifer forests, not on coastal lowland university campuses. But the UCSD campus offered an urban microhabitat with gardens, irrigation, year-round food, and mild temperatures.

### Rapid Phenotypic Change

By the time ecologists Trevor Price and Pamela Yeh began studying the population in 1997, the campus juncos had already diverged noticeably from their mountain source population in less than two decades [12, 15]:

1. **Shorter wings and tails** -- morphological changes consistent with reduced migration and more sedentary behavior
2. **Reduced white tail flash** -- less white in the outer rectrices, suggesting relaxed sexual selection or predator pressure
3. **Reduced aggression** -- males were less territorial, more tolerant of neighbors
4. **Increased comfort around humans** -- reduced flight initiation distance
5. **Higher-pitched songs** -- subtle but measurable shift in song frequency, possibly adaptation to urban noise
6. **Extended breeding season** -- up to four clutches per year over a seven-month breeding season (vs. 1-2 clutches in mountain populations)
7. **Changed nest sites** -- females nesting on buildings and in trees (juncos are typically ground nesters)
8. **Increased monogamy** -- males spending more time at the nest, less time pursuing extra-pair matings

Genetic analysis confirmed that these changes were partly heritable -- not just behavioral plasticity but genuine evolutionary change [12, 15]. The UCSD juncos evolved measurably in approximately 20 years. This is one of the fastest documented cases of evolution in a wild vertebrate population.

### Significance for the Engine

The UCSD campus juncos demonstrate that the Dark-eyed Junco is not just a common bird but an **evolutionarily dynamic** one -- a species actively adapting to novel environments in real time. The engine's 360-species sequence captures species at a snapshot, but the junco reminds us that the snapshot is always in motion. The bird at the feeder today is not genetically identical to the bird at the feeder thirty years ago, even if it looks the same.

## PNW Habitat: Forest Floor and Woodland Edge

### Habitat Description

The Dark-eyed Junco's primary breeding habitat in the PNW is **forest floor and mixed woodland edge** -- the open ground layer beneath conifer and mixed forests, from sea level to the subalpine zone [1, 2, 3, 4, 6]. The junco occupies a broader habitat range than either the Song Sparrow (wetland edge) or the Spotted Towhee (dense understory), moving from deep forest openings to suburban yards to mountain meadow edges.

Specific habitat types in the PNW include [1, 2, 3, 4, 6]:
- Moist conifer forests with open understory -- Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar
- Mixed conifer-deciduous woodland edges
- Second-growth forest with moderate understory density
- Forest clearings and meadow margins at all elevations
- Suburban yards, parks, and gardens -- the most common winter feeder bird in the Seattle area
- Trailhead parking lots and picnic areas -- juncos are the birds hopping on the gravel
- Logged clearings with residual canopy
- Subalpine meadow edges at the tree line
- Ponderosa pine woodlands in eastern Washington

### Vertical Position

The Dark-eyed Junco operates primarily at **0-3 m** above the ground [1, 2, 3, 6]:
- **Foraging:** On the ground, hopping and scratching in leaf litter and on bare soil. Less vigorous than the Spotted Towhee's double-footed scratch but using a similar technique.
- **Singing:** Males sing from elevated perches at 3-10 m -- higher than the Spotted Towhee (1-4 m) but lower than most canopy species. Singing perches are typically at the top of a small tree or on a prominent branch at the forest edge.
- **Nesting:** On the ground, sunk into litter or earth.

Three species, three habitat textures, same vertical zone. The Passerellidae run is a ground-level transect from water to undergrowth to forest floor.

### Altitudinal Migration in the PNW

While the Oregon form is considered "resident" in western Washington, many individuals perform **altitudinal migration** -- breeding at higher elevations in mountain forests and descending to lowland towns, parks, and feeders in winter [1, 2, 4, 6]. This is the "snowbird" behavior that gives the species its nickname: juncos arrive at lowland feeders in October and depart for the mountains in April. The bird is present in the PNW year-round, but the individual you see at your feeder in January may have spent the summer at 1,500 meters in the Cascades.

Males winter farther north and at higher elevations than females -- a pattern called **differential migration** that Ketterson and Nolan documented extensively [9, 11]. This sex-based migration difference means that winter flocks in the PNW lowlands tend to have more females, while winter flocks at higher elevations tend to have more males.

### Why E=3

The Dark-eyed Junco's song is moderately loud -- audible at a distance of 100+ meters in open forest but not a rooftop broadcaster [1, 2, 3, 6]. The trill carries through open woodland with moderate vegetation but does not penetrate dense understory as effectively as the Spotted Towhee's buzzy rasp. The junco sings from higher perches (3-10 m) than the towhee (1-4 m), but its song is cleaner and thinner -- less bandwidth, less penetrating power per unit distance. E=3 captures this middle position: louder than the Song Sparrow's E=2 (which sings from open wetland edge where propagation is easy), equal to the Spotted Towhee's E=3 (which compensates for dense vegetation with a buzzy, broad-bandwidth signal), but quieter than the American Robin's E=4 (which projects from rooftops and treetops).

The three consecutive E=3 entries (actually E=2 then E=3 then E=3 at 45-46-47) create a Passerellidae energy band: this family operates at E=2-3, the lower-middle register. None of the engine's three Passerellidae have the broadcasting power of the thrushes (mostly E=4) or the hummingbirds (E=5). The sparrows are quiet birds. Their songs are intimate, local, ground-level -- designed for the immediate neighborhood, not the landscape.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 5, 14]
- **Global population:** ~220-630 million depending on estimate (Partners in Flight, 2022; BirdLife International) [1, 2, 5, 14]
- **Continental Concern Score:** 8/20 (low concern) [1, 2, 14]
- **BBS trend:** Populations declined approximately 0.7% per year between 1966 and 2019, a cumulative decline of about 31% [1, 2, 14]
- **Population loss since 1970:** Approximately 168 million individuals lost, with regional declines exceeding 40% in some areas [14]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 [1, 2]
- **Threats:** Habitat loss, climate change (range shifts), window strikes, cat predation, pesticide exposure [1, 2, 5, 14]

Despite its enormous population, the Dark-eyed Junco is declining. The 31% cumulative decline since 1966 -- approximately 168 million birds lost -- is significant even for a species with hundreds of millions of individuals. Climate change is projected to shift the junco's breeding range northward and upslope, reducing suitable habitat in the southern and lower-elevation portions of its range [14]. In the PNW, the Oregon form remains common, but altitudinal shifts in breeding range have been documented [4, 14].

The contrast with the Spotted Towhee (degree 46) is instructive: the towhee's population is stable or increasing in western Washington (benefiting from shrubby habitat creation), while the junco's population is slowly declining (losing forest habitat and facing climate-driven range compression). Two Passerellidae, opposite population trajectories.
