### The Ultrasonic Threshold and the Receiver's Silence: Where Perception Ends

Pass 1 documented the Golden-crowned Kinglet's song frequency (6-10 kHz) as near or above the upper limit of adult human hearing, making it inaudible to many birders over 40. Pass 2 develops this into a structural theory of receiver-dependent silence and connects it to Vanguard 1's silent persistence and licorice fern's seasonal disappearance.

The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) established silence as the absence of signal — the transmitter is silent. The Golden-crowned Kinglet (degree 8) establishes silence as the absence of reception — the transmitter is active but the receiver cannot decode the signal. These are fundamentally different kinds of silence, and the engine now has both. The eight degrees between them (1-7) produced sounds within the range of human hearing — signals that were both transmitted and received. Degree 8 crosses the boundary. The circle returns to silence, but the silence is inside the listener, not in the forest.

Vanguard 1's transmitters fell silent in 1964. The satellite continues to orbit — visible to radar, trackable by optical observation — but it does not transmit. For sixty-two years, Vanguard 1 has been a signal source that produces no signal. It exists but cannot be heard. The kinglet is the biological inverse: it produces a signal that many listeners cannot hear. The satellite transmits nothing; the bird transmits something inaudible. Both produce silence at the receiver, through opposite mechanisms.

Licorice fern exhibits its own version of this pattern through seasonal disappearance. The fern is a winter-green, summer-deciduous epiphyte — it grows during the wet autumn and winter months, then desiccates and drops its fronds during the dry summer. A bigleaf maple that is festooned with bright green licorice ferns in January appears bare of ferns in August. The fern has not died. Its rhizome persists on the mossy branch, alive but invisible. The fern is present but imperceptible — the botanical equivalent of the kinglet's inaudible song. The receiver (the hiker's eye in August, the birder's ear at 60 years old) cannot detect what is there.

Three systems, three kinds of imperceptibility:
- **Kinglet:** Signal exists, receiver too old to hear it (biological Nyquist limit)
- **Vanguard 1:** Transmitter exists, signal ceased (hardware failure)
- **Licorice fern:** Organism exists, visual presence seasonal (phenological cycle)

All three are present but undetectable by the standard receiver at certain times or under certain conditions. Degree 8 establishes that the boundary between presence and absence is not in the object. It is in the observer.

### The Chickadee Network Extends: Confirmed Third Node

The Golden-crowned Kinglet is a confirmed participant in the Black-capped Chickadee's interspecies alarm network. Multiple studies have documented that kinglets associate with chickadee-led mixed-species foraging flocks in winter and respond to chickadee alarm calls with increased vigilance behavior. While the experimental evidence is less precise than the Templeton and Greene (2007) nuthatch study (which demonstrated D-note threat-level extraction), the kinglet's responsiveness to chickadee alarms is well-documented in the mixed-species flock literature (Morse 1970; Sullivan 1984).
