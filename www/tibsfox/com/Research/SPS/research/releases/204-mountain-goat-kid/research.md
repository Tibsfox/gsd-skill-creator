## THE ALPINE NURSERY

### Born on the Cliff

Mountain Goat kids enter one of the harshest nursery environments on Earth:

- **Birth timing.** Kids are born in late May to early June, after the worst of winter has passed but while snow still covers much of the alpine zone. Nannies seek out the most inaccessible cliff ledges for birthing -- the same cliff fortress strategy that protects adults from predators protects the newborn from ground-based threats.
- **Precocial development.** Mountain Goat kids are remarkably precocial: they can stand within hours of birth and follow the nanny across rocky terrain within days. This precocity is survival-critical -- a kid that cannot keep up with its mother on cliff terrain will not survive.
- **First steps on rock.** Within the first week, kids are climbing rock faces that would challenge an adult human. Their hooves, while smaller, have the same rubbery-pad design as adult hooves. The grip is innate. The balance is learned through constant practice on the cliff nursery.
- **The nanny's vigilance.** Nannies with kids are hyper-vigilant. They position themselves between the kid and any perceived threat. Golden Eagles are the primary aerial predator of kids -- nannies watch the sky constantly during the first weeks.
- **Nursing and growth.** Kids nurse for 3-4 months, supplementing with alpine vegetation as it becomes available through the summer. By fall, kids weigh 15-25 kg (from a birth weight of 3-4 kg). They stay with the nanny through their first winter.

(Sources: Mountain Goat reproductive biology; kid development; alpine nursery; precocial development; Golden Eagle predation)

## THE KID'S MEW -- THIN VOICE ON STONE

### Sound in the Alpine Nursery

The kid's contact call is adapted to the alpine environment:

- **The mew.** "Meeeh" -- high, thin, wavering. Pitched at 400-1,000 Hz. Higher than the adult bleat (200-600 Hz) by roughly an octave. The mew is produced by smaller vocal cords in a smaller body cavity, giving it a thinner, less resonant quality.
- **Context.** The kid mews when separated from the nanny -- even by a few meters on cliff terrain. Visual contact can be lost quickly behind rock outcrops. The mew is the auditory tether: "Where are you? I cannot see you. Come back."
- **Nanny response.** The nanny responds to the kid's mew immediately, bleating in return and moving toward the kid. The response is urgent because separation on cliff terrain is life-threatening -- a lost kid on an alpine cliff face may be unable to navigate back to the nanny independently.
- **Alpine amplification.** Like the adult bleat, the kid's mew benefits from rock-surface reflection. The thin mew carries further on stone than it would in forest or grassland. Alpine acoustics compensate for the kid's small voice.
- **Eagle context.** The kid's mew may also attract predators -- Golden Eagles can hear high-pitched sounds from considerable distance. The mew is a survival necessity (maintaining maternal contact) that creates a survival risk (attracting aerial predators). This tension drives the nanny's protective behavior: stay close, respond fast, keep the kid's distress duration minimal.

(Sources: Mountain Goat kid vocalization; maternal response; alpine acoustics; Golden Eagle predation; survival trade-offs)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Mountain Goat Kid

**Kid Contact Call (Separation Distress):**
- **Frequency:** 400-1,000 Hz. Higher than adult bleat. Roughly one octave above.
- **Character:** High, thin, wavering mew. "Meeeh." Less resonant than adult. Smaller body cavity.
- **Duration:** 0.3-1.0 seconds per mew. Repeated every 3-8 seconds when separated.
- **Context:** Separation from nanny. Visual contact lost on cliff terrain. First summer (May-September).
- **Carry distance:** 50-200 meters on stone. Rock reflection extends range. Shorter range than adult bleat.

**Kid Contentment (Following/Nursing):**
- **Frequency:** 200-500 Hz. Lower, softer.
- **Character:** Quiet humming or soft bleating while following nanny or nursing. Barely audible beyond a few meters.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=3. Thin, high, infant-voiced. The quietest sound in the alpine zone. Alpine acoustics compensate partially. Sufficient for maternal contact at close range.
