## THE FAULT SYSTEMS BENEATH THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST

### What Is Below the Surface

The Pacific Northwest sits on one of the most seismically complex regions in North America:

- **The Cascadia Subduction Zone.** The dominant tectonic feature of the Pacific Northwest. The Cascadia Subduction Zone (CSZ) is a 700-mile-long convergent boundary where the Juan de Fuca oceanic plate (and its smaller companions the Explorer and Gorda plates) is being driven beneath the North American continental plate at a rate of approximately 40 millimeters per year. The subduction is not smooth or continuous -- the plates are stuck together along a segment of the boundary (the "locked zone"), and strain accumulates over centuries. When the locked zone finally slips, the result is a megathrust earthquake: one of the most powerful categories of seismic event on earth.
- **The Seattle Fault Zone.** Running east-west through the Seattle metropolitan area, the Seattle Fault Zone is a set of east-dipping reverse faults that have been seismically active in the past. The most recent major rupture on the Seattle Fault occurred approximately 1,100 years ago, producing an earthquake estimated at M7.0-7.5 and generating a tsunami in Puget Sound. The fault runs under present-day Seattle neighborhoods; a rupture would produce intense shaking directly beneath the city.
- **The South Whidbey Island Fault.** A fault zone that runs through Whidbey Island and into the eastern Puget Sound region. Less well-characterized than the Seattle Fault but with potential for significant earthquakes.
- **The Tacoma Fault.** Running through the Tacoma area, this fault system is another element of the complex tectonic environment of the Puget Lowland.
- **Deep intraslab earthquakes.** In addition to crustal faults and the CSZ interface, the Pacific Northwest experiences frequent deep intraslab earthquakes -- events occurring within the subducting Juan de Fuca plate, typically at depths of 30-70 km. These events are often moderate in magnitude (M4-6) but can be significant (the 2001 Nisqually earthquake was an intraslab event at approximately 52 km depth, M6.8).

## THE P-WAVE RUMBLE

### The Sound of the Seismic Arrival

The P-wave (primary wave, compressional wave) is the fastest seismic wave and arrives first:

- **P-wave physics.** A P-wave is a compressional wave: the motion of particles in the rock is in the same direction as the wave's propagation. Think of compressing and releasing a Slinky -- the compression pulse travels along the length; the Slinky's coils move back and forth in the direction the pulse is moving. P-waves travel through solid rock at speeds of approximately 5-7 km/second (more than 10,000 mph), making them the first seismic arrivals at any given location. They can also travel through liquid, which S-waves cannot.
- **The acoustic P-wave.** When a P-wave reaches the earth's surface, a portion of its energy converts to acoustic energy: the ground compresses and releases so rapidly that it produces a sound wave in the air. This acoustic P-wave (sometimes called an "air wave") produces the low rolling rumble that precedes the main earthquake shaking. At small-to-moderate earthquake magnitudes, this is often the only audible component of the seismic event. At larger magnitudes, the acoustic P-wave is followed within seconds by the S-wave and surface wave shaking.
- **What the P-wave rumble sounds like.** The acoustic P-wave produces a brief, directional, low-frequency rumble -- typically below 20 Hz but with some energy in the 20-100 Hz range depending on the earthquake's magnitude and distance. It has a specific directional quality: it seems to come from below and travel in a specific horizontal direction as the wave front passes. It lasts only seconds to perhaps 10 seconds at moderate distances from the source. Many people describe it as a low, brief "boom" or "rumble" that arrives before the shaking.
- **E=5 positioning.** E=5 is appropriate for the P-wave rumble: it is a real physical phenomenon with genuine acoustic impact, but it is not at the energy of the full shaking (which would be E=7-8 for a major event) or the catastrophic (E=9 for the lightning strike). The P-wave rumble is the announcement, not the event itself.
- **The warning function.** The P-wave arrives before the damaging S-waves and surface waves. The difference in arrival time between the P-wave and the S-wave is proportional to the distance from the earthquake's hypocenter. At the epicenter, there is no difference; at 100 km distance, the S-wave arrives approximately 10-15 seconds after the P-wave. This window is the basis for earthquake early warning systems (like ShakeAlert, used in the Pacific Northwest): detect the P-wave automatically, broadcast a warning, give people a few seconds before the shaking arrives.

## THE 2001 NISQUALLY EARTHQUAKE

### The Most Recent Major Seismic Event in the Region

The 2001 Nisqually earthquake is the engine's primary modern reference for a PNW earthquake:

- **Date and magnitude.** February 28, 2001. Magnitude 6.8. Epicenter near Nisqually, Washington, in the southern Puget Sound area. Depth: approximately 52 km. A deep intraslab earthquake within the subducting Juan de Fuca plate.
- **The deep source.** The Nisqually earthquake's depth of 52 km is a significant factor in its impact pattern. Deep earthquakes (>30 km) produce shaking that is felt over a wide area but is less intense at any given point than shallow-focus earthquakes of the same magnitude. The shaking from Nisqually was felt from British Columbia to northern California; it was most intense in the Olympia-Tacoma-Seattle corridor.
- **Damage.** The Nisqually earthquake caused approximately $2 billion in damage (2001 dollars) and injured approximately 400 people. One death was directly attributed to the earthquake. The damage was concentrated in older brick and unreinforced masonry structures. The Capitol building in Olympia suffered significant damage. Pioneer Square and the International District in Seattle experienced significant damage to older building stock.
- **The P-wave experience.** Many people in the region reported experiencing the Nisqually earthquake's P-wave arrival as a brief rumble or vibration that preceded the main shaking by 5-10 seconds. This is consistent with the physics: at 52 km depth and ~50 km horizontal distance from Olympia (where the damage was most severe), the P-S differential was approximately 6-7 seconds. The rumble was the announcement; the shaking arrived seconds later.
- **What Nisqually taught.** The 2001 earthquake accelerated seismic retrofitting programs in Seattle and Washington State, updated hazard mapping for the region, and refined understanding of the deep intraslab hazard. It also served as a reference point for public understanding of what a moderate-to-large PNW earthquake feels like.

(Sources: Nisqually earthquake date February 28, 2001; magnitude 6.8; epicenter Nisqually, WA; depth 52 km; intraslab earthquake type; felt area BC to northern California; $2 billion damage; 400 injuries; 1 death; unreinforced masonry damage; Capitol building Olympia; Pioneer Square damage; P-wave rumble as precursor experience; P-S differential 6-7 seconds at distance; seismic retrofitting acceleration post-Nisqually)

## THE CASCADIA SUBDUCTION ZONE -- THE REALLY BIG ONE

### The M9.0+ Event in the Pacific Northwest Future

The Cascadia Subduction Zone megathrust event is the defining unresolved seismic hazard in the Pacific Northwest:

- **The evidence.** Paleoseismic evidence (analysis of ancient earthquake deposits in coastal marshes, lake sediments, and offshore turbidite records) documents 41 CSZ earthquakes over the past 10,000 years, with magnitudes estimated in the M8.0-9.2 range. The most recent major CSZ event occurred on January 26, 1700 -- a full-margin rupture estimated at approximately M9.0. The earthquake produced a tsunami that crossed the Pacific and struck Japan, where it was recorded in historical documents. The 1700 date was established through tree-ring analysis of drowned coastal forests (the earthquake dropped portions of the coast into the ocean, killing trees instantaneously), Japanese historical records, and turbidite analysis.
- **The recurrence interval.** CSZ earthquakes do not occur on a fixed schedule, but the paleoseismic record suggests average recurrence intervals of 200-500 years for major events, with shorter intervals between smaller-magnitude events. The current gap since the 1700 event (326 years) is within the range of recurrence intervals documented in the geological record.
- **The impact of M9.0.** A full-margin CSZ rupture would produce shaking lasting 3-5 minutes (compared to seconds for Nisqually), affecting the entire Pacific Northwest coast from northern California to British Columbia. The shaking would be significantly more severe than Nisqually, especially for structures built on liquefiable soils (such as much of Seattle's waterfront and the Duwamish valley). The tsunami would arrive on the outer coast within 15-30 minutes; Puget Sound would experience secondary tsunami effects.
- **The 2015 reference.** Kathryn Schulz's 2015 New Yorker article "The Really Big One" brought the Cascadia hazard to national attention, winning the Pulitzer Prize for Feature Writing. The article described in accessible terms the geological evidence for the CSZ hazard, the inadequacy of preparation, and the likely consequences. The phrase "The Really Big One" entered the general vocabulary for the Cascadia threat.

(Sources: Paleoseismic evidence for CSZ; 41 earthquakes in 10,000 years; M8.0-9.2 range; January 26, 1700 last full rupture; M9.0 estimated; Pacific-crossing tsunami; Japanese historical records; tree-ring analysis drowned forests; turbidite analysis; 200-500 year average recurrence; 326-year current gap; M9.0 shaking duration 3-5 minutes vs. seconds; Puget Sound tsunami effects; Kathryn Schulz 2015 New Yorker; Pulitzer Prize Feature Writing; "The Really Big One")

## THE EARTHQUAKE EARLY WARNING SYSTEM

### ShakeAlert and the P-wave Window

- **ShakeAlert.** ShakeAlert is the US Geological Survey's earthquake early warning system for the West Coast, deployed across California, Oregon, and Washington. It uses a network of seismometers to detect P-wave arrivals automatically, calculate the earthquake's location and magnitude in near-real-time, and broadcast alerts to users (via mobile phones, emergency management systems, and automated controls for industrial processes) seconds before the damaging S-waves arrive.
- **The window.** The warning window from ShakeAlert ranges from a fraction of a second (for those very close to the epicenter) to tens of seconds (for those farther away). For a CSZ full-margin rupture, communities in the Seattle metropolitan area might receive 30-90 seconds of warning before damaging shaking arrives. This is sufficient time to take cover, stop surgical procedures, halt automated processes, and slow trains.
- **The P-wave as the trigger.** The P-wave rumble that degree 263 documents is the physical phenomenon at the basis of the entire ShakeAlert system. The low rolling subsonic rumble is the information carrier: the seismic network detects the P-wave, computes the parameters, and issues the alert -- all in the seconds before the damaging waves arrive. The acoustic P-wave and the early warning system are the same physical event, one observed by the human ear and one by the seismic network.

(Sources: ShakeAlert system description; USGS Pacific Northwest deployment; seismometer network; P-wave detection and parameter calculation; mobile phone alerts; emergency management systems; industrial process automation; CSZ Seattle warning window 30-90 seconds; ShakeAlert as P-wave-triggered early warning; acoustic P-wave and network P-wave detection as same physical event)
