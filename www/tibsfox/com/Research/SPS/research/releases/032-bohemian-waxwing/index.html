Bohemian Waxwing

Bombycilla garrulus · Family Bombycillidae (Waxwings) · Pair COMPLETE — 2 of 3 species worldwide
Buzzy Bzeee Calls Flock Chorus · Berry Stands · Irruptive · Energy 5 — E=4 STREAK BROKEN
They arrive without warning. One morning the mountain ash on the corner — the same tree that held a quiet flock of Cedar Waxwings last week — is alive with hundreds of larger, grayer, louder birds. The air fills with a buzzing roar: bzeee, bzeee, bzeee — coarser, lower, more assertive than the Cedar’s crystalline whisper. Bold white and yellow wing markings flash as they strip the tree bare in hours. Cinnamon undertails, rusty faces, the same sleek crests but bigger, chunkier, wilder. Bombycilla garrulus. The talkative silk-tail. The Bohemian — the vagabond, the wanderer from the boreal north. They came because the berries failed in the Yukon. They will leave when the berries run out here. The whisper has become a buzz. E=5.

E=5 — The Eleven-Degree E=4 Plateau BREAKS

Degrees 21–31: eleven consecutive E=4 — the longest unbroken energy plateau in engine history. The retrospectives at degrees 30 and 31 both declared it “critically must break.” It breaks here. E=5 is used for the first time. The dynamic range expands upward. The ceiling has been touched. The full five-level energy palette (E=1 through E=5) is now active.

E=5 is justified by: massive flock scale (hundreds to thousands), louder per-individual calls, irruptive arrival intensity, and structural necessity after eleven degrees of constraint.

Waxwing Pair Complete (Degrees 31–32)

Cedar Waxwing (31) + Bohemian Waxwing (32) = the engine’s third same-genus consecutive pair. The first pair with energy asymmetry (E=4 vs. E=5). Bombycillidae CLOSED at 2 of 3 species worldwide (66.7%).

Song Bioacoustics: Buzzy Bzeee Calls Flock Chorus

The Bohemian Waxwing produces a high, rapid, buzzy trill — transcribed as sirrrr or bzeee or zir-r-r-r. Rougher, lower-pitched, and less wavering than the Cedar Waxwing’s ethereal see-see-see. No true song — like its smaller cousin, the Bohemian Waxwing lacks territorial song. The bzeee is a contact and social call.

The 33rd acoustic element: the buzz. After the Cedar Waxwing’s crystalline whisper, the Bohemian delivers a buzzy, communal roar. Same sibilant timbral category, same genus — but amplified and coarsened. The glass harmonica becomes a kazoo choir. The sibilant category now has two textures: sibilant-crystalline (Cedar, 31) and sibilant-buzzy (Bohemian, 32).

Irruptive Ecology: The Sixth Seasonal Category

Irruption is fundamentally different from migration. Migration is predictable and calendar-driven. Irruption is unpredictable and food-driven — a displacement from boreal breeding grounds when winter berry crops cannot support the population. Triggers: interaction of berry crop failure, population overshoot, and autumn food availability. Major irruptions every 2–10 years. The irruptive species is a messenger from elsewhere: its arrival tells you about conditions in a place you cannot see.

Previous irruptive species: Pine Siskin (degree 12). The engine’s second irruptive species. Six seasonal categories now active: resident, migrant, partial migrant, summer, spring, irruptive.

Ecosystem: Berry Stands

Dense concentrations of fruit-bearing trees — mountain ash rows, hawthorn hedges, crabapple plantings, holly in residential yards. Often anthropogenic: ornamental fruit plantings in cities create habitat for irruptive waxwings. Refines “fruiting trees” (degree 31) toward concentrated, high-density resources that support massive flock events.

Key PNW species: mountain ash (Sorbus), juniper, hawthorn, crabapple, holly. Large flocks need dense berry stands — not scattered individual trees. The city as ecological infrastructure.

Taxonomy: Bombycilla garrulus

Bombycilla: Latin bombyx (silk) + -cilla (tail) = “silk-tail.” Garrulus: Latin “talkative, chattering.” Named by Linnaeus in 1758 (earlier applied by Gessner in 1555 as Garrulus Bohemicus). Full binomial: “talkative silk-tail.” Compare: Cedar Waxwing = “silk-tail of the cedars” (named for diet). Bohemian Waxwing = “talkative silk-tail” (named for perceived behavior). “Bohemian” = vagabond, wanderer — not the Czech region.

Bombycillidae CLOSED. 2 of 3 species worldwide in the engine (66.7%). Only the Japanese Waxwing (B. japonica) remains unrepresented. Highest proportional family coverage of any family. The engine’s first Holarctic species — breeds across both North America and Eurasia.

PNW Distribution: Rare Winter Visitor

Unlike the Cedar Waxwing (common year-round resident), the Bohemian Waxwing is a rare and irregular winter visitor to western Washington. Most common in northeastern Washington (Ferry, Okanogan, Stevens counties). Extends to the Kittitas Valley (Cle Elum, Ellensburg) in moderate years. Rarely reaches western Washington — typically found embedded within Cedar Waxwing flocks during major irruptions.

Finding a Bohemian Waxwing in Seattle is a genuine event. The coordinates reflect potential, not typicality. Key field marks: larger and chunkier, gray belly, cinnamon undertail coverts (vs. white in Cedar), bold white and yellow wing markings (absent in Cedar), reddish face tinge.

Conservation: Least Concern with Caveats

IUCN: Least Concern. Global breeding population ~5 million. However, Partners in Flight estimates a ~55% decline in North American populations since 1970 — though this must be interpreted with extreme caution given the species’ nomadic nature, which makes population monitoring difficult. Apparent declines may partly reflect a northward shift in wintering range rather than absolute population loss. Included on Partners in Flight’s list of Common Birds in Steep Decline.
