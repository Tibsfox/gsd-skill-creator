## Context: A New Family Enters (Again)

The waxwing pair closed at degree 32. Bombycillidae is done -- two silk-tailed frugivores, one whispering, one buzzing, the pair's energy contrast cracking the eleven-degree E=4 plateau. The buzz (degree 32) touched E=5 for the first time, then departed as suddenly as the Bohemian Waxwing itself.

Now the energy drops back. **E=4.** The single-degree E=5 spike was exactly that -- a spike. One degree of fortissimo, then a return to the dynamic level that dominated degrees 21-31. But the return is not a regression. The engine has touched E=5. It knows the ceiling is there. Coming back to E=4 after E=5 is different from staying at E=4 because you've never left. The E=4 at degree 33 is a choice, not a default.

And the family changes. **Fringillidae** returns. The true finches were last heard at degree 12 -- the Pine Siskin, *Spinus pinus*, with its ascending electronic zhreee, E=1, irruptive, the sawtooth sweep in feathers. Twenty-one degrees ago. Now a second Fringillidae enters the engine: **Cassin's Finch**, *Haemorhous cassinii*. Same family, different genus, different sound, different energy, different everything. The Pine Siskin was the engine's quietest electronic zap. The Cassin's Finch is a rolling, complex, melodic warble -- one of the most elaborate songs any finch produces in western North America.

Three structural events at degree 33:

1. **Fringillidae returns.** The second species from the true finch family, twenty-one degrees after the first. Pine Siskin (degree 12, *Spinus*, E=1, irruptive, ascending zhreee) and Cassin's Finch (degree 33, *Haemorhous*, E=4, summer, rich warbling song) -- same family, opposite ends of the energy and complexity spectra. Fringillidae is now the engine's second family with members in both the E=1 and E=4 tiers.

2. **Mountain conifer introduces the first explicitly montane ecosystem.** Previous ecosystems have been lowland, riparian, coastal, or generalized forest. "Mountain conifer" is the engine's first high-elevation habitat -- the dry open conifer forests of the eastern Cascades and Rockies at 3,000-10,000 feet. A new ecological dimension: altitude.

3. **Rich melodic warbling returns after whisper and buzz.** The acoustic progression moved from sibilant-crystalline whisper (31) through sibilant-buzzy buzz (32) to rich melodic warbling (33). The engine exits the sibilant zone and re-enters melodic territory -- but at a higher level of complexity than any previous warbler or singer. The Cassin's Finch doesn't just warble. It mimics. It quotes. It borrows phrases from other species and weaves them into its own song. The 34th acoustic element is **"the cadenza"** -- the virtuosic display passage that incorporates borrowed material.

## Taxonomy: The Philadelphia Ornithologist's Mountain Finch

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Fringillidae (True Finches)
- **Genus:** *Haemorhous*
- **Species:** *Haemorhous cassinii* (Baird, 1854)
- **Common Name:** Cassin's Finch
- **Former classification:** *Carpodacus cassinii* (until 2012 AOU reclassification)

### Etymology and the Man Behind the Name

The species honors **John Cassin** (1813-1869), one of the most important American ornithologists of the 19th century and the first American to study birds from outside North America extensively [1, 2, 3]. Cassin served as curator at the **Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia** beginning in 1842, working almost exclusively from the Academy's collection rather than in the field [2, 3]. This was unusual for his era -- most ornithologists were field naturalists first and systematists second. Cassin was a systematist first. He described **194 new species of birds** across the globe, working from skins and specimens that field collectors sent to Philadelphia [2, 3].

Spencer Fullerton Baird described the finch in 1854 from specimens collected during the Pacific Railroad Surveys, the great expeditions that mapped the American West for transcontinental railroad routes [1, 4]. Cassin himself examined the collection and reportedly called this rosy mountain finch "the greatest bird in the lot," prompting Baird to name it in his honor [3]. The original description appeared in the *Proceedings of the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia* [1, 4].

Five North American birds carry Cassin's name: Cassin's Auklet, Cassin's Kingbird, Cassin's Vireo, Cassin's Sparrow, and Cassin's Finch [2, 3]. Cassin died in 1869 of **arsenic poisoning** -- a consequence of years of handling bird skins preserved with arsenical soap, the standard preservation method of the era [2, 3]. The man who named more birds than any American before him was killed by the chemicals used to keep them.

### The Haemorhous Reclassification

Until 2012, Cassin's Finch was classified as *Carpodacus cassinii* -- placed in the same genus as the Old World rosefinches. Molecular phylogenetic analysis by Zuccon et al. (2012) demonstrated that the three North American "rosefinches" (Cassin's Finch, Purple Finch, and House Finch) form a **separate clade not closely related to the Palearctic rosefinches** [5, 6]. The American Ornithologists' Union accepted the reclassification in 2012, moving all three species to the resurrected genus *Haemorhous* [5, 6].

*Haemorhous* derives from Greek *haima* ("blood") and *orrhos* ("rump"), literally "blood-rumped" -- a reference to the reddish coloring of the male's plumage [1, 7]. The genus was originally described by Swainson in 1837 but fell into disuse when the three American species were merged into *Carpodacus*. The molecular evidence resurrected it.

The full binomial *Haemorhous cassinii* translates to approximately **"Cassin's blood-rumped [finch]."** The genus names the color. The species names the person. Compare with degree 12's Pine Siskin (*Spinus pinus*), where both genus and species name the same thing (a spiny plant associated with the bird). The Fringillidae members in the engine now span two naming philosophies: ecological (*Spinus pinus* = "spine of the pine") and honorific (*Haemorhous cassinii* = "Cassin's blood-rump").

### The Haemorhous Trio

Cassin's Finch belongs to a notoriously confusing trio of closely related finches -- the **Haemorhous trio** -- that challenge birders across western North America [8, 9, 10]:

| Species | Scientific Name | Male Plumage | Bill Shape | Habitat | Range |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cassin's Finch** | *H. cassinii* | Bright red peaked crown, rosy-pink wash | Slightly longer, straighter, flat on top | Mountain conifer (3,000-10,000 ft) | Western mountains |
| **Purple Finch** | *H. purpureus* | Vivid rosy-red wash over entire head/breast | Stout, curved culmen | Moist conifer/mixed forest | Northern/eastern + Pacific coast |
| **House Finch** | *H. mexicanus* | Orange-red concentrated on forehead/throat/breast, brown streaked flanks | Short, curved | Urban/suburban, desert scrub | Widespread, human-associated |

The three species separate on five axes: **crown shape** (Cassin's peaked, House round, Purple intermediate), **streak pattern** (Cassin's crisp fine streaks, House blurry broad streaks, Purple diffuse wash), **bill structure** (Cassin's longer and straighter, others shorter and more curved), **habitat** (Cassin's montane, Purple moist forest, House urban), and **song complexity** (Cassin's most complex with mimicry, Purple rich but unmimicking, House variable but simpler) [8, 9, 10].

The trio is a lesson in microhabitat partitioning within a genus. Where all three overlap -- which happens in transitional zones of the Pacific Northwest -- the identification challenge is genuine. Female and immature birds are particularly difficult, requiring attention to streaking pattern, bill shape, and undertail covert markings [8, 9, 10].

### Fringillidae in the Engine: Pine Siskin and Cassin's Finch

The engine now holds **two Fringillidae** from **two different genera**:

| Degree | Species | Genus | Energy | Season | Sound | Ecosystem |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | Pine Siskin | *Spinus* | 1 | Irruptive | Rising zhreee (ascending buzzy sweep) | Conifer seed crop |
| **33** | **Cassin's Finch** | ***Haemorhous*** | **4** | **Summer** | **Rich warbling song male display** | **Mountain conifer** |

The contrast is extreme. Pine Siskin: E=1, the quietest energy tier, a brief electronic zap, irruptive and unpredictable, small and streaky and camouflaged. Cassin's Finch: E=4, a sustained complex warble with mimicry, summer breeding on schedule, rosy-crowned and conspicuous. Same family. Opposite strategies. The Pine Siskin is the nomad who broadcasts a minimal signal. The Cassin's Finch is the territorial singer who broadcasts a maximal one.

Both are conifer-dependent. The Pine Siskin extracts seeds from cones with its specialized tweezer bill. The Cassin's Finch extracts seeds with its stouter finch bill and supplements with buds, berries, and insects. Both respond to conifer seed crops -- the Pine Siskin by irrupting when crops fail, the Cassin's Finch by adjusting breeding territory and winter movements based on food availability [1, 11, 12]. Same family, same substrate (conifer forest), different strategies for dealing with the uncertainty of cone crops.

## Vocalization: The Rich Warbling Song -- A Virtuoso's Cadenza

### The Song

The Cassin's Finch male produces one of the most complex and varied songs of any western North American finch -- a **long, rollicking, warbling series** of rapid, varied syllables that can sustain for 10-20 seconds or longer [1, 11, 13, 14]. The song is delivered from exposed high perches -- the top of a tall conifer, a dead snag, a prominent branch near the canopy -- during the breeding season from May through July [1, 11].

The song's character is often described as "richer," "more complex," and "more varied" than either the Purple Finch's or House Finch's song [11, 13, 14]. Where the House Finch produces a simpler warble and the Purple Finch delivers a rich but relatively structured melody, the Cassin's Finch pours out a **tumbling, unpredictable stream of syllables** that changes from phrase to phrase. The song is not a fixed sequence. It is an improvisation.

Acoustic characteristics:

- **Frequency range:** Approximately 2-8 kHz, with most energy concentrated in the 2.5-6 kHz band [13, 14, 15]. Substantially lower and broader than the waxwings' sibilant zone (5-10 kHz) and the Pine Siskin's zhreee (2-8 kHz sweeping). The Cassin's Finch occupies the rich, warm midrange of the passerine vocal space.
- **Song duration:** 5-20 seconds per bout, sometimes longer. Extended song bouts of 30+ seconds have been recorded during peak display [11, 13, 14]. This is among the longest sustained songs in the engine -- compare with the Pacific Wren's 8-10 second cascade (degree 1) or the Pine Siskin's buzzy warble (5-15 seconds at degree 12).
- **Phrase structure:** Complex and variable. Each song bout consists of rapidly delivered syllables -- warbles, trills, chips, buzzes, and mimicked phrases -- arranged in a non-repeating or loosely repeating sequence [11, 13, 14]. The song has no rigid syntax. New material appears mid-song.
- **Syllable rate:** Rapid -- approximately 8-15 syllables per second in the densest passages, slowing at phrase boundaries [13, 14].
- **Timbre:** Warm, rich, melodic. The fundamental quality is warbling -- a rapid alternation of pitch that creates a rolling, tumbling effect. Unlike the waxwings' sibilant hiss or the siskin's buzzy electronic zap, the Cassin's Finch produces **clearly tonal, pitched notes** with harmonic structure [13, 14]. The sound is musical in the conventional sense: you can hear individual notes, intervals, and phrases.
- **Amplitude:** Moderate to strong. The song projects well from high perches across mountain clearings. E=4 reflects the sustained, carrying quality of the display song.
- **Display context:** The song is primarily a **male territorial and mate-attraction display**. Males sing from prominent perches during breeding season. A **flight song** variant exists -- the male launches from a perch, circles on fluttering wings while singing continuously, and returns to a perch or nearby tree [1, 11]. The flight song is the most energetic vocal display the species produces.

### The Mimicry: Borrowing Everyone Else's Voice

The most remarkable feature of the Cassin's Finch's song is its **mimicry**. Males regularly incorporate imitations of other species' vocalizations into their songs [1, 11, 13, 14, 16]. This is not occasional or marginal -- it is a defining characteristic of the species' vocal behavior.

From the *Birds of the World* account: Cassin's Finches "often produce an astonishing string of imitations of the sounds of other birds as a cadenza to their typical warbly song; many a field worker has searched in vain for another species, fooled by these excellent mimics" [11]. The mimicry is embedded within the warble -- a Cassin's Finch song may contain recognizable fragments of Red Crossbill calls, Western Tanager phrases, Cassin's Vireo motifs, and American Robin caroling, all woven into the rolling warble without pause [11, 13, 16].

Species documented as mimicked by Cassin's Finch include [11, 13, 16]:

- **Red Crossbill** (*Loxia curvirostra*) -- the jip-jip-jip flight call
- **Western Tanager** (*Piranga ludoviciana*) -- the hoarse, robin-like warble
- **American Robin** (*Turdus migratorius*) -- caroling phrases
- **Cassin's Vireo** (*Vireo cassinii*) -- the deliberate paired phrases
- **Mountain Chickadee** (*Poecile gambeli*) -- the fee-bee call
- **Pygmy Nuthatch** (*Sitta pygmaea*) -- the rapid yapping call
- **Various warblers** -- phrases from montane-breeding Parulidae

The mimicry is **learned, not innate**. Young males acquire mimicked phrases from their acoustic environment during development, incorporating the sounds of species that share their montane conifer habitat [11, 16]. This means each male's song is a **record of his acoustic neighborhood** -- the species he heard during his formative period. A Cassin's Finch in the eastern Cascades mimics a different set of species than one in the southern Rockies, because the species assemblages differ.

The biological function of the mimicry is debated. Hypotheses include:

1. **Repertoire size signaling.** Females may prefer males with larger, more varied repertoires, and mimicry is a way to expand repertoire beyond species-typical syllables. Repertoire size often correlates with territory quality, age, and individual quality in passerines [17].
2. **Territory defense through acoustic deception.** By mimicking the vocalizations of other species, a male may create the impression of a more crowded acoustic environment, potentially deterring intruders who perceive the territory as already saturated with competitors [11, 16].
3. **Honest signal of cognitive ability.** Accurate mimicry requires learning, memory, and vocal production skill. It may function as a signal of neural quality to potential mates [17].
4. **No adaptive function.** The mimicry may be a byproduct of a generalized learning system that absorbs environmental sounds without specific functional significance [16].

Regardless of function, the mimicry makes the Cassin's Finch song a **composite sound** -- a mosaic of the mountain conifer soundscape reassembled into a single performance. The bird is not just singing its own song. It is singing the mountain.

### The 34th Acoustic Element: The Cadenza

**The progression (34 elements):** silence -- cascade -- drone -- language -- motif -- metronome -- question -- harmonic spiral -- ultrasonic threshold -- variation -- minimum signal -- trill/tremolo -- sawtooth sweep -- ostinato -- repetition-resolution -- accelerando -- crescendo -- the hook -- ascending spiral -- descending spiral -- sustain/the release -- the hammer -- resonant percussion/groove reset -- the testimony/groove reset -- the invocation -- the congregation -- the caroling -- the liquid gurgle -- the liquid twitter -- the liquid twittering gurgle -- the rough brrit -- the liquid warble synthesis -- the whisper -- the buzz -- **the cadenza**.

The name for this 34th element is **"the cadenza."** In classical music, a cadenza is a **virtuosic passage for the soloist**, typically near the end of a concerto movement, where the soloist plays freely -- often improvising or performing pre-composed material of exceptional technical difficulty -- while the orchestra waits. The cadenza is the moment where the individual voice takes center stage and demonstrates its full capability.

The Cassin's Finch's song is a cadenza in three senses:

1. **Virtuosic complexity.** The song is among the most complex in the engine -- rapid, varied, sustained, unpredictable. It exceeds the Black-headed Grosbeak's smooth warble (degree 23) and the Barn Swallow's liquid synthesis (degree 30) in sheer variety of syllable types and phrase structures.

2. **Incorporation of borrowed material.** A classical cadenza often quotes themes from the preceding movement. The Cassin's Finch quotes other species. Both cadenza forms demonstrate that the performer has absorbed the surrounding material and can reinterpret it within a personal voice.

3. **Solo display from a prominent position.** The Cassin's Finch sings from the top of a tall conifer, exposed and conspicuous, pouring out its virtuosic performance for an audience of potential mates and territorial rivals. The high perch is the soloist's front-of-stage position.

The progression from whisper (31) through buzz (32) to cadenza (33) traces an arc from the simplest possible signal (a thin sibilant trill) through a collective amplification (the buzzy flock chorus) to the most individually complex signal yet (a mimicry-laden virtuosic warble). The sibilant territory yielded collective texture. The melodic territory yields individual virtuosity. The engine has moved from **"everyone says one thing together"** (waxwing flock chorus) to **"one bird says everything alone"** (Cassin's Finch display song).

### Call Notes

In addition to the display song, Cassin's Finch produces several call types [1, 11, 13]:

- **Tidilip / giddy-up call:** A distinctive liquid, two-to-three-syllable call -- often transcribed as *tee-dee-yip* or *giddy-up*. This is the species' most commonly heard vocalization outside of breeding song, given in flight and while perched. The call is diagnostic -- experienced birders can identify Cassin's Finch by this call alone [11, 13].
- **Kip / chip note:** A sharp, short contact call used in flock situations. Similar to other finch chip notes but slightly softer than House Finch's [11, 13].
- **Female song:** Females occasionally produce a quiet, abbreviated version of the male's warble. Female song is uncommon in Fringillidae and notable when it occurs [11].

## Mountain Conifer: The Engine's First Montane Ecosystem

### A New Ecological Dimension: Altitude

Previous ecosystems in the engine have been lowland or non-altitude-specific: estuary, old-growth, mixed woodland, riparian, conifer canopy, forest edge, marsh edge, open woodland, open sky, open water, open field, farmland, fruiting trees, berry stands. None of these specified elevation as a defining characteristic. **Mountain conifer** does.

The Cassin's Finch breeds in **dry, open coniferous forests** of the western mountains at elevations typically between **3,000 and 10,000 feet** (900-3,050 m) [1, 11, 12, 18]. The habitat is defined by specific conifer communities:

- **Ponderosa pine** (*Pinus ponderosa*): The classic Cassin's Finch habitat in the eastern Cascades and northern Rockies. Open, park-like stands with widely spaced trees. The most important single tree species for the bird in Washington state [12, 18].
- **Douglas-fir** (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*): Used throughout the range, especially at mid-elevations. The bird takes seeds from Douglas-fir cones and nests in its branches [1, 11].
- **Lodgepole pine** (*Pinus contorta*): Important in higher-elevation forests of the Rockies and eastern Cascades [1, 11, 12].
- **Subalpine fir** (*Abies lasiocarpa*) and **Engelmann spruce** (*Picea engelmannii*): Used at upper elevations near treeline [1, 11].
- **Ponderosa/Douglas-fir transition zones:** The ecotone between lower ponderosa woodlands and upper mixed conifer forest is prime Cassin's Finch habitat [11, 12].

The "mountain" qualifier matters. The Cassin's Finch is not found in lowland conifer forests west of the Cascades -- not in the Douglas-fir/hemlock forests of the Puget Sound lowlands, not in the Sitka spruce/western hemlock forests of the Olympic Peninsula. It requires the **dry, continental, high-elevation conifer** type that occurs on rain-shadow slopes east of the Cascade crest [12, 18]. This is a fundamentally different forest from the wet, dense, moss-draped old-growth of the western slopes. Mountain conifer is open, sunny, characterized by widely spaced trees, needle litter on the ground, and cold winters at altitude.

### Altitudinal Movement

Cassin's Finches exhibit **altitudinal migration** -- breeding at high elevations in summer and descending to lower valleys and foothills in winter [1, 11, 12]. In Washington, breeding occurs in the eastern Cascades and Okanogan Highlands from May through July, with birds descending to lower-elevation ponderosa pine forests and even the Columbia Basin margins in winter [12, 18]. Some winters bring them to lowland areas east of the Cascades, particularly when mountain seed crops are poor.

This altitudinal movement is not the same as the Pine Siskin's irruption. The Cassin's Finch's seasonal movement is relatively predictable -- up in summer, down in winter, with the degree of descent varying with food conditions. It is a **partial altitudinal migrant**, not an irruptive species. The "summer" season designation in the engine reflects the breeding ecology: the bird is present in its mountain conifer habitat during the breeding season.

### Mountain Conifer as Soundscape

The mountain conifer ecosystem has a distinctive acoustic character that shapes how the Cassin's Finch's song functions. Key properties:

- **Low ambient noise.** High-elevation conifer forests are among the quietest terrestrial habitats. Less wind buffeting than exposed ridges, less stream noise than riparian zones, less anthropogenic noise than lowlands. The Cassin's Finch's complex song carries well in this quiet environment [14].
- **Open structure = long sight/sound lines.** The widely spaced trees of ponderosa pine forests create clear acoustic channels. A song from a treetop can propagate hundreds of meters with relatively little attenuation from vegetation [14].
- **Low species density.** Mountain conifer communities have fewer breeding bird species per hectare than lowland riparian or mixed forests. The acoustic space is less crowded. The Cassin's Finch's complex song can occupy more bandwidth without interference.
- **Echo and reverberation.** Forest structure creates characteristic reverberation patterns. The Cassin's Finch's rapid warble produces a shimmering effect as individual syllables bounce off tree trunks.

## The Cassin's Finch in the PNW

### Distribution in Washington State

In Washington, Cassin's Finch breeds in the **eastern Cascades, Okanogan Highlands, and Blue Mountains** [12, 18]. Core habitat is the ponderosa pine and mixed conifer zone east of the Cascade crest, at elevations from approximately 2,500 to 7,000 feet (760-2,130 m). The species is an uncommon to fairly common breeder in appropriate habitat.

Key distribution details [12, 18]:

- **Eastern Cascades:** Breeding throughout Chelan, Kittitas, and Yakima counties in montane conifer forests. Common in the Blewett Pass area, Teanaway, Swauk Creek drainage, and upper Yakima drainage.
- **Okanogan Highlands:** Present in Okanogan and Ferry counties. Mixed conifer and ponderosa pine forests.
- **Blue Mountains:** Southern breeding range in Washington. Asotin, Columbia, and Garfield counties.
- **NOT west of the Cascades:** Absent from the Puget Sound lowlands, western Washington forests, and Olympic Peninsula as a breeding species. Extremely rare vagrant west of the crest.
- **Winter distribution:** Descends to lower-elevation ponderosa pine forests east of the Cascades. Small numbers winter in the Kittitas Valley and along the eastern Cascade foothills. Occasionally joins mixed finch flocks at feeders in east-side communities.

### Relationship to Other PNW Finches

The Cassin's Finch's mountain conifer habitat overlaps with several other PNW finch species, creating a montane finch community [11, 12]:

- **Red Crossbill** (*Loxia curvirostra*): Shares conifer habitat. Crossbill's specialized bill extracts seeds from closed cones. Cassin's Finch takes seeds from open cones and from the ground. The Cassin's Finch mimics the crossbill's flight call.
- **Pine Siskin** (*Spinus pinus*, degree 12): Shares conifer habitat extensively. Both depend on cone crops. The siskin is irruptive; the Cassin's Finch is a partial altitudinal migrant. Both are Fringillidae.
- **Evening Grosbeak** (*Coccothraustes vespertinus*): Shares high-elevation conifer forests. Much larger bill for larger seeds. Nomadic.
- **Pine Grosbeak** (*Pinicola enucleator*): Higher-elevation overlap. Boreal and subalpine specialist.
- **Purple Finch** (*H. purpureus*): Range overlap in transitional zones. Purple Finch prefers moister forest west and north. In Washington, Purple Finch breeds more commonly in western lowlands and Cassin's in eastern mountains, with limited overlap [12, 18].
- **House Finch** (*H. mexicanus*): Range overlap at lower elevations east of the Cascades. House Finch is human-associated; Cassin's Finch is forest-associated. The two can co-occur at feeders in east-side communities during winter.

## Physical Description

Cassin's Finch is a **medium-sized finch** (15-17 cm, 24-34 g, average ~27 g) -- similar in size to Purple Finch, slightly larger than House Finch [1, 8, 9, 10, 11]. Key features:

### Adult Male
- **Crown:** The most diagnostic feature. A **bright red peaked crown** that gives the head a distinctive angular profile. The red is concentrated and intense on the crown, creating a crimson cap effect [8, 9, 10].
- **Face and throat:** Rosy-pink wash, lighter than the crown. The pink extends across the breast, fading into white on the lower belly [8, 9, 10].
- **Back:** Brown with rosy-pink tinge. Less extensively colored than the Purple Finch male, which appears "dipped in raspberry juice" [8, 9].
- **Flanks:** Finely streaked. The streaks are **crisp and thin**, unlike the House Finch's blurry, broad flank streaks [8, 9, 10].
- **Bill:** Slightly longer and straighter than other *Haemorhous*, with the **upper mandible flat on top** (culmen straight, not curved). This is a reliable structural mark [8, 9, 10].
- **Wings:** Long. When perched, the wingtips project **farther down the tail** than in House Finch or Purple Finch. This long-winged appearance reflects the species' mountain habitat and strong flight [8, 9, 10].

### Adult Female / Immature
- **Overall:** Brown and white, heavily streaked. No red or pink [8, 9, 10].
- **Streaks:** **Crisp, well-defined dark streaks** on breast and flanks -- finer and sharper than House Finch's diffuse streaking [8, 9, 10].
- **Face pattern:** Subtle pale eyebrow stripe. Less contrasting than Purple Finch's bold white eyebrow and moustache.
- **Undertail coverts:** White with narrow dark streaks -- a useful mark for separating from House Finch (which has blurry or absent undertail covert streaking) [8, 9, 10].
- **Bill:** Same straight-topped profile as male. This structural mark works regardless of plumage.

### Flight
Strong, bounding, undulating -- the typical finch flight pattern. The long wings give the bird a more powerful, less frantic flight than the smaller Pine Siskin or House Finch.

## Diet and Foraging

Cassin's Finch is primarily a **seed eater** with a strong emphasis on conifer seeds, supplemented by buds, berries, and insects [1, 11, 12]:

- **Conifer seeds:** The primary food. Seeds of ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, lodgepole pine, and other conifers. Unlike the Red Crossbill, which pries open closed cones, the Cassin's Finch takes seeds from open cones and from the forest floor [1, 11].
- **Buds:** Quaking aspen buds are an important spring food. The species is sometimes considered a minor pest in aspen stands because it consumes large quantities of buds [1, 11].
- **Berries and fruits:** Taken opportunistically, especially during fall and winter. Serviceberry (*Amelanchier*), elderberry (*Sambucus*), and other native fruits [1, 11].
- **Insects:** Taken during breeding season, particularly for feeding nestlings. Caterpillars, aphids, beetles, and other arthropods gleaned from conifer branches [1, 11].
- **Mineral sources:** Cassin's Finches are often observed at **mineral licks, road salt, and calcium-rich soils** -- a behavior shared with crossbills and other mountain finch species. The mineral intake may supplement a seed-heavy diet low in certain minerals [1, 11].

## Breeding Ecology

### Nest and Eggs

The nest is an **open cup** constructed solely by the female, typically placed on a horizontal branch or in an upright fork of a conifer, **10-60 feet (3-18 m) above the ground** [1, 11, 12]. Nest placement tends to be well out on a limb, away from the trunk, in an open area that allows the incubating female a clear view of approaching predators. Nest materials include twigs, bark strips, lichens, rootlets, and a lining of fine grass, plant down, and animal hair [1, 11].

- **Clutch size:** 3-6 eggs, typically 4-5 [1, 11].
- **Egg color:** Pale bluish-green with sparse dark spots, concentrated at the larger end [1, 11].
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days, by the female alone [1, 11].
- **Nestling period:** 12-14 days [1, 11].
- **Breeding season:** May through July in Washington and the northern Rockies. Slightly earlier (late April) in southern portions of the range [1, 11, 12].
- **Broods:** Typically one per season [1, 11].

### Courtship Display

The male's courtship display is directly tied to the display song. During courtship, the male performs a **flight song display** -- rising from a perch on fluttering wings, circling above the territory while singing continuously, then returning to a perch [1, 11]. This aerial singing display combines the song's acoustic complexity with a visual flight display, creating a multimodal signal. The flight song is the "rich warbling song male display" that defines this degree's sound type.

## Conservation and Populations

### Status

Cassin's Finch is classified as **Least Concern** by the IUCN, downlisted from Near Threatened in 2019 as population declines appeared to slow [19]. However, the species remains of conservation concern:

- **Global breeding population:** Estimated at approximately **3.2 million** by Partners in Flight [20].
- **Continental Concern Score:** **13 out of 20** on the Partners in Flight scale -- indicating high conservation concern. Listed on the **PIF Yellow Watch List-D** for species with declining populations and moderate to high threats [20].
- **Population trend:** The North American Breeding Bird Survey indicates a long-term decline of approximately **1.5% per year** from 1980 to 1994, slowing to approximately **0.56% per year** from 2006 to 2015, with a moderate overall decrease since 1967 [19, 20, 21].
- **Threats:** Fire suppression has altered conifer forest structure, producing denser stands less suitable for a species that prefers open montane forests. Climate change-driven drought increases tree mortality and reduces seed production. Logging of old-growth ponderosa pine reduces nest sites [1, 11, 19, 20].

### Comparison with Pine Siskin Conservation

Both Fringillidae members in the engine face population concerns, but from different angles:

| Dimension | Pine Siskin (Degree 12) | Cassin's Finch (Degree 33) |
|---|---|---|
| Population estimate | ~40 million | ~3.2 million |
| Trend | Variable (irruptive species hard to track) | Declining (~0.56%/year recent) |
| Key threat | Irruptive movement masks trends | Fire suppression alters habitat |
| Monitoring challenge | Nomadic, patchy distribution | Montane habitat, sparse BBS coverage |
| PIF concern score | 10/20 | 13/20 (higher concern) |

The Cassin's Finch has the smaller population, the higher concern score, and the more clearly documented decline. Its mountain conifer habitat is under pressure from multiple directions -- fire suppression, climate change, bark beetle outbreaks, logging -- and the species' relatively restricted range (western mountains only) provides less demographic buffer than the Pine Siskin's continent-wide distribution.

## The Energy Return: E=5 to E=4

### A Spike, Not a Plateau

The energy history at the transition from degree 32 to degree 33:

| Degree | Species | Energy | Event |
|---|---|---|---|
| 21-31 | (eleven species) | 4 | The E=4 undecet -- longest plateau |
| 32 | Bohemian Waxwing | **5** | PLATEAU BREAKS -- first E=5 ever |
| **33** | **Cassin's Finch** | **4** | **Return to E=4** |

E=5 lasted exactly one degree. The Bohemian Waxwing's massive irruptive flock chorus touched the ceiling and withdrew. The Cassin's Finch returns to E=4 -- the energy level of the sustained mountain singer, the complex solo warble, the carrying display song. But the return is not a regression. The engine's energy vocabulary has expanded permanently. E=5 exists. It can be used again. The Cassin's Finch at E=4 is not "stuck at E=4" the way degrees 21-31 were stuck. It is at E=4 by choice, in a context where E=5 is available.

The energy arc -- E=4 (whisper) to E=5 (buzz) to E=4 (cadenza) -- maps to musical dynamics: mezzo-forte, then fortissimo, then mezzo-forte again. The fortissimo accent highlights the preceding and following mezzo-forte by contrast. The Cassin's Finch's E=4 sounds different after E=5 than it would have sounded before E=5.
