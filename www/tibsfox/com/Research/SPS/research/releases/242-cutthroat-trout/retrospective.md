## The Two Hundred Forty-Second Degree: The Closing Note

### FINAL SALMONIDAE. William Clark's trout -- named 1805, formalized 1856. Washington State Fish since 1969. The surface rise at dusk on a mountain lake. E=2. Seven degrees of salmon, trout, and char: the arc closes here. At degree 243, jawless fish enter the engine.

## What Worked

1. **The Lewis and Clark connection is the entry's most historically significant element.** *Oncorhynchus clarkii* is one of the few PNW species precisely dated to a specific historical event (the 1805 expedition). The species name permanently honors William Clark. Every scientific reference to this trout is also a reference to the Corps of Discovery.
2. **The surface rise as acoustic profile is philosophically appropriate.** The Cutthroat's E=2 surface rise is the most culturally loaded "sound" in PNW freshwater ecology -- it is the sound that flyfishers have been listening for since the 19th century. Documenting it as the engine's closing Salmonidae entry is satisfying.
3. **The Salmonidae arc table is the entry's most useful structural contribution.** Seeing all seven entries (236-242) side by side demonstrates the family's range: from E=5 spawning splashes to E=2 surface rises, from semelparous Pacific Salmon to resident mountain lake trout.
4. **The Rainbow Trout hybridization threat is underrepresented in conservation literature for general audiences.** "Cuttbows" replacing pure Cutthroat populations over generations is a quiet extinction happening in many streams with little fanfare. The entry documents this clearly.

## Discoveries

1. **The Yellowstone Lake Lake Trout crisis is one of the most documented conservation cascades in North America.** An illegal introduction of Lake Trout in the 1980s triggering a Cutthroat collapse that then reduced Grizzly Bear, Osprey, and Bald Eagle populations dependent on the spawning runs is a textbook case of trophic cascade. The cross-references (Grizzly at 217-218, Osprey at 224, Eagle at 221-222) all appear in the Yellowstone Cutthroat story.
2. **The Westslope Cutthroat (named *lewisi* for Meriwether Lewis) gives both Lewis and Clark species named in their honor within the same complex.** *O. c. clarkii* (coastal) is named for Clark; *O. c. lewisi* (westslope) is named for Lewis. The Lewis and Clark Expedition, as a unit, named both principal Washington State subspecies.
3. **The transition from 242 (Cutthroat) to 243 (Pacific Lamprey) is the most dramatic biological boundary in the SPS engine.** From a species that is visually beautiful, culturally beloved, named after an American hero, and the state fish -- to a species that is jawless, eel-like, 450 million years old, and largely unknown outside indigenous and scientific circles. This contrast is the engine's most instructive taxonomic jump.

## Pair Analysis: Cutthroat Trout + The Wailers

- **Energy:** E=2 + E=7 = E=9. Near equilibrium. A slight lean toward S36.
- **Theme:** THE ORIGINAL NATIVES. The Wailers (1959) are the original PNW rock band -- the founding generation before the Sonics. Cutthroat Trout (*O. clarkii*, 1805) are the original PNW native trout -- the first described, the state fish, the icon. Both represent what was there before the more famous successors arrived. Both deserve more recognition than they typically receive.
