## THE SPECIES -- *ONCORHYNCHUS CLARKII*

### The Cutthroat's Defining Features

- **The cutthroat mark.** Cutthroat Trout are named for the distinctive red-orange slash marks along the jaw, visible on each side of the lower jaw below the mouth. These marks are variable in intensity -- vivid red in some populations, pale orange in others -- but present in virtually all individuals, making identification straightforward. The "cut throat" imagery of the name refers to the appearance of the slashes, as if the fish has been cut.
- **Scientific nomenclature.** The species is *Oncorhynchus clarkii* -- the genus shared with Pacific Salmon, reflecting the evolutionary closeness of the native Pacific trout to the Pacific salmon clade. The species name *clarkii* honors William Clark of the Lewis and Clark Expedition, who was the first to formally document the species in scientific terms in 1805.
- **Multiple subspecies.** The Cutthroat Trout species complex includes approximately 14 recognized subspecies, making it the most taxonomically complex native salmonid in the Pacific Northwest. Washington State hosts several:
  - *O. c. clarkii* -- the coastal cutthroat, distributed in coastal streams and rivers from northern California to southeast Alaska
  - *O. c. lewisi* -- the westslope cutthroat, found in eastern Washington and the upper Columbia drainage
  - *O. c. bouvieri* -- the Yellowstone cutthroat, in eastern drainages
  - *O. c. henshawi* -- Lahontan cutthroat, Great Basin
  - The engine documents the coastal cutthroat (*O. c. clarkii*) as the primary PNW representative
- **Size.** Coastal Cutthroat Trout in Washington streams and lakes typically reach 25-50 cm (10-20 in) and 0.5-2 kg (1-4.5 lbs). Sea-run (anadromous) Cutthroat -- also called "sea trout" -- can reach 60-70 cm (24-28 in) and 2-4 kg (4.5-9 lbs). Interior lake populations (like Yellowstone Lake's famous Cutthroat) can produce large fish.
- **Coloration.** Beyond the diagnostic red-orange jaw slashes, Cutthroat are olive-green to brownish on the back, with yellow, orange, or gold sides covered in black spots. The spotting pattern varies by subspecies: coastal cutthroat tend to have heavier, irregular spotting; westslope cutthroat have smaller, more regular spots.

## LEWIS AND CLARK -- THE FIRST SCIENTIFIC DESCRIPTION

### *Oncorhynchus clarkii* and the Corps of Discovery

- **The 1805 encounter.** Members of the Lewis and Clark Expedition first encountered Cutthroat Trout in the upper Missouri River drainage in what is now Montana in 1805. William Clark noted the distinctive "red streaks" (the jaw slashes) that distinguish this trout from any species previously known to European science. The description in the expedition journals -- careful, detailed, scientific for its era -- represents the first formal documentation of *Oncorhynchus clarkii* in the scientific record.
- **Clark's observation.** Clark described the fish as "a trout of a remarkably fine flavour" and noted its distinctive coloration. The expedition's naturalists -- primarily Meriwether Lewis, who had some formal scientific training -- were tasked with documenting all flora and fauna encountered. The Cutthroat Trout entry in the expedition journals is one of the more detailed ichthyological observations of the journey.
- **The species epithet.** The naming of *Oncorhynchus clarkii* after William Clark (1770-1838) is a permanent scientific honor. Every time the species is referred to by its scientific name, it references Clark's role in the Corps of Discovery. The naming was formalized by the naturalist Charles Girard in 1856, fifty years after the expedition.
- **Historical significance.** The Cutthroat Trout is thus one of the few PNW species whose entry into scientific literature is precisely dated to a specific historical event (the Lewis and Clark Expedition of 1803-1806) and tied to a specific historical figure (William Clark). This adds a layer of American history to what is already a biologically and ecologically important entry.
- **Expedition context.** The Lewis and Clark Expedition was the first American overland journey to the Pacific Ocean, commissioned by President Thomas Jefferson. The expedition's mandate was geographic and scientific: map the territory, establish American presence, document the flora, fauna, and indigenous peoples encountered. The Cutthroat Trout is one of the several hundred new species documented by the expedition.

(Sources: Lewis and Clark Expedition journals 1805; Clark's cutthroat trout description; Charles Girard 1856 formal naming; William Clark biography; Corps of Discovery scientific mandate; President Jefferson's instructions)

## THE SURFACE RISE -- E=2

### The Most Iconic Sound in PNW Flyfishing

The sound documented at degree 242 is specific and culturally loaded:

- **The rise form.** When a trout feeds at the surface on a floating insect (a dry fly, a caddis adult, a mayfly dun), it rises from below and takes the insect with its mouth. As it does so, it breaks the surface film. The disruption creates a distinctive pattern: the "rise form." Different rise forms have different sounds and visual signatures:
  - **The sip rise.** The most subtle: the trout barely breaks the surface, taking a small midge or emerger with a quiet sipping sound. The ring of disturbed water is tiny. This is E=2 at its minimum.
  - **The dimple rise.** Slightly more active: the trout breaks the surface with its snout and creates a small circular ripple. The sound is a quiet "sip" or gentle "plop."
  - **The slash rise.** The most energetic surface feeding: the trout charges an insect on or above the surface and takes it with an audible splash. Still quiet relative to most animal sounds, but clearly audible from the bank.
- **The acoustic signature.** At E=2, the Cutthroat's rise is at the quiet end of the acoustic spectrum. In the stillness of a mountain lake at dusk, the sip of a rising trout can be heard clearly from 20-30 meters. On a fast-moving river, the sound is absorbed into the background of water over rocks.
- **Why flyfishing is listening.** The art of flyfishing is partly visual (watching for rise forms) and partly acoustic (listening for the sip that indicates a feeding fish). The Cutthroat's surface rise is the primary auditory cue in mountain lake flyfishing. The angler who hears the sip before seeing the rings has an advantage in placing the cast.
- **Mountain lake acoustics.** A mountain lake in the PNW in the evening -- the light dropping, the air still, the mountains reflected in the water -- is one of the quietest acoustic environments in the region. In this setting, the sip of a rising Cutthroat Trout is significant: a signal against a near-silent background. E=2 is appropriate. The fish announces itself just enough.

(Sources: Rise form taxonomy; sip rise acoustic profile; dimple vs. slash rise; flyfishing acoustic cues; mountain lake acoustic environment at dusk; E=2 placement rationale)

## ECOLOGY AND LIFECYCLE

### The Mountain Lake System

- **Diet.** Cutthroat Trout are opportunistic predators with a strong preference for surface feeding when conditions allow. Their diet includes aquatic invertebrates (the same mayfly-caddisfly-stonefly triumvirate important for all stream salmonids), terrestrial insects (the source of the "rise" documented here), small fish, and amphibians (frogs and salamanders in some lake systems). In mountain lakes, the summer hatches of mayflies and caddisflies drive surface feeding and are the ecological engine behind the flyfishing tradition.
- **Spawning.** Cutthroat Trout spawn in spring (March-June, depending on elevation and population). They move from lakes into tributary streams, where females construct redds in clean gravel. The spring spawning run brings trout from deeper lake habitat into shallow tributary streams -- a predictable seasonal movement that has made Cutthroat accessible to anglers and predators for thousands of years.
- **Lake vs. stream forms.** Like Dolly Varden (241) and many other salmonids, Cutthroat exist in both lake-dwelling and stream-resident forms. Lake Cutthroat use streams only for spawning; stream Cutthroat are year-round residents. The coastal cutthroat also has an anadromous form (sea-run Cutthroat) that moves between freshwater and the nearshore marine environment.
- **Ecological role.** Cutthroat Trout are apex predators within the mountain lake ecosystem. They structure the invertebrate community through predation, affecting the abundance and distribution of aquatic insects and small crustaceans. Their removal from a lake (through overfishing or other means) typically produces a cascade of ecological changes: aquatic invertebrate populations boom, which affects the aquatic plant community, which affects water clarity and chemistry. The Cutthroat is the top-down regulator of the mountain lake food web.

(Sources: Cutthroat diet including surface insects; mayfly-caddisfly hatch ecology; spring spawning timing; lake vs. stream forms; anadromous sea-run cutthroat; ecological role as apex predator; trophic cascade effects of removal)

## CULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE

### State Fish, Icon of PNW Angling, Conservation Symbol

- **Washington State fish.** The Cutthroat Trout is the Washington State Fish, designated in 1969. This designation reflects the species' cultural centrality: no other fish in Washington State is as strongly associated with the landscape, the flyfishing tradition, and the idea of pristine mountain wilderness. The Cutthroat is what people picture when they imagine a PNW mountain lake.
- **The flyfishing tradition.** The Pacific Northwest's flyfishing culture is built around Cutthroat Trout and Steelhead. Cutthroat on a dry fly -- a surface-feeding fish taking a floating artificial fly -- is the defining image of PNW freshwater angling. The tradition is old: Indigenous peoples fished for Cutthroat with spears and weirs for thousands of years before European settlers developed the flyfishing method. The introduction of flyfishing techniques to the PNW in the 19th century found ready application to Cutthroat in mountain streams and lakes.
- **Indigenous significance.** Cutthroat Trout were an important food source for virtually all Pacific Northwest indigenous groups with access to mountain streams and lakes. The spring spawning run, when Cutthroat moved into shallow tributary streams, made them accessible without the sophisticated fishing technology required for mainstem river salmon. Cutthroat were taken by hand, spear, basket trap, and weir throughout their range.
- **Conservation symbol.** The Cutthroat has become a conservation symbol for mountain stream and lake habitat throughout the West. Because Cutthroat require cold, clean water and are sensitive to habitat degradation, their presence or absence serves as an indicator of watershed health. "There are Cutthroat in this stream" is a statement about the stream's ecological integrity, not just its fish population.

(Sources: Washington State Fish designation 1969; flyfishing tradition and Cutthroat; Indigenous fishing methods for Cutthroat; Cutthroat as watershed health indicator; conservation symbol status)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### A Native Under Pressure

- **Westslope Cutthroat.** Washington's eastern (*O. c. lewisi*) populations have been severely reduced by hybridization with non-native Rainbow Trout (*O. mykiss*), habitat degradation, and historical overfishing. The westslope cutthroat is a Species of Concern in Washington.
- **Coastal Cutthroat.** The coastal form (*O. c. clarkii*) is generally more secure but faces threats from habitat degradation in coastal streams, interactions with hatchery fish, and water temperature increases from climate change.
- **Rainbow Trout hybridization.** The most persistent conservation threat to inland Cutthroat populations across the West is hybridization with non-native Rainbow Trout, which share the *Oncorhynchus* genus and can interbreed with Cutthroat where they co-occur. Hatchery Rainbow Trout introduced throughout the West for sport fishing have been hybridizing with native Cutthroat for over a century. The hybrids ("Cuttbows") can be difficult to distinguish from pure Cutthroat and can replace pure Cutthroat populations entirely over generations.
- **Yellowstone Cutthroat crisis.** In Yellowstone Lake -- the world's largest wild Cutthroat Trout lake -- the illegal introduction of Lake Trout (*Salvelinus namaycush*) in the 1980s triggered a catastrophic decline of the native Yellowstone Cutthroat (*O. c. bouvieri*). Lake Trout prey on young Cutthroat in deep water, where cutthroat predators cannot follow. The Yellowstone Cutthroat decline had cascading effects on Grizzly Bears, Osprey, and Bald Eagles that fed on the abundant Cutthroat spawning runs. Lake Trout netting has been ongoing since the 1990s.
- **Recovery tools.** Barrier construction (waterfalls augmented or artificial barriers created) to prevent non-native fish from colonizing upstream native Cutthroat habitat. Non-native fish removal using rotenone (a piscicide). Hatchery reform. Protective regulations in key streams.

(Sources: Westslope Cutthroat status; Rainbow Trout hybridization; hatchery impacts; Yellowstone Lake Lake Trout introduction; Yellowstone Cutthroat cascade effects on bears and raptors; Lake Trout netting; recovery tools)

The engine crosses a fundamental biological boundary between degrees 242 and 243.
