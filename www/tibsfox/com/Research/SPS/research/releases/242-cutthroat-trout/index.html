The Surface Rise

The sound documented at degree 242 is the most culturally significant acoustic event in PNW freshwater fishing: a Cutthroat Trout rising to take an insect at the surface of a mountain lake. The "sip" or gentle "plop" of the rise form. Expanding rings across still water at dusk. It is an E=2 sound — barely audible, specific, delicate. In the acoustic context of a mountain lake at evening — near silence, mountains reflected in the water — it is unmistakable.

The angler who hears the sip before seeing the rings has the advantage. Flyfishing in the PNW mountains is partly the art of listening for the Cutthroat's announcement of its presence.

Lewis & Clark -- The First Description

William Clark of the Corps of Discovery documented *Oncorhynchus clarkii* during the 1805 expedition in the upper Missouri River drainage — the first scientific description of the species in western science. Clark noted "red streaks" along the jaw (the distinctive cutthroat slashes) and described the fish as "remarkably fine flavour." The formal naming was completed by naturalist Charles Girard in 1856. Every scientific reference to this species forever invokes Clark's name.

Washington State Fish

Coastal Cutthroat

O. c. clarkii

Primary PNW form. Coastal streams, rivers, mountain lakes. Also sea-run (anadromous). Heavier, more irregular spotting.

Westslope Cutthroat

O. c. lewisi

Eastern Washington, upper Columbia drainage. Named for Meriwether Lewis. Under pressure from Rainbow Trout hybridization.

The Cutthroat Mark

Red-orange jaw slash

Diagnostic field mark: vivid red-orange slashes below the jaw, one on each side. Present in virtually all individuals; intensity varies by subspecies.

The Rise Form

Surface feeding behavior

Sip rise (midge/emerger), dimple rise (dry fly), slash rise (energetic feeding). Each has a different sound and visual ring signature.

Conservation

Rainbow Trout hybridization is the primary inland threat. Hatchery Rainbow Trout (*O. mykiss*) introduced throughout the West interbreed with native Cutthroat, producing "Cuttbow" hybrids that can replace pure populations entirely. Westslope Cutthroat in eastern Washington are most affected. Recovery tools: barrier construction, non-native removal, hatchery reform.
