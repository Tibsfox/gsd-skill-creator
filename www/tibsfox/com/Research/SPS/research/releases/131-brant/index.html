Low Musical Honk Flocks — E=4

The Brant’s call is a low, rolling, throaty honk — often written as “crronk” or “r-r-rronk.” More melodious than Canada Goose, softer than Cackling Goose. 200–1,200 Hz. The defining sound is the flock murmur: hundreds of Brant feeding on eelgrass produce a continuous, low-pitched conversational gabble that fills a coastal bay. Not dramatic V-formation honking — intimate, busy, communal sound. The bass note of the winter bay soundscape.

The Eelgrass Specialist — Almost Exclusive Dependency

Brant feed almost exclusively on eelgrass (Zostera marina) during the winter — the most specialized dietary dependency in PNW waterfowl. Head-dipping in shallow water (<1 meter). When eelgrass declines, Brant must switch to sea lettuce and marine algae, with reduced body condition. The Brant is the canary in the eelgrass mine — an indicator species for coastal bay health. Eelgrass also supports juvenile Chinook salmon, Pacific herring, and Dungeness crab. Protecting eelgrass for Brant protects the entire marine food web.

Padilla Bay — Primary PNW Wintering Site

Padilla Bay (Skagit County, WA) hosts the largest eelgrass beds on the Pacific Coast of Washington — approximately 8,000 acres. Thousands of Brant winter here, feeding at low tide on the vast eelgrass flats. The Padilla Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve (NERR) protects the site. Boundary Bay (BC/WA border) is the second major site. Additional PNW sites include Dungeness Bay, Sequim Bay, and Penn Cove (Whidbey Island). Brant breed on Arctic tundra and migrate the full Pacific Coast — some continuing to Baja California.

Fourteenth Consecutive Anatidae (118–131)

The Anatidae streak extends to fourteen. The goose era (129–134) has entered its third entry. The energy arc after the E=6 Trumpeter Swan climax (128): E=5 (Canada Goose), E=4 (Cackling), E=4 (Brant). A post-climax descent that mirrors the pre-climax rise. The Brant adds a new ecological dimension to the streak: the first marine-adapted specialist since the Harlequin Duck (118). The streak now spans from stream specialist to marine specialist — the full freshwater-to-saltwater gradient.
