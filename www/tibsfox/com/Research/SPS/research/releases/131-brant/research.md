## E=4 -- THE MUSICAL FLOCK HONKER

### Why E=4

The Brant registers at E=4 for acoustic and behavioral reasons:

- **Musical quality:** The Brant's honk is distinctly different from the Canada Goose's resonant honking. Brant calls are **lower-pitched, more throaty, more musical** -- a rolling, muttering quality that has been described as "cronking" or a low musical gargling. The individual voice is less powerful than the Canada Goose (E=5) but more melodious.
- **Flock-driven volume:** Like many waterfowl, the Brant's acoustic impact comes from flocks. Hundreds or thousands of Brant feeding on eelgrass beds produce a continuous, low-pitched murmuring that fills a bay. The sound is immersive but not individually commanding -- E=4 reflects the communal acoustic character.
- **Distinctiveness:** The Brant's voice is immediately distinguishable from Canada Goose (deeper, resonant, honking) and Cackling Goose (higher, sharper, cackling). The Brant sound is **softer, more rolling, more musical**. This distinctive quality justifies E=4 rather than E=3 (too quiet for flock impact) or E=5 (reserved for more individually powerful voices).
- **Context:** Brant vocalize constantly while feeding, flying, and resting. The low musical murmuring of a Brant flock on Padilla Bay on a winter morning is one of the defining sounds of the PNW coastal bay ecosystem.

## THE LOW MUSICAL HONK

### A Different Kind of Goose Sound

The Brant's vocalization stands apart from other geese in the engine:

- **Character:** A low, rolling, throaty honk. Not the resonant "honk" of the Canada Goose. Not the sharp "cack" of the Cackling Goose. The Brant produces a **muttering, musical, gargling sound** -- often written as "crronk" or "r-r-rronk." The rolled "r" quality is distinctive and immediately recognizable.
- **Frequency range:** 200-1,200 Hz. Lower than the Cackling Goose (400-2,000 Hz), overlapping with but distinct from the Canada Goose (150-1,500 Hz). The Brant's frequency range is narrower and centered lower, contributing to its muffled, musical quality.
- **Flock sound:** The defining acoustic experience of the Brant is the **flock murmur**. Hundreds of birds feeding on eelgrass produce a continuous, low-pitched conversational sound -- a rolling gabble that fills a bay. This is not the dramatic V-formation honking of Canada Geese; it is an intimate, busy, communal sound. The flock murmur is the Brant's signature.
- **Flight calls:** In flight, Brant produce a slightly higher, more nasal version of the ground call. Flocks of Brant flying low over the water produce a distinctive rolling chorus that experienced birders can identify at distance without seeing the birds.
- **Alarm calls:** Sharper, higher-pitched than the feeding murmur. Given when Bald Eagles or other predators approach. The alarm call triggers mass takeoffs from eelgrass beds.

## THE EELGRASS SPECIALIST

### Almost Exclusive Dependency

The Brant's relationship with eelgrass (*Zostera marina*) is one of the most specialized dietary dependencies in North American waterfowl:

- **Primary diet:** During the wintering season on the Pacific Coast, Brant feed **almost exclusively on eelgrass**. Unlike Canada Geese (generalist grazers that eat grass, grain, and aquatic vegetation) or Cackling Geese (field grazers), Brant have evolved a strong preference for eelgrass leaves. They pull up the long, ribbon-like leaves from shallow subtidal and intertidal beds.
- **Feeding method:** Brant are "head-dipping" feeders -- they submerge their heads and necks to reach eelgrass in shallow water (typically less than 1 meter deep). They do not dive like ducks. This feeding method restricts them to the shallowest portions of eelgrass beds, typically at low to mid-tide.
- **Supplementary foods:** When eelgrass is unavailable, Brant will eat sea lettuce (*Ulva*), other marine algae, and occasionally upland grasses. However, these are secondary foods. Populations with access to healthy eelgrass beds maintain better body condition than those forced to rely on alternatives.
- **The eelgrass dependency makes the Brant the most ecologically constrained goose in the PNW.** Canada Geese thrive in parks, fields, and urban areas. Cackling Geese use agricultural fields. Brant need healthy coastal bays with intact eelgrass beds. This specialization makes them an **indicator species** for coastal bay ecosystem health.

### The Eelgrass Crisis

The Brant's vulnerability is directly linked to the status of eelgrass in Puget Sound:

- **Eelgrass decline:** Eelgrass beds in parts of Puget Sound have declined due to **water quality degradation, coastal development, increased turbidity, boat traffic, and climate change**. Warmer water temperatures stress eelgrass. Nutrient runoff causes algal blooms that shade eelgrass beds. Dredging and shoreline armoring destroy eelgrass habitat directly.
- **The Brant-eelgrass feedback:** As eelgrass declines, Brant populations that depend on those beds must move to alternative sites or switch to less-preferred foods. The birds' body condition declines on alternative diets, affecting survival and breeding success. The Brant is the **canary in the eelgrass mine** -- population changes in Brant can signal eelgrass ecosystem health before the decline becomes visible to humans.
- **Conservation concern:** The Pacific Brant population (the subspecies that winters in the PNW, *Branta bernicla nigricans*, the Black Brant) has shown population fluctuations linked to eelgrass availability. While not federally listed, the species is considered a species of special concern in Washington State.
- **The connection to Puget Sound health:** Eelgrass is a foundational species in Puget Sound's shallow marine ecosystem. It provides nursery habitat for juvenile salmon, herring, and Dungeness crab. It stabilizes sediment. It sequesters carbon. The Brant's eelgrass dependency connects its fate to the broader health of Puget Sound -- the bird, the plant, and the marine ecosystem are a linked system.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Reed et al. 1998; Padilla Bay NERR; WDFW; Puget Sound Partnership)

## PNW WINTERING GROUNDS -- PADILLA BAY AND BOUNDARY BAY

### The Pacific Coast Winter Range

The Brant's PNW wintering distribution is concentrated in specific coastal bays:

- **Padilla Bay (Skagit County, WA):** The primary Brant wintering site in Washington State. Padilla Bay hosts one of the largest eelgrass beds on the Pacific Coast of Washington -- approximately **8,000 acres of eelgrass**, making it the **Padilla Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve** (NERR). Thousands of Brant winter here, feeding on the extensive eelgrass beds at low tide. Padilla Bay is the single most important site for Brant conservation in Washington.
- **Boundary Bay (BC/WA border):** The large bay straddling the US-Canada border is another major Brant wintering site. Extensive mudflats and eelgrass beds support thousands of Brant alongside other waterfowl. The Boundary Bay population connects the US and Canadian management areas.
- **Other PNW sites:** Smaller numbers of Brant winter at Dungeness Bay, Sequim Bay, Penn Cove (Whidbey Island), and various sites along the outer coast of Washington and Oregon. These secondary sites have smaller eelgrass beds and support fewer birds.
- **Migration:** Brant breed on the **Arctic tundra** of Alaska and northern Canada (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta, Banks Island, and other Arctic sites). They migrate along the Pacific Coast in fall, arriving in the PNW by late October through November. Spring migration back to the Arctic begins in April-May.
- **The Baja connection:** Many Pacific Brant continue south past the PNW to winter in **Baja California, Mexico** -- particularly the lagoons of the Baja Pacific coast (San Quintin Bay, Guerrero Negro). The Pacific Brant flyway is the longest of any Brant subspecies: Arctic Alaska to Baja Mexico, with PNW bays as important mid-latitude staging and wintering areas.

### Padilla Bay as Acoustic Landscape

The sound of Brant at Padilla Bay is a defining winter acoustic experience:

- **Low-tide feeding:** At low tide, hundreds of Brant wade and dip on the exposed eelgrass flats, producing a continuous low murmuring. The sound carries across the bay -- a distant, rolling gabble that is audible from the Padilla Bay Shore Trail.
- **Mass flights:** When disturbed (often by Bald Eagles), the entire Brant flock takes off simultaneously. The wingbeats and flight calls of hundreds of birds create a brief, intense acoustic event -- a roaring rush that subsides as the birds settle back to the water.
- **The winter soundscape:** On a quiet December morning at Padilla Bay, the dominant sounds are Brant murmuring, Dunlin flocks overhead, and distant Great Blue Heron croaking. The Brant's low musical honking is the bass note of this soundscape -- the continuous, underlying sound that defines the bay's winter character.

## SUBSPECIES AND IDENTIFICATION

### Black Brant (*Branta bernicla nigricans*)

The Brant that winter in the PNW are primarily the **Black Brant** subspecies:

- **Appearance:** Dark overall. Black head, neck, breast, and back. White necklace mark (incomplete, usually a patch rather than a ring). Dark belly with narrow white barring on the flanks. Short black bill. Small size (1.0-1.8 kg, 56-66 cm body length).
- **Compared to other geese:** The Brant is distinctly smaller than the Canada Goose and similar in size to the Cackling Goose. The all-dark appearance without the white cheek patch (diagnostic of Canada/Cackling Geese) is the quickest field mark. Brant in flight appear dark and compact, with rapid wingbeats.
- **Subspecies differences:** The Atlantic Brant (*B. b. hrota*, which winters on the US Atlantic Coast) has a lighter belly and more complete white necklace. The Black Brant of the Pacific is darker overall. Both feed primarily on eelgrass.
- **Field identification:** In the PNW, the Brant is straightforward to identify: small dark goose on saltwater bays, feeding on eelgrass, with no white cheek patch. The main confusion species is the Cackling Goose (130), which shares the small size but has the white cheek patch and uses different habitat (fields rather than marine bays).

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE SEA GOOSE

### A Marine-Adapted Goose

The Brant is the most marine-adapted of North American geese:

- **Saltwater specialist:** Unlike Canada Geese (freshwater and terrestrial) and Cackling Geese (agricultural fields), Brant spend their winter almost entirely on saltwater. They feed on marine eelgrass, rest on open saltwater, and rarely venture inland.
- **Tidal rhythm:** Brant behavior is tied to the tide cycle. They feed on eelgrass at low to mid-tide when the beds are accessible. At high tide, they rest on open water, socializing and preening. The daily rhythm of feeding-resting-feeding follows the tidal clock, not the solar clock.
- **Flocking behavior:** Brant are highly gregarious. They feed, fly, and rest in dense flocks. The flock is the social unit -- individual Brant are rarely seen alone. This gregarious behavior amplifies the flock murmur: the more birds, the more continuous the sound.
- **Arctic breeding:** Despite their marine winter habits, Brant breed on dry Arctic tundra near coastal areas. Nests are on the ground, often near water. The shift from terrestrial breeding to marine wintering is a seasonal ecological transition that few waterfowl execute so completely.

### The Eelgrass-Brant-Puget Sound Connection

The Brant's eelgrass dependency creates a direct link between this bird and Puget Sound ecosystem health:

- **Eelgrass as foundational species:** Eelgrass beds are among the most productive habitats in Puget Sound. They provide nursery habitat for juvenile Chinook salmon (endangered), Pacific herring (forage fish), and Dungeness crab. Eelgrass stabilizes sediment, reduces erosion, and sequesters carbon.
- **Brant as indicator:** Brant populations track eelgrass health. Declining Brant numbers at a site can indicate eelgrass decline before monitoring programs detect it. The birds are a real-time biological sensor for coastal bay condition.
- **Conservation alignment:** Protecting eelgrass for Brant also protects salmon, herring, crab, and the broader marine food web. The Brant is a charismatic entry point for eelgrass conservation -- an argument for ecosystem protection that transcends any single species.
- **Climate vulnerability:** Both eelgrass and Brant face climate change pressures. Warmer water stresses eelgrass. Shifting Arctic conditions affect Brant breeding success. Ocean acidification threatens the marine invertebrates that share the eelgrass ecosystem. The Brant-eelgrass system is a climate vulnerability nexus.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Puget Sound Partnership; Padilla Bay NERR; WDFW)
