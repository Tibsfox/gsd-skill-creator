The Four Species

Great Horned Owl
Bubo virginianus
200–700 Hz — Sub-bass to bass

The deepest owl call in the PNW. Physical vibration at close range in dense forest. December–January = peak activity: the earliest PNW breeding species, winter is their breeding season. Male/female duet: alternating voices at different pitches. Carries several km in still winter air.

Barred Owl
Strix varia
300–900 Hz — Bass to midrange

“Who cooks for you, who cooks for you-all.” Eastern NA origin, expanded into PNW through 20th century. More vocally aggressive than Spotted Owl. Calls at all hours. Also produces caterwauling. Competitive pressure on the Northern Spotted.

Northern Spotted Owl
Strix occidentalis caurina
400–900 Hz — Similar range to Barred

ESA threatened since 1990. Old-growth forest specialist. Four-note sequence: two short + pause + short + longer. Presence in the chorus = old-growth forest quality indicator. Winter vocalizations primarily territorial against Barred Owl intrusion.

Western Screech-Owl
Megascops kennicottii
700–1,500 Hz — High midrange

Descending tremolo “bouncing ball”: series of whistled notes descending in pitch, increasing in rate. Highest-pitched primary call in the winter chorus. Active at dusk and pre-dawn from low understory perches.

Ecological note: The simultaneous occurrence of Barred Owl and Northern Spotted Owl calls in the same forest documents an ecological transition in progress. The Barred Owl’s expansion has created competitive exclusion pressure on the Spotted Owl in its old-growth habitat. USFWS has initiated experimental Barred Owl removal programs. The winter owl chorus in old-growth PNW forest is an acoustic document of an unresolved species interaction.
