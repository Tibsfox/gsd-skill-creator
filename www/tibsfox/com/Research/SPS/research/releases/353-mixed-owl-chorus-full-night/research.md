## THE OWL COMMUNITY -- FOUR SPECIES

### Each with a Distinct Voice. Each with a Distinct Temporal Pattern.

The Pacific Northwest dense conifer forest winter night owl community includes four species that are regularly co-present and vocally active:

**Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*).** The largest and most acoustically powerful owl in the Pacific Northwest. The Great Horned Owl's territorial call is the deep, resonant "hoo-hoo-hoo, hoo, hoo" that defines the popular conception of owl sound. The call carries extremely far in winter -- the still, cold air of a winter night allows the low-frequency components of the Great Horned's call to propagate across multiple kilometers. Great Horned Owls begin territorial calling in December and January (the earliest breeding species in the Pacific Northwest), making winter their most vocally active season. The male and female often duet, the male's lower voice and the female's higher voice producing a recognizable alternating call pattern.

**Frequency range:** 200-700 Hz fundamental, with harmonics. The lowest-pitched owl call in the Pacific Northwest. The call is felt as a physical vibration at close range in dense forest.

**Barred Owl (*Strix varia*).** A species native to eastern North America that has expanded its range to the Pacific Northwest through the 20th century. The Barred Owl is now common throughout Pacific Northwest dense forest, and its presence has been documented as a significant competitive pressure on the Northern Spotted Owl. The Barred Owl's call is the "who cooks for you, who cooks for you-all" pattern: a rhythmically distinctive eight-note sequence (four beats, four beats) that is among the most immediately recognizable wildlife calls in the Pacific Northwest.

The Barred Owl is more acoustically active and more aggressive in defense of territory than the Spotted Owl. It calls at all hours of the night, responds readily to imitation calls, and produces a variety of calls beyond the primary "who cooks for you" pattern: an extended "hooraw" caterwauling that is startling in its wildness, and a series of shorter vocalizations used in close-range territorial and social contexts.

**Frequency range:** 300-900 Hz primary pattern. Higher-pitched than Great Horned Owl. The "who cooks for you" rhythm is at approximately 1.5-2 second intervals.

**Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*).** The Northern Spotted Owl is one of the most ecologically and politically significant birds in the Pacific Northwest: listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act in 1990, its old-growth habitat requirements were the primary driver of the Pacific Northwest timber wars of the late 1980s and 1990s. The Spotted Owl requires old-growth forest or late-successional forest for nesting and foraging; it is significantly less adaptable to forest fragmentation than the Barred Owl.

The Spotted Owl's call is a series of four notes: two short notes, a pause, one short note, a longer closing note. The sequence sounds less rhythmically emphatic than the Barred Owl's "who cooks for you" and more questioning, exploratory. The voice is medium-pitched, carrying well in dense old-growth forest.

The Northern Spotted Owl is less vocally active in winter than in the breeding season (spring). Winter vocalizations occur primarily in territorial contexts when a Barred Owl's calls are perceived as intrusion. The presence of the Spotted Owl in the winter chorus is a genuine indicator of old-growth forest quality -- its presence means the forest is old enough and large enough to support it.

**Frequency range:** 400-900 Hz. Similar range to Barred Owl but distinctly different call pattern and temporal rhythm.

**Western Screech-Owl (*Megascops kennicottii*).** The smallest owl in the winter chorus. The Western Screech-Owl's primary call is not a screech -- it is a descending tremolo: a series of whistled notes that descend in pitch and increase in rate toward the end of the sequence. This "bouncing ball" call is distinctive and is produced from low perches in shrubby or mixed forest habitat, often within 20 feet of the ground.

The Screech-Owl's call occupies a frequency range (700-1,500 Hz) above the Great Horned and Barred Owls, providing acoustic separation in the crowded frequency space of the winter night chorus. Screech-Owls are year-round residents and vocalize throughout winter.

**Frequency range:** 700-1,500 Hz. The highest-pitched primary call in the winter owl chorus. Occupies the frequency space above the hooting owls.

## THE FULL NIGHT TEMPORAL STRUCTURE

### Dusk to Pre-Dawn. Four Phases.

The mixed owl chorus of a full Pacific Northwest winter night develops through distinct phases from dusk to pre-dawn. This is the temporal development model that distinguishes this entry from the simultaneous-maximum entries: the acoustic character of the forest changes as the night progresses, with different species dominant at different hours.

**Dusk phase (sunset to 2 hours after).** The first owls to become active at dusk are typically the Great Horned Owls: they begin calling shortly after sunset, establishing their territorial positions for the night. The first calls arrive with the last light, placing the acoustic event at the threshold between day and night. Western Screech-Owls also become active at dusk, from lower perches in the forest understory. The dusk phase is characterized by initial territorial establishment -- calling that maps the owl community's spatial distribution.

**Early night phase (2-4 hours after sunset).** The Barred Owls, which often begin calling slightly later than Great Horned Owls, are in full territorial and social calling during the early night. The "who cooks for you" sequences become frequent, and the more complex caterwauling vocalizations occur in the context of inter-individual interactions. Northern Spotted Owls may call during this phase in response to Barred Owl activity if the forest contains both species.

**Deep night phase (4-8 hours after sunset).** The deep hours of winter nights are often the quietest phase of the owl chorus. Activity slows as established territories are not being challenged. Occasional calls mark territory, but the sustained calling of the early phases diminishes. The background becomes the forest itself: wind in the canopy, the creak of large trees, the occasional distant call carrying across the silent winter landscape.

**Pre-dawn phase (1-2 hours before sunrise).** Activity increases again before dawn. Great Horned Owls resume territorial calling in the pre-dawn. Screech-Owls vocalize at high frequency. The chorus builds toward the dawn, at which point the nocturnal chorus gives way to the diurnal: the first Song Sparrows, chickadees, and eventually the dawn chorus species begin as the owls quiet with the arriving light.

## THE BARRED OWL / SPOTTED OWL INTERACTION

### Competitive Exclusion and Forest Conservation

The Barred Owl's expansion into Pacific Northwest old-growth forest is one of the most significant ongoing ecological changes in the region. The Barred Owl is a generalist; the Northern Spotted Owl is a specialist. Where both species occur, the Barred Owl typically displaces the Spotted Owl through direct territorial competition and hybridization. The USFWS has initiated experimental Barred Owl removal programs in parts of the Spotted Owl's range to assess whether Spotted Owl populations can recover when Barred Owl competition is reduced.

In the winter mixed owl chorus, the presence of both species in the same forest is documented by the simultaneous occurrence of their calls: the Barred Owl's "who cooks for you" and the Spotted Owl's four-note sequence in the same acoustic space is a marker of old-growth forest with an ongoing and unresolved species interaction. The acoustic event documents an ecological transition in progress.

(Sources: Barred Owl expansion Pacific Northwest 20th century; Northern Spotted Owl competitive displacement; Barred Owl generalist vs Spotted Owl specialist; USFWS Barred Owl removal experiments; hybridization; simultaneous occurrence = old-growth in transition; acoustic event as ecological document)
