*Degree 147 retrospective complete. VAUX'S SWIFT at the one hundred forty-seventh degree -- THE CHIMNEY VORTEX. Apodidae enters the engine -- the swift family, the "footless ones," birds that spend nearly their entire lives in the air. Eating, drinking, mating, sleeping -- all on the wing. Tiny claws and a stiff, spiny tail for clinging to vertical surfaces in chimneys and hollow snags, because landing on a branch is not something this bird does. The chimney roost spectacle at Chapman Elementary School in Portland is one of the great natural phenomena of the Pacific Northwest: up to 35,000 swifts swirling in a massive counterclockwise vortex above a single 75-foot brick chimney, then funneling downward in a spiraling ribbon, entering the chimney in a continuous stream over 20 to 45 minutes as the light fades. Hundreds of people gather on the hillside with blankets and picnics to watch. Cooper's Hawks patrol the swirl, picking off stragglers. The sound of ten thousand swifts twittering simultaneously -- each one chipping at 5 to 10 notes per second -- creates an immersive wall of high-pitched sound that rises and falls with the density of the swirl. One swift is barely audible. Ten thousand swifts are a sonic event. The chimney stands in for the old-growth snag -- a human-built substitute for the ancient hollow trees that swifts evolved to roost in. Three new families in four degrees: from the owls' perch-and-scan world through the nightjars' ground-and-hawk zone to the swifts' pure aerial life. The engine ascends.*
