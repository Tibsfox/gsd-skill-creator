## STRUCTURAL EVENT: FIRST APODIDAE -- Swifts Enter the Engine

Degree 147 introduces the **swift family** (Apodidae):

- **Apodidae defined:** Swifts are among the most aerial of all birds. The family name comes from Greek *apous* ("without feet") -- a reference to their tiny, nearly vestigial legs. Swifts eat, drink, sleep, and sometimes mate on the wing. They rarely land except to nest.
- **Why Apodidae now?** The engine has moved from owls (Strigidae, 135-144) to nightjars (Caprimulgidae, 145-146) to swifts (Apodidae, 147). All three families are nocturnal or crepuscular, all are insectivorous (or insect-adjacent), and all have cryptic or aerial lifestyles. The progression traces a continuum: sit-and-perch predators (owls) to ground-sitters who hawk (nightjars) to pure aerial insectivores (swifts).
- **First Apodidae:** The Vaux's Swift is the only regularly occurring swift species in the Pacific Northwest, making it the natural first representative.

### The Family Progression

| Degrees | Family | Strategy | Key Trait |
|---------|--------|----------|-----------|
| 135-144 | Strigidae (10) | Perch-and-scan | Vertebrate predation |

Three families, fourteen degrees, a complete ecological progression from predation to pure aerial insectivory.

## THE CHIMNEY ROOST SPECTACLE

### Chapman Elementary School -- Portland's Natural Wonder

The largest known Vaux's Swift roost site is at **Chapman Elementary School** in Portland, Oregon:

- **The chimney:** A 75-foot-tall brick chimney (decommissioned from its original heating function) at Chapman Elementary School in Portland's Northwest District. The chimney has been the primary Vaux's Swift roost site for decades.
- **The numbers:** At peak migration (mid-September), up to **35,000 swifts** have been estimated entering the Chapman chimney in a single evening. The flock is visible from blocks away as a swirling, darkening cloud above the school.
- **The community event:** The Chapman Swift Watch has become a beloved Portland tradition. Hundreds of people gather on the hillside overlooking the school each evening during migration to watch the swifts enter the chimney. Blankets, picnics, children, dogs -- it is a community celebration of a natural phenomenon.
- **The hawks:** Cooper's Hawks sometimes patrol the swirl, picking off individual swifts. The dramatic interplay between predator and prey adds to the spectacle -- the swirl recoils from the hawk's pass, then re-forms.
- **Audubon Portland:** The Audubon Society of Portland has championed the Chapman chimney as a conservation priority. When the Portland Public Schools considered demolishing or capping the chimney, community advocacy (led by Audubon) secured its preservation for the swifts.

### How They Enter

The roosting entry is a choreographed aerial phenomenon:

- **The gathering:** As the sun drops toward the horizon, swifts begin congregating in the sky above the chimney. First dozens, then hundreds, then thousands. The flock swirls in a massive counterclockwise vortex.
- **The vortex:** The swirl tightens as more birds join. The vortex becomes denser, faster, more defined. The sound of thousands of chipping swifts rises in volume.
- **The funnel:** At a critical moment (typically when light levels drop to a threshold), the swirl begins to funnel downward into the chimney. The birds enter in a spiraling stream, descending into the chimney in a continuous ribbon.
- **The duration:** The entry can take 20-45 minutes. The last birds enter in near-darkness. The chimney's interior is lined with clinging swifts, stacked layers deep on the brick walls.
- **Temperature regulation:** Inside the chimney, the mass of swifts generates warmth through collective body heat. The chimney acts as a communal roost where thousands of birds share thermoregulatory benefits.

### Other Roost Sites

While Chapman is the most famous, Vaux's Swifts use communal chimney roosts throughout the PNW:

- **Monroe, Washington:** The Wagner Education Center chimney hosts a significant swift roost.
- **Various schools and buildings:** Throughout Oregon and Washington, old brick chimneys serve as swift roosts. The shift from natural snag cavities to artificial chimneys represents an adaptation to human-modified landscapes.
- **Natural roosts:** In undisturbed old-growth forests, swifts roost in large hollow snags (dead trees). The chimney roost is a human-built substitute for declining old-growth snag habitat.

(Sources: Audubon Society of Portland; Bull & Collins 2007; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## LIFE ON THE WING

### The Most Aerial Life

The Vaux's Swift is among the most aerial birds in the PNW:

- **Eats on the wing:** Swifts capture all prey in flight -- flying insects (mosquitoes, gnats, flies, beetles) are scooped from the air. The wide gape and short bill are adapted for aerial insect capture.
- **Drinks on the wing:** Swifts drink by skimming water surfaces in flight, like swallows. They do not land to drink.
- **Mates on the wing:** Swifts have been observed copulating in mid-air -- a brief aerial coupling during flight.
- **Sleeps on the wing (possibly):** Some swift species (particularly the Alpine Swift and Common Swift of Eurasia) are documented sleeping on the wing -- ascending to high altitude and entering micro-sleep episodes during gliding flight. Whether Vaux's Swifts do this is not definitively established, but the possibility exists.
- **Tiny feet:** The family name Apodidae ("without feet") reflects the near-vestigial legs and feet. Swifts cannot perch on branches like most birds. They cling vertically to surfaces (chimney walls, snag interiors) using their tiny claws and stiff tail feathers as props.

### Flight Characteristics

- **Speed:** Vaux's Swifts fly at estimated speeds of 30-70 km/h during normal flight, with bursts of higher speed during pursuit of prey or evasion of predators.
- **Wing shape:** Long, narrow, swept-back wings adapted for sustained, efficient flight. The silhouette is distinctive: a cigar-shaped body with long, curved wings.
- **Flight pattern:** Rapid, flickering wingbeats interspersed with gliding. The flight looks nervous, twitchy, erratic -- constantly adjusting for micro-gusts and prey targets.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Bull & Collins 2007)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Swarm -- Rapid Chipping in the Vortex

The Vaux's Swift's acoustic contribution is **collective twittering** -- the sound of a swarm in flight:

- **Individual call:** A high-pitched, rapid chip note -- "chip-chip-chip-chip" -- delivered in continuous bursts during flight. Each chip is approximately 0.02-0.05 seconds. The frequency is approximately 5000-8000 Hz -- high, thin, and insect-like.
- **Calling rate:** Extremely fast -- 5-10 chips per second during active flight. The sound is a continuous stream rather than distinct notes at normal listening distances.
- **Flock aggregation:** When hundreds or thousands of swifts swirl together, the individual chip notes merge into a collective **twittering wall of sound** -- a high-pitched, shimmering, continuous noise. The sound rises and falls as the flock density changes, creating a dynamic soundscape.
- **Roost entry sound:** As swifts funnel into a chimney, the chipping intensifies. The acoustic environment inside the chimney (hard, reflective brick walls) amplifies and echoes the twittering. From outside, the chimney produces a distinctive high-pitched buzzing.
- **Amplitude:** Individual: quiet (audible at 20-50m). Flock: moderate to loud (audible at 200m+ for large roost swirls). The acoustic output is cumulative -- one swift is barely audible; ten thousand swifts are a sonic event.
- **Context:** Chipping occurs during all flight activity -- feeding, commuting, displaying, roosting. The sound is inseparable from the bird's aerial lifestyle.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
