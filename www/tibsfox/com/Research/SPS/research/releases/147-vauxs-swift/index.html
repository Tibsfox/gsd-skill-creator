FIRST APODIDAE — Swifts Enter the Engine

Third new family in four degrees: Strigidae (135-144) → Caprimulgidae (145-146) → Apodidae (147). A progression from perch-and-scan predators to ground-sitting insectivores to pure aerial specialists. Apous = “without feet.” Swifts rarely land except to nest.

The Chimney Roost Spectacle

Chapman Elementary School, Portland — up to 35,000 swifts enter a single 75-foot brick chimney at dusk. The flock swirls in a massive counterclockwise vortex, then funnels downward in a spiraling stream. Entry takes 20–45 minutes. Inside: swifts cling to brick walls, layers deep, sharing body heat. Cooper’s Hawks patrol the swirl. Hundreds of people gather on the hillside to watch. A PNW tradition.

Life on the Wing

Eats, drinks, and mates in flight. Captures insects with wide gape in continuous aerial hawking. Drinks by skimming water surfaces. Tiny, near-vestigial feet — clings vertically to surfaces using claws and stiff tail. 30–70 km/h normal flight. Cigar-shaped body, swept-back wings. Flickering wingbeats.
