## SILENCE AS SOUND STRATEGY

### The Evolutionary Logic of Inaudibility

The Harbor Porpoise's silence is not absence -- it is strategy:

- **NBHF echolocation.** Like its family member the Dall's Porpoise (degree 186), the Harbor Porpoise uses Narrow-Band High-Frequency clicks. But the Harbor Porpoise has refined this to an extreme -- its clicks are among the highest-frequency, most directional echolocation signals of any cetacean.
- **Anti-predator adaptation.** The NBHF clicks operate above the hearing range of orcas (Bigg's transient orcas, which prey on porpoises). By echolocating at frequencies orcas cannot detect, the Harbor Porpoise hunts and navigates without alerting its primary predator. Silence = survival.
- **No whistles.** Many dolphins produce whistles for social communication. Harbor Porpoises produce NO whistles. The absence of whistles may be an extension of the anti-predator strategy -- any broadband sound could be detected by orcas. The Harbor Porpoise has eliminated ALL audible sound from its repertoire.
- **Behavioral crypsis.** The small body (1.4-1.9 m), the brief surfacing, the avoidance of boats, the absence of breaching -- all of this reduces the Harbor Porpoise's detectability. Sound silence + behavioral silence = near-invisibility.
- **The cost of silence.** Without whistles, Harbor Porpoises have limited social communication options. Group coordination relies on proximity and visual cues rather than vocal signals. Silence comes at a social cost.

## THE SALISH SEA'S HIDDEN CETACEAN

### Present but Unseen

The Harbor Porpoise is one of the most common cetaceans in the Salish Sea -- yet most people have never seen one:

- **Population.** An estimated 10,000-15,000 Harbor Porpoises inhabit the inland waters of Washington State (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands, outer coast).
- **Recovery story.** Harbor Porpoises largely disappeared from southern Puget Sound in the 1970s-1990s, possibly due to pollution, vessel traffic, and prey declines. Since the 2000s, they have been recolonizing southern Puget Sound -- a quiet recovery.
- **Difficult to observe.** Small body. Brief surfacings. No bow-riding. Avoids boats. Rarely breaches. Does not create visible splashes. The Harbor Porpoise is present but requires patience and sharp eyes to see.
- **Year-round resident.** Present in the Salish Sea all 12 months. Does not migrate. A year-round companion that most people never notice.
- **Diet.** Small fish: herring, anchovy, sand lance, smelt. Forages in shallow water. Dives are typically short (30-90 seconds) and shallow (50-100 meters).
- **Name etymology.** "Harbor Porpoise" because it frequents harbors, bays, and sheltered waters. "Phocoena" from Greek "phokaina" (porpoise). The name describes the habitat, not the behavior -- if named for behavior, it might be called the "invisible porpoise."

(Sources: WDFW cetacean surveys; Salish Sea marine mammal data; population recovery studies; engine internal analysis)

**Echolocation Clicks (INAUDIBLE to humans):**

- **Type:** Narrow-Band High-Frequency. Highest-frequency porpoise clicks measured.
- **Directionality:** Highly directional. Narrow beam.
