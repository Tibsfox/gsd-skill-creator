## THE THREE SPECIES -- ACOUSTIC PROFILES

### Two Apex Predators and One Apex Canid

**Great Horned Owl (Bubo virginianus) -- SPS 135, 136, 299, 309**
The Great Horned Owl is the engine's most-documented species in the SPS sequence: four prior appearances (135: single owl, 136: pair duet, 299: territorial, 309: pair). The acoustic profile is thoroughly established: the deep hooting sequence (typically 3-8 hoots per call sequence, dominant frequency 200-400 Hz), the pair duet (male and female calling at offset pitches, the male's call deeper by approximately a minor third), and the territorial vocalizations that accompany nest establishment in late winter. At degree 328, two Great Horned Owls are present -- a pair in duet.

- **The GHO call structure.** The typical sequence: "Hoo-hoo-hoooo hoo-hoo" (5-note deep hoot). The male: approximately 300-350 Hz fundamental, with harmonics. The female: approximately 400-450 Hz fundamental (higher pitch, smaller bird). In a pair duet, the two birds call alternately, with the female typically initiating and the male responding -- the alternation is rapid enough that the calls overlap slightly at the edges, creating a brief doubled-hoot effect.
- **Winter timing.** Great Horned Owls are the earliest-breeding owl in North America, beginning nest preparation in January in many Pacific Northwest locations. The winter territorial behavior documented at degree 328 -- the pair duet -- is the acoustic signal of this nest preparation. The pair duet establishes and defends a territory against other GHOs and against competing predators (including Coyotes).
- **The territorial signal.** The GHO pair duet is one of the most powerful territorial acoustic signals in the Pacific Northwest's nocturnal soundscape. The deep hooting carries for distances of 1-2 km in still winter air. Multiple owl pairs in adjacent territories can be audible simultaneously from a single listening point.

**Coyote (Canis latrans) -- SPS 208, 209 -- FIRST CANID IN MULTI-SPECIES EVENT**
The Coyote entered SPS at degree 208 (coyote howl, E=5) and degree 209 (coyote pup yipping, E=4). Degree 328 is the FIRST TIME THE COYOTE APPEARS SIMULTANEOUSLY WITH ANOTHER SPECIES in the engine:

- **The Coyote howl.** The coyote's primary long-distance vocalization is the howl -- a sustained, rising-and-falling call typically lasting 2-5 seconds per call, with fundamental frequencies of 800-1,200 Hz and strong harmonics extending to 4,000 Hz and above. The howl carries for distances of 3-5 km in still winter conditions. Coyotes howl to maintain contact with pack members, to advertise territory, and in response to auditory stimuli (including owl vocalizations).
- **The howl-yip sequence.** Coyotes frequently follow a howl with a rapid yip sequence -- short, staccato, high-frequency calls (1,000-3,000 Hz, 0.1-0.3 seconds each) that mark the end of the howl phrase. The howl-yip-yip-yip pattern is the characteristic Coyote vocalization sequence that is most distinctive in the winter landscape.
- **Why Coyotes howl when owls call.** The relationship between Coyote vocalization and Great Horned Owl vocalization is well-documented: Coyotes frequently respond to GHO calls with howls. The mechanism is not fully understood -- possibly related to the Coyote's territorial response to any loud vocalization in the environment, possibly related to the Coyote's assessment of predator presence (GHOs prey on Coyote pups; adult Coyotes may respond to owl calls as territorial signals). The practical result: in winter, a GHO pair duet frequently triggers Coyote howling, and Coyote howling can trigger GHO calling in response. The nocturnal acoustic exchange between these two species is a characteristic feature of Pacific Northwest winter nights.
- **FIRST CANID IN MULTI-SPECIES SPS EVENT.** The Coyote at degree 328 is the first Canidae member to appear in a multi-species simultaneous acoustic event in the engine. All previous Coyote entries were solo-species documentation (208, 209). Degree 328 documents the Coyote in direct acoustic interaction with another species for the first time.

## THE FOREST EDGE ECOSYSTEM

### Why the Forest Edge Is the Setting

The forest edge -- the transition zone between dense forest interior and open habitat (meadow, agricultural land, suburban development, or clearcut) -- is the specific setting for the degree-328 event:

- **Habitat overlap.** Both Great Horned Owls and Coyotes are generalist predators that use both forest interior and open habitats. They are both most active at the forest edge, where the density of edge-associated prey (deer mice, voles, rabbits, snowshoe hares) is typically highest. The forest edge concentrates both species' territories.
- **Acoustic propagation.** The forest edge is acoustically distinct from the forest interior: sound from the interior propagates outward toward the open edge without the absorption that the forest interior's vegetation provides. The GHO pair duet and the Coyote howl-yip sequence, originating from the forest interior or the forest edge, propagate most efficiently across the adjacent open habitat. The forest edge is where these vocalizations are most clearly audible.
- **Winter conditions.** In winter, the forest edge habitat has reduced vegetation cover (deciduous trees have lost their leaves, understory vegetation is dormant) -- the acoustic propagation through the winter forest edge is significantly better than in summer. The leafless winter forest is acoustically more transparent: sound carries further through the forest edge in February than in August. This transparency enhances the audibility of both the GHO and the Coyote at distance.
- **Pacific Northwest lowland distribution.** The forest edge ecosystem is distributed throughout the Pacific Northwest lowlands and foothills -- the transition zones between the Cascade and Olympic foothill forest and the agricultural and developed lowland valleys. The night predator chorus documented at degree 328 is a feature of these distributed forest edges: the Snoqualmie Valley edges, the Cowlitz Valley edges, the Puget Sound foothill zone.

(Sources: Forest edge generalist predator habitat; prey density at forest edge; GHO Coyote habitat overlap; acoustic propagation to open habitat; winter acoustic transparency; leafless forest edge winter; Pacific Northwest lowland and foothill forest edge distribution; Snoqualmie Valley; Cowlitz Valley; Puget Sound foothills)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8 THROUGH NOCTURNAL INTENSITY

### The Night Predator Chorus at Maximum

The E=8 calibration at degree 328 reflects the combined nocturnal intensity of two GHOs duetting and a Coyote responding:

**GHO pair duet component:**
- Male: 300-350 Hz fundamental, 5-note sequence, each note 0.4-0.8 seconds, total sequence 4-6 seconds. Harmonics to 2,000 Hz. Amplitude: 70-80 dB at 100 meters.
- Female: 400-450 Hz fundamental, similar sequence structure. Amplitude similar to male.
- Pair duet: the two birds alternate, with brief overlaps at sequence transitions. The combined pair duet creates a complex interleaved pattern: male calls, female responds within 0.5-2 seconds, brief overlap at the transition, then the pattern repeats. The duet cycle (male + female + interval) repeats every 15-30 seconds during active territorial display.

**Coyote howl-yip component:**
- Howl: 800-1,200 Hz rising fundamental, harmonics to 4,000 Hz, 2-5 second duration, 75-85 dB at 100 meters.
- Yip sequence: 1,000-3,000 Hz, 0.1-0.3 second per yip, rapid sequence of 3-8 yips following the howl.
- The howl-yip sequence total duration: 5-12 seconds. Amplitude peak during howl: 75-85 dB at 100 meters.
- Multiple Coyote response: if a pack is present, additional animals may join the howl. A Coyote chorus (3-5 animals) can reach 80-90 dB at 100 meters.

**Combined acoustic event:**
The night predator chorus at peak: GHO pair duet (300-450 Hz + harmonics to 2,000 Hz, 70-80 dB, approximately 30-second cycle) + Coyote howl-yip (800-4,000 Hz, 75-90 dB, 5-12 second sequences). Combined frequency range: 200-4,000 Hz. Combined amplitude at center of activity: 80-90 dB. Temporal character: structured cycles (GHO duet cycle ~30 sec) plus irregular responses (Coyote triggered by GHO calls). The combined acoustic event is complex: two periodic sources (GHO pair) plus one aperiodic responding source (Coyote) interacting in real time.

**E=8 calibration.** The E=8 reflects the nocturnal intensity of the combined event -- not any individual species at maximum but the combined acoustic presence of two apex avian predators in pair duet and one apex canid in territorial response at the same time, in winter, in the dark. Each species alone is E=5-7; together, in the specific interaction documented at degree 328, they create an E=8 nocturnal chorus.

(Sources: GHO male call 300-350 Hz; female 400-450 Hz; minor third interval; 5-note sequence; duet cycle 15-30 sec; 70-80 dB at 100m; Coyote howl 800-1200 Hz; harmonic 4000 Hz; howl 2-5 sec; yip sequence 3-8 yips; pack howl 80-90 dB at 100m; combined 200-4000 Hz; 80-90 dB; E=8 nocturnal combined intensity)

The Coyote's presence at degree 328 in a multi-species simultaneous acoustic event is a milestone in the engine's documentation:

- **Canidae position in SPS.** The Canidae are unusual in the engine's mammal documentation: the Gray Wolf (degree 210) and the Coyote (208, 209) are the only Canidae members documented in the SPS sequence. Degree 328 is the first time either Canidae member appears in a multi-species simultaneous acoustic context.
- **The Coyote's acoustic range.** The Coyote has the widest vocalization range of any North American canid: howls (long-distance territorial/contact), yips (short-range contact and excitement), barks (alarm), whines (social appeasement), and group choruses. Gray Wolves in the Pacific Northwest occupy higher-elevation wilderness areas (North Cascades, northeast Washington). The Coyote is the Canidae representative at the forest edge because Coyotes are lowland generalists.

## THE WINTER SEASON -- PEAK TERRITORIAL ACTIVITY

### Why Winter Is the Maximum Night Chorus Event

Winter is the optimal season for the night predator chorus because both species are at peak territorial acoustic activity:

- **GHO winter behavior.** Great Horned Owls are the earliest-breeding large owl in North America. In the Pacific Northwest, GHO pairs begin their nest preparation, territory establishment, and pair bonding in January-February. The pair duet vocalization is at its most intense during this period: the two birds are establishing their territory and reinforcing their pair bond through duetting. After egg-laying (late January to March), the female becomes more territory-bound and the male increases his foraging and protective calling. The peak acoustic activity is in the pre-egg period: January-February in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Coyote winter behavior.** Coyotes in the Pacific Northwest breed in January-March, with a peak mating period in February. During the breeding season, male Coyotes are highly vocal -- advertising their presence and territory to females and competing males through increased howling frequency. The night predator chorus at degree 328 is most intense in January-February when both species are simultaneously at peak vocal activity: GHOs in pair-bond reinforcement, Coyotes in breeding advertisement.
- **The winter night.** Winter nights in the Pacific Northwest (latitude 47-49°N) are long -- 14-16 hours of darkness from December through February. The nocturnal predators have extended active periods. The combination of long nights, peak territorial behavior, and winter acoustic transparency (leafless forest edge) makes January-February the acoustic maximum for the night predator chorus.

(Sources: GHO earliest-breeding large owl North America; Pacific Northwest pair preparation January; duet peak pre-egg period January-February; egg laying late January-March; Coyote breeding January-March peak February; male advertising through increased howling; winter night length Pacific Northwest 47-49°N; 14-16 hours darkness; combined peak January-February)
