Why Coyotes Respond to Owls

The acoustic interaction between Great Horned Owls and Coyotes is well-documented. GHOs prey on Coyote pups, creating a predator-prey relationship that may explain Coyotes’ alertness to GHO calls. Additionally, both species are territorial and may respond to any loud vocal signal in the environment as potential competition. The practical result: a GHO pair duet in winter frequently triggers Coyote howling, and Coyote howling can trigger increased GHO calling. The exchange is not aggressive encounter but acoustic cross-species territorial signaling — each species broadcasting its presence into the darkness.

Winter Timing — The Peak

January–February is the acoustic maximum because both species are simultaneously at peak vocal activity: Great Horned Owls in pre-egg pair-bond reinforcement (the earliest breeding large owl in North America), Coyotes in breeding season advertisement (peak mating February). Pacific Northwest winter nights (47–49°N) provide 14–16 hours of darkness. The leafless winter forest edge provides excellent acoustic propagation: sound carries further through the winter forest than in summer. Three conditions converge: long nights, peak vocal behavior, acoustic transparency.

E=8 Through Nocturnal Intensity

Each species alone: GHO solo (SPS 135, E=5). GHO pair duet (SPS 136, E=5-6). Coyote howl (SPS 208, E=5). Together, in active acoustic interaction at the winter forest edge at night, the combined acoustic event reaches E=8. The E=8 is not about amplitude alone (80-90 dB combined) but about the quality of the nocturnal multi-species territorial exchange: three predators, two species, the darkness, winter, and the sounds of territory being established and defended simultaneously across two taxonomic families (Strigidae and Canidae).
