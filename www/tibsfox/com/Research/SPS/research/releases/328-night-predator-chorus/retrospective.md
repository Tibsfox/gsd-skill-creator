## What Worked

1. **The FIRST CANID MULTI-SPECIES milestone is correctly identified and appropriately prominent.** The Coyote's prior entries (208, 209) were solo-species. Degree 328 is the first time the Coyote appears in simultaneous multi-species acoustic context in the engine. This is a genuine milestone -- the first member of the Canidae to participate in a multi-species SPS event.
2. **The GHO-Coyote acoustic interaction documentation is scientifically grounded.** The relationship between GHO calls and Coyote vocal response is documented in the behavioral ecology literature. The mechanism (GHO predation on Coyote pups → adult Coyote alertness to GHO calls; territorial response to any loud vocalization) is accurately framed as documented behavior rather than speculation.
3. **The winter peak overlap is correctly identified as the key ecological variable.** The simultaneous peak of GHO pair-bond reinforcement (January-February, earliest-breeding large owl) and Coyote breeding season advertising (peak February) is the ecological coincidence that makes the night predator chorus an E=8 event. Outside this winter window, the two species are less likely to be simultaneously at maximum vocal activity.
4. **E=8 through nocturnal intensity is the correct calibration.** Each species alone is E=5-6. Together, in active cross-species interaction in winter, they create E=8. The energy is not in the amplitude alone (80-90 dB combined) but in the quality of the nocturnal multi-species territorial exchange.

## Discoveries

1. **The minor third interval between GHO male (300-350 Hz) and female (400-450 Hz) is one of the most musically specific acoustic facts in the engine's mammal/bird documentation.** The pair duet is not just two birds calling at the same time -- the two voices are separated by a musical interval (the minor third). The pair duet has harmonic structure. This acoustic fact links the GHO duet documentation to the engine's music tradition in an unexpected way.
2. **The temporal structure of the combined event is uniquely complex: two periodic sources (GHO pair, 15-30 sec cycle) plus one aperiodic responding source (Coyote, triggered by the periodic GHO call).** The combined acoustic event has both a rhythmic structure (the GHO duet cycle) and an unpredictable responding element (when the Coyote responds and for how long). This combination of periodic and aperiodic sources in real-time interaction is acoustically unusual -- the listener can follow the GHO rhythm while being surprised by the Coyote's responses.
3. **The Coyote's howl carries further than the GHO's call.** The Coyote howl carries 3-5 km in still winter air; the GHO call carries 1-2 km. A listener in the forest edge can hear the Coyote responding from twice the distance at which the owls are audible. The Coyote may be responding to GHOs that are not audible to a human listener at the same location.
