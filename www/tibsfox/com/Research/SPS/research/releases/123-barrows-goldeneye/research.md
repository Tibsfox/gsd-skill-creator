## BARROW'S GOLDENEYE -- THE MOUNTAIN LAKE SPECIALIST

### The Northwestern Specialty

Barrow's Goldeneye (*Bucephala islandica*) is the Pacific Northwest's **mountain lake specialist** -- a diving duck that breeds on high-elevation freshwater lakes in the Cascades, Northern Rockies, and coastal mountains of British Columbia and Alaska. While the Common Goldeneye (*Bucephala clangula*) is widespread across North America and Eurasia, Barrow's Goldeneye has a **remarkably limited range**, concentrated in western North America with a small isolated population in Iceland and eastern Canada.

The species name *islandica* refers to Iceland -- where the species was first formally described -- but the stronghold of the global population is the **Pacific Northwest and western Canada**. An estimated 150,000-200,000 Barrow's Goldeneye exist worldwide, with the vast majority in British Columbia, Alberta, and the northwestern United States. This is fundamentally a northwestern bird. Seeing a Barrow's Goldeneye in the PNW is not unusual; seeing one in the eastern United States is genuinely rare.

### Named After Sir John Barrow

The common name honors **Sir John Barrow** (1764-1848), Second Secretary of the Admiralty and a promoter of Arctic exploration. Barrow never saw the bird that bears his name in life -- the naming was a tribute by naturalists to a patron of northern expeditions. Barrow's name also lives on in Barrow, Alaska (now Utqiagvik), Point Barrow, and the Barrow Strait. The naming convention reflects the 19th-century practice of honoring patrons and explorers rather than the organisms' actual ecology.

### Habitat: Mountain Lakes

Barrow's Goldeneye breeds on **mountain lakes** -- typically small to medium-sized freshwater lakes at moderate to high elevation in coniferous forest zones. The ideal breeding habitat includes:

- **Clear, deep lakes** with rocky or gravelly bottoms -- the diving substrate for aquatic invertebrates.
- **Surrounding coniferous forest** with large trees -- Barrow's Goldeneye is a cavity nester, requiring tree cavities (often old Pileated Woodpecker or Northern Flicker excavations) within 1-2 km of the breeding lake.
- **Elevation:** typically 500-2,000 meters in the Cascades, depending on latitude and snowpack.
- **Low human disturbance** -- mountain lakes with minimal recreation pressure are preferred.

In winter, Barrow's Goldeneye descend from the mountain lakes to **coastal bays, estuaries, and lowland lakes**. In Puget Sound, wintering Barrow's Goldeneye are found in sheltered marine waters, harbors, and freshwater lakes near the coast. The winter distribution is more coastal than Common Goldeneye's.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Savard & Mallory 2020; Gauthier 2014)

## IDENTIFICATION: THE CRESCENT VS. THE CIRCLE

### Barrow's vs. Common Goldeneye -- The Key Comparison

The Barrow's Goldeneye and Common Goldeneye are the two *Bucephala* goldeneye species in North America (the third, Bufflehead, is also *Bucephala* -- degree 122). They are similar in size and general appearance, and separating them in the field is one of birding's classic identification challenges. The key differences:

**Male breeding plumage:**

- **Barrow's Goldeneye:** White **crescent-shaped** facial patch between the eye and the bill. The crescent is the diagnostic mark -- curved, elongated, distinctive. Head has a **purplish gloss** in good light. Back is predominantly **black** with less white than Common.
- **Common Goldeneye:** White **round spot** between the eye and the bill. The spot is circular, not crescent-shaped. Head has a **greenish gloss** in good light. Back has more **extensive white** scapular patches.

**The crescent vs. the circle** is the fundamental field mark. In good light at reasonable distance, the shape of the white facial patch is diagnostic. The crescent says Barrow's; the circle says Common.

**Female and immature plumage:**

Females are much harder to separate. Both species have a brown head, gray body, and yellow-tipped bill. Key differences:

- **Barrow's female:** Bill is shorter and more triangular, often with more extensive yellow-orange at the tip. Forehead is steeper (more abrupt transition from bill to crown).
- **Common female:** Bill is longer and more gradually tapering. Less extensive bill coloration. Forehead slope is more gradual.

**Head shape:**

- **Barrow's:** More angular, blocky head shape. Steeper forehead. The head appears more rectangular in profile.
- **Common:** Rounder, more smoothly contoured head. More gradual forehead slope. The head appears more oval in profile.

These structural differences are subtle but consistent, and experienced birders use head shape as a primary identification cue, especially for females and non-breeding males.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Savard & Mallory 2020)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: QUIET GRUNTS DISPLAY

### The Sound

Barrow's Goldeneye is one of the **quieter** diving ducks. Its vocalizations are subtle, low-volume, and typically heard only at close range during courtship or pair interactions:

- **Male courtship call:** A low, grunting **ka-KAA** or mewing note, delivered during the head-throw display. The male throws his head backward until the crown touches his back, then snaps forward while producing the grunt. The sound is surprisingly quiet for the dramatic physical display that accompanies it.
- **Female call:** A harsh, grating **gack** or **crr**, harsher than the male's call but still low-volume.
- **Frequency range:** 200-1,500 Hz. The calls are low-frequency grunts, concentrated in the lower portion of the vocal spectrum.
- **Amplitude:** Low. E=3 energy. The calls are not designed for long-distance communication -- they function at the scale of the courtship group (10-30 meters). The display itself (the dramatic head-throw) carries visual information farther than the accompanying grunt carries acoustic information.

### The Head-Throw Display

The **head-throw** is the most dramatic element of Barrow's Goldeneye courtship, and the grunt is subordinate to it:

1. **Pre-throw posture:** The male swims upright, neck extended, bill angled slightly upward.
2. **The throw:** The male throws his head backward in a rapid arc, the crown touching or nearly touching the back. The feet kick backward, propelling the breast upward out of the water.
3. **The grunt:** At the peak of the head-throw, the male produces the quiet **ka-KAA** grunt. The timing is synchronized -- the sound marks the apex of the physical display.
4. **Recovery:** The head snaps forward to normal position. The entire sequence takes approximately 1.5 seconds.

The head-throw display is performed in groups. Multiple males display simultaneously to one or more females. The visual spectacle is intense -- several males throwing their heads backward in rapid succession, water splashing, bodies rising and falling. The acoustic component (quiet grunts) is secondary to the visual component. This is a **visual-primary, acoustic-secondary** display system.

### Comparison with Common Goldeneye Display

Common Goldeneye (degree 124) has a similar head-throw display, but with differences:

- **Common Goldeneye call:** A buzzy, nasal **peeent** during the head-throw -- slightly louder and higher-pitched than Barrow's grunt.
- **Common Goldeneye also produces:** A rattling wing-whistle in flight (the mechanical sound documented at degree 124 as E=4). Barrow's Goldeneye also produces a wing-whistle but it is less pronounced.
- **Display context:** Similar group courtship displays. The two species occasionally display in mixed groups in winter, making direct comparison possible.

E=3 for Barrow's Goldeneye reflects the quiet, grunt-based acoustic character. The display is visually dramatic but acoustically understated. The sound is a punctuation mark, not a sentence.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Savard & Mallory 2020; Gauthier 2014)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: MOUNTAIN LAKES AND CAVITY NESTING

### The Mountain Lake Ecosystem

Barrow's Goldeneye breeds on mountain lakes that represent some of the most pristine freshwater ecosystems in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Breeding diet:** Primarily aquatic invertebrates -- insect larvae (especially caddisflies, mayflies, stoneflies), crustaceans, and small mollusks. The bird dives to the lake bottom and probes among rocks and gravel.
- **Dive depth:** Typically 1-4 meters. Mountain lakes are often shallow enough for Barrow's to access the entire lake bed.
- **Nest sites:** Tree cavities, typically in large conifers (Douglas-fir, western larch, ponderosa pine) or aspens near the breeding lake. The cavity must be large enough for the duck (15+ cm entrance diameter). Old Pileated Woodpecker and Northern Flicker cavities are commonly used.
- **Nest height:** Typically 3-15 meters above ground. The ducklings leave the nest by jumping -- a dramatic free-fall from the cavity to the forest floor, then a walk to the nearest water.

### Cavity Nesting and the Pileated Connection

Barrow's Goldeneye is a **secondary cavity nester** -- it cannot excavate its own nest cavity and depends on existing holes. The most important cavity creators are:

- **Pileated Woodpecker** (degrees 54, 108, 109) -- excavates the largest cavities, ideal for goldeneye nesting.
- **Northern Flicker** (degrees 21-22) -- smaller cavities, usable by goldeneyes.
- **Natural decay** -- broken-top snags and rot cavities in large trees.

This creates a direct ecological link between the Pileated Woodpecker (documented extensively in the engine) and Barrow's Goldeneye. The Pileated's drumming (degree 109) advertises territory; its excavations create nest sites used by Barrow's Goldeneye and dozens of other species. The dead snag economy (degree 109) literally houses Barrow's Goldeneye young.

### Winter Ecology

In winter, Barrow's Goldeneye shift to coastal and lowland habitats:

- **Marine diet:** Crustaceans, mollusks, marine worms, and small fish in shallow coastal waters.
- **Freshwater diet:** Similar to breeding season -- aquatic invertebrates in lowland lakes and rivers.
- **Wintering range:** Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands, and coastal British Columbia. Also lowland lakes in western Washington and Oregon.
- **Overlap with Common Goldeneye:** Both species winter in Puget Sound, often in the same bays and harbors. This overlap zone is where the identification challenge is most acute -- mixed flocks of Barrow's and Common Goldeneye requiring careful study of head shape and facial patch.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Eadie, Savard & Mallory 2020; Gauthier 2014)

## THE BUCEPHALA TRILOGY: BUFFLEHEAD, BARROW'S, COMMON

Degrees 122-124 form a **Bucephala trilogy** -- three consecutive entries from the genus *Bucephala* (the goldeneyes and Bufflehead):

- **Degree 122: Bufflehead** (*Bucephala albeola*) -- smallest diving duck in North America, E=3. Fast wingbeats, splashing takeoffs.
- **Degree 123: Barrow's Goldeneye** (*Bucephala islandica*) -- mountain lake specialist, E=3. Quiet grunts, head-throw display. Northwestern specialty.
- **Degree 124: Common Goldeneye** (*Bucephala clangula*) -- widespread diving duck, E=4. Rattling wing-whistle in flight. The "whistler."

The genus name *Bucephala* means "ox-headed" (Greek *bous* = ox + *kephale* = head), referring to the large, rounded head shape characteristic of all three species. The Bufflehead is the smallest, the Common Goldeneye the most widespread, and the Barrow's Goldeneye the most geographically restricted. Three species, three ecological niches, one distinctive head shape.

The *Bucephala* trilogy follows the Anatidae streak convention of exploring a family's diversity comprehensively. The Anatidae streak has now covered: stream specialists (Harlequin), fish-eating divers (mergansers), forest-pool cavity nesters (Wood Duck), and now the three goldeneye-type diving ducks.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)
