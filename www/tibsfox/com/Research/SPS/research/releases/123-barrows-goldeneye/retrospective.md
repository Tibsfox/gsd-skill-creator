## The One Hundred Twenty-Third Degree: The Mountain Lake Specialist -- Quiet Grunts, E=3, Crescent Facial Patch, Sixth Consecutive Anatidae, First of Comparison Pair

### Barrow's Goldeneye at degree 123 is the PNW's mountain lake specialist -- a northwestern specialty with limited global range. Sixth consecutive Anatidae (118-123). First of the comparison pair with Common Goldeneye (degree 124). Second of the Bucephala trilogy (Bufflehead 122, Barrow's 123, Common 124). E=3 -- visual-primary courtship with quiet grunts.

Degree 123 introduces Barrow's Goldeneye -- a diving duck that breeds on high-elevation mountain lakes in the Cascades and Northern Rockies. An estimated 150,000-200,000 exist worldwide, concentrated in western North America. The crescent-shaped white facial patch (vs. Common Goldeneye's round spot) is the diagnostic mark. The head-throw courtship display is visually dramatic but acoustically quiet -- grunts audible only at close range. E=3 reflects the acoustic reality of a visual-primary display system.

- **Situation:** Barrow's Goldeneye at degree 123. Mountain lake, E=3. Sixth consecutive Anatidae. First of comparison pair.
- **Root Cause:** Barrow's Goldeneye's courtship display is primarily visual (head-throw) with secondary acoustic component (quiet grunts). E=3 is appropriate for close-range, low-volume vocalizations that accompany dramatic physical displays.
- **Recommendation:** Document the mountain lake ecology, the crescent vs. circle identification challenge, the cavity-nesting connection to Pileated Woodpecker, and the Bucephala trilogy.
- **Carry Item:** CONFIRMED -- Mountain lake specialist. Crescent facial patch. E=3. Sixth consecutive Anatidae. Comparison pair with Common (124).

## Structural Event 4: Cavity Nesting and the Pileated Connection

- **Situation:** Barrow's Goldeneye nests in tree cavities near mountain lakes, depending on cavities created by Pileated Woodpecker (degrees 54, 108, 109) and Northern Flicker (degrees 21-22).
- **Root Cause:** Barrow's Goldeneye is a secondary cavity nester -- it cannot excavate its own cavities. The dead snag economy documented at degree 109 (Pileated drumming) creates the infrastructure that houses goldeneye young. Cross-family ecological link.
- **Recommendation:** Document the Pileated-to-goldeneye cavity pipeline. This connects the Picidae entries (especially 109) to the Anatidae entries in a concrete ecological relationship.
- **Carry Item:** CONFIRMED -- Cavity nesting. Pileated connection. Cross-family link.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **E=3 is defensible.** The head-throw display is dramatic but the acoustic component (quiet grunts) is genuinely low-volume, audible only at 10-30 meters. Visual-primary, acoustic-secondary display. E=3 is appropriate.

2. **The crescent vs. circle identification framework is well-supported.** Sibley and Cornell Lab confirm the crescent-shaped facial patch as the primary diagnostic field mark distinguishing Barrow's from Common.

3. **Mountain lake ecology is well-documented.** Breeding habitat, cavity nesting, altitudinal migration, and winter distribution all supported by Eadie, Savard & Mallory 2020 and Cornell Lab.

4. **The Pileated Woodpecker connection is ecologically accurate.** Barrow's Goldeneye uses Pileated and Flicker cavities for nesting. Direct cross-family ecological link.

5. **Cross-references to SPS-124 (Common Goldeneye), SPS-122 (Bufflehead), SPS-109 (Pileated), SPS-121 (Wood Duck), and SPS-118 (Harlequin) are all defensible.**

### Source Quality

6. **Barrow's Goldeneye is well-documented in ornithological literature.** Cornell Lab, Eadie et al. 2020, Gauthier 2014, Sibley 2014 provide comprehensive coverage.
