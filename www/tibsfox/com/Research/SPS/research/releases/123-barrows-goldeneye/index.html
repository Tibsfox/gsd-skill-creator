Barrow’s Goldeneye

Bucephala islandica (Anatidae) — E=3 — 6th Consecutive Anatidae — Mountain Lake Specialist
quiet grunts during courtship display — E=3. The PNW’s mountain lake specialist — a northwestern specialty with limited global range. Sixth consecutive Anatidae (118–123). First of a comparison pair with Common Goldeneye (degree 124): crescent facial patch vs. round spot. Named after Sir John Barrow. Second of the Bucephala trilogy (Bufflehead 122, Barrow’s 123, Common 124).

Mountain Lake Specialist — Northwestern Specialty

Barrow’s Goldeneye breeds on high-elevation mountain lakes in the Cascades and Northern Rockies — clear, deep lakes surrounded by coniferous forest. An estimated 150,000–200,000 exist worldwide, concentrated in western North America with a small isolated population in Iceland. This is fundamentally a northwestern bird. Seeing one in the PNW is expected; seeing one in the eastern US is genuinely rare. Winters on coastal waters and lowland lakes in Puget Sound.

The Crescent vs. The Circle — Barrow’s vs. Common

The diagnostic field mark: Barrow’s Goldeneye has a crescent-shaped white facial patch; Common Goldeneye has a round spot. Barrow’s head shows a purplish gloss; Common shows greenish gloss. Barrow’s has a more angular, blocky head shape; Common is rounder. In winter on Puget Sound, both species occur in mixed flocks — careful study of facial patch shape and head structure is required. This is one of birding’s classic identification challenges.

Cavity Nester — The Pileated Connection

Barrow’s Goldeneye nests in tree cavities near mountain lakes, depending on cavities created by Pileated Woodpecker (degrees 54, 108, 109) and Northern Flicker (degrees 21–22). The dead snag economy documented at degree 109 literally houses Barrow’s Goldeneye young. Ducklings leave the nest by jumping — a dramatic free-fall from the cavity (3–15 meters up) to the forest floor, then walking to the nearest water.

The Head-Throw Display

The male’s courtship display is visually dramatic but acoustically quiet. The head-throw: the male throws his head backward in a rapid arc until the crown touches his back, kicks his feet to propel his breast upward from the water, and at the apex produces a quiet ka-KAA grunt. The entire sequence takes approximately 1.5 seconds. Multiple males display simultaneously to females — a visual spectacle of heads snapping backward, water splashing, bodies rising and falling. E=3 reflects the acoustic reality: the grunts are audible only at close range (10–30 meters). This is a visual-primary, acoustic-secondary display system.

Sixth Consecutive Anatidae (118–123)

Six consecutive Anatidae: Harlequin Duck (118, stream, E=5), Common Merganser (119, underwater pursuit, E=4), Hooded Merganser (120, forest pool, E=4), Wood Duck (121, cavity nesting, E=3), Bufflehead (122, smallest diver, E=3), Barrow’s Goldeneye (123, mountain lake, E=3). The streak approaches the Picidae nonet record (9 consecutive). Degree 124 (Common Goldeneye) would make seven consecutive. The energy has descended through the streak: E=5, E=4, E=4, E=3, E=3, E=3 — settling into the quiet Anatidae acoustic register.
