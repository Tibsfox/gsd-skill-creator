Tactical Silence Then Burst

The Bigg’s orca hunts marine mammals in silence. No discrete calls. No whistles. Minimal clicks. The silence is tactical: marine mammals have excellent underwater hearing; an orca that vocalizes during the approach alerts its prey. The Bigg’s orca solution is restraint — controlled energy, held in.

When prey is located and the approach is complete: the burst. High-speed pursuit (30+ km/h), coordinated group action, surface behaviors, physical contact. The silence breaks at the moment of maximum commitment. The silence was always the preparation for the burst.

E=8 through restraint. The silence phase is not quiet energy — it is maximum energy held back. The burst is maximum energy released. Both are E=8.

Named for Michael Bigg (1939–1990)

Michael Bigg was the Canadian biologist who first used photo-identification to study wild killer whale populations — transforming our understanding of population structure and revealing that “the killer whale” was multiple ecotypes. He died of leukemia in 1990. The transient ecotype was named in his honor: Bigg’s killer whale.

The same scientific revolution Bigg initiated (photo-identification of individuals) became the foundation for Ken Balcomb’s Center for Whale Research (Southern Residents, 1976-present). Both populations documented using the same technique, pioneered by the same scientist.

The same genus. The same geographic waters. Two populations that have not interbred for at least 100,000 years. The dietary and acoustic divergence is maintained by cultural transmission — calves learn what their mothers eat and how their mothers hunt.

The Prey Community

Harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina*): The most common prey in Puget Sound. Hauled on rocks and beaches throughout the region. Acoustically sensitive -- evolved to detect the sound of approaching predators. The Bigg’s orca’s silence is specifically adapted to harbor seal acoustic sensitivity.

Steller sea lions (*Eumetopias jubatus*): Larger prey. Up to 1,100 kg. Require coordinated group tactics and commitment. The group uses persistence and positioning rather than pure speed.

Harbor porpoises (*Phocoena phocoena*): Fast. Small. Produce their own high-frequency echolocation clicks -- which the hunting Bigg’s orca may detect passively. The hunter uses the prey’s acoustic signature to locate it.
