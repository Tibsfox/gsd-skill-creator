## BIGG'S KILLER WHALE -- THE SECOND ECOTYPE

### Michael Bigg and the Discovery of Ecotypes

The Bigg's killer whale is named for Michael Bigg (1939-1990), the Canadian biologist who first developed the scientific understanding of killer whale population structure:

- **Michael Bigg.** Michael Bigg was a marine mammal biologist at Canada's Department of Fisheries and Oceans. In the 1970s, Bigg pioneered the use of photo-identification for studying wild killer whale populations -- the same technique now used by Ken Balcomb's Center for Whale Research for the Southern Residents. Bigg's recognition that killer whales could be individually identified transformed cetacean biology: populations went from "unknowable" to "fully documented" in a generation. Bigg died of leukemia in 1990; the transient killer whale ecotype was named in his honor.
- **Ecotype distinction.** Bigg's work revealed that what was called "the killer whale" was in fact multiple, partially sympatric (geographically overlapping) populations with different diets, different behaviors, different acoustics, and different social structures. The Pacific Northwest has three ecotypes: **Southern Residents** (salmon-eating, documented at degrees 270-271), **Northern Residents** (salmon-eating, different range and dialect), and **Bigg's (transient)** (mammal-eating, documented at degree 272).
- **Genetic distinction.** The Southern Residents and Bigg's orcas have been in the same geographic range for at least 100,000 years without interbreeding. The genetic divergence is substantial. They are maintained as distinct populations by assortative mating (within-ecotype preference) and by the behavioral and dietary differences that make inter-ecotype interaction limited.
- **The naming.** The "transient" name was the original scientific designation, reflecting the more peripatetic ranging pattern of the Bigg's orca compared to the more site-faithful residents. "Bigg's killer whale" is the increasingly preferred name in recognition of Michael Bigg's contribution. The engine uses both terms.

(Sources: Michael Bigg biography; photo-identification history; ecotype distinction; Bigg's vs. Southern Resident genetics; Ford and Ellis 1999; transient vs. resident terminology)

## THE HUNT SEQUENCE -- TACTICAL SILENCE THEN BURST

### The Acoustic Predator's Strategy

The defining acoustic characteristic of the Bigg's killer whale is the tactical use of silence during hunting:

- **Why silence?** The Bigg's orca hunts marine mammals: harbor seals, Steller sea lions, harbor porpoises, Dall's porpoises, and (less commonly) minke whales. Marine mammals have excellent underwater hearing and are highly sensitive to the sounds of potential predators. A Bigg's orca that vocalizes during a mammal hunt alerts its prey to its presence -- a critical tactical disadvantage. The Bigg's orca solution: silence during the approach.
- **The silence phase.** Bigg's orcas traveling in hunting mode are among the quietest large predators in the ocean. They produce no discrete calls, no whistles, minimal echolocation clicks. The silence can extend for long periods as the group moves toward potential prey areas or during the approach phase of a specific hunt.
- **Passive listening.** During the silence phase, the Bigg's orca may be using passive listening -- detecting the sounds produced by their prey (seals hauling out, porpoises producing their own high-frequency clicks). The seal's own acoustic behavior may reveal its location to the hunting orca.
- **The burst.** When prey is located and the approach is complete, the hunt becomes explosive: high-speed pursuit (orcas can exceed 30 km/h in short bursts), coordinated group tactics (individuals cutting off prey escape routes), surface behaviors (breaching, spy-hopping to track prey), and post-capture vocalizations. The burst is acoustic and physical simultaneously -- the silence is broken at the moment of maximum commitment.
- **Echolocation use.** Bigg's orcas do produce echolocation clicks, but at much lower rates than Southern Residents during foraging. The clicks are used more carefully -- sporadic rather than continuous, and apparently reduced in the final approach phase when prey detection is at close enough range that acoustic alerting is a risk.

(Sources: Bigg's orca hunting strategy; acoustic silence predator theory; Baird and Dill 1995; mammal hearing sensitivity; passive listening in predators; orca burst speed; coordinated hunt tactics)

## PREY AND DIET

### Mammal-Eating in the Salish Sea

- **Harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina*).** The most common Bigg's orca prey in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea. Harbor seals haul out on rocks and beaches throughout the region; they are also highly alert and responsive to predator presence. The tactical silence of the Bigg's orca approach is specifically adapted to the harbor seal's acoustic sensitivity.
- **Steller sea lions (*Eumetopias jubatus*).** Larger prey. Steller sea lion attacks by Bigg's orcas require coordinated group tactics and significant physical commitment. Steller sea lions can weigh up to 1,100 kg; Bigg's orca groups attacking them use persistence and coordination rather than pure speed.
- **Harbor porpoises (*Phocoena phocoena*) and Dall's porpoises (*Phocoenoides dalli*).** Fast cetacean prey. Porpoises are among the fastest small cetaceans and produce high-frequency echolocation clicks that the hunting orca could potentially use for passive detection. The Bigg's orca tactics with porpoise include high-speed pursuit and group coordination.
- **Minke whales (*Balaenoptera acutorostrata*).** The largest prey of the Bigg's orca. Attacks on minke whales are observed but are less common than seal predation. The attacks involve persistence and group cooperation over extended periods.
- **Diet selectivity.** Bigg's orcas in the Pacific Northwest do not eat fish. This is a behavioral (not physiological) constraint: they are capable of eating fish but do not. The dietary specialization is culturally maintained -- calves learn to eat what their mothers eat.

(Sources: Bigg's orca diet Puget Sound; harbor seal predation; Steller sea lion attacks; porpoise predation; minke whale attacks; dietary specialization cultural maintenance)

## THE CONTRAST WITH SOUTHERN RESIDENTS

### Two Ecotypes, One Genus

The contrast between the Bigg's orca and the Southern Resident is one of the most dramatic ecotype contrasts in animal biology:

| Dimension | Southern Resident | Bigg's (Transient) |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Diet | Chinook salmon (obligate) | Marine mammals (obligate) |
| Acoustic strategy | Continuously vocal | Silent during hunting |
| Social structure | Tight matrilineal pods (J, K, L) | Smaller, more fluid groups |
| Range | Site-faithful to Salish Sea / PNW coast | More peripatetic |
| Echolocation | Continuous during foraging | Minimal during approach |
| Status | ENDANGERED (~73-75) | Not endangered; recovering |
| Cultural transmission | Pod-specific dialect | Group-specific tactics |
| Genetic divergence | Substantial (100,000+ years) | Substantially diverged |

The table is an argument for the power of cultural specialization in animal evolution: two populations of the same species, in the same waters, have diverged dramatically in diet, behavior, and acoustic strategy through cultural transmission over evolutionary time, without significant genetic exchange.

(Sources: Southern Resident vs. Bigg's ecotype comparison; Ford and Ellis 1999; cultural divergence without genetic exchange; acoustic strategy comparison)

## BIGG'S ORCA IN PUGET SOUND

### The Acoustic Shadow Over the Salish Sea

- **Distribution.** Bigg's orcas range throughout the coastal Pacific from Alaska to California, using Puget Sound, Hood Canal, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the San Juan Islands. Unlike the Southern Residents (who follow Chinook salmon distribution), the Bigg's orca distribution follows pinniped (seal and sea lion) abundance.
- **Population size.** The Pacific coast Bigg's orca population is substantially larger than the Southern Resident population -- estimated at approximately 250-350 individuals across the Northeast Pacific. The Bigg's orca population has been recovering as pinniped populations have increased following the Marine Mammal Protection Act (1972).
- **The acoustic shadow.** Because Bigg's orcas hunt in silence, their presence in Puget Sound is an acoustic shadow: they are there, large predators moving through the water, but the acoustic environment does not register them. When a group of Bigg's orcas passes through an area where harbor seals are hauled out, the first acoustic evidence of their presence may be the seals' alarm calls -- not the orcas' vocalizations.
- **Prey response.** Harbor seals and Steller sea lions have evolved specific behavioral responses to the threat of Bigg's orca predation. Seals hauled on shore flee to land when Bigg's orcas approach; seals in the water dive and remain motionless at depth. The prey community's alarm response is the acoustic signature of a Bigg's orca presence -- not the orca itself.

(Sources: Bigg's orca Pacific coast distribution; Bigg's orca population estimate; Marine Mammal Protection Act 1972; pinniped recovery; acoustic shadow predator ecology; harbor seal anti-predator behavior)

## THE ENERGY OF TACTICAL SILENCE

### E=8 Through Restraint

E=8 for the Bigg's orca hunt sequence is an unusual energy assignment:

- **Silence as maximum energy preparation.** The silence phase of the Bigg's orca hunt is not passive energy -- it is controlled energy, held back. The preparation for maximum exertion is itself a form of maximum energy: the tension of the approach, the coordination of the group without vocalizations, the proximity to prey without revelation. The silence contains the burst.
- **The burst as E=8.** When the hunt sequence breaks from silence to pursuit, the energy release is maximum: 30+ km/h sprint, coordinated group action, breaching, physical contact with prey. The entire acoustic and physical event from silence to kill is E=8.
- **Restraint vs. inhibition.** The Bigg's orca's acoustic silence during hunting is restraint -- a behavioral choice. It is not acoustic incapacity. The same individual that hunts in silence will vocalize extensively after a successful kill, during socializing, during resting. The silence is purposeful.

(Sources: Bigg's orca acoustic behavior comparison between hunting and non-hunting; post-kill vocalizations; restraint as behavioral strategy; E=8 energy assignment rationale)
