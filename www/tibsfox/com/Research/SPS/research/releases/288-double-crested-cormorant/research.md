## SECOND PHALACROCORACIDAE: THE FAMILY CHAPTER

### Two Cormorants, Two Ecological Niches

Degree 288 is the second Phalacrocoracidae entry following Brandt's at 287:

- **The contrast.** Brandt's Cormorant (287) and Double-crested Cormorant (288) represent two different Pacific Northwest cormorant ecological strategies. Brandt's is the outer-coast specialist: strictly saltwater, colonial, dense nesting on exposed offshore rocks, prey specialist (schooling fish), highly sensitive to oceanographic conditions. Double-crested is the generalist: freshwater and saltwater, coastal and inland, less dense colonial nesting, broad prey base, widespread across North America. The two entries document the range of the cormorant family's Pacific Northwest expression.
- **The Puget Sound cormorant.** Double-crested Cormorant is the cormorant of Puget Sound. From the Seattle waterfront, from Puget Sound ferry decks, from Eastside bridges -- the cormorant you are most likely to see perching with wings spread on a piling, dock, or buoy is the Double-crested. Brandt's Cormorant is a bird of the outer coast, rarely encountered in inner Sound waters. Double-crested is the urban waterfront cormorant.
- **The wettable plumage posture.** Both cormorant species exhibit the characteristic wing-spreading posture on perches -- standing upright with wings extended to dry after diving. This posture is encountered throughout Puget Sound on any structure that cormorants use as a perch. It is one of Seattle's most common wildlife sightings.

(Sources: Brandt's vs. Double-crested comparison; Double-crested habitat generalism; Puget Sound as primary DCCO habitat; urban waterfront observation; wing-spreading behavior)

## NANNOPTERUM AURITUM: THE DOUBLE-CRESTED CORMORANT

### Taxonomy and Biology

- **Scientific name.** *Nannopterum auritum* (formerly *Phalacrocorax auritus*). Genus *Nannopterum* = "small wing" (Greek: *nannos* = small, *pteron* = wing) -- a reference to the proportionally small wings relative to body size that characterize this and related cormorant genera. *auritum* = "eared" (Latin: *auritum* = eared, from *auris* = ear) -- referring to the double crest, tufts of curled feathers that appear above each eye during the breeding season, resembling ear tufts.
- **The double crest.** The species' common name derives from the pair of tufts of curled feathers that appear on either side of the crown during the breeding season. In Eastern birds the tufts tend to be white; in Western birds (including the Pacific Northwest population) they tend to be black. The tufts are present only during the breeding season and are lost during the post-breeding molt. Outside breeding season, the Double-crested Cormorant is a uniformly dark bird without obvious ornamental features.
- **Size.** 28-35 inches (70-90 cm) length; wingspan 45-48 inches (114-123 cm). Weight approximately 2.5-4 lbs (1.1-1.8 kg). Smaller than Brandt's Cormorant and substantially smaller than the Great Cormorant of the Atlantic coast.
- **Appearance.** Black overall with greenish iridescence; orange-yellow gular pouch (less bright than Brandt's blue pouch); green eyes; hooked bill. The orange-yellow gular pouch distinguishes Double-crested from Brandt's (blue) and from Pelagic Cormorant (no colored gular in field conditions).
- **The four Pacific Northwest subspecies.** North American Double-crested Cormorant comprises multiple subspecies. The Pacific Northwest population is *Nannopterum auritum albociliatum* -- the subspecies with black (not white) head tufts.

(Sources: Nannopterum auritum taxonomy; nannopterum etymology; auritum etymology; double crest; Eastern white vs. Western black tufts; size 28-35 inches; orange-yellow gular pouch; subspecies albociliatum)

## THE VOCALIZATION: GUTTURAL DISPLAY CALLS

### Pig-Grunts at the Nest Site

Double-crested Cormorant vocalizations are consistently described as among the least melodic of any waterbird:

- **The call.** The Double-crested Cormorant produces a series of low, guttural grunts and croaks that are variously described as pig-like, frog-like, or simply "harsh, guttural croaking." The calls are produced at the nest site in behavioral contexts including territorial defense, mate display, and chick recognition. The quality is consistently rough and unmusical.
- **Display calls.** During courtship, males produce display vocalizations associated with gular pouch display and body posturing. The male points bill upward, spreads wings partially, and produces short, rough grunts while showing the orange-yellow gular pouch. The calls are not loud individually; they contribute to colony noise through repetition and overlap.
- **Colony sound vs. individual sound.** Double-crested Cormorant colonies are typically smaller and less dense than Brandt's colonies -- breeding pairs number in the dozens to hundreds rather than the thousands. The colony acoustic environment is therefore less overwhelming than a large Brandt's colony. The sound is present and identifiable but does not produce the same roaring mass effect.
- **E=6 calibration.** E=6: less than Brandt's Cormorant colony (E=7) for several reasons: smaller colony sizes, more dispersed nesting, less extreme colonial density, and a lower individual call intensity. The Double-crested's call is harsh and distinctive but does not produce the mass acoustic effect of a 1,000+ Brandt's colony. E=6 is correct.
- **Puget Sound acoustic presence.** In Puget Sound, Double-crested Cormorant is present year-round and frequently encountered. The calls are audible from watercraft and from shore at locations where the birds roost or nest. The sound is part of the Puget Sound acoustic landscape in a way that Brandt's (outer coast only) is not.

(Sources: Double-crested Cormorant vocalization description; pig-grunt quality; display call context; gular pouch display; colony size comparison with Brandt's; E=6 calibration; Puget Sound acoustic presence)

## ECOLOGY -- THE GENERALIST CORMORANT

### From Puget Sound to Mountain Lakes

Double-crested Cormorant's habitat breadth is its defining character:

- **Saltwater habitats.** Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, coastal bays, estuaries, and river mouths. Any body of saltwater with fish and a suitable perch site is potential Double-crested habitat. In Puget Sound specifically, the birds roost on piers, pilings, navigation markers, buoys, bridges, and any other elevated structure adjacent to water. The characteristic wing-spreading posture is encountered throughout the Sound.
- **Freshwater habitats.** Unlike Brandt's Cormorant, Double-crested readily uses freshwater: rivers, reservoirs, lakes, and ponds. This makes the species the most geographically widespread cormorant in North America, extending from the Pacific coast inland to the Rockies and across the continent to the Atlantic coast.
- **Prey generalism.** Double-crested Cormorant eats a wide variety of fish -- whatever is available and can be caught by pursuit diving. In Puget Sound: Pacific herring, smelt, perch, sculpin, and other demersal and schooling species. In freshwater: trout, salmon (primarily juveniles or injured fish), perch, sunfish, and other species. The prey generalism is what allows the habitat generalism.
- **The salmon conflict.** Double-crested Cormorant's consumption of juvenile salmon has created significant management conflicts in the Pacific Northwest. At Columbia River fish passage sites, cormorants have concentrated in large numbers, preying on juvenile salmon smolts migrating to sea. The annual mortality caused by cormorants at some Columbia River sites has been sufficient to affect endangered salmon run recovery. Management responses have included hazing, nest removal, egg oiling, and in some cases lethal control. This is an ongoing management challenge.
- **The urban interface.** Double-crested Cormorant has adapted to urban environments. In Seattle, the birds are regularly observed on pier structures, freeway bridges over water, navigation markers in Elliott Bay, and other human infrastructure. This is the urban wildlife interface for cormorants in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Double-crested habitat: Puget Sound, coastal, freshwater; wing-spreading on urban infrastructure; prey generalism; Columbia River salmon conflict; juvenile salmon predation; lethal control authorization; urban adaptation Seattle)

## THE SALMON CONFLICT

### A Recovered Predator and an Endangered Prey

The Double-crested Cormorant's recovery under MMPA protection has created a management conflict that parallels the California Sea Lion/steelhead story at Ballard Locks (SPS 284):

- **The recovery.** Double-crested Cormorant populations were suppressed during the DDT era -- the pesticide thinned cormorant eggshells and reduced breeding success. Following DDT restrictions (US ban in 1972) and MMPA protection, cormorant populations recovered substantially. The Pacific Northwest breeding population has increased significantly since the 1970s.
- **The Columbia River system.** East Sand Island in the Columbia River estuary became the largest Double-crested Cormorant colony in western North America during the 1990s-2000s, with up to 13,000 breeding pairs. The colony's proximity to juvenile salmon migration routes -- Columbia River ESA-listed salmon and steelhead smolts pass East Sand Island en route to the ocean -- created documented conflict. Estimated annual consumption of millions of juvenile salmon.
- **The management response.** US Army Corps of Engineers, USDA Wildlife Services, and partner agencies implemented an integrated management program including nest hazing, nest removal, egg oiling, and lethal control (shooting of adults). The program resulted in colony reduction. Colony management at East Sand Island is the most intensive cormorant management program in Pacific Northwest history.
- **The ESA/MMPA tension.** As at Ballard Locks, the conflict is between a recovered protected species and endangered species predated upon. Both are protected by federal law; management requires navigating the interaction between MMPA (cormorant protection) and ESA (salmon protection). The legal and biological complexity of this interaction is a defining feature of Pacific Northwest wildlife management.

(Sources: DDT era suppression; MMPA recovery; East Sand Island colony; 13,000 breeding pairs peak; ESA-listed salmon predation; Army Corps of Engineers management; egg oiling; lethal control; ESA/MMPA tension parallel to Ballard Locks SPS 284)

## PHALACROCORACIDAE CHAPTER -- TWO DEGREES COMPLETE

### The Cormorant Family at 287-288

The two Phalacrocoracidae entries document the full range of Pacific Northwest cormorant ecology:

- **287 Brandt's Cormorant.** *Urile penicillatus*. E=7. Summer outer coast. Dense colonial nesting on offshore exposed rocks. Bright blue gular pouch. Schooling fish specialist. Thousands of breeding pairs per colony. Highly sensitive to oceanographic conditions. First Phalacrocoracidae.
- **288 Double-crested Cormorant.** *Nannopterum auritum*. E=6. Year-round. Rocky shore, urban waterfront, Puget Sound, freshwater. Orange-yellow gular pouch. Prey generalist. Smaller colony sizes. North America's most widespread cormorant. Second Phalacrocoracidae.
- **The third species.** Pelagic Cormorant (*Urile pelagicus*) -- the smallest Pacific Northwest cormorant, a cliff-nesting species on steep rocky sites -- will enter the engine in future degrees. The three-species set documents the full range of Pacific Northwest cormorant ecology.
