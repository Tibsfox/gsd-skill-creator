Second and Final Phalacrocoracidae — Block 285–288 Closes

Nannopterum auritum — the Double-crested Cormorant — is the second and final Phalacrocoracidae in the block, following Brandt's (287). The inland adaptable cormorant: freshwater and saltwater, coastal and urban, the bird on the pier in Elliott Bay and on the navigation buoy in the Sound. North America's most widespread cormorant.

The SPS 285–288 block closes here. The S36 285–288 block also closes here. Four degrees complete: Phocidae (285), Hydrobatidae (286), Phalacrocoracidae x2 (287–288). Three new families in four degrees.

Acoustic Profile — Guttural Display Call

Call type: Low, guttural grunts and croaks. Pig-grunt or frog-like quality. Produced at nest site during territorial defense, mate display, and chick recognition. Individual calls are not loud; colony-level sound is less overwhelming than Brandt's (smaller colony sizes, more dispersed).

E=6: Smaller colony sizes (dozens to hundreds, not thousands) + lower individual call amplitude than Brandt's (E=7) = E=6. Orange-yellow gular pouch display accompanies calls. Urban Puget Sound presence means the call is encountered more frequently by Seattle residents than Brandt's outer coast call.

The Puget Sound Cormorant

Double-crested Cormorant is the cormorant of Puget Sound. From the Seattle waterfront, from ferry decks, from any bridge over water — the dark bird perched with wings spread on a piling or navigation marker is almost certainly a Double-crested. This is the bird that drapes itself on human infrastructure throughout the Sound year-round: pier structures in Elliott Bay, piling rows at marinas, navigation buoys, mooring floats. The characteristic wing-spreading posture (drying partially-wettable plumage after diving) is a fixed element of the Puget Sound visual landscape.

Brandt's Cormorant is rarely encountered in inner Puget Sound waters; it is a bird of the outer coast. Double-crested is the cormorant that defines the urban waterfront experience in Seattle.

The Columbia River Salmon Conflict

East Sand Island in the Columbia River estuary became the largest Double-crested Cormorant colony in western North America during the 1990s–2000s — up to 13,000 breeding pairs — at a location directly in the migration path of ESA-listed juvenile salmon and steelhead smolts. Estimated annual cormorant predation on Columbia River juvenile salmonids reached into the millions. The conflict echoes the Ballard Locks / California Sea Lion story (SPS 284): a recovered protected species (MMPA) preying on endangered species (ESA). The Army Corps of Engineers implemented an integrated management program including hazing, egg oiling, nest removal, and lethal control. The ESA/MMPA tension is a defining feature of Pacific Northwest wildlife management.
