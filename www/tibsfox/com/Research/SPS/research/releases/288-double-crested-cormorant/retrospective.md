## What Worked

1. **Closing Phalacrocoracidae with the inland/urban generalist after opening with the offshore specialist provides the correct ecological contrast.** Brandt's (outer coast, thousands of pairs, blue pouch, El Niño sensitive) vs. Double-crested (urban Puget Sound, smaller colonies, orange pouch, prey generalist) documents the range of the Pacific Northwest cormorant family with two consecutive entries.
2. **The Columbia River East Sand Island conflict is the Pacific Northwest's second major ESA/MMPA tension.** The engine has now documented both: Ballard Locks (sea lion/steelhead, SPS 284) and East Sand Island (cormorant/salmon, SPS 288). Both involve the same structural conflict: recovered protected species predating endangered species. The engine builds a pattern.
3. **The Puget Sound urban waterfront framing is correct.** Double-crested Cormorant is the most frequently observed cormorant from Seattle's urban locations. The wing-spreading posture on pilings is what most Seattle residents know as "cormorant." Documenting this correctly grounds the entry in lived experience.
4. **The DDT connection (eggshell thinning, MMPA recovery) provides historical depth.** DCCO is one of the species most directly documented as impacted by DDT. The recovery since the 1972 DDT ban is measurable and direct.

## Discoveries

1. **The genus change from Phalacrocorax to Nannopterum reflects the same molecular work that moved Brandt's to Urile.** Both PNW cormorant species have been moved out of the large, paraphyletic Phalacrocorax genus. The engine documents current taxonomy.
2. **East Sand Island as the largest western North American DCCO colony is a striking fact.** 13,000 breeding pairs at a single river-mouth island, positioned at the critical juvenile salmon migration bottleneck. The management history here is extensive and contested.
