## ZALOPHUS CALIFORNIANUS: THE FAMILIAR SEA LION

### The "Sea Lion" of Popular Culture

*Zalophus californianus* is the sea lion most people know:

- **The cultural identity.** California Sea Lions are the animals that perform in marine parks and aquariums, the animals depicted in cartoons as balancing balls on their noses, the animals that tourists photograph on Fisherman's Wharf in San Francisco or the docks of Newport, Oregon. The California Sea Lion is the public face of "sea lion" in North American culture. The bark is the sound people mean when they say "sea lion sounds."
- **The training history.** California Sea Lions are among the most trainable marine mammals, with documented capacity for learned behaviors, object permanence, and artificial language comprehension (distinguishing symbols and responding to logical relationships). The US Navy Marine Mammal Program has used California Sea Lions for underwater object location and recovery tasks. The species' cognitive capabilities are genuine.
- **Sexual dimorphism.** Adult males: 6.5-8 feet (2.0-2.4 m), 600-900 lbs (270-410 kg). Distinct sagittal crest (raised forehead bump) that becomes more pronounced with age; adult males are darker than females and have the crest. Females: 4-5.5 feet (1.2-1.7 m), 110-240 lbs (50-110 kg). The size difference between males and females is substantial but less extreme than in the Steller Sea Lion.
- **Coloration.** Tan to dark brown; males typically darker. When wet, darker. The coloration is consistent with the rocky and wooden haul-out surfaces they use: they are camouflaged in the industrial and rocky waterfront.
- **The sagittal crest.** The prominent raised bump on the forehead of adult males is the sagittal crest -- a raised bony ridge on the skull where jaw muscles attach. As males mature, the crest becomes more pronounced and the fur on the crest often lightens. This is how you distinguish an adult male California Sea Lion from a female or subadult.

(Sources: Zalophus californianus species biology; cultural identity; marine park history; US Navy Marine Mammal Program; sexual dimorphism; sagittal crest; coloration)

## TAXONOMY AND NOMENCLATURE

### Zalophus californianus

- **Scientific name:** *Zalophus californianus* (Lesson, 1828). *Zalophus* = "high crest" (Greek: *za* = intensive prefix, *lophos* = crest) -- referring to the prominent sagittal crest of adult males. *californianus* = "of California" -- the species was described from specimens collected along the California coast.
- **René Primevère Lesson (1794-1849).** The species was described by French naturalist René Lesson, who participated in the circumnavigation voyage of the Duperrey expedition (1822-1825). The description was published in 1828.
- **Family Otariidae.** Second Otariidae entry following Steller Sea Lion (degree 283). Same family; different genus, different acoustic and behavioral profile.
- **Genus Zalophus.** Three species have historically been recognized in the genus: *Z. californianus* (California Sea Lion), *Z. wollebaeki* (Galápagos Sea Lion), and the now-extinct *Z. japonicus* (Japanese Sea Lion, extinct mid-20th century). The Japanese Sea Lion represents a significant recent extinction in the North Pacific pinniped record.

(Sources: Zalophus californianus taxonomy; René Lesson 1828 description; Duperrey expedition; genus Zalophus; Z. wollebaeki Galápagos; Z. japonicus extinct)

## THE BARK: ACOUSTIC ANALYSIS

### The Classic Sea Lion Sound

The California Sea Lion bark is one of the most recognizable animal vocalizations in North American popular culture:

- **The bark.** The California Sea Lion bark is a short, loud, percussive vocalization -- the classic "arf" sound. It is produced rapidly, often in sequences: "arf-arf-arf-arf-arf." The bark is a contact and territorial call, used in social contexts at haul-out sites.
- **Frequency.** California Sea Lion barks are concentrated in the range of approximately 200-8,000 Hz, with the most energy in the 500-3,000 Hz range -- well within the human hearing range. Unlike the Steller Sea Lion's deep, low-frequency roar (100-1,000 Hz), the California Sea Lion's bark is higher-pitched and more percussive. It is easily audible to human observers.
- **The dock chorus.** At an active California Sea Lion haul-out site on a Puget Sound dock, multiple animals bark simultaneously. The chorus is overlapping: different animals at different rates, different pitches (males are lower, females and juveniles higher), different rhythms. The overall effect is a cacophony of overlapping "arf" sounds that fills the waterfront acoustic environment. From Elliott Bay on a winter morning, you can hear active haul-out sites from considerable distance.
- **Male vocalizations.** Adult male California Sea Lions also produce a longer, lower-pitched bark and a "trumpeting" vocalization associated with territorial display. The full male vocalization repertoire includes the familiar bark, the trumpet call, and various threat vocalizations. The female vocalization is higher-pitched and primarily functions as a contact call for pup recognition.
- **Mother-pup recognition.** Like the Steller Sea Lion, California Sea Lions use individual vocal recognition to maintain mother-pup bonds at crowded haul-out sites. The mother produces a stereotyped pup-attraction call; the pup responds to its mother's call specifically. The vocal recognition system operates within the acoustic chaos of an active dock chorus.
- **E=7 calibration.** The dock chorus at E=7: less than the Steller Sea Lion bull roar at E=8 (larger animal, lower frequency, sustained roar vs. percussive bark), more than the Dall's Porpoise (E=6, largely human-inaudible). The dock chorus is loud, overlapping, and constant during active haul-out periods, but the individual bark is shorter and less massive than the sustained Steller roar. E=7 is correct.

(Sources: California Sea Lion bark frequency 200-8,000 Hz; energy 500-3,000 Hz; dock chorus acoustic profile; male trumpet call; mother-pup vocal recognition; E=7 calibration vs. Steller E=8)

## CONSERVATION STATUS AND POPULATION

### A Protected and Recovering Population

- **IUCN status:** Least Concern. The California Sea Lion is among the most abundant and successful pinniped species in North America.
- **Population.** The California Sea Lion has experienced significant population growth since the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) was enacted in 1972. Current population estimates for the eastern stock (US Pacific coast, including Puget Sound wintering animals) are approximately 300,000-350,000 individuals. The population has grown substantially since protection.
- **Winter range extension.** California Sea Lions are observed in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea primarily during the winter months (October-April). The animals follow prey fish (primarily Pacific herring, Pacific smelt/eulachon, and salmon) northward into Pacific Northwest waters. Historically, Puget Sound California Sea Lion sightings increased significantly in the late 20th century, associated with the population's growth following MMPA protection.
- **The Ballard Locks controversy.** One of the most documented California Sea Lion conflicts in the Pacific Northwest is the Ballard Locks (Hiram M. Chittenden Locks, Seattle). California Sea Lions learned to prey on winter-run steelhead that congregate at the base of the fish ladder before ascending. The predation, by a small number of individuals, was documented as causing significant mortality to the endangered Puget Sound steelhead population. Management responses included hazing, translocation, and eventually lethal removal under MMPA authority. The Ballard Locks conflict is one of the defining marine mammal management cases in Pacific Northwest history.
- **ESA conflict.** The Ballard Locks case exemplifies a recurring tension: a recovering protected species (California Sea Lion) preying on an endangered species (Puget Sound steelhead). Both are protected under federal law; the management response required legal authority for lethal removal of individual sea lions.

(Sources: California Sea Lion IUCN Least Concern; MMPA 1972; population 300,000-350,000 eastern stock; winter range in Puget Sound; Ballard Locks/Chittenden Locks conflict; steelhead predation; MMPA lethal removal authority; ESA conflict)

## THE URBAN WATERFRONT HAUL-OUT

### Sea Lions in the City

The California Sea Lion is the Pacific Northwest's most urban marine mammal:

- **Haul-out sites.** California Sea Lions haul out on docks, piers, floating docks, navigation buoys, and any available structure at the waterfront. Unlike the Steller Sea Lion (which prefers rocky offshore islets for haul-out), the California Sea Lion readily uses artificial structures. Elliott Bay Marina, the working waterfront of the Port of Seattle, Shilshole Bay, various locations in the Salish Sea -- California Sea Lions occupy the human infrastructure of the waterfront without apparent concern.
- **The dock as habitat.** The floating dock is a specific habitat type that California Sea Lions have adapted to in Puget Sound: stable platforms above the water, available year-round, adjacent to productive fishing grounds. The management of sea lion use of working waterfront infrastructure is an ongoing challenge for port operators and marina managers.
- **Human encounters.** California Sea Lions at urban haul-out sites are one of the most common wildlife encounters available in Puget Sound. Visitors to the Seattle waterfront can observe and hear the animals at relatively close range. The dock chorus is a regular feature of the waterfront soundscape in winter.
- **Prey availability.** California Sea Lions winter in Puget Sound because herring, smelt, and salmon are present. The animals follow the prey; the prey concentrate in productive Puget Sound waters in winter. The sea lion's presence in the Sound is an indicator of the herring and smelt populations that support them.

(Sources: California Sea Lion haul-out on artificial structures; Elliott Bay Marina; Shilshole Bay; Port of Seattle waterfront; dock habitat adaptation; human encounter frequency; winter prey availability)

## THE STELLER-CALIFORNIA COMPARISON: TWO OTARIIDAE DEGREES

### The Otariidae Chapter at 283-284

The two Otariidae entries (degrees 283-284) document the full range of the Pacific Northwest eared seal experience:

- **283 Steller Sea Lion.** Eumetopias jubatus. E=8. Summer. Remote rocky rookery. Bull of up to 2,500 lbs producing a sustained low-frequency roar (100-1,000 Hz). Endangered western stock. Named for an 18th-century naturalist who died at 37. The largest, the loudest, the most endangered, the most remote.
- **284 California Sea Lion.** Zalophus californianus. E=7. Winter. Urban dock haul-out. Animals of up to 900 lbs (males) barking in overlapping chorus (200-8,000 Hz). Least Concern; recovering population. Named for the California coast. The most familiar, the most urban, the most culturally known, the most accessible.
- **The contrast.** The two Otariidae entries are almost complete opposites in their ecological context: remote vs. urban, summer vs. winter, Endangered vs. Least Concern, low-frequency roar vs. high-frequency bark, E=8 vs. E=7. The family's range within the Pacific Northwest is documented by this contrast.
- **The shared character.** Both are eared seals: external ear pinnae visible, rear flippers rotatable forward for terrestrial locomotion, front flipper-based swimming. The family characters are consistent; the ecological niches are different.
