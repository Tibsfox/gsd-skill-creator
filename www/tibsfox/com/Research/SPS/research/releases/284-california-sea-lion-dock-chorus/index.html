Second Otariidae — The Urban Marine Mammal

Zalophus californianus — the California Sea Lion — enters at degree 284. The second and final Otariidae entry, following the Steller Sea Lion at degree 283. The most urban, most culturally familiar marine mammal in the Pacific Northwest. Docks, piers, buoys, marina infrastructure in Elliott Bay and Puget Sound. The animals that haul out on the waterfront in winter.

The bark: the classic "arf arf" sea lion sound. Overlapping dock chorus — multiple animals simultaneously, different pitches, different rates, the cacophony of an active haul-out. E=7. Described by René Lesson, 1828.

Acoustic Profile — The Dock Chorus

Frequency: 200–8,000 Hz; energy concentrated 500–3,000 Hz. Well within human hearing range. Higher-pitched and more percussive than Steller Sea Lion roar (100–1,000 Hz).

The chorus: Multiple simultaneous barks at a haul-out; different animals at different rates and pitches (males lower, females and juveniles higher); overlapping continuous sound. From Elliott Bay in winter, active haul-out sites are audible at considerable distance. E=7: constant overlapping cacophony, not sustained single-animal maximum.

The Urban Waterfront

California Sea Lions haul out on whatever structure is available and useful: docks, floating platforms, navigation buoys, marina slips. They readily use human infrastructure, unlike the Steller Sea Lion's preference for remote rocky sites. Elliott Bay Marina, Shilshole Bay, the Port of Seattle waterfront — California Sea Lions are regulars in winter. The dock chorus is a feature of the Puget Sound waterfront soundscape from October through April.

The Ballard Locks conflict: California Sea Lions learned to prey on winter-run steelhead at the base of the Hiram M. Chittenden Locks fish ladder. A recovering protected species preying on an endangered one. One of the Pacific Northwest's defining marine mammal management cases. The MMPA eventually authorized lethal removal of individual predating animals.

Conservation

IUCN: Least Concern. Population ~300,000-350,000 eastern stock, significantly increased since MMPA protection (1972). The recovery from pre-MMPA hunting pressure is one of the marine mammal management success stories of the late 20th century. Current principal conflicts: ESA-listed fish species predation (steelhead, salmon); dock infrastructure use; vessel interactions.
