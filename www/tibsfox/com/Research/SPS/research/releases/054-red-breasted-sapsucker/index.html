CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Clouded Salamander (Aneides ferreus) at degree 54. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Red-breasted Sapsucker replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s old-growth forest habitat. Both species are intimately associated with old-growth forest structure and are year-round residents. This is the tenth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (Pacific Chorus Frog at 45, Columbia Spotted Frog at 46, Red-legged Frog at 47, Tailed Frog at 48, Western Toad at 49, Rough-skinned Newt at 50, Northwestern Salamander at 51, Long-toed Salamander at 52, Olympic Torrent Salamander at 53, Clouded Salamander at 54) — the engine reaches into the lungless salamander zone of the original CSV’s amphibian belt.

PICIDAE RETURNS — After 32 Degrees, the Woodpeckers Are Back

The Red-breasted Sapsucker brings Picidae back to the engine after a 32-degree absence (last: degree 22, Northern Flicker). This is the 3rd Picidae overall (21, 22, 54) and introduces a new genus: Sphyrapicus (“hammer woodpecker”) vs. Colaptes (“chisel”) for the flickers. Same family, entirely different ecological strategy: the flicker is a ground-foraging ant-eater that drums on dead wood. The sapsucker is a sap-extraction specialist that drills living trees. PASSERIFORMES STREAK BROKEN at 16 — first Piciformes since degree 22, first non-Passeriformes since degree 37.

The 55th Acoustic Element: The Sap-Well Rhythm

An irregular, stuttering drumming pattern that starts fast, slows, and accelerates again — combined with a mewing, cat-like call that sounds nothing like a woodpecker. Named “the sap-well rhythm” for three resonances: (1) the physical rhythm — the irregular drumming that violates the metronomic expectation set by other woodpeckers; (2) the ecological rhythm — the patient cycle of drilling, leaving, returning, maintaining wells; (3) the community rhythm — the sap wells pulse with life on the sapsucker’s schedule, feeding dozens of species.

The sap-well rhythm does not command the forest (corvid alarm, degree 53) or compete with the river (torrent-song, degree 52). It disrupts expectation through arrhythmia. The jazz drummer who refuses to play on the beat.

Keystone Ecosystem Engineer: Sap Wells as Shared Infrastructure

The Red-breasted Sapsucker drills neat rows of tiny holes in living tree bark, tapping phloem sap (sugar-rich) and xylem sap (mineral-rich). The wells are maintained over time — the bird returns periodically to enlarge them and keep sap flowing. These sap wells are a keystone resource: Rufous Hummingbirds (degree 35) nest near sap well trees and follow sapsuckers on maintenance rounds. Anna’s Hummingbirds (degree 36) visit. Warblers drink sap and catch attracted insects. Squirrels lick sap. Bees, wasps, flies, and beetles aggregate at the wells. Abandoned nest cavities house Mountain Bluebirds, Northern Flying Squirrels, and other secondary cavity nesters. Dozens of species depend on infrastructure the sapsucker creates.

The Hidden Thread: Rufous Hummingbird Dependency

The Rufous Hummingbird (degree 35, “the thrum”) and the Red-breasted Sapsucker (degree 54, “the sap-well rhythm”) are separated by 19 degrees but bound by one of the tightest ecological associations in Pacific Northwest forests. Rufous Hummingbirds arrive in early spring before most flowers bloom — sap wells provide a critical sugar source. They nest near active sap well trees, follow sapsuckers on maintenance rounds, and defend wells as territory. The connection was implicit at degree 35. It becomes explicit at degree 54. The sapsucker creates the resource; the hummingbird exploits it.

The Red Hammer-Woodpecker

Sphyrapicus ruber — from Ancient Greek sphyra (“hammer”) + Latin picus (“woodpecker”) + ruber (Latin “red”). Full translation: “Red hammer-woodpecker.” The genus Sphyrapicus contains four North American species: Red-breasted, Red-naped, Yellow-bellied, and Williamson’s Sapsuckers. Formerly lumped with Yellow-bellied and Red-naped as one species; split in 1983. Two subspecies: S. r. ruber (northern, darker — PNW populations) and S. r. daggetti (southern, paler). The PNW birds are the deepest-red form.

The Sound: A Cat in the Forest

Mewing call: A nasal, descending mew or meow — nothing like a typical woodpecker call. Frequency ~1.5–4.0 kHz, peak energy 2.0–3.5 kHz. Given as single notes or loosely spaced series. Plaintive, questioning, cat-like. If you hear a kitten mewing overhead in a PNW forest, look up — it may be a sapsucker.

Irregular drumming: Starts fast (~10–15 strikes/sec), slows (~5–8 strikes/sec), then accelerates again (~10–15 strikes/sec). This fast-slow-fast pattern is unique among PNW woodpeckers and immediately species-diagnostic. Duration ~1–3 seconds per bout. The rhythm the engine has been waiting for — Picidae percussion that refuses to keep strict time.

The Most Colorful Woodpecker in PNW Forests

Entirely red head and upper breast — no other PNW woodpecker has this. Deep, rich crimson covering crown, nape, throat, and upper breast in an unbroken field. Yellow belly below the red. Black-and-white barred back with white “braces” patches. Three-zone color pattern (red/black-white/yellow) is unique and instantly diagnostic. Length 20–22 cm. Weight 53–63 g. Straight, chisel-like bill. Where most woodpeckers wear red as an accent, the sapsucker wears red as its primary identity.

Habitat: Old-Growth and Riparian Alder

Breeds in mixed conifer and riparian forest: Douglas fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, western red cedar, grand fir, ponderosa pine, red alder, bigleaf maple. Strong preference for old-growth and mature forest with large-diameter trees and standing dead snags for nesting. Particularly fond of riparian alder stands — alder bark is soft, easy to drill, and sap flows readily. Year-round resident in western Washington with some altitudinal migration downslope in winter.

Nesting: Primary Cavity Creator

Excavates own nest cavity in dead or dying trees (snags). Both sexes excavate over 2–3 weeks. Entrance ~3.5 cm diameter. Cavity ~20–30 cm deep. 4–7 white eggs, 12–14 day incubation (both sexes, male at night), 25–29 day nestling period. Abandoned cavities used by Mountain Bluebirds, Northern Flying Squirrels, small owls, chickadees — the sapsucker’s engineering extends from food infrastructure (sap wells) to shelter infrastructure (nest cavities).

Twenty-one families across 55 entries. No new family — Picidae was already in the engine. But the family’s return after 32 degrees with a new genus (Sphyrapicus) is a significant structural event. Picidae rises from 2 to 3 members, tying with Trochilidae and Paridae. Passeriformes streak broken at 16. 47 of 55 entries (85.5%) are Passeriformes — the order dominance continues, but the streak is done.

Theory Nodes

R

The Sap-Well Rhythm foundational

The 55th acoustic element. Irregular drumming (fast-slow-fast) + mewing cat-like call. First arrhythmic percussion element — violates metronomic expectation. Physical rhythm, ecological rhythm, community rhythm. Music-theory parallel: rubato — the performer deliberately varies the tempo, speeding up and slowing down against the pulse.

P

Picidae Returns After 32 Degrees foundational

3rd Picidae overall (21, 22, 54). New genus Sphyrapicus vs. Colaptes. Different ecological strategy: sap extraction vs. ground foraging. Passeriformes streak broken. The percussion section expands from timpani and snare to include the brush kit. Music-theory parallel: a drummer switching from sticks to brushes.

K

Keystone Ecosystem Engineering foundational

Sap wells as shared infrastructure for dozens of species. Hummingbirds, warblers, squirrels, insects, cavity nesters all depend on sapsucker-created resources. First species whose primary ecological role is physical creation of shared infrastructure. Music-theory parallel: the ostinato bass line — the foundation that every other instrument builds upon.

H

The Rufous Hummingbird Connection foundational

Degree 35 and degree 54 linked by sap well dependency. 19 degrees apart, one of the tightest ecological associations in PNW forests. Hummingbirds nest near sap well trees, follow sapsuckers, defend wells. Implicit at degree 35, explicit at degree 54. Music-theory parallel: the callback — a passage that recontextualizes an earlier theme.

C

The Most Colorful PNW Woodpecker intermediate

Entirely red head and breast — no other PNW woodpecker has this. Three-zone color pattern: red head, black-and-white back, yellow belly. Red is pigmentary (carotenoid), not structural. Formerly lumped with Yellow-bellied and Red-naped until 1983 split. Music-theory parallel: the key signature — a bold declaration at the top of the score.

D

Irregular Drumming as Species Identity intermediate

Fast-slow-fast drumming pattern is species-diagnostic. Immediately separates sapsucker from all other PNW woodpeckers. The engine now has three Picidae rhythms: power (21), groove (22), rubato (54). Music-theory parallel: syncopation — accents falling where you do not expect them.
