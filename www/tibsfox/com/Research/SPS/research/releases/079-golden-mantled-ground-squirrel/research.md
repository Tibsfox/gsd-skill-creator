## THE GOLDEN-MANTLED GROUND SQUIRREL: THE FOREST EDGE DWELLER

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is a medium-small ground squirrel of the **forest edge** -- rocky clearings, open coniferous forest, and montane meadow margins across western North America. At 170-300 grams (6-10 oz), it is similar in size to the Townsend's Ground Squirrel (78) but inhabits a completely different habitat: the forest-meadow interface rather than open grassland [1, 2, 3].

This is the engine's **FIFTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79) -- the **Sciuridae quintet**, which **matches the Mustelidae quintet** (63-67) and the **Swallow quintet** (26-30) as the longest family run in the engine. **THREE-WAY TIE**. From marmots (75-77) to ground squirrels (78-79) -- same family, three genera (*Marmota*, *Urocitellus*, *Callospermophilus*), five habitats.

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is the mammal most commonly **confused with chipmunks** in the PNW. Both have lateral body stripes. The key distinction: **no facial stripes**. Chipmunks have stripes running through their eyes and across their cheeks. The Golden-mantled has a clean, unstriped face with a distinctive golden-brown "mantle" across the head and shoulders.

## TAXONOMY: *Callospermophilus lateralis*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Callospermophilus** -- from Greek *kalos* ("beautiful") + *spermophilus* ("seed-lover," from *sperma* "seed" + *philos* "loving"). The genus was split from the broader *Spermophilus* in 2009 based on molecular phylogenetics. *Callospermophilus* = "beautiful seed-lover" [1, 2].

- **lateralis** -- from Latin "of the side," referring to the prominent lateral body stripes. Described by Say (1823) [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (standard). The "golden mantle" refers to the golden-brown wash across the head and shoulders.

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (eighteenth mammalian entry; sixteenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- SIXTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- FIFTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75-76-77-78-79). The Sciuridae quintet. Ties Mustelidae (63-67) and Swallows (26-30). THREE-WAY TIE.
- **Genus:** *Callospermophilus* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Third ground squirrel genus after *Marmota* (75-77) and *Urocitellus* (78). "Beautiful seed-lovers."
- **Species:** *C. lateralis* -- the Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel. Distributed across western North America from British Columbia to Arizona and New Mexico [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE BEAUTIFUL SEED-LOVER

### Body Form

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is medium-small [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 230-295 mm (9-12 inches) including tail. Body alone: 150-200 mm.
- **Weight:** 170-300 g (6-10 oz). Similar to Townsend's (78) but stockier and more robust.
- **Tail:** 65-120 mm. Moderately bushy -- more so than Townsend's.
- **Body shape:** Stocky relative to other ground squirrels. Built for rocky terrain and forest floor navigation.

### Pelage -- The Mantle and the Stripes

- **Body stripes:** Two prominent lateral stripes on each side: a white (or cream) central stripe bordered by black. The stripes run from the shoulder to the hip. Similar to chipmunk stripes, which causes the confusion.
- **Head and shoulders:** The golden-brown "mantle" -- a distinctive warm wash of golden-brown across the head, shoulders, and upper back. The namesake feature.
- **Face:** **CLEAN AND UNSTRIPED** -- the critical distinction from chipmunks. No stripes through the eyes or across the cheeks. A plain, rounded face.
- **Belly:** Lighter, buff to white.

### The Chipmunk Confusion

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is the PNW mammal most commonly confused with chipmunks [1, 2, 3]:

## THE SOUND: SHARP CHIP ALARM -- 80th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Chip That Cuts Through Forest

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel's alarm call is a **sharp chip** -- a single, percussive, high-pitched note [1, 2, 3]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 4,000-9,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 0.05-0.15 seconds per chip. Very brief -- sharp onset, rapid decay.
- **Structure:** Single emphatic note, repeated at 1-3 second intervals when alarmed. Unlike the Townsend's rapid trill (78), the Golden-mantled's alarm is a single note with space between repetitions.
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- moderate. Audible at 50-150 meters through forest edge habitat. The sharp frequency cuts through ambient forest sound.
- **Function:** Alarm. Predator detection. Less colonial than Townsend's -- the alarm serves individual warning plus loose neighbor alert rather than colony-wide diving response.

### Why E=4

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel's chip warrants E=4 [1, 2, 3]:

1. **Volume:** Moderate. Audible through forest edge habitat at 50-150 meters.
2. **Sharpness:** The chip cuts through ambient forest sound with percussive clarity.
3. **Emphasis:** Single emphatic note rather than rapid repetition -- each chip carries maximum impact.
4. **Forest edge acoustic:** The forested habitat absorbs more sound than open grassland, but the high frequency penetrates understory vegetation.

## ECOLOGY: THE FOREST EDGE DWELLER

### Habitat: Forest Edge -- The Interface

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel inhabits **forest edge** -- the interface between coniferous forest and open ground [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Elevation:** 1,000-3,500 meters. Montane to subalpine -- higher than Townsend's grassland but lower than the Olympic alpine.
- **Vegetation:** Open coniferous forest (ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, spruce, fir), rocky clearings, talus slopes, montane meadow margins. The ecotone between forest and open ground.
- **Range:** Western North America from British Columbia to Arizona. In the PNW: Cascade Range, Blue Mountains, eastern Oregon/Washington montane zones.
- **Significance:** Returns from pure grassland (78) to forest interface. Each Sciuridae quintet member occupies a different habitat: alpine rock (75), alpine meadow (76), Olympic alpine (77), open grassland (78), forest edge (79).

### Social Structure: Loosely Aggregated

Golden-mantled Ground Squirrels are less colonial than Townsend's Ground Squirrels [1, 2, 3]:

- **Social mode:** Loosely aggregated where habitat is suitable. Not densely colonial. More solitary than grassland ground squirrels.
- **Territory:** Males are somewhat territorial. Home ranges overlap. Encounters at food sources are common but not cooperative.
- **Alarm response:** Less coordinated than Townsend's colonial system. Individual alarm chips alert nearby individuals but do not trigger synchronized colony-wide diving.

### Diet: The Seed-Lover

True to its genus name ("beautiful seed-lover") [1, 2, 3]:

- **Seeds and nuts:** Conifer seeds, grass seeds, nut fragments. Primary food source.
- **Berries and fungi:** Seasonal. Mushrooms are an important food source.
- **Insects:** Small proportion. Opportunistic.
- **Caching:** Scatter-hoarder. Caches seeds and nuts for winter. Unlike the Townsend's continuous dormancy, the Golden-mantled hibernates with food caches to supplement fat reserves.

### Predators: The Forest Edge Network

The forest edge habitat connects the Golden-mantled to predators already in the engine [1, 2, 3]:

- **American Marten** (degree 65) -- canopy-to-ground predator-prey. The marten descends from the conifer canopy to hunt ground squirrels at forest edge. A Mustelidae quintet member (65) preying on a Sciuridae quintet member (79). Cross-quintet predation.
- **Red Fox** (degree 71) -- hunts ground squirrels at forest margins and meadow edges.
- **Short-tailed Weasel** (degree 67) -- forest edge shared. The weasel hunts ground squirrels at the forest-meadow interface.
- **Raptors** -- forest-edge hunting raptors: Northern Goshawk, Red-tailed Hawk.
