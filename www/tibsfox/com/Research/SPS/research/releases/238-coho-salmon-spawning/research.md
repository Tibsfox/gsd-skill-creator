## THE COHO -- THE STREAM SPECIALIST

### Why Coho Salmon Is Second in the Species Chapter

After Chinook (degree 237, the largest, the king), Coho is the natural second species entry in the Salmonidae chapter:

- **Medium size.** Coho (*O. kisutch*) adults are 45-75 cm (18-30 in) in length and 2.5-8 kg (5.5-17.6 lbs) in weight. Significantly smaller than Chinook but larger than Pink (smallest Pacific salmon). The Coho occupies the middle size range of Pacific salmon -- not the king's bulk, not the humpy's smallness.
- **The Silver Salmon.** Coho are called "Silver Salmon" for their bright silver ocean-phase coloration -- a flash of metallic silver in the water, difficult to see until the moment it breaks the surface. At spawning, males develop a dark red to greenish coloration on the sides, with a green head and hooked kype jaw. The spawning male Coho is dramatically different in appearance from the ocean-phase fish.
- **Stream specialist.** Unlike Chinook, which require large deep rivers, Coho are more tolerant of smaller streams. They use small tributary systems -- sometimes streams barely a meter wide -- that Chinook rarely enter. This makes Coho ecologically important in smaller watershed systems that Chinook do not reach. The Coho's ability to penetrate small headwater streams means it delivers marine nutrients (from its decomposing carcass) into parts of the watershed that no other Pacific salmon reaches.
- **Fall timing.** Coho run September-November in most Washington rivers -- peak October in many Puget Sound systems. This fall timing places Coho at the peak of the spawning season, when salmon streams are at their most active acoustically.
- **The sound of this entry.** This degree documents the spawning sound, specifically: male Coho fighting for spawning position in shallow stream riffles. The Pacific Salmon overview at 236 described spawning sounds in general; Chinook at 237 documented the waterfall leap specifically. Coho at 238 documents the species' characteristic spawning behavior -- aggressive male competition in very shallow water.

(Sources: *O. kisutch* biology; Silver Salmon coloration; stream penetration ecology; fall run timing; spawning male morphology; small tributary habitat)

## THE SPAWNING SOUND -- AGGRESSIVE MALES IN SHALLOW RIFFLES, E=6

### What Happens in a Coho Spawning Stream in October

Coho spawning behavior is among the most physically vigorous and acoustically productive of any Pacific salmon:

- **Male competition.** On the spawning grounds, male Coho compete intensely for access to females and redd (spawning nest) locations. The competition involves:
  - **Chasing:** A dominant male will pursue and chase subordinate males away from a female and her redd. In shallow riffles, the chase often involves the fish moving at high speed through very shallow water, with the dorsal fin visible above the surface. The sound of high-speed movement through shallow water is a rapid, irregular splashing.
  - **Fighting:** When two males confront each other, the fight involves circling, biting at the jaw and body, and physical collision. In shallow water (15-30 cm), the collision of two 3-5 kg fish creates substantial splashing.
  - **Spawning position:** The dominant male positions himself alongside the female as she digs the redd and deposits eggs. Other males will make runs at this position repeatedly, creating a cycle of fighting, chasing, and splashing.
- **Female behavior.** Females are less aggressive than males but also produce sound: they dig the redd by turning on their sides and sweeping their tails through gravel (the same tail-slap documented for the genus at degree 236), deposit eggs in the gravel depression, and cover the completed redd by moving upstream and displacing gravel. The female's redd-digging tail-slap is a distinctive sound in the spawning stream.
- **Shallow water amplifies sound.** This is the key acoustic feature of Coho spawning: shallow riffles (less water to absorb splash energy) produce louder, more irregular splashing than deeper pools. A Coho chase in 20 cm of water sounds very different from a Chinook leap into a deep pool -- it is irregular, chaotic, immediate, physically present. The energy cannot go downward (not enough depth), so it goes outward and upward.
- **E=6 assessment.** The combined sound of active Coho spawning -- multiple males fighting, females digging, occasional jumps, continuous riffle background -- is E=6: higher than the E=5 genus-level spawning overview (because Coho spawning specifically is more aggressive, more continuous, and more acoustically irregular than the averaged genus), equal to the Chinook leap (which is episodic heavy impact versus Coho's continuous lighter chaos). The energy levels are equivalent but the acoustic character is completely different.

(Sources: Coho spawning behavior documentation; male competition ecology; redd construction; shallow water acoustics; E=6 assessment rationale)

## 6PPD-QUINONE -- THE 2020 DISCOVERY THAT CHANGED COHO CONSERVATION

### How a Tire Rubber Chemical Became a Coho Killer

The most important recent finding in Coho salmon conservation is also one of the most surprising in any field of environmental science:

- **Urban coho pre-spawn mortality.** Researchers had observed for decades that Coho returning to urban streams in the Pacific Northwest were dying before they could spawn -- collapsing at the stream edge, showing erratic behavior, and dying within hours of entering freshwater. The mortality was severe: in some heavily urbanized streams, up to 90% of returning Coho died before spawning. The cause was unknown.
- **The stormwater connection.** Researchers established that the mortality was associated with urban stormwater runoff -- the rain that washes off roads, parking lots, and urban surfaces into streams. But the specific causative chemical was elusive for years.
- **2020: 6PPD-quinone identified.** In 2020, a research team led by Zhenyu Tian and Edward Kolodziej at the University of Washington identified the causative chemical as 6PPD-quinone -- a transformation product of 6PPD (6-p-phenylenediamine), an antiozonant added to tire rubber to prevent ozone cracking. When 6PPD in tires reacts with atmospheric ozone (which it does continuously, as that is its purpose), it produces 6PPD-quinone. The 6PPD-quinone is present on tire surfaces and washes off into stormwater when it rains. Coho salmon are extraordinarily sensitive to 6PPD-quinone -- the lethal concentration 50% (LC50) for Coho is approximately 0.8 micrograms per liter, a very low threshold.
- **The species-specific toxicity.** Not all salmonids are equally sensitive to 6PPD-quinone. Chinook and Rainbow Trout (the freshwater form of Steelhead) are less sensitive. Coho are uniquely vulnerable. The mechanism involves inhibition of an enzyme (DHODH, dihydroorotate dehydrogenase) that is essential for mitochondrial function. Coho lack a key mutation at the DHODH active site that would confer resistance, making them exceptionally susceptible.
- **Policy response.** Washington State passed legislation (SB 5804, 2023) requiring the study and phase-out of 6PPD in tires sold in the state -- the first legislation anywhere in the world directly targeting tire rubber chemistry for wildlife protection. The federal EPA has also begun regulatory review. Tire manufacturers are under pressure to find alternative antiozonants.
- **Conservation implications.** Urban Coho pre-spawn mortality from 6PPD-quinone is potentially one of the most significant ongoing conservation impacts on Coho in urbanized watersheds. Urban Puget Sound tributaries -- the streams that flow through Bellevue, Kirkland, Renton, and other cities before entering Puget Sound -- have the highest stormwater loading and the highest Coho mortality rates. The discovery of 6PPD-quinone as the cause may be the most important conservation finding in Pacific Northwest salmon biology in the 2020s.

(Sources: Urban Coho pre-spawn mortality history; Tian & Kolodziej 2020 6PPD-quinone identification; DHODH mechanism; species-specific toxicity; Washington State SB 5804 2023; EPA regulatory review; Urban Puget Sound tributary mortality)

## COHO ECOLOGY -- THE SMALL STREAM NETWORK

### Penetrating the Watershed's Smallest Reaches

Coho's ecological role in the PNW is defined by its ability to use streams too small for Chinook:

- **Small tributary significance.** The PNW has a vast network of small streams -- first, second, and third-order tributaries that drain the forested hillsides before joining larger rivers. These small streams are critical habitat for Coho juveniles (young Coho rear in small streams for 1-2 years before migrating to the ocean) and spawning adults. The marine nutrients that spawned-out Coho carcasses deliver to these small streams fertilize portions of the watershed that no other Pacific salmon reaches.
- **Juvenile rearing.** Coho spend one to two years as juveniles in freshwater -- typically in small streams with complex habitat (pools, log jams, undercut banks, beaver ponds). Beaver ponds are particularly important for Coho juvenile rearing: the slow, deep water of beaver impoundments provides winter refugia and food production. The engine's documentation of American Beaver (degree 61) intersects here with Coho ecology in a concrete way.
- **The urban stream challenge.** Coho are one of the most important "indicator species" for urban stream health in the PNW. Streams that can support returning Coho are functioning streams -- they have adequate flow, temperature, substrate, and water quality. Streams that cannot support Coho (most highly urbanized streams) are degraded. The 6PPD-quinone issue has made urban streams actively hostile even when other parameters are within range.
- **Winter steelhead rearing overlap.** Coho and Steelhead (the species to be documented at degree 239) share freshwater habitat in many Washington streams. Both use similar stream types; both rear in small tributaries. The two species' habitat requirements are closely aligned, and restoration efforts for one tend to benefit the other.

(Sources: Small tributary Coho habitat; juvenile rearing in streams and beaver ponds; American Beaver interaction; urban stream indicator species; Coho-Steelhead habitat overlap)

## COHO CONSERVATION -- PUGET SOUND AND BEYOND

### Species of Concern in a Changing Watershed

- **Puget Sound Coho -- Species of Concern.** Puget Sound Coho are listed as a Species of Concern under the ESA -- not Threatened or Endangered, but in need of monitoring and protection measures. The Puget Sound Coho ESU includes fish from rivers draining into Puget Sound: Nisqually, Puyallup, Green/Duwamish, Cedar/Lake Washington, Snoqualmie, Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Skagit, Samish, Nooksack, and many smaller rivers.
- **Olympic Peninsula and coastal populations.** Coastal Coho (rivers draining directly to the Pacific, west of the Cascades) are generally more robust than Puget Sound populations. The absence of the development pressure, lower stormwater loading, and better-preserved riparian habitat contributes to healthier coastal Coho numbers.
- **Lower Columbia Coho -- Threatened.** Coho in the lower Columbia River ESU are listed as Threatened -- a stronger conservation status than Puget Sound fish.
- **The pre-spawn mortality scale.** Before the 6PPD-quinone discovery, the scale of urban Coho pre-spawn mortality was not fully appreciated. Studies in heavily urbanized Puget Sound tributaries found mortality rates of 40-90% before the cause was known. With 6PPD-quinone identified, the full scope of the mortality can be assessed and remediation strategies developed.

(Sources: Puget Sound Coho ESU Species of Concern; Olympic Peninsula Coho; Lower Columbia Coho Threatened; pre-spawn mortality rates by watershed; habitat protection measures)

## KEY COHO SITES IN WASHINGTON

### Where the Spawning Sound Is Heard

- **Cedar River (Maple Valley area).** One of the most productive Coho streams in the Puget Sound watershed. The Cedar River below Landsburg Dam receives both Chinook (documented at degree 237) and Coho returning to spawn in fall. The river is accessible for salmon viewing at several points.
- **Thornton Creek (Seattle, urban).** Thornton Creek drains the north Seattle urban area and has been subject to restoration efforts that include Coho passage. Urban Coho restoration in Seattle's smaller streams is an active management program. The Coho that return to Thornton Creek and other Seattle urban streams are representative of the 6PPD-quinone problem -- returning through stormwater-laden urban drainages.
- **Issaquah Creek.** Issaquah Creek drains the Issaquah and Sammamish valleys into Lake Sammamish and then Lake Washington. One of the most accessible Coho spawning streams in the greater Seattle area. The Issaquah Salmon Hatchery at the creek's lower reach is a public facility where spawning Coho can be viewed.
- **Skagit River tributaries.** The Skagit River system has some of the most productive wild Coho populations in the Puget Sound area. Smaller Skagit tributaries support wild Coho spawning at good numbers.

(Sources: Cedar River Coho; Thornton Creek urban restoration; Issaquah Creek Salmon Days; Skagit tributary Coho; urban stream restoration programs)
