Coho Salmon — Spawning Males

Oncorhynchus kisutch — Salmonidae — Silver Salmon
aggressive male splashing in shallow spawning riffles — E=6 — stream — fall (Sep–Nov) — Salmonidae

SILVER SALMON — STREAM SPECIALIST — 6PPD-QUINONE DISCOVERY 2020

Coho are medium-sized (2.5–8 kg), fall-running, small-stream specialists. Male Coho on spawning grounds are aggressively competitive in shallow riffles — chasing, fighting, biting — producing continuous irregular splashing. Critical 2020 discovery: Coho are uniquely sensitive to 6PPD-quinone, a tire rubber chemical in urban stormwater, causing up to 90% pre-spawn mortality in urbanized streams. Puget Sound Coho: Species of Concern. Pairs with Presidents of the USA (S36 238) at E=6 equilibrium.

The Sound of Aggressive Male Competition

Male Coho on the spawning grounds are relentlessly combative. In shallow riffles (15–45 cm of water), their fights cannot be absorbed by depth — the energy translates directly into splashing. Dominant males chase subordinates, fight for redd access, and position alongside females. The combination of chasing (high-speed movement through shallow water), fighting (body collision, biting), and redd-digging (female tail-slapping gravel) produces a continuous, irregular, energetic acoustic environment.

"The Coho male in a spawning riffle is the silver salmon distilled: bright, aggressive, all motion. The sound is not the heavy impact of the Chinook leap -- it is the continuous chaotic splashing of fish that cannot hold still."

Acoustic Profile — Spawning Male Coho

Male pursuit (chase in shallow water): irregular high-frequency splash, 200–3000 Hz
Male fighting (body collision): broadband impact, 50–1000 Hz, irregular timing
Female redd digging (tail-slap on gravel): 50–500 Hz, intermittent
Background riffle: continuous low-level water sound

E=6: same energy as Chinook leap (237) but completely different acoustic character. Chinook = episodic heavy impacts. Coho = continuous lighter chaotic splashing. Equal energy, different texture. Shallow riffles amplify the sound by preventing energy absorption into depth.

The 6PPD-Quinone Discovery

2020 — The Most Important Salmon Conservation Finding of the Decade

For decades, Coho returning to urban Pacific Northwest streams were dying before spawning — sometimes 40–90% mortality rates in heavily urbanized watersheds. Cause unknown. In 2020, researchers at the University of Washington identified the causative compound: 6PPD-quinone, a transformation product of an antiozonant added to tire rubber to prevent cracking. When it rains, 6PPD-quinone washes off road surfaces into stormwater and enters streams. Coho are exquisitely sensitive (LC50 ~0.8 micrograms/liter) due to a vulnerability in the DHODH enzyme system. Chinook and Rainbow Trout are less sensitive. Washington State passed legislation targeting tire rubber chemistry (SB 5804, 2023) — first law in the world directly addressing this threat.

Stream Specialist Ecology

Coho penetrate smaller tributary streams than Chinook — first and second-order streams, beaver ponds, urban waterways. This makes them ecologically important in the upper watershed reaches that no other Pacific salmon reaches. Coho juvenile rearing (1–2 years in freshwater) depends on complex small-stream habitat: log jams, pools, undercut banks, beaver impoundments. The connection to American Beaver (SPS 62) is direct: beaver ponds create the slow, deep winter refugia that Coho juveniles require. Restore beavers; restore Coho.

Conservation

PUGET SOUND COHO: SPECIES OF CONCERN. Not yet Threatened, but in need of active management. Lower Columbia Coho: Threatened. Urban stormwater (6PPD-quinone) causing up to 90% pre-spawn mortality in some Puget Sound tributaries. Habitat loss in small streams. Restoration programs active in Cedar River, Issaquah Creek, Thornton Creek (urban Seattle), Skagit tributaries.
