# Degree 42 — Yellow-rumped Warbler: Pass 2 Refinement

## Paired Release v1.49.543 — NASA 1.43 Alignment

*Cross-referenced with NASA 1.43 (Gemini 6A, first space rendezvous, Schirra/Stafford station-kept within 30 cm of Gemini 7).*

---

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is the tenth Parulidae — the DECTET — and the fifth consecutive warbler, completing the longest same-family run in the engine. It is also the most abundant warbler in North America, the "butter butt," the generalist that can digest wax from bayberry and survive winter where no other warbler can. The species contains two major subspecies groups: the western Audubon's Warbler (yellow throat, the PNW form) and the eastern Myrtle Warbler (white throat), lumped into a single species in 1973 after John Hubbard documented free hybridization in the southern Canadian Rockies — but the re-split debate continues, with the IOC World Bird List already recognizing them as separate species. Two forms, one name. A rendezvous of lineages.

Gemini 6A achieved the first rendezvous in space on December 15, 1965 — Wally Schirra and Tom Stafford maneuvered their spacecraft to within 30 centimeters of Gemini 7, station-keeping for over five hours, flying in formation around the Earth. Two spacecraft, launched separately, from different orbits, converging to fly as one. The Audubon's and Myrtle warblers are the ornithological rendezvous: two lineages, evolved separately on opposite sides of the continent, meeting in a narrow hybrid zone in the Canadian Rockies where they interbreed freely enough that the AOU lumped them in 1973. Two forms converging. The question of whether they are one species or two — whether the rendezvous is permanent or temporary — remains open, just as the question of docking (the next step after rendezvous) was left to Gemini 8.

The structural parallel deepens at the mission level. Gemini 6A and Gemini 7 flew simultaneously — two crewed spacecraft in orbit at the same time for the first time in history. The warbler dectet at degrees 38-42 represents two genera flying simultaneously through the engine: *Setophaga* at 38-40 and 42, *Leiothlypis* at 41. The Nashville Warbler at degree 41 was the "other spacecraft" — the one that broke the *Setophaga* sequence, just as Gemini 7 launched first and waited for Gemini 6A to arrive. The Yellow-rumped Warbler at degree 42 is the rendezvous completion: *Setophaga* returns to close the warbler quintet, the way Schirra closed the distance to Borman. Two forms — two subspecies, two genera, two spacecraft — converging to prove that convergence itself is possible.
