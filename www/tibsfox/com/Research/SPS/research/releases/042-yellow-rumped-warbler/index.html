Yellow-rumped Warbler

Setophaga coronata
loose trill varied song — the shimmer, 10th Parulidae (extending record), FIFTH consecutive warbler (absolutely unprecedented), Setophaga returns after Leiothlypis break — the most abundant warbler in North America, the one that stayed, the crowned moth-eater, the butter butt

The 43rd Acoustic Element: The Shimmer

A loose, erratic shivering trill: tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee, thin and bell-like, with the terminal pitch direction unpredictable — sometimes rising, sometimes falling. Notes are vaguely doubled, giving the trill its characteristic wavering texture. Named “the shimmer” for five kinds of variability: (1) the song’s internal pitch and direction shifts, (2) the taxonomic shimmer between Audubon’s and Myrtle (split/lumped/debated), (3) the seasonal shimmer between resident and migratory, (4) five yellow patches that flash like gold through dark foliage, (5) the generalist shimmer across all habitats.

The first acoustic element named for inconsistency as a defining trait — variability and unpredictability as named characteristics.

Setophaga Returns — With the Generalist

After the Nashville Warbler’s Leiothlypis break at degree 41, the genus Setophaga returns — but not with another specialist. Setophaga coronata is the generalist: the most abundant warbler in North America (~170 million), the only warbler that can digest wax, the one that overwinters in the PNW. Five Setophaga in the engine (16, 38, 39, 40, 42) — the largest genus representation in any family.

Specialist to generalist: The trio (38–40) were niche specialists in crisis. Degree 42 is the species that refused to specialize — and thrived.

The Wax Adaptation: Why This Warbler Stayed

The Yellow-rumped Warbler produces elevated bile salts that dissolve waxy coatings on bayberry, wax myrtle, juniper, and poison ivy berries — an adaptation unique among New World warblers. When insects vanish in winter, other warblers flee to the tropics. The Yellow-rumped switches to wax-coated berries and stays. The only warbler in the engine’s five-degree run that overwinters in the PNW.

170 million breeding adults. The wax-digestion adaptation is the foundation of the species’ dominance.

The Crowned Moth-Eater

Setophaga coronata — “Crowned moth-eater.” Setophaga = Greek ses (moth) + phagein (to eat). Coronata = Latin coronatus (crowned). Named by Linnaeus in 1766. Referring to the visible yellow crown patch — one of five yellow points on the bird (crown, rump, two flanks, throat in Audubon’s form). After two Leiothlypis with hidden crowns (20, 41), the engine gets a Setophaga whose crown is displayed.

The Sound: Loose Shivering Trill

Tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee — 7–21 notes (mean ~12), duration ~1.0–2.8 s. Thin, bell-like timbre, not buzzy like the Setophaga trio. Notes are vaguely doubled, giving the trill its characteristic “loose” feel. Terminal pitch direction varies: sometimes rising, sometimes falling. Males have at least two song types. The song shimmers — it refuses to be the same thing twice.

Parulidae — 10 Members (Extending Record)

Tenth Parulidae. Leads Hirundinidae and Turdidae (5 each) by five. FIVE consecutive Parulidae (38–42) — absolutely unprecedented. Four arcs plus exclamation point:

  • Quintet (14–20): Five species, five genera, five song endings. All E=3.
  • Trio (38–40): Three Setophaga, displacement cascade. All E=4.
  • Coda (41): Leiothlypis returns — genus shift, habitat descent to brush.
  • Exclamation point (42): Setophaga returns with the generalist — the survivor, the one that stays.
  • Overarching: Acoustic catalog → ecological drama → disturbance → generalist triumph.

10 of ~115 North American Parulidae ≈ 9%.

The Visible Crown — After Hidden Crowns

Three crown species in the engine: Orange-crowned (20) = orange hidden under olive. Nashville (41) = rufous hidden under gray. Yellow-rumped (42) = yellow, displayed. After two Leiothlypis with hidden crowns, the Setophaga puts its gold on show. Coronata = “crowned.” Five yellow patches — crown, rump, two flanks, throat — all visible, all flashing.

Leiothlypis conceals. Setophaga displays. The genus return at degree 42 is a return to maximum ornamentation.

Conifer Edge — The Habitat Between Worlds

Not deep forest. Not open brush. The edge. Where conifer forest meets meadow, clearcut, stream, or mixed woodland. The Yellow-rumped prefers open stands and edges in coniferous forest. In western Washington, it selects canopy gaps, bog edges, and meadow margins. The edge is the generalist’s habitat — where two worlds overlap, offering resources from both.

Audubon’s form (S. c. auduboni): Breeds in coniferous forests throughout the PNW. Some overwinter in lowlands. The main winter warbler in the region.

E=4 Continues — 19th Degree, 22 Without E≤3

E=4 now at 19 degrees (44.2%). E=3 absent for 22 consecutive degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 29 degrees. E=1 absent for 30 degrees. Five consecutive E=4 from the same family. The lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum is absent from degrees 21–42.

The Audubon’s/Myrtle Complex

One of ornithology’s great taxonomic puzzles. Before 1973: two species — Myrtle Warbler (eastern) and Audubon’s Warbler (western). 1973: lumped into Yellow-rumped Warbler based on hybridization in the Canadian Rockies (Hubbard, 1969). 2011: genus changed from Dendroica to Setophaga. Ongoing: genomic studies suggest they may be re-split. The IOC already treats them as separate species. The taxonomy shimmers — the species boundary itself is uncertain.

LEIOTHLYPIS vs. SETOPHAGA CROWN. Orange-crowned hid its crown (celata = hidden). Yellow-rumped displays its crown (coronata = crowned). Two genera, two strategies: conceal vs. display.

SPS-016: Yellow Warbler
