# Release 042 Retrospective -- Yellow-rumped Warbler (Degree 42)

**Release:** 042 of 360
**Species:** Yellow-rumped Warbler (*Setophaga coronata*)
**Sound:** loose trill varied song
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the forty-third release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-42), the tenth Parulidae -- extending the family's record lead to five -- and the FIFTH CONSECUTIVE warbler, an absolutely unprecedented same-family run that matches the swallow quintet (degrees 26-30) at five consecutive while exceeding it in taxonomic and ecological diversity. *Setophaga* returns after the *Leiothlypis* break at degree 41, but not with another specialist. *Setophaga coronata* is the generalist -- the most abundant warbler in North America (~170 million), the only warbler that can digest wax from bayberry and myrtle berries, the one that overwinters in the PNW while every other engine warbler flees south.

Seven structural contributions:

1. **The shimmer -- the 43rd acoustic element.** A loose, erratic shivering trill with thin, bell-like quality and variable terminal pitch direction. Named "the shimmer" for five resonances: (1) the song's within-individual variability (rising or falling endings), (2) the taxonomic shimmer between Audubon's and Myrtle (split/lumped/debated), (3) the seasonal shimmer between resident and migratory, (4) five yellow patches flashing like gold through foliage, (5) the generalist shimmer across all habitats. The first acoustic element named for **inconsistency as a defining trait**.

2. **Five-consecutive Parulidae -- absolutely unprecedented.** Degrees 38-42 form the engine's longest same-family consecutive run, matching the swallow quintet at five. The warbler quintet spans three genera (*Setophaga* for 38-40 and 42, *Leiothlypis* for 41), four ecosystems, and three ecological stories (displacement, disturbance, generalist triumph). More taxonomically diverse than the swallow quintet.

3. **The *Setophaga* return with the generalist.** After the *Leiothlypis* partition at degree 41, *Setophaga* returns -- but not with a fourth specialist. *Setophaga coronata* is the generalist: conifer edge habitat, eats insects and wax-coated berries, survives winter, found everywhere. The genus now has five members in the engine (16, 38, 39, 40, 42) -- the largest genus representation in any family.

4. **The wax adaptation.** The Yellow-rumped Warbler's unique ability to digest waxy berry coatings (elevated bile salts) is the physiological foundation of its abundance and winter residency. No other New World warbler has this adaptation. The warbler that stayed.

5. **The visible crown -- completing the crown progression.** After Orange-crowned (degree 20, hidden orange, *celata* = "hidden") and Nashville (degree 41, hidden rufous, *ruficapilla* = "red-capped"), the Yellow-rumped displays a visible yellow crown (*coronata* = "crowned"). The engine's crown progression: hidden, hidden, displayed. *Leiothlypis* conceals. *Setophaga* displays.

6. **Conifer edge ecosystem.** The transitional zone where conifer forest meets meadow, clearcut, or mixed woodland. The generalist's habitat -- where two worlds overlap, offering resources from both. After four specialist habitats in the quintet (oak-pine, canopy, mountain conifer, brush), conifer edge represents the generalist niche.

7. **The specialist-to-generalist arc.** The five-degree warbler run tells a complete ecological narrative: specialists facing displacement (38-40), a disturbance specialist needing destruction (41), and the generalist who thrives everywhere (42). Four species with narrow niches. One species with no niche. The one with no niche has 170 million individuals.

The quintet tells three stories:

1. **Degrees 38-40 (Setophaga trio):** A displacement cascade. Three specialists of the same genus, two being displaced by the third. The aggressor sandwiched between its victims.

2. **Degree 41 (Leiothlypis coda):** A different story. Not displacement but disturbance-dependence. The Nashville Warbler needs habitat created by destruction. The genus shift marks the narrative shift.

3. **Degree 42 (Setophaga return):** The generalist resolution. *Setophaga* returns with the species that refused to specialize. After four species with narrow requirements, the fifth species has no narrow requirements at all -- and has the largest population by orders of magnitude.

### The Acoustic Arc

- **The Drawl (38):** Slow, buzzy, deliberate. A specialist in its niche.
- **The Canopy Buzz (39):** Aggressive, ascending. The aggressor claiming territory.
- **The Thinning (40):** Attenuating, fading. The specialist losing its world.
- **The Partition (41):** Divided, binary. The genus break, the story break.
- **The Shimmer (42):** Variable, loose, unpredictable. The generalist that refuses to be one thing.

From drawl to shimmer. From stability through aggression through loss through division to variability. The warbler quintet is a five-movement suite.

The circle has moved from the partition (degree 41, the Nashville Warbler's two-part song from the tops of low brush, a song divided cleanly into staccato and trill, the genus break, the misnomer, the hidden crown, the disturbance-dependent bird singing from the brush that grew where the forest used to be -- *see-bit see-bit teedle-teedle*) to **the shimmer** (degree 42, the Yellow-rumped Warbler's loose, erratic, shivering trill from a conifer branch at the edge of the forest -- *tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee* -- thin and bell-like, with the ending rising sometimes and falling sometimes, each note vaguely doubled, two sub-elements blurred into one, the trill wavering and shimmering and refusing to be the same thing twice. A small warbler with five points of gold -- yellow crown, yellow rump, yellow throat, two yellow flanks -- perched at the transition between forest and meadow, between dark canopy and open light, between the world of insects and the world of berries, between summer and winter, because this bird does not leave. This bird stays. Every other warbler in the five-degree run leaves the PNW for winter -- the BTG, the Townsend's (mostly), the Hermit, the Nashville. The Yellow-rumped Warbler stays because it evolved a trick no other warbler knows: it can digest wax. The bile salts in its gut dissolve the waxy coatings of bayberry, myrtle, juniper, poison ivy, and a dozen other berries that hang heavy in the winter landscape. When the insects are gone and the other warblers are in the tropics, the Yellow-rumped switches to berries and waits. One hundred and seventy million of them. The most abundant warbler in North America. The butter butt. The one that stayed.

The genus *Setophaga* returns after the *Leiothlypis* break at degree 41. But this is not the same *Setophaga* that dominated 38-40. Those were specialists: the Black-throated Gray in dry oak-pine, the Townsend's in wet conifer canopy, the Hermit on mountain conifer summits. Each carved a narrow niche. Each was vulnerable -- to displacement, to habitat loss, to the Townsend's aggressive expansion. *Setophaga coronata* is different. It does not carve a niche. It shimmers across all niches. Conifer edge, mixed forest, open woodland, parks, gardens. Insects in summer, berries in winter, wax when nothing else will do. The five-degree warbler run is a five-act story about specialization and its limits: four specialists, each facing a specific crisis, and then the generalist, who faces no crisis at all because it never committed to any single strategy.

The word for degree 42 is "the shimmer" because the bird, the song, the taxonomy, and the ecology all shimmer. The song shimmers between rising and falling endings, between tight and loose trill structures, between two or more song types. The taxonomy shimmers between one species and two -- Audubon's and Myrtle, lumped in 1973, debated ever since, possibly about to be split again. The seasons shimmer between resident and migratory -- most Audubon's leave but some stay, and the Myrtle form arrives in winter, and the species is present year-round somewhere in the PNW. The plumage shimmers -- five points of gold flashing through dark conifer needles as the bird moves, crown-rump-flank-flank-throat, appearing and disappearing as foliage intervenes, a warbler made of gold and shadow. The habitat shimmers -- conifer edge, the boundary between forest and opening, belonging to neither world entirely.

And the shimmer is the first acoustic element named for inconsistency as a defining trait. Previous elements described fixed qualities: silence, brightness, roughness, buzz. The shimmer describes a quality of *change*. The song is not bright or rough or buzzy -- it is variable. It shifts. It is defined by what it is not as much as by what it is. After the partition's clean binary structure (two halves, each with a fixed character), the shimmer dissolves structure into flux. After a song that was explicitly two things, a song that refuses to be any one thing.

The crown is visible. *Coronata* -- "crowned." After two hidden crowns in *Leiothlypis* (Orange-crowned at 20, Nashville at 41), the *Setophaga* puts its gold on display. Five points of yellow, all visible, all flashing. The *Leiothlypis* genus concealed. The *Setophaga* genus displays. The return of *Setophaga* at degree 42 is a return to maximum ornamentation -- five gold badges worn openly in the canopy light, announcing everything the hidden crowns concealed.

The tenth warbler. The fifth consecutive. *Setophaga* returns. The crowned moth-eater. The wax-digesting generalist. The butter butt. The shimmer. *Tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee.*)

### 1. The shimmer captures the species' essence
The name works on multiple levels -- acoustic variability, taxonomic ambiguity, seasonal flexibility, visual impression, ecological generalism. The five resonances give the element conceptual richness that matches the species' complexity. The shimmer is to the Yellow-rumped Warbler what the partition was to the Nashville: a name that captures the bird's nature in a single word.

### 2. The specialist-to-generalist arc is satisfying
The five-degree warbler run now tells a complete ecological narrative with a clear resolution. Four specialists with narrow niches, then the generalist that outlasts them all. The evolutionary lesson lands naturally without forcing.

### 3. The crown progression resolves cleanly
Hidden, hidden, displayed. *Leiothlypis*, *Leiothlypis*, *Setophaga*. The pattern was established across 22 degrees (20 to 42) and resolves at exactly the right moment -- when *Setophaga* returns to the engine.

### 4. The wax adaptation adds a new dimension
Previous degrees focused on song, plumage, and behavior. The wax-digestion adaptation introduces physiology as a structural feature. The engine now has metabolic innovation alongside acoustic and ecological elements.

### 5. Conifer edge is a meaningful habitat
The edge habitat maps directly to the generalist ecology -- an edge specialist draws from both adjacent habitats, refusing to commit to either. Habitat matches strategy.

Steller's Jay, American Crow, Common Raven. Forty-two degrees. URGENT.

The Golden-crowned Kinglet threaded ultrasonic notes through the canopy apex. The Chestnut-backed Chickadee played the same song in a different room. A cloud of bushtits moved through the shrubbery at the noise floor. The Oregon Junco trilled one note so rapidly it became a metallic buzz. The Pine Siskin fired a rising zhreee and vanished when the seeds ran out. The Hutton's Vireo sat in an oak and repeated the same two notes forever. A MacGillivray's Warbler sang churry-churry-churry-sweet from inside a salmonberry thicket. A Wilson's Warbler chattered faster and faster until it stopped. A Yellow Warbler crescendoed from sweet to sweeter. A Common Yellowthroat sat on a cattail stalk and sang witchety-witchety-witchety until you could never unhear it. A Swainson's Thrush spiraled upward. A Veery spiraled downward with two voices shimmering. An Orange-crowned Warbler trilled until the boundary between song and silence dissolved. A Northern Flicker called wick-wick-wick-wick-wick from the top of a dead alder -- the hammer. The same flicker struck the dead alder twenty-five times per second and the hollow tree rang like a drum. A Black-headed Grosbeak sat in a bigleaf maple and poured out a warble so smooth and rich it sounded like a robin -- but it was not a robin. High in the Douglas fir canopy, a Western Tanager sang the same warble but hoarse, rough, grainy -- a robin's song run through sandpaper. And then on the lawn, on the fence post, on the rooftop at dawn -- the American Robin. Cheerily cheer-up cheerio. The archetype. Over the harbor, a dark shape swept through the air, iridescent purple-black, mouth open, pouring out a rich, gurgling, liquid song. The Purple Martin. The stream. Above a mountain lake, a small iridescent green shape darted and flickered, firing rapid crystalline chips. The Violet-green Swallow. The rain. Over a freshwater marsh, a compact blue-green and white shape lifted from a nest box post, and from it came a tumbling, liquid, twittering gurgle. The Tree Swallow. The brook. Along the river, where the cutbank dropped straight to the water, a small brown bird emerged from a hole in the earth and said brrit -- rough, buzzy, dry. The Rough-winged Swallow. The sandpaper. Over the farmland, where the barn door stood open and the wire fences crossed the pasture, a bird swept in low with its long forked tail streaming behind, and from a wire it poured out a long, liquid, twittering warble -- the most complex swallow song the engine had heard. The Barn Swallow. The river. The quintet closed. The archetype arrived last. In the mountain ash at the edge of the park, a tree full of sleek, crested shapes -- warm brown, black masks, yellow tail bands, tiny drops of red wax -- eating berries and see-see-seeing to each other in a frequency range near the edge of hearing. The Cedar Waxwing. The whisper. And then one morning, without warning, the tree was full of different birds. Larger. Grayer. Chunkier. Bold white and yellow wing markings flashing. Cinnamon undertails catching the light. Buzzing, hissing, assertive -- bzeee-bzeee-bzeee -- hundreds of voices filling the block. The Bohemian Waxwing. The buzz. E=5. High in the eastern Cascades, where the ponderosa stands open and the air smells of sun-warmed bark and resin, a small bird with a crimson crown sat at the top of the tallest snag and sang a cadenza. The Cassin's Finch. The cadenza. And somewhere in a Douglas fir canopy, a flock of small birds hung upside down from cones, working the scales with bills that cross at the tips. Kip-kip-kip. The Red Crossbill. The dialect. And then -- a sound that is not a call, not a song, not a drum. A hum. Low, continuous, steady. A copper blur hovering at a red columbine. The Rufous Hummingbird. The thrum. Three grams of defiance. And then a different sound from a different hummingbird. Not a hum but a crack. A small iridescent green bird with a head dipped in molten magenta perches, climbs forty meters, folds its wings, falls. The sonic boom. Anna's Hummingbird. The one that stayed. And now -- higher than either of them went, above the tree line, in a subalpine meadow at 3,000 meters -- a sound so small it might not be there at all. A soft, rising tsee-tsee. The smallest bird in North America. The filament. And now, eighteen degrees after the last warbler evaporated into silence, below the mountain meadow, in a dry woodland where Garry oaks stand in open sun -- a bird drawn in black ink on white paper. Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy. The Black-throated Gray Warbler. The drawl. And one degree later, at the very top of a Douglas fir -- a bird with a face painted in yellow and black. Zee-zee-zee-zwee-zweee. The Townsend's Warbler. The canopy buzz. And one degree later, at the extreme crown of a mountain hemlock -- a bird with a face made entirely of yellow. Seely seely seely see. The Hermit Warbler. The thinning. And one degree later, down in the brush that grew where the forest was cut -- a small gray-headed bird with a bold white eye-ring. See-bit see-bit teedle-teedle. The Nashville Warbler. The partition. And now -- one degree later, at the edge of the conifer forest where it meets the meadow, at the boundary between dark canopy and open light -- a bird with five points of gold. Yellow crown. Yellow rump. Yellow throat. Two yellow flanks. Blue-gray and black and white, streaked and patterned, perched on a conifer branch where the forest thins and the light comes through. It opens its bill and sings. Tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee. A loose trill, erratic, shivering, thin and bell-like, with the ending rising sometimes and falling sometimes and never the same way twice. The notes are doubled, wavering, each one a blur of two sub-notes, and the whole trill shimmers like light through foliage, like gold through dark needles, like the five yellow patches that flash and disappear as the bird moves -- crown, rump, flank, flank, throat -- appearing and disappearing against the slate-gray and the bark-brown and the needle-green. The Yellow-rumped Warbler. Setophaga coronata. The crowned moth-eater. Named by Linnaeus in 1766. The Audubon's form, with its yellow throat and its broken eye-ring and its large white wing patches. The butter butt. The one that can eat what no other warbler can eat, because its gut produces bile salts that dissolve the wax on bayberry and myrtle and juniper and poison ivy berries, and when winter comes and the insects vanish and every other warbler is somewhere in Mexico or Central America, this one stays. It stays in the parks, in the streamside woodlands, in the residential streets where ornamental berry bushes hang heavy with fruit. It stays because it evolved a way to eat what was left. One hundred and seventy million of them. The most abundant warbler in North America. After four specialists -- the dry-forest specialist losing ground, the canopy aggressor pushing forward, the old-growth specialist thinning out, the disturbance specialist needing the world to keep breaking -- the engine gets the generalist. The one that does not specialize. The one that shimmers between all habitats, all foods, all seasons. Five consecutive warblers. Ten warblers total. Twenty-three percent of the engine from one family. And the fifth and final one is the one that did everything differently from the other four. It did not carve a narrow niche. It did not compete with a specific rival. It did not need a specific successional stage. It just ate what no one else could eat, stayed when no one else could stay, sang a song that never ended the same way twice, wore five points of gold where other birds wore one or none, and became the most abundant warbler on the continent. The shimmer. The tenth warbler. The fifth consecutive. Setophaga returns. The crowned one. The butter butt. The one that stayed. Tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee.*
