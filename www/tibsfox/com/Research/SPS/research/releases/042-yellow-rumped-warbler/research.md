## Taxonomy: The Crowned One

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Parulidae (New World Warblers)
- **Genus:** *Setophaga* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Species:** *Setophaga coronata* (Linnaeus, 1766)
- **Common Name:** Yellow-rumped Warbler
- **PNW Subspecies:** *S. c. auduboni* (Townsend, 1837) -- "Audubon's Warbler"

### Etymology

*Setophaga* -- from Ancient Greek *ses* (moth) + *phagein* (to eat) = "moth-eater" [1, 2]. The genus of bold-patterned, active warbler species. This is the fifth *Setophaga* in the engine (16, 38, 39, 40, **42**), returning after the *Leiothlypis* break at degree 41.

*Coronata* -- from Latin *coronatus*, meaning "crowned" [1, 2, 14]. Referring to the distinctive yellow crown patch -- one of five yellow patches on the bird (crown, rump, and flanks, plus yellow throat in the Audubon's form). The name was given by Carl Linnaeus in 1766 in the twelfth edition of *Systema Naturae*, originally as *Motacilla coronata* [1, 14].

Full translation: **"Crowned moth-eater."** A bird named for the golden spot on its head and its insectivorous habits. Unlike the Nashville Warbler (degree 41), which was named for a rest stop, or the Hermit Warbler (degree 40), named for its solitary behavior, *coronata* is named for a physical feature that is actually visible -- the yellow crown. The crown is not hidden. It is displayed. After two *Leiothlypis* with hidden crowns (Orange-crowned at 20, Nashville at 41), the engine gets a *Setophaga* with a **visible** crown. The yellow crown patch is one of the bird's primary field marks.

### The Audubon's and Myrtle Split/Lump History

The Yellow-rumped Warbler has one of the most complex taxonomic histories of any North American warbler [1, 2, 5, 14].

**Before 1973:** Two species. The **Myrtle Warbler** (*Dendroica coronata coronata*) in the East and North, and the **Audubon's Warbler** (*Dendroica coronata auduboni*) in the West. They were considered distinct species for most of the 20th century. The Myrtle Warbler was named for its association with myrtle (bayberry) berries. The Audubon's Warbler was named for John James Audubon, the artist-naturalist [1, 14].

**1973: The Lump.** John Hubbard published research in 1969 showing that Myrtle and Audubon's Warblers hybridized freely in a narrow zone in the southern Canadian Rockies [5, 14]. Based on this evidence, the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU) lumped the two into a single species in April 1973 -- the "Yellow-rumped Warbler." The new name, emphasizing the one shared field mark (the yellow rump patch), was widely perceived as bland and unflattering compared to the original names. Birders immediately dubbed it the "butter butt" [1, 2].

**2011: Genus change.** As part of a larger revision based on molecular phylogenetics, the genus *Dendroica* was merged into *Setophaga* [10]. *Dendroica coronata* became *Setophaga coronata*.

**Ongoing: The Re-Split Debate.** Recent genomic studies have shown that Myrtle and Audubon's Warblers are more genetically distinct than the hybrid zone suggests [1, 5]. A 2017 proposal to re-split them into separate species was rejected by the AOS Checklist Committee. The IOC World Bird List already classifies them as separate species. The taxonomic status remains contested -- the two forms may yet become separate species again [1, 5, 14].

**The Four Forms.** The Yellow-rumped Warbler complex currently includes four subspecies groups [1, 2]:

1. **Myrtle Warbler** (*S. c. coronata*) -- Eastern and northern. White throat. Breeds across boreal Canada and the northeastern US.
2. **Audubon's Warbler** (*S. c. auduboni*) -- Western. **Yellow throat.** Breeds in western mountains. **The PNW form.**
3. **Goldman's Warbler** (*S. c. goldmani*) -- Guatemalan highlands. Black on face extends further. Rare.
4. **Black-fronted Warbler** (*S. c. nigrifrons*) -- Mountains of northwestern Mexico. Sometimes treated as part of Audubon's group.

The PNW Yellow-rumped Warbler is the **Audubon's form** -- *S. c. auduboni*. This is the form with the yellow throat (the Myrtle has a white throat), broken white eye-rings, and large white wing patches [1, 2, 7].

## The Sound: The Loose Trill

### The Shivering Song

The Yellow-rumped Warbler's song is distinctive among engine warblers for its **looseness** -- an erratic, shivering trill with a thin, bell-like quality that rises or falls unpredictably at the end [1, 2, 7, 8, 9]. Unlike the clean partition of the Nashville Warbler (degree 41) or the steady buzz of the Townsend's (degree 39), the Yellow-rumped's song has a wavering, variable character. It shimmers.

The song is rendered as *tuwee-tuwee-tuwee-tuwee* or *tyew-tyew-tyew-tyew*, with the elements vaguely doubled -- each note has a slight internal structure, a hint of two sub-notes blurred into one [1, 2, 9]. The result is a trill that does not feel like a clean mechanical repetition (as in the junco's metallic buzz at degree 11) but rather like a shimmering, irregular warble.

### Vocalization Details

- **Primary song:** An erratic shivering trill of 7-21 notes (mean approximately 12), with thin, bell-like quality. Total duration approximately 1.0-2.8 seconds [1, 2, 9]. The song usually rises (but occasionally falls) at the end -- the terminal pitch direction is variable, not fixed. This variability is the song's defining characteristic.
- **Frequency range:** Approximately 3,500-8,000 Hz, with peak energy in the 4,500-6,500 Hz band [NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Song types:** Males have at least two distinct song types, typical of *Setophaga* species [1, 2, 9]. The primary ("type I") song is the territorial trill used in dawn song. The secondary ("type II") song is a weaker, more disorganized warbling used in interactions.
- **Tempo:** Moderate, with approximately 5-8 notes per second in the main trill. The doubled quality of each note gives the trill its characteristic "loose" feel -- it is not as tight as a junco's trill or as measured as a Nashville's staccato [1, 2].
- **Timbre:** Thin, bell-like, slightly sweet. Not buzzy in the way the *Setophaga* trio (38-40) was buzzy. The Yellow-rumped's song lacks the harsh, insistent quality of the Townsend's *zee-zee-zee* or the Black-throated Gray's lazy *weazy*. It is softer, thinner, with a bell-like overtone that none of the previous *Setophaga* songs had [1, 2, 9].
- **Terminal variability:** The song's ending is unpredictable -- sometimes rising, sometimes falling, sometimes trailing off. This is unusual for warblers in the engine, which typically have consistent terminal patterns (the Yellow Warbler always rises, the Orange-crowned always descends).
- **Call notes:** Two primary calls [1, 2, 9]:
  - A sharp, emphatic *chek* or *tchep* -- given during foraging and flight, year-round.
  - A softer *psit* -- given primarily during breeding season.
  - The Audubon's form call is softer and less metallic than the Myrtle form's call [1, 9].
- **Volume:** Delivered at moderate volume from mid-canopy perches, typically 5-15 m high in conifers along forest edges [1, 2]. The song carries well across forest openings but is not as projecting as the Hermit Warbler's penetrating thin notes from the extreme crown.

### The Shimmer: What Makes This Song Different

The Yellow-rumped Warbler's song stands apart from all previous engine warblers in several ways:

1. **Looseness.** The trill is not tight or mechanical. The notes have a wavering, irregular quality. Unlike the junco's clean metallic buzz or the Nashville's measured staccato, the Yellow-rumped's notes seem to float and shift.

2. **Variability.** Males have multiple song types and vary the terminal direction of the song. The same bird may sing a rising version and then a falling version. No previous engine warbler showed this level of within-individual variation.

3. **Bell-like timbre.** The thin, bell-like quality is distinct from the buzzy timbres of the *Setophaga* trio (38-40). The Yellow-rumped *Setophaga* has a sweeter, cleaner tone than its congeners. The genus has range.

4. **Doubled note structure.** Each note element has a vaguely doubled quality -- two sub-elements blurred into one. This gives the trill its characteristic "shivering" texture.

After the partition's structural duality, the shimmer introduces **tonal variability**. The shimmer is the 43rd acoustic element -- named for the Yellow-rumped Warbler's loose, erratic trill that shimmers between rising and falling, between doubled notes and clean notes, between song types that shift within a single individual.

## Plumage: Five Points of Gold

### The Painted Warbler

The Yellow-rumped Warbler in breeding plumage is one of the most strikingly marked warblers in the engine -- after the understated Nashville Warbler (*Leiothlypis*) at degree 41, the return to *Setophaga* brings bold pattern back [1, 2, 7]:

**Audubon's form -- breeding male (the PNW form):**
- **Head:** Blue-gray with a conspicuous **yellow crown patch** -- the "corona" the species is named for. The crown patch is VISIBLE, displayed, a primary field mark. Broken white eye-rings frame the face [1, 2].
- **Throat:** Bright **yellow** -- the diagnostic feature that separates Audubon's from Myrtle. The Myrtle form has a white throat; the Audubon's form has a yellow throat [1, 2, 7].
- **Back:** Slate blue-gray, streaked with black. Bold and patterned -- the opposite of the Nashville's smooth olive-green back [1, 2].
- **Wings:** Large white wing patches (not bars -- patches). Significant white between the wing-bars in males [1, 2].
- **Breast and flanks:** White with bold black streaking on the breast. Two conspicuous **yellow flank patches** -- one on each side, partially tucked under the wings, flashing when the bird moves [1, 2].
- **Rump:** The namesake -- a bright **yellow rump patch** that flashes conspicuously in flight. The "butter butt." The single most consistent field mark across all forms and seasons [1, 2, 7].
- **Overall:** Five points of yellow: crown, throat, two flanks, rump. Set against blue-gray and black-and-white. This is a *Setophaga* that DISPLAYS -- every part of its plumage is designed to be seen.

## The Wax Adaptation: The Warbler That Stayed

### A Unique Digestive Physiology

The Yellow-rumped Warbler possesses an adaptation shared by no other New World warbler: the ability to **digest wax** [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. Specifically, the species produces elevated levels of bile salts in its gastrointestinal tract that allow it to break down the waxy coating of certain berries -- bayberry (*Morella* spp.), wax myrtle (*Morella cerifera*), juniper (*Juniperus* spp.), poison ivy (*Toxicodendron radicans*), and other waxy-fruited plants [1, 2, 4].

This is not a minor biochemical quirk. It is the single most consequential physiological adaptation in the warbler family for determining winter range. All other North American warblers depend primarily on insects for food. When insect populations collapse in winter, those warblers must migrate to the tropics. The Yellow-rumped Warbler can switch to wax-coated berries. The berries are abundant. The berries are available all winter. The wax-digestion adaptation allows the Yellow-rumped to **overwinter in temperate North America** while every other warbler flees south [1, 2, 4].

### What This Means for the PNW

In the PNW, the Yellow-rumped Warbler is present **year-round** in some areas [1, 2, 7]. The Audubon's form breeds in coniferous forests throughout the region in summer. In winter, most Audubon's Warblers move to lower elevations and more open habitats, but they remain in Washington state [7]. They can be found in:

- **Lowland parks and gardens** -- foraging on berries and gleaning insects from bark
- **Streamside woodlands** -- where berry-producing shrubs are common
- **Open conifer stands** -- especially where juniper is present
- **Urban and suburban areas** -- adaptable to human landscapes in winter

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is the **main winter warbler** in the PNW. While Townsend's Warblers (degree 39) also overwinter along the PNW coast in small numbers, the Yellow-rumped is by far the most common wintering warbler. In the context of the engine's five-consecutive Parulidae run, this is striking: of the five warbler species at degrees 38-42, the Yellow-rumped is the only one that remains in the PNW through winter. The others (BTG, Townsend's partially, Hermit, Nashville) are strict migrants. The Yellow-rumped stayed because it evolved a new way to eat.

### The Warbler That Survived

The wax adaptation carries a deeper resonance. The Nashville Warbler (degree 41) was the disturbance-dependent specialist -- it needs habitat created by destruction. The Hermit Warbler (degree 40) was the old-growth dependent specialist -- it needs ancient forest. The Townsend's Warbler (degree 39) was the aggressive specialist -- it displaces its competitors. The Black-throated Gray Warbler (degree 38) was the dry-woodland specialist -- it stays in its lane.

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is none of these things. It is the **generalist**. It can live in conifer forests, mixed forests, open woodland, brush, parks, gardens. It can eat insects, berries, wax, sap. It can survive temperatures that kill every other warbler's food supply. It is the most abundant warbler in North America -- Partners in Flight estimates 170 million breeding adults [1, 11, 12]. One hundred and seventy million. No other warbler comes close.

The five-consecutive Parulidae run tells a story that ends with the survivor: specialists (38-40) who carve out narrow niches and face displacement, a disturbance-dependent colonizer (41) who needs the world to keep breaking, and finally the generalist (42) who thrives everywhere, eats everything, and outlasts them all. The evolutionary lesson is the shimmer: do not commit to one niche, one food, one season. Shimmer between all of them.

## Conifer Edge: The Transitional Habitat

### A Habitat Between Worlds

Conifer edge. Not deep conifer forest (that was Townsend's at degree 39). Not mountain conifer summit (that was Hermit at degree 40). Not post-disturbance brush (that was Nashville at degree 41). The **edge** -- the transition zone where conifer forest meets something else: meadow, clearcut, road, stream, mixed forest, suburban development [1, 2, 7].

The Yellow-rumped Warbler breeds in coniferous and mixed forests but **prefers more open stands and edges** [1, 2, 7]. In Washington state, the Audubon's form is found breeding in [7]:

- **Open conifer stands** -- not dense closed-canopy forest, but stands with gaps, light, and structure
- **Forest edges** -- where conifer forest meets meadow, clearcut, stream, or other habitat
- **Canopy gaps** -- openings within dense forest, including bogs and meadow areas in western Washington
- **Mixed forests** -- where conifers and broadleaf trees coexist
- **Higher elevations in eastern Washington** -- drier, more open ponderosa pine and Douglas fir forests on the eastern Cascades slopes

In western Washington, where forests tend to be wet and dense, the Yellow-rumped Warbler selects **microhabitats within the forest that are more open** -- canopy gaps, bog edges, meadow margins [7]. It is not a bird of the deep, dark interior. It is a bird of the light that penetrates at the edge.

### The Edge as Habitat Category

Edge is ecologically significant. Forest edges are transition zones -- ecotones -- where two habitats meet. They have unique properties [1, 2]:

- **Higher light levels** than forest interior
- **Greater structural complexity** -- trees of various ages, understory development, shrub layers
- **Higher insect diversity** -- insects from both adjacent habitats converge at edges
- **Berry-producing shrubs** -- edge shrubs produce berries that deep-forest species lack
- **Exposure** -- more wind, temperature variation, predator visibility

The Yellow-rumped Warbler's preference for edges maps to its generalist ecology. An edge specialist is a generalist by another name -- it lives where two worlds overlap, taking resources from both. The conifer edge gives the Yellow-rumped access to conifer insects (like the *Setophaga* trio at 38-40) AND edge berries and shrub insects (like the Nashville at 41). The edge is the shimmer between habitats.

### Nesting

The Yellow-rumped Warbler nests in conifer trees -- on horizontal branches or forks, typically 5-15 m high, though ranging from 1 to 20 m [1, 2, 7]. The female builds a small, flat cup of twigs, grass, moss, and rootlets, lined with plant down and feathers that characteristically **curve over the rim of the nest**, partially covering the eggs [1, 2]. This feather-curling behavior is distinctive -- the nest has a "hooded" appearance.

## The Leiothlypis-to-Setophaga Return at Degree 42

### What the Genus Return Means

Degree 41 was *Leiothlypis* -- the Nashville Warbler, the smooth-feathered red-cap, the quiet bookend genus. Degree 42 returns to *Setophaga* -- but not as a continuation of the trio (38-40). The *Setophaga* at degree 42 is fundamentally different from the *Setophaga* at degrees 38-40:

| Feature | Setophaga trio (38-40) | Setophaga at 42 |
|---|---|---|
| Specialization | High (each in a narrow niche) | **Low (generalist, edge habitat)** |
| Winter presence | Absent (strict migrants) | **Present (resident/winter)** |

| Song character | Buzzy | **Bell-like, shimmering** |
| Plumage | Bold but focused patterns | **Five yellow points, maximum display** |
| Wax digestion | No | **Yes (unique adaptation)** |

The *Setophaga* return at degree 42 is not a return to the displacement-cascade story of 38-40. It is a return to the genus with a *different character*. The trio was about specialists in crisis. Degree 42 is about the generalist in triumph. Same genus. Different moral.

### The Five-Degree Arc: Specialist to Generalist

Four specialists. One generalist. The generalist has the largest population by orders of magnitude. The five-degree warbler run is a lesson in evolutionary strategy: specialize narrowly and you become vulnerable to displacement, disturbance, or habitat loss. Generalize broadly -- eat what others cannot, live where others will not, stay when others leave -- and you become the most abundant warbler on the continent.

## PNW Distribution: The Year-Round Warbler

### Breeding Range

The Audubon's form (*S. c. auduboni*) breeds throughout the PNW [1, 2, 7]:

- **Western Washington:** In coniferous forests, preferring open stands and edges. Common in the Olympics, Cascades foothills, and mountain forests. Less common in dense lowland forest but present in canopy gaps and edges [7].
- **Eastern Washington:** Common in drier conifer forests -- ponderosa pine, Douglas fir, and mixed conifer at higher elevations on the eastern Cascades slopes [7].
- **Oregon:** Throughout the Cascades and Coast Range in coniferous and mixed forest. Eastern Oregon in open ponderosa pine and mixed conifer [8].
- **British Columbia:** Southern interior and coastal mountains [1, 2].

### Winter Range -- The Warbler That Stays

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is the only warbler in the engine's five-degree run that maintains a significant PNW winter presence [1, 2, 7]:

- **Myrtle form:** Common winter visitor and migrant in western Washington. The Myrtle form is more common in winter in the PNW than the Audubon's form [7].
- **Audubon's form:** Most leave for lower-latitude wintering areas (southwestern US, Mexico highlands). But some remain: uncommon but regular in lowland Washington through winter, especially in the Tri-Cities area. Can be locally common where berry-producing shrubs are available [7].
- **Winter habitat:** Open areas with fruiting shrubs -- parks, streamside woodlands, open pine forests, dunes (where bayberry grows on the coast), residential areas with ornamental berry-producing plants [1, 2, 7].

The season designation "resident/summer" captures this dual nature: breeding populations are primarily summer residents, but the species maintains a winter presence that no other engine warbler matches. The Yellow-rumped Warbler shimmers between seasons.

### Migration

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is one of the earliest spring migrants and latest fall migrants among warblers [1, 2]:

- **Spring arrival:** Late March to early April in the PNW -- weeks before other warblers [1, 7].
- **Fall departure:** Late October to November -- weeks after other warblers have left [1, 7].
- **Some individuals:** Present year-round in lowland areas with adequate berry resources [7].

The migration timing reinforces the shimmer: the Yellow-rumped Warbler is the first warbler to arrive and the last to leave. It brackets the warbler season. It bookends the year the way *Leiothlypis* bookends the warbler arcs in the engine.

## The Five-Consecutive Parulidae Arc: A Complete Story

### Degrees 38-42: The Warbler Quintet

| Degree | Species | Genus | Ecosystem | Element | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 38 | Black-throated Gray | *Setophaga* | Oak-pine | The Drawl | Specialist (dry forest) |
| 39 | Townsend's | *Setophaga* | Conifer canopy | The Canopy Buzz | Aggressor (displacement) |
| 40 | Hermit | *Setophaga* | Mountain conifer | The Thinning | Specialist (old-growth) |
| 41 | Nashville | *Leiothlypis* | Brush | The Partition | Specialist (disturbance) |
| **42** | **Yellow-rumped** | ***Setophaga*** | **Conifer edge** | **The Shimmer** | **Generalist (everything)** |

The arc tells three stories:

1. **The Displacement Cascade (38-40):** Three *Setophaga* specialists, one displacing the others. A drama of competition in the canopy.
2. **The Disturbance Break (41):** A *Leiothlypis* specialist that needs a different world entirely -- the brush that grows after the forest is destroyed.
3. **The Generalist Resolution (42):** *Setophaga* returns, but not with another specialist. With the species that refused to specialize. The one that shimmers between all habitats, all seasons, all food sources.

### The Acoustic Arc

The five-degree acoustic progression mirrors the ecological one:

- **The Drawl (38):** Slow, buzzy, deliberate. A specialist sitting in its niche.
- **The Canopy Buzz (39):** Aggressive, ascending, pushing upward. The aggressor claiming canopy.
- **The Thinning (40):** Attenuating, fading, diminishing. The specialist losing its world.
- **The Partition (41):** Divided, binary, two halves. The genus break and the disturbance break.
- **The Shimmer (42):** Variable, loose, unpredictable. The generalist that refuses to be one thing.

From drawl to buzz to thinning to partition to shimmer. The arc moves from stability (drawl) through aggression (buzz) through loss (thinning) through division (partition) to variability (shimmer). It is a story about what happens when specialization fails and generalization succeeds.
