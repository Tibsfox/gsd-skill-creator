## Structural Event 4: Onomatopoeic Naming

- **Situation:** "Scaup" is a direct phonetic transcription of the female's call. This is one of few cases where the English common name IS the sound.
- **Root Cause:** The call is distinctive and simple enough to be directly transcribed. The Scottish/Northern English etymology connects to both voice and habitat (mussel beds = scalps/scaups).
- **Recommendation:** Document the onomatopoeic naming as a linguistic/acoustic note.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **E=3 is defensible.** The soft scaup call is quiet, nasal, close-range (10-30 meters). Consistent with E=3 ratings for Barrow's Goldeneye (quiet grunts) and other low-amplitude vocalizations.

2. **The rafting behavior documentation is well-supported.** Puget Sound rafts of 5,000-20,000 are documented in Cornell Lab and Austin et al. 2020. The rolling foraging machine description is ecologically accurate.

3. **The Greater/Lesser Scaup ID challenge is real and well-documented.** Head shape (rounded vs. peaked) is the primary field mark. Sibley 2014 and Cornell Lab confirm this as one of the hardest North American bird IDs.

4. **The genus transition (Bucephala to Aythya) is clean.** Different nesting strategies, different habitat preferences, different acoustic profiles. The Anatidae streak is not repetitive.

5. **Cross-references to SPS-124 (Common Goldeneye), SPS-118 (Harlequin Duck), SPS-122 (Bufflehead), SPS-121 (Wood Duck), and SPS-123 (Barrow's Goldeneye) are all defensible.**

### Source Quality

6. **Greater Scaup is well-documented.** Cornell Lab, Austin et al. 2020, Sibley 2014 provide comprehensive coverage. Population decline data is supported.
