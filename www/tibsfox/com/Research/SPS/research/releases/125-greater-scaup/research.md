## THE SCAUP CALL: THE SOUND THAT NAMED THE BIRD

### Etymology of "Scaup"

The name **"scaup"** is onomatopoeia. The word comes directly from the bird's call -- a soft, nasal **"scaup"** or **"scaup scaup"** that the female gives as a flight call and alarm call. This is one of the few cases in English bird nomenclature where the common name is a direct phonetic transcription of the bird's vocalization.

The etymology traces to Scottish and Northern English dialects, where "scaup" referred both to the bird and to the mussel beds (scalps/scaups) where the bird fed. The double meaning is appropriate: the Greater Scaup is named both for its voice and for its habitat -- the shellfish beds of northern bays.

### The Call

The Greater Scaup's primary vocalization is **soft, nasal, and understated**:

- **Female flight call:** A soft, descending **"scaup"** or **"scaup scaup"** -- the call that gave the bird its name. Low amplitude, nasal quality, often given in flight or when flushed. This is the diagnostic vocalization.
- **Male courtship call:** A soft, dove-like cooing -- a quiet **"whew"** or **"wah-whew"** given during courtship displays. Lower in pitch than the female's call. Barely audible beyond 15-20 meters.
- **Overall amplitude:** Quiet. E=3. The Greater Scaup is not a loud bird. Both the female's scaup call and the male's cooing are low-amplitude vocalizations, consistent with a species that communicates at close range within rafts.

The soft call makes acoustic sense for a species that lives in enormous rafts. When thousands of birds are packed together on a bay, close-range communication is sufficient. There is no need for long-distance projection when your nearest conspecific is a meter away.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Austin, Custer & Afton 2020; Sibley 2014)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: THE SOFT SCAUP CALL

### Frequency and Character

- **Frequency range:** 500-2,500 Hz. The scaup call occupies the mid-frequency range -- above the deepest duck grunts but well below the Common Goldeneye's wing-whistle (1,000-4,000 Hz).
- **Character:** Nasal, soft, descending. The call has a compressed, almost congested quality -- as if the sound is being produced through a narrow aperture. The nasal timbre is distinctive.
- **Rhythm:** Single or double notes. "Scaup" or "scaup scaup." Not rhythmic or sustained -- brief, discrete calls.
- **Amplitude:** Low. E=3. Audible at 10-30 meters in calm conditions. The call does not carry far over water. It is a close-range vocalization.
- **Spectral signature:** The nasal quality produces a compressed spectral profile -- energy concentrated in a narrow band rather than spread across a wide frequency range. This gives the call its characteristic "pinched" quality.

### Why E=3

The soft scaup call registers at E=3 because:

- **It is quiet.** The call is not projected -- it is given at low volume, audible at close range.
- **It is brief.** Single or double notes, not sustained songs or continuous calls.
- **It is not structurally amplified.** Unlike the Common Goldeneye's wing-whistle (mechanical, E=4) or the Harlequin Duck's stream calls (competitive with rapids, E=5), the scaup call is a simple, quiet vocalization.
- **It is appropriate for raft life.** Close-range communication within a dense aggregation does not require volume.
- **E=3 distinguishes it from silence (E=1) and from the barely-audible grunts of the Barrow's Goldeneye (E=3).** The scaup call is soft but clearly audible at close range -- not a whisper, but not a shout.

| Feature | Barrow's Goldeneye (123) | Common Goldeneye (124) | Greater Scaup (125) |

| **Primary sound** | Quiet grunts | Wing-whistle | Soft "scaup" call |

The return to vocal sound production (after Common Goldeneye's mechanical wing-whistle) and to E=3 (after Common Goldeneye's E=4) signals a shift from the Bucephala acoustic strategy to the Aythya acoustic strategy. The Aythya ducks are vocal communicators, not mechanical sound-producers.

## RAFTING BEHAVIOR: THOUSANDS ON THE BAY

### The Raft as Ecological Strategy

The Greater Scaup's most visually striking behavior is **rafting** -- the formation of enormous aggregations on open water. On Puget Sound and other Pacific Northwest coastal waters, Greater Scaup gather in rafts that can number from **hundreds to tens of thousands of individuals**.

Rafting behavior serves multiple functions:

- **Predator dilution:** In a raft of thousands, any individual bird's probability of being taken by a predator is extremely low. The raft is a statistical defense.
- **Thermoregulation:** Dense aggregations on cold water reduce heat loss. Birds in the center of a raft are sheltered from wind by birds on the perimeter.
- **Foraging coordination:** When scaup dive for food (mollusks, crustaceans, aquatic plants), the raft moves slowly across the water. Individual birds dive from the leading edge, feed on the bottom, and surface at the trailing edge, then swim forward to dive again. The raft functions as a **rolling foraging machine**.
- **Predator detection:** More eyes watching for Bald Eagles, Peregrine Falcons, and other aerial predators. The raft multiplies vigilance.

### Puget Sound Rafts

On Puget Sound, Greater Scaup rafts are one of the most spectacular winter wildlife phenomena. Rafts of **5,000-20,000 birds** are documented in favored locations -- shallow bays with abundant shellfish beds. The rafts are visible from shore as dark masses on the water, shifting and reforming as the birds dive and surface.

The visual spectacle of a scaup raft is in dramatic contrast with the quiet of the individual call. A raft of 10,000 scaup produces a collective murmur -- thousands of soft "scaup" calls blending into an ambient hum that is audible from the shore. The individual call is E=3; the collective sound of the raft is louder but diffuse, a wall of soft nasal sound rather than any single loud call.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Austin, Custer & Afton 2020)

## IDENTIFICATION: THE GREATER/LESSER SCAUP CHALLENGE

### The Hardest ID Problem in Winter Waterfowl

The Greater Scaup / Lesser Scaup identification challenge is one of the **most difficult in North American birding**. Both species are medium-sized diving ducks with dark heads, gray backs, and white flanks. Both winter in large flocks on open water. Both are called "scaup." Separating them in the field requires attention to subtle differences:

**Male breeding plumage:**

- **Greater Scaup:** Head has a **greenish gloss** (can appear purplish in some lights). Head shape is **rounded** -- smoothly curved from crown to nape, with no peak or bump. Back has **fine, pale vermiculation** (appears pale gray at distance). Nail (tip of bill) is **broader** relative to bill width.
- **Lesser Scaup:** Head has a **purplish gloss** (more consistent than Greater's variable gloss). Head shape shows a **peaked crown** -- the highest point of the head is behind the eye, creating a triangular or angular profile. Back vermiculation is **slightly coarser**. Nail narrower.

**The head shape difference** is the most reliable field mark: Greater's head is round and smooth; Lesser's head is peaked and angular. But this is a subtle difference that requires direct comparison, good lighting, and experience.

**Female identification:**

- **Greater Scaup female:** White facial crescent (area around base of bill) is **broader and more prominent**. Bill is slightly larger.
- **Lesser Scaup female:** White facial crescent is **narrower**.

**In the Puget Sound region, both species winter together**, and mixed flocks are common. This makes the identification challenge a regular test for PNW birders. Greater Scaup tends to favor **saltwater** (bays, coastal waters), while Lesser Scaup tends to favor **freshwater** (lakes, ponds, reservoirs), but there is extensive habitat overlap.

(Sources: Sibley 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE BAY DUCK

### Habitat and Range

Greater Scaup breeds on **subarctic and boreal tundra** across North America and Eurasia -- one of the most northerly-breeding diving ducks. Breeding habitat:

- **Arctic and subarctic tundra:** Lakes and ponds on open tundra, from western Alaska across northern Canada.
- **Boreal wetlands:** Scattered breeding populations in boreal forest lakes.
- **Eurasian range:** Breeds across northern Eurasia from Scandinavia to Siberia. Holarctic distribution.

In winter, Greater Scaup migrates to:

- **Coastal waters:** Bays, estuaries, harbors. Greater Scaup is more marine in winter than Lesser Scaup.
- **Large lakes:** The Great Lakes, major reservoirs.
- **Puget Sound:** One of the primary winter destinations on the Pacific Coast. Large rafts form in shallow bays with abundant shellfish beds.

### Diving Ecology

Greater Scaup is a **deep-water diver** that forages by submerging completely:

- **Dive depth:** Typically 1-6 meters, occasionally deeper. Capable of reaching 7+ meters.
- **Diet:** Mollusks (mussels, clams), crustaceans, aquatic plants. In marine winter habitat, bivalves dominate the diet. The name "scaup" also refers to the mussel beds (scalps/scaups) where the bird feeds.
- **Foraging technique:** Dives from the surface, propels underwater using feet. Average dive duration 15-30 seconds. Forages along the bottom, crushing shellfish with the muscular gizzard.
- **Gizzard strength:** Greater Scaup have one of the most powerful gizzards among diving ducks -- necessary for crushing the shells of mussels and clams. The gizzard contains grit and small stones that aid in crushing.

### Population and Conservation

The global Greater Scaup population is estimated at **1.2-1.4 million** individuals. The species is classified as **Least Concern** by IUCN, though populations have shown **concerning declines** in some regions. North American breeding surveys suggest a long-term population decline of approximately **50% since the 1980s**, though the causes are not fully understood.

Possible factors in the decline:
- **Changes in arctic breeding habitat** due to climate change
- **Contaminants** (selenium, heavy metals) in mollusks in some wintering areas
- **Habitat degradation** on winter staging areas
- **Hunting pressure** (Greater Scaup is a legal game species)

The population decline makes every large wintering raft on Puget Sound a conservation asset -- these concentrations represent a significant fraction of the Pacific Flyway population.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Austin, Custer & Afton 2020; Sibley 2014)
