Rafting Behavior — Thousands on the Bay

Greater Scaup gather in enormous winter rafts on Puget Sound — aggregations of 5,000 to 20,000 individuals visible from shore as dark shifting masses on the water. Rafting serves predator dilution, thermoregulation, foraging coordination, and multiplied vigilance. The raft functions as a rolling foraging machine: birds dive from the leading edge, feed on shellfish beds below, surface at the trailing edge, then swim forward to dive again. The collective murmur of thousands of soft “scaup” calls creates an ambient hum audible from shore.

Greater vs. Lesser Scaup — The Hardest ID Problem

One of the most difficult identification challenges in North American birding. Both species: medium diving ducks, dark heads, gray backs, white flanks, winter in mixed flocks. The key: head shape. Greater = rounded, smooth from crown to nape, greenish gloss. Lesser = peaked crown (highest point behind eye), purplish gloss. Greater has broader bill nail, wider female facial crescent. Greater prefers saltwater; Lesser prefers freshwater — but extensive overlap on Puget Sound.

The Sound That Named the Bird

“Scaup” is onomatopoeia — the common name is a direct phonetic transcription of the female’s soft, nasal flight call. The Scottish/Northern English etymology connects to both the bird’s voice and the mussel beds (scalps/scaups) where it feeds. Double meaning: named for its voice and for its habitat. The call itself is quiet (E=3), nasal, descending — a soft “scaup” or “scaup scaup” given in flight or when flushed. Male courtship: a dove-like cooing “wah-whew,” barely audible beyond 15–20 meters.

Bay Duck Ecology

Greater Scaup breeds on subarctic and boreal tundra across the Northern Hemisphere. In winter, it favors saltwater bays and coastal waters — more marine than Lesser Scaup. A deep-water diver (1–6 meters, occasionally 7+) that feeds on mollusks, crustaceans, and aquatic plants. One of the most powerful gizzards among diving ducks — necessary for crushing mussel and clam shells. Global population: 1.2–1.4 million (Least Concern), but concerning ~50% decline since the 1980s. Every large Puget Sound raft is a conservation asset.
