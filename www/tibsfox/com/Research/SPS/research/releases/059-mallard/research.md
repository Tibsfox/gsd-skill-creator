## Context: Back-to-Back Anatidae -- Wood Duck (58) to Mallard (59)

Degree 59 marks the **second consecutive Anatidae species** in the engine -- back-to-back entries from the same family in the same order. This has never happened before in the engine's history. The engine had consecutive species from the same family (the warbler streak, the sparrow quintet, the swallow quintet, the finch sequences) but never two consecutive species from the same family that were so fundamentally different in ecological strategy that placing them next to each other illuminates the concept of adaptive radiation itself.

The Wood Duck (degree 58) is the hidden aristocrat. The Mallard (degree 59) is the ubiquitous generalist. Both are Anatidae. Both are Anseriformes. Both are dabbling ducks that swim on the surface of freshwater ponds. But they occupy opposite ends of nearly every ecological dimension [1, 2, 5, 7]:

### The Familiarity Problem

The Mallard is probably the first duck most people can name. It is the duck in the park, the duck on the pond, the duck on the bread-feeding sign, the duck in the children's book. It is so familiar that it has ceased to register as wildlife. A Mallard on a city park pond is background. A Mallard on a suburban retention pond is scenery. A Mallard flying over a highway is unremarkable. The Mallard's ubiquity has made it invisible.

This is the structural opposite of every prior species in the engine. Every species from degree 0 (Great Blue Heron) through degree 58 (Wood Duck) required some degree of introduction -- most people do not know what a Hutton's Vireo is, or an Olympic Torrent Salamander (before substitution), or even a Red-breasted Sapsucker. The engine's work has been to make the unfamiliar visible. At degree 59, the work reverses: to make the familiar visible again. To look at the Mallard -- really look at it -- and find what hides in plain sight.

## Taxonomy: The Flat-Billed Duck

### Classification

- **Order:** Anseriformes
- **Family:** Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, Swans)
- **Genus:** *Anas* (Linnaeus, 1758)
- **Species:** *Anas platyrhynchos* (Linnaeus, 1758)
- **Common Name:** Mallard

### Etymology

*Anas* -- from Latin *anas* ("duck") [1, 2, 5]. The most ancient, most direct, and most unadorned duck name. Where the Wood Duck's genus *Aix* reached to Aristotle and mystery, the Mallard's genus reaches only to the obvious: duck. The genus *Anas* is large, containing approximately 30 species worldwide -- the core radiation of dabbling ducks. Where *Aix* is an exclusive genus of two spectacularly plumaged species on two continents, *Anas* is the genus of generalists, the workhorses of the duck family, the ducks that adapted to every wetland on every continent except Antarctica.

*platyrhynchos* -- from Ancient Greek *platys* ("broad, flat") + *rhynchos* ("bill, beak") [1, 2, 5]. Full translation: **"flat-billed duck."** The name describes the Mallard's spatulate bill -- wide, flat, and edged with lamellae (comb-like structures) for straining food from water. Compare the Wood Duck's *Aix sponsa* ("Aristotle's waterfowl in bridal dress"): the Wood Duck is named for being beautiful; the Mallard is named for having a flat bill. The contrast in nomenclature mirrors the contrast in ecological strategy -- aesthetic specialization versus functional generalism.

The common name "Mallard" derives from Old French *malart* or *mallart*, possibly from *masle* ("male"), referring to the drake's distinctive appearance [1, 5]. The word has cognates across European languages, reflecting the Mallard's presence in human consciousness for millennia. In many European languages, the word for "wild duck" and "Mallard" are synonymous -- because the Mallard IS the wild duck, the default, the archetype from which all other ducks diverge in the human mind.

### Taxonomic Position Within Anatidae

The Mallard belongs to the tribe **Anatini** (the dabbling ducks or puddle ducks) -- the largest tribe within Anatidae, containing the familiar surface-feeding ducks of ponds and marshes worldwide [1, 2, 5]. Unlike diving ducks (Aythyini), dabbling ducks feed by tipping forward in shallow water, submerging the head and neck while the tail points skyward -- the classic "dabbling" posture. The Mallard is the type species of the genus *Anas* and arguably the most studied duck in the world.

The Mallard occupies a central position in Anatidae systematics. It is the wild ancestor of nearly all domestic duck breeds (except those descended from the Muscovy Duck, *Cairina moschata*) [1, 2, 5, 8]. The Mallard hybridizes readily with many closely related species -- American Black Duck, Mottled Duck, Mexican Duck, Hawaiian Duck, and others -- posing significant conservation challenges for those species. The Mallard's genetic influence extends far beyond its own species boundaries, making it simultaneously one of the most successful wild birds and one of the greatest threats to the genetic integrity of related species.

The Mallard was the **2nd Anatidae in the engine** (after Wood Duck at degree 58). The family count stands at 25 families across 60 entries. This is the first time two consecutive degrees have shared a family since the warbler streak (degrees 14-17, 20, then 38-42 with interruptions). Back-to-back Anatidae without interruption is unprecedented.

## Physical Description: The Icon

The Mallard is a medium-to-large dabbling duck -- bigger than the Wood Duck, stockier, broader-billed, and built for generalist feeding rather than canopy navigation [1, 2, 5, 7, 9].

### Male Breeding Plumage

The breeding male Mallard is one of the most recognizable birds in the world [1, 2, 5, 7, 9]:

- **Head:** Glossy dark green, iridescent in sunlight, often appearing black in shade. No crest. The green head is the single most iconic field mark of any duck species globally.
- **Neck:** White collar separating the green head from the chestnut-brown breast. A clean white ring.
- **Breast:** Rich chestnut-brown, sharply demarcated from the pale gray body.
- **Body:** Pale gray with fine vermiculated patterning -- a subtle, elegant texture visible only at close range.
- **Rump/Tail:** Black undertail coverts and uppertail coverts. Two central tail feathers curl upward in a distinctive "drake curl" -- a small, jaunty upward loop that is diagnostic in profile.
- **Bill:** Bright yellow to olive-yellow, with a black nail at the tip.
- **Speculum:** Iridescent blue-purple bordered by white bars on the secondary feathers -- visible in flight and when wings are flapped.

The male Mallard's appearance is instantly recognizable to most humans worldwide. The green head has become shorthand for "duck" in popular culture, appearing on logos, signage, duck calls, hunting gear, and children's illustrations. It is arguably the most culturally reproduced plumage pattern of any wild bird.

### Female Plumage

The female is **mottled brown** throughout -- camouflage evolved for ground nesting [1, 2, 5, 7, 9]:

- **Head:** Warm brown with darker crown and eyeline. Pale supercilium (eyebrow stripe). The face pattern is subtler than the female Wood Duck's bold white teardrop eye ring.
- **Body:** Mottled brown and buff throughout -- every feather edged in lighter buff, creating a complex pattern that dissolves into marsh vegetation.
- **Bill:** Orange with dark saddle markings.
- **Speculum:** Same iridescent blue-purple with white borders as the male.
- **Size:** Slightly smaller than the male but similarly proportioned.

The female Mallard is the archetype of the "little brown duck" -- the bird that birders must carefully distinguish from female Gadwall, female American Wigeon, female Northern Pintail, and a half-dozen other mottled brown dabbling ducks. Her camouflage is functional: she nests on the ground in vegetation, and her cryptic plumage is the only protection between her eggs and predators.

### Size

Length: 50-65 cm (20-26 in). Wingspan: 81-98 cm (32-39 in). Weight: 750-1,580 g (1.7-3.5 lbs) [1, 2, 5, 9]. Larger and heavier than the Wood Duck (47-54 cm, 454-862 g). One of the largest dabbling ducks. The size difference matters: the Mallard is big enough to be the dominant dabbling duck on most ponds, displacing smaller species through sheer physical presence.

### Sexual Dimorphism -- Degree 58 and 59 Compared

Two consecutive Anatidae species, both with dramatic sexual dimorphism [1, 2, 5, 7]:

| Feature | Wood Duck Male (58) | Mallard Male (59) |
|---|---|---|
| Head color | Iridescent green-purple with white stripes | Glossy dark green, unmarked |
| Crest | Long, swept-back | None |
| Breast | Burgundy-chestnut with white spots | Chestnut-brown, plain |
| Flanks | Buff with black-and-white bar | Pale gray, vermiculated |
| Bill | Multi-colored (red, yellow, black, white) | Yellow with black nail |
| Overall impression | Baroque -- ornate, patterned, jeweled | Clean -- bold blocks of contrasting color |

Both species have extreme male ornamentation. But the Wood Duck's dimorphism is ornate and intricate (bridal dress), while the Mallard's dimorphism is bold and graphic (green-head icon). The Wood Duck is a jeweler's creation; the Mallard is a graphic designer's creation. Baroque versus modernist. Complexity versus clarity.

## Vocalizations: The Quack

### The Female Call: The Quack

The Mallard's most characteristic vocalization -- and arguably the most recognized bird sound in the world -- is the **female's quack** [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 800-2,500 Hz, centered around 1,200-1,500 Hz
- **Duration:** Individual quacks are approximately 0.15-0.4 seconds. The descrescendo series ("QUACK-quack-quack-quack-quack") lasts 2-5 seconds with 5-10 notes decreasing in volume and pitch
- **Structure:** A harsh, nasal, open-voweled "QUACK" -- the first note is loudest and highest, each subsequent note softer and slightly lower in pitch. The descrescendo pattern is diagnostic.
- **Quality:** Harsh, nasal, resonant, penetrating. The opposite of the Wood Duck's thin, breathy whistle. The quack is broad-spectrum, covering a wide frequency range with rich harmonics. It carries across open water.
- **Amplitude:** E=5 -- loud, persistent, and projecting. Audible at 500+ meters across open water. The loudest duck vocalization in the PNW lowland soundscape.

When people say "ducks go quack," they mean the female Mallard. The male does not quack. The phrase "ducks go quack" is specifically and exclusively the sound of the female Mallard -- a fact that surprises most people when they learn it [1, 2, 5, 7]. The association of "duck" with "quack" is the female Mallard's legacy to human culture.

### The Male Call: The Raeb

The male Mallard produces a much softer, quieter vocalization -- the **"raeb"** or **"rab"** [1, 2, 5, 7, 9]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 1,000-3,000 Hz
- **Duration:** approximately 0.1-0.3 seconds
- **Quality:** Soft, nasal, slightly reedy -- a short, quiet, nasal grunt or wheeze. Nothing like the female's loud quack. Often described as a soft "raeb" or "rab" -- a sound most people have never consciously noticed.
- **Amplitude:** E=2-3 -- quiet, close-range, conversational.

The male Mallard also produces a thin, reedy whistle during courtship displays [1, 2, 5]. The courtship vocal repertoire includes the grunt-whistle display, the head-up-tail-up display, and other ritualized movements accompanied by specific vocalizations. These displays are among the most studied in all of animal behavior -- classic ethology documented extensively by Konrad Lorenz and subsequent researchers [8, 10].

### Acoustic Character -- The Quack vs. The Pond Whistle

| Dimension | Wood Duck (58) -- The Pond Whistle | Mallard (59) -- The Quack |
|---|---|---|
| Direction | Ascending (rises in pitch) | Descending (descrescendo series) |
| Frequency | 1,500-4,500 Hz (high, thin) | 800-2,500 Hz (mid-range, broad) |
| Timbre | Thin, breathy, finch-like | Harsh, nasal, resonant |
| Amplitude | E=3 (moderate) | E=5 (loud) |
| Bandwidth | Narrow (tonal) | Wide (harmonically rich) |
| Cultural recognition | Almost unknown | Most recognized duck sound |
| Sex producing primary call | Female (alarm whistle) | Female (quack) |
| Duration | 0.5-1.0 seconds (single note) | 2-5 seconds (descrescendo series) |

Two consecutive Anatidae, both with the female producing the primary call, both ascending or descending in pitch, but acoustically opposite in every other dimension. The Wood Duck whistle is intimate, thin, and rising. The Mallard quack is public, broad, and falling. The pond whistle is a question; the quack is a declaration.

### The 60th Acoustic Element: The Quack

The Mallard's descrescendo quack becomes the **60th acoustic element** in the species-side progression -- **the quack**.

Named simply "the quack" because it needs no further qualification. This is the sound. The one everyone knows. The one children learn first. The one imprinted on rubber ducks, cartoon ducks, and the universal concept of "duck." The 60th element is not exotic or rare or surprising -- it is the most familiar bird sound in the engine's entire acoustic vocabulary. Sixty degrees of accumulating acoustic complexity arrive at the sound everyone already carries in their heads.

The quack is named for three resonances:

1. **The ubiquity** -- the quack is heard in every freshwater habitat in the PNW, from urban parks to remote marshes. Where the pond whistle (degree 58) maps a specific habitat (wooded still ponds), the quack maps all of them. It is the acoustic generalist, the sound that fills every available freshwater niche.

2. **The descrescendo** -- the quack series falls in volume and pitch. After the ascending whistle (degree 58) and the flat rattle (degree 57), the quack adds a fourth directional character: the descrescendo -- a sound that announces itself and then retreats, loud first then quiet, present then fading. Structurally, this is the inverse of the Wood Duck's ascending whistle.

3. **The recognition** -- no other acoustic element in the engine carries pre-existing cultural weight. Every other element required explanation. The quack requires no introduction. It arrives at degree 60 carrying the accumulated recognition of every rubber duck, every children's book, every pond-side feeding, every cartoon. The engine's 60th element is the only one the audience already owns.

## Habitat: The Generalist's Domain

The Mallard occupies **the widest habitat range of any species in the engine** [1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9]. Where every prior species is associated with a specific habitat type -- the Wood Duck with wooded ponds, the Belted Kingfisher with cold streams, the American Dipper with mountain torrents -- the Mallard is found in every freshwater habitat from urban drainage ditches to remote mountain lakes.

### Habitat Range

1. **Urban park ponds** -- the Mallard's signature habitat in public consciousness. City parks, retention ponds, golf course water features, campus ponds. The Mallard is often the only wild bird many urban residents regularly encounter.
2. **Lowland marshes** -- seasonal and permanent marshes, sedge meadows, cattail wetlands. Core nesting habitat.
3. **Lakes and reservoirs** -- from small farm ponds to large reservoirs. Open shorelines for loafing and feeding.
4. **Rivers and streams** -- slow-moving lowland rivers, river mouths, sloughs. Less common on fast water.
5. **Estuaries** -- brackish water at river mouths. The Mallard extends into saltwater-influenced habitats -- farther into saline environments than the Wood Duck.
6. **Agricultural land** -- flooded fields, irrigation canals, livestock watering ponds. The Mallard's association with agriculture is ancient and global.
7. **Suburban developments** -- any standing water: retention basins, stormwater ponds, decorative water features, swimming pools. The Mallard will nest in any available vegetation near any available water.
8. **Mountain lakes** -- present up to approximately 1,500 meters in western Washington, though less common at elevation.

No other species in the engine approaches this habitat breadth. The Wood Duck (degree 58) requires wooded ponds with cavity-nesting trees -- a narrow niche. The Mallard requires any water. The American Robin (degree 25) is a habitat generalist on land; the Mallard is a habitat generalist on water. Together they bracket the concept of ecological generalism.

### PNW Distribution

In the Pacific Northwest, the Mallard is a **year-round resident** throughout the lowlands, with some populations augmented by northern migrants in winter [1, 2, 3, 5, 7]. The species is:

- **Present in every county** in western Washington
- **Breeding in every city** with standing water
- **The most commonly seen duck** at Green Lake, Union Bay Natural Area, Seward Park, the Washington Park Arboretum, and every other Seattle-area waterway
- **The most commonly hunted duck** in Washington State
- **The most commonly banded duck** in North American waterfowl management

The Mallard is so common in the PNW that it is the baseline against which all other duck observations are measured. A birder scanning a pond records the Mallards first and then notes what is not a Mallard. The species is the acoustic and visual background of PNW freshwater environments.

### Nesting

Unlike the Wood Duck's tree-cavity nesting, the Mallard nests **on the ground** -- typically in dense vegetation within 100 meters of water, but sometimes up to a kilometer away [1, 2, 5, 7, 9]:

- **Nest site:** A shallow scrape on the ground, lined with plant material and down feathers. Hidden in tall grass, shrubs, fallen logs, or agricultural stubble.
- **Clutch size:** 7-13 eggs (average 9-10), pale greenish or buff.
- **Incubation:** 23-30 days (average 28), by the female only. The female's camouflage plumage is her only defense.
- **Ducklings:** Precocial -- downy, eyes open, able to walk and swim within hours.
- **Broods:** 1 per season (will renest if the first clutch is destroyed).

The nesting strategy contrasts sharply with the Wood Duck at every point: ground versus tree, exposed versus cavity, camouflage versus spectacular plumage (the female's mottled brown versus the male's green head), horizontal escape versus vertical leap. Two species in the same family with diametrically opposed nesting ecologies.

## Ecological Role: The Anchor Species

The Mallard's ecological importance is proportional to its abundance. As the most numerous dabbling duck in the Northern Hemisphere, it plays foundational roles in wetland ecology [1, 2, 5, 7, 8]:

### Seed Dispersal

Mallards are significant dispersers of aquatic plant seeds, invertebrate eggs, and algae spores across wetland systems [1, 2, 5]. Their long-distance flights connect isolated wetlands, transporting propagules between ponds that would otherwise be biologically disconnected. A single Mallard visiting multiple ponds in a day can transfer seeds and invertebrate cysts across the landscape, maintaining plant and invertebrate diversity in fragmented wetland networks.

### Nutrient Cycling

Through feeding and defecation, Mallards transfer nutrients between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems [1, 2, 5]. Ducks that feed in agricultural fields and loaf on ponds transport phosphorus and nitrogen into wetlands. Conversely, aquatic-fed ducks nesting on land transfer aquatic nutrients to terrestrial soils. This bidirectional nutrient flux is small per individual but massive in aggregate across the Mallard's continental population.

### Prey Base

Mallard eggs, ducklings, and adults are prey for a wide range of predators: raccoons, foxes, coyotes, mink, snapping turtles, Bald Eagles, Red-tailed Hawks, Great Horned Owls, and others [1, 2, 5]. The Mallard's ground-nesting strategy exposes eggs and nesting females to mammalian predators. Duckling survival rates are often below 50%, and in some years below 30%. The Mallard sustains predator populations through its abundance and vulnerability -- a species whose ecological role includes being eaten.

### Indicator Species

Mallard population trends serve as indicators of wetland health [1, 2, 5, 8]. The annual Breeding Bird Survey and the Midwinter Waterfowl Survey use Mallard counts as a baseline metric. Declining Mallard numbers in a region indicate wetland loss, degradation, or contamination. The Mallard is the canary in the wetland coal mine -- not because it is sensitive (it is among the most resilient ducks) but because when even the Mallard declines, something fundamental has gone wrong.

## The Ancestor of Domestic Ducks

The Mallard is the **wild ancestor of nearly all domestic duck breeds** -- Pekin, Rouen, Khaki Campbell, Indian Runner, Muscovy (partial), and dozens of others [1, 2, 5, 8, 10]. Domestication occurred approximately 4,000-2,000 years ago in East Asia and independently in Europe, with archaeological evidence of domestic Mallard descendants dating to at least the Roman period in Europe and the Han Dynasty in China.

The consequences of domestication are enormous:

1. **Domestic ducks worldwide** -- billions of domestic ducks are kept for meat, eggs, and pest control. Nearly all descend from the Mallard. The species' genetic legacy is embedded in global agriculture.
2. **Feral populations** -- domestic Mallard descendants have escaped captivity worldwide, establishing feral populations that interbreed with wild Mallards. The genetic boundary between "wild" and "domestic" Mallard is increasingly blurred in urban areas.
3. **Hybridization threat** -- this is the Mallard's dark ecological edge.

### The Hybridization Problem

The Mallard hybridizes with at least 40 other species of duck [1, 2, 5, 8]. This promiscuous hybridization is driven by the Mallard's abundance, behavioral dominance, and the breakdown of prezygotic isolation barriers in disturbed habitats. The conservation consequences are severe:

- **American Black Duck** (*Anas rubripes*): Mallard hybridization is reducing the genetic distinctiveness of Black Duck populations in eastern North America. Pure Black Ducks are becoming increasingly rare as Mallard genes infiltrate the population [1, 5, 8].
- **Hawaiian Duck** (*Anas wyvilliana*): The endemic Hawaiian Duck is threatened with genetic extinction through hybridization with feral Mallards. Fewer than 2,500 pure Hawaiian Ducks may remain [1, 5].
- **Mottled Duck** (*Anas fulvigula*): Gulf Coast Mottled Ducks are hybridizing with Mallards at increasing rates as feral Mallard populations expand southward [1, 5].
- **Mexican Duck** (*Anas diazi*): Was once considered a subspecies of the Mallard; hybridization complicates conservation of remaining pure populations [1, 5].

The Mallard's hybridization pressure is paradoxical: the most successful duck in the Northern Hemisphere threatens the survival of its closest relatives through its very success. Abundance becomes a conservation weapon. The generalist assimilates the specialist. This is an ecological dynamic the engine has not encountered before -- a species whose threat to biodiversity comes not from predation or habitat competition but from genetic absorption.

## Conservation Status: Not Threatened, But Not Simple

The Mallard is **not a species of conservation concern** in the traditional sense -- its global population is estimated at approximately 19 million individuals in North America alone, with additional millions in Eurasia [1, 2, 5, 8]. It is the most hunted duck in North America, with approximately 10 million harvested annually by hunters, and it sustains this harvest without population decline.

But the Mallard's conservation story is not simple:

### Threats TO Mallards

1. **Wetland loss** -- despite its adaptability, the Mallard is ultimately dependent on wetland habitat. The loss of prairie potholes (the primary breeding habitat for North American Mallards) to agriculture continues to reduce continental populations [1, 5, 8].
2. **Lead poisoning** -- ingestion of spent lead shot in wetland sediments has historically caused significant Mallard mortality. The transition to non-toxic shot has reduced but not eliminated this threat [1, 5].
3. **Climate change** -- shifting precipitation patterns affect prairie pothole hydrology, potentially reducing breeding habitat availability [5].

### Threats FROM Mallards

1. **Hybridization** with American Black Duck, Hawaiian Duck, Mottled Duck, and others (see above).
2. **Disease vector** -- Mallards are carriers of avian influenza, including highly pathogenic strains. Their migration patterns can transport influenza viruses across continental distances [1, 5, 8].
3. **Competition** -- in some habitats, Mallard abundance may suppress populations of smaller, less aggressive dabbling ducks through competitive exclusion [1, 5].

The Mallard is simultaneously a conservation success story (abundant, resilient, sustainably harvested) and a conservation problem (hybridization threat, disease vector, competitive dominant). No other species in the engine occupies this paradoxical position.

## Konrad Lorenz and the Science of Duck Behavior

The Mallard occupies a unique position in the history of behavioral science. **Konrad Lorenz** (1903-1989), one of the founders of ethology (the study of animal behavior), used the Mallard as a primary subject for his groundbreaking research on imprinting, fixed action patterns, and ritualized courtship displays [1, 8, 10, 11].

Lorenz's work with Mallards demonstrated that newly hatched ducklings will "imprint" on the first moving object they encounter -- typically the mother, but in experimental conditions, Lorenz himself, a moving box, or a rubber boot. The imprinting studies, conducted primarily with Greylag Geese but extensively replicated with Mallards, earned Lorenz a share of the 1973 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

The Mallard's courtship displays -- grunt-whistle, head-up-tail-up, nod-swimming, bridling, and others -- were among the first complex animal behaviors to be described with the rigor of experimental science [8, 10, 11]. Lorenz documented the displays as fixed action patterns: genetically determined behavioral sequences that unfold in invariant order when triggered by specific stimuli. This work laid the foundation for modern behavioral ecology and comparative ethology.

The Mallard is thus not only the most familiar duck but also one of the most scientifically important birds. More scientific papers have been published on Mallard behavior, ecology, and genetics than on any other duck species. The Mallard is to waterfowl biology what the fruit fly is to genetics or the lab rat is to medicine -- the model organism, the default subject, the species that built the field.

## The Long Winters Parallel: Familiarity and Depth

The pairing of Mallard with The Long Winters at degree 59 creates resonances that illuminate the problem of ubiquity:

### 1. The Most Visible Member of the Roster

### 3. Accessibility as Strategy

The Long Winters' music is hook-laden, melodically accessible indie rock -- catchy, singable, inviting. Roderick's lyrics reward close attention, but the surface is welcoming. The Mallard is the most accessible duck -- present in every park, approachable, unafraid of humans. Both entities use accessibility as an ecological strategy: the Long Winters' hooks pull you in for the lyrics; the Mallard's visibility keeps it in human consciousness (and human bread baskets) while maintaining a wild population across six continents. Accessibility is not a deficiency. It is a strategy that works.

At degree 59, the Mallard pairs with The Long Winters -- both already visible, both already known, both requiring reexamination rather than introduction. Back-to-back Anatidae, back-to-back Barsuk, back-to-back shifts from intimacy to ubiquity.

## Duwamish/Coast Salish Territorial Acknowledgment

The lowland ponds, marshes, rivers, estuaries, and lake edges where the Mallard breeds and winters throughout the Puget Sound lowlands are the ancestral and current homelands of the **Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Suquamish, Tulalip, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, and other Coast Salish peoples** [3, 7]. These peoples have lived with Mallards for millennia -- the species is documented in the archaeological record of Coast Salish middens. Mallard bones are among the most common avian remains in PNW archaeological sites, indicating both the species' abundance and its cultural importance as a food resource. The wetlands that sustain Mallard populations were shaped by Indigenous land management practices including controlled burning of marshes, maintenance of camas prairies adjacent to wetlands, and management of waterways for salmon -- practices that incidentally created and maintained the open, productive wetland habitats that Mallards require. This acknowledgment recognizes that the ecological systems described in this research document exist within a human landscape of deep Indigenous stewardship and use.
