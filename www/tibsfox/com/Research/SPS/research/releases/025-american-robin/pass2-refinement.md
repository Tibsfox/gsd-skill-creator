### The Archetype Arrives: The Robin After Its Ghosts

For two consecutive degrees (23-24), the engine documented robin-like warbles from non-robin species: the Black-headed Grosbeak (degree 23, rich and smooth) and the Western Tanager (degree 24, hoarse and rough). Two acoustic ghosts — two pre-echoes of a song that had not yet entered the engine. At degree 25, the archetype arrives. The American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*) is the bird whose song the grosbeak and tanager were independently approximating through convergent evolution. The ghost becomes flesh. The echo becomes the source.

The convergence trio is complete:

| Degree | Species | Family | Texture | Role |
|--------|---------|--------|---------|------|
| 23 | Black-headed Grosbeak | Cardinalidae | Rich, smooth | Pre-echo 1 |
| 24 | Western Tanager | Cardinalidae | Hoarse, rough | Pre-echo 2 |
| **25** | **American Robin** | **Turdidae** | **Bright, clipped** | **The archetype** |

Three families (two Cardinalidae, one Turdidae), three textures, one acoustic pattern. The robin's "cheerily cheer-up cheerio" is the original — the song that the other two independently evolved toward. The archetype arrives last. The convergence is documented before the source is identified. This is the engine's deepest convergence event: three species, three degrees, the source preceded by its echoes.

Ranger 1 was a new beginning — the first mission of a new program (Ranger), aimed at the Moon, failing to leave Earth orbit. The American Robin is also a kind of beginning: the first truly ubiquitous, universally known bird in the engine. Every previous species required identification skill — you need to know what a Hermit Thrush looks like, or what a chickadee sounds like, or where to find a Hutton's Vireo. The American Robin requires no skill. Everyone knows the robin. It is the baseline bird — the species that non-birders identify by name, the bird that signals spring, the bird on the lawn. The archetype is the starting point for everyone.

### The Fifth Turdidae: The Thrush Family's Full Voice

The American Robin is the fifth Turdidae in the engine:

| Degree | Species | Song Strategy | Role |
|--------|---------|--------------|------|
| 2 | Varied Thrush | AM drone (one sustained note) | Atmosphere |
| 7 | Hermit Thrush | Harmonic series arch | Mathematics |
| 18 | Swainson's Thrush | Ascending spiral | Aspiration |
| 19 | Veery | Descending spiral | Resolution |
| **25** | **American Robin** | **Bright rolling warble (cheerily)** | **The everyday** |

Five thrushes, five acoustic strategies. The Turdidae are the engine's most species-rich family alongside the Parulidae (5 each). But the thrush quintet differs from the warbler quintet in character: the warblers documented intensification strategies (resolution, accelerando, crescendo, drive, dissipation). The thrushes document directional and timbral strategies (static, arching, ascending, descending, bright-rolling). The two quintets are orthogonal — different dimensions of the acoustic space mapped by different families.

The robin's "cheerily cheer-up cheerio" is the most recognizable bird song in North America — heard on every lawn, in every suburb, from every park. It is the engine's most democratic species: equally accessible to experts and novices, equally present in urban and rural landscapes, equally audible to old and young ears (unlike the Golden-crowned Kinglet at degree 8). The robin is the archetype because it is the bird everyone already knows. The convergence trio built toward it because the robin's song IS the acoustic center — the warble that other species independently optimize toward because it works in the habitats they share.
