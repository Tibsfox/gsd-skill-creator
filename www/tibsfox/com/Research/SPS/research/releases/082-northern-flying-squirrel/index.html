Northern Flying Squirrel

Glaucomys sabrinus
high thin “tseet” contact call — EIGHTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75–82), the OCTET! First GLIDING mammal in the engine. Strictly nocturnal. Old-growth dependent. Truffle mutualism with American Marten (65). Nearly ultrasonic. E=3, twenty-second consecutive original CSV.

The Sciuridae Octet — EIGHT Consecutive!

EIGHTH consecutive Sciuridae: Yellow-bellied Marmot (75), Hoary Marmot (76), Olympic Marmot (77), Townsend’s Ground Squirrel (78), Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (79), Douglas Squirrel (80), Eastern Fox Squirrel (81), Northern Flying Squirrel (82). Eight consecutive Sciuridae — the OCTET. The longest family run in engine history extends to EIGHT.

First Gliding Mammal — The Patagium

The Northern Flying Squirrel is the engine’s first gliding mammal — the first species that can “fly” (glide). A patagium — a membrane of skin stretching from wrist to ankle — allows glides of 20–50 meters between trees. The engine goes AIRBORNE for the first time among mammals.

Truffle Mutualism — Connection to American Marten (65)

The Northern Flying Squirrel eats truffles (hypogeous fungi) and disperses their spores through fecal pellets, inoculating forest soil with mycorrhizal fungi essential for tree growth. The American Marten (degree 65) also depends on truffles. Truffle mutualism connects two Sciuridae entries across 17 degrees: the Marten’s prey (Douglas Squirrel, 80) and the nocturnal glider (82) both serve as truffle dispersers.

Old-Growth Dependent — Nests in Tree Cavities

The Northern Flying Squirrel is old-growth dependent — it requires mature and old-growth coniferous forest with large-diameter trees that provide cavity nests. Habitat loss from old-growth logging is the primary threat. Shares old-growth dependence with Pacific Wren (degree 1) and Pileated Woodpecker (degree 54).

The Death Cab Acoustic Parallel

Degree 82 pairs Northern Flying Squirrel with Death Cab for Cutie (acoustic context) in Part A — Indie Folk, E=3. Both E=3. Both quieter versions of something bigger — Death Cab stripped to acoustic skeleton, the Flying Squirrel nearly inaudible. Both nocturnal/intimate — the acoustic set in a small room, the squirrel gliding through darkness. Both reveal hidden dimensions — acoustic reveals folk bones, the flying squirrel reveals the nocturnal old-growth canopy.

The Sound: High Thin “Tseet” Contact Call (83rd Acoustic Element)

The call: A high, thin, barely audible “tseet” contact call — nearly ultrasonic. Approximately 8,000–20,000+ Hz, at the upper edge of human hearing. Many people cannot hear it at all. The quietest and highest-pitched vocalization in the Sciuridae octet [1, 2, 3].

Why E=3: (1) Nearly inaudible to humans — at the edge of hearing range. (2) Very soft — low amplitude even at close range. (3) Brief — short contact notes, not sustained. (4) Nocturnal context — the call exists in the silence of the night forest. E=3 reflects the presence of vocalization but its extreme subtlety.

Taxonomy: Glaucomys sabrinus

Etymology: Glaucomys from Greek glaukos (“silver/gray”) + mys (“mouse”). sabrinus from Latin Sabrinus, relating to the Severn River. Described by Shaw (1801) [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Sciuridae — EIGHTH consecutive (75–82). Genus Glaucomys — NEW GENUS (sixth Sciuridae genus). Flying squirrels — the first gliding genus [1, 2].

Ecology: The Nocturnal Glider

Habitat: Old-growth coniferous forest. Nests in tree cavities in large-diameter trees. Old-growth dependent — requires mature forest structure [1, 2, 3, 4].

Activity: Strictly NOCTURNAL. Active only at night. Rarely seen by humans. The most cryptic mammal in the Sciuridae octet.

Gliding: Patagium (skin membrane wrist-to-ankle) enables glides of 20–50 meters between trees. Steering via tail. The first “flying” mammal in the engine.

Diet: Truffles (hypogeous fungi) are a primary food source. Also lichens, fungi, seeds, insects. Key truffle spore disperser — mycorrhizal mutualism.

Predators: Northern Spotted Owl (SPS-004) — the NORTHERN SPOTTED OWL PREYS ON FLYING SQUIRRELS. Key ecological link! Also: Great Horned Owl, Northern Goshawk, American Marten (65).
