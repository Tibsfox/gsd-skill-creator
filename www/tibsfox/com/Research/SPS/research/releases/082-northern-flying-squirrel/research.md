## THE NORTHERN FLYING SQUIRREL: THE NOCTURNAL GLIDER

The Northern Flying Squirrel is a small, strictly nocturnal, old-growth-dependent gliding mammal of the Pacific Northwest's ancient coniferous forests. At 110-230 grams (4-8 oz), it is the smallest member of the Sciuridae octet. It is almost never seen -- strictly nocturnal, inhabiting the darkest canopy of old-growth forest, making its barely audible "tseet" contact calls at frequencies most humans cannot hear [1, 2, 3, 4].

This is the engine's **EIGHTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79, Douglas Squirrel 80, Eastern Fox Squirrel 81, Northern Flying Squirrel 82) -- the **Sciuridae OCTET**. The longest family run in engine history extends to **EIGHT**. Three marmots + five squirrels. Six genera. Eight habitats. Alpine to old-growth.

This is also the engine's **first gliding mammal** -- the first species that can "fly" (glide) using a patagium, a membrane of skin stretching from wrist to ankle.

## TAXONOMY: *Glaucomys sabrinus*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Glaucomys** -- from Greek *glaukos* ("silver" or "gray") + *mys* ("mouse"). The silver mouse -- a reference to the squirrel's soft, silvery-gray fur seen in certain lighting [1, 2].

- **sabrinus** -- from Latin *Sabrinus*, relating to the Severn River. An early specimen was associated with this geographic reference. Described by Shaw (1801) [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Northern Flying Squirrel (standard). The "flying" is a misnomer -- the animal glides, it does not fly [1, 2].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (twenty-first mammalian entry; nineteenth mammalian species)
- **Infraclass:** Placentalia
- **Order:** Rodentia -- NINTH Rodentia in the engine.
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- EIGHTH CONSECUTIVE SCIURIDAE (75-82). The Sciuridae OCTET. LONGEST family run: EIGHT.
- **Genus:** *Glaucomys* -- NEW GENUS in the engine. Sixth Sciuridae genus after *Marmota* (75-77), *Urocitellus* (78), *Callospermophilus* (79), *Tamiasciurus* (80), *Sciurus* (81). Flying squirrels -- the first gliding genus.
- **Species:** *G. sabrinus* -- the Northern Flying Squirrel. Distributed across boreal and montane forests of North America. In the PNW: old-growth coniferous forests of western Washington, western Oregon, and the Cascades [1, 2, 3].

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE NOCTURNAL GLIDER

### Body Form

The Northern Flying Squirrel is small and adapted for gliding [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 25-37 cm (10-15 inches) total including tail.
- **Weight:** 110-230 g (4-8 oz). The smallest member of the Sciuridae octet. Roughly half the mass of the Douglas Squirrel (150-300 g).
- **Patagium:** The defining feature. A membrane of furred skin stretching from the wrist to the ankle on each side. When the limbs are extended, the patagium creates a gliding surface. Glides of **20-50 meters** between trees. Steering via the flattened tail.
- **Eyes:** ENORMOUS. Large dark eyes adapted for nocturnal vision. The most prominent feature of the face.
- **Tail:** Flattened dorsoventrally (top to bottom), used as a rudder during glides.

### Pelage

- **Body:** Soft, dense, silky fur. Cinnamon-brown to olive-brown above. Distinctly softer and paler than the Douglas Squirrel.
- **Belly:** White to cream. Clean white underside -- contrast with the Douglas Squirrel's orange-russet belly.
- **Patagium:** Furred on both surfaces. Visible as loose skin along the flanks when the animal is at rest.

## THE SOUND: HIGH THIN "TSEET" CONTACT CALL -- 83rd ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Nearly Ultrasonic Call

The Northern Flying Squirrel's vocalization is a **high, thin, barely audible "tseet" contact call** -- a nearly ultrasonic note used for social coordination in the nocturnal canopy [1, 2, 3]:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 8,000-20,000+ Hz. At the upper edge of human hearing. Many humans cannot hear it at all.
- **Duration:** 0.05-0.15 seconds per note. Very brief.
- **Structure:** Single thin notes. "Tseet." Brief and soft. No sustained vocalization.
- **Amplitude:** **E=3** -- very soft. Audible only at close range and only to listeners with good high-frequency hearing.
- **Function:** CONTACT CALLS between individuals in the nocturnal canopy. Social coordination in darkness. Not territorial aggression.

### Why E=3

1. **Nearly ultrasonic:** At the edge of human hearing. Many people cannot hear it.
2. **Very soft:** Low amplitude even at close range.
3. **Brief:** Short contact notes, not sustained.
4. **Nocturnal context:** The call exists in the silence of the night forest. Even the presence of vocalization warrants E=3, not E=1 or E=2.

## ECOLOGY: THE NOCTURNAL OLD-GROWTH SPECIALIST

### Habitat: Old-Growth -- Cavity Dependent

The Northern Flying Squirrel is **old-growth dependent** [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Forest types:** Old-growth coniferous forest. Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, western red cedar. Requires large-diameter trees with cavities.
- **Nesting:** Tree cavities. Uses woodpecker cavities (especially Pileated Woodpecker, degree 54) and natural cavities in large old-growth trees.
- **Significance:** Shares old-growth dependence with Pacific Wren (degree 1) and Pileated Woodpecker (degree 54). Old-growth logging is the primary threat.

### The Patagium: First Gliding Mammal

The Northern Flying Squirrel is the engine's **first gliding mammal** [1, 2, 3]:

- **Patagium:** Furred skin membrane from wrist to ankle.
- **Glide distance:** 20-50 meters between trees. Some glides exceed 50 meters.
- **Steering:** Flattened tail acts as rudder. Wrist cartilage (styliform process) extends the patagium for better control.
- **Landing:** Approaches target tree at an upward angle, absorbing impact with all four limbs.

### Diet: The Truffle Specialist

The Northern Flying Squirrel is a **truffle specialist** [1, 2, 4, 5]:

- **Truffles:** Hypogeous (underground) fungi are the primary food source, especially in winter and spring.
- **Mutualism:** The squirrel disperses truffle spores through fecal pellets, inoculating forest soil with mycorrhizal fungi essential for tree growth. **Truffle mutualism** -- the squirrel needs the truffles, the trees need the mycorrhizae, the fungi need the squirrel for dispersal.
- **American Marten connection (degree 65):** The Marten also eats truffles and disperses spores. Both Douglas Squirrel (80) and Northern Flying Squirrel (82) serve as truffle dispersers -- and as Marten prey. The truffle-predator-prey network across the Sciuridae octet.
- **Other foods:** Lichens, mushrooms, seeds, insects, bird eggs.

### Predators: The Spotted Owl Connection

The Northern Flying Squirrel's most significant predator is already in the engine [1, 2, 4]:

- **Northern Spotted Owl** (SPS-004) -- the **NORTHERN SPOTTED OWL PREYS ON FLYING SQUIRRELS**. This is a **key ecological link**: both are old-growth dependent, both are nocturnal, the owl depends on the squirrel as a primary prey item. The conservation fates of both species are intertwined -- old-growth loss threatens both.
- **American Marten** (degree 65) -- arboreal predator. Hunts flying squirrels in the canopy.
- **Great Horned Owl** -- nocturnal canopy predator.
- **Northern Goshawk** -- forest-interior raptor.
