### A New Family, An Ancient Predator

The Felidae's entrance at degree 212 is a taxonomic milestone:

- **Felidae defined.** The cat family includes 41 living species, from the tiger (largest) to the rusty-spotted cat (smallest). All felids share key adaptations: retractable claws, binocular vision, obligate carnivore diet, and solitary hunting strategies. The family is defined by predation efficiency.

Also known as mountain lion, puma, catamount, and panther -- the cougar holds the Guinness record for the animal with the most common names in English. This linguistic abundance reflects the cougar's vast range: all of the Americas, from the Yukon to Patagonia.

## THE SCREAM -- TERROR IN THE FOREST

### The Most Disturbing Sound in the Woods

The cougar scream is legendary in PNW folklore:

- **The scream.** A high-pitched, drawn-out, piercing wail -- "AAAAEEEEIII" -- lasting 1-3 seconds. Frequency range: 500-2,500 Hz, with overtones reaching higher. The scream is produced primarily by females in estrus (breeding season) as a long-distance mate attraction call. But its effect on human observers is visceral: the sound is often described as "a woman screaming" or "a child in agony."
- **Estrus function.** Female cougars scream to advertise sexual receptivity across their large territories (50-150 km2 for females, 150-400 km2 for males). Because cougars are solitary and territories are vast, the scream must carry long distances. The piercing quality is an adaptation for propagation through dense forest canopy.
- **The terror response.** The cougar scream triggers a primal fear response in humans. This is not cultural conditioning -- it appears to be innate. The scream's frequency range overlaps with human vocal distress frequencies, and the drawn-out, wavering quality mimics a human scream. Hikers and campers who hear the cougar scream often report "freezing" -- the ancient prey response.
- **Other vocalizations.** Beyond the scream, cougars produce: growls (low, sustained, threat), hisses (sharp, close-range warning), chirps and whistles (mother-kitten communication, surprisingly bird-like), and purrs (contentment, the cougar is the largest cat that can purr). But the scream is the signature sound.
- **Rarity of the sound.** Despite its fame, the cougar scream is rarely heard. Cougars are cryptic, solitary, and mostly silent. Most PNW residents will never hear a cougar scream in the wild. Those who do don't forget it.

## THE APEX LAND PREDATOR

### Solitary, Cryptic, Powerful

The cougar's ecological role is defined by solitary apex predation:

- **Apex status.** In the PNW, the cougar is the largest land predator where wolves are absent (which is most of western Washington). Where wolves have returned, cougars and wolves share apex status with different strategies: wolves hunt in packs, cougars hunt alone. Different solutions to the same problem.
- **Ambush predator.** Cougars are ambush specialists. They stalk prey (primarily deer and elk) using cover, then launch from concealment with a powerful sprint and lethal neck bite. The kill is quick -- a cervical dislocation or suffocation. The cougar does not chase. It waits, stalks, and strikes.
- **Solitary existence.** Adult cougars are solitary except during mating. A male's territory may overlap with several females' territories, but adults actively avoid each other outside of breeding. The scream -- the estrus call -- exists precisely because these animals are so dispersed that acoustic communication is the only way to find each other.
- **Ghost cat.** The cougar's cryptic behavior is legendary. These are large animals (50-100 kg, 1.5-2.4 m nose to tail) that move through human-populated areas largely unseen. Motion-capture cameras have documented cougars in suburban backyards, urban parks, and commuter trails -- all without the residents' knowledge. The ghost cat.
- **Human-cougar interface.** Washington has an estimated 2,000+ cougars. Human encounters are increasing as development pushes into cougar habitat. Fatal attacks are extremely rare but generate enormous public attention. The cougar's ability to exist near humans without being seen creates a "proximity fear" -- knowing the predator is there but never seeing it.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Cougar Scream

**Estrus Scream (Mate Attraction):**
- **Frequency:** 500-2,500 Hz fundamental. Overtones to 4+ kHz.
- **Character:** Piercing, drawn-out, wavering wail. "AAAAEEEEIII." Almost human-sounding.
- **Duration:** 1-3 seconds per scream. May repeat at 10-30 second intervals.
- **Context:** Female in estrus. Breeding season (December-March in PNW). Long-distance mate attraction.
- **Carry distance:** 1-3 km through forest. Piercing quality cuts through canopy.

**Growl (Threat/Dominance):**
- **Frequency:** 100-500 Hz. Low, sustained.
- **Character:** Deep rumbling growl. Warning to other predators or humans.

**Chirp/Whistle (Mother-Kitten):**
- **Frequency:** 1,000-3,000 Hz.
- **Character:** Surprisingly bird-like. Short chirps and whistles. Close-range family communication.

**Purr (Contentment):**
- **Frequency:** 25-50 Hz. Subsonic.
- **Character:** The cougar is the largest cat that can purr. Low rumble. Contentment or self-soothing.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=7. Piercing. Designed for forest penetration over vast territories. The apex voice of the Felidae. Same energy as the wolf howl (210) but utterly different strategy: the wolf fills space, the cougar cuts through it.
