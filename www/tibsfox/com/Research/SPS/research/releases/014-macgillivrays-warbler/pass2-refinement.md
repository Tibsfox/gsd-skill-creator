### Repetition With Resolution: The Minimal Narrative and the Reflected Signal

Pass 1 documented the MacGillivray's Warbler's song as the engine's first species with an explicit narrative arc: repeated identical elements (churry-churry-churry) followed by a contrasting resolution (sweet). Pass 2 develops the repetition-resolution principle and connects it to Echo 1's passive reflection and the Portuguese man o' war's colonial integration.

Echo 1 received a signal (the uplink from a ground station) and returned a transformed version of it (the downlink reflected off the balloon's surface). The transmitted signal was the setup — the churry, repeated across the distance between ground station and satellite. The reflected signal was the resolution — the sweet, arriving at the second ground station as confirmation that the communication path worked. The uplink repeats (transmit, transmit, transmit). The downlink resolves (received, changed, confirmed). The MacGillivray's Warbler's song structure — A-A-A-B — is the acoustic version of the passive-satellite communication loop: repeated transmission followed by a single transformed return.

The Portuguese man o' war's colonial structure offers a second resonance. The colony's specialized polyps perform repeated functions — the tentacles sting repeatedly, the digestive polyps process food repeatedly, the float maintains buoyancy continuously. But the colony's integration — the fact that these repeated functions are coordinated into a single organism — is the resolution. Repetition of individual parts produces the setup. Integration of those parts into a functioning whole produces the resolution. The churry is the individual polyp. The sweet is the colony.

The degree-13/degree-14 transition maps this progression precisely:

| Degree | Species | Song Structure | Principle |
|--------|---------|---------------|-----------|
| 13 | Hutton's Vireo | A-A-A-A-A... | Repetition without resolution |
| **14** | **MacGillivray's Warbler** | **A-A-A-B** | **Repetition WITH resolution** |

The vireo persists without arriving. The warbler persists AND arrives. The addition of the B element — the contrasting terminal syllable, the "sweet" — transforms the song from an endless loop into a narrative with a beginning, middle, and end. This is the smallest possible musical narrative: statement × N, then conclusion × 1. Setup, then punchline. The warbler has invented the joke structure, the essay structure, the sonata structure — at minimum scale.

### The Second Neotropical Migrant: Dense Shrub as Acoustic Hideout

The MacGillivray's Warbler is the engine's second neotropical migrant (after the Pacific-slope Flycatcher at degree 6) — present May through September, wintering in Mexico and Central America. The seasonal voice returns to the engine after an eight-degree absence of migratory species. The flycatcher occupied riparian alder. The warbler occupies dense shrub — a different habitat within the same seasonal-presence category.

The dense-shrub habitat is acoustically significant. The warbler sings from inside dense vegetation — not from an exposed perch like the thrush or an open branch like the flycatcher. The sound must penetrate through multiple layers of leaves, branches, and stems before reaching the listener. This acoustic environment favors the warbler's song structure: the buzzy, harmonically rich "churry" syllables penetrate dense vegetation better than pure tones (broadband energy is more robust against scattering), while the cleaner, lower-frequency "sweet" carries further through open air once it clears the shrub layer. The two-part structure is acoustically optimized for the habitat: the churry penetrates the shrub, the sweet carries beyond it.

Echo 1's communication model also involved signal penetration through an attenuating medium — the ionosphere, which absorbs and refracts radio signals depending on frequency, time of day, and solar activity. The signal had to pass through the ionosphere twice (up to the balloon and back down). The choice of transmission frequency was optimized for ionospheric penetration — the same principle as the warbler's song being optimized for shrub penetration. Both systems face a propagation medium that attenuates the signal, and both optimize their signal structure for maximum penetration through that medium.

### First Parulidae: The Warbler Family Enters the Engine

The MacGillivray's Warbler is the engine's first representative of the New World warbler family (Parulidae) — the most species-rich family of North American landbirds, with approximately 120 species. This is a significant taxonomic addition. The engine has documented Ardeidae (heron), Troglodytidae (wren), Turdidae (thrushes × 2), Paridae (chickadees × 2), Strigidae (owl), Sittidae (nuthatch), Tyrannidae (flycatcher), Regulidae (kinglet), Aegithalidae (bushtit), Emberizidae (junco), Vireonidae (vireo), Fringillidae (siskin) — fourteen families across fourteen degrees. Parulidae at degree 14 is the fifteenth family. The engine is steadily sampling the PNW avifauna's taxonomic breadth.

The MacGillivray's / Mourning Warbler species pair (west/east sibling species) extends the engine's within-genus comparison thread — though the Mourning Warbler is not in the engine, the pair's existence documents the geographic speciation pattern (species pairs separated by the Great Plains / continental divide) that parallels the Oregon Junco's lumping/splitting problem at degree 11. Western and eastern forms, recently diverged, partially hybridizing at the margins — the continent's geographic structure producing species pairs through vicariance.
