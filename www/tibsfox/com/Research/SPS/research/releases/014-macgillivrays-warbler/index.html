MacGillivray’s Warbler

Geothlypis tolmiei · Family Parulidae (New World Warblers)
Churry-Churry-Churry-Sweet · Dense Shrub · Summer Migrant · Energy 2
A voice from inside a salmonberry thicket. Churry-churry-churry-sweet. You cannot see the singer — the shrub is too dense, the bird too low, the leaves too thick. But the song is unmistakable: three harsh syllables building anticipation, then one clear note that resolves the tension. Setup and payoff. Question and answer. The smallest possible story, told from a hiding place.

Taxonomy — Audubon’s Gift to a Scotsman

The MacGillivray’s Warbler is a small passerine of the family Parulidae (New World warblers), the first representative of this family in the engine. Named by John James Audubon for his friend William MacGillivray (1796–1852), a Scottish ornithologist who never visited North America. The scientific epithet tolmiei honors William Fraser Tolmie, a physician at Fort Nisqually on Puget Sound who collected the type specimen. Two Scotsmen, one bird, and the tangled colonial history of PNW natural history.

Formerly placed in genus Oporornis, molecular phylogenetics reclassified the MacGillivray’s into Geothlypis (with the Common Yellowthroat), revealing the old genus was polyphyletic. At 4.7–5.3 inches and 9–13 grams, the male shows a distinctive slate-gray hood, olive-green back, bright yellow belly, and conspicuous broken white eye arcs — crescents above and below the eye, not a complete ring.

The East/West Species Pair

The MacGillivray’s Warbler (west) and Mourning Warbler (east) are sibling species — nearly identical in plumage, divided by the Great Plains. Distinguished by range, eye arcs (MacGillivray’s has distinct arcs; Mourning typically lacks them), and subtle vocal differences. They hybridize where ranges meet.

Bioacoustics — The Churry-Sweet

Repetition With Resolution

The MacGillivray’s Warbler’s song is a distinctive two-part structure: 3–5 identical “churry” syllables (buzzy, rising, 3.5–5.5 kHz) followed by a single contrasting “sweet” (clear, descending, 2–3.5 kHz). Total duration approximately 1.5–2.5 seconds. The song contains an explicit narrative arc: beginning (repeated churry), anticipation (building through repetition), and resolution (the sweet).

The Minimal Narrative: A-A-A-B

This is the first species in the engine whose song contains an explicit narrative arc — a beginning, middle, and end within a single utterance. Where the Hutton’s Vireo (degree 13°) repeated A-A-A-A-A… without resolution, the warbler repeats A-A-A-B — the same repetition, with a contrasting terminal element that resolves the phrase.

Dense Shrub — The Hidden Singer

Behavioral Invisibility

The MacGillivray’s Warbler is one of the most skulking birds in the PNW. It sings from concealed perches inside dense shrub thickets — salal, salmonberry, thimbleberry, snowberry. You hear it far more than you see it. The bird has distinctive plumage (gray hood, yellow belly, eye arcs) but chooses concealment.

Compare to the Pacific-slope Flycatcher (degree 6°): the flycatcher must be heard because Empidonax all look alike (morphological crypticity). The warbler could be seen but refuses (behavioral invisibility). Imposed vs. chosen. In music: the session musician, the ghost writer, the producer behind the board — artists who let the work carry the identity while the maker stays hidden.

The dense shrub layer — salal, salmonberry, thimbleberry, snowberry, willow — is a disturbance-dependent habitat. Fire, selective logging, and windthrow create the dense regrowth the warbler requires. Complete fire suppression allows canopy closure, reducing MacGillivray’s habitat. The species exists in the dynamic space between disturbance and maturity — the successional stage where the thickets are thick and the canopy has not yet closed.

Music Theory Mapping

Repetition-resolution. The warbler’s churry-churry-churry-sweet is the smallest possible musical narrative: setup followed by payoff. The repeated churry syllables build tension through anticipation. The sweet releases it through contrast. Where the vireo (degree 13°) gave us the ostinato — the loop that never resolves — the warbler takes the same repetition and adds a destination. The loop becomes a phrase. The ostinato becomes a cadence.

Ternary form (AAB). The AAAB structure maps to ternary form at its smallest scale. In the 32-bar AABA form (“Over the Rainbow,” “I Got Rhythm”), the A section establishes a theme, the B section (the bridge) provides contrast. The warbler compresses this: multiple A statements followed by a single B. The most compressed version of the most common song form in Western popular music.

Tension-release. The fundamental harmonic/melodic principle. The churry syllables are harmonically tense: rising, buzzy, unresolved. The sweet is the cadence: descending, clear, resolved. Dominant to tonic. Dissonance to consonance. Every cadence in every piece of tonal music follows this trajectory.

Concealment as performance choice. The hidden singer maps to the session musician, the ghost writer, the film composer — artists whose work IS the presence, whose visibility is irrelevant or deliberately withheld. The MacGillivray’s Warbler could perch in the open. It does not. It lets the song do the work.
