## Taxonomy: Audubon's Gift to a Scotsman

The MacGillivray's Warbler (*Geothlypis tolmiei*) is a small passerine of the family **Parulidae** (New World warblers), order Passeriformes. The bird carries two names from two men and a taxonomic history that reflects decades of systematic reclassification. The common name honors **William MacGillivray (1796--1852)**, a Scottish ornithologist and natural historian who never set foot in North America. **John James Audubon** named the bird for MacGillivray in 1839 as a gesture of friendship and professional respect -- MacGillivray had edited and contributed anatomical descriptions to Audubon's *Ornithological Biography*. The scientific epithet *tolmiei* honors **William Fraser Tolmie (1812--1886)**, a Scottish physician and fur trader for the Hudson's Bay Company at Fort Nisqually on Puget Sound, who collected the type specimen. Two Scotsmen, one bird, and the tangled colonial history of Pacific Northwest natural history collection.

**The genus shuffle.** The MacGillivray's Warbler was long placed in the genus *Oporornis* alongside the Mourning Warbler (*Oporornis philadelphia*), Connecticut Warbler (*Oporornis agilis*), and Kentucky Warbler (*Oporornis formosus*). Molecular phylogenetic work in the early 2000s dismantled *Oporornis*, revealing it to be polyphyletic -- its members were not each other's closest relatives. The MacGillivray's and Mourning Warblers were reassigned to *Geothlypis*, the genus of the Common Yellowthroat, reflecting their true evolutionary relationships. The Connecticut and Kentucky Warblers ended up in their own positions. Taxonomy is revisionist history written by DNA.

**Physical description.** Small: **4.7--5.3 inches** (12--13.5 cm), **9--13 grams**, wingspan approximately **7.5 inches** (19 cm). Males are slate-gray on the head and upper breast forming a distinctive **dark hood**, olive-green on the back, bright yellow on the belly and undertail coverts, with conspicuous **broken white eye arcs** (crescents above and below the eye, not a complete ring). Females and immatures are paler, with a lighter gray hood blending to grayish-olive. The overall impression: a small, round, heavy-bodied warbler with disproportionately long pink legs, a short tail, and a tendency to stay hidden in dense vegetation. You will hear this bird long before you see it, and you may never see it at all.

**The East/West species pair.** The MacGillivray's Warbler and the **Mourning Warbler** (*Geothlypis philadelphia*) are one of North American ornithology's most instructive **East/West species pairs** -- sibling species that divided the continent between them. Nearly identical in plumage (both have gray hoods, olive backs, yellow bellies), they are differentiated primarily by range (MacGillivray's west of the Great Plains, Mourning east), subtle eye markings (MacGillivray's has distinct white eye arcs; the Mourning Warbler typically lacks them or has very faint ones), and vocal differences. Where the ranges approach each other in the western Great Plains and boreal zones of Alberta and British Columbia, they occasionally hybridize. They are each other's closest relatives -- recently separated by geographic isolation, diverging slowly. The same bird, in two rooms. The same song, with different lyrics.

**Parulidae context.** The New World warblers (Parulidae) are a family of approximately 120 species, mostly small, insectivorous, and often brightly colored. They are the backbone of North American landbird migration -- dozens of species pour through the continent each spring and fall. PNW Parulidae include the Yellow Warbler, Yellow-rumped Warbler, Wilson's Warbler, Orange-crowned Warbler, Townsend's Warbler, and the MacGillivray's. This is the **first Parulidae representative** in the 360 Species engine. The most species-rich warbler family in the Americas makes its entrance at degree 14, through one of its most secretive members.

## Bioacoustics -- THE CHURRY-SWEET: Repetition With Resolution

### The Song Structure

The MacGillivray's Warbler's song is a distinctive **two-part structure**: a series of **3--5 identical syllables** ("churry" or "chuh-ree") followed by a **contrasting terminal syllable** that drops in pitch and changes in quality ("sweet" or "swee"). The full song: *churry-churry-churry-churry-sweet*. Duration approximately **1.5--2.5 seconds**. Frequency range roughly **2--6 kHz**, with the churry syllables occupying the higher portion and the sweet dropping lower.

The structure is immediately distinctive because it contains two fundamentally different acoustic elements:

1. **The repeated element (churry):** A buzzy, slightly harsh two-note syllable, rising in inflection, delivered at regular intervals. Frequency centered around **3.5--5.5 kHz**. Each churry is acoustically identical to the previous one. This is repetition in the Hutton's Vireo sense -- the same unit, over and over, without variation.

2. **The resolution element (sweet):** A cleaner, more tonal, descending note that breaks the pattern. Lower in pitch (**2--3.5 kHz**), smoother in timbre, longer in duration. The sweet is the structural opposite of the churry: where the churry is buzzy and rising, the sweet is clear and falling. Where the churry repeats, the sweet occurs exactly once. The resolution.

### The Minimal Narrative: Setup and Payoff

This is the engine's first species whose song contains an explicit **narrative arc** -- a beginning, a middle, and an end within a single utterance. The vireo at degree 13 repeated without resolution. The warbler at degree 14 repeats AND resolves. The structural difference is profound:

- **Degree 13** (Hutton's Vireo): A-A-A-A-A-A... (ostinato -- repetition without end, without variation, without resolution. The phrase never arrives anywhere. It persists.)
- **Degree 14** (MacGillivray's Warbler): A-A-A-B (repetition with resolution. The repeated element builds anticipation. The contrasting element releases it. The phrase ARRIVES.)

This is **ternary form at minimum scale**. In classical music, ternary form (ABA or AABA) is the fundamental structural principle of statement-contrast-return. The warbler's song reduces this to its simplest possible expression: repeated statement (A-A-A) followed by a single contrast (B). There is no return to A because the song is complete -- the B resolves the tension that the A's accumulated. The smallest possible narrative: setup, then punchline. Anticipation, then fulfillment. Question asked three times, answered once.

### The Repetition-Resolution Principle

Where the Hutton's Vireo (degree 13) embodied **pure repetition** -- the ostinato, the loop, the signal that never resolves -- the MacGillivray's Warbler embodies **repetition with resolution**. This is the most fundamental structural principle in Western music:

**Tension and release.** The repeated churry syllables build tension through anticipation. Each repetition says "this is not the end." The sweet releases the accumulated tension by introducing contrast -- a different pitch, a different timbre, a different direction. The listener's expectation (more of the same) is broken by something new. The break IS the resolution.

**The 12-bar blues parallel.** The 12-bar blues form follows this exact structure: 4 bars of the I chord (repetition, establishment of the tonal center), 2 bars of the IV chord (first contrast, first departure), return to I, then V-IV-I (final contrast and resolution). The churry-churry-churry-sweet compresses this arc into seconds. The churry IS the I chord. The sweet IS the departure-and-resolution. The same narrative, at two different time scales.

**Call and response.** In its simplest form, call-and-response is a repeated element (the call) answered by a contrasting element (the response). West African vocal traditions, gospel music, blues, and jazz all build on this foundation. The warbler's song is call-and-response with itself: it asks the question (churry-churry-churry) and provides the answer (sweet). A soloist performing both parts.

## Dense Shrub: The Hidden Singer

### Behavioral Invisibility

The MacGillivray's Warbler is one of the most **skulking** birds in the Pacific Northwest avifauna. It inhabits **dense shrubby undergrowth** -- thickets of salal, salmonberry, thimbleberry, snowberry, willow, and young conifers, typically at the edges of forest clearings, along streams, in regenerating logged areas, and in mountain meadow margins. It forages low (usually below **6 feet / 2 meters**), hopping on or near the ground, gleaning insects from leaf litter and low branches. It sings from concealed perches within the shrub layer, rarely exposing itself.

The result: **you hear MacGillivray's Warblers far more than you see them.** The churry-churry-churry-sweet rings out from dense thickets throughout the PNW summer, but the singer remains invisible -- a voice without a body. Birders often spend entire field days hearing MacGillivray's songs without ever getting a clear visual. The bird CAN be seen (it is not morphologically cryptic the way Empidonax flycatchers are), but it **chooses concealment**. Behavioral invisibility, not morphological invisibility.

### Concealment vs. Crypticity: The Flycatcher Comparison

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher at degree 6 was the engine's first species whose identity depended primarily on voice rather than visual field marks. The Empidonax flycatchers are so physically similar that they MUST be heard for reliable identification -- **morphological crypticity**. The MacGillivray's Warbler presents a different problem: it is visually distinctive (the gray hood, the yellow belly, the eye arcs are diagnostic), but it **refuses to be seen**. This is **behavioral invisibility** -- the bird has field marks; it just does not show them. It prefers to be a voice.

The distinction matters. The flycatcher at degree 6 had no choice -- it was born into a genus of near-identical siblings. The warbler at degree 14 has a choice -- it could perch in the open, show its distinctive hood, be identified by sight. It does not. It sings from inside the thicket and lets the song carry its identity. Concealment as performance choice. The hidden singer who could reveal itself but does not.

In music: the **session musician**, the **ghost writer**, the **unseen performer**. The studio guitarist whose riff is on a hundred records but whose face is unknown. The producer who shapes the sound but stays behind the board. The songwriter whose songs are famous but whose name is not. These are artists who let the work -- the sound -- carry the identity, while the maker stays hidden. The MacGillivray's Warbler is the ghost writer of the PNW shrub layer.

## The Second Neotropical Migrant

### Seasonal Presence

The MacGillivray's Warbler is a **neotropical migrant** -- it breeds in the Pacific Northwest during the summer months (arriving late May, departing by September) and winters in Mexico and Central America, from the highlands of Sonora and Jalisco south to Panama. It is the **second neotropical migrant** in the engine, after the Pacific-slope Flycatcher at degree 6. The pattern:

| Species | Degree | Season | Migration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pacific-slope Flycatcher | 6 | Spring-fall | Neotropical migrant (Mexico/Central America) |
| **MacGillivray's Warbler** | **14** | **Summer** | **Neotropical migrant (Mexico to Panama)** |

Twelve of the first fourteen species in the engine are **residents** -- year-round fixtures of the PNW soundscape. Only two are seasonal visitors who appear for the breeding season and vanish. This ratio reflects reality: the PNW's resident bird community is dominated by species that stay through the cold, wet winters. The summer migrants add seasonal layers to the soundscape -- voices that appear in May, sing through June and July, and fade in August. The MacGillivray's Warbler is a summer sound. It is not a permanent part of the forest. It arrives, breeds, and leaves. The churry-churry-churry-sweet is a seasonal phenomenon, like the wildflowers that bloom in the same clearings where the warbler sings.

### East/West Species Pair

The MacGillivray's/Mourning Warbler pair is one of many **East/West species pairs** in North American ornithology -- closely related species that divided the continent along the Great Plains or the Rocky Mountain crest. Others include:

- Bullock's Oriole (west) / Baltimore Oriole (east) -- formerly lumped as "Northern Oriole"
- Black-headed Grosbeak (west) / Rose-breasted Grosbeak (east)
- Spotted Towhee (west) / Eastern Towhee (east) -- formerly lumped as "Rufous-sided Towhee"
- Steller's Jay (west) / Blue Jay (east)

These pairs tell the same evolutionary story: a common ancestor that was split by the geographic barrier of the continental interior, diverging in isolation on each side. The MacGillivray's and Mourning Warblers are an early chapter in this process -- so recently separated that they still hybridize in the narrow contact zone. Given enough time, the divergence will deepen. For now, they are the same bird in two dialects.

## Music Theory Mapping

### Repetition-Resolution: The Minimal Narrative

The warbler's churry-churry-churry-sweet is the smallest possible musical narrative: **setup followed by payoff**. In screenwriting: setup and punchline. In rhetoric: premise and conclusion. In music: tension and resolution. The structure A-A-A-B is the irreducible form of the narrative arc. You cannot have a story with fewer parts. One element establishes a pattern. A second element breaks it. That is the story.

The vireo at degree 13 gave us **pure repetition** -- the loop that never resolves, the signal that persists indefinitely. The warbler at degree 14 takes that same repetition and **adds a destination**. The repeated element is no longer the signal; it is the preparation for the signal. The sweet is the signal. The churry syllables are the approach; the sweet is the arrival. Repetition WITH purpose.

### Ternary Form (AAB / AAAB)

The AAAB structure maps to **ternary form** at its smallest scale. In full ternary form (ABA), the A section establishes a theme, the B section provides contrast, and the return to A creates closure. The warbler compresses this: multiple A statements followed by a single B. There is no return because the B provides closure. The contrast IS the ending.

This structure pervades popular music. The **32-bar AABA form** (the standard form of Tin Pan Alley songs and jazz standards) follows the same principle at a larger scale: establish a theme (A), repeat it (A), introduce contrast (B, the "bridge"), return to the theme (A). "Over the Rainbow," "I Got Rhythm," "Body and Soul" -- all AABA. The warbler's churry-churry-churry-sweet is AABA without the return: AAB. The most compressed version of the most common song form in Western popular music.

### Tension-Release: The Fundamental Harmonic Principle

Tension-release is the engine that drives all tonal music. Dissonance resolves to consonance. Dominant resolves to tonic. Preparation resolves to cadence. The churry syllables are the preparation -- buzzy, rising, harmonically tense. The sweet is the cadence -- clear, descending, tonally resolved. Every cadence in every piece of tonal music follows this same trajectory: tension built through harmonic distance, released through harmonic arrival.

### Concealment as Performance Choice

The hidden singer who sings from inside the thicket, never showing itself, maps to performance traditions where the maker is invisible:

- **The ghost writer** who pens hit songs under another artist's name
- **The session musician** who plays on hundreds of records and is credited on none
- **The film composer** whose score shapes the audience's emotions while they attribute their feelings to the actors
- **The producer** (Lee "Scratch" Perry, Brian Eno, Phil Spector) who IS the sound but is not the face
- **The Foley artist** who creates the sonic world of a film from behind a curtain

The MacGillivray's Warbler could perch on an exposed branch. It has distinctive plumage. It chooses not to. It lets the song do the work. The sound carries the identity. The maker stays hidden.

## Conservation Status

The MacGillivray's Warbler is listed as **Least Concern** by the IUCN, with a broad breeding range across western North America and an estimated population of approximately **8--12 million** (Partners in Flight 2020). The population trend is **stable to slightly declining**, with localized losses attributed to habitat changes.

**Vulnerability factors.** The primary threats are **loss of dense shrubby habitat** through fire suppression (which allows canopy closure, shading out the understory), forest maturation (old growth has less dense shrub than regenerating forest), urbanization in lowland areas, and overgrazing on public lands that degrades shrub structure. Conversely, **timber harvest and forest disturbance** can create temporary MacGillivray's Warbler habitat -- the species thrives in the dense shrubby regrowth that follows logging or fire, making it an early-successional specialist in many parts of its range.

**The paradox of disturbance.** Unlike many species in the engine that are threatened by habitat loss, the MacGillivray's Warbler depends on **habitat disturbance** -- not destruction, but the kind of disturbance that creates and maintains dense shrub layers. Fire, selective logging, windthrow, and avalanche all produce the shrubby regrowth the warbler requires. Complete fire suppression, which saves mature forest, can reduce MacGillivray's habitat by allowing canopy closure. The species exists in the dynamic space between disturbance and maturity -- the successional stage where the thickets are thick and the canopy has not yet closed.

**Wintering ground threats.** As a neotropical migrant, the MacGillivray's Warbler faces threats on its wintering grounds in Mexico and Central America: deforestation, agricultural conversion, and habitat degradation in the highland forests and shrublands where it overwinters. The species is vulnerable to threats in two hemispheres -- a problem shared by all neotropical migrants.
