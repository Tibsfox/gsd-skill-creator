## FIRST ACCIPITRIDAE -- THE HAWK/EAGLE FAMILY ENTERS

### A New Family and a New Era

The arrival of Accipitridae at degree 221 is one of the SPS engine's most significant taxonomic milestones:

- **Accipitridae defined.** The family Accipitridae is one of the three families of diurnal raptors (birds of prey active during the day), alongside Falconidae (falcons, kestrels) and Pandionidae (osprey). Accipitridae is the largest of these families -- over 250 species worldwide. It includes all hawks (buteos, accipiters), eagles, kites, harriers, and Old World vultures. In the Pacific Northwest, Accipitridae is represented by the Bald Eagle, Golden Eagle, Cooper's Hawk, Sharp-shinned Hawk, Red-tailed Hawk, Northern Harrier, and others.
- **Why this family enters now.** The carnivore mammal sequence (Felidae, Mustelidae, Ursidae, Procyonidae -- degrees 212-220) has concluded with the Ringtail (220). The engine now moves into birds of prey -- the avian apex predators that play roles analogous to the mammalian carnivores but from the sky. The transition from ground predators to aerial predators is ecologically logical.
- **The Bald Eagle as first entry.** Of all the Pacific Northwest's Accipitridae species, the Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) is the most ecologically dominant, the most culturally significant, and the most identifiable. Beginning the raptor sequence with the national bird is the correct structural choice.
- **Four Accipitridae in this arc.** Degrees 221-223 cover three Accipitridae entries: Bald Eagle contact call (221), Bald Eagle territorial call (222), and Golden Eagle (223). The Osprey (224) enters a separate family -- Pandionidae. Together, degrees 221-224 constitute the opening of the raptor era.

## THE BALD EAGLE -- *HALIAEETUS LEUCOCEPHALUS*

### North America's Most Recognizable Bird

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Haliaeetus* (sea eagles). Species: *leucocephalus* (white head). The name "bald" comes from the archaic English "balde" meaning white -- the white head, not a hairless head.
- **The sea eagles.** The genus *Haliaeetus* comprises eight species of large eagles associated with water environments: Bald Eagle (*H. leucocephalus*, North America), White-tailed Eagle (*H. albicilla*, Eurasia), Steller's Sea Eagle (*H. pelagicus*, NE Asia), African Fish Eagle (*H. vocifer*, Africa), and others. All are large, powerful, fish-hunting eagles of aquatic habitats. The Bald Eagle is the New World representative of this genus.
- **Size.** Large. Females (the larger sex in raptors) weigh 4.5-6.3 kg (10-14 lbs) with a wingspan of 1.8-2.3 m (6-7.5 ft). Males are smaller: 3-4.3 kg (6.5-9.5 lbs), similar wingspan. Among the largest raptors in North America.
- **Appearance.** Adults: white head and tail, dark brown body, yellow beak and feet. Unmistakable at close range. Juveniles: mottled brown all over, developing adult plumage gradually over 4-5 years. The iconic white head does not appear until the bird reaches sexual maturity.
- **Longevity.** Bald Eagles are long-lived birds. Wild birds typically live 15-25 years; captive birds have reached 40+ years. The investment in raising each individual is substantial.

(Sources: Bald Eagle taxonomy; Haliaeetus genus; leucocephalus etymology; size data; plumage development; longevity)

## THE CONTACT CALL -- kee-kee-kee

### A Voice That Surprises

The Bald Eagle's contact call is one of North America's most surprising wildlife sounds:

- **The sound.** The contact call is a series of 4-10 high-pitched, thin, descending notes: "kee-kee-kee-kee" or "ki-ki-ki-ki." The notes are short (0.1-0.15 seconds each) and descending in pitch. The overall impression is thin, reedy, almost plaintive -- quite unlike the powerful visual presence of the bird.
- **Frequency.** The contact call operates in the 2,000-5,000 Hz range -- relatively high for a large bird. The high frequency gives the call carrying power in forest environments, where lower frequencies would be absorbed by vegetation.
- **The Hollywood substitution.** The call that is universally associated with the Bald Eagle in film and television -- the powerful, screaming raptor cry -- is not actually the Bald Eagle's voice. It is the Red-tailed Hawk's (*Buteo jamaicensis*) call, typically the Harlan's subspecies. The Bald Eagle's actual contact call is too thin and unimpressive for Hollywood's purposes. Filmmakers substituted the Red-tailed Hawk's call decades ago, and the substitution has become the cultural "truth" about what an eagle sounds like.
- **Why this matters.** The gap between the Bald Eagle's visual power and its actual vocal thin-ness is one of the engine's most interesting cross-pair data points. Love Battery (S36 221) pairs with this sound -- both project psychedelic depth (Love Battery's fuzz-reverb haze, the eagle's majestic flight) while sounding, in strict acoustic terms, thinner than expected.
- **Contact function.** The contact call is used for: mate location and coordination (pairs maintaining awareness of each other), adult-chick communication at the nest, and low-level territorial messaging in familiar territory. It is the bird's everyday voice -- conversational, not confrontational.

## ECOLOGY -- THE WATER/FOREST EDGE

### The Pacific Northwest as Bald Eagle Habitat

- **The requirement for water.** Bald Eagles are fish specialists. The primary diet is fish -- salmon, trout, steelhead, carp, herring. The eagle hunts by watching from a perch or in flight, then diving to the water's surface to snatch fish with its talons. They do not dive into the water like the Osprey; they grab from the surface. Dead fish (particularly salmon post-spawn) are also taken opportunistically.
- **The requirement for large trees.** Bald Eagles build the largest nests of any bird in North America. The nest (called an eyrie or aerie) is a massive platform of sticks, built in the top fork of a large tree, added to each year. Mature Douglas fir and Sitka spruce are preferred nesting trees. The nest grows over years -- some Bald Eagle nests measure 2-3 m (6-10 ft) across and 1-4 m (3-13 ft) deep, weighing hundreds of kilograms.
- **PNW habitat.** The Pacific Northwest is among the finest Bald Eagle habitat in the world. The combination of: productive salmon rivers (Columbia, Skagit, Snoqualmie, Hoh, Quillayute), marine coastline (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, outer coast), old-growth and mature second-growth forest for nesting, and mild winters creates optimal conditions. Washington State hosts thousands of resident and wintering Bald Eagles.
- **Skagit Valley.** The Skagit Valley (north of Seattle) is one of North America's premier Bald Eagle congregating sites. In winter, when Skagit River salmon are spawning and dying, hundreds of Bald Eagles concentrate along the river to feed on spent fish. This is one of the PNW's signature wildlife spectacles.
- **Urban adaptation.** Bald Eagles have adapted remarkably well to proximity to human settlement. Eagles nest near suburban developments, fish in urban waterways, and perch on telephone poles. Seattle-area eagles are accustomed to human presence. The bird is simultaneously an icon of wilderness and a practical urban adapter.

(Sources: Bald Eagle fish diet; talons vs. diving; nest size; Sitka spruce nesting trees; Skagit Valley winter congregation; PNW habitat; urban adaptation)

## CONSERVATION HISTORY

### From Endangered to Recovery

The Bald Eagle's conservation story is one of North America's most important wildlife success stories:

- **The decline.** By the mid-20th century, Bald Eagle populations in the contiguous United States had collapsed. The primary cause was DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane) -- a pesticide that entered the food chain through fish. DDT accumulated in eagle body fat and caused eggshell thinning, leading to reproductive failure (crushed eggs, failed nests). By 1963, only 417 nesting pairs of Bald Eagles remained in the lower 48 states.
- **The bald eagle in the 1940 list.** The Bald Eagle Protection Act was passed in 1940, making it illegal to kill, capture, or possess a Bald Eagle. This was the first major legal protection. But it did not address DDT's effects.
- **DDT banned (1972).** The Environmental Protection Agency banned DDT use in the United States in 1972, following Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962) and widespread scientific and public concern. The DDT ban was a turning point.
- **Recovery.** After DDT's ban, Bald Eagle populations began recovering. Breeding pairs increased from a few hundred in the early 1970s to thousands by the 1990s. In 2007, the Bald Eagle was removed from the Endangered Species List -- a complete recovery arc.
- **Current status.** Least Concern (IUCN). US population estimated at 316,700 individuals (2019 survey). Washington State hosts a substantial resident population plus large wintering concentrations.

(Sources: DDT effects; eggshell thinning; 1963 population nadir; Bald Eagle Protection Act 1940; Rachel Carson Silent Spring 1962; EPA DDT ban 1972; recovery data; 2007 delisting; IUCN status)

## THE NATIONAL BIRD

### Symbol and Reality

- **National symbol.** The Bald Eagle was chosen as the symbol of the United States in 1782 by the Continental Congress. It appears on the Great Seal of the United States, on the Presidential seal, on currency, and on countless official documents and buildings. The choice was not unanimous -- Benjamin Franklin famously argued for the Wild Turkey as a "more respectable" bird, calling the Bald Eagle "a bird of bad moral character" for its tendency to steal fish from Osprey.
- **Franklin's critique.** Franklin's objection -- that the eagle is a bully, stealing prey from other birds rather than earning its meal honestly -- is ecologically accurate. Bald Eagles do kleptoparasitize Osprey frequently. The Franklin-vs-eagle debate is a useful window into what the founders valued: the eagle won because it projects power; the turkey lost because it projects domesticity.
- **Cultural weight.** No other bird in the SPS engine carries the cultural weight of the Bald Eagle. Every other bird entered the engine as a natural history subject. The Bald Eagle enters as a natural history subject AND as one of the most loaded national symbols in the world. The engine must account for both.

(Sources: National symbol history 1782; Great Seal; Benjamin Franklin Wild Turkey argument; kleptoparasitism of Osprey; cultural weight analysis)
