FIRST ACCIPITRIDAE — THE HAWK/EAGLE FAMILY ENTERS

Degree 221 is the engine's FIRST ACCIPITRIDAE. After the carnivore mammal sequence (Felidae, Mustelidae, Ursidae, Procyonidae, degrees 212-220), the engine moves into birds of prey. The Bald Eagle — America's national bird, North America's signature raptor — is the opening entry. The raptor era begins.

The Call That Surprises

The Bald Eagle's contact call — the thin, descending kee-kee-kee — surprises almost everyone who hears it for the first time. The bird is visually enormous and culturally overwhelming: the white head, the golden beak, the 7-foot wingspan. The sound is thin, reedy, almost querulous. Not powerful. Not commanding.

Acoustic Profile — Contact Call

Frequency: 2,000–5,000 Hz
Note duration: 0.1–0.15 sec each
Notes per call: 4–10 descending notes

The high frequency gives the call carrying power in forest environments. The descending pitch pattern is characteristic. Function: mate coordination, adult-chick communication, low-level territorial awareness.

"The call that Hollywood uses for the Bald Eagle is actually the Red-tailed Hawk. The eagle's real voice is too thin for cinema. The visual power and the acoustic thin-ness are in inverse proportion."

The Hollywood Substitution

One of the engine's most interesting cross-reference points: the iconic raptor scream universally associated with the Bald Eagle in film and television is not the Bald Eagle's call. It is the Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis). Filmmakers made the substitution decades ago because the eagle's actual contact call is too thin and unimpressive for cinema's purposes. The substitution has become the cultural "truth" about what an eagle sounds like.

The gap between the Bald Eagle's visual power and its actual vocal thin-ness pairs precisely with Love Battery (S36 221): a band whose psychedelic depth and fuzz-guitar density project more power than their E=5 position on the scale might suggest. Both project their presence through texture and visual/cultural weight rather than raw acoustic volume.

Pacific Northwest Habitat

The PNW is among the finest Bald Eagle habitat in the world. The combination of productive salmon rivers (Columbia, Skagit, Snoqualmie, Hoh), marine coastline (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca), and old-growth forest for nesting creates optimal conditions. Washington State hosts thousands of resident and wintering Bald Eagles.

The Skagit Valley (north of Seattle) is one of North America's premier Bald Eagle congregating sites. In winter, when Skagit River salmon are spawning and dying, hundreds of Eagles concentrate to feed on spent fish — one of the PNW's signature wildlife spectacles.

Conservation — From Crisis to Recovery

RECOVERY COMPLETE — DELISTED 2007. The Bald Eagle was nearly wiped out by DDT-induced eggshell thinning. Only 417 nesting pairs remained in the lower 48 in 1963. DDT banned 1972. Recovery followed. Delisted from Endangered Species list in 2007. Current US population: 316,700+ individuals (2019 survey). IUCN: Least Concern.

The National Bird

The Bald Eagle was designated the national symbol of the United States in 1782. Benjamin Franklin objected — he called it "a bird of bad moral character" for its tendency to steal fish from Osprey (kleptoparasitism). Franklin preferred the Wild Turkey. The eagle won because it projects power; power is what national symbols are for.

No other bird in the SPS engine carries this cultural weight. The engine must account for both the natural history (fishing, nesting, vocalizing) and the cultural history (Great Seal, Presidential seal, currency). They are not separable.
