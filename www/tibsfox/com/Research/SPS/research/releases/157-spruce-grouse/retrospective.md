*Degree 157 retrospective complete. SPRUCE GROUSE WING CLAPS at the one hundred fifty-seventh degree -- THE FOOL HEN. In the dense boreal spruce forests of northeastern Washington and the high northern Cascades, where Engelmann spruce grows in dark stands with deep needle duff and the forest floor absorbs sound like a sponge, a medium-sized grouse sits motionless on a branch, nibbling spruce needles. A hiker approaches. The bird does not move. The hiker comes closer. Still the bird sits. Within arm's reach now, the Spruce Grouse -- the "fool hen" -- continues to sit, relying on the cryptic plumage that defeats goshawks and martens but does nothing against the human who recognizes its shape. This is the quietest gamebird in the engine: its wing clap display is a brief, percussive flutter flight -- one to two seconds in the air, wing claps on descent, audible at maybe fifty meters. Its clucking is soft, intimate, a contact call meant for nearby hens and chicks. After the Wild Turkey's mile-carrying gobble, after the Dusky Grouse's subsonic booming felt in the chest, after the Ruffed Grouse's accelerating mechanical drumming, after the Sooty Grouse's ventriloquial mystery that comes from everywhere and nowhere -- the Spruce Grouse offers this: quiet wing claps and soft clucking in the dark spruce forest. The fifth Phasianidae. The coda after the quartet. From loudest to most mysterious to quietest. And across the degree pair, Shabazz Palaces returns at E=6 -- the third consecutive maximum energy, the Afrofuturist climax, the densest and most visionary music in the engine paired with the quietest and most unassuming bird. Cosmic transcendence meets boreal silence. The deep forest contains both: the palace and the fool hen, the alien transmission and the soft cluck, the future imagined and the present accepted.*
