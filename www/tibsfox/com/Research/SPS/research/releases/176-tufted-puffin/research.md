## THE ICON

### The Most Recognizable Seabird in the PNW

The Tufted Puffin is unmistakable:

- **Face:** Breeding adults have a white face contrasting with an entirely black body. The white face is framed by long, drooping golden-yellow tufts (plumes) that sweep back from behind each eye. The effect is dramatic, almost theatrical.
- **Bill:** Massive, laterally compressed, bright orange-red with olive and yellow markings at the base. One of the most striking bills in all of ornithology. The bill is actually larger in breeding season and partially shed after.
- **Overall impression:** A stocky, dark seabird with a white face, golden tufts, and an enormous colorful bill. There is nothing else like it. The puffin is immediately recognizable to birders and non-birders alike.
- **Cultural presence:** Puffins appear on postcards, t-shirts, children's books, plush toys, and nature documentaries. They are the charismatic megafauna of the seabird world. The Tufted Puffin is the Pacific counterpart to the Atlantic Puffin.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; Pacific seabird identification; engine internal analysis)

## THE CRISIS

### Over 90% Decline in Washington

The Tufted Puffin's conservation crisis in Washington is severe:

- **Historical abundance:** In the 1970s-1980s, Washington hosted significant breeding colonies -- notably on the outer coast islands and at Protection Island, Destruction Island, and the San Juan Islands.
- **Current state:** Washington's Tufted Puffin population has declined by over 90%. Many former colony sites are abandoned. Smith Island's colony collapsed from hundreds of pairs to near zero. Protection Island's puffin numbers are a fraction of historical levels.
- **San Juan Islands:** Historically, puffins bred in the San Juan Islands. They no longer do. The San Juans puffin population is functionally extinct.

### Causes

The decline is driven by multiple, interacting factors:

- **Ocean warming:** Warmer sea surface temperatures reduce the availability of forage fish (sand lance, herring, smelt) that puffins depend on to feed chicks. The prey base is collapsing.
- **Prey mismatch:** Even when fish are available, the timing may not match puffin breeding season. Climate-driven phenological mismatch.
- **Bald Eagle recovery:** The successful recovery of Bald Eagles has increased predation pressure on seabird colonies. Eagles are now common where they were once rare, and puffins are vulnerable to eagle predation at the colony surface.
- **Introduced predators:** Rats, raccoons, and mink on some islands. Predator control is critical but not always implemented.
- **Disturbance:** Human disturbance at colony sites. Boat traffic near colonies during breeding season.

### Conservation Response

- **WDFW listing:** The Tufted Puffin is listed as a State Endangered Species in Washington (2015).
- **Recovery plan:** Washington has a Tufted Puffin recovery plan. Colony monitoring. Predator control. Habitat protection.
- **Social attraction:** Experimental efforts using decoys and audio playback to attract puffins back to historical colony sites. Results mixed.
- **Research:** Ongoing monitoring of remaining colonies. Studies of foraging ecology, prey availability, and reproductive success.

(Sources: WDFW Tufted Puffin recovery plan; Hanson et al. 2019; Puget Sound seabird monitoring; conservation biology; engine internal analysis)

## E=4 -- THE DEEP COLONY GROWL

### Why E=4

The Tufted Puffin registers at E=4:

- **Deep growl:** The vocalizations are deep, grunting, growling sounds at the colony. Deeper than the Rhinoceros Auklet. Resonant. Low-frequency.
- **Colony context:** Like the Rhinoceros Auklet, the colony produces a collective sound. Individual growls combine into a colony chorus. But the Tufted Puffin's colony sounds are less continuous than the auklet's -- more intermittent, more punctuated.
- **Volume:** Moderate individually. The colony effect is present but the Tufted Puffin is less vocal than the Rhinoceros Auklet overall.
- **Nocturnal component:** Tufted Puffins also return to colonies at night (though they are more diurnal than Rhinoceros Auklets). The growling sounds occur both day and night.
- **Why E=4?** The deep growl is substantial, the colony effect is immersive, but the bird is less persistently vocal than the auklet. E=4: deep, resonant, colony-based, moderate in persistence.

## FISH CARRYING AND BREEDING ECOLOGY

### The Multi-Fish Bill

The Tufted Puffin's most famous behavior is fish carrying:

- **Multiple fish:** Puffins can carry 5-20 small fish crosswise in their bill simultaneously. The fish are held by the tongue pressed against serrations (denticles) on the upper mandible. This allows the bird to continue catching fish without losing those already held.
- **Species:** Sand lance, herring, smelt, and other small schooling fish. The prey is the crisis point -- warming oceans reduce prey availability.
- **Dive depth:** Tufted Puffins dive to 60+ meters. Deeper than both the Pigeon Guillemot and Rhinoceros Auklet. They pursue fish schools using wing-propelled diving.
- **Burrow nesting:** Like the Rhinoceros Auklet, digs burrows into island soil. 1-3 meters deep. Single egg. Both parents incubate and feed chick.
- **Chick-rearing:** Chick remains in burrow for approximately 45 days. Parents make multiple fish-delivery trips per day. The prey availability crisis means fewer fish per trip and fewer trips per day.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; seabird breeding ecology; fish-carrying studies; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Tufted Puffin

**Deep Grunting Growl:**
- **Trigger:** Colony interactions, burrow defense, mate communication, territorial.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** Deep, grunting, growling vocalizations. "Grrrrr-ah" and "uuuurr." Lower-pitched than Rhinoceros Auklet. Resonant. Intermittent.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 300-1500 Hz. Very low for a seabird. Deep and resonant.
- **Duration:** Individual growls 0.5-2 seconds. Less continuous than Rhinoceros Auklet colony chorus.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Deep and resonant individually. Colony effect present. Less persistent than auklet.

**Other Sounds:**
- Bill clacking in burrow disputes.
- Soft grunting between mates.
- Chick begging calls from within burrow.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; colony acoustics; engine internal analysis)
