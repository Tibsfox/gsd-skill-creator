*Degree 176 retrospective complete. TUFTED PUFFIN at the one hundred seventy-sixth degree -- THE ICON IN CRISIS. The PNW's most recognizable seabird. White face. Golden tufts sweeping back from the eyes. That massive, laterally compressed, bright orange-red bill. The bird on the postcards, the t-shirts, the children's books. Known by everyone. And disappearing. Washington's Tufted Puffin breeding population has declined by over 90% since the 1970s. State Endangered since 2015. Former colony sites abandoned. San Juan Islands puffins functionally extinct. The causes are multiple and compounding: ocean warming reduces the forage fish (sand lance, herring, smelt) that puffins depend on; the timing of prey availability shifts away from breeding season; the successful recovery of Bald Eagles brings new predation pressure to colonies; introduced predators devastate some islands. The most visible species faces the most severe threat. The Tufted Puffin carries 5 to 20 small fish crosswise in its massive bill, held by the tongue against mandible serrations. It dives to 60+ meters -- the deepest of the three Alcidae in the engine. But the prey is disappearing. Fewer fish per trip. Fewer trips per day. Lower chick survival. Population collapse. And the Alcidae trio is complete: Pigeon Guillemot (174, urban, stable), Rhinoceros Auklet (175, nocturnal, stable), Tufted Puffin (176, iconic, endangered). Three modes of auk life. Three consecutive degrees. The arc from urban crevice to offshore burrow to conservation crisis.
