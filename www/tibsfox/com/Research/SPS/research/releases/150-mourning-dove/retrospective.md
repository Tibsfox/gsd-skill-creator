*Degree 150 retrospective complete. MOURNING DOVE at the one hundred fiftieth degree -- THE UNIVERSAL DOVE. There is no more familiar bird sound in America than the Mourning Dove's five-note coo -- "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo" -- drifting from a power line on a summer morning, so embedded in the ambient soundscape that most people hear it every day of their lives without ever consciously registering it. The call was there when you woke up as a child. It is there now, outside your window, if you are in nearly any part of the United States. The bird that makes this sound is named for it: "mourning" for the mournful quality that early European settlers heard as grief. But behind the gentle cooing is a species of extraordinary contrasts. The Mourning Dove is America's most hunted game bird -- 20 million killed annually by hunters in 40 states, more than all duck species combined. It withstands this harvest through reproductive resilience: up to 6 broods per year, 14-day incubation, a one-month cycle from egg to fledging. And the gentle bird has a second sound: a sharp, high-pitched wing whistle on takeoff, produced by the modified ninth primary feather, that functions as an alarm signal and triggers flight in nearby doves. Gentle coo, sharp whistle. Beloved backyard bird, America's most hunted. Everywhere, invisible. The second Columbidae, the second mechanical sound in the engine, the native pigeon contrast pair complete: the Band-tailed Pigeon in the forest canopy declining by 2-3% per year; the Mourning Dove on the power line, 300-400 million strong, named for its voice, hunted for its flight, heard by everyone, known by almost no one. The familiar is always deeper.*
