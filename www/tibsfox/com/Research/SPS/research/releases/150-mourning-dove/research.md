## E=3 -- THE MOURNFUL MURMUR

### Why E=3

The Mourning Dove registers at E=3, matching the Band-tailed Pigeon:

- **Soft amplitude:** The call is quiet. Audible at 50-150 meters in still conditions. Not a broadcast call but a conversational one -- directed at mates and neighbors, not the entire forest. The softness is the defining acoustic quality.
- **Low frequency:** Fundamental frequency 350-550 Hz. Lower than most songbird calls. The low pitch contributes to the mournful quality and the perception of quietness.
- **Melancholy tonal quality:** The call sounds sad. The drooping pitch contour, the breathy tone, the minor-key resonance -- the Mourning Dove has been named for its sound, and the sound is definitionally mournful. E=3 captures the emotional restraint of sadness.
- **Ubiquity paradox:** The Mourning Dove is everywhere, calling constantly, yet most people do not consciously hear it. The call is so integrated into the ambient soundscape that it becomes invisible. E=3 reflects this background-ambient quality.

### Two Columbidae at E=3

The Band-tailed Pigeon and Mourning Dove both register at E=3 for different reasons:

- **Band-tailed (149):** E=3 because the call is deep and muted, heard from high in the forest canopy, infrequent.
- **Mourning Dove (150):** E=3 because the call is soft and mournful, heard from everywhere but noticed by no one, background ambient.

Same energy, different mechanisms. The Columbidae have a characteristic energy signature: low, soft, ambient.

## THE MOURNFUL CALL

### "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo"

The Mourning Dove's call is one of the most recognized sounds in North America:

- **Structure:** Five notes. The second note is the highest and most emphatic: "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo." The pitch contour rises slightly on the second note then descends across the remaining three. The pattern is invariable and diagnostic.
- **Frequency:** Fundamental: 350-550 Hz. Harmonics to 2000 Hz. The low fundamental gives the call its "mourning" quality.
- **Duration:** Complete five-note phrase: 2.5-3.5 seconds. Repeated with 3-8 second pauses. Males call extensively during breeding season (March-September), typically from a high, exposed perch.
- **Misidentification:** The Mourning Dove call is one of the most frequently misidentified bird sounds. People often mistake it for an owl. The low pitch, the repetitive "whoo" quality, and the dawn/dusk calling pattern all contribute to owl confusion.
- **Cultural resonance:** The call is embedded in American cultural memory. It is the sound of summer mornings, of rural landscapes, of suburban dawn. Poets, songwriters, and novelists have used the Mourning Dove's call as shorthand for melancholy, nostalgia, and peaceful solitude.
- **Why "mourning"?** The common name refers directly to the call. Early European settlers heard the cooing and interpreted it as mournful -- a bird in grief. The name has stuck for over 200 years, permanently associating the species with sadness.

### Year-Round Calling

Unlike many bird species that vocalize primarily during breeding season, the Mourning Dove calls through much of the year:

- **Breeding season (March-September):** Heavy calling. Males advertise territory and attract mates. Dawn chorus participation.
- **Fall/winter:** Reduced but not absent. Occasional cooing on warm winter days. The call marks the species' year-round presence.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Otis et al. 2008; Sibley 2014)

## THE WING WHISTLE -- Second Mechanical Sound

### A Sound Made by Feathers, Not Voice

The Mourning Dove produces a distinctive **wing whistle** on takeoff -- a high-pitched, ascending whistle created by air passing through modified primary flight feathers:

- **Mechanism:** The wing whistle is produced by the ninth primary feather, which is narrowed and tapered. During the rapid wingbeats of takeoff, air vibrates through this modified feather at high frequency, producing the distinctive whistle.
- **Function:** The wing whistle serves as an alarm signal. When a Mourning Dove flushes from the ground, the whistle alerts nearby doves to potential danger. Studies have shown that the wing whistle alone (without any vocal call) is sufficient to trigger flight in other doves.
- **Sound character:** High-pitched, ascending whistle. Duration: 0.5-1.5 seconds. Frequency: 1000-3000 Hz. Distinctly different from any vocalization. The sound is percussive and startling -- a sharp contrast to the soft cooing.

### Mechanical Sound Progression in the Engine

## AMERICA'S MOST HUNTED GAME BIRD

### 20 Million Per Year

Behind the gentle cooing is one of the most intensely hunted bird species in America:

- **Annual harvest:** Approximately 20 million Mourning Doves are killed by hunters each year in the United States. This makes it by far the most harvested migratory game bird in the country -- more than all duck species combined.
- **Hunting states:** Mourning Dove hunting is legal in 40 of 50 U.S. states. The season typically runs September-January, with bag limits of 10-15 per day.
- **Non-hunting states:** Ten states (including Washington until 2019) have historically banned Mourning Dove hunting. Washington opened its first Mourning Dove hunting season in 2019 after decades of debate.
- **Sustainability debate:** Despite the harvest, the Mourning Dove population remains large and stable. The species' high reproductive rate (up to 6 broods per year in southern states, 2-3 in the PNW) compensates for hunting mortality. Conservation biologists consider the harvest sustainable at current levels, though the ethical dimensions remain debated.
- **The paradox:** The Mourning Dove is simultaneously one of America's most beloved backyard birds and one of its most hunted game birds. Bird watchers cherish its mournful call; hunters pursue it with shotguns. The same species occupies radically different positions in American culture.

### Reproductive Strategy

The Mourning Dove's ability to sustain heavy hunting pressure reflects its extraordinary reproductive capacity:

- **Multiple broods:** 2-6 broods per year depending on latitude. The most prolific breeder among North American Columbidae.
- **Short cycle:** Incubation: 14 days. Nestling period: 12-14 days. A complete breeding cycle from egg-laying to fledging takes about one month.
- **Year-round breeding:** In southern states, Mourning Doves can breed in every month of the year. Even in the PNW, breeding extends from March through September.
- **Population resilience:** The combination of high reproductive rate, broad habitat tolerance, and adaptability to human landscapes makes the Mourning Dove one of the most resilient bird species in North America.

(Sources: USFWS Mourning Dove harvest data; Otis et al. 2008; Tomlinson et al. 1994; WDFW)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of American Mornings -- Mournful Cooing and Wing Whistle

The Mourning Dove contributes two distinct sounds to the engine -- one vocal, one mechanical:

**Vocal -- The Mournful Coo:**
- **Pattern:** "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo" -- five notes, second highest, descending
- **Frequency:** 350-550 Hz fundamental. Harmonics to 2000 Hz.
- **Duration:** 2.5-3.5 seconds per phrase. Repeated 3-8 second intervals.
- **Amplitude:** E=3. Soft. Audible 50-150 meters. Background ambient.
- **Season:** Year-round, peak March-September.
- **Perch:** Exposed high perch -- power line, dead branch, rooftop.

**Mechanical -- The Wing Whistle:**
- **Trigger:** Takeoff, especially alarm flush
- **Mechanism:** Modified 9th primary feather vibration
- **Frequency:** 1000-3000 Hz. Much higher than the coo.
- **Duration:** 0.5-1.5 seconds
- **Function:** Alarm signal. Triggers flight in nearby doves.
- **Character:** Sharp, ascending, startling. The opposite of the gentle coo.

The two sounds together define the Mourning Dove: the soft, mournful cooing from the power line, then the sharp wing whistle as it flushes. Gentle presence, sudden absence.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Coleman 2008; Hingee & Magrath 2009; engine internal analysis)
