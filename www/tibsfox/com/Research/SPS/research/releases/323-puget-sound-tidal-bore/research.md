## WHAT IS A TIDAL BORE?

### Hydraulics at the Constriction

A tidal bore is the acoustic and visual event produced when a large tidal exchange is forced through a narrow constriction:

- **The basic mechanism.** Puget Sound is a deep fjord-like estuary with a large tidal prism (the volume of water exchanged between high and low tide). At maximum tidal exchange (spring tides, when lunar and solar gravitational forces align), the volume of water that must move in or out of Puget Sound through its various openings is enormous. Where that water must pass through a narrow channel -- Deception Pass (150 meters wide), Tacoma Narrows (approximately 1,600 meters wide but very deep and fast), or the narrow passages between the San Juan Islands -- the velocity of water flow increases dramatically to pass the required volume. This velocity increase is the source of the tidal bore event.
- **Bore vs. current.** A tidal bore in the strict sense is the wave front that forms when a rapidly rising tide enters a narrow channel. In Puget Sound parlance, the term is used more broadly to describe the visible and acoustic turbulence associated with maximum tidal exchange through narrow passages -- the standing waves, whirlpools, and turbulent hydraulic features that form at Deception Pass and similar sites. Both the narrow-definition bore and the broader high-velocity tidal current produce the same acoustic signature: deep, rhythmic, sustained surge.
- **The acoustic principle.** Water moving at high velocity through a constricted channel produces sound through multiple mechanisms: (1) turbulence at the channel walls (the water-rock interface where the current meets the cliff faces or rocky shores produces broadband noise); (2) standing wave formation (waves that maintain their position in the current as the flow equals the wave propagation speed, producing rhythmic hydraulic signals); (3) subsurface acoustic radiation from the turbulent flow itself (water turbulence radiates acoustic energy both above and below the surface); (4) impact of current-driven water against exposed rocks and cliff faces.

(Sources: Puget Sound tidal prism; spring tide maximum exchange; tidal bore mechanism; Deception Pass channel 150m width; Tacoma Narrows dimensions; San Juan Islands passages; velocity increase at constriction; turbulence wall friction; standing wave formation; subsurface acoustic radiation; turbulent flow acoustics)

## DECEPTION PASS

### The Most Dramatic Tidal Bore in Washington State

Deception Pass is the engine's primary Puget Sound tidal bore site:

- **Geography.** Deception Pass is the narrow water passage that separates Whidbey Island (the largest island in Puget Sound) from Fidalgo Island to the north. The pass is approximately 150 meters wide at its narrowest point, with water depths of 20-30 meters in the main channel. The Deception Pass Bridge (State Route 20) spans the passage at approximately 50 meters above mean water level.
- **Tidal exchange.** Puget Sound tidal range is 2.5-4.5 meters (8-15 feet) depending on location and tidal cycle. The tidal range at Deception Pass is similar to the northern Puget Sound average -- approximately 3-4 meters at spring tides. The volume of water that must exchange through this 150-meter passage to fill and empty the bay and channels south of the pass (Oak Harbor, Penn Cove, Saratoga Passage) during each tidal cycle is substantial.
- **Tidal current speeds.** At maximum flood and ebb, tidal current through Deception Pass reaches 8-9 knots (4-4.5 m/s). This is among the highest tidal current velocities in Puget Sound -- comparable to Seymour Narrows in British Columbia, which is one of the most dangerous tidal channels on the Pacific coast. At 8-9 knots, the water surface in the pass is visibly turbulent: white water in the main channel, boils (upwelling columns) rising from the deep channel, whirlpools at the current boundaries, and standing waves that maintain their position in the flow.
- **The acoustic event at maximum exchange.** At maximum exchange -- approximately 2 hours before and after high or low tide at spring tides -- Deception Pass produces the full tidal bore acoustic signature: a continuous deep roar (50-500 Hz primary) from the turbulent flow, rhythmic surges as standing waves build and release, audible from the Deception Pass Bridge (50 meters above the water), and a physical vibration in the rock faces on either side of the pass. The sound carries several hundred meters from the pass on calm days.
- **Visual spectacle as acoustic indicator.** Deception Pass is one of the most popular viewpoints in Washington State -- Deception Pass State Park receives approximately 2 million visitors annually, more than any other Washington State Park. The visual drama of the maximum exchange (white water, standing waves, the tidal rip visible from the bridge) is the public face of the acoustic event the engine documents.

(Sources: Deception Pass geography; 150m channel width; 20-30m depth; Deception Pass Bridge elevation 50m; Puget Sound tidal range 2.5-4.5m; spring tide range 3-4m; Deception Pass maximum current 8-9 knots; 4-4.5 m/s current velocity; standing waves boils whirlpools; acoustic event 50-500 Hz; Deception Pass State Park 2 million annual visitors; Washington State's most visited state park)

## TACOMA NARROWS

### The Deep Surge of the Sound's Waist

Tacoma Narrows is a different kind of tidal bore site -- wider but hydraulically extreme:

- **Geography.** Tacoma Narrows is the passage where Puget Sound narrows between the Kitsap Peninsula and the mainland near Tacoma. The narrows is approximately 1,600 meters (one mile) wide -- much wider than Deception Pass -- but the Sound is deep here (40-60 meters in the main channel) and the tidal exchange is enormous. The Tacoma Narrows Bridges span this passage.
- **The 1940 bridge failure.** The first Tacoma Narrows Bridge (Galloping Gertie, opened July 1940, failed November 1940) was destroyed not by tidal current but by aerodynamic flutter in high winds -- but the resonance principles that caused the bridge failure are related to the hydraulic principles that produce tidal bore acoustics. A structure in a fluid flow (whether water or air) responds to the fluid's frequency if there is resonance between the structure's natural frequency and the vortex-shedding frequency of the fluid. The tidal bore's rhythmic surges interact with submerged structures and the channel geology through the same resonance principles.
- **Maximum current.** Tacoma Narrows tidal current reaches 5-6 knots at maximum exchange -- less than Deception Pass in absolute velocity, but sustained over a wider cross-section and with greater total volume. The acoustic output at Tacoma Narrows is deeper (lower frequency) than Deception Pass because the larger hydraulic mass resonates at lower frequencies. The deep surge character of Tacoma Narrows is the low-frequency component of the degree-323 acoustic profile.
- **The Narrows as acoustic chamber.** The surrounding topography of Tacoma Narrows -- relatively low hills on both sides, the Sound opening wider to the north and south -- means the tidal bore acoustic output is not focused into a small area as at Deception Pass. The Tacoma Narrows acoustic event is broader, more diffuse, and lower in frequency: the bass note to Deception Pass's mid-register turbulence.

## THE SAN JUAN ISLANDS PASSAGES

### Multiple Bore Sites in the Archipelago

The San Juan Islands add multiple tidal bore sites to the degree-323 acoustic catalog:

- **Current Pass.** The passage between San Juan Island and Lopez Island, with current reaching 4-5 knots at maximum. Current Pass has the characteristic standing wave and boil features of a tidal bore passage.
- **San Juan Channel.** Runs between San Juan Island and Shaw Island. Moderate current, regular tidal rips visible from the ferry route.
- **Cattle Pass.** The southern entrance to the San Juan Islands from the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Current reaches 5+ knots. Visible from False Bay on San Juan Island.
- **Rosario Strait.** The eastern passage through the San Juan Islands, between Fidalgo Island and the main San Juan group. Rosario Strait carries a significant fraction of the total tidal exchange between Puget Sound and the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Current at maximum exchange reaches 3-4 knots across a wide front.
- **The collective acoustic contribution.** The San Juan Islands passages collectively contribute to the degree-323 acoustic profile as a distributed multi-site tidal bore system. Each passage produces its own tidal bore signature; together they constitute the acoustic environment of maximum tidal exchange throughout the northern Puget Sound/San Juan Islands region.

(Sources: San Juan Islands tidal passages; Current Pass 4-5 knots; San Juan Channel tidal rips; Cattle Pass 5+ knots; False Bay San Juan Island; Rosario Strait tidal exchange; San Juan Islands distributed tidal bore system; northern Puget Sound tidal exchange volume)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=8

### Deep Rhythmic Surge

The Puget Sound tidal bore calibrates to E=8 through the combination of mass, frequency, and duration:

- **Mass of water in motion.** The tidal bore acoustic event is generated by the mass of water moving through the constriction. At Deception Pass at maximum exchange, the volumetric flow rate is approximately 5,000-10,000 cubic meters per second. Water density is 1,025 kg per cubic meter (salt water). The mass flow rate is therefore 5-10 million kilograms per second. This is a fundamentally different order of magnitude from biological acoustic events -- the mass responsible for the sound is millions of kilograms per second, not the hundreds of kilograms of a Steller Sea Lion bull or the tens of thousands of kilograms of a rock avalanche. E=8 through mass, not behavior.
- **Frequency profile.** The tidal bore acoustic spectrum covers 20-2,000 Hz:
  - Sub-bass (20-100 Hz): generated by large-scale pressure fluctuations in the water column, felt as vibration in the surrounding rock faces and in the body of an observer on the bridge or shore
  - Bass (100-300 Hz): the dominant acoustic energy from the main turbulent flow -- the deep roar that characterizes the bore at Deception Pass
  - Mid (300-1,000 Hz): turbulence at the water-rock interface, the higher-energy components of standing wave formation
  - Upper-mid (1,000-2,000 Hz): the white noise of cavitation (bubbles collapsing in the high-velocity flow) and surface turbulence
- **Duration.** The tidal bore acoustic event is sustained for hours -- not seconds (like the rock avalanche initiation) or minutes (like the rock avalanche full event), but 2-4 hours per tidal cycle at the near-maximum current conditions. The duration of the E=8 event is the tidal bore's most distinctive characteristic: a sustained, slowly changing, deep acoustic presence that cycles with the tide.
- **Rhythm.** The "deep rhythmic surge" of the degree-323 title is not simply the continuous roar of the turbulent flow -- it is the rhythmic variation within that flow as standing waves build and release, as tidal bores and back-bores alternate at the edges of the channel, and as the current strength varies with the tidal cycle. The rhythm has multiple timescales: the short-period oscillation of standing waves (5-30 seconds), the medium-period variation as the tidal current approaches and recedes from maximum (20-40 minutes), and the tidal cycle itself (approximately 6 hours).
- **The E=8 calibration.** E=8 is reached through: (1) the mass of water in motion (millions of kg/second -- exceeds any biological source); (2) the sustained duration (hours vs. seconds for biological events); (3) the low-frequency sub-bass component that produces physical body vibration; (4) the acoustic breadth (20-2,000 Hz, covering the full perceptual range). No single factor is as dramatic as the rock avalanche's single peak impact (degree 322), but the combined sustained, massive, broad-spectrum, rhythmic acoustic output justifies E=8.

## PUGET SOUND TIDAL CONTEXT

### Why Puget Sound Produces Tidal Bores

Puget Sound's physical characteristics make it an exceptionally productive tidal bore environment:

- **The fjord geometry.** Puget Sound is a glacially carved fjord system -- deep (average 62 meters, maximum 284 meters in Hood Canal), long (north-south extent approximately 160 km), and connected to the Pacific Ocean through the relatively narrow Strait of Juan de Fuca. This geometry concentrates tidal energy: the large water body must exchange a large tidal prism through a relatively narrow opening, building up current velocity at constrictions.
- **Tidal range.** Puget Sound experiences a mixed semi-diurnal tidal pattern -- two high tides and two low tides per day, with unequal heights. At Seattle, the tidal range is approximately 3.7 meters (12 feet) at mean spring tides, with extreme spring tides reaching 4.5 meters (15 feet). This range, combined with the enclosed geometry, drives the tidal bore conditions.
- **The multiple sill system.** Puget Sound has multiple sill locations -- shallow submarine ridges that further constrict tidal flow. The main basin of Puget Sound (south of the Admiralty Inlet) is effectively an interior basin whose tidal exchange must pass through the relatively shallow Admiralty Inlet sill (depth approximately 50 meters) before reaching the deep central Sound. This creates hydraulic lag effects and differential tidal timing that enhance the bore conditions at specific passages.
- **No significant river input.** Unlike many estuaries, Puget Sound receives relatively little river input compared to its tidal volume. The Puyallup, Nisqually, and Duwamish rivers contribute modest freshwater, but the Sound's exchange dynamics are dominated by tidal, not riverine, processes. The acoustic environment is therefore tidal-primary: the main acoustic driver is tidal exchange, not river turbulence.

(Sources: Puget Sound fjord geometry; average depth 62m maximum 284m Hood Canal; 160km north-south extent; Strait of Juan de Fuca connection; tidal prism concentration at constrictions; mixed semi-diurnal tidal pattern; Seattle tidal range 3.7m mean spring; extreme spring 4.5m; Admiralty Inlet sill 50m depth; tidal lag effects; freshwater input from Puyallup Nisqually Duwamish; tidal-dominant exchange dynamics)
