## BULL TROUT VS. SALMON -- THE CHAR DIFFERENCE

### *Salvelinus* Is Not *Oncorhynchus*

Bull Trout occupies a genuinely different taxonomic position than the other fish in this chapter:

- **The char genus.** *Salvelinus* (the chars) is the third major genus in Salmonidae, alongside *Salmo* (Atlantic salmon and Brown Trout) and *Oncorhynchus* (Pacific salmon and Rainbow/Steelhead). The chars are characterized by light spots on a dark background (contrasted with *Salmo* and *Oncorhynchus*, which typically have dark spots on lighter backgrounds), cold-water adaptation beyond what salmon and trout require, and different reproductive biology in several ways.
- **Bull Trout identification.** *Salvelinus confluentus* is a large char: adults in some populations reach 8-10+ kg (rare individuals to 14+ kg), though most populations average much smaller (0.5-2 kg in headwater stream populations). The fish is distinctive in appearance: olive-green to dark background with pale yellow, orange, and red spots. No black spots (the spots are always light on dark -- the diagnostic character for chars). The name "Bull Trout" refers to the fish's broad head and generally robust appearance.
- **Dolly Varden connection.** Bull Trout was historically confused with Dolly Varden (*Salvelinus malma*), a closely related char. The two species were treated as one until 1980, when they were formally separated. Bull Trout is typically found further inland in larger rivers; Dolly Varden tends to be more coastal. In Washington, both species occur; Bull Trout dominates interior cold-water systems.
- **The resident vs. migratory dichotomy.** Like Steelhead/Rainbow Trout, Bull Trout occur in resident and migratory forms. Resident populations stay in headwater streams year-round. Migratory (fluvial) populations move downstream to larger rivers or lakes for winter and return to tributary streams to spawn in summer-fall. Some coastal Bull Trout populations are anadromous (they go to sea and return), like Steelhead -- though anadromous Bull Trout are rare and occur primarily in British Columbia and Alaska.

(Sources: Salvelinus taxonomy; char characteristics; Bull Trout identification; Dolly Varden distinction; resident vs. migratory forms; anadromous Bull Trout; species separation 1980)

## THE SOUND -- NEAR-SILENCE, E=2

### What It Means for a Fish to Be Silent

The Bull Trout's acoustic profile is among the most minimal in the SPS engine:

- **Why fish are generally quiet.** Fish in general do not communicate acoustically the way birds and mammals do -- they have no larynx, no syrinx, no specialized sound-producing organ for communication. Some fish produce sounds incidentally (body movements, gill movements, feeding) or through specialized organs (drumming muscles against the swim bladder in some species). Salmonidae are not known for meaningful acoustic behavior in this sense.
- **Why Bull Trout is particularly quiet.** Beyond the general silence of fish, Bull Trout occupy habitats where sound production would not serve them well: deep cold pools, undercut banks, areas of complex underwater structure. They are ambush predators: they wait motionless for prey to pass and then strike with explosive speed. A sit-and-wait predator has no acoustic advantage in announcing its presence. The Bull Trout's behavior is the opposite of the Coho's aggressive spawning -- it is patient, concealed, still.
- **The "sound" of Bull Trout habitat.** The engine's approach to near-silent species is to document the sound of the habitat rather than the animal. The "sound" of Bull Trout habitat is the sound of pristine cold mountain streams: the soft riffle of water over rounded cobble, the occasional deeper pitch of a pool's eddy, the high-frequency of a riffle over fine gravel, the relative absence of turbulence compared to lower-gradient streams. This is the sound of good Bull Trout habitat: cold, clear, undisturbed.
- **E=2 in context.** E=2 is near the bottom of the engine's scale. The lowest documented SPS sounds have been Turkey Vulture (silent soaring) and the very low acoustic profile of some nocturnal mammals. E=2 for Bull Trout is physically accurate: a person standing at a Bull Trout stream hears the stream, not the fish. The fish is present; its presence is audible only as the slight disturbance of water when it repositions -- an E=2 acoustic event.

## THE BULL TROUT AS INDICATOR SPECIES

### What Bull Trout Presence Means About a Stream

The Bull Trout's ecological significance is not primarily acoustic -- it is its role as an ultra-sensitive indicator of water quality:

- **Cold water requirement.** Bull Trout require water temperatures below 12-14°C (53-57°F) for foraging and below 9°C (48°F) for spawning. These are the coldest water temperature requirements of any salmonid in the PNW. As climate change warms rivers and air temperatures increase, the stream reaches that remain cold enough for Bull Trout are contracting upward (into higher elevations) and shrinking.
- **Clean water requirement.** Bull Trout require water with very low sediment load -- the spawning gravel must be clean, with small-particle fine sediment washed out by adequate flow. Logging, road building, and agriculture all increase fine sediment delivery to streams, smothering Bull Trout spawning habitat.
- **The food requirement.** Bull Trout are piscivores (fish-eating predators) in most of their larger-fish life stages. They eat other fish: juvenile salmon and trout, whitefish, and smaller Bull Trout. As a predator of juvenile salmon, Bull Trout occupy an interesting ecological position: they consume the species that the conservation community is trying to restore. Management must account for Bull Trout predation on juvenile Chinook and Coho while simultaneously protecting the Bull Trout.
- **Large patches of connected habitat.** Bull Trout require large, connected patches of cold-water habitat for their life cycle. Barriers (dams, culverts, waterfalls) that prevent movement between stream patches isolate Bull Trout populations, causing genetic bottlenecks and local extinction. The connectivity requirement is one of the most stringent habitat demands of any PNW fish.
- **The indicator species logic.** If Bull Trout are present and reproducing in a stream system, the system has: cold enough water, clean enough water (low sediment), adequate flow, connected habitat, sufficient prey base, and minimal disturbance. Bull Trout presence is a multi-parameter certificate of stream health. If Bull Trout are absent from a stream where they historically occurred, one or more of these parameters has failed.

(Sources: Bull Trout cold water temperature requirements; sediment tolerance; piscivorous feeding; habitat connectivity requirements; indicator species concept; Bull Trout as habitat quality certificate)

## ESA LISTING -- THREATENED THROUGHOUT US RANGE

### A Species Under Pressure Across Its Entire Distribution

Bull Trout (*Salvelinus confluentus*) is listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act throughout its entire US range:

- **The listing.** Bull Trout were listed as Threatened in 1998 under the ESA. The listing covers all US populations: Washington, Oregon, Idaho, Nevada, and Montana. The listing is range-wide, reflecting a decline that has affected the species across its entire US distribution.
- **The causes.** The decline has multiple interacting causes:
  - **Water temperature increase:** Climate change is warming streams. Many historically cold streams have warmed beyond Bull Trout thermal tolerances. Fish are moving upstream to find cold water, becoming isolated in smaller and smaller headwater reaches.
  - **Habitat fragmentation:** Dams, culverts, and road crossings have fragmented Bull Trout populations into small isolated patches. Genetic exchange between patches requires connectivity; barriers prevent it.
  - **Sediment:** Logging, road building, and agricultural land use have increased fine sediment delivery to streams, degrading spawning habitat.
  - **Non-native brook trout:** Brook Trout (*Salvelinus fontinalis*), native to eastern North America but widely introduced throughout the West, are a significant threat to Bull Trout. Where the two chars co-occur, they hybridize (producing infertile offspring called "sparkling trout" or "splake"), and Brook Trout typically outcompete Bull Trout in streams warmer than Bull Trout prefer. Brook Trout invasions are one of the most insidious threats to Bull Trout.
  - **Past overfishing:** Historic fishing pressure (Bull Trout were caught commercially and for sport without regulation in the late 19th and early 20th centuries) depleted many populations before conservation measures were in place.
- **Recovery efforts.** ESA critical habitat designations, dam removal, culvert replacement (to restore connectivity), Brook Trout eradication in some streams, and land use regulations in critical watersheds are all part of Bull Trout recovery. Progress is slow.
- **Washington State populations.** Washington's Bull Trout are found primarily in the North Cascades (Okanogan, Methow, and upper Columbia River tributaries) and in the Olympic Peninsula's cold mountain streams. The upper Columbia River system (Lake Chelan and Wenatchee River drainage) has some of Washington's most important Bull Trout populations.

(Sources: Bull Trout ESA Threatened listing 1998; US range-wide listing; climate warming effects; habitat fragmentation; Brook Trout hybridization and competition; recovery efforts; Washington State populations)

## BULL TROUT IN PACIFIC NORTHWEST CULTURE AND HISTORY

### A Fish That Was Rarely Noticed Until It Was Nearly Gone

- **Historical abundance.** Bull Trout were historically abundant in the cold mountain streams of the PNW. Early European and American observers noted large fish in mountain streams that were likely Bull Trout -- they were fished commercially and for subsistence. But the species never achieved the cultural centrality of salmon or Steelhead; it was the less-celebrated fish of the cold headwaters.
- **Indigenous relationships.** Indigenous peoples of the interior Pacific Northwest -- the Nez Perce, Flathead, Kootenai, and others -- fished Bull Trout historically. The fish's interior distribution means it was more important to interior tribes than to coastal peoples who had access to salmon. Interior tribes' fishing rights and treaty protections extend to Bull Trout in some cases.
- **The "Dolly Varden" confusion.** For most of the 20th century, Bull Trout were called "Dolly Varden" throughout the Pacific Northwest. The name "Dolly Varden" stuck in popular usage even after the species were formally separated in 1980. Many anglers who grew up fishing "Dolly Varden" in interior Pacific Northwest streams were actually fishing Bull Trout, which -- under either name -- required no special protection until the late 20th century.
- **The conservation awakening.** The ESA listing in 1998 forced the issue: Bull Trout were not Dolly Varden, they were a distinct species, and they were Threatened. The listing changed how land managers, anglers, and hydrologists related to the fish. The cold mountain stream that seemed pristine was often insufficient -- if Brook Trout were present, or if the stream temperature had risen 1-2°C from historical levels, Bull Trout were already under pressure.

(Sources: Historical Bull Trout abundance; inland indigenous relationships; Dolly Varden naming confusion; 1980 species separation; 1998 ESA listing; conservation awareness change)
