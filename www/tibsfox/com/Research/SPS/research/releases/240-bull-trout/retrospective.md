## What Worked

1. **The indicator species framing is the entry's most important contribution.** Bull Trout presence = cold enough, clean enough, connected, undisturbed. A single species as a multi-parameter habitat quality certificate. This is a powerful ecological concept that deserves permanent documentation.
2. **The char vs. salmon distinction is correctly documented.** Bull Trout is *Salvelinus*, not *Oncorhynchus*. Light spots on dark background. No black spots. The engine now holds the full taxonomic range of Salmonidae: three *Oncorhynchus* species (237-239) and one *Salvelinus* (240).
3. **The Brook Trout threat is specific and important.** Non-native Brook Trout hybridizing with Bull Trout and outcompeting them in slightly warmer water is one of the more insidious conservation threats the engine has documented. The mechanism (hybridization producing infertile offspring + competitive exclusion at slightly higher temps) is clear and consequential.
4. **E=2 acoustic rationale is sound.** The ambush predator behavior is the correct explanation: a sit-and-wait predator has no acoustic advantage in announcing itself. The silence is functional, not incidental.

## Discoveries

1. **The species was called "Dolly Varden" throughout the Pacific Northwest until 1980.** The species were formally separated in 1980 (49 years after the PNW had been calling them Dolly Varden). Many PNW anglers fishing "Dolly Varden" in the 20th century were fishing Bull Trout. The naming confusion has conservation implications: the species was not protected until it was named correctly.
2. **Anadromous Bull Trout exist.** Though rare (primarily British Columbia and Alaska), some Bull Trout populations are anadromous -- they go to sea and return. The same life-history flexibility that makes Steelhead interesting in *O. mykiss* appears in *Salvelinus* as well.
