CHAR, NOT SALMON — INDICATOR OF PRISTINE COLD WATER — ESA THREATENED

Bull Trout is Salvelinus confluentus — a char, in the genus Salvelinus alongside Brook Trout, Lake Trout, Arctic Char, and Dolly Varden. Not technically a salmon: the engine includes it here as the fifth Salmonidae chapter entry because it lives in the same cold PNW freshwater systems as salmon and Steelhead. ESA Threatened throughout its entire US range (listed 1998). Indicator species: Bull Trout presence = pristine cold water. Where Bull Trout thrive, the stream is cold enough, clean enough, and connected enough.

The Sound of Silence

Bull Trout make almost no sound. They are sit-and-wait ambush predators: motionless in deep cold pools, waiting for prey. The sound of Bull Trout habitat is the sound of the water they require — cold, clear mountain streams. Soft riffle over rounded cobble. The pitch of a slow pool eddy. The near-silence that tells you this water is pristine.

"The Bull Trout does not announce itself. A person standing at the stream hears the water, not the fish. The fish is there — pale spots on dark green-olive sides, waiting in the cold dark of the undercut bank. E=2: the water makes more sound than the fish."

Indicator Species

Bull Trout presence = a passing grade for all of these simultaneously:

  • Water temperature: Below 12–14°C for foraging; below 9°C for spawning. The coldest salmonid requirement.
  • Water clarity: Very low fine sediment. Clean gravel for spawning.
  • Habitat connectivity: Connected cold-water patches. Barriers isolate populations to extinction.
  • Adequate flow: Perennial, cold, consistent stream flow.
  • Absence of Brook Trout: Non-native Brook Trout hybridize with Bull Trout and outcompete them in slightly warmer water.

Conservation

ESA THREATENED throughout US range (listed 1998). Threats: climate warming (cold streams shrinking upward), habitat fragmentation (dams, culverts), sediment (logging, roads), Brook Trout hybridization and competition, historic overfishing. Washington populations: North Cascades (Methow, Okanogan, upper Columbia), Olympic Peninsula cold streams. Recovery requires climate adaptation, dam removal, culvert replacement, and Brook Trout eradication in some systems.
