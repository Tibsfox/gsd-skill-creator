## E=5 -- THE RISING "CURLEEE!"

### Why E=5

The Long-billed Curlew's call registers at E=5:

- **Volume:** The rising "curleee!" is LOUD. The call carries far across open grassland -- over a kilometer in calm conditions. This is the loudest call in the Scolopacidae sequence (168-171).
- **Dramatic quality:** The call rises in pitch and intensity -- a clear, ringing whistle that starts low and sweeps upward. The rising quality gives the call a dramatic, urgent character. The name "curlew" is onomatopoeic -- derived from this call.
- **Open-field projection:** The Long-billed Curlew breeds and calls on open grassland -- no trees, no obstructions. The call is designed for maximum projection across flat, open landscape. The openness of the habitat amplifies the acoustic impact.
- **Size authority:** The Long-billed Curlew is the largest shorebird in North America. The large body produces a proportionally large, powerful voice. Size and sound match.
- **Why not E=6?** The call, while loud and dramatic, is a single-note rising whistle. It lacks the sustained, overwhelming quality of E=6 sounds (like the Pileated Woodpecker's laughing). E=5 reflects a very loud, dramatic, carrying single-note call.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## THE LONGEST BILL IN NORTH AMERICA

### Extreme Morphology

The Long-billed Curlew's bill is an extreme adaptation:

- **Length:** Up to 21 cm (8.5 inches). The longest bill of any North American shorebird. The bill can be nearly as long as the bird's body. Females have longer bills than males -- sexual dimorphism in bill length is more extreme in Long-billed Curlews than in most shorebirds.
- **Shape:** Extremely down-curved, with the greatest curvature in the distal half. The curve follows a smooth, elegant arc.
- **Function:** The extreme bill length allows the Long-billed Curlew to probe deeply into burrows and reach prey that shorter-billed shorebirds cannot access. On grasslands, the bill probes for earthworms and insect larvae deep in the soil. On tidal flats, the bill reaches deep into ghost shrimp and crab burrows.
- **Bill vs Whimbrel:** The Long-billed Curlew's bill (up to 21 cm) is more than twice the length of the Whimbrel's bill (7-9 cm). The comparison is dramatic -- two curlews, one moderate and one extreme.
- **Bill curve vs godwit:** The comparison pair will continue at degree 172 with the Marbled Godwit, whose bill is long but slightly UPTURNED -- the opposite curvature. Down-curved (curlew) vs up-curved (godwit).

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; shorebird morphology studies; engine internal analysis)

## NORTH AMERICA'S LARGEST SHOREBIRD

### The Continental Giant

The Long-billed Curlew holds the record:

- **Size:** Length 50-65 cm. Wingspan 62-68 cm. Weight 490-950g. This is the largest shorebird in North America -- larger than godwits, yellowlegs, avocets, and all other shorebirds on the continent.
- **Breeding habitat:** Great Plains grasslands -- open, treeless prairie. The curlew is a grassland-obligate breeder, dependent on large tracts of native grassland. Habitat loss (conversion of prairie to agriculture) threatens breeding populations.
- **Winter habitat:** Coastal tidal flats, estuaries, and agricultural fields. Some winter in the PNW -- on Oregon and Washington coast tidal flats. Small numbers regular in inland valleys.
- **Conservation concern:** The Long-billed Curlew has declined due to grassland loss. It is a species of conservation concern, listed as Near Threatened in some assessments. The loss of Great Plains grassland to agriculture is the primary threat.
- **Cinnamon plumage:** The Long-billed Curlew's warm cinnamon-buff plumage is distinctive -- richer and warmer in color than the Whimbrel's brown-streaked plumage. The underwings in flight flash bright cinnamon -- a diagnostic field mark.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; conservation assessments; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Long-billed Curlew

**Rising "Curleee!" Call:**
- **Trigger:** Territory, alarm, contact. Given frequently on breeding grounds and during migration.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** A loud, clear, rising whistle -- "curleee!" or "cur-LEE!" The pitch rises through the call. Ringing and dramatic. Onomatopoeic origin of "curlew."
- **Frequency:** Starts approximately 1500 Hz, rises to 2500-3000 Hz.
- **Duration:** Approximately 0.5-1 second per call.
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Very loud. Carries over 1 km on open grassland. The loudest call in the Scolopacidae sequence.

**Display Song:**
- On breeding grounds, a long, elaborate flight display with repeated rising and falling calls. The display is dramatic -- the large bird circling over grassland, calling repeatedly.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
