FIRST OTARIIDAE — 2,500 lbs

Eumetopias jubatus — the Steller Sea Lion — enters the engine at degree 283. The first member of the Otariidae family (eared seals). The largest eared seal. Named for Georg Wilhelm Steller (1709–1746), German naturalist on the Bering Expedition who documented the North Pacific. Described as Eumetopias jubatus: "broad forehead" + "maned."

The bull roar: a sustained, low-frequency, guttural vocalization from an animal that can exceed 2,500 lbs. 100–1,000 Hz. 1–5 seconds sustained. Multiple overlapping bull roars at a summer breeding rookery. E=8: the energy peak of the 281–284 arc. WESTERN STOCK: ENDANGERED.

The Bull Roar — Acoustic Profile

Frequency: Approximately 100–1,000 Hz, energy concentrated below 500 Hz. The massive chest, thick neck, and resonant cranial structures of a 2,500-lb bull produce fundamentally different acoustic output than smaller pinnipeds. Low-frequency vocalizations travel farther in both air and water.

Duration: Individual roars 1–5 seconds or longer. Sustained sequences during territorial confrontation. Colony sound: Multiple simultaneous bull roars at an active rookery, overlapping with female vocalizations and pup contact calls. The summer breeding colony acoustic environment is intense and continuous. E=8: sustained and massive.

Georg Wilhelm Steller (1709–1746)

German naturalist on Vitus Bering’s Second Kamchatka Expedition (1741–1742). First European scientist to document the North Pacific systematically. Named species: Steller Sea Lion, Steller’s Jay (Cyanocitta stelleri), Steller’s Eider, Steller’s Sea Cow (extinct). Died age 37 in Tyumen, Siberia, on the return journey, before most of his scientific contributions were recognized. Note: Steller, not Stellar. Named for a specific person.

Conservation Status — Western Stock: ENDANGERED

Western stock (Aleutians / Gulf of Alaska): Endangered. Population declined ~80% from 1970s to 1990s — from an estimated 140,000–180,000 to approximately 30,000–40,000. One of the largest marine mammal population declines in North American history. ESA listed 1990 (Threatened), 1997 (Endangered for western stock).

Eastern stock (BC to California, including Washington): Near Threatened; showing recovery. Washington rookery sites: Protection Island, Carroll Island, Sea Lion Rock. Causes (contested): nutritional stress (junk food hypothesis), killer whale predation, climate-driven prey shifts, commercial fishery interactions.

The Species

Adult bulls: 10–11 feet (3.0–3.4 m); 1,100–2,500 lbs (500–1,100 kg) — the largest Otariidae species and fourth largest pinniped overall. The pronounced sexual dimorphism (females 440–770 lbs) makes this one of the more extreme size differences in the pinniped world. The bull’s characteristic mane of coarse dark fur around the neck and shoulders provides protection during territorial combat and contributes to the low-frequency resonance of the roar.

Otariidae — The Family Enters the Engine

Otariidae (eared seals) are distinguished from Phocidae (true seals) by: visible external ear pinnae; ability to rotate rear flippers forward for quadrupedal land locomotion; front flipper-based swimming (versus Phocid rear-flipper undulation). The Pacific Northwest hosts two Otariidae species: Steller Sea Lion (degree 283) and California Sea Lion (degree 284). Both enter the engine in consecutive degrees — the Otariidae chapter spans two degrees.

Breeding Season and Rookery Behavior

Bulls arrive at rookery sites in late May–early June, establishing territories before females arrive. Territory quality determines access to females; bulls in prime locations maintain harems of 3–20+ females. Bulls fast for weeks during the breeding season, maintaining territory without foraging. Territorial combat: vocalizations (roaring), visual displays (rearing, head-wagging, open-mouth threats), physical combat (neck-biting, chest-pushing). The thick mane provides protection during combat — the same anatomy that produces the roar also buffers the neck during fighting.
