## GEORG WILHELM STELLER AND THE NAMING

### The Man Behind the Name

*Eumetopias jubatus* -- but it is "Steller" (not "Stellar") Sea Lion, named for a specific naturalist:

- **Georg Wilhelm Steller (1709-1746).** German naturalist and explorer who served as the naturalist on Vitus Bering's Second Kamchatka Expedition (1741-1742), which explored the North Pacific and reached the coast of Alaska. Steller was the first European scientist to document numerous North Pacific species, including the Steller Sea Lion, the Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*), Steller's Eider (*Polysticta stelleri*), and the extinct Steller's Sea Cow (*Hydrodamalis gigas*), which he documented before its extinction less than three decades later.
- **The expedition.** The 1741 voyage reached the coast of Alaska (near present-day Kayak Island) and the Aleutian Islands. Steller's observations during this expedition -- made during brief shore landings while the expedition was primarily focused on navigation and trade -- constitute some of the most important natural history documentation of the North Pacific.
- **Steller's death.** Steller died in 1746 at age 37, in Tyumen, Siberia, on the return journey from the expedition. His extensive field notes and observations were published posthumously. The man who gave his name to the largest eared seal did not live to see most of his scientific contributions recognized.
- **The scientific name.** *Eumetopias jubatus*: *Eumetopias* = "broad forehead" (Greek: *eu* = well/broad, *metopos* = forehead) -- referring to the characteristic broad, massive head of the adult male. *jubatus* = "crested" or "maned" (Latin) -- referring to the thick mane of coarse, darker fur that adult males develop around the neck and shoulders.

(Sources: Georg Wilhelm Steller biography 1709-1746; Bering Second Kamchatka Expedition 1741; Alaska coast documentation; Steller's Jay; Steller's Eider; Steller's Sea Cow; Eumetopias jubatus scientific name etymology; Steller death Tyumen 1746)

## STELLER SEA LION: THE SPECIES

### The Largest Eared Seal

- **Size.** Adult males (bulls) are extraordinarily large: typically 10-11 feet (3.0-3.4 m) in length and 1,100-2,500 lbs (500-1,100 kg). This makes the Steller Sea Lion the largest member of the Otariidae family and the fourth largest pinniped species overall (after the Southern Elephant Seal, Northern Elephant Seal, and Walrus). Adult females are substantially smaller: 7.5-9.5 feet (2.3-2.9 m) and 440-770 lbs (200-350 kg). The sexual dimorphism is pronounced.
- **The bull's mane.** Adult males develop a thick mane of coarse, dark brown fur around the neck and shoulders during maturity. The mane is the visual marker of a dominant bull. It is also acoustically relevant: the mane modifies the resonance of the bull's vocalizations, contributing to the characteristic low-frequency quality of the roar.
- **Coloration.** Light tan to reddish-brown, darker when wet. Bulls are generally darker than females. Pups are dark brown at birth, lightening with age.
- **Two subspecies.** *E. j. monteriensis* (western stock): ranges from the Aleutian Islands to California. *E. j. jubatus* (eastern stock): ranges from the Russian Far East (Kamchatka, Kuril Islands) to California. The western stock has experienced substantial population decline since the 1970s and is listed as Endangered; the eastern stock is listed as Near Threatened.

(Sources: Steller Sea Lion size; bull dimensions; sexual dimorphism; mane morphology; coloration; subspecies E. j. monteriensis and E. j. jubatus; western stock Endangered; eastern stock Near Threatened)

## CONSERVATION STATUS: THE WESTERN STOCK CRISIS

### An Endangered Population

The conservation status of the Steller Sea Lion is one of the most complex and contested marine mammal issues in the North Pacific:

- **Western stock: Endangered.** The western Steller Sea Lion population (Aleutian Islands and Gulf of Alaska) declined by approximately 80% from the 1970s to the 1990s -- one of the largest documented declines of a marine mammal in North American history. At its peak, the western population numbered approximately 140,000-180,000 animals. By the early 2000s, the count had fallen to approximately 30,000-40,000.
- **Eastern stock: Near Threatened, recovering.** The eastern stock (British Columbia to California, including Puget Sound area animals) has shown recovery since protection under the Endangered Species Act. Oregon and California rookery counts have increased.
- **The causes (contested).** The causes of the western stock decline are one of the most extensively studied and contested questions in marine mammal science. Proposed factors: nutritional stress (reduced availability of high-fat prey species -- Atka mackerel, pollock -- potentially linked to commercial fishing pressure); killer whale predation (Bigg's killer whales documented preying on Steller Sea Lions in Alaska); disease; contaminants; climate-driven prey shifts. The "junk food hypothesis" -- that commercially fished prey species were replaced in the diet by lower-calorie species insufficient for maintaining body condition -- has been particularly debated.
- **ESA listing.** Steller Sea Lions were listed under the Endangered Species Act in 1990 (Threatened) and 1997 (Endangered for the western stock). The listing triggered significant fisheries management changes in the Gulf of Alaska and Aleutian Islands.
- **Puget Sound connection.** Steller Sea Lions occur in Puget Sound and the Washington coast as part of the eastern stock. They are observed at Washington rookery sites (Protection Island, Carroll Island, Sea Lion Rock at Point Grenville) and haul-out locations.

(Sources: Western stock Endangered; population decline ~80% 1970s-1990s; western stock estimate 140,000-180,000 peak; 30,000-40,000 early 2000s; eastern stock recovery; ESA listing 1990 Threatened, 1997 Endangered; junk food hypothesis; killer whale predation; fisheries management changes; Washington rookery sites)

## THE BULL ROAR: ACOUSTIC ANALYSIS

### The Loudest Sound in the Pinniped Arc

The bull roar is the defining acoustic signature of the Steller Sea Lion:

- **The sound.** The Steller Sea Lion bull roar is a sustained, low-frequency, guttural vocalization -- a deep, resonant sound that carries considerable distance across open water. It is not a bark (short, percussive) like the California Sea Lion (degree 284) -- it is a sustained roar, held for several seconds, emerging from the massive chest of a 2,500-lb animal.
- **Frequency.** Bull roars are concentrated in the low-frequency range, approximately 100-1,000 Hz, with most energy below 500 Hz. The large body size of the bull -- the massive chest, the thick neck, the resonant cranial structures -- produces a vocalization fundamentally different from smaller pinnipeds. Lower frequency vocalizations travel farther in both air and water.
- **Duration.** Individual bull roars can last 1-5 seconds or longer. During active territorial confrontations at rookeries, bulls may produce sustained sequences of roars interspersed with visual displays (rearing up, head-wagging, open-mouth threats).
- **Colony sound.** At an active Steller Sea Lion rookery during the summer breeding season, multiple bulls are roaring simultaneously -- a continuous, overlapping cacophony of low-frequency sound. The colony acoustic environment is intense: bull roars, female vocalizations (higher-pitched, shorter), pup calls (bleating, contact calls), and the percussive sounds of bulls moving on rock surfaces.
- **E=8 calibration.** E=8 for the sustained colony roar: the combination of the size of the vocalizer (the largest pinniped in the Pacific Northwest), the frequency and power of the sound, the duration of individual roars, and the cumulative acoustic environment of an active rookery. The roar is not explosive (unlike the orca breach at E=9) but it is continuous and massive. E=8 is correct.

(Sources: Steller Sea Lion vocalization research; bull roar frequency 100-1,000 Hz; duration 1-5 seconds; territorial display context; colony acoustic environment; E=8 calibration rationale)

## ROOKERY BEHAVIOR: THE BREEDING SEASON

### The Social Structure of Summer

- **The timing.** Bulls arrive at rookery sites first, in late May or early June, and establish territories before the females arrive. Territory quality is defined by the female landing area: bulls in the best locations can access more females. The competition for territory is intense and energetic.
- **The harem.** Dominant bulls establish harems of 3-20+ females. The harem system is polygynous: one bull monopolizes reproductive access to multiple females. Bulls may go without food for weeks during the breeding season, living off their fat reserves while maintaining their territory.
- **Territorial combat.** Bulls compete through a combination of vocalizations (roaring), visual displays (rearing up, head-wagging), and physical combat (neck-biting, chest-to-chest pushing). Combat can be serious: injuries from bites and abrasion are common at rookeries. The thick mane around the neck provides some protection during combat.
- **Female vocalizations.** Females produce distinct vocalizations for pup identification. A mother-pup pair can locate each other by voice in a crowded rookery through a combination of individual vocal signature (the mother's call) and scent recognition. The vocal recognition system is robust enough to work in the chaotic acoustic environment of an active colony.
- **Pup development.** Pups are born between May and July. They are dark brown at birth and nurse for 1-3 years (some pups nurse for up to 3 years even after being weaned from milk). The nursing period is long relative to many pinnipeds.

(Sources: Steller Sea Lion breeding season timing; bull territorial establishment; harem system 3-20+ females; fasting during breeding; territorial combat mechanics; mane protection function; female-pup vocal recognition; pup coloration and development)

## OTARIIDAE: THE FAMILY

### Eared Seals -- What Distinguishes Them

- **The ear pinnae.** The most visible distinction between Otariidae and Phocidae (true seals): eared seals have small but visible external ear pinnae. True seals do not. The external ear flap is the taxonomic marker.
- **Locomotion.** Otariids can rotate their rear flippers forward under the body, allowing them to walk on all four limbs on land -- a "galloping" gait. Phocids cannot rotate the rear flippers forward and must move on land by undulating the body ("inchworm" motion). Otariids are more mobile on land than true seals.
- **Front flipper use.** Otariids primarily use their front flippers for swimming (generating thrust with the flippers in a rowing or flying motion). Phocids primarily use their rear flippers for swimming (lateral undulation).
- **Social structure.** Otariids tend toward more elaborate social structures and more complex vocalizations than many Phocidae species. The rookery system with territorial bulls and harems is typical of the larger Otariid species.
- **Pacific Northwest Otariidae.** Two Otariidae species are regular in Pacific Northwest waters: Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*, degree 283) and California Sea Lion (*Zalophus californianus*, degree 284). The engine documents both in consecutive degrees.

(Sources: Otariidae family taxonomy; external ear pinnae distinction; rear flipper rotation; locomotion comparison with Phocidae; front flipper swimming; social structure complexity; Pacific Northwest Otariidae species)
