## What Worked

1. **The three-level simultaneous complexity calibration is the correct E=10 justification.** Species richness (maximum acoustic species), multi-layer structure (each meta-layer is itself multiple sub-layers), and full spectrum coverage (insects + birds + rain + river simultaneously covering 20 Hz to 15+ kHz) together constitute the maximum complexity of the temperate rainforest acoustic environment.
2. **The Pacific Wren as the understory voice at disproportionate amplitude is the correct focal point.** A 10-gram bird producing a 4-8 second continuous complex song at up to 10 per minute is the most immediately striking acoustic element in the Hoh Rainforest dawn chorus. The disproportion between body mass and acoustic output is the wren's defining characteristic.
3. **The Hermit Thrush two-syrinx polyphony explanation is the correct basis for the ethereal quality.** The thrush sounds like it is producing two independent voices because it is: the two sides of the syrinx can operate independently. The multi-voice quality of the song is anatomically real, not an illusion.
4. **The "big-drop effect" for rainforest rainfall acoustics is an important ecosystem-specific observation.** Rain in the Hoh does not sound like rain in an open field -- the canopy redistributes droplets to larger sizes, producing a deeper, more irregular acoustic texture on the understory surfaces. The rainforest changes the character of rainfall acoustically.
5. **The simultaneous synthesis pairing with The Accused is analytically correct.** Both entries are about multiple distinct elements present simultaneously at their individual maximums. The Accused's crossover = simultaneous punk velocity + metal technique. The Hoh maximum = simultaneous insects + birds + rain. The same structure in different domains.

## Discoveries

1. **Dolbear's Law as the insect acoustic temperature indicator.** The cricket chirp rate is so reliably temperature-linked that it was historically used as a thermometer. The insect stridulation layer is itself a real-time acoustic temperature readout for the ecosystem.
2. **The canopy drip as post-rain acoustic signature.** The canopy drip continues long after rain stops -- sometimes for hours after a heavy rain event. The Hoh is acoustically "rainy" for longer than it is actually raining because of the mass of water stored in the moss layer. The acoustic signature of the rainforest persists beyond the meteorological event.
3. **The full spectrum coverage is unique to this SPS entry.** No other SPS entry combines species-specific calls (1-15 kHz) with rainfall broadband (full spectrum) with river low-frequency (20-2,000 Hz) simultaneously. The Hoh peak spring covers every octave of the audible range at once.
