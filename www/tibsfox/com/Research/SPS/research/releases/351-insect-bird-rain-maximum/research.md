## THE HOH RAINFOREST -- THE ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT

### Olympic Peninsula. Temperate Rainforest. Maximum Biodiversity.

The Hoh Rainforest on the western slope of the Olympic Peninsula is one of the most biodiverse temperate ecosystems in North America. It receives 12-14 feet of precipitation annually, supporting the most productive temperate rainforest on the continent. The canopy structure is multiple-layered: old-growth Sitka spruce and Western red cedar at the overstory (to 300 feet), bigleaf maple and red alder in the middle canopy, sword fern and oxalis in the understory, and a continuous moss layer on every available surface.

The acoustic character of the Hoh is defined by this multi-layered structure. Sound is absorbed, reflected, and filtered at multiple height levels simultaneously. The rainforest is never fully quiet: the constant moisture dripping from moss and leaves, the creek and river sounds from the Hoh River tributary system, and the continuous biological activity of the most productive temperate ecosystem on the continent create a base acoustic level that the spring maximum layers build upon.

**Peak spring timing.** Peak spring in the Hoh Rainforest occurs from mid-April through mid-June. This window coincides with: maximum insect emergence (temperature-dependent, with warm spring days triggering stridulation), maximum bird dawn chorus activity (breeding territories established, maximum singing effort), and the transition from winter rainfall patterns to spring rainfall (still frequent precipitation but with warming temperatures that produce the specific spring acoustic character of rain on new leaf growth and moss).

## LAYER 1: INSECT STRIDULATION MAXIMUM

### Multiple Species. Multiple Frequency Ranges. Peak Temperature.

Insect stridulation in the Pacific Northwest temperate rainforest is temperature-dependent: stridulation activity (the production of sound by rubbing body parts together, primarily in Orthoptera -- crickets, katydids, and grasshoppers -- and Coleoptera) increases rapidly as temperatures rise above 60°F (15°C). Peak stridulation in the Hoh occurs during warm spring days (above 65°F) and on warm nights following warm days.

The insect acoustic community in the Hoh Rainforest at peak spring is a complex multi-species layer:

**Ground crickets (Gryllidae).** Multiple species of ground crickets produce continuous pulse-repetition-rate calling from ground-level vegetation. Frequency range: 3,000-8,000 Hz. The chirp rate is temperature-dependent and varies with species: the snowy tree cricket's chirp rate is so reliably temperature-linked that it was once used to estimate ambient temperature (Dolbear's Law: add 40 to the number of chirps per 14 seconds to get the temperature in Fahrenheit).

**Katydids (Tettigoniidae).** Several katydid species produce high-frequency stridulation (6,000-15,000+ Hz) from vegetation at varying heights. Katydid calls are often species-specific in their temporal patterning (continuous vs. pulsed vs. irregular) and serve both territory establishment and mate attraction functions in the spring.

**Beetles and other stridulating insects.** Several beetle families (Cerambycidae, Curculionidae) and other insect groups produce stridulation as a secondary acoustic component. These provide a lower-amplitude continuous layer beneath the cricket and katydid calling.

The combined insect stridulation layer at peak spring creates a continuous, spectrally broad, rhythmically complex acoustic environment from ground level to mid-canopy. The layer is so dense at peak temperature that it constitutes a significant component of the overall soundscape energy.

(Sources: Insect stridulation temperature-dependent; Orthoptera: Gryllidae Tettigoniidae; Coleoptera; frequency range 3,000-15,000+ Hz; Dolbear's Law cricket chirp rate temperature; multiple species simultaneous; peak spring warm days; continuous multi-level insect acoustic layer)

## LAYER 2: BIRD DAWN CHORUS MAXIMUM

### Rainforest Species Composition. Maximum Spring Breeding Effort.

The Hoh Rainforest spring dawn chorus is one of the most species-rich bird acoustic events in the Pacific Northwest. The rainforest's biodiversity supports a dense bird community with distinct acoustic niches occupied by each species.

**Primary chorus voices:**

**Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*).** Arguably the most acoustically remarkable bird in the Pacific Northwest rainforest. A bird weighing approximately 10 grams produces a song of extraordinary length and complexity: the full song runs 4-8 seconds of continuous, rapid, multi-note phrases. At peak spring, Pacific Wrens sing at rates of up to 10 songs per minute. The song volume, for the bird's size, is extraordinary -- the wren sings from low vegetation in the understory at close range, filling the surrounding acoustic space with song.

**Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*).** The Hermit Thrush's song is one of the most beautiful in North American birdsong: an opening clear whistle followed by a cascade of spiraling phrases at progressively higher registers. The song has a distinctly ethereal quality produced by the bird's ability to sing two independent notes simultaneously using the two sides of the syrinx. In the Hoh, the Hermit Thrush sings from mid-canopy height, placing its voice above the Pacific Wren but below the canopy chorus.

**Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*).** The ascending, spiraling song of Swainson's Thrush begins in late April in the Hoh as migrants arrive from wintering grounds. The song spirals upward in pitch, giving it a distinctive quality of perpetual ascent. Multiple Swainson's Thrushes active simultaneously in the rainforest create a chorus that is itself complex.

**Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*).** The Pacific Northwest's resident chickadee species produces contact calls and song from all canopy heights throughout the dawn period. The high-frequency call patterns of chickadees provide a continuous background layer above the thrush songs.

**Additional species:** Steller's Jay, Varied Thrush (present year-round, still active in spring), Song Sparrow (from brushy edges), Orange-crowned Warbler and Yellow-rumped Warbler (migrants arriving in April-May), Northwestern Crow, and Raven calls from the forest interior.

(Sources: Hoh Rainforest bird community spring; Pacific Wren Troglodytes pacificus song length 4-8 sec 10 songs/min; Hermit Thrush Catharus guttatus two-syrinx singing; Swainson's Thrush ascending spiral song April arrival; Chestnut-backed Chickadee Poecile rufescens; additional species composition; species-rich acoustic niches; dawn chorus peak spring timing)

## LAYER 3: RAINFALL ACOUSTIC MAXIMUM

### Active Spring Rain. Rainforest Surface Complexity.

The Hoh Rainforest's rainfall acoustic profile at peak spring is distinct from both the winter storm rain (heavy, cold, continuous, low-frequency dominated) and the summer dry season. Spring rain in the Hoh comes in intermittent events: warm fronts bringing moderate-to-heavy rainfall followed by partial clearing, creating a pattern of alternating rain and non-rain periods that activates the insect and bird layers in the intervals.

**Active rainfall on rainforest surfaces.** Rainforest canopy acoustics during moderate-to-heavy rain: water arrives at the leaf surface of the overstory, drips through multiple canopy layers, and arrives at the ground moss layer significantly redistributed in droplet size. The acoustic signature of rain in dense temperate rainforest is very different from rain in open terrain: the overhead canopy redistributes droplets into larger drops (the "big drop" effect), producing a deeper, less even acoustic texture than direct rainfall. The moss layer absorbs most ground impacts; the sword fern and bigleaf maple leaves produce the primary audible rain sounds.

**Canopy drip.** Between active rainfall events and continuing for extended periods afterward, the mosses, ferns, and leaves continue releasing accumulated water in a pattern of intermittent drops that is itself an acoustic event: not the continuous hiss of active rain but the irregular, musical drops of water moving through the canopy at its own pace. The canopy drip layer is one of the most characteristic acoustic signatures of the temperate rainforest.

**River and creek acoustic layer.** The Hoh River and its tributaries respond to spring rainfall with increased flow. The spring flood season brings the river to its maximum acoustic output: the roar of high-volume, high-velocity water over the cobble bed and through the rapids.

(Sources: Hoh Rainforest spring rainfall pattern: warm fronts moderate-to-heavy intermittent; canopy big-drop effect redistribution; moss layer absorption; sword fern bigleaf maple primary audible rain sources; canopy drip layer post-rain; Hoh River spring flood season maximum acoustic output; acoustic distinction: winter storm vs spring rain vs summer dry)
