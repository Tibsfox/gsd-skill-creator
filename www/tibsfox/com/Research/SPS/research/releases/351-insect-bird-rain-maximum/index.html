The Three Meta-Layers

Insect Stridulation

Ground crickets (3,000–8,000 Hz), katydids (6,000–15,000+ Hz), beetles. Temperature-dependent, peak above 65°F. Multiple species simultaneously. Ground to mid-canopy. Continuous broadband coverage. Dolbear’s Law: chirp rate = temperature indicator.

Bird Dawn Chorus

Pacific Wren (understory, 4–8 sec songs at 10/min), Hermit Thrush (two-syrinx polyphony, mid-canopy), Swainson’s Thrush (ascending spiral), Chestnut-backed Chickadee, warblers, jay, crow. 10–15+ acoustic species simultaneously.

Spring Rain + River

Rainforest big-drop effect: canopy redistributes to larger drops, deeper percussion. Sword fern and bigleaf maple leaf percussion. Canopy drip: persists hours after rain stops. Hoh River spring flood: maximum flow roar over cobble.

Key Species

Pacific Wren
Troglodytes pacificus

~10 grams. Song: 4–8 seconds of continuous rapid multi-note phrases. Up to 10 songs/minute at peak. Acoustic output disproportionate to body mass. The defining understory voice of the Hoh.

Hermit Thrush
Catharus guttatus

Two-syrinx singing: the two sides of the syrinx operate independently, producing genuine polyphony from a single bird. The ethereal quality is anatomically real. Mid-canopy voice.

Swainson’s Thrush
Catharus ustulatus

Ascending spiral song: perpetually rising in pitch. Arrives April from wintering grounds. Multiple individuals simultaneously in the Hoh.

Ground Cricket
Gryllidae spp.

Continuous pulse-rate calling, 3,000–8,000 Hz. Dolbear’s Law: chirp rate is temperature-dependent to the degree that it was historically used as a thermometer.
