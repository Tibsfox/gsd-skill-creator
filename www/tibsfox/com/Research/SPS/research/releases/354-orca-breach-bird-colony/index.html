The Breach

A full body breach by an adult male orca: 5,000–6,000 kg of body mass accelerating from depth, clearing 80–100% of body length above the water surface, re-entering at angle with a concussive impact audible over a kilometer in calm conditions. The impact generates a percussive acoustic event in the audible range and low-frequency pressure waves in the infrasonic range simultaneously. The water column carries the pressure wave. The air carries the impact sound. The cliff face receives both within seconds of each other.

The breach is not random — it is a calculated display event, used for communication over distances of several kilometers. Six tons, intentional, structural. The Blood Brothers play the same way.

The Colony Species

Common Murre
Uria aalge

Most numerous. 20 birds per square meter on open ledges. Alarm call: high-pitched cackling screech. 10,000+ birds in full alarm = most sustained high-amplitude colonial seabird event.

Glaucous-winged Gull
Larus glaucescens

Acoustically dominant when alarmed. Long-call: extremely loud, carries great distances. Fills the upper frequency range above the murres during colony alarm.

Tufted Puffin
Fratercula cirrhata

Burrow-nesting. Alarm: low growling + bill-clapping. Mid-register contribution. Less prominent visually; acoustically present in the full alarm event.

Pelagic Cormorant
Phalacrocorax pelagicus

Grunting, croaking base register. Mass takeoff generates broadband wing-sound. Largest-bodied of common colonial species; wing-thunder adds the acoustic floor.

The Convergence

The breach occurs at the cliff base. The concussive impact reaches the cliff face within seconds. Every bird on every ledge receives the trigger simultaneously.

The first wave: Birds on the lowest ledges launch, adding wing-sound and alarm calls to the acoustic field. Their motion triggers the next tier. Colonial alarm propagates up the cliff face like a wave, each tier responding to both the original stimulus and the response of the tier below.

Full colony alarm: Within seconds, the entire cliff is in alarm. Murre screeches in the mid-upper range, gull long-calls in the upper range, cormorant grunts in the lower range, guillemot whistles in the highest register, and broadband wing-sound from thousands of simultaneous wing-beats beneath everything.

The overlap: The orca breach pressure wave (still moving through the acoustic environment) and the colony alarm (aerial, multi-species) coexist in the same acoustic space. The two events overlap. The breach has not finished propagating before the colony’s response begins.

This is the multiplicative event. The orca breach alone would be E=8. The colony alarm alone at full activation would be E=8. The two converging at the same cliff in the same moment, with the breach physically triggering the colony response: E=10.
