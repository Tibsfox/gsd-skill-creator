## THE ORCA BREACH -- THE SINGLE LARGEST ACOUSTIC EVENT

### Six Tons of Body Clearing the Water Surface

A full body breach by a large male orca (Orcinus orca) is the single largest acoustic event produced by any marine mammal in Pacific Northwest coastal waters. The physics:

- **Body mass:** Adult male orca 5,000-6,000 kg (approximately 6 tons). Female 3,000-4,000 kg.
- **The breach mechanics:** The animal accelerates from depth, achieves vertical momentum sufficient to clear the water surface entirely -- in a full breach, 80-100% of the body length clears the water. The jump angle is typically 45-75 degrees from horizontal.
- **The entry:** The body re-enters the water broadside or at angle, generating a concussive entry that is the primary acoustic event. The impact of 6 tons of body mass striking the water surface from a height of 1-4 meters above the surface produces a percussive impact wave that carries through the water column and the air simultaneously.
- **The acoustic signature:** The entry impact generates both a percussive acoustic event in the audible range (broadly detectable at the surface as a loud concussive "boom" or "crack") and low-frequency pressure waves in the infrasonic range. The aerial acoustic event -- the slap -- can be heard over a kilometer away in calm conditions. The water-column acoustic event travels much farther.
- **The air bubble trail:** Immediately before entry and during the breach, the animal's motion generates a turbulent trail of air bubbles that produces additional acoustic texture.

The purpose of breaching is not definitively understood. Documented contexts include social interaction, play behavior, communication over distances (the surface slap audible to pods separated by several kilometers), and parasite removal. In resident killer whale populations (including the SRKW), breaching occurs at highest rates during social gatherings and inter-pod reunions -- contexts where long-range communication serves a social coordination function.

(Sources: Adult male orca 5,000-6,000 kg; full body breach 80-100% body length clearing water; entry impact percussive acoustic event; audible over 1 km in calm conditions; water column propagation of pressure wave; breach behavior contexts: social, communication, play, parasite removal; SRKW breach rates highest during social gatherings; long-range communication function)

## THE COASTAL CLIFF BIRD COLONY -- THE MAXIMUM COLONIAL RESPONSE

### Thousands of Seabirds. All Responding at Once.

Pacific Northwest coastal cliffs host some of the most dense multi-species seabird colonies in North America. The major nesting cliff locations -- Protection Island, Tatoosh Island, Destruction Island, Carroll Island, Three Arch Rocks on the Oregon border -- concentrate species that nest in different microhabitats of the same cliff system simultaneously:

- **Common Murre (Uria aalge):** The most numerous. Colonial nesting on open ledges, packing densities as high as 20 birds per square meter. Vocalizations: low, purring "murr" calls at baseline; high-pitched cackling screech in alarm. A common murre colony of 10,000+ birds in alarm is the most sustained high-amplitude acoustic event the cliff ecosystem generates from seabird sources alone.
- **Glaucous-winged Gull (Larus glaucescens):** Nesting on broader ledges and cliff tops. The most acoustically dominant species when alarmed: the familiar long-call is extremely loud and carries great distances. Large colonies (hundreds to thousands) in alarm produce overlapping long-call sequences that fill the frequency range above the murres.
- **Tufted Puffin (Fratercula cirrhata):** Burrow-nesting, less acoustically prominent during normal activity. In colony alarm, produces low growling calls and bill-clapping. Contributes to the mid-register of the alarm event.
- **Pigeon Guillemot (Cepphus columba):** High-pitched whistling alarm calls, distinctive against the broader acoustic field.
- **Pelagic and Brandt's Cormorants:** Grunting, croaking base register. Largest-bodied of the common colonial species; their wing-thunder on mass takeoff contributes significant acoustic energy.

**The alarm event.** When a large predator approaches a colonial nesting cliff -- a bald eagle, a peregrine falcon, or, crucially, an orca breaching in the adjacent water -- the alarm response is colony-wide and simultaneous. Species with different baseline call rates, different vocalization ranges, and different alarm call types all respond simultaneously. The result is a multi-species, full-spectrum acoustic event that is multiplicative: the combined output of every species responding to the same trigger event, all at once.

(Sources: Protection Island National Wildlife Refuge; Tatoosh Island seabird colony; Destruction Island; common murre Uria aalge packing density 20 per square meter; glaucous-winged gull Larus glaucescens long-call alarm; tufted puffin Fratercula cirrhata burrow-nesting; pigeon guillemot Cepphus columba alarm whistle; pelagic cormorant Phalacrocorax pelagicus; Brandt's cormorant P. penicillatus; alarm response colony-wide simultaneous; multi-species full-spectrum acoustic event)

## THE CONVERGENCE -- BREACH TRIGGERS COLONY

### The Multiplicative Event

The specific event at degree 354 is the convergence: an orca breach occurring in the water immediately adjacent to a multi-species colonial nesting cliff, in summer, at the peak of breeding season when colony populations are at their annual maximum.

The sequence of events:

1. **The breach.** A large male orca -- a transient hunting near the cliff face, or a resident displaying -- accelerates from depth and clears the surface. The concussive entry impact reaches the cliff face within seconds. The entry is audible to every bird on the cliff. For colonial seabirds that spend their lives in proximity to predators, the unexpected large-impact sound is a trigger of the highest order.

2. **The first wave.** The birds nearest the water surface respond first. Murres on lower ledges launch, adding their wing-sound and alarm calls to the acoustic field. The motion of the nearest birds triggers the next tier -- colonial alarm propagates through the colony like a wave, each tier responding to both the original stimulus and the response of the tier below.

3. **The full colony alarm.** Within seconds, the entire cliff is in alarm. Thousands of birds of multiple species are airborne or calling simultaneously. The acoustic field becomes a layered, full-spectrum event: murre screeches in the mid-upper range, gull long-calls in the upper range, cormorant grunts in the lower range, guillemot whistles in the highest register, wing-sound of thousands of simultaneous wing-beats generating a broad-frequency baseline beneath all of it.

4. **The combined event.** The orca breach echo (the water-transmitted pressure wave, still moving through the acoustic environment) and the colony alarm (the air-transmitted multi-species response) coexist in the same acoustic space. The breach has not finished propagating through the water column before the colony's aerial response begins. The two events overlap.

This is why the event is E=10. The orca breach alone would be E=8. The colony alarm alone at full activation would be E=8. The two events converging at the same coastal cliff in the same moment, with the breach physically triggering the colony response -- this is the multiplicative event. The whole exceeds the sum of parts because one event causes the other.

## SUMMER ECOLOGY -- WHY SUMMER IS MAXIMUM

### Breeding Season Peak. All Populations at Maximum.

Summer is when Pacific Northwest coastal cliff ecosystems reach their annual acoustic maximum. The convergence of factors:

- **Bird colony populations at peak.** Colonial seabirds nest in spring and early summer. By July-August, colonies are at their maximum population density: adults on nests or ledges, sub-adults in adjacent areas, fledglings adding to the colony's acoustic output.
- **Prey availability.** Summer brings maximum forage fish abundance near Pacific Northwest coastlines: Pacific sand lance (Ammodytes personatus), herring (Clupea pallasii), rockfish juveniles -- the prey base that concentrates both seabirds and marine mammals near the same coastal systems.
- **Orca coastal presence.** Transient killer whales (Bigg's killer whales, *Orcinus orca* ecotype T) are opportunistic predators of marine mammals. In summer, their coastal movement ranges intersect with harbor seal and Steller sea lion haul-out sites on the same cliff systems that host the bird colonies. The likelihood of a transient orca hunting near a colonial nesting cliff is highest in summer.
- **Calm water amplification.** Summer in the Pacific Northwest is characterized by relatively stable, calm atmospheric conditions compared to the winter storm season. Acoustic events travel farther in calm conditions. The breach's aerial component is audible over greater distances. The colony's alarm carries farther. The combined event has maximum propagation range in summer.

(Sources: Pacific Northwest summer colonial seabird peak; Pacific sand lance Ammodytes personatus summer abundance; Pacific herring Clupea pallasii; transient killer whale Bigg's ecotype; Steller sea lion Eumetopias jubatus haul-out sites; harbor seal Phoca vitulina coastal haul-outs; summer atmospheric stability acoustic propagation; calm water amplification maximum propagation range)
