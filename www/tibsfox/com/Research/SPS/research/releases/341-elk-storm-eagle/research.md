## THE ELK HERD -- BUGLING AT RUT MAXIMUM

### Cervus canadensis in the River Valley

The elk component of degree 341:

- **The rut context.** The Rocky Mountain Elk (Cervus canadensis) rut occurs in September through October in Washington State -- precisely the period when fall storms begin to move in from the Pacific. The timing is ecological: the rut's maximum vocal activity (bull bugling, cow calling, calf responses, satellite bull responses) coincides with the fall weather transition. The elk are at maximum behavioral energy at the same moment the weather turns.
- **The bull bugle.** The bull elk's bugle is one of the most acoustically distinctive sounds in North American wildlife. It begins as a low-frequency grunt or groan (80-200 Hz), transitions through a mid-frequency ascending whistle (300-1,000+ Hz), and often ends with a series of grunts. The full bugle can carry 1.5-3 kilometers in still air in a valley; in a storm context, the lower frequencies carry further through the ground vibration even as the higher frequencies are scattered by wind. The bugle functions as a territorial announcement, a challenge to rival bulls, and a herding call to cows.
- **The herd chorus.** A single bull bugling is E=5-6. A full herd -- multiple bulls competing, cows responding, calves vocalizing -- is qualitatively different. The acoustic overlap of multiple bulls at different distances, each producing the complete bugle cycle, creates a layered acoustic environment with no single dominant source: the entire valley seems to be vocalizing. Multiple simultaneous bugles from different directions create the spatial disorientation of being inside the sound rather than hearing it from the outside.
- **The river valley acoustics.** The river valley channel concentrates and reflects elk vocalizations. The valley walls (forested ridges on both sides) provide reflection surfaces that extend the acoustic reach of each bugle. The river itself provides the continuous background of water sound against which the bugles emerge. The valley is an acoustic amplifier -- the same bugle that would carry 2 km in open terrain carries 3-4 km in a valley corridor, and the reflections add spatial complexity.
- **Washington State elk populations.** The Washington Department of Fish & Wildlife manages approximately 60,000-65,000 elk statewide, in three primary herds: the Yakima, the Blue Mountains, and the coast/Cascades. The east-slope Cascades river valleys -- the Methow, the Entiat, the Wenatchee, the Naches -- are primary elk habitat at the transition between the rainy west-slope forest and the drier east-slope ponderosa pine zone. October in these valleys is peak rut season.

## THE STORM FRONT -- FALL WEATHER ARRIVES

### The Pacific Northwest Fall Storm System

The storm component of degree 341:

- **The fall storm cycle.** The Pacific Northwest's weather transition from late summer's relative stability to fall's storm season typically begins in October. The first major fall storms come from the Gulf of Alaska, tracking southeast across the North Pacific and making landfall on the Washington coast before moving inland across the Cascades. These storms can bring dramatic conditions: falling temperatures, rapidly increasing winds (30-60 mph gusts at ridge level), heavy precipitation (rain at valley level, snow at higher elevations), and lightning.
- **The acoustic signature of an approaching storm.** An approaching Pacific storm system produces a characteristic acoustic sequence in a river valley: first, the wind arrives in the tree canopy (a sustained, directional hiss that builds from one direction, typically from the west or northwest in the Cascades). Then, the mid-frequency wind in the valley bottom begins -- loose leaves rattling, the river increasing in surface chop. Then distant thunder, if the storm has sufficient instability. Finally, the precipitation onset: rain on leaves and rocks, the river's surface becoming acoustic itself as drops hit it.
- **The lightning and thunder component.** Pacific Northwest fall storms are not primarily electrical storms (those are more common in summer). But early-season fall storms moving across the Cascades occasionally produce lightning as warm air masses lift over the mountains. A thunderclap in a river valley -- especially in the Cascades' narrow drainage valleys -- reverberates off the valley walls with extended echo trains that can last 3-5 seconds per thunder event. The valley makes thunder into a percussive event.
- **Wind through conifers.** The dominant acoustic texture of a fall storm in a Cascades river valley is wind through Douglas-fir, ponderosa pine, and western red cedar. Each tree species has a different wind response: Douglas-fir produces a sustained roar at the canopy level; ponderosa pine produces a more intermittent, rhythmic hiss as individual boughs move; western red cedar produces a lower-pitched shudder. The combined wind-through-conifers sound of a Cascades storm is spatially complex -- the canopy above is moving, the valley walls are responding, the river below is amplifying.

## THE BALD EAGLE -- OVERHEAD ON THE THERMAL

### Haliaeetus leucocephalus in the River Valley

The eagle component of degree 341:

- **The fall eagle movement.** Bald Eagles are year-round residents in much of Washington State, but their numbers increase dramatically in fall as eagles from British Columbia and Alaska move south along river valleys following salmon runs. The Skagit, Nooksack, Samish, and Entiat rivers host significant fall eagle concentrations when Chinook, coho, and chum salmon are running. An October river valley in the Cascades with an active salmon run may have dozens of eagles visible simultaneously.
- **The thermal behavior.** Eagles use thermals -- columns of rising warm air -- to gain altitude efficiently for surveying territory and spotting prey. In a river valley, thermals rise off south-facing slopes that have absorbed solar radiation. An eagle circling on a thermal is not expending significant energy -- it is banking to maintain position within the rising air column, gaining altitude with each spiral. The eagle overhead on a thermal is the apex position: above the valley, watching the herd below, the storm approaching from the west.
- **The Bald Eagle vocalization.** The Bald Eagle's call is one of the most frequently misrepresented in North American wildlife media (the bird's call in films is often replaced with the Red-tailed Hawk's scream, which is more dramatic). The actual Bald Eagle call is a descending series of thin, high-pitched notes: kek-kek-kek-kek or a longer squealing series. It is smaller in scale than expected for a bird of this size -- the discrepancy between the bird's visual scale (wingspan 1.8-2.4 meters) and vocal scale (thin, piping notes) is a distinctive characteristic. In the context of a storm and a bugling elk herd, the eagle's call adds a high-frequency counterpoint above the low and mid-frequency ground-level sounds.
- **Eagle and elk interaction.** Eagles and elk interact at the boundary of the river and the valley floor. Eagles take salmon from the river; elk use the river as a travel corridor and mineral source. The two species are not predator-prey in direct terms (eagles cannot take an adult or juvenile elk), but they share the river valley habitat at the same season. An eagle calling overhead while elk bugle in the valley bottom is not unusual in the Cascades river corridors in October.

(Sources: Bald Eagle Haliaeetus leucocephalus fall movement south from BC and Alaska; Skagit Nooksack Samish Entiat salmon run eagle concentrations; thermal soaring behavior south-facing slopes; eagle wingspan 1.8-2.4 meters; Bald Eagle actual call: thin descending kek-kek-kek; Red-tailed Hawk call misrepresentation in film; eagle call as high-frequency counterpoint; eagle-elk river valley shared habitat; October Cascades river corridor)
