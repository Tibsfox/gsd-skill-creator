## Discoveries

1. **The Bald Eagle's actual call is consistently misrepresented in film and popular media.** The bird's call is thin, high-pitched, and piping -- kek-kek-kek, not the screaming Red-tailed Hawk call that is substituted in most media. The discrepancy between the eagle's visual scale (wingspan 1.8-2.4 m) and vocal scale (thin high notes) is one of wildlife acoustics' most striking mismatches. In the convergence context, this mismatch is structurally useful: the eagle's thin high-frequency call sits above the lower-frequency drama of the elk and storm without competing with them.
2. **Elk bugling in a valley creates a spatial listening experience without parallel in PNW wildlife acoustics.** Multiple bulls at different distances, each completing the full 3-6 second bugle cycle at overlapping intervals, creates the experience of being inside the sound field rather than observing it from outside. The valley walls extend and reflect each bugle so the listener cannot identify a single source direction.
3. **The east-slope Cascades river valleys (Methow, Entiat, Wenatchee, Naches) are the specific geography of this convergence.** These valleys are in the ponderosa pine / Douglas-fir transition zone, in the rain shadow of the Cascades, with productive elk habitat, salmon-bearing rivers, and direct storm transit from the west. The convergence is geographically localized to this specific landscape type.
