The Frequency Architecture

The three components of degree 341 occupy separate spectral regions with minimal masking. The elk herd occupies the low-to-mid range (80–1,200+ Hz). The storm occupies broadband mid-range (50–2,000 Hz), dominant in the 100–800 Hz wind frequencies. The eagle occupies the high end (2,000–8,000 Hz). All three are audible simultaneously in their distinct frequency regions. The river valley concentrates and reflects each component: elk bugles carry 3–4 km (vs. 2 km in open terrain), thunder reverberates for 3–5 seconds off the valley walls, and the eagle’s call descends from above the noise floor of the other two.

The October Ecological Calendar

The convergence at degree 341 is not staged. It is the natural consequence of October in a Cascades river valley:

Elk rut peaks September–October in Washington. The bulls are at maximum territorial and reproductive urgency. Multiple bulls bugling simultaneously is the standard rut-season acoustic environment, not an exceptional event.

Pacific storms begin arriving in October as the summer high-pressure pattern breaks down and Gulf of Alaska lows track southeast across the North Pacific. The first fall storms can be dramatic: rapidly falling temperatures, 30–60 mph gusts at ridge level, heavy precipitation, and occasional lightning as warm air masses lift over the Cascades.

Bald Eagles concentrate in Cascades river valleys in October as fall Chinook, coho, and chum salmon run upstream. Eagles from British Columbia and Alaska move south along river corridors to follow the salmon. An October river valley with an active salmon run may have dozens of eagles visible simultaneously, some fishing, some perched, some soaring on thermals.

All three happen in October because the ecological calendar aligns them. The convergence is the calendar made acoustic.

The Bull Elk Bugle

The Rocky Mountain Elk bull’s bugle is one of North America’s most distinctive wildlife sounds. The acoustic sequence: a low guttural grunt (80–200 Hz), a rapid ascending whistle that peaks above 1,000 Hz, a sustained mid-frequency howl, and a trailing series of grunt coughs. Duration: 3–6 seconds per bugle. A single bull at 200 meters is overwhelming. Multiple bulls at different distances in a valley — each producing the complete sequence at overlapping intervals — creates the acoustic environment of being inside the sound rather than observing it from outside. Washington State supports 60,000–65,000 elk. The east-slope Cascades river valleys (Methow, Entiat, Wenatchee, Naches) are primary October rut habitat.
