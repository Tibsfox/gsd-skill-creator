## STRUCTURAL EVENT: The Flicker Connection -- The Only Duck Small Enough

The ecological chain is:

2. Flickers abandon used cavities.

4. The Bufflehead, at 270-450g and with a small body diameter, is the **sole duck species** that can fit through flicker entrance holes.
5. Buffleheads are therefore **obligately dependent** on flicker cavities in much of their breeding range.

This is not a loose ecological connection -- it is a **tight, specific dependency**. Without Northern Flickers, Buffleheads would have drastically reduced nesting habitat. ## BUFFLEHEAD -- ANATIDAE -- THE POCKET DIVER

### The Species

The Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*) is **North America's smallest diving duck** -- a compact, round-bodied, puffy-headed waterfowl that weighs only 270-450g (roughly the weight of a baseball). The name "Bufflehead" derives from **"buffalo head"** -- a reference to the male's disproportionately large, round, puffy head, which gives the bird a top-heavy, almost comical appearance. The genus name *Bucephala* means "ox-headed" in Greek (from *bous* = ox + *kephale* = head), reinforcing the large-head theme. The species name *albeola* means "white" in diminutive form -- "little white one."

The Bufflehead is a member of the **goldeneye group** within Anatidae -- genus *Bucephala*, which also includes the Common Goldeneye (*B. clangula*) and Barrow's Goldeneye (*B. islandica*). The Bufflehead is the smallest member of the genus and the most morphologically distinctive, with its proportionally enormous head and compact body.

**Physical dimensions:** Body length 32-40 cm. Wingspan 54-56 cm. Weight 270-450g. The smallest diving duck in North America. For comparison:
- Hooded Merganser (degree 120): 450-880g -- larger
- Wood Duck (degree 121): 450-680g -- larger
- Harlequin Duck (degree 118): 500-700g -- larger
- Common Merganser (degree 119): 1,300-2,100g -- dramatically larger

The Bufflehead is roughly **one-fifth** the mass of the Common Merganser. It is spectacularly small for a diving duck.

### The Plumage -- Black and White with the Buffalo Head

The male Bufflehead in breeding plumage is a study in **bold graphic simplicity**:

**The head:** The defining feature. An enormous, round, puffy head (relative to body size) that is **iridescent black** (appearing deep purple-green in good light) with a large, prominent **white patch** that wraps from behind the eye across the back of the head. The white patch is roughly triangular/crescent-shaped and is diagnostic -- no other small duck has this combination of dark head with large white rear patch. When light hits the head, the iridescence shifts between purple and green.

**The body:** **White** flanks, breast, and belly contrasting sharply with a **black back**. The black-and-white contrast is stark and clean -- no mottling, no gradations. The visual effect is tuxedo-like: formal, graphic, high-contrast.

**The bill:** Small, gray-blue, compact. Proportionally tiny relative to the large head.

**The eyes:** Dark, set in the black head.

**In flight:** The Bufflehead shows large white wing patches that flash conspicuously. The flight is rapid and direct, with fast wingbeats -- the bird appears to buzz through the air, compact and urgent.

**The female:** More subdued. Dark brown-gray overall with a smaller white cheek patch (oval, behind and below the eye). The female's white patch is smaller and more contained than the male's but still diagnostic for the species.

The overall visual impression of the male Bufflehead is **striking in its simplicity**: a tiny, round bird with a huge dark head, a big white headpatch, and a clean black-and-white body. It is elegant in the way that minimal design is elegant -- no excess, no ornamentation beyond the essential pattern.

### Diving Behavior -- The Pocket Submarine

The Bufflehead is a **diving duck** -- it feeds by diving beneath the surface, propelling itself underwater with its feet. Despite its tiny size, the Bufflehead is an effective and active diver:

1. **Dive duration:** typically 12-25 seconds. Short dives by diving-duck standards, reflecting the shallow waters the species prefers.
2. **Dive depth:** typically 1-4 meters. Shallow diver, matching shallow water habitats.
3. **Prey:** primarily aquatic invertebrates -- insect larvae, crustaceans, mollusks. On saltwater: small crustaceans, snails. On freshwater: insect larvae, seeds.
4. **Diving style:** the Bufflehead dives with a distinctive forward lunge from the water surface, disappearing with almost no splash. The tiny body slips under the surface efficiently. Surfaces pop up quickly -- the bird reappears like a cork.
5. **Foraging pattern:** Buffleheads typically forage in small groups (2-10 birds), diving asynchronously. At least one bird is usually on the surface while others are below -- an informal sentinel system.

The Bufflehead's diving behavior is adapted to its small size: short dives in shallow water, targeting small prey. It does not compete with larger diving ducks (Common Goldeneye, scaup) for deep-water prey. Instead, it exploits the **shallow-water niche** that larger divers overlook.

### Cavity Nesting -- The Flicker Specialist

The Bufflehead is an **obligate cavity nester** with an extraordinary niche restriction: it is the **only duck in North America small enough to use Northern Flicker cavities** for nesting.

The dimensional specifics:
- **Northern Flicker cavity entrance:** approximately 6-7 cm diameter.
- **Bufflehead body diameter:** approximately 6-7 cm -- a tight but functional fit.
- **All other cavity-nesting ducks** (Hooded Merganser, Wood Duck, Common Goldeneye, Barrow's Goldeneye) are **too large** to enter flicker cavities. They require larger cavities created by Pileated Woodpeckers or natural decay.

This creates a **unique nesting niche**: Buffleheads can use flicker cavities that no other duck can access. In forests where Pileated Woodpecker cavities are scarce but flicker cavities are common, the Bufflehead has a competitive advantage. It nests in cavities that are too small for its competitors.

The dependency on flicker cavities means the Bufflehead's breeding distribution is linked to the Northern Flicker's range. Where flickers are common, Buffleheads can breed. Where flickers are absent or rare, Buffleheads must find alternative cavities (natural decay, other small woodpecker holes) or cannot breed.

Buffleheads also use **nest boxes** -- but the boxes must have small entrance holes (6-7 cm) to exclude larger species. Standard Wood Duck nest boxes (with larger entrances) are used by Wood Ducks and Hooded Mergansers but are rarely used by Buffleheads, which prefer the smaller opening.

**Clutch and brooding:** 6-11 eggs per clutch. The female incubates alone for 28-33 days. Ducklings leave the nest within 1-2 days of hatching, leaping from the cavity (like Wood Duck ducklings) but from lower heights, since flicker cavities tend to be lower than Pileated Woodpecker cavities.

### Winter Visitor to the Pacific Northwest

The Bufflehead's presence in the Pacific Northwest is primarily as a **winter visitor**. The species breeds in the boreal forests of Canada and Alaska (where Northern Flickers provide cavities) and migrates south for winter. In the PNW, Buffleheads arrive in October-November and remain through March-April.

Winter habitats in the PNW include:
- **Puget Sound bays and inlets** -- sheltered saltwater. Buffleheads are common on calm marine water throughout Puget Sound.
- **Freshwater lakes** -- any lake with adequate prey. Buffleheads are regular on Lake Washington, Lake Union, Green Lake, and similar urban and suburban lakes.
- **River mouths and estuaries** -- where freshwater meets salt.
- **Protected coastal areas** -- harbors, marinas, sheltered coves.

The Bufflehead is one of the most commonly seen wintering ducks in the Puget Sound region. Small groups of 2-10 birds are regularly observed on lakes and protected saltwater throughout the winter months. Their tiny size, active diving behavior, and bold black-and-white plumage make them conspicuous despite their small stature.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Gauthier 1993; Northern Flicker cavity ecology; PNW wintering waterfowl surveys)

## Acoustic Profile: Squeaky Peeping

The Bufflehead is one of the **quietest ducks in North America**. Its vocalizations are soft, high-pitched, and infrequently given -- a sharp contrast to the louder, more vocal ducks in the Anatidae streak.

### The Sound

- **The male call:** A soft, squeaky, high-pitched **peeping** -- sometimes described as a thin "squeak" or "eeek." The sound is remarkably delicate for a duck. Frequency range approximately 2,000-6,000 Hz -- high-pitched, almost mouse-like.
- **The female call:** A hoarse, low, guttural **"crrrk"** -- softer and less frequently given than even the male's squeaky peep. The female is even quieter than the male.
- **Context:** Vocalizations are given primarily during courtship and in close-range interactions. The Bufflehead does not call in flight (unlike the Wood Duck's ooo-eek). It does not call to maintain flock contact over distance. The calls are **intimate, close-range** vocalizations.
- **Overall volume:** Very soft. The Bufflehead's calls can be missed entirely even when the bird is close. This is a duck you see before you hear -- the visual is more prominent than the acoustic.

## Taxonomy: The Goldeneye Lineage

The Bufflehead belongs to genus *Bucephala* alongside two larger relatives:

1. **Common Goldeneye** (*Bucephala clangula*) -- larger (700-1,200g), also black-and-white, also cavity-nesting. Holarctic distribution. The Common Goldeneye uses larger cavities than the Bufflehead.
2. **Barrow's Goldeneye** (*Bucephala islandica*) -- similar size to Common Goldeneye, also black-and-white, also cavity-nesting. Pacific Northwest specialty. Breeds in montane lakes.

The Bufflehead is the **smallest member of the goldeneye group** by a significant margin -- roughly one-third the mass of its congeners. This size difference drives the niche differentiation: Buffleheads use flicker cavities; goldeneyes use larger woodpecker or natural cavities. Buffleheads dive in shallower water; goldeneyes dive deeper. Buffleheads take smaller prey; goldeneyes take larger prey.

The goldeneye lineage is a story of **size-mediated niche partitioning**: three species in one genus, separated by body size, which determines cavity size, dive depth, prey size, and habitat preference. The Bufflehead occupies the smallest niche in the lineage.
