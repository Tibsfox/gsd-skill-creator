Bufflehead

Bucephala albeola (Anatidae) — The Pocket Diver — Smallest Diving Duck in North America
squeaky peeping — fifth consecutive Anatidae (118–122) — North America’s smallest diving duck (270–450g) — black and white with puffy “buffalo head” — only duck small enough to nest in Northern Flicker cavities — winter visitor to PNW lakes — E=3.

The Buffalo Head — Black and White Simplicity

Male: iridescent black head (purple-green in light) with a large white patch wrapping from behind the eye across the back of the head. White flanks, breast, belly. Black back. Stark, clean contrast. The name “Bufflehead” from “buffalo head” — the disproportionately large, round, puffy head. Tuxedo-like elegance. Minimal design. No excess.

Anatidae Streak — Five Consecutive Waterfowl

118: Harlequin Duck (turbulent stream, E=5) → 119: Common Merganser (river, E=4) → 120: Hooded Merganser (wooded pond, E=4) → 121: Wood Duck (wooded pond, E=4) → 122: Bufflehead (lake, E=3). Five consecutive Anatidae. Five genera. Energy descending: E=5 → 4 → 4 → 4 → 3. The sound fades as the water calms.

The Flicker Connection — The Only Duck Small Enough

The Bufflehead is the only duck in North America small enough to nest in Northern Flicker cavities (~6–7 cm entrance). All other cavity-nesting ducks are too large. Obligate dependency: without flickers, Buffleheads have drastically reduced nesting habitat. Flicker at degree 99. Bufflehead at degree 122. 23 degrees later, the engine reveals the dependency. The Picidae-Anatidae cavity pipeline at its most intimate.

Squeaky Peeping

One of the quietest ducks in North America. A soft, high-pitched squeaky peeping — approximately 2,000–6,000 Hz, almost mouse-like. Given during courtship and close-range interactions. Does not call in flight. Does not call for flock contact. The calls are intimate, close-range. E=3 — the quietest entry in the Anatidae streak. A duck you see before you hear.

The Pocket Submarine

Smallest diving duck in North America: 270–450g (roughly one-fifth the mass of the Common Merganser). Dives 12–25 seconds in 1–4 meters of water. Targets small prey: insect larvae, crustaceans, mollusks. Forward lunge from the surface, almost no splash. Reappears like a cork. Forages in small groups with informal sentinel system.

Winter Visitor

Breeds in boreal Canada/Alaska (where flickers provide cavities). Winters on Puget Sound bays, freshwater lakes, river mouths, and harbors. One of the most commonly seen wintering ducks in the Puget Sound region. Small groups (2–10 birds) on Lake Washington, Lake Union, Green Lake, and protected saltwater throughout winter.
