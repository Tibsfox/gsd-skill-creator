## THE BUGLE -- ACOUSTIC ANATOMY

### A Two-Part Vocalization

The elk bugle is not a single sound but a two-part performance:

- **Part 1: The whistle.** The bugle begins as a high-pitched ascending whistle -- a piercing, rising tone that can reach 4,000 Hz. The sound is produced by the larynx and shaped by the nasal passages. It carries for kilometers through forest and valley. The whistle is the attention-getter, the declaration that a bull is present and in full rut.
- **Part 2: The grunt.** The whistle descends into a series of low, guttural grunts -- 100-300 Hz, rough and forceful. The grunts follow the whistle like punctuation. Some bulls produce a single long whistle followed by 2-3 grunts. Others alternate rapidly.
- **Frequency span.** The bugle spans roughly 100-4,000 Hz -- from deep guttural grunt to piercing whistle. This is one of the widest frequency ranges of any single vocalization in PNW mammals.
- **Duration.** A complete bugle (whistle + grunt sequence) lasts 2-5 seconds. Bulls bugle repeatedly during the rut, sometimes every few minutes.
- **Unique vocal anatomy.** Elk have a descended larynx (unusual in ungulates) that allows them to produce the wide frequency range. The descended larynx creates a longer vocal tract, enabling both the high whistle and the low grunt within a single vocalization.

(Sources: Elk acoustic anatomy; descended larynx studies; bugle frequency analysis; Cervidae vocal production)

## NAMED FOR A PRESIDENT -- THEODORE ROOSEVELT

### Conservation History

The Roosevelt Elk carries the name of Theodore Roosevelt:

- **Named in 1897.** Clinton Hart Merriam named the subspecies *roosevelti* in honor of Theodore Roosevelt, who would become president in 1901. Roosevelt was a committed conservationist and hunter who advocated for wildlife preservation.
- **Olympic National Park.** The Roosevelt Elk was a primary reason for the creation of the Mount Olympus National Monument (1909, by Theodore Roosevelt) and eventually Olympic National Park (1938). The elk needed protection from unregulated hunting and logging that destroyed their old-growth habitat.
- **Largest subspecies.** Roosevelt Elk are the largest of the four North American elk subspecies. Bulls can weigh 300-500 kg (700-1,100 lbs). They are stockier and darker than Rocky Mountain Elk, adapted to the dense, wet forests of the Pacific coast.
- **Population.** Approximately 5,000-7,000 Roosevelt Elk in the Olympic Peninsula. Additional populations in the Cascades, Willapa Hills, and coastal ranges. Total Washington population: approximately 10,000-12,000.

(Sources: Merriam 1897; Theodore Roosevelt conservation; Olympic National Park history; Roosevelt Elk population data; WDFW)

## OLD-GROWTH HABITAT DEPENDENCY

### The Forest Connection

Roosevelt Elk are deeply connected to old-growth forest:

- **Old-growth relationship.** Roosevelt Elk use old-growth forests for thermal cover, security, and winter range. The massive canopy of old-growth Sitka spruce, western red cedar, and Douglas fir provides shelter from rain and moderates temperature extremes. The elk need the forest; the forest is shaped by the elk.
- **Keystone herbivore.** Elk browsing shapes forest understory composition. Their selective feeding on shrubs, grasses, and young trees influences which plant species dominate. In the Hoh Rainforest (Olympic Peninsula), elk grazing maintains the distinctive open understory beneath the old-growth canopy.
- **River valley corridors.** Elk use river valleys as movement corridors between high-elevation summer range and low-elevation winter range. The Hoh, Queets, Quinault, and Elwha river valleys on the Olympic Peninsula are critical elk habitat.
- **Logging impacts.** Old-growth logging has reduced Roosevelt Elk habitat significantly. Elk can use second-growth forest but prefer old-growth for its structural complexity. The protection of old-growth on the Olympic Peninsula has been essential for maintaining the largest elk populations.
- **The bugle in old-growth.** The acoustic properties of old-growth forest -- tall canopy, open understory, soft forest floor -- create a natural amplification chamber for the bugle. The whistle carries through the cathedral-like space between the massive trunks. The grunts resonate against the forest floor.

(Sources: Old-growth forest ecology; Roosevelt Elk habitat; Hoh Rainforest; Olympic Peninsula elk; logging and elk habitat; forest acoustics)

## OLYMPIC PENINSULA POPULATIONS

### The Stronghold

The Olympic Peninsula is the core of Roosevelt Elk habitat:

- **Hoh Rainforest.** The most iconic elk-forest relationship in the PNW. Temperate rainforest with 3-4 meters of annual rainfall. Old-growth Sitka spruce, western hemlock, bigleaf maple draped in mosses and ferns. Elk herds of 20-60 animals move through the river valleys.
- **Queets and Quinault valleys.** Additional rainforest valleys with significant elk populations. Less visited than the Hoh but ecologically similar.
- **Elwha River restoration.** The removal of the Elwha dams (2011-2014) restored salmon habitat and opened river valley corridors for elk movement. The elk and salmon share the river system.
- **Hurricane Ridge.** Subalpine meadows at 1,500 m elevation. Elk use these high meadows in summer, returning to lower elevations for the fall rut and winter. The bugle carries across the open meadows with particular clarity.
- **Population dynamics.** Olympic Peninsula elk populations are relatively stable, managed by natural predation (cougars, bears), hunting (in some zones), and habitat availability. The park serves as a population reserve that exports elk to surrounding areas.

(Sources: Olympic National Park elk; Hoh Rainforest ecology; Elwha restoration; Hurricane Ridge; WDFW population management)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Roosevelt Elk Bull

**Bugle (Rut Display):**
- **Frequency:** Two-part: whistle 1,000-4,000 Hz (ascending), grunt 100-300 Hz (descending/pulsed).
- **Character:** High screaming whistle that breaks into guttural grunting. Piercing, startling, wild. The whistle has a metallic quality. The grunts are rough and forceful.
- **Duration:** 2-5 seconds per complete bugle. Repeated at intervals of 1-10 minutes during active display.
- **Context:** Fall rut (September-October). Bull announces presence, challenges rivals, attracts cows. Often accompanied by antler displays and ground-pawing.
- **Carry distance:** Whistle carries 2-5 km in mountain valleys and open meadows. Less in dense forest but still 1-2 km. One of the farthest-carrying mammalian sounds in the PNW.

**Bark (Alarm):**
- **Frequency:** 300-800 Hz. Sharp, short.
- **Character:** Explosive alarm bark. Used by both sexes. Signals danger.
- **Context:** Predator detection, human approach.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=7. The bugle is a sound of dominance and display -- the screaming whistle commands the valley, the grunts punctuate the claim. One of the most powerful vocal performances in PNW forests.

(Sources: Roosevelt Elk acoustics; bugle frequency analysis; carry distance studies; rut vocalization; engine internal analysis)
