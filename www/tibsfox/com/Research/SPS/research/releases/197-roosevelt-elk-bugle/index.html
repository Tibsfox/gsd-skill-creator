197°

Roosevelt Elk Bugle

Cervus canadensis roosevelti (Cervidae) — E=7 — Third Cervidae
First Roosevelt Elk entry. Elk trio begins (197–199). High screaming whistle (1,000–4,000 Hz) breaking into guttural grunts (100–300 Hz). Fall rut. Old-growth forest. Olympic Peninsula. E=7 — the screaming whistle that commands the valley.

The Bugle — Two-Part Vocalization

Part 1: The Whistle. A piercing ascending tone, 1,000–4,000 Hz. Metallic, wild, carrying 2–5 km through mountain valleys. Part 2: The Grunt. Descending into guttural pulses at 100–300 Hz. The widest frequency range of any single PNW mammal vocalization. Duration: 2–5 seconds. Repeated during the fall rut. E=7.

Old-Growth Habitat — The Forest Connection

Largest land mammal in the PNW. Bulls 300–500 kg. Named for Theodore Roosevelt (Merriam 1897). Old-growth Sitka spruce, western red cedar, Douglas fir — thermal cover, security, winter range. The Hoh Rainforest on the Olympic Peninsula: the iconic elk-forest relationship. The bugle resonates through cathedral-like old-growth.

Elk Trio — Degrees 197-199

197: Bugle (E=7) — the screaming whistle. 198: Cow call (E=5) — bark + mew, maternal. 199: Rut roar (E=7) — deep powerful bellowing. Three consecutive degrees. Three elk vocalizations. The most sustained single-species presence in the engine.

Theory Nodes

B

Two-Part Bugle — Whistle + Grunt

Milestone

High whistle (1,000-4,000 Hz) breaking into guttural grunts (100-300 Hz). Widest frequency range of any single PNW mammal vocalization. Descended larynx enables both extremes.

O

Old-Growth Acoustic Chamber

Advanced

Cathedral-like old-growth forest amplifies the bugle. Tall canopy, open understory, soft floor. The whistle carries through massive trunks. The grunts resonate against the forest floor.

T

Elk Trio — 197-199

Intermediate

Three consecutive degrees of Roosevelt Elk: bugle (E=7), cow call (E=5), rut roar (E=7). The most sustained single-species presence in the engine.

R

Roosevelt — Conservation Naming

Foundational

Named for Theodore Roosevelt (Merriam 1897). Largest elk subspecies. A primary reason for Olympic National Park creation. Conservation history embedded in the name.
