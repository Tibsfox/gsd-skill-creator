Bell-Like Peep Chorus — 91st Acoustic Element

The spring peeper-like chorus is a collective bell-like peeping — high-pitched, clear, crystalline individual “peep” calls produced by small treefrogs. Each call is a single ascending note, bell-like in quality, repeated at regular intervals [1, 2]. When hundreds or thousands chorus together, the individual peeps merge into a shimmering, ringing wall of sound. Higher-pitched and more crystalline than the Pacific Treefrog’s harsher krek-krek. E=4 — the collective chorus fills the wet meadow completely.

Second Hylidae Entry — Same Family, Different Voice

Hylidae (treefrogs) returns. First entry at degree 86 (Pacific Treefrog, krek-krek night chorus). Second entry at degree 90 (spring peeper-like chorus, bell-like peep). Same family, different chorus character. The Pacific Treefrog’s krek-krek is harsh and percussive; the spring peeper-like chorus is pure and bell-like. Two expressions of the treefrog vocal apparatus. Hylidae is the first family to appear twice in the non-mammalian sequence.

The Sound: Bell-Like Peep Chorus (91st Acoustic Element)

The call: The spring peeper-like chorus represents the collective sound of small Hylidae species producing high-pitched, clear, bell-like “peep” calls [1, 2]. Each individual call is a single ascending note — a pure tone that rises slightly in pitch, lasting approximately 0.1–0.3 seconds. The sound is crystalline — clear, bright, ringing. The quality is bell-like: each peep rings out and decays, creating a natural reverb in the wet meadow acoustic environment.

Why E=4: (1) Individual peeps carry well across wet meadow habitat. (2) Hundreds or thousands chorus simultaneously. (3) The collective chorus creates a shimmering wall of sound that fills the entire soundscape. (4) Continuous for hours on spring and early summer nights. (5) E=4 reflects the overwhelming, space-filling quality of the collective chorus.

Taxonomy: Hylidae (Second Entry)

Note: This is a family-level entry representing the spring peeper-like chorus sound. The true spring peeper (Pseudacris crucifer) is eastern; in the PNW, the Pacific chorus frog (Pseudacris regilla) and related Hylidae produce analogous bell-like choruses in wet meadow habitats, though the Pacific Treefrog’s primary call (krek-krek, degree 86) differs from the peeper-like register explored here [1, 2].

Classification: Class Amphibia (second amphibian, after Pacific Treefrog at 86). Order Anura. Family Hylidae — SECOND ENTRY (first at degree 86). The return to Hylidae after three consecutive insects (87-88-89) marks an insect-to-amphibian transition.

Ecology: The Wet Meadow Bell Choir

Habitat: Wet meadow. Saturated grasslands, seasonal wetlands, meadow edges near standing water. Wetter than the cricket’s dry meadow (87) but not open water like a pond. The wet meadow is a transition zone — neither fully terrestrial nor fully aquatic [1, 2].

Activity: Nocturnal and crepuscular. The chorus peaks on warm spring and early summer nights. Males call from concealed positions in grass and low vegetation near water. The chorus is triggered by temperature and humidity.

Context: The insect-to-amphibian transition (89 to 90) is a return to Amphibia after three consecutive insects. The engine revisits a class it had only briefly entered at degree 86.
