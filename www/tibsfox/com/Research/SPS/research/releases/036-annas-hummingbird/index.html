Anna’s Hummingbird

Calypte anna
metallic chip + dive display buzz — the sonic boom, the winter hummingbird, the one that stayed

The 37th Acoustic Element: The Sonic Boom

A brief, explosive, mechanically generated sound produced by tail feathers (R5) vibrating like a clarinet reed at the bottom of a 40-meter courtship dive at 385 body lengths per second — the fastest vertebrate dive by the body-length metric, nearly 2× the peregrine falcon’s stoop speed. A single 60-millisecond chirp at ~4 kHz (C8, four octaves above middle C), louder than the bird’s vocal capacity. ~9 g centripetal acceleration at pullout.

The progression: whisper (31) → buzz (32) → cadenza (33) → dialect (34) → thrum (35) → sonic boom (36). The thrum was continuous. The sonic boom is instantaneous. The thrum says “I am here.” The sonic boom says “LOOK AT ME.”

The Hummingbird Pair — Rufous (35) + Anna’s (36)

The engine’s fourth species pair and its most instructive. Same family (Trochilidae), different genus (Selasphorus vs. Calypte). Opposite strategies:

  • Rufous: Migrant. Summer. Follows the bloom. No song. Near Threatened (65% decline). Natural habitat.
  • Anna’s: Resident. Year-round. Waits for the bloom. Learned song. Least Concern (increasing). Urban habitat.

One leaves. One stays. The migrant is declining. The resident is expanding.

Taxonomy: The Veiled Jewel

Calypte — Ancient Greek kaluptre (“woman’s veil, headdress”), from kalupto (“to cover”). John Gould erected the genus in 1856 for hummingbirds whose males have “not only the throat, but the entire head as glitteringly resplendent as if they had been dipped in molten metal.” Anna — Anna de Belle Masséna, Duchess of Rivoli. Described by René Lesson in 1829.

Compare: Selasphorus rufus = “red flame-bearer” (pure description). Calypte anna = “Anna’s veiled one” (a person’s name and structural color physics). The flame-bearer names what it looks like. The veiled one names who named it.

The Sound: Three Components

1. Metallic Chip (Vocal)

A sharp, hard tsik or chip, approximately 6–8 kHz, with a metallic quality distinctly different from the Rufous’s buzzy chitter. Given by both sexes in foraging, contact, and aggressive contexts.

2. Complex Learned Song (Vocal)

A thin, squeaky, buzzy series lasting over 10 seconds, delivered from a conspicuous perch. Learned by imitation — one of the only hummingbirds with culturally transmitted song. Males begin practicing at 4–7 months; song crystallizes by ~11 months. Geographic dialects exist. First non-passerine vocal learner in the engine.

3. Dive Display Chirp (Mechanical)

Male climbs to ~40 m, plummets at 27.3 m/s (385 body lengths/s), spreads tail for ~60 ms at the nadir. The outer tail feather (R5) vibrates like a clarinet reed, producing an explosive tonal chirp at ~4 kHz (C8). Louder than vocal capacity. ~9 g at pullout. Synchronized with gorget flash.

Learned Song — Convergent Vocal Learning

Three bird lineages independently evolved vocal learning: songbirds, parrots, and hummingbirds. Anna’s Hummingbird is the best-documented hummingbird vocal learner. Males have an enlarged higher vocal center (HVC), androgen-dependent. The Rufous Hummingbird (same family) does NOT learn its songs. Within Trochilidae, the engine encounters both innate and learned vocalizations.

This is the first non-passerine vocal learner in the engine — proof that song learning evolved independently in an entirely different avian lineage.

Urban/Coastal — First Urban Ecosystem

The engine’s first explicitly urban ecosystem. Defined by human modification: residential gardens, ornamental plantings, sugar-water feeders, urban parks. Anna’s Hummingbird did not evolve in Seattle — it expanded into human-created habitat. First records in Seattle ~1964. Range expanded ~900 miles northward in 60 years.

Drivers: Ornamental winter-blooming plants, year-round feeders, climate moderation, urban heat islands. The bird bet its northern range on humans continuing to garden. So far, the bet is paying off.

Year-Round Resident — The Winter Hummingbird

The northernmost year-round resident hummingbird in the world. Every other PNW hummingbird migrates to Mexico. Anna’s stays through January ice, February snow, the long dark months. Present in Seattle twelve months a year.

Survival mechanism: Nightly torpor. Body temperature drops from 40°C to 8.8°C. Heart rate from 1,200 to 50 bpm. Breathing from 245/min to <10. Metabolic rate drops 95%. Gains 16% body weight in fat by day, burns it all by dawn. Razor-thin margin.

The Rufous migrates. Anna’s endures.

Conservation Mirror — Opposite Trajectories

Same family, opposite fates:

  • Rufous (35): Near Threatened. 65% decline. Natural habitat degrading. Migrant losing flowers.
  • Anna’s (36): Least Concern. ~9.6 million, increasing 2%/yr. Urban habitat expanding. Resident gaining feeders.

The migrant depending on wild flowers is losing. The resident depending on human gardens is winning. The sharpest conservation contrast in the engine.

E=5 — Explosive Detonation

Third E=5 in the engine. First back-to-back E=5 (degrees 35–36). Three models of maximum energy:

  • Bohemian Waxwing (32): Collective swarm. Hundreds buzzing.
  • Rufous Hummingbird (35): Sustained intensity. Continuous thrum.
  • Anna’s Hummingbird (36): Explosive detonation. Single 60-ms chirp from a 40-m dive.

Orchestra tutti vs. sustained solo vs. cannon shot.

The Sonic Boom foundational

Tail-feather R5 vibrating like a clarinet reed at 27.3 m/s. 385 body lengths/s — fastest vertebrate. 60 ms, ~4 kHz, ~9 g. The aeromechanical category pushed to its extreme.

U

Urban/Coastal Ecosystem foundational

First urban, first human-defined ecosystem. Ornamental plants, feeders, urban heat islands. Anna’s expanded into human-created habitat within living memory.

R

Year-Round Residence foundational

Only PNW hummingbird that overwinters. Nightly torpor: 40°C→8.8°C, 1,200→50 bpm. The opposite of migration. The price of staying.

L

Learned Song intermediate

First non-passerine vocal learner. Convergent evolution with songbirds. Geographic dialects. The Rufous (same family) has no song. Anna’s has learned, culturally transmitted song.

M

Conservation Mirror intermediate

Same family, opposite trajectories. Rufous: declining 65%. Anna’s: increasing 2%/yr. The migrant losing, the resident winning. The engine’s sharpest conservation contrast.

E=5 Explosive Display intermediate

Third E=5. First back-to-back E=5. Three models: collective (32), sustained (35), explosive (36). The dive chirp concentrates all energy into 60 ms.
