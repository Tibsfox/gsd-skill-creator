# Release 036 Retrospective -- Anna's Hummingbird (Degree 36)

**Release:** 036 of 360
**Species:** Anna's Hummingbird (*Calypte anna*)
**Sound:** metallic chip + dive display buzz
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the thirty-seventh release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-36), the second Trochilidae, and the completion of the hummingbird pair. The engine needed to explore what the second hummingbird could teach about acoustic strategy, temporal ecology, and human-wildlife interaction. Anna's Hummingbird delivers on all three -- and adds the engine's first urban ecosystem, the first non-passerine vocal learner, and the fastest vertebrate dive by the body-length metric.

Four structural contributions:

1. **The sonic boom -- the 37th acoustic element.** A brief, explosive, mechanically generated sound produced by tail feathers (R5) vibrating like a clarinet reed at the bottom of a 40-meter courtship dive at 385 body lengths per second. After the thrum (degree 35, continuous aeromechanical hum), the sonic boom pushes the mechanical sound category to its extreme: instantaneous, explosive, concentrated. The fastest vertebrate dive produces the loudest sound relative to body size.

2. **Urban/coastal -- the engine's first urban ecosystem.** Thirty-six degrees of forest, meadow, riparian, sky, cone crop, and flower meadow -- and now, for the first time, the built environment. Anna's Hummingbird did not evolve in the PNW. It expanded into human-created habitat within living memory (~1964), enabled by ornamental plantings, sugar-water feeders, and urban microclimates. The ecosystem exists because humans built it.

3. **Year-round residence -- the winter hummingbird.** Anna's Hummingbird is the only hummingbird that overwinters in the Pacific Northwest. Every other PNW hummingbird migrates to Mexico. Anna's stays, entering nightly torpor (body temperature dropping from 40 C to 8.8 C, heart rate from 1,200 to 50 bpm, metabolic rate reduced 95%). Same thermodynamic problem as the Rufous -- opposite solution.

4. **Learned song -- the first non-passerine vocal learner.** Anna's Hummingbird learns its song through imitation, with subsong, plastic song, and crystallized adult song stages identical in principle to oscine passerine song development. Geographic dialects exist. The Rufous Hummingbird (same family, degree 35) does NOT learn its songs. Convergent evolution of vocal learning in an entirely different avian lineage.

### What the Pair Teaches

Every previous pair explored one axis of contrast:
- Flicker pair (21-22): two dimensions of the same species (hammer + resonance)
- Waxwing pair (31-32): quiet vs. loud within a family (whisper + buzz)
- Finch pair (33-34): soloist vs. tribe (cadenza + dialect)

The hummingbird pair explores **almost every axis simultaneously**: temporal strategy, ecosystem type, vocal learning, conservation trajectory, range dynamics, human dependence, dive mechanics, and acoustic strategy. It is the engine's densest pair -- the one with the most to teach about how two closely related species can occupy the same landscape with entirely different strategies.

The most striking lesson: **the migrant is dying and the resident is thriving.** The Rufous Hummingbird, which follows natural wildflower blooms across 3,900 miles, is Near Threatened with a 65% population decline. Anna's Hummingbird, which stays in human-modified urban habitat year-round, is Least Concern and increasing. Same family, opposite fates. The specialist depending on nature is losing. The generalist depending on humans is winning.

This is not a simple story of "urban good, wild bad." Anna's dependence on human behavior (feeder culture, ornamental horticulture) creates structural fragility. The expansion is contingent. But right now, in 2026, the trajectories are clear: the bird that stays is the bird that grows.

## The Transition: Thrum to Sonic Boom

### From Continuous to Explosive

| Dimension | Degree 35 (Rufous Hummingbird) | Degree 36 (Anna's Hummingbird) |

| Acoustic element | #36: The Thrum | #37: The Sonic Boom |

| Speed context | Hovering (0 m/s forward) | Diving (27.3 m/s, 385 BL/s) |

The thrum and the sonic boom are the two poles of aeromechanical sound: continuous vs. instantaneous, ambient vs. explosive, sustained vs. concentrated. Together they define the full range of what hummingbird bodies can do with air and feathers. The thrum is the engine's idle. The sonic boom is the redline.

The circle has moved from thrum (degree 35, the Rufous Hummingbird's continuous wingbeat hum, the sound of flight itself, the body as instrument) to **sonic boom** (degree 36, the Anna's Hummingbird's explosive dive chirp, the sound of committed display, 385 body lengths per second concentrated into 60 milliseconds of tail-feather vibration). The word for degree 36 is "sonic boom" because it captures what Anna's Hummingbird adds to the engine's acoustic vocabulary: the instantaneous extreme. The thrum was the first mechanical sound -- gentle, continuous, a hum you could mistake for an insect on a summer afternoon. The sonic boom is the mechanical sound you cannot ignore -- a single chirp that travels across a garden, a park, a hillside, a chirp produced by a 4-gram bird diving from higher than a 13-story building at a speed that would kill most vertebrates, pulling 9 g's at the bottom of the arc, spreading its tail for a fraction of a second, and letting the air do the rest. The reed vibrates. The feather sings. The female watches. The sound says nothing in particular -- it has no phonemic content, no syllable structure, no learned pattern (unlike the bird's actual song, which IS learned). The dive chirp is pure physics: feather shape + airspeed + gravity = sound. And that sound, measured by Clark in 2008 and 2009 with high-speed cameras and wind tunnel experiments, is the loudest thing a vertebrate this small can produce. Not the loudest sound. The loudest relative to body size. A 4-gram animal producing a burst loud enough to startle a human standing nearby. The sonic boom is not the hummingbird's song (that is the thin, squeaky, learned warble from the perch). It is not the chip call (that is the metallic tsik for everyday communication). The sonic boom is the *event* -- the 12-second display that begins with a wavering climb and ends with an explosive descent, the moment when the bird converts altitude into sound, potential energy into acoustic energy, gravity into courtship. And it works. Females watch. Males repeat. The dive is performed again and again, dozens of times in a display session, the male climbing and plunging in an arc that traces a J against the sky. The thrum was everywhere -- present whenever a hummingbird flew. The sonic boom is specific -- present only when a male decides to commit to the dive, to climb to 40 meters, to fold his wings, to fall. The thrum required nothing but flight. The sonic boom requires *intent*. And this bird -- this small, iridescent, winter-surviving, song-learning, feeder-visiting, torpor-enduring jewel of the urban PNW -- performs the dive in January, when no other hummingbird is within a thousand miles, when the feeders ice over and the ornamental camellias are the only flowers, when the sky is gray and the rain falls sideways and the temperature dips below freezing and the bird has to decide each night whether to eat enough to survive the torpor or whether this will be the night the margin runs out. It stays. It dives. It booms. Anna's Hummingbird. The one that stayed. Calypte anna. The veiled jewel of the winter garden. Named for a duchess. Studied by physicists. Fed by gardeners. Surviving on the edge of what vertebrate metabolism permits, in a city that did not exist when this species evolved, in an ecosystem that humans created by accident, performing the fastest dive relative to body size of any vertebrate alive, producing a sound from its tail feathers that it could not produce from its throat, learning a song that its cousin the Rufous never learned, thriving where the Rufous is dying, and doing all of this at 4 grams, in the rain, in the dark, in the cold, in Seattle, in January. The sonic boom.

## Five Structural Advances

### 1. The Sonic Boom: Aeromechanical Sound at Maximum

The engine's mechanical sound vocabulary now spans two poles:

| Sound | Degree | Duration | Speed | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thrum | 35 | Continuous | Hovering (0 m/s forward) | Ambient hum |
| **Sonic boom** | **36** | **60 ms** | **27.3 m/s (385 BL/s)** | **Explosive chirp** |

Together they define the full spectrum of aeromechanical sound production in hummingbirds: the continuous drone of wingbeats and the instantaneous detonation of a dive chirp. The mechanical sound category opened at degree 35 is now characterized at both its extremes.

### 2. Urban/Coastal: The Engine's First Human-Defined Ecosystem

Three consecutive novel ecosystem categories:

| Degree | Ecosystem | Category |
|---|---|---|
| 34 | Cone crop | Resource-defined |
| 35 | Flower meadow | Mutualism-defined |
| **36** | **Urban/coastal** | **Human-defined** |

The engine has moved from natural habitats (degrees 0-33) through resource- and mutualism-defined ecosystems (34-35) to a fully human-modified habitat (36). The ecosystem vocabulary has expanded more in three degrees than in the preceding thirty-four.

### 3. Year-Round Residence at the Metabolic Edge

The engine's "resident" category now spans an enormous range:

| Species | Degree | Winter Strategy | Metabolic Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Black-capped Chickadee | 3 | Caching seeds, reduced metabolism | Moderate |
| Red-breasted Nuthatch | 5 | Caching seeds, bark gleaning | Moderate |
| **Anna's Hummingbird** | **36** | **Nightly torpor (95% metabolic reduction)** | **Extreme** |

"Resident" for a chickadee means caching food and finding shelter. "Resident" for a hummingbird means shutting down to near-death every night and hoping there's enough fat to restart at dawn. The same word covers wildly different survival strategies.

### 4. Learned Song: Vocal Learning Without Passeriformes

Anna's Hummingbird is the engine's first non-passerine vocal learner. The significance:

| Lineage | Order | Song Learning | Engine Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oscine passerines | Passeriformes | Yes (ancestral) | Wren, thrush, warbler, finch, etc. |
| Suboscine passerines | Passeriformes | No (innate) | Pacific-slope Flycatcher (degree 6) |
| **Hummingbirds** | **Apodiformes** | **Yes (convergent)** | **Anna's Hummingbird (degree 36)** |

Vocal learning evolved independently in hummingbirds and songbirds. Same behavior, different lineages, convergent evolution. The engine now contains examples of all three patterns: learned song (most passerines), innate calls (flycatcher, Rufous Hummingbird), and convergent learned song (Anna's Hummingbird).

### 5. Conservation Mirror: Opposite Trajectories in One Family

The hummingbird pair is the engine's sharpest conservation contrast:

| Species | Degree | Status | Trend | Habitat Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rufous Hummingbird | 35 | Near Threatened | Declining 65% | Natural (flower meadow) |
| **Anna's Hummingbird** | **36** | **Least Concern** | **Increasing 2%/yr** | **Human-modified (urban)** |

Same family. Same order. Same basic physiology. Opposite fates. The lesson is stark: in the 21st-century PNW, the species that depends on natural habitat is declining, and the species that depends on human-modified habitat is expanding. This is not an endorsement of urbanization. It is a diagnosis: the natural landscape is degrading faster than the urban landscape, and the species that can adapt to the urban landscape are the ones that survive.

## What Worked

### 1. The hummingbird pair is the engine's most instructive pair
Every previous pair explored one or two axes of contrast. The hummingbird pair explores ten or more: season, ecosystem, song, conservation, range, human dependence, dive mechanics, acoustic element, temporal strategy, gorget color. The density of contrast within a single family teaches more about ecological strategy than any previous pair.

### 2. "Sonic boom" is the right name
After "thrum" (degree 35, continuous), "sonic boom" captures the opposite: explosive, instantaneous, concentrated. The name evokes both the physical phenomenon (extreme speed producing a pressure wave) and the experiential quality (a startling burst from something too small to produce it). The progression from thrum to sonic boom completes the aeromechanical pair.

### 3. Urban ecosystem opens a new structural dimension
Thirty-five degrees of natural and semi-natural habitats, then suddenly: the city. Anna's Hummingbird is the right species to introduce the urban ecosystem because its range expansion IS the story of urbanization. The bird arrived in Seattle within living memory, carried by ornamental horticulture and feeder culture. The ecosystem and the species are inseparable.

### 4. Learned song adds convergent evolution to the engine's vocabulary
Vocal learning has been assumed throughout the passerine arc (degrees 1-34), but it was never explicitly flagged as a distinct structural property. Anna's Hummingbird makes it explicit by being a non-passerine that learns its song -- proof that the behavior evolves independently, that it is not a songbird monopoly.

### 5. The conservation mirror is structurally powerful
Placing a declining species (Rufous, Near Threatened) next to a thriving species (Anna's, Least Concern) in the same family creates an unavoidable contrast. The lesson -- that human-dependent species are expanding while nature-dependent species are declining -- is more compelling when the two species are this closely related.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. CRITICAL: No marine species in 36 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
Thirty-six degrees. The water still has not spoken. The marine gap is now the engine's sole existential structural crisis. This must be resolved at degree 37 or the structural deficit becomes systemic.

### 2. Back-to-back E=5 creates new compression
E=5 at degrees 35 AND 36 is the first consecutive maximum-energy pair. While the pair uses E=5 differently (sustained vs. explosive), the dynamic range continues to compress upward. E=3 has not appeared for 16 degrees (since degree 20). The engine needs a low-energy entry.

### 3. Back-to-back Apodiformes risks a mini-streak
The engine just broke a 12-degree Passeriformes streak, and now it has two consecutive Apodiformes degrees. This is not yet a problem (it is a family pair), but degree 37 should not be a third hummingbird. Taxonomic diversity requires moving to a different order.

### 4. No corvid in 36 degrees
No Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. The PNW's most intelligent, acoustically complex, culturally significant birds remain absent. This is increasingly anomalous.

### 5. No raptor in 36 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Osprey, or Peregrine Falcon. Top predators absent. A marine-associated raptor (Bald Eagle, Osprey) could address both the raptor gap and the marine gap simultaneously.

### 6. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Thirty-two degrees since the Spotted Owl. Barn Owl, Great Horned Owl, Barred Owl, or Common Nighthawk are overdue.

## Recommendations for Release 037

The hummingbird pair is complete. The sonic boom is named. The urban ecosystem is established. Now the urgent priorities:

1. **A MARINE SPECIES (ABSOLUTE PRIORITY -- 36 DEGREES OVERDUE).** This is no longer a recommendation. It is a structural emergency. Glaucous-winged Gull, Common Loon, Western Grebe, Pigeon Guillemot, Rhinoceros Auklet, Marbled Murrelet, or Double-crested Cormorant. The project is named for Puget Sound. The Sound MUST speak at degree 37.

2. **LOW ENERGY (E=2 or E=3).** Many marine species are moderate to low energy. A loon (E=2-3?), a grebe (E=3?), a cormorant (E=2?) would restore the dynamic range. Sixteen degrees without E=3 is unsustainable.

3. **A DIFFERENT ORDER.** Two consecutive Apodiformes is a pair. Three would be a streak. Degree 37 must leave Apodiformes. A marine bird would naturally bring a new order (Gaviiformes, Podicipediformes, Charadriiformes, Procellariiformes, or Suliformes).

4. **A CORVID (URGENT -- 36 degrees).** Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven.

5. **A RAPTOR (URGENT -- 36 degrees).** Bald Eagle or Osprey would address both the raptor gap and the marine adjacency.

6. **A NOCTURNAL SPECIES (OVERDUE -- 32 degrees).** Great Horned Owl, Barred Owl, or Barn Owl.

High in the eastern Cascades, where the ponderosa stands open and the air smells of sun-warmed bark and resin, a small bird with a crimson crown sat at the top of the tallest snag and sang a cadenza -- a rolling, tumbling warble incorporating the crossbill's call, the tanager's hoarse phrase, the robin's carol, the chickadee's fee-bee. The Cassin's Finch. The cadenza. The soloist played every instrument. And somewhere in a Douglas fir canopy, a flock of small birds hung upside down from cones, working the scales with bills that cross at the tips. Kip-kip-kip. Sharp. Percussive. The Red Crossbill. The dialect. The type call that sorts the tribe. And then -- a sound that is not a call, not a song, not a drum. A hum. Low, continuous, steady, the sound of air pushed through feathers fifty-two times every second. A copper blur hovering at a red columbine. The Rufous Hummingbird. The thrum. Three grams of defiance. The red flame-bearer. And now -- a different sound from a different hummingbird. Not a hum but a crack. Not continuous but instantaneous. A small iridescent green bird with a head dipped in molten magenta perches on a bare branch in a Seattle backyard in January. It is raining. The feeder swings in the wind. The camellia is the only thing blooming. The bird has been here since October. It will be here in April. It does not leave. It has nowhere to go that is better than here, because here is where the feeders are, here is where the ornamental plants bloom in winter, here is where it learned its scratchy complex song from the male on the next block, here is where it enters torpor every night and wakes every morning to a world where it is the only hummingbird for a thousand miles. And now it climbs. Wavering, ascending, higher than the roof, higher than the Douglas fir, higher than a thirteen-story building, forty meters into the gray sky. And it folds its wings. And it falls. Straight down. Accelerating. Twenty-seven meters per second. Three hundred and eighty-five body lengths per second. Faster, relative to its size, than a peregrine falcon, faster than a fighter jet. Nine g's at the bottom of the arc. And in that fraction of a second, the split-second when the tail spreads and the outer feathers catch the air and vibrate like a reed in a clarinet -- a chirp. Explosive. Tonal. Four kilohertz. C8. Four octaves above middle C. Louder than anything this bird could produce with its voice. The feather sings what the throat cannot. The dive speaks what the song does not. The sonic boom. And then it arcs upward, circles back, lands on the branch, and waits. The feeder swings. The rain falls. The camellia holds its bloom. It is January in Seattle. The only hummingbird for a thousand miles preens a wing feather and considers another dive. Calypte anna. The veiled jewel. The one that stayed. The sonic boom.*
