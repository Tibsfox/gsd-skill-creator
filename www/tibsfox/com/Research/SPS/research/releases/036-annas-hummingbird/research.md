## Context: The Hummingbird Pair

Degree 36 is the **second consecutive hummingbird**. The Rufous Hummingbird (degree 35) broke the 12-degree Passeriformes streak, introduced Apodiformes and Trochilidae, established the thrum as the 36th acoustic element, and opened the engine to mechanical sound production. Now, at degree 36, Anna's Hummingbird continues in the same family but changes *everything else*.

This is the engine's **fourth species pair** -- two consecutive degrees from the same family:

| Pair | Degrees | Family | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Northern Flicker | 21-22 | Picidae | Same species, two acoustic dimensions (hammer + resonance) |
| Cedar + Bohemian Waxwing | 31-32 | Bombycillidae | Two species, quiet vs. loud (whisper + buzz) |
| Cassin's Finch + Red Crossbill | 33-34 | Fringillidae | Two species, soloist vs. tribe (cadenza + dialect) |
| **Rufous + Anna's Hummingbird** | **35-36** | **Trochilidae** | **Two species, migrant vs. resident (thrum + sonic boom)** |

Each pair has explored a different axis of contrast within a shared family. The hummingbird pair explores **temporal strategy**: the Rufous arrives for the summer, defends flower meadows, and leaves. Anna's *never leaves*. It is the only hummingbird that stays through the PNW winter. The Rufous follows the bloom. Anna's waits for the bloom to return.

And the acoustic contrast is extraordinary. The Rufous introduced the thrum -- the continuous aeromechanical hum of wingbeats. Anna's introduces something the Rufous hinted at but did not deliver in its fullest expression: the **dive-bomb chirp**, a sonic event so loud relative to body size that it rivals anything in the vertebrate world, produced by tail feathers vibrating like a reed in a clarinet at the bottom of a 40-meter vertical plunge at 385 body lengths per second.

Same family. Same order. Different genus. Different strategy. Different sound. Different season. One leaves. One stays.

## Taxonomy: The Veiled Jewel

### Classification

- **Order:** Apodiformes
- **Family:** Trochilidae (Hummingbirds)
- **Genus:** *Calypte* (Gould, 1856)
- **Species:** *Calypte anna* (Lesson, 1829)
- **Common Name:** Anna's Hummingbird

### Etymology

*Calypte* derives from Ancient Greek *kaluptre* ("woman's veil" or "headdress"), from *kalupto* ("to cover") [1, 2]. John Gould erected the genus in 1856 for hummingbirds whose males have "not only the throat, but the entire head as glitteringly resplendent as if they had been dipped in molten metal" [1, 2]. The genus name evokes the iridescent crown and gorget that cover the male's head like a jeweled veil -- a headdress of structural color that shifts from brilliant magenta-rose to dull black as the viewing angle changes. The veil is not pigment. It is physics. Layers of melanosomes in the feather barbules create thin-film interference, reflecting specific wavelengths of light while absorbing others. The color is the veil -- it exists only when the light strikes correctly.

*Anna* honors Anna de Belle Massena, Duchess of Rivoli, wife of the ornithologist and collector Francois Victor Massena, 3rd Duke of Rivoli [1, 2, 3]. The French naturalist Rene Lesson described and illustrated the species in 1829 from a California specimen collected by Paolo-Emilio Botta. Lesson regarded it as "one of the most beautiful hummingbirds" on account of "the bright sparkle of a red cap of the richest amethyst" on the male's head, and named it in honor of the duchess [2, 3].

Compare with the Rufous Hummingbird's binomial: *Selasphorus rufus* = "red flame-bearer" -- light and color, the visual. *Calypte anna* = "Anna's veiled one" -- a person's name and a reference to the iridescent headdress. The flame-bearer is named for what it looks like. The veiled one is named for *who named it* and for the physics of its plumage. One name is pure description. The other is social history -- colonial-era European naturalists naming New World birds after aristocratic patrons.

### Monotypic

Anna's Hummingbird is monotypic -- no subspecies are recognized [1, 2]. Like the Rufous Hummingbird, it is one species across its entire range. But unlike the Rufous, whose range is vast and ancient (Alaska to Mexico, millions of years), Anna's current range is largely a 20th-century expansion. The bird that now breeds from Baja California to British Columbia was historically restricted to the chaparral and oak woodlands of California and northwestern Mexico [1, 2, 4].

### Calypte: A Different Genus

The hummingbird pair spans two genera:

- **Degree 35:** *Selasphorus* (flame-bearers) -- nine species, all small, rufous-toned, characterized by buzzy wing sounds and dramatic tail-feather sonations during dives. Migrant strategies predominate.
- **Degree 36:** *Calypte* (veiled ones) -- two species: Anna's Hummingbird (*C. anna*) and Costa's Hummingbird (*C. costae*). Both are relatively sedentary compared to *Selasphorus*, with males possessing elaborate iridescent crowns and gorgets that cover the entire head. *Calypte* males sing complex, learned songs -- unusual for hummingbirds [5, 6].

The generic contrast maps the behavioral contrast: *Selasphorus* are migrant flame-bearers that chase the bloom across hemispheres. *Calypte* are resident jewels that stay put, defend permanent territories, and sing -- actually *sing*, with learned, culturally transmitted songs. The genus difference is not trivial. It predicts the ecological strategy that makes Anna's Hummingbird unique in the PNW.

### Trochilidae: The Family Continues

Trochilidae entered the engine at degree 35 as the 18th family. Degree 36 adds the second member, making Trochilidae the **seventh family with multiple degrees** in the engine (after Turdidae, Paridae, Parulidae, Fringillidae, Hirundinidae, and Bombycillidae). Two hummingbird species in two degrees -- the same density as the waxwing pair (31-32) and the finch pair (33-34).

## The Sound: Metallic Chip + Dive Display Buzz

### The Metallic Chip -- Vocal Call

Anna's Hummingbird's most frequently heard vocalization is a sharp, metallic *chip* -- a single, hard, high-pitched note given in a variety of contexts [5, 7, 8]:

- **Call characteristics:** A single sharp *tsik* or *chip*, approximately 6-8 kHz, with a hard metallic quality distinct from the Rufous Hummingbird's buzzy chitter [7, 8]. Where the Rufous gives rapid series of buzzy notes ("ch-ch-ch-ch-ch"), Anna's gives isolated, crisp chips -- single percussive strikes rather than machine-gun bursts.
- **Context:** Given during foraging, perching, chasing intruders, and as a contact call. More versatile than the Rufous's chitter, which is almost exclusively aggressive. Anna's chip serves multiple social functions [7, 8].
- **Female and male:** Both sexes give the chip call, making it the species' universal contact note [7, 8].

### The Song -- Learned Complexity

Anna's Hummingbird is **one of the only hummingbirds with a complex, learned song** [5, 6, 9, 10]. This is extraordinary and demands attention:

- **Song structure:** A thin, squeaky, buzzy series of high-pitched phrases lasting over 10 seconds. Interspersed with buzzes, chips, and scratchy warbling notes. Delivered from a conspicuous perch, often the same perch used day after day [5, 7, 8].
- **Song learning:** Males learn their songs by imitation, beginning to practice at approximately 4-7 months of age (subsong). By the onset of the next breeding season (~November), song has crystallized into an adult form. Syllable types, frequency, rhythm, and syntax are all learned [6, 9, 10].
- **Geographic variation:** Neighbors share similar song types, but populations separated by even a few kilometers may differ in syllable structure -- cultural dialects, transmitted socially rather than genetically [6, 10, 11].
- **Neural basis:** Males possess an enlarged higher vocal center (HVC) that develops in response to testosterone. Females and non-singing hummingbird species (including the Rufous Hummingbird) lack this brain structure [5, 6, 10].
- **Uniqueness:** Song learning is rare among non-passerines. Among birds, it is well-documented in three groups: oscine passerines (songbirds), parrots, and hummingbirds. Anna's Hummingbird is the best-studied example of hummingbird vocal learning. The fact that hummingbirds learn their songs *independently* of songbirds represents convergent evolution of vocal learning -- the same complex behavior evolving separately in unrelated lineages [5, 6, 10].

This is structurally significant for the engine. The Rufous Hummingbird (degree 35) had no complex song -- its vocal repertoire was dominated by aggressive chittering. Anna's Hummingbird has a learned, culturally transmitted song. Within the same family, the engine encounters both "no song" and "learned song." The contrast mirrors the passerine world: some species have simple innate calls; others have elaborate learned songs. Trochilidae, it turns out, contains both.

### The Dive Display Buzz -- The Sonic Boom

The male Anna's Hummingbird performs one of the most dramatic courtship displays of any bird, and the sound it produces at the climax of the dive is among the most extraordinary acoustic events in the avian world [5, 7, 12, 13, 14]:

**The dive:**

1. The male begins by hovering 2-4 meters in front of the display object (a female, or sometimes a person, a hawk, or another hummingbird).
2. He climbs in a wavering, ascending flight to a height of approximately **40 meters** (131 feet) -- significantly higher than the Rufous Hummingbird's 10-30 meter display climb [5, 13, 14].
3. At the apex, he folds his wings and plummets in a near-vertical dive.
4. Maximum speed reaches **27.3 m/s** -- equivalent to **385 body lengths per second** [14].
5. At the nadir of the dive, within approximately 0.5 meters of the display object, the bird spreads its tail feathers for a split second, producing an **explosive chirp** -- the dive-bomb buzz [12, 13, 14].
6. He then makes a circular arc back to the starting point.
7. The entire dive lasts approximately **12 seconds** [5, 13].

**385 body lengths per second.** This is the highest known length-specific velocity attained by any vertebrate [14]. For comparison:

| Organism | Max speed (body lengths/s) |
|---|---|
| **Anna's Hummingbird (dive)** | **385** |
| Peregrine Falcon (stoop) | ~200 |
| Cheetah (sprint) | ~16 |
| Fighter jet (combat speed) | ~39 [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- depends on jet model and metric] |

The Anna's Hummingbird in a courtship dive moves nearly twice as fast, relative to its body length, as a peregrine falcon in a hunting stoop -- and about 10 times faster than a fighter jet relative to fuselage length [14]. At the bottom of the dive, the bird pulls approximately **9 g's** of centripetal acceleration as it transitions from vertical plunge to horizontal flight [14].

**The sound mechanism:**

The explosive chirp at the dive's nadir is **not vocal** [12, 13]. Christopher Clark's 2008 research definitively demonstrated that the sound is produced by the bird's outer tail feathers:

- **Which feathers:** The outermost tail feathers (rectrix 5, or R5) are modified in males -- narrower and stiffer than in females [12, 13].
- **How they produce sound:** Wind tunnel tests confirmed that the R5 feathers vibrate like a reed in a clarinet when exposed to airflow at dive speeds. The trailing vane of R5 flutters at a specific frequency, generating a tonal sound [12, 13].
- **The split-second spread:** The bird spreads its tail for an instant (~60 ms) at the nadir, exposing R5 to the maximum airflow. The brief spread at maximum speed produces a loud, sharp chirp [12, 13].
- **Frequency:** The dive chirp is tonal with a fundamental frequency of approximately **4 kHz** (roughly C8, four octaves above middle C), with higher harmonics [12].
- **Loudness:** The dive chirp is louder than the bird's vocal song, and likely too loud to be produced vocally by the bird's small syrinx [12]. This is a mechanical sound amplified by dive speed -- the faster the bird dives, the louder the chirp.

**Comparison with the Rufous J-dive:**

| Dimension | Rufous J-Dive (Degree 35) | Anna's Dive (Degree 36) |
|---|---|---|
| Climb height | 10-30 meters | **~40 meters** |
| Max speed | ~25 m/s [NEEDS VERIFICATION] | **27.3 m/s (385 body lengths/s)** |
| G-force at pullout | Not well documented | **~9 g** |
| Sound source | Tail feathers (general) | **R5 specifically, reed-like vibration** |
| Sound character | Brief buzz | **Explosive chirp (Cdive element)** |
| Sound frequency | Variable | **~4 kHz fundamental (C8)** |
| Scientific documentation | Limited | **Extensive (Clark 2008, 2009)** |
| Speed comparison | Fast | **Fastest vertebrate by body-length metric** |

The Rufous Hummingbird introduced the dive-display sonation. Anna's Hummingbird perfects it. The Rufous dives from moderate heights. Anna's climbs to 40 meters -- higher than a 13-story building -- and drops like a stone. The Rufous's tail feathers buzz. Anna's tail feathers *chirp* -- a single, explosive, frequency-specific event that Clark's research has characterized down to the individual feather. The Rufous hinted at the physics. Anna's has been studied to the physics.

### The 37th Acoustic Element: The Sonic Boom

After the thrum (degree 35) -- the continuous aeromechanical hum of wingbeats, the sound of a body in sustained flight -- Anna's Hummingbird introduces **the sonic boom**: a brief, explosive, mechanically generated sound produced by tail feathers vibrating at extreme airflow speed during a courtship dive, the loudest sound relative to body size in the vertebrate world, a single chirp that concentrates all the energy of a 40-meter plunge into 60 milliseconds of acoustic fury.

The thrum is continuous. The sonic boom is instantaneous. The thrum is the sound of sustained flight. The sonic boom is the sound of *committed* flight -- a dive from which there is no early exit, a plunge that risks everything on a single explosive note. The thrum says "I am here." The sonic boom says "I am HERE -- *look at me*."

**The progression: whisper (31) --> buzz (32) --> cadenza (33) --> dialect (34) --> thrum (35) --> sonic boom (36).** Six consecutive degrees, six fundamentally different acoustic strategies:

- Whisper = sound at the threshold of perception (waxwing flock)
- Buzz = sound amplified by numbers (waxwing swarm)
- Cadenza = sound as individual artistry (finch solo)
- Dialect = sound as group identity marker (crossbill type call)
- Thrum = sound as mechanical byproduct of flight (hummingbird wingbeat)
- **Sonic boom = sound as explosive mechanical display, the loudest event relative to body size (hummingbird dive chirp)**

The sonic boom is the aeromechanical category pushed to its extreme. The thrum was the first mechanical sound -- gentle, continuous, a hum. The sonic boom is mechanical sound as *weapon* -- brief, explosive, aimed. The thrum is the hum of the engine. The sonic boom is the afterburner.

## Ecology: The Urban Winter Hummingbird

### The Urban/Coastal Ecosystem -- A First

The ecosystem designation "urban/coastal" is the engine's **first explicitly urban ecosystem** [1, 2, 4]. Thirty-six degrees of old-growth, mountain conifer, riparian, mixed woodland, sky, cone crop, flower meadow -- and now, for the first time, the built environment.

Anna's Hummingbird is an **urban adapter** -- one of the most successful avian colonizers of human-modified landscapes in western North America [1, 2, 4]:

- **Habitat in the PNW:** Residential neighborhoods, urban parks, garden landscapes, campus plantings, commercial districts with ornamental vegetation. In Seattle, Anna's Hummingbirds are found in virtually every neighborhood with gardens or feeders [1, 4, 15].
- **Nest sites:** Frequently nests in ornamental trees and shrubs -- eucalyptus, camellia, bottlebrush, and other exotic plantings. Also nests on human structures: porch lights, wind chimes, hanging baskets, Christmas wreaths, clotheslines, and electrical wires [1, 2, 4].
- **Food sources:** Nectar from ornamental flowers (especially winter-blooming plants like *Mahonia*, rosemary, *Grevillea*, winter jasmine), sugar-water feeders, tree sap from sapsucker wells, and small insects caught in flight [1, 2, 4].
- **Coastal component:** In the PNW, Anna's is most common in the urban lowlands west of the Cascades -- the Puget Sound corridor, the Willamette Valley, the coastal strip. The "coastal" qualifier reflects its concentration in the maritime-influenced climate zone where winters are mild enough for year-round survival [1, 4].

The urban ecosystem is fundamentally different from every previous ecosystem in the engine:

| Ecosystem Type | Defined By | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Forest-defined | Tree species/structure | Old-growth, mountain conifer, mixed woodland |
| Water-defined | Aquatic features | Riparian, estuary |
| Resource-defined | Food availability | Cone crop (34), flower meadow (35) |
| **Human-defined** | **Built environment + ornamental plantings** | **Urban/coastal (36)** |

The flower meadow (degree 35) was the first ecosystem defined by mutualism. The urban/coastal ecosystem (degree 36) is the first defined by *human modification*. The hummingbird did not evolve in cities. It expanded into them. The ecosystem exists because humans planted ornamental flowers, hung sugar-water feeders, and created microclimates warm enough for a hummingbird to overwinter at 47 degrees north latitude. This is not natural habitat. It is *constructed* habitat -- and Anna's Hummingbird is thriving in it.

### The Year-Round Resident -- The Winter Hummingbird

This is the defining ecological fact of Anna's Hummingbird in the PNW: **it stays** [1, 2, 4, 15, 16].

Every other hummingbird in the Pacific Northwest migrates:

| Species | PNW Strategy | Winter Location |
|---|---|---|
| Rufous Hummingbird | Summer breeder, departs July-Sept | Mexico |
| Calliope Hummingbird | Summer breeder, departs Aug-Sept | Mexico |
| Black-chinned Hummingbird | Summer breeder, east of Cascades | Mexico |
| **Anna's Hummingbird** | **Year-round resident** | **Here. Still here. In January.** |

Anna's Hummingbird is the **northernmost year-round resident hummingbird in the world** [1, 2, 4]. In Seattle, it is present twelve months a year -- through December rains, January ice storms, February snow, and the long dark months when temperatures drop below freezing and no flowers bloom and no other hummingbird exists within a thousand miles. It is alone.

This is remarkable for a bird that weighs 4 grams. Every hummingbird faces the same thermodynamic imperative: the highest metabolic rate by mass of any bird, continuous nectar intake required to fuel hovering flight, starvation a constant threat. The Rufous Hummingbird's solution is migration -- follow the flowers south to Mexico. Anna's solution is torpor -- stay, endure, and shut down.

### Torpor: The Winter Survival Strategy

Anna's Hummingbird survives PNW winters through **nightly torpor** -- but at a scale and duration that exceeds what the Rufous Hummingbird experiences during its summer breeding season [1, 2, 4, 16, 17]:

- **Frequency:** Nightly during winter, whenever temperatures drop or food intake is insufficient. Not occasional but *routine* -- a winter Anna's in Seattle enters torpor most nights [16, 17].
- **Physiological changes:** Body temperature drops from ~40 C (~104 F) to as low as 8.8 C (~48 F). Heart rate drops from ~1,200 bpm to ~50 bpm. Breathing drops from 245 breaths per minute to fewer than 10. Breathing may be suspended for minutes at a time. Metabolic rate drops by as much as 95%, consuming up to 50 times less energy than when awake [16, 17].
- **Energy budget:** A typical winter day: the bird forages intensively during daylight, gaining up to 16% of its body weight in fat. At nightfall, it enters torpor on a sheltered perch, burning the stored fat slowly through the night. By dawn, the fat is largely consumed. The bird warms up (a process that takes 20-30 minutes and costs significant energy) and begins foraging again immediately. The margin is razor-thin [16, 17].
- **Risk:** Torpor is survival, not comfort. A bird that enters torpor with insufficient fat reserves may not survive the night. A bird that cannot find food quickly after waking may not survive the morning. Prolonged cold snaps that prevent foraging can be lethal. Ice storms that coat feeders and flowers in ice can kill [16, 17].

The Rufous Hummingbird enters torpor on cool summer nights -- a routine metabolic strategy. Anna's Hummingbird enters torpor on freezing winter nights as a *survival imperative*. Both use the same physiology, but the stakes are different. The Rufous can flee south if conditions deteriorate. Anna's cannot. It has committed to staying. The torpor is not a convenience. It is the price of year-round residence.

### Range Expansion: A 20th-Century Phenomenon

Anna's Hummingbird's presence in the Pacific Northwest is recent [1, 2, 4, 15]:

- **Historical range:** California and Baja California. Chaparral, oak woodland, coastal scrub. A warm-climate species.
- **Expansion timeline:** Beginning in the 1960s, Anna's Hummingbird expanded northward into Oregon, Washington, and British Columbia. The first Seattle records date to approximately 1964 [4, 15]. By the 1970s and 1980s, it was an established resident in western Washington.
- **Current range:** Year-round from Baja California to British Columbia, with regular occurrences in southeastern Alaska. The range has expanded approximately 900 miles northward in 60 years [1, 2, 4].
- **Drivers of expansion:**
  - **Ornamental plantings:** Exotic flowering plants in gardens and parks (especially winter-blooming species) provide nectar outside the native California bloom season [1, 2, 4].
  - **Hummingbird feeders:** Sugar-water feeders maintained year-round by humans provide a reliable nectar substitute during winter months when few flowers bloom [1, 2, 4, 15].
  - **Climate moderation:** Milder winters associated with climate change may have reduced the physiological cost of overwintering at northern latitudes [1, 2, 4].
  - **Urban heat islands:** Cities are warmer than surrounding countryside, creating microclimates that buffer against extreme cold [4].

This is ecological history in real time. Anna's Hummingbird did not evolve in Seattle. It colonized Seattle within living memory, enabled by human landscaping and feeding behavior. The bird's year-round presence in the PNW is an artifact of urbanization -- a wild species that expanded its range by exploiting the resources of a human-modified landscape. The urban/coastal ecosystem is not just where Anna's lives. It is *why* Anna's lives here at all.

### Conservation: Least Concern -- The Opposite Trajectory

Anna's Hummingbird's conservation status is the **mirror opposite** of the Rufous Hummingbird's [1, 2, 18, 19]:

| Dimension | Rufous Hummingbird (Degree 35) | Anna's Hummingbird (Degree 36) |
|---|---|---|
| IUCN status | **Near Threatened** | **Least Concern** |
| Population trend | **Declining (~65% since 1970s)** | **Increasing (~2% per year since 1966)** |
| Population size | Declining significantly | **~9.6 million and growing** |
| Range trend | Contracting at edges | **Expanding northward** |
| Habitat | Natural flower meadows (declining) | **Urban gardens and feeders (expanding)** |
| Human relationship | Threatened by habitat loss | **Benefiting from urbanization** |

The hummingbird pair at degrees 35-36 presents the sharpest conservation contrast in the engine: two closely related species, same family, same order, with opposite population trajectories. The Rufous is declining because its natural habitat (wildflower meadows, mountain bloom corridors) is degrading. Anna's is increasing because its adopted habitat (urban gardens, ornamental plantings, sugar-water feeders) is expanding. The migrant is losing the flowers it follows. The resident is gaining the feeders that sustain it.

This is not a simple success story. Anna's Hummingbird's dependence on human-provided resources creates fragility: a population crash could follow if feeder culture declines, if ornamental plant palettes shift, or if a severe winter event exceeds the species' torpor threshold. The expansion is real but contingent. The bird has bet its range on humans continuing to plant flowers and hang feeders. So far, the bet is paying off.

## The Learned Song: Convergent Vocal Learning

Anna's Hummingbird's complex song deserves deeper treatment because it represents one of the most remarkable convergences in avian biology [5, 6, 9, 10, 11]:

### Vocal Learning in Birds

Three groups of birds independently evolved the ability to learn their vocalizations by imitation:

1. **Oscine passerines (songbirds):** ~4,000 species. Song learning is the defining trait of the suborder.
2. **Parrots (Psittaciformes):** ~400 species. Learn calls and can mimic speech.
3. **Hummingbirds (Trochilidae):** ~375 species. A subset learn complex songs.

These three lineages are not closely related. Vocal learning evolved independently in each group -- convergent evolution of a complex neural and behavioral trait. Among hummingbirds, Anna's is the **best-documented vocal learner** [5, 6, 9, 10].

### Anna's Song Development

- **Subsong (4-7 months):** Young males begin practicing buzzy, amorphous vocalizations.
- **Plastic song (~7-10 months):** Syllable types, frequency patterns, and temporal structure begin to crystallize through imitation of adult males.
- **Crystallized song (~11+ months):** By the onset of the breeding season (~November), the song has stabilized into an adult form [6, 9, 10].
- **Regional dialects:** Males in the same neighborhood share similar song types. Populations separated by a few kilometers may differ in syllable structure. This geographic variation is maintained by cultural transmission, not genetics [6, 10, 11].

### Contrast with the Rufous Hummingbird

The Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) does not sing a complex song. Its vocal repertoire consists of simple, presumably innate chip notes and chitter calls. Males lack the enlarged higher vocal center (HVC) that supports song learning in Anna's [5, 6]. The Rufous is a "non-singing" hummingbird. Anna's is a "singing" hummingbird. Within the same family, the engine encounters both the absence and the presence of vocal learning.

This parallels the passerine world: suboscine passerines (like the Pacific-slope Flycatcher at degree 6) have simple, innate calls. Oscine passerines (like the warblers, thrushes, and finches throughout the engine) have complex, learned songs. The same split exists within Trochilidae, but it occurs between genera rather than suborders.

### Significance for the Engine

The engine has been tracking vocal complexity since degree 1 (the Pacific Wren's cascade). Every previous species' vocalizations have been either innate (most species) or learned (the passerine songbirds). Anna's Hummingbird is the **first non-passerine vocal learner in the engine** -- proof that song learning is not exclusive to songbirds, that the complex neural circuitry required for vocal imitation can evolve independently in an entirely different avian lineage. The hummingbird's song is simpler than a wren's or a thrush's, but the *process* by which it is acquired -- imitation, practice, crystallization, cultural transmission -- is the same.

**Second consecutive Trochilidae.** The hummingbird pair: Rufous (migrant/summer/thrum/declining) and Anna's (resident/year-round/sonic boom/expanding). Same family, opposite strategies. The Rufous follows the bloom; Anna's waits for it. The Rufous has no song; Anna's has a learned song. The Rufous is Near Threatened (65% decline); Anna's is Least Concern (increasing 2%/year). Maximum contrast within minimum taxonomic distance. The engine's most instructive species pair.

### SPS-034: Red Crossbill (Degree 34) -- LEARNED SONG PARALLEL
**Vocal learning in different lineages.** The Red Crossbill's flight call types may represent cultural transmission of vocal markers. Anna's Hummingbird definitively learns its song through imitation. Both involve culturally transmitted vocalizations in species where learning was not initially expected. The crossbill's type calls sort incipient species. Anna's song dialects vary geographically within one species. Different scales of the same phenomenon.

### SPS-031: Cedar Waxwing (Degree 31) -- RESIDENT VS. RESIDENT
**Two year-round PNW residents with opposite acoustic strategies.** Cedar Waxwing = whisper (E=4, quiet contact calls, flock cohesion). Anna's Hummingbird = sonic boom (E=5, explosive dive chirp, individual display). Both are residents. Both are present in Seattle yards in December. One whispers to its flock. The other dive-bombs for a mate.

### SPS-032: Bohemian Waxwing (Degree 32) -- E=5 TRIO
**Three E=5 entries now in the engine.** Bohemian Waxwing (32, collective energy, swarm buzz), Rufous Hummingbird (35, individual intensity, continuous thrum), Anna's Hummingbird (36, explosive display, instantaneous sonic boom). Three models of maximum energy: mass, persistence, and detonation.

### SPS-021/022: Northern Flicker (Degrees 21-22) -- MECHANICAL SOUND EVOLUTION
**The mechanical sound lineage expands.** Flicker drumming (percussive, bill on wood, degrees 21-22). Rufous wingbeat thrum (aeromechanical, wing feathers, degree 35). Anna's dive chirp (aeromechanical, tail feathers at extreme speed, degree 36). Four degrees, three distinct mechanical sound-production mechanisms, zero involvement of the syrinx.

### SPS-000: Great Blue Heron (Degree 0) -- URBAN HABITAT KINSHIP
**Two species comfortable in urban settings.** The Great Blue Heron hunts in urban shorelines, parks, and retention ponds. Anna's Hummingbird nests in urban gardens and feeds at backyard feeders. Both have adapted to human-modified landscapes, but the heron was always present in diverse habitats. Anna's *expanded into* urban habitat from a different range entirely.

### AVI -- Wings of the Pacific Northwest
Anna's Hummingbird documented as year-round resident in western Washington and Oregon, the only PNW hummingbird present in winter. One of four regularly occurring PNW hummingbird species. Trochilidae family accounts. Population increasing, range expanding northward.

### ECO -- Living Systems of the PNW
Urban ecology, ornamental plant communities, human-wildlife interaction. The role of feeders and exotic plantings in range expansion. Winter survival strategies. Torpor ecology. Climate change and range shift dynamics.
