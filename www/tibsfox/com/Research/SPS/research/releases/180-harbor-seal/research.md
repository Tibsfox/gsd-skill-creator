## E=4 -- THE GUTTURAL BARK

### Why E=4

The Harbor Seal's sound registers at E=4:

- **Bark character:** The alarm bark is low, guttural, and resonant -- a short, sharp vocalization that carries across water. The bark is the primary sound most people associate with harbor seals.
- **Social sounds:** Beyond barking, harbor seals produce a range of soft grunts, moans, growls, and snorts during social interactions. Mothers and pups exchange calls that are individually recognizable. These softer vocalizations create a continuous murmur at haul-out sites.
- **Underwater vocalizations:** Harbor seals also vocalize underwater -- producing clicks, roars, and low-frequency sounds during the breeding season. These underwater sounds are more varied and intense than the above-water vocalizations.
- **Volume:** The bark is moderate in absolute terms -- louder than most bird calls but not the roar of a sea lion (Otariidae). The seal's bark is purposeful rather than overwhelming.
- **Why E=4?** The bark is moderate volume, intermittent (not continuous like a colony roar), and contextual. The soft social sounds are quiet. E=4 reflects a moderate, guttural, intermittent vocalization -- punctuated rather than sustained.

(Sources: NOAA marine mammal acoustics; Riedman 1990; harbor seal behavioral studies; engine internal analysis)

## THE LIQUID GAZE

### Puget Sound's Most Familiar Marine Face

The Harbor Seal's most distinctive feature is its EYES:

- **Large, dark eyes:** Harbor seal eyes are disproportionately large -- adapted for seeing in the dim underwater light of Puget Sound. The iris is dark, creating the appearance of an entirely dark eye.
- **The "liquid gaze":** When a harbor seal surfaces near a kayaker, a dock, or a rocky shore and looks at you, the effect is arresting. The large dark eyes, the round face, the whiskers, the expression that humans read as curiosity or intelligence -- the "liquid gaze" of a harbor seal is one of the most compelling wildlife encounters in the PNW.
- **Whiskers (vibrissae):** The thick, beaded whiskers are sensory organs -- detecting vibrations in the water. A harbor seal can track a fish by the trail of turbulence it leaves. The whiskers are as important as the eyes for hunting.
- **Round face:** The harbor seal's face is round, blunt-nosed, and somewhat dog-like. The facial proportions trigger recognition and empathy in humans -- one reason harbor seals are among the most beloved marine mammals.

(Sources: Marine mammal biology; harbor seal sensory ecology; engine internal analysis)

## HAUL-OUT BEHAVIOR

### The Threshold Between Land and Sea

The Harbor Seal's haul-out behavior is the defining ecological pattern:

- **Haul-out rocks:** Harbor seals regularly leave the water to rest on rocks, sandbars, beaches, and docks. These haul-out sites are used repeatedly -- the same seals return to the same rocks day after day, year after year.
- **Thermoregulation:** Hauling out serves multiple functions: rest, thermoregulation (warming in the sun), predator avoidance (sharks and orcas can't reach them on rocks), social interaction, and pup rearing.
- **Puget Sound haul-outs:** Harbor seals haul out throughout Puget Sound -- on rocks, sandbars, floating logs, marinas, and artificial structures. They are visible from ferries, beaches, and docks across the Sound. They are the marine mammal most people in the PNW see regularly.
- **The threshold:** The haul-out behavior is literally living at the threshold between two worlds. On the rocks, the seal is a terrestrial animal -- breathing air, warming in the sun, interacting on land. In the water, the seal transforms into a sleek, powerful predator -- diving to 500+ meters, holding breath for 25+ minutes, pursuing fish with extraordinary agility. The same animal in two worlds.

(Sources: WDFW marine mammal surveys; harbor seal ecology; Puget Sound marine mammal data; engine internal analysis)

## PUGET SOUND POPULATIONS

### The Most Common Marine Mammal

- **Population:** Approximately 30,000-40,000 harbor seals in Washington State, with a significant portion in Puget Sound and the San Juan Islands.
- **Recovery:** Harbor seal populations have recovered significantly since the Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972 ended commercial hunting. They are now at or near carrying capacity in many areas.
- **Year-round resident:** Unlike many marine mammals, the harbor seal is RESIDENT in Puget Sound year-round. It does not migrate. It is always present.
- **Diet:** Fish, squid, octopus, crustaceans. Harbor seals are generalist predators that eat whatever is locally abundant. In Puget Sound, they eat Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance, salmon (when available), and many other species.
- **Predators:** Transient (Bigg's) orcas and sharks are the primary predators of harbor seals in the PNW. The seal-orca predator-prey relationship is one of the defining ecological interactions in Puget Sound.
- **Human interaction:** Harbor seals are habituated to human presence in many areas. They haul out on docks, swim near boats, and surface near kayakers. The NOAA recommendation is to maintain 100 meters distance. Despite their familiarity, they are wild animals protected by federal law.

(Sources: WDFW; NOAA; Puget Sound marine mammal surveys; harbor seal diet studies; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Harbor Seal

**Alarm Bark:**
- **Trigger:** Disturbance, alarm, territorial. Given when approached too closely or when predators are detected.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (laryngeal).
- **Character:** Low, guttural, short bark. Resonant. Sharp. One to several barks in sequence.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 200-2000 Hz. Low fundamental with harmonics.
- **Duration:** Individual bark 0.2-0.5 seconds. Series of 1-10 barks.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Moderate. Carries across water. Purposeful rather than overwhelming.

**Social Sounds:**
- Soft grunts, moans, growls during haul-out. Mother-pup calls: individually recognizable. Continuous murmur at large haul-outs.

**Underwater Vocalizations:**
- Clicks, roars, low-frequency sounds during breeding season. More varied and intense than above-water sounds. Carries far underwater.

(Sources: NOAA marine mammal acoustics; harbor seal behavioral studies; engine internal analysis)
