Named for Puget Sound: Z. l. pugetensis

The PNW form of the White-crowned Sparrow is Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis — literally “the White-crowned Sparrow of Puget Sound.” Described by Ridgway in 1898 from a Puget Sound type specimen. The only bird in the engine whose subspecific epithet IS the name of the project’s subject. The Sound names the bird and the bird names the Sound. Pugetensis breeds from coastal SE Alaska south through the PNW to NW California and winters in Pacific lowlands south to northern Los Angeles County. It forms approximately 12 distinct song dialects along the PNW coast.

The 49th Acoustic Element: The Dialect

The White-crowned Sparrow’s song is a clear introductory whistle followed by complex notes, buzzy trills, and a terminal trill — 2–3 seconds, 2–7 kHz. Named “the dialect” because this is THE species that established the study of geographic song dialects in birds. Marler & Tamura (1964) described how males learn a shared local song pattern during the first 10–100 days of life — the first acoustic element defined by cultural transmission.

After the preamble’s structural complexity (45), the rasp’s textural intensity (46), the snowbird’s elemental reduction (47), and the dialect’s cultural transmission (48) — the Passerellidae quartet has traversed four fundamentally different organizing principles for birdsong.

Four Consecutive Passerellidae — Degrees 45–48

Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) + White-crowned Sparrow (48) — four consecutive entries from the same family. Four genera (Melospiza, Pipilo, Junco, Zonotrichia). Four song strategies (complex, textural, simple, dialectal). Four habitats (wetland edge, dense understory, forest floor, mountain meadow edge). Marler’s Bird — How Birdsong Is Learned

Peter Marler and Miwako Tamura published their landmark 1964 paper “Culturally Transmitted Patterns of Vocal Behavior in Sparrows” in Science, establishing the White-crowned Sparrow as THE model species for vocal learning. Key discoveries: the sensitive period (10–100 days), the auditory template (innate bias toward conspecific song), and cultural transmission (song learned from adult tutors, not inherited genetically). Marler also studied Song Sparrow (degree 45) — bridging two Passerellidae through one researcher. The quartet’s three great researchers: Margaret Morse Nice (Song Sparrow, degree 45), Ellen Ketterson (Dark-eyed Junco, degree 47), Peter Marler (White-crowned Sparrow, degree 48).

The COVID Experiment — When the City Went Quiet (2020)

When COVID lockdowns silenced San Francisco, Elizabeth Derryberry (U. of Tennessee) documented White-crowned Sparrows singing 35% more quietly, lowering their minimum frequency, and widening their bandwidth — reverting to song patterns resembling 1970s recordings. Published in Science (2020). The birds demonstrated immediate vocal plasticity — a third timescale of vocal change alongside dialect learning (cultural, decades) and subspecies divergence (evolutionary, millennia).

The White-Browed Banded-Hair Bird

Zonotrichia leucophrys — from Ancient Greek zone (“band”) + thrix (“hair”) + leukos (“white”) + ophrys (“eyebrow”). “The white-browed banded-hair bird” — named entirely for its visual appearance. The bold black-and-white head stripes are one of the most recognizable field marks of any North American sparrow. Unlike the Song Sparrow’s pure-acoustic naming (“melody song-finch”), the White-crowned Sparrow’s name contains NO sound reference. Two consecutive Passerellidae (47–48) with non-acoustic names.

The Sound: Whistles, Buzzes, and Dialects

Song: 2–3 seconds, 2–7 kHz. Clear introductory whistle (species ID) + complex notes (individual ID) + buzzy trills (broadband energy) + terminal trill (dialect ID). A four-part structured song — the most functionally differentiated song in the Passerellidae quartet.

Call note: A sharp “pink” or “tseet” — 0.05–0.1 seconds, 4–8 kHz. Contact and alarm call.

Puget Sound dialects: Approximately 12 distinct dialects along the PNW coast (Nelson et al. 2004). Terminal trills stable across decades; complex notes variable. Dialect boundaries do not act as barriers to gene flow — genetically one large population (Soha et al. 2004, Poesel et al. 2017).

Cross-References

SPS-047: Dark-eyed Junco

PASSERELLIDAE CONSECUTIVE (47–48). The snowbird meets the dialect. Elemental reduction followed by cultural transmission. Both are vocal learners, but the junco’s song is simpler and less dialectally structured. Ketterson’s bird (47) meets Marler’s bird (48).

SPS-045: Song Sparrow

PASSERELLIDAE QUARTET ANCHOR + MARLER CONNECTION. Marler studied BOTH Song Sparrow and White-crowned Sparrow. The Song Sparrow is the more flexible learner; the White-crowned Sparrow has the stricter sensitive period. Margaret Morse Nice’s bird (45) meets Marler’s bird (48). Two masterclass species, one family.

SPS-046: Spotted Towhee

PASSERELLIDAE QUARTET MEMBER. The rasp (textural intensity) is two degrees from the dialect (cultural transmission). The Spotted Towhee concentrates; the White-crowned Sparrow transmits. Four consecutive Passerellidae, four organizing principles.

SPS-011: Oregon Junco

PASSERELLIDAE DEEP CONNECTION. Degree 11 is a subspecies group within degree 47 (Dark-eyed Junco), which is the third member of the quartet that degree 48 completes. The Oregon Junco’s metallic trill (degree 11) contrasts with the White-crowned Sparrow’s structured dialect song (degree 48).

SPS-034: Red Crossbill

VOCAL TYPES AS INCIPIENT SPECIES. Red Crossbill has 10+ call types that may be separate species. White-crowned Sparrow has 12 Puget Sound dialects that do NOT create genetic boundaries. Two models of vocal variation: crossbill types may split; sparrow dialects demonstrably do not.

SPS-002: Varied Thrush

SUSTAINED TONE CONTRAST. The Varied Thrush holds a pure, sustained, single-frequency tone. The White-crowned Sparrow begins with a pure whistle (similar) then transitions to complex buzzy trills (opposite). Clean-to-complex within one song vs. clean throughout.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

White-crowned Sparrow documented across Washington. Pugetensis breeds in western WA; gambelii migrates through and winters. Common at feeders and in brushy habitats. Song dialects mapped along PNW coast.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Mountain meadow edge ecology. Alpine/subalpine shrub habitat. Altitudinal migration. Brushy edge as key habitat requirement. Climate-driven range shifts in mountain-breeding populations.
