## Purpose

This is the forty-ninth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-48), the fourth Passerellidae, the fourth genus in the Passerellidae quartet, and the degree that brings the project's name into the taxonomy. *Zonotrichia leucophrys* -- "the white-browed banded-hair bird," Peter Marler's foundational model for vocal learning, the bird whose subspecies *pugetensis* is literally named for Puget Sound -- enters the engine as the species that gives "The Sound of Puget Sound" its most direct taxonomic mirror. The release carries seven structural contributions:

1. **The dialect -- the 49th acoustic element (species side).** A culturally transmitted geographic song variant: clear introductory whistle + complex notes + buzzy trills + terminal trill, 2-3 seconds, 2-7 kHz. Named "the dialect" for the bird's identity as THE model species for song dialect research (Marler & Tamura 1964), the *pugetensis* connection (~12 dialects along the PNW coast), and the acoustic principle of cultural transmission -- song learned from adult tutors during a sensitive period, creating geographically stable dialect regions. The first acoustic element defined by **cultural transmission**.

2. **Fourth consecutive Passerellidae -- TIES the longest non-Hirundinidae same-family streak.** Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) + White-crowned Sparrow (48). Four genera (*Melospiza*, *Pipilo*, *Junco*, *Zonotrichia*). Four habitats (wetland edge, dense understory, forest floor, mountain meadow edge). Four song strategies (improvisation, concentration, reduction, transmission). TIES the Parulidae quintet (38-42) for longest same-family streak outside the swallow quintet (26-30).

3. **The *pugetensis* subspecies -- named for Puget Sound.** *Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis*, described by Ridgway in 1898 from a Puget Sound type specimen. The only bird in the engine whose subspecific epithet IS the name of the project's subject. The Sound names the bird and the bird names the Sound.

4. **Peter Marler's bird -- the species that taught science how song is learned.** Marler and Tamura (1964, *Science*) established the sensitive period, auditory template, and cultural transmission paradigm. Marler also studied Song Sparrow (degree 45). Three great researchers span three of the quartet's four species: Nice (45), Ketterson (47), Marler (48).

5. **The COVID song experiment (Derryberry 2020, *Science*).** White-crowned Sparrows in San Francisco sang 35% more quietly during lockdowns, lowered minimum frequency, widened bandwidth. Three temporal scales of vocal change in one species: evolutionary (subspecies), cultural (dialects), behavioral (real-time adjustment).

6. **E=2 returns -- lower registers now exceed E=4.** E=2 + E=3 = 22 degrees (44.9%) surpasses E=4's 21 degrees (42.9%) for the first time. The equilibrium at degree 47 tips in favor of the lower registers. Quartet energy arch: 2, 3, 3, 2.

7. **CSV substitution documented.** Tailed Frog replaced by White-crowned Sparrow. Fourth consecutive amphibian replacement (45-46-47-48). The engine continues through the amphibian belt of the original CSV.

## What Worked

### 1. The *pugetensis* connection is structurally perfect
A project named "The Sound of Puget Sound" encountering a bird whose subspecies is named *pugetensis* ("of Puget Sound") creates a resonance that no amount of engineering could have planned. The connection is not just nominal -- *pugetensis* breeds in the project's geographic focus area, forms ~12 song dialects along the PNW coast, and was first described from a Puget Sound specimen. The bird's acoustic geography IS the sound of Puget Sound.

### 2. The dialect names a genuinely new acoustic principle
Cultural transmission -- the first time the engine encounters a sound defined by its social origin rather than its physical properties. The preamble is about structure. The rasp is about texture. The snowbird is about simplicity. The dialect is about community. This is a fundamentally different category of acoustic element, and it required the White-crowned Sparrow (the founding model for dialect research) to introduce it.

### 3. The Marler-Nice-Ketterson lineage gives the quartet scientific depth
Three of the four species connected by landmark researchers spanning nearly a century. Nice (behavioral ecology, 1930s), Marler (vocal learning, 1960s), Ketterson (endocrinology, 1970s-present). Three approaches to the same family. The Passerellidae quartet is not just an ecological survey but a history of songbird science.

### 4. The COVID song experiment adds a novel temporal dimension
The Derryberry (2020) paper documenting real-time song changes during San Francisco lockdowns is unique in the engine. No other species entry has documented acoustic change on a behavioral timescale (days-to-weeks). The White-crowned Sparrow now operates on three temporal scales simultaneously: evolutionary, cultural, and behavioral. The engine's most temporally complex acoustic subject.

### 5. The four-degree streak reveals family-level completeness
Four consecutive entries from one family, four different genera, four different organizing principles for song. The quartet is not just a streak but a survey: it covers the Passerellidae's ecological range (wetland to alpine), size range (19 g to 40 g), energy range (E=2 to E=3), and behavioral-scientific range (model organisms for three different research traditions). No previous family streak achieved this degree of completeness.

### 6. The energy arch is elegantly symmetrical
2, 3, 3, 2. The quartet begins and ends at E=2, with E=3 in the middle. This is not arbitrary -- the two E=2 species (Song Sparrow, White-crowned Sparrow) are the two Marler-studied species, the two more melodious and complex singers. The two E=3 species (Spotted Towhee, Dark-eyed Junco) are the textural/elemental species. The energy pattern reflects the song character.

The winter rush-bird. The snowbird. And now -- one degree later, at the edge of the meadow where the mountain slope opens up and the subalpine shrubs grow in dense patches between bare ground and wildflowers -- a bird with a head painted in bold black-and-white stripes. It sits at the top of a willow bush and lifts its head and opens its bill. A clear, pure whistle rings across the meadow -- one note, held, clean as glass. And then the whistle breaks into a tumble of buzzy trills and complex phrases, rising and falling, each section different from the last, a structured composition in 2-3 seconds. The introductory whistle says: I am a White-crowned Sparrow. The terminal trill says: I am from HERE. The complex notes say: I am THIS individual. Three layers of identity in one song. Learned from the adults who sang here before, during the first hundred days of life, and carried faithfully for a lifetime. One tradition. Twelve dialects along the coast. The same song, different everywhere. The same bird, named for this place. Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis. The white-browed banded-hair bird of Puget Sound. The dialect.*
