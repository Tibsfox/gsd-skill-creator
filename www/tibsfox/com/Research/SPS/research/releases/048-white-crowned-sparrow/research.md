## Taxonomy: The Banded-Hair White-Brow

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Passerellidae (New World Sparrows)
- **Genus:** *Zonotrichia* (Swainson, 1832)
- **Species:** *Zonotrichia leucophrys* (Forster, 1772)
- **Common Name:** White-crowned Sparrow

### Etymology

*Zonotrichia* -- from Ancient Greek *zone* (zone, "band") + *thrix* (thrix, genitive *trichos*, "hair") [1, 2, 5]. "Banded hair" -- referring to the genus's diagnostic striped head pattern. The genus contains five species, all characterized by bold head stripes: White-crowned Sparrow, White-throated Sparrow (*Z. albicollis*), Golden-crowned Sparrow (*Z. atricapilla*), Harris's Sparrow (*Z. querula*), and Rufous-collared Sparrow (*Z. capensis*) [1, 5].

*Leucophrys* -- from Ancient Greek *leukos* ("white") + *ophrys* ("eyebrow," "brow") [1, 2, 5]. "White-browed" -- referring to the bird's broad white crown stripes that form conspicuous pale "eyebrows" atop a boldly striped black-and-white head.

Full translation: **"The white-browed banded-hair bird"** -- a name that is entirely visual, entirely about the head pattern, and entirely accurate. The White-crowned Sparrow is named for its most diagnostic field mark: the bold black-and-white stripes on its head.

The naming oscillation reveals a pattern: after the junco's ecological-temporal name (no sound reference), the White-crowned Sparrow's name is pure visual anatomy -- no sound, no season, no habitat, just the appearance of the head. Two consecutive Passerellidae with no acoustic content in their names, following two (Song Sparrow and Spotted Towhee) that had acoustic references. The Passerellidae quartet's naming arc: musical-musical (45), acoustic-visual (46), ecological-temporal (47), visual-visual (48). The family's names progressively abandon sound.

### *Z. l. pugetensis* -- The Puget Sound Subspecies

The PNW form is *Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis* -- **literally named for Puget Sound** [1, 2, 5, 8, 12]. This is the only bird in the 48-degree history of the engine whose subspecific epithet IS the name of the project's subject. The engine is called *The Sound of Puget Sound*. The subspecies is called *pugetensis*. The name is the project.

*Z. l. pugetensis* breeds from coastal southeastern Alaska (Ketchikan) south through coastal British Columbia, western Washington, western Oregon, and into northwestern California (north of Cape Mendocino, Humboldt County) [1, 5, 8, 12]. In Washington, *pugetensis* breeds in brushy habitats from coastal scrub and logged clearings to mountain meadow edges. *Pugetensis* was described by Ridgway in 1898 and assigned to the Puget Sound region because that was where the type specimen was collected [5, 8]. The subspecies is intermediate in many traits between the sedentary *nuttalli* (California coast) and the long-distance migrant *gambelii* (taiga). Together, *nuttalli* and *pugetensis* form the "nuttalli group" -- a Pacific coastal clade that is the most genetically distinct subdivision within the species [5, 8].

## The Sound: Clear Whistles and Buzzy Trills

### Song Structure

The White-crowned Sparrow's song is a **clear, whistled introductory note followed by a series of complex phrases including buzzy trills, frequency sweeps, and a terminal trill** -- a structured, multi-part song lasting approximately 2-3 seconds [1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 9, 10, 15, 17]. The song is typically described in five parts:

1. **Introductory whistle** -- a clear, pure-tone whistle of 0.3-0.8 seconds. This is the species-identification component, highly conserved across populations [1, 9, 15, 17].
2. **Complex note(s)** -- one or more modulated notes with frequency sweeps. This is the most variable component and indicates individual identity [9, 15, 17].
3. **Buzz(es)** -- one or two vibrato elements with rapid frequency modulation. These contain the highest frequencies in the song [1, 9, 15, 17].
4. **Terminal trill** -- a rapid repetition of a simple syllable at the end of the song. This is the dialect-identification component -- the part that defines which dialect group a male belongs to [9, 15, 17].

This is a structurally richer song than any of the other three Passerellidae in the engine:

| Degree | Species | Song Complexity | Parts | Duration | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 45 | Song Sparrow | HIGH -- preamble + elaborate body | Multi-part | 2-4 s | Free improvisation within learned framework |
| 46 | Spotted Towhee | MODERATE -- intro notes + buzzy trill | 1-2 part | ~1.5 s | Concentrated texture |
| 47 | Dark-eyed Junco | SIMPLE -- even trill on single pitch | 1 part | 1-2 s | Elemental reduction |
| **48** | **White-crowned Sparrow** | **HIGH -- whistle + complex + buzz + trill** | **4-5 part** | **2-3 s** | **Dialectally structured, culturally transmitted** |

The Passerellidae quartet's song complexity does not follow a simple gradient. The Song Sparrow (45) elaborates freely; the Spotted Towhee (46) concentrates into texture; the Dark-eyed Junco (47) reduces to the minimum; the White-crowned Sparrow (48) returns to complexity but with a fundamentally different organizing principle: **dialect structure**. The Song Sparrow's complexity is individual (each male improvises). The White-crowned Sparrow's complexity is cultural (each male learns a shared dialect).

### Frequency Analysis

| Vocalization | Frequency Range | Duration | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full song | 2-7 kHz | 2-3 s | Territory, mate attraction |
| Introductory whistle | 3-5 kHz (pure tone) | 0.3-0.8 s | Species identification |
| Complex notes | 2-7 kHz (modulated) | 0.3-0.6 s | Individual identity |
| Buzz elements | 3-7 kHz (vibrato) | 0.2-0.5 s | Highest frequencies in song |
| Terminal trill | 2-5 kHz | 0.3-1.0 s | Dialect identification |

The frequency range (2-7 kHz) is broader than the Dark-eyed Junco's concentrated 3-6 kHz and overlaps with the Song Sparrow's range. The introductory whistle is the narrowband, pure-tone component that contrasts sharply with the broadband buzzy trills later in the song -- a clean-to-complex transition within a single 2-3 second utterance [1, 7, 9, 10, 15].

### The 49th Acoustic Element: The Dialect

The White-crowned Sparrow introduces the engine's 49th acoustic element (species side): **the dialect**.

The dialect is the organizing principle of the White-crowned Sparrow's song -- a culturally transmitted pattern of vocal structure that maps to specific geographic areas, remains stable across decades, and is learned by young males during the first 10-100 days of life from the adult males around them [1, 7, 9, 15, 17]. The dialect is not one sound but a *system* of sounds: an acoustic signature shared by all males within a population and distinct from the signatures of neighboring populations.

The dialect is distinct from all nearby acoustic elements:

| Element | Character | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| The Preamble (45) | Clear opening notes before complexity | Positional structure |
| The Rasp (46) | Concentrated buzzy trill | Textural intensity |
| The Snowbird (47) | Simple even trill on single pitch | Elemental reduction |
| **The Dialect (48)** | **Culturally transmitted geographic song variant** | **Cultural transmission** |

The dialect differs from the snowbird (degree 47) because the snowbird is defined by what the song LACKS (complexity, variation, modulation) -- radical simplicity. The dialect is defined by what the song CARRIES (geographic identity, cultural inheritance, population membership) -- a socially encoded signal. The snowbird is about the individual note. The dialect is about the community of singers.

The dialect also differs from "the cadenza" (degree 33, Cassin's Finch) because the cadenza is about individual virtuosity -- a single bird's improvised performance. The dialect is about collective conformity -- an entire population singing the same learned pattern. The cadenza celebrates what makes one singer unique. The dialect celebrates what makes a population unified.

Named "the dialect" for three resonances:
1. **The bird's scientific identity** -- the White-crowned Sparrow is THE model species for vocal dialect research. Marler and Tamura (1964) first described geographic song dialects in this species, founding the entire field of avian cultural transmission [7, 9, 15].
2. **The Puget Sound connection** -- *Z. l. pugetensis* forms approximately 12 distinct song dialects along the Pacific Northwest coast, mapped, recorded, and tracked across decades by Nelson, Soha, Poesel, and others [15, 17, 18, 19, 22]. The project named for Puget Sound encounters the bird whose dialects DEFINE Puget Sound's acoustic geography.
3. **The acoustic principle** -- a dialect in language is a regional variant of a shared communication system: same language, different pronunciation. A song dialect is the same: same species song, different regional delivery. The White-crowned Sparrow's song is a language with dialects. The engine encounters its first culturally transmitted acoustic element.

The dialect is the engine's first element defined by **cultural transmission** -- an acoustic pattern that is not genetically inherited but socially learned, not individually invented but collectively maintained, not fixed by species identity but variable by geographic location. After the preamble's structural complexity (45), the rasp's textural intensity (46), the snowbird's elemental reduction (47), and now the dialect's cultural transmission (48), the Passerellidae quartet has traversed four fundamentally different organizing principles for birdsong.

### The *Pugetensis* Song Dialects

The songs of male Puget Sound White-crowned Sparrows form approximately **12 distinct dialects** along the Pacific Northwest coast [15, 17, 18, 19]. Each dialect is defined primarily by the structure of the **terminal trill** -- the closing phrase of the song. Most males at each locality share the same simple syllable pattern in this trill, creating sharply bounded dialect regions [15, 17].

Research by Douglas Nelson and colleagues has documented these dialects over a 30+ year period [15, 17, 18, 19]:

- **Dialect stability:** The terminal trill (the dialect marker) remains remarkably stable over decades. Recordings from the 1970s and the 2000s show the same trill types at the same locations [17, 18].
- **Complex note change:** While the terminal trill stays stable, the complex note portion of the song changes significantly over time -- 2.3 times more improvisation in the complex notes than in the terminal trill [17]. The dialect marker is conserved; the individual signature evolves.
- **Functional differentiation:** Different parts of the song serve different functions. The introductory whistle identifies the species. The terminal trill identifies the dialect (population). The complex notes identify the individual. One song, three layers of identity [15, 17].
- **Gene flow across dialect boundaries:** Despite sharp dialect boundaries, genetic analysis reveals no tendency for birds within a dialect area to be more closely related than birds from different dialect areas [19, 22]. Dialect boundaries do not function as barriers to gene flow. The cultural boundaries are not genetic boundaries. The song is learned, not inherited.

### The Marler Legacy: How Birdsong Is Learned

The White-crowned Sparrow is the species that taught science how song learning works. **Peter Marler and Miwako Tamura published their landmark 1964 paper "Culturally Transmitted Patterns of Vocal Behavior in Sparrows"** in *Science*, establishing the White-crowned Sparrow as THE model species for understanding vocal learning in birds [7, 9].

Marler's key discoveries [7, 9, 10, 14, 15]:

1. **The sensitive period:** Young White-crowned Sparrows learn their song during the first 10-50 days of life (later extended to ~100 days). Songs heard during this window are memorized; songs heard after the window closes have no effect on the adult song [7, 9].
2. **The auditory template:** Young birds possess an innate template that biases them to learn conspecific (same-species) songs over heterospecific (other-species) songs. The template is not the song itself but a predisposition to attend to certain acoustic features [7, 9, 10].
3. **Cultural transmission:** Song is not genetically inherited but culturally transmitted -- learned from adult tutors and passed from generation to generation through social learning. Local dialects arise because young birds learn from local adults, creating geographically stable song traditions [7, 9, 15].
4. **The sensorimotor phase:** After memorizing the template during the sensitive period, young birds practice (subsong, plastic song) for months before crystallizing their adult song. Deafening before crystallization prevents normal song development [7, 9, 10].
5. **Selective learning:** White-crowned Sparrows will learn conspecific song but not songs of other species, even when exposed to both. The innate template acts as a filter [7, 9].

This research bridged the gap between the White-crowned Sparrow and the Song Sparrow (degree 45). **Marler studied BOTH species** -- his work on Song Sparrows demonstrated that the sensitive period for song learning varies between species, and that different sparrows have different degrees of flexibility in what they will learn [7, 9, 14]. The White-crowned Sparrow is the stricter learner (learns one dialect during a narrow window). The Song Sparrow is the more flexible learner (learns multiple song types over a longer period). Two Passerellidae, two learning strategies, one researcher.

The Marler connection also bridges to Ellen Ketterson's work on Dark-eyed Juncos (degree 47). While Marler's tradition focused on how songs are LEARNED, Ketterson's tradition focused on how songs are REGULATED by hormones. Two approaches to the same family: cultural transmission (degrees 45, 48 via Marler) and endocrine control (degree 47 via Ketterson). The Passerellidae quartet is a tutorial in the biology of birdsong.

## The COVID Experiment: When the City Went Quiet

In the spring of 2020, when COVID-19 lockdowns silenced San Francisco, White-crowned Sparrows rewrote their songs in real time [11, 13, 16, 20].

**Elizabeth Derryberry** (University of Tennessee, Knoxville), who had been studying noise effects on White-crowned Sparrow song for over a decade, documented the changes in a 2020 paper published in *Science* [11, 16]:

1. **Lower amplitude:** Sparrows sang approximately 35% more quietly -- far more than the 3% predicted by the Lombard effect (the tendency to speak louder in noise). The birds were not just compensating for reduced noise; they were fundamentally resetting their vocal output [11, 16].
2. **Lower minimum frequency:** Males lowered the frequency of their lowest notes, expanding their bandwidth downward into frequency ranges that had been masked by traffic noise [11, 16].
3. **Wider bandwidth:** With low-frequency urban noise removed, the birds used a wider frequency range, producing songs that more closely resembled recordings from the 1970s -- before the city's noise levels increased [11, 16].
4. **Longer communication distance:** The quieter, lower-frequency, wider-bandwidth songs actually traveled farther and were more attractive to females. Paradoxically, singing more softly in a quiet environment was MORE effective than singing loudly in a noisy one [11, 16].

The COVID song shift demonstrated that White-crowned Sparrows possess **immediate vocal plasticity** -- the ability to adjust song parameters within an individual's lifetime, not just across generations. This is distinct from dialect learning (which occurs once, during the sensitive period) and from evolutionary change (which occurs across generations). The COVID experiment revealed a third timescale of vocal change: **real-time adjustment within a crystallized song** [11, 16].

For the engine, the COVID experiment is significant because it reveals the White-crowned Sparrow as a species that operates on three temporal scales simultaneously:
- **Evolutionary:** Subspecies diverge in song structure over millennia (*pugetensis* vs. *nuttalli* vs. *gambelii*)
- **Cultural:** Dialects shift over decades within a subspecies (Nelson's 30-year tracking)
- **Behavioral:** Individual birds adjust song parameters within days in response to ambient noise changes (Derryberry's COVID study)

Three temporal scales, one species, one song. The White-crowned Sparrow is the engine's most temporally complex acoustic subject.

## Biology: The Banded Crown

### Population and Abundance

The White-crowned Sparrow is a common and widespread species across North America [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

- **~79 million** -- Partners in Flight estimate of the global breeding population [1, 2, 6]
- **Continental Concern Score:** 7/20 (low concern) [1, 2, 6]
- **BBS trend:** Populations held approximately stable between 1966 and 2019, though with regional variation -- declining in some western mountain regions, increasing in some northern regions [1, 2, 6]
- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 5]

The population of ~79 million is substantially smaller than the Dark-eyed Junco's ~220-630 million (degree 47) but considerably larger than many other species in the engine. The White-crowned Sparrow is common but not superabundant.

The White-crowned Sparrow is a medium-large sparrow -- slightly larger than the Song Sparrow and the Dark-eyed Junco, smaller than the Spotted Towhee [1, 2, 5, 6]:

The four Passerellidae now span a complete size range: Dark-eyed Junco (smallest, ~19-20 g) to White-crowned Sparrow / Song Sparrow (middle, ~25-28 g) to Spotted Towhee (largest, ~37-40 g).

### Plumage: The Diagnostic Head Pattern

The White-crowned Sparrow's most distinctive feature is its **bold black-and-white head stripes** -- one of the most recognizable field marks of any North American sparrow [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

**Adult:**
- **Crown:** Broad white median crown stripe bordered by bold black lateral crown stripes. The stripes are clean, sharp, and high-contrast.
- **Supercilium (eyebrow):** Broad white stripe above the eye.
- **Eye line:** Black stripe through the eye.
- **Lores:** Variable by subspecies -- dark (gray or black) in *nuttalli* and *pugetensis*; pale (yellow or orange) in *gambelii* and *leucophrys*.
- **Bill:** Orange-yellow (*pugetensis*, *nuttalli*) or pinkish (*gambelii*, *oriantha*, *leucophrys*). The bill color is a key field mark for subspecies identification [1, 2, 5, 8].
- **Upperparts:** Gray-brown with darker streaking.
- **Underparts:** Plain gray breast, unstreaked. The lack of breast streaking distinguishes it from the heavily streaked Song Sparrow.
- **Flanks:** Buffy-brown.

**Immature/first-winter:**
- Crown stripes are brown and tan (not black and white), creating a softer, less contrasting pattern. The tan-striped immature is frequently confused with Golden-crowned Sparrow (*Z. atricapilla*) immatures in the PNW [1, 2, 5].

The *pugetensis* subspecies is distinguished from *gambelii* (the other common Washington subspecies, present as a migrant and winter visitor) by its **dark lores** (black or dark gray between the eye and bill) and **yellowish or orange bill** [1, 2, 5, 8, 12]. *Gambelii* has pale lores (often yellowish or orangish) and a pinkish bill. In western Washington, *pugetensis* is the breeding bird; *gambelii* passes through on migration and may winter alongside *pugetensis* in the lowlands [1, 3, 8].

### Diet

The White-crowned Sparrow is an **omnivorous ground forager** with a seasonal dietary shift similar to the Dark-eyed Junco [1, 2, 3, 5]:

**Breeding season:** Primarily insects -- beetles, caterpillars, flies, ants, and other arthropods. Also spiders. Insect protein is critical for nestling growth.

**Non-breeding season:** Primarily seeds and grain -- grass seeds, weed seeds, waste grain, plus buds, berries, and small fruits. A common feeder visitor in the PNW during winter, favoring mixed seed on the ground or in platform feeders [1, 2, 3, 5, 6].

### Ground Nesting

The White-crowned Sparrow nests on or near the ground -- consistent with the entire Passerellidae quartet [1, 2, 3, 5]:

- **Nest site:** On the ground (northern and high-elevation populations) or in low shrubs up to 1.5-3 m (Pacific coast populations including *pugetensis*). In the PNW, *pugetensis* often places nests a few feet up in dense shrubs, blackberry tangles, or low conifers [1, 2, 3, 5].
- **Nest construction:** Open cup of grass, twigs, bark strips, and rootlets, lined with fine grass, hair, and feathers. Built by the female [1, 2, 5].
- **Eggs:** 3-5, pale greenish or bluish with reddish-brown spots [1, 2, 5].
- **Incubation:** 11-14 days, primarily by the female [1, 2, 5].
- **Nestling period:** 7-12 days. Both parents feed [1, 2, 5].
- **Broods per season:** 1-3, typically 2 in PNW coastal populations [1, 2, 5].

## PNW Habitat: Mountain Meadow Edge and Alpine Shrub

### Habitat Description

The White-crowned Sparrow's breeding habitat in the PNW varies by subspecies, but the engine assigns it to **mountain meadow edge / alpine shrub** to capture the elevational dimension that the species adds to the Passerellidae quartet [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]:

In Washington state, *Z. l. pugetensis* breeds in a variety of brushy habitats [1, 3, 4]:
- Coastal scrub and brushy clearings from sea level upward
- Logged clearings with regenerating shrub growth
- Suburban edges with dense hedgerows and brush piles
- Mountain meadow margins with subalpine shrub (meadow-forest ecotone)
- Avalanche chutes and other natural openings in montane forest
- Brushy alpine and subalpine areas in the Cascades and Olympics

The key habitat requirement is the same across all elevations: **a patchy mosaic of bare ground (for foraging), dense shrubs (for nesting and escape cover), and elevated perches (for singing)** [1, 2, 3, 5]. The White-crowned Sparrow needs edges -- the boundary between open ground and dense vegetation. This is why it thrives in meadow margins, clearing edges, suburban hedgerows, and the transition zone between forest and alpine.

### The Four Passerellidae Habitat Bands

| Degree | Species | Primary Habitat | Elevation | Openness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 45 | Song Sparrow | Wetland edge, pond margin | Lowland | Open, wet |
| 46 | Spotted Towhee | Dense understory, shrub tangle | Lowland-mid | Dense, dry |
| 47 | Dark-eyed Junco | Forest floor, woodland edge | All elevations | Open-moderate |
| **48** | **White-crowned Sparrow** | **Mountain meadow edge, alpine shrub** | **Lowland to alpine** | **Open edge with shrub patches** |

The quartet creates a habitat transect that now extends from water (Song Sparrow) through forest interior (Spotted Towhee, Dark-eyed Junco) to the upper forest edge and above (White-crowned Sparrow). The addition of the alpine/subalpine dimension at degree 48 completes the vertical transect -- the Passerellidae run has walked from the pond to the summit.

### Altitudinal Migration

Like the Dark-eyed Junco (degree 47), the White-crowned Sparrow performs altitudinal migration in the PNW -- breeding at higher elevations and wintering at lower elevations [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. However, the two species migrate in opposite directions along the elevational gradient:

- **Dark-eyed Junco:** Breeds at moderate forest elevations, descends to lowland feeders in winter. The snowbird arrives.
- **White-crowned Sparrow (*pugetensis*):** Breeds in brushy habitats across a wide elevational range (including coastal lowlands), but the mountain-breeding individuals descend to lowland urban and suburban areas in winter. In addition, *gambelii* individuals from far northern breeding grounds pass through and winter in the PNW lowlands.

The result is that Seattle-area birders encounter White-crowned Sparrows most conspicuously in **winter** -- flocks of *pugetensis* and *gambelii* foraging on the ground in parks, yards, and brushy edges [1, 3, 4]. The summer birds disperse into scattered breeding territories in brushy habitats. The winter flocks are denser, more visible, and mixed-subspecies.

## The White-crowned Sparrow in the Engine's Arc

### Structural Position

Degree 48 carries seven structural contributions to the engine:

1. **The dialect -- the 49th acoustic element (species side).** A culturally transmitted geographic song variant. The first element defined by cultural transmission. The song's organizing principle is not individual invention (Song Sparrow), not textural concentration (Spotted Towhee), not elemental reduction (Dark-eyed Junco), but collective learned tradition.

2. **Fourth consecutive Passerellidae -- TIES the longest non-Parulidae same-family streak.** Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) + White-crowned Sparrow (48). Four genera (*Melospiza*, *Pipilo*, *Junco*, *Zonotrichia*). Four habitats, four song strategies. Ties the Parulidae quintet's record for the longest same-family streak outside Hirundinidae.

3. **The *pugetensis* subspecies -- named for Puget Sound.** The only species in the engine whose subspecific epithet matches the project's name. *Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis* is literally "the White-crowned Sparrow of Puget Sound." The Sound names the bird and the bird names the Sound.

4. **Peter Marler's bird -- the species that taught science how song is learned.** Marler and Tamura (1964) established the White-crowned Sparrow as the foundational model for vocal learning, sensitive periods, auditory templates, and cultural transmission. Marler also studied Song Sparrow (degree 45), bridging two Passerellidae through one researcher. Ketterson studied the junco (degree 47). The quartet's three researchers (Nice, Ketterson, Marler) form a complete lineage of songbird science.

5. **The COVID song experiment (Derryberry 2020).** White-crowned Sparrows in San Francisco changed their songs in real time when the city went quiet during COVID lockdowns -- demonstrating vocal plasticity on a behavioral timescale, distinct from both dialect learning (cultural timescale) and subspecies divergence (evolutionary timescale). Three temporal scales of vocal change in one species.

6. **E=2 returns -- lower registers now exceed E=4.** E=2 + E=3 = 22 degrees (44.9%) now surpasses E=4's 21 degrees (42.9%). The equilibrium reached at degree 47 tips in favor of the lower registers. Four Passerellidae at E=2-3 have reversed the upper-register dominance.

7. **CSV substitution documented.** Tailed Frog replaced by White-crowned Sparrow. Fourth consecutive amphibian replacement (45-46-47-48). The engine continues through the amphibian belt of the original CSV.

### Eleven Consecutive Passeriformes

Degree 48 extends the Passeriformes streak to **eleven consecutive entries** (degrees 38-48). Forty-one of 49 entries (83.7%) are Passeriformes. Only 5 orders remain represented. The Passeriformes dominance is now even more critical than at degree 47. A non-passerine is structurally essential.

## Conservation Status

- **IUCN:** Least Concern [1, 2, 5, 6]
- **Global population:** ~79 million (Partners in Flight) [1, 2, 6]
- **Continental Concern Score:** 7/20 (low concern) [1, 2, 6]
- **BBS trend:** Approximately stable overall (1966-2019), with regional declines in some western mountain areas and increases in some northern regions [1, 2, 6]
- **Protected under:** Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 [1, 2]
- **Threats:** Habitat loss (especially degradation of brushy edge habitat), climate change (range shifts affecting mountain-breeding populations), window strikes, cat predation, pesticide exposure [1, 2, 5, 6]

The White-crowned Sparrow's conservation situation contrasts with the Dark-eyed Junco's (degree 47). The junco has a much larger population (~220-630 million) but is declining at -0.7%/year. The White-crowned Sparrow has a smaller population (~79 million) but is approximately stable overall. Two Passerellidae, different population trajectories -- one declining from a vast baseline, the other holding steady at a smaller baseline.

In the PNW, *pugetensis* faces potential threats from loss of brushy edge habitat as forests mature in former clearcut areas, and from climate-driven upslope shifts in vegetation zones that could reduce alpine and subalpine breeding habitat [1, 2, 4, 6]. However, the subspecies' ability to use suburban and urban edge habitat provides some buffering against habitat loss in natural settings.
