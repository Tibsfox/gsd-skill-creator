HOWLING MULTI-TONAL CHAOS — THE RIDGE IN WINTER — E=8

A Pacific frontal system driving over the Olympic Mountains produces: aeolian tones from every obstacle (large trunks producing drones, branches producing whistles, twigs producing hisses), the background roar of turbulent air moving through forest and over rock, the crack-whomp-crash of falling trees, the howl at ridge saddles where topography accelerates the wind. This is not the gentle wind through sitka spruce (documented at degree 249). This is the full violence of winter at ridge elevation in the Olympic Mountains — the same mountains that receive 140-170 inches of precipitation annually and see gusts exceeding 100 mph at frontal passage.

The Sound in Its Parts

Background roarSum of thousands of turbulent events: eddies, vortices, boundary layer turbulence from every surface. The omnipresent low roar beneath all other sounds.
Aeolian tonesWind across obstacles: trunk diameters determine frequency (Strouhal number). Large trunks drone; branches whistle; twigs hiss. Many simultaneously at many pitches = multi-tonal chaos.
Ridge saddle howlTopographic venturi effect: wind accelerated through narrow gap, sound amplified by constriction. The loudest point on the ridge. The howl that can be heard miles away.
Tree fallShallow-rooted Sitka spruce susceptible. Crack at the base (wood failing) → whomp of crown hitting other trees → crash of trunk hitting ground → secondary branch sounds. A significant acoustic event.

The Olympic Mountains

The Olympics are not volcanic (unlike the Cascades) but receive more weather energy: the Hoh Rainforest on the western slopes gets 140-170 inches of precipitation annually. Sequim in the rain shadow, 60 miles away, gets 16 inches. This moisture gradient is produced by the mountains extracting all available Pacific moisture from the prevailing westerly flow. At ridge elevation, the same westerlies that produce the Hoh's rain produce the wind storm.

"The Columbus Day Storm of October 12, 1962 is the reference event: sustained winds exceeding 100 mph at exposed Olympic Peninsula locations, gusts possibly exceeding 150 mph. 15 billion board feet of timber blown down. 46 deaths. The benchmark for what E=9 sounds like on the Olympic Peninsula. A characteristic major Pacific windstorm — the kind that occurs every 10-20 years — is E=8."

Olympic Mountains in Winter

Mount Olympus

7,980 feet (2,432 m). Highest peak. Above treeline in winter. No topographic shelter. Wind speed at summit during major Pacific frontal passage: potentially exceeding 100 mph sustained.

Hoh Rainforest

140-170 inches annual precipitation on western slopes. Same frontal systems that produce the rain produce the wind at elevation. The Pacific moisture delivery system at its maximum.

Sitka Spruce at Risk

The dominant species of the western Olympic slopes. Shallow root system (due to waterlogged soil, dense competition). In major windstorms, Sitka spruce blow over rather than break. Blowdowns create chaotic acoustic events.

Columbus Day Storm 1962

Historical benchmark. Extratropical cyclone with sustained winds exceeding 100 mph. 15 billion board feet blown down. 46 deaths in OR + WA. $235M damage (1962). The reference maximum.

From Coast to Ridge: The Storm Moves Inland

Juan de Fuca Surf (257) is the storm at the coast. Olympic Peninsula Wind Storm (258) is the storm at the first major topographic barrier. The same Pacific frontal system that generates 15-25 foot surf in the strait also drives 80-100+ mph winds across the Olympic ridges. The energy does not decrease as the storm moves inland; the mountains concentrate it at specific elevations and locations. Ridge saddles and notches receive the maximum wind. The forest at these elevations is the acoustic record of decades of storm accumulation: broken snags, wind-combed canopy, the bent forms of the ridge trees shaped by prevailing wind direction.
