## THE OLYMPIC MOUNTAINS IN WINTER

### One of the Wettest and Windiest Ranges in North America

The Olympic Mountains are an unusual mountain range:

- **Geology and elevation.** The Olympic Mountains are not part of the Cascade Range -- they are an isolated horseshoe-shaped range on the Olympic Peninsula, separated from the Cascades by Puget Sound. The range is composed primarily of basalt (subducted oceanic crust that was uplifted) and sedimentary rock. The highest peak is Mount Olympus at 7,980 feet (2,432 meters). The range lacks the volcanic character of the Cascades (no stratovolcanoes), but it more than compensates in precipitation and weather intensity.
- **The wettest spot in the lower 48.** The Hoh Rainforest, on the western slopes of the Olympic Mountains, receives 140-170 inches of precipitation annually -- making it one of the wettest temperate locations in North America. The Hoh gets this precipitation because it faces the prevailing Pacific storm track with no topographic obstruction between it and the ocean. Pacific moisture-laden air rises up the western slopes of the Olympics, cools adiabatically, and deposits enormous amounts of precipitation. The eastern slopes (rain shadow side) receive as little as 16 inches annually.
- **The rain shadow.** The Olympic Mountains create one of the most dramatic rain shadows in North America. Sequim, on the northeastern Olympic Peninsula, lies in the rain shadow and receives only 16 inches of precipitation annually -- less than Los Angeles. The Olympic Mountains extract almost all of the moisture from the westerly flow before it reaches Sequim. This moisture gradient -- 170 inches on the west, 16 inches on the northeast -- all happens across a distance of approximately 60 miles.
- **Wind at elevation.** The Olympic Mountains are exposed to the same Pacific frontal systems that generate the Juan de Fuca surf. At elevation (above treeline, on exposed ridges), these systems produce wind speeds that can exceed 100 mph in major events. The summit of Mount Olympus and the high ridges receive sustained storm winds that uproot trees, strip branches, and reshape the landscape on a geological timescale.

(Sources: Olympic Mountains geology; basalt/sedimentary composition; Mount Olympus 7,980 feet; Hoh Rainforest 140-170 inches annual precipitation; Olympic rain shadow; Sequim 16 inches annual; rain shadow gradient; ridge wind speeds exceeding 100 mph in major events)

## THE ACOUSTICS OF RIDGE WIND STORM

### What Produces the Howling Multi-Tonal Chaos

Ridge wind storm acoustics are distinct from lower-elevation wind:

- **Aeolian tone production.** Wind blowing across obstacles (branches, rocks, tree trunks, ridge features) produces aeolian tones -- notes generated by the vortex shedding from the obstacle's leeward side. The frequency of the aeolian tone is determined by the diameter of the obstacle and the wind speed (the Strouhal number relationship). Larger obstacles at higher wind speeds produce lower-frequency tones; smaller obstacles produce higher-frequency tones. On a mountain ridge with trees of various sizes, many different aeolian tones are produced simultaneously.
- **The multi-tonal quality.** "Multi-tonal chaos" is the apt description because many different aeolian tones are produced simultaneously and chaotically: the large trunks of old-growth Sitka spruce and mountain hemlock produce low drones; the smaller branches produce mid-range whistles; the finest twigs produce high-frequency hisses. These are combined with the roaring of wind through the gaps between trees, the crashing of toppling trees, and the structural sounds of branches breaking. The result is not a single pitch but a complex, unstable, multi-octave acoustic environment.
- **The roar.** The overall background is a roar -- the sound of a large volume of air moving rapidly and turbulently across a large surface area. This roar is the sum of thousands of individual turbulent events: eddies and vortices shedding from every obstacle, boundary layer turbulence from the surface, the wind's interaction with the complex topography of a mountain ridge.
- **Tree fall.** In major storm events, trees fall. The sound of a large Sitka spruce or Douglas fir falling in a storm is not a single crack but a sequence: the initial crack at the base (the wood failing), the whomp of the crown hitting other trees, the crash of the whole tree hitting the ground, the secondary sounds of branches breaking. A large tree fall is a significant acoustic event -- audible over the wind at distances of hundreds of meters.
- **Topographic amplification.** Ridge saddles and notches act as venturi zones: the wind is accelerated through the narrow gap, and the sound is amplified by the topographic constriction. The howl at a ridge notch is louder and more intense than the roar in the open forest below. Exposed ridgetops act as eolian galleries: every obstacle on the ridge produces its aeolian tone, and the density of obstacles (tree snags, rock outcrops, ridge features) creates the maximum acoustic complexity.

(Sources: Aeolian tone production; vortex shedding; Strouhal number; frequency vs. obstacle diameter; multi-tonal character in mixed forest; roar as sum of turbulent events; tree fall sequence of sounds; topographic amplification in saddles and notches; venturi effect; eolian gallery concept)

## OLYMPIC MOUNTAIN WINTER STORMS

### The Frontal System at Its Most Violent

- **Pacific frontal passages.** The Pacific Northwest receives Pacific frontal systems throughout the fall and winter: warm fronts, cold fronts, occluded fronts, and occasionally atmospheric rivers (Pineapple Express events) that produce extreme precipitation. The Olympic Mountains are the first major topographic obstacle these systems encounter after the coast. The frontal passage over the Olympics is always energetic; in major events, it is violent.
- **The wind maximum at frontal passage.** The strongest winds in a Pacific frontal system typically occur at the cold front boundary -- when the cold air behind the front undercuts the warm air ahead of it. At this transition, pressure gradients are at their steepest, and wind speeds at ridge level can exceed 100 mph. This is the sound environment of E=8: not a sustained breeze but a system delivering maximum atmospheric energy to the landscape.
- **Post-frontal clearing and the wind.** After a major cold frontal passage, strong northwesterly winds often persist for 12-24 hours as the cold airmass fills in behind the front. These post-frontal winds are typically at lower speeds than the frontal passage itself but are still vigorous (30-50 mph sustained, gusts to 70+). The post-frontal wind is the sound of the storm's aftermath -- still loud, still present, but gradually diminishing.
- **Damage patterns.** Pacific windstorms in the Olympic Mountains produce characteristic damage: uprooted trees (shallow-rooted Sitka spruce are particularly susceptible), broken snags, stripped branches (creating "wind combing" on the forest canopy), and occasionally catastrophic blowdowns where stands of trees fall as units in the same direction. The Columbus Day Storm of October 1962 is the historical benchmark: winds exceeded 150 mph at exposed Olympic Peninsula locations; vast areas of old-growth blew down.

(Sources: Pacific frontal system dynamics; warm/cold/occluded fronts; atmospheric river events; wind maximum at cold frontal passage; pressure gradients at frontal boundary; post-frontal northwesterly winds; Sitka spruce shallow roots; wind combing; blowdown; Columbus Day Storm 1962, winds exceeding 150 mph)

## THE COLUMBUS DAY STORM -- HISTORICAL MAXIMUM

### The Reference Storm for Olympic Peninsula Wind

- **October 12, 1962.** The Columbus Day Storm is the most destructive extratropical cyclone in Pacific Northwest history. A tropical storm that migrated north from the Pacific and intensified into a powerful extratropical cyclone, it struck the Washington and Oregon coasts with record-breaking winds. At some exposed Olympic Peninsula locations, sustained winds exceeded 100 mph; gusts may have exceeded 150 mph. The storm killed 46 people in Oregon and Washington, destroyed $235 million in property (1962 dollars), and blew down an estimated 15 billion board feet of timber.
- **The acoustic character of a Columbus Day Storm.** During such an event, the sound environment at an exposed Olympic Peninsula ridge is approximately: continuous roaring background at high volume; aeolian tones from all obstacles at high frequencies (high wind speed means higher aeolian frequencies); frequent tree falls with characteristic crack-whomp-crash sequence; structural sounds of the forest being physically reorganized. The E=8 rating for a typical major Pacific windstorm is the correct register for the characteristic event; a Columbus Day Storm level event would arguably be E=9.
- **Recurrence.** Major Pacific windstorms (not Columbus Day Storm level but still producing widespread tree fall and sustained ridge winds above 80 mph) occur approximately every 10-20 years at the Olympic Peninsula. The 2006-2007 winter produced significant windstorms affecting the region. The 1993 and 1995 storms caused significant blowdown. Winter visitors to the Olympic high country regularly experience events at E=7-8.

(Sources: Columbus Day Storm October 12, 1962; extratropical cyclone; sustained winds above 100 mph; 46 deaths; $235 million property damage 1962 dollars; 15 billion board feet blown down; acoustic character at storm maximum; recurrence 10-20 years for major events; 2006-2007 winter storms; 1993/1995 blowdowns)
