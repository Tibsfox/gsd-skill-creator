## What Worked

1. **The accipiter trio structure (225-227) is the engine's cleanest taxonomic arc.** Three species, three consecutive degrees, largest to smallest. The structural descent mirrors the S36 sequence (supergroup to side project to instrumental duo). The parallelism is architecturally satisfying.
2. **The Northern Goshawk as forest quality indicator is the engine's most specific conservation framing to date.** The bird's presence = intact mature forest. Its absence = ecosystem degradation. This indicator species function connects natural history to land management policy in a direct line.
3. **The alarm call analysis is the engine's most aggressive vocalization documented so far.** The Bald Eagle's call (221) was surprisingly thin. The Goshawk's alarm (225) is genuinely alarming -- machine-gun rapid, directional, escalating to physical attack. The contrast within the same family (Accipitridae) is dramatic.
4. **First *Accipiter* genus entry in the engine.** Degrees 221-223 documented Accipitridae eagles. Degree 225 introduces the accipiter genus -- the forest hawks, a very different ecological role within the same family.

## Discoveries

1. **The eye color progression (yellow → orange → red with age) is a reliable maturity indicator for field identification.** This age-to-color relationship is unusual in raptors and gives the Goshawk a visible aging signal that most species lack.
2. **Multiple nest site maintenance (anti-parasite strategy) is a specific behavioral adaptation worth tracking.** The Goshawk alternates between maintained nest sites in successive years, which reduces nest parasite load. This is an evolved behavioral solution to an ecological problem -- exactly the kind of specific natural history detail the engine should document.
3. **The old-growth logging connection makes the Goshawk a political species.** Not politically in the human sense, but in the ecological policy sense: USFS management guidelines buffer confirmed nest sites, and the species has been at the center of Pacific Northwest forest management debates. The bird's biology intersects human land use decisions directly.
