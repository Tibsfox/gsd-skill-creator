## THE ACCIPITER TRIO -- DEGREES 225-227

### Three Forest Hawks, Largest to Smallest

Degrees 225-227 constitute the engine's accipiter sequence -- the three *Accipiter* species resident in the Pacific Northwest, presented in order from largest to smallest:

- **Accipitridae and the genus *Accipiter*.** The family Accipitridae was introduced at degree 221 (Bald Eagle) and expanded with the Golden Eagle (degree 223). The genus *Accipiter* is the largest genus within Accipitridae -- containing approximately 50 species of short-winged, long-tailed, fast-flying forest hawks distributed worldwide. The genus name comes from Latin *accipere* (to take, to seize) -- a reference to the hawks' style of hunting, which involves sudden, explosive seizure of prey rather than the Bald Eagle's soaring patience or the Osprey's plunge-diving.
- **The three PNW accipiters.** Three *Accipiter* species breed and reside in the Pacific Northwest: *A. gentilis* (Northern Goshawk, the largest), *A. cooperii* (Cooper's Hawk, the medium), and *A. striatus* (Sharp-shinned Hawk, the smallest). Together they represent a morphological continuum -- the three hawks look similar in structure (short wings, long tail, barred underparts) but differ dramatically in size, from the crow-sized Sharp-shin to the crow-hunting Goshawk.
- **The taxonomic descent.** Presenting the accipiters from largest to smallest (225-227) creates a taxonomic descent that has a narrative logic: the most powerful forest predator first (225, matched with the most powerful supergroup), then medium (226, matched with a side project), then smallest (227, matched with an instrumental duo). The size sequence mirrors an emotional descent from grandeur to intimate precision.
- **Why accipiters matter ecologically.** The accipiters are the forest's speed hunters. Where the Bald Eagle soars above and watches, where the Osprey plunges from height, the accipiters pursue through dense vegetation at high speed, using their short wings and long tail for maneuverability in tight spaces. They are the forest's interior law -- the predators that keep songbird and medium-bird populations in check within the forest matrix.

(Sources: Accipiter genus 50 species worldwide; accipere Latin etymology; PNW accipiter species; forest interior hunting; morphological continuum; accipiter trio 225-227)

## THE NORTHERN GOSHAWK -- *ACCIPITER GENTILIS*

### The Forest Phantom

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Accipiter*. Species: *gentilis* (of noble birth -- from Latin *gentilis*). The name "goshawk" is from Old English "gos-hafoc" (goose hawk) -- a reference to its size and power sufficient to take waterfowl. The "Northern" designation distinguishes it from similar species in the Southern Hemisphere.
- **Size.** The largest accipiter in North America. Females (the larger sex): 0.8-1.5 kg (1.7-3.3 lbs), body length 55-61 cm (22-24 in), wingspan 98-113 cm (39-45 in). Males are significantly smaller: 0.5-1.0 kg (1.1-2.2 lbs). The size difference between sexes (sexual dimorphism) is pronounced in all accipiters but most dramatic in the Goshawk. A large female Goshawk can weigh twice as much as a small male.
- **Appearance.** Adults: slate-gray above, finely barred gray and white below, with a bold white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) and bright red eye. The supercilium is diagnostic -- it is thicker and more defined than in the other accipiters. The eye color changes with age: yellow in juveniles, orange in subadults, red in mature adults. Juveniles are brown above, streaked below, with yellow eyes. Immatures are identifiable but require care -- they are often confused with juvenile Cooper's Hawks.
- **The flight silhouette.** In flight, the Goshawk's silhouette combines the accipiter family traits -- short wings, long tail -- in a large-bodied form. The wingbeats are powerful and relatively slow compared to the Cooper's Hawk or Sharp-shin. The tail is rounded at the tip. The overall impression in flight is of something powerful moving through forest with a purposefulness that belies the dense vegetation it navigates.

## THE CALL -- ka-ka-ka-ka

### The Most Aggressive Raptor Alarm in the Forest

The Northern Goshawk's alarm call is one of the most striking sounds in Pacific Northwest forests:

- **The alarm.** The alarm call is a loud, rapid, repeated series: ka-ka-ka-ka-ka (or kek-kek-kek-kek-kek, or kak-kak-kak-kak depending on the individual and regional variation). The notes are sharp, hard-edged, and machine-gun rapid -- up to 8-12 notes per second at peak agitation. The call has an almost mechanical quality at high repetition rates.
- **Frequency.** The alarm call operates in the 1,500-4,000 Hz range. The frequency is mid-range -- not the thin high-pitched call of the Sharp-shin, but not the lower frequencies of larger raptors. The mid-range frequency carries well through dense coniferous forest, where higher frequencies are attenuated by foliage.
- **Directional specificity.** The Goshawk's alarm is directionally aimed at the intruder. Unlike broadcast territorial vocalizations that are designed to be heard across a landscape, the Goshawk's alarm is pointed -- the bird faces the threat and calls directly at it. The effect is of being singled out, which is precisely the intention.
- **Escalation.** The call escalates with threat level. At low disturbance, the Goshawk may give a single ka or a brief series. As the intruder approaches the nest or territory center, the call rate increases, the volume rises, and the bird may drop from the canopy toward the intruder while calling. At maximum agitation, the call merges with physical attack -- the Goshawk has been documented striking human heads with its talons during nest approach in the breeding season.
- **E=6.** The alarm call is E=6 -- above the mid-range, urgent and assertive. Not at the ceiling (E=8-9 would be a sustained aerial battle), but emphatically present. The call conveys a message that is impossible to misinterpret: you are unwelcome and I am serious.

## ECOLOGY -- THE MATURE FOREST SPECIALIST

### A Species That Requires Intact Forest

The Northern Goshawk is one of the Pacific Northwest's most sensitive indicator species for old-growth forest quality:

- **The habitat requirement.** Goshawks breed in mature or old-growth coniferous forest. The requirements are specific: large-diameter trees (for nesting), closed canopy (for concealment of the nest), and relatively open understory (for hunting maneuverability). The combination of these requirements means that Goshawk presence is a strong indicator of mature, structurally complex forest. Clearcut or heavily managed forest does not provide suitable breeding habitat.
- **The nest.** The nest (eyrie) is built in a large tree fork, typically 10-25 m (30-80 ft) above the ground. The nest is a substantial stick platform, lined with green conifer sprigs (which are replaced regularly -- fresh green sprigs in an active nest are a diagnostic sign). Clutch size: 2-4 eggs. Incubation: approximately 36-38 days. Goshawk pairs often maintain multiple nest sites within their territory and alternate between them in successive years, which is an anti-parasite strategy.
- **The prey.** The Northern Goshawk is one of the most accomplished bird and mammal hunters in North American forests. Prey includes: small to medium-sized birds (jays, woodpeckers, thrushes, corvids), squirrels (Douglas squirrel, red squirrel), rabbits and hares, chipmunks, and occasionally grouse and pheasants. The Goshawk can take prey up to and including the ruffed grouse in size. This prey range, combined with the Goshawk's forest interior hunting capability, makes it a top-level predator in the forest food web.
- **The hunting method.** The Goshawk hunts by perching in the forest interior and watching, then launching into rapid pursuit. It can fly at high speed through dense forest, using its short wings to fold and its long tail to maneuver around obstacles. The pursuit is brief and explosive -- if the Goshawk does not catch its prey quickly, it typically abandons the chase. The hunting is ambush-based: the goshawk relies on surprise, speed, and forest knowledge rather than the soaring aerial search of the Bald Eagle.
- **Territory size.** Northern Goshawk territory sizes vary by habitat quality and prey density, but typical breeding territories in the Pacific Northwest range from 500 to over 5,000 hectares. Large territories are characteristic of large raptor species -- the Goshawk requires a substantial forest block to maintain sufficient prey populations throughout the breeding season.

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION AND ECOLOGY

### The Old-Growth Connection

- **PNW range.** The Northern Goshawk is distributed across the Pacific Northwest in mature coniferous forests: the Cascades, the Olympics, the Coast Range, the Blue Mountains, and suitable portions of the Okanogan Highlands. In Washington State, breeding populations occur throughout the Cascades above approximately 500 m elevation, in the Olympics, and in northeastern Washington.
- **Elevation preference.** PNW Goshawks tend to breed at mid-to-high elevations, in the zone where mature Douglas fir, western red cedar, and Engelmann spruce provide the large-diameter trees and dense canopy the species requires. At lower elevations, mature forest is rarer (due to historical logging), and Goshawk presence is correspondingly reduced.
- **The old-growth connection.** The Northern Goshawk is one of the species most closely associated with old-growth forest in the Pacific Northwest. Old-growth forest -- with its large-diameter trees, multi-layered canopy, structural complexity, and diverse understory -- provides optimal Goshawk habitat. The loss of old-growth forest to 20th-century logging has reduced PNW Goshawk populations from historical levels, though precise historical data is limited.
- **Winter movements.** Goshawks are partially irruptive in winter -- in years of prey population crashes (particularly hare and squirrel cycles), Goshawks may move southward and to lower elevations in larger numbers than typical years. These irruption years bring Goshawks to suburban and urban margins that they would not normally occupy, and produce unusual sightings that generate attention from birders.
- **Comparison to other Accipitridae in the engine.** The Goshawk occupies a very different ecological position from the Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222) and Golden Eagle (degree 223). The eagles are soaring, open-country or water-edge hunters. The Goshawk is a closed-canopy, forest-interior predator -- the same family, vastly different ecological role. The accipiter trio (225-227) fills in the forest interior that the larger eagles do not access.

(Sources: PNW Goshawk range; Cascades; Olympics; Blue Mountains; elevation preference; old-growth association; historical logging impact; winter irruptions; forest interior vs. eagle comparison)

## CONSERVATION STATUS AND CONCERNS

### The Mature Forest Dependency

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern globally. The Northern Goshawk has a large range across the Northern Hemisphere -- it is found in North America, Europe, and Asia. The global population is substantial.
- **US/PNW status.** The picture in the Pacific Northwest is more complex than the global status suggests. The subspecies *A. g. laingi* (Queen Charlotte Goshawk or Haida Gwaii Goshawk), found on coastal islands, is considered vulnerable and has received conservation attention. Mainland PNW populations have declined from historical levels due to old-growth logging but are not currently federally listed in the US.
- **Forest management conflicts.** The Northern Goshawk has been at the center of Pacific Northwest forest management debates. The bird's dependence on mature and old-growth forest places it in direct tension with timber industry interests. The US Forest Service has established management guidelines for Goshawk in National Forest lands, including buffers around confirmed nest sites and recommendations for maintaining forest structure.
- **The indicator species role.** Goshawk monitoring programs in the Pacific Northwest track the species as an indicator of forest ecosystem health. Healthy Goshawk populations suggest adequate mature forest structure, sufficient prey populations, and intact forest ecosystems. Declining Goshawk populations signal ecosystem degradation. The bird functions as a biological canary for forest management quality.
- **Population monitoring.** Goshawk populations are difficult to survey -- the birds are secretive, occupy remote habitats, and actively avoid detection. Population trend data are less certain than for more easily counted species. Passive acoustic monitoring and systematic nest monitoring are the primary tools.

(Sources: IUCN Least Concern global; A. g. laingi subspecies; Queen Charlotte Goshawk; old-growth logging impact; USFS management guidelines; indicator species role; monitoring challenges)
