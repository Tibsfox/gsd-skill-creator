The Forest Phantom

The Northern Goshawk (Accipiter gentilis) is one of the Pacific Northwest's most secretive and formidable raptors. It inhabits the mature and old-growth coniferous forest interior — the habitat that the Bald Eagle (degrees 221-222) and Golden Eagle (degree 223) do not penetrate, the closed canopy spaces where short wings and a long tail allow rapid pursuit through dense vegetation. The Goshawk is the predator of the forest's interior law.

The alarm call — a rapid, hard-edged ka-ka-ka-ka-ka-ka — is one of the most aggressive vocalizations produced by any North American raptor. At nest approach, the call rate accelerates until it merges with physical action: the Goshawk will leave the canopy and strike intruders with its talons. This is not a metaphorical defense of territory. It is literal.

"The Northern Goshawk's alarm is directionally aimed at the intruder. The bird faces the threat and calls directly at it. The effect is of being singled out."

The Accipiter Trio (Degrees 225-227)

Three Accipiter species in three consecutive degrees — largest to smallest. The genus contains approximately 50 species worldwide; three breed and reside in the PNW. Together they cover the full gradient from mature forest interior (Goshawk) to suburban backyard (Cooper's) to migration corridor and feeder hunting (Sharp-shin). The trio is the engine's most concentrated taxonomic sequence to date.

Ecology and Habitat

Mature Forest Requirement

Indicator Species

Requires large-diameter trees, closed canopy, and open understory. Goshawk presence = intact mature forest. One of the PNW's most sensitive forest health indicators.

The Nest

Accipitridae

Built in a large tree fork, 10-25 m high. Lined with fresh green conifer sprigs (replaced regularly). Multiple nest sites in territory — anti-parasite strategy.

Prey Range

Ecology

Jays, woodpeckers, thrushes, corvids. Squirrels, rabbits. Grouse. Top-level forest food web predator. Can take prey up to ruffed grouse in size.

Territory

PNW

500-5,000+ hectares per breeding pair. The Goshawk requires a substantial intact forest block to support breeding season prey populations.

Size and Identification

The largest accipiter in North America. Females (the larger sex) weigh up to 1.5 kg with a wingspan up to 113 cm. Adults: slate-gray above, finely barred below, with a bold white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) — the diagnostic field mark. Eye color progresses from yellow (juvenile) through orange (subadult) to red (mature adult). The combination of large size, bold supercilium, and habitat (mature forest interior) makes a perched adult Goshawk one of the more striking raptor sightings in the PNW.
