Pied-billed Grebe

Podilymbus podiceps
the submerge call — a loud, cuckoo-like accelerating “cow-cow-cow-cow-cowp-cowp-cowp” series, NEW FAMILY Podicipedidae (first grebe), NEW ORDER Podicipediformes (four new orders in five degrees 56–60), THIRD CONSECUTIVE AQUATIC-PRIMARY SPECIES (58–60), first sustained diver, E=4, year-round resident, lake edge / pond / marsh / slow river — freshwater with emergent vegetation, 16th consecutive CSV substitution

CSV Substitution

The original CSV had Green Frog (Lithobates clamitans) at degree 60. Because the SPS Part B engine is birds-and-mammals only, Pied-billed Grebe replaces it. The substitution preserves the original’s lake edge habitat and E=4 energy. The Green Frog produces a distinctive “banjo plunk” call from lake-edge vegetation (E=4, Ranidae). The Grebe produces a loud cuckoo-like “cow-cow-cow” series from the same lake-edge habitat (E=4, Podicipedidae). Both are vocally distinctive but quiet relative to their surroundings. This is the sixteenth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (Pacific Chorus Frog at 45 through Green Frog at 60) — the engine’s deepest penetration into the amphibian belt.

New Order: Podicipediformes — New Family: Podicipedidae

The first grebe enters the engine. Podicipediformes is the fourth new order in five degrees (56 Columbiformes, 57 Coraciiformes, 58 Anseriformes, 60 Podicipediformes). Five consecutive non-passerine species (56–60) — the longest non-passerine streak. Passeriformes drops to 80.3%. Grebes are an ancient lineage, sister to flamingos (Phoenicopteriformes) in the clade Mirandornithes — the closest relatives of the small brown diver are tall pink waders. Podilymbus podiceps = “rump-footed rump-foot” — one of the most tautological names in ornithology.

Three Consecutive Aquatic Species (58–59–60)

Wood Duck (58, surface specialist, E=3) → Mallard (59, surface generalist, E=5) → Pied-billed Grebe (60, sustained diver, E=4). Three degrees, two orders, three aquatic strata: surface, subsurface tip, full submersion. The engine descends through the water column. Nesting matches the descent: tree cavity (58) → ground (59) → floating platform (60). Naming descends from poetry to anatomy: Aix sponsa (“bridal waterfowl”) → Anas platyrhynchos (“flat-billed duck”) → Podilymbus podiceps (“rump-footed rump-foot”).

The 61st Acoustic Element: The Submerge Call

A loud, cuckoo-like accelerating series — “cow-cow-cow-cow-cowp-cowp-cowp” — starting slow (~2–3 notes/sec) and building to a frantic chattering climax (~5–6 notes/sec) before stopping abruptly. ~500–2,000 Hz. E=4. The first tempo-direction element in the engine — not pitch rising or falling but tempo accelerating. Named “the submerge call” for the paradox: the bird produces a loud, carrying call and then sinks silently beneath the surface. Sound followed by disappearance. Three resonances: (1) acceleration — new acoustic property; (2) mismatch — exotic sound from a small drab bird; (3) disappearance — the 61st element is the first defined by its own erasure.

Sinking Without Diving — Controlled Buoyancy

When alarmed, the Grebe gradually sinks beneath the surface without diving — compressing plumage to expel trapped air, silently reducing buoyancy like a submarine taking on ballast. No splash, no movement, no ripple. Only the head may remain above water, periscope-like. No duck can do this. Where the Wood Duck flies and the Mallard flies or swims, the Grebe sinks. Becoming less visible rather than more active. The opposite of every other waterbird in the engine. Also dives conventionally for 20–30 seconds, propelled by lobed feet, pursuing fish and crayfish underwater — the engine’s first sustained diver.

NOT a Duck — Convergent Evolution

Despite swimming on the same ponds as ducks, the Pied-billed Grebe is not related to ducks. Podicipediformes and Anseriformes are entirely separate orders. Their similarity is convergent evolution: independent lineages evolving similar body plans. Fundamental differences: lobed feet vs. webbed, vestigial tail vs. functional, adjustable buoyancy vs. fixed, cannot walk on land vs. walks freely, floating nest vs. ground/cavity. The engine places two degrees of true ducks (58–59) followed by one degree of not-duck (60) — teaching homology (58–59) and convergence (59–60) in consecutive degrees.

Feather Eating — Internal Feltworks

Grebes eat their own feathers. The feathers form a feltlike mass in the stomach that protects the intestines from sharp fish bones and crustacean exoskeletons. Parents feed feathers to chicks from day one. Found in all grebes and no non-grebes. The thick, laterally compressed bill is adapted for crushing hard-shelled prey (crayfish, snails, diving beetles) — a crushing tool, not a filtering tool like the Mallard’s lamellate bill. Also: carries chicks on its back while swimming — no duck does this.

The Rump-Footed Rump-Foot

Podilymbus podiceps — Podilymbus from Podiceps (grebe type genus) + Latin colymbus (“diver”). podiceps from Latin podex (“rump”) + pes (“foot”). Full translation: “rump-footed rump-foot” — the name says the same thing twice, emphasizing that this bird’s defining feature is legs set at the very rear of the body. Compare: Aix sponsa (“bridal waterfowl” — aesthetic), Anas platyrhynchos (“flat-billed duck” — functional), Podilymbus podiceps (“rump-footed rump-foot” — anatomical). Three consecutive aquatic species, three naming philosophies descending from poetry to prose to body plan.

“Pied-billed” refers to the breeding-season bill: pale with a prominent black vertical band. “Grebe” from French grèbe, possibly from Breton krib (“crest”), though the Pied-billed Grebe lacks a crest. The genus Podilymbus contains only two species: P. podiceps (widespread) and P. gigas (Atitlán Grebe — EXTINCT since 1986).

The Sound: The Submerge Call

Accelerating “cow-cow-cow” series: begins at ~2–3 notes per second, accelerates to ~5–6 notes per second, climaxes in rapid chattering, stops abruptly. ~500–2,000 Hz, hollow, cuckoo-like. E=4. Duration 3–8 seconds. Both sexes vocalize — duetting is common. First-time listeners often mistake it for a mammal or tropical bird. It sounds nothing like a duck.

Vocalizations are innate — no cultural learning. Like the Wood Duck whistle (58), Mallard quack (59), Kingfisher rattle (57), and Band-tailed Pigeon coo (56), the Grebe’s call is genetically determined. Five consecutive non-passerine species (56–60), all with innate vocalizations.

Plumage: The Drab Diver

Overall: Small, compact, brown waterbird (31–38 cm, 253–568 g) that rides low in the water with virtually no visible tail. No bright colors, no iridescence, no bold patterns. After two consecutive degrees of extreme sexual dimorphism (Wood Duck baroque, Mallard graphic), the Grebe arrives with minimal dimorphism — males and females nearly identical. The evolutionary pressure differs: grebe courtship is vocal and behavioral (duetting, synchronous diving), not visual.

Diagnostic mark: Thick, short, pale bill with prominent black vertical band (breeding). Black chin/throat patch (breeding). Non-breeding: band fades, overall duller brown.

Feet: Lobed, NOT webbed — each toe has independent lateral flaps. Legs set extremely far back, nearly at the tail. Superb underwater propulsion, cannot walk on land. The fundamental anatomical distinction from ducks.

Habitat: The Vegetated Lake Edge

Freshwater with emergent vegetation — lakes, ponds, marshes, and slow rivers where cattails, bulrushes, and sedges provide cover. Requires both open water (for diving/foraging) and dense vegetation (for nesting/cover). Narrower than the Mallard’s all-freshwater range but overlapping. Year-round resident in western Washington lowlands. Common at Union Bay Natural Area, Nisqually NWR, Juanita Bay, and similar freshwater marshes.

Nesting: Floating nest — a platform of decaying vegetation anchored to emergent reeds, rising and falling with water level. Adults cover eggs when leaving the nest, staining them from white to brown for camouflage. Carries chicks on its back while swimming for 2–3 weeks after hatching. Clutch 4–7 eggs. Both parents incubate (~23 days).

The Ben Gibbard Solo Parallel

Degree 60 pairs the Pied-billed Grebe with Ben Gibbard solo in Part A — Death Cab for Cutie’s frontman appearing alone, stripped down, acoustic, intimate. Parallel resonances: (1) The quiet version — Death Cab (58) is the full band; Gibbard solo (60) is the voice alone. The Mallard (59) is the loud pond generalist; the Grebe (60) is the secretive diver. Both are the quiet version of something bigger. (2) Sinking vs. flying — the Grebe sinks rather than flies; Gibbard goes solo rather than arena. Both choose the less visible response. (3) Energy drops — Death Cab E=4 to Gibbard E=2; Mallard E=5 to Grebe E=4. The ensemble is louder than the individual. But the quieter version reveals what the louder version obscures.

Theory Nodes

S

The Submerge Call foundational

The 61st acoustic element. A loud, cuckoo-like accelerating series that builds in tempo from deliberate to frantic. E=4. Named for the paradox: loud call followed by silent disappearance. The first tempo-direction element — not pitch rising or falling but tempo accelerating. Music-theory parallel: an accelerando passage building tension through velocity.

P

New Order + New Family foundational

Podicipediformes (new order) + Podicipedidae (new family). Fourth new order in five degrees. First grebe. 26th family. Grebes are sister to flamingos — the closest relative of the small brown diver is the tall pink wader. Passeriformes drops to 80.3%.

T

Aquatic Trilogy (58–59–60) foundational

Three consecutive aquatic species descending through the water column. Surface (Wood Duck) → subsurface tip (Mallard) → full submersion (Grebe). Tree cavity → ground → floating platform. Poetry → prose → body plan. Homology (58–59) then convergence (59–60).

B

Controlled Buoyancy foundational

Sinks without diving by compressing plumage to expel air. Silent, invisible, costs nothing. Where ducks fly when alarmed, the Grebe becomes less visible rather than more active. Also dives 20–30 seconds, foot-propelled. The engine’s first sustained diver.

C

NOT a Duck — Convergent Evolution foundational

Same ponds as ducks, entirely different lineage. Lobed feet vs. webbed. Vestigial tail vs. functional. Adjustable buoyancy vs. fixed. Cannot walk vs. walks freely. The engine teaches homology (58–59) and convergence (59–60) in consecutive degrees. Appearance is not identity.

F

Feather Eating intermediate

Eats own feathers to form gut feltworks protecting against sharp prey. Parents feed feathers to chicks from day one. Found in all grebes, no non-grebes. The most unusual dietary behavior in the engine.

L

Lobed Feet intermediate

Individual toe flaps vs. duck webbing. Arguably more efficient for diving (less recovery-stroke drag). Ducks prioritize surface paddling; grebes prioritize underwater propulsion. Two evolutionary solutions to the same problem, now documented side by side (webbed 58–59, lobed 60).

D

Three Underwater Strategies intermediate

Walking (Dipper, 52), striking (Kingfisher, 57), swimming (Grebe, 60). Three different underwater worlds in eight degrees. The engine’s freshwater ecology is now three-dimensional: surface, benthos, and open water.

M

Sister to Flamingos intermediate

Molecular phylogenetics places grebes as sister to flamingos in clade Mirandornithes. The small brown diver and the tall pink wader are closest relatives. The most unexpected kinship in 61 degrees. Convergent evolution gave grebes a duck-like exterior; their heritage connects to the visually dramatic.

E

E=4 — Common but Cryptic intermediate

Widespread on freshwater lakes but secretive and easy to miss. Heard more often than seen. Prefers vegetated margins, sinks when approached, camouflaged among reeds. The cow-cow call carries; the bird hides. Loud acoustically, invisible visually.

G

Ben Gibbard Solo Parallel intermediate

Both are the quiet version of something bigger. Death Cab → Gibbard solo; Mallard → Grebe. Sinking vs. going solo. Energy drops in both sequences. The ensemble is louder than the individual, but the quieter version reveals what the louder version obscures.

R

Rump-Footed Rump-Foot intermediate

Podilymbus podiceps — the name says the same thing twice. Three consecutive naming philosophies: beauty (Aix sponsa), function (Anas platyrhynchos), anatomy (Podilymbus podiceps). Poetry → prose → body plan. The genus contains one living species and one extinction (Atitlán Grebe, EXTINCT 1986).

UNDERWATER WALKER vs. UNDERWATER SWIMMER. Dipper walks stream bottoms (52). Grebe swims through open water (60). Three underwater strategies: walking, striking, swimming.

SPS-004: Northern Spotted Owl

TWO SECRETIVE SPECIALISTS. Both heard more often than seen. Owl cryptic in old-growth canopy (4); Grebe cryptic in lake-edge vegetation (60). Both produce loud distinctive calls while remaining nearly invisible.

AVI: Wings of the Pacific Northwest

Pied-billed Grebe breeds and winters on freshwater lakes and ponds with emergent vegetation. Year-round resident. First Podicipedidae. New order. Most common grebe in PNW. Declining in some areas due to wetland loss.

ECO: Living Systems of the PNW

Lake-edge specialist dependent on emergent vegetation. Indicator for freshwater marsh health. Cattail/bulrush removal eliminates grebe habitat. Feather-eating behavior unique among engine species. Prey for eagles, herons, mink.
