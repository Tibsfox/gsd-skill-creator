## THE JAW-POP -- LAST WARNING

### The Sound Before Contact

The grizzly jaw-pop is the final warning in the escalation sequence:

- **The jaw-pop.** A sharp, percussive "CLACK" produced by rapidly snapping the jaws together. The sound is remarkably loud for its mechanism -- the grizzly's massive jaw muscles (capable of 1,200+ psi bite force) snap the teeth together with explosive force, creating a cracking sound that echoes through forest.
- **Escalation position.** The grizzly's threat escalation sequence: awareness (standing) → huffing/woofing → jaw-popping → bluff charge → attack. The jaw-pop is stage 3 of 5. By the time a grizzly is jaw-popping, the situation is critical. The bear has tried passive warnings and is now actively threatening.
- **Close-range signal.** Unlike the roar (which carries 1-3 km), the jaw-pop is a close-range signal -- effective at 10-50 meters. The jaw-pop says: "I am this close. I am this agitated. This is your last opportunity to leave."
- **Frequency characteristics.** The jaw-pop is broadband -- a sharp percussive crack with energy across 200-5,000+ Hz. It is not tonal like the roar. It is an impulse sound -- sudden, sharp, startling. The acoustic character is designed to trigger a startle response in the target.
- **Human encounter context.** Bear safety training specifically addresses the jaw-pop: if a grizzly is jaw-popping, you should be slowly backing away, speaking calmly, and preparing bear spray. The jaw-pop is the line between "the bear is uncomfortable" and "the bear is about to charge."

### Distance vs. Proximity

The comparison defines the grizzly's dual threat communication:

- **The roar (217).** Long-range. 50-400 Hz. Sustained. E=8. Dominance display. Territorial. Carries 1-3 km. Communicates size and authority over distance. The roar says: "I am here. I am large. This is my territory."

- **Different escalation stages.** The roar can occur at any threat level -- it is as much a declaration of presence as a threat. The jaw-pop occurs only at high escalation -- it is never casual. The jaw-pop is always serious.

## THE HUMAN-GRIZZLY INTERFACE

### Trail Encounters

The jaw-pop exists at the interface between human recreation and grizzly habitat:

- **Trail encounters.** As trails penetrate deeper into the North Cascades, encounters with grizzlies become more likely. The jaw-pop is the sound of this interface -- the moment when human and grizzly are close enough that the bear must decide between flight and fight.
- **Bear spray.** Bear spray (capsaicin-based aerosol) is the recommended defense at jaw-pop range (10-50 meters). It is 92-98% effective at preventing bear attacks. The jaw-pop is the signal to deploy spray if the bear charges.
- **Bluff charges.** Many grizzly charges are bluffs -- the bear runs toward the human but stops short. The jaw-pop often precedes bluff charges. The distinction between bluff and real charge is critical: stand your ground for bluffs, play dead for contact.
- **North Cascades context.** With fewer than 10 grizzlies in the North Cascades, jaw-pop encounters are exceedingly rare in Washington. But as recovery efforts potentially increase the population, encounters will become more frequent. Understanding the jaw-pop -- what it means, what to do -- becomes increasingly important.
