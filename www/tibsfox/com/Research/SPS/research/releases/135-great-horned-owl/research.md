## E=4 -- THE DEEP RESONANT HOOT

### Why E=4

The Great Horned Owl registers at E=4 for acoustic and ecological reasons:

- **Individual carrying power:** The Great Horned Owl's hoot is one of the most far-carrying bird vocalizations in North America. The deep, resonant "hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo" can be heard at distances exceeding one mile on calm nights. The low frequency (250-600 Hz fundamental) allows the sound to travel through forest canopy with minimal attenuation. This is a powerful voice.
- **Low repetition rate:** Unlike songbirds that sing continuously, the Great Horned Owl hoots periodically -- a series of hoots followed by silence, then another series. The acoustic impact is concentrated but intermittent. E=4 reflects this: powerful when it occurs, but not sustained.
- **Nocturnal amplification:** Night air is typically more stable and sound-conducting than daytime air (thermal inversions, reduced ambient noise). The owl's hoot takes advantage of the nocturnal acoustic window -- the same sound carries further at night. E=4 accounts for this environmental amplification.
- **E=4 not E=5:** The Great Horned Owl does not produce the sustained, flock-amplified volume of Canada Goose (E=5) or the extraordinary tracheal resonance of Trumpeter Swan (E=6). A single hooting owl is acoustically powerful but not overwhelming. E=4 reflects the balance between individual resonance and the intermittent, solitary nature of the vocalization.
- **Psychological impact factor:** The Great Horned Owl's hoot has an outsized psychological impact -- the deep resonance, the nocturnal timing, the association with wildness and predation. E=4 includes a component of this perceptual weight. The sound is more impactful than its raw decibel level would suggest.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998; engine internal analysis)

## THE QUINTESSENTIAL OWL SOUND

### Anatomy of the Hoot

The Great Horned Owl's vocalization is the **most recognized owl sound in North America**:

- **Pattern:** "hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo" -- a series of four to five deep hoots with a characteristic rhythm. The third and fourth notes are typically emphasized, with the second note slightly stuttered ("h'HOO"). The pattern is highly stereotyped but individually variable in timing and pitch.
- **Fundamental frequency:** 250-600 Hz. Deep, resonant, bass-register. Lower than most other North American owls. The low frequency is produced by the syrinx and amplified by the large body cavity and the facial disc, which functions as a parabolic reflector for both incoming and outgoing sound.
- **Duration:** Complete hoot sequence: 2-3 seconds. Individual notes: 0.3-0.6 seconds. Given in series, typically 3-8 repetitions with 10-30 second gaps between series. The rhythm has a deliberate, measured quality.
- **Territorial declaration:** The primary function of the hoot is **territorial declaration and pair communication**. Males hoot to establish territory and attract mates; females respond with a higher-pitched version (covered in degree 136). The hoot carries the message: "this territory is occupied."
- **Year-round hooting:** Unlike many songbirds that vocalize only during breeding season, Great Horned Owls hoot year-round, with peak activity in fall (territory establishment) and winter (courtship, pair bonding). The year-round presence makes this sound a **constant of the PNW night forest**.

### The Facial Disc as Acoustic Instrument

The Great Horned Owl's facial disc is a remarkable acoustic structure:

- **Parabolic reflector:** The concave facial disc concentrates incoming sound waves toward the ear openings, which are asymmetrically positioned (one higher than the other) to enable precise sound localization in both horizontal and vertical planes.
- **Outgoing amplification:** The facial disc also projects the owl's own vocalizations forward, focusing the hoot directionally. This is why the hoot sounds louder from the front than from behind the owl.
- **Asymmetric ears:** The right ear opening is higher than the left. This asymmetry allows the owl to triangulate prey position by comparing arrival times and intensity differences between the two ears. Accuracy: within 1-2 degrees in both azimuth and elevation. The Great Horned Owl can locate a mouse under snow in total darkness by sound alone.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998; Sibley 2014)

## THE TIGER OF THE WOODS -- PREDATORY ECOLOGY

### Apex Nocturnal Predator

The Great Horned Owl is the **most powerful owl in North America** and one of the most formidable aerial predators:

- **Talons:** Grip strength estimated at **300 psi** (pounds per square inch) -- comparable to the bite force of a large dog. The talons are the primary killing weapon. Once locked onto prey, the grip is essentially unbreakable by the prey animal. The talons can pierce the skull of a rabbit or the spine of a house cat.
- **Diet breadth:** The most diverse diet of any North American owl. Documented prey includes: mice, rats, rabbits, hares, squirrels, skunks (they are one of the few predators that regularly take skunks -- they have a poor sense of smell), opossums, raccoons, house cats, small dogs, other raptors (including Red-tailed Hawks, Barred Owls, and even other Great Horned Owls), ducks, geese, herons, crows, snakes, frogs, fish, scorpions, and large insects. The prey range spans from 5-gram insects to 4-kilogram prey -- an extraordinary 800:1 size ratio.
- **Predator of predators:** The Great Horned Owl regularly kills other raptors, including Red-tailed Hawks (taking them off nighttime roosts), Barred Owls, Long-eared Owls, and Cooper's Hawks. It is the primary predator of juvenile Peregrine Falcons at urban nest sites. This makes it a **superpredator** in the raptor community.
- **Hunting technique:** Silent flight (specialized feather edges break up turbulence), exceptional hearing (asymmetric ear openings for 3D sound localization), binocular vision (forward-facing eyes with excellent low-light sensitivity), and explosive strike speed. The owl drops from a perch or glides low, striking with extended talons. The kill is typically instantaneous.

### The Skunk Predator

The Great Horned Owl's willingness to take skunks is one of its most distinctive ecological traits:

- **Olfactory indifference:** Owls generally have a poor sense of smell. The Great Horned Owl exploits this -- skunk spray, which deters virtually all mammalian predators, is no deterrent. Great Horned Owls that have recently eaten skunk can be detected by smell at their roost.
- **Ecological significance:** Skunks are otherwise virtually predator-free as adults (coyotes occasionally take them, but reluctantly). The Great Horned Owl is the only regular skunk predator in the PNW. This makes the owl an important population regulator for skunks.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998)

## PNW DISTRIBUTION AND HABITAT

### The Most Adaptable Owl

The Great Horned Owl is the **most widely distributed owl in the Americas** and occurs throughout the PNW:

- **Habitat breadth:** Old-growth coniferous forest, second-growth mixed forest, deciduous riparian corridors, suburban woodlots, urban parks, desert scrub, agricultural edges, cliff faces. The Great Horned Owl's habitat tolerance is extraordinary -- it can be found from sea level to treeline, from wilderness to urban centers.
- **PNW range:** Throughout western Washington and Oregon, from the Pacific coast to the Cascades and into the eastern deserts. Common in lowland forests, suburban areas (where large trees remain), and along major river corridors. Less common at high elevations but present wherever trees provide nesting and roosting sites.
- **Resident year-round:** Unlike migratory Anatidae, the Great Horned Owl is a permanent resident. It does not migrate. Territory is maintained year-round, with the same pair often using the same territory for decades.
- **Urban adaptation:** Great Horned Owls have adapted to urban and suburban environments where large trees, parks, and open space provide habitat. They nest on building ledges, in abandoned structures, and in urban parks. Urban pairs take pigeons, rats, squirrels, and house cats.

### Nest Ecology

The Great Horned Owl has remarkable nesting behavior:

- **Nest piracy:** Great Horned Owls do not build their own nests. They **appropriate the nests of other large birds** -- Red-tailed Hawks, crows, ravens, herons, and squirrel leaf nests. They also use cliff ledges, tree cavities, and building structures. The owl moves into the existing nest and claims it by force or by precedence (nesting earlier than the original builder).
- **Earliest nester:** The Great Horned Owl is the **earliest nesting bird in North America**. Eggs are laid in January-February, even in northern latitudes where temperatures may be well below freezing. The female incubates through ice storms, snowfall, and sub-zero temperatures. This extreme early nesting means young are fledged by April-May, giving them the maximum time to learn hunting skills before their first winter.
- **Why so early:** The early nesting strategy has two advantages: (1) first access to the best nesting sites before other raptors return from migration, and (2) maximum development time for the young before the lean winter months. The tradeoff is brutal winter incubation conditions.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Houston, Smith & Rohner 1998; Sibley 2014)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound That Defines the Night Forest

The Great Horned Owl's hoot is the **defining sound of the nocturnal PNW forest**:

- **The hoot spectrum:** Deep, resonant, bass-register. "Hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo" at 250-600 Hz fundamental. Four to five notes. Measured, deliberate rhythm. Far-carrying -- audible at one mile on calm nights. The sound that says "owl" to most North Americans.
- **Nocturnal timing:** Peak hooting occurs in the hours after sunset and before dawn. The Great Horned Owl's hoot is often the last sound a person hears before falling asleep in a PNW forest and the first sound they hear before dawn. It brackets the night.
- **Seasonal peak:** Hooting intensifies in November-February during courtship and territory establishment. January and February -- when the owl is nesting -- are the peak hooting months. The deep hooting on a cold winter night is one of the most evocative sounds in the PNW.
- **Solo performance:** Unlike the flock-amplified vocalizations of Anatidae (118-134), the Great Horned Owl's hoot is a **solo performance**. One voice, carrying through the forest canopy. The acoustic structure has shifted fundamentally from the previous seventeen degrees: from collective to individual, from daylight to darkness, from water to forest.
- **The "who's awake" mnemonic:** The Great Horned Owl's hoot pattern is often rendered as "Who's awake? Me too!" -- a popular mnemonic that captures the rhythm and pacing of the hoot sequence.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
