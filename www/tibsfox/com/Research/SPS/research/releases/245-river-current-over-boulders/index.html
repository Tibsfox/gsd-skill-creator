After 244 degrees of living species — mammals, birds, fish, invertebrates, amphibians — the Sound of Puget Sound engine crosses a fundamental boundary. The Physics of River Sound

Turbulence

Smooth flow disrupted by boulders becomes turbulent. Eddies at every scale create pressure waves. Small eddies = high frequency. Large eddies = low frequency. All scales simultaneous: the polyrhythm.

Air Entrainment

Water tumbling over boulders into pools entrains air bubbles. Bubble collapse produces the rushing, high-frequency component of the river’s sound. Increases with turbulence.

Boulder Geometry

Each boulder creates a standing wave at a frequency related to its size and the flow velocity. Multiple boulders = multiple standing waves at different pitches. The superposition is genuine polyrhythm.

Pool Resonance

Pools downstream of boulders act as resonance chambers. Deep pools amplify low frequencies; shallow channels distribute frequencies evenly. River shape determines acoustic character.

Acoustic Profile — Mountain Stream Soundscape

Low-frequency turbulence: 20–200 Hz (boulder-scale eddies)
Mid-frequency standing waves: 200–2000 Hz (individual boulder pitches)
High-frequency air entrainment: 2000+ Hz (bubble collapse)
Overall: E=5 (continuous, present, moderately energetic at all times)

Cascade Range Rivers

  • Skykomish River — Central Cascades, above Index, WA. Steep gradient, massive glacial-outwash boulders, high Cascade snowmelt volume. Most acoustically active stretch.
  • Sauk River — Skagit tributary, drains Glacier Peak area. Glacier-fed: high, cold, fast. Upper reaches characterized by boulder fields.
  • Snoqualmie River (above Falls) — Rapids and boulder fields as the river descends from the Cascade foothills. Different character from the falls themselves (degree 246).
  • Wenatchee River — Eastern Cascades. Drier climate, exposed granite, significant boulder fields through Tumwater Canyon.
  • Elwha River (post-dam-removal) — Olympic Peninsula. After 2011-2014 dam removal, the river reclaims its natural channel above the former dam sites.

The Acoustic Spine

In a mountain watershed, the river is the acoustic spine. Every sound in its riparian zone — bird calls, insect sounds, wind in trees — is layered on top of the constant river sound. Streamside birds (the American Dipper, SPS degree 52, is the engine’s most relevant example) must pitch their calls above the river’s masking frequency range. The river has shaped the evolution of the sounds around it. It is the background against which the entire ecological web the engine has documented takes place.
