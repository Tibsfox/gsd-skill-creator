## FIRST BALAENOPTERIDAE: THE RORQUAL FAMILY

### The Largest Animals in the History of Life

With the humpback whale breach at degree 278, the engine enters the Balaenopteridae family. This is a milestone:

- **The family.** Balaenopteridae is the family of rorqual whales -- the largest whales and the largest animals to have ever existed on Earth. The family includes: Blue whale (*Balaenoptera musculus*, the largest animal in the history of life), Fin whale (*Balaenoptera physalus*, second largest), Sei whale (*Balaenoptera borealis*), Bryde's whale, Minke whale, Eden's whale, and Humpback whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*). The humpback is the only member of its genus (Megaptera), distinguished from the other Balaenopteridae by its long pectoral fins (the longest of any whale relative to body length) and its complex songs.
- **Rorqual.** The word "rorqual" comes from Old Norwegian/Old French, meaning "furrow whale" -- a reference to the pleated throat pouches that characterize the family. The rorquals feed by lunge-feeding: opening their mouths wide, engulfing enormous volumes of water, then straining krill or small fish through their baleen plates as they expel the water. The pleated throat expands to hold the water.
- **Scale.** The humpback whale is large by any measure: adult females average 14-16 meters in length and weigh 25-30 metric tons; adult males are slightly smaller. The largest individuals can weigh up to 40 metric tons. For context: an adult male orca (the apex predator of these waters) weighs approximately 5-7 metric tons. The humpback is six or more times heavier.
- **NE Pacific population.** The North Pacific humpback whale population was severely reduced by commercial whaling in the 20th century. As of recent estimates, the NE Pacific population has recovered substantially from near-extinction levels -- from perhaps a few thousand animals at the population nadir to current estimates of 20,000-40,000 individuals. This recovery is one of the conservation success stories of the marine mammal world.

(Sources: Balaenopteridae family taxonomy; rorqual etymology and feeding mechanism; humpback size and mass data; NE Pacific population recovery; NOAA humpback recovery assessment)

## THE BREACH: WHAT HAPPENS

### Forty Tons Fully Airborne

The humpback whale breach is one of the most spectacular behaviors in the natural world:

- **The mechanics.** A breaching humpback accelerates from depth, typically at a steep angle, using powerful strokes of its tail flukes. The acceleration is sufficient to propel the entire body -- 25-40 metric tons of mass -- out of the water. At the apex of the breach, the whale may be entirely or nearly entirely airborne, with only the tail flukes remaining at or below the water surface.
- **The mass.** The mass involved in a full humpback breach is approximately 25-40 metric tons -- roughly six to eight times the mass of a breaching orca (approximately 4-6 metric tons for an adult female, the typical breacher). The kinetic energy of the breach scales with mass: a heavier animal moving at the same velocity carries proportionally more kinetic energy.
- **The sound of impact.** When 25-40 metric tons of whale body hits the ocean surface, the impact generates a shockwave through both water and air. The underwater sound propagates through the water column and can be detected by hydrophones at considerable distances. The airborne sound is a thunderous concussive report -- described by observers as resembling an explosion or a very large artillery round. Witnesses at close range report the sound as physically felt as well as heard.
- **The spray.** The water displaced by the impact creates an enormous spray. The visual spectacle of a humpback breach -- the mass rising, the brief moment of near-total air-surface exposure, the spectacular cascade of water on impact -- is one of the most dramatic visual events available to observers in Pacific waters.
- **The frequency.** Breaching is not constant behavior. Humpbacks may engage in serial breaching -- multiple successive breaches -- suggesting that breaching has a significant communicative or social function, since the energetic cost of multiple breaches is substantial. Serial breaching is most common when other whales are nearby.

(Sources: Breach mechanics; tail fluke acceleration; mass data for full breach; kinetic energy scaling with mass; impact sound characteristics (underwater and airborne); serial breaching behavior; energetic cost analysis)

## THE SOUND: E=9

### Why Forty Tons Earns Maximum Energy

- **The source level.** The underwater sound of a humpback breach impact has been measured at source levels on the order of 115-125 dB re 1 μPa @ 1m, with the peak transient at impact potentially higher. This is lower by source level measurement than orca echolocation (224 dB) or sperm whale clicks (230+ dB). But the breach is not a focused acoustic signal -- it is a broadband physical impact across a wide surface area.
- **The broadband impact.** Unlike echolocation clicks or even vocalizations, the breach impact is a physical collision of two media (whale body and ocean surface) across an enormous surface area. The acoustic energy is distributed across a very wide frequency range and propagates in all directions. The total acoustic energy released may be comparable to or exceeding a sperm whale click, even if the peak source level at any particular frequency and direction is lower.
- **E=9 by mass and spectacle.** The engine designates the humpback breach E=9 because: (1) the mass involved (40 metric tons) is exceptional; (2) the sensory impact on observers (sound, visual spectacle, felt concussion) is at the maximum of whale behavior; (3) the comparison with orca breach (E=9, degree 273) is direct -- if six tons earns E=9, forty tons earns at least E=9.
- **SPS third E=9.** The catalog now has three E=9 events: orca breach (273, physical maximum, 6 tons), orca multi-pod chorus (277, social maximum), humpback breach (278, physical maximum, 40 tons). Two physical maxima and one social maximum. The humpback breach is the most massive single physical event in the SPS catalog.

(Sources: Breach impact source level data; broadband impact vs. focused signal comparison; E=9 mass-scaling argument; SPS E=9 catalog: three events)

## HUMPBACK BIOLOGY: THE FULL PICTURE

### *Megaptera novaeangliae* -- The Long-Winged New Englander

- **The name.** *Megaptera novaeangliae*: "big-winged" (Megaptera) "New Englander" (novaeangliae). The "big wing" refers to the humpback's most distinctive anatomical feature: the pectoral fins, which can reach one-third of body length -- the longest pectoral fins of any whale relative to body size. The "New Englander" name is historical: early European observers encountered the species primarily in the North Atlantic waters off New England.
- **The pectoral fins.** The humpback's long, knobbly pectoral fins (also called flippers) are its most distinctive external feature. They serve as stabilizers and maneuvering surfaces during swimming. During breaching, the extended pectoral fins are visible during the airborne phase, giving the whale a distinctive winged appearance at the peak of the jump.
- **Feeding.** The humpback is a lunge-feeder. In NE Pacific waters, the primary prey includes krill (euphausiids) and small schooling fish including herring and sand lance. A humpback feeding frenzy -- especially in aggregations, using coordinated bubble-net feeding -- is one of the most spectacular feeding behaviors in the ocean. Bubble-net feeding: one or more humpbacks swim in a spiral below a school of fish, releasing bubbles that rise to form a "net" that concentrates the prey; the whales then lunge up through the center of the net with mouths open.
- **The songs.** Humpback males produce complex songs -- sequences of sounds lasting minutes to hours, repeated continuously. Humpback song is one of the most studied bioacoustic phenomena in the natural world. Songs change gradually over seasons and years; all males in a population sing the same song, which evolves collectively. The songs are produced primarily on breeding grounds (Hawaii for the NE Pacific population), not on feeding grounds. The song is the other major acoustic dimension of humpback biology; the breach sound is the physical acoustic dimension.
- **Migration.** NE Pacific humpbacks migrate seasonally between summer feeding grounds in the North Pacific (Alaska, British Columbia, Washington) and winter breeding grounds in Hawaii and Mexico. The summer feeding season brings humpbacks within observation range of Pacific Northwest waters. Washington State whale-watching operations encounter humpbacks regularly in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and offshore Pacific waters.

(Sources: Megaptera novaeangliae taxonomy; pectoral fin length; feeding behavior -- lunge feeding; bubble-net feeding; prey species in NE Pacific; humpback song biology; NE Pacific migration route; seasonal presence in Pacific Northwest)

## THE NE PACIFIC POPULATION: RECOVERY

### From Near-Extinction to Recovery

- **Commercial whaling impact.** Commercial whaling reduced humpback whale populations worldwide to fractions of their pre-whaling numbers. The North Pacific population was hunted intensively in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Soviet whaling in the 1960s (which illegally took far more whales than reported) brought the population to its nadir.
- **International Whaling Commission protection.** Humpbacks received international commercial whaling protection under the IWC in 1966. The protection, combined with the collapse of commercial whaling more broadly, allowed population recovery to begin.
- **The recovery.** NE Pacific humpback whale numbers have recovered substantially. Current estimates suggest populations have returned to or exceeded historical levels in some regions. This recovery is one of the conservation success stories in marine mammal biology.
- **ESA status.** The US Endangered Species Act listed humpback whales as endangered in 1970. In 2016, NOAA divided the humpback whale into 14 distinct population segments (DPS) for ESA assessment. Several DPS were delisted or downlisted; others remain listed as threatened or endangered. The Central North Pacific DPS (which includes the Hawaiian breeding population and associated NE Pacific feeding populations) was delisted from endangered to threatened in 2016.

(Sources: Commercial whaling history; IWC 1966 protection; Soviet illegal whaling documentation; recovery estimates; ESA listing history; NOAA 2016 DPS assessment; Central North Pacific DPS status)
