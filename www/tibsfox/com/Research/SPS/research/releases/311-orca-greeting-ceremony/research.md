## THE GREETING CEREMONY -- BEHAVIORAL DESCRIPTION

### Full Pod Reunion: The Behavioral and Acoustic Event

The Orca greeting ceremony has been documented by field researchers at the Center for Whale Research (Friday Harbor, Washington) and other Pacific Northwest marine biology institutions over decades of Southern Resident Killer Whale observation:

- **The trigger.** Greeting ceremonies occur when resident pod groups that have been separated (typically for days or weeks while different parts of the extended population range in different parts of their territory) come together again. The event is triggered by reunion, not by a fixed seasonal calendar, though summer months when all three pods are in the Salish Sea simultaneously produce the most frequent superpod events.
- **The behavioral sequence.** The pods form facing lines at the water surface. Both groups mill in place, spy-hopping (lifting heads above the water surface to observe). Then one or both lines surge toward each other at speed. Contact: physical touching, rubbing, rolling, swimming in close parallel. All animals in both groups vocalize at full intensity throughout the approach, contact, and post-contact mixing phases.
- **The duration.** A full superpod greeting ceremony can last 30 minutes to several hours. The high-intensity simultaneous vocalization phase typically lasts 10-30 minutes; lower-intensity social mixing with continued calling continues afterward.
- **The frequency.** Full J-K-L superpod events are relatively rare &mdash; perhaps 20-30 times per year in years when the population is in good condition. Individual pod-to-subgroup greetings are more frequent. The full superpod greeting ceremony represents the maximum acoustic event.

(Sources: Center for Whale Research Friday Harbor WA; greeting ceremony documentation; trigger: pod separation and reunion; facing lines and spy-hopping; surge and contact; rubbing and rolling; simultaneous vocalization; ceremony duration 30 minutes to several hours; superpod frequency 20-30 times per year)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- THREE VOCALIZATION TYPES

### Pulsed Calls, Whistles, and Clicks

Southern Resident Killer Whales produce three primary vocalization types, all of which occur simultaneously during a greeting ceremony:

- **Pulsed calls (pod-specific dialects).** The primary social communication signal. Each pod has a repertoire of 7-17 discrete calls (designated by researchers with alphanumeric codes: S1, S2, etc. for each pod-specific call). The calls are produced by driving air through nasal passages into the phonic lips. They are not learned de novo by each individual; they are culturally transmitted, learned from older family members. The dialects persist across generations and are the primary acoustic signature of pod identity.
- **Whistles.** Higher-frequency, more variable, used primarily in close-contact social situations. Whistles are produced in different nasal passages than pulsed calls and can be produced simultaneously with pulsed calls by the same individual. They cover 2-20 kHz and are associated with close-range coordination and affiliative behavior.
- **Echolocation clicks.** Broadband clicks produced in rapid sequences (click trains) for navigation and prey detection. Significant energy in the 20-100 kHz range (ultrasonic), with some energy in the audible range. During greeting ceremonies, click trains from multiple animals overlap, creating a complex broadband acoustic background.
- **Simultaneous production.** During greeting ceremonies, multiple animals produce all three vocalization types simultaneously. The acoustic result is a dense, wide-bandwidth overlay of: pod-specific pulsed calls (the social identity markers) + high-frequency whistles (the close-range coordination signals) + echolocation clicks (the navigation signals). The full acoustic event covers 500 Hz to 100+ kHz.

(Sources: SRKW pulsed call repertoire; 7-17 discrete calls per pod; alphanumeric call designations; cultural transmission of calls; whistles 2-20 kHz; close-range coordination; echolocation clicks 20-100 kHz; simultaneous production during greeting; full acoustic range 500 Hz to 100+ kHz)

## THE SOUTHERN RESIDENT KILLER WHALES -- POPULATION AND ECOLOGY

### J, K, and L Pods: The Puget Sound Family

The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) are one of the world's best-studied cetacean populations:

- **Population structure.** Three pods: J (the smallest and most year-round Puget Sound resident), K (frequently in outer Salish Sea and coastal waters), and L (the largest pod, most frequently offshore). Total current population: approximately 73 individuals as of 2026, down from approximately 98 in the mid-1990s. The 1999-2001 crash (from 98 to 78 animals) is the most dramatic documented decline.
- **Social structure.** Matrilineal: groups are organized around elder females (matriarchs) and their descendants. Males do not leave their natal group. All offspring remain with their mother for life. The matriarch's knowledge (of seasonal salmon runs, foraging locations, escape routes) is transmitted to her offspring over decades and is a critical component of the population's ecological function.
- **Diet.** Fish specialists, primarily Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*). The SRKW dependency on Chinook salmon is nearly exclusive during summer months when salmon runs are present. The long-term decline in Chinook salmon populations in Pacific Northwest rivers is the primary cause of the SRKW population's continuing struggle.
- **Threats.** Three primary: (1) prey availability (Chinook salmon decline), (2) vessel noise and disturbance (acoustic interference with echolocation and communication, including from whale-watching boats), (3) contaminant loading (PCBs and PBDEs accumulated in fat-rich tissues through the food chain, impairing immune function and reproduction).
- **Endangered Species Act protection.** Listed as Endangered in 2005. Recovery plan developed; implementation constrained by the scale of Chinook salmon recovery required and political complexities of dam removal and fishing regulation.

(Sources: Southern Resident Killer Whale population structure; J K L pods; 73 individuals 2026; 98 animals mid-1990s; 1999-2001 crash; matrilineal social structure; matriarch knowledge transmission; Chinook salmon dependency; primary threats: prey/vessel noise/contaminants; ESA Endangered listing 2005)

## THE WATER COLUMN -- ACOUSTIC PHYSICS IN PUGET SOUND

### Why Orca Calls Fill the Salish Sea

Sound travels approximately 1,500 m/s in seawater (vs. 340 m/s in air). This difference has profound consequences for the Orca greeting ceremony's acoustic reach:

- **Effective radius.** An Orca pulsed call at full greeting intensity, traveling through the Salish Sea at 1,500 m/s, can be detected by hydrophone at ranges of 20-50 km, depending on the specific call frequency and the Salish Sea's bottom topography (which creates acoustic shadow zones and channels). The greeting ceremony fills a volume of the Salish Sea vastly larger than any equivalent air-based acoustic event.
- **Depth and the three-dimensional acoustic space.** Unlike any terrestrial acoustic event, the Orca greeting ceremony occurs in three-dimensional space. Calls travel not only horizontally but downward to the bottom and upward to the surface. The Salish Sea's bathymetry &mdash; up to 280 m depth in some areas &mdash; provides a substantial vertical acoustic path. The three-dimensional sound field of a greeting ceremony is immeasurably more complex than any equivalent terrestrial event.
- **Hydrophone documentation.** The Center for Whale Research and NOAA have maintained hydrophone networks in the Salish Sea for decades. The greeting ceremony acoustic signatures have been documented in detail. The call repertoire of each pod is fully cataloged; individual animal calls can be identified from recordings.

(Sources: Sound speed in seawater 1,500 m/s vs. air 340 m/s; effective radius 20-50 km; Salish Sea bathymetry 280 m maximum depth; three-dimensional acoustic space; Center for Whale Research hydrophone network; NOAA documentation; individual call identification from recordings)
