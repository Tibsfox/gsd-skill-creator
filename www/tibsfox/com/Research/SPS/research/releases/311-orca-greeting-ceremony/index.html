Orcinus orca — The Species

The Killer Whale (Orcinus orca, Linnaeus, 1758) is the largest member of the family Delphinidae (oceanic dolphins). Despite the common name, Orcas are dolphins, not whales in the taxonomic sense. Body length: males 6-8 m, females 5-7 m. Body mass: males up to 5,400 kg, females up to 3,800 kg. The distinctive black-and-white coloration, dorsal fin (up to 1.8 m in males), and saddle patch behind the dorsal fin are the primary field identification features.

In the Pacific Northwest, Orcas are divided into two primary ecotypes: Resident Orcas (Southern and Northern Resident populations) and Transient Orcas (Bigg's Killer Whales). The resident and transient populations have different diets (fish vs. marine mammals), different acoustic repertoires, and different social structures. The Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW) — J, K, and L pods — are the Puget Sound population most familiar to PNW observers and the population most endangered.

The Southern Resident Killer Whale Pods

J Pod

The smallest and most frequently observed SRKW pod. Year-round Puget Sound presence. J1 (Ruffles) was the most studied individual; died 2010. Current matriarch lineage.

K Pod

Smallest of the three SRKW pods. Typically found in the outer Salish Sea and coastal waters in winter. Joins J and L for summer superpod events.

L Pod

Largest SRKW pod. Often found offshore in winter. The most acoustically diverse pod due to its size. Joins J and K for superpod greeting ceremonies.

Superpod

When all three pods — J, K, and L — gather, the event is called a superpod. 60-80+ animals together. The greeting ceremony at superpod scale is the most acoustically extreme SRKW event.

Acoustic Profile — The Greeting Ceremony at E=9

Vocalization types: Resident Orcas produce three primary vocalization types: (1) pulsed calls — the primary social communication signal, pod-specific, culturally transmitted; (2) whistles — higher-frequency, used in close-contact social situations; (3) clicks — echolocation, used for navigation and prey detection. The greeting ceremony involves all three simultaneously.

Frequency range: 500 Hz to over 20 kHz. Pulsed calls: 500 Hz-5 kHz fundamental with harmonics extending higher. Whistles: 2-20 kHz. Echolocation clicks: broadband, up to 150 kHz but with significant energy 20-100 kHz. The full acoustic output of a greeting ceremony covers the entire audible spectrum and extends far into ultrasound.

Pod-specific dialects: Each pod has a set of pulsed calls that are unique to that pod and are culturally transmitted from generation to generation. The calls are learned, not innate. Individual animals within a pod share the same call repertoire. When multiple pods meet, the simultaneous production of different pods' calls creates an acoustic complexity that is unique to the inter-pod encounter.

E=9 calibration: Full pod (20-80 animals) + all three vocalization types simultaneously + inter-pod dialect complexity + water as transmission medium (sound carries 4x faster in water than in air, extending effective radius significantly) + reunion context driving maximum calling intensity. Combined: SPS ceiling at E=9.

Underwater acoustic environment: Sound in water travels approximately 1,500 m/s (vs. 340 m/s in air). Orca calls at full greeting intensity can be detected by hydrophone at ranges of tens of kilometers. The acoustic event at degree 311 is not bounded by normal air-based sound propagation limits; it fills a three-dimensional aquatic volume of significant extent.

The Greeting Ceremony — What It Is

The Orca greeting ceremony occurs when resident pod groups that have been separated — sometimes for days or weeks while different pods range in different parts of their territory — come together again. The behavioral sequence is documented and distinctive:

The pods form two parallel lines facing each other, at the surface. They mill in place, often spy-hopping (lifting heads above the water surface). Then one or both lines surge toward each other. Contact: physical touch, rubbing, rolling. Simultaneously, all animals in both groups vocalize at full intensity. The acoustic event begins as the lines converge and continues through the physical contact and rubbing phase. It is one of the most dramatic behavioral events in marine mammal biology.

The function of the greeting ceremony is not fully understood. It clearly reinforces social bonds at the pod level; the physical contact and shared vocalization are not consistent with any purely utilitarian interpretation. The greeting ceremony is a social and acoustic celebration of reunion. It is, by the available behavioral evidence, an expression of something that resembles joy.

Puget Sound and the SRKW

The Southern Resident Killer Whales are one of the most endangered marine mammal populations in North America. The current population (as of 2026) stands at approximately 73 individuals — down from a historical high and significantly below the population needed for long-term viability. The primary threats are Chinook salmon decline (their preferred prey), vessel noise (acoustic interference with echolocation and communication), and contaminant accumulation (PCBs, PBDEs) that concentrate in the fat-rich bodies of top predators.

The SRKW have been protected under the Endangered Species Act since 2005. Recovery depends primarily on Chinook salmon recovery throughout the Columbia River basin and the Pacific Northwest watershed system. The salmon recovery story is one of the engine's deepest threads: the salmon run (degree 307) and the Orca greeting ceremony (degree 311) are acoustically and ecologically linked. The Orcas need the salmon. The salmon need the rivers. The rivers need the forests. The engine documents the whole system.
