## THE FIRST LAGOMORPH: A NEW ORDER OF MAMMALS

Degree 73 is a taxonomic landmark. The Snowshoe Hare introduces **order Lagomorpha** into the engine -- the engine's **fourth mammalian order** (after Rodentia, Carnivora, and Didelphimorphia). Lagomorpha -- the rabbits, hares, and pikas -- is a distinct order that superficially resembles Rodentia but diverged from rodents approximately **80-90 million years ago** [1, 2, 3].

The distinction matters: lagomorphs are NOT rodents. They were once classified together (as "Glires"), and the two orders share a common ancestor, but they are as distinct as Carnivora is from any other order. Key differences [1, 2, 3]:

| Feature | Lagomorph (Hare) | Rodent (Beaver, degree 61) |
|---|---|---|
| Upper incisors | 4 (2 large + 2 small "peg teeth" behind) | 2 |
| Hind limbs | Elongated for leaping/running | Variable |
| Diet | Strictly herbivorous | Variable (herbivore to omnivore) |
| Cecotrophy | Yes (re-ingests fecal pellets) | No |
| Ecological role | Pure prey | Variable |
| Ear length | Elongated | Variable |

The Snowshoe Hare is the engine's **first pure prey mammal**. This is structurally momentous. Every mammal since degree 61 has been a predator, omnivore, or mixed-strategy species:

| Degree | Species | Ecological Role |

| 61-62 | American Beaver | Herbivore/engineer |

| 72 | Opossum | Omnivore |
| **73** | **Snowshoe Hare** | **Pure prey** |

The beaver at degrees 61-62 is herbivorous but is an ecosystem engineer -- not primarily defined by its role in the food web as prey. The Snowshoe Hare is defined by its prey status. Its entire biology -- the snowshoe feet, the seasonal camouflage, the explosive reproduction, the alarm thump, the distress squeal -- is shaped by the pressure of being hunted. The Snowshoe Hare exists at the center of a web of predators. It IS the food web.

## TAXONOMY: *Lepus americanus*

### Etymology and Nomenclature

- **Lepus** -- from Latin *lepus*, "hare." The genus contains approximately 30 species worldwide, distributed across North America, Europe, Asia, and Africa. Hares are distinguished from rabbits by their larger size, longer ears, and precocial young (born furred and with eyes open, unlike the altricial young of rabbits) [1, 2].

- **americanus** -- "of America." Named by Johann Christian Daniel von Erxleben in 1777. The species epithet is broad, reflecting the Snowshoe Hare's vast distribution across North America from coast to coast [1, 2].

- **Common names:** Snowshoe Hare (standard), Varying Hare (referring to the seasonal coat change), Snowshoe Rabbit (colloquial misnomer -- it is a hare, not a rabbit). The name "Snowshoe" refers to the large, heavily furred hind feet that function like snowshoes, distributing the animal's weight on soft snow [1, 2].

### Classification

- **Kingdom:** Animalia
- **Phylum:** Chordata
- **Class:** Mammalia (13th mammalian entry; 11th mammalian species)
- **Order:** Lagomorpha -- **NEW ORDER (4th mammalian order)**. Approximately 92 species in two families: Leporidae (rabbits and hares) and Ochotonidae (pikas). Diverged from Rodentia approximately 80-90 million years ago. Distributed worldwide except Antarctica, southern South America, and most oceanic islands [1, 2, 3].
- **Family:** Leporidae -- **NEW FAMILY (33rd)**. The rabbit and hare family. Approximately 63 species in 11 genera. Distinguished from Ochotonidae (pikas) by elongated ears, elongated hind limbs, and larger body size. The most speciose family in Lagomorpha [1, 2, 3].
- **Genus:** *Lepus* -- the true hares. Approximately 30 species. Distinguished from rabbits (*Sylvilagus* and others) by: (1) larger body size, (2) longer ears and hind legs, (3) precocial young (born furred, eyes open, mobile within hours), (4) no burrow-digging -- hares rest on the surface in shallow depressions ("forms") [1, 2].
- **Species:** *L. americanus* -- the Snowshoe Hare. Distributed across boreal and montane forests from Alaska to Newfoundland, south to the northern United States and in mountain ranges to New Mexico and California. In Washington, found from sea level to subalpine zones, primarily in areas with dense understory [1, 2, 3, 4].

### Hares vs. Rabbits

The Snowshoe Hare is a **hare**, not a rabbit [1, 2, 3]:

| Feature | Hares (*Lepus*) | Rabbits (*Sylvilagus*) |
|---|---|---|
| Young at birth | Precocial (furred, eyes open) | Altricial (naked, blind) |
| Nest | Surface "form" (no burrow) | Underground burrow |
| Body size | Larger | Smaller |
| Hind legs | Long, powerful | Shorter |
| Ears | Longer | Shorter |
| Social behavior | Largely solitary | Often colonial |

## PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE SNOWSHOE SPECIALIST

### Body Form

The Snowshoe Hare is a medium-sized hare built for life in dense, snowy forest [1, 2, 3]:

- **Length:** 363-520 mm (14-20 inches) total. Ears: 62-70 mm (moderate for a hare -- shorter than jackrabbits, reflecting the forest habitat where long ears are a liability in dense brush). Hind foot: 117-150 mm -- proportionally very large [1, 2, 3].
- **Weight:** 1.0-2.3 kg (2.2-5.0 lbs). Lighter than the Opossum (72, 1.8-6.4 kg) and much lighter than the Raccoon (70, 3.5-9.0 kg). Males and females similar in size [1, 2, 3].
- **Body shape:** Compact, rounded, with powerful hind legs built for explosive acceleration and direction changes. The body is designed for evasion -- sudden sprints, sharp turns, zigzag patterns through dense brush. The Snowshoe Hare can reach speeds of 45 km/h (28 mph) in short bursts and can change direction instantly [1, 2, 3].

### The Snowshoe Feet

The defining physical feature -- the trait that gives the species its name [1, 2, 3]:

- **Structure:** The hind feet are disproportionately large (117-150 mm, nearly a third of body length). In winter, the feet are densely furred with stiff hairs that spread when the foot is placed on snow, creating a natural snowshoe effect.
- **Function:** The snowshoe feet distribute the hare's weight across a larger surface area, preventing it from sinking into soft snow. This gives the Snowshoe Hare a critical advantage over predators in winter -- a Canada Lynx or Coyote sinks deeper into the snow than the hare, allowing the hare to outrun predators on snow surfaces.
- **Seasonal change:** The foot fur becomes thicker and denser in winter, enhancing the snowshoe effect. In summer, the fur thins.

### The Seasonal Coat Change

The Snowshoe Hare changes color seasonally -- **the second species in the engine with seasonal color change** after the Short-tailed Weasel (SPS-067) [1, 2, 3]:

- **Summer coat:** Rich brown to grayish-brown on the back and sides, white on the belly. Effective camouflage against forest floor leaf litter and deadfall.
- **Winter coat:** Pure white (except for black ear tips). Effective camouflage against snow-covered forest floor.
- **Transition:** The molt takes approximately 8-10 weeks and is triggered by photoperiod (day length), not temperature or snow cover. This means the hare's color change is calibrated to the average snow timing for its latitude -- but in years of early or late snow, the hare can be conspicuously mismatched (white hare on brown ground, or brown hare on white snow). Climate change is increasingly causing this mismatch [1, 2, 3, 5].
- **Mechanism:** The winter coat grows in white -- the white hairs are not bleached brown hairs but new white hairs replacing the shed brown ones. The process is reversed in spring.

**Comparison with the Short-tailed Weasel (SPS-067):**

| Feature | Snowshoe Hare (73) | Short-tailed Weasel (67) |
|---|---|---|
| Summer color | Brown | Brown |
| Winter color | White (black ear tips) | White (black tail tip) |
| Retained dark feature | Ear tips | Tail tip |
| Trigger | Photoperiod | Photoperiod |
| Function | Prey camouflage | Predator camouflage |
| Ecological role | Prey | Predator |

Both change color. Both keep one dark accent (ear tips vs. tail tip). Both are triggered by day length. But they are on opposite sides of the predator-prey divide: the weasel changes color to hunt unseen; the hare changes color to hide unseen. The same adaptation serving opposite purposes.

## THE SOUND: THUMP ON GROUND / SQUEAL IF CAUGHT -- 74th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Alarm Sound

The Snowshoe Hare's acoustic contribution is primarily alarm-based [1, 2, 3]:

**Thump:** A sharp, percussive impact produced by the hare striking the ground with its large hind feet. The thump is a warning signal -- an alarm call that alerts nearby hares to danger. The sound carries through the forest floor substrate (ground vibration) as well as through the air. The thump says: "Danger. Predator. Flee." It is one of the simplest alarm signals in the mammalian world -- a single physical impact, requiring no vocalization, produced by the same feet that carry the hare to safety.

**Squeal:** A high-pitched distress cry produced when the hare is caught by a predator. The squeal is sharp, piercing, and involuntary -- a sound of terror and pain. Unlike the thump (which is a deliberate alarm), the squeal is reactive -- produced only upon capture. The squeal may serve to startle the predator momentarily (potentially allowing escape) or to warn other hares of immediate, lethal danger.

### Why Thump / Squeal Is the 74th Acoustic Element

1. **Pure prey vocalization.** The Snowshoe Hare's sounds are entirely defined by its prey status. The thump warns of predators. The squeal accompanies capture by predators. Every sound the hare makes is about predation -- being hunted, fleeing, being caught. This is the engine's first pure prey vocalization.

2. **Ground-coupled alarm.** The thump is distinctive because it is transmitted through the ground as well as the air. The hare's large snowshoe feet striking the forest floor create vibrations that nearby hares can detect through their own feet. This is the first ground-coupled alarm in the engine.

3. **E=3 -- moderate energy.** The hare is more audible than the secretive opossum (E=2) but less conspicuous than the fox's bark (E=4). The thump is loud but brief. The squeal is piercing but rare. The hare is usually silent -- the sounds are events, not routine communication.

4. **Complementary to the hiss/click (72, Opossum).** The Opossum at degree 72 produces defensive vocalizations when cornered. The hare at degree 73 produces alarm and distress vocalizations when detected and caught. Both are responses to predation pressure. The opossum hisses and plays dead. The hare thumps and runs. Different prey strategies: deception versus evasion.

## THE LYNX-HARE CYCLE: ECOLOGY'S MOST FAMOUS PREDATOR-PREY DYNAMIC

### The Population Cycle

The Snowshoe Hare is one half of the most famous predator-prey relationship in ecology -- the **lynx-hare population cycle** [1, 2, 3, 5, 6]:

- **The cycle:** Snowshoe Hare populations in the boreal forest undergo dramatic oscillations on an approximately **10-year cycle**. The population rises from a low of approximately 1-2 hares per hectare to a peak of approximately 15-25 hares per hectare -- a 10-fold to 25-fold increase -- then crashes back to the low point. The cycle has been documented for over 200 years through Hudson's Bay Company fur trapping records.

- **The Canada Lynx follows:** The Canada Lynx (*Lynx canadensis*) is a specialist predator of the Snowshoe Hare -- hares constitute approximately 60-97% of the lynx's diet. As the hare population rises, lynx reproduction increases and lynx numbers grow. When the hare population crashes, the lynx population crashes 1-2 years later (the lag reflecting the lynx's longer generation time). The two populations oscillate in a coupled, time-lagged cycle.

- **The textbook example:** The lynx-hare cycle is the most commonly cited example of predator-prey population dynamics in ecology textbooks. The data -- derived from centuries of Hudson's Bay Company fur records -- produces the classic oscillating curves that appear in every introductory ecology course. Lotka-Volterra equations, predator-prey models, and population dynamics theory are all taught using the lynx-hare system.

- **Complexity:** The cycle is not purely predator-driven. Snowshoe Hare populations appear to cycle even in the absence of lynx, suggesting that food availability (overbrowsing of willows and birch during peak populations) and stress physiology (chronic predation stress affecting reproduction) also drive the cycle. The hare is not merely passive prey -- its own biology and its impact on vegetation contribute to the oscillation.

### The Web of Predators

The Snowshoe Hare does not merely feed the lynx. It is a **keystone prey species** -- the foundation of the boreal and montane forest food web [1, 2, 3]:

Predators documented to regularly hunt Snowshoe Hares:
- **Canada Lynx** -- the specialist. 60-97% of diet.
- **Coyote** -- generalist, hares as primary winter prey.
- **Red Fox** (SPS-071) -- significant prey item.
- **American Marten** (SPS-065) -- takes hares occasionally.
- **Great Horned Owl** -- major avian predator of hares.
- **Northern Goshawk** -- forest raptor, hares as primary prey.
- **Golden Eagle** -- takes hares in open areas.
- **Bobcat** -- secondary predator.
- **Ermine/Short-tailed Weasel** (SPS-067) -- takes leverets (young hares).
- **Fisher** (SPS-066) -- occasional predator.

The hare feeds them all. When the hare population crashes, the entire predator community is affected. Species switch to alternative prey. Lynx starve or emigrate. Goshawk reproduction declines. The Snowshoe Hare is not just prey -- it is the **ecological engine** of the boreal forest food web.

## BRUSHY FOREST HABITAT: THE UNDERSTORY SPECIALIST

The Snowshoe Hare occupies **brushy forest** -- dense, thick-understory habitat that provides concealment from predators [1, 2, 3, 4]:

- **Habitat type:** Dense coniferous and mixed forest with thick shrub understory, young regenerating forest stands, brushy riparian margins, subalpine thickets. The key requirement is **dense ground-level cover** -- the hare needs to be invisible from above (raptors) and concealed from the sides (mammalian predators). Open forest with sparse understory is avoided.

- **Washington distribution:** Found from lowland forests to subalpine zones throughout the Cascades and Olympics. Most common in young, dense forest stands with thick brush -- especially regenerating clearcuts (5-20 years old) and riparian areas with dense willow and alder.

- **Habitat connection:** The brushy forest is distinct from the woodland edge of the Opossum (72) and Striped Skunk (69). The opossum and skunk live at the edge -- the transition between forest and open ground. The hare lives in the **interior** -- deep in the dense brush, where the cover is thickest. The edge specialist versus the understory specialist.

## BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY: THE SILENT RUNNER

### Movement and Activity

The Snowshoe Hare's behavior is defined by predator avoidance [1, 2, 3]:

- **Crepuscular/nocturnal:** Most active at dawn, dusk, and during the night. Rests during the day in a shallow depression ("form") under dense cover. Unlike rabbits, the hare does not dig burrows -- it rests on the surface, relying on camouflage and cover.
- **Home range:** 2-8 hectares, depending on habitat quality and population density. Home ranges overlap -- hares are not territorial. During population peaks, hares may be found in very high densities.
- **Runway system:** Snowshoe Hares establish and maintain a network of trails ("runways") through the brush. These well-worn paths are used repeatedly and provide known escape routes when predators approach. In winter, the runways become compacted snow trails that allow rapid movement. The runway system is communal -- multiple hares use the same trails.
- **Evasion strategy:** When flushed by a predator, the hare explodes from cover in a burst of speed (up to 45 km/h), zigzagging through the brush, using its knowledge of the runway system to navigate dense vegetation that slows pursuing predators. The large hind feet provide traction. The white winter coat provides camouflage against snow.

### Reproduction: The Prey Species Strategy

The Snowshoe Hare's reproductive strategy reflects its prey status [1, 2, 3]:

- **Breeding season:** March-August in Washington (climate-dependent).
- **Gestation:** Approximately 36-37 days.
- **Litters per year:** 2-4 litters per season.
- **Litter size:** 1-8 leverets (average 3-4).
- **Precocial young:** Leverets are born fully furred, eyes open, and capable of movement within hours. This is a hare trait (distinct from rabbits, whose young are altricial). Precocial young can scatter and hide immediately, reducing predation on the litter.
- **Breeding during population peaks:** At peak population density, a single female may produce 15-20 leverets per year. This explosive reproduction is the engine that drives the population upswing.

### Diet: The Herbivore

The Snowshoe Hare is strictly herbivorous [1, 2, 3]:

- **Summer:** Grasses, forbs, clovers, dandelions, ferns, and green vegetation. The diet is diverse and nutritious.
- **Winter:** Twigs, bark, and buds of deciduous trees and shrubs -- willow, birch, alder, aspen. The winter diet is lower in nutrition, and at peak population densities, hares can strip browse plants to the point of killing them (contributing to the population crash).
- **Cecotrophy:** Like all lagomorphs, the Snowshoe Hare re-ingests soft fecal pellets (cecotropes) to extract maximum nutrition from plant material. This double-digestion is a lagomorph adaptation to low-quality plant food.

## THE SHORT-TAILED WEASEL CONNECTION (SPS-067)

Both change color seasonally -- predator and prey [1, 2, 3]:

- **Both brown in summer, white in winter.** The same adaptation on opposite sides of the predator-prey divide. The weasel changes color to hunt unseen. The hare changes color to hide unseen.
- **Both retain one dark accent.** The weasel keeps its black tail tip. The hare keeps its black ear tips. The dark accents may serve different functions: the weasel's black tail tip may deflect predator strikes (a raptor targeting the tail tip misses the body); the hare's black ear tips may enhance visual communication between hares.
- **Both triggered by photoperiod.** Both species' molt cycles are triggered by changing day length, not temperature or snow cover. Both are vulnerable to climate mismatch -- early or late snow creating conspicuous white-on-brown or brown-on-white mismatches.
- **Predator-prey pair.** The Short-tailed Weasel (ermine) preys on leverets (young hares). The two species share habitat and change in parallel. The predator and the prey, both white in winter, both brown in summer, stalking and fleeing through the same forest.

## THE RED FOX CONNECTION (SPS-071)

Predator-prey relationship [1, 2, 3]:

- **Foxes prey on Snowshoe Hares.** The Red Fox is a significant predator of the Snowshoe Hare, especially in winter when other prey is scarce. The fox's hunting strategy -- pouncing through snow -- is partially countered by the hare's snowshoe feet (the hare stays on top of the snow, the fox sinks).
- **Evasion versus pursuit.** The hare's zigzag flight through dense brush versus the fox's dogged pursuit. The hare uses its knowledge of the runway system. The fox uses its endurance and cunning.

## THE AMERICAN MARTEN CONNECTION (SPS-065)

Predator-prey in the deep forest [1, 2, 3]:

- **Martens prey on Snowshoe Hares.** The American Marten is an arboreal predator that occasionally takes hares, especially leverets. Both occupy the same dense forest habitat. The marten hunts from the trees; the hare hides in the brush.
- **Both forest interior species.** Both the marten and the hare are specialists of dense, mature or regenerating forest. Neither thrives in open habitat. Both are the deep forest's creatures.

## THE SPOTTED OWL CONNECTION (SPS-004)

Forest prey connection [1, 2, 3]:

- **Both forest dwellers.** The Spotted Owl (SPS-004) and the Snowshoe Hare both inhabit dense forest. The owl is a raptor that hunts forest mammals; the hare is a forest mammal that is hunted. Though the Spotted Owl primarily hunts smaller prey (flying squirrels, woodrats), the connection is ecological: both species depend on dense forest habitat, and both are affected by forest management practices.
- **Old forest versus young forest.** The Spotted Owl requires old-growth forest. The Snowshoe Hare prefers young, dense forest with thick understory. They represent different successional stages of the same forest type.
