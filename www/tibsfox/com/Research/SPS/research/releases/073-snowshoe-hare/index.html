Snowshoe Hare

Lepus americanus

First Lagomorph — Order Lagomorpha Enters

The Snowshoe Hare introduces order Lagomorpha — the engine’s 4th mammalian order (after Rodentia, Carnivora, Didelphimorphia). Lagomorphs diverged from rodents approximately 80–90 million years ago. NOT rodents. Two families: Leporidae (rabbits and hares) and Ochotonidae (pikas). ~92 species worldwide. Leporidae is the 33rd family — the fifth new family in five consecutive degrees (69–73).

First Pure Prey Mammal

Every mammal since degree 61 has been a predator or omnivore. The Snowshoe Hare is the engine’s first pure prey mammal. Its entire biology — snowshoe feet, seasonal camouflage, explosive reproduction, alarm thump, distress squeal — is shaped by the pressure of being hunted. The hare feeds 10+ predator species. When the hare population crashes, the entire predator community is affected. The Snowshoe Hare IS the food web.

The Seasonal Coat Change

Brown in Summer, White in Winter — Second After Short-tailed Weasel (67)

The second species in the engine with seasonal coat change (after Short-tailed Weasel, SPS-067). Brown in summer, pure white in winter (except black ear tips). Triggered by photoperiod (day length), not temperature. Predator-prey parallel: the weasel changes color to hunt unseen; the hare changes color to hide unseen. Both retain one dark accent — the weasel keeps its black tail tip, the hare keeps its black ear tips. Both are vulnerable to climate mismatch as snow patterns change.

The Lynx-Hare Cycle

Ecology’s Most Famous Predator-Prey Dynamic

The 10-year population cycle of the Snowshoe Hare is the most commonly cited example of predator-prey dynamics in ecology. Documented for 200+ years through Hudson’s Bay Company fur records. Hare populations oscillate from 1–2 per hectare (low) to 15–25 per hectare (peak) — a 10–25x increase. The Canada Lynx follows 1–2 years behind: hares rise, lynx rise; hares crash, lynx crash. The textbook example. The keystone prey species that drives the boreal forest food web.

The Sound: Thump and Squeal

The Snowshoe Hare’s acoustic contribution is entirely alarm-based. The thump — a sharp, percussive impact produced by striking the ground with large hind feet — is a warning signal that alerts nearby hares to predator presence. The thump is ground-coupled: transmitted through the earth as seismic vibration as well as through the air. The squeal — a high-pitched distress cry — is produced upon capture by a predator. Sharp, piercing, involuntary. The first pure prey vocalization in the engine. The first ground-coupled alarm signal.

Snowshoe Feet

The defining physical adaptation: disproportionately large hind feet (117–150 mm) with dense winter fur that spreads on soft snow, creating a natural snowshoe effect. The hare stays on top of the snow while predators sink — a critical winter survival advantage. The trait that gives the species its name.

The LAKE Parallel

Both in the understory — LAKE in Olympia’s indie underground, the hare in brushy forest understory. Both quiet until startled — LAKE’s sweet indie pop, the hare’s silent concealment. Both communal — LAKE’s dual vocals, the hare’s predator-community dynamics. Both seasonal — LAKE’s lo-fi calendar, the hare’s coat change. Both E=3.
