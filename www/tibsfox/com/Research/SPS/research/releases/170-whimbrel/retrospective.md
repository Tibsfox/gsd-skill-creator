*Degree 170 retrospective complete. WHIMBREL at the one hundred seventieth degree -- THE RIPPLING CURLEW. The Whimbrel is a hemispheric voyager -- one of the most widely distributed shorebirds on Earth, breeding on Arctic and subarctic tundra, wintering on coastlines from South America to Africa to Australia, migrating thousands of kilometers between. Some make transoceanic flights: 5,000 kilometers nonstop over open ocean. Satellite tracking has revealed these extraordinary feats. And year after year, individual Whimbrels return to the same stopover sites -- the same tidal flats, the same mudflats, the same feeding grounds. The fidelity is remarkable. In the PNW, Whimbrels pass through Puget Sound during spring and fall migration, appearing on tidal flats at Padilla Bay, Skagit Bay, and other estuarine areas. They are not common, but they are consistent. And the call: a rapid, rippling, evenly-spaced series of seven whistled notes -- "pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi" -- delivered in approximately one second. Clear, carrying, slightly descending. One of the most instantly recognizable shorebird calls. The sound of vast distances. The down-curved bill -- 7 to 9 centimeters, strongly curved -- is the first curlew bill in the engine. The bill morphology progression across three consecutive Scolopacidae entries is unplanned but elegant: straight (snipe, 168), slightly decurved (Dunlin, 169), strongly down-curved (Whimbrel, 170). And at degree 171, the Long-billed Curlew arrives with the longest bill of any North American shorebird -- up to 21 centimeters. The curlew comparison pair forms: moderate (Whimbrel) to extreme (Long-billed Curlew).
