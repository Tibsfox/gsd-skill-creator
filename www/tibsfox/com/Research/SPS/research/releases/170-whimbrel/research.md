### Bill Morphology Progression

The engine is building a bill morphology progression within Scolopacidae:

- **Straight (168):** Wilson's Snipe -- long, straight, flexible-tipped probing bill
- **Slightly decurved (169):** Dunlin -- short, slightly down-curved probing bill
- **Down-curved (170):** Whimbrel -- long, strongly down-curved curlew bill

The progression from straight to curved-down is not accidental. The bill shapes represent different feeding strategies -- straight bills probe straight down into soft mud; curved bills reach around and under objects (crabs, invertebrates in burrows).

## E=4 -- THE RIPPLING CALL

### Why E=4

The Whimbrel's rippling curlew call registers at E=4:

- **Volume:** The rippling call is clear and carrying -- audible at several hundred meters. It is a strong, whistled call designed for long-distance communication during migration and on breeding territory.
- **Distinctiveness:** The seven-note rippling pattern is one of the most instantly recognizable shorebird calls. The even spacing and clear tonality make it diagnostic. Even experienced birders can identify a Whimbrel by call alone at great distance.
- **Atmospheric quality:** The call has a wild, lonesome quality -- the sound of vast distances, Arctic tundra, and open coastline. The rippling pattern evokes the wide spaces through which the Whimbrel travels.
- **Why not E=5?** The call, while carrying and distinctive, is a single brief utterance (approximately 1 second). It does not have the sustained alarm intensity of the Killdeer (E=5) or the sheer volume of the Long-billed Curlew's rising "curleee!" (E=5). E=4 reflects a strong, clear, diagnostic call.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## THE INCREDIBLE MIGRATION

### One of Earth's Great Travelers

The Whimbrel is one of the most widely distributed and long-distance migrant shorebirds:

- **Global distribution:** The Whimbrel breeds across the Arctic and subarctic of both the Old World and New World. It winters on coastlines from the southern US to South America, Africa, Southeast Asia, and Australia. It is found on every continent except Antarctica.
- **Migration distances:** Individual Whimbrels may fly 4,000-8,000+ km between breeding and wintering grounds. Some populations make transoceanic flights -- crossing the Atlantic or Pacific nonstop.
- **Satellite tracking:** Satellite-tagged Whimbrels have revealed extraordinary migration feats. One tracked bird flew nonstop from the Virginia coast to the mouth of the Amazon -- approximately 5,000 km over open ocean -- in approximately 6 days.
- **Puget Sound stopover:** Whimbrels pass through Puget Sound during spring (April-May) and fall (July-September) migration. They are not common breeders or winterers in the PNW but are regular migrants on tidal flats.
- **Fidelity:** Whimbrels show remarkable site fidelity -- returning to the same stopover locations year after year. The tidal flats they visit in the PNW are consistent across decades.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; satellite tracking studies; shorebird migration research; engine internal analysis)

## THE DOWN-CURVED BILL

### Curlew Morphology

The Whimbrel's bill is the defining physical feature:

- **Shape:** Long (7-9 cm), strongly down-curved. The curvature is gradual, following a smooth arc. The bill curves most strongly in the distal (tip) half.
- **Function:** The down-curved bill is adapted for reaching into crevices, around objects, and into the burrows of crabs and other invertebrates. Unlike the straight bill of the snipe (which probes vertically into soft mud), the curlew bill reaches laterally and around obstacles.
- **Prey:** Fiddler crabs and other small crabs are a primary food source. The curved bill can reach into crab burrows. Also takes marine worms, small mollusks, insects, and berries.
- **Comparison pair setup:** The Whimbrel's bill (7-9 cm, down-curved) will be directly compared to the Long-billed Curlew's bill (up to 21 cm, extremely down-curved) at degree 171. The Whimbrel is the moderate curlew; the Long-billed Curlew is the extreme.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; shorebird morphology studies; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Whimbrel

The Whimbrel's acoustic profile centers on the diagnostic rippling call:

**Rippling Curlew Call:**
- **Trigger:** Flight call (migration, alarm, territory). Given frequently in flight.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** A rapid, rippling, evenly-spaced series of 7 whistled notes: "pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi." Clear, descending slightly, approximately 1 second total. Even spacing is diagnostic.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 1500-2500 Hz. Clear whistled tone.
- **Duration:** Approximately 1 second per call. 7 notes.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Carrying, clear, diagnostic. Audible several hundred meters.
- **Distinctiveness:** One of the most instantly recognizable shorebird calls. The even spacing and 7-note pattern are diagnostic.

**Other Calls:**
- **Alarm:** A more drawn-out version of the rippling call.
- **Display song:** On breeding grounds, a long, bubbling, trilling flight display song.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)
