Second Consecutive Cricetidae (83–84)

The Bushy-tailed Woodrat is the second consecutive Cricetidae after the Deer Mouse (83). A Cricetidae streak begins: 83–84. After the Sciuridae octet (75–82), will Cricetidae build its own run? Two entries and counting. The pack rat follows the deer mouse — from the invisible abundant to the industrious builder.

The Midden Builder — Thousands of Years Old

The Bushy-tailed Woodrat is the “pack rat” — famous for collecting objects and building elaborate stick nests (middens). Some middens are thousands of years old and are used by paleontologists to reconstruct ancient environments from preserved plant material, pollen, and seeds found crystallized in woodrat urine (“amberat”). The middens are time capsules — ancient archives of PNW ecology, deposited one twig at a time.

Rocky Outcrop Habitat — Returns to Rocky Terrain

The Bushy-tailed Woodrat inhabits rocky outcrops — cliff faces, talus slopes, boulder fields, rock crevices. This returns the engine to rocky terrain after the Deer Mouse’s varied habitat (83). Rocky outcrop shared with Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (degree 79). Two rocky-outcrop species in six degrees.

The Damien Jurado Late Parallel

Degree 84 pairs Bushy-tailed Woodrat with Damien Jurado (late career) in Part A — Folk, E=2. Both are builders over time: Jurado builds a career over 25 years of songwriting. The woodrat builds middens over thousands of years. Both accumulate — Jurado accumulates songs, the woodrat accumulates sticks and objects. Both are quiet: Jurado at E=2, the woodrat at E=3. Both work in solitude.

The Sound: Chatter + Twig Gnaw (85th Acoustic Element)

The call: The Bushy-tailed Woodrat produces two primary sound categories: (1) chatter — a rapid series of tooth-clacking sounds used as a warning or territorial signal, teeth clacking together rapidly; (2) twig gnaw sounds — the functional sounds of gnawing and manipulating sticks during midden construction. The gnaw sounds are not vocalizations but construction noise — the sound of the animal at work [1, 2, 3].

Why E=3: (1) Chatter is moderately loud — audible at close range, a distinctly mechanical sound. (2) Twig gnaw is audible in quiet environments — the scraping and snapping of wood. (3) Nocturnal context but not ultrasonic — clearly audible to human observers when nearby. (4) E=3 reflects moderate functional sound activity: neither silent nor loud. Comparable to Beaver chewing (degree 62, E=4) but softer and smaller in scale.

Taxonomy: Neotoma cinerea

Etymology: Neotoma from Greek neos (“new”) + toma (“cutting”), referring to the sharp cutting teeth. cinerea from Latin “ash-colored,” describing the gray pelage. Described by Ord (1815) [1, 2].

Classification: Order Rodentia. Family Cricetidae — SECOND consecutive (83–84). Genus Neotoma — NEW GENUS. Woodrats (pack rats) [1, 2].

Ecology: The Pack Rat

Habitat: Rocky outcrops, cliff faces, talus slopes, boulder fields, rock crevices. Uses rock crevices and overhangs for midden placement. Returns to rocky terrain. Shared with Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (79) [1, 2, 3].

Activity: Nocturnal. Active at night collecting materials for midden construction and foraging.

Middens: The defining behavior. Elaborate stick nests constructed from twigs, bark, leaves, bones, and collected objects. Some middens are thousands of years old. Paleontological treasure troves. Preserved plant material and crystallized urine (“amberat”) provide windows into ancient environments.

Diet: Herbivorous. Leaves, shoots, fruits, seeds, fungi, bark. Caches food in and around the midden.

Predators: American Marten (SPS-065) is a key predator. Also owls, weasels, coyotes, bobcats.

SPS-083: Deer Mouse

SPS-062: Beaver

Gnaw parallel. Both produce functional gnaw sounds — Beaver chewing trees (62, E=4) and Woodrat gnawing twigs (84, E=3). Both rodent infrastructure builders. Beaver builds dams; Woodrat builds middens. Different scales, same principle.

SPS-061: Beaver

Rodent infrastructure builder. Both Castoridae (Beaver) and Cricetidae (Woodrat) build structures that modify habitat. Beaver dams create ponds. Woodrat middens preserve ecology. Both accumulate materials over years.
