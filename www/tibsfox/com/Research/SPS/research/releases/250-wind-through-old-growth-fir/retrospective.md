## What Worked

1. **The 249-250 structural acoustic contrast is the entry's most important contribution.** Circular needles vs. flat leaves. Stiff branches vs. branch-mass sweep. These are not minor variations -- they produce genuinely different acoustic experiences. The engine is right to document both.
2. **Old-growth loss framing is necessary.** The sound documented here can only be heard in ~10-15% of its original range. Documenting it without acknowledging this is incomplete. The loss is acoustic as well as ecological.
3. **R. Murray Schafer's keynote sound concept provides the best framework.** Pre-logging, old-growth Douglas fir wind was the omnipresent background sound of the Pacific Northwest forest -- the sound you stopped hearing because it was always there. Its absence in 85-90% of its range is a silence we have learned to mistake for normal.
4. **The round degree 250 holding multi-harmonic resonance has a pleasing aptness.** Completeness at the midpoint.

## Discoveries

1. **The vertical dimension of old-growth canopy is an acoustic design.** Wind velocity varies with height; different canopy layers produce different aeolian frequencies simultaneously. Old-growth is inherently multi-harmonic because of its vertical extent. This is not a discovered property -- it is a consequence of basic physics applied to tall trees.
2. **H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest may be the best-documented acoustic ecology site in the PNW.** Long-term ecological research, including acoustic monitoring. Degree 250's geographic anchor.
