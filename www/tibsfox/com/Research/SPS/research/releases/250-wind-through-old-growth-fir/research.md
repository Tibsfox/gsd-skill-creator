## DOUGLAS FIR VS. SITKA SPRUCE -- WHY THEY SOUND DIFFERENT

### The Structural Basis of Acoustic Difference

The contrast between degrees 249 and 250 is not arbitrary:

- **Leaf structure: needles vs. scale leaves.** Sitka spruce (degree 249) has true needles: sharp-pointed, stiff, individually very small. Douglas fir has flat, slightly soft scale-like leaves (technically needles but structurally different from spruce needles): approximately 2-3 cm long, flat in cross-section, with a groove along the upper surface. The flat cross-section changes the aerodynamics: a flat leaf in wind creates a different vortex pattern than a circular needle, producing different aeolian tone frequencies and a broader-spectrum sound.
- **Leaf arrangement.** Douglas fir leaves are arranged in two roughly parallel rows along the branch (a comb-like arrangement called pectinate), creating a flat fan of leaf surface on each side of the branch. Sitka spruce needles radiate in a spiral around the branch, creating a cylindrical surface. These different arrangements create different wind flow patterns through the canopy.
- **Branch architecture.** Douglas fir branches are generally heavier and longer than Sitka spruce branches of equivalent trunk diameter. The large branch mass means that wind energy drives more branch motion in Douglas fir than in Sitka spruce -- the branches themselves sweep and move, creating a low-frequency "whooshing" sound from the branch mass moving through air in addition to the leaf vibration sounds.
- **Old-growth canopy height.** Old-growth Douglas fir in the Pacific Northwest reaches 70-90 meters (230-300 feet) in the tallest specimens. The canopy at 70-90 meters is above the turbulent boundary layer near the ground; the winds that move through old-growth fir canopy are different in character from the winds at forest edge or in smaller trees. The canopy height creates a sound production zone that is acoustically isolated from ground-level noise.
- **Why E=5 vs. E=4.** The Douglas fir wind is slightly more energetic than the Sitka spruce wind (E=5 vs. E=4) because the branch mass movement adds a lower-frequency, higher-amplitude component to the sound profile that the needle-only Sitka spruce lacks. The Douglas fir canopy moves with the storm in a more substantial way; the sound produced includes both the high-frequency leaf vibrations and the lower-frequency branch sweep.

(Sources: Douglas fir leaf structure; flat vs. circular cross-section aerodynamics; pectinate vs. spiral arrangement; branch architecture comparison; old-growth canopy height 70-90m; boundary layer wind characteristics; E=5 vs E=4 placement rationale)

## OLD-GROWTH DOUGLAS FIR -- THE SPECIES

### *Pseudotsuga menziesii* and the PNW's Defining Tree

Douglas fir is the dominant tree of the Pacific Northwest in every important sense:

- **The most common PNW timber tree.** Douglas fir covers more acres of productive forest land in Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia than any other tree species. It is the species that defined the PNW timber industry, the species whose harvesting transformed the region's ecology in the 20th century, and the species whose old-growth stands represent the most complex and biologically rich terrestrial ecosystems in North America.
- **Dimensions.** Old-growth Douglas fir reaches the largest dimensions of any tree in the world after the giant sequoia and coast redwood. The tallest recorded living Douglas fir (the "Brummit Fir" in the Coos Bay Watershed, Oregon) is approximately 100 meters (328 feet). Trunks in old-growth stands typically reach 2-4 meters in diameter; the largest specimens exceed 5 meters. The age of old-growth Douglas fir commonly reaches 500-1,000 years; the oldest verified specimens exceed 1,400 years.
- **Not a true fir.** Despite the name "Douglas fir," *Pseudotsuga menziesii* is not a true fir (genus *Abies*). Its taxonomy reflects this: *Pseudotsuga* means "false hemlock" -- the tree was initially classified with hemlocks. The unique cone structure (with distinctive three-pointed bracts extending from between the cone scales -- folk name "mouse tails") distinguishes Douglas fir from all other conifers.
- **The old-growth ecosystem.** Old-growth Douglas fir forests are among the most complex terrestrial ecosystems in the world:
  - **Canopy height variation.** Old-growth stands have highly irregular canopy heights, from large emergent trees at 70-100 meters to smaller sub-canopy trees at 30-50 meters, with abundant dead standing snags and fallen logs at all stages of decomposition. This structural complexity creates the acoustic variety that defines old-growth wind sound.
  - **The nurse log system.** Fallen Douglas fir logs -- some 2-3 meters in diameter and 30+ meters long -- decompose over centuries, providing seedbed for new trees, habitat for amphibians and invertebrates, and structural complexity at the forest floor level.
  - **Mycorrhizal networks.** Old-growth Douglas fir is extensively connected through mycorrhizal networks -- fungal threads that connect tree roots and facilitate nutrient sharing between trees. The largest Douglas fir in an old-growth stand are often "hub trees" (colloquially called "mother trees") in these networks, receiving carbon from smaller trees and distributing nitrogen and other nutrients.

(Sources: Douglas fir as dominant PNW timber species; old-growth dimensions; Brummit Fir 100m record; trunk diameters 2-5m; age 500-1400 years; Pseudotsuga menziesii taxonomy; "false hemlock" naming; three-pointed bracts; old-growth canopy structure; nurse logs; mycorrhizal networks; hub/mother tree concept)

## THE ACOUSTIC PHYSICS OF OLD-GROWTH FIR WIND

### Multi-Harmonic Resonance in Detail

The "multi-harmonic resonance" descriptor for Douglas fir wind reflects specific acoustic mechanisms:

- **Harmonic series.** A complex sound is "multi-harmonic" when it contains energy at multiple frequencies that are related by integer ratios to a fundamental frequency -- the structure of a musical chord or an organ tone. Old-growth Douglas fir wind sound contains this harmonic structure because the various elements of the canopy (needles, twigs, small branches, large branches) each resonate at different fundamental frequencies, and these frequencies naturally form harmonic relationships based on the physics of vibrating systems.
- **The branch sweep contribution.** As noted above, large Douglas fir branches sweep through arcs as the storm wind drives them. A 10-meter branch sweeping 2-3 meters in a gust displaces a significant volume of air; the sound of this displacement is a broad, low-frequency "whoosh" that contributes the bass register to the old-growth fir wind sound. This is absent from the Sitka spruce sound, where branches are stiffer and shorter.
- **Turbulence in the canopy.** Old-growth canopy is architecturally complex -- irregular heights, gaps, dense patches, snags, variations in leaf density. Wind moving through this complex structure creates turbulence patterns that generate sound across a broader frequency range than wind through a uniformly dense canopy. The irregularity of old-growth is an acoustic asset: it creates a richer, more varied sound than plantation forest.
- **The vertical dimension.** Old-growth fir canopy extends from 30 meters to 90+ meters above the ground. The wind speed varies with height (higher at the canopy top, lower at the sub-canopy level), meaning different canopy layers produce different aeolian frequencies simultaneously. The vertical acoustic profile of old-growth wind sound is inherently multi-harmonic because the instrument (the forest) has vertical extent that creates simultaneous sound production at multiple height levels.
- **The Karman vortex for flat needles.** For Douglas fir's flat needles, the Karman vortex dynamics are different from circular needles. The flat shape creates a more complex vortex pattern -- rather than clean alternating vortices, flat objects produce a broader, less distinct frequency peak with more harmonic content. This is why Douglas fir wind sounds richer and less pure than Sitka spruce wind.

(Sources: Harmonic series definition; multi-harmonic origin in Douglas fir structure; branch sweep and air displacement; turbulence in irregular canopy vs. plantation; vertical wind gradient in tall old-growth; Karman vortex for flat vs. circular cross-section)

## WHERE THIS SOUND EXISTS -- THE OLD-GROWTH GEOGRAPHIC CONTEXT

### The Remaining Old-Growth

Old-growth Douglas fir is now rare -- its acoustic environment exists only where old-growth survives:

- **The scale of loss.** Old-growth Douglas fir historically covered approximately 8-10 million acres in Washington State alone. Today, approximately 10-15% of the original old-growth remains. Most of the remaining old-growth is in protected federal lands: national parks, wilderness areas, and some national forests.
- **Key locations for old-growth fir wind sound:**
  - **Olympic National Park (interior forests).** The interior valleys of the Olympics (upper Hoh, Elwha, Dungeness) have old-growth Douglas fir at higher elevations where Sitka spruce gives way to fir. These are the quintessential old-growth fir wind locations.
  - **Mount Rainier National Park.** The old-growth Douglas fir stands in the park's lower valleys (Carbon River, Ohanapecosh) contain some of the most accessible and visually dramatic old-growth in the region.
  - **Gifford Pinchot National Forest.** The Indian Heaven and Trapper Creek Wilderness areas within the Gifford Pinchot contain old-growth Douglas fir stands on both sides of the Cascades.
  - **H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest (Oregon).** One of the most studied old-growth ecosystems in the world. Long-term ecological research site. The acoustic environment here has been scientifically documented.
  - **Ancient Forests of the Coast Range.** Fragments in the coast range forests of southwest Washington and northwest Oregon.
- **The Cathedral quality.** Old-growth Douglas fir forests have a specific spatial quality that visitors consistently describe as "cathedral-like" -- the tall, straight trunks forming columns, the canopy high above forming the vault, the filtered light and reduced ground-level wind. This spatial quality shapes the acoustic environment: sounds reverberate off the large trunks; the forest acts as a natural reverb chamber.

(Sources: Old-growth acreage loss; remaining old-growth percentage; Olympic National Park interior; Mount Rainier Carbon River old-growth; Gifford Pinchot; H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest; coast range fragments; cathedral spatial quality; reverberation from large trunks)

## CULTURAL AND ECOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE

### The Sound of the Permanent Landscape

- **Indigenous relationship.** For the coastal and interior Salish, Chinook, and other Pacific Northwest peoples, the old-growth Douglas fir forest is not a resource -- it is the landscape itself. The sound of wind in these forests has been a constant of indigenous experience for thousands of years. The forest's sounds are part of the cultural geography that informs place names, oral traditions, and spiritual practices.
- **The timber era's transformation.** The logging of Pacific Northwest old-growth -- the most intensive forest harvest in human history per unit area, between approximately 1880 and 1990 -- transformed the acoustic landscape of the region as thoroughly as any event since the last glaciation. The sounds documented at degree 250 can now only be heard where old-growth survives. They have been eliminated from 85-90% of their original range.
- **The conservation movement and the sound.** The old-growth protection battles of the 1980s-1990s (the spotted owl controversy, the Northwest Forest Plan of 1994) were partly motivated by people who valued old-growth forests for non-timber reasons -- including the experiential qualities of old-growth, which include sound. The specific acoustic environment of old-growth -- the way sound moves through a room-sized irregular canopy -- is part of what makes old-growth worth protecting.
- **Acoustic ecology.** The field of acoustic ecology (founded by R. Murray Schafer; the World Soundscape Project) specifically studies the sonic environment of natural landscapes. Old-growth Douglas fir wind is one of the acoustic ecology canon's most studied sounds -- a "keynote sound" in Schafer's terminology: a sound that characterizes a particular place and time, forming the background against which other sounds are heard.

(Sources: Indigenous landscape relationship to old-growth; logging transformation of PNW acoustic environment; 1880-1990 old-growth harvest; Northwest Forest Plan 1994; old-growth protection movement; acoustic ecology field; R. Murray Schafer; keynote sound concept)
