OLD-GROWTH DOUGLAS FIR — 200-FOOT CANOPY — MULTI-HARMONIC RESONANCE

Pseudotsuga menziesii. Douglas fir. The PNW's defining tree. In storm wind, the 70-90 meter canopy of old-growth Douglas fir produces multi-harmonic resonance — richer, more complex, and more energetic than the Sitka spruce moan (degree 249). Flat scale-leaves create different vortex patterns than spruce needles. Large branch masses sweep through arcs, adding low-frequency bass to the leaf-vibration frequencies. Vertical canopy extent creates simultaneous sound production at multiple height levels. Old-growth is now rare; the sound exists only where these ancient stands survive.

249 vs. 250 -- Why They Sound Different

Degree 249: Sitka Spruce

Sharp, circular-cross-section needles. Spiral arrangement. Stiff branches. Karman vortex produces relatively pure aeolian tones. Deep moan. E=4. Coastal storm. Olympic coast.

Degree 250: Douglas Fir

Flat scale-leaves. Pectinate (comb-like) arrangement. Heavy branch masses that sweep in wind. Broader-spectrum, richer harmonic content. Multi-harmonic resonance. E=5. Old-growth interior.

The acoustic difference between Sitka spruce and Douglas fir wind is structural. Flat needles create broader vortex patterns than circular ones. Large branch masses add low-frequency sweep to the high-frequency leaf vibrations. The irregular canopy architecture of old-growth — gaps, snags, varying heights — creates turbulence that broadens the acoustic spectrum. The forest as instrument is richer for its irregularity.

"Old-growth Douglas fir forests are cathedral-like: straight trunks forming columns, canopy high above forming the vault. The forest acts as a natural reverb chamber. Wind sound here is not diminished by distance — it is enriched by it."

The Species -- *Pseudotsuga menziesii*

Height records

Tallest living specimen ~100 m (328 ft). Old-growth stands: typical 70-90 m. The most towering forest structure in the continental US after sequoia and redwood.

Trunk diameter

Old-growth: typically 2-4 m. Largest specimens exceed 5 m. Age 500-1,400+ years. A 1,000-year-old fir was growing when the Crusades began.

Not a true fir

Pseudotsuga = "false hemlock." The three-pointed bracts ("mouse tails") extending from cone scales distinguish it from all other conifers. Unique taxonomically.

Canopy architecture

Old-growth: highly irregular heights, large snags, fallen logs. Turbulence in irregular canopy creates richer acoustic spectrum than uniform plantation forest.

Where the Sound Exists Now

85-90% of Pacific Northwest old-growth Douglas fir has been logged. The sound documented at degree 250 can now only be heard where old-growth survives: Olympic National Park interior, Mount Rainier National Park, Gifford Pinchot Wilderness areas, H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest (Oregon). The acoustic landscape of the pre-logging PNW — vast old-growth wind resonance across millions of acres — is gone from most of its original range.

Acoustic Ecology -- Keynote Sounds

The field of acoustic ecology (R. Murray Schafer; the World Soundscape Project) describes old-growth Douglas fir wind as a "keynote sound" — a sound that characterizes a particular place and time, forming the background against which other sounds are heard. In the Pacific Northwest before logging, the wind in old-growth fir would have been the omnipresent background sound of the forest — as constant and unremarkable to indigenous peoples as the sound of traffic is to a modern city resident. The loss of 85-90% of old-growth is simultaneously an acoustic loss: the keynote of the original PNW has been nearly silenced.

Engine Position

Degree 250 is the second consecutive wind entry and the second entry in the meteorological arc (249-252). E=5 exceeds the Sitka spruce moan (E=4) due to the branch mass contribution to the sound. The round degree number at 250 holds the sound defined by harmonic complexity: multi-harmonic resonance is the acoustic equivalent of a chord — complete, containing multiple frequencies simultaneously, richer than any single tone.
