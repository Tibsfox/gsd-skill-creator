The Purest Tone in Nature — Crystalline Sustained Trill

The snowy tree cricket produces one of the purest tones in the natural world [1, 2]. A sustained, crystalline trill — a single frequency delivered with remarkable clarity and consistency. No harmonics, no buzz, no rasp — a clean, clear, sustained pitch that rings through the night like a tuning fork made of biology. E=3. The pure tone is moderate in volume but extraordinary in clarity. One cricket is gentle; many create a shimmering curtain of pure sound.

The Thermometer Cricket — Dolbear’s Law

The snowy tree cricket is called the “thermometer cricket” because its chirp rate correlates precisely with ambient temperature [1, 2, 3]. Dolbear’s Law (1897): count the number of chirps in 13 seconds, add 40, and you have the temperature in Fahrenheit. This is not folklore — it is verified biophysics. The cricket’s metabolic rate, which drives chirp rate, is directly temperature-dependent. The cricket IS a thermometer.

The Sound: Pure Sustained Trill Thermometer (98th Acoustic Element, species side)

The call: The snowy tree cricket (Oecanthus fultoni) produces a pure, sustained trill by rubbing its wings together (stridulation) [1, 2]. Unlike the field cricket’s chirp (a series of discrete pulses), the tree cricket’s trill is continuous and tonal — a sustained pitch with extraordinary purity. The frequency is approximately 2-4 kHz, and the tone is remarkably free of harmonics.

Why E=3: The tree cricket’s call is gentle but persistent. Individual calls are quieter than field crickets (87, E=4). The pure tone carries well but does not dominate the soundscape the way a dense cricket chorus does. E=3 reflects the crystalline clarity at moderate amplitude — pure but not loud. Higher than the stonefly (94, E=2) for sustained audibility. Lower than the cricket chorus (87, E=4) for overall volume.

Gryllidae at degree 87: The field cricket chorus — dense rhythmic chirping from multiple species. Percussive, pulsing, E=4. Dry meadow habitat.

Gryllidae at degree 98: The snowy tree cricket song — pure sustained trill from a single species. Tonal, crystalline, E=3. Shrub habitat.

Same family, different sound world. The engine demonstrates taxonomic depth within a single family.

Ecology: Dolbear’s Law and the Shrub Habitat

Habitat: Shrub. Tree crickets live on shrubs, low trees, and herbaceous plants [1, 2]. They call from vegetation, not from the ground like field crickets. The elevated position helps the pure tone carry through the night air.

Dolbear’s Law: In 1897, physicist Amos Dolbear published the relationship between cricket chirp rate and temperature [3]. For the snowy tree cricket: T(°F) = 40 + (chirps in 13 seconds). The relationship is linear and reliable because the cricket’s metabolic rate — which drives wing-rubbing speed — is directly proportional to ambient temperature. Warmer = faster chirps. Cooler = slower chirps.

The purest tone: The snowy tree cricket’s wing morphology produces an unusually pure tone — the file-and-scraper mechanism generates a nearly sinusoidal waveform with minimal harmonic content [1]. This is rare in stridulating insects, most of which produce complex, harmonic-rich sounds. The tree cricket is an acoustic outlier.
