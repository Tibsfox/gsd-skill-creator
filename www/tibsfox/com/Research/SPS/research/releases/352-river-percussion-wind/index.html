The Three Independent Layers

River Falls Roar

Broadband: 10 Hz to several kHz. Turbulent breakup during fall + chaotic pool impact = full-spectrum noise. At flood: massive sheet flow over basalt sill, gorge reflection creates spatial immersion. Driving mechanism: hydraulic gravity.

Geological Percussion

Boulder transport (30–100+ cm, 20–200 Hz, felt before heard). Turbulence rhythm: irregular, non-periodic, hydraulics-scaled not biologically-scaled. Woody debris impacts: large logs hitting basalt walls. Driving mechanism: turbulence.

Gorge Wind

Falls-driven airflow (falling water drags air downward, downdraft flows outward along gorge). Thermal channeling (gorge temperature differential). Resonance cavity: 20–80 Hz sub-bass, felt as body vibration. Driving mechanism: thermal + hydraulic.

The Geological Timescale

Human rhythmic perception expects period and pulse. The turbulence of flood flow has neither. Boulder transport is organized by hydraulic geometry at the scale of the river system — not at the scale of any biological rhythm. The falls roar is continuous and broadband, not pulsed. The gorge wind is thermally and hydraulically driven, not meteorologically periodic.

The three layers are operating on three different driving mechanisms (hydraulic gravity, turbulence, thermal convection) without synchronization. They do not resolve to a common rhythmic ground. The acoustic event continues at its own scale regardless of what biological activity surrounds it. This is geological impersonality: not responsive, not adaptive, simply operating at the scale of water and stone.

“The falls do not modulate their output for the listener. The acoustic event is not a performance. It is a geological process.”

Acoustic Profile

10 Hz – several kHz Falls broadband. Full spectrum from infrasound through low frequency. Flood stage: maximum total acoustic energy for PNW lowland waterfall.
