## SNOQUALMIE FALLS -- THE SITE

### 268 Feet of Basalt. The Snoqualmie River. King County.

Snoqualmie Falls is a 268-foot waterfall on the Snoqualmie River in King County, Washington, approximately 25 miles east of Seattle. The falls drop over a sill of volcanic basalt -- the same Columbia River Basalt formation that underlies much of the Pacific Northwest -- into a plunge pool below. The gorge geometry below the falls concentrates and amplifies the acoustic output of the falling water.

At normal river flows, Snoqualmie Falls is a major regional acoustic landmark: the roar of the falls is audible from the observation deck at the top and from the trail at the bottom of the gorge, approximately 0.5 miles of maintained separation. The acoustic experience at the bottom of the gorge is immersive: the falls fill the acoustic space from multiple angles because the gorge walls reflect the sound inward.

At flood stage, the acoustic character of Snoqualmie Falls changes fundamentally. The Snoqualmie River drains approximately 1,700 square miles of Cascade Range watershed. Spring snowmelt events routinely bring the river to 2-4 times normal flow. Major flood events (1-in-10-year or larger) can bring the river to 6-8 times normal flow. At these flows, the falls no longer present a single coherent water column dropping over the basalt sill -- the entire sill is submerged in the flood current, and the water moves across and over the basalt as a massive sheet. The acoustic output increases dramatically.

(Sources: Snoqualmie Falls 268-foot basalt drop; Columbia River Basalt formation; King County Washington location; 25 miles east Seattle; plunge pool gorge geometry; acoustic audibility 0.5 miles normal flow; Snoqualmie River watershed 1,700 square miles; spring snowmelt 2-4x normal flow; major flood 6-8x; sill submersion at flood stage; acoustic character change at flood)

## LAYER 1: RIVER FALLS ROAR

### Flood Stage. Maximum Flow. The Acoustic Maximum of the Snoqualmie.

The primary acoustic event at Snoqualmie Falls at flood stage is the falls themselves. The acoustic profile of a major waterfall is well-characterized:

**Broadband noise.** Water falling 268 feet and impacting the plunge pool at the base produces a broadband acoustic event with significant energy across the frequency range from approximately 10 Hz to several kHz. The broadband character of waterfall noise is produced by the turbulent breakup of the water mass during the fall (creating droplets of varying sizes, each producing acoustic energy at different frequencies) and by the chaotic impact of the falling water with the pool surface below.

**Flood-stage intensification.** At flood stage, the flow volume increase changes the acoustic character: the broadband noise maintains its character but increases significantly in total energy (measured in dB). The turbulent mixing of the increased flow is more powerful, the impact with the pool is more energetic, and the gorge wind effects (see Layer 3) are enhanced by the additional vapor and airflow generated by the increased falling water.

**The gorge acoustic geometry.** The walls of the Snoqualmie gorge below the falls are approximately 100 feet apart at the base of the plunge pool, widening to the broader valley above and below. The acoustic geometry of the gorge creates reflections that give the falls' broadband roar a quality of spatial immersion: the sound arrives from multiple directions simultaneously, as the reflected waves from the gorge walls interact with the direct sound from the falls.

(Sources: Snoqualmie Falls waterfall acoustic profile; broadband noise generation turbulent breakup; 10 Hz to several kHz frequency range; flood stage volume increase dB intensification; plunge pool impact energetics at flood; gorge wall acoustic reflection; spatial immersion 100-foot gorge geometry; flood-stage vapor and airflow enhancement)

## LAYER 2: GEOLOGICAL PERCUSSION

### Boulders and Debris in Flood Flow

At flood stage, the Snoqualmie River transports not just water but geological material: boulders, cobbles, gravel, and woody debris from the upper watershed. This transported material creates the geological percussion layer -- the acoustic event of large rocks and debris moving against the riverbed, canyon walls, and each other in the flood current.

**Boulder transport acoustics.** The transport of large boulders (30-100+ cm diameter) along the riverbed during flood events produces acoustic events that are felt as much as heard at close range: deep, low-frequency impacts as boulders roll and collide. The frequency content of boulder-against-boulder or boulder-against-bedrock impacts is primarily in the low range (20-200 Hz), with higher-frequency components from smaller particles.

**Percussion rhythms at geological timescale.** The acoustic rhythm of geological percussion is not aligned with human temporal perception. Boulders do not roll on a regular beat; they move with the fluid dynamics of the flood current, which is complex and non-periodic. The "rhythm" of geological percussion is the rhythm of turbulence -- irregular, energetic, organized at the scale of the hydraulic geometry of the river rather than any biological rhythm. This is the acoustic event that the geological timescale metaphor is documenting: the river's rhythm is geologically-scaled, not biologically-scaled.

**Debris impacts.** Woody debris (logs, large branches) transported by the flood current impacts the gorge walls and the canyon bedrock at irregular intervals, producing loud, brief percussive events that are distinct from the continuous broadband roar of the falls. A large log impacting a basalt canyon wall produces an event of significant amplitude and short duration -- a crack followed by splashing and the roar of the continuing flood.

(Sources: Snoqualmie River boulder transport flood; boulder diameter 30-100+ cm; impact frequency 20-200 Hz; turbulence rhythm vs biological rhythm; hydraulic geometry scale; woody debris transport flood current; log-against-basalt impact acoustic profile; geological percussion irregular non-periodic; turbulence-driven rhythm)

## LAYER 3: GORGE WIND

### Canyon Geometry. Channeled and Amplified.

The Snoqualmie gorge below the falls is a wind funnel. The specific geometry of the canyon -- narrow, deep, with the falls at the head creating a continuous source of cooled air and vapor -- produces a consistent wind pattern in the gorge that is independent of the regional wind conditions above. This gorge wind is driven by two mechanisms:

**Falls-driven airflow.** Large waterfalls generate their own local wind: the falling water mass drags air downward with it, creating a downdraft at the base of the falls that then flows outward along the gorge floor. At flood stage, with massively increased water volume, this falls-driven airflow is significantly stronger than at normal flow.

**Thermal channeling.** The gorge's depth creates a temperature differential between the gorge floor (cooled by the water spray and limited solar input) and the rim above (warmed by sun exposure). This thermal differential drives airflow up the gorge walls and creates a consistent pattern of wind in the canyon that is thermally driven rather than meteorologically driven.

**The gorge wind acoustic profile.** Wind through the Snoqualmie gorge at flood stage produces a sustained low-frequency roar that adds a bass acoustic layer beneath the broadband falls roar. The canyon walls amplify the wind's acoustic output by creating a resonance cavity at certain low frequencies. The specific frequency at which the gorge resonates depends on the gorge dimensions; in a gorge of the Snoqualmie's dimensions, the resonant frequencies are in the 20-80 Hz range -- the sub-bass that is felt as much as heard.

(Sources: Snoqualmie gorge wind funnel geometry; falls-driven airflow downdraft; flood stage airflow intensification; thermal channeling gorge depth temperature differential; gorge wind acoustic profile sustained low-frequency; canyon wall resonance cavity; 20-80 Hz resonant frequency sub-bass; independent of regional wind conditions)
