## The Four-Phase Acoustic Structure

The multi-slope avalanche sequence is the SPS engine's most structurally complex geophysical entry. Previous geophysical entries have documented single events (Cascades full storm, SPS 346; hurricane ridge wind, SPS 335). The multi-slope release event is different: not a single event with a single acoustic profile, but a sequence of four distinct phases repeating across multiple simultaneous or near-simultaneous release events. The structural complexity is the calibration basis.

## What Worked

1. **The four-phase acoustic decomposition is the correct analytical structure.** Fracture crack (infrasound impulse, state change moment), mass in motion (broadband roar, sub-bass seismic, sustained), runout impact (high-amplitude pulse, debris settling, and the silence after), alpine echo decay (bowl geometry memory, terrain map). Each phase has a distinct acoustic character. The four phases together constitute the complete avalanche acoustic event.
2. **The fracture crack as "sound of a state change" is the most analytically precise description.** The fracture crack is the moment when the snowpack's structural integrity fails irreversibly -- a tipping point made audible. The infrasound character of the crack (felt more than heard at close range) gives it a bodily quality distinct from the other phases.
3. **The alpine bowl geometry as acoustic architecture is a key insight.** The glacially carved cirques of the Cascades are not passive containers for the avalanche's acoustic output. They are active reflectors that create echo sequences, amplify certain frequencies, and produce the specific spatial acoustic signature of the alpine environment. The terrain is an acoustic instrument.
4. **The multi-slope overlap creates temporal complexity not present in a single-slope event.** When multiple releases are at different phases simultaneously (Phase 2 of one slide while another is in Phase 1, while a third is in Phase 4), the acoustic environment has a complexity that makes the multi-slope event categorically different from the single-slope event. This is the rarity that earns E=10.
5. **The TAD pairing as physical-force-as-wall is the correct structural parallel.** TAD's wall of sound (simultaneous low-end density from multiple sources) and the avalanche sequence's wall of geophysical force (simultaneous acoustic events from multiple slopes in multiple directions) are parallel structures. Both are about maximum physical force from multiple simultaneous sources.

## Discoveries

1. **The powder blast air pressure wave as acoustic forerunner.** Large, fast-moving dry avalanches develop an air pressure wave that precedes the visible mass by hundreds of meters. The acoustic component of this powder blast arrives at the observer before the main roar of the mass itself. The avalanche announces itself acoustically before it arrives physically.
2. **The "silence after the runout" as a distinct acoustic event.** The sudden cessation of the mass-in-motion roar when the avalanche stops is not a neutral absence of sound -- it is an acoustic event with its own character. The silence is total in a way that background environments are not; the massive broadband noise that the slide has been generating ceases abruptly, leaving the alpine bowl's ambient sounds (wind, distant water, echo decay) to fill the space. The contrast makes the silence loud.
3. **The seismic dimension of large avalanches.** The sub-bass ground vibration from major avalanches propagates as seismic energy and is recorded on regional seismographs. The avalanche is a seismic event as well as an acoustic one. The boundary between the acoustic and the geological dissolves at the E=10 scale.
