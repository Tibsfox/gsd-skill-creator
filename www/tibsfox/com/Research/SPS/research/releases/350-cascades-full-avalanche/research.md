## THE AVALANCHE SEQUENCE -- FOUR ACOUSTIC PHASES

### Phase 1: The Fracture Crack

The initial acoustic event in a major slab avalanche is the fracture crack: the moment when the weak layer within the snowpack fails and the overlying slab begins its release. The acoustic signature of a fracture crack is a low-frequency, short-duration explosive sound -- a deep boom or crack that is felt as much as heard at close range. The crack propagates along the fracture line at speeds of tens to hundreds of meters per second. A large fracture -- extending across multiple hundreds of meters of a single slope face -- produces a crack that is audible across several kilometers in still winter air.

In a multi-slope release event, multiple fracture cracks occur in sequence or near-simultaneously across different aspects of the alpine terrain. The cracks arrive from different directions, at different delays (depending on the triggering relationships between slopes), creating an acoustic staccato of fracture events that locates the listener inside a surrounding landscape of simultaneous geological failure.

The fracture crack is the acoustic signal of a tipping point: the moment when accumulated loading (snow, rain-on-snow, wind slab deposition) exceeds the shear strength of the weak layer. It is instantaneous and irreversible. The sound is the sound of a state change.

**Fracture crack acoustic parameters:** Frequency: predominantly below 50 Hz (infrasound and very low frequency). Duration: milliseconds to seconds per fracture event. Propagation velocity in snow: 100-500 m/s depending on snowpack density and fracture geometry.

### Phase 2: Mass in Motion

Once the slab releases, the mass-in-motion acoustic phase begins. A large avalanche in motion is one of the most acoustically powerful natural events in the Pacific Northwest alpine environment. The acoustic profile is complex:

**Surface roughness noise.** The slide mass (snow, ice, embedded rocks, entrained debris) moving against the slope surface creates a sustained broadband roar. The frequency content depends on the flow regime: dry, powder avalanches produce a higher-pitched continuous roar as snow particles and air turbulence dominate; wet, dense-flow avalanches produce a lower-pitched, more rhythmically irregular sound as chunks of packed snow impact the slope surface.

**Air pressure wave.** Large, fast-moving avalanches develop an air-pressure wave in front of the slide mass: a powder blast that can extend hundreds of meters ahead of the visible snow mass and travels at wind speeds of hundreds of kilometers per hour. The acoustic component of the powder blast is a wind-like roar that precedes the main mass by seconds.

**Sub-bass of mass movement.** The total mass of a large avalanche moving downslope generates a low-frequency ground vibration that propagates outward from the slide path as seismic energy. This sub-bass component is felt as vibration at the chest and feet at close range and measured as seismic events on regional seismographs.

In a multi-slope release event, multiple mass-in-motion acoustic events overlap: the roar of a slide on one aspect while another has just cracked, while a third is arriving at its runout zone. The acoustic environment is not a single avalanche; it is the simultaneous and sequential acoustic complexity of a landscape in motion.

(Sources: Avalanche mass-in-motion acoustic profile; dry powder vs wet dense-flow regimes; surface roughness noise broadband; powder blast air pressure wave; sub-bass ground vibration seismic; multi-slope overlap simultaneous sequential; Washington Cascades dry vs wet avalanche conditions; acoustic complexity landscape in motion)

### Phase 3: Runout Impact

The runout impact is the acoustic event when the moving avalanche mass decelerates and arrives at its stopping zone -- the valley floor, a terrain feature, a counter-slope, or simply the point where momentum is exhausted. The impact acoustic is distinct from the mass-in-motion roar:

**Impact pulse.** The front of a dense-flow avalanche arriving at a flat terrain feature produces a high-amplitude impact pulse: a deep, short-duration boom as the kinetic energy of the moving mass is transferred to the substrate. Large avalanches arriving at valley floors can produce impact pulses equivalent in seismic magnitude to small earthquakes.

**Debris distribution.** As the avalanche decelerates and the mass distributes across the runout zone, a complex acoustic event unfolds: chunks of ice and snow tumbling to rest, secondary collapses of unstable debris piles, and the crackling and settling sounds of the mass finding its resting geometry.

**The silence after the runout.** One of the most acoustically distinctive features of a major avalanche is the sudden near-silence that follows the runout: the roar of mass in motion ceases abruptly, leaving only the echo decay in the alpine bowl and the settling debris sounds. The silence is itself an acoustic event -- it marks the end of the geological action.

(Sources: Runout impact acoustic profile; impact pulse dense-flow arrival flat terrain; seismic magnitude comparison small earthquake; debris distribution secondary collapses crackling; silence after runout as acoustic event; echo decay alpine bowl; sudden cessation of roar)

### Phase 4: Alpine Echo and Decay

The alpine bowl and ridge geometry of the Cascades provides a complex acoustic environment for the decay of all three preceding phases. The fracture crack, the mass-in-motion roar, and the runout impact each generate echo sequences that are shaped by the terrain geometry:

**Bowl geometry.** Glacially carved cirques and avalanche bowls reflect and concentrate sound inward. A single fracture crack in a large cirque can produce multiple echo returns, each diminishing in amplitude, creating a slow decay from the initial impulse. The echo sequence is a geometric record of the terrain.

**Ridge reflection.** Alpine ridgelines reflect sound across valleys, creating distant echo sequences that arrive seconds after the original event. A major avalanche in the upper Cascades is audible many kilometers from the release zone; the echo sequence provides the listener's brain with a map of the intervening terrain.

**The multi-slope echo landscape.** In a full multi-slope release event, the echo sequences from multiple avalanches on multiple aspects overlap, creating an acoustic landscape in which echoes of multiple original events are simultaneously in decay. The alpine bowl is not quiet during this phase -- it is filled with the geological memory of the preceding events.

(Sources: Glacially carved cirques avalanche bowl geometry; bowl echo reflection concentration; multiple echo returns diminishing amplitude; ridge reflection across valleys; echo as terrain map; multi-slope echo landscape overlap; alpine geological memory acoustic; Cascades upper terrain)

## THE MULTI-SLOPE RELEASE -- WHY E=10

### The Rare Event, The Full Sequence

The full multi-slope release event earns E=10 because of:

**Rarity.** A single avalanche is a common winter event in the Cascades -- Washington Avalanche Center documents hundreds of natural releases per season. A multi-slope release event -- multiple avalanche releases across multiple aspects of alpine terrain, triggered in sequence or near-simultaneously -- is significantly rarer. The conditions that produce multi-slope release (widespread weak layer failure, simultaneous storm loading across aspects, or the sympathetic triggering of adjacent slopes by a large initial release) occur a handful of times per decade in any given area of the alpine Cascades.

**Scale.** Multi-slope release generates acoustic energy from multiple simultaneous or near-simultaneous sources across a large geographic area. The acoustic footprint of the event is larger than any single avalanche and is qualitatively different in character: the multi-directional origin of the acoustic events creates a surrounding, immersive acoustic environment rather than a directional one.

**Sequential complexity.** The four-phase sequence (fracture crack, mass-in-motion, runout impact, echo decay) of multiple releases overlapping creates a temporal acoustic structure of exceptional complexity: multiple events at different phases of their sequence occurring simultaneously, with echo sequences from earlier events still decaying as new events begin.

(Sources: Washington Avalanche Center natural releases per season; multi-slope release rarity conditions: widespread weak layer failure, simultaneous storm loading, sympathetic triggering; acoustic footprint multi-directional; four-phase sequential complexity overlap; E=10 rarity + scale + complexity calibration)
