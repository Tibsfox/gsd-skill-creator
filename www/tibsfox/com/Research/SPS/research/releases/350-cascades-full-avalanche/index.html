THE CASCADES GEOPHYSICAL MAXIMUM — MULTI-SLOPE RELEASE

Multiple avalanche events across multiple terrain aspects. Occurring in sequence or near-simultaneously during rare winter conditions. The full four-phase acoustic sequence: fracture crack → mass in motion → runout impact → alpine echo. Multiple releases at different phases simultaneously. The acoustic complexity of a landscape in geological motion. E=10.

Pairs with TAD Peak Intensity (S36 350, E=10) — sustained maximum physical force from multiple simultaneous sources. E=20.

The Four Phases

Phase 1
Fracture Crack
Infrasound impulse. Below 50 Hz. Felt as much as heard. Milliseconds. The sound of a state change.
Phase 2
Mass in Motion
Broadband roar + sub-bass seismic vibration. 30 sec to several minutes. Air pressure wave precedes mass.
Phase 3
Runout Impact
High-amplitude pulse at stopping zone. Debris settling. Then: sudden near-silence. The silence is an acoustic event.
Phase 4
Echo Decay
Alpine bowl geometry. Multiple echo returns. Ridge reflection across valleys. The terrain map in acoustic form.

The Acoustic Profile

Below 50 Hz Fracture crack infrasound. Felt as vibration at chest level before the audible components arrive. Geological-scale impulse.
Broadband roar Mass in motion: dry powder produces higher-pitched continuous roar; wet dense-flow produces lower-pitched irregular impacts.
Seismic Sub-bass ground vibration from major avalanches propagates as seismic energy. Recorded on regional seismographs. The geological scale.
Bowl echo Glacially carved cirques reflect and concentrate. Multiple echo returns per event, diminishing amplitude. The terrain made audible.

The Multi-Slope Event

A single avalanche is common in the Cascades — the Washington Avalanche Center documents hundreds per season. A multi-slope release event is rare: conditions that produce simultaneous or near-simultaneous releases across multiple aspects (widespread weak layer failure, simultaneous storm loading, or sympathetic triggering by a large initial release) occur a handful of times per decade per area.

The multi-slope event is qualitatively different from the single-slope event. Multiple releases at different phases simultaneously: Phase 2 of one slide while another is in Phase 1, while a third is in Phase 4. The acoustic environment is not a single avalanche but the simultaneous and sequential complexity of a landscape in motion.

“The silence after the runout is loud. The sudden cessation of the broadband roar leaves the alpine bowl’s ambient sounds to fill the space. The contrast makes the quiet extraordinary.”
