Hermit Warbler

Setophaga occidentalis
thin buzzy song from the conifer top — the thinning, 8th Parulidae (extending record), THIRD consecutive Setophaga, the displaced species — victim of Townsend’s competitive exclusion

The Displaced Species

Degree 39 documented the aggressor. Degree 40 documents the victim. Townsend’s Warbler is actively displacing the Hermit Warbler through hybridization and competitive exclusion. The range is contracting southward. Pure populations are shrinking. The genome is dissolving through introgression. The victim arrives one degree after its aggressor.

The 2,000 km genetic wake: Townsend’s alleles extend approximately 2,000 km south of the visible range front [5]. The Hermit Warbler’s genome is being colonized even where it still looks like a Hermit Warbler.

The 41st Acoustic Element: The Thinning

A thin, high, buzzy warbler song from the very tops of mountain conifers: seely seely seely see. After the canopy buzz’s ascending robustness (degree 39), the thinning adds attenuation within the buzz. Same Setophaga timbre family, but stretched thin — higher, thinner, less substantial.

Three meanings: (1) the thin quality of the song, (2) the thinning of the species’ geographic range, (3) the thinning of its genetic identity through introgression. One word for three kinds of diminishment. The first acoustic element with ecological meaning in its name.

The Western Moth-Eater

Setophaga occidentalis — “Western moth-eater.” Occidentalis = Latin “western,” a geographic descriptor. Fourth Setophaga naming convention: morphology (petechia), process (nigrescens), patronym (townsendi), geography (occidentalis). Formerly Dendroica occidentalis. The common name “Hermit” reflects its extreme canopy-top behavior — rarely seen, almost always heard from above. The name has acquired a second meaning: the species is retreating, withdrawing from its former range.

The Sound: Thin Buzzy Song

Seely seely seely see — 3–5 thin, high buzzy phrases at approximately 5,000–9,500 Hz [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Thinner and higher than Townsend’s. The phrases have a reedy, almost insect-like quality. Variable song structure — some renditions flat, some with a terminal pitch shift. The variability itself distinguishes it from the more consistent Townsend’s and BTG songs.

Parulidae — 8 Members (Extending Record)

Eighth Parulidae. Leads Hirundinidae and Turdidae (5 each) by three. Three structural arcs:

  • Quintet (14–20): Five species, five genera, five song endings. All E=3. Wet/mesic habitats.
  • Trio (38–40): Three Setophaga, displacement cascade. All E=4. Mountain gradient.
  • Overarching: The narrative connecting acoustic catalog (quintet) to ecological drama (trio).

No other family has three structural arcs. 8 of ~115 North American Parulidae ≈ 7%.

The Yellow Face on Gray

Entirely bright yellow face and crown — the most extensively yellow face of any Setophaga in the engine. No black cheek patch (unlike Townsend’s). Plain gray back, white underparts. All chromatic budget concentrated in one feature: the face that must be visible from the forest floor at 40+ meters.

Setophaga quartet: Yellow (16) = yellow everywhere. BTG (38) = one dot. Townsend’s (39) = bold pattern. Hermit (40) = face-only beacon on muted body.

E=4 Continues — 17th Degree, 20 Without E≤3

E=4 now at 17 degrees (41.5%). E=3 absent for 20 consecutive degrees (since degree 20) — the 20-degree milestone. E=2 absent for 27 degrees. E=1 absent for 28 degrees. Three consecutive E=4 from the same genus. The lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum is absent from degrees 21–40.

The Mountain Conifer Refuge

The Hermit Warbler breeds in mountain conifer forests at 900–2,300 m [NEEDS VERIFICATION] — white fir, red fir, mountain hemlock, Douglas fir. Higher elevation and drier conditions than Townsend’s typical conifer canopy. The southern Cascades and Siskiyou Mountains are the stronghold of pure populations. The species forages at the very tops of the tallest trees — 20–50 m [NEEDS VERIFICATION], the highest warbler stratum in the engine.

Migration: Unlike Townsend’s (which winters along the PNW coast), the Hermit Warbler is a strict long-distance migrant — wintering in highland pine-oak forests of Mexico and Central America. It abandons the PNW entirely each fall.

The Hybrid Zone

In the central and southern Oregon Cascades, where Townsend’s range meets Hermit range, a broad hybrid zone contains birds with intermediate plumage and mixed genomes [3, 4, 5]. The zone moves south at approximately 0.5–1.0 km/year [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Townsend’s males are behaviorally dominant [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Hybrid offspring tend to mate with Townsend’s-like partners. The gene pool drifts toward Townsend’s phenotype over generations.

Conservation paradox: The threat is a native species expanding through natural competitive advantage. Not invasive, not pollution, not habitat loss. Another warbler. If Townsend’s absorbs Hermit through introgression, genes persist but species identity dissolves. Is genetic assimilation extinction?
