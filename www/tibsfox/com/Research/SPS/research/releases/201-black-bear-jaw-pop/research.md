## THE JAW-POP -- MECHANICS

### How It Works

The jaw-pop is a mechanical sound, not a vocalization:

- **Mechanism.** The bear rapidly opens and snaps shut its jaws, causing the teeth to clack together loudly. The sound is produced by the collision of upper and lower teeth, amplified by the resonant cavity of the mouth. It is not a grunt, roar, or vocal sound -- it is a percussion instrument played by the bear's own skull.
- **The "pop."** The resulting sound is sharp, percussive, explosive. A single "POP" that echoes through forest at close range. Audible at 50-100 meters in quiet forest. Often repeated in rapid sequence -- pop-pop-pop -- when agitation is high.
- **Accompanying displays.** The jaw-pop rarely occurs in isolation. It accompanies: (1) head swaying or bobbing, (2) slapping the ground with forepaws ("ground slap"), (3) blowing or huffing (degree 200), (4) lunging (bluff charge). The jaw-pop is the sound component of a multi-modal threat display.
- **Saliva and foam.** During intense jaw-popping, bears often produce visible saliva or foam around the mouth. This is a byproduct of the rapid jaw movement, not a sign of rabies. It adds visual emphasis to the auditory display.
- **Not an attack signal.** The jaw-pop is defensive, not offensive. Bears that jaw-pop are stressed and want distance. A bear that intends to attack is typically silent. The jaw-pop is communication: "I am uncomfortable and I want you to leave." The louder the display, the more stressed the bear.

## TRAIL CONFLICT -- WHEN IT HAPPENS

### The Human-Bear Encounter

The jaw-pop occurs in specific encounter contexts:

- **Surprise encounters.** A hiker rounds a corner on a trail and encounters a bear at close range. Neither expected the other. The bear huffs (200), and if the hiker doesn't retreat, escalates to jaw-popping (201).
- **Food defense.** A bear feeding on berries, salmon, or garbage (campsite) feels approached. The jaw-pop defends the food resource.
- **Cub defense.** A sow with cubs (relevant to degree 202) may jaw-pop to warn off perceived threats to her young. This is the most dangerous context -- a sow defending cubs is the most likely to follow display with contact.
- **Cornered bear.** A bear that feels it cannot retreat (backed against a cliff, trapped in a camp area) will jaw-pop as a last-resort display before potential contact.
- **Appropriate response.** When a bear jaw-pops: stop moving. Speak calmly. Back away slowly. Give the bear an escape route. Do not run. Do not make sudden movements. The jaw-pop is the bear asking for space. Give it space.

(Sources: WDFW bear encounter guidelines; trail conflict research; bear-human interaction studies)

## E=6 -- EXPLOSIVE INTIMIDATION

### Calibration

The jaw-pop at E=6 represents a significant step up from the huff:

- **Why E=6?** The jaw-pop is dramatically louder and more startling than the huff (E=5). It is an explosive sound in quiet forest -- physically startling at close range. E=6 reflects the percussive intensity: a sound designed to shock, to trigger the startle response, to communicate urgency. This is the loudest deliberate sound a black bear makes short of a roar.
- **Huff to jaw-pop: E=5 to E=6.** The +1 escalation matches the behavioral escalation. The huff says "I notice you." The jaw-pop says "Leave. Now." The energy increase is proportional to the threat increase.
- **Comparison.** E=6 matches the Steller Sea Lion roar (SPS-182): both are territorial/defensive displays at high energy. The bear jaw-pop is briefer, more percussive. The sea lion roar is sustained and resonant. Different mechanisms, same intimidation function.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Black Bear Jaw-Pop

**Jaw-Pop (Defensive Intimidation):**
- **Frequency:** Broadband percussive. No single pitch. The impact of teeth produces a wide-spectrum transient: 100-5,000 Hz, with peak energy in the 200-2,000 Hz range.
- **Character:** Sharp, explosive "POP." Percussive. Like a heavy branch snapping. Teeth on teeth, amplified by mouth cavity.
- **Duration:** 0.05-0.15 seconds per pop. Often repeated: pop-pop-pop at 1-3 per second when agitated.
- **Context:** Defensive stress. Trail encounter. Food defense. Cub defense. Always accompanied by other displays (head sway, ground slap, blowing).
- **Carry distance:** 50-100 meters in forest. The sharp transient carries well through vegetation because of its broad frequency content.

**Accompanying Sounds:**
- **Huff/Blow (degree 200):** Low, forceful exhalation. E=5. Often precedes jaw-popping.
- **Ground slap:** Forepaw striking ground. Percussive. Adds visual and auditory emphasis.
- **Growl:** Low, sustained vocalization during extreme stress. Rare. Usually precedes retreat or contact.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=6. Explosive. Percussive. Intimidation at close range. The loudest deliberate sound in the black bear's defensive repertoire.
