## DALL'S PORPOISE: THE FASTEST SMALL CETACEAN

### Speed as the Defining Characteristic

*Phocoenoides dalli* is the fastest member of the Phocoenidae family and one of the fastest cetaceans of any size:

- **The speed record.** Dall's Porpoise is capable of sustained swimming speeds of up to approximately 35 mph (55 km/h), making it the fastest known cetacean of its size class and one of the fastest cetaceans overall. For comparison, the bottlenose dolphin (Tursiops truncatus) typically swims at 15-20 mph; the orca (Orcinus orca) can reach 30-34 mph in short bursts; the common dolphin (Delphinus delphis) reaches approximately 25 mph. The Dall's Porpoise sustains speeds that other cetaceans can only achieve briefly.
- **The hydrodynamic body.** The speed is a product of body morphology: the Dall's Porpoise has an extremely robust, muscular body for its size. Adults reach 5.5-7.5 feet (1.7-2.3 m) in length and weigh 265-485 pounds (120-220 kg). The body is more compact and muscular than a dolphin of similar length -- a body built for power, not agility. The caudal peduncle (the narrowed section connecting the body to the tail flukes) is unusually developed, providing the muscular attachment for the powerful tail strokes that generate the speed.
- **The keel.** Both male and female Dall's Porpoises have a distinctive dorsal keel -- a raised area of tissue on the dorsal ridge and on the ventral side near the tail. The keeled tail stock is associated with the hydrodynamic forces generated at high speed. The keel is a morphological adaptation to the animal's speed.
- **The speed in context.** 35 mph in water is extraordinary. Water is approximately 800 times denser than air. Moving through water at 35 mph requires enormous muscular power and extremely efficient hydrodynamic form. The Dall's Porpoise achieves this through body morphology, muscular development, and the specific mechanics of its tail-driven locomotion.

## THE ROOSTER TAIL: THE VISIBLE SIGNATURE

### What the Rooster Tail Is and How It Forms

The rooster tail is the Dall's Porpoise's most distinctive observable behavior:

- **The mechanism.** When the Dall's Porpoise swims at maximum speed just below the water surface, its body displaces water at high velocity. The combination of the animal's speed, the shape of its body, and its proximity to the surface creates a bow wave effect that pushes water upward and forward. As the water is pushed upward, it breaks the surface in a characteristic V-shaped plume that rises well above the waterline. This plume is the rooster tail.
- **Why it looks the way it does.** The V-shape of the rooster tail is a product of the angle at which the bow wave intersects the surface. The spray is not random -- it has a consistent shape determined by the physics of the animal's passage through the water. The height of the spray is proportional to the speed: the faster the porpoise swims, the taller and more dramatic the rooster tail.
- **The visual scale.** A Dall's Porpoise rooster tail is visible from considerable distance. Observers on vessels or shore who see what appears to be a small fountain of spray moving rapidly through the water are seeing a Dall's Porpoise at speed. The rooster tail is the primary identification feature for the species at a distance.
- **The sound component.** The rooster tail is primarily a visual phenomenon, but it has an associated sound: the hiss and rush of water displaced at high velocity, the splash of the spray landing back on the surface, the percussive churn of the wake behind the animal. This is classified as a physical percussive wake -- not a biological vocalization, but an acoustic event produced by the animal's movement through its environment.
- **E=6 calibration.** The rooster tail is E=6 because it represents directed physical force channeled into speed rather than explosive impact. The orca breach (E=9) is maximum force; the rooster tail is efficient speed. The acoustic event is real -- the percussive splash, the hiss of fast water -- but it is moderate in the engine's scale. The precision of the speed is what matters, not the volume of the sound.

## TAXONOMY AND IDENTIFICATION

### Phocoenoides dalli -- The Species

- **Scientific name:** *Phocoenoides dalli* (True, 1885). Named after William Healey Dall (1845-1927), American naturalist and explorer who first collected the specimens that would lead to the species description. Dall worked extensively in Alaska and the North Pacific. *Phocoenoides* = "porpoise-like"; the genus name indicates the species' placement within Cetacea.
- **Family Phocoenidae.** The porpoise family is distinct from the dolphin family (Delphinidae) in several key ways. Porpoises are generally smaller, have spade-shaped teeth (rather than conical), lack a pronounced beak (melon is rounded), and tend to be less acrobatic and vocal than dolphins. The family contains six species: Dall's Porpoise, Harbor Porpoise, Vaquita, Finless Porpoise, Spectacled Porpoise, and Burmeister's Porpoise.
- **Coloration.** Dall's Porpoise has a highly distinctive and striking coloration: jet black on most of the body with bright white patches on the flanks (the "vest") and a white or gray dorsal fin tip. The contrast is among the most striking of any cetacean. The pattern is visible even in brief encounters and at distance.
- **Size.** Adults: 5.5-7.5 feet (1.7-2.3 m) length; 265-485 pounds (120-220 kg). Large for a porpoise, robust in body shape. Males are typically larger and more robust than females, with a pronounced hump at the caudal peduncle.
- **Two subspecies.** *P. d. dalli* (Dall-type): the more common and widespread subspecies, occurring throughout the North Pacific. *P. d. truei* (True-type): a less common subspecies with a white belly patch extending further forward. The Dall-type is the subspecies found in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea.

(Sources: Phocoenoides dalli taxonomy; William Healey Dall biography; Phocoenidae family description; coloration and identification; subspecies P. d. dalli and P. d. truei; size and sexual dimorphism)

## CONSERVATION STATUS AND POPULATION

### Current Status

- **IUCN status:** Least Concern (global population). The Dall's Porpoise is not listed as endangered or threatened at the global level. It is one of the more abundant cetacean species in the North Pacific.
- **Population estimates.** Global population is estimated at approximately 1.2 million individuals -- making it one of the most numerous cetacean species in the North Pacific. The Puget Sound / Salish Sea population is a subset of this larger North Pacific distribution.
- **Historical hunting pressure.** Japan conducted commercial hunting of Dall's Porpoise in the North Pacific through the 1980s and into the 1990s, with annual takes reported at 40,000-42,000 individuals in some years. This is among the highest recorded annual takes of any cetacean species in history. Japanese commercial hunting of Dall's Porpoise was substantially reduced after international pressure and revised quota management. The impact on population levels remains a subject of research.
- **Current threats.** Bycatch in fishing gear (particularly gillnets and salmon drift nets); vessel strikes in high-traffic areas; pollution and contaminants (PCBs, mercury); habitat degradation. In Puget Sound specifically, the same stressors affecting all marine mammals apply: vessel noise, contaminant loads, prey availability.
- **Puget Sound presence.** Dall's Porpoise are common in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea year-round, particularly in deeper channels and open water areas. They are frequently observed bow-riding on vessels moving at speed -- a behavior that takes advantage of the pressure wave generated by a moving hull.

(Sources: IUCN Red List Phocoenoides dalli; global population estimates 1.2 million; Japanese commercial hunting history; annual take records; bycatch threats; PCB and mercury contamination; Puget Sound population)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: WHAT DALL'S PORPOISE SOUNDS LIKE

### The Acoustic Reality of a Fast Porpoise

- **Echolocation.** Like all porpoises, Dall's Porpoise produces echolocation clicks for navigation and prey detection. Porpoise echolocation clicks are generally higher-frequency and narrower-band than dolphin echolocation -- typically in the range of 120-150 kHz (versus 20-100 kHz for orcas). These are ultrasonic signals, entirely inaudible to human observers.
- **Vocalizations.** Dall's Porpoise is notably quiet compared to dolphins. It does not produce the rich variety of whistles, squeaks, and social calls that characterize dolphins. The relative quiet of the species is consistent with its generally solitary or small-group lifestyle -- extensive social communication is less necessary.
- **The rooster tail sound.** The primary human-audible acoustic event associated with Dall's Porpoise is not a biological vocalization but the physical sound of the rooster tail: the hiss of water displaced at high velocity, the percussive splash of the spray, the churning wake. For a human observer on a vessel or at the shoreline, the rooster tail is often the first acoustic indicator of a Dall's Porpoise at speed.
- **Bow-riding.** When Dall's Porpoise bow-rides on vessels (a common behavior), the audible sounds are the splashing and churning of the animal at close range in the vessel's bow wave. The physical proximity allows observation of the black-and-white coloration, the rooster tail, and the animal's speed relative to the vessel.
- **Sound at 35 mph.** An animal of 200+ kg moving through water at 35 mph generates significant hydrodynamic forces. The acoustic energy of the rooster tail -- the spray, the wake, the impact of displaced water on the surface -- is the engine entry for degree 281. Not the ultrasonic click, but the physical percussive event visible and audible to a human observer from a boat.

(Sources: Porpoise echolocation frequency 120-150 kHz; Dall's Porpoise vocal restraint; rooster tail acoustic profile; bow-riding behavior; hydrodynamic forces at speed; physical percussive wake)

## ECOLOGY AND BEHAVIOR

### Life in the Open Water

- **Diet.** Dall's Porpoise feeds primarily on small schooling fish (herring, anchovies, sand lance), squid, and crustaceans. It is a generalist predator rather than a specialist. In Puget Sound, Pacific herring and small fish species are primary prey.
- **Group size.** Typically small groups of 2-20 individuals, though aggregations of hundreds have been observed in productive feeding areas. The species does not form the large, stable social pods characteristic of resident orcas.
- **Bow-riding behavior.** One of the most frequently observed behaviors of Dall's Porpoise in Puget Sound is bow-riding on ferries, research vessels, and recreational boats. The animals swim in the pressure wave at the bow, using the vessel's movement to their energetic advantage. This behavior brings the porpoises into close contact with human observers.
- **Habitat.** Deep water channels, open ocean, fjords, and the deeper portions of Puget Sound. Dall's Porpoise prefer deeper water (typically >200 m offshore, though they occur in shallower Puget Sound channels). They are less likely to be found in shallow bays and inlets than Harbor Porpoise.
- **Reproduction.** Females give birth to a single calf approximately once every three years. Calves are born at approximately 3 feet in length and nurse for 1-2 years. Lifespan is estimated at 15-22 years.

(Sources: Dall's Porpoise diet; group size; bow-riding behavior documentation; habitat preference; deep water channels; reproduction; lifespan)

## SCIENTIFIC NAME AND NOMENCLATURAL HISTORY

### Phocoenoides dalli: The Names

- **William Healey Dall (1845-1927).** The species is named for Dall, one of the most important American naturalists of the 19th century. Dall spent extensive time in Alaska and the North Pacific conducting biological surveys for the Smithsonian Institution and the U.S. Coast Survey. His collections included the specimens that Frederick William True (1858-1914) used to describe the species in 1885.
- **Frederick William True (1885).** True formally described *Phocoenoides dalli* based on Dall's specimens. True worked at the U.S. National Museum (now the Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History) and was a leading authority on marine mammal taxonomy in the late 19th century.
- **The genus Phocoenoides.** The genus name (*Phocoenoides* = "resembling Phocoena," the harbor porpoise genus) reflects the taxonomic relationship: Dall's Porpoise is recognized as distinct from the harbor porpoise but shares the porpoise body plan.
- **Monotypic genus.** *Phocoenoides* is a monotypic genus -- it contains only one species, *P. dalli*. The two recognized subspecies (*dalli* and *truei*) are within this single species.

(Sources: William Healey Dall biography; Smithsonian Institution; Frederick William True 1885 species description; genus Phocoenoides etymology; monotypic genus; subspecies nomenclature)
