## The Two Hundred Eighty-First Degree: Precision at Speed

## What Worked

1. **FIRST PHOCOENIDAE is a genuine milestone.** After seven degrees of Delphinidae (orca) and a multi-degree gap, the porpoise family enters. The family distinction -- spade-shaped teeth, rounded melon, quieter, less acrobatic -- is documented.
2. **The rooster tail as physical percussive wake is the correct entry mode.** Not the echolocation (ultrasonic, inaudible to humans) but the visible and audible hydrodynamic event. The engine documents what can be observed and heard; the rooster tail is observable and audible.
3. **35 mph in the engine context is significant.** The orca breach (E=9) is the explosive physical maximum. The Dall's Porpoise at 35 mph sustained is E=6 -- not less impressive, differently impressive. Sustained efficiency vs. single explosive event.
4. **The nomenclatural history grounds the entry.** William Healey Dall's North Pacific work, True's 1885 description -- the science of PNW natural history has a human history, and naming it is part of the documentation.

*Degree 281 retrospective complete. DALL'S PORPOISE HIGH-SPEED ROOSTER TAIL at the two hundred eighty-first degree -- Phocoenoides dalli, Phocoenidae. The fastest cetacean of its size class at 35 mph sustained. The rooster tail: a V-shaped spray plume that rises above the water surface when the animal swims at speed just below the waterline, the bow wave effect of an extremely muscular and hydrodynamically efficient body moving through water at extraordinary velocity. Named for William Healey Dall, Smithsonian naturalist and North Pacific explorer. Described by Frederick William True in 1885. Monotypic genus; two subspecies; Dall-type in Puget Sound. IUCN Least Concern globally; ~1.2 million individuals; year-round resident in Puget Sound and Salish Sea. FIRST PHOCOENIDAE in the engine: porpoise family distinct from Delphinidae in spade-shaped teeth, rounded melon, relative quiet. E=6: efficiency and precision, not explosion.
