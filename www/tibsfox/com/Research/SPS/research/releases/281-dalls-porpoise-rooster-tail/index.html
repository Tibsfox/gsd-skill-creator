FIRST PHOCOENIDAE — 35 mph

Phocoenoides dalli — the Dall's Porpoise — enters the engine at degree 281. The first member of the Phocoenidae family. The fastest cetacean of its size class. Named for William Healey Dall (1845–1927), Smithsonian naturalist and North Pacific explorer. Described by Frederick William True in 1885.

The rooster tail: the V-shaped spray plume produced when this animal swims at maximum speed just below the water surface. Physical percussive wake — not a biological vocalization, but the acoustic and visual signature of an animal moving at extraordinary speed. E=6: efficiency and precision, not explosion.

Speed Data — Fastest Small Cetacean

Maximum speed: up to 35 mph (55 km/h). Context: Bottlenose dolphin 15–20 mph; orca (Orcinus orca) 30–34 mph in bursts; common dolphin ~25 mph. The Dall's Porpoise sustains speeds that other cetaceans achieve only briefly.

Body morphology: 5.5–7.5 ft (1.7–2.3 m); 265–485 lb (120–220 kg). Extremely robust, muscular caudal peduncle. Distinctive dorsal and ventral keel — morphological adaptation to high-speed hydrodynamics. Water density: ~800x air. 35 mph through water requires extraordinary muscular power and efficient form.

The Rooster Tail — How It Forms

When the Dall's Porpoise swims at maximum speed just below the surface, its body displaces water at high velocity. The bow wave effect pushes water upward and forward, breaking the surface in a characteristic V-shaped plume that rises well above the waterline. The rooster tail.

The V-shape is determined by the physics of the animal's passage through the water. The height of the spray is proportional to speed. Visible from considerable distance — often the primary identification feature at range. Associated sound: the hiss and rush of displaced water, the percussive splash of spray returning to surface, the churning wake. Physical percussive wake, E=6.

Taxonomy and Identification

Phocoenoides dalli (True, 1885). Family Phocoenidae — the porpoise family, distinct from Delphinidae (dolphins) in several key ways: generally smaller, spade-shaped teeth (not conical), rounded melon without pronounced beak, generally quieter and less acrobatic. Family contains six species worldwide.

Coloration: jet black on most of the body with bright white flanks (the "vest") and white or gray dorsal fin tip. Among the most striking cetacean color patterns. Two subspecies: P. d. dalli (Dall-type, the form found in Puget Sound) and P. d. truei (True-type, less common, white belly patch extends further forward).

Conservation Status

IUCN: Least Concern. Global population estimated at approximately 1.2 million individuals — among the most numerous cetacean species in the North Pacific. Resident in Puget Sound and Salish Sea year-round. Threats include bycatch in gillnets and drift nets; vessel strikes; PCB and mercury contamination; habitat degradation. Historical Japanese commercial hunting reached 40,000–42,000 individuals in peak years during the 1980s–90s, among the highest recorded annual cetacean takes in history.

Acoustic Profile

Dall's Porpoise produces echolocation clicks at approximately 120–150 kHz — higher frequency and narrower-band than dolphin echolocation, entirely ultrasonic. Vocalizations are notably limited compared to dolphins; the species does not produce the rich social call repertoire of Delphinidae. The primary human-audible acoustic signature is the rooster tail: displaced water, percussive spray, churning wake at 35 mph.
