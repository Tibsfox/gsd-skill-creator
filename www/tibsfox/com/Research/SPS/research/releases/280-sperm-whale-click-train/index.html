First Physeteridae — 230+ dB

The Physeteridae family enters the engine at degree 280. The sperm whale: sole member of genus Physeter. Largest toothed animal on Earth. Male: up to 18-20 meters, 57 metric tons.

The click train: 230-236 dB re 1 μPa @ 1m. The loudest biological sound measured on Earth. Directional forward beam. Aimed at giant squid at 2,000+ meters depth. The orca echolocation burst (degree 276) is 224 dB; the sperm whale exceeds it by 6-12 dB. Physeter macrocephalus. Moby Dick.

The Click: 230+ dB Physics

Source level: 230–236 dB re 1 μPa @ 1m. The highest source level measured for any biological sound. Mohl et al. (2003) confirmed this range for regular echolocation clicks.

Directionality: Narrow forward beam produced by the spermaceti organ in the head. Not broadcast broadly — aimed. An observer off to the side of the beam hears a much less intense sound. This is why E=7, not E=9: the power is directional.

Click structure: Complex multi-pulse event. Primary click followed by regularly spaced secondary pulses (reflections within the spermaceti organ). The inter-pulse interval encodes individual identity — a fingerprint in sound.

The creak: As the whale approaches prey, click rate accelerates into a rapid “creak” pattern — the acoustic signature of imminent prey capture.

The Hunt at 2,000 Meters

The prey: Giant squid (Architeuthis dux) and colossal squid (Mesonychoteuthis hamiltoni). The predator-prey interaction between sperm whale and giant squid at depth is one of the most dramatic ecological relationships on Earth — and one that has never been directly observed by humans.

The evidence of battle: Sperm whale skin bears circular scars from squid suckers. Some scars suggest squid of enormous size. The battle in the deep ocean is inferred from these marks; it cannot be observed.

The acoustic hunt: At 2,000 meters, 230+ dB clicks illuminate the dark water. The returning echo from a giant squid allows the whale to locate, track, and approach. The entire hunt conducted in acoustic space, in absolute darkness, at crushing pressure.

224 dB orca vs. 230+ dB sperm whale. Both directional biosonar. Both Odontoceti (toothed whales). Sperm whale is more powerful; orca is more frequency-agile and faster-updating.

SPS-273 Orca Breaching E=9

Orca breach E=9 despite lower source level than sperm whale clicks. Reason: breach is broadcast broadly; click is directional. E reflects broadcast intensity, not directional maximum.
