Delphinidae Returns — The Acrobatic Pod

Lagenorhynchus obliquidens — the Pacific White-sided Dolphin — enters at degree 282. After the orca arc (degrees 270–276, all Orcinus orca, Delphinidae) and Phocoenidae (degree 281, Dall's Porpoise), Delphinidae returns with a different representative: the highly acrobatic, strongly social, pod-based Pacific White-sided Dolphin.

The sound entry is the combined acoustic event of a pod in active aerial display: the splash and impact of leaping and somersaulting dolphins combined with the burst of whistles, clicks, and burst-pulse calls that accompany social activity. E=7: group-generated exuberance. Described by Theodore Gill, 1865.

Acoustic Profile — Aerial + Call Burst

Impact sounds: Full breach impact when 130–200 lb dolphin re-enters water belly-first or back-first. Multiple simultaneous splashes in a pod of 10–50 performing aerial behaviors. The percussive acoustic event of a pod at maximum social activity.

Vocalizations: Frequency-modulated whistles (social coordination, group cohesion); burst-pulse calls (social excitement, elevated call rate during aerial activity); echolocation clicks (20–100 kHz, ultrasonic, for prey detection). The burst-pulse component increases during aerial display and group excitement.

Taxonomy

Lagenorhynchus obliquidens (Gill, 1865). Lagenorhynchus = "flask beak" (Greek: lagenos = flask, rhynchus = snout). obliquidens = "slanting teeth" (Latin). Family Delphinidae — distinguished from Phocoenidae (degree 281) by conical teeth, pronounced beak, complex vocalizations, acrobatic behavior. Genus contains six species across the North Pacific and North Atlantic. Described by Theodore Gill (1837–1914), Smithsonian taxonomist, in 1865.

Conservation Status

IUCN: Least Concern. North Pacific population estimated approximately 900,000–1,000,000 individuals. Regular year-round presence in Puget Sound and Salish Sea; distribution varies with prey (herring, anchovy) availability. Historical bycatch in North Pacific high-seas drift net fisheries significantly reduced after UN drift net moratorium (1993). Current threats: prey availability changes, vessel noise, contaminant loads (PCBs, DDT, mercury), nearshore fishing entanglement.

Pod Behavior and Cooperative Foraging

Typical pod size: 10–100 individuals; aggregations of several thousand documented at productive foraging areas. The pod cooperates when hunting schooling fish: surrounding herring or anchovy schools into compressed bait balls, individuals taking turns rushing through to feed while others maintain the formation. Vocal coordination during foraging is intense: echolocation, social calls, burst-pulse vocalizations.

Pacific White-sided Dolphins frequently associate with other cetaceans and marine mammals at productive foraging sites — including Steller Sea Lions (degree 283) at herring aggregations. Multi-species aggregations are a regular feature of Pacific Northwest marine ecology.
