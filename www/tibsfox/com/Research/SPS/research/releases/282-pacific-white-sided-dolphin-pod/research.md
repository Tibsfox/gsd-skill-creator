## PACIFIC WHITE-SIDED DOLPHIN: THE ACROBATIC POD SPECIES

### Social, Active, Aerial

*Lagenorhynchus obliquidens* is one of the most visually striking and behaviorally active cetaceans in Pacific Northwest waters:

- **The acrobatics.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins are among the most acrobatic of the Pacific cetaceans. They regularly perform full leaps (clearing the water entirely), somersaults (rotating along the long axis in the air), and belly flops. A pod of Pacific White-sided Dolphins in active aerial display is one of the more spectacular cetacean observations available in Puget Sound: multiple animals simultaneously in the air, calling, splashing.
- **The pod.** The Pacific White-sided Dolphin is strongly social and typically travels in pods of 10-100 individuals, though aggregations of several hundred to several thousand have been documented in particularly productive feeding areas. The pod is the fundamental unit of social life -- foraging, traveling, and socializing as a group.
- **The Puget Sound presence.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins are resident in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea, though their presence varies with prey availability. They follow Pacific herring, anchovies, and other schooling fish. When herring aggregations are present in Puget Sound, Pacific White-sided Dolphins follow.
- **The speed.** These dolphins can sustain speeds of approximately 25 mph (40 km/h) -- fast enough to catch schooling fish and to perform the bow-riding behavior that brings them into frequent contact with vessels. They are enthusiastic bow-riders, often traveling with ferry routes and research vessels.
- **Body plan.** Adults reach 5.5-8 feet (1.7-2.4 m) and 130-200 lbs (60-90 kg). Distinctive coloration: black back, white belly, light-gray "suspenders" on the flanks, and a distinctive stripe running from the beak to the tail. The color pattern is immediately recognizable and contrasts sharply in aerial observation.

(Sources: Lagenorhynchus obliquidens aerial behavior; pod size 10-100 typical; aggregation several thousand; Puget Sound presence; speed ~25 mph; coloration and identification)

## TAXONOMY AND NOMENCLATURE

### Lagenorhynchus obliquidens

- **Scientific name:** *Lagenorhynchus obliquidens* (Gill, 1865). *Lagenorhynchus* = "flask beak" (Greek: *lagenos* = flask, *rhynchus* = beak/snout) -- referring to the species' short, moderately developed beak. *obliquidens* = "slanting teeth" (Latin) -- referring to the angle of the tooth crowns.
- **Genus Lagenorhynchus.** The genus is distributed across the North Atlantic and North Pacific and contains six species. The Pacific White-sided Dolphin (*L. obliquidens*) is the North Pacific representative.
- **Family Delphinidae.** The dolphin family returns at degree 282 after the Phocoenidae entry at 281. Delphinidae is distinguished from Phocoenidae by conical teeth (vs. spade-shaped), more pronounced beaks (generally), more varied and complex vocalizations, and more acrobatic behavior.
- **Theodore Gill (1865).** The species was formally described by Theodore Gill (1837-1914), American taxonomist and zoologist at the Smithsonian Institution, in 1865. Gill was a prolific describer of fish and marine mammal species.

(Sources: Lagenorhynchus obliquidens taxonomy; genus Lagenorhynchus etymology; Theodore Gill 1865 description; Delphinidae family; obliquidens tooth description)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE: AERIAL + CALL BURST

### What the Pod Sounds Like

The acoustic event for degree 282 is the combined sound of a Pacific White-sided Dolphin pod in aerial and social activity:

- **Aerial impact sounds.** When a Pacific White-sided Dolphin leaps fully clear of the water and re-enters belly-first or back-first, the impact with the water surface creates a loud smack or splash. A pod of 10-50 dolphins performing aerial behaviors simultaneously produces a percussive acoustic event -- multiple splashes, the rushing sound of bodies entering the water at speed, the churning of a disturbed surface.
- **Vocalizations during social activity.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins produce a complex range of vocalizations: whistles (frequency-modulated tonal signals, primarily social), echolocation clicks (broadband clicks, 20-100 kHz, for navigation and prey detection), and burst-pulse calls (rapid pulse trains with a rougher, buzzing quality, used in social contexts). During aerial and social activity, call rates increase -- a pod in active display is a pod in active acoustic communication.
- **Whistles.** Pacific White-sided Dolphin whistles are the primary social communication signal. The whistle repertoire includes both stereotyped (fixed-pattern) and variable whistles. Unlike the bottlenose dolphin (which uses individual signature whistles for identity), the Pacific White-sided Dolphin uses more general whistle types for group cohesion and coordination.
- **The burst-pulse.** Burst-pulse vocalizations -- rapid click trains with a rougher acoustic quality than echolocation clicks -- are associated with social and emotional contexts in dolphins. During aerial activity and group excitement, burst-pulse calls increase. The burst-pulse component of the degree 282 acoustic entry is the social excitement of a pod at its most active.
- **Echolocation during foraging.** When the pod is feeding on schooling fish, echolocation click trains are active -- the dolphins hunting cooperatively and using biosonar to locate and track prey. The frequency range (20-100 kHz) is largely ultrasonic; the audible component is the lower-frequency click energy.
- **E=7 calibration.** The combined acoustic event -- aerial splashing, social whistles, burst-pulse calls, the energy of a pod in active display -- is E=7. Less than the orca breach (E=9) or the Steller Sea Lion bull roar (E=8, degree 283). The energy is group-generated exuberance rather than single-animal maximum force.

(Sources: Dolphin aerial impact sounds; Pacific White-sided Dolphin whistle repertoire; burst-pulse vocalizations; echolocation frequency 20-100 kHz; group acoustic behavior during display; E=7 calibration)

## CONSERVATION STATUS AND POPULATION

### Current Status

- **IUCN status:** Least Concern (global). The Pacific White-sided Dolphin is not considered endangered or threatened globally.
- **Population.** The total North Pacific population is estimated at approximately 900,000-1,000,000 individuals. The eastern North Pacific stock is considered to be in the range of several hundred thousand. No comprehensive population count specific to Puget Sound exists, but sightings surveys document regular year-round presence.
- **Puget Sound status.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins occur regularly in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea, but their distribution is more variable than the Dall's Porpoise or Harbor Porpoise. Their presence in specific areas fluctuates with herring and anchovy availability.
- **Historical bycatch.** High-seas drift net fisheries in the North Pacific historically killed large numbers of Pacific White-sided Dolphins as bycatch. The United Nations moratorium on large-scale pelagic drift net fishing (implemented 1993) significantly reduced this mortality.
- **Current threats.** Prey availability changes (herring and anchovy population fluctuations driven by climate and fishing pressure); vessel noise and disturbance; entanglement in nearshore fishing gear; contaminant loads (PCBs, DDT, mercury).

(Sources: IUCN Red List Lagenorhynchus obliquidens; North Pacific population estimates; Puget Sound presence variability; drift net bycatch history; UN drift net moratorium 1993; current threats)

## COOPERATIVE FORAGING AND POD BEHAVIOR

### The Collective as Hunting Strategy

- **Herding fish.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins cooperate when foraging on schooling fish. The pod surrounds a school of herring or anchovies, driving the fish into a tight ball near the surface (a "bait ball"). Individuals then take turns rushing through the compressed school to feed, while others maintain the formation. This is cooperative foraging -- the pod's social coordination produces a foraging strategy more effective than individual hunting.
- **Vocal coordination during foraging.** The acoustic activity during cooperative foraging is intense: echolocation from multiple animals, social calls coordinating the group's movements, burst-pulse vocalizations during the excitement of the feed. The pod is simultaneously a sensory system and a social unit.
- **Association with other species.** Pacific White-sided Dolphins frequently associate with other cetaceans and marine mammals during foraging: common dolphins, humpback whales, and Steller Sea Lions (degree 283) at productive upwelling zones and herring aggregations. Multi-species aggregations at food sources are a regular feature of Pacific Northwest marine ecology.
- **Bow-riding.** The pod's speed and social flexibility make bow-riding a common and enthusiastic behavior. The dolphins position themselves in the pressure wave at a vessel's bow, surfing the wave without expending additional energy. Observers on ferries and vessels in Puget Sound regularly encounter Pacific White-sided Dolphins bow-riding at close range.

(Sources: Pacific White-sided Dolphin cooperative foraging; bait ball formation; vocal coordination during foraging; multi-species associations; bow-riding behavior; Puget Sound ferry encounters)

## AERIAL BEHAVIORS: THE VISUAL SIGNATURE

### Leaps, Somersaults, and Why

- **The leap.** A full breach -- the entire body clearing the water surface -- requires substantial muscular effort and speed. Pacific White-sided Dolphins leap frequently, suggesting the behavior has both functional and social components.
- **The somersault.** A somersault -- rotating along the long axis while airborne -- is even more demanding than a simple leap. Pacific White-sided Dolphins perform somersaults regularly, particularly during social activity and when pods are in a highly excited state.
- **Why aerial behaviors?** Several functions have been proposed: communication (aerial displays are visible to distant pod members), parasite removal (the impact dislodges parasites), play (behavioral enrichment, particularly in young animals), social bonding (shared aerial activity reinforces group cohesion), and prey herding (aerial behaviors may help compress fish schools). The functional status of aerial behaviors in Pacific White-sided Dolphins is not fully resolved; multiple functions may operate simultaneously.
- **The observation experience.** Encountering a pod of Pacific White-sided Dolphins in full aerial display is one of the more remarkable wildlife experiences available in Puget Sound. The combination of multiple animals simultaneously airborne, the splash and churning of a large pod in social activity, the vocalizations at the surface -- this is what E=7 looks and sounds like in the SPS catalog.

(Sources: Pacific White-sided Dolphin aerial behavior functions; full breach mechanics; somersault behavior; communication and social function; parasite removal; play behavior; observation experience)
