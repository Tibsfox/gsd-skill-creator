## BUBO VIRGINIANUS -- THE SPECIES

### The Great Horned Owl: Biology and Pacific Northwest Context

*Bubo virginianus* (Gmelin, 1788) is the most widespread and ecologically dominant owl in North America:

- **Identification.** Large, heavy-bodied owl. Wingspan 36-60 inches (91-152 cm). Prominent ear tufts ("horns") that are feather tufts, not ears. Yellow eyes. Rich brown plumage with barred underparts and white throat patch. The face is framed by a facial disc that channels sound to the actual ear openings (located asymmetrically on the skull to enable precise localization of sound). The ear tufts are communication structures, not hearing organs.
- **Scientific name.** *Bubo* (Latin: the bubo or great horned owl -- from the Greek boubos, the Eurasian Eagle-Owl; the name of the owl in classical Latin); *virginianus* (of Virginia -- named for the type locality of the species as described from the Virginia region). Named by Gmelin in 1788 based on specimens from Virginia. The name captures the species' widespread North American distribution.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** The Great Horned Owl is one of the most common large owls in the Pacific Northwest. It occurs in virtually every forested and partially forested habitat type in the region: old-growth forest, second-growth, mixed forest-grassland edge, riparian corridors, suburban parks, farmland with woodlots. The species is a habitat generalist of unusual breadth -- it can live in almost any environment that provides some tree cover and prey.
- **Feeding ecology.** The Great Horned Owl is a powerful generalist predator. Prey in the Pacific Northwest: Northern Flying Squirrel, Douglas squirrel, snowshoe hare, cottontail rabbit, American coot, ducks, American crow, other raptors (including smaller owls and occasional Osprey or Red-tailed Hawk). The Great Horned Owl is the apex avian predator in most Pacific Northwest habitats -- it will prey on other raptors when the opportunity presents.
- **Relationship with other owls.** The Great Horned Owl is the primary predator of Barred Owl and one of the few predators of adult Spotted Owl. The species' predatory dominance shapes the community of nocturnal raptors in any area it occupies. The Spotted Owl (SPS degree 4) is specifically displaced from areas with high Great Horned Owl densities -- the competitive relationship is one of the drivers of Spotted Owl range contraction.

(Sources: Bubo virginianus identification; wingspan 36-60 inches; ear tufts as communication not hearing; asymmetric ear placement; Bubo etymology from classical Latin/Greek; virginianus = Virginia type locality; Gmelin 1788; Pacific Northwest habitat generalist; prey species: flying squirrel, hare, coot, ducks, other raptors; predation of Barred and Spotted Owls; apex avian predator status)

## THE TERRITORIAL HOOTING WAR -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Multi-Night Sustained Interaction

The Great Horned Owl's territorial hooting war is a sustained multi-night acoustic event:

- **The hooting call.** The primary territorial vocalization is the deep, resonant hoo-hoo-hoo sequence: typically 3-8 notes delivered in a low-pitched, rhythmic pattern. The fundamental frequency is approximately 200-500 Hz for the male (lower-pitched) and 300-600 Hz for the female (higher-pitched). The depth and resonance of the call -- one of the lowest-pitched bird vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest -- allows it to propagate across large distances through forest and open terrain.
- **The territorial war structure.** A territorial "war" involves two or more owls exchanging calls across a territorial boundary for extended periods. The interaction begins with one owl calling and a neighboring owl responding from the adjacent territory. The exchange can continue for hours -- each owl calling in the gaps of the other's calls (counter-calling) or calling simultaneously (overlapping calls, which signal heightened territorial competition). The interaction may repeat over multiple consecutive nights.
- **Carrying distance.** The Great Horned Owl's hoo sequence carries 1-3 kilometers in ideal conditions (calm night, clear temperature gradient). In the Pacific Northwest, where winter nights are often calm and cold, the carrying distance is maximized. An owl calling from a Douglas-fir grove can be heard across a valley. The spatial scale of a territorial war is correspondingly large.
- **Male-female duetting.** Mated pairs of Great Horned Owls often call in coordinated duets during territorial and pair-bonding interactions. The male's deeper hoo and the female's higher-pitched hoo produced in alternation create a two-part vocal texture that is distinctively different from a single owl calling. A territorial interaction that involves both members of a pair at one boundary is more acoustically complex than a single-bird interaction.
- **E=6 calibration.** The territorial hooting war is E=6: sustained, resonant, present across a large spatial and temporal scale -- but not maximum in the way of the Bald Eagle combat (E=7) or the Goshawk alarm (E=7). The owl's call does not involve physical commitment or physical risk; it is sustained acoustic negotiation. E=6 is the correct level: significant, weighted, present, but operating through sound rather than physicality.

(Sources: Bubo virginianus hoo call description; fundamental frequency 200-500 Hz male; 300-600 Hz female; territorial war structure: counter-calling, overlapping calls; duration: hours on consecutive nights; carrying distance 1-3 km; male-female duet structure; E=6 calibration vs. E=7 raptor physical behaviors)

## WINTER TERRITORIAL BEHAVIOR -- WHY OWLS CALL IN WINTER

### The Great Horned Owl's Early Nesting Calendar

The winter timing of Great Horned Owl territorial wars is not incidental -- it is driven by the species' extraordinary nesting phenology:

- **The earliest nesting owl.** The Great Horned Owl begins nesting earlier than any other North American owl -- often earlier than most birds in the Pacific Northwest. Eggs are typically laid January-February, with incubation lasting approximately 28-35 days. This means chicks hatch in February-March, when Pacific Northwest temperatures are still cold and prey is limited.
- **Why nest in winter.** The early nesting strategy allows the Great Horned Owl to take advantage of high early spring prey abundance as chicks are growing (March-April, when small mammals and waterfowl are highly active). By the time food demand is maximum (large chicks requiring frequent feeding), spring prey abundance is at its peak.
- **Territorial establishment precedes nesting.** To be ready to nest in January, Great Horned Owls must establish and defend territories in November-December. The hooting wars of December and January are the direct consequence of this early calendar: owls are competing for the territories they need to hold through the breeding season that begins in a matter of weeks.
- **The winter acoustic environment.** December-January in the Pacific Northwest is acoustically sparse: most songbirds are silent or have departed, frogs are dormant, insects are absent. The Great Horned Owl's deep hoo fills the acoustic vacuum of the winter night. In the absence of other voices, the owl's call carries unimpeded through the cold air. The winter territorial war is audible in a way that would be impossible in the acoustic richness of spring.
- **Counter-seasonal loudness.** The Great Horned Owl is one of the loudest sounds in the winter Pacific Northwest night precisely because it is one of the only sounds. E=6 in December is equivalent to a higher energy level in May.

(Sources: Great Horned Owl nesting phenology: eggs January-February; incubation 28-35 days; earliest nesting owl in North America; early nesting strategy: spring prey timing; territorial establishment November-December; acoustic vacuum of winter night; counter-seasonal loudness argument)

## TERRITORIAL ECOLOGY AND THE WINTER LANDSCAPE

### The Pacific Northwest Winter Night

The Great Horned Owl's territorial war takes place in a specific ecological context:

- **Winter forest structure.** In December-January, Pacific Northwest deciduous trees have lost their leaves. The forest is structurally simplified: Douglas-fir and other conifers retain their canopy but the understory and deciduous component is leafless. Sound propagates more easily through the simplified winter structure than through the full canopy of spring or summer.
- **The nocturnal landscape.** Great Horned Owl territorial interactions occur primarily from dusk to midnight and in the hours before dawn -- the periods of peak territorial activity. The Pacific Northwest winter night is long (sunset 4-5 pm, sunrise 7-8 am): twelve or more hours of darkness in which territorial interactions can occur. The extended night is not incidental to the owls' early nesting strategy -- the long winter nights provide more time for territorial negotiation and pair bonding before nesting begins.
- **Human perception.** The Great Horned Owl's territorial hooting war is one of the most commonly reported wildlife sounds by winter hikers, campers, and anyone in rural Pacific Northwest environments in December-January. The deep, resonant hoo carries to human ears as a distinctive, easily recognized call at distances where other bird sounds have become inaudible. The owl's winter prominence in human soundscape perception is a direct function of the acoustic vacuum it fills.
- **The five-sixths mark position.** Being one degree before the five-sixths mark, the Great Horned Owl's territorial war occupies a specific temporal position in the engine: the last ecological entry before the structural milestone. The winter darkness, the long-distance hooting, the multi-night battle across territorial boundaries -- this is the ecological accompaniment to the engine's penultimate degree before degree 300.

(Sources: Winter forest acoustic structure; nocturnal landscape: 12+ hours of darkness in Pacific Northwest winter; human perception of GHO winter hooting; position one degree before five-sixths mark; winter darkness as ecological context)
