Acoustic Profile — Territorial Hooting War

Hoo call: Deep, resonant hoo-hoo-hoo sequences, typically 3-8 notes. Male: 200-500 Hz fundamental. Female: 300-600 Hz (higher-pitched). One of the lowest-pitched bird vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest.

Carrying distance: 1-3 km in ideal conditions (calm winter night, cold air, temperature gradient). Fills the acoustic vacuum of the Pacific Northwest winter. Audible from across a valley.

War structure: One owl calls. The neighboring owl counter-calls from adjacent territory. Exchange continues for hours -- counter-calling in gaps, overlapping calls at peak intensity. Mated pairs may call in coordinated duets (male + female alternating). Repeats on consecutive nights until territory settled.

E=6: Sustained acoustic presence across large spatial and temporal scale. Not physical commitment (vs. Bald Eagle E=7 combat) but sustained negotiation. E=6 is correct: significant, weighted, persistent.

Why Great Horned Owls Call in Winter

The winter timing is driven by the species' extraordinary nesting calendar. The Great Horned Owl lays eggs in January-February — the earliest nesting schedule of any North American owl. To be ready for January nesting, territorial establishment must happen in November-December. The hooting wars of December-January are the acoustic signature of this early commitment: territories are being settled while most birds are silent.

The winter acoustic vacuum amplifies the effect. In December, the forest is stripped of competing voices — no warblers, no flycatchers, no frogs, no insects. The Great Horned Owl's deep hoo fills the silence and carries with unimpeded clarity through the cold winter air. E=6 in December is equivalent to a higher energy level in May.
