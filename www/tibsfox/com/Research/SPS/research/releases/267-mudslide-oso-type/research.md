## A NOTE ON OSO

The Oso landslide occurred on March 22, 2014, in Snohomish County, Washington, approximately 55 miles northeast of Seattle. The slide destroyed the Steelhead Haven neighborhood and claimed 43 lives. It was the deadliest landslide in United States history to that point.

This research documents the geological and acoustic characteristics of this type of event. The community of Oso and the families of those lost are acknowledged with full respect. The focus here is on understanding the natural mechanisms -- the geology, the physics, the acoustics -- because understanding these mechanisms is how communities protect themselves from future events. The documentation is offered in that spirit.

## THE GEOLOGY: GLACIAL TILL ON CLAY

### Why the Slope Failed

The Oso landslide was not a random event -- it was the result of specific geological conditions that had been identified as hazardous by geologists:

- **The landslide history of the site.** The bluff above the North Fork Stillaguamish River valley had a documented history of slope failures. Geologists studying the area had mapped multiple previous landslide events at the same location -- evidence that the slope had failed before and would fail again under the right conditions. This was known, mapped, and studied.
- **Glacial legacy deposits.** The Cascade foothills in this region were overridden by glaciers during the last ice age (the Cordilleran Ice Sheet, which reached its maximum extent approximately 16,000-18,000 years ago). The glaciers deposited deep layers of unsorted sediment -- glacial till -- consisting of sand, gravel, and boulders mixed together without the sorting that flowing water produces. These glacial deposits are common throughout the Puget Lowland and Cascade foothills.
- **The clay layer.** Beneath or interbedded with the glacial till at Oso was a layer of lacustrine clay -- fine-grained sediment deposited in a glacially formed lake. Clay has a critical hydrological property: when saturated with water, it becomes extremely weak in shear. It does not drain quickly because the fine grain size resists water movement. A saturated clay layer beneath a heavy glacial till deposit is a failure waiting for the right conditions.
- **The mechanics of failure.** When the clay layer became sufficiently saturated -- through prolonged rainfall, through the 2014 winter's record snowpack and precipitation -- its shear strength dropped below the weight of the overlying glacial till. The slope failed along the clay interface. Once failure initiated, the saturated materials behaved as a semi-liquid, flowing rapidly downslope across the valley floor.
- **The 2014 precipitation context.** The winter of 2013-2014 had been unusually wet in western Washington. Snowpack was above normal. The weeks preceding March 22 had been rainy. The soil was saturated beyond what the underlying clay could accommodate. This is not the explanation for the tragedy -- responsibility and warning systems are human and institutional questions beyond the scope of this research. This is the geophysical explanation for why the slope failed when it did.

(Sources: Washington State Department of Natural Resources Oso landslide documentation; USGS landslide report; glacial geology of Puget Lowland; clay shear strength; North Fork Stillaguamish River valley geology; 2014 winter precipitation records)

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT: DEEP RUMBLING WALL MOVEMENT

### The Sound of Saturated Earth in Motion

The acoustic signature of a large debris flow is fundamentally different from the other mass-movement events in this trilogy:

- **The primary acoustic character: deep rumbling.** Unlike the sharp crack of glacial ice fracture or the dry percussive impact of rockfall, a major debris flow produces deep, sustained, low-frequency rumbling. The sound comes from the mass of saturated material in continuous motion: the viscous flow of semi-liquid debris produces a low-frequency rumble that is felt as much as heard at close range. The low-frequency content is the defining characteristic.
- **Why low frequency dominates.** The saturated debris mixture -- water, sand, clay, glacial till -- behaves as a dense, viscous fluid when in motion. Viscous materials in turbulent flow produce acoustic energy primarily in low frequencies. The large mass (hundreds of thousands to millions of cubic yards of material) creates acoustic wavelengths that correspond to low-frequency rumble, not the high-frequency crack of rigid material impact.
- **The wall-of-movement character.** Unlike a rockslide, which has a percussive onset and cascade structure, a major debris flow can sound like a moving wall -- the leading edge of the flow producing continuous deep rumbling as it advances. This is the "wall movement" description in the sound type.
- **Duration.** The main flow of a major debris event can last from tens of seconds to several minutes. This is longer than glacier calving (seconds) and longer than most rockslides (seconds to tens of seconds). The sustained duration of the deep rumble is part of what makes a major debris flow overwhelming in acoustic terms.
- **Infrasound component.** Large debris flows generate substantial infrasound (below 20 Hz, below human hearing threshold). The infrasound component is part of why people in proximity to a major debris flow often report a physical sensation -- the sound is felt in the chest and body before it is perceived consciously as audible. Animals that can detect infrasound may have advance warning that humans do not.
- **E=8 energy level.** Eight is the maximum for this arc (E=6 at 265, E=7 at 266, E=8 at 267). The deep rumbling wall movement of a major debris flow at its maximum energy is the most acoustically overwhelming of the three mass-movement events. The low-frequency wall of sound, the felt physical component, the sustained duration -- all of these contribute to the E=8 designation. This is the highest energy event in the Geophysics arc (degrees 261-267).

## ACOUSTIC MONITORING AND EARLY WARNING

### What Sound Can Tell Us

The acoustic signature of debris flows has become an important tool in monitoring and early warning systems:

- **Seismic/acoustic monitoring networks.** Modern debris flow monitoring uses networks of seismic sensors and acoustic sensors deployed along rivers and valleys known to be debris-flow prone. These networks detect the characteristic low-frequency signal of a debris flow in its initiation or early movement phase -- potentially before the flow reaches populated areas downstream.
- **The acoustic signature as fingerprint.** The combination of infrasound and low-frequency rumble produced by a major debris flow is distinct enough that it can be detected and distinguished from other acoustic events (vehicle traffic, wind, thunder) by appropriate sensors. This distinctiveness makes acoustic monitoring viable as an early warning technique.
- **The Stillaguamish watershed.** Following the Oso event, monitoring and early warning systems in the North Fork Stillaguamish valley and other debris-flow-prone drainages in western Washington have been upgraded and expanded. The understanding of debris flow acoustics gained through scientific study has direct application to community safety.

(Sources: USGS debris flow monitoring; acoustic early warning systems; Stillaguamish watershed monitoring post-2014; infrasound detection; seismic signature of debris flows)

## GLACIALLY DEPOSITED LANDSCAPES: REGIONAL CONTEXT

### Why This Is a PNW-Wide Geological Condition

The geological conditions that produced the Oso landslide are not unique to that specific location:

- **Puget Lowland and foothills.** The entire Puget Lowland and Cascade foothills region was shaped by glaciation. Glacial deposits -- till, outwash, lacustrine clay -- are present throughout the region. Many hillsides and valley walls in western Washington have the same general structure that failed at Oso: glacial sediments over weaker materials, with slopes steepened by river erosion at their bases.
- **River undercutting.** The rivers that drain the Cascades -- the Stillaguamish, the Snohomish, the Skagit, the Sauk -- actively undercut the valley walls as they meander. The removal of slope material at the base progressively destabilizes the slope above. This process operates continuously and makes valley walls throughout the region inherently susceptible to failure under appropriate conditions.
- **The role of forest cover.** Forest cover provides some slope stability -- root systems bind near-surface soil and intercept rainfall before it reaches the mineral soil. Clear-cutting removes this protection and has been studied as a contributing factor to debris flow initiation in the Cascades. The relationship between forest management and slope stability is an ongoing subject of research and policy discussion in Washington State.

(Sources: Puget Lowland glacial geology; river undercutting of valley walls; Cascade watershed stability; forest cover and slope stability research; Washington State landslide hazard mapping)
