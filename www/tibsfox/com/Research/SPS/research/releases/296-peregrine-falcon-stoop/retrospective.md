## What Worked

1. **The non-vocalization acoustic event is a new category for the SPS engine.** All previous bird entries document vocalizations or structural sounds (drumming, wingbeats in ambient context). The Peregrine stoop at degree 296 is the first entry documenting a purely aerodynamic acoustic event: the compressed-air crack produced by a bird's body displacing air at 240 mph. Not a call. 3. **The predator-prey consecutive pair (295-296) is the engine's most ecologically precise sequential design.** The Rhinoceros Auklet's nocturnal strategy (burrow-entering moans, degree 295) exists partly because of the Peregrine Falcon's diurnal hunting capability (stoop wingcut, degree 296). The prey's adaptation immediately before the predator's event. The ecological logic encoded in the degree sequence.
4. **The DDT recovery is correctly documented as one of American conservation's most successful outcomes.** Extirpated from most of the continental US east of the Rockies by the early 1970s; banned from the ESA in 1999. Urban Seattle pairs breeding on building ledges are the outcome of this recovery. The Peregrine Falcon is both a conservation success story and a living presence in the Pacific Northwest's urban and rural environments.
5. **The stoop physics are documented at the right level of detail.** The fold, the velocity progression, the nasal tubercle adaptation, the notched kill-bill, the closed-fist strike -- enough to understand why 240 mph is possible and what happens acoustically as a consequence.

## Discoveries

1. **The Peregrine's global range is the most exceptional of any raptor species.** Every continent except Antarctica, 17-19 subspecies, from high Arctic tundra to tropics, from sea level to 4,000 meters. No other raptor matches this range. The Pacific Northwest's co-occurrence of three subspecies (pealei, anatum, tundrius) during migration makes it one of the most interesting regional Peregrine study areas.
