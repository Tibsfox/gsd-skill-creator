9th Consecutive Anatidae — TIES ALL-TIME RECORD

Nine consecutive Anatidae: Harlequin (118) through Canvasback (126). TIES the Picidae nonet (107–115) as the longest family streak in engine history. Four genera, nine ecological niches, nine acoustic profiles. The Picidae nonet included the engine’s first E=6 entries; the Anatidae nonet operates in a lower energy register (E=3–5) but matches the streak length. Degree 127 is the deciding degree: a 10th Anatidae would break the all-time record. A non-Anatidae would leave the record tied.

The Aristocrat of Ducks — Speed, Elegance, Prestige

The Canvasback is North America’s fastest-flying duck at 72 mph (some sources report 80 mph). The distinctive sloping forehead-to-bill profile — a smooth concave curve from crown to bill tip — is both aerodynamically functional and the definitive field mark. No other North American duck has this shape. Prized in hunting culture for speed, difficulty, and the rich flavor from a wild celery (Vallisneria) diet. The “aristocrat” title dates to the 19th century.

The Sloping Profile — Canvasback vs. Redhead

The identification challenge: Canvasback vs. Redhead (Aythya americana). Both Aythya with reddish heads. The key: Canvasback = sloping profile (smooth concave forehead-to-bill), longer bill, paler canvas-colored back, richer red head. Redhead = rounded head (standard duck shape), shorter bill with pale band, darker gray back. The profile difference is definitive at any range where head shape is visible.

Low Grunts Display

The Canvasback’s courtship involves a dramatic head-throw display: the male arches his neck back until the head nearly touches the back, then snaps forward with a low, guttural “ik-ik-ik” grunt. The display is visually spectacular but acoustically quiet (E=3). The grunt is deep (200–1,800 Hz), percussive, brief. Audible at 10–25 meters. The female gives a soft, harsh “crrr” — a growling purr. The Canvasback communicates with restraint: the aristocrat does not shout.

Prairie Pothole Breeder

Breeds in the Prairie Pothole Region — the “duck factory” of North America (Dakotas, Montana, Saskatchewan, Alberta, Manitoba). Prefers deep-water marshes with wild celery. The species name valisineria comes from the plant genus Vallisneria — the aquatic plant that provides both food and rich flavor. Population: 600,000–700,000 (Least Concern but closely monitored). A bellwether for prairie pothole health — when Canvasback declines, it signals broader wetland degradation.
