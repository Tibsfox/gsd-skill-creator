## THE CASCADE RANGE -- THE GEOGRAPHIC STAGE

### From 14,411 Feet to Sea Level

The Cascade Range in Washington State is one of the most meteorologically complex mountain ranges in North America. The elevation profile creates distinct climatic zones that are simultaneously active during a major storm event:

- **The volcanic summits.** Mount Rainier (14,411 feet / 4,392 meters) is the dominant peak and the highest point in Washington State. Mount Baker (10,781 feet), Glacier Peak (10,541 feet), Mount Adams (12,281 feet), and Mount St. Helens (8,365 feet, post-1980 eruption) are the major volcanic peaks. These summits receive the most extreme weather conditions in the Pacific Northwest: year-round snowpack, hurricane-force winds during storm events, sub-zero temperatures. During a major Pacific storm, the summit conditions at Mount Rainier are Antarctic in their intensity.
- **The subalpine zone (7,000-10,000 feet).** Heavy snowfall, full-force storm winds, visibility near zero. The stunted krummholz trees at the upper subalpine zone flex and whip in gale conditions. The snowpack accumulates and compresses. Avalanche conditions develop rapidly.
- **The montane forest zone (3,000-7,000 feet).** Old-growth Douglas fir, western red cedar, and western hemlock forests. During a storm, these enormous trees flex under the wind. The acoustic event of a major storm in old-growth montane forest is extraordinary: the creak and flex of massive trunks, the roar of wind through dense canopies, the percussion of limbs and cones, the amplification of the storm's sound by the reflective surfaces of thousands of closely spaced tree trunks.
- **The river valleys (sea level to 3,000 feet).** The major Cascade drainages &mdash; the Cowlitz, the Lewis, the Puyallup, the Nisqually, the Carbon, the White River, the Snoqualmie, the Skykomish, the Nooksack &mdash; carry the storm's precipitation to Puget Sound and the Columbia River. During a major storm, river levels rise rapidly. The acoustic event at a flooding river is continuous and massive: not the gentle sound of flowing water but the roar of a drainage system near capacity.
- **Puget Sound (sea level).** The enclosed inland sea that receives both the rivers draining the west Cascades and the direct atmospheric energy of the storm. During a major Pacific storm, Puget Sound experiences storm surge, elevated wave action, and the combined acoustic event of atmospheric storm energy plus multiple river drainages simultaneously emptying elevated volumes of runoff.

## THE STORM EVENT -- WHAT PRODUCES E=10

### Atmospheric River + Bomb Cyclone: The Maximum Event

The Cascades Full Storm at E=10 requires a specific meteorological condition: not a routine Pacific storm but the most intense class of event that affects the range. Two storm types reach this calibration:

**The Atmospheric River:**
An atmospheric river is a narrow corridor of moisture-laden air originating in the tropical Pacific and transported northward and eastward by the Pacific jet stream. The largest atmospheric rivers carry water vapor comparable to the discharge of the Amazon River at their peak. When an atmospheric river strikes the Cascade Range, the orographic lifting effect forces the moisture-laden air rapidly upward, producing precipitation at extreme rates:
- At sea level: 2-4 inches of rain per hour
- At 3,000-5,000 feet: rain transitioning to snow, snow water equivalent 1-3 inches per hour
- At 7,000-10,000 feet: heavy snow, accumulation rates 3-6 inches per hour
- At summit (14,000+ feet): blizzard conditions, horizontal snow driven by 80-120 mph winds

The largest atmospheric rivers to strike Washington State produce catastrophic flooding throughout the Cascade drainages, avalanche conditions throughout the range, and conditions at the volcanic summits that are among the most extreme on Earth outside Antarctica.

**The Bomb Cyclone (Explosive Cyclogenesis):**
A bomb cyclone is an extratropical cyclone that undergoes explosive deepening &mdash; central pressure dropping by 24+ millibars in 24 hours. Pacific Northwest bomb cyclones produce sustained winds of 60-80 mph at sea level and 100-150 mph at summit elevations. The Cascades in a bomb cyclone event experience the full force of a major hurricane-class wind event.

**The Combined Event:**
When an atmospheric river delivers an extreme moisture load simultaneously with a bomb cyclone's wind event, the result is the maximum Cascade storm scenario: extreme precipitation at all elevations, hurricane-force winds at the summits, flooding in the river valleys, storm surge in Puget Sound. This is the Full Storm at E=10.

## THE ACOUSTIC EVENT -- SUMMIT TO SEA SIMULTANEOUSLY

### What E=10 Sounds Like Across 14,000 Feet of Elevation Change

The Cascades Full Storm at E=10 is a layered acoustic event occurring across the full elevation profile simultaneously:

**Summit acoustic zone (14,000+ feet):**
- Wind at 100-150 mph produces continuous broadband acoustic energy. At these velocities, wind produces a roar rather than a howl: the atmospheric turbulence generates noise across the full audible spectrum, with peak energy in the 100-500 Hz range.
- Snow crystals driven by 100+ mph winds create a hiss that layers above the wind roar: fine-grained, high-frequency, continuous.
- Glacial creak and ice expansion: at low temperatures with rapid temperature fluctuation, glacial ice produces cracking sounds detectable above the wind.
- The absence of any acoustic softening by vegetation (no trees above timberline) means the wind's acoustic energy is unmitigated.

**Subalpine and montane forest zone (3,000-10,000 feet):**
- Old-growth Douglas firs flexing under gale-force wind: the creak and groan of trunks with diameters of 6-10 feet, 150-250 feet tall, under forces approaching their structural limits. This is a profound acoustic event: the sound of living structures of enormous mass in extreme mechanical stress.
- Wind through dense canopy creates a sustained roar that is distinct from the summit wind: filtered by millions of branch and needle surfaces, producing a more complex frequency spectrum.
- The percussion of limbs, cones, and debris falling through the canopy is continuous during a major storm.
- Waterfalls and cascades at full flood: every creek and stream at full discharge, the acoustic event of falling water multiplied by the storm's precipitation rate.

**River valley zone (sea level to 3,000 feet):**
- Rivers at or near flood stage: the acoustic event of major drainage systems at maximum discharge. The Snoqualmie River at full flood (occurring several times per decade) roars with a continuous broadband sound that can be heard miles from the riverbank.
- The sound of large debris (logs, boulders) moving through flood-stage rivers adds percussive low-frequency impacts to the continuous water roar.
- Storm-driven rain on river surfaces and lake surfaces: the acoustic texture of extreme rainfall is itself a broadband event, the density of raindrop impacts on open water producing a continuous white noise layer.

**Puget Sound storm zone (sea level):**
- Storm surge and elevated wave action: wave heights of 3-6 feet within the enclosed sound (compared to typical 1-2 feet during calm conditions).
- The combined acoustic event of multiple rivers entering Puget Sound at elevated discharge, atmospheric storm energy at the water surface, and wave impacts on the shoreline.
- The interaction of the outgoing tidal current with storm surge creates complex wave interference patterns with their own acoustic signature.

## MOUNT RAINIER -- THE SUMMIT REFERENCE

### The Highest Point and the Most Extreme Conditions

Mount Rainier is the reference point for understanding the summit conditions of a Cascades Full Storm:

- **The weather records.** Mount Rainier holds numerous Pacific Northwest weather records. Paradise (5,400 feet on the south slope) averaged 692 inches of snowfall per year over a multi-decade period &mdash; one of the highest average snowfall totals recorded anywhere in North America. At the summit, conditions during major storms include: temperatures of -20 to -40°F, wind speeds of 100-180 mph, visibility near zero, ice accretion that can coat all exposed surfaces.
- **The Paradise acoustic environment.** At Paradise during a full storm, the acoustic environment is overwhelming: hurricane-force wind producing a continuous broadband roar, the creak and crack of the Paradise visitor center's structure under wind loading, the percussion of wind-driven ice crystals against all hard surfaces, the occasional crack of ice or tree limbs above the visitor facilities.
- **The summit volcanic geology.** Mount Rainier's volcanic geology creates additional acoustic possibilities during major storms. The thermal heat at the summit crater (Columbia Crest) produces vapor vents that remain active even during extreme cold events. The vapor from these vents, forced by storm winds, produces sounds that are distinct from the wind itself: a higher-frequency hiss that layers above the wind roar.
- **The glacier system.** Rainier's glaciers &mdash; the most extensive glacial system on any individual peak in the contiguous United States &mdash; respond dynamically to major storm events. Precipitation adds to the snowpack; temperature fluctuations produce glacial sounds. The Carbon, Emmons, Nisqually, and other major glaciers are active acoustic participants in a full storm event.

(Sources: Mount Rainier weather records; Paradise 692 inches average snowfall; summit wind 100-180 mph; summit temperature range; vapor vents Columbia Crest; glacier system: Carbon, Emmons, Nisqually; NOAA climate data Mount Rainier)

## THE HYDROLOGICAL CASCADE -- WATER IN MOTION

### When the Storm's Energy Becomes River Discharge

A major Cascade storm event produces an extraordinary hydrological event throughout the range's drainage systems:

- **Snowmelt and rain-on-snow events.** The most catastrophically flooding condition in the Cascades is rain-on-snow: when a warm atmospheric river deposits heavy rain on an existing snowpack, the rain combines with rapid snowmelt to produce runoff volumes far in excess of the precipitation rate alone. The Snoqualmie River's major flood events (1990, 2006, 2009) were rain-on-snow events. These floods produce the maximum discharge acoustic event: rivers carrying 5-10 times their normal volume at high velocity.
- **The Snoqualmie Falls at flood.** Snoqualmie Falls (270 feet, 16 feet higher than Niagara Falls) at full flood is one of the most acoustically powerful natural events in Washington State. During major flood events, the falls' roar can be heard from several miles away. The acoustic energy produced is extraordinary: the kinetic energy of thousands of cubic feet per second of water falling 270 feet, converted entirely to sound and turbulence.
- **Puget Sound receiving the cascade.** The Puget Sound watershed includes essentially all of the western Cascades from the Canadian border to the Cowlitz drainage. During a major storm event, Puget Sound receives elevated discharge from dozens of major rivers simultaneously. The acoustic effect of this on Puget Sound's near-shore environment &mdash; muddy, fast-moving freshwater mixing with salt water at the river mouths, currents disrupted, the smell and sound of the land arriving in the sea &mdash; is a specific Pacific Northwest experience that the engine documents here.

## ECOLOGICAL RESPONSE TO THE FULL STORM

### How Wildlife Responds to E=10 Conditions

The Cascades Full Storm at E=10 produces a distinctive ecological response across the range's species:

- **Large mammals.** Roosevelt elk and black-tailed deer move to lower elevations and sheltered terrain. Grizzly bears in the North Cascades (a very small population) den well before major winter storms. Black bears are typically in torpor during the extreme storm season. Mountain goats, the most storm-adapted large mammal in the range, move to steep rocky terrain where snow accumulation is minimal and the rock provides thermal mass.
- **Birds.** Most songbirds and raptors reduce activity to near zero during extreme storm events. Clark's nutcrackers and gray jays, the most storm-adapted corvids, continue foraging at lower intensities. Golden eagles and bald eagles move to lower elevations. The few birds that remain at high elevation during storms demonstrate extraordinary wind and cold tolerance.
- **Salmon.** The rivers in flood carry salmon upstream more efficiently in some respects but also create migration barriers when debris and turbulence are extreme. The fall and early winter Cascade storm season coincides with late Chinook and coho runs. The flooding rivers and the migrating salmon are simultaneous events.
- **Silence after the storm.** One of the most distinctive acoustic events associated with the Cascades Full Storm is the silence that follows. When a major storm passes, the acoustic environment of the Cascades undergoes a rapid transition: wind decreases, precipitation stops, and the forest is quiet except for dripping water and settling snow. The contrast between the storm's E=10 acoustic event and the post-storm silence amplifies the experience of both.

(Sources: Roosevelt elk and black-tailed deer elevation response; mountain goat storm adaptation; Clark's nutcracker and gray jay storm behavior; salmon migration in flood-stage rivers; post-storm acoustic silence; Cascade Range ecology winter storm response)
