## What Worked

1. **The summit-to-sea vertical profile is the essential analytical structure.** Not "a storm on Mount Rainier" but the full vertical range from 14,411 feet to sea level, all simultaneously active during a single extreme event. The depth of the structure -- each elevation zone contributing its characteristic sound -- is what makes this E=10. No single-elevation storm event earns E=10; the full range does.
2. **The boulder-transport observation is the most distinctive acoustic detail.** During true river flooding (when water velocity exceeds the threshold for boulder transport), 100-pound rocks roll along riverbeds. The resulting deep, irregular percussion is audible only during extreme flood events. It is the sonic signature of maximum water power -- the acoustic marker that distinguishes a flooding river from a fast-flowing one.
3. **The old-growth forest acoustics analysis is essential and rarely documented.** Old-growth Douglas fir canopy in full gale is not the same as young forest. The complexity of old-growth -- multi-layered canopy, centuries of structural differentiation -- creates broadband turbulence noise that young plantation forest does not replicate. This detail matters for accuracy.
4. **The Mount Rainier summit acoustic analysis is the most novel section.** The summit at 14,411 feet vocalizing during extreme storm -- continuous broadband wind roar, structural vibration of rock and ice, infrasound from katabatic flows -- is not commonly documented. The summit as a sound source, not merely a geographic marker, is a useful framing.

## Discoveries

1. **Atmospheric rivers vs. bomb cyclones as distinct extreme event types produce different acoustic signatures.** An atmospheric river (narrow corridor of concentrated moisture from the tropical Pacific) produces prolonged, intense precipitation across the entire vertical range. A bomb cyclone (rapidly deepening low-pressure system, 24 mbar pressure drop in 24 hours) produces extreme wind speeds and rapid precipitation onset. Both can generate Cascades Full Storm conditions, but the wind-dominant bomb cyclone produces more extreme summit acoustics while the moisture-dominant atmospheric river produces more extreme flooding acoustics.
2. **Puget Sound storm surge is the least-discussed acoustic element of Cascade storms.** When extreme Pacific systems drive down the Cascades and over the lowlands, Puget Sound's enclosed geometry allows surge to develop -- water piling up against the sound's eastern shores. The surge creates wave action on otherwise sheltered water and produces breaking-wave acoustics normally associated with exposed Pacific coast. Puget Sound in surge mode sounds different from its normal state; the acoustic difference is the storm's acoustic signature at sea level.
3. **The vertical range of 14,411 feet within a single storm system is genuinely rare.** Most storm documentation focuses on a single location or elevation. The Cascade Range's combination of extreme summit altitude (Rainier at 14,411 ft, Adams at 12,281 ft, Hood at 11,249 ft) with sea-level tidal waters within 100 miles horizontal distance means a single Pacific system can simultaneously generate Arctic-level summit conditions and tidal-zone storm conditions. This is unusual even in global context.
