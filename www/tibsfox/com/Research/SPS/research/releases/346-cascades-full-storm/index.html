The Entire Mountain Range from Summit to Sea in a Single Extreme Storm

Degree 346 is the first SPS entry to reach E=10 — the absolute ceiling. The Cascades Full Storm is not a single weather event but the complete simultaneous expression of a major Pacific atmospheric river or bomb cyclone striking the full Cascade Range: Mount Rainier's summit at 14,411 feet in 100-150 mph winds and blizzard; subalpine zones under heavy snow accumulation; old-growth montane forests flexing under gale force; Cascade rivers approaching flood stage; Puget Sound in surge mode. All simultaneously. All of it sound. The most extreme meteorological event in the Pacific Northwest system, covering the full geographic range from the highest point in Washington to sea level. The Storm Types That Produce E=10

Atmospheric River

Narrow corridor of tropical Pacific moisture transported by the jet stream. The largest carry water vapor equivalent to the Amazon River discharge. Striking the Cascades: 2-4 in/hr rain at sea level, 1-3 in/hr snow water equivalent at 5,000 ft, 3-6 in/hr snow at 7,000-10,000 ft. Catastrophic flooding throughout Cascade drainages.

Bomb Cyclone

Explosive deepening: central pressure drops 24+ millibars in 24 hours. Pacific Northwest bomb cyclones produce 60-80 mph sustained winds at sea level, 100-150 mph at summit elevations. Hurricane-force conditions across the full range. When an atmospheric river and bomb cyclone arrive simultaneously: E=10.

The Old-Growth Forest in the Storm

The montane zone of the Washington Cascades contains some of the largest trees in North America. Douglas firs with trunk diameters of 6-10 feet and heights of 150-250 feet. Western red cedars with base diameters exceeding 15 feet and ages exceeding 1,000 years. During a major storm event, these trees flex under wind loads that approach their structural limits. The acoustic event of old-growth Douglas fir in gale-force wind is unlike anything in a managed or second-growth forest: the creak and groan of trunks at the lower limit of their structural tolerance, the roar of wind through canopies of enormous complexity, the percussion of limbs and debris falling through the understory. This is living architecture under extreme stress, producing sounds that have no parallel in the acoustic catalog of the PNW ecosystem.

The engine has documented individual components of this acoustic environment elsewhere: old-growth windstorm (degree 338) at E=9 documented the forest alone. Degree 346 embeds the forest storm within the complete summit-to-sea acoustic event — one layer in a stack that extends from the Rainier summit at 14,411 feet to Puget Sound at sea level.

Ecological Response — Who Experiences the Storm

Mountain goats (Oreamnos americanus): The most storm-adapted large mammal in the Cascades. When the storm hits at E=10 intensity, mountain goats move to steep rocky terrain where snow accumulation is minimal and the rock provides thermal mass. They do not leave the alpine zone; they find the one configuration of geography that allows them to withstand it.

Roosevelt elk (Cervus canadensis roosevelti): Move to lower elevations and sheltered terrain in old-growth forest. The dense old-growth canopy intercepts precipitation and wind, creating shelter zones that elk seek during extreme events. The herds cluster in these zones, vocalizing less than usual, waiting for the storm's passage.

Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha): The fall storm season coincides with late Chinook migration. Flooding rivers both assist migration (higher water removes obstacles) and create hazards (turbulence, debris, altered channel structure). The river and the salmon are simultaneous occupants of the same flooding system.

The silence after: When the storm passes, the acoustic transition is abrupt and profound. The wind drops. The precipitation stops. The forest settles into the sound of dripping water and the creak of settling snow. The contrast between the E=10 storm and the post-storm stillness is one of the most dramatic acoustic transitions in the PNW environment. The engine documents the storm; the silence belongs to the next moment.
