## THE RHINOCEROS HORN

### A Unique Bill Structure

The Rhinoceros Auklet's most distinctive feature is the **horn-like protuberance** at the base of the upper bill:

- **Structure:** A vertical, horn-shaped casque of keratinized tissue at the base of the upper mandible. Present in breeding adults. Absent or greatly reduced in juveniles and non-breeding adults.
- **Function:** The horn's function is debated. Likely serves as a signal of breeding condition -- larger horns may indicate better condition. May also serve in species recognition.
- **Name origin:** "Rhinoceros" directly refers to this horn. *Cerorhinca* from Greek *keras* (horn) + *rhynchos* (bill). *Monocerata* from Greek *monos* (single) + *keras* (horn).
- **Visual:** In breeding plumage, the bird also has white plumes behind the eye and at the base of the bill, giving it a distinguished, almost scholarly appearance. The orange bill with its horn is unmistakable.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; alcid morphology; engine internal analysis)

## E=4 -- THE NOCTURNAL COLONY CHORUS

### Why E=4

The Rhinoceros Auklet registers at E=4:

- **Colony sound:** Individual calls are grunts and moans -- not particularly loud. But a colony of hundreds or thousands produces a collective sound that is eerie and substantial. The colony is the instrument, not the individual.
- **Nocturnal context:** The calls are given at night, which amplifies their perceived intensity. In darkness, sound dominates. The colony chorus at night on an offshore island is an immersive acoustic experience.
- **Deep frequency:** The grunts and moans are lower-frequency than the Pigeon Guillemot's thin whistle. Deeper. More resonant. More felt than heard at distance.
- **Volume:** Individual birds are moderate. The colony aggregation is louder. E=4 reflects the collective nocturnal chorus -- louder and more immersive than the guillemot's E=3 whistle, but not the piercing broadcast of the Scolopacidae.
- **Why not E=5?** The sound is diffuse -- a collective chorus rather than a single clear call. Individually the calls are moderate. The colony effect elevates to E=4 but doesn't reach the piercing alarm quality of E=5.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; colony acoustics; engine internal analysis)

## NOCTURNAL COLONY BEHAVIOR

### The Night Shift

The Rhinoceros Auklet's nocturnal behavior is a defining adaptation:

- **Predator avoidance:** By visiting the colony only at night, Rhinoceros Auklets avoid avian predators (Bald Eagles, Peregrine Falcons, large gulls) that hunt by sight during the day. The darkness is protection.
- **Arrival waves:** On breeding islands, auklets arrive in waves after dark. The ocean around the island transitions from empty to full of floating auklets waiting for full darkness.
- **Burrow nesting:** Unlike the Pigeon Guillemot's crevice nesting, the Rhinoceros Auklet digs burrows -- 1-5 meters deep into island soil. The burrow is excavated by the bird using bill and feet.
- **Fish carrying at night:** Auklets return to the colony carrying multiple fish crosswise in their bill -- the same carrying behavior as the guillemot, but performed in total darkness. Navigation to the correct burrow in darkness among thousands of burrows is remarkable.
- **Protection Island:** Washington's Protection Island (in the Strait of Juan de Fuca) hosts one of the largest Rhinoceros Auklet colonies in the world -- approximately 36,000 nesting pairs. The island is a National Wildlife Refuge.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Protection Island NWR; Puget Sound seabird ecology; engine internal analysis)

## BURROW NESTING AND ISLAND ECOLOGY

### Underground Seabird

- **Burrow depth:** 1-5 meters. Dug into island soil or under tree roots. Single egg laid at the end of the tunnel.
- **Island dependency:** Rhinoceros Auklets are strictly island breeders -- they require predator-free or predator-reduced islands with soil deep enough for burrow excavation.
- **Threats:** Introduced predators (rats, raccoons, mink) on nesting islands are catastrophic. A single predator introduction can eliminate an entire colony. Island predator control is critical conservation.
- **PNW colonies:** Protection Island (36,000 pairs), Destruction Island, Smith Island, and several San Juan Islands sites. Washington holds globally significant breeding populations.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; USFWS Protection Island NWR; PNW seabird conservation; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Rhinoceros Auklet

**Grunting Moaning Colony:**
- **Trigger:** Colony return at night. Mate communication. Burrow defense.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** Deep, grunting, moaning vocalizations. "Mmmrraah" and "urrr-urrr-urrr." Individual calls are moderate; colony effect is immersive. Eerie nocturnal chorus.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 500-2000 Hz. Low for a seabird. Deep and resonant.
- **Duration:** Individual calls 0.5-2 seconds. Colony chorus continuous for hours after dark.
- **Amplitude:** E=4. Collective nocturnal effect. Moderate individually, immersive as colony. Eerie in darkness.

**Other Sounds:**
- Bill clacking during burrow defense.
- Soft grunting between mates inside burrow.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; colony acoustics; engine internal analysis)
