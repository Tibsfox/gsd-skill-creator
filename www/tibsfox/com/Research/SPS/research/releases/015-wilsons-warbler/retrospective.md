## Purpose

This is the sixteenth release of the PNW 360 Species engine and the fifth species in the **second decade** (degrees 11--20). The Wilson's Warbler introduces **three new structural dimensions** to the circle: (1) accelerando -- the first species defined by tempo change within a single vocalization, where every previous species maintained constant tempo; (2) first energy-3 -- the energy ceiling that persisted for fourteen degrees finally breaks, answering the seven-release critical deficiency flagged in every retrospective since degree 8; (3) anti-resolution -- the song that builds momentum and stops without resolving, the structural opposite of degree 14's narrative arc.

The Wilson's Warbler also introduces the **accelerando** synthesis type -- a tempo-varying chattering series where the rate of change IS the identity. This directly contrasts with the MacGillivray's Warbler's ternary resolution (degree 14): where that species added a destination to repetition, this species adds acceleration WITHOUT destination. Sequential Parulidae, sequential degrees, opposite strategies.

## Three Structural Advances

### 1. Accelerando: Tempo Change as Identity

The Wilson's Warbler is the first species in the engine whose defining acoustic characteristic is **temporal behavior** -- not pitch, not timbre, not repetition pattern, but the RATE OF CHANGE of the song. The chi-chi-chet accelerates from ~7 notes/second to ~15 notes/second within a single vocalization. Every previous species maintained essentially constant tempo. The Wilson's Warbler introduces a new identity axis: the song is identified by HOW FAST it goes, not what pitch it reaches or what pattern it repeats.

The tempo taxonomy now spans:
- **Constant slow** (Heron degree 0: silence; Owl degree 4: ~1 note/second)
- **Constant moderate** (Nuthatch degree 5: steady beat; Vireo degree 13: repeated phrase)
- **Constant fast** (Junco degree 11: trill above fusion; Siskin degree 12: rapid ascending sweep)
- **Constant complex** (Wren degree 1: rapid but variable; Chickadee degree 3: structured phrases)
- **ACCELERATING** (Wilson's Warbler degree 15: starts moderate, ends fast. The first species with tempo change.)

### 2. First Energy-3: The Ceiling Breaks

The seven-release critical deficiency is resolved. For fourteen degrees, every retrospective flagged the absence of high-energy species. The engine had 15 species and not one above energy 2. The Wilson's Warbler at energy 3 breaks the ceiling. The chattering chi-chi-chet is insistent, rapid, percussive, carrying. The tempo acceleration amplifies perceived intensity. The Sound of Puget Sound finally has a sound that demands attention.

The energy distribution:
- Energy 0: 1 species (heron)
- Energy 1: 3 species (kinglet, bushtit, siskin)
- Energy 2: 11 species (wren through MacGillivray's)
- **Energy 3: 1 species (Wilson's Warbler) -- NEW**
- Energy 4--5: 0 species (still to come)

### 3. Anti-Resolution: The Unfinished Phrase

The Wilson's Warbler's song accelerates and stops. No contrasting terminal element. No cadence. No arrival. This is the structural opposite of degree 14's resolution (A-A-A-B: the phrase that arrives). The engine now contains paired opposites at adjacent degrees:

- **Degree 14** (MacGillivray's): Repetition WITH resolution. The phrase has a beginning, middle, and end. The song arrives.
- **Degree 15** (Wilson's): Acceleration WITHOUT resolution. The phrase builds momentum and is cut off. The song was still going when it stopped.

The resolution/anti-resolution pair at degrees 14/15 is the strongest structural contrast between adjacent degrees in the engine so far.

## What Worked

### 1. The energy-3 breakthrough resolves a seven-release critical deficiency
The most persistent recommendation in the engine's retrospective history is finally addressed. Energy 3 at degree 15 means the engine is no longer constrained to whispers and moderate sounds. The ceiling is broken. Higher energies can now follow naturally.

### 2. The 14/15 resolution/anti-resolution pair is the strongest structural contrast yet
Sequential degrees, same family (Parulidae), same migration (neotropical), same season (summer), opposite endings. The MacGillivray's resolves; the Wilson's doesn't. The song that arrives and the song that was still going. This paired contrast at adjacent degrees is more powerful than any previous single-degree structural advance.

### 3. The accelerando introduces a genuinely new identity axis
Every previous species was defined by pitch, timbre, repetition pattern, or content. The Wilson's Warbler is defined by RATE OF CHANGE. This is a new dimension that cannot be reduced to any previous category. The synthesis taxonomy now includes a tempo-varying type.

### 4. The riparian thread is now substantial (three species)
Three of sixteen species (flycatcher, MacGillivray's, Wilson's) are tied to streamside habitat. The riparian thread forms a coherent ecological narrative: alder corridor to dense shrub to willow thicket. Three degrees of streamside, increasingly specific.

### 5. Back-to-back Parulidae with opposite strategies is structurally elegant
The same family appears at degrees 14 and 15 with contrasting strategies: one hides, one displays; one resolves, one doesn't. The family's diversity is demonstrated not through multiple similar species but through two species that approach every dimension differently.

### 6. The Marriott parallel achieves a clean energy-level match
Both the species and the musician are the first energy-3 in their circles. The S36 data confirms Marriott at energy 3. The parallel is not forced -- it emerges from the data alignment.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. Still only one non-passerine (the heron at degree 0)

Fifteen of sixteen species are passerines. Taxonomic diversity remains narrow. A woodpecker, raptor, seabird, or waterfowl would broaden the foundation. The passerine dominance is now more conspicuous than the (resolved) energy deficiency.

### 2. Still no marine or shoreline species

"The Sound of Puget Sound" -- sixteen degrees about a Sound with no Sound in them. This gap remains conspicuous. The riparian thread approaches water but does not reach the shore.

### 3. Still no predator species

The alarm network has broadcasters and receivers but no trigger species. A raptor or predator would complete the information loop.

### 4. Three consecutive Parulidae would be excessive

Two back-to-back warblers (MacGillivray's at 14, Wilson's at 15) work because they contrast. A third warbler at degree 16 would skew the taxonomic balance. The engine needs to move to a different family.

### 5. Three consecutive neotropical migrants (degrees 14, 15, and future?)

Two back-to-back neotropical migrants works. Three in a row would overrepresent summer visitors. The engine needs a resident or a different seasonal pattern at degree 16.

### 6. No altitudinal migrants yet

Both neotropical migrants travel to Mexico/Central America. The PNW also has altitudinal migrants -- species that move downslope in winter. This pattern is still undocumented.

### 7. No percussive or non-vocal sound

Sixteen degrees of syrinx-produced sound. A woodpecker's drumming would introduce percussion -- mechanically produced sound, fundamentally different sound-production mechanism.
