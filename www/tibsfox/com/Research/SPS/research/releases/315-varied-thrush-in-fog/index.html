First Turdidae Single-Note — E=7 — The Quiet After the Storm

The most iconic single-note bird sound in the Pacific Northwest. One bird. One pure sustained tone. Silence. A different pitch. Silence again. The Varied Thrush sings into fog-filled canyons in old-growth winter, and the fog and the canyon walls extend the note beyond its natural duration, hanging it in the cold air. Degree 315 is the first Turdidae entry documenting the single-note call type. All prior Turdidae in the engine sing complex melodies. The Varied Thrush sings one note. The simplicity is the point.

The Song — One Note Into Fog

Structure: A single sustained pure tone (1–3 seconds), followed by silence (5–15 seconds), followed by a single pure tone at a different pitch. Repeat. No melody in the conventional sense — no phrase, no development, no resolution. Just notes, separated by silence.

Frequency: 1,800–5,000 Hz per note (varies by individual and note position). Remarkably pure: very low harmonic content compared to most passerine songs. The purity is what makes the note stand out in cold fog. It does not sound like most birds. It sounds like something between a musical instrument and a forest spirit.

Pitch variation: No two consecutive notes are at the same pitch. The range across a full singing sequence covers approximately 1.5 octaves. Each note arrives at an unexpected pitch, creating a melodic sequence without a recognizable melodic pattern.

The reverb: In fog-filled canyon terrain, the pure tone reflects off canyon walls and tree masses and extends beyond its natural duration. The fog itself softly diffuses the high-frequency note. The winter silence — no insects, no breeding birds, minimal wind — means the note stands in near-silence with exceptional clarity before fading and giving way to the canyon’s quiet.

The Fog-Filled Canyon

The specific acoustic environment of this entry is not incidental. Western Washington winter fog — advection fog pooling in the drainage systems of the Coast Range and western Cascades — creates the concert hall. The fog reduces visibility to tens of meters. The canyon walls trap and amplify sound. The dense coniferous canopy provides overhead reflection. The flowing river provides an ambient acoustic baseline of white noise against which the Varied Thrush’s pure tone stands in complete contrast.

The Varied Thrush occupies precisely this habitat in winter: lower elevation fog zones in the western slope forest, riparian corridors, old-growth canyon systems. The fog and the bird have a natural ecological relationship. The habitat produces the acoustic event; the acoustic event is the habitat expressed in sound.

The Species

Ixoreus naevius, Gmelin, 1789. The only member of its genus. Body length 9.5–10 inches — large for a thrush. Blue-gray upperparts, warm orange underparts, broad orange supercilium, orange wingbar. Males: bold dark breast band. Females: narrower band, slightly duller. Unmistakable in the field — one of the most striking-looking birds in the PNW winter landscape.

Breeds in old-growth and mature forest of the Pacific mountains from northern California through Alaska. Winters along the Pacific coast, most abundantly in Washington, Oregon, and northern California. In Washington: present year-round in the Coast Range and western Cascades, descending to lower elevations in winter.
