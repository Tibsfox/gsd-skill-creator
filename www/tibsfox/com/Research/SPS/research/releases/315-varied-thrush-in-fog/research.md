## FIRST TURDIDAE -- WHY THIS FAMILY MATTERS

### The Thrush Family in the PNW Engine

Turdidae -- the true thrushes -- is one of the most ecologically and acoustically significant bird families in the Pacific Northwest. The family includes some of the most complex and beautiful songbirds in the North American avifauna. The SPS engine has documented:

- Swainson's Thrush (degree 018): dense forest understory, ascending spiral song
- Veery (degree 019): descending veer-like spiral, riparian
- Hermit Thrush (degree 007): the acclaimed forest flute
- American Robin (degree 025): the most widely distributed Turdidae, dawn chorus anchor

The Varied Thrush at degree 315 is the first Turdidae entry to document the single-note call type -- the haunting, long-held tone -- rather than the complex melodic song structure that characterizes Swainson's Thrush, Hermit Thrush, and Veery. This is also the first winter Turdidae: Swainson's and Hermit Thrushes are neotropical migrants, absent in winter. The Varied Thrush is a resident and short-distance migrant, most present in the lowland fog zones during winter.

The Varied Thrush is, arguably, the most iconic bird sound in the Pacific Northwest winter. The Hermit Thrush is more acclaimed by ornithologists for song complexity. But the Varied Thrush's single, suspended note, delivered in winter fog, is the sound that stops PNW hikers in their tracks in February and makes them stand still for a moment before they remember where they were going.

## *Ixoreus naevius* -- THE SPECIES

### The Robin That Is Not a Robin

The Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*, Gmelin, 1789) is the only member of the genus *Ixoreus*. It is large for a thrush: body length 9.5-10 inches (24-25 cm), comparable to an American Robin but with a dramatically different appearance:

- **Field marks.** Orange-and-gray coloration: blue-gray upperparts, warm orange underparts, a distinctive orange supercilium (eyebrow stripe), and an orange wing bar. Males have a broad dark breast band; females have a narrower or incomplete breast band and somewhat duller coloration overall. Unmistakable when seen -- one of the most striking-looking birds in the PNW winter landscape.
- **Taxonomic note.** The Varied Thrush was formerly placed in the genus *Zoothera* (typical thrushes). Its current monotypic genus placement in *Ixoreus* reflects its distinct genetic and morphological position within the Turdidae. It is most closely related to the genera *Catharus* (the North American spotted thrushes: Hermit, Swainson's, Veery, Gray-cheeked, Bicknell's) and *Hylocichla* (Wood Thrush).
- **Range.** Breeds in the mountains and coastal forests of the Pacific Northwest and Alaska, from northern California through British Columbia, the Yukon, and Alaska. Winters in the lowlands of the Pacific coast: Oregon, Washington, and northern California. In Washington, the Varied Thrush is present year-round in the Coast Range and western Cascades, with lowland winter concentrations in the Coast Range foothills and the Puget Sound lowlands.
- **Habitat.** Breeds in old-growth and mature forest, particularly in the western Cascade and Olympic forest zones. Prefers steep, heavily vegetated terrain with dense understory and abundant moisture. In winter, descends to lower elevations, using suburban and urban areas with large conifers but maintaining a preference for natural forest when available.

(Sources: Ixoreus naevius taxonomy Gmelin 1789; monotypic genus; body length 24-25 cm; field marks: orange-gray, breast band, supercilium; taxonomic history: Zoothera; closest relatives Catharus, Hylocichla; breeding range: California through Alaska; winter range: Pacific coast lowlands; Washington year-round old-growth presence; winter lowland descension)

## THE SONG -- THE MOST BEAUTIFUL SIMPLICITY IN PNW ORNITHOLOGY

### One Note. Silence. A Different Note.

The Varied Thrush's song is the structural opposite of the Hermit Thrush's or Swainson's Thrush's complex, cascading melodies. It is:

- **Structure.** A single, pure tone, sustained for 1-3 seconds, followed by silence of 2-6 seconds, followed by another single tone at a different pitch. Then silence. Then another tone. The sequence is not rapid; it is not complex; it does not develop melodically across multiple phrases. It is a series of isolated, pure tones at different pitches, separated by silence, proceeding without apparent melodic structure.
- **Frequency.** Each individual note is remarkably pure: fundamental frequency 1,800-5,000 Hz, depending on the individual and the note in the sequence. Very low harmonic content compared to most passerine songs. The purity of the note -- the low harmonic content, the clean onset and sustained hold -- is what makes it so distinctive. It does not sound like most birds. It sounds like something between a musical instrument and a forest spirit.
- **Pitch variation.** The Varied Thrush changes pitch between successive notes. No two consecutive notes are at the same pitch. The range of pitches used in a full singing sequence covers 1,800-5,000 Hz (approximately 1.5 octaves). The effect: each note arrives at an unexpected pitch, creating a melodic sequence without a melodic pattern.
- **Duration and spacing.** Individuals may sing for extended periods in the same general location, delivering notes every 5-15 seconds. The long silence between notes is an essential element of the acoustic character: the note fades into the fog, and the silence that follows allows the forest to return to its ambient state before the next note arrives.
- **The reverberation effect.** In a fog-filled canyon with old-growth tree cover on the slopes, the Varied Thrush's pure tone does not simply fade -- it reflects off the canyon walls and the tree masses and reverberates, extending the apparent duration of the note and filling the acoustic space with the note's frequency. The combination of long-held pure tone + canyon reverb + fog diffusion produces an acoustic experience that is unusual in the natural world.

(Sources: Varied Thrush song structure; single pure tone; frequency 1800-5000 Hz; low harmonic content; pitch variation between notes; silence as compositional element; note spacing 5-15 seconds; canyon reverb effect; fog diffusion acoustic effect)

## THE FOG-FILLED CANYON -- THE ACOUSTIC SPACE

### Winter PNW Fog: The Concert Hall

The Varied Thrush's association with fog-filled canyons is not coincidental:

- **The fog ecology.** Western Washington and Oregon in winter are characterized by advection fog: marine air masses moving inland, encountering the cooler land surface, producing persistent low-level fog in the river valleys and canyon bottoms. This fog is dense, persistent, and particularly concentrated in the drainage systems of the Coast Range and western Cascades. The canyons that cut through these mountains are fog channels -- the fog pools in the valley bottoms, hangs in the trees, and reduces visibility to tens of meters.
- **The acoustic effect of fog.** Fog does not simply reduce visibility; it alters the acoustic properties of the air. Water droplets in dense fog absorb and scatter high-frequency sound more strongly than they scatter lower-frequency sound. For the Varied Thrush's song (1,800-5,000 Hz), this creates a subtle diffusion effect: the high-frequency pure tone, passing through fog, gains a slight softness, a slightly widened apparent source. The effect is not dramatic but contributes to the quality listeners describe as "haunting."
- **The canyon reverb.** Canyon geometry creates natural reverberation. Sound produced on the canyon floor reaches the forested slopes, reflects, and returns to the valley. The reverb time in a typical western Cascade canyon depends on the canyon width and the amount of absorbing material (dense vegetation absorbs more than rock walls), but can extend 0.5-2 seconds for the Varied Thrush's frequency range. The reverb tail of a Varied Thrush note in a fog-filled canyon creates the impression of the note extending beyond its natural duration -- hanging in the air longer than it should, fading slowly rather than cutting off.
- **The winter silence.** The Varied Thrush's winter songs are delivered into a forest with significantly reduced ambient noise compared to spring and summer: no insect sound, no frog chorus, no breeding songbird activity, minimal wind in the dense fog. The signal-to-noise ratio in the winter foggy canyon is extremely high, which means the Varied Thrush's pure tone stands out against the near-silence with exceptional clarity.

(Sources: Advection fog western Washington winter; Coast Range and Cascade fog concentration; fog acoustic effect: water droplet absorption high-frequency; canyon geometry reverb; reverb time western Cascade canyons 0.5-2 sec; winter acoustic silence: no insects, frogs, breeding birds; high signal-to-noise ratio winter fog)

## WINTER ECOLOGY AND OLD-GROWTH DEPENDENCE

### Why Old-Growth Matters for This Species

The Varied Thrush's preference for old-growth and mature forest during breeding and its use of old-growth riparian corridors during winter is documented in the literature:

- **Breeding habitat.** Old-growth requirement for breeding: large-diameter trees with high canopy closure, complex understory, abundant moisture. The Olympic Peninsula and western Cascade old-growth zones are primary breeding habitat.
- **Winter movement.** In severe winters, Varied Thrushes move downslope and into lower-elevation forests, parks, and urban areas. During mild PNW winters, many individuals remain in the Coast Range foothills and lower western Cascades throughout.
- **Old-growth as winter corridor.** Riparian old-growth corridors -- the river bottom forest that is also elk rut habitat (degree 314) -- provide winter food (fallen fruit, ground invertebrates under leaf litter) and cover for Varied Thrushes descending from the breeding elevation.
- **The fog canyon as winter habitat.** The fog-filled canyons that produce the specific acoustic environment this entry documents are most commonly found in the western slope canyon systems of the Cascades and Coast Range -- precisely the habitat the Varied Thrush uses during winter. The fog is not incidental to the habitat selection; it is a feature of the winter ecology that the Varied Thrush occupies.

(Sources: Varied Thrush old-growth breeding habitat; Olympic Peninsula and western Cascade zones; winter downslope movement; severe winter urban use; riparian corridor winter food: fruit, ground invertebrates; fog canyon habitat selection; fog as winter habitat feature)
