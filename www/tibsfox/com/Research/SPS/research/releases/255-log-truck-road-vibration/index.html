*** FIRST ANTHROPOGENIC SOUND IN THE SPS ENGINE ***
Degree 255 is the first entry in the Anthropogenic family. After 254 degrees of biophony, geophony, and one maritime infrastructure entry, the SPS engine documents its first purely human-caused industrial sound: the log truck on the Pacific Northwest forest road.

80,000 LBS ON GRAVEL — THE WORKING FOREST SPEAKS — E=3

A fully loaded log truck: 80,000 lbs gross vehicle weight, 40,000-60,000 lbs of Douglas fir, cedar, or hemlock on the bunks. Gravel forest road, constructed on natural terrain contours, surfaced with angular volcanic rock. Multiple drive axles, each tire compressing and rebounding over road irregularities at travel speed. The aggregate: a continuous low-frequency road percussion transmitted simultaneously through air and ground. E=3. The first anthropogenic sound in the SPS engine. The sound the working forest makes.

What "Anthropogenic" Means Here

In ecological acoustics, the soundscape divides into three categories: geophony (non-biological physical processes — wind, rain, waterfalls, tidal sounds), biophony (biological organisms — bird vocalizations, frog calls, fish sounds), and anthrophony (human activity). Degrees 000-254 have been geophony, biophony, and one maritime infrastructure entry (fog horn, 254). Degree 255 is the SPS series' first pure anthrophony: sound caused by human industrial activity. The log truck is the correct first entry. The Pacific Northwest logging industry has been the dominant anthropogenic force in the region's soundscape since the 1880s.

"The log truck on the forest road is the sound of the working relationship between Pacific Northwest civilization and Pacific Northwest forests. It has been part of the regional soundscape since the first ox teams hauled logs to the mill in the 1850s. It continues today."

The Sound -- Four Simultaneous Mechanisms

  • Tire-road contact noiseEach tire compresses/rebounds over gravel irregularities. Multiple drive axles, paired tires. Aggregate: continuous dense low-frequency percussion.
  • Ground vibrationAxle weight creates ground pressure that propagates as seismic vibration. Felt rather than heard at distances of hundreds of meters from road.
  • Structural vibrationCab, trailer frame, log load, binder chains all vibrate. Log load (irregularly shaped, loosely constrained) adds rattling and shifting.
  • Engine/exhaust noiseDiesel engine at high load on grades. Maximum load = maximum acoustic output from exhaust.

The Vehicle

Gross vehicle weight

80,000 lbs legal limit (federal highways). State logging permits allow more on designated routes. Among the heaviest vehicles on public roads.

Log load

40,000-60,000 lbs typical. 8-16 logs. Douglas fir, western red cedar, hemlock, Sitka spruce. Species determines density and legal load limit.

Forest road surface

Unpaved: gravel or compacted dirt. Built on natural terrain contours. Grades up to 10% on steep hillsides. Angular volcanic rock typical surface.

The Jake brake

Engine brake on steep descents: staccato "blat-blat-blat" exhaust. A separate acoustic event from cruise road vibration. Critical safety function.

The Pairing: Valley of the Giants

Valley of the Giants (S36 255) take their name from the old-growth Douglas fir grove in the Oregon Coast Range. The log truck road vibration is the sound of the industry that harvested most such groves. The stoner rock band contemplates the forest; the log truck works in it. Both are sounds from within the same Pacific Northwest landscape, heard from different positions. The band in the rehearsal space; the truck on the forest road. The contemplated giant trees; the harvested giant trees. Both at E=6 and E=3 respectively. The forest holds both.
