Cedar Waxwing

Bombycilla cedrorum · Family Bombycillidae (Waxwings) · NEW FAMILY — 17th in Engine
High Thin See-See-See Trilled · Fruiting Trees · Resident · Energy 4
In the mountain ash at the edge of the park, the tree is alive with birds — dozens of sleek, crested shapes passing berries from branch to beak in quick succession. A thin, high, shimmering chorus fills the air: see-see-see, see-see-see — not one voice but many, overlapping, blending into a crystalline whisper that sounds like the tree itself is vibrating. Warm brown plumage fading to gray, black masks, yellow tail bands, tiny drops of red wax on the wings. Bombycilla cedrorum. The silk-tail of the cedars. A new family enters the engine. After five swallows and a river of liquid song, the whisper arrives.

New Family: Bombycillidae (Waxwings) — 17th Family

Bombycillidae enters the engine for the first time — the 17th family across 32 degrees. Only 3 species worldwide: Cedar Waxwing, Bohemian Waxwing, Japanese Waxwing. The engine will hold 2 of 3 (66.7% of the family) — the highest proportional coverage of any family. Phylogenetically isolated: nearest relatives are silky-flycatchers and Palmchat, neither in the PNW. A new node on the taxonomic graph with no family-level neighbors.

Bombycilla = Latin bombyx (silk) + -cilla (tail). Cedrorum = of the cedars. Full name: “silk-tail of the cedars.” Named for texture (silk) + diet (cedar berries). The first genus in the engine named for tactile quality.

Waxwing Pair Beginning (Degrees 31–32)

Cedar Waxwing (31) + Bohemian Waxwing (32) form the engine’s third same-genus consecutive pair, after Catharus (18–19) and Tachycineta (27–28). Each pair explores a different contrast axis:

Song Bioacoustics: High Thin See-See-See Trilled

The Cedar Waxwing produces a high-pitched, thin, sibilant trill — a metallic whisper at the edge of audibility. Not a song: Cedar Waxwings are among the few passerines that lack true territorial song. The see-see-see is an innate contact call used for flock cohesion, feeding coordination, and predator alert. No territory to defend means no song to defend it with.

The 32nd acoustic element: the whisper. After the swallow quintet’s liquid/rough explorations, the engine introduces a third timbral category: sibilant. Not liquid, not rough — crystalline, thin, high, glassy. The sharpest frequency shift in the engine: from the Barn Swallow’s mid-wide 2–7.5 kHz to the Cedar Waxwing’s high-narrow 6–10 kHz. From the river to the wire.

Frugivory: The First Fruit-Defined Ecosystem

The first ecosystem in the engine defined by food source rather than habitat structure. Previous ecosystems: estuary, old-growth, farmland. “Fruiting trees” describes what the bird eats, not where it lives. ~84% fruit by volume in non-breeding diet. Gut passage 16–45 minutes. Hundreds of berries per bird per day. Key PNW fruits: mountain ash, serviceberry, hawthorn, holly, crabapple, madrone, Oregon grape.

Nomadic movements track fruit availability — no fixed territory, no migration route. Where the berries are, the waxwings are. Seed dispersal mutualism: plant provides food, bird provides seed transport. Ethanol risk: fermented berries (crabapple, mountain ash) cause documented intoxication and mortality — a cost of dietary specialization.

The Wax Tips: A Bird That Wears Its Diet

Red waxy tips on secondary feathers — modified keratin coated with carotenoid pigment (astaxanthin). Number increases with age: age signal driving assortative mating. Since the 1960s, orange tips observed from non-native honeysuckle berries (rhodoxanthin replaces astaxanthin). An introduced plant is literally recoloring a native bird. The Cedar Waxwing wears its diet on its wings.

Taxonomy: Bombycilla cedrorum

Bombycilla: Latin bombyx (silk) + -cilla (tail) = “silk-tail.” Cedrorum: Latin “of the cedars.” Named by Vieillot in 1808. Full binomial: “silk-tail of the cedars.” The first genus in the engine named for tactile quality. Bombycillidae is a small family — only 3 species worldwide. Phylogenetically placed in superfamily Bombycilloidea with silky-flycatchers and Palmchat.

17th family. The engine now spans 17 families across 32 degrees. Bombycillidae is phylogenetically isolated — no close relatives among the engine’s other 16 families.

Ecosystem: Fruiting Trees

Urban parks, residential yards, riparian corridors, forest edges — anywhere fruit-bearing trees and shrubs concentrate. The Cedar Waxwing’s habitat is a phenological event, not a geological feature. Mountain ash in October, holly in December, serviceberry in June. The flock follows the fruit. Year-round resident in the PNW, with nomadic movements within the region. Peak flock sizes in Seattle: 200–400 individuals in a single fruiting tree.

Conservation: Stable to Increasing

Unlike the declining Barn Swallow, the Cedar Waxwing is stable or slightly increasing (~0.2–0.5% per year). Favored by non-native fruit plantings (ornamental crabapple, holly, honeysuckle), edge habitat creation, and climate warming extending growing seasons. Broad breeding range from Alaska to Mexico buffers against local declines. One of the PNW’s conservation success stories — a native species that benefits from human landscape modification.

HIGH-FREQUENCY PARALLEL. Both thin, high, sibilant calls at 7–10 kHz. Kinglet = “threshold” (element 9). Waxwing = “whisper” (element 32).

SPS-010: Bushtit

MINIMUM SIGNAL PARALLEL. Both thin, quiet individually. Bushtit: one thread in silence. Waxwing: one voice in a flock chorus. Minimum per individual, maximum per collective.

SPS-025: American Robin

FRUGIVORY CONNECTION. Robin is seasonal frugivore. Waxwing is year-round specialist. Both use mountain ash, holly, crabapple. Specialist vs. generalist.
