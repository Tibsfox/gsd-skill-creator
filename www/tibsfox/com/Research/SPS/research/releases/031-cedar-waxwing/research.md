## Context: After the Swallow Quintet

The swallow quintet just closed. Five consecutive Hirundinidae across degrees 26 through 30 -- Purple Martin, Violet-green Swallow, Tree Swallow, Rough-winged Swallow, Barn Swallow -- with 100% density, zero gaps, four genera, and the type genus arriving last. The engine spent five degrees inside one family, mapping liquid and rough timbres, colonial and solitary nesting, high sky and low farmland. It was the most compressed family exploration in engine history. And now it is done.

Degree 31 must leave Hirundinidae. It does. But it does not leave families entirely. It enters a **new** family: **Bombycillidae**. The waxwings. This is the engine's **17th family** and the first entirely new family since Hirundinidae entered at degree 26. The engine left one family and immediately opened another -- not as a continuation of the swallow run, but as its structural counterweight. Where the swallows gave us five species across five consecutive degrees, the waxwings will give us two species across two consecutive degrees. The Cedar Waxwing at 31 and the Bohemian Waxwing at 32. A pair, not a quintet. The engine is learning to work in smaller family units after the five-degree commitment of the swallows.

The Cedar Waxwing also brings a **new ecosystem**: fruiting trees. This is the first species in the engine defined by its food source rather than by a habitat structure. The swallows were defined by where they nested and foraged -- sky, cliff, field, river bank, farmland. The Cedar Waxwing is defined by what it eats. Its presence in any landscape is determined not by terrain or structures but by fruit availability. Where the berries are, the waxwings are. This is the engine's first **frugivore** -- a species whose ecology is organized around fruit consumption.

And the sound: **high thin see-see-see trilled**. After the swallow quintet's liquid explorations -- gurgle, twitter, twittering gurgle, buzzy brrit, long twittering warble -- the Cedar Waxwing introduces something entirely different. Not liquid. Not rough. Not melodic in the warbling sense. The Cedar Waxwing's call is a high-pitched, thin, sibilant trill -- a metallic whisper, almost like a fine wire vibrating at the edge of audibility. It has an ethereal, glassy quality that no previous species in the engine has produced. The swallows worked in the mid-frequency liquid register. The Cedar Waxwing works higher, thinner, more delicate. The transition from degree 30 to degree 31 is a shift from rich, complex, sustained melodic warbling to thin, high, repetitive sibilance. From the river to the wire. From water to glass.

The Cedar Waxwing is the 30th unique species across 32 degrees. It brings the 17th family (Bombycillidae), the first frugivorous ecosystem, the beginning of a waxwing pair, and an acoustic quality -- the metallic whisper -- that opens new timbral territory after five degrees of swallow song.

## Taxonomy: The Silk-Tailed Berry Eater

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Bombycillidae (Waxwings)
- **Genus:** *Bombycilla*
- **Species:** *Bombycilla cedrorum* (Vieillot, 1808)
- **Common Name:** Cedar Waxwing
- **Etymology:** *Bombycilla* derives from the Latin *bombyx* ("silk" or "silkworm") and the Modern Latin suffix *-cilla* ("tail"), yielding "silk-tail" -- a reference to the smooth, sleek plumage that gives waxwings their distinctive satiny appearance [1, 2]. The species epithet *cedrorum* is Latin genitive plural meaning "of the cedars," referring to the eastern red cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*) whose blue-gray berries are a critical winter food source [1]. The common name "Cedar Waxwing" compounds this cedar association with "waxwing" -- a reference to the distinctive waxy red tips on the secondary flight feathers, unique droplets of modified keratin whose function has been debated for over two centuries.

The full binomial translates to **"silk-tail of the cedars."** A name built from texture (silk), anatomy (tail), and diet (cedar berries). Where the swallow genera named themselves by myth (*Progne*), behavior (*Tachycineta*), anatomy (*Stelgidopteryx*), and identity (*Hirundo*), the waxwing genus names itself by **texture** -- what the feathers feel like, not what the bird does. This is the first genus in the engine named for tactile quality. *Bombycilla* does not describe flight or feeding or singing. It describes the bird as an object: silk-tailed. The naming strategy is aesthetic, not functional.

None of these close relatives are PNW species. Bombycillidae stands alone in the engine's taxonomic graph -- an isolated node with no family-level neighbors.

The Cedar Waxwing at degree 31 begins a **waxwing pair** -- two consecutive Bombycillidae, mirroring the Tachycineta pair at degrees 27-28 (Violet-green Swallow + Tree Swallow). The structural echo is deliberate:

Both are frugivores, both are nomadic, both produce thin high-pitched calls. The pair explores how the same body plan and ecological strategy play out at different latitudes.

The waxwing pair also contrasts with the swallow quintet in scale: two species versus five, two degrees versus five, one genus versus four. The engine is compressing. After the most expansive family exploration in its history (5 in 5), it immediately delivers the most compact (2 in 2). The message: family representation need not be maximal. Two species from one genus can illuminate a family as clearly as five species from four genera -- differently, but clearly.

## Vocalization: The High Thin See-See-See

### A New Timbral Territory

The Cedar Waxwing's primary vocalization is a **high-pitched, thin, sibilant trill** -- typically transcribed as *see-see-see* or *sreee*. It is not a song in the traditional oscine sense. Cedar Waxwings are among the few passerines that lack a true territorial song [1, 6]. What they produce instead is a contact call -- a thin, high, trilled whistle used to maintain flock cohesion, communicate during feeding, and coordinate movements. This call is their primary vocalization and the sound by which they are identified in the field.

The call's acoustic properties place it in fundamentally different territory from anything the engine has produced in the swallow quintet:

- **Fundamental frequency range:** ~6.0-10.0 kHz. This is **higher than any swallow** in the engine. The Violet-green Swallow's upper range reached 9.0 kHz; the Cedar Waxwing's fundamental sits at 6-10 kHz, with energy often concentrated above 7 kHz [6, 7]. This is approaching the upper limit of comfortable human hearing for many adults. The sound is genuinely high-pitched -- not in the metaphorical sense of "bright" but in the literal sense of "near the edge of audibility for older listeners."
- **Peak energy band:** ~7.0-9.0 kHz. Extremely high. The Barn Swallow's peak energy was 3.0-6.0 kHz. The Cedar Waxwing's peak energy is nearly an octave higher. This is one of the highest-pitched calls of any common PNW passerine [6, 7].
- **Call duration:** 0.3-0.8 seconds per call. Very brief. The Barn Swallow's warble lasted 3-8 seconds. The Cedar Waxwing's call is an order of magnitude shorter. It is a fragment, not a performance.
- **Note rate:** The "trilled" quality comes from rapid frequency modulation within the call -- subtle wavering that gives the see-see-see its tremolo character. The modulation rate is approximately 15-30 Hz, fast enough to produce a perceived trill but not so fast as to blur into a continuous tone [6].
- **Note structure:** Narrow-bandwidth, high-frequency sibilant. The spectrogram shows a thin horizontal band -- not the wide, sweeping frequency modulations of the Barn Swallow's warble, but a tightly constrained tonal whisper with subtle internal frequency modulation [6, 7]. The sound is spectrally simple compared to the swallows' complex frequency-modulated notes.
- **Timbre:** Thin, glassy, metallic, sibilant. The sound has the quality of a fine wire being drawn through air -- not liquid, not rough, but something closer to crystalline vibration. Some observers describe it as "cedary" -- a dry, thin, slightly buzzy whistle that evokes the texture of cedar wood itself [1]. The timbre is unlike anything in the swallow quintet. It does not flow (liquid) or grate (rough). It shimmers.
- **Amplitude:** Low to moderate. The call does not project like the Barn Swallow's warble or the Purple Martin's gurgle. It is a close-range communication -- audible within a flock but not across a field. Energy 4 reflects the persistence and frequency of calling rather than volume. A flock of Cedar Waxwings produces a continuous thin chorus of see-see-see calls that fills the acoustic space through repetition, not through power.
- **Context:** Given almost continuously when flocks are active -- perched, feeding, flying. Cedar Waxwings are among the most vocal flock birds in the PNW. A feeding flock in a mountain ash tree produces a shimmering curtain of thin calls, each individual adding its own see-see-see to the collective texture. The sound is inseparable from the flock. A single Cedar Waxwing calling alone sounds thin and lost. A flock calling together sounds like the tree itself is vibrating.

### The 32nd Acoustic Element: The High Thin See-See-See

**The progression (32 elements):** silence -- cascade -- drone -- language -- motif -- metronome -- question -- harmonic spiral -- ultrasonic threshold -- variation -- minimum signal -- trill/tremolo -- sawtooth sweep -- ostinato -- repetition-resolution -- accelerando -- crescendo -- the hook -- ascending spiral -- descending spiral -- sustain/the release -- the hammer -- resonant percussion/groove reset -- the testimony/groove reset -- the invocation -- the congregation -- the caroling -- the liquid gurgle -- the liquid twitter -- the liquid twittering gurgle -- the rough brrit -- the liquid warble synthesis -- **the high thin see-see-see**.

The name for this 32nd element is **"the high thin see-see-see"** -- or, in the engine's one-word vocabulary: **"the whisper."** After the swallow quintet explored liquid in all its articulations and delivered one rough dissenter, the engine moves to something that is neither liquid nor rough. The Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see is **sibilant** -- a hissing, thin, high tonal whisper that introduces a third timbral category. The engine's timbral palette has been: tonal (degrees 0-25), liquid (degrees 26-28, 30), rough (degree 29). Now: **sibilant** (degree 31). A new color on the palette.

The shift from degree 30 to degree 31 is one of the sharpest timbral transitions in the engine:

The Barn Swallow sang to be heard as an individual -- a male on a wire, performing for territory and mate. The Cedar Waxwing calls to be heard as part of a flock -- an individual adding its voice to a collective texture. The swallow quintet was about individual virtuosity (culminating in the archetype's most complex solo). The waxwing pair will be about collective texture (many thin voices creating a shimmering field).

### Why Cedar Waxwings Lack True Song

The Cedar Waxwing is one of the few passerines that does not defend territories with song [1, 6]. Most oscine passerines (suborder Passeri) have a learned song used for territorial defense and mate attraction. The Cedar Waxwing does not. Its primary vocalization -- the see-see-see -- is innate, not learned. It does not vary geographically (no dialects). It does not become more complex with age. It does not change during the breeding season [1].

The absence of true song is tied to the species' **nomadic, non-territorial ecology**. Cedar Waxwings do not hold fixed territories. They are nomadic frugivores that move to wherever fruit is available. A bird that does not defend a territory does not need a territorial song. The see-see-see call serves the functions that matter for a nomadic flocking species: flock cohesion, feeding coordination, predator alarm. These functions require a simple, consistent, easily produced call -- not an elaborate song [1, 6].

This makes the Cedar Waxwing an **acoustic minimalist** in a family (Bombycillidae) and an order (Passeriformes) dominated by elaborate singers. The engine has explored vocal complexity (Barn Swallow's warble), vocal simplicity (Bushtit's seep), and vocal absence (Great Blue Heron's silence). The Cedar Waxwing adds a fourth category: **vocal sufficiency**. The see-see-see is exactly enough. Not more, not less. It does what needs doing and nothing else. In a landscape where warblers, thrushes, and swallows pour out complex songs, the Cedar Waxwing whispers -- and the whisper carries.

## Ecology: Frugivory and Nomadism

### The First Fruit-Defined Ecosystem in the Engine

The Cedar Waxwing's ecosystem is **fruiting trees**. This is the first ecosystem in the engine defined by a food source rather than a habitat structure. Previous ecosystems: estuary, old-growth forest, conifer canopy, riparian corridor, marsh edge, open farmland. All describe physical landscape features -- where the bird lives. "Fruiting trees" describes what the bird eats. The Cedar Waxwing's habitat is wherever fruit exists. It is not tied to a forest type, an elevation band, or a geological feature. It is tied to a phenological event: fruiting.

This makes the Cedar Waxwing the engine's first **nomadic frugivore** -- a species whose distribution is determined by the spatial and temporal pattern of fruit availability rather than by fixed habitat features [1, 8]. The bird goes where the food is. When the mountain ash in one neighborhood finishes fruiting, the flock moves to the holly in another neighborhood. When winter berries are exhausted in Seattle, the flock may move to Portland or Medford or wherever fruit remains. The Cedar Waxwing's "territory" is a moving target -- a shifting mosaic of fruiting events across the landscape.

### Cedar Berries: The Name Connection

The "cedar" in Cedar Waxwing refers to the eastern red cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*), a juniper species whose small blue-gray berry-like cones are a critical winter food source across eastern North America [1, 2]. The species was named *cedrorum* ("of the cedars") by Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot in 1808 based on this dietary association. In the PNW, the analogous food plants include western red cedar berries (seldom consumed), but more importantly Pacific madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), mountain ash (*Sorbus*), serviceberry (*Amelanchier*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), holly (*Ilex*), crabapple (*Malus*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia*), and various *Viburnum* species [1, 8].

The naming is significant because it links the bird to its food source. Most bird names reference appearance (Blue Jay), habitat (Marsh Wren), behavior (Flycatcher), or sound (Chickadee). "Cedar Waxwing" references **diet** -- the bird that eats cedar berries. The name tells you what the bird does, not where it lives or what it looks like. Combined with the genus *Bombycilla* ("silk-tail"), which references texture, the full common-and-scientific name describes a bird through **touch and taste**: a silky-feathered cedar-berry eater.

### Frugivory in Detail

Cedar Waxwings are among the most specialized frugivores in North American avifauna [1, 8]. Key aspects of their frugivorous ecology:

**1. Fruit dominance in diet.** During the non-breeding season (fall through early spring), fruit comprises approximately **84% of the diet** by volume [1, 8]. During breeding season, the proportion shifts as insects become important for feeding nestlings, but fruit remains the dominant food source for adults year-round. Few other North American passerines are this fruit-dependent [1].

**2. Fruit processing adaptations.** The Cedar Waxwing has a relatively wide gape for its body size, allowing it to swallow berries whole [1]. The digestive tract is short and efficient -- berries pass through in as little as 16-45 minutes [9]. This rapid processing means each bird must consume large quantities of fruit daily to meet caloric needs. A single Cedar Waxwing can eat several hundred berries per day [1, 8].

**3. Seed dispersal function.** Because berries pass through the gut quickly and seeds are deposited intact in droppings, Cedar Waxwings are among the most important **seed dispersers** in North American ecosystems [1, 8, 9]. They move seeds from fruiting trees to new locations, facilitating plant reproduction and forest regeneration. The bird-fruit mutualism is bidirectional: the plant provides food, the bird provides seed transport.

**4. Flocking behavior.** Cedar Waxwings are **obligate flock birds** during the non-breeding season [1]. Flocks range from a dozen to several hundred individuals. Flocking is tied to frugivory: concentrated fruit resources (a single tree laden with berries) can support a large flock for a brief period, then the flock moves on when the resource is exhausted. The boom-and-bust pattern of fruit availability drives the flocking-nomadic lifestyle.

**5. Nomadic movements.** Cedar Waxwing movements are **irruptive and unpredictable** [1, 5]. They do not follow fixed migration routes. In years of high fruit production, they may remain in northern latitudes through winter. In years of crop failure, they move southward in large numbers. Their presence in any location is determined by fruit availability, not by calendar. This makes them one of the most difficult PNW birds to predict -- here today, gone tomorrow, back next week when the holly fruits.

### Ethanol Intoxication: The Fermented Berry Problem

One of the Cedar Waxwing's most unusual ecological features is its susceptibility to **ethanol intoxication from fermented berries** [10, 11]. When late-season berries ferment on the branch (especially crabapples, mountain ash, and hawthorn), they can accumulate ethanol concentrations of 1-5% [10]. Cedar Waxwings, which consume large quantities of berries rapidly, can ingest enough ethanol to become visibly intoxicated -- showing disorientation, impaired flight, and loss of coordination [10, 11].

Cases of Cedar Waxwing mortality from ethanol intoxication are documented in the veterinary and ornithological literature [10, 11]. Necropsy studies have found liver damage consistent with acute ethanol toxicity. Flocks of intoxicated waxwings occasionally attract public attention when birds collide with windows or sit on the ground unable to fly.

The fermented berry problem is ecologically significant because it represents a **cost of frugivory** that most frugivores do not bear. A species that eats insects or seeds does not risk intoxication. A species that eats hundreds of berries per day from a potentially fermented source does. The Cedar Waxwing's extreme dietary specialization exposes it to a risk that generalist feeders avoid. The specialist pays a price the generalist does not.

The Cedar Waxwing has some physiological capacity to metabolize ethanol -- its liver produces alcohol dehydrogenase more efficiently than many passerines [10]. But this capacity has limits. When fermented berry availability coincides with cold weather (which slows ethanol metabolism), the risk of intoxication increases. The waxwing's body has evolved partial tolerance to a toxin that its diet creates. Evolution mitigating the cost of specialization.

### The Wax Tips: Red Secondary Tips

The "waxwing" in the common name refers to the small, brilliantly red, waxy tips on the secondary flight feathers [1, 2, 12]. These tips are unique among birds -- they are not pigmented feather barbs but modified keratin structures: flattened extensions of the feather shaft coated with a carotenoid pigment (astaxanthin) that produces the red color [12]. The tips look like drops of red sealing wax, hence "waxwing."

The number and size of wax tips increase with age [1, 12]. Young birds may have few or no red tips; older birds may have tips on all secondaries plus some tail feathers. The tips function as an **age signal** -- they advertise the bearer's maturity. Studies have shown that Cedar Waxwings preferentially associate with and mate with individuals that have similar numbers of wax tips, suggesting **assortative mating by age class** [12, 13].

Orange wax tips have been observed in Cedar Waxwings since the late 1960s, coinciding with the introduction of non-native honeysuckle (*Lonicera*) species with red-pigmented berries to North America [14]. The orange tips result from rhodoxanthin, a different carotenoid obtained from honeysuckle berries, being incorporated into the wax tips instead of astaxanthin. This is a **diet-driven plumage change** -- the bird's appearance changes based on what it eats. An introduced plant is literally recoloring a native bird. The Cedar Waxwing wears its diet on its wings.

## Physical Description

The Cedar Waxwing is a **medium-small passerine** (15-18 cm, 30-36 g) with an exceptionally sleek, smooth appearance [1, 2]. Key features:

- **Crest:** Prominent, pointed crest that gives the head a swept-back, aerodynamic profile. The crest can be raised or lowered expressively.
- **Mask:** Black mask extending from the base of the bill through the eye, bordered above by a thin white line. The mask gives the bird a bandit-like appearance.
- **Body plumage:** Warm brown fading to gray on the wings and tail. The plumage is uniquely smooth and silky -- individual feathers are barely distinguishable, giving the bird its "silk-tail" (*Bombycilla*) appearance.
- **Belly:** Pale yellow wash on the lower belly and undertail coverts.
- **Tail:** Medium-length tail with a bright yellow terminal band (sometimes orange, due to diet -- see wax tips section above). The yellow tail band is diagnostic.
- **Wax tips:** Red (or orange) waxy tips on secondary feathers. Variable in number.

The overall impression is of a bird designed by a graphic artist -- smooth gradients, clean lines, no harsh boundaries. The Cedar Waxwing is one of the most aesthetically unified birds in North America. Where the Barn Swallow had distinct color blocks (steel blue back, russet breast, dark throat), the Cedar Waxwing has continuous color gradients -- brown melting into gray melting into yellow, with precise accent marks (black mask, yellow tail band, red wax tips) placed like typographic elements.

## Conservation and Populations

### Status: Stable to Increasing

Unlike the Barn Swallow (declining ~1% per year), the Cedar Waxwing is **stable or slightly increasing** across most of its North American range [1, 15]. Breeding Bird Survey data show a slight long-term increase of approximately 0.2-0.5% per year since the 1960s, though trends vary regionally [15].

The Cedar Waxwing's population stability is likely tied to its **dietary flexibility** and its **tolerance of human-modified landscapes** [1]. Several factors favor the species:

**1. Non-native fruit.** The proliferation of non-native fruit-bearing plants in urban and suburban landscapes -- ornamental crabapples, European mountain ash, Amur honeysuckle, English holly, firethorn (*Pyracantha*) -- has increased winter fruit availability across much of North America [1, 8]. The Cedar Waxwing benefits directly from horticultural plantings that other species cannot exploit as efficiently.

**2. Edge habitat creation.** Forest fragmentation creates edge habitat with abundant fruiting shrubs. While fragmentation harms many forest-interior species, it benefits species like the Cedar Waxwing that feed on edge-associated fruit-bearing plants [1].

**3. Climate change.** Warming temperatures may be extending the growing season for some fruit species, potentially increasing food availability for waxwings in northern latitudes [1]. Additionally, milder winters may reduce mortality in resident and partially migratory populations.

**4. Broad range.** The Cedar Waxwing breeds across a vast range -- from southeastern Alaska and British Columbia across southern Canada and throughout the United States [1]. This geographic breadth buffers the species against localized declines.

### PNW Populations

In the Puget Sound region, Cedar Waxwings are **common year-round residents** [1, 16]. They are most conspicuous in fall and winter when large flocks gather in fruiting trees in urban parks, residential areas, and riparian corridors. Peak flock sizes in the Seattle area can reach 200-400 individuals concentrated in a single fruiting tree [16].

Breeding occurs from late May through August in the PNW, later than most passerines because the species times its nesting to coincide with peak summer fruit availability [1]. Nests are built in deciduous trees and conifers, typically in loose colonies of 3-15 pairs. The Cedar Waxwing is one of the latest-nesting passerines in the PNW -- sometimes raising young into September.

The species' year-round presence in the PNW makes it a **resident** in the engine's seasonal classification. This contrasts with the summer-only swallows (degrees 26-30, all migrants) and marks a return to resident status after five consecutive migrant/summer species.

The pair explores geographic range contrast: Cedar Waxwing (temperate resident) vs. Bohemian Waxwing (boreal irruptive).

- **SPS-027/028 (Tachycineta pair, degrees 27-28):** STRUCTURAL ECHO. The Tachycineta pair explored two species from one genus with contrasting ecology. The Bombycilla pair will explore two species from one genus with contrasting range. Same structural pattern, different contrasting axis.

- **SPS-026-030 (Swallow quintet):** QUINTET-TO-PAIR TRANSITION. After five consecutive Hirundinidae (100% density), the engine immediately enters two consecutive Bombycillidae. The scale contracts: quintet to pair. The density stays at 100%. The engine is exploring family representation at two different scales.

- **SPS-008 (Golden-crowned Kinglet, degree 8):** HIGH-FREQUENCY PARALLEL. The kinglet's ascending zee-zee-zee at degree 8 was the engine's previous high-frequency specialist (~7-10 kHz). The Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see operates in a similar register. Both are thin, high, sibilant. The kinglet was "ultrasonic threshold" (the 9th element). The waxwing is "the whisper" (the 32nd element). Both explore the upper edge of common human hearing.

- **SPS-010 (Bushtit, degree 10):** MINIMUM SIGNAL PARALLEL. The Bushtit's thin seep at degree 10 was the engine's acoustic floor -- minimum viable signal. The Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see is similarly thin and quiet individually, but it operates differently: where the Bushtit's seep was a single thread in silence, the Cedar Waxwing's call is one voice in a flock chorus. Minimum signal per individual, maximum signal per flock.

- **SPS-025 (American Robin, degree 25):** FRUGIVORY CONNECTION. The robin is a seasonal frugivore -- switching to berries in fall and winter. The Cedar Waxwing is a year-round frugivore that takes the robin's seasonal strategy to an extreme. The robin shares many fruiting trees with waxwings (mountain ash, holly, crabapple) but also eats earthworms and insects extensively. The waxwing is the specialist; the robin is the generalist.
