# Release 031 Retrospective -- Cedar Waxwing (Degree 31)

**Release:** 031 of 360
**Species:** Cedar Waxwing (*Bombycilla cedrorum*)
**Sound:** High thin see-see-see trilled
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the thirty-second release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-31), the first Bombycillidae in the engine, and the opening voice of the waxwing pair (degrees 31-32). The engine needed to leave the swallow quintet (closed at degree 30), introduce a new family, begin a new same-genus pair, bring the first fruit-defined ecosystem, open a new timbral category (sibilant), and extend or break the E=4 energy plateau.

Six structural contributions:

1. **Bombycillidae enters the engine.** The 17th family. A new evolutionary lineage with no close relatives among the engine's other 16 families. Only 3 species worldwide -- the engine will hold 2 of 3 (66.7%), the highest proportional family coverage in the engine. Genus *Bombycilla* ("silk-tail") is the first genus named for tactile quality rather than behavior, anatomy, or identity.

2. **The waxwing pair begins.** Cedar Waxwing (31) + Bohemian Waxwing (32) form the engine's third same-genus consecutive pair, after Catharus (18-19) and Tachycineta (27-28). Each pair explores a different contrast axis: spiral direction (Catharus), ecological niche (Tachycineta), geographic range (Bombycilla). The quintet-to-pair compression -- from 5 consecutive Hirundinidae to 2 consecutive Bombycillidae -- demonstrates that family representation need not be maximal.

3. **The sibilant timbral category opens.** The engine's timbral palette has been tonal (degrees 0-25) and liquid/rough (degrees 26-30). The Cedar Waxwing's high thin see-see-see introduces a third category: **sibilant** -- thin, high, crystalline, glassy. This is the sharpest frequency shift in the engine: from the Barn Swallow's mid-wide 2-7.5 kHz to the Cedar Waxwing's high-narrow 6-10 kHz. From the river to the wire.

4. **The first fruit-defined ecosystem.** "Fruiting trees" is the first ecosystem in the engine defined by food source rather than habitat structure. The Cedar Waxwing's habitat is a phenological event, not a geological feature. Where the berries are, the waxwings are. This introduces nomadism, phenological tracking, seed dispersal mutualism, and ethanol intoxication risk as ecological themes.

5. **Vocal sufficiency: a passerine without song.** The Cedar Waxwing is among the few Passeri (oscine passerines) that lack true territorial song. The see-see-see is an innate contact call, not a learned song. Nomadic frugivores that do not defend territory do not need territorial song. The call is exactly enough -- acoustic minimalism as an ecological strategy, not a limitation.

6. **The energy undecet -- eleventh consecutive E=4.** Degrees 21-31. Eleven degrees. The plateau refused to break at degree 31, extending from decet to undecet. The retrospective at degree 30 declared this "critically must break." It did not break. The degree 32 Bohemian Waxwing will either continue the streak to twelve or finally end it.

## The Transition: Swallow Quintet to Waxwing Pair

### From Five to Two

The transition from degree 30 to degree 31 is the sharpest structural boundary in the engine since the silence-to-cascade transition at degree 0-1. The changes are multi-dimensional:

| Dimension | Degree 30 (Barn Swallow) | Degree 31 (Cedar Waxwing) |
|---|---|---|
| Family | Hirundinidae (16th) | Bombycillidae (17th, NEW) |
| Family position | 5th of 5 (quintet CLOSED) | 1st of 2 (pair OPEN) |
| Timbre | Liquid (melodic warble) | Sibilant (thin trill) |
| Frequency | 2.0-7.5 kHz (mid-wide) | 6.0-10.0 kHz (high-narrow) |
| Duration | 3-8 seconds | 0.3-0.8 seconds |
| Function | Territory + mate (song) | Flock cohesion (contact call) |
| Ecosystem | Open farmland | Fruiting trees |
| Diet | Aerial insects | Fruit (~84%) |
| Social | Loosely colonial breeder | Nomadic obligate flock |
| Season | Summer migrant | Year-round resident |

Nearly every axis changed. The only constant is Energy = 4 and Order = Passeriformes. The engine crossed a major structural boundary while the energy floor held absolutely still.

### What the Quintet-to-Pair Compression Means

The swallow quintet (5 species, 5 degrees, 4 genera) was the engine's most expansive family exploration. The waxwing pair (2 species, 2 degrees, 1 genus) is among the most compact. The contrast is structural: the engine is demonstrating that family representation works at multiple scales. A family can be explored through five species across four genera (Hirundinidae), or through two species in one genus (Bombycillidae). The quintet approach maps the family's breadth. The pair approach maps within-genus variation. Both are valid. Both are informative. The engine uses both.

## Six Structural Advances

### 1. Bombycillidae: The 17th Family

A new family is a taxonomic event. The engine has added families at decreasing rates: families arrived rapidly in degrees 0-13 (14 families in 14 degrees, almost 1:1), then slowed to 1 new family every 3-5 degrees (Parulidae at 14, Picidae at 21, Cardinalidae at 23, Thraupidae at 24, Hirundinidae at 26, Bombycillidae at 31). The rate of new family acquisition is decelerating -- which is expected as the engine samples more of the PNW's taxonomic diversity. The easy families (large, common) were sampled first. The remaining families (smaller, more specialized) take longer to reach.

Bombycillidae is notable for being the engine's first **small family** -- only 3 species worldwide. Previous new families contained hundreds of species (Turdidae: ~174, Parulidae: ~119, Hirundinidae: ~90). Bombycillidae is tiny. And the engine will hold 2 of its 3 species (66.7% coverage), making it by far the most thoroughly sampled family proportionally.

### 2. The Waxwing Pair: Third Same-Genus Pair

The three same-genus pairs now in the engine each explore a different contrast axis:

| Pair | Degrees | Contrast Axis | What the Pair Teaches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Catharus | 18-19 | Spiral direction | Same genus, opposite acoustic motion |
| Tachycineta | 27-28 | Ecological niche | Same genus, different habitat strategy |
| Bombycilla | 31-32 | Geographic range | Same genus, different latitude/abundance |

The pairs are structurally similar (same genus, consecutive degrees, 100% density within the pair) but explore different dimensions of within-genus variation. The engine is building a library of how closely related species differ.

### 3. Sibilant: The Third Timbral Category

The engine's timbral vocabulary has expanded:

| Category | Degrees | Description | Introduced By |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tonal | 0-25 | Clear pitch, harmonic structure, melodic | Great Blue Heron through American Robin |
| Liquid | 26-28, 30 | Flowing, water-like, continuous modulation | Purple Martin (26) |
| Rough | 29 | Broadband, buzzy, grating | Rough-winged Swallow (29) |
| **Sibilant** | **31** | **Thin, high, crystalline, glassy** | **Cedar Waxwing (31)** |

The addition of sibilant as a fourth timbral category enriches the engine's descriptive power. Previous sounds could be classified as tonal (most), liquid (swallows), or rough (Rough-winged). The Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see is none of these -- it is a new thing. The palette grows.

### 4. Frugivory as Ecosystem: A New Organizing Principle

"Fruiting trees" as an ecosystem introduces a fundamentally different organizing principle for the engine. Previous ecosystems described places (estuary, old-growth, farmland). This ecosystem describes a resource (fruit). The implication: the Cedar Waxwing's "place" changes with the seasons and with crop success. Its habitat is mobile, ephemeral, unpredictable. The engine has learned to define ecosystems by resource rather than geography. This expands the range of ecological relationships the engine can describe.

### 5. Vocal Sufficiency: The Absence of Song

The Cedar Waxwing is the first passerine in the engine that does not sing in the traditional sense. Hermit Thrush, warblers, swallows, grosbeak, tanager, robin -- all produce learned territorial songs. The Cedar Waxwing produces only an innate contact call. The absence of song is not a deficiency -- it is an adaptation. Nomadic frugivores that do not defend territory do not need territorial song. The engine has learned that vocal complexity is not always adaptive. Sometimes simplicity is the optimal strategy. The see-see-see is enough.

### 6. The Energy Undecet: The Ground Bass Continues

Eleven consecutive E=4. The retrospective at degree 30 said "must break." It did not. The ground bass holds. The plateau has now survived a family transition (Hirundinidae to Bombycillidae), a timbral category change (liquid to sibilant), an ecosystem change (farmland to fruiting trees), a seasonal change (summer to resident), and a social structure change (loosely colonial to nomadic flock). The E=4 pedal is independent of every other parameter. It is the one thing that does not change. Breaking it will be seismic.

The Cedar Waxwing arrives at a transitional point in the engine: after the swallow quintet's raw power, before whatever comes next. Both occupy a hinge point.

3. **Mercury Records.** The label known for clean, professional production. The Cedar Waxwing is the most professionally finished-looking bird in the PNW -- clean lines, smooth gradients, precise accent marks. Both are products of careful aesthetic curation.

4. **Eleventh consecutive E=4.** The energy plateau extends again. Eager's consistent mid-career output mirrors the plateau's stubborn constancy.

## What Worked

### 1. The new family introduction is a genuine structural event
Bombycillidae adds a 17th family and opens new taxonomic territory. The engine had been running through established families for five degrees (Hirundinidae). A new family is a reset -- new phylogenetic context, new ecological package, new timbral space. The Cedar Waxwing brought all three.

### 2. The sibilant timbral category enriches the palette
After 30 degrees of tonal, liquid, and rough sounds, the engine needed a fourth color. The see-see-see provides it. The sibilant category -- thin, high, crystalline -- is genuinely different from anything the engine has produced. The palette is now four-dimensional.

### 3. Frugivory as ecosystem is a conceptual advance
Defining an ecosystem by food source rather than physical habitat is a new organizing principle. It introduces nomadism, phenological tracking, and resource-driven distribution in a way that habitat-based ecosystems cannot. The engine's ecological vocabulary has expanded.

### 4. Vocal sufficiency is a valuable lesson
The Cedar Waxwing demonstrates that vocal complexity is not always adaptive. After the Barn Swallow's elaborate warble, the waxwing's simple contact call is a corrective: sometimes less is more. The engine's acoustic theory now includes the concept of "enough" -- a sound that does what it needs to and nothing else.

### 5. The waxwing pair echoes the Tachycineta pair effectively
The structural parallel is clear and productive. Same-genus pairs at consecutive degrees, each exploring a different contrast axis, is an emerging engine pattern. Three pairs now (Catharus, Tachycineta, Bombycilla) establish the pattern firmly.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. CRITICAL: No marine species in 31 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
Thirty-one degrees. Not a single species from Puget Sound itself -- no gull, no loon, no grebe, no cormorant, no alcid. This is now **31 degrees overdue**. The project is named for Puget Sound. The Sound must speak. After two consecutive waxwings (31-32), the marine gap will reach 33 degrees. The urgency cannot be overstated.

### 2. CRITICAL: Nine consecutive Passeriformes (degrees 23-31)
The longest order-level streak in the engine. The last non-passerine was Northern Flicker (Piciformes) at degrees 21-22. A marine species (gull = Charadriiformes, loon = Gaviiformes) would break this streak.

### 3. CRITICAL: Eleven consecutive E=4 (degrees 21-31)
The undecet. Over one-third of all degrees at one energy level. The retrospective at degree 30 said "must break at 31." It did not. The degree 32 Bohemian Waxwing is the next chance. If it stays E=4, the plateau reaches twelve consecutive degrees -- fully one-third of the engine's output. The dynamic range would be essentially destroyed.

### 4. CRITICAL: No corvid in 31 degrees
No Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. The most intelligent, acoustically complex PNW birds remain absent.

### 5. CRITICAL: No raptor in 31 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, or Osprey. Top predators are absent.

### 6. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Twenty-seven degrees since the Spotted Owl. The night has one voice.

Over the farmland, where the barn door stood open and the wire fences crossed the pasture, a bird swept in low with its long forked tail streaming behind, and from a wire it poured out a long, liquid, twittering warble -- the most complex swallow song the engine had heard. The Barn Swallow. The river. The quintet closed. The archetype arrived last. And now. In the mountain ash at the edge of the park. A tree full of sleek, crested shapes -- dozens of them, warm brown melting to gray, black masks sharp against the pale sky, yellow tail bands bright as punctuation, tiny drops of red wax catching the light on their wings. They are eating. They are passing berries from branch to beak with mechanical efficiency, 16 minutes from mouth to seed, hundreds of berries per bird per day. And from the tree comes a sound that is not a song but something else -- a high, thin, shimmering see-see-see, see-see-see, see-see-see, dozens of voices overlapping, each one a whisper, all of them together a crystalline curtain of sound that fills the air above the tree like heat shimmer. The tree is vibrating. The silk-tails are calling. Bombycilla cedrorum. The silk-tail of the cedars. Thirty to thirty-six grams of warm brown and gray and yellow and red wax, eating mountain ash berries in a Seattle park in March, see-see-seeing to each other in a frequency range that older humans cannot hear. No territory. No song. No fixed address. Just fruit and flock and the thin high whisper that holds them together. A new family. The 17th. After five swallows made a river of liquid song, the Cedar Waxwing whispers. The whisper is not less than the river. It is sufficient. It is exactly enough. The swallow quintet closed with synthesis -- every liquid voice absorbed into the archetype's warble. The waxwing pair opens with a whisper -- thin, high, sibilant, a new color on the palette, the third timbral category, the wire after the river. The engine has added a new instrument to the ensemble. The glass harmonica. The sound that shimmers rather than flows. And the flock in the mountain ash keeps calling, keeps eating, keeps moving -- here today, wherever the fruit is tomorrow. The whisper carries. Thirty-one degrees of The Sound of Puget Sound, and the Sound itself -- the actual water, the salt water, the tidal inland sea -- has still not spoken. Not once. Not a gull. Not a loon. Not a grebe. Thirty-one degrees of forest, field, farmland, and fruiting tree. The swallows filled the sky. The waxwings fill the mountain ash. But the Sound is waiting. It has been waiting for thirty-one degrees. It cannot wait much longer.*
