Pacific-slope Flycatcher

Empidonax difficilis · Family Tyrannidae (Tyrant Flycatchers)
Upslurred Wheeet · Riparian Alder · Spring–Fall (Neotropical Migrant) · Energy 2
The Pacific-slope Flycatcher lifts a single upslurred whistle from the riparian alder — “ptseet” — an ascending question that hangs in the air, unresolved. You cannot see this bird and know its name. You must hear it. Degree 6 adds the question — and for the first time, the voice arrives in spring and departs in fall.

Taxonomy & Physical Description

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is a small passerine of the family Tyrannidae (tyrant flycatchers), order Passeriformes — the largest bird family in the Americas. The genus Empidonax (“king of the gnats”) contains 15 North American species, all nearly identical in plumage. The species epithet difficilis means “difficult” — named for the genus’s notorious visual indistinguishability. At 5.5 inches and 8–12 grams, olive-green above, yellowish below, with a teardrop-shaped eye-ring and two pale wing bars.

The 1989 split. Until 1989, this bird and the Cordilleran Flycatcher (E. occidentalis) were a single species: the Western Flycatcher. The AOU split them based almost entirely on vocal differences. The species exists as a recognized taxon because of its call. Remove the voice, and the taxonomy collapses. Among birders, Empidonax flycatchers are known as “Empids” — the standard advice is: if you cannot hear it, you cannot identify it.

Bioacoustics — The Upslurred Wheeet

The Ascending Question

The diagnostic call is a sharp, upslurred “ptseet” or “su-wheet” — a single whistle that rises from approximately 2.5–3.0 kHz to 3.5–4.5 kHz over 0.3–0.5 seconds. The frequency contour is a smooth, continuous upward glide — not a step, not a jump, but a sweep. The attack is crisp, sometimes preceded by a faint consonant-like “pt-” or “ts-” noise.

This upslurred whistle is the diagnostic character. Not the plumage, not the eye-ring, not the wing bars. One note, and identification is certain. Silence, and the bird becomes “Empidonax sp.”

Dawn Song: Three-Phrase Signature

The male’s dawn song delivers three distinct phrases in a fixed order: ptseet (upslurred whistle), ptsik (sharp downslurred chip), suweet (burry descending two-part note). This creates a micro-melody with internal shape — rise, fall, resolution — the simplest possible ternary form (A-B-C). The first species in the circle with multi-section formal structure.

First Neotropical Migrant — Seasonal Presence

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher arrives in April and departs by September. It winters in western Mexico (Sonora to Oaxaca). This is the first species in the circle whose acoustic contribution has a temporal envelope — it fades in, sustains through the breeding season, then fades out. All previous degrees are year-round residents.

Voice as Identity — The Inversion

In most bird species, the identification pathway is: see first, hear second. Field guides are organized by plumage. Sound is supplementary. In Empidonax flycatchers, this hierarchy is completely inverted. Visual identification is unreliable or impossible. The ONLY reliable diagnostic character is the call. Voice is not supplementary. Voice IS identity.

Every species in degrees 0–5 has visual or behavioral features that establish identity before sound is considered. The heron’s size, the wren’s skulking behavior, the thrush’s plumage, the chickadee’s black cap, the owl’s facial disk, the nuthatch’s head-first descent. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher has none that are reliable. One upslurred whistle — instant certainty. Silence — permanent ambiguity. The bird teaches you that your eyes are insufficient. The forest has a species that can only be known by ear.

The Arc: Silence → Cascade → Drone → Language → Motif → Metronome → Question

Degree 6 adds the UPSLURRED QUESTION. The ascending pitch contour that implies continuation — a half cadence in the acoustic landscape. And for the first time, the voice is seasonal: present in spring-fall, absent in winter.

Riparian Alder — A New Ecosystem

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is strongly associated with riparian corridors — the narrow bands of red alder, bigleaf maple, and sword fern along streams and creeks. This is a different ecosystem from degrees 0–5: not the estuary (heron), not conifer forest (wren, thrush, owl, nuthatch), but the riparian transition zone where water meets forest and deciduous alder replaces evergreen conifer.

Riparian corridors shade streams (regulating temperature for salmon), filter sediment, stabilize banks, and support disproportionately high biodiversity. The flycatcher is an indicator species for intact riparian habitat — its presence confirms the stream corridor has maintained the structure a small aerial insectivore requires. A sally-gleaner, it perches, waits, launches to capture flying insects mid-air, and returns. An ambush predator of the air column.

Acoustic Theory Mapping

Q

Upslurred Question (Ascending Pitch Contour) Advanced

Rising pitch signals continuation, not completion. The flycatcher’s “ptseet” is a half cadence — an ascending gesture that hangs unresolved, like a question mark in tonal language.

T

Ternary Form (Three-Phrase Dawn Song) Intermediate

Ptseet… ptsik… suweet: A-B-C structure. The simplest complete formal structure in music — minuet and trio, da capo aria. First multi-section song form in the circle.

V

Voice as Primary Identity (Personal Tone) Foundational

Knowing a musician by their sound, not their face. Coltrane’s tone IS Coltrane. The flycatcher’s call IS the flycatcher. Visual inspection fails; only the ear resolves identity.

S

Seasonal Presence as Temporal Form Advanced

Spring = verse, summer = chorus, fall = verse, winter = rest. The annual cycle as song form. The first species whose contribution has a temporal envelope — fade in, sustain, fade out.

H

Half Cadence (Unresolved Tension) Intermediate

A phrase ending on the dominant — tension that demands resolution. The upslurred call ends at its highest pitch, creating expectation. Every previous species either resolves or sustains. This one opens.

P

Pitch Sweep as Synthesis Type Intermediate

A continuous frequency glide from low to high — not FM modulation, not AM modulation, but a sweep. A new synthesis type: controlled directional pitch change.

Music Theory Mapping

The upslur as musical question. An ascending pitch contour at the end of a phrase signals incompleteness in both speech and music. The flycatcher’s “ptseet” is a half cadence — it rises and stops without resolution. In the ensemble, every previous voice either resolves (owl’s motif), sustains (thrush’s drone), or repeats without pitch change (nuthatch’s beat). The flycatcher adds the unresolved ascending gesture.

Voice as identity: the jazz parallel. In jazz, you recognize Coltrane by a single note on the tenor saxophone — the tone IS the player. The Empidonax genus forces the same relationship: know the sound or don’t know the bird. No visual shortcut exists. Degree 6 teaches the ear to lead the eye.

Ternary form. The three-phrase dawn song (ptseet… ptsik… suweet) is the simplest formal structure — A-B-C in fixed order. Previous species contributed single elements (one note, one cascade, one motif). The flycatcher introduces sections — distinct phrases arranged deliberately, the building block of musical form.

Seasonal presence as temporal form. The migrant’s annual cycle maps to large-scale musical form: exposition (arrival), development (breeding), coda (departure), rest (winter absence). No previous species has this temporal architecture. The residents play continuously. The migrant plays one movement per year.
