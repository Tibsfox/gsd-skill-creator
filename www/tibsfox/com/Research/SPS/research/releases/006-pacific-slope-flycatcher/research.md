## Taxonomy and Physical Description

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher (*Empidonax difficilis*) is a small passerine of the family **Tyrannidae** (tyrant flycatchers), order Passeriformes. Tyrannidae is the largest family of birds in the Americas, with over 400 species -- the "tyrant" flycatchers, named for their aggressive defense of nesting territory despite their small size. The genus *Empidonax* (from Greek *empis*, "gnat," and *anax*, "king" -- king of the gnats) contains 15 species in North America, all of them small, olive-green, eye-ringed flycatchers that are nearly impossible to distinguish by sight alone. The species epithet **difficilis** means exactly what it says: "difficult." It was named by Baird in 1858, and the difficulty has not diminished since.

**The taxonomic split.** Until 1989, the Pacific-slope Flycatcher and the Cordilleran Flycatcher (*Empidonax occidentalis*) were considered a single species: the Western Flycatcher. The American Ornithologists' Union split them based on differences in vocalizations, contact zone behavior, and genetic divergence. The two species are virtually **identical in plumage** -- the split was driven almost entirely by voice. This is the taxonomic event that defines the species: it exists as a recognized species *because of its call*. Remove the voice, and the taxonomy collapses back into one species. Voice is not supplementary identification for this bird. Voice IS identity.

**Physical description.** A small flycatcher, 5.5 inches (14 cm) in length, wingspan approximately 8 inches (20 cm), weight 8--12 grams. Olive-green to olive-brown upperparts, yellowish underparts. Two pale wing bars. A conspicuous teardrop-shaped **eye-ring**, yellowish to whitish -- the single most useful visual field mark, but even this overlaps with the Cordilleran Flycatcher and several other Empidonax. Broad, flat bill built for snapping insects out of the air. Lower mandible pale orange-pink.

**The "difficult" genus.** Among birders, Empidonax flycatchers are collectively known as **"Empids"** -- a group so visually similar that the standard field advice is: *if you cannot hear it, you cannot identify it*. Even experienced observers list them as "Empidonax sp." when the bird is silent. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is the canonical example: it looks like a Cordilleran, Hammond's, Dusky, or Yellow-bellied Flycatcher depending on lighting and optimism. You know it by its voice or you do not know it at all.

## Bioacoustics -- THE UPSLURRED WHEEET: THE QUESTION

### The Primary Vocalization: An Ascending Whistle

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher's diagnostic call is a sharp, upslurred **"ptseet"** or **"su-wheet"** -- a single whistle that rises in pitch from start to finish. The note begins at approximately **2.5--3.0 kHz** and sweeps upward to **3.5--4.5 kHz** over a duration of approximately **0.3--0.5 seconds**. The frequency contour is a smooth, continuous upward glide -- not a step, not a jump, but a **sweep**. The attack is crisp, sometimes preceded by a faint consonant-like noise ("pt-" or "ts-") that gives the transcription its initial cluster.

This is the **diagnostic character** for the species. Not the plumage, not the wing bars, not the eye-ring. The upslurred "ptseet" is what separates *E. difficilis* from *E. occidentalis* (which gives a sharp, two-note "pit-peet" with a different contour). In the field, the Pacific-slope Flycatcher announces itself with this call from dense riparian vegetation where visual identification is often impossible. The call is the bird. The bird is the call.

### The Dawn Song: Three-Phrase Signature

The male's dawn song is a structured series of **three distinct phrase types** repeated in a consistent order:

1. **"Ptseet"** -- the upslurred whistle (the primary call)
2. **"Ptsik"** or **"tsik"** -- a short, sharp downslurred chip
3. **"Suweet"** or **"suwee"** -- a burry, slightly descending two-part note

The sequence **ptseet... ptsik... suweet** repeats cyclically, delivered from a consistent perch in the pre-dawn darkness. This three-phrase pattern is the species' **dawn signature** -- a ternary form delivered when acoustic competition is lowest and transmission distance is greatest. The individual phrases differ in contour (up, down, level-to-down), creating a micro-melody with internal shape: rise, fall, resolution. This is not a single repeated note like the nuthatch's yank-yank. It is a **phrase set** -- three distinct elements in a fixed order, creating the simplest possible song form.

### The Upslur as Acoustic Gesture

An upslurred note is acoustically and perceptually distinct from a downslurred, level, or modulated note. In tonal languages (Mandarin Chinese, Yoruba, many Southeast Asian languages), a rising tone on a syllable changes the word's meaning -- a rising pitch contour signals a question in English intonation, an incomplete thought, an invitation to respond. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher's upslurred "ptseet" functions similarly in the acoustic landscape: it is **an ascending pitch contour that implies continuation**. It does not close. It does not resolve. It lifts and opens.

In contrast, the Varied Thrush's sustained tone (degree 2) sits at a single frequency -- a statement, a declaration. The chickadee's "chick-a-dee" descends slightly -- a completed phrase. The owl's four-note motif resolves downward. The nuthatch's yank-yank is level -- no pitch movement at all. The flycatcher is the first species in the circle whose primary vocalization **ascends and remains unresolved**. It is, acoustically and functionally, a question mark.

## Voice as Identity: The Inversion of the Normal Hierarchy

### The Key Insight of Degree 6

In the vast majority of bird species, the identification pathway is: **see the bird first, hear it second**. Field guides are organized by visual features -- plumage, size, shape, habitat. Sound is supplementary. You see a robin, you confirm it by song. You see a hawk, you note the call. Vision leads; sound follows.

In Empidonax flycatchers, this hierarchy is **completely inverted**. The identification pathway is: **hear the bird first; visual identification is unreliable or impossible**. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is the purest expression of this inversion in the PNW avifauna. Its plumage overlaps with at least four other Empidonax species. Its eye-ring, wing bars, bill color, and body proportions fall within the range of variation of its congeners. The ONLY reliable diagnostic character is the upslurred "ptseet."

This is voice as **primary identity** -- not supplementary, not confirmatory, but constitutive. The bird IS its call. The species EXISTS as a distinct taxon because of vocal differences. The 1989 split from Cordilleran Flycatcher was justified by voice. The field identification depends on voice. The taxonomic integrity rests on voice.

In the species engine, this is the first species where **sound is the defining axis of identity**. Every species in degrees 0--5 has visual or behavioral features that establish identity before sound is considered: the heron's size, the wren's behavior, the thrush's plumage, the chickadee's black cap, the owl's facial disk, the nuthatch's head-first descent. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher has none that are reliable. Voice is the ONLY way in.

### The Birder's Dilemma

Every experienced birder has stood beneath a canopy of red alder, heard the sharp "ptseet" from somewhere in the foliage, and known immediately: Pacific-slope Flycatcher. And every experienced birder has watched a small olive flycatcher perch silently on a branch and thought: *I cannot identify this bird without hearing it call.* One note -- instant certainty. Silence -- permanent ambiguity. The bird teaches you that your eyes are insufficient. The forest has a species that can only be known by ear.

## The First Neotropical Migrant: Temporal Presence and Absence

### Breaking the Resident Pattern

Degrees 0 through 5 are all **resident** species in the Puget Sound lowlands. The Great Blue Heron, Pacific Wren, Varied Thrush, Black-capped Chickadee, Northern Spotted Owl, and Red-breasted Nuthatch are present year-round (with the thrush partially migratory and the nuthatch occasionally irruptive, but functionally resident in most years). The acoustic landscape described in the first six degrees is a **permanent** soundscape -- these voices are always available, always audible, through every season.

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher **arrives in April and departs by September**. It breeds in the Pacific Northwest but winters in western Mexico, from southern Sonora to Oaxaca. This is a **neotropical migrant** -- a species that makes a round-trip journey of over 2,000 miles twice each year, nesting in PNW riparian corridors during the breeding season and retreating to the tropics for the non-breeding months.

This introduces a new concept to the species engine: **temporal presence and absence**. The flycatcher's upslurred "ptseet" is heard from April through early September. Then it stops. The bird is gone. The acoustic niche it occupied goes silent. For six months, the riparian alder corridor lacks this voice entirely. The return of the "ptseet" in spring is an **acoustic event** -- the arrival of a voice that has been absent, a seasonal marker as reliable as the first blooming of salmonberry or the return of the Rufous Hummingbird.

### Seasonal Form as Musical Structure

The annual cycle maps to **formal sections with contrasting presence**:

- **Spring (April--May):** Arrival. The upslurred call appears in the riparian corridor. The opening phrase.
- **Summer (June--August):** Breeding at full intensity. The chorus.
- **Fall (September):** Departure. The calls thin and cease. The closing phrase.
- **Winter (October--March):** Absence. The rest between movements.

This is the first species in the circle whose acoustic contribution has a **temporal envelope** -- it fades in, sustains, and fades out over months. Every previous species has continuous presence. The flycatcher introduces the **seasonal voice** -- a sound that marks time by its appearance and disappearance. You know what month it is by whether you can hear the "ptseet."

## Riparian Alder: A New Ecosystem

### Stream-Adjacent Habitat

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is strongly associated with **riparian corridors** -- the narrow bands of vegetation along streams, rivers, and creeks. In western Washington, this means **red alder** (*Alnus rubra*) stands, bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) canopy, sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*) understory, and the cool, shaded, moist conditions created by proximity to flowing water. The species nests on sheltered surfaces -- cliff faces, stream bank cutouts, the undersides of bridges, root wads of fallen trees, and occasionally human structures near water. It prefers shaded, damp sites with overhead cover and access to open air for flycatching.

This is a **different ecosystem** from degrees 0--5. The heron occupied the estuary. The wren, thrush, owl, and nuthatch occupied conifer forest (old-growth, canopy, or generalist conifer). The flycatcher occupies the **riparian transition zone** -- the strip where water meets forest, where deciduous alder replaces evergreen conifer, where the canopy opens slightly to allow the light gaps that flycatching requires. This is the first species in the circle associated with **deciduous riparian habitat** rather than conifer forest or open water.

Riparian corridors provide shade that regulates stream temperature (critical for salmon), filter sediment, stabilize stream banks, and support disproportionately high biodiversity relative to their area. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is an **indicator species** for intact riparian habitat -- its presence confirms that the stream corridor has maintained the vegetative structure, moisture regime, and insect productivity that a small aerial insectivore requires.

### Flycatching as Foraging Strategy

The species is a **sally-gleaner** and **aerial hawker** -- it perches on an exposed branch, waits, then launches to capture a flying insect mid-air, returning to the same or nearby perch. The diet is almost entirely invertebrate during the breeding season: flies, mosquitoes, small moths, beetles, wasps, and spiders. The broad, flat bill with rictal bristles is adapted to snap shut on fast-moving aerial prey. Watch, launch, snap, return -- an ambush predator of the air column.

## Music Theory Mapping

### The Upslur as Musical Question

In Western tonal music, a phrase ending on an ascending pitch contour is perceived as **incomplete** -- it signals a question, an unresolved thought demanding a following phrase. Rising pitch at phrase end signals continuation; falling pitch signals completion. The technical term is the **half cadence** -- a phrase ending on the dominant, creating tension that resolves only when the tonic arrives.

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher's "ptseet" is a **half cadence in the acoustic landscape**. It rises and stops. It does not resolve. In the ensemble, the heron provides silence, the wren cascading melody, the thrush a pedal point, the chickadee syntax, the owl an ostinato, the nuthatch a steady beat. The flycatcher adds the **unresolved ascending gesture** -- the upward-lifting question that hangs in the air.

### Three-Phrase Dawn Song as Ternary Form

The male's dawn song -- ptseet... ptsik... suweet -- is a **ternary form**: three distinct sections (A-B-C or A-B-A') arranged in a fixed order. Ternary form is the **simplest complete formal structure** in music. A minuet and trio. An ABA da capo aria. A verse-chorus-verse pop song. The three-phrase dawn song of the flycatcher is the first species in the circle whose vocalization exhibits **multi-section formal structure**. The wren's cascade is through-composed (no repeated sections). The thrush's tone is a single sustained element. The chickadee's syntax is combinatorial but not formally structured. The owl's four-note motif is a single phrase repeated. The nuthatch's yank-yank is one note repeated. The flycatcher introduces **form** -- distinct sections arranged in a deliberate order.

### Voice as Primary Identity: Knowing by Tone

In jazz, the concept of **personal tone** is fundamental. You recognize John Coltrane not by looking at him but by hearing his sound -- a single note on the tenor saxophone, and you know who it is. The tone IS the identity. Miles Davis on trumpet. Billie Holiday on vocals. Thelonious Monk on piano. Each player has a sound that is theirs alone, recognizable in a single phrase, often in a single note. This is the sonic equivalent of what the Pacific-slope Flycatcher achieves in the avian world: **identity through voice alone**, when visual inspection cannot distinguish you from your nearest relatives.

The Empidonax genus forces the listener into the same relationship a jazz audience has with musicians: you must know the sound to know the player. There is no visual shortcut. The plumage-first birder, like the face-first concert-goer, will fail. Degree 6 teaches the ear to lead.

### Seasonal Presence as Temporal Form

The flycatcher's annual cycle maps to **large-scale temporal form**. A piece unfolds in time: exposition, development, recapitulation, coda. The form is defined by which elements are present at any given moment. The flycatcher's annual presence in the PNW is a **movement** in a larger work -- it enters, dominates the acoustic space for a season, then exits. The silence of winter is the rest between movements. The return in spring is the recapitulation. No previous species has this temporal architecture. The residents play continuously. The migrant plays one movement per year.

## Conservation Status

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is currently listed as **Least Concern** by the IUCN. Partners in Flight (2020) estimates the North American breeding population at approximately **6.5 million** individuals. However, Breeding Bird Survey data show **declining population trends** across much of the species' range, including western Washington, with an estimated annual decline of approximately 2--3% per year since the 1970s. The causes are not fully resolved but likely include **habitat loss on both the breeding and wintering grounds** -- riparian forest degradation in the Pacific Northwest and tropical forest loss in Mexico.

It is not yet endangered, but it is **declining** -- a trajectory species, the first in the circle with a clearly negative trend. Its dependence on intact riparian corridors makes it vulnerable to streamside development, logging, and water diversion. Unlike the nuthatch or chickadee, which persist in degraded suburban habitat, the flycatcher requires functional riparian forest structure. Lose the stream corridor, lose the flycatcher.

**Attribution note (OCAP/CARE compliance).** Riparian corridors in western Washington are the ancestral and continuing homelands of Coast Salish peoples, including the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Snoqualmie, Tulalip, Stillaguamish, Puyallup, and Nisqually nations, among others. Indigenous land and water management practices shaped and sustained the riparian ecosystems these species depend on. This section uses only publicly available sources. For nation-specific cultural context, primary sources are the relevant tribal cultural departments.
