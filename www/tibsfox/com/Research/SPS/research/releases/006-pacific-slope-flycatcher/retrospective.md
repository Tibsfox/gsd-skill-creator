## Three Structural Firsts

### 1. First Migrant: Seasonal vs. Resident

This is the most significant structural advance at degree 6. All six previous species are functionally resident -- their vocalizations are available year-round in the Puget Sound lowlands. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher arrives in April and departs in September. Its upslurred "ptseet" exists in the acoustic landscape for approximately six months, then goes silent -- not because the bird stops calling, but because the bird **leaves**.

This introduces the concept of **temporal presence and absence** to the species engine. The acoustic landscape is no longer static. It has seasonal layers. The resident voices (degrees 0--5) form the year-round foundation. The migrant voice (degree 6) is a **seasonal overlay** -- present during the breeding season, absent during winter. This is the beginning of acoustic seasonality in the circle, and future migrant species will add additional seasonal layers.

The annual cycle of the flycatcher maps to large-scale temporal form in music: arrival as exposition, breeding as development, departure as coda, winter absence as the rest between movements. No previous species has this temporal architecture. The residents play continuously. The migrant plays one movement per year.

### 2. First Riparian Species: Ecosystem Expansion

Degrees 0--5 occupied two ecosystem types: estuary/tidal (heron) and conifer forest (wren, thrush, chickadee, owl, nuthatch -- varying from old-growth to generalist conifer). The circle claimed to represent "the sound of Puget Sound" but had not yet included the **riparian corridors** that thread through the lowland landscape.

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher occupies red alder stands along streams and creeks -- deciduous riparian habitat fundamentally different in structure, composition, and acoustic properties from evergreen conifer forest. This is the first species associated with deciduous trees, the first associated with stream-adjacent habitat, and the first whose ecological role (insect pest regulation, riparian indicator) directly connects to watershed health and salmon ecology.

The ecosystem progression through seven degrees: estuary --> old-growth understory --> mature conifer canopy --> mixed woodland --> old-growth Douglas fir --> generalist conifer --> **riparian alder**. The circle now includes marine-influenced (estuary), conifer-dominated (5 degrees), and riparian-deciduous (1 degree) ecosystems.

### 3. Voice as Primary Identity: The Hierarchy Inversion

In degrees 0--5, every species has visual or behavioral features that establish identity before sound is even considered. The heron's size, the wren's skulking behavior, the thrush's distinctive plumage, the chickadee's black cap, the owl's facial disk, the nuthatch's head-first descent. Sound is important in all cases, but it is not the *only* path to identification.

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher has no reliable visual diagnostic. Its plumage overlaps with at least four other Empidonax species. Its eye-ring, wing bars, bill color, and body proportions fall within the range of variation of its congeners. The species exists as a recognized taxon because of vocal differences (the 1989 AOU split was justified primarily by call structure). Field identification depends entirely on voice. The taxonomy itself rests on voice.

This is the first species in the circle where **sound is not a property of the organism but the defining axis of its identity**. The voice-as-identity principle is a major new concept for the engine. It connects to the jazz concept of personal tone (knowing Coltrane by a single note), to the broader philosophical question of what constitutes identity when visual appearance is unreliable, and to the practical reality of field ornithology where the ear succeeds when the eye fails.

## The Patti Brown Parallel: Known by Those Who Listen

The Part A parallel at degree 6 maps to **Patti Brown** -- vocalist on the Seattle jazz scene. The parallel operates on four axes:

1. **Underrecognition despite excellence.** Brown was not the name casual listeners associated with Seattle jazz -- more prominent vocalists drew the attention. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is not the bird casual birders notice in the riparian corridor -- more colorful species (tanagers, warblers) catch the eye. Both are underrecognized relative to their actual significance.
2. **Voice as defining characteristic.** Brown's voice was her instrument -- her tone, phrasing, and delivery were unmistakable to anyone who actually listened. The flycatcher's call is the only reliable diagnostic character -- one upslurred whistle and identification is certain. Both are known by their sound.
3. **Overshadowed by visual spectacle.** Brown worked alongside more visually prominent performers. The flycatcher shares habitat with more colorful species. In both cases, the eye is drawn elsewhere while the ear catches what matters.
4. **Seasonal/temporal presence.** Brown appeared on stages, performed, then the set ended. The flycatcher arrives, breeds, departs. Both leave silence when they go. The performance is bounded in time.

The parallel is particularly strong because it maps to the voice-as-identity theme that defines degree 6: both Brown and the flycatcher are known by those who listen, and invisible to those who only look.

## What Worked

### 1. The voice-as-identity principle opened a genuinely new conceptual dimension

This is the strongest theoretical contribution since the interspecies eavesdropping network at degree 5. The idea that a species can only be identified by sound -- that visual inspection is insufficient and the taxonomy itself depends on vocal characters -- is not merely an interesting fact about Empidonax flycatchers. It is a fundamental insight about what constitutes identity. It maps cleanly to jazz (personal tone), to epistemology (how we know what we know), and to the practical experience of field ornithology. The mapping is not decorative. It is structural.

### 2. The first migrant species introduced temporal depth to the circle

The transition from an all-resident circle to one that includes a seasonal voice is a qualitative change in the engine's architecture. The acoustic landscape is no longer static. It now has a temporal dimension -- year-round foundation vs. seasonal overlay. This creates the framework for all subsequent migrant species and begins to capture the actual seasonal rhythm of the PNW soundscape.

### 3. The riparian ecosystem expanded spatial diversity

Five of six previous degrees occupied conifer forest (varying subtypes). The addition of riparian alder is the first ecosystem that is neither marine-influenced nor conifer-dominated. This expansion was specifically recommended in the degree 5 retrospective ("A species from a non-forest habitat"), and while riparian alder is still forest-adjacent, it is structurally distinct from conifer canopy.

### 4. The upslurred pitch contour added a new synthesis type

The synthesis type taxonomy now includes: silence, FM, AM, combinatorial, rhythmic cell, metronomic pulse, and **pitch sweep**. The continuous ascending glide is a distinct acoustic phenomenon not covered by any previous type. Each degree has introduced a genuinely new synthesis type -- six degrees, six new types, no redundancy.

### 5. The three-phrase dawn song introduced formal structure

The A-B-C ternary form of the dawn song is the first multi-section song structure in the circle. Previous species contributed single elements (one note, one cascade, one motif, one beat). The flycatcher contributes **sections** -- distinct phrases arranged in a fixed order. This is the building block from which all larger formal structures are constructed, and it arrives at degree 6 precisely when the ensemble has enough complexity to support it.

### 6. The half-cadence mapping was musically precise

An upslurred note ending at its highest pitch is the acoustic equivalent of a half cadence -- a phrase that ends on the dominant and creates tension. This is the first tension-creating voice in the ensemble. Degrees 0--5 contributed rest, melody, harmony, language, rhythm, and pulse. Degree 6 contributes **tension** -- forward momentum through incompleteness. The ensemble now has a voice that opens rather than closes.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. Audio reference still not included

The degree 5 retrospective declared this must be resolved at degree 6 ("three consecutive retrospectives flagging the same gap is the limit"). It was again not fully resolved -- the Macaulay Library is cited but no spectrogram image or embedded audio link was produced. The acoustic claims (ascending frequency sweep, 2.5--4.5 kHz range, dawn song structure) are precisely the kind of claims that demand visual or auditory evidence. **This is now a pattern failure. It must be resolved at degree 7.**

### 2. The Empidonax complex deserves a comparison table

The research document discusses four other Empidonax species that overlap visually with the Pacific-slope Flycatcher (Cordilleran, Hammond's, Dusky, Yellow-bellied) but does not provide a structured comparison table showing exactly which features overlap and which calls differ. A table showing the vocal diagnostic for each Pacific Northwest Empidonax species would strengthen the voice-as-identity argument with specificity.

### 3. The interspecies network was not extended

Degree 5 recommended extending the eavesdropping network. Degree 6 did not add a new node or edge. The flycatcher is not a known participant in the chickadee alarm broadcast network (it is territorial and not typically found in mixed-species flocks). The network remains at 2 nodes and 1 edge. Future degrees should actively seek species that extend the network graph.

### 4. The conservation section could develop the decline trajectory more

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is declining at 2--3% per year -- the first species in the circle with a clearly negative population trend. The conservation section notes this but does not deeply explore the dual-threat mechanism (breeding habitat degradation in PNW + wintering habitat loss in Mexico). This connection between two hemispheres is unique to neotropical migrants and deserves fuller treatment.

## Recommendations for Release 007

Seven degrees now form an ensemble with distinct acoustic roles and, for the first time, seasonal variation:

1. **Silence** (the foundation)
2. **Complexity** (FM cascade -- the soloist)
3. **Simplicity** (AM drone -- the pedal point)
4. **Meaning** (combinatorial syntax -- the vocalist)
5. **Rhythm** (motif -- the rhythmic figure)
6. **Pulse** (metronome -- the rhythmic ground)
7. **Question** (upslurred half cadence -- the tension)

Degree 7 should consider:

1. **A species with higher energy level.** All seven degrees so far have energy levels of 1--2 (low). A species with energy 3+ would introduce **dynamics** -- the difference between quiet and loud. The engine has not yet explored amplitude as a variable. A loud, high-energy species (a corvid, a raptor, a woodpecker) would add the dynamics dimension.
2. **A species that extends the interspecies network.** The network has been static since degree 5 (2 nodes, 1 edge). A known receiver of the chickadee alarm broadcast (Downy Woodpecker, Dark-eyed Junco, Golden-crowned Kinglet) or a broadcaster in its own right would add a second edge. Three consecutive degrees without a network addition makes it a priority.
3. **Audio/spectrogram evidence must be included.** This is the fourth consecutive retrospective flagging this gap. The pattern is now a structural failure. Degree 7 MUST include either a spectrogram analysis or an embedded Macaulay Library reference with direct link.
4. **A nocturnal or crepuscular migrant vs. resident contrast.** The circle now has both residents and a migrant, but only one nocturnal species (the owl, a resident). A migrant that is also nocturnal or crepuscular would create an interesting intersection of temporal dimensions.
5. **A marine or open-water species.** The circle claims to represent "the sound of Puget Sound" but only degree 0 (heron) is associated with water. A species of open water, shoreline, or marine habitat would bring the Sound itself back into focus.
