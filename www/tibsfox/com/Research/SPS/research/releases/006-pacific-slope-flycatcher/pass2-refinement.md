### Voice as Identity and the Species That Exists Because of Its Call

Pass 1 documented the Pacific-slope Flycatcher as the first species in the engine whose primary identity is acoustic — you know it by its voice or you do not know it at all. Pass 2 deepens this into a structural analysis of voice-constituted identity and connects it to Explorer 6's scan-line imaging and turkey tail's concentric growth bands as systems where identity is constructed incrementally from minimal data.

The 1989 taxonomic split of the Western Flycatcher into the Pacific-slope Flycatcher (*Empidonax difficilis*) and the Cordilleran Flycatcher (*E. occidentalis*) was driven almost entirely by vocal differences. The two species are visually identical — same olive-green plumage, same teardrop eye-ring, same wing bars, same bill shape. Genetic divergence exists but is subtle. What is not subtle is the voice: the Pacific-slope gives an upslurred "ptseet," the Cordilleran gives a two-note "pit-peet" with a different contour. The vocal difference is the diagnostic character that justified the split. The species exists as a recognized taxon because of its call.

This is the inverse of every previous species in the engine. The Great Blue Heron (degree 0) is identified by size and posture — the voice confirms but does not constitute. The Pacific Wren (degree 1) is identified by behavior (skulking, tail-cocked) and habitat — the song is diagnostic but the bird has other identifying features. The Varied Thrush (degree 2) has distinctive plumage (orange breast, dark breast band). The Black-capped Chickadee (degree 3) has a black cap and bib. The Northern Spotted Owl (degree 4) has a facial disk and dark eyes. The Red-breasted Nuthatch (degree 5) has a rusty breast and bold eye-line. For all six, visual features establish identity before voice is considered. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher inverts this hierarchy: voice is the primary — in many cases the only — reliable identification pathway.

Explorer 6's photocell scanner produced identity from minimal data. A single photocell — one pixel — sweeping across the scene, building up an image line by line. The identity of the subject (Earth) emerged not from a single comprehensive view but from the accumulation of hundreds of narrow-band brightness measurements, each one insufficient on its own, collectively producing a recognizable pattern. The flycatcher's identity emerges through the same process: the upslurred "ptseet" is a single narrow-band signal that, on its own, could be confused with other sounds. But repeated across a breeding season — hundreds of calls from the same territory, the same perch, the same contour — the pattern becomes diagnostic. Identity through accumulation of minimal signals.

Turkey tail's concentric bands operate on the same principle. Each band is a ring of pigment — brown, tan, gray, white — that records one period of growth. A single band is not diagnostic; many fungi produce colored zones. But the accumulation of bands in turkey tail's characteristic pattern — the specific sequence of colors, the specific alternation of velvety and smooth textures, the specific width and curvature — produces a visual identity that experienced mycologists recognize instantly. The bracket's identity, like the flycatcher's and Explorer 6's photograph, is constructed incrementally from sequential passes.

The Bown parallel (degree 6 S36) completes the resonance. Bown's identity as a significant jazz pianist was constructed incrementally — Columbia debut, DownBeat reviews, arranger connections, session credits — but the accumulation was not preserved. The scan lines were transmitted and lost. The industry's ground station did not reconstruct the portrait. The flycatcher is luckier: its call is produced annually, refreshing the acoustic identity each spring. Bown's scan lines stopped accumulating when the career became invisible. The flycatcher's identity is renewable. Bown's was not.

### The First Neotropical Migrant and the Seasonal Scan: Temporal Presence as Data

Pass 1 documented the Pacific-slope Flycatcher as the first neotropical migrant in the engine — present April through September, absent October through March. Pass 2 develops the implications of seasonal presence for the SPS communication architecture and connects it to Explorer 6's orbital periodicity.

Explorer 6 orbited Earth in a highly eccentric orbit — perigee approximately 245 km, apogee approximately 42,400 km, orbital period approximately 12.5 hours. The spacecraft was not continuously in contact with tracking stations. It was in range during a fraction of each orbit, producing data during the flyby, then receding beyond communication range until the next pass. The data arrived in bursts — periodic windows of contact separated by periods of silence. The ground stations learned to schedule their observations around the orbital periodicity: when Explorer 6 was in range, they collected data furiously; when it was out of range, they processed what they had and waited.

The Pacific-slope Flycatcher's annual cycle operates on the same principle at a different timescale. The bird is "in range" from April through September — present, calling, producing acoustic data. It is "out of range" from October through March — absent, silent, in western Mexico. The acoustic landscape of the PNW riparian corridor has a seasonal data window: six months of "ptseet" followed by six months of silence. Any acoustic monitoring system (biological or electronic) must account for this periodicity — the absence of the signal in January does not mean the species has disappeared; it means the orbital geometry has carried the transmitter out of range.

This introduces a new concept to the SPS communication architecture: **scheduled absence**. Every previous species (degrees 0-5) is resident — their signals are available year-round (with minor seasonal variation in intensity). The flycatcher's signal has a binary temporal envelope: present or absent, on or off, in range or out of range. The communication architecture now has seven elements:

| Degree | Species | Acoustic Role | Temporal Mode |
|--------|---------|--------------|---------------|
| 0 | Great Blue Heron | Silence | Year-round |
| 1 | Pacific Wren | Cascade | Year-round |
| 2 | Varied Thrush | Drone | Year-round (seasonal intensity shift) |
| 3 | Black-capped Chickadee | Language | Year-round |
| 4 | Northern Spotted Owl | Rhythmic motif | Year-round (nocturnal) |
| 5 | Red-breasted Nuthatch | Steady beat | Year-round |
| **6** | **Pacific-slope Flycatcher** | **Ascending question** | **Seasonal (April-September only)** |

The seasonal mode is structurally significant. An ensemble that includes a seasonal voice has a different character in summer than in winter. The riparian corridor in July — with the flycatcher's "ptseet" layered over the nuthatch's yank-yank, the chickadee's chick-a-dee, the wren's cascade — has an acoustic complexity that the same corridor in January lacks. The flycatcher's presence is additive (summer gains a voice) and its absence is subtractive (winter loses one). The ensemble's membership changes with the calendar. This is the first time the SPS engine has documented a voice that is not always available.

### The Upslurred Question and the Three-Phrase Dawn Song: Formal Structure Enters the Engine

Pass 1 documented the flycatcher's upslurred "ptseet" as an ascending pitch contour — a musical question mark, an unresolved gesture. Pass 2 develops the three-phrase dawn song (ptseet... ptsik... suweet) as the engine's first multi-section formal structure and connects it to Explorer 6's multi-instrument data collection.

Explorer 6 carried multiple instruments — magnetometer, scintillation counter, ion chamber, Geiger counter, micrometeorite detector, photocell scanner — each measuring a different aspect of the near-Earth environment. The instruments were not redundant. Each one addressed a different component of the complex magnetospheric system. The magnetometer measured the field. The particle detectors measured the trapped radiation. The photocell scanned the visual scene. Together, they produced a multi-channel portrait of the environment that no single instrument could have generated.

The flycatcher's three-phrase dawn song is a multi-instrument composition at the simplest possible scale. Three distinct phrase types — "ptseet" (ascending), "ptsik" (descending chip), "suweet" (burry, descending) — each with a different pitch contour, each occupying a different acoustic niche. The ascending phrase lifts. The descending chip punctuates. The burry resolution settles. Together, they create a micro-melody with internal direction: rise, fall, resolution. This is ternary form — ABA or ABC — the simplest complete formal structure in music.

No previous species in the engine produces multi-section formal structure. The wren's cascade is through-composed (continuous, non-repeating). The thrush's tone is a single sustained element. The chickadee's syntax is combinatorial but not formally organized into sections. The owl's four-note motif is a single phrase repeated. The nuthatch's yank-yank is one note repeated. The flycatcher's dawn song is the first vocalization in the engine that has **sections** — distinct elements arranged in a deliberate, repeating order. Form enters the acoustic architecture at degree 6.

### Riparian Alder as New Ecosystem: The Deciduous Transition

Pass 1 noted that the flycatcher occupies riparian alder corridors — a different ecosystem from the conifer forest and estuary habitats of degrees 0-5. Pass 2 develops the ecological implications and connects the riparian transition zone to turkey tail's role as the decomposer that bridges living tree and soil.

The riparian corridor is a transition zone — the boundary where water meets forest, where deciduous alder replaces evergreen conifer, where the canopy opens to admit the light gaps that flycatching requires. Red alder (*Alnus rubra*) is the dominant tree of PNW riparian corridors — a fast-growing, nitrogen-fixing deciduous tree that colonizes disturbed streamside sites and provides the canopy structure that the flycatcher depends on. Alder is also the preferred substrate for turkey tail. The connection is direct: the flycatcher nests in the living alder canopy; turkey tail decomposes the dead alder on the ground beneath. The degree-6 organism and the degree-6 species share a substrate, the way the degree-5 nuthatch and degree-5 Douglas-fir shared a trunk.

The riparian ecosystem introduces the first deciduous-dominated habitat in the engine. Degrees 0-5 were conifer forest (wren, thrush, owl, nuthatch) or estuary (heron). The chickadee (degree 3) occupies mixed forest but is not ecosystem-restricted. The flycatcher is the first species tightly associated with deciduous riparian vegetation — a habitat type that covers a small fraction of the PNW landscape (narrow streamside corridors) but supports disproportionately high biodiversity. Riparian corridors are the thin lines on the landscape map where the highest density of species per unit area occurs: breeding birds, amphibians, invertebrates, fish, riparian plants, and the fungi that decompose the constant supply of alder leaf litter and fallen wood.

The flycatcher's declining population trend (approximately 2-3% per year decline since the 1970s) is connected to riparian habitat degradation — streamside development, logging of riparian buffers, water diversion, and loss of deciduous canopy structure. This is the first species in the engine with a clearly negative population trajectory, contrasting with the nuthatch (stable), the chickadee (stable), and the owl (declining but ESA-listed with active management). The flycatcher's decline is quieter — no ESA listing, no federal timber policy battle, no headline-generating conservation crisis. The erasure of the flycatcher from degraded riparian corridors parallels Bown's erasure from the jazz canon: a real decline that proceeds without generating sufficient alarm to trigger institutional response.
