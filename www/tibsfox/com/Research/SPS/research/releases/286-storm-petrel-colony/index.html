First Hydrobatidae — The Storm-Petrels Enter

Oceanodroma leucorhoa — Leach's Storm-Petrel — is the FIRST HYDROBATIDAE in the engine. Storm-petrels are among the smallest seabirds that venture into the open Pacific. These 35–55 g birds spend most of their lives at sea; they return to land only to breed on remote predator-free islands. Their breeding colonies are nocturnal.

The midnight chattering: hundreds to thousands of birds calling from burrows and in flight at night, creating a cacophony that appears to come from everywhere and nowhere simultaneously. One of the Pacific Northwest's most extraordinary and least-known soundscapes.

chur-r-r-r — chatter — purr — chur-r-r-r
The nocturnal colony call of Leach's Storm-Petrel — from burrows underground, from rock crevices, from invisible birds passing overhead in darkness

Acoustic Profile — The Midnight Colony

Call type: Complex multi-phrase churring, purring, chattering. Rapid staccato sequences; male and female calls differ slightly. Used for mate attraction, individual recognition, burrow location.

Colony environment: Hundreds to thousands of birds calling simultaneously. From burrows (muffled, below ground), from rock crevices, from birds in flight overhead. Calls appear to come from everywhere in darkness. E=6: acoustic mass via numbers; individual birds are small (55 g); cumulative colony effect produces meaningful sound mass but not individual high amplitude.

Temporal: Night only. Daylight colony = silence. The temporal restriction makes this one of the least-encountered seabird soundscapes in the Pacific Northwest despite large colony populations.

The Darkness Strategy

Storm-petrels are nocturnal at breeding colonies because their primary predators are gulls. Gulls cannot hunt effectively in darkness. The storm-petrel's solution to predation is temporal: arrive and depart only at night. During the day at an active storm-petrel colony, you would see nothing and hear nothing. After dark, the colony erupts. Hundreds of birds arriving, calling, fighting for burrow access, engaging in mate display — all in complete darkness. The sound fills the island while the birds remain invisible.

The nocturnal foraging habit at sea parallels this. Leach's Storm-Petrel forages primarily at night on the open Pacific, pattering across the surface to take small fish, squid, and crustaceans. The bird is adapted for a life in darkness: breeding in darkness, foraging in darkness, crossing the Pacific in darkness.

The Name "Petrel"

The word "petrel" derives from the French "Pétrel," a diminutive of "Pierre" (Peter), referring to the birds' habit of pattering across the ocean surface as if walking on water — echoing the Biblical account of St. Peter walking on the Sea of Galilee. Early sailors named them "Mother Carey's Chickens" and believed they were the souls of drowned sailors. The "storm" in "storm-petrel" reflects their association with approaching bad weather: forced closer to shore by deteriorating conditions, they were often seen as storms arrived.

Pacific Northwest Breeding Colonies

Triangle Island, British Columbia

Approximately 25 km off the northern tip of Vancouver Island. Estimated 50,000–100,000 breeding pairs of Leach's Storm-Petrel — one of the largest colonies in the eastern Pacific. The midnight colony here is among the most remarkable wildlife soundscapes in the Pacific Northwest.

Three Arch Rocks, Oregon

Three Arch Rocks National Wildlife Refuge, Cape Meares area. Largest PNW seabird colony south of British Columbia. Leach's Storm-Petrels breed alongside Common Murres, Tufted Puffins, and other colonial species.

Destruction Island, Washington

Washington outer coast. Smaller numbers. Limited storm-petrel breeding habitat on the Washington coast; most significant colonies are offshore and in British Columbia.

Conservation — Island Biosecurity

IUCN: Vulnerable. Documented declines at major colonies. Primary threat: introduced predators (rats, American Mink) on breeding islands. A single rat can eliminate breeding success on a small island. Island biosecurity programs — eradication and exclusion of introduced mammals — are the primary management tool. Light attraction mortality (offshore platforms, lighthouses) is a secondary impact. Climate change affecting offshore prey availability is a tertiary pressure.
