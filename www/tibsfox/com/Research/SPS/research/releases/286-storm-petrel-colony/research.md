## FIRST HYDROBATIDAE: THE MILESTONE

### The Storm-Petrels Enter the Engine

Degree 286 marks the first appearance of Hydrobatidae in the engine:

- **What are storm-petrels?** Storm-petrels (family Hydrobatidae, or split into Hydrobatidae and Oceanitidae in recent taxonomy) are small, pelagic seabirds. They are among the smallest seabirds that venture into the open ocean. Leach's Storm-Petrel, the most common breeding storm-petrel in the northern Pacific, weighs approximately 1.2-1.9 oz (35-55 g) -- smaller than a robin. These tiny birds spend months at sea in the open Pacific, foraging over vast distances, sleeping on the ocean surface, and returning to land only during the breeding season.
- **Why nocturnal?** Storm-petrels are nocturnal at breeding colonies specifically because their primary predators at colony sites are gulls -- which cannot hunt effectively in darkness. The storm-petrel's solution to gull predation is temporal separation: the birds arrive at and depart from their burrows only at night. During the day at an active storm-petrel colony, you would hear and see almost nothing; at night, the colony erupts in sound.
- **The colony sound.** The Leach's Storm-Petrel has a complex, multi-syllable call that is described variously as chattering, purring, and churring. At a colony of hundreds or thousands of birds calling simultaneously from burrows and in flight overhead, the effect is a midnight soundscape of extraordinary density and strangeness. It is the Pacific Northwest's most surprising seabird sound.

(Sources: Hydrobatidae family; Leach's Storm-Petrel size 35-55 g; nocturnal behavior as gull predation avoidance; colony sound description; Oceanitidae split taxonomy)

## OCEANODROMA LEUCORHOA: THE LEACH'S STORM-PETREL

### Taxonomy and Biology

- **Scientific name.** *Oceanodroma leucorhoa*. *Oceanodroma* = "ocean runner" (Greek: *okeanos* = ocean, *dromos* = runner, race-course). *leucorhoa* = "white-rumped" (Greek: *leukos* = white, *rhoea* = flowing -- referring to the white rump patch). The white rump patch is the most reliable field identification mark for Leach's Storm-Petrel.
- **The species complex.** Recent DNA analysis has suggested that what has been called "Leach's Storm-Petrel" may actually represent multiple cryptic species, with the Pacific population showing substantial genetic differentiation from the Atlantic population. Taxonomic revision is ongoing. For the engine's purposes, the Pacific Northwest breeding population is documented as *Oceanodroma leucorhoa* following conventional usage, with a note that cryptic species diversity within this group is recognized.
- **Size.** 7-8 inches (18-21 cm) wingspan 17-19 inches (44-48 cm). Weight 35-55 g. One of the larger storm-petrels but still extremely small for a seabird that ventures into the open Pacific.
- **Appearance.** Dark brown-black overall; distinctive white rump patch; forked tail (distinguishing it from Wilson's Storm-Petrel, which has a square tail); pale wing bars. The white rump patch is the diagnostic field mark visible in flight even at distance.
- **Flight style.** Leach's Storm-Petrel has an erratic, bounding, moth-like flight style -- irregular wingbeats with glides, changing direction unpredictably. This flight style is characteristic of the species and differs markedly from Wilson's Storm-Petrel's more swallow-like flight. The erratic flight may make the birds less predictable targets for avian predators.

(Sources: Oceanodroma leucorhoa taxonomy; leucorhoa etymology "white-rumped"; size 35-55 g; white rump patch; forked tail vs. Wilson's square tail; cryptic species DNA analysis; erratic flight style)

## THE COLONY SOUNDSCAPE

### Midnight Chattering at an Offshore Island

The storm-petrel colony's nocturnal acoustic environment is the entry's primary subject:

- **The call itself.** Leach's Storm-Petrel produces a complex, multi-phrase call during colony activity. The full advertising call is a series of rapid, churring, purring, and chattering phrases -- typically described as a long, repetitive series of sounds with a staccato quality, produced at speed. Male and female calls differ slightly; the calls are used for mate attraction, individual recognition, and burrow location. The call is not beautiful in any conventional sense; it is strange, complex, and immediately distinctive.
- **The colony acoustic environment.** At an active Leach's Storm-Petrel colony at night, hundreds or thousands of birds are calling simultaneously. Birds in burrows call; birds in flight overhead call; birds engaged in mate interactions on the surface call. The combined effect is a dense, multilayered cacophony of churring and chattering that fills the acoustic space of the colony. To a human observer arriving at the colony after dark, the sound appears to come from everywhere -- underground, overhead, from within the rocks -- from invisible sources.
- **The darkness effect.** Storm-petrel colony activity occurs only in darkness. The temporal restriction amplifies the acoustic effect: there are no visual cues to organize the experience. You hear the colony; you cannot see it. The contrast with the daytime silence of the same location is complete.
- **Burrow depth.** Leach's Storm-Petrels nest in burrows up to 3 feet (0.9 m) deep in soil, or in rock crevices, or under vegetation. Calls from within burrows are somewhat muffled but still audible. Multiple burrows in proximity create a distributed sound source -- the colony calls from below ground, from the surface, and from the air simultaneously.
- **E=6 calibration.** The colony night chorus is loud and dense, but the individual bird is small (55 g) and the call, while complex, is not extremely high amplitude. E=6 reflects the cumulative effect of many small birds calling simultaneously -- meaningful acoustic mass through numbers rather than individual power. The SPS 285-288 block's second-lowest energy level (SPS 288 DCCO is also E=6), reflecting the contrast between the massive Elephant Seal bull (E=8) and the tiny storm-petrel colony.

(Sources: Leach's Storm-Petrel call description; churring/purring/chattering terminology; male vs. female call differences; colony acoustic environment; nocturnal restriction; burrow depth 0.9 m; E=6 calibration by numbers vs. individual amplitude)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST BREEDING COLONIES

### Where the Storm-Petrels Go at Night

Leach's Storm-Petrel breeds on a small number of offshore islands along the Pacific Northwest coast:

- **Triangle Island (British Columbia).** The largest seabird colony in British Columbia, Triangle Island supports an estimated 50,000-100,000 Leach's Storm-Petrel pairs. Located approximately 25 km off the northern tip of Vancouver Island, Triangle Island is one of the largest Leach's Storm-Petrel colonies in the eastern Pacific. The midnight cacophony at Triangle Island during peak breeding season is one of the most remarkable wildlife soundscapes in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Three Arch Rocks (Oregon).** The Three Arch Rocks National Wildlife Refuge off the Oregon coast (Cape Meares) is the largest Pacific Northwest seabird colony south of BC. Leach's Storm-Petrels breed here alongside Common Murres, Tufted Puffins, and other colonial seabirds.
- **Destruction Island (Washington).** The Washington outer coast has limited storm-petrel breeding habitat; Destruction Island off the northern Washington coast supports small numbers.
- **The island requirement.** Storm-petrels require predator-free islands or headlands for successful breeding. The primary predators they are avoiding are rats (introduced to many islands), American Mink (introduced to some BC islands), and large gulls. Islands that have been cleared of introduced mammals have seen storm-petrel populations recover substantially.

(Sources: Triangle Island BC storm-petrel population 50,000-100,000 pairs; Three Arch Rocks Oregon NWR; Destruction Island Washington; predator-free island requirement; introduced predator impacts; mink predation BC islands)

## PELAGIC ECOLOGY -- LIFE AT SEA

### The Storm-Petrel as Ocean Wanderer

Storm-petrels spend the majority of their lives on the open ocean:

- **The pelagic season.** From approximately September through April, Leach's Storm-Petrels are at sea in the open Pacific. They range southward into subtropical waters in winter. They forage by pattering across the ocean surface, picking up prey (small fish, squid, crustaceans, marine invertebrates) from the surface film. The pattering flight -- where the bird flies slowly into the wind while dangling its feet into the water surface -- is characteristic and gives rise to the mythological association with St. Peter walking on water (hence "petrel" -- derived from "Pétrel," French diminutive of "Pierre," meaning Peter).
- **The name etymology.** "Petrel" derives from the French "Pétrel," a diminutive of "Pierre" (Peter), referring to the birds' habit of appearing to walk on water while pattering across the ocean surface -- echoing the Biblical account of St. Peter walking on the Sea of Galilee. The association between the small seabird's foraging behavior and a Biblical miracle is one of natural history nomenclature's more striking examples.
- **Surface foraging.** Leach's Storm-Petrel forages primarily by night and in low-light conditions, taking prey from the ocean surface. The nocturnal foraging behavior at sea parallels the nocturnal colony activity on land: the bird is adapted for a life in darkness.
- **Following ships.** Storm-petrels frequently follow ships, feeding on material stirred up in the wake. Early sailors named them "Mother Carey's Chickens" -- believed to be the souls of drowned sailors, or harbingers of storms. The "storm" in "storm-petrel" refers to their association with bad weather: they are often seen as storms approach because they are forced closer to shore by adverse conditions at sea.

(Sources: Leach's Storm-Petrel pelagic range; southward winter migration; pattering foraging technique; petrel etymology from Peter/Pierre; nocturnal foraging; Mother Carey's Chickens sailor mythology; storm association)

## CONSERVATION

### Island Biosecurity as Primary Management Tool

- **IUCN status.** Vulnerable (Leach's Storm-Petrel). The species has experienced documented declines at major colonies, primarily attributed to introduced predators on breeding islands and at-sea impacts (bycatch in fisheries, light attraction at offshore oil platforms and lighthouses causing collision mortality).
- **Introduced predator impacts.** Rats, mink, and other introduced mammals on breeding islands are the most significant threat to storm-petrel colonies. A single rat can dramatically reduce breeding success on an island by preying on eggs, chicks, and incubating adults. Island biosecurity -- the exclusion and removal of introduced predators -- is the primary management tool for storm-petrel conservation in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Light attraction.** Leach's Storm-Petrels are attracted to artificial lights at sea -- offshore oil platforms, lighthouses, vessels. Attracted birds circle the light sources until exhausted, then fall to the deck or ocean and die. This is a significant mortality source at some locations. Light management (shielded or red-spectrum lighting) reduces the attraction effect.
- **Climate change.** Shifts in prey availability associated with ocean warming affect storm-petrel breeding success. Years of poor prey availability in the offshore Pacific Northwest result in reduced chick survival.

(Sources: Leach's Storm-Petrel IUCN Vulnerable; introduced predator impacts; island biosecurity; light attraction mortality; climate change prey impacts; colony decline documentation)
