THE FIRST INSECT SOUND IN THE SPS ENGINE

329 degrees of sound: marine mammals, pinnipeds, orcas, birds, amphibians, fish, geological events, atmospheric phenomena. No insect sound until degree 329. The class Insecta enters the engine at maximum impact: thousands of stoneflies and caddisflies simultaneously completing metamorphosis, filling an entire river corridor with the wing-buzz of mass emergence. E=9. The engine's entomological origin.

Two Orders, One River, One April Morning

Stoneflies — Plecoptera

Pteronarcys californica + multi-species

Giant salmonfly: 4-5 cm body, 6-8 cm wingspan. Wing-beat 30-40 Hz. Individually audible at close range. Nymphal period 1-3 years in cold clean gravel. The most sensitive water quality indicator in North American streams. 3,500 species worldwide; ~600 in North America.

Caddisflies — Trichoptera

Rhyacophila, Hydropsyche, Brachycentrus + multi-species

Hair-winged, moth-related. Wing-beat 200-400 Hz. Case-builders: sand, pebble, plant material. Holometabolism: egg, larva, pupa, adult. 14,500 species worldwide. At mass emergence: swarms visible on weather radar over productive rivers.

Both orders use water temperature as emergence trigger. When Pacific Northwest rivers reach 10–12°C in late April, the hatches coincide. Stonefly nymphs crawl from the water; pupal cases of caddisflies split at the surface. Thousands of individuals along miles of river corridor complete metamorphosis simultaneously — mass synchronized emergence as antipredator strategy, swamping the predator field with more individuals than can be consumed.

The giant salmonfly's wing-beat at 30-40 Hz is individually audible at close range — a low heavy buzz unlike any bird or mammal sound. The caddisfly wing-beat at 200-400 Hz is higher-pitched, audible only as combined mass. Together: the full lower-to-mid acoustic spectrum covered by biological sound, 75-90 dB at the river's edge during peak emergence. Duration: mid-morning through late afternoon during active mating flight.

Water Quality Indicator

The stonefly is the most sensitive aquatic insect to water degradation. Large Plecoptera (especially Pteronarcys californica) require cold, clean, well-oxygenated water with a stable gravel streambed. Their presence is the aquatic biologist's highest-quality certification. A river that produces a mass stonefly + caddisfly emergence has passed the strictest biological quality test the Pacific Northwest stream ecosystem offers. The acoustic event at degree 329 is simultaneously a health certificate for the river that produces it.

Key Pacific Northwest rivers for mass emergence: Methow River (Okanogan County, WA), Yakima River (Kittitas/Yakima Counties, WA), North Fork Stillaguamish, Hoh River (Olympic National Park). The salmonfly hatch on the Methow in late April — visible from the highway, audible from 50 meters — is one of the most spectacular biological events in the state.
