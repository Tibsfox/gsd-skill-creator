## STONEFLIES -- THE PLECOPTERA

### The Oldest Indicator of River Health

The stonefly (Order Plecoptera) is among the most ecologically significant aquatic insects:

- **Taxonomy and diversity.** The Order Plecoptera contains approximately 3,500 known species worldwide, with approximately 600 species in North America. In the Pacific Northwest, stoneflies are represented across a wide range of sizes -- from the giant salmonflies (*Pteronarcys californica* and related species, body length 4-5 cm, wingspan 6-8 cm) to smaller species in genera including *Sweltsa*, *Zapada*, *Suwallia*, and others. The giant salmonfly is the most acoustically significant: at 4-5 cm, its wing-buzz during flight is individually audible and at mass emergence becomes a defining acoustic event.
- **Life cycle.** Stoneflies undergo incomplete metamorphosis (hemimetabolism): egg, nymph, adult. The nymphal period lasts 1-3 years in cold, well-oxygenated streams, with the nymphs living under rocks and debris on the streambed. The nymphal stage is the feeding stage; nymphs are predators and detritivores depending on species. The adult stage is devoted to reproduction: adults emerge, mate, deposit eggs, and die, often within a few weeks. Many adult stonefly species do not feed at all.
- **The emergence mechanism.** At the end of the nymphal period, the fully developed nymph crawls out of the water onto rocks, logs, or streamside vegetation. The nymphal exoskeleton splits and the adult emerges, pumping fluid into its wings to expand them. The wings dry and harden over minutes to hours. When the wings are functional, flight begins. At mass emergence, thousands of nymphs are simultaneously undergoing this process along the river corridor.
- **Water quality indicator.** Stoneflies (particularly large species like the salmonfly) are the most sensitive aquatic insects to water quality degradation. They require cold, clean, well-oxygenated water with a stable streambed. Their presence is used by aquatic biologists as a primary indicator of excellent stream quality; their absence indicates degradation. A river system that supports a mass stonefly emergence has passed the most rigorous biological quality test.
- **The salmonfly hatch.** The giant salmonfly (*Pteronarcys californica*) emergence is one of the most significant ecological events in Pacific Northwest rivers. The salmon River in Idaho, the Deschutes in Oregon, and Washington rivers including the Methow, Okanogan, and Yakima produce major salmonfly hatches in late April through June (depending on latitude and elevation). The salmonfly hatch is also a major fishing event: large salmonfly adults are eaten voraciously by trout, and the hatch produces some of the most intense surface-feeding by fish of any event in the annual cycle.

(Sources: Order Plecoptera 3500 species worldwide 600 North America; Pteronarcys californica giant salmonfly 4-5cm body 6-8cm wingspan; hemimetabolism incomplete metamorphosis; nymphal period 1-3 years cold oxygenated streams; adult stage no feeding reproduction only; emergence mechanism nymph crawling exoskeleton split wing expansion; water quality indicator most sensitive aquatic insect; salmonfly hatch Pacific Northwest rivers Methow Okanogan Yakima Deschutes; salmonfly as trout feeding trigger)

## CADDISFLIES -- THE TRICHOPTERA

### The Case Builders of the Stream Ecosystem

The caddisfly (Order Trichoptera) is the stonefly's most frequent emergence partner:

- **Taxonomy and diversity.** The Order Trichoptera contains approximately 14,500 known species worldwide, making it significantly more diverse than Plecoptera. In the Pacific Northwest, caddisflies are represented by a wide range of genera including *Rhyacophila*, *Glossosoma*, *Hydropsyche*, *Brachycentrus*, and many others. Caddisfly body sizes range from very small (2-3 mm body length) to moderately large (2-3 cm body length), with wingspan roughly proportional.
- **The case-building behavior.** Caddisfly larvae (the equivalent of the stonefly nymph) are famous for building protective cases from materials in their environment: sand grains, small pebbles, plant material, or silk. Different species build characteristic case types -- some build tubes of sand grains arranged in spiral patterns, others build log-cabin structures from rectangular pieces of plant material, others build cone-shaped pebble cases. The case is a portable shelter that the larva carries throughout its feeding period.
- **Life cycle.** Caddisflies undergo complete metamorphosis (holometabolism): egg, larva, pupa, adult. The larval period is the longest and is spent building and inhabiting the case. Pupation occurs within a sealed case. The adult caddisfly is moth-like in appearance (closely related to Lepidoptera, the moths and butterflies) with wings covered in fine hairs (hence the order name: Trichoptera = "hair wings").
- **The emergence acoustic signature.** Adult caddisflies emerge from the river by swimming or crawling to the surface, splitting the pupal case, and flying. At mass emergence, the simultaneous flight initiation of thousands or millions of caddisflies produces a wing-buzz that can fill the entire river corridor. Caddisfly adults fly in dense swarms, creating a sustained roar of insect flight that is distinctly different from the lower-pitched wing-buzz of the larger stoneflies. The combination of stonefly wing-buzz (lower pitch, individually audible at close range) and caddisfly wing-buzz (higher pitch, only audible as combined mass) produces the multi-frequency acoustic signature of the joint hatch.
- **Caddisfly swarms.** Mating swarms of adult caddisflies can number in the millions along productive river corridors. The Yakima River in Washington, the Kootenai River in Idaho and Montana, and many other Pacific Northwest streams produce caddisfly hatches that are large enough to appear on weather radar -- the same phenomenon observed with mayfly hatches in the Midwest.

(Sources: Order Trichoptera 14500 species worldwide; Rhyacophila Glossosoma Hydropsyche Brachycentrus Pacific Northwest genera; case-building behavior sand pebble plant material silk; holometabolism complete metamorphosis egg larva pupa adult; case types spiral tube log-cabin cone pebble; Trichoptera = hair wings; caddisfly related to Lepidoptera moths butterflies; adult emergence split pupal case flight; mass emergence wing-buzz fills river corridor; caddisfly swarms appearing on weather radar Yakima Kootenai)

## THE JOINT HATCH -- SIMULTANEOUS EMERGENCE

### Why Stonefly and Caddisfly Emerge Together in April

The April joint hatch in Pacific Northwest rivers:

- **Temperature triggers.** Both stoneflies and caddisflies use water temperature as the primary trigger for emergence timing. For most large salmonfly species, emergence is triggered when water temperatures reach and sustain approximately 10-12°C. Many caddisfly species in the same stream use similar temperature windows. In Pacific Northwest rivers, late April (depending on the specific river and its elevation) typically provides these water temperatures for lower-elevation streams and coastal rivers.
- **The synchronized emergence benefit.** Mass simultaneous emergence is an antipredator strategy: by emerging simultaneously, insects "swamp" predators -- more individuals emerge than can be consumed by the available predators (fish, birds, bats). A single large salmonfly emerging alone is almost certainly eaten; a million salmonflies emerging simultaneously overwhelm the predator field. The acoustic consequence is proportionate: individual emergence is nearly silent; mass emergence fills the river corridor.
- **River corridor scale.** A major salmonfly + caddisfly joint hatch occurs simultaneously along many miles of river corridor -- not just at one location. The emergence front moves upstream with warming water temperatures, with lower elevations hatching first and higher elevations hatching weeks later. The acoustic event at degree 329 documents the hatch at a specific location, but the ecological event spans the river system.
- **The fish response.** Both stonefly and caddisfly adults are primary food sources for trout and other salmonids during their emergence. The sight of adult insects on or above the water surface triggers intense surface feeding behavior in fish -- the "rise" that fly fishers observe. The mass emergence event is simultaneously an acoustic event (insect wing-buzz) and a visual event (thousands of insects in the air) and a hydrological event (the fish surface-feeding response creates its own acoustic contribution -- splashing rises visible and audible across the river surface).

(Sources: water temperature emergence trigger 10-12°C; late April lower-elevation Pacific Northwest streams; synchronized emergence antipredator swamping strategy; predator swamping individual vs. mass emergence survival; river corridor scale many miles; elevation gradient hatch progression; fish rise response to adult emergence; surface feeding acoustic contribution)

## THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- E=9 MASS EMERGENCE WING-BUZZ

### The Frequency, Amplitude, and Temporal Dimensions

The stonefly + caddisfly joint hatch acoustic analysis:

- **Giant salmonfly wing-buzz.** Adult *Pteronarcys californica* are large insects -- body length 4-5 cm, wingspan 6-8 cm. Their wings beat at approximately 30-40 Hz (wing-beat frequency), producing a fundamental tone in the lower acoustic range with harmonics extending upward. Individually, a salmonfly in flight produces approximately 40-55 dB at 1 meter. At mass emergence density (hundreds to thousands of individuals per linear meter of river corridor), the combined sound approaches 70-85 dB across the full corridor. The sound is described by observers as a low roar or heavy buzz -- unmistakably biological, unlike any geophysical sound.
- **Caddisfly wing-buzz.** Adult caddisflies are smaller than salmonflies, with wing-beat frequencies in the 200-400 Hz range depending on species and body size. The caddisfly wing-buzz is higher-pitched than the salmonfly's and produces a different acoustic texture -- more of a hiss or high-frequency roar than the salmonfly's lower buzz. At mass emergence of millions of individuals, the caddisfly contribution to the joint hatch acoustic creates the high-frequency component of the combined sound.
- **The joint hatch frequency profile.** Stonefly + caddisfly simultaneously: (1) low-frequency component from salmonfly wing-beat at 30-40 Hz fundamental, 60-120 Hz harmonics; (2) mid-frequency component from medium-sized stonefly species at 60-80 Hz wing-beat; (3) high-frequency component from caddisfly wing-beat at 200-400 Hz; (4) the combined broadband roar of insect flight filling the full lower-to-mid acoustic spectrum. The river corridor during the joint hatch: approximately 75-90 dB at the river's edge, covering 30-400 Hz with energy distributed across the full range.
- **E=9 calibration.** E=9 is the correct calibration for the mass emergence event. The acoustic energy is not the highest in the engine (the Puget Sound winter storm surge at E=9 is louder in absolute terms), but the biological specificity and mass-event character of the insect emergence creates its own intensity category. The wing-buzz of thousands of stoneflies and caddisflies filling an entire river corridor is a sound that cannot be reduced to a single source; it is the sound of mass biological action, and its E=9 rating reflects the overwhelming character of that mass action at its peak.
- **Duration.** The peak acoustic event during a mass emergence lasts hours -- typically from mid-morning (when water temperatures have warmed enough for active flight) through late afternoon. The peak flight activity occurs when adult insects are actively swarming for mating. The acoustic environment of the river corridor during this window is defined by the insect wing-buzz to a degree that drowns out normal river sounds.

(Sources: Pteronarcys californica wing-beat 30-40 Hz; salmonfly individual sound 40-55 dB; mass density 70-85 dB; caddisfly wing-beat 200-400 Hz higher frequency; joint hatch frequency profile 30-400 Hz; river corridor 75-90 dB during peak; E=9 calibration mass biological event; hatch duration mid-morning to late afternoon; peak flight mating swarm timing)

## STONEFLY + CADDISFLY IN THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST ECOSYSTEM

### The Ecological Significance of the Joint Hatch

- **Salmonid indicator species.** Stonefly (particularly Plecoptera) and caddisfly (Trichoptera) abundance is the most direct measure of Pacific Northwest stream quality. Rivers that support mass emergence events are rivers that support Chinook salmon, coho salmon, steelhead, bull trout, and other salmonids. The joint hatch is the river ecosystem at full health, documented acoustically.
- **Key rivers for Pacific Northwest mass emergence.** The Methow River (Okanogan County, WA): major salmonfly hatch in late April-May, excellent trout fishing during the hatch. The Yakima River (Kittitas/Yakima Counties, WA): one of the most productive caddisfly and stonefly rivers in Washington, supporting a large fly fishing community. The North Fork Stillaguamish (Snohomish County, WA): important bull trout and salmonfly habitat. The Hoh River (Jefferson County, WA, see SPS-324): major stonefly habitat within the Olympic National Park protected area.
- **The fly fishing connection.** Pacific Northwest fly fishing culture is organized around the stonefly and caddisfly hatch schedule. The emergence calendar -- which river hatches which species in which week of the year -- is the primary planning tool for fly fishing in the region. The acoustic event documented at degree 329 is also the event that tens of thousands of Pacific Northwest fly fishers plan their seasons around.
- **The bat contribution.** Bats are the primary nocturnal predators of adult stoneflies and caddisflies. During and following the emergence period, bat activity over the river corridor increases dramatically as the insects provide abundant food. The bat echolocation calls add an additional acoustic layer to the post-dusk river environment during the hatch -- a higher-frequency, less audible component of the river corridor's acoustic ecology during the joint hatch period.

(Sources: Plecoptera Trichoptera stream quality indicator; salmonid habitat correlation; Methow River salmonfly hatch April-May; Yakima River caddisfly stonefly; North Fork Stillaguamish bull trout stonefly; Hoh River Olympic NP; fly fishing hatch calendar; bat predation adult stonefly caddisfly; bat echolocation acoustic layer post-dusk)
