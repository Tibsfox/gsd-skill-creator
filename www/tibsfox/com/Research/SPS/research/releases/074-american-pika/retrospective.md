## The Seventy-Fourth Degree: The Alpine Sentinel

### New Family, Back-to-Back Lagomorpha, Climate Indicator, Hay-Gathering, Highest Elevation

This is the seventy-fifth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-74), the engine's THIRTEENTH MAMMALIAN ENTRY (eleventh mammalian species), NEW FAMILY Ochotonidae (34th family), BACK-TO-BACK LAGOMORPHA with Snowshoe Hare (73, Leporidae), the HIGHEST ELEVATION species in the engine, the first CLIMATE CHANGE INDICATOR (populations retreating upslope, low-elevation extinctions), HAY-GATHERING behavior (cuts and dries vegetation for winter), does NOT HIBERNATE, and E=4 on the energy scale. The release carries seven structural contributions:

1. **NEW FAMILY: OCHOTONIDAE (34th FAMILY).** The pika family. Contains one genus with approximately 30 species. Fifth new family in six degrees (Mephitidae 69, Procyonidae 70, Canidae 71, Didelphidae 72, Ochotonidae 74).

2. **BACK-TO-BACK LAGOMORPHA (73-74).** Snowshoe Hare (73, Leporidae) and American Pika (74, Ochotonidae). Same order, consecutive degrees, different families. Parallels back-to-back Anatidae (58-59). Both North American lagomorph families now represented.

3. **HIGH SHARP "EENK" BARK -- 75th acoustic element.** Piercing alarm call. 3,000-10,000 Hz. Audible at 200-300 meters. E=4. The highest-elevation sound in the engine. Alpine acoustic.

4. **CLIMATE CHANGE INDICATOR.** The engine's first species whose conservation is primarily driven by warming temperatures. Populations retreating upslope. Low-elevation extinctions documented. Escalator to extinction. Requires global climate action.

5. **HAY-GATHERING.** Cuts and dries vegetation for winter storage. Haypiles of 15-30 kg (100+ times body weight). Does not hibernate. Active year-round beneath snowpack.

6. **HIGHEST ELEVATION SPECIES.** Alpine talus at 1,500-3,000+ meters. Above treeline. The engine's vertical frontier.

7. **THIRTEENTH MAMMALIAN ENTRY.** Eleventh species. Four orders. Eight families. Two infraclasses.

## Structural Event 3: Climate Change Indicator

- **Situation:** The American Pika is the engine's first species whose conservation status is primarily driven by warming temperatures. Populations retreating upslope. Low-elevation extinctions in the Great Basin. Cannot be saved by local action alone.
- **Root Cause:** Pikas are exquisitely heat-sensitive (lethal at 25.5 C). Dense alpine fur and high metabolic rate preclude warm-climate survival. The "escalator to extinction" -- habitable zone shrinks from below as temperatures rise. Mountain summits are finite.
- **Recommendation:** Document as the engine's first climate-driven conservation narrative. Note the distinction from other conservation stories (Spotted Owl: logging; Fisher: trapping/fragmentation). The Pika requires global climate action. This is a fundamentally different conservation challenge.
- **Carry Item:** Climate indicator species: 1 (American Pika, degree 74). First climate-driven conservation narrative. Track climate-sensitive species.

## Structural Event 4: Highest Elevation Species

- **Situation:** The American Pika inhabits alpine talus at 1,500-3,000+ meters. The highest elevation of any species in the engine. All previous species inhabit lower elevations.
- **Root Cause:** The Pika is obligately alpine -- it requires talus habitat above treeline. No previous species in the engine has been primarily alpine.
- **Recommendation:** Document as the engine's elevation ceiling. Note the elevation contrast with Snowshoe Hare (73, forest floor). Track elevation range of future species.
- **Carry Item:** Highest elevation: American Pika (74), 1,500-3,000+ meters. Previous species below 1,000 meters. Alpine habitat enters the engine.

## Structural Event 5: E=4 -- High Energy Returns

- **Situation:** E=4 at degree 74. The Pika's piercing bark is loud relative to body size and frequently heard by alpine visitors. Diurnal activity adds visibility. E=4 is the highest energy in recent mammalian entries.
- **Root Cause:** The Pika is diurnal (unlike recent nocturnal mammals), vocal (unlike secretive species), and lives in exposed alpine habitat where its bark carries far. The combination of visibility and acoustic conspicuousness warrants E=4.
- **Recommendation:** Document E=4 as a break from the recent low-energy mammalian run (Opossum E=2, Hare E=3). Note diurnal activity as a contributing factor.
- **Carry Item:** E=4 at degree 74. Energy distribution update. Diurnal mammal -- first since Fisher (66).

The Pika at 1,500-3,000+ meters versus all previous species below 1,000 meters. The elevation leap is dramatic and opens the engine's alpine ecological portrait. The vertical frontier.
