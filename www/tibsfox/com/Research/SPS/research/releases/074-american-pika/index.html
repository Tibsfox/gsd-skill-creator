American Pika

Ochotona princeps
high sharp “eenk” bark — CLIMATE CHANGE INDICATOR, alpine/subalpine talus slopes, NEW FAMILY Ochotonidae (34th family), back-to-back Lagomorpha with Snowshoe Hare (73), highest elevation species in the engine, hay-gathering (alpine farming), does NOT hibernate, E=4, thirteenth mammalian entry

Climate Change Indicator — The Escalator to Extinction

The American Pika is the engine’s first species whose conservation status is primarily driven by climate warming. Pikas can die from heat stress at temperatures as low as 25.5°C (78°F). As temperatures warm, Pika populations are retreating upslope — and some low-elevation populations have already gone extinct. The Pika cannot move higher when it is already at the summit. It cannot adapt faster than the climate is changing. The escalator to extinction: the habitable zone shrinks from below. The summit is finite. This is the canary in the alpine coal mine.

The 75th Acoustic Element: High Sharp “Eenk” Bark

A short, loud, piercing alarm call — approximately 3,000–10,000 Hz, audible at 200–300 meters across open alpine terrain. The Pika sits on a prominent boulder, body erect, and delivers the bark with its whole body. E=4 — the loudest mammalian call relative to body size in the engine. The highest-elevation sound in the engine. Alpine acoustic: thin air, vast sightlines, rock echo. The Pika is far easier to hear than to see.

Hay-Gathering — The Alpine Farmer

The Pika’s most remarkable behavior: cutting and drying vegetation for winter food storage. Fresh plants are carried to flat rocks and laid out to dry in the sun — literally making hay. Once dried, moved into talus interior. Haypiles weigh 15–30 kg — more than 100 times the Pika’s own body weight. The Pika does NOT hibernate — it feeds on stored hay through 6–8 months of alpine winter, active year-round beneath the snowpack.

Back-to-Back Lagomorpha (73–74) — Like Back-to-Back Anatidae (58–59)

The Snowshoe Hare (73, Leporidae) and American Pika (74, Ochotonidae) are consecutive Lagomorpha — same order, different families. This parallels the back-to-back Anatidae at degrees 58–59 (Wood Duck and Mallard). Forest hare versus alpine pika. Low elevation versus highest elevation. 1.3–2.2 kg versus 120–185 grams. Color change versus haypile. Thumping alarm versus barking alarm. Both active year-round. Neither hibernates. The Lagomorpha span the entire elevation gradient.

The Sound: High Sharp “Eenk” Bark (75th Acoustic Element)

The bark: A short, loud, high-pitched alarm call — approximately 0.1–0.2 seconds in duration, centered at approximately 3,000–10,000 Hz. Variously transcribed as “eenk,” “eek,” or “meep.” One of the sharpest, most piercing mammalian vocalizations in the PNW. The Pika sits on a prominent boulder and delivers the bark with its whole body, the sound carrying across the talus field [1, 2, 3].

Two call types: (1) Short call (alarm) — single sharp “eenk” when a predator is detected. Given singly or in series of 2–5 calls. (2) Long call (territorial/mating) — a longer series of linked calls over 1–3 seconds. More common during breeding season [1, 2, 3, 6].

Why E=4: Diurnal (unlike recent nocturnal mammals). Vocal and acoustically conspicuous. Lives in exposed alpine habitat where the bark carries far. The Pika is frequently heard by hikers — far easier to hear than to see. The loudest mammalian call relative to body size in the engine.

Taxonomy: Ochotona princeps

Etymology: Ochotona from Mongolian ogdoi/ochodona, the local name for pikas. princeps from Latin “chief” — the first American pika described scientifically (Richardson, 1828). Common name “Pika” from Tungusic piika [1, 2].

Classification: Order Lagomorpha. Family Ochotonidae — NEW FAMILY (34th). The pika family, containing one genus (Ochotona) with approximately 30 species worldwide. Fifth new family in six degrees. The Pika is NOT a rodent — Lagomorpha diverged from Rodentia approximately 80–90 million years ago [1, 2, 3].

Physical Description: The Alpine Tennis Ball

Size: 162–216 mm, 120–185 grams. About the size of a tennis ball with ears. No visible tail (vestigial, ~9–12 mm, hidden in fur). Compact, egg-shaped body. Short limbs. Fur-soled feet [1, 2, 3].

Ears: Short, rounded, 20–26 mm — unlike the long ears of rabbits and hares. Dark-edged. Minimizes heat loss at alpine elevations.

Fur: Dense, soft, grayish-brown to cinnamon-brown. Exceptionally thick for body size — critical insulation for alpine cold. Two molts per year. Summer: shorter, more reddish. Winter: longer, denser, grayer.

Climate Change: The Escalator to Extinction

Heat sensitivity: Lethal at 25.5°C (78°F) with extended exposure. Dense fur and high metabolic rate adapted for cold make pikas extremely vulnerable to warming. Primary thermoregulation: behavioral retreat to cool talus interstices [1, 2, 3, 4, 5].

Population losses: In the Great Basin mountains, surveys document loss of Pika populations from lower-elevation sites over the past century. Sites recorded in the early 1900s are now empty. Range contracting from lower and southern margins [4, 5].

Washington status: Currently relatively secure due to cooler, wetter climate. But warming trends push the lower boundary higher. Long-term projections suggest significant habitat loss by mid-century. Monitored as a climate indicator species [1, 2, 3].

Conservation contrast: The Spotted Owl (4) can be protected by forest management. The Fisher (66) can be protected by trapping bans. The Pika can only be protected by slowing global warming. The first species in the engine requiring planetary-scale intervention.

Hay-Gathering: Alpine Farming

The process: Mid-summer, the Pika cuts grasses, sedges, and wildflowers, carries them to flat rocks, and lays them out to dry. Once dry, the vegetation is cached in the talus interior. Haypiles of 15–30 kg sustain the Pika through 6–8 months of winter [1, 2, 3, 6].

Selectivity: Pikas preferentially cache high-calorie plants. Sometimes cache mildly toxic plants whose toxins break down during drying — a natural preservative. Sophisticated food quality assessment.

No hibernation: The Pika remains active year-round. Lives beneath the snowpack in winter, feeding on stored hay. The snowpack insulates — near-freezing temperatures in the talus while surface temperatures plunge far below zero.

Talus Habitat: The Highest Elevation in the Engine

Elevation: 1,500–3,000+ meters in Washington — the highest elevation of any species in the engine. Alpine and subalpine talus: broken rock with interstitial spaces. Above treeline or at its upper edge [1, 2, 3].

Thermal refuge: Talus interstices maintain cool internal temperatures even during warm summer days. Air circulation through boulder gaps creates natural refrigeration. The Pika exploits this: active on the surface during cooler periods, retreating to talus during warm midday.
