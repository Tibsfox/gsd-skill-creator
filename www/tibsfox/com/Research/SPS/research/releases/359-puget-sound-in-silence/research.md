## THE FINAL SPS ENTRY -- THE SILENCE THAT IS NOT SILENCE

### What Remains When the Human Noise Is Removed

Puget Sound -- the inland sea of western Washington -- is one of the most acoustically busy bodies of water in the Pacific Northwest. Vessel traffic, ferry routes, industrial operations, and recreational boating create a sustained anthropogenic acoustic environment that begins before 6 AM and continues past midnight throughout the year. The ambient noise level of central Puget Sound under normal conditions is dominated by broadband vessel noise in the 10-1,000 Hz range.

But the engine's final SPS entry is not about the Sound as humans have made it. It is about what the Sound sounds like in conditions of low human acoustic disturbance -- the acoustic baseline that existed before the anthropogenic era and that persists in the gaps between vessel passages, in the deeper basins where vessel noise attenuates, and in the rare moments when weather and scheduling create windows of quiet.

Puget Sound in silence is not silent. It is the acoustic environment of the water column itself: the sound of water moving through the fjord basins, the biological sounds of the Sound's resident species, the infrasonic and low-frequency geological sounds of the region, and the acoustic properties of the fjord's geometry that shape all sound within it. The "silence" is the removal of the human acoustic layer. What remains is the Sound.

## THE WATER COLUMN -- THE ACOUSTIC PHYSICS OF PUGET SOUND

### A Fjord System. Deep Basins. Sound Channeling.

Puget Sound is not a simple bay or coastal inlet. It is a glacially carved fjord system: a series of deep basins separated by shallow sills, connected to the Strait of Juan de Fuca and ultimately the Pacific Ocean. The fjord geometry creates specific acoustic properties that distinguish the Sound from open coastal waters.

**The basins.** The main basin of Puget Sound has depths to approximately 280 meters. Hood Canal (the elongated fjord to the west) has depths to approximately 177 meters. These deep basins create an acoustic environment where low-frequency sounds (below approximately 100 Hz) can propagate with minimal attenuation -- the deep water is a waveguide for low-frequency acoustic energy.

**The sills.** The shallow sills separating Puget Sound's basins (particularly the Tacoma Narrows, where current speeds reach 8-9 knots during tidal exchange) create acoustic reflectors and transmission barriers at certain frequencies. The tidal dynamics of the sills also generate their own acoustic signature: the turbulent flow of tidal exchange through the narrows is an ongoing source of broadband low-frequency acoustic energy.

**Sound speed in Puget Sound.** Sound travels approximately 1,480-1,520 m/s in Puget Sound, varying with temperature and salinity stratification. The Sound has a well-developed seasonal thermocline (a layer of rapidly changing temperature at 20-50 meter depth) that creates an acoustic refraction layer -- sounds produced above and below the thermocline behave differently. The thermocline is itself an acoustic structure.

**The fjord as resonator.** At certain frequencies, the fjord geometry (length, width, depth) creates resonant modes -- standing acoustic waves. The fundamental resonant frequency of Puget Sound's main basin is in the infrasonic range (below 20 Hz). In conditions of low disturbance, these infrasonic resonant modes are the Sound's own voice.

## THE BIOLOGICAL ACOUSTIC LAYER -- LIFE IN THE WATER COLUMN

### Snapping Shrimp. Harbor Seal. Orca. The Resident Acoustic Community.

Puget Sound is an acoustically rich biological environment. The resident species that contribute to the ambient acoustic environment in conditions of low human disturbance:

**Snapping Shrimp (*Alpheidae*).** The most acoustically active invertebrate community in temperate coastal waters. Snapping shrimp produce loud clicks (by rapidly closing their enlarged snapper claw) that collectively create a sustained crackling background noise in the 2-15 kHz range. In warm, shallow areas of Puget Sound (particularly sheltered bays and rocky shorelines), snapping shrimp choruses produce the dominant background acoustic signal when vessel noise is absent. The sound is distinctive: not a single snap but a continuous high-frequency crackle, like static, that rises and falls with temperature and density of the shrimp populations.

**Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina*) underwater calls.** Harbor seals produce underwater vocalizations in the 100-8,000 Hz range, including breeding calls, threat vocalizations, and contact calls. The underwater calls are more complex than their above-water vocalizations and include tonal signals, pulsed signals, and complex frequency-modulated calls that carry in the fjord's water column. In haul-out areas and in waters near haul-out sites, harbor seal underwater vocalizations contribute a low-to-mid frequency biological acoustic layer.

**Southern Resident Killer Whale ambient calls.** In the absence of active foraging, Southern Resident Killer Whales produce ambient calls at lower rates than during active foraging. The discrete call repertoire, the pod-specific dialects, and the long-range communication calls of orca are present in Puget Sound acoustic recordings whenever the population is present. These calls (typically 1-15 kHz) propagate through the fjord's waveguide and can be detected far from the calling animals.

**Rockfish and groundfish acoustic contributions.** Some Puget Sound resident fish species produce sound: certain rockfish (*Sebastes* spp.) and sculpin produce acoustic signals during spawning and territorial contexts. These contributions are less documented than cetacean and pinniped acoustics but are present in the baseline ambient.

## THE INFRASONIC LAYER -- BELOW THE THRESHOLD OF HEARING

### What Is Present That Cannot Be Heard

The infrasonic layer of Puget Sound's acoustic environment -- sounds below 20 Hz, below the nominal threshold of human hearing -- is present continuously and is the Sound's most fundamental acoustic dimension.

**Microseisms.** The continuous background of seismic microseisms (below 1 Hz) produced by the interaction of ocean swell with the continental shelf and coastline is detectable in Puget Sound. The Pacific Ocean's dominant swell period (8-18 seconds, corresponding to 0.06-0.12 Hz) generates microseismic energy that propagates through the solid earth and into the water column of the inland sea. Puget Sound sits at the terminus of a seismic pathway from the open Pacific.

**Tectonic background.** The Cascadia Subduction Zone -- the most seismically significant tectonic structure in the Pacific Northwest -- generates continuous low-level seismic background and occasional discrete events that are acoustic as well as seismic. The Juan de Fuca Plate's subduction beneath the North American Plate produces a background tectonic acoustic signature that is measurable in the water column and is the geological context of the entire engine's regional ecology.

**Near-infrasonic weather.** Large pressure systems passing over the region generate infrasonic acoustic signatures from the interaction of wind with topography. The Olympic Mountains to the west and the Cascades to the east channel atmospheric infrasound that propagates into the Sound.

**The water itself.** Water molecules in tidal flow, in thermal stratification mixing, and in the basic hydraulic dynamics of the fjord produce acoustic energy at frequencies too low to hear and too distributed in the water column to be experienced as a point source. The Sound has a voice that is entirely below the range of human experience.

(Sources: Microseisms below 1 Hz Pacific swell 8-18 seconds 0.06-0.12 Hz continental shelf; Puget Sound terminus seismic pathway; Cascadia Subduction Zone Juan de Fuca Plate seismic background; tectonic acoustic signature water column; near-infrasonic weather pressure systems Olympic Mountains Cascades channeling; tidal flow thermal mixing hydraulic dynamics infrasonic; Sound's voice below human hearing range)

## THE ACOUSTIC BASELINE -- THE ANTHROPOCENE CONTRAST

### What the Sound Was Before. What Returns in Quiet Windows.

The concept of acoustic baseline -- the sound of an environment in the absence of human disturbance -- was developed formally in acoustic ecology, particularly in the work of R. Murray Schafer and the World Soundscape Project (Simon Fraser University, 1970s) and expanded in the work of Bernie Krause (soundscape ecology, "biophony, geophony, anthrophony" framework). Puget Sound's acoustic baseline is one of the most documented because the contrast with current conditions is extreme and measurable.

Pre-contact Puget Sound acoustic environment: dominated by biophony (the biological sounds of the ecosystem) and geophony (the geological and meteorological acoustic sources). The Sound's baseline was approximately 40-60 dB SPL in the 10-1,000 Hz range, produced by biological and geological sources. Current vessel-traffic-influenced ambient noise: 80-110 dB SPL in the same frequency range during active vessel transit. The difference is 20-50 dB -- a factor of 100 to 100,000 in acoustic power.

In low-disturbance windows -- early mornings before ferry traffic, during weather events that reduce vessel traffic, in the deeper basins where attenuation reduces vessel noise -- Puget Sound's ambient noise approaches the pre-anthropogenic baseline. These windows are rare, brief, and getting rarer as vessel traffic increases. But they exist.

The final SPS entry is this window: the Sound at its baseline acoustic state, in a moment of low human disturbance, with the water column's own acoustic environment as the primary signal. Not the absence of sound. The presence of the Sound's own voice.

The E=10 calibration for Puget Sound in Silence is the engine's most unusual calibration: E=10 for a quiet acoustic environment, not a loud one. The calibration rests on a different application of the ceiling concept: this is not the loudest entry in the engine. It is the deepest.

The depth principle: the acoustic environment of Puget Sound's water column in low-disturbance conditions represents the greatest temporal depth in the engine's entire 360-degree span. The infrasonic geological baseline has been continuous since the fjord's formation at the close of the last ice age, approximately 15,000 years ago. The Cascadia Subduction Zone's acoustic signature has been continuous for longer. The snapping shrimp communities have been producing their crackling chorus for millions of years of Pacific coastal evolution. The water itself has been moving through these basins for 15,000 years.

E=10 for the oldest, deepest, most temporally extended acoustic event in the engine. The engine opened at degree 000 with Quincy Jones -- born 1933, the most recent origin in human terms. It closes at degree 359 with an acoustic environment that precedes the human species by millions of years. The depth is the ceiling.
