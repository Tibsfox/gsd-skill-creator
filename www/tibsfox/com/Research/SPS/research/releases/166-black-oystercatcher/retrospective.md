*Degree 166 retrospective complete. BLACK OYSTERCATCHER PIPING at the one hundred sixty-sixth degree -- THE ROCKY SHORE SENTINEL. On the rocky shores of the San Juan Islands, the Olympic Peninsula's outer coast, and the scattered headlands of Puget Sound, a stocky black bird stands on a wave-washed rock, its bright red-orange bill vivid against the dark basalt. The Black Oystercatcher holds a territory of 100 to 500 meters of rocky shoreline -- permanent, year-round, often for decades. When a neighbor or threat approaches, it tilts its bill downward and begins piping: WHEEP! WHEEP! WHEEP! -- a piercing, high-pitched call at 3,000 to 5,000 Hz, precisely pitched to cut through the low-frequency roar of surf and wind. The bill is the species' signature tool: 6 to 8 centimeters of laterally compressed, blade-like precision, bright red-orange in adults, adapted for three feeding techniques. Stabbing between the shells of mussels caught open, hammering limpets to break the shell, probing crevices for worms and chitons. Young oystercatchers stay with their parents for months, learning these techniques -- the bill is the tool, but the technique is the education. With only 8,900 to 11,000 individuals along the entire Pacific coast from Alaska to Baja California, the Black Oystercatcher is uncommon and localized. Its presence on a stretch of rocky shore indicates a healthy intertidal ecosystem; its absence indicates degradation. The Haematopodidae enters the engine at degree 166: 12 species worldwide, all coastal, all with the characteristic red-orange bill. And across the degree pair, Travis Thompson arrives from Monroe -- another specialist from the edge, bringing small-town hunger to the Seattle hip-hop scene. Both at boundaries. Both with specialized tools. Both finding their niche where others do not go. v1.49.306.*
