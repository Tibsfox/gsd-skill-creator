## HALIAEETUS LEUCOCEPHALUS -- THE SPECIES

### The Bald Eagle: Biology and Pacific Northwest Context

*Haliaeetus leucocephalus* (Linnaeus, 1766) is the only eagle endemic to North America:

- **Identification.** Adults: white head and tail, dark brown body, massive yellow bill, wingspan 6-7.5 feet (180-230 cm). One of the largest raptors in North America. Immature birds are mottled brown-white through approximately four years before reaching adult plumage. The adult plumage -- white head and tail -- is iconic but not present until the eagle reaches reproductive maturity at 4-5 years.
- **Scientific name.** *Haliaeetus* (from Greek: halos = sea, aetos = eagle -- "sea eagle"); *leucocephalus* (from Greek: leukos = white, kephale = head -- "white head"). The white-headed sea eagle. Named by Linnaeus in 1766. The name captures both the bird's habitat preference (water) and its diagnostic field mark (white head).
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** The Bald Eagle is one of the most characteristic large raptors of the Pacific Northwest. Washington State has one of the highest Bald Eagle populations in the contiguous United States, with the highest concentrations along the Skagit River (for smelt and salmon runs), the San Juan Islands, Puget Sound shorelines, and major coastal rivers. The Skagit River Bald Eagle interpretive site documents hundreds of eagles congregating in December-January for the chum salmon run.
- **Feeding ecology.** Primarily a fish specialist but highly opportunistic. In the Pacific Northwest, the primary food sources are salmon and steelhead (fresh-caught or scavenged from spawning runs), waterfowl, and carrion. The dependence on salmon connects Bald Eagle ecology directly to the salmon ecosystem -- one of the central threads in the SPS engine's ecological documentation.
- **Population status.** Removed from the federal endangered species list in 2007 after a remarkable recovery from DDT-related decline. Bald Eagle populations in the Pacific Northwest have been growing steadily since the 1970s. The recovery is one of the most successful conservation stories in North American wildlife management.

(Sources: Haliaeetus leucocephalus identification; wingspan 6-7.5 feet; Haliaeetus = sea eagle etymology; leucocephalus = white head etymology; Linnaeus 1766; Washington State Bald Eagle population; Skagit River concentration; San Juan Islands; salmon dependency; delisted from ESA 2007; population recovery history)

## THE LOCKED-TALON AERIAL COMBAT DISPLAY

### Physics and Biology of Aerial Cartwheeling

The locked-talon combat display is one of the most extraordinary behaviors in North American raptor biology:

- **The mechanics.** Two eagles in aerial conflict approach each other at speed. At the moment of conflict, both extend their talons and lock together -- grasping each other's feet with their powerful talons. The combined weight of two eagles (4-14 lbs each, typically 5-9 lbs for adults) plus the aerodynamic forces acting on two locked birds in freefall produces a tumbling, cartwheeling motion. The birds spin around their combined center of mass, sometimes completing multiple full rotations before separating.
- **Freefall duration.** Eagles have been documented tumbling in locked-talon freefall for 100 feet or more. The descent is rapid. The commitment required -- to lock talons with an adversary at altitude and accept the freefall -- is physiologically and behaviorally extreme. The display demonstrates willingness to accept serious risk in defense of territory.
- **Separation.** Before impact, the birds generally separate -- releasing their grip and spreading their wings to arrest the descent. The timing of separation requires judgment and physical capability. Occasionally eagles fail to separate in time and hit the ground or water together; this can result in injury or drowning.
- **Not combat for its own sake.** The locked-talon display is primarily communicative: it establishes which eagle is willing to commit to the most extreme territorial defense. An intruder that witnesses or experiences the locked-talon response learns that the territory holder will escalate to maximum commitment. The display often ends the territorial dispute without further escalation.
- **The sound of the display.** The screaming that accompanies the locked-talon display is the kee-kee-kee of the contact call pushed to maximum: continuous, high-pitched, urgent. Both birds vocalize throughout. The combined screaming of two adult Bald Eagles in maximum territorial engagement is audible from long distances and is one of the most striking sounds in the Pacific Northwest sky.

(Sources: Locked-talon cartwheeling mechanics; eagle weight 4-14 lbs; freefall physics; separation timing; injury risk if separation fails; display as territorial communication; distance audibility of combat screaming; kee-kee-kee at maximum intensity)

## TERRITORIAL BEHAVIOR AND NESTING ECOLOGY

### The Territory as Ecological Resource

Bald Eagle territorial behavior is inseparable from nesting ecology:

- **Nesting territory.** Bald Eagles maintain nesting territories centered on the nest site (aerie). The territory size varies with habitat quality and prey availability: in areas with abundant salmon (Skagit River, coastal rivers), territories may be smaller because prey is concentrated. In areas with dispersed prey, territories may extend over several miles of waterfront.
- **Nest structure.** The Bald Eagle nest is one of the largest bird nests in North America. Eagles use the same nest for many years, adding material each season. The largest recorded nests are 10 feet across, 20 feet deep, and weigh over 2,000 lbs. The nest is itself a territorial marker: visible from long distances, a permanent structure that signals occupancy.
- **Year-round territory defense.** Bald Eagles are year-round residents in most of their Pacific Northwest range. Territory defense is most intense during breeding season (nest building in November-January, incubation February-March, chick-rearing April-July), but territories are maintained year-round in areas with resident populations.
- **The aerial combat timing.** The locked-talon aerial combat is most commonly observed during late winter and early spring -- when egg-laying is imminent and the investment in the territory is at its annual maximum. A territory holder protecting a nest with eggs has maximum motivation to commit to extreme territorial defense. This timing makes the combat display most intense precisely when the stakes are highest.
- **Skagit River context.** The Skagit River valley in northwest Washington is one of the most significant Bald Eagle territories in the Pacific Northwest. The annual chum salmon run (November-January) concentrates hundreds of eagles along specific river reaches. During this period, territorial conflicts between resident and wintering eagles are frequent. The locked-talon displays are most commonly observed here.

(Sources: Bald Eagle nesting territory; nest (aerie) size: up to 10 feet across, 20 feet deep, 2000+ lbs; year-round residency; breeding season November-July; aerial combat timing: late winter/early spring when nest investment maximum; Skagit River eagle concentration; resident vs. wintering eagle conflicts)
