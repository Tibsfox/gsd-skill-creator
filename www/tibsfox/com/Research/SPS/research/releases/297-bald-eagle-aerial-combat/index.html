Acoustic Profile — Aerial Combat Sound

Vocalization: The kee-kee-kee contact call pushed to maximum urgency — continuous, high-pitched screaming from both birds throughout the locked-talon display. Not a different call: the same call, fully amplified and sustained.

Audibility: Audible from hundreds of meters. Two adult Bald Eagles at maximum territorial commitment produce a combined sound that carries across the territory. Witnesses report hearing the screaming before seeing the display.

E=7 calibration: Maximum territorial behavior. The display requires full physical commitment, accepts risk of injury, and communicates willingness to escalate beyond ambient contact. E=7: not ambient (E=3-4), not the contact call (E=5), but maximum behavioral engagement short of actual injurious combat.

The Locked-Talon Tumble — The Physics

Two eagles approach in conflict. At the moment of contact, both extend their talons and lock together — grasping each other's feet with their powerful grip. The combined weight of two adults (typically 5-9 lbs each) plus aerodynamic forces acting on the locked pair produces tumbling, cartwheeling freefall. Eagles have been documented tumbling 100 feet or more before separation. The timing of separation — releasing the grip and spreading wings to arrest descent — requires precision. Occasionally birds fail to separate in time and hit the ground or water together; drowning is a documented outcome when birds land in water while unable to disengage.

The display is primarily communicative: the territory holder demonstrates willingness to commit to extreme physical engagement. An intruder that experiences or witnesses this response learns the territory holder will escalate without limit. The display ends most territorial disputes without further escalation.

Haliaeetus leucocephalus — Pacific Northwest Context

Washington State has one of the highest Bald Eagle populations in the contiguous United States. The Skagit River valley concentrates hundreds of eagles in November-January for the chum salmon run — one of the most significant wildlife spectacles in the Pacific Northwest. The San Juan Islands, Puget Sound shorelines, and major coastal rivers hold resident breeding populations year-round. The species was delisted from the federal endangered species list in 2007 after a remarkable recovery from DDT-related population collapse. The recovery is one of North American conservation's most successful stories.
