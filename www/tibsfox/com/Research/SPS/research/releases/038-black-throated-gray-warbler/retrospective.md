# Release 038 Retrospective -- Black-throated Gray Warbler (Degree 38)

**Release:** 038 of 360
**Species:** Black-throated Gray Warbler (*Setophaga nigrescens*)
**Sound:** lazy buzzy wheeze song
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Engine:** PNW 360 Species (Part B of The Sound of Puget Sound)

---

## Purpose

This is the thirty-ninth release of the PNW 360 Species engine (degrees 0-38), the sixth Parulidae, and the return of the warbler family after an eighteen-degree absence. The engine needed to explore what happens when a structurally complete family arc (the warbler quintet at degrees 14-17, 20) reopens in a new key: different energy, different ecosystem, different timbre, different tempo. The Black-throated Gray Warbler delivers the engine's first dry woodland habitat, its laziest warbler song, and the transformation of the *Setophaga* genus from vivid yellow to monochrome ink.

Four structural contributions:

1. **The drawl -- the 39th acoustic element.** A slow, buzzy, unhurried warbler song from an oak-pine canopy. After three consecutive hummingbird sounds that traded in extremes (thrum, sonic boom, filament), the drawl decelerates the engine into warbler time. Each buzzy phrase gets its full measure. No acceleration. No crescendo. No evaporation. The laziest sound in the Parulidae. The antithesis of urgency.

2. **Parulidae returns after 18 degrees -- the longest family gap followed by a return.** The warbler quintet (14-17, 20) mapped five song endings and was structurally closed. The sixth warbler does not reopen the quintet. It opens a new chapter: E=4 (first Parulidae above E=3), dry woodland (first in engine), buzzy timbre (new for warblers), slow tempo (unprecedented in engine Parulidae). Parulidae now leads all families with six members.

3. **Oak-pine -- the engine's first dry woodland habitat.** Garry oak savanna, ponderosa pine, sun-dappled open canopy. After 37 degrees of wet, urban, montane, and aerial ecosystems, the engine finally touches the rain shadow side of the PNW. Garry oak is the PNW's only native oak and its ecosystem is among the most endangered in the region -- less than 10% of historical range remains.

4. **The *Setophaga* pair -- Yellow Warbler (degree 16) and Black-throated Gray (degree 38).** Same genus, maximum contrast. Vivid yellow vs. black-and-white ink drawing with one yellow dot. Ascending crescendo vs. lazy drawl. Wet riparian vs. dry woodland. The genus *Setophaga* now spans both extremes of warbler aesthetics: maximum color and minimum color.

## Five Structural Advances

### 1. The Drawl: A New Warbler Tempo

The engine's Parulidae acoustic vocabulary now spans six strategies:

| Degree | Species | Strategy | Tempo |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14 | MacGillivray's | Narrative (AAB resolution) | Moderate |
| 15 | Wilson's | Accelerando (builds and stops) | Fast |
| 16 | Yellow | Crescendo (ascending) | Moderate |
| 17 | Yellowthroat | Hook (metronomic repetition) | Fast |
| 20 | Orange-crowned | Fade (descending trill) | Fast |
| **38** | **Black-throated Gray** | **Drawl (steady-state buzz)** | **Slow** |

The quintet's five members were all moderate-to-fast tempo. The sixth warbler is slow -- the first slow Parulidae. This extends the family's acoustic range into a tempo zone it had not occupied.

### 2. Oak-Pine: The Engine's First Dry Habitat

The ecosystem inventory gains a new axis:

| Biome Type | First Appearance | Species |
|---|---|---|
| Old-growth conifer | Degree 0 | Great Blue Heron |
| Urban | Degree 3 | Black-capped Chickadee |

Thirty-eight degrees to reach the dry side of the PNW. The engine has been wet, urban, and montane. It is now also dry. Garry oak and ponderosa pine -- the rain shadow landscape.

### 3. Parulidae Leads: Six Members

With six members, Parulidae surpasses Hirundinidae and Turdidae (five each) as the engine's most represented family. This reflects reality -- Parulidae is the largest songbird family in North America. It should lead. And with ~115 species in the family, six is barely 5% of the pool. There are many more warblers to come.

### 4. The *Setophaga* Pair: Maximum Contrast

The genus *Setophaga* now contains the engine's brightest warbler (Yellow, degree 16) and one of its most graphically stark (Black-throated Gray, degree 38). Same genus. One is entirely vivid yellow. The other is entirely black-and-white with one dot of yellow. The genus spans the full chromatic range of warbler aesthetics, from maximum saturation to minimum.

### 5. The Yellow Loral Spot: Minimal Color Theory

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's single yellow dot between eye and bill connects to the engine's ongoing exploration of minimal features: the Great Blue Heron's silence (degree 0), the Spotted Owl's darkness (degree 4), the Bushtit's noise floor (degree 10), the Orange-crowned Warbler's hidden crown (degree 20). Features that function by being barely there. The yellow loral spot is visible but tiny -- the minimum amount of color that serves as a diagnostic field mark. One dot does the work.

## What Worked

### 1. The Parulidae return is well-timed
Eighteen degrees is long enough that the return feels significant -- not repetitive. The engine explored eight other families in the interim. Coming back to warblers after the hummingbird trio creates a natural contrast: from hummingbird extremes to warbler normalcy. The pacing is right.

### 2. "Drawl" captures the song quality
After 37 acoustic elements, finding names that are both accurate and evocative is increasingly difficult. "Drawl" works because it captures the defining quality -- unhurriedness -- in a single word with strong human associations. A drawl is immediately understood: slow, relaxed, confident. The name maps the bird's song to a human vocal quality without forcing the analogy.

### 3. Oak-pine fills a genuine gap
The engine had no dry woodland habitat. Thirty-eight degrees in, this is a real gap -- the PNW is not just rain and conifers. The oak-pine ecosystem introduces fire ecology, Garry oak conservation, rain shadow habitats, and the dry side of the Cascades. The engine becomes more geographically honest.

### 4. The *Setophaga* contrast is productive
Same genus, opposite aesthetics. The Yellow Warbler and Black-throated Gray Warbler make each other more interesting by proximity. What does *Setophaga* mean when it contains both the highlighter pen and the ink drawing? The genus question generates genuine taxonomic insight.

### 5. The quintet closure holds
The quintet is not reopened or extended. The sixth warbler is clearly something different -- different energy, different ecosystem, different timbre. The structural integrity of the quintet as a closed set of five song endings is preserved.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. EXISTENTIAL CRISIS: No marine species in 38 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound"
Thirty-eight degrees. The water has not spoken. This is the engine's defining structural deficit. The project is named for PUGET SOUND. Degree 39 MUST be a marine or shoreline species. Common Loon, Glaucous-winged Gull, Pigeon Guillemot, Western Grebe, Rhinoceros Auklet, Marbled Murrelet, Harlequin Duck, Surf Scoter. ANY marine species. This cannot wait longer.

### 2. E=3 absent for 18 degrees -- WORSENING
The Black-throated Gray Warbler is E=4 -- the first Parulidae above E=3, but also the 18th consecutive degree without E=3. The last E=3 was degree 20 (Orange-crowned Warbler). E=2 has been absent since degree 13 (25 degrees). E=1 since degree 12 (26 degrees). The dynamic range compression is severe. Degrees 21-38 have been exclusively E=4 and E=5. The lower half of the energy spectrum is completely absent from the recent arc. A marine species at E=2 or E=3 would address both the marine crisis and the energy compression.

### 3. No corvid in 38 degrees
Steller's Jay, American Crow, Common Raven. Thirty-eight degrees. The PNW's most intelligent and acoustically complex birds remain absent. URGENT.

### 4. No raptor in 38 degrees
No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Osprey, Peregrine Falcon. A marine raptor (Bald Eagle, Osprey) could address both gaps.

### 5. No nocturnal species since degree 4
Thirty-four degrees since the Spotted Owl. The night has been silent too long.

### 6. Summer season continues
Degree 38 is another summer breeder. The engine has heavy summer representation. Resident and winter species are underrepresented in the recent arc.

The red flame-bearer. And then a different sound from a different hummingbird. Not a hum but a crack. Not continuous but instantaneous. A small iridescent green bird with a head dipped in molten magenta perches on a bare branch in a Seattle backyard in January. It climbs. Forty meters. It folds its wings. It falls. Twenty-seven meters per second. Three hundred and eighty-five body lengths per second. The tail spreads. The feather vibrates. A chirp. Explosive. The sonic boom. Anna's Hummingbird. The one that stayed. And now -- higher than either of them went, above the tree line, above the urban gardens, above the flower meadows, in a subalpine meadow at 3,000 meters where the paintbrush burns red against gray talus -- a sound so small it might not be there at all. A soft, rising tsee-tsee. The smallest bird in North America. The filament. The thinnest thread of sound. The pilot light that still burns. And now, eighteen degrees after the last warbler evaporated into silence, below the mountain meadow, below the hummingbird trio's extremes, in a dry woodland where Garry oaks stand in open sun and ponderosa pines drop their needles on bunchgrass -- a bird drawn in black ink on white paper, with one small dot of yellow between its eye and its bill, sits on an oak branch in the dappled light and sings. Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy. Slow. Buzzy. Unhurried. The Black-throated Gray Warbler. The darkening moth-eater. The sixth Parulidae, returning after eighteen degrees of absence, entering an engine that has learned from flickers and swallows and waxwings and hummingbirds, carrying a song that the quintet never had: the drawl. Not narrative, not urgency, not brightness, not catchiness, not release. Just a slow, buzzy, confident statement from a bird in an oak tree on the dry side of the mountains, in a habitat that has been burning and growing and burning again since the last ice age, in an ecosystem that is now among the most endangered in the Pacific Northwest, on a branch that catches the sun that the wet-side warblers never see. The drawl. The sound of the dry side. The warbler that took its time.*
