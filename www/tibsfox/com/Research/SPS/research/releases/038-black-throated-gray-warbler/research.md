## Taxonomy: The Darkening Moth-Eater

### Classification

- **Order:** Passeriformes
- **Family:** Parulidae (New World Warblers)
- **Genus:** *Setophaga* (Swainson, 1827)
- **Species:** *Setophaga nigrescens* (Townsend, 1837)
- **Common Name:** Black-throated Gray Warbler

### Etymology

*Setophaga* -- from Ancient Greek *ses* (moth, from *setos* "that which eats") + *phagos* (eater) = "moth-eater" [1, 2, 3]. The genus name references the insectivorous feeding habit. Originally applied to the American Redstart (*S. ruticilla*), the genus was massively expanded in 2011 when the AOS merged the former genus *Dendroica* into *Setophaga* based on molecular phylogenetics [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- 2011 AOS revision]. The Black-throated Gray Warbler was formerly *Dendroica nigrescens* in most field guides published before 2012.

*Nigrescens* -- from Latin *nigrescens*, the present participle of *nigrescere* = "to become black" or "to darken" [1, 2]. Not "black" but "blackening" -- becoming dark. The name references the male's bold black-and-white plumage pattern: black throat, black crown, black cheek patch, black streaks on white underparts, gray back. The bird is in the process of darkening -- the Latin catches it mid-transformation, as if the black is still spreading.

Full translation: **"the darkening moth-eater."** An insectivore whose plumage is becoming black. The name has a quality of process rather than state -- *nigrescens* describes a becoming, not a being. The bird is not *niger* (black). It is *nigrescens* (blackening). The species epithet contains motion.

Compare with previous warbler names:
- **MacGillivray's (14):** *Geothlypis tolmiei* = "Tolmie's ground-finch" -- named for a person
- **Wilson's (15):** *Cardellina pusilla* = "little thistle-finch" -- named for a person
- **Yellow (16):** *Setophaga petechia* = "moth-eater with spots" -- physical description
- **Yellowthroat (17):** *Geothlypis trichas* = "three-part ground-finch" -- morphology
- **Orange-crowned (20):** *Leiothlypis celata* = "smooth-feathered hidden one" -- concealment
- **Black-throated Gray (38):** *Setophaga nigrescens* = "darkening moth-eater" -- process

Three are named for people (14, 15, 16 partially). Three are descriptive (16, 17, 20). The Black-throated Gray is the first warbler whose species epithet describes an ongoing process rather than a static feature. *Nigrescens* is a verb form, not an adjective. The bird is becoming.

## The Sound: Lazy Buzzy Wheeze Song

### The Quality of Laziness

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's song is one of the most distinctive in the Parulidae, and its character is captured in two words: **lazy** and **buzzy** [1, 2, 3, 4, 5].

The song is typically transcribed as *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy* or *zeedle zeedle zeedle zee* -- a series of 4-5 buzzy phrases, the first several at one pitch, the last phrase typically rising or falling slightly [1, 2, 4, 5, 6]. The overall impression is of a song that is in no hurry. Where the Wilson's Warbler (degree 15) accelerated and the Yellow Warbler (degree 16) crescendoed, the Black-throated Gray Warbler repeats its buzzy phrase at a steady, unhurried tempo, barely varying in pitch or amplitude. The song is a drawl.

### Vocalization Details

- **Primary song:** 4-5 buzzy notes, typically rendered as *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy* or *zee zee zee zh-zhee*. Duration approximately 1.5-2.5 seconds [1, 2, 5, 6].
- **Frequency range:** Approximately 3,500-7,000 Hz, with most energy concentrated in the 4,000-6,000 Hz band [NEEDS VERIFICATION].
- **Tempo:** Moderate to slow for a warbler -- approximately 2-3 phrases per second [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Noticeably slower than Wilson's Warbler or Orange-crowned Warbler trills.
- **Timbre:** Buzzy, husky, wheezy. The phrases have a rough, insect-like quality -- more buzz than whistle [1, 2, 5]. This is dramatically different from the clean, pure-toned notes of the Yellow Warbler's sweet-sweet-sweeter or the MacGillivray's clear churry.
- **Phrase structure:** The first 3-4 phrases are typically on the same pitch (isometric). The final phrase often rises or drops slightly, creating a gentle terminal accent [1, 2, 5, 6]. This is a much subtler ending gesture than MacGillivray's dramatic terminal sweet.
- **Calling note:** A distinctive *tup* or *tchep* -- flatter and duller than many warbler chip notes [1, 2, 5].

### Comparison with the Warbler Quintet

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's song quality is fundamentally different from all five quintet members:

| Degree | Species | Song Quality | Tempo | Ending |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 14 | MacGillivray's | Clear, musical | Moderate | Resolution (sweet) |
| 15 | Wilson's | Chattering, accelerating | Fast | Interruption (stops) |
| 16 | Yellow | Clear, ascending | Moderate | Climax (peak) |
| 17 | Yellowthroat | Loud, repetitive | Fast | Repetition (loops) |
| 20 | Orange-crowned | Thin, trilling | Fast | Evaporation (fades) |
| **38** | **Black-throated Gray** | **Buzzy, wheezy** | **Slow** | **Drawl (lazy terminal accent)** |

The quintet's five members were all moving somewhere -- arriving, interrupting, climbing, persisting, evaporating. The Black-throated Gray Warbler is not going anywhere in particular. Its song is closer to a statement of position than a trajectory of energy. It drawls. It buzzes. It is in no rush. This is the laziest warbler in the engine.

The word "lazy" is not pejorative. In acoustic ecology, a slow, buzzy delivery is an adaptation to the bird's habitat. In open woodland with moderate ambient noise, a buzzy quality carries well through vegetation because buzz contains energy across a broader frequency band than a pure tone [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. The laziness is efficient. The buzzy timbre cuts through dappled oak canopy where a clear whistle might scatter.

### The 39th Acoustic Element: The Drawl

The Calliope Hummingbird gave the engine the filament (degree 37) -- a thread of sound so thin it barely exists. Before that, the sonic boom (degree 36) -- an explosion. Before that, the thrum (degree 35) -- a continuous mechanical hum.

Now, after three consecutive hummingbird sounds that were mechanical, explosive, and threadlike, the engine gets a **drawl** -- a slow, buzzy, unhurried warbler song from an oak-pine woodland. The contrast with the hummingbird trio is dramatic:

| Degree | Element | Character | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 35 | Thrum | Continuous mechanical hum | 52-62 Hz wingbeat |
| 36 | Sonic Boom | Explosive dive chirp | 27.3 m/s |
| 37 | Filament | Threadlike vocal whisper | Brief |
| **38** | **The Drawl** | **Slow buzzy wheeze** | **Unhurried** |

The hummingbird trio was all about extremes -- speed, explosion, minimum. The drawl is about deceleration. After three degrees of mechanical and threadlike hummingbird sounds, a warbler sits in an oak tree and sings *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy* as if it has all the time in the world. The tempo shifts from hummingbird time (milliseconds, kilohertz, body lengths per second) to warbler time (phrases repeated at walking pace, a song that takes two seconds and does not rush any of them).

The drawl is named for what it is: an unhurried delivery. A drawl in human speech is a slow, relaxed pronunciation where vowels are elongated and consonants are soft. The Black-throated Gray Warbler's song drawls: each buzzy phrase is delivered at a pace that suggests no predator is near, no rival is threatening, no urgency exists. The song is at rest in motion.

## The Oak-Pine Ecosystem: A New Habitat Type

### First Dry Woodland in the Engine

The oak-pine ecosystem at degree 38 is the engine's **first dry woodland habitat** -- a fundamentally new biome that has not appeared in any previous degree [1, 2, 3, 7, 8].

Every previous ecosystem in the engine has been either wet, urban, montane, or generalized:

| Category | Ecosystems (degrees) |
|---|---|
| **Wet/riparian** | old-growth (0, 4, 5), mixed conifer (1, 2, 7, 8, 9), riparian (15, 16), freshwater marsh (17), shrubby slope (20) |
| **Urban/suburban** | urban (3, 10, 11, 25), urban/coastal (36) |
| **Open/generalized** | understory (6, 13, 14), grassland edge (23, 24), open woodland (12), cone crop (34) |
| **Montane** | mountain conifer (33), mountain meadow (37), flower meadow (35) |
| **Aerial** | aerial/migration (26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 31, 32) |
| **Dry woodland** | **none -- until degree 38** |

Oak-pine woodland is a dry, sun-drenched habitat characterized by Garry oak (*Quercus garryana*), ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*), and Oregon white oak standing in open, savanna-like arrangements [7, 8, 9]. The understory is sparse -- bunchgrass, wildflowers, scattered shrubs. The canopy is open enough to cast dappled light, not dense enough to create deep shade. This is the rain shadow landscape: the dry side of the Cascades, the Willamette Valley's remaining oak prairies, the San Juan Islands' dryland forests.

The PNW is defined in the popular imagination by rain and dense conifers. But the region also contains significant dry woodland habitat, and the Black-throated Gray Warbler is one of its signature species [1, 2, 3, 7].

### Garry Oak -- The Signature Tree

Garry oak (*Quercus garryana*) is the Pacific Northwest's **only native oak species** [7, 8, 9]. It ranges from southern British Columbia through Washington and Oregon to northern California, typically in dry lowland areas where precipitation is moderate and fire historically maintained open woodland structure.

Garry oak ecosystems are among the most endangered habitats in the PNW [7, 8, 9]:

- **Historical range:** Estimated 1-1.5 million acres of Garry oak woodland/savanna in pre-European-contact Washington and Oregon [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Current range:** Less than 10% of historical extent remains intact [7, 8]
- **Threats:** Fire suppression (allows Douglas fir to overtop and shade out oaks), urban development, agricultural conversion, invasive species (Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry)
- **Conservation status:** Garry oak ecosystems are listed as critically imperiled (S1) in British Columbia and imperiled (S2) in Washington [NEEDS VERIFICATION]

The Black-throated Gray Warbler is not exclusively a Garry oak bird -- it also breeds in mixed oak-pine, juniper-oak, and chaparral habitats across western North America [1, 2, 3]. But in the PNW, Garry oak woodlands are among its primary breeding habitats, and the species serves as an indicator of oak ecosystem health [7].

### Ponderosa Pine Component

The "oak-pine" in the ecosystem name refers to the common association between Garry oak and ponderosa pine in transitional habitats [7, 8]:

- **East side of Cascades:** Open ponderosa pine forests with scattered oaks at lower elevations
- **Transition zones:** Where wet Douglas fir forests give way to dry oak-pine woodlands
- **Willamette Valley margins:** Foothills where oaks and pines meet

The Black-throated Gray Warbler uses both oak and pine components of these woodlands, foraging in the canopy of either tree species [1, 2, 3]. It is not an oak specialist or a pine specialist -- it is a dry woodland specialist, and oaks and pines are the defining trees of the dry woodland belt.

### PNW Distribution

In the PNW, the Black-throated Gray Warbler breeds in [1, 2, 3, 7]:

- **Western Washington:** Garry oak woodlands of the south Puget Sound lowlands (Joint Base Lewis-McChord area, Mima Mounds, Scatter Creek), San Juan Islands, and southern Whidbey Island
- **Eastern Washington:** Ponderosa pine/oak transition zones in the eastern Cascades foothills, Klickitat County, and the Okanogan
- **Willamette Valley:** Remaining Garry oak prairies and woodland patches
- **Southern Oregon:** Rogue Valley, Klamath Mountains, oak-pine woodland
- **Throughout range:** Dry conifer and mixed woodland, typically below 2,000 m [1, 2, 3]

The species is **locally common** in appropriate habitat but uncommon to rare in the wet west-side conifer forests that dominate the PNW [1, 2, 3]. It is a bird of the dry side -- the rain shadow, the oak savanna, the sunny woodland that most PNW visitors never see.

## Plumage: The Ink Drawing

### Black, White, and Gray -- With One Yellow Spot

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's plumage is a study in graphic contrast [1, 2, 3]:

- **Head:** Bold black-and-white striping. Black crown, white supercilium (eyebrow), black cheek patch, white malar stripe. The face is a field mark -- bold geometric patterns in pure black and white.
- **Throat:** Black (male). Gray (female and immature).
- **Underparts:** White with black streaks on the flanks.
- **Upperparts:** Gray back with black streaks. Two white wing bars. White outer tail feathers visible in flight.
- **The yellow spot:** A small but diagnostic patch of yellow between the eye and the bill -- the loral spot [1, 2, 3]. This single point of warm color on an otherwise achromatic bird is the field mark that separates the Black-throated Gray from the similar Black-and-white Warbler or Blackpoll Warbler at a distance.

The overall impression is of a bird drawn in ink on white paper. No color except that one dot of yellow. The black is crisp. The white is clean. The gray back is the pencil shading. It is a bird designed by a printmaker, not a painter.

Compare with the warbler quintet's plumage:
- **MacGillivray's (14):** Olive-gray with slate hood -- muted, cryptic
- **Wilson's (15):** Bright olive-yellow with black cap -- vivid
- **Yellow (16):** Vivid yellow with chestnut streaks -- the brightest warbler
- **Yellowthroat (17):** Olive with black mask -- theatrical
- **Orange-crowned (20):** Olive-green, featureless -- the plainest warbler

The quintet ranged from plain (Orange-crowned) to vivid (Yellow). The Black-throated Gray introduces a new aesthetic: **graphic**. Not colorful, not plain -- patterned. High contrast but low chroma. The design is about structure, not pigment. This is the warbler that uses black ink where the Yellow Warbler uses highlighter.

And then that yellow loral spot. One point of color on a monochrome bird. A signature. A dot of warmth that proves the bird is a warbler, not a chickadee. The hidden crown of the Orange-crowned Warbler was concealed. The yellow spot of the Black-throated Gray is visible but tiny -- not hidden but minimal. A yellow period at the end of a black-and-white sentence.

## The 39th Acoustic Element: The Drawl

After the filament -- the thinnest thread of sound from the smallest bird -- the drawl arrives from a completely different direction. This is not a thread. It is a lazy, buzzy phrase delivered with the unhurried confidence of a bird that is exactly where it wants to be.

### Naming the Element

The drawl captures three qualities of the Black-throated Gray Warbler's song:

1. **Slow tempo.** The song is delivered at a walking pace -- each buzzy phrase gets its full measure of time. No acceleration (Wilson's), no crescendo (Yellow), no evaporation (Orange-crowned). Just steady, unhurried repetition.

2. **Buzzy timbre.** The notes are not pure-toned. They are buzzy, wheezy, rough-edged -- like a voice that has been sitting in the sun too long. The buzz distinguishes this from every previous warbler in the engine (all of which had relatively clean or thin tonal quality).

3. **Relaxed energy.** E=4 -- the same energy level as the filament (degree 37), but the quality is utterly different. The filament was quiet because the Calliope Hummingbird is tiny. The drawl is moderate because the Black-throated Gray Warbler is unhurried. One is quiet by constraint. The other is moderate by choice.

### The Drawl vs. Previous Elements

| Element | Degree | Character | Speed | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Filament | 37 | Threadlike whisper | Brief | Vocal (syrinx, 3.1 g bird) |
| Sonic Boom | 36 | Explosive chirp | 27.3 m/s | Mechanical (tail R5 feather) |
| Thrum | 35 | Continuous hum | 52-62 Hz | Mechanical (wingbeats) |
| **Drawl** | **38** | **Lazy buzzy wheeze** | **Slow for a warbler** | **Vocal (syrinx, ~8 g bird)** |

The drawl also connects to the buzz (degree 32, Bohemian Waxwing) -- both are buzzy sounds. But the Bohemian Waxwing's buzz was assertive, collective, E=5 -- hundreds of birds filling a block with *bzeee-bzeee-bzeee*. The Black-throated Gray's drawl is a single bird in an oak tree, buzzing lazily at E=4. The social buzz and the solitary drawl. Two flavors of buzz separated by six degrees.

And the drawl connects backward to the release (degree 20, Orange-crowned Warbler). Both are Parulidae. The release was a song that faded into silence -- the evaporation ending. The drawl is a song that sits in one place and does not fade. The release dissolved. The drawl persists. The last warbler disappeared. This one stays.

## Ecology: The Dry Woodland Specialist

### Habitat Preferences

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's breeding habitat is strongly associated with dry, open woodland and forest [1, 2, 3, 7, 8]:

- **Garry oak woodland/savanna:** Open-canopy oak habitat in western Washington and Oregon
- **Ponderosa pine forest:** Open pine stands in the eastern Cascades and Blue Mountains
- **Oak-pine mixed woodland:** Transitional habitats where oaks and pines coexist
- **Juniper woodland:** In drier portions of its range (eastern Oregon, northern California)
- **Chaparral and manzanita:** In California and southern Oregon
- **Mixed conifer with oak understory:** Occasionally in more complex forest, but always with an open-canopy component

The common thread is **openness and warmth**. The Black-throated Gray Warbler avoids dense, wet forests [1, 2, 3]. It wants sun-dappled canopy, dry air, and an open understory where it can forage at mid-canopy level. This is the opposite of the MacGillivray's Warbler (degree 14), which skulks in dense salmonberry thickets, and the Wilson's Warbler (degree 15), which haunts riparian willow tangles.

### Foraging Behavior

The Black-throated Gray Warbler is primarily an **insectivore** that forages by gleaning from leaves and branches at mid-canopy height [1, 2, 3]:

- **Method:** Systematic gleaning -- hopping along branches and peering at leaf surfaces for insects and spiders. Occasionally hover-gleaning from leaf tips.
- **Height:** Mid-canopy, typically 5-15 m above ground [1, 2, 3]. Higher than the understory warblers (MacGillivray's, Yellowthroat) but lower than canopy-top species.
- **Prey:** Small insects, caterpillars, spiders, leaf-rolling moths, aphids [1, 2, 3].
- **Feeding rate:** Moderate -- moves deliberately through canopy, checking each branch and leaf cluster. Not the frantic foraging of a Wilson's Warbler or the hovering intensity of a hummingbird.

The foraging style matches the song: unhurried, methodical, deliberate. The Black-throated Gray Warbler works its oak canopy the way it sings its song -- systematically, without rushing, covering ground at a walking pace.

### Nesting

Nests are typically placed in oaks, pines, or other woodland trees at 2-10 m height [1, 2, 3]:

- **Nest type:** Open cup of plant fibers, grass, and bark strips, lined with feathers and animal hair
- **Placement:** On a horizontal branch or in a fork, often in a cluster of leaves or needles
- **Eggs:** Typically 3-5, white with brown speckles
- **Incubation:** ~12 days [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Breeding season in PNW:** Late May through July

### Migration

The Black-throated Gray Warbler is a **short- to medium-distance migrant** [1, 2, 3, 10]:

- **Breeding range:** Western North America from southern British Columbia through California, east to Colorado and New Mexico
- **Wintering range:** Western Mexico, primarily Sonora, Sinaloa, and Baja California. Some overwinter in southern California and southern Arizona [1, 2, 10]
- **Migration timing in PNW:** Arrives late April to mid-May. Departs August to September.
- **Migration distance:** Shorter than many Parulidae -- estimated 1,000-2,500 miles one way [NEEDS VERIFICATION]

Compare with the warbler quintet's migration patterns:
- **MacGillivray's (14):** Neotropical migrant to Central America
- **Wilson's (15):** Neotropical migrant to Mexico/Central America
- **Yellow (16):** Neotropical migrant to Central/South America
- **Yellowthroat (17):** Short-distance to southern US/Mexico
- **Orange-crowned (20):** Partial migrant -- some overwinter on Pacific coast
- **Black-throated Gray (38):** Short- to medium-distance to western Mexico/southern CA

The Black-throated Gray's migration is among the shortest of the engine's warblers. This matches the dry woodland ecology -- the bird's wintering habitat in western Mexico (dry thorn-scrub, oak woodland) is structurally similar to its breeding habitat. It does not make the long neotropical crossing that Wilson's and Yellow warblers undertake. It shifts latitude within the same biome: dry woodland in summer, dry woodland in winter.

- Waxwing pair (31-32, Bombycillidae)
- Mountain finches (33-34, Fringillidae)
- The hummingbird trio (35-37, Trochilidae)

Eight families. Eighteen degrees. The engine diversified enormously during the Parulidae absence. When the warblers left at degree 20, the engine had explored 5 orders (Pelecaniformes, Passeriformes, Strigiformes, Piciformes, and the upcoming Apodiformes). When they return at degree 38, the engine has 5 orders still but has expanded into families it had never touched: Hirundinidae, Bombycillidae, Trochilidae, and deepened Fringillidae, Turdidae, and Picidae.

The Parulidae absence was productive. The engine needed the diversity. But the return is significant because it signals that the engine is not done with warblers -- the quintet was a chapter, not the whole book.

### Quintet vs. Sixth Member

The warbler quintet was structurally closed: five song endings, five genera, five species. The Black-throated Gray Warbler does not reopen the quintet's structure. It opens a new conversation.

| Dimension | Quintet (14-17, 20) | Black-throated Gray (38) |
|---|---|---|
| Energy | All E=3 | **E=4** |
| Ecosystem | Wet/mixed (thicket, riparian, marsh, slope) | **Dry woodland (oak-pine)** |
| Song quality | Clear, trilling, chattering, ascending, fading | **Buzzy, wheezy, slow** |
| Song tempo | Fast to moderate | **Slow** |
| Timbre | Clean to thin | **Rough, buzzy** |
| Habitat moisture | Wet to mesic | **Dry (xeric)** |

The quintet was a wet-side, E=3 phenomenon. The Black-throated Gray is a dry-side, E=4 bird. The quintet sang with clean or thin tones. The Black-throated Gray buzzes with rough timbre. The break is clean: the sixth warbler is not quintet-plus-one. It is something else.

### Family Count Context

With six members, Parulidae is now the engine's most represented family. But consider: Parulidae has approximately 115 species in North America [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- exact count varies by taxonomy]. Six of 115 is 5.2%. The engine has barely begun to explore the warbler family. The Hirundinidae quintet (5 members) represents a much higher fraction of its total species pool (of about 8-10 PNW swallow species, 5 are in the engine). The Parulidae will continue to grow.

## The Yellow Loral Spot: Color Theory in One Dot

The Black-throated Gray Warbler's yellow loral spot -- a small patch of warm yellow pigment between the eye and the bill -- is one of the most elegant field marks in North American ornithology [1, 2, 3].

On a bird that is otherwise entirely black, white, and gray, this single point of color is:

1. **Diagnostic:** It separates the Black-throated Gray from all similar black-and-white warblers
2. **Minimal:** It is the absolute minimum amount of color needed to function as a field mark
3. **Warm:** Yellow against cool black-and-white creates maximum chromatic contrast with minimum pigment

The Yellow Warbler (degree 16) was entirely yellow -- saturated, vivid, the living highlighter pen. The Black-throated Gray Warbler is entirely achromatic except for one dot. The *Setophaga* genus pair now maps the extremes of warbler color: maximum yellow (S. petechia) and minimal yellow (S. nigrescens, one small spot). Both are *Setophaga*. One is the canvas filled with color. The other is the canvas left bare with one brushstroke of warmth.

This connects to the hidden crown of the Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20, *Leiothlypis celata*). The orange crown is concealed -- named but never seen. The yellow loral spot is visible but tiny -- visible but almost not there. Two warblers, two strategies of minimal color: one hides it, one shrinks it. Both challenge the assumption that a field mark should be conspicuous.

## Conservation: Least Concern -- But Habitat Declining

The Black-throated Gray Warbler is listed as **Least Concern** by IUCN [1, 2, 11], but its primary PNW habitat -- Garry oak woodland -- is critically endangered:

- **IUCN status:** Least Concern [11]
- **Population trend:** Stable to slightly declining [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Population size:** Estimated several million [NEEDS VERIFICATION]
- **Breeding Bird Survey trend:** Slight decline in some western regions [NEEDS VERIFICATION]

The paradox: the bird is secure but its best habitat is disappearing. The species persists because it uses a range of dry woodland types, not just Garry oak. But in the PNW specifically, the loss of Garry oak woodland is reducing available breeding habitat [7, 8, 9]:

- **Fire suppression:** Without periodic low-intensity fire, Douglas fir encroaches on oak savanna, eventually overtopping and shading out the oaks. The Black-throated Gray Warbler needs open canopy; dense Douglas fir does not provide it.
- **Urban development:** South Puget Sound Garry oak prairies are being converted to residential and commercial use.
- **Invasive species:** Scotch broom and Himalayan blackberry alter understory structure.
- **Agricultural conversion:** Willamette Valley oak woodlands have been extensively converted to agriculture.

The conservation story here is about habitat, not species -- the bird will survive in other dry woodland types, but the Garry oak ecosystem it inhabits in the PNW is among the most endangered ecosystems in the region [7, 8]. The Black-throated Gray Warbler is an indicator of oak woodland health. Where the warbler sings, the oaks are still standing.
