Black-throated Gray Warbler

Setophaga nigrescens
lazy buzzy wheeze song — the drawl, Parulidae returns after 18 degrees, the engine’s first dry woodland habitat

The 39th Acoustic Element: The Drawl

A slow, buzzy, unhurried warbler song from an oak-pine canopy: weazy weazy weazy wee-zy. After three hummingbird sounds — thrum (35, continuous hum), sonic boom (36, explosive chirp), filament (37, threadlike whisper) — the engine decelerates. The drawl is the laziest sound in the Parulidae. Each phrase gets its full measure of time. No acceleration. No crescendo. No evaporation. Just a bird in an oak tree, buzzing at the pace of a summer afternoon.

Hummingbird trio → warbler drawl: thrum (35, E=5) → sonic boom (36, E=5) → filament (37, E=4) → drawl (38, E=4). Mechanical extremes → lazy vocal buzz.

Parulidae Returns — 18-Degree Gap

Sixth Parulidae. The warbler quintet (14–17, 20) closed at degree 20. Eighteen degrees of silence — the longest family gap followed by a return. The quintet mapped five song endings. The sixth warbler opens a new chapter:

  • Quintet (14–20): All E=3. Wet/mixed habitats. Clean/thin tones. Fast tempo.
  • Sixth (38): E=4. Dry woodland. Buzzy timbre. Slow tempo.

Parulidae now leads with 6 members, surpassing Hirundinidae and Turdidae (5 each).

The Darkening Moth-Eater

Setophaga nigrescens — “the darkening moth-eater.” Setophaga = Greek “moth-eater.” Nigrescens = Latin “becoming black” or “darkening” — not niger (black) but nigrescens (blackening). The name describes a process, not a state. The species epithet contains motion. Formerly Dendroica nigrescens before the 2011 AOS genus merger.

The Sound: Lazy Buzzy Wheeze

Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy — 4–5 buzzy phrases at approximately 3,500–7,000 Hz [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Slow tempo for a warbler (~2–3 phrases/second). The first 3–4 phrases are isometric (same pitch), the final phrase rises or falls slightly — a gentle terminal accent. Buzzy, husky, wheezy timbre — more insect than flute. Dramatically different from the clean tones of Yellow Warbler or the thin trill of Orange-crowned.

Oak-Pine — First Dry Woodland

The engine’s first explicitly dry habitat. Garry oak (Quercus garryana) — the PNW’s only native oak — and ponderosa pine in open, sun-dappled, savanna-like arrangements. Bunchgrass understory. Rain shadow landscape. The dry side of the PNW that most visitors never see.

Critically endangered ecosystem: less than 10% of historical Garry oak range remains [NEEDS VERIFICATION]. Fire suppression allows Douglas fir to overtop and shade out oaks. Where the warbler sings, the oaks are still standing.

The Ink Drawing — With One Yellow Dot

Black, white, and gray. Bold black-and-white face pattern. Black throat (male). Gray back with black streaks. White wing bars. No color except one small yellow loral spot between eye and bill — the minimum field mark. One dot of warmth on an achromatic bird. The Yellow Warbler (16) was entirely yellow. The Black-throated Gray is entirely monochrome except for that one brushstroke.

E=4 — First Parulidae Above E=3

All five quintet warblers were E=3. The sixth breaks the ceiling. E=4 now at 15 degrees (38.5%). E=3 absent for 18 degrees (since degree 20). E=2 absent for 25 degrees. E=1 absent for 26 degrees. Dynamic range compression is worsening — the lower half of the energy spectrum has been completely absent for 18+ degrees.

Theory Nodes

D

The Drawl foundational

The 39th acoustic element. Slow, buzzy, unhurried. After three hummingbird elements (thrum, sonic boom, filament), the engine decelerates into warbler time. The laziest sound in the Parulidae. The antithesis of urgency (degree 15).

P

Parulidae Returns foundational

18-degree absence — longest family gap followed by return. Quintet (E=3, wet, clean) gives way to sixth member (E=4, dry, buzzy). The family’s tone has changed. Parulidae now leads all families with 6 members.

O

Oak-Pine Ecosystem foundational

First dry woodland in the engine. Garry oak savanna, ponderosa pine. The rain shadow side of the PNW. Critically endangered habitat — less than 10% of historical range remains.

S

Setophaga Pair intermediate

Yellow Warbler (16) and Black-throated Gray (38) — same genus, maximum contrast. Vivid yellow vs. monochrome ink drawing. Ascending crescendo vs. lazy drawl. Wet riparian vs. dry woodland.

Y

The Yellow Loral Spot intermediate

One dot of yellow on a monochrome bird. The minimum field mark. Connects to Orange-crowned’s hidden crown (degree 20): two warblers, two strategies of minimal color — one hides it, one shrinks it.

4

E=4 — First Parulidae Above E=3 intermediate

Quintet was all E=3. Sixth member breaks the ceiling. E=4 now at 15 degrees. E=3 absent 18 degrees. Dynamic range compression deepens.

SPS-020: Orange-crowned Warbler

PARULIDAE RETURNS. 18-degree gap. Orange-crowned = evaporation (E=3, shrubby slope). Black-throated Gray = drawl (E=4, oak-pine). The quintet’s closer and the sextet’s opener.

SPS-016: Yellow Warbler

SETOPHAGA PAIR. Same genus, maximum contrast. Vivid yellow vs. monochrome + one dot. Ascending crescendo vs. lazy drawl. Wet riparian vs. dry woodland.

SPS-014: MacGillivray’s Warbler

FIRST vs. SIXTH WARBLER. MacGillivray’s introduced Parulidae (wet understory, narrative, E=3). Black-throated Gray reopens it (dry canopy, drawl, E=4).

SPS-037: Calliope Hummingbird

ORDER CHANGE. Three Apodiformes (35–37) end. Passeriformes returns. Filament (3.1 g, mountain meadow) → drawl (~8 g, oak-pine). Extremes → normalcy.

SPS-032: Bohemian Waxwing

BUZZ CONNECTION. Bohemian = collective buzz (E=5, hundreds). Black-throated Gray = solitary drawl (E=4, one bird). Social buzz vs. lazy drawl.

SPS-033: Cassin’s Finch

DRY WOODLAND CONNECTION. Mountain conifer (high, closed) vs. oak-pine (low, open). Both dry-side species with ponderosa pine association.

AVI: Wings of the PNW

Summer breeder in dry woodland. Locally common in Garry oak. One of ~25 PNW Parulidae species.

ECO: Living Systems

Garry oak woodland ecology. Fire-dependent ecosystem. Douglas fir encroachment. Among PNW’s most endangered habitats.
