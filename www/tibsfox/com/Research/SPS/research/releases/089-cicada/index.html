Third Consecutive Insect — The Insect Trio (87–88–89)

The cicada is the third consecutive insect (degrees 87–88–89): cricket chorus (87), katydid (88), cicada (89). Three insects in three degrees — after 86 degrees of vertebrates (birds, mammals, one amphibian), the engine produces an insect trio. Each uses a different sound production mechanism: cricket = leg stridulation against wings, katydid = wing-on-wing stridulation, cicada = tymbal vibration. Three insects, three mechanisms. The engine explores insect acoustics comprehensively.

Electric Buzzing Throb — 90th Acoustic Element — TYMBAL MECHANISM

The cicada produces sound via tymbals — paired membranes on the sides of the abdomen that are rapidly buckled inward and released by muscles [1, 2]. This is a completely different mechanism from stridulation (cricket/katydid). The tymbal mechanism produces a buzzing, throbbing, electric sound that can reach 100+ dB — louder than a motorcycle, louder than a lawn mower. The loudest insect on Earth. E=4 — the cicada fills the forest edge with a wall of electric sound that overwhelms all other sources.

Cicadidae — 40th Family (MILESTONE!) — NEW ORDER Hemiptera

Cicadidae (cicadas) is a NEW FAMILY — the 40th in the engine. Forty families is a milestone. NEW ORDER: Hemiptera (true bugs, cicadas, aphids, leafhoppers). Hemiptera is fundamentally different from Orthoptera (crickets/katydids at 87-88). Different body plan, different feeding strategy (piercing-sucking mouthparts for plant fluids), different sound mechanism (tymbals, not stridulation). Three consecutive insects across two different orders.

The Sound: Electric Buzzing Throb (90th Acoustic Element)

The call: Male cicadas produce sound using tymbals — ribbed membranes on the sides of the abdomen. Rapid muscle contractions buckle the tymbal inward and release it, producing clicks at rates of 100–500 per second. These clicks fuse into a continuous buzzing, throbbing, electric drone [1, 2, 3]. The sound is amplified by the largely hollow abdomen, which acts as a resonating chamber.

Why E=4: (1) Individual cicadas can produce sounds exceeding 100 dB at close range — the loudest insect on Earth [1, 2]. (2) Multiple cicadas chorus together, creating an overwhelming wall of sound. (3) The sound carries hundreds of meters through forest edge habitat. (4) The buzzing is continuous during peak calling, filling the soundscape completely. (5) E=4 reflects the absolute dominance of the cicada call in its habitat during summer peaks.

Taxonomy: Cicadidae (Family-Level Entry)

Note: Like the cricket chorus (degree 87) and katydid (degree 88), this is a family-level entry. Multiple annual cicada species contribute to PNW forest-edge soundscapes. Dog-day cicadas and related annual species are present in the Pacific Northwest [1, 2].

Classification: Class Insecta (third insect). Order Hemiptera — NEW ORDER. Different from Orthoptera (87-88). Family Cicadidae — NEW FAMILY (40th — milestone). Annual cicadas, not periodical (periodical cicadas are eastern North American) [1, 2].

Ecology: The Forest Edge Throb

Habitat: Forest edge. Cicadas are arboreal — they perch on tree trunks and branches to call. Forest edge habitat connects to meadow (cricket 87, katydid 88) through the transition zone. Annual cicadas emerge every year (unlike periodical cicadas with their 13/17-year cycles) [1, 2, 3].

Life cycle: Nymphs live underground for 2–5 years, feeding on root fluids (piercing-sucking mouthparts). Adults emerge, climb trees, molt into winged form. Males call. Females deposit eggs in tree branches. The cycle repeats.

Predators: Birds (primary), cicada killer wasps (Sphecius speciosus), squirrels, and other predators. The cicada’s strategy is predator satiation — so many emerge simultaneously that predators cannot consume them all.
