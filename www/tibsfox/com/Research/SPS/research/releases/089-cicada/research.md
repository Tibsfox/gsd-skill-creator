The hollow abdomen acts as a resonating chamber, amplifying the sound to extraordinary levels [1, 2, 3].

## THE SOUND: ELECTRIC BUZZING THROB -- 90th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Tymbal

The cicada sound is produced by **tymbals** -- paired ribbed membranes on the sides of the abdomen [1, 2, 3]:

**Mechanism:** Rapid muscle contractions buckle the tymbal inward (click) and release it (click). Each cycle produces two clicks. At rates of 100-500 cycles per second, the individual clicks fuse into a continuous **buzzing, throbbing, electric** drone.

**Amplification:** The male cicada's abdomen is largely hollow, acting as a resonating chamber. This amplification system is remarkably efficient -- the sound output per unit body mass is among the highest in the animal kingdom.

**Volume:** Individual cicadas can exceed **100 dB** at close range. This is louder than a motorcycle, louder than a lawn mower, approaching the threshold of pain. The **loudest insect on Earth** [1, 2].

**Frequency:** Typically 2,000-8,000 Hz depending on species. The sound is described as electric, buzzing, throbbing -- it sounds more like a power line than a biological organism.

### Why E=4

1. **Individual cicadas are extraordinarily loud** -- 100+ dB.
2. **Multiple cicadas chorus together** -- the combined sound is overwhelming.
3. **The sound carries hundreds of meters** through forest edge habitat.
4. **Continuous during peak calling** -- fills the soundscape completely.
5. **E=4 reflects absolute acoustic dominance** during summer peaks.

### Contrast with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **Continuous electric buzz** (degree 88, Katydid) -- both continuous, both insect. But the katydid's buzz is produced by stridulation; the cicada's is produced by tymbals. Different mechanism, higher volume.
- **Rhythmic chirp field chorus** (degree 87, Cricket) -- rhythmic vs continuous. Both E=4. The cricket retains rhythm; the cicada produces a wall of electric sound.
- **Krek-krek night chorus** (degree 86, Pacific Treefrog) -- both chorus species, both E=4. But the treefrog is nocturnal; the cicada is diurnal. Night and day.

## ECOLOGY: THE FOREST EDGE THROB

### Habitat

Forest edge [1, 2, 3]:

- **Primary:** Forest edge, woodland margins, tree-lined areas. Cicadas perch on tree trunks and branches to call.
- **Transition zone:** Forest edge connects to meadow (cricket 87, katydid 88). The insect trio spans meadow to forest edge.
- **Vertical:** Arboreal -- calling from elevated positions on tree trunks, major limbs.

### Life Cycle

- **Nymphs:** Live underground for 2-5 years (annual cicadas), feeding on root fluids via piercing-sucking mouthparts.
- **Emergence:** Nymphs climb tree trunks, molt into winged adult form. The discarded exoskeletons (exuviae) remain clinging to bark.
- **Calling:** Males call from tree perches during hot summer days. Peak calling in the heat of the day.
- **Reproduction:** Females deposit eggs in slits cut in tree branches with their ovipositor. Eggs hatch, first-instar nymphs drop to the ground, burrow into soil. The cycle repeats.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Nymphs feed on root xylem fluid. Adults feed on tree xylem fluid. Piercing-sucking mouthparts (characteristic of Hemiptera).
- **Predators:** Birds (primary), cicada killer wasps (*Sphecius speciosus*), squirrels, and other predators. Strategy: predator satiation through mass emergence -- so many emerge simultaneously that predators cannot consume them all.
