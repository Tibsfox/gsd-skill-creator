## THE RINGTAIL -- THE CREATURE YOU'VE NEVER HEARD OF

### *Bassariscus astutus* -- The Cunning Little Fox

The ringtail is one of the PNW's most remarkable and least known mammals:

- **Appearance.** Cat-sized (0.7-1.5 kg, 30-42 cm body length) with an enormously long, banded black-and-white tail (31-44 cm -- as long as the body). Huge dark eyes with white eye rings. Pointed snout. Large rounded ears. The overall appearance is cat-like but the tail is unmistakable.
- **The tail.** The ringtail's banded tail is its most distinctive feature -- 14-16 alternating black and white rings. The tail serves as a balancing organ for the ringtail's extraordinary agility in rocky terrain, and as a visual signal in the darkness.
- **Climbing ability.** The ringtail is one of the most agile climbers in North American wildlife. Rotating rear ankles allow it to descend vertical surfaces headfirst. It can climb sheer rock faces, navigate narrow ledges, and move through talus with a fluidity that resembles a cat more than a raccoon.
- **Nocturnal specialist.** Strictly nocturnal. The enormous eyes are adapted for low-light vision. During the day, ringtails sleep in rock crevices, hollow trees, or abandoned structures. They emerge at dusk to hunt.
- **Diet.** Omnivorous: insects, small rodents, birds, fruit, berries, lizards. An opportunistic and adaptable forager.
- **Historical human connection.** Ringtails were sometimes kept as mousers in frontier mining camps -- their excellent rodent-hunting ability earned them the nickname "miner's cat." They are not cats, but the association persists.

## THE BARK AND CHITTER

### A Vocal Nocturnal

The ringtail is more vocal than its secretive reputation suggests:

- **The bark.** A sharp, fox-like bark -- "YAP" or "BARK" -- lasting 0.1-0.3 seconds. Frequency: 1,000-4,000 Hz. Surprisingly loud for a 1 kg animal. The bark is used as an alarm call when startled or threatened.
- **The chitter.** Rapid, excitable vocalization: "cht-cht-cht-cht." Frequency: 2,000-6,000 Hz. Duration: 0.5-3 seconds. Used in family communication, especially between mothers and young, and between mated pairs maintaining territory.
- **Other vocalizations.** Squeals (distress), hisses (defensive), and a remarkable long-distance "wahoo" call used between mated pairs separated in rocky terrain.
- **Nocturnal vocal ecology.** Because ringtails are strictly nocturnal, their vocalizations must work in darkness. The sharp bark carries through rocky canyons (echo amplification). The chitter is quieter, for close-range family communication in the den.

(Sources: Ringtail vocalization; bark alarm call; chitter family communication; nocturnal acoustic ecology)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Ringtail

**Alarm Bark:**
- **Frequency:** 1,000-4,000 Hz. Sharp, fox-like.
- **Character:** "YAP" or "BARK." Surprisingly loud. Quick.
- **Duration:** 0.1-0.3 seconds. May repeat 3-5 times.
- **Context:** Alarm. Startled or threatened. Rocky canyon amplification.

**Family Chitter:**
- **Frequency:** 2,000-6,000 Hz. Rapid clicking.
- **Character:** "cht-cht-cht-cht." Excitable. Close-range.
- **Duration:** 0.5-3 seconds.
- **Context:** Mother-young communication. Mated pairs. Den proximity.

**Long-Distance "Wahoo":**
- **Frequency:** 500-2,000 Hz.
- **Character:** Drawn-out, two-syllable call. Between separated mates.
- **Duration:** 1-2 seconds.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=5. Moderate, nocturnal, surprisingly vocal for a secretive animal. FIRST PROCYONIDAE.
