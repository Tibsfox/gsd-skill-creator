SUSTAINED LOW-FREQUENCY ROAR — THE OPEN PACIFIC NORTHWEST COAST — E=8

The Strait of Juan de Fuca is 96 miles long and 12-17 miles wide, connecting the Pacific Ocean to the Salish Sea. Pacific Ocean swell — generated by North Pacific storms thousands of miles away, traveling 3-5 days before arrival — enters the strait at Cape Flattery and breaks on the Olympic Peninsula shore. The result is a sustained, continuous, low-frequency roar that is not a sequence of discrete events but a wall of sound that has been present through the winter for as long as the coast has existed. E=8. Felt in the chest. Audible miles away. The infrasound component is not heard but pressed against the body.

The Acoustic Physics of Open Coast Surf

Open coast surf produces sound through wave breaking: when a wave breaks, it entraps air bubbles in the turbulent water. These bubbles collapse under pressure, releasing acoustic energy across a broad frequency spectrum. The low-frequency component (1-50 Hz) comes from large-scale turbulence; the higher frequencies come from individual bubble collapse events. At any moment, multiple waves are breaking at different stages of their approach — the combined output is continuous. New breaking events constantly replace completing ones. The roar never ends while the swell runs.

"The infrasound component of breaking surf is not heard — it is felt. A physical pressure on the chest, a vibration in the ground. Recordings capture the audio range. They cannot capture what the body knows when standing at the edge of the Pacific."

Where the Energy Comes From

North Pacific Storm Tracks

Winter extratropical cyclones generate swell over thousands of miles of fetch. A storm near Japan produces swell that arrives at the Washington coast 3-5 days later with periods of 12-18 seconds.

Gulf of Alaska

The Aleutian Low drives storms eastward. Gulf of Alaska storms produce the highest-amplitude, most frequent winter swell at shorter travel times. Significant wave heights 15-25 feet in major winter events.

Cape Flattery

The northwesternmost point of the contiguous United States. The first land the Pacific swell encounters. Surf here is among the most intense on the Washington coast. Makah Nation traditional territory.

La Push / Quileute

The most accessible open-coast surf on the outer Olympic Peninsula. The Quileute people have lived here for thousands of years. The surf is not background; it is information. E=8 as baseline winter soundscape.

Cultural Context: Makah and Quileute Coastal Knowledge

The Makah Nation at Cape Flattery and the Quileute Nation at La Push have maintained continuous presence on the outer Olympic Peninsula coast for thousands of years. The Makah are the only tribe in the United States with a treaty-guaranteed right to hunt whales — a right maintained because of their deep ocean-going tradition. For the Makah, the surf of the Strait of Juan de Fuca is not a natural phenomenon to be observed from safety; it is the medium of their livelihood. The sound of E=8 surf is, in this context, the sound of the working ocean.
