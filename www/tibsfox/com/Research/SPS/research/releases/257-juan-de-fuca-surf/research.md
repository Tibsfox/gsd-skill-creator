## THE STRAIT OF JUAN DE FUCA

### Geography and Scale

The Strait of Juan de Fuca is one of the most significant oceanic passages on the West Coast:

- **Physical dimensions.** The Strait of Juan de Fuca is approximately 96 miles (154 km) long and 12-17 miles (19-27 km) wide, connecting the Pacific Ocean at its western mouth (between Cape Flattery, Washington, and Vancouver Island, British Columbia) to the Salish Sea (Puget Sound + Strait of Georgia) at its eastern end. The strait runs roughly east-west, with the Olympic Peninsula forming its southern shore and Vancouver Island forming its northern shore.
- **Cape Flattery.** The western mouth of the strait at Cape Flattery is the most exposed point on the Washington coast and the northwesternmost point of the contiguous United States. Cape Flattery is the first land that Pacific Ocean swells encounter when they approach from the west and northwest. The surf at Cape Flattery is among the most intense on the Washington coast.
- **The swell corridor.** The Strait of Juan de Fuca is, in effect, a swell corridor: Pacific Ocean swells generated thousands of miles away in the North Pacific and the Gulf of Alaska travel eastward and enter the strait at its western mouth. As the swell travels eastward along the strait, it interacts with the shoreline in specific ways: headlands produce intense surf; protected embayments are calmer; the wave energy decreases gradually as the swell moves away from the open ocean.
- **Olympic Peninsula coastal morphology.** The Olympic Peninsula's outer coast -- the strip of wilderness between the coast and the mountains -- is one of the most remote stretches of shoreline in the contiguous United States. The coast is accessible only by a few roads and trails; the interior is Olympic National Park. This remoteness means that the coastal soundscape exists largely without human development. The surf sounds as it would have 10,000 years ago.

(Sources: Strait of Juan de Fuca dimensions 96 miles long, 12-17 miles wide; Cape Flattery as northwest corner of contiguous US; swell corridor dynamics; Pacific Ocean swell from North Pacific and Gulf of Alaska; Olympic Peninsula outer coast morphology; Olympic National Park; coastal remoteness)

## THE ACOUSTICS OF OPEN COAST SURF

### What Produces the Low-Frequency Roar

The sustained low-frequency roar of open coast surf has specific physical origins:

- **Wave breaking.** The primary sound source in surf is wave breaking -- the turbulent mixing of air and water as a wave breaks. When a wave breaks, it entraps air bubbles in the turbulent water. These bubbles collapse under pressure, releasing acoustic energy across a broad frequency spectrum. The low-frequency component (below 100 Hz) is produced by the large-scale turbulent motions; the higher-frequency components (above 100 Hz) are produced by individual bubble collapse events.
- **Multiple simultaneous breaking events.** At any given moment on the open coast, multiple waves are breaking at different stages of their approach: some breaking far offshore on submerged sandbars or reefs, others breaking at the shoreline. The combined acoustic output of many simultaneous breaking events creates the sustained roar quality -- it is not a sequence of discrete loud moments but a continuous acoustic environment where new breaking events constantly replace completing ones.
- **Frequency character at E=8.** Open coast surf in winter swell conditions produces a sound that is heavily weighted toward low frequencies: the fundamental energy is in the 1-50 Hz range (infrasound to low-bass audio). This low-frequency energy propagates over long distances -- surf can be heard miles from the coastline in favorable conditions. The low-frequency component is the component felt in the body, not just heard: a physical pressure on the chest and a vibration in the ground.
- **The infrasound component.** Much of the energy in breaking surf is in the infrasound range (below 20 Hz, below the threshold of human hearing). This infrasound is not heard but felt -- it creates a physical sensation of pressure and vibration. The infrasound component is part of why large surf feels overwhelming in a way that a recording does not fully capture: recordings capture the audio-range sound; they cannot capture the infrasound physical sensation.
- **E=8 placement.** E=8 is the near-maximum of the SPS energy scale. Juan de Fuca Surf at E=8 is placed here because: the sound is continuous (not episodic); it is massive (audible miles away); it is physically felt (infrasound component); and it dominates the acoustic environment completely -- all other sounds within range are overwhelmed. A coastal bird call, a human voice, a rain shower -- all are drowned in the sustained roar.

## WINTER SWELL IN THE NORTH PACIFIC

### Where the Energy Comes From

The Juan de Fuca Surf is not generated locally -- it arrives from thousands of miles away:

- **North Pacific storm tracks.** The North Pacific Ocean is one of the world's most energetic ocean basins. In winter, extratropical cyclones track across the North Pacific from west to east, generating sustained winds over fetch distances of thousands of miles. The surface winds impose momentum on the water surface, generating waves that grow in height and period as they travel downwind.
- **Swell generation.** When the storm passes and the wind dies, the waves continue to travel as swell -- organized wave energy that moves across the ocean in coherent packets. Swell from North Pacific storms typically has wave periods of 12-18 seconds (the time between successive wave crests). These long-period swells travel faster and farther than shorter-period wind chop. A swell generated by a storm near Japan can reach the Washington coast three to five days later.
- **The Gulf of Alaska contribution.** The Gulf of Alaska, directly north of the Pacific Northwest, generates its own storm systems in winter -- the Aleutian Low pressure system, a persistent low-pressure center that drives storm after storm eastward across the North Pacific and into the Gulf of Alaska. Swell from Gulf of Alaska storms reaches the Washington coast with shorter travel times than trans-Pacific swell, contributing the highest-amplitude, most frequent winter swell.
- **Swell height at the Washington coast.** Winter significant wave heights (the average of the top one-third of all waves) at the outer Washington coast can reach 15-25 feet (4.5-7.5 meters) during major storm swell events. Exceptional events exceed 30 feet. The NOAA buoys offshore of the Washington coast regularly record 10-15 foot seas in winter. At these wave heights, the surf at the coast is violent, continuous, and loud.

(Sources: North Pacific storm tracks; extratropical cyclone generation of swell; swell period 12-18 seconds; trans-Pacific swell travel time 3-5 days from Japan; Gulf of Alaska Aleutian Low; Gulf of Alaska swell contribution; Washington coast significant wave heights 15-25 feet winter; exceptional events exceeding 30 feet; NOAA buoy data Washington coast)

## THE OLYMPIC PENINSULA COAST

### One of the Last Wild Coasts in North America

The setting for Juan de Fuca Surf:

- **Olympic National Park coastal strip.** The Olympic Peninsula's outer coast is administered primarily by Olympic National Park and the Olympic Coast National Marine Sanctuary. The coastal strip is a narrow wilderness: beach, sea stacks, headlands, and dense rainforest backing to the mountains. There are no coastal cities on the outer Olympic Peninsula coast; there are a few small towns (Forks, La Push, Neah Bay) and wilderness camping areas.
- **Sea stacks.** The Olympic Peninsula coast has extensive sea stacks -- isolated rock pillars standing offshore, remnants of headlands eroded by wave action. These sea stacks modify the surf pattern: waves wrap around them, producing complex interference patterns. The sound of surf around sea stacks is more complex than straight-beach surf -- multiple wave fronts arrive at different angles, creating overlapping break patterns.
- **La Push.** La Push (Quileute Nation land) is the most accessible surf location on the outer Olympic Peninsula. The beach at La Push faces directly west into the Pacific, receives open-ocean swell without obstruction, and has a wave exposure that is as direct as any on the Washington coast. The Quileute people have lived at La Push for thousands of years -- this soundscape is embedded in their cultural history.
- **Makah Nation and Cape Flattery.** Cape Flattery is in the Makah Nation's traditional territory. The Makah are the only tribe in the United States with a treaty-guaranteed right to hunt whales -- a right maintained because of their traditional ocean-going culture. The surf at Cape Flattery, the loudest and most exposed location in the region, is central to that ocean-going tradition. The sound of the surf is not background; it is information.

(Sources: Olympic National Park coastal strip; Olympic Coast National Marine Sanctuary; Olympic Peninsula coastal towns: Forks, La Push, Neah Bay; sea stacks; La Push / Quileute Nation; wave exposure at La Push; Cape Flattery / Makah Nation; Makah whale hunting treaty right; surf as cultural information for maritime people)
