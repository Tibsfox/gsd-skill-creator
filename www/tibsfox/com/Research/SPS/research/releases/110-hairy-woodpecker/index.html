Fourth Consecutive Picidae — Halfway to the Sciuridae Record

Four consecutive Picidae: Acorn Woodpecker (107), Pileated laughing (108), Pileated drumming (109), Hairy Woodpecker (110). The Picidae streak is now at half the Sciuridae octet record (8 consecutive). Each entry is acoustically distinct: colonial laughter, solitary primal scream, machine-gun percussion, sharp staccato call. No two Picidae entries sound alike. The family’s acoustic diversity is wider than any other in the engine.

Genus Dryobates Enters — The Tree-Treader

Dryobates from Greek drys (“tree”) + bates (“one who treads”) — “tree-treader.” Where Dryocopus (Pileated, degrees 108–109) means “tree-beater,” Dryobates means “tree-treader” — a softer, more exploratory relationship with the tree. The Downy Woodpecker (Dryobates pubescens, degree 111) is in the same genus — consecutive same-genus entries documenting acoustic scaling in closely related species.

The Hairy-Downy Confusion Problem — Degree 111 Preview

The Hairy and Downy Woodpeckers are the most commonly confused bird pair in North American birding. Nearly identical plumage. Different sizes (Hairy is ~50% longer, ~3x heavier). The bill proportion is the key visual mark: Hairy’s bill is nearly as long as the head; Downy’s bill is ~1/3 head length. The calls are the key acoustic mark: Hairy’s sharp “PEEK!” vs. Downy’s soft “pik.” Degrees 110–111 document this comparison in full.

Sharp Peek! — The Call

The Hairy Woodpecker’s primary call is a sharp, emphatic “peek!” — a single loud note that rings through the forest at 2,000–6,000 Hz. Brief (0.05–0.1 sec) and explosive. The call is metallic, commanding, and immediately attention-grabbing. It is louder, sharper, and lower-pitched than the Downy’s softer “pik” — the most reliable acoustic field mark separating the two species.

The Stuttering Drum

The Hairy Woodpecker’s drum is a rapid, stuttering roll at approximately 25 strikes per second — significantly faster than the Pileated’s 15–16/sec but with less force per strike. Duration 0.5–1.5 seconds. Fundamental frequency 100–200 Hz. The drum is sharp and snappy compared to the Pileated’s deep, booming roll. Detection range 200–500 meters.

E=5 — Sharp and Emphatic, Not Maximum

Intermediate

The Hairy's peek! and drum are loud and commanding but not overwhelming. E=5 is the energy correction from the Pileated's E=6. Still powerful, but controlled. The Hairy is the medium percussionist.

!

Sharp Peek! — The Diagnostic Call

Intermediate

Sharp, emphatic, metallic "PEEK!" at 2,000-6,000 Hz. The most reliable acoustic field mark separating Hairy from Downy. Louder, sharper, lower than the Downy's soft "pik."

D

Genus Dryobates — Tree-Treader

Advanced

New genus enters. Dryobates = "tree-treader." Dryocopus = "tree-beater." The etymological shift from beating to treading reflects the size and force difference. Consecutive same-genus entries at 110-111 (Hairy/Downy).

P

Fourth Consecutive Picidae (107–110)

Intermediate

Acorn (107), Pileated laughing (108), Pileated drumming (109), Hairy (110). Halfway to the Sciuridae record (8). Each entry acoustically unique.

G

Habitat Generalist

Foundational

The Hairy occupies virtually any forest type: coniferous, mixed, deciduous, suburban, burned. More flexible than the Pileated (old-growth specialist) or Acorn (oak savanna). Generalist in the Picidae streak.
