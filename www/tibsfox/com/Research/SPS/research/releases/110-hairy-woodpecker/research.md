**Genus etymology:** *Dryobates* from Greek *drys* ("tree") + *bates* ("one who treads") -- "tree-treader." Where *Dryocopus* (Pileated) means "tree-beater," *Dryobates* means "tree-treader" -- a softer, more exploratory relationship with the tree.

## THE HAIRY WOODPECKER -- *DRYOBATES VILLOSUS*

### Overview

The Hairy Woodpecker is a **medium-sized woodpecker** found throughout North America -- one of the most widespread and adaptable woodpecker species on the continent. In the PNW, it inhabits coniferous, mixed, and deciduous forest from sea level to timberline. It is a year-round resident, non-migratory, and a common presence in mature forest.

The Hairy Woodpecker is most frequently confused with the **Downy Woodpecker** (*Dryobates pubescens*, degree 111). The two species are remarkably similar in plumage pattern but differ dramatically in size: the Hairy is approximately 50% longer and nearly three times heavier than the Downy. This size difference produces correspondingly different acoustic profiles -- a comparison that degree 111 will document in detail.

**Physical characteristics:**
- **Length:** 18-26 cm (significantly larger than Downy's 14-18 cm)
- **Weight:** 40-95 g (roughly 3x the Downy's 21-28 g)
- **Wingspan:** 33-41 cm
- **Bill:** proportionally long and chisel-shaped -- nearly as long as the head (Downy's bill is proportionally much shorter, about 1/3 head length)
- **Plumage:** black and white. Black upperparts with white back stripe. White underparts. Black wings with white spotting. Males have a red patch on the back of the head. Females lack the red patch.

### Habitat and Behavior

The Hairy Woodpecker is a **generalist** compared to the Pileated (old-growth specialist) and the Acorn Woodpecker (oak savanna specialist). The Hairy occupies virtually any forest type in the PNW:

- **Coniferous forest** -- Douglas-fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, Sitka spruce
- **Mixed forest** -- conifer-hardwood mixtures
- **Deciduous forest** -- especially riparian areas with cottonwood and alder
- **Suburban parks and neighborhoods** with large trees
- **Burned forest** -- the Hairy Woodpecker is often one of the first species to colonize recently burned forest, foraging on bark beetles attracted to damaged trees

This habitat flexibility is reflected in the species' abundance -- the Hairy Woodpecker is one of the most commonly encountered woodpeckers in PNW forests.

### Foraging Ecology

The Hairy Woodpecker is primarily an **insectivore** that extracts insects and their larvae from bark and wood:

- **Bark gleaning** -- picks insects from bark surfaces
- **Excavation** -- drills into wood to extract wood-boring beetle larvae (the primary food source)
- **Scaling** -- flakes off bark to access insects beneath
- **Probing** -- uses its long, barbed tongue (extending up to 4 cm beyond the bill tip) to extract larvae from galleries

The foraging technique is diagnostic: the Hairy strikes the wood with a strong, deliberate blow, then pauses to listen for insect movement, then strikes again or moves. The foraging strikes are louder and more forceful than the Downy's, reflecting the larger bill and greater body mass.

(Sources: Jackson, Ouellet & Jackson 2002; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE: SHARP PEEK! + STUTTERING DRUM

### The Peek! Call

The Hairy Woodpecker's primary call is a **sharp, emphatic "peek!"** -- a single loud note that rings through the forest. The peek! call is:

- **Frequency:** 2,000-6,000 Hz. Higher-pitched than the Pileated's calls but lower than many songbirds.
- **Duration:** approximately 0.05-0.1 seconds per note. The call is brief and explosive.
- **Character:** sharp, metallic, emphatic. The peek! is described as sounding like a loud, sharp tap on a hard surface. It is louder, sharper, and lower-pitched than the Downy Woodpecker's softer "pik" call.
- **Function:** contact call, alarm call, agitation. Given year-round by both sexes.
- **Amplitude:** E=5 territory. The peek! is loud -- it carries well through forest and is immediately attention-grabbing. It is not the overwhelming, physically dominant sound of the Pileated (E=6), but it is sharp and commanding.

The **Hairy vs. Downy call comparison** is one of the most useful acoustic identification tools in PNW birding:
- **Hairy:** sharp, loud, emphatic "PEEK!" -- lower-pitched, more forceful
- **Downy:** soft, high, gentle "pik" -- higher-pitched, less forceful

The difference is dramatic and diagnostic. If you hear a sharp, ringing "PEEK!" from a black-and-white woodpecker, it is a Hairy. If you hear a soft, gentle "pik," it is a Downy.

### The Stuttering Drum

The Hairy Woodpecker's drum is a **rapid, stuttering roll** that differs from the Pileated's machine-gun burst:

- **Strike rate:** approximately 25 strikes per second -- significantly faster than the Pileated's 15-16/sec but with less force per strike.
- **Duration:** 0.5-1.5 seconds. Shorter than the Pileated's 1-3 second rolls.
- **Frequency:** 100-200 Hz fundamental. Higher than the Pileated's 40-80 Hz because the Hairy is smaller and selects smaller-diameter drumming surfaces.
- **Character:** a rapid, stuttering roll with a slight acceleration and sometimes a terminal flourish. The drum is sharp and snappy compared to the Pileated's deep, booming roll.
- **Amplitude:** loud but not overwhelming. The drum carries 200-500 meters through forest -- impressive but not the 1+ km range of the Pileated.

Smaller body, faster rate, higher pitch, less power. The physics of woodpecker drumming scales predictably with body size.

## THE HAIRY-DOWNY CONFUSION PROBLEM

The Hairy Woodpecker and the Downy Woodpecker are the most commonly confused bird pair in North American birding. The confusion is understandable -- they share nearly identical plumage patterns (black and white, white back stripe, males with red on the head) and often occur in the same habitats. The key differences are:

### Size
- **Hairy:** 18-26 cm, 40-95 g -- roughly robin-sized
- **Downy:** 14-18 cm, 21-28 g -- roughly sparrow-sized
- The Hairy is ~50% longer and ~3x heavier

### Bill Proportion
- **Hairy:** bill nearly as long as the head -- chisel-shaped and substantial
- **Downy:** bill about 1/3 the length of the head -- short and dainty
- **This is the most reliable visual field mark.** If the bill looks proportionally large, it's a Hairy. If the bill looks small relative to the head, it's a Downy.

### Calls
- **Hairy:** sharp, loud "PEEK!" -- emphatic and ringing
- **Downy:** soft, gentle "pik" -- understated and quiet
- The call difference is the most reliable acoustic field mark.

### Drumming
- **Hairy:** faster (~25/sec), shorter (0.5-1.5 sec), sharper
- **Downy:** somewhat slower (15-25/sec), shorter (0.5-1 sec), softer and higher-pitched

The Hairy-Downy comparison at degrees 110-111 gives the engine consecutive entries in the same genus, documenting the acoustic scaling of closely related species.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014)
