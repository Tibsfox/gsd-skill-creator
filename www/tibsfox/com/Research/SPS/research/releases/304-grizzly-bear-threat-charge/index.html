The Most Physically Overwhelming Sound in the Pacific Northwest

The grizzly bear threat charge in a surprise encounter is the SPS engine's most physically overwhelming acoustic event. Ursus arctos horribilis — the North American grizzly bear — produces the full roar plus ground percussion: a multi-frequency vocalization combining deep chest resonance with intense laryngeal burst, arriving simultaneously with the physical percussion of front paw impacts from an animal weighing 400-790 pounds charging at up to 35 mph. At the close range of a surprise encounter (typically 30-100 feet), the sound is not just heard but felt — vibration in the chest, the ground trembling with impacts, the total acoustic experience of maximum physical force moving rapidly toward the listener.

The threat charge vocalization is the same for bluff charges and full charges. The listener cannot distinguish. This uncertainty is part of the encounter’s psychological intensity: the acoustic experience is identical whether contact follows or not. The roar that stops the charge after ten feet produces the same sound as the roar that does not. At E=9, this is the correct calibration.

Acoustic Profile — Roar + Ground Percussion

The roar: Multi-frequency. Deep chest resonance (low end: 50-200 Hz) combined with intense laryngeal burst (upper end: 500-2,000 Hz). Not purely bass — the burst quality gives the roar its cutting character. Wildlife encounter literature: "not just heard but felt." Vibration at close range.

Ground percussion: Front paw impacts at 35 mph charge speed. 400-790 lbs mass on hard ground = audible percussion at significant distance. Combined with roar: multi-channel acoustic event arriving from different heights and directions simultaneously.

The total event: Roar + paw impacts + vegetation displacement + charge speed = most physically overwhelming single-animal acoustic encounter in PNW.

E=9: Physical force + close range + vibrational + bluff/full uncertainty = maximum psychological and acoustic intensity. Third independent E=9 justification in arc.

Ursus arctos horribilis — The Species

Adult male grizzlies in the Pacific Northwest: 400-790 pounds, 3.5-5 feet at shoulder. The shoulder hump (muscle mass for digging) and 5-7 cm claws (adapted for digging, not climbing) distinguish the grizzly from the black bear documented in an earlier SPS degree. The grizzly is substantially larger; the acoustic difference is a direct function of mass. A larger body produces a more overwhelming threat charge roar. Charge speed: up to 35 mph for short distances. Over open ground, a human cannot outrun a grizzly.

The North Cascades Grizzly: Recovery or Absence?

The North Cascades ecosystem — 9,800 square miles of national park, wilderness, and national forest — is designated by USFWS as a grizzly bear recovery zone. The local population, if it persists, may number fewer than 10 individuals, possibly 3-5. Survey efforts with camera traps and hair snares have produced inconclusive results in recent years. The habitat quality is high; the population may be functionally absent. Federal agencies have evaluated supplemental augmentation — introducing grizzlies from Greater Yellowstone or Northern Continental Divide — with intense local opposition from Okanogan, Chelan, and Whatcom counties and varying tribal positions. The engine documents the sound the North Cascades could again contain.
