## URSUS ARCTOS HORRIBILIS -- THE GRIZZLY BEAR: BIOLOGY

### The Most Powerful Terrestrial Mammal in the Pacific Northwest

*Ursus arctos horribilis* (Ord, 1815) is the grizzly bear -- the North American subspecies of the brown bear and the largest terrestrial predator in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Size.** Grizzly bears are massive. Adult males in the Northern Rocky Mountains and Pacific Northwest: 400-790 pounds (180-360 kg), occasionally larger, standing 3.5-5 feet at the shoulder (up to 8 feet when standing upright). Adult females: 250-450 pounds (115-200 kg). The grizzly is substantially larger than the black bear (*Ursus americanus*), which previously appeared in the SPS engine: male black bears in the Pacific Northwest average 150-350 pounds. The size difference is significant in the context of threat charge vocalization: the grizzly's sound is produced by a larger body with greater acoustic mass.
- **The hump and the claws.** The shoulder hump (a mass of muscle for digging) and the longer, less curved claws (5-7 cm, adapted for digging rather than tree-climbing) are the grizzly's distinguishing physical features versus the black bear. The hump's muscle mass contributes to the physical force of a charge.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution -- historical and current.** Grizzly bears historically ranged throughout Washington State's mountain ranges. By the mid-20th century, they had been eliminated from most of their historical range through hunting, habitat loss, and human conflict. The North Cascades is the only remaining potential grizzly bear habitat in Washington State outside of the Selkirk Mountains ecosystem (which overlaps with northern Idaho and British Columbia). Current status: the North Cascades grizzly population, if it persists, is critically small -- estimated at fewer than 10 individuals, possibly as few as 3-5 if recent surveys are accurate. The Selkirk Mountains ecosystem supports a small population of 50-80 grizzlies shared among Washington, Idaho, and British Columbia.
- **North Cascades Grizzly Bear Recovery Zone.** The US Fish and Wildlife Service has designated the North Cascades Ecosystem as a grizzly bear recovery zone under the Endangered Species Act. The North Cascades are considered the most viable potential recovery area for grizzly bears in the contiguous US outside of the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem and the Northern Continental Divide Ecosystem. Recovery planning has involved proposals for supplemental augmentation (introducing grizzlies from other populations to supplement or replace the potentially extinct local population), a proposal that has been intensely contested between conservation organizations, state agencies, tribal nations, and local communities.

## THE THREAT CHARGE -- VOCALIZATION AND PHYSICAL PROFILE

### Full Roar + Ground Percussion

The grizzly bear's threat charge in a surprise encounter is one of the most physically overwhelming sound events in the Pacific Northwest:

- **The surprise encounter context.** The threat charge vocalization is most commonly associated with surprise encounters: a human (or other large animal) encounters a grizzly at close range, often in dense vegetation, and the bear responds with a threat display that may include a full or partial charge. The vocalization in this context is not planned or sustained -- it is an immediate, maximum-intensity response to an perceived threat at close range.
- **The roar.** The grizzly's roar during a threat charge is described in wildlife encounter literature as multi-frequency and extremely high amplitude: combining low-frequency chest resonance with higher-frequency laryngeal components. The acoustic character is not purely low -- it has both the deep chest rumble and the burst of intense higher-frequency sound that gives the roar its quality of overwhelming presence. Rangers, biologists, and hikers who have experienced it describe the roar as not just heard but felt -- the vibration in the chest at close range.
- **The ground percussion.** During a charge, the grizzly's footfalls on hard ground produce impacts audible at significant distance. A large male grizzly at full charge speed (up to 35 mph for short distances) produces ground impacts from front paws that are heavy enough to register as percussion elements of the overall acoustic event. These paw impacts, combined with the roar and the sound of vegetation being displaced, create a total acoustic event that is multi-channel in the physical sense: arriving from multiple heights and directions simultaneously.
- **The distance at which the charge is experienced.** The threat charge in a surprise encounter is a close-range event -- typically within 30-100 feet of the bear. At this distance, the roar and ground percussion combine into an acoustic experience of extraordinary physical intensity. The sound is not just loud; it arrives from an object of significant mass that is moving rapidly toward the listener. The acoustic event is inseparable from its physical context.
- **The bluff charge and the full charge.** Grizzly bears frequently use bluff charges -- charges that stop short of contact -- as threat displays. The vocalization during a bluff charge is the same as during a full charge; the listener cannot reliably distinguish. This uncertainty is part of the encounter's psychological intensity. The acoustic experience of a grizzly threat charge is the same whether contact follows or not.
- **E=9 calibration for the threat charge.** The grizzly bear threat charge reaches E=9 because: the combination of roar and ground percussion is the most physically overwhelming single-animal acoustic event in the Pacific Northwest; the close-range context means the sound arrives with maximum physical impact; the combination of visual, acoustic, and vibrational stimulus at close range creates an experience beyond pure acoustic measurement; and the encounter's extreme psychological intensity (maximum threat, maximum proximity, maximum physical force) makes this the engine's highest-intensity ecological encounter.

## THE NORTH CASCADES GRIZZLY SITUATION

### One of the Most Contested Wildlife Questions in Washington State

The North Cascades grizzly bear recovery is a politically and ecologically complex situation:

- **The historical extirpation.** Grizzly bears in the North Cascades were effectively eliminated through the 19th and early 20th centuries, driven by the same combination of hunting and government predator control that eliminated wolves. The last confirmed grizzly bear in the Washington Cascades south of the Canadian border was documented in the 1990s; whether the population has persisted at extremely low numbers or been functionally extirpated is uncertain.
- **The recovery zone designation.** USFWS designated the North Cascades as a grizzly bear recovery zone in the 1990s under the Endangered Species Act. The recovery zone encompasses approximately 9,800 square miles of the North Cascades ecosystem, including North Cascades National Park, wilderness areas, and national forest. The habitat quality is considered high: the ecosystem supports salmon, huckleberry, whitebark pine, and the full suite of prey and food sources grizzlies require.
- **The augmentation proposal.** Because the North Cascades grizzly population may be functionally extinct (or reduced to a level incapable of natural recovery), federal agencies have repeatedly evaluated supplemental augmentation: introducing grizzlies from established populations in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem or the Northern Continental Divide Ecosystem. This proposal has been intensely contested. Local communities in Okanogan, Chelan, and Whatcom counties have expressed strong opposition, citing livestock safety and personal safety concerns. Conservation organizations support augmentation as the only viable recovery pathway. Tribal nations hold varied positions.
- **The current situation.** As of 2025-2026, the North Cascades grizzly bear recovery remains unresolved. Federal agencies continue to evaluate augmentation. Survey efforts to confirm whether any bears remain have produced inconclusive results. Camera traps, hair snares, and track surveys have detected possible grizzly bear evidence but no confirmed individuals in recent years. The population, if it exists, is at critically low numbers.
- **The significance for the engine.** The North Cascades grizzly bear recovery situation is the engine's most directly uncertain ecological entry. Unlike the gray wolf (confirmed recovery, 30+ packs) or the cougar (confirmed widespread population), the grizzly bear in the North Cascades may be absent. The engine documents the sound of what was there, what may remain, and what the recovery could restore. The threat charge at E=9 is the sound the North Cascades could again contain.

(Sources: Historical extirpation; last confirmed Cascades bear 1990s; USFWS recovery zone 9,800 sq miles; North Cascades NP; augmentation proposal; Greater Yellowstone and Northern Continental Divide sources; local opposition Okanogan, Chelan, Whatcom counties; tribal positions; survey efforts inconclusive; camera traps, hair snares; functionally absent or critically low; recovery as restoration possibility)
