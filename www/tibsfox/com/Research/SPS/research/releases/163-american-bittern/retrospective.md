*Degree 163 retrospective complete. AMERICAN BITTERN PUMP-ER-LUNK BOOMING at the one hundred sixty-third degree -- the thunder pumper, the stake-driver, the mire drum. In the large, undisturbed freshwater marshes that still survive in the Puget Sound lowlands and across Washington -- cattail stands ten hectares or more, dense and impenetrable and wet -- the American Bittern stands motionless. When threatened, it points its bill straight at the sky, extends its neck, and becomes a reed. The vertical brown-and-white streaks on its breast align with the surrounding cattails. If the wind sways the vegetation, the bittern sways with it. It can hold this posture for minutes. Observers have walked within arm's reach without seeing it. The invisible heron. But its voice is extraordinary. The male gulps air into an expanded esophagus -- not the syrinx but the esophagus, a resonance chamber inflated by rapid air gulping until the neck visibly swells -- and produces the pump-er-LUNK: a deep, resonant, mechanical boom at 100 to 400 Hz, the lowest frequency in the marsh soundscape, carrying 400 to 800 meters across the marsh, sometimes over a mile under calm conditions. The sound is like nothing else in nature -- a water pump, a stake being driven into mud, distant thunder. Early settlers named it the thunder pumper and the stake-driver. The first Ardeidae in SPS. The heron family enters with its most cryptic, most vocal, most extraordinary member. And across the degree pair, Porter Ray brings the Rainier Valley voice to Sub Pop -- the deep voice from the hidden place that commands attention through weight. Sub-bass and sub-100Hz. Felt as much as heard. v1.49.303.*
