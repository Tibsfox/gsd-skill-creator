Anatidae — NEW FAMILY — The Duck Family Enters

Anatidae enters the engine for the first time — ducks, geese, and swans. One of the most species-rich bird families in the PNW (~174 species globally). The Harlequin Duck leads the family in — not the common Mallard but a turbulent-water specialist with the most striking plumage of any PNW duck. The family enters with maximum visual impact.

The Painted Player — Commedia Dell’Arte in Feathers

Named after Harlequin (Arlecchino) of Italian commedia dell’arte — the acrobatic, elaborately costumed trickster. Scientific name Histrionicus histrionicus = “actor actor” (from Latin histrio). The male’s plumage looks painted on: slate blue body, bold white crescents, chestnut flanks and crown. Each mark precisely placed. The bird wears a theatrical costume that nature designed. The most theatrical bird in North American ornithology.

Turbulent Water Specialist — Stream to Surf

The Harlequin occupies turbulent water at every stage. Breeding: fast-flowing mountain streams (the same habitat as the American Dipper at 116–117). Dives in rapids for caddisfly, stonefly, mayfly larvae. Winter: rocky coastlines where waves crash against rock. Forages in the surf zone for mussels, barnacles, small crabs. Mountain torrent or ocean breaker — the Harlequin seeks chaos.

Extraordinary Plumage

The male Harlequin Duck’s plumage is among the most visually striking of any North American bird — a theatrical costume in slate blue, white, and chestnut.

Body

Slate blue, luminous in direct sun, dark in shadow. The canvas for all other marks.

Head

White crescent between eye and bill. White ear spot. Chestnut crown stripe. Bold geometric marks.

Flanks

Chestnut-red, contrasting with slate blue body. The warmth against the cool.

Wing Pattern

White crescents and spots along flanks/scapulars. Geometric precision. Looks painted on.

Female

Brownish overall, lighter face patches. Extreme sexual dimorphism. Camouflage for nesting.

Eclipse Molt

Males go dull post-breeding (late summer). The costume comes off temporarily. Seasonal theater.

Acoustic Profile

Squeak + high whistle — not the quack you expect from a duck. Male: thin high whistle (weee-ooo, 3,000–6,000 Hz). Female: harsh ek-ek-ek (1,000–3,000 Hz). In groups: thin whistles and harsh notes create an unusual acoustic texture. E=4 — audible in chaos, not dominating it. Cuts through turbulent water noise by being pitched above it.

Cross-References

SPS-116 American Dipper (song)

Habitat overlap. Both breed on turbulent mountain streams. Same prey: caddisfly, stonefly, mayfly larvae. Dipper walks underwater; Harlequin dives from surface. Three consecutive aquatic degrees (116-117-118).

SPS-117 American Dipper (dunking)

Diving comparison. Both dive in fast-flowing streams. Dipper uses wing hydrofoils to walk bottom; Harlequin swims and dives. Passerine adapted to water vs. duck adapted to turbulence.

SPS-115 Black-backed Woodpecker

Water continuation (4 degrees). Fire (115) → stream/song (116) → stream/dunk (117) → turbulent stream/duck (118). The aquatic era.

SPS-021 Northern Flicker

First Picidae vs first Anatidae. Flicker introduced woodpeckers (degree 21). Harlequin introduces ducks (degree 118). Both family firsts.

SPS-004 Northern Spotted Owl

Visual contrast. Spotted Owl: cryptic (brown, camouflaged). Harlequin Duck: spectacular (slate blue, white crescents, chestnut). The owl hides; the duck displays.
