### The Counter-Curve

After four degrees of bills curving progressively downward (168-171), the Marbled Godwit reverses direction:

- **Curlew down vs godwit up:** The curlew bill curves downward. The godwit bill curves slightly upward. This is not a subtle difference -- it is a fundamental reversal of bill morphology. The two shapes represent different feeding strategies.
- **Function:** The upturned bill allows the godwit to sweep laterally through soft mud and shallow water, capturing prey by touch and movement. The curlew's downcurved bill probes down into burrows. Different curves access different prey zones.
- **Visual contrast:** On a tidal flat, a Long-billed Curlew and a Marbled Godwit feeding side by side present one of the most striking bill contrasts in shorebird ecology -- one curving dramatically down, the other curving gently up.

## THE UPTURNED BILL

### Godwit Morphology

The Marbled Godwit's bill is a distinctive adaptation:

- **Length:** 10-13 cm. Long, but not as extreme as the Long-billed Curlew (up to 21 cm).
- **Shape:** Slightly upturned. The bill has a gentle upward curve, especially in the distal half. This is the opposite of the curlew's downward curve.
- **Bicolored:** The bill is bicolored -- pink-orange at the base, darkening to black at the tip. The coloring is distinctive and aids in field identification.
- **Function:** The upturned bill is adapted for sweeping through soft mud and shallow water. The godwit sweeps its bill side to side, detecting prey by touch. The upward curve keeps the bill tip near the surface during the sweeping motion, while the curlew's downcurve pushes the tip deeper.
- **Comparison table:**

| Feature | Curlew (171) | Godwit (172) |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Bill direction | Down-curved | Slightly upturned |
| Bill length | Up to 21 cm | 10-13 cm |
| Feeding style | Deep probing | Lateral sweeping |
| Bill color | Dark throughout | Pink-orange base, dark tip |
| Primary prey zone | Deep burrows | Shallow mud/water |

## CINNAMON PLUMAGE AND TIDAL FLAT ECOLOGY

### The Warm-Toned Shorebird

- **Plumage:** The Marbled Godwit is warm cinnamon-buff throughout -- similar in warmth to the Long-billed Curlew. The name "Marbled" refers to the intricate barring and marbling pattern on the back and wings. In flight, the underwings flash bright cinnamon (diagnostic, shared with Long-billed Curlew).
- **Tidal flat feeding:** On tidal flats, Marbled Godwits feed in shallow water and on exposed mud, sweeping the bill through soft substrate. They often feed in mixed flocks with curlews, willets, and other large shorebirds.
- **Breeding:** Like the Long-billed Curlew, the Marbled Godwit breeds on northern Great Plains grasslands. Both species are grassland-obligate breeders affected by prairie conversion to agriculture.
- **Winter and migration:** Marbled Godwits winter on the Pacific Coast from Washington to Mexico. They are regular on PNW tidal flats from fall through spring. More common in the PNW than Long-billed Curlews.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Marbled Godwit

**"Godwit! Godwit!" Call:**
- **Trigger:** Flight, alarm, contact. Given frequently and insistently.
- **Mechanism:** Vocal (syringeal).
- **Character:** A loud, nasal, two-syllable call: "godWIT! godWIT!" Repeated insistently. The stress falls on the second syllable. Clear, carrying, demanding.
- **Frequency:** Approximately 1800-2800 Hz. Nasal quality.
- **Duration:** Each "godwit" is approximately 0.3-0.5 seconds. Repeated 3-10+ times in sequence.
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Very loud. Carries far across open tidal flats. Insistent repetition amplifies impact.

**Display Call:**
- On breeding grounds, a more elaborate repeated call during flight display over grassland.
