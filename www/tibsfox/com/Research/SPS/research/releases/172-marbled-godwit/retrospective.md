After four degrees of Scolopacidae bills curving progressively downward -- straight (snipe, 168), slightly decurved (Dunlin, 169), strongly down-curved (Whimbrel, 170), extremely down-curved (Long-billed Curlew, 171) -- the Marbled Godwit arrives with a bill that curves UP. The counter-curve. A fundamental reversal of bill morphology within the same family. The upturned bill is adapted for lateral sweeping through soft mud and shallow water, a different feeding strategy from the curlew's deep downward probing. On a tidal flat, a Long-billed Curlew and a Marbled Godwit feeding side by side present one of the most striking bill contrasts in shorebird ecology: one curving dramatically down, the other curving gently up. Both are large, warm cinnamon-colored shorebirds with bright cinnamon underwings. Both breed on the Great Plains. Both winter on Pacific Coast tidal flats. But their bills curve in opposite directions, accessing different prey zones. The godwit's call is the other half of the story: "godwit! godwit! godwit!" -- a loud, nasal, insistent two-syllable call repeated 3 to 10 or more times. Onomatopoeic, like "curlew."
