## THE FAWN BLEAT -- A SOUND OF NEED

### The Contact Call

The black-tailed deer fawn produces a distinctive bleat:

- **The sound.** A thin, high-pitched mew -- "maaaa" or "meee" -- wavering and plaintive. Pitched at approximately 500-1,500 Hz. Quiet but carrying in forest environments. The sound is unmistakable if you've heard it.
- **Context.** The fawn bleats when separated from its mother. The bleat is a contact call -- "I'm here, where are you?" The mother responds by approaching, sometimes with a low grunt. The fawn-mother bond depends on this vocal exchange.
- **Frequency and purpose.** The bleat's frequency range (500-1,500 Hz) is optimized for short-range forest communication. Higher frequencies carry less far but are more directional, allowing the mother to locate the fawn precisely. The sound does not carry far enough to alert distant predators.
- **Vulnerability.** The fawn bleat is the sound of vulnerability. A newborn fawn weighs 3-4 kg and cannot outrun predators. Its survival depends entirely on its mother's response to the bleat and on its cryptic spotted coat (which breaks up its outline in dappled forest light).
- **Duration.** Individual bleats last 0.5-1.5 seconds. The fawn may bleat repeatedly at intervals of several seconds when seeking its mother.

## THE BLACK-TAILED DEER -- PNW'S MOST COMMON LARGE MAMMAL

### Ubiquitous Presence

The Black-tailed Deer is the PNW's most familiar large mammal:

- **Subspecies.** The Columbian Black-tailed Deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*) is a subspecies of the Mule Deer. Found from northern California through western Oregon and Washington to coastal British Columbia.
- **Population.** Estimated 300,000-500,000 in Washington state alone. Present in virtually every habitat: old-growth forest, second-growth, clearcuts, suburban gardens, urban parks.
- **Diet.** Browse: leaves, twigs, herbs, forbs, grasses, garden plants. The Black-tailed Deer is a generalist browser, adaptable to a wide range of food sources.
- **Size.** Adults: 50-90 kg (110-200 lbs). Bucks slightly larger. Much smaller than elk or moose -- the Black-tailed Deer is a medium-sized deer.
- **Predators.** Cougars (primary predator), coyotes (on fawns), black bears (on fawns), domestic dogs, vehicles. Fawn mortality is high: 40-60% in the first year, primarily from predation.
- **Suburban deer.** Many PNW residents see Black-tailed Deer daily. They browse in gardens, cross roads, bed down in parks. The most common large wild mammal in the everyday experience of PNW residents.

(Sources: WDFW deer biology; Columbian Black-tailed Deer ecology; PNW mammal surveys; suburban wildlife studies)

## THE FAWN-MOTHER BOND

### Survival Strategy

The fawn's survival depends on its relationship with its mother:

- **The hiding strategy.** For the first 2-3 weeks of life, fawns spend most of their time lying motionless in vegetation. The spotted coat provides camouflage. The mother returns 2-4 times per day to nurse. The fawn bleats when the mother approaches and when it needs to locate her.
- **Scent minimization.** Newborn fawns have very little scent, reducing their detectability by predators. The mother stays away to avoid leading predators to the fawn. The fawn's best defense is to remain motionless and silent.
- **The bleat's risk.** Every bleat is a calculated risk -- the sound attracts the mother but could also attract predators. The fawn bleats when the cost of separation (starvation, exposure) exceeds the cost of vocalization (predator detection). The calculus is life and death.
- **Weaning.** Fawns are weaned at 3-4 months but may stay with the mother through the first winter. The bleating contact call evolves into softer grunts and proximity maintenance as the fawn matures.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of a Black-tailed Deer Fawn

**Fawn Bleat (Contact Call):**
- **Frequency:** 500-1,500 Hz. Thin, high-pitched.
- **Character:** Wavering mew -- "maaaa" or "meee." Plaintive. Carries a few hundred meters in forest.
- **Duration:** 0.5-1.5 seconds per bleat. Repeated at intervals of 3-10 seconds.
- **Context:** Separation from mother. Hunger. Distress. Contact seeking.

**Mother Response (Low Grunt):**
- **Frequency:** 100-400 Hz. Low, soft.
- **Character:** Short grunt or murmur. Reassuring. Signals approach.
- **Duration:** 0.2-0.5 seconds.

**Overall Amplitude:** E=3. Thin, high, quiet but audible. The sound of an infant mammal calling for its mother. Emotionally affecting. Physically small. The sound of vulnerability.
