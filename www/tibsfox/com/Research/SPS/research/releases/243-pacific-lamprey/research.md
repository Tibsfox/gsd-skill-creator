## THE MOST ANCIENT VERTEBRATE IN THE ENGINE

### 450 Million Years of Evolution

The Pacific Lamprey's arrival at degree 243 is the most significant taxonomic event since the Salmonidae entered at degree 236:

- **The jawless vertebrates.** For nearly 450 million years, the ancestors of modern lampreys have existed in the world's waters. The lamprey lineage (Petromyzontida and related groups) predates the evolution of jaws -- the anatomical innovation that produced sharks, bony fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals. To understand what this means: lampreys are *more* primitive than sharks in evolutionary terms. Sharks have jaws; lampreys do not. The jaw is one of the most important innovations in vertebrate evolution, enabling predation, defense, and dietary diversity that was impossible in the jawless lineage. Lampreys have survived 450 million years without it.
- **The geological timeline.** 450 million years ago, Earth was in the Ordovician and Silurian periods. There were no land-living vertebrates. The continents were arranged differently -- what would become North America was partly in the tropics. The first plants were colonizing land. The ancestors of modern lampreys were already swimming in the seas and rivers of that world. Everything in the SPS engine that preceded this entry -- every mammal, every bird, every teleost fish -- belongs to lineages that evolved after the lamprey family had already been established for tens or hundreds of millions of years.
- **Convergent evolution with eel form.** Pacific Lamprey are eel-like in body form: elongated, no paired fins, smooth skin without scales. This superficial resemblance to eels is convergent evolution -- eels (Anguilliformes) are bony fish that independently evolved a similar body form. The resemblance is a function of similar lifestyle (burrowing, swimming through confined spaces, a semi-parasitic life history) rather than shared ancestry. A lamprey is not an eel. A lamprey is a Petromyzontid -- a member of the most ancient surviving vertebrate lineage.
- **No jaws -- but a formidable mouth.** Lampreys have an oral disc rather than jaws: a circular, sucker-like structure ringed with concentric rows of teeth. The teeth are not the same as the teeth of jawed vertebrates -- they are keratinous (made of keratin, like fingernails) rather than enamel-and-dentin structures. The oral disc allows the lamprey to attach to a host fish (or rock, for nest-building) with enormous suction force. The Pacific Lamprey's oral disc is large and conspicuous: an unsettling, alien-looking structure that has contributed to the species' cultural reputation as monstrous.
- **Living fossil status.** The fossil record includes agnathan (jawless) vertebrates from the Ordovician and Silurian periods whose morphology is strikingly similar to modern lampreys. The lamprey body plan has been stable for hundreds of millions of years because it has been successful: the combination of a filter-feeding larval phase, a parasitic or predatory adult phase, and a semelparous reproductive strategy has worked well enough that natural selection has not fundamentally changed it since the Devonian.

(Sources: Petromyzontida evolutionary timeline; Ordovician-Silurian geological context; jawless vertebrate phylogeny; convergent evolution with eels; oral disc structure; keratinous teeth; living fossil morphological stability; agnathan fossil record)

## THE SPECIES -- *LAMPETRA TRIDENTATA*

### Biology of the Pacific Lamprey

- **Scientific name.** *Lampetra tridentata* -- the genus *Lampetra* (from Latin *lambere*, "to lick," and *petra*, "stone" -- "stone licker," referring to the lamprey's attachment behavior) and the species *tridentata* (Latin for "three-toothed," referring to a characteristic dental feature of this species).
- **Size and form.** Adult Pacific Lamprey reach 50-80 cm (20-32 in) in length, occasionally to 90 cm (35 in). They are the largest lamprey species in the Pacific Northwest. The body is cylindrical, elongated, smooth (no scales), and muscular. The dorsal surface ranges from dark gray to olive-brown; the ventral surface is pale. There are two dorsal fins and no paired fins (no pectoral or pelvic fins -- another distinction from jawed fish). The seven circular gill openings along each side of the head (distinctive in lampreys, which lack opercular covers) are a key identification feature.
- **Distribution.** Pacific Lamprey are distributed throughout the Pacific Rim, from southern California north through Oregon, Washington, British Columbia, Alaska, and across the North Pacific to Japan, Korea, and the Russian Far East. In Washington State, they occur in most major river systems -- the Columbia, Snake, Willamette (through Oregon), the coastal rivers of the Olympic Peninsula, and others. Historical abundance was dramatically higher than current levels throughout the range.
- **Taxonomy within Petromyzontidae.** The family Petromyzontidae includes approximately 40 species of lampreys worldwide. Several species occur in the Pacific Northwest:
  - *Lampetra tridentata* (Pacific Lamprey) -- the largest, most widespread, anadromous
  - *Lampetra ayresii* (River Lamprey) -- smaller, more limited range, parasitic
  - *Lampetra richardsoni* (Western Brook Lamprey) -- non-parasitic, resident in streams
  - The Pacific Lamprey (*L. tridentata*) is the species documented here.

(Sources: *Lampetra tridentata* etymology; size and form; coloration; gill opening identification; Pacific Rim distribution; Washington State river systems; Petromyzontidae family overview; PNW lamprey species)

## THE LAMPREY LIFECYCLE -- FIVE PHASES

### The Most Complex Life History in the Engine

The Pacific Lamprey lifecycle is one of the most unusual in the vertebrate world:

### Phase 1: The Egg

Adult Pacific Lamprey spawn in spring (April-June) in clean, shallow gravel streams -- the same habitat used by Pacific Salmon redds. Adults construct nests by moving small rocks with their oral discs, creating depressions in the gravel where eggs are deposited. A single spawning event produces several thousand eggs. Adults die shortly after spawning -- like Pacific Salmon, the Pacific Lamprey is semelparous. The spawning sound: the gentle movement of small rocks by suction, the subtle disturbance of gravel.

### Phase 2: The Ammocoete (Larval Phase)

The most significant and least-known phase of the lamprey's life. Upon hatching, lamprey larvae (called ammocoetes -- a term specific to lamprey larvae) are tiny, blind, toothless filter feeders that immediately burrow into the silty sediment of the stream bed:

- **Duration.** The ammocoete phase lasts 5-7 years in Pacific Lamprey -- the longest larval phase of any Pacific Northwest vertebrate. For 5-7 years, the young lamprey lives buried in the sediment of the stream bed, completely invisible to observers at the surface.
- **Feeding.** Ammocoetes have no eyes (they are vestigial, covered with skin) and no functional teeth. They feed by drawing water and organic matter (bacteria, algae, detritus) through their mouths and filtering it with gill arches -- a completely different feeding mechanism than the adult parasitic stage. The ammocoete is essentially a biological filter pump.
- **Habitat requirements.** Ammocoetes require fine silty sediment (not coarse gravel) in the lower velocity edges of streams -- areas that accumulate organic matter. The introduction of sediment into streams (from logging, road building, agriculture) can paradoxically both harm and help lamprey ammocoetes, depending on the particle size: fine silt is ammocoete habitat; coarse gravel and sand are not.
- **The transformation.** After 5-7 years, the ammocoete undergoes metamorphosis -- a profound physiological transformation that produces the adult body form: eyes develop, the oral disc forms with its rings of keratinous teeth, the body becomes capable of sustaining itself in fast water. The transformation takes approximately 6 months and occurs in late summer and fall.

### Phase 3: The Juvenile / Downstream Migration

Following metamorphosis, juvenile Pacific Lamprey migrate downstream in fall and winter, moving from their natal streams into larger rivers and eventually to the ocean. This downstream migration is passive and active: the juveniles use stream current to assist downstream movement, active only when necessary.

### Phase 4: The Parasitic Marine Phase

Adult Pacific Lamprey spend 1-2 years in the ocean in a parasitic phase:

- **The host relationship.** Pacific Lamprey attach to large fish (salmon, Pacific Halibut, rockfish, and others) using their oral discs. The lamprey's teeth rasp through the host's skin and scale, and the lamprey feeds on the host's blood and body fluids, aided by an anticoagulant secretion that prevents clotting at the wound site.
- **Host survival.** The lamprey-host relationship is parasitic, not predatory: most hosts survive lamprey attachment. The host fish typically carry a distinctive oval wound after the lamprey detaches. Pacific Salmon returning to spawn often carry lamprey wounds.
- **Growth.** The marine parasitic phase is the period of maximum growth. Pacific Lamprey enter the ocean as small juveniles and return as large (50-80 cm) adults. The ocean's rich food supply, accessed through host fish, fuels this rapid growth.
- **Ocean duration.** Approximately 1-2 years at sea, during which the Pacific Lamprey may travel widely through the North Pacific.

### Phase 5: The Spawning Migration

Adult Pacific Lamprey return from the ocean to freshwater rivers in late summer and fall (typically July-October in Pacific Northwest rivers). They move upstream to spawning habitat, stopping to feed on migrating salmon as they travel. By the time they reach spawning grounds, many stop feeding. In spring (April-June), they spawn and die.

## THE ORAL DISC -- THE LAMPREY'S DEFINING STRUCTURE

### The Mouth Without a Jaw

The oral disc is the lamprey's most distinctive anatomical feature and the source of much of its cultural stigma:

- **Structure.** The oral disc is a circular, sucker-like structure surrounding the mouth. It is internally lined with concentric rows of keratinous "teeth" (plates and cusps of a hard keratin-like material). The disc is flexible and can conform to the surface of a host fish or a smooth rock. When a lamprey attaches, it creates a strong vacuum seal between the disc and the substrate -- strong enough to hold the lamprey against strong current or to anchor it to a moving fish.
- **Teeth patterns.** The arrangement and number of teeth (cusps) on the oral disc are taxonomically important: species are distinguished partly by the number and arrangement of their dental cusps. The species name *tridentata* refers to specific dental features. Pacific Lamprey have large, bicuspid lateral teeth and a distinctive supraoral lamina (the structure above the mouth).
- **The sucker sound.** The E=1 "sound" documented in this entry includes the subtle acoustic signature of the oral disc -- the slight hydraulic displacement of water as the disc attaches to or releases from a substrate. In a quiet stream, the sound of a lamprey moving along a rock face with its oral disc is a low, soft adhesion sound -- barely audible, the acoustic minimum of a living vertebrate.
- **Cultural response.** The lamprey's oral disc has generated strong cultural responses. To people unfamiliar with the animal, the disc appears monstrous -- a circular array of teeth surrounding a sucker, attached to an eel-like body. This appearance has contributed to negative cultural associations with lampreys in European and Euro-American tradition. The cultural response to the lamprey is almost the opposite of the cultural response to the salmon -- where salmon are beautiful, culturally beloved, and ecologically celebrated, lampreys are often described as "primitive," "ugly," or "vampire fish."
- **Indigenous perspective.** For Pacific Northwest indigenous peoples, the oral disc and sucker feeding mechanism are not monstrous -- they are simply the anatomy of an important and sacred food species. The same structure that horrifies uninitiated observers is the structure that has made the lamprey a reliable food source for thousands of years of indigenous Pacific Northwest culture.

## FIRST NATIONS AND THE PACIFIC LAMPREY

### A Sacred Species, A Threatened Tradition

For Pacific Northwest indigenous peoples, the Pacific Lamprey is a First Foods species of immense cultural, spiritual, and nutritional importance:

- **First Foods status.** For most tribes in the Columbia River system -- Warm Springs, Yakama, Umatilla, Nez Perce, and others -- Pacific Lamprey are one of the five First Foods: salmon, deer, camas, roots, and lamprey. First Foods are the foods that the Creator placed in the world to sustain the people, and they are treated with ceremonial respect. The First Foods feast, held in spring, includes lamprey alongside salmon as the primary fish food.
- **Nutritional profile.** Pacific Lamprey are nutritionally exceptional: extremely high in fat (the fat is stored throughout the body, not just in specific organs), high in protein, and rich in omega-3 fatty acids. In the traditional indigenous diet, lamprey fat was particularly valued -- both as a food and as a preservative for other foods. Dried and smoked lamprey with its fat intact could be stored for long periods and traded widely.
- **Eel Fishing at Willamette Falls.** Willamette Falls (Oregon City, Oregon) was -- and remains -- one of the most important lamprey fishing sites in the Pacific Northwest. The Clackamas Chinook and other tribes fished for lamprey at the falls with traditional dip nets and basket traps. The lamprey run at Willamette Falls historically produced hundreds of thousands of adult lamprey annually. The falls have now been significantly modified (a paper mill operation affects access), but some tribal lamprey fishing continues.
- **Celilo Falls connection.** Celilo Falls on the Columbia -- the great salmon fishing site destroyed by The Dalles Dam in 1957 (referenced in the Pacific Salmon entry, degree 236) -- was also a major lamprey fishing site. The double loss of Celilo: salmon AND lamprey fishing traditions, a cultural wound that has not healed.
- **Traditional preparation.** Pacific Lamprey are prepared in diverse traditional ways: roasted directly over fire, smoked, dried, rendered for their fat, used in soups and stews. The preparation of lamprey requires skill: the notochord (a stiff rod that functions as a spine) must be removed, and the fat must be properly handled. Traditional processing knowledge was transmitted over generations.
- **Ceremonial importance.** Lamprey are not merely food -- they are the subject of ceremony, prayer, and spiritual relationship. In the Nez Perce tradition, lamprey are a sacred relation. Traditional ecological knowledge about lamprey migration timing, habitat requirements, and population behavior represents thousands of years of observation and is recognized by fish biologists as valuable scientific information.
- **Tribal lamprey programs.** Many Columbia River tribes have established lamprey restoration programs -- both to recover populations for cultural and food use and as part of broader watershed restoration efforts. The Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation, the Yakama Nation, and others have invested in lamprey habitat restoration, juvenile lamprey transport around dams, and genetic monitoring.

(Sources: First Foods doctrine; Columbia River tribes and lamprey; First Foods feast ceremonies; lamprey fat nutritional value; Willamette Falls lamprey fishing; Clackamas Chinook fishing methods; Celilo Falls double loss; traditional preparation methods; Nez Perce spiritual relationship; tribal lamprey restoration programs)

## DECLINE AND CONSERVATION

### One of the Most Severe Declines of Any PNW Species

The Pacific Lamprey's decline is among the most severe of any Pacific Northwest vertebrate:

- **The scale of decline.** Estimates suggest Pacific Lamprey have declined by 90-95% from historical abundance in the Columbia River basin. Historical lamprey runs numbered in the millions of adults. Current counts at some Columbia River dams record only a few thousand passing in years with good conditions. This is not a species that is "somewhat reduced" -- it has been functionally eliminated from much of its historical habitat.
- **Dam barriers.** This is the primary cause of the Pacific Lamprey's decline. Unlike salmon, which are addressed by fish ladders at most Columbia River dams, lampreys cannot effectively use fish ladders designed for salmon. Fish ladders are engineered for salmon swimming behavior -- powerful upstream swimmers that navigate vertical drops by jumping and swimming against current. Lampreys navigate waterfalls differently: they attach to rocky surfaces with their oral discs and "inch" their way up, pausing in any turbulent pocket behind a rock to rest. Fish ladders don't have these resting pockets. The result: most lampreys cannot pass through fish ladders. Each of the more than 400 dams in the Columbia River basin is effectively a near-complete barrier to lamprey migration.
- **Habitat degradation.** Logging and agriculture have degraded the silty sediment habitat that ammocoetes require for their 5-7 year larval development. Without ammocoete habitat, the population has no future. Road building that introduces fine sediment from road surfaces into streams can paradoxically improve or harm ammocoete habitat depending on particle size -- a management challenge without simple solutions.
- **Water quality.** Lampreys have been proposed as sensitive indicators of water quality because the ammocoete's filter-feeding lifestyle concentrates toxins from the water column. Pesticides, herbicides, and industrial chemicals in stream water are filtered through ammocoetes and accumulate. This makes lamprey monitoring a potential early-warning system for stream pollution, but it also means that degraded water quality directly harms lamprey ammocoete survival.
- **Salmon hatcheries and sea lamprey (Petromyzon marinus) confusion.** In the Great Lakes, the invasive Sea Lamprey (*Petromyzon marinus*, a different species) has devastated native fish populations and is subject to active control using lampricides (lamprey-killing chemicals). This history has created a strongly negative cultural framework for lampreys in general that has sometimes contaminated management discussions of the Pacific Lamprey -- a native species that is an important part of PNW ecosystems, not an invasive pest.
- **Recovery efforts.** Tribal programs, NOAA Fisheries listing discussions, dam modification to improve lamprey passage, habitat restoration, and community education. The Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission (CRITFC) has led many lamprey recovery initiatives. Lamprey passage improvement at some dams (shallow-water collector channels that simulate natural rock surfaces) has shown some improvement in lamprey passage rates.

(Sources: 90-95% decline estimate; Columbia River dam count data; fish ladder failure for lamprey; eel-like navigation method vs. salmon ladder design; ammocoete habitat degradation; lamprey as water quality indicator; Sea Lamprey confusion; Great Lakes lampricide history; CRITFC recovery programs; dam modification for lamprey passage)

## E=1 -- THE ACOUSTIC MINIMUM

### What Silence Means in the Engine

The Pacific Lamprey at E=1 ties the engine's absolute minimum energy reading:

- **Previous E=1.** The Harbor Porpoise (degree 187) was the engine's first E=1 reading -- the near-silence of a porpoise at rest on the surface, its quiet exhalation the acoustic minimum of the marine mammal arc. The Pacific Lamprey at E=1 is the second instance of the absolute minimum.
- **The lamprey's acoustic signature.** Lampreys produce essentially no sound through any biological sound-producing mechanism. The E=1 acoustic signature is:
  - The very subtle hydraulic displacement of water as the animal moves
  - The low-frequency vibration of the oral disc attaching to or releasing from substrate
  - During spawning, the gentle movement of small rocks by adult pairs constructing nests
  - The passive passage of water through the lamprey's gills
- **Why E=1 is appropriate.** The lamprey is an organism that evolved in a world before sound was a significant ecological variable for vertebrates. The Paleozoic ocean was not the acoustic environment of the modern ocean -- cetacean sound production, insect calls, bird songs were all in the future. The jawless vertebrates' acoustic world was the near-silent world of moving water and rock. E=1 is the echo of that ancient silence.

This is what the engine is for: documenting the full acoustic and biological range of the Pacific Northwest at every degree.
