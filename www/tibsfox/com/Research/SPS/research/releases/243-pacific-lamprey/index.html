Before Jaws -- The Jawless Vertebrate Lineage

The evolution of jaws is one of the most consequential events in vertebrate history. Jaws enabled predation, defense, and dietary diversity that transformed the animal kingdom. Sharks have jaws. Bony fish have jaws. Amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals — all jawed. The lamprey lineage diverged from the vertebrate family tree before jaws evolved. For 450 million years, lampreys have survived without them.

450 million years ago, Earth was in the Silurian period. No land-dwelling vertebrates existed. What would become North America lay partly in the tropics. The ancestors of modern lampreys were already established in those ancient seas.

The Pacific Lamprey belongs to a lineage so ancient that every other vertebrate in the SPS engine — every mammal, bird, and fish previously documented — belongs to lineages that evolved long after the lamprey family was established. The Salmonidae entered the engine at degree 236: a family approximately 50 million years old. Petromyzontidae is 9 times older.

The Five-Phase Lifecycle

Phase 1

Egg

Adults spawn in spring (April-June) in clean shallow gravel. Oral disc used to move small rocks. Semelparous: adults die after spawning.

Phase 2 — 5-7 YEARS

Ammocoete (Larval)

Blind, toothless larvae burrow into stream sediment and filter-feed on bacteria and algae for 5-7 years. The longest larval phase of any PNW vertebrate. Invisible to surface observers.

Phase 3

Metamorphosis

~6-month transformation: eyes develop, oral disc forms with keratinous teeth, body adapts for fast-water swimming. Summer-fall transition.

Phase 4 — 1-2 YEARS

Marine Parasitic Phase

Attaches to host fish (salmon, halibut) with oral disc. Feeds on blood and body fluids via anticoagulant secretion. Grows from juvenile to 50-80 cm adult.

Phase 5

Spawning Migration

Returns from ocean July-October. Migrates upstream. Spawns April-June. Dies. The cycle repeats.

First Nations: A Sacred Species

FIRST FOODS STATUS — Columbia River Tribes

For the Warm Springs, Yakama, Umatilla, Nez Perce, and other Columbia River tribes, Pacific Lamprey is one of the five First Foods: salmon, deer, camas, roots, and lamprey. First Foods are the species the Creator placed in the world to sustain the people. The lamprey fat is particularly valued — nutritionally dense, used as food and preservative. Willamette Falls and Celilo Falls (destroyed by The Dalles Dam in 1957) were the most important lamprey fishing sites. Lamprey are not merely food; they are a sacred relation, subject to ceremony and protocol.

Conservation: 90-95% Decline

Pacific Lamprey have declined by an estimated 90-95% from historical abundance in the Columbia River basin. Historical runs numbered in the millions. Current dam counts record thousands. Primary cause: fish ladders designed for salmon don't work for lamprey. Lampreys "inch" up rock faces with their oral discs, requiring resting pockets not present in conventional fish ladders. Each of 400+ Columbia River dams is a near-complete barrier. Tribal programs (CRITFC, Yakama Nation, Umatilla) leading recovery efforts. Dam modification for lamprey passage showing limited improvement.
