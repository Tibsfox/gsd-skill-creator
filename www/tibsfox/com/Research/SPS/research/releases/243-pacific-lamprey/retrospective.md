## The Two Hundred Forty-Third Degree: The Ancient

### FIRST PETROMYZONTIDAE. E=1 TIES ABSOLUTE MINIMUM. 450 million years. The jawless vertebrate. An oral disc of keratinous teeth, no jaws, no paired fins, the most ancient body plan among living vertebrates. First Foods of the Columbia River tribes. 90-95% decline. Fish ladders built for salmon that do not work for lampreys. The most consequential conservation failure not caused by habitat loss alone.

---

## What Worked

1. **The 450-million-year frame is the entry's most important structural element.** No other number in the engine contextualizes a species' position in evolutionary history as powerfully as 450 million years. Every other family in the engine is younger by a factor of 5-9. The engine needed to document this lineage fully.
2. **The five-phase lifecycle is the entry's most biologically complex structure.** The ammocoete phase (5-7 years buried in sediment, filter-feeding, blind and toothless) is completely unknown outside fish biology circles. It is genuinely extraordinary: the most significant phase of the lamprey's life is the most invisible.
3. **The First Nations First Foods status is the entry's most important cultural element.** The lamprey is not a culturally marginal species in the PNW -- it is a First Foods sacred relation for multiple Columbia River tribes. The engine documents the full relationship: Willamette Falls, Celilo Falls (the double loss), the nutritional and spiritual significance, the tribal recovery programs.
4. **The fish ladder design failure is the entry's most important conservation insight.** The lamprey declined not (primarily) because of overfishing or habitat loss but because the mitigation infrastructure built for salmon physically cannot be used by lampreys. This is an engineering failure with catastrophic biological consequences.

## Discoveries

1. **The ammocoete phase is the longest larval phase of any PNW vertebrate.** 5-7 years buried in stream sediment. No other PNW species -- not any bird, mammal, or fish -- has a longer larval/juvenile period relative to its total lifespan. The engine had not previously documented anything like this.
2. **The Sea Lamprey (Great Lakes invasive) confusion has harmed Pacific Lamprey conservation.** The invasive *Petromyzon marinus* in the Great Lakes is subject to lampricide programs and has created a strongly negative public framework for "lampreys" generally. The Pacific *Lampetra tridentata* is a native species that is part of a healthy ecosystem, not a pest. Conflating the two has delayed conservation action.
3. **Celilo Falls was both a salmon AND a lamprey fishing site.** The 1957 inundation by The Dalles Dam destroyed both traditions simultaneously. The Pacific Salmon entry (236) documents the Celilo Falls loss for salmon; the Pacific Lamprey entry documents the same loss for lamprey. One dam, one inundation, double cultural destruction.
4. **The oral disc attachment mechanism makes fish ladders useless.** Lampreys navigate waterfalls by attaching to rock faces with their oral discs and "inching" upward, pausing in turbulent pockets to rest. Salmon ladders provide no such resting pockets. This is a design failure that could, in principle, be corrected -- and tribal programs have begun developing lamprey-specific passage solutions.
