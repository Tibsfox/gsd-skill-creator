## E=5 -- THE FLOCK CHORUS

### Why E=5

The Canada Goose registers at E=5 -- one notch below the Trumpeter Swan's E=6 maximum but solidly in the high-energy range:

- **Flock-driven volume:** A single Canada Goose honk is moderate -- loud but not overwhelming. But Canada Geese are almost never alone. A flock of 50-200 geese honking simultaneously produces a **wall of sound** that dominates the acoustic environment. E=5 reflects this collective power: the whole is louder than the sum of its parts.
- **Flight chorus:** The honking V-formation is one of the most recognizable sounds in North American birdlife. A large flock in flight produces a continuous, rhythmic, overlapping chorus of deep honks that can be heard from hundreds of meters away. The sound fills the sky.
- **Aggression calls:** Canada Geese defending territory or nests produce loud, aggressive hissing and honking that can startle and intimidate animals much larger than themselves. The defensive energy is high.
- **Ubiquity multiplier:** The sheer number of Canada Geese in the PNW -- hundreds of thousands of resident birds, augmented by migrants -- means the honking is constant. Parks, lakes, golf courses, playing fields, parking lots. The acoustic presence is inescapable. E=5 reflects not just the volume of a single flock but the cumulative acoustic impact of a species that is everywhere.
- **Why E=5 and not E=6:** E=6 is reserved for maximum individual or collective acoustic power (Trumpeter Swan, Pileated Woodpecker). A Canada Goose flock is loud but does not reach the per-individual amplitude of a Trumpeter bugle. The honk is deep and carrying but does not have the tracheal-resonance power of the Trumpeter. E=5 is correct: loud, commanding, flock-amplified, but one step below maximum.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; engine internal analysis)

## THE DEEP HONK -- ANATOMY AND ACOUSTICS

### A Sound Everyone Knows

The Canada Goose honk is one of the most universally recognized bird sounds in North America:

- **Character:** Deep, resonant, two-syllable honk -- often transcribed as "ah-HONK" or "ka-RONK." The second syllable is louder and more emphatic. The sound is nasal, brassy, and carries well over distance. Each individual honk is distinct enough that geese can recognize each other by voice.
- **Frequency range:** 200-2,500 Hz. The fundamental is in the low range (200-500 Hz), with harmonics extending upward. The depth of the honk is proportional to body size -- larger subspecies produce deeper honks, smaller subspecies produce higher-pitched honks.
- **Production mechanism:** Air forced through the syrinx (the avian vocal organ, located at the junction of the trachea and bronchi). The Canada Goose's syrinx is relatively simple compared to passerines (songbirds) but is powered by a large body and substantial lung capacity. The result is a loud, resonant, somewhat unmusical but deeply characteristic sound.
- **Flock chorus structure:** In a flock, honks overlap asynchronously -- each bird honks at its own rate, creating a continuous, rhythmic but non-synchronized chorus. The effect is a **textured wall of honking** rather than a coordinated unison. This is distinct from the Tundra Swan's baying (similar asynchronous overlap) and the Trumpeter Swan's bugling (more synchronized pair duets). The goose flock chorus has a rougher, more urgent quality.
- **V-formation calls:** Geese in V-formation honk frequently, often in response to the bird ahead. The acoustic pattern of a passing V-formation is a Doppler-shifted sweep of honking -- approaching, overhead, receding -- that is one of the iconic sounds of migration (even though many PNW Canada Geese are year-round residents).

### Individual Recognition

Canada Geese can **recognize individual flock members by voice**. Research has demonstrated that:

- **Mate recognition:** Paired geese recognize each other's honks and respond differentially to mate calls vs. stranger calls.
- **Family recognition:** Goslings learn their parents' voices within the first days of life and can distinguish parental honks from other adults.
- **Dominance signaling:** The honk encodes information about the caller's size and dominance status. Larger geese produce deeper, louder honks. Other geese use this information to assess potential adversaries without physical confrontation.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2002)

## RESIDENT VS. MIGRATORY -- THE DUAL POPULATION

### The PNW's Year-Round Goose Problem

The Canada Goose in the Pacific Northwest exists in two distinct population types:

**Resident (non-migratory) populations:**
- **Origin:** Most PNW resident Canada Geese are descendants of birds introduced or transplanted by wildlife agencies in the mid-20th century. The original intent was to establish huntable populations. The birds were transplanted from migratory stocks but, raised in the mild PNW climate, lost the migratory impulse.
- **Numbers:** Resident populations have grown explosively. Washington State's resident Canada Goose population is estimated at 100,000-200,000+ birds. Oregon's is similar in scale. The birds thrive in urban and suburban environments.
- **Habitat:** Parks, golf courses, corporate campuses, playing fields, lakeshores, agricultural fields. Any landscape with short grass near water is ideal Canada Goose habitat. The PNW's abundance of rain-watered lawns and ponds creates nearly unlimited habitat.
- **Year-round presence:** Resident geese do not migrate. They remain in the same area year-round, breeding in spring, raising goslings in summer, and forming large flocks in fall/winter. The honking is a 365-day phenomenon.

**Migratory populations:**
- **Origin:** Migratory Canada Geese breed in Alaska, northern BC, and the Canadian prairies and winter in the PNW lowlands. These are the "classic" migrating geese -- the V-formations that mark the seasons.
- **Timing:** Fall migration peaks October-November. Spring migration peaks March-April. During migration and winter, migratory birds mix with resident birds in agricultural fields and wetlands.
- **Numbers:** Migratory populations are smaller in the PNW than resident populations (the reverse of the historical situation). The V-formation flocks that pass overhead are a fraction of the resident birds on the ground.

### The Urban Adaptation Story

The Canada Goose's success in urban environments is one of the most remarkable examples of wildlife adaptation in North America:

- **Behavioral plasticity:** Canada Geese have learned to exploit human-modified landscapes. Mowed lawns provide preferred forage (short grass with good sight lines for predator detection). Artificial ponds provide year-round water. Low-disturbance environments (parks, corporate campuses) provide safe nesting and resting areas.
- **Loss of wariness:** Urban geese have lost much of the wariness of humans that characterizes wild/migratory populations. They tolerate close approach, nest on building rooftops and in planters, and aggressively defend territory against joggers, cyclists, and dogs.
- **Overgrazing:** Large resident flocks can denude grass areas, creating muddy, goose-dropping-covered landscapes. The droppings are copious (an adult Canada Goose produces approximately 500 grams of droppings per day) and create sanitation concerns in parks and playing fields.
- **Population management challenges:** The success of resident Canada Geese has created management conflicts. Methods include egg addling (oiling eggs to prevent hatching), hazing (using dogs, pyrotechnics, or lasers to move geese), habitat modification (allowing grass to grow tall near water to reduce attractiveness), and in some cases lethal removal.

The Canada Goose is, in some sense, a **victim of its own success** -- so well-adapted to human-modified landscapes that it has become a nuisance in the very environments that made its population explosion possible.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2002; WDFW Canada Goose management)

## THE V-FORMATION -- AERODYNAMICS AND ACOUSTICS

### Why V?

The Canada Goose's V-formation flight is one of the most studied phenomena in avian biology:

- **Aerodynamic advantage:** Each bird (except the leader) flies in the upwash zone of the bird ahead. The upwash is the upward-moving air generated by the wingtip vortex of the preceding bird's downstroke. Flying in the upwash zone reduces the energy required for flight by an estimated **10-14%** compared to solo flight. The V-formation is an energy-saving strategy that allows geese to cover greater distances with less fatigue.
- **Leader rotation:** The lead position generates no aerodynamic benefit (no bird ahead to provide upwash). Geese rotate the lead position, with the leader dropping back to a trailing position when fatigued. This cooperative behavior distributes the cost of leadership across the flock.
- **Visual contact:** The V-formation keeps all birds in visual contact with each other. Each bird can see the bird ahead and to the side, maintaining formation integrity.
- **Acoustic coordination:** The honking during V-formation flight serves coordination functions: maintaining contact, signaling position changes, and (possibly) encouraging continued flight. The honking increases during formation adjustments and decreases during stable cruise.

### The Sound of Migration

The acoustic experience of a Canada Goose V-formation passing overhead:

- **Approach:** Deep honking grows louder. The overlapping honks create a rhythmic, complex texture. Individual voices can be distinguished in the collective.
- **Overhead:** Maximum volume. The wingbeat rhythm is audible at close range -- a deep, rhythmic whooshing that accompanies the honking. The sky fills with sound.
- **Receding:** The honking fades gradually. The Doppler shift lowers the perceived pitch slightly as the formation recedes. The sound is one of the most evocative in the natural world -- the sound of seasons changing.

For many PNW residents, the honking V-formation is the first wild bird sound they learn to identify. It is the acoustic signature of the Canada Goose even for people who never think about birds.

(Sources: Cutts & Speakman 1994; Portugal et al. 2014; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## TAXONOMY AND SUBSPECIES

### A Complex of Sizes

The Canada Goose is one of the most variable species in North American ornithology, with **seven recognized subspecies** ranging enormously in size:

- **B. c. maxima** (Giant Canada Goose): The largest subspecies. Males up to 6.5 kg (14.3 lbs). The subspecies most commonly established as resident populations in the PNW lowlands. Once thought extinct but rediscovered in the 1960s.
- **B. c. moffitti** (Moffitt's Canada Goose): The primary breeding subspecies in the western US, including the PNW. Intermediate size. The most common wild-breeding Canada Goose in Washington and Oregon.
- **B. c. occidentalis** (Dusky Canada Goose): Breeds on the Copper River Delta in Alaska, winters exclusively in the Willamette Valley of Oregon. One of the most range-restricted waterfowl populations in North America. Conservation concern.
- **B. c. parvipes** (Lesser Canada Goose): Smaller subspecies. Breeds in interior Alaska and northwestern Canada. Some winter in the PNW.
- **B. c. canadensis** (Atlantic Canada Goose): The nominate subspecies. Eastern North America. Not typically in the PNW.
- **B. c. interior** (Interior Canada Goose): Central North America. Occasionally recorded in the PNW.
- **B. c. fulva** (Vancouver Canada Goose): Resident coastal subspecies of BC and southeastern Alaska. Limited PNW presence.

### The 2004 Split

In 2004, the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU) split the four smallest forms from the Canada Goose complex, elevating them to a separate species: the **Cackling Goose** (*Branta hutchinsii*). This split is directly relevant to degree 130, where the Cackling Goose will be documented as a comparison pair.

The split was based on:
- **Size:** A distinct size gap between the smallest "Canada Geese" and the largest "Canada Geese" -- the small forms are barely larger than a Mallard, while the large forms are among the heaviest flying birds in North America.
- **Genetics:** Molecular studies confirmed that the small forms are a distinct lineage, not just small versions of the large forms.
- **Ecology:** The small forms breed in different habitats (high Arctic tundra) and have different migratory patterns than the large forms.

(Sources: Mowbray et al. 2002; Banks et al. 2004 AOU split decision; Cornell Lab of Ornithology)

## ECOLOGICAL CONTEXT: THE URBAN DOMINANT

### The Goose's Year

The Canada Goose's annual cycle in the PNW:

- **Spring (March-May):** Pairs establish territories. Nesting begins. The female selects the nest site (usually near water, elevated, with good visibility). Clutch of 2-8 eggs (typically 5-6). Incubation 25-28 days by the female; the male guards. Aggressive territorial defense -- hissing, honking, wing-spreading, physical attacks on intruders.
- **Summer (June-August):** Goslings hatch and are led to water within 24 hours. Family groups forage together. Adults undergo a simultaneous wing molt (flightless for approximately 3-4 weeks) timed to coincide with gosling rearing. Large molting aggregations form on safe water bodies.
- **Fall (September-November):** Families join large flocks. Migratory populations depart breeding grounds. V-formations pass through the PNW. Flock honking intensifies.
- **Winter (December-February):** Large flocks on agricultural fields, parks, and wetlands. Resident and migratory birds mix. The Skagit Valley, Ridgefield NWR, and Willamette Valley host major concentrations.

### Predator Relationships

Despite their size and aggression, Canada Geese have predators:

- **Eggs:** Crows, ravens, gulls, raccoons, foxes, coyotes all prey on eggs. Nest predation is the primary cause of reproductive failure.
- **Goslings:** Bald Eagles, Great Horned Owls, coyotes, foxes, snapping turtles, large fish (northern pike). Goslings are particularly vulnerable in the first 2-3 weeks.
- **Adults:** Bald Eagles are the primary avian predator of adult Canada Geese in the PNW. Coyotes and bobcats take adults occasionally, particularly molting (flightless) birds.

The urban environment reduces predation pressure dramatically, which is one of the key factors in the explosive growth of resident populations. Parks and corporate campuses are predator-poor environments.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2002)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound That Defines Waterfowl

The Canada Goose honk is the default "bird sound" that most North Americans associate with waterfowl:

- **The honk spectrum:** Deep, nasal, brassy. The two-syllable "ah-HONK" structure with emphasis on the second syllable. Frequency 200-2,500 Hz with most energy in the 300-1,000 Hz band. Loud enough to be heard at 500+ meters from a single bird; a flock is audible at 1-2 km.
- **The flock texture:** Asynchronous overlapping honks create a continuous, complex texture. Unlike the coordinated dawn chorus of passerines, the goose flock chorus is uncoordinated but continuous -- a wall of honking that fills the acoustic space.
- **The aggression sound:** Hissing and low, guttural honking during territorial defense. The hiss is distinctive -- a surprisingly loud, menacing sound from a large, aggressive bird at close range.
- **The gosling sound:** High-pitched, peeping, whistling calls from goslings. The contrast between the deep adult honk and the high gosling peep is dramatic -- the voice drops as the bird grows, tracking body size.
- **The alarm call:** A sharp, rapid series of honks given when a predator (or dog, or jogger) approaches. The alarm spreads through the flock rapidly, with birds picking up the honking and beginning to move toward water.

### Soundscape Dominance

In many PNW urban environments, the Canada Goose is the **acoustically dominant bird species**:

- **Parks:** Green Lake in Seattle, Greenlake Park in Portland, any urban lake with surrounding grass -- the goose honking is the dominant sound.
- **Golf courses:** The early-morning flock chorus on a golf course can be overwhelming. Dozens to hundreds of geese honking, feeding, and squabbling.
- **Agricultural fields:** The Skagit Valley in winter: thousands of geese in fields, the collective honking creating a low, continuous drone punctuated by louder individual calls.

The Canada Goose's acoustic dominance in urban environments parallels its ecological dominance. It is the most visible, most audible, and most numerous large bird in many PNW landscapes.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Mowbray et al. 2002; engine internal analysis)
