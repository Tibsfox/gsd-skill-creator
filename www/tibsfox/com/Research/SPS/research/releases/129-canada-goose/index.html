Canada Goose

Branta canadensis (Anatidae) — E=5 — 12th Consecutive Anatidae — RECORD EXTENDS
deep honk flock chorus — E=5 flock-amplified power. The most ubiquitous waterfowl in North America. V-formation honking is one of the most recognizable bird sounds on the continent. First Branta (goose) genus in the engine — genus shift from swans (127–128) to geese (129+). Twelfth consecutive Anatidae (118–129). Resident + migratory. Urban dominant. Begins goose comparison pair with Cackling Goose (130).

Deep Honk Flock Chorus — E=5

The two-syllable “ah-HONK” at 200–2,500 Hz. Nasal, brassy, carrying. A single honk is moderate — but Canada Geese are almost never alone. A flock of 50–200 birds honking asynchronously creates a wall of sound that dominates the acoustic environment. E=5 through collective power. Individual recognition by voice — mates and family members distinguish each other’s calls. The most universally recognized waterfowl sound in North America.

Urban Adaptation — The Ubiquitous Giant

Resident (non-migratory) populations have exploded in the PNW — estimated 100,000–200,000+ in Washington State alone. Descendants of mid-20th century transplants, these geese thrive in parks, golf courses, corporate campuses, and any landscape with short grass near water. They’ve lost wariness of humans, aggressively defend territory, and produce ~500 grams of droppings per bird per day. The most visible, most audible, most numerous large bird in many PNW landscapes. A species so successful it has become a management challenge.

V-Formation Flight — Aerodynamics and Sound

Each trailing bird flies in the upwash zone of the bird ahead, saving 10–14% flight energy. Leaders rotate to distribute fatigue. The honking V-formation passing overhead is the iconic sound of migration: approaching, maximum overhead, receding with Doppler shift. Even non-birders recognize it. For many PNW residents, the V-formation honk is the first wild bird sound they learn to identify.

Twelfth Consecutive Anatidae (118–129)

The streak enters goose territory. Genus shift from Cygnus (swans, 127–128) to Branta (black geese, 129). The energy descends from the Trumpeter’s E=6 climax to E=5 — still high. The streak now covers all three major Anatidae subfamilies in the PNW: ducks (118–126), swans (127–128), geese (129+). Twelve consecutive and counting.

Resident vs. Migratory Populations

Resident birds (descendants of mid-20th century transplants) stay year-round, breed in parks and lakeshores, and form the large urban flocks. Migratory birds breed in Alaska and northern Canada, pass through in V-formations October–November and March–April. In winter, both populations mix in agricultural fields and wetlands — the Skagit Valley and Ridgefield NWR host major concentrations.

Taxonomy — The 2004 Split

In 2004, the smallest four subspecies were split from Canada Goose as the Cackling Goose (Branta hutchinsii, degree 130). The split was based on a size gap, genetic divergence, and ecological differences. The Canada/Cackling pair mirrors other comparison pairs in the engine: Hairy/Downy Woodpecker (110–111), Barrow’s/Common Goldeneye (123–124), Tundra/Trumpeter Swan (127–128). Degree 130 will complete the pair.
