SPS Metal Summit Opens — Full-Spectrum Maximum

A Pacific winter storm on Washington's outer coast does not produce a single sound — it activates every acoustic domain simultaneously. Infrasound from ocean swell compression (0.1 Hz). Storm wind roar (20-100 Hz). Surf pound (100-500 Hz). Rain impact (1,000-5,000 Hz). Spray transients (5,000-15,000+ Hz). Atmospheric pressure pulse (0.01 Hz). Six domains, zero silence in any frequency range, all at maximum simultaneous activation. E=8 through breadth, not through any single maximum.

The Outer Coast Setting

Washington's outer coast — the Pacific-facing shore of the Olympic Peninsula from Cape Flattery south to Grays Harbor — is the first continental landfall for North Pacific storm systems. The fetch from the outer coast to the nearest upwind landmass is 3,000-5,000 miles, producing some of the largest swells that strike any populated coast in the Pacific Basin. The rocky headlands (Cape Flattery, Cape Alava, Point Grenville) amplify every acoustic domain: unlike sandy beaches that absorb wave energy gradually, rock concentrates and redirects it explosively.

The Olympic National Park coastal unit protects a significant portion of this coast from development, maintaining the full-spectrum storm acoustic event as a pristine natural occurrence — not masked by roads, buildings, or machinery. When a storm arrives here, it is heard in full.

E=8 — Maximum Breadth, Not Maximum Amplitude

E=8 rather than E=9 reflects the storm's character: it is not the maximum of any single acoustic domain, but the maximum simultaneous engagement of all domains. A deeper cyclone could produce louder wind; larger swells could produce heavier surf. What the outer coast winter storm produces reliably and at maximum expression is the full-spectrum simultaneous activation — nothing quiet, everything engaged. E=8 is the maximum of breadth.
