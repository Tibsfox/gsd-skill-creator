## THE PACIFIC STORM SYSTEM -- HOW A WINTER STORM DEVELOPS

### The Atmospheric Engine That Produces Full-Spectrum Sound

A Pacific winter storm on Washington's outer coast is not a local event -- it is the terminal expression of a planetary atmospheric system:

- **The North Pacific storm track.** The North Pacific Ocean generates some of the most powerful extratropical cyclones on Earth, driven by the temperature gradient between the cold polar air mass and the warm subtropical Pacific. Between October and March, this storm track directs a succession of deep low-pressure systems toward the Pacific Northwest coast. Washington State's outer coast -- from Cape Flattery south to the Grays Harbor area -- is the first continental landfall for these systems.
- **The Olympic Peninsula as landfall.** Washington's Olympic Peninsula juts into the Pacific, creating a geographic focusing mechanism for incoming systems. Cape Flattery (the northwesternmost point of the contiguous United States, 48.4°N, 124.7°W) and the headlands south of it -- Cape Alava, Point Grenville, Destruction Island -- are the most exposed points on the Washington coast, receiving the full energy of incoming Pacific storms before the barrier effect of the Olympic Mountains redistributes the wind and moisture inland.
- **Storm development metrics.** A strong Pacific winter storm (central pressure 950-970 mb, sustained winds 50-70+ mph at coast) is classified as a "bomb cyclone" when it deepens at more than 24 mb in 24 hours (explosive cyclogenesis). These events occur multiple times per year on the North Pacific. When the storm reaches the Washington coast, it brings simultaneous maximum expression of all its acoustic components: infrasound, low-frequency wind, rain, surf, and spray.
- **The winter seasonal concentration.** Peak storm season for the Washington outer coast is November-March. During these months, the sea surface temperature gradient is maximum, the polar vortex is most displaced toward the Pacific, and the storm track is most directly aimed at Washington's coast. The storms that arrive in this window are the strongest of the year and produce the most complete full-spectrum acoustic activation.

## THE FULL SPECTRUM -- SIX SIMULTANEOUS ACOUSTIC DOMAINS

### What "All Frequencies Simultaneous" Means

The Pacific storm is a full-spectrum acoustic event because it activates six distinct acoustic domains simultaneously:

- **Domain 1: Infrasound (0.1-20 Hz) -- Ocean swell.** Ocean swells generated by the storm (or preceding storms thousands of miles away) arrive at the coast as long-period waves (12-25 second periods). The interaction of these swells with the coast -- the compression of water against cliffs, the explosion of water into sea caves, the resonance of surge channels -- produces infrasound at 0.1-1 Hz. This is below the threshold of normal hearing but detectable as pressure variation in the chest and abdomen. The infrasound of a major ocean swell is felt, not heard. It is the lowest layer of the storm's acoustic expression.
- **Domain 2: Low-frequency wind (20-100 Hz) -- Storm roar.** Wind at sustained speeds of 50-80 mph generates low-frequency acoustic energy as it flows over and around obstacles -- headlands, rocks, sea stacks, vegetation. The "roar" of a major storm is primarily in the 20-100 Hz range: a broadband low-frequency sound with enormous dynamic range (90-110 dB at exposed coastal positions during peak gusts). This is the sound that registers as physically oppressive in high wind events.
- **Domain 3: Mid-frequency surf (100-500 Hz) -- Wave break and surge.** The breaking of large ocean waves is the most energetic acoustic event in the coastal environment. A wave breaking on a rocky headland produces 95-115 dB in the 100-500 Hz range, with the primary energy in the wave-break impact (the leading edge compression) and the surge (the following water movement over rock and through crevices). In a major storm, waves with periods of 12-18 seconds and heights of 20-40 feet arrive continuously. The surf zone is never silent between waves during a major storm; the overlapping surge and retreat of successive waves creates a continuous 100-500 Hz roar.
- **Domain 4: Mid-high frequency rain (1,000-5,000 Hz) -- Impact and accumulation.** Heavy rain on an outer coast storm (1-3+ inches per hour during intense events) produces acoustic energy across the 1,000-5,000 Hz range as individual rain drops impact rock, water, and vegetation surfaces. The overlapping impact patterns produce a dense continuous mid-high frequency hiss that is specific in timbre to the precipitation rate: heavy rain is clearly distinguishable from light rain by its acoustic signature. On an exposed headland during peak storm, the rain component contributes a high-amplitude layer to the mid-high frequency range.
- **Domain 5: High-frequency spray and surge (5,000-15,000+ Hz) -- The air-water boundary.** When large waves impact cliffs and headlands, the collision between water and rock produces explosive spray events -- water droplets and mist launched into the air at high velocity, producing broadband high-frequency sound (5,000-15,000+ Hz) from the droplet impacts and the air turbulence at the water's surface. The spray sound from a major wave impact is a sharp high-amplitude transient in the high-frequency range, distinctly different from the sustained low-frequency roar of the wind and the mid-frequency rumble of the surf.
- **Domain 6: Very low frequency storm pressure pulses (0.01-0.1 Hz) -- Atmospheric infrasound.** The storm system itself -- the low-pressure center, the pressure gradients driving the wind -- produces very low frequency atmospheric pressure variations at periods of 30 seconds to several minutes. This is below the infrasound of ocean swells and operates at a time scale that is closer to the barometric experience (the pressure drop associated with storm arrival) than to acoustic hearing. This domain is the storm's atmospheric signature, registered as the body's sense of pressure change rather than as sound.

## THE OUTER COAST SETTING -- WHERE THE STORM MEETS LAND

### The Acoustic Environment of Washington's Pacific Shore

The Washington outer coast is the correct setting for the full-spectrum storm event:

- **Exposure.** Washington's outer coast (the Pacific-facing shore of the Olympic Peninsula and the Grays Harbor/North Beach area) is fully exposed to the North Pacific fetch -- the distance over open water across which waves and wind have been building. The fetch from the outer Washington coast to the nearest upwind landmass (Siberia or the Aleutian chain, depending on storm direction) is 3,000-5,000 miles. This fetch produces some of the largest waves that strike any populated coast in the Pacific Basin.
- **The rocky headlands.** The outer coast alternates between sandy beaches (the "coast strip" south of Kalaloch) and rocky headlands and sea stacks. The rock sections -- Cape Flattery, the Ozette area, the Point Grenville headland -- are where the full-spectrum storm acoustic event is most complete. Sandy beaches absorb wave energy more gradually; rocky headlands reflect, concentrate, and explosively redirect the wave energy, maximizing the acoustic output across all domains.
- **Olympic National Park coastal zone.** A significant portion of the Washington outer coast is within Olympic National Park's coastal unit -- one of the most remote and pristine coastlines in the contiguous United States. The lack of development on this coast means that the storm's acoustic event is not masked by human-generated noise. The full-spectrum storm on the outer Olympic coast is as pure a natural acoustic event as exists in the Pacific Northwest.
- **The Hoh and Queets river estuaries.** Where major rivers meet the outer coast -- the Hoh, the Queets, the Quinault -- the storm's acoustic character adds an estuary dimension: the wave and surge sound of the open coast combined with the river current sounds immediately upstream. During major storms, the wave surge penetrates the estuary mouth, creating hydraulic events (surge waves moving up the river channel) that produce additional infrasonic and low-frequency acoustic output.

## SYNTHESIS -- HOW THE SIX DOMAINS COMBINE

### The Full-Spectrum Experience

The synthesis of six simultaneous acoustic domains is what makes the Pacific storm a full-spectrum event rather than simply a loud event:

- **The layering.** At any moment during a major storm on an exposed headland, all six domains are active: the atmospheric pressure pulse (0.01-0.1 Hz) in the body, the ocean swell infrasound (0.1-1 Hz) in the chest, the wind roar (20-100 Hz) as physical force, the surf pound (100-500 Hz) as rhythmic impact, the rain hiss (1,000-5,000 Hz) as continuous texture, and the spray transients (5,000-15,000+ Hz) as sharp high-frequency events. The acoustic spectrum from 0.01 Hz to 15,000+ Hz is simultaneously occupied.
- **The dynamic range.** The storm's dynamic range is extreme: the atmospheric pressure pulse varies over tens of seconds; the surf rhythm repeats every 12-18 seconds; the rain is continuous; the spray transients are millisecond events. The different time scales of these acoustic domains create a temporal complexity (all happening simultaneously, each at its own time scale) that is the storm's acoustic signature.
