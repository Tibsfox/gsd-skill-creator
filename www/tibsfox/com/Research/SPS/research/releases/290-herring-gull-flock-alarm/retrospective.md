## The Two Hundred Ninetieth Degree: The Winter Alarm

### Second Laridae. *Larus argentatus*. The archetypal seagull of popular imagination. E=7 -- triggered flock alarm, not colony ambient. Winter beach and harbor. Pacific Northwest coast and Puget Sound. Salmon carcass foraging at river mouths. The alarm event: one bird's call triggers a cascade through dozens of birds in seconds, producing a wall of overlapping raucous screams. Named argentatus (silvery) by Pontoppidan, 1763.

## What Worked

1. **The triggered alarm vs. ambient chorus distinction is the key insight for this degree.** The Laridae arc needed an entry that showed a different acoustic mode than the colonial chorus. The Herring Gull flock alarm provides exactly this: it is event-driven, sudden, brief, and intense rather than continuous, emergent, and sustained. The arc's acoustic vocabulary expands at 290.
2. **E=6→7 transition is correctly calibrated.** The flock alarm is higher intensity than the colony ambient per unit time because it mobilizes maximum vocal output from all birds simultaneously. The E step is justified.
3. **Winter ecology is a genuine seasonal expansion of the arc.** Three of the four Laridae entries (289, 291, 292) are summer breeding contexts. The 290 winter entry documents the family's year-round PNW ecological range. The arc is not only about breeding season -- it is about the family in all its ecological contexts.
4. **Salmon carcass foraging is the most PNW-specific element of Herring Gull winter ecology.** The connection between Herring Gull winter flock behavior and the Pacific salmon spawning cycle is a genuinely Pacific Northwest ecological link -- the salmonid-seabird connection that runs through multiple entries in the SPS engine.

## Discoveries

1. **The Herring Gull complex taxonomy is underappreciated.** American Herring Gull smithsonianus is now sometimes treated as a species separate from European Herring Gull. The PNW winter birds are the American subspecies. The complex illustrates Laridae's general taxonomic fluidity -- hybridization, clinal variation, and subspecific complexity are characteristic of the family.
2. **Mixed-species winter gull flock composition is important natural history.** The Pacific Northwest winter gull flock is not single-species. Sorting Herring from Glaucous-winged, Thayer's/Iceland, Mew, and Ring-billed gulls in a winter flock is a demanding exercise. The alarm events at these mixed flocks involve all species simultaneously -- a true multi-species acoustic event.
