Acoustic Profile — The Flock Alarm Event

Trigger: Raptor overflight (Bald Eagle, Peregrine Falcon), sudden human approach, dog charge. One bird's alarm triggers adjacent birds in seconds.

Alarm call: "kyow-kyow-kyow" or "keeow-keeow-keeow" — harsh, sharp, emphatic. Higher-pitched than contact mewing. Not musical; optimized for rapid information propagation through flock.

Flock overlay: Dozens to hundreds of birds calling simultaneously. Overlapping alarm calls create wall of sound. Total acoustic energy significantly higher than any individual call.

Duration: Brief and intense — 30 seconds to a few minutes. E=7: triggered alarm event, higher intensity than colonial ambient (E=6), shorter duration.

Colony to Flock — A Different Acoustic Mode

The Laridae arc opened at degree 289 with the sustained ambient colonial chorus of the Glaucous-winged Gull (E=6). Degree 290 introduces a fundamentally different acoustic mode: the triggered flock alarm. Where the colonial chorus is continuous and emergent — always present, arising from biological activity — the flock alarm is sudden, reactive, event-driven. The flock is at rest; the threat appears; the alarm erupts.

This is why E rises from 6 to 7: triggered alarm events mobilize maximum vocal output from all birds simultaneously in a brief window. The alarm is more intense per unit time than the colony ambient, even though the colony ambient is more sustained.

Winter Beach Ecology

The Herring Gull is primarily a winter visitor to the Pacific Northwest, arriving from Canadian and Alaskan breeding grounds to winter along the coast and in Puget Sound. Winter gull flocks occupy fishing harbors, river mouths, exposed beaches, and tidal roost sites. At river mouths, Herring Gulls gather in large numbers to exploit Pacific salmon carcasses after the spawning runs — one of the most ecologically significant winter feeding behaviors in the PNW waterbird community.

Winter beach and harbor flocks in the PNW are mixed-species: Herring Gulls aggregate alongside Glaucous-winged Gulls, Mew Gulls, Ring-billed Gulls, and occasional rarer species. Working through a mixed winter gull flock is one of Pacific Northwest birding's more demanding pleasures. The Herring Gull is recognizable by its black wingtips (contrasting with the Glaucous-winged's gray) and yellow orbital ring (vs. pink-red in Glaucous-winged).

Larus argentatus — The Archetypal Gull

The Herring Gull is the bird most people visualize when they say "seagull" — the large white-headed, black-wingtipped gull of English seaside towns, American fishing harbors, and parking lot fast food contexts. Named by Erik Pontoppidan in 1763 (*argentatus*: silvery, for the gray back), the species is one of the most studied birds in the world. The Herring Gull complex is taxonomically complicated — the American Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus smithsonianus*) wintering in the PNW was long lumped with European populations now sometimes treated as separate species.
