## SECOND LARIDAE -- THE ARC CONTINUES

### From Colony to Flock: A Different Acoustic Mode

Degree 290 continues the Laridae arc with a fundamentally different acoustic context than degree 289:

- **289: Colony soundscape.** The Glaucous-winged Gull colony (289) was an ambient acoustic environment -- continuous, multi-layered, emerging from the biological activity of hundreds of birds at a breeding site. The colony sound is sustained, present, not driven by any single event.
- **290: Flock alarm.** The Herring Gull flock alarm is a triggered event: the flock is at rest (foraging, loafing, roosting) and an alarm stimulus causes sudden, explosive, overlapping vocal response from multiple birds simultaneously. The alarm is not ambient -- it is a specific acoustic event with a beginning, peak intensity, and gradual de-escalation.
- **E=6 to E=7.** The transition from the colony ambient (E=6) to the flock alarm (E=7) reflects this acoustic shift: triggered alarm events are higher intensity than ambient colonial chorus because they mobilize maximum vocal output from multiple birds simultaneously in a brief window. The alarm is more intense; the colony is more sustained.
- **Winter vs. summer.** The Glaucous-winged colony is a summer phenomenon (breeding season). The Herring Gull winter flock is the opposite season: the largest gull aggregations in the Pacific Northwest occur during winter when post-breeding dispersal and migration bring maximum numbers to coastal and harbor sites.
- **Beach and harbor ecology.** The winter flock habitat is the accessible, often urban waterfront -- not the remote rocky islands of the breeding colony. Herring Gulls in winter occupy fishing harbors, river mouths, beaches, garbage disposal sites, and parking lots adjacent to water. The alarm event in this context is triggered by human disturbance, raptor overflights, or sudden changes in the flock's environment.

## LARUS ARGENTATUS -- THE SPECIES

### The Archetypal Gull of Popular Imagination

*Larus argentatus* (Pontoppidan, 1763) is the most studied, most recognizable large gull in the world:

- **Identification.** A large gull (wingspan 54-58 inches / 137-147 cm), very similar to the Glaucous-winged Gull in size. Adults in breeding plumage have a white head and underparts, gray back and upperwing, and black wingtips with white spots (mirrors) -- the classic large white-headed gull pattern. Bill yellow with red gonydeal spot. Eye yellow with yellow orbital ring. Pink legs. The black wingtips are the key distinction from the Glaucous-winged Gull's gray wingtips.
- **Scientific name.** *Larus argentatus*. *Larus* (Latin: seagull); *argentatus* (Latin: silvery, from *argentum*, silver) -- referring to the silvery-gray back and upperwing. Named by Erik Pontoppidan (1763), Danish bishop and naturalist.
- **Global distribution.** The Herring Gull is one of the most widespread gull species in the Northern Hemisphere: breeding across North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. In North America, the species breeds across Canada, Alaska, and the northeastern United States. It is the dominant large gull in eastern North America and northern Europe.
- **Pacific Northwest status.** In the Pacific Northwest, the Herring Gull is primarily a winter visitor. The species breeds in Alaska and Canada but winters along the Pacific coast from Alaska south through Washington, Oregon, and California. In Puget Sound and the Salish Sea, Herring Gulls are common winter birds in fishing harbors, along beaches, and at river mouths.
- **Taxonomy note.** The Herring Gull complex is taxonomically complicated. The American Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus smithsonianus*) is the subspecies that winters in the Pacific Northwest. European and other populations are now sometimes treated as separate species. The complex taxonomy reflects the Laridae family's general tendency toward hybridization and clinal variation.
- **The 'seagull' of popular culture.** The Herring Gull is the species that most people visualize when they say "seagull" -- the large, white-headed, black-wingtipped gull of English seaside towns, American fishing harbors, and parking lots adjacent to fast food restaurants. The cultural image of the gull is built primarily on this species.

(Sources: Larus argentatus identification; Erik Pontoppidan 1763; argentatus name silver; global distribution; North America breeding range; Pacific Northwest winter visitor; American Herring Gull smithsonianus; Herring Gull complex taxonomy; cultural seagull image)

## THE FLOCK ALARM -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### The Sound of Sudden Disturbance

The Herring Gull flock alarm is a specific acoustic event produced by a specific behavioral trigger:

- **The trigger.** A winter gull flock at a beach or harbor is suddenly disturbed: a Bald Eagle or Peregrine Falcon stoops overhead, a human approaches too quickly, a dog charges the flock. The birds nearest the threat are the first to alarm; their calls trigger adjacent birds in a rapid cascade. Within seconds, the entire flock is calling simultaneously.
- **The alarm call.** The Herring Gull's alarm call is a loud, harsh, repeated "kyow-kyow-kyow" or "keeow-keeow-keeow" -- higher pitched and more emphatic than the contact mewing call, less melodic, more abrupt. The alarm is not musical; it is functional, optimized for carrying information rapidly through the flock about the nature and direction of the threat.
- **Overlapping simultaneous calls.** In a flock of dozens or hundreds of birds, the alarm propagates in a wave but all birds reach alarm state within a few seconds. For the duration of the alarm event, many birds are calling simultaneously -- the overlapping of individual alarm calls creates a wall of sound qualitatively different from any single bird's call.
- **Frequency range.** Herring Gull alarm vocalizations are concentrated approximately 800-4,000 Hz, similar to other large gull species. The alarm call is generally higher in pitch than the contact call. At a flock alarm event, the simultaneous production by many birds creates acoustic energy across a broad range within this band.
- **Duration of the alarm event.** Alarm events are brief compared to the ambient colonial chorus: they may last 30 seconds to a few minutes before the threat passes or the flock relocates. The intensity is higher than the colony ambient (E=7 vs. E=6), but the event is shorter. This is the trade-off: more intense, less sustained.
- **Flock size and alarm intensity.** A larger flock produces a more intense alarm event: more birds calling simultaneously, greater total acoustic energy. Winter beach flocks of Herring Gulls in the Pacific Northwest can range from a few dozen to several hundred birds. At the upper end of flock size, the alarm event approaches the acoustic scale of the colonial chorus.
- **Comparison to Glaucous-winged.** The Herring Gull's alarm call is generally considered harsher and more raucous than the Glaucous-winged Gull's -- more abrupt, less melodic. This is a characteristic of the species and contributes to the E=7 rating relative to the Glaucous-winged's colony E=6.

(Sources: Herring Gull alarm trigger; alarm call description kyow-kyow-kyow; overlapping simultaneous calls in flock; frequency range 800-4,000 Hz; alarm event duration; flock size and intensity; alarm call character comparison)

## WINTER FLOCK ECOLOGY

### The Pacific Northwest Beach and Harbor Community

The winter gull flock is a specific ecological community with its own structure:

- **Flock composition.** Winter gull flocks in the Pacific Northwest are rarely single-species. Herring Gulls mix with Glaucous-winged Gulls, Thayer's Gulls (now recognized as Iceland Gull *Larus glaucoides thayeri*), Mew Gulls (*Larus canus*), Ring-billed Gulls (*Larus delawarensis*), and others depending on location. Sorting through a mixed winter gull flock is one of the more demanding exercises in Pacific Northwest birding.
- **Habitat preferences.** Herring Gulls in winter favor fishing harbors (where fish scraps are available), river mouths (where salmonid carcasses concentrate after spawning runs), beaches adjacent to shellfish operations, and refuse disposal sites. The species is an opportunistic omnivore in winter: fish, invertebrates, carrion, garbage, and the remains of spawned-out salmon.
- **Salmon carcass foraging.** In the Pacific Northwest, one of the most ecologically significant winter feeding behaviors of Herring Gulls is the consumption of salmon carcasses. After Pacific salmon (Chinook, Coho, Chum, Pink, Sockeye) spawn in Washington and Oregon rivers and streams, the carcasses provide a massive food resource for gulls, eagles, ravens, and scavengers. Large winter gull flocks gather at river mouths and lower river sections to exploit this resource.
- **Tidal roost sites.** Winter gulls in the PNW often aggregate at tidal roost sites: exposed beaches, breakwaters, and sandbars where birds gather at high tide to rest. These roost aggregations are the sites of the most intense flock alarm events: many birds in close proximity, all alert to potential threats.
- **Foraging competition.** Within the winter flock, foraging competition is constant. Kleptoparasitism (stealing food from other birds) is a common behavior: Herring Gulls are accomplished kleptoparasites, capable of harassing smaller gulls or other waterbirds into dropping food items. The alarm vocalizations within the flock are sometimes related to this foraging competition rather than to external threats.

(Sources: Winter gull flock species composition: Glaucous-winged, Herring, Thayer's/Iceland, Mew, Ring-billed; habitat preferences: harbors, river mouths, beaches; salmon carcass foraging ecology; tidal roost aggregations; kleptoparasitism in gull flocks)

## THE LARIDAE ARC AT 290

### Energy Ascent: E=6 to E=7

- **289-290 acoustic transition.** The arc moves from the sustained ambient colonial chorus (E=6) to the triggered intense alarm event (E=7). The Laridae arc's energy progression is an ascent: E=6 (289) → E=7 (290) → E=7 (291) → E=7 (292). The Glaucous-winged colony at 289 is the arc's lowest energy entry; all subsequent entries hold at E=7.
- **Colony vs. flock.** Three of the four Laridae arc entries (289, 291, 292) are colonial nesting soundscapes. Degree 290 is the exception: the winter flock alarm is not a colonial breeding context but a wintering flock behavioral event. This variation in acoustic context within the arc documents the Laridae family's year-round ecological range -- summer colony, winter flock, both equally characteristic.
- **The Herring Gull as transitional entry.** The Herring Gull bridges the arc between the Glaucous-winged Gull colony (the most common PNW gull in its breeding context) and the Western Gull colony (the Pacific coast specialist in its rocky island context). The Herring Gull flock alarm is the arc's pivot: from summer colony to winter flock and back to summer colony at 291.
