## THE SPY-HOP: WHAT IT IS

### The Behavior in Detail

The spy-hop is one of the most visually distinctive behaviors in the orca repertoire:

- **The rise.** Starting from at or just below the surface, the orca propels itself vertically. The animal rises until its head is fully above the surface and, in a full spy-hop, until the level of the pectoral fins or higher is exposed. This is not a breach -- the animal does not leave the water entirely and does not fall forward. The rise is controlled, vertical, and deliberate.
- **The hold.** Once the orca has reached the peak of the spy-hop, it holds position briefly -- typically 2 to 10 seconds. This is the observational phase. With its eyes above the water surface, the animal can see in the aerial environment: the horizon, other animals at the surface, vessels, shoreline features. The orca is looking.
- **The body orientation.** The orca typically faces a specific direction during the spy-hop -- toward a point of interest. Researchers document orcas spy-hopping and tracking moving vessels, other marine mammals, or orca individuals at the surface. The directional orientation of the spy-hop is evidence that it is specifically observational, not random.
- **The return.** The orca's controlled descent back into the water. In a full spy-hop, this involves the animal's weight returning through the water surface with a significant splash. Not a crash equivalent to a breach impact, but a substantial water event -- E=8 on the engine's scale.
- **The acoustic event.** The spy-hop itself is not primarily a vocal event. The sound is the water displacement: the rise (water rushing around the ascending body), the hold (minimal), and the return (water impact). Vocalizations may accompany the behavior but are not definitively associated with it.

(Sources: Ford orca spy-hop documentation; spy-hop behavioral description; directional orientation studies; spy-hop acoustic profile; spy-hop return impact)

## THE CULTURAL BEHAVIOR HYPOTHESIS

### Learning and Social Transmission

- **Cultural transmission.** The spy-hop is not a genetically programmed reflex -- it is a learned behavior that is transmitted within pods and across generations. Young orcas observe adults spy-hopping and learn the behavior. This is one of the clearest examples of cultural transmission in non-human animals.
- **What cultural transmission means.** For a behavior to be culturally transmitted rather than genetically determined: (a) it must show within-population variation (some pods spy-hop more than others); (b) it must be learned during a developmental period through observation; (c) it must persist in the population through teaching and practice rather than through genetic inheritance. The spy-hop meets all three criteria.
- **Intra-species variation.** Not all orca populations spy-hop at the same rate or in the same contexts. Some populations appear to use spy-hopping more frequently in specific ecological contexts (when approaching boats, when on the surface near prey). This variation is evidence that the behavior is shaped by local culture, not just genetic programming.
- **The sophistication argument.** The spy-hop is evidence that orcas model the above-water world. To rise deliberately and orient toward a point of interest, to hold position long enough to gather visual information, to use above-water vision as a navigational and social tool -- this requires a cognitive model of the world that extends beyond the animal's primary underwater sensory environment. The orca spy-hops because it has a concept of the above-water world as a source of useful information.

(Sources: Cetacean cultural transmission research; Rendell and Whitehead cultural behavior studies; orca learning and development; within-population behavioral variation; cognitive modeling and sensory integration in orcas)

## THE VISUAL CAPABILITY OF THE ORCA

### Seeing Above and Below

- **Bifocal vision.** Orcas have eyes positioned on the sides of the head, providing a wide field of view underwater. The eye anatomy supports vision in both water and air -- the cornea and lens are adapted to function in both media. When the orca raises its head above the surface, it is making active use of a visual capability that most marine mammals lack.
- **Aerial visual acuity.** Studies of orca visual acuity suggest that the animals can resolve detail at the surface from distances of several meters. They can track moving objects (vessels, other animals) visually from above the water. This visual tracking during spy-hops is consistent with an observational function.
- **The perspective difference.** From underwater, even a shallow-swimming orca has a refraction-limited view of the world above the surface. The critical angle of total internal reflection means that objects above the water are visible only through a narrow cone (Snell's window). A spy-hop expands the visual field enormously -- the orca gains an unrestricted aerial view of the immediate environment. The behavioral cost (energy expenditure) is justified by the information gain.
- **Night spy-hopping.** Orcas have also been documented spy-hopping in low-light or darkness conditions, suggesting that the behavior may serve functions beyond visual observation -- perhaps acoustic orientation or tactile sensing of air currents.

(Sources: Orca visual anatomy; bifocal vision research; aerial visual acuity; Snell's window and underwater vision; night spy-hopping documentation)

## THE SOUND PROFILE: E=8

### Water Displacement and Return

- **The ascent sound.** As the orca's body rises through the water surface, the displacement of water creates a rushing, surging sound. For an adult animal (3,600-6,000 kg depending on sex and age), this displacement is substantial -- a significant water event.
- **The hold.** Near silence above the surface except for breathing (the blow through the dorsal melon/spiracle). This brief moment of near-quiet between the ascent and descent is characteristic of spy-hopping sound profiles.
- **The descent.** The controlled return to the water. Not the impact crash of a breach (E=9), but a significant water event -- the animal's mass returning through the surface in a controlled descent. E=8.
- **Why E=8 and not E=9.** The difference between the spy-hop return (E=8) and the breach impact (E=9) is the manner of return. The breach is a lateral body-slam -- maximum surface area, maximum impact. The spy-hop is a vertical descent, more controlled, involving a smaller effective impact surface. Still substantial; still E=8.
- **Underwater transmission.** The sounds of spy-hop water events are audible underwater as well as at the surface. Orcas in the vicinity of a spy-hopping individual can detect the behavior acoustically. This may be a secondary communicative function.

(Sources: Spy-hop water displacement acoustics; ascent/descent sound profiles; E=8 vs. E=9 breach comparison; underwater transmission of surface events)

## SPY-HOP VS. BREACH: TWO MODES OF ABOVE-WATER PRESENCE

### The Behavioral Distinction

Degrees 273 and 274 document two different above-water orca behaviors. Comparing them clarifies what each is:

- **The breach (E=9).** Full energy expenditure: the animal leaves the water entirely, achieves maximum height, and returns in a body-slam. Function uncertain but may include communication, ectoparasite removal, play, or sensory exploration. Energy maximum.
- **The spy-hop (E=8).** Controlled, vertical, deliberate. The animal rises to observe. The function is clearer than for the breach: the orca is gathering visual information about the above-water environment. The energy expenditure is less than the breach because the intent is observational, not explosive.
- **Two modes of presence.** The breach is the orca's maximum physical assertion -- going fully airborne and crashing back. The spy-hop is the orca's maximum observational presence -- rising to see and holding position. One is about going completely; the other is about watching carefully. Both are remarkable. Both are above the surface. Both are cultural behaviors.
- **The arc's narrative.** Degree 273 (breach) is the orca at maximum physical force. Degree 274 (spy-hop) is the orca as observer. The arc is documenting the range of the animal's behavioral repertoire.

(Sources: Breach vs. spy-hop behavioral comparison; functional distinctions; orca behavioral ecology; above-water presence modes)

## SOUTHERN RESIDENTS: BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY

### The Context for Spy-Hopping in the Salish Sea

- **The Salish Sea environment.** The Southern Residents live in a complex environment: the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, Puget Sound, and the waters between Vancouver Island and the mainland. The surface environment is active: commercial ships, ferries, whale-watching vessels, recreational boats, and the animals themselves. Spy-hopping is a functional behavior in this environment -- it allows the orca to gather information about what is happening at and above the surface.
- **Vessel awareness.** SRKWs have been documented spy-hopping in response to vessels -- rising to observe approaching boats, tracking their movement, apparently gathering information about their presence. This is both fascinating and concerning: the whales are aware of vessels, which means they are living with constant awareness of the acoustic and visual noise that vessels create.
- **Pod dynamics.** Spy-hopping occurs in social contexts as well as in response to environmental stimuli. Pod members may spy-hop together, apparently sharing the observational moment. This social dimension is consistent with the cultural transmission hypothesis.
- **The salmon question.** With Chinook salmon as the primary prey (cross-reference degree 237), the SRKWs must constantly assess their environment for salmon presence. Spy-hopping may assist with prey detection at the surface -- surface-schooling salmon can sometimes be spotted from above the water.

(Sources: Salish Sea vessel traffic; SRKW vessel interaction research; pod social spy-hopping; salmon detection from above surface; environmental complexity and spy-hop function)
