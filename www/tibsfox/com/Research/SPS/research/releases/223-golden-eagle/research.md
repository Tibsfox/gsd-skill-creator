## THE GOLDEN EAGLE -- *AQUILA CHRYSAETOS*

### The True Eagle

If the Bald Eagle is North America's symbolic eagle, the Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*) is the world's eagle -- the most widespread eagle species on Earth, found across the Northern Hemisphere:

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Accipitriformes. Family: Accipitridae. Genus: *Aquila* (true eagles, Booted Eagles). Species: *chrysaetos* (golden eagle, from Greek: chrysos = gold, aetos = eagle).
- **The genus *Aquila*.** The Booted Eagles (*Aquila* and related genera) are the "true eagles" of the Old World tradition -- used in falconry for millennia, depicted in heraldry across Eurasia, and ecologically significant across multiple continents. *Aquila* includes the Imperial Eagle, Wedge-tailed Eagle, and others. The Golden Eagle is the only *Aquila* species in North America.
- **Global distribution.** The Golden Eagle is found across North America, Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It is the most cosmopolitan large eagle in the world. The PNW population is part of a global species -- a very different ecological situation from the Bald Eagle, which is strictly North American.
- **Size.** Comparable to Bald Eagle. Females (larger sex): 3.8-6.7 kg (8.4-14.7 lbs), wingspan 1.8-2.34 m (5.9-7.7 ft). The species shows significant size variation across its range -- western North American birds tend to be larger than eastern birds.
- **Plumage.** Adult Golden Eagles are dark brown overall with a distinctive golden-brown nape (back of neck) -- the "golden" that gives the species its name. The nape shimmers in sunlight. Juveniles have white patches at the base of the tail feathers and at the base of the primary feathers, creating a distinctive two-toned underwing pattern visible in flight. Unlike Bald Eagles, there is no dramatic adult plumage transformation -- Golden Eagles are recognizably similar in pattern from juvenile through adult.
- **The relationship to Bald Eagle.** The Golden Eagle predates the Bald Eagle as the "eagle" of Old World tradition. European heraldry (Holy Roman Empire, Germany, Austria, Russia, Mexico) uses the Golden Eagle as the national eagle symbol in most cases. The Bald Eagle replaced it in American iconography as a New World species.

(Sources: Aquila chrysaetos taxonomy; Aquila genus; global distribution; size data; plumage; heraldry comparison; Old World vs. New World eagle tradition)

## THE HIGH THIN WHISTLE -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### A Quiet Eagle

The Golden Eagle's vocalizations are less studied and less prominent than those of the Bald Eagle or many other large raptors:

- **The primary call.** A thin, high-pitched yelp or whistle: "kyee" or "kyip" (also rendered as "kiii"). The call is quiet relative to the bird's size -- the Golden Eagle is not a vocal species. When a Golden Eagle calls in the wild, the sound is often described as surprisingly weak for such a large and powerful bird.
- **Frequency.** Approximately 1,500-4,000 Hz. The call is in the middle-high range of bird vocalizations -- neither the ultrasonic nor the low-frequency booming, but a thin, clear whistle.
- **Vocalization contexts.** Golden Eagles vocalize during: pair bonding (softer calls between mates), nest defense (louder, more repeated calls -- still quieter than Bald Eagle territorial calls), food begging by young (persistent, repetitive calls), and alarm (sharper, more urgent single calls). The species is not generally vocal during hunting or territorial patrol.
- **Communication through flight.** Where the Bald Eagle communicates through its voice (the loud territorial call carries across the landscape), the Golden Eagle communicates more through flight behavior. The undulating territorial display flight (called a "sky dance"), stooping, and soaring over the territory are the primary territorial signals. The voice is secondary.
- **E=4 -- below equilibrium.** The Golden Eagle's vocalization energy is lower than either Bald Eagle entry (E=5 contact, E=6 territorial). E=4 reflects the species' generally quiet nature. The energy is in the flight, not the voice.

(Sources: Golden Eagle vocalization; kyee/kyip call; frequency analysis; vocal contexts; sky dance display flight; E=4 assessment)

## ECOLOGY -- THE OPEN COUNTRY HUNTER

### A Different World from the Bald Eagle

Where the Bald Eagle is a water/forest specialist, the Golden Eagle is an open country specialist:

- **Habitat.** Montane grasslands, alpine meadows, sagebrush steppe, chaparral, open ridgelines, cliff faces for nesting. The Golden Eagle requires two things: open terrain for hunting (where prey is visible from the air) and cliffs or large trees for nesting. It does not require water as a primary habitat element.
- **Diet.** The Golden Eagle's diet is dominated by medium-sized mammals: rabbits, hares (especially jackrabbits and snowshoe hares), ground squirrels, prairie dogs, and other ground-dwelling rodents. It also takes marmots, foxes, small ungulates (deer fawns), birds, and reptiles. In the PNW, black-tailed jackrabbits, Columbia ground squirrels, and Townsend's ground squirrels are primary prey items.
- **Hunting technique.** The Golden Eagle hunts by soaring over open terrain, detecting prey visually from heights of 15-30 m (50-100 ft), and stooping in a controlled dive at speeds that can exceed 300 km/h (190 mph). The stoop is one of the most dramatic predatory acts in North American wildlife -- the eagle drops from height, folding its wings partially, and strikes prey with its talons. Death is typically instantaneous.
- **Carrying capacity.** The Golden Eagle can carry prey up to approximately 1 kg in flight. It will cache heavier prey (drag it to a sheltered location) and feed on it over multiple visits.
- **Comparison to Bald Eagle.** The ecological divergence is clear: Bald Eagle = water/fish/carrion specialist; Golden Eagle = open country/mammal specialist. They rarely compete directly because their habitat preferences rarely overlap substantially.

(Sources: Golden Eagle habitat; diet; hunting technique; stoop speed; carrying capacity; Bald Eagle comparison; PNW prey species)

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION

### Where Golden Eagles Live in the PNW

- **Eastern Washington.** The Golden Eagle is most common in eastern Washington -- the drier, more open country east of the Cascades. The channeled scablands, Palouse grasslands, and Columbia Basin's sagebrush steppe are productive Golden Eagle habitat. The combination of jackrabbits, ground squirrels, and abundant thermals for soaring makes eastern Washington an important part of the regional population.
- **Cascade foothills and alpine zones.** Golden Eagles nest at some sites in the Cascade Range and the adjacent foothills, using cliff faces and rocky outcrops. They are uncommon but present in alpine zones above treeline where open terrain allows hunting.
- **Western Washington.** Uncommon in western Washington due to the forest-dominated landscape. Occasional sightings in the Salish Sea region and along the coast, but not a breeding species in the lowland west. The Olympic Peninsula's alpine zones have some breeding activity.
- **Winter movement.** Some altitudinal movement occurs in winter -- birds that nest at high elevation may move to lower valleys during deep snowpack. Eastern Washington's relatively mild winters (compared to the mountain zones) attract some winter visitors from further north.
- **Rarity context.** In western Washington (including the Seattle area), the Golden Eagle is genuinely rare -- a sighting is notable. The Bald Eagle is common and expected; the Golden Eagle is a special occasion. This rarity makes it E=4 in terms of encounter frequency and acoustic prominence.

(Sources: Golden Eagle Washington distribution; eastern Washington habitat; Cascade foothills; western Washington rarity; winter movement; Columbia Basin)

## FALCONRY AND CULTURAL HISTORY

### The Eagle That Humans Have Flown for 4,000 Years

The Golden Eagle has a cultural history that the Bald Eagle cannot match -- not in American symbolism, but in the global tradition of falconry:

- **Falconry.** The Golden Eagle has been used in falconry -- the art of training raptors to hunt with humans -- for at least 4,000 years. Central Asian falconry traditions (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Mongolia) use Golden Eagles to hunt wolves, foxes, and other large prey. The Eagle Hunters of Mongolia and Kazakhstan represent one of the oldest continuous falconry traditions in the world.
- **Eagle Hunters.** Berkutchi (eagle hunters in Central Asia) train Golden Eagles from eaglet stage, forming a bond with the bird that lasts the eagle's hunting career (typically 7-10 years, then released). A mature berkutchi eagle can bring down a fox, hare, or even a wolf in cooperation with the hunter on horseback. This tradition is UNESCO-recognized as an Intangible Cultural Heritage.
- **European heraldry.** The Golden Eagle appears in the heraldry of the Holy Roman Empire, the German Empire, Austria, Poland, Mexico, and numerous other nations. The double-headed eagle of Byzantine origin appears in Russian, Albanian, and other heraldry. These traditions pre-date American national symbolism by centuries.
- **PNW Indigenous traditions.** Golden Eagle feathers are sacred in many North American Indigenous traditions, particularly for Plains tribes but also for some PNW nations. The eagle feathers are used in regalia, prayer, and ceremony. Regulatory frameworks (Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act 1940, amended to include Golden Eagle in 1962) protect eagles while allowing Indigenous use for ceremonial purposes.

(Sources: Falconry history 4,000 years; Central Asian berkutchi; UNESCO heritage; European heraldry; PNW Indigenous traditions; Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act)

## CONSERVATION STATUS

### A Stable but Monitored Population

- **IUCN status.** Least Concern globally -- the Golden Eagle's global population (estimated 300,000+ individuals) is broadly stable. The species is not endangered.
- **North American status.** The North American population is less well-characterized than the global population. Golden Eagles are monitored by the Raptor Population Index and annual migration counts at established hawk watch sites (Hawk Mountain, PA; Golden Gate Raptor Observatory, CA; Bonney Butte, OR).
- **Threats.** Primary threats: lead poisoning (from ingesting carcasses containing lead shot), electrocution on power lines (a documented problem, particularly on older-design utility structures), wind turbine collisions, and rodenticide poisoning (secondary -- eagles that consume poisoned rodents).
- **Washington State.** Washington's Golden Eagle population is subject to the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act, which makes it illegal to disturb, harm, or possess Golden Eagles or their feathers without a permit. The eastern Washington population is monitored but population trends are not fully quantified.

(Sources: IUCN Least Concern; North American population; Raptor Population Index; Bonney Butte hawkwatch; lead poisoning; electrocution; wind turbine; rodenticide; Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act)
