First Geophysical Entry — E=5 — The Plate Boundary Beneath Everything

The Cascadia Subduction Zone is the Pacific Northwest’s most consequential geological feature: the Juan de Fuca Plate subducting beneath North America at 4–5 cm/year along a 1,100 km boundary. The acoustic signatures — deep current infrasound, Episodic Tremor and Slip (ETS) at 1–10 Hz, hydrothermal vent signals — are documented by hydrophone networks on the Pacific floor. E=5: the geological baseline beneath all other PNW acoustic events.

The Plates

Juan de Fuca Plate

Oceanic plate, ~250,000 km². Subducting beneath North America at 4–5 cm/year. Created at Juan de Fuca Ridge (~300 km offshore). Dives to 100+ km depth beneath the continent. The plate driving Cascadia.

North American Plate

Continental plate. Riding over the Juan de Fuca Plate as it subducts. The locked zone: 30–40 km depth along the continental margin. Stress accumulating since 1700. The plate being overridden.

Episodic Tremor and Slip (ETS) — The 14-Month Signal

Every ~14 months, the CSZ transition zone undergoes Episodic Tremor and Slip: a slow-slip event that releases stress over weeks rather than seconds, accompanied by non-volcanic tremor at 1–10 Hz. This is the geological acoustic signal the engine documents at degree 318 — not the catastrophic earthquake (the 1700 event, M9.0) but the continuous between-event baseline. The ETS rhythm is one of the most precisely timed geological phenomena in the Pacific Northwest. The tremor is infrasonic at the frequencies of concern but detectable by the Pacific seafloor hydrophone networks.

The 1700 Event — The Last Full Rupture

January 26, 1700 — M9.0 — Full-Margin Rupture

The most recent CSZ full-margin rupture is documented by: Japanese orphan tsunami records (a large tsunami struck Japan the morning of January 27, 1700, with no local earthquake); Pacific Northwest tree ring chronologies (coastal forests killed by saltwater inundation 1699–1700); Native oral traditions of coastal peoples describing catastrophic events; turbidite seafloor deposits (earthquake-triggered underwater landslides). Combined evidence: M9.0, January 26, 1700, approximately 9 PM Pacific time. Tsunami run-up: 10–30 meters on the Pacific Northwest coast. Japan reached: 10 hours.

Return interval: 300–500 years. The Pacific Northwest is currently within that window.

The Acoustic Signal — What Degree 318 Documents

The engine documents the between-event CSZ acoustic baseline: not the megathrust (1700) but the continuous geological murmur of a locked subduction zone. ETS tremor (1–10 Hz) during 14-month cycles. Deep ocean current pressure variations below 1 Hz. Hydrothermal vent acoustics at the Juan de Fuca Ridge (Axial Seamount, ~300 km offshore, monitored by Ocean Observatories Initiative). Microseismic noise from ocean surface wave pressure (0.05–0.5 Hz).

Converted to audio (shifted 100x upward to audible range), CSZ ETS tremor sounds like sustained distant rain or low-frequency surf — a continuous wash with intermittent bursts. Physically real. Acoustically below human hearing in situ. Documented by instrument.

E=5 — Scale vs. Intensity

By physical scale, the CSZ is the largest object the SPS engine has documented: 250,000 km² of subducting plate, 1,100 km fault, 1700 megathrust energy equivalent to millions of Hiroshima bombs. By E-scale, it is E=5. The distinction: E-value reflects perceptual acoustic intensity as encountered in the environment, not the absolute physical scale of the generating system. The geological system is enormous; the continuous ambient signal it produces is in the infrasonic range, near-imperceptible to humans at surface. E=5 is the correct calibration: physically real, geologically significant, perceptually near-absent at the surface.
