## THE CASCADIA SUBDUCTION ZONE -- GEOLOGICAL CONTEXT

### The Plate Boundary Beneath the Pacific Northwest

The Cascadia Subduction Zone (CSZ) is the dominant geological feature of the Pacific Northwest:

- **The plates.** The Juan de Fuca Plate -- a remnant oceanic tectonic plate -- is being subducted (pushed beneath) the North American Plate along a convergent boundary that runs from northern California through Oregon, Washington, and British Columbia to southern Alaska. The rate of subduction: approximately 4-5 centimeters per year. The Juan de Fuca Plate is 200+ kilometers wide and extends to a depth of approximately 100 km beneath the North American continent.
- **The subduction zone.** The subduction boundary (where the two plates are in contact and the oceanic plate dives beneath the continental plate) runs roughly parallel to the Pacific coast, approximately 50-100 km offshore. The locked zone -- where the plates are in contact and stress is accumulating -- extends from the ocean floor to depths of 30-40 km beneath the continental margin.
- **The megathrust.** The CSZ is capable of megathrust earthquakes: moment magnitude 8-9+ events that occur when the locked zone ruptures. The most recent CSZ megathrust event was January 26, 1700 (M9.0 estimated from Japanese tsunami records and Pacific Northwest Native oral traditions and geological evidence). The return interval for CSZ full-margin ruptures: approximately 300-500 years. The Pacific Northwest is currently in that return interval.
- **The extent.** The CSZ runs approximately 1,100 km (700 miles) along strike. A full-rupture event would affect the entire Pacific Northwest coast simultaneously. The geological acoustic baseline documented here is the continuous expression of the same system that will eventually produce the next megathrust.

(Sources: Juan de Fuca Plate subduction; 4-5 cm/year convergence rate; locked zone depth 30-40 km; 1700 CSZ megathrust M9.0; Japanese tsunami records January 26 1700; Native oral traditions; CSZ return interval 300-500 years; 1,100 km along-strike length; USGS Cascadia Subduction Zone documentation)

## EPISODIC TREMOR AND SLIP (ETS) -- THE REGULAR SIGNAL

### What the CSZ Does Every 14 Months

The Cascadia Subduction Zone produces a well-documented, recurring acoustic/seismic signal called Episodic Tremor and Slip (ETS):

- **What ETS is.** In the transition zone between the fully locked shallow subduction zone and the freely sliding deep zone, the plates undergo periodic slow slip events. These events are called "slow earthquakes" or "silent earthquakes" -- they involve plate motion but release energy over weeks rather than seconds, producing no felt shaking at the surface. The slow slip is accompanied by non-volcanic tremor: low-frequency seismic signals (1-10 Hz) generated by fluid movement and micro-cracking along the plate interface.
- **The rhythm.** CSZ ETS events occur approximately every 14 months along the full Cascadia margin, with sub-events occurring more frequently. The most recent major ETS events have been well-documented by the Pacific Northwest seismic network. The regularity of the ETS cycle is one of the most precisely timed geological phenomena in the Pacific Northwest.
- **The acoustic signature.** Non-volcanic tremor associated with ETS events produces low-frequency seismic radiation (1-10 Hz) that propagates both through the rock and, at the ocean floor, into the water column. This is infrasonic by the definition relevant to the SPS engine: below the 20 Hz human hearing threshold at the frequencies of interest, but detectable by hydrophone networks (NEPTUNE Canada, the Ocean Networks Canada network of seafloor observatories).
- **Geographic location.** ETS occurs primarily in the transition zone at depths of 25-45 km beneath the PNW, roughly beneath the Puget Sound basin and extending south through western Washington and Oregon. The very low infrasonic signals propagate through the crust and into the water column above.

(Sources: Episodic Tremor and Slip; Cascadia ETS 14-month recurrence; non-volcanic tremor 1-10 Hz; slow earthquakes; fluid movement and micro-cracking; Pacific Northwest seismic network; NEPTUNE Canada; Ocean Networks Canada; ETS depth 25-45 km; Puget Sound basin)

## THE DEEP OCEAN CURRENT ACOUSTIC SIGNAL

### What the Pacific Ocean Floor Sounds Like

Independent of the tectonic signals, the deep Pacific Ocean produces its own acoustic baseline:

- **Deep ocean ambient noise.** The deep Pacific Ocean is acoustically complex. The dominant low-frequency acoustic sources at depth include: (1) microseismic noise -- ocean surface waves generating pressure fluctuations that propagate to the seafloor (primary microseismic peak: approximately 0.05-0.25 Hz, secondary peak 0.1-0.5 Hz); (2) deep ocean currents -- the thermohaline circulation produces near-silent low-frequency pressure variations as water masses move at different temperatures and salinities; (3) geological signals -- volcanic activity, hydrothermal vent acoustics, and plate boundary signals.
- **The SOFAR channel.** The Sound Fixing and Ranging (SOFAR) channel is a depth layer in the ocean (typically 600-1,200 m depth in the Pacific) where acoustic velocity is at a minimum, allowing long-distance propagation of low-frequency sounds. Below the SOFAR channel, sounds generated by geological processes (including Cascadia-related ETS tremor) can propagate over thousands of kilometers.
- **Hydrothermal vents.** The Juan de Fuca Ridge -- the spreading center that creates the Juan de Fuca Plate before it subducts at Cascadia -- hosts hydrothermal vent fields (including the Axial Seamount, one of the most monitored volcanoes in the world, located ~300 km off the Washington coast). These vents produce acoustic signals as superheated water is expelled into the cold deep ocean. The acoustic signatures are documented by the Ocean Observatories Initiative (OOI) seafloor network.
- **Frequency range.** The combined deep-ocean/tectonic acoustic baseline described here: 0.01-10 Hz. This is the infrasonic deep -- below human hearing at ambient conditions, but physically present and measurable. The E=5 calibration reflects the geological significance and physical reality of this acoustic environment without overstating the human-perceptible intensity.

(Sources: Deep ocean ambient noise; microseismic noise 0.05-0.25 Hz; SOFAR channel 600-1200m depth; long-range acoustic propagation; Juan de Fuca Ridge; Axial Seamount; hydrothermal vent acoustics; OOI seafloor network; infrasonic frequency range 0.01-10 Hz; E=5 calibration)

## THE 1700 EVENT -- THE LAST FULL-MARGIN RUPTURE

### What the CSZ Has Already Done and Will Do Again

The January 26, 1700 CSZ megathrust is the most important geological fact in PNW history:

- **The evidence.** The 1700 event is documented through: (1) Japanese historical records of an "orphan tsunami" (a large tsunami with no local earthquake report) that struck Japan in the early hours of January 27, 1700; (2) Pacific Northwest tree ring chronologies showing coastal forests killed by sudden land subsidence and saltwater inundation in 1699-1700; (3) oral traditions of coastal Pacific Northwest Native peoples describing catastrophic events; (4) turbidite records on the seafloor (sediment deposits left by earthquake-triggered underwater landslides). The combined evidence supports an M9.0 event on January 26, 1700 at approximately 9 PM Pacific time.
- **The rupture area.** The 1700 event ruptured the full CSZ from northern California to British Columbia -- approximately 1,000 km of fault. This is a full-margin rupture. The resulting tsunami reached Japan in approximately 10 hours. The Pacific Northwest coast experienced run-up heights of 10-30 meters.
- **The acoustic legacy.** What the CSZ produced acoustically in 1700 -- the sudden release of centuries of accumulated strain over approximately 1,000 km -- is the opposite of what degree 318 documents. Degree 318 is the quiet geological baseline: the continuous infrasonic current and tremor of a locked subduction zone in its between-event phase. The 1700 event was the catastrophic acoustic event that degree 318 is the prelude to.

(Sources: 1700 CSZ evidence: Japanese orphan tsunami records; Pacific Northwest tree ring chronologies; Native oral traditions; turbidite seafloor deposits; M9.0 estimated magnitude; January 26 1700 9PM Pacific; full-margin rupture; 1,000 km; Japan tsunami 10 hours; run-up heights 10-30 meters)
