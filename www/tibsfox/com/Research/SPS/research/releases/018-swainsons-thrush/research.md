## Taxonomy: The Second Catharus -- Purity Returns

The Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*) is a medium-small passerine of the family **Turdidae** (thrushes), order Passeriformes. The genus *Catharus* -- from Greek *katharos*, "pure" -- contains five North American species. At degree 7, the Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*) introduced the genus to the engine and brought with it the harmonic series. Now at degree 18, the Swainson's Thrush becomes the **second Catharus thrush in the circle** -- the engine's first return to this genus, and the first opportunity to compare two compositional strategies within the genus whose name means "pure."

The species epithet **ustulatus** means "scorched" or "singed" (Latin *ustulare*, "to scorch"), referring to the warm, tawny-brown tones of the upperparts -- as if the plumage had been held briefly over a flame. The name was given by Thomas Nuttall in 1840. The common name honors William Swainson, the English naturalist and illustrator, one of the most prolific bird artists of the nineteenth century.

**Two subspecies groups in the PNW.** The Swainson's Thrush contains two well-differentiated subspecies groups that some researchers argue represent distinct species: the **olive-backed** (*ustulatus* group, coastal Pacific -- the primary PNW birds) and the **russet-backed** (*swainsoni* group, interior mountains). They differ in plumage tone, migration routes, and vocalizations -- a potential species-level split that molecular data increasingly supports (Delmore et al. 2012). For the engine, the coastal olive-backed population is the primary reference.

**Physical description.** Compact and upright: **6.3--7.5 inches** (16--19 cm), **23--45 grams**. Uniform olive-brown upperparts. Pale underparts with bold dark spots as smudged chevrons. The diagnostic field mark: a **bold, buffy eyering** and a warm **buffy wash on the face and upper breast** -- the "spectacled" look, distinguishing the Swainson's from the Hermit Thrush (rufous tail, thin pale eyering) and from the Gray-cheeked Thrush (no eyering).

## Bioacoustics -- THE ASCENDING SPIRAL

### The Song That Climbs

The Swainson's Thrush's song is one of the most beautiful and distinctive sounds in PNW forests. It is an **upward-spiraling series of fluty phrases**, each phrase pitched higher than the last, creating a staircase of sound that literally climbs through pitch space. The song ascends. It rises. Each phrase builds on the one before, stepping upward in frequency until the sequence reaches its ceiling and either stops or drops back to restart the cycle.

This is a **SPIRAL STAIRCASE in sound** -- each step higher, each phrase building on the last, the melody winding upward through the evening forest. No other PNW species produces this particular contour: a series of discrete musical phrases, each one higher in pitch than the previous, accumulating into an upward trajectory. Each individual phrase consists of several fluty notes with internal turns and ornaments, but the defining feature is the **inter-phrase pitch trajectory**: each successive phrase begins at a higher pitch. The cumulative effect: a melody that ascends.

### The Ascending Spiral vs. the Spiraling Arch

This is the critical comparison with the Hermit Thrush (degree 7). Both are *Catharus*. Both are "spiraling." Both are "fluty." But the spiral goes in **different directions**, and the compositional logic is fundamentally different:

| Dimension | Hermit Thrush (7) | Swainson's Thrush (18) |
|---|---|---|
| **Phrase contour** | Arch (ascending then descending within one phrase) | Ascending (each phrase higher than the last) |
| **Spiral type** | Orbiting -- successive phrases at different starting pitches create orbit | Climbing -- successive phrases at progressively higher pitches create ascent |
| **Pitch relationship** | Notes within a phrase follow the harmonic series (vertical intervals) | Phrases follow a sequential ascent (horizontal progression) |
| **Musical thinking** | HARMONIC (vertical -- chord tones, overtone series) | MELODIC (horizontal -- sequential steps, scalar ascent) |
| **Compositional strategy** | Each phrase is a self-contained harmonic unit | Each phrase is a step in a larger ascending line |
| **Form** | Phrase-level arch form (each phrase has beginning, climax, resolution) | Song-level ascending form (the whole song is one directional gesture) |
| **Emotional arc** | Contemplation (the arch turns back on itself) | Aspiration (the line keeps climbing) |

**Harmonic thinking vs. melodic thinking.** This is one of the most fundamental distinctions in music theory. **Harmonic thinking** is vertical: which notes sound together, how they relate as chord members -- intervals, triads, overtone relationships. The Hermit Thrush's song is harmonic: within each phrase, notes are drawn from the overtone series. **Melodic thinking** is horizontal: where the line goes next, the direction of the phrase through pitch space over time. The Swainson's Thrush's song is melodic: the defining feature is the direction of the sequence -- each phrase higher, the line ascending.

The same genus (*Catharus*, "pure") has produced both strategies. Two ways of organizing pitch. Two axes of musical thought.

### The Ascending Spiral as Musical Structure

In music theory, the **ascending sequence** is a compositional device of building tension and aspiration. A musical idea is stated, then restated at a higher pitch level, then higher again, creating a directional trajectory that the listener experiences as **reaching upward**. The emotional content of ascending sequences is remarkably consistent across cultures and contexts: hope, yearning, striving, aspiration, transcendence, reaching for something just beyond grasp.

The Swainson's Thrush's song IS an ascending sequence. The same compositional device, evolved independently in a biological system. Each phrase is a musical idea. Each repetition is transposed upward. The cumulative effect: a melody that aspires.

Examples of the ascending sequence in human music:

- **"The Star-Spangled Banner"** -- the opening phrase ("Oh, say can you see") ascends through a broken major triad, each melodic peak higher than the last. The national anthem is an ascending sequence. The emotional content: aspiration, reaching, dawn.
- **Coltrane's "A Love Supreme"** -- the four-note motif is transposed upward through ascending modal explorations. Coltrane builds spiritual intensity through sequential ascent. The musical prayer climbs.
- **Bach's sequences** -- Baroque keyboard works frequently use ascending sequential patterns to build harmonic tension over a ground bass. The sequence ascends; the harmony intensifies; the resolution is earned by the climb.
- **Ravel's "Bolero"** -- the theme is restated at progressively higher registers and with increasing orchestral density. Ascending register + increasing dynamics = the spiral staircase in orchestral form.

The Swainson's Thrush executes this device every time it sings. Not by analogy. By parallel evolution.

### The Matched Pair: Ascending and Descending Spirals

The Swainson's Thrush (degree 18) spirals **UPWARD**. The Veery (*Catharus fuscescens*, degree 19) spirals **DOWNWARD**. Together they form a **matched pair** -- the ascending and descending spirals, placed at adjacent degrees in the circle, both members of the genus *Catharus*. The spiral goes both ways. At degree 18, it climbs. At degree 19, it falls. Three Catharus thrushes in the circle (degrees 7, 18, 19), three different strategies: harmonic series (7), ascending spiral (18), descending spiral (19). The genus whose name means "pure" provides the engine with its most complete exploration of pitch organization.

## Riparian Forest: The Stream-Edge Canopy

### Habitat as Acoustic Architecture

The Swainson's Thrush breeds in **riparian forest** -- the dense, moist, deciduous-conifer mixed forest along streams, rivers, and wet drainages. In the PNW, this means:

- **Alder-willow-cottonwood corridors** along rivers and streams in the lowlands
- **Dense understory of salmonberry, thimbleberry, and sword fern** beneath a canopy of red alder, bigleaf maple, and western redcedar
- **Moist conifer forest** with a well-developed shrub understory
- **Wet ravines and gullies** in second-growth and mature forest

The riparian corridor is the Swainson's Thrush's home. It sings from concealed perches within the mid-canopy (10--30 feet above ground). The ascending song **penetrates upward through the canopy layers**: each phrase, pitched higher than the last, pushes into a different frequency band. Lower phrases propagate through the dense understory; higher phrases escape upward through canopy gaps. The ascending spiral is acoustically functional -- it sweeps through the frequency channels of the riparian forest in sequence, sampling each layer in turn. A spotlight moving vertically through the canopy.

## The Second Catharus: Harmonic vs. Melodic Thinking

### Vertical and Horizontal in Music Theory

The distinction maps onto the two axes of a musical score. **The vertical axis** (y-axis) represents simultaneous pitch relationships -- harmony: intervals, chords, consonance. The Hermit Thrush navigates this axis (Doolittle & Brumm 2014). **The horizontal axis** (x-axis) represents sequential pitch relationships -- melody: where the line goes, trajectory through time. The Swainson's Thrush navigates this axis.

Western music integrates both. A Bach chorale has vertical harmony and horizontal voice leading. Different traditions emphasize one over the other -- gamelan emphasizes the horizontal (interlocking melodic patterns), Romantic orchestral writing the vertical (rich harmonic progressions). The *Catharus* genus contains both extremes. The Hermit Thrush is the harmonist. The Swainson's Thrush is the melodist. Same genus, different axis.

### The Ascending Sequence as Emotional Narrative

Ascending pitch in vocal communication -- across languages and species -- is associated with increased arousal, attention-seeking, and incompleteness. A rising inflection is a question. A rising melody is unresolved tension. The Swainson's Thrush's ascending song carries this weight. Each phrase higher, each step lifting the listener. The song does not resolve. It does not come back down (unlike the Hermit Thrush's arch, which descends after its peak). It climbs and stops. The resolution is in the silence after the final phrase, where the listener is left at the top of the staircase.

## Conservation Status

The Swainson's Thrush is listed as **Least Concern** by the IUCN. The North American breeding population is approximately **100 million** (Partners in Flight 2020), making it one of the most abundant forest songbirds on the continent. However, the Breeding Bird Survey shows a **moderate long-term decline** of approximately **0.7% per year** since 1966, with steeper declines in some western populations. The cumulative loss over six decades approaches **35--40%**.

**Riparian habitat loss is the primary threat.** The Swainson's Thrush's dependence on dense riparian forest and moist understory makes it vulnerable to logging of riparian corridors, agricultural conversion of bottomlands, and urban development of river valleys. In the PNW, riparian buffer regulations have helped maintain breeding habitat along major waterways, but smaller streams and drainages remain poorly protected.

**Neotropical migration risk.** As a long-distance nocturnal migrant, the Swainson's Thrush faces window collisions, tower strikes, light pollution disorientation, and wintering ground habitat loss. Building collisions alone kill an estimated **1--4 billion birds** annually in the US (Loss et al. 2014), with nocturnal migrants disproportionately affected. As one of the most prominent **dusk singers** in PNW forests, the Swainson's Thrush is particularly vulnerable to artificial light at night (ALAN), which disrupts both migratory orientation and the natural dusk singing period.

## Music Theory Mapping

### Harmonic vs. Melodic: The Two Axes of Music

The Catharus genus now occupies both axes of musical thought in the engine:

| Axis | Direction | Strategy | Catharus species |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vertical (harmonic) | Up-down within a phrase | Overtone series intervals -- consonance from mathematical ratios | Hermit Thrush (degree 7) |
| Horizontal (melodic) | Forward through time | Sequential ascending phrases -- aspiration from directional trajectory | Swainson's Thrush (degree 18) |

This is the distinction between a chord and a melody. Between a harmony and a tune. Between knowing what notes sound good together (vertical) and knowing where the line should go next (horizontal). The genus *Catharus* ("pure") has independently evolved representatives of both fundamental dimensions of pitch organization.

### The Dusk Song as Coda

The Swainson's Thrush is a prominent **dusk singer** -- the ascending spiral fills riparian forests as evening light fades. In musical form, the coda is the closing section -- the passage that follows the main body and brings the work to its end. The Swainson's Thrush's dusk song functions as a daily coda for the riparian forest soundscape: the ascending melody that plays as the day closes, the last voice to spiral upward before night arrives.

### Aspiration as Emotional Content

The ascending line carries aspiration, yearning, reaching -- not as metaphor for the bird's intent but as a description of the acoustic phenomenon's effect on any pitch-sensitive listener. Rising pitch = increasing tension = incompleteness = reaching. The bird does not intend to express hope. But the physics of ascending pitch produce the perception of hope in any vertebrate listener. The biology and the aesthetics converge.
