Taxonomy — The Second Catharus

The Swainson’s Thrush is a medium-small passerine of the family Turdidae (thrushes). The genus Catharus — from Greek katharos, “pure” — contains five North American species. At degree 7°, the Hermit Thrush introduced the genus to the engine. Now at degree 18°, the Swainson’s Thrush becomes the second Catharus in the circle. The species epithet ustulatus means “scorched” (Latin), referring to the warm tawny-brown tones of the plumage.

Two well-differentiated subspecies groups, sometimes argued as distinct species: olive-backed (ustulatus group, coastal Pacific — the primary PNW birds) and russet-backed (swainsoni group, interior). They differ in plumage tone, migration routes, and vocalizations. At 6.3–7.5 inches and 23–45 grams. The diagnostic field mark: a bold, buffy eyering and warm buffy face wash — the “spectacled” Catharus.

Bioacoustics — The Ascending Spiral

The Song That Climbs

The Swainson’s Thrush’s song is an upward-spiraling series of fluty phrases, each phrase pitched higher than the last, creating a staircase of sound that literally climbs through pitch space. Each individual phrase consists of several fluty notes — breathy, warm, with internal turns and ornaments. But the defining feature is the inter-phrase pitch trajectory: each successive phrase begins at a higher pitch. The cumulative effect is a melody that ascends.

The Ascending Sequence in Music

The ascending sequence is a compositional device of building tension and aspiration. A musical idea is stated, then restated at a higher pitch level, creating directional momentum experienced as reaching upward. Think of the opening of “The Star-Spangled Banner” (ascending broken triad), Coltrane’s “A Love Supreme” (ascending modal explorations), Ravel’s “Bolero” (theme restated at progressively higher registers). The Swainson’s Thrush’s song IS an ascending sequence — the same compositional device, evolved independently in a biological system.

Riparian Forest — The Stream-Edge Canopy

Habitat as Acoustic Architecture

Dense, moist, deciduous-conifer mixed forest along streams and rivers. Alder-willow-cottonwood corridors with salmonberry understory. The bird sings from concealed mid-canopy perches (10–30 ft). The ascending song sweeps through the vertical acoustic layers of the riparian forest:

The ascending spiral is acoustically functional: it sweeps through the frequency channels of the riparian forest, sampling each layer in turn. A spotlight moving vertically through the canopy.

Music Theory Mapping

Harmonic vs. melodic thinking. The distinction between harmonic and melodic thinking is one of the deepest in music theory. Harmonic thinking is VERTICAL: which notes sound good together, what intervals, what chords. The Hermit Thrush at degree 7°. Melodic thinking is HORIZONTAL: where does the line go next, what is the trajectory through time. The Swainson’s Thrush at degree 18°. The Catharus genus contains both axes. Same genus, different questions. The two fundamental questions of music theory, answered by two thrushes from the genus named “pure.”

The dusk song as coda. The Swainson’s Thrush is a prominent dusk singer — its ascending spiral fills riparian corridors as evening light fades. In musical form, the coda is the closing section. The dusk song is the daily coda of the riparian forest soundscape: the ascending melody that plays as the day closes, the last voice to spiral upward before night arrives. Aspiration as the closing gesture. Hope as the final note.

Ascending pitch as emotional narrative. Rising pitch in vocal communication — across languages and species — is associated with increased arousal, attention-seeking, and incompleteness. A rising inflection is a question. A rising melody is unresolved tension. The Swainson’s Thrush’s ascending song carries this emotional weight: each phrase higher, the trajectory upward, the resolution in the silence at the top of the staircase.
