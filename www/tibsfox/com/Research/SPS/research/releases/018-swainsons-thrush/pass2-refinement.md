### The Ascending Spiral and the Orbit: Climbing Without Falling Back

The Swainson's Thrush's song is an ascending spiral — each phrase higher than the last, a staircase of sound winding upward through pitch space. Pass 2 connects this to Enos's orbital flight and develops the Catharus genus comparison between the Hermit Thrush (degree 7) and the Swainson's Thrush (degree 18).

Enos orbited Earth twice — continuous forward motion around a closed curve, each orbit passing through the same regions but at a slightly different time and orientation. An orbit is an ascending process that never falls back — the spacecraft achieves sufficient velocity to balance gravity's pull, and the result is perpetual forward motion without descent. The Swainson's Thrush's ascending spiral is the acoustic orbit: each phrase is higher, the melody climbs, and unlike the Hermit Thrush's arch form (which ascends AND descends within each phrase), the Swainson's just keeps climbing. The trajectory is upward. The melody does not come back down.

Bull kelp grows from the ocean floor to the surface in a single season — up to 36 meters of vertical growth, the fastest linear growth of any organism in the ocean. The kelp's trajectory is upward: from holdfast on the rocky substrate to float at the surface, a single continuous ascent. The Swainson's Thrush's ascending phrase sequence and the bull kelp's vertical growth trajectory share the same structural principle: persistent upward motion, each increment building on the previous, the organism reaching higher with each cycle.

### The Second Catharus: Harmonic Arch vs. Sequential Ascent

The engine's second Catharus thrush allows a within-genus comparison that reveals two fundamentally different approaches to melodic beauty within the same taxonomic group:

| Dimension | Hermit Thrush (Degree 7) | Swainson's Thrush (Degree 18) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Song contour | Arch — ascending then descending within each phrase | Ascending staircase — each phrase higher than the last |
| Harmonic strategy | Vertical (harmonic series ratios within phrases) | Horizontal (sequential pitch escalation across phrases) |
| Musical analog | Chorale or hymn (vertical harmony) | Ravel *Bolero* / staircase modulation (horizontal ascent) |
| Phrase resolution | Each phrase resolves (arch descends) | Phrases don't resolve individually — the sequence ascends |
| Internal complexity | High (harmonic series intervals within each phrase) | Moderate (fluty ornaments within phrases, but the ascending contour dominates) |
| Emotional quality | "Ethereal" — spiraling in place, contemplative | "Aspirational" — climbing, yearning, reaching |
| Migration | Altitudinal migrant (elevation shifts) | Full neotropical migrant (continental distance) |

The Hermit Thrush's harmonic-series song (degree 7) was the engine's deepest music-theory discovery: a bird that independently arrived at the mathematical ratios underlying tonal music. The Swainson's Thrush's ascending spiral is a different kind of musical principle: not harmonic (vertical, chord-based) but melodic (horizontal, line-based). The Hermit Thrush stacks intervals vertically. The Swainson's Thrush chains phrases horizontally. Both are beautiful. Both are in the genus *Catharus* — "pure." The purity expresses differently.

The Turdidae family now has three entries in the engine:
- **Varied Thrush** (degree 2): AM drone, one note sustained
- **Hermit Thrush** (degree 7): Harmonic series arch, vertical
- **Swainson's Thrush** (degree 18): Ascending spiral, horizontal

Three thrushes, three acoustic strategies, the same family. The Turdidae are the engine's most acoustically diverse family alongside the Parulidae warbler quartet (degrees 14-17). The thrush trio covers the full range from minimalism (one note, degree 2) through mathematical complexity (harmonic series, degree 7) to aspirational ascent (staircase, degree 18).

It is the voice of the crepuscular transition — not nocturnal (the owl, degree 4) and not diurnal (most other species), but positioned at the exact boundary between day and night.
