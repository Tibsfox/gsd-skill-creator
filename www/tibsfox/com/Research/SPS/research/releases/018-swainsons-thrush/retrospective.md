## Purpose

This is the nineteenth release of the PNW 360 Species engine and the eighth species in the **second decade** (degrees 11--20). The Swainson's Thrush introduces **three new structural dimensions** to the circle: (1) the ascending spiral -- the first species whose defining acoustic feature is an upward directional trajectory across successive phrases, each phrase pitched higher than the last, creating a staircase of aspiration; (2) harmonic vs. melodic thinking -- the second *Catharus* thrush enables the engine's first same-genus comparison of vertical (harmonic, degree 7) and horizontal (melodic, degree 18) pitch organization, the two fundamental axes of music theory; (3) the matched pair begins -- degrees 18 (ascending) and 19 (descending) form a paired set of opposite spirals, both *Catharus*, placed at adjacent degrees.

The Swainson's Thrush also extends Turdidae representation to three species (degrees 2, 7, 18) with three distinct acoustic strategies, making the thrush family the most acoustically diverse family in the circle.

## Three Structural Advances

### 1. The Ascending Spiral: Directional Trajectory as Form

The Swainson's Thrush is the first species in the engine whose defining acoustic feature is a **directional pitch trajectory across successive phrases**. Previous species had ascending elements within a single phrase (the flycatcher's upslurred sweep at degree 6, the kinglet's ascending zee-zee at degree 8, the siskin's ascending zhreee at degree 12), but in every case the ascending element was contained within a single vocalization unit. The Swainson's Thrush ascends across MULTIPLE phrases -- each phrase is a step on a staircase, and the staircase is the song.

This introduces **form-level directionality** to the engine. Previous species had phrase-level structure (the Hermit Thrush's arch form, the MacGillivray's AAB). The Swainson's Thrush has song-level structure: the meaning emerges not from any single phrase but from the relationship between successive phrases -- each one higher, the trajectory ascending. The form IS the direction.

### 2. Harmonic vs. Melodic Thinking: The Two Axes

The second *Catharus* thrush creates the engine's first opportunity to compare vertical and horizontal pitch organization within the same genus. The Hermit Thrush (degree 7) navigates pitch vertically: harmonic series intervals, overtone ratios, consonance from mathematical relationships within phrases. The Swainson's Thrush (degree 18) navigates pitch horizontally: sequential ascending phrases, directional trajectory, form from the movement of the line through time.

These are the two fundamental axes of music theory:
- **Vertical axis** = harmony: what notes sound good together
- **Horizontal axis** = melody: where does the line go next

The genus *Catharus* ("pure") has independently evolved representatives of both axes. This is the engine's deepest structural insight about pitch organization to date. The thrush pair (degrees 7 and 18) maps the two axes of the musical staff itself: y-axis (pitch, harmony) and x-axis (time, melody).

### 3. The Matched Pair Begins: Ascending and Descending Spirals

Degrees 18 and 19 form a matched pair -- the ascending and descending spirals, both *Catharus*, placed at adjacent degrees. The Swainson's Thrush spirals upward (aspiration, hope, reaching). The Veery (degree 19) spirals downward (resolution, settling, ethereal descent). Together with the Hermit Thrush at degree 7, three *Catharus* thrushes form a triptych of pitch strategies: harmonic series (7), ascending spiral (18), descending spiral (19). The genus provides the engine's most complete exploration of how pitch can be organized.

## The Maktub Parallel: The Collective Climb

The Part A parallel at degree 18 maps to **Maktub** from S36. The parallel operates on four axes:

4. **Dusk energy.** Maktub was known for late-night performances where energy built as the evening deepened. The Swainson's Thrush is a prominent dusk singer -- the ascending song intensifies as evening light fades. Both are artists of the approaching night.

## What Worked

### 1. The harmonic vs. melodic distinction is the engine's deepest music theory insight
The two axes of pitch organization, mapped to two species from the same genus. This is not analogy -- it is a genuine structural correspondence between biological acoustic strategies and the two fundamental dimensions of pitch in music theory. The vertical (harmonic series, degree 7) and horizontal (ascending sequence, degree 18) axes are the y-axis and x-axis of the musical staff. The genus *Catharus* occupies both.

### 2. Breaking the warbler streak was essential
Four consecutive Parulidae (degrees 14--17) threatened taxonomic monotony. Returning to Turdidae at degree 18 restores family diversity and creates a powerful cross-engine comparison. The thrush family now holds three species with three distinct strategies -- the most acoustically diverse family in the circle.

### 3. The matched pair setup (ascending at 18, descending at 19) is structurally elegant
Adjacent degrees, same genus, opposite trajectories. This is the engine's cleanest paired comparison: a single variable (direction of spiral) isolated against a shared background (genus, timbre, forest habitat). The pair design was declared in advance (degree 19 = Veery, descending spiral), creating an explicit structural anticipation.

### 4. The riparian acoustic architecture analysis adds functional depth
The ascending spiral as a frequency sweep through the vertical layers of riparian forest -- lower phrases in the understory, higher phrases through canopy gaps -- connects the song's form to its habitat's acoustic properties. This is not just "the bird lives in riparian forest." It is "the song's form is adapted to the forest's vertical acoustic structure."

### 5. The dusk singer as coda is a natural form-mapping
The Swainson's Thrush sings most prominently at dusk. The coda is the closing section of a musical work. The daily coda of the riparian forest soundscape -- the ascending melody that plays as the day closes. This is a clean form-level mapping: temporal position in the daily soundscape maps to structural position in musical form.

### 6. The Maktub parallel captures the ensemble-ascent principle
Both are about building through layers. Both are collaborative. Both deliver aspiration through accumulation. The parallel is mechanistic, not metaphorical.

## What Could Be Better

### 1. Still only one non-passerine (the heron at degree 0)
Eighteen of nineteen species are passerines (Passeriformes). The taxonomic diversity gap is now the engine's most critical structural deficit. A woodpecker, raptor, seabird, or waterfowl is overdue. This has been flagged since degree 8 -- ten consecutive retrospectives. **Non-negotiable at degree 20 or earlier.**

### 2. Six neotropical migrants and counting
Six migrants in the engine (degrees 6, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18), five of them in the last five degrees (14--18). The migration bias is severe. A year-round RESIDENT is urgently needed.

### 3. Energy-3 plateau is now four species deep
Four consecutive energy-3 species (degrees 15, 16, 17, 18) without advancing to energy 4 or 5. The energy arc has stalled at 3. High-energy species (woodpecker drumming, raptor scream, corvid call) remain absent.

### 4. Still no percussive / non-vocal sound
All nineteen degrees produce sound via the syrinx. A woodpecker's drumming would introduce mechanical percussion -- a fundamentally different sound-production mechanism. Nineteen syrinx-produced vocalizations. The engine needs percussion.

### 5. Still no marine or true shoreline species
Nineteen degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" and still no species of the Sound itself. The estuary edge (heron, degree 0) remains the only aquatic connection. A seabird or true marine species is needed.

### 6. Riparian habitat recurrence
Degree 18's riparian forest habitat is the sixth riparian-associated ecosystem. While the closed-canopy riparian forest is genuinely distinct from open willow riparian (degrees 15, 16) and marsh edge (degree 17), the continued concentration on water-associated habitats reduces ecosystem diversity. Upland forest, grassland, and marine habitats remain underrepresented.
