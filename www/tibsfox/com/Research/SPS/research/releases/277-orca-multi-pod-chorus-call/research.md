## THE GREETING CEREMONY: WHAT HAPPENS

### When Pods Converge

The orca greeting ceremony is one of the most remarkable social behaviors documented in any non-human species. It occurs when pods of Southern Resident Killer Whales that have been separated meet again:

- **The separation context.** The three Southern Resident pods -- J Pod (~23 individuals), K Pod (~17 individuals), and L Pod (~35 individuals) -- do not always travel together. They separate, travel independently, and reunite. The greeting ceremony occurs at the moment of reunification.
- **The approach.** As two pods approach each other, their behavior changes. They slow down. They begin to vocalize. The approaching groups line up -- a documented behavior in which the members of each group form a rough line, oriented toward the other group, and the two lines face each other across a short gap of water.
- **The charge.** The two groups rush toward each other. This is not aggression -- it is greeting. The charge brings the members of both pods into close physical proximity: orcas surface together, dive together, rub together.
- **The simultaneous vocalization.** During and after the charge, all individuals in both groups vocalize simultaneously. This is the multi-pod chorus: dozens of individual voices producing calls at the same time, creating a layered, complex, chaotic sound of overlapping calls in which individual voices can be distinguished but the whole is something more than the sum of its parts.
- **The duration.** Greeting ceremonies can last from minutes to hours. The extended ceremony involves extended periods of close physical contact, rubbing, surfacing together, and continued vocalization. The social bond is renewed through physical proximity and acoustic connection.
- **The function.** The greeting ceremony appears to serve the function of social bond renewal -- reconnecting individuals who have been separated and reestablishing the social cohesion of the clan. It is a ritual in the functional sense: a repeated, formalized behavior that serves a social purpose beyond its immediate content.

(Sources: Ford 1989 SRKW vocal repertoire; greeting ceremony behavioral documentation; J/K/L pod separation and reunion patterns; lining up and charging behavior; simultaneous vocalization; ceremony duration; social bond renewal function)

## THE SOUND: E=9 IN DETAIL

### What the Multi-Pod Chorus Sounds Like

- **The individual calls.** Each Southern Resident pod has a distinctive call repertoire -- a set of specific vocalizations that are unique to that pod and function as acoustic identity markers. J Pod calls are recognizably different from K Pod calls and L Pod calls. The individual calls are in the range of approximately 1-10 kHz -- within or near the human hearing range.
- **The simultaneous production.** During the greeting ceremony, all individuals are producing calls simultaneously. The J Pod repertoire, the K Pod repertoire, and the L Pod repertoire are all sounding at the same time. The acoustic environment becomes extremely complex: individual voices can be distinguished by trained listeners, but the overall sound is a rich, layered, overlapping chorus.
- **The intensity.** The source level of social vocalizations is lower than echolocation clicks (compare degree 276: 224 dB echolocation to approximately 150-160 dB for social calls). But with dozens of individuals vocalizing simultaneously, the combined acoustic energy in the water is substantial. The intensity of the chorus is a function of numbers, not individual power.
- **The emotional charge.** The E=9 designation for this event is unusual: it is assigned not primarily for acoustic intensity (source level) but for emotional and social intensity. The greeting ceremony is the most emotionally charged behavior in Southern Resident Killer Whale culture. The simultaneous vocalization of an entire clan at the moment of reunion is a sound that carries the weight of social identity, relationship, and belonging.
- **The human experience.** Observers in boats near a SRKW greeting ceremony report the experience as overwhelming. The orcas are physically present in large numbers, close to the surface, close to each other, and vocalizing. The sound through a hydrophone is an extraordinary density of overlapping voices. The visual and acoustic experience together constitute one of the most powerful wildlife observation events available in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Individual call repertoire (J/K/L pod distinctions); simultaneous production analysis; source level of social vocalizations vs. echolocation; combined acoustic energy of multiple simultaneous callers; E=9 emotional designation rationale; human observer testimony)

## THE SOCIAL STRUCTURE: WHY THE CEREMONY EXISTS

### Southern Resident Killer Whale Clan Dynamics

- **The clan.** J, K, and L pods together constitute the Southern Resident Killer Whale (SRKW) clan. The clan is the largest social unit in SRKW society. All 75 (approximately) Southern Resident Killer Whales belong to this single clan. They share overlapping call repertoires and recognize each other as kin despite being members of different pods.
- **The pod.** Each pod is a matrilineal group: led by an eldest female, containing her offspring and their offspring. Pods are stable -- their composition changes slowly through birth and death, not through individual transfer between pods. Adult males typically associate with their birth pod throughout their lives.
- **Pod separation.** The three pods are not always together. They separate and travel independently, sometimes for weeks or months. During separation, each pod is a self-sufficient social unit with its own matriarchs, its own foraging decisions, and its own daily behavior.
- **The reason for reunion.** Pods reunite for various reasons -- convergence in productive Chinook salmon feeding areas, seasonal movements, or simply chance encounters. The greeting ceremony marks the transition from separated to reunited -- from the individual pod unit to the full clan.
- **The greeting as culture.** The greeting ceremony is a cultural behavior: it is specific to Southern Residents (Bigg's killer whales -- transient orcas -- do not perform the same elaborate greeting ceremony), it is transmitted socially from older to younger individuals, and it serves a social function that goes beyond immediate biological necessity. The ceremony is SRKW culture.

(Sources: J/K/L pod composition; SRKW clan structure; matrilineal pod organization; adult male pod fidelity; pod separation patterns; clan reunion dynamics; greeting ceremony as cultural behavior specific to SRKW ecotype)

## THE ACOUSTIC ECOLOGY: CALLS IN CONTEXT

### What the Simultaneous Vocalization Tells Us

- **Dialects and identity.** Each pod has a distinctive dialect -- a set of calls that are unique to that pod and serve as acoustic identity markers. The J Pod dialect is recognizable by trained researchers; K Pod and L Pod have their own. These dialects are learned -- young orcas acquire them from their mothers and pod members. The dialect is transmitted culturally.
- **Acoustic greeting as identification.** The simultaneous vocalization during the greeting ceremony is not random -- each individual produces its own pod's calls. The multi-pod chorus is therefore a simultaneous assertion of acoustic identity: each pod is saying "we are here, this is who we are" in its own dialect while receiving the same assertion from the other pods. The greeting is an acoustic exchange of identity.
- **Cross-pod familiarity.** Despite having different dialects, members of different pods recognize each other's calls. J Pod individuals recognize K Pod calls as belonging to a known group, even though the specific calls are different from their own. This cross-pod acoustic familiarity is evidence of the extended social network that the clan represents.
- **The role of matriarchs.** The oldest females in each pod -- the matriarchs -- are believed to lead the pod's behavioral responses during encounters. In greeting ceremonies, the matriarchs may initiate the approach and charge. Their knowledge of inter-pod relationships -- built over decades of experience -- informs the ceremony's execution.
- **Post-ceremony behavior.** After the initial greeting ceremony, reunited pods often remain in close proximity for extended periods, foraging, resting, and socializing together. The ceremony is the beginning of a period of intensified social interaction.

(Sources: Pod dialect research (Ford 1991); acoustic identity in SRKW dialects; dialect transmission (cultural learning); cross-pod acoustic recognition; matriarch leadership in behavioral responses; post-ceremony behavior)

## E=9: THE ENERGY DESIGNATION

### Why This Is Maximum

The SPS engine uses E=9 only twice: orca breaching (degree 273) and orca multi-pod chorus call (degree 277). The comparison is instructive:

- **E=9 breach (degree 273):** Maximum physical energy. Six tons of mass fully airborne. The physical impact on the water surface. The explosive release of kinetic energy. The breach is maximum energy in the physical register.
- **E=9 chorus (degree 277):** Maximum social energy. The entire SRKW clan -- all three pods -- vocalizing simultaneously at the moment of reunion. The social impact on the acoustic environment. The coordinated release of social identity. The chorus is maximum energy in the social register.
- **Two kinds of maximum.** The engine holds that there are different kinds of maximum -- physical and social -- and that both are genuine extremes. The breach is the most physically intense event an orca can perform. The greeting ceremony chorus is the most socially intense event an orca community can perform. Both are E=9 because both represent the ceiling of their respective registers.
- **Why not E=10?** The engine's scale has no E=10. E=9 is the maximum. Two events in the SPS catalog achieve it. Both are orca. The most physically powerful and the most socially powerful events in the catalog are the same species. The orca occupies both poles of the maximum.

## THE CONTEXT: SALISH SEA

### Where the Ceremony Occurs

- **The Salish Sea.** The Southern Resident Killer Whales' primary range includes the Salish Sea -- the interconnected body of water that includes Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Strait of Georgia. The greeting ceremony happens within this range, typically in productive foraging areas where pods are most likely to converge.
- **San Juan Islands.** The San Juan Islands -- a group of islands in Washington State between the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the Strait of Georgia -- are a primary summer foraging area for SRKWs following Chinook salmon. Greeting ceremonies are frequently observed here by researchers and whale-watching operations.
- **Haro Strait.** The narrow strait between San Juan Island and Vancouver Island is a primary travel corridor and foraging area. The depth and currents of Haro Strait concentrate Chinook salmon and therefore concentrate SRKWs.
- **The acoustic environment.** The Salish Sea is an acoustically complex environment: vessel noise, shipping traffic, wind, and biological sounds all contribute to a complex ambient acoustic field. The SRKW greeting ceremony -- already the most acoustically intense social event in the regional marine biology -- occurs within this complex background. The ceremony's acoustic power (dozens of simultaneous callers) is sufficient to dominate the local acoustic environment even in the presence of substantial vessel noise.

(Sources: SRKW range documentation; San Juan Islands as primary summer habitat; Haro Strait foraging corridor; Salish Sea acoustic environment; vessel noise and SRKW acoustics; ceremony dominance of local acoustic field)

## CONSERVATION NOTE

### The Greeting Ceremony and Population Decline

- **The ~75 individuals.** As of recent surveys, there are approximately 75 Southern Resident Killer Whales in J, K, and L pods combined. The population has been below 100 for decades and has declined since the late 1990s. Each individual in the greeting ceremony is a known, named, photographed whale.
- **The ceremony as demographic indicator.** As the population declines, the greeting ceremony becomes smaller. Fewer individuals means fewer voices in the chorus. The acoustic character of the ceremony -- its density, its complexity, its emotional power -- is directly related to the number of participants. A clan of 75 generates a different ceremony than a clan of 100 would.
- **The Chinook link.** The greeting ceremonies often occur in areas of concentrated Chinook salmon -- the SRKW's primary prey. The pods' movements are driven by salmon availability, and their convergences in productive areas are the occasions for greeting ceremonies. Declining Chinook populations reduce the frequency and location of these convergences.
- **Vessel noise.** SRKW vocalizations -- including the greeting ceremony -- are affected by vessel noise. High levels of ambient noise from shipping and whale-watching vessels raise the acoustic threshold for communication, potentially reducing the effectiveness of the greeting ceremony's acoustic exchange.

(Sources: SRKW population data; population decline trend; individual ID documentation (Center for Whale Research); ceremony size and population size; Chinook dependency and convergence frequency; vessel noise effects on vocalization)
