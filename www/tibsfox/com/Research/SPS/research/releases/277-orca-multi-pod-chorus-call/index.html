SPS Maximum Tied — E=9

Two E=9 events in the SPS catalog. Orca breaching (degree 273): the physical maximum — six tons of mass fully airborne. Orca multi-pod chorus (degree 277): the social maximum — an entire clan vocalizing simultaneously at the moment of reunion.

The greeting ceremony is the most emotionally charged acoustic event in Pacific Northwest marine biology. J Pod, K Pod, and L Pod converging. Dozens of individual voices. Simultaneous, overlapping, layered. Each pod's dialect present at once. The entire Southern Resident clan singing together. The engine has never documented anything more socially powerful than this.

The Greeting Ceremony

When Southern Resident Killer Whale pods that have been separated encounter each other, a formalized behavioral sequence begins. The pods approach each other. They line up — members of each group forming a rough line facing the other group across a short gap of water. Then they charge: rushing together, surfacing in close proximity, rubbing against each other. And they vocalize — all individuals simultaneously.

The Ceremony Sequence

1. Approach. The pods detect each other — acoustically and visually. Their behavior changes. They slow, orient toward each other, begin to vocalize.

2. Lining up. Members of each pod form a rough line, facing the other pod. Two lines of orcas face each other across open water. A documented behavior specific to greeting context.

3. The charge. Both groups rush toward each other. Not aggression — greeting. Members of both pods surface in close physical proximity. Rubbing. Physical contact. The social bond renewed through touch.

4. The chorus. All individuals vocalize simultaneously. J Pod dialect, K Pod dialect, L Pod dialect — all sounding at once. Overlapping, layered, multi-voiced. The most acoustically complex social event in Salish Sea biology.

5. Extended association. After the initial ceremony, reunited pods often remain together for hours or days. The ceremony is the beginning of intensified social contact.

The Three Pods

J Pod

~23 individuals. The most frequently observed pod in the Salish Sea. Year-round residents. Home range includes Puget Sound and Juan de Fuca Strait. Distinctive J-dialect call repertoire.

K Pod

~17 individuals. The smallest of the three pods. Frequently travels offshore in winter. K-dialect calls distinguishable from J and L. Often associated with L Pod in summer.

L Pod

~35 individuals. The largest pod. Several subgroups. L-dialect has multiple recognized variants. Summer presence in San Juan Islands. The largest single contributor to the chorus.
