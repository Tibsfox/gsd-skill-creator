## THE MERLIN -- *FALCO COLUMBARIUS*

### North America's Compact Falcon

- **Taxonomy.** Kingdom: Animalia. Phylum: Chordata. Class: Aves. Order: Falconiformes. Family: Falconidae. Genus: *Falco*. Species: *columbarius*. The species name *columbarius* means "dove-like" in Latin -- a reference to the Merlin's flight style, which is swift and direct like a dove, and to early confusion about the bird's family membership by some early naturalists.
- **Common name origin.** The name "Merlin" predates scientific naming and derives from Old French *esmerillon*, which referred to a small falcon used in falconry. In European falconry tradition, the Merlin was a lady's falcon -- small enough to be carried on the wrist by a woman (while larger falcons like the Peregrine were assigned to nobility of both sexes). The association with the Arthurian wizard Merlin is linguistic coincidence.
- **Size.** The Merlin is one of the smallest falcons in North America and the world. Adult birds are 24-33 cm (9.5-13 in) in length, with a wingspan of 53-73 cm (21-29 in). Weight: 125-300 grams (4.4-10.6 oz) -- in the same weight range as a small songbird. For comparison: the Peregrine Falcon weighs 400-1,500 grams. The Merlin is approximately one-quarter the Peregrine's size.
- **Appearance.** Males are slate-blue above (in the taiga subspecies most common in the PNW) with orange-buff streaking below. Females and immatures are brown above with buff-brown streaking below. The face lacks the heavy mustache marks of the Peregrine. The tail has several dark bands.
- **Three subspecies in North America.** The taiga Merlin (*F.c. columbarius*) -- the most widespread; the prairie Merlin (*F.c. richardsonii*) -- paler, of the Great Plains; and the black Merlin (*F.c. suckleyi*) -- the darkest subspecies, resident on the wet Pacific coast from Washington State south to California.

## THE CALL -- RAPID CHATTER ki-ki-ki

### The Voice of the Small Falcon

The Merlin's call is distinctive and energetic:

- **The sound.** The primary vocalization is a rapid, staccato series of high-pitched notes: ki-ki-ki-ki or kee-kee-kee-kee. The calls are delivered quickly -- 10-15 notes per second is common in the most intense sequences. The rapid-fire tempo distinguishes the Merlin's call from the Peregrine's slower, more deliberate cacking and the Kestrel's more evenly-spaced killy sequence.
- **Frequency.** The Merlin's call operates in the 3,000-7,000 Hz range -- higher than the Peregrine, appropriate to the Merlin's smaller syrinx (the bird's vocal organ). Higher-frequency calls from smaller birds is a consistent pattern in avian acoustics: the size of the resonating chamber determines the fundamental frequency.
- **Function.** The ki-ki-ki is used primarily in territorial defense and alarm -- a rapid burst of calls when a potential threat is detected or when rivals approach the nesting territory. The call is also used in courtship; a mated pair will exchange rapid sequences of the call during the breeding season.
- **Energy at E=5.** The Merlin's call is E=5 -- not the peak energy of the Peregrine or Bald Eagle at their most aggressive, but assertive and insistent. The rapid-fire quality gives the call a kind of percussion -- the individual notes are short, but the combined burst is present and demanding.
- **The assertiveness paradox.** The Merlin is a small bird, but it is aggressive out of proportion to its size. Merlins will attack crows, ravens, and even raptors much larger than themselves if their territory is threatened. The rapid ki-ki-ki chatter is the vocalization of this assertiveness -- the small bird that refuses to be small.

## ECOLOGY AND HUNTING

### A Small Falcon with Big Ambitions

- **Hunting style.** The Merlin is a bird-hunting specialist. Unlike the Kestrel (which primarily eats insects and small mammals) or the Peregrine (which hunts by high-speed stooping), the Merlin pursues its prey -- primarily small songbirds -- in direct, sustained pursuit at low altitude. The Merlin is a sprinter, not a diver. It corners songbirds in the open, using speed and maneuverability to cut off escape routes.
- **Prey.** Small birds make up the majority of the Merlin's diet: sparrows, starlings, warblers, pipits, larks, and shorebirds. The Merlin will also take large insects (dragonflies, butterflies) and occasionally small rodents, but birds are the primary target.
- **The surprise factor.** The Merlin hunts without the dramatic stoop of the Peregrine or the hovering approach of the Kestrel. It simply appears -- a compact, swift bird materializing from low cover at high speed. The prey bird often has no warning. This hunting approach requires the Merlin to be in the right place and moving at full speed before the prey is aware.
- **Habitat selection.** In the PNW, Merlins use open and semi-open habitats: coastal areas (particularly during migration), open farmland and grasslands, forest edges, and urban parks with open grass. The Merlin avoids dense forest -- the hunting approach requires open space to pursue prey.
- **Urban adaptation.** Merlins have shown increasing tolerance of urban environments. In some Canadian and northern US cities, Merlins nest in parks and tree-lined neighborhoods, hunting House Sparrows and European Starlings (both non-native species). The urban Merlin is a post-20th-century adaptation.

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST DISTRIBUTION

### Winter in the PNW

The Merlin's relationship with the Pacific Northwest is primarily as a wintering and migrant species:

- **Breeding range.** Merlins breed across the boreal forest zone of North America -- from Alaska and Yukon through Canada to Newfoundland. They also breed in the northern Rocky Mountains and in the Pacific coastal forests of British Columbia and Washington State (the black Merlin subspecies *F.c. suckleyi* is a resident breeder in coastal Washington and Oregon).
- **PNW winter presence.** The taiga Merlin subspecies, which breeds across Canada's boreal forest, migrates south in fall and winters in the western US, including Washington State. Merlins are regular winter residents in the Puget Sound lowlands, the Columbia Plateau, and coastal Washington. Their numbers increase in October-November and decline in March-April.
- **The black Merlin.** The Pacific coast subspecies (*suckleyi*) is darker than any other North American Merlin -- nearly black above and heavily streaked below. This subspecies is resident year-round in the coastal PNW, breeding in mature forest near the coast and hunting in coastal grasslands, beaches, and headlands. The black Merlin is the PNW's residential Merlin, even if not the most commonly encountered.
- **Migration.** During fall migration, Merlins move along ridges and coastlines. Ridges that concentrate Raptors (like Raptors Ridge in the Cascades) record Merlin movements in October. The coast of Washington State sees migrant Merlins moving south in fall and north in spring.

(Sources: Merlin breeding range; boreal forest; taiga subspecies migration; black Merlin suckleyi; Pacific coast resident; PNW winter distribution; migration timing)

## FALCONIDAE -- THE FAMILY IN SUMMARY

### What the Falcon Family Represents

Having covered three Falconidae entries (231-233), the engine can now summarize the family:

- **Family identity.** Falconidae is the family of true falcons, kestrels, hobbies, and merlins. Approximately 65 species worldwide. The family's defining characteristics: pointed wings for high-speed flight, a notched "tomial tooth" on the upper bill for severing prey's spinal column, and a direct hunting approach that emphasizes speed. Falconidae is not closely related to Accipitridae (hawks, eagles) despite superficial similarities; genetic studies place falcons closer to parrots and songbirds than to hawks.
- **PNW representatives.** In the Pacific Northwest, Falconidae is represented by: Gyrfalcon (rare winter visitor), Peregrine Falcon (resident), Prairie Falcon (interior east of Cascades), Merlin (winter resident and migrant), and American Kestrel (resident).
- **The falcon arc in SPS.** Degrees 231-234 cover four Falconidae entries: Peregrine, Prairie, Merlin, and Kestrel. This is a thorough documentation of the PNW's falcon fauna. After degree 234, Falconidae is complete in the engine.
- **The speed family.** Falconidae is the speed family of raptors. The Peregrine Falcon's 240+ mph stoop is the fastest motion of any animal on Earth. The Merlin's sustained 30+ mph pursuit is fast for its size. The Kestrel's hovering precision is a different kind of speed. Speed, precision, and direct aggression are the falcon family's signature.

(Sources: Falconidae taxonomy; family characteristics; tomial tooth; Falconidae-Accipitridae genetic distance; PNW Falconidae species; SPS falcon arc 231-234)

## CONSERVATION

### Merlin Status and PNW Population Trends

- **Current status.** The Merlin is listed as Least Concern by the IUCN. North American populations are stable and, in some regions, increasing -- particularly as urban Merlins adapt to city environments and benefit from increased House Sparrow and starling prey populations.
- **Historical context.** Like many raptors, the Merlin experienced population declines in the mid-20th century due to DDT-induced eggshell thinning. The DDT ban in 1972 allowed recovery. The Merlin's recovery has been strong in most portions of its range.
- **PNW populations.** Washington State and Oregon have stable Merlin populations. The black Merlin (*suckleyi*) is less well-studied than the other subspecies; its coastal forest habitat faces pressure from timber harvest and development. Monitoring of this subspecies is ongoing.

(Sources: Merlin IUCN status; North American population trends; DDT recovery; urban Merlins; black Merlin conservation status; PNW monitoring)
