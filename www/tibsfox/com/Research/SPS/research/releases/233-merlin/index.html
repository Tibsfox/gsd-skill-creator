FALCON CLOSE — 3rd of 4 FALCONIDAE

Degrees 231-234 constitute the engine's Falconidae arc: Peregrine (231), Prairie Falcon (232), Merlin (233), American Kestrel (234). The Merlin closes the three-degree arc of the core Falconidae run. Size gradient: large (Peregrine) → medium (Prairie) → small (Merlin). The Kestrel at 234 is the final family member. The falcon era ends at degree 234.

The Compact Falcon

The Merlin is the Pacific Northwest's smallest regularly-occurring falcon and one of the most aggressive birds relative to its size. Adults weigh 125-300 grams — roughly the same as a large songbird — yet they hunt other birds in direct pursuit, will attack crows and ravens many times their size, and deliver a rapid, insistent ki-ki-ki chatter that announces their presence with complete confidence. Size and ferocity are not equivalent in the falcon family.

Acoustic Profile — Rapid Chatter

Frequency: 3,000–7,000 Hz
Rate: 10–15 notes per second in alarm
Pattern: rapid staccato bursts, each 3–20 notes

Rapid machine-gun tempo distinguishes Merlin from Peregrine (slower kak) and Kestrel (evenly-spaced killy). Function: territorial alarm, courtship exchange, rival warning. Higher frequency than Peregrine due to smaller syrinx.

The Hunting Strategy

The Merlin hunts by direct pursuit — not the Peregrine's dramatic stoop from altitude, not the Kestrel's hovering. The Merlin simply appears at full speed, materializing from low cover, cutting off a sparrow's or pipit's escape routes. The prey bird often has no warning. This ambush-sprint approach requires the Merlin to be perfectly positioned before the prey is aware.

Diet: primarily small birds (sparrows, starlings, warblers, pipits, larks). Large insects in season. The Merlin is a bird-hunter first, as its rapid pursuit flight is optimized for catching other birds in the open.

The Black Merlin — PNW Resident

Washington State hosts a resident subspecies: the black Merlin (F.c. suckleyi), the darkest North American Merlin — nearly black above, heavily streaked below. This subspecies inhabits the wet Pacific coast year-round, breeding in mature coastal forest and hunting in coastal grasslands, beaches, and headlands. The taiga Merlin (F.c. columbarius) is the winter visitor, arriving from the boreal forest in October.
