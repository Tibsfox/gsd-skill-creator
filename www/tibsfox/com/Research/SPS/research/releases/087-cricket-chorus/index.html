INSECTA Enters — A Fourth Class Joins the Engine

After birds (degrees 0–60 with substitutions), mammals (degrees 61–85), and an amphibian (degree 86), a FOURTH CLASS enters the engine. Insecta — insects. The cricket chorus is the first insect in the engine. Four classes now represented: Aves, Mammalia, Amphibia, Insecta. The class diversity accelerates: one class for 60 degrees (Aves), one for 25 (Mammalia), one for 1 (Amphibia), now a fourth at degree 87.

Rhythmic Chirp Field Chorus — 88th Acoustic Element

The cricket chorus is not a single animal — it is the entire field singing together. Hundreds or thousands of male crickets stridulating in a dry meadow on a summer night. The sound is rhythmic — each cricket produces a regular “chirp-chirp-chirp” by rubbing its wings together. The chorus is a field chorus — the combined, overlapping rhythmic chirps of an entire population. E=4 — the collective volume fills the meadow. A parallel to the Pacific Treefrog (degree 86) — both collective breeding sounds, both chorus phenomena, both E=4.

Three taxonomic levels added in a single entry. The family jump from Hylidae (Amphibia) to Gryllidae (Insecta) crosses the class boundary again — second consecutive class crossing (85→86 Mammalia→Amphibia, 86→87 Amphibia→Insecta).

The Sound: Rhythmic Chirp Field Chorus (88th Acoustic Element)

The call: Male crickets produce their characteristic chirp through stridulation — rubbing a scraper on one wing against a file on the other. The sound is a rhythmic, regularly spaced chirp — “chirp-chirp-chirp” at regular intervals. Individual chirps are brief (approximately 50–100 ms) with regular pauses between them. The chirp rate is temperature-dependent — faster in warmer conditions (Dolbear’s Law) [1, 2, 3].

Why E=4: (1) Individual calls carry well — crickets are audible at 10–20 meters. (2) The collective chorus is overwhelming — hundreds or thousands chirping simultaneously. (3) Fills the entire meadow soundscape on summer nights. (4) Continuous for hours. (5) E=4 reflects the collective volume and persistence — the sound dominates the habitat. Like the treefrog chorus (86), E=4 through collective amplification.

Taxonomy: Gryllidae (Family-Level Entry)

Note: This is a family-level entry, not a single species. The cricket chorus represents the collective sound of field crickets (Gryllidae) as a group. Multiple species contribute to the chorus in PNW meadows, including Gryllus spp. (field crickets) and Acheta spp. (house crickets) [1, 2].

Classification: Class Insecta — INSECTA ENTERS. Fourth class. Order Orthoptera — NEW ORDER (crickets, grasshoppers, katydids). Family Gryllidae — NEW FAMILY (38th). True crickets [1, 2, 3].

Ecology: The Summer Field Chorus

Habitat: Dry meadow. Grasslands, fields, meadow edges, lawns. Crickets inhabit the ground layer and low vegetation. Dry meadow contrasts with the marsh habitat of the Pacific Treefrog (86) — different habitat, similar acoustic strategy [1, 2, 3].

Activity: Primarily nocturnal and crepuscular. The chorus peaks on warm summer nights. Males call from concealed positions on the ground or in low vegetation.

Dolbear’s Law: The chirp rate of crickets is correlated with ambient temperature. Count the chirps in 14 seconds and add 40 to get the approximate temperature in Fahrenheit. This relationship (T = N/4 + 40, where N = chirps per minute) was first described by Amos Dolbear in 1897 [3].

Diet: Omnivorous. Plant material, fungi, other insects. Decomposers and scavengers.

Predators: Birds, bats, spiders, frogs (including Pacific Treefrog), small mammals. Crickets are prey for many engine species.
