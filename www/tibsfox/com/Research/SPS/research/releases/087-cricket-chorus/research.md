## THE SOUND: RHYTHMIC CHIRP FIELD CHORUS -- 88th ACOUSTIC ELEMENT

### The Chirp

The cricket chirp is produced by **stridulation** -- rubbing a scraper on one forewing against a file on the other [1, 2, 3]:

**Individual chirp:** A brief, sharp pulse (approximately 50-100 ms) repeated at regular intervals. The chirp is a rapid series of wing-stroke pulses grouped into a single audible "chirp." Field crickets typically produce 1-4 chirps per second, depending on temperature.

**Frequency:** Approximately 3,000-5,000 Hz for field crickets. Higher for tree crickets (2,000-4,000 Hz). The frequency is determined by wing structure and is relatively constant for a given species.

**The chorus:** When hundreds or thousands of males chirp simultaneously, the individual rhythmic chirps overlap into a **continuous field chorus**. Unlike the treefrog chorus (which is a dense wall of sound), the cricket chorus retains its **rhythmic quality** -- the regular pulse is still perceptible even in large choruses. The result is a pulsing, rhythmic, omnidirectional soundscape.

### Dolbear's Law

The chirp rate is **temperature-dependent** [3]:

**T(°F) = N/4 + 40** (where N = chirps per minute for the snowy tree cricket)

More practically: count chirps in 14 seconds and add 40 to get approximate Fahrenheit temperature. This relationship was first described by Amos Dolbear in 1897. The cricket is a **living thermometer** -- its metabolism and muscle contraction rates are directly tied to ambient temperature. Warmer nights = faster chirps. Cooler nights = slower chirps.

### Why E=4

1. **Individual chirps carry well** -- audible at 10-20 meters for field crickets.
2. **The collective chorus is overwhelming** -- hundreds or thousands chirping simultaneously.
3. **Fills the entire meadow soundscape** on summer nights -- the dominant sound.
4. **Continuous for hours** -- the chorus persists from dusk through night.
5. **E=4 through collective amplification** -- same mechanism as the treefrog chorus (86).

- **Krek-krek night chorus** (degree 86, Pacific Treefrog) -- also a collective chorus, also E=4. But the treefrog chorus is a wall of sound (no gaps); the cricket chorus is rhythmic (pulsing). Both collective. Different texture.

## ECOLOGY: THE SUMMER FIELD CHORUS

### Habitat

Dry meadow [1, 2, 3]:

- **Primary:** Grasslands, dry meadows, fields, pastures, meadow edges.
- **Also:** Lawns, gardens, forest clearings, roadsides.
- **Contrast with treefrog (86):** Treefrog = marsh (wet). Cricket = meadow (dry). Different habitats, similar acoustic strategies.
- **Shared with Snowshoe Hare (73):** Both inhabit meadow edges and transitional habitats.

### Breeding Behavior

- **Calling:** Males stridulate to attract females. The loudest, most persistent callers tend to attract more mates.
- **Timing:** Peak chorus on warm summer nights. Activity declines with cooler temperatures.
- **Competition:** Males compete acoustically. Some species exhibit satellite male behavior -- non-calling males intercept females attracted to calling males.

### Diet and Predators

- **Diet:** Omnivorous. Plant material, fungi, other insects, carrion. Decomposers and scavengers.
- **Predators:** Birds, bats, spiders, frogs (including Pacific Treefrog), lizards, small mammals. Crickets are a foundational prey item in meadow food webs.
