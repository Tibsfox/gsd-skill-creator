FIRST WIND ENTRY IN ENGINE — AEOLIAN ACOUSTICS — THE SINGING TREE

Picea sitchensis. Sitka spruce. The Pacific Northwest's coastal giant. In storm winds of 40-60+ mph, the needle-laden branches of old-growth Sitka spruce produce a deep, organ-like moan — aeolian tones generated by thousands of individual needles vibrating in the Karman vortex street. This is not metaphor. It is physics: each needle acts as a resonating element; the ensemble of thousands produces a complex chord in the low-to-mid audible range. The Olympic coast forests are the most storm-exposed conifer stands in the continental United States. This is where the singing is loudest.

The Physics of the Moan

Aeolian tone frequency:
f = 0.198 × v / d

f = frequency (Hz), v = wind velocity (m/s), d = object diameter (m). For a Sitka spruce needle approximately 1 mm in diameter at storm wind speeds of 20 m/s (~45 mph): f ≈ 3,960 Hz — upper mid-range. The deep moan comes from the ensemble: larger-diameter branches producing lower frequencies, smaller needles producing higher, the complex spectrum spanning 50–500+ Hz.

The Karman vortex street forms behind each needle as wind separates around it — alternating vortices that generate pressure fluctuations at a characteristic frequency. It is the same physics as an aeolian harp. Each Sitka spruce needle is a tiny string in an instrument with thousands of strings, each tuned by its diameter and the local wind velocity at that moment.

"The impression that the tree is alive and breathing comes from the gustiness of coastal storm wind. Each gust swells the moan; each lull diminishes it. The forest breathes at the storm's rhythm."

Sitka Spruce -- The Acoustic Instrument

Needle structure

Sharp-pointed, stiff, 1.5-2.5 cm long. Spiral arrangement on branches. Thousands per branch. Each needle vibrates independently; the ensemble is the instrument.

Branch stiffness

Stiffer than willow or birch. Wind energy transfers to needle vibration rather than branch movement. Stiffness is acoustic: it means the vibration goes into sound, not motion.

Trunk diameter

Old-growth: 2-4 m. The massive trunk accelerates wind via Bernoulli effect, driving higher-velocity air through the canopy. The trunk amplifies the instrument.

Olympic dimensions

Hoh Rainforest specimens: 50-75 m tall, 2-4 m trunk diameter. 140-170 inches of rain per year. The most storm-exposed spruce forest in the continental US.

Geographic Context

Sitka spruce grows in a narrow coastal strip — typically no more than 80-100 km inland — from northern California to southeastern Alaska. The Olympic Peninsula's western valleys (Hoh, Queets, Quinault, Bogachiel) host some of the densest old-growth Sitka spruce stands in the world. These valleys face the open Pacific directly; storms arriving from the Gulf of Alaska make landfall here at full marine fetch, with sustained winds of 50-80+ mph and storm durations of days.

Olympic Coast Storm Track

Gulf of Alaska low-pressure systems → North Pacific fetch → Olympic Peninsula landfall. The Sitka spruce forests of the Olympic coast are the first tall vegetation that Pacific storm winds encounter after crossing thousands of miles of open ocean. The trees have adapted to this exposure over thousands of years. The adaptation produces the specific acoustic properties that generate the moan.

Engine Position

Degree 249 is the SPS engine's first aeolian entry — the first sound produced by moving air interacting with a physical structure. Previous abiotic entries have involved liquid water (river, waterfall, rain on leaves) or were at the edge of the biological-abiotic boundary. Wind through Sitka spruce is pure aeolian acoustics: no living process produces the sound; the sound is entirely a product of physics, structure, and atmosphere.
