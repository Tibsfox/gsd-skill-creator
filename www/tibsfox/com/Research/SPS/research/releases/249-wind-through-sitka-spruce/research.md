## SITKA SPRUCE -- THE SINGING TREE

### *Picea sitchensis* and Its Acoustic Properties

The Sitka spruce is not merely the tree through which the wind happens to blow -- it is specifically suited by its biology and structure to produce the sounds documented here:

- **Needle structure.** Sitka spruce needles are sharp-pointed, stiff, and individually quite small (approximately 1.5-2.5 cm long). They radiate outward from the branch in a spiral arrangement, creating a dense, uniformly distributed surface area around each branch. This needle arrangement is the acoustic key: when wind moves through a Sitka spruce branch, it encounters a very large number of small, stiff, individually vibrating elements. Each needle acts as a tiny resonator, and the ensemble of thousands of needles creates a complex, multi-layered vibration.
- **Branch structure.** Sitka spruce branches are relatively stiff compared to, for instance, willow or birch. They do not bend dramatically in wind; they hold their position while the needles vibrate around them. This stiffness means that the wind energy is not absorbed in branch movement but transferred to needle vibration -- which is what produces sound.
- **Trunk diameter.** Large Sitka spruce (old-growth specimens) have trunks 2-4 meters in diameter. The massive trunk creates a physical boundary that forces wind to accelerate around the tree -- the Bernoulli effect -- creating pressure differentials that drive the air through the canopy at higher velocity than the ambient wind. The large trunk is an acoustic amplifier.
- **The "singing" quality.** The deep, organ-like moan produced by Sitka spruce in storm wind is not metaphor -- it is physics. When wind passes through the needle arrangement at certain velocities, it creates aeolian tones: sounds produced by the Karman vortex street (alternating vortices that form behind each needle as air separates). The frequency of these tones depends on the needle diameter and wind velocity. For Sitka spruce needles at storm wind speeds, the frequencies fall in the low-to-mid audible range: 50-500 Hz, with the fundamental resonances producing the moaning quality.
- **Why "organ-like."** Pipe organs produce sound when air is forced through pipes of specific dimensions -- the pipe diameter and length determine the frequency. Sitka spruce needles function similarly: each needle is effectively a tiny pipe, and the ensemble of thousands of needles produces a complex chord of organ-like tones. The analogy is acoustic, not metaphorical.

(Sources: Sitka spruce needle structure; needle dimensions and spiral arrangement; branch stiffness vs. willow/birch; trunk diameter and Bernoulli acceleration; aeolian tone physics; Karman vortex street; frequency analysis 50-500 Hz; organ analogy as acoustic description)

## THE PHYSICS OF WIND THROUGH VEGETATION

### Aeolian Acoustics

The sound produced by wind through Sitka spruce is an instance of aeolian acoustics -- the physics of sound produced by wind interacting with solid objects:

- **Aeolian tones.** The aeolian harp is one of the oldest acoustic instruments: a set of strings of different gauges stretched in the wind, each producing a different pitch as the wind velocity causes alternating vortices to form on the string's lee side. The Karman vortex street -- the name for the alternating vortex pattern -- generates pressure fluctuations at a frequency that depends on the object's diameter and the wind velocity. For a circular object: f = 0.198 × v / d, where f is frequency in Hz, v is wind velocity in meters per second, and d is object diameter in meters.
- **Sitka spruce needle calculation.** For a Sitka spruce needle approximately 1 mm in diameter (0.001 m) in a 20 m/s wind (approximately 45 mph, a moderate coastal storm):
  - f = 0.198 × 20 / 0.001 = 3,960 Hz
  - This is in the upper mid-range of human hearing -- a relatively high-pitched tone.
  - At lower wind speeds (10 m/s, approximately 22 mph): f = 1,980 Hz.
  - The deep moan comes from the interaction of many different needle sizes, branch diameters, and local wind velocities, producing a complex spectrum that includes lower frequencies from larger-diameter elements and higher frequencies from smaller elements.
- **The complex spectrum.** What the ear perceives as a "moan" or "organ chord" is a complex acoustic spectrum with energy distributed across many frequencies. The specific quality of Sitka spruce sound (vs. Douglas fir or cedar) comes from the specific distribution of element sizes -- needles of specific diameter range, branches of specific diameter, in specific spatial arrangements.
- **Wind velocity dependence.** As wind velocity increases, the aeolian frequencies shift upward. At low wind speeds (10-15 mph), the sound is a soft whisper. At storm wind speeds (40-60+ mph), the sound becomes a sustained, deep moan. The change in character between light wind and storm wind is not just an increase in volume -- it is a change in the frequency profile of the sound.
- **Gustiness and amplitude modulation.** Coastal storm winds are typically gusty -- they fluctuate in velocity on timescales of seconds to minutes. This gustiness creates amplitude modulation of the aeolian tones: the sound swells with each gust and diminishes in the lulls between gusts. This amplitude modulation is what creates the "breathing" quality of wind-in-tree sound -- the impression that the tree is alive and breathing.

## THE GEOGRAPHIC CONTEXT -- OLYMPIC COAST AND OREGON COAST

### Where This Sound Exists

The specific geography of Sitka spruce wind sound:

- **Sitka spruce range.** Sitka spruce is one of the most range-restricted major conifers in North America. It grows in a narrow coastal strip, typically no more than 80-100 km inland, from northern California through Oregon, Washington, British Columbia, southeastern Alaska, and the Aleutian Islands. The species requires the specific microclimate of the Pacific coast: high moisture (precipitation often 100-300+ inches per year), cool temperatures, maritime fog, and the salt-laden ocean air that most inland conifers cannot tolerate.
- **Olympic Peninsula.** The Olympic Peninsula in Washington State has one of the densest Sitka spruce concentrations in the world. The Hoh Rainforest (within Olympic National Park) receives approximately 140-170 inches of rain per year -- one of the wettest temperate rainforests in the Western Hemisphere. Sitka spruce here grow to extraordinary dimensions: old-growth specimens of 50-75 meters (165-250 feet) tall and 2-4 meters in trunk diameter. The Hoh Rainforest is where the quintessential Sitka spruce wind sound exists.
- **Storm exposure.** The Olympic coast faces the open Pacific directly. Storms arriving from the Gulf of Alaska and the central Pacific make landfall on the Olympic coast with their full marine fetch -- wind speeds of 50-80+ mph, wave heights of 20-40+ feet, sustained wind patterns lasting days. The Sitka spruce forests of the Olympic coast are the most storm-exposed conifer stands in the continental United States.
- **Quinault, Queets, Hoh valleys.** The major river valleys on the western slope of the Olympic Peninsula -- Quinault, Queets, Hoh, Bogachiel -- all host large Sitka spruce stands in their lower reaches. These are the primary locations for the sounds documented in this degree. The valleys funnel storm winds through dense spruce forest, creating the conditions for sustained, high-intensity aeolian sound.
- **Oregon coast.** The Oregon coast also has significant Sitka spruce stands, particularly around Cannon Beach, the Tillamook State Forest, and the Oregon Coast Range. The sound is similar to the Olympic coast but with different landscape characteristics (more mixed forest, different valley geometry).

(Sources: Sitka spruce range; coastal strip limitation; Olympic Peninsula geography; Hoh Rainforest precipitation; old-growth dimensions; Olympic coast storm exposure; Gulf of Alaska storm track; Quinault/Queets/Hoh valleys; Oregon coast spruce stands)

## CULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE OF THE SINGING TREE

### The Sound in Human Experience

Wind through Sitka spruce has a specific place in the cultural landscape of the Pacific Northwest:

- **Indigenous relationship.** For the Quinault, Hoh, Quileute, Makah, and other Pacific Northwest coastal peoples, the sound of wind through Sitka spruce is a fundamental feature of their homeland's acoustic environment. The coastal rainforest is not a backdrop -- it is the landscape in which these peoples have lived for millennia. The "singing" of the forest in storm is not a novel experience or a scenic attraction; it is a constant feature of life in this place.
- **The storm as experience.** Pacific coast storms are not incidental weather events for coastal communities -- they are recurring, powerful presences that shape how people live, build, travel, and relate to the landscape. The sound of the forest in a storm is part of the full experience of a Pacific coast storm: the smell of the salt air, the physical impact of the wind, the noise of the ocean surf, and the moaning of the spruce.
- **Naturalist records.** Early naturalists and explorers documenting the Olympic Peninsula frequently noted the sound of wind in the forest as one of the most distinctive acoustic features of the place. The descriptions -- "moaning," "singing," "organ-like" -- appear across multiple sources from the 19th and 20th centuries, demonstrating that the sound is distinctive enough to be consistently remarked upon.
- **The aeolian harp tradition.** The aeolian harp -- strings set in the wind to produce tones -- was a popular instrument and poetic image in Romantic literature and philosophy. Samuel Taylor Coleridge wrote "The Eolian Harp" (1795); Percy Bysshe Shelley referenced aeolian sound in multiple poems. The cultural tradition of wind-as-music provides a framework for understanding why the Sitka spruce wind sound has always been described in musical terms.
- **Modern recording.** Field recording artists have documented Sitka spruce wind sound extensively. The Pacific Northwest soundscape tradition includes numerous recordings of Olympic coast storms, coastal rainforest wind events, and the specific sound of Sitka spruce in storm. These recordings circulate in natural sound recording communities and in the broader field of acoustic ecology.

(Sources: Quinault/Hoh/Quileute/Makah relationship to coastal forest; storm as recurring presence for coastal communities; naturalist and explorer records; aeolian harp Romantic tradition; Coleridge and Shelley; field recording tradition; acoustic ecology)
