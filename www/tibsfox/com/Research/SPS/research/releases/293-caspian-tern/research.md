## THE CASPIAN TERN -- THE LARGEST TERN

### Body, Form, and Presence

The Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*, formerly *Sterna caspia*) holds a specific ecological and acoustic position:

- **World's largest tern.** The Caspian Tern is not merely large for a tern -- it is substantially larger than any other tern species, with a wingspan of 50-57 inches and a body length of 19-24 inches. For scale: a Caspian Tern's wingspan exceeds that of many gulls. The species was once classified in its own genus (*Hydroprogne*) to distinguish it from all other terns; recent taxonomy sometimes merges it back into *Sterna* but the monotypic-genus designation captures the bird's outlier size.
- **The blood-red bill.** The Caspian Tern's most visually striking feature: a massive, bright blood-red to orange-red bill with a dark subterminal spot near the tip. The bill is not merely colored for identification -- it is a functional tool. Caspian Terns are aggressive predators that plunge-dive for fish from heights and pursue prey with force. The large bill enables capture of fish too large for smaller tern species.
- **Black cap.** The Caspian Tern in breeding plumage carries a full black cap that extends from bill to nape. Unlike many tern species, the Caspian's cap does not develop a streaked or mottled pattern in winter -- it retains the solid black cap through much of the year. The black cap + blood-red bill is the field mark combination that makes a perched or flying Caspian Tern unmistakable.
- **Gull-like presence.** Because of its size, the Caspian Tern is often associated with gulls in mixed-species flocks at estuary and coastal sites. It flies with the heavier, more powerful wingbeats of a large bird, not the buoyant swallow-like flight of smaller terns. A Caspian Tern in flight reads as something between a gull and a tern -- the evolutionary logic of scale made visible.

## THE KA-ARR CALL -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Loud, Raspy, Direct

The Caspian Tern's vocalization is among the most recognizable in the Laridae family:

- **The ka-arr.** A loud, low-pitched, raspy, emphatic single call -- rendered as "ka-arr," "kaar," "kraa," or "kraah" in various field guides. The syllable is approximately one second in duration with a characteristic descending rasp -- it begins at a higher frequency and drops in pitch over the syllable's duration, creating the raspy quality. The fundamental frequency is lower than most tern calls (approximately 800-2,000 Hz, versus Common Tern's keerr at 2,000-4,000 Hz). Lower pitch + larger body = the powerful, projecting quality that distinguishes the Caspian from other terns.
- **Carrying distance.** The ka-arr call carries across open estuary habitat with exceptional distance. A calling Caspian Tern can be heard from 300-500 meters in calm conditions -- significantly farther than smaller tern species. The combination of large body, powerful syrinx, and lower-frequency vocalization produces a call that cuts through wind and ambient noise at estuarine sites.
- **Colony vocal behavior.** At colonial nesting sites (East Sand Island at the Columbia River mouth being the most significant PNW site), Caspian Terns call continuously during active colony periods: partner recognition calls during incubation exchange, food-begging calls from chicks (high-pitched, insistent), alarm calls during predator approach, and the steady ambient ka-arr of a colony going about its business. The overall acoustic character is lower-pitched and more raucous than Common Tern colonies at the same sites.
- **E=6 calibration.** The E=6 placement reflects the Caspian Tern's loud, imposing call versus the slightly more piercing, higher-pitched, staccato urgency of the Common Tern colony at E=7. The Caspian's ka-arr is powerful but not rapid-fire -- it is a single emphatic call rather than a barrage. The energy is substantial, direct, and projected; it is not the constant-fire urgency of the highest tern alarm registers.

## PACIFIC NORTHWEST ECOLOGY -- ESTUARY AND COAST

### East Sand Island and the Columbia River Mouth

The Caspian Tern's Pacific Northwest ecology is inseparable from a specific site:

- **East Sand Island.** Located at the mouth of the Columbia River where it meets the Pacific Ocean (Clatsop County, Oregon), East Sand Island is one of the most ecologically and management-intensively documented colonial waterbird sites in North America. The island hosts Caspian Terns, Common Terns, Forster's Terns, Brandt's Cormorants, Double-crested Cormorants, and large gull species in varying proportions across years. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers manages the island as part of the Columbia River navigation channel maintenance.
- **The Caspian Tern management controversy.** For much of the 1990s-2010s, East Sand Island hosted the largest Caspian Tern colony in the world -- up to 10,000 breeding pairs -- attracted by the enormous Columbia River estuary food supply. However, the colony's predation on juvenile Chinook and steelhead salmon smolts (endangered populations) created one of the most significant wildlife management conflicts in the Pacific Northwest. Managers undertook hazing, habitat reduction, and colony relocation programs to disperse the Caspian Tern colony away from the smolt migration corridor. The colony has been substantially reduced and partially relocated to alternative sites (Fern Ridge Reservoir, OR; Lower Klamath NWR, CA).
- **Puget Sound and Salish Sea.** Caspian Terns breed in smaller numbers at various Puget Sound and Salish Sea sites. They are conspicuous at Commencement Bay (Tacoma), the Nisqually River delta, Padilla Bay, and other major estuarine systems. Their foraging behavior -- hovering over shallow water before plunge-diving -- is visible from shore at many PNW estuary sites throughout summer.
- **Migration routes.** Pacific Northwest Caspian Terns are migratory. They arrive from wintering grounds along the California, Mexico, and Central American coast in April-May, breed through summer, and depart in August-September. The Columbia River mouth serves as both a breeding site and a major migratory staging area.

(Sources: East Sand Island ecology; Columbia River management documents; USFWS Caspian Tern management plan; Puget Sound waterbird data; eBird Washington Caspian Tern distribution)

## FORAGING BEHAVIOR -- HOVER AND PLUNGE

### The Predator of the Estuary

The Caspian Tern's foraging strategy is essential to understanding its ecology:

- **Hover-and-plunge.** Like most terns, the Caspian Tern forages by hovering over water at height (3-10 meters), spotting a fish near the surface, and plunge-diving to capture it. The large body and powerful bill allow capture of fish up to approximately 20 cm in length -- significantly larger prey than smaller tern species can handle.
- **Diet.** The Pacific Northwest Caspian Tern's diet is dominated by juvenile salmonids (Chinook, coho, steelhead smolts), juvenile eulachon (Columbia River smelt), and various small forage fish (juvenile American shad, Pacific herring, surf smelt). The salmonid predation is the ecological basis for the East Sand Island management controversy: at the colony's peak, researchers estimated that Columbia River Caspian Terns consumed millions of PIT-tagged juvenile salmon per year during the spring smolt migration.
- **Cooperative foraging.** Caspian Terns sometimes forage in loose aggregations at productive sites -- not the tight cooperative fishing of pelicans, but a loose concentration of individual hunters working the same productive area. The combined visual spectacle (many large terns hovering and plunging) and acoustic output (multiple ka-arr calls overlapping) at a productive estuary foraging site is one of the Pacific Northwest's most dramatic wildlife experiences.

(Sources: Caspian Tern diet studies; Columbia River salmonid ecology; East Sand Island foraging data; USFWS predation assessments; cooperative foraging behavior in Laridae)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### The Management Story

The Caspian Tern's Pacific Northwest legacy is inseparable from the management controversy:

- **The conflict documented.** The East Sand Island Caspian Tern situation is the Pacific Northwest's most significant example of the tension between colonial waterbird conservation and endangered fish species recovery. The birds are native, protected, part of a functioning ecosystem; the fish are endangered, culturally significant, subject to major recovery investments. The management response -- hazing, habitat removal, colony relocation -- is controversial within the conservation community. The Caspian Tern is both a conservation success story (large, stable global population) and a management subject in the Pacific Northwest (local colony reduced for salmon recovery reasons).
- **Global context.** The Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*) has a global range -- it breeds on every continent except Antarctica. The Pacific Northwest population is one regional expression of this cosmopolitan species. The global population is stable and not threatened; the Pacific Northwest management controversy is a local, specific conflict arising from the particular combination of salmon recovery needs and tern colony behavior at the Columbia River mouth.
- **Acoustic legacy.** The Caspian Tern's ka-arr call is one of the Pacific Northwest's most recognizable bird sounds at estuary and coastal sites in summer. The loud, raspy, projecting quality -- lower-pitched than other tern calls, carrying across open water -- is a signature of the estuary soundscape at peak summer season.

(Sources: Caspian Tern global range; IUCN conservation status; East Sand Island management history; PNW conservation policy; acoustic ecology of PNW estuaries)
