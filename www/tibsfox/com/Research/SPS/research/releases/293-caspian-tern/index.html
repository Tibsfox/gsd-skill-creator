World's Largest Tern — Laridae Postscript

The Laridae arc closed at degree 292 (four entries, 289–292). The Caspian Tern arrives at 293 as the fifth Laridae entry — the family's most physically imposing and ecologically most significant Pacific Northwest member. Wingspan exceeding many gulls. Blood-red bill. Ka-arr call projecting 300–500 meters across open estuary. East Sand Island at the Columbia River mouth: once the largest Caspian Tern colony in the world.

Acoustic Profile — Ka-Arr

Ka-arr call: Single emphatic descending call, approximately 1 second duration. Harsh, raspy quality. Fundamental frequency 800–2,000 Hz — lower than the Common Tern's keerr (2,000–4,000 Hz). The acoustic shift is the size shift: larger body, larger syrinx, lower fundamental.

Carrying distance: 300–500 meters in calm conditions — exceptional for a tern-sized bird. The combination of large body and lower frequency gives the call unusual projection across open water and mudflat habitat.

Colony ambient: At East Sand Island and other colony sites, multiple ka-arr calls overlapping at different phases create a continuous lower-pitched raucous soundscape. More guttural and less staccato than Common Tern colony (292).

E=6: Loud and projecting, but the single-call structure (rather than continuous rapid-fire alarm barrage) and lower frequency produce a driving rather than piercing quality. E=6 as powerful and direct.

The Largest Tern — Body and Field Marks

The Caspian Tern (Hydroprogne caspia, sometimes placed in Sterna) is the world's largest tern species: wingspan 50–57 inches, body length 19–24 inches. The wingspan exceeds many gull species. The blood-red to orange-red bill is massive and laterally compressed — a functional predation tool that enables capture of fish too large for smaller terns. The black cap is solid and dark in all seasons, one of the most distinctive field marks in the Laridae family. The bird reads as something between a gull and a tern: the size and flight weight of a gull, the forked tail and plunge-diving behavior of a tern.

East Sand Island — Columbia River Mouth

East Sand Island at the mouth of the Columbia River (Clatsop County, Oregon) was for much of the 1990s–2000s the largest Caspian Tern colony in the world — up to 10,000 breeding pairs. The colony's predation on juvenile Chinook and steelhead salmon smolts during the spring migration created one of the Pacific Northwest's most significant wildlife management conflicts: the bird is native and protected; the fish are endangered and subject to major recovery investments. USFWS hazing, habitat reduction, and colony relocation programs have substantially reduced the East Sand Island colony and partially dispersed it to alternative sites (Fern Ridge Reservoir, Oregon; Lower Klamath NWR, California). The Caspian Tern's Pacific Northwest story is inseparable from this management history.

Estuary Ecosystem — First Estuarine Entry

The Caspian Tern at degree 293 is the SPS engine's first entry explicitly documenting estuary habitat: the transition zones between river and ocean where productivity is highest, where foraging birds concentrate, where the Pacific Northwest's most important fish runs pass through. Estuaries — the Nisqually River delta, Padilla Bay, Commencement Bay, the Columbia River mouth — are the region's most productive waterbird foraging habitats. The Caspian Tern's hover-and-plunge foraging on juvenile salmonids and forage fish makes it the estuary's apex avian predator.
