## What Worked

1. **The Tufted Puffin is the correct first Alcidae entry.** The most visually iconic alcid in the Pacific Northwest, the species that draws the bird tours and fills the field guide covers. The family's most charismatic representative opens the Alcidae chapter. Future alcid entries are calibrated against this first appearance.
2. **The acoustic transition from Laridae to Alcidae is the engine's most dramatic frequency shift in the coastal seabird sequence.** Common Tern keerr (2,000-4,000 Hz) → Caspian Tern ka-arr (800-2,000 Hz) → Tufted Puffin grunt (200-800 Hz). A two-octave drop over three consecutive entries. The taxonomy is audible in the frequency progression.
3. **The breeding plumage description fully documents the species' field identity.** The four-color face -- black body, white face, gold tufts, blood-red bill -- is one of the most distinctive field marks of any North American seabird. The winter transformation (all-dark, tufts shed, bill dulled) demonstrates the seasonal range of the species' appearance.
4. **Two consecutive Alcidae entries (294 and 295) is the correct structure.** Tufted Puffin (diurnal) + Rhinoceros Auklet (nocturnal) = the full temporal range of the outer coast alcid colony documented over two degrees.

## Discoveries

1. **Pacific Northwest Tufted Puffin populations are declining.** The engine's first Alcidae entry documents a species of regional conservation concern. Some major colony sites have experienced significant population reductions. Climate change effects on prey distribution are the primary hypothesized driver. The species' global population is stable; the regional trend is concerning.
