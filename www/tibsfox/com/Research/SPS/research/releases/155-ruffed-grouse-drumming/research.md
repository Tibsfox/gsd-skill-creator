### Mechanical Sound Evolution

The four mechanical sounds show a progression in mechanism:

1. **Goldeneye (124):** Sound produced by air flowing over modified feathers DURING FLIGHT. The bird must be flying. Aeroelastic vibration.
2. **Mourning Dove (150):** Sound produced by a modified feather DURING TAKEOFF. Brief, transitional.
3. **Rock Pigeon (152):** Sound produced by wings striking each other ABOVE THE BODY during takeoff. Physical collision.
4. **Ruffed Grouse (155):** Sound produced by wings beating AIR WHILE STATIONARY. The bird stands still on a log and creates sound through wing compression of air alone. No flight, no feather modification, no wing-to-wing contact.

The progression: in-flight > takeoff > takeoff contact > stationary. From sound as byproduct of locomotion to sound as pure display -- the wing as musical instrument, the log as stage.

## E=5 -- THE ACCELERATING POWER

### Why E=5

The Ruffed Grouse drumming registers at E=5:

- **Acoustic impact:** The drumming is surprisingly loud and far-carrying for a non-vocal, non-airborne sound. The low-frequency thumps propagate through the forest understory effectively. Audible at 200-400 meters under favorable conditions.
- **Acceleration intensity:** The defining characteristic of the drumming is its acceleration. The beats start slow (approximately 2 per second) and accelerate to a blur (approximately 20 per second) over 8-11 seconds. The acceleration creates an intensity that builds inexorably.
- **Physical effort:** The drumming requires enormous physical effort. The grouse braces its tail against the log and beats its wings forward and upward with maximum force, approximately 50 times per drumming bout. The energy expenditure is comparable to a brief sprint.
- **Frequency and persistence:** Males drum repeatedly throughout the breeding season (April-June), with peak activity at dawn and dusk. A single male may perform hundreds of drumming bouts per day during peak breeding.
- **Territorial and mating function:** The drumming serves both to establish territories and attract mates. The sound carries the information of the male's presence, fitness, and territorial claim.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Rusch et al. 2000; engine internal analysis)

## THE DRUMMING -- HOW IT WORKS

### The Mechanics of the Wing-Drum

The Ruffed Grouse drumming display is one of the most studied mechanical sounds in ornithology:

- **The log:** The male selects a fallen log (the "drumming log") as his display stage. Log selection is critical -- the male needs a log of appropriate diameter (25-40 cm), height above the forest floor, and location (in dense cover but with clear sightlines). Males may use the same log for multiple years.
- **Posture:** The male stands erect on the log, braces his stiffened tail against the log surface for support, and cups his wings slightly forward.
- **The stroke:** Each "drum beat" is produced by a single, powerful forward-and-upward wing stroke. The wing does not strike the body or the log -- it strikes AIR. The sound is produced by the rapid compression of air in the cupped wing as it accelerates forward.
- **The acceleration pattern:**
  1. **Slow phase:** The first 3-4 beats are slow, spaced approximately 0.5 seconds apart. *Thump... thump... thump...*
  2. **Acceleration:** The beats increase in speed gradually. *Thump.. thump.. thump. thump. thump thump thump*
  3. **Blur:** The final beats are so rapid (approximately 20 per second) that they merge into a continuous low-frequency rumble or "roar." *thumpthumpthumpthrrrrrr*
  4. **Stop:** The drumming ends abruptly. Total bout duration: approximately 8-11 seconds.
- **Frequency:** The individual thumps are low-frequency -- approximately 40-100 Hz fundamental. At the peak of the acceleration, the repetition rate itself creates a "buzz" frequency of approximately 20 Hz, which is at the threshold of human hearing.
- **The air compression model:** Research has shown that the sound is produced by the sudden forward acceleration of the cupped wing, which creates a localized vacuum behind the wing followed by an air rush to fill the vacuum. This produces a low-frequency pressure pulse -- essentially a small sonic boom at each wing stroke.

### The Distant Motor Analogy

The Ruffed Grouse drumming is often described as sounding like "a distant motor starting" or "someone starting a chainsaw far away." This is remarkably accurate:

- **The slow initial beats** sound like the first compression strokes of a motor.
- **The acceleration** mirrors the increasing RPM of a motor warming up.
- **The final blur** sounds like a motor running at full speed.
- **The abrupt stop** sounds like the motor being switched off.

This mechanical analogy is so strong that many people hearing the drumming for the first time do not identify it as a bird sound at all.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Rusch et al. 2000; Archibald 1974; engine internal analysis)

## LOG SELECTION AND TERRITORY

### The Drumming Log

The fallen log is central to the Ruffed Grouse's acoustic display:

- **Site fidelity:** Males are highly faithful to their drumming logs. A male may use the same log for multiple years, defending it against rival males. The loss of a drumming log (through decay or disturbance) forces the male to find a new site, which can be a significant disadvantage.
- **Log characteristics:** Preferred logs are 25-40 cm in diameter, 3-10 meters long, elevated above the forest floor, and located in dense cover with some visual sightlines. The log provides a stable platform for the bracing and wing-beating of the display.
- **Sound amplification:** The log may function as a resonating platform, amplifying the low-frequency vibrations of the drumming. Research suggests that the log's mass and the air space beneath it contribute to sound projection.
- **Territory center:** The drumming log is the center of the male's territory. The territory radiates outward from the log, and the drumming serves as the acoustic boundary marker.
- **Multiple logs:** Some males maintain 2-3 drumming logs within their territory, using different logs at different times.

### Habitat Requirements

The Ruffed Grouse requires:

- **Young forest:** Dense, regenerating forest with aspen, birch, alder, and mixed conifers. The species benefits from disturbance (fire, logging) that creates young forest patches.
- **Dense understory:** Thick ground cover for concealment from predators. The grouse is a ground-dwelling bird that relies on camouflage and cover.
- **Aspen connection:** In much of its range, the Ruffed Grouse is strongly associated with quaking aspen. Aspen buds are a critical winter food source, and aspen forests provide the ideal habitat structure.
- **PNW range:** In the Pacific Northwest, Ruffed Grouse are found in forests with alder, birch, cottonwood, and mixed conifers, particularly in riparian corridors and regenerating clearcuts.

(Sources: Rusch et al. 2000; Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Sibley 2014; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Forest Floor -- The Accelerating Wing-Drum

The Ruffed Grouse drumming is the focal sound for this entry:

**The Drumming (mechanical):**
- **Trigger:** Breeding season (April-June, peak April-May). Dawn and dusk primarily. Male territorial/courtship display from drumming log.
- **Mechanism:** Wings beat air while bird stands stationary on log. Tail braced against log for support. Cupped wing forward-stroke creates air compression/vacuum cycle producing low-frequency pressure pulses.
- **Character:** Accelerating series of deep thumps -- starts slow, builds to a blur. "A distant motor starting." Low-frequency. More felt than heard at distance.
- **Frequency:** Individual beats: approximately 40-100 Hz fundamental. Peak acceleration: beat repetition rate of ~20/second creates 20 Hz buzz at threshold of hearing.
- **Duration:** Complete bout: 8-11 seconds. Approximately 50 wing beats per bout.
- **Amplitude:** E=5. Audible at 200-400 meters. Low-frequency propagation through forest understory. Not extremely loud in absolute dB but effectively far-carrying due to low frequency.
- **Acceleration pattern:** ~2 beats/second (initial) accelerating to ~20 beats/second (final). The acceleration is the signature characteristic.
- **Seasonal peak:** April-May. Dawn and dusk peaks. Hundreds of bouts per day during peak breeding.

**Vocalizations (secondary):**
- **Clucking:** Contact and alarm calls. Short, sharp notes.
- **Hissing:** Distress or aggressive display when cornered.
- **Peeping:** Chick contact calls.

(Sources: Cornell Lab of Ornithology; Rusch et al. 2000; Archibald 1974; engine internal analysis)
