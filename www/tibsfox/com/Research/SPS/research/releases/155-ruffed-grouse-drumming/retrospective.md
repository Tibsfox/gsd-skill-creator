*Degree 155 retrospective complete. RUFFED GROUSE DRUMMING at the one hundred fifty-fifth degree -- THE ACCELERATING WING-DRUM. In the young forest where aspen and alder grow thick and fallen logs rest on the forest floor, a stocky bird with a black tail band and a ruff of neck feathers stands on a log that it has used for years -- perhaps the same log its father used before it. The bird braces its stiffened tail against the bark, cups its wings slightly forward, and begins. Thump. A pause. Thump. Another pause. Thump. The pauses shorten. Thump thump. Shorter still. Thump thump thump. Now the beats are coming fast, the wings striking forward and upward with maximum force, each stroke compressing the air in the cup of the wing and creating a localized vacuum that collapses with a low-frequency pressure pulse -- a small sonic boom, forty to a hundred hertz, more felt in the chest than heard by the ears at any distance. The acceleration continues: ten beats per second, fifteen, twenty, and the individual thumps merge into a continuous low-frequency roar, a distant motor running at full speed, a sound that seems to come from the forest itself. Then silence. Abrupt stop. Eight to eleven seconds from the first thump to the final roar to the sudden silence. The fourth mechanical sound in the engine. The first three were all associated with flight: the Goldeneye's wing whistle produced by modified primaries during flight at degree 124, the Mourning Dove's wing whistle from a modified feather during takeoff at degree 150, the Rock Pigeon's wing clap from wings striking each other during takeoff display at degree 152. But the Ruffed Grouse produces its mechanical sound while standing perfectly still. The wings strike nothing but air. The log provides the stage, the tail provides the brace, the cupped wing provides the instrument, and the air provides the drum surface. From locomotion byproduct to pure display. The mechanical sound series reaches its most refined expression: the wing as percussion instrument, the air as drum, the forest floor as concert hall.*
