Ruffed Grouse — Drumming

Bonasa umbellus (Phasianidae) — E=5 — Accelerating Wing-Drum on Log
THIRD PHASIANIDAE. FOURTH MECHANICAL SOUND. Wings beat air while stationary on a fallen log. Acceleration: 2 beats/sec to 20 beats/sec over 8–11 seconds. “A distant motor starting.” 40–100 Hz fundamental. Air compression creates pressure pulses — a small sonic boom at each wing stroke. The log as stage. The wing as instrument.

The Wing-Drum — A Distant Motor Starting

Male stands on fallen log, braces tail, cups wings forward. Each stroke: powerful forward-and-upward wing beat striking nothing but air. Cupped wing creates localized vacuum followed by air rush — a low-frequency pressure pulse. ~50 beats per bout. Slow start: thump... thump... thump... Acceleration: thump thump thump thumpthumpthrrrrrr. Final blur: ~20 beats/second, merging into continuous low-frequency roar. Abrupt stop. 8–11 seconds.

Fourth Mechanical Sound in the Engine

Four mechanical sounds now documented:
124: Common Goldeneye wing whistle (in flight, modified primaries).
150: Mourning Dove wing whistle (takeoff, modified feather).
152: Rock Pigeon wing clap (takeoff, wings strike each other).
155: Ruffed Grouse wing-drum (stationary, wings beat air).
Progression: in-flight → takeoff → takeoff contact → stationary. From byproduct of locomotion to pure display.

The Drumming Log — The Stage

Log selection is critical: 25–40 cm diameter, 3–10 meters long, elevated, dense cover. Males use the same log for years. The log may function as a resonating platform — mass and air space beneath amplify low frequencies. Territory radiates outward from the log. The log is the center. The drumming is the boundary.
