The Orca Arc Closes — 224 dB Source Level

Seven degrees. Degrees 270–276: all Orcinus orca. The most sustained single-species documentation in the SPS catalog. From pod call (270, vocal identity, social communication) to echolocation burst (276, biosonar, hunting perception). Voice to body to perception. The Southern Resident Killer Whale’s complete behavioral and sensory repertoire.

The echolocation click train at up to 224 decibels source level — among the loudest biological sound production on Earth. 20–100 kHz, primarily ultrasonic. 200–1,000+ clicks per second. The invisible intensity: most of this is inaudible and invisible to human observers. The 224 dB is happening in the water. The orca hunting with sound.

The Physics: 224 dB

Source level: up to 224 dB re 1 μPa @ 1m (underwater acoustic reference). Among the highest source levels measured for any biological sound. Near the sperm whale (230–236 dB), the loudest known biological sound. Frequency: 20–100+ kHz. Primarily ultrasonic. Click rate: 200–1,000+ per second during active hunting. Duration per click: microseconds.

The inverse square law: at 1 meter, 224 dB; at 100 meters, ~140 dB. The click must be powerful enough to reach a Chinook salmon at depth, reflect, and return to the orca with detectable energy. This is the power required to make echolocation work at hunting range.

How It Works

The orca produces clicks using the nasal sac system and the phonic lips (also called "monkey lips") — specialized anatomical structures in the nasal passage that produce the click when controlled air movement causes them to vibrate. The sound is channeled through the melon — the fat-filled acoustic lens in the forehead — which shapes the click into a directed forward beam.

The click beam travels through the water, illuminating the environment ahead of the orca. When it encounters a Chinook salmon, acoustic energy reflects back. The returning echo enters the orca through acoustic fat in the lower jaw, which channels it to the middle ear. The time delay between click and echo gives distance. The frequency characteristics of the echo give size, shape, and composition. The orca is seeing with sound.

Voice and Biosonar — Same Anatomy, Two Functions

The pod call (degree 270) and the echolocation click (degree 276) are both produced by the same basic anatomical system: the nasal complex and phonic lips. Social communication (0.5–20 kHz, within human hearing range) and active acoustic sensing (20–100+ kHz, ultrasonic). The same apparatus serves two entirely different functional purposes.

Orcas can produce vocalizations and echolocation clicks simultaneously — using the same anatomy for both at once. The arc from degree 270 (voice) to degree 276 (biosonar) documents both poles of a single anatomical system. The orca talks and hunts with the same instrument.
