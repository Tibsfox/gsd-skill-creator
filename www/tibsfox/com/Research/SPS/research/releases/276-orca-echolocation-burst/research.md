## ECHOLOCATION: THE PERCEPTUAL ARCHITECTURE

### How the Orca Hunts with Sound

Echolocation is not supplementary to the orca's sensory experience -- it is the primary tool for hunting in the Pacific Northwest's waters:

- **The need.** The Salish Sea and its connected waters are not clear. Glacial runoff, phytoplankton blooms, suspended sediment, and the turbidity of active water systems reduce visual range significantly. Chinook salmon -- the SRKW's primary prey -- do not conveniently hold still in well-lit water. To find, track, and pursue salmon in the conditions of the Salish Sea, the orca needs a sensory system that works at distance, at depth, and in the dark. Echolocation is that system.
- **The click.** An echolocating orca produces individual acoustic clicks using a specialized anatomical structure: the melon (a fat-filled acoustic lens in the forehead) combined with the nasal sac system and the phonic lips (sometimes called "monkey lips"). The click is produced by the controlled movement of air through the phonic lips, shaped by the melon into a directional beam. Each click is extremely brief -- on the order of microseconds -- but extremely loud.
- **The beam.** The orca's click beam is directional: it is projected forward from the melon in a focused acoustic field. The animal can control the direction of the beam to some extent by adjusting its head orientation. The beam allows the orca to "illuminate" specific regions of the water in front of it with acoustic energy.
- **The return.** When the click beam encounters an object -- a school of Chinook salmon, a reef wall, another orca -- some acoustic energy is reflected back toward the source. The returning echo reaches the orca's lower jaw, which is filled with acoustic fat that channels the sound to the middle ear. The orca's auditory system processes the returning echo: the time delay between click and echo gives distance; the frequency characteristics of the echo give information about the reflecting object's size, shape, and composition.

(Sources: Au 1993 cetacean echolocation; phonic lips / monkey lips anatomy; melon acoustic lens; directional beam production; acoustic fat in lower jaw; time-delay distance measurement; echo analysis)

## THE PHYSICS: 224 dB SOURCE LEVEL

### The Loudest Biological Sound

- **224 dB.** Source levels for orca click trains have been measured at up to 224 decibels referenced to 1 micropascal at 1 meter (dB re 1 μPa @ 1m). This is the standard reference for underwater acoustics. To put this in context: the decibel scale in water is referenced differently from the decibel scale in air, making direct comparisons complex. But 224 dB underwater is one of the highest source levels measured for any biological sound production on Earth. The sperm whale (Physeter macrocephalus) produces the loudest known biological sound at approximately 230-236 dB; the orca's 224 dB is close to this level.
- **Why so loud?** Echolocation at range requires sufficient acoustic power in the transmitted signal to produce a detectable echo from a distant target. Chinook salmon at depth in turbid water are small targets. To detect them reliably -- to resolve their position, track their movement, and guide a pursuit -- the orca needs an extremely powerful signal. The 224 dB source level is the power needed to make echolocation work at the ranges and in the conditions where SRKWs hunt.
- **Inverse square law attenuation.** Sound attenuates with distance. At the source (1 meter), 224 dB. At 10 meters, approximately 44 dB less. At 100 meters, approximately 84 dB less. The click reaches the salmon, reflects, and returns -- the round-trip attenuation is substantial. The original signal must be extremely powerful to produce a detectable echo.
- **Safe for the orca?** The orca's auditory system is protected from its own click production. The anatomy of the sound-producing system and the acoustic fat channels directs the click forward while protecting the inner ear from the source-level energy. The orca is not deafened by its own sonar.

(Sources: Au 1993 orca click source level measurements; 224 dB reference; sperm whale comparison; inverse square law attenuation; orca auditory protection anatomy; biosonar range and sensitivity)

## THE CLICK TRAIN: FREQUENCY AND RATE

### What the Echolocation Burst Sounds Like

- **Frequency range.** Orca echolocation clicks are broadband, with energy concentrated in the range of approximately 20-100 kHz. The center frequency of the click is typically around 20-30 kHz for most SRKW clicks, with harmonics extending above 100 kHz. Most of this is ultrasonic -- above the human hearing range (approximately 20 Hz to 20 kHz). What humans hear when an orca echolocates (if close enough) is the low-frequency components of the click: a sharp tick or snap.
- **Click rate.** During active hunting, orcas produce click trains at rates of approximately 200-1,000+ clicks per second. This is rapid -- the clicks are produced faster than the human ear can resolve as individual sounds. The click train sounds continuous when heard by a human; the orca's auditory system can process the individual clicks and their echoes in sequence.
- **The burst-pulse.** A specific type of click train production -- the burst-pulse -- involves even higher click rates produced in a rapid burst. Burst-pulses are associated with social contexts and communication as well as echolocation. The distinction between echolocation clicks and social sounds in orca communication is not always clear; the same anatomical system produces both.
- **What the orca hears.** The orca's auditory system is vastly more sensitive to the frequencies of its own click production (20-100 kHz) than the human ear is. The orca processes the returning echoes at these ultrasonic frequencies with a resolution and precision that has no human perceptual equivalent. The orca "sees" with sound -- an acoustic image of the environment constructed from the echoes of its own clicks.

(Sources: SRKW click frequency range; click rate during hunting; burst-pulse analysis; orca auditory sensitivity; acoustic image construction; ultrasonic frequency processing)

## HUNTING: THE APPLIED FUNCTION

### Echolocation in Action

- **Finding salmon.** Chinook salmon (cross-reference degree 237) are the SRKW's primary prey. Finding a Chinook salmon in the Salish Sea using echolocation requires detecting a target approximately 0.7-1.0 meters long at distances of tens to hundreds of meters. The orca's biosonar system can resolve targets of this size at these ranges.
- **The pursuit.** Once a salmon is detected and located by echolocation, the orca can track its movement by maintaining continuous click train production and processing the changing echo patterns. The time delay of the echo decreases as the orca approaches the salmon; the orca uses this changing delay to guide its pursuit.
- **Depth.** Chinook salmon can be at considerable depth during their Salish Sea residence -- not always accessible at or near the surface. The orca can dive and echolocate at depth, extending its sensory range vertically as well as horizontally.
- **Group hunting.** SRKWs often hunt in coordinated groups. Echolocation from multiple individuals in a hunting pod creates overlapping acoustic fields. The coordination of multiple biosonar systems in a group hunt has been observed but is not fully understood -- the orcas may be sharing acoustic information or simply using individual echolocation in proximity.
- **The Chinook connection.** The SRKW echolocation system is specifically tuned to the acoustic properties of its primary prey. The relationship between predator and prey acoustics (what Chinook salmon look like to orca echolocation) is an active area of research. Chinook swim bladders are acoustically reflective structures that may be particularly detectable to SRKW biosonar.

(Sources: SRKW Chinook hunting; salmon target detection; tracking via changing echo delay; depth hunting; coordinated group hunting; prey acoustic properties; swim bladder reflectivity)

## ECHOLOCATION VS. VOCALIZATION: TWO ACOUSTIC SYSTEMS

### The Distinction

- **Vocalization.** The pod call (degree 270) is a vocalization -- a social acoustic signal used for communication. Vocalizations are in the range of approximately 0.5-20 kHz, within or near the human hearing range. They are the orca's "language" -- the signals used for pod recognition, coordination, and social bonding.
- **Echolocation.** The click train (degree 276) is biosonar -- an active acoustic sensing system. Click trains are primarily ultrasonic (20-100+ kHz), far above the human hearing range. They are not communications -- they are questions sent into the water and answered by echoes.
- **Same anatomy, different uses.** Both vocalizations and echolocation are produced by the same basic anatomical system (the nasal complex and phonic lips). The orca uses the same acoustic apparatus for two entirely different functional purposes. This anatomical economy is remarkable.
- **Simultaneous production.** Orcas can produce vocalizations and echolocation clicks simultaneously -- they are using the same anatomical system for two functional purposes at the same time. The mechanisms for this simultaneous production are an area of active research.

(Sources: Vocalization vs. echolocation frequency ranges; same anatomy, different uses; simultaneous production; orca acoustic anatomy; communication vs. biosonar distinction)
