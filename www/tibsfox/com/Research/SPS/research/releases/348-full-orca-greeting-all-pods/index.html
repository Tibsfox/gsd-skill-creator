The Greeting Ceremony

When two or more pods meet after separation, they engage in the greeting ceremony: the pods swim toward each other, pause at the surface 50–100 meters apart, then mingle with body contact — rubbing, spy-hopping, lobtailing, breaching. The acoustic accompaniment is the maximum vocalization event the community is capable of producing.

During the full greeting, all members of all participating pods call simultaneously. Three pod dialects interweave in the same acoustic space. The calls are pod-specific: SRKW ears can distinguish J Pod calls from K Pod calls from L Pod calls even when all three are produced simultaneously. The event is not noise — it is a complex multi-layered acoustic structure. The complete cultural vocabulary of the community, produced simultaneously.

Why E=10

Rarity. K Pod and L Pod range widely along the outer Pacific coast. A full tri-pod greeting ceremony — not just shared waters, but active greeting with surface behaviors and maximum vocalization — may occur only a handful of times per year.

Scale. ~73 individuals is the entire community. There are no other SRKW. When all three pods greet, it is not a sample. It is the whole.

Irreplaceability. The three-dialect call system represents decades of cultural transmission from mothers to calves. If any pod were lost, this acoustic event could not be reconstructed. E=10 acknowledges what cannot be replaced.

The Chinook connection. The full tri-pod reunion is driven by salmon: when Fraser River Chinook runs are adequate to concentrate all three pods in the same waters, the greeting occurs. The event is a visible indicator of salmon ecosystem health. When all three pods converge, the salmon are running.
