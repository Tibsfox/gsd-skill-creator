## THE SRKW COMMUNITY -- WHO THEY ARE

### J Pod, K Pod, L Pod: The Three Families of the Salish Sea

The Southern Resident Killer Whale community consists of three pods -- family groups -- that share a common ancestry, a common dialect, and a common range in the Pacific Northwest:

- **J Pod.** The smallest of the three pods and the one most frequently observed in Puget Sound, particularly in winter months when K and L Pods are often offshore. J Pod is the most studied pod of the three and has the longest continuous observational record. The pod's matriarchs are the eldest members of the community. As of 2024, J Pod consists of approximately 25 individuals.
- **K Pod.** A mid-sized pod that spends more time offshore than J Pod. K Pod ranges from Puget Sound to the outer coast of Washington and Oregon, following Chinook salmon runs to waters that J Pod does not typically reach. Approximately 17 individuals as of 2024.
- **L Pod.** The largest pod and the one with the widest range. L Pod individuals have been documented as far south as Monterey Bay, California, and as far north as southeastern Alaska. The pod's size (approximately 31 individuals as of 2024) makes it the largest component of the SRKW community. L Pod's extended range means it is most often absent from the Salish Sea during winter months.

**The total community: approximately 73 individuals as of 2024.** The community reached a population low of 74 in 2001 (a single individual, Orca J52 "Sonic," was born to bring the count from 73 to 74). The population has fluctuated between 71 and 98 individuals over the past four decades, responding to Chinook salmon abundance in ways that clearly demonstrate the direct dependence of the community on its primary prey.

**The critical endangered status.** The SRKW community has been listed under the US Endangered Species Act as critically endangered since 2005. The primary threats are: (1) prey limitation -- the decline of Chinook salmon populations across the Pacific Northwest, particularly from the Fraser River and Snake River systems, has reduced the community's food supply; (2) vessel noise and disturbance -- the Salish Sea carries heavy commercial and recreational vessel traffic that masks SRKW echolocation and communication; (3) contaminants -- accumulated PCBs, PBDEs, and other persistent organic pollutants in blubber affect immune function and reproductive success.

(Sources: Center for Whale Research SRKW census 2024; J Pod ~25 individuals; K Pod ~17 individuals; L Pod ~31 individuals; total ~73; ESA critical endangered 2005; Chinook salmon prey dependence; vessel noise masking; PCB/PBDE contamination; population history 71-98 range; J52 Sonic 2001 low)

## THE GREETING CEREMONY -- WHAT HAPPENS

### The Rarest Acoustic Event in Pacific Northwest Waters

The SRKW greeting ceremony is one of the most well-documented and studied behaviors in killer whale research. When two or more pods meet after a period of separation, they engage in a specific behavioral sequence:

**The approach.** The pods swim toward each other from their respective positions. Before physical contact, the approach slows and both groups stop at the surface approximately 50-100 meters apart. This pause -- which may last several minutes -- is the moment before the greeting.

**The greeting ceremony.** The pods mingle at the surface, members of each pod swimming through and around the other. The behavior includes physical contact: body rubbing, spy-hopping (rising vertically with the head out of the water), lobtailing, and breaching. The acoustic accompaniment during the greeting ceremony is the maximum vocalization event in the SRKW community: all members of all participating pods calling simultaneously, the calls overlapping and interweaving.

**The acoustic character.** SRKW vocalizations consist of calls (tonal sounds used for communication), clicks (ultrasonic pulses used for echolocation), and whistles (higher-frequency tonal sounds). During the greeting ceremony, calls predominate. SRKW calls are pod-specific: each pod has a dialect, a set of discrete call types unique to that pod. When J Pod, K Pod, and L Pod greet simultaneously, three distinct call dialects are produced simultaneously in the same acoustic space. The result is the most complex acoustic event that the SRKW community is capable of producing: three-dialect simultaneous vocalization, all ~73 individuals contributing.

**The frequency range.** SRKW calls occur in the 500-20,000 Hz range, with peak acoustic energy in the 1,000-8,000 Hz range. Echolocation clicks extend to 80,000+ Hz. During the full greeting, the acoustic spectrum from 500 Hz to 80 kHz is occupied by SRKW sounds. The water column becomes a full-spectrum acoustic event.

## THE THREE DIALECTS -- J, K, L

### Linguistic Architecture of the Pacific Northwest's Rarest Community

The SRKW community's three dialects are among the most studied examples of cultural transmission in non-human animals:

- **The dialect concept.** Each pod has a set of discrete call types -- specific acoustic patterns that members of that pod consistently produce and that members of other pods do not produce (or produce in modified forms). The call types are transmitted culturally: calves learn them from their mothers and other pod members, not through genetic programming. The dialects are therefore cultural artifacts, maintained and transmitted across generations through social learning.
- **J Pod calls.** J Pod has approximately 16 distinct call types. Several of these calls are shared with K Pod in modified forms (the two pods are more closely related historically and have more recent common ancestry than either does with L Pod). J Pod's call repertoire has been monitored for more than four decades, allowing researchers to document changes in call structure over time.
- **K Pod calls.** K Pod has approximately 12 distinct call types. As noted, several overlap with J Pod calls (the J/K supergroup has been documented historically and has acoustic evidence of shared cultural history). K Pod's extended offshore range means its members spend less time in the core research area and are less well-documented acoustically than J Pod.
- **L Pod calls.** L Pod has the most distinct dialect of the three -- fewer shared call types with J and K, reflecting greater divergence in cultural transmission over time. L Pod has approximately 14 distinct call types. Given L Pod's wider geographic range, its dialect may have been shaped by acoustic environments (outer coast, California) that J and K Pod members rarely encounter.
- **The full greeting acoustic event.** When all three dialects are produced simultaneously during the full greeting ceremony, the acoustic result is not cacophony but complexity: three structured call systems interweaving in the same acoustic space, each individually comprehensible to SRKW ears, collectively representing the full acoustic archive of the community's cultural heritage. This is why the full SRKW greeting is E=10: it is not just noise. It is the acoustic expression of a community's complete cultural vocabulary at maximum volume.

## THE SALISH SEA -- WHERE THE CEREMONY HAPPENS

### The Pacific Northwest's Marine Heart

The Salish Sea -- the enclosed inland sea formed by Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the southern Strait of Georgia -- is the geographic center of SRKW community life. The specific locations where the full tri-pod reunion most commonly occurs:

- **Haro Strait.** The primary summer feeding ground. Haro Strait, between San Juan Island and the southern tip of Vancouver Island, is where the SRKW community most reliably concentrates during summer months when Chinook salmon are migrating through Fraser River-origin runs. The Whale Museum on Friday Harbor (San Juan Island) has maintained the longest continuous observational record of SRKW behavior in the world. The full greeting ceremony is most commonly observed in Haro Strait or adjacent waters.
- **The Strait of Juan de Fuca.** The SRKW community uses the Strait of Juan de Fuca as a transit corridor between the open Pacific and the inner Salish Sea. K and L Pod members coming from offshore often transit the Strait and enter the inner waters through this corridor. The meeting of inbound K and L Pod members with resident J Pod in the Strait produces greeting events.
- **The acoustic environment.** The Salish Sea is acoustically complex: high levels of vessel traffic (commercial shipping from the Port of Seattle, Port of Tacoma, and Vancouver; Washington State ferries; recreational vessels; military vessels from Puget Sound Naval Shipyard) create a continuous broadband noise floor that masks SRKW vocalizations and echolocation. During the full greeting ceremony, SRKW vocalization levels are at their highest -- the community is producing maximum acoustic output. Even so, the human presence in their acoustic environment is one of the primary threats to the community's ability to find prey and maintain social cohesion.

**The San Juan Islands.** The San Juan Archipelago -- San Juan Island, Orcas Island, Lopez Island, and the surrounding smaller islands -- is the cultural and geographic heart of SRKW range in the United States. The whale-watching industry centered on the San Juan Islands has created both economic incentive for SRKW protection and ongoing challenges for acoustic disturbance management.

## THE CHINOOK SALMON DEPENDENCY -- THE ACOUSTIC EVENT'S ECOLOGICAL FOUNDATION

### Why the Pods Converge

The full tri-pod reunion is not random; it is driven by salmon:

- **The prey dependency.** SRKW community members eat almost exclusively Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha) -- the largest Pacific salmon species. While other killer whale ecotypes eat a variety of prey (Bigg's killer whales, also known as transient orcas, eat marine mammals), SRKW are salmon specialists whose prey preference is so strong that they will not eat other available prey even in conditions of apparent starvation. This dietary specialization makes the community completely dependent on Chinook salmon abundance.
- **The Fraser River connection.** The Fraser River (British Columbia) produces the largest Chinook salmon runs that enter the Salish Sea. When Fraser River Chinook are running -- typically July through September -- SRKW community members concentrate in the San Juan Islands and Haro Strait to intercept the runs. All three pods are drawn to the same waters by the same food source. The full greeting ceremony is most likely to occur during this concentration.
- **The salmon decline.** Chinook salmon populations in the Pacific Northwest have declined significantly over the past century due to dam construction (particularly on the Snake River), habitat degradation, and changing ocean conditions. The SRKW community's decline in lean years tracks directly with Chinook salmon abundance. The full tri-pod reunion is not just an acoustic event; it is a visible indicator of salmon ecosystem health. When all three pods converge, it means the salmon are running. When the pods are dispersed and rare, it means the salmon are scarce.
- **The E=10 ecological grounding.** The full SRKW greeting is E=10 not only because it is acoustically maximum but because it is ecologically dependent: it requires that the salmon ecosystem be healthy enough to concentrate all three pods in the same waters simultaneously. The rarity of the event reflects in part the rarity of salmon abundance. When it occurs, it is the maximum expression of a healthy Pacific Northwest marine ecosystem -- the apex predator community at maximum social activity, made possible by the primary producers and forage fish that support the salmon that support the orcas.

(Sources: SRKW Chinook salmon specialization; Bigg's orca prey comparison; Fraser River Chinook July-September; SRKW summer concentration San Juan Islands; Chinook decline Snake River dams habitat; SRKW population tracks Chinook abundance; salmon ecosystem health indicator; E=10 ecological grounding)

## ACOUSTIC PROFILE -- THE FULL GREETING EVENT

### What ~73 Individuals Sound Like in the Salish Sea

The acoustic profile of the full SRKW greeting ceremony:

**Call density.** During normal foraging, SRKW members produce calls at rates of roughly 1-5 per minute per individual. During greeting ceremonies, call rates increase dramatically -- individual call rates have been documented at 10-20 per minute or higher. With ~73 individuals all producing calls at elevated rates simultaneously, the acoustic output during the full greeting is in the range of 700-1,500+ discrete call events per minute.

**The layered acoustic structure.** The calls from three pods do not merge into an undifferentiated wall of sound. Researchers using directional hydrophone arrays have demonstrated that SRKW calls retain their pod-specific dialect structure even during high-density vocalization events. The three dialects are acoustically identifiable even when all three are produced simultaneously. The event is not noise; it is a complex multi-layered acoustic structure.

**The physical dimension.** The full greeting ceremony includes surface behaviors that generate additional acoustic events: breaching (a full orca breach generates an enormous surface impact -- an adult male at 6 tons breaching from the water is audible to nearby vessels without hydrophones), lobtailing (tail slap on the water surface, lower frequency, lower amplitude than a breach), spy-hopping (less acoustic impact). The visual and acoustic event is the full-body expression of the community at maximum social intensity.

**The hydrophone record.** The Orca Network and associated research organizations maintain hydrophone arrays in the San Juan Islands area that have recorded SRKW greeting ceremonies in high acoustic detail. These recordings represent the most complete acoustic documentation of the event. The calls are annotated, pod-identified, and archived in multiple research databases.

## E=10 CALIBRATION -- WHY THIS IS THE MAXIMUM

### Rarity, Scale, Irreplaceability

The full SRKW greeting at E=10:

- **Rarity.** The three pods are together in the same waters simultaneously for only part of the year. K Pod and L Pod spend extended periods offshore, ranging south along the Pacific coast. A full tri-pod greeting ceremony -- not just the pods being in the same general area, but an active greeting with surface behaviors and maximum vocalization -- may occur only a handful of times per year, and only when salmon conditions are adequate to draw all three pods to the same waters.
- **Scale.** ~73 individuals is the entire surviving community. There are no more SRKW. There is no other population that can provide an equivalent event. When all three pods greet, it is not a representative sample of the community; it is the community. The entirety of the surviving SRKW acoustic culture, produced simultaneously, in the Salish Sea.
- **Irreplaceability.** The SRKW community's three-dialect call system represents a unique cultural archive: call types that have been transmitted from mothers to calves across generations, that vary between pods in ways that reflect decades or centuries of cultural divergence, that are found nowhere else on Earth. The full greeting is the simultaneous expression of this archive. If the community were to decline further -- if any of the three pods were to be lost -- the acoustic event documented here could not be reconstructed. E=10 acknowledges the irreplaceability.
- **The combination.** Rarity + scale + irreplaceability + maximum acoustic complexity (three dialects, ~73 individuals, surface behaviors, full-spectrum vocalization from 500 Hz to 80 kHz) = E=10. The maximum possible acoustic event from the Pacific Northwest's most endangered community.
