## What Worked

1. **The "rarity + scale + irreplaceability + acoustic complexity" four-factor E=10 calibration is the correct structure.** Each factor alone would not earn E=10; the combination does. Rarity (K and L Pods offshore for extended periods, full tri-pod greeting only a few times per year); scale (~73 individuals = the entire community, no more SRKW exist); irreplaceability (three-dialect cultural archive, pod loss = permanent loss); acoustic complexity (three dialects + surface behaviors + 500 Hz to 80 kHz spectrum). All four simultaneously.
2. **The Chinook salmon dependency as the ecological driver of the reunion is essential context.** The full tri-pod greeting is not random; it is driven by Fraser River Chinook salmon concentrations in the Haro Strait / San Juan Islands area in late summer. The reunion is simultaneously an acoustic maximum and an ecosystem health indicator. When the pods converge, the salmon are running. This ecological grounding makes the entry scientifically coherent.
3. **The "not noise -- complex multi-layered acoustic structure" framing for the greeting ceremony is important and accurate.** Research using directional hydrophone arrays has confirmed that pod-specific dialects remain acoustically identifiable even during high-density multi-pod vocalization events. Three dialects interweaving is not cacophony; it is three structured systems simultaneously. This distinction matters for the E=10 calibration.
4. **The Haro Strait specificity anchors the event geographically.** The Whale Museum on Friday Harbor (San Juan Island) maintains the longest continuous SRKW observational record in the world. Haro Strait is where the summer Chinook concentrations draw all three pods. The geographic specificity grounds the imagined event in a real research context.
5. **The "pod loss = permanent acoustic archive loss" argument is the most powerful case for irreplaceability.** The three call dialects are transmitted culturally from mothers to calves. They are not encoded genetically; they are learned. If K Pod were lost, its ~12 call types -- many unique to that pod -- would be permanently gone. This is what makes the SRKW greeting E=10: it documents something that can be permanently lost.

## Discoveries

1. **The J/K supergroup historical relationship is the most interesting dialect evolution finding.** J Pod and K Pod share several call types in modified forms, suggesting more recent common cultural ancestry than either has with L Pod. L Pod's more divergent dialect reflects either greater temporal divergence or the influence of the wider geographic range (outer coast, California) that J and K Pod members rarely encounter. Dialect divergence as cultural evidence of pod history is a genuinely interesting analytical angle.
2. **The SRKW population low of 74 in 2001 (briefly 73 when a death preceded a birth) is a stark data point.** The population reached a level at which a single birth or death moved the count by a meaningful percentage of the total. The current ~73 is near that historic low. The full greeting of 73 individuals contains the awareness that this number represents both the community's entirety and its precarity.
3. **The overlap between SRKW conservation visibility and Pacific Northwest cultural identity is the most interesting cultural observation.** The SRKW community has become a symbol of the Pacific Northwest in ways that no other species has. They appear on the Washington State ferry livery. They are referenced in environmental policy discussions at the federal level. The salmon-orca-Pacific Northwest identity connection runs as deep as any cultural association in the region. The terminal pairing with Nirvana (the other Pacific Northwest entity that erupted into global visibility) is coherent at this deeper level.
