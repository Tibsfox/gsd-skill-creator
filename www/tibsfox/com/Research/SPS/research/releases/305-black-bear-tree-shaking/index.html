The Tree-Shaking Display

When a Black Bear encounters a threat at close range and feels cornered or challenged, it may seize a nearby small tree or shrub and shake it vigorously with its forepaws. The behavior is defensive confrontation: the bear asserts its presence and capability without committing to attack. The display makes the bear large, makes the environment react around it, and produces an acoustic profile that is startling at close range.

The acoustic components are simultaneous: wood-crack as the tree's fibers bend under force; branch-snap as smaller branches break; foliage crash as the canopy collapses toward the ground; and the characteristic huff — the Black Bear's explosive exhalation, repeated rapidly, at high arousal. The combined sound at 10–20 meters is overwhelming. At 50–100 meters, clearly audible through forest.

Acoustic Profile — Wood-Crack + Huff

Wood-crack: Structural sound of tree fibers bending and fracturing under bear force. Sharp in dry conditions; deeper in PNW wet conditions. The primary identifying component.

Branch-snap: Multiple simultaneous fractures as smaller branches break. Rapid, irregular, unpredictable interval.

Foliage crash: Leaves and needles in sudden motion. Fills the acoustic environment after the crack and snap.

Huff: The Black Bear's explosive nasal exhalation. Short, abrupt, loud. Multiple rapid huffs at high arousal. Not a roar — a warning.

E=7 vs. E=8 Grizzly: The Grizzly charge is kinetic — the bear moves, generating percussive locomotion toward the listener. The Black Bear tree-shaking is stationary — all energy concentrated into the shaking point. E=7 for stationary confrontation; E=8 for kinetic charge.

Ursus americanus in the Pacific Northwest

The American Black Bear is the Pacific Northwest's common bear — distributed throughout western Washington and Oregon, the Cascades, Olympics, Klamath Mountains. Adult males 130–190 kg in the PNW; adult females 90–140 kg. Black phase is the characteristic PNW color. Habitat generalist: old-growth and mature forest preferred, but second-growth and logged areas used as well. Diet omnivore with seasonal hyperphagia in fall (intensive berry and calorie consumption before hibernation). Salmon consumed where accessible.

Unlike the Grizzly Bear, extirpated from western Washington and now persisting only in small numbers in the North Cascades, the Black Bear is common and stable throughout the PNW. Population approximately 25,000–30,000 in Washington state alone. Least Concern (IUCN). The bear you encounter in any PNW forest is a Black Bear.
