## THE AMERICAN BLACK BEAR -- SPECIES PROFILE

### *Ursus americanus* in the Pacific Northwest

*Ursus americanus* (Pallas, 1780) is North America's most widespread and numerous bear species:

- **Size and identification.** Smaller than the Grizzly: adult males typically 130-190 kg (300-420 lbs) in the Pacific Northwest, with some larger individuals recorded. Adult females typically 90-140 kg. Shoulder height 70-100 cm. Color variable: black phase is most common in the Pacific Northwest (unlike the brown and cinnamon phases common in drier interior regions). The black phase is the defining PNW Black Bear image.
- **Pacific Northwest distribution.** Common throughout the Pacific Northwest, including western Washington and Oregon (forest lowlands and mountains), the Cascade Range, the Olympic Peninsula, and the Klamath Mountains. Unlike the Grizzly, which was extirpated from western Washington and now only persists in small numbers in the North Cascades, the Black Bear is common and widespread. It is the bear you are likely to encounter in any PNW forest.
- **Habitat.** Black Bears are habitat generalists relative to Grizzlies. They use forest, forest edge, shrublands, and occasionally agricultural margins. Old-growth and mature forest are preferred when available (the structural complexity provides escape cover, cavity dens, and diverse foraging substrates), but Black Bears use second-growth and logged areas as well.
- **Diet.** Omnivorous, with seasonal diet shifts. Spring: new vegetation, carrion, insects. Summer: berries, fruits, insects. Fall: hyperphagia -- intensive berry and high-calorie food consumption before hibernation; salmon where available. Winter: hibernation at higher elevations; low-elevation bears may remain active through mild PNW winters.
- **Conservation status.** Least Concern (IUCN). Common and stable throughout the PNW. Population estimated at approximately 25,000-30,000 in Washington state alone.

(Sources: Ursus americanus biology; size range 130-190 kg males PNW; black phase color; Pacific Northwest distribution; common throughout western WA; Cascade Range; Olympic Peninsula; Grizzly extirpation from western WA; habitat generalist; diet omnivore; seasonal hyperphagia; salmon predation; hibernation; population 25,000-30,000 Washington)

## THE TREE-SHAKING DISPLAY -- ACOUSTIC PROFILE

### Wood-Crack + Huff: The Confrontation Sound

The Black Bear tree-shaking display is one of the Pacific Northwest's most dramatic close-range wildlife sounds:

- **The behavior.** When a Black Bear encounters a threat (human, another bear, a predator) at close range and feels cornered or challenged, it may engage in a tree-shaking display. The bear rises on its hind legs or stands on all fours, grips the trunk or major branch of a nearby small tree or shrub with its forepaws, and shakes it vigorously. The shaking is rapid and forceful -- the bear's full upper body strength applied to the tree structure.
- **The acoustic components.** Tree-shaking produces several simultaneous sounds:
  1. *Wood-crack*: the structural sound of the tree's wood fibers bending and sometimes fracturing. In dry conditions, this is a sharp crack; in wet PNW conditions, a deeper, less explosive crack.
  2. *Branch-snap*: smaller branches breaking off. Multiple simultaneous snaps.
  3. *Foliage crash*: leaves and needles colliding with each other and with the ground.
  4. *Huff*: the bear's explosive exhalation -- a loud, abrupt "whuff" or "huff" sound produced through the nostrils. Multiple huffs in rapid succession often accompany the shaking.
- **The intensity.** The combined sound -- wood cracking, branches snapping, foliage crashing, repeated huffs -- is startling at close range. The display can be heard clearly at 50-100 meters in forest. At close range (10-20 meters), it is overwhelming.
- **Vs. Grizzly charge (SPS 304).** The Grizzly charge is kinetic -- the sound comes toward the listener, generated by a large animal in motion. The Black Bear tree-shaking is stationary -- the sound emanates from a fixed point. The Grizzly charge has the added acoustic component of locomotion; the Black Bear tree-shaking concentrates all energy into the shaking event.
- **E=7 calibration.** High-energy confrontational behavior: the shaking is forceful, the huffs are loud, the acoustic profile is sudden and dramatic. Below the Grizzly charge (E=8) because stationary; above a standard bear vocalization (E=5-6) because confrontational and physically intense.

## URSIDAE BEHAVIOR -- THE CONFRONTATION SPECTRUM

### From Curiosity to Aggression

Black Bear confrontation behavior exists on a spectrum:

- **Habituated vs. wild behavior.** Habituated Black Bears (those accustomed to human presence, typically in areas with regular human-wildlife contact) are less likely to perform dramatic confrontation displays -- they are more likely to continue foraging or withdraw. Wild Black Bears encountering humans unexpectedly at close range are the most likely to display.
- **Defensive vs. predatory.** The tree-shaking display is defensive: the bear is asserting itself but not committed to attack. True predatory behavior (rare in Black Bears) would involve stalking and approach without display. The display is a communication: *I am here, I am large, I am capable of harm, give me space.*
- **Intraspecific confrontation.** Male Black Bears in competition for territory or mating access also engage in tree-shaking and similar displays. The display communicates dominance without requiring physical contact. High-energy confrontation without necessarily high-energy resolution.
- **The huff vocalizations.** The explosive huff is the Black Bear's primary close-range vocal signal. Multiple huffs in rapid succession indicate high arousal. The huff is not the roar of the Grizzly -- it is an abrupt, forceful exhalation that reads as warning rather than threat.
