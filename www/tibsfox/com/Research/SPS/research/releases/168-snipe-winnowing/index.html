Wilson’s Snipe — Winnowing

Gallinago delicata (Scolopacidae) — E=4 — The Invisible Winnower
FIRST SCOLOPACIDAE — sandpiper family enters. 5TH MECHANICAL SOUND — outer tail feathers vibrate during steep display dives, creating an eerie tremolo that fills the twilight sky. NOT vocal. The body in flight is the instrument. The origin of the “snipe hunt.” E=4. v1.49.308.

5th Mechanical Sound — Tail Feathers as Instrument

The outer tail feathers (rectrices 7 and 8) extend into the airstream during steep dives from 60–100m altitude. Air rushing past causes the stiff, narrow-vaned feathers to vibrate at ~25 Hz, producing a pulsating tremolo: “whoo-whoo-whoo-whoo.” Proven by wind tunnel experiments — the feathers alone produce the sound. The body is the instrument.

The Origin of the “Snipe Hunt”

The snipe is absolutely real and common. But it is nearly invisible — cryptic plumage matching dead vegetation, freezing in place until stepped on, then flushing explosively. The winnowing fills the sky from an unseen source. A bird that is real but cannot be found became the metaphor for a fool’s errand.

First Scolopacidae — Sandpiper Family Enters

98 species worldwide. Sandpipers, snipes, woodcocks, phalaropes, curlews, godwits. The largest shorebird family. The snipe enters through its extraordinary mechanical sound rather than its typical shorebird ecology.
