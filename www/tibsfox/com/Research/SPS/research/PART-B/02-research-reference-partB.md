### Puget Sound Acoustic Environment
Puget Sound is one of the world's most acoustically complex inland waterways. It
receives sound from: marine mammals (SRKW, harbor seals, porpoises), shipping traffic,
tidal flow through narrow passages, rainfall on water surface, wind through nearshore
vegetation, and bird colonies on rocky islands. It is simultaneously one of the most
sonically rich and most acoustically threatened ecosystems on Earth (shipping noise
is a documented threat to SRKW communication). Source: NOAA NWFSC.

Southern Resident Killer Whales (J, K, L pods) are critically endangered (IUCN Red List,
ESA-listed). Their population as of 2025: approximately 73 individuals. Every release
discussing SRKW must include conservation status, primary threats (prey depletion,
noise pollution, contaminants), and must not trivialize their situation.
Source: NOAA Fisheries SRKW Recovery Plan, 2023.

### Orca Acoustic Culture
SRKW have **clan-specific vocal dialects** — repertoires of calls shared within matrilineal
groups. This is cultural transmission of acoustic information across generations — the
cetacean equivalent of musical tradition. Key structural features:
- Discrete calls: stereotyped, repetitive, clan-specific (S-calls for SRKW)
- Whistles: continuous frequency modulations; contact/social functions
- Echolocation clicks: broadband bursts for navigation and prey detection
- Call sharing: all members of a pod produce the same calls — true group singing
Source: Ford 1989, J. Acoust. Soc. Am.; Deecke et al. 2000, Animal Behaviour.

**Pacific Wren Acoustic Analysis (degree=1):**
The Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*) song has been formally analyzed as one of the
most acoustically complex avian vocalizations relative to body mass. A single song bout:
duration: 5–10 seconds; phrases: 12–36 distinct elements; frequency range: 2–10 kHz;
temporal resolution: elements as short as 30ms. The wren sings in two-voice syrinx
simultaneously — literal polyphony from a single bird. Source: Kroodsma 2005,
"The Singing Life of Birds"; confirmed by Macaulay Library spectrograms.

**Pacific Chorus Frog Chorus (degree=45):**
The breeding chorus of Pacific Chorus Frogs (*Pseudacris regilla*) exhibits emergent
synchrony — males synchronize calling rates through acoustic feedback, producing a
collective rhythm that no individual frog "plans." This is the precise biological
equivalent of rhythmic entrainment in human music. Source: Aihara et al. 2011,
J. Theoretical Biology; Greenfield & Roizen 1993, Animal Behaviour.

## §6: Mathematics Bridges (Acoustic Physics)

### Frequency and Pitch
Every sound is a pressure wave with frequency measured in Hz. The mathematical structure:
- Middle A = 440 Hz; each octave doubles frequency (880, 1760...)
- Perfect fifth = 3:2 frequency ratio (e.g., 440 Hz A → 660 Hz E)
- Whale calls at 20 Hz = just above human hearing floor; Blue Whale at 10 Hz = infrasound
- Pacific Wren's 4–8 kHz = upper range of human speech; above middle of piano keyboard

**College node:** `.college/mathematics/number-theory/ratios/` — frequency ratios ARE
musical interval ratios. Same mathematics, different substrate. Mandatory bridge for
every species with a defined fundamental frequency.

### Fourier Analysis and Timbre
Every sound is a sum of sinusoidal components (Fourier's theorem). An orca's call has:
- Fundamental frequency (pitch)
- Harmonic series (overtones that define timbre/identity)
- Noise component (broadband elements)
- Frequency modulation (pitch changes over time)

This is identical to how a synthesizer builds sound from oscillators — the bioacoustics
IS the synthesis. Mandatory bridge: `.college/mathematics/calculus/fourier/` for every
species with complex call structure (estimated 200+ releases).

### Rhythm and Integer Mathematics
Calling rates, inter-call intervals, and chorus synchrony are integer-ratio operations:
- Frog chorus synchrony: males lock to integer multiples of a shared period
- Woodpecker drumming: 15–25 strikes/second = 15/1 or 25/1 beat subdivisions
- SRKW call repertoire: distinct calls with specific temporal patterns
- Cricket chirp rate: directly proportional to temperature (Dolbear's Law — a mathematical formula)

**College node:** `.college/mathematics/sequences/` — biological rhythm IS number theory.

### Echolocation and Signal Processing
Bat and dolphin echolocation implements the same principles as radar/sonar:
- Emit a broadband pulse
- Measure return time (distance = speed × time/2)
- Analyze frequency shift (Doppler — moving target detection)
- Neurally compute 3D spatial map

This is Fourier analysis + Nyquist theorem + Doppler physics implemented in
biological neural hardware. Bridge: `.college/mathematics/calculus/fourier/`,
`.college/physics/waves/` (future), `.college/mathematics/signals/`.

## §7: Indigenous Attribution Standards

### Coast Salish Nations and PNW Sound Knowledge

The species in this dataset exist on the unceded territories of, and within the cultural
knowledge traditions of, multiple Coast Salish and other First Nations peoples.
Specific attribution requirements:

| Species Context | Nation | Attribution Requirement |
|----------------|--------|------------------------|
| Salmon (all runs) | Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Tulalip, Lummi | Salmon as cultural/spiritual keystone; not just "fish" |
| Orca / Killer Whale | Lummi Nation (traditional kinship), all Coast Salish | Qwe 'lhol mechen (relatives in the sea) — Lummi relationship |
| Eagle | Many nations | Ceremonial significance; do not reduce to "bird" |
| Raven | Tlingit, Haida, Coast Salish | Creator/trickster figure; complex cultural role |
| Cedar | Coast Salish broadly | "Tree of Life" — not just a tree |
| Wolf | Multiple nations | Clan affiliation; ceremonial; not to be sensationalized |
| Thunderbird (lightning/thunder) | Multiple PNW nations | Supernatural being; storms have cultural meaning |

**Mandatory language rules:**
- NEVER: "Indigenous people believed that..." (past tense for living knowledge = wrong)
- USE: "Lummi Nation maintains that..." or "In Coast Salish traditions..."
- NEVER: "myths and legends" for Indigenous knowledge systems
- USE: "oral histories," "knowledge traditions," "cultural teachings"
- ALWAYS cite the specific nation, never "local tribes" or "Native Americans"

**OCAP® Application:**
- Ownership: Indigenous communities own their cultural knowledge
- Control: Do not reproduce specific ceremonial or restricted knowledge
- Access: Public ecological knowledge (e.g., salmon behavior observations) may be cited
- Possession: Do not claim Indigenous knowledge as discovered/invented by researchers
Source: First Nations Information Governance Centre (fnigc.ca)
