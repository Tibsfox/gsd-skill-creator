The Through-Line

360 musicians and 360 wild voices — both mapped to the same unit circle, both teaching the same mathematics, both carrying the same lineage of the Pacific Northwest. Quincy Jones and the Great Blue Heron share degree 0° because both understand call-and-response as architecture, not decoration. Nirvana and the orca greeting ceremony share degree 311° because both are full community convergence calls — every voice present, every frequency committed. The circle is not a metaphor. It is the structure that was already there, in the water and in the music, before anyone wrote it down.

Part A mapped the musicians. Part B maps the species. Part C will discover what they already knew about each other. Part D will refine those discoveries until the signal is clean. Part E will document what the whole exercise taught — not about music or ecology, but about the mathematics that connects them. The unit circle does not distinguish between a chord progression and an orca pod's greeting. Both are periodic, both are structured, both carry information through frequency and silence. The Sound of Puget Sound is one project told in five releases, each one a different angle on the same truth: the Pacific Northwest has been composing longer than any of us have been listening.
