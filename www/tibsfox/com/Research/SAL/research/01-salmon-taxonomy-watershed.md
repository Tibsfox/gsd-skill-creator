## 1. Five Pacific Salmon Species

The Pacific Northwest Coast is defined by five species of Pacific salmon, plus steelhead and sea-run cutthroat trout. Each species occupies a distinct ecological niche, returns at different times, serves different nutritional and ceremonial roles, and connects different parts of the watershed system. Together they form the biological spine of the region -- the thread that ties snowmelt on the Cascades to the deep Pacific.

| Common Name | Binomial | Heritage Name | Knowledge Domain |
|-------------|----------|---------------|------------------|
| Chinook | *Oncorhynchus tshawytscha* | Tyee (King) | Leadership & Governance |
| Sockeye | *O. nerka* | Red | Sustenance & Preservation |
| Coho | *O. kisutch* | Silver | Craft & Material Culture |
| Chum | *O. keta* | Dog | Community & Trade Networks |
| Pink | *O. gorbuscha* | Humpy | Cycles & Seasonal Knowledge |
| Steelhead | *O. mykiss* | -- | Resilience & Adaptation |
| Cutthroat | *O. clarkii* | -- | Local Watershed Knowledge |

The mapping of species to knowledge domains is not arbitrary. Chinook are the largest and first to return -- the leaders. Sockeye form the backbone of food preservation systems. Coho's coastal adaptability mirrors the versatility of material culture. Chum's widespread abundance fueled trade networks. Pink's strict two-year cycle is the purest expression of seasonal knowledge. Steelhead, uniquely among Pacific salmon, can spawn and survive to spawn again -- resilience encoded in biology [1][2].

## 2. Western Taxonomy: The Oncorhynchus Classification

NOAA Fisheries manages and protects Pacific salmon and steelhead under both the Endangered Species Act and the Magnuson-Stevens Act. The Western taxonomic framework organizes populations into Evolutionarily Significant Units (ESUs) and recovery domains [1].

There are 28 ESA-listed species of salmon and steelhead on the West Coast. Key ESU designations for the Puget Sound region include:

- **Puget Sound Chinook** -- Threatened (listed 1999)
- **Puget Sound Steelhead** -- Threatened (listed 2007)
- **Ozette Lake Sockeye** -- Threatened
- **Hood Canal Summer Chum** -- Threatened
- **Lower Columbia Coho** -- Threatened

The Western classification system excels at population genetics, migration patterns, and conservation management. It answers the question: *What is this organism, and how is it related to other organisms?*

## 3. Indigenous Classification: Relationship and Role

PNW Coast nations classify salmon not by morphology or genetics but by relationship: when they return, what they provide, which ceremonies honor them, which families hold fishing rights at which sites. This is not a less precise classification -- it is a different kind of precision, optimized for a different set of questions [3][4].

### 3.1 Seasonal Names

Many nations have distinct names for salmon based on the season of their return. A spring-run Chinook and a fall-run Chinook are, in Western taxonomy, the same species with different run timing. In Indigenous classification, they may carry different names, different ceremonial significance, and different harvest protocols -- because they serve different roles in the annual food cycle.

### 3.2 The Five Knowledge Domains

The mapping of salmon species to knowledge domains reflects their actual cultural roles:

- **Chinook (Tyee)** -- "Chief" or "King." The first and largest salmon to return. Its arrival signals the beginning of the fishing season and triggers the First Salmon Ceremony. Leadership knowledge: governance, protocol, responsibility.
- **Sockeye (Red)** -- The backbone of the food economy. Sockeye's high oil content makes it ideal for smoking and drying -- the preservation technology that sustained communities through winter. Sustenance knowledge: food systems, preservation, abundance management.
- **Coho (Silver)** -- Found in smaller coastal streams as well as major rivers. Coho's adaptability to diverse habitats mirrors the versatility of material culture -- cedar bark, canoe design, tool-making. Craft knowledge: material culture, adaptation, resourcefulness.
- **Chum (Dog)** -- The most widespread Pacific salmon, reaching rivers from California to Alaska and across to Asia. Chum's abundance and wide distribution made it a trade species. Community knowledge: trade networks, inter-nation relationships, commerce.
- **Pink (Humpy)** -- Fixed two-year lifecycle with no variation. Even-year and odd-year populations are genetically distinct. Pink salmon are the purest biological clock in the watershed. Cycle knowledge: seasons, timing, renewal, predictability.

## 4. The Dual Taxonomy Bridge

Both classification systems are valid. Neither alone captures the full picture. The Heritage Pack must encode both taxonomies and make the space between them visible as a teaching tool [5].

| Western Question | Indigenous Question |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| What species is this? | When does it return? |
| What is its genetic lineage? | What ceremony honors it? |
| What is its conservation status? | Which families hold fishing rights? |
| What watershed does it spawn in? | What does its return mean for the community? |

The Ontological Bridge does not resolve these differences -- it makes them visible. A Chinook salmon is simultaneously *Oncorhynchus tshawytscha* (Western binomial), Tyee (Lushootseed leadership designation), a threatened ESU under the ESA, and the being whose return renews the covenant between humans and Salmon People. All of these descriptions are true. They answer different questions asked from different positions.

## 5. Salmon Life Cycle as Ecological Thread

Pacific salmon are anadromous -- born in freshwater, they migrate to coastal estuaries, enter the ocean to mature, and return to their natal streams to spawn and die. This lifecycle connects mountain headwaters to open ocean in a single ecological narrative [1][2].

### 5.1 The Nutrient Cycle

When salmon return from the ocean and die after spawning, their bodies deliver marine-derived nutrients -- nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon -- deep into forest ecosystems. Research has documented that trees within 500 meters of salmon-bearing streams contain marine-derived nitrogen in their growth rings. Eagles, bears, and other predators scatter carcasses into the forest, fertilizing the riparian zone. The salmon feeds the forest; the forest shades the stream; the stream nurtures the next generation of salmon.

### 5.2 Life Cycle as Learning Progression

| Life Stage | Learning Level | Description |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Freshwater Birth | Explorer | Foundations -- where you come from |
| Ocean Journey | Apprentice | Breadth of knowledge -- the wider world |
| Spawning Return | Journeyman | Deep understanding -- bringing it home |
| Next Generation | Keeper | Teaching, documenting -- the cycle continues |

## 7. Conservation Status and ESA Listings

Salish Sea Chinook populations have declined approximately 60% since the Pacific Salmon Commission began tracking abundance in 1984. Habitat alteration, hydroelectric development, and consumptive fisheries have impacted most populations in the Pacific Northwest [1][7].

The EPA's Health of the Salish Sea Report documents declining trends across multiple environmental indicators: marine dissolved oxygen levels declining from 2010-2019, 10 of 17 studied rivers showing decreasing summer flow trends since 1975, and marine species at risk doubling from 2002 to 2015 [7].

Nine ESUs of Chinook salmon are listed as threatened or endangered under the ESA, plus multiple ESUs of steelhead, coho, sockeye, and chum. The Elwha watershed represents a rare success story: following the removal of the Elwha and Glines Canyon dams (2011-2014), salmon have begun recolonizing over 70 miles of previously blocked habitat.

## 8. The Salmon-as-Taxonomy Model

The organizing principle: five Pacific salmon species organize five knowledge domains, mapped by watershed to specific nations and encoded using Foxfire documentation techniques.

```
FIVE SALMON = FIVE KNOWLEDGE DOMAINS
-------------------------------------
Chinook (Tyee)     -> Leadership & Governance
Sockeye (Red)      -> Sustenance & Preservation
Coho (Silver)      -> Craft & Material Culture
Chum (Dog)         -> Community & Trade Networks
Pink (Humpy)       -> Cycles & Seasonal Knowledge
+ Steelhead        -> Resilience & Adaptation

WATERSHED -> NATION -> PRACTICE -> BADGE
```

The taxonomy is not imposed -- it emerges from the relationships the water reveals. One salmon species, properly understood in its full ecological and cultural context, teaches more than a library of disconnected facts.
