## 1. Cultural Sovereignty Classification

This module operates strictly within the Cultural Sovereignty classification system. Every piece of content is assessed against this framework before inclusion [1][2].

| Level | Classification | Pack Treatment |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | Publicly shared | Encoded in full. Published works, museum programs, widely taught. |
| 2 | Community-taught | Encoded with attribution. Available through cultural programs. |
| 3 | Restricted | Not encoded. Referred to community sources with explanation. |
| 4 | Sacred/Ceremonial | Hard block. Never encoded. Community sovereignty absolute. |

**The governing principles:** OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession), Indigenous Quotient, CARE Principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics), and UNDRIP Article 31 (the right of Indigenous peoples to maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions).

Every teaching in this module is drawn from published ethnographic works, peer-reviewed academic research, museum educational programs, or community-authorized curricula. Every reference names the specific nation. No content is sourced from entertainment media, personal blogs, or unsourced claims.

## 2. Transformer Stories

The Transformer -- Dokibeet in Lushootseed, Xays in Halkomelem -- is a central figure in Coast Salish narrative traditions. The Transformer traveled through the Salish Sea region and the Cascades, setting things right and establishing the world as it is now [3][4].

### 2.1 Mountains as Beings (Level 1)

Mountains and rock formations along the Coast Salish landscape are understood as ancient beings turned to stone. In some traditions, these were beings who failed to live in balance, transformed by the Transformer as a consequence and a teaching. The mountain is simultaneously a geological feature and a moral lesson -- the landscape itself is the textbook.

This is not metaphor imposed on landscape. It is knowledge encoded in landscape. A young person who learns that a particular mountain formation was once a being who acted a certain way has learned:
- The location and features of the mountain (geography)
- The behavior that was corrected (ethics)
- The consequence of the behavior (governance)
- The ongoing presence of the teaching (continuity)

All in a single story that is inherently memorable because it is attached to a specific, visible feature of the world.

### 2.2 Encoding Ecological Knowledge

Transformer stories encode ecological knowledge within narrative frameworks. Species relationships, migration patterns, seasonal cycles, and watershed connections are embedded in stories about how the world came to be as it is. When a story says "the salmon agreed to return every year as long as the people honored the water," it is encoding the observation that salmon populations are sustainable when watersheds are healthy -- an ecological finding that took Western science centuries to formalize.

## 3. The Salmon's Promise

The pact between humans and Salmon People: salmon return every year as long as people respect the water. This is the foundational teaching of the salmon-human relationship across the PNW Coast [5][6].

### 3.1 The Covenant Structure

The Salmon's Promise is a covenant -- a binding agreement between two parties with obligations on both sides:

**Salmon's obligation:** Return each year to the natal streams. Provide food, nutrients, and the thread that connects ocean to forest.

**Human's obligation:** Respect the water. Maintain the watershed. Honor the first salmon. Return the bones to the river. Take only what is needed and ensure enough pass upstream to spawn the next generation.

### 3.2 First Salmon Ceremony as Covenant Renewal

The First Salmon Ceremony renews this covenant annually. The first salmon caught is prepared with elaborate respect and shared among the community. The bones are returned to the water, enabling the Salmon Person to be reborn and report to the others that the humans have kept their promise.

This teaching is widely published and taught through museum programs: Smithsonian NMAI NK360 PNW curriculum, Burke Museum educational resources, and published ethnographic works [5][6].

### 3.3 Ecological Precision

The covenant is ecologically precise. Salmon populations collapse when:
- Watersheds are damaged (habitat degradation)
- Too many fish are taken (overharvest)
- Migration routes are blocked (dams)
- Water quality declines (pollution)

Each of these failure modes corresponds to a violation of the human side of the covenant. "Respect the water" is not vague spiritual advice -- it is a comprehensive environmental management directive.

## 4. Cedar Origin Narratives

The cedar teachings (detailed in Module 02) carry origin narratives that encode ethical principles within creation stories [3][7].

### 4.1 The Generous Man (Level 1)

The central cedar teaching: a man so generous that he gave everything to others was transformed by the Creator into the western red cedar, so that he could continue providing forever. This narrative encodes:

- **Ethical principle:** True strength comes from what you give, not what you keep
- **Ecological principle:** The cedar provides indefinitely when treated with respect
- **Sustainability principle:** The giver becomes the provider; the cycle is self-renewing

### 4.2 Cedar and Salmon Together

Cedar and salmon together form the material foundation of PNW Coast life. Cedar provides the canoe to reach the salmon, the net to catch it, the rack to smoke it, the box to store it, and the longhouse to shelter the people who eat it. The origin narratives of both species encode the same ethic: the natural world provides abundantly when the relationship is honored.

## 5. The Potlatch as Social Technology

The potlatch is a ceremonial feast and gift-giving event that channels human drives for status and competition into a system that strengthens community bonds rather than destroying them [8][9][10].

### 5.1 How It Works (Level 1-2)

The host family or chief demonstrates status not by accumulating wealth but by distributing it. The more you give away, the higher your standing. Guests are fed, entertained, and given gifts -- often of enormous value. The social debt created by accepting these gifts generates its own form of reciprocal power, as recipients are obligated to reciprocate at future potlatches.

### 5.2 Social Engineering

This is social engineering of extraordinary sophistication. The potlatch:

- **Redistributes surplus** -- Prevents dangerous accumulation of wealth by any one family
- **Establishes status through generosity** -- Inverts the accumulation instinct
- **Creates binding social obligations** -- Gifts create debts that tie communities together
- **Records governance** -- Names, titles, rights, and privileges are publicly witnessed and confirmed
- **Manages conflict** -- Rival claims can be contested through competitive giving rather than violence

### 5.3 Criminalization and Survival

The potlatch was criminalized in Canada from 1884 to 1951 under Section 149 of the Indian Act. The ban was explicitly designed to suppress Indigenous governance and social organization. Despite criminalization, many nations continued potlatching in secret. The repeal of the ban in 1951 did not undo the damage -- generations of disrupted transmission.

Published scholarship on the potlatch: Codere (1950), Cole & Chaikin (1990), Bracken (1997) [8][9][10].

## 6. Flood Stories and Ecological Memory

Great Flood stories are found across PNW Coast nations. These narratives describe a catastrophic flood from which people survived by tying cedar canoes to the highest Cascade peaks and Vancouver Island mountains, tethered to high ground by ancient ropes [3][4].

### 6.1 Geological Correlation

These narratives correlate with documented geological events: post-glacial flooding (approximately 10,000-12,000 years ago), tsunamis from the Cascadia Subduction Zone (the most recent major event in 1700), and localized flooding events from glacial lake outbursts. The stories encode geological memory across hundreds of generations -- a transmission achievement no written record in Western civilization has matched for duration.

### 6.2 Knowledge Encoded

Flood stories encode:
- **Survival technology** -- Cedar canoes and ropes as life-saving tools
- **Geographic knowledge** -- Which peaks are high enough to survive a major flood
- **Preparedness ethic** -- Readiness for catastrophic events
- **Social continuity** -- The community survives by working together

## 7. What This Module Does Not Contain

This section explicitly acknowledges what is not encoded, and why [1][2].

### Level 3 -- Restricted Knowledge

- Specific ceremonial protocols for individual nations' First Salmon ceremonies
- Governance details of specific potlatch proceedings
- Family-specific origin stories and hereditary privileges
- Ceremonial songs, dances, and regalia descriptions
- Specific protocols for addressing spiritual beings

**Why not encoded:** This knowledge belongs to specific communities and families. Access is controlled by those communities. The Heritage Pack respects this sovereignty absolutely.

### Level 4 -- Sacred/Ceremonial

- Sacred ceremonial content of any kind
- Initiation rites and their content
- Winter ceremonial details
- Spirit dance teachings

**Why not encoded:** Hard block. These are never appropriate for external documentation regardless of source. Community sovereignty is absolute.
