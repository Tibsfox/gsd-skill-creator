## 1. Indigenous Salmon Management Systems

A landmark 2021 paper in *BioScience* (Atlas et al.) documents how Indigenous communities around the North Pacific maintained sustainable salmon harvests for over 10,000 years using in-river and selective fishing tools: weirs, traps, wheels, reef nets, and dip nets. Following European contact, these traditional fisheries and governance systems were suppressed and often outlawed, as commercial fishing interests came to dominate [1].

The paper identifies a critical finding: Indigenous management systems offer pathways to sustainable fisheries that contemporary management approaches struggle to achieve. The core difference is philosophical. Western fisheries management asks: *How many fish can we take?* Indigenous management asks: *How many fish must we leave?*

### 1.1 The Heiltsuk Principle

The Heiltsuk Nation's management principle captures the ethic: "the right to use a river system comes with the responsibility to maintain a river system" and "the primary focus should be on what is left behind, not what is taken" (Housty et al. 2014, as cited in Atlas et al. 2021) [1].

This is not conservation as sacrifice. It is conservation as engineering -- the system is designed to produce surplus indefinitely, and the manager's job is to ensure the surplus continues. The technology (weir, reef net, trap) is inseparable from the governance (who fishes, when, how much, and what is returned).

## 2. Reef Net Fishing

Reef nets were invented by Straits Salish peoples -- specifically the Lummi and Saanich nations -- and represent one of the most sophisticated fishing technologies developed anywhere in the world [1][2][3].

### 2.1 How It Works

Two canoes are anchored parallel to each other in relatively shallow tidal waters along known salmon migration routes. Between them, a large net is suspended underwater, held down by stone weights. Artificial "reefs" made of weighted kelp or rock lead-lines guide migrating salmon toward the heart of the net. Spotters on raised platforms in each canoe watch the water below.

When salmon enter the heart of the net, the crews simultaneously raise the sides, trapping the fish. The catch is transferred to the canoes. Fish can be individually inspected -- wrong species, wrong size, or spawning females can be released alive.

### 2.2 What Makes It Extraordinary

| Feature | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| **Selective harvest** | Individual fish can be released; no bycatch problem |
| **Non-destructive** | Does not alter the riverbed, dam flow, or block migration |
| **Cooperative** | Requires multi-family coordination and shared ecological knowledge |
| **Knowledge-intensive** | Requires understanding of tidal patterns, salmon migration routes, underwater topography |
| **Sustainable** | Maintained healthy salmon runs for millennia before contact |

### 2.3 Suppression and Survival

Despite treaty protections, reef nets were outlawed in Canada in the early 1900s. In Washington State, Lummi fishers continued to use reef nets under treaty rights, though the practice was marginalized by industrial fishing. Contemporary reef net fishing continues among the Lummi Nation, representing both cultural continuity and active traditional ecological knowledge [3].

## 3. Weir Construction

Fish weirs are structures built across streams or rivers to control and direct salmon movement. PNW Coast nations constructed weirs from wood, stone, and woven materials that allowed water to pass while guiding fish toward capture points [2][4].

### 3.1 Types of Weirs

- **Fence weirs** -- Vertical stakes driven into the streambed with woven barriers between them
- **Stone weirs** -- Rock walls constructed across shallow stream sections
- **Combination weirs** -- Wood and stone structures with removable sections

### 3.2 Management Function

Weirs served a dual function: harvest and management. By controlling which fish passed upstream and which were captured, weir operators effectively managed the spawning population. This is selective breeding at the population level -- allowing the largest, healthiest fish to continue upstream ensures the next generation inherits those traits.

The governance of weirs was typically family-based or house-based, with specific families holding the right and responsibility to operate weirs at specific sites. This is decentralized resource management -- each family manages its section of the watershed, with the aggregate result being system-wide sustainability.

## 4. Fish Traps and Beach Seining

### 4.1 Intertidal Fish Traps

Stone fish traps -- walls of stacked rocks in intertidal zones that capture fish as the tide recedes -- are documented along the PNW Coast. Some stone traps are massive engineering works visible in aerial photography, covering hundreds of meters of shoreline. They require no maintenance during operation: the tide does the work.

### 4.2 Beach Seining

Beach seining with cedar-bark nets was practiced in estuaries and river mouths. Nets were constructed from processed cedar bark fiber, with stone weights and wood floats. The technique involved encircling a school of fish near shore and drawing the net to the beach.

### 4.3 Dip Nets

Used at falls, rapids, and narrow river sections where salmon concentrate during upstream migration. Dip net fishing at traditional sites continues as an active practice, notably at the Cascade Falls on the Fraser River system.

## 5. The First Salmon Ceremony

The First Salmon Ceremony is a near-ubiquitous practice among Indigenous salmon-fishing communities from California to Alaska. It is the ceremonial expression of the reciprocal relationship between humans and Salmon People [1][5].

### 5.1 The Core Teaching (Level 1 -- Publicly Shared)

The salmon return every year as long as people respect the water. This is the Salmon's Promise -- a covenant between humans and Salmon People, renewed annually through ceremony. The First Salmon Ceremony honors the first salmon caught in the season. The fish is treated with elaborate respect: prepared in a specific way, shared among the community, and the bones returned to the water so that the Salmon Person can be reborn and tell the others that the humans kept their promise.

This teaching encodes a profound ecological ethic: sustainable harvest is not merely a management strategy but a covenant. The ceremony makes the ethic visible, communal, and non-negotiable.

### 5.2 Cultural Sovereignty Classification

The First Salmon Ceremony is encoded at **Level 1** -- the principle of reciprocity and honoring what sustains you. This is the publicly taught, widely published general teaching.

Specific ceremonial protocols for individual nations remain **Level 3** -- restricted knowledge referred to community sources. The Heritage Pack does not encode how any specific nation conducts the ceremony. It encodes the principle, and directs the learner toward the communities that hold the specific knowledge.

## 6. Food Preservation Systems

The food economy of the PNW Coast was built on salmon preservation. The techniques allowed communities to store surplus from the summer and fall runs for consumption through the winter months when fresh fish was unavailable [2][6].

### 6.1 Smoking

Two methods were used:
- **Cold smoking** -- Low-temperature smoke over extended periods (days to weeks) for long-term preservation
- **Hot smoking** -- Higher temperatures for shorter periods, producing a different flavor and shorter shelf life

Smoking racks were constructed from green wood over carefully managed fire pits. The species of wood used for smoking affected flavor -- alder was common in many areas, with specific preferences varying by nation.

### 6.2 Wind Drying

In drier areas (rain shadow zones, interior valleys), salmon was cut into thin strips and dried in open air on wooden racks. Wind drying produces a hard, shelf-stable product that can be stored for months and reconstituted by soaking.

### 6.3 Oil Rendering

Eulachon (candlefish) oil was the primary fat source and trade commodity -- so valuable it defined the "grease trails" (trade routes) between coastal and interior nations. While not salmon, eulachon processing represents a parallel food technology using similar preservation principles.

### 6.4 Pemmican and Dried Salmon Flour

Dried salmon was sometimes pounded into a flour-like substance and mixed with berries and oil to create a calorie-dense, portable food. This is nutritional engineering: combining protein, fat, and carbohydrates into a storable, transportable form.

## 7. Seasonal Rounds

The fishing economy operated on a strict seasonal calendar, with different species available at different times and in different locations throughout the year [2][6]:

| Season | Primary Activity | Species | Location |
|--------|-----------------|---------|----------|
| Spring (Apr-May) | First Salmon, spring Chinook | Chinook | River mouths, estuaries |
| Early Summer (Jun-Jul) | Sockeye runs, reef net season | Sockeye, Chinook | Marine approaches, rivers |
| Late Summer (Aug-Sep) | Peak harvest, smoking season | Coho, Pink, Chum | Rivers, camps |
| Fall (Oct-Nov) | Late runs, final preservation | Chum, Coho, Steelhead | Upriver sites |
| Winter (Dec-Mar) | Stored food consumption, ceremony | None (preserved fish) | Villages, longhouses |

The seasonal round connected fishing to every other aspect of life: cedar bark harvesting happened in spring when the sap was running, berry gathering overlapped with late summer fishing, and winter was the season for ceremony, storytelling, and consuming the preserved surplus.

## 8. Contemporary Restoration

The intersection of traditional ecological knowledge and modern fisheries science is producing restoration approaches that neither could achieve alone [1][7].

### 8.1 Elwha Dam Removal

The removal of the Elwha and Glines Canyon dams (2011-2014) represents the largest dam removal project in U.S. history. The Lower Elwha Klallam Tribe was central to the advocacy and planning. Salmon have begun recolonizing over 70 miles of previously blocked habitat, and the river is rebuilding its sediment, gravel, and nutrient cycles.

### 8.2 Co-Management Models

In both Washington State (through treaty rights upheld in the Boldt Decision, 1974) and British Columbia, Indigenous nations are increasingly recognized as co-managers of salmon fisheries. These arrangements combine Western stock assessment data with Indigenous knowledge of run timing, habitat conditions, and sustainable harvest levels.
