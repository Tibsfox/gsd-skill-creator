## 1. Cedar as Foundation Technology

The western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) is the foundational technology of PNW Coast peoples. Hilary Stewart's *Cedar: Tree of Life to the Northwest Coast Indians* (Douglas & McIntyre, 1984) documents the comprehensive material culture: canoes, longhouses, clothing, rope, baskets, bentwood boxes, and ceremonial objects [1].

Cedar's properties make it uniquely suited to the PNW environment:

- **Rot resistance** -- heartwood contains thujaplicin, a natural fungicide
- **Straight grain** -- splits cleanly along its length, enabling plank construction without saws
- **Lightweight** -- low density relative to strength, ideal for watercraft
- **Workability** -- soft enough to carve with traditional tools (bone, stone, shell, later iron)
- **Bark flexibility** -- inner bark can be processed into fabric-like material for weaving
- **Aromatic properties** -- natural insect repellent; ceremonial significance of scent

No single tree species in any other temperate region provides this combination of properties. Cedar made possible a material culture of extraordinary sophistication -- ocean-going canoes, multi-family longhouses, waterproof clothing, storage containers that could hold boiling water -- all from a single species.

## 2. The Cedar Teachings

In Coast Salish tradition, cedar carries origin teachings that encode ecological and ethical principles within narrative. These teachings are publicly shared through museum educational programs and published ethnographic works [1][2].

The central teaching: A man so generous that he gave everything he had to others was transformed by the Creator into the western red cedar, so that he could continue providing for the people forever. This is not merely a story -- it is a design principle. The cedar provides canoes (transportation), longhouses (shelter), clothing (protection), rope (connection), baskets (storage), and ceremonial objects (spiritual life). The teaching encodes the ethic that true root strength comes from what you give back to community.

### 2.1 Teaching as Technology Transfer

The Foxfire documentation methodology -- students interviewing elders, practitioners teaching by doing, knowledge preserved through the act of recording it -- maps directly onto how cedar knowledge is traditionally transmitted. A young person learns bark harvesting not from a manual but by walking into the forest with someone who knows. The teaching is in the doing. The Heritage Pack encodes this methodology: each skill is documented as a practitioner would teach it, not as an observer would describe it.

## 3. The Law of the Bark

Never take the whole circle of bark from a tree. Take only one strip from the sun-facing side. Ensure the tree lives [1][3].

This is sustainability architecture encoded in harvesting practice -- a living protocol that predates Western conservation science by millennia. The Law of the Bark is not a cultural abstraction; it is engineering. A cedar tree with its bark removed in a full circle will die. A tree with a single strip removed will heal the wound and continue growing. The stripped area, called a culturally modified tree (CMT), is visible for centuries -- archaeological evidence of sustainable harvest practices dating back thousands of years.

### 3.1 Culturally Modified Trees as Archaeological Record

CMTs are found throughout the PNW Coast and are protected under heritage legislation in both Canada and the United States. The presence of CMTs in a forest is evidence of sustained Indigenous occupation and resource management. Some documented CMTs have bark-stripping scars dating to the 1400s -- living trees that have been harvested sustainably for over 600 years.

## 4. Bark Harvesting and Processing

Cedar bark is harvested in late spring and early summer, when the sap is running and the bark separates most readily from the wood. The harvesting process, documented by Stewart (1984) and community-authorized cultural programs [1]:

### 4.1 Harvest Method

1. **Selection** -- Choose a straight, healthy tree with few branches on the lower trunk
2. **Initial cut** -- Score the bark vertically at chest height, extending upward
3. **Strip removal** -- Peel the bark strip away from the tree, pulling steadily upward
4. **Limitation** -- Never take more than one-third of the circumference; sun-facing side preferred
5. **Acknowledgment** -- Traditional practice includes acknowledgment/gratitude to the tree

### 4.2 Processing

- **Outer bark** -- Used for construction, roofing, fuel, and rough containers
- **Inner bark** -- Separated by soaking and beating; processed into fiber for weaving
- **Shredded bark** -- Beaten into soft, fabric-like material for clothing and blankets
- **Cedar withes** -- Thin, flexible strips used for lashing and binding

The inner bark, when properly processed, becomes as supple as woven fabric. Coast Salish weavers produce cedar bark hats, capes, and skirts that are both waterproof and breathable -- a material technology that synthetic fabrics have only recently matched.

## 5. Canoe Construction

The canoe is the central transportation technology of the PNW Coast. Stewart (1977) and Neel (1995) document multiple canoe types, each designed for specific water conditions [4][1]:

| Canoe Type | Primary Use | Approximate Length | Key Design Features |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Shovel-nose | River travel | 3-6 meters | Flat bottom, shallow draft, maneuverability in currents |
| Racing canoe | Ceremonial races | 8-12 meters | Narrow beam, deep hull, speed-optimized |
| War canoe | Open water, transport | 10-15+ meters | High prow and stern, seaworthy, capacity for 20+ paddlers |
| Freight canoe | Cargo transport | 8-12 meters | Wide beam, deep hull, stability under load |
| Whaling canoe | Open ocean (Nuu-chah-nulth, Makah) | 8-11 meters | Seaworthy, stable platform for harpooning |

### 5.1 Construction Process

A canoe is carved from a single cedar log. The process:

1. **Tree selection** -- Months of searching for the right tree (straight, no twist, correct diameter)
2. **Rough shaping** -- Exterior carved to approximate hull form
3. **Hollowing** -- Interior removed using controlled burning and adze work
4. **Steaming** -- Boiling water poured inside, heated with hot rocks, to soften the wood
5. **Spreading** -- Thwarts inserted to spread the steamed hull to final beam width
6. **Finishing** -- Final shaping, sanding, painting, and fitting of seats and gunwales

The steaming and spreading technique transforms a round log into a vessel with flared sides and a wider beam than the original tree's diameter -- a sophisticated engineering process that creates a hull form impossible to achieve by carving alone.

## 6. Bentwood Box Technique

The bentwood box is one of the most technically demanding cedar constructions. A single plank is kerfed (scored with grooves) at three points, steamed, and bent into a rectangular box with only one seam. The technique produces watertight containers used for cooking, storage, and ceremonial purposes [1][5].

### 6.1 Cooking Application

Bentwood boxes were used for boiling food. Hot rocks, heated in a fire, were placed into the box filled with water and food. The cedar walls are thick enough to insulate but thin enough at the kerf bends to flex without breaking. This is thermal engineering: the box is simultaneously a vessel, an insulator, and a cooking surface.

## 7. Longhouse Architecture

PNW Coast longhouses are post-and-beam structures using massive cedar timbers. A single longhouse could shelter multiple related families -- 20 to 100+ people -- with internal partitions separating family areas while sharing a common roof and central fire row [1][2].

Key architectural features:

- **Post-and-beam frame** -- Massive cedar posts (some 1+ meter diameter) supporting horizontal beams
- **Plank walls** -- Removable cedar planks allowing ventilation control and portability
- **Shed roof** -- Single-slope or double-slope roof with cedar bark or plank covering
- **Central fire row** -- Series of fire pits along the long axis, shared for heating and cooking
- **Raised sleeping platforms** -- Elevated platforms along the walls, above cold ground air
- **Smoke management** -- Adjustable roof planks for smoke ventilation

The longhouse is not merely shelter -- it is a social technology. The arrangement of families within the house, their relative positions, and their proximity to the chief's area encode social relationships in architectural space.

## 8. Rope, Baskets, and Weaving

### 8.1 Cedar Rope

Cedar bark rope is made by twisting processed inner bark fibers. Two-ply and three-ply ropes were produced for applications ranging from fishing line to canoe anchor lines to structural lashing. Cedar rope provided the tension system for reef net fishing -- a technology requiring coordinated rope management across multiple family groups [1].

### 8.2 Baskets

Cedar root and bark baskets serve storage, carrying, and ceremonial functions. Basket weaving traditions continue as active cultural practice, with notable revitalization programs among Musqueam, Cowichan, and other Coast Salish nations. Contemporary weavers maintain traditional techniques while adapting to new applications.

### 8.3 Weaving Revival

The Cowichan sweater tradition, while using wool introduced post-contact, employs Coast Salish weaving techniques with deep pre-contact roots. The Musqueam weaving revival, supported by the Museum of Anthropology at UBC, has documented and revitalized traditional weaving practices that were disrupted by colonial policies.

## 9. The Canoe as Being: An Ontological Bridge

The canoe in Coast Salish epistemology is not merely an artifact. In Western ontology (and in SUMO formal ontology), a canoe is classified as: `Canoe -> WaterVehicle -> TransportationDevice -> Artifact`. It is a thing made by humans for transportation [6].

In Coast Salish understanding, the canoe is a living being with its own spirit. It represents the journey of life. Everyone must paddle in rhythm. The puller (the person at the bow) trusts the skipper (the person at the stern, who represents the ancestors). The canoe journey is simultaneously physical travel and spiritual practice.

This is precisely the kind of ontological bridge the Heritage Pack makes visible without resolving. Both descriptions are true. They answer different questions:

| Western Ontology | Coast Salish Epistemology |
|-----------------|--------------------------|
| What is it made of? | Who is it? |
| How does it function? | What does the journey mean? |
| What class of artifact is it? | What relationship does the crew share? |

The bridge does not say one framework is correct and the other is metaphor. It says: here are two complete descriptions of the same entity, each internally consistent, each answering questions the other does not ask.
