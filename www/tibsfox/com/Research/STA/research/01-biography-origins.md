## 1. Birth and Parentage

**Stephen Valentine Patrick William Allen** was born December 26, 1921, in New York City. His parents were Billy Allen (born Carroll Abler, of German ancestry) and Belle Montrose (born Isabelle Donohue) — a husband-and-wife vaudeville comedy team who performed professionally.

Milton Berle later described Belle Montrose as "the funniest woman in vaudeville." The middle names Patrick and William were acquired during childhood; Valentine came from Val Stanton, a vaudeville actor and close family friend who served as godfather. The name Stephen itself was his given birth name.

**His father died when Allen was approximately eighteen months old.** This single biographical fact shaped the arc of his life: raised without a father, primarily by his mother's Irish Catholic family in Chicago, while his mother continued performing and was frequently absent.

---

## 2. The Vaudeville Heritage

Billy Allen and Belle Montrose worked the vaudeville circuit in the era when vaudeville was American entertainment — before radio, before film, before television. Vaudeville performers were generalists: comedians, musicians, actors, and ad-libbers who worked a live audience with no safety net.

This heritage transmitted to Allen not through instruction but through genetics and immersion:
- The comfort with a live audience
- The instinct for reading a room
- The ability to improvise when the script fails
- The discipline of timing
- The habit of watching other performers

Allen never trained formally as a comedian. He absorbed vaudeville by being born into it.

---

## 3. Chicago Childhood and the Reading Habit

After his father's death, the infant Allen was relocated to Chicago's South Side, where he was raised primarily by his mother's Irish Catholic family. His mother continued performing and was frequently absent. The result was a peripatetic childhood — Allen attended an estimated **18 different schools** as family circumstances shifted.

The instability had a compensating effect: Allen developed a **voracious reading habit** as both escape and anchor. Books were portable, reliable, and did not require social stability. The reading habit would eventually produce:
- 54+ authored books across multiple genres
- Deep engagement with biblical criticism, philosophy, and humanism
- The intellectual substrate for *Meeting of Minds*

## 4. Education: From Drake to Arizona State

Allen enrolled at **Drake University** in Des Moines, Iowa in 1941 on a journalism scholarship. He transferred to **Arizona State Teachers College** (now Arizona State University) in Tempe, Arizona, where he met his first wife, Dorothy Goodman.

He left during his sophomore year in 1942 to take a radio job at station KOY in Phoenix — the same decision pattern that would appear throughout his career: the practical opportunity preempts the formal credential.

Arizona State University later recognized the relationship:
- Alumni Achievement Award (1966)
- Honorary doctorate (1982)

---

## 5. Army Service

Allen enlisted in the U.S. Army during World War II and was trained as an infantryman at Camp Roberts, California. He saw no combat — he was discharged due to asthma. He returned to KOY after discharge.

The Army interlude is notable primarily for what it was not: it was not a formative military experience. Allen's wartime contribution was staying home, recovering from asthma, and returning to radio. The war had ended the vaudeville era while he was in the service.

## 6. Radio Career: KOY to KNX

Allen's radio career traces a clear arc:

| Year | Station | Market | Activity |
|------|---------|--------|---------|
| 1942 | KOY | Phoenix, AZ | First radio job; left Drake for this |
| 1943-1944 | KOY | Phoenix, AZ | Developed comedy-talk format |
| 1945 | KNX (CBS) / Mutual | Los Angeles | Relocated with Wendell Noble; *Smile Time* on Mutual |
| 1946+ | KNX | Los Angeles | Half-hour music-and-talk format; most popular nighttime radio program in LA history |

### 6.1 The Comedy Team with Wendell Noble

After returning from the Army, Allen formed a comedy team with fellow radio announcer Wendell Noble. Both relocated to Los Angeles in 1945, where they debuted *Smile Time* on the Mutual Broadcasting System network. The show ran for two years.

### 6.2 KNX Los Angeles

By 1946, Allen had moved to KNX, CBS's Los Angeles affiliate. His half-hour music-and-talk format gradually evolved — more conversation, less music, increasingly informal. The show became the **most popular nighttime radio program in Los Angeles** — a remarkable achievement for a format that was, in essence, Allen improvising.

## 7. The Doris Day Moment: Discovery of Improvisation

The pivotal event in Allen's creative development did not happen on television. It happened on radio at KNX:

A scheduled guest, **Doris Day**, failed to appear for her segment. Faced with empty airtime, Allen picked up a microphone, walked into the studio audience, and **ad-libbed** — talking to audience members, asking questions, riffing on their answers.

The technique worked. The audience responded. Allen discovered something he would use for the rest of his career: **the conversation between a performer and an audience is itself entertainment**.

This was the origin of:
- The man-on-the-street interviews on the Tonight Show
- The audience participation segments on the Steve Allen Show
- The cameras-outside-the-studio stunts that Letterman later made famous
- The informal, conversational register of all his television work

## 8. The Wrestling Announcer Detour

In 1949, Allen answered a newspaper advertisement for a television wrestling announcer. He had never watched wrestling and knew nothing about it.

His solution: he watched several shows and **invented descriptive names for the holds**. Some of these names — improvised by a radio comedian to describe maneuvers he'd never attempted — remain in use in wrestling commentary today.

The anecdote reveals Allen's method: when you don't know the terminology, make it up. If the made-up term is better than the official term, it persists. This is the same approach he applied to television format: he didn't know what a late-night talk show was because there wasn't one yet. So he invented it.

## 9. First Marriage and Family

Allen married **Dorothy Goodman** on August 23, 1943. They had three sons:
- **Steve Jr.** — later a doctor in Elmira, New York
- **Brian** — later a real estate broker in Seattle
- **David** — later a songwriter in Los Angeles

The marriage ended in divorce in 1952.

### 9.1 Brian's Commune Experience

In the mid-1970s, Allen's son Brian joined a commune — an experience that "hurt and stunned" Allen initially. He later wrote about it in his book *Beloved Son*, eventually coming to better understand his son's beliefs. Allen's willingness to write publicly about a painful family experience, and to ultimately reach understanding, reflects his intellectual approach to personal difficulty.

---

## 10. Jayne Meadows and the Second Marriage

Allen met actress **Jayne Meadows** at a dinner party in 1952. According to accounts, Allen was uncharacteristically speechless throughout the evening. At the end of dinner, Meadows remarked that he was either the rudest or the shyest man she had ever met.

They married on **July 31, 1954**, in Waterford, Connecticut. They had one son:
- **William Christopher Allen** — later an executive at MTM Television

Allen and Meadows remained married for **46 years** until his death in 2000. Meadows described him as "my best friend and my partner on stage and off for more than 48 years." She played most of the female historical roles in *Meeting of Minds*. She died in 2015 and is buried beside him at Forest Lawn Hollywood Hills.

---

## 11. The Move to New York and CBS Television

In 1950, Allen relocated to New York to host *The Steve Allen Show* on CBS — a half-hour program that served as a proving ground for his television style. The show ran until 1952 and was only moderately successful but established his presence in the New York broadcast community.

The CBS show led directly to the Tonight Show opportunity: Allen was visible, available, and had demonstrated he could fill time with wit and conversation. When WNBT-TV in New York needed a late-night host in 1953, Allen was the answer.
