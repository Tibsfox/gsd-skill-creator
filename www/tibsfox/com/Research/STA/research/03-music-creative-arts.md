## 1. The Guinness Record: Most Prolific Modern Composer

Allen estimated that he composed more than **8,500 songs** across his lifetime. He was listed in the *Guinness Book of World Records* as the **most prolific modern composer of songs** — the 1984 record edition cited over 4,000 compositions; later estimates from Allen himself climbed higher as he continued writing.

Key context:
- His musical training was limited to **three years of piano lessons** beginning at age seven
- The vast majority of his songs were never recorded or performed publicly
- Only a small fraction became commercially significant
- The count is an estimate, not a verified catalog

The composing habit was daily, compulsive, and lifelong — not a burst of creativity that faded but a sustained practice across seven decades.

## 2. The Wallach's Music City Window Bet

In one of the most remarkable demonstrations of compositional speed in music history, Allen bet singer **Frankie Laine** that he could write **50 songs per day for a week**.

The terms: Allen would compose publicly, in the window of **Wallach's Music City**, a Hollywood music store. He met the quota and won $1,000.

One song from the marathon — **"Let's Go to Church (Next Sunday Morning)"** — became a chart hit for Jimmy Wakely and Margaret Whiting, reaching:
- No. 13 on the pop chart
- No. 2 on the country chart (1950)

The stunt established that Allen's composing was not a special-occasion activity but an always-available capability — like a jazz improviser who can generate material on demand.

## 4. The Grammy Award: "Gravy Waltz"

**"Gravy Waltz"** was originally composed and performed by bassist **Ray Brown** as an instrumental in the early 1960s. Allen later wrote **lyrics** for it.

The collaboration won the **1964 Grammy Award for Best Original Jazz Composition**.

This Grammy (his only Grammy as a recording artist) arrived at an unusual angle — not for a comedic song, not for a pop standard, but for a jazz composition that began as someone else's instrumental and became Allen's through the addition of lyrics. It demonstrated his musical range beyond novelty and beyond his television persona.

## 5. Jazz Piano Career: 50+ Albums

Allen recorded approximately **50–75 albums** across his career, establishing himself as a legitimate jazz pianist beyond his television celebrity:

| Album / Label | Year | Notes |
|--------------|------|-------|
| *Steve Allen At The Piano* (Columbia) | 1951 | First album |
| Various (Decca, Brunswick, Coral) | 1950s | Prolific recording period |
| *Music for Tonight* (Dot) | 1955 | No. 7 Billboard |
| *Gravy Waltz & 11 Current Hits* (Dot) | 1963 | No. 41 Billboard |
| *Funny Fone-Calls* (Dot) | 1963 | No. 70 Billboard |

Allen maintained a long-term musical partnership with vibraphonist **Terry Gibbs**, performing duets as late as 1997 — 46 years after his first album.

His piano playing was jazz-inflected but accessible — not a bebop virtuoso but a skilled, versatile, lyrical pianist who could accompany, solo, and collaborate equally well.

## 6. The Jack Kerouac Collaboration

### 6.1 The Village Vanguard Disaster

In 1957, Jack Kerouac was booked for a jazz-accompanied poetry reading at the **Village Vanguard** in New York. The first night was a disaster: Kerouac was nervous and did not connect with the band. The planned collaboration failed in front of a live audience.

### 6.2 Allen's Intervention

On the second night, Steve Allen attended and offered **impromptu piano accompaniment**. The collaboration was immediately successful — Allen's gentle, jazz-inflected piano provided sympathetic counterpoint to Kerouac's spoken-word delivery without competing with it.

This is the same instinct he'd shown at KNX with the Doris Day no-show: when the planned thing fails, improvise something better.

### 6.3 The Recording Session

The session was arranged and recorded in approximately **one hour**. Kerouac insisted: **the first take would be the only take**.

The resulting album:
- **Title:** *Poetry for the Beat Generation*
- **Label:** Hanover Records (1959, after rejection controversy — see below)
- **Status:** Landmark recording; reissued multiple times; considered canonical in the jazz-poetry genre

### 6.4 The Dot Records Controversy

The album was nearly suppressed when Dot Records president **Randy Wood** declared Kerouac's language obscene. This necessitated release on the smaller Hanover label — giving the album a more authentic underground provenance and protecting its Beat Generation identity.

**Source:** The Beat Museum; All About Jazz album review.

## 7. Broadway: Sophie and Belle Starr

Allen's Broadway contributions were ambitious, if not acclaimed:

### 7.1 *Sophie* (1963)

Allen wrote the music and lyrics for *Sophie*, a musical based on the life of **Sophie Tucker** (the self-proclaimed "Last of the Red Hot Mamas"). The show opened at the **Winter Garden Theatre** in New York. It received poor reviews and closed quickly.

### 7.2 *Belle Starr* (1969)

*Belle Starr*, a London musical **starring Betty Grable**, also received poor reviews.

Both failures are notable because Allen attempted them at all — the television star who composed prolifically, who had proven himself in music and comedy, reaching for a theatrical format that never quite accepted him. The failures did not prevent the attempt.

## 8. Film: The Benny Goodman Story

Allen's most notable film role was the title character in **The Benny Goodman Story** (1956, Universal-International).

He recalled that his one contribution to the film's music came in early scenes requiring the sound of a clarinet beginner: "the only sound Allen could produce on a clarinet." The real Benny Goodman provided the actual clarinet performances for the mature character sequences.

Other film appearances included cameos as himself in:
- **Robert Altman's *The Player*** (1992)
- **Martin Scorsese's *Casino*** (1995)

The casting in *The Player* and *Casino* reflects Allen's cultural stature at the end of his career — directors of that generation used him as a shorthand for American entertainment history.

---

## 9. Television Music: Alice in Wonderland

In 1985, Allen wrote **19 songs** for a CBS television musical adaptation of **Alice in Wonderland**, with Jayne Meadows as the **Queen of Hearts**. The production was an example of Allen integrating his musical, television, and family life into a single project.

## 10. Allen's Complex Relationship with Rock and Roll

Allen's feelings about rock and roll were genuinely mixed. He:
- Booked Presley, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Fats Domino on his prime-time show
- Staged the famous "Hound Dog" comedy bit that simultaneously promoted and mocked Presley
- Made clear through framing and commentary that he considered himself a jazz musician first

The tension was generational and aesthetic, not commercial. Allen's musical reference points were jazz, standards, and the Great American Songbook — he understood rock and roll as a cultural phenomenon but not as music he personally valued.

Later in life, he extended his criticism to what he perceived as broader cultural coarsening (see Module 04: Intellectual Life), but his mixed feelings about rock were present from the beginning.
