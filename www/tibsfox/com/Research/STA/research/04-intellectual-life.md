## 1. The Literary Output: 54+ Books

Allen authored **over 54 books** across a remarkable range of genres. The books represent the full breadth of his intellectual and creative life — not a narrow specialty but a voracious generalism:

| Genre | Key Titles |
|-------|-----------|
| **Social criticism** | *Dumbth: The Lost Art of Thinking* (1989/1998) |
| **Religious criticism** | *Steve Allen on the Bible, Religion, and Morality* (1990), sequel volume |
| **Humor analysis** | *Funny People* (1981) |
| **Mystery novels** | *The Talk Show Murders* (1982) and series |
| **Autobiography** | *Mark It and Strike It* (1960); *Hi-Ho Steverino!* (1992) |
| **Political commentary** | *But Seriously...* |
| **Philosophical essays** | *Reflections* |
| **Family memoir** | *Beloved Son* (about Brian's commune experience) |
| **Poetry** | *Windfall* (1946) — his first published collection |
| **Short stories** | *The Man Who Turned Back the Clock* |
| **Children's/educational** | *Gullible's Travels* (audio) |
| **Posthumous** | *Vulgarians at the Gate: Trash TV and Raunch Radio* (2001) |

Allen worked with publisher **Paul Kurtz at Prometheus Books** on 15 titles — Prometheus being the leading publisher of skeptical, humanist, and freethought literature.

## 2. Key Works: Dumbth and the Critical Thinking Mission

**Dumbth: The Lost Art of Thinking with 101 Ways to Reason Better and Improve Your Mind** (1989; expanded edition 1998) is Allen's most sustained and direct statement of a concern he carried throughout his life: that American culture was becoming intellectually lazy.

The argument of *Dumbth*:
- Americans increasingly could not think clearly, evaluate evidence, or draw valid conclusions
- This was not a failure of intelligence but a failure of practice and discipline
- The solution was explicit training in reasoning — what general semantics called "extensional orientation"
- The book provided 101 specific techniques for thinking more clearly

The book sold steadily and was revised and expanded in 1998. It was recommended reading in the scientific skepticism community and is still cited in critical thinking education contexts.

## 3. Religious and Philosophical Evolution

### 3.1 The Catholic Upbringing

Allen was raised **Roman Catholic** by his mother's Irish Catholic family on Chicago's South Side. Catholicism was not merely nominal — it provided the moral seriousness and the rhetorical rigor that shaped even his criticism of religion.

### 3.2 Automatic Excommunication

Allen was **automatically excommunicated** from the Catholic Church in his early thirties due to his second marriage (to Jayne Meadows after his divorce from Dorothy Goodman). Church law excommunicates Catholics who remarry after divorce.

### 3.3 The Intellectual Transition

His doubts about Catholicism began, by his own account, in his mid-twenties. The transition followed a pattern:
1. Doubt the specific claims and authority of Roman Catholicism
2. Study the broader philosophical literature on religion
3. Reach secular humanism — not atheism as he insisted, but a position that acknowledged "time-honored, creative, and intellectually respectable arguments for the existence of God" while rejecting organized religion and biblical literalism

### 3.4 The Voltaire Comparison

Reviewers compared his biblical criticism volumes to Voltaire's *Philosophical Dictionary* and Thomas Paine's *The Age of Reason*. The comparison is apt: all three are critical intellects applying systematic analysis to religious texts and authority, writing for general audiences rather than academic specialists.

## 4. The Biblical Criticism Volumes

*Steve Allen on the Bible, Religion, and Morality* (1990) and its sequel addressed topics arranged alphabetically — from abortion to capital punishment to evolution to original sin — drawing on scholarship from Catholic, Protestant, and Jewish biblical scholars.

The project was:
- A public intellectual exercise in applying scholarly biblical criticism to popular religious belief
- A direct challenge to fundamentalist literalism
- An attempt to model the kind of careful thinking about scripture that Allen felt most believers avoided

The books were controversial in religious circles and ignored in many mainstream media venues. They were published by Prometheus Books, which specialized in exactly this kind of work.

## 5. CSICOP and Scientific Skepticism

The **Committee for the Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal (CSICOP)** — later renamed the Committee for Skeptical Inquiry (CSI) — was founded in 1976 by Paul Kurtz, with Allen as a prominent member and supporter.

### 5.1 Allen's Role

Allen became a **Humanist Laureate** for the Academy of Humanism and an active CSICOP member who:
- Wrote for *Skeptical Inquirer* magazine
- Appeared at skeptics conferences
- Publicly challenged pseudoscientific claims on television
- Served on the Council for Media Integrity (debunking pseudoscientific TV claims)

### 5.2 Awards

| Award | From | Year |
|-------|------|------|
| Martin Gardner Lifetime Achievement Award | CSICOP | 1996 |
| Inclusion in Pantheon of Skeptics | Committee for Skeptical Inquiry | 2011 (posthumous) |

### 5.3 Topics Allen Addressed in Skeptic Writing

- Scientology (critical analysis)
- The concept of genius (what it is and isn't)
- The legacy of Isaac Asimov
- Television's promotion of supernatural belief

## 6. Council for Secular Humanism

Allen was an active member of the **Council for Secular Humanism**, the organization founded by Paul Kurtz as a practical ethics and humanist organization distinct from academic philosophy.

He also received the **Rose Elizabeth Bird Commitment to Justice Award** from Death Penalty Focus in 1998 — connecting his secular humanism to practical criminal justice advocacy.

## 7. General Semantics: The Korzybski Connection

**General semantics** is the discipline founded by Alfred Korzybski (1879–1950), based on the claim that much human suffering and confusion arises from the misuse of language — treating words as things, confusing maps with territories, making unconscious linguistic assumptions.

Allen was a student and supporter of general semantics. He:
- Recommended it in *Dumbth* as a tool for clearer thinking
- Connected it to his concern about "dumbth" — imprecise language as both symptom and cause of imprecise thinking
- Delivered the **Alfred Korzybski Memorial Lecture in 1992** — a significant honor within the general semantics community

The connection between general semantics and Allen's career is coherent: a man who made his career through precision of language (comedy depends on exact word choice) finding in Korzybski a formal theory of why language precision matters.

## 8. The LSD Experiment

In the **late 1950s** — before LSD was classified as a controlled substance — Allen participated in supervised clinical experiments with LSD.

### 8.1 The Context

The sessions were arranged by author and philosopher **Gerald Heard** and conducted by psychiatrist **Sidney Cohen**, both of whom were running a supervised clinical program for "intelligent, adventurous people." The program predated both the counterculture embrace of LSD and the government's subsequent prohibition.

### 8.2 Allen's Positioning

Allen was neither an enthusiast nor a moralist about the experience. He documented it matter-of-factly as a historical fact about his life in its specific context — not as an endorsement of recreational drug use, and not as a cautionary tale.

The LSD experiment is one of several places where Allen's biography intersects with 1950s countercultural figures (Kerouac collaboration, Heard friendship) while he simultaneously represented mainstream television entertainment.

## 9. Media Criticism: Vulgarians at the Gate

**"Vulgarians at the Gate: Trash TV and Raunch Radio"** (2001, posthumous) was Allen's final book — completed before his death but published afterward.

Despite being a lifelong political liberal and Democrat, Allen was a **moral conservative** regarding media content. He:
- Actively supported the Parents Television Council
- Sponsored newspaper advertisements urging broadcasters and parents to exercise responsibility
- Was highly critical of Howard Stern and shock jocks
- On the day before his death, placed a full-page newspaper advertisement urging people to alert sponsors that "TV is leading children down a moral sewer"

The paradox of Allen as entertainment innovator AND media moral critic is genuine, not hypocrisy. He built the medium; he believed the medium had obligations it was failing to meet.

## 10. The Mystery Novel Series

Beginning with *The Talk Show Murders* (1982), Allen authored a series of mystery novels featuring himself and Jayne Meadows as amateur detectives. The books were written under his name and marketed on his celebrity.

They were later revealed to have been **ghostwritten** — first by Walter J. Sheldon, then by Robert Westbrook. Allen's name and celebrity provided the commercial hook; the actual prose was someone else's.

This was a fairly common commercial arrangement in celebrity publishing. Allen's contribution was the concept, the character based on himself, and the endorsement. The revelation did not significantly damage his reputation because the books were clearly a commercial side project rather than a core creative statement.

## 11. Critical Thinking for Children: Gullible's Travels

Allen produced **Gullible's Travels** — an audiotape with original music designed to **introduce children to critical thinking**. The project extends his *Dumbth* mission to a younger audience, using the combination of music and narrative that had been central to his career from the beginning.
