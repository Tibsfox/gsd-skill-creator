# The PNW Naturalist -- A Cross-Project Learning Path

A unified learning experience that connects five research projects into a single journey: from indoor study through outdoor field work to genuine Pacific Northwest naturalist competence. This path weaves together raccoon ecology, wild birds, PNW timber, plein air painting, and citizen preparedness into something larger than any of them alone -- an integrated understanding of the place you live.

The Pacific Northwest is a landscape of extremes: temperate rainforest and alpine desert, volcanic peaks and tidal flats, old-growth giants and backyard raccoons. A naturalist is someone who pays attention to all of it. Not a specialist, but a generalist with depth -- someone who can identify the tree, the bird in the tree, the tracks beneath the tree, and who can sit down and paint what they see, knowing they're prepared if the weather turns.

**Projects combined:** RCN (Raccoons), FWL (Fowl), WDC (Woodworking/Timber), PNT (Painting), FSR (First Response)
**Total estimated time:** 60-100 hours across 10 modules
**Difficulty:** Beginner to intermediate. No prior outdoor or scientific experience needed.
**College of Knowledge departments:** Nature Studies, Science, Art, Trades, Physical Education

---

## Module 1 -- Eyes Open: Learning to See the Landscape

*The first skill of a naturalist is attention. Before you identify anything, you learn to notice.*

**Time:** 3-4 hours

This module is entirely indoors. You're building the foundation of knowledge that turns a walk in the woods from "nice trees" into a rich, layered experience.

### Readings
- **[RCN: 01-biology-taxonomy.md](../RCN/research/01-biology-taxonomy.md)** -- Introduction to raccoon biology. You share this landscape with them; learn who they are.
- **[FWL: 08-wild-galliformes-pnw.md](../FWL/research/08-wild-galliformes-pnw.md)** -- The wild relatives of chickens that live in PNW forests: grouse, quail, pheasant. You'll start hearing them before you see them.
- **[WDC: 02-wood-science.md](../WDC/research/02-wood-science.md)** -- What wood actually is: cell structure, grain, growth rings. Every tree you'll encounter is built from this.

### Outcome
You can name the three kingdoms of PNW wildlife observation (mammal, bird, tree) and explain basic biology for each. You know what a raccoon is, what a Ruffed Grouse sounds like, and why a tree has rings.

## Module 2 -- Tracks and Signs: Reading the Forest Floor

*A naturalist sees what's there now. A tracker sees what was there before.*

**Time:** 3-4 hours (reading + first field session)

### Activities
- **[RCN: try-sessions/track-identification.md](../RCN/research/try-sessions/track-identification.md)** -- Learn raccoon tracks and distinguish them from other PNW animals. Then go outside and look for them.
- **[WDC: study-guides/wood-identification-guide.md](../WDC/research/study-guides/wood-identification-guide.md)** -- How to identify common PNW tree species by bark, leaves, needles, and wood grain.

### Readings
- **[RCN: 03-diet-foraging.md](../RCN/research/03-diet-foraging.md)** -- What raccoons eat and how they find it. Foraging signs are everywhere once you know what to look for.

### Outcome
You can identify raccoon tracks in mud or snow, distinguish them from opossum and skunk tracks, and identify at least 5 PNW tree species by sight. You're starting to read the forest floor as a text, not just a surface.

## Module 3 -- Timber: The Trees That Built This Place

*Every PNW naturalist needs to know the trees. They're the foundation of everything else.*

**Time:** 4-5 hours

### Readings
- **[WDC: species/western-red-cedar.md](../WDC/research/species/western-red-cedar.md)** -- The tree that defines PNW Indigenous culture and PNW architecture. Aromatic, rot-resistant, sacred.
- **[WDC: species/douglas-fir-profile.md](../WDC/research/species/douglas-fir-profile.md)** -- The tree that built the Pacific Northwest economy. The most commercially important timber species in the region.
- **[WDC: 09-pnw-timber.md](../WDC/research/09-pnw-timber.md)** -- The full PNW timber story: old growth, logging history, sustainable forestry, and the relationship between humans and forests.

### Readings -- Ecological Context
- **[RCN: 06-pnw-ecology.md](../RCN/research/06-pnw-ecology.md)** -- Raccoons in the PNW ecosystem. The trees you're learning to identify are the trees raccoons den in.

### Outcome
You can identify Western Red Cedar and Douglas Fir in the field by bark, foliage, and scent. You understand the ecological and economic significance of PNW forests. You know that the forest is habitat, not just scenery.

## Module 4 -- Night Shift: Nocturnal Observation

*Most of the PNW's mammalian drama happens after dark. A naturalist learns to work the night shift.*

**Time:** 6-10 hours (multiple evening/night sessions)

### Activities
- **[RCN: try-sessions/night-observation.md](../RCN/research/try-sessions/night-observation.md)** -- Structured nocturnal observation protocol. Where to sit, how to use red light, what to watch for.
- **[RCN: deep-dives/raccoon-sounds.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/raccoon-sounds.md)** -- Learn raccoon vocalizations. Once you know these sounds, the night forest becomes a conversation you can eavesdrop on.

### Readings
- **[RCN: 02-intelligence-behavior.md](../RCN/research/02-intelligence-behavior.md)** -- Raccoon intelligence and behavior. Context for what you observe during night sessions.

### Outcome
You can conduct a productive nocturnal wildlife observation session. You can identify raccoon vocalizations and interpret basic behaviors. The night forest is no longer a void -- it's a populated landscape you can navigate.

## Module 5 -- Backyard Birds: From Window to Field

*The easiest wildlife to observe is the wildlife that comes to you.*

**Time:** 4-6 hours

### Activities
- **[FWL: try-sessions/breed-identification.md](../FWL/research/try-sessions/breed-identification.md)** -- Train your eye for distinguishing bird characteristics. This is designed for domestic breeds but the identification skills transfer directly to wild birds.

### Readings
- **[FWL: 01-domestication-history.md](../FWL/research/01-domestication-history.md)** -- Where domestic birds came from. Understanding domestication deepens your understanding of wild birds.
- **[FWL: guides/predator-defense-pnw.md](../FWL/research/guides/predator-defense-pnw.md)** -- PNW predators: hawks, raccoons, coyotes. This guide was written for chicken keepers, but every species mentioned is a species you'll encounter as a naturalist.

### Connections
- **[RCN: study-guides/pnw-mesocarnivore-comparison.md](../RCN/research/study-guides/pnw-mesocarnivore-comparison.md)** -- Compare raccoons to other PNW mesocarnivores. These are the predators in the predator-prey web that includes the birds you're observing.

### Outcome
You can identify PNW backyard birds with reasonable confidence. You understand the predator-prey dynamics that play out in your yard every day. You see the food web, not just individual species.

## Module 6 -- Painting the Landscape: Plein Air Basics

*A naturalist who can sketch captures more than one who only writes.*

**Time:** 6-8 hours (reading + at least 2 outdoor painting sessions)

### Activities
- **[PNT: try-sessions/paint-a-landscape.md](../PNT/research/try-sessions/paint-a-landscape.md)** -- Your first plein air (outdoor) painting. Take your paints outside and paint what you see. The PNW is one of the greatest plein air subjects on Earth.
- **[PNT: techniques/plein-air-guide.md](../PNT/research/techniques/plein-air-guide.md)** -- The practical guide to painting outdoors in the PNW: managing rain, changing light, and the gear you need.

### Readings
- **[PNT: 05-color-theory-technique.md](../PNT/research/05-color-theory-technique.md)** -- Color theory for painters. Understanding color transforms your observation -- you start seeing the blue in shadows, the warm light on cedar bark, the grey-green of Douglas Fir needles.
- **[PNT: artists/northwest-school.md](../PNT/research/artists/northwest-school.md)** -- The Northwest School painters who defined PNW art. Their work is the artistic expression of the landscape you're learning.

### Outcome
You can set up and paint outdoors in PNW conditions. Your nature observation is now dual-channel: scientific (identification, behavior, ecology) and artistic (color, light, composition). Your field sketches are becoming a visual record of your naturalist practice.

*You can't be in the field 24 hours a day. But a camera can.*

**Time:** 4-6 hours (setup + first week of monitoring)

### Activities
- **[RCN: try-sessions/build-a-trail-cam-station.md](../RCN/research/try-sessions/build-a-trail-cam-station.md)** -- Set up a trail camera to monitor wildlife activity when you're not present. Camera selection, placement strategy, data management.

### Readings
- **[RCN: 05-lifecycle-reproduction.md](../RCN/research/05-lifecycle-reproduction.md)** -- Raccoon life cycle and reproduction. Understanding seasonal patterns helps you predict what your trail camera will capture.
- **[RCN: 04-urban-adaptation.md](../RCN/research/04-urban-adaptation.md)** -- How raccoons adapt to urban environments. If your trail camera is in a suburban yard, this is the ecological context for what you'll see.

### Outcome
You have a functioning trail camera station producing regular wildlife data. You can analyze footage for species identification, behavioral patterns, and seasonal variation. You're doing real wildlife monitoring.

**Time:** 4-5 hours

- **[RCN: deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease.md)** -- Zoonotic disease ecology. Not to be afraid, but to be informed.
- **[FWL: study-guides/genetics-study-guide.md](../FWL/research/study-guides/genetics-study-guide.md)** -- Genetics basics. Understanding heredity gives depth to every population you observe.
- **[FWL: 05-egg-science.md](../FWL/research/05-egg-science.md)** -- Egg biology. Whether wild or domestic, eggs are a window into avian reproduction.

### Outcome
You understand the PNW ecosystem as a web of relationships, not a collection of species. You can discuss predator-prey dynamics, seasonal patterns, habitat use, and human-wildlife coexistence with genuine understanding.

## Module 9 -- Prepared for the Field: Safety and Self-Reliance

*A naturalist goes where the wildlife is. That means knowing how to take care of yourself.*

**Time:** 4-6 hours

### Activities

### Readings
- **[FSR: guides/citizen-preparedness.md](../FSR/research/guides/citizen-preparedness.md)** -- Citizen preparedness fundamentals. Being prepared for weather, injury, and getting lost is part of being a responsible outdoor naturalist.
- **[RCN: deep-dives/baby-raccoons.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/baby-raccoons.md)** -- What to do when you find baby raccoons (or any wildlife). The naturalist's "when to act and when to leave alone" guide.
- **[RCN: try-sessions/latrine-survey.md](../RCN/research/try-sessions/latrine-survey.md)** -- Safety awareness for raccoon latrines (Baylisascaris). Know the risk, use PPE, stay safe.

You have basic emergency medical skills for field situations. You know how to prepare for outdoor field work safely. You can make responsible decisions about wildlife encounters (when to help, when to observe, when to leave).

## Module 10 -- The Living Notebook: An Ongoing Practice

*A naturalist is not a degree. It's a practice. It starts the day you pay attention and it never ends.*

**Time:** Ongoing

### Integration Activities

- **Seasonal observation cycle.** Visit the same location monthly. Note what changes: bird activity, raccoon signs, tree phenology (budding, leafing, dropping), light quality for painting. Your trail camera data creates a parallel record.

- **Field journal.** Combine written observations, quick sketches (from your PNT training), and data (tracks, species counts, trail camera captures). The field journal is your naturalist's primary tool.

- **Citizen science.** Log observations on iNaturalist. Submit trail camera data to wildlife surveys. Your observations become data that researchers use. Every entry adds to the collective understanding of PNW ecology.

- **Share what you know.** Take a friend on a nature walk and point out the raccoon tracks, the Western Red Cedar bark, the Ruffed Grouse drumming in the distance. The naturalist tradition is a tradition of sharing.

### Readings for Ongoing Study
- **[RCN: 07-cultural-history.md](../RCN/research/07-cultural-history.md)** -- The raccoon in human culture, from Indigenous stories to modern urban encounters.
- **[FWL: 07-heritage-conservation.md](../FWL/research/07-heritage-conservation.md)** -- Heritage breed conservation. The naturalist's ethic extends to domesticated biodiversity.
- **[WDC: 01-history-of-woodworking.md](../WDC/research/01-history-of-woodworking.md)** -- The history of working with wood. Every PNW timber tree you identify has a human story attached.
- **[PNT: 09-pnw-painting-scene.md](../PNT/research/09-pnw-painting-scene.md)** -- The contemporary PNW painting scene. Plein air groups often overlap with naturalist communities.

### Outcome
You have an integrated practice that combines observation, documentation, art, and ecology into a way of seeing the Pacific Northwest. You're contributing to citizen science. You're a naturalist -- not because of a title, but because of how you pay attention.

## College of Knowledge Departments

This path touches these College departments:

- **Nature Studies** -- The core discipline. Ecology, field observation, species identification, ecosystem thinking.
- **Science** -- Biology, genetics, ecology, zoonotic disease, wood science.
- **Art** -- Plein air painting, field sketching, visual documentation, the Northwest School.
- **Trades** -- Tree identification for woodworking, practical field skills.
- **Physical Education** -- Outdoor fitness, field safety, endurance for long observation sessions.
- **Mind-Body** -- Patience, stillness, sensory attention, the meditative quality of nature observation.
