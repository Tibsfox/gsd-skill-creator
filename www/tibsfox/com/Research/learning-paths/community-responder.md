# The Community Responder -- A Cross-Project Learning Path

A unified learning experience that connects four research projects into a single journey through community preparedness, emergency response, and the practical skills that make you the person your neighborhood turns to when something goes wrong. This path weaves together first response training, wildlife encounter management, animal first aid basics, and emergency construction skills into a comprehensive picture of what it means to be a community asset.

A community responder is not a professional first responder. You're not a firefighter, a paramedic, or a police officer. You're the person who knows CPR, who has a go bag, who understands the Incident Command System, who can stop bleeding, who knows what to do when a raccoon is acting strangely, and who can build emergency shelter from available materials. You're the person who stays calm, who acts, and who holds things together until the professionals arrive. Every community needs more of you.

**Projects combined:** FSR (First Response), RCN (Raccoons), FWL (Fowl), WDC (Woodworking)
**Total estimated time:** 50-80 hours across 10 modules (some modules require external in-person training)
**Difficulty:** Beginner to intermediate. Physical fitness helps but isn't required to start.
**College of Knowledge departments:** Science, Mind-Body, Physical Education, Learning, Nature Studies, Trades

## Module 1 -- The First Five Minutes: Life-Saving Basics

*Most emergencies are decided in the first five minutes. Learn what to do in those five minutes.*

**Time:** 3-4 hours

This is the most important module in the entire path. If you complete nothing else, complete this. The skills in Module 1 -- calling 911, CPR, and bleeding control -- cover the vast majority of civilian emergency situations.

### Activities
- **[FSR: try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md](../FSR/research/try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md)** -- Hands-only CPR: chest compressions at the right rate and depth. No mouth-to-mouth needed. Nearly as effective as conventional CPR for the first several minutes of sudden cardiac arrest.
- **[FSR: try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md](../FSR/research/try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md)** -- Stop the Bleed: direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application. Hemorrhage is the #1 preventable cause of death in trauma.
- **[FSR: try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill.md](../FSR/research/try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill.md)** -- Fire extinguisher: PASS technique, when to fight and when to evacuate.

**Critical follow-up:** Take in-person CPR and Stop the Bleed classes. The try-sessions prepare you, but hands-on training with an instructor is irreplaceable. CPR classes are available through the Red Cross and American Heart Association. Stop the Bleed classes are free -- check stopthebleed.org.

### Readings
- **[FSR: 01-history-first-responders.md](../FSR/research/01-history-first-responders.md)** -- The history of emergency response. Context for the system you're learning to support.

### Outcome
You can perform CPR, control life-threatening bleeding, and use a fire extinguisher. You know how to make an effective 911 call. You are now more capable in an emergency than approximately 95% of the population. That's not an exaggeration.

## Module 2 -- The Go Bag: Personal Preparedness

*You can't help your community if you're not prepared yourself. Start with your own household.*

**Time:** 4-5 hours

### Activities
- **[FSR: try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md](../FSR/research/try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md)** -- Build a 72-hour go bag: the supplies you grab when you need to leave your home in a hurry. What to include, what to skip, how to maintain it.

### Readings
- **[FSR: guides/citizen-preparedness.md](../FSR/research/guides/citizen-preparedness.md)** -- Comprehensive citizen preparedness: household plans, neighborhood organizing, special needs, pets.
- **[FSR: case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md](../FSR/research/case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md)** -- The Cascadia Subduction Zone earthquake: the event that defines PNW preparedness. When (not if) this happens, professional response will be overwhelmed for days or weeks. Community responders fill the gap.

### Outcome
You have a maintained go bag. Your household has an emergency plan. You understand the specific threats the PNW faces (earthquake, lahar, flooding, windstorms) and have prepared for them. You can evacuate in 5 minutes.

## Module 4 -- Wildlife Encounters: When Animals Need Help (Or Space)

*In the PNW, wildlife encounters are part of emergency response. Know when to help, when to back away, and when to call a professional.*

**Time:** 4-5 hours

### Readings
- **[RCN: 04-urban-adaptation.md](../RCN/research/04-urban-adaptation.md)** -- Urban raccoons: behavioral changes, boldness, innovation. The raccoon you encounter in an emergency is an urban animal with urban behaviors.
- **[RCN: deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/raccoons-and-disease.md)** -- Zoonotic disease: rabies, Baylisascaris, leptospirosis. Evidence-based risk assessment. A community responder needs to know the real risks, not the panicked assumptions.
- **[RCN: deep-dives/baby-raccoons.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/baby-raccoons.md)** -- Found baby raccoons? The answer is almost always "leave them alone, their mom is coming back." Know when to wait, when to call a rehabber, and how to tell the difference.
- **[RCN: deep-dives/raccoon-proofing.md](../RCN/research/deep-dives/raccoon-proofing.md)** -- Raccoon-proofing: practical coexistence. After a disaster, displaced animals become a community issue. Knowing how to manage the situation calmly is a community service.

### Safety
- **[RCN: try-sessions/latrine-survey.md](../RCN/research/try-sessions/latrine-survey.md)** -- Raccoon latrine safety. Baylisascaris procyonis is a real health risk. Know how to identify latrines, avoid exposure, and clean up safely if needed. PPE protocols matter.

### Outcome
You can assess wildlife encounters calmly and make appropriate decisions: observe, avoid, contact animal control, or contact wildlife rehabilitation. You know the real zoonotic disease risks (not zero, not catastrophic) and how to protect yourself. You can advise anxious neighbors with evidence-based information.

## Module 5 -- Animal First Aid: Basics for Non-Veterinarians

*Your chickens during an earthquake. Your neighbor's dog hit by a car. An injured bird on the trail. Basic principles apply.*

**Time:** 3-4 hours

### Readings
- **[FWL: study-guides/poultry-health-basics.md](../FWL/research/study-guides/poultry-health-basics.md)** -- Poultry health basics: common diseases, injury treatment, when to call a vet. Written for chicken keepers, but the principles of animal first aid (stop bleeding, prevent infection, reduce stress, seek professional help) are universal.
- **[FWL: 06-husbandry-guide.md](../FWL/research/06-husbandry-guide.md)** -- Animal husbandry: daily care, recognizing distress, maintaining health. The fundamentals of keeping animals alive and comfortable apply to emergency animal care.
- **[FWL: guides/predator-defense-pnw.md](../FWL/research/guides/predator-defense-pnw.md)** -- PNW predators: hawks, raccoons, coyotes, bears. Understanding the threats helps you anticipate and prevent animal emergencies.

### Connections
- **[RCN: 06-pnw-ecology.md](../RCN/research/06-pnw-ecology.md)** -- PNW ecology. Context for the wildlife you may encounter during emergencies -- displaced by fire, flood, earthquake, or storm.

### Outcome
You understand basic animal first aid principles: stop bleeding, prevent shock, minimize stress, prevent infection, transport safely, get professional help. You can recognize common signs of animal distress. You know which PNW wildlife to approach (none, generally) and which domestic animals you can safely help.

**Important:** This module teaches awareness and basic principles, not veterinary skills. Always defer to veterinary professionals when available.

## Module 6 -- Emergency Shelter: Building with What's Available

*After a disaster, shelter is a survival need. Woodworking skills translate directly to emergency construction.*

**Time:** 5-7 hours

### Activities
- **[WDC: try-sessions/first-joint.md](../WDC/research/try-sessions/first-joint.md)** -- Cut your first wood joints. In an emergency, the ability to join wood into a structure -- even a crude one -- is a survival skill.
- **[WDC: try-sessions/sharpen-a-chisel.md](../WDC/research/try-sessions/sharpen-a-chisel.md)** -- Sharpen a blade. In a prolonged emergency, edge tool maintenance keeps your tools functional.

### Readings
- **[WDC: 08-construction-framing.md](../WDC/research/08-construction-framing.md)** -- Construction and framing: how wooden structures work, load paths, basic structural principles. Understanding how buildings stand up helps you assess damage and build emergency shelter.
- **[WDC: 03-hand-tools.md](../WDC/research/03-hand-tools.md)** -- Hand tools: the tools that work when there's no power. In a grid-down emergency, hand tools are the only tools.
- **[WDC: species/western-red-cedar.md](../WDC/research/species/western-red-cedar.md)** -- Western Red Cedar: the PNW's most versatile structural wood. Rot-resistant, lightweight, splittable. The Indigenous peoples of the PNW built entire civilizations with this one species.
- **[WDC: species/douglas-fir-profile.md](../WDC/research/species/douglas-fir-profile.md)** -- Douglas Fir: the structural backbone of PNW buildings. If you're assessing earthquake damage to wood-frame structures, this is the species you're looking at.

### Outcome
You can assess basic structural integrity of wood-frame buildings (is it safe to enter?). You understand how to join wood for emergency structures. You can use hand tools when power tools aren't available. You know which PNW trees provide useful emergency building material.

### Case Studies
- **[FSR: case-studies/portland-street-response.md](../FSR/research/case-studies/portland-street-response.md)** -- Portland Street Response: unarmed crisis workers responding to behavioral health emergencies. An alternative model that reconceives "response."
- **[FSR: case-studies/cahoots-model.md](../FSR/research/case-studies/cahoots-model.md)** -- The CAHOOTS model from Eugene, OR: the original mobile crisis intervention program. Proof that a different kind of response is possible.

You can recognize the signs of a mental health crisis and respond with de-escalation rather than escalation. You understand compassion fatigue and how to protect yourself from burnout. You know that "response" doesn't always mean force or urgency -- sometimes it means presence, patience, and listening.

## Module 9 -- Formal Training: Getting Certified

*Knowledge without certification is preparation. Knowledge with certification is capability recognized by the system.*

**Time:** 20-40 hours (external, in-person)

### External Training (Choose at least one)

- **CERT (Community Emergency Response Team)** -- The recommended first certification. Covers fire safety, light search and rescue, disaster medical operations, and team organization. Usually free, offered by local fire departments and emergency management agencies. 20-24 hours in-person.

- **Wilderness First Aid (WFA)** -- Emergency care in remote settings where help is hours away. Essential for PNW hikers, campers, and rural residents. 16 hours in-person, offered by NOLS, SOLO, and others.

- **Stop the Bleed certification** -- Formal certification for bleeding control. Free, 2 hours, available nationwide.

- **CPR/AED certification** -- American Heart Association or Red Cross. 4-6 hours, widely available.

### Readings
- **[FSR: guides/becoming-a-firefighter.md](../FSR/research/guides/becoming-a-firefighter.md)** -- The path to becoming a firefighter. Even if you don't plan to, understanding the career deepens your respect for the professionals you'll work alongside.
- **[FSR: guides/becoming-a-paramedic.md](../FSR/research/guides/becoming-a-paramedic.md)** -- The path to becoming a paramedic. Same reasoning -- know the people in the system.

### Outcome
You hold at least one formal certification recognized by the emergency response system. You can be deployed in an organized emergency response. The gap between "interested civilian" and "trained community responder" has been crossed.

## Module 10 -- The Community Asset: Service and Sustainment

*A community responder is not a one-time achievement. It's an ongoing commitment to readiness, service, and care.*

**Time:** Ongoing

### Ongoing Activities

- **Volunteer.** CERT deployment, fire department volunteer programs, Search and Rescue, Red Cross, Medical Reserve Corps. Your training makes you useful. Your presence makes the community more resilient.

- **Teach.** Run a CPR class for your neighbors. Show your coworkers how to use a fire extinguisher. Help a friend build a go bag. The skills in this pathway multiply exponentially when shared.

- **Maintain.** Quarterly go bag checks. Annual CPR recertification. Regular drill participation. Skills decay. The person who trained three years ago and never practiced is not prepared -- they're a liability.

- **Advocate.** Push for neighborhood preparedness meetings. Support emergency services funding. Know your community's emergency management plan and help fill gaps. Community resilience is built neighbor by neighbor.

- **Care for yourself.** Responder wellness is not optional. Recognize compassion fatigue. Set boundaries. Seek support. You're in this for the long haul, and burnout helps nobody.

### Cross-Project Integration

The community responder practice connects naturally to ongoing engagement with all four contributing projects:

- **[FSR](../FSR/research/pathways/learning-pathway.md)** -- Continue through the FSR learning pathway to Level 4 (Trained) and Level 5 (Community Asset) for deeper study.
- **[RCN](../RCN/research/pathways/learning-pathway.md)** -- Continue wildlife observation. Log sightings on iNaturalist. Your trail camera data contributes to population monitoring.
- **[FWL](../FWL/research/pathways/learning-pathway.md)** -- If you keep chickens, maintain their emergency plan. If you don't, the animal care principles extend to all domestic animals.
- **[WDC](../WDC/research/pathways/learning-pathway.md)** -- Build your woodworking skills. The more capable you are with tools, the more useful you are after a disaster.

### Readings for Ongoing Study
- **[FSR: 04-law-enforcement.md](../FSR/research/04-law-enforcement.md)** -- Law enforcement response. Understanding the full triad of emergency services.
- **[RCN: 07-cultural-history.md](../RCN/research/07-cultural-history.md)** -- The cultural history of human-animal relationships. Context for community attitudes toward wildlife.
- **[WDC: 01-history-of-woodworking.md](../WDC/research/01-history-of-woodworking.md)** -- The history of building. Humans have been constructing shelter from wood for 10,000 years.
- **[FWL: 07-heritage-conservation.md](../FWL/research/07-heritage-conservation.md)** -- Heritage conservation. Community resilience includes preserving agricultural biodiversity.

### Outcome
You are a recognized, trusted, trained member of your community's preparedness infrastructure. You maintain your skills. You teach others. You volunteer. When something goes wrong -- earthquake, fire, flood, medical emergency, displaced wildlife -- people look to you. Not because of a title, but because you showed up, you trained, and you stayed ready.
