## Introduction

Earth generates a magnetic field that extends from the planet's interior through the magnetosphere, shielding the surface from solar wind and providing a globally available navigational signal. This field has been present for at least 3.5 billion years, long enough for evolution to produce biological sensors capable of detecting and interpreting it. The discovery that organisms synthesize magnetite crystals (Fe3O4) and use them to transduce magnetic field information into neural signals is among the most remarkable findings in sensory biology.

This page documents the physics of Earth's magnetic field, the biological mechanisms that detect it, and the Pacific Northwest species that depend on it for navigation, migration, and survival. The through-line is direct: the same Maxwell's equations that govern compass needles and magnetometers also govern the alignment of biogenic magnetite crystals in the snouts of trout and the beaks of pigeons. Biology did not invent new physics. It evolved transducers for existing fields.

## Earth's Magnetic Field — The Planetary Signal

### Origin

Earth's magnetic field is generated by convective motion of electrically conductive molten iron in the outer core — the geodynamo. The resulting field is approximately dipolar at the surface, resembling the field of a bar magnet tilted approximately 11 degrees from the rotational axis. This is not a permanent magnet; it is a self-sustaining dynamo maintained by the kinetic energy of fluid motion, Coriolis forces, and Ohmic dissipation [G2].

### Field Characteristics at the Surface

The geomagnetic field at Earth's surface is characterized by three primary parameters:

| Parameter | Symbol | Definition | Range at Surface |
|-----------|--------|------------|------------------|
| Total field intensity | F | Magnitude of the full field vector | 25-65 uT |
| Inclination (dip angle) | I | Angle between field vector and horizontal plane | -90 deg (south magnetic pole) to +90 deg (north magnetic pole) |
| Declination | D | Angle between magnetic north and geographic north | Varies by location, typically -20 deg to +20 deg |

The field vector **B** at any point can be decomposed into horizontal (H) and vertical (Z) components:

```
B = (Bx, By, Bz)

where:
  Bx = H * cos(D)      — northward component
  By = H * sin(D)      — eastward component
  Bz = Z               — downward component (positive into Earth in N. hemisphere)

  H = F * cos(I)       — horizontal intensity
  Z = F * sin(I)       — vertical intensity
  F = sqrt(H^2 + Z^2)  — total field intensity

  tan(I) = Z / H       — inclination from horizontal and vertical components
  tan(D) = By / Bx     — declination from horizontal components
```

All quantities measured in microtesla (uT) for intensity and degrees for angles [G2].

### Why the Field Varies Spatially

The field is not uniform. Because the magnetic dipole axis is offset from the rotational axis, and because non-dipole contributions from the core add complexity, the field intensity and direction vary systematically with latitude and longitude:

- **Intensity** increases from the magnetic equator (~25 uT) to the magnetic poles (~65 uT)
- **Inclination** is 0 degrees at the magnetic equator and +/-90 degrees at the magnetic poles
- **Declination** varies with longitude and is influenced by regional crustal anomalies

This spatial variation is precisely what makes the field useful for navigation. An organism that can measure any two of these three parameters (intensity, inclination, declination) can determine its position on the globe — provided those two parameters create a unique coordinate pair at the relevant spatial scale [P1, P5].

## Governing Equations

### Maxwell's Equations — The Foundation

All electromagnetic phenomena, including Earth's magnetic field and biological magnetoreception, are governed by Maxwell's equations. In their differential form:

```
(1) Gauss's Law for Electricity:
    div(E) = rho / epsilon_0

(2) Gauss's Law for Magnetism:
    div(B) = 0

(3) Faraday's Law of Induction:
    curl(E) = -dB/dt

(4) Ampere-Maxwell Law:
    curl(B) = mu_0 * J + mu_0 * epsilon_0 * dE/dt

where:
  E = electric field (V/m)
  B = magnetic field (T)
  rho = charge density (C/m^3)
  J = current density (A/m^2)
  epsilon_0 = permittivity of free space (8.854 x 10^-12 F/m)
  mu_0 = permeability of free space (4*pi x 10^-7 T*m/A)
```

For magnetoreception, the key equation is (2): **div(B) = 0**. This states that magnetic field lines have no beginning or end — they form closed loops. At Earth's surface, the field lines emerge from the southern hemisphere, arc through space, and re-enter in the northern hemisphere. An organism at any point on the surface experiences a field vector that points along these lines [G2].

### Magnetic Torque on a Dipole

A magnetite crystal in a biological tissue behaves as a magnetic dipole. The torque exerted by an external magnetic field on a magnetic dipole is:

```
tau = m x B = m * B * sin(theta)

where:
  tau = torque (N*m)
  m = magnetic dipole moment of the crystal (A*m^2)
  B = external magnetic field strength (T)
  theta = angle between the dipole axis and the field direction
```

This torque is the fundamental transduction mechanism for magnetite-based sensing. The crystal attempts to align with the external field, and the resulting mechanical stress on surrounding tissue generates a signal that can be detected by mechanoreceptor neurons [P10, P5].

### Magnetic Energy of a Dipole in an External Field

The potential energy of a magnetic dipole in an external field is:

```
U = -m . B = -m * B * cos(theta)

where:
  U = potential energy (J)
  m = magnetic moment (A*m^2)
  B = field strength (T)
  theta = angle between m and B
```

Energy is minimized when the dipole is aligned with the field (theta = 0). The energy difference between aligned and anti-aligned states determines the signal-to-noise ratio of the biological sensor. For a single-domain magnetite crystal (~50 nm diameter) in Earth's field (~50 uT):

```
U_max = m * B

For a single-domain magnetite crystal:
  m ~ 5 x 10^-17 A*m^2  (for a 50 nm cube of magnetite)
  B ~ 50 x 10^-6 T

  U_max ~ 2.5 x 10^-21 J

Compare to thermal energy at body temperature (310 K):
  k_B * T = 1.38 x 10^-23 * 310 = 4.28 x 10^-21 J

  U_max / (k_B * T) ~ 0.58
```

This ratio of less than 1 for a single crystal means that individual crystals would be overwhelmed by thermal noise. This is why biological magnetoreceptors contain chains or clusters of magnetite crystals — to amplify the signal above the thermal noise floor [P5].

### Magnetic Susceptibility and Magnetite

Magnetite (Fe3O4) is a ferrimagnetic iron oxide with the inverse spinel crystal structure. Its magnetic properties make it uniquely suited for biological magnetoreception:

```
Properties of magnetite (Fe3O4):
  Saturation magnetization:  M_s = 4.8 x 10^5 A/m
  Curie temperature:          T_c = 858 K (585 deg C)
  Crystal system:             Cubic (inverse spinel)
  Density:                    5,175 kg/m^3
  Single-domain size range:   ~30-120 nm (below: superparamagnetic; above: multi-domain)
```

In the single-domain size range, each crystal behaves as a permanent magnet — all atomic moments are aligned in one direction. This maximizes the magnetic moment per unit volume and provides the strongest possible response to an external field [P10].

## Field Geometry: Intensity, Inclination, and Declination

### The Two-Coordinate Navigation System

The critical insight for understanding biological magnetic navigation is that the combination of field intensity (F) and inclination angle (I) provides a two-coordinate position system analogous to latitude and longitude. This works because:

1. **Isolines of intensity** (lines of constant F) run roughly east-west but are not identical to lines of latitude
2. **Isolines of inclination** (lines of constant I) also run roughly east-west but at different angles than intensity isolines

Where these two sets of isolines cross, they create a grid. At most locations on Earth, knowing F and I uniquely identifies position — just as knowing latitude and longitude does [P1].

```
Geomagnetic coordinate system (biological GPS analogue):

  "Latitude" analogue:  Total field intensity F (increases pole-ward)
  "Longitude" analogue: Inclination angle I (varies with both latitude and longitude)

  Resolution depends on:
    - Local gradient of F: dF/dx (typically 2-5 nT/km in mid-latitudes)
    - Local gradient of I: dI/dx (typically 0.01-0.05 deg/km)
    - Sensor sensitivity threshold
```

This is the system that Pacific pink salmon use to navigate the open ocean [P1].

### Inclination in the Northern Hemisphere

In the Pacific Northwest, the magnetic field inclination is approximately 60-70 degrees below horizontal. This means the field lines enter the Earth at a steep angle:

```
PNW Magnetic Inclination:

  Seattle, WA:     I ~ 69.5 deg (field dips steeply into Earth)
  Portland, OR:    I ~ 67.8 deg
  Victoria, BC:    I ~ 70.1 deg
  Astoria, OR:     I ~ 68.2 deg

  At these inclinations:
    H = F * cos(69 deg) = F * 0.358     (horizontal component is ~36% of total)
    Z = F * sin(69 deg) = F * 0.934     (vertical component is ~93% of total)

  The field is predominantly vertical — nearly pointing straight down.
```

This steep inclination is directly relevant to the fox magnetic rangefinder mechanism described in [Fox Magnetic Rangefinder](11-fox-magnetic-rangefinder.md) — the fox uses this steep field angle as one leg of a triangulation system [P4].

### Secular Variation

The geomagnetic field changes slowly over time (secular variation). Declination in the PNW has been drifting approximately 0.1 degrees per year westward over recent decades. This drift means that biological magnetic maps must be either (a) recalibrated each generation or (b) robust to small positional errors. Evidence suggests that salmon populations adapt to secular variation over generations — populations displaced by field changes adjust their migratory routes accordingly [P1, P5].

### Crustal Anomalies in the PNW

The Cascade Range contains significant volcanic and metamorphic rock formations that produce local magnetic anomalies — deviations from the smooth dipole field. These anomalies can be substantial:

```
Crustal anomaly characteristics:
  Amplitude:     Typically +/- 100 to 500 nT (0.1 to 0.5 uT)
  Spatial scale:  1-100 km
  Source:         Magnetized volcanic rock, iron ore deposits, tectonic features

  For comparison:
    Earth's field at PNW: ~55,000 nT (55 uT)
    Crustal anomaly:      ~100-500 nT
    Ratio:                ~0.2-1% of total field
```

These anomalies could serve as local landmarks for magnetically sensitive organisms, or they could be a source of navigational noise that must be filtered. The USGS aeromagnetic survey data for the PNW reveals detailed crustal anomaly maps that correlate with known geological features [G2].

## Magnetite-Based Sensing — The Ferrimagnetic Transducer

### The Biological Discovery

The discovery that organisms synthesize magnetite crystals and use them for sensing was a watershed moment in sensory biology. Magnetite is not merely passively absorbed from the environment — it is biologically precipitated within specialized cells through a precisely controlled biomineralization process [P10].

### Biomineralization of Magnetite

The biological synthesis of magnetite follows a multi-step process:

```
Magnetite biomineralization pathway:

1. Iron uptake:
   Fe^3+ transported into cell via transferrin/ferritin pathway

2. Vesicle formation:
   Intracellular vesicle (magnetosome) with lipid bilayer membrane

3. Iron reduction and oxidation:
   Fe^3+ partially reduced to Fe^2+
   Controlled oxidation produces Fe3O4 (magnetite)

4. Crystal growth:
   Single-domain crystal grows to 30-120 nm
   Crystal morphology controlled by membrane proteins

5. Chain assembly:
   Crystals aligned in chains via cytoskeletal filaments
   Chain amplifies magnetic moment: m_chain = N * m_single
```

The chain organization is critical. A chain of N crystals has a magnetic moment N times greater than a single crystal, which increases the signal-to-noise ratio proportionally:

```
Signal-to-noise for a magnetite chain:

  SNR = (N * m * B) / (k_B * T)

  For N = 20 crystals in chain:
    SNR ~ 20 * 0.58 = 11.6

  This is well above the detection threshold (SNR > 1).
```

### Transduction Mechanism

The physical mechanism by which magnetite crystals generate a neural signal involves mechanical coupling:

```
Transduction chain:

  External field B
    -> Torque on magnetite chain: tau = m_chain x B
    -> Mechanical deformation of cell membrane
    -> Stretch-activated ion channels open
    -> Ion flux (Ca^2+, Na+) changes membrane potential
    -> Action potential in afferent nerve
    -> Signal transmitted to brain

  This is a magneto-mechanical transduction:
    Magnetic energy -> Mechanical energy -> Electrochemical signal
```

The sensitivity depends on the coupling between the magnetite chain and the ion channels. The mechanical energy available from the chain must exceed the gating energy of the ion channels — typically on the order of a few k_B*T [P5].

## Magnetite in Fish: Rainbow Trout

### Walker et al. (1977) — The Discovery

The identification of magnetite in the snouts of rainbow trout (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) by Walker and colleagues in 1977 was one of the first demonstrations of biogenic magnetite in vertebrates [P10].

### Location and Structure

Magnetite crystals were found concentrated in the ethmoid region of the trout snout — the area between the olfactory organs, within the dermethmoid bone and surrounding connective tissue:

```
Trout magnetite characteristics:
  Location:            Ethmoid region (snout)
  Crystal size:         ~50 nm (single-domain range)
  Crystal morphology:   Cubo-octahedral
  Organization:         Chains of 10-20 crystals
  Associated tissue:    Connective tissue near trigeminal nerve branches
  Innervation:          Trigeminal nerve (ophthalmic branch, ros V)
```

### Neural Pathway

The trigeminal nerve carries the magnetic signal from the snout to the brain:

```
Neural pathway for trout magnetoreception:

  Magnetite crystals in ethmoid tissue
    -> Trigeminal nerve (ophthalmic branch)
    -> Trigeminal ganglion
    -> Brainstem trigeminal nucleus
    -> Thalamus
    -> Telencephalon (pallium)

  Response characteristics:
    - Neurons respond to changes in magnetic field direction
    - Response is abolished by local anesthetic applied to snout
    - Response is abolished by strong pulse magnetization
      (which randomizes single-domain crystal orientations)
```

The demonstration that a strong magnetic pulse (which disrupts the orientation of single-domain magnetite crystals) abolishes the magnetic response is strong evidence that magnetite is the receptor material rather than some other mechanism [P10, P5].

### Functional Significance for Trout

Rainbow trout in Pacific Northwest rivers use magnetoreception for:
- **Orientation** in turbid water where visual cues are limited
- **Homing** to natal streams (in conjunction with olfactory cues)
- **Migration** orientation during anadromous phases (steelhead runs)

Steelhead trout (the anadromous form of rainbow trout) in PNW rivers such as the Columbia, Willamette, and Puget Sound tributaries likely depend on this magnetic sense during their ocean phase when no other positional cues are available [P10, G2].

## Magnetite in Birds: Homing Pigeons

### Fleissner et al. (2003) — Pigeon Upper Beak

Fleissner and colleagues identified highly organized magnetite-containing structures in the upper beaks of homing pigeons in 2003 [P11].

### Structure — The Six-Cluster System

The pigeon magnetite system is remarkably organized:

```
Pigeon beak magnetite system:
  Location:         Upper beak skin, 6 bilateral clusters
  Cluster count:     3 pairs (left/right symmetry)
  Position:          Arranged along the beak in a specific geometry

  Each cluster contains:
    - Subcellular iron-rich dendrites (tree-like branching structures)
    - Iron in three forms:
      (a) Magnetite nanocrystals (Fe3O4) — single-domain, 2-5 nm
      (b) Maghemite platelets (gamma-Fe2O3) — superparamagnetic
      (c) Amorphous iron — within vesicles

  Innervation: Trigeminal nerve (ophthalmic branch)
```

### Proposed Three-Component Transducer

The Fleissner model proposes that the three iron phases serve different functions:

```
Three-component magnetite transducer model:

  Component 1: Magnetite nanocrystals (permanent dipole)
    -> Respond to field direction
    -> Provide compass information

  Component 2: Maghemite platelets (superparamagnetic)
    -> Respond to field intensity
    -> Provide "map" information (intensity-based position)

  Component 3: Amorphous iron (paramagnetic)
    -> Flux concentrator
    -> Channels field lines toward the other components

  Combined function:
    Direction (compass) + Intensity (map) = Full navigation information
```

This three-component system would provide both compass and map information from a single anatomical structure — a complete biological magnetometer [P11].

### Relevance to PNW Birds

The pigeon findings extend to other avian species. Migratory birds using the Pacific Flyway through the PNW — including raptors, waterfowl, and songbirds — likely possess analogous magnetite-based systems. The steep magnetic inclination in the PNW (67-70 degrees) provides a strong vertical field component that enhances the torque on magnetite chains oriented along the bird's body axis [P11, G2].

## Magnetite in Sea Turtles, Whales, and Salmon

### Sea Turtles

Loggerhead sea turtles (*Caretta caretta*) and green sea turtles (*Chelonia mydas*) have been demonstrated to use magnetic maps for transoceanic navigation. Lohmann Lab experiments showed that turtles exposed to magnetic fields matching specific locations in the Atlantic responded with swimming directions appropriate for those locations — even though they had never visited them [P5].

```
Sea turtle magnetic navigation:
  Mechanism:           Magnetite-based (proposed) + possible radical-pair
  Navigation type:     True bicoordinate map (intensity + inclination)
  Demonstrated:        Displacement experiments, magnetic field manipulation
  Resolution:          Sufficient for open-ocean navigation (~100 km accuracy)
```

### Cetaceans (Whales and Dolphins)

Several cetacean species possess magnetite in their brain tissue. The connection between cetacean stranding events and geomagnetic anomalies (local deviations in field intensity) suggests that whales use magnetic maps for migration:

```
Cetacean magnetoreception evidence:
  - Magnetite identified in dura mater of several cetacean species
  - Mass strandings correlate with geomagnetic minima (low-field zones)
  - Gray whale (Eschrichtius robustus) migration along PNW coast
    follows routes that correlate with geomagnetic features
  - Humpback whale migration routes also correlate with field structure
```

Gray whales migrating along the Pacific Northwest coast between Alaska and Baja California pass through the PNW magnetic field gradient — a ~10 uT change in total intensity over the full migration route. The PNW segment represents a region of relatively smooth field gradient, potentially serving as a "magnetic highway" [P5, G2].

### Salmon — Multiple Species

Magnetite has been identified in several Pacific salmon species:

```
Salmon species with magnetite evidence:
  - Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha)
  - Sockeye salmon (Oncorhynchus nerka)
  - Pink salmon (Oncorhynchus gorbuscha)
  - Coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch)
  - Steelhead trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss)

  All PNW-spawning anadromous salmonids.
  All undergo ocean migrations requiring navigational capacity.
  All return to natal streams with remarkable precision.
```

## Pacific Pink Salmon Magnetic Map Navigation

### Putman et al. (2020) — The Magnetic Map

The work of Putman and colleagues, published in the Journal of Comparative Physiology A, provides the most detailed evidence for magnetic map navigation in any fish species [P1].

### The Migratory Route

Pacific pink salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*) from the Pacific Northwest follow an elliptical migratory route:

```
Pink salmon migratory cycle:

  Year 1 (juvenile):
    Natal stream (PNW) -> Estuary -> Nearshore Pacific

  Year 1-2 (ocean phase):
    Nearshore Pacific
    -> North Pacific (northwestward to Gulf of Alaska / Aleutian Islands)
    -> Bering Sea margin
    -> South through North Pacific (southeastward toward origin)

  Year 2 (return):
    North Pacific -> PNW coast -> Natal stream

  Total distance: ~3,000-5,000 km elliptical loop
  Duration: ~18 months in ocean
  Navigation challenge: No landmarks, no current boundaries, featureless open ocean
```

### The Two-Coordinate Magnetic Map

Putman et al. demonstrated that juvenile pink salmon respond to magnetic fields corresponding to different locations along their migratory route. When exposed to the magnetic signature (intensity + inclination) of a location north of their route, salmon oriented southward; when exposed to the signature of a location south of their route, they oriented northward [P1].

```
Magnetic map coordinate system for pink salmon:

  Coordinate 1: Total field intensity (F)
    - Varies from ~52 uT (southern range) to ~57 uT (northern range)
    - Gradient: ~2-5 nT/km (sufficient for ~10 km resolution with
      biological magnetometer sensitivity of ~10-50 nT)

  Coordinate 2: Inclination angle (I)
    - Varies from ~62 deg (southern range) to ~74 deg (northern range)
    - Gradient: ~0.01-0.05 deg/km

  These two parameters together specify position within the
  North Pacific migratory range with sufficient resolution
  for large-scale route correction.
```

### The GPS Analogy

The salmon magnetic map is functionally analogous to a GPS system, but using Earth's natural magnetic field instead of satellite signals:

```
GPS vs Salmon Magnetic Map:

  GPS:                              Salmon Magnetic Map:
  ----                              --------------------
  Signal source: Satellites         Signal source: Earth's core dynamo
  Parameters: Time delays from      Parameters: Field intensity (F)
              multiple satellites                and inclination (I)
  Coordinate 1: Latitude            Coordinate 1: F isoline
  Coordinate 2: Longitude           Coordinate 2: I isoline
  Resolution: ~1 m                  Resolution: ~10-100 km
  Requires: Electronic receiver     Requires: Magnetite crystals +
            + atomic clock                     neural processing
  Power: Battery                    Power: Metabolic
```

The resolution of the salmon system (~10-100 km) is vastly lower than GPS, but it is sufficient for open-ocean navigation. The salmon does not need to know its position to within meters — it needs to know whether to turn left or right at the scale of hundreds of kilometers [P1].

### Inherited vs Learned Map

A striking feature of the pink salmon system is that the magnetic map appears to be inherited rather than learned. Juvenile pink salmon respond appropriately to magnetic displacements before they have ever made the migratory journey. This means the mapping between magnetic parameters and appropriate swimming direction is genetically encoded [P1, P5].

```
Inherited magnetic map properties:
  - Functional in naive juveniles (no prior experience)
  - Must encode: "if F = X and I = Y, swim in direction Z"
  - Requires genetic encoding of field parameter -> behavior mapping
  - Subject to natural selection:
    Fish with incorrect maps fail to return -> genes eliminated
    Fish with correct maps return and reproduce -> genes propagated
  - Map must be updated across generations as secular variation
    shifts field parameters (~0.1 deg/year declination drift)
```

## Chinook Salmon Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion

### The Three-Phase Navigation System

Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) — the primary prey species of Southern Resident orcas in the PNW — use a remarkable multi-phase navigation system that transitions between sensing modalities as the fish approaches its natal stream [P1, P5, G1]:

```
Chinook salmon navigation phases:

  Phase 1 — MAGNETIC MAP (open ocean, 1000+ km from coast)
    Sensor:     Magnetite-based magnetoreception
    Parameters: Field intensity + inclination
    Function:   Determine approximate position, set course toward coast
    Accuracy:   ~10-100 km

  Phase 2 — MAGNETIC COMPASS (coastal approach, 10-100 km)
    Sensor:     Magnetite-based magnetoreception
    Parameters: Field direction (compass bearing)
    Function:   Maintain heading toward river mouth
    Accuracy:   ~1-10 km

  Phase 3 — CHEMICAL GRADIENT (river system, <10 km)
    Sensor:     Olfactory epithelium
    Parameters: Dissolved amino acids, bile salts specific to natal stream
    Function:   Follow chemical gradient upstream to exact spawning site
    Accuracy:   ~1-100 m
```

### Sensor Fusion Architecture

The transition between phases is not abrupt — it is a gradual handoff where the weighting of each sensory channel shifts:

```
Sensor fusion weighting (conceptual):

  Distance from natal stream:  1000 km   100 km    10 km    1 km    0.1 km
  Magnetic map weight:          0.9       0.5       0.1      0.0     0.0
  Magnetic compass weight:      0.1       0.4       0.5      0.2     0.0
  Olfactory weight:             0.0       0.1       0.4      0.8     1.0

  This is multi-modal sensor fusion — the biological equivalent of
  Kalman filtering across multiple sensor channels with different
  precision and range characteristics.
```

This three-phase system is an elegant example of sensor fusion: using the best available sensor for each spatial scale, and transitioning smoothly between them. In engineering terms, this is analogous to a navigation system that uses GPS for initial positioning, radar for intermediate approach, and optical/chemical sensing for final docking [P1, P5].

### Implications for PNW Conservation

Understanding Chinook salmon magnetoreception has direct conservation implications. Electromagnetic interference from human infrastructure — power lines crossing rivers, electromagnetic fields from dam operations, underwater cables — could potentially disrupt magnetic navigation. The Columbia River system, with its 14 mainstem dams and extensive electrical infrastructure, represents a significant electromagnetic environment that salmon must navigate through [G1, G4].

```
  2. Total field intensity maps:
     - Gridded F values for PNW region
     - Temporal variation (diurnal, storm-time)

  3. Inclination maps:
     - Gridded I values for PNW region
     - Isocline charts (lines of constant I)

  4. Magnetic storm alerts:
     - Kp index (planetary disturbance level)
     - Storm-time field variations can exceed 100 nT
     - Relevant to biological navigation disruption
```

### Magnetic Storms and Biological Navigation

Geomagnetic storms — caused by solar wind disturbances — can produce field variations of 100-1000 nT at PNW latitudes. This is 0.2-2% of the background field, comparable to the gradients that salmon use for position sensing:

```
Magnetic storm impact on biological navigation:

  Quiet-time field variation (diurnal):  ~30 nT
  Moderate storm (Kp = 5):               ~100-300 nT
  Major storm (Kp = 7+):                 ~500-1000 nT

  Salmon position sensing gradient:      ~2-5 nT/km
  Storm-equivalent position error:        ~20-200 km (moderate storm)

  This suggests that salmon may experience navigational
  confusion during major geomagnetic storms.
```

Some evidence links whale stranding events to periods of high geomagnetic activity, suggesting that magnetic storms can disrupt cetacean navigation. Similar effects may occur in salmon, though the data are less clear [G2, P5].

### Compass

The magnetic compass is perhaps the most direct engineering analogue to biological magnetoreception:

```
Compass types and biological parallels:

  1. Mechanical compass:
     Magnetized needle on low-friction pivot
     -> Biological: magnetite crystal chain on flexible cytoskeletal mount

  2. Fluxgate compass:
     Alternating current in high-permeability core; field offset measured
     -> No direct biological analogue (requires AC drive)

  3. Magnetoresistive compass:
     Resistance change in thin film in magnetic field
     -> No direct biological analogue

  4. Quantum compass:
     Radical-pair mechanism in molecular system
     -> Biological: cryptochrome protein (see 12-cryptochrome-quantum-compass.md)
```

### GPS — The Two-Parameter Position System

As described above, the salmon magnetic map is functionally a two-parameter GPS system. The engineering GPS and the biological magnetic map solve the same problem (position determination) using entirely different physics but the same mathematical principle: two independent measurements that create a unique coordinate pair [P1].

## Signal Processing Chain — Magnetoreception

```
Complete signal processing chain for magnetite-based magnetoreception:

  SIGNAL SOURCE
    Earth's magnetic field (25-65 uT, DC to ~0.001 Hz secular variation)
    |
  PROPAGATION
    Direct — magnetic field penetrates tissue with negligible attenuation
    (tissue has permeability ~ mu_0, so field is essentially unchanged)
    |
  TRANSDUCER
    Magnetite crystal chain in specialized cells
    Mechanical torque: tau = m x B
    -> Membrane deformation -> Ion channel gating
    |
  SIGNAL CONDITIONING
    Neural integration in trigeminal ganglion
    Temporal averaging (reduces noise from transient field fluctuations)
    Comparison across multiple receptor clusters (spatial averaging)
    |
  FEATURE EXTRACTION
    Brain computes:
      - Field direction (compass heading)
      - Field intensity (position along intensity gradient)
      - Field inclination (position along inclination gradient)
    |
  DECISION / ACTION
    Navigate: correct course toward target
    Phase transition: switch from magnetic to olfactory when approaching coast
```

### PNW Conservation Context

The intersection of biological magnetoreception and PNW conservation concerns includes:

1. **Dam electromagnetic interference:** Columbia-Snake River dams produce electromagnetic fields from generators, turbines, and transmission lines. Potential impact on salmon magnetic navigation at critical junctures [G1, G4]

2. **Submarine cables:** Undersea power and communication cables off the PNW coast generate electromagnetic fields that could affect marine species navigation [G2]

3. **Urban electromagnetic pollution:** Growing RF and ELF electromagnetic pollution in PNW urban waterways (Puget Sound, Willamette River) could mask or distort the geomagnetic signal

4. **Climate-driven range shifts:** As species ranges shift northward with climate change, the magnetic map encoded in their genes may no longer match the actual field at new locations, creating navigational mismatches [P1, P5]

5. **Southern Resident orcas:** The orcas depend on Chinook salmon, which depend on magnetic navigation. Any factor that disrupts Chinook magnetic navigation cascades through the food web to affect this ESA-listed orca population [G1, G5]
