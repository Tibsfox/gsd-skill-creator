# History and Mandate

> **Domain:** Canadian Public Broadcasting
> **Module:** 1 -- Legislative Foundation and Institutional Arc, 1929--2026
> **Through-line:** *It's the state, or the United States.* Graham Spry's seven-word aphorism, coined in 1932 to rally support for public broadcasting, became the founding theorem of the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation. Ninety-four years later, the same sentence summarizes the live political debate. The architecture has shifted -- from shortwave towers to satellites to streaming APIs -- but the underlying topology has not: a large country, thinly populated, sharing a continent with the most powerful media economy in human history, trying to find a frequency that is recognizably its own.

## 1. The Problem That Created the CBC

Canada's broadcasting challenge has always been geographic. The country stretches 5,515 kilometres east to west, yet 90 percent of its population lives within 150 miles of the United States border. By the late 1920s, private radio stations were being absorbed by American networks, the airwaves were saturated with American programming, and no private operator had the economic incentive to produce Canadian content at scale [1]. The fundamental problem was structural: the same border proximity that made American signals easy to receive made Canadian stations easy to outcompete.

The Canadian National Railways (CNR) had demonstrated that government broadcasting was viable. CNR's radio network, established under Sir Henry Thornton, carried programming to passengers on transcontinental trains and then extended to a small public audience. By 1929, it offered only three hours of weekly programming, but the principle was proven: the state could produce and distribute broadcasts [2].

What the CNR experiment could not solve was the commercial incentive problem. Private stations found it cheaper to relay American content than to produce Canadian programming. The advertising revenue model rewarded audience maximization, which in a cross-border market meant adopting whatever American networks were already producing. This was not a failure of Canadian broadcasters' ambition -- it was a structural consequence of geography and economics.

## 2. The Aird Commission: 1929

Prime Minister Mackenzie King established the Royal Commission on Radio Broadcasting in December 1928, chaired by Sir John Aird, president of the Canadian Bank of Commerce. The commission visited 25 Canadian cities, toured European broadcasting systems (including the BBC in Britain), and reported on September 11, 1929 -- 48 days before the stock market crash [3].

The Aird Commission's findings were unambiguous. It found that:

- Private stations were being absorbed by American companies at an accelerating rate
- The airwaves were flooded with American programming that did not reflect Canadian life
- Private operators lacked the resources to counter American content with Canadian material
- National broadcasting could not be left to commercial interests alone

The commission recommended a publicly owned corporation capable of "fostering a national spirit and interpreting national citizenship" [3]. This language -- the idea that broadcasting was not merely entertainment but a tool for national cohesion -- became the philosophical DNA of every broadcasting act that followed.

The Aird Commission drew explicitly on the BBC model: a publicly funded broadcaster independent from government editorial control, funded by a combination of license fees and parliamentary appropriation. The key insight was that broadcasting infrastructure was a public utility, not merely a commercial opportunity.

> **CAUTION:** The Aird Commission report predates the stock market crash of October 29, 1929. The economic context that followed -- the Great Depression -- shaped the political environment in which its recommendations were eventually implemented, but the commission's analysis was conducted during a period of relative prosperity.

## 3. The CRBC Experiment: 1932--1936

The Canadian Radio Broadcasting Act of May 26, 1932 created the Canadian Radio Broadcasting Commission (CRBC), absorbing the CNR radio network's facilities. The CRBC was Canada's first attempt at national public broadcasting, but it was structurally compromised from the start [4].

The CRBC operated under an unsuitable three-person commission structure, was chronically underfunded, and lacked the organizational capacity to build a national network. It could regulate private stations and produce programming, but it could not do both effectively with the resources allocated.

Graham Spry and the Canadian Radio League lobbied aggressively for a better-organized replacement. Spry, a journalist and lobbyist, provided the intellectual framework for Canadian public broadcasting. His argument was economic and cultural simultaneously: without a public broadcaster, the economics of the border would inevitably deliver Canadian audiences to American networks. The state was the only entity with both the mandate and the scale to resist that gravitational pull [5].

Spry's campaign succeeded. The 1936 Canadian Broadcasting Act dissolved the CRBC and created the CBC with improved organization, better funding mechanisms, and reduced vulnerability to direct political interference.

## 4. Founding the CBC: November 2, 1936

The Canadian Broadcasting Corporation was formally established as a Crown Corporation on November 2, 1936. It is the oldest continually existing broadcasting network in Canada [6]. The 1936 Act gave the CBC a dual mandate:

- **Production and distribution:** Build and operate a national radio network in both English and French
- **Regulation:** Oversee all broadcasting in Canada (a role later transferred to the Board of Broadcast Governors in 1958 and then to the CRTC in 1968)

The bilingual mandate was foundational. From its creation, the CBC was designed to serve two linguistic communities simultaneously -- a structural requirement that shapes the institution to this day and makes proposals to split English from French services architecturally complex.

The CBC initially operated primarily through radio, inheriting the CNR network's facilities and expanding into a national service. By the end of World War II, CBC Radio had become the primary instrument of national communication, carrying news, entertainment, and cultural programming coast to coast [7].

### The War Years: 1939--1945

World War II transformed the CBC from a national broadcaster into a wartime communication instrument. The corporation carried government announcements, war news, morale programming, and -- critically -- the voice of Canadian soldiers through the CBC Overseas Service [7].

The war years established two precedents that shaped the institution permanently:

1. **The CBC as national infrastructure:** During wartime, the CBC was not optional. It was the mechanism by which a geographically dispersed population received coordinated national communication. This cemented the "infrastructure" framing that persists in the cultural sovereignty argument.

2. **Editorial independence under pressure:** The CBC's wartime coverage, while subject to military censorship, established a tradition of journalistic ambition that would define its news operations. The tension between government funding and editorial independence -- present since the founding -- became acute under wartime conditions and was never fully resolved.

### Post-War Expansion

After 1945, the CBC expanded its radio network coast to coast, becoming the primary cultural broadcasting institution in Canada. The Dominion Network (English popular programming) and the Trans-Canada Network (English cultural programming) served different audience segments. The French network served Quebec and francophone communities nationally. By the early 1950s, radio penetration had reached effectively 100% of Canadian households, and the CBC was the dominant radio institution in the country [7].

The post-war period also brought the first serious private broadcasting competition. Private stations, licensed by the CBC itself (which still held the regulatory role), argued for greater freedom from CBC oversight. This tension between the public broadcaster as regulator and as competitor would be resolved by the creation of the Board of Broadcast Governors in 1958.

## 5. Television and the Commission Era: 1952--1968

### The Massey Commission (1951)

The Royal Commission on National Development in the Arts, Letters, and Sciences, chaired by Vincent Massey, examined the state of Canadian culture broadly and recommended that the CBC lead Canada's entry into television. The commission's report affirmed the cultural sovereignty argument: without public television, the American networks -- already broadcasting from border cities receivable deep into Canadian territory -- would dominate the new medium as thoroughly as they had dominated pre-CBC radio [8].

### CBC Television Launch

CBC Television launched on September 6, 1952, from Montreal (CBFT, French) and Toronto (CBLT, English). At launch, approximately 30 percent of the Canadian population had access; within two years, that figure rose to 66 percent with coverage in both languages [9].

### The Fowler Commission (1957)

The Royal Commission on Broadcasting, chaired by Robert Fowler, found that American productions had become as prominent as Canadian productions on CBC Television. This finding led directly to the Broadcasting Act of 1958 and the creation of the Board of Broadcast Governors (BBG), which introduced Canadian content quotas in 1961 -- the CanCon regulations that remain in evolved form today [10].

### The Birth of the CRTC (1968)

The Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC) was created in 1968, replacing the BBG as the independent regulatory body for Canadian broadcasting. The CRTC assumed responsibility for licensing, content regulation, and enforcement of Canadian content requirements. The CBC was no longer both broadcaster and regulator -- a separation of powers that clarified the institution's role [11].

## 6. The Broadcasting Act of 1991

The Broadcasting Act of 1991 (S.C. 1991, c.11) is the governing legislation for the CBC. It remains in force, though amendments have been proposed as recently as 2025 [12].

### Key Provisions

**Section 3(1)(l)** requires the CBC to "provide radio and television services incorporating a wide range of programming that informs, enlightens and entertains."

**Section 3(1)(m)** specifies that CBC programming should be:

- **Predominantly and distinctly Canadian** in content and character
- Reflective of Canada's **regions** and their diverse cultures
- Available in **both official languages** (English and French)
- **Available throughout Canada**, including remote and northern communities
- Serving the special needs of **Indigenous peoples** and **official-language minority communities**

The Act establishes the CBC as a Crown corporation: quasi-autonomous from government in day-to-day operations, accountable to Parliament through the Department of Canadian Heritage, and editorially independent. The government can make high-level mandate and funding decisions through legislation but cannot direct programming content [12].

> **Related:** See [Funding and Governance](02-funding-and-governance.md) for the Crown corporation structure and parliamentary appropriation mechanics.

### The Mandate in Practice

The 1991 Act's mandate is simultaneously specific and aspirational. "Predominantly and distinctly Canadian" is a measurable standard -- the CRTC can verify Canadian content percentages. But "informs, enlightens and entertains" is a qualitative aspiration that defies regulatory measurement. The CBC's perennial challenge is satisfying both the measurable and the aspirational dimensions of the mandate simultaneously [12].

The Act also establishes the CBC's obligation to serve official-language minority communities -- English speakers in Quebec and French speakers outside Quebec. This obligation shapes service design and resource allocation in ways that pure audience-size metrics would not justify, reflecting the Act's conception of the CBC as a tool for national cohesion rather than audience maximization.

### Proposed Amendments (2025)

Heritage Minister St-Onge's February 2025 white paper proposed several amendments to the Broadcasting Act, the most significant being the statutory appropriation mechanism. Additional proposed changes include updated language around digital distribution obligations, platform-neutral content requirements, and explicit reference to the CBC's role in the online streaming ecosystem. As of March 2026, these amendments have been proposed but not enacted [17].

## 7. Canadian Content Regulation

Canadian content (CanCon) requirements emerged from the Fowler Commission's finding that American programming dominated Canadian airwaves even on the public broadcaster. The BBG introduced quotas in 1961, and the CRTC has maintained and evolved them since 1968 [10].

### Radio CanCon

CRTC regulations require that Canadian radio stations broadcast a minimum percentage of Canadian musical selections during each broadcast week:

- **Commercial radio:** 35% Canadian content in popular music categories
- **CBC Radio:** subject to conditions of license specifying higher Canadian content levels
- **French-language stations:** additional requirements for French-language musical content

The MAPL system (Music, Artist, Production, Lyrics) determines what qualifies as Canadian: a musical selection must fulfill at least two of four criteria to qualify [13].

### Television CanCon

Canadian television stations must ensure that a percentage of programming broadcast during the day and during prime time is certified as Canadian content. Programs qualify through a points system based on the nationality of key creative positions (writer, director, lead performers, etc.) [13].

### The Online Streaming Act (2023)

Bill C-11, the Online Streaming Act, received Royal Assent in April 2023. It extends CRTC regulatory authority to online streaming services operating in Canada, requiring them to contribute to the Canadian broadcasting system. This represents the first major legislative update to the broadcasting framework since 1991 and directly affects CBC Gem's competitive position relative to Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime Video, and other global platforms [14].

## 8. The CRTC Regulatory Framework

The CRTC issues CBC/Radio-Canada's broadcasting license, which defines specific conditions including content requirements, spending obligations, and accessibility standards. The current license period runs from 2022 to 2027 [15].

The CRTC's regulatory authority encompasses:

- **Licensing:** Issuing, renewing, and amending broadcasting licenses
- **Content regulation:** Setting and enforcing Canadian content quotas
- **Competition:** Managing the relationship between public and private broadcasters
- **Accessibility:** Mandating closed captioning, described video, and other accessibility features
- **Complaints:** Processing public complaints about broadcasting content

The CRTC operates at arm's length from Parliament. Its commissioners are appointed by the Governor in Council, but its regulatory decisions are made independently. This independence is critical for maintaining the separation between political authority and editorial content [15].

### License Renewal Process

The CRTC renews CBC's broadcasting license on a periodic basis, most recently in 2022 for the 2022-2027 period. The renewal process involves:

- **Application:** CBC submits a detailed application describing programming commitments, financial projections, and mandate compliance plans
- **Public consultation:** The CRTC holds public hearings where individuals, organizations, and competing broadcasters can submit interventions
- **Decision:** The CRTC issues a decision with specific conditions of license that the CBC must meet during the renewal period
- **Compliance monitoring:** The CRTC monitors compliance throughout the license period and can impose consequences for non-compliance

The renewal process is the CRTC's primary mechanism for shaping CBC's operations. License conditions can specify Canadian content expenditure minimums, programming commitments for underserved communities, accessibility requirements, and reporting obligations [15].

### The CRTC-Government Relationship

While the CRTC operates independently of government in its regulatory decisions, the government can issue policy directives to the CRTC through Orders in Council. This creates a layered governance structure: Parliament sets the legislative framework (Broadcasting Act), the government can issue policy direction, and the CRTC makes specific regulatory decisions. The CBC operates within all three layers while maintaining editorial independence from each [15].

## 9. Cross-Border PNW Reception

The Pacific Northwest exemplifies the cross-border signal dynamics that motivated the CBC's creation. Vancouver (CBC) and Seattle (American networks) are separated by approximately 230 kilometres, with relatively flat terrain through the Fraser Valley and Puget Sound lowlands that facilitates radio and television signal propagation [16].

### Historical Significance

In the pre-cable, pre-satellite era, viewers in southern British Columbia could receive American stations from Seattle, Bellingham, and other border cities. The reverse was also true: CBC signals from Vancouver reached into Washington State. This signal overlap made the Pacific Northwest a natural laboratory for the cultural sovereignty question: Canadian audiences had effortless access to American content, and the only counter was programming that was worth choosing over the American alternative.

### Modern PNW Context

The cross-border dynamic persists in the digital era. CBC's streaming platform (CBC Gem) is geo-restricted to Canadian IP addresses, while American streaming services operate freely in Canada. For PNW residents on either side of the border, the practical distinction between "Canadian media" and "American media" is primarily a function of licensing agreements and geo-fencing rather than signal propagation.

CBC Radio One's Vancouver station (CBU-FM, 690 AM / 88.1 FM) remains receivable in the Puget Sound area. CBC Music and Ici Radio-Canada Premiere have weaker cross-border reach but are accessible via streaming in both countries [16].

### The PNW as Cultural Laboratory

The Pacific Northwest is a natural case study for the cultural sovereignty question because the cross-border cultural exchange is bidirectional. Canadians in Vancouver consume American media; Americans in Seattle can receive CBC. The question is not whether cross-border cultural exchange occurs -- it does, constantly -- but whether the exchange is balanced or asymmetric.

The asymmetry is measurable. The number of American programs available to Canadian viewers vastly exceeds the number of Canadian programs available to American viewers. This asymmetry is what the cultural sovereignty doctrine identifies as the problem: not that Canadians watch American content, but that without the CBC, Canadians would have very little Canadian content to watch at all.

In the digital era, the PNW case study extends to streaming. CBC Gem is geo-restricted to Canadian IP addresses; Netflix, Disney+, and Amazon Prime operate freely in both countries. A viewer in Vancouver and a viewer in Seattle have nearly identical access to American streaming content, but only the Vancouver viewer has access to CBC Gem. The border that once defined signal reach now defines geo-fencing -- a different technology, the same topology.

## 11. The Cultural Sovereignty Doctrine

The cultural sovereignty argument runs through every chapter of the CBC's history. It holds that a country's ability to tell its own stories, in its own languages, to its own citizens is not a luxury that can be left to market forces -- it is infrastructure that requires public investment, like roads or telecommunications.

The doctrine has been expressed in different vocabularies across nine decades:

- **1929 (Aird):** "Fostering a national spirit and interpreting national citizenship" [3]
- **1932 (Spry):** "It's the state, or the United States" [5]
- **1951 (Massey):** Broadcasting as an instrument of "national development in the arts, letters, and sciences" [8]
- **1991 (Broadcasting Act):** Programming "predominantly and distinctly Canadian" [12]
- **2025 (CCPA focus groups):** "Defunding the CBC would be like burning books" [17]

The consistency of the argument is notable. The medium changes -- radio, television, cable, streaming -- but the structural problem does not. Canada shares a continent with the most prolific content-producing nation in history. Without deliberate public investment in Canadian content, the default outcome is cultural absorption. The CBC exists to ensure that outcome is not default.

> **SAFETY NOTE:** This module documents the cultural sovereignty doctrine as expressed by policymakers, commissions, and advocacy organizations. It does not endorse or reject the doctrine.
