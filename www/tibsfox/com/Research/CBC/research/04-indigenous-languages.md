## 1. OCAP Framework and Research Principles

This module applies the OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession) principles established by the First Nations Information Governance Centre [1]. OCAP is a registered trademark and a framework for how research about First Nations, Inuit, and Metis peoples should be conducted, managed, and disseminated.

### Application in This Module

- **Ownership:** Indigenous communities own information about their peoples, languages, and cultural practices. This module documents CBC's role as a broadcasting service, not as an authority on Indigenous languages themselves.
- **Control:** Indigenous communities must control how information about them is collected, used, and disclosed. This module uses only publicly available CBC corporate documents, government records, and on-the-record statements.
- **Access:** Indigenous communities have the right to access information about themselves. Where this module references Indigenous-language archives, it notes the community access provisions.
- **Possession:** Physical control of data and information. This module does not reproduce, archive, or redistribute Indigenous-language content.

### Attribution Principles

Throughout this module, nation-specific attribution is mandatory:

- **Never** use generic "Indigenous" as a standalone descriptor without specifying the nation, people, or community
- **Always** name the specific First Nation, Inuit community, or Metis nation when describing language services, speakers, or content
- Ceremonial and restricted knowledge is Level 3-4 and **outside the scope** of this study

> **SAFETY NOTE:** This module documents CBC's broadcasting services to Indigenous communities as described in CBC's own corporate documents, parliamentary records, and publicly available research. It does not represent, interpret, or speak for Indigenous communities. Nation-specific attribution is used throughout. All OCAP principles apply.

## 2. Historical Arc: CBC Northern Service

CBC established its Northern Service in 1958, extending broadcasting into Canada's northern territories at a time when many communities had no local media infrastructure [2]. The service was created to fulfill the Broadcasting Act's requirement that CBC programming be "available throughout Canada," including remote and northern communities.

The first Inuktitut-language broadcast occurred in 1960, originating from CBC's Montreal studios. By 1972, Inuktitut programming constituted 17% of CBC's shortwave service to the North [3].

The Northern Service operates from studios in:
- **Yellowknife, NWT** (primary northern hub)
- **Iqaluit, Nunavut** (Inuktitut service center)
- **Whitehorse, Yukon**
- **Additional bureaus** across Nunavut, NWT, and northern regions

```
CBC NORTHERN SERVICE TIMELINE
================================================================

  1958      CBC Northern Service established
  1960      First Inuktitut broadcast (from Montreal studios)
  1972      Inuktitut = 17% of CBC shortwave to the North
  1973      Satellite begins beaming southern TV into North
  1973-81   Indigenous broadcasting activism intensifies
  1981      Inuit Broadcasting Corporation (IBC) licensed by CRTC
  1991      Television Northern Canada (TVNC) created
  1999      TVNC becomes APTN (Aboriginal Peoples Television)
  2024      National Indigenous Strategy launched
  2025      Language Archives: 64,000+ programs catalogued
```

## 3. The Satellite Crisis: 1973

The pivotal moment in Indigenous broadcasting history came in 1973, when the CBC began beaming southern Canadian and American television via satellite into northern communities that had previously received only local or regional radio [4].

The effect was immediate and culturally destabilizing. Communities that had been served by local Inuktitut, Cree, and Dene radio suddenly received an unfiltered stream of English-language programming produced by and for southern audiences. Rosemary Kuptana, who would later become president of the Inuit Broadcasting Corporation, described the onslaught of unrepresentative southern programming as culturally damaging to Inuit communities [4].

This activism produced results:

- **1981:** The Inuit Broadcasting Corporation (IBC) was licensed by the CRTC as the first Inuit-controlled broadcaster
- **1991:** Television Northern Canada (TVNC) was created as a northern broadcasting network
- **1999:** TVNC was reorganized as the Aboriginal Peoples Television Network (APTN), becoming the first national Indigenous television network in the world

The 1973 satellite crisis is foundational to understanding the CBC's current Indigenous broadcasting mandate: the institution that brought southern television into the North is now tasked with supporting the Indigenous broadcasting infrastructure that arose in response to that disruption.

## 4. Current Language Services

CBC North currently broadcasts in eight Indigenous languages, each associated with specific nations and geographic regions [5]:

| Language | Nation(s) | Geographic Region | Broadcasting Level |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Inuktitut | Inuit (Nunavut, Nunavik) | Nunavut, northern Quebec | Most of the day in Nunavut and Nunavik |
| Eastern Cree (Eeyou Istchee) | Eeyou (James Bay Cree) | Northern Quebec (Eeyou Istchee) | Regular daily programming |
| Gwich'in | Gwich'in Nation | NWT (Mackenzie Delta region) | Regular programming |
| Inuvialuktun | Inuvialuit | NWT (Western Arctic, Inuvialuit Settlement Region) | Regular programming |
| North Slavey (Dene Yati) | Dehcho First Nations | NWT (Dehcho region) | Regular programming |
| South Slavey (Got'ine Godi) | Sahtu Dene | NWT (Sahtu region) | Regular programming |
| Tlicho (Dogrib) | Tlicho Government | NWT (Tlicho region) | Regular programming |
| Chipewyan (Denesulini) | Various Dene nations | NWT (multiple regions) | Regular programming |

Inuktitut is the most extensively broadcast language, constituting the majority of daily airtime in Nunavut and Nunavik communities. The other seven languages receive regular but less extensive programming, reflecting both community population sizes and available broadcasting resources [5].

> **SAFETY NOTE:** Language names, nation associations, and geographic attributions are sourced from CBC's own service descriptions and the Government of Canada's official records. Some communities may use variant spellings or alternative names for their languages.

### Language Endangerment Context

The eight languages broadcast by CBC North exist on a spectrum of vitality. Inuktitut, with the largest speaker population among the broadcast languages, is used in daily life across Nunavut and Nunavik. Other languages on the list -- particularly Gwich'in, Tlicho, and the Slavey dialects -- have much smaller speaker populations and face more acute endangerment [5].

The UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger classifies most Indigenous languages in Canada's Northwest Territories as either "definitely endangered" or "severely endangered." Broadcasting in these languages does not by itself reverse language shift, but it contributes to the linguistic ecosystem by:

- Creating professional employment that uses the language
- Producing contemporary content that demonstrates the language's ongoing vitality
- Providing listening material for learners and semi-speakers
- Maintaining community connection across geographically dispersed settlements

### Broadcasting as Language Infrastructure

The relationship between broadcasting and language vitality is reciprocal. Broadcasting requires speakers (journalists, translators, hosts). The availability of broadcasting employment creates economic incentive to maintain and develop language proficiency. The content produced by broadcasters enters community use and reinforces the language's presence in daily life.

This reciprocal relationship means that cuts to Indigenous-language broadcasting have effects beyond media: they remove professional employment that uses the language, reduce the volume of contemporary content available to speakers and learners, and signal that the language is not valued at the institutional level.

The per-capita cost of Indigenous-language broadcasting is high relative to English or French service. The communities are small and remote. The infrastructure required is disproportionate to the audience size. The cultural sovereignty argument that justifies the CBC's existence at the national level applies with even greater force at the Indigenous-language level: without deliberate public investment, these languages lose a critical piece of their institutional support infrastructure.

## 5. The National Indigenous Strategy 2024

In 2024, CBC/Radio-Canada launched its first-ever National Indigenous Strategy, titled "Strengthening Relations, Walking Together." The strategy was announced at the Canadian Museum for Human Rights in Winnipeg, located on Treaty 1 Territory and the National Homeland of the Red River Metis [6].

The strategy commits to four pillars:

### Pillar 1: Narratives

- Ground Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) principles in content development across all platforms
- Support First Nations, Inuit, and Metis language reclamation in news and content strategy
- Increase the volume and prominence of Indigenous-led storytelling

### Pillar 2: People

- Increase Indigenous representation at all levels of the organization, including leadership positions
- Renew and expand the Pathways recruitment program for Indigenous employees
- Invest in professional development and mentorship for Indigenous staff

### Pillar 3: Relationships

- Include more Indigenous-owned vendors in procurement and contracting
- Deepen collaboration with APTN and the Indigenous Screen Office (ISO)
- Develop an Indigenous podcast initiative in partnership with the Australian Broadcasting Corporation (ABC)
- Formalize community advisory relationships with Indigenous nations served by CBC North

### Pillar 4: Truth and Reconciliation

- Commission a historical review of CBC archives' representation of First Nations, Inuit, and Metis experiences
- Expand the Beyond 94 project, which has tracked progress on TRC recommendations since 2018
- Establish an Indigenous Office within CBC corporate structure to coordinate strategy implementation

## 6. Indigenous Language Archives Project

CBC North has undertaken a systematic cataloguing of its Indigenous-language broadcast archive -- one of the largest collections of Indigenous-language audio and video content in Canada [7].

### Archive Scope

As of mid-2025, the archive contains:

| Metric | Count |
|--------|-------|
| Total programs catalogued | 64,000+ |
| Total stories indexed | 141,000+ (approx.) |
| Eastern Cree (Eeyou Istchee) programs remaining to catalogue | 2,349 programs (5,167 stories) |
| Inuktitut programs remaining to catalogue | 13,406 programs (~30,000 stories) |

### The Archival Process

The cataloguing work is not a technical exercise -- it is language work. The project employs Indigenous translators and archivists for whom the process of listening to, transcribing, translating, and indexing broadcasts constitutes active language use [7].

Named researchers include:
- **Maxine Angoo** (Whale Cove, Nunavut) -- Inuktitut archivist and translator
- **Susie Zettler** (Pangnirtung, Nunavut) -- Inuktitut archivist and translator

For these researchers, the archival work serves a dual purpose: preserving historical broadcasts for future access and maintaining active professional use of Inuktitut in a workplace setting. The archive is not a museum -- it is a language revitalization tool [7].

### Access and Preservation

The archive raises OCAP questions about ownership and access. The broadcasts were produced by CBC (a Crown corporation) using Indigenous-language speakers as contributors and talent. Questions of who owns the archival content -- the corporation that funded production or the communities whose languages and stories it contains -- remain relevant to the strategy's implementation.

### Dual Nature of Archival Work

The archive project illustrates a principle that applies across language revitalization efforts: the process of documentation is itself a form of language use. When Maxine Angoo listens to a 1970s Inuktitut radio broadcast, identifies the speakers, transcribes the content, translates context notes, and enters metadata, she is not merely filing documents. She is exercising professional Inuktitut proficiency in a contemporary workplace setting [7].

This distinction matters because language revitalization strategies often focus on formal education (school-based language instruction) or cultural events (ceremonies, community gatherings). Broadcasting and archival work represent a third vector: professional employment that requires daily, sustained language use in a workplace context. The archive project employs people who are paid to use their language -- a form of economic language infrastructure that complements educational and cultural approaches.

### Archive Access Questions

The archive raises important OCAP questions that the National Indigenous Strategy has acknowledged but not fully resolved:

- **Who owns the recordings?** The broadcasts were produced by CBC (a Crown corporation) using public funds, but they contain the voices, stories, and cultural expressions of Indigenous community members
- **Who controls access?** Currently, CBC controls the archive as a corporate asset. Community access is facilitated by CBC North but not governed by community-controlled protocols
- **What is restricted?** Some archival content may contain culturally sensitive material -- ceremonial content, traditional knowledge, or personal stories -- that communities would want to manage differently from general programming

The strategy commits to a historical review of CBC archives' representation of Indigenous experiences (Pillar 4), which will presumably address some of these access and ownership questions [6].

## 7. CBC Indigenous Digital Platform

CBC Indigenous operates as a dedicated digital platform within the broader CBC ecosystem, providing news, feature stories, and multimedia content focused on First Nations, Inuit, and Metis communities across Canada [8].

The platform publishes:
- Daily news coverage of Indigenous affairs, governance, and community events
- Long-form feature journalism on Indigenous culture, language, and land
- Video and podcast content from Indigenous creators
- Coverage of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women, Girls, and Two-Spirit People (MMIWG2S) issues
- Environmental and land-rights reporting from Indigenous perspectives

CBC Indigenous represents the digital extension of the Northern Service mandate: ensuring that Indigenous voices and stories are available not only within northern communities but to all Canadians through digital distribution.

## 8. Beyond 94: Tracking TRC Recommendations

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada issued 94 Calls to Action in 2015. CBC launched "Beyond 94" in 2018 as an ongoing tracking project that monitors and reports on the progress of implementing each recommendation [9].

The project:
- Maintains a public tracker showing the status of all 94 Calls to Action
- Publishes regular updates when progress is made on specific recommendations
- Provides contextual reporting on why certain Calls to Action have stalled
- Operates as part of CBC News, with editorial independence from corporate strategy

As of 2025, the Beyond 94 tracker indicates that the majority of the 94 Calls to Action remain incomplete or partially implemented. The project serves as both journalism and institutional accountability -- the CBC tracking the country's progress on commitments the CBC itself covers as news.

### Tracking Methodology

The Beyond 94 project classifies each Call to Action into categories:

- **Completed:** Specific, measurable action has been taken
- **In progress:** Work has begun but is not complete
- **Not started:** No significant action has been taken
- **Proposed:** Legislation or policy has been proposed but not enacted

The project's editorial independence from CBC corporate strategy is important: Beyond 94 reports on government progress (or lack thereof) regardless of whether the findings are politically convenient for the CBC's own institutional interests. When the CBC itself is subject to a Call to Action, Beyond 94 reports on the corporation's compliance alongside other institutions [9].

### The Indigenous Languages Act (2019)

The federal Indigenous Languages Act (S.C. 2019, c. 23) recognizes the rights of Indigenous peoples to reclaim, revitalize, maintain, and strengthen their languages. While not CBC-specific, the Act provides a legislative framework that supports the CBC's Indigenous-language broadcasting mandate by:

- Affirming the federal government's role in supporting Indigenous language revitalization
- Establishing a Commissioner of Indigenous Languages
- Providing funding mechanisms for language programs and services
- Creating accountability structures for federal institutions' Indigenous-language commitments

The Act intersects with the CBC's mandate at the point where broadcasting becomes language infrastructure: if the federal government has committed to supporting Indigenous language revitalization, then Indigenous-language broadcasting on the national public broadcaster is a natural expression of that commitment [15].

## 9. The APTN Relationship

The Aboriginal Peoples Television Network (APTN) launched in 1999 as the successor to Television Northern Canada (TVNC) and operates as the first national Indigenous television network in the world [10].

APTN and CBC maintain a complex relationship:
- **Collaboration:** Co-productions such as "North of North" (with Netflix as third partner)
- **Complementarity:** APTN provides Indigenous-led, Indigenous-controlled programming that the CBC, as a Crown corporation, structurally cannot
- **History:** APTN exists partly because of the 1973 satellite crisis created by CBC's own actions
- **Strategy alignment:** The 2024 National Indigenous Strategy commits to deeper collaboration with APTN and the Indigenous Screen Office

The National Indigenous Strategy explicitly names APTN as a key partner, acknowledging that Indigenous broadcasting requires Indigenous-controlled institutions alongside the public broadcaster's own efforts [6].

## 10. Olympic Coverage in Indigenous Languages

Paris 2024 marked an expansion of CBC's Indigenous-language Olympic coverage. For the first time, Olympic broadcasts were carried in multiple Indigenous languages [11]:

- **Cree** -- coverage for Cree-speaking communities
- **Innu** -- coverage for Innu communities in Quebec and Labrador
- **Atikamekw** -- coverage for Atikamekw communities in Quebec
- **Inuktitut** -- coverage for Inuit communities across Nunavut and Nunavik

This expansion beyond the traditional Inuktitut-only coverage demonstrated the CBC's commitment to multilingual Indigenous broadcasting and generated significant community engagement. The broadcasts served both a practical function (carrying major national events in community languages) and a symbolic one (affirming that these languages belong on the national stage) [11].

## 11. Language Revitalization as Broadcasting Mission

The Broadcasting Act requires the CBC to serve the "special needs" of Indigenous peoples and to make programming available throughout Canada, including remote and northern communities. The intersection of these mandates with the reality of Indigenous language endangerment creates a broadcasting mission that goes beyond content delivery [12].

### The Revitalization Argument

Broadcasting in Indigenous languages serves language revitalization by:
- **Maintaining active professional use:** Journalists, producers, translators, and archivists use Indigenous languages in their daily work
- **Creating contemporary content:** Broadcasting demonstrates that Indigenous languages are living languages for current affairs, not only historical or ceremonial contexts
- **Building audience expectations:** Regular programming in community languages normalizes their public use
- **Training new speakers:** Broadcast content provides listening material for language learners

### The Funding Challenge

Indigenous-language broadcasting is expensive on a per-listener basis relative to English or French services. The communities served are small and geographically remote. The infrastructure required to reach them (satellite uplinks, remote studios, translator/journalist salaries) is the same as that required to serve larger populations [12].

This creates a chronic tension between the mandate ("available throughout Canada" including "remote and northern communities") and the per-capita funding reality ($32/person for a service that must cover 8 Indigenous languages, 2 official languages, and a geography spanning 6 time zones).
