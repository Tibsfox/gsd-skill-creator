## 1. The Question of Voice

Every adaptation of African stories by non-African storytellers faces a fundamental question: whose voice is speaking?

The question is not academic.

It determines whether the result is cultural engagement or cultural extraction.

Whether the source communities see themselves honored or consumed.

Whether the adaptation enriches the tradition it draws from or merely monetizes it.

The Lion King franchise, across its three major iterations (1994, 2019, 2024), provides a thirty-year case study in the evolution of this engagement [1][2][3].

The question of voice connects directly to the griot tradition documented in Module 2.

In the Mandinka system, the right to tell a story belongs to specific people by hereditary right.

The Kouyate family tells the Sundiata epic because they are the designated keepers of that story, as they have been for seven hundred years.

No outsider can claim the same authority.

The question for Western adaptations of African cultural material is not whether outsiders can tell the stories.

They will, and some of the results have been extraordinary.

The question is how they position themselves relative to the communities whose stories they are telling [1][2].

This module traces the evolution from the 1994 original (a corporate production that included genuine African cultural elements alongside Hollywood conventions), through the 2019 photorealistic remake (which added visual fidelity without significantly advancing cultural engagement), to Barry Jenkins' *Mufasa* (2024), which represents a qualitatively different approach to cultural collaboration [1][2][3].

The trajectory is instructive for the GSD heritage documentation ecosystem and for anyone working with cultural material that is not their own [1][2][3].

## 2. The 1994 Original -- Kenya to Hollywood

Disney's production team for the original Lion King included a research trip to Kenya's Hell's Gate National Park and the wider Amboseli area. Artistic director Andy Gaskill, production designer Chris Sanders, and other team members spent approximately two weeks studying the East African landscape, observing wildlife, and photographing the savanna light, vegetation, and geological formations that would become the visual vocabulary of the film [4][5].

The research trip was genuine and its influence on the film is visible: the volcanic cliffs of Hell's Gate inspired the design of Pride Rock, the Amboseli plains with their view of Mount Kilimanjaro provided the expanse of the Pride Lands, and the team's observational sketches of animal behavior informed the animation. The visual authenticity of The Lion King's African landscape is one of the film's enduring strengths [4][5].

However, the production was fundamentally a Hollywood product with African visual and musical elements layered onto a narrative structure acknowledged as Shakespearean. The writing team (Irene Mecchi, Jonathan Roberts, Linda Woolverton) worked within American screenwriting conventions. The voice cast was predominantly American (James Earl Jones, Jeremy Irons, Matthew Broderick, Whoopi Goldberg), though Jones's gravitas as Mufasa transcends any question of cultural provenance -- his performance is one of the most iconic in animation history. The cultural engagement was real but asymmetric: African elements were imported into a Western production framework rather than the production framework adapting to African storytelling conventions [4][5][6].

## 3. Swahili Names and Their Meanings

The use of Swahili names in The Lion King demonstrates both the production team's genuine interest in African language and the limitations of that engagement. The names encode character traits and relationships with varying degrees of accuracy [4][5][7].

**Simba** means "lion" in Swahili -- a straightforward choice that is also the most widely known Swahili word in the English-speaking world, largely because of The Lion King. **Nala** means "gift" or "successful." **Rafiki** means "friend." **Sarabi** means "mirage" or "optical illusion." **Shenzi** means "uncouth" or "barbarous." **Banzai** means "lurk" or "skulk" [4][7].

**Mufasa** is more complex. It is not a standard Swahili word. Research suggests it may derive from the Manazoto language of eastern Congo, where it relates to concepts of kingship and authority. The choice of a name from outside the Swahili-speaking area for the most regal character may reflect the production team's research going beyond the most commonly known East African language [4][7].

**Scar** (whose birth name is Taka in some expanded universe materials) is English, not Swahili -- a choice that implicitly codes the villain as "less African" than the other characters. **Timon** and **Pumbaa** are drawn from Swahili (*timon* relates to "respect" in some interpretations; *pumbaa* means "foolish" or "lazy") but the characters themselves -- a meerkat and a warthog performing vaudeville comedy -- are animated in a distinctly American entertainment tradition [4][5][7].

The hyenas' Swahili names carry a particular charge. *Shenzi* (barbarous), *Banzai* (skulk), and *Ed* (the non-Swahili name for the "stupid" hyena) -- combined with the hyenas' coding as an underclass living in a ghetto-like elephant graveyard -- drew criticism for reproducing racial stereotypes within the film's otherwise African-coded world. This is the risk of cultural adaptation without cultural guidance: elements borrowed with aesthetic intent can carry social meanings that the borrowers do not perceive [5][6].

## 4. Lebo M. and the Zulu Choral Voice

The single most powerful African cultural element in the 1994 Lion King is the choral work of Lebo M. (Lebohang Morake), a South African musician who was living in exile in the United States during the anti-apartheid era. Lebo M.'s contribution transformed the film from a Hollywood animated feature with an African setting into something that carries genuine African musical power [4][5][8].

The opening of The Lion King -- the rising sun, the animals gathering, and the choral invocation *"Nants ingonyama bagithi baba / Sithi uhm ingonyama"* -- is entirely Lebo M.'s creation. The Zulu lyrics translate approximately as: "Here comes a lion, Father / Oh yes, it's a lion." The word *ingonyama* carries particular weight: in Zulu, it means not just "lion" but "Zulu king" -- the everyday word for the animal is *ibhubesi*. By opening the film with this invocation, Lebo M. encoded a double meaning: the audience sees a lion cub being presented; the Zulu-speaking listener hears a king being crowned [4][5][8].

Lebo M. composed and arranged the Zulu choral elements throughout the film, working with the South African choir to create a vocal landscape that grounds the film in African musical tradition. His work operates at a level that most Western audiences cannot fully access -- the Zulu vocal techniques, the harmonic patterns, the rhythmic structures are drawn from a specific musical tradition that gives the film its emotional depth even for listeners who do not understand the words [4][5][8].

Lebo M.'s contribution represents the best case for cultural engagement in the 1994 film: an African artist given creative space within a Western production, contributing work that is authentically rooted in his own tradition and that elevates the entire project. The contrast between Lebo M.'s contributions (deeply rooted in Zulu musical culture) and the hyenas' Swahili names (borrowed without cultural guidance) illustrates the difference between cultural collaboration and cultural borrowing [4][5][8].

## 5. The 2019 Remake -- Photorealism Without Voice

Jon Favreau's 2019 photorealistic remake of The Lion King advanced the visual fidelity of the African landscape to extraordinary levels -- the virtual cinematography recreated the East African savanna with a precision that made the 1994 original's paintings look like impressionist sketches. But the 2019 film did not significantly advance the cultural engagement beyond the original [2][9].

The voice cast was more diverse than the original, with Chiwetel Ejiofor (British-Nigerian) as Scar, Alfre Woodard as Sarabi, and the South African cast of Lebo M.'s choir reprising their roles. Beyonce, cast as Nala, contributed a new song ("Spirit") that drew on African musical influences. Donald Glover voiced Simba. James Earl Jones returned as Mufasa at age 87, providing the last major performance of the role before his passing [9].

The 2019 film's contribution to the cultural engagement question is primarily negative -- it demonstrated that visual realism alone does not constitute cultural authenticity. The landscapes were photorealistic, but the storytelling was an almost scene-for-scene recreation of the 1994 original. The African setting remained a backdrop rather than a cultural participant in the narrative. The film grossed $1.6 billion worldwide, proving the commercial viability of the franchise but not advancing the cultural conversation [2][9].

## 6. Barry Jenkins and Mufasa (2024)

Barry Jenkins' *Mufasa: The Lion King* (2024) represents a qualitative shift in the Lion King franchise's relationship with African culture. Jenkins, the Oscar-winning director of *Moonlight* (2016) and *If Beale Street Could Talk* (2018), approached the project not as a children's film franchise entry but as a story about "nobility, ancestry, and community" -- themes he described as indistinguishable from his earlier work about marginalized characters finding identity [1][2][3].

Jenkins' statement of creative philosophy is the key text for understanding the shift: "I do not live there, I do not know the place as well as them... being open to the actors driving the process, and especially the actors from the African continent." This is a fundamentally different posture from the 1994 production team's approach. The 1994 team traveled to Kenya, observed, photographed, and brought African visual elements back to Hollywood to be processed through Western storytelling conventions. Jenkins inverted the relationship: he positioned himself as a facilitator for African artists to express their own cultural knowledge within the production framework [1][2][3].

The result is a film that, while still operating within the Disney franchise structure, contains moments of genuine African cultural expression that emerged from the actors' own lives and traditions rather than being scripted by the writing team. The distinction matters: it is the difference between a production that uses African elements and a production that creates space for African voices [1][2][3].

## 7. John Kani -- The Elder's Authority

John Kani, the 81-year-old South African actor, playwright, and director, voiced the older Rafiki in *Mufasa*. Kani's involvement goes beyond voice acting -- Jenkins described entire scenes that were "made up on the spot" by Kani, drawn from his personal experiences living on the African continent [1][2][10].

Kani's authority to improvise scenes about African life and culture is not a credential conferred by the production -- it is the accumulated weight of eight decades of life as a Black South African who lived through apartheid, participated in the anti-apartheid struggle, co-founded the Market Theatre in Johannesburg (a venue that defied racial segregation during the apartheid era), and built a career as one of the most respected actors in African theatrical history. When Kani tells a story about life in Africa, he is not performing a role; he is sharing testimony [10].

Jenkins' willingness to incorporate Kani's improvised material into the film -- to let a scene written by Hollywood writers be replaced by a scene created by an African elder from his own experience -- represents the most significant cultural engagement in the franchise's history. It is the griot principle in action: the keeper of the story is given the authority to tell it in his own way, and the production adapts to the keeper rather than the keeper adapting to the production [1][2][10].

The specific scenes that Kani improvised have not been individually identified in published interviews, maintaining a creative ambiguity that honors Kani's contribution without reducing it to a list of "African additions" to a Western product. The contribution is woven into the fabric of the film, not isolated as a special feature [1][2].

## 8. Kagiso Lediga -- The Pan-Africanist Voice

Kagiso Lediga, the South African actor and comedian, voiced the younger Rafiki in *Mufasa*. Jenkins gave Lediga "license" to speak freely in Zulu, Tswana, Sepedi, and Xhosa -- the multiple languages of Lediga's own multilingual South African identity. This linguistic freedom was integrated into Rafiki's characterization as a "pan-Africanist" traveler who moves between cultures and speaks multiple languages [1][2][3].

The significance of this choice extends beyond the individual character. In traditional Hollywood practice, characters in English-language films speak English, with occasional foreign-language phrases included as flavor or exoticism. Jenkins' approach treats Lediga's multilingualism not as an exotic garnish but as a fundamental expression of the character's identity and of the African reality that the film is representing. South Africa has eleven official languages; multilingualism is not an exception but the norm. By allowing Lediga to express Rafiki's character through his own linguistic repertoire, the film represents a genuine aspect of African cultural reality that previous iterations had flattened into English-only dialogue [1][2][3].

Lediga's contribution also demonstrates a principle relevant to the GSD heritage documentation ecosystem: the most authentic representation of a culture comes not from research about that culture but from practitioners of that culture expressing themselves in their own medium. No amount of Swahili vocabulary research by a Hollywood writing team can produce what Lediga produced naturally: a character who moves between languages because the person voicing him moves between languages [1][2].

## 9. The Language Dimension

The Lion King franchise's engagement with African languages has evolved significantly across its iterations and deserves specific attention as a dimension of cultural authenticity [1][2][3][7].

The 1994 original used Swahili primarily for character names and for Lebo M.'s Zulu choral lyrics. The screenplay itself was in English, and the voice acting was performed by American and British actors speaking English. The African languages were decorative -- they added atmosphere and cultural texture but did not carry narrative content. An audience member who did not understand Swahili or Zulu would miss the etymological meanings of the names (Simba = lion, Rafiki = friend) but would not miss any plot information [4][5][7].

Jenkins' *Mufasa* (2024) expanded the linguistic dimension by giving Kagiso Lediga license to speak in Zulu, Tswana, Sepedi, and Xhosa within his performance as young Rafiki. These are not subtitle-dependent foreign-language interludes but organic expressions of a multilingual character -- Rafiki switches languages as naturally as any multilingual person does, using the language that fits the moment. For audiences who speak these languages, Lediga's performance carries additional layers of meaning. For audiences who do not, the performance still communicates through tone, rhythm, and context [1][2][3].

This linguistic evolution parallels the broader cultural engagement trajectory: from African languages as decoration (1994) to African languages as expression (2024). The same pattern appears in global cinema more broadly -- films like *Black Panther* (2018, dir. Ryan Coogler), with its use of Xhosa for the Wakandan language, and *The Woman King* (2022, dir. Gina Prince-Bythewood), with its use of Fon and Yoruba, reflect a growing recognition that African-set stories benefit from African linguistic presence, not as exoticism but as authenticity [1][2].

## 10. The Landscape Project

One of the most innovative aspects of *Mufasa*'s production process was the landscape photography initiative. Unable to travel to Africa during COVID-19 production restrictions, the team hired people across the African continent to photograph and video their own landscapes. The result was a broader, more diverse range of African landscape reference imagery than any single location-scouting trip could have produced [1][2][3].

This decision was born of logistical necessity (COVID travel restrictions) but produced a culturally significant outcome. Instead of a Hollywood crew's perspective on African landscapes -- filtered through the visual conventions and aesthetic priorities of Western filmmaking -- the production received images made by Africans of their own environments, reflecting their own sense of what is beautiful, significant, and representative about the places they live [1][2].

The landscape project also shifted economic participation: instead of flying a Western crew to Africa and housing them in tourist lodges, the production paid local photographers across the continent for their work. This is a small-scale example of a principle that the heritage documentation ecosystem should adopt: when documenting a place, pay the people who live there to document it themselves, and use their documentation as the primary reference rather than your own [1][2][3].

The visual result in the film is a depiction of African landscapes that is notably more varied and geographically diverse than the Kenya-centric landscapes of the 1994 and 2019 films. *Mufasa*'s journey takes the character through a wider range of African environments -- forests, deserts, mountains, coastlines, rivers -- reflecting the continental scope of the photography project [1][2].

## 11. What Respectful Adaptation Looks Like

The trajectory from the 1994 Lion King to Jenkins' *Mufasa* provides a framework for understanding what respectful cultural adaptation looks like in practice. This is not an abstract ethical question -- it has practical implications for anyone working with cultural material that is not their own, including the GSD heritage documentation ecosystem, the Foxfire methodology, and any AI-assisted system that processes, organizes, or presents cultural knowledge. The evolution can be mapped across several dimensions [1][2][3][4].

Before mapping the dimensions, it is worth noting what has remained constant across all three iterations: a genuine commitment to visual and musical excellence, a willingness to invest in research and reference (the Kenya trip, the landscape photography project), and the involvement of African artists in the musical dimension (Lebo M. has been a constant across all three major films). The evolution is not from "bad" to "good" but from a model of engagement that was sincere but limited to a model that is more structurally equitable [1][2][3][4].

**Authority.** 1994: Western production team makes all creative decisions, with African elements as input material. 2024: African actors are given creative authority to shape scenes, language, and cultural expression within the production.

**Voice.** 1994: African culture is represented primarily through visual setting and Lebo M.'s music; dialogue and storytelling follow Western conventions. 2024: African actors speak in their own languages, improvise from their own experience, and the production adapts to accommodate their contributions.

**Landscape.** 1994: Western crew photographs Kenya. 2024: Africans across the continent photograph their own landscapes, shifting both the perspective and the economic participation.

**Collaboration.** 1994: Consultation model -- African elements are consulted and incorporated into a Western framework. 2024: Collaboration model -- African artists participate in shaping the framework itself.

**Accountability.** 1994: The production team is accountable to Disney and to the audience. 2024: Jenkins explicitly positions himself as accountable to the African actors and their cultural knowledge -- "I do not live there, I do not know the place as well as them."

The evolution is not from "wrong" to "right" -- the 1994 Lion King is a masterwork of animated filmmaking, and its genuine engagement with African visual and musical culture should be honored. The evolution is from a model in which African culture is material (something the production uses) to a model in which African culture is authority (something the production defers to). Both models produce art. Only the second model produces partnership [1][2][3].

## 12. Lessons for Heritage Documentation

The Lion King franchise's thirty-year evolution provides specific, actionable lessons for the GSD heritage documentation ecosystem and for any AI-assisted system that works with cultural material [1][2][11][12].

**Lesson 1: Presence is not partnership.** Traveling to Kenya (1994) demonstrated interest and produced valuable visual reference, but it did not constitute cultural collaboration. Documentation tools that ingest cultural material without the participation of its keepers are doing the same thing: extracting without partnering.

**Lesson 2: Technology does not equal authenticity.** The 2019 photorealistic remake proved that increasing technical fidelity (from hand-drawn animation to virtual cinematography) does not increase cultural authenticity. Better cameras, better rendering, better AI -- none of these automatically produce better engagement with the cultures being documented.

**Lesson 3: Authority must be given, not taken.** Jenkins' most important creative decision was not a decision about the film but a decision about the process: letting African actors drive scenes, letting them speak in their own languages, letting their improvisation shape the final product. Heritage documentation tools should create similar structures: the communities whose heritage is being documented must have authority over the documentation, not merely input into it.

**Lesson 4: The documenters should be from the community.** The landscape photography initiative -- hiring Africans to photograph their own landscapes instead of sending a Western crew -- produced a more authentic result and distributed economic benefit more equitably. AI-assisted heritage documentation should prioritize tools that enable communities to document their own heritage, not tools that enable outsiders to document it for them.

**Lesson 5: Accountability runs upward.** Jenkins positioned himself as accountable to the African artists on the project, not the reverse. This inverts the typical Hollywood power structure and produces a fundamentally different creative dynamic. Heritage documentation tools should be accountable to the communities they serve, with review and approval authority resting with those communities.

## 13. The Musical Dimension in Detail

The musical evolution across the three Lion King films deserves specific attention because music is the medium through which African cultural expression has been most consistently and powerfully present in the franchise [4][5][8].

**1994:** The score by Hans Zimmer blended orchestral composition with Lebo M.'s Zulu choral arrangements and Elton John/Tim Rice songs. The result was a hybrid that worked commercially (the soundtrack won two Academy Awards and has sold over 14 million copies worldwide) but that prioritized Western pop songwriting conventions. Lebo M.'s choral contributions, while powerful, were layered onto a Western framework -- they occupied the sonic space of "atmosphere" and "world-building" while the narrative-advancing songs were in English, in Western pop form [4][5][8].

**2019:** The remake largely reproduced the original's musical structure. Beyonce's "Spirit" was the primary new addition, drawing on African musical influences but remaining firmly within contemporary R&B/pop conventions. Lebo M.'s choir returned, performing essentially the same arrangements. Hans Zimmer returned as composer. The musical engagement did not significantly advance beyond the original [8][9].

**2024:** Nicholas Britell's score for *Mufasa* represents a genuine evolution. Britell, classically trained at Yale and known for his Oscar-nominated scores for *Moonlight* and *If Beale Street Could Talk* (both directed by Jenkins), composed in active collaboration with Lebo M. and the South African Cultural Gospel Choir. Lin-Manuel Miranda contributed new songs. The resulting score is more integrated than the 1994 original -- the African choral elements and the orchestral elements interact as equals rather than the choral serving as atmospheric support for the orchestral [3][8].

## 14. The Evolution of Engagement

The Lion King franchise's cultural trajectory -- from the 1994 original through the 2019 remake to Jenkins' *Mufasa* -- maps a broader cultural evolution in how Western institutions engage with non-Western cultural material. This evolution is neither complete nor linear, but the direction is clear: from extraction to collaboration, from using to partnering, from speaking about to creating space for [1][2][3].

The Nicholas Britell score for *Mufasa*, composed in collaboration with Lebo M. and the South African Cultural Gospel Choir, represents this evolution in musical form. Britell, a classically trained American composer, worked with Lebo M. not as an arranger hired to add "African flavor" to a Western score but as a creative partner whose musical authority in the African idiom was recognized and deferred to. The resulting soundtrack is a genuine dialogue between Western orchestral and African choral traditions, not a one-way extraction [3][8].

The evolution is not finished. Future iterations of the franchise -- and future AI-assisted heritage documentation projects -- will need to go further: not just creating space for African voices within Western production frameworks but supporting the development of African-led production frameworks that tell these stories on their own terms. The griot tradition has maintained the Sundiata epic for seven hundred years without Western assistance. The question is not whether African storytelling needs Western technology but whether Western technology can learn to serve African storytelling without colonizing it [1][2][12].

## 15. The Broadway Dimension

The Lion King's cultural engagement extends beyond film to its Broadway musical adaptation, which premiered in 1997 under the direction of Julie Taymor. Taymor's production represents yet another mode of cultural engagement with African material -- one that operates through theatrical convention rather than cinematic realism [4][5].

Taymor's design philosophy drew on African mask-making traditions, Indonesian shadow puppetry, and Japanese Bunraku puppet theatre. The animal costumes are not literal -- they are interpretive, combining the actor's visible human body with animal elements (a lion mask worn above the head, a giraffe on stilts, grasslands represented by dancers with grass headdresses). This theatrical approach actually achieves something that the photorealistic 2019 film could not: it foregrounds the artistry of representation itself, reminding the audience that they are watching a performance -- which is precisely how the griot tradition works [4][5].

The Broadway production's music includes additional songs by Lebo M., Mark Mancina, Jay Rifkin, and Taymor herself (with lyrics in Zulu, Swahili, and Congolese languages), deepening the African musical dimension beyond the original film's score. The show has been seen by over 110 million people worldwide and has grossed more than $10 billion, making it the highest-grossing entertainment title of all time across all media. Its longevity (running continuously since 1997) demonstrates the enduring resonance of the story [4][5].

The Broadway production's use of visible theatrical artifice -- the exposed puppetry mechanisms, the visible human bodies within the animal costumes, the abstract scenic design -- connects to a principle relevant to heritage documentation: transparency about the process of representation is itself a form of respect. When you show the audience how the illusion is created, you honor their intelligence and you honor the source material by refusing to pretend that you have captured it "objectively." The griot performs in public, where the audience can see the performance; Taymor shows the audience the strings [4][5].

## 16. The Critical Response

The Lion King franchise has not been without criticism regarding its cultural engagement, and documenting those critiques is essential to a balanced assessment [5][6][9].

**The hyena criticism.** Multiple critics and scholars have noted that the hyenas in the 1994 film are voiced by actors of color (Whoopi Goldberg, Cheech Marin) and are coded as a racialized underclass living in a segregated "elephant graveyard" -- a ghetto-like space separated from the Pride Lands. The 2019 remake addressed this somewhat by de-emphasizing the comedic coding, but the structural issue -- that the antagonist's army is coded as a racial underclass -- remains a valid critical concern [5][6].

**The generic "Africa" criticism.** The 1994 and 2019 films present "Africa" as a unified landscape and culture, rather than the continent of 54 nations, thousands of ethnic groups, and enormous geographic and cultural diversity that it actually is. Swahili names from East Africa, Zulu music from South Africa, and a visual landscape inspired by Kenya are blended into a single "African" setting that does not correspond to any actual cultural or geographic reality. Jenkins' *Mufasa* partially addresses this by depicting Mufasa's journey across multiple distinct landscapes, but the fundamental conflation persists [5][6].

**The appropriation question.** Some critics have argued that the Lion King franchise -- owned and profited from by an American corporation -- represents cultural appropriation: the use of African cultural elements for commercial gain without adequate attribution, compensation, or partnership with the source communities. This critique is distinct from the question of artistic quality; it is a question of economic and cultural justice. The franchise has generated over $15 billion in total revenue (film, Broadway, merchandise); how much of that has flowed back to the African communities whose cultural heritage it draws upon? [5][6].

These critiques do not invalidate the franchise's genuine engagement with African culture -- Lebo M.'s contributions, Jenkins' collaborative process, the visual research trips. They do, however, illustrate the complexity of cross-cultural adaptation and the distance between artistic intention and cultural impact. The GSD heritage documentation ecosystem must grapple with the same complexity: good intentions do not guarantee respectful outcomes, and the measure of cultural engagement is not the adapter's intent but the source community's experience [5][6][11].

## 17. What Remains To Be Done

This module has traced the evolution of cultural engagement across thirty years of the Lion King franchise, from sincere but limited engagement (1994) to genuine collaboration (2024). But the evolution is not complete, and documenting what remains to be done is as important as documenting what has been achieved [1][2][3].

**African-led storytelling infrastructure.**
The Lion King franchise remains a product of the Walt Disney Company, an American corporation.

Even Jenkins' collaborative process operated within Disney's production framework: the studio greenlit the project, set the budget, approved the final cut, and collected the revenue.

A fully realized evolution would involve African production companies and storytellers originating Lion King-scale projects from African creative soil, with global distribution partnerships that preserve African creative authority.

**Economic reciprocity.**
The Lion King franchise has generated over $15 billion in total revenue across film, Broadway, merchandise, and licensing.

The question of how much economic benefit has flowed back to the African communities whose cultural heritage the franchise draws upon remains unanswered.

Future iterations should address this directly -- through revenue-sharing agreements, cultural foundation funding, or direct investment in African creative infrastructure.

**Source community involvement in critical assessment.**
This module's analysis of cultural authenticity has been conducted primarily through Western critical frameworks and published interviews.

A more complete assessment would include the perspectives of Mandinka griots, Zulu musicians, East African cultural practitioners, and other source community members on how they experience the franchise's use of their cultural heritage.

Those voices are essential and largely absent from the published critical literature.
