## 1. The Jali System

The *jali* (plural *jeliw*; also known by the French-derived term *griot*) occupies a hereditary social role in Mande societies across West Africa.

The tradition is primarily found in Mali, Guinea, Senegal, The Gambia, and Burkina Faso.

The Mande social structure traditionally divides people into three broad categories:

- *horon* (freeborn/nobles)
- *nyamakala* (artisan/specialist castes)
- *jon* (a historical enslaved class)

The *jeliw* belong to the *nyamakala* -- they are specialists, and their specialty is *nyama*: the vital energy or power that resides in words, in music, and in the knowledge of who people are [1][2][3].

The word *jali* itself is debated among scholars.

Some derive it from the Mande root *jali* meaning "blood" -- the griot carries the blood-knowledge, the genealogical memory of the community.

Others connect it to the concept of speaking or calling.

The French colonial term *griot* (likely derived from the Portuguese *criado*, meaning servant or attendant) has been widely adopted but carries colonial connotations that some scholars and practitioners resist.

In contemporary scholarly and cultural usage, *jali* is preferred as the term that comes from the tradition itself [1][2].

The *jali*'s position in Mande society is both honored and complex. Griots are essential -- no ceremony, no political event, no marriage, no naming ceremony can properly occur without a griot's participation. Yet the griot's power is also feared: a griot who withdraws their praise or speaks genealogical truth that someone wishes hidden can cause social devastation. The griot's art, *jeliya*, is a form of social power that operates outside the structures of political or military authority [1][2][3].

## 2. Multiple Roles of the Griot

The Western tendency to call the griot a "storyteller" or "musician" dramatically understates the role.

The *jali* simultaneously fills at least six distinct social functions.

Each of these would constitute a separate professional specialization in Western societies [1][2][4].

**Genealogist.** The griot maintains the genealogical records of families, clans, and entire nations. These are not decorative -- they are constitutive of social identity. In traditional Mandinka society, knowing one's lineage determines one's social role, marriage eligibility, and political standing. The griot's recitation of genealogy before battle or ceremony is not historical entertainment but an assertion of legitimate identity and claim. The Sundiata epic itself begins with genealogy: "Listen then, sons of Manding, children of the black people, listen to my word, for I am going to tell you of Sundiata, the father of the Bright Country, of the savanna land, the ancestor of those who draw the bow..." [1][8].

**Historian.** The griot preserves the history of the community in narrative form. This includes not only epic tales like Sundiata but also the records of wars, alliances, migrations, treaties, natural disasters, and political events stretching back centuries. The griot's history is not "objective" in the Western academic sense -- it is shaped by the griot's patron relationships and community allegiances -- but neither is any history. The griot's advantage is transparency: the audience knows who the griot serves and can calibrate accordingly [1][2][4].

**Musician.** The griot is a master of one or more instruments: the kora (a 21-string bridge-harp), the balafon (a wooden-keyed xylophone), the ngoni (a plucked string instrument ancestor of the banjo), and the voice. Musical mastery is not separate from the other roles -- it is the medium through which genealogy, history, and praise are transmitted. The melodic and rhythmic structures of griot music are integral to the knowledge-preservation function [1][5][6].

**Counselor.** Griots serve as advisors to rulers, using their knowledge of history and genealogy to provide context for political decisions. The relationship between a king and his griot is not that of ruler and servant but of decision-maker and institutional memory. The griot can say things to a king that no one else can -- invoking ancestral examples, reminding the ruler of promises made, or warning of historical patterns that the current situation resembles [1][2].

**Diplomat.** In inter-clan or inter-kingdom disputes, griots serve as mediators and negotiators. Their hereditary neutrality -- they belong to the *nyamakala* caste, outside the political hierarchy of nobles -- gives them a unique position from which to mediate. A griot sent as an emissary between warring factions carries the authority of the tradition itself, not merely the authority of the king who sent them [1][2][3].

**Praise-Singer.** At ceremonies, the griot performs *baka* (praise-singing) -- recounting the honored deeds of the person being celebrated, connecting them to their ancestors, and affirming their social identity. Praise-singing at weddings, naming ceremonies, and political gatherings is not flattery but a ritual affirmation of social bonds. The withdrawal of praise -- a griot's refusal to sing for someone -- is a powerful social sanction [1][2].

## 3. The Hereditary Lineage

*Jeliya* is inherited, not learned. One cannot become a griot by study or choice -- one is born into a griot family and trained from childhood by one's parents and extended family. The major griot lineages (family names) include the Kouyate, Diabate, Kante, Koite, and Sissoko families. These family names carry immediate social recognition across Mande-speaking West Africa: if your family name is Kouyate, everyone knows you are a griot. Marriage between griots and non-griots was traditionally prohibited, maintaining the hereditary boundary [1][2][3].

The training of a young griot begins in early childhood and continues for decades. A young *jali* learns first by listening -- absorbing the songs, stories, and genealogies performed by parents and relatives. Formal training on instruments typically begins around age seven. Apprenticeship to a master griot (often an uncle or grandparent) involves years of study: memorizing genealogies that may contain hundreds of names across dozens of generations, learning the musical repertoire, developing the vocal techniques specific to griot performance, and absorbing the social and diplomatic skills that the role requires [1][2][6].

This hereditary system means that the knowledge preserved by griots has been transmitted through the same family lines for centuries. The Kouyate family's connection to the Sundiata epic is not abstract -- they claim direct descent from Balla Fasseke, the griot assigned to Sundiata before his father's death. Seven hundred years of continuous family-to-family transmission is not a metaphor; it is an auditable chain of custody [1][8][9].

## 4. The Kouyate Dynasty

The Kouyate lineage deserves specific attention as the exemplary case of griot continuity. According to the Sundiata epic itself, Balla Fasseke was the griot assigned to the young Sundiata by Sundiata's father, King Maghan Kon Fatta. When Sundiata was exiled, Balla Fasseke was separated from him -- in some versions, he was captured by the tyrant Soumaoro Kante, who had also seized the sacred balafon (the Sosso Bala). When Sundiata defeated Soumaoro at the Battle of Kirina (c. 1235 CE), Balla Fasseke was reunited with his king, and the Kouyate lineage was given the hereditary responsibility of preserving the Sundiata epic and the Sosso Bala [8][9].

The Sosso Bala -- a balafon believed to be the original instrument associated with the Sundiata epic -- is still in the custody of the Kouyate family in Niagassola, Guinea. In 2001, UNESCO inscribed the Sosso Bala on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, recognizing both the instrument and the oral tradition it embodies. The current custodian is a Kouyate descendant, maintaining an unbroken chain of guardianship from the 13th century [7][9].

Balla Kouyate, a contemporary balafon player based in the United States, was named a 2019 National Endowment for the Arts National Heritage Fellow -- the highest honor the U.S. government bestows on traditional artists. His recognition represents the continuation of the Kouyate lineage's musical and cultural role into the 21st century, adapted to a diasporic context while maintaining its core identity [9][10].

## 5. Instruments of the Jali

The three primary instruments of the *jali* are the kora, the balafon, and the ngoni. Each has a distinct role in the musical ecosystem of *jeliya* and each carries its own symbolic and historical significance [5][6].

**The Kora.** A 21-string bridge-harp unique to West Africa, the kora is constructed from a large calabash (gourd) cut in half and covered with cowhide, with a long hardwood neck and two rows of strings (11 on the left, 10 on the right) attached to the neck with leather tuning rings. The player holds the instrument vertically by two wooden dowels that protrude from the calabash and plucks the strings with the thumbs and index fingers of both hands. The resulting sound is often compared to a harp or a harpsichord but is distinct -- bright, rippling cascades of notes that provide both melody and accompaniment simultaneously. The kora is the instrument most associated with *jeliya* today and is the primary vehicle for the performance of the Sundiata epic [5][6].

**The Balafon.** A wooden-keyed percussion instrument with gourd resonators, the balafon is one of the oldest instruments in West Africa. The Sosso Bala -- the sacred balafon associated with the Sundiata epic -- is a balafon with 20 wooden keys, each with a gourd resonator tuned to a specific pitch. The balafon's percussive, melodic sound provides a rhythmic foundation for narrative recitation. Balafon players in the griot tradition develop extraordinary technical skill, playing rapid melodic passages while maintaining complex polyrhythmic patterns [5][6][7].

**The Ngoni.** A plucked string instrument with a wooden or gourd body and a skin soundboard, the ngoni is the ancestor of instruments that, through the transatlantic slave trade, eventually evolved into the banjo. The connection is not speculative -- musicologists have traced a direct organological lineage from the Mande ngoni through the Caribbean akonting and similar instruments to the early American banjo. The ngoni's history thus connects West African *jeliya* to the development of American roots music, blues, and eventually rock and roll [5][11].

## 6. Oral Tradition as Technology

Western scholarship has historically positioned oral tradition as a "lesser" form of knowledge preservation -- a primitive precursor to writing that suffers from inevitable degradation, like a game of telephone. Contemporary research has fundamentally challenged this view. Oral tradition, as practiced by griots and other specialist knowledge-keepers, is a sophisticated technology with built-in integrity mechanisms that writing does not possess [1][4][12].

The key advantages of oral tradition as a knowledge technology include: **redundancy** (multiple griots in multiple families across multiple regions maintain overlapping versions of the same narratives, providing natural backup); **performance context** (oral traditions are transmitted in public performance contexts where errors can be detected and corrected by other knowledgeable community members); **musical scaffolding** (melodic and rhythmic structures constrain the text, making certain kinds of errors immediately audible); **social accountability** (the griot's reputation and livelihood depend on accuracy, creating strong incentive structures for fidelity); and **adaptive capacity** (oral tradition can incorporate new information, adapt to new contexts, and remain relevant in ways that fixed written texts cannot) [1][4][12].

The limitation of oral tradition is not accuracy but scalability: an oral tradition can maintain extraordinary fidelity within its community of practice but cannot easily be transmitted to communities outside that practice without the contextual knowledge that makes the transmission work. Writing solves the scalability problem at the cost of the performance context and social accountability that give oral tradition its integrity [4][12].

## 7. Error Correction in Song

The musical dimension of griot performance is not ornamental -- it is functional. Musicological research by scholars including Eric Charry, Lucy Duran, and Roderic Knight has demonstrated that the melodic and rhythmic structures of *jeliya* function as error-correction mechanisms analogous to checksums in digital data transmission [5][6][12].

In a sung narrative performance, the griot's text must fit the melodic contour and rhythmic pattern of the song. If a word is substituted or a phrase altered, the change disrupts the musical structure in ways that are immediately perceptible to both the performer and the audience. The songs constrain improvisation -- they allow for creative variation in phrasing and embellishment but resist changes to core narrative content because such changes would produce melodically or rhythmically incorrect sequences [5][6].

This is analogous to how a sonnet's rhyme scheme and meter constrain the poet's word choices: certain substitutions are simply impossible because they would break the form. In the griot tradition, the "form" is not a written template but a musical one, encoded in the performer's trained ear and muscle memory. The result is a system that permits creative variation (each performance is unique) while maintaining structural fidelity (core narrative elements remain stable across performances and across generations) [5][6][12].

The evidence for this stability is empirical: the more than sixty published variants of the Sundiata epic, collected from different griots across different countries over a span of decades, show remarkable consistency in core structural elements -- the same characters, the same key events, the same causal sequences -- despite significant variation in detail, emphasis, and narrative framing [8][10].

## 8. Stability Across Variants

The published variants of the Sundiata epic provide a natural experiment in oral transmission fidelity. Niane's 1960 version (from Djeli Mamoudou Kouyate in Guinea), Johnson's 1986 version (from a different oral tradition in Mali), and subsequent scholarly collections demonstrate both the range and the limits of variation [8][10].

Core elements that remain stable across virtually all variants: Sundiata's parentage (son of Maghan Kon Fatta and Sogolon Conde); his childhood disability; his miraculous first steps; his exile; his growth to power in a foreign kingdom; his defeat of Soumaoro Kante at the Battle of Kirina; his establishment of the Mali Empire; and his promulgation of the Kouroukan Fouga. These structural elements are the "checksummed" core of the epic -- the content that the musical and narrative framework protects [8][9][10].

Elements that vary significantly across versions: the specific details of Sundiata's childhood, the exact itinerary of his exile, the names and roles of secondary characters, the precise nature of Soumaoro's magical powers, the specific terms of the Kouroukan Fouga, and the balance between historical narrative and praise-song. These variations represent the "improvisation space" that the tradition permits -- the creative freedom that makes each performance a living event rather than a rote recitation [8][10].

This pattern -- stable core with variable periphery -- is precisely what one would expect from an effective knowledge-preservation technology. The system preserves what matters most (the social charter, the genealogical claims, the political lessons) while allowing the performer to adapt the presentation to the audience, the occasion, and the moment [4][12].

## 9. The Sundiata Epic in Performance

A full performance of the Sundiata epic is not a reading or a recitation -- it is a communal event that transforms everyone present into participants rather than spectators. Performances can last from several hours to multiple days, depending on the occasion and the griot's chosen level of detail. The performance typically combines singing, instrumental accompaniment (usually kora or balafon), spoken narration, dramatic vocal characterization, and audience participation. The setting matters: griots traditionally perform at significant social occasions -- weddings, naming ceremonies, political gatherings, harvest festivals -- where the community is already assembled and the social bonds that the performance reinforces are already activated [1][6][8].

The economics of performance are also significant. Griots are traditionally compensated through gifts from the audience -- particularly from the individuals or families being praised. A skilled griot at a wealthy wedding may receive substantial gifts of money, clothing, or other valuables. This economic model means that the griot's livelihood depends on their reputation, which depends on their accuracy, skill, and social intelligence. The economic incentive structure reinforces the accuracy of the tradition: a griot who fabricates genealogies or makes errors in historical narrative will lose patrons and income [1][2][6].

The griot begins with an invocation: an address to the ancestors, to the great griots of the past, and to the audience, establishing the authority to tell the story. The opening genealogy may take a significant portion of the performance, tracing Sundiata's lineage back through generations. This is not filler -- it is the foundation of the story's authority. The genealogy establishes that the griot knows who Sundiata was, where he came from, and therefore has the right to tell his story [1][8].

The narrative unfolds through alternating passages of song and speech. Key moments -- Sundiata's first steps, the exile, the Battle of Kirina -- are marked by specific songs that the audience may recognize and anticipate. The audience's role is active: they respond with verbal affirmations, applause, and sometimes gifts of money. A skilled griot reads the audience and adjusts the performance accordingly -- expanding sections that resonate, abbreviating sections that lose attention, adding commentary that connects the ancient narrative to contemporary concerns [1][6][8].

The emotional climax of most performances is the Battle of Kirina and its aftermath: Sundiata's triumph over Soumaoro, the founding of the empire, and the promulgation of the Kouroukan Fouga -- the oral constitution that established the principles of Mandinka governance. This climax is not merely narrative but political: it affirms the legitimacy of the social order that the audience inhabits. The epic is not a story about the past -- it is a charter for the present [8][9].

The performance's relationship to truth is also distinctive. Western scholarship tends to ask "did this really happen?" -- seeking historical verifiability for the events narrated. The griot tradition operates on a different epistemological framework: the truth of the performance is not primarily historical but social. The genealogies establish real social relationships. The values encoded in the narrative (loyalty, courage, justice, the balance of *badenya* and *fadenya*) are real ethical commitments. The political lessons (the consequences of tyranny, the importance of legitimate succession, the role of counselors and mediators) are real political wisdom. Whether Sundiata literally could not walk as a child, or whether the iron rod literally bent when he stood, is less important than what those episodes teach about perseverance, destiny, and the relationship between physical limitation and spiritual strength [1][8][9].

## 10. The Geography of Jeliya

The griot tradition is not a single, uniform practice but a family of related traditions distributed across the Mande cultural sphere of West Africa. The specific practices, repertoires, and social functions of *jeliya* vary by region, language group, and historical context [1][2][3].

**Mali** is the heartland of the Sundiata epic tradition. Bamako, the capital, is a center of contemporary griot performance, with major families maintaining urban practices alongside their rural roots. The town of Kangaba, near the Mandinka heartland, hosts a septennial ceremony (every seven years) at which the Kamabolon -- a sacred hut associated with Sundiata -- is re-roofed, accompanied by extended griot performances. This ceremony represents one of the most important living connections to the Sundiata tradition [1][2][8].

**Guinea** -- particularly the Mandinka region around Kankan and the Sosso Bala repository in Niagassola -- maintains distinct performance traditions that emphasize the balafon alongside the kora. The Guinean variant of the Sundiata epic has its own narrative emphases and regional characters. Guinea's political history (the socialist period under Sekou Toure, 1958-1984, which institutionalized some aspects of griot performance through national cultural troupes) has shaped the tradition in ways distinct from Mali [1][2][7].

**Senegal and The Gambia** emphasize the kora tradition, with Gambian kora players (including Toumani Diabate's father, Sidiki Diabate) having played a crucial role in bringing kora music to international audiences. The Senegambian variant of *jeliya* integrates more strongly with Wolof and Fula musical traditions, producing a distinctive sound world [2][6].

**Burkina Faso, Guinea-Bissau, and northern Cote d'Ivoire** maintain related but distinct griot traditions, each with regional instruments, repertoires, and social conventions. The diversity of the tradition across this geographic range is itself evidence of its vitality: it is not a museum piece preserved in a single location but a living practice that adapts to local conditions while maintaining core functions [1][2][3].

## 11. Jeliya in Modern Context

The griot tradition has not remained static -- it has adapted to modern contexts while maintaining its core functions. Contemporary *jeliw* perform at presidential inaugurations, appear on international concert stages, release recordings on major and independent labels, and maintain active presences on social media and streaming platforms [1][2][10].

Toumani Diabate (Mali), one of the most internationally renowned kora players, has released over twenty albums and collaborated with artists including Bjork, Damon Albarn, and the London Symphony Orchestra. His work demonstrates how *jeliya* can engage with global musical traditions without abandoning its core identity. Diabate's kora improvisations are rooted in the same melodic and rhythmic structures that his ancestors used to perform the Sundiata epic, even when the context is a concert hall in London or a recording studio in Bamako [6][10].

Seckou Keita (Senegal/UK) has brought the kora to audiences across Europe and the Americas, performing both traditional *jeliya* repertoire and contemporary compositions that blend Mande musical structures with jazz, classical, and popular forms. His educational work -- teaching workshops on the kora and the griot tradition -- represents a new mode of transmission that complements the hereditary family-to-family model [10].

The griot tradition faces challenges in the modern era: urbanization has disrupted the village communities that were the traditional context for *jeliya*; recorded media allows people to experience griot music without the social relationship that traditionally accompanies it; and economic pressures push some griots toward commercial entertainment models that may dilute the tradition's social functions. But the tradition has survived colonialism, independence, military coups, and economic crises. Its resilience suggests that the core functions of *jeliya* -- preserving identity, maintaining social bonds, and transmitting knowledge across generations -- remain essential even as the contexts change [1][2][10].

## 12. Connection to Heritage Documentation

The griot model parallels the Foxfire heritage documentation methodology in several key respects that illuminate what AI-assisted heritage documentation should aspire to [13][14].

**Person-to-person transmission.** Both the griot tradition and the Foxfire methodology prioritize direct human-to-human knowledge transfer. Foxfire, founded by Eliot Wigginton in 1966 at Rabun Gap-Nacoochee School in Georgia, taught high school students to interview their Appalachian elders and publish the resulting cultural knowledge in magazines and books. The process of interviewing -- of sitting with someone and listening while they explain how they make soap, build a chimney, or cure leather -- is itself a form of cultural transmission that creates bonds between generations [13][14].

**The documenter is honored.** In the griot tradition, the role of the person who preserves knowledge is not subordinate to the knowledge itself. The griot is not a servant of the story but a co-creator -- each performance adds the griot's artistry, interpretation, and personality to the tradition. Similarly, Foxfire honored the student interviewers as active participants in cultural preservation, not mere transcription machines [13][14].

**Preservation is production.** The griot's act of performing the Sundiata epic is simultaneously an act of preservation and an act of cultural creation -- the tradition is maintained because it is continually performed, and each performance renews and revitalizes it. Foxfire's publications were not dusty archives but living documents that brought Appalachian cultural knowledge to millions of readers and inspired similar programs worldwide [13][14].

The GSD ecosystem's heritage documentation tools can study the *jeliya* as a model for what AI-assisted heritage preservation should aspire to: not replacing the human keeper but amplifying their reach. The griot's role is irreplaceable -- no recording, no archive, no AI can perform the social functions of the *jali*. But the tools can do what the tools are good at: store, index, cross-reference, and make searchable the knowledge that the human keeper generates. The griot sings; the tool remembers the song [13][14].

## 13. The Griot and the GSD Ecosystem

The griot tradition offers specific lessons for the GSD ecosystem's approach to knowledge preservation and cultural documentation [1][13][14].

**Redundancy over centralization.** The griot tradition survives because multiple families across multiple regions maintain overlapping versions of the same knowledge. No single griot's death can destroy the tradition because the knowledge exists in many minds simultaneously. The GSD ecosystem's distributed architecture -- multiple agents, multiple skill packs, multiple storage locations -- mirrors this redundancy principle.

**Performance context matters.** A recorded griot performance lacks the social context, the audience interaction, and the real-time adaptation that make a live performance a living event. Similarly, documentation stored in an archive is less valuable than documentation embedded in an active workflow where it is regularly consulted, updated, and applied. The GSD skill system's context-sensitive activation -- skills that load based on what the user is doing -- is an attempt to preserve the "performance context" that gives knowledge its practical value.

**Accuracy requires accountability.** The griot's accuracy is maintained not by peer review but by social accountability -- the griot's reputation depends on fidelity, and the community can detect errors. In the GSD ecosystem, verification agents, test suites, and the CAPCOM gate structure serve a similar function: they create accountability structures that catch errors before they propagate.

**The chain of custody is the authority.** A Kouyate griot's authority to tell the Sundiata epic comes from the Kouyate lineage's seven-hundred-year chain of custody. In the GSD ecosystem, the provenance of knowledge -- where it came from, who validated it, what sources support it -- is the equivalent of the genealogical chain. Source attribution in research modules is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the chain of custody that gives the knowledge its authority.

## 14. The Griot and the Lion King

The griot tradition's connection to The Lion King is not incidental -- it is structural. The character of Rafiki in The Lion King fills a griot-like role: he is the keeper of knowledge, the advisor to the king, the one who remembers the genealogy (he marks Simba's birth on the baobab tree, a form of genealogical record-keeping), and the one who calls the heir back to his duty. Rafiki's famous line -- "The question is, who are you?" -- echoes the griot's fundamental function: asserting identity through genealogical knowledge [8][1].

In Barry Jenkins' *Mufasa* (2024), Rafiki's griot function is made even more explicit. The older Rafiki (voiced by John Kani) serves as the narrator, telling the story of Mufasa's origins to the next generation -- precisely the griot's role. Kagiso Lediga's younger Rafiki is characterized as a "pan-Africanist" traveler, moving between cultures and languages, which maps to the griot's diplomatic function: the intermediary who moves between communities, carrying knowledge and building bridges [1][8].

The baobab tree where Rafiki lives is itself significant. In many West African traditions, the baobab (*Adansonia digitata*) is a sacred tree associated with wisdom, age, and community gathering. Griots traditionally performed under specific trees -- often baobabs -- that served as community meeting places. The tree was not merely a location but a participant in the performance: its age and permanence symbolized the continuity of the tradition. Rafiki's baobab tree thus functions as more than a set piece; it is a cultural reference to the specific spaces where griots practice their art [1][2].

The parallel extends to the concept of *nyama* -- the vital energy or power that griots channel through their words and music. Rafiki's spiritual powers in the film (his ability to sense Simba's presence, his shamanic practices, his communion with natural forces) map loosely to the *nyama* concept: the griot's power is not political or military but spiritual and verbal, operating through a different register of authority than the king's [1][2][3].

## 15. Threats and Resilience

The griot tradition faces significant challenges in the 21st century, but it has shown remarkable resilience across centuries of upheaval [1][2][10].

**Colonialism** imposed European administrative and educational systems that did not recognize the griot's social role. French colonial policy in West Africa promoted French-language schooling and marginalized indigenous knowledge systems, including oral tradition. Yet the griots survived -- they adapted by incorporating colonial-era events into their narratives, documenting the colonial experience through the same oral technology that had preserved the Sundiata epic [1][2].

**Urbanization** has disrupted the village communities that were the traditional performance context for *jeliya*. Young people in Bamako, Dakar, and Conakry may be more familiar with American pop music than with traditional kora performance. Yet urban griots have adapted: they perform at weddings and naming ceremonies in city settings, they record albums, they tour internationally, and they use social media and streaming platforms to reach audiences that their grandparents could not have imagined [2][10].

**Globalization** presents both threat and opportunity. The threat is dilution: when griot music becomes "world music" -- a marketing category that strips cultural context and reduces traditions to sonic ingredients for Western consumption -- the social functions of *jeliya* are lost. The opportunity is amplification: when griot musicians tour, teach, and record, they bring the tradition to new audiences and create new contexts for its practice. The challenge is maintaining the core functions -- genealogical preservation, social counseling, historical transmission -- while adapting the forms to new contexts [2][10].

**Digital technology** offers tools that could complement the oral tradition's knowledge-preservation functions: audio and video recording, digital archives, searchable databases of genealogical information. But these tools also carry risks: they can create a false sense that the tradition has been "captured" and no longer needs living practitioners, when in fact the social functions of the *jali* cannot be replicated by any technology. The griot is not a database; the griot is a relationship [2][10].

The tradition's survival through these challenges suggests that the core functions of *jeliya* -- preserving identity, maintaining social bonds, transmitting knowledge across generations -- remain essential even as the contexts change. The griot tradition has outlived kingdoms, colonial empires, and independence-era dictatorships. It will likely outlive the current technological revolution as well, adapting its forms while maintaining its functions, as it has for seven hundred years [1][2].

## 16. The Kora's Twenty-One Strings

The kora deserves a final meditation because it is the instrument most associated with the Sundiata epic and the one that most powerfully connects the griot tradition to contemporary global music [5][6].

The kora's twenty-one strings are traditionally tuned to one of several modes that correspond to different emotional and narrative registers.

The *silaba* mode is the most common tuning for narrative performance -- it provides a bright, major-key sound suitable for heroic recitation.

The *sauta* mode is darker, more introspective, used for laments and reflective passages.

The *hardino* mode is used for dance music and celebratory performances.

A skilled kora player can retune the instrument during performance by adjusting the leather tuning rings, shifting between modes as the narrative demands -- bright for triumph, dark for loss, rhythmic for battle.

The kora's sound is often described as "waterfall-like" -- cascades of notes that flow continuously, creating a texture that is both melody and accompaniment simultaneously.

The player's two thumbs and index fingers pluck the strings independently, producing polyrhythmic patterns that Western musicians find extraordinarily difficult to replicate.

This is not decorative virtuosity.

The polyrhythmic structure of kora performance is the musical substrate that carries the narrative content.

The rhythms constrain the text (as documented in Section 7), the modes set the emotional register, and the cascading melodic patterns create a sonic space within which the griot's voice operates.

The kora is not an accompaniment to the story.

It is the architecture of the story.

The twenty-one strings are the twenty-one pillars that hold up the seven-hundred-year palace of the Sundiata epic.
