## 1. Infrastructure as Sovereign Asset

Tribal radio infrastructure is not comparable to commercial radio infrastructure. A commercial station can close and its market share redistributes to competitors. When a tribal station goes dark, there is no competitor. There is no redistribution. There is silence -- and that silence has immediate, material consequences for public safety (loss of EAS), cultural continuity (loss of language programming), and community cohesion (loss of the shared information space) [1].

The physical infrastructure of tribal stations -- transmitters, control boards, antennas, solar arrays, studio equipment -- represents sovereign communication assets. Their vulnerability to theft, natural disaster, and funding withdrawal is a direct threat to the sovereign communication capacity of the nations they serve.

> **SOVEREIGNTY NOTE:** When KWSO's CPB funding is at risk, the threat is not merely financial. It is a threat to the communication infrastructure of a sovereign nation -- the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs. When KYNR was robbed, the theft was not merely property crime. It was the destruction of a sovereign nation's communication capacity. The framing matters because it determines the policy response: this is not a nonprofit funding issue, it is a sovereignty issue.

## 2. CPB Funding Architecture

The Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB) provides Community Service Grants (CSGs) to qualifying public radio and television stations across the United States. For tribal radio stations, these grants often represent a disproportionately large share of operating budgets because tribal stations have fewer alternative revenue sources than urban public radio stations [1].

### CPB-Tribal Station Relationship

- CPB provides annual Community Service Grants based on station size and service area
- Grants fund core operations: staff positions, equipment maintenance, programming acquisition
- For small tribal stations, CPB grants can represent 30-50% of total operating budget [1]
- CPB also funds national programming through Native Voice One / Koahnic Broadcast Corporation [2]

### KWSO's CPB Dependency

At KWSO, 2 of 6 staff positions are funded by CPB Community Service Grants [1]. This represents 33% of the station's human capacity funded by a single external source. The station's total operating budget is not publicly documented, but Sue Matters estimated that CPB cuts "could lose up to 40% of operating budget" [1].

## 3. The 2025 CPB Rescission

In July 2025, the U.S. Senate voted to rescind Corporation for Public Broadcasting funding, triggering a financial crisis for tribal radio stations across the country [1]. The impact on PNW tribal broadcasting was documented by Oregon Public Broadcasting and the Nieman Journalism Lab [1][2].

| Station/Entity | Documented Impact |
|---------------|-------------------|
| KWSO (Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs) | Could lose up to 40% of operating budget; 2 of 6 staff positions funded by CPB |
| Native Public Media network | Characterized CPB funding as "irreplaceable"; CEO Loris Taylor described cuts as "devastating" |
| Network scale | 57 tribal radio stations + 3 tribal TV stations in 20 states at risk |
| Proposed workaround | Sen. Rounds (R-SD) proposed diverting environmental program funds to Indigenous stations; characterized by APTS as a "half-measure" |
| Licensing complexity | Some stations licensed to tribe; some to nonprofits; no clean definition of eligible entities |

### Key Quotes

Sue Matters, KWSO Station Manager: "What's most troubling isn't just the short-term scramble for funding -- it's the long-term risk of dismantling an information ecosystem built over decades" [1].

Loris Taylor, CEO of Native Public Media: characterized CPB funding as "irreplaceable" and described the cuts as "devastating" [3].

Jaclyn Sallee, President and CEO of Koahnic Broadcast Corporation: noted in 2025 that stations are uncertain whether they can continue operations from October 1 or later in the fiscal year [2].

> **CAUTION:** This module presents the CPB funding crisis factually. No policy advocacy for or against CPB funding is included. The documented impact data speaks for itself.

## 4. KWSO: Quantified Impact

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Total staff | 6 | KWSO/OPB [1] |
| CPB-funded positions | 2 | KWSO/OPB [1] |
| Estimated budget impact | Up to 40% | KWSO Station Manager Sue Matters [1] |
| Coverage population | 50,000+ | KWSO [4] |
| Transmitter power | 4,300 watts | KWSO [4] |
| Solar array | 11.4 kW | KWSO [4] |
| EAS provider | Yes (Warm Springs area) | KWSO [4] |

The Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs indicated openness to temporarily covering the funding gap. However, long-term sustainability without CPB is uncertain [1]. KWSO is technically a tribal government department (Health & Human Services), which means the tribal government bears the cost of any unfunded operations -- competing with healthcare, education, housing, and social services for limited tribal revenue.

### Cascading Impact Analysis

The loss of 2 CPB-funded positions at a 6-person station is not a marginal reduction. It is a 33% workforce cut. At a station that provides:
- Daily local news programming
- Emergency Alert System broadcasts
- Cultural and language programming in three languages (Ichishkin Sinwit, Kiksht, Northern Paiute)
- Community calendar, birthday announcements, obituary services
- Fire season extended coverage
- Informal community information hotline function

A 33% staffing cut means some of these functions stop. The question is which ones, and the answer has direct consequences for public safety and cultural continuity [1].

## 5. Native Public Media Network-Wide Analysis

Native Public Media (NPM) functions as an NPR-equivalent infrastructure organization for tribal stations. As of 2025, the NPM network includes 57 tribal radio stations and 3 tribal television stations across 20 states [3].

### Network Vulnerability

The CPB rescission threatens the entire network simultaneously. Unlike commercial broadcasting, where individual station closures create market opportunities for competitors, tribal station closures create coverage voids with no replacement:

- Each station serves a specific sovereign nation or community with culturally embedded programming
- There is no "market" that redistributes the audience to another provider
- Many stations are the sole local broadcast outlet for their community
- National Native News (distributed to 400+ stations by Koahnic Broadcast Corporation) becomes unaffordable or unavailable if CPB funding for NV1 is cut [2]

### Proposed Legislative Workaround

Senator Rounds (R-SD) proposed diverting funds from environmental programs to Indigenous stations. The Association of Public Television Stations (APTS) characterized this as a "half-measure" [1]. The proposal highlights the definitional problem: which stations qualify? Some are licensed to the tribe directly; some are licensed to nonprofits. There is no clean legal definition of "tribal station" that maps neatly to funding eligibility [1].

## 6. Emergency Alert System Function

KWSO serves as the Emergency Alert System (EAS) provider for the Warm Springs area [4]. This function is not optional and not replaceable. It includes:

- **Wildfire alerts:** KWSO provides extensive fire coverage during wildfire season, reflecting both safety imperatives and the community's substantial involvement in wildland firefighting work [1]
- **Winter weather:** Snowstorm warnings, road closures, school closures
- **Missing Endangered Persons Alert:** The FCC adopted MEPA (analogous to AMBER alerts) in August 2024, with national launch pending. Tribal stations are a critical node in this new alert infrastructure for reservation communities with cell coverage gaps [5]
- **General emergency:** Any emergency requiring immediate community notification

### The EAS-CPB Connection

If CPB funding cuts force KWSO to reduce staff, the EAS function is at risk. Emergency alerts require:
- Staff physically present at the station or on-call
- Equipment maintained and tested
- Automated alert relay systems functioning
- Knowledge of local conditions (which roads flood, which areas evacuate first, where cell service fails)

This knowledge is institutional and local. It cannot be provided by a remote automated system. The loss of CPB-funded staff directly threatens the EAS capacity of KWSO, creating a public safety risk for the entire Warm Springs community [1].

```
EMERGENCY ALERT SYSTEM -- KWSO COVERAGE
================================================================

  EAS Chain:
  National Weather Service / FEMA / State Emergency
       |
       v
  KWSO 91.9 FM (Eagle Butte transmitter, 4,300W)
       |
       v
  Jefferson County + parts of Wasco, Crook, Deschutes Counties
  (~50,000 people, many without reliable cell/broadband)
       |
       v
  Community response:
  - Evacuation
  - School closure
  - Road closure awareness
  - Wildfire crew deployment
  - Missing person search

  KEY: For many in the coverage area, KWSO is the ONLY
  reliable real-time emergency information source.
```

## 7. KYNR Equipment Vulnerability: 2018 Case Study

In October 2018, KYNR was burglarized. The thieves took the control board, microphones, and equipment necessary for broadcast, taking the station completely off-air [6].

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Date of burglary | October 2018 |
| Equipment stolen | Control board, microphones, broadcast equipment |
| Estimated value stolen | ~$20,000 |
| Duration off-air | ~11 months |
| Affected listeners | 6,000+ (tribal members and non-Native listeners) |
| Recovery funding | $34,000 (Yakama Tribal Council allocation) |
| Healing request | Staff asked tribal leaders to bless the building upon return |

### The Wound and the Healing

Morning Host Roy Dick: "I was about ready to cry. There was nothing we could work with to even tell everybody we're off the air" [6].

Staff member Ronny Washines connected the station's return to cultural healing: when KYNR came back on the air, staff asked that tribal leaders come bless the building. "Because we have to heal" [6].

This response is the clearest evidence that tribal radio stations function as cultural organs rather than mere media outlets. Their loss is experienced as physical harm. Their restoration requires spiritual healing. No commercial radio station closure produces this response, because no commercial radio station holds this relationship to its community [6].

### Vulnerability Pattern

The KYNR burglary reveals a structural vulnerability in tribal broadcasting:

- **Physical concentration:** All critical equipment in one location
- **No redundancy:** A single theft eliminates all broadcast capability
- **Recovery timeline:** Eleven months from theft to return (bureaucratic, procurement, installation)
- **Financial dependency:** Recovery required Tribal Council allocation ($34,000) beyond station budget
- **Knowledge concentration:** Small staff means institutional knowledge is fragile

This vulnerability pattern applies to all small tribal stations and is exacerbated by the CPB funding crisis: stations with fewer resources have less capacity for redundancy, backup equipment, and security [6].

## 8. The Digital Divide on Reservation Lands

Native Public Media and the Nieman Journalism Lab document that many tribal nations, particularly in rural areas, lack reliable broadband and cell service [2][3]. Radio remains the most dependable communication tool in these communities.

### The Infrastructure Gap

The digital divide on reservation lands is not merely an inconvenience; it is a structural condition that determines which communication technologies work:

- **Broadband:** Many reservation communities have no wired broadband or unreliable satellite service
- **Cell coverage:** Coverage gaps are common, particularly in mountainous terrain (like the Warm Springs Reservation's 640,000 acres of central Oregon landscape)
- **Internet-dependent services:** Streaming radio, news apps, social media, email -- all require connectivity that may not exist
- **Radio:** FM and AM signals propagate through terrain and weather. They require no user equipment beyond a receiver. They work during power outages (battery-powered radios). They are the baseline [2]

### Radio as Baseline Infrastructure

The transition to digital-first media models -- streaming, podcasting, apps -- risks bypassing reservation communities unless tribal stations can maintain their terrestrial radio infrastructure while simultaneously developing digital presence [2]. Daybreak Star Radio represents an urban digital model; KWSO and KYNR represent the terrestrial rural model. Both are necessary; neither substitutes for the other.

For emergency alerts specifically, the digital divide means that a community with poor cell coverage cannot rely on wireless emergency alerts (WEA) sent to mobile phones. KWSO's FM broadcast is the fallback -- and in many cases, the primary channel [1].

## 9. The Warm Springs Media Center

The Warm Springs Media Center, opened in 2009, represents the most advanced physical infrastructure of any PNW tribal broadcasting facility [4]:

- **11.4-kilowatt solar panel array** -- one of the most energy-independent tribal media facilities in the region
- **Houses KWSO 91.9 FM** and the *Spilyay Tymoo* newspaper in a shared facility
- **Eco-friendly construction** -- designed for environmental sustainability
- **Funded by:** Meyer Memorial Trust and Corporation for Public Broadcasting [4]

The co-location of radio and newspaper in a single media center creates operational efficiencies: shared reporting staff, shared facilities, shared institutional knowledge. It also concentrates risk: a single facility fire or infrastructure failure could disable both the radio station and the newspaper simultaneously.

The solar array provides a degree of energy independence that is particularly valuable for emergency operations. During a power grid failure, KWSO's solar-powered facility can potentially continue broadcasting when other stations would go dark -- a critical advantage for EAS operations [4].

## 10. Alternative Funding Pathways

The CPB rescission forces tribal stations to seek alternative funding, but the pathways available to them are structurally limited:

### Challenges

- **No development staff:** KWSO has 6 total staff. None are dedicated grant writers or fundraisers [1]
- **Licensing ambiguity:** Tribe-licensed stations and nonprofit-licensed stations face different eligibility criteria for grants and government programs [1]
- **Tribal government budgets:** The Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs indicated openness to temporarily covering the gap, but tribal budgets compete among healthcare, education, housing, and social services [1]
- **Underwriting vs. advertising:** Non-commercial stations cannot run advertising. Underwriting (sponsor acknowledgment) is permitted but generates modest revenue in small rural markets [2]
- **Foundation grants:** Available but typically project-based (new equipment, specific programs) rather than operating support [2]

### Potential Pathways

- **Tribal government operating allocation:** Direct funding from tribal government budgets, treating the station as essential government infrastructure like law enforcement or health services
- **Foundation grants:** Meyer Memorial Trust (historically funded KWSO), other philanthropic organizations
- **Tribal enterprise revenue:** Revenue from tribal businesses directed to station operations
- **Listener support:** Membership/donation model (limited by small coverage population and economic conditions on reservations)
- **Inter-tribal partnerships:** Shared content production to reduce per-station costs

None of these pathways are equivalent to CPB Community Service Grants in scale, reliability, or administrative simplicity [1].

## 11. Lessons from the KYNR Recovery

The KYNR 2018 burglary and subsequent recovery provides a case study in tribal broadcasting resilience that illuminates both strengths and vulnerabilities of the sovereign broadcasting model [6].

### What Worked

- **Tribal Council response:** The Yakama Tribal Council allocated $34,000 for station recovery -- more than the $20,000 value of stolen equipment. This demonstrates that when a station is embedded in tribal government, the government can mobilize recovery resources that an independent nonprofit might struggle to access [6].
- **Community demand:** The community's vocal support for station recovery -- staff received consistent messages of support during the 11-month silence -- demonstrated the station's value in a way that no audience survey could replicate. The demand for restoration was cultural, not merely informational [6].
- **Healing ceremony:** The request that tribal leaders bless the station upon its return transformed the recovery from a technical process into a community healing event. This spiritual dimension strengthened the station's relationship to the community and reaffirmed its role as a cultural organ [6].

### What Exposed Vulnerabilities

- **Single point of failure:** All broadcast equipment in one location, with no backup capability. When the control board was stolen, there was no fallback.
- **No insurance documentation:** The sources do not document whether KYNR carried equipment insurance. Many small tribal stations may be underinsured or uninsured for theft and disaster.
- **Procurement delay:** The 11-month recovery period reflects the time required to secure funding, procure replacement equipment, install it, and return to broadcast. Shortening this gap requires pre-positioned backup equipment or mutual aid agreements with neighboring stations.
- **Information blackout:** During the 11 months off-air, 6,000+ listeners had no local radio source. There was no interim communication solution -- no temporary low-power transmitter, no online stream, no substitute channel [6].

### Recommendations from the Case Study

The KYNR experience suggests that tribal broadcasting resilience planning should address:

1. **Physical security:** Improved building security for stations with irreplaceable equipment
2. **Equipment redundancy:** Backup control board, microphone, and broadcast chain stored separately from the main studio
3. **Emergency broadcast plan:** A documented procedure for getting back on air within days rather than months after equipment loss
4. **Mutual aid network:** Agreements between PNW tribal stations for emergency equipment loans
5. **Insurance review:** Ensuring adequate coverage for equipment replacement at full current replacement cost

INFRASTRUCTURE RESILIENCE -- PNW TRIBAL STATIONS
================================================================

  KWSO (Warm Springs)
  + Solar power (energy independence)
  + Shared facility (media center efficiency)
  + EAS designation (institutional importance)
  + Tribal government integration (sovereign backing)
  - 6-person staff (minimal redundancy)

  KYNR (Yakama)
  + Tribal Council funding commitment ($34K recovery)
  + Music library (cultural archive value)
  + Multimedia program integration (newspaper + radio)
  - AM transmission (lower fidelity, interference susceptibility)
  - 2018 burglary demonstrated physical vulnerability
  - Small staff (single-theft takedown proven)

  Daybreak Star (Seattle)
  + Urban location (infrastructure access)
  + Online model (no transmitter vulnerability)
  + App distribution (cross-platform reach)
  + International audience (diversified listener base)
  - Internet dependency (no terrestrial fallback)
  - No EAS function (cannot serve emergency role)
  - Foundation funding model (grant-dependent)
