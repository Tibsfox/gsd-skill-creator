## 1. Language as Sovereign Infrastructure

On-air language programming at tribal radio stations is among the most scalable revitalization tools available. A single broadcast reaches the entire coverage area simultaneously -- elders speaking to children, teachers reinforcing classroom learning, community members hearing their language used naturally in context rather than in artificial educational settings. The radio signal makes language part of daily life rather than a subject studied in isolation [1].

> **OCAP NOTE:** This module follows the principle of Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession (OCAP) as articulated by the First Nations Information Governance Centre. Language documentation here describes only what stations have themselves made public through their own broadcasts and published materials. The languages belong to their nations. What appears on public airwaves and in station-published materials is documented here; internal language curricula and sacred ceremonial language are outside the scope of this research and must remain under the exclusive control of their respective nations.

The stations documented in this module serve communities with distinct living languages -- not museum languages, not reconstructed languages, but languages spoken in homes, at ceremonies, on the radio, in daily life. The broadcasting infrastructure serves as both amplifier and archive: it puts the languages in the air where people hear them, and it creates recordings that can serve future generations.

## 2. KWSO: Three Peoples, Three Languages

The Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs encompasses three distinct nations, each with their own language. Station Manager Sue Matters has explicitly described this context: "Three distinct cultures. Three distinct languages" [2]. KWSO's programming must navigate this multilingual reality and serves language revitalization goals for all three communities.

| Nation | Language | Language Family | Context |
|--------|----------|----------------|---------|
| Warm Springs (Tenino) | Ichishkin Sinwit (Sahaptin) | Sahaptian language family | Related to Yakama language; part of Plateau culture area |
| Wasco (Wishxam) | Kiksht | Upper Chinookan (Chinookan family) | Distinct language family from Sahaptin; historically trade language speakers |
| Paiute | Northern Paiute (Numu) | Numic branch, Uto-Aztecan family | Great Basin origin; distinct from both Sahaptin and Chinookan |

This trilingual context is not merely linguistic diversity -- it reflects three distinct cultural traditions, three distinct histories, and three distinct relationships to the land and water of central Oregon. KWSO serves all three simultaneously, which is itself an act of confederated governance: honoring the 1855 Treaty structure that brought these three peoples together on one reservation while maintaining their distinct identities [3].

### Language Programming Strategy

KWSO's cultural programming includes content specifically addressing tribal traditions and the 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon, which defines reservation boundaries and ensures access to traditional hunting, fishing, and gathering areas [3]. The station's language programming operates within the larger mission of education, cultural knowledge promotion, and community awareness that the station was founded to serve [1].

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** The specific details of language curriculum content, ceremonial language use, and internal tribal education programs are matters of tribal sovereignty. This module documents only what KWSO has made publicly available through its broadcasts, website, and published interviews. Researchers seeking deeper engagement with language revitalization programs must work directly with the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs through appropriate tribal government channels.

## 3. The Sahaptin Language Family

Ichishkin Sinwit (also written Ichishkiin Sinwit) is the Sahaptin language spoken by the Warm Springs people (Tenino). It belongs to the Sahaptian language family, which includes multiple dialects spoken across the Columbia Plateau region. The Yakama dialect of Sahaptin (spoken at KYNR's community) is closely related but distinct -- the two dialects share mutual intelligibility but reflect different communities and cultural contexts [4].

The Sahaptin language family has deep roots in the Plateau culture area of the Pacific Northwest. Sahaptin speakers historically inhabited the Columbia River basin and its tributaries, with language boundaries roughly following major watershed divisions. The language carries within it place names, ecological knowledge, and seasonal cycle information that predates European contact by millennia [4].

### Ichishkin Sinwit at KWSO

On-air use of Ichishkin Sinwit at KWSO serves multiple functions simultaneously:

- **Intergenerational transmission:** Elders speaking the language on air reach younger generations who may not have daily conversational exposure at home
- **Normalization:** Hearing the language used in everyday broadcast contexts (news, weather, community announcements) reinforces that it is a living language of daily use, not an artifact
- **Vocabulary expansion:** Broadcast contexts require the language to express modern concepts, driving natural vocabulary development
- **Geographic reach:** The 4,300-watt FM signal reaches far beyond individual households, extending language exposure across the entire reservation and surrounding area [1]

## 4. Kiksht: Upper Chinookan at Warm Springs

Kiksht (also known as Upper Chinookan or Wishram-Wasco) is the language of the Wasco people at Warm Springs. It belongs to the Chinookan language family, which is entirely distinct from the Sahaptian family of the Warm Springs and Yakama peoples. The Chinookan languages historically dominated the lower and middle Columbia River, with Wasco-Wishram speakers controlling key trade sites along the Columbia [5].

Kiksht is critically endangered. The number of fluent speakers has declined dramatically over the past century, making every instance of on-air broadcasting in Kiksht an act of cultural preservation as well as communication. KWSO's inclusion of Kiksht content serves the Wasco community specifically -- it is their language, and its presence on the shared airwaves of the Confederated Tribes affirms the Wasco people's distinct identity within the confederation [5].

> **UNDRIP NOTE:** UNDRIP Article 13 affirms that "Indigenous peoples have the right to revitalize, use, develop and transmit to future generations their histories, languages, oral traditions, philosophies, writing systems and literatures." Kiksht broadcasting at KWSO is a direct exercise of this right by the Wasco people through the confederated media infrastructure.

## 5. Northern Paiute: Numu at Warm Springs

Northern Paiute (Numu) is the language of the Paiute people at Warm Springs. It belongs to the Numic branch of the Uto-Aztecan language family -- a completely different language family from both Sahaptin and Chinookan. The Northern Paiute language is spoken across a wide area of the Great Basin and eastern Oregon, but the Warm Springs Paiute community represents a distinct population with its own history of incorporation into the Confederated Tribes [6].

The Uto-Aztecan language family extends from the Great Basin through Mexico and Central America. Northern Paiute's presence at Warm Springs reflects the historical migrations and treaty processes that brought the Paiute people into the confederation alongside the Warm Springs and Wasco peoples. Its inclusion in KWSO's programming serves the smallest of the three constituent populations and represents a commitment to linguistic equity within the confederation [6].

### Linguistic Distance

Northern Paiute (Numu) is as linguistically distant from Ichishkin Sinwit as English is from Chinese. They share no common ancestor within any traceable timeframe. A Warm Springs person fluent in Ichishkin Sinwit cannot understand Numu without separate study, and vice versa. Kiksht (Chinookan) adds a third completely separate language family. This means KWSO navigates a trilingual context where the three languages are not dialects or variants but entirely independent linguistic systems [4][5][6].

For KWSO's programming staff, this creates a unique challenge: cultural and language content must be produced in or for three completely separate linguistic communities, each with its own vocabulary, grammar, oral tradition, and cultural context. No single bilingual speaker can bridge all three; the station requires separate language competence for each community it serves [1].

### The Smallest Voice Matters Most

The Paiute people at Warm Springs represent the smallest of the three constituent populations. Their language, Northern Paiute, has a broader speaker base across the Great Basin than Kiksht, but the specific Warm Springs Paiute community is small. KWSO's commitment to including Numu content ensures that the smallest voice in the confederation is not drowned out by the larger Warm Springs and Wasco populations. This is linguistic equity practiced through broadcast infrastructure -- the transmitter does not discriminate by population size [6].

## 6. KYNR: Yakama Language Sovereignty

KYNR broadcasts content addressing the importance of Yakama language preservation. The Yakama language (Ichishkin Sinwit, a dialect of Sahaptin) is the cultural foundation that KYNR's local production staff work to support and amplify [7].

Staff member Ronny Washines articulated the station's role in language with particular clarity: "We're dedicated to getting tribal news out from our perspective. We want to do some stories that tell us why, in our own words, our language is important. Because it was the creator's gift to us" [7]. This framing -- language as gift from the creator -- places language revitalization within a sacred context that distinguishes it from secular education initiatives. The radio station becomes not merely a language tool but a vehicle for honoring a spiritual obligation.

### Connection to Warm Springs Sahaptin

The Yakama dialect of Sahaptin and the Warm Springs dialect of Sahaptin (Ichishkin Sinwit) are closely related but reflect distinct community identities. Both KWSO and KYNR serve Sahaptin-speaking communities, but they serve *different* Sahaptin-speaking communities with different governance structures, different treaty histories, and different cultural contexts. The shared language family creates a natural affinity but does not merge the communities' distinct identities [4].

### Language as Creator's Gift

Washines' framing of language as "the creator's gift" is significant for understanding the stakes of language loss in this context. In many Plateau culture traditions, language is not merely a communication tool -- it is the form in which the creator addressed the people. Losing the language is not merely losing vocabulary and grammar; it is losing the ability to hear the creator's original voice. This theological dimension elevates language preservation from a cultural project to a sacred obligation, and it explains why KYNR's language programming carries weight that no external language preservation program can replicate [7].

When KYNR was robbed in 2018, the loss was not merely infrastructure. The silence meant the creator's language was not being carried on the airwaves. The healing ceremony requested when the station returned was not metaphorical -- it addressed a spiritual wound caused by the interruption of sovereign communication in the creator's language [7].

### Storytelling in Yakama Tradition

KYNR's role in Yakama language preservation extends beyond language instruction into the continuation of storytelling traditions. The Yakama people have rich oral narrative traditions -- stories of Coyote, of the creation of the Columbia River, of the origin of salmon runs, of the history of the Yakama Confederation itself. Radio provides a modern medium for these narratives, allowing elders to tell stories that reach the entire community simultaneously [7].

The narrative function of KYNR differs from KWSO's more diversified multilingual approach: KYNR serves one nation's language and one nation's stories, with the depth and intimacy that this single-nation focus enables. Where KWSO must balance three languages and three traditions, KYNR can devote its full cultural programming capacity to Yakama voices [3].

## 7. Daybreak Star Radio: Music as Cultural Expression

Daybreak Star Radio's curatorial philosophy explicitly encompasses the full breadth of Indigenous musical expression -- from traditional drums, powwow, and Native flute to hip-hop, metal, and R&B -- as a form of cultural sovereignty. This is not fusion for fusion's sake; it is an assertion that Indigenous peoples create in every genre, and that every genre is valid cultural expression [8].

Station manager Sherry Steele (Peoria Indians of Oklahoma) describes the mission: "to show the world we're not extinct, that there is Native music being made all the time" [9]. Production Manager RONN!E (Cowlitz Tribe), who identifies as Two-Spirit, notes that Daybreak Star creates space for Indigenous artists to discuss issues they would not feel comfortable raising with non-Native interviewers on mainstream platforms [8].

### Musical Range

Daybreak Star Radio features artists across the full spectrum of contemporary music:

- **Traditional and powwow:** Drum groups, traditional singing, ceremony-adjacent music
- **Hip-hop:** Litefoot (Cherokee and Chichimeca, Seattle-based), Blue Flamez (Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs)
- **Indie rock:** Katherine Paul / Black Belt Eagle Scout (Swinomish/Inupiaq, Portland)
- **Global Indigenous:** Sami Nation artists (Norway), Indigenous death metal from Ecuador
- **All genres:** R&B, rock, metal, jazz, country -- the only criterion is Indigenous authorship [8]

The 90%-plus Indigenous music mandate is not a restriction but an assertion: the station exists because mainstream radio does not make space for these artists. The mandate creates that space [8].

### Cultural Sovereignty Through Curation

The act of curation itself is an exercise of cultural sovereignty. Who decides what counts as "Indigenous music"? Daybreak Star's answer: Indigenous people do. The station's programming decisions are made by Indigenous staff (RONN!E, Cowlitz Tribe; DJ Big Rez, David Hillaire, Lummi Nation; Dominick Joseph, Tulalip Tribes) who bring their own cultural knowledge and community connections to the curatorial process [8].

### The Two-Spirit Safe Space

RONN!E, the station's production manager and a member of the Cowlitz Tribe, identifies as Two-Spirit -- a term specific to Indigenous communities that encompasses gender identities and roles outside the Western binary framework. Daybreak Star Radio provides a space where Two-Spirit and LGBTQ+ Indigenous artists can discuss their work, identity, and communities with interviewers who share their cultural context [8].

This safe-space function is not incidental to the station's mission -- it is integral. Mainstream media interviews place Indigenous artists in environments where they must simultaneously explain their cultural context, defend their identity, and promote their work. Daybreak Star removes the first two burdens, allowing artists to simply be themselves and talk about what they create [9].

### Music as Living Language

While Daybreak Star Radio does not broadcast in specific Indigenous languages the way KWSO broadcasts in Ichishkin Sinwit, the station asserts that music itself is a form of cultural language. Indigenous hip-hop, rock, metal, and R&B are not genres borrowed from non-Indigenous culture -- they are genres that Indigenous artists have made their own, expressing their specific experiences, histories, and visions. The station's 90%+ Indigenous music mandate creates a sonic environment in which Indigenous cultural expression is the default, not the exception [8].

Artists like Black Belt Eagle Scout (Katherine Paul, Swinomish/Inupiaq) blend indie rock with themes drawn from Indigenous experience on the Pacific Northwest coast. Litefoot (Cherokee and Chichimeca) has been producing Indigenous hip-hop since the 1990s. Blue Flamez (Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs) connects hip-hop to Warm Springs identity. Each of these artists carries cultural information in their music -- place names, stories, attitudes, perspectives -- that constitutes a form of cultural transmission even when the lyrics are in English [8].

## 8. Oral Tradition and On-Air Storytelling

Tribal radio adapts oral tradition to the broadcast medium. The stories, announcements, birthday greetings, and obituaries that fill the programming schedule of KWSO and KYNR are the contemporary expression of oral culture -- information shared through voice, in community, in real time [10].

The format maps directly: where oral tradition relied on the physical presence of speaker and listener, radio extends that presence across the coverage area. The intimacy of voice remains. The trust relationship between speaker and listener remains. The ephemeral nature of spoken word is preserved (though recording changes this). The community function -- maintaining shared knowledge, marking life events, transmitting values -- is identical [10].

KWSO's function as an informal local search engine (community members calling the station to ask questions) is itself an oral tradition pattern: you go to the knowledgeable person and ask. The station staff are the knowledgeable people. The microphone extends their reach. The format is ancient; the medium is modern [2].

### The Birthday Announcement as Oral Culture

The practice of reading birthday announcements on tribal radio may seem trivial in a media analysis context, but it is a continuation of the oral tradition of public recognition within community. In oral cultures, a person's milestones are acknowledged publicly, in the presence of the community. Radio transforms this acknowledgment: the announcement goes out to the entire coverage area simultaneously, carrying the person's name, their family connections, and the community's recognition of their continued presence. Obituaries serve the same function in reverse -- the community is informed of a loss, and the public acknowledgment serves as collective mourning [2].

These seemingly small programming elements are the connective tissue of community. They are what make the station a cultural organ rather than an information utility. A website could list birthdays. A social media post could announce deaths. But the voice of a known community member reading the name on the radio carries weight that text on a screen does not -- because it is the contemporary form of the village crier, the campfire storyteller, the public voice of the community [2].

### Treaty Education as Oral Transmission

KWSO's 1994 broadcast "Treaties and Traditions" represents the use of radio for intergenerational transmission of treaty knowledge [5]. Understanding the 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon is essential for every member of the Confederated Tribes -- it defines their land, their rights, their relationship to the federal government, and the legal foundation of their sovereignty. Transmitting this knowledge through radio reaches community members who may not attend formal educational settings, read treaty documents, or engage with the tribal government directly.

The radio broadcast makes treaty education ambient -- it enters homes, vehicles, and workplaces as part of the daily sonic environment. A listener who would never pick up a legal document may absorb treaty knowledge through repeated on-air discussion. This ambient education function is one of radio's unique strengths for cultural transmission [5].

### The Voice as Cultural Marker

In oral traditions, the voice itself carries cultural information: accent, cadence, emotional register, the rhythmic patterns specific to a language and community. When KWSO staff speak on air in Ichishkin Sinwit, the sound of the language -- its phonetic texture, its prosody, its emotional coloring -- transmits cultural information beyond the semantic content of the words. Listeners hear not just what is said but how it sounds, and that sound is part of the cultural heritage being preserved [1].

This is why language on radio is different from language in text. A dictionary preserves vocabulary and grammar. A recording preserves a speaker's voice and delivery. Radio broadcasting preserves the living practice of using the language in real communicative contexts -- news, weather, community information, conversation. The language is alive on the radio in a way that no archive, however complete, can replicate [1].

## 9. American Archive of Public Broadcasting Holdings

The American Archive of Public Broadcasting (AAPB) holds historical KWSO programs including:

- **"Salmon in the Native American Culture"** (1994) -- documenting the relationship between salmon runs and cultural practices of the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs
- **"Treaties and Traditions"** (1994) -- examining the 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon and its ongoing significance [11]

These holdings demonstrate that KWSO has been producing cultural documentation programming for at least three decades. The AAPB archive serves as an institutional backup for content that might otherwise exist only in station files, providing some protection against the kind of catastrophic loss that KYNR experienced in 2018 [11].

### Significance of the 1994 Recordings

The 1994 recordings are significant for several reasons:

- **Age:** They are now over 30 years old, potentially capturing voices of speakers who may no longer be living. Each passing year makes historical recordings more valuable as primary linguistic evidence.
- **Context:** "Salmon in the Native American Culture" documents the relationship between salmon runs and cultural practices -- ecological knowledge embedded in cultural programming that links language, food systems, treaty rights, and seasonal cycles.
- **Treaty documentation:** "Treaties and Traditions" provides an on-air explanation of the 1855 Treaty, creating an accessible record of treaty education that predates the internet era.
- **Production quality:** As professionally broadcast content, these recordings have higher audio quality than many field recordings, making them potentially more useful for language education and archival purposes [11].

### The Archive Gap: 1986-Present

KWSO has been broadcasting since 1986. The AAPB holds programs from 1994. What about the other 30+ years of broadcasts? The archive gap represents an enormous loss of potentially irreplaceable cultural content. Every day from 1986 to the present that was not recorded and archived is a day of language, storytelling, community news, and cultural programming that exists only in the memories of those who heard it [11].

> **OCAP NOTE:** The existence of these recordings in the AAPB raises questions about data sovereignty. The AAPB is a national archive; the content was produced by the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs through their station. The question of who controls access to archived tribal broadcasting content is a live issue in Indigenous data governance. Under OCAP principles, the Confederated Tribes should maintain control over how their cultural content is accessed and used, regardless of where copies are physically stored.

## 10. Archiving Capacity and Curriculum Integration

Stations like KWSO serve communities with three distinct living languages but operate with minimal staff (six total employees, two CPB-funded) and lack resources for systematic archiving and curriculum integration [2]. The gap between what these stations produce daily and what is systematically preserved represents an ongoing loss of irreplaceable cultural documentation.

### The Archiving Gap

Every day, KWSO broadcasts language content, cultural programming, community news, and oral tradition in three languages. Much of this content is ephemeral by default -- it goes out on the air and is gone. Without systematic recording, cataloging, and preservation, decades of on-air language use could be lost. The 2018 KYNR burglary demonstrated how physically fragile this infrastructure is; the archiving gap demonstrates how fragile it is institutionally [7].

### Curriculum Potential

On-air language programming represents a potential curriculum resource that is largely untapped. Recordings of elders speaking Ichishkin Sinwit, Kiksht, or Northern Paiute on KWSO could be integrated into tribal education programs, creating a bridge between broadcast media and formal language instruction. This integration requires resources that stations currently lack: archival equipment, cataloging staff, and curriculum development partnerships [1].

### The KWSO-Classroom Pipeline

A potential workflow for integrating broadcast content into language education:

```
BROADCAST-TO-CLASSROOM PIPELINE (theoretical)
================================================================

  1. BROADCAST: Elder speaks Ichishkin Sinwit on KWSO
       |
  2. RECORD: Station archives the broadcast segment
       |
  3. CATALOG: Segment tagged by language, speaker, topic, date
       |
  4. CURATE: Education staff selects segments for curriculum
       |
  5. PACKAGE: Segments prepared with context for classroom use
       |
  6. DELIVER: Made available to tribal education programs
       |
  7. ASSESS: Students engage with authentic language material
       |
  8. FEEDBACK: Education program reports back to station on
     which content types are most effective

  REQUIREMENT: Entire pipeline must be governed by tribal
  authority. Steps 1-8 all under OCAP control.
  CURRENT BARRIER: Steps 2-8 require staff and equipment
  that 6-person station does not have.
```

This pipeline does not exist at KWSO because the station lacks the resources to implement it. Two of six staff positions are CPB-funded; there is no dedicated archivist, no curriculum developer, no educational technology specialist. The broadcast content -- the most valuable step in the pipeline -- happens daily and disappears into the air [2].

> **CARE NOTE:** The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics) apply directly to any archiving or curriculum development work. The data belongs to the nations. Any external system that stores, indexes, or distributes tribal language content must operate under the authority and direction of the tribal government, not independently.

## 11. Radio as Language Revitalization Infrastructure

### The Scalability Advantage

Radio broadcasting offers a unique scalability advantage for language revitalization that other methods cannot match. A single elder speaking Ichishkin Sinwit on KWSO reaches the entire 50,000-person coverage area simultaneously. By contrast, classroom instruction reaches one class at a time. Immersion programs serve limited enrollment. One-on-one mentoring, while invaluable, serves one learner at a time. Radio multiplies the reach of every speaker who approaches the microphone [1].

### Passive vs. Active Exposure

Language acquisition research consistently demonstrates that passive exposure -- hearing a language used naturally in context -- supports acquisition differently from active instruction. Radio provides extensive passive exposure: listeners hear their language during commutes, meal preparation, work, and daily routines. This creates an ambient language environment that reinforces active learning without requiring dedicated study time [4].

For critically endangered languages like Kiksht, where fluent speakers number in the single digits or low double digits, passive radio exposure may be one of the last opportunities for community-wide language contact. Each broadcast creates a language event that cannot be replicated after the last fluent speakers are gone [5].

### The Normalization Function

Language revitalization requires more than teaching -- it requires normalization. When Ichishkin Sinwit is heard on the radio alongside English-language news, weather, and music, it occupies the same communicative space as the dominant language. This normalization sends a powerful message: this language is alive, this language is current, this language belongs in modern communication. Radio achieves normalization at community scale [1].

### Intergenerational Bridge

Radio creates an intergenerational bridge that is difficult to achieve through other means. When an elder speaks on KWSO, a grandchild in a car, a teenager at home, a young adult at work, and a middle-aged parent in a kitchen all hear the same words at the same time. The broadcast creates a shared linguistic moment across generations -- the same function that evening storytelling served before radio, but at the scale of the entire reservation [2].

### The Documentation Paradox

On-air language use creates a documentation paradox: broadcasting is inherently public, but language documentation raises sovereignty concerns. A broadcast in Kiksht reaches anyone within KWSO's coverage area, but a recording of that broadcast -- if stored, copied, or redistributed without authorization -- may violate the Wasco people's data sovereignty over their language. The paradox: the most effective revitalization tool (public broadcasting) creates the most accessible documentation (receivable by anyone with a radio), which must still be governed by OCAP principles when captured in permanent form [8].

## 12. Language Endangerment Context

### Pacific Northwest Language Diversity

The Pacific Northwest is one of the most linguistically diverse regions in North America. Prior to European contact, the region hosted dozens of distinct language families -- more linguistic diversity per square mile than most of the continent. The three languages at KWSO (Sahaptian, Chinookan, Uto-Aztecan) represent three entirely separate language families with no known genetic relationship to each other [4][5][6].

### Endangerment Status

All three languages served by KWSO face varying degrees of endangerment:

| Language | Family | Approximate Status | Key Challenge |
|----------|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Ichishkin Sinwit (Warm Springs dialect) | Sahaptian | Severely endangered | Declining intergenerational transmission |
| Ichishkin Sinwit (Yakama dialect) | Sahaptian | Severely endangered | Similar challenges; KYNR provides one broadcast channel |
| Kiksht (Wasco-Wishram) | Chinookan | Critically endangered | Very few fluent speakers remain |
| Northern Paiute (Numu) | Uto-Aztecan (Numic) | Severely endangered | Small speaker population at Warm Springs |

The endangerment of these languages is not a natural process. It is the result of specific historical policies: boarding schools that punished children for speaking their languages, English-only policies in federal institutions, and the structural marginalization of Indigenous languages in education, media, and governance. Radio broadcasting is one tool for reversing this history -- not by recreating the past, but by carrying the languages into the future through modern communication infrastructure [4].

### The Time Dimension

Language revitalization operates against a biological clock: fluent elder speakers age and pass away. Each year without systematic recording, broadcasting, and transmission reduces the available linguistic knowledge base. KWSO's on-air language programming is not merely cultural programming -- it is an urgent race against time, conducted with six staff members and a 4,300-watt transmitter [2].

## 13. OCAP Principles Applied to Language Broadcasting

The First Nations Information Governance Centre's OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) provide a framework for understanding the data sovereignty dimensions of tribal language broadcasting [12]:

- **Ownership:** The languages broadcast on KWSO and KYNR are owned by their respective nations. Ichishkin Sinwit belongs to the Warm Springs and Yakama peoples. Kiksht belongs to the Wasco people. Northern Paiute belongs to the Paiute people. The broadcast does not transfer ownership.
- **Control:** Tribal stations exercise control over how their languages are used in broadcast. Programming decisions about language content are made by tribal staff, not by external funders or network syndication providers.
- **Access:** The broadcast makes language accessible to the coverage area, but this is access granted by the station (and by extension, the tribal government that operates it). Recording and redistribution of language content requires tribal authorization.
- **Possession:** Physical possession of recordings, archives, and broadcast content should remain with the tribal station and its governing body. External archives (like AAPB) hold copies, but the originals and the authority over them belong to the nation.

The UNDRIP reinforces these principles. Article 31 affirms that "Indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge and traditional cultural expressions" [13]. Language broadcasting is a direct exercise of this right. The CPB funding crisis (2025) represents a threat to this right: when external funding withdrawal forces staff cuts, language programming capacity diminishes, and the nation's ability to exercise its Article 31 rights through broadcasting is materially harmed.
