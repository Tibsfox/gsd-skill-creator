## 1. Sovereignty and Broadcasting Rights

The Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs entered into a Treaty with the United States in 1855 (Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon), which defines reservation boundaries and ensures access to traditional hunting, fishing, and gathering areas [1]. The Tribal Constitution and By-Laws were adopted in 1938. KWSO operates as a department of the tribal government's Health and Human Services branch; the Tribal Council acts as the station's Board of Directors [2].

This governance structure means KWSO is not merely a licensed broadcaster -- it is a function of sovereign governance, embedded in the same branch that handles health, social services, and community welfare. The station's existence is an act of sovereign communication capacity, not a commercial or nonprofit enterprise that happens to serve a tribal community [2].

> **SOVEREIGNTY NOTE:** The distinction between "community radio that serves a tribal community" and "sovereign media infrastructure operated by a tribal government" is not semantic. It determines legal standing, funding eligibility, regulatory treatment, and the fundamental relationship between the station and the FCC. KWSO's license is held by the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs Reservation -- the sovereign entity itself.

### Sovereign Communication as Treaty Right

While no specific treaty article explicitly mentions radio broadcasting (the technology did not exist in 1855), the broader principle of sovereign self-governance -- the right of a nation to govern its own affairs, including communication within and to its community -- is foundational to the treaty relationship. Broadcasting is the modern expression of the sovereign communication capacity that treaties recognize [1].

## 2. UNDRIP Article 31 Framework

The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), adopted in 2007, provides the international human rights framework within which tribal broadcasting operates. Article 31 is the key provision [3]:

> *"Indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge and traditional cultural expressions, as well as the manifestations of their sciences, technologies and cultures, including human and genetic resources, seeds, medicines, knowledge of the properties of fauna and flora, oral traditions, literatures, designs, sports and traditional games and visual and performing arts. They also have the right to maintain, control, protect and develop their intellectual property over such cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions."*

### Application to Tribal Broadcasting

| UNDRIP Right | Broadcasting Expression | PNW Station Example |
|-------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Maintain cultural heritage | On-air language programming | KWSO: 3 languages (Ichishkin Sinwit, Kiksht, Northern Paiute) |
| Control traditional knowledge | Station-produced cultural content | KYNR: "our perspective... in our own words" (Ronny Washines) |
| Protect cultural expressions | Safe space for Indigenous artists | Daybreak Star: Two-Spirit representation (RONN!E, Cowlitz Tribe) |
| Develop oral traditions | Radio as modern oral medium | KWSO: community Q&A, birthday/obituary on-air traditions |
| Intellectual property | Music library and cultural archive | KYNR: largest Native music library in NW (Roy Dick) |

### UNDRIP Article 13: Language Rights

Article 13 reinforces Article 31 specifically for language: "Indigenous peoples have the right to revitalize, use, develop and transmit to future generations their histories, languages, oral traditions, philosophies, writing systems and literatures" [3]. Every on-air use of Ichishkin Sinwit, Kiksht, or Northern Paiute at KWSO is a direct exercise of this right.

### The CPB Funding Crisis as UNDRIP Article 31 Threat

When external funding withdrawal (CPB rescission) forces station staff cuts that reduce language programming capacity, it constitutes a structural threat to the rights articulated in UNDRIP Articles 13 and 31. The loss is not merely operational -- it is the diminishment of a sovereign nation's capacity to exercise its right to maintain and develop its cultural heritage through media [4].

> **CAUTION:** This analysis applies UNDRIP as an analytical framework, not as a policy advocacy position. UNDRIP was adopted by the UN General Assembly and has been endorsed by the United States (2010). Its provisions are not legally binding in U.S. domestic law but provide the international human rights standard against which domestic policy can be evaluated.

## 3. The FCC Licensing Paradox

The FCC licensing framework was not designed with tribal sovereignty in mind. Tribal stations exist in two configurations, creating a paradox that affects funding access and regulatory treatment [4]:

### Configuration 1: Tribe-Licensed

KWSO is licensed to the "Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs Reservation" -- the sovereign tribal government itself. This means:
- The tribal government is the licensee
- The Tribal Council serves as the Board of Directors
- The station operates as a government department
- Regulatory obligations (FCC compliance) fall on the tribal government
- Funding eligibility may differ from nonprofit-licensed stations [2]

### Configuration 2: Nonprofit-Licensed

Some tribal stations are licensed to nonprofit organizations rather than to the tribal government directly. This creates:
- A separate legal entity between the tribal government and the FCC
- Different funding eligibility criteria for grants and government programs
- Potential misalignment between nonprofit governance and tribal sovereignty
- Greater access to some grant programs designed for 501(c)(3) organizations [4]

### The Definitional Problem

Sue Matters (KWSO) identified this as a structural challenge: "Some tribal stations are licensed by their tribe. Some tribal stations are nonprofits. And so what's the definition of who that is?" [4]. This question becomes critical when emergency funding proposals attempt to target "tribal stations" -- which ones qualify? The licensing configuration, not the community served, often determines eligibility.

```
FCC LICENSING PARADOX
================================================================

  TRIBE-LICENSED                    NONPROFIT-LICENSED
  +--------------------------+     +--------------------------+
  | Sovereign tribal         |     | 501(c)(3) nonprofit      |
  | government is licensee   |     | entity is licensee       |
  | (e.g., KWSO)             |     | (e.g., some other        |
  |                          |     | tribal stations)         |
  | Tribal Council = Board   |     | Nonprofit Board = Board  |
  | Station = govt. dept.    |     | Station = org. program   |
  +-----------+--------------+     +-----------+--------------+
              |                                |
              v                                v
  +-----------+--------------+     +-----------+--------------+
  | FCC compliance: tribal   |     | FCC compliance: nonprofit|
  | government responsibility|     | board responsibility     |
  | CPB eligibility: govt.   |     | CPB eligibility: 501c3   |
  | Grant access: limited    |     | Grant access: broader    |
  | Sovereignty: direct      |     | Sovereignty: mediated    |
  +--------------------------+     +--------------------------+

  PARADOX: The station serving the same community may have
  different funding access depending on its licensing structure.
  Emergency proposals struggle to define "tribal station" in a
  way that covers both configurations equally.
```

### Small Station Capacity

The paradox is compounded by the fact that small stations lack the administrative capacity to navigate complex regulatory and funding systems. KWSO has 6 total staff. None are dedicated grant writers, regulatory compliance officers, or government relations specialists. The complexity of the licensing landscape falls on people whose primary job is running a radio station for their community [4].

## 4. Treaty Rights and Broadcasting Context

### The 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon

The Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs operate under the 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon, which:
- Defines reservation boundaries (640,000 acres in central Oregon)
- Ensures access to traditional hunting, fishing, and gathering areas (off-reservation rights)
- Establishes the government-to-government relationship between the Confederated Tribes and the United States
- Predates the FCC, the Communications Act of 1934, and all federal broadcast regulation [1]

KWSO's cultural programming includes content specifically addressing this treaty and its ongoing significance. The station broadcast "Treaties and Traditions" in 1994, documented in the American Archive of Public Broadcasting [5].

### The Yakama Treaty of 1855

The Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Yakama Nation operate under a separate 1855 treaty (Treaty with the Yakamas) that similarly defines reservation boundaries and ensures off-reservation resource access rights. KYNR's broadcasting serves a nation with its own distinct treaty relationship to the United States [6].

### Treaty Rights and Communication Sovereignty

Neither treaty explicitly mentions broadcasting. But both treaties establish the sovereignty of the tribal nations -- the right to govern their own affairs, maintain their cultures, and communicate within their communities. Broadcasting is the modern infrastructure through which these sovereign communications occur. A threat to broadcasting infrastructure is therefore a threat to the exercise of sovereign communication rights that treaties recognize [1].

## 5. Tribal Council Governance Models

### KWSO: Government Department Model

- **Governing body:** Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs Tribal Council
- **Station relationship:** KWSO operates as a department of Health & Human Services
- **Accountability:** Station Manager reports to HHS, which reports to Tribal Council
- **Budget source:** Tribal government allocation + CPB Community Service Grant
- **Policy authority:** Tribal Council sets policy direction; day-to-day operations managed by station staff [2]

### KYNR: Multimedia Services Model

- **Governing body:** Yakama Tribal Council
- **Station relationship:** KYNR operates under Yakama Nation Multimedia Services Program
- **Media integration:** Radio station paired with Yakama Nation Review newspaper (est. 1970 by Tribal Council Resolution)
- **Budget source:** Tribal government allocation (demonstrated by $34,000 recovery funding in 2018)
- **Policy authority:** Tribal Council oversight through Multimedia Services [6]

### Daybreak Star Radio: Foundation Model

- **Governing body:** United Indians of All Tribes Foundation (UIATF) Board
- **Station relationship:** Radio is one program of the UIATF
- **Facility:** Daybreak Star Indian Cultural Center, Discovery Park, Seattle
- **Budget source:** Foundation grants, donations
- **Policy authority:** UIATF Board; not a tribal government function [7]

The governance model determines more than administrative structure. It determines the station's relationship to sovereignty. KWSO and KYNR are expressions of sovereign governance. Daybreak Star Radio is an expression of organized pan-Indigenous cultural initiative. Both are valuable; they are structurally different [2][7].

## 6. OCAP Principles Applied to Broadcasting

The First Nations Information Governance Centre's OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) provide the framework for understanding data sovereignty in the context of tribal broadcasting [8]:

### Ownership

The cultural content produced by tribal radio stations -- language programming, cultural education, oral tradition, community news -- is owned by the communities that produce it. The broadcast medium (radio waves) makes the content publicly accessible within the coverage area, but this accessibility does not transfer ownership. A recording of Ichishkin Sinwit broadcast on KWSO is still owned by the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs, not by anyone who happens to receive the signal [8].

### Control

Tribal stations exercise control through governance: the Tribal Council (for tribe-licensed stations) or the foundation board (for nonprofit stations) determines programming policy. This control extends to what languages are broadcast, what cultural content is shared, what boundaries exist between public and sacred knowledge, and who has authority to represent the community's voice on air [8].

### Access

Radio broadcast inherently makes content accessible to the coverage area. This is a deliberate choice: the station broadcasts because it wants the community to hear. But access to recordings, archives, and metadata should be governed by the station and its governing body. External archives (like the AAPB) hold copies of KWSO material; access to those copies should be mediated by the Confederated Tribes [8].

### Possession

Physical and digital possession of broadcast content, archives, music libraries, and station documentation should remain with the tribal station and its sovereign governing body. The KYNR burglary demonstrated how physical possession can be violently disrupted; the digital equivalent would be unauthorized copying, redistribution, or commercialization of tribal broadcast content [8].

## 7. CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance

The CARE Principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics), developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance, complement OCAP with a focus on benefit and ethical obligation [9]:

- **Collective Benefit:** Broadcasting data (content, archives, audience information) should benefit the Indigenous communities that produce it. Revenue from any commercialization should flow to the community.
- **Authority to Control:** Nations have the authority to control how their broadcast content is collected, used, shared, and stored. This includes the right to restrict access to culturally sensitive material.
- **Responsibility:** Those who handle tribal broadcast data have a responsibility to support Indigenous peoples' rights and interests in that data.
- **Ethics:** Ethical use of tribal broadcast data requires understanding the cultural context and obtaining appropriate permissions from the governing body, not merely from individual staff members.

---

## 8. FAIR Data in Indigenous Context

The FAIR data principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) are standard in open data practice, but their application to tribal broadcast data requires qualification [9]:

- **Findable:** Tribal broadcast archives should be findable *by the community that produced them*. Whether they should be findable by external researchers is a decision for the tribal government.
- **Accessible:** Access should be governed by OCAP principles, not by default openness. Some content is appropriate for public access; some is not.
- **Interoperable:** Technical interoperability should not override sovereignty. A tribal archive can use standard formats without granting open access.
- **Reusable:** Reuse of tribal broadcast content requires permission from the governing body. The default is not "open for reuse" but "governed by the nation."

CARE and FAIR are not contradictory but represent different priority orderings: FAIR prioritizes data utility; CARE prioritizes Indigenous rights and benefit. In tribal broadcasting, CARE takes precedence [9].

## 9. The Digital Expansion Question

Daybreak Star Radio represents a digital-native Indigenous broadcasting model: online streaming, app distribution, pan-Indigenous scope, and an explicit mission to create international reach. This model is complementary to, not competitive with, the terrestrial reservation model [7].

### Digital Opportunities

- **Global reach:** Online streaming removes geographic boundaries, allowing diaspora communities and international audiences to access Indigenous content
- **Archival potential:** Digital content is inherently easier to archive, catalog, and preserve than analog broadcasts
- **Youth engagement:** Younger generations are digital-native; app-based and streaming delivery meets them where they are
- **Multimedia expansion:** Video, social media, podcasting extend the radio station's presence across platforms

### Digital Risks

- **Digital divide dependency:** Moving to digital-first risks abandoning terrestrial listeners who have no broadband or reliable cell service
- **Platform dependency:** Streaming via Spotify, Apple Music, or other platforms places Indigenous content within corporate-controlled ecosystems
- **Data sovereignty:** Digital platforms collect listener data; who controls that data under OCAP principles?
- **Funding model shift:** Digital advertising revenue models may not generate sufficient income for small Indigenous stations
- **Cultural safety:** Online platforms are not controlled spaces; comments, algorithm curation, and adjacent content may create hostile environments

### The Complementary Path

The future of PNW Indigenous broadcasting likely requires both models operating simultaneously:

```
DIGITAL EXPANSION -- COMPLEMENTARY MODEL
================================================================

  TERRESTRIAL (must maintain)        DIGITAL (must develop)
  +--------------------------+      +--------------------------+
  | FM/AM broadcast          |      | Online streaming         |
  | EAS function             |      | App distribution         |
  | Language programming     |      | Podcast/on-demand        |
  | Local news/calendar      |      | Social media presence    |
  | Reservation coverage     |      | International reach      |
  | No-internet dependency   |      | Youth engagement         |
  | Emergency reliability    |      | Archival/searchable      |
  +-----------+--------------+      +-----------+--------------+
              |                                 |
              +----------------+----------------+
                               |
                     +---------+---------+
                     | SHARED GOVERNANCE |
                     | Tribal Council or |
                     | Foundation Board  |
                     | OCAP principles   |
                     | CARE compliance   |
                     +-------------------+
```

Maintaining terrestrial infrastructure while developing digital capacity requires resources that the CPB funding crisis directly threatens. The expansion question cannot be answered until the survival question is resolved [4].

## 11. The Sovereign Signal Concept

### What Makes a Signal Sovereign

A sovereign signal is not merely a radio broadcast produced by Indigenous people. It is a communication act performed by a sovereign nation through infrastructure it owns, governs, and controls. The distinction is definitional:

- **A sovereign signal** is broadcast by a station licensed to and operated by a tribal government (e.g., KWSO, licensed to the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs Reservation)
- **A community signal** is broadcast by a station that serves an Indigenous community but is operated by a nonprofit or other non-governmental entity (e.g., Daybreak Star Radio, operated by the UIATF)
- **A mainstream signal** carries Indigenous content but is controlled by non-Indigenous entities (e.g., NPR affiliates carrying National Native News)

All three have value. Only the first is sovereign in the full sense: the signal originates from, is controlled by, and serves the interests of a federally recognized sovereign nation [2].

### Sovereign Spectrum Access

The electromagnetic spectrum is a public resource allocated by the FCC. When the FCC issues a broadcast license to a tribal government, it is recognizing that government's right to access the public spectrum for the purpose of communicating with its citizens. This is analogous to (though legally distinct from) the right of a sovereign nation to operate postal services, publish official communications, and maintain public information systems [4].

The spectrum access question becomes critical when funding cuts threaten a station's ability to maintain its license. FCC licenses require ongoing operation and compliance; a station that goes off-air for extended periods may face license forfeiture. The 11-month KYNR silence was close to the threshold at which license questions could arise. Protecting the license means protecting sovereign spectrum access [10].

### The Spectrum as Treaty Resource

An argument can be made -- though it has not been litigated -- that the electromagnetic spectrum over treaty lands is a resource analogous to the fish, game, and timber resources that treaties explicitly protect. Just as the 1855 Treaty with the Tribes of Middle Oregon ensures the Confederated Tribes' access to salmon runs, a sovereign interpretation of communication rights would suggest that access to the radio spectrum for the purpose of sovereign communication is a treaty-implicit right [1].

This argument is strengthened by UNDRIP Article 31, which extends the right to "maintain, control, protect and develop" cultural heritage to include "technologies and cultures." Broadcasting technology is a culture-maintenance technology; the right to use it is, under UNDRIP, a fundamental Indigenous right [3].

## 12. Integration: Infrastructure, Language, and Sovereignty

This module draws together the threads from all four preceding modules:

### The Cascade: Funding to Language to Rights

CPB funding cuts (Module 3) threaten staff positions. Staff cuts reduce language programming capacity (Module 2). Reduced language programming diminishes the nation's exercise of UNDRIP Article 31 rights (this module). The cascade is direct and measurable:

1. **$X in CPB funding lost** -> 2 staff positions cut at KWSO
2. **2 staff positions cut** -> reduced on-air language programming in Ichishkin Sinwit, Kiksht, Northern Paiute
3. **Reduced language programming** -> diminished exercise of UNDRIP Article 31 right to maintain and develop cultural heritage
4. **Diminished cultural heritage capacity** -> structural harm to sovereignty that no subsequent funding can fully repair (language speakers age, recordings not made, knowledge not transmitted)

### The Infrastructure-Sovereignty Link

The KYNR burglary (Module 3) demonstrated that physical infrastructure and sovereign communication capacity are inseparable. When the control board was stolen, the Yakama Nation's radio voice was silenced -- not by policy, not by regulation, but by the physical absence of the equipment that carries the sovereign signal. Infrastructure is not separate from sovereignty; it is the physical embodiment of it [10].

### The Digital Divide as Sovereignty Constraint

The digital divide (Module 3) constrains digital expansion (this module). Nations cannot exercise their communication rights through digital media if their communities lack broadband. Terrestrial radio is the sovereignty-compatible medium because it works independently of corporate infrastructure, internet service providers, and platform terms of service. A 4,300-watt FM transmitter on Eagle Butte answers to the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs, not to a streaming platform's content policy [4].
