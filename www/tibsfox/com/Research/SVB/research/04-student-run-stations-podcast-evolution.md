# Student-Run Stations & Podcast Evolution

> **Domain:** Educational Broadcasting
> **Module:** 4 -- The Landscape of Student-Operated Media and the Platform Shift
> **Through-line:** *For a century, student broadcasting required a transmitter: a physical device that converted audio into electromagnetic waves and sent them into the air, subject to federal regulation, institutional oversight, and the laws of physics.* Podcasting made the transmitter optional. What was gained -- access, democratization, creative freedom -- and what was lost -- the regulatory framework that taught discipline, the shared audience that created community, the physical signal that connected broadcaster to geography -- is the central tension of this module.

## 1. The National Landscape of Student Stations

The number of active high school radio stations in the United States is difficult to determine precisely because there is no comprehensive registry. The FCC license database tracks NCE stations by licensee type, but the distinction between "high school station" and "school-district station operated by university students" is not reliably encoded. Estimates from the National Federation of Community Broadcasters and from media education surveys suggest approximately 50-100 active high school radio stations in the United States as of 2024, down from a peak of approximately 200-300 in the 1990s [1].

### Geographic Distribution

High school radio stations cluster in regions with strong educational broadcasting traditions:

| Region | Notable Stations | Context |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Pacific Northwest | KNHC (Seattle), KBPS (Portland) | Strong NCE tradition; university station ecosystem |
| Upper Midwest | Multiple small stations | Land-grant university influence; educational radio culture |
| Northeast Corridor | WHHS (Havertown, PA), others | Early broadcasting history; dense population supports small-station viability |
| California | KECG (El Cerrito), others | Large school districts; media production curricula |
| Indiana | WNAS (New Albany) | Historical; oldest HS FM station |

The decline from peak to current levels is attributable to several factors: the Class D elimination (see Module 3), school district budget pressure, the retirement of founding faculty advisors without replacement, and the perception that podcasting and social media have made broadcast radio obsolete as an educational tool [2].

### What Survival Requires

Stations that have survived the 50-year mark share common characteristics:

- **Curricular integration:** The station is part of a graded academic program, not an extracurricular club
- **Faculty champion continuity:** At least one faculty member or professional staff person has advocated for the station across multiple administrative cycles
- **Power sufficient for relevance:** Stations with enough power to reach beyond the school grounds maintain audience and community identity
- **Institutional ownership:** The school district (not an individual teacher) holds the license and treats the station as institutional infrastructure
- **Community identity:** The station has a recognized brand in the local media landscape

KNHC, KBPS, and KECG all satisfy these criteria. Stations that have gone silent typically failed on one or more -- most commonly, the departure of a faculty champion without a successor [3].

## 2. KBPS Portland: The Centenarian

KBPS at Benson Polytechnic High School in Portland, Oregon, is the oldest continuously operating high school radio station in the United States. Licensed in 1923 on AM 1450, KBPS has broadcast continuously for over 100 years under the governance of Portland Public Schools [4].

### KBPS Timeline

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1923 | Licensed on AM 1450 | First HS radio station; Benson Polytechnic electronics program |
| 1926 | Early programming standardized | Educational and vocational content |
| 1940s | WWII service | Station used for civil defense programming |
| 1950s | Television era | AM radio declining nationally; KBPS continues as educational resource |
| 1970s | FM expansion nationally | KBPS remains AM-only; lower profile but continuous operation |
| 2000s | Digital transition | Streaming added; maintaining AM broadcast |
| 2023 | Centennial celebration | 100 years of continuous student broadcasting |

### The Portland-Seattle Axis

KBPS and KNHC represent the two endpoints of the high school radio spectrum: KBPS is a low-power AM station with a century of history and a modest audience; KNHC is a high-power FM station with five decades of history and a metropolitan audience. Together, they bracket the range of the American high school radio experience.

The Portland-Seattle axis is also notable for the broader PNW educational broadcasting ecosystem. Portland's KBOO (community radio, founded 1968) and Seattle's KEXP (evolved from student radio at UW) create a regional context in which non-commercial, community-oriented broadcasting is culturally normalized. Students at KBPS and KNHC grow up in a media environment where non-commercial radio is visible and valued, unlike students in markets where only commercial stations exist [5].

### KBPS and the Vocational Model

KBPS's embedding within Benson Polytechnic's vocational curriculum provides institutional durability that extracurricular stations cannot match. Radio operation at Benson is a trade -- like welding, machining, or electrical work. The vocational framing protects the station from budget cuts that target non-essential programs because the station is part of an accredited vocational program. This is the same protective mechanism that saved KNHC during the 1981 budget crisis, when the station's connection to the Mass Communications magnet program provided institutional justification for continued operation [6].

## 3. WNAS and the Early FM Pioneers

WNAS at New Albany High School in New Albany, Indiana, is the oldest high school FM station in the United States, licensed in 1949 [7]. WNAS represents the first generation of FM educational stations -- stations licensed in the years immediately following the FCC's 1945 NCE spectrum reservation.

### The 1949 Generation

The stations licensed between 1945 and 1955 constitute the "first FM generation" of high school broadcasting. These stations were typically established by individual teachers with electronics expertise, operating at minimal power, and serving as laboratory facilities for vocational education programs. The regulatory environment was permissive: FCC Class D applications were straightforward, equipment costs were modest, and the NCE band was largely empty [8].

WNAS's significance is not its power or audience but its continuity. The station has operated for over 75 years, through every technological transition (tubes to transistors to digital), every format era, and every administrative change in the New Albany school district. This durability is itself a form of institutional knowledge that the oral history framework should capture: what does it take for a high school radio station to survive three-quarters of a century?

### WHHS Haverford

WHHS at Haverford Senior High School in Havertown, Pennsylvania, also dates to 1949. The station operated from a converted closet for its first decades and has survived through a combination of student enthusiasm and administrative tolerance. WHHS illustrates the "benign neglect" model of station survival: the station costs the district almost nothing, bothers no one, and provides a visible extracurricular credential. In the absence of active opposition, inertia becomes the station's greatest protector [9].

## 4. KECG and the NPR Partnership Model

KECG at El Cerrito High School in El Cerrito, California, represents a newer model of high school broadcasting: the NPR partnership station. KECG operates in partnership with local NPR affiliates, providing students with access to professional production standards, national content, and institutional support networks that standalone high school stations lack [10].

### The KECG Story

KECG was featured in EdSource reporting in 2023, which documented the station's student-run programming and highlighted the station's role in media education. Reporter Jeanne Marie Acceturo described students running their own radio shows with a level of autonomy and production quality that surprised professional observers [11].

The KECG model is notable for two reasons:

1. **Institutional scaffolding:** The NPR partnership provides professional mentorship, production standards, and distribution infrastructure that a standalone high school station would need to develop independently
2. **National recognition:** KECG students have produced content that reached the New York Times' list of top 10 student podcasts, demonstrating that the NPR partnership model can produce nationally competitive student media

### The NPR Partnership Trade-Off

The NPR partnership model trades autonomy for infrastructure. Students at KECG benefit from professional mentorship and national distribution, but operate within the programming standards and editorial guidelines of the NPR system. Students at KNHC have no NPR affiliation and greater programming freedom, but must develop their own production standards and audience relationships.

This trade-off is experientially significant. A student at KECG learns to produce content within an established professional framework. A student at KNHC learns to create a framework from scratch. Both are valuable; they are different kinds of valuable [12].

## 5. The PNW Student Radio Ecosystem

The Pacific Northwest's student radio ecosystem extends beyond KNHC and KBPS to include college stations, community stations, and the LPFM stations that emerged after 2000.

### PNW Student and Educational Stations

| Station | Type | Location | Power | Status |
|---------|------|----------|-------|--------|
| KNHC/C89.5 | High School FM | Seattle, WA | 8.5 kW | Active |
| KBPS | High School AM | Portland, OR | 1 kW | Active |
| KEXP | Independent (ex-college) | Seattle, WA | 4.7 kW | Active |
| KAOS | College FM | Olympia, WA | 1.2 kW | Active |
| KWCW | College FM | Walla Walla, WA | 130 W | Active |
| KDUP | University FM | Portland, OR | 100 W | Active (U of Portland) |
| KUPS | University FM | Tacoma, WA | 400 W | Active (U of Puget Sound) |
| KUGS | University FM | Bellingham, WA | 100 W | Active (Western Washington U) |
| KSVR | Community FM | Mount Vernon, WA | 1 kW | Active (Skagit Valley College) |
| KMUN | Community FM | Astoria, OR | 10 kW | Active |

The ecosystem creates a progression path for student broadcasters: high school stations (KNHC, KBPS) feed into college stations (KAOS, KUPS, KUGS), which feed into community stations (KBOO, KSVR) or professional stations (KEXP, KUOW). This pipeline is informal but well-established in the PNW -- students who develop broadcasting skills in high school frequently continue at college stations and sometimes transition to professional broadcasting or media careers [13].

### The Bellingham-to-Eugene Corridor

The I-5 corridor from Bellingham, Washington to Eugene, Oregon contains the densest concentration of educational and community broadcasting stations in the western United States. Within this 350-mile corridor:

- **4 university/college stations** (KUGS-Bellingham, KAOS-Olympia, KUPS-Tacoma, KDUP-Portland)
- **2 high school stations** (KNHC-Seattle, KBPS-Portland)
- **5+ community stations** (KSVR-Mount Vernon, KBOO-Portland, KMUN-Astoria, and others)
- **2 major public stations** (KUOW-Seattle, OPB-Portland)
- **1 globally influential independent** (KEXP-Seattle)

This density means that a PNW student interested in broadcasting has access to multiple models, multiple entry points, and a visible career progression within their geographic region. The ecosystem effect is unmeasured but likely significant in explaining the PNW's outsized contribution to American broadcasting innovation [14].

## 6. The Rise of Student Podcasting

The podcast revolution, typically dated from Apple's addition of podcast support to iTunes in 2005, fundamentally altered the economics and accessibility of audio production for students [15]. Where broadcast radio required an FCC license, a transmitter, an antenna, and institutional support, podcasting required a computer, a microphone, an internet connection, and a hosting account.

### Podcasting by the Numbers

| Metric | Broadcast Radio (2005) | Podcasting (2005) | Podcasting (2024) |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|--------------------|
| Startup cost | $10,000-$100,000+ | $200-$500 | $100-$300 |
| Regulatory burden | FCC license + compliance | None | None |
| Distribution | Physical signal (geographic) | Internet (global) | Internet (global) |
| Audience discovery | Dial scanning | iTunes directory | Algorithms + social media |
| Production standard | Real-time (live) | Post-production (edited) | Post-production (edited) |
| Schedule | Fixed airtime | On-demand | On-demand |
| Failure mode | Dead air (public) | Bad episode (private) | Bad episode (private) |

The cost reduction is the most visible change, but the shift from live to post-production is the most experientially significant. Broadcast radio is a real-time medium: what you say goes out immediately, mistakes included. Podcasting is an edited medium: you can record, review, re-record, and edit before publishing. The developmental intensity of live broadcasting -- the awareness that you cannot take it back -- is absent from podcasting [16].

### Student Podcast Programs

By 2020, student podcast programs had become a standard offering in media education curricula, often replacing or supplementing traditional broadcast radio:

- **NPR Student Podcast Challenge:** Annual competition for students in grades 5-12; thousands of entries
- **New York Times Student Podcast Contest:** Showcases top student audio productions
- **Scholastic Audio Awards:** Recognizes excellence in student audio journalism
- **iHeart Student Podcasting Initiative:** Corporate-sponsored student podcasting programs

These programs have significantly expanded the number of students engaged in audio production. However, they operate within a fundamentally different experiential framework than broadcast radio. A student podcast is a creative project. A student radio broadcast is a civic act [17].

## 7. What the Transmitter Taught

The physical transmitter -- the device that converts audio to electromagnetic waves and broadcasts them across a geographic area -- teaches lessons that no podcast platform can replicate.

### Lessons of the Transmitter

1. **Geographic presence:** A transmitter connects the broadcaster to a physical place. The signal has a footprint. The broadcaster exists in relationship to a community defined by geography, not by algorithm.

2. **Temporal discipline:** Broadcast radio operates on a schedule. Your shift starts at a specific time. The station ID happens at the top of the hour. The EAS test happens when it happens. Time is not flexible.

3. **Consequential speech:** What you say on a licensed broadcast is subject to federal regulation. This is not a simulation of consequences -- it is consequences. The FCC can fine your school district.

4. **Shared audience:** Listeners tune in at the same time. The broadcast creates a shared temporal experience -- a community of simultaneous attention. Podcasting creates isolated, asynchronous consumption.

5. **Public failure:** Dead air, fumbled transitions, wrong tracks, microphone left open -- broadcast failures are public and immediate. There is no edit button. The learning that comes from public failure is qualitatively different from the learning that comes from private iteration.

6. **Infrastructure awareness:** Operating a transmitter requires understanding power, frequency, antenna, modulation -- the physics of electromagnetic radiation. This infrastructure awareness connects the broadcaster to the physical world in a way that uploading an MP3 does not [18].

### What Podcasting Teaches Instead

Podcasting teaches its own lessons: audio editing, narrative structure, audience development through marketing, platform strategy, and the creative discipline of post-production. These are valuable skills. They are different skills. The question for media education is not "podcast or broadcast?" but "what do we lose when the transmitter is optional?"

## 8. The Platform Shift: Broadcast to On-Demand

The shift from broadcast to on-demand audio production represents the most significant platform change in the history of student media. The change affects every dimension of the student experience: production workflow, audience relationship, failure mode, institutional context, and developmental impact.

### Structural Comparison

```
PLATFORM SHIFT -- STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS
================================================================

  BROADCAST MODEL                    ON-DEMAND MODEL
  +-----------------------+          +-----------------------+
  | Live production       |          | Post-production       |
  | Fixed schedule        |          | Flexible timeline     |
  | Geographic audience   |          | Global/algorithmic    |
  | FCC regulated         |          | Unregulated           |
  | Institutional license |          | Individual account    |
  | Hardware infrastructure|         | Software + hosting    |
  | Public failure mode   |          | Private iteration     |
  | Community identity    |          | Personal brand        |
  +-----------------------+          +-----------------------+
         |                                  |
         v                                  v
  EXPERIENTIAL OUTPUT              EXPERIENTIAL OUTPUT
  +-----------------------+        +-----------------------+
  | Temporal discipline   |        | Creative autonomy     |
  | Consequential speech  |        | Narrative craft       |
  | Infrastructure        |        | Platform literacy     |
  |   awareness           |        | Marketing skills      |
  | Geographic identity   |        | Global perspective    |
  | Regulatory literacy   |        | Self-directed         |
  | Public performance    |        |   learning            |
  +-----------------------+        +-----------------------+
```

Neither model is superior. Both produce valuable developmental outcomes. The concern is not that podcasting has replaced broadcasting but that the replacement has been treated as an upgrade when it is more accurately described as a trade -- different capabilities gained, different capabilities lost [19].

### The Hybrid Opportunity

The most educationally complete student media programs combine broadcast and podcast production, allowing students to experience both the discipline of live broadcasting and the craft of post-production editing. KNHC's combination of live FM broadcasting, internet streaming, and (increasingly) podcast production represents this hybrid model in practice [20].

## 9. Hybrid Models: Stations That Stream and Pod

The contemporary student media landscape includes stations that have adapted to the platform shift by integrating streaming and podcasting into their broadcast operations.

### Integration Patterns

| Station | Broadcast | Stream | Podcast | Hybrid Strategy |
|---------|-----------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| KNHC/C89.5 | FM 89.5 | Yes (1997+) | Limited | FM primary; stream extends reach; podcast experimental |
| KBPS | AM 1450 | Yes | Limited | AM primary; stream supplements |
| KECG | FM | Yes | Yes (NPR) | FM + podcast; NPR partnership enables podcast distribution |
| WHHS | FM | Yes | Yes | FM + podcast; student-driven content creation |
| Various new programs | None | None | Yes | Podcast-only; no broadcast license |

The hybrid model preserves the developmental benefits of live broadcasting while adding the creative possibilities of podcast production. The key architectural decision is whether the broadcast or the podcast is primary. At stations like KNHC, the broadcast is primary and the podcast is supplementary. At podcast-only programs, there is no broadcast component and no transmitter-based learning [21].

### The Streaming Bridge

Internet streaming serves as a bridge between the broadcast and podcast models. A stream is live (like broadcast) but digital (like podcasting). It preserves the temporal discipline of broadcasting while removing the geographic constraint. For student stations, streaming extends audience reach without requiring additional FCC licensing -- the stream is a secondary distribution of the licensed broadcast, not a separate service [22].

## 10. The Current Generation

The current generation of student broadcasters (roughly, students who entered high school after 2020) is the first generation for whom podcasting is a native medium. They grew up listening to podcasts, not radio. Their relationship to broadcast radio is mediated by nostalgia (from parents and teachers) rather than direct experience.

### Generational Characteristics

- **Digital native assumption:** The current generation assumes that audio content is produced on computers and distributed via the internet. The concept of a physical transmitter broadcasting electromagnetic waves is counterintuitive rather than obvious.
- **On-demand expectation:** Students expect to consume (and produce) audio on their own schedule. The broadcast model's fixed schedule feels antiquated.
- **Algorithm awareness:** Students understand that audience discovery is algorithmic. The broadcast model's audience discovery mechanism (dial scanning) is unfamiliar.
- **Lower production anxiety:** Post-production editing normalizes iteration. The broadcast model's unforgiving real-time performance is experienced as stressful rather than exciting.
- **Higher content fluency:** Students are accustomed to producing audio/video content (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube). The technical skills of audio production are less intimidating than they were for earlier generations.

### The Counter-Narrative

Despite these generational tendencies, students who encounter broadcast radio frequently describe the experience as transformative -- precisely because it contradicts their native media assumptions. The live broadcast experience offers something that their digital media diet does not: the uneditable moment. Students who have never performed in real time, with real consequences, in front of an invisible audience, report that the broadcast experience is qualitatively different from any other media production they have done [23].

This counter-narrative is significant for the oral history project: the current generation's experience of encountering broadcast radio as something alien and transformative may produce testimonies that are, in some ways, more articulate about the medium's developmental impact than accounts from earlier generations for whom broadcast was the default.

### The COVID Cohort

The COVID cohort (students who entered high school broadcasting programs between 2020 and 2023) experienced institutional disruption as their entry point. At KNHC, these students found a station with broken mentorship chains, unfamiliar equipment, and a history they had no direct connection to. Their experience of rebuilding constitutes a unique oral history opportunity -- they are documenting institutional recovery in real time, without the assumption of continuity that earlier cohorts took for granted [24].

### The TikTok Generation and Audio Identity

The current generation's relationship to audio is mediated by short-form video platforms. TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts have made audio production a ubiquitous skill -- but in a specific form. Students are fluent in 15-60 second audio clips, soundtrack selection, and audio-visual synchronization. This fluency is real but narrow. The broadcast model requires a different kind of audio identity: sustained speech over minutes or hours, real-time pacing, conversational flow, and the ability to fill silence without panic [25].

Students who arrive at a station like KNHC from a TikTok background frequently describe a mismatch between their audio production confidence (high, based on short-form experience) and their broadcast performance capability (initially low, because long-form live audio is a fundamentally different skill). The transition from "I can make a great 30-second clip" to "I can hold an audience for a two-hour show" is a developmental arc that the current generation experiences more acutely than any previous cohort.

### The Accessibility Question

The platform shift raises accessibility questions that the broadcast model did not face. Broadcast radio is accessible by default: anyone with a radio receiver can listen, regardless of internet access, device capability, or platform subscription. Podcasting is accessible only to those with internet access, a compatible device, and knowledge of how to find and subscribe to content.

For student media programs in under-resourced school districts, the platform shift has paradoxical implications. Podcasting is cheaper to produce (no transmitter, no license), but its audience reach is narrower and more socioeconomically filtered. A 10-watt FM station reaches everyone within its coverage radius equally. A podcast reaches only those who actively seek it out through digital platforms.

This accessibility dimension has direct implications for the civic function of student media. If student broadcasting serves a community service function (Module 5), then the delivery mechanism matters. A broadcast signal is a public good; a podcast is a niche product. The platform shift, from this perspective, is not just a technological transition but a shift in the civic character of student media [26].

### Station Alumni Networks

The survival and vitality of student stations correlates with the strength of their alumni networks. Stations with active alumni -- who donate, volunteer, advocate to school boards, and maintain social connections -- are significantly more resilient than stations without alumni engagement.

| Station | Alumni Network | Effect |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| KNHC/C89.5 | Washington Gives donor community; informal | Financial support; McKenna's donor profile demonstrates pipeline |
| KBPS | Benson Polytechnic alumni association | Institutional advocacy; centennial celebration support |
| KECG | NPR partnership provides institutional continuity | Less dependent on alumni; professional network compensates |
| KEXP | Robust volunteer and donor community | Post-independence growth funded by alumni-equivalent supporter base |

For the oral history project, alumni networks serve a dual function: they are both the source of narrators and the mechanism for narrator discovery. An active alumni network makes oral history feasible; a dormant network makes it an archaeological excavation [27].
