# C89.5 as Model Station

> **Domain:** Educational Broadcasting
> **Module:** 2 -- KNHC/C89.5 at Nathan Hale High School, Seattle
> **Through-line:** *KNHC began in December 1969 as a 100-milliwatt AM station built by an electronics teacher who wanted to give students hands-on work. Fifty-five years later, C89.5 is the most powerful high school radio station in the history of American broadcasting -- and the experiential record of the students who operated it across five decades is almost entirely unwritten.* This module builds the primary case study: the documented timeline, the felt experience gap, and the oral history framework for capturing what the institutional record cannot.

## 1. Origins: Larry Adams and the Electronics Class

KNHC began in December 1969 as a 100-milliwatt AM station at 1210 kHz, constructed by Nathan Hale High School electronics teacher Larry Adams. The station was conceived as a pedagogical tool: Adams wanted to give students "hands-on, practical work in electronics" [1]. The initial equipment was assembled from surplus and donated components. The AM signal was barely audible beyond the school grounds.

The FM construction permit followed in September 1970. First FM broadcast was January 25, 1971 at 10 watts on 89.5 MHz, covering approximately a 5-mile circle in north Seattle with an estimated 5,000-15,000 potential listeners [2]. The call letters KNHC combined Nathan Hale (NH) with Cleveland High School (C), reflecting the original institutional partnership.

Adams described the KNHC facility in a 1980 course manual as "a vocationally approved classroom designed to provide students with the opportunity to apply their electricity and electronics training in the development of entry-level employment skills" [3]. This framing is revealing for what it includes and what it omits. The description is vocational-utilitarian: the station exists to produce employable graduates. The experiential and developmental dimensions -- what the broadcasting experience did to students beyond employment skills -- are not addressed.

### The Call Sign Story

The call letters KNHC encode institutional history in four characters. "KN" follows the FCC's convention for stations west of the Mississippi River (K-prefix). "NH" stands for Nathan Hale, the school. "C" stands for Cleveland High School, which was part of the original institutional partnership. The Cleveland connection is largely forgotten -- the partnership dissolved decades ago -- but the C remains in the call sign, a fossil of an institutional relationship preserved in regulatory amber.

For students, the call sign functions as a totem. "KNHC" and its commercial identity "C89.5" are badges of belonging. Students who introduce themselves as "from C89.5" invoke a community identity that persists long after graduation. The call sign connects them to every student who came before and every student who will come after. It is a form of institutional continuity that transcends the individual and outlives the cohort.

### The Founding Generation

The students who built and operated KNHC in its first years (1969-1975) are now in their late 60s and early 70s. They represent the most fragile narrator cohort: the only living witnesses to the station's origins as a classroom electronics project. Their memories of building the transmitter, wiring the studio, and conducting the first broadcasts are irreplaceable primary sources. No oral history of this period exists [4].

The founding generation's experience is fundamentally different from every subsequent cohort's because they built the station rather than inheriting it. Every student after 1975 walked into an existing facility with an existing license, existing equipment, and an existing identity. The founding students walked into an empty room with a teacher who had a plan. Their relationship to the station is one of authorship, not stewardship. This distinction matters for the oral history framework because the interview questions appropriate for a founding narrator ("How did you build it?") are entirely different from those appropriate for a later narrator ("How did you find it?").

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** KNHC/C89.5 broadcasts from Nathan Hale High School in Seattle's Meadowbrook neighborhood, on the traditional territories of the Duwamish people. The station's signal at various power levels has reached across the traditional territories of the Muckleshoot, Suquamish, and Snoqualmie peoples. This geographic context is integral to the station's community identity.

## 2. The Power Milestone Timeline

The history of KNHC can be read as a sequence of power increases, each representing an institutional investment decision and a transformation in the student experience.

| Year | Power / Event | Signal Reach | Institutional Context |
|------|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1969 | 100 mW AM (1210 kHz) | School grounds only | Electronics class project; Larry Adams |
| 1971 | 10 W FM (89.5 MHz) | ~5 mile radius, north Seattle | First FM broadcast; KNHC call sign |
| 1972 | 320 W | ~10 mile radius | Stereo operations added 1973 |
| 1974 | 1,500 W directional | North Seattle, parts of Shoreline | Directional antenna required |
| 1977 | 5,000 W | Greater north Seattle | Mass Communications magnet begins |
| 1981 | 3,000 W non-directional | Similar reach, improved coverage | Budget crisis; community college partnership saves station |
| 1982 | Format shift: R&B/Urban | -- | "More funk than junk"; rapid listener growth |
| 1988 | 30 kW application filed | -- | FCC power increase application; grant secured |
| 1991 | 30 kW operational | Greater Seattle metro; ~200,000 weekly | Full-power broadcasts begin |
| 1995 | Digital audio workstations | -- | Three digital editing stations installed |
| 1997 | Internet streaming | Global (theoretically) | Among earliest US stations streaming online |
| 2002 | Transmitter to Cougar Mountain | Everett to Tacoma, across Puget Sound | 8,500 W from elevated site; expanded effective coverage |
| 2003 | Village Voice recognition | -- | "Best High School Radio Station" (NYC internet listeners) |
| 2004 | Billboard chart presence | -- | First non-commercial US station on Billboard Dance chart |
| 2008 | Lady Gaga campus show | -- | Students book pre-fame Lady Gaga for 400-person show |
| 2009 | New digital studios | -- | Complete facility renovation; HD Radio capability |
| 2021 | 50th FM anniversary | -- | Post-COVID resumption; 50 years continuous FM operation |

Each power increase transformed the student experience in ways the timeline cannot capture. The jump from 10 watts to 1,500 watts meant that a student's voice could be heard by their family at home. The jump to 30 kW meant their voice could be heard by strangers across the metropolitan area. These are phenomenologically different experiences, and no account of either transition from a student's perspective exists in the public record [5].

## 3. The Mass Communications Magnet Program

In 1977, Nathan Hale High School established a Mass Communications magnet program, embedding the radio station within a structured curriculum that included journalism, broadcast production, and media analysis [6]. This was a pivotal institutional decision. Prior to the magnet program, KNHC was an electronics class project -- vocational training in transmitter operation. After 1977, it became part of a broader media education program that attracted students interested in broadcasting as a career or creative practice.

### Magnet Program Effects

- **Student recruitment:** The magnet program attracted students from across the Seattle Public Schools district, not just the Nathan Hale attendance area
- **Curricular integration:** Radio operation became embedded in graded coursework rather than existing as an extracurricular
- **Funding stability:** Magnet program funding provided institutional protection that pure extracurricular status could not
- **Student diversity:** Drawing from multiple schools increased the demographic diversity of the student broadcaster population

The magnet program era (1977-present) represents the longest and most stable phase of KNHC's history. It also represents the period for which the experiential record is richest in potential narrators but poorest in actual documentation. Students who went through the magnet program in the late 1970s through the 1990s are now in their 40s-60s -- old enough to have perspective on their experience, young enough for their memories to be vivid [7].

### The Magnet Curriculum Structure

The Mass Communications magnet program structured the broadcasting experience within a formal educational framework that included:

- **Broadcast Production:** Hands-on studio operation, board skills, microphone technique, live programming
- **Journalism Fundamentals:** News writing, interview technique, source evaluation, editorial ethics
- **Media Analysis:** Critical examination of commercial broadcasting, format strategy, audience research
- **Technical Operations:** Transmitter fundamentals, FCC compliance, emergency procedures, equipment maintenance
- **Music Programming:** Format development, music curation, playlist construction, genre analysis

This curriculum transformed KNHC from an electronics class project into a comprehensive media education laboratory. Students who completed the magnet program received not just broadcasting skills but a framework for understanding media as a system -- production, distribution, regulation, and audience all integrated into a single educational experience.

The magnet curriculum also created a selection effect: students who chose the Mass Communications magnet were, by definition, students who had an interest in media before entering the program. This self-selection means that the magnet-era narrators are likely to be more articulate about their broadcasting experience than Founding Era narrators who stumbled into radio through an electronics class. The oral history framework must account for this difference in narrator preparation and motivation.

### The Budget Crisis of 1981

The 1981 budget crisis is a pivotal moment in KNHC's institutional history that illustrates the fragility of educational broadcasting. The Seattle Public Schools district, facing budget pressure, threatened to shut down the station. The survival of KNHC was secured through a partnership with a local community college -- an institutional arrangement that preserved the station's license while distributing the financial burden [7].

For the students who were at the station during the 1981 crisis, the threat of closure was an experiential event with no parallel in normal school life. The possibility that their station -- their studio, their frequency, their community -- might simply cease to exist created a relationship to institutional fragility that students in stable programs never develop. The 1981 crisis cohort represents a unique narrator population: students who experienced the station not as a permanent institution but as something that could be taken away.

## 4. Format Transitions as Identity Events

The institutional record treats format transitions as programming decisions. For the students inside the station, they were identity events -- moments when the station's sound, and therefore their broadcast identity, changed.

### The R&B Moment (1982)

The 1982 shift to R&B and urban contemporary programming was made by students, not administrators. The decision reflected students' observation that Seattle's commercial radio landscape underserved Black and Brown audiences. By programming R&B, C89.5 filled a market gap while asserting a distinct cultural identity. The tagline "More funk than junk" captured the station's self-image [8].

For students who joined the station during this era, their broadcast identity was inseparable from the R&B format. Their on-air personas, their audience relationships, their sense of the station's purpose were all shaped by the format. When the format shifted toward electronic dance music in the 1990s, students who had built their identities around R&B experienced the transition as a personal loss -- even though the institutional record describes it as a neutral programming evolution.

### The Dance/Electronic Transition (1990s)

The move toward electronic dance music aligned KNHC with a growing Seattle scene that commercial radio was not serving. This format decision, again student-driven, would eventually place C89.5 on the Billboard Dance chart in 2004 -- the first non-commercial station in the United States to achieve that distinction [9]. The Billboard moment represents the apex of student programming autonomy: a format chosen by high school students outperforming commercial stations on the industry's own metrics.

### What Format Memory Tells Us

Format transitions are uniquely valuable for oral history because they anchor memory to specific time periods and create natural narrator cohort boundaries. A student who identifies as "an R&B era DJ" provides a temporal, cultural, and experiential marker that is more richly textured than any institutional timeline can offer [10].

### The Seattle Music Context

KNHC's format history cannot be understood outside the broader Seattle music ecosystem. The city that produced grunge (Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains) simultaneously hosted a student radio station programming R&B and electronic dance music. While commercial Seattle radio was dominated by alternative rock in the 1990s, C89.5 was serving an audience that the grunge explosion had rendered invisible.

This countercultural positioning -- serving the audience that mainstream Seattle radio ignored -- is a recurring theme in KNHC's history. The station has consistently programmed what commercial radio will not: R&B when Seattle was ignoring Black music, electronic dance music when Seattle was obsessed with guitar bands, hip-hop when the city's music identity was still defined by Sub Pop and 4AD. Students at C89.5 have, for decades, been curatorial contrarians -- and that contrarian identity is itself a form of experiential knowledge worth capturing.

The Seattle music context also created professional opportunities for KNHC students. The city's dense music scene -- labels, venues, promoters, producers, DJs -- provided a natural ecosystem for students whose broadcasting experience had given them industry contacts and curatorial credibility. The pipeline from KNHC to Seattle's professional music industry is informal but documented anecdotally by alumni who credit the station for their entry into the scene.

## 5. The 30-Kilowatt Moment

In December 1988, KNHC filed an FCC application to increase power from 3 kW to 30 kW. The application was approved and the power increase was implemented in 1991, transforming C89.5 from a neighborhood station into a metropolitan broadcaster [11].

The 30 kW moment is the single most consequential event in the station's history from the experiential perspective. Before 30 kW, a student broadcasting on C89.5 was reaching a local audience of perhaps 50,000 potential listeners in north Seattle. After 30 kW, the same student was reaching an estimated 200,000 weekly listeners across the greater Seattle metropolitan area, from Everett in the north to well south of the city, and across Puget Sound to the Kitsap Peninsula [12].

### The Scale Problem

What does it mean for a seventeen-year-old to know -- or not know -- that 200,000 people can hear them? This is not a rhetorical question. It is an empirical one that has never been asked. The psychological literature on performance anxiety and audience awareness addresses professional performers, public speakers, and competitive athletes. It does not address adolescent broadcasters whose audience is invisible, distributed across a metropolitan area, and largely unknown to them [13].

The scale problem has a temporal dimension as well. The 30 kW power increase was not a sudden event from the student perspective -- it was a gradual realization. The transmitter was upgraded. The signal coverage maps changed. The phone calls from listeners further away began. The awareness that "people in Tacoma can hear me" emerged over time, differently for each student. This gradual awareness is precisely the kind of experiential datum that institutional records cannot capture and oral history is designed to preserve.

### The Jack Straw Foundation Challenge

The power increase was contested. In 1983, the Jack Straw Foundation -- a Seattle arts organization -- filed with the FCC to share the 89.5 frequency. The case was resolved in KNHC's favor in 1988, clearing the path for the 30 kW application [14]. This regulatory battle occurred entirely in the institutional domain; there is no record of how students experienced the threat of losing their frequency to an adult organization.

### The Sound of 30 Kilowatts

From an engineering perspective, 30 kW is an abstraction -- a power measurement that determines signal propagation according to the inverse-square law. From the student perspective, 30 kW had a sound. The Puget Sound region's terrain -- water, hills, valleys, the Olympic and Cascade ranges -- created a signal geography that was not uniform. Students discovered the boundaries of their signal empirically: a friend in Tacoma could hear them; a friend in Olympia could not. The signal crossed water better than it crossed mountains. Students who cared about where their voice reached -- and the oral history hypothesis is that many did -- experienced their broadcast geography as a personal map.

The 2002 transmitter relocation to Cougar Mountain (8,500 watts from an elevated site) changed this map. The coverage shifted. Areas that had been in-range at 30 kW from the Nathan Hale antenna were out of range from Cougar Mountain, and vice versa. For students who experienced the transition, the coverage change was experienced as a spatial reorganization of their audience -- a cartographic event that no institutional record was designed to capture.

### The Professional Staff Relationship

The relationship between students and professional staff at C89.5 deserves focused attention because it is the primary locus of power dynamics within the station. General Manager June Fox and Operations Manager Drew Bailey serve as the adult authorities in a space where students exercise unusual autonomy [15].

The professional staff hold the FCC license obligations. They are responsible for regulatory compliance, content standards, and institutional relationships with the school district. Students hold the programming. They choose the music, develop their on-air personas, and build audience relationships. The boundary between these domains is negotiated daily, and the negotiation is rarely documented.

For an oral history project, the student-staff relationship is one of the most sensitive and most valuable areas of inquiry. Students may describe relationships with staff that are mentoring, constraining, supportive, adversarial, or (most commonly) all of these simultaneously. The OHA framework requires that questions about authority figures hold space for complexity without leading the narrator toward either positive or negative characterization.

### The Studio Environment

The physical studio at Nathan Hale High School has changed several times over the station's history. The original studio (1969-1970s) was assembled from surplus equipment in a converted classroom. The 2009 renovation created a modern digital studio with professional-grade equipment. For the oral history project, the physical environment of the studio is a rich source of sensory memory that anchors narrative recall.

Anchor questions for the studio environment include:

- What did the studio look like when you were there?
- What did it smell like? (Equipment, carpet, food, people)
- What sounds did you hear when the microphone was off?
- Where did you sit? Was there a preferred chair?
- Was there a window? What could you see?

These sensory questions activate episodic memory more effectively than abstract questions about "the experience." A narrator who remembers the smell of warm electronics and carpet cleaner is accessing a richer memory trace than a narrator who is asked to characterize their experience in general terms.

## 6. The Felt Experience Gap

The term "felt experience gap" describes the systematic absence of experiential data from the institutional record. This gap is not unique to KNHC, but KNHC is the ideal case study because the gap is so stark: one of the most significant broadcast operations in American high school history has no systematic first-person account from the students who ran it [15].

### Gap Categories

| Category | Description | Institutional Source | Experiential Record |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| First airshift | First solo on-air broadcast | Not documented | No account exists |
| Audience awareness | Realization of listener scale | Listenership surveys (station) | No account exists |
| Studio as sanctuary | Station as social/identity space | Not documented | No account exists |
| Failure and recovery | Dead air, errors, live mistakes | Incident logs (if they exist) | No account exists |
| Format transition | Personal experience of format change | Programming records | No account exists |
| Staff dynamics | Student-professional relationships | Employment records | No account exists |
| Signal boundary | Awareness of broadcast geography | Coverage maps | No account exists |
| Coming of age | Identity formation through broadcasting | Not documented | No account exists |

The gap is total. For every experiential category identified, the public record contains zero first-person student accounts. The closest existing account is James McKenna's donor profile (see Section 8), which provides a single sentence about "virtually living at the station" -- an outlier account from a notable career trajectory that is not representative of the general student experience [16].

### The Methodological Opportunity

The totality of the gap is, paradoxically, a methodological opportunity. Because no prior oral history exists, the SVB project has no predecessor to compete with, no existing narrative to contradict, and no prior interpretation to navigate. Every narrator interviewed will be providing the first systematic account of their experience. This first-generation oral history status means that the project will establish the baseline narrative against which all future accounts are measured.

The opportunity also has an ethical dimension. A first-generation oral history project carries the responsibility of setting the framing. The questions asked in the first interviews will shape the categories of experience that subsequent researchers explore. If the first interviews focus on technical operations, the technical narrative will dominate the archive. If they focus on emotional and developmental experience, that narrative will dominate. The SVB framework deliberately balances both dimensions, but the first interviews will inevitably privilege some categories over others based on narrator responsiveness and interviewer emphasis.

### The Silence of the Record

It is worth pausing on what it means for one of the most significant high school broadcasting operations in American history to have no systematic first-person student account across five decades. The silence is not a failure of documentation technology -- tape recorders, cameras, and journals have been available throughout the station's history. The silence is a structural product of how institutions document themselves: they document what they need to document for regulatory, financial, and administrative purposes. They do not document what the people inside them felt, because feelings are not line items.

The Smithsonian Institution Archives' *How to Do Oral History* guide explicitly identifies this structural silence as the primary justification for oral history work: "Oral history interviews seek to fill gaps in the documentary record" [15]. At KNHC, the gap is not partial -- it is comprehensive. The oral history project is not supplementing an existing narrative. It is creating one from silence.

## 7. Narrator Cohort Windows

For an oral history project, narrator selection must be intentional about era coverage. The following cohort windows are defined by station history milestones and represent distinct experiential periods.

| Window | Years | Power Level | Format | Est. Living Narrators | Priority |
|--------|-------|------------|--------|----------------------|----------|
| Founding Era | 1969-1976 | 100 mW - 1,500 W | Eclectic/Educational | 50-100 | CRITICAL |
| Magnet Launch | 1977-1981 | 1,500 W - 3 kW | Progressive/Mixed | 100-200 | HIGH |
| R&B Golden Age | 1982-1990 | 3 kW - 30 kW | R&B/Urban | 200-400 | HIGH |
| 30 kW Expansion | 1991-1999 | 30 kW | Dance/Electronic | 300-500 | MEDIUM |
| Digital Transition | 2000-2009 | 30 kW / 8.5 kW | Electronic/Dance | 300-500 | MEDIUM |
| Streaming Era | 2010-2019 | 8.5 kW + streaming | Electronic/Dance/Hip-Hop | 200-400 | MEDIUM |
| COVID/Post-COVID | 2020-present | 8.5 kW + streaming | Electronic/Dance/Hip-Hop | 50-100 | HIGH |

The Founding Era cohort is labeled CRITICAL because its members are the oldest and represent the only witnesses to the station's construction and earliest broadcasts. The COVID/Post-COVID cohort is labeled HIGH because the broken chain of institutional memory makes their experience a unique case study in discontinuity and recovery [17].

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** Narrator cohort estimation is approximate and based on Nathan Hale High School enrollment data and typical program participation rates. Actual living narrator counts may be lower due to mortality, relocation, and loss of contact. Any outreach effort must account for the possibility that Founding Era narrators may have cognitive decline that affects participation capacity.

## 8. The James McKenna Outlier

The C89.5 Washington Gives donor profile of James McKenna contains the richest existing first-person account of the student experience:

> "I discovered c89.5 when I was in 9th grade and ended up virtually living at the station. My radio teacher got me my first job in radio. A few years later I'm a young producer on a comedy series called Almost Live! That's where I met Bill [Nye] and Erren and the rest is history." [18]

McKenna later co-created *Bill Nye the Science Guy*, which debuted on KCTS (Seattle PBS) in 1993 and ran for five seasons. His career trajectory -- from high school radio to local television to nationally syndicated science education -- is remarkable. It is also an outlier: McKenna's path represents the upper bound of what the station-to-career pipeline could produce, not the median experience.

### Why the Outlier Matters

The McKenna account matters for the oral history project not because it is representative but because it reveals what a fuller oral history might contain. In two sentences, McKenna provides: a temporal marker ("9th grade"), a spatial marker ("living at the station"), an institutional relationship ("my radio teacher"), a career link ("first job in radio"), and a cultural impact chain (Almost Live! to Bill Nye). Each of these elements could be expanded into an hour of interview material. Multiply this density by hundreds of alumni across five decades, and the potential oral history archive is immense [19].

### What McKenna's Account Omits

Even the richest existing account is a donor testimonial -- a marketing text produced for a fundraising context. It omits: what the studio looked like, what the equipment felt like, what the first airshift was like, what the relationship with professional staff was like, what it meant to be a minor operating a licensed broadcast facility. These are the gaps the oral history framework is designed to fill.

## 9. Post-Digital: Streaming and the Expanded Audience

The addition of internet streaming in 1997 made KNHC one of the earliest radio stations in the United States to broadcast online [20]. The 2003 Village Voice recognition as "Best High School Radio Station" (based on the internet stream, from New York City listeners) demonstrated that the station's audience had become geographically unbound.

### The Expanded Audience Problem

Streaming created a new experiential dimension: the awareness that listeners could be anywhere. For a student who grew up thinking of the audience as "north Seattle," the realization that someone in Brooklyn was listening required a cognitive adjustment. The student's broadcast persona was no longer local -- it was, potentially, global.

This expansion of audience context has implications for the oral history framework. Students who broadcast before streaming (pre-1997) experienced a geographically bounded audience. Students who broadcast after streaming experienced an unbounded audience. The experiential difference between these two conditions is significant and represents a natural boundary in narrator cohort analysis [21].

### The Billboard Moment (2004)

When C89.5 appeared on the Billboard Dance chart in 2004, the station achieved a marker of cultural legitimacy that transcended its institutional identity. A high school radio station was outperforming commercial stations on the music industry's own metrics. For the students who programmed the music that earned the Billboard placement, this was a validation of their curatorial judgment -- a demonstration that student taste, unconstrained by commercial format consultants, could find and elevate music before the industry noticed it [22].

## 10. COVID and the Broken Chain

The COVID-19 pandemic forced Nathan Hale High School to close in March 2020. Station operations were suspended for approximately 18 months. The resumption in mid-2021, documented in the Seattle Times Pacific NW Magazine, described a station rebuilding its student culture from near-zero [23].

### The Memory Chain

High school radio stations transmit institutional knowledge through direct mentorship: senior students train junior students. This chain has operated continuously at KNHC for most of its history. COVID broke it. The graduating class of 2020 never trained the class of 2021 in person. The class of 2021 never received in-person mentorship from anyone who had operated the station pre-COVID.

This break is relevant for two reasons:

1. **It demonstrates the fragility of oral tradition in institutional settings.** The informal knowledge transfer that sustains a student radio station is more fragile than the institutional record. Equipment manuals survive closures. Mentorship chains do not.
2. **It creates a unique narrator cohort.** Students who experienced the COVID break and the post-COVID restart have a perspective on institutional discontinuity that no prior cohort possesses. Their accounts of rebuilding the station's culture are a form of institutional archaeology conducted in real time [24].

## 11. The Station Today

C89.5 operates today at 8,500 watts from a transmitter on Cougar Mountain, with coverage from Everett to south of Seattle and across Puget Sound. The station streams globally. Programming is primarily electronic dance music, hip-hop, and related genres, curated by students under the supervision of General Manager June Fox and Operations Manager Drew Bailey [25].

The station's current operation represents a mature institutional model: 50+ years of continuous FM broadcasting, deep community identity, a recognized brand in Seattle's music scene, and an ongoing commitment to student-operated programming. It is this maturity -- the weight of five decades of student experience -- that makes the oral history project both urgent and feasible. The station has produced thousands of alumni whose experiences span the entire arc from 100-milliwatt classroom project to metropolitan broadcast powerhouse.

The question is not whether those experiences are worth documenting. The question is whether they will be documented before the Founding Era narrators are gone.

### The Teaching-Hospital Comparison

C89.5's current operational model is often described using the teaching-hospital metaphor: students perform real work for real audiences under professional supervision. The comparison is apt but incomplete. In a teaching hospital, the patient is the primary beneficiary. In a student radio station, the listener is a beneficiary, but so is the student -- and the nature of the student's benefit is developmental in a way that the teaching-hospital model does not fully capture [25].

A medical resident performing surgery under supervision acquires a professional skill. A seventeen-year-old performing a solo airshift under supervision acquires something more diffuse and more permanent: a sense of themselves as someone with a public voice, someone whose choices reach beyond their immediate social circle, someone who can hold an audience's attention and shape a community's cultural experience. This developmental outcome is not a skill. It is an identity shift. And it occurs during the precise developmental window when identity is most plastic.

### The Revenue Model

C89.5 operates on a hybrid funding model that distinguishes it from both commercial stations (advertising-funded) and major public stations (CPB + member-funded):

| Revenue Source | Contribution | Notes |
|---------------|-------------|-------|
| Seattle Public Schools budget | Primary (operations) | Institutional support; vulnerable to district budget cycles |
| Washington Gives / listener support | Secondary (equipment, events) | Donor platform; James McKenna profile |
| Grants | Occasional | Equipment upgrades, special projects |
| Underwriting | Minimal | FCC-compliant underwriting; not advertising |

The funding model's dependence on school district support means that C89.5's continued existence is ultimately a political decision made by the Seattle Public Schools board. Every budget cycle is an implicit referendum on whether student radio is worth institutional investment. The station has survived every cycle since 1969, but the survival is never guaranteed -- and each near-miss budget crisis is an experiential event for the students who were there when it happened.

### Cougar Mountain and the Signal Geography

The 2002 transmitter relocation to Cougar Mountain (elevation ~1,595 feet) transformed C89.5's signal geography. At 8,500 watts from an elevated site, the station's coverage extends from Everett in the north to approximately midway to Tacoma in the south, and across the Puget Sound to the Kitsap Peninsula. The effective coverage area encompasses approximately 2,000 square miles and a population of over 3 million people.

The signal geography creates a cartographic dimension of the student experience: the station's footprint is a physical fact that students can see on a coverage map. A student who knows that their voice reaches across the Sound is having a different experience from a student who knows their voice reaches three miles. The Cougar Mountain relocation did not change the studio experience -- the microphone is the same, the console is the same -- but it changed the meaning of what the microphone does.

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** The Cougar Mountain transmitter site is located on the traditional territories of the Snoqualmie people. The signal footprint encompasses the traditional territories of the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Suquamish, Snoqualmie, Puyallup, Tulalip, and Stillaguamish peoples. This geographic context is integral to understanding the community the signal reaches.

### The Lady Gaga Moment (2008)

In 2008, students at C89.5 booked Lady Gaga for a campus show at Nathan Hale High School, attended by approximately 400 people. At the time, Gaga was pre-fame -- "Just Dance" had been released but had not yet charted nationally. The booking was a curatorial decision by student programmers who recognized the artist's potential before the commercial industry did.

The Lady Gaga moment illustrates a recurring pattern at C89.5: student curators, unconstrained by commercial format consultants and ratings pressure, consistently identify emerging artists before they break commercially. This curatorial independence is a direct consequence of the non-commercial educational license -- the station has no ratings pressure, no advertising clients to placate, and no format consultant telling students what to play. The students' curatorial instincts are, in effect, the station's format.

For the students who booked the show, the Lady Gaga moment was probably unremarkable at the time -- just another booking. In retrospect, it represents a validation of student curatorial judgment that most commercial radio programmers never achieve. This kind of retrospective revaluation -- where an experience that seemed ordinary at the time becomes significant in hindsight -- is precisely the kind of narrative that oral history is designed to capture.
