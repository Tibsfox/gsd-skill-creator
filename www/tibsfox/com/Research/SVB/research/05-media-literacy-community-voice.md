# Media Literacy & Community Voice

> **Domain:** Educational Broadcasting
> **Module:** 5 -- Media Literacy as Civic Infrastructure and the Oral History Methodology
> **Through-line:** *Media literacy is not a subject you teach. It is a condition you produce by requiring students to make media -- to sit behind a console, choose what to broadcast, live with the consequences of that choice, and understand that the signal they send shapes the community that receives it.* Student broadcasting teaches critical media consumption not through analysis but through production. This module connects that insight to the oral history methodology that preserves the evidence, and to the community radio tradition that extends the student voice beyond the school walls.

## 1. Media Literacy Through Production

The National Association for Media Literacy Education (NAMLE) defines media literacy as "the ability to access, analyze, evaluate, create, and act using all forms of communication" [1]. The definition is comprehensive but the emphasis in most educational settings falls on analysis and evaluation -- teaching students to critically examine media they consume. The creation component is treated as supplementary.

Student broadcasting inverts this priority. At a station like KNHC/C89.5, students do not begin with media analysis. They begin with a microphone, a console, and an audience. The analysis comes later, prompted by the experience of having created something that went out into the world. This production-first model of media literacy is more effective than the analysis-first model for a developmental reason: adolescents learn by doing, and the stakes of doing -- the real audience, the real consequences -- provide motivation that classroom analysis cannot [2].

### The Embodied Curriculum

Student broadcasting is an embodied curriculum. The student physically sits in a chair, physically operates equipment, physically speaks into a microphone, and physically sends a signal into the physical environment. This embodiment matters because media literacy at its deepest level is about understanding the relationship between the producer, the medium, and the audience. A student who has produced media -- who has felt the weight of a live broadcast -- understands media production from the inside in a way that no amount of analysis can replicate [3].

### Production Literacy vs. Analysis Literacy

| Dimension | Analysis Literacy (classroom) | Production Literacy (broadcasting) |
|-----------|------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Mode | Cognitive | Embodied + cognitive |
| Motivation | External (grades) | Internal (audience, identity, stakes) |
| Feedback | Delayed (teacher evaluation) | Immediate (listener response, dead air, errors) |
| Stakes | Academic | Social, professional, regulatory |
| Ownership | Assignment | Personal broadcast identity |
| Duration | Semester | Ongoing (multi-year program participation) |
| Transfer | Analytical skill | Professional practice + analytical skill |

The production literacy model does not replace analysis literacy -- it creates the experiential foundation on which analysis becomes meaningful. A student who has made programming decisions understands programming bias. A student who has complied with FCC indecency rules understands content regulation. A student who has experienced the editorial power of choosing what to broadcast understands gatekeeping [4].

## 2. The Production-Consumption Loop

Media literacy research describes a "production-consumption loop" in which the experience of producing media transforms the way a person consumes media [5]. Student broadcasters who have made programming decisions become more attentive to programming decisions made by commercial stations. Students who have operated under FCC regulations become more aware of the regulatory environment of commercial media. Students who have experienced the editorial power of the console become more critical consumers of the editorial choices made by others.

### Evidence of the Loop

The production-consumption loop in student broadcasting is well-attested in anecdotal evidence (alumni descriptions of how the experience changed their media consumption) but poorly documented in formal research. The oral history framework proposed in this module is, in part, designed to systematically capture evidence of the loop across multiple narrator cohorts [6].

### The Developmental Timing Advantage

The production-consumption loop operates most powerfully when production experience occurs during the identity formation stage (ages 12-18 in Erikson's framework) [7]. A student who acquires production literacy during this window does not merely add a skill -- they integrate media production into their developing identity. The broadcast voice becomes part of who they are, not just what they can do. This identity-level integration produces media literacy that persists across the lifespan, unlike classroom-based analysis literacy, which research suggests decays significantly after the course ends [8].

## 3. Student Broadcasting as Civic Education

Student broadcasting is civic education that happens to use a transmitter. The civic dimensions of student radio are not incidental to the broadcasting experience -- they are the broadcasting experience, viewed from a different angle.

### Civic Functions of Student Broadcasting

1. **Public speech:** Broadcasting is a form of public speech protected by the First Amendment and regulated by federal law. Students learn to exercise speech rights under real regulatory constraints.

2. **Community service:** A radio station serving 150,000-200,000 weekly listeners is a public service. Students learn that their work serves a community beyond themselves.

3. **Institutional accountability:** The FCC license creates accountability to a federal agency. Students experience institutional accountability directly, not abstractly.

4. **Cultural curation:** Programming decisions are cultural decisions. Choosing what music to broadcast, what topics to discuss, what voices to amplify is an exercise in cultural leadership.

5. **Infrastructure stewardship:** Students are temporary stewards of permanent infrastructure. The transmitter, the studio, the license were there before them and will be there after them. Learning to maintain and improve infrastructure you do not own is a civic virtue [9].

### The Civic Dimension and Oral History

The civic dimensions of student broadcasting are especially relevant for the oral history project because they connect individual student experience to community impact. An oral history question that asks "Did you think about who was listening?" is simultaneously a question about personal experience and civic awareness. The oral history framework bridges these domains by design [10].

## 4. The Foxfire Principle

The Foxfire principle -- named for the Foxfire magazine and book series created by Eliot Wigginton's students in Rabun Gap, Georgia, beginning in 1966 -- is the preservation of living knowledge before it becomes archaeology [11]. The Foxfire project sent high school students to interview Appalachian elders about traditional crafts, skills, and stories. The resulting publications preserved knowledge that existed only in living memory.

### Foxfire and Student Broadcasting

The SVB oral history project is a Foxfire project. The "elders" are KNHC alumni from the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s. The "traditional knowledge" is the felt experience of student broadcasting -- knowledge that exists only in the memories of the people who lived it. The methodology is adapted from the Oral History Association rather than the Foxfire classroom model, but the principle is identical: capture living knowledge before the people who hold it are gone [12].

### The Urgency Window

The Foxfire principle has a temporal dimension that makes it an organizing urgency for the SVB project:

| Narrator Cohort | Age Range (2026) | Memory Status | Priority |
|----------------|------------------|---------------|----------|
| Founding Era (1969-1976) | 65-74 | Vivid episodic memory declining; semantic memory intact | CRITICAL |
| Magnet Launch (1977-1981) | 59-67 | Strong; approaching late-career retrospective stage | HIGH |
| R&B Golden Age (1982-1990) | 50-62 | Strong; mid-career; may be difficult to contact | HIGH |
| 30 kW Expansion (1991-1999) | 41-53 | Strong; peak career; accessible via professional networks | MEDIUM |
| Digital Transition (2000-2009) | 31-44 | Strong; early career; social media accessible | MEDIUM |
| Streaming Era (2010-2019) | 21-34 | Very strong; recent; easy to contact | MEDIUM |
| COVID/Post-COVID (2020+) | 14-24 | Current; real-time access | HIGH |

The Founding Era cohort represents the most urgent narrator population. Their memories of building the station and conducting the first broadcasts are the most fragile and the most irreplaceable. Every year of delay in the oral history project represents an irreversible loss of potential narrators from this cohort [13].

## 5. Oral History Methodology for Youth Broadcasters

The oral history methodology for the SVB project adapts the Oral History Association's (OHA) Core Principles (2020) and Best Practices (2022) for the specific context of youth broadcaster narrators -- adults who are recounting experiences that occurred during their adolescence [14].

### OHA Core Principles (Summary)

- **Narrator-centered:** Narrators are co-creators of the interview. The interviewer's authority does not supersede the narrator's right to their own story.
- **Informed consent is ongoing:** Consent is not a one-time signature; it is an evolving agreement throughout the process.
- **Power sensitivity:** Practitioners must be sensitive to differences in power between interviewer and narrator.
- **Narrator owns their words:** Copyright in the interview rests with the narrator until transferred via deed of gift.
- **Preservation is an obligation:** Interviews should be preserved and made accessible to future researchers [15].

### Youth-Specific Adaptations

The standard OHA framework requires four adaptations for the youth broadcaster context:

| Dimension | Standard OHA Practice | Youth Broadcaster Adaptation |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Memory reliability | Trust narrator's account | Anchor questions to specific concrete events (first airshift, a specific broadcast, a crisis moment) to activate episodic rather than semantic memory; adolescent-era memories are subject to telescoping and affective distortion |
| Power dynamics | Balanced interviewer-narrator relationship | Many narrators will describe relationships with teachers and professional staff who had authority over them as minors; questions must hold space for complex accounts without leading |
| Consent framework | Standard adult informed consent | Narrators are adults describing experiences as minors; standard adult consent is sufficient; however, references to third parties who were minors require anonymization protocol |
| Multi-generational selection | Representative sampling | Intentional era coverage; Founding Era narrators are irreplaceable and must be prioritized; COVID cohort provides unique discontinuity perspective |

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** The consent framework must address the possibility that narrators may describe experiences that were formative but also painful -- bullying, professional rejection, social exclusion, institutional failure. The interviewer must be prepared to pause, redirect, and respect the narrator's boundaries. Trauma-informed interview protocols (adapted from Voice of Witness methodology) should be incorporated into pre-interview training [16].

## 6. The OHA Framework Adapted

The adapted framework structures the interview process into three phases: pre-interview, interview, and post-interview.

### Pre-Interview Phase

1. **Narrator identification and outreach:** Through alumni networks, social media, station archives, and referral chains
2. **Background research:** Station history for narrator's era; format, power level, professional staff, significant events
3. **Narrator intake form:** Demographic information, years at station, roles held, consent to record and archive
4. **Pre-interview conversation:** Informal discussion to build rapport, explain the project, establish expectations
5. **Equipment check:** Recording device tested; backup recorder prepared; environment assessed for noise

### Interview Phase (Two-Session Model)

**Session 1: Experiential Narrative (90 minutes)**
- Opening: Arrival story -- how did you end up at the station?
- Core: Chronological narrative anchored to concrete events
- Domains: Technical, Social, Emotional, Audience, Career/Legacy
- Closing: What would you tell a current student?

**Session 2: Reflection and Follow-Up (60 minutes)**
- Review of Session 1 themes
- Clarification of specific events or claims
- Deeper exploration of identified themes
- Reflection on the experience from current perspective
- Final questions: What did we miss?

### Post-Interview Phase

1. **Transcript production:** Verbatim transcription with time-indexes
2. **Narrator review:** Transcript shared with narrator for correction and approval
3. **Release and deed of gift:** Formal transfer of usage rights to archive
4. **Archival deposit:** Audio (FLAC + WAV) and transcript deposited to repository
5. **Metadata tagging:** Dublin Core + OHA vocabulary indexing [17]

## 7. Interview Design: Anchor Questions and Era Variants

The interview question bank is organized across five thematic domains, with anchor questions designed to activate episodic memory rather than general period recall.

### Domain 1: Technical

- "Walk me through the first time you were alone at the console. What did the room look, sound, and smell like?"
- "What equipment did you use? Can you describe the board, the microphone, the cart machines or CDs or computer?"
- "Did you ever have a technical failure on air? Tell me about it."
- "Did you understand the transmitter -- what it was doing, where the signal went?"

### Domain 2: Social

- "Describe the social world of the station. Who were the characters? What were the unwritten rules?"
- "What was your relationship with the professional staff -- the general manager, the operations manager, the teachers?"
- "Was the station a social group? Did you hang out with station people outside the studio?"
- "Were there rivalries, cliques, hierarchies among the student DJs?"

### Domain 3: Emotional

- "Was there a moment when the scale of the broadcast -- the signal reach -- became real to you? What happened?"
- "What did it feel like to hear yourself on the radio for the first time?"
- "Was there a moment of pride? Of embarrassment? Of fear?"
- "Did you have a broadcast persona -- a voice that was different from your regular voice?"

### Domain 4: Audience

- "Did you ever get a call or a message from a listener? Tell me about it."
- "Did you think about who was listening? What did you imagine?"
- "Did your friends listen? Your family? Did they tell you about it?"
- "Did you know where the signal reached? Did it matter to you?"

### Domain 5: Career/Legacy

- "What did the station give you that your regular classes didn't?"
- "How did the experience shape what you did after high school?"
- "If you could go back and tell yourself something on your first day at the station, what would it be?"
- "What do you want people to understand about what it was like?"

### Era Variants

Questions are adapted for specific narrator cohorts:

| Era | Variant Examples |
|-----|------------------|
| Founding (1969-1976) | "You built this station. Tell me about the construction." / "What did 10 watts feel like?" |
| R&B (1982-1990) | "The station played R&B when nobody else in Seattle was. Whose idea was that?" / "More funk than junk -- what did that mean to you?" |
| 30 kW (1991-1999) | "When the power went to 30,000 watts, did you feel it?" / "Did you know people in Tacoma were listening?" |
| Digital (2000-2009) | "When streaming started, did it change how you thought about the audience?" / "The Village Voice called you the best HS station. Did you know?" |
| COVID (2020+) | "What was it like coming into a station that had been empty for a year?" / "Did anyone teach you, or did you figure it out?" |

## 8. Community Radio Crossover

Community radio stations occupy a space between student radio and professional public radio. They share student radio's reliance on volunteer labor and commitment to non-commercial programming, but serve community rather than educational missions. The crossover between these models is significant and bidirectional [18].

### The Pipeline

Student broadcasting creates community radio volunteers. Students who develop broadcasting skills in high school or college frequently continue at community stations after graduation. The skills transfer is direct: console operation, programming, FCC compliance, audience relationship. The community radio sector depends on this pipeline for its volunteer labor force [19].

### PNW Community Radio Stations

| Station | Location | Founded | Connection to Student Radio |
|---------|----------|---------|---------------------------|
| KBOO | Portland, OR | 1968 | Volunteer pipeline from KBPS and Portland college stations |
| KSVR | Mount Vernon, WA | 1979 | Licensed to Skagit Valley College; student + community hybrid |
| KMUN | Astoria, OR | 1983 | Community volunteers include former student broadcasters |
| KYRS | Spokane, WA | 2003 | LPFM; community station with youth programming |

### The Community Voice Principle

Community radio is predicated on the principle that communities have a right to their own media voice -- that the voices heard on the airwaves should include the voices of the community, not just the voices selected by commercial media companies. This principle extends the student broadcasting philosophy: if students have a right to broadcast their own voice, communities have the same right [20].

The SVB oral history project connects to the community voice principle because the student experience at stations like KNHC is a community experience. The station's signal reached across Seattle for decades. The students who operated it were community voices, whether or not they thought of themselves that way. Capturing their stories is, in a meaningful sense, capturing the community's stories [21].

## 9. Archive and Preservation

The oral history framework requires an archive strategy: where will completed interviews be deposited, how will they be preserved, and how will they be made accessible to future researchers?

### Repository Options

| Repository | Type | Strengths | Risks |
|------------|------|-----------|-------|
| University of Washington Libraries Special Collections | University archive | Robust digital preservation; PNW collection context; oral history experience | Access policies may limit some uses |
| Seattle Public Library Special Collections | Public library | Broad public access; city institutional relationship; low barrier | Less specialized in oral history methodology |
| C89.5/KNHC Station Archive | Self-archive | Deepest contextual knowledge; station identity | Risk of institutional discontinuity; requires preservation partnership |
| Washington State Digital Archives | State government | Appropriate for SPS-owned content; WARC-compliant | Less public-facing; bureaucratic access |
| Internet Archive | Non-profit | Global access; robust preservation; community-oriented | Less curatorial control; permanence dependent on organization survival |

The recommended strategy is a dual-deposit model: primary deposit at the University of Washington Libraries Special Collections (for professional archival management and PNW contextual collection) with secondary deposit at the Internet Archive (for broad public access). The C89.5 station archive should maintain its own copies but should not serve as the sole repository due to the risk of institutional discontinuity [22].

### Metadata Schema

Each interview record carries the following metadata, compatible with Dublin Core and OHA indexing vocabulary:

```
INTERVIEW METADATA SCHEMA
================================================================

  narrator_name:        [Full legal name or playa name if pseudonym elected]
  narrator_birth_year:  [YYYY]
  years_at_station:     [Start year - End year]
  era_window_code:      [FOUNDING | MAGNET | RNB | 30KW | DIGITAL | STREAM | COVID]
  roles_held:           [DJ, Producer, Music Director, etc.]
  recording_date:       [YYYY-MM-DD]
  interviewer_name:     [Full name]
  recording_format:     [WAV 96kHz/24bit primary; FLAC archive]
  duration_minutes:     [Total across sessions]
  session_count:        [1, 2, or more]
  thematic_tags:        [From 5-domain taxonomy: Technical, Social, Emotional, Audience, Career]
  consent_status:       [FULL | RESTRICTED | EMBARGO_UNTIL_DATE]
  release_type:         [Deed of Gift | License | Restricted]
  anonymization:        [NONE | PARTIAL_THIRD_PARTY | FULL]
  dc_subject:           [Dublin Core subject terms]
  dc_coverage:          [Geographic: Seattle, WA; Temporal: era window years]
  dc_rights:            [Per deed of gift or license terms]
```

### Digital Access Layer

The long-term vision includes a public-facing discovery interface: a searchable web application indexed to the thematic domain taxonomy, allowing researchers and community members to browse interviews by era, theme, narrator role, or keyword. This discovery layer is a Phase 2 deliverable, to be specified after the initial archival deposit is complete [23].

## 10. The Felt History as Teaching Tool

The completed oral history archive is not merely a preservation project -- it is a teaching tool. The felt history of student broadcasting, captured in the voices of the people who lived it, becomes a resource for current and future student broadcasters, media educators, and oral history practitioners.

### Teaching Applications

1. **New student orientation:** Incoming students at KNHC (or any student station) listen to excerpts from alumni oral histories, connecting them to the station's tradition before they touch the equipment
2. **Media literacy curriculum:** Oral history excerpts serve as primary sources in media literacy courses, providing insider accounts of media production that published analysis cannot offer
3. **Oral history methodology training:** The SVB framework itself serves as a teaching resource for students learning oral history methodology
4. **Community engagement:** Public presentations of oral history excerpts connect the station to its community of former listeners and supporters
5. **Institutional memory:** The archive preserves institutional knowledge that the mentorship chain cannot transmit when it is broken (as COVID demonstrated) [24].

### The Recursive Value

The oral history project is recursive: the act of capturing student voices about broadcasting produces a media artifact (the recorded interview) that itself teaches media literacy. Students who participate in the interview process -- as interviewers or narrators -- acquire oral history skills and media production experience through the act of documenting the experience. The project produces its own next generation of practitioners [25].

### The Generalizability Principle

The SVB oral history framework is designed around KNHC/C89.5 as a primary case, but the methodology is explicitly generalizable. Any high school, college, or community radio station with a history of student participation can adapt the framework by substituting:

- Station-specific milestone timelines for the KNHC timeline
- Local narrator cohort windows for the KNHC cohort map
- Station-appropriate era variant questions for the KNHC era variants
- Regional archive partners for the UW/SPL repositories

The framework is a template, not a prescription. The questions, the methodology, the archive specification, and the consent framework are designed to be portable across institutional contexts. A station in rural Indiana can use the same interview protocol as a station in metropolitan Seattle; only the station-specific details change [26].

### The Replication Model

If the SVB framework succeeds at KNHC, the natural next step is replication across peer stations. The replication model follows a hub-and-spoke architecture:

```

================================================================

            ┌──────────────────────────┐
            │  SVB Core Framework      │
            │  (OHA-adapted, portable) │
            └────────────┬─────────────┘

    ┌─────┴─────┐  ┌────┴────┐  ┌──────┴──────┐
    │ KNHC/C89.5│  │ KBPS    │  │ KECG        │

```

The hub holds the methodology. Each spoke produces its own station-specific oral history. The spokes can be connected through a national-level discovery interface that allows cross-station comparison -- what was it like to broadcast at a 1 kW AM station in Portland in 1975 compared to a 30 kW FM station in Seattle in 1995? This cross-station comparative dimension would be a unique contribution to the literature on student media experience [27].

### The Voice of Witness Connection

The Voice of Witness organization, founded by Dave Eggers and Lola Vollen in 2004, has developed a methodology for community-based oral history that shares significant DNA with the SVB framework. Voice of Witness focuses on oral narratives from marginalized communities, using narrative structure to create empathy and understanding across social boundaries.

The SVB project adapts Voice of Witness principles in two specific ways:

1. **Narrator agency:** Like Voice of Witness, SVB treats narrators as the primary authorities on their own experience. The interviewer is a facilitator, not an editor. The narrator's account stands on its own terms.
2. **Trauma sensitivity:** Voice of Witness's protocols for interviewing narrators about difficult experiences (developed for oral histories of incarceration, displacement, and violence) are adapted for the lower-stakes but still emotionally significant context of student broadcasting. A student who experienced public failure on air, social exclusion at the station, or conflict with authority figures may carry emotional weight around those memories that requires sensitive handling [28].

### The Through-Line

Media literacy, community voice, oral history methodology, and archive preservation converge on a single point: the felt experience of the student broadcaster is worth capturing, preserving, and teaching from. The institutional record documents the station. The oral history documents the person inside it. The person inside it -- the seventeen-year-old at the console, aware of the signal, aware of the audience, constructing a voice in real time -- is the irreplaceable evidence that only oral history can preserve.

The student voice is the signal that never quite made it to tape. This module specifies the antenna that points at what was broadcast between the lines.
