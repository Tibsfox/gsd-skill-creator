## 1. The Dawn of Educational Broadcasting

The first educational radio broadcasts in the United States predate the creation of the Federal Radio Commission by nearly a decade. The University of Wisconsin's WHA, which traces its origins to experimental transmissions by Professor Earle Terry in 1917, began regular programming in 1921 and claims the title of oldest educational station in the country [1]. Within five years, over 200 educational institutions held broadcast licenses, although the number would collapse to fewer than 40 by 1930 as commercial stations acquired spectrum and the regulatory environment favored revenue-generating licensees [2].

The pattern established in this first decade would repeat for a century: educational broadcasting survived not because institutions prioritized it, but because individual advocates -- teachers, professors, engineers with educational convictions -- fought for spectrum access against economic forces that would have consumed every available frequency for commercial use. The 1934 Communications Act created the FCC but provided no reserved spectrum for education. It was not until the FCC's 1945 allocation of the 88.1-91.9 MHz band exclusively for non-commercial educational (NCE) use that educational broadcasting gained a protected home [3].

This spectrum reservation is the single most consequential regulatory act in the history of student radio. Without it, every station discussed in this module -- KNHC, KBPS, KEXP's predecessor, WNAS -- would either not exist or would have been displaced by commercial operators decades ago.

### The Carrier Current Era

Before FM allocation, many campus stations operated as "carrier current" systems -- transmitting low-power AM signals through the building wiring of dormitories and campus structures. These stations had effectively zero range beyond the physical infrastructure, which made them invisible to both the FCC and the general public. The carrier current era (roughly 1936-1965) is almost entirely undocumented from the student experience perspective [4]. Students operated these stations in near-total anonymity, broadcasting to audiences measured in dozens. The contrast with a station like KNHC -- which at its peak broadcast at 30 kW to an estimated 200,000 weekly listeners -- illustrates the extraordinary range of the "student radio" category.

## 2. Land-Grant Stations and the University Model

The Morrill Act of 1862, which created the land-grant university system, established a philosophical foundation that would eventually support educational broadcasting: the principle that publicly funded institutions have an obligation to disseminate knowledge beyond their walls [5]. University radio stations were a natural extension of this principle.

### Key University Stations

| Station | University | Founded | Notable |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|
| WHA | University of Wisconsin | 1917/1921 | Oldest educational station; "The oldest station in the nation" |
| KUOM | University of Minnesota | 1921 | Early agricultural extension programming |
| WBAA | Purdue University | 1922 | Engineering school origins |
| KUOW | University of Washington | 1952 | PNW's primary university station; NPR member |
| KCMU/KEXP | University of Washington | 1972 | Student-run; became independent KEXP in 2001 |

The university model established several patterns that high school stations would later inherit: faculty advisors serving as licensee representatives, student volunteers providing the majority of on-air labor, format decisions reflecting educational mission rather than market research, and chronic underfunding offset by institutional prestige [6].

### The Extension Service Analogy

University radio stations were, in their original conception, the broadcast equivalent of the agricultural extension service -- a mechanism for delivering expert knowledge to a geographically dispersed public. This framing was explicitly invoked in early FCC license applications. What the framing obscured was that students were not merely the labor force operating the delivery mechanism; they were also the recipients of a form of experiential education that no classroom could replicate. The extension service analogy described the output. It said nothing about what the process did to the people running it [7].

This gap -- between the institutional purpose of the station and the developmental experience of the student operating it -- is the central silence that the SVB project addresses.

## 3. The FCC's Class D Experiment

In 1978, the FCC created the Class D license category specifically for 10-watt non-commercial educational stations [8]. The regulatory intent was to provide a low-cost entry point for schools and small community organizations that could not afford the engineering requirements of full-power FM. Class D stations operated at 10 watts with minimal directional requirements, covering a radius of approximately 3-5 miles.

### Class D by the Numbers

- **Power:** 10 watts ERP (effective radiated power)
- **Coverage:** Approximately 3-7 mile radius depending on terrain and antenna height
- **Audience:** Estimated 5,000-25,000 potential listeners in typical suburban deployment
- **Engineering:** Minimal application requirements; no directional antenna mandate
- **Cost:** Initial equipment investment as low as $5,000-$15,000 in 1978 dollars [9]

The Class D category was, in retrospect, the single greatest catalyst for high school radio. It lowered the regulatory and financial barriers to the point where a motivated electronics teacher with district support could build a licensed broadcast facility inside a public school. KNHC began as exactly this kind of project -- Larry Adams, an electronics teacher at Nathan Hale High School, constructed a 100-milliwatt AM station in 1969 and secured an FM Class D license for 10-watt operation in 1971 [10].

### The Class D Paradox

The paradox of Class D was that it created stations powerful enough to be real -- real licenses, real call signs, real FCC obligations -- but small enough to be invisible. A 10-watt station in a suburban neighborhood is audible on a car radio within a few miles of the antenna but is effectively nonexistent beyond that radius. Students operating these stations occupied a liminal space: they were licensed broadcasters subject to federal regulation, but their audiences were often smaller than the student body of the school housing the transmitter.

> **SENSITIVITY NOTE:** The Class D licensing framework operated within the traditional territories of the indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest. Station broadcast footprints in the Seattle metropolitan area encompassed the traditional territories of the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Suquamish, and Snoqualmie peoples. This geographic context is relevant to understanding the community the signal served.

This liminal quality made the developmental experience of Class D student broadcasters fundamentally different from that of students at high-power stations. The first-airshift experience at a 10-watt station is an intimate act; at 30 kW, it is a civic one. Both are undocumented.

### Class D as Laboratory

The Class D station functioned as a broadcasting laboratory in the most literal sense: a controlled environment in which students could experiment with real broadcasting under conditions of minimal consequence. A mistake at a 10-watt station -- dead air, a fumbled ID, an inappropriate comment -- had no practical impact beyond the immediate listening area. The same mistake at a 30 kW station could reach 200,000 listeners and trigger FCC scrutiny.

This laboratory quality meant that Class D stations were, paradoxically, better learning environments for beginners than high-power stations. The low stakes allowed students to fail safely, to experiment with formats and personas, and to develop confidence before the audience scaled. Many of the alumni who later became competent broadcasters at higher-power stations began their careers at Class D stations where the consequences of error were minimal.

The elimination of Class D in 2000 therefore removed not just a license category but a developmental stage. Students entering the broadcasting pipeline after 2000 had no low-stakes entry point. They went directly from zero broadcasting experience to full-power FCC-licensed broadcasting -- a steeper learning curve with higher consequences for error. The implications for the student experience are significant and entirely unexamined in the literature.

## 4. High School Radio Emerges

High school radio stations in the United States represent a distinct institutional category from college stations. While college stations typically operate within communications or journalism departments with professional staff oversight, high school stations emerged from vocational education programs -- electronics classes, media production courses, and (in a few cases) dedicated magnet school programs [11].

### The Oldest High School Stations

| Station | School | City | Founded | Current Status |
|---------|--------|------|---------|----------------|
| KBPS | Benson Polytechnic High School | Portland, OR | 1923 | Active; AM 1450; oldest HS station in US |
| WNAS | New Albany High School | New Albany, IN | 1949 | Active; oldest HS FM station |
| WHHS | Haverford Senior High School | Havertown, PA | 1949 | Active |
| KNHC/C89.5 | Nathan Hale High School | Seattle, WA | 1969 (AM), 1971 (FM) | Active; highest-power HS station in US history |
| KECG | El Cerrito High School | El Cerrito, CA | 1978 | Active; NPR partnership |

The geographic clustering of early high school stations in the Pacific Northwest is notable: KBPS in Portland (1923) and KNHC in Seattle (1971) are among the most significant high school stations in the country, and both emerged from vocational-technical education traditions. The PNW's educational broadcasting culture -- which also produced KUOW and KEXP at the University of Washington -- created an ecosystem in which student broadcasting was normalized rather than exceptional [12].

### KBPS: The Portland Pioneer

KBPS at Benson Polytechnic High School in Portland, Oregon, is the oldest continuously operating high school radio station in the United States. Licensed in 1923 on AM 1450, KBPS has operated for over a century under the governance of the Portland Public Schools district. The station's longevity is attributable to its embedding within a polytechnic curriculum -- radio operation was treated as a trade skill, giving it vocational-education funding protection that purely extracurricular stations lacked [13].

The KBPS model demonstrates that institutional durability in high school radio is directly correlated with curricular integration. Stations that exist as extracurricular activities are vulnerable to budget cuts, administrative indifference, and the departure of their faculty champion. Stations embedded in accredited programs survive because eliminating them requires eliminating the program.

## 5. KEXP and the UW Connection

KEXP's origin story is a college radio story that became something else. The station began in 1972 as KCMU, a 10-watt student-run station at the University of Washington, operating from a closet-sized studio in the UW's Communications Building. For nearly three decades, KCMU was staffed primarily by student volunteers, funded by student fees, and governed by the university's student media board [14].

### The KCMU-to-KEXP Timeline

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1972 | KCMU launches at 10 watts | Student-operated from UW Communications Building |
| 1981 | Power increase to 400 watts | First significant audience growth |
| 1986 | "Audioasis" programming begins | Music curation identity solidifies |
| 1995 | Streaming begins | Among first US stations to stream online |
| 2000 | Paul Allen Foundation gift | $4.2 million; enables independence |
| 2001 | Renamed KEXP; independent 501(c)(3) | Separates from UW governance |
| 2016 | New facility at Seattle Center | 75,000 sq ft; public-facing campus |

The KCMU-to-KEXP transition illustrates both the potential and the fragility of student-originated broadcasting. The station's curatorial identity -- which prioritized musical discovery over format conformity -- was developed by student DJs who had the freedom to play what commercial radio would not. When Paul Allen's foundation provided the financial base for independence, the station retained that curatorial DNA while shedding the institutional constraints of university governance [15].

### What KEXP Preserves and What It Lost

KEXP is, by most measures, a spectacular success: a listener-supported, independent radio station with a global streaming audience, a world-class facility, and a reputation as one of the most important music discovery platforms in the world. What it is not, and cannot be, is a student radio station. The professionalization that enabled KEXP's growth necessarily displaced the amateur, volunteer, learning-by-doing culture that created it.

This is not a criticism -- it is a structural observation. The qualities that make student radio developmentally powerful (low stakes for failure, permission to experiment, the presence of peers at the same learning stage) are incompatible with the qualities that make a professional station excellent (consistency, reliability, production values). The KEXP story matters to this module because it demonstrates that the most valuable product of student radio is not the station -- it is the people the station makes [16].

## 6. The Teaching-Hospital Model

The metaphor that best captures the educational function of high-power student radio stations is the "teaching hospital." In a teaching hospital, student practitioners perform real procedures on real patients under professional supervision. The stakes are genuine. The learning is experiential. The institution accepts a measured degree of risk in exchange for producing competent practitioners [17].

KNHC/C89.5 has been described as "the radio equivalent of a teaching hospital" -- student broadcasters operating real infrastructure (a licensed FCC transmitter) for real audiences (150,000-200,000 weekly listeners at peak power) under professional supervision (a general manager and operations manager employed by the school district) [18].

### What the Metaphor Captures

- **Real stakes:** FCC violations carry real penalties; dead air costs real listeners
- **Professional supervision:** Licensed professionals (General Manager, Operations Manager) oversee student work
- **Graduated responsibility:** Students progress from training shifts to solo on-air to production roles
- **Dual benefit:** The institution gets a functioning station; the student gets irreplaceable experience

### What the Metaphor Misses

The teaching-hospital analogy is accurate for the operational dimension but incomplete for the developmental one. A medical student performing their first supervised procedure is acquiring a professional skill. A seventeen-year-old performing their first solo airshift is doing something more: constructing a public identity during the precise developmental window (Erikson's identity vs. role confusion stage, ages 12-18) when voice, authority, and self-presentation are under active construction [19].

The developmental dimension is entirely absent from the institutional record. The FCC filings document power levels. The school district documents budget allocations. The station website documents programming schedules. What none of these sources capture is the phenomenology of broadcasting -- the felt experience of being the person in the room when the signal goes out.

### The Graduated Responsibility Ladder

The teaching-hospital model at its most effective implements a graduated responsibility ladder that takes students from observers to practitioners over a defined curriculum:

| Stage | Student Role | Supervision Level | Typical Duration |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Orientation | Observer / assistant | Full supervision; no solo tasks | 2-4 weeks |
| Training shift | Board operator under supervision | Direct oversight; errors corrected in real time | 4-8 weeks |
| Supervised solo | On-air with supervisor available | Supervisor in building; available for emergency | 8-12 weeks |
| Independent solo | On-air solo during regular shifts | Professional staff available by phone | Ongoing |
| Senior roles | Music Director, Production, Training | Peer supervision + professional oversight | Final year |

At KNHC, this ladder has evolved organically over decades. The exact structure varies by era and by the professional staff in charge, but the pattern is consistent: students earn increasing autonomy through demonstrated competence. The ladder itself is rarely documented -- it exists as institutional practice rather than written policy -- which makes it a prime target for oral history capture.

The graduated responsibility ladder also creates natural narrative anchors for the interview framework. "Tell me about your first solo shift" maps to the transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3. "When did you start making programming decisions?" maps to the transition from Stage 4 to Stage 5. The ladder provides temporal structure for narrator recall.

## 7. The PNW Educational Broadcasting Ecosystem

The Pacific Northwest has produced a disproportionate concentration of significant educational broadcast stations. This is not accidental. The region's educational broadcasting culture reflects the intersection of several factors: strong public education traditions, early land-grant university investment in extension services, geographic isolation that made local broadcasting valuable, and a political culture receptive to non-commercial public goods [20].

### PNW Educational Broadcasting Map

| Station | Type | City | Power | Notable |
|---------|------|------|-------|---------|
| KUOW | University (NPR) | Seattle, WA | 100 kW | UW's primary public station; professional staff |
| KEXP | Independent (ex-UW) | Seattle, WA | 4.7 kW | Former KCMU; global streaming audience |
| KNHC/C89.5 | High School | Seattle, WA | 8.5 kW | Nathan Hale HS; highest-power HS station in US |
| KBPS | High School (AM) | Portland, OR | 1 kW | Benson Polytechnic; oldest HS station (1923) |
| KBOO | Community | Portland, OR | 28 kW | Volunteer-run since 1968 |
| KAOS | College | Olympia, WA | 1.2 kW | Evergreen State College; freeform |
| KWCW | College | Walla Walla, WA | 0.13 kW | Whitman College |
| OPB | Public (NPR/PBS) | Portland, OR | varies | Oregon Public Broadcasting network |

The ecosystem creates a network of stations at different scales and institutional models, all operating within the same geographic and cultural context. A student at Nathan Hale who develops an interest in broadcasting through C89.5 can observe KUOW's professional model, KEXP's independent model, and KBOO's community model -- all within the same metropolitan area. This density of models is unusual nationally and contributes to the PNW's outsized role in educational broadcasting innovation [21].

### The Community Radio Crossover

Community radio stations like KBOO in Portland and KSVR in Mount Vernon occupy a space between student radio and professional public radio. They share student radio's reliance on volunteer labor and commitment to non-commercial programming, but serve community rather than educational missions. The crossover between these models is significant: many community radio volunteers are former student broadcasters, and the skills transfer is direct [22].

The Pacifica Foundation's influence on PNW community radio is also relevant. KPFK (Los Angeles) and KPFA (Berkeley), both Pacifica stations, established the listener-supported, volunteer-operated model that KBOO and similar stations adopted. The Pacifica model's emphasis on free speech, community access, and non-commercial operation aligns with the educational broadcasting philosophy, creating a pipeline from student station to community station that has operated in the PNW for over fifty years.

## 8. Format Wars and Identity Crises

Every student radio station of significant duration has faced format conflicts -- disputes over what the station should sound like, who decides, and whether the station's mission is educational, entertainment, or community service. These conflicts are rarely documented in institutional records but are central to the student experience [23].

### The KNHC Format Timeline

| Era | Format | Context |
|-----|--------|---------|
| 1971-1978 | Eclectic/Educational | Low-power; curriculum-driven; small audience |
| 1978-1982 | Progressive/Mixed | Growing power; students begin to assert programming identity |
| 1982-1990 | R&B/Urban | "More funk than junk"; rapid audience growth; listener identity emerges |
| 1990-2000 | Dance/Electronic | Transition to electronic dance music; Billboard chart presence |
| 2000-present | Electronic/Dance/Hip-Hop | Digital era; streaming; consistent identity |

The 1982 shift to R&B/Urban programming at KNHC is a pivotal moment that illustrates the intersection of format, identity, and audience. Students chose a format that was underserved by commercial Seattle radio. The decision was made by students, not administrators. The audience response was immediate and significant -- C89.5 became a cultural force in Seattle's music scene precisely because students had the freedom to program what commercial stations would not [24].

Format transitions are experienced by students as personal events. A student who joined C89.5 during the R&B era and witnessed the transition to electronic dance music experienced a format change as a disruption to their broadcast identity. This experiential dimension of format transitions is entirely absent from station histories, which record format changes as programming decisions rather than identity events.

### The Format Authority Question

Who decides the format? This question contains the most politically charged dynamics in student radio. At KNHC, format decisions have been substantially student-driven -- a rare degree of autonomy that reflects the station's institutional commitment to student agency. At other stations, format decisions are made by faculty advisors, professional staff, or school administrators, with student input ranging from meaningful to performative.

The format authority question is directly relevant to the oral history project because it reveals the actual (as opposed to stated) power structure of the station. A narrator who says "we chose the music" is describing a different institutional reality from a narrator who says "the teacher told us what to play." The format authority question also varies by era: early KNHC under Larry Adams may have had more faculty-directed programming; the R&B shift in 1982 was explicitly student-initiated.

### The Music Discovery Pipeline

Student radio stations serve as music discovery engines. Because they are free from commercial format constraints, student DJs can program music that has not been validated by the commercial industry. This freedom creates a pipeline: artists who receive their first radio airplay on student stations sometimes transition to commercial success.

The KNHC pipeline is particularly notable. The station's programming of electronic dance music in the 1990s and 2000s placed it at the leading edge of a genre that would eventually dominate commercial pop. Students at C89.5 were programming Daft Punk, Chemical Brothers, and Fatboy Slim before commercial radio acknowledged their existence. The Lady Gaga booking in 2008 is the most visible example, but the daily programming -- the album tracks, the B-sides, the imports -- represented a continuous curatorial practice that commercial stations could not replicate because their economic model demanded proven hits rather than speculative picks [24].

## 9. The Digital Transition

The digital transition in educational broadcasting occurred in three overlapping phases: digital audio production (mid-1990s), internet streaming (late 1990s), and digital transmission (HD Radio, 2000s). Each phase altered the student experience in ways the institutional record does not capture [25].

### Digital Milestones at KNHC

| Year | Technology | Impact on Student Experience |
|------|-----------|------------------------------|
| 1995 | Digital editing workstations | Students shifted from tape editing to screen-based production |
| 1997 | Internet streaming | Audience became theoretically global; physical broadcast range became irrelevant |
| 2003 | Village Voice recognition | Internet stream brought national attention; NYC listeners |
| 2009 | New digital studios + HD Radio | Full digital workflow; multi-channel capability |
| 2020-2021 | COVID remote operations | Students broadcast from home; studio continuity broken |

The 1997 addition of internet streaming is particularly significant for the student experience. Before streaming, a student's sense of audience was bounded by the physical reach of the transmitter -- a circle of known geography. After streaming, the audience became abstract and global. A student at Nathan Hale could be heard by a listener in New York (as the Village Voice recognition in 2003 demonstrated). The psychological shift from "I am broadcasting to north Seattle" to "I am broadcasting to anyone with an internet connection" has never been studied or documented [26].

### COVID and the Broken Thread

The COVID-19 pandemic interrupted station operations at KNHC and peer stations for approximately 18 months (March 2020 through mid-2021). During this period, the chain of institutional memory -- in which current students train the next cohort through direct mentorship -- was broken. Students who graduated during COVID never trained their successors in person. The 2021 resumption required rebuilding institutional knowledge from scratch in some areas [27].

Operations Manager Drew Bailey described the post-COVID resumption as a moment of institutional renewal: students were "such a big part of it, and it's designed for them." But the 18-month gap represents an irreplaceable loss of oral tradition -- the informal knowledge transfer that occurs between student cohorts in the studio, the hallway, and the control room.

## 10. The Undocumented Interior

The institutional record of educational broadcasting is extensive. FCC license databases, school district archives, station websites, and journalism provide a detailed account of call signs, power levels, format changes, budget allocations, and programming schedules. What this record systematically excludes is the experiential dimension -- what it felt like to be inside the station, behind the console, aware of the signal [28].

### Categories of Undocumented Experience

- **The first airshift:** No first-person account of a student's first solo on-air broadcast exists in the public record for any high school station
- **Audience awareness at scale:** What it felt like to know (or not know) that 200,000 people could hear you
- **The studio as sanctuary:** The station as social space, identity space, or escape from the rest of the school
- **Failure and recovery:** Dead air, wrong carts, technical errors -- the learning-by-doing dimension
- **Format transition as personal event:** Being present when the station's sound changed
- **Student-professional staff dynamics:** Navigating the authority structure of a licensed station while being a minor
- **The signal boundary:** Did students think about where the signal reached? Did they listen from outside?

These categories were identified through analysis of existing documentary sources (see Module 2) and represent the experiential gaps that an oral history methodology (see Module 3, Module 5) is designed to fill.

### Why the Gap Exists

The gap is not accidental. Institutional records are produced by institutions for institutional purposes. FCC filings document compliance. School district archives document expenditure. Station websites document programming. None of these sources has a reason to document what a seventeen-year-old felt when they keyed the microphone for the first time.

The gap is also structural. Students cycle through high school stations in 3-4 year cohorts. By the time alumni might have the perspective and motivation to document their experience, they are years removed from the institution and have no mechanism for contribution. There is no "alumni oral history program" at any high school radio station in the United States [29].

### The Analog-to-Digital Memory Boundary

The experiential gap has a technological dimension that makes documentation increasingly urgent. Students who operated stations in the pre-digital era (before approximately 1995) worked with analog equipment: reel-to-reel tape, cart machines, vinyl turntables, analog mixing consoles with physical faders. Their muscle memory, their sensory experience of the studio, and their operational vocabulary are rooted in physical media that no longer exists in most stations.

Students who operate stations today work with digital audio workstations, software mixing, and file-based playback. The experiential gap between these two groups is not merely temporal -- it is sensory. A student who cued a cart machine experienced a fundamentally different tactile relationship with the medium than a student who drags a file into a playlist. The sounds are different (the click of a cart engaging, the hiss of tape, the thump of a turntable needle), the failures are different (tape jams vs. software crashes), and the recovery techniques are different [30].

This analog-to-digital memory boundary means that the oldest narrator cohorts hold not only the earliest institutional memories but also the last embodied knowledge of an operational mode that is now extinct. When the last student who operated a cart machine at KNHC dies, that entire category of sensory experience is lost permanently.

### The Faculty Champion Problem

The single greatest threat to high school radio station survival is the departure of the faculty champion -- the teacher or advisor who serves as the institutional advocate, the FCC compliance officer, and the bridge between student enthusiasm and administrative tolerance. Across the country, the pattern is consistent: a station operates for decades under the protection of a committed faculty member; that person retires, relocates, or dies; the administration does not replace them; the station goes silent within 2-5 years [31].

KNHC has survived this pattern because the station has professional staff (General Manager, Operations Manager) whose positions are funded by the school district rather than depending on a single teacher's presence. This institutional architecture is unusual among high school stations and represents a critical factor in KNHC's longevity. The transition from Larry Adams (the founding electronics teacher) to a professionalized management structure was a pivotal institutional decision that most peer stations have not made.

The faculty champion problem is relevant to the oral history project because many of the stations that have gone silent took their experiential records with them. Students at defunct stations have no institutional home for their memories, no alumni network to maintain contact, and no mechanism for preservation. The SVB methodology is designed to be applicable not just to active stations like KNHC but to defunct stations whose alumni are still alive -- a much larger and more dispersed population.

### The International Context

While this module focuses on American educational broadcasting (bounded by FCC jurisdiction), high school and secondary-school radio stations exist internationally. Notable examples include:

| Country | Station / Program | Context |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| Australia | Various school stations | Licensed under ACMA; community radio licensing framework |
| United Kingdom | Ofcom school licenses | Short-term RSL (Restricted Service Licence) for school broadcasts |
| Canada | Campus radio (CRTC) | Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission licensing |
| Netherlands | Student stations | Liberal licensing; strong educational broadcasting tradition |

The international context is relevant because it demonstrates that the American model of student radio -- FCC-licensed, spectrum-protected, institutionally embedded -- is not the only possible model. Other countries achieve student broadcasting through different regulatory and institutional frameworks. A comparative oral history project, mapping student broadcasting experience across regulatory environments, would be a natural extension of the SVB methodology [32].
