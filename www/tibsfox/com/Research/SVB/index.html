Overview College Radio C89.5 Model FCC & Licensing Student Stations Media Literacy Mission Pack
There is a category error at the heart of how we document student radio stations. The institutional record is meticulous — FCC license filings, power increase applications, transmitter specifications, budget line items. What it cannot tell you is what it felt like at seventeen to sit behind a console broadcasting at 30 kilowatts across the Puget Sound. The documents record the institution. They do not record the person inside it. The signal travels; something happens in the person who sent it. That something is what oral history is designed to preserve.

Research Modules

M1: College Radio History & Heritage

Heritage Track A · Institutional origins

From crystal sets to streaming: the arc of educational broadcasting in America. Land-grant university stations, 10-watt carriers, the FCC's Class D experiment, and how high school stations like KNHC emerged from the same regulatory soil that produced KEXP, KCRW, and WFMU. The teaching-hospital model and its undocumented interior.

M2: C89.5 as Model Station

Heritage Track A · Primary case study

KNHC/C89.5 at Nathan Hale High School, Seattle: 100 milliwatts AM in 1969 to 30 kW FM covering Puget Sound. Five decades of student broadcasters operating real infrastructure for real audiences. Power milestones, format transitions, the felt experience gap, and why James McKenna's path from C89.5 to co-creating Bill Nye the Science Guy is the outlier that reveals the rule.

M3: Educational Broadcasting & FCC Licensing

Regulatory Track B · License framework

The FCC's non-commercial educational license framework: Class D origins, LPFM expansion, spectrum allocation for schools. How regulatory architecture shapes what students can broadcast, at what power, and under what constraints. The tension between educational mission and commercial viability that defines every student station's operating reality.

M4: Student-Run Stations & Podcast Evolution

Media Track B · Platform shift

The landscape of student-operated broadcast infrastructure: KBPS Portland (1923), WNAS Indiana (1949), KECG El Cerrito, and the current generation. How podcasting democratized audio production, what was gained and what was lost when the transmitter became optional. The PNW student radio ecosystem from Bellingham to Eugene.

M5: Media Literacy & Community Voice

Oral History Track C · Methodology & impact

Media literacy as civic infrastructure: how student broadcasting teaches critical consumption by requiring production. The oral history methodology for capturing youth broadcaster experience. Community radio crossover, the Foxfire principle, and why the felt history of student stations matters more than the institutional record will ever admit.
