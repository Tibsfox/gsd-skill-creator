## 1. Founding

SMOFcon was founded in 1984 as an annual convention specifically for convention organizers. The concept was straightforward: create a dedicated space where the people who run science fiction conventions could meet, share knowledge, discuss problems, and learn from each other -- without the distraction of the conventions they were organizing. No general programming. No dealers' room. No masquerade. Just the people who make conventions happen, talking about how to make conventions happen [1][2].

The founding arose from a recognized need. By the early 1980s, the science fiction convention ecosystem had grown complex enough that running a convention well required skills that fans could not easily acquire on their own. Hotel contract negotiation, programming scheduling, volunteer management, financial planning, safety protocols -- these were specialized competencies that developed through experience and mentoring. The community needed a dedicated venue for transmitting them [1][3].

## 2. The Precursor: Smofcon 0

A precursor convention focused on convention-running had been held in New York City in the early 1970s. This gathering was later retroactively designated "Smofcon 0," acknowledging it as the conceptual ancestor of the formal series. The retroactive numbering is characteristic of fan communities, which value continuity and precedent even when the formal connection is loose [1][2].

The gap between Smofcon 0 and Smofcon 1 -- roughly a decade -- reflects the organic nature of fan institutions. The need for a convention-about-conventions existed for years before the community formalized it. The informal knowledge-sharing happened at room parties, in consuites, and over drinks at other conventions. SMOFcon took what was already happening informally and gave it a name, a date, and a location [1].

## 3. Naming the Convention

The founding committee originally attempted to name the convention "ConCon" -- a convention about conventions. The fan community insisted on "Smofcon," preferring the term that already identified them as a community. The name won because it carried decades of history and humor. "ConCon" was descriptive; "Smofcon" was identity [1][2].

The naming decision reveals something about the community. SMOFs could have chosen a neutral, professional-sounding name. Instead, they chose a name rooted in a conspiracy joke, embracing the self-deprecating humor that defines their culture. The convention was not trying to be respectable. It was trying to be useful -- and it did not need a serious name to accomplish that.

## 4. Scale and Character

SMOFcon is deliberately small. Typical attendance ranges from 100 to 150 people -- a single-track convention that fits in one conference room and a hospitality suite. Smofcon 28 (San Jose, 2010) recorded 154 attendees. Most gatherings are in the same range [1][3].

This scale is not a failure of growth; it is a design choice. The value of SMOFcon lies in direct conversation between experienced convention organizers. A gathering of 150 people all engaged in the same specialized work creates a density of relevant expertise that no larger convention can match. Everyone in the room has run or is running a convention. Everyone speaks the same operational language. The ratio of relevant expertise to total attendees approaches 1:1 [1][3].

By comparison, a typical regional convention might have 500 to 2,000 attendees, of whom perhaps 50 to 100 are actively involved in convention-running. A Worldcon might have 5,000 to 10,000 attendees, of whom perhaps 500 are on the organizing committee. SMOFcon inverts this ratio: everyone present is part of the infrastructure that makes conventions exist.

## 5. The American Decades

For the first 27 years of its existence (1984-2010), every SMOFcon was held in the United States. The convention moved among cities in the northeastern US, the Midwest, and California, typically hosted by established fan organizations in cities with strong convention-running traditions [1][2].

This geographic concentration was not intentional policy -- it reflected the reality that the smof community's critical mass was in the United States, where the majority of major science fiction conventions occurred. The organizational infrastructure (fan organizations willing to host, volunteers willing to run the event, hotels willing to accommodate a small convention in December) existed most reliably in American cities [1].

The US concentration also reflected the economic reality. Attending a convention requires travel, and international travel is more expensive and more complicated than domestic travel. A convention for 100 to 150 people cannot generate enough revenue to subsidize international attendees. The result was a self-reinforcing pattern: the convention was held in the US because that's where most attendees were, and most attendees were American because the convention was held in the US [1][2].

## 6. Key Host Organizations

SMOFcon is organized by independent committees, typically operating under the umbrella of established fan organizations. Several organizations have hosted multiple SMOFcons:

| Organization | Full Name | SMOFcons Hosted | Base |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|------|
| MCFI | Massachusetts Convention Fandom, Inc. | 3, 15, 25, 40 | Boston area |
| SFSFC | San Francisco Science Fiction Conventions, Inc. | 28 (sponsor) | Bay Area |
| SWOC | -- | 41 | Seattle area |

MCFI has been the most prolific host, organizing four SMOFcons over three decades. The Boston area's strong convention-running tradition -- home to multiple Worldcons, Boskone, and other conventions -- provides a deep pool of experienced volunteers and institutional knowledge [1][3].

The hosting model mirrors Worldcon's: each instance is independently organized by a different group. This distributes the burden and prevents any single organization from dominating the convention's direction. It also means that each SMOFcon has a slightly different character, reflecting the personality and priorities of its organizing committee [1].

## 8. The International Turn

The geographic expansion of SMOFcon tells a significant story about the internationalization of convention-running culture:

- **2011: Amsterdam** -- Smofcon 29 was the first SMOFcon held outside the United States. After 27 years as an exclusively American gathering, the convention crossed the Atlantic [1][2].
- **2021: Lisbon** -- Smofcon 39 moved to Portugal, establishing a second European venue in the series [4].
- **2025: Stockholm** -- Smofcon 42 was the first SMOFcon in a Nordic country. Sweden [4][5].
- **2026: Lisbon** -- Smofcon 43 returns to the same Lisbon venue as Smofcon 39, establishing it as a recurring European hub [4].

Three European SMOFcons in six years (2021, 2025, 2026) represents a dramatic acceleration of internationalization after 27 years of US-only gatherings. The pattern reflects both the globalization of the Worldcon community (with recent Worldcons in Helsinki, Dublin, Glasgow, and upcoming bids from Montreal, Brisbane, Nuremberg, and Kigali) and the growing recognition that convention-running knowledge transfer must cross borders [4][5].

The internationalization brings challenges. At Smofcon 42 (Stockholm, 2025), the ConKigali bid team -- proposing a Worldcon in Rwanda -- was denied Swedish visas. The incident highlighted structural barriers facing convention organizers from the Global South in participating in the international convention planning community [5].

## 9. The December Tradition

SMOFcon has traditionally been held on the first weekend of December. The timing is deliberate and functional:

- **Post-convention season.** The major convention season runs from spring through fall. December falls after the last major conventions of the year, giving organizers time to reflect on what they learned.
- **Pre-planning season.** Convention planning for the following year typically intensifies in January. December SMOFcon provides a final opportunity to gather knowledge before the next planning cycle begins.
- **Holiday proximity.** The early December timing avoids direct conflict with holiday travel while taking advantage of year-end flexibility in some attendees' schedules.

The December timing also creates a natural rhythm for the community. SMOFcon is the closing event of the convention year -- the annual debrief, assessment, and knowledge transfer that prepares the community for the next cycle [1][3].
