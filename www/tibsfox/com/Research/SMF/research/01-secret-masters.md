## 1. The Name

"SMOF" stands for "Secret Masters of Fandom." It is a tongue-in-cheek title used by people who organize science fiction conventions to describe themselves. The term predates the SMOFcon convention by roughly two decades and carries a layered history of humor, self-deprecation, and genuine community identity.

The humor works because of its absurdity. Convention organizers are neither secret nor masters. They are volunteers who spend months negotiating hotel contracts, managing programming spreadsheets, and answering emails from attendees who want to know where to park. The "secret" is that convention-running is unglamorous work that most fans never see. The "mastery" is knowing which hotel function rooms have load-bearing pillars that block sightlines and which volunteer shifts are hardest to fill on Sunday morning [1][2].

---

## 2. Etymology and Origin

The phrase "Secret Masters of Fandom" derives from a humorous conspiracy theory that circulated in science fiction fan circles during the early 1960s. The concept -- that a shadowy cabal of powerful fans secretly controlled fandom from behind the scenes -- drew inspiration from Gerald Kersh's 1953 novel *The Secret Masters*, which Bob Tucker referenced in a 1950s fan article [1][2].

The initialism "SMOF" was likely coined by Jack L. Chalker around 1962-1963. Chalker himself indicated the term originated in correspondence with Ted Johnstone (Dave McDaniel) in late 1962 and began spreading through fan circles during 1963. The earliest documented print appearance came in the New York Times on September 6, 1971, in an article covering the World Science Fiction Convention in Boston [2][3].

The term subsequently appeared in the 1973 third edition of Wilson Tucker's *Neo-Fan's Guide to Science Fiction Fandom*, though it was absent from Tucker's original 1955 edition. A "SMOF Award" was presented to Theodore Sturgeon at the 1963 Worldcon, with Forrest J Ackerman accepting on Sturgeon's behalf [1][2].

## 3. The Conspiracy Joke

In its original form, "Secret Masters of Fandom" was pure satire. The joke was that science fiction fandom -- a community of a few thousand people in the 1960s, organized through mimeographed fanzines and regional club meetings -- was secretly controlled by a powerful cabal pulling strings from behind the scenes. The humor lay in the disproportion: the thing being secretly controlled was a volunteer hobby community with no money, no formal hierarchy, and no power worth having [1].

The conspiracy joke resonated because it played on a recognizable truth. In any volunteer community, a small number of experienced people do a disproportionate share of the work. These people naturally develop influence -- not through conspiracy, but through competence and reliability. When you have organized three Worldcons, people listen to your opinion about hotel contracts. When you have served on six convention committees, newcomers ask for your advice. The "secret masters" were simply the people who showed up, did the work, and accumulated expertise. The joke was calling this utterly ordinary social dynamic a "conspiracy" [1][2].

## 4. Semantic Evolution

The term evolved through three distinct phases:

**Phase 1: Conspiracy Humor (1960s).** "SMOF" functioned purely as a joke about powerful fans who supposedly controlled fandom from smoke-filled rooms. It was insider humor -- fans making fun of other fans, and of themselves, for taking a hobby community's politics too seriously.

**Phase 2: Practical Identity (1970s-1980s).** As the number of science fiction conventions proliferated, the work of organizing them became more visible and more valued. "SMOF" attached itself to the practical community of experienced convention problem-solvers: former Worldcon chairs, hotel negotiators, programming directors, and the volunteers who kept the apparatus running. The term shifted from describing a fictional conspiracy to identifying a real community of practice.

**Phase 3: Formalized Community (1984-present).** The creation of the Smofs mailing list and the founding of SMOFcon in 1984 formalized the community identity. "Smof" (lowercase) is now used as both noun and verb: "smoffing" means discussing convention operations. The collective community of convention runners is sometimes called "smofdom." The conspiracy joke has become a professional identity [1][2][4].

## 5. Who SMOFs Are

SMOFs are, overwhelmingly, volunteers. They are not convention industry professionals, event planners, or entertainment executives. They are people with day jobs -- software engineers, teachers, librarians, accountants, retired professionals -- who spend their evenings and weekends organizing events for their community [1][5].

A typical SMOF might chair a regional convention one year, serve as programming head for another convention the next year, volunteer at a third convention's registration desk, and attend SMOFcon in December to discuss what they learned from all three. The same person might serve on a Worldcon bid committee for three years, then spend two years on the organizing committee if the bid wins [1][4].

The commitment is enormous. A Worldcon chair typically spends two to three years on the project, managing hundreds of volunteers and a budget that can reach into six figures. A regional convention chair manages a smaller operation but with fewer people to share the work. The compensation for all of this is exactly zero dollars. Convention organizers receive no pay, no discounts, and often no time to actually attend the convention they organized [1][5].

This is why the "secret masters" joke works. The "power" of running a convention is the power to spend three hundred hours negotiating a hotel contract, solve a registration software crisis at 2 AM on Friday night, and receive a complaint email on Monday about the coffee not being hot enough. The "mastery" is doing all of this well enough that the next convention exists at all.

## 6. The Self-Deprecating Humor

The SMOF identity is defined by self-deprecation. The community that runs science fiction conventions chose to name itself after a mock conspiracy theory about powerful overlords -- and the joke is that convention-running is the least glamorous form of power imaginable.

Jodie Offutt offered a characteristically fannish definition at Discon 2: the real Secret Masters of Fandom were the people who kept convention hotel bathtubs filled with ice and beer. Bruce Pelz once declared himself "Smof #2 -- leaving it to others to fight out who is #1" [1].

The humor serves a function. It deflects the accusation of power-seeking that sometimes attaches to active convention organizers. By calling themselves "secret masters" with obvious irony, SMOFs acknowledge the influence they have while simultaneously undermining any suggestion that they sought it. The name is a defense mechanism: you cannot accuse someone of having a power trip if they are already making fun of themselves for it [1][2].

The humor also builds community. Shared jokes create belonging. When someone calls themselves a "smof," they are signaling membership in a specific community of practice -- people who understand the absurdity and difficulty and satisfaction of volunteer convention-running. The term is both a badge and a punchline.

## 7. The Volunteer Culture

Convention-running in science fiction fandom operates on a pure volunteer model that is unusual in the broader events industry. Professional event planners manage conferences with paid staff, corporate budgets, and industry infrastructure. Fan conventions manage events of comparable complexity with no paid staff, no corporate backing, and no institutional support beyond what the community provides for itself [1][5].

This volunteer model has profound consequences:

**Burnout is systemic.** Convention organizing demands hundreds of hours from people who are not compensated for them. The typical convention organizer has a finite number of conventions in them before exhaustion, frustration, or life changes pull them away. The community's greatest institutional challenge is the continuous loss of experienced volunteers [1][5].

**Knowledge is personal.** Because convention-running is learned through apprenticeship rather than training programs, knowledge accumulates in individuals rather than institutions. When an experienced hotel negotiator retires from fandom, their negotiation skills leave with them. When a programming director burns out, the next one starts from scratch [1][4].

**Authority is earned through service.** In a volunteer community with no formal hierarchy, influence comes from having done the work. A person's opinion about convention management carries weight in proportion to how many conventions they have successfully organized. This is a meritocracy of effort, not credentials [1][5].

## 8. SMOFdom as Community

The community of SMOFs is distinct from the broader community of science fiction fans. While fans attend conventions to enjoy programming, meet authors, buy books, and socialize, SMOFs attend conventions to make them happen. The two communities overlap -- most SMOFs are also fans -- but the SMOF identity is centered on making rather than consuming [1][4].

SMOFdom has its own social structures. The Smofs mailing list provides continuous communication between annual gatherings. SMOFcon provides the face-to-face connection that builds trust and enables mentoring. Regional networks of convention organizers share volunteers, resources, and institutional knowledge. The community spans decades of shared volunteer labor [1][4][5].

The community also has its tensions. Experienced SMOFs sometimes resist new approaches to convention-running. Newcomers sometimes feel excluded by the insider knowledge and social networks they do not yet possess. The balance between preserving institutional memory and welcoming fresh perspectives is a perpetual negotiation -- one that SMOFcon explicitly tries to facilitate [1][5].

Like all volunteer communities, SMOFdom depends on renewal. The people who run conventions today will eventually stop. The question is whether they pass their knowledge to the next generation before they do. This is the challenge that defines both the community and the convention that serves it.
