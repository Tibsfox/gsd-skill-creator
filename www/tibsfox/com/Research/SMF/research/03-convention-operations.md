## 1. The Operational Challenge

Running a science fiction convention is a project management exercise of significant complexity. A typical regional convention involves:

- Negotiating contracts with a hotel for function space, room blocks, and food/beverage service
- Designing and scheduling programming across multiple tracks for multiple days
- Recruiting, training, and scheduling dozens to hundreds of volunteers
- Managing registration, membership databases, and at-door sales
- Operating an art show, dealers' room, and potentially a masquerade
- Coordinating guest of honor logistics including travel, accommodation, and scheduling
- Managing a budget that might range from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars
- Ensuring safety, accessibility, and code of conduct compliance
- Handling crises: medical emergencies, disruptive attendees, facility failures, weather events

All of this is done by volunteers. No one is paid. Many participants hold full-time day jobs and manage convention responsibilities during evenings and weekends over the course of months or years [1][2].

A Worldcon amplifies every dimension. Attendance may reach 5,000 to 10,000 people. The budget may exceed $1 million. The planning cycle spans two to three years. The organizing committee may include hundreds of volunteers across dozens of divisions. The Hugo Award ceremony alone requires its own production team [1][2][3].

SMOFcon exists to teach the people who do this work how to do it better.

## 2. Programming at SMOFcon

SMOFcon programming is entirely focused on the craft of convention-running. There are no author readings, no film screenings, no gaming rooms. Every panel, workshop, and presentation addresses an operational challenge that convention organizers face [1][2].

Typical programming tracks include:

- **Hotel relations** -- contract negotiation, room block management, attrition clauses, food and beverage minimums, function space allocation
- **Programming** -- scheduling algorithms, diversity without tokenization, balancing literary and media content, guest management
- **Registration** -- membership management systems, badge design, at-door vs. advance registration, electronic payment processing
- **Finance** -- revenue projections, expense management, break-even analysis, surplus vs. deficit planning, insurance
- **Marketing** -- reaching new audiences, social media strategy, press relations, community outreach, convention branding
- **Operations** -- day-of logistics, signage, room setup, technical equipment, emergency procedures

Smofcon 25 (Boston, 2007) exemplified the thematic approach, emphasizing marketing and publicity with a keynote by Dr. Gloria Boone on convention branding. The themed approach gave the convention a coherent focus while still including sessions on other operational topics [2][4].

Smofcon 35 (Boston, 2017) featured a two-hour panel on "Diversifying Programme" that addressed one of the most challenging operational problems in convention-running: how to expand the range of perspectives represented in programming without tokenizing participants or creating panels that exist solely to demonstrate diversity [4].

## 3. Hotel Contract Negotiation

Hotel contracts are among the most consequential decisions a convention committee makes, and among the hardest skills to learn without direct experience. The relationship between a convention and its hotel involves:

- **Room blocks** -- the number of sleeping rooms the convention commits to filling. Hotels offer discounted function space rates in exchange for room block commitments. If the convention does not fill its block, it may owe an attrition penalty.
- **Function space** -- the meeting rooms, ballrooms, and pre-function areas used for programming, the art show, the dealers' room, registration, and other convention functions.
- **Food and beverage minimums** -- many hotels require conventions to purchase a minimum amount of catering. This affects programming scheduling (can you hold panels during meal times?) and budget allocation.
- **Contract clauses** -- force majeure, cancellation penalties, early departure fees, complimentary room ratios, rate caps, and dozens of other provisions that can make or break a convention's financial viability.

This knowledge is nearly impossible to acquire from written sources. The Worldcon Runners' Guide addresses hotel relations, but the practical skills -- reading a hotel sales manager's body language, knowing which clauses are negotiable in a given market, understanding when to walk away from a deal -- develop only through experience. SMOFcon is where experienced hotel negotiators share these skills with people facing their first contract [1][2][5].

## 4. Volunteer Management

A convention's volunteers are its primary resource and its greatest vulnerability. Without volunteers, there is no convention. With poorly managed volunteers, the convention exists but fails its attendees [1][2].

Volunteer management at a fan convention involves challenges that professional event managers rarely face:

- **No employment relationship.** Volunteers can leave at any time for any reason. There is no contract, no paycheck, no performance review. The only leverage a convention has is making the experience rewarding enough that people want to continue.
- **Variable skill levels.** Volunteers range from experienced convention runners to first-time participants who have never staffed an event. The same training session must serve both.
- **Scheduling constraints.** Volunteers typically have their own convention they want to attend. Managing shift schedules that allow volunteers to participate in programming, attend events, and still cover their assigned responsibilities is a complex optimization problem.
- **Recognition without hierarchy.** Volunteers need to feel appreciated, but fan convention culture is resistant to formal hierarchies. Finding ways to recognize and thank volunteers without creating tiers of privilege is a cultural challenge.

SMOFcon sessions on volunteer management address these challenges through case studies, shared experiences, and practical techniques. The knowledge is transferred through stories -- this is what we tried, this is what worked, this is what failed [1][2].

## 5. Safety and Code of Conduct

The development of safety policies and codes of conduct at fan conventions has been one of the most significant operational evolutions in recent decades. SMOFcon has been a central venue for discussing these changes [1][4].

Convention safety encompasses:

- **Code of conduct development** -- creating policies that set behavioral expectations, define violations, and establish response procedures. The challenge is writing policies that are clear enough to enforce, broad enough to cover unforeseen situations, and accepted by the community.
- **Response team training** -- volunteers who respond to code of conduct reports need skills in de-escalation, documentation, and decision-making under pressure. These skills are often taught at SMOFcon through workshops and case study discussions.
- **Incident documentation** -- recording what happened, when, involving whom, and what actions were taken. Legal considerations, privacy concerns, and the need for consistent recordkeeping across volunteer shifts all complicate this task.
- **Medical emergency preparedness** -- conventions assemble thousands of people in hotel spaces that may not be optimally designed for events. Medical emergencies happen. Convention staff need to know how to respond, whom to contact, and how to manage the situation until professional help arrives.

The evolution of convention safety practices illustrates the SMOFcon knowledge transfer mechanism. Policies developed at one convention are shared at SMOFcon, adapted by other conventions, refined through experience, and shared again. The cycle is continuous and incremental [1][4].

## 6. Accessibility and Inclusion

Accessibility at fan conventions extends beyond ADA compliance to encompass multiple dimensions:

- **Physical accessibility** -- wheelchair access, seating accommodations, accessible restrooms, elevator access, clear pathways in function spaces
- **Sensory accommodations** -- sign language interpretation, captioning, quiet rooms, reduced-stimulation spaces, consideration for hearing and vision differences
- **Financial accessibility** -- sliding scale memberships, volunteer-in-lieu-of-membership programs, scholarship funds, keeping costs manageable for fans on limited incomes
- **Neurodiversity** -- programming schedules that accommodate different processing styles, clear signage and wayfinding, predictable scheduling, sensory-friendly spaces

SMOFcon sessions on accessibility share practical solutions: which accommodations have the highest impact for the lowest cost, how to survey your community for needs, how to train volunteers in accessibility awareness, and how to build accessibility into convention planning from the start rather than retrofitting it later [1][2][4].

## 7. The Worldcon Q&A

One of SMOFcon's most significant recurring program elements is the Worldcon bid Q&A session. Seated Worldcons (those already selected through WSFS site selection) and active bids present updates and answer questions from an audience of experienced convention runners [2][3].

This functions as an informal accountability mechanism. Bid committees must demonstrate:

- **Organizational readiness** -- they have a committee, a structure, division heads, and a plan
- **Financial viability** -- they have a budget, revenue projections, and contingency plans
- **Facility adequacy** -- the hotel or convention center can accommodate a Worldcon-scale event
- **Community support** -- they have enough volunteers and community backing to execute

The audience asking the questions consists of people who have organized Worldcons, chaired regional conventions, and managed every aspect of convention operations. They know exactly which questions to ask and can evaluate the answers with expert precision [2][3].

At SMOFcon 40 (Providence, 2023), these presentations were recorded and uploaded to YouTube by host MCFI. At SMOFcon 42 (Stockholm, 2025), the Q&A featured presentations from LAcon V (2026 Worldcon), Montreal 2027, and three active 2028 bids: Brisbane, Nuremberg, and ConKigali/Rwanda [3][4].

## 8. The Consuite

The consuite -- convention suite -- is a hospitality area providing continuous food and drinks, funded by the convention and often supplemented by sponsoring conventions and organizations. It is a distinctive feature of fan convention culture, and at SMOFcon it serves a specific function [4].

The consuite is where the informal work of SMOFcon happens. The formal programming provides structure -- panels, workshops, presentations. But the most valuable knowledge transfer often happens in the consuite at 11 PM, when an experienced hotel negotiator shares stories with a first-time convention chair over coffee. The consuite is the convention's social infrastructure, the space where relationships form and deepen [4].

At Smofcon 35 (Boston, 2017), Dublin 2019 and Arisia sponsored portions of the consuite food. This sponsorship model -- where conventions with resources support the gathering that serves the broader community -- exemplifies the gift economy that underlies smofdom [4].

## 9. The Zoo Con

Some SMOFcons feature a "zoo con" -- a role-playing simulation game where participants take on convention committee roles and navigate scenarios requiring real-time decision-making. A participant might be assigned the role of convention chair, hotel liaison, or safety team lead, then presented with a crisis: the keynote speaker just cancelled, a water main broke in the dealers' room, or a registered attendee has been reported for harassment [4].

The zoo con is one of the few formal mechanisms for training new convention organizers outside of actual convention work. It compresses months of committee experience into hours of gameplay, allowing participants to practice decision-making, delegation, and crisis response in a low-stakes environment. Mistakes in a zoo con are learning opportunities; mistakes at a real convention affect real people [4].

## 10. Dealing with Problem Attendees

Every convention eventually encounters attendees who violate the code of conduct, disrupt programming, or create unsafe situations for other attendees. Managing these situations is one of the most difficult skills in convention-running, and one of the hardest to teach outside of direct experience [1][2].

The challenge involves multiple competing priorities:

- **Safety of other attendees** -- the primary obligation
- **Fairness to the accused** -- due process, even in a volunteer context
- **Privacy** -- handling sensitive situations discretely
- **Legal considerations** -- liability, harassment law, defamation risk
- **Volunteer limitations** -- convention staff are not law enforcement and should not pretend to be

SMOFcon sessions on this topic draw on accumulated experience from dozens of conventions. The discussions are frank, detailed, and intensely practical -- what worked, what failed, what they would do differently. This is knowledge that genuinely cannot be learned from a manual [1][2].

## 11. The Skills That Don't Transfer in Writing

The most valuable knowledge exchanged at SMOFcon is the kind that resists documentation. Written guides can explain hotel contract terms, but they cannot teach you how to read a hotel sales manager's priorities during negotiation. Written policies can define code of conduct violations, but they cannot prepare you for the emotional weight of telling a long-time community member they are being expelled from the convention. Volunteer management guides can recommend shift scheduling templates, but they cannot teach you how to recognize that a volunteer is about to burn out and needs to be relieved before they collapse [1][2][5].

This is tacit knowledge -- the kind that lives in experience, develops through practice, and transfers through mentoring. SMOFcon is the community's primary mechanism for making tacit knowledge transfer happen at scale. By assembling 100 to 150 experienced convention organizers in one room for a weekend, SMOFcon creates the conditions for the kind of learning that no documentation can replace.

The convention is, in this sense, a technology for knowledge transmission. Not an efficient one -- gathering 150 people in a hotel for a weekend is expensive and excludes everyone who cannot attend. But an effective one, because the knowledge it transfers is the knowledge that matters most: judgment, relationship, and the accumulated wisdom of thousands of conventions worth of experience.
