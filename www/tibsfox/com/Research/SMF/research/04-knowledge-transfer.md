## 1. The Fragility of Convention Knowledge

Convention-running knowledge is fragile because it exists primarily in the minds of the people who do the work. The skills required to organize a multi-day event for hundreds or thousands of people -- hotel negotiation, programming scheduling, volunteer management, crisis response, financial planning -- are learned through experience rather than study. An experienced convention chair carries decades of institutional knowledge that no document fully captures [1][2].

Consider what an experienced hotel negotiator knows:

- Which contract clauses are standard and which are negotiable in the current hotel market
- Which hotel chains are convention-friendly and which treat conventions as low-priority business
- How to read a sales manager's priorities and adjust the negotiation accordingly
- What the actual consequences are when a convention fails to meet its room block commitment
- Which competing events affect hotel pricing in specific cities during specific months

None of this is written down in any comprehensive way. It exists in the accumulated experience of people who have negotiated twenty, thirty, fifty hotel contracts over the course of decades. When those people retire from fandom, their knowledge retires with them [1][2][3].

## 2. Why Volunteers Leave

Convention-running is entirely volunteer work, and volunteer work has a natural lifecycle. The community loses experienced organizers through multiple pathways:

**Burnout.** Convention organizing demands hundreds of hours from people who receive no compensation. A Worldcon chair typically spends two to three years managing the project alongside a full-time job and personal responsibilities. The emotional toll of managing volunteer conflicts, handling crises, and absorbing complaints wears people down. Many experienced convention runners reach a point where they simply cannot do it anymore [1][2].

**Life changes.** People move to new cities, change careers, have children, develop health conditions, or shift their priorities. Fandom is a leisure activity, and when life demands more, leisure gives way.

**Generational succession.** The smof community skews older. The founding generation of SMOFcon participants is aging, and recruiting new convention runners -- training them, integrating them into the social network of trust, bringing them up to operational competence -- is a recognized challenge that SMOFcon explicitly tries to address, with mixed results [1][2].

**Conflict.** Fan communities, like all communities, experience interpersonal conflicts. Convention organizing intensifies these conflicts because the stakes are tangible: real money, real events, real consequences for real people. Some experienced organizers leave because the conflicts become intolerable.

## 3. What Gets Lost

When an experienced convention runner leaves the community, the loss is not just their labor. It is their knowledge -- the accumulated judgment, relationships, and institutional memory that made their labor effective.

Specific categories of knowledge at risk:

- **Vendor relationships.** Years of working with specific hotels, AV companies, printers, and caterers build trust and negotiating leverage. When the person who maintained those relationships leaves, the next convention starts from zero.
- **Institutional precedent.** Knowing why a particular policy was adopted, what problem it solved, and what alternatives were considered. Without this context, the next committee may revisit settled questions or repeat mistakes that were already corrected.
- **Crisis response patterns.** Knowing what to do when things go wrong -- not from a manual, but from having been there. The volunteer who has already handled a medical emergency in the dealers' room brings calm competence that no training can fully replicate.
- **Community context.** Knowing the community's history, its fault lines, its sensitivities, and its strengths. Knowing which attendee relationships are supportive and which are volatile. Knowing the community well enough to anticipate problems before they materialize.

## 5. The Worldcon Runners' Guide

The Worldcon Runners' Guide (WCRG) is maintained by a standing committee of the WSFS Business Meeting -- the Worldcon Runners' Guide Editorial Committee (WRGEC). It compiles best practices for Worldcon organization covering bidding, facilities, budgeting, publications, registration, WSFS functions, programming, events, and exhibits [3].

The guide is structured as a wiki at wcrg.conrunner.net, with parallel PDF sections on wsfs.org. It includes a distinctive mechanism for handling disagreement: recommended approaches are stated, "dissenting views" are noted with the names of key partisans, and options historically proven disastrous are explicitly flagged [3].

Despite this structure, the guide is perpetually under revision. The WSFS website notes that the guide "is in the process of being updated" and provides older PDF versions as interim resources. This perpetual incompleteness is not a failure of the editors -- it reflects the fundamental challenge of documenting a moving target. Convention-running practices evolve continuously, and the guide can never be fully current [3].

## 6. The ConRunner Wiki

The ConRunner wiki (conrunner.net) is a broader resource not limited to Worldcon, covering convention operations generally. It includes articles on WSFS, Worldcon, smof terminology, and various operational topics. The "Smof" article alone has been accessed over 16,000 times as of 2022 [4].

The wiki's strength is its accessibility -- anyone can read it, and the format allows incremental improvement. Its weakness is the same as any wiki: coverage depends on who writes, and many experienced convention runners do not write documentation. The knowledge that most needs documenting is held by people who are too busy running conventions to document it.

## 7. Fancyclopedia 3

Fancyclopedia 3 (fancyclopedia.org) is a comprehensive fan-historical encyclopedia with over 71,000 active pages as of 2025. It documents conventions, fan organizations, terminology, people, and publications. Individual SMOFcon entries provide dates, locations, chairs, attendance figures, and hosting organizations [1].

Fancyclopedia serves as the fan-historical record of reference. It captures what happened -- dates, names, places, organizational details. What it cannot capture is how things happened: the negotiations, the conflicts, the judgment calls, the lessons learned. It is the ledger; SMOFcon is the oral history.

---

## 8. The Smofs Mailing List

The Smofs mailing list is a conrunners email list specifically for Worldcon management discussion. It predates modern social media and facilitated coordination that was previously possible only at face-to-face gatherings. The list is referenced consistently across fan-historical sources as a key communication channel for the SMOF community [1][4].

The mailing list represents an intermediate layer between face-to-face knowledge transfer and formal documentation. Conversations on the list capture some of the nuance and context that formal documents miss, while being more persistent than in-person conversations. However, mailing list archives are typically not publicly accessible, limiting their value as a knowledge resource for newcomers.

## 9. File 770 as Journal of Record

Mike Glyer's File 770 (file770.com) functions as the journal of record for fan news, including detailed coverage of SMOFcon proceedings, Worldcon bids, WSFS governance developments, and community discussions. Its "smofcon" tag aggregates years of reporting [5].

File 770 fills a specific niche in the knowledge ecosystem: contemporaneous reporting on events and decisions. Convention proceedings are documented while they are still fresh, governance decisions are reported with context, and community developments are recorded for future reference. For fan historians and researchers, File 770 is often the most reliable source for understanding what happened at a specific SMOFcon or during a specific governance debate.

## 10. Scholarship and Bursary Programs

Multiple organizations fund attendance at SMOFcon, recognizing that financial barriers limit knowledge transfer:

| Program | Amount | Details |
|---------|--------|---------|
| SFSFC Scholarships | $500 each | Operating since at least 2004; two awards per year; targets promising convention runners |
| CanSMOF Scholarships | Varies | Canadian smof organization; funded attendance for international convention organizers |
| Glasgow 2024 Bursaries | Travel + hotel + membership | Up to 18 bursaries for Smofcon 42; open to all backgrounds and experience levels |

These programs address a structural problem: the people who most need SMOFcon's knowledge transfer -- new convention organizers, international participants, convention runners from smaller communities -- are often the people least able to afford attendance. Scholarship programs partially bridge this gap, but demand consistently exceeds supply [2][5][6].

## 11. Oral Tradition vs. Codified Knowledge

The tension between oral tradition and codified knowledge is the central challenge of convention-running knowledge management. Each mode has distinct strengths and limitations:

**Oral tradition strengths:**
- Transmits tacit knowledge -- judgment, intuition, situational awareness
- Adapts to context -- the teacher adjusts the lesson to the learner's situation
- Builds relationships -- knowledge transfer happens through human connection, creating the trust networks that enable future collaboration
- Captures nuance -- the "it depends" that written rules cannot accommodate

**Oral tradition weaknesses:**
- Requires physical proximity -- you must be in the same room as the knowledge holder
- Limited scalability -- one person can mentor a handful of people, not hundreds
- Vulnerability to disruption -- if the knowledge holder leaves, the knowledge leaves
- Selection bias -- who gets mentored depends on who knows whom, creating access inequities

**Codified knowledge strengths:**
- Persistent -- documents survive the departure of their authors
- Scalable -- one document can serve unlimited readers
- Accessible -- available to anyone who can find it, regardless of social connections
- Auditable -- what is written can be reviewed, corrected, and improved

**Codified knowledge weaknesses:**
- Loses context -- written rules cannot capture the judgment that applies them
- Quickly outdated -- convention practices evolve faster than documents are updated
- Incomplete by nature -- the most valuable knowledge is the hardest to write down
- Assumes a reader -- documentation helps only those who know to look for it

SMOFcon's role is to bridge this gap. It operates primarily in the oral tradition mode, but it creates conditions that partially compensate for oral tradition's weaknesses: annual frequency, inclusive attendance (aided by scholarships), recorded sessions (increasingly), and a culture of explicit knowledge sharing [1][2].

## 12. Parallels to Other Knowledge Challenges

The SMOFcon knowledge problem is not unique to fandom. It mirrors challenges in multiple domains:

**Indigenous knowledge systems.** Cultural knowledge transmitted through oral tradition is vulnerable to generational disruption. Codification risks losing the context that makes it meaningful. The relationship between elder knowledge-keepers and younger learners parallels the SMOF mentoring dynamic [7].

**Trade skills and craftsmanship.** Apprenticeship-based learning creates expertise that formal education cannot replicate. A master carpenter's feel for wood grain, a chef's judgment of seasoning, a blacksmith's sense of heat -- these are tacit skills that develop through guided practice, not reading [7].

**Institutional memory in organizations.** When key personnel depart, organizations lose knowledge that documentation cannot fully prevent. The "bus factor" -- how many people must be hit by a bus before a project or organization fails -- is a recognized measure of institutional fragility [7].

**Open source software communities.** The sustainability of volunteer-driven software projects depends on knowledge transfer between contributors. Projects with good documentation, mentoring programs, and onboarding processes survive contributor turnover. Projects that depend on a small number of key contributors are fragile [7].

In each case, the fundamental challenge is the same: valuable knowledge that lives in people rather than documents, transmitted through relationships rather than reading, and vulnerable to the departure of key individuals. SMOFcon is one community's answer to this challenge -- an annual gathering designed to make the oral tradition more resilient by making it more frequent and more inclusive.
