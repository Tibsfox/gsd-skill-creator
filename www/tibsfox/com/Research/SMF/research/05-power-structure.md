## 1. The Informal Governance Layer

SMOFcon occupies a specific position in the governance architecture of science fiction fandom. It is not a decision-making body. It has no formal authority over any convention, organization, or process. It cannot approve or reject Worldcon bids, amend the WSFS Constitution, or direct any convention committee [1][2].

What it does is more subtle and, in many ways, more influential. SMOFcon is the venue where the people who participate in fan governance -- who vote in site selections, attend Business Meetings, serve on convention committees, and administer the Hugo Awards -- gather to discuss, argue, inform, and prepare. It is the space between formal decisions where the groundwork for those decisions is laid [1][2].

This makes SMOFcon simultaneously essential and invisible. The formal governance processes -- site selection votes, Business Meeting motions, constitutional amendments -- are documented and public. The informal conversations at SMOFcon that inform those processes are not. The result is a governance layer that is widely understood within the community but opaque to outsiders.

## 2. WSFS: The Unincorporated Society

The World Science Fiction Society (WSFS) is an unincorporated literary society with no permanent officers, board of directors, chair, or president. Its membership consists of all members of the current year's Worldcon. Authority is exercised through the annual WSFS Business Meeting held at each Worldcon [2][3].

This governance model is unique in the convention world. Most convention organizations -- even fan-run ones -- have a board of directors, a set of officers, and some form of institutional continuity between events. WSFS has none of these. Each Worldcon is organized by an independently incorporated committee, typically a 501(c)(3) non-profit in the US or a company limited by guarantee in the UK. These committees operate autonomously within the framework of the WSFS Constitution [2][3].

The WSFS Constitution governs three primary domains:
- **Hugo Award administration** -- categories, eligibility, nomination and voting procedures
- **Site Selection** -- how Worldcon locations are chosen, geographic restrictions, voting mechanics
- **Business Meeting procedure** -- how the Constitution itself is amended, which requires a two-year ratification cycle

Everything else -- programming, guests of honor, budget, marketing, staffing -- is left to each Worldcon committee's discretion [2][3].

## 3. The Business Meeting

The WSFS Business Meeting is held at each Worldcon, typically in three morning sessions on successive days. It is open to all attending members of the Worldcon. Business conducted includes:

- **Constitutional amendments** -- proposed at one year's Business Meeting, ratified (or not) at the next year's Business Meeting. This two-year cycle ensures that no single year's attendees can unilaterally change the rules.
- **Standing rules** -- procedural rules that can be adopted or modified by a single Business Meeting
- **Committee reports** -- including the Mark Protection Committee, the Worldcon Runners' Guide Editorial Committee, and other standing and special committees
- **Site selection results** -- the announcement of the next Worldcon site, based on member vote

The Business Meeting operates under parliamentary procedure, which creates both transparency and complexity. The rules ensure that any member can participate, but the procedural knowledge required for effective participation creates a de facto barrier. Experienced Business Meeting participants -- who understand how to propose motions, offer amendments, raise points of order, and navigate the procedural landscape -- have disproportionate influence [2][3].

SMOFcon is where much of this procedural knowledge is transmitted. Understanding how the Business Meeting works, what amendments are likely to be proposed, and how the political landscape is shifting -- this is the kind of knowledge that circulates at SMOFcon and enables effective participation at Worldcon [1][2].

## 4. Site Selection Mechanics

Worldcon sites are selected two years in advance by secret ballot of WSFS members. The mechanics are specific [2][3]:

- **Eligibility to vote:** Must hold a membership in the administering Worldcon and purchase an Advance WSFS Membership in the convention being selected
- **Bid eligibility:** The proposed site must be at least 500 miles (800 km) from the administering convention
- **Ballot format:** "None of the Above" appears on every ballot. If it wins, the Business Meeting selects the site
- **Electronic voting:** Has been explored but requires unanimous agreement of all filed bids
- **Pre-selection presentations:** Bids present at the Worldcon and, increasingly, at SMOFcon

The site selection process is democratic in form but informed by expertise. Any WSFS member can vote. But the information that informs those votes -- which bids are organizationally ready, which facilities are adequate, which committees have the depth of volunteer support -- flows disproportionately through the SMOF community [2][3].

## 5. The Mark Protection Committee

The Mark Protection Committee (MPC) is the only permanent standing committee of WSFS. It is responsible for maintaining trademarks including "Worldcon," "WSFS," "Hugo Award," and "NASFiC." Additional standing committees include the Nitpicking and Flyspecking Committee, the WRGEC, and the Hugo Eligibility Rest of the World Committee (HEROW) [2][3].

The MPC's role is fundamentally protective -- ensuring that the names and marks associated with fan governance remain under community control rather than being appropriated by commercial interests or unauthorized parties. This protective function reflects a broader principle in WSFS governance: the community's institutional assets belong to the community, not to any individual convention committee [2][3].

## 6. Where Worldcon Bids Are Discussed

The Worldcon bid cycle is long and complex. A typical bid might develop over three to five years:

1. **Concept phase:** A group of fans identifies a potential host city and begins informal discussions
2. **Pre-bid phase:** The group forms a committee, identifies a venue, and begins building community support
3. **Active bidding:** The bid files with the current Worldcon, begins formal presentations, and campaigns for votes
4. **Site selection:** Members of the current Worldcon vote
5. **Post-selection:** The winning bid transitions from bid committee to organizing committee

SMOFcon is critical during phases 2 and 3. Bid committees present their plans, answer questions, and gauge community support. The Worldcon Q&A session at SMOFcon is where bids face their first serious scrutiny from experienced convention runners [1][4].

At SMOFcon 42 (Stockholm, 2025), the Q&A featured presentations from:
- **LAcon V** -- the seated 2026 Worldcon in Los Angeles
- **Montreal 2027** -- the seated 2027 Worldcon
- **Three 2028 bids:** Brisbane (Australia), Nuremberg (Germany), and ConKigali (Rwanda)
- **Expressions of interest** extending as far as 2035

The host noted the relevance of the approaching Worldcon centennial in 2039 -- 100 years since the first World Science Fiction Convention in New York in 1939 [4][5].

## 7. Westercon Site Selection

Westercon uses a similar site selection model: member vote, competitive bids, advance selection. SMOFcon is where Westercon bid politics happen alongside Worldcon discussions. The same community of experienced convention runners evaluates Westercon bids, and many of the same individuals are involved in both governance systems [6].

Westercon's geographic restriction (west of the 104th meridian or Hawaii) creates a smaller competitive landscape than Worldcon's global scope. But the operational challenges are the same: assembling a volunteer committee, securing a venue, building community support, and demonstrating organizational readiness [6].

## 8. The Tension: Democracy vs. Expertise

The relationship between SMOFcon and the formal governance processes it supports creates a persistent tension. Site selection is democratic: every member gets one vote. But the information that informs those votes flows disproportionately through a small community of experienced convention runners who gather at SMOFcon [1][2].

This is not corruption. It is the natural consequence of a governance system where complex decisions are made by a broad electorate that varies in its level of operational knowledge. A voter who has attended SMOFcon, heard bid presentations, and asked detailed questions about hotel contracts and committee structure will cast a more informed vote than a voter who has only read a bid's marketing materials [1][2].

The tension is manageable as long as SMOFcon remains accessible -- as long as anyone who wants to participate in the informal governance layer can do so. The scholarship programs, the recorded sessions, the virtual participation initiatives, and the internationalization of the convention's geographic footprint all serve to broaden access to the knowledge that informs governance [4][5].

The tension becomes problematic if SMOFcon becomes exclusionary -- if the informal governance layer closes to outsiders, creating a de facto oligarchy within a de jure democracy. The smof community is generally aware of this risk, and the discussions about accessibility, bursary programs, and virtual participation reflect that awareness [4][5].

## 9. SMOFcon as Back-Channel

Every governance system has a back-channel -- an informal space where participants discuss decisions before formal proceedings. In corporate governance, it is the boardroom dinner. In politics, it is the caucus. In WSFS governance, it is SMOFcon [1][2].

The back-channel serves legitimate functions:
- **Information sharing:** Bid committees learn what questions voters will ask and prepare better answers
- **Coordination:** Potential constitutional amendments are discussed informally before being formally proposed, allowing proponents and opponents to understand each other's positions
- **Mentoring:** New convention organizers learn how governance works from experienced participants
- **Community building:** The relationships formed at SMOFcon create the trust networks that enable effective collaboration in formal governance settings

The back-channel also carries risks:
- **Exclusion:** Those who cannot attend SMOFcon are excluded from the informal information flow
- **Groupthink:** A small, cohesive community may converge on positions that do not represent the broader fandom
- **Insider advantage:** Experienced SMOFs may have disproportionate influence over outcomes

The community manages these risks imperfectly but consciously. The recorded Q&A sessions, the scholarship programs, the virtual participation experiments, and the deliberate welcoming of new attendees are all mechanisms for mitigating the insider-advantage problem [4][5].

## 10. Accessibility and the Power Question

The question of who has access to SMOFcon is, ultimately, a question about who has access to the informal governance of fandom. Every barrier to SMOFcon attendance -- financial, geographic, linguistic, visa-related -- is a barrier to participation in the informal governance layer [4][5].

The ConKigali visa denial at SMOFcon 42 illustrated this starkly. A bid committee proposing a Worldcon in Rwanda -- representing a significant expansion of Worldcon's geographic and cultural reach -- was unable to participate in the informal governance venue where bids are evaluated. The formal site selection process remained open to their bid, but they were excluded from the informal back-channel that shapes how bids are perceived [5].

This is not a problem that SMOFcon can solve on its own. It is a structural issue embedded in global patterns of mobility, wealth, and privilege. But the smof community's response -- expanded bursary programs, virtual participation, geographic diversification of the convention -- demonstrates an awareness that the power question cannot be separated from the accessibility question.
