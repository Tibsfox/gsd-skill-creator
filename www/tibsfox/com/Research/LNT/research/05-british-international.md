# British & International Traditions (1971-Present)

## Michael Parkinson: The Interview as Art

Michael Parkinson's *Parkinson* ran on BBC One from 1971 to 1982 and again from 1998 to 2007, making him the longest-running chat show host in British history. Unlike the American model, Parkinson came from a journalism background and approached celebrity interviews with a reporter's rigor.

Hello! Magazine describes him as "often dubbed the greatest British talk show host." His four Muhammad Ali interviews and Tony Blair Iraq invasion interview became landmark television moments. The final episode in 2007 attracted over eight million viewers.

## Terry Wogan's Warmth

Terry Wogan's BBC talk show (1982-1992) brought Irish warmth and gentle subversiveness to a mainstream BBC audience, regularly achieving audiences of 8-10 million. Wogan's style was deliberately "anti-showbiz" compared to American models -- a warmth that masked a sharp intelligence and willingness to let silences do the work.

## Jonathan Ross's Letterman-Inspired Irreverence

Jonathan Ross deliberately modeled his approach on David Letterman rather than the polite British tradition. His BBC show *Friday Night with Jonathan Ross* (2001-2010) earned him a reported 6 million pounds annual salary and made him the BBC's most-valued broadcaster.

His 2008 controversy with Russell Brand (leaving obscene messages for actor Andrew Sachs) led to his departure from the BBC and move to ITV's *The Jonathan Ross Show*, demonstrating that the boundary-pushing approach borrowed from American late night could have different consequences in the British broadcasting ecosystem.

## Graham Norton's Ensemble Revolution

Graham Norton's fundamental innovation is structural: rather than the American model of one guest at a time, Norton brings all guests out simultaneously, places them on a single sofa, and facilitates lateral conversation between them.

British Brief (2025) notes that the show's producer has revealed the precise "hierarchy" governing celebrity seating, with the sofa spot closest to Norton representing the primary aim for publicists. The show airs weekly (not nightly) -- a key structural difference that allows for higher-profile guest lineups.

## Structural Differences: British vs. American

The British chat show tradition differs from its American counterpart in several fundamental ways:

- **Weekly vs. nightly:** British shows air weekly, allowing higher-profile booking and more preparation
- **Single vs. sequential guests:** Norton's ensemble format creates unpredictable chemistry
- **Journalism vs. entertainment:** British hosts (Parkinson, Ross) often come from journalism backgrounds
- **Public broadcaster vs. commercial:** BBC funding removes direct advertiser pressure
- **Tone:** British shows tend toward conversational warmth; American shows toward performative energy

## International Variants

Beyond the UK, the chat show format has been adapted across many cultures:

- **Australia:** Andrew Denton, Rove McManus
- **Ireland:** The Late Late Show (RTE, 1962-present, the world's longest-running late night show)
- **Canada:** George Stroumboulopoulos
- **Germany:** Harald Schmidt, Markus Lanz

Each national variant adapts the core format to local broadcast traditions, audience expectations, and cultural norms around celebrity, politics, and humor.
