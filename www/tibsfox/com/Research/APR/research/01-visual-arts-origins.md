## 1. The First 40,000 Years

Human mark-making begins in the deep past. The oldest symbolic marks -- pierced shells and ochre-etched geometry -- come from Blombos Cave, South Africa, approximately 75,000 years old. The oldest confirmed figurative cave paintings are found in the caves of Maros, Sulawesi, Indonesia, dating to more than 40,000 years ago. Art, music, and ritual were a unified practice long before civilization began to differentiate them.

The technology of art begins with pigment: red and orange from iron oxides (hematite, ochre), black from manganese dioxide and charcoal. Paint was applied with fingers, crude brushes of hair or moss, and blown from the mouth through bone tubes over hand stencils. The medium dictated the method.

## 2. Paleolithic Cave Art

Cave paintings represent the earliest confirmed figurative art in the human record. The caves of Europe and Indonesia provide the primary evidence, though new discoveries continue to push dates back and geography outward.

**Pigment Technology:**

- **Red/Orange:** Iron oxides (hematite, ochre) -- the earliest pigments
- **Black:** Manganese dioxide, charcoal -- used for outlines and detail
- **Application:** Fingers, moss brushes, bone-tube blow pipes (hand stencils)
- **Binders:** Animal fat, plant sap, water -- mixed with pigment on stone palettes

**Purpose Theories:**

- Ceremonial and shamanic ritual (cave acoustics suggest chant)
- Educational and navigational mapping of animal species
- Hunting magic -- sympathetic representation of prey
- Social bonding and identity marking within groups

Many painted chambers have extraordinary acoustic properties, suggesting that sound -- chant, percussion, vocalization -- accompanied the images. The caves were not galleries; they were theaters.

## 4. Sculptural Forms

**The Lion-Man of Hohlenstein-Stadel** (Germany, c. 40,000 BCE, carved mammoth ivory) is the oldest confirmed composite imaginary creature -- a human body with a lion's head. It demonstrates that imagination, symbolic thought, and sculptural technique existed together at the earliest confirmed dates of figurative art.

**Venus Figurines** -- Willendorf, Laussel, Dolni Vestonice -- represent stylized female forms of uncertain but likely ceremonial significance. The Venus of Willendorf (c. 25,000 BCE) was carved from oolitic limestone not local to the find site, indicating trade or migration networks.

## 5. Ancient Egyptian Art

Egyptian art (c. 3100-30 BCE) was distinguished by remarkably consistent formal conventions maintained over three thousand years:

- **Hierarchical scaling:** Important figures drawn larger
- **Composite perspective:** Head and legs in profile, torso frontal
- **Subordination of individuality:** The artist was a skilled craftsperson executing established visual language
- **Function:** Religious, dynastic, and funerary -- art served power and the afterlife

Key monuments: the Great Pyramids of Giza, the tomb paintings of the Valley of the Kings, the bust of Nefertiti (Amarna period, c. 1345 BCE -- a rare break from convention), and the Book of the Dead papyrus scrolls.

## 6. Mesopotamian Art

Mesopotamian art (c. 3500-539 BCE) encompassed Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, and Assyrian traditions. Art functioned primarily to glorify divine kingship.

- **Stele of Hammurabi** (c. 1754 BCE): Law code inscribed on basalt; king receiving authority from the sun god Shamash
- **Assyrian palace reliefs** of Nineveh: Massive narrative stone panels depicting military campaigns, lion hunts, and royal rituals
- **Ziggurat architecture** of Ur: Stepped temple platforms connecting earth to heaven
- **Cylinder seals:** Miniature carved cylinders producing repeating impressions -- portable art and administrative tools

## 7. Ancient Greek Art

Greek art (c. 900-31 BCE) underwent a dramatic evolution:

- **Archaic period:** Geometric abstraction, kouros and kore figures with the "archaic smile"
- **Classical period** (5th century BCE): Idealization of the human figure (Phidias, Praxiteles, Polykleitos). The Canon of Polykleitos codified proportional systems.
- **Hellenistic period:** More emotionally expressive, dynamic compositions (Laocoon Group, Venus de Milo, Nike of Samothrace)

The Greeks codified the proportional systems of the human figure that the Renaissance would rediscover 1,500 years later. Greek pottery (black-figure and red-figure technique) provides the most complete visual record of daily life in the ancient world.

## 8. Roman Art

Roman art (c. 509 BCE-476 CE) absorbed and extended Greek models, adding particular strengths:

- **Portraiture:** Veristic (hyper-realistic) portrait busts -- warts, wrinkles, and all
- **Narrative relief:** Trajan's Column (113 CE) -- a continuous spiral relief 200 meters long depicting the Dacian Wars
- **Mosaic and fresco:** The Villa of the Mysteries at Pompeii preserves complete painted rooms
- **Architectural decoration:** The Ara Pacis (Altar of Peace) demonstrates mastery of narrative pictorial space

## 9. African, Asian, and Pre-Columbian Origins

Artistic traditions developed simultaneously and independently across the globe:

- **Saharan rock art** (Tassili n'Ajjer, Tadrart Acacus): Records pastoralist societies from 14,000 years ago
- **Nok terracotta** (Nigeria, c. 1500 BCE-500 CE): Earliest large-scale figurative sculpture in sub-Saharan Africa
- **Chinese Shang Dynasty bronzes** (c. 1600-1046 BCE): Ritual vessels of extraordinary technical sophistication
- **Jomon pottery** (Japan, c. 14,000 BCE): Among the world's oldest ceramic vessels
- **Olmec monumental sculpture** (Mesoamerica, c. 1500-400 BCE): Colossal basalt heads up to 3.4 meters tall
