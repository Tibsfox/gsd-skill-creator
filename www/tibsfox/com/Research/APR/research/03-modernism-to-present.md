## 1. Post-Impressionism and Art Nouveau

Post-Impressionists each developed the Impressionist vocabulary in different directions: Van Gogh into expressive emotional color and turbulent brushwork; Cezanne into structural geometric analysis of form (proto-Cubism); Gauguin into symbolic primitivism; Seurat into systematic pointillism.

**Art Nouveau** (c. 1890-1910) explored organic forms, sinuous line, and the total work of art across architecture, furniture, poster design, and painting. Gustav Klimt, Antoni Gaudi, and Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec are its major figures. Klimt's *The Kiss* (1907-08) encases human intimacy in gold leaf pattern -- Byzantine icon meets erotic modernism.

## 2. Expressionism, Fauvism, Cubism

**Fauvism** ("wild beasts", 1905) used non-naturalistic color for maximum emotional impact. Matisse's *Woman with a Hat* scandalized the Salon d'Automne with its green and purple face.

**German Expressionism** (Kirchner, Beckmann, Nolde) used distorted form and raw color to express psychological anguish in an industrializing Europe. The Bridge (Die Brucke) and Blue Rider (Der Blaue Reiter) groups were its organizational centers.

**Cubism** (Picasso and Braque, c. 1907-1915) is perhaps the most radical formal revolution in the history of painting: simultaneous representation of multiple viewpoints on a single flat surface. Analytical Cubism (1910-12) deconstructed objects into geometric facets. Synthetic Cubism (1912-14) reintroduced color and collage.

## 3. Dada, Surrealism, Futurism

**Dada** (Zurich, 1916) was anti-art: a nihilistic response to World War I. Duchamp's *Fountain* (a urinal submitted as art, 1917) remains the most provocative single gesture in Western art history.

**Surrealism** (Breton, Dali, Ernst, Magritte, c. 1917-1950) used Freudian dream logic to access the unconscious. Dali's *Persistence of Memory* (1931) melted clocks across a barren landscape. Magritte's *Treachery of Images* ("Ceci n'est pas une pipe") interrogated the relationship between representation and reality.

**Futurism** (Italy, 1909) celebrated speed, machines, and violence as aesthetic values. Boccioni's *Unique Forms of Continuity in Space* (1913) captured motion in bronze.

## 4. Abstract Expressionism, Pop Art, Minimalism

**Abstract Expressionism** (New York, 1940s-50s): Pollock's drip paintings made process visible. De Kooning's *Woman* series attacked figuration with gestural fury. Rothko's color field paintings sought transcendence through pure color.

**Pop Art** (Warhol, Lichtenstein, Hamilton, c. 1950s-60s) reclaimed mass media, advertising, and commodity culture as artistic subject matter. Warhol's Campbell's Soup Cans (1962) collapsed the distinction between high and low art.

**Minimalism** (Judd, Stella, Flavin, c. 1960s-70s) stripped art to pure form, material, and industrial fabrication. "What you see is what you see" -- Frank Stella.

## 5. Contemporary Art: 1970 to Present

Contemporary art resists definition by style; it is defined by its time period.

| Movement | Period | Key Artists | Innovation |
|----------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Conceptual Art | 1960s-present | Sol LeWitt, Joseph Kosuth | The idea as the work itself |
| Street Art / Graffiti | 1970s-present | Basquiat, Banksy, Keith Haring | Art as public intervention |
| Digital Art | 1990s-present | Generative systems, interactive | Computer as creative medium |
| Video Art | 1960s-present | Nam June Paik, Bill Viola | Time-based visual media |
| NFT Art | 2020s | Various | Blockchain-authenticated digital work |
| AI Art | 2022-present | Text-to-image models | Machine as collaborator/tool |

Living artists including Kehinde Wiley, Kerry James Marshall, Kara Walker, and Yayoi Kusama represent the global diversity of contemporary practice.
