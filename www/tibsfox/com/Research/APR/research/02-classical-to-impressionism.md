## 1. Medieval Art

Medieval art in the Western tradition encompasses three major styles spanning nearly a millennium.

**Byzantine Art (c. 330-1453 CE):** Centered at Constantinople, the Byzantine tradition developed the icon as a spiritual technology -- a window to the divine, formally rigid by theological design. Mosaics at Ravenna and Constantinople set the visual grammar of Christian devotional art. The gold background was not decorative; it represented divine light, the absence of earthly space.

**Romanesque (c. 1000-1200):** Massive stone church architecture with thick walls, rounded arches, and vigorously carved portal sculpture. The tympanum of Autun Cathedral (Gislebertus, c. 1130) demonstrates narrative sculpture of extraordinary emotional force.

**Gothic (c. 1150-1400):** Transformed Romanesque solidity into soaring vertical space: pointed arches, ribbed vaulting, flying buttresses, and the stained glass window as painted light. Chartres Cathedral's windows contain over 22,000 square feet of medieval stained glass. Artists: Duccio, Cimabue, Giotto, Jan Van Eyck.

## 2. The Renaissance Revolution

The Renaissance (Italian: "rebirth") began in Florence in the 14th century. Its three central innovations transformed Western art permanently:

1. **Linear perspective** (Brunelleschi, c. 1420): Mathematically systematized spatial recession on a flat surface
2. **Humanist subject matter:** Restoring the Greek dignity of the individual human figure
3. **Oil paint technique** (perfected by Van Eyck in Northern Europe): Enabled gradations of light and shadow of unprecedented subtlety

**Key Phases:**

| Phase | Period | Key Artists | Innovation |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Early Renaissance | c. 1400-1480 | Masaccio, Donatello, Botticelli | Perspective, anatomical study |
| High Renaissance | c. 1480-1520 | Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael | Classical harmony at its apex |
| Mannerism | c. 1520-1600 | El Greco, Parmigianino | Deliberate distortion of Renaissance ideals |
| Northern Renaissance | c. 1430-1580 | Durer, Holbein, Bruegel | Italian perspective + northern detail |

Leonardo's sfumato (smoky gradation of tone), Michelangelo's terribilita (awe-inspiring grandeur), and Raphael's compositional harmony together define the High Renaissance -- a brief period of roughly 40 years that became the standard against which all subsequent Western art measured itself.

## 3. Baroque Art

Baroque art (c. 1600-1750) emerged from the Counter-Reformation Catholic Church's need for emotionally persuasive religious imagery. Its hallmarks: dramatic chiaroscuro, dynamic movement, emotional intensity, expanded heroic scale.

**Caravaggio** revolutionized painting with tenebrism -- theatrical light against deep shadow. Saints shown as rough-handed peasants, divine moments rendered with street-level grit. His *Calling of Saint Matthew* (1600) puts God's light in a Roman tavern.

**Rembrandt** extended chiaroscuro into psychological portraiture of extraordinary depth. His late self-portraits are among the most honest depictions of aging in the history of painting.

**Other masters:** Rubens (exuberant physical energy), Vermeer (domestic intimate light), Velazquez (royal portraiture with subversive depth), Bernini (sculpture fusing spiritual and physical ecstasy -- *Ecstasy of Saint Teresa*).

## 4. Rococo

Rococo (c. 1700-1775): A French court style softening Baroque grandeur into lightness, pastel colors, asymmetric ornamentation, and aristocratic leisure. The word derives from *rocaille* (shellwork). Watteau's *fetes galantes*, Fragonard's swings, Boucher's pastorals. Rococo was the style of the French aristocracy in the decades before the Revolution swept it away.

## 5. Neoclassicism

Neoclassicism (c. 1750-1850) arose as a reaction against Rococo excess, inspired by the archaeological rediscovery of Pompeii (1748) and Herculaneum. Jacques-Louis David's *Oath of the Horatii* (1784) exemplifies its moral severity and classical order. The style aligned with republican ideals and the French Revolution -- David was the Revolution's official painter.

## 6. Romanticism

Romanticism (c. 1780-1850) rejected Enlightenment rationalism for the sublime -- nature as overwhelming spiritual force, the individual imagination as the highest authority. Turner's skies dissolve into pure light and atmosphere. Caspar David Friedrich's lone figures contemplate vast landscapes. Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* (1830) channels revolutionary energy through Baroque color.

## 7. Realism

Realism (c. 1848-1900) rejected both Neoclassical idealism and Romantic emotionalism. Courbet, Millet, and Daumier painted peasants, workers, and the urban poor with unflinching directness. Courbet declared: "Show me an angel and I'll paint one." The mundane everyday world, previously beneath artistic consideration, became the primary subject.

## 8. Impressionism

Impressionism (c. 1865-1885) was simultaneously a perceptual revolution and a social rebellion. Rejected by the official Paris Salon, Monet, Renoir, Pissarro, and Sisley began painting *en plein air* (outdoors) to capture transient light conditions directly.

**Key innovations:**
- Short, visible brushstrokes -- the painter's hand made visible
- Unmixed colors placed adjacent rather than blended on the palette
- Modern life as subject matter: cafes, dance halls, regatta courses
- Painting light itself, not objects illuminated by light

The name came from a hostile critic mocking Monet's *Impression, Sunrise* (1872). Historians call Impressionism the hinge between the classical tradition and modern art -- the moment when how you see became as important as what you see.
