## 1. Prehistoric and Ancient Music

The oldest known musical instruments are bone flutes from Hohle Fels and Vogelherd, Germany, dated to approximately 40,000-42,000 BCE. The Divje Babe flute (Slovenia, c. 60,000 BCE) made from a cave bear femur may be older, though debated.

**Ancient Egypt (c. 3000 BCE):** Music central to all life -- religious ceremony, mummification, royal court. Instruments: harps, flutes, lutes, drums. Musicians held significant social status.

**Ancient Greece (c. 1000-31 BCE):** Greek music theory forms the mathematical foundation of the entire Western tonal tradition. The modes (Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian) that medieval theorists inherited. Pythagoras's discovery: string length ratios produce harmonious intervals.

**India (500 BCE-present):** Indian Classical music, rooted in the Vedic tradition. The raga (melodic framework) and tala (rhythmic cycle) systems. Two traditions: Hindustani (North) and Carnatic (South).

## 2. Medieval Music

**Gregorian chant:** Monophonic, unaccompanied, Latin liturgical text. Named for Pope Gregory I. The development of musical notation by Guido of Arezzo (c. 1040 CE) was one of the most consequential innovations in music history -- enabling compositions to be transmitted beyond living memory.

**Polyphony** (multiple simultaneous melodic lines) emerged in the 12th century at Notre Dame (Leonin, Perotin). Secular music flourished through troubadours (southern France), trouveres (northern France), and Minnesingers (German). The *ars nova* of the 14th century (Machaut) brought rhythmic complexity approaching modern notation.

## 3. Renaissance Music

The printing press (Gutenberg, 1450) for music allowed rapid dissemination. Renaissance music was primarily **polyphonic**: Palestrina, Lassus, Victoria, Byrd. Secular forms: madrigal, chanson, lute song. The **Florentine Camerata** (1570s-80s) developed monody and invented opera, directly triggering the Baroque.

## 4. Baroque Music

The Baroque era (c. 1600-1750) invented the major institutions of Western classical music: opera, orchestra, concerto, oratorio, cantata, fugue. Music moved from modal to **tonal** (major/minor keys).

| Composer | Contribution | Key Works |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| J.S. Bach | Supreme architect of counterpoint | Well-Tempered Clavier, Brandenburg Concertos |
| Handel | Oratorio mastery | Messiah (1741) |
| Vivaldi | Concerto form | The Four Seasons (c. 1723) |
| Monteverdi | Early opera pioneer | L'Orfeo (1607) |

## 5. Classical Period

Classical music (c. 1750-1820): **homophonic texture** (clear melody over accompaniment), structural clarity, **sonata form** (exposition/development/recapitulation). Haydn ("father of the symphony", 104 symphonies), Mozart (41 symphonies, supreme operatic genius), Beethoven (whose late works broke the classical mold).

## 6. Romantic Period

Romanticism in music (c. 1820-1900): intense emotional expression, expanded orchestras, nationalist identity, cult of the virtuoso. Chopin (piano as confessional), Liszt (virtuosic piano), Brahms (classical structure with Romantic warmth), Wagner (Gesamtkunstwerk -- total artwork), Berlioz, Tchaikovsky, Dvorak, Verdi.
