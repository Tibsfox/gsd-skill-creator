# Kids' Programming Eras (1992-2023)

## The What A Cartoon! Pipeline (1994-1999)

Fred Seibert proposed an animation anthology model: 48 short cartoon pilots produced by independent animators. Of the 48 shorts, six became full series:

| Show | Creator | Significance | Full Series |
|------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Dexter's Laboratory | Genndy Tartakovsky | Highest-rated short; primetime Emmy nominee | 1996-2003 |
| The Powerpuff Girls | Craig McCracken | Highest-rated CN premiere at debut | 1998-2005 |
| Johnny Bravo | Van Partible | Adult comedy sensibility in children's programming | 1997-2004 |
| Cow and Chicken | David Feiss | Absurdist humor; spawned I Am Weasel | 1997-1999 |
| Courage the Cowardly Dog | John R. Dilworth | Horror-comedy; Oscar-nominated short origin | 1999-2002 |

Seth MacFarlane's "Larry and Steve" short contained prototypes of Peter and Brian Griffin -- Family Guy's DNA originated here.

## The Golden Age (1996-2005)

The Cartoon Cartoons brand launched July 1997. By 2005, Cartoon Network was running approximately 20 original series. Samurai Jack (2001-2004, revived 2017) represented the artistic peak -- cinematic direction, minimal dialogue, unprecedented serialized narrative in American children's animation.

## The Dark Age: CN Real (2006-2009)

After Betty Cohen's 2001 departure, the Stuart Snyder presidency experimented with live-action programming. CN Real was uniformly identified as a "Dark Age" -- the channel's identity was built on animation, and live-action eroded the brand without capturing audience from Nickelodeon or Disney Channel.

## The 2010s Renaissance

Adventure Time's April 5, 2010 premiere marked the beginning of a new era. Pendleton Ward demonstrated that children's animated series could sustain ongoing narrative arcs and world-building. Regular Show (J.G. Quintel, September 2010) ran concurrently, offering adult slacker comedy calibrated for children's television. Both averaged 2-3 million viewers per episode.

**Steven Universe** (Rebecca Sugar, 2013-2019) represented a further paradigm shift: the first Cartoon Network series created by a non-binary showrunner, and the most emotionally radical American children's animated series of its generation. Its LGBTQ+ representation -- including an on-screen same-sex wedding and non-binary characters -- was unprecedented for children's animation on a major US network.
