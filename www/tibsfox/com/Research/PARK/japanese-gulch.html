History

From 1903 to 1930, hundreds of Japanese immigrants lived in a community in this gulch, working for the Crown Lumber Company. They built wooden dwellings among the trees and wetlands — a settlement nestled in the ravine between what is now Mukilteo Speedway and the Puget Sound shoreline. When the sawmill closed in 1930 during the Great Depression, the community dispersed. The wooden structures deteriorated and were gradually reclaimed by the forest.

During World War II, the area was used for coastal defense. In the 1960s, Boeing used portions of the property. For decades after, the land sat largely undeveloped — second-growth forest filling in where the old-growth had been logged, the ravine's creek running through ferns and alder, the trails worn by informal use.

In 2007, a developer sought to purchase the property for an industrial park. The community of Mukilteo organized, protested, fundraised, and ultimately preserved the gulch. In 2014, the land was purchased for public use. On June 20, 2016, the Mukilteo City Council adopted the Japanese Gulch Master Plan, creating a formal public park and preserve.

Trails

Currently 2.6 miles of formal trails on publicly owned land, with approximately 5 miles of informal trails connecting through the property. The master plan envisions expansion to 8+ miles total, including a future waterfront connection. The terrain is ravine forest — moderately hilly with creek crossings, second-growth canopy of alder, cedar, and Douglas-fir.

  • Main Loop Trail — 1.5 miles. Well-maintained. Moderate elevation change through the central ravine. Ferns, moss, creek crossings. Year-round accessible.
  • Upper Ridge Trail — Connects to Mukilteo Speedway trailhead via 76th St SW. Views through canopy. Steeper sections.
  • Creek Trail — Follows the main drainage through the gulch bottom. Wetland habitat. Bridge crossings. Best for bird watching.
  • Dog Park Connector — Links to the off-leash dog area at the 76th St trailhead. Popular morning route.
  • Mountain Bike Trails — Informal single-track through the forest. Shared use with hikers. Some technical sections with roots and mud.

Ecology

The gulch is a recovering second-growth forest in the PNW lowland conifer zone. The ravine creates a moist microclimate that supports species typically found at higher elevations. The creek system provides year-round water.

Trees: Red alder (pioneer canopy), Western red cedar, Douglas-fir, bigleaf maple, Western hemlock (understory). The alder represents the mid-succession stage — the forest is transitioning from alder-dominated to conifer-dominated, a process that takes 80-120 years.

Understory: Sword fern (Polystichum munitum — NASA v1.2), salal (Gaultheria shallon — NASA v1.3), Oregon grape, salmonberry, thimbleberry. Dense fern coverage along the creek corridor.

Birds: Varied Thrush (S36 deg 2), Pacific Wren (deg 1), Black-capped Chickadee (deg 3), Spotted Towhee (deg 46), Dark-eyed Junco (deg 47), Pileated Woodpecker, Red-breasted Nuthatch (deg 5), Brown Creeper (deg 51). Great Horned Owl resident. Barred Owl increasingly common (displacing Spotted Owl range-wide).

Mammals: Black-tailed deer, raccoon, coyote, Douglas squirrel, Townsend's chipmunk, deer mice, bats (Little Brown Myotis, Big Brown Bat). Possible river otter in the creek.

Access

Main trailhead: 76th St SW off Mukilteo Speedway. Parking lot, dog park, trail map sign.

Secondary access: Multiple informal entry points along Mukilteo Speedway and from adjacent neighborhoods.

Hours: Dawn to dusk. No fee. Dogs allowed (on-leash on trails, off-leash in designated area).

Conditions: Trails can be muddy in wet season (Oct-Apr). Creek crossings may be slippery. Bring waterproof shoes. The forest floor is the real-time expression of KPAE weather — what the simulation models, the gulch demonstrates.
