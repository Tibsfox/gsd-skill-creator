## 1. The Corvid Dual System of the PNW

The Pacific Northwest is one of the great corvid theaters of the world. Two species of the genus *Corvus* have partitioned an entire landscape between them: the American Crow claims the lowlands, the cities, the ferry landings and parking lots; the Common Raven holds the mountains, the old-growth forests, the coastal cliffs. Between them lies one of the most fascinating stories in modern ornithology -- the 2020 taxonomic absorption of the Northwestern Crow, a species that dissolved not through extinction but through the realization that the lines we drew never matched the lines the birds drew themselves [1][2].

Understanding this dual system requires precise identification skills. Crows and ravens share the same genus but differ substantially in size, structure, voice, flight style, and habitat preference. The field marks are learnable, but the boundary between them -- especially in the coastal overlap zone -- reveals the limitations of species concepts built on phenotypic characters alone.

This module provides complete species profiles, the genomic evidence behind the 2020 merger, and practical field identification keys for distinguishing crows from ravens across the PNW. It covers subspecies variation, historical taxonomic debate, and the implications of the merger for birders, wildlife managers, and educators.

## 2. American Crow -- Species Profile

**Scientific name:** *Corvus brachyrhynchos* Brehm, 1822

**Common name:** American Crow

**Order:** Passeriformes. **Family:** Corvidae.

**Length:** 40--53 cm (15.7--20.9 in). **Wingspan:** 85--100 cm (33.5--39.4 in). **Weight:** 316--620 g (11.1--21.9 oz).

**Plumage:** All-black with a strong iridescent sheen visible in direct sunlight -- purple, blue, and green tones across the mantle and wing coverts. Feathers are dense, sleek, and tight-fitting compared to the shaggier appearance of ravens.

**Bill:** Moderately sized, straight, and relatively slender compared to the raven's massive curved bill. The bill is proportional to the head -- a useful field mark distinguishing crows from juvenile ravens.

**Tail:** Fan-shaped when spread in flight -- the single most reliable field mark separating crows from ravens at distance. The fan shape results from roughly equal-length rectrices.

**Eyes:** Dark brown iris. Juveniles may show a slightly paler iris in their first year.

**Legs and feet:** Black, strong, adapted for walking and hopping on the ground. Crows spend significantly more time on the ground than ravens.

**Voice:** The familiar higher-pitched "caw" -- typically rendered as *caw-caw-caw*. Variable in pitch and cadence, with context-dependent patterns. Three-caw sequences, four-caw sequences, and rapid-fire cawing serve different communicative functions. Individual crows have distinctive voice signatures identifiable through acoustic analysis [3][4].

**Population:** Estimated at approximately 28--31 million individuals across North America (BirdLife International, 2012; recent monitoring data). The species experienced a sharp 45% decline from 1999 to 2020, primarily attributed to West Nile virus, with partial recovery observed in some regions since approximately 2010 [5][6].

**Range:** Nearly all of North America south of the boreal treeline, from coast to coast. In the PNW, American Crows are most common west of the Cascade Range in lowland, urban, and suburban environments. They are present but less abundant east of the Cascades in agricultural valleys and riparian corridors [7].

**Habitat preference:** Urban and suburban areas, agricultural land, riparian corridors, coastal areas, forest edges. Strongly associated with human-modified landscapes. Rarely found in continuous dense forest or alpine areas -- those niches are held by ravens.

**Diet:** Omnivorous generalist with one of the most diverse diets of any North American bird. Insects, spiders, snails, earthworms, small vertebrates (fish, snakes, nestling birds, eggs), cultivated fruits, nuts, vegetables, and extensive anthropogenic food sources including bread, dog food, french fries, and garbage. A USGS campground study found American Crows had the most diverse diet among all corvid species studied [8].

**Breeding:** Monogamous with long-term pair bonds. Cooperative breeder -- offspring from previous years frequently remain as "helpers at the nest," assisting with feeding nestlings and defending the territory. Typical clutch size is 4--6 eggs. Average breeding success yields 1--2 fledglings per pair. Breeding territory is relatively small, allowing sixfold territory overlap compared to raven territories [9].

**Lifespan:** Average 7--8 years in the wild. Maximum recorded lifespan approximately 17 years.

**Recognized subspecies:** Three to five, including:
- *C. b. brachyrhynchos* -- eastern and central North America
- *C. b. hesperis* -- western interior (the "western crow")
- *C. b. caurinus* -- Pacific coast (the former Northwestern Crow)
- *C. b. pascuus* -- southeastern United States (sometimes recognized)

## 3. Common Raven -- Species Profile

**Scientific name:** *Corvus corax* Linnaeus, 1758. PNW subspecies: *C. c. principalis* Ridgway, 1887 (Northern Raven).

**Common name:** Common Raven

**Order:** Passeriformes. **Family:** Corvidae.

**Length:** 56--69 cm (22.1--27.2 in). **Wingspan:** 116--118 cm (45.7--46.5 in). **Weight:** 689 g--1.63 kg (1.5--3.6 lb).

The Common Raven is substantially larger than the American Crow -- a size difference immediately apparent when the two species are seen together but sometimes difficult to judge on a lone bird without reference. Ravens are roughly the size of a Red-tailed Hawk.

**Plumage:** All-black with a strong iridescent sheen. The throat feathers are elongated, shaggy, and pointed -- the "hackles" or "beard" that give ravens their distinctive silhouette. This throat shagginess is diagnostic and absent in crows.

**Bill:** Massive, heavy, and distinctly curved -- the single largest bill of any North American passerine. The bill has a pronounced arch to the upper mandible and prominent nasal bristles covering the base. In flight, the bill gives the raven a heavier, more front-loaded appearance than a crow.

**Tail:** Wedge-shaped or diamond-shaped in flight -- the central rectrices are significantly longer than the outer ones, creating a pointed or graduated tail profile. This is the single most reliable field mark at distance and is visible in every flight posture [10].

**Eyes:** Dark brown iris. Nictitating membrane visible during close observation.

**Voice:** Deep, guttural, resonant croaking -- fundamentally different in quality from any crow vocalization. The primary call is a hollow *kronk* or *pruk* that carries long distances in mountain terrain. Ravens possess over 30 distinct call types, including knocking sounds, bell-like notes, and complex warbling sequences. They can imitate a variety of sounds including the human voice [11].

**Population:** Approximately 7.7 million individuals in the Americas. The most widely distributed corvid in the Northern Hemisphere, found across North America, Europe, Asia, and North Africa. Eleven accepted subspecies globally [10].

**Range:** In the PNW, ravens are found throughout Washington and Oregon except in the cores of major urban areas. They replace crows as the dominant corvid in mountainous areas, deserts, rimrock, and old-growth forest. More common east of the Cascade Range and at higher elevations west of the Cascades. Population densities are highest east of the Cascades in Oregon (ODFW) and in mountainous terrain throughout Washington [7][12].

**Habitat preference:** Heavily contoured landscapes with cliffs, mountains, and deep valleys -- possibly because these terrains produce thermals used for long-distance foraging flights. Old-growth forest, alpine meadows, coastal cliffs, high desert, rimrock. Ravens historically avoided PNW cities, probably due to earlier human harassment, competition with increasingly abundant crows, and lack of appropriate cliff-analog nest sites [13].

**Diet:** Omnivorous and highly opportunistic. Carrion is a major component -- ravens are significant scavengers associated with wolf packs in winter nearly 100% of the time (Cornell Lab). They also take small mammals up to adult rock pigeon size, nestling herons, eggs, grasshoppers, beetles, scorpions, fish, and grains. Subadult ravens that discover carcasses guarded by territorial adults will return to communal roosts and communicate the find, returning with numbers to overwhelm territory holders [8][10].

**Breeding:** Monogamous with lifelong pair bonds. Each mated pair defends a large exclusive territory year-round -- significantly larger than crow territories with no overlap. Eggs incubated 18--21 days. Young remain in the nest 38--44 days, fed by both parents. Nests typically placed on cliff ledges or in tall conifers. Unlike crows, ravens do not exhibit cooperative breeding with helpers at the nest [9].

**Lifespan:** Exceeds 23 years in the wild. The oldest known wild raven was recorded at 17 years, 2 months by the National Park Service. Captive ravens have lived beyond 40 years [10].

## 4. The Former Northwestern Crow

**Historical scientific name:** *Corvus caurinus* Baird, 1858 (now *C. b. caurinus*)

The Northwestern Crow was historically described as a smaller, darker, coastal-adapted crow restricted to the Pacific coast from the Olympic Peninsula to southern Alaska. It was closely associated with intertidal habitats -- rocky shorelines, tidal flats, and coastal towns -- and exhibited distinctive behaviors including dropping shellfish (clams, mussels, sea urchins) onto rocks and paved surfaces, and caching food during low tide for retrieval at high tide [1][14].

Key characteristics attributed to the Northwestern Crow included:
- Slightly smaller overall size compared to inland American Crows
- Lower, hoarser voice -- described as more nasal and less sharp than the typical American Crow *caw*
- Strong association with marine intertidal habitats
- Shellfish-dropping behavior (shared with some coastal American Crow populations)
- Range restricted to within a few kilometers of the Pacific coast

The fundamental problem with the Northwestern Crow as a species concept was that differentiating it from the American Crow on phenotypic features -- size, voice, behavior -- proved nearly impossible in practice, especially in Washington State where the ranges overlapped. The traits attributed to the Northwestern Crow fell within the range of individual variation observed in American Crow populations. Coastal American Crows in areas far from the putative Northwestern Crow range exhibited similar size reduction, voice variation, and intertidal foraging behavior [2][14].

The Northwestern Crow's status oscillated repeatedly over 160 years of ornithological history. It was variously treated as a full species and as a subspecies depending on the prevailing species concept and the available evidence. This oscillation itself was diagnostic of the underlying problem: the signal was too weak to resolve with morphological and behavioral data alone. Resolution required genomic evidence that did not become available until 2020.

## 5. The 2020 Taxonomic Merger

On June 30, 2020, the American Ornithological Society (AOS) North American Classification Committee voted to absorb *Corvus caurinus* into *Corvus brachyrhynchos*, published in the 61st Supplement to the AOS Checklist (*The Auk: Ornithological Advances*, 2020). The Northwestern Crow ceased to exist as a recognized species. It is now treated as subspecies *C. b. caurinus* within the western crow group [15].

Richard Chesser, chair of the AOS Classification Committee, stated that the genomic data indicated the observed variation represented variation within a single species rather than two distinct species. The committee's decision was based primarily on the Slager et al. (2020) genomic analysis published in *Molecular Ecology* [1].

The merger had immediate practical consequences:
- The ABA (American Birding Association) life list lost one species overnight
- Field guides required revision (many remain outdated as of 2026)
- eBird and other citizen science platforms updated their taxonomies
- Wildlife management plans referencing "Northwestern Crow" required updating
- The Pacific coast crow population needed reinterpretation as a contact/hybrid zone within a single species

The merger also carried a deeper lesson about taxonomic practice: a species that had been recognized for 160 years could dissolve not through extinction but through improved understanding. The lines we drew were real in our field guides but not real in the birds' genomes.

## 6. Slager et al. Genomic Evidence

The definitive study was Slager DL, Epperly KL, Ha RR, Rohwer S, Wood C, Van Hemert C, Klicka J (2020), "Cryptic and extensive hybridization between ancient lineages of American crows," published in *Molecular Ecology* (DOI: 10.1111/mec.15377) [1].

The study examined genomic data from crow specimens collected along the Pacific coast from California to Alaska. The key findings were:

1. **No genetically pure individuals in the contact zone.** Along the shared 900 km range from coastal Washington to British Columbia, the researchers found no individual that was genetically "pure" Northwestern Crow or "pure" American Crow. Every individual in the overlap zone showed genetic admixture.

2. **Ancient hybridization.** The hybridization had been occurring since well before colonial-era landscape changes. This was not a recent phenomenon caused by human habitat modification -- it was a deep, longstanding pattern of gene flow.

3. **Phenotypic variation was clinal, not categorical.** The physical differences attributed to the Northwestern Crow (smaller size, lower voice, intertidal habitat preference) varied gradually along a geographic gradient rather than showing the sharp discontinuity expected between two distinct species. These differences reflected local adaptations and individual variation rather than species-level divergence.

4. **Two ancient lineages, one species.** The genomic data revealed two ancient mitochondrial lineages within American Crows that had been diverging for a long time but had never achieved reproductive isolation. The lineages freely interbred wherever they came into contact.

5. **The species boundary was an artifact of sampling.** Historical descriptions of the Northwestern Crow were based on specimens collected at the geographic extremes of the contact zone -- birds from southeastern Alaska (maximally "Northwestern") compared with birds from inland Washington (maximally "American"). In between, the populations graded smoothly into each other.

Dr. Kaeli Swift of the University of Washington, a leading corvid researcher, wrote extensively about the merger on her Corvid Research blog, noting that the decision "was a long time coming" and that many corvid researchers had privately doubted the species distinction for decades [14].

## 7. Field Identification Keys

Despite the taxonomic merger of the Northwestern Crow, the practical need to distinguish American Crows from Common Ravens remains important for birders, researchers, and wildlife managers throughout the PNW. The following field marks are reliable when used in combination.

No single field mark is diagnostic in isolation. Experienced observers use a gestalt approach combining multiple characters simultaneously. The four most reliable characters, in order of diagnostic value, are: tail shape, overall size, bill structure, and voice.

---

## 8. Size and Structure

**The size difference is substantial but requires context.** Ravens are 40--60% larger than crows by linear measurement and roughly three times heavier at average weights. A raven is approximately the size of a Red-tailed Hawk; a crow is roughly the size of a small pigeon.

However, size is difficult to judge on a lone bird without reference objects. Where the two species occur together, the size difference is immediately obvious. In areas where only one species is present (e.g., downtown Seattle where only crows occur, or alpine areas east of the Cascades where only ravens occur), other field marks become more important.

**Structural proportions differ beyond absolute size.** Ravens have proportionally larger heads, heavier bills, and longer wings relative to body length. In flight, ravens show a distinctly front-heavy, "big-headed" profile. Crows appear more balanced and compact.

---

## 9. Plumage and Bill

Both species are all-black with iridescent sheens, so plumage color alone is not diagnostic. However, feather texture and arrangement provide useful marks at close range:

- **Raven throat hackles:** Ravens have elongated, shaggy, pointed feathers on the throat and upper breast that fluff out conspicuously, especially during calling or display. These create a distinctive "bearded" appearance. Crows have smooth, sleek throat feathers.

- **Bill shape:** The raven's bill is massive, heavy, and distinctly arched along the upper mandible. It is the largest bill of any North American passerine. Prominent nasal bristles cover the basal third of the upper mandible. The crow's bill is proportionally much smaller and straighter.

- **Body contour:** Ravens appear shaggier overall, with looser-fitting feathers, especially on the belly and flanks. Crows appear sleeker and more compact.

---

## 10. Tail Shape -- The Diagnostic Feature

**Tail shape is the single most reliable field mark at any distance.** It is visible in soaring, gliding, powered flight, and perched posture (when the tail extends beyond the folded wings).

- **Crow:** Fan-shaped tail. The rectrices are roughly equal in length, producing a rounded or slightly squared trailing edge when the tail is spread.

- **Raven:** Wedge-shaped or diamond-shaped tail. The central rectrices are significantly longer than the outer ones, producing a pointed or graduated trailing edge. In some flight postures, the tail appears distinctly diamond-shaped.

This mark is visible at distances where size, bill shape, and throat texture are impossible to judge. At extreme distance, a black bird with a wedge-shaped tail is a raven; a black bird with a fan-shaped tail is a crow. This holds across all PNW habitats.

---

## 11. Vocalization Differences

Voice is often the first character that alerts an observer to which species is present, especially in forested habitat where visual contact is intermittent.

**American Crow vocalization:** The familiar higher-pitched *caw-caw-caw*. The fundamental frequency is higher than raven calls, and the tonal quality is sharper, clearer, and more nasal. Crows produce a diverse repertoire including rattles, bell-like notes, rapid-fire caws, and drawn-out sounds, but the primary *caw* is unmistakable.

**Common Raven vocalization:** Deep, guttural, resonant croaking -- *kronk*, *pruk*, *tok*. The fundamental frequency is dramatically lower than crow calls. The tonal quality is hollow, resonant, and often somewhat musical. Ravens possess over 30 distinct call types and can produce complex sequences including knocking, popping, and gurgling sounds that no crow produces [11].

The difference in voice quality between the two species is greater than the difference in any visual character. With practice, most observers can reliably identify crows and ravens by voice alone without visual contact.

---

## 12. Flight Style

Flight behavior provides identification cues at all distances:

- **Crow flight:** Direct, purposeful, with steady wingbeats. Crows rarely soar or glide for extended periods. They fly with a rowing motion -- methodical flapping with relatively shallow wingbeats.

- **Raven flight:** More varied and acrobatic than crow flight. Ravens frequently soar on thermals, glide on set wings, and perform aerial maneuvers including barrel rolls, half-rolls, and tumbling dives. Powered flight involves deeper, slower wingbeats with distinctly pointed wingtips visible at distance. Ravens are capable of sustained soaring comparable to raptors.

- **Crows in flocks; ravens in pairs.** Crows are typically seen in groups or flocks, often moving together in a single direction. Ravens are most often seen singly or in pairs, except at communal feeding sites (carcasses, dumps) where non-breeding subadults may gather in larger groups.

## 13. Habitat as Identification Tool

In many PNW locations, habitat alone provides a strong prior probability for species identification:

- **Urban core (Seattle, Portland, Eugene, Victoria):** American Crow overwhelmingly dominant. Ravens are absent from most PNW city centers.

- **Suburban and agricultural lowlands west of Cascades:** American Crow dominant, with ravens occasionally at periphery.

- **Coastal areas:** Both species present. Crows dominate populated coastal towns; ravens more common on remote headlands, sea cliffs, and undeveloped coastline.

- **Cascade Range alpine and subalpine:** Common Raven dominant. Crows generally absent above treeline.

- **East of Cascades (high desert, rimrock, sagebrush steppe):** Common Raven dominant. Crows present in agricultural valleys and riparian corridors but at lower density.

- **Old-growth forest:** Common Raven more likely. Crows prefer forest edges and openings rather than closed-canopy old-growth.

This habitat partitioning is not absolute -- exceptions occur at range boundaries and overlap zones -- but it provides a useful starting framework. A large black bird soaring over Snoqualmie Pass is almost certainly a raven. A large black bird walking across a Safeway parking lot in Bellevue is almost certainly a crow.

## 14. Subspecies Variation in the PNW

The PNW hosts notable subspecies variation within both American Crows and Common Ravens. Understanding this variation helps contextualize the 2020 merger and the ongoing scientific questions about corvid population structure.

---

## 15. The Western Crow Group

American Crows in the western United States are divided into two primary subspecies groups:

**The western crow (*C. b. hesperis*):** Found throughout the western interior from British Columbia to Mexico, east of the Cascade-Sierra crest. This subspecies tends to be slightly smaller and more slender-billed than eastern *C. b. brachyrhynchos*, with subtly different vocalizations.

**The former Northwestern Crow (*C. b. caurinus*):** Now treated as part of the western coastal population, grading clinally into *hesperis* inland and *brachyrhynchos* to the east and south. The coastal birds retain their size reduction and intertidal associations, but these characters are understood as local adaptations within a continuous population rather than markers of species-level distinction [1][2].

The practical result for PNW birders is that crows along the Pacific coast from Washington to Alaska may average smaller and show stronger intertidal habitat association than inland crows. These differences are real and observable in the field. They simply do not warrant species-level separation under modern genomic species concepts.

---

## 16. Raven Subspecies

The Common Raven shows substantial geographic variation across its enormous global range, with eleven accepted subspecies. The PNW subspecies is *C. c. principalis* Ridgway, 1887 -- the Northern Raven, which ranges across northern North America from Alaska to Greenland and south through the western mountains.

*C. c. principalis* is among the largest raven subspecies, approaching the maximum dimensions for the species. PNW ravens in alpine environments can be very large birds -- approaching Red-tailed Hawk dimensions -- while ravens at lower elevations and in coastal areas may average slightly smaller.

The subspecies taxonomy of Common Ravens is itself subject to ongoing revision. Some molecular studies suggest that the species may be better treated as a species complex with multiple cryptic lineages. This question remains unresolved as of 2026.

## 17. Historical Taxonomic Oscillation

The taxonomic history of the Northwestern Crow illustrates how species concepts change with available evidence:

| Year | Treatment | Authority | Basis |
|------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1858 | Described as *Corvus caurinus* (new species) | Baird | Morphology -- smaller size, coastal habits |
| 1886 | Full species in AOU Checklist | AOU | Continued recognition |
| 1931 | Demoted to subspecies | AOU | Insufficient differentiation |
| 1957 | Restored to full species | AOU | Voice and habitat differences |
| 2020 | Absorbed into *C. brachyrhynchos* | AOS | Slager et al. genomic evidence |

This 162-year oscillation between species and subspecies status was not the product of carelessness. It reflected genuine difficulty in applying morphological species concepts to populations that grade continuously into each other. Each decision was reasonable given the available evidence at the time. The 2020 resolution required genomic tools that did not exist when the previous decisions were made [1][15].

## 18. What the Merger Means for Birders

For practical birding in the PNW, the merger changes very little about field identification. Birders still need to distinguish crows from ravens, and the field marks remain exactly the same. What the merger removes is the additional challenge of trying to distinguish American Crows from Northwestern Crows in the overlap zone -- a challenge that was never reliably met in the field anyway.

The eBird platform now lumps all PNW coastal crow records under American Crow. Historical records attributed to Northwestern Crow remain in the database but are treated as American Crow for analytical purposes.

For life-listers, the merger removed one species from the ABA checklist. For working ornithologists, it clarified population structure. For wildlife managers, it simplified regulatory treatment of crow populations across the PNW.

## 19. Ongoing Questions

Several taxonomic and population-genetic questions remain active research areas:

1. **The western crow subspecies boundary.** Where does *C. b. hesperis* end and *C. b. caurinus* begin? Or are they a single clinal population with no meaningful subspecies boundary?

2. **Raven species complex.** Is *Corvus corax* a single species or a complex of cryptic species? Molecular evidence suggests deep lineage separation in some populations, particularly between Old World and New World ravens.

3. **Urban range expansion.** Ravens are slowly increasing their tolerance for urban environments in the PNW. Will the crow-raven competitive dynamic shift as ravens colonize cities?

4. **Genetic consequences of West Nile virus.** Has WNV mortality created selection pressure on PNW crow populations? Has genetic diversity been affected?

5. **Climate-driven range shifts.** As treeline moves upward and habitat zones shift, will the crow-raven niche partition shift with them?
