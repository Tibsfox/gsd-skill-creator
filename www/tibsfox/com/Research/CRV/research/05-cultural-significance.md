## 1. Raven Across Nations

Raven is the most prominent animal figure in the cultural traditions of the Indigenous nations of the Pacific Northwest Coast, from southeastern Alaska through British Columbia to the Puget Sound lowlands and beyond. But "Raven" is not a single character with a single meaning. Each nation has its own Raven traditions, its own narratives, its own theological weight, and its own artistic expression of the figure [1][2].

Collapsing these distinct traditions into a generic "Native American Raven mythology" is not merely imprecise -- it is an erasure of the specific cultural knowledge, artistic achievement, and theological sophistication of each nation. This module resists that erasure by treating each tradition as a discrete body of knowledge, naming the nation, noting the specific role Raven plays, and acknowledging the differences between traditions that are at least as significant as the similarities.

The prominence of Raven diminishes as one moves south along the coast. Tlingit and Haida traditions give Raven the most prominent creator role. Tsimshian and Kwakwaka'wakw traditions maintain Raven as a significant figure but with different emphasis. Coast Salish traditions incorporate Raven but with less prominence than northern traditions. This gradient is itself significant -- it reflects distinct cultural histories, ecological contexts, and theological frameworks, not a uniform "Raven culture" that stretches from Alaska to Oregon [1].

## 2. Tlingit Raven -- Creator and Trickster

In Tlingit culture, Raven occupies a unique position: he is simultaneously the supreme creator figure and the supreme trickster. This dual nature is not a contradiction but a deliberate theological structure that encodes a sophisticated understanding of how worlds come into being [1][3].

The creator Raven is responsible for bringing the world into being -- arranging land, water, and sky; creating animals and humans; establishing the fundamental order of the natural world. He is a revered transformer figure whose actions shape reality at the most fundamental level.

The trickster Raven is driven by appetite, impulse, and curiosity. His schemes frequently backfire, creating chaos and absurdity. But the chaos he creates is also transformative -- trickster Raven's misadventures often produce beneficial outcomes that were not his original intention.

The key insight of the dual Raven is that creation is not a deliberate, intentional act by a benevolent deity. Creation is a side effect of a trickster's appetite. Raven brought light to the world not because he wanted to help humanity but because he wanted to see what he was eating. The benefit to humanity was incidental. This framing encodes a view of the world as arising from accident and appetite rather than design -- a theological position that is, in its own way, as sophisticated as any systematic philosophy.

## 3. The Dual Raven -- A Theological Structure

Some Tlingit traditions distinguish between two separate Raven characters: the Creator Raven and the Childish Raven (or Raven at the Head of Nass). Whether these are understood as two aspects of a single being or as two distinct beings varies among communities and storytellers [1][3].

The Creator Raven is associated with:
- Bringing the world into existence
- Arranging the fundamental features of the landscape
- Establishing the conditions for life
- Serious, transformative action with lasting consequences

The Childish or Trickster Raven is associated with:
- Stealing the Sun (or Moon, or Stars, or Fire) through deception
- Transforming his shape to achieve his goals
- Getting into trouble through gluttony, impulsiveness, or curiosity
- Creating new conditions through the unintended consequences of his schemes

The interplay between these two aspects or characters creates a narrative framework that can accommodate both cosmic creation and earthy comedy. The same figure who brought light into existence also got stuck in a pitch trap trying to steal food. This is not inconsistency -- it is a complete picture of creative force.

## 4. Raven's Journey Along the Coast

In Tlingit tradition, Raven travelled extensively along the coast, first south then north, then inland along the great rivers: the Stikine, the Nass, the Skeena, and the Taku. Each location along his journey is associated with specific stories, and the journey itself maps the geography of the Tlingit world [1][3].

The journey narrative is simultaneously a creation story, a geographical reference, and a cultural atlas. Places where Raven stopped, rested, tricked, or transformed become significant in the cultural landscape -- they are not merely settings for stories but locations charged with meaning by Raven's presence.

This connection between narrative and geography is directly relevant to corvid ecology: Raven's journey follows the same coastal corridor and river systems that real Common Ravens use as foraging routes and territorial boundaries. The narrative encodes ecological knowledge about raven distribution, movement patterns, and habitat associations in a form that predates Western ornithological survey by thousands of years.

## 5. Haida Raven -- The First Men

In the Haida creation narrative, Raven walks along the beach at Rose Spit on Haida Gwaii (the northern tip of the Queen Charlotte Islands) and discovers humans hiding inside a giant clamshell. He coaxes them out, and they become the first Haida people [4][5].

Subsequently, Raven brings the essentials for human existence: Sun, Moon, Stars, Fire, Salmon, and Cedar. Each of these gifts has a story, and each story encodes a specific ecological and cultural relationship:

- **Sun, Moon, Stars:** Light, time, navigation -- the fundamental orientation of human existence in the world
- **Fire:** Warmth, cooking, technology -- the transformation of raw nature into culture
- **Salmon:** Food, the annual cycle, the river as lifeline -- the ecological foundation of Northwest Coast cultures
- **Cedar:** Shelter, canoes, basketry, clothing, art -- the material foundation of Northwest Coast civilizations

The Haida Raven creation narrative is not simply a myth about origins. It is a compressed encyclopedia of cultural ecology -- a single narrative framework that organizes the essential relationships between humans, resources, and the natural world.

## 6. The Eagle-Raven Moiety System

Haida society is divided into two moieties: Eagle and Raven. Every Haida person belongs to one of these two groups, determined by matrilineal descent (you belong to your mother's moiety). The moiety system governs marriage (you must marry someone from the opposite moiety), ceremonial responsibilities, artistic traditions, and property rights [4][5].

The Raven moiety is not simply "the moiety named after Raven." It is a fundamental social institution that organizes kinship, economy, governance, and ceremonial life. Raven's image is carved on totem poles, painted on house fronts, woven into blankets, and displayed on regalia throughout Haida Gwaii.

The Eagle-Raven system encodes the principle of complementary duality -- two halves that require each other to form a complete whole. Neither moiety is superior; each depends on the other for marriage partners, ceremonial balance, and social completeness. This structural principle mirrors the ecological duality of crows and ravens across the PNW landscape: two species, complementary niches, neither complete without the other.

## 7. Haida Gwaii -- Where Time Begins

Haida Gwaii -- the archipelago of over 150 islands off the coast of British Columbia -- is where time begins in Haida cosmology. It is the location of the clamshell creation event, the departure point for Raven's gift-bringing journeys, and the place where the fundamental relationships between Haida people and the natural world were established [4][5].

The islands are home to both American Crows and Common Ravens. The ecological landscape of Haida Gwaii -- with its temperate rainforest, coastal shores, and rich marine resources -- supports both corvid species and provides the environmental context for the Raven traditions that emerged there.

The Haida name "Haida Gwaii" means "Islands of the People." The previous colonial name (Queen Charlotte Islands) has been formally replaced. The renaming represents a broader pattern of cultural reclamation that is relevant to how we discuss Indigenous knowledge in research contexts.

## 8. Bill Reid -- The Raven and The First Men

Bill Reid (1920--1998), a Haida artist of mixed Haida and European descent, created *The Raven and The First Men* in 1980. The sculpture, now at the UBC Museum of Anthropology in Vancouver, depicts the Haida creation narrative: Raven stands on a partially opened clamshell, coaxing out the first humans who peer up at him from inside [5][6].

The sculpture is widely considered one of the defining works of Canadian art and one of the most significant pieces of Northwest Coast Indigenous art of the 20th century. It was featured on the Canadian $20 bill from 2004 to 2012.

Reid's work bridges multiple traditions:
- **Haida artistic tradition:** The sculpture draws on formline design principles and Haida narrative tradition
- **Western sculptural technique:** Reid trained as a jeweler and sculptor in European traditions and incorporated modernist sensibilities
- **Cultural reclamation:** Reid's artistic career was part of a broader movement to revitalize Northwest Coast Indigenous art after decades of suppression and colonial disruption

*The Raven and The First Men* is relevant to this module because it demonstrates how corvid imagery functions as a carrier of cultural identity, historical memory, and artistic achievement. Raven is not merely a character in stories -- he is a visual and sculptural presence that anchors Haida identity in the physical world.

## 9. Tsimshian Raven -- Transformer and Teacher

To the Tsimshian people, Raven is the original organizer -- the Transformer, teacher, catalyst, and chief spirit who brought order to the world. The Tsimshian Raven emphasizes the ordering function: Raven does not merely create but organizes, arranges, and instructs [1][2].

The Tsimshian tradition shares some narrative elements with Tlingit and Haida traditions (Raven stealing the light, Raven as shapechanger) but frames them with distinct emphasis. Where Tlingit Raven is a creator-trickster duality and Haida Raven is a clan ancestor who brings gifts, Tsimshian Raven is primarily a Transformer who changes the state of the world from disorder to order.

The Tsimshian term for this transformative role carries connotations that the English word "transformer" does not fully capture. It implies agency, intelligence, and purposeful rearrangement -- not merely changing things but changing them in the direction of greater coherence and functionality.

## 10. Kwakwaka'wakw -- Ceremonial Masks

For the Kwakwaka'wakw people, Raven is a ceremonial figure embodied through elaborate transformation masks used in potlatches and ceremonies [2][7].

Kwakwaka'wakw Raven masks are engineering marvels as well as artistic achievements. The masks feature articulated beaks that open and close through a system of strings and hinges operated by the dancer from inside. When the beak opens, it may reveal a human face inside -- representing Raven's ability to transform between human and animal forms. The dancer wearing the mask embodies the spirit, reenacting Raven myths through choreographed movement and the physical transformation of the mask.

The potlatch context is essential to understanding these masks. Potlatches are ceremonial gatherings involving feasting, gift-giving, speeches, and the performance of hereditary rights and privileges. The right to perform specific Raven dances, wear specific Raven masks, and tell specific Raven stories is a form of property -- owned by families and passed through inheritance. The masks are not generic art objects but specific hereditary possessions with legal and spiritual standing.

## 11. Heiltsuk Traditions

The Heiltsuk people (historically called Bella Bella) of the central coast of British Columbia incorporate Raven as a shapechanger and transformer in their cultural traditions. Heiltsuk Raven shares the shapeshifting attribute common to Northwest Coast Raven traditions but within a distinct cultural and artistic framework [2].

The Heiltsuk artistic tradition, like other central coast traditions, has its own stylistic characteristics that distinguish it from northern (Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian) and southern (Coast Salish) artistic traditions. Heiltsuk depictions of Raven reflect these stylistic differences while maintaining the core attributes of the Raven figure: prominent beak, shapeshifting capacity, transformative power.

## 12. Coast Salish -- The Living Connection

Coast Salish peoples -- including the Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Tulalip, Snoqualmie, Puyallup, Squamish, and many other nations -- have distinct artistic styles and cultural traditions for representing Raven. Coast Salish art is characteristically more fluid, curvilinear, and varied in form than the highly codified northern formline tradition [2][8].

The UW Bothell campus sits on Coast Salish land. This places the contemporary crow roost phenomenon in direct physical and cultural relationship with ancestral Coast Salish connections to corvids. The sixteen thousand crows that gathered nightly over Coast Salish territory were walking the same landscape corridors that Coast Salish peoples have navigated for millennia.

Raven's prominence in Coast Salish tradition is generally lower than in Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian traditions. Other animal figures -- Salmon, Bear, Cedar -- may carry greater cultural weight in some Coast Salish nations. But Raven remains a recognized figure, and the Coast Salish relationship with corvids extends beyond narrative tradition to practical ecological knowledge about crow and raven behavior, habitat, and seasonal patterns.

## 13. Koyukon -- The Distant Traveler

The Koyukon people (Athabaskan/Dene) of interior Alaska incorporate Raven as both creator and trickster, with distinct emphases reflecting the ecological context of subarctic continental environments rather than the maritime environments of the Northwest Coast [1][9].

Koyukon Raven operates in a landscape of boreal forest, frozen rivers, and extreme cold -- a very different environment from the temperate rainforest coast. The Raven stories encode ecological knowledge specific to interior Alaska: raven behavior in winter, the relationship between ravens and caribou (analogous to the wolf-raven partnership), and the role of ravens as the first birds to appear when winter begins to break.

Richard Nelson's ethnographic work with the Koyukon (*Make Prayers to the Raven*, 1983) documented the depth of Koyukon ecological knowledge about ravens and other animals. Nelson's work demonstrated that Koyukon knowledge of raven behavior, intelligence, and ecological relationships predated Western ornithological understanding by centuries -- and in some cases exceeded it in observational detail.

## 14. Inuit Traditions

Inuit traditions in Alaska and the Arctic incorporate Raven as creator and trickster, with emphases reflecting the arctic and subarctic ecological context. In some Inuit traditions, Raven created the world by dropping pebbles into the primordial ocean, forming the land [1][2].

The Inuit Raven traditions are distinct from the Northwest Coast traditions in their ecological framing, ceremonial context, and narrative structure. The arctic environment produces different corvid ecology -- ravens are year-round residents of the Arctic, one of the few birds that overwinter in the extreme cold. Their persistence through arctic winter is itself a source of cultural significance: the raven endures conditions that drive almost all other life south or underground.

## 15. Shared Elements Across Nations

Despite significant differences between traditions, certain elements appear across multiple nations [1][2]:

**Shapeshifting.** Across nations, Raven is a shapechanger who takes human, animal, and inanimate forms. This ability is fundamental to Raven's nature -- he is not a bird who sometimes transforms but a transformative force who sometimes appears as a bird.

**Creator and trickster duality.** In most traditions, Raven's creative acts arise from selfish motivation (hunger, curiosity, desire) rather than altruism. The world benefits from Raven's actions, but the benefit is incidental to Raven's personal agenda.

**Bringer of light.** Multiple traditions attribute the origin of light (Sun, Moon, Stars, Fire) to Raven's actions, typically through trickery rather than generosity.

**Cultural gifts.** Raven brings not only light but also the resources essential for human life: salmon, cedar, fresh water, fire.

**Insatiable appetite.** Raven is universally characterized by enormous appetite -- for food, for knowledge, for experience. His appetite drives his adventures and creates their consequences.

## 16. Important Distinctions Between Traditions

The shared elements can mask critical differences [1][2]:

1. **Theological weight varies dramatically.** In Tlingit and Haida traditions, Raven is the supreme creative figure -- comparable in theological importance to creation deities in other world traditions. In Coast Salish traditions, Raven is one figure among many, with less structural importance.

2. **Moiety systems are not universal.** The Eagle-Raven moiety system is specific to Haida (and with variations, Tlingit). Other nations have different social organizational principles.

3. **Artistic traditions are distinct.** Northern formline, central coast, and Coast Salish artistic traditions are visually and technically different. A Haida Raven image and a Coast Salish Raven image are recognizably different works from different traditions.

4. **Narrative specificity matters.** The specific stories, characters, locations, and sequences differ between nations. Haida Raven finds humans in a clamshell at Rose Spit. Tlingit Raven travels the rivers. These are not interchangeable narratives.

5. **Ceremonial contexts differ.** Kwakwaka'wakw potlatch masks serve a different ceremonial function than Tlingit clan regalia. The social contexts of Raven imagery are not equivalent across nations.

Generic attribution erases these differences. Saying "Indigenous peoples of the Northwest Coast believe that Raven created the world" is not merely imprecise -- it implies a cultural uniformity that does not exist and obscures the specific intellectual and artistic achievements of each nation.

## 17. The Shapechanger Principle

The shapechanger principle -- Raven's ability to take any form -- encodes a philosophical insight that maps onto modern ecological and cognitive science:

**Form is not fixed.** Raven changes between human, animal, and object forms effortlessly. This fluidity suggests a worldview in which the boundaries between categories (human/animal, animate/inanimate, natural/supernatural) are permeable rather than absolute.

This principle resonates with the 2020 taxonomic merger of the Northwestern Crow: the boundary between species was a line we drew, not a line the birds recognized. The organisms refused to stay in the category we assigned them. Raven, the shapechanger, would not be surprised.

It also resonates with the cognitive research in Module 2: the boundary between "intelligent" (primate) and "not-intelligent" (bird) cognitive architectures turns out to be far more permeable than Western science assumed. Corvid cognition crossed a category boundary that was supposed to be firm.

## 18. Formline Art and Corvid Imagery

Formline art -- the design tradition of the northern Northwest Coast nations (Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian) -- is one of the most sophisticated and immediately recognizable art traditions in the world [7][8].

Formline design principles include:
- **Ovoid shapes** as the primary organizing element
- **U-forms** as secondary connecting elements
- **Split representation** -- showing both sides of a figure simultaneously
- **Positive/negative space interplay** -- every area of the design surface carries meaning
- **Color coding** -- traditionally black (primary formlines), red (secondary elements), and blue-green (tertiary elements)

Raven is among the most commonly depicted figures in formline art. The Raven's diagnostic visual features in formline representation include the prominent beak (often shown open or holding an object), round eye with distinct iris, and wing forms that encode both bird anatomy and design convention.

The rigor of formline design -- its systematic rules, proportional relationships, and compositional grammar -- makes it a visual language as structured as any written system. A Haida artist working in formline is not simply drawing a bird; they are composing a statement in a visual language with centuries of grammatical tradition.

## 19. Contemporary Indigenous Artists

The Raven tradition is not a historical artifact. Contemporary Indigenous artists across the Northwest Coast work with corvid imagery, extending traditional forms into new media and contexts [8]:

- **Robert Davidson** (Haida) -- master carver, printmaker, and painter whose Raven works extend the formline tradition into contemporary artistic expression
- **Susan Point** (Musqueam, Coast Salish) -- widely recognized for bringing Coast Salish design into public art, architecture, and contemporary contexts
- **Preston Singletary** (Tlingit) -- glass artist who translates formline design into blown and cast glass, including Raven imagery
- **Alano Edzerza** (Tahltan/Tlingit) -- graphic designer and artist who brings formline principles into modern graphic design

These artists demonstrate that Indigenous Raven traditions are living cultural practices, not museum exhibits. The imagery evolves, the media expand, and the cultural meaning deepens with each generation of artistic production.

## 20. Ecological Knowledge in Cultural Narrative

The Raven traditions of Northwest Coast nations encode ecological knowledge that Western science has only recently documented [1][9]:

- **Raven intelligence.** Traditions across nations emphasize Raven's cleverness, problem-solving ability, and capacity for deception. Western science's "discovery" of corvid intelligence in the late 20th century was a rediscovery of what these traditions had known for millennia.

- **Wolf-raven association.** Indigenous ecological knowledge across multiple nations recognizes the foraging partnership between wolves and ravens. Western ecology documented the same partnership centuries later (see Module 3).

- **Salmon-raven connection.** Multiple traditions link Raven to salmon -- both as a bringer of salmon and as a species that depends on salmon runs. The ecological connection between salmon, forests, and corvids is a subject of ongoing ecological research.

- **Raven territoriality.** Narratives depicting Raven's vast territorial range mirror the actual territorial behavior of Common Ravens, which defend territories spanning tens of square kilometers.

- **Raven-crow differences.** Some traditions distinguish between Raven and Crow as separate characters with different attributes -- mirroring the ecological and behavioral differences between the two species documented in Western ornithology.

The existence of this ecological knowledge within cultural traditions raises questions about the relationship between "indigenous knowledge" and "scientific knowledge." They are not rival systems; they are complementary perspectives on the same ecological reality, developed through different methodologies over different timescales.

## 21. OCAP and CARE Principles

This module adheres to OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics) principles for working with Indigenous knowledge [10]:

**OCAP** asserts that Indigenous communities own and control their cultural knowledge. This module does not claim to present definitive accounts of any nation's Raven traditions. It summarizes publicly available information from published sources for educational purposes.

**CARE** emphasizes that data and knowledge related to Indigenous peoples should benefit those peoples, should be collected and used under their authority, and should be handled responsibly and ethically.

Practical applications in this module:
- **Nation-specific attribution:** Every tradition is attributed to a specific nation. No generic "Indigenous" or "Native American" labels are used.
- **Published sources:** Information is drawn from published, publicly available sources. No restricted or sacred knowledge is presented.
- **Oral tradition acknowledged:** Where information derives from oral tradition, this is acknowledged rather than presenting oral narratives as if they were published literature.
- **Living traditions:** Raven traditions are described as living cultural practices, not historical curiosities.
- **No appropriation:** This module describes traditions for educational purposes; it does not claim to interpret, reinterpret, or extend them.

## 22. What Western Science Learned Late

A candid assessment of the relationship between Indigenous knowledge and Western science regarding corvids:

- Indigenous nations of the Northwest Coast recognized corvid intelligence, social complexity, and ecological relationships centuries before Western science documented the same phenomena.
- The specific mechanisms identified by Western science (facial recognition, episodic-like memory, tool use, theory of mind) provide additional detail, but the fundamental recognition of corvid intelligence was not new information.
- Western taxonomy's 160-year struggle with the Northwestern Crow -- creating, demoting, restoring, and finally dissolving a species boundary -- was an attempt to impose categorical order on biological variation that Indigenous peoples navigated without the need for categorical species boundaries.
- The observation that ravens and wolves form ecological partnerships was encoded in Indigenous knowledge long before Western ecology described the same relationship as a "mutualistic scavenging association."

This is not to diminish the value of Western scientific methodology. Peer-reviewed research provides mechanisms, quantification, and reproducibility. But it is to recognize that Western science arrived at some conclusions late, and that the traditions it sometimes dismisses as "mythology" contain empirical ecological knowledge of substantial depth and accuracy.
