## Overview

The story of Geggy Tah begins not with two musicians but with ten -- a sprawling, anarchic experimental collective called CoCu that operated at the margins of the Los Angeles club scene in the late 1980s. From that collective emerged a duo whose creative DNA would eventually thread through some of the most commercially successful pop music of the 21st century. But in 1993, when David Byrne received their demo tape, they were simply two musicians from Pomona, California, who had absorbed everything -- jazz, punk, found-sound experimentation, children's songs -- and produced something that Byrne described as "truly like nothing else."

This module traces the path from CoCu's formation circa 1987 through Geggy Tah's signing to Luaka Bop in 1993, documenting the creative foundations, the individuals involved, and the unlikely chain of events that placed an American experimental duo on a label built to showcase the music of the world.

## CoCu: Collaborating Cultures

### The Collective Model

Tommy Jordan formed CoCu -- an acronym variously standing for **Collaborating Cultures** or **Combating Cultures** -- in approximately 1987 in Los Angeles. The group was deliberately heterogeneous: ten members divided between five highly trained musicians and five non-musicians. This was not a band in any conventional sense. Jordan described CoCu as "more of a workshop than a band," emphasizing audience involvement and collaborative experimentation over rehearsed performance.

> "More of a workshop than a band." -- Tommy Jordan, describing CoCu's operating philosophy

The collective's named musicians included:

| Member | Role | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| Tommy Jordan | Founder, vocals, multi-instrumentalist | Based in Pomona, CA |
| John McKnight | Musician | Later introduced Kurstin to Jordan |
| Danny Moynahan | Musician | Part of the trained-musician contingent |
| Greg Kurstin | Keyboards (joined 1988) | Auditioned via McKnight connection |
| *+ 1 additional musician* | Unknown | Completing the five-musician cohort |

The five non-musicians are not individually documented in available sources, but their presence was architecturally significant. CoCu's creative model was built on the premise that the collision between trained musicianship and untrained creative impulse would produce something neither group could achieve alone. This is an early expression of what would later become Geggy Tah's core artistic principle: the *inclusionary wave* -- the idea that every influence, every skill level, every emotional register is valid material.

### The LA Club Scene

CoCu established residencies at two exclusive Los Angeles clubs:

- **Flaming Colossus** -- An LA venue known for experimental and boundary-pushing music
- **Vertigo** -- Part of the 1980s LA club circuit that supported avant-garde performance

These were not mainstream venues. The late-1980s LA music scene was dominated by the tail end of hair metal on the Sunset Strip and the emerging punk/alternative underground. CoCu occupied neither space -- they were experimental in a way that drew from jazz, world music, and performance art traditions rather than rock conventions. Their residencies at Flaming Colossus and Vertigo positioned them in a creative stratum that attracted attention from unexpected quarters.

The LA club scene of this period was extraordinarily stratified. At the commercial end: the Sunset Strip clubs (The Roxy, The Whisky, The Viper Room) hosting bands competing for major-label attention. At the underground end: house shows, warehouse parties, and the emerging rave scene. CoCu's residency venues sat in a different zone entirely -- spaces that valued experimentation and audience participation over commercial potential or scene credibility. The collective's emphasis on involving non-musicians and creating a "workshop" atmosphere aligned with performance art traditions more than with rock club conventions.

This positioning was both creatively liberating and commercially isolating. CoCu never had a natural industry pathway -- no A&R representatives were scouting Flaming Colossus for the next signing. The attention they attracted came from artists (Bono) and label founders (eventually Byrne) who were themselves operating outside conventional industry structures. The path from CoCu to Luaka Bop ran entirely through the alternative infrastructure of personal connections, demo tapes, and creative recognition -- never through the standard industry pipeline of showcases, management deals, and bidding wars.

### Bono and the Studio Sessions

Among those who took notice was **Bono** of U2. The details of how Bono encountered CoCu are not extensively documented, but the outcome is concrete: Bono purchased **three weeks of studio time** for the group and requested that Jordan send him the resulting recordings.

This is a remarkable detail for several reasons. In the late 1980s, Bono and U2 were at the peak of their commercial and cultural influence, coming off *The Joshua Tree* (1987) and *Rattle and Hum* (1988). For Bono to invest in studio time for an obscure ten-member experimental collective in LA speaks to the raw creative energy CoCu was generating in their live performances. It also establishes a pattern that would repeat throughout Geggy Tah's career: established artists of significant stature recognizing something exceptional in this group's work, even when commercial structures couldn't categorize it.

### Greg Kurstin Joins

Greg Kurstin auditioned for and joined CoCu in **1988**, introduced to Tommy Jordan by **John McKnight**. Kurstin was already a formally trained jazz musician -- he had studied at The New School for Jazz and Contemporary Music in New York City under **Jaki Byard**, who had been pianist for Charles Mingus. He had also earned a degree from the California Institute of the Arts.

The McKnight introduction was pivotal. Without it, the Jordan-Kurstin partnership that became Geggy Tah -- and the subsequent creative lineage that produced Adele's "Hello," Sia's "Chandelier," and nine Grammy Awards -- would not exist. McKnight is one of those figures in music history who appears at a single critical juncture and changes everything by making a connection.

Within CoCu, Jordan and Kurstin discovered a creative complementarity that went beyond the collective's workshop model. Jordan brought multi-instrumental versatility, lyrical imagination, and a willingness to treat any sound source as music. Kurstin brought jazz training, harmonic sophistication, and the kind of keyboard fluency that could navigate any genre. Together, they began experimenting as a duo in **mid-1988**, even while CoCu continued.

## The Duo Emerges

### From Workshop to Partnership

After CoCu disbanded in the **early 1990s** (exact date undocumented), Jordan and Kurstin focused exclusively on their partnership. The transition from a ten-member collective to a duo was not a reduction but a distillation. Everything CoCu had been -- the collision of trained and untrained, the embrace of audience participation, the refusal to observe genre boundaries -- carried forward into the duo's work, but now filtered through two musicians who had developed an intuitive creative language over several years of collaboration.

The shift also reflected a practical reality: it is extraordinarily difficult to sustain a ten-member experimental collective. Economics, logistics, and the sheer complexity of coordinating ten creative personalities make such groups inherently unstable. The duo format gave Jordan and Kurstin creative freedom with minimal overhead -- a configuration that would prove ideal for their home-recording approach.

The distillation process was not instantaneous. Between mid-1988 (when Jordan and Kurstin began their duo experiments within CoCu) and the early 1990s (when CoCu disbanded), the two operated in parallel -- participating in the collective while simultaneously developing their own creative language. This overlap period was formative: they could test ideas in the pressure cooker of CoCu's ten-person workshop and then refine them in the intimacy of duo sessions. By the time CoCu ended, Jordan and Kurstin had already developed the creative partnership that would define Geggy Tah -- the collective's dissolution was a formality that recognized what had already happened organically.

The demo recordings from this transitional period -- the material that would eventually reach David Byrne -- were products of the duo's home-recording practice. Working outside professional studios, Jordan and Kurstin developed the techniques that would characterize all three Geggy Tah albums: unconventional sound sources, domestic acoustics treated as creative material rather than obstacles, and a willingness to let the recording environment shape the music rather than controlling it. The home studio was not a budget compromise -- it was an artistic choice, consistent with Jordan's philosophy that "music is an expression of life" and that life happens at home, not in isolation booths.

### The Name

The name "Geggy Tah" derived from the musicians' first names, filtered through the mispronunciations of childhood:

- **"Geggy"** -- How Greg Kurstin's younger sister pronounced "Greg" as a toddler
- **"Tah"** -- How Tommy Jordan's twin sister called him throughout their childhood

This origin story is significant beyond being a charming anecdote. The name embeds the band's aesthetic in its very identity: language as it comes through the filter of someone who hasn't yet learned the rules. A toddler doesn't mispronounce a name -- they pronounce it the way it sounds to them, uncorrupted by convention. "Geggy Tah" is the sound of "Greg and Tommy" heard by ears that haven't been trained to hear it "correctly." The inclusionary wave starts with the name itself.

### The Demo Tape

In **1993**, a demo tape from Jordan and Kurstin reached David Byrne. The exact mechanism of how the tape arrived at Byrne's desk is not documented in detail in available sources, but the response it generated is extensively recorded. Byrne described it as:

> "One of the weirdest, most surprising things we'd ever heard. It was truly like nothing else."

This was not faint praise. Byrne, as the frontman of Talking Heads and the founder of Luaka Bop, had spent a decade actively seeking out the most unusual, boundary-dissolving music on the planet. For him to characterize a demo as "truly like nothing else" indicates that Geggy Tah had achieved something genuinely novel -- not merely eclectic but synthesized into a coherent artistic voice that defied existing categories.

## David Byrne and Luaka Bop

### The Signing

Byrne signed Geggy Tah to **Luaka Bop** in **1993**. This was an unusual decision for several reasons, all of which illuminate both the band's character and the label's curatorial philosophy.

Luaka Bop had been founded by Byrne in **1988** to release music from outside the United States -- initially Brazilian compilations, expanding to Cuba, Africa, and beyond. The label's identity was built on international curation: discovering and presenting music that American audiences might otherwise never encounter. Every significant release on the label's roster reinforced this identity.

Geggy Tah was an American band from Pomona, California. They did not fit. **That was the point.**

Byrne's willingness to sign an American duo to a label defined by its international scope reveals a crucial aspect of his curatorial philosophy: what mattered was not where the music came from, but whether it represented a genuinely different way of thinking about sound. Geggy Tah's inclusionary aesthetic -- absorbing jazz, funk, punk, found sound, children's songs, and experimental noise into a seamless whole -- was as alien to the American mainstream as anything coming out of Lagos or Sao Paulo. They were outsiders at home.

### An American Band on a World Label

The paradox of Geggy Tah's position on Luaka Bop would persist throughout their career. The label's audience expected to discover unfamiliar sounds from unfamiliar places. An American band, no matter how strange, occupied a different category in the listener's imagination. This positioning may have contributed to the commercial challenges Geggy Tah faced: they were too experimental for mainstream rock audiences and not "exotic" enough for the Luaka Bop core audience.

> See also: [Cultural Context](05-cultural-context.md) for extended analysis of the Luaka Bop paradox and curatorial identity

The tension was productive, though. It meant that Geggy Tah's label home was never generic. They shared a roster with Os Mutantes, Tim Maia, Susana Baca, and later William Onyeabor and Alice Coltrane Turiyasangitananda. The company they kept -- even if most listeners never thought to draw the connection -- reinforced the cosmopolitan, boundary-dissolving character of their music.

### Byrne's Assessment

Byrne's published statements about Geggy Tah constitute some of the most articulate characterizations of the band's aesthetic from any source. Three key quotes, all attributed through Wikipedia and Luaka Bop documentation:

1. **"One of the weirdest, most surprising things we'd ever heard. It was truly like nothing else."** -- On receiving their demo tape.

2. **"A cross between Ween and Prince, but with deeper and more articulate lyrics."** -- Positioning them in relation to established artists. This comparison is telling: Ween for the genre-dissolving eclecticism and willingness to inhabit multiple musical personas; Prince for the multi-instrumental virtuosity and complete creative self-sufficiency. The qualifier "deeper and more articulate lyrics" elevates them beyond both comparisons.

3. **"So postmodern they've come out the other side."** -- The most philosophically significant assessment. This characterization suggests that Geggy Tah's approach to genre wasn't the knowing pastiche of postmodern irony but something that had passed through irony and emerged into genuine synthesis. They weren't quoting genres -- they were absorbing them. The distinction matters: pastiche keeps its references at arm's length; synthesis metabolizes them.

> See also: [Cultural Context](05-cultural-context.md) for Byrne's "inclusionary wave" concept and its relationship to postmodern aesthetics

## Musical Training and Foundations

### Kurstin's Jazz Education

Greg Kurstin's formal training was extensive and distinguished:

| Institution | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| The New School for Jazz and Contemporary Music | Studied under Jaki Byard (Charles Mingus's pianist) |
| California Institute of the Arts | Degree earned |
| Village Vanguard | Residency as jazz pianist |
| Peers | Performed with Bobby Hutcherson and George Coleman |

The Jaki Byard connection is particularly notable. Byard (1922-1999) was one of the most eclectic pianists in jazz history -- equally comfortable playing stride, bebop, free jazz, and classical music, often within the same performance. His approach to the instrument was encyclopedic rather than doctrinaire: every style was material. As Mingus's pianist, Byard participated in some of the most ambitious compositions in jazz history, works that demanded the ability to shift between musical languages within a single piece. This is precisely the sensibility Kurstin would bring to Geggy Tah and, later, to his work as a pop producer. The through-line from Byard to Geggy Tah to "Hello" by Adele runs through an approach to music-making that treats genre boundaries as suggestions.

The New School's jazz program (now part of The New School for Jazz and Contemporary Music) was not a conservatory in the traditional sense. Founded in 1986, it emphasized creative individuality and cross-genre exploration alongside rigorous technique -- a pedagogical philosophy that aligned with CoCu's workshop ethos more than with a traditional jazz conservatory's focus on canonical repertoire. For Kurstin, the school provided both the technical vocabulary and the intellectual permission to be eclectic -- a permission that Byard himself embodied.

Bobby Hutcherson (vibraphone) and George Coleman (saxophone) were both established jazz musicians whose work spanned hard bop, post-bop, and modal jazz. Performing with them, and holding a residency at the legendary Village Vanguard -- the most storied jazz club in America, a Greenwich Village institution since 1935 -- gave Kurstin a depth of live performance experience and harmonic vocabulary that most pop musicians never acquire.

The Village Vanguard residency deserves emphasis. The Vanguard is not a club that books unknown musicians casually. A residency there indicates recognition by the jazz community that Kurstin was operating at a professional level before he joined CoCu, before Geggy Tah existed, and long before he produced his first pop hit. His subsequent career as a pop producer is sometimes presented as a departure from his jazz roots, but the jazz roots were professional-grade, not amateur -- a foundation that gave him the harmonic and improvisational toolkit to work in any genre.

> See also: [Post-Geggy Tah Trajectories](04-post-geggy-tah-trajectories.md) for how Kurstin's jazz training manifested in his Grammy-winning production work

### Jordan's Multi-Instrumental Path

Tommy Jordan's training and creative identity were different in character from Kurstin's. Based in Pomona, California, Jordan has been described variously as a vocalist, multi-instrumentalist, bassist, and songwriter. When asked about his primary instrument, Jordan's response was revealing: he described his primary instrument as **writing** rather than any single instrument.

This is a critical distinction. Where Kurstin's identity was rooted in instrumental mastery and harmonic knowledge, Jordan's was rooted in composition and creative vision. He was the conceptual engine -- the one who could imagine a song that incorporated dog claw scratches, garage door squeals, and submerged drums, and who had the multi-instrumental fluency to execute that vision across whatever instruments the idea required.

Jordan wrote the song that would become Geggy Tah's most commercially successful track -- "Whoever You Are" -- at approximately **age 8**, as a way of thanking thoughtful drivers and cheering up his father during frustrating commutes. The fact that a childhood composition became a Billboard-charting single speaks to the durability of Jordan's melodic instinct and to Geggy Tah's willingness to honor the creative impulses that preceded formal training.

> See also: [Discography](02-discography.md) for the full story of "Whoever You Are" from childhood composition to Mercedes-Benz commercial

### The Complementary Dynamic

The Jordan-Kurstin partnership worked because their strengths were complementary rather than competitive:

| Dimension | Jordan | Kurstin |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Training | Self-directed, multi-instrumental | Formal jazz (New School, CalArts) |
| Creative mode | Conceptual, visionary, lyrical | Harmonic, structural, virtuosic |
| Instrument identity | Writing is the instrument | Keyboard is the instrument |
| Sound palette | Found sounds, unconventional sources | Jazz standards, synthesizers, organs |
| Role in duo | Vocals, bass, lyrics, creative direction | Keyboards, guitar, arrangement |

This complementarity echoes other great creative partnerships in music: Lennon's raw emotional directness paired with McCartney's melodic sophistication; Jagger's performance energy paired with Richards' riff architecture. The difference is that Jordan and Kurstin were operating in a space far more experimental than any of these precedents, and their partnership was forged not in the pressure cooker of commercial ambition but in the low-stakes creative laboratory of CoCu.

The partnership also benefited from a shared willingness to subordinate ego to process. In CoCu, both men had learned to work within a collective -- to contribute ideas without owning them, to follow another musician's lead without resentment, and to value the ensemble result over individual credit. These habits, forged in a ten-person workshop, proved even more valuable in a duo, where every creative decision is a negotiation between two equally invested participants. The durability of the Jordan-Kurstin partnership across three albums and nearly a decade of collaboration suggests that the CoCu experience gave them interpersonal tools as well as musical ones.

The contrast between their training backgrounds also created a productive tension. Kurstin's jazz education provided a vocabulary of harmonic possibility -- the knowledge of what *could* be done with chords, voicings, and rhythmic structures. Jordan's self-directed, multi-instrumental approach provided a vocabulary of creative permission -- the conviction that anything *should* be tried, regardless of whether it fit within established musical frameworks. Jazz training tells you where the notes can go; inclusionary instinct tells you that non-notes are equally valid. Together, these orientations produced music that was harmonically sophisticated but sonically wild -- the combination that critics struggled to name and that Byrne recognized as something genuinely new.

## The Pomona Scene

Pomona, California, sits approximately 30 miles east of downtown Los Angeles in the Inland Empire. It is not a music industry city. There are no major recording studios, no concentration of labels, no scene infrastructure comparable to Hollywood, Silver Lake, or even Long Beach. This matters because Geggy Tah's creative development happened outside the gravitational pull of the LA music industry. They were close enough to access the city's clubs and recording facilities when needed but far enough away that the industry's commercial pressures and stylistic fashions didn't shape their aesthetic.

The home-recording approach that characterized all three Geggy Tah albums -- particularly the unconventional sound sources and lo-fi textures that reviewers consistently noted -- was partly a product of this geographic position. When you're recording at home in Pomona rather than at a commercial studio in Hollywood, the sounds available to you are different: neighborhood noises, household objects, the acoustic character of residential spaces rather than purpose-built rooms. Geggy Tah's aesthetic of inclusionary eclecticism was, in part, an aesthetic of *place* -- the sounds of a specific California suburb absorbed into the music alongside jazz chords and funk grooves.

Pomona's position in the Inland Empire also placed the band in a demographic and cultural context distinct from coastal LA. The Inland Empire in the 1990s was working-class, suburban, and culturally diverse -- a region where the music industry's glamour and pretension were distant abstractions. The authenticity that critics and peers recognized in Geggy Tah's work may owe something to this remove: they were not performing their outsider status for a hipster audience; they were genuinely outside, making music in a place where nobody was watching.

The city has since developed its own creative identity -- Pomona's arts district has grown significantly since the 2000s -- but during Geggy Tah's formative period, it was simply home: a quiet, affordable place where two musicians could work without interference, develop their sound without pressure, and record albums in spaces that smelled like dinner cooking rather than acoustic foam and coffee.

The timeline reveals a six-year creative gestation: from CoCu's
formation circa 1987 through the Luaka Bop signing in 1993,
Jordan and Kurstin had roughly six years of collaborative
experimentation -- first within the collective, then as a
duo -- before releasing their first album.
This extended incubation period is unusual
in popular music, where bands typically form and seek
recording deals within one to two years.
The length of the gestation suggests
that the creative synthesis Byrne heard in the demo
was not a sudden invention but the product
of sustained, deliberate artistic development.

The six-year gestation also meant that by the time Geggy Tah
entered a professional studio with Susan Rogers to record
*Grand Opening*, they were not a young band finding their
sound -- they were a mature creative partnership with
thousands of hours of collaborative experience.
This maturity is audible on the debut: *Rolling Stone*'s
assessment that the album "creates its own aural planet"
reflects a level of creative coherence that typically
requires multiple albums to achieve.
Geggy Tah arrived fully formed
because their formation had already taken half a decade.
