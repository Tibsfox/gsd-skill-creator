## Overview

Geggy Tah released three studio albums on Luaka Bop between 1994 and 2001. Each album expanded the duo's sonic vocabulary while maintaining the core principle that David Byrne identified as the "inclusionary wave" -- the practice of treating every genre, instrument, found sound, and emotional register as valid material. The catalog spans from a home-recorded debut co-produced with Prince's engineer to a final album featuring a legendary session drummer, a theremin pioneer, and a spoken-word performance from one of the most important figures in contemporary art.

The commercial arc is paradoxical: their most acclaimed work generated the least chart activity, while their sole charting single -- "Whoever You Are" -- was a song written by Tommy Jordan at age eight and achieved its greatest commercial success five years after its initial release, driven by a Mercedes-Benz television commercial. This is a discography defined by the gap between critical recognition and commercial penetration.

## Grand Opening (1994)

### Album Details

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Label** | Luaka Bop |
| **Year** | 1994 |
| **Producers** | Tommy Jordan, Greg Kurstin, Susan Rogers |
| **Executive Producers** | David Byrne, Yale Evelev |
| **Format** | CD |
| **Commercial Result** | Not a commercial success; critical acclaim |
| **Genre** | Experimental pop / alternative rock / jazz-inflected |

### Production: Susan Rogers

The album was co-produced by Jordan and Kurstin with **Susan Rogers**. Rogers' credentials at the time of the *Grand Opening* sessions were extraordinary: she had served as Prince's staff engineer from 1983 to 1988, engineering *Purple Rain*, *Around the World in a Day*, *Parade*, *Sign o' the Times*, and *The Black Album*. She had also worked with The Jacksons, David Byrne, and Barenaked Ladies.

Rogers typically produced higher-profile musicians. She accepted a **lower fee** to work with Geggy Tah after hearing their demo. This decision proved transformative -- not just for the album but for Rogers herself. In a Tape Op interview, she described the experience:

> "From those two guys [Jordan and Kurstin] I learned the art and the craft of record making in a whole new way."

> "Tommy taught me that music is an expression of life. Life is not perfect. Life has mistakes, and it's flawed. It's really beautiful, and it's really ugly. It smells pretty, and it stinks. Life is simple as pie, and it's complicated. Life is mysterious and dark, and life is bright and dazzling. So your music should be all of that."

For Rogers, a veteran of Prince's meticulously controlled Paisley Park environment, the contrast with Geggy Tah's home-studio eclecticism was revelatory. She described the experience as having "changed her life and boosted her career by teaching her to think differently about what music was and could be."

> See also: [The Creative Network](03-the-creative-network.md) for Rogers' full biography and the deeper implications of this creative relationship

### Sound and Reception

*Rolling Stone* provided the most frequently cited assessment of the debut:

> The duo's "classical and jazz backgrounds prop up songs whose structural inventiveness rewards saturation listening."

The review characterized *Grand Opening* as "a debut that creates its own aural planet." This is strong language for a review of an unknown band on an indie label, and it captures something essential about the album: it was not derivative of any existing genre or scene. The jazz training was audible but not foregrounded; the experimental impulse was present but not alienating; the pop sensibility was genuine but not pandering.

### The "Aural Planet"

The "aural planet" metaphor deserves unpacking because it captures the fundamental difference between eclecticism and synthesis. An eclectic album hops between genres -- a jazz track here, a punk track there, a folk ballad in between. The listener recognizes each genre as a separate entity being visited. Geggy Tah's *Grand Opening* did something different: it dissolved the boundaries between genres within individual songs and across the album as a whole, creating a unified sonic world that owed debts to multiple traditions but belonged to none of them.

This is the practical expression of the inclusionary wave. It is also, arguably, what made the album commercially challenging. When a listener or a record store clerk or a radio programmer asks "what does this sound like?", the honest answer -- "nothing else" -- is simultaneously the highest compliment and the most effective commercial death sentence.

## Sacred Cow (1996)

### Album Details

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Label** | Luaka Bop |
| **Year** | 1996 |
| **Tracks** | 13 |
| **Producers** | Susan Rogers, Tommy Jordan, Greg Kurstin |
| **Executive Producers** | David Byrne, Yale Evelev |
| **Added Member** | Daren Hahn (drums) |
| **Hit Single** | "Whoever You Are" (#16 Billboard Modern Rock) |
| **Tour Partners** | Sting, Soul Coughing, Barenaked Ladies |
| **Key Guest** | Glen Phillips (Toad the Wet Sprocket) on "House of Usher" |

### Becoming a Trio: Daren Hahn

For *Sacred Cow*, Geggy Tah expanded to a trio with the addition of **Daren Hahn** on drums. This was a significant shift: the duo's first album had relied on programmed rhythms, Jordan's percussion, and the kind of rhythmic flexibility that comes from two musicians who think as one. Adding a dedicated drummer introduced a new rhythmic foundation -- more structured, more powerful, and more suitable for the live performance circuit that the band was about to enter.

Hahn's addition enabled the touring that would define the *Sacred Cow* era. A two-piece experimental band playing support slots for Sting requires a rhythmic solidity that the duo format couldn't consistently deliver in arena-scale venues.

### The Sound of Sacred Cow

*Entertainment Weekly* reviewed the album, noting that it dared to "be taken seriously" with its "blend of punk, funk, and prog-rock." The review also observed that "the silliness is tempered by keen musicianship and closet tenderness." This captures the tonal balance that made *Sacred Cow* the band's most commercially viable work: the experimental impulse was still present, but it was wrapped in arrangements accessible enough for radio play.

The album was recorded at home with producer Susan Rogers -- her second collaboration with the band. The home-recording environment continued to shape the album's sonic character, even as the band's growing ambition pushed toward bigger sounds.

### "Whoever You Are" -- The Complete Story

The story of "Whoever You Are" is one of the most unusual hit-single origin stories in alternative rock history. It unfolds across decades:

**Composition (circa 1978):** Tommy Jordan wrote the tune and refrain at approximately age 8. The song was a way of thanking thoughtful drivers who allowed lane changes and cheering up his father, who sometimes became frustrated in traffic. The lyrics center on gratitude toward considerate strangers -- a childhood expression of kindness as social contract.

**Recording (circa 1995-1996):** The song was recorded for *Sacred Cow* with Susan Rogers producing. The home-recording approach preserved the song's simplicity while giving it a rhythmic foundation strong enough for radio.

**Initial Release (1996):** "Whoever You Are" was released as a single from *Sacred Cow*. The music video featured the band playing while driving, woven into footage from a vintage driving safety film. The video's aesthetic -- contemporary musicians intercut with archival educational footage -- reinforced the song's themes of traffic courtesy and civic mindfulness.

**Chart Performance:** The single peaked at **#16 on the Billboard Modern Rock Tracks chart**. This remains Geggy Tah's only charting single, the metric by which they are frequently categorized as a "one-hit wonder."

**The Mercedes-Benz Commercial (circa 2001):** In approximately 2001, **Mercedes-Benz** licensed "Whoever You Are" for a television commercial. This caused a significant resurgence in airplay and chart activity -- roughly five years after the single's initial release. A song about thanking polite drivers, written by an eight-year-old, became the soundtrack for a luxury car commercial. The irony was not lost on observers, but the commercial introduced the song to an audience far larger than Luaka Bop's distribution could have reached.

> See also: [Cultural Context](05-cultural-context.md) for analysis of the "one-hit wonder" characterization and what it obscures

The arc of "Whoever You Are" is a case study in the difference between artistic merit and commercial timing. The song's qualities -- its melodic directness, its lyrical warmth, its unusual origin story -- were constant. What changed was the mechanism of exposure. Without the Mercedes-Benz commercial, it would have remained a modest alternative rock hit from 1996. With it, the song reached an audience that had never heard of Geggy Tah, Luaka Bop, or David Byrne's curatorial project.

### Guest Appearances and Details

**Glen Phillips**, lead singer of **Toad the Wet Sprocket**, contributed guest vocals on the track "House of Usher." Phillips was a natural collaborator for Geggy Tah: Toad the Wet Sprocket occupied a similar space in the 1990s alternative landscape -- melodic, intelligent, genre-fluid, and commercially successful enough to survive but not dominant enough to define an era.

The final track on *Sacred Cow*, "Gina," was named after Susan Rogers' **Tera Terrier**, who appeared on both the *Grand Opening* and *Sacred Cow* album covers. This detail -- a producer's dog appearing on album artwork and having a song named after her -- captures the familial, low-key creative environment in which these albums were made. This was not a corporate production. These were friends making records together in living rooms.

### Touring: Sting, Soul Coughing, Barenaked Ladies

The *Sacred Cow* era marked Geggy Tah's most extensive touring period. Their support slots included:

| Tour Partner | Context |
|-------------|---------|
| **Sting** | Arena-scale touring; exposed GT to mainstream audiences |
| **Soul Coughing** | Alternative/experimental peers; M. Doughty's spoken-word-over-beats approach shared GT's genre-fluid sensibility |
| **Barenaked Ladies** | Pop-alternative crossover; Susan Rogers connection (she later co-produced their #1 hit "One Week") |

The Barenaked Ladies connection is particularly notable because it illustrates the network effect of the Susan Rogers relationship. Rogers produced both Geggy Tah and Barenaked Ladies; the two bands toured together; Rogers went on to co-produce "One Week" (from the 1998 album *Stunt*), which reached #1 on the Billboard Hot 100. The creative network centered on Geggy Tah radiated outward in multiple directions.

## Into the Oh (2001)

### Album Details

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Label** | Luaka Bop / Virgin |
| **Year** | 2001 |
| **Tracks** | 12 |
| **Originally Titled** | *Music Inspired by the Fragrance* |
| **New Members** | "Lavey" Bruce Millstein (percussion, synth, tabla) |
| **Notable Personnel** | Pamelia Kurstin (theremin), James Gadson (drums) |
| **Guest Appearances** | Laurie Anderson |
| **Additional Drums** | Cyril Atef (tracks 2 and 10) |
| **Mastered By** | Bob Ludwig (tracks 1-11) at Gateway Mastering |
| **Primary Recording** | Stephens 2" 16-track tape machine |
| **Genre (Discogs)** | Pop Rock, Downtempo, Avantgarde |

### The Title Change

*Into the Oh* was originally announced in **1999** as *Music Inspired by the Fragrance*. Two tracks -- "Space Heater" and "Sweat" -- were released as free MP3 downloads on the web, making Geggy Tah early adopters of digital distribution strategies that would become industry standard within a decade.

The album was delayed and retitled due to **Luaka Bop's transition from Warner Bros. to Virgin Records**. This label restructuring had significant implications beyond the title change: it disrupted the distribution infrastructure, promotional momentum, and release timeline. The gap between *Sacred Cow* (1996) and *Into the Oh* (2001) -- five years -- was long enough to lose whatever commercial momentum "Whoever You Are" had generated. By 2001, the alternative rock landscape had shifted dramatically, and the audience that might have followed up on *Sacred Cow* had largely moved on.

> See also: [Cultural Context](05-cultural-context.md) for analysis of the Luaka Bop/Warner/Virgin transition and its impact on the label's roster

### The Expanded Ensemble

For *Into the Oh*, the ensemble expanded significantly beyond the core duo:

| Musician | Instrument | Tracks/Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Tommy Jordan | Vocals, bass, multi-instrumentals | Throughout |
| Greg Kurstin | Pianos, clavinets, Wurlitzers, B-3 organs, Moog synthesizers, guitar | Throughout |
| Pamelia Kurstin | Theremin | Multiple tracks |
| James Gadson | Drums | "Holly Oak," "Love Is Alone," "Special Someone" |
| "Lavey" Bruce Millstein | Percussion, synth, tabla, backing vocals | LA session musician/composer |
| Cyril Atef | Drums | Tracks 2 and 10 |
| Laurie Anderson | Spoken word | "Aliens Somewhere" |
| Bob Ludwig | Mastering | Tracks 1-11 (Gateway Mastering) |

Kurstin's keyboard arsenal on this album was encyclopedic: pianos, clavinets, Wurlitzers, B-3 organs, and Moog synthesizers. This is a collection of instruments that spans the entire history of keyboard-based popular music, from the acoustic piano through the electromechanical instruments of the 1960s-70s to the analog synthesizers of the 1970s-80s. Each instrument carries its own sonic signature and cultural associations, and Kurstin's ability to deploy all of them within a single album -- moving fluidly between jazz harmony, funk clavinet grooves, and ambient synthesizer textures -- is a direct product of the eclectic training documented in the band's formation story.

### Pamelia Kurstin and the Theremin

**Pamelia Kurstin** (now Stickney) was introduced to the theremin during the production of *Into the Oh* in 1999 by Tommy Jordan and Greg Kurstin. She was a trained jazz upright bassist who had never played the instrument before. Her performances on the album mark the beginning of a career that would make her one of the world's foremost thereminists -- a path that led to collaboration with Bob Moog himself, appearances on Saturday Night Live, a TED talk, and a solo album on John Zorn's Tzadik label.

The theremin's presence on *Into the Oh* is not a novelty effect. Stickney developed a distinctive "walking bass" technique on the instrument, derived from her jazz background, that gave the theremin a harmonic and rhythmic role it rarely occupies. Bob Moog later called her "the instrument's true modern-day maestro."

> See also: [The Creative Network](03-the-creative-network.md) for Pamelia Stickney's full biography
> See also: [Post-Geggy Tah Trajectories](04-post-geggy-tah-trajectories.md) for her subsequent theremin career

### James Gadson

**James Gadson** contributed drums on three tracks: "Holly Oak," "Love Is Alone," and "Special Someone." Gadson is one of the most recorded session drummers in popular music history, with credits including Aretha Franklin, Bill Withers ("Lean on Me," "Use Me"), Marvin Gaye, Albert King, and hundreds of other recordings. His presence on a Geggy Tah album -- an experimental indie release on a boutique label -- speaks to the respect the band commanded among professional musicians.

Gadson's drumming on these tracks brought a warm, organic feel rooted in R&B and soul traditions, providing an anchoring rhythmic presence against which the album's more experimental elements could float.

### Laurie Anderson

**Laurie Anderson** provided a spoken-word appearance on "Aliens Somewhere." Anderson -- performance artist, musician, composer, and multimedia pioneer -- is one of the most important figures in American avant-garde art since the 1970s. Her work has consistently explored the intersections of technology, language, music, and visual art. A guest appearance by Anderson on a Geggy Tah album is a statement of creative kinship: these are artists who operate in the spaces between disciplines, treating the boundaries between music, speech, art, and everyday life as permeable.

### Recording Techniques

*Into the Oh* was recorded primarily on a **Stephens 2" 16-track tape machine**. The choice of analog tape in 2001 -- when digital recording was rapidly becoming industry standard -- was a deliberate aesthetic decision. Tape imparts a warmth, saturation, and gentle compression to recordings that digital formats of that era could not replicate. The 16-track limitation also imposed creative discipline: with only 16 tracks available, every recording decision is a commitment. There is no "we'll fix it later" with 16 tracks and tape.

The opening track, "Goodnight to the Machine," consisted of a **voicemail message** from one of the band members' grandfathers. Starting an album with an unprocessed voicemail -- a found recording of familial intimacy -- is the inclusionary wave in miniature: the boundary between "music" and "life" is treated as nonexistent. Everything is material. Everything is valid.

### Live Show Evolution

*Variety* reviewed the live show supporting *Into the Oh*, noting that Jordan "assembles bands -- sometimes on the fly -- that perform under the name Geggy Tah." This reveals a fluid, improvisational approach to live performance: the band was not a fixed lineup but a variable ensemble centered on Jordan and Kurstin. The review compared Jordan's lyrical sensibility to David Byrne's while noting that the rhythms "gently slide under the listener's feet and force them to respond."

This approach -- the core duo augmented by variable collaborators -- anticipates the kind of flexible, project-based ensemble structure that would become common in the 2010s indie scene. In 2001, it was unusual enough to warrant critical comment.

## Unconventional Sound Sources

Across all three albums, Geggy Tah incorporated sound sources that challenged the definition of "instrument." Documented examples include:

- **Dog claw scratches** -- Percussive textures derived from animal movement
- **Garage door squeals** -- Metallic, environmental sounds used as timbral elements
- **Submerged drums** -- Drums recorded underwater for altered acoustic properties
- **Voicemail recordings** -- Found audio from personal communications
- **Vintage driving safety film audio** -- Archival sound repurposed in music video context

These are not gimmicks or novelty effects. They represent a consistent aesthetic philosophy: if a sound has the right quality for a musical moment, its origin is irrelevant. A garage door squeal has as much right to exist in a song as a Moog synthesizer. This is the core of inclusionary aesthetics -- the refusal to privilege certain sound sources over others based on convention rather than sonic reality.

## The Three-Album Arc

Viewed as a whole, the three-album arc traces a progression:

| Album | Year | Character | Direction |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|
| *Grand Opening* | 1994 | Duo, home-recorded, Susan Rogers, "aural planet" | Establishing the language |
| *Sacred Cow* | 1996 | Trio (Hahn), touring, chart single, accessible | Engaging the world |
| *Into the Oh* | 2001 | Expanded ensemble, theremin, Gadson, Anderson, tape | Deepening the practice |

The arc is not the typical indie trajectory of "raw debut, polished sophomore, experimental third." Instead, it moves from intimate experiment to public engagement and back to intimate experiment -- but the final intimacy is richer, more populated with collaborators, and more technically ambitious than the first. *Into the Oh* is not a retreat but a deepening. The inclusionary wave expanded to absorb more voices, more instruments, more histories.
