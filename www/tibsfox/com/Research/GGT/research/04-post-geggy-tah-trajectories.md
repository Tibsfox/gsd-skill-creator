## Overview

After *Into the Oh* (2001), the members of Geggy Tah's creative orbit dispersed along radically different trajectories. Greg Kurstin became one of the most decorated producers in modern music history, winning nine Grammy Awards and co-writing songs that collectively represent billions of streams. Tommy Jordan continued as a creative multi-instrumentalist in the LA music scene, contributing a defining performance to one of the most successful debut albums of the 2000s but maintaining a relatively low public profile. Pamelia Stickney built an international career as a theremin virtuoso. Susan Rogers left the studio for academia, earning a doctorate in psychology and becoming a professor at Berklee.

These divergent paths illustrate a fundamental truth about the music industry: artistic excellence and commercial visibility are independent variables. The same creative environment -- Geggy Tah's home studio in Pomona -- produced a future nine-time Grammy winner and a musician whose extraordinary talent remained largely outside the commercial spotlight. The inclusionary wave dispersed, and the world received it unevenly.

## Greg Kurstin: The Producer's Arc

### Action Figure Party (1999)

Even before *Into the Oh* was released, Kurstin was expanding his creative portfolio. In **1999**, he released **Action Figure Party** on **Verve Records** -- a solo project under a pseudonym that allowed him to explore instrumental, jazz-inflected territory distinct from Geggy Tah's aesthetic. The Verve Records affiliation (a label with deep jazz roots) is notable: it connects Kurstin's post-Geggy Tah work back to his formal jazz training under Jaki Byard at The New School.

### Touring with Beck

In the **early 2000s**, Kurstin toured as a member of **Beck's** band. This was a pivotal transitional experience. Beck -- particularly in the *Midnite Vultures* (1999) and *Sea Change* (2002) era -- represented a sensibility closely aligned with the inclusionary wave: genre-fluid, multi-instrumental, simultaneously ironic and sincere, and commercially successful enough to sustain a touring band. Playing in Beck's ensemble gave Kurstin exposure to arena-scale production while reinforcing the aesthetic principles he had developed with Geggy Tah.

Beck's influence on Kurstin's subsequent production career is visible in the eclecticism that characterizes his biggest hits. A producer who can move from Adele's piano-ballad vulnerability to Foo Fighters' guitar-driven rock to Kendrick Lamar's hip-hop production is a producer who learned, somewhere, that genre boundaries are suggestions. That learning happened in CoCu, crystallized in Geggy Tah, and was reinforced on Beck's stage.

### The Bird and the Bee

In **2004**, Kurstin met **Inara George** (daughter of Lowell George, founder of Little Feat). Together they formed **The Bird and the Bee**, signing to **Blue Note Records** -- another jazz-heritage label. The Bird and the Bee's sound -- dreamy, synth-driven pop with sophisticated harmonic structures and George's airy vocals -- represented a distillation of elements from Kurstin's entire creative history: the jazz training, the Geggy Tah eclecticism, the multi-instrumental mastery, and a new comfort with pure pop accessibility.

The Bird and the Bee released multiple albums (self-titled, 2007; *Ray Guns Are Not Just the Future*, 2009; *Recreational Love*, 2015) and developed a cult following. Their cover album of Hall & Oates songs (*Interpreting the Masters Volume 1*, 2010) demonstrated the same "absorb and transform" approach that had defined Geggy Tah, applied to 1980s yacht rock with complete sincerity.

### The Production Breakthrough: Lily Allen

Kurstin's career as an independent producer took off with **Lily Allen**:

- **2006:** First major outside production: Allen's debut *Alright, Still*
- **2009:** Sole producer on Allen's *It's Not Me, It's You* -- the album that established Kurstin as a first-call pop producer. The album earned **3 Ivor Novello Awards** (the UK's premier songwriting honors).

The *It's Not Me, It's You* sessions marked a turning point. Kurstin demonstrated that he could take his eclectic musicianship and channel it entirely in service of a pop artist's vision, creating an album that was both commercially successful and sonically distinctive. The album's sound -- punchy, synth-forward, with unexpected harmonic turns -- bears traces of the Geggy Tah aesthetic filtered through mainstream pop production values.

### Grammy Dominance

Between 2017 and 2023, Kurstin won **nine Grammy Awards**, including back-to-back **Producer of the Year** honors in 2017 and 2018. The Grammy trajectory:

| Year | Awards | Key Work |
|------|--------|----------|
| 2017 | 4 Grammys | Record of the Year, Song of the Year, Album of the Year (Adele's *25*), Producer of the Year |
| 2018 | 1 Grammy | Producer of the Year (2nd consecutive) |
| 2023 | 3 Grammys | Including Best Rap Performance (Kendrick Lamar) |
| **Total** | **9 Grammys** | Across pop, rock, and hip-hop categories |

The breadth of genres represented in Kurstin's Grammy wins is remarkable. Producer of the Year typically goes to specialists -- producers identified with a particular sound or genre. Kurstin won it for work that spanned Adele's adult contemporary ballads, Foo Fighters' guitar rock, Sia's electronic pop, and Beck's alternative experimentalism. No other producer in recent history has demonstrated comparable range.

### The Complete Grammy Record

Kurstin's nine wins came from nominations across multiple categories:

- **Record of the Year** -- Adele, "Hello" (2017)
- **Song of the Year** -- Adele, "Hello" (2017)
- **Album of the Year** -- Adele, *25* (2017)
- **Producer of the Year, Non-Classical** -- (2017)
- **Producer of the Year, Non-Classical** -- (2018)
- **Best Rap Performance** -- Kendrick Lamar (2023)
- Plus 3 additional wins across other categories (2023)

### Kurstin on Geggy Tah

In a **PopMatters** interview (2015), Kurstin reflected on the Geggy Tah years:

> "It was such a different time in my life... I sort of grew up [with them] and it was an important part of growing up. I learned how to produce and record music. Everything happened."

This statement is significant because it locates the Geggy Tah period not as an early career footnote but as the **foundational experience** in which Kurstin learned the core skills that would define his subsequent career. "I learned how to produce and record music. Everything happened." The nine Grammy Awards, the Adele collaborations, the Kendrick Lamar productions -- all of it traces back to a home studio in Pomona where two musicians from CoCu taught themselves to make records.

### The Geggy Tah DNA in Pop Production

The creative principles Kurstin developed during the Geggy Tah years are visible throughout his mainstream production work:

| Geggy Tah Principle | Manifestation in Kurstin's Pop Production |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Multi-instrumental fluency | "He can play anything phenomenally" (Clarkson) |
| Genre dissolution | Producing across pop, rock, hip-hop, and electronic |
| Jazz harmonic vocabulary | Unexpected chord choices in pop contexts (Adele's "Hello") |
| Inclusionary aesthetics | Absorbing each artist's voice rather than imposing a "Kurstin sound" |
| Home-recording intimacy | Productions that feel personal even at massive commercial scale |

The through-line from Geggy Tah to "Hello" is not a line of stylistic imitation -- Adele's music sounds nothing like *Grand Opening*. It is a line of **creative philosophy**: the belief that every influence is valid material, that genre boundaries are artificial, and that music should express the full range of human experience.

## Tommy Jordan: The Quiet Path

### Jack Johnson's "Flake"

Tommy Jordan's most commercially visible post-Geggy Tah contribution came in **2000**, when he played **steel drums** on Jack Johnson's "Flake." The song was the debut single from Johnson's *Brushfire Fairytales* (2001), which became one of the most commercially successful debut albums of the 2000s.

"Flake" **topped the Billboard Triple-A chart for three weeks** and became the **best-performing song of 2002** on that chart listing. Jordan's steel drum performance is a defining element of the track's sound -- the bright, percussive melody that gives "Flake" its distinctive tropical character. Without Jordan's steel drums, "Flake" would be a different song entirely.

The Triple-A (Adult Album Alternative) chart is significant because it represents the radio format most aligned with Geggy Tah's own audience: listeners who value musicianship, eclecticism, and artistic integrity over genre purity. Jordan's contribution to the #1 Triple-A song of 2002 is a commercial achievement that operates in the same aesthetic space as Geggy Tah's own work, even if it arrived through a very different artist.

### Endorsements from Masters

Two of the most authoritative voices in American music have endorsed Tommy Jordan's talent:

**Ben Harper** called Jordan "one of the most creative and versatile multi-instrumentalists of our generation." Harper -- himself a multi-instrumentalist known for his work on lap steel guitar, Weissenborn slide guitar, and conventional guitar -- is not given to empty praise. His assessment positions Jordan alongside the most capable musicians working in the acoustic and alternative traditions.

**T Bone Burnett** -- producer, songwriter, and one of the most respected creative figures in the music industry -- described Jordan as "one of the five most creative people working in the music business." This is an extraordinary statement from someone whose production credits include the *O Brother, Where Art Thou?* soundtrack, Robert Plant and Alison Krauss's *Raising Sand*, and collaborations with Bob Dylan, Elvis Costello, and The Civil Wars.

When Ben Harper and T Bone Burnett both independently single out a musician as exceptional, the assessment carries weight disproportionate to the musician's public visibility. These are not industry figures who trade in hype -- they are artists and producers whose reputations are built on taste and judgment.

### Continued Creative Life

Jordan continued to perform and assemble bands in the LA area after Geggy Tah's final album. A **2025 podcast interview** (The Hustle, Episode 519) documented his continued music career, though the details of his activities between 2001 and 2025 are less publicly documented than Kurstin's high-profile trajectory.

What is known: Jordan continued to describe himself primarily as a **writer** rather than an instrumentalist, maintained his base in the Pomona/LA area, and continued the creative practice that had defined his work since the CoCu days -- treating every instrument and sound source as available material, assembling variable ensembles as projects required, and maintaining the "workshop" sensibility that had originated in the CoCu collective.

### The Visibility Gap

The gap between Jordan's and Kurstin's post-Geggy Tah visibility is significant but should not be interpreted as a measure of relative talent or creative value. The music industry's visibility mechanisms -- Grammy nominations, Billboard chart positions, major-label distribution -- are biased toward production and songwriting credits on hit records. Jordan's creative contributions have been to live performance, ensemble assembly, and multi-instrumental work in contexts that don't generate the same kinds of public metrics.

T Bone Burnett and Ben Harper both recognized Jordan's talent independently and publicly. That their assessments haven't been amplified into mainstream visibility says more about the industry's attention economy than about Jordan's capabilities.

## Pamelia Stickney: The Theremin Path

### The Kurstins

Before her solo career fully launched, Pamelia Stickney (then Pamelia Kurstin, married to Greg Kurstin) recorded **Gymnopedie** (2000) as "**The Kurstins**" -- a collaborative album with Greg Kurstin that bridged the gap between Geggy Tah and their independent careers. The album represents a transitional moment: the creative partnership between Kurstin and Stickney exploring territory distinct from Geggy Tah's aesthetic, with the theremin already becoming central to Stickney's musical identity.

### Bob Moog and the Instrument's Future

Stickney's relationship with **Robert Moog** represents one of the most significant outcomes of the Geggy Tah creative network. As the **primary test musician** for the **Etherwave Pro Theremin**, Stickney played a direct role in shaping the instrument's design. Her feedback influenced the sensitivity, range, and playability characteristics of the professional-grade theremin that Moog Music produced.

Moog's statement -- that Stickney was "the instrument's true modern-day maestro" -- is a canonizing endorsement from the person most qualified to make it. Moog (1934-2005) had spent his entire career in electronic music, beginning with theremin kits as a teenager in the 1950s. His Moog synthesizer changed the course of music history. For him to identify Stickney as the theremin's modern master is the equivalent of Stradivari identifying his era's finest violinist.

### Solo Work and Collaborations

Stickney's post-Geggy Tah career:

| Year | Work | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| 2000 | *Gymnopedie* (as The Kurstins) | Duo album with Greg Kurstin |
| 2004 | *Moog* documentary | Appearance documenting Moog's legacy |
| 2004 | Saturday Night Live | Live theremin performance on national television |
| 2007 | *Thinking Out Loud* (Tzadik Records) | Solo album on John Zorn's avant-garde label |
| Ongoing | TED talk | Demonstrated theremin technique to global audience |
| Ongoing | David Byrne | Collaboration with the man who signed Geggy Tah |
| Ongoing | Yoko Ono | Collaboration crossing music/art boundary |
| Ongoing | Bela Fleck and the Flecktones | Genre-crossing instrumental collaboration |
| Ongoing | Ulver | Norwegian experimental/electronic band |
| Ongoing | Seb Rochford, Barbez | European experimental scene collaborations |

The **Tzadik** label placement is significant. John Zorn's label is the gold standard for experimental and avant-garde music, releasing work by artists including Zorn himself, Marc Ribot, Ikue Mori, and dozens of other leading figures in experimental music. A solo album on Tzadik represents recognition at the highest level of the experimental music world.

The **David Byrne** collaboration closes a remarkable circle: the man who signed Geggy Tah to Luaka Bop went on to collaborate with a musician whose career was launched by the band he signed.

### Vienna and the European Scene

Stickney relocated to **Vienna, Austria** in **2005**, integrating into the European experimental music scene. Vienna's deep classical and avant-garde traditions -- from the Vienna Philharmonic to the electronic music communities that have thrived in the city since the 1960s -- provided a cultural context that valued the kind of rigorous instrumental mastery and experimental openness that Stickney embodied.

Her European work includes performances and collaborations across multiple countries, participation in festivals, and integration into a network of experimental musicians who share the Geggy Tah sensibility of treating genre boundaries as permeable. The geographic relocation -- from Southern California to Central Europe -- mirrors the artistic journey: from the American pop/alternative scene to the European experimental tradition.

## Susan Rogers: From Studio to Academy

Rogers' post-Geggy Tah trajectory is covered in detail in [The Creative Network](03-the-creative-network.md), but the key milestones bear repeating in the context of trajectories:

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 1998 | Co-produced Barenaked Ladies' *Stunt* (including #1 hit "One Week") |
| 2000s | Pursued doctorate at McGill University |
| Doctorate | Ph.D. in psychology (auditory perception) from McGill |
| Current | Associate Professor, Berklee College of Music |
| Current | Director, Berklee Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory |
| 2022 | Published *This Is What It Sounds Like* (W.W. Norton) |

The transformation from Prince's engineer to psychology researcher to Berklee professor is one of the most unusual career arcs in music history. Rogers credits the Geggy Tah experience as a crucial catalyst in this journey -- the sessions that taught her "music is an expression of life" and shifted her understanding of what music could be.

## The Divergent Trajectories

The four principal post-Geggy Tah trajectories form a pattern:

| Person | Path | Domain | Visibility |
|--------|------|--------|-----------|
| Greg Kurstin | Producer/songwriter | Mainstream pop | Global (9 Grammys) |
| Tommy Jordan | Multi-instrumentalist/writer | LA music scene | Local/specialized |
| Pamelia Stickney | Theremin virtuoso | International experimental | Niche but canonical |
| Susan Rogers | Academic/researcher | Music psychology | Academic/public intellectual |

Each trajectory represents a different way that the inclusionary wave dispersed into the broader culture:

- **Kurstin** carried it into the mainstream, where it became invisible -- absorbed so thoroughly into pop production practice that its origins in a Pomona home studio are forgotten.
- **Jordan** carried it forward in the form that most closely resembled its original expression -- live performance, variable ensembles, multi-instrumental creativity, workshop sensibility.
- **Stickney** carried it into a single instrument, pursuing the theremin with the same "everything is valid" philosophy that had characterized the Geggy Tah sessions where she first encountered it.
- **Rogers** carried it into intellectual inquiry, asking the scientific questions that the creative practice had raised intuitively.

None of these trajectories is more "successful" than the others in any meaningful sense. They are four different expressions of what happens when the inclusionary wave encounters the world beyond the studio.
