## Overview

Geggy Tah's creative network extends far beyond the Jordan-Kurstin partnership at its center. The band's orbit included Prince's legendary engineer, one of the most recorded session drummers in history, a performance artist who redefined the boundaries between technology and music, and a theremin player who didn't know the instrument existed until she was handed one during an album session. These are not peripheral figures. Each person who intersected with Geggy Tah carried away something that altered the trajectory of their subsequent work, and each brought something that altered the band's sound.

This module profiles the key individuals in the Geggy Tah creative network, tracing the influence flows in both directions -- what they gave to the band and what the band gave to them. The network is a case study in how creative relationships work in practice: not as transactions but as mutual transformations.

## Susan Rogers

### From Prince to Geggy Tah

**Susan Rogers** (born August 3, 1956, Southern California) is one of the most important figures in Geggy Tah's story -- and one of the most important figures in the broader history of recorded music.

Her career timeline leading to Geggy Tah:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1978 | Began career as audio technician at Audio Industries Corporation |
| 1983 | Became Prince's staff engineer at Paisley Park |
| 1983-1988 | Engineered *Purple Rain*, *Around the World in a Day*, *Parade*, *Sign o' the Times*, *The Black Album* |
| 1988 | Founded Prince's Vault -- the archival system that preserved recordings from the 2008 Universal Studios fire |
| 1988 | Left Prince's employ; relocated to Los Angeles |
| ~1993 | Heard Geggy Tah's demo; agreed to co-produce at reduced fee |
| 1994 | Co-produced *Grand Opening* |
| 1996 | Co-produced *Sacred Cow* |

Rogers' engineering credits with Prince alone would place her among the most consequential audio professionals of the 20th century. *Purple Rain* and *Sign o' the Times* are consensus masterpieces; *The Black Album* is one of the most mythologized unreleased-then-released recordings in pop history. The Prince's Vault she established proved its value in 2008 when the Universal Studios fire destroyed masters from dozens of artists -- Prince's recordings survived because Rogers had built the archival infrastructure to protect them.

When Rogers agreed to work with Geggy Tah, she was coming from the most high-profile, meticulously controlled recording environment in popular music. Prince's studio was a space of total creative authority -- one artist's vision executed with unlimited resources and absolute precision. Geggy Tah's home studio in Pomona was the opposite: two musicians in a domestic space, working with whatever was available, treating imperfection as material.

She accepted a **lower fee** than her normal rate. This was not charity -- it was artistic instinct. Rogers recognized something in the demo that warranted her investment.

### The Transformative Impact

Rogers' statements about her experience with Geggy Tah, documented primarily in a Tape Op Magazine interview (Issue 117) and in a WePresent/WeTransfer career profile, constitute some of the most revealing testimony about the band's creative process from any source.

The key statement, worth examining in full:

> "From those two guys [Jordan and Kurstin] I learned the art and the craft of record making in a whole new way."

This is a remarkable claim from someone who had spent five years engineering Prince's records. Rogers was not a novice being educated -- she was one of the most experienced recording professionals in the industry. For her to describe the experience as learning "the art and the craft of record making in a whole new way" indicates that Geggy Tah's approach represented a genuinely different paradigm, not merely a different style.

The elaboration makes the nature of that paradigm explicit:

> "Tommy taught me that music is an expression of life. Life is not perfect. Life has mistakes, and it's flawed. It's really beautiful, and it's really ugly. It smells pretty, and it stinks. Life is simple as pie, and it's complicated. Life is mysterious and dark, and life is bright and dazzling. So your music should be all of that."

This philosophy -- music as an unfiltered expression of life's full range, imperfections included -- stands in direct contrast to the hyper-controlled perfectionism of the Prince/Paisley Park environment. It's not that one approach is superior to the other; it's that they represent fundamentally different relationships with the recording process. Prince's approach says: "I will impose my vision on the sound with absolute precision." Jordan's approach says: "I will let life into the sound, including the parts that aren't perfect."

For Rogers, encountering this philosophy at a moment when she was transitioning away from Prince's orbit was catalytic. She described the experience as having "changed her life and boosted her career by teaching her to think differently about what music was and could be."

### After Geggy Tah: Barenaked Ladies and Academia

The influence of her Geggy Tah experience can be traced through Rogers' subsequent career:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1998 | Co-produced Barenaked Ladies' *Stunt*, including #1 hit "One Week" (Billboard Hot 100) |
| ~2000s | Returned to academic study |
| Doctorate | Ph.D. in psychology from McGill University (auditory perception research) |
| Current | Associate Professor at Berklee College of Music |
| Current | Director, Berklee Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory |
| 2022 | Published *This Is What It Sounds Like: What the Music You Love Says About You* (co-written with Ogi Ogas, W.W. Norton) |

Rogers' transition from studio production to academic research in the psychology of music perception can be read as an extension of the insight she credits to Tommy Jordan. If "music is an expression of life," then understanding music requires understanding the psychology of perception -- how and why sounds affect humans the way they do. Her academic work at Berklee, directing the Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory, is the scholarly formalization of questions that the Geggy Tah sessions raised intuitively.

The Barenaked Ladies connection is also significant. Rogers co-produced "One Week," which reached #1 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1998 -- the biggest commercial success of her production career. The Barenaked Ladies and Geggy Tah toured together during the *Sacred Cow* era. Rogers was the connective tissue between both acts, and the creative confidence she developed through the Geggy Tah sessions -- the willingness to embrace unconventional approaches and imperfection -- informed her work on a record that sold millions of copies.

### The Rogers Through-Line

The Susan Rogers through-line can be expressed as a creative transmission chain:

```
Prince (perfectionism, total control, virtuosity)
    |
    v
Rogers encounters Geggy Tah (imperfection, life-as-material, inclusionary aesthetics)
    |
    v
Rogers produces Barenaked Ladies' #1 hit (commercial breakthrough)
    |
    v
Rogers pursues doctorate in psychology of music perception
    |
    v
Rogers publishes book on how music affects human experience
    |
    v
Rogers teaches at Berklee (transmitting accumulated wisdom to next generation)
```

This chain illustrates how Geggy Tah's creative influence operated: not through direct commercial imitation but through the transformation of the people who encountered their work. Rogers didn't copy Geggy Tah's sound. She absorbed their philosophy and carried it forward in forms they could never have predicted.

## David Byrne

### The Curator as Collaborator

**David Byrne's** relationship with Geggy Tah was simultaneously artistic and institutional. As the founder of Luaka Bop and the executive producer on all three Geggy Tah albums, he occupied a dual role: the person who created the institutional space that made the band's career possible, and a creative collaborator whose own aesthetic was both reflected in and influenced by their work.

Byrne founded Luaka Bop in 1988 -- the name taken from the packaging of a Sri Lankan tea, "Luaka BOP" printed on a silver foil block, a phrase Byrne found "strange, but musical." The logo was conceived by Byrne and illustrated by **Tibor Kalman**, the legendary graphic designer. From the beginning, Luaka Bop was as much a design project and curatorial statement as a record label.

> See also: [Cultural Context](05-cultural-context.md) for extended analysis of Luaka Bop's curatorial philosophy

### Byrne's Published Assessments

Byrne's statements about Geggy Tah constitute the most authoritative external characterization of the band's artistic significance. Three key quotes:

1. **On the demo tape:** "One of the weirdest, most surprising things we'd ever heard. It was truly like nothing else."

2. **Positioning:** "A cross between Ween and Prince, but with deeper and more articulate lyrics."

3. **The defining assessment:** "Geggy Tah are so postmodern they've come out the other side... They incorporate so many disparate elements into their sound that one senses a new sensibility afoot, an inclusionary wave... like nothing I've heard before."

The third quote is the most significant. Byrne -- himself one of the most intellectually articulate musicians in rock history, the author of books on music, architecture, and bicycling -- identifies in Geggy Tah not just an unusual sound but a "new sensibility." He coins the term "inclusionary wave" to describe it, distinguishing it from postmodern pastiche by arguing that they have "come out the other side" of postmodernism.

This is a philosophical claim, not just a musical one. Postmodernism, in its musical expression, typically involves the knowing quotation of prior styles -- the wink that says "I know this is a funk groove, you know I know it, and we're both in on the joke." Byrne's claim is that Geggy Tah passed through this phase and emerged into genuine synthesis -- a place where the quotation marks dissolve and the genres become raw material rather than references.

### Executive Production Role

Byrne and **Yale Evelev** served as executive producers on all three Geggy Tah albums. The executive producer role in music varies widely in its practical implications -- it can mean anything from creative oversight to financial sponsorship. In the Luaka Bop context, Byrne and Evelev's executive production likely encompassed curatorial approval, label resource allocation, and a degree of creative mentorship, while leaving day-to-day production decisions to Rogers, Jordan, and Kurstin.

## Yale Evelev

**Yale Evelev** served as president of Luaka Bop and co-executive producer (with Byrne) on all three Geggy Tah albums. Evelev's role was operational as well as curatorial: he ran the business side of the label while Byrne provided artistic direction. Together, they made the signing decision that brought an American experimental duo onto a label defined by international music.

Evelev's contribution is less publicly documented than Byrne's, but his sustained presence across all three albums indicates consistent institutional support for the band throughout their active period -- including through the Warner Bros. to Virgin transition that delayed *Into the Oh*.

## Pamelia Stickney

### Introduction to the Theremin

**Pamelia Stickney** (born May 28, 1976, Southern California; credited as Pamelia Kurstin on *Into the Oh*) is perhaps the most dramatic example of Geggy Tah's creative influence on the people who entered their orbit. Trained as a **jazz upright bassist**, she was introduced to the theremin during the production of *Into the Oh* in **1999** by Tommy Jordan and Greg Kurstin.

She married Greg Kurstin (later divorced, reverting to the name Stickney). But the professional trajectory that the theremin introduction initiated is independent of personal relationships: it represents a creative awakening triggered by an encounter with an instrument she hadn't known existed.

### The Walking Bass Technique

Stickney developed a distinctive **"walking bass" theremin technique** derived from her jazz training. The theremin is typically associated with eerie, oscillating tones -- the soundtrack of 1950s science fiction films. Stickney's innovation was to apply jazz bassist's rhythmic and harmonic sensibility to the instrument, giving it a melodic articulation and rhythmic drive that expanded the theremin's expressive range beyond anything in its prior history.

This technique -- jazz vocabulary expressed through an electronic instrument most people associate with novelty -- is itself an expression of the inclusionary wave. It dissolves the boundary between "jazz bass" and "electronic theremin" by recognizing that the musical principles are transferable across any sound-producing mechanism.

### Bob Moog and the Etherwave Pro

Stickney's theremin artistry drew the attention of **Robert Moog** himself, the inventor of the Moog synthesizer and a lifelong advocate for the theremin (which had inspired his original interest in electronic music). She became the **primary test musician** for Moog's **Etherwave Pro Theremin**, playing an instrumental role in the instrument's final design. Her feedback directly shaped the professional-grade theremin that Moog Music produced.

Moog's assessment was unequivocal: he called her **"the instrument's true modern-day maestro."**

### Career After Geggy Tah

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2000 | Recorded *Gymnopedie* as "The Kurstins" with Greg Kurstin |
| 2004 | Appeared in documentary *Moog* |
| 2004 | Performed on Saturday Night Live |
| 2005 | Relocated to Vienna, Austria |
| 2007 | Solo album *Thinking Out Loud* on John Zorn's Tzadik label |
| Ongoing | TED talk on theremin technique |
| Ongoing | Collaborations with David Byrne, Yoko Ono, Bela Fleck and the Flecktones, Ulver, Seb Rochford |
| Current | Active in European experimental music scene; performed with group Barbez |

The career trajectory is extraordinary: from not knowing the theremin existed to being called its "true modern-day maestro" by the instrument's foremost advocate, all catalyzed by a studio introduction during a Geggy Tah session. Her solo album on Tzadik -- John Zorn's label for experimental and avant-garde music -- and her collaborations with artists ranging from Yoko Ono to the Norwegian experimental band Ulver demonstrate the range of her post-Geggy-Tah artistic life.

## James Gadson

### Session Drummer Legacy

**James Gadson** is one of the most prolific session drummers in the history of popular music. His credits include:

| Artist | Notable Work |
|--------|-------------|
| Aretha Franklin | Queen of Soul sessions |
| Bill Withers | "Lean on Me," "Use Me," "Ain't No Sunshine" sessions |
| Marvin Gaye | Multiple sessions |
| Albert King | Blues recordings |
| Dyke and the Blazers | Funk recordings |
| Charles Wright & the Watts 103rd Street Rhythm Band | LA soul/funk |

Gadson's drumming is characterized by deep pocket, rhythmic precision, and a warmth that comes from decades of playing in rhythm sections where the groove is everything. He is a musician's musician -- not widely known to the general public but revered by anyone who has studied R&B, soul, or funk drumming.

### Contribution to Into the Oh

Gadson contributed drums on three tracks from *Into the Oh*:

- **"Holly Oak"**
- **"Love Is Alone"**
- **"Special Someone"**

His presence on a Geggy Tah record might seem incongruous -- a legendary session drummer from the golden age of R&B playing on an experimental indie album. But this is precisely the point of the inclusionary wave: the boundary between "session professional from the Aretha Franklin tradition" and "experimental duo from Pomona" is a convention, not a physical law. Gadson's drumming on these tracks provided an organic, deeply grounded rhythmic foundation against which the album's more experimental elements -- theremin, found sounds, Moog synthesizers -- could float without losing their connection to the body.

## Daren Hahn

**Daren Hahn** joined Geggy Tah as a drummer for *Sacred Cow* (1996), making the duo a trio for their most commercially visible period. Hahn's addition enabled the extensive touring that defined the *Sacred Cow* era, including support slots with Sting, Soul Coughing, and Barenaked Ladies.

Hahn's contribution is structural rather than conceptual: he provided the rhythmic power and stage presence that a duo playing arena-scale venues with Sting required. The decision to expand to a trio for *Sacred Cow* and then to a larger ensemble for *Into the Oh* reflects the band's evolving understanding of how their music could be realized in different contexts.

## Laurie Anderson

**Laurie Anderson** provided a spoken-word appearance on "Aliens Somewhere" from *Into the Oh*. Anderson is one of the most important figures in American performance art and experimental music since the 1970s. Her work spans music, visual art, film, electronics, and language, consistently exploring the borders between disciplines.

Her 1982 single "O Superman" -- an eight-minute spoken-word piece with electronic accompaniment -- reached #2 on the UK Singles Chart, making her one of the only artists to achieve mainstream chart success with genuinely avant-garde work. Her presence on a Geggy Tah album is a gesture of mutual recognition between artists who share a fundamental commitment to dissolving boundaries between art forms.

Anderson's appearance also connects Geggy Tah to a broader lineage of New York-based experimental art. While the band's roots were in the LA club scene and Pomona's home-studio environment, the Anderson connection places them in conversation with the downtown Manhattan tradition of performance art, minimalist music, and intermedia experimentation.

---

## Glen Phillips

**Glen Phillips**, lead singer of **Toad the Wet Sprocket**, contributed guest vocals on "House of Usher" from *Sacred Cow*. Toad the Wet Sprocket was one of the more melodically sophisticated bands in the 1990s alternative landscape, known for literate lyrics and folk-influenced arrangements. Phillips' presence on a Geggy Tah track connects the band to the Southern California singer-songwriter tradition while illustrating the creative fluidity of the mid-90s alternative scene -- a period when artists moved between each other's projects with relative ease.

## The Network as System

Viewed as a system rather than a list of collaborators, the Geggy Tah creative network reveals several structural properties:

**Bidirectional influence:** Every significant collaborator both gave to and received from the Geggy Tah creative environment. Rogers learned a new philosophy of recording. Stickney discovered her life's instrument. Byrne found a living example of the aesthetic he had been curating. The network was not a hierarchy of influence flowing from established artists to an unknown band -- it was a mutual transformation engine.

**Cross-generational reach:** The network spans from James Gadson (active since the 1960s) through Laurie Anderson (1970s-present) to Pamelia Stickney (career launched in 1999). Geggy Tah served as a node connecting musical generations that might otherwise have remained isolated.

**Multiplier effect:** The creative energy that passed through the Geggy Tah network amplified as it dispersed. Rogers went on to produce a #1 hit and become a leading academic. Stickney became a world-class thereminist. Kurstin became one of the most decorated producers in modern music. The total creative output enabled by the network vastly exceeds what the band produced directly.

**The Amiga Principle in practice:** The network operated like the Amiga's custom chipset -- specialized components communicating through a shared bus. Jordan provided creative direction (Agnus/DMA), Rogers provided audio engineering excellence (Paula/I/O), Kurstin provided harmonic and instrumental mastery (Denise/output), and Byrne provided the institutional infrastructure (the bus itself). No single component dominated; each did its job brilliantly.
