## 1. The First Decision

Entity formation is the foundational act of business law. Before revenue, before hiring, before capital -- you choose the legal structure that will govern everything that follows. The choice determines who is personally liable for business debts, how income is taxed, how many owners can participate, what classes of ownership are available, and whether the business can raise capital from investors [1][2].

The decision is not merely administrative. It is architectural. A sole proprietorship chosen for simplicity leaves personal assets exposed to every business risk. An LLC chosen for flexibility may limit future fundraising options. A C-Corp chosen for its capital-raising power subjects all earnings to double taxation. Each structure is a set of tradeoffs, and the tradeoffs compound over time [1].

There is no universally correct answer. The right entity depends on the specific business -- its risk profile, tax situation, growth plans, ownership structure, and industry. What matters is that the choice is deliberate and informed, because changing entity types later is possible but never free [2].

## 2. Sole Proprietorship

The simplest business structure requires no formation filing at all. A sole proprietorship exists the moment a person begins conducting business. The owner files Schedule C with their personal Form 1040. There is no separate tax return, no annual report, no operating agreement [1][3].

The simplicity is genuine but so is the exposure. A sole proprietorship offers **zero liability protection**. Every business debt, every lawsuit, every contractual obligation reaches through to the owner's personal assets -- home, savings, vehicles. There is no legal separation between the person and the business [1][3].

Sole proprietorships are appropriate for very small, low-risk operations -- freelance writing, consulting, lawn care -- where the owner has minimal assets at risk and no employees. The moment a business takes on significant risk (employees, contracts, inventory, equipment), the absence of liability protection becomes a structural vulnerability [2].

**Tax treatment:** All income and losses flow directly to the owner's personal tax return (Schedule C). Subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base, 2.9% Medicare above that). No ability to split income between salary and distributions [3].

## 3. Partnerships

### General Partnership

A general partnership forms automatically when two or more persons associate to carry on a business for profit. Like sole proprietorships, general partnerships require no formation filing (though a partnership agreement is strongly advisable). Each general partner has unlimited personal liability for all partnership debts and obligations -- including those incurred by other partners [1][4].

The partnership files Form 1065 (informational return) and issues K-1s to each partner showing their share of income, deductions, and credits. Each partner reports their share on their personal return. The partnership itself pays no income tax [4].

### Limited Partnership (LP)

A limited partnership has at least one general partner (unlimited liability, management authority) and one or more limited partners (liability limited to investment, no management role). LPs are formed by filing a certificate of limited partnership with the state. They are commonly used for real estate investment, private equity funds, and family wealth planning [1][4].

### Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

An LLP extends liability protection to all partners. Each partner is protected from the malpractice, negligence, or misconduct of other partners (but remains liable for their own actions). LLPs are commonly used by professional firms -- law firms, accounting firms, architectural firms -- where professional liability insurance provides additional coverage [1][4].

## 4. Limited Liability Company

The LLC is the most versatile and most popular entity choice for small to mid-sized businesses in the United States. Created by filing articles of organization with the state, the LLC combines limited liability (member personal assets protected) with flexible tax treatment [1][5].

### Formation

LLC formation requires filing articles of organization with the state secretary of state, designating a registered agent (a person or service authorized to receive legal documents on behalf of the LLC), and paying a filing fee. Most states also require an operating agreement, though the form and content vary [5].

### Tax Flexibility

By default, a single-member LLC is a "disregarded entity" taxed like a sole proprietorship (Schedule C). A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership (Form 1065). But an LLC can elect to be taxed as an S-Corp (Form 2553) or C-Corp (Form 8832). This flexibility allows the entity to optimize its tax treatment as circumstances change [5][6].

### Operating Agreements

The operating agreement is the LLC's internal constitution. It defines: ownership percentages, capital contributions, profit and loss allocation, management structure (member-managed vs. manager-managed), voting rights, transfer restrictions, buyout provisions, dissolution procedures, and dispute resolution mechanisms [5].

In Washington, an LLC operating agreement can be oral, but written agreements are strongly advisable. Washington's LLC Act (RCW 25.15) provides default rules that apply in the absence of an operating agreement -- and those defaults may not match the members' intent [7].

### Liability Protection

LLC liability protection is not absolute. Courts may "pierce the LLC veil" if members fail to maintain separation between personal and business finances (commingling funds), fail to observe LLC formalities, undercapitalize the LLC, or use the LLC to perpetrate fraud. Maintaining a separate bank account, signing contracts in the LLC's name, and filing required annual reports preserves the liability shield [5].

## 5. S-Corporation

An S-Corp is not a distinct entity type. It is a tax election available to qualifying corporations (or LLCs that elect corporate taxation). The S-Corp election under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC Sections 1361-1379) allows corporate income to pass through to shareholders' personal returns, avoiding the double taxation that C-Corps face [1][8].

### Qualification Requirements

- Maximum 100 shareholders
- All shareholders must be U.S. citizens or resident aliens
- Only one class of stock (though voting and non-voting common shares are permitted)
- Shareholders cannot be other corporations, partnerships, or non-resident aliens
- Certain financial institutions, insurance companies, and domestic international sales corporations are ineligible [8]

### The Reasonable Salary Requirement

S-Corp shareholders who work in the business must receive a "reasonable salary" subject to payroll taxes (FICA). Distributions above reasonable compensation are not subject to self-employment tax. This creates a legitimate tax planning opportunity -- but the IRS scrutinizes S-Corp salaries that appear unreasonably low. "Reasonable" generally means what the market would pay a non-owner employee performing the same functions [8][9].

### S-Corp vs. LLC

The S-Corp election makes sense when the business consistently generates enough profit above reasonable compensation that the self-employment tax savings justify the additional administrative burden (payroll processing, quarterly payroll tax filings, separate tax return). For most businesses earning less than $80,000-$100,000 in net profit, the LLC taxed as a partnership or sole proprietorship is simpler and comparably efficient [8][9].

## 6. C-Corporation

The C-Corporation is the standard corporate entity under Subchapter C of the Internal Revenue Code. It is a separate taxpaying entity with its own EIN, its own tax return (Form 1120), and its own tax rate -- a flat 21% federal rate since the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 [1][10].

### Double Taxation

The defining characteristic of C-Corp taxation: the corporation pays tax on its income (21%), and shareholders pay tax again when income is distributed as dividends (qualified dividends taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on the shareholder's income bracket). This double taxation is the primary disadvantage of the C-Corp structure [10].

### Why Choose a C-Corp

Despite double taxation, the C-Corp offers structural advantages that no other entity provides:

**Unlimited shareholders.** No cap on the number of owners. Essential for public companies and companies seeking venture capital investment [10].

**Multiple classes of stock.** Common stock, preferred stock (with various preference structures), convertible preferred -- the flexibility to create complex capital structures with different economic and voting rights. This is not a theoretical advantage; it is the mechanism by which venture capital investment works [10][11].

**Retained earnings.** The C-Corp can retain earnings at the 21% corporate rate rather than distributing them to shareholders taxed at individual rates (up to 37%). For businesses that reinvest heavily, this can be advantageous [10].

**Institutional requirements.** Venture capital firms, most angel investors, and public markets require C-Corp status. SAFEs, convertible notes, and priced equity rounds are all structured around C-Corp capitalization [11].

## 7. Washington State Formation

Washington is a business-friendly state with several distinctive characteristics:

**No state income tax.** Washington does not impose a personal or corporate income tax. Instead, the state levies the Business & Occupation (B&O) tax on gross receipts -- revenue before any deductions for costs, expenses, or losses [7][12].

**B&O Tax Rates:**

| Classification | Rate |
|---------------|------|
| Retailing | 0.471% |
| Wholesaling | 0.484% |
| Manufacturing | 0.484% |
| Services & Other (under $5M) | 1.5% |
| Services & Other ($5M+) | 2.1% |
| Surcharge (over $250M gross) | Additional 0.5% |

**Filing fees:** LLC articles of organization $200, corporation articles of incorporation $200. Annual report $60 for all entity types. Registered agent required (RCW 23.95) [7][12].

**Cooperative corporations** are formed under RCW 23.86 (Cooperative Associations). Any number of persons may associate as a cooperative for the transaction of any lawful business on the cooperative plan. Employee cooperative corporations are governed by RCW 23.78 with additional governance tools for internal capital accounts and member equity [7][13].

**Public Benefit Corporations** are formed under RCW 23B.25, which allows corporations to pursue social purposes alongside shareholder value. The PBC designation legally protects the stated public benefit purpose in the articles of incorporation [7].

## 8. Oregon State Formation

Oregon's formation environment differs from Washington in several key respects:

**State income tax.** Oregon levies a corporate income tax at 6.6% on the first $1M of taxable income and 7.6% on income above $1M. A minimum corporate excise tax applies ($150 for businesses with less than $500K in Oregon sales) [14].

**Commercial Activity Tax (CAT).** Oregon's CAT imposes a 0.57% tax on Oregon-sourced commercial activity exceeding $1M (plus a flat $250 base). The CAT is separate from and in addition to the corporate income tax [14].

**Filing fees:** LLC articles of organization $100. Corporation articles of incorporation $100. Annual report $100. Lower filing fees than Washington but the presence of state income tax and the CAT change the total cost calculus [14].

**No sales tax.** Oregon has no general sales tax, which benefits retail-facing businesses and consumer-facing cooperatives operating in the state [14].

## 9. Articles and Operating Agreements

### Articles of Organization (LLC) / Articles of Incorporation (Corporation)

The formation document filed with the state creates the entity's legal existence. Required contents typically include: entity name (complying with state naming requirements), registered agent name and address, principal office address, management structure (member-managed or manager-managed for LLCs), authorized shares and par value (for corporations), and the incorporator's or organizer's name and signature [5][7].

### Operating Agreements and Bylaws

The operating agreement (LLC) or bylaws (corporation) is the internal governance document. It is not filed with the state but governs the relationship among owners and the operation of the business. Key provisions include:

- **Ownership and capital:** Who owns what percentage, initial capital contributions, mechanisms for additional capital calls
- **Profit and loss allocation:** How income and losses are distributed among owners
- **Management and voting:** Decision-making authority, voting thresholds for major decisions, officer appointment
- **Transfer restrictions:** Limitations on selling or transferring ownership interests, right of first refusal, buy-sell agreements
- **Dissolution:** Conditions under which the entity will be dissolved and the process for winding up operations
- **Dispute resolution:** Mediation, arbitration, or litigation provisions [5][7]

## 10. The Choice Matrix

| Factor | Sole Prop | Partnership | LLC | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----|--------|--------|
| **Liability protection** | None | None (GP) / Limited (LP) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Formation complexity** | None | Low | Low | Medium | Medium |
| **Tax treatment** | Pass-through | Pass-through | Flexible | Pass-through | Double taxation |
| **Self-employment tax** | Full | Full | Full (default) | Reduced | N/A (wages) |
| **Fundraising** | Very limited | Limited | Limited | Limited | Unlimited |
| **Owners** | 1 | 2+ | 1+ | 1-100 | Unlimited |
| **Stock classes** | N/A | N/A | N/A | 1 | Unlimited |
| **Annual compliance** | Minimal | Low | Low | Medium | High |
| **WA filing fee** | None | $200 (LP) | $200 | $200 | $200 |
| **OR filing fee** | None | $100 (LP) | $100 | $100 | $100 |

**The through-line applies:** The entity you choose determines not just tax treatment but governance structure, capital-raising capability, liability exposure, and administrative burden. Every subsequent legal and financial decision -- hiring, contracting, fundraising, dissolving -- plays by the rules of the structure you chose at formation. Choose deliberately [1][2].
