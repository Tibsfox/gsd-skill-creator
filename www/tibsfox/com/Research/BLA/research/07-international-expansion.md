## 1. The Multiplier Effect

International expansion multiplies every legal and accounting obligation. A U.S. company operating solely domestically deals with federal tax, state tax, federal employment law, state employment law, and one set of accounting standards. The moment that company establishes a presence in a second country, every obligation doubles -- and the interaction between the two systems creates an additional layer of complexity that exceeds the sum of the parts [1][2].

Each jurisdiction brings its own corporate law, tax regime, employment law, data privacy requirements, customs regulations, and accounting standards. A company operating in five countries does not face five times the compliance burden -- it faces five times the compliance plus the interaction effects between all five systems. Transfer pricing, tax treaties, double taxation relief, cross-border data transfer, and international employment law create obligations that exist solely because of the multi-jurisdictional structure [1].

The through-line of this module is honest: international expansion is entity formation repeated in every jurisdiction, with tax treaties as the connective tissue that prevents the same income from being taxed in every country it touches. The mechanics differ by country; the principle is universal [2].

## 2. Subsidiaries vs. Branches

The first structural decision in international expansion is whether to establish a subsidiary or a branch in the foreign jurisdiction [1][2].

### Subsidiary

A subsidiary is a separate legal entity incorporated under the laws of the foreign country. The parent company owns shares in the subsidiary. Key characteristics:

- **Separate legal personality.** The subsidiary is a distinct legal entity with its own assets, liabilities, contracts, and employees.
- **Limited liability.** The parent's liability is generally limited to its investment in the subsidiary.
- **Local taxation.** The subsidiary is taxed as a resident of the foreign country on its locally-sourced income.
- **U.S. taxation.** Subsidiary income is generally not taxed in the U.S. until repatriated to the parent (with exceptions under GILTI, Subpart F, and other anti-deferral rules post-TCJA).
- **Complexity.** Separate accounting, separate filings, local board of directors, compliance with local corporate law [1][2].

### Branch

A branch is an extension of the parent company operating in the foreign country. It is not a separate legal entity. Key characteristics:

- **No separate legal personality.** The branch is the same entity as the parent.
- **Unlimited liability.** The parent is directly liable for all branch obligations.
- **Dual taxation.** Branch income may be subject to both foreign tax and U.S. tax (with foreign tax credits available to avoid double taxation).
- **Simplicity.** No separate incorporation, no separate board, simpler accounting.
- **Branch profits tax.** Many countries impose an additional tax on branch profits remitted to the foreign parent [1][2].

### Decision Factors

| Factor | Subsidiary | Branch |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Liability protection** | Yes | No |
| **Tax deferral (U.S.)** | Generally yes | No |
| **Local perception** | Better (local entity) | May be perceived as foreign intrusion |
| **Setup cost** | Higher | Lower |
| **Operating losses** | Stay in subsidiary | Flow to parent (may offset U.S. income) |
| **Exit complexity** | Must dissolve or sell subsidiary | Close branch |

Most companies establishing significant long-term foreign operations choose subsidiaries for liability protection and tax planning flexibility. Branches are more common for early-stage exploration, temporary projects, or markets where subsidiary formation is particularly complex [1][2].

## 3. Transfer Pricing

Transfer pricing refers to the pricing of transactions between related entities (parent-subsidiary, sibling subsidiaries) across different tax jurisdictions. Because related parties can set prices at whatever level benefits the group overall, tax authorities scrutinize intercompany pricing to prevent profit shifting [3][4].

### The Arm's Length Principle

The OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines and IRC Section 482 both require that intercompany transactions be priced at "arm's length" -- the price that unrelated parties would agree upon in comparable circumstances. The five approved methods for establishing arm's length pricing [3]:

| Method | Description | When Used |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP)** | Compare to identical/similar transactions between unrelated parties | When comparable transactions exist |
| **Resale Price Method** | Deduct appropriate gross margin from resale price to arrive at arm's length purchase price | Distribution/resale transactions |
| **Cost Plus Method** | Add appropriate markup to costs incurred | Manufacturing, service provision |
| **Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM)** | Compare net profit margin to comparable unrelated companies | When transaction-level data unavailable |
| **Profit Split Method** | Split combined profits based on relative value contributions | Complex, integrated operations |

### Documentation Requirements

The IRS requires contemporaneous transfer pricing documentation demonstrating that intercompany prices are arm's length. The OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) Action 13 introduced a three-tier documentation framework: Master File (global overview), Local File (detailed country-specific documentation), and Country-by-Country Report (CbCR) for multinationals with revenue exceeding EUR 750 million [3][4].

### Penalties

Failure to maintain adequate transfer pricing documentation or setting non-arm's length prices can result in adjustments (the IRS re-prices the transaction), penalties (up to 40% of the underpayment attributable to transfer pricing adjustments), and double taxation (if two countries both claim taxing rights over the same income) [3].

## 4. Permanent Establishment

A "permanent establishment" (PE) is a fixed place of business through which an enterprise carries on its business in a foreign country. The concept matters because it determines whether a country has the right to tax a foreign enterprise's business profits [1][5].

### What Creates a PE

Under most tax treaties (following the OECD Model Tax Convention):

- A **fixed place of business** (office, factory, workshop, mine, construction site lasting 12+ months)
- A **dependent agent** who habitually exercises authority to conclude contracts on behalf of the enterprise
- Certain **service activities** (employees providing services for more than a specified period, often 183 days in a 12-month period) [5]

### What Does Not Create a PE (Generally)

- Storage, display, or delivery of goods
- Maintaining stock solely for processing by another enterprise
- Activities of a preparatory or auxiliary character
- An **independent agent** acting in the ordinary course of their business [5]

### Risk

If a company inadvertently creates a PE in a foreign country, it becomes subject to that country's taxation on the profits attributable to the PE. This can result in unexpected tax liability, compliance obligations, and penalties. Common triggers: employees working remotely from another country, sales representatives who can bind the company to contracts, and construction projects that exceed treaty thresholds [5].

## 5. IFRS vs. GAAP Reconciliation

Companies operating internationally often face the challenge of maintaining financial records under two accounting frameworks: GAAP (required for U.S. reporting) and IFRS (required or permitted in 140+ countries) [6][7].

### Key Differences

| Topic | GAAP | IFRS |
|-------|------|------|
| **Inventory costing** | LIFO, FIFO, weighted average | FIFO, weighted average (LIFO prohibited) |
| **Inventory write-down** | Not reversible | Reversible (up to original cost) |
| **Asset revaluation** | Generally not permitted | Permitted for property, plant, equipment |
| **Development costs** | Generally expensed | Capitalized if six criteria met (IAS 38) |
| **Revenue recognition** | ASC 606 (five-step model) | IFRS 15 (five-step model, largely converged) |
| **Lease accounting** | ASC 842: operating and finance leases | IFRS 16: single right-of-use model |
| **Contingent liabilities** | Accrue if probable (>50%) | Accrue if probable (>50%), but "probable" interpreted differently |

### Reconciliation in Practice

Companies that report under both frameworks maintain either dual books or a single set of books with reconciliation adjustments. The reconciliation process identifies and quantifies every difference between GAAP and IFRS treatment for each material transaction. For companies preparing for cross-listing (listing on both U.S. and foreign exchanges) or with foreign subsidiaries reporting under IFRS, the reconciliation is an ongoing operational requirement [6][7].

## 6. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act

The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act of 1977 (FCPA) prohibits U.S. companies (and their agents, subsidiaries, and affiliates) from making corrupt payments to foreign government officials for the purpose of obtaining or retaining business [8].

### Two Prongs

**Anti-bribery provisions.** Prohibit payments (or offers, promises, or authorizations of payments) of "anything of value" to foreign government officials (including employees of state-owned enterprises) to influence official actions or obtain an improper advantage. Willful violations carry criminal penalties: up to $250,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment for individuals; up to $2 million fine for companies [8][9].

**Accounting provisions.** Require companies to maintain accurate books and records that reflect transactions in reasonable detail, and to maintain a system of internal accounting controls sufficient to provide reasonable assurances that transactions are executed in accordance with management authorization. The accounting provisions apply to all SEC registrants regardless of whether they have foreign operations [8].

### Enforcement

FCPA enforcement has intensified significantly since the mid-2000s. The Department of Justice (DOJ) and SEC pursue FCPA violations aggressively, with settlements routinely exceeding $100 million. Notable settlements include Siemens ($800M, 2008), Alstom ($772M, 2014), Goldman Sachs ($2.9B, 2020), and Ericsson ($1.06B, 2019) [8][9].

### Compliance Programs

Effective FCPA compliance programs include: written anti-corruption policies, risk assessment procedures, third-party due diligence (agents, consultants, distributors), training programs, internal reporting mechanisms (whistleblower hotlines), monitoring and auditing, and disciplinary procedures. The DOJ's Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs (updated periodically) provides detailed guidance on what constitutes an effective program [8][9].

## 7. VAT and GST

Value-Added Tax (VAT) and Goods and Services Tax (GST) are consumption taxes levied at each stage of the supply chain on the value added at that stage. Unlike the U.S. sales tax (levied only at the retail point of sale), VAT/GST is collected at every transaction between businesses, with each business remitting the tax on its value added and claiming credits for tax paid on inputs [10].

### How VAT Works

1. Manufacturer buys raw materials for $100 + $20 VAT (20% rate). Pays $120.
2. Manufacturer sells finished goods to distributor for $200 + $40 VAT. Collects $240.
3. Manufacturer remits $20 to tax authority ($40 collected minus $20 paid on inputs).
4. Distributor sells to retailer for $300 + $60 VAT. Remits $20 ($60 minus $40).
5. Retailer sells to consumer for $400 + $80 VAT. Remits $20 ($80 minus $60).
6. **Total tax collected by government: $80** (same as if a 20% sales tax had been applied at retail).

### Cross-Border VAT

For international transactions:

- **Exports** are generally zero-rated (0% VAT) -- the exporter can reclaim input VAT but charges no output VAT
- **Imports** are subject to the destination country's VAT at the border
- **Services** follow complex "place of supply" rules that determine which country's VAT applies [10]

### VAT Rates (Selected)

| Country | Standard Rate |
|---------|-------------|
| UK | 20% |
| Germany | 19% |
| France | 20% |
| Japan (consumption tax) | 10% |
| Canada (GST) | 5% (federal) + provincial |
| Australia (GST) | 10% |
| Singapore (GST) | 9% |

The U.S. is one of the few developed economies that does not have a national VAT/GST. U.S. sales tax is levied at the state and local level, varies by jurisdiction, and applies only at the retail point of sale [10].

## 8. Cross-Border Employment

Employing workers in foreign countries requires compliance with local employment law, which varies dramatically by jurisdiction [1][11].

### Key Compliance Areas

- **Employment contracts:** Many countries require written employment contracts with specific mandatory provisions (notice periods, severance terms, working hours, leave entitlements)
- **Termination protection:** Many countries restrict at-will termination. European countries generally require cause for dismissal and specify notice periods and severance amounts based on tenure
- **Benefits:** Mandatory benefits vary widely: health insurance, retirement contributions, paid leave (often 20-30 days annually in Europe), parental leave, sick leave
- **Data privacy:** Employee data is subject to local data protection laws (GDPR in the EU, PIPA in South Korea, LGPD in Brazil) [11]

### Employer of Record (EOR)

The Employer of Record model has emerged as the primary mechanism for companies to hire internationally without establishing local entities. An EOR is a third-party organization that legally employs the worker in the foreign country on behalf of the client company [11].

The EOR handles: local employment contracts, payroll and tax withholding, mandatory benefits and social contributions, compliance with local labor law, and termination procedures. The client company directs the worker's day-to-day activities. Companies like Deel, Remote, Papaya Global, and Oyster provide global EOR services [11].

### When EOR Is Not Enough

EOR works for hiring individual employees in foreign jurisdictions. It does not establish a corporate presence, does not allow the company to hold local assets or contracts in its own name, and may create PE risk if the EOR employees are conducting core business activities. For significant foreign operations, a local subsidiary is typically required [1][11].

## 9. Tax Treaties

Tax treaties (bilateral agreements between countries) are the connective tissue of international taxation. They serve three primary functions [2][5]:

### Avoiding Double Taxation

Without treaties, the same income could be taxed by both the country where it was earned (source country) and the country where the earner is resident (residence country). Treaties allocate taxing rights between the two countries and require the residence country to provide credits or exemptions for tax paid in the source country [2][5].

### Reducing Withholding Taxes

Source countries typically impose withholding taxes on payments (dividends, interest, royalties) made to foreign recipients. Treaty rates are generally lower than domestic withholding rates:

| Payment Type | Typical Domestic Rate | Typical Treaty Rate |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Dividends | 30% | 5-15% |
| Interest | 30% | 0-15% |
| Royalties | 30% | 0-10% |

### Preventing Tax Evasion

Modern tax treaties include information exchange provisions, mutual agreement procedures for resolving disputes, and anti-abuse rules (Limitation on Benefits clauses) that prevent treaty shopping -- routing income through treaty countries solely to access lower rates [2][5].

The U.S. has tax treaties with approximately 65 countries. The network covers most major trading partners but significant gaps exist (e.g., no comprehensive treaty with Brazil). Treaty terms vary by country and are periodically renegotiated [5].
