## 1. The Cooperative Difference

A cooperative is a business owned and democratically controlled by the people who use its services or by the people who work there. The fundamental governance principle is one-member-one-vote, regardless of capital contribution. This is not a detail -- it is the defining characteristic that makes cooperative accounting different from corporate accounting [1][2].

In a standard corporation, profit is distributed to shareholders based on the number of shares they hold. The more capital invested, the greater the return. In a cooperative, surplus is distributed to members based on their patronage -- how much business they did with the cooperative. The worker who contributed 2,000 hours receives twice the patronage dividend of the worker who contributed 1,000 hours. The consumer who purchased $10,000 in goods receives proportionally more than the consumer who purchased $5,000. Capital investment determines your right to participate; patronage determines your share of the surplus [1][2][3].

This distinction reshapes every accounting entry. Revenue is not "profit" -- it is the cooperative's income from transactions with its members. Surplus is not "retained earnings available for dividends" -- it is patronage to be returned to the members who generated it, minus the portion retained for collective reserves and operating capital. The chart of accounts looks similar; the economic logic underneath is inverted [3].

## 2. Types of Cooperatives

### Worker Cooperatives

Owned and governed by the employees. Each worker-member has one vote on major decisions and elects the board of directors. Surplus is allocated as patronage dividends based on labor contributed -- typically measured by hours worked, compensation earned, or a defined "work unit." Worker cooperatives range from small bakeries and cleaning services to industrial-scale enterprises. The U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives estimates there are 600+ worker cooperatives in the United States [1][4].

### Consumer Cooperatives

Owned by the people who purchase goods or services from the cooperative. Members pay a membership fee or purchase a share, receive voting rights, and earn patronage dividends based on purchases. Consumer cooperatives are common in grocery (food co-ops), housing, utilities (rural electric cooperatives), and financial services (credit unions) [1][2].

### Producer Cooperatives

Owned by the producers who supply raw materials or products for processing, marketing, or distribution. Agricultural cooperatives are the classic example: farmers pool their output for collective bargaining, processing, and marketing. Producer cooperatives give individual producers access to economies of scale and market power they could not achieve independently [1][5].

### Purchasing Cooperatives

Groups of independent businesses that pool purchasing power to negotiate better prices, terms, or services. Hardware cooperatives (Ace Hardware, True Value), pharmacy cooperatives, and independent grocery cooperatives operate on this model [1].

## 3. The Rochdale Principles

The Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers, founded in 1844 in Rochdale, England, established the foundational principles that define cooperative identity worldwide. Twenty-eight weavers and other artisans pooled one pound each to open a store selling food and goods at fair prices. Their governance rules became the template for all cooperatives that followed [2][6].

The International Co-operative Alliance (ICA) maintains the modern expression of these principles:

1. **Voluntary and open membership.** Cooperatives are open to all persons who can use their services and accept the responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, political, or religious discrimination.
2. **Democratic member control.** One member, one vote. Members participate in setting policies and making decisions. Elected representatives are accountable to the membership.
3. **Member economic participation.** Members contribute equitably to the cooperative's capital. Surplus is allocated to developing the cooperative, benefiting members in proportion to their patronage, or supporting activities approved by the membership.
4. **Autonomy and independence.** Cooperatives are autonomous, self-help organizations controlled by their members.
5. **Education, training, and information.** Cooperatives provide education and training for their members, elected representatives, managers, and employees.
6. **Cooperation among cooperatives.** Cooperatives serve their members most effectively by working together through local, national, regional, and international structures.
7. **Concern for community.** Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their communities through policies approved by their members [2][6].

These are not aspirational ideals. They are governance requirements that distinguish cooperatives from conventional businesses. An entity that does not practice democratic member control is not a cooperative regardless of what it calls itself [6].

## 4. Patronage Dividends

The patronage dividend is the cooperative's primary mechanism for returning surplus to members. At the end of each accounting period, the cooperative calculates its net income from business done with members, then allocates that income to individual members based on their proportional patronage [3][7].

### Calculating Patronage

**Step 1: Determine net income from patronage sources.** Only income generated from transactions with members qualifies for patronage allocation. Non-patronage income (investment returns, rental income from non-members, sales to non-members) is taxed at standard corporate rates [3][7].

**Step 2: Determine each member's patronage percentage.** In a worker cooperative, this is typically hours worked or compensation earned. In a consumer cooperative, total purchases. In a producer cooperative, volume or value of products delivered [3].

**Step 3: Allocate.** Multiply each member's patronage percentage by the total patronage dividend pool. Example: if the cooperative has $100,000 in patronage-sourced net income and member Alice worked 2,000 of the cooperative's total 20,000 member-hours (10%), Alice's patronage allocation is $10,000 [3][7].

**Step 4: Distribute.** The board determines how much of the allocation is paid in cash and how much is retained in the member's internal capital account. Federal law requires a minimum of 20% of qualified patronage dividends be paid in cash [7][8].

### Patronage vs. Profit

The distinction matters for both accounting and governance. Profit in a conventional corporation flows to shareholders based on share ownership. Patronage in a cooperative flows to members based on use. A worker who owns $1,000 in membership shares and a worker who owns $100 in membership shares receive the same patronage per hour worked. The capital contribution buys participation; the patronage reflects contribution [3].

## 5. Internal Capital Accounts

Internal capital accounts (ICAs) track each member's equity stake in the cooperative. The account reflects [8][9]:

- **Membership share purchase.** The initial capital contribution required to become a member.
- **Accumulated patronage allocations.** The retained portion of each year's patronage dividend (the 80% not paid in cash for qualified dividends).
- **Interest credited.** Some cooperatives credit interest on internal capital account balances.
- **Adjustments for losses.** If the cooperative incurs losses, member accounts may be adjusted proportionally.

### The ICA Lifecycle

1. **Entry:** New member purchases membership share and/or meets minimum capital contribution.
2. **Accumulation:** Patronage dividends are credited annually. Cash portion paid out; retained portion added to ICA.
3. **Ongoing:** Member receives annual statements showing ICA balance, patronage allocated, interest credited.
4. **Departure:** Upon leaving the cooperative, the member's ICA is redeemed -- typically through an initial cash payment followed by scheduled installments over 3-7 years.
5. **Inheritance:** If the member dies, the ICA passes to their estate. Cooperative bylaws must address estate succession procedures [8][9].

### Aggregate Constraint

Under Washington's Employee Cooperative Corporations Act (RCW 23.78), at the end of each accounting period, internal capital account balances must be adjusted so that their sum equals the net book value of the cooperative. This constraint ensures that individual member accounts collectively reflect the cooperative's actual financial position -- no more, no less [10].

## 6. Subchapter T Taxation

Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC Sections 1381-1388) provides the tax framework for cooperatives. The five code sections address [7][8]:

| Section | Topic |
|---------|-------|
| IRC 1381 | What constitutes a cooperative under Subchapter T |
| IRC 1382 | Taxable income and patronage dividend deductions |
| IRC 1383 | Treatment of nonqualified patronage |
| IRC 1385 | Patron taxation on dividends received |
| IRC 1388 | Definitions (patronage dividend, written notice of allocation, qualified/nonqualified) |

### The Single-Tax Result

The cooperative's mechanism for avoiding double taxation:

1. Cooperative earns $100,000 from member transactions.
2. Cooperative allocates $80,000 as patronage dividends to members (deductible from cooperative's income).
3. Cooperative pays tax on the remaining $20,000 (collective reserve).
4. Members report $80,000 on their individual tax returns.

The income is taxed once, at the member level. The cooperative achieves this deduction only for patronage-sourced income and only if it meets three criteria: (1) the dividend is based on patronage volume/value, (2) a pre-existing obligation to pay exists (typically in bylaws), and (3) the amount is determined by reference to net earnings from member business [7][8].

## 7. Qualified vs. Nonqualified Notices

Written notices of allocation are the cooperative's instrument for retaining patronage allocations in members' internal capital accounts while still achieving the tax deduction. Two forms exist [7][8]:

### Qualified Written Notices

- **Cooperative:** Deducts the full allocation in the year of allocation.
- **Member:** Pays tax on the full allocation in the year of allocation (even though most of it is retained in the ICA, not received in cash).
- **On redemption:** No additional tax to the member.
- **Requirement:** At least 20% of the allocation must be paid in cash, and the member must consent to include the allocation in income.

### Nonqualified Written Notices

- **Cooperative:** No deduction in the year of allocation. Deduction taken when the notice is later redeemed in cash.
- **Member:** No tax in the year of allocation. Pays tax when the notice is redeemed.
- **Advantage:** Defers member's tax obligation until cash is actually received.
- **Disadvantage:** Cooperative does not receive the immediate deduction.

Most worker cooperatives use qualified written notices because the cooperative receives the immediate tax deduction. The 20% cash payment helps members cover their tax obligation on the portion retained in the ICA. The choice between qualified and nonqualified notices is a significant financial planning decision that affects both cooperative cash flow and member tax burden [7][8].

## 8. Collective Reserves

Not all cooperative surplus is allocated to individual members. Cooperatives maintain collective reserve accounts -- sometimes called "indivisible reserves" -- that belong to the cooperative as an institution, not to any individual member [3][9].

Collective reserves serve several purposes:

- **Working capital.** Day-to-day operating liquidity.
- **Capital investment.** Equipment, facilities, technology upgrades.
- **Risk buffer.** Protection against downturns, unexpected expenses, or seasonal variability.
- **Growth funding.** Expansion into new markets, products, or services.

The allocation between individual patronage dividends and collective reserves is a governance decision made by the board (often subject to bylaws and member approval). A cooperative that allocates heavily to individual patronage maximizes member income but may underinvest in long-term capacity. A cooperative that allocates heavily to reserves builds institutional strength but delivers less to individual members. The balance is a core expression of cooperative values [3][9].

In some cooperative traditions (particularly in Europe and Latin America), indivisible reserves cannot be distributed to individual members even upon dissolution of the cooperative. They pass to another cooperative or to a community purpose. This reflects the principle that the reserve was built by all members over time and belongs to the cooperative purpose, not to the current membership [6].

## 9. Real-World Examples

### REI (Recreational Equipment, Inc.)

The largest consumer cooperative in the United States. Founded in 1938 by Lloyd and Mary Anderson, who pooled $1 each with 21 fellow mountain climbers to buy quality ice axes from Austria. Today: 23+ million members, $3.8B+ annual revenue, 180+ stores. Members pay a one-time $30 fee, receive an annual patronage dividend (typically 10% of qualifying purchases), and vote on the board of directors. REI is proof that cooperative structure works at national retail scale [11].

### Credit Unions

Credit unions are consumer cooperatives in the financial sector. Member-owned, not-for-profit, and democratically governed (one-member-one-vote regardless of deposit size). As of 2025, 4,700+ credit unions serve 130+ million Americans. Credit unions typically offer higher savings rates and lower loan rates than commercial banks because surplus is returned to members through better pricing rather than paid to external shareholders [12].

### Mondragon Corporation

The world's largest federation of worker cooperatives. Founded in 1956 in the Basque Country of Spain by Father Jose Maria Arizmendiarrieta. Today: 96 cooperatives, 80,000+ worker-owners, operating across manufacturing, retail (Eroski supermarkets), finance (Laboral Kutxa bank), and education (Mondragon University). The pay ratio between highest and lowest compensated worker is capped (approximately 6:1 in most cooperatives). During the 2008 financial crisis, Mondragon cooperatives redeployed workers between entities rather than laying them off -- the cooperative commitment to member employment over quarterly returns [4][13].

### Land O'Lakes

A producer cooperative owned by 1,700+ member-owners (dairy farmers and agricultural cooperatives). Founded in 1921 in Minnesota. Annual sales exceed $16B. Members deliver milk and dairy products for collective processing, marketing, and distribution. Patronage dividends return surplus to member-farmers based on volume of product delivered. Land O'Lakes demonstrates cooperative accounting at industrial agricultural scale [5][14].
