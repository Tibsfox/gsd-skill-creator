## 1. The Function of Compliance

Regulatory compliance serves a specific economic function: it creates trust between parties who do not know each other. When an investor buys shares in a company, they trust the financial statements because an independent auditor verified them under standards set by the PCAOB. When a bank accepts a deposit, the customer trusts the bank because it operates under FDIC insurance and OCC regulation. When a startup raises capital, investors trust the terms because securities law mandates specific disclosures [1][2].

Compliance is often described as a cost. It is a cost -- but it is the cost of market participation. Without mandatory disclosure, investors cannot evaluate risk. Without anti-fraud provisions, capital flows to the best storyteller rather than the best business. Without audit requirements, financial statements are unverifiable assertions. The compliance stack is not a burden on commerce; it is the foundation on which commerce operates at scale [1].

The burden scales with the stakes. A sole proprietor selling handmade goods at a farmers market faces minimal compliance requirements. A publicly traded corporation managing billions in assets faces hundreds of specific regulatory obligations across multiple agencies. The progression is not arbitrary -- it reflects the increasing number of people who depend on the accuracy and integrity of the company's operations [2].

## 2. SEC Registration

The Securities Act of 1933 requires that any offer or sale of securities be registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) unless an exemption applies. "Securities" includes not just stock but any "investment contract" -- a term the Supreme Court defined in *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.* (1946) as an investment of money in a common enterprise with the expectation of profits derived from the efforts of others [1][3].

### Registration Process

Full SEC registration requires:

- **Registration statement (Form S-1).** Detailed disclosure of the company's business, financial condition, management, risk factors, and planned use of proceeds.
- **Audited financial statements.** Prepared under GAAP and audited by a PCAOB-registered auditor.
- **SEC review.** The SEC staff reviews the registration statement and issues comments. The company must respond to all comments before the registration becomes effective.
- **Ongoing reporting.** Once registered, the company must file quarterly reports (10-Q), annual reports (10-K), and current reports (8-K) for material events [1][3].

### Cost and Timeline

Full SEC registration typically costs $100,000 to $500,000+ in legal and accounting fees and takes 3-6+ months from filing to effectiveness. This cost makes full registration impractical for most private companies, which is why exemptions exist [3].

## 3. Regulation D Exemptions

Regulation D (Rules 501-508 of the Securities Act) provides the most commonly used exemptions for private capital raises. Companies using Regulation D do not register their securities with the SEC but must file Form D (a brief notice) within 15 days of first sale [3][4].

### Rule 506(b)

The traditional private placement exemption:

- **No limit** on the amount of capital raised
- Unlimited **accredited investors** may participate
- Up to **35 non-accredited sophisticated investors** may participate (must have sufficient financial knowledge and experience)
- **No general solicitation or advertising** permitted -- the issuer must have a pre-existing relationship with all investors
- Non-accredited investors must receive disclosure documents substantially similar to registration documents [3][4]

### Rule 506(c)

Added by the JOBS Act (2012), Rule 506(c) permits **general solicitation** (public advertising of the offering):

- **No limit** on the amount raised
- **Only accredited investors** may purchase -- no non-accredited investors permitted
- The issuer must take **reasonable steps to verify** accredited status (tax returns, bank statements, broker letter, or third-party verification service)
- The verification requirement is the trade-off for the ability to advertise [3][4]

### Rule 504

- Maximum offering of **$10 million** in a 12-month period
- Generally no specific disclosure requirements
- Securities may be "restricted" (cannot be freely resold)
- Less commonly used than 506(b) or 506(c) [3]

## 4. Accredited Investors

The accredited investor definition determines who can participate in most private securities offerings. The definition serves as a proxy for the investor's ability to bear financial risk and evaluate complex investments [3][5].

### Individual Qualification (any one of the following)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Income** | $200,000+ individual ($300,000+ joint with spouse) in each of the last two years, with reasonable expectation of the same |
| **Net worth** | $1,000,000+ excluding primary residence |
| **Professional license** | Series 7, Series 65, or Series 82 license holders (added 2020) |
| **Knowledgeable employee** | Executive officers, directors, or general partners of the issuer; "knowledgeable employees" of private fund issuers |

### Entity Qualification

| Entity Type | Threshold |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Institutions** | Banks, insurance companies, registered investment companies, BDCs, SBICs |
| **Trusts** | Assets exceeding $5,000,000, not formed specifically to acquire offered securities |
| **Entities** | All owners are individually accredited |
| **Family offices** | Assets under management of $5,000,000+ with a sophisticated person directing investments |

### 2020 Amendments

The SEC's 2020 amendments to the accredited investor definition added professional credentials (Series 7, 65, 82) and "knowledgeable employees" as qualification paths, recognizing that financial sophistication is not solely a function of wealth. The amendments also expanded the definition to include family offices and entities meeting the "investments test" ($5M+) regardless of entity type [5].

## 5. Regulation A+ and Regulation CF

### Regulation A+ (Tier 2)

Regulation A+ (sometimes called a "mini-IPO") allows companies to raise up to **$75 million per year** from both accredited and non-accredited investors:

- Requires SEC qualification of an offering circular (similar to but less extensive than a full registration statement)
- Ongoing annual, semi-annual, and current event reporting
- Audited financial statements required
- Federal preemption of state securities registration (Blue Sky laws)
- Securities are not "restricted" -- they can be freely traded
- Non-accredited investors are limited to investing the greater of 10% of income or 10% of net worth per offering [3][6]

Regulation A+ bridges the gap between private placements (Reg D) and full public registration (IPO). It is well-suited for community-oriented offerings where broad participation is desired [6].

### Regulation CF (Crowdfunding)

The JOBS Act created Regulation CF, allowing companies to raise up to **$5 million per year** through SEC-registered crowdfunding portals:

- Open to all investors, with investment limits based on income and net worth
- Must use a registered funding portal or broker-dealer intermediary
- Annual financial statements required (reviewed by CPA for raises over $618K; audited for raises over $1.235M)
- Securities have a one-year transfer restriction
- Best suited for small community offerings and early-stage grassroots fundraising [3][7]

## 6. Sarbanes-Oxley Act

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) was enacted in response to the accounting scandals at Enron, WorldCom, Tyco, and other public companies that destroyed hundreds of billions of dollars in shareholder value and decimated employee retirement accounts. SOX imposed sweeping corporate governance and financial reporting requirements on public companies [8][9].

### Key Provisions

**Section 302: CEO/CFO Certification.** The CEO and CFO must personally certify that financial statements are materially accurate and that internal controls are effective. Criminal penalties for knowingly false certification [8].

**Section 404(a): Management Assessment.** Companies must maintain and annually assess the effectiveness of internal controls over financial reporting. Management's assessment is disclosed in the annual report [8].

**Section 404(b): Auditor Attestation.** The external auditor must independently evaluate and attest to the effectiveness of internal controls. This requirement applies to large accelerated filers (public float > $700M) and accelerated filers (public float > $75M but < $700M). The auditor attestation was the most costly SOX provision, driving the creation of entire compliance consulting industries [8][9].

**Section 802: Document Destruction.** Criminal penalties (up to 20 years imprisonment) for destroying, altering, or concealing documents with intent to obstruct a federal investigation [8].

**Section 906: Criminal Certification.** Separate from Section 302, imposes criminal penalties (up to $5M fine, 20 years imprisonment) for CEO/CFO who certify financial statements knowing they do not comply with SEC requirements [8].

### SOX and Private Companies

SOX applies directly only to public companies registered with the SEC. However, its influence extends to private companies through several channels: lenders may require SOX-level internal controls as loan covenants, investors may demand SOX-compliant governance, and companies preparing for IPO must establish SOX compliance before going public [9].

## 7. Anti-Money Laundering and KYC

### Bank Secrecy Act (BSA)

The Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 established the foundation of U.S. anti-money laundering (AML) law. The BSA requires financial institutions to [10]:

- Maintain records of cash purchases of negotiable instruments
- File **Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs)** for cash transactions exceeding $10,000
- File **Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)** for transactions that appear to involve funds from illegal activity or structured to evade reporting requirements
- Implement AML compliance programs including customer due diligence

### Know Your Customer (KYC)

KYC requirements mandate that financial institutions verify the identity of their customers and assess the risk of illegal activity. Customer Identification Programs (CIPs) must collect and verify: name, date of birth, address, and identification number (SSN or EIN for U.S. persons; passport for non-U.S. persons) [10][11].

### Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Rule

The 2016 CDD Rule (effective 2018) added a requirement to identify and verify the identity of beneficial owners who own 25% or more of a legal entity customer (or a single individual with significant management responsibility). This requirement has been substantially superseded by the Corporate Transparency Act's more comprehensive beneficial ownership reporting [10][11].

## 8. The Corporate Transparency Act

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), enacted as part of the Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 and effective January 1, 2024, requires most U.S. companies to report beneficial ownership information (BOI) to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) [12].

### Who Must Report

All domestic companies and foreign companies registered to do business in any U.S. state, unless an exemption applies. Exemptions exist for large operating companies (500+ employees AND $5M+ gross receipts AND physical U.S. office), regulated entities (banks, insurance companies, SEC registrants), and certain other categories [12].

### What Must Be Reported

For each beneficial owner (individuals who directly or indirectly own 25%+ or exercise substantial control):

- Full legal name
- Date of birth
- Residential address
- Identifying document number and image (driver's license, passport) [12]

### Compliance Timeline

Companies formed before January 1, 2024 had until January 1, 2025 to file initial reports. Companies formed after January 1, 2024 must file within 90 days of formation (reduced to 30 days for companies formed after January 1, 2025). Updates must be filed within 30 days of any change [12].

## 9. The Compliance Stack by Stage

The compliance burden builds in layers as a business grows:

| Stage | Key Compliance Requirements |
|-------|---------------------------|
| **Formation** | State filing, registered agent, EIN application, beneficial ownership report (CTA), business licenses |
| **First revenue** | Tax registration (federal, state, local), sales tax collection (if applicable), B&O tax (WA) |
| **First employees** | Employer tax registration, I-9 verification, workers' compensation, OSHA compliance, FLSA wage/hour, anti-discrimination (Title VII, ADA) |
| **Raising capital (private)** | Securities exemption selection, Form D filing (Reg D), investor verification (506(c)), PPM preparation |
| **Revenue > $1M** | Potential B&O surtax (WA), audit readiness, enhanced recordkeeping |
| **Revenue > $10M** | Formal internal controls, board governance, potential SOX-readiness if IPO contemplated |
| **Raising capital (public)** | Full SEC registration (Form S-1), PCAOB audit, SOX compliance, ongoing reporting (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K) |
| **Public company** | Full SOX compliance, quarterly/annual SEC filings, proxy statements, insider trading compliance, Regulation FD |

The stack is cumulative. Each layer adds to the previous layers -- nothing falls away. A public company still files its annual state report, still collects I-9s, still registers its beneficial owners. The foundation laid at formation supports every subsequent layer [1][2].
