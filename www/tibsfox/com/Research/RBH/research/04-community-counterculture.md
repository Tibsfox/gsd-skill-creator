# Community and Counterculture Radio (1962--1984)

> **Domain:** Broadcasting History
> **Module:** 4 -- Community and Counterculture
> **Through-line:** *A man who grew up wealthy bought a donut shop, dragged a transmitter inside, and launched a movement that would create forty community radio stations across the country. Lorenzo Milam's KRAB was not the first community station -- Berkeley's KPFA holds that honor -- but KRAB became the seed of a network, the "KRAB Nebula," that proved community radio was not an anomaly but a viable, replicable model.*

## 1. The Community Radio Concept

### 1.1 Origins in Berkeley

Community radio in the United States began with KPFA-FM in Berkeley, California, founded in 1949 by Lewis Hill and the Pacifica Foundation. KPFA operated on a then-radical premise: a radio station could be funded by its listeners rather than by advertisers or government, and its programming could serve artistic, intellectual, and political purposes that commercial radio ignored.

### 1.2 The Pacifica Model

Pacifica's model -- listener-supported, noncommercial, eclectic programming controlled by volunteers and staff rather than advertisers -- inspired a generation of broadcasters who saw commercial radio as culturally bankrupt and public radio (which did not yet exist in its modern form) as insufficiently independent. Lorenzo Milam was among the people who volunteered at KPFA and carried the Pacifica vision north to the Pacific Northwest.

### 1.3 What Community Radio Means

Community radio is distinguished from commercial and public radio by three characteristics: listener funding (no advertising), volunteer participation (community members produce programming), and editorial independence (no corporate or government content control). These characteristics produce stations that sound different from anything else on the dial -- eclectic, unpredictable, sometimes rough around the edges, but unmistakably local and unmistakably free.

### 1.4 The PNW as Fertile Ground

The Pacific Northwest proved exceptionally fertile ground for community radio. The region's traditions of independence, environmental activism, progressive politics, and cultural experimentation created audiences hungry for programming that commercial radio did not provide. The relatively low cost of living and real estate in the 1960s and 1970s made it possible to start stations on minimal budgets. And the geographic isolation of PNW communities created demand for local voices that network-affiliated commercial stations could not satisfy.

## 2. Lorenzo Milam and KRAB-FM

### 2.1 Background

Lorenzo Milam was born into wealth but chose to spend his inheritance on radio rather than conventional investments. After volunteering at KPFA in Berkeley, he conceived the idea of building a community radio station in Seattle -- a city he believed had the right combination of cultural sophistication, progressive politics, and unserved audiences.

### 2.2 The Donut Shop

In spring 1962, Milam purchased a former donut shop at 91st and Roosevelt Way in Seattle's Maple Leaf neighborhood for $7,500. The location was chosen for practical reasons: the building was cheap, it had a corner lot suitable for a transmitter tower, and the structure could be adapted for studio use with minimal renovation. The donut shop's commercial kitchen became the station's control room.

### 2.3 Call Letters

Milam's first choice of call letters was KANT -- a reference to the philosopher, reflecting the station's intellectual aspirations. The FCC, which assigns call letters, instead assigned KRAB. Milam accepted the name and turned it into an identity: the crab became the station's mascot and a symbol of its sideways approach to broadcasting convention.

### 2.4 Sign-On

KRAB debuted December 13, 1962, on 107.7 FM -- unusually positioned in the commercial portion of the FM spectrum rather than the noncommercial band below 92 MHz. The choice of 107.7 was driven by availability rather than philosophy, but it placed KRAB on the dial alongside commercial stations, inviting direct comparison and making it accessible to listeners who might not have ventured to the lower end of the FM band.

### 2.5 Milam's Departure

Milam left KRAB in March 1968, just over five years after founding the station. His departure was driven partly by the interpersonal challenges of running a volunteer organization and partly by his desire to replicate the KRAB model in other cities. After leaving KRAB, Milam continued founding community stations across the country, earning the title "Johnny Appleseed of community radio."

## 3. KRAB's Programming Philosophy

### 3.1 Freeform Radio

KRAB's programming was radically eclectic -- "freeform" in the terminology of the era. There was no fixed format, no playlist, and no requirement that programming appeal to any particular demographic. Individual programmers chose their own content, subject only to the station's general commitment to quality and noncommercialism.

### 3.2 What KRAB Sounded Like

A typical evening on KRAB might include:

- Intellectual roundtable discussions on philosophy, politics, or science
- African indigenous music recordings
- Readings from 14th-century texts
- Gramophone recordings from the 1920s
- Live performances by local musicians
- Poetry readings
- Discussions of urban planning, environmental policy, or civil rights

### 3.3 The Rock Music Policy

KRAB initially avoided rock music, broadcasting it only in late-night hours. This was a deliberate programming decision: Milam and the station's early volunteers believed that rock music was adequately served by commercial radio and that KRAB's mission was to provide what commercial stations did not. The policy softened over time as the station's programming evolved with its volunteer base.

### 3.4 Pioneering Categories

KRAB pioneered several programming categories that had never appeared on PNW radio:

- **World music:** Before the term existed, KRAB was playing music from Africa, Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America
- **LGBT programming:** KRAB produced content by and for the gay and lesbian community at a time when the subject was taboo on commercial radio
- **Environmental programming:** The station's coverage of environmental issues predated the mainstream environmental movement
- **Experimental and avant-garde music:** Electronic music, free jazz, musique concrete -- forms that commercial radio would never touch

### 3.5 The Volunteer Culture

KRAB operated primarily with volunteer labor. Programmers were unpaid community members who brought their own records, books, and expertise to the station. This volunteer model kept costs low but also produced the programming's distinctive quality: because programmers were not professionals working to a formula, they brought genuine passion, specialized knowledge, and personal taste to their shows.

## 4. The KRAB Nebula

### 4.1 The Network Concept

The "KRAB Nebula" -- a name that played on the astronomical nebula associated with the Crab constellation -- referred to the network of community radio stations that Milam helped found or inspire across the country. The KRAB model was explicitly designed to be replicated: low startup costs, volunteer labor, listener funding, freeform programming.

### 4.2 Stations in the Nebula

| Station | City | Year | Connection to KRAB |
|---------|------|------|-------------------|
| KRAB | Seattle, WA | 1962 | Origin station |
| KBOO | Portland, OR | 1968 | KRAB volunteer David Calhoun organized |
| KDNA | St. Louis, MO | 1969 | KRAB engineer Jeremy Lansman founded |
| KCHU | Dallas, TX | -- | Milam helped found |
| WORT | Madison, WI | -- | Milam helped found |
| KUSP | Santa Cruz, CA | -- | Milam helped found |
| KPOO | San Francisco, CA | -- | Milam helped found |
| KTAO | Los Gatos, CA | late 1960s | Milam purchased and renamed |

### 4.3 Growth of the Network

The KRAB Nebula eventually included approximately 40 stations across the United States. Not all survived -- community radio is inherently fragile, dependent on volunteer energy and listener donations that can fluctuate dramatically -- but enough survived to establish community radio as a permanent presence on the American dial. The network demonstrated that KRAB was not a one-off experiment but a replicable model.

### 4.4 The PNW as Incubator

The PNW served as the incubator for the community radio movement. KRAB in Seattle, KBOO in Portland, and KAOS in Olympia formed a triangle of community stations that supported each other, shared programming, and trained volunteers who went on to start stations elsewhere. The region's concentration of community radio stations was unmatched anywhere else in the country.

## 5. KBOO Portland

### 5.1 Origins

KBOO signed on June 3, 1968, in Portland, Oregon. The station was organized by David Calhoun, a KRAB volunteer who carried the community radio model south to Portland. KBOO started with a total investment of under $4,000 and a monthly budget of approximately $50, broadcasting as a 10-watt repeater of KRAB's signal.

### 5.2 Growth and Independence

By 1970, KBOO had acquired a used 1,000-watt transmitter that expanded its coverage to much of Northwest Oregon. The power increase marked KBOO's transition from a KRAB repeater to an independent community station with its own programming and identity.

### 5.3 Native American Programming

KBOO is notable as the only station in the country with a daily, locally produced Native American news program. This programming, produced by and for Native American community members, provides coverage of tribal governance, cultural events, land and resource issues, and federal Indian policy that is available nowhere else on the Portland radio dial.

### 5.4 Community Diversity

Over 400 volunteers produce programming for KBOO, and 35% of the station's programming is produced by people of color. The station broadcasts in multiple languages, serving Portland's diverse immigrant communities as well as its English-speaking audience. KBOO's commitment to diversity is not a recent addition -- it has been part of the station's mission since its founding.

### 5.5 Organizational Model

KBOO operates as a listener-sponsored nonprofit with a staff of approximately twelve paid employees supplemented by its volunteer corps. The station's governing board includes community representatives, and programming decisions are made through a participatory process that gives volunteers significant influence. This model is both the station's strength (it ensures community accountability) and its challenge (participatory governance is slow and sometimes contentious).

### 5.6 Persistence

KBOO has broadcast continuously since 1968, surviving multiple financial crises, internal conflicts, and changes in the Portland media landscape. The station's persistence reflects both the depth of its community support and the ongoing need for programming that commercial and public radio do not provide. In a Portland radio market dominated by corporate-owned commercial stations and NPR-affiliated public stations, KBOO remains the only station fully controlled by its community.

## 6. KAOS at Evergreen State College

### 6.1 Founding

KAOS began broadcasting on January 1, 1973 from The Evergreen State College in Olympia, Washington. Founded by Dean Katz, the station was designed to serve both the college community and the broader Olympia area.

### 6.2 Mission

KAOS's mission centers on voices underrepresented in mainstream media. The station's programming includes Native American cultural programming, women's programming, Hispanic community programming, alternative news coverage, independent music, and syndicated public affairs programming from Pacifica Radio.

### 6.3 The Evergreen Connection

Evergreen State College's unconventional educational philosophy -- no grades, interdisciplinary seminars, student-designed curricula -- created a campus culture that naturally supported freeform community radio. KAOS was not an extracurricular activity tacked onto a conventional institution; it was an organic expression of Evergreen's commitment to experiential learning and community engagement.

### 6.4 Olympia's Music Scene

KAOS played a significant role in nurturing Olympia's independent music scene, which would later produce bands associated with the riot grrrl movement and the broader Pacific Northwest indie music culture. The station provided airplay for local bands that could not get attention from commercial stations, creating an audience for independent music that commercial radio's format restrictions would not accommodate.

### 6.5 The Olympia Triangle

Together, KAOS (Olympia), KRAB (Seattle), and KBOO (Portland) formed a triangle of community radio stations covering the I-5 corridor. This concentration of community radio in a single region was unique in the United States and created a cultural infrastructure that supported independent music, progressive politics, environmental activism, and cultural experimentation throughout the Pacific Northwest.

## 7. The Counterculture Connection

### 7.1 KRAB and Seattle's Underground

KRAB was deeply embedded in Seattle's 1960s counterculture. The station's offices and studios served as a gathering place for activists, artists, and intellectuals. Staff and volunteers overlapped with the city's underground newspaper, the *Helix*, creating a network of alternative media that challenged mainstream narratives.

### 7.2 Tom Robbins and KRAB

Novelist Tom Robbins contributed "Notes from the Underground" segments to KRAB, bringing literary sensibility to community radio. Robbins, who would later achieve fame with novels like *Even Cowgirls Get the Blues* and *Still Life with Woodpecker*, was part of the Seattle literary and artistic community that found a home at KRAB.

### 7.3 Timothy Leary Broadcasts

KRAB broadcast recordings of Timothy Leary, the Harvard psychologist turned psychedelic evangelist. These broadcasts were part of KRAB's broader commitment to presenting voices and ideas that commercial radio excluded -- whether those voices were radical philosophers, avant-garde musicians, or political activists.

### 7.4 The Counterculture's Radio Needs

The counterculture needed radio for the same reasons every community needs media: to communicate, to build shared identity, and to organize. Commercial radio, with its advertiser-driven content and format restrictions, could not serve these needs. Community radio -- with its volunteer programmers, its commitment to unfiltered content, and its independence from commercial pressure -- was the counterculture's natural medium.

### 7.5 Beyond Politics

KRAB's counterculture programming was not exclusively political. The station's world music programming, its avant-garde art discussions, its poetry readings, and its environmental coverage all served countercultural audiences who were seeking alternatives to mainstream culture broadly, not just mainstream politics. This breadth distinguished KRAB from political radio stations and contributed to its lasting influence.

## 8. Punk Radio and Life Elsewhere

### 8.1 Norman Batley's Show

In the late 1970s, KRAB programmer Norman Batley launched "Life Elsewhere," a show dedicated to punk rock and the emerging post-punk scene. The show brought the Ramones, the Clash, Wire, Siouxsie and the Banshees, and other punk and post-punk artists to PNW airwaves years before commercial radio acknowledged the genre's existence.

### 8.2 Early Seattle Punk

"Life Elsewhere" provided critical early airplay for Seattle's nascent punk scene. Local bands that could not get played on commercial stations -- and that were too aggressive for most community radio programmers -- found a home on Batley's show. This pattern of community radio incubating underground music scenes would repeat with even greater impact in the next decade, when KCMU performed the same function for grunge.

### 8.3 The Pipeline

Batley's show established a pipeline that would become central to PNW music culture: local bands record a demo, get played on community or college radio, build a following through live shows and radio exposure, and eventually attract attention from record labels. This pipeline, running through community and college radio stations rather than commercial outlets, was the mechanism by which the PNW produced national music movements. Punk was the first test case; grunge would be the most famous.

### 8.4 Freeform to Format

"Life Elsewhere" represented a tension within freeform radio: as programmers developed expertise and followings around specific genres, their shows began to resemble the format radio that community stations defined themselves against. A show dedicated exclusively to punk rock was, in a sense, a punk rock format show -- more specialized than anything on commercial radio, but also more structured than KRAB's original freeform ideal.

## 9. Reagan-Era Funding Cuts and KRAB's Closure

### 9.1 The Funding Crisis

The election of Ronald Reagan in 1980 and the subsequent budget cuts to the Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB) and the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) devastated noncommercial radio. Community radio stations, which had received modest but essential grants from these agencies, found themselves cut off from funding that had helped cover basic operating costs.

### 9.2 KRAB's Financial Struggle

KRAB, never financially robust, was hit hard by the Reagan-era cuts. The station's listener support, while loyal, was insufficient to cover operating costs without supplementary grant funding. The small staff was stretched thin, equipment was aging, and the cost of maintaining a transmitter on a commercial FM frequency (107.7) was higher than the costs faced by stations on the noncommercial band.

### 9.3 Closure

KRAB closed on April 15, 1984, twenty-one years and four months after its founding. The closure was mourned by the station's listeners and by community radio advocates nationwide. KRAB had been the movement's proof of concept -- the station that demonstrated community radio could work outside Berkeley -- and its closure raised questions about the model's long-term viability.

### 9.4 The KSER Legacy

KRAB's assets -- equipment, archives, and the proceeds from the sale of its license -- enabled the founding of KSER-FM (90.7, Everett) in 1991. KSER carried forward KRAB's community radio mission, though in a different form and in a different city. The KRAB-to-KSER transition demonstrated that community radio stations, even when they close, can seed new stations if their communities and assets are preserved.

### 9.5 The Broader Impact

KRAB's closure was part of a wave of community radio station closures in the 1980s, driven by funding cuts, rising costs, and volunteer burnout. The stations that survived -- KBOO, KAOS, and others -- emerged from the decade stronger and more disciplined, having learned that financial sustainability required deliberate effort, not just ideological commitment.

### 9.6 Lessons from the Closure

KRAB's closure taught the community radio movement several lessons. First, ideological purity is not a business plan -- stations need sustainable revenue models, not just passionate volunteers. Second, volunteer organizations require institutional structures (boards, policies, succession plans) to survive beyond their founders. Third, the commercial FM spectrum is an expensive place to operate a noncommercial station; future community stations would overwhelmingly choose the noncommercial band below 92 MHz. These lessons informed the next generation of community stations and contributed to the survival of KBOO, KAOS, and the stations that KRAB's closure would seed.

## 10. The Legacy Frequencies

### 10.1 107.7: From KRAB to KNDD

KRAB's 107.7 FM frequency did not stay dark for long. On August 23, 1991, the frequency was relaunched as KNDD ("The End"), a commercial alternative rock station. The timing was extraordinary: KNDD debuted just weeks before Nirvana's *Nevermind* album transformed alternative rock from a niche genre to a global commercial force. The station that had incubated punk rock in the 1970s was reborn as the commercial outlet for punk's grandchildren.

### 10.2 The Irony

The KRAB-to-KNDD transition encapsulates a recurring pattern in media history: noncommercial pioneers create audiences and cultural spaces that commercial operators then exploit. KRAB's freeform programming, punk shows, and counterculture ethos created a listener base that valued independent music. When commercial radio finally recognized the market, it was KRAB's old frequency -- and KRAB's old audience -- that provided the foundation.

### 10.3 Continuity and Rupture

The transition from KRAB to KNDD represents both continuity and rupture. Continuity: alternative music continued to be broadcast on 107.7. Rupture: the community-controlled, volunteer-driven, noncommercial model was replaced by a corporate-owned, commercially formatted station. The music survived; the organizational model did not.

## 11. The Era's Lasting Impact

### 11.1 The Organizational Model

The community radio model that KRAB, KBOO, and KAOS pioneered proved more durable than any individual station. The organizational principles -- volunteer governance, listener funding, programming autonomy, community accountability -- were adopted by hundreds of stations nationwide. Even as KRAB itself closed, the model it demonstrated continued to generate new stations and sustain existing ones.

### 11.2 The Programming Legacy

KRAB's freeform programming philosophy influenced not just community radio but the broader independent media landscape. The idea that a media outlet could serve artistic and intellectual purposes rather than commercial ones -- that programming decisions could be made by people who cared about content rather than ratings -- anticipated the independent media explosion of the digital age. Podcasting, YouTube channels, and independent online publications all operate on principles that KRAB articulated in 1962: find an audience that cares about what you do, serve that audience honestly, and let the content justify the enterprise.

### 11.3 The PNW Identity

Community radio became part of the PNW's cultural identity. The region is known for environmental consciousness, indie music, progressive politics, and cultural independence -- and community radio both reflected and reinforced these values. KRAB, KBOO, and KAOS were not just radio stations; they were institutions that helped define what it meant to live in the Pacific Northwest. Their programming gave voice to the region's distinctive culture, and their organizational models demonstrated that PNW communities could build and sustain media institutions on their own terms.
