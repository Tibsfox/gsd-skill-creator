> **Domain:** Broadcasting History
> **Module:** 1 -- Pioneers and Spark
> **Through-line:** *Before there were call letters, before the Department of Commerce distinguished broadcast from experimental, before anyone imagined advertising on radio -- there were amateurs in garages and basements, draped tapestries on the walls, sending voices into static. ## 1. The Pre-Commercial Spark

The earliest radio broadcasting experiments in the Pacific Northwest predate commercial licensing by several years. These experiments emerged from a culture of amateur wireless operators who had been sending Morse code signals since before World War I. The region's geography -- separated from East Coast broadcasting centers by thousands of miles of open terrain -- meant that PNW operators were largely self-taught, drawing from technical manuals, correspondence courses, and each other.

### 1.1 The Wireless Telegraph Era

Before voice broadcasting existed, the PNW had an active community of wireless telegraph operators. These operators used spark-gap transmitters to send Morse code, communicating with ships along the Pacific Coast and with other amateur stations inland. The U.S. Navy's Puget Sound Naval Shipyard at Bremerton maintained a powerful wireless station, and civilian operators often listened in on naval traffic -- a practice that would eventually prompt regulatory action.

### 1.2 The Transition to Voice

The transition from Morse code to voice broadcasting required different equipment: a continuous-wave transmitter instead of a spark-gap, a microphone instead of a telegraph key, and a receiver capable of demodulating amplitude-modulated signals. In the PNW, this transition happened station by station between 1919 and 1922, driven by individual experimenters rather than corporate investment.

The Department of Commerce, which regulated radio before the creation of the Federal Radio Commission (1927) and later the FCC (1934), issued experimental licenses with call signs beginning with "7" for the Pacific Northwest's seventh radio district. These experimental stations operated without fixed schedules, broadcast ranges, or content expectations.

## 2. Experimental Wireless in the PNW

### 2.1 Portland's KFU (1915)

The earliest documented radio broadcasting experiment in the Pacific Northwest occurred in Portland in 1915, when station KFU in the Lents neighborhood transmitted single words by voice. This predates the commonly cited "first broadcasts" by several years. KFU's transmissions were primitive -- single words sent to verify that the equipment worked -- but they mark the PNW's entry into voice broadcasting.

### 2.2 Station 7XF -- Northwestern Radio Manufacturing (Portland)

Charles Austin's Northwestern Radio Manufacturing Company operated experimental station 7XF in Portland. By early 1922, the station was broadcasting phonograph concerts nightly, reaching an estimated 400 listener sets in the Portland area. Austin's station demonstrated a model that would become common in early radio: a hardware manufacturer using broadcasting to create demand for radio receivers.

### 2.3 Station 7YS -- Saint Martin's College (Lacey, WA)

Station 7YS at Saint Martin's College in Lacey, Washington is considered the state's first broadcast station. Founded by faculty and students at the small Catholic college, 7YS began as a Morse code station before transitioning to voice. It would receive the commercial call letters KGY in 1922 and later relocate to Olympia, where it became the region's longest-running continuously licensed station.

### 2.4 Oregon Agricultural College -- Station 7XH

Oregon Agricultural College (now Oregon State University) announced a new wireless station, 7XH, in January 1922. The station served both educational and experimental purposes, with faculty using it to distribute agricultural information to farmers across the Willamette Valley.

### 2.5 Willard Hawley Jr. -- Station 7XG

Willard Hawley Jr.'s station 7XG was broadcasting on a regular schedule by March 1922, with signals received as far as 2,500 miles away near Honolulu. This remarkable range -- achieved with modest power -- illustrates the uncongested radio spectrum of the era. With few stations on the air, signals could travel vast distances without interference. Hawley's station demonstrated that the Pacific Northwest's location on the Pacific Rim could make it a broadcasting bridge between the American mainland and Hawaii -- a geographic advantage that later commercial stations would exploit for military and commercial communication.

### 2.6 The Experimental Community

The experimental wireless operators of the pre-1922 era formed a tight-knit community connected by shared technical interests and the novelty of their medium. Operators knew each other by call sign, exchanged technical advice by mail and Morse code, and gathered at occasional meetings organized by local radio clubs. This community of practice -- amateur, voluntary, technically sophisticated -- established a culture of experimentation and mutual support that would reappear in every subsequent era of PNW broadcasting, from community radio volunteers to college radio DJs to podcast producers.

## 3. The Class of 1922

The pivotal year for PNW broadcasting was 1922. The U.S. Department of Commerce began distinguishing experimental stations from broadcast service stations, issuing commercial broadcast licenses for the first time. In a single year, the PNW went from a handful of experimental operators to a region dotted with licensed commercial stations.

### 3.2 The Licensing Rush

The Class of 1922 reflects a national phenomenon: across the United States, hundreds of stations were licensed in that single year as the Department of Commerce struggled to keep pace with demand. In the PNW, the rush was driven by newspapers (the Oregonian, the Post-Intelligencer), electronics retailers (Hallock & Watson), educational institutions (Washington State College, Oregon Agricultural College), and individual enthusiasts (Kraft, Wasmer, Hawley).

### 3.3 Newspaper-Owned Stations

Newspapers were among the first commercial entities to embrace radio, viewing it not as competition but as a promotional tool that would drive newspaper sales. The Oregonian's KGW and the Post-Intelligencer's KFC both followed this model. The newspapers provided content (news, entertainment) and the stations provided publicity for the newspaper brand. This symbiotic relationship would persist through the 1930s before radio advertising revenue began competing with newspaper advertising. The newspaper-radio connection also gave these stations a journalistic identity from the start -- news was part of their programming DNA, not an afterthought.

## 4. Vincent Kraft and KJR

Vincent Kraft's trajectory from amateur wireless operator to commercial broadcaster illustrates the entire arc of early PNW radio in a single biography.

### 4.1 From Morse Code to Music

Kraft began with a Morse code station (call sign 7AC) in 1917, operating from his home in Seattle's Ravenna neighborhood. In 1919, he upgraded to voice broadcasting with experimental call sign 7XC, using a five-watt transmitter, a microphone, a phonograph, and a piano. He draped tapestries on the garage walls to reduce noise and improve acoustics -- an improvised solution that anticipated the acoustic treatment studios would use for decades.

### 4.2 KJR Licensed

KJR received its commercial license on March 9, 1922, making it among the earliest licensed broadcast stations in the Pacific Northwest. The station continued to operate from Kraft's garage, broadcasting music, news, and live performances. Kraft's wife and friends served as announcers and performers.

### 4.3 Growth and Power Increases

KJR's 1925 upgrade to 1,000 watts dramatically extended its reach. Listeners in Alaska began writing in to report receiving the signal clearly. This power increase marked KJR's transition from a local curiosity to a regional broadcaster, and it set the stage for the station's eventual network affiliation in the late 1920s.

### 4.4 The Ravenna Garage as Broadcasting Cradle

The Ravenna garage where KJR was born encapsulates the Amiga Principle: a small, purpose-built system achieving results that conventional wisdom said required far greater resources. Kraft spent very little on his initial setup -- a transmitter, a microphone, some furniture, and the tapestries. The station's early success came not from capital investment but from Kraft's technical ingenuity and his understanding that content mattered more than power.

## 5. KGW and Portland's Radio Dawn

### 5.1 The 98th License

The Oregonian received the 98th radio license in the country when KGW was authorized on March 21, 1922. The station's debut broadcast on March 25 featured soprano Edith Mason of the Chicago Grand Opera, setting a high standard for programming quality that would characterize KGW throughout the 1920s.

### 5.2 Class B Designation

By September 1922, KGW was assigned the exclusive "Class B" wavelength (400 meters), a designation recognizing the station's quality equipment and programming. The Class B license carried higher technical standards than the basic broadcast license, and KGW was among a select group of stations nationwide to receive the designation.

### 5.3 Early Programming

KGW's early programming reflected the eclectic nature of 1920s radio. The station broadcast live music, lectures, weather reports, agricultural market prices, and community announcements. Local performers treated the station as a showcase, and Portland audiences tuned in nightly to hear what was on the air. The lack of recording technology meant that everything was live -- a constraint that fostered a spontaneous, intimate broadcasting style.

### 5.4 NBC Affiliation

KGW affiliated with NBC Red Network in 1927, carrying the network's dramas, comedies, news, and sports programming alongside locally produced content. This affiliation connected Portland listeners to the national broadcasting mainstream for the first time, but it also began the process of standardization that would gradually replace local programming with network feeds.

### 5.5 The Oregonian Connection

The Oregonian's ownership of KGW gave the station access to the newspaper's reporting staff, wire services, and editorial resources. This integration of print and broadcast journalism was ahead of its time, and it gave KGW an advantage in news programming that most early radio stations -- owned by hobbyists or electronics retailers -- could not match.

## 6. Washington State College and KFAE

### 6.1 Educational Broadcasting Pioneer

Washington State College (now Washington State University) in Pullman signed on station KFAE on December 10, 1922. The station was among the first educational broadcasters in the nation, using radio to extend the land-grant university's mission of public service beyond the campus.

### 6.2 The Land-Grant Mission on Air

KFAE broadcast agricultural extension programming, weather reports, and educational lectures to farmers and rural communities across eastern Washington. In a region where many homesteads were hours from the nearest town, radio provided a lifeline of information that had previously been available only through sporadic mail delivery of printed bulletins.

### 6.3 Call Sign Evolution

The station's call sign traced a revealing arc: KFAE became KWSC (for Washington State College) and eventually KWSU (for Washington State University). Each name change reflected institutional identity shifts, but the station's core mission -- serving the public through educational broadcasting -- remained constant across nine decades.

### 6.4 The Radio Courses

WSC was among the few institutions in the country offering courses in radio broadcasting by the mid-1920s. These courses, taught by faculty including Maynard Lee Daggy, gave students hands-on experience producing programs for KFAE/KWSC. Among the students who passed through these courses was a young Edward Roscoe Murrow, who received a B in the radio class -- a grade that would prove to be one of history's more consequential academic performances.

## 7. The Station Proliferation

### 7.1 Beyond Seattle and Portland

By the mid-1920s, radio stations had spread across the Pacific Northwest, reaching smaller communities that the initial urban stations could not serve. KHQ moved from Louis Wasmer's Capitol Hill apartment in Seattle to Spokane, where it became the dominant station in eastern Washington. KGY continued broadcasting from Olympia. Stations appeared in Tacoma, Bellingham, Eugene, and other regional centers.

### 7.2 The Economics of Early Radio

Early radio stations operated without advertising revenue. The Department of Commerce initially prohibited commercial messages on the air, viewing radio as a public resource. Stations were funded by their owners -- newspapers subsidized them as promotional tools, retailers used them to sell radio receivers, universities funded them as educational outreach, and individual enthusiasts absorbed the costs as a hobby.

This model was unsustainable. By the mid-1920s, stations began experimenting with "sponsored" programs -- shows paid for by a commercial entity that received a brief mention at the beginning and end of the broadcast. This modest beginning would eventually transform into the advertising-supported model that defined commercial broadcasting for the rest of the century.

### 7.3 The Listener Community

The PNW's early radio audience was a self-selected community of technology enthusiasts. Receiving a radio signal required either building a crystal set from components or purchasing a manufactured receiver -- both of which required a degree of technical interest and disposable income. Listeners wrote letters to stations reporting reception quality, requesting favorite songs, and describing the experience of hearing voices emerge from static. These letters served as the era's audience measurement system.

## 8. Technical Foundations

### 8.1 Transmitter Technology

The progression from spark-gap to continuous-wave to vacuum-tube transmitters defined the technical trajectory of the era. Spark-gap transmitters, used for Morse code, produced a broad, noisy signal unsuitable for voice. Continuous-wave transmitters, enabled by the audion tube invented by Lee de Forest, produced a clean carrier wave that could be modulated with voice or music.

### 8.2 Power and Range

Power levels in the early 1920s ranged from 5 watts (Kraft's original KJR transmitter) to several hundred watts for the larger stations. The uncongested spectrum meant that even low-power stations could achieve remarkable range -- Hawley's 7XG was received in Hawaii. As more stations came on air, interference became a growing problem, leading to the regulatory interventions of the late 1920s.

### 8.3 Antenna Design

Early PNW broadcasters improvised antenna systems using whatever structures were available. KFC operated from a rooftop shack atop the Post-Intelligencer building, using the building's height to improve signal propagation. Rural stations strung wire antennas between trees or erected simple wooden towers. The science of antenna design was still evolving, and most early broadcasters relied on trial and error.

### 8.4 Receiver Technology

On the receiving end, the crystal set was the entry-level device -- cheap, simple, and capable of picking up local stations with a headphone. More sophisticated superheterodyne receivers, available commercially by the mid-1920s, offered loudspeaker output and better selectivity, allowing listeners to tune between stations. The spread of affordable receivers drove audience growth, which in turn motivated more stations to go on the air.

### 8.5 The PNW's Terrain Challenge

The Pacific Northwest's geography posed unique challenges for early broadcasters. The Cascade Range bisected both Washington and Oregon, creating a natural barrier between the wet, populated western slopes and the dry, sparsely settled eastern interior. AM radio signals, which follow the earth's curvature, could bend over mountains to some degree, but the dense forests and rugged terrain of the Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Range attenuated signals significantly. Stations in Seattle and Portland could not reliably serve communities east of the Cascades, necessitating separate stations in Spokane, Pullman, and other eastern population centers.

### 8.6 Power Sources and Reliability

Early radio transmitters required reliable electrical power, which was not universally available in the PNW of the 1920s. Urban stations could draw from city power grids, but rural stations and early experimental operations sometimes relied on batteries or small generators. The PNW's abundance of hydroelectric power -- the Bonneville and Grand Coulee dams were still years away, but smaller hydro operations were common -- gave the region an advantage in reliable, affordable electricity that would support broadcasting's growth through the 1930s and beyond.

## 9. The Regulatory Landscape

### 9.1 The Department of Commerce Era

Radio regulation in the 1920s was handled by the Department of Commerce under Secretary Herbert Hoover. The department issued licenses, assigned call letters, and attempted to manage frequency allocations, but it lacked enforcement authority. When stations interfered with each other, the department could do little beyond issuing polite requests to change frequencies.

### 9.2 The Radio Act of 1927

The chaos of unregulated broadcasting led to the Radio Act of 1927, which created the Federal Radio Commission (FRC) with authority to assign frequencies, set power limits, and revoke licenses. The act established the principle that the radio spectrum was public property, licensed to broadcasters who served the "public interest, convenience, and necessity" -- a phrase that would shape broadcasting regulation for the next century.

### 9.3 Impact on PNW Stations

The new regulatory framework forced some PNW stations off the air entirely, as the FRC consolidated frequencies and eliminated stations that could not demonstrate adequate equipment or programming. Stations that survived the consolidation emerged stronger, with clearer frequencies and less interference. The survivors included KJR, KGW, KHQ, KOIN, and the stations that would become the anchors of network radio in the Northwest.

### 9.4 Call Sign Conventions

The early call sign system reflected the ad hoc nature of radio regulation. Pacific Northwest stations received call signs beginning with "K" (west of the Mississippi), while their East Coast counterparts received "W" call signs. Some call signs carried meaning -- KFAE's letters were said to stand for nothing in particular -- while others were deliberately chosen: the Oregonian reportedly selected KGW for its connection to the paper's name.

## 10. The Cultural Impact of Early Broadcasting

### 10.1 Radio as Community Builder

In a region defined by geographic isolation, early radio stations served as community builders in ways that are difficult to appreciate from a modern perspective. Before radio, a farmer in the Palouse or a logger in Grays Harbor received news days late via mail. Radio brought simultaneity -- the experience of hearing something at the same moment as thousands of other listeners. This shared experience created a sense of regional identity that the PNW had previously lacked.

### 10.2 The Oral Culture Shift

Radio reintroduced oral culture into a society that had been dominated by print for centuries. The spoken word -- with its inflections, pauses, accents, and emotional coloring -- carried information that printed text could not convey. PNW listeners heard the voices of their neighbors, their elected officials, and eventually the wider world. The intimacy of voice created a relationship between broadcaster and listener that print could not replicate.

### 10.3 Women in Early PNW Radio

Women participated in early PNW broadcasting primarily as performers -- singers, pianists, and readers on programs like KGW's "Stories by Aunt Nell." The industry's technical and managerial positions were overwhelmingly male, reflecting broader social patterns of the 1920s. Blanche McBride Virgin's ownership of KMED in 1928 was exceptional precisely because it defied these norms. The full integration of women into PNW broadcasting -- as engineers, managers, news anchors, and owners -- would take decades.

### 10.4 The Foundation for What Follows

Every subsequent development in PNW broadcasting -- the network era, the media empires, community radio, college radio, digital streaming -- grew from the foundations laid between 1915 and 1929. The stations licensed in 1922 established the institutional structures. The regulatory decisions of the 1920s set the rules. The audience habits formed during this era -- tuning in, listening together, writing letters to stations -- created the demand that drove the medium's growth. The pioneers did not know what they were building, but what they built endured.

### 10.5 Preservation Challenges

The documentation of early PNW broadcasting faces significant preservation challenges. Few recordings survive from the 1920s -- most early radio was live and unrecorded. Station records were often discarded during ownership changes or closures. KGW's early archives were damaged in a 1943 fire. The historical record depends heavily on newspaper accounts, regulatory filings, and the memories of participants and listeners -- sources that grow scarcer with each passing year. Organizations like HistoryLink.org, the Oregon Historical Society, and university special collections preserve what remains, but gaps are permanent.

### 10.6 The Bellingham Connection

KVOS in Bellingham, near the Canadian border, illustrates the regional reach of the pioneer era. The station served not just Bellingham but the cross-border community that included nearby British Columbia. Border stations operated in a regulatory gray zone, technically subject to U.S. regulations but reaching Canadian listeners who were beyond the Department of Commerce's jurisdiction. This cross-border dynamic would persist throughout PNW broadcasting history, as stations in Bellingham, Spokane, and other border communities served audiences in both countries.

### 10.7 The Tacoma Stations

Tacoma, Washington's third-largest city, developed its own broadcasting culture in the 1920s. KMO and other Tacoma stations served an industrial city whose identity was distinct from neighboring Seattle. Tacoma's stations covered the port, the timber industry, and the military installations at Fort Lewis and McChord Field -- topics that Seattle stations addressed only peripherally. The relationship between Tacoma and Seattle broadcasting -- cooperative in coverage, competitive in identity -- foreshadowed the multi-city dynamics that would characterize PNW media markets for the next century.

### 10.8 The Eugene and Southern Oregon Frontier

In southern Oregon, stations in Eugene, Medford, and the Rogue Valley served communities that were even more isolated from the broadcasting centers of Seattle and Portland. KMED in Medford, which Blanche McBride Virgin would own by 1928, served the agricultural communities of the Rogue Valley. Eugene stations served the university community and the timber industry. These southern Oregon stations operated at the frontier of PNW broadcasting -- distant from network hubs, dependent on local talent, and serving audiences whose needs were distinctly different from those of urban listeners.

### 10.9 The Infrastructure Pattern

The pioneer era established an infrastructure pattern that would repeat throughout PNW broadcasting history: an individual or small group builds something with minimal resources, the audience grows organically, institutional support follows, and the station becomes a community fixture. Kraft's garage became KJR. The Oregonian's experiment became KGW. Washington State College's educational station became the seed of Northwest Public Broadcasting. In each case, the founding act was modest -- a transmitter, a microphone, an antenna -- and the outcome far exceeded the initial investment. This pattern of asymmetric returns from small, well-placed investments is the through-line of PNW broadcasting.

### 10.10 Setting the Stage

By 1929, the Pacific Northwest had a functioning broadcasting ecosystem: stations in major cities, a growing listener base, network connections to the national broadcasting mainstream, and a regulatory framework that, while imperfect, provided enough structure for the industry to grow. The garage experimenters had become professionals. The hobby had become an industry. And the stage was set for the Golden Age -- when the PNW would produce the voices, the programs, and the innovations that would shape American broadcasting for the next twenty years.

### 10.11 The Amateur Legacy

The amateur radio operators who preceded commercial broadcasting did not disappear when licensed stations arrived. They continued operating, forming clubs, building equipment, and experimenting with frequencies and techniques that commercial broadcasters did not use. The amateur community served as a talent pipeline for commercial broadcasting -- many early station engineers had learned their skills as hams. The amateur-to-professional pipeline would repeat throughout PNW broadcasting history, from the ham operators of the 1920s through the volunteer DJs of community and college radio decades later. The pioneer spirit of those first experimenters -- building something from nothing, sharing it freely, iterating through failure -- became embedded in the region's broadcasting DNA.
