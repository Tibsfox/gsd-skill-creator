# College Radio and the Grunge Revolution (1972--2001)

> **Domain:** Broadcasting History
> **Module:** 5 -- College Radio and Grunge
> **Through-line:** *A 10-watt transmitter in a campus communications building, faithfully iterated upon by volunteers and visionaries over fifty years, becomes KEXP -- a global cultural force broadcasting from Seattle Center. That trajectory is the Amiga Principle in action: architectural leverage over brute force, specialized execution paths producing outcomes that raw computational power alone cannot match.*

## 1. KCMU's Founding

### 1.1 The Four Founders

KCMU was founded in 1972 by University of Washington students John Kean, Cliff Noonan, Victoria Fiedler, and Brent Wilcox. The four students envisioned a campus radio station that would serve the University District community as well as the UW campus -- a station that would program music and content that Seattle's commercial stations ignored.

### 1.2 The Canadian Treaty Channel

The founders petitioned the FCC to use a Canadian treaty channel at 90.5 FM. Treaty channels are frequencies allocated through international agreements between the United States and Canada to manage cross-border interference along the northern border. The 90.5 frequency sat in the noncommercial portion of the FM band, below 92 MHz, which meant KCMU would be a noncommercial educational station -- a classification that shaped its identity and its relationship with the university.

### 1.3 The 10-Watt Beginning

KCMU began broadcasting with a 10-watt transmitter from McMahon Hall on the UW campus. At 10 watts, the station's signal barely reached beyond the University District. Listeners more than a few miles from campus could not hear it. This extreme low power was both a limitation and a defining characteristic: KCMU was a hyperlocal station, serving a neighborhood rather than a city, and its programming reflected that intimate scale.

### 1.4 Early Programming

KCMU's early programming was determined by its student volunteers, who brought their own record collections and musical interests to the station. The result was an eclectic mix that drew from rock, jazz, blues, folk, international music, and whatever else the volunteers cared about. There was no professional music director, no market research, and no playlist -- just students sharing what they loved with whoever could pick up the signal.

## 2. The Community Support Transition

### 2.1 The 1981 Budget Crisis

In 1981, University of Washington budget cuts threatened KCMU's existence. The university could no longer justify funding a student radio station when academic programs were being cut. The budget crisis forced KCMU to find alternative revenue -- and the solution was the community-supported model that KPFA and KRAB had pioneered.

### 2.2 Becoming Community-Supported

KCMU launched its first on-air fundraising drive and began soliciting listener memberships. The transition from university funding to community support changed the station's character: it was no longer a student activity funded by tuition dollars but a community institution funded by listeners who valued what the station provided. This shift gave community members -- not just students -- a stake in the station's programming.

### 2.3 The Wattage Increase

By 1982, KCMU had increased its transmitter power, allowing listeners to hear the station outside the University District for the first time. The expanded coverage area brought in new listeners, new volunteers, and new community support. For the first time, KCMU could be heard across much of Seattle, and the broader audience changed the station's programming dynamic.

### 2.4 Identity Shift

The community support transition marked KCMU's evolution from a student radio station to a hybrid institution -- nominally connected to the university but increasingly driven by community volunteers and community funding. This hybrid identity created tensions (the university still held the license and had institutional requirements; the community wanted programming autonomy) but also gave KCMU a stability and reach that purely student stations could not achieve.

## 3. Chris Knab and the Format Expansion

### 3.1 A Professional Manager

In 1985, Chris Knab became KCMU's station manager. Knab brought professional credentials: he was co-founder of 415 Records and former owner of Aquarius Records in San Francisco. His appointment signaled KCMU's intention to be taken seriously as a music station, not just as a campus activity.

### 3.2 Broadening the Format

Knab broadened KCMU's format to include hip hop, roots music, blues, world music, reggae, and jazz alongside the rock and indie music that had been the station's core. This expansion reflected Knab's belief that a music station should serve the full spectrum of musical expression, not just the genres that its current volunteers happened to prefer.

### 3.3 Rolling Stone Recognition

The year after Knab's arrival, Rolling Stone magazine featured KCMU as a growing "taste maker" -- a station whose playlist influenced what listeners bought and what other stations programmed. The Rolling Stone feature validated KCMU's approach and attracted attention from music industry professionals who had previously ignored college radio.

### 3.4 The Taste-Maker Role

KCMU's taste-maker status was not accidental. The station's volunteer DJs were genuine music enthusiasts who dug deep into new releases, local demos, and import recordings that commercial radio never touched. Their programming decisions -- what to play, how often to play it, what to say about it -- shaped the musical tastes of their audience and, through that audience, the broader Seattle music scene.

## 4. The Sub Pop Connection

### 4.1 Jonathan Poneman at KCMU

Jonathan Poneman hosted KCMU's "Audioasis" show, programming a mix of independent and underground music. His position at KCMU put him at the center of Seattle's music community, where he heard new bands, met musicians, and developed relationships with the independent labels that were releasing the most interesting music of the era.

### 4.2 Meeting Bruce Pavitt

Poneman met Bruce Pavitt at KCMU. Pavitt, who had published the influential *Subterranean Pop* fanzine (later shortened to *Sub Pop*), shared Poneman's passion for underground music and his belief that the Pacific Northwest was producing music of national significance that the major labels were ignoring.

### 4.3 Founding Sub Pop Records

Together, Poneman and Pavitt founded Sub Pop Records, the label that would release early recordings by Nirvana, Soundgarden, Mudhoney, and other bands that defined the grunge movement. Sub Pop's founding at KCMU is one of the most consequential events in popular music history: the label that launched grunge was born in the studios and hallways of a 10-watt college radio station.

### 4.4 The KCMU-Sub Pop Ecosystem

The relationship between KCMU and Sub Pop was symbiotic. KCMU gave Sub Pop bands airplay that commercial stations would not provide. Sub Pop gave KCMU access to the most exciting new music being made in Seattle. Together, they created an ecosystem -- bands, label, radio station, audience -- that incubated a musical movement before the rest of the world knew it existed.

## 5. Faith Henschel and Bands That Will Make You Money

### 5.1 The Music Director

Faith Henschel served as KCMU's music director during the mid-1980s, a period when the station was transitioning from campus obscurity to regional influence. As music director, she determined what new releases received airplay and how prominently they were featured in the station's programming.

### 5.2 The Compilation Cassette

In 1986, Henschel produced the legendary double-cassette compilation "Bands That Will Make You Money," featuring overlooked Northwest bands including Green River, Soundgarden, Treeclimbers, Chemistry Set, and others. The title was deliberately provocative -- aimed at music industry professionals who spoke the language of commerce, not art.

### 5.3 The Mailing

Henschel mailed the compilation to music industry figures nationwide -- A&R representatives, music journalists, radio programmers, and label executives. The cassettes arrived on desks in New York and Los Angeles at a time when most of those industry figures could not have located Seattle on a map, let alone named a single Seattle band.

### 5.4 Impact

"Bands That Will Make You Money" is credited with drawing the earliest outside attention to the emerging Seattle grunge scene. The compilation demonstrated that Seattle had a critical mass of bands producing distinctive, high-quality music -- not just one or two acts, but a scene. This compilation, produced by a college radio music director with no budget and no corporate backing, was the first shot fired in what would become the grunge revolution.

### 5.5 The Amiga Principle

Henschel's compilation is a textbook example of the Amiga Principle: a specialized execution path (a hand-curated cassette compilation mailed to targeted recipients) producing an outsized outcome (national attention for a previously unknown music scene) through architectural leverage rather than brute force. No advertising budget, no marketing campaign, no corporate strategy -- just a knowledgeable music director, a cassette duplicator, and a mailing list.

## 6. Soundgarden, Mudhoney, and the Volunteer DJs

### 6.1 Kim Thayil at KCMU

Kim Thayil, future lead guitarist of Soundgarden, began his relationship with KCMU by winning telephone music trivia contests. His encyclopedic knowledge of music -- particularly punk, post-punk, and heavy metal -- made him a natural fit for the station, and he eventually began hosting his own show. Thayil's transition from listener to trivia winner to DJ to internationally famous musician encapsulates KCMU's role as a gateway between the audience and the stage.

### 6.2 Soundgarden Members as DJs

Multiple Soundgarden members served as volunteer DJs at KCMU. The band's 1987 single "Hunted Down" received heavy play on the station -- not because of corporate promotion but because the band's own members worked at the station and their colleagues were genuinely excited about the music. This is grassroots music promotion in its purest form: the people making the music and the people broadcasting it were the same community.

### 6.3 Mudhoney at KCMU

Mudhoney members also served as KCMU volunteer DJs. Mudhoney, often credited as the first grunge band (their 1988 single "Touch Me I'm Sick" is a genre touchstone), maintained a close relationship with the station throughout their career. The band's connection to KCMU was not just promotional -- it was cultural. The station was where they found their audience, met their peers, and developed their sound.

### 6.4 The Volunteer DJ Pipeline

The fact that future rock stars were working as unpaid volunteer DJs at a college radio station says something important about the PNW music ecosystem of the 1980s. There was no clear boundary between the musicians and the broadcasters; they were the same people, moving between roles fluidly. This permeability -- impossible in the hierarchical world of commercial radio -- was what made KCMU so effective at identifying and promoting new music.

## 7. Nirvana and KCMU

### 7.1 Early Airplay

KCMU debuted tracks from Nirvana's debut album *Bleach* (released on Sub Pop, June 1989) before the album was widely available. The station's DJs recognized the music's power and gave it heavy rotation -- or as close to heavy rotation as a freeform station gets. For listeners tuned to 90.5 FM in the summer of 1989, Nirvana was not yet a phenomenon; they were a local band getting played on the local station.

### 7.2 Pre-Bleach Airplay

KCMU's support for Nirvana predated *Bleach*. The station had been playing tracks like "Floyd the Barber" since 1988, when the band's demo recordings circulated among the Seattle music community. KCMU was among the first stations anywhere to play Nirvana, and its early airplay helped build the local audience that supported the band's earliest shows.

### 7.3 The Paramount Theatre Dedication

On September 23, 1991 -- two days before *Nevermind* was released -- Kurt Cobain dedicated Nirvana's performance at Seattle's Paramount Theatre to KCMU, acknowledging the station's early support. The dedication was a gesture of gratitude from an artist on the verge of global fame to the community that had believed in him before anyone else did.

### 7.4 Historical Significance

Cobain's KCMU dedication is one of the most significant moments in the relationship between independent radio and popular music. It drew a direct line from a 10-watt college radio station to the biggest band in the world, demonstrating that KCMU's early airplay had been more than a footnote -- it had been foundational. Without KCMU, Nirvana's trajectory would have been different; without Nirvana, KCMU's story would lack its most dramatic chapter.

### 7.5 Rap Attack

KCMU's Sunday night "Rap Attack" was the first Seattle radio program to play Ice-T, Eazy-E, and N.W.A. While grunge dominated the station's legacy, KCMU's hip hop programming was equally pioneering. "Rap Attack" introduced Seattle audiences to West Coast and East Coast hip hop at a time when commercial radio in the Pacific Northwest programmed almost no rap music.

## 8. The Grunge Explosion and Commercial Radio

### 8.1 From Underground to Mainstream

The release of Nirvana's *Nevermind* in September 1991 -- and its unexpected rise to #1 on the Billboard 200 in January 1992, displacing Michael Jackson -- transformed grunge from a regional phenomenon into a global commercial force. Commercial radio stations that had ignored Seattle music for years scrambled to program it. The "alternative rock" format, which had existed as a niche, suddenly became commercially viable.

### 8.2 The Format Shift

Across the country, commercial stations switched to alternative rock formats to capitalize on the grunge explosion. In Seattle, the most significant format switch occurred on KRAB's old frequency: 107.7 FM became KNDD ("The End"), a commercial alternative rock station, on August 23, 1991 -- one month before *Nevermind*'s release. The timing was coincidental but symbolically perfect.

### 8.3 Commercial vs. College Radio

The grunge explosion created a tension between commercial and college radio that persists to this day. Commercial alternative rock stations played a narrower selection of grunge and alternative bands in heavy rotation, driven by market research and advertising demographics. College stations like KCMU played a broader range of music with less repetition, driven by volunteer taste and community values. The two models served different functions: commercial radio made grunge accessible to mass audiences, while college radio continued to incubate new music.

### 8.4 The Aftermath

By the mid-1990s, the initial grunge explosion had subsided. Kurt Cobain's death in April 1994 marked an emotional endpoint for many fans and musicians. Commercial alternative rock stations continued to program the format, but the sense of cultural discovery that had characterized the late 1980s and early 1991 was gone. KCMU, however, continued doing what it had always done: playing new, interesting, and underrepresented music for its community of listeners.

## 9. KNDD: The End on KRAB's Frequency

### 9.1 The Frequency's Journey

The journey of 107.7 FM from KRAB (community radio, 1962--1984) to KNDD (commercial alternative rock, 1991--present) is one of the most symbolically loaded frequency histories in American broadcasting. The same spot on the dial went from counterculture freeform to commercial format, from volunteer-run to corporate-owned, from noncommercial to advertiser-supported.

### 9.2 KNDD's Launch

KNDD debuted on August 23, 1991, with a format focused on alternative rock. The station benefited from extraordinary timing: grunge exploded within weeks of its launch, and "The End" became the commercial home of the Seattle sound. The station's success was built on an audience and a musical culture that KRAB and KCMU had cultivated for decades.

### 9.3 The Cultural Debt

KNDD's commercial success was, in a sense, built on KRAB's cultural investment. KRAB had spent twenty-one years cultivating a Seattle audience for non-mainstream music. When KNDD launched on KRAB's frequency with an alternative music format, it inherited an audience that already knew how to listen to music outside the mainstream. The cultural infrastructure was already in place; KNDD simply monetized it.

## 10. The KCMU-to-KEXP Transformation

### 10.1 The Paul Allen Partnership

In 2001, KCMU partnered with Paul Allen's Experience Music Project (EMP, later MoPOP -- the Museum of Pop Culture). The partnership brought significant financial support and institutional backing. As part of the partnership, KCMU was renamed KEXP, with the "EXP" referencing the Experience Music Project.

### 10.2 The Name Change

The renaming was controversial among longtime listeners and volunteers who felt that KCMU's identity -- built over nearly thirty years of community broadcasting -- was being subsumed by a corporate partnership. Supporters argued that the Paul Allen funding would allow the station to reach a global audience and invest in technology that volunteer-funded KCMU could never afford.

### 10.3 The Hybrid Model

KEXP's model combined elements of community radio (volunteer DJs, listener support, noncommercial programming) with elements of institutional backing (Paul Allen's funding, the EMP brand, professional management). This hybrid was unusual in American radio -- neither purely community nor purely institutional -- and it enabled a combination of artistic freedom and financial stability that few stations have achieved.

### 10.4 The New Facility

KEXP moved to a new, purpose-built facility at Seattle Center in 2015. The facility, designed by SkB Architects, opened to the public in April 2016 with an event attended by 12,000 people. The new building included street-level studios visible to passersby, a performance space, a music library, and public gathering areas -- making the station a physical community hub as well as a radio station.

## 11. KEXP's Digital Pioneering

### 11.1 Technology Firsts

KEXP pioneered a series of digital broadcasting innovations that established it as a model for radio's digital transformation:

| Year | Innovation | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 2000 | Uncompressed CD-quality internet stream | First station to stream at full CD quality |
| 2001 | Real-time playlist | Listeners could see what was playing on the website |
| 2002 | 14-day streaming archive | On-demand access to two weeks of programming |
| 2003 | Cell phone streaming | Mobile listening before the smartphone era |
| 2004 | Webby Award for Best Radio Website | Industry recognition of digital excellence |
| 2008+ | YouTube channel and video streaming | Visual extension of radio programming |

### 11.2 The Global Audience

By 2022, KEXP had achieved metrics that would have been unimaginable for a 10-watt college station:

- Over 50,000 sustaining members
- YouTube channel with 1 million+ subscribers and 500 million+ views
- Annual revenue of $12.5 million
- Weekly listening audience reaching 200,000+
- Listeners in virtually every country with internet access

### 11.3 The Content Strategy

KEXP's digital success was built on content, not technology. The station's in-studio live performance sessions -- broadcast live on the radio, streamed online, and archived on YouTube -- became its most distinctive product. These sessions showcased emerging and established artists in an intimate studio setting, producing video content that attracted viewers far beyond the station's radio audience.

### 11.4 The Full-Song Podcast

KEXP produced one of the first full-song podcasts, allowing listeners to download complete recordings of featured artists. This innovation tested the boundaries of music licensing and copyright law, but it also demonstrated that radio stations could use digital distribution to extend their reach and deepen their relationships with listeners.

### 11.5 From 10 Watts to Global

The trajectory from KCMU's 10-watt transmitter in McMahon Hall to KEXP's global streaming platform is the central narrative of PNW college radio. Each step -- the community support transition, the wattage increase, the Sub Pop connection, the Paul Allen partnership, the digital innovations -- built on the previous one. The station's identity remained consistent through three decades of change: a commitment to new, interesting, and underrepresented music, programmed by people who genuinely love what they play.

### 11.6 The Broader College Radio Ecosystem

KCMU/KEXP was the flagship of PNW college radio, but it was not alone. Other college stations across the region played similar roles on smaller scales: KUPS at the University of Puget Sound, KWCW at Whitman College, KZUU at Washington State University, and stations at community colleges and smaller universities. Each served its campus and local community, provided training for aspiring broadcasters, and programmed music and content that commercial stations ignored. Together, these stations formed a network of alternative media outlets that gave the PNW's independent music and cultural scenes a broadcasting infrastructure that most other American regions lacked.

### 11.7 The Institutional Memory Question

The KCMU-to-KEXP transition raised questions about institutional memory in broadcasting. When a station changes its name, its management, and its facilities, does it remain the same station? KEXP's programming philosophy is recognizably continuous with KCMU's -- the same commitment to new music, the same volunteer DJ culture, the same community orientation. But the institutional context changed dramatically. The scrappy college station funded by bake sales and fund drives became a well-resourced nonprofit with a purpose-built facility and a $12.5 million annual budget. Whether this represents evolution or transformation depends on which aspects of the station's identity one considers essential.
