## 1. The Network Era Arrives

### 1.1 The Three-Network System

By 1929, the American broadcasting landscape had consolidated around three major networks: NBC (which operated both the Red and Blue networks), CBS, and the Mutual Broadcasting System. Each network signed affiliate stations in major markets, providing a pipeline of nationally produced programming in exchange for advertising revenue sharing. For PNW stations, network affiliation was transformative -- it meant access to production values, talent, and content that no local station could match independently. The network era turned radio from a local medium into a national one, and PNW listeners could now hear the same programs as their counterparts in New York and Chicago.

### 1.2 NBC's Pacific Coast Operations

NBC's reach into the Pacific Northwest came through its Pacific Coast chains. The Red Network, NBC's premium service, affiliated with KGW in Portland. The Gold Network (later called the Blue Network, and eventually spun off to become ABC) affiliated with KJR in Seattle and KEX in Portland. The Orange Network served as an additional western feed. These overlapping network identities reflected NBC's complex corporate structure, which the FCC would eventually force to simplify.

### 1.3 CBS and the Columbia Connection

CBS established its Northwest presence through affiliates KIRO in Seattle and KOIN (940 AM) in Portland. KOIN, launched by the group that included prominent Portland businessman Arthur Kirkham, carried CBS programming including drama, comedy, and the network's growing news division. The CBS-KIRO connection would prove historically significant during World War II, when KIRO served as the regional conduit for Edward R. Murrow's London broadcasts.

### 1.4 Mutual Broadcasting System

The Mutual Broadcasting System made its Northwest debut on September 26, 1937, bringing a fourth network option to PNW listeners. Mutual affiliates included KOL in Seattle, KALE in Portland, KMO in Tacoma, KORE in Eugene, KIT in Yakima, KVOS in Bellingham, KGY in Olympia, KPQ in Wenatchee, and stations across the region. Mutual's cooperative ownership model -- affiliates owned the network, rather than the other way around -- reflected a more decentralized philosophy than NBC or CBS.

## 2. The Hoot Owls: America's First Variety Show

### 2.1 Origins at KGW

KGW's "Wiser Order of Hoot Owls" launched January 29, 1923, created by Oregonian editor Edgar Piper and prominent Portland civic figure Charles F. Berg. The late-night variety program broadcast 90-minute live shows weekly from the KGW studios in the Oregonian building. The show's name reflected its late-night time slot -- only night owls would be up to hear it.

### 2.2 Multiple Firsts

The Hoot Owls program holds a remarkable cluster of broadcasting firsts:

- **First variety show on radio** -- combining comedy, music, and audience interaction in a single program format
- **First variety broadcast from a script** -- moving beyond improvisation to structured entertainment
- **First quiz show** -- incorporating audience questions and answers as a programming element
- **First live audience program from a remote location** -- taking the show outside the studio to broadcast from venues around Portland

These innovations were not minor variations on existing forms. They were inventions of new entertainment categories that would define radio (and later television) programming for the rest of the century.

### 2.3 National Reach

Despite broadcasting primarily over KGW, the Hoot Owls achieved national celebrity. The uncongested radio spectrum of the early 1920s allowed KGW's signal to be received far beyond the Portland area, and the program's reputation spread through word of mouth and newspaper coverage. Listeners from across the western United States wrote to the station praising the show.

### 2.4 The Portland Police Sunshine Division

One of the Hoot Owls' most lasting contributions was the founding of the Portland Police Sunshine Division, a charitable organization created by the show's participants. The Sunshine Division, which provides food and clothing to Portland residents in need, continues to operate as a nonprofit organization -- a direct legacy of a 1920s radio variety show.

### 2.5 Cultural Significance

The Hoot Owls demonstrate a pattern that recurs throughout PNW broadcasting history: innovations that originate in the region, achieve success locally, and then influence national broadcasting practice -- often without receiving proper credit. The variety show format that the Hoot Owls pioneered would be perfected by national programs like The Fleischmann's Yeast Hour (1929) and eventually television's The Ed Sullivan Show (1948), but the Portland program came first.

## 3. Mel Blanc's Portland Radio Career

### 3.1 Early Life and Portland Roots

Mel Blanc (born Melvin Jerome Blank, May 30, 1908, San Francisco) moved to Portland at age six. Portland was not a detour in Blanc's career -- it was the crucible where he developed the vocal techniques, comedic timing, and character work that would make him the most famous voice actor in history.

### 3.2 Radio Debut on KGW

Blanc made his radio debut in June 1923, at age fifteen, on KGW's children's show "Stories by Aunt Nell." He was not yet performing the character voices that would define his career; the children's show was his introduction to the medium of radio and to the discipline of performing for an unseen audience.

### 3.3 Joining the Hoot Owls

In 1927, at age nineteen, Blanc joined the Hoot Owls cast. He wrote and performed comedy using dialects and character voices, developing the rapid-fire switching between distinct vocal personas that would later distinguish his work for Warner Bros. The Hoot Owls gave Blanc a weekly platform to experiment with voices before a live and broadcast audience -- an apprenticeship that few performers of his generation could match.

### 3.4 San Francisco Interlude and Return

After a brief stint at San Francisco's KFWI, Blanc returned to KGW and the Hoot Owls. The San Francisco experience showed him a larger radio market but did not offer the creative freedom he enjoyed in Portland. His return to KGW suggests that the PNW environment -- smaller market, greater creative latitude -- was essential to his development.

### 3.5 KEX and Cobwebs and Nuts

In 1933, Blanc moved to KEX to create "Cobwebs and Nuts," a six-day-a-week late-night show. The grueling schedule demanded constant invention of new material and characters. Blanc essentially ran a one-man comedy factory, producing hours of original content every week. The show demonstrated his range and stamina but also exhausted him -- the low pay and relentless pace eventually pushed him to seek opportunities in Los Angeles.

### 3.6 Departure for Los Angeles

In 1935, Blanc left Portland for Los Angeles, where he would audition at Warner Bros. and begin voicing Bugs Bunny, Daffy Duck, Porky Pig, Tweety Bird, Sylvester, Yosemite Sam, and over 400 other characters. His Portland radio career -- from "Stories by Aunt Nell" at age fifteen to "Cobwebs and Nuts" at age twenty-seven -- spanned twelve years and constituted one of the most productive apprenticeships in entertainment history.

### 3.7 The Portland Connection

Blanc's Portland period is often treated as a footnote in biographies that focus on his Warner Bros. career. But the Portland radio stations -- KGW and KEX -- were where he learned the craft. The Hoot Owls taught him to write for radio. "Cobwebs and Nuts" taught him endurance. The PNW's smaller, more tolerant market gave him permission to fail and experiment in ways that a larger, more competitive market would not have allowed.

## 4. Edward R. Murrow: From Skagit County to CBS

### 4.1 Skagit County Childhood

Edward Roscoe Murrow (born Egbert Roscoe Murrow, April 25, 1908, Polecat Creek, North Carolina) moved with his family to Blanchard on Samish Bay in Skagit County, Washington, when he was five. His father Roscoe worked on a logging railroad. Young Ed spent summers in logging camps, absorbing the rhythms of physical labor and the culture of working-class rural Washington. He changed his name from Egbert to Edward during his college years.

### 4.2 Edison High School

Murrow graduated from Edison High School in Skagit County. The small rural school gave him his first opportunities in public speaking and debate -- skills that his college mentor would later refine into the deliberate, measured delivery that defined broadcast journalism.

### 4.3 Washington State College

Murrow enrolled at Washington State College (now WSU) in Pullman in 1926. At WSC, he studied speech under Ida Lou Anderson, who became the most important mentor of his early career.

### 4.4 Ida Lou Anderson's Influence

Anderson, who had childhood polio, taught a humanities course and coached Murrow in poetry, literature, and broadcasting technique. She had him sit behind a screen to simulate radio conditions -- forcing him to communicate entirely through voice, without the visual cues of face-to-face interaction. This exercise was prescient: Anderson was training Murrow for a medium that did not yet have the cultural prominence it would achieve within a decade.

Anderson's most famous contribution to Murrow's technique was her suggestion for the half-second pause after "This--" in his signature "This is London" broadcast opening. That pause -- a moment of silence that made listeners lean forward -- became one of the most recognized vocal signatures in broadcasting history.

### 4.5 The Radio Class

The college was among the few institutions in the country offering radio broadcasting courses. Maynard Lee Daggy taught the course, with students producing programs aired on campus station KWSC (formerly KFAE). Murrow received a B in the class. The grade is notable less for its mediocrity than for what it represents: Murrow was already more interested in content and delivery than in the technical aspects of broadcasting that the course emphasized.

### 4.6 College Activities and Graduation

Murrow graduated on June 2, 1930 as student body president, ROTC cadet colonel, leading actor in campus theatrical productions, star debater, and head of the Pacific Student Presidents Association. This constellation of leadership roles and communication skills -- honed at a land-grant college in eastern Washington -- was the foundation on which his CBS career was built.

### 4.7 The Path to CBS

After graduation, Murrow worked for the Institute of International Education before joining CBS in 1935 as director of talks. His London Blitz broadcasts, beginning in September 1940, reached American listeners via affiliates including KIRO in Seattle -- bringing Murrow's voice back to the Pacific Northwest, where his broadcasting education had begun.

### 4.8 The PNW Thread

Murrow's PNW roots shaped his broadcasting philosophy in ways that are often underappreciated. His directness, his preference for plain language over ornate prose, his identification with working people, and his skepticism of institutional authority all trace back to Skagit County logging camps and Washington State College classrooms. The "velvet sledgehammer" quality of his delivery -- polished but forceful -- was Anderson's teaching, applied to the material that Murrow's rural upbringing had given him.

## 5. Network Affiliates in the Northwest

### 5.1 The Affiliate Map

| Mutual/Don Lee | KOL | KALE | NW debut Sept 26, 1937 |

### 5.2 Programming Flow

Network programming flowed west via dedicated telephone lines leased from AT&T's Long Lines division. Affiliate stations received the network feed and broadcast it locally, interspersing local programming and advertising during designated breaks. The economics were straightforward: the network provided expensive, high-quality programming at no cost to the affiliate, and in return the affiliate provided the network with geographic coverage for its advertisers.

### 5.3 Local vs. Network Identity

The tension between local identity and network programming became a defining feature of the era. Stations like KGW maintained strong local programming -- news, community events, agricultural reports -- alongside their NBC network feed. Others, particularly smaller stations, became essentially relay transmitters for network content, contributing little original programming. This tension between local and national would persist throughout broadcasting history and resurface in the community radio movement of the 1960s.

## 6. War of the Worlds in the PNW

### 6.1 The Broadcast

On October 30, 1938, CBS affiliates KVI and KIRO broadcast Orson Welles's dramatization of H.G. Wells's *The War of the Worlds* across Western Washington. The program, presented as a series of news bulletins interrupting regular programming, described a Martian invasion of New Jersey. Nationally, the broadcast produced scattered panic; in the PNW, it produced one of the era's most remarkable local incidents.

### 6.2 Concrete, Washington

In the town of Concrete (population approximately 1,000), a coincidental power failure during the broadcast plunged the town into darkness. Residents who had been listening to the radio heard reports of alien invasion and simultaneously lost all electric light and power. The combination of alarming radio reports and physical darkness produced genuine terror. Residents fainted. Families grabbed children and fled toward the mountains. Some men grabbed guns.

### 6.3 Aftermath and Significance

The Concrete incident illustrates both the power of radio as a medium and the isolation of rural PNW communities in the 1930s. Residents had no way to verify the broadcast's claims -- no television, no internet, no quick way to contact the outside world. Radio was their primary connection to events beyond their town, and when that connection reported catastrophe while the lights went out, the response was visceral. The incident became a touchstone in discussions of media power and audience vulnerability.

## 7. Wartime Broadcasting

### 7.1 PNW Stations During WWII

World War II transformed PNW broadcasting. Stations carried war news around the clock, broadcast government messages about rationing and war bond drives, and served as the primary information channel for a region deeply connected to the war effort through shipyards (Kaiser's Swan Island and Oregon Shipbuilding Corporation in Portland, Puget Sound Naval Shipyard in Bremerton) and military bases (Fort Lewis, McChord Field, Naval Air Station Whidbey Island).

### 7.2 Murrow's London Connection

Murrow's CBS broadcasts from London during the Blitz reached PNW listeners through KIRO in Seattle and KOIN in Portland. For listeners in Skagit County -- Murrow's childhood home -- the broadcasts carried a personal dimension: they were hearing a local boy describing the most dramatic events of the war. Murrow's voice, shaped by Ida Lou Anderson at WSC and refined through years of CBS broadcasting, became the voice of the war for millions of Americans.

### 7.3 Censorship and Self-Censorship

Wartime broadcasting operated under the Office of Censorship's voluntary Code of Wartime Practices. PNW stations complied with restrictions on reporting troop movements, weather conditions (which could aid enemy navigation), and specific military installation activities. The code was voluntary but compliance was near-universal -- stations understood that violating it could result in loss of license and public opprobrium.

### 7.4 Japanese-American Internment

PNW radio stations played a role in the lead-up to and execution of Japanese-American internment. Some stations broadcast anti-Japanese sentiment that contributed to the climate of fear; others carried the government's relocation orders. The radio coverage of internment -- and its failure to question the policy -- represents one of the darker chapters in PNW broadcasting history. The industry's self-criticism on this point would not come for decades.

## 8. Spokane and Eastern Washington

### 8.1 KHQ's Move to Spokane

Louis Wasmer's KHQ, originally built in a Capitol Hill apartment in Seattle, moved to Spokane where it became the dominant station in eastern Washington. The move reflected the commercial reality that Spokane needed a strong regional broadcaster, and Wasmer saw an opportunity to be the leading station in a market rather than one of several competitors in Seattle.

### 8.2 Regional Coverage Challenges

Eastern Washington's vast distances and sparse population presented unique broadcasting challenges. A single station in Spokane could serve the city but not the farming communities scattered across the Palouse, the Columbia Basin, or the Yakima Valley. The need for broader coverage would eventually drive the development of translator stations and regional networks -- solutions that would not fully materialize until the public broadcasting era of the 1970s and 1980s.

### 8.3 The Spokane Broadcasting Culture

Spokane developed a distinctive broadcasting culture during the Golden Age, centered on KHQ and its competitors. The city served as the commercial hub for eastern Washington, northern Idaho, and western Montana, and its stations reflected this cross-state identity. Spokane broadcasters covered wheat harvests, timber operations, mining activities, and the military presence at Fairchild Air Force Base. The city's distance from Seattle -- 280 miles over Snoqualmie Pass -- ensured that Spokane's broadcasting developed its own identity rather than functioning as a satellite of the western Washington market.

## 9. The Business of Radio

### 9.1 The Advertising Model Matures

By the 1930s, radio advertising had evolved from tentative sponsored programs to a sophisticated industry. National advertisers bought time on network programs, local advertisers bought spots on local stations, and the revenue model that would sustain commercial broadcasting for the next seventy years was firmly established.

### 9.2 Station Ownership Patterns

PNW stations were owned by a mix of newspapers (Oregonian/KGW), individual entrepreneurs (Kraft/KJR, Wasmer/KHQ), and corporations that saw broadcasting as a complement to other business interests. The era of concentrated media ownership -- with single companies controlling multiple stations and multiple media types -- was still decades away, but the foundations were being laid.

### 9.3 Labor and Talent

The business side of radio required a growing workforce: engineers, announcers, salespeople, writers, and musicians. PNW stations drew from local talent pools, creating a regional broadcasting culture distinct from the East Coast establishment. This local talent pipeline would produce figures like Blanc and Murrow who went on to national prominence, but it also sustained hundreds of careers that remained regional -- the announcers, engineers, and salespeople who built and maintained the PNW broadcasting infrastructure.

## 10. The Through-Line: A Region Finds Its Voice

### 10.1 From Local to National

The Golden Age demonstrated that the PNW could produce talent, programming, and broadcasting innovations that influenced the national stage. The Hoot Owls invented the variety show format. Blanc perfected voice acting. Murrow transformed broadcast journalism. In each case, the PNW provided the incubation environment -- smaller markets with greater creative freedom, mentors who recognized potential, and audiences willing to support experimentation.

### 10.2 The Network Paradox

Network affiliation brought the national mainstream to PNW living rooms, but it also began the erosion of local identity that would eventually provoke the community radio movement. The same telephone lines that carried Jack Benny and Bing Crosby to Seattle and Portland carried away the incentive for local production. Why produce a local drama when NBC provided a better one for free? The answer -- because local stories matter, because community identity requires local voices -- would not be fully articulated until Lorenzo Milam bought a donut shop in 1962.

### 10.3 The War's Legacy

World War II left PNW broadcasting larger, more professional, and more connected to national networks than before the war. The wartime experience trained a generation of broadcasters, established radio as an essential information utility, and demonstrated the medium's power to unite and mobilize a nation. But the war also planted the seeds of radio's decline: the same technological advances that improved broadcasting also enabled television, which would challenge radio's dominance within a decade.

### 10.4 Connections Forward

The Golden Age established patterns that echo through subsequent eras. KIRO's relationship with CBS connected Seattle to Murrow's London broadcasts; a generation later, KIRO would be the station that carried national news to a region still defined by Murrow's legacy. The network affiliate model -- centralized production distributed through local stations -- anticipated the public broadcasting model that KWSU and NPR would later adopt. And Mel Blanc's Portland-to-Hollywood trajectory foreshadowed the pipeline that would later carry grunge from KCMU to the Billboard charts.

### 10.5 What Was Lost

The Golden Age also marked the beginning of losses. Local programming declined as networks took over prime evening hours. Live performance on radio gave way to recorded content. The intimate, personal relationship between broadcaster and listener -- possible when stations were operated by individuals like Kraft and owned by local institutions like the Oregonian -- began to yield to corporate formulas. The PNW's subsequent broadcasting history is, in part, a story of communities fighting to preserve what the Golden Age's success threatened to erase.

### 10.6 The Sound of the Era

The PNW Golden Age had a distinctive sound: live orchestras broadcasting from hotel ballrooms, the crack of bat and roar of crowd from Portland Beavers baseball games, the solemn tones of news bulletins reporting Pearl Harbor and D-Day, the conversational intimacy of local announcers who knew their listeners by name. This sound -- warmer, less polished, more personal than what came later -- survives only in fragmentary recordings and the memories of listeners who have now mostly passed. The oral culture of Golden Age radio is itself an endangered cultural heritage, preserved in archives that researchers are still cataloging.

### 10.7 Advertising's Regional Flavor

Radio advertising in the PNW reflected the region's economy and culture. Lumber companies, fishing operations, agricultural suppliers, and department stores like Frederick & Nelson and Meier & Frank sponsored programs and bought airtime. The advertisements themselves were cultural documents -- they tell us what PNW residents bought, what they aspired to, and how they saw themselves. National brands advertised through network programming; local sponsors used local stations, creating an advertising ecosystem that supported both national and regional broadcasting.

### 10.8 The Bridge to Television

The Golden Age's most consequential legacy was the infrastructure -- human, institutional, and technical -- that enabled the transition to television. The broadcast engineers who maintained AM transmitters learned the skills needed to operate television stations. The advertising sales teams who sold radio time adapted to selling television time. The news departments that had covered World War II over radio pivoted to covering the postwar world on television. In the PNW, this bridge was built most visibly by Dorothy Bullitt, who used her radio stations as the financial and organizational foundation for KING-TV. The Golden Age did not end; it transformed.

### 10.9 The Oral History Gap

Many first-person accounts of Golden Age PNW broadcasting have been lost or remain uncollected. Engineers who built stations in garages and attics, announcers who ad-libbed through power failures and equipment malfunctions, listeners who heard Murrow's voice for the first time on a crystal set -- their stories exist in family memories and scattered documents. The Oregon Historical Society, HistoryLink.org, and university special collections have preserved significant materials, but systematic oral history collection for PNW broadcasting has been limited. Each year, more firsthand witnesses pass away, and with them go details that no archive can replace. The urgency of preservation work grows with each passing decade.
