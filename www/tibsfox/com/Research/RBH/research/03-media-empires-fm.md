# Media Empires and FM (1945--1962)

> **Domain:** Broadcasting History
> **Module:** 3 -- Media Empires and FM Revolution

## 1. The Postwar Broadcasting Landscape

### 1.1 Returning to Peacetime

The end of World War II in 1945 transformed American broadcasting. Wartime manufacturing restrictions were lifted, consumer electronics production resumed, and returning veterans created new demand for radio receivers and -- soon -- television sets. For PNW broadcasting, the postwar period brought both enormous opportunity and existential challenge: the opportunity to build new stations and reach new audiences, and the challenge of a new medium -- television -- that threatened to make radio obsolete.

### 1.2 The Technology Surplus

The war had produced enormous advances in electronics technology. Radar, communications equipment, and signal processing techniques developed for military use found civilian applications in broadcasting. Surplus military electronics flooded the market, making components cheap and accessible. This technology surplus lowered the barrier to entry for new broadcasters and enabled existing stations to upgrade their equipment. The engineers who had maintained military communications systems brought their expertise home to PNW radio stations, raising the technical quality of broadcasting across the region.

### 1.3 The FCC License Freeze

The FCC's freeze on new television licenses from 1948 to 1952 had an outsized impact on the PNW. Only one television station -- KRSC-TV in Seattle, which Dorothy Bullitt would purchase and rename KING-TV -- was licensed before the freeze. This gave KING-TV a monopoly on Seattle television for five years, a commercial advantage that helped fund the entire King Broadcasting empire.

## 2. Dorothy Bullitt and King Broadcasting

### 2.1 Dorothy Stimson Bullitt

Dorothy Stimson Bullitt (February 5, 1892 -- June 27, 1989) was born into one of Seattle's most prominent families. Her father, C.D. Stimson, was a lumber baron and real estate developer. Her husband, Scott Bullitt, was a lawyer and politician who died in 1932, leaving Dorothy a widow with three children and substantial but not unlimited wealth. She entered broadcasting in 1946 at age fifty-four -- an age when most executives of the era were contemplating retirement, not founding media empires.

### 2.2 The KEVR Purchase

Bullitt purchased Seattle radio station KEVR (1090 AM) in 1946 for her company, initially called Western Waves. The purchase was her entry into broadcasting, and she immediately set about building something larger than a single radio station.

### 2.3 Acquiring the KING Call Letters

Bullitt wanted to change KEVR's call letters to KING, for King County. The letters were registered to the merchant vessel SS Watertown, operated by the Isthmian Steamship Company. The story of how Bullitt acquired them has become broadcasting legend: according to one account, she rowed out to the freighter anchored in Puget Sound, bringing champagne. Another version has her negotiating with the ship's owner onshore. In all versions, the captain asked only for a donation to his church. The call letters changed in 1947.

### 2.4 The "Velvet Steamroller"

Bullitt was known in the industry as the "velvet steamroller" -- her suggestions were always interpreted as orders, delivered with such grace that employees did not realize they were being directed until they were already carrying out her wishes. This management style -- firm authority wrapped in courtesy -- was characteristic of her approach to business and to broadcasting philosophy.

### 2.5 Public Service Philosophy

Bullitt's broadcasting philosophy centered on public service. She believed that stations operated on public airwaves and therefore owed the public more than pure entertainment. This philosophy manifested in King Broadcasting's news operations, which consistently earned national recognition, and in decisions like maintaining KING-FM as a commercial classical music station -- a format that served cultural enrichment rather than maximum profit.

### 2.6 Named Seattle's First Citizen

In 1959, Bullitt was named Seattle's First Citizen, recognizing her contributions to the city through broadcasting, philanthropy, and civic leadership. The honor reflected her status as one of the most powerful and respected figures in Pacific Northwest business.

## 3. The KING Empire Expands

### 3.1 KING-FM (1948)

KING-FM (98.1) launched in 1948 as a classical music station. In an era when FM radio was still a curiosity with few listeners, Bullitt's decision to program classical music on FM was both a cultural statement and a business gamble. The station served an audience that commercial radio largely ignored, and it established King Broadcasting's reputation as a broadcaster that valued quality over mass appeal.

### 3.2 KING-TV: The Television Monopoly

Bullitt purchased KRSC-TV in 1949 for $375,000 and renamed it KING-TV. The station became the first television station in the Pacific Northwest. Due to the FCC's freeze on new television licenses (1948--1953), KING-TV held a monopoly on Seattle television for approximately five years. This monopoly provided the revenue base that funded King Broadcasting's expansion across the region.

### 3.3 The Peabody Award

King Broadcasting stations won the first George Foster Peabody Award given for a locally produced television show -- for *Wunda Wunda*, a children's program, in 1957. The Peabody is broadcasting's most prestigious award, and winning it for local production (rather than network programming) validated Bullitt's commitment to original content.

### 3.4 Regional Expansion

King Broadcasting expanded aggressively through the 1950s and beyond:

| 1948 | KING-FM | Seattle | Classical music format |

### 3.5 Diversity Pioneer

KING-TV appointed Jean Enersen as evening news anchor in 1972, making her one of the first women to anchor a major-market evening newscast in the United States. The decision reflected Bullitt's willingness to challenge industry norms and her belief that talent mattered more than convention.

### 3.6 The Sale and Legacy

After Dorothy Bullitt's death in 1989, her daughters Harriet and Priscilla sold King Broadcasting to the Providence Journal Company in 1991 for an estimated $300--400 million. The sale ended the Bullitt family's direct control of the empire Dorothy had built, but her influence on PNW broadcasting -- particularly her public-service philosophy and her commitment to quality local programming -- persisted long after the company changed hands.

## 4. FM Broadcasting Arrives

### 4.1 KGW-FM: The Northwest's First

KGW-FM went on air May 7, 1946 -- the Northwest's first FM station. Initially, the station simulcast KGW's 620 AM programming, offering the same content with FM's superior audio quality but reaching a much smaller audience (few households owned FM receivers in 1946).

### 4.2 Early FM Adoption

In 1946, the FCC approved FM engineering plans for four Portland stations: KGW, KOIN, and two others. FM adoption was slow in the PNW, as it was nationally. The technology offered better sound quality and freedom from static interference, but the chicken-and-egg problem -- few stations broadcasting on FM meant few people bought FM receivers, and few FM receivers in use meant little incentive to broadcast on FM -- kept the technology marginal for years.

### 4.3 KGW-FM's Later History

KGW-FM was sold in 1954, eventually becoming KKRZ (100.3). The sale reflected the Oregonian's calculation that FM was not yet commercially viable as a separate enterprise. This assessment would prove premature -- by the 1960s, FM would begin its ascent to dominance -- but in the mid-1950s, AM remained king.

### 4.4 The FM Spectrum's Significance

The FM band's importance to PNW broadcasting history extends far beyond KGW-FM's pioneering signal. The FM spectrum -- particularly the portion below 92 MHz reserved for noncommercial use -- would eventually enable the community radio movement (KRAB at 107.7), college radio (KCMU at 90.5), and public radio (KUOW, NWPR) that became central to the region's broadcasting identity. The FM dial was where the PNW's most distinctive broadcasting would ultimately live.

### 4.5 Technical Advantages of FM

FM (frequency modulation) offered several technical advantages over AM (amplitude modulation): wider audio bandwidth supporting higher-fidelity music reproduction, inherent noise rejection (static, electrical interference), and the ability to transmit in stereo (after the FCC approved FM stereo in 1961). These advantages made FM the natural home for music-focused programming, and the PNW's strong musical culture would eventually exploit FM's capabilities fully.

## 5. Blanche McBride Virgin: First Woman Station Owner

### 5.1 KMED Medford

Blanche McBride Virgin became the first woman in the United States to own and operate a professional radio station when she took ownership of KMED in Medford, Oregon in 1928. This achievement predates Dorothy Bullitt's entry into broadcasting by nearly two decades.

### 5.2 Southern Oregon Context

Medford, in southern Oregon's Rogue Valley, was far from the broadcasting centers of Seattle and Portland. KMED served a rural community of farmers, ranchers, and timber workers who relied on radio as their primary connection to the wider world. Virgin's ownership of the station placed a woman at the center of this community's information infrastructure at a time when women's roles in business were severely constrained by social convention.

### 5.3 Historical Significance

Virgin's achievement is often overlooked in broadcasting histories that focus on larger markets and more prominent figures. Her story deserves recognition not just as a "first" but as evidence that the PNW's broadcasting culture -- shaped by geographic isolation and a frontier tradition of pragmatism -- was sometimes more open to unconventional leadership than the industry's East Coast establishment.

### 5.4 The Women of PNW Broadcasting

Virgin and Bullitt bookend a tradition of women in PNW broadcasting leadership that extended through the decades. While the industry remained male-dominated nationally, the PNW produced an above-average share of women who owned, managed, and shaped broadcasting institutions. This pattern may reflect the region's progressive political culture, its relative distance from the old-boys networks of East Coast media, or simply the smaller market size that created opportunities unavailable in New York or Los Angeles.

### 5.5 The Southern Oregon Broadcasting Identity

KMED served Medford and the Rogue Valley during an era when southern Oregon was more culturally connected to northern California than to Portland, 270 miles north. The station's programming reflected the agricultural economy of the region -- pear orchards, cattle ranches, and timber operations. Virgin's management of KMED gave the station a distinctive character: responsive to its community, independent in its editorial decisions, and operationally lean. The station survived and evolved through multiple ownership changes, but its founding under Virgin's leadership established a precedent for independent station ownership in markets too small for corporate broadcasting chains.

## 6. Television Transforms Radio

### 6.1 The Existential Threat

Television's arrival in the late 1940s posed an existential threat to radio. The new medium offered everything radio provided -- news, entertainment, music, sports -- plus pictures. Radio audiences declined as television ownership grew. Advertising revenue shifted from radio to television. Industry observers predicted radio's demise.

### 6.2 Radio's Adaptation

Radio survived by adapting. Stations that had depended on network dramatic programming shifted to music formats, which television could not replicate as effectively. News programming became more frequent and more local, serving audiences who wanted quick updates rather than the extended narratives that television news programs offered. Talk programming emerged as a format that exploited radio's intimacy and low production costs.

### 6.3 Format Radio Emerges

The concept of station "formats" -- top 40, country, classical, news/talk -- emerged in the 1950s as radio stations specialized to serve niche audiences rather than trying to be all things to all listeners (as they had been in the network era). This format specialization would intensify over the following decades, eventually producing the highly segmented radio landscape of the present day.

### 6.4 The PNW Transition

PNW radio stations followed the national pattern but with regional variations. The region's strong musical culture made music formats particularly viable. The vast distances between communities made local news programming essential. And the PNW's independent streak -- evident from the earliest days of Kraft's garage transmitter -- meant that some stations resisted national format trends in favor of distinctive local programming.

## 7. Station Ownership and Consolidation

### 7.1 The Era of Proprietors

Through the 1950s, most PNW radio stations were still owned by individuals, families, or local businesses. The regulatory framework limited the number of stations a single entity could own (the FCC's "duopoly" rule prohibited owning two stations of the same service in the same market), and the economics of small-market radio made ownership more a calling than a fortune-building enterprise.

### 7.2 Group Ownership Begins

King Broadcasting's expansion represented the leading edge of group ownership in the PNW. By acquiring stations in Seattle, Portland, Spokane, and Boise, Bullitt created a regional media group that could leverage economies of scale in news gathering, programming, and advertising sales. Other operators began similar, if smaller, consolidations.

### 7.3 The Regulatory Framework

The FCC's ownership rules during this period were designed to preserve media diversity. The agency limited the total number of stations a single entity could own nationwide and prohibited cross-ownership of newspapers and broadcast stations in the same market (a rule that was later relaxed and eventually eliminated). These rules shaped PNW broadcasting by ensuring that most markets had multiple independent voices rather than a single dominant owner.

### 7.4 Seeds of Future Concentration

The ownership patterns established in the 1950s contained the seeds of future concentration. As stations became more valuable and regulatory limits were loosened in later decades, the family-owned and entrepreneur-owned stations of this era would increasingly be sold to corporate groups. King Broadcasting's 1991 sale was the most prominent example, but hundreds of smaller PNW stations followed the same trajectory.

## 8. Programming Evolution

### 8.1 From Live to Recorded

The shift from live programming to recorded content transformed radio production. In the network era, virtually all programming was live -- performers, musicians, and announcers worked in real time. The availability of magnetic tape recording (another wartime technology surplus) allowed stations to pre-record programs, repeat popular content, and build music libraries. This shift reduced costs and expanded programming options but also reduced the spontaneity and intimacy that had characterized early radio.

### 8.2 The Disc Jockey

The emergence of the disc jockey as a radio personality transformed the medium. DJs selected and introduced recorded music, developed on-air personas, and built personal followings. The DJ format was cheaper than live performance programming and allowed stations to fill airtime with popular music interspersed with advertising -- the economic model that sustains commercial music radio to this day.

### 8.3 News and Public Affairs

PNW radio news operations matured during this period. King Broadcasting's news team set the regional standard for broadcast journalism, winning awards and attracting talented reporters. Smaller stations maintained news operations of varying quality, depending on ownership commitment and market size. The Murrow tradition -- rigorous, fact-based reporting delivered with clarity and authority -- influenced PNW broadcast journalism even at stations with no direct connection to Murrow or CBS.

### 8.4 Agricultural and Community Programming

Radio's role as an information lifeline for rural PNW communities continued through this period. Stations broadcast market prices, weather forecasts, agricultural extension information, and community event announcements. For listeners in remote areas -- eastern Oregon ranchers, Palouse wheat farmers, Olympic Peninsula timber workers -- radio remained the primary source of daily information.

## 9. Regional Coverage and the Rural Gap

### 9.1 The Urban-Rural Divide

By 1962, PNW radio coverage was comprehensive in urban areas -- Seattle, Portland, Spokane, Tacoma, and Eugene all had multiple stations serving diverse audiences. Rural coverage remained spotty, however. The region's mountainous terrain blocked signals, and the sparse population of many rural areas made station operation economically marginal.

### 9.2 Translator Stations

The solution to the rural coverage gap was the translator station -- a low-power relay that received a station's signal and rebroadcast it on a different frequency. Translators were cheap to build and operate, required minimal licensing, and could be placed on mountaintops to serve valleys and communities that the main station's signal could not reach. The translator system would become the backbone of public radio coverage in the PNW, eventually growing into a network of dozens of relay stations.

### 9.3 The Foundation for Public Broadcasting

The coverage gaps and economic challenges of rural broadcasting created the conditions that would make public broadcasting essential in the PNW. Commercial stations could not justify the cost of serving small, scattered audiences. Only a publicly funded broadcasting system -- supported by government appropriations and listener donations rather than advertising revenue -- could afford to reach every community. This realization would drive the development of Northwest Public Radio in the coming decades.

## 10. The Era's Legacy

### 10.1 The Bullitt Model

Dorothy Bullitt's King Broadcasting established a model that influenced PNW broadcasting for decades: locally owned, publicly minded, willing to invest in quality programming even when cheaper alternatives were available. The Bullitt model was not unique -- other family-owned broadcasting companies operated with similar values -- but in the PNW, King Broadcasting set the standard that other stations were measured against.

### 10.2 FM's Unfinished Revolution

By 1962, FM radio was still a minority medium -- most listeners tuned to AM, and most stations broadcast on AM frequencies. But the FM band's potential was becoming clear. Its superior audio quality, its reserved noncommercial spectrum, and its availability in areas where the AM band was congested made it the natural home for the programming innovations of the next two decades. Community radio, college radio, and public radio would all build on FM. The FM revolution that KGW-FM began in 1946 would not reach its full expression for another twenty years.

### 10.3 The Consolidation Precedent

King Broadcasting's expansion across four states established a precedent for media consolidation in the PNW. While the company's public-service philosophy earned it goodwill, the pattern of single-entity ownership of multiple stations in multiple markets raised questions that would become more urgent as ownership rules were loosened in later decades. The 1996 Telecommunications Act would dramatically accelerate consolidation nationwide; the PNW's experience with King Broadcasting provided an early case study in both the benefits and risks of concentrated media ownership.

### 10.4 Women's Leadership Thread

The era's most significant PNW broadcasting figures were women: Blanche McBride Virgin, who pioneered station ownership in 1928, and Dorothy Bullitt, who built the region's dominant media company. Their leadership -- achieved in an industry and an era that systematically excluded women from power -- demonstrated that the PNW's broadcasting culture could be more progressive than the national norm. This thread would continue through Jean Enersen's appointment as evening news anchor in 1972 and through the women who would later lead community and public radio stations across the region.

### 10.5 The Television Question

Television did not kill radio in the PNW, but it permanently changed radio's role. The medium that had been the center of home entertainment -- families gathered around the radio for evening programming -- became a companion medium, playing in the background during other activities. This shift freed radio from the obligation to provide dramatic programming, musical variety shows, and other forms that television did better, and pushed it toward the specialized formats -- music, news, talk -- that would define its future. The PNW's subsequent radio innovations -- community freeform, college alternative, public news -- were all products of this post-television reinvention.

### 10.6 The Spokane Market

Spokane's broadcasting landscape matured significantly during this era. KHQ (now a television station) and KREM (acquired by King Broadcasting in 1957) served a market that was geographically isolated from both Seattle and Portland. Spokane's broadcasters served eastern Washington, northern Idaho, and parts of Montana -- a territory larger than many eastern states but with a fraction of the population. The Spokane market demonstrated that PNW broadcasting was not a two-city story; the region's geographic extent required broadcasting infrastructure in multiple urban centers, each serving its own hinterland.

### 10.7 The Car Radio Revolution

The spread of car radios in the 1950s transformed radio listening habits. Radio was no longer confined to the living room; it traveled with the listener. For PNW residents -- who drove long distances between communities, through mountain passes, and along coastal highways -- car radio made the medium portable and personal. The car radio's rise also shifted programming: stations that had been evening-oriented (when families gathered at home) began focusing on morning and afternoon drive times, creating the daypart programming structure that commercial radio uses to this day.

### 10.8 The Classical Music Tradition

KING-FM's 1948 launch established a classical music broadcasting tradition in the PNW that persists into the present. Dorothy Bullitt's decision to dedicate an FM station to classical music was not commercially motivated -- classical music stations have always attracted small audiences relative to popular music formats. The decision reflected a belief that broadcasting should serve cultural enrichment, not just entertainment and profit. This philosophy -- that some programming is worth sustaining even without commercial justification -- would later inform community radio, public radio, and KEXP's commitment to music that defies commercial logic.

### 10.9 The Boise Extension

King Broadcasting's 1980 acquisition of KTVB in Boise extended the PNW broadcasting footprint into Idaho. This expansion demonstrated that the PNW broadcasting region -- defined not by state boundaries but by cultural and economic connections -- extended east of the Cascades and across the Snake River plain. Idaho's broadcasting needs resembled eastern Washington's: vast distances, scattered communities, reliance on translator stations for extended coverage. The Boise acquisition gave King Broadcasting a presence in every major PNW market.

### 10.10 Preparing for Community Radio

By 1962, the conditions were in place for a new kind of broadcasting. FM frequencies were available. The noncommercial spectrum had been reserved. Television had freed radio from its obligation to be the primary entertainment medium. And a generation of listeners had grown up with radio and understood its potential for community service, cultural enrichment, and political engagement. The empire-building era had created the infrastructure; the community radio era would challenge the assumptions that infrastructure embodied.

### 10.11 The Regulatory Environment

The FCC during this era balanced promotion of broadcasting growth with protection of media diversity. Rules limiting station ownership, requiring local service obligations, and reserving spectrum for noncommercial use shaped the PNW broadcasting landscape in ways that persisted for decades. The regulatory decisions of the 1950s and early 1960s -- particularly the reservation of FM spectrum for educational and noncommercial use -- created the regulatory space that community radio, college radio, and public radio would later occupy. Without these regulations, the entire FM dial might have been consumed by commercial interests, and the distinctive PNW broadcasting culture that emerged from the noncommercial spectrum would never have developed. The regulatory foresight of reserving noncommercial spectrum stands as one of the most consequential decisions in American broadcasting history.
