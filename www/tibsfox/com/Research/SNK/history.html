Python was born over a Christmas break in December 1989 at Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica (CWI) in Amsterdam — the same research institute where Edsger Dijkstra had famously refined structured programming. Guido van Rossum, bored and looking for “a hobby programming project that would keep me occupied,” started writing an interpreter for a new scripting language descended from ABC. He named it after Monty Python’s Flying Circus.

Guido van Rossum — The Reluctant Dictator

Born 1956, Netherlands. Master’s in mathematics and computer science from University of Amsterdam, 1982. Joined CWI in Amsterdam, where he worked on the ABC language — a teaching language by Lambert Meertens and Leo Geurts. ABC left a deep imprint: Guido admired its clean syntax, indentation over braces, high-level data types, and respect for the reader. He also saw its weaknesses: monolithic, hard to extend, couldn’t talk to Unix.

December 1989. CWI’s offices closed for Christmas. Guido wanted a hobby project. He started writing Python — ABC’s readability with C’s pragmatism. He needed a name: short, unique, slightly mysterious. He was a fan of Monty Python’s Flying Circus. The language is named after the comedy show, not the reptile.

BDFL Era

As Python grew through the 1990s, Guido became the final arbiter of every contentious design question. The community gave him a half-joking, half-affectionate title: Benevolent Dictator For Life (BDFL). The “benevolent” mattered. He listened exhaustively on mailing list threads, but when consensus failed, he made the call and the community moved on. The BDFL model produced an unusually coherent language because one taste was filtering every PEP.

The July 2018 Abdication

In July 2018, after a long and bruising mailing list battle over PEP 572 (the “walrus operator” :=), Guido stunned the community by stepping down as BDFL. His announcement to python-committers said, in part, that he was “tired” and “no longer want[ed] to fight.” He was not leaving Python; he simply refused to be its dictator anymore. He told the core developers to figure out their own governance — and he meant it.

The python-dev community responded with PEP 8016 and adopted a Steering Council: five elected core developers, reelected each year, who would collectively make the final calls Guido used to make alone. The first council: Barry Warsaw, Brett Cannon, Carol Willing, Guido himself (yes, elected), and Nick Coghlan. Python survived the loss of its dictator. That is a rare thing in language communities.

The Python 2/3 Schism — The Great Migration

Python 2 was a roaring success through the 2000s. It was also accumulating warts: a confused string-vs-bytes model, integer division that surprised every newcomer (1/2 == 0), print as a magical statement, classic vs. new-style classes, and a standard library full of inconsistent naming. Guido decided the warts could not be fixed in place. They needed a hard cut.

The 2-to-3 migration was announced as a multi-year project. What no one anticipated was that it would take over a decade. The Python ecosystem was effectively split. Libraries had to support both simultaneously via six, future, and other shims. Major projects (NumPy, Django, Twisted, Pillow) gradually committed to drop Python 2. The python3statement.org campaign organized projects to set firm 2.x sunset dates. The Python 2 EOL date was extended again and again as the ecosystem dragged its feet. January 1, 2020 finally closed the book.

The Zen of Python — PEP 20

In June 1999, Tim Peters posted 19 aphorisms summarizing Python’s design philosophy to python-list. They were collected as PEP 20 and became a kind of constitution. Type import this in any Python interpreter to read them.

The most famous line is the 13th: “There should be one— and preferably only one —obvious way to do it.” Pythonistas abbreviate it TSBOWTDI. It is a deliberate, public rebuke to Perl’s beloved TMTOWTDI. Where Perl celebrated expressive freedom and the linguist’s joy of synonyms, Python staked its identity on the opposite claim: that consistency between programmers is more valuable than personal style.

The “unless you’re Dutch” line (14) is a wink at Guido. The Zen leaves a hidden 20th slot in PEP 20 itself, intentionally empty — reserved for Guido. It is a piece of deadpan community folklore.

The PEP Process

Python’s governance is built on PEPs — Python Enhancement Proposals. PEP 1 (Barry Warsaw, Jeremy Hylton, David Goodger, August 2000) is modeled on the IETF’s RFC process. If you want to change the language, write a PEP. Describe motivation, rationale, specification, backwards compatibility, reference implementation, and rejected alternatives. Post it. Argue about it. The Steering Council will pronounce.

The PEP process is deliberately slow. Most PEPs are rejected, deferred, or quietly forgotten. The slowness is the feature. Python rarely regrets a feature it has shipped, because the friction filters out most bad ideas before they reach master.

The Python Software Foundation

Incorporated 2001 as a 501(c)(3) non-profit in Delaware. Mission: “to promote, protect, and advance the Python programming language, and to support and facilitate the growth of a diverse and international community of Python programmers.”

What the PSF does:

  • Holds the copyright to CPython under the PSF License (permissive, GPL-compatible)
  • Runs PyCon US, the largest annual Python conference (2003–present, draws thousands)
  • Sponsors regional PyCons worldwide: EuroPython, PyCon AU, PyCon India, PyCon JP, PyCon Africa
  • Issues grants for events, education, and infrastructure, especially in underrepresented regions
  • Funds the Packaging Working Group and tools like pip, PyPI, and Warehouse
  • Funded the first Developer-in-Residence position (Łukasz Langa, 2021, sponsored by Google)
  • Maintains the Code of Conduct governing PSF events and community spaces

Sponsors: Google, Microsoft, Meta, Bloomberg, JetBrains, AWS, NVIDIA, Intel, Capital One. Bloomberg in particular has been a long-time visible patron, contributing developer time as well as money.
