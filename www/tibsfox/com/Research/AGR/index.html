The same rain shadow that makes eastern Washington a desert made it the most productive irrigated farmland in America — all it needed was water. One dam turned sagebrush into a breadbasket. Twenty years of science produced one perfect apple. Fifteen thousand years of volcanic geology determine what wine you drink tonight. The codling moth has been eating apples longer than humans have been growing them, and wildfire smoke from the forests that make the PNW beautiful is killing the wines that make it famous. Agriculture is where all the PNW’s tensions converge.

Land & Crop Science

The Land That Feeds

Overview

Washington: #1 apples, #1 hops, top 5 cherries and potatoes. Oregon: Willamette Valley wine, hazelnuts, grass seed. The Columbia Basin as irrigated agricultural empire. Billions annually, hundreds of crops.

Cosmic Crisp

Science

WSU Tree Fruit Research Center. Enterprise x Honeycrisp, 20 years, $10M launch. High-density orchards at 3,000+ trees per acre. Genomic-assisted breeding. Rootstock revolution.

Wine & Water

Terroir

Wine

Oregon Willamette Valley Pinot Noir on volcanic Jory soils. Washington’s 21 AVAs across the Columbia Basin. Volcanic basalt from a flood 15,000 years ago determines what wine you drink tonight.

Grand Coulee

Water

Grand Coulee Dam turned sagebrush into farmland. 671,000 irrigated acres. The Odessa Aquifer running dry. Water rights, snowpack decline, and the infrastructure climate change threatens.

Threats & Resilience

Codling Moth

Pest Science

The apple’s worst enemy for 200 years. Mating disruption, sterile insect technique, degree-day models. Fire blight resistance. Apple maggot quarantine zones.

When the Forest Burns, the Wine Dies

Smoke Taint

Wildfire smoke absorbed by grape skins. The 2020 Oregon vintage devastated. Thiophenol discovery at OSU. Vine gene expression changes within 2 hours. $7.65M USDA research program.

Orchards and Vineyards as One System

Deep Research

Apples and wine grapes as interdependent systems sharing water, soil, labor, and converging threats. Regenerative agriculture, soil architecture, the varietal transition, climate adaptation, indigenous food sovereignty, and the Amiga Principle in agricultural systems.
