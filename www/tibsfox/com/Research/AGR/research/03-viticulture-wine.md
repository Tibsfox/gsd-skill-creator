## 1. Terroir Defined

Terroir -- the French concept that a wine expresses the total environment in which its grapes were grown -- is not mysticism. It is geology, climate, soil biology, hydrology, and microclimate interacting at the scale of a single vineyard. In the PNW, terroir is unusually legible because the geological history is both dramatic and recent. The soils are young. The volcanoes are still active. The floods that shaped the landscape occurred only 15,000 years ago -- yesterday in geological time [1][2].

What makes the PNW wine story remarkable is that the same geological events created two radically different wine regions within 200 miles of each other. Oregon's Willamette Valley is a cool-climate maritime region defined by volcanic and marine sedimentary soils. Washington's Columbia Valley is a warm-climate continental region defined by basalt, loess, and catastrophic flood deposits. Both are world-class. Both owe their character to the Cascade Range [1][2][3].

## 2. Willamette Valley: The Pinot Noir Capital

The Willamette Valley's suitability for Pinot Noir was first recognized by David Lett, who founded The Eyrie Vineyards in 1966 after concluding that the valley's climate and soils were analogous to Burgundy, France -- the spiritual home of Pinot Noir. Lett was considered eccentric at best. Oregon's wine industry at that time consisted of a handful of fruit wine producers [2][4].

Lett's vindication came in 1979-1980 when his 1975 South Block Reserve Pinot Noir placed in the top five at Gault-Millau's Wine Olympiad in Paris, beating several established Burgundies. The result was considered a fluke. A rematch was organized. Eyrie placed second -- by two-tenths of a point. Robert Drouhin of Maison Joseph Drouhin was so impressed that he purchased land in the Willamette Valley and established Domaine Drouhin Oregon in 1987 [2][4].

Today the Willamette Valley has over 700 wineries, 40,000+ acres of vineyard, and 11 nested AVAs (American Viticultural Areas) including the Dundee Hills, Eola-Amity Hills, McMinnville, Yamhill-Carlton, Ribbon Ridge, Chehalem Mountains, Laurelwood District, Tualatin Hills, Van Duzer Corridor, Lower Long Tom, and Mount Pisgah [2][5].

## 3. Three Soils, Three Wines

The Willamette Valley's extraordinary complexity arises from three distinct soil types that produce detectably different wines from the same grape variety [1][2][6]:

### Volcanic Jory Soils

Oregon's official state soil (designated 2011), Jory is a red, iron-rich clay-loam formed from basaltic lava that flowed west from eastern Washington 15-17 million years ago during the Columbia River Basalt Group eruptions. Found above 300 feet elevation -- safely above the Missoula Flood deposits -- Jory soils are 4-6 feet deep with exceptional water-holding capacity [1][6].

Pinot Noir from Jory soils characteristically shows bright red cherry fruit, spice notes (cinnamon, clove, allspice), high acidity, and soft tannins. The Dundee Hills and parts of the Eola-Amity Hills have the highest concentration of Jory soils [1][6].

David Lett's son Jason noted that his father "first identified the climate for Pinot Noir, and then the soil" -- selecting Jory specifically for its low fertility and superior water retention, which force vines to develop deep root systems and produce concentrated fruit [6].

### Marine Sedimentary Willakenzie Soils

The oldest soils in the valley, Willakenzie originated as ocean floor sediment that was uplifted when the Pacific and North American tectonic plates collided, forming the Coast Range. These coarse-grained, well-drained silty-clay loams over sandstone produce wines with darker fruit profiles -- black cherry, chocolate, cola -- with more structured tannins [1][6].

The Yamhill-Carlton AVA has the highest concentration of Willakenzie soils. Wines from this area tend to be broader and more full-bodied than their Jory-grown counterparts [1][6].

### Loess/Laurelwood Soils

The youngest of the three primary soil types, loess was deposited as windblown silt between 50,000 and 1 million years ago during ice age glacial periods. Found primarily on northeast-facing hills in the Chehalem Mountains AVA, Laurelwood soils combine a basalt base with a loess top layer [1][6].

Wines from Laurelwood soils show distinctive earthiness, white pepper notes, and elevated acidity -- characteristics that distinguish them from both the bright fruit of Jory and the dark richness of Willakenzie [1][6].

## 4. The Missoula Floods

Between 15,500 and 12,700 years ago, Glacial Lake Missoula -- an enormous ice-dammed lake in western Montana -- repeatedly breached its ice dam, sending catastrophic floods across eastern Washington and into the Willamette Valley. Peak flows are estimated at 10 million cubic meters per second. There were approximately 36 flood events [7][8].

In the Willamette Valley, the floods deposited fertile silt across the valley floor to depths of 300 feet. This alluvial deposit created the rich agricultural bottomland that today produces hazelnuts, berries, grass seed, and Christmas trees -- but is generally too vigorous (too nutrient-rich) for premium wine grapes, which favor the poorer hillside soils above the flood line [7][8].

The flood line is visible in the landscape. Below 300 feet: flat, fertile farmland with deep alluvial soils. Above 300 feet: hillside vineyards on ancient volcanic or marine sedimentary soils. The best Pinot Noir comes from above the flood. The hazelnuts grow below it. The same cataclysmic event determined the agricultural geography of the entire valley [7][8].

In eastern Washington, the floods scoured the channeled scablands -- a landscape of exposed basalt and dry coulees that now host some of Washington's most distinctive vineyard sites. The flood deposits of gravel, sand, and silt created the soil profiles that define AVAs like Ancient Lakes and The Rocks District of Milton-Freewater [7][8].

## 5. Washington: 21 AVAs and Counting

Washington's wine geography is almost entirely east of the Cascades in the Columbia River Basin's rain shadow. The state's 21 AVAs form a hierarchical structure [3][9][10]:

### The Columbia Valley AVA

The parent AVA encompasses 11 million acres and contains most other Washington AVAs. It extends into a small portion of Oregon. The defining characteristics: volcanic basalt bedrock from the Columbia River Basalt Group, loess topsoil from ice age windstorms, and irrigation from the Columbia River and its tributaries. Annual rainfall is 6-12 inches -- viticulture is impossible without irrigation [3][9].

### Sub-AVA Architecture

Within the Columbia Valley, key sub-appellations include:

- **Yakima Valley:** The first Washington AVA (1983), containing Rattlesnake Hills, Snipes Mountain, Red Mountain, Goose Gap, and Candy Mountain sub-AVAs [9].
- **Walla Walla Valley:** Straddling the Oregon border, known for premium Cabernet Sauvignon and Syrah. Includes The Rocks District of Milton-Freewater, defined by cobblestone soils [9][10].
- **Horse Heaven Hills:** Source of approximately 25% of Washington's total wine grape production [9].
- **Wahluke Slope:** Producing approximately 20% of state wine grapes, one of the warmest and driest regions [9].
- **Ancient Lakes:** Dramatic landscape above the Columbia River with glacial flood deposits [9].
- **Lake Chelan:** Washington's northernmost AVA, with a lake-moderated microclimate [9].

The **Columbia Gorge AVA** straddles the Columbia River in both Oregon and Washington, with a dramatic climate gradient from 36 inches of annual rainfall in the west to 10 inches in the east. The **Puget Sound AVA** is Washington's only AVA west of the Cascades. A petition for a **Mount St. Helens AVA** has been submitted to the TTB [9][10].

## 6. Key Washington AVAs

### Red Mountain

The smallest Washington AVA at just 4,040 acres, Red Mountain is also among the warmest. The south-facing slope receives intense solar radiation, producing some of Washington's most concentrated and age-worthy Cabernet Sauvignon. Wines from Red Mountain are typically powerful, tannic, and deeply colored [9][10].

### Walla Walla Valley

Walla Walla transformed from a small agricultural town into one of the Pacific Northwest's most prestigious wine destinations. The AVA's soils range from deep loess to basalt-derived clay to the legendary cobblestones of The Rocks District, where fist-sized river stones from ancient Walla Walla River channels absorb solar heat and radiate it back to the vines at night [9][10].

### Yakima Valley

The oldest and most established AVA in Washington, Yakima Valley is the workhorse of the state's wine industry. Its sub-AVAs span a range of elevations and exposures, producing everything from value-oriented Riesling and Chardonnay to premium Cabernet Sauvignon from the warmer sub-regions [9].

## 7. The Climate Advantage

Washington vineyards benefit from climatic conditions that California cannot match [3][9]:

- **Sunlight duration:** Up to 17 hours of summer sunlight per day during peak growing season -- approximately one hour more than California's prime growing regions. More photosynthesis means more flavor development [3][9].
- **Diurnal temperature variation:** Summer days can reach 95-100 degrees Fahrenheit while nights drop to 50-55 degrees. This 40-45 degree swing preserves natural acidity (which breaks down at high temperatures) while building sugar and phenolic compounds during the hot days [3][9].
- **Low humidity:** East of the Cascades, humidity is chronically low. This reduces fungal disease pressure significantly compared to maritime climates, reducing the need for fungicide applications [3][9].
- **Phylloxera absence:** The devastating grape pest phylloxera, which destroyed European vineyards in the 19th century and requires grafting on resistant rootstocks, is largely absent from eastern Washington's sandy soils. Many Washington vineyards are planted on their own roots [3][9].

## 8. Climate Change: Opportunity and Threat

Climate scientist Greg Jones assessed that Oregon has shifted from the cooler limits of Pinot Noir suitability in the 1960s-70s to the current "Goldilocks" sweet spot for the variety. Warmer average temperatures have extended the growing season, improved ripening consistency, and enabled production of fuller-bodied wines than were possible 50 years ago [11].

But the same warming trend poses existential risks:

- **Wildfire smoke:** Module 06 covers this in detail. Smoke taint has devastated recent vintages [12].
- **Water stress:** Earlier snowmelt reduces late-season water availability for irrigation [13].
- **Phenological disruption:** Warmer springs cause earlier bud break, increasing vulnerability to late frost events [11].
- **Heat events:** Extreme heat domes (like the 2021 Pacific Northwest event, which reached 116 degrees Fahrenheit in parts of Washington) can literally cook grapes on the vine, causing sunburn and dehydration [11].

The response is adaptive: growers are experimenting with heat-tolerant varieties (Tempranillo, Mourvedre, Grenache), adjusting canopy management to provide more shade, and investing in irrigation infrastructure to buffer against drought [11].

## 9. Sustainability and Certification

The PNW has developed the most comprehensive regional sustainability certification infrastructure in US agriculture [5][14]:

- **LIVE (Low Input Viticulture and Enology):** Established 1995 in Oregon, expanded to Washington and Idaho. Covers 27,000+ vineyard acres. Takes a whole-farm and whole-winery approach. Salmon-Safe requirements are embedded, connecting vineyard sustainability to Pacific salmon habitat protection [5][14].
- **Sustainable WA:** The newest certification, built specifically by and for the Washington wine industry. Rooted in the Vinewise and Winerywise educational programs [14].
- **Oregon sustainability credentials:** 47% of growers LIVE-certified, 35-40% of planted acreage in organic or sustainable certification, 52% of all US Demeter-certified biodynamic wineries [5].
- **Salmon-Safe:** A certification that evaluates agricultural practices for their impact on Pacific salmon habitat -- uniquely PNW [5][14].

## 10. The Economics of PNW Wine

| Metric | Oregon | Washington |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Wineries | 900+ | 1,000+ |
| Vineyard acres | 40,000+ | 60,000+ |
| Total economic impact | ~$6.6B | ~$8B |
| Annual winery visits | 1.8 million | 3+ million |
| Enotourism revenue | $207.5M | est. $300M+ |
| Median winery size | <5,000 cases | <5,000 cases |

*Sources: Oregon Wine Board, Washington State Wine Commission [5][9]*

Both states are dominated by small producers: 75% of Oregon wineries and 95% of Washington wineries make fewer than 5,000 cases annually. The industry is bifurcated: large operations (Ste. Michelle Wine Estates, acquired by Sycamore Partners for $1.2 billion in 2021) pursue economies of scale while small producers depend on direct-to-consumer sales and enotourism [5][9][15].
