## 1. The Apple's Worst Enemy

The codling moth (*Cydia pomonella*) has been the principal pest of apple in North America for over 200 years, since its introduction from Europe with the first apple trees brought by colonists. It is arguably the most economically important insect pest in the history of temperate fruit production, responsible for billions of dollars in crop losses and pest management expenditures worldwide [1][2].

In Washington State alone, the apple industry spends an estimated $100 million annually on codling moth management. Without control, infestation rates can exceed 90% of fruit within two to three seasons, rendering entire orchards commercially unviable. The pest's evolutionary relationship with apple is ancient -- *Cydia pomonella* co-evolved with wild apple species (*Malus sieversii*) in Central Asia, the center of origin for the domesticated apple [1][2][3].

## 2. Biology of Cydia pomonella

Understanding the codling moth's biology is essential to managing it. In the PNW, there are typically two generations per year, each corresponding to a damage period [1][2]:

### First Generation (spring-summer)

- **Emergence:** Overwintering pupae (in cocoons under bark, in soil, or in orchard debris) produce adult moths in spring, timed with warming temperatures
- **Mating and oviposition:** Female moths lay eggs on leaves and fruit surfaces, typically one egg per site
- **Larval entry:** Newly hatched larvae (neonates) crawl to fruit and bore through the calyx (bottom of the apple) or through contact points between adjacent fruit
- **Feeding:** Larvae tunnel to the core, feeding on seeds for 3-5 weeks
- **Exit and pupation:** Mature larvae exit the fruit, spin cocoons, and pupate for the second generation

### Second Generation (mid-summer to fall)

- **Emergence:** Second-generation adults appear in mid to late summer
- **Damage:** Second-generation larvae attack maturing fruit, with damage appearing at harvest
- **Overwintering:** Some second-generation larvae enter diapause (dormancy) and overwinter as mature larvae in cocoons [1][2]

The characteristic damage is a small entry hole at the calyx end, filled with brown frass (excrement), with a tunnel leading to the seed cavity. A single infested apple is unmarketable. In processing fruit (juice, sauce), some infestation is tolerable, but fresh-market apples require near-zero codling moth damage [1][2].

## 3. Degree-Day Models

Codling moth development is temperature-dependent, not calendar-dependent. A moth does not emerge on a specific date; it emerges when sufficient heat has accumulated. This makes degree-day models -- which track cumulative heat above a base temperature -- the foundation of codling moth IPM [1][4]:

### How It Works

1. **Biofix:** The start of the degree-day clock is set by pheromone trap catches. Traps baited with synthetic female pheromone are placed in the upper canopy. The first consistent capture of multiple males establishes the biofix date [1][4].

2. **Accumulation:** Degree-days are calculated as (daily maximum temperature + daily minimum temperature) / 2, minus the base temperature (50 degrees Fahrenheit for codling moth). Only positive values count [1][4].

3. **Critical thresholds:**

| Degree-Days (from biofix) | Event | Management Action |
|---------------------------|-------|-------------------|
| 100 DD | First egg laying | Begin monitoring |
| 250 DD | First egg hatch | Apply first larvicide if thresholds exceeded |
| 500 DD | Peak first-generation hatch | Critical spray timing |
| 1060 DD | First second-generation adults | Reset monitoring |
| 1200 DD | Second-generation egg hatch | Apply second larvicide if needed |

*Sources: PNW Pest Management Handbooks, Brunner and Hoyt model [1][4].*

The precision of this system is remarkable. A spray applied at 250 DD will catch neonates before they enter the fruit. A spray applied at 150 DD is too early (eggs haven't hatched). A spray at 300 DD is too late (larvae are already inside). The window is narrow, and the penalty for missing it is significant [1][4].

Two models are in use: the Brunner and Hoyt (1987) model for PNW locations south of 46 degrees N, and the "no-biofix" model (Jones et al. 2008) for northern locations where trap catches are less reliable [4].

## 4. Mating Disruption

Mating disruption has become the foundation of codling moth IPM, fundamentally changing how orchards are managed [1][5]:

### The Principle

Synthetic pheromone dispensers are deployed throughout the orchard at rates of 200-400 per acre. The dispensers release the same chemical compound that female codling moths use to attract males for mating. The result: the entire orchard is saturated with pheromone, and male moths cannot locate individual females. Mating frequency drops dramatically, and the next generation's population crashes [1][5].

### Implementation

- **Timing:** Dispensers are deployed before the first adult emergence (before biofix)
- **Coverage:** Must be applied to the entire orchard; edge effects reduce efficacy where treated orchards border untreated areas
- **Duration:** Modern dispensers last 120-180 days, covering the entire growing season
- **Supplementation:** Mating disruption alone rarely achieves the 99%+ control required for fresh-market fruit. Targeted larvicide applications at specific degree-day thresholds supplement the pheromone program [1][5]

### Economics

Mating disruption costs approximately $100-200 per acre per season for dispensers alone. This is competitive with calendar-based spray programs (which can cost $300-500 per acre for materials alone), and the reduced spray applications provide environmental benefits: fewer non-target insect kills, reduced pesticide residues on fruit, and lower worker exposure [1][5].

## 5. Sterile Insect Technique

The Okanogan-Similkameen Sterile Insect Release (OKSIR) program in British Columbia (adjacent to Washington's Okanogan Valley) has operated the world's largest codling moth SIT program since 1994 [6][7]:

### The Method

1. Codling moths are mass-reared in a production facility
2. Pupae are irradiated with a calibrated dose of gamma radiation -- enough to sterilize the insects but not enough to impair their ability to fly, locate mates, and copulate
3. Sterile males are released by air (small aircraft) or ground application at rates of 1,000-3,000 per acre, per week, throughout the mating season
4. Sterile males mate with wild females, who lay infertile eggs. The wild population declines over successive generations [6][7]

### Results

The OKSIR program has reduced codling moth populations in the treatment zone by over 90% compared to pre-program levels. Fruit damage rates in participating orchards have declined from 5-15% to less than 0.2% in many areas [6][7].

Washington State has explored similar SIT programs for its apple-growing regions, though the scale of the Washington industry (10x larger than BC's Okanogan region) presents logistical challenges for mass rearing and release [7].

## 6. Apple Maggot: The Quarantine Pest

The apple maggot (*Rhagoletis pomonella*) is a native North American pest originally hosted by hawthorn (*Crataegus* species). It shifted to domestic apple in the eastern US approximately 150 years ago and was first confirmed in the Northwest in Oregon in 1979 [8][9].

### The Quarantine

Washington State maintains an Apple Maggot Quarantine Area (WAC 16-470-100) that restricts movement of host fruit from infested counties. As of 2024, 22 of Washington's 39 counties have confirmed apple maggot presence, mostly in western Washington and along the Oregon border [8][9].

The quarantine is critical because:

- **Export markets** (especially Japan, Taiwan, South Korea) maintain zero-tolerance for apple maggot infestation
- **Eastern Washington** (where the major apple industry is located) remains largely free of the pest
- **Fruit packed in quarantine counties** cannot be shipped to non-quarantine packinghouses without certified inspection [8][9]

The Washington State Department of Agriculture operates an extensive trapping program using yellow sticky boards baited with ammonium carbonate (a food attractant) to detect new infestations and monitor spread. The trapping network is dense in buffer zones between quarantine and non-quarantine areas [8][9].

### Biology

Unlike codling moth, which bores to the core, apple maggot larvae feed throughout the flesh of the fruit, leaving brown tunnels visible when the apple is cut open. Infested fruit often appears dimpled or pitted on the surface. Larvae pupate in soil and can overwinter for 1-3 years before emerging as adults, making eradication extremely difficult once established [8][9].

## 7. Fire Blight: Bacterial Devastation

Fire blight, caused by the bacterium *Erwinia amylovora*, is the most destructive bacterial disease of apple and pear worldwide. The disease can kill individual branches, entire trees, or -- in severe outbreaks -- devastate whole orchard blocks within a single growing season [10][11].

### Symptoms and Spread

The bacterium enters through blossoms during warm, wet spring weather. Infected blossoms turn brown and die. The infection moves down the branch, killing tissue as it advances. Infected branches display a characteristic "shepherd's crook" -- the terminal growth curls downward like a hook. In severe cases, the bacteria reach the rootstock and the tree dies [10][11].

Transmission occurs through:

- **Rain splash** from infected tissue to healthy blossoms
- **Pollinating insects** carrying bacteria between flowers
- **Pruning tools** spreading the pathogen from infected to healthy wood
- **Wind-driven rain** during bloom

### The Geneva Rootstock Solution

The most dramatic fire blight story in PNW agriculture involves rootstock resistance. A Washington grower planted two orchards of 150,000 trees each -- one on the standard M.9 rootstock and one on the fire-blight-resistant Geneva G.41 rootstock. During the 2018 fire blight epidemic in Washington [11][12]:

- **G.41 orchard:** Zero trees lost
- **M.9 orchard:** Approximately 20% of trees (30,000) died
- **Estimated savings from G.41:** $20+ million

This single field-scale demonstration accelerated adoption of Geneva-series rootstocks across the Washington apple industry. The Geneva breeding program at Cornell University and USDA-ARS has been developing fire-blight-resistant rootstocks since the 1960s, but adoption was slow until field-scale losses proved the economic case [11][12].

## 8. Integrated Pest Management

IPM in PNW apple production is a systems approach that layers multiple control methods [1][2][5]:

1. **Cultural controls:** Orchard sanitation (removing infected wood and dropped fruit), proper irrigation management, variety selection
2. **Biological controls:** Conservation of natural predators (parasitoid wasps, predatory mites, lacewings, birds)
3. **Mating disruption:** Pheromone-based population suppression (foundation layer)
4. **Monitoring:** Degree-day models, pheromone traps, visual inspections
5. **Targeted chemical controls:** Applied only when monitoring indicates threshold exceedance, timed to degree-day precision
6. **Sterile insect technique:** Area-wide population suppression

The philosophy is explicitly hierarchical: cultural and biological controls first, monitoring to determine if additional intervention is needed, and chemical controls only when thresholds are exceeded. This approach has reduced per-acre pesticide use in Washington apple orchards by 50-80% compared to calendar-based spray programs used in the 1970s-1980s [1][2][5].

## 9. The Cost of Protection

The economic scale of pest management in PNW agriculture is substantial [1][3]:

| Category | Estimated Annual Cost |
|----------|---------------------|
| Codling moth management (WA) | $100M+ |
| Fire blight losses and prevention | $50-100M in outbreak years |
| Apple maggot monitoring/quarantine | $5-10M (state + grower costs) |
| Total pest/disease management (WA apples) | $200-300M |

*Sources: WSU Extension, WSDA, industry estimates [1][3].*

These costs are embedded in the price of every apple. When you buy a Cosmic Crisp at the grocery store, roughly $0.10-0.15 of the retail price represents pest management costs. The alternative -- unmanaged orchards -- would produce fruit with 50-90% damage rates, rendering the industry non-viable [1][3].

The WSU Codling Moth Task Force, established in 2020, coordinates research, extension, and industry response to evolving pest management challenges, including insecticide resistance monitoring and evaluation of new control technologies [4][5].
