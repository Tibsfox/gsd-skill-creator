## 1. The Two PNWs

The Cascade Range splits the Pacific Northwest into two radically different agricultural worlds. West of the Cascades: 60-100 inches of annual rainfall, temperate maritime climate, mild winters, cool summers. East of the Cascades: 6-15 inches of rainfall, continental climate, hot summers, cold winters, 300+ days of sunshine. This single geological fact -- a volcanic mountain range running north-south through the heart of the region -- determines what grows where, how it grows, and what it costs to grow it [1][2].

West-side agriculture is rainfed and diversified: berries, hazelnuts, nursery stock, grass seed, vegetables, wine grapes (primarily Pinot Noir in the Willamette Valley). The maritime climate limits heat accumulation but provides abundant moisture. East-side agriculture is irrigated and intensive: apples, cherries, hops, wine grapes (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah), potatoes, wheat, onions, corn. The continental climate provides the sunshine and heat that crops need, but rain must be replaced by engineered water systems [1][2][3].

The interaction between these two zones is what makes PNW agriculture extraordinary. Neither side alone would rank among the world's premier agricultural regions. Together, they produce an agricultural diversity matched by few places on earth.

## 2. Washington: The Apple State

Washington is the number one apple-producing state in the United States, growing approximately 70% of all US apples. The industry is centered in the Wenatchee and Yakima valleys of central Washington, where volcanic soils, irrigation from the Columbia Basin Project, long summer days, and the frost-free period between the Cascade rain shadow and the continental cold create ideal growing conditions [1][4].

But Washington's agricultural dominance extends far beyond apples:

- **Hops:** Over 70% of US production, centered in the Yakima Valley. Washington hops supply the craft beer revolution nationwide [1].
- **Sweet cherries:** Washington leads all states, with the Yakima and Wenatchee valleys as primary production zones [1].
- **Pears:** Consistently among the top two states, alongside Oregon [1][4].
- **Potatoes:** The Columbia Basin is one of the world's most productive potato-growing regions, rivaling Idaho's Snake River Plain [1].
- **Wheat:** The Palouse region of eastern Washington and northern Idaho produces some of the highest-quality soft white wheat in the world, exported primarily to Asian markets [1][5].
- **Wine grapes:** Washington is the second-largest wine-producing state after California, with 21 American Viticultural Areas and over 1,000 wineries [6].
- **Blueberries, raspberries, and cranberries:** Washington ranks in the top five nationally for all three berry categories [1].

The state's agricultural exports exceeded $7 billion in 2023, with apples alone accounting for over $2 billion in farm gate value [1][4].

## 3. Oregon: Valley and Range

Oregon's agricultural identity is defined by the Willamette Valley -- a 150-mile-long, 20-40 mile wide trough between the Coast Range and the Cascades that produces an astonishing range of crops in one of the most fertile agricultural landscapes in the Western Hemisphere [3][7].

**Oregon leads the world** in production of:

- **Hazelnuts (filberts):** 99% of US production, almost entirely in the Willamette Valley. Oregon is the world's third-largest hazelnut producer after Turkey and Italy [3][7].
- **Grass seed:** The Willamette Valley is the grass seed capital of the world, producing ryegrass, fescue, and bluegrass seed for lawns and turf worldwide [3].
- **Christmas trees:** Oregon is the nation's largest producer, with Noble fir as the signature species [3][7].
- **Blackberries:** Both marionberries (a cultivar developed at Oregon State University) and other varieties [3].

The Willamette Valley is also one of the world's premier wine regions, particularly for Pinot Noir, with over 700 wineries and 40,000+ acres of vineyard. Module 03 covers this in detail [8].

Beyond the valley, Oregon's agriculture extends to cranberry bogs on the southern coast, cattle ranches in the high desert east of the Cascades, wheat farms in the Pendleton area, and onion and potato production in the Treasure Valley along the Idaho border [3][7].

## 4. The Columbia Basin Empire

The Columbia Basin Project is perhaps the most dramatic agricultural transformation story in American history. Before Grand Coulee Dam was completed in 1942, the region east of the Cascades was sagebrush steppe -- arid, windswept, receiving 6-8 inches of annual rainfall, and seemingly worthless for farming [9][10].

The dam changed everything. Water pumped from Lake Roosevelt into Banks Lake flows by gravity through a canal system that irrigates 671,000 acres, producing:

- **Potatoes:** The Columbia Basin produces billions of pounds annually, supplying processors like Lamb Weston and J.R. Simplot [9].
- **Corn and alfalfa:** Feed crops supporting the region's dairy and cattle industries [9].
- **Onions, mint, asparagus:** Specialty crops that thrive in the long, hot growing season [9].
- **Wine grapes:** Several AVAs (Wahluke Slope, Ancient Lakes) sit within the irrigated region [6].

The original plan called for irrigating 1.1 million acres. Only 671,000 have been developed, leaving over 400,000 acres of unrealized potential. As the Odessa Aquifer declines (see Module 04), pressure to complete the original vision has intensified [9][10].

## 5. The Crop Diversity Paradox

The PNW grows over 300 distinct crop categories -- one of the highest diversity figures in the United States. This diversity is not despite the rain shadow but because of it. The rain shadow creates two distinct growing environments in close proximity, and the irrigation infrastructure on the east side allows farmers to choose precisely how much water each crop receives, rather than being at the mercy of rainfall patterns [1][2][3].

This precision, combined with the region's range of microclimates from sea level to 4,000 feet elevation, enables production of everything from tropical-climate-adapted hops to cool-climate wine grapes to arid-adapted potatoes -- often within a few hours' drive of each other. The Willamette Valley grows Pinot Noir because it's too cool for Cabernet Sauvignon; the Yakima Valley grows Cabernet Sauvignon because it's too warm for Pinot Noir. Both are world-class. The Cascades created both [1][2][3].

## 6. Economic Scale

The combined agricultural economy of Washington and Oregon generates over $15 billion annually in farm gate receipts [1][3]:

| Category | Washington | Oregon | Combined |
|----------|-----------|--------|----------|
| Total farm receipts | ~$10.5B | ~$5.5B | ~$16B |
| Agricultural exports | ~$7B | ~$3.5B | ~$10.5B |
| Wine industry revenue | ~$8B (economic impact) | ~$6.6B (economic impact) | ~$14.6B |
| Agricultural employment | ~160,000 | ~100,000 | ~260,000 |
| Number of farms | ~35,800 | ~37,600 | ~73,400 |

*Sources: USDA NASS, state agriculture departments, wine commissions [1][3][4][7]*

The wine industry's economic impact exceeds its farm gate value by an order of magnitude when tourism, restaurants, retail, and employment are included. Oregon wine tourism alone generates an estimated $207.5 million annually, with 1.8 million winery visits per year [8].

## 7. The Seasons of Labor

PNW agriculture is deeply seasonal. The labor calendar follows the crops:

- **January-March:** Pruning (orchards and vineyards), greenhouse starts
- **April-May:** Bud break, planting, thinning (apple orchards require 25-60 hours of hand thinning per acre)
- **June-July:** Cherry harvest, berry harvest, vineyard management
- **August-September:** Apple harvest begins, wine grape harvest, hop harvest
- **October-November:** Late apple varieties, wine crush, fall planting
- **December:** Processing, storage, planning

Washington's apple industry alone employs 40,000-50,000 seasonal workers annually, with H-2A visa positions accounting for up to 96% of all certified agricultural positions in the state [11][12]. The labor challenge is one of the defining constraints on PNW agriculture, driving investment in mechanization, harvest platforms, and high-density orchard systems that reduce per-acre labor requirements.

## 8. The Rain Shadow as Agricultural Engine

The through-line of PNW agriculture is the rain shadow. The Cascades intercept Pacific moisture, dropping 60-100 inches of rain on the western slopes and leaving 6-15 inches for the eastern side. This creates the paradox that defines the region: the most productive agricultural land in the PNW is the driest land, and it became productive only when humans engineered water delivery from the rivers that the wet side feeds [1][2][9].

The rain shadow also provides the sunshine. Three hundred days of cloudless skies give crops the photosynthetic energy they need. Low humidity reduces disease pressure. Cold winters provide the chill hours that deciduous fruit trees require. Hot days and cool nights build flavor in wine grapes and sweetness in apples [1][2].

Without the rain shadow, eastern Washington would be western Washington -- wet, cloudy, and suited for grass and berries but not for the crops that generate billions. Without the water from the Columbia River -- itself fed by the rain and snowmelt that falls on the wet side -- the rain shadow would remain sagebrush. Agriculture is the product of both: the mountain that blocks the rain and the river that delivers the water [9][10].
