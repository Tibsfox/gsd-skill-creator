## 1. AGR as Convergence Point

Agriculture is not one domain. It is the intersection of all domains. To grow food, you need land (ecology), water (hydrology), energy (power), labor (economics), infrastructure (engineering), pest management (entomology), soil science (geology), weather (climatology), markets (business), and policy (governance). Every Research project in the PNW series connects to agriculture because agriculture touches everything [1].

AGR sits at the center of the bioregion map not because it is the most important project, but because it is the most connected. It cannot exist without the water from HGE's dams, the soils from CAS's volcanic geology, the pollinators from ECO's food webs, the pest predators from AVI's bird populations, the infrastructure from NND's public works, or the markets from WSB's business systems. Agriculture is applied ecology, applied hydrology, applied geology, applied everything [1].

## 2. The Water Trinity

The Columbia River is the organizing principle of PNW agriculture, and it embodies the central tension of PNW land use. The same river serves three purposes that compete with each other [2][3][4]:

### 1. Irrigation

Grand Coulee Dam and the Columbia Basin Project irrigate 671,000 acres. The Bureau of Reclamation pumps 2.5 million acre-feet annually from Lake Roosevelt into Banks Lake, from which it flows by gravity through the canal system. This water transforms sagebrush into potatoes, corn, wine grapes, and hundreds of other crops [2].

### 2. Hydroelectric Power

Grand Coulee Dam generates 6,809 MW of electricity -- among the largest power-generating facilities in the United States. The dam's six reversible pump-generator turbines blur the line between irrigation and power: the same water that irrigates farms can be returned to Lake Roosevelt to generate electricity during peak demand [2][3].

### 3. Salmon Habitat

Anadromous salmon and steelhead require passage through the Columbia River system to reach spawning grounds in the upper watershed. Grand Coulee Dam has no fish passage facilities -- salmon were permanently blocked from the upper Columbia when the dam was completed. Downstream dams (Bonneville, The Dalles, John Day, McNary) have fish ladders, but passage mortality remains significant. Every dam that stores irrigation water is a dam that impedes fish migration [3][4].

### The Conflict

Every gallon diverted for irrigation is a gallon not flowing over turbines for electricity and not flowing downstream for salmon. Every dam that stores water for summer irrigation reduces spring peak flows that trigger smolt outmigration. The Yakima Basin faces the most acute version of this conflict: in drought years, irrigation curtailment directly benefits in-stream flows for fish, and vice versa [2][3][4].

The tension is not resolvable -- it can only be managed. The Yakima Basin Integrated Plan ($4 billion, 30 years) attempts to serve all three purposes simultaneously through new storage, habitat restoration, and operational changes. But the fundamental arithmetic remains: there is one river and three competing claims [3][4].

## 3. The Cascade Rain Shadow

The Cascade Range is the most important agricultural fact in the Pacific Northwest. It is also the most important ecological fact, the most important climatological fact, and the most important cultural fact. Agriculture is where the rain shadow becomes tangible -- visible in every irrigated field, every sprinkler system, every canal [5][6].

### West Side: Maritime Agriculture

- 60-100 inches annual rainfall
- Cool summers (70-80 degrees Fahrenheit peak)
- Mild winters (rarely below 25 degrees Fahrenheit)
- Crops: berries, hazelnuts, grass seed, nursery stock, wine grapes (Pinot Noir)
- Agriculture is rainfed -- irrigation supplemental only

### East Side: Continental Agriculture

- 6-15 inches annual rainfall
- Hot summers (90-105 degrees Fahrenheit)
- Cold winters (below 0 degrees Fahrenheit in some areas)
- Crops: apples, cherries, hops, wine grapes (Cabernet, Merlot), potatoes, wheat
- Agriculture is impossible without irrigation

The rain shadow doesn't just create two climate zones. It creates the conditions for agricultural excellence on the dry side: abundant sunshine for photosynthesis, low humidity for disease suppression, clear cold nights for fruit quality, and complete human control over water delivery. The rain shadow is not a limitation -- it is an advantage, provided the water infrastructure exists [5][6].

## 4. The Ecology Cluster

Agriculture is embedded in ecological systems. The connections to ecology-domain projects are deep [7][8][9]:

### ECO (Living Systems Taxonomy)

- **Elevation zonation:** The same elevation bands that organize plant communities organize crop suitability. Apples grow at 750-2,000 feet in central Washington. Wine grapes at 300-1,500 feet. Wheat at 1,500-3,000 feet in the Palouse.
- **Pollinator networks:** Apple production depends on honeybee pollination (approximately 1.5 million colonies are rented annually for Washington apple bloom). Native pollinators supplement commercial hives. Pollinator health is an agricultural concern [7][8].
- **Soil ecology:** Mycorrhizal networks in orchard soils. Cover crop ecology in vineyards. The microbiome of agricultural soils is a direct extension of ECO's living systems framework [7].

### AVI (Birds of the PNW)

- **Pest predation:** Woodpeckers (downy, hairy, Lewis's) excavate overwintering codling moth larvae from bark. Warblers and flycatchers consume adult moths during flight season. Barn owls hunt voles that damage orchard roots [8][9].
- **Habitat corridors:** Orchards and vineyards can function as habitat corridors when hedgerows and cover crops are maintained, connecting fragmented natural areas [8][9].
- **Barn owl boxes:** WSU Extension promotes barn owl nest boxes in orchards for rodent control -- a direct biological control practice [9].

### CAS (Cascade Range)

- **Rain shadow:** Module 3 in detail
- **Volcanic soils:** Jory soils in the Willamette Valley are derived from Columbia River Basalt Group lava flows. The terroir IS the volcanism [5][6].
- **Fire ecology:** Wildfire from Cascade and Coast Range forests produces the smoke that taints wine grapes (Module 06) [5].

## 5. The Energy Cluster

Agriculture and energy are inseparable in the PNW [2][3][10]:

### HGE (Hydro-Geothermal Energy)

- **Same dams, dual purpose:** Grand Coulee generates both irrigation water and electricity. The six reversible pump-generators explicitly link the two systems [2][3].
- **Volcanic geology:** The same magmatic systems that built the Cascades (creating the rain shadow) created the basalt that became wine-grape terroir [10].

### THE (Thermal Energy)

- **Energy-water nexus:** The Columbia River system generates approximately 40% of US hydroelectric power. Every operational decision about dam releases affects both energy production and irrigation water availability [3][10].
- **Climate change:** Warming temperatures increase both energy demand (air conditioning) and irrigation demand while reducing water supply (earlier snowmelt). The competition for water intensifies on both the energy and agriculture sides [10].

## 6. The Infrastructure Cluster

Agriculture depends on infrastructure that was built for multiple purposes [2][11][12]:

### NND (New New Deal)

- **The Columbia Basin Project as New Deal:** Grand Coulee Dam was authorized in 1933 as a New Deal public works project. The political economy of public infrastructure investment -- federal dollars transforming a regional landscape -- is NND's territory [11].
- **Infrastructure corridors:** The canal system, power transmission lines, and road network built for the Columbia Basin Project opened the region to development beyond agriculture [11].

### WYR (Weyerhaeuser)

- **Land use transformation:** The Willamette Valley's conversion from timber to farmland -- and from farmland to vineyard -- is a land use story that connects WYR's forest industry perspective with AGR's agricultural perspective [12].
- **Timber-to-farmland pipeline:** Cleared forest land became grass seed fields, which became hazelnut orchards, which became vineyards. Each transition reflected changing economics and markets [12].

## 7. The Business Cluster

Agriculture is a business, and its economic structures connect to business-domain projects [13][14]:

### WSB (Small Business)

- **Small wineries as small businesses:** 75% of Oregon wineries produce fewer than 5,000 cases. These are family businesses navigating cash flow, labor, marketing, insurance, and regulatory compliance -- exactly WSB's domain [13].
- **Direct-to-consumer:** Winery tasting rooms are retail operations. Enotourism is a hospitality business. The shift from wholesale distribution to DTC sales is a small-business transformation story [13].

### ACC (Accounting)

- **Agricultural accounting:** Crop valuation, depreciation of orchard assets (trees, trellises, rootstock), crop insurance claims, water rights as balance-sheet items. The financial structure of a farm or winery is complex [14].

## 8. The Culture Cluster

Agriculture is culture. The PNW agricultural landscape is not just productive -- it is meaningful [15][16]:

### GDN (PNW Gardening)

- **Personal-scale agriculture:** Everything in AGR scales down to the garden. The same rootstock science applies to a backyard apple tree. The same codling moth biology applies to a home orchard. The same soil science applies to a vegetable bed [15].
- **Food sovereignty:** Growing your own food is the most direct connection between person and agriculture. GDN is AGR at human scale [15].

### SAL (Salmon Heritage)

- **The original agricultural conflict:** Salmon vs. dams is the ur-conflict of PNW land use. The same river that irrigates farms blocks fish passage. The same water that grows potatoes could carry smolts to the ocean. Agriculture and salmon are not enemies -- they are competing beneficiaries of the same resource [4][16].
- **Tribal water rights:** The Yakama Nation, Confederated Tribes of the Colville Reservation, and other tribal nations hold water rights that predate and in some cases supersede agricultural claims. The intersection of tribal sovereignty, treaty rights, and agricultural water use is a live and sensitive issue [4][16].
