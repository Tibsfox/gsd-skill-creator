## 1. Before the Dam

Before Grand Coulee Dam was completed in 1942, the Columbia Basin of eastern Washington was sagebrush steppe. Annual rainfall: 6-8 inches. Summer temperatures: routinely above 100 degrees Fahrenheit. The landscape was beautiful in its severity -- basalt coulees carved by the Missoula Floods 15,000 years ago, rolling wheat fields in the Palouse to the east, and endless dry prairie in between. But it was not farmland. Not without water [1][2].

The idea of irrigating the Columbia Basin predated the dam by decades. Boosters in the 1910s-1920s envisioned a "gravity plan" using canals from the Pend Oreille River in northern Idaho, but the distances and elevation changes were prohibitive. The "pump plan" -- using Grand Coulee Dam to lift Columbia River water into the natural reservoir of the Upper Grand Coulee -- was championed by Rufus Woods, editor of the Wenatchee Daily World, and eventually adopted by the Bureau of Reclamation as part of the New Deal [1][2][3].

The political battle was intense. Eastern Washington farmers wanted water. Private power companies opposed a massive federal dam that would generate cheap public hydroelectricity. The project was approved in 1933 and construction began in December of that year [1][2].

## 2. Grand Coulee: The Engineering

Grand Coulee Dam is a gravity dam on the Columbia River in Grant County, Washington. At completion it was the largest concrete structure in the United States [1][2]:

| Specification | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Height | 550 feet |
| Length | 5,223 feet (nearly a mile) |
| Concrete volume | 11.975 million cubic yards |
| Lake Roosevelt (reservoir) | 151 miles long, 82 miles of shoreline |
| Total generating capacity | 6,809 MW |
| Pump capacity | 593 MW (12 pump units) |
| Annual irrigation water | 2.5 million acre-feet |

*Sources: Bureau of Reclamation, Northwest Power and Conservation Council [1][2].*

The dam serves dual purposes: hydroelectric power generation and irrigation water supply. Six of the twelve pump units are reversible pump-generator turbines with 314 MW generating capacity, enabling water to be pumped up to Banks Lake during low-demand periods and returned to Lake Roosevelt for power generation during peak demand. This flexibility makes the dam both an irrigation engine and a grid-balancing tool [1][2].

The dam's construction employed over 7,000 workers at peak. At least 77 workers died during construction. The project displaced the Colville Confederated Tribes from their ancestral fishing grounds at Kettle Falls, where salmon had been harvested for thousands of years. The dam has no fish passage facilities -- salmon and steelhead were permanently blocked from the upper Columbia River [1][2][4].

## 3. The Columbia Basin Project

Water is pumped from Lake Roosevelt by the twelve pump units, lifted 280 feet into Banks Lake -- the 27-mile-long reservoir occupying the Upper Grand Coulee, a natural basalt-walled canyon carved by the Missoula Floods. From Banks Lake, water flows by gravity through the Main Canal southward into the project area [1][5].

The project irrigates 671,000 acres across Grant, Adams, Franklin, and Walla Walla counties. The transformation was total:

| Before (pre-1952) | After (current) |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Sagebrush steppe | 671,000 irrigated acres |
| 6-8 inches rainfall | Irrigation water on demand |
| Seasonal grazing | Potatoes, corn, wheat, alfalfa, onions, mint, wine grapes |
| Scattered homesteads | Towns: Moses Lake, Othello, Ephrata, Quincy, Mattawa |
| Near-zero farm output | Billions in annual agricultural production |

*Sources: Bureau of Reclamation, ECBID [1][5][6].*

The project was developed in stages. The first water deliveries began in 1952 to the northern portion. Development proceeded southward through the 1960s and 1970s. The original authorization called for 1.1 million acres of irrigation, but only 671,000 have been developed, leaving over 400,000 acres of authorized but unbuilt irrigation capacity [1][5].

## 4. The Canal System

The water delivery infrastructure is a network of canals, laterals, siphons, and reservoirs [1][5]:

- **Main Canal:** Flows from Banks Lake southward, the primary artery of the system
- **West Canal:** Serves the western portion of the project area
- **East Low Canal:** Serves the eastern areas at lower elevation
- **Potholes Reservoir:** A critical intermediate storage facility that captures return flows (water that drains off irrigated fields) from northern farms. This recycled water is redistributed to the southern portion through the Potholes Canal, yielding an additional 1 million acre-feet of effective use beyond the 2.5 million pumped from the Columbia [1][5]
- **Siphons and tunnels:** The system includes dozens of siphons that carry water across coulees and under roads, and tunnels that pass through basalt ridges

The irrigation efficiency is notable. Unlike flood irrigation systems that waste significant water to evaporation and runoff, the Columbia Basin Project uses primarily sprinkler and center-pivot systems that deliver water directly to crops. Return flows are captured and reused. The system's overall efficiency has improved substantially since the early decades [1][5].

## 5. The Odessa Aquifer Crisis

The Odessa Subarea Aquifer crisis is the most urgent water issue in the Columbia Basin and one of the most serious in the Pacific Northwest [6][7][8].

### The Problem

Farmers within the Columbia Basin Project boundaries but outside the developed irrigation infrastructure were authorized to drill wells into the basalt aquifer as a "temporary" measure while surface water canal infrastructure was built. That "temporary" period stretched from the 1960s to the present. The aquifer is a confined basalt aquifer system -- water trapped in layers of fractured basalt from the Columbia River Basalt Group. Unlike alluvial aquifers that are replenished by surface water infiltration, the basalt aquifer recharges extremely slowly, essentially on geological timescales [6][7].

Decades of pumping have depleted the aquifer dramatically:

- **Well depths:** Some wells now exceed 2,000 feet, compared to original depths of 200-400 feet [6][7]
- **Water levels:** Declining 10-15 feet per year in some areas [6][7]
- **Well failures:** Increasing numbers of wells going dry or producing insufficient water [6][7]
- **Water quality:** As the aquifer depletes, remaining water has elevated mineral content [6][7]

### The Response

The Odessa Groundwater Replacement Program (OGWRP) is building infrastructure to replace groundwater pumping with Columbia River surface water [6][8]:

| Funding Source | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Washington State capital budget | $158M+ |
| Bureau of Reclamation | $45M |
| Landowner-funded municipal bonds | $16.8M |
| 2023 Washington Legislature | $32.8M additional |
| **Total mobilized** | **$252M+** |

*Sources: ECBID, Washington State Legislature [6][8].*

The East Columbia Basin Irrigation District (ECBID) is authorized to serve up to 472,000 acres but currently provides surface water to only 171,000. The gap represents both the scale of the problem and the infrastructure yet to be built [6].

## 6. Water Rights: First in Time, First in Right

PNW water allocation operates under the prior appropriation doctrine, which originated in the mining camps of the 19th century American West. The principle is simple: the first person to put water to "beneficial use" holds the senior right, and in times of shortage, junior rights holders are curtailed before senior rights holders lose a single drop [9][10].

In practice, this means:

- **Senior rights** (pre-1917 in Washington, some dating to the 1880s) are nearly inviolable. Many are held by municipalities, old irrigation districts, and ranching operations [9].
- **Junior rights** (post-1917, especially post-1945) are subject to curtailment during drought years. Some agricultural operations with junior rights have been shut off entirely during severe drought [9][10].
- **The Columbia Basin Project** holds water rights dating to 1938 (the project authorization), making it one of the most senior irrigators on the Columbia system. This seniority provides structural resilience against drought [9][10].

The tension between agricultural water rights, municipal water needs, and in-stream flows for fish habitat (particularly salmon) is the defining water conflict in the PNW. Module 07 explores this convergence in detail [4][9].

## 7. Climate Vulnerability: The Timing Problem

The most critical climate change impact on PNW agriculture is not total water supply reduction but **timing shift**. The 2021 Columbia River Basin Long-Term Water Supply and Demand Forecast found [11][12]:

- **Earlier snowmelt:** Spring snowmelt is projected to occur 3-4 weeks earlier by mid-century. The Cascades snowpack -- the region's natural reservoir -- is releasing its water before crops need it [11][12].
- **Summer flow decline:** As peak runoff shifts from summer to spring, summer stream flows decrease during the exact months when irrigation demand is highest [11][12].
- **Increased demand:** Warmer temperatures mean crops transpire more water, increasing irrigation requirements during the period when supply is declining [11][12].

The Columbia Basin Project has structural advantages: the Columbia River watershed extends into the Canadian Rockies (a northerly latitude that retains more snow), Lake Roosevelt stores three times annual project demand, and the project's senior water rights insulate it from curtailment [12]. But the Yakima Basin (see Section 8) faces severe vulnerability.

The 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome -- which reached 116 degrees Fahrenheit in parts of Washington -- killed an estimated 50 million pounds of cherries on the tree and caused heat damage to apples, blueberries, and wine grapes across the region. Heat events are projected to increase in frequency and intensity [13].

## 8. The Yakima Basin

The Yakima River watershed faces the most severe water timing mismatch in the Pacific Northwest. The Yakima Basin supplies irrigation water to approximately 500,000 acres of agricultural land producing apples, hops, wine grapes, tree fruit, and row crops worth billions annually [11][14].

Unlike the Columbia Basin Project, which draws from the massive Columbia River, the Yakima system depends on a smaller, more climate-sensitive watershed. The Bureau of Reclamation operates five reservoirs on the Yakima and its tributaries, but total storage capacity is only 1.07 million acre-feet -- less than half of annual agricultural demand [14].

During drought years (most recently 2015 and 2019), junior water rights holders in the Yakima Basin received as little as 37% of their normal allocation. The economic impact cascaded through the regional economy: reduced crop production, fallowed fields, increased groundwater pumping, and stress on the Yakima River fisheries [14].

The Yakima Basin Integrated Plan -- a $4 billion, 30-year initiative involving the Bureau of Reclamation, Washington Department of Ecology, Yakama Nation, and irrigation districts -- proposes new storage, fish passage improvements, and habitat restoration. The plan acknowledges that water for agriculture and water for salmon are not separable problems [14].

## 9. The Unfinished Project

The Columbia Basin Project was authorized for 1.1 million acres. Only 671,000 have been developed. The remaining 400,000+ acres represent the original vision unfulfilled -- an irony given that the Odessa Aquifer crisis exists precisely because farmers in the undeveloped portion resorted to groundwater pumping when surface water infrastructure was not built [1][5][6].

Completing the project would require:

- Additional canal and lateral construction
- New pump stations and distribution infrastructure
- Environmental review under modern regulations (NEPA, ESA)
- Significant federal and state investment

The Bureau of Reclamation estimates that completing the project would cost several billion dollars. But the alternative -- continuing to deplete a non-renewable aquifer -- has its own costs, both economic and ecological [1][5][6].

The Office of Columbia River, created by the Washington Legislature, is tasked with developing stored water from existing reservoirs, enhancing conservation, and building infrastructure to serve irrigators, fish habitat, and municipalities. The agency represents a modern approach to the water allocation challenge: integrated, multi-stakeholder, and explicitly acknowledging the competing claims on the same water [10].
