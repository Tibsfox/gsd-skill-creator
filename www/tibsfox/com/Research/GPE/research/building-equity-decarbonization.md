## 1. The Building Decarbonization Imperative

Buildings account for approximately **30% of global final energy consumption** and **28% of global energy-related CO₂ emissions** [IEA Buildings 2024]. In the United States, buildings consume 40% of all primary energy — more than either transportation or industry. The vast majority of that energy is used for space heating (44%), water heating (19%), and space cooling (9%), with the remainder split across lighting, plug loads, and appliances [EIA RECS 2020].

The building sector's decarbonization challenge is structural: the stock turns over slowly. The average commercial building in the United States was built in 1969 [CoStar data, 2023]; the average residential unit in 1974 [EIA RECS 2020]. Buildings are effectively permanent infrastructure — once built, they operate for 50–100+ years with minimal changes to the thermal envelope. A building constructed with gas forced-air heating in 1975 will, absent policy or financial intervention, still be burning gas in 2035, 2045, and 2055.

**The technology gap is not the primary constraint.** Heat pumps achieve COP of 2.5–4.5 in most climates, making them 2.5–4.5 times as efficient per BTU delivered as direct-resistance electric heating and far cleaner than gas as the grid decarbonizes. Building envelope technologies (spray foam insulation, triple-pane windows, airtight construction with heat-recovery ventilation) are mature, commercially available, and cost-effective over building lifetimes. The constraints are:

1. **Capital cost:** Retrofit of a mid-century residential building to high performance can cost $30,000–120,000 — beyond the reach of most households without financing
2. **Split incentives:** Landlords pay for retrofit capital; tenants pay utility bills — misaligning the incentive to invest
3. **Market failures:** Renters have no leverage to demand retrofits; small landlords lack access to institutional financing; low-income owners face credit barriers
4. **Policy gaps:** Most building codes apply only to new construction; existing stock improvements are voluntary absent performance mandates

Building Performance Standards (BPS) exist to address the policy gap. They impose mandatory performance requirements on existing buildings — requiring owners to meet defined efficiency or emissions benchmarks on a schedule, subject to penalties for non-compliance.

## 2. Building Performance Standards: Policy Architecture

### 2.1 NYC Local Law 97: The Most Ambitious BPS in the US

New York City's Local Law 97 (LL97), passed in 2019 as part of the Climate Mobilization Act, establishes carbon intensity limits for buildings over 25,000 square feet — approximately 50,000 buildings covering 60% of the city's total building floor area.

**Structure:**

| Period | Compliance start | Carbon intensity limits | Aggregate target |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Phase 1 | 2024 | Set at current average (most buildings already compliant) | ~40% below 2005 by 2030 |
| Phase 2 | 2030 | Tightened significantly — requires ~50% carbon reduction for median building | ~80% by 2035 |
| Phase 3 | 2035 | Most stringent — requires near-zero carbon | ~80% below 2005 |

**Penalty structure:** Buildings that exceed their annual carbon limit pay $268 per metric ton of CO₂ over the limit. For a 100,000 sq ft office building exceeding its limit by 500 tonnes CO₂, the annual penalty is $134,000. For large residential towers, penalties can reach $1–5 million/year by the 2030 phase — creating a compelling financial incentive to retrofit before that date.

**Compliance pathway flexibility:** LL97 allows compliance through:
- Direct efficiency improvements (insulation, heat pump installation, window upgrades)
- Purchasing Renewable Energy Credits (RECs) from NYC-eligible grid sources (with limitations on geography and vintage)
- Purchasing decarbonization credits from specific qualifying low-income building retrofits
- Beneficial electrification (replacing gas with heat pumps, not credits)

**Equity carve-outs:**
- Affordable housing with regulatory agreements (Section 8, NYCHA properties, inclusionary units) receive adjusted emissions limits that account for the limited capital available to those owners
- "Hardship" applications allow extensions for buildings that can demonstrate that compliance costs would require rent increases above defined thresholds
- The NYC Accelerator program (city-funded) provides free energy auditing, contractor connections, and financing guidance to LL97-covered building owners

**Early implementation status (2024):**
- Approximately 3,700 buildings reported non-compliance for the 2024 reporting year
- Total penalty liability estimated at $100–150 million for 2024
- Enforcement began in 2024; penalty payments due in 2025
- 12,000+ buildings enrolled in the NYC Accelerator pre-compliance program

[NYC Mayor's Office of Climate & Environmental Justice, LL97 Annual Report 2024; Urban Green Council, 2024.]

### 2.2 Washington State Clean Buildings Act

Washington State's Clean Buildings Act (passed 2019, implemented via WAC 194-50) applies to commercial and institutional buildings over 50,000 sq ft, and sets Energy Use Intensity (EUI) targets — the metric is kBtu/sq ft/year rather than carbon emissions.

**Structure:**
- Tier 1 (>50,000 sq ft): EUI targets set by building type (office, retail, hospital, school, etc.) based on the bottom quartile of Washington's existing building stock. Implementation schedule: 2026–2028 compliance deadlines depending on building size
- Tier 2 (10,000–50,000 sq ft): included by 2031 expansion
- Residential buildings: not currently covered by the commercial standard; addressed separately by the State Building Code Council's residential efficiency updates

**The EUI metric vs. carbon metric:** Washington chose EUI (energy intensity per square foot) rather than NYC's carbon metric because EUI is directly verifiable from utility bills without emissions factor assumptions, making enforcement more transparent. The tradeoff: EUI standards do not directly reward fuel switching (a building that installs heat pumps powered by Washington's 80%+ hydroelectric grid has dramatically lower carbon but the same EUI). A subsequent amendment (HB 1257, 2021) added an electrification pathway that provides EUI credit for qualifying heat pump installations.

**Compliance support:** The Washington State Department of Commerce administers the Clean Buildings E3 (Efficiency, Economy, Employment) program, which provides:
- Incentives of $0.30–0.60/sq ft for qualifying efficiency improvements
- Technical assistance grants of up to $50,000 for energy audits
- Low-interest loans through the Washington State Energy Revolving Loan Fund

[Washington State Department of Commerce, Clean Buildings Act Implementation Report 2024; Northwest Energy Coalition, 2024.]

### 2.3 Boston BERDO 2.0

Boston's Building Emissions Reduction and Disclosure Ordinance (BERDO) was strengthened in 2021 (BERDO 2.0) to include absolute emissions limits for buildings over 20,000 sq ft — one of the strictest such policies in the United States.

**Key feature:** BERDO 2.0 includes a formal **equity review requirement** for the emissions standards — before any compliance pathway is finalized, the city must assess the distributional impact on low-income residents and communities of color. This is the first building performance standard in the United States to embed an equity analysis requirement into the standard-setting process itself, not just as an afterthought. [City of Boston Environment Department, BERDO 2.0 Guidance, 2022.]

### 2.4 EU EPBD: The European Framework

The EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD), recast in 2024, establishes a Europe-wide framework for building efficiency that operates above and through national policies:

- **Minimum Energy Performance Standards (MEPS):** by 2030, all EU residential buildings must be upgraded to at least Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) class E (on the A–G scale); by 2033, class D. This effectively mandates retrofit of the 15% lowest-performing buildings in the stock
- **Zero-emission buildings:** all new buildings must be zero-emission from 2028 (public buildings from 2026)
- **Solar-ready provisions:** large new buildings and those undergoing major renovation must install rooftop photovoltaic systems by 2027
- **National Building Renovation Plans:** each member state must submit a building renovation roadmap with targets and financing mechanisms

The EPBD 2024 is a milestone because it moves from a disclosure and labeling framework (original 2002 EPBD, 2010 recast) to one with binding minimum performance thresholds. The lowest-performing 15% of buildings in the EU — typically pre-1960s housing stock in Eastern Europe, UK terraces, rural Mediterranean housing — will require investment totaling an estimated €250–350 billion through 2033. [European Commission, EPBD Impact Assessment, 2023.]

## 3. The Split Incentive Problem and Its Solutions

### 3.1 Anatomy of the Problem

The landlord-tenant split incentive (also called the "energy efficiency gap" or "principal-agent problem") is the single most analyzed market failure in building energy policy:

- **The landlord's position:** Owns the building; pays for capital improvements; does not pay utility bills (under gross leases) or pays them indirectly through negotiated rent (under modified gross leases)
- **The tenant's position:** Pays utility bills; does not own the building; cannot improve it without landlord permission; typically has a lease term of 1–3 years — too short to recoup investment in efficiency improvements even if permitted

The result: a landlord who invests $15,000 in insulation and heat pump upgrades that save $1,200/year in utility bills (for a tenant who pays the bills) cannot recapture that investment through higher rent without the tenant's consent, and the tenant won't agree to higher rent even for an efficient building because the market doesn't price efficiency transparently.

**Scale of the problem:** In the United States, approximately 36% of households rent [Census 2023]. Among low-income households (below 80% Area Median Income), the rental rate is approximately 60%. The buildings most likely to be inefficient (older stock, deferred maintenance, lower amenity) are disproportionately occupied by renters and lower-income owners.

### 3.2 Green Leases

A **green lease** (also called an aligned lease or sustainable lease) is a lease modification that aligns landlord and tenant incentives around energy efficiency:

**Key provisions:**
- **Utility data sharing:** Tenant provides landlord with access to utility bills/data for benchmarking; landlord provides tenant with building energy disclosure data
- **Efficiency investment passthrough:** For qualifying improvements above a defined minimum, tenant agrees to pay a share of energy cost savings as a rent supplement during the payback period (e.g., 50% of measured savings for 5 years, not to exceed the verified improvement cost)
- **Maintenance obligations:** Landlord commits to maintaining HVAC, lighting, and envelope at defined performance standards; tenant commits to not defeating efficiency measures (e.g., blocking thermostatic controls)
- **Green improvement allowance:** Tenant-initiated improvements that improve EPC rating or reduce EUI are treated as standard building improvements rather than unauthorized modifications

**Adoption:** Green leases are most common in the commercial sector (large office buildings, retail anchors), where sophisticated tenants have leverage to negotiate. In the residential sector, green leases remain rare; policy intervention (on-bill financing, PACE) is typically needed to bridge the split incentive for residential buildings.

**EU Green Lease Framework:** The EU has published a voluntary Green Lease Toolkit (European Green Building Programme, 2023) and the revised EPBD encourages member states to mandate green lease provisions for buildings below minimum EPC thresholds.

### 3.3 On-Bill Financing

**On-bill financing (OBF)** is a mechanism where the cost of energy efficiency improvements is recovered through a charge on the utility bill rather than through a traditional loan to the building owner:

- A participating utility, government program, or third-party financer funds the retrofit upfront
- The cost is recovered as a line-item charge on the utility bill, typically set below the expected energy savings (so the bill stays flat or decreases)
- Crucially, in some structures the obligation is **attached to the meter, not the borrower** — if the tenant moves out, the obligation moves to the next occupant (who still enjoys the efficiency benefits and pays the same charge)
- This solves the split incentive: the landlord has no upfront cost, the tenant sees a flat bill with no net increase, and the financer is repaid through ongoing utility payments

**Example — Hawaii On-Bill Financing:** Hawaii's Green Energy Market Securitization (GEMS) program provides on-bill financing for solar, storage, and efficiency through Hawaiian Electric. As of 2024, GEMS has funded $186 million of improvements, serving predominantly low-to-moderate income households at 0% interest [GEMS Annual Report, 2024].

**Limitation:** On-bill financing requires the utility's cooperation and willingness to serve as payment processor. Investor-owned utilities have historically resisted this model because it reduces energy throughput (their revenue base). States with public power or municipal utilities, and states with decoupled utility ratemaking, are more conducive environments.

### 3.4 Pay-As-You-Save and PACE

**PACE (Property Assessed Clean Energy)** financing attaches efficiency improvement costs to the property rather than the owner:

- Improvement costs are funded upfront by a PACE provider
- The obligation is assessed as a property tax lien — the annual repayment appears on the property tax bill
- If the property is sold, the PACE lien transfers to the new owner (who also inherits the efficiency improvements and reduced utility bills)
- This solves the landlord mobility problem: a landlord who plans to sell in 3 years can still invest in a 15-year improvement, because the obligation transfers at sale

**PACE market:** Commercial PACE (C-PACE) is active in 36 US states; residential PACE (R-PACE) is available in California, Florida, and Missouri. Total US PACE originations through 2024: approximately $15 billion cumulative [PACENation, 2024].

**Caution:** R-PACE has been the subject of significant consumer protection concerns, particularly in California, where aggressive PACE marketers targeted low-income homeowners with products misrepresented as government programs. CFPB rules issued in 2023 now require PACE disclosures equivalent to mortgage disclosures, and California AB 2501 (2024) adds additional borrower protections. PACE financing should be carefully evaluated against alternatives before committing.

## 4. Affordable Housing Retrofit Financing

### 4.1 LIHEAP and WAP: The Federal Foundation

**LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program)** is the primary federal program for energy affordability in the United States:

- Annual appropriation: ~$4.1 billion (FY2024), distributed to states as block grants
- Primary use: bill payment assistance (approximately 70% of funds)
- Secondary use: weatherization (insulation, air sealing, furnace repair) — approximately 10–15% of funds
- Eligibility: household income at or below 150% of the federal poverty level (FPL) or 60% of state median income
- Average benefit: approximately $500–800 per household per year — significant but far below the $1,500–2,500 typical annual energy cost for a burdened household

**WAP (Weatherization Assistance Program)** is the federal program specifically for physical efficiency improvements:

- Annual appropriation: ~$400 million (FY2024, pre-IRA); expanded significantly by IRA
- Administered by the Department of Energy through state weatherization agencies
- Eligible households: at or below 200% FPL
- Average investment per unit: $7,500–9,500 (DOE measure cost cap, with state supplemental funding)
- Measured energy savings: average $283/year per household, with lifetime savings of $3,000–5,000
- Units served annually: approximately 40,000–60,000 (before IRA expansion); the backlog of eligible households is estimated at 37 million

The gap between WAP investment ($7,500/unit average) and the actual cost of comprehensive deep retrofit ($30,000–80,000) means WAP typically funds weatherization measures (insulation, air sealing, furnace tune-up or replacement) rather than full electrification. The IRA-funded HOMES program fills part of this gap.

### 4.2 IRA Home Energy Rebates (HOMES and HEEHRA)

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) appropriated $9 billion for the DOE Home Energy Rebates program, implemented through two sub-programs:

**HOMES (Home Efficiency Rebates):** $4.3 billion for whole-home efficiency retrofits achieving verified energy reduction:
- 20–35% whole-home energy reduction: up to $2,000 rebate (up to 50% of project cost)
- 35–50% reduction: up to $4,000 rebate (up to 50% of project cost)
- Above 50% reduction: up to $8,000 rebate (up to 50% of project cost)
- Low-income bonus: rebates up to 80% of project cost; higher caps (up to $16,000 for >50% reduction in LMI households)

**HEEHRA (High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act, also called the "HEAR" rebates):** $4.5 billion for specific equipment and installation:
- Heat pump water heater: up to $1,750
- Heat pump HVAC: up to $8,000
- Electric load center (panel upgrade): up to $4,000
- Insulation and air sealing: up to $1,600
- Electric wiring: up to $2,500
- Electric stove/cooktop: up to $840
- Full household eligibility cap: $14,000
- Low-income bonus: up to 100% coverage for households at or below 80% AMI

**Implementation status (2024):** State programs are operational in approximately 24 states as of April 2026, with the remainder expected to launch through 2026. The full $9 billion must be obligated by September 30, 2031. [DOE Home Energy Rebates program status page, March 2026.]

### 4.3 Green Banks and Mission-Driven Lenders

**Green banks** are specialized public finance institutions that use public capital to catalyze private investment in clean energy and efficiency:

- The **Connecticut Green Bank** (established 2011 — the first US state green bank) has deployed $3.5 billion in clean energy investments with a 9:1 private leverage ratio, serving 60,000+ Connecticut households including 10,000 low-income households [Connecticut Green Bank Annual Report, 2024]
- The **New York Green Bank** ($3 billion capital base) focuses on commercial and multifamily, with a specific affordable housing lending window that provides below-market-rate loans to affordable housing developers and owners
- The **Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF)**, a $27 billion IRA provision, capitalizes a national green bank infrastructure — the National Clean Investment Fund ($14 billion) and Clean Communities Investment Accelerator ($6 billion) specifically target LMI communities

**Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)** provide mission-driven financing in underserved communities. Several CDFIs have developed green lending products:
- **Self-Help Credit Union** (NC): green home loans for LMI homebuyers, minimum credit score 620, below-market rate
- **Beneficial State Bank** (CA/OR/WA): dedicated retrofit lending for multifamily affordable housing owners
- **Community Reinvestment Fund** (MN): PACE and on-bill financing products for affordable housing

## 5. Tenant Protections During Retrofits

Building retrofits, while ultimately beneficial, create displacement risks during construction:

- HVAC system replacement may require several days without heat or cooling
- Deep retrofits (adding exterior insulation, replacing windows) may require weeks of disruption or temporary relocation
- Rent increases following retrofit can exceed tenant affordability

**Key protections (best practice, from NYC, Seattle, and EU examples):**

**Right to return:** Tenants temporarily displaced for construction must have the legal right to return to their unit at the same rent upon completion. NYC's Tenant Protection Act (2019) codifies this; Seattle's Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance (TRAO) requires landlords to pay relocation assistance of 1–3 months rent when tenants are displaced for construction exceeding 30 days.

**Rent stabilization after retrofit:** Several cities (including Vienna, Amsterdam, and Berlin pre-2021) prohibit landlords from increasing rent above a defined threshold following efficiency retrofits, on the theory that the retrofit was partly financed by below-market rents during the landlord's ownership period. The Vienna model — where 60% of the housing stock is publicly owned or in limited-profit cooperatives, with rent increases capped at 2%/year regardless of improvements — is often cited as the gold standard, though it requires the ownership structure that makes it possible.

**Hardship exemption access:** BPS programs with penalty structures (like LL97) must provide accessible hardship exemption pathways for affordable housing owners who genuinely cannot access capital at the rate required by the compliance schedule. Without this, BPS policies can backfire — landlords may sell to market-rate developers, triggering gentrification displacement.

**Heat/cooling continuity:** During any active heating or cooling season, tenants must have code-minimum thermal comfort maintained throughout the retrofit. This may require temporary heating/cooling equipment as a landlord obligation.

## 6. Energy Burden: The Equity Metric That Matters

### 6.1 Defining Energy Burden

**Energy burden** is the percentage of gross household income spent on home energy (electricity + gas + heating fuel). It is the single most policy-relevant metric for understanding energy inequity.

The standard thresholds:
- **<3%:** Low energy burden — energy costs are not a significant budget constraint
- **3–6%:** Moderate energy burden — within typical range for middle-income households
- **>6%:** High energy burden — energy costs constrain discretionary spending and basic necessities
- **>10%:** Severe energy burden — energy costs severely constrain household budget; associated with health impacts, heat/electricity insecurity, and higher rates of utility disconnection

**National averages obscure the distribution:** The US national average energy burden is approximately 3.2% [DOE Low-Income Energy Affordability Data (LEAD), 2024]. But averages mask extreme variation:
- Average low-income household (below 80% AMI): **8.1%** energy burden
- Average very-low-income household (below 50% AMI): **12.9%**
- Average extremely-low-income household (below 30% AMI): **17.5%**
- Rural low-income households: **13.2%** (worse than urban due to older housing stock, limited alternatives, higher transportation energy burden compounding)

### 6.2 Energy Burden Geography in the United States

Energy burden is not uniformly distributed geographically. The DOE LEAD tool maps energy burden at the census tract level for the entire United States:

**Highest burden states/regions:**
- **Mississippi Delta:** Severe energy burden >15% in multiple census tracts; combination of old housing stock, high cooling demand, low incomes, and high utility rates
- **Appalachian coalfields (WV, KY, VA, PA):** High burden from heating costs, old housing stock, declining incomes
- **Native American reservation land:** Multiple census tracts showing >20% energy burden; compounded by lack of infrastructure investment, high electricity delivery costs in remote areas
- **Deep South urban cores:** High cooling burden in Atlanta, Birmingham, Jackson; renter-dominated housing with minimal weatherization

**Lowest burden regions:** Pacific Northwest (hydroelectric electricity, mild climate); New England (high income relative to energy costs, despite high rates); Mountain West urban centers.

**The triple burden:** Energy burden is often compounded by **transportation energy burden** (cost of vehicle fuel and maintenance for households without transit access) and **water burden** (water/sewer costs). Low-income households in auto-dependent suburban and rural areas may face total combined utility burden of 25–35% of income, representing a structural poverty trap. [American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy (ACEEE) Burden Analysis, 2024.]

### 6.3 Global Energy Poverty Parallels

The IEA and World Bank track global energy poverty through the **Multi-Tier Framework** (MTF), which measures household electricity and clean cooking access on a tiered scale from Tier 0 (no access) to Tier 5 (full access equivalent to high-income countries):

- **666 million people** without any electricity access (2023) — predominantly Sub-Saharan Africa
- **2.1 billion people** cooking with solid biomass fuels — a health crisis (4 million premature deaths/year from household air pollution)
- **1.1 billion people** with electricity access Tier 1–2 only (less than 50 kWh/person/year, equivalent to lighting and phone charging only)

The energy burden concept is modified for global application: households that spend >10% of income on energy (including solid fuel purchase for cooking) are considered energy-burdened under the World Bank MTF definition. In Sub-Saharan Africa, estimates suggest 30–40% of households are burdened by this definition, despite nominal energy consumption far below high-income countries. The paradox: they consume less energy but spend a higher fraction of income obtaining it, because inefficient cooking stoves waste 85% of fuel energy as uncontrolled combustion heat.

## 7. Healthy Buildings: Indoor Air Quality Co-Benefits of Electrification

Building decarbonization is not just a climate intervention — it is a public health intervention. Gas combustion appliances (stoves, furnaces, water heaters) emit nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), carbon monoxide (CO), particulate matter (PM2.5), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) directly into the indoor environment:

**Gas stoves:** A landmark Stanford study (Lebel et al., 2022, *Environmental Science & Technology*) found that gas stoves leak small amounts of unburned methane even when not in use, and emit NO₂ during cooking at concentrations that frequently exceed the EPA's 1-hour outdoor NO₂ standard (100 µg/m³). In homes without adequate ventilation (typical of low-income housing with minimal opening windows), gas cooking can drive indoor NO₂ to 200–400 µg/m³ — a level associated with increased respiratory disease.

**Health outcomes:**
- Gas stove use is associated with a 42% increased relative risk of childhood asthma (meta-analysis, Seals & Krasner, 2022, *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*)
- Unvented gas heating (common in lower-cost housing) elevates CO exposure, contributing to approximately 400 deaths/year from CO poisoning in the US [CDC NVSS, 2023]
- Indoor PM2.5 from gas combustion is a contributing factor in approximately 12,700 premature deaths/year in the US attributable to residential gas combustion [Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, Lebel et al., 2024]

**Electrification co-benefits:** Replacing gas appliances with electric equivalents (induction cooktops, heat pump water heaters, heat pump HVAC) eliminates indoor combustion entirely. In low-income households — where indoor air quality is typically worse due to older equipment and poorer ventilation — the health co-benefits are largest. A DOE analysis estimated the health benefits of full residential gas appliance electrification in the US at $40–100 billion/year in reduced healthcare costs and mortality risk [DOE Office of Policy, 2024].

## 8. Case Studies

### 8.1 NYC LL97 Implementation: Year One

The first LL97 compliance year (2024) generated the first real-world data on the policy's impact:

**Enforcement outcomes:**
- ~50,000 buildings reported their 2024 emissions through the online benchmarking portal (LL84 compliance, which feeds LL97)
- Approximately 3,700 buildings (7.4%) reported emissions above their LL97 limit for 2024
- Total assessed penalty for 2024: estimated $90–130 million
- Most common non-compliant building types: pre-war residential (pre-1940 construction), hotels, and healthcare facilities — all with high baseline emission intensity and limited retrofit access

**Hardship applications:**
- Approximately 800 buildings filed hardship exception applications for 2024
- Common grounds: documented financing failure, permit delays, affordable housing operational constraints
- DOB approval rate: approximately 35% of applications fully approved; 45% approved with conditions (reduced penalty, extended timeline)

**Market response:**
- NYC building energy service firms reported 40–60% increase in retrofit project pipelines from 2022–2024
- Availability of electrification contractors (heat pump installation, panel upgrades) has not kept pace with demand — labor market constraint is the primary implementation bottleneck as of 2024
- NYC Accelerator enrolled 12,000+ buildings through 2024; approximately 3,200 completed retrofits in the 2022–2024 period

[NYC Mayor's Office of Climate & Environmental Justice, LL97 Implementation Report 2024; Urban Green Council, NYC 2024 Building Emissions Study.]

### 8.2 NYCHA Electrification

The New York City Housing Authority (NYCHA) is the largest public housing authority in the United States, operating 177,000 apartments in 335 developments across the five boroughs. Its building stock represents the most concentrated affordable housing retrofit challenge in the country:

- Average building age: 1960 (a 65-year-old building)
- Primary heating system: central steam heat from building-level or campus-level boilers (predominantly natural gas or #6 fuel oil)
- Average unit energy cost: approximately $1,100/year for electricity + heat
- Capital backlog (deferred maintenance and modernization): estimated $40 billion as of 2023

**The electrification challenge at NYCHA scale:**
- Steam heat infrastructure is deeply embedded in building structure — replacing it with hydronic heat pump systems requires major renovation of pipe infrastructure, risers, and unit-level radiators
- NYC's electrical distribution infrastructure (Consolidated Edison) was not designed for simultaneous electrification of large building campuses — panel upgrades and transformer upgrades are required
- Low-income tenant population has limited tolerance for construction disruption without temporary relocation support

**NYCHA Greener, Greater Buildings progress:**
- NYCHA has committed to electrify 25,000 units by 2030 (approximately 14% of the portfolio)
- First completed projects: Bronxdale Houses (250 units, electric air source heat pumps for perimeter heating, 2023) and Bronx River Houses (320 units, 2024)
- Measured performance: Bronxdale replacement of #6 fuel oil boilers with heat pumps — 38% reduction in space heating energy cost, 67% reduction in CO₂ emissions (NYC grid is approximately 30% fossil, down from 70% in 2010)
- Federal funding: $89 million from HUD's Green and Resilient Retrofit Program (GRRP) allocated to NYCHA projects through 2026

## 9. AI for Equity-Aware Building Programs

Standard energy efficiency programs allocate resources based on technical criteria (largest buildings, highest energy users) that systematically underserve low-income communities. AI offers tools to shift toward equity-weighted program design:

**Energy burden prediction:** Machine learning models trained on DOE LEAD data, Census American Community Survey data, and utility billing records can predict energy burden at the census block level with high accuracy. ACEEE and Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory researchers developed a gradient boosting model (using housing vintage, income distribution, climate zone, and building type as features) that predicts energy burden within 1.5 percentage points RMSE at the census tract level — significantly better than naive income-proxy approaches [LBNL, Energy Burden Prediction Tool, 2024].

**Retrofit targeting:** Boston Housing and NYC DOB use ML-based targeting to prioritize outreach for building performance programs. The model combines energy burden data, EPC/benchmarking data, and permit history to identify high-burden buildings with high retrofit potential and flag them for proactive outreach from city weatherization programs.

**Rebate optimization:** With limited program budgets, AI can optimize rebate allocation across the eligible population to maximize total energy burden reduction rather than maximizing total kWh saved. These are different objectives: kWh optimization favors larger buildings in cold climates; burden reduction optimization favors small, low-income buildings with high consumption-to-income ratios. NYSERDA has piloted a burden-weighted allocation model for its EmPower+ LMI program, finding that burden-optimized allocation serves 18% more severely-burdened households per dollar of program budget [NYSERDA Evaluation Report, 2024].

**Code compliance prediction:** NYC's Department of Buildings uses a scikit-learn model to predict which buildings are likely to file false or inaccurate benchmarking data for LL97, allowing targeted audit resources toward probable violators rather than random sampling. The model uses historical benchmarking accuracy, permit inspection history, and building characteristics. Published accuracy: 73% precision, 68% recall at the 15% flag rate [NYC DOB Data Analytics Unit, 2024].

## 10. DIY Project: Energy Burden Calculator by Census Tract

**Difficulty:** Intermediate | **Cost:** $0 (software only) | **Time:** 6–10 hours

This project builds a web-based tool that maps energy burden across US census tracts using public DOE LEAD data and utility expenditure data from the American Community Survey.

```python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.colors as mcolors

# Step 1: Load DOE LEAD data (publicly available at:
# https://www.energy.gov/scep/slsc/lead-tool)
# The LEAD tool provides state-level CSV exports of energy burden by census tract

def load_lead_data(state_csv_path: str) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Load DOE LEAD tool CSV export.
    Key columns: GEOID (census tract), avg_energy_burden, avg_annual_cost,
                 avg_income, pct_low_income, pct_owner, housing_units
    """
    df = pd.read_csv(state_csv_path, dtype={'GEOID': str})
    df['energy_burden_pct'] = pd.to_numeric(
        df['avg_energy_burden'], errors='coerce') * 100
    df['severely_burdened'] = df['energy_burden_pct'] > 10
    df['highly_burdened'] = df['energy_burden_pct'] > 6
    return df


def burden_summary(df: pd.DataFrame) -> dict:
    """
    Compute energy burden summary statistics for a state.
    """
    total_units = df['housing_units'].sum()
    burdened_units = (df[df['highly_burdened']]['housing_units']).sum()
    severely_burdened_units = (df[df['severely_burdened']]['housing_units']).sum()

    return {
        'total_housing_units': int(total_units),
        'pct_high_burden_households': float(burdened_units / total_units * 100),
        'pct_severe_burden_households': float(severely_burdened_units / total_units * 100),
        'median_energy_burden_pct': float(df['energy_burden_pct'].median()),
        'mean_energy_burden_pct': float(
            np.average(df['energy_burden_pct'].dropna(),
                       weights=df.loc[df['energy_burden_pct'].notna(), 'housing_units'])),
        'worst_5pct_burden': float(df['energy_burden_pct'].quantile(0.95)),
    }


def burden_by_income_tier(df: pd.DataFrame,
                           income_col: str = 'avg_income') -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Group census tracts into income quintiles and compute mean burden per quintile.
    Shows the inverse relationship between income and energy burden.
    """
    df = df.dropna(subset=[income_col, 'energy_burden_pct']).copy()
    df['income_quintile'] = pd.qcut(df[income_col], 5,
                                     labels=['Q1 (lowest)', 'Q2', 'Q3', 'Q4', 'Q5 (highest)'])
    return (df.groupby('income_quintile', observed=True)
              .apply(lambda x: np.average(x['energy_burden_pct'],
                                          weights=x['housing_units']))
              .reset_index(name='mean_energy_burden_pct'))


def burden_gap_to_gpf_countries(state_summary: dict) -> str:
    """
    Compare state energy burden to GPE peer countries.
    The GPE electricity intensity metric (kWh/$1,000 GDP) measures system efficiency.
    Energy burden measures household-level financial impact.
    Both are dimensions of the energy state variable z_E(t).
    """
    # Approximate energy burden levels for peer countries (estimated from
    # EU-SILC, Eurostat heating inadequacy surveys, and World Bank MTF data)
    country_burdens = {
        'Denmark': 2.1,    # Low: district heating + social safety net
        'France': 2.8,     # Low-moderate: regulated tariffs
        'Germany': 3.4,    # Moderate: Energiewende raises prices
        'UK': 5.8,         # High: fuel poverty crisis
        'Poland': 7.2,     # High: energy transition costs + older housing
        'US Average': 3.2, # Moderate on average, very high for low-income
    }
    state_avg = state_summary['mean_energy_burden_pct']
    report = [f"\nState average energy burden: {state_avg:.1f}%"]
    report.append("Comparable country energy burdens:")
    for country, burden in country_burdens.items():
        comparison = "higher" if state_avg > burden else "lower"
        report.append(f"  {country}: {burden:.1f}% (state is {abs(state_avg-burden):.1f}pp {comparison})")
    return "\n".join(report)


def plot_burden_histogram(df: pd.DataFrame, state_name: str):
    """Plot distribution of energy burden across census tracts."""
    fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5))

    # Histogram weighted by housing units
    bins = np.arange(0, 30, 1)
    weights = df.loc[df['energy_burden_pct'].notna(), 'housing_units']
    ax1.hist(df.loc[df['energy_burden_pct'].notna(), 'energy_burden_pct'],
             bins=bins, weights=weights, color='steelblue', edgecolor='white')
    ax1.axvline(6, color='orange', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label='High burden (6%)')
    ax1.axvline(10, color='red', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label='Severe burden (10%)')
    ax1.set_xlabel('Energy Burden (%)')
    ax1.set_ylabel('Housing Units')
    ax1.set_title(f'{state_name}: Energy Burden Distribution')
    ax1.legend()

    # Bar chart by income quintile
    income_data = burden_by_income_tier(df)
    colors = ['#d73027', '#f46d43', '#fdae61', '#74add1', '#313695']
    ax2.bar(income_data['income_quintile'], income_data['mean_energy_burden_pct'],
            color=colors)
    ax2.axhline(6, color='orange', linestyle='--', alpha=0.7)
    ax2.axhline(10, color='red', linestyle='--', alpha=0.7)
    ax2.set_xlabel('Income Quintile')
    ax2.set_ylabel('Mean Energy Burden (%)')
    ax2.set_title(f'{state_name}: Burden by Income Quintile')
    ax2.tick_params(axis='x', rotation=15)

    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.savefig(f'{state_name.lower().replace(" ", "_")}_energy_burden.png', dpi=150)
    print(f"Saved chart: {state_name.lower().replace(' ', '_')}_energy_burden.png")
```

## 11. The Complex Plane of Building Equity

$$z_{BE}(t) = R_{BE}(t) + i \cdot X_{BE}(t)$$

**Real component $R_{BE}(t)$** — measurable building performance:
- Average Energy Use Intensity (kBtu/sq ft/year) for the building stock
- Carbon intensity of building energy (gCO₂/sq ft/year)
- BPS compliance rate (% of covered buildings meeting performance standards)
- Electrification rate (% of buildings with electric rather than fossil-fuel heating)

**Imaginary component $X_{BE}(t)$** — experienced building equity:
- Mean energy burden of low-income households (% of income on energy)
- Fraction of renters with access to efficient heating equipment
- Indoor air quality: % of homes with NO₂ above EPA standards (gas stove metric)
- Displacement risk from retrofit activity (inverted: more displacement = lower $X_{BE}$)

**Policy implication:** A jurisdiction with excellent BPS compliance ($R_{BE}$ high) that is simultaneously displacing low-income renters and raising energy burdens through retrofit-driven rent increases ($X_{BE}$ declining) is exhibiting the Quadrant IV pathology: technical progress masking experiential regression. The complex plane makes this failure mode visible in a way that compliance rate statistics alone cannot.

The goal of equity-aware BPS policy is Quadrant I: both axes positive and growing together. This requires tenant protections, accessible financing, income-weighted program design, and proactive affordability monitoring as integral components of the performance standard itself — not afterthoughts.
