# Study Guide: Know Your Fur Fabrics

## The Material Is the Medium

Every fursuit, every plush, every textile creature begins with a choice of fabric. That choice determines the look, the feel, the durability, the construction method, the cost, and ultimately whether the finished object does what it was meant to do. A fursuit head made from the wrong fur looks wrong no matter how skilled the maker. A plush stuffed animal made from the wrong fleece feels wrong no matter how precise the pattern. The fabric is not an accessory to the craft. It is the craft.

This study guide is a self-study reference card for the 10 fabric types most commonly used in fursuit and plush construction. For each fabric, you will learn what it is, what it is used for, how to identify it, how to cut it, how to sew it, where to buy it, and what it costs. At the end, a quiz tests your ability to select the right fabric for the right application.

Use this alongside the [Faux Fur Guide deep dive](../deep-dives/faux-fur-guide.md) for comprehensive fur-specific coverage and the [Textile Craft module (FFA-03)](../03-textile-craft.md) for broader textile fundamentals.

## 1. Long Pile Shag (2-4 inches)

### What It Is
Long pile faux fur with pile length between 2 and 4 inches. The pile is thick, dense, and dramatic. This is the "big fur" --- the fabric that creates the visual impact of a full fursuit body.

### What It Is Used For
- Fursuit bodies, legs, and arms (the primary use)
- Fursuit tails (especially for characters with fluffy tails: foxes, wolves, raccoons)
- Manes and ruffs
- Large plush animals where a shaggy appearance is desired

### How to Identify It
Run your hand across the fabric. The pile should be at least 2 inches long --- long enough to completely hide your fingers when you press them into the pile. The backing is typically a sturdy woven or knitted base. When you fold the fabric, the pile should fall naturally to one side (the nap direction). High-quality long pile has a consistent pile length and density; low-quality long pile has uneven tufts, thin spots, or a visibly cheap backing.

### How to Cut It
**Always cut from the back.** Place the fabric pile-side-down, trace your pattern on the backing, and cut through the backing only using small, sharp scissors (embroidery scissors) or a craft knife. Never use large fabric shears --- they will cut through the pile and create visible trim lines and bald edges. Part the pile along your cut line as you go, letting the fibers fall to either side of the cut.

### How to Sew It
Long pile is sewn with the pile tucked inward. Push the pile away from the seam line as you sew to prevent pile getting caught in the stitches. After sewing, use a pin or needle to pull trapped pile fibers out of the seam from the right side. A sewing machine handles long pile well; use a walking foot if available to prevent the layers from shifting. For hand sewing, use a sturdy needle (tapestry or leather needle) and heavy-duty thread. Seam allowance: 3/8 to 1/2 inch.

### Where to Buy It
- **Specialty retailers:** FurSuit Supplies, BigZ Fabric, Mendels Far Out Fabrics (San Francisco)
- **Fabric stores:** Joann Fabrics carries "Luxury Shag" and similar lines, though the color selection is more limited than specialty retailers
- **Online:** Fabric.com, Etsy (search "faux fur long pile")
- **International:** Howl Fabric (UK), NFT (National Faux Taxidermy, UK)

### Price Range
$15-35 per yard, depending on quality and pile density. High-quality long pile from specialty retailers is at the upper end. Craft-store shag is at the lower end. Budget $20-25/yard for a good all-purpose long pile.

## 2. Medium Pile (1-2 inches)

### What It Is
Faux fur with pile length between 1 and 2 inches. The versatile middle ground --- shorter than shag, longer than minky. This is the most commonly used pile length across all fursuit construction.

### What It Is Used For
- Fursuit heads, hands/paws, feet/paw boots
- Fursuit bodies for characters with shorter fur (cats, ferrets, otters)
- Partial fursuits (head and paws without a bodysuit)
- Plush animals (mid-size to large)
- Tails, ears, and accent pieces

### How to Identify It
The pile is long enough to brush in a visible direction but short enough to control. You can see the backing through the pile if you push the fibers aside. The nap direction is obvious when you run your hand across the surface.

### How to Cut It
Same technique as long pile: cut from the back with small sharp scissors, cutting only the backing. Slightly more forgiving than long pile because the shorter fibers are less likely to get caught in the scissors.

### How to Sew It
Similar to long pile but easier. The shorter pile produces less bulk in seams. Tuck pile away from the seam allowance before sewing. Pull trapped pile out of seams after sewing. Both hand and machine sewing work well. Seam allowance: 1/4 to 3/8 inch.

### Where to Buy It
Same sources as long pile. Medium pile has the widest color selection of any pile length. Joann Fabrics and other craft stores typically carry a reasonable selection.

### Price Range
$12-30 per yard. Generally slightly less expensive than long pile due to lower material volume.

## 3. Short Pile (Under 1 inch)

### What It Is
Faux fur with pile length under 1 inch. Smooth, sleek, and detail-friendly. This is the precision fabric --- used where fine detail matters more than fluffiness.

### What It Is Used For
- Fursuit faces and muzzles (where expression detail must read clearly)
- Inner ears
- Paw pads (as an alternative to fleece/minky)
- Small detail areas (markings, eyebrow patches, cheek patches)
- Small plush animals where a clean, defined shape is important
- Kemono-style fursuits (which use short pile extensively for their smooth aesthetic)

### How to Identify It
The pile is barely long enough to show a direction. The surface looks and feels more like velvet than like fur. When you fold the fabric, the fold line is sharp and clean, not obscured by long pile. The backing is visible with minimal effort.

### How to Cut It
Can be cut from either the front or back, as the short pile is less prone to trim-line damage. Standard fabric scissors work if you cut carefully. Rotary cutters with a cutting mat produce the cleanest edges. Still best to cut from the back with small scissors for maximum control.

### How to Sew It
The easiest faux fur to sew. Minimal bulk, clean seam lines, no pile management needed during sewing. Works well with both hand and machine sewing. Standard sewing machine foot is sufficient. Seam allowance: 1/4 inch.

### Where to Buy It
Same sources as other faux furs. Short pile is also available from general fabric retailers because it overlaps with upholstery and craft fabric categories.

### Price Range
$10-25 per yard.

## 4. Minky / Cuddle (Ultra-Short Pile)

### What It Is
A plush fabric with an extremely short, dense, velvety pile --- typically under 1/4 inch. "Minky" and "Cuddle" are trade names (Cuddle is Shannon Fabrics' brand; minky is used generically). The surface is ultra-soft to the touch, smooth, and slightly stretchy.

### What It Is Used For
- Plush animals (the primary fabric for modern commercially-made plush toys and high-end handmade plush)
- Fursuit paw pads (the most common pad material in the community)
- Fursuit inner ears
- Baby blankets and comfort items
- Plush accessories (tails, scarves, hats)

### How to Identify It
The surface feels like petting a chinchilla --- impossibly soft, with a subtle sheen. The pile is so short that the nap direction is barely visible; you detect it by feel (stroke in both directions and notice which way feels smoother). The fabric is stretchy in one or both directions. The backing is a knit (not woven), which gives it its stretch.

### How to Cut It
Standard fabric scissors. Minky does not shed pile the way longer faux fur does, so cutting from the front is fine. However, it is slippery --- pin carefully and cut slowly. A rotary cutter on a self-healing mat provides the best control.

### How to Sew It
Minky is notoriously slippery. The smooth surface causes layers to shift during sewing. Tips:
- Pin densely (every 1-2 inches)
- Use a walking foot on a sewing machine
- Sew slowly
- If hand sewing, use a backstitch (running stitch will not hold in the stretchy fabric)
- Seam allowance: 3/8 inch (the stretch can pull narrow seams apart)

### Where to Buy It
- **Shannon Fabrics** (the major manufacturer): Available through fabric stores and online
- **Joann Fabrics:** Carries Shannon Cuddle under the "Cuddle" brand name
- **Fabric.com, Etsy, Amazon:** Wide selection
- **Note:** Quality varies dramatically. Cheap minky pills and mats after washing. Shannon Cuddle is the industry standard for a reason.

### Price Range
$12-22 per yard. Shannon Cuddle is typically $15-20/yard. Budget minky from generic suppliers can be $8-12/yard but lower quality.

## 5. Fleece (No Pile)

### What It Is
A soft, synthetic knit fabric with no pile. Made from polyester, fleece is warm, lightweight, does not fray, and is extremely easy to sew. It is the most beginner-friendly fabric in this guide.

### What It Is Used For
- Fursuit paw pads (second most common pad material after minky)
- Fursuit inner ears
- Fursuit mouths and gum lines
- Plush animals (especially beginner projects --- see [First Plush Animal try session](../try-sessions/first-plush-animal.md))
- Anti-pill fleece is preferred for wearable items; standard fleece for non-wearable plush

### How to Identify It
Fleece has a slightly fuzzy surface but no distinct pile direction. It stretches more in one direction than the other (cross-grain stretch). When you stretch it, it does not snap back like minky does --- it has a soft, gentle stretch. It comes in solid colors and prints. Anti-pill fleece has a smoother surface than standard fleece.

### How to Cut It
The easiest fabric to cut in this guide. Standard scissors, rotary cutter, or even craft scissors work. No nap direction to worry about. Does not fray, so raw edges are not a concern.

### How to Sew It
The easiest fabric to sew in this guide. No fraying, no shifting, no pile management. Any stitch works. Any needle works. Sewing machines handle it effortlessly. Hand sewing is smooth and predictable. Seam allowance: 1/4 inch.

### Where to Buy It
Everywhere. Joann, Walmart, Hobby Lobby, online retailers. Fleece is one of the most commonly stocked fabrics in any fabric store.

### Price Range
$4-12 per yard. One of the most affordable fabrics. Anti-pill fleece is at the upper end; standard fleece at the lower end.

## 6. Felt (Stiff, No Stretch)

### What It Is
A non-woven fabric made by compressing and matting fibers together (typically wool, polyester, or a blend). Felt has no grain, no stretch, no fray, and holds its shape rigidly.

### What It Is Used For
- Applique details (eyes, noses, teeth, decorative markings on plush)
- Craft plush for display (not for heavily handled items --- felt pills and wears)
- Stiffening elements inside fursuit components
- Prototyping pattern pieces (felt is cheap and holds shape, making it good for test cuts)
- Not recommended for functional fursuit parts or heavily handled plush

### How to Identify It
Stiff, dense, and will not stretch in any direction. Available in sheets (typically 9x12 inches at craft stores) and by the yard. Craft felt (acrylic/polyester) is thin and inexpensive. Wool felt is thicker, denser, and more expensive. Wool-blend felt is the best balance of quality and cost.

### How to Cut It
Scissors or rotary cutter. Felt cuts cleanly with no fraying. Complex shapes (small circles, intricate outlines) are easier to cut from felt than from any other fabric.

### How to Sew It
Felt sews easily but does not stretch, which limits its use for three-dimensional shapes. Best joined with a blanket stitch (for visible decorative seams) or a whip stitch. Hot glue also works well. Seam allowance: minimal (1/8 inch) because felt does not fray.

### Where to Buy It
Craft stores (Joann, Hobby Lobby, Michaels), dollar stores, Amazon. Wool felt is available from specialty retailers (Benzie Design, National Nonwovens).

### Price Range
Craft felt sheets: $0.25-0.50 each. Craft felt by the yard: $3-6/yard. Wool felt: $10-25/yard. Wool-blend felt: $8-15/yard.

## 7. Buckram (Stiff Mesh)

### What It Is
A stiff, open-weave mesh fabric traditionally used in millinery (hat making) and now widely used in fursuit construction for eye and vision systems. Buckram is translucent from the inside but opaque from the outside when painted or covered, making it ideal for see-through structures.

### What It Is Used For
- **Fursuit eyes:** The dominant method for fursuit follow-me eyes. Buckram is shaped into eye domes, painted with the eye design, and mounted in the head. The performer sees through the mesh from inside while the audience sees the painted eye from outside.
- Fursuit mouth screens (sometimes, for ventilation that is hidden behind the mouth opening)
- Structural reinforcement in fursuit heads

### How to Identify It
Buckram is stiff enough to hold a shape when molded, with a visible grid/mesh structure. It crinkles when flexed. When held up to light, you can see through it. When painted with acrylic and viewed at arm's length, the mesh becomes invisible and only the paint is seen.

### How to Cut It
Scissors. Buckram cuts cleanly. It can also be heat-shaped (using a heat gun or by steaming) into curved eye shapes.

### How to Sew It
Buckram is more often glued or wired than sewn. Hot glue bonds well to buckram. If sewing, use a sharp needle and expect some resistance from the stiff weave. Buckram is not typically used in seam construction --- it is a structural component mounted inside a fursuit head.

### Where to Buy It
- **Millinery supply shops** (the original source)
- **Craft stores** (Joann carries buckram in the interfacing section)
- **Online:** Amazon, Etsy (search "buckram for fursuit eyes")
- **Fursuit-specific suppliers:** FurSuit Supplies

### Price Range
$5-12 per yard. A single yard is enough for dozens of fursuit eyes.

## 8. Foam (Not Fabric, But Essential)

### What It Is
Open-cell polyurethane foam --- upholstery foam, specifically. This is not a fabric but it is so fundamental to fursuit construction that no materials guide is complete without it. Foam is the structural material from which fursuit heads, feet, and body padding are carved.

### What It Is Used For
- **Fursuit head base:** The head starts as a block or sheet of foam, carved into the shape of the character's head. The foam provides structure, shape, and the surface over which fur is glued.
- **Digitigrade padding:** The leg padding that creates the illusion of animal-like leg structure
- **Fursuit feet/paw boots:** Foam shapes the toe box and foot structure
- **Body padding:** Chest, hip, and shoulder shaping for character silhouettes

### How to Identify It
Squeeze it. Open-cell foam compresses and springs back. It is lightweight, breathable (air passes through it, which matters for fursuit ventilation), and can be carved with scissors, a knife, or an electric carving knife. Upholstery foam comes in various densities; medium density (1.5-2.0 lb/cu ft) is most commonly used for fursuit construction.

### How to Cut It
Electric carving knife (the kind used for Thanksgiving turkey) is the preferred tool --- it produces clean, smooth cuts. Scissors work for thin sheets. A serrated bread knife works for rough shaping. Hot wire cutters work but produce toxic fumes (do not use without ventilation and respiratory protection).

### How to Sew It
You do not sew foam. You glue it. Contact cement (Barge cement is the fursuit community standard), hot glue, or spray adhesive are used to bond foam pieces together and to bond fur fabric to foam surfaces.

### Where to Buy It
- **Upholstery suppliers:** The best source for consistent quality and specific densities
- **Craft stores:** Joann and Hobby Lobby carry foam sheets and blocks, though selection is limited
- **Walmart/Target:** Thin foam sheets in the craft section
- **Home improvement stores:** Upholstery foam in the fabric/craft section
- **Online:** Amazon, Foam Factory, FurSuit Supplies

### Price Range
$5-15 per sheet (varies enormously by size and density). A 2-inch-thick sheet of medium-density foam, approximately 24x72 inches, costs $15-30 and is enough for one fursuit head with material to spare.

## 9. Interfacing (Fusible, Structural Support)

### What It Is
A stabilizing material that is fused to the back of fabric using heat (an iron). Interfacing adds stiffness, structure, and body to fabric that is otherwise too soft or stretchy for a particular application.

### What It Is Used For
- Stiffening fursuit ear tips so they stand upright
- Reinforcing fursuit paw pads so they hold their shape
- Adding structure to plush ears, tails, and other elements that need to maintain a shape
- Backing fabric before applique work
- Preventing stretchy fabrics (minky, knit-backed fur) from distorting during sewing

### How to Identify It
Fusible interfacing has a rough, slightly glossy side (the adhesive side) and a smooth side. When held up, it is translucent. It comes in light, medium, and heavy weights. For fursuit and plush work, medium-weight is the most versatile.

### How to Cut It
Scissors. Cut to the same shape as the fabric piece it will reinforce, minus the seam allowance (the interfacing should not extend into the seam).

### How to Apply It
Place the adhesive (rough/glossy) side against the wrong side of your fabric. Cover with a press cloth. Apply a hot iron (medium heat, no steam) for 10-15 seconds per area. Let cool. The interfacing is now permanently bonded to the fabric.

**Important:** Test on a scrap first. Some faux furs and minkies are heat-sensitive and will melt or flatten under a hot iron. Use a lower heat setting or a press cloth for delicate fabrics. For heat-sensitive fabrics, sew-in interfacing (non-fusible) is an alternative --- it is stitched into the seam rather than fused.

### Where to Buy It
Fabric stores (in the interfacing/stabilizer section), online retailers. Pellon is the dominant brand.

### Price Range
$3-8 per yard. Inexpensive and essential.

## 10. Elastic / Lycra / Spandex (Stretch Base)

### What It Is
Highly elastic fabrics used as the base layer for bodysuit-style fursuits. These fabrics stretch in two or four directions, providing a skin-tight fit that moves with the performer's body.

### What It Is Used For
- **Fursuit bodysuit base:** A lycra/spandex bodysuit (often a pre-made dance leotard or zentai suit) serves as the foundation onto which fur is attached for full bodysuits
- **Fursuit facings:** The inner face of a fursuit head, against the performer's skin
- **Stretch panels:** In areas where the fursuit needs to accommodate a range of motion (armpits, crotch, backs of knees)
- **Plush doll clothing:** When making dressed plush characters

### How to Identify It
Stretches dramatically in at least one direction (two-way stretch) or in all directions (four-way stretch). Snaps back to its original shape when released. The fabric is thin, smooth, and lightweight.

### How to Cut It
Rotary cutter on a self-healing mat for best results. The fabric shifts and curls when cut with scissors. Pin to a cutting mat or use pattern weights.

### How to Sew It
Requires a stretch stitch (zigzag stitch on a standard sewing machine, or a serger/overlock machine for professional results). Straight stitches will pop when the fabric stretches. Use a ballpoint needle (designed for knit fabrics --- the rounded tip slides between fibers rather than piercing them, preventing runs). Seam allowance: 3/8 inch.

### Where to Buy It
- **Dance/costume supply retailers:** The best source for bodysuit-weight lycra
- **Fabric stores:** Joann carries spandex/lycra in limited colors
- **Spandex World, Spandex House:** Specialty retailers with wide color and weight selection
- **Online:** Amazon, Etsy

### Price Range
$8-20 per yard. Specialty dance-weight lycra is at the upper end.

## Quiz: Which Fabric Would You Use?

Test your understanding. Select the best fabric for each application. Answers follow.

**1.** You are building the body of a wolf fursuit. The character has thick, fluffy fur. You want maximum visual impact.

**2.** You are making paw pads for a cat fursuit. The pads should be smooth, soft, and slightly puffy.

**3.** You are sewing a beginner stuffed animal with a friend who has never sewn before. You want the easiest possible fabric.

**4.** You need to create follow-me eyes for a fursuit head. The performer needs to see through the eyes from inside.

**5.** You are making a plush cat and want to applique a small heart shape onto its chest. The heart needs to have clean, non-fraying edges and hold its shape.

**6.** You are carving the base shape of a fursuit head. You need a material that can be carved into a three-dimensional shape and that fur can be glued to.

**7.** You are sewing the face of a kemono-style fursuit head. The style calls for a smooth, sleek surface that shows clean lines and subtle airbrushed details.

**8.** You are making ear tips for a fursuit and they keep flopping over. You need to add internal stiffness without adding visible bulk.

**9.** You are building a full plantigrade fursuit and need a skin-tight base layer to attach the fur panels to.

**10.** You are sewing a large, huggable plush bear and want the softest possible surface texture. The fabric should feel luxurious when squeezed.

## Quiz Answers

1. **Long pile shag (2-4")** --- The thick, dramatic pile creates the fluffy wolf aesthetic. Medium pile could work for a shorter-furred wolf, but "thick, fluffy, maximum impact" is long pile territory.

2. **Minky/Cuddle** --- The ultra-soft, smooth surface is the community standard for paw pads. Fleece is the runner-up. Short pile fur would also work but is less commonly used for pads.

3. **Fleece** --- No pile to manage, no fraying, no shifting, easy to cut, easy to sew. The most forgiving fabric for a beginner.

4. **Buckram** --- The stiff mesh that is translucent from inside (for performer vision) and opaque when painted from outside (for the audience). This is the standard fursuit eye material.

5. **Felt** --- No fray, stiff, holds shape, cuts cleanly in small complex shapes. Perfect for applique on plush.

6. **Foam (open-cell upholstery foam)** --- Not a fabric, but the standard structural material for fursuit heads. Carved with an electric knife, shaped with scissors, glued with contact cement.

7. **Short pile faux fur (<1")** --- The smooth, sleek surface shows airbrushed details and clean lines. Kemono style relies on short pile for its distinctive aesthetic.

8. **Fusible interfacing (medium weight)** --- Fused to the back of the ear fabric, it adds stiffness without bulk or visibility. The ear stands up but still looks and feels like fabric.

9. **Elastic / Lycra / Spandex** --- A four-way stretch fabric that moves with the body and provides a smooth foundation for fur panel attachment.

10. **Minky/Cuddle** --- The softest surface texture of any fabric in this guide. The ultra-short, dense pile creates the luxurious squeeze-feel that defines premium plush.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [Materials and Making]: Every fabric in this guide is a medium with its own visual and tactile properties. Selecting the right fabric for a project is an artistic decision --- pile length affects silhouette, fabric drape affects form, surface texture affects how light interacts with the piece. Material literacy is aesthetic literacy.
- **Trades** --- [Textile Identification]: The ability to identify fabrics by sight and touch --- recognizing pile length, backing construction, stretch characteristics, nap direction, and fiber content --- is a trade skill with applications far beyond fursuit and plush making. It is the same skill used by upholsterers, tailors, costume designers, and textile buyers.
- **Materials** --- [Material Science]: Faux fur, minky, fleece, buckram, and foam are all engineered materials with specific properties determined by their manufacturing process. Understanding why open-cell foam breathes, why buckram holds a shape, why minky is slippery, and why fleece does not fray connects craft practice to materials science --- the study of how a material's structure determines its behavior.
