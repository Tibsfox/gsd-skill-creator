# Building a Fursuit Head --- From Foam to Character

## The First Cut

There is a moment in every fursuit head build where the maker holds a block of upholstery foam, looks at their character reference sheet, and thinks: somehow, this becomes that. The foam is an inert, vanilla-colored slab. The character is a fox with expressive eyes, a tapered muzzle, and ears that suggest alertness and personality. Between the two states lie forty to eighty hours of cutting, gluing, carving, shaping, patterning, sewing, and detailing --- the physical craft of turning flat, dead material into a three-dimensional face with character.

This document walks through the complete process of building a foam-based fursuit head. It is not the only method --- 3D printing, resin casting, and hybrid techniques are covered in [CRAFT-SUIT Section 1] --- but foam carving remains the most accessible, most forgiving, and most widely practiced head construction method in the fursuit community. It requires no digital design skills, no expensive equipment, and no prior experience beyond a willingness to cut, fail, learn, and cut again.

Your first head will be rough. That is normal. That is expected. The fifth one will be good. The craft is in the iteration.

## Materials and Tools

Assemble everything before you begin. A fursuit head build has long drying times (contact cement, hot glue), and interrupting the process to go buy supplies produces bad results.

### Core Materials

**Upholstery foam** --- The structural material. Open-cell polyurethane foam in two thicknesses:

- **1/2-inch (12mm) sheets** --- Used for the outer shell, ear blanks, fine detail work, and wrapping over structural foam to create smooth surfaces. Available in large sheets from upholstery supply stores or online. Choose medium-firm density.
- **2-inch (50mm) blocks** --- Used for the muzzle, cheek builds, brow ridge, and any area that requires significant volume. Some builds require 3-inch blocks for very large muzzles (wolves, dragons, mascot-scale heads).

**Contact cement** --- The primary structural adhesive. Barge All-Purpose Cement is the community standard. DAP Weldwood Contact Cement (the original, not the water-based version) is the alternative. Contact cement creates a permanent, flexible bond between foam surfaces that will not peel apart under stress.

> **Safety:** Contact cement contains volatile organic compounds --- toluene, hexane, naphtha. These are neurotoxic and flammable. Work outdoors or in a space with active exhaust ventilation. Wear an organic vapor respirator (3M 6001 cartridge or equivalent). An N95 dust mask does NOT filter organic vapors. Keep away from heat, flame, and sparks. Allow bonded pieces to off-gas for 24 hours before wearing.

**Hot glue gun** --- Secondary adhesive for tacking, temporary positioning, and small reinforcements. Use a low-temperature gun for foam (high-temp can melt thin foam). Hot glue is faster than contact cement but creates a rigid, brittle bond that can crack under stress. Use it for temporary positioning during the build process, then reinforce with contact cement where structural integrity is needed.

**Faux fur** --- The outer covering. Choose pile length appropriate to the character species (short pile for cats, medium for canines, long for foxes/huskies). Budget at minimum 1/2 yard for a standard head. Buy extra --- you will make cutting errors, and dye lots vary. See [CRAFT-SEW] for detailed fur selection guidance.

**Base** --- What the foam sits on. Two options:

- **Balaclava base:** A fabric balaclava provides a lightweight, breathable, close-fitting foundation. Cheapest and simplest option. Less durable, less rigid. Good for first builds.
- **Hard hat base:** A construction hard hat (with the internal suspension system) provides a rigid, adjustable, well-ventilated foundation. More durable, maintains shape better, distributes weight across the head rather than pressing on the skull. Standard choice for higher-quality builds.

**Follow-me eyes** --- The character's eyes. Constructed from concave hemispheres (resin, vacuum-formed plastic, or 3D printed) with the pupil opening on the concave side. The concavity creates a parallax illusion --- the eyes appear to follow the viewer from any angle. You see outward through the pupil opening, which also provides ventilation. Follow-me eyes can be purchased from fursuit supply shops or made at home with resin casting or 3D printing.

### Tools

- **Electric carving knife** --- The primary shaping tool. A serrated electric knife (the kind used for carving turkey) cuts foam cleanly and quickly. It is dramatically superior to scissors or utility knives for bulk shaping.
- **Sharp scissors** --- For detail cuts, trimming, and fur cutting. Fabric shears for fur (cut from the backing only, never through the pile).
- **Heat gun** --- For smoothing carved foam surfaces. Passing a heat gun over carved foam briefly melts and firms the surface, eliminating rough texture and sealing loose cells. Work in passes --- too much heat melts the foam into lumpy goo. Practice on scrap first.
- **Duct tape** --- For the patterning process (see below).
- **Plastic wrap** (cling film) --- For the patterning process.
- **Permanent marker** --- For marking pattern lines on duct tape.
- **Pins** --- Straight pins for positioning fur before sewing.
- **Needle and thread** --- Heavy-duty needle and upholstery thread for hand-stitching fur seams. A sewing machine with a walking foot handles straight seams; complex curves and tight spaces require hand-stitching.
- **Awl or crochet hook** --- For pulling trapped fur pile out of seams after sewing.

## Step-by-Step Construction

### Step 1: Measurements

Measure your head. You need three numbers:

1. **Circumference** --- Wrap a tape measure around your head at the widest point (forehead to occiput). Typical range: 22-24 inches.
2. **Chin to crown** --- From the bottom of your chin, over the top of your head, to the nape of your neck. Typical range: 24-27 inches.
3. **Ear to ear over top** --- From one ear, over the crown, to the other ear. Typical range: 13-15 inches.

These measurements determine the size of your base and the amount of foam buildup required. A head that is too tight is unwearable. A head that is too large wobbles and shifts, misaligning the vision openings.

### Step 2: Prepare the Base

If using a balaclava: put it on, ensure it sits comfortably, and mark the eye positions with a marker while wearing it. If using a hard hat: adjust the suspension system to fit your head securely. The hard hat should sit level and not rock forward or backward.

Place the base on a wig head or foam mannequin head for the build process. You will be building foam up on the base, and you need it stable and at working height. A wig head duct-taped to a PVC pipe in a weighted bucket is the standard workshop setup.

### Step 3: Build the Basic Shape

This is where the character begins to emerge from the foam. You are building the underlying skull structure --- the muzzle, the cheeks, the brow ridge, the cranium profile --- using 2-inch foam blocks bonded with contact cement.

**The process:**

1. Reference your character design. Identify the key proportions: how long is the muzzle relative to the cranium? How wide are the cheeks? How prominent is the brow ridge? Where do the eyes sit?

2. Cut 2-inch foam blocks to rough dimensions. Do not try to shape them precisely at this stage. Cut blocks slightly larger than you need --- you will carve away excess, and it is much easier to remove foam than to add it.

3. Apply contact cement to both surfaces to be bonded. Spread a thin, even coat with the brush or applicator. Wait until the cement is tacky --- it should feel sticky to the touch but not wet, typically 3-5 minutes depending on temperature and humidity. Then press the surfaces together firmly. Contact cement bonds on contact and cannot be repositioned once the surfaces touch. Get it right the first time.

4. Build the muzzle first. Stack and glue foam blocks on the front of the base, extending forward to create muzzle length. The muzzle is the defining feature of most fursuit heads --- too short and the character looks flat; too long and it becomes unwieldy and blocks downward vision.

5. Build the cheeks. Add foam to the sides of the head, below and slightly behind the eye positions. Cheeks provide the head's width and contribute to species identity --- broad cheeks for bears, narrow for foxes, rounded for cats.

6. Build the brow ridge. Add foam above the eyes to create the ridge that gives the face its expression. A heavy brow reads as serious or stern. A lighter brow reads as friendly or surprised. The brow ridge also creates the hood over the eye openings that shades the eyes and prevents the wearer's face from being visible.

7. Build up the cranium as needed to smooth the profile from brow ridge to the back of the head.

At this stage, the head looks like a rough, blocky approximation of the character. That is exactly right. Do not try to refine the shape with block-building. Refinement comes from carving.

### Step 4: Carve

The electric carving knife is your primary tool. Hold the head at arm's length, compare to your reference, and begin removing foam.

**Carving principles:**

- **Work slowly.** You can always cut more. You cannot put foam back (easily). Make multiple light passes rather than one aggressive cut.
- **Rotate constantly.** Check the head from every angle --- front, side, three-quarter, top, bottom. It is easy to get the front profile right while leaving the sides lumpy, or to get the profile right while making the head asymmetric from the front.
- **Symmetry is the hardest part.** Measure both sides. Use a ruler. Mark centerlines with a marker. Your first head will not be perfectly symmetric. That is normal. Perfecting symmetry comes with practice.
- **Carve the cheekbones.** The zygomatic arch --- the cheekbone ridge --- is the feature that gives the most three-dimensional character to a fursuit face. Carve a ridge from the outer corner of the eye socket back toward the ear opening. Below the ridge, carve inward to define the hollow of the cheek.
- **Shape the jawline.** The transition from cheek to jaw to chin defines the lower face. Carve a clean jawline that tapers from the widest point of the cheeks down to the chin.
- **Taper the muzzle.** Most animal muzzles narrow toward the nose. Carve the sides of the muzzle inward from cheek to nose tip, maintaining symmetry. The bridge of the nose --- the flat or slightly convex surface on top of the muzzle --- should be smooth and continuous from brow ridge to nose.
- **Check from below.** The underside of the muzzle is visible when people look at you, and it is the area most often neglected in carving. The under-jaw and chin area should be smooth and shaped, not a flat, unfinished block.

### Step 5: Heat Gun Finishing

After carving, the foam surface is rough --- covered in the serrated marks left by the electric knife. A heat gun smooths these marks and firms the surface.

Hold the heat gun 6-8 inches from the foam surface and move in steady passes. The surface will visibly smooth and slightly contract. Do not hold the gun in one place --- foam melts quickly, and a momentary lapse of attention produces a lumpy depression that is difficult to fix.

Heat gun finishing also slightly hardens the foam surface, which helps the head hold its shape under the compression of fur covering and the stress of wearing.

### Step 6: Tape Patterning

This is the step that translates a three-dimensional foam sculpture into flat pattern pieces for cutting fur. The technique is borrowed from the garment industry's draping method, adapted for irregular sculptural surfaces.

1. Wrap the entire carved foam head in plastic wrap (cling film). Press it into every contour --- muzzle, cheeks, brow, ears, jaw. The plastic wrap prevents the duct tape from bonding permanently to the foam.

2. Cover the plastic wrap with duct tape. Apply strips smoothly, overlapping slightly, conforming to the shape. Press the tape into the contours. Add extra layers over complex curves (muzzle tip, eye area, ear base) where you will need structural integrity in the pattern.

3. Using a permanent marker, draw seam lines on the duct tape surface. These lines define where you will cut the fur covering into separate pieces. Seam placement is a design decision:
   - Place seams where they follow natural feature lines (the centerline of the muzzle, the brow ridge, the jaw line).
   - Place seams where they will be hidden by long pile or by detail work (inside ear bases, under the chin).
   - Place seams where color changes occur (if the character has different colored areas, each area is a separate pattern piece).
   - Avoid placing seams on the high point of a curve (the top of the muzzle, the crown of the head) where they will be most visible.

4. Number or label each section on both sides of every seam line (so you know which pieces adjoin after cutting).

5. Carefully cut the duct tape shell off the head along the seam lines. Use scissors, not a knife (to avoid cutting the foam underneath the plastic wrap).

6. Flatten the cut pieces. Each piece is now a flat pattern template for a corresponding fur piece. Transfer the patterns to paper or card stock, adding 1/4-inch seam allowance on all edges.

### Step 7: Cut and Sew Fur

Using the pattern pieces from Step 6:

1. **Mark nap direction.** On every pattern piece, draw an arrow indicating the direction the fur pile should run. Generally, fur runs from forehead toward nose on the muzzle, from crown toward ears on the top, and from jaw toward chest on the cheeks and chin. Species-specific nap direction matters --- reference photos of the actual animal.

2. **Lay patterns on fur backing.** Place patterns on the BACK (wrong side) of the fur fabric. Trace with marker. Align the nap arrows with the fur pile direction.

3. **Cut from the backing only.** Use sharp scissors or a rotary cutter. Cut ONLY through the backing fabric, NOT through the fur pile. Slide the scissor blade under the pile and cut the backing in small snips. If you cut through the pile, you get a visible shaved line along the seam that cannot be fixed.

4. **Sew the pieces together.** Pin pieces right-sides together. Sew with a 1/4-inch seam allowance. For machine sewing, use a walking foot and a long stitch length (3.5-4mm) --- the walking foot prevents the fur from feeding unevenly, and the long stitch length prevents the thread from binding in the pile. For hand sewing, use a ladder stitch (also called a slip stitch or mattress stitch) --- this stitch is nearly invisible and produces a flat, seamless-looking join in fur.

5. **Pull out trapped pile.** After sewing, fur pile gets trapped in the seams. Using a pin, crochet hook, or awl, carefully pull the trapped pile out through the seam line from the right side. This is tedious. It is also the single most important finishing step. Un-picked seams show as visible bald lines. Properly picked seams disappear into the pile.

### Step 8: Apply Fur to Foam

Stretch the sewn fur shell over the carved foam head. This is a fitting process --- the fur must conform to every contour without excessive bunching or pulling.

1. Position the fur shell on the head, aligning the centerline and eye openings.
2. Use hot glue to tack the fur in place at key anchor points --- the nose tip, the brow ridge, behind the ears, and along the jawline.
3. Working from the anchor points outward, stretch and smooth the fur over the foam. Use hot glue for temporary positioning and contact cement for permanent adhesion. Fold and tuck excess fur at concave transitions (the junction of muzzle and cheek, the inner corner of the eye, the ear base). Cut small darts in the fur if necessary to reduce bunching around tight curves.
4. The underside of the head (inside the jawline and neck opening) is finished last. Fold the raw edges of the fur under and glue them to the inside of the foam shell for a clean edge.

### Step 9: Install Follow-Me Eyes

1. **Carve eye sockets.** Using scissors or a small knife, carve out the eye areas in the foam to create shallow concavities that will seat the follow-me eye shells. The socket should be slightly larger than the eye piece to allow for adjustment.

2. **Set the angle.** Follow-me eyes work best when set at a slight inward angle --- the pupils should converge slightly toward the centerline, as in normal binocular vision. Eyes set perfectly parallel look vacant and unfocused. Eyes set at too steep an angle look cross-eyed. The sweet spot is subtle --- 2-3 degrees of inward convergence.

3. **Vision alignment.** You will be seeing out through the pupil opening. With the head on, have someone verify that your natural eye position aligns with the pupil openings and provides adequate forward vision. Adjust socket depth and eye position as needed. Vision is non-negotiable --- you must be able to see well enough to navigate safely.

4. **Secure the eyes.** Hot glue the eye shells into the sockets. Apply a ring of glue around the edge of the socket and press the eye into position. The glue should be invisible from the outside.

5. **Add eyelids and brow detailing.** Fur is trimmed and shaped around the eye openings to create eyelids. The upper eyelid is the primary expression tool --- lowered lids read as sleepy or smug, raised lids read as alert or surprised, asymmetric lids read as skeptical or playful. Build the lids from small pieces of fur glued over the foam brow ridge and sculpted with scissors.

### Step 10: Nose and Mouth Detailing

The nose and mouth are the character's final personality elements.

**Nose options:**
- **Sculpted fleece:** Cut and glue layers of anti-pill fleece into a nose shape. Lightweight, soft (safe for convention hugs), easy to shape with scissors and hot glue.
- **Resin nose:** Cast a nose from resin in a silicone mold. Hard, shiny, durable, can be painted to match any species. Heavier than fleece, and the hard surface requires smoothing the edges to prevent discomfort.
- **Foam clay nose:** Foam clay (Padico or similar) air-dries into a lightweight, semi-firm material that can be sanded, painted, and sealed. Good middle ground between fleece and resin.

**Mouth options:**
- **Closed mouth:** The simplest option. Fur covers the muzzle completely, with a seam line or glued detail marking the mouth line.
- **Open mouth:** Carve the jaw area to create a mouth opening. Line the interior with fabric (usually black or dark red fleece). Add a tongue (fleece-covered foam), teeth (sculpted from polymer clay, foam clay, or resin), and gums (pink fleece). An open mouth provides additional ventilation --- air flows in through the mouth opening, across the wearer's face, and out through gaps in the fur and foam.
- **Moving jaw:** A more advanced feature. The lower jaw is a separate foam piece attached to the head with elastic straps that connect to the wearer's chin. When the wearer opens their mouth, the elastic pulls the jaw down. When they close their mouth, the elastic returns the jaw to the closed position. Moving jaw mechanisms require careful engineering of the hinge point, the elastic tension, and the jaw weight to produce natural-looking movement. See [CRAFT-SUIT Section 7] for detailed moving jaw construction.

### Step 11: Ears

Ears are simpler than the head but still require attention to structure and expression.

1. **Cut foam ear blanks.** Two layers of 1/2-inch foam per ear, glued together, give a 1-inch-thick ear that is rigid enough to stand upright but flexible enough to survive compression in a bag.
2. **Shape the ears.** Carve to species-appropriate shape --- pointed for foxes and cats, rounded for bears and dogs, tall and narrow for rabbits.
3. **Cover in fur.** Cut fur for the outer ear (species-colored) and inner ear (contrasting color --- pink, cream, or lighter shade). Sew or glue the pieces together, then stretch over the foam blank.
4. **Attach to head.** Hot glue and hand-stitch the ears to the head at the appropriate position and angle. Ear position drastically affects expression --- ears forward read as alert, ears back read as aggressive or scared, ears to the side read as relaxed. Choose the position that matches the character's personality.

## Time, Cost, and Expectations

A first-time fursuit head build takes 40-80 hours spread over several weeks (drying times for contact cement and paint require overnight pauses). Experienced makers can build a head in 20-30 hours.

**Material costs for a foam-based head:**

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Upholstery foam (2" block + 1/2" sheets) | $15-$30 |
| Contact cement (1 quart) | $15-$25 |
| Hot glue sticks (bag) | $5-$10 |
| Faux fur (1/2-1 yard) | $20-$50 |
| Balaclava or hard hat base | $10-$25 |
| Follow-me eyes (pair, purchased) | $15-$40 |
| Fleece, foam clay, misc. detailing | $10-$20 |
| Tools (if not owned): electric knife, heat gun, scissors | $30-$60 |
| **Total (first build, including tools)** | **$120-$260** |
| **Total (subsequent builds, tools owned)** | **$90-$200** |

### The Learning Curve

Your first head will have visible seams. The muzzle will be slightly asymmetric. The fur coverage will bunch in one spot and pull in another. The eyes may not align perfectly. The ears will be a little crooked.

This is the normal, expected outcome of a first attempt at a complex craft. The makers whose heads sell for $2,000-$5,000 have built dozens or hundreds of heads. They developed their techniques over years. They made every mistake you will make, and then they made it again, and then they learned how to not make it.

The path from first head to good head is:

- **Head 1:** Learn the basic process. Make every mistake. Finish it even though it is rough. Wear it. Learn what works and what does not work from the wearing experience --- vision, ventilation, weight distribution, fit.
- **Head 2:** Better carving. Better symmetry. Better fur coverage. Still imperfect.
- **Head 3:** Tape patterning becomes intuitive. Seam placement improves. Fur handling gets cleaner.
- **Head 4:** Expression work --- eyelids, brow shaping, ear positioning --- starts to convey genuine character.
- **Head 5:** This is typically when a maker's work starts to look professional. The cumulative learning from four previous builds produces a head with clean lines, good symmetry, expressive features, and the confidence that comes from knowing the materials.

The most common mistake in fursuit head construction is not technical. It is giving up after the first attempt because the result did not match the vision. The vision is always ahead of the skill. Close the gap by building the next one.
