# Choosing Faux Fur --- A Fabric Selection Guide for Fursuit and Plush Makers

## The Decision That Defines Everything

There is no single material decision in fursuit or plush making that matters more than the fur. The foam can be carved beautifully, the pattern can be engineered perfectly, the sewing can be flawless --- and if the fur is wrong, the finished piece looks wrong. Cheap fur looks cheap. The wrong pile length makes the species unrecognizable. A bad color match turns your client's character into someone else's. Fur that pills after three conventions makes the suit look five years old after six months.

This is the decision. Take your time with it. Order swatches. Compare them in person, not just on screen (monitors lie about color, and you cannot feel pile density through a photograph). Understand what you are buying and why. The $15/yard fabric and the $45/yard fabric are not interchangeable, and the difference between them shows in the finished product in ways that no amount of construction skill can compensate for.

This guide covers everything you need to know to make that decision well: pile types, backing types, the major suppliers, color matching, pile direction, fiber content, cutting technique, and how much to buy. It is written for fursuit makers, plush makers, and anyone working with faux fur at any scale, from a first head build to a professional studio operation.

## 1. Pile Types

Faux fur is classified by pile length --- the height of the fiber strands above the backing. Pile length is the single most important characteristic for species selection, because it determines the visual texture, the way light plays across the surface, and how the finished piece reads to the eye.

### Shag / Long Pile (2-4 inches)

Long pile fur has fibers 2 to 4 inches tall. It is dramatic, luxurious, and the most forgiving of construction imperfections --- the long fibers conceal seams, smooth over minor asymmetries, and create a lush visual surface.

**Best for:** Long-haired species --- foxes (especially tail and cheeks), huskies, wolves (mane and ruff areas), lions (mane), yetis, and any character design that calls for a shaggy, textured look. Also used for accent areas on mixed-pile builds (long pile on cheeks and tail, shorter pile on the body).

**Considerations:** Long pile is harder to sew because the fibers get caught in the machine feed dogs and tangle in the seam. Use a walking foot, increase stitch length to 4mm, and push the pile away from the seam line with your fingers or a seam ripper as you feed the fabric through the machine. Trimming and shaping long pile requires practice --- you are sculpting with scissors after the fur is applied, blending different pieces together, and creating the illusion of natural fur growth patterns. Long pile also traps more heat, which is a comfort consideration for fursuits.

**Weight:** Heavier per yard than shorter piles. A full fursuit in long pile shag weighs noticeably more than one in medium pile.

### Medium Pile (1-2 inches)

Medium pile is the workhorse of fursuit construction. Fibers 1 to 2 inches tall provide enough length to conceal seams and create a soft, plush surface while remaining manageable to sew and shape.

**Best for:** The majority of fursuit body coverage for most species. Canines (wolves, foxes, dogs), felines (cats, big cats), bears, rabbits, and most other common species work well in medium pile. Medium pile is versatile enough to be trimmed shorter in detail areas (around the eyes, on the muzzle tip) and left full-length on the body.

**Considerations:** Medium pile is the easiest to work with for beginners. It sews more smoothly than long pile, shapes more predictably, and the seam-hiding capability is good. Most fursuit construction tutorials assume medium pile fur.

### Short Pile (under 1 inch)

Short pile fur has fibers under 1 inch. It provides a sleek, smooth surface that shows the underlying shape of the foam or pattern more than longer piles. Construction imperfections are more visible in short pile because there is less fiber to conceal them.

**Best for:** Sleek species --- short-haired cats, dragons, reptilian characters, aquatic species (otters, seals), and any design that calls for a smooth, tight-coated look. Also used for accent areas on mixed-pile builds (short pile on the muzzle, inside the ears, on the paw pads).

**Considerations:** Short pile demands better construction quality because imperfections show. Seams must be tighter, fur application must be smoother, and symmetry must be more precise. Short pile is easier to sew than long pile (less tangling) but less forgiving of the results. Trimming is usually minimal --- the pile is already at its intended length.

### Minky / Cuddle (Ultra-Short, Plush Texture)

Minky (also branded as Cuddle by Shannon Fabrics) is an ultra-short pile (typically 3-10mm) knit fabric with an extremely soft, plush hand feel. It is not technically faux fur --- it lacks the distinct guard hairs and undercoat structure of fur fabric --- but it is widely used in plush making and for accent areas in fursuits.

**Best for:** Plush construction (the entire Squishmallow category is essentially minky), fursuit inner ears, belly panels, paw pads, muzzle insides, and any area where a smooth, soft, contrasting texture is desired. Minky comes in a vast range of solid colors, prints, and specialty textures (dimple dot, embossed, rose swirl).

**Considerations:** Minky is slippery to sew. It stretches and shifts under the presser foot. Use a walking foot, pin aggressively (perpendicular to the seam, every inch), and use a longer stitch length (3.5mm). Do not iron minky --- it melts. Minky does not conceal seams at all; every construction joint is visible. This is fine for plush (where visible seams are part of the aesthetic) but requires careful seam placement in fursuit applications.

## 2. Backing Types

The backing --- the base fabric to which the pile fibers are attached --- determines how the fur handles, stretches, and conforms to three-dimensional shapes.

### Knit Backing

Knit-backed fur has a stretchy, flexible backing that conforms to curved surfaces. When you pull the fabric diagonally, it stretches and recovers.

**Best for:** Fursuits. The stretch allows the fur to conform to foam sculpture contours without excessive bunching or puckering. When you stretch a knit-backed fur over a carved foam head, it hugs the muzzle, wraps around the cheeks, and conforms to the brow ridge. Knit backing is essentially mandatory for fursuit head and body construction.

**Considerations:** Knit backing can stretch unevenly if not handled carefully during cutting and sewing. Cut all pieces with the stretch going in the same direction (typically cross-grain) to prevent distortion. Pin generously before sewing to prevent shifting.

### Woven Backing

Woven-backed fur has a stable, non-stretch backing similar to a canvas or duck cloth. It holds its shape firmly and does not conform to curves without darting or easing.

**Best for:** Plush construction. The stability of woven backing holds the intended shape of pattern pieces --- a body panel stays the shape you cut it, the seam allowances lie flat, and the finished piece maintains its form through stuffing. Woven backing is easier to sew precisely because it does not shift or stretch.

**Considerations:** Woven-backed fur does not stretch over foam, which makes it unsuitable for most fursuit applications. Attempting to pull a woven-backed fur over a carved foam head produces bunching, wrinkles, and a poor fit. Reserve woven-backed fur for flat or gently curved applications, or for plush where the fabric is the structural shell.

## 3. Brands and Suppliers

### National Fiber Technology (NFT)

**What it is:** The gold standard for fursuit-grade faux fur.
**Location:** Manufacturing in the United States.
**Price range:** $30-$60+ per yard, depending on pile length and color.
**Where to buy:** Directly from NFT or through authorized fursuit supply retailers (Howl Fabric, FursuitSupplies.com).

National Fiber Technology produces the luxury faux fur that professional fursuit makers reach for when quality is the priority. NFT fur is dense (you cannot see the backing through the pile), lustrous (the fibers have a sheen that catches light beautifully), and consistent (the dye lots are more reliable than most suppliers, though even NFT varies batch to batch). The fiber is soft, the backing is knit (stretch-friendly), and the pile recovery after compression is excellent --- an NFT-furred suit that gets packed in a suitcase for a convention returns to its full pile height after a gentle brushing.

**The signature NFT product:** Luxury Shag, available in a range of pile lengths from short to very long. The longer shags (3-4 inch pile) are the standard for high-end fox and wolf cheek ruffs and tails. The medium piles (1.5-2 inch) are the standard for body coverage on professional-quality suits.

**Custom colors:** NFT offers custom color runs for volume orders. Professional studios that produce enough suits to warrant a custom order can get fur in exact character colors. For individual makers, the standard color range is extensive but not infinite --- you may need to supplement with airbrushed details for colors outside the standard palette.

**Why it costs what it costs:** NFT fur is denser, softer, more lustrous, and more consistent than budget alternatives. The difference between a suit made with NFT fur and one made with budget fur is visible from across a convention floor. The premium is real, and it shows in the finished product.

### Shannon Fabrics

**What it is:** The dominant manufacturer of Cuddle (minky) fabric and an increasingly significant player in faux fur.
**Location:** United States (designed in California, manufactured globally).
**Price range:** $15-$30 per yard for Cuddle/minky; $20-$40 per yard for faux fur lines.
**Where to buy:** JoAnn Fabrics (the largest retail source), Fabric.com, online fabric retailers.

Shannon Fabrics is most known for their Cuddle (minky) line, which dominates the plush-making market. But their Faux Fur collection has expanded significantly and now includes medium and long pile options in a range of colors. Shannon fur is widely available (you can walk into a JoAnn and buy it today, which is not true of NFT), reasonably priced, and consistent enough for hobbyist and mid-range professional use.

**Luxe Cuddle:** Shannon's premium minky line, available in a variety of textures (galaxy, frost, seal, hide) that mimic animal coats at ultra-short pile lengths. Excellent for plush, inner ears, belly panels, and accent work.

**The JoAnn factor:** The single biggest advantage of Shannon fur is availability. JoAnn Fabrics stocks Shannon products in most locations, and JoAnn runs sales constantly (40-50% off coupons are routine). At sale price, Shannon fur becomes an exceptional value. Always use a coupon.

### BigZ Fabric (Online)

**What it is:** An online fabric retailer with one of the widest selections of faux fur available anywhere.
**Location:** Online (bigzfabric.com).
**Price range:** $10-$30 per yard.
**Where to buy:** Direct from website.

BigZ carries a vast inventory of faux fur in dozens of colors, pile lengths, and textures. The quality ranges from budget to mid-range --- this is not NFT territory, but for hobbyist builds, first projects, and budget-conscious makers, BigZ provides acceptable fur at accessible prices. The color range is one of the widest available from any single source, which matters when you are trying to match a character design that calls for a very specific teal or a particular shade of rust.

**Note on quality:** BigZ fur tends to be less dense and less lustrous than NFT or premium Shannon products. The backing is thinner on some products. For a first build or a budget build, this is fine. For a commission where the client is paying $3,000+, it may not meet expectations.

### Distinctive Fabric (Online)

**What it is:** An online faux fur supplier popular in the fursuit community for mid-range quality at reasonable prices.
**Location:** Online (distinctivefabric.com).
**Price range:** $12-$25 per yard.
**Where to buy:** Direct from website.

Distinctive Fabric has built a following in the fursuit maker community for offering a decent range of colors and pile lengths at mid-range pricing. The quality is a step above BigZ for most products but below NFT. Distinctive is a good choice for builders who have outgrown budget fur but are not yet at the volume or price point where NFT makes sense for every build.

### Howl Fabric (Online)

**What it is:** A fursuit-community-oriented faux fur retailer that curates its selection specifically for fursuit makers.
**Location:** Online (howlfabric.com).
**Price range:** $15-$45 per yard.
**Where to buy:** Direct from website.

Howl Fabric differentiates itself by curating specifically for the fursuit market. Their selection is not the widest, but everything they stock is chosen for suitability to fursuit construction --- appropriate pile lengths, knit backings, colors popular in character designs. They also carry NFT fur, making them one of the few retailers where you can get NFT products without ordering directly from the manufacturer.

### Monterey Club Fabric

**What it is:** A source for long pile and specialty faux fur.
**Price range:** $15-$35 per yard.

Monterey Club Fabric carries long pile options that are useful for shag-heavy builds. The selection is more limited than BigZ but the quality of their long pile products is generally good for the price point.

## 4. Color Matching

Color matching is one of the most frustrating aspects of working with faux fur, and it deserves dedicated attention because the consequences of getting it wrong are expensive and demoralizing.

### Screens Lie

The color you see on your monitor is not the color of the fabric. Monitor calibration, ambient lighting, and the difference between backlit pixels and reflected fabric light all conspire to make on-screen color unreliable. NEVER commit to a large fabric order based on how the color looks on your screen.

### Dye Lots Vary

Faux fur is dyed in batches (dye lots), and the color varies between lots. A "teal" purchased in January may not match the "teal" purchased in June, even from the same supplier with the same product number. The variation can be subtle (barely noticeable side-by-side) or dramatic (clearly different colors under the same lighting).

**The mitigation:** Buy all the fur for a single project at the same time, from the same bolt if possible. If you are ordering online, order enough for the entire project in a single order and note in the order comments that you need all yardage from the same dye lot. Not all suppliers will accommodate this, but asking is free.

### The Test Swatch

**Always buy a test swatch before committing to yardage.** Order 1/4 yard (or the smallest available cut) of every color you are considering. When the swatches arrive:

- Compare them to the character reference sheet under natural daylight (not fluorescent, not incandescent, not LED --- daylight, near a window)
- Compare them to each other to check whether the colors work together
- Feel the pile density and softness --- is it what you expected from the product photos?
- Check the backing type (knit vs. woven) and stretch direction
- Note the nap direction and pile length

The $15 spent on swatches saves the $200 mistake of ordering six yards of the wrong color.

## 5. Pile Direction (Nap)

Faux fur has a nap --- a direction in which the pile lies flat. Run your hand across the surface: smooth in one direction, ruffled in the other. The smooth direction is "with the nap."

### Why Nap Matters

The nap direction on every pattern piece must align with the natural fur growth direction of the animal being represented. Fur grows from the top of the head toward the nose on the muzzle, from the head toward the tail on the body, from the hip toward the paw on the legs. If you cut a body panel with the nap running the wrong direction, the fur on the finished piece flows upward instead of downward, creating a visual discontinuity that no amount of brushing will fix. If two adjacent panels have nap running in different directions, the seam between them will show as a visible ridge where the fur suddenly changes direction.

### How to Mark It

Before cutting any piece, determine the nap direction of the fabric. Mark an arrow on the backing showing the nap direction (the direction the fur lies flat, typically toward the selvage on most bolts but not always --- check). Then mark a corresponding arrow on every pattern piece showing the intended nap direction on that piece. When laying pieces on the fabric for cutting, align every arrow.

### The Cost of Nap Direction

Aligning nap direction on all pieces means you cannot pack pattern pieces as tightly as you would on a non-directional fabric. Pieces cannot be rotated 180 degrees to save space because that reverses the nap. This results in more fabric waste per project. Accept the waste. A suit with correct nap direction on all panels looks dramatically better than one with inconsistent nap, and the visual difference is permanent. Save money somewhere else.

## 6. Fiber Content

### Polyester: The Standard

The vast majority of faux fur is polyester fiber. Polyester is durable, dyeable in a wide color range, machine-washable (gentle cycle, cold water, low heat or air dry), and resistant to fading. It does not attract moths (a significant advantage over natural fiber). Polyester fur is the default and the correct choice for 95% of fursuit and plush applications.

### Acrylic: Avoid

Some budget faux fur uses acrylic fiber instead of or blended with polyester. Acrylic fiber pills --- it forms small balls of matted fiber on the surface through friction and wear. A fursuit made with acrylic fur will develop visible pilling after one or two conventions, giving the suit a worn, shabby appearance that cannot be fully corrected. If a fabric label says "acrylic" or "acrylic/polyester blend," reconsider the purchase. The money saved on cheaper acrylic fur is lost when the suit looks terrible after its first year.

### Modacrylic: Fire-Resistant

Modacrylic fiber is used in some high-end faux fur, particularly products marketed for children's costumes and professional mascots, because it is inherently flame-resistant. For fursuits worn in environments with pyrotechnics, fire effects, or heavy stage lighting, modacrylic fur provides an additional safety margin. It is more expensive than polyester and less widely available.

## 7. Cutting Technique

### The Cardinal Rule: Cut from the Backing

Place the pattern piece on the BACK (wrong side) of the fur fabric. Trace the pattern onto the backing with a marker. Cut ONLY through the backing, not through the pile. Use sharp fabric scissors, a rotary cutter, or a razor blade. Slide the cutting tool under the pile, against the backing, and cut in small snips (for scissors) or in a single smooth stroke (for rotary cutter).

**Why:** If you cut through the pile with scissors, you shear the fibers along the cut line. This creates a visible bald stripe along every cut edge --- a line of trimmed pile shorter than the surrounding fur. On a finished piece, these sheared edges show at every seam as a thin bald line. Cutting from the backing preserves the full-length pile on both sides of the cut, and when the pieces are sewn together and the seam pile is picked out, the fibers from both sides intermingle and the seam disappears.

### Rotary Cutter vs. Scissors

**Rotary cutter:** Faster for long, straight cuts. Use a self-healing cutting mat, a metal straight edge for straight lines, and freehand for curves. The rotary blade slices through the backing cleanly without disturbing the pile. Best for body panels and other large pieces.

**Scissors:** Better for complex curves and small pieces. Use sharp fabric shears with a pointed tip. Slide the lower blade under the pile, lift slightly to separate the pile from the backing, and cut in short snips. The small cutting motion prevents you from accidentally shearing a swath of pile.

**Utility knife / razor blade:** Some makers prefer a sharp blade drawn along the backing for maximum control. This works well on a cutting mat but requires a steady hand and a very sharp blade.

### Pile Extraction After Sewing

After sewing any fur seam, fur pile gets trapped in the seam. This trapped pile creates a visible bald line at the seam unless it is pulled out. Using a straight pin, crochet hook, or seam ripper tip (carefully, to avoid cutting threads), work along the seam from the right side, pulling trapped pile out through the stitch line. This is tedious. On a full fursuit head, seam extraction may take an hour or more. It is also the single most important finishing step. The difference between a professional-looking suit and an amateur one is often nothing more than whether the maker took the time to extract every trapped fiber from every seam.

## 8. How Much to Buy

Buying too little fur mid-project and discovering that the exact color is out of stock or the dye lot has changed is one of the most stressful experiences in fursuit making. Buy more than you think you need. Leftover fur has value --- it can be used for repairs, for test patches, or sold to other makers. Running out has no upside.

### General Yardage Estimates

| Project | Yardage (60" wide fabric) |
|---|---|
| Fursuit head only | 1-2 yards |
| Partial (head + paws + tail) | 2-3 yards |
| Full plantigrade bodysuit | 4-6 yards (primary color) |
| Full digitigrade bodysuit | 5-7 yards (primary color) |
| Plush animal (12-18 inch) | 1/4-1/2 yard |
| Plush animal (24-36 inch) | 1/2-1 yard |

These estimates assume a single primary color. Multi-color designs require additional yardage of each accent color --- typically 1/2 to 2 yards per accent depending on coverage.

### Add 20%

Whatever the calculation says, add 20 percent. This covers cutting waste from nap alignment, errors, test pieces, and the fabric you ruin while learning a new technique. On a first project, add 30 percent. The cost of extra fabric is nothing compared to the cost of the project stalling because you are one foot short.

### Accent Colors and Specialty Pieces

Inner ears, belly panels, paw pads, muzzle insides, and other accent areas typically use contrasting fabric --- shorter pile, different texture, different color. Budget 1/4 to 1/2 yard of each accent fabric. Minky (Shannon Cuddle) is the standard for most accent applications.

## 9. Storage and Care

**Store fur fabric rolled, not folded.** Folding creates permanent creases in the pile that can be difficult to remove. If the fabric arrives folded (as most shipped fabric does), re-roll it around a cardboard tube for storage.

**Keep away from direct sunlight.** UV exposure fades dyed polyester over time. Store in a closet or covered area.

**Machine washing (finished pieces):** Gentle cycle, cold water, inside a pillowcase or mesh laundry bag. Air dry --- machine dryer heat can melt or mat polyester pile. After drying, brush the pile with a slicker brush (a pet grooming brush works perfectly) to restore the nap.

**Spot cleaning:** A damp cloth with mild soap removes most surface contamination without requiring a full wash.
