# Keeping It Fresh --- Fursuit Care and Maintenance

## The Enemy

Sweat. Bacteria. Odor. Matting. UV degradation. Foam decay. These are the forces that destroy fursuits, and every one of them is manageable with consistent care. A well-maintained fursuit lasts 5 to 10 years of regular use. A neglected one becomes unwearable in 1 to 2 years --- matted, stiff, foul-smelling, and structurally compromised.

The investment in a fursuit is substantial. Material costs for a self-built suit range from $200 to $900. Professional commissions range from $2,000 to $8,000 or more. The hours of labor --- whether your own or a maker's --- are measured in hundreds. That investment deserves maintenance. More importantly, the community you share space with at conventions, meetups, and events deserves a suiter who does not clear a room.

This is not optional. This is the hygiene social contract. Fursuiting is a close-quarters activity --- photos, hugs, dance circles, hallway interactions. The suiter who maintains their suit is the one people want to be around. The suiter who does not is the one people talk about behind their back, and not kindly. Be the suiter who smells like fabric softener, not the one who clears a room.

## After Every Wear: The Non-Negotiables

These steps happen every single time you take the suit off. No exceptions. Not "when you get around to it." Not "tomorrow." Now.

### 1. Spray the Interior with Isopropyl Alcohol

70% isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol) is your primary antimicrobial tool. It kills the bacteria that produce odor, it evaporates quickly without leaving residue, and it does not damage foam or fabric at this concentration.

**How to apply:**
- Use a standard spray bottle. Fill it with 70% isopropyl alcohol (available at any pharmacy or grocery store for $2 to $4 per bottle).
- Spray the interior surfaces of the head thoroughly: the foam that contacts your forehead, cheeks, chin, and the top of your head. Spray the interior of the mouth area.
- Spray the interior of hand paws, especially the palm area and between the fingers.
- Spray the interior of foot paws, especially the insole area.
- Spray the bodysuit interior at the armpits, crotch, and any area where sweat concentrated.

**Why 70% and not 99%?** The 70% concentration is more effective as a disinfectant than higher concentrations. The water content allows the alcohol to penetrate bacterial cell walls more effectively. Pure alcohol evaporates too quickly to fully disinfect. This is established microbiology, not folk wisdom.

**Safety:** Isopropyl alcohol is flammable. Spray in a well-ventilated area. Do not spray near open flames, heaters, or electrical sparks. Allow the alcohol to evaporate fully before storing the suit near heat sources.

### 2. Hang in a Well-Ventilated Area

NOT in a closet. NOT in a plastic bag. NOT in a pile on the floor of your hotel room. Airflow is essential. The moisture from your sweat must evaporate, and the alcohol must off-gas. A closet traps both, creating the exact conditions that breed mold and amplify odor.

**Head:** Place on a wig stand, mannequin head, or a wire stand that holds the head upright with the mouth opening and any ventilation holes exposed to air. If you do not have a stand, stuff the head loosely with newspaper or paper towels to maintain shape and absorb residual moisture, and set it on a shelf or table where air can circulate around it. A fan directed at the head accelerates drying.

**Body:** Hang on a broad-shouldered hanger or a garment rack. A regular wire hanger will stretch the shoulder area --- use a padded hanger, a wooden suit hanger, or lay the bodysuit flat on a drying rack. If space permits, hang it inside-out to expose the interior to air.

**Paws and feet:** Set open-side-up on a drying rack or stuff loosely with newspaper. The newspaper absorbs moisture from the interior foam.

**Minimum air-dry time:** 4 to 6 hours in a well-ventilated room. Longer is better. Overnight is ideal. Do not pack a wet suit into a suitcase or storage container.

### 3. Brush the Exterior Fur

Fursuit fur mats. That is its nature. Faux fur pile compresses against foam, catches on itself, absorbs environmental moisture, and gradually loses its loft and directionality. Brushing after every wear prevents matting from becoming permanent.

**Tools:**
- **Slicker brush (pet brush):** The standard tool. A rectangular pad of fine bent-wire bristles. Available at any pet supply store for $5 to $15. The wire bristles penetrate the pile and separate individual fibers without pulling them out.
- **Wide-tooth comb:** For long-pile fur (2 inches or longer), a wide-tooth comb detangles without snagging. Work from the tips inward toward the base --- do not start combing at the root and drag through a tangle.
- **Wire pet brush (pin brush):** For very long or very thick pile. Stiffer than a slicker brush, better for heavy mats. Use with care on thin or delicate fur.

**Technique:** Brush in the direction of the pile. Short strokes, starting at the tips. Work section by section. Do not yank or pull --- matted fibers that resist gentle brushing need time and patience, not force. Stubborn mats can be separated with the tip of a seam ripper before brushing.

**Focus areas:** Under the chin, inside the elbows, behind the knees, the seat area, and anywhere the fur is compressed during wear or transport.

## Monthly Deep Clean: The Bodysuit

The bodysuit absorbs more sweat than any other component. Even with after-wear alcohol spraying, residual body oils, skin cells, and environmental contamination accumulate. A monthly deep clean (or after every 3 to 5 wearing sessions, whichever comes first) removes what the alcohol does not.

### The Bathtub Method

The bodysuit is too large and too delicate for a standard washing machine (the agitator can tear seams and damage foam padding). Hand washing in a bathtub is the gold standard.

**Materials:**
- Bathtub or large utility basin
- Cold water (hot water damages synthetic fur and can shrink foam padding)
- Gentle detergent: Woolite (designed for delicates), Dawn dish soap (a few drops --- it is concentrated), or Nikwax Tech Wash (a sport fabric cleaner that does not leave residue). Do NOT use bleach, fabric softener, or heavily scented detergents. Bleach destroys synthetic fibers. Fabric softener coats the fur and attracts dirt. Scented detergents mask odor without eliminating the bacteria that cause it.

**Process:**

1. **Fill the tub** with enough cold water to fully submerge the bodysuit. Add detergent: 1 to 2 tablespoons of Woolite, or 3 to 5 drops of Dawn. Swirl to mix.

2. **Submerge the bodysuit.** Turn it inside-out if the interior is the primary concern (it usually is). Press it down gently to saturate all the fabric. Let it soak for 15 to 30 minutes. The soak time allows the detergent to penetrate the fiber base and dissolve oils and residue.

3. **Agitate gently.** Squeeze the fabric, press it against the tub, lift and dunk it. Do NOT wring, twist, or scrub aggressively. Wringing damages foam padding, distorts the bodysuit shape, and can tear seams. The goal is to work the soapy water through the fabric, not to mechanically abrade it.

4. **Drain and rinse.** Drain the tub. Refill with clean cold water. Submerge the suit and press to rinse. Drain and repeat until the water runs clear and soap-free. Residual soap attracts dirt and creates a film on the fur. Rinse thoroughly.

5. **Extract water.** Do NOT wring. Press the suit against the tub to squeeze out water. Roll it in clean towels and press to absorb more water. The goal is to remove as much water as possible before hanging, because a waterlogged suit takes days to dry and the weight of the water can stretch the fabric.

6. **Hang to dry.** Hang the bodysuit right-side-out on a broad hanger or garment rack in a well-ventilated area. Direct a fan at the suit to accelerate drying. Drying time: 24 to 72 hours depending on humidity, airflow, and suit thickness. In a humid environment with no air circulation, a thick bodysuit with foam padding can take 72 hours or longer. Do NOT put the suit in a dryer --- the heat will melt synthetic fur, shrink foam, and potentially damage elastic, zippers, and other components.

7. **Brush the fur** once the suit is completely dry. Brushing while wet mats the fibers. Wait until dry, then brush in the direction of the pile.

### Odor Stubborn Spots

If the bathtub wash does not fully eliminate odor (especially in the underarm and crotch areas):

- **White vinegar:** Add 1/2 cup of white vinegar to the soak water. Vinegar neutralizes odor-causing bacteria and does not harm synthetic fibers. The vinegar smell dissipates as the suit dries.
- **Enzyme-based odor remover:** Nature's Miracle (originally designed for pet odor), Rockin' Green Active Wear Detergent, or a similar enzyme cleaner. Enzymes break down the organic compounds (sweat proteins, skin oils) that bacteria feed on. Apply to problem areas before the full soak, or add to the soak water.
- **Baking soda:** Dissolve 1/2 cup of baking soda in the soak water for general deodorizing. Less effective than enzyme cleaners for severe odor but useful for maintenance washes.

### Machine Washing (When It Is Acceptable)

Some suiters machine-wash their bodysuits successfully. The conditions:

- **Front-loading washer ONLY.** No top-loader with an agitator --- the agitator catches and tears seams.
- **Delicate cycle, cold water.**
- **Bodysuit in a large mesh laundry bag** (or a pillowcase tied shut) to prevent snagging.
- **Gentle detergent, no fabric softener, no bleach.**
- **Remove immediately after the cycle** --- do not let it sit wet in the machine.
- **Hang to dry. Never machine dry.**

Machine washing is a calculated risk. It is faster and less labor-intensive than the bathtub method, but the mechanical action is harder on seams, padding, and fur. For budget suits and suits with no delicate foam padding, it is a reasonable option. For high-end commissions, suits with complex padding, or suits with delicate fur, hand wash.

## Head Cleaning: The Foam Problem

Fursuit heads are the most challenging component to clean because they contain foam --- and foam is a moisture sponge that takes days to dry if saturated. NEVER submerge a foam-based head in water. The foam absorbs water, takes 48 to 96 hours to dry even with fans, and risks mold growth in the interior if not dried completely.

### Interior Cleaning

- **Wipe with isopropyl alcohol** on a cloth or paper towel after every wear (part of the after-wear routine).
- **For deeper cleaning:** Dampen a cloth with a solution of warm water and a small amount of gentle detergent (Dawn, Woolite). Wipe the interior foam surfaces. Follow with a cloth dampened with clean water to remove soap residue. Follow with a dry cloth to absorb moisture. Allow to air-dry on a stand with good airflow.
- **For persistent odor in the head interior:** Spray the interior with an enzyme-based odor remover (Nature's Miracle). Allow to sit for 10 minutes. Wipe with a damp cloth. Air-dry thoroughly with fan assistance.

### Exterior Cleaning

- **Spot-cleaning:** Dampen a cloth with warm water and a drop of gentle detergent. Gently work the cloth through the fur in the direction of the pile. Follow with a clean, damp cloth to remove soap. Allow to air-dry. Brush the area once dry.
- **For food, drink, or makeup stains:** Address immediately. Blot (do not rub) with a damp cloth. For grease-based stains, apply a small amount of Dawn dish soap directly to the stain, work gently, and blot away. For marker or paint: test a cleaning solvent (rubbing alcohol, Goo Gone) on an inconspicuous area first.

### The Foam Lining

Over time, the foam padding inside the head --- particularly the forehead pad and cheek pads --- absorbs oils and odor that surface cleaning cannot fully address. When the interior odor persists despite cleaning:

- **Removable foam liners (if designed with them):** Remove, hand wash with gentle detergent in cool water, squeeze dry (do not wring), and air-dry completely before reinstalling.
- **Non-removable foam:** If the head design does not have removable liners, consider retrofitting with removable foam pieces attached with Velcro. This allows regular replacement of the most contaminated padding. A strip of soft Velcro (loop side) glued to the head interior, and the corresponding hook side on the foam pad, creates a removable system.
- **Replacement:** Upholstery foam is inexpensive ($5 to $15 per sheet). If the interior foam is irredeemably saturated with odor, cut it out and replace it. This is maintenance, not failure.

## Paw and Foot Care

Hand paws and foot paws are high-wear, high-moisture components. They contact surfaces directly and absorb more sweat per square inch than any other suit component.

### Hand Paws

- After every wear: spray interior with 70% isopropyl alcohol. Air-dry open-side-up on a drying rack.
- Monthly: hand wash in cool water with gentle detergent. Squeeze dry (do not wring). Air-dry completely before storage.
- **Padding replacement:** If hand paws have internal foam padding (common in toony styles with oversized paw shapes), the foam will degrade and absorb odor over time. Replace the foam annually or as needed.

### Foot Paws

Foot paws are the highest-moisture component and the first to develop odor problems.

- **Removable insoles:** If your foot paws have removable insoles, remove them after every wear. Spray with alcohol, air-dry separately. Replace insoles every 3 to 6 months (or more often with heavy use). Craft foam (EVA), orthotic insoles, or cut-to-fit shoe insoles all work. The insole absorbs the worst of the foot moisture and can be replaced cheaply, extending the life of the foot paw itself.
- **No removable insoles:** Spray interior with alcohol. Stuff with newspaper to absorb moisture. Air-dry in a well-ventilated area. Consider retrofitting with removable insoles to simplify future maintenance.
- **Sole replacement:** The bottom of foot paws wears out from walking. Many builders use indoor/outdoor fabric, sheet rubber, or hot-glued EVA foam as the sole material. When the sole wears through, peel off the old material and apply new. This is routine maintenance, not a defect.
- **Disinfect:** In addition to alcohol spray, periodically wipe foot paw interiors with a disinfectant wipe (Clorox or Lysol wipes). Foot bacteria are particularly persistent and benefit from the more aggressive disinfectant.

## Storage: Long-Term Preservation

How you store the suit between wears determines how long it lasts.

### Head Storage

- **On a stand.** A wig stand, mannequin head, or a custom head stand (PVC pipe frame, foam block, or 3D-printed stand) keeps the head in its correct shape. Without a stand, the head deforms under its own weight: cheeks compress, ears droop, muzzle flattens. Over months, the deformation becomes permanent.
- **Stuffed with newspaper.** If you do not have a stand, stuff the head loosely with crumpled newspaper or acid-free tissue paper. The paper maintains the head's shape and absorbs residual moisture. Replace the paper if it becomes damp or discolored.
- **NOT in a plastic bag.** Sealed plastic traps moisture and creates a mold-friendly environment. If you need to enclose the head for transport or dust protection, use a breathable fabric bag (cotton pillowcase, muslin bag).

### Body Storage

- **On a broad hanger.** A wide-shouldered wooden or padded hanger distributes the suit's weight and prevents shoulder stretching. Do NOT use a wire hanger --- the narrow form digs into the shoulders and creates permanent indentations in the padding and fabric.
- **In a breathable garment bag.** A fabric garment bag (cotton, canvas) protects the suit from dust, light, and insects while allowing air circulation. Avoid sealed plastic garment bags for the same reason as with heads.
- **Folded loosely** if hanging space is not available. Do not compress the suit into a tight ball or stuff it into a small container. Compressed fur mats permanently. Fold the suit loosely with the fur facing inward (protecting the pile from abrasion) and place in a large breathable container.

### Environmental Conditions

- **Cool:** Heat accelerates foam degradation and can warp 3D-printed components (PLA softens at 140 degrees Fahrenheit --- an attic in summer exceeds this easily). Store in a climate-controlled interior space.
- **Dry:** Humidity promotes mold and mildew. In humid climates, add silica gel packets to storage containers. A small, inexpensive dehumidifier in the storage room is a worthwhile investment.
- **Dark:** UV light fades synthetic fur, particularly bright colors. Keep the suit out of direct sunlight during storage. UV damage is cumulative and irreversible --- sun-bleached fur cannot be restored, only replaced.

### Pest Protection

Carpet beetles and clothes moths feed on the protein fibers and residual organic matter (sweat, skin oils) in stored textiles. Faux fur is synthetic and not a primary food source, but contamination with organic material makes it attractive to these pests.

- **Clean before long-term storage.** A clean suit is less attractive to pests than a dirty one.
- **Cedar blocks or lavender sachets** in the storage area deter moths. Replace annually as the scent fades.
- **Inspect periodically.** Check stored suits every 2 to 3 months for signs of pest activity: small holes, shed larval skins, frass (insect droppings). Early detection prevents catastrophic damage.

## Emergency Repairs

Fursuits are performance costumes. They get stressed, pulled, caught, stepped on, and occasionally torn during normal use. Field repairs keep you performing until a proper repair can be done.

### Seam Pops (Split Seams)

The most common damage. A seam opens under stress, exposing the backing or foam beneath.

- **Field fix:** Hot glue. Apply hot glue to the inside of the seam opening, press the edges together, hold until set. This is ugly, impermanent, and effective enough to finish a convention day.
- **Proper repair:** Ladder stitch (invisible stitch). Thread a needle with heavy-duty thread matching the fur backing color. Working from inside the seam, stitch back and forth across the gap, pulling the edges together. The stitches are hidden inside the seam. Brush the fur over the repaired area to conceal the seam line.

### Fur Tears

A rip in the fur fabric itself (not just a seam separation).

- **Field fix:** Hot glue or fabric tape (gaffer tape on the interior backing) to close the tear.
- **Proper repair:** Cut a patch of matching fur slightly larger than the tear. Glue or sew the patch to the interior backing, covering the tear. Brush the surrounding pile over the patch edges to blend. For tears in visible areas, a maker may need to do a professional fur replacement of the entire panel.

### Foam Detachment

Foam features (muzzle, cheek pads, brow) separate from the head base.

- **Field fix:** Hot glue. Reattach the foam to the base.
- **Proper repair:** Contact cement (Barge, DAP Weldwood). Apply to both surfaces, let dry until tacky, press together. Contact cement creates a stronger and more durable bond than hot glue for foam-to-foam and foam-to-fabric joints. (Safety note: use contact cement in a well-ventilated area with an organic vapor respirator. See [FFA-04: Fursuit Fabrication](../04-fursuit-fabrication.md) for full safety guidance.)

### Broken Zipper

The back zipper is the highest-stress component on a bodysuit. When it fails (slider breaks, teeth separate, zipper tape tears), the suit is unwearable.

- **Field fix:** Safety pins to close the back opening (not elegant, but functional).
- **Proper repair:** Replace the entire zipper. Unpick the old zipper with a seam ripper. Install a new heavy-duty separating zipper (Vislon #10 or equivalent) in the same channel. Resew the fur flap over it. This is a 2 to 4 hour repair and is well within the capability of any suiter who can operate a sewing machine.

### Matted Fur (Severe)

When fur is matted beyond what normal brushing can fix --- compacted, stiff, or forming solid clumps:

- **Seam ripper separation.** Insert the tip of a seam ripper into the mat and gently separate the fibers. Work slowly. The seam ripper cuts through the tangles without cutting the base fibers (if used carefully).
- **Steam.** Hold a garment steamer or steam iron (on the steam setting, NOT touching the fabric) 2 to 3 inches from the matted area. The steam relaxes the synthetic fibers. Brush immediately while the fibers are warm and pliable. This is the most effective technique for severe matting.
- **Brushing.** After separation and/or steaming, brush with a slicker brush in the direction of the pile. Multiple passes, gentle pressure.

If the fur is matted beyond recovery (the fibers are permanently kinked or broken), the only option is re-furring: removing the old fur and replacing it with new material. This is a significant repair and may warrant professional help.

## When to Send to a Maker for Refurbishment

Some damage and deterioration exceeds what home maintenance can address. A professional refurbishment by the original maker (or another experienced builder) is appropriate when:

- **Foam degradation is extensive.** The foam inside the head has crumbled, compressed, or lost its shape to the point where the head no longer fits correctly or holds its form. Professional refurbishment involves removing the old foam, rebuilding the internal structure, and re-furring if necessary.
- **Major seam failure across multiple seams.** A bodysuit that is coming apart at multiple seam lines needs professional attention --- not just repair but reinforcement of the seam construction throughout the suit.
- **Fur replacement is needed.** Re-furring a head or body panel requires pattern-making skill, fur-matching ability, and sewing expertise. If the fur is faded, matted beyond recovery, or damaged across large areas, a maker can strip and re-fur the affected components.
- **Structural rework.** If the head base is cracked (3D-printed), warped (foam), or otherwise structurally compromised, professional repair or rebuild is necessary.
- **You want it done right.** There is no shame in sending a suit to a professional for maintenance that exceeds your skill level. The makers listed in [FFA-10: Fursuit Makers Index](../10-fursuit-makers-index.md) include many who offer refurbishment and repair services.

## The Maintenance Schedule (Summary)

| When | Action |
|---|---|
| After EVERY wear | Spray interior with 70% isopropyl alcohol. Hang in ventilated area. Brush exterior fur. |
| Monthly (or every 3-5 wears) | Bathtub wash bodysuit. Deep-clean head interior. Inspect paws, feet. Replace foot paw insoles if worn. |
| Every 3-6 months | Inspect storage conditions. Check for pest activity. Rotate insoles. Clean zippers with zipper lubricant. Inspect seams for stress. |
| Annually | Full deep clean of all components. Assess foam condition in head. Check for UV fading. Assess overall structural integrity. Replace worn padding. |
| As needed | Emergency repairs (seam pops, tears, detachment). Send to maker for professional refurbishment when home maintenance is insufficient. |
