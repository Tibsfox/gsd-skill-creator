# DIY Project: Build Convention Badge Holders and Lanyards

## Your Identity, Handmade

Research module FFA-TS-06: a guided maker project that produces custom badge holders, lanyards, and display accessories for convention attendance. Three badge holder designs (vinyl pocket, resin badge, fabric lanyard), plus LED-lit and embroidered customization options. Materials cost: $10-30 per design. Time: 1-3 hours per design.

**Cross-references:** [FFA-04] Fursuit Fabrication, [FFA-03] Textile Craft, [FFA-12] Fursuit History & Culture, [FFA-TS-01] Design Your Fursona, [FFA study-guide/sewing-basics-for-fursuit-makers], [PNT-TS-04] Convention Badge Painting.

## Why Make Your Own?

At a convention --- furry, anime, comic, gaming, any convention with badge culture --- your badge is your face. In a community where thousands of people go by character names, where many attendees wear costumes or fursuits, where you may meet someone six times before you learn their legal name, the badge is how you are recognized. It displays your name, your character, your species, your pronouns, and --- if the artist was skilled --- your personality. The badge is the first thing people look at when they approach you. It is your introduction before you open your mouth.

Most convention-goers receive a generic plastic badge holder with their registration. It works. It holds the badge. It is also identical to the one worn by every other attendee, which means it does nothing to express the identity it is supposed to represent. A custom badge holder, made by hand, tells a different story. It says you cared enough to make something. It says the badge inside --- the art, the character, the name --- matters enough to deserve a frame worthy of it.

This is a maker project in the truest sense. You are not building a technical system or a piece of furniture. You are building something small, personal, and expressive, using skills that range from basic sewing to resin casting to simple electronics. Each design option in this guide produces something you can wear, something you can give as a gift, and something that starts conversations. At conventions, starting conversations is the entire point.

The connection to painting is direct: if you painted your own convention badge using the techniques from the [convention badge painting session](../../PNT/research/try-sessions/convention-badge-painting.md), building a custom holder for that badge completes the creative loop. You made the art. You made the frame. The whole thing is yours.

## Design 1: Vinyl Pocket Badge Holder

The vinyl pocket is the most practical design --- it protects the badge while displaying it, it is waterproof (important at outdoor events and in convention halls where drink spills happen), and it can be opened to swap badges for different characters or different days.

### Materials ($8-12)

- **Clear vinyl** (12-gauge or thicker): One piece approximately 6" x 8". Available at fabric stores in the home decor section (tablecloth material) or the marine/outdoor section (boat window material). 12-gauge vinyl is stiff enough to hold its shape but flexible enough to sew through a domestic sewing machine.
- **Bias tape** (1/2" wide, single fold): 24 inches in your character's color. Bias tape is pre-folded fabric tape used for finishing raw edges. It comes in every color imaginable and costs $2-3 per package. Choose a color that complements the badge art.
- **Snap or Velcro:** One snap set (sew-on type) or a 2" strip of sew-on Velcro for the top closure.
- **D-ring or swivel clip:** One small D-ring or lobster claw clip ($1-2) for attaching the holder to a lanyard.
- **Thread:** Matching the bias tape color. Heavy-duty polyester.

### Construction (45 minutes)

1. **Cut two vinyl rectangles:** Each 3" x 4.5" (slightly larger than a standard 2.5" x 3.5" badge, to allow room for the badge to slide in and out without forcing). Round the bottom corners with scissors for a cleaner look.

2. **Stack the rectangles.** Place them exactly on top of each other. Pin or clip them together (use binder clips --- pins leave permanent holes in vinyl).

3. **Bind the edges with bias tape.** Starting at the bottom left corner, fold the bias tape over the raw edge of the stacked vinyl sheets and sew through all layers (vinyl + bias tape) with a straight stitch, 1/4" from the tape edge. Sew the bottom edge, then the right side, then the left side. Leave the top edge open --- this is where the badge slides in.

4. **Finish the top edge.** Bind each top edge separately (the front piece and the back piece get their own bias tape finishing on the top edge). This creates a clean opening.

5. **Add the closure.** Sew one half of the snap (or Velcro) to the inside of the front top edge, and the other half to the outside of the back top edge. When folded over, the snap closes the pocket, keeping the badge from sliding out.

6. **Add the D-ring.** Fold a 2" strip of bias tape through the D-ring and sew it to the top-back corner of the holder. The D-ring accepts a lanyard clip.

**Tips for sewing vinyl:** Use a Teflon presser foot or place a strip of tissue paper between the vinyl and the regular presser foot. Vinyl is sticky and will not feed smoothly under a standard metal foot. The tissue paper tears away after sewing. Use a size 14 or 16 needle --- vinyl is tough on fine needles.

## Design 2: Resin Badge

The resin badge is a permanent display piece --- a badge encased in crystal-clear epoxy resin, creating a glossy, glass-like surface that protects the art and transforms a piece of paper into a wearable art object. This design does not allow swapping badges (the art is permanently embedded), so it is best for your primary character or a badge you are particularly proud of.

### Materials ($15-20)

- **Clear epoxy resin** (2-part mix): A small kit (8oz total) is more than enough for multiple badges. Art resin brands (ArtResin, Alumilite Amazing Clear Cast, TotalBoat) produce crystal-clear results with minimal yellowing. $12-18 for a small kit.
- **Silicone mold:** A rectangular mold approximately 3" x 4" x 1/4" deep. You can buy pre-made molds from craft stores or cast your own from silicone putty. $5-8 for a pre-made mold.
- **The badge:** Your painted or printed badge. Ensure it is completely dry and free of smudges. If the badge is painted with watercolor or a water-soluble medium, seal it first with a thin coat of clear acrylic spray (Krylon Crystal Clear or similar) to prevent the resin from reactivating the paint.
- **Pin back or magnetic closure:** For attaching the finished badge to clothing or a fursuit. Pin backs ($2 for a pack of 10) are traditional. Magnetic closures ($3-5 for a set) are better for fursuits because pins leave holes in fabric and fur. Neodymium magnets are strong enough to hold through fursuit fabric without penetrating.
- **Disposable mixing cups and stir sticks:** For mixing the resin. Graduated cups make the 1:1 ratio measurement easier.
- **Disposable gloves:** Uncured resin is a skin irritant. Wear nitrile gloves during mixing and pouring.

### Construction (30 minutes active + 24-48 hours curing)

1. **Prepare the badge.** Trim the badge to its final size. If the badge has any loose fibers, rough edges, or wrinkled corners, fix them now --- once they are in resin, they are permanent.

2. **Mix the resin.** Follow the manufacturer's instructions exactly. Most art resins are a 1:1 ratio by volume (equal parts resin and hardener). Pour into a mixing cup, stir slowly for 2-3 minutes, scraping the sides and bottom of the cup. Slow stirring minimizes air bubbles. Fast stirring creates a frothy mess that takes hours to degas.

3. **Pour a thin base layer.** Pour approximately 1/8" of mixed resin into the silicone mold. This base layer will be the back of the badge. Pop any surface bubbles with a heat gun or torch (a quick pass of a torch flame bursts bubbles instantly --- the resin is not flammable at this stage, so a brief pass is safe). Let this layer begin to gel (15-30 minutes, depending on the resin brand).

4. **Place the badge.** Lay the badge face-down into the partially gelled base layer. The art faces down because you are building the badge in reverse --- the base layer becomes the back, the badge goes in the middle, and the top pour becomes the front (the glossy surface the viewer sees). Press gently to eliminate air trapped under the paper.

5. **Pour the top layer.** Mix a second small batch of resin and pour it over the back of the badge (which will become the front of the finished piece). Fill to the top of the mold. Pop bubbles with the torch. The resin should fully cover the badge with at least 1/16" of depth.

6. **Cure.** Cover the mold loosely with a box or bin (to prevent dust from settling on the wet resin) and let it cure for 24-48 hours at room temperature. Do not move or touch it during curing. Resin is self-leveling --- it will find its own flat surface if left undisturbed.

7. **Demold and finish.** Pop the cured badge out of the silicone mold. The surface that was against the mold bottom (now the front of the badge) should be glass-smooth and crystal-clear. If there are any rough edges, sand them with 400-grit wet sandpaper, then 800-grit, then 1500-grit, and polish with a buffing compound.

8. **Attach the back.** Glue a pin back or magnetic closure to the back of the resin badge with E6000 adhesive or hot glue.

The finished resin badge is waterproof, scratch-resistant, and has a depth and clarity that makes the badge art look like it is floating under glass. It is a conversation piece. People will pick it up, turn it over, and ask how you made it.

## Design 3: Faux Fur Lanyard

This is the design that makes people at conventions stop and stare. A lanyard made from faux fur --- specifically, from leftover scraps of the same fur used to build your fursuit or plush projects. The lanyard matches your character. It is soft, distinctive, immediately recognizable as handmade, and it uses materials you already have if you have done any [fursuit paw construction](first-fursuit-paws.md) or [tail building](fursuit-tail-construction.md).

### Materials ($5-10)

- **Faux fur scraps:** Two strips, each approximately 1.5" x 20". Cut from leftover fur in your character's colors. The pile should be short to medium --- long pile makes a bulky lanyard that is hard to clip things to.
- **Interfacing or ribbon:** One strip of 1" wide woven interfacing or grosgrain ribbon, 20" long. This is the structural core of the lanyard --- the fur wraps around it. Without the core, the lanyard stretches and distorts under the weight of the badge.
- **Swivel clip:** One lobster claw or trigger snap clip ($1-2). This clip attaches the lanyard to the badge holder.
- **Thread:** Heavy-duty polyester, matching the fur backing color.
- **Sewing machine or needle and thread:** A sewing machine is faster; hand-sewing is perfectly functional.

### Construction (30-45 minutes)

1. **Cut the fur strips.** Using sharp scissors or a rotary cutter, cut two strips of faux fur, each 1.5" wide by 20" long. Cut from the back (the fabric backing), not the front --- cutting through the pile from the front severs the fur fibers and creates a visible bald line. Cutting from the back, along the backing only, preserves the pile on both sides of the cut. See [sewing basics for fursuit makers](../study-guides/sewing-basics-for-fursuit-makers.md) for detailed fur-cutting technique.

2. **Sew the strips together.** Place the two fur strips right sides together (pile facing pile). Sew along both long edges with a 1/4" seam allowance, creating a tube. Leave both short ends open. Turn the tube right-side-out (pull one end through the other, like turning a sock). The fur is now on the outside, the seams are hidden inside.

3. **Insert the core.** Feed the interfacing or ribbon strip through the fur tube. Center it so the fur extends slightly beyond the interfacing at both ends. The interfacing prevents the lanyard from stretching and gives it body.

4. **Finish the ends.** Fold the raw fur edges inward at each end and hand-stitch closed. At one end, thread the swivel clip onto the lanyard before closing. The clip hangs from the bottom of the lanyard. At the other end, fold the lanyard into a loop (about 3" of overlap) and stitch the overlap to create the neck loop.

5. **Fluff and groom.** Use a fine-toothed comb or a pet slicker brush to comb out any fur fibers that were caught in the seams. This step is the difference between a lanyard that looks manufactured and one that looks fluffy and intentional.

## Custom Touches

These additions transform a functional badge holder into something personal and memorable.

### Embroidered Name

Using a basic embroidery stitch (backstitch or chain stitch), embroider your character's name onto the lanyard. Work on the fur tube before assembling (it is easier to embroider flat than cylindrical). Use embroidery floss in a contrasting color. The name should be centered and large enough to read at conversation distance (2-3 feet). If your character name is short (4-6 letters), it fits comfortably on a 1.5" wide lanyard.

### Species Paw Prints

Cut small paw print shapes from contrasting-color minky or fleece (use the same paw pad material from the [fursuit paws project](first-fursuit-paws.md)). Applique them to the fur lanyard with a few stitches or a dot of fabric glue. Three or four paw prints walking along the length of the lanyard is a subtle but charming detail.

### LED-Lit Badge

For maximum impact in the dim lighting of convention dance floors and evening events, add an LED to your badge holder. The simplest approach:

1. Buy a small coin-cell battery holder with an integrated LED (available at electronics stores and Amazon for $1-3, often sold as "LED throwies" or "LED badge lights").
2. Position the LED behind the badge, between the badge and the back panel of the holder. For vinyl pocket holders, the LED shines through the translucent badge paper, creating a backlit effect. For resin badges, embed a thin LED strip in the resin during the pour (between the base layer and the badge) for an internal glow.
3. Secure the battery holder to the back of the badge holder with a small piece of Velcro (for easy battery replacement).

The effect is striking --- a glowing badge in a dim convention hall. It draws attention, starts conversations, and is visible from across a crowded room. Use a warm white LED for a natural look, or a color-changing LED for maximum spectacle.

## Convention Prep Checklist

Whether you built one holder or all three, here is what to bring:

**Primary badge and holder.** Your main character, your daily wear. This is the badge that represents you for the duration of the convention. It should be in the holder that best protects and displays it.

**Backup badge.** Conventions are crowded, physical environments. Badges get stepped on, spilled on, and (occasionally) lost. A backup badge in a ziplock bag costs nothing to bring and saves a day of anonymous wandering. If you painted the primary badge by hand ([PNT-TS-04](../../PNT/research/try-sessions/convention-badge-painting.md)), consider a printed copy as the backup.

**Alternate character badges.** If you have multiple fursonas or characters, bring a badge for each. The vinyl pocket holder lets you swap badges between events. Attending a dance? Swap to your party character. Attending an art panel? Swap to your artist badge.

**Fursuit badge attachment.** If you wear a fursuit, your badge cannot hang from a standard lanyard (lanyards sit under the suit head and are not visible). Options:
- **Magnetic closure:** Attach the badge to the outside of the suit with a neodymium magnet on the inside. Strong, non-damaging, easy to reposition. This is the preferred method for most fursuiters.
- **Pin-back:** Pins through the fur backing. Works but leaves tiny holes. Acceptable for bodysuit fabric; avoid on the head or hand-paws.
- **Clip-on:** A large binder clip or badge clip attached to the suit's collar area or chest seam. Functional but can distort the fur.

**Lanyard (your handmade faux fur lanyard).** Even if you use a magnetic or clip attachment for your fursuit badge, the lanyard is for when you are out of suit. It is also a visible marker of your craft skill --- people recognize handmade work, and the lanyard is one of the first things they notice.

**Badge sleeves.** If you are traveling, protect your badges during transit. Rigid card sleeves (the kind used for trading cards) fit standard 2.5" x 3.5" badges perfectly and cost pennies each. Pack them in a hard case (a pencil box or small Pelican case) so they survive luggage handling.

## The Community Connection

Your badge is your identity at a convention. It is your name, your art, your species, your first impression. In the furry community specifically, where identity is chosen and curated rather than given, the badge carries weight that goes beyond a name tag. When someone reads your badge, they are not just learning your name --- they are seeing your character, your aesthetic, your creative investment. A hand-painted badge in a handmade holder says something that a printed sticker in a plastic sleeve does not: that you care about craft, that you value the work of your hands, and that you take this seriously enough to make things from scratch.

That signal draws like-minded people. Makers find each other at conventions. The person who stops to compliment your fur lanyard is probably someone who sews. The person who asks about your resin badge probably works with resin. The person who wants to know how the LED works is an electronics hobbyist. Your badge holder is not just a display --- it is a beacon. It broadcasts your interests and invites connection.

This is what making is for. Not the object. The connection.

> **Try This:** Paint a convention badge first using the [PNT convention badge painting session](../../PNT/research/try-sessions/convention-badge-painting.md), then build a custom holder for it using this guide. The complete loop --- painting the art AND building the frame --- is deeply satisfying.

> **Try This:** If you have leftover fur scraps from the [fursuit paws project](first-fursuit-paws.md) or the [tail construction project](fursuit-tail-construction.md), use them for the faux fur lanyard. The lanyard matches your suit pieces, creating a unified look.

> **Try This:** Use [airbrush techniques](airbrush-basics-for-fursuits.md) to add shading or markings to your faux fur lanyard. A gradient from your character's primary color to their secondary color, airbrushed along the length of the lanyard, is a subtle detail that demonstrates genuine craft.

> **Try This:** Build a resin badge as a gift for a friend. Choose their character's badge art, cast it in resin with a magnetic back, and present it at a convention. Handmade gifts in this community are remembered.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [materials-making]: Each badge holder design involves a different material system --- vinyl sewing, epoxy resin casting, faux fur textile construction. Understanding the properties and working characteristics of these materials (vinyl's stiffness and sewing challenges, resin's exothermic curing and bubble behavior, faux fur's nap direction and cutting requirements) is applied materials knowledge that transfers to any maker discipline. The choice of material for a given application is a design decision rooted in understanding what each material can and cannot do.
- **Art** --- [creative-process]: This project moves from concept (what does my badge holder need to do?) through design (which format best serves that need?) through fabrication (hands-on construction) through evaluation (does it work at the convention?). This is the creative process at its most tangible --- every step produces a physical artifact, and the final evaluation happens in the real world, with real feedback from the community.
- **Trades** --- [basic-sewing]: The vinyl pocket and faux fur lanyard designs both use fundamental sewing skills --- straight stitching, seam allowances, edge finishing with bias tape, turning tubes, attaching hardware. These are the same skills from the [sewing basics study guide](../study-guides/sewing-basics-for-fursuit-makers.md), applied to a small, fast project that builds confidence before tackling larger fursuit components.
