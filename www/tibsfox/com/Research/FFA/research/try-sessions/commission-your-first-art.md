# Try Session: Commission Your First Furry Art

*The buyer's guide --- for people who want custom art of their character*

---

## The Moment It Becomes Real

There is a specific moment in the furry experience when you realize that the character in your head --- the one you designed on paper, described in text, maybe sketched in a notebook --- could exist as a real piece of art. Not a doodle. Not a screenshot from a character creator. A commissioned illustration, drawn by a professional artist, rendered with skill and intention and care, depicting YOUR character exactly as you imagine them. The first time you see that finished commission --- your fursona, brought to life by someone who took your description and reference sheet and MADE something from it --- is genuinely moving. It is one of the most emotionally significant moments in the furry community experience, and it is available to anyone willing to learn how the commission process works.

This try session is the buyer's guide. It covers how to find artists, how the commission process works from first contact through final delivery, what things cost, how to be a good client, and why supporting artists is not just a transaction but an investment in the community that makes furry art possible.

---

## Part 1: Finding Artists

The furry art community is enormous, distributed across multiple platforms, and astonishingly talented. The challenge is not finding artists who do commission work --- it is navigating the sheer abundance of options to find the right artist for your character, your aesthetic preferences, and your budget.

### Fur Affinity (furaffinity.net)

The oldest and largest dedicated furry art platform. Fur Affinity hosts millions of submissions across every style, medium, and skill level. It remains the primary platform for commission advertising in the furry community.

**How to find commission artists on FA:**
- Browse the **Featured** submissions on the front page. These rotate regularly and showcase a range of styles and skill levels.
- Use the **search function** with your species name + "commission" (e.g., "fox commission," "dragon commission"). Filter by type (digital, traditional) if you have a preference.
- Check the **artist's profile page** for a "commissions open" or "commissions closed" notice. Many artists maintain a dedicated commission info journal or submission.
- Look at the **favorites** of artists whose work you admire. Artists favorite each other's work, and following the favorites trail often leads you to artists in a similar style or skill tier.

**Advantages:** Largest concentrated pool of furry artists. Dedicated commission culture (artists know the process, clients know the process). Commission terms of service are standard and well-understood.

**Disadvantages:** The interface is aging. Search functionality is limited. Discovery can be overwhelming for newcomers.

### Twitter/X

Many furry artists use Twitter/X as their primary portfolio and commission advertising platform. The hashtag ecosystem makes discovery efficient:

- **#furryart** --- the broad tag; everything from sketches to full illustrations
- **#fursona** --- character-focused art
- **#opencommissions** --- artists actively taking commissions right now
- **#commissionsopen** --- variant of the above
- **#furryartist** --- artist self-identification
- **#artshare** --- mutual promotion threads where artists share each other's work

**How to use it:** Search the commission hashtags, browse the results, and follow artists whose style appeals to you. Many artists post commission sheets (images showing examples and prices) as pinned tweets, making it easy to see what they offer and what it costs.

### Bluesky

The furry community has established a significant presence on Bluesky. Many artists who left Twitter/X maintain active Bluesky accounts. The same hashtags apply, and the feed algorithm (or lack thereof, depending on your settings) tends to surface art more reliably than Twitter's increasingly engagement-optimized feed.

### Convention Artist Alleys

If you attend furry conventions --- Anthrocon, Midwest FurFest, Further Confusion, Furlandia (Portland), RainFurrest's successors, or any regional con --- the artist alley (also called dealers' den or art show) is the single best way to find an artist in person. You can see their work at full resolution, printed or displayed on a tablet. You can talk to them about your character. You can commission on the spot (convention commissions are often simpler pieces --- badges, sketches, busts --- because the artist is working on a convention timeline) or exchange contact information for a more complex piece after the convention.

**The advantage of in-person discovery:** You see the art in physical form. You meet the artist as a person. The conversation about your character is immediate and interactive. Many long-term artist-client relationships begin with a convention badge commission.

### Ko-fi and Patreon

Many artists maintain Ko-fi shops or Patreon tiers that include commission slots. Ko-fi in particular has become a popular platform for commission management --- artists post commission forms, track queues, and accept payment through the platform. Patreon-based commissions are often structured as monthly slots available to subscribers at specific tiers.

### Artist Directories and Referrals

- **The Dealers Den** (thedealersden.com) --- an auction and sales platform for furry art and merchandise. Not a commission platform per se, but browsing active sellers shows you who is producing work.
- **Word of mouth** --- ask friends in the community who they have commissioned. Personal referrals are the most reliable way to find artists who are communicative, reliable, and produce work that matches their examples.
- **Art fight** (artfight.net) --- an annual event where artists draw each other's characters. Browsing Art Fight profiles reveals artists across every skill level, and the characters they draw during the event demonstrate how they handle other people's designs.

## Part 2: The Commission Process --- Step by Step

### Step 1: Check Availability

Before contacting an artist, check whether their commissions are open. This information is typically found in one or more of these locations:

- **Pinned tweet/post** on their social media
- **Profile bio** ("comms open" / "comms closed")
- **Commission info page** (a dedicated post, journal, or webpage with prices, terms, and availability status)
- **Link in bio** to a Google Form, Ko-fi page, or Trello board

**If commissions are closed:** Do not ask the artist to make an exception. Their queue is full, and asking puts them in an uncomfortable position. Instead, follow the artist and enable notifications so you know when they open again. Many artists announce openings in advance --- "opening 5 commission slots on Friday at noon PST" --- and slots fill within minutes for popular artists.

### Step 2: Read the Terms of Service (TOS)

Every professional commission artist has a TOS, whether it is a formal document, a section of their commission sheet, or a journal post. Read it entirely before contacting the artist. The TOS typically covers:

- **What they will and will not draw** (content restrictions, species preferences, complexity limits)
- **Payment terms** (deposit amount, payment schedule, accepted payment methods)
- **Revision policy** (how many revisions are included, what constitutes a revision vs. a change of concept)
- **Turnaround time** (expected delivery timeline, which can range from one week to six months depending on the artist's queue)
- **Usage rights** (who can post the finished art, whether the client can print it, whether the artist retains portfolio rights)
- **Cancellation and refund policy**

Respecting the TOS is not optional. It is the contract. If the TOS says "no NSFW," do not request NSFW. If the TOS says "50% deposit upfront," have the deposit ready.

### Step 3: Fill Out the Commission Form

Most artists use a structured commission form (Google Form, Ko-fi form, or a template provided in their commission info). The form typically asks for:

**Character reference sheet:** This is the most important thing you provide. A clear reference sheet --- showing your character from the front and side, with color swatches, distinguishing markings, and key details labeled --- gives the artist the information they need to draw your character accurately. If you do not have a reference sheet yet, see the [Design Your Fursona](design-your-fursona.md) try session for guidance on creating one. A text description is acceptable but less reliable --- artists interpret descriptions differently, and a visual reference eliminates ambiguity.

**Pose and expression:** What do you want your character doing? Standing casually, sitting, in action, looking at the viewer, looking away? What expression? Happy, confident, mischievous, contemplative? Be specific but not prescriptive --- "sitting on a rock, looking at the sunset, relaxed smile" gives the artist a clear concept with room for artistic interpretation.

**Background preference:** Simple color/gradient, abstract, environmental (specific setting), or none (transparent/white). Backgrounds add cost and time. If you are on a budget, a simple background or no background keeps the price down without affecting the character art quality.

**Commission type:** Sketch, line art, flat color, full color/shaded, painted, etc. These terms have specific meanings in the furry art commission ecosystem:

| Type | Description | Typical Detail Level |
|---|---|---|
| Sketch | Rough pencil/digital lines, minimal cleanup | Low --- captures gesture and character |
| Line art | Clean, finished lines, no color | Medium --- polished drawing |
| Flat color | Line art with solid colors, no shading | Medium-high --- character is fully colored |
| Cell shaded | Flat color with hard-edged shadows | High --- stylized, clean, graphic |
| Full render / painted | Fully shaded and rendered, often painterly | Highest --- gallery-quality illustration |

**Any additional details:** Accessories, clothing, props, specific lighting, mood, or anything else relevant. The more information the artist has, the fewer surprises in the WIP stage.

### Step 4: Pay the Deposit

Standard practice in furry commissions is a 50% deposit upfront, with the remaining 50% due upon completion (before final file delivery). Some artists require full payment upfront; some offer payment plans for larger commissions. The TOS specifies the payment structure.

**Common payment methods:**
- **PayPal** --- the most widely used. Send as "goods and services" for buyer protection (some artists request "friends and family" to avoid fees, but goods and services protects both parties in a dispute).
- **Ko-fi** --- streamlined for creative transactions. Commission-specific payment links are common.
- **Venmo / CashApp** --- used by some artists but offer less buyer protection than PayPal.
- **Invoice** --- some artists send formal invoices through PayPal or Ko-fi. Pay promptly.

**Never pay with cryptocurrency** unless you have an established, trusted relationship with the artist and understand that crypto transactions are irreversible with no buyer protection.

### Step 5: Wait

This is the hardest part. Commission queues vary enormously:

- **Quick turnaround artists:** 1--2 weeks
- **Moderately busy artists:** 3--6 weeks
- **Popular artists with long queues:** 2--6 months
- **Convention/event season:** Add extra time; artists are often traveling and producing convention inventory

During the wait, resist the urge to message the artist asking for updates more frequently than once every 2--3 weeks. Artists work on multiple commissions simultaneously, and frequent check-ins feel like pressure even when they are well-intentioned. If the artist provides an estimated delivery date, do not contact them before that date unless they have gone silent for significantly longer than the estimate.

### Step 6: Review the WIP (Work in Progress)

Most artists send at least one WIP --- typically a rough sketch or line art stage --- for your approval before proceeding to color and final rendering. This is your opportunity to request changes.

**Appropriate WIP feedback:**
- "The left ear should be rounded, not pointed --- here is the reference again"
- "Could the expression be a bit more confident? The reference shows a wider smile"
- "The tail should be bushier --- more like the reference sheet"

**Inappropriate WIP feedback:**
- Completely changing the concept ("Actually, can we do a different pose?")
- Requesting changes to the artist's style ("Can you make it more like [other artist's] style?")
- Adding elements not in the original brief ("Can you add a second character and a background?")

Changes to concept, additional characters, or significant scope increases require additional payment. The WIP review is for accuracy and minor adjustments, not redesign.

### Step 7: Pay the Remainder and Receive the Final File

Once you approve the WIP and the artist completes the final rendering, you pay the remaining balance. The artist then delivers the final file --- typically a high-resolution PNG or PSD, sometimes with a web-resolution version for posting.

**File sizes:** Full-resolution commission files are often 3000--5000 pixels on the longest side at 300 DPI. Keep the original high-resolution file stored safely. You can resize it for posting, but you cannot upscale a web-resolution file to print quality.

## Part 3: Pricing --- What Things Cost

Art prices reflect the artist's skill, time, materials (for traditional art), overhead (software, hardware, platform fees), and market position. Furry commission prices are generally lower than comparable work in the commercial illustration market, which makes the furry community one of the most accessible places to get custom art.

| Commission Type | Typical Price Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sketch (bust/headshot) | $15--30 | Quick, expressive, great for avatars |
| Sketch (full body) | $25--50 | Captures the whole character design |
| Line art (bust) | $20--40 | Clean, polished drawing |
| Line art (full body) | $35--75 | Portfolio-quality line work |
| Flat color (bust) | $30--60 | Character in full color, minimal shading |
| Flat color (full body) | $50--100 | Full character render, clean and graphic |
| Full illustration (one character) | $100--300 | Fully rendered, shaded, possibly with background |
| Full illustration (complex, background, multiple characters) | $200--500+ | Gallery-quality work, significant time investment |
| Reference sheet (front + back + details) | $100--300 | The foundational document; worth every dollar |
| Badge art (convention badge) | $15--50 | Small, portable, often laminated |
| Telegram/Discord sticker set | $50--200 | 5--20 expression stickers for messaging apps |

**Prices vary enormously** based on the artist's experience, demand, speed, and market. An emerging artist might charge $30 for a full-body flat color; an established artist with years of portfolio work and a long queue might charge $200 for the same scope. Both prices are valid. You are paying for the artist's skill AND their time AND the years of practice that produced that skill.

## Part 4: Etiquette --- Being a Good Client

The furry commission ecosystem runs on mutual respect between artists and clients. Being a good client is not complicated, but it makes a significant difference in the artist's experience and, by extension, in the quality of the work you receive.

### Do Not Haggle

Artists set their prices based on their costs, their time, and their assessment of their own skill and market position. Asking for a discount --- "Can you do it for less?" --- communicates that you do not value their labor. If the price is beyond your budget, either choose a simpler commission type (sketch instead of full render) or save until you can afford the piece you want. There is no shame in budget constraints. There is shame in asking an artist to undervalue their work.

### Do Not Rush

If the estimated turnaround is four weeks, do not ask for it in two. Artists manage complex queues with multiple clients. Rushing one commission means deprioritizing someone else's. If you need art by a specific deadline (a convention, a birthday), communicate the deadline upfront --- before the commission starts --- so the artist can decide whether they can accommodate it.

### Credit the Artist When Posting

When you post your commission on social media, forums, or anywhere else, credit the artist by name and link. "Art by @[artist handle]" takes five seconds and is the single most valuable thing you can do for an artist besides paying them. Credit drives traffic. Traffic drives future commissions. Future commissions keep the artist doing what they do.

### Tip If You Love It

Tipping is not required. It is not expected. It is deeply appreciated. If the finished commission exceeds your expectations --- if the artist captured something about your character that you could not articulate, if the quality is beyond what you paid for, if the piece makes you feel something --- send an extra $10, $20, or whatever you can. Artists remember clients who tip. Not because they play favorites, but because the gesture communicates genuine appreciation in the most tangible form available.

### Communicate Clearly, Then Trust the Artist

Provide a clear brief, a good reference sheet, and specific (but not micromanaging) direction. Then step back and let the artist do their job. You hired them because you like their style. Let them apply that style to your character. The best commissions result from clear input and artistic freedom --- the client provides the "what" and the artist provides the "how."

## Part 5: The Emotional Reality

There is something that happens when you receive your first commission that no description fully prepares you for. Your character --- the one that existed only in your imagination, maybe in a rough sketch, maybe in a text description --- is suddenly THERE. Rendered in full color, with light and shadow and expression, by someone who understood what you were trying to communicate and made it real.

For many furry community members, the first commission is the moment the fursona transitions from concept to identity. The character stops being "a character I am working on" and becomes "my character." The emotional weight of that transition is real. It is why people frame their commissions, make them their phone wallpaper, set them as profile pictures across every platform, and sometimes cry a little when they first see the finished piece.

This is not trivial. It is not silly. It is the experience of being seen --- of having an aspect of your identity recognized, interpreted, and rendered with skill and care by another human being. It is art functioning as art has always functioned: as a mirror, a window, and a bridge between inner experience and outer expression.

## Part 6: Supporting Artists

The furry art ecosystem is one of the largest independent creative economies in the world. Thousands of artists --- from hobbyists earning supplemental income to full-time professionals whose commission work is their livelihood --- depend on the community's willingness to pay for custom art. When you commission an artist, you are not just buying a picture. You are:

- Funding someone's rent, groceries, and art supplies
- Validating years of practice and skill development
- Contributing to a creative economy that exists outside corporate gatekeeping
- Ensuring that the next generation of furry artists sees a viable path to making art for a living

The furry community's commission culture is genuinely remarkable. In most creative industries, artists struggle to find clients willing to pay fair prices for custom work. In the furry community, the demand for custom art consistently outpaces supply. This is a community that values art, pays for art, and treats artists as essential members of the ecosystem rather than as interchangeable service providers.

Support artists. Commission art. Pay on time. Tip when you can. Credit always. The community is built on this exchange, and every commission sustains it.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [Creative Process, Patronage]: Commissioning art is participation in the oldest model of art production: patronage. The client provides the concept and the funding; the artist provides the skill, interpretation, and execution. Understanding the commission process from the client side teaches the student how creative collaboration works --- the balance between direction and freedom, the role of reference materials in visual communication, and the value of skilled labor in producing original work.
- **Home Economics** --- [Budgeting, Service Procurement]: Commissioning custom art is a financial decision that requires budgeting (saving for the commission, choosing the right scope for your budget), comparison shopping (evaluating artists by style, price, and reliability), and contract literacy (reading and understanding the artist's TOS). These are the same skills applied to any service procurement --- hiring a contractor, engaging a freelancer, purchasing custom goods.
- **Art** --- [Community and Ecosystem]: The furry commission ecosystem is a case study in creative community economics. Artists price their labor, clients fund the ecosystem through purchases, platforms facilitate discovery and transaction, and conventions provide physical marketplaces. Understanding how this ecosystem functions --- how prices are set, how reputation is built, how trust operates between strangers transacting for custom creative work --- teaches economic literacy through direct participation.
