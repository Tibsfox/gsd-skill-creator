# Try Session: Design Your First Fursona Character

## The Character That Is Yours

Before the fur, before the foam, before the sewing machine and the hot glue and the wire armature --- before any of it --- there is a character. A fursona is an anthropomorphic animal character that represents you in the furry community. It is an expression of identity, personality, and creative vision distilled into an animal form. Some people design their fursona in five minutes (a fox, orange, with green eyes --- done). Some people spend months refining details, commissioning art, and iterating through versions. Both approaches are valid. There is no wrong way to design a fursona, and there is no deadline.

What there is, eventually, is a reference sheet --- a visual document that shows your character from multiple angles with color specifications, distinguishing features, and key details. The reference sheet is the blueprint for everything that follows: art commissions, fursuit construction, avatar creation, badge art, and the mental image that other people in the community associate with you. A clear, detailed reference sheet is the single most useful document in the furry creative ecosystem, because it allows anyone --- artist, maker, or friend --- to represent your character accurately.

This try session walks you through the design process, from choosing a species to producing a basic reference sheet. You do not need to be an artist. You do not need drawing software. You do not need to spend money (though commissioning a professional reference sheet is an option covered at the end). You need a pencil, paper, colored pencils or markers, and the willingness to make creative decisions about who your character is.

## Step 1: Choose Your Species

The species choice is the foundation. It determines the silhouette, the proportions, the ear shape, the tail, the paws, and the cultural associations that your character carries. Some species are common in the furry community; some are rare. There is no hierarchy. The only criterion is: what animal resonates with you?

### Common Species and Their Associations

| Species | Community Prevalence | Cultural Vibe | Physical Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fox | Very common (most popular) | Clever, playful, mischievous | Pointed ears, bushy tail, narrow muzzle |
| Wolf | Very common | Loyal, strong, pack-oriented | Tall pointed ears, thick bushy tail, powerful build |
| Dog (various breeds) | Common | Friendly, loyal, expressive | Breed-specific features (floppy ears, curled tail, etc.) |
| Cat (domestic) | Common | Independent, curious, graceful | Triangle ears, long thin tail, lithe body |
| Dragon | Common | Powerful, mystical, unique | Wings, horns, scales, long tail, infinite variation |
| Rabbit | Moderate | Gentle, quick, approachable | Long ears, short round tail, large eyes |
| Deer | Moderate | Gentle, elegant, forest-associated | Antlers (optional, usually male), short tail, slender build |
| Bird (various) | Moderate | Freedom, vision, grace | Wings (functional or decorative), beak, feathers/plumage |
| Sergal | Moderate (fandom-original) | Alien, sleek, unique (originated by Mick Ono/Trancy Mick) | Wedge-shaped head, long ears, digitigrade legs |
| Protogen | Growing (fandom-original) | Tech-savvy, expressive, futuristic | LED visor face, armored limbs, digital expressions |
| Big cat (lion, tiger, etc.) | Moderate | Regal, powerful, confident | Species-specific patterns (stripes, mane, spots) |
| Otter | Growing | Playful, social, aquatic | Sleek body, webbed paws, thick rudder tail |
| Bat | Niche | Nocturnal, unique, misunderstood | Large ears, wing membranes, compact body |
| Hybrid | Common | Creative, unique, "best of both" | Mix features from two or more species |

### Choosing Guidance

**If you already know:** Great. Go with it. Intuition is valid.

**If you are torn between two species:** Make a hybrid. Fox-wolf, cat-dragon, deer-rabbit --- hybrids are common and let you incorporate features from multiple animals that resonate with you. There are no rules about which species can be combined.

**If you have no idea:** Ask yourself:
- What animal have you always been drawn to?
- What animal do friends or family associate with you?
- What animal's personality traits feel like your own?
- What animal do you enjoy watching, reading about, or drawing?
- When you imagine yourself as an animal, what do you see?

**If the answer is still "I don't know":** Start with a fox or a wolf (the community defaults), build the rest of the design, and change the species later if something else resonates. A fursona is not permanent. It evolves as you evolve. Many people have gone through multiple species before finding the one that fits.

### Non-Traditional Species

You are not limited to mammals. Avian fursonas (birds, gryphons), reptilian fursonas (dragons, lizards, snakes), aquatic fursonas (sharks, dolphins, fish), insect fursonas (moths, beetles), and entirely original species (closed or open species, personal creations) are all present in the community. If the animal you connect with is a moth, your fursona is a moth. The only limitation is your own creativity.

## Step 2: Design Your Color Palette

Color is what makes your character visually distinct. A well-chosen color palette is more memorable than complex markings or unusual features.

### Primary Color

This is the dominant color of your character's fur, feathers, or scales. It covers the largest area of the body (back, sides, head, limbs). Choose a color that you love --- not one you think is expected. Orange foxes and gray wolves are common, but a blue fox or a purple wolf is equally valid. Realistic colors create a naturalistic feel; unnatural colors create a fantastical or stylized feel. Both are legitimate.

### Secondary Color

The secondary color covers smaller areas: chest, belly, muzzle, inner ears, paw pads, tail underside. In natural animals, the secondary color is usually lighter than the primary (countershading). In fursona design, it can be anything. The secondary color provides contrast and visual interest against the primary.

### Accent Color

One or two accent colors for details: eye color, markings, hair color (if your character has a hairstyle), accessories, claw color. Accent colors should contrast with both the primary and secondary colors to be visible.

### Building Your Palette

**Method 1: Start from nature.** Look at reference photos of your species. What are the natural colorations? A red fox has orange-red primary, white secondary (chest, muzzle, tail tip), and black accents (ears, legs, nose). Starting from nature and tweaking gives a character that reads as believable.

**Method 2: Start from a color you love.** Pick your favorite color as the primary. Choose a lighter or contrasting color for the secondary. Pick an eye color that pops against both.

**Method 3: Use a palette generator.** Websites like coolors.co or paletton.com generate harmonious color palettes from a starting color. Input your primary color and explore complementary, analogous, or triadic combinations.

### Record Your Colors as Hex Codes

When you commission art or build a fursuit, the artist or maker needs your exact colors. "Kind of a teal blue" is ambiguous. #2B7A78 is not. Use a color picker (available in any image editing software, or at htmlcolorcodes.com) to select your exact colors and record the hex codes.

| Element | Color Name | Hex Code |
|---|---|---|
| Primary (body) | ___________ | #______ |
| Secondary (chest/belly) | ___________ | #______ |
| Accent 1 (eyes) | ___________ | #______ |
| Accent 2 (markings) | ___________ | #______ |
| Nose/paw pads | ___________ | #______ |
| Hair (if applicable) | ___________ | #______ |

Fill this table in as you design. It becomes part of your reference sheet.

## Step 3: Define Distinguishing Features

Distinguishing features are what make your character YOUR character, not "a generic fox." These are the details that an artist includes to make the character recognizable.

### Physical Features

- **Eye color and shape:** Heterochromia (two different colored eyes) is a popular distinguishing feature. Unusual eye colors (red, gold, purple) stand out.
- **Markings:** Stripes, spots, patches, gradients, symmetrical or asymmetrical patterns. A single distinctive marking (a star on the forehead, a heart-shaped chest patch, an asymmetric ear color) is more memorable than many small details.
- **Scars:** A scar across the muzzle, over one eye, on an ear. Scars suggest history and personality.
- **Horns or antlers:** Even on species that do not naturally have them (a fox with small antlers, a cat with curved horns).
- **Wings:** Functional, decorative, or vestigial. Wings can be added to any species.
- **Hair/mane:** A hairstyle or mane that differs from the body fur. Color, length, and style are all design variables.
- **Tail features:** Unusual tail shape, multiple tails, a tail tip of a different color.
- **Ear features:** Notched ears, extra-long ears, ears with piercings or accessories, floppy vs. pointed.
- **Body type:** Muscular, slim, chubby, tall, short. Your character's body type is a design choice.

### Personality Features (Inform the Visual Design)

- A cheerful character might have bright, warm colors and an open, friendly expression on the reference sheet.
- A mysterious character might have darker colors, half-lidded eyes, and subtle markings.
- A playful character might have asymmetric markings, a tongue-out expression, and a dynamic pose.

The visual design communicates personality before a single word is spoken.

### Accessories and Clothing

Some fursonas have signature accessories:
- Glasses, goggles, or a visor
- A scarf, bandana, or collar
- Jewelry (ear piercings, necklace, bracelet)
- A hat or hoodie
- Armor elements (bracers, pauldrons)
- A specific outfit or uniform

Accessories are optional and should not overwhelm the character design. One or two signature items are more memorable than a character wearing everything.

## Step 4: Draw the Reference Sheet

The reference sheet (ref sheet) is the visual document that defines your character. A basic ref sheet includes:

1. **Front view:** Full body, standing, arms slightly away from the body so all markings are visible.
2. **Side view:** Full body, profile, showing ear shape, muzzle profile, tail, and body proportions.
3. **Back view:** Full body, showing tail, back markings, and any features not visible from the front.
4. **Color palette:** Swatches with hex codes for every color used.
5. **Important details:** Close-ups of eyes, paw pads, distinctive markings, or features that might be missed in the full-body views.

### If You Can Draw

Sketch the three views on a single sheet of paper. Keep proportions consistent between views. Use light pencil lines for the initial sketch, then refine and ink the final lines. Color with colored pencils, markers, or digitally if you scan the line art.

**Tips:**
- Start with simple geometric shapes (circles for the head, ovals for the torso, cylinders for limbs) to establish proportions before adding detail.
- The front and side views should be the same height.
- Mark the color boundaries clearly --- where does the primary color end and the secondary begin?
- Include the tail in all views where it is visible (front, side, and back).

### If You Cannot Draw (Or Do Not Want To)

**Option 1: Use a base.** Free furry reference sheet bases (blank template outlines) are available on DeviantArt, FurAffinity, and Toyhouse. Search "free furry ref sheet base" or "free [species] ref sheet base." These are blank line-art templates that you can print and color in, or import into a digital art program and color digitally. Many artists create these bases specifically for the community to use.

**Option 2: Use a character creator.** Online character creation tools allow you to assemble a fursona visually from pre-made parts:
- **Picrew** (picrew.me): Many user-created furry/anthro character creators. Search for your species.
- **FurAffinity character generators:** Various community-created tools.
- **VRoid Studio** (for 3D): Free 3D character creation tool; some users have adapted it for anthro characters.

Character creators do not produce reference sheets, but they produce a visual representation that you can use as a reference when describing your character to an artist.

**Option 3: Commission an artist.** A professional reference sheet commission from a furry artist typically costs $20-100 for a basic sheet (front view + color palette) and $50-200+ for a detailed sheet (multiple views, expressions, detail callouts). Furry art communities on FurAffinity, DeviantArt, Twitter, and Telegram are the primary marketplaces. See the commissioning section below.

## Step 5: Commissioning a Professional Reference Sheet (Optional)

A professionally drawn reference sheet is a significant upgrade over a self-drawn or base-colored version. It establishes your character's visual canon and gives you a polished document to share with other artists and makers.

### Where to Find Artists

- **FurAffinity** (furaffinity.net): The largest furry art community. Search for "commission open" or browse artist profiles.
- **DeviantArt** (deviantart.com): Large art community with an active furry section.
- **Twitter/X:** Many furry artists post commission opening announcements. Follow artists whose style you like.
- **Telegram:** Furry art auction and commission groups.
- **Artists' Alley at conventions:** Many artists take reference sheet commissions in person at furry conventions.

### Price Ranges (2024-2026)

| Type | Description | Price Range |
|---|---|---|
| Flat color ref sheet | Front view, simple color palette, minimal detail | $20-50 |
| Standard ref sheet | Front + side views, color palette, basic details | $50-100 |
| Detailed ref sheet | Front + side + back, expressions, detail callouts, full palette | $100-200 |
| Premium ref sheet | All of the above plus multiple expressions, accessories, alternate forms | $200-400+ |

### Commissioning Tips

1. **Write a clear description.** Include species, color hex codes, distinguishing features, personality, and any must-have details. The more specific you are, the more accurate the result.
2. **Provide visual references.** If you have sketches, color-in bases, or photos of real animals that match your vision, include them. Artists work better with visual references than text alone.
3. **Respect the artist's process.** Most artists provide a sketch for approval before finalizing. Give feedback at the sketch stage --- changes after coloring/inking are much more work.
4. **Pay on time and in full.** The furry art community runs on trust and reputation. Timely payment builds a good reputation that makes future commissions easier.
5. **Credit the artist.** When sharing your reference sheet, credit the artist who drew it. This is both courteous and community standard.

## Step 6: Living With Your Fursona

A fursona is not a one-time design exercise. It is a living character that grows as you grow.

### It Can Change

Species, colors, markings, accessories, personality --- all of these can evolve. Many community members have gone through multiple fursona iterations. A character redesign is not a failure; it is creative growth. Your fursona at 20 may look different from your fursona at 30, and that is exactly right.

### It Can Have Depth

A fursona can have a backstory, a personality, relationships with other characters, a home world, a profession, hobbies, fears, and dreams. Some people develop extensive lore for their character. Some keep it simple (name, species, colors). Both approaches are valid. The depth is up to you.

### It Can Be Private or Public

Some people share their fursona widely: social media profiles, convention badges, commissioned art, fursuit. Some people keep their fursona private or semi-private. The furry community respects both choices. Your character, your rules.

### It Can Be Separate From You

A fursona is an expression of identity, but it is not required to be autobiographical. Your fursona can be a different gender, a different body type, a different personality from your everyday self. It can be an aspirational version of you, an alternate version of you, or a character entirely independent of you. The relationship between you and your fursona is personal and does not need to be explained to anyone.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [Creative Process and Visual Design]: Designing a fursona is a complete creative cycle: concept development (choosing species and personality), visual design (color palette, silhouette, distinguishing features), technical execution (reference sheet production), and iteration (refinement over time). It exercises color theory, character design principles, proportional drawing, and visual communication --- the same skills used in concept art, illustration, and graphic design.
- **Psychology** --- [Identity Expression]: The fursona is a vehicle for exploring and expressing aspects of identity --- personality, gender, body image, social presentation --- in a context that is creative, playful, and low-stakes. Research from the International Anthropomorphic Research Project (Furscience) documents positive psychological outcomes associated with fursona development, including improved self-concept and social belonging.
- **Learning** --- [Self-Directed Creative Development]: The fursona design process is entirely self-directed. There is no curriculum, no grading rubric, no external standard of correctness. The designer sets their own goals, makes their own aesthetic judgments, seeks feedback on their own terms, and iterates based on their own satisfaction. This is intrinsically motivated creative learning --- the purest form of the creative process.
