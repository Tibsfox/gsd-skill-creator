# Try Session: Build Your First Fursuit Paws

## The Gateway Project

Every fursuit maker started somewhere. Most of them did not start with a full suit, or even a head. They started with paws. Fursuit paws --- the glove-like hand coverings that transform human hands into animal feet, cartoon mitts, or clawed appendages --- are the simplest, most forgiving, most immediately gratifying entry point into the world of fursuit construction.

There are good reasons for this. Paws are small, so materials cost less ($30-50 for a first pair). They are structurally simple compared to heads (no foam carving, no jaw mechanisms, no vision systems). They are forgiving of mistakes --- a slightly uneven seam or a misaligned toe pad is barely noticeable in the finished product. They can be worn with regular clothes, meaning you get to experience the magic of wearing something you made without committing to a full suit. And they teach the foundational skills --- pattern making, fur cutting, sewing, and finishing --- that scale directly to more complex fursuit components.

This try session walks you through building a pair of fursuit hand paws from scratch. No prior sewing experience is assumed, though you should be comfortable with a needle and thread (a sewing machine is helpful but not required). By the end, you will have a wearable pair of paws and the confidence to know that you can make things with your hands that did not exist before you made them.

That confidence is the real product. The paws are just the proof.

## Materials List ($30-50)

Before you start, gather everything. There is nothing more frustrating than getting halfway through a project and realizing you are missing a critical material.

### Base Gloves ($5-10)

You need one pair of gloves that fit your hands comfortably. These form the structural base onto which you will build the paws. Options:

- **Work gloves** (cotton or leather): Sturdy, good structure, easy to glue to. The best option for a first pair.
- **Winter gloves** (fleece or knit): Softer, more flexible, easier to sew through. Good if you plan to sew rather than hot-glue the fur.
- **Gardening gloves** (knit with rubberized palm): Inexpensive, snug fit, widely available.

Avoid latex, nitrile, or rubber gloves --- they do not hold glue or stitches. Avoid gloves that are too thick (ski gloves) or too loose (oversized work gloves). The glove should fit like a glove. You will be wearing this for hours; comfort matters.

### Faux Fur (1/2 Yard, $8-15)

One half-yard piece of faux fur in your character's primary color. For a first pair, choose a **short to medium pile** fur (under 1.5 inches). Long pile fur (the shaggy stuff used for fursuit bodies) is harder to work with --- it sheds everywhere, obscures seam lines, and is more difficult to cut cleanly. Short pile gives cleaner results and is more forgiving for beginners.

**Where to buy:**
- **Fabric stores** (Joann, Hobby Lobby, Fabric.com): Widest selection. Joann's "Luxury Shag" and Shannon Fabrics "Cuddle" lines are popular starting points.
- **Online specialty retailers** (FurSuit Supplies, BigZ Fabric, Distinctive Fabric): Better quality faux fur than craft stores, particularly for unusual colors and longer piles.
- **Etsy:** Many sellers offer pre-cut faux fur bundles specifically for fursuit projects.

**Cost-saving tip:** Half a yard is more than you need for paws alone. Save the scraps for practice, small projects, or matching pieces later.

### Paw Pad Material (1/4 Yard, $3-8)

The paw pad is the contrasting-color palm pad and toe beans that give fursuits their character. You need a material that is smooth, slightly stretchy, and available in your character's pad color (pink, black, gray, and dark brown are the most common).

**Best options:**
- **Minky fabric** (also called "cuddle" or "minkee"): Ultra-soft, slightly stretchy, excellent for paw pads. The most commonly used paw pad material in the fursuit community.
- **Fleece:** Readily available, inexpensive, easy to work with. Less plush than minky but perfectly adequate.
- **Anti-pill fleece:** A subset of fleece with a smoother surface. Good for a cleaner paw pad look.

Avoid felt for paw pads on wearable pieces --- it is stiff, does not stretch, and wears out quickly with use. Felt is fine for decorative plush but not for functional paw pads that will contact surfaces.

### Construction Supplies ($10-15)

- **Hot glue gun and glue sticks:** The primary bonding method for fursuit construction. A full-size (not mini) glue gun with a temperature setting is ideal. High-temperature glue creates stronger bonds; low-temperature glue is more forgiving but less durable. If you have never used a hot glue gun, buy extra sticks --- you will use more than you expect.
- **Fabric scissors:** Sharp scissors dedicated to fabric. Do not use your paper scissors. Faux fur dulls blades quickly; craft scissors will be destroyed. A pair of sharp fabric shears ($5-10) is essential. For cutting faux fur, many makers prefer small, sharp embroidery scissors or a rotary cutter --- the goal is to cut the backing without cutting the pile.
- **Pins:** Standard sewing pins for holding pieces in place before sewing or gluing.
- **Needle and thread:** Heavy-duty hand-sewing needles (sharps or tapestry needles) and strong thread (upholstery thread or heavy-duty polyester). You will be hand-sewing through multiple layers of fur and glove.
- **Pattern paper or newspaper:** For tracing your hand and creating pattern pieces.
- **Pen or marker:** For tracing and marking.
- **Ruler or measuring tape:** For measuring and marking seam allowances.

### Optional: Claws ($5-10)

Claws are not required but add visual impact. The simplest method:

- **Sculpey or Fimo polymer clay:** Available at any craft store. Sculpt claw shapes, bake at the temperature specified on the package (usually 275F for 15 minutes per 1/4 inch of thickness), let cool, paint with acrylic paint, seal with clear coat, and hot-glue to the fingertips of the finished paws.
- **Pre-made plastic claws:** Available from fursuit supply shops on Etsy. Faster, more uniform, but less personal.

## Step 1: Create Your Pattern (30-45 Minutes)

The pattern is the blueprint for every piece of fur you will cut. Taking time here saves time (and material) later.

### Trace Your Hand

1. Place your hand flat on the pattern paper, fingers spread comfortably. Not splayed wide --- just naturally spread, as your hand would rest in the glove.
2. Trace around your hand with a pen, following the outline of each finger, the thumb, the palm, and the wrist. Trace loosely --- about 1/4 inch away from your actual skin. This is your basic hand outline.
3. Repeat for the other hand (or flip the pattern, since hands are mirror images).

### Create Finger Pieces

Each finger (and the thumb) needs a fur tube that will slide over the corresponding glove finger. For each finger:

1. Measure the circumference of the glove finger at its widest point (usually the base).
2. Measure the length of the glove finger from base to tip.
3. Draw a rectangle: width = finger circumference + 1/2 inch (for seam allowance), height = finger length + 1/2 inch.

You will need 10 rectangles total (5 per hand). Label them: L-thumb, L-index, L-middle, L-ring, L-pinky, R-thumb, R-index, R-middle, R-ring, R-pinky. (In practice, all the non-thumb fingers are often similar enough to use the same pattern piece, but measuring each gives a better fit.)

### Create Palm/Back Pieces

For the top (back) of the hand:
1. Use your hand trace as the base shape.
2. Extend the outline 1/4 inch on all sides for seam allowance.
3. Cut off the finger portions (the fur tubes will cover the fingers separately).
4. You should have a shape that covers from the wrist to the base of the fingers, including the thumb web.

For the bottom (palm) of the hand:
1. Same shape as the top, but this is where the paw pad will go.
2. Trace the same outline.

### Create Paw Pad Pieces

The paw pad is the most character-defining element. A typical paw pad consists of:
- **1 large palm pad:** An oval or slightly heart-shaped piece centered on the palm. Approximately 2-3 inches wide and 2-3 inches tall, depending on hand size.
- **4 toe beans** (or 5, if your character has a bean for each finger): Small oval shapes, approximately 3/4 inch to 1 inch long. These sit at the base of each finger.

Draw these shapes on your pattern paper, add 1/4 inch seam allowance, and label them. The pad and bean shapes vary by species and personal style --- look at reference images of your character (or real animal paws, or other fursuits you admire) for inspiration.

---

## Step 2: Cut the Fur (30-45 Minutes)

Cutting faux fur correctly is the single most important skill in fursuit construction. Done right, the seams will be nearly invisible in the finished product. Done wrong, you will have visible cut lines and bald spots.

### The Cardinal Rule: Cut the Backing, Not the Pile

Faux fur has a woven or knitted backing with pile (the fuzzy part) attached to one side. When you cut, you want to slice through the backing only, leaving the pile intact. This means:

1. **Place the fur face-down** (pile side down, backing facing you).
2. **Trace your pattern pieces on the backing** with a pen or marker.
3. **Check the nap direction.** Faux fur has a nap --- the pile lies in one direction, like petting a cat from head to tail. Run your hand over the pile to feel which way it lays flat. For paws, the nap should generally run from wrist toward fingers (downward). Mark the nap direction on each pattern piece with an arrow.
4. **Cut with small, sharp scissors** (embroidery scissors or a craft knife), cutting only the backing. Slide the scissors or blade along the backing, lifting the pile out of the way as you go. Do not use large fabric shears for this --- they will cut through the pile and leave visible trim lines.
5. **Mirror your pieces.** Remember that left and right paws are mirror images. When cutting the second set, flip your pattern pieces over.

### Cut the Paw Pad Pieces

Paw pad material (minky, fleece) does not have the same cutting challenges as faux fur. Standard fabric scissors work fine. Cut on the traced line. If using minky, it has a nap --- for small pieces like toe beans, nap direction is not critical, but for the palm pad, orient the nap consistently.

---

## Step 3: Sew the Finger Tubes (1-2 Hours)

Each finger piece is a rectangle that becomes a tube.

1. **Fold the rectangle in half lengthwise**, fur side (pile side) facing inward. The backing should be on the outside.
2. **Sew or hot-glue the long edge** to create a tube. If sewing by hand, use a whip stitch or backstitch with heavy-duty thread, keeping the seam allowance at 1/4 inch. If hot-gluing, apply a thin line of glue along the edge and press firmly.
3. **Close one end of the tube** (the fingertip). Gather the raw edges at one end and sew or glue them together. It does not need to be pretty --- this end will be at the fingertip and covered by the seam.
4. **Turn the tube right-side-out.** The fur should now be on the outside, the seam on the inside.
5. **Slide the tube over the corresponding glove finger.** It should fit snugly but not so tightly that it compresses the finger. If it is too tight, you need a wider rectangle. If it is too loose, you can take in the seam.
6. **Glue or stitch the base of the tube to the glove** at the base of the finger. A ring of hot glue around the base, pressed firmly, creates a secure bond.

Repeat for all 10 fingers. This is the most time-consuming step, but it is repetitive and you will get faster with each finger.

---

## Step 4: Attach the Hand Panels (1-1.5 Hours)

With the fingers covered, you now cover the palm and back of the hand.

### Back (Top) Panel

1. Place the back-of-hand fur piece over the glove, fur side out, aligning it with the glove shape. The finger edge should butt up against the base of the fur finger tubes.
2. Pin in place. Check the fit --- it should lie flat without bunching or pulling.
3. Glue or sew the edges of the panel to the glove. Start at the wrist, work around the thumb web, across the base of the fingers, and back to the wrist on the other side.
4. Where the panel meets the finger tubes, tuck the raw edges under and glue/sew to create a clean transition. This seam does not need to be invisible --- the fur pile will hide minor imperfections once you brush it.

### Palm Panel

1. Same process as the back panel, but on the palm side.
2. **Before attaching the palm panel**, attach the paw pad and toe beans to it (see Step 5). It is much easier to attach the pads to a flat piece of fabric than to a piece already glued to a curved glove.

---

## Step 5: Attach the Paw Pads (30-45 Minutes)

This is the step that brings the paws to life. The paw pads --- the palm pad and toe beans --- are the defining feature that makes a fur-covered glove into a fursuit paw.

### Palm Pad

1. Position the palm pad piece (minky or fleece) on the palm panel (fur piece, face up). Center it in the palm area.
2. Pin in place.
3. **Applique the pad onto the fur.** The cleanest method is to fold the raw edges of the pad under (about 1/8 inch), pin, and then whip-stitch the folded edge to the fur using a ladder stitch or blind stitch. Hot glue works but can create a stiffer edge. For a first pair, hot glue is faster and perfectly acceptable.
4. Press firmly and let the glue set.

### Toe Beans

1. Position the toe bean pieces at the base of each finger area on the palm panel. Typically four beans --- one for each finger (not the thumb), evenly spaced.
2. Attach using the same method as the palm pad: fold edges under and stitch, or hot-glue.
3. For extra dimension, you can stuff a tiny amount of fiberfill behind each toe bean before sealing it. This makes the beans puffy and three-dimensional --- a small detail that makes a big difference in the finished look.

### Now Attach the Palm Panel to the Glove

With the paw pads attached, follow the same procedure as the back panel: position, pin, glue/sew around the edges, tuck and finish where it meets the finger tubes.

---

## Step 6: Finishing (30-45 Minutes)

### Wrist Finishing

The wrist edge of the paw needs a clean finish. Options:

- **Fold and glue:** Fold the raw edge of the fur under by 1/2 inch and hot-glue it to the inside of the glove wrist. Simple, clean, slightly stiff.
- **Elastic cuff:** Sew a strip of elastic inside the wrist edge to create a snug cuff that holds the paw on your hand. More comfortable, more professional-looking.
- **Raw edge (lazy method):** If the fur does not fray (most faux fur does not fray significantly), you can leave the wrist edge as-is. The pile will cover the raw edge. This is the fastest option and looks fine for a first pair.

### Brush the Seams

Take a wire pet brush (a slicker brush, available at any pet store for $3-5) and gently brush the fur along every seam. The pile gets trapped in the seams during construction, making the seam lines visible. Brushing pulls the trapped pile out and across the seam, blending it and making the seam nearly invisible. This single finishing step transforms the look of the paws from "I can see every seam" to "where did the seams go?"

### Add Claws (Optional)

If you made polymer clay claws:
1. Ensure they are fully cooled after baking.
2. Paint with acrylic paint (usually black, white, or a color matching your character). Let dry. Apply a clear sealant (Mod Podge, clear nail polish, or spray clear coat).
3. Hot-glue each claw to the tip of the corresponding fur finger tube. Position them on the top (back) of the fingertip, angled slightly downward. Hold in place until the glue sets.

## Time Estimate and Expectations

- **Total time for a first pair:** 4-6 hours, spread over one or two sessions.
- **Experienced makers:** 2-3 hours per pair.
- **Quality expectation:** Your first pair will not look like something from a professional studio. They will have uneven seams, slightly crooked toe beans, and glue strings in unexpected places. This is normal. This is how every maker started. The difference between the first pair and the tenth pair is practice, not talent.

## The Emotional Payoff

There is a moment, near the end of this project, when you slide the finished paws onto your hands, flex your fingers, and look at what you made. Something that did not exist four hours ago now exists because you made it. Not because you bought it. Not because a machine made it. Because you measured, cut, sewed, and glued with your own hands.

For many people in the fursuit community, this moment is transformative. It is the moment when "I wish I could make things" becomes "I make things." The paws might be imperfect. They will almost certainly be imperfect. But they are yours, and they are real, and they work. You can wave. You can high-five. You can wrap your paws around a cup of coffee and feel the fur against the ceramic.

That is the gateway. From here, some people make tails. Some people make arm sleeves. Some people start planning a head. Some people are content with paws and never go further. All of these are valid. The point was never to build a full fursuit. The point was to discover that you can build anything you decide to build.

## Troubleshooting

**The fur is shedding everywhere.**
This is normal, especially with long-pile fur. It gets better after the first few cuts. Work in an area you can vacuum. Wear clothes you do not mind getting furry. Accept that your life will contain fur fibers for the next several days.

**The glue soaked through the fur.**
You used too much glue. Scrape off the excess while it is still warm. For future applications, use thin lines of glue rather than large blobs. Hot glue on faux fur is an art --- you develop a feel for the right amount.

**The finger tubes are too tight/too loose.**
Seam allowance is the fix. If too tight, add 1/4 inch to the width of the rectangle and remake the tube. If too loose, take the seam in by sewing a new seam slightly inside the first one.

**The paw pads are peeling off.**
If hot-glued, the bond may have failed because the glue cooled before the pieces were pressed together (common in cold rooms). Re-glue, pressing firmly and holding for 10 seconds. If the pads will see heavy use, reinforce with a few stitches at the corners.

**One paw looks different from the other.**
Asymmetry is normal in handmade items. It is also not visible when the paws are worn. Your brain will notice the difference when they are side by side on a table. Other people will not notice when they are on your hands.
