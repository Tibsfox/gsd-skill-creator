# DIY Project: Build a Fursuit Tail

## The Easiest Piece with the Biggest Impact

A tail is the simplest fursuit component that is visible from across a room. It swings when you walk, bounces when you dance, and reads as "animal character" instantly --- even paired with jeans and a t-shirt. Unlike a fursuit head, which requires foam carving, vision systems, and jaw mechanisms, or paws, which demand fitted finger construction, a tail is fundamentally a tube of faux fur with something inside it. The construction is forgiving. The materials are inexpensive. And the result is wearable immediately, in any context from a convention floor to a costume party to a Tuesday afternoon because you felt like it.

This project presents three tail designs, from a two-hour beginner build to a more involved mechanism that swings on its own. Each design uses the same basic materials and sewing skills, with the internal structure as the variable. If you have built fursuit paws (see the [First Fursuit Paws try session](first-fursuit-paws.md)), you have every skill you need. If you have not, this is an equally good starting point --- arguably better, because a tail is even more forgiving than paws and the emotional payoff of something that moves is immediate.

## Materials List (All Three Designs)

### Faux Fur: 1/2 Yard ($8-15)

One half-yard of faux fur in your character's primary tail color. Short to medium pile (under 2 inches) is easiest to work with for a first tail. Long pile (2-4 inches) creates a fluffier, more dramatic tail but is harder to sew and sheds more during construction.

If your character has a multi-color tail (fox with a white tip, raccoon with dark rings), you will need a second color. For a fox tail, 1/2 yard of the primary color (orange, red, or brown) plus a small piece (6x12 inches is enough) of white for the tip.

**Where to buy:** Joann Fabrics, Hobby Lobby, Fabric.com, FurSuit Supplies on Etsy. See the [Fur Fabric study guide](../study-guides/fur-fabric-study-guide.md) for detailed buying guidance.

### Polyfil Stuffing ($4-8)

A 12-ounce bag of polyester fiberfill. Available at any craft store. You will use about half the bag for a standard-length tail (18-24 inches). Save the rest for future projects.

### Belt or Belt Loop Attachment ($0-5)

The tail must attach to your body. Options:
- **Belt loop:** A fabric loop sewn to the base of the tail that threads onto your belt. The simplest method.
- **Dedicated belt:** A cheap fabric belt ($3-5) sewn directly into the tail's base. The tail and belt become one unit.
- **Safety pins:** For temporary attachment to pants or a waistband. Quick and removable but less secure.
- **Carabiner clip:** A small carabiner ($2) sewn into the base of the tail clips onto a belt loop. Easy on/off.

### Sewing Supplies ($5-10 if you do not already have them)

- Sewing needle (hand-sewing sharp or tapestry needle)
- Heavy-duty polyester thread (match to fur backing color, not pile color)
- Pins (straight pins for holding pattern pieces)
- Fabric scissors (sharp, dedicated to fabric)
- Measuring tape or ruler
- Pen or marker (for marking the fur backing)

### Design-Specific Materials

| Design | Additional Materials | Additional Cost |
|---|---|---|
| BASIC Stuffed | None | $0 |
| WAGGING | 1/4-inch steel rod or wood dowel (24"), fishing sinker or small weight (2-4 oz) | $3-5 |
| POSEABLE | 14-gauge armature wire (3 feet), quilt batting (1/4 yard) | $5-8 |

## Design 1: BASIC Stuffed Tail (2 Hours, $15)

The classic entry-level tail. A tapered tube of faux fur, stuffed with polyfil, with a belt loop at the base. No internal structure. It hangs and swings naturally with body movement.

### Pattern

1. **Measure your desired tail length.** Most tails are 18-24 inches for a first build. Measure from the small of your back to where you want the tail tip to fall (mid-thigh is standard for canine/feline characters).

2. **Create a tapered pattern piece.** On the back of the fur (pile side down), draw a long, tapered shape:
   - Base width: 10-12 inches (this will form a circumference of 5-6 inches when sewn into a tube)
   - Tip width: 2-3 inches (tapers to a point or a rounded tip)
   - Length: your measured tail length plus 1/2 inch seam allowance at each end
   - The taper should be gradual and symmetrical. Draw a center line first, then measure equal widths on each side.

3. **Cut two identical panels** (front and back of the tail). Place the pattern on the fur backing, mark the pile direction (pile should flow from base toward tip --- base-to-tip is the natural direction for all animal tails), and cut through the backing only. Use small, sharp scissors. Never cut through the pile.

### Sewing

4. **Pin the two panels right sides together** (fur facing inward). Pin along both long edges and around the tip. Leave the base open.

5. **Sew the seam.** Hand-stitch with a backstitch along both long edges and around the tip, maintaining 1/4-inch seam allowance. If using a sewing machine, use a long stitch (3.0-4.0 mm) and a walking foot if available. Tuck pile away from the seam line as you sew.

6. **Turn the tail right-side-out.** Reach into the open base, grab the tip, and pull the whole tail through itself. The fur is now on the outside. Use a chopstick or the eraser end of a pencil to push the tip fully out.

7. **Extract trapped pile from the seams.** Use a pin or the tip of a tapestry needle to pull fur fibers that got caught in the seam out to the correct side. Then brush the seam lines with a wire pet brush (slicker brush) to blend the pile across the seams. This single step transforms visible seams into invisible ones.

### Stuffing

8. **Stuff the tail.** Pull polyfil apart into small, fluffy tufts. Do not stuff with large clumps --- they create lumps. Starting at the tip, push small tufts in with a dowel or chopstick, filling evenly and firmly. The tail should feel solid but not rock-hard. The tip should be firmly packed (it takes the most abuse). The base can be slightly less dense (it will compress against your body).

9. **Create the belt loop.** Cut a strip of sturdy fabric (denim, canvas, or a folded strip of the fur backing) approximately 2 inches wide and 6 inches long. Fold the long edges inward, press or pin, and sew into a flat strap. Form the strap into a loop large enough to thread a belt through (about 3 inches of loop) and sew the ends into the inside of the tail's open base.

10. **Close the base.** Tuck the raw edges of the fur inward and hand-sew the base closed with a ladder stitch (the invisible closure stitch --- see the [Sewing Basics study guide](../study-guides/sewing-basics-for-fursuit-makers.md)). Sew through and around the belt loop attachment to secure it.

11. **Groom.** Brush the entire tail with a wire pet brush, paying special attention to the seam lines. The tail should look like a single continuous surface of fur, with no visible seams or construction lines.

**Done.** Thread your belt through the loop, clip it on, and admire the fact that you now have a tail.

## Design 2: WAGGING Tail (4 Hours, $25)

The wagging tail uses a counterweight and a semi-rigid core to create a pendulum effect. When you walk, your hips shift side to side. That hip movement transfers through the core to the counterweight at the tip, causing the tail to swing --- a convincing, organic-looking wag that happens automatically with natural movement.

### Internal Structure

1. **The core:** A 1/4-inch wooden dowel or steel rod, cut to approximately 2/3 the length of the tail. The rod provides semi-rigidity that transmits hip movement to the tip. If using wood, sand the ends smooth. If using steel, file the cut ends.

2. **The counterweight:** A fishing sinker (2-4 ounces), a steel nut, or a small pouch of sand or BBs. The weight is attached at the tip of the rod. The heavier the weight, the more dramatic the wag --- but too much weight makes the tail feel cumbersome. Start with 2 ounces and adjust.

3. **Attach the counterweight to the rod.** For a fishing sinker: wrap wire around the sinker and the rod tip, then duct-tape the connection for security. For a nut: bolt it to the rod end. For a sand pouch: sew a small fabric pouch, fill it, sew it closed, and lash it to the rod tip with wire and tape.

### Assembly

4. **Construct the fur tube** using the same pattern and sewing method as Design 1 (steps 1-7).

5. **Insert the counterweighted rod into the fur tube.** Slide it in with the weight end going to the tip. Center the rod inside the tube.

6. **Stuff around the rod.** Pack polyfil around the rod to hold it centered and prevent it from shifting. The stuffing should be firm enough to keep the rod from rattling but not so tight that the tail becomes completely rigid. The tail needs some flex to translate hip movement into swing.

7. **At the base:** The rod should extend to within 2-3 inches of the opening. The base of the rod rests against your lower back, transferring your hip movement into the pendulum. Pad the base end with extra stuffing so the rod end does not poke through.

8. **Create the belt attachment and close the base** using the same method as Design 1 (steps 9-10).

### Tuning the Wag

The wag is a function of three variables: rod stiffness, counterweight mass, and tail length. A longer tail with a heavier weight swings more slowly and dramatically. A shorter tail with a lighter weight swings quickly. Adjust to match your character's personality: a happy golden retriever tail wags fast and wide; a dignified wolf tail swings slow and measured.

**Test by walking.** Put the tail on, walk around your living room, and observe the swing in a mirror. If the wag is too subtle, add weight or increase rod stiffness. If the wag is too wild, reduce weight or add stuffing to dampen the swing.

## Design 3: POSEABLE Tail (3 Hours, $20)

The poseable tail uses an armature wire core that holds any shape you bend it into. Curl it over your arm for photos. Drape it to one side for a relaxed look. Curve it upward in an alert, happy pose. The wire holds the pose indefinitely until you decide to change it.

### Internal Structure

1. **Armature wire:** 14-gauge aluminum armature wire, available at art supply stores and online (Dick Blick, Amazon). Cut a length equal to your tail length plus 6 inches (extra for the base anchor). 14-gauge is stiff enough to hold a pose but flexible enough to bend by hand without tools.

2. **Quilt batting:** One quarter-yard of quilt batting (the flat, sheet-like stuffing material used in quilts). You will wrap the wire in batting to create a padded, smooth core that prevents the wire from creating visible bumps through the fur.

### Assembly

3. **Prepare the wire core.** Cut the armature wire to tail length plus 6 inches. At the base end, bend the last 3 inches into a loop (this prevents the wire from poking through the fabric and provides an attachment point). At the tip, file or tape the cut end to prevent a sharp point.

4. **Wrap the wire in batting.** Starting at the tip, wrap quilt batting around the wire in a spiral, building up thickness. The base of the tail should be wrapped to about 1.5-2 inches in diameter. The tip should taper to about 1/2 inch. Secure the batting by wrapping thread around it at several points along the length, or use small dots of hot glue to tack it in place.

5. **Construct the fur tube** using the same pattern and sewing method as Design 1 (steps 1-7). Make the tube slightly larger in diameter than the batting-wrapped wire to accommodate the core without stretching the seams.

6. **Insert the wrapped wire core** into the fur tube. Slide it in carefully, tip first. The batting should fit snugly inside the fur tube without bunching.

7. **Add polyfil as needed.** If there are gaps between the batting core and the fur tube, stuff small amounts of polyfil to fill them. The tail should feel uniformly smooth, with no visible wire bumps or hollow spots.

8. **Secure the base.** The wire loop at the base can be sewn to the belt loop or belt for attachment. The loop also prevents the wire from sliding up into the tail. Close the base with a ladder stitch.

### Posing

Bend the tail into any shape: a gentle S-curve, a sharp upward curl, a relaxed downward droop, a question-mark shape. The 14-gauge wire will hold the pose. To change poses, simply bend to the new shape. The wire will work-harden over time with repeated bending at the same point --- eventually it will weaken and break at that point. For maximum longevity, avoid bending sharply at the same spot repeatedly. Use gentle curves distributed along the length.

## Species-Specific Tail Shapes

The silhouette and proportions of your tail should match your character's species. The emotional read of a tail is strongly species-coded: a fluffy, white-tipped tail reads as "fox" even on a character that is not explicitly a fox.

| Species | Length | Shape | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fox | 14-18" | Bushy, slightly curved downward | White tip (classic), tapered from thick base to medium tip, dense pile |
| Wolf | 16-22" | Bushy, straight to slightly curved | Thick and full throughout, darker tip or dorsal stripe, heavy pile |
| Cat (domestic) | 12-16" | Thin, curved upward or S-shaped | Slender, graceful, short to medium pile, expressive curve |
| Big cat (lion) | 18-24" | Long, slender with tuft at tip | Thin shaft, fur tuft at the very end, short pile on shaft |
| Raccoon | 10-14" | Medium thickness, straight, RINGED | Alternating dark/light rings (4-6 rings), the defining feature |
| Dog (husky) | 12-16" | Bushy, curled over the back | Thick, sickle-shaped curl, dense undercoat pile |
| Dragon | 18-30" | Long, thick, may have spade or fin at tip | Scaled or smooth texture, spine ridges, arrowhead tip optional |
| Rabbit | 2-4" | Short, round puffball | Cotton-ball shape, almost spherical, very short build |

### Raccoon Rings: A Special Case

A raccoon tail requires alternating bands of light and dark fur. To construct:

1. Cut the tail into horizontal sections (4-6 sections, alternating colors).
2. Sew each section from its two panels first (a dark ring, a light ring, etc.).
3. Sew the rings together end-to-end in alternating colors, matching the seam lines.
4. The result is a striped tube that, when turned right-side-out and stuffed, displays the characteristic raccoon banding.

Alternatively, cut the entire tail from one color and airbrush the rings onto the finished, stuffed tail using textile paint. This is easier but less durable --- painted fur can mat or fade with handling.

## Attachment Methods

How the tail attaches to your body determines how secure, comfortable, and adjustable it is.

### Belt Loop (Easiest)

A fabric loop sewn into the tail base, threaded onto your belt. The tail hangs from the belt and swings freely. Pros: simple, secure, works with any belt. Cons: the tail sits at belt height, which may be higher than anatomically correct for some species; requires wearing a belt.

### Safety Pins (Temporary)

Two large safety pins through the tail base fabric and your waistband. Pros: no permanent attachment, works with any clothing. Cons: can slip, may damage clothing, not secure during vigorous movement.

### Integrated Waistband

Sew the tail directly into a fabric waistband (an elastic band or a separate belt that wraps the waist and closes with Velcro or a buckle). The tail becomes a self-contained wearable with its own waist closure. Pros: most secure, adjustable position, distributes weight. Cons: more construction, dedicated attachment (cannot easily move between outfits).

### Bodysuit Integration

For a complete fursuit, the tail is sewn directly into the bodysuit at the anatomically correct position (lower back/sacrum area). The tail is permanent to the suit. See [FFA-04 Fursuit Fabrication](../04-fursuit-fabrication.md) for bodysuit construction details.

## Troubleshooting

**The tail is floppy and lifeless.**
Under-stuffed (for BASIC design) or insufficient counterweight (for WAGGING design). For BASIC: remove some stuffing from the base, add more at the tip, and pack more firmly throughout. For WAGGING: increase the counterweight by 1-2 ounces.

**The wagging tail wags too violently.**
The counterweight is too heavy or the rod is too stiff. Reduce weight, or replace the steel rod with a wooden dowel (which flexes more and dampens the swing). You can also add more stuffing around the rod to increase damping.

**The poseable tail will not hold its shape.**
The armature wire is too thin. 14-gauge is the minimum for a tail that holds a pose. If you used thinner wire (16- or 18-gauge), replace it with 14-gauge. Alternatively, double the wire by twisting two strands together.

**The seams are visible.**
Brush the seam lines aggressively with a wire pet brush (slicker brush). Pull trapped pile out of the seam with a pin or needle. For long-pile fur, vigorous brushing will make seams nearly invisible. For short-pile fur, seams are more visible --- careful sewing and pile extraction are essential.

**The fur is shedding everywhere.**
Normal, especially with long-pile fur. It gets better after the first day. Vacuum frequently. Wear dark clothing if the fur is light, or light clothing if the fur is dark. Accept the shedding as part of the craft.

**One side of the tail looks different from the other.**
Pile direction mismatch. When cutting your two panels, ensure the pile flows in the same direction on both pieces (base to tip). If the pile flows opposite on one panel, it will catch light differently and the two sides will appear to be different colors.
