# Try Session: Sew Your First Stuffed Animal --- The Simple Cat

## The Complete Beginner's Project

There is a particular satisfaction in holding something soft that you made. Not assembled from a kit, not downloaded and printed, but conceived, cut, stitched, stuffed, and finished by your own hands. A stuffed animal --- a plush --- is one of the most accessible entries into textile craft because the outcome is forgiving (seams hide in fur, shapes are approximate by nature), the materials are inexpensive, and the finished object has an emotional weight that far exceeds its physical weight.

This try session teaches you to sew a simple cat plush from scratch. The cat is the traditional first plush project across the making community for good reason: cats have simple silhouettes, forgiving proportions, and a shape that reads clearly even when the execution is imperfect. A lumpy cat is still recognizably a cat. A lumpy horse is a cryptid.

The design used here is adapted from the principles covered in the [Plush Pattern Making deep dive](../deep-dives/plush-pattern-making.md), simplified for a first attempt. The pattern has 10 pieces. The construction requires basic hand sewing. No sewing machine is needed (though one speeds things up). The finished cat will stand approximately 8 inches tall and 10 inches long, firmly stuffed, with safety eyes and a personality that emerges somewhere between the first stitch and the last.

---

## Materials List ($15-25)

### Fabric: 1/4 Yard ($5-10)

Choose one of:
- **Faux fur (short pile, under 1/2 inch):** The most plush-like result. Hides seams beautifully. Slightly harder to sew by hand due to thickness. A great choice if you want the finished cat to look like a stuffed animal from a store.
- **Fleece (anti-pill):** The easiest fabric for a first plush. Soft, stretchy, does not fray, and sews easily by hand or machine. Available in dozens of solid colors at any fabric store. The most commonly recommended starter fabric.
- **Minky/Cuddle:** Ultra-soft, slightly stretchy, slippery. Gorgeous finished texture but harder to control during sewing. Save this for your second or third project.

**Color:** Whatever makes you happy. Calico cats need multiple colors; solid-color cats need one. For a first project, a single solid color simplifies cutting and sewing significantly.

**Amount:** One quarter-yard (9 x 44-60 inches, depending on bolt width) is more than enough for one cat. You will have scraps left over.

### Thread ($2-3)

Heavy-duty polyester thread in a color matching your fabric. Standard all-purpose polyester thread works but is thinner; upholstery thread or heavy-duty hand-quilting thread is stronger and easier to work with for hand sewing. You will use more thread than you expect --- buy a full spool.

### Stuffing: Polyester Fiberfill ($4-8)

A 12-ounce bag of polyester fiberfill is more than enough for one cat. Available at any craft store (Poly-Fil is the most common brand). You want the loose, fluffy fiberfill, not the batting sheets used for quilting.

**Stuffing tip:** Pull the fiberfill apart into small, fluffy tufts before stuffing. Shoving large clumps into the body creates lumps. Small tufts, pushed in gradually and evenly, create a smooth, firm plush.

### Safety Eyes: 2 x 12mm ($2-4)

Safety eyes are the standard eyes for stuffed animals. They consist of a colored plastic eye (the part visible on the outside) and a metal or plastic washer (the part that locks onto the post from the inside). Once the washer is snapped onto the post through the fabric, it cannot be removed without destroying it. This is the safety feature --- the eye cannot be pulled out by a child.

**Size:** 12mm is appropriate for a cat plush of this size. Sizes range from 6mm to 30mm. Larger eyes create a cuter, more cartoon-like look. Smaller eyes create a more realistic or understated look.

**Color:** Black is standard. Amber, green, and blue are available for more specific character designs.

**Where to buy:** Craft stores, Amazon, Etsy (search "safety eyes for amigurumi" or "safety eyes for stuffed animals"). They are sold in packs of 10-50 pairs for $3-8.

### Tools ($3-5, if you don't already have them)

- **Hand sewing needles:** Sharps (standard pointed needles) in medium size. A variety pack is useful.
- **Pins:** Standard straight pins for holding fabric pieces together before sewing.
- **Fabric scissors:** Sharp scissors dedicated to fabric cutting.
- **Pen or fabric marker:** For tracing pattern pieces onto fabric. A washable fabric marker is ideal; a ballpoint pen on the wrong side of the fabric works fine.
- **Ruler or measuring tape**
- **Chopstick or stuffing tool:** For pushing fiberfill into small areas (ears, tail). A chopstick, the blunt end of a pencil, or a dedicated stuffing tool all work.

## The Pattern: 10 Pieces

This pattern creates a cat with a two-panel body (left and right), a belly gusset that forms the bottom, a head top gusset that rounds the skull, four ear pieces (two per ear), and a tail. All measurements include a 1/4-inch seam allowance.

### Piece 1 & 2: Body Side Panels (cut 2, mirror)

Each body side panel is a single piece that forms one side of the cat from nose to tail base. The shape resembles a simplified cat silhouette in profile.

**Dimensions (approximate):**
- Total length (nose to tail base): 10 inches
- Maximum height (top of head to belly): 5 inches
- Head portion: Rounded dome, approximately 3.5 inches wide and 3.5 inches tall
- Neck: Gentle inward curve, narrowing to about 2.5 inches
- Body: Slightly oval, about 4 inches wide and 3.5 inches tall
- Legs: Two simple tab-like extensions at the bottom of the body, each about 1.5 inches wide and 1.5 inches long (these are simplified legs --- not individually articulated)
- Tail base: A slight extension at the rear end, about 1 inch, where the separate tail piece will attach

**Curves:** The top line of the body panel curves smoothly from the nose, up over the head, down through the neck, and along the back to the tail. The bottom line curves from the chin, under the chest, across the belly (with the two leg tabs), and up to the tail base. All curves should be smooth and gentle --- no sharp angles.

**Mirror note:** Cut one panel, then flip the pattern to cut the second. These are left and right sides of the cat. If using fabric with a nap (faux fur), ensure the nap runs in the same direction on both panels (nose to tail, or head to foot).

### Piece 3: Belly Gusset (cut 1)

The belly gusset is a long, tapered oval that forms the underside of the cat from chin to tail base. It connects the two body side panels along the bottom.

**Dimensions:**
- Length: 8 inches (from chin to tail base, matching the bottom curve of the body panel)
- Maximum width: 2.5 inches (at the widest point, which is the belly)
- Tapers to about 1 inch at both ends (chin and tail base)

**Shape:** Think of an elongated eye shape, or a surfboard viewed from above. Wider in the middle, narrowing at both ends.

### Piece 4: Head Top Gusset (cut 1)

The head top gusset is a long, narrow piece that runs from the nose, over the top of the head, and down to the back of the neck. It gives the head its three-dimensional roundness. Without it, the head would be flat --- two side panels pressed together.

**Dimensions:**
- Length: 6 inches (from nose tip to the back of the neck, where it terminates)
- Maximum width: 2 inches (at the widest point, which is the crown of the head)
- Tapers to a point at the nose end
- Tapers to about 1 inch at the neck end

**Shape:** A long teardrop, wide at the crown and pointed at the nose.

### Pieces 5-8: Ear Pieces (cut 4 --- two pairs)

Each ear is made from two identical triangles sewn together and then turned right-side-out. A cat's ear is roughly an equilateral triangle with slightly curved sides.

**Dimensions:**
- Base: 2 inches
- Height: 2 inches
- Sides: Gently curved outward (convex) for a natural ear shape

Cut 4 total: 2 for the left ear, 2 for the right ear. If using two colors (e.g., fur on the outside, pink fleece on the inside), cut 2 in each fabric.

### Pieces 9-10: Tail (cut 2)

The tail is a long, gently tapered tube. Each tail piece is a long, narrow shape, wider at the base and tapering to a rounded point at the tip.

**Dimensions:**
- Length: 5 inches
- Width at base: 1.5 inches
- Width at tip: 0.75 inches (rounded, not pointed)

Cut 2, these will be sewn together face-to-face and turned right-side-out.

## Construction: Step by Step

### Step 1: Trace and Cut (30-45 Minutes)

1. Lay your fabric wrong-side-up (if using faux fur, backing facing you; if using fleece, either side works as fleece has no "wrong side," but be consistent).
2. Trace all pattern pieces onto the fabric using your marker. Leave at least 1/2 inch between pieces for cutting room.
3. **Mark the nap direction** on every piece if using faux fur. All pieces should have the nap running in the same general direction (top to bottom, or nose to tail).
4. Cut out all pieces. If using faux fur, cut from the back using small scissors, cutting only the backing (see the [First Fursuit Paws try session](first-fursuit-paws.md) for the full technique). If using fleece, standard fabric scissors work fine.

**Inventory check:** You should have: 2 body side panels (mirrored), 1 belly gusset, 1 head top gusset, 4 ear pieces, 2 tail pieces. Total: 10 pieces.

### Step 2: Sew the Ears (15-20 Minutes)

1. Take two ear pieces and place them together, right sides facing (fur/pretty side inward).
2. Pin along the two curved sides, leaving the base (bottom) open.
3. Sew the two curved sides using a backstitch, 1/4 inch from the edge. Use small, even stitches.
4. **Turn right-side-out** through the open base. Use a chopstick to push the point out from inside. The ear should now be a puffy triangle with fur on the outside and a clean seam.
5. **(Optional) Topstitch:** Sew a line of stitches about 1/8 inch from the edge around the curved sides. This flattens the seam and gives the ear a more defined shape. This is purely cosmetic and can be skipped.
6. Repeat for the second ear.

Set the ears aside. They will be inserted later.

### Step 3: Sew the Head Top Gusset (20-30 Minutes)

This is the step that gives the cat a three-dimensional head rather than a flat profile.

1. Take one body side panel and the head top gusset.
2. Find the nose point on the body panel (the front-most point) and the nose point on the head top gusset (the tapered point).
3. Place them right sides together, aligning the nose points.
4. Pin the gusset to the body panel along the top curve of the head, from the nose, over the crown, to the back of the neck. The gusset will curve along the top of the body panel's head section.
5. Sew along this curve using a backstitch, 1/4 inch from the edge. Start at the nose point and work toward the neck. Go slowly around the curves --- ease the fabric as needed.
6. Repeat with the second body side panel on the other side of the gusset. You should now have a head with two sides and a rounded top, open at the bottom.

### Step 4: Install Safety Eyes --- BEFORE CLOSING (10 Minutes)

**This step must happen now.** Safety eyes are installed from the outside through the fabric and locked with a washer from the inside. Once the body is sewn closed and stuffed, you cannot install them. This is the most common beginner mistake: sewing the whole animal closed and then realizing the eyes needed to go in earlier.

1. Determine eye placement on the body side panels. For a cat, the eyes sit on the head, about 1/3 of the way down from the top of the head, spaced about 1.5 inches apart (adjusting for the gusset width). Mark the positions with pins.
2. **Push the safety eye post through the fabric from the outside.** Use the point of a closed pair of scissors or an awl to poke a small hole first if the fabric is thick.
3. **From the inside, snap the washer onto the post.** Push it down firmly until it clicks. It should be tight against the fabric. **Once the washer is on, it cannot be removed.** Make sure the eye is exactly where you want it before snapping the washer.
4. Repeat for the second eye.
5. **Check symmetry.** Look at the face straight on. Are the eyes level? Are they evenly spaced? Adjust now if possible (which means cutting off a washer with pliers and re-doing the eye, or accepting the asymmetry --- remember, handmade things have character).

### Step 5: Sew the Body (30-45 Minutes)

Now you join the two body panels with the belly gusset to create the three-dimensional body.

1. Place one body side panel and the belly gusset right sides together.
2. Align the chin point of the body panel with one end of the belly gusset.
3. Pin along the bottom curve of the body panel, from the chin, under the chest, along the belly, between and around the legs, to the tail base.
4. Sew with a backstitch, 1/4 inch from the edge. The legs require careful pinning --- sew down one side of each leg tab, across the bottom, and up the other side.
5. Repeat with the second body side panel on the other side of the belly gusset.
6. Sew the remaining open seams: the back of the neck (where the head gusset ends) down the back to the tail base. This closes the back seam.

**Leave a 3-inch opening in the belly.** This is your turning and stuffing hole. Mark it with pins so you do not accidentally sew it closed. The opening should be in a relatively flat area of the belly, away from curves and leg junctions.

### Step 6: Insert the Ears (10-15 Minutes)

Before turning the body right-side-out, insert the ears.

1. Determine ear placement on the head. Ears sit on the top of the head, on either side of the head gusset, angled slightly outward.
2. **Tuck each ear into the head** with the ear tips pointing downward, toward the nose (because the body is still inside-out, this means the ear tips will point upward when the body is turned right-side-out).
3. Pin the raw base edges of each ear to the head seam, aligning them with the seam between the head gusset and the body panels.
4. Sew the ear bases into the seam with a few reinforcing stitches. The ears need to withstand being pulled, so stitch firmly.

### Step 7: Turn Right-Side-Out (5 Minutes)

Reach through the 3-inch belly opening and gently pull the entire cat through the hole, turning it right-side-out. The fur/pretty side should now be on the outside. The seams should be on the inside. The ears should be standing up. The safety eyes should be looking at you.

Use a chopstick to push out any areas that did not turn fully --- the nose, the leg bottoms, the ears. Take your time. Fabric that is not fully turned will show as dents or flattened areas in the finished plush.

### Step 8: Sew and Attach the Tail (15-20 Minutes)

1. Place the two tail pieces right sides together.
2. Pin and sew around the curved sides and tip, leaving the base (wide end) open.
3. Turn right-side-out. Use a chopstick to push the stuffing-tool through the narrow tip.
4. Stuff the tail firmly. Use small tufts of fiberfill, pushed in from the base.
5. Pin the open base of the tail to the tail-base area on the body, with the raw edges tucked in.
6. Stitch the tail to the body using a ladder stitch (see Step 10 for ladder stitch technique). Sew around the full circumference of the tail base.

### Step 9: Stuff Firmly (15-20 Minutes)

This is where the cat comes to life.

1. **Start with the head.** Push small tufts of fiberfill through the belly opening, up into the head. Use a chopstick to push stuffing into the nose and cheeks. The head should be VERY firm --- firmer than you think. A well-stuffed head holds its shape, shows off the eyes properly, and looks alert rather than deflated. Most beginners under-stuff the head. Then they add more and it still is not enough. Then they add more and it finally looks right.

2. **Stuff the legs.** Push fiberfill into each leg with the chopstick. Pack it in. The legs should be firm enough to support the cat if you want it to stand (though standing is not guaranteed on a first project).

3. **Stuff the body.** Fill the body cavity evenly. Slightly less firm than the head --- you want the body to be huggable, with a little give, but not floppy. No lumps. If you feel a lump, pull it out, tear the fiberfill into smaller tufts, and re-stuff.

4. **Check for symmetry.** Squeeze the cat. Is one side fuller than the other? Add stuffing where needed. Does the belly have a flat spot? Add more.

### Step 10: Close the Belly Opening --- The Ladder Stitch (15-20 Minutes)

The ladder stitch (also called a slip stitch or blind stitch) is the invisible closing technique used by every plush maker. It is the single most useful stitch you will learn in this project, and you will use it on every plush you ever make.

**How it works:**

1. Thread your needle with a double length of matching thread. Knot the end.
2. Insert the needle into the fold of fabric on one side of the opening, hiding the knot inside. Come out about 1/8 inch ahead.
3. Cross to the other side of the opening and insert the needle into the fold of fabric directly across from where you exited. Travel 1/8 inch inside the fold and come out.
4. Cross back to the first side. Insert directly across, travel 1/8 inch, come out.
5. Continue this pattern: the thread crosses the opening in parallel horizontal lines (like the rungs of a ladder, hence the name).
6. After every 4-5 stitches, pull the thread gently but firmly. The two sides of the opening will draw together and close. The stitches disappear between the folds.
7. When the opening is fully closed, take a few small reinforcing stitches, then bury the needle into the body, come out an inch or two away, pull tight, and clip the thread. The tail of the thread will retract into the stuffing and disappear.

The ladder stitch takes practice to make truly invisible. On your first cat, it will be visible but acceptable. By your third or fourth plush, it will vanish.

## Time Estimate

- **First attempt:** 6-10 hours total, best spread over 2-3 sessions
- **With practice:** 3-5 hours
- **The longest part:** Sewing the body seams by hand. A sewing machine cuts this to a fraction of the time, but hand sewing works and teaches you the fundamentals of how seams behave.

## What You Will Learn

By completing this project, you will have practiced:
- **Pattern making:** Translating a three-dimensional shape into flat pattern pieces
- **Nap direction management:** Aligning fabric grain/pile for consistent appearance
- **Seam construction:** Backstitch, whip stitch, and ladder stitch
- **Three-dimensional shaping:** Using gussets to create volume from flat pieces
- **Safety eye installation:** The one step you cannot undo
- **Stuffing technique:** The art of even, firm filling without lumps

These skills are the foundation of all plush construction, from simple animals to complex characters to the competition-grade art plush covered in [FFA-06] Plush Construction.

## Troubleshooting

**The head is lumpy.**
Under-stuffed. Pull some stuffing out, break it into smaller tufts, and re-stuff more evenly and more firmly. The head should feel like a firm stress ball, not a pillow.

**The body is floppy.**
Under-stuffed. Add more fiberfill. A plush that flops over needs more stuffing in the core of the body. Pack it tighter.

**The seams are visible.**
For faux fur: brush the seams with a pet slicker brush to pull trapped pile across the seam line. For fleece: seams will always be slightly visible, but they blend in at arm's length. Smaller, more even stitches help.

**The ears are crooked.**
They probably are. Handmade ears are notoriously asymmetric on a first project. You can remove them (carefully cut the stitches), reposition, and re-attach. Or you can accept the asymmetry as character. Real cats have expressive, independently movable ears --- yours just happen to be permanently expressive.

**I sewed the body closed without installing the safety eyes.**
This happens to almost every beginner. You have two options: carefully open a seam near the eye area, install the eye, and re-sew the seam; or use embroidered eyes (satin stitch a circle of black thread where the eye should be) or glue-on craft eyes (less secure, but functional for a display piece).

**The fabric frays at the edges.**
Faux fur and fleece generally do not fray. If you are using a woven fabric, the edges will fray. Apply a thin line of fray-check or clear nail polish to the seam allowance edges before sewing.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Art** --- [Materials and Making]: The transformation of flat fabric into a three-dimensional form is a sculptural act. Pattern design, color selection, proportion, and finishing details (eye placement, ear angle, stuffing firmness) are all aesthetic decisions that shape the character and emotional impact of the finished object.
- **Trades** --- [Sewing]: Every stitch in this project --- backstitch for seams, whip stitch for edges, ladder stitch for closures --- is a fundamental sewing technique that transfers to garment construction, upholstery, quilting, and any textile trade. The hand-sewing skills practiced here are the foundation of machine sewing literacy.
- **Home Economics** --- [Textile Craft]: Plush making sits at the intersection of craft and domestic skill. Understanding fabric properties, thread types, stuffing materials, and construction methods is textile literacy --- the ability to look at any soft object and understand how it was made and how to make it better.
