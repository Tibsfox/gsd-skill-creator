# From Pincushion Elephants to Giant Giraffes -- The History of Stuffed Animals

A chronological survey of the stuffed animal industry from the 1880s through the present day. Traces the origins of the teddy bear through dual competing claims, the rise and consolidation of major manufacturers, the pivotal role of licensing and pop culture, the shift of production to Asia, the Beanie Baby speculative bubble, and the emergence of new forms -- from Build-A-Bear customization to Squishmallow soft sculpture. Examines how safety regulation, material science, and cultural forces shaped the plush animal from a handmade novelty into a global industry generating billions of dollars annually.

## 1. Before the Bear: Origins of the Stuffed Animal

Rag dolls and stuffed cloth figures appear in archaeological records across cultures -- ancient Egyptian tombs contained fabric animals, and medieval European households produced stuffed cloth toys from scraps. But these were one-off, handmade objects. The stuffed animal as a manufactured consumer product is a phenomenon of the late nineteenth century, inseparable from the industrialization of textile production and the emergence of childhood as a distinct consumer category.

Before the stuffed animal industry existed, there were toy animals made of wood, tin, papier-mache, porcelain, and carved bone. The stuffed animal's advantage was tactile: it was soft, squeezable, warm to the touch, safe to sleep with. It could be hugged. No wooden horse could compete with that. The raw materials that made the industry possible were themselves products of industrialization: machine-woven felt, commercially produced cotton batting, and later mohair plush fabric (made from the fleece of Angora goats, processed in Yorkshire mills).

## 2. Margarete Steiff and the Felt Elephant

The conventional starting point of the stuffed animal industry is 1880, in Giengen an der Brenz, a small town in the Swabian Alps of Baden-Württemberg, Germany. Margarete Steiff, born in 1847, had contracted polio as an infant and was partially paralyzed, using a wheelchair for most of her life. She was trained as a seamstress and ran a small felt clothing business from her home, producing coats, vests, and household textiles.

In 1880, Margarete encountered a pattern for a small felt elephant pincushion in a German fashion magazine -- *Modenwelt*, the story goes. She adapted the pattern and began producing the elephants as functional objects: pincushions for her customers' sewing rooms. But the children who accompanied those customers had other ideas. They kept taking the elephants to play with. The pincushions became toys through the force of children's desire.

Margarete recognized the market signal. She expanded production of the felt elephants and began developing other animal forms -- donkeys, horses, camels, pigs. By 1886, she had sold over 5,000 felt animals. By 1893, the operation had grown enough to be registered as the Margarete Steiff Felt Toy Company. The product line expanded, the workforce grew, and by the turn of the century, Steiff was one of the largest toy manufacturers in Germany.

What made Margarete Steiff remarkable beyond the founding narrative was her insistence on quality as a non-negotiable principle. Her personal motto, sewn into the company ethos, was "Only the best is good enough for children." This was not marketing copy. It was a manufacturing standard that dictated material selection, construction methods, and quality control at every stage. That standard survived her death in 1909 and continues to define the company's identity over a century later.

## 3. The Teddy Bear: A Dual Origin Story

The teddy bear -- the single most iconic stuffed animal in history -- has two origin stories, and the historical record supports both of them as roughly simultaneous, independent developments. The convergence is one of the great coincidences of toy history.

### 3.1 The American Story: Roosevelt and Michtom

On November 14, 1902, President Theodore Roosevelt was on a hunting trip in Sharkey County, Mississippi. His hosts, eager to ensure the President had a successful hunt, captured a black bear and tied it to a tree so Roosevelt could shoot it. Roosevelt refused, declaring the staged kill unsportsmanlike. The incident was depicted in a political cartoon by Clifford Berryman in The Washington Post on November 16, 1902, showing Roosevelt turning away from a small, sympathetic-looking bear.

Morris Michtom, a Russian immigrant who ran a candy and novelty shop in Brooklyn, saw the cartoon. His wife Rose made stuffed toys as a sideline. Together they produced a small stuffed bear, displayed it with a sign reading "Teddy's Bear," and -- according to family tradition -- wrote to Roosevelt requesting permission to use his name. Roosevelt reportedly replied that he could not imagine what use his name would be to the toy business, but they were welcome to it.

Michtom could not keep bears in stock. By 1903 he had founded the Ideal Novelty and Toy Company to manufacture them. No original Michtom-Roosevelt correspondence has been found, but Ideal became one of the largest toy manufacturers in America.

### 3.2 The German Story: Richard Steiff and Bear 55 PB

Simultaneously and independently, Richard Steiff -- Margarete's nephew, who had studied art at the Kunstgewerbeschule in Stuttgart -- was sketching bears at the Stuttgart Zoo and the traveling circuses that passed through Swabia. Richard designed a jointed stuffed bear with movable head, arms, and legs -- a significant engineering advance over the fixed-limb stuffed animals the company had been producing. The internal mechanism used disc joints connected by string or wire, allowing the limbs and head to rotate.

The prototype was cataloged internally as "Bear 55 PB" -- 55 centimeters tall, Plush, Beweglich (movable). Richard presented the bear at the Leipzig Toy Fair in March 1903. Initial response was tepid. But late in the fair, an American buyer -- Hermann Berg, representing George Borgfeldt & Company, a New York toy importer -- placed an order for 3,000 bears. The order changed everything.

Teddy bear mania swept both sides of the Atlantic simultaneously. American newspapers ran features on the new craze. Songs were written -- "The Teddy Bears' Picnic" was composed by John Walter Bratton in 1907. By that year, Steiff's Giengen factory was producing bears at an astonishing rate. The production records are precise: in 1907, the Steiff factory produced **973,999 stuffed bears** in a single year. The company called it the *Bärenjahre* -- the Bear Years.

Whether the teddy bear was born in Brooklyn or Giengen is a question that generates partisan heat among collectors and historians. The honest answer is: both, nearly simultaneously, for different reasons. Michtom gave the bear its name and its American identity. Steiff gave it jointed construction, mohair materials, and industrial production at scale. Together, they created the archetype.

## 4. The Button in Ear and the First Golden Age

In 1904, Franz Steiff (another of Margarete's nephews) had an idea that would prove to be one of the most consequential branding decisions in consumer product history. He proposed affixing a small metal button to the left ear of every Steiff animal as a mark of authenticity. The "Knopf im Ohr" -- Button in Ear -- was registered as a trademark in 1904, making it one of the first product trademarks in the world and certainly the first in the toy industry.

The button served dual purposes: quality guarantee for consumers and anti-counterfeiting measure for Steiff (imitators were already flooding the market by 1903). The system evolved into a layered code that collectors read like a language: brass button with red tag (standard production), yellow tag (classic replica), white tag (limited edition), blank button (pre-tag era, most valuable). The presence and condition of the button remain primary factors in authentication and valuation of vintage Steiff animals.

The period from 1903 through World War I constitutes the first golden age of the stuffed animal industry. The bear moved from novelty to cultural institution in barely a decade.

## 5. Gund and the American Plush Tradition

While Steiff defined the European tradition of premium handcrafted plush, the American market developed its own ecosystem. Adolph Gund, a German immigrant, founded Gund Manufacturing Company in Norwalk, Connecticut in 1898 -- predating even the teddy bear craze. Gund originally produced novelties, belts, and accessories before pivoting to stuffed toys.

Jacob Swedlin, who had worked for Gund, purchased the company in 1925 and refocused it exclusively on stuffed animals. Under Swedlin family leadership (which continued for decades), Gund became synonymous with affordable, well-made plush in the American mass market. They were not competing with Steiff on craftsmanship or prestige. They were making the stuffed animals that showed up in department stores, carnival prize booths, and baby shower gift bags across America.

Gund's contribution to the industry was scale and accessibility. They demonstrated that a quality stuffed animal could be produced at a price point that put it within reach of middle-class American families. Their "Snuffles" bear, introduced in the 1980s, became one of the best-selling stuffed animals in American history. The company remains family-controlled and privately held -- a rarity in an industry dominated by conglomerates.

## 6. Disney, Licensing, and Character Plush

The partnership between Walt Disney and the Steiff company in 1931 marked the beginning of what would become the dominant economic model for the stuffed animal industry: character licensing. Steiff produced Mickey Mouse stuffed figures under license from Disney, creating a new category -- the character plush, in which the consumer is buying not just a soft animal but a specific fictional character with an existing emotional relationship to the buyer.

This model would eventually dwarf the traditional stuffed animal market. Once consumers could buy a plush version of a character they already loved, the animal was selling a pre-existing emotional bond. Disney's consumer products division generated billions from plush alone. Every major animated film became a product line. Other studios followed: Warner Bros., Universal, Sanrio (Hello Kitty, 1974), and eventually the entire media-industrial complex oriented around the principle that every popular character could be a stuffed animal.

The cultural consequence was a shift in what stuffed animals meant. In the pre-licensing era, a bear was a blank canvas -- the child projected personality onto it. In the licensing era, the character came pre-loaded with backstory and narrative. Both models coexist, but licensing dominates commercial volume.

## 7. Post-War Transformation: Synthetics and Safety

World War II disrupted the global toy industry. German manufacturers like Steiff lost access to export markets. Raw materials were diverted to military production. The post-war period brought two transformations that permanently altered the stuffed animal industry: synthetic materials and safety regulation.

Synthetic plush fabrics developed in the 1950s and 1960s -- particularly polyester-based materials -- offered advantages over mohair: cheaper to produce, available in a wider range of colors, machine-washable, hypoallergenic, and independent of animal fiber supply chains. Polyester fiberfill replaced excelsior (wood wool) and cotton batting as the dominant stuffing material. The trade-off was character. Mohair has a depth of texture -- a nap, a sheen, a way of catching light -- that synthetic fabrics have never fully replicated. Premium manufacturers like Steiff continued to offer mohair, but the mass market moved decisively to synthetics by the 1970s.

Safety regulation arrived in earnest with the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC, established 1972) and the European EN 71 standard. The CPSIA of 2008 tightened U.S. requirements further, mandating third-party testing and certification for all children's products. These regulations reduced injuries dramatically but raised barriers for small manufacturers and independent artisans.

## 8. The Manufacturing Migration

Through the 1960s, the major manufacturing centers were Germany (Steiff, Kosen, Schuco, Hermann), the United States (Gund, Ideal, Knickerbocker), the United Kingdom (Merrythought, Chad Valley, Farnell), and Japan. Beginning in the 1970s and accelerating through the 1990s, production shifted to lower-cost countries: first South Korea and Taiwan, then China, later Vietnam, Indonesia, and the Philippines. By the early 2000s, an estimated 70-80% of the world's plush toys were made in Chinese factories, primarily in Guangdong Province.

The migration was driven by economics. Stuffed animal production is labor-intensive -- every animal requires cutting, sewing, turning, stuffing, and finishing by hand or semi-automated processes. The assembly of three-dimensional soft forms from flat fabric pieces resists full automation. A plush animal is a sewn garment in the shape of a creature. Sewing garments at low cost requires low-cost labor. The exceptions prove the rule: Steiff still manufactures in Giengen, Germany, and Merrythought in Ironbridge, England, but their retail prices reflect the labor cost differential.

## 9. Beanie Babies: Speculation, Mania, and Collapse

No account of the stuffed animal industry can omit Beanie Babies, because the phenomenon reshaped the industry's economics, consumer psychology, and relationship to collecting culture in ways that are still felt today.

Ty Warner founded Ty Inc. in 1986 in Oakbrook, Illinois. Warner had worked for Dakin, a plush manufacturer, and understood the market intimately. His initial product insight was that stuffed animals over-stuffed with polyester fiberfill felt stiff and unnatural. He under-stuffed his animals with PVC plastic pellets ("beans"), giving them a floppy, poseable quality that felt more alive in the hand. The original nine Beanie Babies launched at the 1993 Chicago toy show.

The early Beanie Babies were good products -- small, affordable ($5 retail), tactile, and charming. Each had a name, a birthday, and a short poem on the tag. They sold well. Then Ty Warner made a series of decisions that transformed a successful toy line into a speculative mania.

First, he restricted distribution. Beanie Babies were sold only through small gift shops and specialty retailers -- no Walmart, no Toys "R" Us. This created scarcity. Second, he "retired" designs regularly, removing specific animals from production. This created urgency. Third, he refused to disclose production numbers. This created uncertainty. The combination of scarcity, urgency, and uncertainty -- the same ingredients that drive speculative bubbles in any market -- produced a craze.

By 1997, Beanie Babies were generating $700 million in annual revenue. Secondary market prices for retired designs escalated exponentially. Peanut the Royal Blue Elephant, a production error that shipped in a darker blue than intended, was selling for $5,000. Adults were hoarding sealed cases, convinced they were building retirement funds. Ty's website, ty.com, became one of the most trafficked sites on the early internet.

The peak was 1998-1999. Then Ty announced that all Beanie Babies would be retired on December 31, 1999. The announcement was eventually reversed, but the spell was broken. Secondary market prices collapsed. Collectors who had invested thousands found their collections worth a fraction of the purchase price. The parallel to tulip mania, the South Sea Bubble, and every other speculative collapse is exact: the underlying product had modest intrinsic value; the speculative premium was sustained by belief in perpetual price appreciation; once belief cracked, prices reverted to fundamentals.

Ty Warner became a billionaire. The company remains private and profitable, though Warner's personal story took darker turns (a 2013 federal tax evasion conviction, $53 million penalty). The Beanie Baby legacy was double-edged: it demonstrated that the market for plush was far larger than anyone imagined, but it left a generation of consumers burned by speculation, skeptical of any claim that a stuffed animal might appreciate in value.

## 10. Build-A-Bear and the Experience Economy

Maxine Clark, a former president of Payless ShoeSource, opened the first Build-A-Bear Workshop in the Saint Louis Galleria mall in St. Louis, Missouri, in October 1997. The concept was elegantly simple: instead of buying a finished stuffed animal, customers would assemble their own. They would choose an unstuffed skin, watch it filled at a stuffing station, insert a small fabric heart (with a ritual wish), stitch it closed, name it, and create a birth certificate.

The product was secondary to the experience. Build-A-Bear was selling the process of creation -- the emotional labor of bringing a toy to life. Clark understood something about the psychology of attachment that the rest of the industry had overlooked: people value things they helped create far more than things they simply purchased. (Behavioral economists would later formalize this as the "IKEA effect.")

The model worked spectacularly. Build-A-Bear expanded to over 400 stores worldwide, peaked at over $480 million in annual revenue, and went public in 2004. Licensing partnerships with Disney, Marvel, Star Wars, and Pokemon merged the experience model with the licensing model. The customer base spanned children, teenagers, college students, and adults -- birthday parties, date nights, and therapeutic programs all found homes in Build-A-Bear stores.

## 11. Hansa Creation: The Counter-Trend

While the mainstream industry moved toward synthetics, Asian mass manufacturing, and character licensing, Hansa Creation carved out a remarkable counter-position. Founded in 1972 in the Philippines by Hans J. Axthelm, a German-born toymaker with Ideal Toys experience, Hansa pursued anatomical fidelity at every scale -- from fingertip miniatures to a life-size giraffe standing over 5 meters tall.

The catalog exceeds 7,000 distinct species. A single animal may require up to 240 individually hand-cut fabric panels, with airbrushed details, wire armatures for posability, and custom-dyed fabrics matched to species-specific coloration. Life-size elephants, polar bears, and gorillas require internal steel armatures and can weigh over 100 pounds. Their animals are stuffed with recycled PE bottles, converting post-consumer waste into product.

Hansa represents a counter-trend in nearly every dimension: handmade, Philippines-based, anatomically accurate, filling institutional niches (museums, zoos) rather than competing for mass-market shelf space. See [FFA-13] Section 1.2 for full maker profile.

## 12. JAAG Plush and the Giant Specialty Market

JAAG Plush, based in Birmingham, Michigan, occupies another specialized niche: giant and oversized stuffed animals for resorts, theme parks, carnivals, and retail spectacle. Their products range from large cuddly bears to animals standing several feet tall, designed as visual anchors for retail displays, hotel lobbies, and carnival prize games.

The giant plush market is small but persistent. It serves a specific psychological function: the oversized stuffed animal triggers an exaggerated version of the comfort response. A bear that is physically larger than the person holding it inverts the normal scale relationship between human and toy, creating an experience that is simultaneously ridiculous and deeply comforting. There is a reason that carnival midways continue to hang enormous stuffed animals as top prizes -- they are irresistible as spectacle.

JAAG and similar manufacturers (including Hansa's large-format division) demonstrate that the stuffed animal industry encompasses far more than the standard shelf-sized product. The range extends from keychain miniatures to room-scale installations.

## 13. Squishmallows and the Gen Z Revolution

In 2017, Kellytoy (a subsidiary of Jazwares, itself owned by Alleghany Capital Corporation) launched Squishmallows -- a line of round, marshmallow-soft plush figures made from super-soft spandex fabric stuffed with polyester fiberfill. The design language was deliberately simple: round bodies, minimal limbs, embroidered faces, pastel and saturated colors. No hard parts. No buttons, zippers, or plastic eyes. Pure softness, pure squish.

The line grew steadily until the COVID-19 pandemic made Squishmallows one of the defining consumer products of lockdown culture. By 2022, over 500 million had been sold worldwide. The phenomenon was driven by TikTok (#Squishmallows and #plushtok generated billions of views) and a collecting culture borrowing from sneaker culture, trading cards, and Beanie Babies -- but without the speculation. Most collectors were hunting for comfort, aesthetic pleasure, and community, not investment.

Over 2,000 distinct designs exist across multiple size categories, with retailer exclusives, seasonal releases, and regional variants creating scarcity at accessible price points ($5-$30). The community developed its own trading language -- ISO (in search of), UFT (up for trade), DISO (desperately in search of) -- and an anti-scalping ethic that distinguishes it sharply from the Beanie Baby era.

Squishmallows matter because they proved the stuffed animal market could sustain a mass phenomenon without character licensing. These are original characters -- Cam the Cat, Benny the Bigfoot -- with backstories printed on their tags. In an industry dominated by Disney and Pokemon, that is a significant counter-example.

## 14. Safety Standards and Regulation

The modern stuffed animal must comply with a dense web of safety regulations. The primary frameworks: **CPSIA** (U.S., 2008) mandates third-party testing including lead content limits (100 ppm), phthalate restrictions, flammability standards (16 CFR 1610), small parts testing (16 CFR 1501), and tracking labels. **EN 71** (EU) covers mechanical properties, flammability, and migration of hazardous elements, harmonized across member states. **ASTM F963** (U.S.) is referenced by CPSIA, effectively mandatory. Post-Brexit, the UK's **UKCA** marking creates a parallel compliance requirement for manufacturers selling in both markets.

Third-party testing for a single product SKU can cost $1,000-$5,000. For a large manufacturer, this is routine overhead. For an independent artisan selling handmade bears at a craft fair, it can be prohibitive -- shaping the industry's structure as much as any market force.

## 15. The Industry Today

The global stuffed animal market was valued at approximately $9-12 billion in 2023, depending on which market research firm's methodology you trust, with projections for continued growth driven by adult collecting, comfort culture, and the expansion of the plush concept beyond traditional animal forms.

The industry's structure is a pyramid. At the base: mass-market production in China, Vietnam, and Indonesia for major brands (Ty, Jazwares/Kellytoy, Gund, Aurora World). In the middle: specialty manufacturers like Hansa, Jellycat, and Douglas Cuddle Toys. At the apex: heritage producers like Steiff and Merrythought, still manufacturing in their home countries.

The most significant cultural shift of the past decade is the normalization of adult plush ownership. Studies consistently find that approximately 40% of adults sleep with a stuffed animal. Weighted plush -- functioning as portable weighted blankets -- have become a significant product category marketed for anxiety relief.

The stuffed animal began as a felt elephant pincushion in a Swabian seamstress's workshop. Through teddy bear mania, two world wars, synthetic materials, Asian manufacturing, speculative bubbles, experience retail, and social media virality, it became a global industry. The fundamental appeal has not changed: it is soft, warm, huggable, and available for emotional projection. It does not judge, criticize, or leave. That turns out to be an enduring value proposition.
