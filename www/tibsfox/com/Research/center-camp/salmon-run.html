The Nutrient Cycle Made Visible

Every autumn, Chinook salmon return from the Pacific Ocean to the rivers where they were born. They carry with them marine-derived nitrogen — the isotope N-15, concentrated through years of ocean feeding. This nitrogen is the ocean's gift to the forest.

Bears intercept the run, dragging carcasses up to 500 meters into the forest. Eagles scatter remains across the canopy. The salmon die on the gravel beds where they spawned, and their bodies dissolve into the stream. In every case, the nitrogen enters the soil.

Beneath the forest floor, mycorrhizal networks — the fungal web connecting root to root — distribute this marine nitrogen to trees that have never touched the river. Cedar seedlings kilometers from the stream carry ocean signatures in their rings. The forest feeds on the sea.

Rain falls. Water gathers. It flows downhill through the roots, through the gravel, back to the river. Young salmon — fry, parr, smolt — ride this water out to the Pacific. In three to five years, they will return, and the cycle completes. The water already knows the way.
