# PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — Module 2C: Eulipotyphla (Shrews & Moles) and Chiroptera (Bats)

> **"Fur, Fin & Fang" — Pacific Northwest Mammalian Biodiversity**
>
> This module profiles approximately 30 species of shrews, moles, and bats documented in the Pacific Northwest bioregion (Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia). These are the region's most underappreciated mammals — creatures of twilight, tunnel, and torrent, whose ecological contributions far exceed their visibility. Shrews are the smallest mammals in the PNW, operating at metabolic rates so extreme that starvation is never more than hours away. Moles are subterranean engineers whose tunneling aerates soils, redistributes nutrients, and creates microhabitats exploited by dozens of other species. Bats are the only true flying mammals, and in the PNW they consume billions of insects annually, providing pest suppression services valued at billions of dollars across North American agriculture.
>
> Six species receive extended treatment: the Pacific water shrew (*Sorex bendirii*), a semi-aquatic insectivore that hunts underwater using trapped air bubbles and venomous saliva; the American shrew-mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*), the smallest North American mole and a PNW endemic that represents the sole living member of its genus; Townsend's mole (*Scapanus townsendii*), the largest mole on the continent and the architect of the iconic molehills of western Oregon and Washington valley floors; the little brown bat (*Myotis lucifugus*), once the most abundant bat in North America, now facing population collapse from white-nose syndrome; the hoary bat (*Lasiurus cinereus*), the PNW's largest bat and the species most devastated by wind energy development; and Townsend's big-eared bat (*Corynorhinus townsendii*), a cave-obligate specialist whose extreme sensitivity to human disturbance makes it a bellwether for subterranean habitat conservation.
>
> **Design principle:** Each species profile follows the standardized template from `00-taxonomy-schema.md`. Rich prose narrative integrates morphometrics, habitat, diet, reproduction, social structure, population, ecological role, ecosystem engineering, evolutionary notes, and sources. Extended profiles explore the deeper ecological and cultural dimensions of keystone and indicator species.
>

## 1. Order Eulipotyphla — Shrews and Moles

The Eulipotyphla — a name meaning "truly fat and blind," coined with taxonomic irony given that these animals are among the leanest and most sensorially acute mammals alive — encompasses the shrews, moles, and hedgehogs. No hedgehogs are native to the Americas, but the PNW hosts a remarkable radiation of both shrews and moles, totaling approximately 15 species. These are ancient mammalian lineages. Shrews diverged from other insectivores in the Eocene, roughly 45 million years ago, and the basic soricid body plan — elongated snout, tiny eyes, rapid metabolism, venomous saliva — has proven so successful that it has persisted essentially unchanged while entire orders of mammals have risen and gone extinct around it.

What unites the Eulipotyphla ecologically is their position at the base of the mammalian size spectrum and the extreme end of mammalian metabolic intensity. A shrew's heart beats 800-1,200 times per minute. Its metabolic rate per gram of body tissue exceeds that of any other mammal, and it must consume 80-120% of its body weight in food every day or die within hours. This metabolic furnace drives an ecological role far out of proportion to body size: PNW shrews collectively consume millions of tons of invertebrates annually, regulating populations of soil-dwelling insects, spiders, centipedes, snails, and earthworms across every terrestrial habitat in the region. They are, in turn, consumed by owls, hawks, weasels, snakes, and even large spiders — though many predators find shrew flesh distasteful due to lateral gland secretions that produce a musky, acrid scent.

Moles have taken the eulipotyphlan body plan underground, evolving powerful forelimbs rotated outward for digging, vestigial eyes, and dense, velvety fur that can lie in any direction — allowing the animal to move forward or backward through tunnels without resistance. Their tunneling activity makes them significant soil engineers, turning over thousands of cubic centimeters of earth per day and creating habitat structure exploited by amphibians, invertebrates, and other small mammals.

### Vagrant Shrew (*Sorex vagrans*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — most widespread shrew in the PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- ID: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 5.0-6.5 cm (head-body); tail 3.5-4.5 cm
- Mass: 4-9 g, averaging 5-7 g
- Sexual dimorphism: Negligible
- Distinguishing features: Tricolored pelage — dark brown to reddish-brown dorsally, paler flanks, grayish-white venter. Medium-sized for a *Sorex*, with relatively short tail (60-75% of head-body length). Rostrum moderately elongated, ears barely visible above fur. Dental formula 3/1, 1/1, 3/1, 3/3 = 32 teeth; upper incisors strongly pigmented dark reddish-brown at tips, a characteristic of all soricine shrews reflecting iron deposition that hardens the enamel for processing chitinous prey.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Generalist — moist meadows, marshes, riparian corridors, forest edges, clearcuts with ground cover, alpine meadows, suburban gardens
- Elevation range: Sea level to 9,000+ ft; found at every elevation where sufficient ground moisture exists
- Ecoregion: All 8 zones — the vagrant shrew is the ecological equivalent of the deer mouse among insectivores, occupying virtually every habitat in the PNW
- Home range: 0.02-0.1 ha; one of the smallest home ranges of any mammal
- Cover: Requires dense ground cover — leaf litter, grass thatch, moss, or logs. Cannot survive in bare or heavily grazed habitats

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Invertebores — insects (beetles, caterpillars, flies, ants), earthworms, spiders, centipedes, snails, slugs; occasionally small salamanders, seeds, and fungal fruiting bodies
- Foraging strategy: Cathemeral — active in short bouts (15-30 minutes) throughout 24-hour cycle, separated by rest periods of similar duration; must eat every 2-3 hours to avoid starvation
- Seasonal variation: Insects dominate summer diet; earthworms and other soil invertebrates become critical in winter when surface arthropods decline
- Daily intake: Consumes 80-100% of body weight daily; metabolic rate approximately 12x that of a mouse per gram of tissue

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; males range widely during breeding season seeking receptive females
- Breeding season: March-September; multiple litters possible
- Gestation: 20 days
- Litter size: 2-9 (typically 5-7), born altricial — naked, blind, weighing <0.5 g
- Weaning: 20-22 days; juveniles disperse immediately and are independent within days
- Sexual maturity: Some females breed in their birth year; most breed the following spring
- Lifespan: 12-18 months maximum; few survive to breed in a second year. The shrew's metabolic intensity exacts a biological cost — teeth wear down, organs accumulate damage, and senescence is rapid. A shrew entering its second winter is physiologically ancient.

**Social Structure:** Aggressively territorial. Vagrant shrews defend core territories with vocalizations (high-pitched squeaks and chatters audible to human ears) and physical confrontation. Encounters between adults frequently involve combat, and bite wounds are common. Shrews produce flank gland secretions that mark territories and may function in individual recognition.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: Not estimated at regional scale; density varies from 5-50 per hectare depending on habitat moisture and invertebrate availability
- Threats: Pesticide bioaccumulation (shrews consume large quantities of treated invertebrates); habitat conversion that eliminates ground cover; domestic and feral cats are significant predators in suburban settings
- Source: [P1], [P2], [P4], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The vagrant shrew is the most abundant insectivore in the PNW and a critical regulator of soil invertebrate communities. By consuming enormous quantities of beetle larvae, caterpillars, and other phytophagous insects, shrews provide pest suppression that benefits forest regeneration and agricultural production. They are also prey for a wide range of predators — barn owls take vagrant shrews frequently, though the musky gland secretions make shrews a secondary preference to voles and mice. In the food web, the vagrant shrew occupies the niche between invertebrate consumers and vertebrate predators, converting arthropod biomass into mammalian tissue available to raptors and mustelids.

**Evolutionary Note:** The genus *Sorex* is Holarctic in distribution, with approximately 80 species worldwide. The PNW sits at a center of soricid diversity in North America, likely reflecting the region's topographic complexity and moisture gradients, which create the mosaic of microhabitats that shrews exploit through ecological partitioning. The vagrant shrew's specific epithet *vagrans* ("wandering") reflects its broad habitat tolerance, though the name somewhat mischaracterizes a species that is, in fact, strongly site-faithful within its tiny home range.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Barn owl, short-eared owl, and Northern saw-whet owl all consume vagrant shrews
- Predator-prey: Weasels (long-tailed, short-tailed) are significant shrew predators
- Soil ecosystem: Shrew predation on invertebrates interacts with mole tunneling and pocket gopher soil engineering (Zone 8)

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; Churchfield 1990; [O4] Burke Museum

### Pacific Water Shrew (*Sorex bendirii*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — semi-aquatic, Pacific coastal streams
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed, but considered Species of Concern
- IUCN: Least Concern (range-wide), though populations are patchy and declining in urbanized areas
- WA: State Candidate species; SGCN
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species
- ID: Not present (range does not extend to Idaho)
- BC: Red-listed (at risk); COSEWIC Endangered

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 7.5-9.0 cm (head-body); tail 6.0-8.0 cm, conspicuously long and stiff
- Mass: 10-21 g, averaging 14-18 g; the largest *Sorex* in the PNW and among the largest shrews in North America
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; females may average slightly smaller
- Distinguishing features: Large body size immediately distinguishes this species from all other PNW shrews. Pelage uniformly dark — nearly black dorsally, dark brown to blackish ventrally, lacking the bicolored pattern of most *Sorex*. The tail is notably long, thick at the base, and fringed with stiff hairs on the ventral surface that function as a keel during swimming. Hind feet are large (18-21 mm) and bordered with stiff bristle-like hairs that increase surface area during aquatic locomotion, creating a partial webbing effect. Eyes small, even for a shrew. The overall impression is of a miniature, dark, streamlined mammal adapted for life in and around water.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Riparian corridors of perennial streams, particularly small, cold, clear, forested headwater streams with dense bankside vegetation and coarse woody debris; also marshes, seeps, and wet meadows adjacent to flowing water
- Elevation range: Sea level to approximately 3,000 ft; concentrated in coastal lowlands and foothill stream valleys
- Ecoregion: Riparian/Wetland (Zone 5, primary), Lowland Forest (Zone 3), marginally Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2)
- Home range: Linear along stream corridors, typically 50-200 m of stream bank; rarely found more than 5-10 m from water
- Cover: Absolutely dependent on dense riparian vegetation — sword fern, salal, devil's club, red alder understory — and accumulations of coarse woody debris along stream banks. Logs that span or overhang streams are especially important as foraging platforms and cover

**Aquatic Adaptations:**
The Pacific water shrew is the most aquatic member of the genus *Sorex* in North America and ranks among the most aquatic shrews globally, rivaled only by the Eurasian water shrew (*Neomys fodiens*) and the elegant water shrew (*Nectogale elegans*) of the Himalayas. Its suite of aquatic adaptations represents convergent evolution with these Old World lineages:

The hind feet bear stiff, silvery hairs along their margins that splay outward during the power stroke of swimming, increasing foot surface area by approximately 30% and functioning as biological swim fins. When the foot is drawn forward for recovery, the hairs collapse against the toes, reducing drag. This system is mechanically analogous to feathering an oar.

The tail is laterally compressed and fringed with stiff hairs along its ventral surface, creating a rudder-keel structure that provides directional stability during underwater pursuit of prey. The shrew swims with rapid alternating strokes of the hind feet, using the tail for steering — a locomotor pattern more reminiscent of a diving bird than a typical mammal.

When the water shrew enters water, air becomes trapped in its dense, fine underfur, creating a silvery sheath around the body that serves multiple functions: thermal insulation in cold water (PNW headwater streams run 4-12 degrees Celsius year-round), buoyancy (the air layer keeps the shrew positively buoyant, requiring active swimming effort to submerge), and possibly enhanced tactile sensitivity, as the air-water interface along the fur may amplify hydromechanical disturbances created by prey movement. The air layer limits dive duration to approximately 10-20 seconds, after which the shrew must surface to replenish the trapped air.

**Venomous Saliva:**
Like all soricine shrews, *Sorex bendirii* produces toxic saliva containing blarina toxin (named for the short-tailed shrew *Blarina brevicauda*, in which it was first characterized). This kallikrein-like protease causes vasodilation, hypotension, and paralysis in invertebrate and small vertebrate prey. In the Pacific water shrew, venomous saliva is delivered through grooved lower incisors that channel saliva into bite wounds. The toxin is sufficient to immobilize aquatic insect larvae, small fish, and tadpoles, allowing the shrew to overcome prey that would otherwise be too large or too agile to subdue. This is not a venom delivery system comparable to a snake's fangs — there are no hollow teeth or pressurized glands — but the functional outcome is the same: a chemical advantage in predation.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Aquatic and riparian invertebrates — caddisfly larvae (Trichoptera), stonefly nymphs (Plecoptera), mayfly nymphs (Ephemeroptera), crane fly larvae (Tipulidae), aquatic beetles, aquatic earthworms, small snails; also terrestrial invertebrates (spiders, centipedes, beetles) along stream banks
- Foraging strategy: Hunts both underwater and along stream banks. Enters water headfirst from logs or overhanging banks, dives to the substrate, and uses vibrissae (whiskers) and possibly echolocation-like clicks to locate prey in turbid or dark conditions. Captures prey in the mouth, surfaces, and carries items to a feeding platform — typically a log or rock — to consume. Also forages actively through leaf litter and under coarse woody debris along stream margins.
- Aquatic prey capture: Documented taking small sculpin (*Cottus* spp.) fry, Pacific giant salamander larvae (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*), and tadpoles — prey items that outweigh the shrew by a substantial margin. The venomous saliva is likely critical for subduing these larger prey.
- Daily intake: Must consume approximately 100-120% of body weight daily, requiring nearly continuous foraging activity

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous
- Breeding season: February-June; 2-3 litters possible per season
- Gestation: Approximately 20 days (estimated; reproductive biology poorly studied in this species)
- Litter size: 3-7 (typically 4-5)
- Weaning: Approximately 20-25 days
- Sexual maturity: Some females may breed in their birth year under favorable conditions
- Lifespan: 14-18 months maximum; as with all shrews, second-year survival is rare

**Social Structure:** Solitary and territorial. Individuals defend linear stream territories, with males occupying longer stream reaches during the breeding season. Aggressive encounters along territory boundaries are common, and bite wounds are frequently observed on captured individuals.

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining in urbanized areas; stable in intact forested stream corridors
- Estimate: No regional population estimate exists. Density is low even in optimal habitat — typically 1-5 individuals per 100 m of stream corridor
- Threats: Riparian buffer removal (logging, agriculture, development), stream channelization, culverts that fragment linear habitat, water pollution (sedimentation reduces aquatic invertebrate prey), urbanization of lowland stream corridors, climate change (reduced summer stream flows in rain-dependent watersheds)
- Source: [P1], [P2], [G9], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The Pacific water shrew is an indicator species for riparian health. Its presence in a stream corridor signals intact riparian vegetation, good water quality, and abundant aquatic invertebrate communities — the same conditions required by salmon, amphibians, and a host of other sensitive species. In this sense, the water shrew functions as a biological integrator of stream ecosystem condition. Its predation on aquatic insect larvae may influence benthic invertebrate community composition in small streams, though this effect has not been quantified in the PNW.

The species also serves as a model system for studying aquatic adaptations in mammals. The convergent evolution of swimming adaptations in *S. bendirii*, the Eurasian water shrew, and the Himalayan water shrew demonstrates that the soricid body plan is remarkably plastic, capable of evolving semi-aquatic specializations in response to ecological opportunity despite the severe constraints imposed by small body size and extreme metabolic demand.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Sorex bendirii* is a member of the *S. bendirii* species group, which includes only *S. bendirii* and *S. palustris* (the American water shrew, not confirmed in the PNW lowlands). The two species may have diverged from a common semi-aquatic ancestor during Pleistocene glacial cycles, with *S. bendirii* becoming isolated in Pacific coastal refugia. The species' restricted range — from southern British Columbia to northern California, west of the Cascade crest — reflects its dependence on the mild, wet climate of the Pacific slope.

**Cultural Note:** Pacific water shrews are rarely observed by humans due to their small size, dark coloration, and riparian habitat. They do not figure prominently in the ethnographic literature of PNW nations, though Suttles (1987) notes that Coast Salish peoples recognized distinct categories of small stream-dwelling animals, and shrew-like creatures appear in Nlaka'pamux (Thompson) oral traditions as creatures of liminality — beings that move between the worlds of water and land [O6-C6].

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: American dipper — shares headwater stream habitat, forages on the same aquatic invertebrate prey assemblage
- Salmon linkage: Water shrew presence indicates stream conditions favorable for salmon spawning and rearing
- Amphibian interaction: Predation on Pacific giant salamander larvae creates trophic connection to amphibian module
- Zone 5 indicator species alongside beaver and muskrat, but occupying the smallest-bodied niche of the riparian mammal community

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [G9] WDFW State Wildlife Action Plan; Beneski & Stinson (1987) — *Sorex palustris* and allies; Churchfield 1990; Conaway (1952) — Life history of the water shrew; [O6-C6] Suttles 1987

### Trowbridge's Shrew (*Sorex trowbridgii*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — coniferous forests west of the Cascades
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); at northern range limit in southwestern BC

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 5.5-7.0 cm (head-body); tail 4.5-6.0 cm, distinctly bicolored (dark above, pale below)
- Mass: 4-7 g, averaging 5-6 g
- Distinguishing features: Bicolored tail is the key field character — sharply dark above and whitish below, unlike the uniformly dark tails of most PNW *Sorex*. Pelage uniform dark brown to charcoal-gray, darker than the vagrant shrew and lacking any reddish tones. Slightly larger than the vagrant shrew, with longer tail relative to body length. Feet notably pale.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Closed-canopy coniferous forest — Douglas-fir, western hemlock, western redcedar — with deep leaf litter and rotting logs. Strongly associated with mature and old-growth forest structure.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft; concentrated below 3,000 ft on the west side of the Cascades
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest (Zone 3, primary), Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Riparian (Zone 5 — adjacent to forested streams)
- Home range: 0.02-0.08 ha
- Cover: Deep conifer litter (5-10 cm depth), rotting logs in advanced decay, moss mats on the forest floor

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (beetles, caterpillars, ants), centipedes, spiders, earthworms, and notably — Douglas-fir seeds and fungal spores, making this species more omnivorous than most shrews
- Foraging strategy: Cathemeral; forages extensively within and beneath rotting logs, where invertebrate prey concentrations are highest
- Seasonal variation: Conifer seed consumption increases in autumn and winter when invertebrate availability declines

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous
- Breeding season: March-July
- Gestation: ~20 days
- Litter size: 3-6 (typically 4)
- Lifespan: 12-16 months

**Social Structure:** Territorial; less aggressive than the vagrant shrew in overlapping habitat, with evidence of vertical habitat partitioning — Trowbridge's shrew forages more within rotting log interiors while vagrant shrews forage in the surface litter layer.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in intact forests; declines following clearcutting due to loss of coarse woody debris
- Estimate: Locally abundant — can exceed 20 per hectare in old-growth Douglas-fir stands
- Threats: Old-growth logging removes coarse woody debris and deep litter; intensive forest management that removes fallen logs reduces habitat quality; may take 80-100 years for managed forests to develop sufficient coarse woody debris for Trowbridge's shrew recolonization
- Source: [P1], [P2], [G12]

**Ecological Role:** Trowbridge's shrew is an old-growth indicator among shrews. Its dependence on deep litter and coarse woody debris makes it sensitive to forest management practices, and its abundance correlates with stand age and structural complexity. By consuming Douglas-fir seeds, it may influence tree recruitment patterns — a rare herbivory role for a shrew. Its consumption of fungal spores and hypogeous fungi parallels the mycorrhizal dispersal role documented for deer mice and flying squirrels, though the shrew's contribution is less well quantified.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [G12] USFS PNW Research Station; Terry 1981

### Montane Shrew (*Sorex monticolus*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — montane forests and meadows
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common in mountains; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- ID: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 5.5-7.0 cm (head-body); tail 4.5-5.5 cm
- Mass: 5-9 g, averaging 6-7 g
- Distinguishing features: Medium-brown dorsally with distinctly paler flanks and belly, creating clear tricolored pattern. Similar to the vagrant shrew and frequently confused with it in the field; distinguished by slightly larger size, longer tail relative to body, and subtle cranial differences (wider interorbital region, slightly different tooth morphology) that require specimen examination. Previously classified as a subspecies of *S. vagrans*; elevated to full species status based on genetic and morphometric analysis.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Montane and subalpine meadows, streamside habitats in mountain forests, avalanche chutes with dense herbaceous growth, subalpine parkland edges
- Elevation range: 2,000-9,000 ft; the high-elevation complement to the vagrant shrew
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2, primary), Alpine/Subalpine (Zone 1), Riparian (Zone 5 — mountain streams)
- Home range: 0.03-0.1 ha

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, centipedes, earthworms; diet composition broadly similar to vagrant shrew but shifted toward high-elevation invertebrate assemblages
- Foraging strategy: Cathemeral; forages in dense meadow vegetation and under logs at montane elevations
- Daily intake: ~90% of body weight

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: May-August (compressed by shorter mountain growing season)
- Gestation: ~20 days
- Litter size: 4-8 (typically 5-6)
- Lifespan: 14-16 months

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; potentially vulnerable to climate-driven upslope habitat shifts
- Threats: Climate change — montane meadows may contract as treeline advances upward; ski area development and trail construction fragment mountain meadow habitat
- Source: [P1], [P2], [P4]

**Ecological Role:** Fills the same ecological niche as the vagrant shrew but at montane and subalpine elevations. The elevational partitioning between *S. vagrans* (lowlands) and *S. monticolus* (mountains) illustrates classical parapatric speciation along an elevation gradient, with a narrow contact zone where both species occur.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Demboski & Cook (2001) — Phylogeography of *Sorex monticolus* complex

### Masked Shrew (*Sorex cinereus*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — widespread in eastern PNW and northern BC
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common east of Cascades; uncommon west-side
- OR: Uncommon; restricted to northeastern Oregon mountains
- ID: Common; widespread
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); widespread

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 4.5-6.0 cm (head-body); tail 3.0-4.5 cm
- Mass: 3-6 g, averaging 3.5-5 g; one of the smallest PNW mammals
- Distinguishing features: Small size, long pointed snout, grayish-brown dorsal pelage with paler flanks and silvery-gray venter. Tail bicolored but less sharply than Trowbridge's shrew. Ears small, partially concealed by fur. This is the most widely distributed shrew in North America, ranging from Alaska to the Appalachians, and in the PNW it reaches its southwestern range limit.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Moist habitats — marshes, wet meadows, stream margins, moist forest floors; more moisture-dependent than the vagrant shrew in the PNW
- Elevation range: 2,000-8,000 ft in the PNW; occurs at lower elevations farther north in BC
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Riparian (Zone 5), Alpine (Zone 1 — northern populations)
- Home range: 0.01-0.05 ha

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, earthworms, snails; diet essentially identical to vagrant shrew
- Daily intake: ~100% of body weight — at 3-5 g, the masked shrew operates at the extreme lower limit of mammalian body size, where surface-area-to-volume ratios make thermoregulation extraordinarily expensive

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: April-September
- Gestation: ~18-20 days
- Litter size: 4-10 (typically 6-7); among the largest litters of any PNW shrew
- Lifespan: 12-14 months

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Threats: No significant threats in the PNW; abundant across its range
- Source: [P1], [P7]

**Ecological Role:** In the eastern PNW, the masked shrew replaces or co-occurs with the vagrant shrew, filling the same generalist insectivore niche. Where both species occur, there is evidence of microhabitat partitioning, with the masked shrew preferring slightly moister microsites.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P7] Dalquest 1948; Whitaker (2004) — *Sorex cinereus*, Mammalian Species

### Pygmy Shrew (*Sorex hoyi*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — rare, northeastern PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (range-wide); rare and localized in the PNW
- WA: State Monitor species; very few records
- OR: Not documented
- ID: Uncommon; scattered records
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); widespread in northern BC but uncommon in southern BC

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 4.0-5.0 cm (head-body); tail 2.5-3.5 cm
- Mass: 2.0-4.5 g, averaging 2.5-3.5 g; the smallest mammal in the PNW and one of the smallest in the world, rivaled only by the Etruscan shrew (*Suncus etruscus*) of the Old World. A pygmy shrew weighs less than a U.S. penny.
- Distinguishing features: Tiny size is diagnostic — smaller than any other PNW mammal. Pelage reddish-brown dorsally, grayish venter. Proportionally shorter tail than other *Sorex*. Third upper unicuspid tooth smaller than fourth (reversed from most *Sorex* — a key diagnostic character requiring skull examination).

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Boreal and montane forests, sphagnum bogs, moist meadows; in the PNW, restricted to the northeastern corner of the region
- Elevation range: 2,500-7,000 ft in the PNW
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Riparian (Zone 5)

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small insects, insect larvae, spiders; prey size constrained by the shrew's diminutive gape
- Daily intake: Must consume 125%+ of body weight daily — the highest mass-specific metabolic rate of any PNW mammal. At 3 g, a pygmy shrew that goes 3-4 hours without food may die of hypoglycemia.

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: May-August
- Litter size: 3-8 (typically 5-6)
- Lifespan: 12-14 months

**Population:**
- Trend: Unknown in the PNW; too few records for trend analysis
- Threats: Poorly understood; presumably vulnerable to habitat loss in boreal/montane wetlands
- Source: [P1], [P7]

**Ecological Role:** The pygmy shrew occupies the absolute lower limit of mammalian body size. At 2.5-3.5 g, it demonstrates the thermodynamic constraints on miniaturization in endotherms — the surface-area-to-volume ratio is so unfavorable that the animal must maintain a heart rate exceeding 1,000 beats per minute and consume food almost continuously during active periods. Its ecological role in the PNW is poorly quantified due to rarity, but it presumably consumes micro-invertebrates not efficiently exploited by larger shrews.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P7] Dalquest 1948; Long (1974) — *Sorex hoyi* in the PNW

### Merriam's Shrew (*Sorex merriami*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Soricidae > Soricinae
**Status:** Resident — arid shrub-steppe, east-side
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: State Candidate species; SGCN
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species in some regions
- ID: Uncommon; scattered records

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 5.0-6.5 cm (head-body); tail 3.0-4.0 cm
- Mass: 4-7 g
- Distinguishing features: Notably pale pelage — grayish-brown dorsally with distinctly whitish venter and flanks, paler than any other PNW *Sorex*. This pallor reflects adaptation to the xeric habitats it occupies, in marked contrast to the dark-pelaged forest shrews. Tail short and bicolored.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Sagebrush-steppe, bunchgrass prairie, dry open woodlands — the driest habitats occupied by any PNW shrew
- Elevation range: 500-5,000 ft
- Ecoregion: East-Side Steppe/Shrubland (Zone 7, primary); Foothill/Oak Woodland (Zone 4)
- Cover: Shelters under sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*), bunch grass clumps, and rock piles; does not require the moist conditions essential for most shrews

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (especially beetles and caterpillars), spiders, and other arthropods of arid habitats
- Daily intake: ~80-100% of body weight; may obtain water from prey metabolism rather than free water

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: April-August
- Litter size: 5-7
- Lifespan: 12-14 months

**Population:**
- Trend: Poorly known; presumed stable but potentially declining with sagebrush loss
- Threats: Conversion of sagebrush-steppe to agriculture (especially in the Columbia Basin); wildfire in sagebrush (which recovers slowly, leaving shrews without cover); cheatgrass invasion altering fire regimes
- Source: [P1], [P2]

**Ecological Role:** Merriam's shrew is the east-side equivalent of the vagrant shrew — a generalist insectivore adapted to the PNW's driest habitats. Its ability to survive in arid sagebrush-steppe without free water sources is unusual for a shrew and represents a physiological adaptation to xeric conditions rare in the family Soricidae. Its presence in sagebrush habitats links it to the broader sagebrush ecosystem conservation concern shared with sage-grouse, pygmy rabbit, and sagebrush-dependent reptiles.

### 1.2 Family Talpidae — Moles

The moles of the PNW belong to the family Talpidae, a Holarctic family of approximately 50 species that have independently evolved fossorial specializations multiple times. Three genera of true moles occur in the PNW — *Scapanus* (endemic to western North America), *Neurotrichus* (the shrew-mole, endemic to the Pacific coastal forests and the sole member of its genus worldwide), and occasionally *Condylura* (the star-nosed mole, reaching the region marginally in northeastern BC). The *Scapanus* moles are the dominant fossorial mammals west of the Cascades, and their tunneling creates a subterranean infrastructure that influences soil structure, water infiltration, invertebrate distribution, and plant community composition across hundreds of thousands of hectares of lowland and foothill habitat.

### Townsend's Mole (*Scapanus townsendii*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Talpidae > Scalopinae
**Status:** Resident — Zone 8 indicator species
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common west of Cascades; no special status (but a significant agricultural pest in certain contexts)
- OR: Common in western valleys; no special status
- BC: Red-listed (at risk) — restricted to extreme southwestern BC (Lower Fraser Valley)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 18-24 cm (head-body); tail 3-5 cm, thick and nearly hairless
- Mass: 100-175 g, with exceptional individuals exceeding 180 g; males substantially larger than females
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced; males 20-40% heavier than females, with larger forefeet
- Distinguishing features: The largest mole in North America and one of the largest in the world, rivaled only by the Russian desman (*Desmana moschata*). Body massive, cylindrical, and densely furred with velvety pelage that is uniformly dark purplish-black to dark brown — the hair shafts lack directional orientation, allowing the fur to lie flat in any direction as the animal moves forward or backward through its tunnels. Eyes extremely reduced — vestigial, covered by skin, detecting only light/dark. No external ear pinnae. The forefeet are extraordinary: broad, flat, and rotated permanently outward (laterally), with massive claws — each forefoot is wider than it is long, creating a shovel-shaped digging tool powered by hugely developed pectoral and brachial musculature. The sternum bears a pronounced keel (manubrial keel) for muscle attachment, analogous to the keel on a bird's sternum, reflecting the extreme mechanical demands of burrowing. The snout is elongated, flexible, and densely covered with Eimer's organs — specialized touch receptors that make the mole's nose one of the most sensitive tactile structures in the mammalian world.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Deep, moist, loamy to clay soils in lowland valleys, floodplains, meadows, pastures, suburban lawns, and gardens; requires soils moist enough to maintain tunnel integrity but not permanently waterlogged
- Elevation range: Sea level to 2,500 ft; concentrated in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, lower Columbia River corridor, and Chehalis Valley
- Ecoregion: Fossorial/Subterranean (Zone 8, primary), Lowland Forest (Zone 3), Riparian (Zone 5), Foothill/Oak Woodland (Zone 4)
- Home range: 0.1-0.3 ha; highly three-dimensional — tunnel systems extend to depths of 30-60 cm for permanent galleries and 5-15 cm for surface feeding tunnels
- Tunnel architecture: Townsend's mole constructs two distinct tunnel types. **Deep tunnels** (30-60 cm below surface) are permanent galleries used year-round for travel, nesting, and food storage; they are maintained for years and may be inherited by successive occupants. **Shallow tunnels** (5-15 cm) are temporary feeding runs constructed daily, radiating outward from deep tunnel junctions; these are the tunnels that produce the characteristic surface ridges visible in lawns and pastures. The nest chamber is a spherical cavity 15-20 cm in diameter, lined with dry grass and located in the deep tunnel system, often under a prominent surface mound.

**Fossorial Engineering:**
Townsend's mole is the preeminent subterranean engineer of the PNW lowlands. A single mole moves an estimated 200-500 kg of soil per year to the surface in the form of molehills — the conical mounds of excavated earth that are the mole's most visible sign. These molehills, averaging 15-30 cm in height and 20-40 cm in diameter, have measurable effects on soil ecology:

Soil aeration: Mole tunneling creates macropores that increase water infiltration and gas exchange in compacted soils, counteracting the effects of soil compaction from livestock and machinery. In pastures, mole tunnel networks can increase water infiltration rates by 10-30%.

Nutrient redistribution: The earth brought to the surface from 30-60 cm depth differs in chemistry from surface soils — deeper soils contain different mineral concentrations, pH values, and microbial communities. Mole-mound soil has been shown to have higher concentrations of certain minerals and lower organic matter content than surrounding surface soil, creating nutrient heterogeneity that influences plant community composition. Pioneer plants colonizing molehill surfaces often differ from the surrounding sward.

Invertebrate habitat: Abandoned mole tunnels provide below-ground corridors used by voles, shrews, salamanders, and invertebrates. The tunnel network constitutes a parallel below-ground transportation and habitat system that connects otherwise isolated microhabitats.

Seed burial: Molehills bury vegetation and seeds, creating small-scale disturbances that maintain plant diversity in meadows by preventing any single species from achieving competitive dominance — functionally analogous to the role of pocket gopher mounds in grassland ecology.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Earthworms (*Lumbricus terrestris* and native earthworm species) comprise 60-80% of the diet by volume; supplemented by insect larvae (especially scarab beetle grubs), centipedes, and occasionally plant material (roots, tubers, bulbs)
- Foraging strategy: Patrols shallow feeding tunnels, detecting earthworms and larvae through vibration and Eimer's organ tactile sensitivity. Earthworms that penetrate tunnel walls are seized and consumed; the mole may paralyze large worms with a bite to the anterior segments and store them alive in subterranean food caches, sometimes accumulating dozens of immobilized earthworms in a single cache. This caching behavior, documented in both European and American moles, extends the mole's food supply through periods of reduced prey availability.
- Seasonal variation: Earthworm consumption peaks in winter and spring when soil moisture drives worms into the mole's tunnel depth; in summer, dry surface soils push prey deeper and reduce shallow-tunnel foraging efficiency

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Solitary except during breeding; males expand their tunnel networks in late winter to locate females
- Breeding season: January-March; single litter per year (unusual among small mammals — most shrews and rodents produce multiple litters)
- Gestation: Approximately 30-35 days
- Litter size: 2-4 (typically 3), born in the deep nest chamber, altricial — hairless, pink, with visible forefeet already disproportionately large
- Weaning: 30-36 days; young moles disperse above ground, traveling overland — this is the only period in a Townsend's mole's life when it is regularly above the surface, and it is the period of highest mortality from predation
- Sexual maturity: 10-12 months
- Lifespan: 3-5 years; substantially longer-lived than shrews, reflecting the safety of subterranean existence

**Social Structure:** Aggressively solitary. Adult Townsend's moles are intolerant of conspecifics except during the brief breeding period. Males that encounter each other in connecting tunnels fight violently, and territorial disputes can result in fatal injuries. Each individual maintains an exclusive tunnel system, though boundaries between adjacent territories may shift seasonally. Population density is self-regulating: when a mole dies, its tunnel system is rapidly colonized by a neighboring individual or a dispersing juvenile.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; abundant in western valleys
- Estimate: Density varies from 5-25 per hectare in optimal pasture habitat; lower (2-8 per hectare) in forests
- Threats: Pest control activities (trapping, poisoning) in agricultural and suburban settings; some populations reduced by urban development paving over soils. In BC, the species is at risk due to habitat loss in the Lower Fraser Valley to urbanization and agriculture.
- Source: [P1], [P2], [P3], [O4]

**Human Conflict:** Townsend's mole is the most conspicuous "pest" mammal in western Oregon and Washington lowlands. Its molehills damage lawns, golf courses, pastures, and crop fields. Root and bulb consumption causes losses in vegetable gardens and bulb nurseries — the Willamette Valley flower bulb industry has historically suffered economic damage from mole feeding. Control methods include trapping (scissor-jaw traps, harpoon traps), toxic baits (zinc phosphide), and exclusion barriers. No bounty or damage control program has achieved lasting population reduction, reflecting the species' high reproductive potential and rapid recolonization of vacant territories. The conflict is fundamentally irreconcilable: the deep, moist valley-bottom soils that humans prize for agriculture are exactly the soils that Townsend's mole requires.

**Evolutionary Note:** The genus *Scapanus* is endemic to western North America, with three species in the PNW (*S. townsendii*, *S. orarius*, *S. latimanus*). The genus represents a radiation from ancestors that colonized North America via Beringia, subsequently evolving fossorial specializations independently from European moles (*Talpa*). The remarkable convergence in body form between *Scapanus* and *Talpa* — velvety fur, rotated forefeet, vestigial eyes, elongated snout — is a textbook example of convergent evolution driven by identical selective pressures in the subterranean environment.

**Cultural Note:** Moles feature in the oral traditions of several PNW nations. In some Coast Salish traditions, Mole is associated with the underground world and with earth-working — a thematic connection to the animal's observable behavior that appears across cultures worldwide. The Nlaka'pamux (Thompson) include Mole in stories about the transformation of the world in mythic time [O6-C6].

**Cross-Links:**
- Zone 8 indicator species: Townsend's mole and pocket gophers (Module 2B) are the co-dominant fossorial engineers of PNW lowland soils
- Predator-prey: Coyotes, great horned owls, barn owls, red-tailed hawks, and long-tailed weasels are significant mole predators, primarily capturing dispersing juveniles on the surface
- Soil engineering: Mole tunneling interacts with earthworm ecology, mycorrhizal networks, and plant community composition
- AVI companion: Owls and hawks prey on surface-dispersing juveniles

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [P7] Dalquest 1948; Hartman & Yates (2003) — *Scapanus townsendii*, Mammalian Species; [O4] Burke Museum; [O6-C6] Suttles 1987

### Coast Mole (*Scapanus orarius*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Talpidae > Scalopinae
**Status:** Resident — western lowlands and foothills
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); more widespread than Townsend's mole in BC

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 12-16 cm (head-body); tail 3-4 cm
- Mass: 50-80 g; substantially smaller than Townsend's mole
- Sexual dimorphism: Moderate; males larger
- Distinguishing features: Smaller, darker, and more slender than Townsend's mole, with proportionally smaller forefeet. Pelage dark gray to blackish, very fine and velvety. The two species are readily distinguished by size when side by side, but separating their surface sign (molehills) in the field is difficult — coast mole molehills are smaller, but overlap in size range with those of small Townsend's moles.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Drier, lighter soils than Townsend's mole — well-drained forest soils, meadow margins, gardens, sandy to loamy soils in foothills; tolerates less moisture and lighter soil texture than Townsend's
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 ft; extends to higher elevations and drier sites than Townsend's mole
- Ecoregion: Fossorial (Zone 8), Lowland Forest (Zone 3), Foothill/Oak Woodland (Zone 4)
- Habitat partitioning: Where coast mole and Townsend's mole co-occur (common in western Oregon and Washington valleys), they partition habitat by soil type — Townsend's occupies deep, moist, clay-rich soils while coast mole occupies drier, sandier, and shallower soils. This soil-texture partitioning allows coexistence across broad geographic overlap.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Earthworms, insect larvae, centipedes; proportionally more insects and fewer earthworms than Townsend's mole, reflecting the lower earthworm densities in drier soils
- Foraging strategy: Similar to Townsend's mole but with shallower tunnel systems reflecting lighter soil profiles

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-April
- Gestation: ~28-30 days
- Litter size: 2-4 (typically 3)
- Lifespan: 3-4 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Threats: Urban development; less conflict with agriculture than Townsend's mole due to preference for drier, less productive soils
- Source: [P1], [P2], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The coast mole fills the fossorial engineer niche in drier, lighter soils where Townsend's mole cannot sustain tunnels. Together, the two *Scapanus* species cover the full range of soil types in western PNW lowlands, from saturated clay valley floors (Townsend's) to well-drained sandy foothills (coast mole), ensuring that subterranean engineering influences soil ecology across the entire west-side landscape.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [O4] Burke Museum

### American Shrew-Mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*)

**Taxonomy:** Eulipotyphla > Talpidae > Uropsilinae (some authorities) or Scalopinae
**Status:** Resident — PNW endemic; sole member of genus worldwide
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status; locally common in appropriate habitat
- OR: No special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); restricted to southwestern BC

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 6.0-8.5 cm (head-body); tail 2.5-3.5 cm, scaly and sparsely haired, thick and slightly constricted at base
- Mass: 9-14 g, averaging 10-12 g; the smallest mole in North America
- Sexual dimorphism: Negligible
- Distinguishing features: This animal defies easy categorization. It is a mole by taxonomy but looks and behaves partly like a shrew. It is smaller than any other North American mole by a factor of five or more, and its body proportions — relatively slender build, only moderately enlarged forefeet, visible but small eyes, a long flexible snout — place it morphologically between the true moles and the shrews. The forefeet are broadened for digging but not rotated outward or massively muscled like those of *Scapanus*; they are functional digging tools but also adequate for surface locomotion and even climbing. The pelage is dense, soft, and dark slate-gray to black, with a subtle iridescent sheen. The tail is thick, scaly, and slightly constricted at its base — unique among PNW insectivores. The snout is elongated, flexible, and covered with vibrissae and Eimer's organs, though fewer than in *Scapanus*.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Moist, well-vegetated ravines and steep hillsides in mature coniferous and mixed forests — the shrew-mole is strongly associated with steep slopes, ravine bottoms, and deeply shaded habitats with heavy moss cover, dense sword fern understory, and accumulations of rotting logs
- Elevation range: Sea level to 2,500 ft; concentrated in coastal lowlands and Coast Range foothills
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest (Zone 3, primary), Riparian (Zone 5), Fossorial (Zone 8 — cross-cutting), Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2 — lower elevations)
- Home range: 0.02-0.1 ha; three-dimensionally complex, incorporating both below-ground tunnels and above-ground surface trails through moss and leaf litter
- Microhabitat: Unlike true moles, the shrew-mole is not exclusively subterranean. It constructs shallow tunnel systems in humus and surface soil layers but also forages extensively on the surface, in leaf litter, through moss mats, and even in low shrubs. It has been observed climbing into sword fern fronds and low brush — behaviors completely outside the repertoire of any *Scapanus* mole. This semi-fossorial lifestyle places the shrew-mole ecologically between the fully subterranean moles and the surface-dwelling shrews, occupying a niche partition that no other PNW mammal fills.

**Unique Biology:**
The American shrew-mole is one of the most evolutionarily distinctive mammals in the Pacific Northwest. It is the sole living species in the genus *Neurotrichus*, which has no close living relatives. Its phylogenetic position within the Talpidae has been debated for over a century: some authorities place it in the subfamily Uropsilinae alongside the Asian shrew-moles (*Uropsilus*), which would make it the only New World representative of an otherwise East Asian lineage — a biogeographic pattern suggesting a Beringian dispersal in the Miocene or Pliocene, followed by extinction across the entire intervening range. Others place *Neurotrichus* in the Scalopinae alongside *Scapanus* and *Condylura*, implying independent evolution of the shrew-like body plan within the New World mole radiation.

Regardless of its phylogenetic placement, the shrew-mole's morphology is clearly ancestral relative to the fully fossorial *Scapanus* moles. Its moderately enlarged forefeet, functional eyes, slender build, and surface-foraging behavior may approximate the body plan of the common ancestor of all North American moles — a semi-fossorial, forest-floor insectivore that had not yet committed fully to subterranean life. In this interpretation, *Neurotrichus* is a living window into the evolutionary transition from surface-dwelling insectivore to fully subterranean mole, preserved in the mild, moist forests of the Pacific coast where the selective pressures driving complete fossoriality may have been weaker than in the continental interior.

The species' geographic range is tightly restricted to the Pacific coastal forests from southern BC to northern California — the same "Pacific madrone" biogeographic zone that harbors other endemic or relict taxa including the mountain beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*), the Pacific giant salamander (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*), and the tailed frog (*Ascaphus truei*). This pattern of endemism reflects the deep geological and climatic history of the Pacific coast, where stable, mild, wet conditions have persisted since the Miocene, allowing ancient lineages to survive that were eliminated by continental glaciation, desiccation, and grassland expansion elsewhere.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Earthworms, insect larvae (especially beetle grubs), centipedes, spiders, sowbugs (isopods), snails, and slugs; also consumes small amounts of plant material and fungal spores
- Foraging strategy: Unlike *Scapanus*, which forages exclusively underground, the shrew-mole forages in multiple strata — beneath the surface in shallow humus tunnels, on the surface through leaf litter and moss, and occasionally above the surface in low vegetation. This three-dimensional foraging strategy gives it access to prey items unavailable to either fully fossorial moles or surface-dwelling shrews.
- Activity pattern: Cathemeral — active in short bursts throughout the 24-hour cycle, with no consistent diel pattern. Like shrews, the shrew-mole's small body size demands frequent feeding, though its metabolic rate is lower than a comparably sized *Sorex*.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Poorly documented; presumably solitary
- Breeding season: February-May; 1-2 litters per year
- Gestation: Unknown (no confirmed data; estimated 28-30 days based on related species)
- Litter size: 1-4 (typically 2-3), born in shallow underground nest chambers lined with leaf litter and moss
- Weaning: Unknown
- Sexual maturity: Presumed 8-10 months
- Lifespan: 2-3 years (estimated)

**Social Structure:** Solitary but less aggressively territorial than *Scapanus*. Multiple shrew-moles have been captured in close proximity, suggesting some degree of home range overlap, though it is unclear whether this represents tolerance or simply high-density populations in optimal habitat.

**Population:**
- Trend: Presumed stable where mature forest persists; vulnerable to old-growth logging
- Estimate: No regional estimate; locally common in appropriate habitat (ravine bottoms in old-growth forests), but patchily distributed
- Threats: Old-growth logging and forest conversion; the shrew-mole's dependence on deep humus layers, coarse woody debris, and dense understory vegetation makes it sensitive to forestry practices that remove these structural elements. Managed forests younger than 60-80 years typically lack sufficient moss cover and humus accumulation to support shrew-mole populations.
- Source: [P1], [P2], [P3], Carraway & Verts 1991

**Ecological Role:** The American shrew-mole bridges the ecological gap between the surface insectivore niche occupied by shrews and the subterranean engineer niche occupied by true moles. By foraging across multiple strata — underground, on the surface, and in low vegetation — it exploits invertebrate prey that neither shrews nor moles efficiently access. Its tunnel networks are shallower and less extensive than those of *Scapanus*, but they create microhabitat structure in the humus layer that benefits other small forest-floor organisms. As a PNW endemic restricted to old-growth and mature forest, the shrew-mole is a conservation indicator for intact Pacific coastal forest ecosystems.

**Cross-Links:**
- Mountain beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*): Shares Pacific coastal endemic biogeographic pattern and dependence on old-growth forest structure
- Pacific giant salamander: Co-occurs in ravine-bottom habitats; both species depend on coarse woody debris and deep humus
- Zone 8 contributor: Semi-fossorial soil engineering connects to the broader fossorial ecosystem service provided by *Scapanus* moles and pocket gophers
- AVI companion: Northern pygmy-owl and Northern saw-whet owl may prey on surface-foraging shrew-moles

## 2. Order Chiroptera — Bats

The Chiroptera — the "hand-winged" mammals — are the second most species-rich order of mammals globally (after Rodentia) and the only mammals capable of true powered flight. In the Pacific Northwest, approximately 15 species of bats have been documented, all belonging to the suborder Yangochiroptera and the family Vespertilionidae (evening bats), with one species in the family Molossidae (free-tailed bats). These are exclusively insectivorous bats — no fruit bats, nectar bats, vampire bats, or carnivorous bats occur in the PNW.

PNW bats face an unprecedented conservation crisis. White-nose syndrome (WNS), caused by the fungus *Pseudogymnoascus destructans*, has killed an estimated 6-7 million bats across eastern North America since its discovery in 2006 and is advancing westward. Wind energy development kills hundreds of thousands of migratory bats annually, with the hoary bat bearing disproportionate mortality. And chronic, low-visibility threats — habitat loss, roost disturbance, pesticide exposure, and climate change — continue to erode bat populations in ways that are difficult to detect because bats are nocturnal, cryptic, and poorly monitored compared to birds or large mammals.

Despite these threats, bats provide ecosystem services of enormous value. A single little brown bat consumes an estimated 4-8 g of insects per night — roughly half its body weight. Scaled to the PNW's bat population, this represents billions of insects consumed nightly during the active season, including agricultural pests (moths, beetles, mosquitoes) whose suppression would otherwise require chemical intervention. Economic valuations of bat pest suppression services in North American agriculture range from $3.7 billion to $53 billion annually (Boyles et al. 2011).

The PNW sits at a critical juncture in bat conservation. WNS has not yet reached catastrophic levels in the region, but the fungus has been detected in Washington and is expected to spread. The decisions made now about roost protection, habitat management, wind energy siting, and disease surveillance will determine whether the PNW's bat fauna survives the coming decades intact.

---

### White-Nose Syndrome: Regional Context

White-nose syndrome (WNS) is caused by *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* (Pd), a cold-adapted fungus that invades the skin of hibernating bats during torpor, causing abnormal arousal cycles that deplete fat reserves and lead to starvation, dehydration, and death. The fungus was first detected in a New York cave in 2006 and has since spread to 40+ US states and 8 Canadian provinces. In affected hibernacula in eastern North America, mortality rates have reached 90-100%, with some regional populations of little brown bats declining by more than 90%.

*Pseudogymnoascus destructans* was confirmed in Washington state in 2016 on a little brown bat near North Bend (King County). As of 2024, Pd has been detected at multiple sites in Washington but large-scale die-offs comparable to eastern populations have not yet occurred in the PNW. Several hypotheses may explain this temporal delay: PNW bat hibernation behavior differs from eastern populations (many PNW bats hibernate in smaller aggregations, in structures other than large caves, or at different temperatures); the PNW strain may have arrived recently and not yet reached epizootic levels; and western bat species may have different susceptibility profiles than eastern species.

The Washington State Bat Conservation Plan [G10] identifies WNS preparedness as a top priority and recommends hibernaculum surveys, pre-WNS population baselines, and coordinated monitoring with USFS and USFWS. The plan's core concern is that the PNW's relatively modest number of known hibernacula makes the region's bat fauna particularly vulnerable — if WNS establishes in the few large known hibernation sites, population collapse could be swift and severe.

**No specific roost or hibernaculum locations are provided in this document, consistent with bat conservation best practices and safety boundaries.**

### Little Brown Bat (*Myotis lucifugus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Myotinae
**Status:** Resident — year-round; historically most abundant bat in the PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed, but under active review for listing; USFWS 90-day finding positive (2022)
- IUCN: Endangered (uplisted from Least Concern in 2018 due to WNS)
- WA: State Candidate species; SGCN; priority species in WA Bat Conservation Plan [G10]
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species
- ID: SGCN
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure), but monitoring intensifying; COSEWIC Endangered

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 6-10 cm (head-body); tail 3-5 cm
- Wingspan: 22-27 cm
- Mass: 5-14 g, averaging 7-9 g; mass varies substantially with season — heaviest in late autumn before hibernation, lightest in spring upon emergence
- Sexual dimorphism: Females slightly larger than males (reversed sexual dimorphism, common in bats)
- Distinguishing features: Medium-sized *Myotis* with glossy brown pelage (ranging from yellowish-brown to dark olive-brown), darker on the back and paler on the venter. Ears medium-length, tragus (the fleshy projection inside the ear) blunt-tipped and approximately half the ear length — a key identification character for the genus *Myotis*. Wing membranes dark brown. Calcar (ankle spur supporting the tail membrane) without a keel. The species is frustratingly similar in appearance to several congeners (Yuma myotis, long-legged myotis, California myotis), and reliable field identification often requires examination in hand or acoustic analysis of echolocation calls.

**Echolocation:**
The little brown bat echolocates using frequency-modulated (FM) sweeps, typically from approximately 80 kHz down to 40 kHz, with a dominant frequency around 45-50 kHz. Call duration, sweep rate, and bandwidth vary with context: in open habitat, calls are longer and narrower in bandwidth (approaching quasi-constant-frequency calls that maximize detection range), while in cluttered forest interiors, calls are shorter and broader in bandwidth (maximizing spatial resolution for obstacle avoidance). The echolocation system is sophisticated enough to detect, localize, and identify flying insects at distances of 2-5 meters, even against the acoustic clutter of vegetation — a sonar performance that human engineering has not fully replicated at comparable scales.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Generalist roost user — maternity colonies in buildings (attics, barns, churches, bridges), tree cavities, and bat boxes; solitary males roost in tree bark crevices, rock fissures, and buildings; hibernates in caves, mines, and other underground structures with stable temperatures (1-9 degrees Celsius) and high humidity (>90%)
- Elevation range: Sea level to 6,000+ ft; maternity colonies concentrated in lowlands, hibernacula often at higher elevations
- Ecoregion: All zones except Marine (Zone 6) — forages over Lowland Forest (Zone 3), Riparian (Zone 5), Foothill (Zone 4), and Montane (Zone 2); hibernates in Fossorial/Subterranean (Zone 8) environments
- Foraging habitat: Water surfaces (lakes, ponds, slow rivers), forest edges, forest openings, meadows, and suburban landscapes; strongly associated with water for both foraging and drinking

**Seasonal Cycle:**
The little brown bat's annual cycle revolves around two imperatives: reproduction (summer) and hibernation (winter).

*Spring emergence (March-April):* Bats emerge from hibernacula having lost 25-35% of their pre-hibernation body mass. Females are already pregnant — mating occurred in autumn, with sperm stored in the female reproductive tract through hibernation and fertilization delayed until spring arousal (a reproductive strategy unique to temperate-zone bats and a handful of other mammals). Females migrate from hibernacula to maternity colony sites, which may be 10-100+ km distant.

*Maternity season (May-August):* Females congregate in maternity colonies of 20-1,000+ individuals, typically in warm structures (attics, barns) where temperatures reach 30-40 degrees Celsius, accelerating fetal development and pup growth. Males do not form maternity colonies and are largely solitary or in small bachelor groups during summer. A single pup is born per female in June or July (twinning is rare). The pup is altricial, born naked with closed eyes, weighing approximately 2 g. Growth is rapid: eyes open at 2-3 days, flight begins at 18-21 days, and the pup is independent by 4-5 weeks of age.

*Swarming and mating (August-October):* Bats congregate at hibernaculum entrances in "swarming" aggregations that function as mating arenas. Males patrol cave entrances and mate with females as they arrive. Swarming also serves as a knowledge-transfer mechanism — juvenile bats learn the locations of hibernacula by following adults, and the behavioral tradition of returning to specific hibernation sites is maintained across generations.

*Hibernation (October-April):* Bats enter torpor in hibernacula, reducing heart rate from approximately 200 bpm (active) to 10-20 bpm, body temperature from 37 degrees Celsius to near ambient (1-9 degrees Celsius), and metabolic rate to approximately 1-2% of active levels. Hibernation is not continuous — bats arouse periodically (every 2-4 weeks in healthy individuals) to drink, urinate, and occasionally relocate within the hibernaculum. These arousals are energetically expensive, consuming approximately 80% of the total winter energy budget. This is the mechanism by which WNS kills: the fungal infection causes irritation that triggers abnormally frequent arousals, each of which depletes fat reserves, ultimately leading to starvation weeks or months before spring emergence.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — midges (Chironomidae), mosquitoes (Culicidae), moths (Lepidoptera), beetles (Coleoptera), caddisflies (Trichoptera), mayflies (Ephemeroptera); diet composition varies with habitat and season
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking — captures insects in flight using the tail membrane as a scoop, transferring prey to the mouth in flight. Typically forages 2-10 m above water surfaces or along forest edges. A single bat can capture 500-1,000 insects per hour during peak foraging.
- Foraging range: 1-5 km from roost to foraging areas; may use multiple foraging patches per night
- Water dependence: Must drink regularly, swooping to water surfaces to lap while in flight; foraging over water combines prey capture and drinking

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; both sexes mate with multiple partners during autumn swarming
- Delayed fertilization: Sperm stored in female reproductive tract for 5-7 months (October to March/April)
- Gestation: 50-60 days (after spring fertilization)
- Litter size: 1 pup per year (rarely 2); among the lowest reproductive rates of any PNW small mammal
- Weaning: 18-25 days; volant (capable of flight) by 18-21 days
- Sexual maturity: Females breed at 1 year; males may not successfully compete until 2+ years
- Lifespan: Extraordinary for a mammal of this size — documented individuals have survived 30+ years in the wild (the record is 34 years for a banded individual). This exceptional longevity, combined with the low reproductive rate of 1 pup per year, means that population recovery from catastrophic events like WNS is measured in decades to centuries.

**Social Structure:** Highly colonial during maternity season. Females show strong natal philopatry — they return to the same maternity colony year after year, often the colony where they were born. This fidelity creates multi-generational maternity colonies that can persist for decades in the same building or structure. Males are largely solitary in summer. Both sexes aggregate at swarming sites and in hibernacula.

**Population:**
- Trend: Catastrophic decline in eastern North America (>90% in many populations) due to WNS; PNW populations currently stable but at imminent risk
- Estimate: No comprehensive PNW population estimate exists. The WA Bat Conservation Plan [G10] identifies the little brown bat as the most frequently documented species at bat survey sites statewide, suggesting it remains relatively common — for now.
- Threats: White-nose syndrome (primary existential threat); building renovations that exclude maternity colonies; pesticide reduction of insect prey; artificial lighting that disrupts foraging patterns; wind turbines (minor for this species compared to migratory bats); climate change (altering insect emergence timing, hibernation duration)
- Source: [G10], [G12], [P5], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The little brown bat is a keystone insectivore in PNW freshwater and forest-edge ecosystems. Its consumption of aquatic insects — midges, mosquitoes, caddisflies — links it directly to stream and lake food webs. Maternity colonies near water bodies can reduce local mosquito populations measurably, providing pest suppression benefits to human communities. The species' colonial roosting habit also creates nutrient hotspots: guano accumulations beneath maternity colonies support specialized invertebrate communities and provide fertilizer to the soil below.

The catastrophic loss of little brown bats from WNS in eastern North America has provided a grim natural experiment in the ecological consequences of bat loss. In regions where little brown bats have declined by 90%+, increases in nocturnal insect abundance have been documented, though the cascading effects on agriculture, forestry, and human health (via mosquito-borne disease) are still being quantified.

**Cultural Note:** Bats in PNW Indigenous traditions carry varied symbolic weight. In some Coast Salish oral traditions, Bat appears as a liminal figure — neither bird nor mouse, inhabiting the boundary between worlds. The Kwakwaka'wakw include Bat masks in certain ceremonial contexts. The Nlaka'pamux (Thompson) associate bats with the spirit world and with twilight [O6-C6]. These traditions recognize the bat's ecological distinctiveness — a mammal that flies, hunts in darkness, and roosts in hidden places — without the negative associations that colonial European culture imposed on bats.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Nightjars (common nighthawk) occupy the crepuscular insectivore niche that bats dominate after dark; minimal direct competition due to temporal partitioning
- Aquatic prey: Bat consumption of emerged aquatic insects (midges, caddisflies) creates a trophic link to stream ecosystems documented in the aquatic modules
- WNS: Disease ecology intersects with cave/mine habitat management
- Predators: Great horned owls, barn owls, and American kestrels occasionally capture bats at roost emergence

(2010) — An emerging disease causes regional population collapse of little brown myotis; Boyles et al. (2011) — Economic importance of bats; [O6-C6] Suttles 1987

### Big Brown Bat (*Eptesicus fuscus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Vespertilioninae
**Status:** Resident — year-round; urban-tolerant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- ID: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 10-13 cm (head-body); tail 3.5-5 cm
- Wingspan: 32-35 cm
- Mass: 14-25 g; the largest vespertilionid bat in the PNW
- Distinguishing features: Large for a vespertilionid, with broad muzzle, dark brown glossy pelage, thick leathery wing membranes, and a robust, muscular build. Ears relatively short and rounded, tragus broad and blunt. Distinguished from all *Myotis* species by larger size, broader muzzle, and keeled calcar.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Urban and suburban environments — buildings, bridges, stadium lights, barn lofts; the most anthropogenic bat in the PNW. Also uses tree hollows and rock crevices in wilder settings.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest/Urban (Zone 3, primary), Foothill (Zone 4), Riparian (Zone 5)
- Foraging habitat: Street lights, parking lot lights, forest edges, meadows, and over water; strongly attracted to artificial lighting that concentrates insect prey

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Beetles (Coleoptera) dominate — comprising 40-60%+ of diet by volume; also moths, flies, and other large-bodied flying insects. The big brown bat specializes in harder-bodied prey than the *Myotis* species, using its robust jaw and dentition to crush beetle elytra.
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking; flies at higher altitudes and faster speeds than *Myotis* species, reflecting its larger body size and wing loading. Often forages around street lights and other artificial light sources, exploiting the insect aggregations they create.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; autumn mating with delayed fertilization (as in little brown bat)
- Gestation: 60 days after spring fertilization
- Litter size: 1-2 pups (twins more common than in *Myotis*)
- Lifespan: Up to 19 years documented

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; appears less susceptible to WNS than *Myotis* species, possibly due to different hibernation behavior (hibernates at lower temperatures, in smaller groups, or in different microhabitats within hibernacula)
- Threats: Building exclusion during maternity season (timing is critical — excluding bats while flightless pups are present causes pup mortality); pesticide reduction of beetle prey
- Source: [G10], [P5], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The big brown bat is the dominant urban-adapted bat in the PNW, filling the nocturnal aerial insectivore niche in human-modified landscapes. Its specialization on beetles makes it a significant predator of agricultural pest beetles, including corn earworm moths and cucumber beetles. Its tolerance of human structures makes it the bat species most commonly encountered by PNW residents — and the species most often involved in human-bat conflicts (roosting in attics, wall voids, and commercial buildings).

**Key Sources:** [G10] WDFW Bat Conservation Plan; [P5] Nagorsen & Brigham 1993; [P1] Maser 1998; Kurta & Baker (1990) — *Eptesicus fuscus*, Mammalian Species

### Hoary Bat (*Lasiurus cinereus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Lasiurinae
**Status:** Migratory — summer resident, winters south of the PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed; under active consideration for listing due to wind energy mortality
- IUCN: Least Concern (range-wide), but trend declining
- WA: State Candidate species; SGCN
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species
- ID: SGCN
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); migratory

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 13-15 cm (head-body); tail 4-6 cm
- Wingspan: 34-41 cm — the largest wingspan of any PNW bat
- Mass: 20-35 g; the largest bat in the PNW by mass
- Sexual dimorphism: Females larger than males (reversed sexual dimorphism)
- Distinguishing features: Unmistakable — the hoary bat is the most striking bat in North America. Pelage thick and dense (an adaptation for long-distance migration at high altitudes and cold temperatures), dark brown to mahogany with heavy silver-white frosting on the tips of dorsal hairs, giving the "hoary" appearance. Fur extends onto the dorsal surface of the tail membrane — diagnostic for the genus *Lasiurus* and unique among PNW bats. Ears short and rounded, edged in black with conspicuous patches of yellowish fur at the base. Face framed by dark fur with a dramatic pale collar. Wing membranes dark, long, and narrow — an aerodynamic profile adapted for fast, long-distance flight rather than the slow, maneuverable flight of *Myotis*.

**Migration:**
The hoary bat is the most migratory bat in the Western Hemisphere. PNW populations arrive in April-May and depart in August-October, wintering in the southwestern US, Mexico, and possibly Central America. Migration routes are poorly known — unlike bird migration, bat migration is invisible to radar at most scales and has been studied primarily through fatality records at wind energy facilities, scattered banding recoveries, and stable isotope analysis.

Migration altitude appears to vary from treetop level to several hundred meters above ground. Hoary bats have been recorded at altitudes exceeding 2,400 m above ground level during migration, and they regularly cross mountain ranges and large bodies of water. Their migration coincides with autumn movements of noctuid moths, their primary prey during passage, and some researchers hypothesize that hoary bats track moth migrations in a pattern analogous to raptor-songbird predator-prey migration systems.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Solitary tree roosting — hoary bats do not use caves, buildings, or colonial roost sites. Instead, they roost singly in the foliage of large trees, hanging from a branch 3-5 m above the ground, concealed by leaves. Preferred roost trees include large conifers (Douglas-fir, western hemlock, ponderosa pine) and deciduous trees (cottonwood, bigleaf maple) with dense canopy cover above and open flight space below the roost.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 6,000+ ft; concentrated in lowlands and foothills during maternity season
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest (Zone 3), Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Foothill/Oak Woodland (Zone 4), Riparian (Zone 5)
- Foraging habitat: Open areas — above forest canopy, over large clearings, meadows, lakes, and rivers; prefers to forage in open airspace where its fast, direct flight style is most effective. Forages at heights of 5-30+ m, higher than most *Myotis*.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Moths (Lepidoptera) dominate — 40-60% of diet; also beetles, true bugs (Hemiptera), dragonflies, and other large-bodied flying insects. The hoary bat is capable of taking prey larger than those consumed by any other PNW bat, including large noctuid moths and even dragonflies.
- Foraging strategy: Fast, direct aerial hawking — the hoary bat flies faster and less maneuverably than *Myotis* species, covering large foraging areas and pursuing prey in open airspace. Echolocation calls are low-frequency (18-25 kHz dominant frequency), loud, and adapted for long-range detection in open habitat — a sonar strategy termed "narrow-beam, long-range."
- Foraging range: May travel 10-20+ km from roost to foraging areas

**Wind Energy Mortality:**
The hoary bat is the single most impacted bat species at wind energy facilities in North America, accounting for approximately 38% of all bat fatalities at turbines continent-wide. In the PNW, wind energy development in the Columbia Plateau, Kittitas County, and coastal ridgelines places turbines directly in hoary bat migration corridors.

The mechanism of mortality involves two components. Direct blade strike kills bats that fly into the swept area of rotating turbines. Barotrauma — rapid air pressure changes near blade tips — causes fatal pulmonary hemorrhage in bats that pass near (but do not contact) spinning blades. The lungs of bats are more susceptible to barotrauma than those of birds due to their compliant thoracic structure, which is adapted for the extreme volume changes required during flight-coupled respiration.

Mortality is concentrated during autumn migration (August-October) and is disproportionately high on low-wind nights when bats are most active and turbine cut-in speeds are lowest. Curtailment strategies — raising the wind speed threshold at which turbines begin operating (cut-in speed) during high-risk periods — have been shown to reduce bat mortality by 50-80% with relatively modest reductions in energy production (typically 1-3% annual output loss). This is the most effective and most cost-efficient mitigation measure currently available, and it has been implemented at several PNW wind facilities.

Why hoary bats are so vulnerable to wind turbines remains incompletely understood. Hypotheses include: (1) attraction to tall structures during migration (possibly mistaken for tall trees suitable for roosting); (2) attraction to the insects that aggregate around turbine towers and nacelles; (3) attraction to the sounds produced by operating turbines; (4) migratory flight paths that follow ridgelines where wind facilities are preferentially sited. The cumulative population-level effect is concerning — population modeling suggests that current mortality rates may be unsustainable for a species that produces only 1-2 pups per year.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Poorly documented; mating occurs during autumn migration or on wintering grounds
- Gestation: Approximately 56 days (after delayed fertilization or delayed implantation — the mechanism is debated in *Lasiurus*)
- Litter size: 2 pups (twins are the norm, unusual among bats); occasionally 1 or 3
- Weaning: Approximately 30-35 days; pups are volant by ~33 days
- Lifespan: Up to 12-13 years documented; likely less than *Myotis* due to the physiological costs of long-distance migration

**Social Structure:** Essentially solitary. Hoary bats do not form colonies at any stage of their annual cycle — they roost alone, forage alone, and migrate alone or in loose, transient aggregations. Females with dependent young are the only regular social unit. This solitary lifestyle makes the hoary bat difficult to study and monitor — there are no maternity colonies to count, no hibernacula to survey, and no aggregation sites to observe. Population estimation relies on acoustic monitoring, mist-net surveys, and wind turbine fatality data — all of which have significant limitations.

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining; the combination of wind energy mortality and naturally low reproductive rate makes this species one of the most conservation-urgent bats in North America
- Estimate: No reliable population estimate exists for any region. The species' solitary habits and migratory behavior make population assessment extremely difficult.
- Threats: Wind energy mortality (primary); habitat loss (removal of large roost trees); pesticide reduction of moth prey; climate change (altering migration timing and moth availability)
- Source: [G10], [P5], [P1]

**Ecological Role:** The hoary bat is the apex nocturnal aerial predator of the PNW — no other bat in the region approaches its body size, foraging altitude, or prey size range. Its specialization on moths makes it a significant predator of forest and agricultural pest species. Its migratory lifestyle connects the PNW's summer insect communities to wintering ecosystems thousands of kilometers south, representing a seasonal energy and nutrient transfer comparable in concept (though not in scale) to the salmon-bear-forest nitrogen cycle.

**Evolutionary Note:** The genus *Lasiurus* is restricted to the Americas and represents a uniquely New World bat radiation. The hoary bat has the largest geographic range of any American bat — from northern Canada to southern Argentina, and including Hawaii (the Hawaiian hoary bat, *L. cinereus semotus*, is the only native land mammal of Hawaii). This extraordinary range likely reflects the species' migratory capability and tolerance of cold temperatures. Molecular phylogenetics suggests the genus diverged from other vespertilionids approximately 10-15 million years ago.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Common nighthawk occupies the crepuscular/nocturnal aerial insectivore niche; no direct competition due to temporal and spatial partitioning
- Wind energy: Hoary bat mortality data informs the broader conversation about renewable energy siting and wildlife impacts
- Migration ecology: Connects PNW summer ecosystems to Neotropical wintering grounds

(2008) — Patterns of bat fatalities at wind energy facilities; Cryan & Barclay (2009) — Causes of bat fatalities at wind turbines

### Silver-haired Bat (*Lasionycteris noctivagans*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Vespertilioninae
**Status:** Migratory — summer resident; some winter residents west of Cascades
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status; common in forests
- OR: No special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Wingspan: 27-31 cm
- Mass: 8-14 g
- Distinguishing features: Medium-sized bat with striking pelage — nearly black fur tipped with silver-white, creating a distinctive frosted appearance. Fur extends slightly onto the tail membrane. Ears short, rounded, and bare. One of the most visually distinctive PNW bats.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Old-growth and mature coniferous forest; roosts in tree cavities, under bark slabs, and in woodpecker holes; strongly associated with forest-interior habitat. One of the most forest-dependent bats in the PNW.
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest (Zone 3), Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Riparian (Zone 5)
- Foraging habitat: Over ponds, streams, and in forest openings; flies slowly and close to vegetation, capturing insects in cluttered airspace

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Moths, flies, beetles, caddisflies, mosquitoes; generalist among forest-flying insects
- Foraging strategy: Slow, maneuverable flight in cluttered forest habitat; echolocation calls broad-bandwidth FM sweeps (40-80 kHz) adapted for detecting prey among vegetation

**Reproduction:**
- Litter size: 2 pups (twins typical, unusual among bats)
- Maternity roosts: Small groups (10-50 females) in tree cavities and under bark; does not use buildings
- Lifespan: Up to 12 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; potentially impacted by wind energy (second most common bat species at wind turbine fatality sites after hoary bat)
- Threats: Old-growth logging (removes roost trees); wind energy mortality during migration; pesticides
- Source: [G10], [G12], [P5]

**Ecological Role:** The silver-haired bat is the dominant forest-interior bat in the PNW — the species most likely to be encountered by biologists conducting bat surveys in old-growth Douglas-fir and western hemlock forests. Its dependence on tree cavities and mature forest structure makes it an indicator species for forest management practices that retain snags and large-diameter trees.

### Townsend's Big-eared Bat (*Corynorhinus townsendii*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Plecotinae
**Status:** Resident — year-round; cave specialist
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed; considered Species of Concern by USFWS
- IUCN: Least Concern (range-wide), but populations declining
- WA: State Candidate species; SGCN; priority species in WA Bat Conservation Plan [G10]
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species; state Sensitive-Critical
- ID: SGCN
- BC: Blue-listed (Special Concern)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 9-11 cm (head-body); tail 4-5 cm
- Wingspan: 30-34 cm
- Mass: 9-14 g
- Sexual dimorphism: Females slightly larger than males
- Distinguishing features: Unmistakable — enormous ears (30-39 mm from notch to tip, nearly half the body length) that can be curled backward along the body when the bat is at rest, resembling ram's horns. The ears are connected across the forehead by a thin band of skin. Two prominent fleshy lumps (glandular masses) on either side of the nose give the face a distinctive profile. Pelage variable — pale brown to dark brown dorsally, paler ventrally. The combination of huge ears and nasal lumps makes this species instantly identifiable, even in dim light.

**Cave Specialization:**
Townsend's big-eared bat is the most cave-dependent bat in the PNW and one of the most cave-dependent in North America. Unlike the little brown bat, which uses caves primarily for hibernation and readily adopts buildings for maternity roosting, Townsend's big-eared bat uses caves and mine adits for all life stages — maternity colonies, hibernation, night roosts (temporary resting sites used between foraging bouts), and transient roosting during spring and autumn movements.

This obligate cave dependence creates extraordinary vulnerability to human disturbance. Townsend's big-eared bat is one of the most sensitive bat species to roost disturbance documented anywhere in the world. A single visit to a maternity cave during the critical period (May-August) can cause colony abandonment — females may drop non-volant pups and flee, resulting in 100% pup mortality for the disturbed colony. Hibernating individuals aroused by human visitors may expend enough energy in a single arousal event to shorten their hibernation endurance by 2-3 weeks, potentially causing pre-spring starvation.

This sensitivity is not a failure of the species — it is a reflection of its evolutionary history. Townsend's big-eared bat evolved in a landscape where caves were reliably undisturbed. The species lacks the behavioral plasticity to tolerate disturbance and has not adapted to human structures the way little brown bats and big brown bats have. Its conservation therefore depends entirely on protecting the integrity of its cave and mine roost sites — a task that requires knowing where those sites are, which conflicts with the conservation imperative of keeping roost locations confidential to prevent disturbance.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Caves, lava tubes, mine adits, and occasionally large hollow trees and abandoned buildings (buildings used primarily in the western portion of its range). Requires large open chambers for maternity colonies — not tight crevice rooster like many *Myotis*.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 7,000 ft
- Ecoregion: All terrestrial zones; roost sites concentrated in Montane Conifer Forest (Zone 2), Foothill/Oak Woodland (Zone 4), East-Side Steppe (Zone 7 — lava tubes), and Fossorial/Subterranean (Zone 8)
- Foraging habitat: Forest edges, riparian corridors, meadows, oak woodland — typically within 10 km of the roost site

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Moths (Lepidoptera) — specialist moth predator, with moths comprising 80-90%+ of the diet by volume. This is one of the most specialized diets of any PNW bat.
- Foraging strategy: Gleaning and slow aerial hawking — the enormous ears are adapted for detecting the faint echoes of moth wings against cluttered vegetation backgrounds. Townsend's big-eared bat is one of the few PNW bats that can glean moths from foliage (plucking stationary prey from leaf surfaces), a skill that requires extraordinary spatial resolution in its echolocation system. Echolocation calls are low-intensity, high-frequency (40-50 kHz dominant), and short-duration — a "whispering" sonar strategy that reduces the distance at which prey can detect the approaching bat. (Some moths have evolved ears that detect bat echolocation calls, allowing evasive maneuvers; low-intensity calls counter this defense.)
- Foraging range: 1-10 km from roost

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; autumn mating at hibernaculum/swarming sites
- Delayed fertilization: Sperm stored through winter (as in *Myotis*)
- Gestation: 56-100 days (highly variable with ambient temperature — development slows in cold roost temperatures)
- Litter size: 1 pup per year
- Weaning: ~6 weeks; pup volant by 2.5-3 weeks
- Sexual maturity: Females breed at 1 year
- Lifespan: Up to 16 years documented

**Social Structure:** Maternity colonies range from 12-200+ females. Colony fidelity is extreme — females return to the same cave or mine year after year, and maternity colonies may occupy a single roost for decades. Males are largely solitary during summer. Hibernation is typically solitary or in small clusters (unlike the dense aggregations formed by little brown bats), with individuals hanging free from cave ceilings rather than tucked into crevices.

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining across much of range; populations reduced by roost disturbance, mine closures, and recreational caving
- Estimate: No comprehensive PNW estimate; the WA Bat Conservation Plan [G10] identifies approximately 50 known maternity and hibernation roost sites statewide, but actual numbers are likely higher due to incomplete survey coverage
- Threats: Roost disturbance (recreational caving, mine exploration, research activities conducted without proper protocols); mine closure with bat-incompatible gates; habitat conversion near roost sites; pesticide reduction of moth prey; WNS (susceptibility unknown but concerning given cave dependence); wind energy (minor for this non-migratory species)
- Source: [G10], [G12], [P5]

**Ecological Role:** Townsend's big-eared bat is the premier moth specialist among PNW bats, and its gleaning foraging strategy gives it access to moth species unavailable to aerial-hawking bats. Its extreme cave dependence makes it a flagship species for subterranean habitat conservation — protecting Townsend's big-eared bat roosts simultaneously benefits other cave-dwelling species including other bat species, cave-adapted invertebrates, and cave-nesting birds (swifts, swallows at cave entrances).

**Cross-Links:**
- Cave ecosystem: Roost sites shared with other cave-dwelling species; guano deposits support specialized invertebrate communities
- AVI companion: Vaux's swift uses similar cave/hollow tree roosting habitats
- Zone 8 connection: Cave and mine dependence links to the fossorial/subterranean ecosystem zone

**Key Sources:** [G10] WDFW Bat Conservation Plan; [G12] USFS bat biology; [P5] Nagorsen & Brigham 1993; [P1] Maser 1998; Kunz & Martin (1982) — *Plecotus townsendii*, Mammalian Species

### Canyon Bat (*Parastrellus hesperus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Vespertilioninae
**Status:** Resident — arid east-side; smallest bat in North America
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Uncommon; restricted to southeastern Washington
- OR: Present in arid eastern Oregon
- ID: Present in southwestern Idaho

**Morphometrics:**
- Wingspan: 19-22 cm — the smallest wingspan of any PNW bat
- Mass: 3-6 g; the smallest bat in North America (formerly known as the western pipistrelle, now reclassified into its own genus)
- Distinguishing features: Tiny size, pale yellowish-brown to grayish pelage, dark facial mask, dark wing membranes contrasting with pale body fur. Flight is slow, fluttery, and butterfly-like — the canyon bat's silhouette against a dusk sky is instantly recognizable to anyone familiar with it.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Desert canyons, rimrock, cliff faces, and lava flows in the driest habitats occupied by any PNW bat. Roosts in rock crevices, cliff fissures, and the spaces behind exfoliated rock faces.
- Ecoregion: East-Side Steppe (Zone 7)

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small swarming insects — gnats, midges, small moths, leafhoppers; prey size extremely small, matching the bat's diminutive gape
- Foraging strategy: Slow, erratic flight over canyon rims and along cliff faces; often the first bat active in the evening, emerging well before full darkness — sometimes foraging while the sun is still above the horizon, a behavior unusual among vespertilionid bats

**Reproduction:**
- Litter size: 2 pups (twins, unusual for vespertilionids this small)
- Lifespan: Unknown; few marked individuals recovered

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable within its limited PNW range
- Source: [G10], [P5]

**Ecological Role:** The canyon bat fills the micro-insectivore niche in PNW desert canyons — taking insects too small for larger bats. Its early evening emergence time reduces competition with other bat species that do not begin foraging until later.

**Key Sources:** [G10] WDFW Bat Conservation Plan; [P5] Nagorsen & Brigham 1993; Brigham et al. (2019) — canyon bat ecology

### Pallid Bat (*Antrozous pallidus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae > Antrozoinae
**Status:** Resident — arid east-side; ground-foraging specialist
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: State Monitor species; SGCN
- OR: Conservation Strategy Species
- ID: SGCN

**Morphometrics:**
- Wingspan: 33-38 cm
- Mass: 14-25 g; one of the larger PNW bats
- Distinguishing features: Large, pale bat with enormous ears (25-35 mm) and a blunt, piglike face with prominent nostrils and thick, fleshy lips. Pelage pale creamy-brown to yellowish, matching the arid substrates of its habitat. Eyes large for a bat — the pallid bat relies more on vision and passive hearing than echolocation for prey detection, a trait that distinguishes it from virtually all other PNW bats. Produces a distinctive musky odor from glands near the muzzle.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Arid canyons, rocky outcrops, cliff faces, dry grasslands, and open shrub-steppe; roosts in rock crevices, caves, mine adits, buildings, and bridges. Restricted to east-side habitats in the PNW.
- Ecoregion: East-Side Steppe (Zone 7, primary), Foothill (Zone 4)

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Ground-dwelling arthropods — scorpions, Jerusalem crickets, large beetles (darkling beetles, ground beetles), centipedes, and sphinx moths. The pallid bat is the only PNW bat that regularly forages on the ground, landing to capture terrestrial prey — a behavior more reminiscent of a nightjar than a bat.
- Foraging strategy: Passive listening — the pallid bat detects prey by listening for the sounds of arthropods walking on the ground, using its enormous ears as directional sound collectors. Upon detecting prey, it swoops down, lands, pins the prey with its wings, and delivers a killing bite. This "listen-and-pounce" strategy operates independently of echolocation, which the pallid bat uses primarily for navigation rather than prey detection.
- Scorpion predation: The pallid bat is apparently immune to scorpion venom — it regularly consumes scorpions (*Hadrurus*, *Paruroctonus*) without apparent ill effects, a trait shared by few other predators. The mechanism of venom resistance is not fully characterized but may involve modified pain receptors or venom-neutralizing serum proteins.

**Reproduction:**
- Litter size: 1-2 pups (twins common)
- Maternity colonies: 20-100+ females in rock crevices or buildings
- Lifespan: Up to 14 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable but limited in PNW; at northwestern range limit
- Threats: Roost disturbance; building exclusion; pesticide reduction of prey arthropods
- Source: [G10], [P5]

**Ecological Role:** The pallid bat occupies a unique niche among PNW bats — it is the only ground-foraging bat in the region and the only bat that relies primarily on passive acoustic detection rather than echolocation for prey capture. Its consumption of scorpions and large ground-dwelling arthropods is ecologically distinctive and provides pest suppression in arid habitats where few other nocturnal predators operate at this scale. Additionally, the pallid bat has been documented as a pollinator of columnar cacti in the southwestern US, though this ecological role is not relevant in the PNW.

**Key Sources:** [G10] WDFW Bat Conservation Plan; [P5] Nagorsen & Brigham 1993; Hermanson & O'Shea (1983) — *Antrozous pallidus*, Mammalian Species

### Brazilian Free-tailed Bat (*Tadarida brasiliensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Molossidae
**Status:** Resident/seasonal — southern Oregon, expanding northward
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- OR: No special status; uncommon, restricted to southern Oregon
- WA: Occasional records; not established as a breeding species

**Morphometrics:**
- Wingspan: 28-33 cm
- Mass: 8-14 g
- Distinguishing features: Medium-sized bat distinguished by a thick, muscular tail that extends well beyond the tail membrane (uropatagium) — the "free tail" that gives the family its common name. Ears broad, thick, and leathery, nearly meeting at the midline of the forehead. Pelage dark brown to grayish-brown. Wings long, narrow, and pointed — adapted for fast, high-altitude flight. In flight, the free-tailed bat is noticeably faster and more direct than any vespertilionid, with narrow, swept-back wings that resemble a small swift more than a typical bat.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Caves, buildings, and bridges; in the PNW, at the northern limit of its range, most records come from buildings and bridges in southern Oregon's arid interior (Klamath, Lake, Jackson counties)
- Ecoregion: East-Side Steppe (Zone 7), Foothill (Zone 4)

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Moths, beetles, and other high-altitude flying insects; the Brazilian free-tailed bat forages at higher altitudes (up to 3,000+ m above ground level) than any other PNW bat, exploiting insect layers that no other regional bat species can reach
- Foraging strategy: Fast, direct aerial hawking at high altitude; echolocation calls low-frequency (20-30 kHz dominant), loud, and adapted for long-range detection in open airspace. Documented flying at speeds exceeding 160 km/h in level flight, making it one of the fastest-flying animals in the world.

**Reproduction:**
- Litter size: 1 pup per year
- Maternity colonies: Can be enormous (millions of individuals at Bracken Cave, Texas) but PNW colonies are small (tens to hundreds)
- Lifespan: Up to 8 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Possibly expanding northward in the PNW as climate warms
- Threats: Building exclusion; pesticide reduction of insect prey at agricultural margins; climate uncertainty (northward expansion may bring the species into novel ecological contexts)
- Source: [G10], [P5]

**Ecological Role:** The Brazilian free-tailed bat is the ultimate high-altitude nocturnal insectivore — it forages at altitudes unreachable by any other PNW bat, connecting the region's upper-atmosphere insect ecology to the terrestrial food web. In southern portions of its range (Texas, Mexico), the species provides agricultural pest suppression services valued at hundreds of millions of dollars annually through consumption of corn earworm moths. Its ecological role in the PNW is currently modest due to its small population and peripheral range status, but northward expansion with climate change may increase its regional significance.

## 3. Bat Conservation in the PNW — Summary

The conservation outlook for PNW bats is defined by three converging threats, each operating at a different scale but collectively capable of transforming the region's bat fauna within a generation.

**White-nose syndrome** is the most immediate existential threat. The fungus *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* is present in Washington and will likely spread to Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia. Eastern North American bat populations have experienced catastrophic declines — the little brown bat, once the continent's most abundant bat, has declined by >90% in many eastern hibernacula. If WNS reaches equivalent lethality in the PNW, the consequences for insect suppression, agricultural pest control, and freshwater ecosystem function will be substantial. The WA Bat Conservation Plan [G10] and USFS research [G12] have established baseline monitoring and hibernaculum surveys to track the disease's advance, but no treatment or prevention technology currently exists at the population scale.

**Wind energy development** poses a chronic, population-level threat to migratory tree-roosting bats — hoary bat, silver-haired bat, and to a lesser extent the big brown bat. The PNW's wind energy capacity is expanding, with major facilities in the Columbia Plateau, Kittitas County, and coastal ridgelines. Curtailment (raising turbine cut-in speeds during high-risk periods) is the most effective available mitigation, but adoption is inconsistent and the cumulative population effect of current mortality rates may be unsustainable for species with low reproductive rates. The hoary bat, producing only 1-2 pups per year, is the species most at risk from turbine mortality at the population level.

**Habitat loss** operates at the slowest timescale but is the most pervasive threat. Maternity roost destruction through building renovation, mine closure without bat-compatible gates, old-growth logging that removes roost trees, and riparian degradation that reduces foraging habitat all erode the carrying capacity of the landscape for bats. The WA Bat Conservation Plan identifies roost protection — both known and undiscovered — as the highest-priority conservation action, because the loss of a single maternity colony can eliminate a local population that may have occupied that roost for decades.

The PNW is currently in a window of opportunity. WNS has not yet caused mass mortality in the region. Wind energy curtailment strategies are available and proven. Roost protection is achievable with relatively modest investment. The ecological and economic value of bat populations — billions of insects consumed annually, pest suppression services worth billions of dollars, and irreplaceable roles in nocturnal food webs — is well documented. What remains to be seen is whether the region's conservation institutions will act with sufficient speed and coordination to protect bat populations before the threats fully manifest.
