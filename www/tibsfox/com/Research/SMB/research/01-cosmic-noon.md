# Cosmic Noon & the SMBH Growth Arc

## Defining Cosmic Noon

Cosmic noon refers to the epoch approximately 10 billion years ago (redshift z ~ 2, lookback time ~10.3 Gyr) when SMBH growth reached its peak across observable history. During this period, black holes powered luminous quasars -- quasi-stellar objects first identified in the late 1950s as anomalously bright radio sources.

The term reflects a parallel with cosmic star formation history: star formation rate density also peaks near z ~ 2, suggesting the two phenomena share the same cold gas supply.

## The Growth Rate Table

| Epoch | Redshift / Age | SMBH Growth State |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Quasar Dawn | z ~ 6 / 0.9 Gyr | Rapid seed growth; quasars lighting up |
| Cosmic Noon | z ~ 2 / 3.3 Gyr | Peak accretion; universe-wide maximum |
| Post-Noon | z ~ 1 / 7.8 Gyr | Significant decline; "leisurely" phase |
| Recent | z ~ 0.5 / 10.6 Gyr | Major slowdown; "glacial" phase |
| Today | z = 0 / 13.8 Gyr | Near-dormant; continued decline expected |

## The Decline

X-ray observations have tracked the decline by studying black holes at different distances -- each distance corresponding to a different cosmic epoch. The decline was observed for decades before its cause was identified.
