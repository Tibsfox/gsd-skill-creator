# The Wedding-Cake Survey Architecture

## Why Multiple Telescopes?

No single telescope covers the required dynamic range of black hole activity across cosmic time. The Yu et al. study used a tiered "wedding-cake" structure:

| Observatory | Tier | Coverage | Role |
|------------|------|---------|------|
| NASA Chandra | Top (deep) | Small area, very deep | Faint and distant AGN near cosmic noon |
| ESA XMM-Newton | Middle | Medium area, medium depth | Intermediate-luminosity AGN |
| eROSITA | Bottom (shallow) | Wide area, shallow | Large-scale statistical context |

The combined dataset: approximately **1.3 million galaxies** and **8,000 growing SMBHs**.

## The Disentanglement Problem

Both more massive black holes and faster-accreting black holes produce brighter X-ray emission. Without correcting for mass, a decline in observed brightness could be misattributed to slower accretion when it might reflect a shift in typical mass. The multi-wavelength approach -- optical/infrared for mass estimates, X-ray for accretion signatures -- allowed the team to isolate these contributions.

## Supporting Data

The team also used Hubble Space Telescope and James Webb Space Telescope observations to estimate black hole masses. This multi-observatory approach represents the state of the art in X-ray survey methodology and demonstrates why comprehensive answers to cosmological questions require coordinated observational campaigns.
