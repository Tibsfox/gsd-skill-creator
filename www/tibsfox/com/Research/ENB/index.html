Eskimo North BBS

BBS History · Community Computing · Dial-Up to Shell · Digital Commons · The Dial-Up Log
Overview Origins Unix Transition ISP Pivot Survival BBS Context Mission Pack
In late 1981, a man named Robert Dinse bought a Tandy TRS-80 Model III to produce a newsletter. He added a 300-baud modem so collaborators could access the machine remotely. War dialers found it almost immediately. He put up a BBS front-end to stop them from wrecking his files. He didn't know that casual act of self-defense was the founding moment of a system that would still be running forty-four years later. Eskimo North — Shoreline, Washington — 1982 to present.

Research Modules

M1: Origins & Technical Foundations

History Track A · 1978–1985

Pre-BBS background, pirate radio, Pacific Northwest Bell. The TRS-80, COMBASIC, STIX, the Minibin relationship, and the naming of Eskimo North. 3 MB of storage, Infocom games, and the 4 AM instant-connect moment.

M2: Unix Transition & Community Growth

Technical Track A · 1985–1992

The decision for Unix over CP/M and OS/9. Tandy 16B to Sun SPARC. The “Nanook” handle. CompuServe as inspiration, Pirates of Puget Sound as proof. mmbbs, Usenet, and the shell account model.

M3: ISP Pivot & Commercial Era

Technical Track A · 1992–2000

The CIX, Sprint T1 lines, 256 phone lines in a basement. Incorporation in 1995. The move to co-location. The BBS collapse kills 50,000 systems. Why Eskimo North survived.

M4: Long-Term Survival & Adaptation

Community Track B · 2000–Present

Intel i7-2600 homebrew servers. The Integra failure and Isomedia move. phpBB web BBS, Citadel revival. Five operator philosophy principles. Still answering the phone in 2025.

M5: BBS Historical Context & Ecosystem

Network Track B · 1978–2004

CBBS and the blizzard of 1978. The Hayes Smartmodem effect. FidoNet, UUCP, Usenet gateways. ANSI art and door games. The collapse: 60,000 to 10,000 in two years. The ISP metamorphosis. The WELL, PTT, and the digital commons.
