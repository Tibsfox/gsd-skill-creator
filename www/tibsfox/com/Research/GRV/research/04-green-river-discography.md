# Come On Down, Dry as a Bone, Rehab Doll

> **Domain:** Music History & Discography
> **Module:** 4 -- Green River Discography
> **Through-line:** *Three releases. Nineteen tracks. Thirty-eight minutes of music that spawned a genre. The entire catalog fits on one CD with room to spare.* Green River's complete recorded output is remarkably small -- two EPs and one full-length album, recorded between 1984 and 1988. The brevity makes the impact all the more striking: fewer than forty minutes of music seeded an entire musical revolution.

## 1. Overview

Green River's complete studio discography consists of three releases:

| Release | Year | Label | Catalog | Runtime | Tracks |
|---------|------|-------|---------|---------|--------|
| *Come On Down* | 1985 | Homestead Records | HMS 031 | 27:43 | 6 |
| *Dry as a Bone* | 1987 | Sub Pop Records | SP 11 | 19:24 | 5 |
| *Rehab Doll* | 1988 | Sub Pop Records | -- | 28:40 | 8 (+1 bonus) |

**Combined runtime:** 75:47 (approximately 76 minutes)

Nineteen tracks across three releases. The band also contributed two tracks to the *Deep Six* compilation (1986, C/Z Records) and recorded demos at Reciprocal Recording on June 23, 1984. Sub Pop released expanded deluxe editions of *Dry as a Bone* and *Rehab Doll* in 2019 with bonus material [16][24][25][26].

## 2. Come On Down (1985)

**Label:** Homestead Records | **Catalog:** HMS 031 | **Recorded:** December 1984 | **Studio:** Crow Studios, Seattle | **Released:** November 1985 | **Produced by:** Green River and Chris Hanzsek

| # | Title | Composer | Length |
|---|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Come On Down | Ament/Gossard | 3:23 |
| 2 | New God | Alex Vincent | 4:29 |
| 3 | Swallow My Pride | Steve Turner | 3:04 |
| 4 | Ride of Your Life | Ament/Gossard | 4:16 |
| 5 | Corner of My Eye | Ament/Gossard | 5:40 |
| 6 | Tunnel of Love | Jeff Ament | 7:27 |

**Total runtime:** 27:43

All lyrics by Mark Arm. Engineering by Chris Hanzsek. Photography and cover concept by Charles Peterson, who would go on to become the defining visual documentarian of the Seattle scene [16][24].

### 2.1 The First Grunge Record

*Come On Down* is considered the first grunge record -- released several months before the *Deep Six* compilation and predating every other recording by a band of this style. It was recorded while Steve Turner was still in the band, meaning this is the only Green River release featuring the original five-piece lineup [16][24].

The EP was released on Homestead Records, a New York-based independent label run by Gerard Cosloy (who had also signed Dinosaur Jr. and Big Black), because no Seattle label existed yet that could handle a vinyl release. The irony is significant: the first grunge record had to be released by a New York label because Seattle's independent infrastructure hadn't been built yet [16][24].

### 2.2 The Sound

The sound is raw, somewhere between punk and metal -- described as "dirty" and "a mashup of 1980s metal together with early Mudhoney." Which makes sense: half the band literally became Mudhoney. The recording quality reflects Crow Studios' limitations and the band's budget -- or lack thereof. But the rawness is not a deficiency. It is the sound. The grit, the distortion, the sense of a band barely containing its own energy -- these are features, not bugs [16][24].

### 2.3 Songwriting Credits

The songwriting credits reveal the band's internal dynamics. Ament and Gossard co-wrote three of the six tracks -- the rock-oriented, riff-driven material. Turner wrote "Swallow My Pride" -- the punkiest track. Vincent wrote "New God." Ament wrote "Tunnel of Love" alone. Even at this early stage, the creative tensions that would eventually split the band are visible in the credits [16][24].

## 3. Dry as a Bone (1987)

**Label:** Sub Pop Records | **Catalog:** SP 11 | **Recorded:** July 1986 | **Studio:** Reciprocal Recording, Seattle | **Released:** June 1987 | **Produced by:** Jeff Ament and Jack Endino

| # | Title | Length |
|---|-------|--------|
| 1 | Unwind | 4:42 |
| 2 | Baby Takes | 4:24 |
| 3 | This Town | 3:23 |
| 4 | P.C.C. | 3:44 |
| 5 | Ozzie | 3:11 |

**Total runtime:** 19:24

"Ozzie" is a cover of a song by Tales of Terror, a Sacramento hardcore band. All other songs written by Green River. All lyrics by Mark Arm. Kim Gordon of Sonic Youth contributed vocals [16][25].

### 3.1 Sub Pop's First Non-Compilation Release

This is the record that matters most historically, for three reasons:

**First:** It was Sub Pop's first non-compilation release (SP11), making Green River the first band on what would become the most important independent label of the late 1980s and early 1990s [22][25].

**Second:** Bruce Pavitt's catalog description -- "ultra-loose GRUNGE that destroyed the morals of a generation" -- was the first commercial use of the word "grunge" to describe the Seattle sound. A marketing blurb became a genre name [22][25].

**Third:** Jack Endino produced it. This was an early collaboration between Endino and the Seattle scene at Reciprocal Recording. Endino would go on to produce Soundgarden's *Screaming Life*, Mudhoney's *Superfuzz Bigmuff*, and Nirvana's *Bleach* -- all at the same studio, all with the same raw aesthetic. The *Dry as a Bone* sessions helped establish the sonic template for the entire movement [16][25].

### 3.2 The Delayed Release

The recording was delayed a full year because Pavitt couldn't afford to press it. The sessions happened in July 1986, but it wasn't until Poneman invested in Sub Pop in 1987 that the resources existed to release the EP. The gap between recording and release is itself a document of the scene's economic reality: the music existed, the infrastructure to distribute it did not [22][25].

### 3.3 The Sound

Critics noted that the recording "felt spontaneous, like the band got locked in a room... he hit record and they just saw what happened." That was Endino's approach: capture the energy, don't polish it away. The result is looser, louder, and more confident than *Come On Down* -- a band that had spent two years playing live and knew exactly how much chaos they could contain [25].

## 4. Rehab Doll (1988)

**Label:** Sub Pop Records | **Recorded:** July 1987 -- January 1988 | **Studios:** Steve Lawson Productions and Reciprocal Recording, Seattle | **Released:** 1988 | **Produced by:** Bruce Calder | **Pre-production:** Jack Endino

| # | Title | Length |
|---|-------|--------|
| 1 | Forever Means | 4:20 |
| 2 | Rehab Doll | 3:23 |
| 3 | Swallow My Pride | 2:59 |
| 4 | Together We'll Never | 4:01 |
| 5 | Smilin' and Dyin' | 3:23 |
| 6 | Porkfist | 3:13 |
| 7 | Take a Dive | 3:28 |
| 8 | One More Stitch | 3:53 |

**Total runtime:** 28:40

**Cassette bonus track:** "Queen Bitch" (2:58) -- a David Bowie cover [16][26].

### 4.1 Recorded by a Dead Band

*Rehab Doll* is Green River's only full-length album, and it was recorded by a band that was already breaking apart. The recording sessions spanned from July 1987 to January 1988 -- but the band had effectively ceased to exist by late October 1987, when Ament, Gossard, and Fairweather announced their intention to quit. The members honored their commitment to finish the record, but the sessions were conducted under the shadow of dissolution [16][26].

### 4.2 "Swallow My Pride" -- The Re-Recording

"Swallow My Pride" appears here in a re-recorded version -- the original appeared on *Come On Down* three years earlier, written by Steve Turner, who was no longer in the band. The re-recording is tighter, more polished, and less raw -- which tells you everything about the direction Gossard and Ament wanted to take the music. The two versions of the same song are an audio document of the split: same words, different philosophy [16][26][28].

### 4.3 "Take a Dive"

"Take a Dive" is reportedly about the internal tensions within the band -- Arm's perspective on the commercial ambitions of his bandmates. If "Swallow My Pride" documents the split sonically, "Take a Dive" documents it lyrically. The band was writing about its own dissolution while completing the record that dissolution had interrupted [16][26].

### 4.4 The Bowie Cover

The inclusion of "Queen Bitch" as a cassette bonus track is revealing. Bowie was glam -- theatrical, image-conscious, commercially ambitious. He was exactly the kind of artist Arm's punk ethic should have rejected. But Green River covered him anyway, because the band's taste was broader than its ideology. The Bowie cover is a small act of honesty: admitting you like the thing you're supposed to reject [16][26].

## 5. Compilation and Reissue History

In 1990, Sub Pop released *Dry as a Bone / Rehab Doll* as a combined CD, which became the most widely available Green River release and the way most people encountered the band's music [25][26].

In 2019, Sub Pop released expanded deluxe editions of both *Dry as a Bone* and *Rehab Doll* separately, adding bonus tracks, alternate versions, and previously unreleased recordings. The reissues reflected a critical reassessment of Green River's importance -- no longer a footnote to Pearl Jam and Mudhoney, but a subject worthy of the kind of archival attention usually reserved for canonical bands [25][26][29].

## 6. The Producers

Three producers shaped Green River's recorded sound, each contributing a different aesthetic:

| Producer | Release | Approach |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **Chris Hanzsek** | *Come On Down* | Raw capture, minimal intervention |
| **Jack Endino** | *Dry as a Bone* | Energy preservation, lo-fi warmth |
| **Bruce Calder** | *Rehab Doll* | More polished, wider dynamic range |

### 6.1 Jack Endino and Reciprocal Recording

Endino's role extends far beyond Green River. As the operator of Reciprocal Recording and the producer of choice for the Seattle underground, he shaped the sonic identity of the entire grunge movement. His philosophy -- capture the energy, preserve the rawness, don't overproduce -- became the default approach for Seattle recordings. *Dry as a Bone*, *Superfuzz Bigmuff*, *Bleach* -- the three records that defined the Sub Pop sound -- were all recorded at Reciprocal with Endino at the board [16][22][25].

His rate of $10 per hour made professional recording accessible to bands with no money. The economic accessibility of Reciprocal Recording was as important to the grunge movement as any musical innovation. You can't release records if you can't afford to record them [17][25].

## 7. Musical Analysis

### 7.1 The Fusion

Green River's sound was a collision:

- **Downtuned, distorted guitars** layered thick, often with feedback used as texture
- **Rhythmic heaviness** drawn from metal and hard rock, played with punk looseness
- **Tempo shifts** between grinding sludge and frantic bursts
- **Vocals that snarled** rather than sang -- attitude and texture over melody [16][19]

### 7.2 Why "First Grunge Band"

The claim rests on chronology and influence. *Come On Down*, released November 1985, predates both the Melvins' debut EP and the *Deep Six* compilation. It is the first record released by a band playing what would later be called grunge [16][24].

More importantly, Green River was the band that most clearly embodied the fusion that defined grunge. Soundgarden leaned heavier toward metal. The Melvins were more experimental and sludgy. Malfunkshun was more glam. Green River sat at the exact intersection where punk energy met rock ambition [16][19].

## 8. The Internal Tension in Sound

The progression across the three releases tells the story of the split in audio:

**Come On Down** (1985): Raw, punk-inflected, Turner's garage influence still present. The five-piece lineup is balanced between punk and rock impulses.

**Dry as a Bone** (1987): Turner is gone, Fairweather's tighter guitar style has replaced him. The sound is heavier, more riff-oriented, but Endino's production keeps the rawness. The balance is shifting toward rock.

**Rehab Doll** (1988): The most polished recording, produced by Bruce Calder rather than Endino. Wider dynamics, cleaner separation between instruments. The rock side of the band has won the production argument -- but the songs still carry punk energy in the vocals and attitude.

Listen to the three releases in order and you hear a band pulling itself apart in slow motion. The sound gets bigger. The production gets cleaner. The tension between what Arm wants and what Gossard wants becomes audible in the gap between the vocals and the guitars. By *Rehab Doll*, the vocals and the instruments sound like they belong to different bands -- which, in a few months, they would [16][19][26].
