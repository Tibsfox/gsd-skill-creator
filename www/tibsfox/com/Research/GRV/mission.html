Mission Pack

The original research brief and mission package that initiated the Green River & the Seattle Sound research project. This document provides the foundational research from which all eight modules were developed, covering the complete arc of Seattle’s recorded music history from Coast Salish traditions through the grunge explosion to the present.

Source Document

The mission pack source material is a comprehensive 999-line TeX document and a 451-line deep research document, together covering band formation, discography, the Seattle scene context, musical style, the band’s breakup and its consequences, and the full century of Seattle music from Jackson Street jazz through hip-hop and electronic.

Research Scope

  • Ancestral Sounds & Jackson Street: Coast Salish musical traditions, the jazz corridor, Ray Charles and Quincy Jones in Seattle, Ernestine Anderson, union desegregation
  • Garage Rock Era: The Wailers, the Sonics, the Ventures, Jimi Hendrix, Etiquette Records, the “Louie Louie” explosion
  • Proto-Grunge & Green River: Mr. Epp, Green River formation (1984), the Deep Six compilation (1986), Sub Pop founding, Come On Down EP
  • Green River Discography: Come On Down, Dry as a Bone (SP11), Rehab Doll — complete track listings, production, and analysis
  • The Split: Punk vs. rock tension, the October 1987 breakup, Mudhoney, Mother Love Bone, Temple of the Dog, Pearl Jam
  • Grunge Explosion: Nevermind, Ten, Badmotorfinger, Sub Pop’s marketing, MTV, Cobain’s death, cultural absorption
  • Legacy & Living Scene: Post-grunge indie wave, Seattle hip-hop, electronic music, KEXP’s global transformation, Green River reunion shows
  • 31+ Sources: HistoryLink, Rolling Stone, NPR, KEXP, Sub Pop, Wikipedia, Discogs, King County, and scholarly works

Key Findings

Green River is literally a Rosetta Stone — a single artifact that translates into two different languages. The same band, playing the same music, contained the seeds of both Mudhoney (underground, indie, lo-fi, Sub Pop, punk credibility) and Pearl Jam (mainstream, major label, arena rock, global reach). One source text, two translations. The band proves that a single creative source can contain multiple valid interpretations simultaneously, and that the act of splitting — of reading the same text through different lenses — produces distinct but equally legitimate outputs.
