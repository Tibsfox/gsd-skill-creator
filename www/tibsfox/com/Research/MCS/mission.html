Mission Package

The Amiga Principle does not stop at software architecture. The same insight that built the Amiga — that specialized, purpose-built components working in concert produce outcomes impossible for generalized brute-force systems — applies to physical infrastructure at maritime scale. A shore-based data center optimized for compute but paying $0.12/kWh and running chiller plants is a generalized system. A maritime platform where the ocean provides cooling, the cable provides both power and backhaul, the vessels provide both compute and logistics, and the BPA dam provides baseload at $0.02/kWh is an Amiga. The question this mission answers is not whether the architecture is elegant. That was settled in the vision. The question is what it costs to build the Amiga at sea — and whether the convergence credits are large enough to close the gap.

The Through-Line

The space between the components is where the economics live. Individual cost sheets for data centers, subsea cables, offshore wind farms, and container vessels are available from IRENA, NREL, CBRE, and a dozen industry reports. What nobody has assembled is the integrated cost model — the one that treats convergence credits as first-class cost items, that quantifies what happens when the ocean provides free cooling, when one cable trench carries both power and data, when vessel hulls serve double duty as compute chassis and cargo platforms. The Amiga Principle, applied to maritime infrastructure: specialized components sharing physical media produce total system economics that general-purpose standalone deployment cannot match. This mission produces the number that proves it — or reveals the gap that remains.
